# Dependence of the Shell-Model Single-Particle Energies on Different Components of the Nucleon-Nucleon Interaction

> The spin-orbit splittings in the spectra of nuclei with mass numbers 5, 15 and 17 are studied within the framework of shell-model configuration mixing calculations including 2$\hbar ω$ excitations. The contributions of the two-body spin-orbit and tensor components of the nucleon-nucleon interaction are studied in various model spaces. It is found that the effects of the two-body spin-orbit interaction are dominant and quite sensitive to the size of the model-space considered. The effects of the tensor interaction are weaker. The correlations effects which are included in the larger (0+2) $\hbar ω$ shell-model space reduce the spin-orbit splitting in the case of A=5 by 20%, and enhance it for A=15 by about the same 20%. However, it is found that the correlations have a very small effect on the $d_{3/2} - d_{5/2}$ splitting in A=17.

# Dependence of the Shell-Model Single-Particle Energies on Different Components of the Nucleon-Nucleon Interaction

## Key claim

The two-body spin-orbit component of the nucleon-nucleon interaction dominates the effective spin-orbit splitting in nuclei with mass numbers 5, 15, and 17, while the tensor component contributes only weakly (less than 10% of the spin-orbit contribution) when using modern nucleon-nucleon interactions with weaker tensor components than older potentials like the Reid soft-core potential. The effects of higher-shell correlations reduce the spin-orbit splitting by about 20% for A=5, enhance it by about 20% for A=15, and have very little effect (less than 3%) for A=17.

## Method

Shell-model configuration mixing calculations were performed including up to 2ℏω excitations (and in some cases up to 4ℏω for A=5) using the OXBASH program, with the effective Hamiltonian based on a parameterized G-matrix derived from the Bonn A potential. The two-body spin-orbit and tensor interaction strengths were varied independently via parameters x and y in the interaction V(r)=V_c(r)+x·V_{s.o.}+y·V_t to isolate their separate contributions to the effective spin-orbit splitting in the A=5, A=15, and A=17 systems.

## Key numbers

- For A=5 with the realistic interaction (x=1, y=1), the spin-orbit splitting ΔE = E(1/2⁻) - E(3/2⁻) is 3.375 MeV in the 0ℏω space, 2.959 MeV in the (0+2)ℏω space, and 2.659 MeV in the (0+2+4)ℏω space.
- For A=5 with x=1 and y=0 (no tensor force), the spin-orbit splitting decreases from 3.375 MeV (0ℏω) to 2.716 MeV ((0+2)ℏω) to 2.431 MeV ((0+2+4)ℏω), a reduction of about 20% then 10%.
- For A=15 with x=1 and y=0, the spin-orbit splitting increases from 5.063 MeV (0ℏω) to 6.008 MeV ((0+2)ℏω), an enhancement of about 20%.
- For A=15 with x=1, y=1, the spin-orbit splitting is 5.063 MeV (0ℏω) and 5.698 MeV ((0+2)ℏω).
- For A=17 with x=1, y=1, the d₃/₂ - d₅/₂ splitting is 5.562 MeV (0ℏω) and 5.662 MeV ((0+2)ℏω), an increase of less than 3%.
- The observed spin-orbit splitting for A=15 is 6.324 MeV for ¹⁵N and 6.176 MeV for ¹⁵O.
- The observed spin-orbit splitting for A=5 is 7.5 ± 2.5 MeV for ⁵Li and 4.0 ± 1.0 MeV for ⁵He.
- For A=17, the experimental E(1s₁/₂) - E(0d₅/₂) difference yields E_com - E(1s₁/₂) = 1.465 MeV from observed data.

## Context

This work extends the perturbative studies of Scheerbaum (1976) and the earlier work of Zheng, Zamick, and Muther (1992) by performing non-perturbative diagonalizations in large model spaces, rather than relying on second-order perturbation theory. The results contradict the earlier findings of Terasawa (1960) and Arima and Terasawa (1960) who obtained large spin-orbit splittings from the tensor interaction alone, and instead support the modern understanding that the tensor component in modern nucleon-nucleon interactions (such as the Bonn potentials) is weaker due to compensation between one-pion-exchange and rho-meson-exchange contributions. The work enables systematic shell-model studies of spin-orbit splitting across the nuclear chart using realistic interactions, and highlights the need for large-space calculations to properly account for differences between particle states and hole states.
