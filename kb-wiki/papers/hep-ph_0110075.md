# Femto-Photography of Protons to Nuclei with Deeply Virtual Compton Scattering

> Developments in deeply virtual Compton scattering allow the direct measurements of scattering amplitudes for exchange of a highly virtual photon with fine spatial resolution. Real-space images of the target can be obtained from this information. Spatial resolution is determined by the momentum transfer rather than the wavelength of the detected photon. Quantum photographs of the proton, nuclei, and other elementary particles with resolution on the scale of a fraction of a femtometer is feasible with existing experimental technology.

## Key claim

Deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS) allows direct measurement of scattering amplitudes that can be Fourier-transformed to produce real-space images of the proton and nuclei with spatial resolution determined by the momentum transfer rather than the wavelength of the detected photon, with resolution on the scale of a fraction of a femtometer feasible using existing experimental technology. The authors hedge that the extracted images represent what was observable under the conditions of the experiment, and that skewness values close to zero or one will involve different physics, as for Bjorken x dependence in deeply inelastic scattering.

## Method

The authors propose extracting the DVCS amplitude from the interference term between the Bethe-Heitler amplitude (electron emission) and the target emission amplitude, using electron-positron charge asymmetry to extract the real part and longitudinal lepton polarization to extract the imaginary part. The measured amplitudes are fitted as smooth functions of the momentum transfer Δ, then Fourier-transformed in transverse momentum Δ_T to obtain a profile amplitude function f(b_T) in transverse spatial coordinates, which is squared to produce the image representing a weighted probability to find quarks in the transverse image plane.

## Key numbers

- Spatial resolution is of order Δb_T ~ ħ/√(Q²), with Q² > 1 GeV² required for the short-distance regime
- The proton charge radius from Hofstadter's experiments was found to be about 0.7 femtometer
- The momentum transfer range for measurement is from -t_min to -t ~ 1 GeV²
- The proton's size is about 10⁻⁷ of the size of interesting biological molecules explored via Bragg diffraction
- For imaging biological molecules via nuclear locations, one needs |Δ| < ~ ħ/Å ~ keV, requiring relative beam precision of order 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻⁷ with relativistic beams in the 10 MeV to GeV range

## Context

This work extends the classic electron scattering experiments of Hofstadter (1961) and the subsequent development of deeply inelastic scattering and Quantum Chromodynamics, proposing that DVCS can overcome the limitations of the non-relativistic impulse approximation that made the traditional interpretation of form factors as charge distributions questionable. The approach enables a new era of systematic comparison of partonic structure of hadrons, including the deuteron (where the quark and gluon wave function has never been measured), and can address open questions about the proton's size, shape, and the distribution of up quarks, strange quarks, and gluons, as well as quark orbital angular momentum.
