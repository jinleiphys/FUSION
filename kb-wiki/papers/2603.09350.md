# High-Order Matrix Numerov for Singular Potentials

> The matrix Numerov method provides an efficient framework for solving the time-independent Schrödinger equation as a matrix eigenvalue problem. However, for singular potentials such as the Coulomb interaction, the expected fourth-order convergence deteriorates for low angular momenta due to the behavior of the potential near the origin. We show that this loss of accuracy originates from an implicit boundary assumption in the standard formulation. By incorporating analytic near-origin information into the discretized Hamiltonian, we derive simple boundary corrections that restore fourth-order convergence and can even produce higher convergence rates for $s$- and $p$-wave energies. The resulting scheme preserves the simplicity and computational efficiency of the original method while significantly improving its accuracy for singular potentials.

## Key claim

The standard matrix Numerov method's fourth-order convergence degrades to second order for s-waves and third order for p-waves when applied to singular potentials like the Coulomb interaction, but this loss of accuracy originates from an implicit boundary assumption at the origin rather than from the singularity itself; incorporating analytic near-origin information into the discretized Hamiltonian restores fourth-order convergence and can even yield fifth-order convergence for s-waves.

## Method

The author discretizes the radial Schrodinger equation on a uniform grid and reformulates the standard Numerov scheme as a generalized matrix eigenvalue problem. By analyzing the Taylor expansion of the wavefunction near r=0 and using the Schrodinger equation to relate derivatives at the origin, the author derives boundary corrections that modify only the first row of the Hamiltonian matrix. These corrections are computed to first, second, and third order for s-waves (ell=0) and to first and second order for p-waves (ell=1), with the corrections expressed as simple additive terms to specific Hamiltonian matrix elements involving the grid spacing Delta and nuclear charge Z.

## Key numbers

- For the hydrogen atom ground state (n=1, ell=0), standard matrix Numerov yields convergence exponent q=2 for both energy and radius; first-order correction raises q to 3; second-order correction raises q to 4; third-order correction raises q to 5.
- For the hydrogen atom n=1, ell=1 state, standard matrix Numerov yields q=3; first-order correction restores q=4 for both energy and radius.
- For the hydrogen atom n=1, ell=2 state, standard matrix Numerov already achieves q=4 with no corrections needed.
- For the harmonic oscillator n=0, ell=1 state, standard matrix Numerov yields q=3 for energy; first-order correction restores q=4.
- The first-order boundary correction for ell=0 modifies H_11 by subtracting Z/(12*Delta); the second-order correction modifies H_11 by subtracting 4Z/(24*Delta) and H_12 by adding Z/(24*Delta).
- The first-order boundary correction for ell=1 modifies H_11 by adding 1/(12*Delta^2).

## Context

This work extends the matrix Numerov method introduced by Pillai, Goglio, and Walker (2012), who noted the reduced convergence for the hydrogen atom but did not resolve it. The author identifies that the standard formulation implicitly assumes W_0*u_0=0 at the origin, which fails for s-waves with Coulomb potentials and for p-waves due to the centrifugal term. The corrected method preserves the simplicity of the original scheme while enabling high-precision calculations of hydrogenic systems and can be extended to other central potentials with similar singular behavior.
