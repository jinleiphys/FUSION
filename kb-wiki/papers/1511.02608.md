# Correlations between the fragmentation modes and light charged particles emission in heavy ion collisions

> The correlations between the shape of rapidity distribution of the yield of light charged particles and the fragmentation modes in semi-peripheral collisions for $^{70}$Zn+$^{70}$Zn, $^{64}$Zn+$^{64}$Zn and $^{64}$Ni+$^{64}$Ni at the beam energy of 35MeV/nucleon are investigated based on ImQMD05 code. Our studies show there is an interplay between the binary, ternary and multi-fragmentation break-up modes. The binary and ternary break-up modes more prefer to emit light charged particles at middle rapidity and give larger values of $R_{yield}^{mid}$ compared with the multi-fragmentation break-up mode does. The reduced rapidity distribution for the normalized yields of p, d, t, $^3$He, $^4$He and $^6$He and the corresponding values of $R_{yield}^{mid}$ can be used to estimate the probability of multi-fragmentation break-up modes. By comparing to experimental data, our results illustrate that $\ge$40\% of the collisions events belong to the multi-fragmentation break-up mode for the reactions we studied.

## Key claim
The shape of the reduced rapidity distribution of light charged particles (LCPs) and the corresponding \(R_{yield}^{mid}\) values can be used to estimate the probability of multi-fragmentation break-up modes in semi-peripheral heavy ion collisions, with the condition that reproducing experimental data requires at least 40% of collision events to belong to the multi-fragmentation mode in dynamical approaches.

## Method
The authors used the ImQMD05 transport code, an improved quantum molecular dynamics model, to simulate semi-peripheral collisions of \(^{70}\)Zn+\(^{70}\)Zn, \(^{64}\)Zn+\(^{64}\)Zn, and \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{64}\)Ni at a beam energy of 35 MeV/nucleon and an impact parameter of 4 fm. Fragments were identified using the isospin-dependent minimum spanning tree (iso-MST) cluster recognition method, and events were classified into binary, ternary, and multi-fragmentation break-up modes based on the multiplicity of fragments with charge Z \(\ge\) 3. The rapidity distributions of p, d, t, \(^3\)He, \(^4\)He, and \(^6\)He were analyzed for each break-up mode, and the ratio \(R_{yield}^{mid}\) (yield at mid-rapidity divided by yield at projectile rapidity) was computed to quantify the shape of the distributions.

## Key numbers
- For \(^{70}\)Zn+\(^{70}\)Zn at 35 MeV/nucleon and b=4 fm with \(\gamma_i=2.0\) (\(\gamma_i=0.5\)): \(\sim\)18% (\(\sim\)19%) of events are binary break-up, \(\sim\)36% (\(\sim\)32%) are ternary break-up, and \(\sim\)46% (\(\sim\)49%) are multi-fragmentation break-up.
- For \(^{70}\)Zn+\(^{70}\)Zn at 35 MeV/nucleon and b=4 fm with \(\gamma_i=2.0\), the yield of \(^3\)He at \(y_r\sim0\) from binary mode is 35% larger than from multi-fragmentation mode; the yield of \(^6\)He at \(y_r\sim0\) from binary mode is 70% larger than from multi-fragmentation mode.
- For \(^{70}\)Zn+\(^{70}\)Zn at 35 MeV/nucleon and b=4 fm with \(\gamma_i=2.0\), the \(R_{yield}^{mid}\) value for \(^6\)He is 2.9 in binary break-up events, 2.5 in ternary break-up events, and 2.4 in multi-fragmentation break-up events; with \(\gamma_i=0.5\), the corresponding values are 2.1, 1.8, and 1.8.
- The analysis suggests that \(\ge\)40% of collision events belong to the multi-fragmentation break-up mode for the reactions studied.

## Context
This work extends previous studies by Kohley et al. on semi-peripheral collisions of \(^{70}\)Zn+\(^{70}\)Zn, \(^{64}\)Zn+\(^{64}\)Zn, and \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{64}\)Ni at 35 MeV/nucleon, where discrepancies between experimental rapidity distributions and Stochastic Mean Field (SMF) model calculations were noted. The authors build on earlier findings by Li (1991) regarding the interplay between binary/ternary and multi-fragmentation modes at low-intermediate energies (20-40 MeV/nucleon). The results enable future experimental reanalysis of the correlation between multi-fragmentation probability and \(R_{yield}^{mid}\) to better constrain the symmetry energy and reaction mechanism in heavy ion collisions.
