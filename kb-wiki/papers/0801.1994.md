# Variation of fundamental constants in space and time: theory and observations

> Review of recent works devoted to the temporal and spatial variation of the fundamental constants and dependence of the fundamental constants on the gravitational potential (violation of local position invariance) is presented. We discuss the variation of the fine structure constant $α=e^2/\hbar c$, strong interaction and fundamental masses (Higgs vacuum), e.g. the electron-to-proton mass ratio $μ=m_e/M_p$ or $X_e=m_e/Λ_{QCD}$ and $X_q=m_q/Λ_{QCD}$. We also present new results from Big Bang nucleosynthesis and Oklo natural nuclear reactor data and propose new measurements of enhanced effects in atoms, nuclei and molecules, both in quasar and laboratory spectra.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that variations of fundamental constants (fine structure constant α, electron-to-proton mass ratio μ, and quark-mass-to-QCD-scale ratios X_q and X_s) may be detectable through enhanced effects in atoms, nuclei, and molecules, but the claimed variations from quasar absorption spectra and Big Bang nucleosynthesis remain controversial, with some results showing nonzero signals and others giving null limits depending on the data set and analysis methods used.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical frameworks connecting variations of different fundamental constants through Grand Unification relations, then examines observational constraints from Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) abundances of ²H, ⁴He, and ⁷Li, the Oklo natural nuclear reactor resonance in ¹⁴⁹Sm, quasar absorption spectra of multiple atomic species, and laboratory atomic and molecular clock comparisons. Relativistic many-body calculations are used to compute sensitivity coefficients q for atomic transitions, and nuclear calculations relate binding energy variations to quark mass changes.

## Key numbers

- BBN analysis yields δX_q/X_q = 0.013 ± 0.002 (statistically weighted average dominated by ⁷Li data), with a theoretical uncertainty factor K ∼ 1 (expected accuracy about a factor of 2)
- Oklo data gives limit |δE_r| < 0.1 eV for the 0.1 eV resonance in ¹⁴⁹Sm, leading to |0.01 δα/α − 0.1 δX_q/X_q + δX_s/X_s| < 10⁻⁹ and |Ẋ_s/X_s| < 10⁻¹⁸ yr⁻¹ assuming linear time dependence over 2 billion years
- Quasar absorption spectra (Keck telescope, 143 absorption systems, redshift range 0.2 < z < 4.2) give δα/α = (−0.543 ± 0.116) × 10⁻⁵, or d ln α/dt = (6.40 ± 1.35) × 10⁻¹⁶ per year
- Re-analysis of VLT data gives δα/α = (−0.64 ± 0.36) × 10⁻⁵ (instead of (−0.06 ± 0.06) × 10⁻⁵ in the original analysis)
- Hyperfine-to-optical comparison in quasar spectra gives δF/F = (6.3 ± 9.9) × 10⁻⁶ and Ḟ/F = (−6 ± 12) × 10⁻¹⁶ yr⁻¹
- Dysprosium atomic clock gives current limit ȧ/α = (−2.7 ± 2.6) × 10⁻¹⁵ yr⁻¹
- Best combined limit from ammonia inversion spectra and atomic clocks: μ̇/μ = Ẋ_e/X_e = (1 ± 3) × 10⁻¹⁶ yr⁻¹ and ȧ/α = (−0.8 ± 0.8) × 10⁻¹⁶ yr⁻¹
- ²²⁹Th nuclear transition (7.6 ± 0.5) eV above ground state has estimated width ∼ 10⁻⁴ Hz and enhancement factor ∼ 10⁵ for variations of α and X_q
- Molecular enhancement factors: LaS hyperfine-rotational cancellation gives δω/ω ≈ 600 δα/α; Cl₂⁺ fine-structure-vibrational cancellation gives K = 1600; HfF⁺ gives δω ≈ 20000 cm⁻¹ (δα/α + 0.04 δμ/μ)

## Context

This review extends the author's prior work on enhanced effects in atomic and molecular spectra (Dzuba, Flambaum, Webb 1999; Flambaum and Shuryak 2002, 2003) and addresses the ongoing controversy between the Keck telescope results (Webb et al. 2001; Murphy et al. 2003) showing nonzero α variation and VLT results (Chand et al. 2004) giving null results, with the possibility that spatial variation of α could explain the difference between Northern and Southern hemisphere observations. The paper enables future searches using nuclear clocks (²²⁹Th), ultracold molecules near Feshbach resonances, and diatomic molecules with accidental degeneracies, which could improve sensitivity to fundamental constant variations by many orders of magnitude compared to current atomic clock limits.
