# Validity of a finite temperature expansion for dense nuclear matter

> In this work we provide a new, well-controlled expansion of the equation of state of dense matter from zero to finite temperatures ($T$) while covering a wide range of charge fractions ($Y_Q$), from pure neutron to isospin symmetric nuclear matter. Our expansion can be used to describe neutron star mergers using the equation of state inferred from neutron star observations. We discuss how knowledge from low-energy nuclear experiments and heavy-ion collisions can be directly incorporated into the expansion. We also suggest new thermodynamic quantities of interest that can be calculated from theoretical models or directly inferred by experimental data that can be used to infer the finite temperature equation of state. With our new method, we can quantify the uncertainty in our finite $T$ and $Y_Q$ expansions without making assumptions about the underlying degrees of freedom. We can reproduce results from a microscopic equation of state up to $T=100$ MeV for baryon chemical potential $μ_B\gtrsim 1100$ MeV ($\sim1-2 \ n_{\rm sat}$) within $5\%$ error, with even better results for larger $μ_B$ and/or lower $T$. We investigate the sources of numerical and theoretical uncertainty and discuss future directions of study.

# Validity of a finite temperature expansion for dense nuclear matter

## Key claim

The authors present a Taylor expansion of the equation of state in powers of (T/μ_B) that can extend any cold, beta-equilibrated nuclear matter model to finite temperatures without assuming specific degrees of freedom, but the expansion is only valid for baryon chemical potentials μ_B ≳ 1100 MeV (approximately 1-2 times nuclear saturation density) and temperatures up to 100 MeV, and it cannot capture critical scaling near the liquid-gas phase transition.

## Method

The authors perform a Taylor expansion of the pressure in temperature at fixed chemical potentials, truncating at O(T²) after noting that the linear term vanishes by the third law of thermodynamics. They then expand the entropy per baryon around symmetric nuclear matter (δ=0) in powers of the isospin asymmetry parameter δ = 1-2Y_Q, keeping terms up to O(δ²). The resulting framework combines a zero-temperature symmetry energy expansion (Yao 2023) with a finite-temperature expansion, requiring two density-dependent coefficients that can be constrained by microscopic models or heavy-ion collision data. The method is tested against a relativistic mean-field model with nucleon-meson couplings that reproduce chiral effective field theory results and astrophysical constraints.

## Key numbers

- The expansion reproduces a microscopic equation of state up to T = 100 MeV for baryon chemical potential μ_B ≳ 1100 MeV (approximately 1-2 n_sat) within 5% error, with better results for larger μ_B or lower T
- Nuclear saturation density n_sat is taken as 0.16 fm⁻³
- The expansion parameter (T/μ_B) is less than 0.1 in the region relevant for neutron star mergers (T ≲ 70 MeV, μ_B ≳ 950 MeV)
- The liquid-gas phase transition for symmetric nuclear matter is estimated to occur around μ_B = 900 MeV with a critical point at T ∼ 15-20 MeV
- The relativistic mean-field model uses coupling constants g_σ = 8.3965, g_ω = 10.1845, and g_ρ = 10.9176 with meson-meson couplings b = 0.00438046, c = -0.00359399, and b_1 = 5.18964

## Context

This work extends the "hybrid" approach of Janka (1993) and the effective mass parametrization of Raithel (2019) by removing the assumption that thermal contributions can be modeled as an ideal fluid or that degrees of freedom are limited to protons, neutrons, and electrons. The method builds on the symmetry energy expansion of Yao (2023) for zero-temperature equations of state and enables the use of any cold neutron star equation of state in simulations of neutron star mergers. The framework also connects to heavy-ion collision data through freeze-out parameter extraction, though the authors note that current heavy-ion data only covers Y_Q ≳ 0.4, far from the neutron-rich matter in mergers, and that upcoming FAIR experiments could fill this gap.
