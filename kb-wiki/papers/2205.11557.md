# Effect of shake-up on the rate of a neutrinoless double electronic capture in $^{164}$Er

> Traditionally double neutrinoless electronic capture is considered as a resonance process. We have fulfilled shake-off probability calculations, leading to ionization of the electron shell, in the case of $^{164}$Er. Allowance for the shake-off removes the requirement of resonance, leading to an increase of the capture rate. The contribution of the new mechanism increases the capture rate by a factor of 5.6 compared to the conventional resonance fluorescence mechanism. It also increases the probability of electron capture from higher shells, which must be foreseen in an experimental study. Moreover, effect of the shake-off can potentially expand the list of candidate nuclei for experiments.

## Key claim

The paper argues that including shake-off (ionization of the electron shell) removes the strict resonance requirement for neutrinoless double electronic capture in $^{164}$Er, increasing the capture rate by a factor of 5.6 compared to the conventional resonance-fluorescence mechanism; this factor is conditional on the specific energy defect of 6.82 keV for the $L_1L_1$ capture channel.

## Method

The authors calculated shake-off probabilities using the RAINE program suite in a one-electron approximation with self-consistent Dirac-Fock wavefunctions. They computed overlap integrals between the initial electron wavefunction in the neutral parent atom and the final electron wavefunction in the continuum of the daughter atom with three vacancies, for a range of hypothetical energy defects from 0.05 to 20 keV. The total gain factor was obtained by summing contributions from all energetically allowed electron shells and comparing to the Breit-Wigner resonance factor for the conventional mechanism.

## Key numbers

* For $^{164}$Er, the energy defect $\Delta$ for the $L_1L_1$ capture channel is 6.82 keV.
* The total gain factor from shake-off relative to the resonance-fluorescence mechanism is 5.6 for $^{164}$Er.
* For the $L_1L_1$ channel alone, the gain factor is 2.81; including higher shells ($LM$, $MM$, $LN$, $MN$, $NN$) adds contributions of 1.22, 0.20, 0.29, 0.05, and 0.01 respectively, summing to 4.58.
* The updated half-life estimate for $^{164}$Er is $T_{1/2}^{0\nu} \approx 3.6 \times 10^{29} \left| \frac{1\text{ eV}}{m_{\beta\beta}} \right|^2$ years, compared to the earlier resonance-based estimate of $2 \times 10^{30}$ years.
* For $^{152}$Gd, the non-resonant half-life is $8 \times 10^{26}$ years versus $10^{27}$ years for the resonant mechanism; for $^{180}$W, the non-resonant half-life is $3 \times 10^{27}$ years versus $3 \times 10^{28}$ years.
* The total width $\Gamma$ of the $L_1$ hole in Dy is 4.3 eV.

## Context

This work extends the shake-off mechanism previously applied to $^{152}$Gd by Karpeshin and Trzhaskovskaya (2020), where the shake-off contribution was 23% of the resonant mechanism. For $^{164}$Er, with a larger energy defect of 6.82 keV, the shake-off mechanism becomes dominant, increasing the rate by a factor of 5.6. The results enable more realistic half-life estimates for candidate nuclei and suggest that $^{180}$W, with a non-resonant half-life only four times larger than $^{152}$Gd, may become a competitive candidate given the low natural abundance (0.2%) of $^{152}$Gd. The work also predicts that 20% of captures occur from shells higher than $L_1$, producing additional satellite fluorescence lines that could aid experimental identification.
