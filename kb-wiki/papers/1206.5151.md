# Quantifying short-range correlations in nuclei

> An approximate method to quantify the magnitude of the two-nucleon (2N) and three-nucleon (3N) short-range correlations (SRC) and their mass dependence is proposed. The proposed method relies on the concept of the "universality" or "local nuclear character" of the SRC. We quantify the SRC by computing the number of independent-particle model (IPM) nucleon pairs and triples which reveal beyond-mean-field behavior. It is argued that those can be identified by counting the number of nucleon pairs and triples in a zero relative orbital momentum state. A method to determine the quantum numbers of pairs and triples in an arbitrary mean-field basis is outlined. The mass dependence of the 2N and 3N SRC is studied. The predictions are compared to measurements. This includes the ratio of the inclusive inelastic electron scattering cross sections of nuclei to 2H and 3He at large values of the Bjorken variable. Corrections stemming from the center-of-mass motion of the pairs are estimated. We find that the relative probability per nucleon for 2N and 3N SRC has a soft dependence with mass number A and that the proton-neutron 2N SRC outnumber the proton-proton (neutron-neutron) 2N SRC. A linear relationship between the magnitude of the EMC effect and the predicted number of proton-neutron SRC pairs is observed. This provides support for the role of local nuclear dynamics on the EMC effect.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the number of two-nucleon (2N) and three-nucleon (3N) short-range correlations (SRC) in any nucleus can be reasonably quantified by counting nucleon pairs and triples that occupy a zero relative orbital momentum state in a mean-field ground-state wave function, relying on the "universality" or "local nuclear character" of SRC rather than performing full ab-initio calculations.

## Method

The authors compute the number of independent-particle model (IPM) nucleon pairs and triples that exhibit beyond-mean-field behavior by identifying those in a zero relative orbital momentum state using Moshinsky brackets to transform from single-particle coordinates to relative and center-of-mass coordinates. For an arbitrary mean-field basis, the single-particle states are expanded in a harmonic oscillator basis to perform this counting. The predicted numbers of SRC pairs are then compared to experimental data from inclusive inelastic electron scattering cross section ratios and to the magnitude of the EMC effect.

## Key numbers

- The fraction of nucleons that are mean-field like is approximately 60-80%, as indicated by the normalization condition $\int dk k^2 n^{(0)}_1(k) \approx 0.6 - 0.8$.
- The Boltzmann fit to the mean-field momentum distribution yields $kT \approx 12.0 \pm 0.5$ MeV for $^{12}$C, $14 \pm 1$ MeV for $^{56}$Fe, and $16 \pm 1$ MeV for $^{208}$Pb.
- The typical energy exchange per momentum degree-of-freedom $\frac{1}{2}kT$ from the IPM part of the momentum distribution is of order 6-8 MeV.
- The correlated part of the pn two-body momentum distribution is described for relative momenta $2k_F \lesssim k_{12}$ and center-of-mass momenta $P_{12} \lesssim 150$ MeV.
- The tensor correlation function has its largest effect for moderate relative momenta in the range $100 \lesssim k_{12} \lesssim 500$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of SRC as a universal, local nuclear phenomenon previously studied in light nuclei through ab-initio calculations (Schiavilla 2006, Wiringa 2008, Feldmeier 2011) and in medium-weight nuclei through cluster expansion methods (Alvioli 2007) and correlated-basis function theory (Bisconti 2006, 2007). The method enables systematic study of the mass and isospin dependence of SRC across a broad range of nuclei from He to Pb without combining results from different types of calculations, and provides support for the role of local nuclear dynamics in the EMC effect by establishing a linear relationship between the predicted number of proton-neutron SRC pairs and the magnitude of the EMC effect, corroborating the phenomenological relationship observed by Weinstein (2010), Hen (2012), and Arrington (2012).
