# On the timescale of quasi fission and Coulomb fission

> Coulomb fission mechanism may take place if the maximum Coulomb-excitation energy transfer in a reaction exceeds the fission barrier of either the projectile or target. This condition is satisfied by all the reactions used for the earlier blocking measurements except one reaction 208 Pb + Natural Ge crystal, where the measured timescale was below the measuring limit of the blocking measurements < 1 as. Hence, inclusion of the Coulomb fission in the data analysis of the blocking experiments leads us to interpret that the measured time longer than a few attoseconds (about 2-2.5 as) is nothing but belonging to the Coulomb fission timescale and shorter than 1 as are due to the quasifission. Consequently, this finding resolves the critical discrepancies between the fission timescale measurements using the nuclear and blocking techniques. This, in turn, validates the fact that the quasifission timescale is indeed of the order of zeptoseconds in accordance with the nuclear experiments and theories. It thus provides a radical input in understanding the reaction mechanism for heavy element formation via fusion evaporation processes

## Key claim

The paper argues that the attosecond-scale fission times measured in crystal blocking experiments (approximately 2-2.5 as) are actually Coulomb fission timescales, not quasifission timescales, and that quasifission timescales are indeed of the order of zeptoseconds as nuclear techniques and theories predict. This reinterpretation resolves the long-standing discrepancy between fission times measured by nuclear techniques (zeptoseconds) and atomic techniques (attoseconds), provided that the maximum Coulomb-excitation energy transfer exceeds the fission barrier of the heavier reaction partner.

## Method

The authors calculated the maximum Coulomb-excitation energy transfer (MCET) for each reaction used in previous crystal blocking experiments and compared it to the fission barrier of the heavier projectile or target nucleus. They evaluated quasifission lifetimes using a dinuclear system model with a decay constant formalism, fusion-fission lifetimes using a statistical model, and Coulomb fission cross-sections using virtual photon spectra and photoabsorption cross-sections via the LISE++ code. The highest rotational spin states populated before exceeding the fission barrier were estimated and compared with experimentally observed values from Coulomb excitation experiments.

## Key numbers

- Quasifission lifetimes ($\tau_{qf}$) for all reactions studied are less than 50 zeptoseconds (zs), with values ranging from 1.2 zs to 50.5 zs depending on the reaction
- Fusion-fission lifetimes ($\tau_{ff}$) are greater than 13 attoseconds (as), ranging from 13.8 as to 2505 as across the reactions
- The measured timescale from blocking experiments attributed to Coulomb fission is approximately 2-2.5 as
- For the $^{208}$Pb + Ge single crystal reactions, the MCET (4.5-5.8 MeV) is far below the fission barrier of $^{208}$Pb (24.9 MeV), and the Coulomb fission cross-section is eight orders of magnitude lower than the quasifission cross-section (e.g., 2.8E-8 mb vs 3.3 mb for $^{208}$Pb + $^{70}$Ge)
- For $^{238}$U + $^{28-30}$Si reactions at 5712 MeV, the Coulomb fission cross-section (12.2-12.8 mb) is four orders of magnitude higher than the quasifission cross-section (1.1E-4 mb)
- The MCET for $^{238}$U + $^{28}$Si is 224.5 MeV, exceeding the fission barrier of $^{238}$U (4.9 MeV) by a factor of approximately 46

## Context

This work resolves the critical discrepancy between fission timescale measurements using nuclear techniques (mass-angle distribution, neutron-clock method, giant dipole resonances) and atomic techniques (crystal blocking, x-ray fluorescence) that had persisted for decades. Prior work by Toke (1985), Hinde (1992), and others using nuclear techniques consistently found zeptosecond timescales for quasifission, while blocking experiments by Goldenbaum (1999), Andersen (2007, 2008), and Morjean (2008) reported attosecond timescales. The authors' reinterpretation enables a revised compound nucleus formation probability that includes both quasifission and Coulomb fission hindrance, which may guide future superheavy element synthesis experiments by choosing beam energies that avoid Coulomb fission.
