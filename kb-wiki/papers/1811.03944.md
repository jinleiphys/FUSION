# Unwrapping phase fluctuations in one dimension

> Correlation functions in one-dimensional complex scalar field theory provide a toy model for phase fluctuations, sign problems, and signal-to-noise problems in lattice field theory. Phase unwrapping techniques from signal processing are applied to lattice field theory in order to map compact random phases to noncompact random variables that can be numerically sampled without sign or signal-to-noise problems. A cumulant expansion can be used to reconstruct average correlation functions from moments of unwrapped phases, but points where the field magnitude fluctuates close to zero lead to ambiguities in the definition of the unwrapped phase and significant noise at higher orders in the cumulant expansion. Phase unwrapping algorithms that average fluctuations over physical length scales improve, but do not completely resolve, these issues in one dimension. Similar issues are seen in other applications of phase unwrapping, where they are found to be more tractable in higher dimensions.

## Key claim

Phase unwrapping techniques from signal processing can map compact random phases to noncompact random variables that can be numerically sampled without sign or signal-to-noise problems in one-dimensional complex scalar field theory, but points where the field magnitude fluctuates close to zero lead to ambiguities in the definition of the unwrapped phase and significant noise at higher orders in the cumulant expansion, and these issues are not completely resolved by phase unwrapping algorithms that average fluctuations over physical length scales.

## Method

The authors applied three numerical phase unwrapping schemes (single-point integration, windowed integration, and Gaussian-weighted integration) to Monte Carlo ensembles of (0+1)D complex scalar field theory with both free and interacting actions, using a cumulant expansion to reconstruct average correlation functions from moments of unwrapped phases. They generated five families of ensembles (A through E) with different mass parameters M^2 and lattice spacings, including both non-interacting and interacting cases with positive and negative mass-squared, and compared phase-unwrapped results to dual-variable ensembles where phase fluctuations are analytically integrated out.

## Key numbers

- The signal-to-noise ratio for standard correlation functions in the non-interacting theory scales as StN(Re[G_Q,2P(t)]) = sqrt(2N) e^{-Q E t} [1 + O(e^{-2Et}) + O(N^{-1/2})], showing exponential degradation with charge Q and time t
- For unwrapped phases with normally-distributed fluctuations, the signal-to-noise ratio for the correlation function estimate scales as StN(tilde-G(t)) ≈ sqrt(N)/(sqrt(2) Et), giving polynomial rather than exponential degradation
- The second-order cumulant estimate of ground-state energy for charge sectors Q=1 through Q=8 showed minimal degradation of signal-to-noise with time separation, with the second-order estimate becoming more precise than dual-variable estimates at large t
- The probability of large phase jumps (|θ(t) - θ(t-1)| > π/2) fell off much slower than the exponential prediction from the von Mises distribution, and the expected number of large phase jumps per field configuration grew with increasing correlation length

## Context

This work extends the analysis of phase fluctuations in lattice QCD baryon correlation functions by Wagman (2016) to a simpler (0+1)D complex scalar field toy model where analytical results are tractable. The cumulant expansion approach builds on methods explored by Endres (2011) for signal-to-noise problems, and the phase unwrapping techniques are analogous to those used in signal processing, radar interferometry, x-ray crystallography, and magnetic resonance imaging. The results suggest that while phase unwrapping can reduce exponential signal-to-noise problems to polynomial scaling in one dimension, the accumulation of errors from near-zero field magnitude points makes one-dimensional theories particularly challenging, and multidimensional phase unwrapping algorithms that avoid analogous numerical instabilities might be more promising for lattice field theory applications.
