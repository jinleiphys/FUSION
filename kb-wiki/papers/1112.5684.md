# Nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernova explosions triggered by a quark-hadron phase transition

> We explore heavy element nucleosynthesis in the explosion of massive stars which are triggered by a quark-hadron phase transition during the early post bounce phase of core-collapse supernovae. The present study is based on general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics simulations with three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport in spherical symmetry, which utilize a quark-hadron hybrid equation of state based on the MIT bag model for strange quark matter. The quark-hadron phase transition inside the stellar core forms a shock wave propagating towards the surface of the proto-neutron star. The shock wave results in an explosion and ejects neutron-rich matter which is piled up or accreting on the proto-neutron star. Later, during the cooling phase, the proto-neutron star develops a proton-rich neutrino-driven wind. We present a detailed analysis of the nucleosynthesis outcome in both neutron-rich and proton-rich ejecta and compare our integrated nucleosynthesis with observations of metal poor stars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a quark-hadron phase transition during the early post-bounce phase of core-collapse supernovae can trigger an explosion that ejects neutron-rich material, enabling a "weak" r-process that produces elements up to the second peak (A ~ 130); however, the strong r-process producing the third peak (A ~ 195) and actinides is only barely possible under uncertainties in the explosion dynamics.

## Method

The authors performed general-relativistic radiation hydrodynamics simulations in spherical symmetry using the AGILE-BOLTZTRAN code with three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport, employing a quark-hadron hybrid equation of state based on the MIT bag model for strange quark matter. They selected a 10.8 solar mass progenitor model with an early phase transition to quark matter close to normal nuclear density, then post-processed the hydrodynamic trajectories of 120 Lagrangian mass zones through a nuclear reaction network containing over 4000 nuclei from neutrons up to fermium (Z=100), including neutrino interactions with matter.

## Key numbers

- The progenitor model has a mass of 10.8 solar masses, with an explosion energy consistent with the expected order of magnitude of 10^51 erg
- The maximum gravitational mass for the adopted hybrid EOS is 1.5026 solar masses
- The quark-hadron phase transition occurs about 0.4 seconds after the initial core bounce
- Electron fractions (Ye) in the ejected neutron-rich matter range from 0.32 to 0.48 for the outer prompt-ejected zones, while the neutrino-driven wind produces proton-rich ejecta with Ye up to 0.55
- The neutrino-driven wind ejecta have entropies up to about 85 k_B per baryon, while the outer ejected regions have entropies of 30-50 k_B per baryon
- The difference between mean anti-neutrino and neutrino energies is less than 4 MeV, which is less than 4 times the neutron-proton mass difference of 1.293 MeV
- The weak r-process produces elements up to mass number A ~ 130 (second peak), while the strong r-process would produce A ~ 195 (third peak)

## Context

This work extends the quark-nova scenario proposed by Jaikumar (2007) by investigating core-collapse supernova explosions triggered by a quark-hadron phase transition during the early post-bounce phase, rather than after the explosion. It addresses the long-standing problem that steady-state neutrino-driven wind models (Thompson 2001, Wanajo 2006) and fully hydrodynamical simulations (Arcones 2007) showed it is very hard to attain the high entropies required for a strong r-process in standard core-collapse supernovae. The results enable future investigations of whether quark-hadron hybrid equations of state that allow maximum masses above 2 solar masses (as observed for pulsars J1903+0327 and J1614-2230) can produce similar dynamical evolutions and nucleosynthesis outcomes.
