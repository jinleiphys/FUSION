# Caloric curves of atomic nuclei and other small systems

> Caloric curves have traditionally been derived within the microcanonical ensemble via dS/dE=1/T or within the canonical ensemble via E=T^2*d(ln Z)/dT. In the thermodynamical limit, i.e., for large systems, both caloric curves give the same result. For small systems like nuclei, the two caloric curves are in general different from each other and neither one is reasonable. Using dS/dE=1/T, spurious structures like negative temperatures and negative heat capacities can occur and have indeed been discussed in the literature. Using E=T^2*d(ln Z)/dT a very featureless caloric curve is obtained which generally smoothes too much over structural changes in the system. A new approach for caloric curves based on the two-dimensional probability distribution P(E,T) will be discussed.

## Key claim

For small systems like atomic nuclei, the traditional microcanonical and canonical caloric curves are in general different from each other and neither one is reasonable; the authors propose a new construction based on the two-dimensional probability distribution P(E,T) that avoids spurious structures like negative temperatures and negative heat capacities while retaining more features than the overly smooth canonical curve.

## Method

The authors derive the traditional microcanonical caloric curve from the condition ∂A(E,T)/∂E = 0 (equivalent to ∂S/∂E = 1/T) and the canonical caloric curve from ∂A(E,T)/∂T = 0 (equivalent to E = T² ∂lnZ/∂T). They then propose a new caloric curve defined by the differential equation A_T dE = A_E dT, which describes curves that cross the contour lines of constant probability P(E,T) perpendicularly, with the additional condition that the new curve always lies between the two traditional caloric curves. The new curve is obtained by solving this differential equation starting from the saddlepoint of the probability distribution and following the correct asymptotic behavior.

## Key numbers

- For a multiplicity Ω(E) ∝ Eⁿ with n=1, the microcanonical caloric curve gives Ê = nT while the canonical gives T̂ = E/(n+1), showing the two curves do not coincide.
- For a Fermi-gas level density ρ ∝ exp(2√(aE)), the microcanonical caloric curve is Ê = aT², while the canonical expression includes additional terms: E = aT̂² + T̂[1 + 1/(2+1/x(T̂))] where x(T) = √(aT) e^(aT) ∫_{-∞}^{√(aT)} e^{-τ²} dτ.
- For large T̂, the canonical Fermi-gas expression simplifies to E = aT̂² + (3/2)T̂ + O(T̂⁻¹); for small T̂, E = β̂⁻¹ + (√(πa)/2)β̂⁻³/² + a(2-π/2)β̂⁻² + O(β̂⁻⁵/²) where β̂ = 1/T̂.
- Experimental nuclear level densities for ⁵⁷Fe show a local decrease below 2 MeV excitation energy, creating a negative branch in the microcanonical caloric curve.
- Experimental data on nuclear level densities are binned into approximately 120 keV broad excitation energy intervals.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of caloric curves in small systems previously examined by Melby et al. (1999) for atomic nuclei, and connects to studies of convex intruders in the microcanonical entropy observed in atomic clusters by Schmidt et al. (2001), nuclear fragmentation by Gross (1997), and star clusters by Thirring (1970). The new caloric curve construction can be applied to study the breaking of nucleon Cooper pairs where complete level-density information is available, and could also be used in analysis of multifragmentation data or searches for the transition to the quark-gluon plasma.
