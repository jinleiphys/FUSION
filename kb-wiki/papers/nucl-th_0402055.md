# Description of the Low-Energy Doublet Neutron-Deuteron Scattering on the Basis of the Triton Bound and Virtual State Parameters

> Low-energy doublet neutron-deuteron scattering is described on the basis of the triton bound and virtual state parameters - the energies and the nuclear vertex constants of these states. The van Oers-Seagrave formula is derived from the Bargmann representation of the S matrix for a system having two states. The presence of a pole in this formula is shown to be a direct corollary of the existence of a low-energy triton virtual state. Simple explicit expressions for the nd scattering length and for the pole of the function $k\cot δ$ are obtained in terms of the triton bound and virtual state parameters. Numerical calculations of the nd low-energy scattering parameters show their high sensitivity to variations in the asymptotic normalization constant of the virtual state $C_{v}^{2}$. The $C_{v}^{2}$ value fitted in our model to the experimental result for the nd scattering length is $C_{v}^{2}=0.0592$.

## Key claim

The van Oers-Seagrave formula for low-energy doublet neutron-deuteron scattering, previously known only empirically, is derived theoretically from the Bargmann representation of the S matrix for a system having two states (the triton bound state and the triton virtual state), and the pole in the formula is shown to be a direct consequence of the existence of the low-energy triton virtual state.

## Method

The authors start from the Bargmann representation of the Jost function as a rational function, first for a system with one state (neutron-proton scattering) and then generalize to a system with two states (neutron-deuteron scattering in the doublet spin state). They derive explicit expressions for the S matrix, the function k cot δ, and the van Oers-Seagrave parameters in terms of the S-matrix parameters α, β, λ, μ, and then express these in terms of the physical parameters of the triton bound and virtual states (their energies and nuclear vertex constants). Numerical calculations of the low-energy nd scattering parameters are performed using fixed values for the triton binding energy, virtual state energy, and bound-state asymptotic normalization constant, while varying the virtual-state asymptotic normalization constant C_v^2.

## Key numbers

- The experimental nd scattering length is a^{expt} = 0.65 fm (from Dilg, Koester, and Nistler, 1971).
- The value of the virtual-state asymptotic normalization constant that fits this experimental scattering length in the model is C_v^2 = 0.0592.
- The triton binding energy used in calculations is E_T = 8.48 MeV (from Tilley, Weller, and Hasan, 1987).
- The virtual state energy used is B_v = |E_v| = 0.482 MeV (from Girard and Fuda, 1979).
- The bound-state asymptotic normalization constant used is C_T^2 = 3.5 (from Petrov, 1988; Orlov, Petrov, and Teneva, 1992; Simenog, Sitnichenko, and Shapoval, 1987).
- With C_v^2 = 0.0592, the calculated pole of k cot δ is ε_0 = -0.1313 MeV, and the amplitude slope parameter is (1/2) α_d^3 a^2 r_e = 1.52.
- The van Oers-Seagrave parameters calculated with C_v^2 = 0.0592 are: A = 0.3198 fm^{-1}, B = 0.7698 fm, C = 1.2190 fm^{-1}, D = 236.927 fm^2.
- For comparison, the currently accepted experimental value C_v^2 = 0.0504 (from Girard and Fuda, 1979) gives a calculated nd scattering length a = 1.03 fm, which differs from the experimental value.

## Context

This work extends the two-body model approach of Petrov (1988) and Orlov, Petrov, and Teneva (1992), which used the Hulthén potential to study three-body systems and revealed the importance of the virtual triton state. It provides a theoretical foundation for the empirical van Oers-Seagrave formula from van Oers and Seagrave (1967) and relates its parameters to fundamental nuclear vertex constants. The results enable future experimental refinement of the virtual-state asymptotic normalization constant C_v^2, which the authors show is crucial for accurate description of low-energy nd scattering, and connect the formalism to Faddeev equation calculations such as those of Simenog, Sitnichenko, and Shapoval (1987).
