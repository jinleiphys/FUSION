# Dynamical aspects of fragmentation

> In this short communication we address the problem of reducibility in a highly excited Lennard-Jones system. We show that the probability of emitting $n$ fragments can be described in terms of a single probability through the binomial expression. However, the Arrhenius law does not hold and the process can be viewed as a mixture of sequential and simultaneous fragmentation events.

# Dynamical aspects of fragmentation

## Key claim

The probability of emitting n fragments from a highly excited Lennard-Jones system can be described by a binomial distribution, but the Arrhenius law does not hold because the temperature of the emitting source is approximately linear with energy only at low energies, meaning the process is a mixture of sequential and simultaneous fragmentation events rather than purely sequential thermal binary decays.

## Method

Molecular dynamics simulations were performed for a system of N=147 particles interacting via a truncated Lennard-Jones potential, initialized as hot dense drops without artificial constraining volumes (particles evolved freely into vacuum). Fragments were defined as quasi-stable clusters of at least four particles that remained together for at least 5t₀ in natural Lennard-Jones units, detected in configuration space.

## Key numbers

- System size: N=147 particles interacting via a truncated Lennard-Jones potential.
- Fragment definition: quasi-stable clusters of at least four particles, stable for at least 5t₀ (in natural Lennard-Jones units).
- Energy range explored: from E = -2.0 ε (U-shaped mass spectra) to E = 2.0 ε (exponentially decaying mass distribution).
- Binomial fits were performed for energies E = -2.0 ε, -0.2 ε, 0.0 ε, 0.2 ε, 1.0 ε, and 2.0 ε.

## Context

This work extends the debate on sequentiality versus simultaneity in multifragmentation, originally framed by Moretto and coworkers (Moretto, year unspecified) who proposed binomial distributions for fragment emission as evidence of sequential thermal binary events, and challenged by Gross (Gross, year unspecified) who argued for simultaneous breakup. The authors' previous molecular dynamics studies (Ison and Dorso, year unspecified) provided the framework for the present analysis. The results suggest that the Arrhenius law holds only when evaporation is the principal decay mode, and that the fragmentation process combines both sequential and simultaneous events, which may inform future studies of nonequilibrium thermodynamics in nuclear fragmentation.
