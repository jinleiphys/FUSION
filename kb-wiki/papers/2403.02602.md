# Macroscopic neutrinoless double beta decay: long range quantum coherence

> We re-introduce, in light of our modern understanding of neutrinos, the concept of ``macroscopic neutrinoless double beta decay" (MDBD) for Majorana neutrinos. In this process an antineutrino produced by a nucleus undergoing beta decay, $X\to Y + e^- + \bar ν_e$, is absorbed as a neutrino by another identical $X$ nucleus via the inverse beta decay reaction, $ν_e + X \to e^-+Y$. The distinct signature of MDBD is that the total kinetic energy of the two electrons equals twice the endpoint energy of single beta decay. The amplitude for MDBD, a coherent sum over the contribution of different mass states of the intermediate neutrinos, reflects quantum coherence over macroscopic distances, and is a new macroscopic quantum effect. We evaluate the rate of MDBD for a macroscopic sample of ``$X$" material, e.g., tritium, acting both as the source and the target. The accidental background for MDBD originating from two separate single beta decays, which contains two final state neutrinos, can be readily rejected by measuring the energy of the detected two electrons. We discuss the similarities and differences between the MDBD and conventional neutrinoless double beta decay.

## Key claim

Macroscopic neutrinoless double beta decay (MDBD) is a process in which a Majorana antineutrino emitted from one nucleus undergoing beta decay is absorbed as a neutrino by a second identical nucleus via inverse beta decay, with the total kinetic energy of the two emitted electrons equaling twice the endpoint energy of single beta decay. The authors explicitly state that MDBD is "clearly not a viable replacement for traditional 0νDBD experiments" and that realistic experiments are "not feasible within today's technology," but the concept offers new perspectives on neutrinoless weak interactions.

## Method

The authors calculate the MDBD rate by combining the amplitude for single beta decay (XBD) with the amplitude for inverse beta decay (IXBD) for a Majorana neutrino, summing coherently over neutrino mass states and helicities. They evaluate the total rate for a macroscopic sample of identical nuclei acting as both source and target, deriving the dependence on sample geometry and showing the rate scales as the 4/3 power of the number of nuclei. The calculation uses the known matrix elements for beta and inverse beta decay, with the helicity factors for massive neutrinos providing the necessary chirality flip amplitude proportional to mν/Eν.

## Key numbers

- For a 100 g spherical tritium sample, the MDBD rate is approximately 2.32 × 10⁻⁵ (m̄/1 eV)² per year, where m̄ is the effective Majorana neutrino mass
- For tritium with m̄ = 0.1 eV, the expected yield is 2.3 × 10⁻⁷ events per 100 g-yr exposure
- For a 100 g spherical neutron sample (same volume as tritium), the MDBD rate is a factor of approximately 10⁵ larger than for tritium
- For ¹¹C (β⁺ decay, Q = 1.98 MeV, half-life 22.3 min), the MDBD rate is 5.14 × 10⁻⁵ per year for m̄ = 0.1 eV
- The dimensionless integral J entering the MDBD rate approaches π/4 in the limit ΔM - mₑ ≪ mₑ (as in tritium), and 8/15 in the limit ΔM ≫ mₑ
- The characteristic neutrino oscillation length is L_osc ∼ Eν(MeV) km for Δm₃₁² ≈ 2.5 × 10⁻³ eV²
- The decoherence length is L_dec ∼ 10⁴ Eν(MeV)/Δmν²(eV²) meters

## Context

This paper re-introduces and analyzes in detail the concept of macroscopic neutrinoless double beta decay, originally recognized by Pacheco (year not specified) and followed up by Kohyama (year not specified) and Skalsey (year not specified). The work extends the standard picture of neutrinoless double beta decay within a single nucleus to a process where the two weak interactions occur in separate nuclei separated by macroscopic distances. The analysis enables new perspectives on quantum coherence in neutrinoless weak interactions over macroscopic scales, though the authors note that MDBD rates are four to five orders of magnitude lower than conventional 0νDBD rates for typical candidate nuclei such as ⁷⁶Ge (Q = 2.04 MeV) and ¹³⁶Xe (Q = 2.46 MeV) as studied by GERDA, Majorana, KamLAND-Zen, and EXO-200.
