# Quantum decoherence from complex saddle points

> Quantum decoherence is the effect that bridges quantum physics to well-understood classical physics. As such, it plays a crucial role in understanding the mysterious nature of quantum physics. Quantum decoherence is also a source of quantum noise that has to be well under control in quantum computing and in various experiments based on quantum technologies. Here we point out that quantum decoherence can be captured by $\textit{complex}$ saddle points in the Feynman path integral in much the same way as quantum tunneling can be captured by instantons. In particular, we present some first-principle calculations in the Caldeira-Leggett model, which reproduce the predicted scaling behavior of quantum decoherence with respect to the parameters of the environment, such as the temperature and the coupling to the system of interest. We also discuss how to extend our approach to general models by Monte Carlo calculations using a recently developed method to overcome the sign problem.

## Key claim

Quantum decoherence can be captured by complex saddle points in the real-time Feynman path integral, analogous to how quantum tunneling is captured by instantons in imaginary-time path integrals; the authors present first-principle calculations in the Caldeira-Leggett model that reproduce the predicted scaling behavior of decoherence with respect to temperature and coupling, though the overall factor is larger than the predicted value by 40%, which the authors note deserves further investigation.

## Method

The authors discretize the Caldeira-Leggett model with a system harmonic oscillator coupled to an environment of \(N_\mathcal{E}\) harmonic oscillators with an Ohmic spectral density, using a frequency distribution \(\omega_k = \omega_\mathrm{cut} (k/N_\mathcal{E})^{1/3}\). They evaluate the reduced density matrix of the system via the Feynman path integral, which reduces to a multi-variable Gaussian integral whose saddle point is obtained by solving a linear equation with a sparse complex-valued coefficient matrix. The initial state of the system is a Gaussian wave packet, and the environment is initialized in a canonical ensemble at temperature \(T = \beta^{-1}\), implemented via an additional imaginary-time path integral.

## Key numbers

* The coupling constant \(c\) is related to the effective coupling \(\gamma\) in the Caldeira-Leggett master equation by \(c^2 = \frac{4\gamma}{\pi} \omega_\mathrm{cut}^3 \left\{ \sum_{k=1}^{N_\mathcal{E}} (N_\mathcal{E}/k)^{2/3} \right\}^{-1}\).
* The overall factor of the scaling behavior of quantum decoherence with respect to temperature and coupling is larger than the predicted value from the master equation by 40%.
* The number of integration variables in the path integral is \(D = 2N_t (1 + N_\mathcal{E}) + N_\beta N_\mathcal{E}\), where \(N_t\) is the number of real-time steps and \(N_\beta\) is the number of imaginary-time steps.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Nishimura (2023) on complex saddle points for quantum tunneling in real-time path integrals to the phenomenon of quantum decoherence. It provides a first-principle alternative to the master equation approach of Lindblad (1975) and Gorini (1975), which relies on assumptions such as the Born and Markov approximations and high temperature. The results enable first-principle calculations of decoherence in general models via Monte Carlo methods using a recently developed technique to overcome the sign problem, without relying on the approximations required for master equations.
