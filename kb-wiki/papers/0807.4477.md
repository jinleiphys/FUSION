# Locating the inner edge of neutron star crust using terrestrial nuclear laboratory data

> Within both dynamical and thermodynamical approaches using the equation of state for neutron-rich nuclear matter constrained by the recent isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion reactions in the same sub-saturation density range as the neutron star crust, the density and pressure at the inner edge separating the liquid core from the solid crust of neutron stars are determined to be 0.040 fm$^{-3}$ $\leq ρ_{t}\leq 0.065$ fm$^{-3}$ and 0.01 MeV/fm$^{3}$ $\leq P_{t}\leq 0.26$ MeV/fm$^{3}$, respectively. These together with the observed minimum crustal fraction of the total moment of inertia allow us to set a new limit for the radius of the Vela pulsar significantly different from the previous estimate. It is further shown that the widely used parabolic approximation to the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter leads systematically to significantly higher core-crust transition densities and pressures, especially with stiffer symmetry energy functionals.

## Key claim

Using the MDI equation of state constrained by isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion reactions, the density and pressure at the inner edge of neutron star crust are determined to be 0.040 fm$^{-3}$ $\leq \rho_{t}\leq 0.065$ fm$^{-3}$ and 0.01 MeV/fm$^{3}$ $\leq P_{t}\leq 0.26$ MeV/fm$^{3}$, respectively, but these ranges carry a roughly 30% systematic error inherited from the uncertainty in the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections used in the transport model analysis.

## Method

The authors applied both the dynamical method (which includes density gradient terms and Coulomb interaction) and the thermodynamical method (which ignores finite-size effects) to locate the density at which homogeneous $npe$ matter becomes unstable against small density fluctuations. They used the MDI (Momentum Dependent Interaction) equation of state, with the parameter $x$ varied to change the density dependence of the symmetry energy while keeping the saturation properties fixed. The slope parameter $L$ of the symmetry energy was constrained to $L=86\pm 25$ MeV from the analysis of isospin diffusion data in heavy-ion collisions, and this constraint was then mapped onto the transition density and pressure.

## Key numbers

- Transition density range: $0.040$ fm$^{-3}$ $\leq \rho_{t}\leq 0.065$ fm$^{-3}$, determined from the MDI EOS with the experimentally constrained slope parameter $L=86\pm 25$ MeV.
- Transition pressure range: $0.01$ MeV/fm$^{3}$ $\leq P_{t}\leq 0.26$ MeV/fm$^{3}$, from the same constraint.
- For the MDI interaction with $x=0$, the dynamical method gives $\rho_{t}=0.065$ fm$^{-3}$ and the thermodynamical method gives $\rho_{t}=0.073$ fm$^{-3}$ using the full EOS; using the parabolic approximation, these become $0.080$ fm$^{-3}$ and $0.090$ fm$^{-3}$, respectively.
- The slope parameter of the symmetry energy at saturation density is $L=86\pm 25$ MeV, with the error being systematic in nature.
- Using the upper bounds on $\rho_{t}$ and $P_{t}$, the minimum radius constraint for the Vela pulsar is $R\geq 4.7+4.0M/M_{\odot}$ km, giving $R\geq 10.5$ km for a $1.4 M_{\odot}$ neutron star.
- Using the full MDI EOS with $x=0$ and $x=-1$, the radius of a canonical $1.4 M_{\odot}$ neutron star is constrained between $11.9$ km and $13.2$ km.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Kubis (2007) and earlier studies by Lattimer and Prakash (2000, 2007) by using the MDI equation of state that has been directly constrained by isospin diffusion data from heavy-ion collisions (Tsang et al., 2004; Chen et al., 2005; Li et al., 2005) in the same sub-saturation density range as the neutron star crust. The results provide a new, more stringent constraint on the mass-radius relation of the Vela pulsar compared to the earlier estimate of Link et al. (1999), which used $\rho_{t}=0.075$ fm$^{-3}$ and $P_{t}=0.65$ MeV/fm$^{3}$. The finding that the parabolic approximation to the equation of state systematically overestimates transition densities and pressures, especially for stiffer symmetry energies, indicates that future work should use the full equation of state rather than the commonly used parabolic form.
