# Density-dependent nuclear interactions and the beta decay of 14C: chiral three-nucleon forces and Brown-Rho scaling

> We study the role of density-dependent low-momentum nucleon-nucleon interactions in describing the anomalously long beta decay lifetime of 14C. We approach this problem both from the perspective of chiral effective field theory, in which genuine three-body forces generate an effective density-dependent two-body interaction, as well as from the perspective of Brown-Rho scaling, in which the masses and form factor cutoffs in one-boson-exchange interactions are modified in a dense nuclear medium due to the partial restoration of chiral symmetry. The beta decay transition of 14C to the ground state of 14N is calculated within the shell model using a model space consisting of two 0p-shell holes within a closed 16O core. The effective 0p-shell interaction is calculated up to second order in perturbation theory with single-particle energies extracted from experiment. We find that both three-nucleon forces and Brown-Rho scaling medium modifications give qualitatively similar results not only for the ground state to ground state Gamow-Teller transition but also for Gamow-Teller transitions from excited states of 14C to the ground state of 14N. In this way, it is observed that at a low-momentum scale of V_(low-k) = 2.1 fm-1, medium-modifications of the nuclear force play an essential role in increasing the lifetime of 14C from a few minutes to an archaeologically long one of 5730 years.

## Key claim

Both chiral three-nucleon forces and Brown-Rho scaling medium modifications to the nucleon-nucleon interaction produce qualitatively similar suppression of the Gamow-Teller transition matrix element for the beta decay of 14C, with the effect arising primarily from increased short-distance repulsion in the nuclear medium rather than from changes to the tensor force specifically.

## Method

The authors performed shell-model calculations for the beta decay transition of 14C to the ground state of 14N using a model space of two 0p-shell holes within a closed 16O core. They constructed effective density-dependent nucleon-nucleon interactions by two independent methods: (1) deriving an in-medium two-body force from the leading-order chiral three-nucleon interaction by summing over the filled Fermi sea of nucleons, and (2) implementing Brown-Rho scaling by modifying meson masses and form factor cutoffs in one-boson-exchange interactions according to the density-dependent scaling relation. The effective 0p-shell interaction was calculated up to second order in perturbation theory with single-particle energies extracted from experiment.

## Key numbers

- The Gamow-Teller transition matrix element for 14C beta decay is anomalously small at M_GT approximately 0.002, resulting from cancellation between terms otherwise on the order of 1.
- At a low-momentum cutoff of Lambda_low-k = 2.1 fm^-1, medium modifications increase the 14C lifetime from a few minutes to 5730 years.
- The pion-nucleon sigma term sigma_piN is approximately 45 MeV, leading to a scaling factor Phi(n_0) approximately 0.83 at nuclear matter saturation density n_0 = 0.16 fm^-3.
- The low-energy constants for the chiral three-nucleon force at Lambda_low-k = 2.1 fm^-1 are c_D = -2.062 and c_E = -0.625, fitted to the binding energies of 3H and 4He.
- The medium-range one-pion exchange three-body force V_NN^(med,4) reduces bare one-pion exchange by roughly 16% at normal nuclear matter density.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Holt et al. (2008, 2009) that first demonstrated the sensitivity of the 14C beta decay lifetime to density-dependent nuclear interactions, and connects to early work by Inglis and Talmi highlighting the role of the tensor force. The paper provides a unified comparison between two approaches: chiral effective field theory three-nucleon forces as developed by Epelbaum (2006) and Nogga et al. (2006), and the Brown-Rho scaling hypothesis originally proposed by Brown and Rho (1991, 1996). The results enable future studies of other nuclear structure phenomena sensitive to in-medium modifications of the nuclear force, particularly in systems with high local densities.
