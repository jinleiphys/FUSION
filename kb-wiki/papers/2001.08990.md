# Role of interaction on electrical conductivity of QGP in presence of magnetic field

> Effect of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) interaction in quark-gluon plasma on electrical conductivity is studied, where lattice quantum chromodynamics (LQCD) results are mapped through quark and gluon degeneracy.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the suppression of thermodynamic quantities in lattice QCD at finite temperature and magnetic field can be mapped onto a temperature- and magnetic-field-dependent quark-gluon degeneracy factor \(g(T,B)\), and that using this effective interaction further suppresses the electrical conductivity of the quark-gluon plasma beyond the non-interacting case.

## Method

The authors extract a parametric form for the effective degeneracy factor \(g(T)\) by fitting lattice QCD data for pressure, energy density, and entropy density at five magnetic field strengths (\(eB = 0, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.4\) GeV\(^2\)). They then insert this \(g(T,B)\) into the standard relativistic expressions for the normal (\(\sigma_0\)) and Hall (\(\sigma_1\)) components of electrical conductivity, which depend on the relaxation time \(\tau_c\) and the magnetic time scale \(\tau_B = \omega/(eB)\), and evaluate the integrals for up, down, and strange quarks.

## Key numbers

* The degeneracy factor \(g(T)\) is parameterized as \(g(T)= a_0 - a_1/(e^{a_2(T-0.17)}+ a_3)\); for \(eB=0\) the fit parameters are \(a_0 = 0.834334\), \(a_1 = 0.122845\), \(a_2 = 3.87082\), \(a_3 = -0.620074\).
* For \(eB=0.4\) GeV\(^2\) the fit parameters are \(a_0 = 0.864099\), \(a_1 = 0.0778032\), \(a_2 = 2.03964\), \(a_3 = -0.77928\).
* The quark masses used are \(m_u = m_d = 0.005\) GeV and \(m_s = 0.100\) GeV.
* The normal component \(\sigma_0\) is proportional to \(1/(1+(\tau_c/\tau_B)^2)\) and the Hall component \(\sigma_1\) is proportional to \((\tau_c/\tau_B)/(1+(\tau_c/\tau_B)^2)\).

## Context

This work extends the quasi-particle mapping of lattice QCD thermodynamics at zero magnetic field (LQCD_QGP, 2015) to finite magnetic fields by using the lattice data of Bali et al. (2012). It provides a temperature- and field-dependent degeneracy factor that can be used to compute transport coefficients, specifically electrical conductivity, which is needed to model the time evolution of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions as studied by Tuchin (2013). The results suggest that the normal component of conductivity increases as the magnetic field decreases, which may slow the decay of the external magnetic field in RHIC and LHC experiments.
