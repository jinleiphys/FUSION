# Nucleon Strangeness in Dispersion Theory

> Dispersion relations provide a powerful tool to describe the low-energy structure of hadrons. We review the status of the strange vector form factors of the nucleon in dispersion theory. We also comment on open questions and the relation to chiral perturbation theory.

# Nucleon Strangeness in Dispersion Theory

## Key claim
Dispersion theory calculations of the nucleon's strange vector form factors, when including the full $K\bar{K}$ continuum with rescattering and resonance contributions, produce results that are consistent with chiral perturbation theory at next-to-leading order, resolving an apparent discrepancy that existed at leading order. However, the systematic error from using the vector meson pole approximation for other low-mass intermediate states remains difficult to quantify.

## Method
The author reviews dispersion relation calculations of the strange electric and magnetic form factors $G_E^s$ and $G_M^s$, where the spectral functions are constructed from unitarity and analyticity. The $K\bar{K}$ continuum contribution is obtained by analytically continuing experimental $KN$ scattering amplitudes into the unphysical $t$-channel region using backward dispersion relations, combined with the kaon strangeness form factor from flavor rotation of electromagnetic kaon form factors. The remaining low-mass contributions ($\omega$, $\phi$, $S'$ poles) are treated in a vector meson dominance approximation, with the $S'$ coupling to strangeness treated as an unknown.

## Key numbers
- Strange magnetic moment $\mu^s = -0.24 \pm 0.03$ from the updated pole analysis (Hammer et al., 1996), with error including only the uncertainty in the $\phi$-$\omega$ mixing parameter
- Dirac radius $\langle r^2 \rangle_D^s = (0.21 \pm 0.03) \text{ fm}^2$ from the same pole analysis
- Low-mass value for the strange magnetic moment $\mu^s = -0.28$, with a reasonable range of $-0.15$ to $-0.41$ when including the $S'$ contribution with unknown coupling sign
- Low-mass value for the Dirac radius $\langle r^2 \rangle_D^s = 0.42 \text{ fm}^2$, with reasonable range $(0.41\ldots 0.43) \text{ fm}^2$
- Low-mass value for the magnetic radius $\langle r^2 \rangle_M^s = 0.34 \text{ fm}^2$, with reasonable range $(0.32\ldots 0.36) \text{ fm}^2$
- The SAMPLE collaboration measurement of $G_M^s$ at $Q^2 \approx 0.1 \text{ GeV}^2$ provides the first experimental data point
- The HAPPEX collaboration measurement of $G_E^s + 0.39 G_M^s$ at $t = -0.48 \text{ GeV}^2$ disagrees with the pole model extrapolation
- The leading-order chiral perturbation theory prediction for the magnetic radius $\langle r^2 \rangle_M^s = (-0.16 \ldots -0.60) \text{ fm}^2$ (Hemmert et al., 1998) is not compatible with the dispersion result
- At next-to-leading order, the magnetic radius becomes $\langle r^2 \rangle_M^s = -[0.04 + 0.3\, b_s^r(\mu=1.0 \text{ GeV}^2)] \text{ fm}^2$, and matching to the dispersion result gives $b_s^r \approx -1.1$

## Context
This review extends the dispersion theory approach to nucleon strangeness form factors originally developed by Jaffe (1989), who used a three-pole vector meson dominance model based on the electromagnetic form factor analysis of Hoehler et al. (1976). The work updates that analysis with newer electromagnetic data from Mergell et al. (1996) and incorporates the full $K\bar{K}$ continuum contribution, which was shown by Hammer et al. (1999) to account for most of the $\phi$ pole strength. The results enable improved extraction of the strange magnetic moment from parity-violating electron scattering experiments (SAMPLE, HAPPEX, G0, A4) and resolve an apparent tension with chiral perturbation theory at leading order (Hemmert et al., 1998) by showing consistency at next-to-leading order (Hammer, 2002). Open questions include the treatment of other low-mass intermediate states ($3\pi$, $K\bar{K}\pi$, $K\bar{K}\pi\pi$) and the systematic uncertainty from the vector meson pole approximation.
