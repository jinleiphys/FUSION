# Three-body calculations of triple-alpha reaction

> Recently, the triple-α(3α) process, by which three 4He nuclei are fused into a 12C nucleus in stars, was studied by different methods in solving the quantum mechanical three-body problem. Their results of the thermonuclear reaction rate for the process differ by several orders at low stellar temperatures of 10^7 - 10^8 K. In this paper, we will present calculations of the 3αprocess by a modified Faddeev three-body formalism in which the long-range effects of Coulomb interactions are accommodated. The reaction rate of the process is calculated via an inverse process, three-alpha (3-α) photodisintegration of a 12C nucleus. Calculated reaction rate is about 10 times larger than that of the Nuclear Astrophysics Compilation of Reaction Rates (NACRE) at 10^7 K, and is remarkably smaller than the results of the recent three-body calculations. We will discuss a possible reason of the difference.

# Three-body calculations of triple-alpha reaction

## Key claim

The triple-alpha reaction rate calculated using a modified Faddeev three-body formalism that accommodates long-range Coulomb effects is about 10 times larger than the NACRE compilation rate at 10^7 K, but is remarkably smaller than other recent three-body calculations (OKK and HHR rates) which differ by orders of magnitude at low stellar temperatures. The author suggests that differences arise from how each method treats the long-range Coulomb interactions and the three-body continuum states.

## Method

The author solves the three-body Schrödinger equation for the 3-alpha system using a modified Faddeev formalism that includes auxiliary Coulomb potentials to handle long-range effects, then calculates the triple-alpha reaction rate via the inverse process of E2 photodisintegration of the 12C(2_1^+) state. Two different alpha-alpha potentials are used (Ali-Bodmer model A' and model D), with a three-body potential adjusted to reproduce the empirical binding energy of the 12C(2_1^+) state and the Hoyle state resonance energy. The photodisintegration cross sections are normalized to reproduce the empirical gamma decay width of the Hoyle state, and the reaction rate is obtained by numerical integration over photon energies.

## Key numbers

- Calculated reaction rate is about 10 times larger than the NACRE rate at T = 10^7 K (T_7 = 1)
- OKK rate is about 10^26 times larger than the NACRE rate at T_7 = 1
- HHR rate is about 10^18 times larger than the NACRE rate at T_7 = 1
- For AB(A') potential: Hoyle state resonance energy E_r,3alpha = 376.966 keV, total width Gamma_3alpha = 9.1 eV, gamma width Gamma_gamma = 1.8 meV
- For AB(D) potential: Hoyle state resonance energy E_r,3alpha = 377.956 keV, total width Gamma_3alpha = 6.9 eV, gamma width Gamma_gamma = 2.4 meV
- Empirical Hoyle state values: E_r = 379.4 keV, Gamma_3alpha = 8.3 +/- 1.0 eV, Gamma_gamma = 3.7 +/- 0.5 meV
- 12C(2_1^+) binding energy: -2.840 MeV (AB(A')) and -2.830 MeV (AB(D)), compared to empirical -2.8357 MeV
- 8Be(0_1^+) resonance energy: 93.4 keV (AB(A')) and 95.1 keV (AB(D)), compared to empirical 91.8 keV
- 8Be(0_1^+) width: 8.59 eV (AB(A')) and 8.32 eV (AB(D)), compared to empirical 5.57 +/- 0.25 eV
- Cutoff radius R_C = 35 fm used for convergence of the auxiliary potential treatment
- At T_7 > 10, present results agree with NACRE rate within a few percent

## Context

This work extends the author's previous Faddeev calculations (Ishikawa 2012, 2013) and contrasts with the CDCC calculations of Ogata et al. (2009) and the hyperspherical harmonics R-matrix calculations of Nguyen et al. (2011, 2012), which give vastly larger reaction rates at low temperatures. The present rate satisfies constraints from stellar evolution theory derived by Suda et al. (2011): the rate is below 10^-29 cm^6 s^-1 mol^-2 at T ≈ 10^7.8 K, and the temperature-dependence parameter d log10<aaa>/d log10 T is at least 10 at T_7 ≈ 10-12. The author performs additional CDCC calculations to investigate the source of discrepancies, suggesting that differences arise from how each method handles the long-range Coulomb interactions in the three-body breakup continuum.
