# Phase-Space methods for neutrino oscillations: extension to multi-beams

> The Phase-Space approach (PSA), which was originally introduced in [Lacroix et al., Phys. Rev. D 106, 123006 (2022)] to describe neutrino flavor oscillations for interacting neutrinos emitted from stellar objects is extended to describe arbitrary numbers of neutrino beams. The PSA is based on mapping the quantum fluctuations into a statistical treatment by sampling initial conditions followed by independent mean-field evolution. A new method is proposed to perform this sampling that allows treating an arbitrary number of neutrinos in each neutrino beam. We validate the technique successfully and confirm its predictive power on several examples where a reference exact calculation is possible. We show that it can describe many-body effects, such as entanglement and dissipation induced by the interaction between neutrinos. Due to the complexity of the problem, exact solutions can only be calculated for rather limited cases, with a limited number of beams and/or neutrinos in each beam. The PSA approach considerably reduces the numerical cost and provides an efficient technique to accurately simulate arbitrary numbers of beams. Examples of PSA results are given here, including up to 200 beams with time-independent or time-dependent Hamiltonian. We anticipate that this approach will be useful to bridge exact microscopic techniques with more traditional transport theories used in neutrino oscillations. It will also provide important reference calculations for future quantum computer applications where other techniques are not applicable to classical computers.

## Key claim

The Phase-Space Approach (PSA) is extended from two neutrino beams to an arbitrary number of beams, and a new "bi-valued sampling" method is introduced to handle the extreme case of one neutrino per beam, where the previously used Gaussian sampling fails. The authors validate the technique against exact calculations for limited cases and demonstrate that it can describe many-body effects such as entanglement and dissipation while drastically reducing numerical cost.

## Method

The PSA maps quantum fluctuations of the initial state into a statistical ensemble of initial conditions for each beam's total spin (or polarization vector), then evolves each sample independently using mean-field equations of motion. Three sampling methods are employed: Gaussian sampling (original), Husimi sampling (based on the Q-probability distribution), and a new "bi-valued sampling" inspired by quantum measurement theory, which uses a two-valued probability distribution for spin components to correctly reproduce moments for single neutrinos. The method is tested on the two-flavor neutrino beam Hamiltonian with time-independent and time-dependent couplings, comparing PSA results to exact full configuration interaction calculations for small systems and demonstrating scalability to up to 200 beams.

## Key numbers

- PSA results are shown for systems with up to **200 beams** with time-independent or time-dependent Hamiltonian.
- For two beams, the previous work (Lacroix et al., 2022) considered at most **50 neutrinos per beam**; the present work extends to arbitrary numbers.
- The coupling strength is given by \( G_{\alpha\beta}(t) = \frac{\mu}{N} [1 - \cos(\theta_{\alpha\beta}(t))] \), where \(\mu = \sqrt{2} G_F \rho_\nu\) and \(N = \sum_\alpha N_\alpha\) is the total number of neutrinos.
- For a single neutrino per beam, the bi-valued sampling uses random variables \((x^{(\lambda)}_\alpha, y^{(\lambda)}_\alpha)\) taking values \((1, -1)\) each with probability \(1/2\).
- The Gaussian sampling for a coherent state uses variances \(\sigma^2_{\mathcal{J}^\alpha_x} = \sigma^2_{\mathcal{J}^\alpha_y} = N_\alpha^2 / 4\) and \(\sigma^2_{\mathcal{J}^\alpha_z} = 0\).

## Context

This work extends the Phase-Space Approach originally introduced by Lacroix et al. (2022) for two neutrino beams to arbitrary numbers of beams, building on the many-body treatment of neutrino oscillations pioneered by Pehlivan et al. (2011) and subsequent exact full configuration interaction studies (Marino et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2022; Illa et al., 2022; Roggero et al., 2022). The PSA provides a computationally efficient alternative to exact methods (which become prohibitive beyond a few beams) and to other approximate techniques such as Bethe ansatz (Pehlivan et al., 2011; Birol et al., 2018; Patwardhan et al., 2019) or tensor networks (Roggero et al., 2021; Cervia et al., 2022). The authors anticipate that this approach will bridge exact microscopic techniques with traditional transport theories and provide reference calculations for future quantum computer applications.
