# Dynamical dark energy and variation of fundamental "constants"

> In this thesis we study the influence of a possible variation of fundamental "constants" on the process of Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN). Our findings are combined with further studies on variations of constants in other physical processes to constrain models of grand unification (GUT) and quintessence. We will find that the 7Li problem of BBN can be ameliorated if one allows for varying constants, where especially varying light quark masses show a strong influence. Furthermore, we show that recent studies of varying constants are in contradiction with each other and BBN in the framework of six exemplary GUT scenarios, if one assumes monotonic variation with time. We conclude that there is strong tension between recent claims of varying constants, hence either some claims have to be revised, or there are much more sophisticated GUT relations (and/or non-monotonic variations) realized in nature. The methods introduced in this thesis prove to be powerful tools to probe regimes well beyond the Standard Model of particle physics or the concordance model of cosmology, which are currently inaccessible by experiments. Once the first irrefutable proofs of varying constants are available, our method will allow for probing the consistency of models beyond the standard theories like GUT or quintessence and also the compatibility between these models.

## Key claim

This thesis investigates whether allowing fundamental "constants" (especially light quark masses) to vary over cosmic time can resolve the "7Li problem" in Big Bang Nucleosynthesis, finding that such variations can ameliorate the discrepancy but that different observational claims of varying constants are in strong tension with each other and with BBN under six exemplary Grand Unified Theory (GUT) scenarios if monotonic variation is assumed.

## Method

The author modified the Kawano 1992 BBN code, updating nuclear reaction cross sections from NACRE (1999) and NETGEN, and improved numerical accuracy using an adaptive second-order Runge-Kutta method. The code tracks the evolution of photon and neutrino temperatures, electron chemical potential, baryon density, and elemental abundances by integrating coupled differential equations for 11 main nuclear reactions in an expanding, radiation-dominated Friedmann-Robertson-Walker universe, with the QCD scale kept constant as the unit. The dependence of final abundances on variations in reaction cross sections and binding energies was computed by linearly perturbing input parameters and observing the resulting changes in output abundances.

## Key numbers

- Primordial helium-4 abundance (mass fraction Y_P): observed value 0.249 ± 0.009, with conservative allowable range 0.232 ≤ Y_P ≤ 0.258 (Olive and Skillman 2004)
- Primordial deuterium abundance D/H = (2.8 ± 0.4) × 10⁻⁵ (O'Meara 2006, Kirkman 2003)
- Neutron-proton mass difference q = (m_n - m_p)/m_e = 1.29 MeV
- Deuterium binding energy B_D = 2.2 MeV; deuterium fusion reaction drops out of equilibrium at T ≈ 80 keV
- Temperature-time relation in early Universe: t ∝ (k_B T)⁻²
- Photon-to-neutrino temperature ratio today: T_γ⁰ / T_ν⁰ = (11/4)^(1/3), with T_γ⁰ = 2.73 K and T_ν⁰ = 1.95 K
- Baryon-to-photon ratio η = n_B/n_γ = 2.74 × 10⁻⁸ Ω_baryon h²
- Replacing NETGEN rates with fitted rates changed abundances: D by -0.3%, ³He by +0.9%, ⁴He by less than 0.1%, ⁷Li by +3%
- For the d(α,γ)⁶Li reaction, fitting to Kiener data instead of NETGEN gave a ⁶Li abundance larger by a factor of 3.3

## Context

This work extends the numerical BBN framework of Peebles (1966), Wagoner (1969, 1972), and Kawano (1988, 1992) to study variations in fundamental constants, building on earlier analytical estimates that proved insufficiently accurate. The method enables testing of GUT and quintessence models against BBN constraints, and the author finds that allowing varying constants can ameliorate the 7Li problem (the discrepancy between predicted and observed primordial lithium-7). The results contradict claims of varying constants from other studies (e.g., Webb et al.) when assuming monotonic variation within six exemplary GUT scenarios, suggesting either those claims must be revised or nature realizes more sophisticated GUT relations or non-monotonic variations. The approach provides a tool to probe physics beyond the Standard Model and the concordance cosmology model that is currently inaccessible by direct experiment.
