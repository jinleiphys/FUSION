# Superheavy nuclei with the vector self-coupling of the $ω$-meson in the relativistic mean-field theory

> We have studied properties and shell structure of the superheavy elements from Z=102 to Z=120 within the framework of the RMF theory. The region of study spans nuclides with neutron numbers N=150-190. The Lagrangian model NL-SV1 with the inclusion of the vector self-coupling of the omega-meson has been employed in this work. We have performed RMF + BCS calculations for an axially deformed configuration of nuclei. The ground-state binding energies, single-particle properties and quadrupole deformation of nuclei have been obtained from the mean-field minimizations. Two-neutron separation energies, $Q_α$ values and alpha-decay half-life have been evaluated. It is shown that a large number of nuclides exhibit the phenomenon of shape-coexistence over a significant region of the superheavy elements. Shape coexistence of a prolate and an oblate shape is prevalent in nuclides far below N=184, whilst nuclei in the vicinity of N=184 tend to show a shape coexistence between a spherical and an oblate shape. The shell structure and 2-neutron separation energies obtained with the RMF theory reinforce the neutron number N=184 as a major magic number. It is shown that the neutron number N=172 acts akin to a magic number in the deformed region. It is suggested that the combination Z=120 and N=172 has the potential of being a doubly magic number in the superheavy region.

## Key claim

The RMF theory with the NL-SV1 force, which includes the vector self-coupling of the ω-meson, predicts that the neutron number N=184 acts as a major magic number for superheavy elements from Z=102 to Z=120, while N=172 behaves as a magic number in the deformed region; the combination Z=120 and N=172 is suggested as a potential doubly magic number, though the authors hedge that shell closures in superheavy nuclei are not robust and that the shell strength at N=184 shows signs of softening for heavier elements such as Z=116.

## Method

The authors performed axially deformed relativistic mean-field (RMF) plus BCS calculations using the NL-SV1 Lagrangian, which includes a quartic vector self-coupling term for the ω-meson. They expanded fermionic and bosonic wavefunctions in a basis of 20 harmonic oscillator shells and used the Möller-Nix pairing gap formula (Δ = 4.8 A^{-1/3}) for both neutrons and protons. For each nucleus, initial basis deformations of both prolate and oblate type were taken to search for minima in both regions of deformation space, covering isotopic chains from Z=102 to Z=120 with neutron numbers N=150 to N=190.

## Key numbers

- Quadrupole deformation β₂ for nuclei in the neutron range N=150-170 typically shows a prolate deformation of β₂ ~ 0.2-0.3.
- For isotopic chains of No (Z=102), Rf (Z=104), and Sg (Z=106), a shape transition from prolate to oblate occurs at N ~ 170.
- For Hs (Z=108) the prolate-to-oblate transition occurs at N=178; for Ds (Z=110) the transition point advances to N=180.
- For Z=116 and Z=118, the shape transition occurs at N=174 and N=172, respectively; for Z=120 the transition falls back to N=170.
- The ETF-SI mass formula predicts a nearly constant β₂ ~ 0.4 for all neutron numbers N=150-180 for elements Z=112-120, in contrast to NL-SV1.
- Shape coexistence (two minima within ~1 MeV) is observed for a large number of nuclides, with prolate-oblate coexistence prevalent from N=170 to N=180 and spherical-oblate coexistence near N=184.

## Context

This work extends previous RMF studies of superheavy nuclei (e.g., Lala.96 using force NL-SH, and Kruppa.00 comparing Skyrme and RMF forces) by employing the NL-SV1 Lagrangian with vector self-coupling of the ω-meson, which was shown by Sharma.00 to improve shell gaps in stable nuclei. The results are compared with the macroscopic-microscopic FRDM (Möller.95) and the ETF-SI mass formula (ETFSI). The finding that N=184 remains a major magic number while N=172 acts as a deformed shell closure contrasts with some non-relativistic predictions and with earlier RMF studies that did not show a clear shell gap at N=184 for heavier superheavy elements. The work enables further experimental searches for superheavy nuclei near Z=120 and N=172 or N=184, and tests of the spin-orbit force in the superheavy region.
