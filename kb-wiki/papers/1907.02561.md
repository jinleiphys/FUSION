# Neutron rich matter in heaven and on Earth

> Despite a length-scale difference of 18 orders of magnitude, the internal structure of neutron stars and the spatial distribution of neutrons in atomic nuclei are profoundly connected.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the neutron skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb and the radius of a neutron star are both controlled by the same fundamental quantity, the symmetry pressure \(L\), despite an 18-order-of-magnitude difference in scale. However, the abstract glosses over the fact that the correlation is strongest for low-mass neutron stars (0.8 solar masses, r=0.99) and weakens slightly for 1.4 solar mass stars (r=0.95), and that the PREX measurement of the neutron skin thickness had insufficient precision to distinguish between competing models.

## Method

The authors review the parity-violating electron scattering experiment (PREX) at Jefferson Lab, which measured the parity-violating asymmetry between right- and left-handed longitudinally polarized electrons to infer the neutron distribution in \({}^{208}\)Pb. They then compare predictions from a large set of theoretical nuclear models that reproduce properties of finite nuclei and neutron stars, correlating the symmetry pressure \(L\) with both the neutron skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb and neutron star radii. The analysis also incorporates constraints from the gravitational-wave event GW170817, using limits on the tidal polarizability to disfavor overly large stellar radii.

## Key numbers

- The saturation density of nuclear matter is \(\rho_{0}\approx 0.15\,{\rm fm}^{-3}\).
- The weak charge of the proton is \(Q_{\rm wk}^{p}=1-4\sin^{2}\theta_{\rm W}\approx 0.072\).
- The correlation coefficient between the symmetry pressure \(L\) and the neutron skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb is nearly one (r nearly 1); for the correlation between \(L\) and neutron star radius, r=0.99 for a 0.8 solar mass star and r=0.95 for a 1.4 solar mass star.
- Oppenheimer and Volkoff showed that a neutron star supported only by neutron degeneracy pressure collapses above \(0.7\) solar masses; today at least two neutron stars are known with masses as large as two solar masses.
- The tidal polarizability scales as the fifth power of the compactness (ratio of stellar radius to Schwarzschild radius); the Schwarzschild radius for the Sun is approximately 3 km.
- Photometric determinations of stellar radii previously had uncertainties as large as 5–6 km, now reduced to about a couple of kilometers.
- The PREX experiment reported a fairly large value for \(R_{\rm skin}^{208}\), but with large error bars; the follow-up PREX-II aims to reduce the experimental uncertainty by a factor of three.

## Context

This work extends the correlation first established by Horowitz (2000) between the neutron skin thickness of \({}^{208}\)Pb and neutron star radii, and builds on the PREX measurement by Abrahamyan (2012). It also incorporates constraints from the gravitational-wave event GW170817 reported by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration (2017), which disfavors overly large stellar radii and thus suggests a neutron skin thickness well below the PREX central value (Fattoyev 2017). The paper enables future synergy between the PREX-II and MREX campaigns, the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), and upcoming LIGO-Virgo runs to test whether the symmetry energy is stiff at nuclear densities but soft at higher densities, potentially indicating an exotic phase transition in neutron star interiors.
