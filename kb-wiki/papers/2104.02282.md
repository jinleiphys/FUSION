# Measurement of relative isotopic yield distribution of even-even fission fragments from $^{235}$U($n_{th}$,$f$) following $γ$ ray spectroscopy

> A detailed investigation on the relative isotopic distributions has been carried out for the first time in case of even-even correlated fission fragments for the $^{235}$U($n_{th}$,$f$) fission reaction. High-statistics data were obtained in a prompt $γ$ ray spectroscopy measurement during the EXILL campaign at ILL, Grenoble, France. The extensive off-line analysis of the coincidence data have been carried out using four different coincidence methods. Combining the results from 2-dimensional $γ-γ$ and 3-dimensional $γ-γ-γ$ coincidence analysis, a comprehensive picture of the relative isotopic yield distributions of the even-even neutron-rich fission fragments has emerged. The experimentally observed results have been substantiated by the theoretical calculations based on a novel approach of isospin conservation, and a reasonable agreement has been obtained. The calculations following the semi-empirical GEF model have also been carried out. The results from the GEF model calculations are found to be in fair agreement with the experimental results.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for the first time, relative isotopic yield distributions have been measured for even-even correlated fission fragment pairs from thermal neutron induced fission of $^{235}$U using prompt gamma ray spectroscopy, with the caveat that the results are limited to fragments within the sensitivity limit of the EXILL detector array and that the isospin conservation formalism shows only "reasonable agreement" (not exact) with experimental data.

## Method

The experiment used the EXILL gamma detector array at the Institut Laue-Langevin (ILL) to detect prompt gamma rays from fission fragments produced by a thermal neutron beam (flux approximately 10$^8$ n s$^{-1}$ cm$^{-2}$) impinging on a $^{235}$UO$_2$ target of thickness approximately 600 $\mu$g/cm$^2$. The authors analyzed approximately 2$\times$10$^{10}$ $\gamma$-$\gamma$ and 3.5$\times$10$^{9}$ $\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$ coincidence events using four different coincidence methods (Method-1 through Method-4) to extract relative isotopic yields for five complementary even-even fragment pairs: (Mo-Sn), (Zr-Te), (Sr-Xe), (Kr-Ba), and (Se-Ce). Corrections were applied for conversion electron probability using the BrIcc code, precursors' beta decay contributions, low-spin microsecond isomers, and side-feedings from 2$^{+}_{2}$, 3$^{-}_{1}$, and 4$^{+}_{2}$ levels.

## Key numbers

* Total recorded events: approximately 2$\times$10$^{10}$ $\gamma$-$\gamma$ coincidence events and approximately 3.5$\times$10$^{9}$ $\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$ coincidence events from the $^{235}$U($n_{th}$,$f$) reaction at the ILL PF1B beam line.
* Neutron beam flux: approximately 10$^8$ n s$^{-1}$ cm$^{-2}$ at the target position.
* Target thickness: approximately 600 $\mu$g/cm$^2$ of $^{235}$UO$_2$ with 99.7% enrichment in $^{235}$U.
* Total photo-peak efficiency of the EXILL array: approximately 6%.
* Prompt coincidence time window: 200 ns.
* Mean dispersion parameter from Gaussian fitting of yield distributions: $\sigma_{Z}$ = 1.38.
* Percentage differences in maximum yields for the Mo-Sn complementary pair: approximately 63% for Method-1, 5% for Method-2, and 27% for Method-3.
* Estimated side-feedings from 2$^{+}_{2}$ levels to ground states: 18% for $^{88}$Se, 16% for $^{90}$Kr, 7% for $^{96}$Sr, and 12% for $^{98}$Zr.

## Context

This work extends previous prompt gamma ray spectroscopic yield measurements by Mukhopadhyay et al. (2012), which were limited by a small detector array of only two Compton suppressed clover Ge detectors, and provides the first comprehensive isotopic yield data for even-even correlated fragments from $^{235}$U($n_{th}$,$f$) using a large detector array. The results are compared with previous absolute yield measurements using the Lohengrin mass separator by Lang et al. (1980) and the HIAWATHA recoil mass spectrometer by Strittmatter et al. (1978), showing consistent yield distribution patterns for lighter fragments. The experimental data enable testing of the isospin conservation formalism (ISCF) developed by Jain et al. (2014) and Garg et al. (2017, 2018, 2019) for compound-nucleus based fissioning systems, as well as the semi-empirical GEF model by Schmidt et al. (2016), with both theoretical approaches showing fair to reasonable agreement with the measured yields.
