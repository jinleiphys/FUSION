# Sensitivity of $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition to variation of the fine-structure constant

> Peik and Tamm [Europhys. Lett. 61, 181 (2003)] proposed a nuclear clock based on the isomeric transition between the ground state and the first excited state of thorium-229. This transition was recognized as a potentially sensitive probe of possible temporal variation of the fine-structure constant, $α$. The sensitivity to such a variation can be determined from measurements of the mean-square charge radius and quadrupole moment of the different isomers. However, current measurements of the quadrupole moment are yet to achieve an accuracy high enough to resolve non-zero sensitivity. Here we determine this sensitivity using existing measurements of the change in the mean-square charge radius, coupled with the ansatz of constant nuclear density. The enhancement factor for $α$ variation is $K = -(0.82 \pm 0.25) \times 10^4$. For the current experimental limit, $δα/α\lesssim 10^{-17}$ per year, the corresponding frequency shift is $\sim\!200$ Hz per year. This shift is six orders of magnitude larger than the projected accuracy of the nuclear clock, paving the way for increased accuracy of the determination of $δα$ and interaction strength with low-mass scalar dark matter. We verify that the constant-nuclear-density ansatz is supported by nuclear theory and propose how to verify it experimentally. We also consider a possible effect of the octupole deformation on the sensitivity to $α$ variation, and calculate the effects of $α$ variation in a number of Mössbauer transitions.

# Sensitivity of $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition to variation of the fine-structure constant

## Key claim

The enhancement factor for variation of the fine-structure constant $\alpha$ in the $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition is $K = -(0.82 \pm 0.25) \times 10^4$, obtained by using existing measurements of the change in mean-square charge radius coupled with the ansatz of constant nuclear density, rather than relying on the poorly constrained quadrupole moment measurements that previously gave a result consistent with zero.

## Method

The authors model the $^{229}$Th nucleus as a prolate spheroid and derive the change in Coulomb energy $\Delta E_{\text{C}}$ between the ground and isomeric states from the change in mean-square charge radius $\Delta \langle r^2 \rangle$, using the constant-volume (constant-density) ansatz to relate changes in quadrupole moment $Q_0$ to changes in $\langle r^2 \rangle$. They verify this ansatz against Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov nuclear calculations from Litvinova (2009) using two energy functionals (SkM$^*$ and SIII), and also extend the analysis to a quadrupole-octupole deformation model. The method is further tested by applying it to Mössbauer transitions in seven other nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Enhancement factor $K = -(0.82 \pm 0.25) \times 10^4$ for the $^{229}$Th 8 eV isomeric transition
- Change in Coulomb energy $\Delta E_{\text{C}} = -0.067 \pm 0.019$ MeV for $^{229}$Th
- For the current experimental limit $\delta\alpha/\alpha \lesssim 10^{-17}$ per year, the corresponding frequency shift is $\sim 200$ Hz per year
- The frequency shift is six orders of magnitude larger than the projected accuracy of the nuclear clock ($10^{-19}$)
- Measured change in mean-square charge radius $\Delta \langle r^2 \rangle = 0.0105 \pm 0.0013$ fm$^2$ (from Safronova 2018)
- Ground-state mean-square radius $\langle r^2 \rangle = (5.76~\text{fm})^2$ (from Angeli 2013)
- Predicted $\Delta Q_0/Q_0 = 0.0075 \pm 0.0020$ fm$^2$, within the experimental error of $-0.01 \pm 0.04$ fm$^2$ from Thielking (2018)
- Enhancement factors for Mössbauer transitions range from $|K| \sim 0.28$ to $30$, much smaller than for $^{229}$Th

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Peik and Tamm (2003) for a nuclear clock based on the $^{229}$Th isomeric transition and the recognition by Flambaum (2006) that this transition is a sensitive probe of $\alpha$ variation. Previous work by Thielking (2018) using the general formula with independent quadrupole moment and charge radius measurements gave $\Delta E_{\text{C}} = -0.29 \pm 0.43$ MeV, consistent with zero sensitivity. By using the constant-density ansatz, the authors obtain a nonzero result with smaller uncertainty, resolving the ambiguity. The result enables improved searches for temporal variation of $\alpha$ and for low-mass scalar dark matter, as the projected clock accuracy of $10^{-19}$ combined with the large enhancement factor allows testing $\delta\alpha$ six orders of magnitude beyond current limits. The authors also test the constant-density ansatz against nuclear theory calculations by Litvinova (2009) and against Mössbauer transition data for nuclei including $^{151}$Eu, $^{153}$Eu, $^{155}$Gd, $^{157}$Gd, $^{161}$Dy, $^{181}$Ta, and $^{243}$Am.
