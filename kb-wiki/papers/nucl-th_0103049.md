# Changes in r-process abundances at late times

> We explore changes in abundance patterns that occur late in the r process. As the neutrons available for capture begin to disappear, a quasiequilibrium funnel shifts material into the large peaks at A=130 and A=195, and into the rare-earth "bump" at A=160. A bit later, after the free-neutron abundance has dropped and beta-decay has begun to compete seriously with neutron capture, the peaks can widen. The degree of widening depends largely on neutron-capture rates near closed neutron shells and relatively close to stability. We identify particular nuclei the capture rates of which should be examined experimentally, perhaps at a radioactive beam facility.

## Key claim
The paper argues that late-time changes in r-process abundance patterns, specifically the funneling of material into peaks and subsequent peak widening, depend largely on neutron-capture rates near closed neutron shells and relatively close to stability; the degree of peak widening is sensitive to these rates, but only during the brief period when neutron capture and beta decay compete on equal footing.

## Method
The authors performed simulations of the neutron-capture part of the r process, assuming an exponential decay of temperature and density with a time scale of 2.8 seconds (their "standard simulation"), starting from a post-alpha-process seed distribution. They varied initial temperature, density, and neutron-to-seed ratio, and also used conditions from Frei (2000) for a fast r process. They compared results using four different sets of calculated neutron-capture rates, and selectively modified rates for specific nuclei near closed neutron shells to identify which rates most affect final abundances.

## Key numbers
- The standard simulation used an exponential decay time scale of 2.8 seconds, the same as in Meyer (1994).
- The initial temperature in the standard simulation was fixed at T9 = 1.5, with entropy kept equal to 300.
- For the fast r process using Frei (2000) conditions, temperature and density dropped with a time scale of 50 ms and then leveled off at T9 < 1 and density < 100 g/cm^3.
- The calculated beta-decay lifetimes of the N=126 nuclei in the Z=69-72 range increase from about 0.07 s to 4.2 s.
- For the rare-earth region, the nucleus Z=62, N=102 (164Sm) has estimated yields of about 10^5/sec at a RIA ISOL facility; Z=63, N=102 (165Eu) yields about 10^6/sec; Z=63, N=104 (167Eu) yields about 10^4/sec.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of funneling dynamics first presented in Surman (2000) for the rare-earth element bump, and applies similar ideas to the large peaks at A=130 and A=195, particularly in the context of the fast r process proposed by Frei (2000). The paper identifies specific nuclei near closed neutron shells (Z=69-72 for the A=195 peak, Z=62-63 for the rare-earth bump, and Z=48-51 for the A=130 peak) whose neutron-capture rates should be measured experimentally, potentially at a radioactive beam facility, to better constrain r-process conditions.
