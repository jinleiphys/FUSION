# General Treatment of Vortical, Toroidal, and Compression Modes

> The multipole vortical, toroidal, and compression modes are analyzed. Following the vorticity concept of Ravenhall and Wambach, the vortical operator is derived and related in a simple way to the toroidal and compression operators. The strength functions and velocity fields of the modes are analyzed in $^{208}$Pb within the random-phase-approximation using the Skyrme force SLy6. Both convection and magnetization nuclear currents are taken into account. It is shown that the isoscalar (isovector) vortical and toroidal modes are dominated by the convection (magnetization) nuclear current while the compression mode is fully convective. The relation between the above concept of the vorticity to the hydrodynamical vorticity is briefly discussed.

# General Treatment of Vortical, Toroidal, and Compression Modes

## Key claim

The vortical operator derived following the vorticity concept of Ravenhall and Wambach (1987) is shown to be exactly the sum of the toroidal and compression operators at second order in the long-wavelength expansion, but the presence of the $j_{\lambda\lambda+1}(r)$ current component alone does not guarantee hydrodynamic vorticity, as demonstrated by the compression mode which has zero velocity curl despite containing that component.

## Method

The authors derived the vortical operator from the electric multipole operator by subtracting a subsidiary operator that removes the charge-conservation constraint, following the prescription of Ravenhall and Wambach (1987). They performed self-consistent separable random-phase-approximation (SRPA) calculations for $^{208}$Pb using the Skyrme force SLy6, including both convection and magnetization nuclear currents, to compute strength functions and velocity fields for the isoscalar and isovector dipole vortical, toroidal, and compression modes.

## Key numbers

- The SRPA calculations used a cylindrical coordinate-space grid with mesh size 0.7 fm and a particle-hole basis extending up to approximately 65 MeV
- For E1(T=1) excitations, the energy-weighted sum rule with the isovector effective mass was exhausted by approximately 95%
- The Lorentz smoothing width was set to $\Delta = 1$ MeV to simulate broadening effects beyond RPA
- The isoscalar gyromagnetic factor used was $g^{n,p}_s(T=0) = 0.88\varsigma$ and the isovector factor was $g^{n,p}_s(T=1) = -4.70\varsigma$, with quenching parameter $\varsigma = 0.7$
- Bare proton and neutron $g$-factors were $g^p_s = 5.58\varsigma$ and $g^n_s = -3.82\varsigma$

## Context

This work extends the vorticity concept of Ravenhall and Wambach (1987) by deriving an explicit vortical operator and establishing its formal relation to the toroidal and compression operators, which had been treated separately in prior studies such as Semenko (1981) and Kvasil et al. (2003). The analysis clarifies the difference between the vorticity criterion based on the $j_{\lambda\lambda+1}(r)$ current component and the hydrodynamic vorticity definition, and it enables future studies to properly separate and identify these modes in experimental data, particularly in electron scattering form factors as discussed by Richter et al. (2004).
