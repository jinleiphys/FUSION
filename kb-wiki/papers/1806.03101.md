# Ab initio calculation of nuclear structure corrections in muonic atoms

> The measurement of the Lamb shift in muonic hydrogen and the subsequent emergence of the proton-radius puzzle have motivated an experimental campaign devoted to measuring the Lamb shift in other light muonic atoms, such as muonic deuterium and helium. For these systems it has been shown that two-photon exchange nuclear structure corrections are the largest source of uncertainty and consequently the bottleneck for exploiting the experimental precision to extract the nuclear charge radius. Utilizing techniques and methods developed to study electromagnetic reactions in light nuclei, recent calculations of nuclear structure corrections to the muonic Lamb shift have reached unprecedented precision, reducing the uncertainty with respect to previous estimates by a factor of 5 in certain cases. These results will be useful for shedding light on the nature of the proton-radius puzzle and other open questions pertaining to it. Here, we review and update calculations for muonic deuterium and tritium atoms, and for muonic helium-3 and helium-4 ions. We present a thorough derivation of the formalism and discuss the results in relation to other approaches where available. We also describe how to assess theoretical uncertainties, for which the language of chiral effective field theory furnishes a systematic approach that could be further exploited in the future.

## Key claim

The paper reviews and updates ab initio calculations of nuclear-structure corrections (two-photon exchange, or δ_TPE) to the Lamb shift in light muonic atoms, claiming that these calculations have reduced theoretical uncertainties by up to a factor of 5 compared to previous estimates for muonic helium-3 and helium-4 ions, but noting that the theoretical uncertainties remain much larger than the experimental precision and that reducing them to match experimental accuracy is currently unrealistic.

## Method

The authors compute the two-photon exchange nuclear-structure correction δ_TPE for muonic deuterium, tritium, helium-3, and helium-4 by evaluating sum rules of nuclear electromagnetic response functions using ab initio few-body methods: the hyperspherical harmonics expansion (EIHH) method for A=3 and A=4 systems, and harmonic oscillator basis expansions for A=2. They employ nuclear potentials from both phenomenological models (AV18/UIX) and chiral effective field theory (χEFT) at various orders, and separate the correction into nuclear and nucleon contributions, each further divided into elastic (Zemach) and inelastic (polarizability) parts.

## Key numbers

* For muonic helium-3 and helium-4 ions, the theoretical uncertainty in the nuclear polarizability correction δ^A_pol has been reduced by a factor of 5 compared to previously available estimates.
* For muonic deuterium, the systematic uncertainty from χEFT potentials was found to be about 50% larger than the estimate obtained by comparing the AV18 potential with a single χEFT interaction.
* The Coulomb distortion correction δ_C^(0) is given by an expression proportional to (Zα)^3, where Zα ≈ 0.7% for Z=1 and Zα ≈ 1.5% for Z=2.
* For muonic lithium-6 (μ^6Li^(2+)), the uncertainty in the TPE contribution from the Rinker semi-empirical formula is 27% (1σ).
* The leading non-relativistic polarizability contribution δ^(0)_(D1) is proportional to (Zα)^2 φ^2(0), where φ^2(0) = (m_r Zα)^3/(8π) for the 2S state.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous ab initio calculations of nuclear-structure corrections for muonic atoms, which were motivated by the proton-radius puzzle from the CREMA collaboration's Lamb shift measurements in muonic hydrogen. The paper reviews and updates calculations for muonic deuterium, tritium, helium-3, and helium-4, comparing results from phenomenological potentials (AV18/UIX) and chiral effective field theory potentials (Entem 2003, Navratil 2007). The results are crucial for extracting nuclear charge radii from muonic atom experiments, where δ_TPE is currently the bottleneck limiting the exploitation of experimental precision. The authors note that future work could extend these calculations to muonic lithium and beryllium ions, as well as to hyperfine splitting transitions in muonic atoms.
