# Relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics from kinetic theory with relaxation time approximation

> Starting from Boltzmann equation with relaxation time approximation for the collision term and using Chapman-Enskog like expansion for distribution function close to equilibrium, we derive hydrodynamic evolution equations for the dissipative quantities directly from their definition. Although the form of the equations is identical to those obtained in traditional Israel-Stewart approaches employing Grad's 14-moment approximation and second moment of Boltzmann equation, the coefficients obtained are different. In the case of one-dimensional scaling expansion, we demonstrate that our results are in better agreement with numerical solution of Boltzmann equation as compared to Israel-Stewart results. We also show that including approximate higher-order corrections in viscous evolution significantly improves this agreement, thus justifying the relaxation time approximation for the collision term.

# Relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics from kinetic theory with relaxation time approximation

## Key claim
Using Chapman-Enskog expansion of the Boltzmann equation with relaxation time approximation (RTA) instead of the traditional Israel-Stewart 14-moment approximation and second-moment choice, the derived second-order hydrodynamic equations have the same form but different transport coefficients; these new coefficients yield better agreement with numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation than Israel-Stewart results, particularly when approximate higher-order corrections are included.

## Method
The author starts from the relativistic Boltzmann equation with a relaxation time approximation for the collision term and performs a Chapman-Enskog expansion of the distribution function in powers of space-time gradients. The first-order and second-order evolution equations for the dissipative quantities (bulk viscous pressure, shear stress tensor, and particle diffusion current) are derived directly from their kinetic-theory definitions as momentum integrals of the distribution function, rather than from the second moment of the Boltzmann equation as in the Israel-Stewart approach. The resulting equations are then applied to the one-dimensional boost-invariant Bjorken expansion scenario for a massless Boltzmann gas at vanishing net baryon number density.

## Key numbers
- For a massless Boltzmann gas, the shear transport coefficients from the present work are: beta_pi = 4P/5, tau_pipi = 10/7, delta_pipi = 4/3; while the Israel-Stewart coefficients are: beta_pi^IS = 2P/3, tau_pipi^IS = 2, delta_pipi^IS = 4/3.
- The combined second-order coefficient lambda = (1/3)tau_pipi + delta_pipi equals 38/21 in the present work versus lambda^IS = 2 in the Israel-Stewart approach.
- Initial conditions for the Bjorken evolution: temperature T0 = 500 MeV at proper time tau0 = 0.4 fm/c, corresponding to LHC initial conditions.
- The heuristic higher-order correction coefficient chi = 36/175 is obtained from the free-streaming limit.
- The second-order results show good agreement with BAMPS transport calculations for shear viscosity to entropy density ratio eta/s <= 0.4.

## Context
This work extends the derivation of relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics from kinetic theory by avoiding two key assumptions of the Israel-Stewart approach (Israel 1979): the 14-moment approximation for the distribution function and the use of the second moment of the Boltzmann equation. The method follows the direct-definition approach advocated by Denicol (2010) and uses Chapman-Enskog expansion rather than Grad's moment method. The results enable more accurate hydrodynamic modeling of the quark-gluon plasma formed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, particularly for the small shear viscosity to entropy density ratios (eta/s approximately 0.08-0.2) relevant to RHIC and LHC conditions. The work also demonstrates that the relaxation time approximation is sufficiently reliable for heavy-ion collision applications, and opens the door to systematic derivation of third-order and higher-order hydrodynamic equations.
