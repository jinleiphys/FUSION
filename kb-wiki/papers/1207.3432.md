# Have Superheavy Elements been Produced in Nature?

> We discuss the possibility whether superheavy elements can be produced in Nature by the astrophysical rapid neutron capture process. To this end we have performed fully dynamical network r-process calculations assuming an environment with neutron-to-seed ratio large enough to produce superheavy nuclei. Our calculations include two sets of nuclear masses and fission barriers and include all possible fission channels and the associated fission yield distributions. Our calculations produce superheavy nuclei with A ~ 300 that however decay on timescales of days.

## Key claim

Superheavy nuclei with mass numbers around A ~ 300 can be produced in astrophysical r-process environments, but only under specific nuclear model assumptions; the ETFSI/ETFSI combination of fission barriers and masses allows production of such nuclei, while the TF/FRDM combination blocks production via neutron-induced fission, and in both cases the produced superheavy nuclei decay on timescales of days and do not reach the valley of beta-stability as long-lived species.

## Method

The authors performed fully dynamical network r-process calculations using two sets of nuclear masses and fission barriers (ETFSI/ETFSI and TF/FRDM), including all possible fission channels (neutron-induced, beta-delayed, and spontaneous fission) with associated fission yield distributions. They assumed an environment with a neutron-to-seed ratio large enough to produce superheavy nuclei, using a homologous adiabatic expansion model with two extreme cases ("hot" and "cold" r-process) defined by different late-time temperature evolutions. The network code, based on XNet, was extended to treat all fission reactions implicitly, with beta-decay half-lives from Moeller, Pfeiffer, and Kratz (2003) and spontaneous fission half-lives estimated from a parametrization fitted to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Neutron-to-seed ratio of 290 at 3 GK, achieved with entropy of 200 k/nucleon and initial electron fraction Y_e = 0.35
- Expansion timescale tau = 3 ms for the homologous phase, with Delta = 2 s for the late-time power-law density evolution
- Superheavy nuclei with A ~ 300 are produced in ETFSI/ETFSI calculations, but decay on timescales of days (Z=109 nuclei still present after approximately 12 hours)
- For TF/FRDM, B_f - S_n values around 2 MeV for nuclei near Z ~ 94, N ~ 186 cause neutron-induced fission to dominate, inhibiting production of heavier nuclei
- For ETFSI/ETFSI, B_f - S_n values around 8 MeV in the same region allow continued build-up to heavier nuclei

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Thielemann, Metzinger, and Klapdor (1983) who concluded that the r-process path ends in a 100% beta-delayed fission region and superheavies cannot be reached, a result now understood to have overestimated fission effects due to deficiencies in the Hilf mass formula and Howard and Moller (1980) fission barriers. The paper uses improved fission barrier tabulations from Mamdouh et al. (1998) and Myers and Swiatecki (1999), and addresses questions raised by Schramm and Fowler (1971) about natural production of superheavy elements. The results enable future exploration of nuclei beyond Z=110 using newer theoretical predictions from Erler, Langanke et al. (2012), and leave open the question of whether producing progenitors with even higher charge numbers could circumvent fission during decay to stability.
