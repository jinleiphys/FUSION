# Nuclear weak interaction rates in primordial nucleosynthesis

> We calculate the weak interaction rates of selected light nuclei during the epoch of Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN), and we assess the impact of these rates on nuclear abundance flow histories and on final light element abundance yields. We consider electron and electron antineutrino captures on 3He and 7Be, and the reverse processes of positron capture and electron neutrino capture on 3H and 7Li. We also compute the rates of positron and electron neutrino capture on 6He. We calculate beta and positron decay transitions where appropriate. As expected, the final standard BBN abundance yields are little affected by addition of these weak processes, though there can be slight alterations of nuclear flow histories. However, non-standard BBN scenarios, e.g., those involving out of equilibrium particle decay with energetic final state neutrinos, may be affected by these processes.

## Key claim

The weak interaction rates of light nuclei (3He, 7Be, 3H, 7Li, 6He) during Big Bang Nucleosynthesis produce only very slight alterations in final abundance yields in standard BBN, but these processes may be more important in non-standard BBN scenarios involving out-of-equilibrium particle decay with energetic final state neutrinos.

## Method

The authors calculated charged-current weak interaction rates (electron and antineutrino capture, positron and neutrino capture, beta and positron decay) for selected light nuclei using a modified version of the standard Kawano/Wagoner-Fowler-Hoyle BBN code. The rates were computed by summing over thermally-populated parent states and daughter states, using measured ft-values and phase space integrals that include Coulomb wave correction factors, neutrino and plasma temperature ratios, and blocking factors from final-state leptons. The code consistently followed thermodynamics, the Hubble expansion rate, and photon and decoupled neutrino temperatures with zero neutrino chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- Baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP: η ≈ 6.11×10⁻¹⁰, corresponding to entropy per baryon s/k_b ≈ 5.9×10⁹
- 7Be ground-state-to-ground-state Q-value: Q_n ≈ 0.3509 MeV with log₁₀ ft ≈ 3.3; antineutrino threshold energy E^ν_th ≈ 0.1601 MeV
- 7Be ground-to-first-excited-state Q-value: Q_n ≈ -0.1267 MeV with log₁₀ ft ≈ 3.5; antineutrino threshold E^ν_th ≈ 0.6377 MeV
- 7Be first excited state at 0.4292 MeV excitation; estimated log₁₀ ft ≈ 3.2 with Q_n ≈ 0.7801 MeV and zero antineutrino threshold
- 3He→3H forward process Q_n ≈ -0.5296 MeV with log₁₀ ft = 3.1; antineutrino threshold E^ν_th ≈ 1.0406 MeV
- 3H beta decay half-life: 12.33 yr, corresponding to rate 1.78×10⁻⁹ s⁻¹
- 6He→6Li Q_n ≈ 4.0207 MeV with log₁₀ ft = 2.9; neutrino threshold E^ν_th = 0; positron capture minimum final-state antineutrino energy ≈ 4.5317 MeV
- 6He beta decay half-life: 0.808 s, corresponding to unblocked decay rate λ_beta ≈ 0.858 s⁻¹
- Weak freeze-out occurs at T ∼ 0.7 MeV; NSE freeze-out at T ∼ 0.1 MeV
- Ratio of neutrino-to-plasma temperatures after e± pair disappearance: (4/11)^(1/3)
- At T ∼ 0.1 MeV, the first excited state of 7Be has population factor P_1 ≈ 0.5 exp(-0.4292/T), approximately 1%
- Weak processes with 3H as target result in 10⁻²% decreases in deuterium and 3H final yields, 10⁻²% increase in 3He, and 10⁻³% increase in 7Li
- Adding 7Be weak rates decreases final abundances of 7Li, 2H, 3He, and 4He by about 10⁻⁴% to 10⁻⁵%

## Context

This work extends the standard BBN reaction network by calculating previously neglected weak interaction rates for light nuclei, complementing the free-nucleon charged-current rates discussed in detail by Smith and Fuller (2009) and Smith, Fuller, and Smith (2009). The calculations use a modified version of the Kawano/Wagoner-Fowler-Hoyle code. The results show that these rates have negligible impact on standard BBN yields, but the authors note that non-standard BBN models involving decaying massive particles (as studied by Kusenko (2004), Dolgov (2000), Fuller (2009), and Cyburt (2010)) may be affected. The work also addresses the ongoing lithium problem, where standard BBN predicts 7Li abundance a factor of two to three larger than observed on the Spite plateau, and the claimed detection of 6Li at levels three orders of magnitude above standard BBN predictions.
