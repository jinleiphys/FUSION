# X(3872) is not a true molecule

> A solvable coordinate-space model is employed to study the $c\bar{c}$ component of the X(3872) wave function, by coupling a confined $^3P_1$ $c\bar{c}$ state to the almost unbound $S$-wave $D^0\bar{D}^{*0}$ channel via the $^3P_0$ mechanism. The two-component wave function is calculated for different values of the binding energy and the transition radius $a$, always resulting in a significant $c\bar{c}$ component. However, the long tail of the $D^0\bar{D}^{*0}$ wave function, in the case of small binding, strongly limits the $c\bar{c}$ probability, which roughly lies in the range 7-11%, for the average experimental binding energy of 0.16 MeV and $a$ between 2 and 3 GeV$^{-1}$. Furthermore, a reasonable value of 7.8 fm is obtained for the X(3872) r.m.s. radius at the latter binding energy, as well as an $S$-wave $D^0\bar{D}^{*0}$ scattering length of 11.6 fm. Finally, the $\mathcal{S}$-matrix pole trajectories as a function of coupling constant show that X(3872) can be generated either as a dynamical pole or as one connected to the bare $c\bar{c}$ confinement spectrum, depending on details of the model. From these results we conclude that X(3872) is not a genuine meson-meson molecule, nor actually any other mesonic system with non-exotic quantum numbers, due to inevitable mixing with the corresponding quark-antiquark states.

## Key claim
The X(3872) is not a genuine meson-meson molecule, nor any other mesonic system with non-exotic quantum numbers, because inevitable mixing with quark-antiquark states produces a significant charmonium component in its wave function, with the specific nature of the pole (dynamical vs. confinement) depending on model details.

## Method
A solvable coordinate-space coupled-channel model was employed, coupling a confined \(^3P_1\) \(c\bar{c}\) state to an \(S\)-wave \(D^0\bar{D}^{*0}\) channel via a delta-shell transition potential that mimics string breaking at a distance \(a\). The two-component radial wave function was computed analytically for different binding energies and transition radii, and \(\mathcal{S}\)-matrix pole trajectories were studied as a function of the coupling constant \(g\).

## Key numbers
- \(c\bar{c}\) probability of 7.48% for \(a = 2.0\) GeV\(^{-1}\) and 11.18% for \(a = 3.0\) GeV\(^{-1}\), at the average experimental binding energy of 0.16 MeV (case X)
- X(3872) root-mean-square radius of 7.82 fm for \(a = 2.0\) GeV\(^{-1}\) and 7.76 fm for \(a = 3.0\) GeV\(^{-1}\), at 0.16 MeV binding energy
- \(S\)-wave \(D^0\bar{D}^{*0}\) scattering length of 11.55 fm for \(a = 2.0\) GeV\(^{-1}\) at 0.16 MeV binding energy
- Bare \(^3P_1\) \(c\bar{c}\) harmonic-oscillator states at 3599 MeV (1P) and 3979 MeV (2P), with constituent charm quark mass 1562 MeV and oscillator frequency 190 MeV

## Context
This work extends the authors' prior coupled-channel analysis of X(3872) as a unitarized charmonium state (Coito, Rupp, van Beveren 2011) and addresses claims by Braaten and collaborators (2004, 2006) that the X(3872) must be either a charm meson molecule or a virtual state. The results are compatible with other recent approaches finding 7% charmonium probability (Guo et al. 2009, 2012) and with general arguments about near-threshold states (Baranov 2008). The model enables future studies of how the charmonium component varies with additional decay channels and parameter choices.
