# Bose-Einstein correlations as reflection of correlations of fluctuations

> Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) observed between identical bosons produced in high energy multiparticle collisions are regarded as very important tool in investigations of multiparticle production processes. We present here their stochastic feature stressing the fact that they can be regarded as a reflection of correlations of fluctuations present in hadronizing system. We show in particular that such approach allows for simple modelling of BEC in numerical event generators used to describe the multiparticle production processes at high energy collisions.

# Bose-Einstein correlations as reflection of correlations of fluctuations

## Key claim

Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) in high-energy multiparticle collisions can be understood not as a purely quantum-statistical effect requiring full wave-function symmetrization, but rather as a manifestation of correlations of fluctuations present in the hadronizing system, provided that charges are reassigned among produced particles in a specific stochastic manner that mimics Bose statistics.

## Method

The authors propose a numerical algorithm that operates on the output of any Monte Carlo event generator: after particles are produced with their momenta and space-time positions fixed, the charges are reassigned by grouping like-charged particles into "elementary emitting cells" in phase space, where each cell is filled sequentially with a fixed probability P until a failure occurs, then a new cell is started. The algorithm is applied to two simple hadronization models, a cascade model (CAS) and a maximum-entropy model (MaxEnt), and the resulting two-particle correlation function C₂(Q) is computed as a function of momentum difference Q.

## Key numbers

- For the cascade model with P=0.5, the extracted "size" parameter R (from exponential fits C₂(Q) ~ 1 + λ·exp(-Q·R)) varies weakly between 0.66 fm and 0.87 fm as the initial mass M increases from 10 GeV to 100 GeV.
- For the "minimal" weight P_C given by exp[-½ δ²_ij(x)·δ²_ij(p)], the extracted R varies from 0.64 fm to 0.44 fm over the same mass range.
- For "Split" type sources (M=100 GeV, P=0.5) with increasing number of subsources (n_l = 2^k, k=1,2,3), the extracted R increases from 0.87 fm to 1.29 fm, 1.99 fm, and 3.35 fm respectively.
- For "Indep" type sources with the same parameters, C₂(0)-1 = λ falls roughly as 1/2^k, i.e., inversely with the number of subsources.
- Best fit to DELPHI e⁺e⁻ annihilation data at M=91.3 GeV was obtained with P=0.23 and two or more subsources.

## Context

This work extends the quantum-optical approach to BEC (HBT effect) previously applied to hadronizing sources by Zajc (1987) and by the authors themselves in an earlier information-theory-based Monte Carlo generator (Utyuzh, Wilk, Wlodarczyk, 2001). It contrasts with the standard space-time approach where C₂(Q) is interpreted as a Fourier transform of the source size distribution, and with existing numerical methods that either shift momenta (Lonnblad and Sjostrand, 1998) or reweight events (Fialkowski and Wit, 2000). The proposed algorithm enables simple inclusion of BEC into essentially any event generator without violating conservation laws, and can simultaneously reproduce intermittency patterns and charge fluctuations. The "Indep" type of sources provides a natural explanation for the observed absence of inter-W BEC in W⁺W⁻ final states.
