# Charge symmetry breaking as a probe for the real part of eta--nucleus scattering lengths

> We demonstrate that one can use the occurrence of charge symmetry breaking as a tool to explore the eta--nucleus interaction near the eta threshold. Based on indications that the cross section ratio of pi+ and pi0 production on nuclei deviates from the isotopic value in the vicinity of the eta production threshold, due to, e.g., pi0-eta mixing, we argue that a systematic study of this ratio as a function of the energy would allow to pin down the sign of the real part of the eta-nucleus scattering length. This sign plays an important role in the context of the possible existence of eta-nucleus bound states.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the energy dependence of the cross section ratio \(R = \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}(pd \to ^3\mathrm{H}\pi^+) / \frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}(pd \to ^3\mathrm{He}\pi^0)\) near the \(\eta\) production threshold can be used to determine the sign of the real part of the \(\eta\)-nucleus scattering length, which is not accessible from \(\eta\) production cross sections alone. The feasibility depends on the unknown phase \(\phi\) between the pion and eta production amplitudes, but the authors claim that different signs of the real part lead to qualitatively different energy dependences that can be distinguished experimentally even without knowing \(\phi\).

## Method

The authors model the \(\eta\)-nucleus production amplitude as \(\mathcal{M}_\eta = \mathcal{M}^0_\eta \, T_\eta\) with \(T_\eta = 1/(1 - i p_\eta a(\eta A))\), where \(a(\eta A)\) is the complex \(\eta\)-nucleus scattering length and \(p_\eta\) is the relative \(\eta\)-nucleus momentum. They then compute the cross section ratio \(R\) for \(pd \to ^3\mathrm{H}\pi^+ / pd \to ^3\mathrm{He}\pi^0\) using a phenomenological model that includes \(\pi^0-\eta\) mixing (mixing angle \(\theta_m = 0.0015\)) and the interference term \(\mathrm{Re}(\mathcal{M}_\eta \mathcal{M}_{\tilde\pi^0}^*)\). The energy dependence of \(R\) is evaluated above and below the \(\eta\) threshold for different signs of the real part of the \(\eta\,^3\mathrm{He}\) scattering length, using values extracted from fits to \(pd \to ^3\mathrm{He}\eta\) cross section data, and for various choices of the unknown phase \(\phi\).

## Key numbers

*   Extracted from \(pd \to ^3\mathrm{He}\eta\) data: \(|\mathrm{Re}(a(\eta ^3\mathrm{He}))| = (3.8 \pm 0.6)\) fm and \(\mathrm{Im}(a(\eta ^3\mathrm{He})) = (1.6 \pm 1.1)\) fm.
*   Correlation relation for the \(\eta\,^3\mathrm{He}\) scattering length (units in fm): \(a^2_R + 0.449 a^2_I + 4.509 a_I = 21.44\).
*   Extreme values used: \(a_R = \pm 4.3\) fm with \(a_I = 0.5\) fm; \(a_R = \pm 2.4\) fm with \(a_I = 2.7\) fm.
*   \(\pi^0-\eta\) mixing angle: \(\theta_m = 0.0015\).
*   Pion production amplitude squared: \(|\mathcal{M}_{\tilde\pi^0}|^2 = 0.06\) \(\mu\)b/sr (at \(\theta_{p-\pi} = 180^\circ\) near the \(^3\mathrm{He}\eta\) threshold).
*   Fitted eta production amplitude squared: \(|\mathcal{M}^0_\eta|^2 = 1.51\) \(\mu\)b/sr (for \(a_R = \pm 4.3 + i0.5\) fm), 1.74 \(\mu\)b/sr (for \(\pm 3.8 + i1.6\) fm), and 1.93 \(\mu\)b/sr (for \(\pm 2.4 + i2.7\) fm).
*   The \(\eta\) production threshold corresponds to a proton momentum of approximately 1563 MeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Magiera and Machner (2000) and Machner (2002) to use the cross section ratio \(R\) in \(pd\) collisions to study charge symmetry breaking via \(\pi^0-\eta\) mixing. The authors argue that the same ratio can also determine the sign of the real part of the \(\eta\)-nucleus scattering length, which is crucial for assessing the existence of \(\eta\)-nucleus bound states predicted by Haider and Liu (1986). Prior analyses of \(pd \to ^3\mathrm{He}\eta\) data by Wilkin (1993) and Wycech, Green, and Niskanen (1995) gave conflicting signs for \(\mathrm{Re}\, a(\eta A)\), and this method offers a way to resolve that ambiguity. The approach could be extended to other systems (e.g., \(\eta d\), \(\eta\alpha\)) and is feasible at facilities like CELSIUS and COSY.
