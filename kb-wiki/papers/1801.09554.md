# Neutrinos, supernovae, and the origin of the heavy elements

> Stars of ~8-100 solar masses end their lives as core-collapse supernovae (SNe). In the process they emit a powerful burst of neutrinos, produce a variety of elements, and leave behind either a neutron star or a black hole. The wide mass range for SN progenitors results in diverse neutrino signals, explosion energies, and nucleosynthesis products. A major mechanism to produce nuclei heavier than iron is rapid neutron capture, or the r process. This process may be connected to SNe in several ways. A brief review is presented on current understanding of neutrino emission, explosion, and nucleosynthesis of SNe.

# Neutrinos, supernovae, and the origin of the heavy elements

## Key claim
Core-collapse supernovae from stars of approximately 8 to 100 solar masses can provide neutrons for rapid neutron capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis through several distinct channels, but the neutrino-driven explosion mechanism has been consistently demonstrated only for stars of about 8.8 solar masses, while its operation in more massive stars remains uncertain and under active investigation.

## Method
The paper reviews the current understanding of neutrino emission, explosion mechanisms, and nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae, drawing on hydrodynamic simulations of neutrino-driven explosions, neutrino transport calculations, and nucleosynthesis network studies. It examines three specific r-process production sites: neutrino-driven winds from proto-neutron stars, neutrino-induced reactions in helium shells of metal-poor supernova progenitors, and neutron star mergers. The review synthesizes results from multiple modeling groups, including the Garching group's exploratory studies of neutrino-driven explosions and detailed simulations of neutron star mergers.

## Key numbers
- Gravitational binding energy of a neutron star: approximately 3 × 10^53 ergs for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star with 10 km radius, corresponding to about 100 MeV per nucleon
- Typical supernova photon energy: approximately 10^49 ergs; kinetic energy of supernova debris: approximately 10^51 ergs
- Neutrino-driven explosion timescale: approximately 0.1 to 1 second; explosion energy varies from approximately 10^50 to 10^51 ergs
- Cooling phase of proto-neutron star lasts approximately 10 seconds; neutrino burst from SN 1987A lasted approximately 13 seconds
- For an 18 solar mass supernova model during accretion phase: average ν_e energy approximately 8-11 MeV, average anti-ν_e energy approximately 11-12 MeV, average ν_x energy approximately 16-14 MeV
- Shock-breakout ν_e pulse: luminosity approximately (4-5) × 10^53 erg/s with width of approximately 10 ms, typical of all supernovae
- Accretion phase duration for 18 solar mass model: approximately 0.6 seconds, about 3 times longer than for 8.8 solar mass model
- Threshold for charged-current reaction anti-ν_e + ⁴He → ³H + n + e⁺: 21.6 MeV
- Neutrino-induced r-process in helium shells can produce nuclei up to A > 200 for an 11 solar mass supernova model with initial metallicity [Fe/H] approximately -4.5
- Neutrino-induced r-process ceases to operate at [Fe/H] greater than approximately -3

## Context
This review extends the foundational work of Baade and Zwicky (1934) connecting supernovae to neutron star formation, and the neutrino-driven supernova mechanism developed by Bethe and collaborators. It synthesizes results from multiple recent studies including the Garching group's simulations (Janka and collaborators) showing that neutrino-driven explosions are not a simple function of progenitor mass, and the work of Martinez-Pinedo, Roberts, and others on neutrino opacities in proto-neutron star surface layers. The review also incorporates the strong observational support for neutron star mergers as r-process sites from the gravitational wave event GW170817 and its associated kilonova (Smartt and collaborators, Kasen and collaborators). The paper enables future work by clarifying which r-process production channels are viable under which progenitor conditions, and by identifying the critical role of neutrino flavor oscillations in enabling the neutrino-induced r-process in helium shells.
