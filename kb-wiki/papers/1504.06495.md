# Systematic Coarse-Graining in Nucleation Theory

> In this work we show that the standard method to obtain nucleation rate-predictions with the aid of atomistic Monte-Carlo simulations leads to nucleation rate predictions that deviate $3-5$ orders of magnitude from the recent brute-force molecular dynamics simulations [J. Diemand, R. Angélil, K. K. Tanaka, and H. Tanaka, J. Chem. Phys. \textbf{139}, 074309 (2013)] conducted in the experimental accessible supersaturation regime for Lennard-Jones argon. We argue that this is due to the truncated state space literature mostly relies on, where the number of atoms in a nucleus is considered the only relevant order parameter. We here formulate the nonequilibrium statistical mechanics of nucleation in an extended state space, where the internal energy and momentum of the nuclei is additionally incorporated. We show that the extended model explains the lack in agreement between the molecular dynamics simulations by Diemand et al.\ and the truncated state space. We demonstrate additional benefits of using the extended state space; in particular, the definition of a nucleus temperature arrises very naturally and can be shown without further approximation to obey the fluctuation law of McGraw and Laviolette. In addition, we illustrate that our theory conveniently allows to extend existing theories to richer sets of order parameters.

## Key claim

The standard method of using atomistic Monte-Carlo simulations with a truncated state space (considering only nucleus size) to predict nucleation rates yields predictions that deviate 3–5 orders of magnitude from brute-force molecular dynamics simulations in the experimentally accessible supersaturation regime for Lennard-Jones argon; the authors argue this discrepancy arises from neglecting internal energy and momentum as additional order parameters in the nucleus state space.

## Method

The authors formulate a nonequilibrium statistical mechanics framework for nucleation using the GENERIC projection-operator technique, extending the nucleus state space to include number of atoms, total momentum, and internal energy. They derive microscopic expressions for the nucleation landscape (entropic potential) and the diffusion tensor in the resulting Fokker-Planck equation, and apply the framework to compute nucleation rates for Lennard-Jones argon droplets in a metastable gas, comparing results to the brute-force molecular dynamics simulations of Diemand et al. (2013).

## Key numbers

- Nucleation rate predictions from the standard truncated state space deviate by 3–5 orders of magnitude from the brute-force molecular dynamics simulations of Diemand et al. (2013) for Lennard-Jones argon in the experimentally accessible supersaturation regime.
- The extended model incorporating internal energy and momentum eliminates this 3–5 order of magnitude discrepancy.
- Typical laboratory experiments measure nucleation rates less than \(10^{10} \text{cm}^{-3} s^{-1}\); the supersonic nozzle experiment achieves rates up to \(10^{17} \text{cm}^{-3} s^{-1}\).
- Molecular dynamics studies typically operate at nucleation rates 5–10 orders of magnitude above the supersonic nozzle experiment and 12–17 orders of magnitude above typical experiments.
- The work of Feder et al. (1966) showed that including internal energy fluctuations leads to corrections of 1–3 orders of magnitude to the nucleation rate.
- The Lothe-Pound translational-rotational paradox, when addressed by Reguera and Rubi via inclusion of momentum and angular momentum, yields corrections of order \(10^2\)–\(10^5\) for typical nucleation regimes.

## Context

This work extends the classical insight of Feder et al. (1966) that latent heat release necessitates including internal energy as a state variable, and addresses the translational-rotational paradox raised by Lothe and Pound (1962) by incorporating momentum into the state space, following the approach of Reguera and Rubi. The framework provides a systematic coarse-graining procedure that can extend existing theories such as mean-field kinetic theory (MFKT) to richer state spaces, enabling quantitative nucleation rate predictions in experimentally relevant low-supersaturation regimes where brute-force molecular dynamics is computationally prohibitive.
