# On the Role of Dissipation in the Early Stages of Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> The influence of the dissipative terms on the conditions of formation and the characteristic parameters of shock waves in relativistic nuclear collisions is investigated for three types of equation of state (non linear QHD-1, resonance gas and lattice QCD). Energy and velocity profiles are obtained in a one-dimensional model; the duration of the shock phase and width of the shock front are calculated. It is shown that the presence of a phase transition results in a strong enhancement of the width of the shock front, which results in an increase of transparency. This effect, combined with the fact that the nuclei have a finite size, prevents the energy density to rise to its maximum value (full stopping) as would be predicted by a non dissipative shock model.

## Key claim

The paper shows that including dissipative terms in a one-dimensional model of relativistic nuclear collisions causes the shock front width to increase significantly when a phase transition is present, which in turn increases transparency and prevents the energy density from reaching the maximum "full stopping" value predicted by non-dissipative shock models.

## Method

The authors solved a one-dimensional relativistic fluid-dynamical model including dissipative (viscous) terms for three different equations of state: non-linear QHD-1, a resonance gas, and lattice QCD. They calculated the energy and velocity profiles, the duration of the shock phase, and the width of the shock front for each equation of state.

## Key numbers

*   Shock front width is strongly enhanced by the presence of a phase transition, leading to an increase in transparency.
*   The energy density is prevented from rising to its maximum value (full stopping) that would be predicted by a non-dissipative shock model, due to the combined effect of dissipation and finite nuclear size.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of shock waves in relativistic heavy-ion collisions by incorporating dissipative effects, which were often neglected in prior ideal-fluid treatments. The use of three distinct equations of state (non-linear QHD-1, resonance gas, and lattice QCD) allows the authors to compare how the phase transition present in the latter two influences shock dynamics. The results enable more realistic modeling of the early, non-equilibrium stage of a collision and the conditions for shock formation.
