# Accessing subnuclear fluctuations and saturation with multiplicity dependent $J/ψ$ production in p+p and p+Pb collisions

> We study the production of $J/ψ$ vector mesons as a function of charged hadron multiplicity in p+p and p+Pb collisions at LHC energies. We employ the color glass condensate framework, using running coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov evolved dipole amplitudes, to compute gluon and $c\bar{c}$-pair production. We use fragmentation functions to obtain charged hadrons, and explore two different hadronization schemes for the $J/ψ$: non-relativistic quantum chromodynamics and the improved color evaporation model. In our framework, event-by-event multiplicity fluctuations of both hadrons and $J/ψ$ are driven by geometric and saturation scale normalization fluctuations. Studying the correlation between $J/ψ$ and hadron multiplicity, we show that the characteristic difference between forward and backward rapidity in p+Pb collisions is a result of different degrees of saturation probed at different rapidities. We demonstrate that experimental data on heavy-flavor production as a function of event activity provide stringent constraints on the fluctuating proton structure.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the characteristic difference between forward and backward rapidity in the correlation between $J/\psi$ and charged hadron multiplicities in p+Pb collisions is a result of different degrees of saturation probed at different rapidities, but this conclusion depends on the specific hadronization scheme (NRQCD or ICEM) and on the inclusion of event-by-event fluctuations of both geometry and saturation scale normalization.

## Method

The authors employ the color glass condensate (CGC) framework with running coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov (rcBK) evolved dipole amplitudes to compute gluon and $c\bar{c}$-pair production in p+p and p+Pb collisions at LHC energies. Charged hadrons are obtained using KKP fragmentation functions, while $J/\psi$ production is computed using two hadronization schemes: non-relativistic quantum chromodynamics (NRQCD) and the improved color evaporation model (ICEM). Event-by-event multiplicity fluctuations are driven by geometric fluctuations (hot spot positions and sizes in the proton) and saturation scale normalization fluctuations, with the proton modeled as three hot spots whose widths grow with decreasing $x$ to approximate Gribov diffusion.

## Key numbers

* The proton hot spot width $B_q = 1\ {\rm GeV}^{-2}$ at mid-rapidity, with a growth function $w(y) = 0.15 + 0.042(\pm y - 4.6)^2$ applied to model the $x$-dependent proton size
* The saturation scale normalization fluctuation width $\sigma_{Q_s^2} = 0.1$ and hot spot size fluctuation width $\sigma_{B_q} = 0.7$
* The transverse area of the proton $S_\perp = 13\ {\rm mb}$, approximately 30% smaller than that used in Lappi and Mantysaari (2013)
* The strong coupling constant $\alpha_s = 0.16$ and infrared regulator $m_{\rm IR} = 0.2\ {\rm GeV}$
* The inelastic cross sections used for normalization: $\sigma_{\rm inel} = 2161\ {\rm mb}$ for $\sqrt{s}=8.16\ {\rm TeV}$ p+Pb collisions and $\sigma_{\rm inel} = 60\ {\rm mb}$ for $7\ {\rm TeV}$ p+p collisions
* The NRQCD long-distance matrix elements: $\langle \mathcal{O}^{J/\psi}_{{}^{1} S^{[8]}_0}\rangle = 0.089\ {\rm GeV}^3$, $\langle \mathcal{O}^{J/\psi}_{{}^{3} S^{[8]}_1}\rangle = 0.0030\ {\rm GeV}^3$, $\langle \mathcal{O}^{J/\psi}_{{}^{3} P^{[8]}_J}\rangle = 0.0056\ {\rm GeV}^3$, and $\langle \mathcal{O}^{J/\psi}_{{}^{3} S^{[1]}_1}\rangle = 1.16/(2N_c)\ {\rm GeV}^3$

## Context

This work extends previous CGC calculations by Ma and collaborators (2018) that varied the saturation scale by hand to generate high multiplicity configurations, by instead including realistic event-by-event fluctuations of geometry and local densities in both projectile and target. The results are compared to ALICE experimental data on $J/\psi$ production as a function of charged hadron multiplicity in p+p and p+Pb collisions at LHC energies. The framework enables future constraints on the fluctuating proton structure from heavy-flavor production data, and the authors note that a systematic study of the quadrupole-to-dipole approximation used for the color singlet will be assessed in a separate publication.
