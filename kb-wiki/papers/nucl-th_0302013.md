# Hydrodynamics Near a Chiral Critical Point

> We introduce a model for the real-time evolution of a relativistic fluid of quarks coupled to non-equilibrium dynamics of the long wavelength (classical) modes of the chiral condensate. We solve the equations of motion numerically in 3+1 space-time dimensions. Starting the evolution at high temperature in the symmetric phase, we study dynamical trajectories that either cross the line of first-order phase transitions or evolve through its critical endpoint. For those cases, we predict the behavior of the azimuthal momentum asymmetry for high-energy heavy-ion collisions at nonzero impact parameter.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that the azimuthal momentum asymmetry (elliptic flow) of quarks in non-central heavy-ion collisions will exhibit qualitatively different behavior depending on whether the dynamical trajectory of the expanding fireball crosses a first-order phase transition line or passes through its critical endpoint, but the study is limited to locally baryon-symmetric matter (equal numbers of quarks and antiquarks) and uses a simplified phenomenological model rather than a quantitatively reliable QCD calculation.

## Method

The authors couple 3+1 dimensional relativistic hydrodynamics of a perfect-fluid quark heat bath to classical equations of motion for the long-wavelength modes of the chiral condensate (sigma field) from the linear sigma model, including field fluctuations beyond mean-field approximation. They solve the coupled system numerically on a 160^3 grid with 0.2 fm spacing, using the SHASTA flux-corrected transport algorithm for the fluid and a staggered leap-frog algorithm for the fields, starting from an ellipsoidal initial energy distribution resembling the almond-shaped overlap region of two colliding nuclei at nonzero impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- The critical endpoint for the chiral phase transition in the model occurs at quark-field coupling \(g_c \simeq 3.7\), where the barrier between degenerate minima disappears and the latent heat vanishes.
- For \(g = 5.5\), the first-order phase transition has a critical temperature \(T_c \simeq 123.3\) MeV.
- The initial temperature of the quark fluid is taken as \(T_i \approx 160\) MeV.
- The computational grid uses spacing \(\Delta x = 0.2\) fm in a cube of volume \(L^3 = (32\ \text{fm})^3\), with time step \(\Delta t = 0.4\Delta x\) for the fluid and \(\Delta t = 0.1\Delta x\) for the fields.
- The initial fireball has longitudinal length \(2l_z = 12\) fm, nuclear radius \(r_A = 6.5\) fm, and impact parameter \(\tilde{b} = 6\) fm.
- The initial maximum longitudinal velocity is \(v_{max} = 0.2\).
- The initial field fluctuations have variance \(\sqrt{\langle\delta\sigma^2\rangle} = v/3\), where \(v\) is related to the vacuum expectation value \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier dynamical studies of chiral phase transitions that employed mean-field approximation and simplified symmetries (e.g., CsMi, MPS, Abada, Scavenius (1999)) by including field fluctuations and solving in full 3+1 dimensions without imposed space-time symmetries. It shifts focus from disoriented chiral condensate formation to the bulk hydrodynamic flow of the quark fluid, enabling future studies of how the chiral critical point predicted by Stephanov (1999) and located on the lattice by Fodor (2001) at \(T = 160 \pm 3.5\) MeV and \(\mu_B = 725 \pm 35\) MeV might manifest in elliptic flow observables at beam energies \(E_{lab} \simeq 20-80\) AGeV.
