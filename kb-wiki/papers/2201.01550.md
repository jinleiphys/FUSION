# Nucleon momentum gap in asymmetric nuclear matter

> For more than half a century, nucleons are considered to move continuously in the nuclei. Recent electron-scattering experiments indicate about 20$\sim$25\% nucleons in heavier nuclei are involved in the neutron-proton short-range correlations. Nucleons in the nuclei thus have abnormal behavior unlike those in the non-interaction fermi gas. In the neutron-rich nuclei, around the Fermi momentum, the neutron-proton short-range correlations lead to a momentum gap in the proton momentum distribution, which is circumstantially supported by the pionic experimental data. Likewise, there should also be a neutron momentum gap in the proton-rich nuclei. Nucleon momentum gap is thought to have profound and extensive implications in the studies ranging from particle physics to neutron stars as well as ultra-cold atomic gases.

## Key claim

Due to neutron-proton short-range correlations, in neutron-rich nuclei the proton momentum distribution exhibits a "jump" or gap around its Fermi momentum, meaning the high-momentum tail of protons starts not at the proton Fermi momentum but at the neutron Fermi momentum; this claim is supported circumstantially by comparisons of pion production data from heavy-ion collisions with transport model calculations.

## Method

The author uses an isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck transport model that incorporates nucleon-nucleon short-range correlations via a 1/k⁴ high-momentum tail in the nucleon momentum distribution, with the n-p dominance model requiring equal numbers of neutrons and protons in the high-momentum tail. The proton momentum gap is implemented by setting the starting momentum of the proton high-momentum tail equal to the neutron Fermi momentum rather than the proton Fermi momentum in neutron-rich matter. The model is applied to calculate pion production in C+Au collisions at 85A MeV and Ne+U and Ne+NaF collisions at 250A MeV, and the results are compared with experimental data from the 1980s.

## Key numbers

- About 20–25% of nucleons in heavier nuclei have momenta greater than the Fermi momentum, as indicated by electron-scattering experiments.
- The number of n-p short-range correlation pairs is about 18 times the number of p-p or n-n short-range correlation pairs.
- The high-momentum tail of nucleon momentum distributions decreases as 1/k⁴.
- The threshold energy for pion production is 290 MeV; the C+Au collisions at 85A MeV are far below this threshold.
- The symmetry energy slope used in the calculations is about 58.7 MeV at saturation density.

## Context

This work extends the study of short-range correlations in nuclei, following experimental findings from electron-scattering (Nature 2018, RMP 2017, Claudio 2015, Science 2014, Science 2008, Piasetzky 2006, Tang 2003, Fomin 2012, Egiyan 2006) that show about 20–25% of nucleons in heavier nuclei have momenta above the Fermi momentum. It addresses a contradiction in the standard picture where protons and neutrons in asymmetric matter would have very different high-momentum starting points, conflicting with the n-p short-range correlation picture from Piasetzky (2006) and Schiavilla (2007). The work uses pion production data from CERN (1982) and Noll (1984) and pion ratio data from Benenson (1979) to provide circumstantial evidence for the proton momentum gap, which was previously smoothed out in microscopic calculations by Rios (2014) using self-consistent Green's functions at finite temperature.
