# Microscopic description of fission in neutron-rich Radium isotopes with the Gogny energy density functional

> Mean field calculations, based on the D1S, D1N and D1M parametrizations of the Gogny energy density functional, have been carried out to obtain the potential energy surfaces relevant to fission in several Ra isotopes with the neutron number 144 $\le$ N $\le$ 176. Inner and outer barrier heights as well as first and second isomer excitation energies are given. The existence of a well developed third minimum along the fission paths of Ra nuclei, is analyzed in terms of the energetics of the "fragments" defining such elongated configuration. The masses and charges of the fission fragments are studied as functions of the neutron number in the parent Ra isotope. The comparison between fission and $α$-decay half-lives, reveals that the former becomes faster for increasing neutron numbers. Though there exists a strong variance of the results with respect to the parameters used in the computation of the spontaneous fission rate, a change in tendency is observed at N=164 with a steady increase that makes heavier neutron-rich Ra isotopes stable against fission, diminishing the importance of fission recycling in the r-process.

## Key claim

Mean-field calculations with three Gogny energy density functional parametrizations (D1S, D1N, D1M) predict that for neutron-rich Radium isotopes with N ≥ 164, spontaneous fission half-lives increase steadily, making these nuclei stable against fission and diminishing the importance of fission recycling in the r-process, though the authors note a strong variance of results with respect to the parameters used in computing the spontaneous fission rate.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the D1S, D1N, and D1M parametrizations of the Gogny energy density functional to obtain potential energy surfaces for even-even Radium isotopes with neutron numbers 144 ≤ N ≤ 176. Spontaneous fission half-lives were computed using the WKB formalism with both the Adiabatic Time Dependent HFB (ATDHFB) and Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) approaches for collective masses, while α-decay half-lives were calculated using the Viola-Seaborg formula. Fragment masses and charges were determined from two-fragment (2F) solutions at large quadrupole deformations by constraining on the necking operator.

## Key numbers

- Inner barrier heights B_I for ²⁵²Ra (N=164): 4.51 MeV (D1S), 3.85 MeV (D1N), 4.86 MeV (D1M), representing the minimal values across the isotopic chain
- Second barrier heights B_II for ²⁵²Ra: 6.90 MeV (D1S), 6.92 MeV (D1N), 8.21 MeV (D1M)
- First isomer excitation energies for ²⁴⁰Ra: 1.74 MeV (D1S), 3.23 MeV (D1N), 3.86 MeV (D1M)
- Two-neutron separation energies: 8.59 MeV (D1S), 9.42 MeV (D1N), 9.66 MeV (D1M) for ²³⁴Ra; 4.36 MeV (D1S), 5.48 MeV (D1N), 5.36 MeV (D1M) for ²⁶⁴Ra
- GCM spontaneous fission half-life for ²³⁴Ra with E₀=1.0 MeV: 5.492 × 10²⁵ s (D1S), 2.951 × 10³¹ s (D1N), 5.247 × 10³⁹ s (D1M)
- ATDHFB spontaneous fission half-life for ²³⁴Ra with E₀=1.0 MeV: 5.884 × 10³⁶ s (D1S), 2.513 × 10⁴³ s (D1N), 8.511 × 10⁵² s (D1M)
- Differences between GCM and ATDHFB predictions reach 20, 24, and 22 orders of magnitude for ²⁶⁴Ra with D1S, D1N, and D1M respectively (E₀=1.0 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous HFB fission studies on neutron-rich Uranium and Plutonium isotopes (Rodriguez-Guzman and Robledo, 2014) down to Z=88, examining whether similar features persist in Radium. The results agree with earlier Extended Thomas-Fermi results (Mamdouth) and macroscopic-microscopic calculations (Sierk, 2015) regarding the increase of inner barrier heights for neutron-rich isotopes. The systematic presence of second fission isomers (third minima) in the fission paths confirms findings from BCPM energy density functional studies (Robledo and Giuliani), Skyrme calculations (McDonnell et al., 2014), and relativistic mean-field approaches (Vretenar et al.), suggesting these shell effects are robust across mean-field frameworks. The predicted oblate lighter fragments challenge the usual assumption of prolate deformations in fission fragments from macroscopic-microscopic models (Moller et al.).
