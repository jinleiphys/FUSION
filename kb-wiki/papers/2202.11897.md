# Deep learning on nuclear mass and $α$ decay half-lives

> Ab-initio calculations of nuclear masses, the binding energy and the $α$ decay half-lives are intractable for heavy nucleus, because of the curse of dimensionality in many body quantum simulations as proton number($\mathrm{N}$) and neutron number($\mathrm{Z}$) grow. We take advantage of the powerful non-linear transformation and feature representation ability of deep neural network(DNN) to predict the nuclear masses and $α$ decay half-lives. For nuclear binding energy prediction problem we achieve standard deviation $σ=0.263$ MeV on 10-fold cross validation on 2149 nuclei. Word-vectors which are high dimensional representation of nuclei from the hidden layers of mass-regression DNN help us to calculate $α$ decay half-lives. For this task, we get $σ=0.797$ on 100 times 10-fold cross validation on 350 nuclei on $log_{10}T_{1/2}$ and $σ=0.731 $ on 486 nuclei. We also find physical a priori such as shell structure, magic numbers and augmented inputs inspired by Finite Range Droplet Model are important for this small data regression task.

## Key claim

A deep neural network can predict nuclear binding energies with a standard deviation of 0.263 MeV on 10-fold cross validation for 2149 nuclei, and the latent representations ("word-vectors") learned from this mass-prediction task can improve alpha decay half-life predictions compared to using only native features, but only when the mass residual includes microscopic (shell) structure rather than only macroscopic terms.

## Method

The authors trained a feedforward deep neural network with adjustable depth (n+2 hidden layers, with n ranging from 0 to 16) and width (m = 8, 64, 256, or 512 neurons per hidden layer) to predict the residual between experimental nuclear masses and the Liquid-Droplet Model (LDM) or Bethe-Weizsäcker model (BWM). For alpha decay half-life prediction, they used either 64 native features (Z, N, A for mother, daughter, and alpha particle, plus Q-value and odd-even indicators) or 517 features that included 256-dimensional word-vectors from the hidden layers of the mass-prediction network. Performance was evaluated using 100 repetitions of 10-fold cross validation.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear mass prediction: standard deviation sigma = 0.263 MeV on 10-fold cross validation for 2149 nuclei using 26 physics-informed features
- Nuclear mass prediction with only 3 native features (Z, N, A): sigma = 0.332 MeV on 10-fold cross validation
- Prediction for 322 new nuclei from AME2020 not used in training: RMS error = 0.605 MeV (compared to LDM RMS of 2.542 MeV)
- Global alpha decay half-life prediction on 350 nuclei using word-vectors: sigma = 0.797 on log10(T1/2) from 100 times 10-fold cross validation
- Global alpha decay half-life prediction on 486 nuclei using 64 native features: sigma = 0.731 on log10(T1/2) from 100 times 10-fold cross validation
- Even-even nuclei alpha decay half-life: DNN reduced residual of three-parameter Gamow formula from 0.3627 to 0.2297 on log10(T1/2) using 100 times 10-fold cross validation
- Q-value prediction from the mass network: RMS deviation = 0.15 MeV for 486 nuclei

## Context

This work extends prior machine learning approaches to nuclear mass prediction, including Bayesian Neural Networks (Niu, 2018; Moller, 2012), LightGBM (Gao, 2021), and earlier neural network attempts that achieved RMS deviations above 1 MeV (Yuksel, 2021). The authors note that the best performance from improved Weizsacker-Skyrme models reaches 0.336 MeV (WS-2), while LightGBM achieves around 0.170 MeV (Gao, 2021). The novel contribution is using learned latent representations from mass prediction to assist alpha decay half-life prediction, analogous to word-vectors in natural language processing. This enables future applications to beta decay, r-process calculations, and other nuclear physics tasks where experimental data are sparse.
