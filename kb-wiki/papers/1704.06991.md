# Anomalous Transport Properties of Dense QCD in a Magnetic Field

> Despite recent advancements in the study and understanding of the phase diagram of strongly interacting matter, the region of high baryonic densities and low temperatures has remained difficult to reach in the lab. Things are expected to change with the planned HIC experiments at FAIR in Germany and NICA in Russia, which will open a window to the high-density-low-temperature segment of the QCD phase map, providing a unique opportunity to test the validity of model calculations that have predicted the formation of spatially inhomogeneous phases with broken chiral symmetry at intermediate-to-high densities. Such a density region is also especially relevant for the physics of neutron stars, as they have cores that can have several times the nuclear saturation density. On the other hand, strong magnetic fields, whose presence is fairly common in HIC and in neutron stars, can affect the properties of these exotic phases and lead to signatures potentially observable in these two settings. In this paper, I examine the anomalous transport properties produced by the spectral asymmetry of the lowest Landau level (LLL) in a QCD-inspired NJL model with a background magnetic field that exhibits chiral symmetry breaking at high density via the formation of a Dual Chiral Density Wave (DCDW) condensate. It turns out that in this model the electromagnetic interactions are described by the axion electrodynamics equations and there is a dissipationless Hall current.

# Anomalous Transport Properties of Dense QCD in a Magnetic Field

## Key claim
In a QCD-inspired Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a background magnetic field that exhibits chiral symmetry breaking at high density via a Dual Chiral Density Wave (DCDW) condensate, the electromagnetic interactions are described by axion electrodynamics equations, producing a dissipationless Hall current and an anomalous electric charge; these effects arise specifically from the spectral asymmetry of the lowest Landau level (LLL) and are not canceled by ordinary tadpole contributions.

## Method
The author examines a three-color, two-flavor NJL model with a constant uniform magnetic field along the x3-direction, where the DCDW condensate takes the form of a density wave with magnitude Δ and modulation vector q along the field direction. A local chiral transformation eliminates the spatially dependent mass term, revealing that the fermion measure in the path integral produces an axion term κ/4 θ Fμν F̃μν, which is regularized following Fujikawa's approach. The effective electromagnetic action is then expanded to obtain the linear equations of motion, which take the form of axion electrodynamics.

## Key numbers
- The anomalous baryon (quark) number density when q < 2m is ρ^A_B = 3|e|/(4π²) qB, where q is the modulation vector and B is the magnetic field strength.
- The anomalous electric charge density is J^{anom}_0 = e²/(4π²) qB, obtained by multiplying the anomalous quark number density of each flavor by its electric charge and summing over flavors.
- The Hall conductance in the magnetic DCDW phase is σ_H = e²q/(4π²), which is not quantized (unlike in topological insulators).
- The inhomogeneity length scale of the DCDW phase is Δx = ħ/q ∼ 0.6 fm for q ∼ μ = 300 MeV, much smaller than the characteristic scale L ∼ 10 fm of the quark-gluon plasma at RHIC, NICA, and FAIR.
- The anomalous Hall current is J^{anom} = -e²/(4π²) q × E, which is perpendicular to both the electric field E and the condensate modulation q (parallel to B).

## Context
This work extends studies of inhomogeneous chiral condensates in NJL models (reviewed in InhCRev) and builds on prior calculations showing that the DCDW phase is favored in a magnetic field (Klimenko (2010) and de la Incera (2015)). The results connect to the physics of quarkyonic chiral spirals (Kojo et al. (2010)) and double quarkyonic chiral spirals (Tatara et al. (2015)) at zero and nonzero magnetic field, respectively. The anomalous Hall current could provide observable signatures in future heavy-ion collision experiments at FAIR in Germany and NICA in Russia, as well as in neutron star cores, where the axion polariton mode (gapped proportionally to the background magnetic field) could attenuate certain light frequencies and serve as a probe of the magnetic DCDW phase.
