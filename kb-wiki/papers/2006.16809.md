# Merging of transport theory with TDHF: multinucleon transfer in U+U collisions

> Multinucleon transfer mechanism in the collision of ${}^{238}\text{U}+{}^{238}\text{U}$ system is investigated at $E_\text{c.m.} =833$ MeV in the framework of the quantal diffusion description based on the stochastic mean-field approach (SMF). Double cross-sections $σ(N,Z)$ as a function of the neutron and proton numbers, the cross-sections $σ(Z)$ and $σ(A)$ as a function of the atomic numbers and the mass numbers are calculated for production of the primary fragments. The calculation indicates the ${}^{238} \text{U}+{}^{238} \text{U}$ system may be located at an unstable equilibrium state at the potential energy surface with a slightly negative curvature along the beta stability line on the $(N,Z)-$plane. This behavior may lead to rather large diffusion along the beta stability direction.

## Key claim

The authors claim that the symmetric \({}^{238}\text{U}+{}^{238}\text{U}\) system may be located at an unstable equilibrium state on the potential energy surface, with a slightly negative curvature along the beta stability line, which could lead to rather large diffusion along that direction. This claim is conditional on the estimated value of the reduced iso-scalar curvature parameter \(\beta = -0.02\), which was determined by fitting to experimental cross-section data for gold isotope production from a previous investigation.

## Method

The authors employ the quantal diffusion description based on the stochastic mean-field (SMF) approach, merging transport theory with time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations using the SLy4d Skyrme functional. They solve coupled Langevin equations for the neutron and proton numbers of the projectile-like fragment, linearized around the mean-field TDHF trajectories, and compute quantal diffusion coefficients from the occupied single-particle wave functions. The dispersions are obtained by solving coupled differential equations for the neutron, proton, and mixed variances, with drift coefficient derivatives determined from a parabolic parameterization of the potential energy surface using curvature parameters \(\alpha = 0.13\) (iso-vector) and \(\beta = -0.02\) (iso-scalar). Cross-sections are then calculated by summing over initial orbital angular momenta \(\ell = (100-460)\hbar\) for two collision geometries (tip-tip and side-side).

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energy: \(E_\text{c.m.} = 833\) MeV for the \({}^{238}\text{U}+{}^{238}\text{U}\) system.
- Reduced iso-vector curvature parameter: \(\alpha = 0.13\); reduced iso-scalar curvature parameter: \(\beta = -0.02\).
- For tip-tip geometry at \(\ell = 300\hbar\): asymptotic neutron dispersion \(\sigma_{NN} = 13.6\), proton dispersion \(\sigma_{ZZ} = 6.32\), mixed dispersion \(\sigma_{NZ} = 8.71\), mass dispersion \(\sigma_{AA} = 19.4\).
- For side-side geometry at \(\ell = 300\hbar\): asymptotic neutron dispersion \(\sigma_{NN} = 7.75\), proton dispersion \(\sigma_{ZZ} = 3.91\), mixed dispersion \(\sigma_{NZ} = 4.53\), mass dispersion \(\sigma_{AA} = 10.8\).
- Range of initial orbital angular momentum considered: \(\ell = (100-460)\hbar\).
- Memory time estimated as \(\tau_T = \tau_P \approx 25\) fm/c, much shorter than the contact time of about 600 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the stochastic mean-field approach developed by Ayik et al. (2017, 2018, 2019) and Yilmaz et al. (2018, 2020) to the symmetric \({}^{238}\text{U}+{}^{238}\text{U}\) system, merging transport theory with TDHF to overcome the well-known shortcoming of mean-field theory that severely underestimates fragment mass and charge distributions, as demonstrated by Simenel (2010) and Sekizawa (2016) using particle number projection. The approach enables calculation of primary fragment cross-sections for multinucleon transfer in massive systems, and the results can be compared with experimental data such as those from Kratz et al. (2013) for gold isotope production, potentially guiding future experiments aimed at synthesizing neutron-rich heavy nuclei near the Coulomb barrier.
