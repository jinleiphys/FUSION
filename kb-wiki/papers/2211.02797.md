# Optimization of generator coordinate method with machine-learning techniques for nuclear spectra and neutrinoless double-beta decay: ridge regression for nuclei with axial deformation

> The generator coordinate method (GCM) is an important tool of choice for modeling large-amplitude collective motion in atomic nuclei. The computational complexity of the GCM increases rapidly with the number of collective coordinates. It imposes a strong restriction on the applicability of the method. In this work, we propose a subspace-reduction algorithm that employs optimal statistical ML models as surrogates for exact quantum-number projection calculations for norm and Hamiltonian kernels. The model space of the original GCM is reduced to a subspace relevant for nuclear low energy spectra and the NME of ground state to ground state $0νββ$ decay based on the orthogonality condition (OC) and the energy-transition-orthogonality procedure (ENTROP), respectively. For simplicity, the polynomial ridge regression (RR) algorithm is used to learn the norm and Hamiltonian kernels of axially deformed configurations. The efficiency and accuracy of this algorithm are illustrated for 76Ge and 76Se by comparing results obtained using the optimal RR models to direct GCM calculations. The low-lying energy spectra of $^{76}$Ge and $^{76}$Se, as well as the $0νββ$-decay NME between their ground states, are computed. The results show that the performance of the GCM+OC/ENTROP+RR is more robust than that of the GCM+RR alone, and the former can reproduce the results of the original GCM calculation accurately with a significantly reduced computational cost.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that combining the generator coordinate method (GCM) with polynomial ridge regression (RR) and either the orthogonality condition (OC) or the energy-transition-orthogonality procedure (ENTROP) can reproduce full GCM results for nuclear spectra and neutrinoless double-beta decay matrix elements with significantly reduced computational cost, but only when the subspace reduction is performed using the OC or ENTROP methods rather than using RR alone.

## Method
The authors used polynomial ridge regression to learn the norm and Hamiltonian kernels of axially deformed nuclear configurations, with the logarithm of the norm kernels as the training target. They then applied either the orthogonality condition (OC) method for low-lying spectra or the ENTROP algorithm for neutrinoless double-beta decay matrix elements to select a reduced subspace of configurations, and performed exact quantum-number projected GCM calculations within that subspace. The method was tested on 76Ge and 76Se using the Gogny D1S energy density functional, the relativistic PC-PK1 functional, a valence-space shell-model Hamiltonian, and a chiral effective field theory interaction.

## Key numbers
- Computation time reduced by a factor of three for energy spectra and a factor of nine for neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements, when using the GCM+OC/ENTROP+RR approach compared to full GCM.
- The training set comprised approximately 1/4 of the total norm and Hamiltonian kernels (those equally distributed with step size of 2Δβ or 4Δβ in the deformation space).
- The polynomial ridge regression used a polynomial degree N as a hyperparameter, with the number of features given by (N+2)(N+1)/2.

## Context
This work extends prior GCM studies that used the orthogonality condition (OC) method and the ENTROP algorithm (Romero 2021) for subspace selection, and applies machine-learning techniques to accelerate these calculations. The approach is related to eigenvector continuation methods (Frame 2018, Sarkar 2021) used for emulating many-body calculations with control parameters. The paper enables more efficient GCM calculations for medium-mass and heavy deformed nuclei, particularly for neutrinoless double-beta decay candidates, and is expected to become more advantageous as the number of generator coordinates increases.
