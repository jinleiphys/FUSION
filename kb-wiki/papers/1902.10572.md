# Exposing Novel Quark and Gluon Effects in Nuclei

> The fundamental theory of the strong interaction -- quantum chromodynamics (QCD) -- provides the foundational framework with which to describe and understand the key properties of atomic nuclei. A deep understanding of the explicit role of quarks and gluons in nuclei remains elusive however, as these effects have thus far been well-disguised by confinement effects in QCD which are encapsulated by a successful description in terms of effective hadronic degrees of freedom. The observation of the EMC effect has provided an enduring indication for explicit QCD effects in nuclei, and points to the medium modification of the bound protons and neutrons in the nuclear medium. Understanding the EMC effect is a major challenge for modern nuclear physics, and several key questions remain, such as understanding its flavor, spin, and momentum dependence. This manuscript provides a contemporary snapshot of our understanding of the role of QCD in nuclei and outlines possible pathways in experiment and theory that will help deepen our understanding of nuclei in the context of QCD.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the EMC effect provides enduring evidence for quark and gluon effects in nuclei, a complete understanding remains elusive because confinement effects are well-described by effective hadronic degrees of freedom; the authors present a roadmap for future experiments and theory to expose these QCD effects, particularly emphasizing that the EMC effect may be driven by local nuclear environment and short-range correlations rather than bulk nuclear density.

## Method

The paper reviews the current experimental and theoretical status of nuclear QCD effects, synthesizing results from deep inelastic scattering (DIS), Drell-Yan processes, deeply virtual Compton scattering (DVCS), and spectator tagging experiments. It outlines specific future measurements at Jefferson Lab (JLab) and proposed electron-ion colliders, including inclusive and exclusive reactions on light nuclei (deuteron, helium-3, helium-4), Drell-Yan with pion beams, and nuclear generalized parton distribution (GPD) studies. The authors also discuss theoretical frameworks such as the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, light-front spectral functions, and plane wave impulse approximation calculations with realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials.

## Key numbers

- The minimum Q^2 value for observing the x>1 plateau in A/D cross-section ratios is taken to be about 1.4 GeV^2, illustrated with data from Egiyan (2003)
- High-momentum nucleons in inclusive x>1 scattering are defined as those with momenta greater than or equal to 300 MeV/c, about a few tens of MeV greater than the Fermi momentum for nuclei with A>12
- The BoNuS experiment at JLab measured spectator tagging on the deuteron at low spectator momenta (Baillie, 2011)
- The DEEPS experiment at JLab measured spectator tagging at high spectator momenta (Klimenko, 2005)
- Pion beam incident momenta between 100 and 200 GeV/c are discussed for Drell-Yan experiments at CERN
- The HERMES experiment provided the only existing measurement of the b_1 structure function on the deuteron (Airapetian, 2005)

## Context

This white paper extends the ongoing investigation of the EMC effect first observed by the European Muon Collaboration (Aubert, 1983) and builds on nuclear parton distribution function studies from experiments such as E772 (Alde, 1990), E866 (Vasilev, 1999), and SeaQuest at Fermilab. It enables future experimental programs including JLab experiments E12-10-008 (EMC effect), E12-06-105 (x>1 scattering), E12-10-103 (MARATHON on mirror nuclei), and E12-14-011 (short-range correlations in tritium and helium-3), as well as the proposed ALERT detector project for nuclear DVCS measurements. The paper also identifies open questions regarding the flavor, spin, and momentum dependence of the EMC effect, and the need for realistic calculations of nuclear GPDs using state-of-the-art nucleon-nucleon potentials.
