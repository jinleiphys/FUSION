# Heavy Quark Energy Loss in the Hybrid Model

> Heavy quarks offer an invaluable hard probe of the droplets of quark gluon plasma (QGP) formed in heavy ion collisions at the LHC and RHIC. Given their large mass, they are predominantly produced in hard scattering processes at the earliest moment of a collision and given their rarity they almost never annihilate with a heavy antiquark subsequently. This means that they experience, and probe, the entire history of the expanding, cooling, droplet of QGP from hydrodynamization through hadronization. Quantitative measurements of heavy quark final state observables therefore give us access to information about the transport properties of QGP as well as about medium modifications of hadronization. To date, the Hybrid strong/weak coupling Model of jet quenching has not included any implementation of the heavy-quark sector, which has made it impossible to confront its predictions with measurements of heavy quark and jet observables together, in a unified fashion. Here, we extend the Hybrid Model to investigate heavy quark observables for the first time. We introduce a strongly-coupled calculation of heavy-quark energy loss with the correct behavior when the heavy quarks are either ultrarelativistic or non-relativistic, Gaussian momentum broadening, and recombination of heavy quarks with medium partons using a local color neutralization model of hadronization. We compare our results for the suppression $R_{\rm AA}$ and azimuthal anisotropies $v_2$ of B- and D-mesons and $Λ_c$ baryons, the $R_{\rm AA}$ of B-tagged jets, as well as baryon-to-meson ratios, with available experimental data from ALICE, ATLAS and CMS.

## Key claim

The authors extend the Hybrid Model of jet quenching to include heavy quarks for the first time, introducing a composite energy loss formula that interpolates between the ultrarelativistic and non-relativistic limits, but they acknowledge that this composite formula is not derived from first principles and that future holographic calculations could yield a fully unified treatment.

## Method

The authors embed heavy quark production from perturbative QCD (using FONLL spectra) into the Hybrid Model framework, which couples PYTHIA 8 parton showers with a relativistic viscous hydrodynamic background from Shen (2014). They introduce a composite energy loss formula for heavy quarks that matches the holographic result for massless quarks at high energy and the drag force result for infinitely massive quarks at low energy, with Gaussian momentum broadening tied to the energy loss rate via the Einstein relation. Heavy quark hadronization is treated via two distinct mechanisms: Lund string hadronization in PYTHIA for quarks that hadronize with shower partons, and the Local Color Neutralization model for quarks that recombine with light partons from the flowing QGP.

## Key numbers

* The stopping distance for light quarks in strongly coupled plasma is given by $x_{\rm stop} = (1/2\kappa_{\rm sc})(E_{\rm in}/T^4)^{1/3}$, with $\kappa_{\rm sc}=0.404$ obtained from fits to jet and high-$p_T$ hadron suppression data
* The drag coefficient for infinitely massive quarks is $\eta_D = (\kappa_{\rm HQ}/2)(T^2/M)$, where $\kappa_{\rm HQ}$ is treated as a free parameter expected to be smaller than the ${\cal N}=4$ SYM value $\kappa_{\rm HQ}^{{\cal N}=4}=\pi\sqrt{\lambda}$
* The gluon stopping distance is reduced by a factor $(C_A/C_F)^{1/3} = (9/4)^{1/3}$ compared to quarks, giving $\kappa_{\rm gluon} = (9/4)^{1/3}\kappa_{\rm sc}$
* Energy loss is applied only where the hydrodynamic temperature $T > 145$ MeV, after which no further energy loss is assumed

## Context

This work extends the Hybrid Model developed by Casalderrey-Solana et al. (2014-2019), which previously treated all partons as massless, to now include heavy quarks. It builds upon holographic calculations of heavy quark energy loss by Herzog (2006) and Gubser (2006) for the non-relativistic regime, and by Chesler (2014, 2015) for the ultrarelativistic regime. The Local Color Neutralization model for hadronization was introduced by Beraudo (2022, 2023). This unified framework enables simultaneous comparison with heavy quark and jet observables from ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS, which was previously impossible with the Hybrid Model.
