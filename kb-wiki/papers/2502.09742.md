# Experimental Search for the Chiral Magnetic Effect in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions: A Perspective

> The chiral magnetic effect (CME) refers to generation of the electric current along a magnetic field in a chirally imbalanced system of quarks. The latter is predicted by quantum chromodynamics to arise from quark interaction with non-trivial topological fluctuations of the vacuum gluonic field. The CME has been actively searched for in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where such gluonic field fluctuations and a strong magnetic field are believed to be present. The CME-sensitive observables are unfortunately subject to a possibly large non-CME background, and firm conclusions on a CME observation have not yet been reached. In this perspective, we review the experimental status and progress in the CME search, from the initial measurements more than a decade ago, to the dedicated program of isobar collisions in 2018 and the release of the isobar blind analysis result in 2022, to intriguing hints of a possible CME signal in Au+Au collisions, and discuss future prospects of a potential CME discovery in the anticipated high-statistic Au+Au collision data at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider by 2025. We hope such a perspective will help sharpening our focus on the fundamental physics of the CME and steer its experimental search.

# Experimental Search for the Chiral Magnetic Effect in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions: A Perspective

## Key claim

The chiral magnetic effect (CME) has not yet been definitively observed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, despite over a decade of experimental searches, because the CME-sensitive observables are subject to possibly large non-CME background contributions that cannot be rigorously separated with current techniques. The authors suggest that a potential CME discovery may be possible with high-statistics Au+Au collision data expected at RHIC by 2025, but only if the background can be sufficiently controlled.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental measurements of the charge-dependent three-point azimuthal correlator gamma (gamma correlator) and its difference Delta-gamma between opposite-sign and same-sign particle pairs, measured relative to the reaction plane or event plane in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. The authors examine multiple approaches to disentangle CME signals from background, including measurements with higher harmonic planes, event-shape selection techniques, pair invariant mass analysis, comparisons between small and large collision systems, and the dedicated isobar collision program (Ru+Ru versus Zr+Zr) with blind analysis released in 2022.

## Key numbers

- The first STAR measurements in Au+Au and Cu+Cu collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV showed a clear difference between opposite-sign and same-sign gamma correlators, consistent with CME expectations on top of a common negative background
- At lower RHIC energies from the Beam Energy Scan phase-I, differences between opposite-sign and same-sign gamma correlators were observed at all collision energies except the two lowest energies of 11.5 and 7.7 GeV
- ALICE measured the gamma correlator in Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, finding similar trends and magnitudes to STAR measurements at RHIC, despite differences in multiplicities, magnetic field lifetimes, and elliptic flow values
- Transport model calculations could explain only about 1/3 of the observed signal, while Blast-Wave calculations with local charge conservation could reproduce the STAR measurements with reasonable parameters
- Measurements in small systems (p+Pb, p+Au, d+Au) showed large differences between opposite-sign and same-sign gamma correlators comparable to those in peripheral heavy-ion collisions, but these are dominated by reaction-plane-independent three-particle correlations
- The most precise techniques allow estimation of the CME fraction with an accuracy of a few percent

## Context

This perspective reviews the experimental search for the chiral magnetic effect from the initial STAR measurements in 2009 through the isobar blind analysis result released in 2022, extending earlier reviews by Kharzeev (2013, 2015), Zhao (2018, 2019), and others. The paper discusses how the Blast-Wave model calculations by Schlichting (2010) and Wu (2022) incorporating local charge conservation remain the strongest candidate for background description, while noting that event-shape selection methods give conflicting results across different models (AVFD, AMPT, EPOS, HYDJET) in the absence of CME signals. The anticipated high-statistics Au+Au data at RHIC by 2025 may enable a potential CME discovery if background control methods can be sufficiently refined.
