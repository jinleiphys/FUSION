# The physics of dense hadronic matter and compact stars

> This review describes the properties of hadronic phases of dense matter in compact stars. The theory is developed within the method of real-time Green's functions and is applied to study of baryonic matter at and above the saturation density. The non-relativistic and covariant theories based on continuum Green's functions and the T-matrix and related approximations to the self-energies are reviewed. The effects of symmetry energy, onset of hyperons and meson condensation on the properties of stellar configurations are demonstrated on specific examples. Neutrino interactions with baryonic matter are introduced within a kinetic theory. We concentrate on the classification, analysis and first principle derivation of neutrino radiation processes from unpaired and superfluid hadronic phases. We then demonstrate how neutrino radiation rates from various microscopic processes affect the macroscopic cooling of neutron stars and how the observed X-ray fluxes from pulsars constrain the properties of dense hadronic matter.

## Key claim

This review argues that the properties of dense hadronic matter in compact stars can be systematically described using real-time Green's functions and T-matrix theory, and that confronting theoretical cooling models with observed X-ray fluxes from pulsars provides constraints on dense matter properties, though the actual state of matter above 2-3 times nuclear saturation density remains unknown.

## Method

The author develops the theory within the framework of continuum Green's function technique at finite temperature and density, using the Kadanoff-Baym transport equations derived from the Schwinger-Keldysh contour formalism. The non-relativistic and covariant T-matrix (ladder) approximations to the self-energies are reviewed, including extensions to superfluid phases and three-body correlations. Neutrino interactions with baryonic matter are introduced within a kinetic theory framework, and the derived neutrino emissivities are applied to cooling simulations of neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- The density range covered for uniform hadronic phases is ρ₀ ≤ ρ ≤ 3ρ₀, where ρ₀ = 2.8 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ is the nuclear saturation density
- The low-density region of the star has densities about ρ = 10⁶ g cm⁻³ composed of electrons and ions of ⁵⁶Fe
- In the density range 10⁷ ≤ ρ ≤ 10¹¹ g cm⁻³, a typical sequence of stable nuclei includes ⁶²Ni, ⁸⁶Kr, ⁸⁴Se, ⁸²Ge, ⁸⁰Zn, ¹²⁴Mo, ¹²²Zr, ¹²⁰Sr and their neutron rich isotopes
- Above ρ ≃ 4 × 10¹¹ g cm⁻³, free neutrons form a continuum with a positive chemical potential
- The inner crust contains neutron rich isotopes of Zr and Sn with proton numbers Z=40 and 50 respectively and mass numbers in the range 100 ≤ A ≤ 1500
- Below critical temperature T_c ∼ 10¹⁰ K (corresponding to 1 MeV), neutrons in the continuum undergo a superfluid phase transition
- At about half the nuclear saturation density (ρ₀/2 = 1.4 × 10¹⁴ g cm⁻³), clusters merge into uniform fluid
- The proton, electron and muon abundances in uniform npeμ-phase are in the range 5-10%
- Neutrons and protons condense in superfluid states below critical temperatures of order 10⁹ K
- Densities reached in the center of a massive NS are about 10 × ρ₀
- At densities ρ ≥ 2ρ₀, Bose-Einstein condensates of pions, kaons, and heavier mesons can arise
- The free-space nuclear interactions are fitted to reproduce experimental phase-shifts below laboratory energies 350 MeV

## Context

This review extends the many-body theory of dense matter in neutron stars developed over four decades since the discovery of pulsars, building on the real-time Green's function formalism established by Schwinger (1961), Kadanoff and Baym (1962), and Keldysh (1964). It connects to comprehensive textbooks by Shapiro and Teukolsky, Zel'dovich and Novikov, and monographs by Glendenning, Weber, and Saakyan, as well as specialized reviews on strangeness in compact stars by Weber, cooling of neutron stars by Page and by Pethick, neutrino propagation by Volkas, quark matter phases by Rischke, Alford, and Rajagopal, and equations of state by Baldo, Lattimer, and Prakash. The work enables future confrontation of theoretical cooling models with observations from Chandra, XMM-Newton, and RXTE X-ray satellites, and anticipates constraints from gravitational wave observatories VIRGO, LIGO, GEO, TAMA, and the future LISA mission.
