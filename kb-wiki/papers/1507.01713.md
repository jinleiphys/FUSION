# Thermal conductivity of hot pionic medium due to pion self-energy for $πσ$ and $πρ$ loops

> The thermal conductivity of pionic medium has been evaluated with the help of its standard expression from the relaxation time approximation, where inverse of pion relaxation time or pion thermal width has been obtained from the imaginary part of pion self-energy. In the real-time formalism of thermal field theory, the finite temperature calculations of pion self-energy for $πσ$ and $πρ$ loops have been done. The numerical value of our thermal conductivity increases with temperature very softly, though at particular temperature, our estimation has to consider a large band of phenomenological uncertainty.

## Key claim

The thermal conductivity of a hot pionic medium increases very softly with temperature, but the estimation carries a large band of phenomenological uncertainty due to the choice of sigma meson mass and width parameters and the inclusion of a hadronic form factor.

## Method

The thermal conductivity was calculated using the relaxation time approximation, where the inverse pion relaxation time (thermal width) was obtained from the imaginary part of the pion self-energy. The self-energy was computed in the real-time formalism of thermal field theory for πσ and πρ loops, using effective Lagrangian densities with coupling constants fixed from experimental decay widths of σ and ρ mesons. A hadronic form factor with cutoff Λ = 1 GeV was applied to the vertices, and the thermal width was folded with the vacuum spectral functions of the σ and ρ resonances.

## Key numbers

- At T = 0.15 GeV, the thermal conductivity κ = 0.0086–0.013 GeV² when considering the form factor effect alone.
- At T = 0.15 GeV, including the uncertainty from σ meson parameters and the form factor, κ = 0.0086–0.16 GeV².
- The coupling constants used were gσ = 5.82 and gρ = 6, fixed from experimental decay widths.
- The hadronic form factor cutoff was Λ = 1 GeV.
- The temperature range considered was approximately T = 0.12–0.17 GeV, corresponding to the hadronic temperature domain near the transition temperature.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of thermal conductivity for strongly interacting matter, which collectively exhibit a band of predictions as shown in Table 1 of the paper, including results from Gavin (1990), Greiner (2005), Sarkar (2009), Nicola (2012), Prakash (1993), Davesne (1996), Nam (2013), and Juan (2014). The calculation uses the same effective hadronic model and folding procedure previously applied by Ghosh (2013) to compute shear viscosity. The results enable inclusion of thermal conductivity in hydrodynamic simulations of the expanding fireball at forward rapidities or lower collision energies, such as at FAIR and low-energy RHIC runs, where net baryon density is non-zero.
