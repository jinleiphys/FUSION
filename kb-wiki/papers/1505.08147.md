# Quark Nova Model for Fast Radio Bursts

> FRBs are puzzling, millisecond, energetic radio transients with no discernible source; observations show no counterparts in other frequency bands. The birth of a quark star from a parent neutron star experiencing a quark nova - previously thought undetectable when born in isolation - provides a natural explanation for the emission characteristics of FRBs. The generation of unstable r-process elements in the quark nova ejecta provides millisecond exponential injection of electrons into the surrounding strong magnetic field at the parent neutron star's light cylinder via $β$-decay. This radio synchrotron emission has a total duration of hundreds of milliseconds and matches the observed spectrum while reducing the inferred dispersion measure by approximately 200 cm$^{-3}$ pc. The model allows indirect measurement of neutron star magnetic fields and periods in addition to providing astronomical measurements of $β$-decay chains of unstable neutron rich nuclei. Using this model, we can calculate expected FRB average energies ($\sim$ 10$^{41}$ ergs), spectra shapes and provide a theoretical framework for determining distances.

## Key claim

The Quark Nova model explains Fast Radio Bursts as synchrotron emission from electrons injected by beta-decay of unstable r-process nuclei in the ejecta of a quark star birth, but the observed millisecond duration is an artifact of the de-dispersion process that stacks frequency channels; the intrinsic emission lasts several seconds.

## Method

The authors model the explosive phase transition of a neutron star to a quark star (Quark Nova), which ejects neutron-rich crust material that undergoes r-process nucleosynthesis with fission recycling, maintaining a stationary distribution of unstable nuclei. When this relativistic ejecta crosses the light cylinder of the parent neutron star's magnetic field, decompression triggers rapid beta-decay chains that inject MeV electrons into the magnetic field, producing radio synchrotron emission. The emission spectrum is computed by integrating over latitude angles at the light cylinder, accounting for the time delay of arrival at different latitudes and the magnetic field geometry (dipolar inside 30 degrees, monopolar beyond).

## Key numbers

- Average FRB energy predicted: approximately 10^41 ergs
- Dispersion measure reduction for FRB 110220: from 944 cm^-3 pc to 725 cm^-3 pc
- Parent neutron star magnetic field used in fits: 10^12 G
- Parent neutron star period used in fits: 10 s
- Ejecta mass used in fits: 10^-3 solar masses
- Ejecta velocity: 0.99c
- Light cylinder magnetic field for a 10 s period and 10^12 G neutron star: approximately 300 G
- Maximum frequency equation: nu_max = kappa_nu * gamma^2 * B_0 * (2*pi*r_0*cos(phi_cut)/(c*P))^2
- Predicted FRB rate: approximately 0.0028 per year per galaxy (consistent across three estimation methods)
- Fraction of neutron stars as Quark Nova candidates: approximately 14%

## Context

This work extends the Quark Nova model previously developed by Ouyed (2002), Staff (2006), and others, which predicted r-process nucleosynthesis in quark nova ejecta (Jaikumar 2007, r-Java simulations). It provides an alternative to other FRB explanations such as blitzars, neutron star mergers, magnetars (various authors 2007-2015), asteroid collisions with neutron stars, and dark matter induced collapse. The model enables indirect measurement of neutron star magnetic fields and periods from FRB observations, and provides astronomical measurements of beta-decay chains of unstable neutron-rich nuclei.
