# A Quantum Annealing Protocol to Solve the Nuclear Shell Model

> The nuclear shell model accurately describes the structure and dynamics of atomic nuclei. However, the exponential scaling of the basis size with the number of degrees of freedom hampers a direct numerical solution for heavy nuclei. In this work, we present a quantum annealing protocol to obtain nuclear ground states. We propose a tailored driver Hamiltonian that preserves a large gap and validate our approach in a dozen nuclei with basis sizes up to $10^5$ using classical simulations of the annealing evolution. We explore the relation between the spectral gap and the total time of the annealing protocol, assessing its accuracy by comparing the fidelity and energy relative error to classical benchmarks. While the nuclear Hamiltonian is non-local and thus challenging to implement in current setups, the estimated computational cost of our annealing protocol on quantum circuits is polynomial in the single particle basis size, paving the way to study heavier nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper presents a quantum annealing protocol for solving the nuclear shell model that achieves ground-state energies with relative errors below 10⁻⁴ for a dozen nuclei with basis sizes up to 10⁵, but the nuclear Hamiltonian is non-local and thus challenging to implement in current quantum computing setups, with the estimated computational cost on quantum circuits being polynomial in the single-particle basis size rather than exponential.

## Method

The authors simulate a quantum annealing protocol using a time-dependent Hamiltonian that interpolates linearly between a tailored driver Hamiltonian and the target nuclear shell model Hamiltonian, evolving the system from t=0 to t=τ with uniform time steps Δtω=0.1. They decompose the unitary time evolution operator using sparse matrix exponentiation from the Scipy library, computing instantaneous energy levels and eigenstates via exact diagonalization for nuclei in the p shell (using the Cohen-Kurath interaction) and sd shell (using the USDB interaction). The driver Hamiltonian is constructed from the bitstring corresponding to the Slater determinant that occupies the lowest-energy single-particle orbitals, with degenerate orbitals occupied starting from the highest |m|, and is diagonal in both the single-particle and many-body bases while not commuting with the target Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- For p-shell nuclei at τω=30, relative energy errors range from 8×10⁻⁶ to 2×10⁻⁴ and infidelities from 6×10⁻⁷ to 10⁻³
- For sd-shell nuclei at τω=30, all nuclei reach infidelity 1-F(τ) < 2×10⁻²
- ²²O at τω=30 achieves Δ_re(τ) ∼ 5×10⁻⁶ and 1-F(τ) ∼ 5×10⁻⁵
- ²⁸Si at τω=30 achieves Δ_re(τ) ∼ 2×10⁻⁵ and 1-F(τ) ∼ 3×10⁻⁴, despite having the largest many-body basis among all considered nuclei with dim F₀ ≈ 10⁵
- The minimum spectral gap Δ is of order 2-5 MeV for the three nuclei studied in detail (⁸Be, ²⁰Ne, ²⁸Si)
- The time τ* needed to reach fidelity F=0.99 follows the theoretical prediction τ ∝ Δ⁻²

## Context

This work extends previous quantum computing approaches to the nuclear shell model, including ADAPT-VQE algorithms applied by Grimsley (2019) and Perez-Obiol (2023), by providing an alternative method that avoids classical optimization and its associated local minima problems. The results enable future studies of heavier nuclei by demonstrating that the computational cost of implementing the annealing protocol on quantum circuits scales polynomially with the single-particle basis size, and the protocol achieves best accuracy for mid-shell nuclei like ²⁸Si that are most challenging for classical diagonalization.
