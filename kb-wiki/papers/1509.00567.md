# Neutron Capture Reactions near N=82 Shell-Closure

> Neutron capture cross-sections have been calculated in nuclei near the N=82 neutron shell closure. These nuclei are of astrophysical interest, participating in s-process and p-process. A semi-microscopic optical model have been used with the potential being obtained through folding the target density with the DDM3Y nucleon nucleon interaction. Theoretical density values have been calculated in the relativistic mean field approach. The calculated cross-section, as a function of neutron energy, agree reasonably well with experimental measurements. Maxwellian averaged cross-sections, important for astrophysical processes, have been calculated.

## Key claim

Neutron capture cross-sections calculated using a semi-microscopic optical model with densities from relativistic mean field theory agree reasonably well with experimental measurements for nuclei near the N=82 shell closure, though agreement becomes poorer for isotopes further from the closed shell, possibly due to deformation effects not captured by the spherical model.

## Method

The authors calculated nuclear densities in coordinate space using the relativistic mean field (RMF) approach with the FSU Gold Lagrangian density, then folded these densities with the DDM3Y nucleon-nucleon interaction to obtain the optical model potential. The imaginary part of the potential was taken to be identical to the real part. Neutron capture cross-sections were computed using the TALYS1.6 code with Hauser-Feshbach calculations including width fluctuation corrections, microscopic level densities from Goriely, and E1 gamma-strength functions from Goriely's hybrid model.

## Context

This work extends the semi-microscopic approach previously applied to proton capture reactions by Chakraborty et al. (2015) to neutron capture reactions near the N=82 shell closure, which is a bottleneck region for the s-process. The calculated MACS values generally show better agreement with experimental data from the KADONIS database than the MOST Hauser-Feshbach calculations, which derive all nuclear inputs from global microscopic models. The results provide theoretical cross-sections for unstable nuclei such as $^{134,136}$Cs, $^{141}$Ce, $^{147}$Nd, and $^{147,148}$Pm that are important for determining s-process branching ratios but lack experimental measurements, and enable MACS calculations at multiple thermal energies needed for modern stellar model calculations.
