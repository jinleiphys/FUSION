# Finite size effects on the phase diagram of the thermodynamical cluster model

> The thermodynamical cluster model is known to present a first-order liquid-gas phase transition in the idealized case of an uncharged, infinitely extended medium. However, in most practical applications of this model, the system is finite and charged. In this paper we study how the phase diagram is modified by finite size and Coulomb effects. We show that the thermodynamic anomalies which are associated to the finite system counterpart of first order phase transitions, are correctly reproduced by this effective model. However, approximations in the calculation of the grandcanonical partition sum prevent obtaining the exact mapping between statistical ensembles which should be associated to finite systems. The ensemble inequivalence associated to the transition persists in the presence of Coulomb, but the phase diagram is deeply modified with respect to the simple liquid-gas phase transition characteristic of the neutral system.

## Key claim
The thermodynamical cluster model reproduces the thermodynamic anomalies associated with first-order phase transitions in finite systems, but approximations in the grandcanonical partition sum prevent exact mapping between statistical ensembles; the phase diagram is deeply modified by Coulomb effects compared to the simple liquid-gas transition of the neutral system.

## Method
The authors compute the canonical partition function via a recursion relation for a system of incompressible clusters with a volume and surface free energy parameterized to reproduce nuclear phenomenology. They extract the phase diagram by analyzing bimodality in the grandcanonical particle number distribution and by applying an equal-area Maxwell construction to the canonical chemical potential and pressure. For charged systems, the Coulomb energy is included via the Wigner-Seitz approximation.

## Key numbers
- The volume term in the cluster free energy uses parameters \(W_0 = 15.8\) MeV and \(\epsilon_0 = 16.0\) MeV; the surface tension is \(\sigma_0 = 18.0\) MeV with \(\beta_c = 1/18.0\) MeV\(^{-1}\).
- For a volume \(V = 5 V_0(200)\) (where \(V_0(200)\) is the volume of a nucleus with 200 particles at normal density), the maximum particle number is \(N_{\text{max}}(V) = 1000\).
- At \(T = 6\) MeV, the canonical chemical potential backbends and the equal-area construction defines the coexistence region; the transition chemical potential increases with decreasing volume.
- For a system of \(N = 200\) neutral particles, the canonical pressure isotherms show negative compressibility at higher temperatures, with the coexistence zone defined by an equal-area construction on the \(P(V)\) curve.

## Context
This work extends the thermodynamical cluster model to finite systems, building on prior studies of phase transitions in finite systems such as the exact solvable models of Chomaz et al. (2001) and the lattice gas model of Lehaute et al. (2015). It contrasts with mean-field models that systematically show a backbending in the \(P(\rho)\) equation of state, finding that this signal only appears when varying particle number in a fixed volume, not in the physical situation relevant for nuclear fragmentation. The results enable quantitative study of Coulomb-induced fragmentation transitions in nuclear multifragmentation and neutron star crusts, complementing the qualitative findings from the classical lattice gas model.
