# Volume Dependence of N-Body Bound States

> We derive the finite-volume correction to the binding energy of an N-particle quantum bound state in a cubic periodic volume. Our results are applicable to bound states with arbitrary composition and total angular momentum, and in any number of spatial dimensions. The only assumptions are that the interactions have finite range. The finite-volume correction is a sum of contributions from all possible breakup channels. In the case where the separation is into two bound clusters, our result gives the leading volume dependence up to exponentially small corrections. If the separation is into three or more clusters, there is a power-law factor that is beyond the scope of this work, however our result again determines the leading exponential dependence. We also present two independent methods that use finite-volume data to determine asymptotic normalization coefficients. The coefficients are useful to determine low-energy capture reactions into weakly bound states relevant for nuclear astrophysics. Using the techniques introduced here, one can even extract the infinite-volume energy limit using data from a single-volume calculation. The derived relations are tested using several exactly solvable systems and numerical examples. We anticipate immediate applications to lattice calculations of hadronic, nuclear, and cold atomic systems.

## Key claim

The finite-volume correction to the binding energy of an N-particle bound state in a cubic periodic volume can be expressed as a sum of contributions from all possible breakup channels, with the leading exponential dependence determined by the smallest value of the binding momentum among the channels; however, when the separation is into three or more clusters, there is an additional power-law factor whose general form is beyond the scope of this work.

## Method

The authors derive the asymptotic form of the N-body wave function when one cluster of particles is separated from the rest, using separation of variables in the region where interactions are absent due to finite range. They then apply periodic boundary conditions to obtain the finite-volume correction to the binding energy, and present two independent methods for extracting asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) from finite-volume data: one using the wave function ratio and another using the finite-volume energy shift. The derived relations are tested against exactly solvable systems and numerical calculations with Gaussian potentials in one, two, and three spatial dimensions.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional delta-function potential with equal masses, exact ground state binding energies are B_N = N(N^2-1)B_2/6, where B_2 = c^2 m/4, and the wave function decays as exp(-A(N-A)kappa|r|) for cluster separation.
- For the three-particle system at unitarity in three dimensions, the leading finite-volume correction is proportional to exp(-sqrt(4mB_3/3)L) times (sqrt(4mB_3/3)L)^(-3/2), matching the result of Meissner (2014).
- Numerical fits for Gaussian potentials in d=1 with V0=-1.0, R=1.0 give fitted kappa values: for N=2, kappa_fit=0.59536(3) vs predicted 0.59625; for N=3, 1.1062(14) vs 1.1070; for N=4, 1.539(3) vs 1.541; for N=5, 1.916(21) vs 1.920.
- For d=2 with V0=-1.5, R=1.5: N=2 gives kappa_fit=0.58195(6) vs 0.58140; N=3 gives 1.20409(3) vs 1.20339; N=4 gives 1.743(8) vs 1.743.
- For d=3 with V0=-5.0, R=1.0: N=2 gives kappa_fit=0.6694(2) vs 0.6700; N=3 gives 1.798(3) vs 1.814.
- Extracted ANCs for d=1, N=2: |gamma|_FV=0.8652(4), |gamma|_WF=0.8627(4) at L=48; for d=2, N=2: 1.923(2) vs 1.921(9) at L=36; for d=3, N=2: 1.891(3) vs 1.89(1) at L=24.
- Single-volume extrapolation at L=8 for d=1, N=2 gives Delta B_N estimate -1.32(2)e-2 vs actual -1.42e-2; for N=3: -3.9(4)e-3 vs -3.75e-3; for N=4: -4.3(7)e-4 vs -4.69e-4; for N=5: -0.6(2)e-4 vs -0.64e-4.

## Context

This work extends the seminal finite-volume relations of Luscher (1985, 1986, 1990) for two-particle bound states and scattering states to general N-particle bound states with arbitrary composition and total angular momentum. It builds on prior extensions to non-zero angular momenta (Konig 2011), moving frames (Rummukainen 1995, Kim 2005), and three-particle systems (Polejaeva 2012, Briceno 2012, Meissner 2014). The results enable immediate applications to lattice QCD and lattice effective field theory calculations of nuclei and cold atomic systems, and provide a method to extract asymptotic normalization coefficients relevant for low-energy capture reactions in nuclear astrophysics, as discussed in the context of halo effective field theory (Hammer 2017).
