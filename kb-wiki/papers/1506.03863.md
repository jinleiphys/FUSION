# Hydrodynamic Approaches in Relativistic Heavy Ion Reactions

> We review several facets of the hydrodynamic description of the relativistic heavy ion collisions, starting from the historical motivation to the present understandings of the observed collective aspects of experimental data, especially those of the most recent RHIC and LHC results. In this report, we particularly focus on the conceptual questions and the physical foundations of the validity of the hydrodynamic approach itself. We also discuss recent efforts to clarify some of the points in this direction, such as the various forms of derivations of relativistic hydrodynamics together with the limitations intrinsic to the traditional approaches, variational approaches, known analytic solutions for special cases, and several new theoretical developments. Throughout this review, we stress the role of course-graining procedure in the hydrodynamic description and discuss its relation to the physical observables through the analysis of a hydrodynamic mapping of a microscopic transport model. Several questions to be answered to clarify the physics of collective phenomena in the relativistic heavy ion collisions are pointed out.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while hydrodynamic modeling of relativistic heavy ion collisions has been highly successful, its validity is not automatically guaranteed for such small, explosive systems; the approach relies on a coarse-graining procedure whose scale must be clearly separated from microscopic dynamics, and the initial conditions, equation of state, and freeze-out process are inputs that lie outside the hydrodynamic formalism itself.

## Method

The authors review the historical development and conceptual foundations of relativistic hydrodynamics as applied to heavy ion collisions, focusing on the physical assumptions behind the coarse-graining procedure, the derivation of ideal and dissipative relativistic hydrodynamics, and the role of the equation of state and transport coefficients. They also analyze a hydrodynamic mapping of a microscopic transport model to illustrate how coarse-graining affects physical observables, and they discuss analytic solutions, variational approaches, and limitations of traditional formulations.

## Key numbers

* The transition from hadronic phase to QGP at vanishing baryonic chemical potential is a smooth cross-over, as established by lattice QCD calculations (Aoki, 2006).
* Thermal model fits to particle ratios from Pb–Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}=2.76\) TeV are shown, with the fit characterized by temperature \(T\) and baryonic chemical potential \(\mu_B\) (Stachel, 2013).
* The Knudsen number \(K\) is defined as the ratio of the mean-free path to the coarse-graining scale, and the continuum limit is achieved when this ratio is much less than 1.

## Context

This review extends earlier comprehensive reviews and books on hydrodynamics in heavy ion collisions, such as those by Kolb (2003), Heinz (2009), Ollitrault (2008), and Huovinen (2006), but focuses on the conceptual and foundational questions (categories 2 and 3) rather than the mainstream quantitative modeling (category 1). It builds on the early thermodynamic and hydrodynamic ideas of Fermi (1950) and Landau (1953), and on the later development of the Cooper-Frye freeze-out formula (1974). The work enables a deeper understanding of when and why hydrodynamics applies to subatomic systems, and it points to open questions for future beam-energy scan programs at RHIC, FAIR, and NICA.
