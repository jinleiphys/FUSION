# The maximum mass and radius of neutron stars and the nuclear symmetry energy

> We calculate the equation of state of neutron matter with realistic two- and three-nucleon interactions using quantum Monte Carlo techniques, and illustrate that the short-range three-neutron interaction determines the correlation between neutron matter energy at nuclear saturation density and higher densities relevant to neutron stars. Our model also makes an experimentally testable prediction for the correlation between the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence -- determined solely by the strength of the short-range terms in the three neutron force. The same force provides a significant constraint on the maximum mass and radius of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the short-range three-neutron (3n) interaction, once its strength is tuned to reproduce the experimentally constrained nuclear symmetry energy, determines the correlation between neutron matter energy at saturation density and higher densities, and that this same force provides a significant constraint on the maximum mass and radius of neutron stars. The hedge is that the model assumes relativistic effects, spin-dependent short-range 3n interactions, and four-nucleon forces can all be mimicked by a simplified spin-independent short-range 3n term, and that this assumption can only be tested by the predicted correlation between neutron-rich nuclei and neutron star properties.

## Method

The authors solve the non-perturbative many-body nuclear Hamiltonian using the auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method, employing the Argonne AV8' two-nucleon potential and a three-nucleon interaction inspired by the Urbana IX and Illinois models. The three-neutron force includes long-distance two-pion exchange, intermediate-range three-pion loop contributions, and a spin-independent short-range repulsive term, with the short-range strength adjusted to reproduce the neutron matter energy at saturation density corresponding to the experimentally suggested symmetry energy range of 32 ± 2 MeV. The resulting equation of state is then used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for neutron star structure, with the equation of state above a critical density replaced by the maximally stiff causal equation of state to obtain upper bounds on mass and radius.

## Key numbers

- Neutron matter energy at saturation density: E_neutron(ρ₀) = 16 ± 2 MeV, derived from E_sym = 32 ± 2 MeV and E_nuclear(ρ₀) = -16.0 ± 0.1 MeV
- Nuclear symmetry energy range explored: E_sym = 30.5 MeV (no 3n force), 32.0 MeV, 33.7 MeV, and 35.1 MeV (Urbana IX)
- Symmetry energy derivative L values: ranging from 31.3 MeV (no 3n force) to 63.6 MeV (Urbana IX)
- For E_sym = 32.0 MeV, the fitting parameters for the equation of state E(ρ) = a(ρ/ρ₀)^α + b(ρ/ρ₀)^β give a ≈ 12.7-13.0 MeV, α ≈ 0.48-0.49, b ≈ 3.19-3.45 MeV, β ≈ 2.12-2.47 depending on the 3n force form
- For E_sym = 33.7-33.8 MeV, b ranges from 4.71 to 5.16 MeV
- Uncertainty in measured symmetry energy of ±2 MeV leads to an uncertainty of about 3 km for the neutron star radius at 1.4 M_solar
- Uncertainty in short-distance structure of the 3n force predicts a radius uncertainty of ≤1 km
- For E_sym ≤ 32 MeV, the model predicts M_max < 2.2 M_solar and R < 12 km for a 1.4 M_solar neutron star
- The radius of a 1.4 M_solar neutron star can be as large as 16 km if the critical density ρ_c = ρ₀
- Neutron star radii predicted in the 10-13 km range for E_sym in the 32-34 MeV range

## Context

This work extends quantum Monte Carlo calculations of neutron matter (Gandolfi 2009, Sarsa 2003) by incorporating three-neutron forces to connect nuclear symmetry energy measurements to neutron star properties. It contrasts with mean-field theories (Brown 2000) where the slope of the symmetry energy was found to be very sensitive to effective interactions, and with chiral perturbation theory calculations (Hebeler 2010) that found significant repulsion from the two-pion exchange long-range 3n interaction. The results enable observational tests through neutron star mass-radius measurements and provide a framework for using improved experimental determinations of the symmetry energy and its density derivative L to constrain the high-density equation of state.
