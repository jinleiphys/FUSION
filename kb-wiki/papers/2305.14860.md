# Structure of the medium formed in heavy ion collisions

> We investigate the structure of the medium formed in heavy ion collisions using three different models: the Color String Percolation Model (CSPM), the Core-Shell-Color String Percolation Model (CSCSPM), and the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework. We analyze the radial distribution function of the transverse representation of color flux tubes in each model to determine the medium's structure. Our results indicate that the CSPM behaves as an ideal gas, while the CSCSPM exhibits a structural phase transition from a gas-like to a liquid-like structure. Additionally, our analysis of the CGC framework suggests that it produces systems that behave like interacting gases for AuAu central collisions at RHIC energies and liquid-like structures for PbPb central collisions at LHC energies.

# Structure of the medium formed in heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The radial distribution function analysis shows that the Color String Percolation Model (CSPM) behaves as an ideal gas, while the Core-Shell-Color String Percolation Model (CSCSPM) can exhibit a structural phase transition from gas-like to liquid-like structure; the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework produces systems that behave like interacting gases for AuAu central collisions at RHIC energies and liquid-like structures for PbPb central collisions at LHC energies, though the authors note that more simulations are needed to fully confirm the liquid-like behavior.

## Method

The authors computed the radial distribution function g(r) for the transverse representation of color flux tubes in each model: fully penetrable disks for CSPM, disks with a concentric core region of exclusion and a probability parameter for core-core interactions in CSCSPM, and gluons with saturation scale fluctuations following a log-normal distribution in the CGC framework. For CSPM and CSCSPM, configurations were generated using random sequential addition algorithms; for CGC, the authors sampled Q_s^2 values from a log-normal distribution with width parameter 0.6, computed the running coupling alpha_s using the MS-bar scheme, and generated gluon configurations with constraints on nearest-neighbor distances derived from the gluon density distribution. The radial distribution function was averaged over 10^6 realizations for CSCSPM and 10^4 simulation runs for CGC.

## Key numbers

- For AuAu collisions at sqrt(s) = 130 GeV, the average saturation scale is taken as <Q_s^2> = 2 GeV^2
- For AuAu collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV, the saturation scale is estimated to be 2.23 GeV^2
- For PbPb collisions at sqrt(s) = 2760 GeV, the saturation scale is estimated to be 4.399 GeV^2
- For PbPb collisions at sqrt(s) = 5020 GeV, the saturation scale is estimated to be 5.114 GeV^2
- The saturation scale energy dependence uses exponent lambda = 0.252 in the power law ~ sqrt(s)^lambda
- The CSCSPM predicts that the structural and geometrical phase transitions occur with a difference in temperature of 1 MeV
- The QCD scale used is Lambda_QCD = 0.332 GeV with flavor number n_f = 3
- The radial distribution function was computed over distances r/sigma from 0 to 3.5 with increments of 0.035
- The simulation box size was L = 8 sigma for all models

## Context

This work extends the analysis of radial distribution functions previously performed for CSPM and CSCSPM in JBP2021 to include the CGC framework. The CSPM has been used by Sahoo and collaborators (2018-2019) to study electrical and thermal conductivity of QGP, finding ideal gas behavior, and by Jerc (2020) for finite-size analysis of the speed of sound. The CGC analysis uses the stochastic framework with saturation scale fluctuations as described by Dusling (2012) and McLerran (2015), with the width parameter from Schenke (2020). The results enable comparison of the structural predictions of these three models for the quark-gluon plasma, and suggest that the CSCSPM may provide a unified description where the geometric percolation transition (QGP formation) and structural phase transition (gas to liquid) occur nearly simultaneously.
