# Applications of dissipative and anisotropic hydrodynamics in description of early stages of relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Kinetic and hydrodynamic models describing early stages of relativistic heavy-ion collisions are discussed. We emphasise the role of the shear-bulk coupling for the correct determination of the time dependence of the bulk viscous pressure.

## Key claim

The paper argues that standard second-order viscous hydrodynamic equations fail to reproduce the exact kinetic-theory evolution of the bulk viscous pressure in the early stages of heavy-ion collisions, and that this failure is primarily due to the omission of shear-bulk coupling terms.

## Method

The author solves the relaxation-time-approximation kinetic equation for a boost-invariant, transversally homogeneous system with two different initial momentum-space anisotropies (Romatschke-Strickland form). The resulting exact time evolution of the bulk viscous pressure is compared with predictions from three common second-order viscous hydrodynamic equations (labeled A, B, C) and from two versions of anisotropic hydrodynamics (aHydro). The comparison is performed for a massive gas with effective particle mass m = 300 MeV, initial temperature T0 = 600 MeV, and relaxation time τeq = 0.5 fm.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature of the system: T0 = 600 MeV.
- Effective particle mass: m = 300 MeV.
- Equilibration time: τeq = 0.5 fm.
- Two initial anisotropy parameters are used: ξ0 = 0 and ξ0 = 100.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous comparisons between kinetic theory and viscous hydrodynamics for shear viscosity (Florkowski 2013) to the case of bulk viscosity. It directly tests the standard Israel-Stewart approach (Israel 1979, Muronga 2003) and the complete second-order formulation (Denicol 2012) against exact kinetic solutions. The results show that including shear-bulk coupling (Denicol 2014, Jaiswal 2014) significantly improves the agreement, and that anisotropic hydrodynamics (Florkowski 2010, Martinez 2010, Ryblewski 2010) reproduces the kinetic-theory bulk pressure much better than the standard viscous equations. This enables the selection of correct hydrodynamic equation structures for modeling relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
