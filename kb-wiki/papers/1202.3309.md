# Imaginary-time method for radiative capture reaction rate

> We propose a new computational method for astrophysical reaction rate of radiative capture process. In the method, an evolution of a wave function is calculated along the imaginary-time axis which is identified as the inverse temperature. It enables direct evaluation of reaction rate as a function of temperature without solving any scattering problem. The method is tested for two-body radiative capture reaction, ${^{16}{\rm O}}(α,γ){^{20}{\rm Ne}}$, showing that it gives identical results to that calculated by the ordinary procedure. The new method will be suited for calculation of triple-alpha radiative capture rate for which an explicit construction of the scattering solution is difficult.

## Key claim

The authors propose a new computational method for radiative capture reaction rates that directly evaluates the rate as a function of temperature by solving a time-dependent Schrödinger equation along the imaginary-time axis, without solving any scattering problem. The method is tested only for a two-body reaction (¹⁶O(α,γ)²⁰Ne) and shown to give identical results to the ordinary procedure, but the abstract's claim that it "will be suited for" triple-alpha capture is a forward-looking statement, not a demonstrated result.

## Method

The method employs a spectral representation of the Hamiltonian to rewrite the thermonuclear reaction rate as an expectation value involving the operator \( e^{-\beta \hat H} (\hat H - E_f)^{2\lambda+1} \hat P M_{\lambda\mu}^\dagger \), where \(\beta = 1/k_B T\) is the inverse temperature. A wave function \(\Psi_{\lambda\mu,f}(\beta)\) is defined that satisfies a time-dependent Schrödinger equation along the imaginary-time axis, \(-\partial \Psi/\partial \beta = \hat H \Psi\), with an initial condition constructed from the final bound-state wave function and the transition operator. The evolution is performed numerically using repeated small imaginary-time steps \(\Delta\beta\) with a Taylor expansion method, applying a projector \(\hat P\) at each step to remove bound states. The method is tested on the ¹⁶O(α,γ)²⁰Ne reaction using a simple Woods-Saxon potential model with partial wave expansion, focusing on the dominant E2 transition from \(l_i=0\) initial scattering state to the \(2^+\) final state at 1.63 MeV excitation.

## Key numbers

- The reaction rate \(q^{(\lambda=2)}_{l_f=2,l_i=0}(\beta)\) calculated with the imaginary-time method using radial cutoff \(R_{max}=500\) fm coincides almost completely with the ordinary method for the entire temperature region shown (approximately \(T = 1.1 \times 10^7\) K to \(1.1 \times 10^9\) K).
- For the imaginary-time method at \(T \sim 10^7\) K, a radial space up to 500 fm is required to obtain accurate reaction rates.
- The lowest positive-energy eigenvalue of the Hamiltonian with \(R_{max}=500\) fm is approximately 0.046 MeV, corresponding to the Coulomb potential minimum at the radial cutoff.
- For \(R_{max}=100\) fm and 200 fm, the lowest positive eigenvalues are approximately 0.23 MeV and 0.115 MeV, respectively.
- The estimated radial cutoff distance from the Gamow window formula gives \(R_{max} = 85\) fm for \(\beta=10^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) and \(R_{max} = 394\) fm for \(\beta=10^3\) MeV\(^{-1}\).
- The reaction rate is insensitive to the radial cutoff distance for preparing the final wave function, converging for \(R^{(f)}_{max} \geq 25\) fm.
- Changing the nuclear potential depth \(V_0\) in the initial channel (including setting \(V_0=0\)) changes the reaction rate by less than a factor of 1.5 in the range \(950\ {\rm MeV}^{-1} < \beta < 1000\ {\rm MeV}^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of de Diego et al. (2011), who calculated triple-alpha capture via an inverse photo-absorption process using bound state approximation, by offering an alternative that avoids calculating scattering solutions or multiple bound states. The method is intended to address the difficulty of evaluating the triple-alpha radiative capture rate, for which Ogata et al. (2009) reported a surprisingly large rate below 0.1 GK using the CDCC method, contradicting standard stellar evolution rates from Angulo (1999). The new method enables direct calculation of reaction rates as a function of temperature without solving scattering problems, which is particularly promising for three-body processes where analytic asymptotic forms of scattering wave functions are unknown.
