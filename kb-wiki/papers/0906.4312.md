# Duality between QCD Perturbative Series and Power Corrections

> We elaborate on the relation between perturbative and power-like corrections to short-distance sensitive QCD observables. We confront theoretical expectations with explicit perturbative calculations existing in literature. As is expected, the quadratic correction is dual to a long perturbative series and one should use one of them but not both. However, this might be true only for very long perturbative series, with number of terms needed in most cases exceeding the number of terms available. What has not been foreseen, the quartic corrections might also be dual to the perturbative series. If confirmed, this would imply a crucial modification of the dogma. We confront this quadratic correction against existing phenomenology (QCD (spectral) sum rules scales, determinations of light quark masses and of α_s from τ-decay). We find no contradiction and (to some extent) better agreement with the data and with recent lattice calculations.

## Key claim
The paper argues that quadratic power corrections (such as the linear term in the heavy quark potential) are dual to long perturbative series, meaning one should use either a short perturbative series plus the quadratic correction, or a long perturbative series without it, but not both; however, this duality may only hold for very long series exceeding the number of terms typically available, and quartic corrections might also be dual to the perturbative series, which would modify the standard dogma.

## Method
The authors confront theoretical expectations about the duality between perturbative series and power corrections with explicit perturbative calculations from lattice studies of the gluon condensate, specifically the work of Rakow et al. (2005, 2002) and Burgio et al. (1998). They analyze the dependence of fitted power corrections on the number of perturbative terms included explicitly, and compare the pattern with predictions from holographic dual models, particularly the metric ansatz of Andreev and Zakharov (2006). They also examine existing perturbative series for various QCD observables (Adler function, pseudoscalar channel, deep-inelastic sum rules) to test whether coefficients show factorial growth or geometric behavior.

## Key numbers
- The string tension from the Cornell potential is approximately 0.2 GeV^2.
- For the gluon condensate on the lattice, up to N approximately 10 perturbative terms, the power corrections are dominated by a quadratic term; at N greater than or equal to 10, a quartic correction emerges.
- At about N approximately 16 perturbative terms, the extracted value of the gluon condensate from lattice data is approximately 0.12 GeV^4, with a large error.
- The ratio of successive perturbative coefficients for the gluon condensate follows a geometric series with fitting parameters u = 0.961(9), q = 0.99(7), s = 0.44(10).
- The holographic model predicts a gluon condensate value of approximately 0.03 GeV^4.
- The constant c in the holographic metric is fixed as c^2 = (0.9 GeV)^2.

## Context
This work extends the line of research on the relation between perturbative QCD series and non-perturbative power corrections, building on the operator product expansion framework of Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1979) and the QCD spectral sum rules reviewed by Narison (1989, 2004). It directly confronts the standard view that power corrections are related to divergences in perturbative series due to factorial growth of coefficients, as reviewed by Beneke (1999). The paper argues that explicit lattice calculations of the gluon condensate by Rakow et al. (2005, 2002) and Burgio et al. (1998) show that the asymptotic behavior of perturbative series may only set in for much longer series than are available in practice, and that holographic dual models provide a natural framework for quadratic corrections. The results enable a phenomenological approach where relatively short perturbative series are combined with quadratic corrections, and suggest that quartic corrections may also be dual to perturbative series, potentially modifying the standard OPE-based understanding.
