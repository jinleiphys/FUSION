# Extended Pairing Model for Heavy Nuclei

> We study binding energies in three isotopic chains ($^{100-130}$Sn, $^{152-181}$Yb, and $^{181-202}$Pb) using the extended pairing model with Nilsson single-particle energies. The exactly solvable nature of the model means that the pairing strength G(A) required to reproduce the experimental binding energies can be determined uniquely. The valence space consists of the neutron single-particle levels between two closed shells corresponding to the magic numbers 50-82 and 82-126. In all three isotopic chains, log(G(A)) has a smooth quadratic behavior for even as well as odd nucleon numbers A; log(G(A)) for even and odd A are very similar. Remarkably, G(A) for all the Pb isotopes can be also described by a two parameter expression that is inversely proportional to the dimensionality of the model space.

## Key claim

The extended pairing model, which includes many-pair interaction terms beyond the standard pairing interaction, can reproduce experimental binding energies of heavy nuclei across three isotopic chains when the pairing strength G(A) is allowed to vary smoothly with nucleon number A; however, the model's applicability is limited by an upper bound on the binding energy that can be reproduced, given by the energy of the lowest "grand boson" configuration.

## Method

The authors used the exactly solvable extended pairing Hamiltonian with Nilsson single-particle energies to calculate binding energies for three isotopic chains (Sn, Yb, Pb). For each nucleus, the pairing strength G(A) was determined uniquely by requiring that the theoretical binding energy exactly match the experimental value, using the experimental binding energies from Audi (1997) and Nilsson deformation parameters from Nix and Kratz (1997). The valence space consisted of neutron single-particle levels between magic numbers 50-82 and 82-126, with binding energies calculated relative to closed-shell cores (100Sn, 152Yb, and 208Pb).

## Key numbers

- For 102-130Sn isotopes (50-82 neutron shell), log(G(A)) was fit with quadratic polynomials: log(G(A)) = 365.0584 - 6.4836 A + 0.0284 A^2 for even A, and log(G(A)) = 398.2277 - 7.0349 A + 0.0307 A^2 for odd A
- For 154-181Yb isotopes, log(G(A)) was fit with: log(G(A)) = 662.2247 - 7.7912 A + 0.0226 A^2 for even A, and log(G(A)) = 716.3279 - 8.4049 A + 0.0244 A^2 for odd A
- For 181-202Pb isotopes, log(G(A)) was fit with: log(G(A)) = 382.3502 - 4.1375 A + 0.0111 A^2 for even A, and log(G(A)) = 391.6113 - 4.2374 A + 0.0114 A^2 for odd A
- For Pb isotopes, G(A) for all 21 nuclei (A=181-202) was described by a two-parameter expression G(A) = 366.7702 / [dim(A)]^0.9972, where dim(A) is the dimensionality of the model space

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous introduction of the extended pairing model (Gueorguiev, Pan, and Draayer, 2004) to heavy deformed nuclei, building on the standard Nilsson plus pairing Hamiltonian approach. The model offers a computational advantage over the standard Richardson approach for solving the pairing problem, which requires solving coupled nonlinear equations that become difficult for large numbers of pairs and levels. The results enable future studies of whether the inverse proportionality between G(A) and model space dimensionality observed for Pb isotopes holds for other isotopic chains with larger model spaces, and suggest investigating the effects of using Woods-Saxon potentials or other methods for generating single-particle energies.
