# Nuclear Electron Capture in a Plasma

> We consider the electron density at the position of an ion of charge Ze in a plasma under conditions approximating those in the core of the sun. Numerical calculations have shown that the plasma effects on the density, over and above the ordinary Coulomb factors that are obtained in the absence of electron-electron interactions, are well represented by a reduction factor, exp{- Z e^2 beta kappa_D }, where beta is the inverse temperature and kappa_D is the Debye wave length. Although this factor is the direct analogue of the Salpeter enhancement factor for the fusion rates in stars, the elementary considerations that establish it in the fusion case are not applicable to the determination of the electron density and the resulting electron capture rates. We show analytically, through a sum rule that leads to a well-defined perturbative approach, that in the limit of Boltzmann statistics the Salpeter factor indeed provides the leading correction. We estimate residual effects, both from Fermi statistics and from short range terms.

# Nuclear Electron Capture in a Plasma

## Key claim

The paper shows analytically that in the Boltzmann limit, the Salpeter factor exp{-Z e^2 beta kappa_D} provides the leading correction to the electron density at a nucleus in a plasma, despite the fact that the elementary arguments justifying this factor for fusion rates do not apply to electron capture. The authors demonstrate this through a sum rule and perturbative approach, and estimate that residual effects from Fermi statistics and short-range terms are at the 1% level for solar conditions.

## Method

The authors use many-body field theory to express the electron density at the nucleus as the coincident-point limit of the single-particle electron Green's function, including a fixed point charge Ze. They separate the electron self-energy into a part that defines an effective local screened potential V_S(r) (containing all interactions communicated by a single Coulomb photon exchange) and a remainder containing multi-photon exchange effects. They then derive a sum rule relating the squared wave functions at the origin for the Debye and Coulomb potentials, and use this to express the screened density D_D(beta, mu_e) in terms of the Coulomb density with a shifted chemical potential, D_C(beta, mu_e - Z e^2 kappa_D), plus a small remainder R_D that they evaluate analytically in the high-temperature limit.

## Key numbers

* Solar interior temperature: T = 1.27 KeV, corresponding to beta = 0.0215 (a_0 / e^2)
* Electron thermal wavelength: lambda_e = 0.368 a_0
* Bulk electron density: <n_e>_beta = 9.10 / a_0^3
* Debye wave number: kappa_D = 2.22 / a_0, Debye length: kappa_D^{-1} = 0.451 a_0
* For Z=4 (beryllium), the leading correction R_D = 0.04 <n_e>_beta, while the capture density is about 4 <n_e>_beta, giving a 1% correction
* The correction from short-range terms (difference between Debye and true screened potential) is Delta D = 0.001 Z <n_e>_beta, negligible for solar conditions
* Enhancement factor w_S (full Fermi-Dirac with shifted chemical potential) for Z=4: 3.67; for Z=6: 7.19
* Enhancement factor w_{S,B} (Maxwell-Boltzmann with shifted chemical potential) for Z=4: 3.84; for Z=6: 7.82
* Ratio w_{S,B} / w_{GB} (comparison with Gruzinov and Bahcall 1997) equals 1.00 for Z=1 through 4, and 0.99 for Z=5 and Z=6
* Ratio w_S / w_{S,B} (effect of Fermi statistics) ranges from 0.99 for Z=1 to 0.92 for Z=6
* Salpeter factor exp{-beta Z e^2 kappa_D} varies from 0.95 for Z=1 through 0.83 for Z=4 to 0.75 for Z=6

## Context

This work extends the numerical calculations of Iben, Kalata and Schwartz (1967), Bahcall and Moeller (1969), and Johnson, Kolbe, Koonin, and Langanke (1992), who found that screening-induced reductions to the electron density at the nucleus in 7Be are close to the Salpeter formula. The paper is complementary to the quantum diffusion equation approach of Gruzinov and Bahcall (1997), providing an analytic derivation that does not require separating bound from continuum states or using the Saha equation. The results confirm that existing calculations are sufficiently accurate for determining electron capture rates in 7Be for solar neutrino analysis, and the formalism provides a rigorous basis for addressing related problems in plasma physics.
