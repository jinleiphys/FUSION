# Curvature effect on nuclear pasta: Is it helpful for gyroid appearance?

> In supernova cores and neutron star crusts, nuclei are thought to deform to rodlike and slablike shapes, which are often called nuclear pasta. We study the equilibrium properties of the nuclear pasta by using a liquid drop model with curvature corrections. It is confirmed that the curvature effect acts to lower the transition densities between different shapes. We also examine the gyroid structure, which was recently suggested as a different type of nuclear pasta by analogy with the polymer systems. The gyroid structure investigated in this paper is approximately formulated as an extension of the periodic minimal surface whose mean curvature vanishes. In contrast to our expectations, we find from the present approximate formulation that the curvature corrections act to slightly disfavor the appearance of the gyroid structure. By comparing the energy corrections in the gyroid phase and the hypothetical phases composed of d-dimensional spheres, where d is a general dimensionality, we show that the gyroid is unlikely to belong to a family of the generalized dimensional spheres.

## Key claim

The curvature correction to the surface energy, when included in a liquid drop model of nuclear pasta, slightly disfavors the appearance of the gyroid structure, contrary to the authors' expectation that it might help stabilize this phase.

## Method

The authors used a liquid drop model with curvature corrections to calculate the total energy density of nuclear matter at subnuclear densities for seven candidate nuclear shapes: sphere, cylinder, slab, cylindrical hole, spherical hole, gyroid, and gyroid hole. The gyroid structure was approximated by level surfaces of a trigonometric function, and the surface integrals for the curvature energy were evaluated numerically by converting them to volume integrals via Gauss' divergence theorem, while the Coulomb energy was computed using discrete Fourier transforms. The equilibrium nuclear shape was determined by minimizing the total energy density with respect to the size of the unit cell and then comparing the resulting energy densities for different shapes at a given volume fraction.

## Key numbers

- The transition point from the cylinder phase to the slab phase occurs at a volume fraction of nuclei of approximately 0.35, and the corresponding transition from slab to cylindrical hole occurs at approximately 0.65.
- The saturation density used is n0 = 0.165 fm^-3, and the surface tension is set to sigma = 0.73 MeV fm^-2.
- The curvature coefficient omega is treated as a free parameter in the range omega <= 1 MeV fm^-1, based on calculations from Skyrme interactions by Kolehmainen (1985) and Douchin and Haensel (2000).
- The curvature effect on the total energy density difference is at most about 10% for omega <= 1 MeV fm^-1.

## Context

This work extends the liquid drop model analysis of nuclear pasta phases by Nakazato, Oyamatsu, and Iida (2009), which evaluated surface and Coulomb energies for the gyroid phase but did not include curvature corrections. The curvature correction was previously shown to be important for nonspherical nuclei by Pethick and Ravenhall (1983), and its coefficient was calculated from Skyrme interactions by Kolehmainen (1985) and Douchin and Haensel (2000). The present results indicate that the gyroid phase is unlikely to belong to the family of generalized dimensional spheres proposed by Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson (1983), as the curvature corrections for the gyroid do not follow the pattern expected for non-integer-dimensional spheres.
