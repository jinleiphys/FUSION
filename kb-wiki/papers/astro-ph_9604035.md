# Quark Matter, Massive Stars and Strange Planets

> This paper gives an overview of the properties of all possible equilibrium sequences of compact strange-matter stars with nuclear crusts, which range from strange stars to strange dwarfs. In contrast to their non-strange counterparts, --neutron stars and white dwarfs--, their properties are determined by two (rather than one) parameters, the central star density and the density at the base of the nuclear crust. This leads to stellar strange-matter configurations whose properties are much more complex than those of the conventional sequence. As an example, two generically different categories of stable strange dwarfs are found, which could be the observed white dwarfs. Furthermore we find very low-mass strange stellar objects, with masses as small as those of Jupiter or even lighter planets. Such objects, if abundant enough in our Galaxy, should be seen by the presently performed gravitational microlensing searches. Further aspects studied in this paper concern the limiting rotational periods and the cooling behavior of neutron stars and their strange counterparts.

## Key claim

Strange-matter stars with nuclear crusts have properties determined by two parameters (central star density and density at the base of the nuclear crust) rather than one, leading to equilibrium sequences that include stable strange dwarfs and very low-mass objects down to planetary masses, but the paper hedges that definitive conclusions about the true nature of observed pulsars cannot yet be drawn due to uncertainties in the behavior of superdense nuclear and strange matter.

## Method

The authors compute complete equilibrium sequences of strange-matter stars with nuclear crusts by combining a bag-model equation of state for the strange quark matter core with the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state for the nuclear crust below neutron drip density. They solve the general relativistic stellar structure equations (Einstein's equations) for non-rotating and rotating configurations, and perform numerical simulations of thermal evolution using neutrino emission rates from modified and direct Urca processes, pion/kaon condensates, and superfluid suppression factors.

## Key numbers

- Maximum inner crust density set by neutron drip: \(4.3 \times 10^{11}~\mathrm{g/cm}^3\)
- Minimum-mass strange star configurations: \(\sim 0.017\,M_\odot\) (about 17 Jupiter masses) and \(10^{-4}\,M_\odot\), depending on inner crust density
- For a strange star with mass \(\sim 1.45\,M_\odot\), Kepler periods range from \(0.55 \lesssim P_K/\mathrm{msec} \lesssim 0.8\), compared to \(P_K \sim 1~\mathrm{msec}\) for neutron stars of the same mass
- Strange dwarfs with inner crust densities \(10^9~\mathrm{g/cm}^3 < \epsilon_{\mathrm{crust}} < 4 \times 10^{11}~\mathrm{g/cm}^3\) contain nuclear material up to \(\sim 4 \times 10^4\) times denser than ordinary white dwarfs of average mass \(M \sim 0.6\,M_\odot\) (central density \(\sim 10^7~\mathrm{g/cm}^3\))
- Baryon numbers of strange cores in white dwarfs and planets are smaller than \(\lesssim 2 \times 10^{55}\)

## Context

This work extends the strange matter hypothesis proposed by Bodmer (1971), Witten (1984), and Terazawa (1989) to the full range of stellar configurations with nuclear crusts, building on prior calculations of strange star crusts by Alcock (1986) and Glendenning (1992). The paper enables future gravitational microlensing searches to potentially detect very low-mass strange stellar objects, and suggests that continued observation of SN 1987A could test whether rapid early cooling provides a definitive signature distinguishing strange stars from neutron stars.
