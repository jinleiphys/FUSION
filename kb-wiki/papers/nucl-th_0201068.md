# Angle-dependent normalization of neutron-proton differential cross sections

> Systematic errors in the database of $np$ differential cross sections below 350 MeV are studied. By applying angle-dependent normalizations with the help of the energy-dependent Nijmegen partial-wave analysis PWA93 the $χ^2$-values of some seriously flawed data sets can be reduced significantly at the expense of a few degrees of freedom. It turns out that in these special cases the renormalized data sets can be made statistically acceptable such that they do not have to be discarded any longer in partial-wave analyses of the two-nucleon scattering data.

# Angle-dependent normalization of neutron-proton differential cross sections

## Key claim

Systematic errors in many np differential cross section data sets below 350 MeV have a smooth, angle-dependent structure that can be parametrized and corrected using the "adnorm" method, making previously discarded data sets statistically acceptable for inclusion in partial-wave analyses, but only when the systematic errors are genuinely experimental in origin, as demonstrated by the disagreement of slope parameters between different experiments at similar energies and angles.

## Method

The authors compare np differential cross section data sets to the energy-dependent Nijmegen partial-wave analysis PWA93 and introduce an angle-dependent normalization N(θ) expanded in orthogonal polynomials on the experimental angular interval. The number of adnorm parameters is determined by a 3 standard deviation criterion: a parameter is introduced only when χ² drops by at least 9. The method is applied to Uppsala data at 162 MeV, Freiburg data at 8 energies between 199.9 and 340.0 MeV, and Princeton data at 9 energies below 350 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Uppsala data at 162 MeV: χ² drops from 243 (standard normalization) to 92 with 4 θ-adnorm parameters, or to 94 with 3 z-adnorm parameters, for 87 data points
- Uppsala data: the expectation value ⟨χ²⟩ = 82(13); the initial χ²₀ = 243 is 12 standard deviations above expectation
- Freiburg data (859 points): χ² drops from 2139 to 831 after introducing 45 θ-adnorm parameters plus 32 normalizations; the expectation value ⟨χ²₀⟩ = 827(41) means the initial χ²₀ is 32 standard deviations above expectation
- Freiburg experiments I, II, and IV (647 points): χ²(θ) = 571 with ⟨χ²⟩ = 588(34), which is statistically acceptable
- Princeton data (156 points): χ² drops from 582 to 195 with 14 adnorm parameters; ⟨χ²⟩ = 133(16), still 3.9 standard deviations too high
- Princeton data after removing 3 additional outliers (153 points): χ²(θ) = 169 with ⟨χ²⟩ = 130(16), 2.4 standard deviations above expectation
- Slope parameter 10³α for backward-angle experiments: Uppsala set 5 at 162 MeV = −4.9(5); Freiburg expt I at 199.9 MeV = −2.8(4); Freiburg expt II at 199.9 MeV = −2.5(4); Princeton at 182 MeV = −0.7(4)

## Context

This work extends the Nijmegen group's ongoing partial-wave analysis program (Stoks 1993) and addresses systematic flaws in np differential cross section measurements that had forced exclusion of data sets from Princeton (Shepard 1974), Freiburg (Hurster 1980, Franz 2000), and Uppsala (Ericson 1995, Rahm 1998). The adnorm method was previously proposed by the Zurich group (Thomann 1978) for low-energy pp cross sections. The paper enables the salvage of these data sets for future partial-wave analyses, though the authors note that the systematic errors are of experimental origin and cannot be explained by biases in PWA93 itself.
