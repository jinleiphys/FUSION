# Surface Symmetry Energy

> Binding energy of symmetric nuclear matter can be accessed straightforwardly with the textbook mass-formula and a sample of nuclear masses. We show that, with a minimally modified formula (along the lines of the droplet model), the symmetry energy of nuclear matter can be accessed nearly as easily. Elementary considerations for a macroscopic nucleus show that the surface tension needs to depend on asymmetry. That dependence modifies the surface energy and implies the emergence of asymmetry skin. In the mass formula, the volume and surface and (a)symmetry energies combine as energies of two connected capacitors, with the volume and surface capacitances proportional to the volume and area, respectively. The net asymmetry partitions itself into volume and surface contributions in proportion to the capacitances. A combination of data on skin sizes and masses constrains the volume symmetry parameter to 27 MeV < alpha < 31 MeV and the volume-to-surface symmetry-parameter ratio to 2.0 < alpha/beta < 2.8. In Thomas-Fermi theory, the surface asymmetry-capacitance stems from a drop of the symmetry energy per nucleon S with density. We establish limits on the drop at half of normal density, to 0.57 < S(rho_0/2)/S(rho_0) < 0.83. In considering the feeding of surface by an asymmetry flux from interior, we obtain a universal condition for the collective asymmetry oscillations, in terms of the asymmetry-capacitance ratio.

## Key claim

The symmetry energy of nuclear matter can be accessed nearly as easily as the binding energy of symmetric nuclear matter using a minimally modified mass formula along the lines of the droplet model, but the volume symmetry parameter is constrained only to a range (27 MeV < alpha < 31 MeV) and the volume-to-surface symmetry-parameter ratio to a range (2.0 < alpha/beta < 2.8) rather than a single value, because the fit minimum is flat along a correlation valley.

## Method

The author modified the standard Weizsacker semiempirical mass formula by replacing the volume-only symmetry term with a combined volume-plus-surface symmetry term derived from a capacitor analogy, where the net asymmetry partitions between volume and surface in proportion to their capacitances. He performed global fits to measured ground-state binding energies from Audi (1995) for all nuclei with A >= 2, optimizing the sum of absolute deviations, and separately analyzed data on neutron skin sizes from multiple experiments (Ray 1979, Shlomo 1979, Batty 1989, Gibbs 1992, Starodubsky 1994, Suzuki 1995, Karataglidis 2002, Clark 2002) to obtain additional constraints. The surface symmetry energy was also interpreted within the extended Thomas-Fermi model by relating the surface capacitance to the drop of symmetry energy per nucleon S with density.

## Key numbers

* Volume symmetry parameter constrained to 27 MeV < alpha < 31 MeV from combined analysis of skin sizes and masses.
* Volume-to-surface symmetry-parameter ratio constrained to 2.0 < alpha/beta < 2.8 from combined analysis of skin sizes and masses.
* Ratio of symmetry energy per nucleon at half normal density to that at normal density constrained to 0.57 < S(rho_0/2)/S(rho_0) < 0.83 from Thomas-Fermi analysis.
* Average per-nucleus deviation of theoretical energy from experimental minimized at 1.97 MeV along the correlation valley in the fit with finite Coulomb corrections.
* For nuclei with 5 <= A <= 50 and |N-Z|/A > 0.2, average deviation improved from 7.4 MeV at alpha/beta = 0 to 2.3 MeV at alpha/beta ~ 1.7.

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop model of Myers and Swiatecki (1966) and the droplet model of Myers and Swiatecki (1969, 1974, 1975) by providing a capacitor analogy that clarifies the role of surface symmetry energy and by quoting uncertainties rather than single optimal values for symmetry parameters. The results enable improved constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which is critical for extrapolations to neutron matter and neutron-star properties (Lattimer 2001, Danielewicz 2002). The analysis of skin sizes in relation to the difference of proton-neutron separation energies provides a new probe of the surface energy coefficient, and the derived universal boundary condition for collective asymmetry oscillations extends the treatment of isoscalar modes by Bohr and Mottelson (1975).
