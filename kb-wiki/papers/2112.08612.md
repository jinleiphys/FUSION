# Pairing strength in the relativistic mean-field theory determined from fission barrier heights of actinide nuclei and verified by pairing rotation and binding energies

> We have studied strength in the BCS pairing force, used as a residual interaction to the relativistic mean-field to reproduce the height of the inner fission barriers for actinide nuclei. It was found that increasing the pairing strength by about 13% makes reproduce the inner fission barriers better over a wide range of actinide nuclei. This result was verified by using the moment-of-inertia of pairing rotational energy, which was introduced to avoid mean-field and odd-mass effects in the pairing interaction to deduce purely the pairing strength. The pairing interaction thus determined could simultaneously improve the description of the binding energy of heavy nuclei as well. As a result, a consistent picture among inner fission barrier, binding energy, and pairing moment of inertia could be obtained in terms of the relativistic mean-field + BCS theory for a broad region of the actinide nuclei.

## Key claim

Increasing the BCS pairing strength by about 13% relative to the original value (determined from even-odd mass staggering) improves reproduction of inner fission barrier heights across a broad range of actinide nuclei, and this adjustment simultaneously yields better agreement with experimental pairing rotational moments-of-inertia and binding energies, provided the pairing strength is validated using only even-even nuclei to avoid mean-field and odd-mass ambiguities.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory with the NLV-20 parameter set, adding a constant BCS pairing interaction with strengths initially set to \(G_n = -348\) MeV fm\(^3\) and \(G_p = -349\) MeV fm\(^3\). They calculated total energies as functions of quadrupole deformation for six actinide nuclei (\(^{234}\)U, \(^{236}\)U, \(^{240}\)Pu, \(^{242}\)Pu, \(^{242}\)Cm, \(^{244}\)Cm) and determined inner fission barrier heights from the energy difference between saddle point and ground state. The pairing strength was scaled by a single multiplier \(\alpha\) applied to both neutrons and protons, and the resulting barrier heights were compared to experimental values from RIPL-3. The adjusted pairing strength was then validated by computing the pairing moment-of-inertia (from second differences of binding energies of even-even nuclei) and ground-state binding energies, using experimental masses from AME2016.

## Key numbers

* The original pairing strengths are \(G_n = -348\) MeV fm\(^3\) and \(G_p = -349\) MeV fm\(^3\), determined from pairing gaps of nuclei including \(^{44}\)Ca, \(^{106}\)Sn–\(^{128}\)Sn, and \(^{201}\)Pb–\(^{204}\)Pb for neutrons, and \(^{52}\)Cr, \(^{82}_{50}\)Ge–\(^{94}_{50}\)Ru, \(^{136}_{82}\)Xe–\(^{147}_{82}\)Tb, and \(^{212}_{126}\)Rn–\(^{215}_{126}\)Ac for protons.
* For \(^{240}\)Pu, the experimental inner fission barrier height is 6.05 MeV (RIPL-3); the RMF+BCS calculation with original pairing strength \(G\) gives 7.54 MeV, with \(1.2G\) gives 5.08 MeV, and with \(0.8G\) gives 9.71 MeV.
* The NLV-20 parameter set yields a nuclear matter saturation density of 0.151 fm\(^{-3}\), binding energy per nucleon of 16.24 MeV, incompressibility of 190 MeV, and symmetry energy of 42.1 MeV for symmetric nuclear matter.
* For \(^{240}\)Pu, the pairing rotational energy computed with the increased pairing strength (1.2G) shows the smallest total deviation over neutron numbers \(N = 142\)–\(150\) compared to the original and reduced strengths.

## Context

This work extends prior RMF+BCS calculations of fission barriers (e.g., Karatzikos et al., and the RMF+point coupling model by Lu et al. (2012–2014)) by systematically adjusting the pairing strength to improve barrier heights without retuning mean-field parameters. The authors introduce the pairing moment-of-inertia (pairing rotation), following the concept from the works of the pairing rotation formalism, as a cleaner observable than the pairing gap or odd-even staggering because it avoids mean-field contamination and the need for odd-nucleus blocking calculations. This enables a consistent description of fission barriers, binding energies, and pairing correlations across the actinide region, and suggests that the pairing rotation can serve as a systematic tool for determining pairing strengths in future studies.
