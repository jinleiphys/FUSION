# How large is the spreading width of a superdeformed band?

> Recent models of the decay out of superdeformed bands can broadly be divided into two categories. One approach is based on the similarity between the tunneling process involved in the decay and that involved in the fusion of heavy ions, and builds on the formalism of nuclear reaction theory. The other arises from an analogy between the superdeformed decay and transport between coupled quantum dots. These models suggest conflicting values for the spreading width of the decaying superdeformed states. In this paper, the decay of superdeformed bands in the five even-even nuclei in which the SD excitation energies have been determined experimentally is considered in the framework of both approaches, and the significance of the difference in the resulting spreading widths is considered. The results of the two models are also compared to tunneling widths estimated from previous barrier height predictions and a parabolic approximation to the barrier shape.

## Key claim

The spreading widths extracted from superdeformed band decay data differ by several orders of magnitude depending on whether a statistical mixing model or a two-level mixing model is used, and this difference is physically significant rather than merely a matter of interpretation.

## Method

The authors applied two versions of a statistical mixing model (the Gu-Weidenmüller ensemble-averaging approach and the Sargeant-Hussein energy-averaging approach) and one two-level mixing model (the Stafford-Barrett quantum-dot analogy) to the decay intensity profiles of yrast superdeformed bands in five even-even nuclei (¹⁹²Hg, ¹⁹⁴Hg, ¹⁹²Pb, ¹⁹⁴Pb, and ¹⁵²Dy) where excitation energies and spins have been experimentally determined. Input parameters (ND level spacing D, ND γ-decay width Γ_N, SD in-band γ-decay width Γ_S, and in-band intensity I) were taken from published experimental data and Fermi gas model estimates. The resulting spreading widths from each model were compared to each other and to tunneling widths estimated from theoretical barrier height predictions using a parabolic barrier approximation.

## Key numbers

* Spreading width for ¹⁹²Hg at spin 10ℏ: Γ^↓ = 30.5 eV (GW model), 48.0 eV (SH model), 2.7×10⁻³ eV (SB model)
* Spreading width for ¹⁹²Hg at spin 12ℏ: Γ^↓ = 0.35 eV (GW), 24.1 eV (SH), 4.9×10⁻⁵ eV (SB)
* Spreading width for ¹⁹⁴Hg at spin 10ℏ: Γ^↓ ≥ 2.12 eV (GW), ≥ 3.96 eV (SH), ≥ 1.1×10⁻³ eV (SB)
* Spreading width for ¹⁹⁴Hg at spin 12ℏ: Γ^↓ = 0.058 eV (GW), 1.29 eV (SH), 6.0×10⁻⁵ eV (SB)
* Spreading width for ¹⁹²Pb at spin 10ℏ: Γ^↓ = 7509 eV (GW), 1910 eV (SH), negative (SB)
* Spreading width for ¹⁹⁴Pb at spin 8ℏ: Γ^↓ = 0.223 eV (GW), 8.41 eV (SH), 7.7×10⁻⁶ eV (SB)
* Spreading width for ¹⁵²Dy at spin 26ℏ: Γ^↓ = 68 eV (GW), 267 eV (SH), negative (SB)
* Tunneling width estimated from barrier height predictions at spin 8ℏ: 1.6 eV for ¹⁹⁴Hg, 508 eV for ¹⁹²Pb
* Ratio Γ^↓_SB / Γ^↓ for Hg and Pb isotopes ranges from 10⁻³ to 10⁻⁶

## Context

This work extends the debate between two conflicting approaches to superdeformed band decay: the statistical mixing model developed by Gu and Weidenmüller (1995) and Sargeant, Hussein and collaborators, and the two-level mixing model of Stafford and Barrett (1999) based on quantum-dot transport analogies. Previous work by Reiner (2000) and Cardamone, Stafford and Barrett had suggested the differences in extracted interaction strengths were not important, but this paper argues the spreading width differences are physically significant. The results enable future quantitative comparisons between experimental decay data and theoretical barrier height predictions from Strutinsky and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations, which require knowing whether the mixing is statistical or predominantly two-level.
