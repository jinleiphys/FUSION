# Can chiral EFT give us satisfaction?

> We compare nuclear forces derived from chiral effective field theory (EFT) with those obtained from traditional (phenomenological and meson) models. By means of a careful analysis of paralleles and differences, we show that chiral EFT is superior to all earlier approaches in terms of both formal aspects and successful applications in ab initio calculations. However, in spite of the considerable progress made possible by chiral EFT, complete satisfaction cannot be claimed until outstanding problems---the renormalization issue being the most important one---are finally settled.

## Key claim

Chiral effective field theory (EFT) is superior to all earlier phenomenological and meson-theoretic approaches to nuclear forces in both formal aspects and ab initio applications, but complete satisfaction cannot be claimed until the renormalization issue, the most important outstanding problem, is finally settled.

## Method

The authors compare nuclear forces derived from chiral EFT with those from traditional phenomenological and meson-exchange models by analyzing the historical development across three eras: fundamental meson field theories (1935–1960), diverse nuclear force models including one-boson-exchange and dispersion-theoretic potentials (1960–2000), and chiral EFT (1990–present). They examine the formal foundations, power counting schemes, and empirical performance of each approach in describing nucleon-nucleon scattering, nuclear matter saturation, and few-body systems.

## Key numbers

- The pion mass is approximately 138 MeV, serving as the soft scale Q in chiral EFT, while the rho meson mass of about 770 MeV sets the hard scale Lambda_chi ~ 1 GeV for the expansion in Q/Lambda_chi.
- The pion-nucleon coupling constant squared over 4 pi is approximately 14, making perturbation theory unsuitable in traditional meson theories.
- The scalar isoscalar sigma boson has a mass around 500 MeV and a width of 400–700 MeV according to the Particle Data Group, and its role in OBE models simulates correlated S-wave 2-pion exchange.
- The delta isobar has mass 1232 MeV with spin 3/2 and isospin 3/2, and is the lowest pion-nucleon resonance included in field-theoretic 2-pion exchange models.
- The Paris potential includes a phenomenological short-range part for distances r < 1.5 fm to improve the fit to nucleon-nucleon data.

## Context

This review extends the comparison of nuclear force models initiated by earlier works such as the Bonn Full Model (Machleidt, Holinde, and Elster 1987) and the Paris potential (Lacombe et al. 1980), and contrasts chiral EFT with phenomenological approaches like the Argonne v18 potential (Wiringa, Stoks, and Schiavilla 1995) and the CD-Bonn potential (Machleidt 2001). The paper enables future work focused on resolving the renormalization problem in chiral EFT, which remains the primary obstacle to achieving a fully satisfactory theory of nuclear forces.
