# Core States of Neutron Stars from Anatomizing their Scaled Structure Equations

> Given an Equation of State (EOS) for neutron star (NS) matter, there is a unique mass-radius sequence characterized by a maximum mass $M_{\rm{NS}}^{\max}$ at radius $R_{\max}$. We first show analytically that the $M_{\rm{NS}}^{\max}$ and $R_{\max}$ scale linearly with two different combinations of NS central pressure $P_{\rm{c}}$ and energy density $\varepsilon_{\rm{c}}$ by dissecting perturbatively the dimensionless Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations governing NS internal variables. The scaling relations are then verified via 87 widely used and rather diverse phenomenological as well as 17 microscopic NS EOSs with/without considering hadron-quark phase transitions and hyperons by solving numerically the original TOV equations. The EOS of densest NS matter allowed before it collapses into a black hole (BH) is then obtained. Using the universal $M_{\rm{NS}}^{\max}$ and $R_{\max}$ scalings and NICER (Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer) and XMM-Newton mass-radius observational data for PSR J0740+6620, a very narrow constraining band on the NS central EOS is extracted directly from the data for the first time without using any specific input EOS model.

## Key claim

The authors show analytically and verify numerically that the maximum mass and corresponding radius of any neutron star scale linearly with specific combinations of central pressure and energy density, enabling direct extraction of the core equation of state from observational data without using any specific input EOS model, though the scaling is most accurate for massive neutron stars near the maximum-mass configuration (roughly with M_NS^max > 1.2 solar masses).

## Method

The authors perturbatively solve the dimensionless Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to derive scaling relations between the maximum mass M_NS^max and the combination Gamma_c = epsilon_c^{-1/2} [P_c/(1+3P_c^2+4P_c)]^{3/2}, and between the corresponding radius R_max and the combination nu_c = epsilon_c^{-1/2} [P_c/(1+3P_c^2+4P_c)]^{1/2}. They verify these scalings by numerically solving the original TOV equations using 87 phenomenological and 17 microscopic neutron star equations of state, including models with hadron-quark phase transitions and hyperons. The universal scalings are then applied to NICER and XMM-Newton mass-radius observational data for PSR J0740+6620 to constrain the central equation of state.

## Key numbers

- Linear fit for R_max: R_max/km ≈ 1.05_{-0.03}^{+0.03} × 10^3 nu_c + 0.64_{-0.29}^{+0.29}, with Pearson coefficient 0.958, where nu_c is measured in fm^{3/2}/MeV^{1/2}
- Linear fit for M_NS^max: M_NS^max/M_sun ≈ 0.173_{-0.003}^{+0.003} × 10^4 Gamma_c - 0.106_{-0.035}^{+0.035}, with Pearson coefficient 0.986, where Gamma_c is measured in fm^{3/2}/MeV^{1/2}
- Standard errors for radius and mass fittings: 0.39 km and 0.05 M_sun respectively
- For PSR J0740+6620 (mass 2.08_{-0.07}^{+0.07} M_sun, radius 12.39_{-0.98}^{+1.30} km from Riley 2021): 10.3 < 10^3 nu_c/(fm^{3/2}/MeV^{1/2}) < 12.4 and 12.2 < 10^4 Gamma_c/(fm^{3/2}/MeV^{1/2}) < 13.1
- Most probable central values for PSR J0740+6620: epsilon_c ≈ 901_{-287}^{+214} MeV/fm^3, P_c ≈ 218_{-125}^{+93} MeV/fm^3, P_c/epsilon_c ≈ 0.24_{-0.07}^{+0.05}
- Central speed of sound squared for PSR J0740+6620: s_c^2 ≈ 0.45_{-0.18}^{+0.14}
- Causality upper bound: P_c/epsilon_c < 0.374

## Context

This work extends earlier scaling relations for neutron star bulk properties found by Hartle (1978), Kalogera (1996), and Lattimer and Prakash (2005), which showed M_NS^max proportional to epsilon_c^{-1/2} but with empirically determined coefficients that had EOS model dependence. The novel contribution is that the scalings with Gamma_c and nu_c enable direct extraction of the core equation of state P_c(epsilon_c) rather than just individual values of central density or pressure. The method enables future refinement as more precise radius measurements become available from advanced observatories, and the extracted central EOS band is narrower than previous constraints from approaches combining nuclear theory, perturbative QCD, and gravitational wave observations.
