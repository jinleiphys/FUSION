# A hard thermal loop benchmark for the extraction of the nonperturbative $Q\bar{Q}$ potential

> The extraction of the finite temperature heavy quark potential from lattice QCD relies on a spectral analysis of the Wilson loop. General arguments tell us that the lowest lying spectral peak encodes, through its position and shape, the real and imaginary part of this complex potential. Here we benchmark this extraction strategy using leading order hard-thermal loop (HTL) calculations. I.e. we analytically calculate the Wilson loop and determine the corresponding spectrum. By fitting its lowest lying peak we obtain the real- and imaginary part and confirm that the knowledge of the lowest peak alone is sufficient for obtaining the potential. Access to the full spectrum allows an investigation of spectral features that do not contribute to the potential but can pose a challenge to numerical attempts of an analytic continuation from imaginary time data. Differences in these contributions between the Wilson loop and gauge fixed Wilson line correlators are discussed. To better understand the difficulties in a numerical extraction we deploy the Maximum Entropy method with extended search space to HTL correlators in Euclidean time and observe how well the known spectral function and values for the real and imaginary part are reproduced. Possible venues for improvement of the extraction strategy are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper confirms that fitting only the lowest-lying spectral peak of the Wilson loop is sufficient to extract both the real and imaginary parts of the finite-temperature heavy quark potential, provided that the peak's shape accounts for early-time non-potential physics (skewing). This holds for both the Wilson loop and Coulomb-gauge fixed Wilson line correlators at leading order in hard-thermal-loop (HTL) perturbation theory.

## Method

The authors analytically computed the real-time and Euclidean Wilson loop and Coulomb-gauge Wilson line correlator in leading-order HTL resummed perturbation theory, using a momentum cutoff to mimic lattice regularization. They then extracted the spectral function from the analytic expressions, fitted the lowest-lying peak with a skewed Lorentzian form derived from pNRQCD, and compared the resulting potential to the known HTL result. Separately, they applied the Maximum Entropy Method (MEM) with extended search space to the Euclidean HTL correlators to test how well the known spectral function and potential values could be numerically reconstructed.

## Key numbers

* The real part of the HTL potential is given by Re[V](r) = -(g² C_F)/(4π) [ m_D + e^(-m_D r)/r ], with the Debye mass m_D and coupling g.
* The imaginary part of the HTL potential is Im[V](r) = -(g² C_F)/(4π) T φ(m_D r), where φ(x) = 2 ∫₀^∞ dz z/(z²+1)² [1 - sin(zx)/(zx)].
* The momentum cutoff was set to Λ = π/a with a = 0.04 fm, corresponding to Λ ≈ 5π GeV for the numerical evaluations.
* The temperature used in numerical evaluations was T = 2.33 × 270 MeV.
* The cusp divergence from transverse gluon poles in the Wilson loop gives a contribution of C_F g²/(2π² ε) in dimensional regularization.

## Context

This work extends the method proposed by Rothkopf (2011) and improved by Burnier (2012) for extracting the nonperturbative heavy quark potential from lattice QCD, which previously yielded unexpected linear growth of both real and imaginary parts at high temperatures. By using HTL perturbation theory as a controlled benchmark, the authors demonstrate that the improved peak-fitting procedure resolves the discrepancy and yields a potential compatible with Debye screening. The results enable future lattice studies to use Coulomb-gauge Wilson line correlators (which have better signal-to-noise ratios than Wilson loops) for potential extraction, and provide a testing ground for improving Bayesian analytic continuation methods like MEM.
