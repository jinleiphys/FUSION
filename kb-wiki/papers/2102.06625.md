# Corrections to the hadron resonance gas from lattice QCD and their effect on fluctuation-ratios at finite density

> The hadron resonance gas (HRG) model is often believed to correctly describe the confined phase of QCD. This assumption is the basis of many phenomenological works on QCD thermodynamics and of the analysis of hadron yields in relativistic heavy ion collisions. We use first-principle lattice simulations to calculate corrections to the ideal HRG. Namely, we determine the sub-leading fugacity expansion coefficients of the grand canonical free energy, receiving contributions from processes like kaon-kaon or baryon-baryon scattering. We achieve this goal by performing a two dimensional scan on the imaginary baryon number chemical potential ($μ_B$) - strangeness chemical potential ($μ_S$) plane, where the fugacity expansion coefficients become Fourier coefficients. We carry out a continuum limit estimation of these coefficients by performing lattice simulations with temporal extents of $N_τ=8,10,12$ using the 4stout-improved staggered action. We then use the truncated fugacity expansion to extrapolate ratios of baryon number and strangeness fluctuations and correlations to finite chemical potentials. Evaluating the fugacity expansion along the crossover line, we reproduce the trend seen in the experimental data on net-proton fluctuations by the STAR collaboration.

## Key claim

The ideal hadron resonance gas (HRG) model fails to describe sub-leading fugacity expansion coefficients of the QCD grand canonical free energy, even in the confined phase below the crossover temperature; these coefficients, which receive contributions from non-resonant scattering processes such as kaon-kaon and baryon-baryon scattering, can be determined from first-principle lattice QCD simulations and used to improve the description of fluctuation ratios at finite baryon density.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations using a 4stout-improved staggered fermion action on lattices with temporal extents Nτ = 8, 10, and 12, scanning a two-dimensional grid of 144 points in the plane of imaginary baryon number chemical potential (μB^I) and imaginary strangeness chemical potential (μS^I). At each point, they computed baryon number and strangeness fluctuations and correlations, then extracted sub-leading fugacity expansion coefficients (sector coefficients P^BS_jk) via a correlated fit to the Fourier expansion of the pressure. Continuum estimates were obtained through a combined fit in temperature and lattice spacing, with systematic errors assessed by varying the fit ansatz and the number of included sectors.

## Key numbers

- Lattice simulations were performed at four temperatures: T = 145 MeV, 150 MeV, 155 MeV, and 160 MeV.
- The two-dimensional scan covered chemical potential values (μB^I, μS^I) = (π/8)(i, j) with i = 0,...,15 and j = 0,...,9, totaling 144 simulation points.
- The three-kaon scattering sector coefficient P^BS_03 is consistent with zero in the entire temperature range, with an upper limit of 2 × 10^(-3) at 1σ uncertainty.
- At T = 155 MeV, the ideal HRG model prediction for the sum ∑_k P^BS_2k is of order 10^(-5), orders of magnitude smaller than the lattice results.
- The sector P^BS_02 (kaon-kaon scattering) goes slightly below zero at around 155 MeV within 1σ uncertainty.

## Context

This work extends earlier lattice studies of fugacity expansion coefficients by the same collaboration (Alba et al., 2017; Vovchenko et al., 2017; Bellwied et al., 2019) by performing a two-dimensional scan in the imaginary (μB, μS) plane, enabling separation of scattering contributions by net strangeness quantum number for the first time. The results provide first-principle input for improving hadron resonance gas models beyond the ideal gas approximation, particularly for describing baryon-baryon and kaon-kaon interactions that are absent in the standard HRG. The extracted coefficients can be used to tune freeze-out models in heavy ion phenomenology and to construct more realistic excluded-volume models, which previously relied on arbitrary flavor-dependent parameters (Vovchenko et al., 2016; Alba et al., 2016; Huovinen et al., 2017).
