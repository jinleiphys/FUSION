# Perspectives on few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei

> It is a fascinating phenomenon in nuclear physics that states with a pronounced few-body structure can emerge from the complex dynamics of many nucleons. Such halo or cluster states often appear near the boundaries of nuclear stability. As such, they are an important part of the experimental program beginning at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB). A concerted effort of theory and experiment is necessary both to analyze experiments involving effective few-body states, as well as to constrain and refine theories of the nuclear force in light of new data from these experiments. As a contribution to exactly this effort, this paper compiles a collection of ``perspectives'' that emerged out of the Topical Program ``Few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei and their role in FRIB experiments'' that was held at FRIB in August 2022 and brought together theorists and experimentalists working on this topic.

## Key claim
This paper compiles a collection of "perspectives" that emerged from the Topical Program "Few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei and their role in FRIB experiments" held at FRIB in August 2022, rather than presenting new experimental or theoretical results; the authors argue that a concerted effort between theory and experiment is necessary to analyze experiments involving effective few-body states and to constrain theories of the nuclear force, but acknowledge that the interplay between few-body effects and nuclear shell structure is not yet well understood.

## Method
The paper is a multi-author perspective piece resulting from a topical program that brought together theorists and experimentalists working on few-body cluster structures in exotic nuclei. The authors organized discussions into thematic sections covering the theory-to-experiment pipeline, the emergence of few-body effects in exotic nuclei, and related topics, with each section written by a small subgroup of participants.

## Key numbers
- The Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) will give access to more than a thousand new exotic nuclei, with beam energies ranging from keV/u to GeV/u; reactions up to about 200 MeV/u are currently accessible, while those up to 400 MeV/u will require the high-energy upgrade.
- Ab initio nuclear many-body problem has so far only been solved exactly for selected light nuclei with A < 12.
- The paper mentions that the first excited state of 16O is the head of a strongly deformed rotational band, and recent coupled-cluster computations reproduced the ground-state rotational band of 34Mg.

## Context
This perspective paper extends discussions from the FRIB Theory Alliance Topical Program held in August 2022, building on prior work on continuum shell model approaches (Rotter 1991, Okolowicz 2003), halo effective field theories (Bertulani 2002, Ryberg 2014, Ji 2014, Hammer 2017, Capel 2018), and the need to unify nuclear structure and reactions recognized during a previous FRIB Theory Alliance Topical Program (Johnson 2020). It enables future development of new models and effective field theories that treat cluster effects explicitly, consistently, and efficiently, to be constrained by experimental data from FRIB and other radioactive beam facilities.
