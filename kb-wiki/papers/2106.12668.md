# On the calculation of neutron sources generating steady prescribed power distributions in subcritical systems using multigroup X,Y-geometry discrete ordinates models

> In this paper a methodology is described to estimate multigroup neutron source distributions which must be added into a subcritical system to drive it to a steady state prescribed power distribution. This work has been motivated by the principle of operation of the ADS (Accelerator Driven System) reactors, which have subcritical cores stabilized by the action of external sources. We use the energy multigroup two-dimensional neutron transport equation in the discrete ordinates formulation (SN) and the equation which is adjoint to it, whose solution is interpreted here as a distribution measuring the importance of the angular flux of neutrons to a linear functional. These equations are correlated through a reciprocity relation, leading to a relationship between the interior sources of neutrons and the power produced by unit length of height of the domain. A coarse-mesh numerical method of the spectral nodal class, referred to as adjoint response matrix constant-nodal method, is applied to numerically solve the adjoint SN equations. Numerical experiments are performed to analyze the accuracy of the present methodology so as to illustrate its potential practical applications.

## Key claim

The paper describes a methodology to determine the multigroup neutron source distribution that must be added into a subcritical system to drive it to a prescribed steady-state power distribution, using the adjoint discrete ordinates (SN) transport equations; however, for most practical problems with more than one energy group (G > 1) and fewer fuel regions than total spatial regions (I*J* < IJ), the resulting linear system has GIJ - I*J* degrees of freedom and requires auxiliary equations to yield a unique solution, meaning the method does not directly give a unique source distribution without additional user-supplied constraints.

## Method

The authors use the energy multigroup two-dimensional neutron transport equation in the discrete ordinates (SN) formulation and its adjoint equation, correlating them through a reciprocity relation to obtain a relationship between interior neutron sources and the power produced per unit height of the domain. They define an "importance matrix" L† composed of adjoint scalar fluxes averaged over each spatial region, which explicitly correlates the neutron source distribution vector Q to the power density vector P via the matrix equation P = L†Q. To solve the adjoint SN equations efficiently on coarse spatial grids, they apply the adjoint response matrix constant-nodal (RM†-CN) method, a spectral nodal coarse-mesh numerical method that uses transverse integration and exponential basis functions.

## Key numbers

- The total number of discrete directions M = N(N+2)/2 for a given SN order N, where the Level Symmetric Quadrature (LQN) is used.
- The importance matrix L† has dimensions I*J* × GIJ, where I*J* is the number of fuel regions, G is the number of energy groups, and I,J are the number of spatial regions in x and y directions.
- For monoenergetic problems (G=1) composed only of fuel regions (I*J* = IJ), the neutron source distribution is directly given by Q = L†^{-1}P, provided L† has an inverse.
- For problems with G > 1 and I*J* < IJ, the linear system has GIJ - I*J* degrees of freedom, requiring that many auxiliary equations for a unique solution.

## Context

This work extends the application of adjoint transport theory and reciprocity relations, previously developed by Gandini (1987) and Lewins (1965), to the specific problem of determining external neutron sources for Accelerator Driven System (ADS) reactors, which are Generation IV reactors with subcritical cores stabilized by external spallation neutron sources. The methodology enables the design of neutron source distributions that achieve desired power profiles in subcritical systems, and the use of the RM†-CN coarse-mesh method (previously developed by Moraes et al., 2020) makes the computation of the importance matrix practical by avoiding fine-mesh solutions of the adjoint SN equations.
