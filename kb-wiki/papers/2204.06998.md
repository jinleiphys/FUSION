# QCD Phase Boundary and the Hadrochemical Horizon

> I review the physics of the phase boundary between hadronic matter and quark matter from several different points of view. These include thermodynamics, statistical physics, and chemical kinetics. In particular, the review focuses on the role of the chemical freeze-out line and its relation to the concept of valence quark percolation. The review ends with some recollections of Jean Cleymans.

## Key claim

The chemical freeze-out line (hadrochemical horizon) serves as an experimentally measurable proxy for the QCD phase boundary, but it is not identical to the thermodynamic phase boundary; at high net-baryon density the chemical horizon lies increasingly below the true phase boundary, and the empirical rule that freeze-out occurs along a line of constant energy per particle (Cleymans' Law) still lacks a simple explanation.

## Method

The paper reviews the QCD phase boundary from thermodynamic, statistical, and kinetic perspectives, focusing on the chemical freeze-out line and its relation to valence-quark percolation. It presents the excluded volume statistical bootstrap model of Hagedorn and Rafelski, where the partition function is solved using a bootstrap condition with 148 elementary hadron states (pseudoscalar and vector meson nonets, baryon and antibaryon octets and decuplets) as input, and the phase boundary is identified where the unoccupied volume fraction vanishes. The review also compares thermal model fits to experimental hadron yield data from AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC experiments, and discusses comparisons of net-charge and net-baryon number fluctuations with lattice-QCD susceptibilities.

## Key numbers

* Pseudocritical temperature at zero baryochemical potential: \(T_c = 156.5 \pm 1.5\) MeV from lattice-QCD (HotQCD 2018).
* Critical endpoint from functional renormalization group: \((T_\mathrm{CEP}, \mu_{B,\mathrm{CEP}}) \approx (107~\mathrm{MeV}, 635~\mathrm{MeV})\), corresponding to \(\mu_{B,\mathrm{CEP}}/T_\mathrm{CEP} \approx 5.54\) (Fu 2019).
* Critical endpoint from holographic QCD model: \((T_\mathrm{CEP}, \mu_{B,\mathrm{CEP}}) \approx (89~\mathrm{MeV}, 724~\mathrm{MeV})\) (Critelli 2017).
* Bag constant: \({\cal B} \approx (145~\mathrm{MeV})^4\).
* Critical energy density from statistical bootstrap model: \(\varepsilon_{\rm c} = 0.23~\mathrm{GeV/fm}^3\); minimum of speed of sound from lattice-QCD: \(\varepsilon_\mathrm{min} = 0.20~\mathrm{GeV/fm}^3\) (Borsanyi 2010).
* Critical temperature at \(\mu_B = 0\) in the excluded volume bootstrap model with full ground-state hadrons: \(T_c(0) = 162~\mathrm{MeV}\).
* Bootstrap constant: \(H = 0.724~\mathrm{(GeV)}^{-2}\).
* Occupied volume fraction at percolation threshold for hard spheres: \(\xi_{\rm cl} \approx 0.6\)–\(0.75\); close-packing fraction: \(\xi_\mathrm{CP} = \pi/(3\sqrt{2}) \approx 0.74\).
* Lattice-QCD constraint: critical endpoint must lie in region \(\mu_B/T > 3.5\) (Borsanyi 2021).
* Constant energy per particle along freeze-out line: \(\langle E \rangle / \langle N \rangle \approx 1~\mathrm{GeV}\) (Cleymans and Redlich 1998).
* Estimated bias in freeze-out temperature from hadronic transport model: 5–15 MeV below the black dots in Figure 1 (Reichert 2020).
* Net-kaon fluctuations suggest strangeness freeze-out at approximately 10 MeV higher temperature (Bellwied 2018).

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to map the QCD phase boundary, building on the statistical bootstrap model of Hagedorn and Rafelski (1980) and the early observation by Koch, Muller, and Rafelski (1986) that strange hadron abundances freeze out near the critical temperature. It contrasts the thermodynamic phase boundary from lattice-QCD (Bazavov 2017, Borsanyi 2020) with the kinetic chemical freeze-out line, and relates the latter to the concept of valence-quark percolation (Magas 2003), which differs from color string percolation models (Armesto 1996, Braun 1997) used for the initial stage of nuclear collisions. The review enables future work on hybrid collision models that include a hydrodynamic quark-gluon plasma phase, more detailed models of valence-quark percolation, and lattice studies of percolation observables.
