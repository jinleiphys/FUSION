# Giant Monopole Resonances and nuclear incompressibilities studied for the zero-range and separable pairing interactions

> Background: Following the 2007 precise measurements of monopole strengths in tin isotopes, there has been a continuous theoretical effort to obtain a precise description of the experimental results. Up to now, there is no satisfactory explanation of why the tin nuclei appear to be significantly softer than 208Pb. Purpose: We determine the influence of finite-range and separable pairing interactions on monopole strength functions in semi-magic nuclei. Methods: We employ self-consistently the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation on top of spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov solutions. We use the Arnoldi method to solve the linear-response problem with pairing. Results: We found that the difference between centroids of Giant Monopole Resonances measured in lead and tin (about 1 MeV) always turns out to be overestimated by about 100%. We also found that the volume incompressibility, obtained by adjusting the liquid-drop expression to microscopic results, is significantly larger than the infinite-matter incompressibility. Conclusions: The zero-range and separable pairing forces cannot induce modifications of monopole strength functions in tin to match experimental data.

# Giant Monopole Resonances and nuclear incompressibilities studied for the zero-range and separable pairing interactions

## Key claim

The difference between Giant Monopole Resonance centroids measured in lead and tin (about 1 MeV) is always overestimated by about 100% in calculations using zero-range or separable pairing interactions, and neither type of pairing force can induce modifications of monopole strength functions in tin to match experimental data.

## Method

The authors employed self-consistent Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) on top of spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) solutions, using the Arnoldi method to solve the linear-response problem with pairing. They implemented a finite-range, fully separable, translationally invariant Gaussian pairing interaction alongside a standard zero-range pairing force, using two Skyrme functionals (SLy4 and UNEDF0) for the particle-hole channel, and performed calculations for all particle-bound semi-magic nuclei from Z=8 or N=8 up to Z=82 or N=126.

## Key numbers

- The difference between GMR centroids in lead and tin (about 1 MeV) is overestimated by about 100% in all calculations.
- The volume incompressibility parameter K_V, averaged over both functionals and both pairing forces, equals 254 +/- 5 MeV, significantly higher than the infinite-matter incompressibility K_infinity = 230 MeV.
- For the separable pairing force with the SLy4 functional, K_V = 252 +/- 5 MeV; with UNEDF0, K_V = 249 +/- 5 MeV.
- For the zero-range pairing force with the SLy4 functional, K_V = 258 +/- 5 MeV; with UNEDF0, K_V = 257 +/- 4 MeV.
- Calculated m1/m0 values in tin overestimate measured values by 0.6-0.8 MeV, while in lead they underestimate measured values by 0.4 MeV.
- The separable-force strength parameters used were G_n = 631 MeV fm^3 and G_p = 647 MeV fm^3 for SLy4, and G_n = 473 MeV fm^3 and G_p = 521 MeV fm^3 for UNEDF0.
- The zero-range force parameters were V_n = 195 MeV fm^3 and V_p = 221 MeV fm^3 for SLy4, and V_n = 126 MeV fm^3 and V_p = 157 MeV fm^3 for UNEDF0.
- The LD formula fit to 210 (SLy4 separable), 211 (SLy4 zero-range), 204 (UNEDF0 separable), and 195 (UNEDF0 zero-range) semi-magic nuclei gave average deviations of about 5 MeV (approximately 3% of typical K_A values).

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Piechaczek et al. (2007, 2010) that showed self-consistent models reproducing the GMR energy in 208Pb systematically overestimate GMR energies in tin isotopes, and by Khan et al. (2009a, 2009b) that were restricted to zero-range pairing effects. The study implements the finite-range separable pairing interaction of Duguet et al. (2004), Tian et al. (2009), and Niksic et al. (2010) together with the quasilocal energy density functional of Carlsson et al. (2008). The results confirm that the discrepancy between tin and lead incompressibilities, previously seen in relativistic calculations by Niksic et al. (2008), persists regardless of pairing interaction type, indicating that the "why is tin so soft" question raised by Piechaczek et al. (2007, 2010) remains unresolved and requires modifications beyond the pairing channel in nuclear energy density functionals.
