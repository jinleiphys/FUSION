# Why Stellar Sequences Turn Over: Fixed Points, Instability, and Equation-of-State Universality

> We reformulate the stellar structure equations in the language of dynamical systems and show that the maximum mass of stellar sequences arises from the existence of a fixed point in the relativistic regime. In an appropriate representation of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, this fixed point becomes manifest and is directly associated with the turnover of the mass-radius curve. The existence of a fixed point implies an effective reduction in dimensionality near the onset of instability, which provides a simple explanation for several equation-of-state-insensitive relations and predicts new ones. In the weakly relativistic limit, we identify a complementary universal structure shared by stellar sequences at their maximum mass, which we term the "compressible limit," and derive distinct universal relations governing the maximum mass in the Newtonian and post-Newtonian regimes. Combining these theoretical results with current astrophysical constraints, we show that the J0740+6620 pulsar is unlikely to lie near the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff maximum mass unless the equation of state exhibits a strong first-order phase transition at densities just above its central density.

## Key claim

The maximum mass of stellar sequences arises from a fixed point in the relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations when reformulated as a dynamical system, but the J0740+6620 pulsar is unlikely to lie near this maximum mass unless the equation of state exhibits a strong first-order phase transition at densities just above its central density.

## Method

The authors reformulate the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations using the Lindblom enthalpy-based formulation into a two-dimensional, non-autonomous dynamical system in variables \((\tilde e, v)\), where \(\tilde e \propto r^2 e\) is a dimensionless energy-density variable and \(v = Gm(r)/(rc^2)\) is the compactness of enclosed mass. They identify a fixed point of the frozen-time system and analyze the linearized flow near it using the trace-determinant plane, then extend the analysis to the Newtonian and post-Newtonian regimes where they identify a separate "compressible limit" governing maximum mass behavior.

## Key numbers

- Central energy densities for neutron stars are defined as \(e_c \gtrsim 10^{10} \; {\rm{g}}\, {\rm{cm}}^{-3} \sim 5 \times 10^{-3} \; {\rm{MeV}} \, {\rm{fm}}^{-3}\)
- Near-center expansion gives \(\tilde e(\ln h) = \frac{6}{1+3w_c}(\ln h_c - \ln h) + \mathcal{O}(\ln h_c-\ln h)^2\) and \(v(\ln h) = \frac{2}{1+3w_c}(\ln h_c - \ln h) + \mathcal{O}(\ln h_c-\ln h)^2\), where \(w_c = p_c/e_c\) is the central pressure-to-energy-density ratio
- The relation \(v = \tilde e/3 + \mathcal{O}(\ln h_c-\ln h)^2\) holds near the center

## Context

This work extends the dynamical-systems approach to stellar structure first introduced by Heinzle (2003) under the hypothesis of a particular functional form of the equation of state, but uses a different independent variable (\(\ln h\) instead of a function of \(r\)) and analyzes a non-autonomous system of 2 variables rather than a 3-variable autonomous system. The reformulation provides a unified explanation for equation-of-state-insensitive relations observed in neutron star structure and enables diagnostics for strong first-order phase transitions by connecting the fixed-point geometry to tidal deformability constraints from gravitational-wave observations.
