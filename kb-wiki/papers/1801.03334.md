# Applications of deep learning to relativistic hydrodynamics

> Relativistic hydrodynamics is a powerful tool to simulate the evolution of the quark gluon plasma (QGP) in relativistic heavy ion collisions. Using 10000 initial and final profiles generated from 2+1-d relativistic hydrodynamics VISH2+1 with MC-Glauber initial conditions, we train a deep neural network based on stacked U-net, and use it to predict the final profiles associated with various initial conditions, including MC-Glauber, MC-KLN and AMPT and TRENTo. A comparison with the VISH2+1 results shows that the network predictions can nicely capture the magnitude and inhomogeneous structures of the final profiles, and nicely describe the related eccentricity distributions $P(\varepsilon_n)$ (n=2, 3, 4). These results indicate that deep learning technique can capture the main features of the non-linear evolution of hydrodynamics, showing its potential to largely accelerate the event-by-event simulations of relativistic hydrodynamics.

## Key claim

Deep learning can capture the main features of the non-linear evolution of 2+1-d relativistic ideal hydrodynamics, but the combined stacked U-net series tends to accumulate errors with more U-net blocks added, so predictions are less accurate for longer evolution times (above 4.0 fm/c).

## Method

The authors used 10000 initial and final energy-momentum tensor profiles generated from 2+1-d relativistic ideal hydrodynamics (VISH2+1 with MC-Glauber initial conditions) to train a stacked U-net deep neural network. The network was then tested on initial profiles from MC-Glauber, MC-KLN, AMPT, and TRENTo models. For longer evolution times, the total evolution was divided into parts of 2.0 fm/c each, with a separate stacked U-net trained for each part and applied sequentially.

## Key numbers

- 10000 initial and final image sets from VISH2+1 with MC-Glauber initial conditions were used for training
- The network predicts final profiles at evolution times tau-tau_0 = 2.0, 4.0, and 6.0 fm/c (with tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c)
- The simulation grid covers a fixed transverse area of 13 fm x 13 fm with grid spacing dx = dy = 0.1 fm and time step dtau = 0.04 fm/c
- Training took about 1 day on a single NVIDIA Tesla P40 GPU
- Traditional CPU-based single-event hydrodynamic evolution takes 10-20 minutes, while the network takes several seconds on a P40 GPU

## Context

This work extends the application of deep learning to relativistic hydrodynamics, building on Bayesian emulators (Pratt, 2015; Everett, 2020) which are powerful for constraining equation of state and transport coefficients but are not designed to predict whole profiles of energy density and flow velocity. The results enable potential acceleration of event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations in relativistic heavy ion collisions, though the authors note that 50-100x speedup of hydro simulations can already be achieved by switching from CPU to GPU (Pang, 2018; Bazow, 2016).
