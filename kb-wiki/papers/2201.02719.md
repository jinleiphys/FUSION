# Theory of Nuclear Fission

> Atomic nuclei are quantum many-body systems of protons and neutrons held together by strong nuclear forces. Under the proper conditions, nuclei can break into two (sometimes three) fragments which will subsequently decay by emitting particles. This phenomenon is called nuclear fission. Since different fission events may produce different fragmentations, the end-products of all fissions that occurred in a small chemical sample of matter comprise hundreds of different isotopes, including $α$ particles, together with a large number of emitted neutrons, photons, electrons and antineutrinos. The extraordinary complexity of this process, which happens at length scales of the order of a femtometer, mostly takes less than a femtosecond but is not completely over until all the lingering $β$ decays have completed - which can take years - is a fascinating window into the physics of atomic nuclei. While fission may be more naturally known in the context of its technological applications, it also plays a pivotal role in the synthesis of heavy elements in astrophysical environments. In both cases, experimental measurements are not sufficient to provide complete data. Simulations are needed, yet at levels of accuracy and precision that pose formidable challenges to nuclear theory. The goal of this article is to provide a comprehensive overview of the theoretical methods employed in the description of nuclear fission.

## Key claim

This review provides a comprehensive overview of theoretical methods for nuclear fission, covering the entire process from deformation to fragment decay, but explicitly acknowledges that experimental measurements are insufficient to provide complete data and that simulations are needed at levels of accuracy and precision that pose formidable challenges to nuclear theory.

## Method

The paper surveys the theoretical framework of nuclear fission organized chronologically: it first describes nuclear deformation using both phenomenological macroscopic-microscopic models (liquid drop model with shell and pairing corrections) and energy density functional (EDF) theory based on Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) methods. It then reviews calculations of fission probabilities (spontaneous fission half-lives and induced fission cross sections), large-amplitude collective motion describing the dynamics from saddle to scission, and finally the deexcitation of fission fragments through prompt neutron and photon emission followed by delayed beta decays.

## Key numbers

- The fissility parameter x is approximated as x ≈ Z² / [47A(1 − ηI²)] with I = (N−Z)/A, where η is the ratio of surface-symmetry to surface coefficients
- The macroscopic energy represents nearly 99% of the total binding of a heavy atomic nucleus such as an actinide
- Prompt particle emission occurs on timescales τ ≲ 10⁻¹³ s
- Beta decay takes place over time scales ranging from a few picoseconds up to years
- The distance in deformation space between the scission point and the fission isomer may be four to five times larger than the distance between the fission isomer and the ground state
- Uncertainties from nuclear models alone can reach a few MeV in fission barrier heights, corresponding to dozens of orders of magnitude in spontaneous fission half-lives
- The energy released in fission is of the order of about 200 MeV for actinide nuclei

## Context

This review extends earlier work by Schunck (2016) on microscopic methods, Andreyev (2017) on experimental results, and Schmidt (2018) on phenomenology, but differs by covering the entire fission process including fragment deexcitation, which previous reviews had seldom addressed. The paper aims to bridge the disconnect between the basic nuclear science community focused on understanding fission mechanisms and the specialized user communities that employ fission data in evaluated nuclear data libraries such as JEFF and ENDF, as well as in applications like nucleosynthesis studies and the antineutrino reactor anomaly.
