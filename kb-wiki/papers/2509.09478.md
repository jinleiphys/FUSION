# The role of near neutron drip-line nuclei in the $r$-process

> The role of near neutron-drip-line nuclei in the rapid neutron-capture process ($r$-process) is studied with the classical $r$-process model. Simulations under different astrophysical conditions ($T$, $n_n$) show that $r$-process paths approach the neutron-drip line under low-temperature and high-neutron-density conditions. A sensitivity study reveals that variations in the nuclear masses of these exotic nuclei significantly impact the abundances of superheavy nuclei, and lead to obvious abundance variations in the $A=110-125$, $A=175-185$, and $A=200-205$ regions. By contrast, the $r$-process rare-earth peak and the $A=130,195$ peaks remain largely unaffected. The nuclei that obviously impact $r$-process abundances are mainly distributed in the region of $25\leq Z\leq 90$ and $50\leq N\leq 180$, with the nuclei around neutron magic numbers found to be particularly important for the $r$-process, even in the near-neutron-drip-line region.

## Key claim

Under low-temperature and high-neutron-density astrophysical conditions, the r-process path approaches the neutron drip line, and variations in the nuclear masses of these near-drip-line nuclei significantly impact the abundances of superheavy nuclei and produce abundance variations in the A=110-125, A=175-185, and A=200-205 regions, while the rare-earth peak and the A=130 and A=195 peaks remain largely unaffected.

## Method

The authors used the classical r-process model, a site-independent approach where iron-group seed nuclei are irradiated by high-density neutron sources at temperatures above 1.5 GK. They performed r-process simulations under different combinations of temperature (T) and neutron number density (n_n), using nuclear masses from the WS4 mass model (or experimental data where available) and beta-decay rates from an empirical formula. A sensitivity study was conducted by varying predicted nuclear masses by ±0.5 MeV for collections of neutron-rich nuclei classified by their distance from the neutron-drip line, and the resulting abundance variations were quantified.

## Key numbers

- Four sets of astrophysical conditions were selected for sensitivity studies: n_n = 10^25 cm^-3, 10^27 cm^-3, 10^28 cm^-3, and 10^30 cm^-3, all with fixed irradiation time τ = 850 ms and temperature T = 1.5 GK.
- Nuclear masses were varied by ΔM = ±0.5 MeV in the sensitivity studies.
- The nuclei that obviously impact r-process abundances are distributed in the region 25 ≤ Z ≤ 90 and 50 ≤ N ≤ 180.
- Abundance variations were observed in the mass regions A = 110-125, A = 175-185, and A = 200-205.
- The average distance L between the r-process path and the neutron-drip line decreases monotonically with increasing neutron density and decreasing temperature across all mass regions.

## Context

This work extends sensitivity studies similar to those of Mumpower (2015, 2016) and Jiang (2021) by focusing specifically on near-neutron-drip-line nuclei and their role under extreme astrophysical conditions. The results highlight that nuclei around neutron magic numbers (N=50, 82, 126) are particularly important even near the drip line, and that the rare-earth peak region (between N=82 and N=126) shows significant sensitivity for nuclei located over a dozen nucleons from the drip line, motivating more accurate nuclear mass predictions for these exotic nuclei. The study enables future work to prioritize which near-drip-line nuclei require experimental or theoretical attention to reduce r-process abundance uncertainties.
