# Kinetic equilibration in heavy ion collisions: The role of elastic processes

> We study the kinetic equilibration of gluons produced in the very early stages of a high energy heavy ion collision in a ``self-consistent'' relaxation time approximation. We compare two scenarios describing the initial state of the gluon system, namely the saturation and the minijet scenarios, both at RHIC and LHC energies. We argue that, in order to characterize kinetic equilibration, it is relevant to test the isotropy of various observables. As a consequence, we find in particular that in both scenarios elastic processes are not sufficient for the system to reach kinetic equilibrium at RHIC energies. More generally, we show that, contrary to what is often assumed in the literature, elastic collisions alone are not sufficient to rapidly achieve kinetic equilibration. Because of longitudinal expansion at early times, the actual equilibration time is at least of the order of a few fermis.

# Kinetic equilibration in heavy ion collisions: The role of elastic processes

## Key claim

Elastic (2→2) collisions alone are not sufficient to rapidly achieve kinetic equilibration of the gluon system produced in high-energy heavy ion collisions, contrary to what is often assumed in the literature; because of longitudinal expansion at early times, the actual equilibration time is at least of the order of a few fermis, and at RHIC energies the system does not reach kinetic equilibrium via elastic processes in either the saturation or minijet scenario.

## Method

The authors solve the Boltzmann equation for gluons in a self-consistent relaxation time approximation, considering only elastic gg→gg collisions in the small-angle scattering limit with logarithmic accuracy. They assume one-dimensional longitudinal expansion with boost invariance and treat two initial-state scenarios: the saturation scenario (with initial gluons having transverse momentum ~ Qs and zero longitudinal momentum) and the minijet scenario (initial Boltzmann distribution from perturbative QCD). The relaxation time is computed self-consistently from the Landau-Mueller collision term, and the system is evolved using conservation of energy and particle number.

## Key numbers

- Saturation scenario at RHIC: Qs = 1.0 GeV, initial time t0 = 0.4 fm, initial gluon density n(t0) = 18.1 fm⁻³, initial energy density ε(t0) = 12.0 GeV/fm³
- Saturation scenario at LHC: Qs = 2.0 GeV, t0 = 0.18 fm, n(t0) = 163.4 fm⁻³, ε(t0) = 217.9 GeV/fm³
- Minijet scenario at RHIC: t0 = 0.18 fm, njet = 34.3 fm⁻³, εjet = 56.0 GeV/fm³, Tjet = 0.535 GeV
- Minijet scenario at LHC: t0 = 0.09 fm, njet = 321.6 fm⁻³, εjet = 1110.0 GeV/fm³, Tjet = 1.13 GeV
- In the saturation scenario at RHIC, the anisotropy ratio Rk (ratio of longitudinal to transverse momentum moments) remains far from 1 even at t ~ 10 fm
- In the saturation scenario at LHC, Rk ≈ 0.8 at t ~ 10 fm
- In the minijet scenario at LHC, Rk ≲ 0.8 until t ~ 30 fm
- The maximum time for partonic description (density > 1 fm⁻³) is tmax ~ 10 fm at RHIC and tmax ~ 30 fm at LHC for both scenarios

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Bjoraker and Venugopalan (2001) on the saturation scenario and by Nayak, Dumitru, McLerran, and Greiner (2001) on the minijet scenario, using a simpler relaxation time method that reproduces their exact solutions semi-quantitatively. The authors contradict the common assumption (e.g., Biro, Muller, Thoma, Wang, 1993; Baym, Monien, Pethick, Ravenhall, 1990) that kinetic equilibrium is achieved on timescales ≲ 1 fm via elastic collisions, confirming earlier suggestions by Wong (1996) and Heiselberg and Wang (1996). The results motivate the inclusion of inelastic branching processes, as proposed by Baier, Mueller, Schiff, and Son (2001), which may play a more important role in achieving kinetic equilibration.
