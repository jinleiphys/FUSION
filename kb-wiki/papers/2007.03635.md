# Efficient emulators for scattering using eigenvector continuation

> Eigenvector continuation EC has been shown to accurately and efficiently reproduce ground states for targeted sets of Hamiltonian parameters. It uses as variational basis vectors the corresponding ground-state eigensolutions from selected other sets of parameters. Here we extend the EC approach to scattering using the Kohn variational principle. We first test it using a model for S-wave nucleon-nucleon scattering and then demonstrate that it also works to give accurate predictions for non-local potentials, charged-particle scattering, complex optical potentials, and higher partial waves. These proofs-of-principle validate EC as an accurate emulator for applying Bayesian inference to parameter estimation constrained by scattering observables. The efficiency of such emulators is because the accuracy is achieved with a small number of variational basis elements and the central computations are just linear algebra calculations in the space spanned by this basis.

## Key claim
Eigenvector continuation (EC), previously demonstrated for bound-state problems, is extended to scattering by combining it with the Kohn variational principle, yielding accurate emulators for two-body scattering observables across a range of potential types and partial waves, with the central computations reduced to linear algebra in a low-dimensional space.

## Method
The authors construct a trial scattering wave function as a linear combination of exact scattering solutions from a small set of Hamiltonian parameter values (the EC basis). They apply the Kohn variational principle to derive a stationary approximation to the partial-wave K-matrix (tan delta), which reduces to solving a small matrix inversion problem involving matrix elements of the potential differences between the target and basis Hamiltonians. The method is tested on S-wave nucleon-nucleon scattering using the Minnesota potential, then extended to non-local potentials, charged-particle scattering with Coulomb potentials, complex optical potentials, and higher partial waves.

## Key numbers
- Basis size Nb ~ 10 is sufficient for accurate emulation in the test examples
- A nugget (regularization parameter) between 10^-10 and 10^-8 is used to stabilize the matrix inversion when ill-conditioning occurs
- The direct R-matrix calculations involve matrices with dimensions of order 10^2, compared to the EC basis dimension of ~10
- The Minnesota potential test uses S-wave channels (^1S_0 and ^3S_1) with a sum of local Gaussian terms

## Context
This work extends the eigenvector continuation approach of Frame (2017) and Frame (2019) from ground-state bound systems to scattering problems, building on the Kohn variational principle (Kohn, 1948) and prior variational scattering methods by Newton (2002), Taylor (2006), and Nesbet (1980). The emulators enable Bayesian inference for parameter estimation constrained by scattering observables, as previously demonstrated for bound-state properties by Konig (2019) and Ekstrom (2019) using chiral effective field theory Hamiltonians. The method is particularly aimed at enabling computationally expensive few-body scattering calculations (e.g., nucleon-deuteron scattering as in Kievsky (1997) and Witala (2019)) to be used in large-scale parameter sampling.
