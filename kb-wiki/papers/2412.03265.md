# Evolution of shell structure at $\mathbf{N=32}$ and 34: Insights from realistic nuclear forces

> We investigated the evolution of shell structure at $N=32$ and 34 in neutron-rich nuclei beyond the stability line using realistic nuclear forces, employing the state-of-the-art valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group method. The shell gaps are discussed from the excitation energies of the first $2^+$ states and the evolution of effective single-particle energies. We addressed different components of the nuclear interaction--central, spin-orbit, and tensor--and their roles in the development of shell gaps far from stability. The calculated results align well with the available experimental data and suggest a strengthening of the $N=34$ subshell gap and a weakening of the $N=32$ subshell gap below Ca. Additionally, the low-energy structures of the exotic $N=32$ isotones below Ca revealed that their ground states exhibit large deformation and coexist with a weakly deformed band at low excitation energy. The present work demonstrates essential components of the nuclear force in shaping magic numbers far from stability and provides deeper insights into the structure of exotic nuclei from the underlying nuclear forces.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the $N=34$ subshell gap strengthens and persists below Ca (down to Si), while the $N=32$ subshell gap weakens and vanishes below Ca, based on valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations using chiral two- and three-nucleon forces. The abstract's claim of "suggest a strengthening of the $N=34$ subshell gap and a weakening of the $N=32$ subshell gap below Ca" is qualified by the full text's finding that the $N=32$ subshell gap is "weak or does not extend below Ca" and that the $N=34$ shell gap "continues to widen" in S and Si.

## Method
The authors used the valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) method starting from the chiral EM1.8/2.0 interaction (N3LO two-nucleon potential evolved to lambda=1.8 fm^-1 and N2LO three-nucleon force with cutoff Lambda=2.0 fm^-1). They performed IMSRG(2) calculations in a harmonic oscillator basis at hbar omega=16 MeV with emax=12 and E3max=24, decoupling effective Hamiltonians for the fp shell (Z>=20) or sd proton and fp neutron shells (Z<20) above a 40Ca or 28O core, then diagonalized with the KSHELL code. Spin-tensor decomposition of the VS-IMSRG interactions separated central (k=0), spin-orbit (k=1), and tensor (k=2) components to analyze their contributions to shell gaps.

## Key numbers
- The N=32 subshell gap (nu p1/2 - nu p3/2 splitting) decreases by approximately 0.35 MeV from Ca to Ar within VS-IMSRG interactions.
- The N=34 shell gap in 52Ar is comparatively larger (by approximately 0.3 MeV) than that in 54Ca, and continues to widen in 50S and 46Si.
- Ground state neutron occupancy of the nu p1/2 orbital in 52Ca is approximately 0.16, while in 50Ar it is approximately 1.005, indicating quenching of the N=32 shell gap.
- Ground state neutron occupancy of the nu f5/2 orbital in 52Ar and 50S remains small (approximately 0.7), reflecting the prominence of the N=34 shell gap.
- Spectroscopic quadrupole moments Q_s(2_1^+) are 10.8 e fm^2 for 50Ar, 6.1 e fm^2 for 48S, and 8.5 e fm^2 for 46Si, indicating oblate deformation in the ground state band.

## Context
This work extends ab initio studies of shell evolution using the VS-IMSRG method, which previously reproduced the breaking of N=20 and N=28 shell gaps and the appearance of N=14 as a magic number (Miyagi 2022, Yuan 2024). It contrasts with phenomenological interactions like GXPF1Br (Steppenbeck 2013) by starting from chiral two- and three-nucleon forces, and addresses limitations of earlier microscopic approaches (Smirnova 2012) that lacked three-body forces. The results provide predictions for exotic N=32 isotones (48S, 46Si) that are of current experimental interest (Rosenbusch 2015, Liu 2019, Cortes 2020, Linh 2024) and demonstrate the essential roles of central and tensor force components in shaping magic numbers far from stability.
