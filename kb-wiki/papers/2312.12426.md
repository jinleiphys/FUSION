# ROSE: A reduced-order scattering emulator for optical models

> A new generation of phenomenological optical potentials requires robust calibration and uncertainty quantification, motivating the use of Bayesian statistical methods. These Bayesian methods usually require calculating observables for thousands or even millions of parameter sets, making fast and accurate emulators highly desirable or even essential. Emulating scattering across different energies or with interactions such as optical potentials is challenging because of the non-affine parameter dependence, meaning the parameters do not all factorize from individual operators. Here we introduce and demonstrate the Reduced Order Scattering Emulator (ROSE) framework, a reduced basis emulator that can handle non-affine problems. ROSE is fully extensible and works within the publicly available BAND Framework software suite for calibration, model mixing, and experimental design. As a demonstration problem, we use ROSE to calibrate a realistic nucleon-target scattering model through the calculation of elastic cross sections. This problem shows the practical value of the ROSE framework for Bayesian uncertainty quantification with controlled trade-offs between emulator speed and accuracy as compared to high-fidelity solvers. Planned extensions of ROSE are discussed.

## Key claim

ROSE introduces a reduced basis emulator that can handle non-affine parameter dependence in optical-model scattering, enabling Bayesian calibration with controlled trade-offs between emulator speed and accuracy compared to high-fidelity solvers, but the demonstration is limited to neutrons (emulated across energies and potential parameters) and protons (emulated only across potential parameters, not energy).

## Method

The authors construct a reduced basis for the radial Schrödinger equation solutions using proper orthogonal decomposition (PCA) of high-fidelity "snapshot" wavefunctions, then apply a Galerkin projection to create a reduced linear system for the expansion coefficients. To handle non-affine parameters (radius, diffuseness, energy), they employ the empirical interpolation method (EIM) to build a separable approximation of the Woods-Saxon optical potential, selecting interpolation points via the MaxVol algorithm. The emulator is trained offline on a set of parameter snapshots, then evaluated online by solving a small linear system for each new parameter set, with separate reduced bases for each partial-wave channel.

## Key numbers

* The emulator target is to perform more than one million evaluations per hour on commodity hardware while maintaining less than 10% error.
* The optical potential has ten parameters: V_v, W_v, R_v, a_v, W_d, R_d, a_d, V_so, R_so, a_so.
* The high-fidelity solver uses absolute and relative tolerances of 10^{-9} in scipy.integrate.solve_ivp.
* The spin-orbit scale factor uses (hbar/m_pi c)^2 ≈ 2 fm^2.

## Context

This work extends the reduced basis method (RBM) framework previously applied to scattering problems by Furnstahl et al. (2020), Drischler et al. (2021), and Melendez et al. (2021, 2022), which work best for affine parameter dependence. ROSE specifically addresses the non-affine challenge posed by Woods-Saxon radii, diffuseness, and energy dependence in optical potentials, building on the Galerkin formulation of Bonilla et al. (2022). The emulator is integrated into the BAND Framework software suite for calibration, model mixing, and experimental design, and is intended to enable Bayesian uncertainty quantification for the new generation of global optical potentials called for by Hebborn et al. (2023).
