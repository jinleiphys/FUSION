# To what extent is Gluon Confinement an empirical fact?

> Experimental verifications of Confinement in hadron physics have established the absence of charges with a fraction of the electron's charge by studying the energy deposited in ionization tracks at high energies, and performing Millikan experiments with charged droplets at rest. These experiments test only the absence of particles with fractional charge in the asymptotic spectrum, and thus "Quark" Confinement. However what theory suggests is that Color is confined, that is, all asymptotic particles are color singlets. Since QCD is a non-Abelian theory, the gluon force carriers (indirectly revealed in hadron jets) are colored. We empirically examine what can be said about Gluon Confinement based on the lack of detection of appropriate events, aiming at an upper bound for high-energy free-gluon production.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while quark confinement is empirically well-established through searches for fractional electric charges, gluon confinement (the absence of asymptotic colored particles that are electrically neutral) has not been quantitatively tested. The authors aim to provide a methodology for setting an upper bound on free gluon production cross-sections, acknowledging that their bound of approximately 10 fbarn is an ultimate limit that current experimental methods cannot yet reach.

## Method

The authors propose using secondary proton tagging in detector material to identify hypothetical free gluons produced in proton-proton collisions. A free gluon would propagate as a neutral particle, then interact with nucleons in the beampipe or inner tracker via the reaction g + A → g' + p + X, ejecting a detectable proton. They estimate the gluon-proton cross-section using Regge theory with color-factor scaling from proton-proton scattering, finding it to be about 81 times larger than the proton-proton cross-section due to color factors. They then simulate the dominant neutron background using PYTHIA 8 and propose using detector tomography (the different mean free paths of gluons versus neutrons) to distinguish signal from background.

## Key numbers

- The estimated gluon-proton total cross-section is approximately 3 barn at high energies, compared to the proton-proton cross-section of about 40-50 mbarn
- The mean free path of a gluon in beryllium is λ_max = 0.6 cm, and in silicon λ = 0.5 cm
- The ultimate bound on free gluon production cross-section is σ(E_g ≥ 12 GeV)_{pp→g+X} ≤ O(10 fbarn) for gluon energies above 12 GeV
- The bound calculation uses integrated luminosity L ≃ 760 pb^{-1} (LHC data accumulated by June 5th 2011), yielding a numerical value of 3.5 fbarn
- The minimum momentum transfer to the secondary proton for TOF identification is t_0 = -(190 MeV)^2, corresponding to a minimum kinetic energy of 40 MeV
- The ALICE detector acceptance is Ω/(4π) ≃ 0.76 for pseudorapidity range (-1,1)
- The neutron background at 5 GeV energy threshold is approximately 0.5 mbarn; at 12 GeV it falls to the microbarn level

## Context

This work extends the experimental tradition of quark confinement searches, specifically the Millikan-type experiments by Perl (2009) and ionization track measurements from the Particle Data Group (Nakamura 2010), which established bounds on free quark production cross-sections below 10 fbarn in proton collisions. The authors note that theoretical consensus from lattice computations, Dyson-Schwinger equations, and renormalization group studies (Alkofer 2003, Cucchieri 2007, Fischer 2009) indicates the gluon propagator violates reflection positivity, preventing gluon propagation beyond about a Fermi. The paper provides a quantitative framework for experimental collaborations like ALICE to convert the absence of gluon-like events into actual cross-section bounds, and notes potential applications to mixed hadron-quark-gluon phases in heavy-ion collisions at facilities such as ALICE, STAR, and CBM at FAIR.
