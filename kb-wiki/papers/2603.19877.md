# Phase Diagram and Finite Temperature Properties of Negative Coupling Scalar Field Theory

> In this work, I consider scalar field theory with negative quartic self-interaction, corresponding to an upside-down classical potential. Despite not possessing a classically stable ground state, such potentials are known to behave properly when treated quantum mechanically, leading to stable and unitary time evolution. Using two different saddle-point expansions for the same theory, I discuss the phase diagram in terms of bare parameters in Euclidean dimensions one to four, as well as the generalization to finite temperature. Comparing to other methods where available, I find that negative coupling field theory is a promising candidate for an interacting scalar field theory in the continuum. In particular, in four dimensions it exploits a loophole in mathematical proofs of quantum triviality, suggesting that negative coupling scalar field theory could offer a UV-complete and interacting description of the Higgs.

## Key claim

Negative coupling scalar field theory, despite lacking a classically stable ground state, can be treated quantum mechanically to yield stable and unitary time evolution; in four dimensions it exploits a loophole in mathematical proofs of quantum triviality, potentially offering a UV-complete and interacting description of the Higgs field, contingent on the theory not being in the Griffiths-Simon class for which the triviality proofs apply.

## Method

The author uses two distinct saddle-point expansions (symmetric and broken phase) of the Euclidean path integral for a scalar field with action \(S = \int dx [\frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu \phi \partial_\mu \phi + \frac{1}{2}m_B^2 \phi^2 - g_B \phi^4]\), applying the R1 resummation technique to rewrite the quartic interaction. The resulting saddle-point equations for the pole mass and pressure are solved analytically or numerically in dimensions \(d = 1, 2, 3, 4\) at both zero and finite temperature, with non-perturbative renormalization performed in \(d = 2\) and \(d = 4\) using the \(\overline{\rm MS}\) scheme. The thermodynamically preferred phase is determined by comparing the pressures from the two saddle expansions.

## Key numbers

* In \(d=1\) at zero temperature, the transition from broken to symmetric phase occurs at \(m_B^2 \simeq 6.66 \, g_B^{2/3}\).
* In \(d=2\) at zero temperature, the critical coupling for the transition is \(g_c \equiv g_B / \Lambda_{\overline{\rm MS}}^2 \simeq 3.2894\).
* In \(d=2\) at zero temperature, at the critical coupling the symmetric pole mass satisfies \(g_B / M^2 \simeq 0.64\).
* In \(d=3\) at zero temperature, the critical value is \(m_B^2 / g_B^2 \simeq -0.21\) with \(g_B^2 / M^2 \simeq 2.68\).
* In \(d=3\) for \(m_B^2 = 0.5 g_B^2\), the critical temperature is \(T_c \simeq 0.71 \, g_B\).
* In \(d=4\) at high temperature, the approximate transition temperature is \(T_c \simeq 0.564 \, \Lambda_{\overline{\rm MS}}\).

## Context

This work extends recent studies of negative coupling and non-Hermitian quantum field theories (e.g., Romatschke (2022), Lawrence (2023), Ai (2022)) by providing a systematic survey of the phase diagram across dimensions using saddle-point expansions that were cross-checked against other methods for positive coupling in Romatschke (2026). It contradicts the assumption that scalar field theory in four dimensions must be trivial, exploiting a loophole in the triviality proof of Aizenman (2019) that only applies to the Griffiths-Simon class, as noted earlier by Aizenman (1983). The results enable future numerical lattice studies using contour deformation methods (Romatschke (2023)) to probe the continuum limit of negative coupling scalar field theory.
