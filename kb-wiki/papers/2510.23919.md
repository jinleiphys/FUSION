# Thermal nature of confining strings

> We investigate the quantum statistical properties of the confining string connecting a static fermion-antifermion pair in the massive Schwinger model. By analyzing the reduced density matrix of the subsystem located in between the fermion and antifermion, we demonstrate that as the interfermion separation approaches the string-breaking distance, the overlap between the microscopic density matrix and an effective thermal density matrix exhibits a pronounced, narrow peak, approaching unity at the onset of string breaking. This behavior reveals that the confining flux tube evolves toward a genuinely thermal state as the separation between the charges grows, even in the absence of an external heat bath. In other words, one cannot tell whether a reduced state of the subsystem arises from a surrounding heat bath or from entanglement with the rest of the system. The entanglement spectrum near the critical string-breaking distance exhibits a rapid transition from the dominance of a single state describing the confining electric string towards a strongly entangled state containing virtual fermion-antifermion pairs. Our findings establish a quantitative link between confinement, entanglement, and emergent thermality, and suggest that string breaking corresponds to a microscopic thermalization transition within the flux tube.

## Key claim

As the separation between a static fermion and antifermion in the massive Schwinger model approaches the string-breaking distance, the reduced density matrix of the subsystem located between the charges becomes nearly indistinguishable from a thermal density matrix, with the overlap between the two approaching unity at the onset of string breaking, but this thermalization occurs only for the subsystem region and only near the critical separation, not for all separations or for the full system.

## Method

The authors study the ground state of the massive Schwinger model on a spatial lattice with N=220 sites, using staggered fermions and the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map the system to a spin chain. They introduce static external charges at varying separations d to create a confining flux tube, then compute the reduced density matrix of a centered subsystem of length L (either 8 or 12 sites) by tracing out all other sites. They compare this reduced density matrix to thermal density matrices constructed via purification for a system of the same size without external sources, using the normalized overlap and Hilbert-Schmidt distance as metrics to extract an effective temperature.

## Key numbers

* Critical string-breaking distance: d_c · M_s ≃ 7, defined as the distance where the derivative of the ground-state potential drops by 80% of its plateau value
* Linear fit of the ground-state potential at short distances: V/M_s ∼ 0.193 d · M_s
* Plateau value of the ground-state potential after string breaking: V/M_s ∼ 1.09
* Fermion mass: m = 0.045 a⁻¹; gauge coupling: g = 0.09 a⁻¹
* Lattice size: N = 220 sites; subsystem sizes studied: L = 8 and L = 12 sites

## Context

This work extends the real-time simulations of jet fragmentation in the massive Schwinger model by Florio et al. (2025), which showed that the reduced density matrix becomes thermal at late times during dynamical evolution. The present study demonstrates that this emergent thermality is present already in static confining strings, not only as a consequence of real-time evolution. The results build on the tensor-network study of Buyens et al. (2015) that characterized confinement and string breaking through static observables and bipartite entanglement entropy in the same model. The finding that string breaking corresponds to a microscopic thermalization transition within the flux tube provides a stationary realization of entanglement-induced thermality that may underlie the universal thermal patterns observed in QCD hadronization.
