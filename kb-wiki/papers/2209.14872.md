# Precision QCD, Hadronic Structure & Forward QCD, Heavy Ions: Report of Energy Frontier Topical Groups 5, 6, 7 submitted to Snowmass 2021

> This report was prepared on behalf of three Energy Frontier Topical Groups of the Snowmass 2021 Community Planning Exercise. It summarizes the status and implications of studies of strong interactions in high-energy experiments and QCD theory. We emphasize the rich landscape and broad impact of these studies in the decade ahead. Hadronic interactions play a central role in the high-luminosity Large Hadron Collider (LHC) physics program, and strong synergies exist between the (HL-)LHC and planned or proposed experiments at the U.S. Electron-Ion Collider, CERN forward physics experiments, high-intensity facilities, and future TeV-range lepton and hadron colliders. Prospects for precision determinations of the strong coupling and a variety of nonperturbative distribution and fragmentation functions are examined. We also review the potential of envisioned tests of new dynamical regimes of QCD in high-energy and high-density scattering processes with nucleon, ion, and photon initial states. The important role of the high-energy heavy-ion program in studies of nuclear structure and the nuclear medium, and its connections with QCD involving nucleons are summarized. We address ongoing and future theoretical advancements in multi-loop QCD computations, lattice QCD, jet substructure, and event generators. Cross-cutting connections between experimental measurements, theoretical predictions, large-scale data analysis, and high-performance computing are emphasized.

## Key claim

This Snowmass 2021 community report summarizes the status and future opportunities for QCD studies across multiple Energy Frontier topical groups, arguing that the upcoming decade featuring the HL-LHC, Belle II, the EIC, and potential Higgs factories will constitute a "new golden age for QCD" rivaling the 1990s era of Tevatron, HERA, and LEP, provided that precision theoretical calculations and Monte Carlo simulations keep pace with experimental capabilities.

## Method

The report synthesizes contributions from three Energy Frontier Topical Groups (EF05, EF06, EF07) of the Snowmass 2021 Community Planning Exercise, drawing on white papers and feedback presented at topical group meetings. It surveys the status and projected capabilities of multiple experimental facilities (HL-LHC, Forward Physics Facility, EIC, Belle II, future lepton colliders) and theoretical approaches (multi-loop QCD computations, lattice QCD, jet substructure, event generators) to identify synergies and priorities for the coming decade.

## Key numbers

- Projected reduction in uncertainty on the strong coupling alpha_s to the permille range from future e+e- facilities and lattice QCD
- EIC center-of-mass energy range sqrt(s) = 20-140 GeV with instantaneous luminosity up to L = 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1
- HL-LHC average pileup expected to reach 150-200 events per bunch crossing
- Forward Physics Facility neutrinos with rapidity y greater than 6 can be identified
- Subpercent precision expected for bottom quark mass at future Higgs factories
- Belle II multi-ab^-1 data set enabling new approaches to suppress systematic uncertainties

## Context

This report extends the community planning efforts of previous Snowmass exercises and the European Strategy for Particle Physics, synthesizing input from multiple white papers including those on the Forward Physics Facility (Anchordoqui 2021, Feng 2022), the Electron-Ion Collider (AbdulKhalek 2022), the strong coupling constant (d'Enterria 2022), and event generators (Campbell 2022). It identifies strong synergies between the HL-LHC program and planned experiments at the EIC, CERN forward physics experiments, high-intensity facilities, and future TeV-range lepton and hadron colliders. The report enables coordinated planning across experimental, theoretical, and computational domains, emphasizing that progress in any one area of QCD depends on advances in the others.
