# Importance of chiral constraints for the pole content of the $\bar{K}N$ scattering amplitude

> We critically examine the $\bar{K}N$ coupled-channel approach presented in [1] and demonstrate that it violates constraints imposed by chiral symmetry of QCD. The origin of this violation can be traced back to the off-shell treatment of the chiral-effective vertices, in combination with the use of non-relativistic approximations and the chosen regularization scheme. We propose an improved version of the approach, which is directly given by a resummation of relativistic Feynman graphs of baryon chiral perturbation theory, and is in accord with the chiral symmetry constraint. Within this improved model, two poles are generated dynamically in the isoscalar $πΣ- \bar{K}N$ coupled channels sector, in contrast with the non-relativistic model of [1] in which only one such pole was reported.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the non-relativistic coupled-channel approach of Revai (2017) violates chiral symmetry constraints due to its off-shell treatment of vertices, non-relativistic approximations, and regularization scheme; when these issues are corrected with a relativistic formulation, two poles are generated dynamically in the isoscalar πΣ–\(\bar{K}N\) sector, contradicting the claim that only one pole exists.

## Method

The authors critically examine the Revai (2017) approach by rewriting its Lippmann-Schwinger equation solution in a form that reveals the effective potential contains tadpole integrals that break chiral symmetry. They then construct an improved "BC" model that uses relativistic Feynman graph resummation of baryon chiral perturbation theory, with the same cutoff function but a softened effective interaction kernel proportional to \(\sqrt{s}-M\). Both models are fitted to low-energy \(K^{-}p\) experimental data including total cross sections, threshold branching ratios, and kaonic hydrogen observables, with the pole content extracted from the second Riemann sheet.

## Key numbers

- The JR model yields only one isoscalar pole in the \(\Lambda(1405)\) region, while the BC model generates two isoscalar poles (positions given in Table 3 of the paper).
- The BC\(_1\) fit gives \(F_\pi = 105.9\) MeV and \(F_K = 105.9\) MeV (fixed ratio \(F_K/F_\pi = 1\)), with \(\chi^2/\text{dof} = 2.39\).
- The BC\(_2\) fit gives \(F_\pi = 89.4\) MeV and \(F_K = 106.6\) MeV (ratio \(F_K/F_\pi = 1.193\)), with \(\chi^2/\text{dof} = 2.93\).
- The BC\(_1\) model predicts kaonic hydrogen energy shift \(\Delta E_{1s} = 285\) eV and width \(\Gamma_{1s} = 666\) eV, compared to experimental values \(283(42)\) eV and \(541(111)\) eV respectively.
- The BC model scattering length vanishes in the three-flavor chiral limit as required by chiral symmetry, unlike the JR model which gives a non-zero value of order \(\mathcal{O}(m^0)\).

## Context

This work extends the ongoing debate about the two-pole structure of the \(\Lambda(1405)\) resonance in chirally-motivated coupled-channel approaches. It directly contradicts the conclusion of Revai (2017) that chiral-based interactions lead to only one pole when on-shell factorization is avoided. The improved BC model confirms the findings of Morimatsu (2019) and Mai (2012) that two poles persist even without on-shell factorization, and enables more reliable few-body and many-body calculations of antikaon-nucleon interactions by providing amplitudes that respect chiral symmetry constraints.
