# A model for projectile fragmentation

> A model for projectile fragmentation is developed whose origin can be traced back to the Bevalac era. The model positions itself between the phenomenological EPAX parametrization and transport models like "Heavy Ion Phase Space Exploration" (HIPSE) model and antisymmetrised molecular dynamics (AMD) model. A very simple impact parameter dependence of input temperature is incorporated in the model which helps to analyze the more peripheral collisions. The model is applied to calculate the charge, isotopic distributions, average number of intermediate mass fragments and the average size of largest cluster at different Z_{bound} of different projectile fragmentation reactions at different energies.

# A model for projectile fragmentation

## Key claim
The model positions itself between the phenomenological EPAX parametrization and transport models (HIPSE and AMD), offering a computationally less intensive approach that incorporates a simple impact-parameter-dependent temperature profile to analyze peripheral collisions. The model is applicable and implementable above 100 MeV/nucleon, specifically in the limiting fragmentation region where temperature becomes independent of incident beam energy.

## Method
The model proceeds in three stages: (i) abrasion, where straight-line geometry determines the projectile spectator volume as a function of impact parameter, with a minimal distribution model converting average neutron and proton numbers into integer values; (ii) multifragmentation, where each abraded projectile spectator undergoes breakup calculated via the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) at a temperature that depends on impact parameter through the relation T(b) = 7.5 - 4.5 × (A_s(b)/A_0); and (iii) evaporation, where excited fragments decay via Weisskopf's evaporation theory including emission of n, p, d, t, ³He, ⁴He, gamma rays, and fission.

## Key numbers
- Temperature profile parameters: D₀ = 7.5 and D₁ = -4.5, giving T(b) = 7.5 - 4.5 × (A_s(b)/A_0) where A_s(b)/A_0 is the fraction of projectile remaining after abrasion
- Volume energy coefficient: W₀ = 15.8 MeV
- Surface energy coefficient: σ₀ = 18.0 MeV with critical temperature T_c = 18.0 MeV
- Coulomb energy coefficient: a_c = 0.72 MeV with Wigner-Seitz approximation
- Symmetry energy: C_sym = 23.5 MeV
- Level density parameter: ε₀ = 16.0 MeV
- Freeze-out volume: V_f = 3V₀ (three times normal nuclear volume)
- IMF definition: fragments with charge 3 ≤ Z ≤ 20
- Reactions studied include ⁵⁸Ni on ⁹Be at 140 MeV/nucleon, ⁵⁸Ni on ¹⁸¹Ta at 140 MeV/nucleon, ¹²⁹Xe on ²⁷Al at 790 MeV/nucleon, ¹³⁶Xe on ²⁰⁸Pb at 1 GeV/nucleon, and ¹⁰⁷Sn and ¹²⁴Sn on ¹¹⁹Sn at 600A MeV

## Context
This work extends the Bevalac-era concepts of projectile fragmentation by bridging the gap between the purely empirical EPAX parametrization (Summerer) and transport-based models such as HIPSE (Lacroix) and AMD (Ono). The model enables systematic calculation of charge distributions, isotopic distributions, intermediate mass fragment multiplicities, and largest cluster sizes across a wide range of projectile-target combinations and energies, using a single temperature profile. The authors plan to improve the model by deriving the initial projectile spectator size and excitation from microscopic BUU calculations.
