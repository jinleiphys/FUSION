# The shapes of nuclei

> Gerry Brown initiated some early studies on the coexistence of different nuclear shapes. The subject has continued to be of interest and is crucial for understanding nuclear fission. We now have a very good picture of the potential energy surface with respect to shape degrees of freedom in heavy nuclei, but the dynamics remain problematic. In contrast, the early studies on light nuclei were quite successful in describing the mixing between shapes. Perhaps a new approach in the spirit of the old calculations could better elucidate the character of the fission dynamics and explain phenomena that current theory does not model well.

# The shapes of nuclei

## Key claim

The Generator Coordinate Method (GCM), the only practical approach for treating shape dynamics in heavy nuclei, has significant limitations including numerical instabilities from non-orthogonal configurations and ambiguous interaction matrix elements when using density functional theory; a discrete configuration approach using DFT constrained by $K^\pi$ quantum number partitioning may provide a better alternative for understanding fission dynamics, though the interaction matrix elements still need to be determined.

## Method

The paper compares the GCM approach with a proposed $K^\pi$-constrained minimization method across three nuclear systems: $^{40}$Ca, $^{80}$Zr, and $^{236}$U. In the GCM approach, configurations are generated by minimizing the Hamiltonian in the presence of an external quadrupole field $\lambda \hat Q_{20}$. The alternative method constrains DFT minimization by the number of nucleons having given values of orbital quantum number $K$, parity $\pi$, and isospin projection $\tau_z$, producing automatically orthogonal wave functions. Calculations use the Gogny D1S density functional with 14 parameters.

## Key numbers

- $^{40}$Ca: measured quadrupole transition strength $B(E2, 2^+_1\!\rightarrow\!{0^+_2}) = 250 \pm 35$ e$^2$ fm$^4$; intrinsic mass quadrupole moment of the deformed band $\langle \hat Q_{20} \rangle = 117$ fm$^2$; observed band head at 3.35 MeV
- $^{40}$Ca: GCM calculation at the experimental deformation gives excitation energy 13 MeV, far above the observed band head; $K^\pi$-constrained 4p-4h configuration gives quadrupole moment 105 fm$^2$, agreeing well with experiment
- $^{80}$Zr: 12p-12h Nilsson configuration has quadrupole moment $Q_{20}\approx 400$ fm$^2$ at energy $\sim 3.5$ MeV above the global minimum; deformation parameter $\beta = 0.44\pm 0.01$ across multiple functionals
- $^{236}$U: path from first minimum (I) to barrier top (A) requires 6 pair jumps; from barrier top (A) to second minimum (II) requires 8 pair jumps; configuration II can be reached from I by changing only 6 pairs
- $^{208}$Pb: semiclassical formula for $M$ distribution uses $R=7.1$ fm, $k_F= 1.35$ fm$^{-1}$
- $^{40}$Ca: axis ratio $a_z/a_\perp \approx 1.55$ to model the band as an ellipsoidal rotor

## Context

This paper extends the early work on shape coexistence in light nuclei initiated by Gerry Brown and collaborators (Brown 1966) and continued by Gerace and Green (1967), who successfully described shape mixing with realistic interactions derived from nucleon-nucleon scattering data. The paper contrasts the successful configuration-interaction approach in light nuclei with the problematic GCM approach for heavy nuclei, citing the systematic Gogny DFT study by Delaroche et al. (2010) that achieved 10% accuracy for quadrupole transition moments in deformed nuclei. The proposed $K^\pi$-constrained method aims to enable a discrete configuration approach to fission dynamics, potentially explaining unexplained fluctuations in fission cross sections observed by Moore et al. (1978) and angular distribution anomalies found by Huizenga, Loveland, and collaborators (1969).
