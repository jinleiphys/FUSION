# Nucleosynthesis in neutron-rich ejecta from quark-novae

> We explore heavy-element nucleosynthesis by rapid neutron capture (r-process) in the decompressing ejecta from the surface of a neutron star. The decompression is triggered by a violent phase transition to strange quark matter (quark-nova scenario). The presence of neutron-rich large Z nuclei (40,95) < (Z,A) < (70,177), the large neutron-to-seed ratio, and the low electron fraction Ye ~ 0.03 in the decompressing ejecta present favorable conditions for the r-process. We perform network calculations that are adapted to the quark-nova conditions, and which mimic usual (n-gamma) equilibrium r-process calculations during the initially cold decompression phase. They match to dynamical r-process calculations at densities below neutron drip (4.10^11 g cm-3). We present results for the final element abundance distribution with and without heating from nuclear reactions, and compare to the solar abundance pattern of r-process elements. We highlight the distinguishing features of quark-novae by contrasting it with conventional nucleosynthetic sites such as type II supernovae and neutron star mergers, especially in the context of heavy-element compositions of extremely metal-deficient stars.

## Key claim

The quark-nova scenario can provide favorable conditions for the r-process (rapid neutron capture nucleosynthesis) in its decompressing ejecta, but the final abundance pattern is highly sensitive to the expansion timescale and the treatment of nuclear heating from beta-decays; the most promising reproduction of the heavy solar r-process distribution requires expansion timescales of order 1 ms, which are slower than the 0.01-0.1 ms timescales expected from the quark-nova's rapid conversion to strange quark matter.

## Method

The authors performed network calculations using the Clemson University nucleosynthesis code, tracking species from neutrons and protons up to uranium (Z=92, A=276) with fission cycling included. They modeled the decompression of initially cold neutron star matter (Ye=0.03, T9=1, entropy per nucleon s/kB=1) using a simplified pressure-driven expansion model, and compared results with and without heating from nuclear reactions, as well as with partial (50%) energy deposition to account for neutrino losses.

## Key numbers

- Initial electron fraction Ye = 0.03, corresponding to a Wigner-Seitz cell with Z=36, N=157 nucleus at density ~10^14 g/cm^3
- Expansion timescales explored: 0.01 ms (alpha=1), 0.05 ms (alpha=0.1), 0.1 ms (alpha=0.01), and 1 ms (alpha=0.0001)
- Neutron drip density: 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3, below which dynamical r-process calculations are matched
- Ejected mass fraction expected to be about 10^-2 solar masses (with core bounce), compared to 10^-5 solar masses without core bounce
- Only about 1% (10^51 ergs out of 10^53 ergs) of the energy released in the phase conversion leaves the core as neutrinos over typical mass ejection timescales
- Event rate for quark-nova from spin-down: 10^-4 to 10^-6 per year per Galaxy

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of r-process nucleosynthesis in decompressing neutron star matter by Meyer (1989) and Goriely et al. (2004), who found good agreement with the solar abundance pattern for A >= 140 nuclei but did not propose a specific decompression mechanism. The quark-nova scenario is contrasted with conventional sites such as type II supernovae (which face challenges in reproducing r-process conditions without parameterization) and neutron star mergers (which occur at lower rates of 3 x 10^-4 to 10^-6 per year and struggle to explain early Galactic chemical evolution). The results enable future comparisons with observations of extremely metal-poor stars such as CS 22892-052 and CS 31082-001, and motivate more detailed hydrodynamical models of the quark-nova explosion.
