# Chemical Equilibration at the Hagedorn Temperature

> One important question in relativistic heavy ion collisions is if hadrons, specifically anti-hyperons, are in equilibrium before thermal freezeout because strangeness enhancement has long been pointed to as a signature for Quark Gluon Plasma. Because anti-baryons have long equilibration times in the hadron gas phase it has been suggested that they are "born" into equilibrium. However, Hagedorn states, massive resonances, which are thought to appear near the critical temperature, contribute to fast chemical equilibration times for a hadron gas by providing extra degrees of freedom. Here we use master equations to describe the interplay between Hagedorn resonances, pions, and baryon anti-baryon pairs as they equilibrate over time and observe if the baryons and anti-baryons are fully equilibrated within the fireball.

# Chemical Equilibration at the Hagedorn Temperature

## Key claim

Baryon anti-baryon pairs can be "born" out of equilibrium after hadronization and then equilibrated by the subsequent population and decay of Hagedorn states, provided the temperature is between 160 and 180 MeV (i.e., slightly below the phase transition). The claim is hedged by the observation that when all particles start out of equilibrium, a minor deviation from full chemical equilibrium remains for the baryon anti-baryon pairs, and longer time scales arise from the need to reduce the effective number of pions in the system.

## Method

The authors solve a coupled set of rate equations (master equations) describing the time evolution of number densities for Hagedorn resonances, pions, and baryon anti-baryon pairs, assuming no net baryon density. The Hagedorn states are modeled with an exponential mass spectrum from 2 GeV to 7 GeV, with decay widths given by a linear fit to Particle Data Group data and branching ratios described by a Gaussian distribution. The baryon anti-baryon production proceeds via the reaction channel nπ ↔ HS ↔ nπ + B\bar{B}, with decay widths for the baryon channel estimated from microcanonical model multiplicities for protons, neutrons, and lambdas.

## Key numbers

- Hagedorn temperature: T_H = 180 MeV, with minimum mass m_0 = 500 MeV and maximum mass M = 7 GeV; free parameter A = 0.5
- Decay width of Hagedorn states: Γ_i = 0.17 m_i – 88, giving a range of Γ_i = 250 MeV to 1100 MeV
- Average pion number per Hagedorn decay: ⟨n⟩ = 0.6 + 0.3 (m_i / m_π), ranging from approximately 5 to 16
- Width of branching ratio distribution: σ = 0.26 √(m_i / m_π)
- Equilibration time for Hagedorn states decaying only to pions (pions held in equilibrium): τ ≈ 0.2 fm/c to 0.5 fm/c
- Effective pion equilibration time (Hagedorn resonances held in equilibrium): τ ≈ 0.02 fm/c to 0.25 fm/c
- Total baryon multiplicity per Hagedorn decay: between 0.2 and 0.4, giving decay width Γ_{i,B\bar{B}}^{tot} = 50 MeV to 450 MeV
- Effective chemical equilibration time for baryon anti-baryon pairs: τ = 0.2 fm/c to 1 fm/c
- At T = 180 MeV, baryon anti-baryon pairs populate near chemical equilibrium with equilibration times faster than or equal to 2 fm/c
- For comparison: standard hadron gas equilibration time for Ω at RHIC (T = 170 MeV) is τ_Ω ≈ 10 fm/c, using cross section σ ≈ 30 mb and baryon density N_B^{eq} = 0.04 fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Greiner (2004) that Hagedorn resonances can drive multi-pionic reactions and produce baryon anti-baryon pairs near the critical temperature. It addresses the long-standing problem that standard hadron gas models (Koch, 1986) predicted equilibration times too long to explain observed anti-hyperon abundances at RHIC, leading some authors (Heinz, 2006) to suggest hadrons are "born" into equilibrium at the phase transition. The results here show that Hagedorn states can catalyze rapid chemical equilibration of baryon anti-baryon pairs within the fireball's hadronic lifetime, providing a dynamical mechanism that does not require pre-equilibrium freeze-out. Future work will extend the model to include strangeness and study anti-hyperon and multi-strange baryon equilibration, as well as the effects of a Bjorken expansion on the fireball cooling.
