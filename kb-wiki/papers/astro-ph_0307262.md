# Electrically charged compact stars and formation of charged black holes

> We study the effect of electric charge in compact stars assuming that the charge distribution is proportional to the mass density. The pressure and the density of the matter inside the stars are large, and the gravitational field is intense. This indicates that electric charge and a strong electric field can also be present. The relativistic hydrostatic equilibrium equation, i.e., the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation, is modified in order to include electric charge. We perform a detailed numerical study of the effect of electric charge using a polytropic equation of state. We conclude that in order to see any appreciable effect on the phenomenology of the compact stars, the electric fields have to be huge (~ 10^{21} V/m), which implies that the total charge is Q ~ 10^{20} Coulomb. From the local effect of the forces experienced on a single charged particle, it is expected that each individual charged particle is quickly ejected from the star. This in turn produces a huge force imbalance, and the gravitational force overwhelms the repulsive Coulomb and fluid pressure forces. The star can then collapse to form a charged black hole before all the charge leaves the system.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for electric charge to produce any appreciable effect on the structure of compact stars, the electric fields must be huge (∼10²¹ V/m) and the total charge must be ∼10²⁰ Coulomb, but such configurations are unstable because individual charged particles are quickly ejected, leading to gravitational collapse into a charged black hole before all charge can escape.

## Method

The authors modified the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation of hydrostatic equilibrium to include electric charge via the Einstein-Maxwell stress tensor, assuming the charge density is proportional to the mass density (ρ_ch = α ρ). They solved the resulting set of four coupled differential equations numerically for a polytropic equation of state with index n = 1.5 (Γ = 5/3) and κ = 0.05 [fm]^(8/3), varying the charge fraction f (related to α) to study the mass, radius, charge, and electric field profiles of the stars.

## Key numbers

* Maximum mass for the chargeless case (f = 0.0001) is 1.428 M_⊙ with radius 11.87 km and central energy density 1550.41 MeV/fm³.
* For charge fraction f = 0.001, the maximum mass reaches 4.384 M_⊙ with radius 31.47 km and central energy density 226.55 MeV/fm³.
* For the limiting charge fraction f = 0.0011, the maximum mass is 9.76 M_⊙ with radius 68.7 km and central energy density 47.04 MeV/fm³.
* The total charge for f = 0.0011 is 18.314 × 10²⁰ Coulomb; for f = 0.001 it is 7.576 × 10²⁰ Coulomb.
* The electric field required for appreciable effect is ∼10²¹ V/m, derived from the condition U ≃ √(8πP) with P ∼ 10 MeV/fm³.
* The charge-to-mass ratio at the surface follows Q ≃ √G M_⊙ (M/M_⊙) ≃ 10²⁰ (M/M_⊙) Coulomb.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Bekenstein (1971), Zhang et al. (1982), de Felice and Yu (1995), and Anninos and Rothman (2001) on charged compact stars, confirming Bekenstein's estimate that huge charges and fields are needed to affect neutron star structure. The paper shows that while hydrostatic equilibrium allows charges up to Q ∼ √G M (where ρ_ch ∼ ρ in geometric units), the local force on individual charged particles destabilizes the star, leading to collapse into a charged black hole. This provides a possible formation mechanism for charged black holes from newly born charged compact stars.
