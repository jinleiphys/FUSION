# In-medium $k$-body reduction of $n$-body operators

> The computational cost of ab initio nuclear structure calculations is rendered particularly acute by the presence of (at least) three-nucleon interactions. This feature becomes especially critical now that many-body methods aim at extending their reach beyond mid-mass nuclei. Consequently, state-of-the-art ab initio calculations are typically performed while approximating three-nucleon interactions in terms of effective, i.e. system-dependent, zero-, one- and two-nucleon operators. While straightforward in doubly closed-shell nuclei, existing approximation methods based on normal-ordering techniques involve either two- and three-body density matrices or a symmetry-breaking one-body density matrix in open-shell systems. In order to avoid such complications, a simple, flexible, universal and accurate approximation technique involving the convolution of the initial operator with a sole symmetry-invariant one-body matrix is presently formulated and tested numerically. Employed with a low-resolution Hamiltonian, the novel approximation method is shown to induce errors below $2-3\%$ across a large range of nuclei, observables and many-body methods.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a novel method for approximating three-nucleon interactions as effective zero-, one-, and two-body operators that, unlike existing normal-ordering techniques, uses only a symmetry-invariant one-body density matrix (rather than two- or three-body density matrices or symmetry-breaking ones) and induces errors below 2-3% across a large range of nuclei, observables, and many-body methods when employed with a low-resolution Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors formulate a generalized k-body approximation that first defines intermediate tensors via convolution of the original n-body operator with the m-fold tensor product of a symmetry-invariant one-body density matrix (Eq. 2.12), then truncates tensors for l > k to zero, and finally applies the inverse transformation to obtain approximate operators in normal order with respect to the particle vacuum. They test this 2-body approximation (H2B) on a Hamiltonian containing up to three-body interactions by solving the A-body Schrodinger equation using five different many-body methods: axially deformed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (dHFB) theory, particle-number- and angular-momentum-projected HFB (PHFB), projected generator coordinate method (PGCM), quasi-particle random phase approximation for deformed nuclei (dQRPA), and deformed Bogoliubov many-body perturbation theory at third order (dBMBPT(3)).

## Key numbers

- Errors induced by the novel approximation method are below 2-3% across a large range of nuclei, observables and many-body methods, when employed with a low-resolution Hamiltonian
- In large-scale no-core shell-model calculations, the error induced by the standard NO2B approximation of the Hamiltonian was estimated to be of the order of 1-3% up to the oxygen region (for low-resolution Hamiltonians)

## Context

This work extends the standard normal-ordered two-body (NO2B) approximation originally designed by normal ordering the Hamiltonian with respect to a Slater determinant through Wick's theorem (Wick 1950), which was straightforward for doubly closed-shell nuclei but problematic for open-shell systems. It also generalizes the particle-number-conserving normal-ordered k-body (PNOkB) approximation formulated by Ripoche (2020) for U(1)-breaking Bogoliubov reference states, now extending the approach to handle SU(2) symmetry breaking associated with angular-momentum conservation. The method avoids complications of Kutzelnigg-Mukherjee's Wick theorem (Kutzelnigg 1997) which requires two- and three-body density matrices, and enables ab initio calculations on doubly open-shell nuclei using symmetry-breaking and restored formalisms.
