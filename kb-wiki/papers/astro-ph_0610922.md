# Cosmic Neutrino Bound on the Dark Matter Annihilation Rate in the Late Universe

> How large can the dark matter self-annihilation rate in the late universe be? This rate depends on (rho_DM/m_chi)^2 <sigma_A v>, where rho_DM/m_chi is the number density of dark matter, and the annihilation cross section is averaged over the velocity distribution. Since the clustering of dark matter is known, this amounts to asking how large the annihilation cross section can be. Kaplinghat, Knox, and Turner proposed that a very large annihilation cross section could turn a halo cusp into a core, improving agreement between simulations and observations; Hui showed that unitarity prohibits this for large dark matter masses. We show that if the annihilation products are Standard Model particles, even just neutrinos, the consequent fluxes are ruled out by orders of magnitude, even at small masses. Equivalently, to invoke such large annihilation cross sections, one must now require that essentially no Standard Model particles are produced.

## Key claim

The paper shows that if dark matter annihilation produces any Standard Model particles, the resulting neutrino flux from all halos in the universe is orders of magnitude larger than observed backgrounds, ruling out the large annihilation cross sections needed to flatten halo cusps into cores, even for small dark matter masses and even if only neutrinos are produced.

## Method

The authors calculate the cosmic diffuse neutrino flux from dark matter annihilations in all halos in the late universe, assuming 100% branching ratio to \(\bar{\nu}\nu\) as the most conservative case for Standard Model final states. They compare this signal to the measured atmospheric neutrino background from Super-Kamiokande and other experiments, determining the maximum allowed annihilation cross section \(\langle \sigma_A v \rangle\) as a function of dark matter mass \(m_\chi\) by requiring the signal not exceed the background by more than the observational uncertainty.

## Key numbers

- For \(m_\chi = 10\) GeV, the bound on \(\langle \sigma_A v \rangle\) is orders of magnitude stronger than the unitarity bound of Hui (2004), ruling out the cross section required by the Kaplinghat, Knox, and Turner (2000) model.
- For all \(m_\chi > 0.1\) GeV, the bound implies a core density \(\rho_A > 5 \times 10^3\) GeV/cm\(^3\), which only occurs at radii \(< 1\) pc in the Milky Way for an NFW profile.
- The KKT model requires \(\langle \sigma_A v \rangle\) values \(> 10^7\) times larger than the natural scale for a thermal relic.

## Context

This work extends the unitarity bound of Hui (2004) for late-universe dark matter annihilation by adding a direct observational constraint from neutrino fluxes, and it contradicts the proposal of Kaplinghat, Knox, and Turner (2000) that large annihilation cross sections could resolve the cusp-core problem in galactic halos. The result enables future analyses by Super-Kamiokande and AMANDA to improve the bound by a factor 10–100 over the whole mass range, potentially reaching sensitivity close to the natural thermal relic scale.
