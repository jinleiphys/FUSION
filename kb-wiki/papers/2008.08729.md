# Gluonic Hot Spot Initial Conditions in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The initial conditions in heavy-ion collisions are calculated in many different frameworks. The importance of nucleon position fluctuations within the nucleus and sub-nucleon structure has been established when modeling initial conditions for input to hydrodynamic calculations. However, there remain outstanding puzzles regarding these initial conditions, including the measurement of the near equivalence of the elliptical $v_{2}$ and triangular $v_{3}$ flow coefficients in ultra-central 0-1% Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC. Recently a calculation termed MAGMA incorporating gluonic hot spots via two-point correlators in the Color Glass Condensate framework, and no nucleons, provided a simultaneous match to these flow coefficients measured by the ATLAS experiment, including in ultra-central 0-1% collisions. Our calculations reveal that the MAGMA initial conditions do not describe the experimental data when run through full hydrodynamic SONIC simulations or when the hot spots from one nucleus resolve hot spots from the other nucleus, as predicted in the Color Glass Condensate framework. We also explore alternative initial condition calculations and discuss their implications.

# Gluonic Hot Spot Initial Conditions in Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The MAGMA initial condition model, which incorporates gluonic hot spots via two-point correlators in the Color Glass Condensate framework without explicit nucleons, appears to simultaneously match elliptic and triangular flow coefficients in ultra-central Pb+Pb collisions when using linear response scaling factors; however, when run through full hydrodynamic SONIC simulations, the MAGMA initial conditions do not describe the experimental data, and the assumption that the response coefficients kappa_2 and kappa_3 are approximately equal and centrality-independent is significantly in error.

## Method

The authors reproduced the MAGMA calculation using its publicly available Python code, generating one million Pb+Pb events with approximately 100 localized color charges per nucleus distributed within a Woods-Saxon profile (R=6.62 fm, a=0.55 fm), with energy deposit computed as the sum of projectile hot spots times a smooth target nucleus plus target hot spots times a smooth projectile nucleus. They then ran 1000 MAGMA initial conditions for 0-1% and 25-26% centrality selections through full viscous hydrodynamic simulations using the SONIC code with shear viscosity to entropy density eta/s = 1/4pi, initial time tau_0 = 0.4 fm/c, and freeze-out temperature T_f = 170 MeV, extracting kappa_2 and kappa_3 response coefficients as a function of centrality and transverse momentum. They also modified the MAGMA code to compute energy deposit as the direct product A x B (matching the IP-Glasma approach) and explored alternative initial conditions using the IP-JAZMA framework in both A x B and sqrt(A x B) modes.

## Key numbers

- Woods-Saxon parameters for Pb nucleus: R = 6.62 fm and a = 0.55 fm
- Number of localized color charges in MAGMA: approximately 100 per nucleus (about half the number of nucleons)
- For 0-1% Pb+Pb collisions with SONIC hydrodynamics: kappa_2 = 0.38 and kappa_3 = 0.28 (p_T-integrated over 0.5-3.0 GeV), giving ratio kappa_3/kappa_2 = 0.73
- For 25-26% Pb+Pb collisions with SONIC hydrodynamics: kappa_2 = 0.28 and kappa_3 = 0.24 (p_T-integrated over 0.5-3.0 GeV), giving ratio kappa_3/kappa_2 = 0.85
- For nearly ideal hydrodynamics (eta/s = 0.02) in 0-1% Pb+Pb: kappa_2 = 0.53 and kappa_3 = 0.36
- MAGMA fit values from eccentricity scaling: kappa_2 = 0.32 and kappa_3 = 0.31, giving ratio 0.97
- Modified MAGMA (A x B mode) fit values: kappa_2 = 0.28 and kappa_3 = 0.15
- Hydrodynamic initial time: tau_0 = 0.4 fm/c; freeze-out temperature: T_f = 170 MeV
- ATLAS data range: charged particles with 0.5 < p_T < 5 GeV and |eta| < 2.5, minimum pseudorapidity gap of 1.67 units

## Context

This work tests the MAGMA initial condition model of Gelis (2019) against full hydrodynamic simulations, finding that the model's apparent success in matching ATLAS flow data (Aaboud 2019) depends on non-standard energy deposition and incorrect assumptions about linear response coefficients. The study extends comparisons between the IP-Glasma framework (Schenke 2012) and phenomenological models like T-RENTo (Moreland 2014) and IP-JAZMA (Nagle 2018), showing that the IP-Glasma and IP-JAZMA calculations yield essentially identical eccentricity distributions when using the same Monte Carlo Glauber inputs. The results indicate that the ultra-central flow puzzle (near-equivalence of v_2 and v_3 in 0-1% Pb+Pb collisions) remains unresolved and requires further investigation with full hydrodynamic evolution rather than simple eccentricity scaling.
