# Dynamical description of the fission process using the TD-BCS theory

> The description of fission remains a challenge for nuclear microscopic theories. The time-dependent Hartree-Fock approach with BCS pairing is applied to study the last stage of the fission process. A good agreement is found for the one-body observables: the total kinetic energy and the average mass asymmetry. The non-physical dependence of two-body observables with the initial shape is discussed.

## Key claim

The time-dependent Hartree-Fock with BCS pairing (TD-BCS) reproduces experimental one-body observables (total kinetic energy and average mass asymmetry) for the last stage of fission, but two-body observables (odd-even effects, mass distribution width) show a non-physical dependence on the initial shape chosen for the dynamical calculation, indicating the need for finite-temperature initial states.

## Method

The authors initialized the system after the fission barrier on the adiabatic path using Constrained Hartree-Fock with BCS pairing (CHF+BCS) for three fission modes of 258Fm: symmetric compact, symmetric elongated, and asymmetric. They then performed time-dependent evolutions with TD-BCS from different initial fragment distances (between 9.5 and 12.5 fm) and extracted one-body observables (total kinetic energy, mass asymmetry) and two-body observables (neutron number distributions, mass distribution widths) using projection techniques.

## Key numbers

- Total kinetic energy (TKE) changed by less than 1 MeV for the symmetric compact mode when initial fragment distance varied between 9.5 and 12.5 fm
- For the asymmetric mode, only events with TKE less than 220 MeV were compared to experimental mass distribution
- Time steps between density snapshots were 0.675 zeptoseconds (zs) for symmetric compact, 1.8 zs for symmetric elongated, and 1.08 zs for asymmetric modes

## Context

This work extends time-dependent mean-field studies of fission by including pairing correlations through the BCS approximation, which is less numerically demanding than full time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (TDHFB) as noted by Avez et al. (2008). The results confirm earlier findings by Scamps et al. (2013) on the importance of pairing in dynamical processes. The observed dependence of two-body observables on initial conditions points to the need for finite-temperature calculations to account for excitation energy thermalized during the slow initial part of fission, and highlights that mean-field dynamics cannot start from too compact shapes as shown by Goddard et al. (2015) and Tanimura et al. (2015).
