# Hadron production in relativistic nuclear collisions: thermal hadron source or hadronizing quark-gluon plasma?

> Measured hadron yields from relativistic nuclear collisions can be equally well understood in two physically distinct models, namely a static thermal hadronic source vs.~a time-dependent, nonequilibrium hadronization off a quark-gluon plasma droplet. Due to the time-dependent particle evaporation off the hadronic surface in the latter approach the hadron ratios change (by factors of $<\approx 5$) in time. Final particle yields reflect time averages over the actual thermodynamic properties of the system at a certain stage of the evolution. Calculated hadron, strangelet and (anti-)cluster yields as well as freeze-out times are presented for different systems. Due to strangeness distillation the system moves rapidly out of the T, $μ_q$ plane into the $μ_s$-sector. Strangeness to baryon ratios f_s=1-2 prevail during a considerable fraction (50%) of the time evolution (i.e. $Λ$-droplets or even $Ξ^-$-droplets form the system at the late stage: The possibility of observing this time evolution via HBT correlations is discussed). The observed hadron ratios require $T_c\approx 160 MeV$ and $B^{1/4}>\approx 200 MeV$. If the present model is fit to the extrapolated hadron yields, metastable hypermatter can only be produced with a probability $p< 10^{-8}$ for $A \ge 4$.

# Hadron production in relativistic nuclear collisions: thermal hadron source or hadronizing quark-gluon plasma?

## Key claim

Measured hadron yields from relativistic nuclear collisions can be equally well reproduced by two physically distinct models: a static thermal hadron source in chemical equilibrium and a time-dependent, nonequilibrium hadronization of a quark-gluon plasma droplet. The final particle ratios are nearly identical between the two scenarios, making them indistinguishable based on hadron abundances alone, though the dynamic model predicts that hadron ratios change by factors of up to approximately 5 during the evolution.

## Method

The authors compare a static equilibrium hadron gas model (with Hagedorn eigenvolume correction, all well-established hadrons up to 2 GeV mass, and feeding from resonance decays) against a dynamic hadronization model that assumes a first-order phase transition from quark-gluon plasma to hadron gas under Gibbs conditions. The dynamic model incorporates time-dependent particle evaporation from the hadronic surface surrounding a QGP droplet, solving coupled differential equations for baryon number, entropy, and net strangeness of the total system with a universal evaporation rate. Particle yields are calculated for different bag constants and initial specific entropies, with final yields including feeding from higher resonance decays.

## Key numbers

- Hadron ratios change by factors of approximately 2-5 during the course of hadronization and evaporation in the dynamic model.
- The observed hadron ratios require a critical temperature T_c approximately 160 MeV and a bag constant B^(1/4) greater than approximately 200 MeV.
- For the dynamic model fit to extrapolated hadron yields, metastable hypermatter can only be produced with a probability p less than 10^(-8) for baryon number A greater than or equal to 4.
- The static fit yields a strange quark chemical potential mu_s = 24 MeV, while the dynamic model gives mu_s approximately 50 MeV at the end of evolution and mu_q approximately 15 MeV (dropping from an initial mu_q approximately 110 MeV).
- For Pb+Pb collisions with initial baryon number A_B = 416, S/A = 40, and B^(1/4) = 235 MeV, the model predicts: p = 180.0, pi^+ = 852.2, K^+ = 175.4, K^- = 122.3, Lambda = 79.8, anti-Lambda = 15.0, Xi^- = 5.4, anti-Xi^- = 1.3, Omega = 1.3, anti-Omega = 0.49 (total yields including feeding).
- Average freeze-out times for the same conditions: p = 4.1 fm/c, pi^+ = 5.6 fm/c, K^+ = 5.0 fm/c, K^- = 6.6 fm/c, anti-p = 7.6 fm/c, Lambda = 5.1 fm/c, anti-Lambda = 6.4 fm/c.
- The K_s^0/h^- ratio from the dynamic model with B^(1/4) = 235 MeV is approximately 0.15, compared to experimental values of 0.11-0.12 from NA49.
- For B^(1/4) = 160 MeV and S/A = 40, a cold strangelet of baryon number A approximately 35 emerges; for S/A = 150, A approximately 42.
- The specific entropy extracted from the dynamic fit (S/A = 35-45) is higher than values of S/A approximately 20-25 from one-dimensional hydrodynamic calculations by Rischke et al. (1990).

## Context

This work extends the static thermal model approach of Braun-Munzinger and Stachel (1995, 1996) by demonstrating that a dynamic hadronization model based on the work of Greiner and Stocker (1991) reproduces the same experimental hadron ratios equally well. The dynamic model incorporates strangeness distillation, previously described by Greiner, Koch, and Stocker (1987), which drives the system into the mu_s-sector with strangeness-to-baryon ratios f_s = 1-2 during a considerable fraction of the evolution. The paper enables future discrimination between the two scenarios through Hanbury-Brown-Twiss correlation measurements of freeze-out radii and times, which differ between particle species in the dynamic model. The results also constrain the bag constant to values that would exclude stable strangelets, in contrast to the lower bag constant scenarios explored by Barz et al. (1988, 1989).
