# Neck fragmentation reaction mechanism

> Based on a microscopic transport model, we study the origin of nonstatistical Intermediate Mass Fragment ($IMF$) production in semicentral heavy ion collisions at the Fermi energies. We show that a fast, dynamical $IMF$ formation process, the {\it neck fragmentation mechanism}, can explain the experimentally observed features: deviations from Viola systematics and anisotropic, narrow angular distributions. It may be regarded as the continuation of the multifragmentation mechanism towards intermediate impact parameters. Its relation to other dynamical mechanisms, the induced fission and the abrasion of the spectator zones, that can also contribute to mid-rapidity $IMF$ production, is discussed. The dependence on beam energy and centrality of the collision is carefully analysed. The competition between volume and surface instabilities makes this mechanism very sensitive to the in-medium nucleon-nucleon interactions, from the cross sections for hard collisions to the compressibility and other Equation of State ($EOS$) properties. For charge asymmetric collisions the sensitivity of various observables to the symmetry energy is investigated. Of particular interest appears the isospin diffusion dynamics with no signal of isospin equilibration. However, in spite of the short time scales and of the dynamical aspects, we notice isoscaling features of the neck mechanism. We observe that isospin enrichement of the neck zone as well as the isoscaling parameters are sensitive to the density dependence of asymmetry term of EOS around and below saturation value.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a fast, dynamical intermediate mass fragment (IMF) formation process called "neck fragmentation" can explain experimentally observed nonstatistical IMF production in semicentral heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies, but this mechanism is presented as one of several possible contributions (along with dynamically induced fission and abrasion of spectator zones) that can coexist, and its manifestation depends sensitively on impact parameter, beam energy, and in-medium nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Method

The authors performed Boltzmann-Nordheim-Vlasov (BNV) transport model simulations with stochastic fluctuations for the reaction 124Sn+64Ni at 35 AMeV, using test particle evolution on a lattice with free NN cross sections and Pauli blocking. They classified events at freeze-out into three categories (binary with small deformations, binary with large deformations, and ternary with direct IMF emission) and analyzed the neck-originating fragments (NOFs) for impact parameters from b=4 fm to b=9 fm, comparing three different density dependencies of the symmetry energy (asysoft, asystiff, and superasystiff).

## Key numbers

- Ternary event probability reaches a maximum of about 25% at impact parameters b=6-7 fm for the neutron-rich reaction 124Sn+64Ni at 35 AMeV
- The neutron-poor reaction 112Sn+58Ni shows ternary probabilities reduced by about 25% compared to the neutron-rich system
- NOF production occurs between 120 fm/c and 280 fm/c from the reaction beginning, with touching time around 20 fm/c
- Primary NOF yields follow exponential dependencies: Y(A) ~ exp(-0.12 A) and Y(Z) ~ exp(-0.32 Z)
- Relative velocities of NOFs with respect to PLF/TLF are 25% to 70% larger than values predicted by Viola systematics
- The angular distribution width ΔΦ_plane is approximately 80° for b=5 fm and becomes narrower with increasing impact parameter
- The center-of-mass velocity of the system is approximately 5.4 cm/ns

## Context

This work extends the stochastic mean-field transport approach previously developed by Colonna, Di Toro, and Guarnera (1995) to systematically investigate the neck fragmentation mechanism as an alternative to statistical fission explanations for mid-rapidity IMF production observed in experiments by Montoya (1994), Toke (1995), Lukasik (1997), and others. The study enables future experimental tests at radioactive ion beam facilities (such as EURISOL) by predicting that isospin enrichment of the neck zone and isoscaling parameters are sensitive to the density dependence of the symmetry energy around and below saturation density, potentially allowing discrimination between different asy-EOS parametrizations.
