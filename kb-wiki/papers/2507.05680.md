# The nuclear charge radius of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$

> The size is a key property of a nucleus. Accurate nuclear radii are extracted from elastic electron scattering, laser spectroscopy, and muonic atom spectroscopy. The results are not always compatible, as the proton-radius puzzle has shown most dramatically. Beyond helium, precision data from muonic and electronic sources are scarce in the light-mass region. The stable isotopes of carbon are an exception. We present a laser spectroscopic measurement of the root-mean-square (rms) charge radius of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ and compare this with ab initio nuclear structure calculations. Measuring all hyperfine components of the $2\,^3\mathrm{S} \rightarrow 2\,^3\mathrm{P}$ fine-structure triplet in $^{13}\mathrm{C}^{4+}$ ions referenced to a frequency comb allows us to determine its center-of-gravity with accuracy better than $2\,\mathrm{MHz}$ although second-order hyperfine-structure effects shift individual lines by several $\mathrm{GHz}$. We improved the uncertainty of $R_\mathrm{c}(^{13}\mathrm{C})$ determined with electrons by a factor of $6$ and found a $3σ$ discrepancy with the muonic atom result of similar accuracy.

# The nuclear charge radius of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$

## Key claim
The laser spectroscopic measurement of the root-mean-square charge radius of $^{13}\mathrm{C}$ improves the uncertainty from electron-based methods by a factor of 6, but reveals a $3\sigma$ discrepancy with the muonic atom result of similar accuracy, indicating a systematic offset between radii determined via electromagnetic interaction with electrons versus those with muons.

## Method
Collinear and anticollinear laser spectroscopy was performed on the $2\,^3\mathrm{S} \rightarrow 2\,^3\mathrm{P}$ fine-structure triplet in He-like $^{13}\mathrm{C}^{4+}$ ions produced in an electron-beam ion source. All nine hyperfine components were measured using two continuous-wave Ti:sapphire lasers locked to a frequency comb, with the geometric average of collinear and anticollinear resonance frequencies yielding the rest-frame transition frequencies. The isotope shift relative to $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ was combined with NRQED mass-shift calculations and the field-shift factor to extract the differential mean-square charge radius in a nuclear-model independent way.

## Key numbers
- Center-of-gravity isotope shift $\delta\nu^{12,13} = 51\,746.1(1.4)$ MHz for the $^3\mathrm{S}_1 \rightarrow {}^3\mathrm{P}$ transition
- Field-shift factor $F = -211.5(1)$ MHz/fm$^2$
- Differential mean-square charge radius $\delta\!\braket{r^2}^{12,13} = -0.1245(66)$ fm$^2$
- Differential rms charge radius $\delta\!R_\mathrm{c}^{12,13} = -0.0253(14)$ fm
- Absolute rms charge radius $R_\mathrm{c}(^{13}\mathrm{C}) = 2.4464(45)$ fm from combining laser spectroscopy with electron-scattering $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ radius
- Total systematic uncertainty of transition frequencies: $1.8$ MHz, dominated by laser beam alignment uncertainty of $1.7$ MHz
- Mass-shift contribution $\delta\nu_\mathrm{M} = 51\,719.29(25)$ MHz

## Context
This work extends laser spectroscopic charge radius measurements beyond beryllium into the second row of the periodic table, following earlier spectroscopy on $^{12}\mathrm{C}^{4+}$ by Imgram (2023) and on $^{10,11}$B by Maass (2019). The $3\sigma$ discrepancy with the muonic atom result of Ruckstuhl (1984) and Boer (1985) parallels the proton radius puzzle but shows a different pattern: the charge radius difference measured in ordinary ions agrees with muonic atoms, while absolute radii from electron scattering do not. The results are compared with ab initio valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) and in-medium no-core shell model (IM-NCSM) calculations using seven different chiral effective field theory Hamiltonians, all of which overestimate the observed radius reduction by up to a factor of 2, suggesting missing many-body correlations.
