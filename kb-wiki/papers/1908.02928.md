# Influence of density dependence of symmetry energy in hot and dense matter for supernova simulations

> We study the influence of density-dependent symmetry energy at high densities in simulations of core-collapse supernovae, black hole formation and proto-neutron star cooling by extending the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory used for the Shen EOS table. We adopt the extended RMF theory to examine the density dependence of the symmetry energy with a small value of the slope parameter $L$, while the original properties of the symmetric nuclear matter are unchanged. In order to assess matter effects at high densities, we perform numerical simulations of gravitational collapse of massive stars adopting the EOS table at high densities beyond $10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$ with the small $L$ value, which is in accord with the experimental and observational constraints, and compare them with the results obtained by using the Shen EOS. Numerical results for 11.2M$_{\odot}$ and 15M$_{\odot}$ stars exhibit minor effects around the core bounce and in the following evolution for 200 ms. Numerical results for 40M$_{\odot}$ and 50M$_{\odot}$ stars reveal a shorter duration toward the black hole formation with a smaller maximum mass for the small $L$ case. Numerical simulations of proto-neutron star cooling over 10 s through neutrino emissions demonstrate increasing effects of the symmetry energy at high densities. Neutrino cooling drastically proceeds in a relatively long timescale with high luminosities and average energies with the small symmetry energy. Evolution toward the cold neutron star is affected because of the different behavior of neutron-rich matter while supernova dynamics around core bounce remains similar in less neutron-rich environments.

## Key claim

The influence of the density-dependent symmetry energy at high densities is moderate in the early post-bounce evolution of core-collapse supernovae (up to 200 ms) but becomes notable in the late phase of proto-neutron star cooling over 10 seconds, with the effect depending on the neutron-richness of the environment and the stellar mass.

## Method

The authors extended the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory used for the Shen EOS by adding a coupling term between vector and isovector-vector mesons to adjust the slope parameter of symmetry energy L from 110.8 MeV to 40 MeV while keeping symmetric nuclear matter properties unchanged. They constructed a modified EOS table for uniform matter at densities above approximately 10^14 g/cm^3 with the low L value, using the original Shen EOS for lower densities, and performed spherically symmetric general relativistic neutrino-radiation hydrodynamics simulations of core collapse for 11.2, 15, 40, and 50 solar mass stars, as well as thermal evolution simulations of proto-neutron star cooling over 10 seconds.

## Key numbers

- Slope parameter L = 40 MeV (TM1e) compared to L = 110.8 MeV (original TM1), with symmetry energy at saturation density of 31.38 MeV for TM1e versus 36.89 MeV for TM1
- Incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter is 281 MeV for both cases
- Simulations performed for progenitor masses of 11.2, 15, 40, and 50 solar masses
- Post-bounce evolution followed for 200 ms in the 11.2 and 15 solar mass cases
- Proto-neutron star cooling simulations performed over 10 seconds
- EOS table constructed for densities above 10^14 g/cm^3, with smooth connection around 10^14.1 g/cm^3

## Context

This work extends the series of Shen EOS tables (Shen 1998a, 1998b, 2011) by modifying the density dependence of symmetry energy to address the known issue that the original Shen EOS has a symmetry energy at saturation that is large compared to nuclear experiments and produces neutron star radii too large compared to X-ray observations and the GW170817 tidal deformability constraint (Abbott 2018). The modification follows the systematic study of symmetry energy in RMF by Bao (2014a, 2014b, 2015) and Hu (2019), who added the omega-rho coupling term to vary the slope parameter L while preserving the good description of finite nuclei. This work enables future studies using a fully revised Shen EOS table covering the whole density range with Thomas-Fermi calculations and nuclear statistical equilibrium, which will allow investigation of symmetry energy effects from both uniform and non-uniform matter.
