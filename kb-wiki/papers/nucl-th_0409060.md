# Nuclear structure far from stability

> Modern nuclear structure theory is rapidly evolving towards regions of exotic short-lived nuclei far from stability, nuclear astrophysics applications, and bridging the gap between low-energy QCD and the phenomenology of finite nuclei. The principal objective is to build a consistent microscopic theoretical framework that will provide a unified description of bulk properties, nuclear excitations and reactions. Stringent constraints on the microscopic approach to nuclear dynamics, effective nuclear interactions, and nuclear energy density functionals, are obtained from studies of the structure and stability of exotic nuclei with extreme isospin values, as well as extended asymmetric nucleonic matter. Recent theoretical advances in the description of structure phenomena in exotic nuclei far from stability are reviewed.

# Nuclear structure far from stability

## Key claim
Modern nuclear structure theory is rapidly evolving toward a consistent microscopic framework that will provide a unified description of bulk properties, nuclear excitations, and reactions, but the predictive power of current self-consistent mean-field methods remains unsatisfactory, with global effective interactions typically adjusted to ground-state properties of no more than about ten spherical nuclei and producing nuclear masses with a typical rms deviation of approximately 2 MeV when compared with experimental mass tables.

## Method
The paper reviews recent theoretical advances in the description of structure phenomena in exotic nuclei far from stability, covering ab initio Quantum Monte Carlo and no-core shell model calculations for light nuclei, large-scale shell model approaches for medium-heavy systems, and self-consistent mean-field models (Skyrme-HFB, Gogny-HFB, and relativistic mean-field theory) extended with beyond-mean-field correlations including angular momentum projection, particle-number projection, and generator coordinate method configuration mixing. The review also discusses the development of microscopic mass tables, the extension of the Gamow Shell Model to treat bound states and the particle continuum consistently, and the construction of density-dependent relativistic effective interactions for improved description of asymmetric nuclear matter and exotic nuclei.

## Key numbers
- Energies of all bound and narrow states of up to ten nucleons can be reproduced almost exactly (within 2%) by employing bare nuclear forces that fit NN scattering data with the addition of realistic NNN forces.
- QMC calculations have been extended to A ≤ 12 nuclei and to neutron droplets; the auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo method has been applied to large neutron systems at zero temperature (≈ 60 neutrons).
- The Gamow Shell Model has been successfully tested in calculations involving several valence neutrons outside doubly-magic cores: ⁶⁻¹⁰He and ¹⁸⁻²²O, and in the description of ⁵⁻¹¹Li.
- Skyrme-HFB mass tables of more than 9000 nuclei (Z,N ≥ 8 and Z ≤ 120) have been constructed; the measured masses of 2135 nuclei with Z,N ≥ 8 have been fitted with an rms error of less than 700 keV using approximately 20 parameters.
- Charge radii from Skyrme-HFB calculations show an rms deviation of approximately 0.025 fm for absolute charge radii and charge isotope shifts of more than 500 nuclei.
- The nuclear matter compression modulus from relativistic mean-field models is restricted to K_nm ≈ 250–270 MeV by experimental data on giant monopole resonances.
- The nuclear matter symmetry energy at saturation from these effective interactions is limited to 32 MeV ≤ a_4 ≤ 36 MeV by isovector giant dipole resonances and data on differences between neutron and proton radii.

## Context
This review extends the line of work on microscopic nuclear structure theory summarized in earlier reviews such as Bender, Heenen, and Reinhard (2003) and Lunney, Pearson, and Thibault (2003), and it synthesizes advances from multiple groups including the Quantum Monte Carlo calculations of Pieper and Wiringa (2001), the no-core shell model developments of Navratil and Ormand (2002, 2003), the Gamow Shell Model of Michel, Nazarewicz, Ploszajczak, and collaborators (2002–2004), and the self-consistent mean-field studies of the Gogny interaction by Rodriguez, Egido, and Robledo (2002–2004). The review enables future work toward a universal nuclear energy density functional that would provide reliable microscopic descriptions of infinite nuclear and neutron matter, ground-state properties of all bound nuclei, and low-energy excitations, with properties of nuclei far from stability providing stringent constraints on the isovector channels of effective nuclear interactions.
