# Novel Non-equilibrium Phase Transition Caused by Non-linear Hadronic-quark Phase Structure

> We consider how the occurrence of first-order phase transitions in non-constant pressure differs from those at constant pressure. The former has shown the non-linear phase structure of mixed matter, which implies a particle number dependence of the binding energies of the two species. If the mixed matter is mixed hadron-quark phase, nucleon outgoing from hadronic phase and ingoing to quark phase probably reduces the system to a non-equilibrium state, in other words, there exists the imbalance of the two phases when deconfinement takes place. This novel non-equilibrium process is very analogous to the nuclear reactions that nuclei emit neutrons and absorb them under appropriate conditions. We present self-consistent thermodynamics in description for the processes and identify the microphysics responsible for the processes. The microphysics is an inevitable consequence of non-linear phase structure instead of the effect of an additional dissipation force. When applying our findings to the neutron star containing mixed hadron-quark matter, it is found that the newly discovered energy release might strongly change the thermal evolution behavior of the star.

## Key claim

First-order phase transitions in systems with more than one conserved charge (such as hadron-quark mixed matter) can proceed out of chemical equilibrium because the non-linear dependence of binding energies on baryon number in each phase allows energy release during conversion, unlike the constant-pressure textbook case where no such dissipation occurs.

## Method

The authors analyze the thermodynamics of a mixed hadron-quark phase using the fraction in baryon number (eta) rather than the fraction in volume, showing that the energy per baryon is a non-linear function of eta. They derive a self-consistency condition for the thermodynamic formulae that requires a non-zero chemical potential difference between the two phases, and they model the microphysics as analogous to neutron emission and absorption by nuclei, where the change in binding energy of each "giant nucleus" (hadronic and quark clusters) releases heat. They then apply this formalism to neutron star matter using the relativistic mean-field theory for hadronic matter and the MIT bag model for quark matter, with bag constants B^(1/4) = 170, 180, and 190 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The mean heat per baryon (q-bar) is of order 0.1 MeV for the mixed hadron-quark phase in neutron stars, compared to 0.01 MeV for the rotochemical heating mechanism.
- For a standard dipole magnetic field B = 6.4 pi x 10^19 (-Omega Omega-dot)^(1/2) / Omega^2 and baryon number 10^56, the heat production rate H is approximately 10^41 erg/s for q-bar = 0.1 MeV, B = 10^12 G, and Omega = 6000 rad/s.
- The bag constant values used are B^(1/4) = 170 MeV, 180 MeV, and 190 MeV.

## Context

This work extends Glendenning's (1992, 1997) realization that first-order phase transitions in systems with multiple conserved charges have a non-linear phase structure with varying pressure, in contrast to the constant-pressure Maxwell construction. It contradicts the assumption by Heiselberg et al. (1993) that the two phases remain in balance during deconfinement due to strong-reaction timescales. The mechanism enables a new internal heating source for neutron stars containing mixed hadron-quark matter, which could explain the high thermal luminosity of old pulsars such as PSR J0437-4715 (Kargaltsev et al., 2004; Durant et al., 2012) and may affect the cooling of X-ray transients (Yakovlev et al., 2003; Brown et al., 2004; Cumming et al., 2006; Brown et al., 2009).
