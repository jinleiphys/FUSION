# On the Contribution of Gamma Ray Bursts to the Galactic Inventory of Some Intermediate Mass Nuclei

> Light curves from a growing number of Gamma Ray Bursts (GRBs) indicate that GRBs copiously produce radioactive Ni moving outward at fractions of the speed of light. We calculate nuclear abundances of elements accompanying the outflowing Ni under the assumption that this Ni originates from a wind blown off of a viscous accretion disk. We also show that GRB's likely contribute appreciably to the galactic inventory of 42Ca, 45Sc, 46Ti, 49Ti, 63Cu, and may be a principal site for the production of 64Zn.

## Key claim

GRBs likely contribute appreciably to the galactic inventory of several intermediate-mass nuclei (42Ca, 45Sc, 46Ti, 49Ti, 63Cu, and 64Zn), but this conclusion depends on the assumption that the observed 56Ni in GRB outflows originates from a wind blown off a viscous accretion disk rather than from explosive shock nucleosynthesis, and on the fraction of core-collapse supernovae that become collapsars (fc) being at least 1/50 to 1/10.

## Method

The authors calculated nuclear abundances in a relativistic wind from a viscous accretion disk, parameterizing the outflow by its dynamic timescale (expressed as β = Ṁ−1 / v0.1³), entropy per baryon s, and electron fraction Ye. They constrained these parameters using the observational requirement that the wind efficiently produce 56Ni (mass fraction X(56Ni) ≳ 1/4–1/2) and be relativistic (v ≳ 0.1c). Nucleosynthesis yields were computed for a range of wind parameters thought to bracket the expected conditions, and production factors were compared to those from standard supernova models (Woosley & Weaver 1995) and to solar abundances.

## Key numbers

- The mass fraction of 56Ni in the wind must satisfy X(56Ni) ≳ 1/4–1/2 to account for the ~0.5 M⊙ of Ni inferred from SN1998bw and SN2003dh light curves, given that the disk ejects at most 1–2 M⊙ of fast material.
- Efficient Ni production requires Ye ≳ 0.505; for a "canonical" disk (α=0.1, Ṁ=0.1 M⊙/s, Kerr parameter a=0.95), neutrino capture increases Ye by δYe ≈ 0.05, and the largest Ye consistent with efficient Ni production is Ye ≈ 0.60.
- For a wind with s=30, β=4, and Ye=0.51, production factors for 45Sc and 64Zn are 100 or greater, compared to typical production factors of order 10 for type II supernovae (Woosley & Weaver 1995).
- If fc ≳ 1/50, half or more of the galactic abundance of 45Sc comes from collapsars; if fc ≳ 0.05–0.1, then 42Ca, 45Sc, 46Ti, 49Ti, 51V, 50Cr, 63Cu, and 64Zn can all have significant contributions.
- The GRB rate is ~1/100 the supernova rate, giving a lower limit fc ≳ 0.01; Heger et al. (2003) estimate fc could be as large as 0.1 depending on metallicity and initial mass function.

## Context

This work extends the collapsar model of Woosley (1993) and MacFadyen & Woosley (1999) by calculating nucleosynthesis in disk winds, complementing the explosive shock nucleosynthesis scenario of Maeda et al. (2003). The results show that GRB disk winds produce a distinct abundance pattern (with large overproduction of 42Ca, 45Sc, 46Ti, 49Ti, 63Cu, and 64Zn) that differs from standard supernova yields (Woosley & Weaver 1995). The paper enables future searches for collapsar enrichment signatures in metal-poor stars (e.g., high 42Ca/40Ca ratios) and addresses long-standing puzzles in chemical evolution, such as the underproduction of 45Sc (by a factor ~1.5), 49Ti (by a factor ~2), and 64Zn (by a factor ~5) in standard models.
