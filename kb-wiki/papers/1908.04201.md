# Influence of correlations between yields on the chemical freeze-out temperature

> A statistical (thermal) model is applied to the description of hadron yields measured at central nucleus-nucleus collisions at the top RHIC energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV and the LHC energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 2.76$ TeV. In contrast to the previous analyzes a more general form of the least squares test statistic is used, which takes into account also possible correlations between different species of yields. In addition to hadrons also light nuclei are included in the fits. Because of the lack of data, a toy model is constructed where the correlation coefficients are just free parameters. Because of this the presented considerations are speculative and should be treated as an imperfect illustration of the problem. Within these limitations it is impossible to formulate any definite conclusion, it might be only mentioned that for the considered examples the dependence of the freeze-out temperature and baryon chemical potential on correlations turned out to be weak.

## Key claim

Using a toy model where correlation coefficients between hadron yields are treated as free parameters, the dependence of the chemical freeze-out temperature and baryon chemical potential on these correlations turned out to be weak; the author states that the considerations are speculative and that no definite conclusion can be formulated.

## Method

A statistical (thermal) model in the grand-canonical ensemble was fitted to measured hadron and light-nucleus yields from central Au-Au collisions at RHIC (sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV) and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC (sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV). Instead of the usual diagonal chi-squared statistic, a general least-squares test statistic with a full covariance matrix was used, where off-diagonal elements were modeled by two free correlation coefficients (rho_1 and rho_2) representing correlations between resonances and their daughter particles. The fits minimized chi-squared with respect to temperature T_ch, baryochemical potential mu_B, and volume V.

## Key numbers

- For LHC Pb-Pb at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV with light nuclei included and maximal correlations (rho_1 = 0.140, rho_2 = 0.341): T_ch = 157.9 +/- 1.1 MeV, mu_B = 0.08 +/- 3.85 MeV, chi^2/n_dof = 1.90, p-value = 1.0%.
- For LHC Pb-Pb at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV with zero correlations and light nuclei included: T_ch = 155.8 +/- 1.2 MeV, mu_B = 0.79 +/- 3.65 MeV, chi^2/n_dof = 1.52, p-value = 6.8%.
- For RHIC Au-Au at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with light nuclei included and maximal correlations (rho_1 = 0.181, rho_2 = 0.380): T_ch = 157.9 +/- 0.6 MeV, mu_B = 28.2 +/- 3.5 MeV, chi^2/n_dof = 1.64, p-value = 4.2%.
- For RHIC Au-Au at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV with zero correlations and light nuclei included: T_ch = 156.2 +/- 1.2 MeV, mu_B = 29.1 +/- 3.2 MeV, chi^2/n_dof = 1.70, p-value = 3.2%.
- The difference in freeze-out temperature between correlated and uncorrelated cases was at most 2% across all considered ranges of correlation coefficients.
- For the LHC case without correlations and without light nuclei, T_ch = 157.4 +/- 2.0 MeV, which agrees with the value T_ch = 156.5 +/- 1.5 MeV reported by Andronic (2017).

## Context

This work extends the standard statistical model fits to hadron yields (e.g., Andronic (2017), Floris (2014)) by including a covariance matrix that accounts for correlations between yields, particularly those arising from resonance reconstruction from common daughter particles. The analysis also includes light nuclei, following the approach of Siemens (1979) and Braun-Munzinger (1994, 2001). The results are speculative due to the toy-model treatment of correlation coefficients, and the author calls for experimental estimates of the covariance matrix to enable more definitive conclusions.
