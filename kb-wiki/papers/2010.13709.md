# Systematizing and addressing theory uncertainties of unitarization with the Inverse Amplitude Method

> Effective Field Theories (EFTs) constructed as derivative expansions in powers of momentum, in the spirit of Chiral Perturbation Theory (ChPT), are a controllable approximation to strong dynamics as long as the energy of the interacting particles remains small, as they do not respect exact elastic unitarity. This limits their predictive power towards new physics at a higher scale if small separations from the Standard Model are found at the LHC or elsewhere. Unitarized chiral perturbation theory techniques have been devised to extend the reach of the EFT to regimes where partial waves are saturating unitarity, but their uncertainties have hitherto not been addressed thoroughly. Here we take one of the best known of them, the Inverse Amplitude Method (IAM), and carefully following its derivation, we quantify the uncertainty introduced at each step. We compare its hadron ChPT and its electroweak sector Higgs EFT applications. We find that the relative theoretical uncertainty of the IAM at the mass of the first resonance encountered in a partial-wave is of the same order in the counting as the starting uncertainty of the EFT at near-threshold energies, so that its unitarized extension should \textit{a priori} be expected to be reasonably successful. This is so provided a check for zeroes of the partial wave amplitude is carried out and, if they appear near the resonance region, we show how to modify adequately the IAM to take them into account.

## Key claim

The relative theoretical uncertainty of the Inverse Amplitude Method (IAM) at the mass of the first resonance encountered in a partial-wave is of the same order in the counting as the starting uncertainty of the Effective Field Theory at near-threshold energies, provided a check for zeroes of the partial wave amplitude is carried out and, if they appear near the resonance region, the IAM is modified adequately to take them into account.

## Method

The authors systematically review the dispersive derivation of the IAM, identifying each approximation step: neglecting Adler zeroes of the amplitude, approximating the left-cut integral by NLO chiral perturbation theory, truncating the perturbative expansion at NLO, and neglecting inelastic channels. They quantify the uncertainty introduced at each step by comparing the approximate IAM amplitude to the exact one, using the relative separation Δ(s) = (t_IAM(s) - t(s))/t_IAM(s), and apply this analysis to both hadron Chiral Perturbation Theory (ChPT) and the electroweak sector Higgs Effective Field Theory (HEFT).

## Key numbers

- The ratio of the left-cut integral contribution to the right-cut integral contribution at the ρ-resonance mass m_ρ = 770 MeV is approximately 0.5%, using a lower cutoff λ = 470 MeV and an upper cutoff Λ = 20 GeV.
- The uncertainty from neglecting the Adler zero in the scalar isoscalar channel at the f_0(500) pole is of order m_π^4/M_f0^4 ≈ 0.6% in hadron physics.
- For the Electroweak Chiral Lagrangian or HEFT, the analogous uncertainty from Adler zeroes is (m_Z/1 TeV)^4 ≈ 7 × 10^(-5).
- Not taking care of a CDD pole near the resonance region was found to make the prediction of a new resonance off by as much as 25% in prior work.

## Context

This work extends the systematic uncertainty analysis of unitarization methods, which had not been thoroughly addressed previously despite the widespread use of the IAM in hadron physics (Truong 1988, Dobado 1996) and its recent deployment in Higgs Effective Field Theory. The authors build on prior studies that checked the IAM reliability by eliminating a heavy scalar and reconstructing its mass (Corbett 2015), and on the modified IAM that handles Adler zeroes (Gomez Nicola 2007). The analysis enables firmer predictions for any new electroweak physics that might be discovered at the High-Luminosity LHC, where unitarization techniques would be needed to extrapolate Effective Field Theory results to the resonance region.
