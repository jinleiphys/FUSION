# Classical Computation of Elliptic Flow at Large Transverse Momentum

> We compute the contribution of classical fields to the second Fourier coefficient (v_2) of the azimuthal gluon distribution at large transverse momentum in heavy ion collisions. We find that the classical contribution to the flow alone cannot account for the experimentally observed behavior of v_2(p_t) at large transverse momentum p_t.

## Key claim

The classical field contribution to elliptic flow at large transverse momentum (p_t >> Q_s) is small and cannot account for the experimentally observed behavior of v_2(p_t), even though this contribution is generated at very early times in the collision.

## Method

The authors linearize the classical Yang-Mills equations in the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework to compute the azimuthal gluon distribution at large transverse momentum (p_t > Q_s) from a collision of two nuclei with non-uniform color charge distributions. They evaluate the average over valence color charge densities using a Gaussian weight function, expand the resulting integrals to order (Q_s R)^{-2}, and extract the second Fourier coefficient v_2(p_t) from the gluon distribution.

## Key numbers

- For realistic values of Q_s ~ 1 GeV for RHIC and R_x ~ 3 fm for a peripheral collision, the contribution of classical fields to v_2 from momenta k > Q_s is a fraction of a percent.
- For a Gaussian charge distribution, the resulting elliptic flow is v_2(y,k) ≈ (1/(4 k^2)) (1/R_x^2 - 1/R_y^2).
- The integrated v_2 from momenta k > Q_s scales as v_2 ∝ (1/Q_s^2)(1/R_x^2 - 1/R_y^2).
- The experimentally measured v_2(p_t) can be as large as ~25% at p_t ~ 3 GeV for peripheral collisions (34%–85% central collisions).

## Context

This work extends the Color Glass Condensate approach of Kovner, McLerran, and Weigert (KMW) to compute azimuthal anisotropy, generalizing their calculation of the classical gluon spectrum to finite nuclei with non-uniform color charge distributions. The result shows that classical fields alone cannot explain the large p_t behavior of v_2(p_t) observed by RHIC experiments, suggesting that non-flow azimuthal correlations or other mechanisms beyond the classical perturbative expansion are needed. The analysis also explains why numerical simulations of the Yang-Mills equations by Krasnitz and collaborators show significant anisotropy at early times after the collision.
