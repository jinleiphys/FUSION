# Observational Constraints on Neutron Star Masses and Radii

> Precise and reliable measurements of the masses and radii of neutron stars with a variety of masses would provide valuable guidance for improving models of the properties of cold matter with densities above the saturation density of nuclear matter. Several different approaches for measuring the masses and radii of neutron stars have been tried or proposed, including analyzing the X-ray fluxes and spectra of the emission from neutron stars in quiescent low-mass X-ray binary systems and thermonuclear burst sources; fitting the energy-dependent X-ray waveforms of rotation-powered millisecond pulsars, burst oscillations with millisecond periods, and accretion-powered millisecond pulsars; and modeling the gravitational radiation waveforms of coalescing double neutron star and neutron star -- black hole binary systems. We describe the strengths and weaknesses of these approaches, most of which currently have substantial systematic errors, and discuss the prospects for decreasing the systematic errors in each method.

## Key claim

Precise and reliable measurements of neutron star masses and radii would provide valuable guidance for improving models of cold matter above nuclear saturation density, but most current methods have substantial systematic errors that prevent confident use of the results.

## Method

The paper reviews multiple astrophysical approaches for measuring neutron star masses and radii: analyzing X-ray fluxes and spectra from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries and thermonuclear burst sources; fitting energy-dependent X-ray waveforms of rotation-powered millisecond pulsars, burst oscillations, and accretion-powered millisecond pulsars; and modeling gravitational radiation waveforms from coalescing double neutron star and neutron star-black hole binary systems. For mass measurements, the most reliable results come from binary pulsar systems using Keplerian mass functions combined with post-Keplerian effects such as Shapiro delay or gravitational redshift. For radius measurements, the paper examines methods using thermal emission from non-accreting neutron stars and the flux-color temperature method applied to thermonuclear X-ray bursts, particularly photospheric radius expansion bursts.

## Key numbers

- Mass of PSR J1614-2230: 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses, measured via Shapiro delay
- Mass of PSR J0348+0432: 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses, measured via gravitational redshift of white dwarf companion spectral lines
- Best estimate for mass of PSR B1757+20: 2.40 ± 0.12 solar masses, but systematic uncertainty allows mass as low as 1.66 solar masses
- Estimate for mass of PSR J1311-3430 from basic light curve model: 2.68 ± 0.14 solar masses, but systematic effects could allow mass as low as 1.8 solar masses
- Radius estimates from fits of nonmagnetic hydrogen model atmospheres to quiescent low-mass X-ray binary spectra: approximately 9-10 km
- Color factor f_c for X-ray burst atmospheres: typically 1.4-1.9 (color temperature 40-90% higher than effective temperature)
- Range of predicted radii for a 1.5 solar mass neutron star: approximately 10 km to approximately 15 km, with most models predicting between approximately 11 km and approximately 13 km

## Context

This review extends earlier work on neutron star mass and radius measurements, referencing reviews by Miller (2013) and Lattimer (2014) and an alternative view by Özel (2013). It discusses the implications for nuclear physics as reviewed by Hebeler et al. (2013). The paper highlights that fits of nonmagnetic helium model atmospheres to quiescent low-mass X-ray binary spectra commonly yield radius estimates roughly 50% larger than fits using nonmagnetic hydrogen model atmospheres to the same data, as shown by Suleimanov et al. (2012), Catuneanu et al. (2013), and Heinke et al. (2014). The paper enables future progress by identifying the key systematic errors that must be reduced, particularly through better modeling and data from upcoming missions such as Athena.
