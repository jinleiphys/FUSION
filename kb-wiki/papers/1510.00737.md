# Modifications of Nucleons in Nuclei

> I summarize recent results and discuss upcoming and planned experiments that attempt to elucidate how the structure of nucleons might be modified by nuclear binding.

## Key claim
Non-trivial modifications of nucleon structure inside nuclei exist, but a quantitative comparison between experimental signals and rigorous QCD-based predictions remains elusive due to difficulties in low-energy QCD calculations and larger uncertainties in hadronic experiments compared to atomic spectroscopy.

## Method
The paper reviews experimental evidence for nucleon modifications in nuclei, primarily from deep inelastic scattering (DIS) measurements of the EMC effect, where the per-nucleon structure function F2(x) in the valence region (0.2 ≤ x ≤ 0.8) shows a significant reduction relative to free nucleons. It also discusses form factor measurements on bound nuclei, neutrino scattering experiments, Drell-Yan experiments, and upcoming experiments at the energy-upgraded Jefferson Lab that will use spectator tagging, polarized targets, and isotope comparisons to probe the mechanism.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy of individual nucleons in nuclei lies between 0.12% and 0.9% of their masses
- The EMC effect shows a roughly linear reduction in the per-nucleon structure function F2(x) for 0.2 ≤ x ≤ 0.8, with a slope that increases for heavier and denser nuclei
- A first attempt to measure the EMC effect in deuterium found suggestive evidence of an EMC slope of order -0.1 (within large experimental uncertainties)
- The BONuS experiment extracted the structure function F2n of the free neutron for the first time
- A measurement of the ratio of electric to magnetic form factors of a proton bound in 4He found a substantial reduction relative to a free proton
- The correlation between EMC effect strength and short-range correlation probability (a2) is nearly perfect over a wide range of nuclei
- 8 out of 9 nucleons in 9Be are located inside tightly bound alpha clusters, with only the remaining neutron further out

## Context
This review extends the 30-year-old puzzle from the original EMC experiment (1983) and corroborates findings from many labs including Dasu, Gomez, NMC, and BCDMS. A recent precision experiment by Seely (Jefferson Lab) extended knowledge to lighter nuclei including 3,4He, 9Be and 12C, finding that the EMC slope correlates with local nuclear density rather than average density. The correlation with short-range correlations (Larry, Or) supports models by Frankfurt and Strikman (quark-meson coupling) and others. The upcoming Jefferson Lab experiments (E12-10-008, E12-14-001, E12-11-107, BONuS12, Marathon) will enable precise measurements of isotope dependence, spin structure modifications, and spectator-tagged DIS to distinguish between competing models.
