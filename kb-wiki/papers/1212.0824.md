# Macroscopic approximation to relativistic kinetic theory from a nonlinear closure

> We use a macroscopic description of a system of relativistic particles based on adding a nonequilibrium tensor to the usual hydrodynamic variables. The nonequilibrium tensor is linked to relativistic kinetic theory through a nonlinear closure suggested by the Entropy Production Principle; the evolution equation is obtained by the method of moments, and together with energy-momentum conservation closes the system. Transport coefficients are chosen to reproduce second order fluid dynamics if gradients are small. We compare the resulting formalism to exact solutions of Boltzmann's equation in 0+1 dimensions and show that it tracks kinetic theory better than second order fluid dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a nonequilibrium effective theory (NET) based on a nonlinear closure from the Entropy Production Principle tracks exact solutions of Boltzmann's equation better than second-order fluid dynamics (SOFD), even for very large shear viscosity-to-entropy ratios (η/s up to 3.0) and strong off-equilibrium initial conditions, but the transport coefficients are chosen to reproduce SOFD when gradients are small.

## Method

The authors construct a macroscopic description by adding a nonequilibrium tensor γ^{μν} to the usual hydrodynamic variables (energy density ε and four-velocity u^μ). They link this tensor to relativistic kinetic theory through a nonlinear closure derived from the Entropy Production Principle, obtaining the distribution function f as a quadratic expansion in γ^{ij}. The evolution equation for γ^{ij} is obtained via the method of moments applied to the second moment of the Boltzmann equation with a relaxation-time collision kernel, and together with energy-momentum conservation closes the system. The formalism is tested against exact solutions of the Boltzmann equation for 0+1 dimensional boost-invariant flow using a Lattice Boltzmann implementation.

- For η/s = 0.8 with equilibrium initial condition, the NET tracks kinetic theory better than SOFD, while SOFD deviates significantly (Figure 1, upper panel).
- For η/s = 3.0 with equilibrium initial condition, the pressure anisotropy from SOFD becomes negative, while the NET still tracks kinetic theory (Figure 1, lower panel).
- For η/s = 0.08 with strong off-equilibrium initial condition (P_L/P_T ≈ 1.5, corresponding to |Π_0| ≈ 2P_0/7), agreement between SOFD, NET, and kinetic theory is excellent (Figure 2, upper panel).
- For η/s = 0.8 with strong off-equilibrium initial condition, SOFD deviates significantly from kinetic theory while the NET tracks it well (Figure 2, upper panel).
- For η/s = 3.0 with strong off-equilibrium initial condition, SOFD pressure anisotropy becomes negative for τ − τ_0 > 2 fm/c, while the NET tracks kinetic theory remarkably well (Figure 2, lower panel).
- Initial conditions: τ_0 = 0.5 fm/c, T_0 = 295 MeV, ε_0 = 4.9 GeV/fm^3.
- Transport coefficients used: τ_π = 6η/(sT) (Boltzmann gas value) and λ_1 = η/(2π T).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of a nonequilibrium effective theory (Peralta-Ramos and Calzetta, 2011) and its application to heavy ion collisions (Peralta-Ramos and Calzetta, 2012), with the link to kinetic theory established in a prior paper (Peralta-Ramos and Calzetta, 2012). It addresses limitations of standard Israel-Stewart theory (Israel and Stewart, 1979), which breaks down at η/s > 0.2-0.3 and at early times with large momentum-space anisotropies. The NET is compared to second-order fluid dynamics (SOFD) as formulated by Baier et al. (2008) and to anisotropic hydrodynamics (Martinez and Strickland, 2010; Florkowski and Ryblewski, 2011), which similarly introduces a nonhydrodynamic variable. The results enable unified modeling of heavy ion collision dynamics from the early anisotropic stage through the deconfinement transition to freeze-out, including a realistic temperature-dependent η/s that reaches large values in the hadronic phase.
