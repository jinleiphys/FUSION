# Report from the A.I. For Nuclear Physics Workshop

> This report is an outcome of the workshop "AI for Nuclear Physics" held at Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility on March 4-6, 2020. The workshop brought together 184 scientists to explore opportunities for Nuclear Physics in the area of Artificial Intelligence. The workshop consisted of plenary talks, as well as six working groups. The report includes the workshop deliberations and additional contributions to describe prospects for using AI across Nuclear Physics research.

# A.I. for Nuclear Physics

## Key claim
The workshop report asserts that artificial intelligence methods have "tremendous potential" to transform nuclear physics research across experiment, theory, and accelerator operations, but that current AI tools and methodologies have limitations that must be addressed through workforce development, problem-specific tool adaptation, uncertainty quantification, and adequate computing resources before this potential can be fully realized.

## Method
The workshop brought together 184 scientists at Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility on March 4-6, 2020, for plenary talks and six working group sessions covering lattice QCD, low-energy nuclear theory, nuclear experiment, accelerator science, and data infrastructure. The report synthesizes these deliberations along with additional contributions to identify priority research directions, community needs, and commonalities across nuclear physics subfields for applying AI techniques.

## Key numbers
- 184 scientists attended the workshop at Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility on March 4-6, 2020
- 8 teams of 4 members each participated in an adjunct hackathon event on March 3, 2020
- The workshop consisted of plenary talks and six working group sessions

## Context
This report extends the 2015 Long-Range Plan on Research Opportunities and Directions (Geesaman, 2015) by identifying how AI can benefit all top priorities of that plan. It parallels the workforce development challenges outlined in the NSAC Report on "Nuclear Physics and Quantum Information Science" (QIS report) and connects to broader DOE efforts including the Exascale Computing Project applications ExaLearn and CANDLE. The workshop enables future cross-disciplinary partnerships between nuclear physics researchers and AI/ML/data science experts, and identifies specific areas where AI could address long-standing computational bottlenecks such as the fermionic sign problem in lattice QCD, Bayesian calibration for nuclear mass extrapolations, and fast emulation for many-body simulations of nuclear fission.
