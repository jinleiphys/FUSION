# Relativistic Mean-Field and Beyond Approaches for Deformed Hypernuclei

> We report the recent progress in relativistic mean-field (RMF) and beyond approaches for the low-energy structure of deformed hypernuclei. We show that the $Λ$ hyperon with orbital angular momentum $\ell=0$ (or $\ell>1$) generally reduces (enhances) nuclear quadrupole collectivity. The beyond mean-field studies of hypernuclear low-lying states demonstrate that there is generally a large configuration mixing between the two components $[^{A-1}Z (I^+) \otimes Λp_{1/2}]^J$ and $[^{A-1}Z (I\pm2 ^+) \otimes Λp_{3/2}]^J$ in the hypernuclear $1/2^-_1, 3/2^-_1$ states. The mixing weight increases as the collective correlation of nuclear core becomes stronger. Finally, we show how the energies of hypernuclear low-lying states are sensitive to parameters in the effective $N Λ$ interaction, the uncertainty of which has a large impact on the predicted maximal mass of neutron stars.

# Relativistic Mean-Field and Beyond Approaches for Deformed Hypernuclei

## Key claim

The paper claims that the energies of hypernuclear low-lying states are sensitive to parameters in the effective NΛ interaction, and that this sensitivity means the uncertainty in the NΛ interaction parameters has a large impact on the predicted maximal mass of neutron stars, but this claim is conditional on the specific relativistic mean-field framework used and on the fact that multiple parameter sets (e.g., PCY-S1 through PCY-S4) can reproduce the same Λ binding energies while yielding different predictions for excited states.

## Method

The authors employed two beyond-mean-field approaches built on relativistic mean-field (RMF) solutions: the generator coordinate method (GCM), which diagonalizes the hypernuclear Hamiltonian in a basis of quantum-number projected mean-field states with deformation as the generator coordinate, and the particle-core coupling (PCC) model, which describes the hypernucleus as a Λ hyperon coupled to a core nucleus whose low-lying states are obtained from a separate GCM calculation. Both approaches use the same effective NN and NΛ interactions (meson-exchange or point-coupling forms) and solve coupled equations for the hypernuclear wave functions, with the PCC model yielding a set of coupled Dirac equations for the radial wave functions of the Λ in different channels.

## Key numbers

- For ⁵¹_ΛV, the Λ binding energy B_Λ changes by -0.1 MeV (Λ_s), +0.8 MeV (Λ_p), and +2.2 MeV (Λ_d) when deformation is included, compared to spherical RMF calculations.
- For ¹⁵⁵_ΛSm, the configuration mixing weights in the 1/2⁻_1 state are 36.3% for [0₁⁺ ⊗ Λp₃/₂], 28.1% for [2₁⁺ ⊗ Λp₁/₂], and 31.8% for [2₁⁺ ⊗ Λp₃/₂].
- The energy splitting between 1/2⁻_1 and 3/2⁻_1 states in ¹³_ΛC is predicted to be 0.253 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 152 ± 54 (stat) ± 36 (syst) keV.
- For ¹⁵_ΛC, the predicted splitting is 0.344 MeV; for ¹⁷_ΛC it is 0.067 MeV; for ¹⁹_ΛC it is 0.033 MeV.
- In the Sm hypernuclei, the energy of the 1/2⁻_1 and 3/2⁻_1 states decreases from 4.7 MeV to 3.5 MeV as neutron number increases from 82 to 92, and the splitting decreases from 68 keV to 4 keV.
- The single-channel calculation for Sm hypernuclei gives excitation energies around 4.8 MeV for the 1/2⁻_1 and 3/2⁻_1 states, with a splitting of about 70 keV.

## Context

This work extends previous RMF studies of spherical hypernuclei (reviewed in Hagino (2016)) and deformed hypernuclei (Win (2008), Lu (2011), Xu (2015), Xue (2015)) by incorporating beyond-mean-field correlations through GCM and PCC methods. The results contradict the naive interpretation of the 1/2⁻-3/2⁻ energy difference as pure spin-orbit splitting in cases with strong collective correlations (e.g., ¹⁷_ΛC and ¹⁹_ΛC), while confirming that ¹³_ΛC and especially ¹⁵_ΛC are suitable for extracting the p-orbital Λ spin-orbit splitting. The work enables future calibration of effective NΛ interactions using hypernuclear γ-ray spectroscopy data, which is important for resolving the "hyperon puzzle" in neutron star maximum mass predictions within the RMF framework, as previously discussed by Glendenning (1991), Long (2012), and Sun (2018).
