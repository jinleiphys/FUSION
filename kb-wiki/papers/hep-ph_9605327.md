# Lattice Computations of Small-x Parton Distributions in a Model of Parton Densities in Very Large Nuclei

> Using weak coupling methods McLerran and Venugopalan~\cite{LV1} expressed the parton distributions in large nuclei as correlation functions of a two dimensional Euclidean field theory. The theory has the dimensionful coupling $g^2 μ$, where $μ^2\sim A^{1/3}$ is the valence quark color charge squared per unit area. We use a lattice regularization to investigate these correlation functions both analytically and numerically for the simplified case of $SU(2)$ gauge theory. In weak coupling ($g^2μL<< 5$), where $L$ is the transverse size of the nucleus, the numerical results agree with the {\it analytic} lattice weak coupling results. For $g^2μL>> 5$, no solutions exist at O($a^4$) where $a$ is the lattice spacing. This suggests an ill-defined infrared behavior for the two dimensional theory. A recent proposal of McLerran et al.~\cite{Kovner} for an {\it analytic} solution of the classical problem is discussed briefly.

## Key claim

The authors test the conjecture that classical gluon distributions in very large nuclei generate a screening mass at strong coupling, but find that for the dimensionless coupling parameter \(g^2\mu L > 5\) (where \(L\) is the transverse nuclear size), no solutions to the stochastic lattice equations exist at O(\(a^4\)) accuracy, suggesting an ill-defined infrared behavior for the two-dimensional theory rather than confirming the existence of a screening mass.

## Method

The authors formulated the stochastic differential equation \(\vec{\nabla} \cdot U \vec{\nabla} U^\dagger = -ig^2 \rho(x_t)\) on a two-dimensional lattice for SU(2) gauge theory, where \(\rho\) are Gaussian-distributed valence quark color charges. They solved the resulting difference equations using a conjugate gradient method to minimize a function \(F\) that enforces both the field equations and unitarity, then computed "reduced" one-dimensional projections of the gauge field correlation functions. The coupling parameter \(g^2\mu L\) was varied by changing the lattice size \(N\) while keeping the lattice spacing \(a\) approximately fixed at 0.1.

## Key numbers

- The weak coupling condition is \(g^2\mu L << 5\), derived from requiring \(g^2\mu a \cdot \phi << 1\) in the expansion \(U = \exp(i g^2 \mu a \cdot \phi)\).
- For \(g^2\mu L = 0.5\) on a \(41 \times 41\) lattice, numerical results for \(\langle \alpha^r_2(x) \alpha^r_2(0) \rangle / g^2\mu^2\) agree with analytic lattice weak coupling results.
- The \(\langle \alpha^r_1 \alpha^r_1 \rangle\) correlation function grows as \((g^2\mu L)^2\) in the weak coupling region, confirmed for \(g^2\mu L\) up to 5.
- For \(g^2\mu L \ge 10\), no solutions to the stochastic equations exist at O(\(a^4\)) accuracy, with runs on \(N=111\) and \(N=211\) lattices (11 and 6 iterations respectively) showing minima too high compared to O(\(a^4\)).

## Context

This work extends the McLerran-Venugopalan model (1994) which expressed parton distributions in large nuclei as correlation functions of a two-dimensional Euclidean field theory, and the classical Yang-Mills solutions of McLerran and Venugopalan (1994) that reduced the problem to solving stochastic differential equations. The authors attempted to test the conjecture from that earlier work that a screening mass \(m_{screen} \sim \alpha_S \mu\) would appear at strong coupling, but instead found that the classical theory breaks down for \(g^2\mu L > 5\), contradicting the expectation that the continuum limit \(L \rightarrow \infty\) would be well-defined. The results suggest that quantum fluctuations beyond the classical approximation, as discussed in subsequent work by McLerran et al. (1995), may be necessary to resolve the infrared behavior.
