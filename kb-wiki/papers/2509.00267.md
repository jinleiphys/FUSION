# Multi-messengers from the radioactive decay of $r$-process nuclei

> The radioactive $β$-decay of nuclei synthesized in the rapid neutron capture process ($r$-process) releases a variety of particles, including electrons, $γ$-rays, neutrinos, and neutrons. These particles provide a rich set of multimessenger signals that carry information about the astrophysical environments where neutron-rich nucleosynthesis occurs. In this work, we calculate from first principles the emission spectra resulting from the $β$-decay of $r$-process nuclei. Our approach incorporates detailed nuclear structure and decay data to model the energy distributions of each particle species. We couple the spectra with a nuclear reaction network simulation to obtain the temporal evolution of these distributions. We find that the emission distributions vary significantly in time and are non-thermal, with substantial average energies. We investigate these nuclear signals as a direct probe of heavy element formation and show that they are complementary observables to kilonova.

## Key claim

The paper calculates from first principles the time-dependent emission spectra of electrons, gamma-rays, neutrinos, and neutrons from r-process beta-decay, finding that these spectra are decisively non-thermal with average energies far higher than expected from a thermal distribution; however, the calculations assume only allowed beta transitions and neglect first-forbidden and higher-order transitions, and the authors note it is uncertain how much these limitations affect the results.

## Method

The authors combine tabulated beta-decay emission spectra from Mumpower (2025) for individual neutron-rich nuclei with the PRISM nuclear reaction network (version 1.6.0) to compute the time-dependent emission spectra for each particle species. The individual spectra are calculated using a statistical multi-phase approach: a beta-strength function is obtained from experimental data or the Quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation, electron and neutrino spectra are derived from statistical phase-space considerations, and gamma-ray and neutron emissions are computed via a Hauser-Feshbach statistical approach. The nucleosynthesis simulation uses a gamma-ray burst cocoon trajectory from Mumpower (2025) that produces a robust r-process with actinide production, matching solar r-process residuals above mass number 130.

## Key numbers

- Average electron and neutrino energies are approximately 5 MeV for the first second, then 1.5-2.5 MeV until about 10 seconds, not dropping below 1 MeV until neutron capture ends at about 100 seconds
- Neutrinos carry the largest fraction of decay energy, between 40% and 50% of the total flux at all times
- The total neutrino fluence in the first 10 seconds is estimated as 1.54 x 10^8 cm^-2 for an ejecta mass of 0.01 solar masses at 15 kpc distance
- Estimated number of inverse beta-decay events in Hyper-Kamiokande (260 kton) is approximately 2.2 events for a galactic r-process event at 15 kpc
- The gamma-ray spectrum at 1 week shows identifiable lines including the 2.6 MeV line from Tl-208 decay and 2.0, 3.1, and 3.3 MeV lines from La-140 decay
- The cocoon trajectory uses initial density rho_0 = 3.2 x 10^4 g/cm^3, initial temperature T_0 = 2 GK, and initial electron fraction Y_e = 0.034

## Context

This work extends the framework of Mumpower (2025) for calculating individual beta-decay emission spectra by coupling those spectra with a full nuclear reaction network to obtain time-dependent multi-messenger signals from r-process nucleosynthesis. The results provide input for kilonova modeling that goes beyond the simple Q-value partitioning typically used in models such as those by Wollaeger (2021) and Kasen (2017), and complement the detailed gamma-ray spectra work of Siqueira (2023) which only used experimentally measured emissions. The paper enables future radiative transport calculations to incorporate self-consistent, non-thermal emission spectra and suggests that direct observation of gamma-ray lines (such as the Tl-208 line proposed by Vassh (2024)) and neutrino signals from galactic r-process events may be possible with next-generation detectors.
