# A Method to Calculate Fission-Fragment Yields $Y(Z,N)$ versus Proton and Neutron Number in the Brownian Shape-Motion Model. Application to calculations of U and Pu charge yields

> We propose a method to calculate the two-dimensional (2D) fission-fragment yield $Y(Z,N)$ versus both proton and neutron number, with inclusion of odd-even staggering effects in both variables. The approach is to use Brownian shape-motion on a macroscopic-microscopic potential-energy surface which, for a particular compound system is calculated versus four shape variables: elongation (quadrupole moment $Q_2$), neck $d$, left nascent fragment spheroidal deformation $ε_{\rm f1}$, right nascent fragment deformation $ε_{\rm f2}$ and two asymmetry variables, namely proton and neutron numbers in each of the two fragments. The extension of previous models 1) introduces a method to calculate this generalized potential-energy function and 2) allows the correlated transfer of nucleon pairs in one step, in addition to sequential transfer. In the previous version the potential energy was calculated as a function of $Z$ and $N$ of the compound system and its shape, including the asymmetry of the shape. We outline here how to generalize the model from the "compound-system" model to a model where the emerging fragment proton and neutron numbers also enter, over and above the compound system composition.

## Key claim

The authors propose a method to calculate two-dimensional fission-fragment yields Y(Z,N) as a function of both proton and neutron number by extending the Brownian shape-motion model to include odd-even staggering effects, but the method is initially tested only on charge yields (summing over neutrons) and requires the assumption that the proton and neutron shell-plus-pairing corrections can be calculated independently and combined additively.

## Method

The authors calculate a six-dimensional potential-energy surface for a fissioning compound nucleus using the macroscopic-microscopic method with a three-quadratic-surface shape parameterization, where the shape is specified by five parameters: quadrupole moment Q2, neck diameter d, left and right fragment spheroidal deformations εf1 and εf2, and mass asymmetry αg. They then extend this to a sixth dimension by allowing the proton and neutron numbers in each nascent fragment to vary independently, using tabulated proton and neutron shell-plus-pairing corrections calculated separately on grids corresponding to integer Z1s and N1s, and adding a shape-dependent odd-pairing energy term for odd fragment splits. Random walks (Brownian shape-motion) are performed on this potential-energy surface, starting from the ground state, with steps selected via a Metropolis-type algorithm using temperature-dependent acceptance probabilities, until the neck radius reaches 2.5 fm.

## Key numbers

- The critical neck radius at which the mass split is frozen is 2.5 fm (compared to the radius of spherical 240Pu which is 7.2 fm, and the radius of 16O which is 2.92 fm)
- The pairing-gap parameter Δ is chosen as 1.0 MeV for the odd-pairing energy term
- For 270Hs108162, the proton and neutron shell-plus-pairing corrections are -3.7023 MeV and -5.3715 MeV respectively; for 274Cn112162, they are -3.6305 MeV and -5.3353 MeV respectively, showing a change of only 0.0362 MeV in the neutron correction when proton number changes by 4 units
- The difference in macroscopic energy between spherical 102Zr and 106Zr is 12.865 MeV, while for deformed shapes with ε2=0.5 the difference is 12.846 MeV, a negligible change of 0.019 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Brownian shape-motion model previously developed by Randrup, Moller, and collaborators (Randrup 2011a, 2011b, 2013a) which successfully reproduced 70 charge-yield distributions measured at GSI (Schmidt 2000) but could not describe odd-even staggering because pairing was treated for the compound system as a whole. The authors had previously shown (Moller 2014b) that odd-even staggering magnitude correlates with excitation energy above the potential-energy surface, motivating this extension. The new method enables calculation of full two-dimensional yield distributions Y(Z,N) and incorporates correlated nucleon-pair transfer (discussed in Broglia 2012 and references therein, including Oertzen 2001 and Corradi 2011), going beyond earlier models that relied on macroscopic potential-energy surfaces or postulated terms fitted to observed yields.
