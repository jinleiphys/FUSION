# Magnetic moments of long isotopic chains

> Dipole magnetic moments of several long isotopic chains are analyzed within the self-consistent Finite Fermi System theory based on the Generalized Energy Density Functional method with exact account for the pairing and quasi-particle continuum. New data for nuclei far from the beta-stability valley are included in the analysis. For a number of semi-magic isotopes of the tin and lead chains a good description of the data is obtained, with accuracy of 0.1 - 0.2 mu_N. A chain of non-magic isotopes of copper is also analyzed in detail. It is found that the systematic analysis of magnetic moments of this long chain yields rich information on the evolution of the nuclear structure of the Cu isotopes. In particular, it may give a signal of deformation for the ground state of some nuclei in the chain.

## Key claim
The self-consistent Finite Fermi System theory, based on the Generalized Energy Density Functional method with exact pairing and continuum treatment, describes magnetic moments of semi-magic tin and lead isotopes with accuracy of 0.1–0.2 μ_N, but for the non-magic copper chain the spherical one-quasiparticle approximation fails at the wings, where deformation or strong particle-phonon coupling must be invoked; the analysis suggests that systematic study of magnetic moments across a long chain can signal ground-state deformation for some nuclei.

## Method
The authors applied the self-consistent Finite Fermi System (FFS) theory using the Generalized Energy Density Functional (EDF) method, which includes pairing correlations and exact treatment of the quasiparticle continuum. For odd-A nuclei, the one-quasiparticle approximation was used, with the effective field equation solved including the Landau–Migdal interaction amplitude and local quasiparticle charges (spin, orbital, and tensor). For the copper chain, particle-phonon coupling (via the 2⁺₁ state) and a simple rigid-rotor deformation model were additionally tested.

## Key numbers
- For Sn isotopes (12 odd isotopes, 25 data points): as a rule, |δμ| < 0.1 μ_N; only in four cases 0.1 μ_N < |δμ| < 0.15 μ_N; except for ²¹³Sn and ²¹⁵Sn, all experimental moments reproduced within 10%.
- For Pb isotopes (15 odd isotopes, 19 data points): |δμ| < 0.1 μ_N in 15 cases; 0.1 μ_N < |δμ| < 0.15 μ_N in four cases; except for ²⁹⁷Pb* and ²⁰⁷Pb, all moments reproduced within 10%.
- For the Cu chain (11 odd isotopes, ⁵⁷Cu to ⁷⁷Cu): spherical one-quasiparticle calculations deviate significantly at the wings; for ⁵⁷Cu (μ_exp = 2.00(5) μ_N) the spherical prediction is 2.850 μ_N, while a deformed model gives qualitative agreement; for ⁶⁹Cu (μ_exp = 2.84(2) μ_N) the spherical result (2.789 μ_N) matches without phonon admixture (w = 0).
- The maximum relative neutron excess for the Cu chain is ε₊ = 33.3% (δN₊ = 12, N^β_max = 36); for Sn it is ε₊ = 17.4%; for Pb it is ε₋ = 24.8% (deficiency).
- For the N=82 isotones (Z=51 to 65): theoretical results differ at most 15% from experimental values; absolute deviations δμ are somewhat larger than for the isotopic chains.

## Context
This work extends the self-consistent FFS theory calculations of Borzov et al. (2005) (BST) to long isotopic chains, incorporating new experimental data for Cu (Minamisono et al., ISOLDE collaboration) and Pb isotopes. It confirms the universal character of spin-dependent FFS parameters found for β-stable nuclei, and for the Cu chain it contrasts with many-particle shell-model calculations by Brown (2001) which covered only three Cu isotopes (A=61,63,65). The analysis enables future hybrid approaches combining the many-particle shell model with FFS theory, and suggests that magnetic moment measurements can signal shape transitions, particularly at the extremes of isotopic chains where deformation is predicted by the Advanced Thomas-Fermi method of Hilaire and Girod (2007).
