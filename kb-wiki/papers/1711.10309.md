# $\textit{Ab initio}$ calculation of the calorimetric electron capture spectrum of $^{163}$Holmium: Intra-atomic decay into bound-states

> The determination of the electron neutrino mass by electron capture in $^{163}$Ho relies on a precise understanding of the deexcitation of a core hole after an electron capture event. We here present an \textit{ab intio} calculation of the electron capture spectrum in $^{163}$Ho, including all intra-atomic decay channels into bound-states. We use theoretical methods developed for the calculation of core level spectroscopy on correlated electron compounds. Our comparison critically tests the reality of these theories. We find that relativistic interactions beyond the Dirac equation, i.e. quantum-electro dynamics, only lead to minor shifts of the spectral peaks. The electronic relaxation after an electron capture event due to the changed nuclear potential leads to a mixing of different edges, but due to conservation of angular momentum of each scattered electron, no additional structures emerge. Many-body Coulomb interactions lead to the formation of multiplets and to additional peaks with multiple core-holes due to Auger decay. Multiplets crucially change the appearance of the resonances on a Rydberg energy scale. The additional structures due to Auger decay are, although clearly visible, relatively weak compared to the one core hole states and accidentally far away from the end-point region of the spectrum. As the end-point of the spectrum is effected most by the neutrino mass these additional states do not influence the statistics for determining the neutrino mass directly. The multiplet broadening and Auger shake-up of the main core-level edges do change the apparent line-width and accompanying lifetime of these edges, thereby invalidating experimentally obtained lifetimes at the resonance for regions far away from the resonance.

## Key claim

The paper presents an *ab initio* calculation of the electron capture spectrum of 163Ho that includes all intra-atomic decay channels into bound states, but the authors explicitly note that the calculation is restricted to bound states only and does not include decay into continuum states, meaning the spectrum is given by a discrete set of delta functions that require additional broadening to compare with experiment.

## Method

The authors use configuration interaction methods from quantum chemistry to calculate the many-body ground state of 163Ho, and Green's function methods to describe the electron capture process. The weak interaction is treated as a time-dependent perturbation via a transition operator, and the electron capture spectrum is computed from the Lehmann spectral representation of the Green's function, which describes the time evolution of a Dy atom after an electron is captured from a 163Ho atom. The calculation systematically adds relaxation channels: first no relaxation, then relaxation due to modified nuclear and core hole potentials, then inter-core relaxation due to Coulomb repulsion, and finally relaxation that changes the occupation of the 4f valence shell via Auger decay.

## Key numbers

- The total decay energy available for electron capture in 163Ho is Q_EC = 2833 ± 30 (stat) ± 15 (syst) eV, from Eliseev (2015).
- The maximum discrepancy between measured and calculated peak positions is about 20 eV.
- The calculation includes 7 resonances labelled M1 to P1 and M2 to O2 for capture events from the 3s to 6s and 3p1/2 to 5p1/2 shells, respectively.
- The K and L shells are outside the spectrum window given by Q_EC.
- The major peaks shift by a maximum of approximately 3 eV when including relaxation due to modified nuclear and core hole potentials.
- There are 144 different ways to create states with two core holes via Auger decay within the allowed energy range.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical calculations of the 163Ho electron capture spectrum by Faessler (2015, 2017), Robertson (2015), and De Rujula (2016), which stressed the importance of additional satellites. The authors use methods developed for core level spectroscopy in condensed matter physics, as described by de Groot (2008), Tanaka (1995), and Rehr (2009), and apply them to electron capture for the first time. The calculation enables improved understanding of the spectral shape near the endpoint for neutrino mass experiments such as ECHo, HOLMES, and NUMECS, and the authors note that explicit calculations of core hole lifetime due to Auger and fluorescence decay into continuum states are needed for accurate neutrino mass determination.
