# Caloric curve in fragmentation

> We study phase transitions of small two dimensional Lennard Jones drops via microcanonical molecular dynamics in a broad energy range. We found that the caloric curve can be extended to high energies to comprehend high evaporation rates and highly non-equilibrium phenomena such as multifragmentation. Multifragmentation appears as a constant temperature region in the caloric curve like the solid-liquid phase transition.

## Key claim

The microcanonical caloric curve of small two-dimensional Lennard Jones drops can be extended from the solid-liquid transition region into the high-energy multifragmentation regime, where multifragmentation appears as a second constant-temperature plateau similar to the solid-liquid phase transition, but the authors hedge that fragmentation is not a phase transition in a strict sense (it does not show phase coexistence).

## Method

The authors performed microcanonical molecular dynamics simulations of 100 Lennard Jones particles in two dimensions with a cutoff radius of 3 sigma. For the low-energy caloric curve, they evolved equilibrated drops with free boundary conditions, rescaling velocities to increase energy stepwise while discarding evaporated particles. For multifragmentation, they prepared hot, compressed drops by cutting circular drops from a thermalized periodic system and tracked the local temperature (velocity fluctuations around the mean radial expansion) during the expansion and breakup, as well as the temperature of asymptotic fragments after breakup.

## Key numbers

- The limit temperature for the liquid drop is approximately 0.37 epsilon, above which the system cannot be heated without entering the multifragmentation regime.
- The local temperature at breakup time lies between 0.35 epsilon and 0.4 epsilon, independent of initial conditions (density and energy).
- The energy range studied for the equilibrium caloric curve was -2.8 epsilon to -1.4 epsilon.
- The initial temperature range for multifragmentation experiments was 1 epsilon to 4 epsilon.
- The time step for integration was 0.0025 t0, conserving energy and total angular momentum better than 0.01%.
- The time average for equilibrium temperature calculations was performed over 150 t0, with the first 2x10^4 time steps discarded for thermalization.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of solid-liquid phase transitions in small clusters (Labastie and Whetten, 1990; Kunz and Berry, 1994; Nayak, Ramaswamy, and Chakravarty, 1995) to higher energies where evaporation and multifragmentation occur. It connects to nuclear multifragmentation experiments by the ALADIN collaboration (Pochodzalla et al., 1995) and the EOS collaboration (Gilkes et al., 1994), and the results are in agreement with recent experimental nuclear caloric curves (Hauger et al., 1996; Serfling et al., submitted). The authors contrast their findings with simulations by Bondorf et al. (1996) that showed a rising temperature in the fragmentation region, which they attribute to improper treatment of collective flow. This work enables a full characterization of the breakup state in multifragmentation by establishing the limit temperature that determines the velocity fluctuations over the collective expansion.
