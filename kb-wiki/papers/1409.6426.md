# Pairing effect in thermal shape fluctuation model on the width of giant dipole resonance

> We present an approach that includes temperature-dependent shell effects and fluctuations of the pairing field in the thermal shape fluctuation model (TSFM). We apply this approach to study the width of giant dipole resonance (GDR) in $^{97}$Tc, $^{120}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb. Our results demonstrate that the TSFM that includes pairing fluctuations can explain the recently observed quenching in the GDR width. We also show that to validate pairing prescriptions and the parameters involved, we require more and precise data.

# Pairing effect in thermal shape fluctuation model on the width of giant dipole resonance

## Key claim

The thermal shape fluctuation model (TSFM) can explain the recently observed quenching of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) width at low temperatures in open-shell nuclei, but only when both temperature-dependent shell effects and fluctuations of the pairing field are included; the simple BCS treatment of pairing is insufficient, and the effect of pairing fluctuations is crucial for a precise match with experimental data.

## Method

The authors extended the TSFM by incorporating pairing fluctuations through a grand canonical ensemble approach, where the free energy is computed using the finite-temperature Nilsson-Strutinsky method with temperature-dependent shell corrections and the pairing gap treated as a fluctuating variable. The GDR cross section was constructed from Lorentzian components whose frequencies were modified by the pairing interaction, and the observable GDR width was obtained by averaging over both quadrupole shape deformations (β, γ) and proton and neutron pairing gaps (Δ_P, Δ_N) using a Boltzmann-weighted integration of the free energy surface. Calculations were performed for three nuclei: ⁹⁷Tc, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²⁰Sn at T = 0.4 MeV, the free energy surface with pairing becomes much crisper around the minimum compared to the case without pairing, leading to a quenched GDR width; the average neutron pairing gap with pairing fluctuations is sustained even at T = 2 MeV, whereas the BCS neutron pairing gap vanishes at T = 0.8 MeV.
- For ⁹⁷Tc, the BCS proton pairing gap vanishes at T = 0.5 MeV and the neutron pairing gap vanishes at T ≈ 0.65 MeV, but with pairing fluctuations the average pairing gaps remain strong up to T = 2 MeV.
- For ²⁰⁸Pb, the shell effects alone quench the GDR width, and the pairing fluctuations have no additional contribution to the width; the GDR width is dominated by shell effects only.
- The pairing strength parameters used were G_p,n = [19.2 ± 7.4(N−Z)]/A²; for ¹²⁰Sn, η₀ = 2.6 and χ₀ = 3.5 MeV⁻⁵/²; for ⁹⁷Tc, η₀ = 2.6 and χ₀ = 1.7 MeV⁻⁵/².

## Context

This work extends the TSFM approach of Arumugam et al. (2005) and Alhassid et al. (1990s) by including pairing fluctuations for the first time within that framework, addressing a known shortcoming where the standard TSFM overestimates the GDR width in open-shell nuclei at low temperatures. The results are consistent with the phonon damping model (PDM) of Dang (2000s), which also reproduces the low-temperature quenching, but the present approach provides a macroscopic shape-fluctuation interpretation. The study enables future tests of pairing prescriptions and parameters, and calls for more precise experimental data at several temperatures for a single nucleus, especially at T = 0.
