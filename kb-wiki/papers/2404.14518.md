# Effective Field Theory for Spontaneously Broken Symmetry

> This is a preprint of an open-access book published by Springer Cham. The book gives an up-to-date overview of the physics of spontaneously broken continuous symmetry from the point of view of effective field theory. Upon building up the necessary foundations, the text reviews the classification of Nambu-Goldstone bosons and then focuses on the construction of the effective-field-theoretic description of their dynamics. Both internal and spacetime broken symmetries are covered, including numerous illustrative examples and a few selected applications, worked out in greater detail.

## Key claim

This book provides an up-to-date, systematic overview of the physics of spontaneously broken continuous symmetry from the effective field theory perspective, covering both internal and spacetime symmetries, but it is a pedagogical review rather than a presentation of new original research results.

## Method

The text builds the necessary foundations of Lie groups, differential geometry, and effective field theory, then reviews the classification of Nambu-Goldstone bosons and the construction of effective Lagrangians for their dynamics. It covers both internal and spacetime broken symmetries, including illustrative examples and selected applications worked out in detail, and includes an appendix on differential geometry to support the more advanced parts of the main text.

## Key numbers

Review-type paper: no original numerics.

## Context

This book extends the line of work on effective field theories for spontaneously broken symmetries, complementing and systematizing prior treatments such as those by Stone (2009), Nakahara (2003), Fecko (2011), and Frankel (2012) for the differential geometry background. It enables researchers and graduate students to access a unified, self-contained treatment of Nambu-Goldstone boson classification and effective Lagrangian construction, covering both relativistic and nonrelativistic systems, and opens the door to further applications in condensed matter and high-energy physics.
