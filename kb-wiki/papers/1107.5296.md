# Bose--Einstein Condensation and Thermalization of the Quark Gluon Plasma

> In ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions, the matter formed shortly after the collision is a dense, out of equilibrium, system of gluons characterized by a semi-hard momentum scale $Q_{\rm s}$. Simple power counting arguments indicate that this system is over-occupied: the gluon occupation number is parametrically large when compared to a system in thermal equilibrium with the same energy density. On short time scales, soft elastic scatterings tend to drive the system towards the formation of a Bose--Einstein condensate that contains a large fraction of the gluons while contributing little to the energy density. The lifetime and existence of this condensate depends on whether inelastic processes, that occur on the same time scale as the elastic ones, preferably increase or decrease the number of gluons. During this overpopulated stage, and all the way to thermalization, the system behaves as a strongly interacting fluid, even though the elementary coupling constant is small. We argue that while complete isotropization may never be reached, the system may yet evolve for a long time with a fixed anisotropy between average longitudinal and transverse momenta.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the initially overpopulated gluon system formed in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions can evolve toward a Bose-Einstein condensate containing most gluons but little energy, but the existence and lifetime of this condensate depend on whether inelastic processes (which occur on the same time scale as elastic ones) increase or decrease gluon number; complete isotropization may never be reached, yet the system may evolve for a long time with a fixed anisotropy between average longitudinal and transverse momenta.

## Method

The authors use parametric estimates based on power counting and a simplified kinetic description with a collision integral in the small-angle approximation, assuming a two-scale form for the gluon distribution function characterized by a saturation scale Lambda_s and a hard cutoff Lambda. They analyze the evolution of these scales under energy conservation and a scattering time proportional to t, first in a static box and then including longitudinal expansion via a drift term in the kinetic equation, treating the anisotropy through a fixed parameter delta relating longitudinal pressure to energy density.

## Key numbers

- Initial gluon number density: n_0 ~ Q_s^3 / alpha_s, where Q_s is the saturation scale and alpha_s the strong coupling constant
- Initial energy density: epsilon_0 ~ Q_s^4 / alpha_s
- Maximum number density achievable with a chemical potential: n_max ~ T^3 ~ Q_s^3 / alpha_s^{3/4}, parametrically smaller than n_0
- Condensate density in the elastic-only scenario: n_c ~ (Q_s^3 / alpha_s) (1 - alpha_s^{1/4})
- Condensate energy contribution: n_c m ~ alpha_s^{1/4} T^4, much less than epsilon_0
- Thermalization time in static case: t_th ~ (1/Q_s) (1/alpha_s)^{7/4}
- Scattering time scaling: t_scat ~ t, independent of alpha_s
- In the expanding case with anisotropy parameter delta: thermalization time t_th/t_0 ~ (1/alpha_s)^{7/(3-delta)}
- Gluon density in expanding case: n_g ~ (Q_s^3/alpha_s) (t_0/t)^{(6+5delta)/7}
- Debye mass in expanding case: m^2 ~ Q_s^2 (t_0/t)^{(5+3delta)/7}

## Context

This work extends the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario of Baier (2000) by incorporating the possibility of Bose-Einstein condensation in the overpopulated gluon system, and addresses the role of anisotropy-driven instabilities studied by Romatschke (2005) and Arnold (2003). The paper builds on the Color Glass Condensate effective field theory of Iancu (2003) and the Glasma description of Lappi (2006), and enables future numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation to test whether a transient condensate survives alongside inelastic processes until thermalization.
