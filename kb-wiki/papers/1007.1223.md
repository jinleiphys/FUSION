# Open problems in hot QCD

> We try to give a comprehensive review of the main methods used in modern multi-loop calculations in finite-temperature field theory. While going through explicit examples, we point out similarities and differences with respect to the zero-temperature case, utilizing common techniques in a transparent way whenever possible.

# Open problems in hot QCD

## Key claim

The paper presents a systematic method for evaluating multi-loop sum-integrals in finite-temperature field theory, demonstrating that zero-temperature integration-by-parts (IBP) techniques can be partially adapted to the thermal case, but only after careful subtraction of both ultraviolet and infrared divergences; the method is illustrated on a specific 3-loop basketball-type sum-integral, yielding both a re-derivation of a known result and a new result for a different index value.

## Method

The authors decompose a 3-loop massless basketball-type sum-integral \(B_N\) (defined with a parameter \(N\) in one propagator) by first separating the large-momentum behavior of the 1-loop propagator into a zero-temperature part, a finite-temperature tadpole part, and a UV-subtracted remainder. They then perform IR subtractions by splitting off zero-mode contributions (\(\delta_{q_0}\)) and apply IBP identities (using the FIRE program) to reduce the remaining 3d sub-integrals to master integrals. The finite, convergent pieces are transformed to coordinate space via spatial Fourier transforms, yielding one-dimensional integral representations over polylogarithms and hyperbolic functions, which are evaluated numerically using Mathematica.

## Key numbers

- For \(N=2\): \(B_2^I = -\frac{T^2}{(4\pi)^4} \times 0.0269726622737(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=2\): \(B_2^{II} = +\frac{T^2}{(4\pi)^4} \times 0.0134942763002(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=2\): \(B_2^{III} = -\frac{T^2}{(4\pi)^4} \times 0.0042655281176(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=2\): \(B_2^{IV} = -\frac{T^2}{(4\pi)^4} \times 0.0004627085472(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=3\): \(B_3^I = -\frac{1}{(4\pi)^6} \times 0.0512974438185(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=3\): \(B_3^{II} = -\frac{1}{(4\pi)^6} \times 0.0163807421945(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=3\): \(B_3^{III} = -\frac{1}{(4\pi)^6} \times 0.0114452205501(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)
- For \(N=3\): \(B_3^{IV} = -\frac{1}{(4\pi)^6} \times 0.0035204424540(1) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon)\)

## Context

This work extends the case-by-case approach to multi-loop sum-integrals previously used by Andersen and Kyllingstad (2008) and by the authors of the phi4 paper (2008), who computed the 4-loop pressure of scalar theory. The paper re-derives the known result for \(B_2\) (which contributes to the 4-loop pressure of scalar theory) and obtains a new result for \(B_3\) (which contributes to the matching coefficient \(g_E^2\) in the dimensional reduction framework of hot QCD). The method enables future computer-algebraic treatment of multi-loop sum-integrals, potentially streamlining higher-order perturbative computations in finite-temperature field theories.
