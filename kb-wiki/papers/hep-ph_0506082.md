# Some Applications of Thermal Field Theory to Quark-Gluon Plasma

> The lecture provides a brief introduction of thermal field theory within imaginary time formalism, the Hard Thermal Loop perturbation theory and some of its application to the physics of the quark-gluon plasma, possibly created in relativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The lecture provides a pedagogical introduction to thermal field theory and Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) perturbation theory, but the abstract's claim that these methods apply to "the physics of the quark-gluon plasma, possibly created in relativistic heavy ion collisions" glosses over the fact that HTL perturbation theory is reliable only at high temperatures (T >> T_c) and suffers from infrared singularities, while lattice QCD is needed for non-perturbative access to temperatures near the phase transition.

## Method

The paper develops the imaginary-time (Matsubara) formalism for finite-temperature field theory, deriving Feynman rules where loop integrals become sums over discrete Matsubara frequencies. It then introduces the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) resummation technique of Braaten and Pisarski (1990) and Frenkel and Taylor (1990), which separates hard (∼ T) and soft (∼ gT) momentum scales to construct effective propagators and vertices that are gauge invariant and infrared-improved. These HTL-resummed Green functions are applied to compute hadronic spectral functions, meson correlation functions, dilepton production rates, and quark number susceptibilities in the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key numbers

- At a typical temperature of T = 250 MeV, the strong coupling constant is expected to be α_s = 0.3–0.5
- The effective thermal quark mass is given by m_q² = g²T²/6
- For pseudoscalar and vector meson spectral functions with m_q/T = 1 (corresponding to g = √6), the transition process between upper and lower quark branches has a maximum energy difference ω = 0.47 m_q at k = 1.18 m_q, and plasmino annihilation starts at ω = 1.86 m_q with a Van Hove singularity at k = 0.41 m_q
- The free meson spectral function is given by σ_M^free(ω) = (N_C/4π²) ω² tanh(ω/4T) a_M, where a_M = +1 for pseudoscalar, -1 for scalar, and -2 for vector channels
- For dilepton production rates, lattice data and HTL results show very small differences for ω/T ≥ 4, while for ω/T ≤ 3 the lattice rate drops rapidly and differs from the HTL rate

## Context

This lecture extends the pedagogical tradition of thermal field theory reviews by Kapusta (1989) and Le Bellac (1996), while specifically focusing on the HTL resummation technique developed by Braaten and Pisarski (1990) and Frenkel and Taylor (1990). The work enables systematic calculations of QGP signatures such as dilepton production rates and meson correlation functions that can be compared with lattice QCD results from Karsch (2002) and with experimental data from CERN SPS and BNL RHIC heavy-ion collision programs. The comparison with lattice data for dilepton rates and pseudoscalar correlators shows that HTL medium effects may not be sufficient to explain deviations from free behavior, motivating further next-to-leading order calculations.
