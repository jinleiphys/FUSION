# Hadron Physics: The Essence of Matter

> Dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) is a remarkably effective mass generating mechanism. It is also, amongst other things, the foundation for a successful application of chiral effective field theories, the origin of constituent-quark masses, and intimately connected with confinement in QCD. Using the Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs), we explain the origin and nature of DCSB, and elucidate some of its consequences, e.g.: a model-independent result for the pion susceptibility; the generation of a quark anomalous chromomagnetic moment, which may explain the longstanding puzzle of the a_1-rho mass splitting; its impact on the behaviour of the electromagnetic pion form factor -- thereby illustrating how data can be used to chart the momentum-dependence of the dressed-quark mass function; in the form of the pion and kaon valence-quark parton distribution functions, and the relation between them; and aspects of the neutron's electromagnetic form factors, in particular F_1^u/F_1^d and G_M^n. We argue that in solving QCD, a constructive feedback between theory and extant and forthcoming experiments will most rapidly enable constraints to be placed on the infrared behaviour of QCD's β-function, the nonperturbative quantity at the core of hadron physics; and emphasise throughout the role played by confrontation with data as a means of verifying our understanding of Nature.

## Key claim

Dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) is the dominant mass-generating mechanism for light-quark hadrons, responsible for approximately 98% of a proton's mass, but the origin of the interaction strength at infrared momenta that guarantees DCSB through the gap equation is currently unknown; the authors argue that a constructive feedback between theory and experiment will most rapidly enable constraints on the infrared behavior of QCD's β-function.

## Method

The authors employ QCD's Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs), specifically the gap equation for the dressed-quark propagator and the Bethe-Salpeter equations for meson bound states, to study DCSB and its consequences. They introduce a symmetry-preserving truncation scheme based on the axial-vector Ward-Takahashi identity, which allows construction of a Bethe-Salpeter kernel matched to any Ansatz for the dressed-quark-gluon vertex, including the Ball-Chiu vertex and an additional quark anomalous chromomagnetic moment term. The inhomogeneous vector- and axial-vector-vertex Bethe-Salpeter equations are solved at spacelike momenta, with Padé approximants used to extrapolate to timelike momenta and extract bound-state masses.

## Key numbers

- DCSB is responsible for 98% of a proton's mass (no explicit numerical precision given for this fraction)
- Pseudoscalar meson mixing angles: θ_{ηη'} = -15°, θ_{π⁰η} = 1° (from exact results proved using the systematic truncation scheme)
- Rainbow-ladder truncation yields ε_σ^{RL} = (0.3 ± 0.1) for the scalar meson binding measure, while the Ball-Chiu consistent truncation yields ε_σ^{BC} ≤ 0.1
- Rainbow-ladder truncation: a₁ mass = 759 MeV, ρ mass = 644 MeV, mass splitting = 115 MeV
- Ball-Chiu consistent truncation: a₁ mass = 1066 MeV, ρ mass = 924 MeV, mass splitting = 142 MeV
- Ball-Chiu plus anomalous chromomagnetic moment: a₁ mass = 1230 MeV, ρ mass = 745 MeV, mass splitting = 485 MeV (experimental values: a₁ = 1230 MeV, ρ = 775 MeV, splitting = 455 MeV)

## Context

This review extends the DSE-based approach to hadron physics developed in prior work by Roberts (1994, 2000, 2007), Alkofer (2000), Maris (2003), and Fischer (2006), among others. The paper addresses the longstanding failure of symmetry-preserving calculations to reproduce the a₁-ρ mass splitting, which previous studies by Watson (2004), Maris (2006), and Fischer (2009) produced at only about one-quarter of the experimental value. The new symmetry-preserving kernel construction introduced by Chang (2009) enables the first reliable Poincaré invariant calculation of meson spectra above 1 GeV, and the inclusion of a quark anomalous chromomagnetic moment generated by DCSB resolves the a₁-ρ puzzle. The work enables future charting of QCD's β-function at infrared momenta through comparison with experimental data on hadron form factors and spectra from facilities such as Jefferson Lab.
