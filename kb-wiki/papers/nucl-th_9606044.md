# Regularization, Renormalization and Range: The Nucleon-Nucleon Interaction from Effective Field Theory

> Regularization and renormalization is discussed in the context of low-energy effective field theory treatments of two or more heavy particles (such as nucleons). It is desirable to regulate the contact interactions from the outset by treating them as having a finite range. The low energy physical observables should be insensitive to this range provided that the range is of a similar or greater scale than that of the interaction. Alternative schemes, such as dimensional regularization, lead to paradoxical conclusions such as the impossibility of repulsive interactions for truly low energy effective theories where all of the exchange particles are integrated out. This difficulty arises because a nonrelativistic field theory with repulsive contact interactions is trivial in the sense that the $S$ matrix is unity and the renormalized coupling constant zero. Possible consequences of low energy attraction are also discussed. It is argued that in the case of large or small scattering lengths, the region of validity of effective field theory expansion is much larger if the contact interactions are given a finite range from the beginning.

## Key claim

Effective field theory treatments of nucleon-nucleon interactions that treat contact interactions as literally zero-range (via dimensional regularization or an infinite regulator mass) cannot describe repulsive interactions and also break down for attractive interactions with very large or very small scattering lengths; these difficulties are artifacts of the renormalization scheme and can be avoided by regulating contact interactions with a finite range from the outset.

## Method

The paper analyzes the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for s-wave scattering of heavy fermions using a contact potential, first in the standard Weinberg/KSW approach where the zero-range limit is taken implicitly via dimensional regularization or an infinite-cutoff renormalization scheme. It then introduces a finite-range regulator by replacing the delta function with a square well of radius 1/μ, solving for the phase shifts analytically, and imposing the scattering length as a renormalization condition to study the behavior as the regulator mass μ is taken to infinity.

## Key numbers

- The T matrix from the zero-range treatment is T₀(p',p) = (4π/M) / (1/a + i√(ME + iε)), where a is the scattering length and M the nucleon mass.
- For a repulsive interaction regulated with a square well of radius 1/μ, the renormalization condition can only be satisfied when μ < 1/a; taking μ → ∞ forces a → 0.
- For a surface-delta-function regulator of radius 1/μ, the renormalization condition gives C(μ) = 4πa / [M(1 - μa)], again requiring μ < 1/a for repulsive interactions (a > 0).
- In the zero-range treatment including the two-derivative contact term, the T matrix becomes T₂ = (4π/M) / [(a + ½ a² r₀ p²)⁻¹ + i√(ME + iε)], and the correction relative to T₀ is ½ a r₀ p², so power counting breaks down when p ∼ (a r₀)⁻¹/² for large a.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory approach to nucleon-nucleon interactions pioneered by Weinberg (1990) and later developed by Kaplan, Savage and Wise (1996), who used dimensional regularization and obtained the same T matrix. The paper shows that the inability to describe repulsion is a consequence of Friedman's theorem on the triviality of repulsive delta functions in nonrelativistic quantum mechanics, as discussed by Bég and Furlong. It argues that the breakdown of Weinberg's scheme for large scattering lengths, noted by Kaplan, Savage and Wise (1996), is also an artifact of taking the regulator mass to infinity, and that choosing a finite regulator mass comparable to the inverse effective range can restore the validity of the expansion.
