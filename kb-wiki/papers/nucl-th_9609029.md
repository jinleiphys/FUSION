# Hanbury-Brown/Twiss Interferometry for Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions: Theoretical Aspects

> I discuss two-particle intensity interferometry as a method to extract from measured 1- and 2-particle momentum spectra information on the space-time geometry and dynamics of the particle emitting source. Particular attention is given to the rapid expansion and short lifetime of the sources created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Model-independent expressions for the HBT size parameters in terms of the space-time variances of the source are derived, and a new parametrization of the correlation function is suggested which allows to separate the transverse, longitudinal and temporal extension of the source and to measure its transverse and longitudinal expansion velocity. The effects of resonance decays are also discussed.

## Key claim
Two-particle intensity interferometry (HBT) can extract the space-time geometry and dynamics of the particle-emitting source created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but the simple picture of measuring the full source size fails because the sources have short lifetimes, inhomogeneous temperature profiles, and strong collective expansion. Instead, the HBT radius parameters measure only "space-time regions of homogeneity" within which the momentum distribution varies sufficiently little for quantum statistical correlations to appear.

## Method
The paper derives model-independent expressions for the HBT correlation function in terms of the Wigner transform of the source density matrix (the "emission function" S(x,K)), showing that the two-particle correlation function can be written as C(q,K) = 1 + |∫ d⁴x e^{iq·x} S(x,K)|² / (∫ d⁴x S(x,K+q/2) ∫ d⁴x S(x,K-q/2)). The author then shows that for small relative momentum q, the off-shell behavior of the emission function can be approximated by an on-shell classical phase-space distribution, and that for exponential single-particle spectra the denominator simplifies to the square of the single-particle spectrum at average momentum K. A new parametrization of the correlation function is suggested to separate transverse, longitudinal, and temporal source extensions and to measure expansion velocities.

## Key numbers
- The Compton wavelength of pions at rest is 1.4 fm, which is "comfortably smaller than any typical nuclear source size," justifying the on-shell approximation for the emission function.
- The critical relative momentum q* at which correlations set in is approximately ħ/R, where R is the source radius (or homogeneity region size).
- The critical energy density for the quark-gluon plasma phase transition is about 1 GeV/fm³, mentioned as the threshold above which one expects deconfinement.
- The typical confinement length scale (hadron radius) is about 1 fm.

## Context
This work extends the Hanbury-Brown/Twiss interferometry method from astronomy and non-relativistic nuclear physics to the relativistic heavy-ion collision regime, where sources expand rapidly and have short lifetimes. The paper builds on the formal framework of Gyulassy (1979) for chaotic source ensembles and Pratt (1984) for three-dimensional source parametrizations, and provides the theoretical foundation for extracting collective flow velocities from the momentum dependence of HBT radius parameters. The analysis enables quantitative comparison with data from the Brookhaven AGS and CERN SPS experiments, though the author notes that at the time of writing (1996) no such quantitative comparison yet existed.
