# Potential problems with interpolating fields

> A potential can have features that do not reflect the dynamics of the system it describes but rather arise from the choice of interpolating fields used to define it. This is illustrated using a toy model of scattering with two coupled channels. A Bethe-Salpeter amplitude is constructed which is a mixture of the waves in the two channels. The potential derived from this has a strong repulsive core, which arises from the admixture of the closed channel in the wave function and not from the dynamics of the model.

## Key claim

A potential extracted from a Bethe-Salpeter amplitude can exhibit a strong repulsive core that arises entirely from the admixture of a closed channel in the interpolating field used to define the amplitude, rather than from the underlying dynamics of the system.

## Method

A toy model of two-body scattering in one dimension with two coupled channels (an open "NN" channel and a closed "NN*" channel) was constructed. The NN channel had an attractive square-well potential, the NN* channel had zero potential, and the channels were coupled by a contact interaction. A Bethe-Salpeter amplitude was formed as a linear combination of the two channel wave functions with a mixing parameter p, and a potential was derived by demanding that this amplitude satisfy a Schrödinger equation.

## Key numbers

- The model parameters used for the representative results were: \(MV_0 = 1/a^2\), \(M\Delta = 6/a^2\), and \(Mg = 6/a\), with \(a=1\) for plotting.
- The repulsive core in the derived potential appears for \(x \lesssim 0.5\) (in units where \(a=1\)).
- The core arises because \(d^2\psi_1(x)/dx^2 \simeq M\Delta \psi_1(x)\) for \(x \lesssim 1/\alpha\), where \(\alpha^2 = M(\Delta - E)\).
- The zero-energy potential \(V_{BS}(x;0)\) was shown for mixing parameter values \(p = 0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2\).
- The potential \(V_{BS}(x;E)\) was shown for \(ME = 0, 1, 2\) with \(p=1\).

## Context

This work extends the critique of the HAL-QCD collaboration's method for extracting baryon-baryon potentials from lattice simulations, as previously discussed by Dosch (2007) and Bicudo (2008). The toy model demonstrates that repulsive cores similar to those seen in HAL-QCD potentials (Ishii et al., 2007) and in traditional nuclear potentials like Argonne \(v_{18}\) (Wiringa et al., 1995) could be artifacts of the choice of interpolating fields rather than genuine dynamical features. The model can be used to test whether the time-dependent version of the HAL-QCD approach (Ishii et al., 2012; Sugiura et al., 2017) reproduces correct phase shifts.
