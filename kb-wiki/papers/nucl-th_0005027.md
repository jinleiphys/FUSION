# On the effects of the final state interaction in the electro-disintegration of the deuteron at intermediate and high energies

> The role of the final state interactions (FSI) in the inclusive quasi-elastic disintegration of the deuteron is investigated treating the two-nucleon final state within the exact continuum solutions of the non-relativistic Schroedinger equation, as well as within the Glauber multiple scattering approach. It is shown that for values of the Bjorken scaling variable $x_{Bj}\simeq 1$ both approaches provide similar results, unless the case $x_{Bj}\gtrsim 1$, where they appreciably disagree. It is demonstrated that present experimental data, which are mostly limited to a region of four-momentum transfer ($Q^2 \lesssim 4 (GeV/c)^2$) where the Center-of-Mass energy of the final state is below the pion threshold production, can be satisfactorily reproduced by the approach based on the exact solution of the Schroedinger equation and not by the Glauber approach. It is also pointed out that the latter, unlike the former, does not satisfy the inelastic Coulomb sum rule, the violation being of the order of about 20%.

# On the effects of the final state interaction in the electro-disintegration of the deuteron at intermediate and high energies

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that for inclusive quasi-elastic deuteron electro-disintegration at Bjorken scaling variable $x_{Bj} \gtrsim 1$, the exact non-relativistic Schroedinger treatment of final state interactions (FSI) reproduces existing experimental data satisfactorily, whereas the Glauber multiple scattering approach fails to do so, except near $x_{Bj} \simeq 1$ where both approaches agree. The Glauber approach also violates the inelastic Coulomb sum rule by about 20%, unlike the Schroedinger approach.

## Method

The inclusive cross section $D(e,e')X$ was calculated in the quasi-elastic region ($\nu \le Q^2/2M$, $x_{Bj}>1$) using two methods to treat the two-nucleon final state: (i) the exact continuum solutions of the non-relativistic Schroedinger equation with the Reid Soft Core potential, including all partial waves with $J_f < 3$; and (ii) the Glauber high-energy approximation with an eikonal S-matrix depending on the elastic profile function, using energy-dependent parameters $\sigma_{tot}$, $\alpha$, and $b_0$ from experimental nucleon-nucleon scattering data. Results were presented both as cross sections and as $y$-scaling functions $F(|{\bf q}|,y)$.

## Key numbers

- The Glauber approach violates the inelastic Coulomb sum rule by about 20%.
- Existing experimental data are limited to four-momentum transfer $Q^2 \lesssim 4 (GeV/c)^2$, where the center-of-mass energy of the final state is below the pion production threshold.
- The condition for non-relativistic $y$-scaling requires $Q^2 > 0.5 GeV^2/c^2$ and $|{\bf q}| \gtrsim 2m$.
- The inelastic channel contribution becomes relevant at laboratory momentum $p_{lab} \simeq 1.2 GeV/c$, with the threshold for inelastic channels at $\sqrt{s} \gtrsim 2 GeV$ (or $p_{lab} \gtrsim 0.8 GeV/c$).
- At high $|{\bf q}|$, the total cross section scales to its asymptotic value $\sigma_{tot} \sim 44 mb$ ($\alpha = -0.4$, $b_0 = 0.5 fm$).
- The calculations cover the range $-500 MeV/c < y \lesssim 0$ and $3.3 GeV^2/c^2 < |{\bf q}|^2 < 4.3 GeV^2/c^2$ for the electron beam energy ${\cal E} = 9.761 GeV$ and scattering angle $\theta = 10^\circ$.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Ciofi degli Atti, Pace, and Salme (1987, 1991) on $y$-scaling in deuteron electro-disintegration, and directly compares with the Glauber approach used by Nikolaev, Speth, and Zakharov (1996). The paper resolves a longstanding controversy about FSI treatment in inclusive $A(e,e')X$ processes by showing that the Schroedinger approach is valid below the pion production threshold ($\sqrt{s} \lesssim 4 GeV^2$), while the Glauber approach fails at $x_{Bj}>1$ because the ejected nucleon direction differs significantly from the momentum transfer direction. The results enable future work on inelastic rescattering effects above the pion threshold, as proposed by Braun (reference cited as in progress).
