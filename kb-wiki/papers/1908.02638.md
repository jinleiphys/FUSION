# Spin-polarized neutron matter, the maximum mass of neutron stars, and GW170817

> We investigate how a phase transition from neutron-star matter to spin-polarized neutron matter affects the equation of state and mass-radius relation of neutron stars. While general extension schemes for the equation of state allow for high pressures inside neutron stars, we find that a phase transition to spin-polarized neutron matter excludes extreme regimes. Hence, such a transition limits the maximum mass of neutron stars to lie below 2.6$-$$2.9 \, M_{\odot}$, depending on the microscopic nuclear forces used, while significantly larger masses could be reached without these constraints. These limits are in good agreement with recent constraints extracted from the neutron-star merger GW170817 and its electromagnetic counterpart. Assuming the description in terms of spin-polarized neutron matter to be valid in the center of neutron stars, we find that stars with a large spin-polarized domain in their core are ruled out by GW170817.

## Key claim
A phase transition from unpolarized neutron-star matter to spin-polarized neutron matter (SPM) limits the maximum mass of neutron stars to lie below 2.6–2.9 solar masses, depending on the microscopic nuclear forces used, whereas without such constraints significantly larger masses could be reached. The authors find that stars with a large spin-polarized domain in their core are ruled out by the gravitational-wave event GW170817 and its electromagnetic counterpart.

## Method
The authors start from a general family of equations of state (EOS) constrained at nuclear densities by microscopic calculations using local chiral effective field theory interactions and auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) methods, extended to higher densities via a speed-of-sound parameterization. For each EOS in this band, they construct a new EOS that includes a first-order phase transition to spin-polarized neutron matter, using three different calculations for the SPM EOS: AFDMC with local chiral interactions to N²LO, many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) from Kruger (2014) based on chiral EFT interactions to N³LO, and Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations based on the phenomenological Reid93 and Nijmegen potentials. The phase transition is implemented via a Maxwell construction, and the resulting mass-radius relations are obtained by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass of neutron stars with phase transition to SPM: below 2.6–2.9 solar masses, depending on the microscopic nuclear forces used.
- For AFDMC calculations: maximum mass range 1.75–2.93 solar masses, with centroid at 2.29 solar masses.
- For MBPT calculations: maximum mass range 1.84–2.59 solar masses.
- For BHF calculations: maximum mass ≤ 2.61 solar masses (Nijmegen) or ≤ 2.44 solar masses (Reid93).
- Mass of spin-polarized domain in neutron-star core: typically ≤ 0.005 solar masses; for the stiffest possible SPM EOSs, ≤ 0.19 solar masses; after excluding EOSs ruled out by GW170817 radius constraints, ≤ 0.02 solar masses.
- Radius of a typical 1.4-solar-mass neutron star from the general EOS band: 8.4–15.2 km.
- Upper limit on maximum mass without SPM constraints: ≤ 4.0 solar masses (with chiral EFT constraints up to saturation density) or ≤ 2.9 solar masses (with constraints up to twice saturation density).

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical studies of neutron-star maximum masses, such as those by Hebeler (2013), Kruger (2013), Annala (2017), and Greif (2018), which found maximum masses ranging between 2.9 and 3.2 solar masses using general extension schemes. The results are complementary to observational constraints from GW170817 by Margalit (2017), Shibata (2017), and Rezzolla (2017), who inferred upper limits on the maximum mass of non-rotating neutron stars. The paper enables future work by showing that a phase transition to spin-polarized neutron matter provides a theoretical upper bound on neutron-star masses that is consistent with gravitational-wave observations, and that improved calculations of spin-polarized neutron matter could further tighten these limits.
