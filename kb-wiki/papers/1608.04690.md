# Stellar Helium Burning in Other Universes: A solution to the triple alpha fine-tuning problem

> Motivated by the possible existence of other universes, with different values for the fundamental constants, this paper considers stellar models in universes where $^8$Be is stable. Many previous authors have noted that stars in our universe would have difficulty producing carbon and other heavy elements in the absence of the well-known $^{12}$C resonance at 7.6 MeV. This resonance is necessary because $^8$Be is unstable in our universe, so that carbon must be produced via the triple alpha reaction to achieve the requisite abundance. Although a moderate change in the energy of the resonance (200 -- 300 keV) will indeed affect carbon production, an even smaller change in the binding energy of beryllium ($\sim100$ keV) would allow $^8$Be to be stable. A stable isotope with $A=8$ would obviate the need for the triple alpha process in general, and the $^{12}$C resonance in particular, for carbon production. This paper explores the possibility that $^8$Be can be stable in other universes. Simple nuclear considerations indicate that bound states can be realized, with binding energy $\sim0.1-1$ MeV, if the fundamental constants vary by a $\sim {\rm few}-10$ percent. In such cases, $^8$Be can be synthesized through helium burning, and $^{12}$C can be produced later through nuclear burning of beryllium. This paper focuses on stellar models that burn helium into beryllium; once the universe in question has a supply of stable beryllium, carbon production can take place during subsequent evolution in the same star or in later stellar generations. Using both a semi-analytic stellar structure model as well as a state-of-the-art stellar evolution code, we find that viable stellar configurations that produce beryllium exist over a wide range of parameter space. Finally, we demonstrate that carbon can be produced during later evolutionary stages.

## Key claim

If the fundamental constants vary by a few to ten percent, \(^8\)Be can become stable (binding energy \(\sim 0.1-1\) MeV), which removes the need for the triple-alpha process and its fine-tuned \(^{12}\)C resonance for carbon production; the paper demonstrates that viable stellar configurations burning helium into beryllium exist over a wide range of parameter space, but only about half of the allowed variations in constants lead to stable \(^8\)Be (the binding energy must become lower, not higher).

## Method

The authors used both a semi-analytic polytropic stellar structure model (Lane-Emden equation) and the state-of-the-art stellar evolution code MESA to model helium burning into stable \(^8\)Be in universes where the strong coupling constant is increased by a few percent. Nuclear binding was explored via four approaches: lattice chiral effective field theory (reviewing prior calculations), a square well potential model for two alpha particles, a Bohr-type orbit model with a Yukawa potential, and the semiempirical mass formula. Stellar models assumed big bang nucleosynthesis yields similar to our universe (dominated by H and He) and kept the fine structure constant and gravitational constant fixed at their standard values.

## Key numbers

- In our universe, \(^8\)Be is unstable with \(\Delta E_b \approx +92\) keV (energy above two separate alpha particles); stable \(^8\)Be requires \(\Delta E_b < 0\).
- Lattice chiral effective field theory indicates that changes in light quark masses of \(2-3\%\) and/or changes of \(\sim 2.5\%\) in the fine structure constant can produce a bound \(^8\)Be state.
- Square well model: to obtain a bound state with binding energy 1 MeV, the potential well depth must increase by a factor \(f \approx 1.27\); for 0.1 MeV binding, \(f \approx 1.081\).
- Bohr model with Yukawa potential: a 15% change in the strong coupling constant gives a 1 MeV bound state; a 1.5% change gives a 0.1 MeV bound state.
- Semiempirical mass formula estimate: a \(\sim 2.7\%\) increase in the strong coupling constant is sufficient to make \(^8\)Be stable with \(\sim 1\) MeV binding.
- The fundamental stellar mass scale is \(\starmass \approx 3.7 \times 10^{33}\) g \(\approx 1.85 M_\odot\).
- For proton-proton fusion in our universe, the nuclear burning parameter \(\conlum \approx 2 \times 10^4\) cm\(^5\) s\(^{-3}\) g\(^{-1}\); for deuterium fusion, \(\conlum \approx 2.3 \times 10^{21}\) cm\(^5\) s\(^{-3}\) g\(^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends prior studies of stellar structure under varying fundamental constants by Adams, Coppess, and Barnes (2016) and addresses the triple-alpha fine-tuning problem raised by Carr, Hogan, and others. Previous work by Livio (1989), Oberhummer (2000), and Schlattl (2004) showed that the \(^{12}\)C resonance at 7.644 MeV cannot shift by more than \(\sim 100\) keV without compromising carbon production via the triple-alpha process. The paper demonstrates that stable \(^8\)Be provides an alternative pathway to carbon through non-resonant two-body reactions (\(^4\)He + \(^8\)Be \(\to\) \(^{12}\)C), enabling future exploration of beryllium burning into carbon and heavier elements in later stellar generations.
