# Density constrained TDHF

> In this manuscript we provide an outline of the numerical methods used in implementing the density constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF) method and provide a few examples of its application to nuclear fusion. In this approach, dynamic microscopic calculations are carried out on a three-dimensional lattice and there are no adjustable parameters, the only input is the Skyrme effective NN interaction. After a review of the DC-TDHF theory and the numerical methods, we present results for heavy-ion potentials $V(R)$, coordinate-dependent mass parameters $M(R)$, and precompound excitation energies $E^{*}(R)$ for a variety of heavy-ion reactions. Using fusion barrier penetrabilities, we calculate total fusion cross sections $σ(E_\mathrm{c.m.})$ for reactions between both stable and neutron-rich nuclei. We also determine capture cross sections for hot fusion reactions leading to the formation of superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The Density Constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method provides a fully microscopic, parameter-free approach to calculate heavy-ion interaction potentials, mass parameters, and precompound excitation energies directly from TDHF dynamics, but the resulting fusion barriers are inherently energy-dependent because dynamical density changes (neck formation, particle exchange) develop fully only at energies near the barrier top where the colliding partners spend sufficient time together.

## Method

The TDHF time-evolution of the colliding nuclei is carried out on a three-dimensional Cartesian lattice with no symmetry restrictions, using the Skyrme energy density functional as the only input. At selected time steps during the evolution, the instantaneous TDHF density is used as a constraint in a static Hartree-Fock minimization to obtain the density-constrained energy, from which the ion-ion potential V(R) is extracted by subtracting the binding energies of the two isolated nuclei. The coordinate-dependent mass parameter M(R) is obtained from energy conservation using the collective velocity from the TDHF evolution, and fusion cross sections are computed by solving the Schrodinger equation with the incoming wave boundary condition method.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction systems studied, the DC-TDHF method yields fusion cross sections sigma(E_c.m.) that are compared to experimental data for reactions between both stable and neutron-rich nuclei, as well as capture cross sections for hot fusion reactions leading to superheavy element formation.
- The density constraint is applied every 10-20 time steps during a typical DC-TDHF run that utilizes a few thousand time steps.
- The precompound excitation energy E*(t) is defined as the difference between the conserved TDHF energy and the density-constrained collective energy: E*(t) = E_TDHF - E_coll(t).
- The ion-ion potential V(R) is asymptotically correct, exactly reproducing V_Coulomb(R_max) at large initial separations.

## Context

This work extends the original density-constraint concept introduced by Cusson, Maruhn, and collaborators in 1985 for calculating collective states from TDHF time-evolution, and builds on subsequent developments by the authors (Umar and Oberacker, 2006 onward) to extract ion-ion potentials and fusion cross sections. The DC-TDHF method provides a fully microscopic alternative to phenomenological approaches such as the coupled-channels method (Misicu and Esbensen, 2006; Hagino et al., 2012) and the Sao Paulo potential (Chamon et al., 2002), incorporating dynamical effects like neck formation, particle exchange, and deformation to all orders that are absent in frozen-density double-folding potentials. The method enables systematic calculations of fusion barriers and cross sections for reactions with neutron-rich nuclei relevant to radioactive ion beam facilities, superheavy element synthesis, and nuclear astrophysics applications such as the composition and heating of accreting neutron star crusts.
