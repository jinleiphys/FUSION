# Elliptic Anisotropy from Quantum Diffraction

> The surprising manifestation of collectivity in small collision systems, such as nucleon-nucleon and nucleon-nucleus collisions, is perhaps even more striking when discussed at higher momenta. In larger systems, high-$p_T$ elliptic anisotropy is understood as a selection bias effect due to the smaller energy loss experienced along the shorter direction that aligns with the event plane. However, in small systems the amount of energy loss appears insufficient to reproduce the sizable angular anisotropy observed experimentally. In this work, we explore a new mechanism generating preferred orientations for energetic particles without the need of energy loss. We exploit a simple model that is based on two basic although inalienable ingredients: geometry and quantum mechanics. Our findings suggest that this sum-over-paths mechanism can provide a relevant contribution to so-called flow coefficients of energetic particles traversing deconfined media of any size.

# Elliptic Anisotropy from Quantum Diffraction

## Key claim
The authors propose that elliptic anisotropy (v₂) for high-pT particles can arise from quantum diffraction at the boundary of a deconfined medium, without requiring energy loss. This mechanism is based solely on geometry (the shape of the medium boundary) and quantum mechanics (phase interference in a sum-over-paths picture), and the authors claim it can provide a relevant contribution to flow coefficients in systems of any size, including small systems where energy loss is insufficient to explain observed anisotropies.

## Method
The authors model a scalar particle propagating through a constant real potential confined to a closed spatial region, solving the Helmholtz equation with different wavenumbers inside and outside the medium. They use two complementary approaches: (1) the stationary phase approximation (SPA) in the high-momentum limit, which yields an analytical expression for the angular distribution based on Snell's law and boundary curvature at stationary points; and (2) an exact solution for an elliptical medium using Mathieu functions, which allows exploration of arbitrary particle momenta. The elliptic flow coefficient v₂ is computed via the event plane method from the angular-dependent intensity at the far-field detector.

## Key numbers
- v₂ ≈ V(2-e²)e² / [4(1-e²)k₀ + 2e⁴V] for a massless particle from the approximate analytical expression, where V is the potential strength, e is eccentricity, and k₀ is the outside wavenumber
- v₂ ∼ 1/k₀ scaling predicted by the analytical expression
- Results shown for V = 1 GeV, producing v₂ ∼ 0.1 at k₀ ∼ 10 GeV for the 30-40% centrality class
- System sizes studied: R = 0.5 fm, R = 1 fm (proton radius), and R = 2 fm
- v₂ vanishes when V = 0 (no medium) or e = 0 (circular geometry)

## Context
This work addresses the puzzle of high-pT elliptic anisotropy observed in small collision systems (pp and pA) at the LHC, where energy loss mechanisms are too small to explain the measured v₂ (as quantified by the tension between v₂ and RAA discussed by Zhang (2013) and Roux (2024)). The mechanism extends the understanding of high-pT anisotropy beyond the standard selection bias picture from energy loss differences (Gyulassy, 2000; Wang, 2000) used for large systems. The authors note that alternative explanations have been proposed, including gluon saturation correlations (Dusling, 2013), double parton scattering (Hagiwara, 2017), multiple parton scatterings (Blok, 2017), momentum conservation (Soudi, 2023), and the escape mechanism in kinetic theories (He, 2015; Kurkela, 2021), but claim no fully satisfactory explanation exists. This work enables future studies of quantum diffraction effects in jet quenching phenomenology, particularly for small systems where the phase shift from the medium potential is expected to be the leading effect.
