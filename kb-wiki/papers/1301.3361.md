# Time delays and advances in classical and quantum systems

> The paper reviews positive and negative time delays in various processes of classical and quantum physics. In the beginning, we demonstrate how a time-shifted response of a system to an external perturbation appears in classical mechanics and classical electrodynamics. Then we quantify durations of various quantum mechanical processes. The duration of the quantum tunneling is studied. An interpretation of the Hartman paradox is suggested. Time delays and advances appearing in the three-dimensional scattering problem on a central potential are considered. Then we discuss delays and advances appearing in quantum field theory and after that we focus on the issue of time delays and advancements in quantum kinetics. We discuss problems of the application of generalized kinetic equations in simulations of the system relaxation towards equilibrium and analyze the kinetic entropy flow. Possible measurements of time delays and advancements in experiments similar to the recent OPERA neutrino experiment are also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper reviews both positive and negative time delays (advancements) across classical and quantum systems, arguing that apparent superluminal tunneling times (the Hartman effect) should be reinterpreted as wave-packet "live times" measuring energy dissipation at barrier edges rather than actual particle travel times, and that the choice between different forms of the quantum kinetic equation (Kadanoff-Baym, Botermans-Malfliet, or non-local) has practical consequences for simulations of heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors systematically define and compare multiple time-delay quantities (sojourn time, dwell time, group time delay, phase time delay, Wigner time delay) in classical mechanics, classical electrodynamics, one-dimensional quantum tunneling, three-dimensional scattering, and non-equilibrium quantum field theory. They then analyze three forms of the generalized kinetic equation (KB, BM, and non-local) derived from the Schwinger-Keldysh real-time formalism, computing the kinetic entropy flow for each and examining their equivalence under gradient expansion.

## Key numbers

- For a classical damped oscillator, the decay time is \( t_{\rm dec}^{\rm (cl)} = 2/\Gamma \) and the phase time delay is \( \delta t_{\rm ph}^{\rm (cl)} = \beta / \omega_{\rm R} > 0 \), with \( \beta = \arctan(\Gamma / (2\omega_{\rm R})) \).
- For a classical 1D motion, the time shift is negative (advance) for an attractive potential \( U < 0 \) and positive (delay) for a repulsive potential \( U > 0 \).
- For a classical 3D central potential \( U = a/r^\alpha \) with \( \alpha > 0 \), there is a delay (\( \delta t_{\rm W}^{\rm (cl)} > 0 \)) for \( a(2-\alpha) > 0 \) and an advance (\( \delta t_{\rm W}^{\rm (cl)} < 0 \)) for \( a(2-\alpha) < 0 \); for \( \alpha = 2 \) there is no time shift.
- For the damped oscillator under a sudden force cessation (Example 1), as \( \Gamma \to 2E_{\rm R} \) the ratio \( \delta t_{\rm ph}^{\rm (cl)} / P \to 1/4 \), where \( P = 2\pi / \omega_{\rm R} \).
- The retarded Green's function for the damped oscillator satisfies the sum rule \( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} A \, 2\omega \, d\omega/(2\pi) = 1 \) with \( A = -2 \Im G_0 \).

## Context

This review extends the discussion of time delays from Wigner's scattering theory (Wigner, 1955) and the Hartman effect (Hartman, 1962) into classical mechanics and non-equilibrium quantum kinetics. It connects to prior work by Winful (2006) on reinterpreting tunneling times as energy-storage times, and to the Botermans-Malfliet (1990) form of kinetic equations used in heavy-ion collision simulations. The paper enables a critical assessment of which kinetic equation form (KB, BM, or non-local) should be used in simulations, particularly questioning the applicability of the BM form for violent heavy-ion collisions due to conservation-law violations and gradient-expansion limitations.
