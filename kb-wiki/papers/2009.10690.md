# BigApple force and its implications to finite nuclei and astrophysical objects

> The secondary component of the GW190814 event left us with a question, "whether it is a supermassive neutron star or lightest black-hole?". Recently, Fattoyev et al. have obtained an energy density functional (EDF) named as BigApple, which reproduces the mass of the neutron star is 2.60 $M_\odot$ which is well consistent with GW190814 data. This study explores the properties of finite nuclei, nuclear matter, and neutron stars by using the BigApple EDF along with four well-known relativistic mean-field forces, namely NL3, G3, IOPB-I, and FSUGarnet. The finite nuclei properties like binding energy per particle, skin thickness, charge radius, single-particle energy, and two-neutron separation energy are well predicted by the BigApple for a series of nuclei. The calculated nuclear matter quantities such as incompressibility, symmetry energy, and slope parameters at saturation density are consistent with the empirical or experimental values where ever available. The predicted canonical tidal deformability by the BigApple parameter set is well-matched with the GW190814 data. Also, the dimensionless moment of inertia lies in the range given by the analysis of PSR J0737-3039A.

## Key claim

The BigApple energy density functional (EDF) can reproduce a neutron star maximum mass of 2.60 solar masses consistent with the GW190814 secondary component, but its equation of state is too stiff to satisfy heavy-ion collision flow data or the GW170817 tidal deformability constraint, leading the authors to conclude the secondary component is likely the lightest black hole rather than a supermassive neutron star.

## Method

The authors employed the effective field theory motivated relativistic mean-field (E-RMF) formalism to calculate ground-state properties of finite nuclei, nuclear matter, and neutron stars using the BigApple parameter set alongside four other relativistic mean-field forces (NL3, FSUGarnet, G3, IOPB-I). They solved the mean-field equations self-consistently to obtain binding energies, charge radii, neutron-skin thickness, single-particle energies, and two-neutron separation energies for a series of spherical nuclei, then extended the calculations to nuclear matter equation of state, symmetry energy coefficients, and neutron star observables including mass-radius relation, tidal deformability, and moment of inertia.

## Key numbers

- BigApple predicts a neutron star maximum mass of 2.60 solar masses, consistent with the GW190814 secondary component mass range of 2.50–2.67 solar masses
- The neutron-skin thickness of lead-208 predicted by BigApple is 0.151 fm, which lies within the proton elastic scattering range of 0.148–0.265 fm but is inconsistent with the PREX-II result of 0.283 ± 0.071 fm
- Nuclear matter incompressibility K = 227 MeV for BigApple, within the empirical range of 220–260 MeV from isoscalar giant monopole resonance data
- Symmetry energy J = 31.32 MeV and slope L = 39.80 MeV for BigApple, both within the empirical ranges of 30.20–33.70 MeV and 35.00–70.00 MeV respectively
- Saturation density for BigApple is 0.155 fm^{-3}, within the empirical range of 0.148–0.185 fm^{-3}
- Binding energy per nucleon at saturation for BigApple is -16.34 MeV, within the empirical range of -15.00 to -17.00 MeV

## Context

This work extends the BigApple EDF developed by Fattoyev et al. (2020) to systematically test its predictions across finite nuclei, nuclear matter, and neutron star properties, comparing with established parameter sets NL3 (Lalazissis et al., 1997), FSUGarnet (Chen et al., 2015), G3 (Kumar et al., 2017), and IOPB-I (Kumar et al., 2018). The study addresses the tension between stiff equations of state needed for supermassive neutron stars (as suggested by GW190814) and softer equations of state favored by GW170817 tidal deformability constraints and NICER data; the results enable future work to calibrate whether the BigApple parameter set can simultaneously satisfy finite-nuclei observables and astrophysical constraints, while highlighting that its failure to match heavy-ion collision flow data and PREX-II skin thickness suggests the GW190814 secondary is more likely a black hole.
