# Two-proton emission systematics

> The simultaneous emission of two protons is an exotic and complex three-body process. It is very important for experimental groups investigating the nuclear stability on the proton drip line to have a simple rule predicting the two-proton decay widths with a reasonable accuracy for transitions between ground as well as excited states in terms of relevant physical variables. In spite of its complexity, we show that the two-proton emission process obeys similar rules as for binary emission processes like proton, alpha and heavy cluster decays. It turns out that the logarithm of the decay width, corrected by the centrifugal barrier, linearly depends upon the Coulomb parameter within one order of magnitude. On the other hand, the universal linear dependence with a negative slope between the logarithm of the reduced width and the fragmentation potential, valid for any kind of binary decay process, is also fulfilled for the two-proton emission with a relative good accuracy. As a consequence of pairing correlations the two protons are simultaneously emitted from a singlet paired state. We evidence that indeed one obtains a linear dependence between the logarithm of the reduced width and pairing gap within a factor of two, giving a good predictive power to this law. It turns out that the diproton and alpha-cluster formation probabilities have similar patterns versus the pairing gap, while in the one-proton case one has a quasi-constant behavior.

## Key claim

Despite being a complex three-body process, two-proton emission obeys the same universal linear systematics as binary decays (proton, alpha, heavy cluster) when the logarithm of the reduced width is plotted against the fragmentation potential, and also shows a linear dependence on the pairing gap within a factor of two, giving predictive power to this law.

## Method

The authors analyzed 13 experimental two-proton emitters from Table 1, extracting decay widths and reduced widths from measured half-lives. They corrected the decay width for the centrifugal barrier to obtain a monopole width, then tested linear fits of the logarithm of the monopole width versus the Coulomb parameter, the logarithm of the reduced width versus the fragmentation potential, and the logarithm of the reduced width versus the pairing gap (Δ = 12 A⁻¹/²). The diproton was treated as a binary particle with a touching radius R = 1.2(A_D¹/³ + 2¹/³) fm, and the penetrability was computed using semiclassical Coulomb wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The linear fit of log₁₀(monopole width) versus Coulomb parameter χ has an overall rms error σ₀ = 1.238 (about one order of magnitude); excluding cases 4 and 13 gives σ₀ = 1.010.
- The linear fit of log₁₀(reduced width) versus fragmentation potential V_frag has slope a₁ = -0.183, intercept b₁ = -1.757, and rms σ₁ = 0.742 (factor of 5); excluding cases 4 and 13 gives σ₁ = 0.396 (factor of 2.5).
- The extracted harmonic oscillator frequency from the fragmentation potential fit is ℏω₁ = 7.456 MeV, with spectroscopic factor s = 0.017.
- The linear fit of log₁₀(reduced width) versus pairing gap Δ has slope a₂ = 0.622, intercept b₂ = -4.876, and rms σ₂ = 0.702 (factor of 3); excluding cases 4 and 13 gives σ₂ = 0.333 (factor of 2).
- For the ¹⁴⁵Fe emitter (case 8), the BCS formation probability was estimated as γ²_BCS ∼ 10⁻², while the experimental reduced width at V_frag = 10.938 MeV is γ²_exp ∼ 10⁻⁴.
- The quadrupole deformation effect on the width is about 15% for β = 0.3.

## Context

This work extends the universal binary decay systematics previously established for proton emission (Delion 2006), alpha decay, and heavy cluster decays (Delion 2009, 2020) to the three-body two-proton emission process. It also builds on the diproton model proposed by Delion (2013) and the pairing BCS approach for two-proton formation probability. The results enable experimental groups to predict two-proton decay widths for ground and excited states near the proton drip line using simple linear rules, complementing more complex approaches such as the hyperspherical formalism (Grigorenko 2001, 2003, 2010), R-matrix methods (Kryger 1995, Brown 2003), and the Gamow coupled channel approach (Wang 2018).
