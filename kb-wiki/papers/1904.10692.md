# Active Learning Algorithm for Computational Physics

> In large-scale computation of physics problems, one often encounters the problem of determining a multi-dimensional function, which can be time-consuming when computing each point in this multi-dimensional space is already time-demanding. In the work, we propose that the active learning algorithm can speed up such calculations. The basic idea is to fit a multi-dimensional function by neural networks, and the key point is to make the query of labeled data economically by using a stratagem called "query by committee". We present the general protocol of this fitting scheme, as well as the procedure of how to further compute physical observables with the fitted functions. We show that this method can work well with two examples, which are quantum three-body problem in atomic physics and the anomalous Hall conductivity in condensed matter physics, respectively. In these examples, we show that one reaches an accuracy of few percent error for computing physical observables with less than $10\%$ of total data points compared with uniform sampling. With these two examples, we also visualize that by using the active learning algorithm, the required data are added mostly in the regime where the function varies most rapidly, which explains the mechanism for the efficiency of the algorithm. We expect broad applications of our method on various kind of computational physics problems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that an active learning algorithm using a "query by committee" strategy with multiple neural networks can fit multi-dimensional functions in computational physics using less than 10% of the data points required by uniform sampling, achieving few-percent accuracy on physical observables, but only when the computational cost of training the neural networks is much less than the cost of evaluating the function at each point.

## Method

The authors fit a multi-dimensional function using an ensemble of five different fully-connected neural networks with varying architectures. Starting from an initial dataset of 100 uniformly sampled points, they iteratively compute the prediction variance across the committee of networks for all points in the discretized parameter space, select the points with largest variance (10 or 50 per iteration), compute the exact function values at those points, add them to the training set, and retrain all networks. After convergence, physical observables are computed from the fitted function by averaging over the committee predictions, discarding the largest and smallest values, and applying a self-average over five successive iterations.

## Key numbers

- For the quantum three-body problem: only 300 data points out of 10,000 total (3% of total) were needed to achieve a relative error of about 1% in the atom-dimer scattering length \(a_\text{ad}\) at \(a_\text{s}\Lambda=10\).
- For the anomalous Hall conductivity problem: less than 700 data points out of 10,000 total (less than 7% of total) were needed to converge the Hall conductivity, with a relative error of about 0.8% (8 per thousand).
- For the three-body problem, the relative error around the first Efimov resonance was kept within a few thousandths (units of per mille).
- The anomalous Hall conductivity of Mn\(_3\)Ge was computed as 296 S/cm using the full uniform sampling.

## Context

This work extends the application of active learning, previously used for determining inter-atomic potentials (PotentialAL1, PotentialAL2, PotentialAL3) and optimizing quantum experiments (ExpControl1, ExpControl2), to the general task of fitting multi-dimensional functions in computational physics. The method enables efficient computation of physical observables such as atom-dimer scattering lengths in few-body problems (where Efimov physics is described by Braaten (2006)) and anomalous Hall conductivity in condensed matter systems, reducing the required number of expensive numerical evaluations by an order of magnitude compared to uniform sampling.
