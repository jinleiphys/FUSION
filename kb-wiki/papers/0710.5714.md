# Fusion of neutron rich oxygen isotopes in the crust of accreting neutron stars

> Fusion reactions in the crust of an accreting neutron star are an important source of heat, and the depth at which these reactions occur is important for determining the temperature profile of the star. Fusion reactions depend strongly on the nuclear charge $Z$. Nuclei with $Z\le 6$ can fuse at low densities in a liquid ocean. However, nuclei with Z=8 or 10 may not burn until higher densities where the crust is solid and electron capture has made the nuclei neutron rich. We calculate the $S$ factor for fusion reactions of neutron rich nuclei including $^{24}$O + $^{24}$O and $^{28}$Ne + $^{28}$Ne. We use a simple barrier penetration model. The $S$ factor could be further enhanced by dynamical effects involving the neutron rich skin. This possible enhancement in $S$ should be studied in the laboratory with neutron rich radioactive beams. We model the structure of the crust with molecular dynamics simulations. We find that the crust of accreting neutron stars may contain micro-crystals or regions of phase separation. Nevertheless, the screening factors that we determine for the enhancement of the rate of thermonuclear reactions are insensitive to these features. Finally, we calculate the rate of thermonuclear $^{24}$O + $^{24}$O fusion and find that $^{24}$O should burn at densities near $10^{11}$ g/cm$^3$. The energy released from this and similar reactions may be important for the temperature profile of the star.

## Key claim

Fusion of neutron-rich oxygen isotopes like 24O may occur at densities near 10^11 g/cm^3 in accreting neutron star crusts, which is lower than some previous estimates, but this result depends on a simple barrier penetration model that may provide only a lower limit; dynamical effects from the neutron-rich skin could further enhance the cross section.

## Method

The authors used a simple barrier penetration model with the Sao Paulo double folding potential and WKB tunneling to calculate astrophysical S factors for 24O+24O and 28Ne+28Ne fusion. They performed classical molecular dynamics simulations of a multicomponent plasma (MCP) with 3456 ions to determine crust structure and screening factors, using the composition from Gupta et al. (2007) at a density of 2.16×10^11 g/cm^3. The radial distribution functions from the simulations were used to extract screening potentials, which were then applied to compute thermonuclear reaction rates.

## Key numbers

- The S factor for 24O+24O at E=1 MeV is 1.7×10^35 MeV-barn, over eight orders of magnitude larger than for 16O+16O fusion.
- The S factor for 28Ne+28Ne at E=1.5 MeV is 1.0×10^48 MeV-barn.
- 24O is predicted to burn at densities near 10^11 g/cm^3, with a reaction rate per ion of 1.2×10^-11 s^-1 at that density.
- 28Ne is estimated to burn at densities near 10^12 g/cm^3 via pycnonuclear fusion.
- 24O fusion releases 0.52 MeV per nucleon; 28Ne fusion releases 0.64 MeV per nucleon.
- The molecular dynamics simulations were run at a density of 7.18×10^-5 fm^-3 (1×10^13 g/cm^3) with Coulomb coupling parameters Γ=261.6 and Γ=242.9.
- The screening potential h^j(r=0) for oxygen from the MD simulations was 32.5 at Γ=261.6, compared to an analytic value of 31.5.

## Context

This work extends the pycnonuclear fusion calculations of Haensel and Zdunik (2007) by considering a full multicomponent plasma instead of a one-component plasma, finding that fusion may occur at lower densities than their estimates above 10^12 g/cm^3. The results also build on the electron capture heating calculations of Gupta et al. (2007) by identifying an additional heat source from fusion reactions. The paper enables future studies of pycnonuclear reaction rates for heavier nuclei and thermal conductivity of the crust, and suggests that laboratory measurements of fusion cross sections for neutron-rich oxygen isotopes with stable targets like 16O or 12C could test the predicted enhancements from neutron skin dynamics.
