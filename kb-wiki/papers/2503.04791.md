# Development and Benchmarking of JANGOFETT: A Novel Geant4-Operated Fission Event Tracking Tool

> Experiments measuring fission observables encounter false coincidences arising from timing overlap of separate fission product decays. Simulations of both fission observables and particles in detector systems exist, but have not yet been combined to produce accurate event-by-event outputs in a time-dependent manner. Geant4 is a powerful simulation tool for nuclear physics studies, but it does not handle multiple initial particles in a single simulation instance, nor does it feature high fidelity fission sampling. JANGOFETT: A Novel Geant4-Operated Fission Event Tracking Tool has been developed to address this challenge. The tool utilizes simulated fission data from an external program in conjunction with Geant4, which has been modified to produce a single timeline of events over an entire simulated experiment. The physical accuracy of the simulated overlapping energy depositions within detectors has been verified via simulation of fission products from the spontaneous fission of 252Cf.

## Key claim

JANGOFETT enables Geant4 to simulate time-correlated fission events with overlapping detector signals by reading primary fission product pairs from an external fission code (CGMF) and applying Poisson-distributed time shifts, but the verification is limited to a low-statistics test run of approximately 24 simulated hours of \(^{252}\)Cf(sf) due to current computational constraints.

## Method

The tool parses CGMF output into a .csv file containing post-neutron-evaporation fission product properties (momentum, kinetic energy, charge, mass, neutron multiplicity, and excitation energy). Geant4 then fires each primary fission product pair as separate particles, applies a Poisson-distributed time shift to each fission event based on a user-selected fission rate, and sums energy depositions within detector volumes that fall within a 20 ns coincidence window. A Gaussian blur is applied to time and energy values based on detector resolutions, and the final hits are saved to a ROOT output file with detector number, energy deposition, and experimental time.

## Key numbers

- TKE distribution from coincident PFPs in silicon detectors peaked at 180.083 ± 0.001 MeV, compared to the literature calibration standard of 181.03 MeV for \(^{252}\)Cf(sf) (Weissenberger 1986).
- Individual KE peaks were observed at 79.544 ± 0.002 MeV and 102.229 ± 0.001 MeV, compared to literature values of 78.42 and 102.61 MeV.
- The simulated fission rate was 1000/second over approximately five 24-hour days of experimental time.
- A negative excitation energy of -0.32 MeV occurred for one nucleus pair (\(^{139}\)Sb and \(^{112}\)Ag) in 58 out of \(8.7 \times 10^{7}\) fission events.
- The default silicon detector time resolution sigma was 2 ns, with a 20 ns coincidence window for summing hits in the same detector volume.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to improve fission modeling in Geant4 (Constantin 2016; Tan 2017) by combining the covariant fission observables from external codes such as CGMF (Talou 2021), FREYA (Verbeke 2015), GEF (Schmidt 2016), and TALYS (Fujio 2023) with Geant4's particle tracking and detector response capabilities. The tool enables simulation of false coincidences from overlapping fission product decays, which are a known challenge in experiments at facilities such as DANCE (Ullmann 2004), GAMMASPHERE (Lee 1990), and \(\nu\)-ball (Lebois 2020). Future work with larger datasets on the upcoming Oregon State University supercomputer will enable higher-statistics benchmarks and the inclusion of evaporated neutrons in JANGOFETT 1.01.
