# Effects of quantum statistics near the critical point of nuclear matter

> Effects of quantum statistics for nuclear matter equation of state are analyzed in terms of the recently proposed quantum van der Waals model. The system pressure is expanded over a small parameter $δ\propto n(mT)^{-3/2}[g(1-bn)]^{-1}$, where $n$ and $T$ are, respectively, the particle number density and temperature, $m$ and $g$ the particle mass and degeneracy factor. The parameter $b$ corresponds to the van der Waals excluded volume. The corrections due to quantum statistics for the critical point values of $T_c$, $n_c$, and the critical pressure $P_c$ are found within the linear and quadratic orders over $δ$. These approximate analytical results appear to be in a good agreement with exact numerical calculations in the quantum van der Waals model for interacting Fermi particles: the symmetric nuclear matter ($g=4$) and the pure neutron matter ($g=2$). They can be also applied to the system of interacting Bose particles like the matter composed of $α$ nuclei.

## Key claim

The quantum van der Waals (QvdW) model predicts that the critical point parameters of nuclear matter (critical temperature, density, and pressure) are significantly shifted from their classical van der Waals values due to quantum statistics, with the size of the shift depending on the particle mass and degeneracy factor; the first-order analytical correction in a small expansion parameter δ already reproduces the exact numerical QvdW results with good accuracy for symmetric nuclear matter, though the agreement is somewhat worse for pure neutron matter where δ is larger.

## Method

The authors extended the classical van der Waals equation of state by replacing the ideal classical gas pressure with the pressure of an ideal quantum gas (Fermi or Bose) in the grand canonical ensemble, while retaining the attractive term −an² and the excluded-volume substitution n → n/(1−bn). They expanded the resulting QvdW pressure in powers of a small parameter δ ∝ n(mT)^{-3/2}[g(1−bn)]^{-1} to obtain analytical first- and second-order corrections for the critical point (CP) parameters T_c, n_c, and P_c, and compared these with exact numerical solutions of the full QvdW model for symmetric nuclear matter (g=4), pure neutron matter (g=2), and α-particle matter (g=1).

## Key numbers

- For symmetric nuclear matter (g=4), the classical van der Waals CP values are T_c^(0) = 29.2 MeV, n_c^(0) = 0.100 fm⁻³, P_c^(0) = 1.09 MeV·fm⁻³; the first-order QvdW correction gives T_c^(1) = 19.0 MeV, n_c^(1) = 0.065 fm⁻³, P_c^(1) = 0.48 MeV·fm⁻³; the full QvdW numerical calculation yields T_c = 19.7 MeV, n_c = 0.072 fm⁻³, P_c = 0.52 MeV·fm⁻³.
- For pure neutron matter (g=2), the first-order QvdW correction gives T_c^(1) = 8.7 MeV, n_c^(1) = 0.030 fm⁻³, P_c^(1) = 0.13 MeV·fm⁻³; the full QvdW numerical calculation yields T_c = 10.8 MeV, n_c = 0.051 fm⁻³, P_c = 0.20 MeV·fm⁻³.
- For α-particle matter (g=1, Bose statistics), the classical van der Waals CP temperature T_c^(0) = 11.2 MeV increases by about 10% to T_c^(1) = 12.3 MeV within the first-order QvdW approximation.
- The expansion parameter δ near the CP is approximately 0.2 for symmetric nuclear matter, 0.5 for neutron matter, and about 0.05 for α matter.
- The van der Waals parameters fixed from ground-state nuclear matter are a = 329.8 MeV·fm³ and b = 3.35 fm³.

## Context

This work extends the recently proposed quantum van der Waals model of Marik (2018) by providing analytical expansions for the quantum statistics corrections to the critical point, complementing the exact numerical calculations of that earlier study. The results show that quantum statistics effects are significant for nuclear matter (unlike most atomic gases where δ is very small) and become even larger for pure neutron matter, which is relevant for neutron star physics. The approach enables systematic analytical estimates of how the critical point depends on particle mass and degeneracy factor, and can be applied to systems of interacting bosons such as α-particle matter, where the Bose statistics shift the CP in the opposite direction (to higher T_c and n_c) compared to fermions.
