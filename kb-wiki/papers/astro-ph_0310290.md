# Nuclear pasta structure in hot neutron stars

> Structure of cold and hot dense matter at subnuclear densities is investigated by quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations. Obtained phase diagrams show that the density of the phase boundaries between the different nuclear structures decreases with increasing temperature due to the thermal expansion of nuclear matter region.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the density of phase boundaries between different nuclear pasta shapes decreases with increasing temperature due to thermal expansion of the nuclear matter region, but this effect is only observed at temperatures where a nuclear surface can still be identified (typically below about 3 MeV).

## Method

Quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations were performed for a system of 2048 nucleons in a cubic periodic box at proton fractions x=0.3 and 0.5, with relativistic degenerate electrons treated as a uniform background and Coulomb interactions calculated by the Ewald method. The nuclear shape was characterized using the Euler characteristic density and averaged mean curvature as functions of threshold density for isodensity surfaces, and the phase separation line was determined by disappearance of long-range correlation in the two-point correlation function.

## Key numbers

- Critical point for phase separation located at nucleon density approximately 0.25 times nuclear saturation density (rho_0) and temperature above about 6 MeV
- Nuclear surface cannot be identified typically at temperatures above about 3 MeV, even in the phase-separating region
- Simulations performed at proton fractions x=0.3 and 0.5 with 2048 nucleons

## Context

This work extends previous zero-temperature studies of nuclear pasta (Hashimoto, Seki, and Yamada 1984; Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson 1983) to finite temperatures relevant for supernova cores and crusts of young hot neutron stars, which had only been studied in limited cases (Lassaut et al. 1987; Watanabe, Iida, and Sato 2000, 2001, 2003). The results suggest that pasta phases and spongelike phases with negative Euler characteristic exist in hot neutron star crusts and supernova cores at temperatures below about 3 MeV, enabling more realistic modeling of these astrophysical environments.
