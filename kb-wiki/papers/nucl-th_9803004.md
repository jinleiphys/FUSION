# Pion Dissociation In Hot Quark Medium

> Pion dissociation in a medium of hot quark matter is studied. The decay width of pion is found to be large but finite at temperatures much higher than the so called critical temperature of chiral or deconfinement transition. Consequently, pions should coexist with quarks and gluons at such high temperatures. The result is in agreement with the lattice calculations. The implication of the above result in the study of Quark-Gluon plasma is discussed.

## Key claim

Pions can coexist with quarks and gluons at temperatures well above the chiral/deconfinement transition temperature because their decay width, while large, remains finite; the decay width goes to zero only around 160 MeV, not at the critical temperature itself.

## Method

The authors computed the decay width of a pion into a quark-antiquark pair in a hot quark medium using a chiral interaction Lagrangian derived from the Goldstone-boson nature of the pion. The calculation employed the standard expression for the decay width in the pion rest frame, including Pauli blocking factors for the final-state quarks via Fermi-Dirac distributions, and used lattice results for the temperature-dependent pion mass. Three choices for the quark mass were considered: the current quark mass (10 MeV), the constituent quark mass (300 MeV), and a thermal quark mass that depends on temperature and the QCD scale parameter Lambda.

## Key numbers

- Decay width at T = 290 MeV for constituent quark mass: 0.8 GeV.
- Decay width goes to zero at approximately T = 0.16 GeV (160 MeV).
- Three values of the QCD scale parameter Lambda used: 0.3, 0.2, and 0.1 GeV.
- Current quark mass taken as 10 MeV; constituent quark mass taken as 300 MeV.
- Pion decay constant f_pi = 93 MeV.

## Context

This work extends lattice QCD results showing that hadron-like excitations survive above the chiral transition temperature (lattice studies cited as [i] and [j]) by calculating the dynamical decay width of pions in a hot quark medium. It contradicts the simple picture of a sharp phase transition between quark-gluon plasma and hadrons, supporting instead a gradual hadronisation process. The results enable more realistic hydrodynamic or kinetic models of the space-time evolution of heavy-ion collisions, where pions and quarks coexist over a broad temperature range rather than appearing only after a sudden phase transition.
