# Particle production in field theories coupled to strong external sources. II: Generating functions

> We discuss a method for computing the generating function for the multiplicity distribution in field theories with strong time dependent external sources. At leading order, the computation of the generating function reduces to finding a pair of solutions of the classical equations of motion, with non-standard temporal boundary conditions.

## Key claim

The generating function for the multiplicity distribution in field theories with strong time-dependent external sources can be expressed at leading order in terms of two solutions of the classical equations of motion, but these solutions require non-standard temporal boundary conditions at both initial and final times, unlike the purely retarded boundary conditions sufficient for computing the average multiplicity.

## Method

The authors derive the generating function for the multiplicity distribution from the sum of vacuum-vacuum diagrams in the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism, with off-diagonal propagators weighted by a factor z. They show that the derivative of the generating function at leading order reduces to an expression involving two fields, Φ₊ and Φ₋, which satisfy the classical equation of motion but with boundary conditions imposed at both x₀ = -∞ and x₀ = +∞. The fields are determined from integral equations that mix retarded and advanced propagators with the z-dependent weighting.

## Key numbers

- The source strength is taken as 1/g, meaning the dimensionless number ∫ d⁴x g j(x) ∼ O(1) for power counting purposes
- At leading order, the logarithm of the generating function and its derivative are of order 1/g²; next-to-leading order is order g⁰
- The average multiplicity ⟨n⟩ is set to 1000 in the toy model used to illustrate the Poisson distribution in Figure 1
- In the second toy model, 25% of particles are produced individually, 25% in bunches of 10, 25% in bunches of 20, and 25% in bunches of 30

## Context

This paper extends the formalism developed by Gelis and Venugopalan (2005) for computing the average multiplicity in field theories with strong external sources, now targeting the full generating function that encodes all moments of the multiplicity distribution. The work is motivated by the Color Glass Condensate effective theory for high-energy nuclear collisions, where the McLerran-Venugopalan model separates hard static sources from soft dynamical fields. The approach enables access to the full probability distribution Pₙ rather than just the average multiplicity, which is crucial because different production mechanisms can yield the same average but vastly different distributions, as illustrated by the Poisson versus correlated bunch toy models.
