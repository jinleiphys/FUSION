# Atomic Parity Non-Conservation, Neutron Radii, and Effective Field Theories of Nuclei

> Accurately calibrated effective field theories are used to compute atomic parity non-conserving (APNC) observables. Although accurately calibrated, these effective field theories predict a large spread in the neutron skin of heavy nuclei. While the neutron skin is strongly correlated to a large number of physical observables, in this contribution we focus on its impact on new physics through APNC observables. The addition of an isoscalar-isovector coupling constant to the effective Lagrangian generates a wide range of values for the neutron skin of heavy nuclei without compromising the success of the model in reproducing well constrained nuclear observables. Earlier studies have suggested that the use of isotopic ratios of APNC observables may eliminate their sensitivity to atomic structure. This leaves nuclear structure uncertainties as the main impediment for identifying physics beyond the standard model. We establish that uncertainties in the neutron skin of heavy nuclei are at present too large to measure isotopic ratios to better than the 0.1% accuracy required to test the standard model. However, we argue that such uncertainties will be significantly reduced by the upcoming measurement of the neutron radius in 208Pb at the Jefferson Laboratory.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that uncertainties in the neutron skin of heavy nuclei are too large to measure isotopic ratios of atomic parity non-conserving (APNC) observables to better than the 0.1% accuracy required to test the standard model, but argues that these uncertainties will be significantly reduced by the upcoming measurement of the neutron radius in 208Pb at the Jefferson Laboratory.

## Method

The authors used relativistic mean field (RMF) effective field theories based on quantum hadrodynamics, employing the NL3 parameter set modified by an isoscalar-isovector coupling constant (Lambda_v) to vary the density dependence of the symmetry energy. They extended previous spherical calculations to include quadrupole deformation and BCS pairing correlations, solving the Dirac and Klein-Gordon equations in an axially deformed basis expansion for isotopic chains of cesium, barium, dysprosium, and ytterbium. The neutron skin and weak charge corrections were then computed for each isotope as a function of Lambda_v, and correlations with the neutron skin of 208Pb were established.

## Key numbers

- The charge radius of 208Pb is known with high accuracy: r_ch = 5.5010 ± 0.0009 fm, exceeding knowledge of its neutron radius by at least two orders of magnitude
- The neutron skin in 208Pb varies from 0.280 fm (Lambda_v = 0) to 0.209 fm (Lambda_v = 0.025), giving a theoretical spread of approximately 0.07 fm within the RMF model
- Non-relativistic Skyrme and Gogny forces predict neutron skins in 208Pb between 0.1 and 0.2 fm, while relativistic parametrizations predict about 0.3 fm, yielding a total spread of 0.1–0.2 fm across all models
- The expected experimental error from the PREX experiment at Jefferson Laboratory is Delta r_n(208Pb) ≈ 0.056 fm, about five times smaller than the current theoretical spread
- The spread in neutron skin for cesium isotopes varies from approximately 0.03 fm for 123Cs to 0.06 fm for 137Cs
- The relative chi-squared value between theoretical and experimental charge radii for all isotopic chains (Cs, Ba, Dy, Yb) is smaller than 0.25% for both Lambda_v = 0 and Lambda_v = 0.025 parameter sets
- A measurement of the isotopic ratio R_1 with accuracy better than 0.1% is required for a significant test of the standard model
- The cesium 133Cs APNC measurement achieved 0.35% accuracy (Wood, 1997)

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Tod1 (2004) that established correlations between the neutron radius of 208Pb and neutron radii of 138Ba, 158Dy, and 176Yb, but were limited to spherical, closed-shell nuclei. The present study includes deformation and pairing correlations, enabling analysis of entire isotopic chains relevant for APNC experiments (Cs, Ba, Dy, Yb). The results confirm that the strong linear correlation between neutron skins of these nuclei and that of 208Pb holds even in deformed systems with pairing, meaning the upcoming PREX measurement of the neutron radius of 208Pb at Jefferson Laboratory will indirectly reduce nuclear structure uncertainties for APNC observables. The paper contradicts earlier optimism that isotopic ratios could eliminate all nuclear structure uncertainties, showing that neutron skin uncertainties remain too large for 0.1% precision tests of the standard model.
