# Surface Partition of Large Fragments

> The surface partition of large fragments is derived analytically within a simple statistical model by the Laplace-Fourier transformation method. In the limit of small amplitude deformations, a suggested Hills and Dales Model reproduces the leading term of the famous Fisher result for the surface entropy with an accuracy of a few percent. The surface partition of finite fragments is discussed as well.

## Key claim

The paper derives an upper limit for the surface entropy of large fragments within a simple statistical model (Hills and Dales Model) and shows that, in the limit of vanishing amplitude deformations, the leading term of the Fisher result for the surface entropy is reproduced with an accuracy of a few percent; however, the actual critical temperature of the three-dimensional Fisher model should be 6% smaller than Fisher originally supposed.

## Method

The authors develop a Hills and Dales Model (HDM) in which cylindrical deformations (hills of positive height and dales of negative height) are placed on the surface of an A-nucleon fragment, with the constraint that the total volume is conserved by giving equal statistical weight to hills and dales of the same base. The grand canonical surface partition (GCSP) is solved analytically using a Laplace-Fourier transformation technique applied to the isochoric ensemble, which accounts for the excluded volume of deformations on the fragment surface. The singularities of the resulting partition function are analyzed to extract the leading contribution to the surface entropy in the limit of large fragments and vanishing deformation amplitude.

## Key numbers

- The leading term of the surface partition for infinite fragments gives a factor \( R_0 \approx 1.06009 \) in the exponent, leading to a surface tension \( \sigma(T) = \sigma_0 [1 - 1.06009 \, T/\sigma_0] \), implying a critical temperature \( T_c = \sigma_0 / 1.06009 \), which is 6% smaller than Fisher's original value.
- The prefactor for the leading term in the GCSP for infinite fragments is \( 0.6402 \), which is 12.5% larger than the corresponding factor for self-avoiding polygons on a two-dimensional square lattice.
- The next-largest real part among the complex poles is \( R_1 \approx 0.0582 \), about 18 times smaller than \( R_0 \), making its contribution negligible for fragments of a few nucleons.
- For \( n > 2 \), the upper limit of the contribution from complex poles is bounded by \( \exp(S_A / (2\pi^2 n^2 s_1)) / (2\pi^2 n^2) \), which is exponentially small for fragments of a few nucleons.

## Context

This work extends the Fisher droplet model (Fisher, 1964) by providing a physical derivation of the surface entropy term from a statistical model of surface deformations, rather than relying on the original combinatorial postulates. It also relates to the statistical multifragmentation model (Bondorf, 1995) and earlier work on excluded volume partitions (Bugaev, 2000; Bugaev, 2001; Goren, 1981). The results enable a more precise determination of the critical temperature in three-dimensional Fisher-type models and provide a framework for studying the statistical mechanics of volume-conserving deformations in finite or small fragments.
