# New level of precision in study of neutron beta-decay -- Theoretical basics and experimental backgrounds

> The article provides a theoretical substantiation for a significant increase in the level of accuracy in determining the neutron lifetime using an alternative concept of neutron beta decay. Neutrons are distributed among different subsets depending on the sign of the scalar product of the neutron spin vector and momentum of the emitted electron during decay. Therefore, the neutron lifetime can be determined separately for each of the subsets as the inverse frequency of its decay, which is to be averaged over the number of neutrons in this subset. The total neutron lifetime on the total set of neutrons is calculated by averaging the partial (basic) lifetimes, considering their weights. The weights of the basic lifetimes are calculated for two weighing methods, leading, respectively, to the so-called lifetime of "nonpolarized" neutrons, the weighted average lifetime of neutrons and the central lifetime, i.e. the arithmetic mean for the basic lifetimes of neutrons. The dependence between different average neutron lifetimes through the integral asymmetry parameter calculated by using known experimental data leads to simple analytical expressions. The numerical estimate of the weighted average neutron lifetime gives a value that is in good agreement with the results of well-known experiments, which proves the validity of the proposed concept of neutron beta decay. This article includes necessary conditions for a new experiment to bring the neutron lifetime determination to a new level of accuracy.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that neutron beta decay should be treated as a two-channel process based on the sign of the scalar product of the neutron spin and the emitted electron momentum, leading to distinct lifetimes for neutrons that emit electrons against their spin (L-neutrons) versus along their spin (R-neutrons). The author claims this alternative concept can resolve the long-standing discrepancy between neutron lifetime measurements that cluster around 880 s and 890-900 s, but the numerical agreement depends on which value of the electron-spin correlation coefficient A is used (A_{2010} = -0.1173 or A_{2020} = -0.11958).

## Method

The author reformulates the Jackson-Treiman-Wilde formula (1957) for neutron beta-decay probability by separating neutrons into two disjoint subsets based on the sign of (s_n · p_e), defining separate decay constants λ_nL and λ_nR for each subset. Using set theory and probability theory, the total decay constant is expressed as a weighted average of these partial constants, with weights determined by the asymmetry parameter Δ = |A| · (v_e/c). The author then applies a "scale adjustment method" using an error functional F_{μ,p}(τ) to extract lifetimes from electron counting rates without measuring neutron numbers directly, and estimates numerical values using literature data for the asymmetry coefficient A and the average electron velocity.

## Key numbers

* Average electron kinetic energy from neutron decay: T_e = 391 keV, yielding average electron velocity v_e/c = 0.824
* Central neutron lifetime τ_nCentre = 900.00 ± 0.15 s (from VV2, 2021)
* For split-parameter η = 107 (using A_{2010} = -0.1173): lifetime of "nonpolarized" neutrons = 891.59 s; weighted average neutron lifetime = 883.33 s; neutron mirror lifetime = 916.67 s
* For split-parameter η = 103 (using A_{2020} = -0.11958): lifetime of "nonpolarized" neutrons = 891.26 s; weighted average neutron lifetime = 882.69 s; neutron mirror lifetime = 917.31 s
* Basic lifetimes for η = 107: τ_nL = 812.99 s; τ_nR = 987.01 s
* Basic lifetimes for η = 103: τ_nL = 811.32 s; τ_nR = 988.68 s
* The weighted average neutron lifetime (883.33 s for η=107) is compared to NIST result τ_n = 887.7 ± 1.2 (stat) ± 1.9 (syst) s (Yue, 2013) and PNPI result τ_n = 881.5 ± 0.9 s (Serebrov, 2018)

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of neutron beta-decay asymmetry first described by Jackson, Treiman, and Wilde (1957) and challenges the conventional "full width rule" for parallel decay channels from Blatt and Goldberger. The author aims to reconcile the persistent discrepancy between neutron lifetime measurements from beam experiments (typically 875-900 s) and ultracold neutron trap experiments (typically 900-937 s), as summarized in the PDG review (Zyla, 2020). The proposed two-channel concept enables a new experiment design using electron detection with polarized neutron beams to separately measure the basic lifetimes of L-neutrons and R-neutrons, potentially resolving the neutron lifetime puzzle that has persisted for over thirty years.
