# Phase Transition in Interacting Boson System at Finite Temperatures

> Thermodynamical properties of an interacting boson system at finite temperatures and zero chemical potential are studied within the framework of the Skyrme-like mean-field toy model. It is assumed that the mean field contains both attractive and repulsive terms. Self-consistency relations between the mean field and thermodynamic functions are derived. It is shown that for sufficiently strong attractive interactions this system develops a first-order phase transition via formation of Bose condensate. An interesting prediction of the model is that the condensed phase is characterized by a constant total density of particles. The thermodynamical characteristics of the system are calculated for the liquid-gas and condensed phases. The energy density exhibits a jump at the critical temperature.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a boson system at zero chemical potential can undergo a first-order phase transition via Bose condensation, but only if the attractive mean-field interaction is sufficiently strong (parameter \(\kappa > 1\)). The condensed phase is characterized by a constant total particle density, a condition that arises from the requirement that the single-particle energy at zero momentum remains non-negative.

## Method

The authors model an interacting boson (pion) system at zero chemical potential using a Skyrme-like mean field \(U(n) = -A n + B n^2\), where \(A\) and \(B\) are positive parameters. They derive thermodynamically consistent self-consistency relations between the mean field, particle density, and thermodynamic functions, then solve the self-consistent equation for particle density as a function of temperature iteratively. For super-critical attraction (\(\kappa > 1\)), they identify a density interval where the mean field makes the single-particle energy negative, leading to a first-order phase transition via Bose condensation; the condensed phase is treated by fixing the total density at \(n = n_2\) and allowing a condensate component at zero momentum.

## Key numbers

* Critical value of the attractive parameter: \(A_c = 2\sqrt{mB} \approx 395\) MeV\(\cdot\)fm\(^3\), corresponding to \(\kappa_c = 1.0\).
* Critical pion density at the onset of condensation: \(n_0 = A_c/(2B) = 1/(\sqrt{10}b) \approx 0.7\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* For \(\kappa = 1.098\), the critical temperature of the first-order phase transition: \(T_c = 116\) MeV (also given as 116.1 MeV).
* At \(T_c\), the particle density jumps from \(n_c = 0.12\) fm\(^{-3}\) (liquid-gas phase) to \(n_2 = 1.09\) fm\(^{-3}\) (condensed phase).
* The condensed phase exists from \(T_c = 116.1\) MeV up to \(T_2 = 219\) MeV, with constant density \(n = n_2\).
* The energy density exhibits an upward jump (latent heat) of about 30 MeV/fm\(^3\) at the critical temperature.

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamically consistent mean-field approach previously applied to hadron-resonance gas (Anchishkin and Vovchenko, 2014) and pionic gas (Anchishkin, 2016) to the case of Bose condensation. It builds on earlier studies of Bose condensation in interacting boson systems, including the pioneering work of Migdal and coworkers (1972-1990) on pion condensation in nuclear matter, and more recent lattice studies of pionic systems with finite isospin chemical potential (Brandt, 2016; Brandt, 2017). The model enables future investigation of Bose condensation in systems with non-zero isospin chemical potential and in quark-antiquark systems described by QCD-motivated effective models.
