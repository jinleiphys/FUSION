# On Nucleon Electromagnetic Form Factors

> A Poincare' covariant Faddeev equation, which describes baryons as composites of confined-quarks and -nonpointlike-diquarks, is solved to obtain masses and Faddeev amplitudes for the nucleon and Delta. The amplitudes are a component of a nucleon-photon vertex that automatically fulfills the Ward-Takahashi identity for on-shell nucleons. These elements are sufficient for the calculation of a quark core contribution to the nucleons' electromagnetic form factors. An accurate description of the static properties is not possible with the core alone but the error is uniformly reduced by the incorporation of meson-loop contributions. Such contributions to form factors are noticeable for Q^2 < ~2 GeV^2 but vanish with increasing momentum transfer. Hence, larger Q^2 experiments probe the quark core. The calculated behaviour of G_E^p(Q^2)/G_M^p(Q^2) on Q^2 \in [2,6] GeV^2 agrees with that inferred from polarisation transfer data. Moreover, \sqrt{Q^2} F_2(Q^2)/F_1(Q^2) is approximately constant on this domain. These outcomes result from correlations in the proton's amplitude.

## Key claim

A Poincaré covariant Faddeev equation describing baryons as composites of confined quarks and nonpointlike diquarks can yield a quark core contribution to nucleon electromagnetic form factors, but an accurate description of static properties requires the additional incorporation of meson-loop contributions; the core-only calculation is insufficient at low momentum transfer but becomes reliable for \(Q^2 \gtrsim 2\) GeV\(^2\).

## Method

The authors solved a Poincaré covariant Faddeev equation for the nucleon and \(\Delta\) baryon, using dressed-quark propagators parametrized from Dyson-Schwinger equation studies and diquark Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes with scalar and axial-vector correlations. They constructed a nucleon-photon vertex that automatically satisfies the Ward-Takahashi identity for on-shell nucleons, incorporating six diagrams including photon coupling to the bystander quark, to scalar and axial-vector diquarks, and to the quark-diquark interaction kernel. The resulting form factors were computed for the quark core alone, with meson-loop contributions discussed qualitatively.

## Key numbers

- Dressed-quark parametrization mass scale: \(\lambda = 0.566\) GeV; current quark mass \(m_{u,d} = 5.1\) MeV; Euclidean constituent-quark mass \(M_{u,d}^E = 0.33\) GeV.
- Scalar diquark mass range from models: \(m_{(ud)_{0^+}} = 0.74 - 0.82\) GeV; axial-vector diquark mass range: \(m_{(uu)_{1^+}} = m_{(ud)_{1^+}} = m_{(dd)_{1^+}} = 0.95 - 1.02\) GeV.
- Parameter Set A: nucleon mass \(M_N = 0.94\) GeV, \(\Delta\) mass \(M_\Delta = 1.23\) GeV, scalar diquark mass \(m_{0^+} = 0.63\) GeV, axial-vector diquark mass \(m_{1^+} = 0.84\) GeV.
- Parameter Set B: \(M_N = 1.18\) GeV, \(M_\Delta = 1.33\) GeV, \(m_{0^+} = 0.79\) GeV, \(m_{1^+} = 0.89\) GeV (inflated to allow for pion cloud contributions).
- Meson-loop contributions to form factors are noticeable for \(Q^2 \lesssim 2\) GeV\(^2\) and vanish with increasing momentum transfer.
- The calculated ratio \(G_E^p(Q^2)/G_M^p(Q^2)\) agrees with polarization transfer data on \(Q^2 \in [2,6]\) GeV\(^2\).
- The quantity \(\sqrt{Q^2} F_2(Q^2)/F_1(Q^2)\) is approximately constant on the domain \(Q^2 \in [2,6]\) GeV\(^2\).

## Context

This work extends the Poincaré covariant Faddeev equation approach previously developed by Oettel et al. (2000) and Hecht et al. (2002), incorporating both scalar and axial-vector diquark correlations to describe baryon structure. The calculation enables a systematic separation of quark core contributions from meson-loop (pion cloud) effects, showing that high-\(Q^2\) experiments (\(Q^2 \gtrsim 2\) GeV\(^2\)) directly probe the quark core, while lower momentum transfer data require inclusion of pion cloud contributions as studied in cloudy bag model calculations and by Ishii (2001). The results provide a framework for interpreting the discrepancy between Rosenbluth and polarization transfer measurements of the proton form factor ratio.
