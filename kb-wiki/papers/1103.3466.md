# Nuclear Scission and Quantum Localization

> We examine nuclear scission within a fully quantum-mechanical microscopic framework, focusing on the non-local aspects of the theory. Using $^{240}\textrm{Pu}$ hot fission as an example, we discuss the identification of the fragments and the calculation of their kinetic, excitation, and interaction energies, through the localization of the orbital wave functions. We show that the "disentanglement" of the fragment wave functions is essential to the quantum-mechanical definition of scission and the calculation of physical observables. Finally, we discuss the fragments' pre-scission excitation mechanisms and give a non-adiabatic description of their evolution beyond scission.

## Key claim

The paper provides a fully quantum-mechanical definition of nuclear scission based on the localization of quasiparticle wave functions onto nascent fragments, rather than relying on classical density-based neck-rupture criteria. The authors show that this approach yields total kinetic energy (TKE) predictions within 10 MeV of experiment and total excitation energy (TXE) predictions that can match experiment if approximately half of the available 25 MeV potential energy from saddle to scission is dissipated into fragment excitation.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations of hot fission in $^{240}\mathrm{Pu}$ were performed using the finite-range D1S interaction, with constraints on quadrupole moment $Q_{20}=370$ b and neck size $Q_N$. A unitary transformation of the quasiparticle operators was applied to localize individual quasiparticle states onto the left or right pre-fragment by maximizing a localization parameter $\sqrt{\ell_i^2+\ell_j^2}$ for pairs of quasiparticles with energies within 2 MeV of each other, excluding mirror states. The interaction energy between fragments was then calculated from the HFB mean and pairing fields coupling quasiparticles in complementary fragments.

## Key numbers

- The scission point was identified at neck size $Q_N = 0.35$, where the Coulomb force is approximately 30 times larger than the nuclear force between fragments.
- The two-body exchange contribution to the interaction energy at scission is only -0.7 MeV.
- The total kinetic energy (TKE) predicted from the scission configuration plus adiabatic pre-kinetic energy is 195 MeV, compared to the experimental average of 184.8 $\pm$ 1.7 MeV.
- Fragment excitation energies of 4.5 MeV (heavy fragment, average mass $\approx$ 132) and 7 MeV (light fragment, average mass $\approx$ 108) were obtained, yielding a total excitation energy (TXE) of approximately 11.5 MeV.
- The nuclear interaction energy reduction from tail reduction was approximately 20 MeV even when $Q_N < 0.5$.
- The analysis included approximately 1100 proton and neutron quasiparticle states.

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic fission calculations by Goutte (2005), Dubray (2008), and Younes (2009) that treated the entire fissioning nucleus within a single framework but still relied on nuclear density to identify scission. It contrasts with the statistical-mechanic treatment of energy partition by Schmidt (2010) and earlier classical scission criteria based on vanishing neck size (Nix, 1969) or Coulomb-nuclear force balance (Davies, 1977). The localization technique is adapted from the Localized Molecular Orbital method introduced by Lennard-Jones (1949) in molecular physics. The approach enables a fully quantum-mechanical definition of scission that can be used to predict fragment properties without adjustable parameters, and opens the possibility of imposing separate collective coordinates on individual pre-fragments beyond scission.
