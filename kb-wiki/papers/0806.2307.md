# Continuum discretization methods in a composite-particle scattering off a nucleus: the benchmark calculations

> The direct comparison of two different continuum discretization methods towards the solution of a composite particle scattering off a nucleus is presented. The first approach -- the Continumm-Discretized Coupled Channel method -- is based on the differential equation formalism, while the second one -- the Wave-Packet Continuum Discretization method -- uses the integral equation formulation for the composite-particle scattering problem. As benchmark calculations we have chosen the deuteron off \nuc{58}{Ni} target scattering (as a realistic illustrative example) at three different incident energies: high, middle and low. Clear non-vanishing effects of closed inelastic channels at small and intermediate energies are established. The elastic cross sections found in both approaches are very close to each other for all three considered energies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Wave-Packet Continuum Discretization (WPCD) method and the Continuum-Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method produce nearly identical elastic cross sections for deuteron scattering off a \(^{58}\)Ni target at three incident energies (80, 21.6, and 12 MeV), but only when the CDCC method is extended via an R-matrix formulation to include closed inelastic channels, which are usually neglected in standard CDCC calculations and become significant at lower energies.

## Method

The authors benchmarked two continuum discretization approaches for composite-particle scattering: the differential-equation-based CDCC method (solved with the Numerov method at high energy and with an R-matrix formulation at lower energies to handle closed channels) and the integral-equation-based WPCD method, which uses stationary wave packets for both the internal projectile motion and the projectile center-of-mass motion. Both methods were applied to deuteron elastic scattering off a \(^{58}\)Ni target at laboratory energies of 80, 21.6, and 12 MeV, using a Gaussian n-p interaction and Koning–Delaroche optical potentials for the nucleon-nucleus interactions, with the continuum truncated at a maximum excitation energy \(E_{\mathrm{max}}\) and discretized into bins.

## Key numbers

- At \(E_{\mathrm{lab}} = 80\) MeV, the CDCC calculation used \(N=20\) bins up to \(E_{\mathrm{max}} = 70\) MeV and \(L_{\mathrm{max}} = 45\) partial waves; the WPCD calculation used \(N=13\) n-p wave packets and \(M=500\) Coulomb packets.
- At \(E_{\mathrm{lab}} = 21.6\) MeV, the CDCC calculation required \(N_{\mathrm{open}} \simeq 20\) and \(N_{\mathrm{closed}} \simeq 15\) bins up to \(E_{\mathrm{max}} \sim 140\) MeV, with an R-matrix radius of \(R_m = 30\) fm; the WPCD calculation used \(N=15\) n-p wave packets and \(M=150\) Coulomb packets.
- At \(E_{\mathrm{lab}} = 12\) MeV, the converged CDCC and WPCD results required \(E_{\mathrm{max}} \sim 100\) MeV to account for closed-channel effects.
- The n-p interaction used a Gaussian form with \(V_0 = 66.99\) MeV and \(\beta = 0.415\) fm\(^{-2}\).
- The Gaussian basis for WPCD used \(N_G = 20\) (80 MeV), \(N_G = 30\) (21.6 MeV), with parameters \(t=2\) or \(t=3\) and \(\alpha_0 = 0.1\) fm\(^{-2}\).

## Context

This work extends the validation of continuum discretization methods for composite-particle scattering, following earlier convergence studies by P (year not given) that compared midpoint and average binning in CDCC for the same \(d+^{58}\)Ni system. It directly compares the CDCC method (as implemented in the code FRESCO by Thompson, year not given) with the WPCD integral approach developed by Rubtsova and Kukulin (K1, K2, K3, K4, years not given), and also references a recent comparison between CDCC and Faddeev approaches by Deltuva (year not given) for the same reaction. The results enable future use of the WPCD method to construct explicit non-local optical potentials and to treat closed channels on equal footing with open channels, and they highlight the necessity of including closed channels in CDCC calculations at intermediate and low energies.
