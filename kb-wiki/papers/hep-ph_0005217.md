# Muons from strangelets

## Key claim

The hypothesis is discussed that muon bundles of extremely high multiplicity (up to 150 muons) observed by the ALEPH detector in its dedicated cosmic-ray run can originate from strangelets colliding with the atmosphere, but only if the strangelet flux is a very small admixture of the primary cosmic-ray flux.

## Method

The authors used the SHOWERSIM modular software system, modified for their purpose, to simulate the interaction of primary particles at the top of the atmosphere and follow the resulting electromagnetic and hadronic cascades down to the observation level. Muons with momenta exceeding 70 GeV were registered in a sensitive area of 16 m², randomly scattered with respect to the shower axes. Primaries were sampled from a power spectrum P(E) ∝ E^{-γ} with slope index γ = 2.7 and energies above 10·A TeV, using a "normal" chemical composition of primaries (40% protons, 20% helium, 20% C-N-O mixture, 10% Ne-S mixture, 10% Fe) plus a small admixture of strangelets with mass number A = 400.

## Key numbers

- More than 3.7 × 10⁵ muon events were recorded in an effective run time of 10⁶ seconds in the ALEPH detector.
- Five events were observed with unexpectedly large muon multiplicities N_μ up to 150, which cannot be explained even assuming pure iron primaries.
- The critical mass number for strangelet stability is A_crit = 300–400, with A_crit ∼ 320 used in the simulations.
- To describe the observed rate of high multiplicity events, the relative flux of strangelets needed is F_S/F_total ≃ 2.4 × 10⁻⁵ (at the same energy per particle).
- High multiplicity events with N_μ ≃ 110 recorded on 16 m² correspond to ∼5600 muons with E_μ ≥ 70 GeV or 1000 muons with energies above 220 GeV.
- The underground location of LEP detectors (between 30 and 140 meters) gives a muon momentum cut-off between 15 and 70 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Rybczynski, Wlodarczyk, Wilk, 1999) on strangelet propagation through the atmosphere and their interpretation of direct strangelet candidates. The results are consistent with existing experimental estimations from other experiments such as Baksan Valley, where 7 events with more than 3000 muons of energies exceeding 220 GeV were observed. The study enables future CosmoLEP studies of multi-muon bundles to significantly improve understanding of the nature and importance of Strange Quark Matter candidates, and the estimated strangelet flux is also consistent with the observed flux of Centauro events.
