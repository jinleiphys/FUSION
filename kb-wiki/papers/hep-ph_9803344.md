# Screening effects in Relativistic Models of Dense Matter at Finite Temperature

> We investigate screening effects of the medium on the potential interaction between two static 'charges' for different models of dense plasmas in the one-boson exchange approximation. The potential can exhibit an oscillatory behavior, which is related to the analytic structure of the corresponding boson propagators in the complex $q$-plane. We have first revisited the one-pion exchange in a nuclear medium. In addition to Friedel oscillations, which are associated to branch cuts in the $q$-plane, there appears another oscillatory component, which arises from a pole on the pion propagator. This pole is located appart from the axes, giving rise to an oscillating Yukawa-like potential. Therefore, we call this phenomenon 'Yukawa oscillations'. This phenomenon does not appear in the Debye component of the QED screened potential, even if the coupling constant is artificially increased. We have also studied a model of QCD quark-gluon plasma. In this case, the one-gluon propagator also shows this kind of poles. At high densities and/or temperatures, where one expects perturbative QCD to be valid, the pole shifts towards large momenta.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in dense plasmas described by relativistic quantum field theories, the in-medium two-body potential can exhibit "Yukawa oscillations" (an oscillatory, exponentially damped component) arising from a pole of the boson propagator in the complex momentum plane, in addition to the well-known Friedel oscillations; however, this phenomenon is not universal, as it appears in the one-pion exchange model of nuclear matter and in a QCD quark-gluon plasma, but does not appear in QED even if the coupling constant is artificially increased.

## Method

The authors compute the static two-body interaction potential in the one-boson exchange approximation, using the linear response formalism where the potential is given by an integral over the dressed boson propagator in the static limit. For the nuclear matter case, they use the relativistic Hartree approximation for the ground state and a one-loop approximation for the pion polarization, including both matter and renormalized vacuum contributions, with monopolar form factors to remove spurious tachyonic poles. For the QCD case, they use the one-loop gluon polarization at finite temperature and density, with massless u and d quarks. The analytic structure of the propagators in the complex momentum plane is analyzed to identify poles and branch cuts, and the potential is evaluated by contour integration.

## Key numbers

- For the pion-nucleon plasma at saturation density (Fermi momentum \(p_f \simeq 0.3m\), where \(m\) is the free nucleon mass), Friedel oscillations fade away as temperature approaches \(T \sim 20\) MeV.
- Yukawa oscillations in the pion-nucleon model are present up to \(T \sim 60\) MeV at saturation density.
- In the QCD quark-gluon plasma at zero temperature, the Yukawa pole appears when the quark Fermi momentum reaches \(p_f \gtrsim 120\) MeV.
- At zero temperature and \(p_f \approx 300\) MeV in the QCD plasma, the magnitude of the pole position is \(|q_0| \approx 200\) MeV, which is the value of \(\Lambda_{QCD}\) used (200 MeV).
- For the QCD plasma, the contribution from the branch cut on the imaginary axis at large distances is approximated as \(V_{im}(r \rightarrow \infty) \simeq \frac{\pi (11N-2N_f)}{8N} \frac{(N^2-1)}{N_f^2 p_f^4 r^5}\).
- The electric screening mass in the QCD plasma is \(m_{el}^2 = \frac{g^2 N_f p_f^2}{2\pi^2}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of screening in degenerate quantum plasmas, including the non-relativistic and relativistic Friedel oscillations in QED (FR52, SI85, De80) and the analysis of the in-medium nucleon-nucleon potential in Quantum Hadrodynamics (GDP94, DPS89). It also builds on the work of Kapusta et al. (KA88) who evidenced Friedel oscillations in a quark-gluon plasma. The paper identifies a new oscillatory component ("Yukawa oscillations") in both nuclear matter and QCD plasmas, and contrasts it with the Debye screening in QED, which shows no such oscillations. The findings suggest that these oscillations might correspond to an underlying physical phenomenon, potentially manifesting in new phases of dense matter, but the authors caution that they could also be an artifact of the one-loop approximation, to be investigated in future work.
