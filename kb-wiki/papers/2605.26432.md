# Muon-Catalyzed Nuclear Fusion: Physical Mechanism, Bottleneck Breakthroughs, and an Engineering Pathway

> Muon-catalyzed nuclear fusion (\mucf) replaces atomic electrons with negative muons, compressing atomic orbitals by about two orders of magnitude and enabling deuterium--tritium (D--T) fusion under near-room-temperature conditions. This paper reviews the physical principles of \mucf{} and formulates its essential dynamics as a four-step cycle: muonic-atom formation, muon transfer, resonant \dtmu{} molecular formation, and D--T fusion with muon release and recycling. A kinetic model is used to quantify the number of catalysis cycles per muon and the corresponding energy gain. We focus on the central limitation of catalytic efficiency, namely the alpha-sticking effect, and discuss possible breakthrough routes including nuclear-spin and muon dual polarization, in-flight muon-catalyzed fusion, and heavy-ion-driven magneto-inertial fusion. Within the idealized assumptions of the present model, a four-dimensional synergistic scheme combining dual polarization, high-density confinement, electric-field-assisted muon recovery, and resonant enhancement may increase the number of catalysis cycles per muon from the present experimental record of about 150 to more than 500, potentially enabling an energy gain \(Q>2\). On this basis, we propose a conceptual fusion--fission fuel-breeding hybrid reactor, denoted as \mucf-FBR, which exploits the 14.1-MeV neutron yield of \mucf{} to breed \({}^{239}\mathrm{Pu}\) from a \({}^{238}\mathrm{U}\) blanket in a decoupled fusion--fission operating mode. This concept may offer advantages in engineering robustness, radiation-damage tolerance, and natural-uranium utilization.

# Muon-Catalyzed Nuclear Fusion: Physical Mechanism, Bottleneck Breakthroughs, and an Engineering Pathway

## Key claim

Under idealized assumptions of a four-dimensional synergistic kinetic model combining dual polarization, high-density confinement, electric-field-assisted muon recovery, and resonant enhancement, the number of catalysis cycles per muon may increase from the present experimental record of about 150 to more than 500, potentially enabling an energy gain Q > 2. The authors explicitly state that these schemes should be regarded as model-based projections rather than experimentally demonstrated performance benchmarks, and that experimental validation of the proposed reduction in alpha-sticking probability through dual polarization and assisted muon recovery remains a central challenge.

## Method

The paper formulates muon-catalyzed fusion dynamics as a four-step cycle: muonic-atom formation, muon transfer, resonant dtmu molecular formation, and D-T fusion with muon release and recycling. A kinetic model is constructed that relates the number of catalysis cycles per muon X_mu to the muon decay rate lambda_mu, the catalytic-cycle rate lambda_c, and the alpha-sticking probability omega_s via the equation X_mu = 1/(lambda_mu/lambda_c + omega_s). The energy-gain factor Q is then calculated as Q = (X_mu x 17.6 MeV)/E_mu, where E_mu is taken as 5 GeV for the average effective energy cost to obtain one negative muon in present pion beam lines such as PSI and J-PARC MUSE.

## Key numbers

- The Bohr radius of a muonic atom is approximately 1/207 of that of a hydrogenic atom with an electron, compressing the internuclear distance in a dtmu molecule to approximately 280 fm, about 1/264 of the internuclear distance of an ordinary hydrogen molecule (approximately 74000 fm)
- The muon lifetime tau_mu is approximately 2.197 microseconds, corresponding to a muon decay rate lambda_mu of approximately 4.55 x 10^5 s^-1
- The resonant dtmu molecular formation rate at 800 K can reach (100-150) x 10^8 s^-1, about 50-75 times faster than at room temperature
- The landmark Los Alamos experiment measured approximately 150 catalysis cycles per muon, corresponding to omega_s approximately 0.45%, giving Q approximately 0.53
- The break-even condition Q = 1 requires at least approximately 284 D-T fusion events per muon
- Under full polarization alone, the alpha-sticking probability omega_s decreases from approximately 0.45% to approximately 0.34%, the catalytic-cycle rate lambda_c increases by approximately 30%-50%, and X_mu increases from 148 to approximately 193
- Under multi-technology synergistic conditions, X_mu may reach 300-350 and Q may exceed 1.1
- Under engineering-limit optimized conditions, X_mu may exceed 500 with Q potentially exceeding 2
- The average effective energy cost for obtaining one negative muon is taken as 5 GeV
- The D-T fusion reaction releases 17.6 MeV of energy, producing a 3.5 MeV alpha particle and a 14.1 MeV neutron
- The muon transfer rate is of order 10^5-10^6 s^-1, with kinetic energy release of approximately 48 eV
- The dtmu molecular binding energy is approximately 0.66 eV
- The fusion time scale is approximately 10^-12 s

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the line of muon-catalyzed fusion research originating with Frank (1947) and subsequently developed by Sakharov and Zel'dovich (1948, 1954), and later by Jackson (1957), Jones (1986), Breunlich (1987), and Rafelski and Muller (1985). The work builds on the experimental record of approximately 150 catalysis cycles per muon from Los Alamos experiments and discusses breakthrough routes including in-flight muon-catalyzed fusion proposed by Iiyoshi (2019) and Liu (2022), and heavy-ion-driven magneto-inertial fusion proposed by Zhan (2024). The paper proposes a conceptual fusion-fission fuel-breeding hybrid reactor (muCF-FBR) that exploits the 14.1-MeV neutron yield to breed Pu-239 from a U-238 blanket, potentially increasing uranium-resource utilization by more than two orders of magnitude. The next experimental directions include constructing a polarized D-T target experimental station based on the Huizhou CiADS high-intensity muon source EMuS, with a post-2028 flux expected to reach at least 10^8 muons per second.
