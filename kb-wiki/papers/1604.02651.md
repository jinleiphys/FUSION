# Relativistic Kinematics

> This lecture note covers Relativistic Kinematics, which is very useful for the beginners in the field of high-energy physics. A very practical approach has been taken, which answers "why and how" of the kinematics useful for students working in the related areas.

## Key claim

This lecture note provides a pedagogical introduction to relativistic kinematics for beginners in high-energy physics, emphasizing practical "why and how" explanations rather than rigorous derivations. The author explicitly frames the content as introductory material for students working in related experimental areas, not as a research contribution with novel results.

## Method

The paper derives the Lorentz transformation and its consequences for particle kinematics, introducing four-vectors, the energy-momentum four-vector, and the invariant mass. It then develops the formalism for center-of-mass energy calculations in both collider and fixed-target configurations, including symmetric and asymmetric nucleus-nucleus collisions. The treatment covers rapidity and pseudorapidity variables, light-cone coordinates, and the transformation of kinematic quantities between reference frames using the Mandelstam invariant \(s\).

## Key numbers

* The de Broglie wavelength relation gives a required beam momentum of 1.47 GeV to probe a dimension of 1 Fermi (the proton charge radius is approximately 0.840 fm).
* In natural units (\(\hbar = c = 1\)): 1 fm = 5.07 GeV\(^{-1}\), 1 fm = 3.33 \(\times\) 10\(^{-23}\) sec, and 197 MeV = 1 fm\(^{-1}\).
* For a fixed-target experiment, the center-of-mass energy scales as \(E_{cm} \simeq \sqrt{2m_2E_1}\), while for a collider with equal beam energies it scales as \(E_{cm} \simeq 2E\).
* At the SPS fixed-target experiment, achieving a CM energy of 17.3 AGeV requires an incident beam energy of 158 AGeV.
* For p+Pb collisions, the rapidity shift is \(\Delta y = -0.465\) (calculated as \(\frac{1}{2} \ln[82 \times 1 / (1 \times 208)]\)).
* At LHC, the maximum proton beam energy is 7 TeV and the maximum Pb beam energy is 2.75 TeV, giving \(\sqrt{s} = 8.775\) TeV; the rapidity shift between beams is \(\Delta y_{cm} = 0.47\).

## Context

This lecture note is a pedagogical resource that consolidates standard relativistic kinematics formalism used across high-energy and nuclear physics, referencing Perkins (for de Broglie resolution arguments), Hegedorn (for center-of-momentum calculations), and an asymmetric collision reference (for pA kinematics). It enables beginners to understand the kinematic basis for comparing collider versus fixed-target experiments, calculating invariant masses, and applying rapidity variables in detector design and data analysis for heavy-ion collisions at facilities such as SPS and LHC.
