# Possible Cosmological Implications of the Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

> We study the quark-hadron phase transition within an effective model of QCD, and find that in a reasonable range of the main parameters of the model, bodies with quark content between $10^{-2}$ and 10 solar masses can have been formed in the early universe. In addition, we show that a significant amount of entropy is released during the transition. This may imply the existence of a higher baryon number density than what is usually expected at temperatures above the QCD scale. The cosmological QCD transition may then provide a natural way for decreasing the high baryon asymmetry created by an Affleck-Dine like mechanism down to the value required by primordial nucleosynthesis.

# Possible Cosmological Implications of the Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

## Key claim

The quark-hadron phase transition in the early universe can produce quark bodies with masses between 10⁻² and 10 solar masses, but only if the transition involves significant supercooling and exponential expansion that dilutes the baryon asymmetry by a factor of about 10⁹, requiring an initially high baryon asymmetry (of order unity) at temperatures above the QCD scale, such as that produced by an Affleck-Dine mechanism.

## Method

The authors model the quark-hadron phase transition using an effective chiral Lagrangian with a sigma field and pions, incorporating finite-temperature one-loop corrections and, in the later analysis, finite quark chemical potential. They solve the coupled Friedmann equation and bubble nucleation dynamics (using homogeneous nucleation theory with the thin-wall approximation) to track the evolution of temperature, scale factor, and the fraction of the universe converted to hadronic phase, then compute the number of quarks trapped in surviving quark plasma lumps at the end of the transition.

## Key numbers

- For B^(1/4) = 200 MeV, m_s = 100 MeV, and λ = 18, the number of quarks trapped per nugget is N_q ≈ 10⁴⁰, corresponding to masses below 10⁻⁸ solar masses.
- For B^(1/4) = 120 MeV, m_s = 100 MeV, and λ ≈ 17.77, the critical temperature is T_c = 90.1 MeV, and the number of quarks trapped is N_q ≈ 10⁵⁷, corresponding to approximately 0.3 solar masses.
- For this intermediate parameter set, the scale factor grows by a factor R_f/R_c ≈ 7 × 10³, the temperature drops from 90.1 MeV to T_f = 17.7 MeV, and the entropy increases by a factor of 3 × 10⁹.
- With finite chemical potential (μ(t_c) = 31.44 MeV, giving n_q/n_γ(t_c) = 1), T_c = 89.96 MeV, λ ≈ 17.77, the final quark content is N_q ≈ 2 × 10⁵⁷ (approximately 0.5 solar masses), with R_f/R_c ≈ 4 × 10³, T_f = 17.91 MeV, and final quark-to-photon ratio n_q/n_γ ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁹.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Witten (1984), Fuller (1988), and Iso (1986) which assumed the transition occurred at the critical temperature and produced only small quark objects (below 10⁻⁸ solar masses). The authors build on their own previous work (Cottingham and Vinh Mau, 1994, 1998) and that of Goyal (1998) on delayed phase transitions. The model enables a natural connection between high baryon asymmetry from Affleck-Dine baryogenesis (Affleck and Dine, 1985) and the value required by primordial nucleosynthesis, while also suggesting that relic quark objects in the 0.3–0.5 solar mass range could constitute some fraction of dark matter.
