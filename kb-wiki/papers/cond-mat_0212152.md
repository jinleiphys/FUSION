# Budd-Vannimenus theorem for superconductors

## Key claim

The Budd-Vannimenus theorem is modified to apply to the surface of superconductors in the Meissner state, linking the surface value of the electrostatic potential to the free energy in the bulk. This allows evaluation of the observed potential without explicitly solving the charge profile at the surface, and the authors show that the resulting surface potential formula reproduces the experimental result of Morris and Brown (1971) where the electrostatic potential equals the magnetic pressure and is independent of temperature.

## Method

The authors derive a modified Budd-Vannimenus theorem using a virtual compression of the crystal lattice near the surface, applying the Feynman-Hellmann theorem to relate the work done by removing lattice charge from a surface layer to the change in free energy. They then combine this result with the Ginzburg-Landau (GL) theory extended by electrostatic interaction, using variational principles to obtain the equations of motion and the condition that the electrostatic potential in the bulk equals the derivative of the free energy with respect to electron density. For a slab geometry, they use the Legendre transformation of the free energy and the integral of motion to evaluate the surface potential directly from the magnetic field values on either side of the slab.

## Key numbers

- The relative charge deviation (rho_lat + en)/rho_lat is typically of order 10^{-8} to 10^{-4}, leading to comparably small corrections in the GL equation.
- The surface potential is given by phi_0 = f_el / rho_lat, where f_el is the surface value of the GL free energy and rho_lat is the lattice charge density.
- For a slab geometry, the potential difference across the surface is rho_lat (phi_0^L - phi_0^R) = (B_L^2 - B_R^2) / (2 mu_0), matching the experimental result of Morris and Brown (1971) where phi = (1/rho_lat) * (1/(2 mu_0)) B^2.

## Context

This work extends the original Budd-Vannimenus theorem (Budd and Vannimenus, 1973) from normal metals to superconductors, and resolves a long-standing disagreement between theory and experiment. Earlier theoretical work by Rickayzen (1969) predicted a pairing contribution to the surface potential that was not observed in the high-accuracy measurements of Morris and Brown (1971). The authors show that an additional surface dipole, localized on the BCS coherence length scale, cancels the pairing contribution to the surface potential seen in capacitive measurements. The modified theorem enables evaluation of the surface potential for superconductors in the Meissner state and is expected to be applicable to the mixed state, particularly for evaluating the electric field generated by vortices penetrating the surface.
