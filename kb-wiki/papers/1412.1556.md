# Boson star at finite temperature

> By using a simple thermodynamical method we confirm the finding of Chavanis and Harko that stable Bose-Einstein condensate stars can form. However, by using a thermodynamically consistent boson equation of state, we obtain a less massive Bose-Einstein condensate star compared to the one predicted by Chavanis and Harko. We also obtain that the maximum mass of a boson star is insensitive to the change of matter temperature. However, the mass of boson star with relatively large radius depends significantly on the temperature of the boson matter.

# Boson star at finite temperature

## Key claim

Using a thermodynamically consistent boson equation of state (including a term proportional to the square of the ground-state number density in the energy density) yields a less massive Bose-Einstein condensate star than predicted by Chavanis and Harko (2012), who omitted that term. The maximum mass is insensitive to temperature changes, but the mass of a boson star with relatively large radius depends significantly on the temperature of the boson matter.

## Method

The authors construct a grand canonical partition function for a non-relativistic interacting boson system using a mean-field approximation in the Hamiltonian density, with a contact interaction characterized by the s-wave scattering length. From this partition function they derive thermodynamically consistent expressions for pressure, energy density, and number density at finite temperature. These equations of state are then used as input to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to compute the mass-radius relation of non-rotating boson stars.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of the boson star at zero temperature: approximately 0.56 solar masses, compared to approximately 0.7 solar masses predicted by Chavanis and Harko (2012), both with a minimum radius of approximately 4.2 km.
- For the example calculation with scattering length a = 1 fm and boson mass m = 2 nucleon masses, the scaling parameters are R* = 2.106 km and M* = 1.420 solar masses.
- The Schwarzschild radius for the maximum mass configuration is R^Scd = 2 G M_max less than or approximately 3.0 km, giving R^Scd / R_min less than or approximately 0.7, which fulfills the Buchdahl limit.
- Temperature variation was studied in the range from 0 to 19 MeV.

## Context

This work confirms the finding of Chavanis and Harko (2012) that stable Bose-Einstein condensate stars can form, but extends the calculation to finite temperature and uses a thermodynamically consistent equation of state that includes a term the earlier work omitted. The result that the maximum mass is insensitive to temperature variation is similar to the behavior found for isothermal neutron star matter based on Walecka or quark meson coupling models (Panda, Providencia, and Menezes, 2010), as well as for compact stars based on the Bag model (Menezes and Providencia, 2003). The work enables further investigation of boson star structure using more refined numerical approximations, and suggests that the recently observed approximately 2 solar mass pulsar (Demorest, 2010) might be connected to Bose-Einstein condensate stars, though the authors note that existing neutron star models also successfully explain such massive pulsars.
