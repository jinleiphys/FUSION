# Event simulations in a transport model for intermediate energy heavy ion collisions: Applications to multiplicity distributions

> We perform transport model calculations for central collisions of mass 120 on mass 120 at laboratory beam energy in the range 20 MeV/nucleon to 200 MeV/nucleon. A simplified yet accurate method allows calculation of fluctuations in systems much larger than what was considered feasible in a well-known and already existing model. The calculations produce clusters. The distribution of clusters is remarkably similar to that obtained in equilibrium statistical model.

# Event simulations in a transport model for intermediate energy heavy ion collisions: Applications to multiplicity distributions

## Key claim
The authors present a reformulation of the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) model that reduces computational cost by a factor of approximately 1/Ñ² (where Ñ is typically 100), enabling event-by-event simulations for systems as large as mass 120 on 120 over a beam energy range of 20–200 MeV/nucleon, while producing multiplicity distributions that are "remarkably similar" to those from the equilibrium Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM).

## Method
The authors modify the standard BUU model by restricting nucleon-nucleon collisions to pairs within the same event (e.g., event 1 uses only collisions between projectile nucleon 1 and target nucleon 1'), then following the prescription of Bauer et al. by selecting Ñ−1 test particles closest in phase space to each colliding nucleon and giving them the same momentum change. This reduces the number of collision combinations by a factor of 1/Ñ² compared to the original Bauer method, where all test particles across all events must be checked for collisions. The Vlasov propagation uses the lattice Hamiltonian method of Lenk and Pandharipande, and clusters are identified by contiguous spatial boxes with density exceeding 0.01 fm⁻³.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy range: 20 MeV/nucleon to 200 MeV/nucleon for central collisions of mass 120 on mass 120.
- Number of test particles Ñ = 100, reducing collision combinations by a factor of 1/10000 compared to the Bauer method.
- Simulation box size: 200 × 200 × 200 fm³ with 1 fm³ boxes; time step Δt = 0.3 fm/c; simulation run from t = 0 to t = 200 fm/c.
- Mean field parameters: A = −2230.0 MeV·fm³, B = 2577.85 MeV·fm⁷/², σ = 7/6, ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, c = −6.5 MeV·fm⁵/².
- Cluster identification uses minimum density d_min = 0.01 fm⁻³; values of 0.002, 0.005, 0.01, 0.015, and 0.02 fm⁻³ were tested with little sensitivity.
- For mass 40 on 40, 500 events were used at beam energies 25, 50, and 100 MeV/nucleon for validation against the Bauer method.
- For mass 120 on 120, 1000 events were used at beam energies 50, 75, 100, and 150 MeV/nucleon.
- CTM calculations used a system of A₀ = 192 particles at temperatures 6.5, 7.5, 10, and 14 MeV.
- Approximately 20% of nucleons are emitted as fast pre-equilibrium particles and are discarded when calculating excitation energy.

## Context
This work extends the fluctuating BUU model of Bauer et al. (1992) from systems limited to approximately mass 30 on 30 to much larger systems (mass 120 on 120), enabling the study of bulk effects in multifragmentation. The authors demonstrate that their multiplicity distributions closely match those from the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) of Das Gupta and Das (1995), providing microscopic support for the statistical model approach to multifragmentation and for the interpretation of a liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclear systems. The work also relates to quantum molecular dynamics approaches (Aichelin, 1991; Beauvais, 1993) and Langevin-based models (Ayik, 1995; Randrup, 1995; Chomaz, 2004; Rizzo, 2008; Napolitani, 2010), though the authors note that alternative methods for handling fermionic occupation limits have been proposed by Rizzo (2008) and Napolitani (2010) but without providing multiplicity distributions for comparison.
