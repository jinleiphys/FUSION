# The East Lansing Model: a Bayesian uncertainty quantified optical potential for rare isotopes

> The East Lansing Model is a global, uncertainty-quantified optical potential for neutron and proton projectiles, with a novel form for the neutron-proton asymmetry component, with the goal to improve extrapolations away from stability. Our Bayesian calibration relies on (n,n), (p,p) and (p,n) experimental data for angular distributions on spherical targets with mass $A\geq 40$, and beam energies in the range $E = 10-100$ MeV. When considering the stable nuclei for which data is available, our results demonstrate that the inclusion of the $(p,n)$ data alone does not significantly change the parameterization. The additional information contained in (p,n) only becomes evident by introducing a new parameterization, one that gives the flexibility to encode neutron skins in the optical potential through an asymmetry dependent term. Finally, extrapolations of ELM toward the limits of stability (namely toward the proton and neutron driplines) leads to reduced uncertainties when compared to other global optical potentials in use.

## Key claim
The East Lansing Model (ELM) improves extrapolations of optical potentials to neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei by introducing independent geometric form factors for the isoscalar and isovector components, but the inclusion of (p,n) data alone does not significantly change the parameterization unless this new geometric freedom is also introduced.

## Method
The authors performed a Bayesian calibration of a 21-parameter global optical potential using experimental differential cross sections for (n,n), (p,p), and (p,n) charge-exchange reactions to the isobaric analog state, on spherical targets with mass A ≥ 40 and beam energies 10–100 MeV. The model uses the Lane form with independent Woods-Saxon form factors for the isoscalar and isovector terms, and the calibration employed Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling with a parametric diagonal model discrepancy, using the CH parameterization as prior information.

## Key numbers
- Data set: 17 entries for (p,n) with 710 total data points, and 57 entries for elastic scattering with 5965 data points
- Energy range: 10–100 MeV beam energies
- Target mass range: A ≥ 40 for spherical targets with 0+ ground states
- Model parameters: 21 free parameters for full ELM, 18 for simplified ELM0
- ELM achieves roughly 10% reduction in uncertainty over ELM0 in the (p,n) sector
- Neutron skin predictions from ELM scale as R_skin(^A Z) = x · (N−Z)/A, where x is roughly 1.59 fm for A ∼ 48, decreasing linearly to 1.35 fm for A ∼ 208

## Context
This work extends the uncertainty-quantified global optical potentials KDUQ and CHUQ developed by Pruitt et al. (2023), which showed that the isovector strength remained poorly constrained when using only elastic scattering data. The ELM framework addresses the finding by Danielewicz (2017) that the isovector component should have an independent radial profile, and improves upon earlier efforts by Lovas (1976) to include (p,n) reactions in fitting. The model enables more reliable predictions of nucleon scattering on rare isotopes near the driplines, which is critical for astrophysical nucleosynthesis networks and fission fragment studies.
