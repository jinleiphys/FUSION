# The Present and Future of QCD

> This White Paper presents the community inputs and scientific conclusions from the Hot and Cold QCD Town Meeting that took place September 23-25, 2022 at MIT, as part of the Nuclear Science Advisory Committee (NSAC) 2023 Long Range Planning process. A total of 424 physicists registered for the meeting. The meeting highlighted progress in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) nuclear physics since the 2015 LRP (LRP15) and identified key questions and plausible paths to obtaining answers to those questions, defining priorities for our research over the coming decade. In defining the priority of outstanding physics opportunities for the future, both prospects for the short (~ 5 years) and longer term (5-10 years and beyond) are identified together with the facilities, personnel and other resources needed to maximize the discovery potential and maintain United States leadership in QCD physics worldwide. This White Paper is organized as follows: In the Executive Summary, we detail the Recommendations and Initiatives that were presented and discussed at the Town Meeting, and their supporting rationales. Section 2 highlights major progress and accomplishments of the past seven years. It is followed, in Section 3, by an overview of the physics opportunities for the immediate future, and in relation with the next QCD frontier: the EIC. Section 4 provides an overview of the physics motivations and goals associated with the EIC. Section 5 is devoted to the workforce development and support of diversity, equity and inclusion. This is followed by a dedicated section on computing in Section 6. Section 7 describes the national need for nuclear data science and the relevance to QCD research.

## Key claim

This White Paper presents the community consensus from the Hot and Cold QCD Town Meeting (September 2022) that the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) represents the next QCD frontier, while also identifying specific CEBAF upgrade paths (positron beams and energy doubling via fixed-field alternating gradient technology) as complementary short-to-medium-term priorities that address physics questions the EIC alone cannot fully resolve, such as two-photon exchange effects in proton form factors and direct access to the DVCS amplitude through lepton-charge comparisons.

## Method

The paper synthesizes inputs from 424 registered physicists at a town meeting held as part of the Nuclear Science Advisory Committee 2023 Long Range Planning process. It reviews progress since the 2015 Long Range Plan, identifies outstanding physics questions in hot and cold QCD, and formulates prioritized recommendations for facilities, workforce development, computing, and nuclear data science over the coming decade. The document is organized into sections covering past accomplishments, near-term physics opportunities, the EIC physics case, workforce diversity, computing needs, and nuclear data science relevance.

## Key numbers

- CEBAF could operate at the "luminosity frontier" up to 10^39 cm^-2 s^-1 with fixed-target programs, providing complementary capabilities in the EIC era.
- The proposed positron injector at JLab would accelerate positrons to 123 MeV before injection into CEBAF for multi-pass acceleration up to 12 GeV.
- CEBAF energy doubling via FFA technology could extend the energy reach up to 22 GeV within the existing tunnel footprint.
- The DDVCS cross section is a factor 100 lower than DVCS, making it not viable at the EIC and requiring study at JLab.
- Further recirculation beyond 22 GeV is limited by synchrotron radiation energy loss, which depends on energy to the fourth power.

## Context

This White Paper updates the community roadmap established by the 2015 Long Range Plan (LRP15) and builds on experimental and theoretical progress at JLab, RHIC, and LHC over the past seven years. It extends the physics case for the EIC as articulated in prior planning documents (Arrington 2021) and identifies specific CEBAF upgrades (positron beams and FFA-based energy doubling) that would address open questions in nucleon femtography and two-photon exchange physics. The document also connects to accelerator science developments such as the CBETA demonstration (Bartnik 2020) and the PEPPo positron source concept (PEPPo 2016), and it enables future prioritization of US investments in QCD facilities, workforce, and computing infrastructure.
