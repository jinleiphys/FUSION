# Nuclei beyond the drip line

> In a Thomas-Fermi model, calculations are presented for nuclei beyond the nuclear drip line at zero temperature. These nuclei are in equilibrium by the presence of an external gas, as may be envisaged in the astrophysical scenario. We find that there is a limiting asymmetry beyond which these nuclei can no longer be made stable.

## Key claim

The paper finds that there is a limiting asymmetry beyond which nuclei immersed in a nucleonic gas can no longer be made stable, even with the stabilizing influence of the external gas. This limit exists despite the fact that in infinite or semi-infinite nuclear matter, asymmetry could be increased arbitrarily until the two phases merge.

## Method

The authors use a Thomas-Fermi model with the Skyrme SkM* interaction to calculate the properties of finite nuclei beyond the drip lines at zero temperature. They employ a subtraction procedure where they solve self-consistently for the density of the nucleus-plus-gas phase and the gas phase alone, then isolate the nucleus by subtracting the gas density from the combined density. This method ensures mechanical and chemical equilibrium between the nuclear phase and the surrounding drip phase.

## Key numbers

- For Ca isotopes, the neutron drip-line nucleus is found to be \(^{68}\)Ca, but with a surrounding drip phase one can reach \(^{88}\)Ca as a stable system.
- For Ca isotopes, the proton drip-line nucleus is found to be \(^{35}\)Ca, but with a surrounding proton gas one can go further to \(^{25}\)Ca.
- For Pb isotopes, the neutron drip-line nucleus is \(^{277}\)Pb, and the nucleus \(^{340}\)Pb (with 63 more neutrons) is shown as a stable system with a surrounding neutron gas.
- In \(^{340}\)Pb, the neutron drip-phase density grows to more than one-tenth of the central neutron density.
- In \(^{340}\)Pb, the central proton density is decreased to almost half of its value in \(^{208}\)Pb.

## Context

This work extends the ideas of Baym, Bethe and Pethick (1971) on nuclei in neutron star matter, and of Centelles et al. (1998) on semi-infinite nuclear matter with a surrounding gas, to finite nuclei at zero temperature. The authors find that unlike the infinite matter case where asymmetry could be increased arbitrarily, finite nuclei have a limiting asymmetry beyond which no Thomas-Fermi solution exists, similar to the limiting temperatures found for hot nuclei by other authors. This work provides a guide for understanding the properties of isolated nuclei immersed in a nucleonic gas, which can be used as input for studying the conditions under which nuclei can exist in neutron star matter.
