# Sigma meson mass and width at finite density

> The sigma meson mass and width are studied at finite baryonic density in the framework of a chiral unitary approach which successfully reproduces the meson meson phase shifts and generates the f0 and sigma resonances in vacuum.

## Key claim

The sigma meson mass and width decrease as baryon density increases, reaching a mass around 250 MeV and a similar width at 1.5 times nuclear density, but the width remains relatively large even when the mass drops below the two-pion threshold due to medium decay channels such as pion–particle-hole excitations.

## Method

The authors use a chiral unitary approach that solves a Bethe-Salpeter equation for coupled ππ and K\(\bar{K}\) channels, with the potential taken from the lowest-order chiral Lagrangian. Nuclear medium effects are incorporated by modifying the pion propagators to include p-wave selfenergies from particle-hole and Delta-hole excitations, using the Lehmann representation to analytically continue the scattering amplitude into the complex energy plane and search for the sigma pole.

## Key numbers

- Vacuum sigma pole position: mass around 500 MeV, width around 400 MeV.
- At 1.5 times nuclear density (ρ₀): sigma mass around 250 MeV, width around 250 MeV.
- Effective potential parametrization: \(V_\rho = a (\rho/\rho_0) + b (\rho/\rho_0)^2\) with \(a = -358 - i\,108\) MeV and \(b = 140 + i\,23.6\) MeV.
- At normal nuclear density (ρ₀): width around 300 MeV.
- Cutoff for regularization of the loop integral: Λ = 1.03 GeV.
- Landau-Migdal parameter: \(g' = 0.7\).
- \(2ph\) pion selfenergy coefficient: \(C_0^* = (0.105 + i\,0.096) m_\pi^{-6}\).

## Context

This work extends the chiral unitary approach of Oller (1997) and Oset (2000) to study the sigma meson pole at finite density, building on earlier studies of ππ correlations in nuclear matter by Schuck (1988), Rapp (1996), and Chiang (1998). The results agree qualitatively with the linear sigma model calculations of Hatsuda (1999) and Jido (2001) regarding mass decrease, but the mechanism here is driven by p-wave pion–baryon interactions rather than a reduction of the pion decay constant. The findings enable predictions for two-pion invariant mass distributions in nuclear reactions such as A(γ,2π) and A(π,2π).
