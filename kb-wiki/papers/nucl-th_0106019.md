# Comparison of Canonical and Grand Canonical Models for selected multifragmentation data

> Calculations for a set of nuclear multifragmentation data are made using a Canonical and a Grand Canonical Model. The physics assumptions are identical but the Canonical Model has an exact number of particles, whereas, the Grand Canonical Model has a varying number of particles, hence, is less exact. Interesting differences are found.

# Comparison of Canonical and Grand Canonical Models for selected multifragmentation data

## Key claim

The canonical model, which conserves particle number exactly, produces mirror-nucleus yield ratios that, when interpreted using the simpler grand canonical formalism, yield a systematically lower apparent temperature than the true temperature used in the calculation; this discrepancy grows with the mass of the emitted composite and diminishes as the disintegrating system becomes larger.

## Method

The authors performed canonical model calculations for a system with neutron number N=104 and proton number Z=70 at a freeze-out density of one-quarter of normal density, simulating central 124Sn+124Sn collisions. They compared the resulting mirror-nucleus yield ratios and isoscaling observables to predictions from the grand canonical model, using saddle-point approximations to relate the canonical partition function to the grand canonical partition function. They also examined the Albergo temperature formula applied to canonical model yields.

## Key numbers

- The true temperature used in the canonical calculation is 5 MeV; fitting the canonical mirror-nucleus yield ratios with the grand canonical formula (Eq. 1.4) yields a best-fit temperature of 3.395 MeV.
- The correction to the grand canonical result for the mirror-nucleus ratio scales linearly with the mass number a = 2k+1 of the emitted composite, as shown in Eq. (2.12).
- The binding energy difference ΔE_B for mirror nuclei is approximately 0.235a MeV for masses between a=7 and a=15, compared to the uniformly charged sphere estimate of 0.72a^(2/3) MeV.
- The Albergo temperature formula applied to canonical primary yields gives temperatures slightly below 5 MeV for binding energy differences B greater than 10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the statistical multifragmentation models of Bondorf (1995) and Gross (1997) by comparing the canonical ensemble approach, developed by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) and Majumder and Das Gupta (1999), with the more approximate grand canonical ensemble used by Randrup and Koonin (1981) and Albergo (1985). The paper resolves discrepancies between temperatures extracted from mirror-nucleus ratios (which gave T < 2 MeV) and those from isotope yield ratios (about 5 MeV for Sn+Sn systems, as reported by Xu (2001) and Kunde (1998)), showing that the canonical model naturally produces this apparent temperature suppression. The analysis enables more reliable temperature extraction from experimental multifragmentation data and clarifies the conditions under which the grand canonical approximation is valid.
