# Chiral vortical effect for vector fields

> We consider photonic vortical effect, i.e. the difference of the flows of left- and right-handed photons along the vector of angular velocity in rotating photonic medium. Two alternative frameworks to evaluate the effect are considered, both of which have already been tried in the literature. First, the standard thermal fied theory and, alternatively, Hawking-radiation-type derivation. In our earlier attempt to compare the two approaches, we found a crucial factor of two difference. Here we revisit the problem, paying more attention to details of infrared regularizations. We find out that introduction of an infinitesimal mass of the vector field brings the two ways of evaluating the chiral vortical effect into agreement with each other. Some implications, both on the theoretical and phenomenological sides, are mentioned.

# Chiral vortical effect for vector fields

## Key claim

The chiral vortical effect for photons, previously thought to differ by a factor of two between thermal field theory and gravitational-anomaly-based derivations, can be brought into full agreement by introducing an infinitesimal photon mass as an infrared regularization. This resolution is specific to the vector field case and does not automatically settle the disagreement for higher-spin fields.

## Method

The authors compare two frameworks for evaluating the photonic chiral vortical effect: (1) direct statistical averaging of the magnetic-helicity current over rotating thermal states (a la Vilenkin), and (2) derivation from the gravitational chiral anomaly for vector fields, following the horizon-based approach of Stone (2012). They introduce a finite photon mass \(m_{\gamma}\neq 0\) as an infrared regulator in both approaches, compute the Kubo correlator between the chiral current \(K^{i}\) and the energy-momentum tensor component \(T^{0j}\) for massive photons, and compare the resulting vortical coefficient \(\sigma_V\) with the prediction from the gravitational anomaly.

## Key numbers

* For massless spin-1/2 fermions, the chiral vortical current is \(\vec{J}_N(S=1/2) = \left(\frac{\mu_R^2}{4\pi^2} + \frac{T^2}{12}\right)\vec{\Omega}\) (Eq. 1).
* For massive photons (\(m_{\gamma}\neq 0\)), the statistically averaged chiral current yields \(|\vec{K}|_{m_{\gamma}\neq 0} = 4\frac{T^2}{12}\Omega\) (Eq. statistical).
* The Kubo-type calculation for massive photons gives \(\sigma_V(S=1, m_{\gamma}\neq 0) = \frac{T^2}{3}\) (Eq. final result), in agreement with the gravitational anomaly prediction.
* For massless photons, the direct statistical evaluation gives a factor of 2/3 relative to the massive case: \(|\vec{K}| = \frac{2}{3}\frac{1}{8\pi^3}\int_0^{\infty} d\epsilon (4\pi\epsilon^2)\left(\frac{1}{e^{+\beta(\epsilon-\Omega)}-1} - \frac{1}{e^{+\beta(\epsilon+\Omega)}-1}\right)\) (Eq. spherical4).
* The general formula for any spin \(S\) from the gravitational anomaly is \(\sigma_V(S) = \frac{T^2}{12}(-1)^{2S}4(2S^3 - S)\) (Eq. anyspin).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Stone (2012), who derived the \(T^2\) term of the chiral vortical effect for spin-1/2 fermions from the gravitational chiral anomaly using a horizon-based metric. The authors previously found (Prokhorov et al., 2019) a factor-of-two discrepancy between anomaly-based and thermal-field-theory results for photons. By introducing an infinitesimal photon mass as an infrared regulator, following the regularization procedure used by Vainshtein (1972) to derive the photonic gravitational anomaly, they reconcile the two approaches. The resolution suggests that the disagreement for higher-spin fields (\(S \ge 3/2\)) may also be regularization-dependent, and that the contribution of vector mesons to the axial charge in heavy-ion collisions may be comparable to that of hyperons.
