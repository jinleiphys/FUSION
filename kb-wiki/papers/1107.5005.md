# Computing Heavy Elements

> Reliable calculations of the structure of heavy elements are crucial to address fundamental science questions such as the origin of the elements in the universe. Applications relevant for energy production, medicine, or national security also rely on theoretical predictions of basic properties of atomic nuclei. Heavy elements are best described within the nuclear density functional theory (DFT) and its various extensions. While relatively mature, DFT has never been implemented in its full power, as it relies on a very large number (~ 10^9-10^12) of expensive calculations (~ day). The advent of leadership-class computers, as well as dedicated large-scale collaborative efforts such as the SciDAC 2 UNEDF project, have dramatically changed the field. This article gives an overview of the various computational challenges related to the nuclear DFT, as well as some of the recent achievements.

## Key claim

Nuclear density functional theory (DFT) has never been implemented in its full power because it relies on a very large number (approximately 10^9 to 10^12) of expensive calculations (each taking about a day), but the advent of leadership-class computers and the SciDAC 2 UNEDF project have dramatically changed the field, enabling progress that was previously impossible.

## Method

The paper reviews the computational implementation of nuclear DFT via the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations, which are solved either by direct numerical integration in coordinate space (spherical, cylindrical, or full 3D) or by expansion in a harmonic oscillator (HO) basis. The HFB equations are solved iteratively using linear mixing or modified Broyden mixing until self-consistency is reached. For large-scale applications, a hybrid MPI/OpenMP programming model decomposes the process grid into many small MPI communicators, each handling one HFB calculation, with OpenMP threading within nodes to accelerate dense linear algebra operations.

## Key numbers

* The number of independent HFB calculations needed for a realistic problem is on the order of 10^9 to 10^12, each taking on the order of a few days on a single core for high-precision results.
* For a heavy nucleus, the size of the HO basis for a precise calculation contains typically more than 20 shells.
* In double-precision arithmetic, the size of the complex tensor v_{abcd} would be on the order of 80 TB if stored, so current implementations compute it on the fly and store the matrix Gamma_{ac} instead (approximately 50 MB storage).
* Ground-state properties of a spherical nucleus may take as little as 30 iterations to converge, while the scission configuration in 240Pu may take as much as 5,000 iterations.
* A typical estimate for the number of points for each deformation degree of freedom in fission studies is 500 x 40 x 20 x 20 = 8 x 10^6 points, and repeating with 10 points per basis parameter adds a factor of 1,000.

## Context

This paper reviews the computational challenges and recent achievements of nuclear DFT, which is the only theoretical framework applicable to all nuclei from lightest to heaviest. It extends prior work on self-consistent mean-field theory (Ben03) and reports progress enabled by the SciDAC 2 UNEDF project, including parameter optimization of energy functionals (Kor10) and systematic self-consistent surveys of fission pathways (Sta09, Pei09, She09). The work enables future high-precision nuclear simulations, but requires further advances in parallel dense linear algebra, real-time simulation steering, and scalable I/O and data management tools.
