# Anharmonic collective excitation in a solvable model

> We apply the time-dependent variational principle, the nuclear field theory, and the boson expansion method to the Lipkin model to discuss anharmonicities of collective vibrational excitations. It is shown that all of these approaches lead to the same anharmonicity to leading order in the number of particles. Comparison with the exact solution of the Lipkin model shows that these theories reproduce it quite well.

## Key claim

All three theoretical approaches, the time-dependent variational principle, the nuclear field theory, and the boson expansion method, yield identical leading-order anharmonicities for the Lipkin model, resolving apparent conflicts in the literature about the A-dependence of anharmonicity in giant resonances; the anharmonicity is inversely proportional to the number of particles when other parameters are fixed to keep the harmonic frequency the same.

## Method

The authors apply the time-dependent variational principle (TDVP), the nuclear field theory (NFT), and the Holstein-Primakoff boson expansion method to the second Lipkin model, a solvable two-level N-particle Hamiltonian. For each method, they derive the anharmonicity of the two-phonon state to leading order in 1/N and compare the results with exact numerical diagonalization of the Lipkin model Hamiltonian. The TDVP uses a time-dependent wavefunction ansatz with two variational parameters, the NFT employs diagrammatic perturbation theory with RPA phonons and particle-vibration coupling, and the boson expansion maps fermionic quasi-spin operators to boson operators preserving commutation relations.

## Key numbers

- The RPA frequency for the second Lipkin model is ω = ε √(1 − 2χ), where χ = V(N−1)/ε and V/ε = 0.01 is used as a typical example.
- For χ = 0.25, the anharmonicity Δ⁽²⁾E = (2χ ε³)/(N ω²) (1 − χ/2), which scales as 1/N.
- The anharmonicity vanishes when there is no interaction (ω = ε), as the Hamiltonian reduces to ε J_z.
- At N = 51 and χ = 0.5, the RPA frequency becomes zero, indicating a phase transition from spherical to deformed.

## Context

This work extends the earlier TDVP study of Bertsch and Feldmeier (1997), which found Δω/ω ∼ A⁻⁴/³ for giant resonances, and the NFT result of Ponomarev et al. (1996) giving Δω/ω ∼ A⁻²/³. The authors resolve this discrepancy by showing that the different A-dependences arise from different Hamiltonian assumptions (full A-nucleon space versus valence-shell space), not from methodological differences. The results also clarify a contradiction between the TDVP and the boson expansion method regarding anharmonicities of plasmon resonances in metal clusters, showing that the methods themselves agree on the solvable model and the discrepancy must originate elsewhere. The work enables reliable application of these methods to low-lying collective vibrations, where anharmonicities can be large due to small energy denominators.
