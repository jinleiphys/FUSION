# Quantum Simulations of Fundamental Physics

> Simulating the dynamics of non-equilibrium matter under extreme conditions lies beyond the capabilities of classical computation alone. Remarkable advances in quantum information science and technology are profoundly changing how we understand and explore fundamental quantum many-body systems, and have brought us to the point of simulating essential aspects of these systems using quantum computers. I discuss highlights, opportunities and the challenges that lie ahead.

# Quantum Simulations of Fundamental Physics

## Key claim
Quantum computers are now capable of simulating essential aspects of non-equilibrium quantum many-body systems and gauge theories that lie beyond classical high-performance computing, but robust predictive capabilities with quantified uncertainties remain a future goal, to be achieved as the field transitions from noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices to fault-tolerant quantum computers.

## Method
The paper reviews progress in mapping quantum field theories and many-body systems, including the Schwinger model, SU(2) and SU(3) gauge theories, neutrino flavor evolution, and nuclear models, to quantum circuits executed on superconducting, trapped-ion, and cold-atom quantum processors. Algorithm development includes state preparation via ADAPT-VQE, Trotterized time evolution, error mitigation, and the use of qudits (e.g., qu8its for quarks, qutrits for neutrinos) to reduce circuit depth, with classical simulations used to tune scalable operators and validate results.

## Key numbers
- Present-day quantum computers include superconducting-qubit systems with more than 100 qubits, trapped-ion systems with more than 50 qubits, and cold-atom systems with more than 1000 atoms.
- A single spatial lattice site in non-Abelian gauge theory requires 2 n_f n_c qubits using the Jordan-Wigner mapping.
- Quantum simulations of the Schwinger model vacuum and wavepacket evolution were performed on IBM's quantum computers (ibm_cusco and ibm_torino), utilizing more than 100 qubits.
- Simulations of neutrino flavor evolution have examined systems of less than ten neutrinos in simplified models, with recent extensions to qutrit embeddings for three flavors.
- The gap-ratio distribution of the entanglement Hamiltonian for a Z_2 lattice gauge theory on a dual-rail ring with 10 plaquettes and 6 randomly selected initial states evolved from Poissonian at early times to Gaussian Unitary Ensemble at late times.
- A simulation of SU(2) Yang-Mills viscosity on a honeycomb lattice produced a result consistent with that found in heavy-ion collisions, though without quantified uncertainties.

## Context
This review extends the foundational work of Feynman (1982), Lloyd (1996), and Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2012–2018) on quantum simulation of field theories, and reports progress since the first cloud-accessible 5-qubit superconducting quantum computers became available in 2017. The paper surveys developments in simulating the Schwinger model (Farrell et al., 2023–2024), non-Abelian gauge theories (Klco et al., 2019; Atas et al., 2021; Farrell et al., 2022), neutrino flavor evolution (Yeter-Aydeniz et al., 2021; Illa et al., 2023), and nuclear models such as the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick and Agassi models (Robin et al., 2023; Illa et al., 2023). It also discusses emerging concepts like quantum magic and Stabilizer Renyi Entropies (Leone et al., 2022; Haug et al., 2023) as independent measures of computational complexity alongside entanglement. The work enables future fault-tolerant simulations of QCD dynamics, transport properties, and nuclear reactions that are inaccessible to classical methods due to sign problems.
