# A Genetic Algorithm Analysis of N* Resonances in $p(γ,K^{+})Λ$ Reactions

> The problem of extracting information on new and known $N^{*}$ resonances by fitting isobar models to photonuclear data is addressed. A new fitting strategy, incorporating a genetic algorithm, is outlined. As an example, the method is applied to a typical tree-level analysis of published $p(γ,K^{+})Λ$ data. It is shown that, within the limitations of this tree-level analysis, a resonance in addition to the known set is required to obtain a reasonable fit. An additional $P_{11}$ resonance, with a mass of about 1.9 GeV, gives the best agreement with the published data, but additional $S_{11}$ or $D_{13}$ resonances cannot be ruled out. Our genetic algorithm method predicts that photon beam asymmetry and double polarization $p(γ,K^{+})Λ$ measurements should provide the most sensitive information with respect to missing resonances.

## Key claim

Within the limitations of a tree-level hadrodynamical model, an additional resonance beyond the known set is required to obtain a reasonable fit to published \(p(\gamma,K^{+})\Lambda\) data, but the quantum numbers of that resonance cannot be definitively ascertained; an additional \(P_{11}\) resonance with a mass of about 1.9 GeV gives the best agreement, but additional \(S_{11}\) or \(D_{13}\) resonances cannot be ruled out.

## Method

The authors employed a genetic algorithm combined with the MINUIT minimizer to fit a tree-level isobar model to published \(p(\gamma,K^{+})\Lambda\) data, including total and differential cross sections, recoil polarizations from SAPHIR, photon beam asymmetries from SPring-8, and electroproduction data from Jefferson Lab. They defined five model variants: a "Core" set of known resonances (\(S_{11}(1650)\), \(P_{11}(1710)\), \(P_{13}(1720)\)) and four variants each adding one additional resonance of mass 1895 MeV with different quantum numbers (\(S_{11}\), \(P_{11}\), \(P_{13}\), or \(D_{13}\)). For each variant, 100 independent minimization calculations were performed, and the variants were compared using a Bayesian likelihood ratio that includes an Occam factor to penalize models with more free parameters.

## Key numbers

* The Core variant (no additional resonance) achieved a \(\chi^{2}\) per data point of 5.14.
* The \(D_{13}\) variant achieved the lowest raw \(\chi^{2}\) per data point of 3.35, while the \(P_{11}\) variant achieved 3.47 and the \(P_{13}\) variant achieved 3.75.
* After applying the Occam factor, the ratio of posterior probabilities (normalized to Core = 1.000) was 12.571 for the \(P_{11}\) variant, 4.500 for the \(S_{11}\) variant, 2.786 for the \(D_{13}\) variant, and 0.035 for the \(P_{13}\) variant.
* The additional \(P_{11}\) resonance has a mass of about 1.9 GeV.
* The number of free parameters for the \(s\)-channel resonance coupling constants was 4 for Core, 5 for \(S_{11}\) and \(P_{11}\), and 6 for \(P_{13}\) and \(D_{13}\).

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of the \(p(\gamma,K^{+})\Lambda\) channel by Mart and Bennhold (2000), who introduced a \(D_{13}(1895)\) resonance, and by Saghai (2001), who argued that tuning background processes could remove the need for an extra resonance. The authors had previously shown (Janssen et al., 2003) that many different solutions with similar \(\chi^{2}\) values existed, and that polarization observables could resolve ambiguities. The present study incorporates new photon beam asymmetry data from SPring-8 (Zegers et al., 2003) and electroproduction data from Jefferson Lab (Mohring et al., 2003), which reduced parameter ambiguities. The results enable future measurements of photon beam asymmetry and double polarization observables to discriminate between the possible additional resonances.
