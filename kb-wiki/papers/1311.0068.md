# Pre-neutron-emission mass distributions for reaction $^{232}$Th(n, f) up to 60 MeV

> The pre-neutron-emission mass distributions for reaction $^{232}$Th(n, f) up to 60 MeV are systematically studied with an empirical fission potential model. The energy dependences of the peaks and valleys of the pre-neutron-emission mass distributions are described by the exponential expressions based on the newly measured data. The energy dependence of evaporation neutrons before scission, which plays a crucial role for the reasonable description of the mass distribution, is also considered. Both the double-humped and triple-humped shape of the measured pre-neutron-emission mass distributions for reaction $^{232}$Th(n, f) are reasonably well reproduced at incident energies up to 60 MeV. The mass distributions at unmeasured energies and the critical energies at which the humped pre-neutron-emission mass distributions are transformed into each other are also predicted.

## Key claim

The paper claims that an empirical fission potential model can "reasonably well reproduce" both double-humped and triple-humped pre-neutron-emission mass distributions for the reaction $^{232}$Th(n, f) at incident neutron energies up to 60 MeV, but this reproduction depends on empirically parameterized exponential expressions for the energy dependence of peaks, valleys, and the number of evaporation neutrons before scission.

## Method

The authors constructed a phenomenological fission potential using five harmonic-oscillator functions to describe the triple-humped mass distributions. They determined the potential parameters by fitting exponential expressions to experimental data for the heights of the light fragment peak P(A1), the symmetric fission point P(A0), and the light fragment valley P(B1), as well as the width between valleys, as functions of incident neutron energy. The number of evaporation neutrons before scission was assigned in discrete steps based on incident energy intervals (from zero neutrons below 6 MeV up to five neutrons between 52 and 60 MeV), which modifies the mass number of the fissile nucleus and the positions of the fragment peaks.

## Key numbers

- The width between valleys is parameterized as $\Delta B = 22.509 - 69.774 \exp(-0.114 E_n)$, where $E_n$ is the incident neutron energy in MeV.
- The height of the light fragment peak is given by $P(A_1) = 2.570 + 4.371 \exp(-0.023 E_n)$.
- The height of the symmetric fission point is given by $P(A_0) = 5.543 - 6.220 \exp(-0.020 E_n)$.
- The height of the light fragment valley is given by $P(B_1) = 2.934 - 4.139 \exp(-0.050 E_n)$.
- The critical incident energy for the transition from double-humped to triple-humped shape is predicted to be approximately 23 MeV.
- The critical incident energy for the transition from triple-humped to single-humped shape is predicted to be at or above 110 MeV.
- The evaporation neutron number is set to zero for $E_{th} \leq E_n \leq 6.0$ MeV, one for $6.0 < E_n \leq 14.0$ MeV, two for $14.0 < E_n \leq 21.0$ MeV, three for $21.0 < E_n \leq 31.0$ MeV, four for $31.0 < E_n \leq 52.0$ MeV, and five for $52.0 < E_n \leq 60.0$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous empirical fission potential model (Sun 2012), which was limited to low incident energies (below 6 MeV) and used a linear energy dependence for peaks and valleys, to intermediate energies up to 60 MeV by incorporating exponential energy dependences and multi-chance fission channels. The model is compared against several existing codes and methods, including TALYS1.4 (Koning 2008), the liquid drop model (Seeger 1976), a modified TALYS1.0 code (Simutkin 2011), Yukawa (Dobrowolski 2007), and the UKFY4.1 library (Mill 2008), with the authors claiming their results reproduce experimental data better than these alternatives. The work enables prediction of mass distributions at unmeasured energies and identifies critical transition energies between different distribution shapes, which is relevant for nuclear data needs in accelerator-driven systems and thorium-based fuel cycles.
