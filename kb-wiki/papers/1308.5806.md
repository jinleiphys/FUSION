# AFTER@LHC: a precision machine to study the interface between particle and nuclear physics

> We outline the opportunities to study with high precision the interface between nuclear and particle physics, which are offered by a next generation and multi-purpose fixed-target experiment exploiting the proton and ion LHC beams extracted by a bent crystal.

## Key claim

AFTER@LHC proposes a next-generation fixed-target experiment using bent-crystal extraction of LHC proton and ion beams to achieve extremely high luminosities, enabling precision studies of the interface between nuclear and particle physics, particularly in the kinematical region of large momentum fractions at small distances, which has been seldom explored.

## Method

The paper outlines a design where a bent crystal is positioned in the LHC beam halo to channel and deflect a small fraction of protons (or lead ions) per bunch per pass by a couple of milliradians, producing an extracted beam for fixed-target collisions. The extracted beam would then interact with various solid or liquid targets (1 cm thick) to enable proton-proton, proton-deuteron, and proton-nucleus collisions at center-of-mass energies of 115 GeV for protons and 72 GeV per nucleon-nucleon for lead ions, with the backward c.m.s. hemisphere easily accessible due to the large boost.

## Context

This work extends the fixed-target approach previously demonstrated by the CERN UA9 collaboration (Scandale, 2011) for lead beam extraction at SPS, and builds on the bent-crystal "smart collimator" concept originally proposed by Biryukov (2003). The proposal enables physics studies complementary to RHIC and LHC collider experiments, particularly in the backward rapidity region where large momentum fractions in the target can be probed, and would allow systematic studies of nuclear effects across different target species at the same energy. A more complete survey of physics opportunities is provided in Brodsky (2012).
