# A variational approach to approximate particle number projection with effective forces

> Kamlah's second order method for approximate particle number projection is applied for the first time to variational calculations with effective forces. High spin states of normal and superdeformed nuclei have been calculated with the finite range density dependent Gogny force for several nuclei. Advantages and drawbacks of the Kamlah second order method as compared to the Lipkin-Nogami recipe are thoroughly discussed. We find that the Lipkin-Nogami prescription occasionally may fail to find the right energy minimum in the strong pairing regime and that Kamlah's second order approach, though providing better results than the LN one, may break down in some limiting situations.

# A variational approach to approximate particle number projection with effective forces

## Key claim

The fully variational selfconsistent Kamlah second-order (SCK2) method provides results closer to experiment than the non-variational Lipkin-Nogami (LN) approach in regimes where the Kamlah expansion is valid, but the SCK2 method may break down in weak-pairing situations (small particle number fluctuations), while the LN approach occasionally fails to find the correct energy minimum in the strong pairing regime due to its non-variational character.

## Method

The authors performed selfconsistent calculations of approximate particle number projection using Kamlah's second-order expansion, applied for the first time with the finite-range density-dependent Gogny force (D1S parameterization). They solved both the standard Lipkin-Nogami equations (with \(h_2\) held constant during variation) and the fully variational selfconsistent Kamlah second-order (SCK2) equations (allowing \(h_2\) to vary) for high-spin states, using a triaxial harmonic oscillator basis with a conjugate gradient minimization method and cranking constraint \(\langle J_x \rangle = \sqrt{I(I+1)}\).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{164}\)Er: neutron pairing energy \(E_{pp}\) in LN decreases from \(-12\) MeV at low spin to \(-6\) MeV at \(20\hbar\) then to \(-4\) MeV at \(30\hbar\); in SCK2 it drops sharply from \(-6\) MeV at \(16\hbar\) to an almost unpaired regime.
- For \(^{164}\)Er: neutron number fluctuation \(\langle (\Delta \hat{N})^2 \rangle\) in SCK2 decreases sharply from about 6.5 to around 2 at spin \(18\hbar\).
- For \(^{152}\)Dy: experimental charge quadrupole moment \(17.5 \pm 0.2\) eb; LN results agree well while SCK2 results are slightly higher.
- For \(^{190}\)Hg: experimental charge quadrupole moment \(17.7^{+1}_{-1.2}\) eb; both LN and SCK2 predictions are slightly larger.
- For \(^{152}\)Dy: SCK2 expansion breaks down above spin \(56\hbar\) with \(h_2\) values larger than one and very small \(\langle (\Delta \hat{N})^2 \rangle\).
- LN calculations required convergence criterion \(\varepsilon = 5 \times 10^{-5}\) MeV due to slow convergence, compared to \(10^{-3}-10^{-4}\) MeV typical for selfconsistent calculations.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Zheng, Sprung and Flocard (1992) and Dobaczewski and Nazarewicz (1993), who examined LN and SCK2 approximations in a simple two-level model with monopole pairing, to realistic calculations with the Gogny force in large configuration spaces. The paper enables systematic assessment of when approximate particle number projection methods are reliable for high-spin and superdeformed nuclear states, and identifies regimes where each method fails: SCK2 breaks down in weak pairing (small fluctuations), while LN can miss the correct minimum in strong pairing.
