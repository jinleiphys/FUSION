# Probing The Universe With Neutrinos At 20 Minutes And 400 Thousand Years

> Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) and the Cosmic Background Radiation (CBR) provide complementary probes of the early evolution of the Universe and of its particle content. Neutrinos play important roles in both cases, influencing the primordial abundances of the nuclides produced by BBN during the first 20 minutes as well as the spectrum of temperature fluctuations imprinted on the CBR when the Universe is some 400 thousand years old. In this talk I review the physical effects of neutrinos at these different epochs in the evolution of the Universe and compare the theoretical predictions with the observational data to explore the consistency of the standard models of cosmology and particle physics and to constrain neutrino physics as well as more general, beyond-the-standard-model physics.

## Key claim

The standard models of cosmology and particle physics are consistent at the ~2 sigma level when comparing Big Bang Nucleosynthesis (BBN) predictions with observed relic abundances of deuterium and helium-4, and when comparing those BBN results with Cosmic Background Radiation (CBR) data; however, the constraints on the effective number of neutrino species from the CBR remain uncertain and should be regarded with caution until discrepancies among different analyses are resolved.

## Method

The author compared BBN-predicted abundances of deuterium and helium-4, computed as functions of baryon density, expansion rate parameter S (or effective neutrino number N_nu), and electron neutrino asymmetry xi_e, against observational determinations of primordial D/H from six high-redshift QSO absorption line systems and of primordial helium mass fraction Y_P from low-metallicity extragalactic H II regions. The resulting constraints on baryon density and expansion rate were then compared with those inferred from the angular power spectrum of CBR temperature fluctuations, using first-year and three-year WMAP data.

## Key numbers

- Adopted relic deuterium abundance: y_D = 2.68 (+0.27, -0.25), corresponding to eta_D = 5.95 (+0.36, -0.39)
- Adopted primordial helium mass fraction: Y_P = 0.238 +/- 0.005 (95% confidence range 0.228 to 0.248), corresponding to eta_He = -0.25 +/- 3.15
- From BBN, assuming no lepton asymmetry: S = 0.942 +/- 0.030, N_nu = 2.30 (+0.35, -0.34), eta_10 = 5.60 (+0.38, -0.41), Omega_B h^2 = 0.0204 (+0.0014, -0.0015)
- From BBN, assuming standard expansion rate (S=1, N_nu=3): xi_e = 0.043 +/- 0.022, eta_10 = 5.90 (+0.36, -0.39), Omega_B h^2 = 0.0215 (+0.0013, -0.0014)
- From first-year WMAP data (Barger et al. 2003): best-fit Omega_B h^2 = 0.0230, N_nu = 2.75, with 2-sigma range for N_nu of 0.9 to 8.3
- From three-year WMAP data (Spergel et al. 2006, N_nu=3): Omega_B h^2 = 0.0223 (+0.0007, -0.0009)
- From preliminary three-year WMAP analysis (Simha and Steigman, in progress): Omega_B h^2 = 0.0222 +/- 0.0007, with 2-sigma range for N_nu of 2.1 to 8.3

## Context

This work extends the author's own review (Steigman, 2006) and the analysis of Kneller and Steigman (2004) providing fitting formulae for BBN abundances. It compares BBN constraints with CBR results from Hu and Dodelson (2002), Barger et al. (2003), and the WMAP team (Spergel et al. 2003, 2006). The consistency found at ~2 sigma between BBN and CBR probes of baryon density and relativistic energy density enables joint constraints on beyond-standard-model physics, particularly on the effective number of neutrino species and possible electron neutrino asymmetry, while noting that the CBR constraints on N_nu remain imprecise and somewhat discordant across different analyses.
