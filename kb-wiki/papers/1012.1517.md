# Signatures of collective flow in high multiplicity pp collisions

> A blast-wave parametrization, including a full set of hadronic resonances, is used to model a small system, with total particle multiplicity comparable to the one measured in the high-multiplicity pp collisions at the LHC. Calculations are preformed for three cases: with negligible, regular and strong radial flow on the blast-wave hypersurface. We investigate the effects of flow on inclusive p_T spectra as well as on 1D and 3D femtoscopic radii for pions. Special emphasis is put on the role of pions from resonance decays. In particular we show that they magnify the flow effects present in the blast-wave stage and significantly influence the shape of the correlation functions. A specific observable, the R^E_out/R^G_side ratio is proposed as a sensitive probe of the collective effects. Model results for the high multiplicity pp collisions, for scenarios with small and large radial flow are compared.

# Signatures of collective flow in high multiplicity pp collisions

## Key claim

A blast-wave model with hadronic resonances can produce femtoscopic signatures of radial flow in small systems with multiplicities comparable to high-multiplicity pp collisions at the LHC, but the observable effects are significantly magnified by pions from resonance decays rather than arising solely from primordial particles; the paper proposes the $R^E_{out}/R^G_{side}$ ratio as a sensitive probe, but notes that the model does not address the origin of such space-momentum correlations.

## Method

The THERMINATOR model was used with a blast-wave freeze-out hypersurface and a full set of 381 hadronic resonance types from the PDG, with particle abundances calculated from a chemical model at temperature $T_f = 165.6$ MeV and baryochemical potential $\mu_B = 28.5$ MeV. Three simulations of 2 million events each were performed, differing only in the $v_T$ parameter controlling radial flow strength: $v_T = 10$ (no flow), $v_T = 1$ (regular flow), and $v_T = 0.5$ (strong flow), with fixed transverse size $\rho_{max} = 1.5$ fm and proper time $\tau_0 = 2.0$ fm/c. Identical pion femtoscopic correlations were analyzed using the two-particle method, fitting 1D correlation functions with Gaussian and exponential forms and 3D correlation functions with a mixed functional form (Gaussian in side, exponential in out and long directions).

## Key numbers

- Average charged particle multiplicity $\langle dN_{ch}/d\eta \rangle = 8.3$ for the strong flow sample, about twice the average multiplicity reported by ALICE at $\sqrt{s} = 900$ GeV
- Slope parameters for all pions: 234 $\pm$ 1 MeV (strong flow) vs. 157 $\pm$ 1 MeV (no flow); for all kaons: 323 $\pm$ 4 MeV (strong flow) vs. 168 $\pm$ 2 MeV (no flow); for all protons: 414 $\pm$ 8 MeV (strong flow) vs. 157 $\pm$ 3 MeV (no flow)
- Slope parameters for primordial pions: 285 $\pm$ 2 MeV (strong flow) vs. 183 $\pm$ 1 MeV (no flow); primordial kaons: 360 $\pm$ 6 MeV (strong flow) vs. 181 $\pm$ 3 MeV (no flow); primordial protons: 465 $\pm$ 2 MeV (strong flow) vs. 176 $\pm$ 8 MeV (no flow)
- Transverse size parameter $\rho_{max} = 1.5$ fm, proper time $\tau_0 = 2.0$ fm/c, freeze-out temperature $T_f = 165.6$ MeV
- The $R^E_{out}/R^G_{side}$ ratio for all particles is up to a factor of 1.5 larger for no flow compared to strong flow

## Context

This work extends the blast-wave parametrization approach previously applied to heavy-ion collisions at RHIC (Schnedermann 1993, Retiere 2003, Kisiel 2006) to small systems comparable to high-multiplicity pp collisions at the LHC. It builds on observations of long-range ridge structures in pp collisions by CMS (2010) and attempts to explain them via radial flow or Color Glass Condensate frameworks (Dumitru 2010, Bozek 2010). The study is motivated by ALICE femtoscopy data (Aamodt 2010) and STAR comparisons of pp and heavy-ion radii (Aggarwal 2010) that suggest a common scaling mechanism. The results enable experimental tests of collectivity in high-multiplicity pp collisions using the proposed $R^E_{out}/R^G_{side}$ ratio, which can be measured with minimum-bias data already collected at the LHC.
