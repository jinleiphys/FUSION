# Compositeness of hadrons, nuclei, and atomic systems

> Recent observations of exotic hadrons have stimulated the theoretical investigation of the internal structure of hadrons. While all hadrons are ultimately composed of quarks and gluons bound by the strong interaction, quark clustering phenomena can generate hadronic molecules -- weakly bound systems of hadrons -- which are expected to emerge near two-hadron thresholds. However, it should be noted that a pure hadronic molecule is not realized, as the strong interaction induces mixing with other possible configurations. The compositeness of hadrons has been developed as a promising concept to quantitatively characterize the fraction of the hadronic molecular component. Here we summarize the modern understanding of the compositeness to study the internal structure of hadrons and review the application of the compositeness to various quantum systems in different energy scales, such as nuclei and atomic systems, in addition to hadrons.

## Key claim

The compositeness of a hadronic molecule, the probability that a bound state is a composite of two hadrons rather than an elementary particle, is inherently model-dependent in general, but becomes model-independent and determined solely by the scattering length and effective range in the weak-binding limit where the binding energy is much smaller than the typical energy scale of the system.

## Method

The authors formulate compositeness in non-relativistic quantum mechanics by decomposing the physical bound state into a bare elementary state and continuum scattering states using the completeness relation of the free Hamiltonian. They derive expressions for compositeness in terms of the transition form factor and the scattering amplitude, then analyze the weak-binding limit by expanding the form factor in powers of the ratio of binding energy to the typical energy scale, showing that the leading-order expression depends only on the residue of the bound state pole and the energy derivative of the loop function.

## Key numbers

* The compositeness \(X\) in the weak-binding limit is given by \(X = 2\pi^2\sqrt{2\mu^3}\, g_{\rm th}^2 / \sqrt{B}\) to leading order, with corrections of order \(\sqrt{B/E_{\rm typ}}\).
* The residue of the bound state pole \(g^2\) relates to the threshold coupling as \(g^2 = g_{\rm th}^2 [1 + \mathcal{O}(B/E_{\rm typ})]\).
* The weak-binding condition is defined as \(B \ll E_{\rm typ}\), where \(E_{\rm typ}\) is the typical energy scale of the system (e.g., \(m_\pi^2/(2\mu)\) for nuclear systems).
* The compositeness can be expressed as \(X = -g^2\, dG(E)/dE|_{E=-B} [1 + \mathcal{O}(B/E_{\rm typ})]\), where \(G(E)\) is the loop function.

## Context

This work extends the foundational compositeness framework of Weinberg (1965), who derived the weak-binding relation for the deuteron, and the review by Hyodo (2013) on compositeness for exotic hadrons. The paper unifies applications across hadron physics, nuclear physics, and atomic physics by exploiting low-energy universality, and it addresses the challenge of defining compositeness for unstable resonances, which is essential for interpreting states like \(X(3872)\), \(P_c\), and \(T_{cc}\) observed by Belle and LHCb. The formalism enables future model-independent determinations of the internal structure of near-threshold states using only scattering length and effective range data.
