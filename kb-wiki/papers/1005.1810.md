# Energy density functional on a microscopic basis

> In recent years impressive progress has been made in the development of highly accurate energy density functionals, which allow to treat medium-heavy nuclei. In this approach one tries to describe not only the ground state but also the first relevant excited states. In general, higher accuracy requires a larger set of parameters, which must be carefully chosen to avoid redundancy. Following this line of development, it is unavoidable that the connection of the functional with the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction becomes more and more elusive. In principle, the construction of a density functional from a density matrix expansion based on the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction is possible, and indeed the approach has been followed by few authors. However, to what extent a density functional based on such a microscopic approach can reach the accuracy of the fully phenomenological ones remains an open question. A related question is to establish which part of a functional can be actually derived by a microscopic approach and which part, on the contrary, must be left as purely phenomenological. In this paper we discuss the main problems that are encountered when the microscopic approach is followed. To this purpose we will use the method we have recently introduced to illustrate the different aspects of these problems. In particular we will discuss the possible connection of the density functional with the nuclear matter Equation of State and the distinct features of finite size effects proper of nuclei.

# Energy density functional on a microscopic basis

## Key claim

The paper argues that an energy density functional (EDF) for nuclear structure can be constructed primarily from microscopic nuclear matter calculations of the equation of state (EOS), with only a minimal set of phenomenological parameters for surface and spin-orbit terms. However, the accuracy of such a microscopically-based functional depends critically on the precise value chosen for the saturation energy per particle, and it remains an open question whether this approach can match the accuracy of fully phenomenological functionals.

## Method

The authors construct a Kohn-Sham density functional theory (KS-DFT) functional for nuclei by splitting the energy into a bulk correlation part taken from microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations of infinite nuclear matter (using a realistic bare nucleon-nucleon force plus three-body forces), a finite-range Gaussian surface term with three free parameters, and a spin-orbit term. The bulk part is incorporated via local density approximation (LDA) using interpolating polynomials for symmetric and neutron matter EOS, constrained to a saturation energy of -16.00 MeV per nucleon at density 0.16 fm^{-3}. The four free parameters (three for the surface Gaussian and one for spin-orbit strength) are fitted to reproduce the binding energies of 84 well-deformed nuclei in the rare earth, actinide, and super-heavy regions, using the linearized method of Bertsch et al. (2005) for the three parameters that enter linearly.

## Key numbers

- Saturation point of the fitted EOS: binding energy per nucleon E/A = -16.00 MeV at Fermi momentum k_F = 1.36 fm^{-1} (density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}), with compressibility 220 MeV.
- Optimal surface range parameter: r_0 = 0.9 fm, yielding sigma_E = 0.545 MeV for the 84 deformed nuclei.
- Fitted parameters at r_0 = 0.9 fm: V_U = -137.024 MeV, V_L = -117.854 MeV, spin-orbit strength W_LS = 95.43 MeV fm^5.
- For 467 spherical nuclei (161 even-even and 306 odd), the BCP09 functional gives a global rms deviation in binding energy sigma_E = 1.2 MeV.
- For 199 spherical nuclei with known charge radii, the BCP09 functional gives rms deviation sigma_R = 0.030 fm, with an average offset of -0.02 fm.
- A shift as small as 0.1 MeV in the saturation energy per particle can appreciably deteriorate the quality of the nuclear data fit.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Negele and Vautherin (1972) and the density matrix expansion, following the Kohn-Sham DFT strategy used in condensed matter physics. It contrasts with the standard nuclear physics approach of globally parametrizing functionals like Skyrme forces (e.g., the Saclay-Lyon SLy4 force of 1998) or Gogny forces, which mix bulk and surface parameters. The BCP09 functional is compared directly to the Gogny D1M parametrization, achieving comparable accuracy (sigma_E = 0.545 MeV versus 0.54 MeV for D1M on the same 84 deformed nuclei) at lower computational cost. The work enables future systematic studies of nuclear masses, charge radii, and fission barriers from a functional with a clear microscopic foundation, and identifies the sensitivity to the saturation point and the treatment of three-body forces as key open problems.
