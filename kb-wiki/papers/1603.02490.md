# Challenges in Nuclear Structure Theory

> The goal of nuclear structure theory is to build a comprehensive microscopic framework in which properties of nuclei and extended nuclear matter, and nuclear reactions and decays can all be consistently described. Due to novel theoretical concepts, breakthroughs in the experimentation with rare isotopes, increased exchange of ideas across different research areas, and the progress in computer technologies and numerical algorithms, nuclear theorists have been quite successful in solving various bits and pieces of the nuclear many-body puzzle and the prospects are exciting. This article contains a brief, personal perspective on the status of the field.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear structure theory is making significant progress toward a comprehensive microscopic framework, but all current approaches remain phenomenological at some level because low-energy coupling constants must be optimized to experimental data; the superlatives "fully microscopic" or "from first principles" cannot be viewed as absolute.

## Method

The paper provides a personal perspective on the hierarchy of nuclear many-body approaches, from lattice QCD and ab-initio methods for light nuclei through configuration-interaction techniques for medium-mass systems to nuclear density functional theory for heavy nuclei and collective models. It discusses the need for uncertainty quantification, the importance of large multi-institutional collaborations such as NUCLEI and its UNEDF predecessor, and the role of high-performance computing in connecting different resolution scales across the nuclear landscape.

## Key numbers

- The paper lists ten anticipated "Greatest Hits" for nuclear structure theory over the next 10-15 years, including that ab-initio methods will reach heavy nuclei and that predictive models will describe nuclear matter from 0.1 to twice saturation density
- The NUCLEI collaboration involves nuclear structure theorists, computer scientists, and mathematicians from 16 institutions, including 10 universities and 6 national labs
- A paper on the mass of the Higgs boson with 5154 authors is cited as breaking the record for largest number of contributors to a research article

## Context

This perspective extends the roadmap discussions from the 2007 and 2015 NSAC Long Range Plans and the 2012 Decadal Study of nuclear physics. It builds on the UNEDF collaboration (Bog13) and the NUCLEI SciDAC-3 collaboration to argue for large-scale theory teams. The paper enables future work by identifying specific challenges: the need for validated high-quality input, accounting for many-body dynamics and correlations, and incorporating open-quantum-system aspects of the nucleus. It points to the FRIB Theory Alliance as a new mechanism for reaching beyond traditional fields of nuclear structure and reactions.
