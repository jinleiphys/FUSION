# Magnetization of neutron star matter

> The magnetization of neutron star matter in magnetic fields is studied by employing the FSUGold interaction. It is found that the magnetic susceptibilities of the charged particles (proton, electron and muon) can be larger than that of neutron. The effects of the anomalous magnetic moments (AMM) of each component on the magnetic susceptibility are examined in detail. It is found that the proton and electron AMM affect their respective magnetic susceptibility evidently in strong magnetic fields. In addition, they are the protons instead of the electrons that contribute most significantly to the magnetization of the neutron star matter in a relative weak magnetic field, and the induced magnetic field due to the magnetization can be appear to be very large. Finally, the effect of the density-dependent symmetry energy on the magnetization is discussed.

# Magnetization of neutron star matter

## Key claim
The magnetic susceptibility of charged particles (proton, electron, muon) in neutron star matter can exceed that of neutrons, and protons, not electrons, dominate the magnetization in relatively weak magnetic fields (below about 10² times the electron critical field). This contradicts earlier work (Peng, undated) that suggested the relativistic electron gas could boost the magnetic field, and also revises the conclusion of Ref. GM3 that magnetization never becomes very large.

## Method
The authors employed the relativistic mean field (RMF) approach with the FSUGold interaction, which includes nonlinear self-couplings for the ω meson and a mixed isoscalar-isovector coupling Λᵥ to control the density dependence of the symmetry energy. They computed the magnetization M = −(∂Ω/∂B) at zero temperature for β-stable matter composed of protons, neutrons, electrons, and muons in a uniform magnetic field, including the anomalous magnetic moments (AMM) of all four particle species. The magnetization of charged particles was obtained from the thermodynamic potential with Landau level summation, while the neutron magnetization was calculated directly from spin polarization.

## Key numbers
- Proton AMM: κₚ = 1.7928 μₙ; neutron AMM: κₙ = −1.9130 μₙ; electron AMM: κₑ = 1.15965 × 10⁻³ μ_B; muon AMM: κ_μ = 1.16592 × 10⁻³ μ_B.
- The electron critical field B_c^e = 4.414 × 10¹³ G is used as the unit of magnetic field throughout.
- At a density ρ = 0.16 fm⁻³, the magnetic susceptibility of charged particles shows de Haas–van Alphen oscillations; the proton magnetization dominates for B < 10² B_c^e.
- The muon AMM is about 1/207 of the electron AMM and its effect on susceptibility is negligible.
- A stiffer symmetry energy (Λᵥ = 0.00) yields a larger total magnetic susceptibility than a softer one (Λᵥ = 0.04) at strong magnetic fields B > 10⁴ B_c^e.

## Context
This work extends prior RMF studies of magnetization in pure neutron matter or nuclear matter (Refs. GM5, GM4, GM6) to β-stable matter including leptons. It directly contradicts the conclusion of Peng (undated) that the relativistic electron gas could produce super-strong magnetic fields, and revises the finding of Ref. GM3 that magnetization never becomes large, the discrepancy arises because GM3 did not consider very low magnetic fields. The results also show that the density-dependent symmetry energy, controlled by the Λᵥ coupling in FSUGold, significantly affects magnetization at strong fields. The authors note that pairing correlations in the ¹S₀ and ³PF₂ channels at low temperature and weak fields may dominate the magnetic response and require further investigation.
