# Electromagnetic fingerprints of the Little Bang

> Measurements of thermal photons emitted from the rapidly expanding hot and dense medium ("Little Bang") formed in ultra relativistic heavy-ion collisions, and their current theoretical interpretation, are reviewed.

## Key claim

Thermal photons emitted from the Little Bang are dominated by fireball temperatures below 250 MeV that are strongly blue-shifted by radial flow, not by the highest temperatures; the measured inverse slope parameters of 221 MeV at RHIC and 304 MeV at the LHC therefore do not directly reflect the initial temperature of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Method

The authors performed (2+1)-dimensional longitudinally boost-invariant viscous hydrodynamic simulations with Monte Carlo sampled fluctuating initial density profiles and a lattice-QCD-based equation of state, computing thermal photon emission rates both with and without shear viscous corrections. They compared these calculations to experimental data from PHENIX at RHIC and ALICE at the LHC, and also evaluated the effects of pre-equilibrium free-streaming and post-freeze-out hadronic resonance decay photons on the anisotropic flow coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Inverse slope parameter of the thermal photon excess: 221 +/- 19 +/- 19 MeV for central (0-20%) Au+Au collisions at 200 A GeV at RHIC (PHENIX)
- Inverse slope parameter of the thermal photon excess: 304 +/- 51 MeV for semi-central (0-40%) Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 A TeV at the LHC (ALICE)
- More than 2/3 of the observed thermal photon yield is emitted with inverse slopes around 240 MeV at RHIC and around 280 MeV at the LHC
- Direct photon elliptic flow peaks at values around 10-12% near pT = 2 GeV/c in both RHIC and LHC data
- Ideal fluid dynamic predictions gave thermal photon v2 peaking around 3-4%, dropping below 2% when prompt photons were included
- Adding pre-equilibrium flow and post-freeze-out resonance decay contributions enhances photon elliptic and triangular flows by 40-50%
- Hadronic emissivity may be increased by up to a factor 4 at low pT by including baryon-induced medium modifications and bremsstrahlung

## Context

This review extends the theoretical interpretation of thermal photon measurements first unambiguously observed by PHENIX in 2008 (Adare 2008) and confirmed by ALICE in 2012 (Wilde 2012). It addresses the "direct photon flow puzzle" where hydrodynamic predictions from Chatterjee (2005) and Holopainen (2011) gave v2 values of 3-4% while experimental data showed 10-12%, a discrepancy that viscous corrections from Shen (2013) and Dion (2011) actually worsened. The work enables future resolution of this puzzle through inclusion of pre-equilibrium dynamics, post-freeze-out resonance decays, and improved hadronic emission rates, potentially resolving the discrepancy through accumulation of multiple smaller effects.
