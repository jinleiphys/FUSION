# Modified Hagedorn formula including temperature fluctuation - Estimation of temperatures at RHIC experiments -

> We have systematically estimated the possible temperatures obtained from an analysis of recent data on $p_t$ distributions observed at RHIC experiments. Using the fact that observed $p_t$ distributions cannot be described by the original Hagedorn formula in the whole range of transverse momenta (in particular above 6 GeV/c), we propose a modified Hagedorn formula including temperature fluctuation. We show that by using it we can fit $p_t$ distributions in the whole range and can estimate consistently the relevant temperatures, including their fluctuations.

## Key claim

The original Hagedorn formula cannot describe the full range of transverse momentum ($p_t$) distributions measured at RHIC, particularly above 6 GeV/c; the authors propose a modified Hagedorn formula that includes temperature fluctuation (via a nonextensive parameter $q$) and show it can fit the whole $p_t$ range, allowing consistent estimation of temperatures and their fluctuations.

## Method

The authors fit RHIC data on $p_t$ distributions from STAR, BRAHMS, and PHENIX using three approaches: the original Hagedorn formula (Eq. 3), a QCD-inspired power-law formula (Eq. 6), and a modified Hagedorn formula (Eq. 8) that incorporates temperature fluctuation through a gamma distribution of inverse temperatures, parameterized by $q$. The modified formula integrates over rapidity $y$ and resonance mass $m$ using the Hagedorn resonance spectrum $\rho(m)$, with the nonextensive factor $[1 - \beta_0 (1-q) m_t \cosh y]^{1/(1-q)}$ replacing the simple exponential. Fits are performed for different centrality classes, and the resulting $q-1$ values are interpreted as measures of temperature fluctuation.

## Key numbers

* For STAR data (0-5% centrality), the original Hagedorn formula gives $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 532/32 with $T_H = 0.086$ GeV and $T_0 = 0.085$ GeV; the modified Hagedorn formula gives $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 56.6/31 with $q-1 = 1.48 \times 10^{-4}$, $T_H = 0.140$ GeV, and $T_0 = 0.132$ GeV.
* For BRAHMS 0-10% centrality, the modified Hagedorn formula yields $q-1 = 0.00$, $T_H = 0.192$ GeV, $T_0 = 0.178$ GeV, and $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 15.4/22.
* For PHENIX 0-5% centrality, the modified Hagedorn formula yields $q-1 = 1.21 \times 10^{-4}$, $T_H = 0.16$ GeV, $T_0 = 0.152$ GeV, and $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 4.98/29.
* For STAR 60-80% centrality, the modified Hagedorn formula gives $q-1 = 2.80 \times 10^{-5}$, $T_H = 0.0797$ GeV, $T_0 = 0.0782$ GeV, and $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 23.4/31.
* For pp collisions (non-single-diffractive), the modified Hagedorn formula gives $q-1 = 2.87 \times 10^{-5}$, $T_H = 0.0725$ GeV, $T_0 = 0.0711$ GeV, and $\chi^2$/n.d.f. = 38.2/28.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Biyajima et al., 2004) which showed that the original Hagedorn formula fails for $p_t > 6$ GeV/c and that a nonextensive approach (Eq. 5) can fit the whole range. The present paper systematically compares the original Hagedorn formula, the QCD-inspired power-law formula (used by UA1, CERN-MPI, and RHIC collaborations), and the proposed modified Hagedorn formula. The key finding is that including the Hagedorn resonance spectrum $\rho(m)$ in the nonextensive framework substantially lowers the $q-1$ values compared to the simpler nonextensive approach, because part of the fluctuation is absorbed by the resonance spectrum. The results enable more careful interpretation of temperatures extracted from heavy-ion data, with implications for the search for Quark-Gluon Plasma, and suggest that future data at higher $p_t$ could test the modified formula further.
