# Fixed-Point Quantum Circuits for Quantum Field Theories

> Renormalization group ideas and effective operators are used to efficiently determine localized unitaries for preparing the ground states of non-interacting scalar field theories on digital quantum devices. With these methods, classically computed ground states in a small spatial volume can be used to determine operators for preparing the ground state in a beyond-classical quantum register, even for interacting scalar field theories. Due to the exponential decay of correlation functions and the double exponential suppression of digitization artifacts, the derived quantum circuits are expected to be relevant already for near-term quantum devices.

# Fixed-Point Quantum Circuits for Quantum Field Theories

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that ground-state preparation circuits for non-interacting scalar field theories on digital quantum devices can be defined by "fixed-point" unitary operators that become independent of lattice volume for large systems, with systematic errors from finite-volume and digitization artifacts suppressed exponentially (by e^{-mN}) and double-exponentially (by e^{-2^{2n_Q}}), respectively. This means classically tractable calculations in small volumes can determine the circuit elements needed to prepare ground states on quantum registers larger than can be stored classically, even for interacting theories.

## Method

The authors start from the Gaussian ground-state wavefunction of a non-interacting scalar field on a spatial lattice, expressed in the field-eigenstate basis. They construct a quantum circuit of controlled Ry rotations that prepares this state site-by-site, then decompose these rotations into "alpha-angles" that are localized in spatial extent according to the exponentially decaying correlation matrix K. By marginalizing (tracing) over field degrees of freedom on sites below the rotation being applied, they derive effective operators that capture the influence of the integrated-out sites; in the limit of infinite volume and continuous field digitization, these effective operators become fixed-point expressions depending only on the asymptotic K-matrix elements and a determinant ratio that converges to a constant. The fixed-point angles are then computed from these continuum effective operators, and the convergence is verified by comparing with exact digitized calculations for systems up to 20 qubits.

## Key numbers

- Systematic errors from finite lattice volume scale as O(e^{-m N}) up to polynomial factors of approximately 1/sqrt(N), where m is the scalar mass and N is the number of lattice sites.
- For a coarse digitization of n_Q = 2 qubits per site, the fixed-point angle computed in the continuum without field truncation agrees with the exact digitized calculation to approximately 1%.
- Increasing digitization to n_Q = 3 qubits per site yields agreement between continuum and digitized angles at the level of approximately 0.001%.
- The total number of gates required for ground-state preparation scales as O(N 2^{2 n_Q}).
- The asymptotic K-matrix elements for the non-interacting scalar field are given analytically: K_{00}^\infty = (2 sqrt(4+m^2)/pi) E(4/(4+m^2)) and K_{01}^\infty = (sqrt(4+m^2)/(3 pi)) [m^2 K(4/(4+m^2)) - (2+m^2) E(4/(4+m^2))], where K and E are complete elliptic integrals of the first and second kind.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior demonstration (Klco 2019) that ground-state preparation circuits for massive scalar fields can be spatially localized due to exponentially decaying correlations, with rotation angles becoming exponentially suppressed as operators become more non-local. It builds on the "Somma Inflation" technique (Somma 2016) for preparing Gaussian states and on earlier resource estimates for scalar field simulation (Jordan 2011, 2014). The fixed-point approach enables ground-state preparation on quantum devices for lattices larger than can be stored classically, and the authors suggest it can be extended to interacting scalar field theories and confining gauge theories such as QCD, providing an alternative to adiabatic state preparation that avoids circuit depth scaling with unknown spectral gaps.
