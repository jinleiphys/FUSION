# Coarse graining scale and effectiveness of hydrodynamic modeling

> Some basic questions about the hydrodynamical approach to relativistic heavy ion collisions are discussed aiming to clarify how far we can go with such an approach to extract useful information on the properties and dynamics of the QCD matter created. We emphasize the importance of the coarse-graining scale required for the hydrodynamic modeling which determines the space-time resolution and the associated limitations of collective flow observables. We show that certain kinds of observables can indicate the degree of inhomogeneity of the initial condition under less stringent condition than the local thermal equilibrium subjected to the coarse-graining scale compatible to the scenario.

## Key claim

The success of ideal hydrodynamic modeling in relativistic heavy ion collisions does not necessarily require local thermal equilibrium; instead, it can be understood as an effective description based on coarse-grained variables, where the validity depends on the coarse-graining scale being compatible with the space-time resolution of the observables considered.

## Method

The authors derive relativistic ideal hydrodynamics from a variational principle using Lagrangian coordinates, then introduce a coarse-graining procedure by replacing delta functions with smoothing functions (mollifiers) of spatial scale \(h\) and temporal scale \(\tau\) to define smoothed density, current, and energy-momentum tensor. They analyze how the coarse-graining scale determines the ensemble of microscopic configurations that produce a given hydrodynamic profile, and test the sensitivity of collective flow observables to initial-state granularity using controlled sets of initial conditions with different degrees of inhomogeneity.

## Key numbers

- The coarse-graining spatial scale \(h\) and temporal scale \(\tau\) are introduced as positive parameters defining the resolution of the smoothed density distribution; the smoothing functions satisfy \(\int U_\tau(t) dt = 1\) and \(\int W_h(\vec{r}) d^3\vec{r} = 1\), with the limits \(\tau \to 0\) and \(h \to 0\) recovering the original delta-function description.
- The Hamiltonian density of the ideal fluid system is given by \(T^{00} = (\varepsilon + P) \vec{u}^2 + \varepsilon\), where \(\varepsilon\) is the proper energy density and \(P\) the pressure.
- The total momentum of the system is \(\vec{P} = \int d^3\vec{x} \, (\varepsilon + P) \gamma \vec{u}\), which integrates the space part of \(T^{0i}\).

## Context

This work extends the discussion of hydrodynamic modeling in relativistic heavy ion collisions, particularly addressing the question of why hydrodynamics works despite the apparent lack of local thermal equilibrium. It builds on prior studies showing that ideal hydrodynamics with fluctuating initial conditions can reproduce similar \(p_t\) dependence of \(v_2\) to viscous hydrodynamics (Hama 2005, PhysRevLett.101.112301), and that event-averaged integrated \(v_2\) is insensitive to initial granularity and transport coefficients (PTPS.193.315, G. Denicol). The paper enables a framework to identify observables that are genuinely sensitive to local dynamics on an event-by-event basis, rather than relying on event-averaged quantities that mask the coarse-graining scale.
