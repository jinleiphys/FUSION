# Bayesian analysis of QGP jet transport using multi-scale modeling applied to inclusive hadron and reconstructed jet data

> The JETSCAPE Collaboration reports a new determination of jet transport coefficients in the Quark-Gluon Plasma, using both reconstructed jet and hadron data measured at RHIC and the LHC. The JETSCAPE framework incorporates detailed modeling of the dynamical evolution of the QGP; a multi-stage theoretical approach to in-medium jet evolution and medium response; and Bayesian inference for quantitative comparison of model calculations and data. The multi-stage framework incorporates multiple models to cover a broad range in scale of the in-medium parton shower evolution, with dynamical choice of model that depends on the current virtuality or energy of the parton. We will discuss the physics of the multi-stage modeling, and then present a new Bayesian analysis incorporating it. This analysis extends the recently published JETSCAPE determination of the jet transport parameter $\hat{q}$ that was based solely on inclusive hadron suppression data, by incorporating reconstructed jet measurements of quenching. We explore the functional dependence of jet transport coefficients on QGP temperature and jet energy and virtuality, and report the consistency and tensions found for current jet quenching modeling with hadron and reconstructed jet data over a wide range in kinematics and $\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}}$. This analysis represents the next step in the program of comprehensive analysis of jet quenching phenomenology and its constraint of properties of the QGP.

## Key claim

The JETSCAPE Collaboration presents a Bayesian inference analysis of jet transport coefficients that, for the first time, simultaneously incorporates both inclusive hadron and reconstructed jet suppression data from RHIC and the LHC, finding broad consistency between model and data but also revealing tensions at high transverse momentum that suggest current multi-stage modeling may not fully capture the physics across all kinematic regimes.

## Method

The analysis uses the JETSCAPE framework with a multi-stage approach combining MATTER (for high-virtuality partons) and LBT (for lower-virtuality partons) to model in-medium jet evolution, with the jet transport coefficient q-hat parametrized as a function of temperature, parton energy, and virtuality via a physics-inspired expression containing six tunable parameters. Simulations are performed at a set of design points in parameter space, Gaussian Process Emulation is used for interpolation, and Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling extracts posterior distributions by comparing model calculations to inclusive charged hadron R_AA and inclusive jet R_AA measurements from STAR, PHENIX, ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, 2.76 TeV, and 5.02 TeV for 0-10% most central collisions.

## Key numbers

* The analysis includes inclusive charged hadron R_AA data at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV (RHIC), 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV (LHC), and inclusive jet R_AA data with jet radii R = 0.2, 0.4 from STAR, ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS at the same collision energies.
* The q-hat parametrization contains six parameters: three coefficients (c1, c2, c3) controlling the virtuality dependence, plus alpha_s, tau_0, and the switching scale Q_switch between MATTER and LBT.
* The posterior distribution for the temperature dependence of q-hat from the hadron-only analysis is significantly constrained compared to the prior, and values from three different models (MATTER, LBT, MATTER+LBT) are consistent with each other and with the previous JET collaboration determination.
* At sqrt(s_NN) = 5.02 TeV, the posterior tends to describe hadron R_AA while underpredicting jet R_AA at lower pT, then transitions to underpredict hadron R_AA while describing ATLAS jet R_AA at higher pT.

## Context

This work extends the JETSCAPE Collaboration's previous Bayesian analysis (2021) that used only inclusive charged hadron R_AA data, by adding reconstructed jet measurements to constrain jet transport coefficients. The analysis builds on prior Bayesian inference applications in heavy-ion physics from Bernhard (2016) and Novak (2013) in the soft sector, and the JET collaboration's earlier determination of q-hat (2013). The results enable future comprehensive studies of jet quenching phenomenology, including exploration of observable discriminating power, expansion to additional jet observables, and identification of tensions that motivate improved theoretical modeling.
