# Parton self-energies for general momentum-space anisotropy

> We introduce an efficient general method for calculating the self-energies, collective modes, and dispersion relations of quarks and gluons in a momentum-anisotropic high-temperature quark-gluon plasma. The method introduced is applicable to the most general classes of deformed anisotropic momentum distributions and the resulting self-energies are expressed in terms of a series of hypergeometric basis functions which are valid in the entire complex phase-velocity plane. Comparing to direct numerical integration of the self-energies, the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster and provides results with similar or better accuracy. To extend previous studies and demonstrate the application of the proposed method, we present numerical results for the parton self-energies and dispersion relations of partonic collective excitations for the case of an ellipsoidal momentum-space anisotropy. Finally, we also present, for the first time, the gluon unstable mode growth rate for the case of an ellipsoidal momentum-space anisotropy.

## Key claim

The paper introduces an efficient general method for calculating parton self-energies in a momentum-anisotropic quark-gluon plasma that is applicable to the most general classes of deformed anisotropic momentum distributions, with the caveat that the method's accuracy depends on fitting the imaginary part of the self-energy in the real interval \(z \in [-1,1]\) using a finite series of basis functions, and the authors demonstrate it specifically for ellipsoidal anisotropy.

## Method

The authors express the hard-loop quark and gluon self-energies as integrals over the momentum-space solid angle, using a generalized Romatschke-Strickland parameterization of anisotropic distribution functions with a function \(H(\theta,\phi)\). They compute the imaginary part of the self-energy in the real space-like region \(z \in [-1,1]\) via a straightforward one-dimensional integration over the azimuthal angle \(\phi\), then fit this imaginary part to a polynomial series in \(x = \cos\theta\). Using the analytic form of the integral \(\int_{-1}^1 dx \, x^n/(z-x+i0^+)\) expressed in terms of hypergeometric functions \({}_2F_1\), they reconstruct both the real and imaginary parts of the self-energy in the entire complex phase-velocity plane.

## Key numbers

* For the hypergeometric expansion method, 20 polynomial coefficients provide results with a relative error of less than \(10^{-6}\) in the entire complex plane even for extremely anisotropic distributions.
* The quark effective mass is defined as \(m_q^2 = \frac{g^2 C_F}{8 \pi^2} \int_0^{\infty} dp \, p \, f_{\rm iso}(p/\Lambda)\).
* The gluon Debye mass is defined as \(m_D^2 = -\frac{g^2}{2 \pi^2} \int_0^{\infty} dp \, p^2 \, \frac{df_{\rm iso}(p/\Lambda)}{dp}\).
* Numerical results for quark self-energy components are shown for anisotropy parameters \(\xi_1=20\) and \(\xi_2 \in \{-0.5, 0, 3, 12\}\) at \(\theta_k=\pi/3\), \(\phi_k=\pi/4\), and for extreme anisotropy \(\xi_1=120\) with \(\xi_2 \in \{25, 50, 100\}\).
* Gluon self-energy components are shown for \(\xi_1=20\) and \(\xi_2 \in \{-0.5, 0, 3, 12\}\) at \(\theta_k=\pi/3\), \(\phi_k=\pi/4\).
* Dispersion relations for quark collective modes are shown for isotropic (\(\xi_1=\xi_2=0\)), spheroidal (\(\xi_1=10, \xi_2=0\)), and ellipsoidal (\(\xi_1=10, \xi_2=5\)) cases at \(\theta_k=\pi/3\), \(\phi_k=\pi/6\).
* Gluon unstable mode growth rates are shown for spheroidal (\(\xi_1=10, \xi_2=0\)) and ellipsoidal (\(\xi_1=10, \xi_2=5\)) anisotropy at \(\theta_k=\pi/3\) and multiple \(\phi_k\) values.

## Context

This work extends the prior parameterization of momentum anisotropy introduced by Romatschke and Strickland (2003, 2004) from spheroidal anisotropy (one anisotropy parameter) to ellipsoidal anisotropy (two independent anisotropy parameters). It also generalizes the earlier calculation of the quark self-energy for ellipsoidal anisotropy by Kasmaei and Strickland (2016) to include gluon self-energies and unstable mode growth rates. The method enables efficient computation of self-energies, dispersion relations, and unstable mode growth rates for arbitrary anisotropy forms, which can be applied to studies of photon and dilepton production, heavy quark potential, and bottomonia suppression in heavy-ion collisions.
