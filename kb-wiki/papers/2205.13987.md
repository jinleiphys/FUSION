# $^{10}$Be-nucleus optical potentials developed from chiral effective field theory $NN$ interactions

> We present a determination of optical potentials for $^{10}$Be-nucleus collisions using the double-folding method to compute the real part and Kramers-Kronig dispersion relations to derive the imaginary part. As microscopic inputs we use chiral effective field theory nucleon-nucleon interactions at next-to-next-to-leading order combined with state-of-the-art nucleonic densities. With these potentials, we compute elastic scattering cross sections for the exotic nucleus 10 Be off various targets, and compare them to experiment. Without any fitting parameter, we obtain good agreement with data. For collisions on light targets, we observe significant uncertainty related to the short-range physics, whereas for heavy targets that uncertainty remains small.

# $^{10}$Be-nucleus optical potentials developed from chiral effective field theory $NN$ interactions

## Key claim
Optical potentials for $^{10}$Be-nucleus collisions can be constructed from chiral effective field theory nucleon-nucleon interactions using the double-folding method for the real part and Kramers-Kronig dispersion relations for the imaginary part, yielding good agreement with elastic scattering data without any fitting parameter. However, the method works best at high energies; at lower energies (e.g., $E_\text{lab}=28.3$ MeV for $^{64}$Zn), the dispersion relations alone are insufficient to fully describe the imaginary part, and beyond-Hartree-Fock effects such as excitations to low-lying states should be included.

## Method
The real part of the optical potential was constructed using the antisymmetrized double-folding method, summing direct and exchange contributions from local chiral effective field theory nucleon-nucleon interactions at next-to-next-to-leading order (N$^2$LO) folded over realistic nucleonic densities. The imaginary part was derived either from Kramers-Kronig dispersion relations applied to the energy-dependent exchange part of the double-folding potential, or from a zeroth-order approximation setting the imaginary part proportional to the real folding potential with a fitted factor $N_W$. Elastic scattering cross sections were computed for $^{10}$Be on $^{12}$C, $^{208}$Pb, and $^{64}$Zn targets and compared to experimental data, testing sensitivity to the short-range cutoff $R_0$ (1.2 and 1.6 fm) of the $NN$ interaction and to different choices of nuclear densities.

## Key numbers
- $^{10}$Be-$^{12}$C elastic scattering at $E_\text{lab}=595$ MeV: using Kramers-Kronig relations with $R_0=1.6$ fm gives good agreement with data; with $R_0=1.2$ fm the cross section is not in phase with data beyond the second minimum.
- $^{10}$Be-$^{208}$Pb elastic scattering at $E_\text{lab}=127$ MeV: the $R_0$ band (1.2 to 1.6 fm) is narrow, indicating small sensitivity to short-range physics; Kramers-Kronig relations give good agreement without fitting parameters.
- $^{10}$Be-$^{64}$Zn elastic scattering at $E_\text{lab}=28.3$ MeV: both Kramers-Kronig relations and $N_W=1.0$ give results shifted toward larger angles compared to experiment; the $R_0$ band is narrow due to Coulomb dominance.
- For $^{10}$Be-$^{12}$C, the $N_W$ value that best fits data is 0.6; for $^{10}$Be-$^{208}$Pb and $^{10}$Be-$^{64}$Zn, the best $N_W$ value is 1.0.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous double-folding approach (Durant 2017, 2020) from stable projectiles like $\alpha$, $^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O to the radioactive, deformed nucleus $^{10}$Be. It builds on chiral EFT $NN$ interactions (Gezerlis 2013, 2014) and uses dispersion relations (Carlson 1989, Gonzalez 2001) to constrain the imaginary part without free parameters, unlike phenomenological optical potentials (POPs) that require fitting to data. The method enables construction of reliable nucleus-nucleus interactions for reactions involving halo nuclei such as $^{11}$Be, which are key inputs to few-body reaction models (Baye 2012). Future improvements include accounting for excited states of colliding nuclei, using deformed densities, and incorporating $3N$ interactions and ab initio densities from chiral EFT.
