# Color superconductivity and the strange quark

> At ultra-high density, matter is expected to form a degenerate Fermi gas of quarks in which there is a condensate of Cooper pairs of quarks near the Fermi surface: color superconductivity. In these proceedings I review some of the underlying physics, and discuss outstanding questions about the phase structure of ultra-dense quark matter.

## Key claim

At ultra-high density, quark matter is expected to form a color superconductor with a color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase, but as density decreases the strange quark mass imposes stresses that may lead to a gapless CFL (gCFL) phase; however, the gCFL phase itself exhibits an instability (imaginary Meissner masses for some gluons) indicating that the true ground state at intermediate densities remains uncertain.

## Method

The paper reviews the phase structure of dense quark matter using a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model calculation that allows different pairing strengths for different flavor combinations (Δ_ud, Δ_ds, Δ_us). The analysis expands in powers of M_s/μ and Δ/μ to study the energetic balance between the cost of keeping Fermi surfaces together and the benefit of cross-species pairing, while also considering constraints from electromagnetic and color neutrality and weak interaction equilibration.

## Key numbers

- The strange quark mass is ~100 MeV, while up and down quark masses are ≲5 MeV
- The CFL phase is stable when M_s^2/μ < 2Δ_CFL, where Δ_CFL is the CFL gap at μ~500 MeV if M_s were zero
- For pairing strength Δ_CFL ≲ 25 MeV, the transition from CFL to gCFL occurs at M_s^2/μ ≈ 2Δ_CFL
- The gCFL phase remains favored beyond M_s^2/μ ≈ 4Δ_CFL, where the CFL phase would become unfavored
- Critical temperatures for most color-superconducting phases are expected to be of order 10 MeV or higher
- In secondary pairing calculations, the secondary gap Δ_s is found to be from ten to hundreds of times smaller than the primary gap at reasonable values of the secondary coupling

## Context

This review extends the line of work on color superconductivity initiated by the BCS mechanism applied to QCD, building on the CFL phase first described by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1999). It addresses outstanding questions about the phase structure between the CFL phase and nuclear matter, including the gapless CFL phase discovered by Alford, Kouvaris, and Rajagopal (2004) and the crystalline LOFF phase proposed by Alford, Bowers, and Rajagopal (2000). The paper enables future work on identifying the true ground state in the intermediate density regime, which could be a mixed phase, a crystalline LOFF phase, a p-wave meson condensate, or involve secondary pairing.
