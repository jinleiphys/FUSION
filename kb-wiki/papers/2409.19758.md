# On elliptic flow and the blast-wave model

> Extensions of the blast-wave model for the description of non-central collisions are reviewed and the physics behind them is explained and illustrated. It is shown how the second-order anisotropy in expansion velocity together with the anisotropy in the shape or the density profile of the fireball determine the elliptic flow of the produced hadrons. Ambiguities and limitations of the models are discussed and different models are compared among themselves and with example data. It is concluded that model results should always be compared to $v_2(p_t)$ from several identified species in order to receive meaningful results about the freeze-out stage of the fireball and that even such a comparison may not be conclusive as the models may not be able to reproduce all relevant data.

## Key claim

The blast-wave model extensions for non-central collisions suffer from fundamental ambiguities: the same elliptic flow signal can be produced by different combinations of flow anisotropy and shape/density anisotropy, and when fits are performed simultaneously to pions and protons, no single parameter set can reproduce both species, indicating that these models do not provide a valid effective description of the freeze-out stage.

## Method

The author reviews two extensions of the blast-wave model for non-central collisions: the STAR parametrization, which adds an azimuthally-varying density weight factor (1 + 2s₂cos2φ) to a circular fireball profile, and a shape-anisotropy model where the transverse radius oscillates as R(φ) = R₀(1 - a₂cos2φ) and flow is directed perpendicular to the deformed surface. Both models incorporate an azimuthally-varying transverse rapidity η_t = ṝρ₀(1 + 2ρ₂cosφ_b), and elliptic flow v₂(p_t) is computed via the Cooper-Frye formula using Boltzmann-approximated thermal distributions. The author then compares calculated v₂(p_t) for pions and protons across parameter space and fits to ALICE data from Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV, centrality 30-40%.

## Key numbers

- Temperature fixed to T = 106 MeV and radial flow gradient ρ₀ = 0.91 (or ρ̄₀ = 0.6 for STAR parametrization), guided by ALICE data from Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV, centrality 30-40%
- For the STAR parametrization best overall fit to both pions and protons: ρ₂ = 0.10300, s₂ = 0.05690 (set #14)
- For the shape-anisotropy model best overall fit: ρ₂ = 0.04075, a₂ = 0.08400 (set #17)
- The flow anisotropy ρ₂ required in the STAR parametrization is more than twice as large as in the shape-anisotropy model (0.103 vs 0.04075)
- The difference between calculated v₂(p_t) curves is quantified by Δ = ∫₀^{1.5 GeV} (v₂,ref(p_t) - v₂(p_t))² dp_t, with contours shown at 10⁻⁶, 10⁻⁵, 10⁻⁴, and 10⁻³

## Context

This minireview extends the line of work on blast-wave model parametrizations for heavy-ion collisions, building on the azimuthally symmetric version established by Siemens and Rasmussen (1978), Schnedermann et al. (1993), and Csörgő et al. (1995). The STAR collaboration (2001) introduced the density-weighting parametrization, while Retière and Lisa (2003) and Tomášik (2004) developed the shape-anisotropy formalism later reformulated by Cimerman et al. (2017). The paper demonstrates that fitting v₂(p_t) from multiple identified species simultaneously can either determine parameters uniquely or, as in the case shown with ALICE data, exclude the models entirely. This enables future work to develop more realistic freeze-out descriptions that can simultaneously reproduce pion and proton elliptic flow.
