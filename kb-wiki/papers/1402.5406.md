# SPQR -- Spectroscopy: Prospects, Questions & Results

> Tremendous progress has been made in mapping out the spectrum of hadrons over the past decade with plans to make further advances in the decade ahead. Baryons and mesons, both expected and unexpected, have been found, the results of precision experiments often with polarized beams, polarized targets and sometimes polarization of the final states. All these hadrons generate poles in the complex energy plane that are consequences of strong coupling QCD. They reveal how this works.

## Key claim

The hadron spectrum is not simply a collection of states to be identified by "bump-hunting"; rather, extracting the true spectrum requires determining poles in the complex energy plane through amplitude analyses that satisfy causality, relativity, and unitarity, with close cooperation between theorists and experimentalists.

## Method

The paper reviews the current state of hadron spectroscopy, comparing results from two amplitude analysis approaches: the Bonn-Gatchina team's traditional amplitude analysis and the ANL-Osaka group's dynamical approach based on the Sato-Lee effective Lagrangian. It discusses how precision experiments with polarized beams, polarized targets, and polarization of final states at facilities including ELSA, MAMI, CLAS6, BESIII, COMPASS, LHCb, and Jefferson Lab are providing data for these analyses. The paper also describes the establishment of the JLab Physics Analysis Center to network theorists and experimentalists for applying S-matrix technology to extract resonance poles from multi-hadron final states.

## Key numbers

- The Bonn-Gatchina and ANL-Osaka analyses show N* and Delta* spectra up to approximately 2.2 GeV, with spin-3 components becoming important above 1.55 GeV
- Lattice QCD calculations from the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration predict hybrid meson multiplets around 2 GeV, computed with a pion mass of 400 MeV
- The X(3872) is closely associated with the D D* channel; the charged Zc(4430) and Y(2175) in the phi f0(980) channel are also discussed as states with S-wave coupling to nearby hadron channels

## Context

This paper extends the line of work on hadron spectroscopy that began with the quark model and the discovery of the Omega- baryon, and it addresses the need to move beyond simple constituent quark models (Capstick, Barnes) toward approaches that incorporate decay channels as essential dynamical components (Pennington-Wilson, Santopinto). The work enables future experimental programs at Jefferson Lab's Hall D (GlueX) and other facilities to search for hybrid mesons and exotic quantum numbers, building on lattice QCD predictions from the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration (Dudek) and requiring practical tools for implementing two- and three-body unitarity in amplitude analyses.
