# Nonlocal nucleon-nucleus optical potentials from chiral effective field theory

> We investigate the nonlocality in microscopic optical potentials derived from chiral effective field theory. For this purpose we employ the Perey-Buck ansatz, which connects the energy dependence of purely local optical potentials to a Gaussian spatial nonlocality. We find that the dominant source of energy dependence in the microscopic real optical potential indeed arises from spatial nonlocalities, while the energy dependence associated with the microscopic imaginary optical potential is a genuine time nonlocality. We present results for nonlocal nucleon-nucleus optical potentials for the calcium isotopic chain and study the dependence of the Woods-Saxon shape parameters on the isotopic number.

## Key claim

The dominant source of energy dependence in the microscopic real optical potential arises from spatial nonlocalities (momentum dependence) that can be captured by the Perey-Buck ansatz, while the energy dependence of the microscopic imaginary optical potential is a genuine time nonlocality that cannot be described by the same Gaussian spatial nonlocality framework.

## Method

The authors computed the nucleon self-energy in homogeneous nuclear matter up to second order in many-body perturbation theory using chiral effective field theory two-body forces at N3LO and three-body forces at N2LO with a momentum-space cutoff of Λ = 450 MeV. They then applied the Perey-Buck ansatz, which relates energy-dependent local optical potentials to energy-independent nonlocal potentials through a Gaussian spatial nonlocality, fitting the parameters U_N and β to minimize variation over projectile energies from 0 to 100 MeV. The resulting nonlocal potential parameters were folded with nuclear density distributions using the improved local density approximation (ILDA) with a range parameter t = 1.2 fm to construct nonlocal nucleon-nucleus optical potentials for the calcium isotopic chain.

## Key numbers

- The Perey-Buck ansatz was found to be applicable for projectile energies up to E ~ 100 MeV, beyond which the assumption breaks down for both first-order and combined first- plus second-order self-energy contributions.
- The ILDA range parameter was set to t = 1.2 fm, chosen to be characteristic of the range of the nuclear interaction.
- Nuclear matter calculations were performed at saturation density n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ and over a density range from 0.01 to 0.20 fm⁻³.
- Isospin asymmetry values δ_np = 0, 0.2, 0.4, and 0.6 were studied at saturation density.
- The inclusion of second-order self-energy leads to an isovector optical potential contribution at least twice as large across all energies compared to first-order contributions alone.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic global optical potential developed by Whitehead, Lim, and Holt (WLH) (2019-2021), which used chiral effective field theory to construct energy-dependent local optical potentials but made the on-shell approximation that combines momentum and energy dependence. The present study investigates the validity of the Perey-Buck approach (Perey and Buck, 1962) for separating spatial and temporal nonlocalities in these microscopic potentials, enabling improved treatment of inelastic reaction processes such as transfer reactions where nonlocalities in the wavefunction periphery have been shown to be important (Titus et al., 2014, 2016). The results enable construction of energy-independent nonlocal optical potentials for use in reaction codes that can better describe scattering from exotic nuclei far from stability.
