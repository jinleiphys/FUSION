# Nuclear physics for geo-neutrino studies

> Geo-neutrino studies are based on theoretical estimates of geo-neutrino spectra. We propose a method for a direct measurement of the energy distribution of antineutrinos from decays of long-lived radioactive isotopes. We present preliminary results for the geo-neutrinos from Bi-214 decay, a process which accounts for about one half of the total geo-neutrino signal. The feeding probability of the lowest state of Bi-214 - the most important for geo-neutrino signal - is found to be p_0 = 0.177 \pm 0.004 (stat) ^{+0.003}_{-0.001} (sys), under the hypothesis of Universal Neutrino Spectrum Shape (UNSS). This value is consistent with the (indirect) estimate of the Table of Isotopes (ToI). We show that achievable larger statistics and reduction of systematics should allow to test possible distortions of the neutrino spectrum from that predicted using the UNSS hypothesis. Implications on the geo-neutrino signal are discussed.

## Key claim

The authors propose a direct calorimetric method for measuring geo-neutrino energy spectra from long-lived radioactive isotopes, rather than relying on indirect determinations from gamma-ray spectroscopy. The preliminary measurement of the Bi-214 feeding probability to the lowest state yields p_0 = 0.177 ± 0.004 (stat) +0.003/-0.001 (sys), but this result depends on the hypothesis of Universal Neutrino Spectrum Shape (UNSS), and the data show a 2.4 sigma hint of spectral distortion that would require larger statistics and reduced systematics to confirm.

## Method

The authors used the Counting Test Facility (CTF) at Gran Sasso, a liquid scintillator detector, to measure the visible energy deposited by electrons and gammas from Bi-214 decays in a radon-contaminated scintillator volume. Bi-214 events were identified by the characteristic Bi-Po coincidence (the subsequent Po-214 alpha decay with a mean time delay of 237 microseconds), and the visible energy spectrum was compared to Monte Carlo simulations that account for detector response, quenching factors, and gamma escape. The feeding probability p_0 of the lowest Po-214 state was extracted by fitting the observed energy distribution with a sum of universal beta-decay shapes for each transition, with the excited-state populations fixed to values from the Table of Isotopes.

## Key numbers

* Feeding probability of the lowest Po-214 state: p_0 = 0.177 ± 0.004 (stat) +0.003/-0.001 (sys), measured under the UNSS hypothesis from 3.14 × 10^4 candidate Bi-214 decays in a 42 cm fiducial sphere.
* The best-fit light yield was L = 321 photoelectrons per MeV, within 3% of the value determined from C-14 events at lower energy.
* The shape parameter y, describing a linear deviation from the universal spectrum, was found to be y = -0.11 ± 0.06 (stat), with a systematic error of approximately 0.10, giving a 2.4 sigma statistical preference for a deformed spectrum.
* The specific geo-neutrino signal for Bi-214 derived from CTF data is s = (1.42 ± 0.03 (stat) +0.023/-0.008 (sys)) × 10^{-44} cm^2, compared to the Table of Isotopes value of (1.46 ± 0.05) × 10^{-44} cm^2.

## Context

This work extends the nuclear physics basis for geo-neutrino studies, which previously relied on indirect determinations of feeding probabilities from gamma-ray intensities and the assumption of universal beta-decay shapes, as compiled in the Table of Isotopes (Akovali, 1995) and used in reference Earth models by Mantovani (2003), Fogli (2006), and Enomoto (2007). The proposed direct calorimetric method, if applied with higher statistics and reduced systematics to the three dominant transitions (Bi-214, Pa-234, and Bi-212), could test the UNSS hypothesis and reduce uncertainties in the geo-neutrino signal interpretation for experiments such as KamLAND (Araki, 2005; Abe, 2008) and Borexino (Alimonti, 2000; Alimonti, 2008), and for future detectors like SNO+, LENA, and HANOHANO.
