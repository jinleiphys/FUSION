# Restoring broken symmetries using oracles

> We present a new method to perform variation after projection in many-body systems on quantum computers that does not require performing explicit projection. The technique employs the notion of ``oracle'', generally used in quantum search algorithms. We show how to construct the oracle and the projector associated with a symmetry operator. The procedure is illustrated for the parity, particle number, and total spin symmetries. The oracle is used to restore symmetry by indirect measurements using a single ancillary qubit. An Illustration of the technique is made to obtain the approximate ground state energy for the pairing model Hamiltonian.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to perform variation after projection (VAP) on quantum computers that uses an oracle operator to directly access expectation values on symmetry-restored states without explicitly constructing the projected wave function, reducing the required circuit depth to a single ancillary qubit compared to prior quantum phase estimation approaches.

## Method

The authors construct an oracle operator that multiplies states in the symmetry-respecting subspace by a phase φ and states in the complementary subspace by a different phase μ, then use a Hadamard test with a single ancillary qubit to extract expectation values of operators (such as the Hamiltonian) evaluated on the symmetry-restored component of a variational state. The oracle is built from a linear combination of unitary (LCU) decomposition of the symmetry projector, with explicit constructions given for parity, particle number, and total spin symmetries; the energy of the projected state is obtained either by direct measurement of the Hamiltonian or by time-derivative of the generating function F_G(t) = ⟨Ψ_G|e^{-itH}|Ψ_G⟩ using short-time propagation.

## Key numbers

* For the parity operator, the projector is P_{ε_π} = (1/2)(I + ε_π π̂) where ε_π = ±1 are the eigenvalues.
* For particle number projection with n_q qubits, the decomposition uses M ≥ n_q terms with a=1, λ_1=0, and m_α = A for projection onto particle number A.
* For even n_q, total spin S takes values 0,1,...,n_q/2 with eigenvalues S(S+1) = 0,2,...,n_q(n_q+2)/4; for odd n_q, S = 1/2,3/2,...,n_q/2 with λ_1 = 3/4.
* The optimal gate count for an arbitrary diagonal operator on n_q qubits is 2^{n_q+1} - 3, but for the parity and particle number oracles each controlled operator can be implemented with a number of gates linear in n_LCU and n_q.

## Context

This work extends the quantum variation-after-projection (Q-VAP) method introduced by Ruiz Guzman and Lacroix (2021, 2022) and the symmetry restoration approach of Lacroix (2020), which used quantum phase estimation with multiple ancillary qubits and required explicit projection of the variational state. The oracle-based method reduces the resource requirements compared to those prior approaches and avoids discarding measurement events, which was a limitation of the probabilistic projection methods discussed in Lacroix (2022). The technique enables continuous monitoring of symmetry restoration during variational optimization on NISQ-era quantum computers and can be applied to parity, particle number, and total spin symmetries in many-body systems.
