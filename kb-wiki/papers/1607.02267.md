# Production of light nuclei in the thermal and coalescence models

> The thermal model properly describes the production yields of light nuclei in relativistic heavy-ion collisions even so the loosely bound sizable nuclei cannot exist in the dense and hot hadron gas at a chemical freeze-out. Within the coalescence model, light nuclei are formed at the latest stage of nuclear collisions - a kinetic freeze-out - due to final state interactions. After discussing the models, we derive simple analytic formulas and, using model parameters directly inferred from experimental data, we show that the thermal and coalescence model predictions are quantitatively close to each other. A possibility to falsify one of the two models is suggested.

# Production of light nuclei in the thermal and coalescence models

## Key claim

The thermal and coalescence models predict deuteron-to-proton ratios that are of the same order of magnitude (within a factor of 6 to 8) when model parameters are directly inferred from experimental data, contrary to the common perception that the two models are fundamentally incompatible. The author suggests that comparing yields of nuclei with the same mass number but different spatial structures (e.g., ⁴He and ⁴Li) could falsify one of the two models.

## Method

The author derives analytic formulas for the deuteron-to-proton yield ratio in both the thermal model (using a hadron gas at chemical freeze-out temperature with vanishing baryon chemical potential) and the coalescence model (where deuterons form at kinetic freeze-out via final-state interactions). For the coalescence model, the deuteron formation rate is computed using Gaussian parameterizations of both the source function and the deuteron wave function. All model parameters are taken directly from experimental data on Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV from the ALICE collaboration, including the chemical freeze-out temperature of 156 MeV, the deuteron yield per event, the mean transverse momentum of deuterons, and femtoscopic radii from pion-pion correlations.

## Key numbers

- The thermal model d/p ratio equals the experimental value of 3.6 × 10⁻³ when the parameter λ = 2.51, with proton mass m = 938 MeV and chemical freeze-out temperature T_chem = 156 MeV for Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV.
- The ratio Q = (d/p)_CM / (d/p)_TM ranges from 0.13 to 0.17 across four centrality bins (0-10%, 10-20%, 20-40%, 40-60%), meaning thermal model predictions are larger by a factor of 6 to 8 than coalescence model predictions.
- The chemical freeze-out volume V_chem ranges from 696 fm³ (40-60% centrality) to 3590 fm³ (0-10% centrality), corresponding to R_chem from 3.5 fm to 6.1 fm.
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature T_kin ranges from 760 MeV (40-60% centrality) to 900 MeV (0-10% centrality), determined from mean deuteron transverse momentum ⟨p_T⟩ ranging from 1.63 GeV to 2.12 GeV.
- The kinetic freeze-out radius R_kin ranges from 4.0 fm (40-60% centrality) to 7.0 fm (0-10% centrality).
- The deuteron root-mean-square radius R_d = 2 fm.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing observation by Baltz (1993) that thermal and coalescence model predictions are quantitatively similar, and refines the comparison using modern experimental data from the ALICE collaboration (Adam 2015) on Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies. The author builds on the Sato-Yazaki formulation (1981) of the coalescence model, which treats deuteron formation as a final-state interaction process without the phenomenological coalescence radius parameter. The analysis enables a proposed experimental test: comparing yields of ⁴He and ⁴Li, which have similar masses but very different spatial structures, to distinguish between the two models, building on prior coalescence calculations for ⁴He and ⁵,⁶Li by Sun (2015).
