# A Novel Mechanism for Type-I Superconductivity in Neutron Stars

> We suggest a mechanism that may resolve a conflict raised by Link between the precession of a neutron star and the standard picture in which its core is composed of a mixture of a neutron superfluid and a type-II proton superconductor. We will show that if there is a persistent, non-dissipating current running along the magnetic flux tubes, the force between magnetic flux tubes may be attractive, resulting in a type-I, rather than a type-II, superconductor. If this is the case, the conflict between the observed precession and the canonical estimation of the Landau-Ginzburg parameter (which suggests type II behaviour) will be automatically resolved. Such a current arises in some condensed matter systems and may also appear in QCD dense matter as a consequence of quantum anomalies. We calculate the interaction between two vortices carrying a current j and find a constraint on the magnitude of j where a superconductor is always type-I, even when the cannonical Landau-Ginzburg parameter indicates type-II behaviour. If this condition is met, the magnetic field is expelled from the superconducting regions of the neutron star leading to the formation of the intermediate state where alternating domains of superconducting matter and normal matter coexist. We further argue that even when the induced current is small the vortex Abrikosov lattice will nevertheless be destroyed due to the helical instability studied previously in many condensed matter systems. This would also resolve the apparent contradiction with the precession of the neutron stars. We also discuss some instances where anomalous induced current may play a crucial role, such as the neutron star kicks, pulsar glitches and the toroidal magnetic field.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that if a persistent, non-dissipating current runs along magnetic flux tubes in a neutron star's core, the force between flux tubes becomes attractive, making the proton condensate behave as a type-I superconductor even when the canonical Landau-Ginzburg parameter (kappa ~ 2.6) indicates type-II behavior. This resolves the conflict between observed neutron star precession and the standard picture of a type-II proton superconductor.

## Method

The authors model a superconducting vortex carrying an induced electromagnetic current along its core by adding a current source term to the two-dimensional Landau-Ginzburg free energy. They solve the linearized equations of motion for the vector potential and order parameter in the asymptotic limit, then calculate the interaction energy between two parallel current-carrying vortices using a point-source method that subtracts individual vortex energies. The interaction energy contains three terms: an attractive order-parameter term, a repulsive gauge-field term, and an additional attractive term from the longitudinal currents.

## Key numbers

- The canonical Landau-Ginzburg parameter for neutron star matter is kappa ~ 2.6, based on lambda ~ 80 fm and xi ~ 30 fm, which normally indicates type-II superconductivity (kappa > 1/sqrt(2)).
- The condition for the current to force type-I behavior is j > (hbar c^2)/(2 q lambda), where q is the Cooper pair charge and lambda is the Meissner penetration depth.
- Neutron star rotation frequencies are 1-10^3 Hz, and the magnetic field is ~10^12 G.
- The core temperature is ~10^8 K and density is ~10^15 g/cm^3.
- Neutron vortex density is ~10^10 per m^2, while proton vortex density is ~10^24 per m^2.
- Observed precession amplitude is ~3 degrees and frequency is ~1 per year.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Link (2003), who showed that the coexistence of neutron superfluid vortices and type-II proton flux tubes in a neutron star core is incompatible with observed precession. The paper builds on Sedrakian (2005), who assumed type-I superconductivity resolves the precession conflict but did not provide a mechanism, and on Buckley (2003zf), who proposed an alternative mechanism based on an effective Landau-Ginzburg parameter from isotopic symmetry. The mechanism here enables the formation of an intermediate state with alternating superconducting and normal domains, which would allow neutron vortices to move unhindered and permit long-period precession; it also has implications for neutron star kicks, pulsar glitches, and toroidal magnetic fields.
