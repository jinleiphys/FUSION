# Candidates for Long Lived High-K Ground States in Superheavy Nuclei

> On the basis of systematic calculations for 1364 heavy and superheavy nuclei, including odd-systems, we have found a few candidates for high-K ground states in superheavy nuclei. The macroscopic-microscopic model based on the deformed Woods-Saxon single particle potential which we use offers a reasonable description of SH systems, including known: nuclear masses, $Q_α$-values, fission barriers, ground state deformations, super- and hyper-deformed minima in the heaviest nuclei. %For odd and odd-odd systems, both ways of including pairing correlations, % blocking and the quasi-particle method, have been applied. Exceptionally untypical high-K intruder contents of the g.s. found for some nuclei accompanied by a sizable excitation of the parent configuration in daughter suggest a dramatic hindrance of the $α$-decay. Multidimensional hyper-cube configuration - constrained calculations of the Potential Energy Surfaces (PES's) for one especially promising candidate, $^{272}$ Mt, shows a $\backsimeq$ 6 MeV increase in the fission barrier above the configuration- unconstrained barrier. There is a possibility, that one such high-K ground- or low-lying state may be the longest lived superheavy isotope.

## Key claim
The macroscopic-microscopic Woods-Saxon model predicts that certain odd and odd-odd superheavy nuclei may have high-K ground states whose configuration lies significantly above the ground state in the daughter nucleus, potentially causing dramatic hindrance of alpha decay and spontaneous fission. The authors hedge that such a long-lived state "may be the longest lived superheavy isotope," but note that this depends on the degree of configuration hindrance, which is configuration-dependent and difficult to predict precisely.

## Method
The authors performed systematic calculations for 1364 heavy and superheavy nuclei using a macroscopic-microscopic model based on a deformed Woods-Saxon single-particle potential with Yukawa-plus-exponential macroscopic energy. For odd and odd-odd systems, they applied the blocking method and the quasiparticle method to include pairing correlations, minimizing over configurations and axially-symmetric deformations (β20, β40, β60, β80) to find ground states. For the candidate nucleus 272Mt, they performed multidimensional hyper-cube configuration-constrained calculations of potential energy surfaces to compare adiabatic (minimized over configurations) and diabatic (configuration-constrained) fission barriers.

## Key numbers
- The rms deviation in masses for 252 nuclei was about 400 keV with blocking and around 300 keV with the quasiparticle method; for 204 Qα values, similar rms errors resulted; for 88 measured Qα values in superheavy nuclei outside the fit region, the rms deviation was about 250 keV.
- The excitation of the parent ground-state configuration in the daughter nucleus (ΔQα) is about 2 MeV for 272Mt and about 1.5 MeV for 256Lr.
- For 272Mt, the configuration-preserving alpha-decay hindrance reaches six orders of magnitude, taking the lifetime from milliseconds to hours.
- The calculated fission barrier minimized over configurations in 272Mt amounts to 6.3 MeV, nearly the same as in 270Hs.
- The diabatic (configuration-constrained) saddle point for 272Mt lies about 6 MeV higher than the adiabatic saddle point.
- With pairing strengths increased by 10% and 15%, the diabatic fission barriers were 11.8 MeV and 10.3 MeV, respectively.
- The predicted Qβ value of 272Mt is 4.2 MeV, implying a beta half-life in the range of minutes.

## Context
This work extends systematic calculations of superheavy nuclei properties (masses, Qα values, fission barriers, ground state deformations) previously performed by the same group using the Woods-Saxon model with parameters adjusted to heavy nuclei (Kowal, Skalski, and collaborators, various years). It builds on studies of high-K isomers in even-even superheavy nuclei by Xu and collaborators, and on the known 6.0 ms isomer in 270Ds (Hofmann and collaborators). The predictions suggest that high-K ground states in odd-odd nuclei like 272Mt could be produced but missed by current detection procedures tuned to millisecond-scale coincidences, and that experimental searches should check for longer-lived superheavy configurations.
