# Oscillations in finite Fermi systems

> A semiclassical linear response theory based on the Vlasov equation is reviewed. The approach discussed here differs from the classical one of Vlasov and Landau for the fact that the finite size of the system is explicitly taken into account. The non-trivial problem of deciding which boundary conditions are more appropriate for the fluctuations of the phase-space density has been circumvented by studying solutions corresponding to different boundary conditions (fixed and moving surface). The fixed-surface theory has been applied to systems having both spherical and spheroidal equilibrium shapes. The moving-surface theory is related to the liquid-drop model of the nucleus and from it one can obtain a kinetic-theory description of surface and compression modes in nuclei. Quantum corrections to the semiclassical theory are also briefly discussed.

## Key claim

This paper reviews a semiclassical linear response theory based on the Vlasov equation that explicitly accounts for the finite size of the system, circumventing the problem of choosing appropriate boundary conditions by studying solutions for both fixed and moving surfaces. The approach is presented as a framework for describing giant resonances in nuclei and surface plasmons in atomic clusters, but the authors note that the existence of plasmon-like excitations in heavy atoms was not confirmed by their calculations.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Vlasov kinetic equation for small-amplitude density oscillations in finite Fermi systems, using action-angle variables for integrable systems and a multipole expansion for spherical and spheroidal equilibrium shapes. For the moving-surface case, they modify the boundary conditions to allow the sharp nuclear surface to vibrate according to the liquid-drop model, then derive an approximate expression for the collective surface coordinates by relating the surface pressure from kinetic theory to the restoring force parameter of the liquid-drop model.

## Key numbers

- For spherical square-well potentials, the nuclear radius is approximated as \(R \approx 1.2 A^{1/3} \, \text{fm}\)
- The surface-tension parameter is \(\sigma \approx 1 \, \text{MeV fm}^{-2}\)
- The restoring force parameter for surface vibrations (neglecting Coulomb repulsion) is \(C_L = \sigma R^2 (L-1)(L+2)\)
- The Mie frequency \(\omega_{Mie}\) is used as the unit for dipole strength functions in deformed sodium clusters, with deformation parameter \(\eta\) defined as the ratio between the larger and smaller radius of the spheroid
- For oblate deformed clusters, the formalism predicts three surface plasmon peaks instead of two for deformations \(\eta = 1.25\) and \(\eta = 1.5\)

## Context

This work extends the Vlasov-Landau approach, originally developed for infinite homogeneous systems by Vlasov (1938) and Landau (1946), to finite Fermi systems by incorporating surface effects. It builds on the earlier work of Kirzhnitz et al. (1975) for atomic systems and Bertsch (1977) for nuclear giant resonances, while also relating to the liquid-drop model of Bohr (1975). The formalism enables a kinetic-theory description of surface and compression modes in nuclei and can be applied to study collective excitations in atomic clusters, with the prediction of three-peak structures in oblate deformed clusters awaiting experimental verification.
