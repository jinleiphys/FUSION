# Bernoulli potential in type-I and weak type-II superconductors: III. Electrostatic potential above the vortex lattice

> The electrostatic potential above the Abrikosov vortex lattice, discussed earlier by Blatter {\em et al.} {[}PRL {\bf 77}, 566 (1996){]}, is evaluated within the Ginzburg-Landau theory. Unlike previous studies we include the surface dipole. Close to the critical temperature, the surface dipole reduces the electrostatic potential to values below a sensitivity of recent sensors. At low temperatures the surface dipole is less effective and the electrostatic potential remains observable as predicted earlier.

## Key claim

The electrostatic potential above an Abrikosov vortex lattice is strongly reduced by the surface dipole near the critical temperature, making it unobservable with current sensors; at low temperatures the surface dipole is less effective and the potential remains observable as predicted earlier by Blatter et al. (1996).

## Method

The authors use Ginzburg-Landau (GL) theory to evaluate the electrostatic (Bernoulli) potential at the surface of a thin superconducting film containing an Abrikosov vortex lattice, including the surface dipole contribution derived from the free energy per electron. The GL equation is solved numerically for the wave function, and the surface potential is obtained from the relation eφ₀ = (1/2n)β|ψ|⁴. The potential above the surface is then constructed via Fourier decomposition of the surface potential, with the mean value and spatial modulation computed for the triangular vortex lattice.

## Key numbers

- For Niobium, the condensation energy per particle is ε_con/n = 4.59 μeV.
- The upper estimate of the surface potential amplitude near T_c is eφ₀ ≤ (ε_con/n) × 4 × (1−t)², where t = T/T_c.
- For Niobium, the material parameter (1/2)γT_c(∂T_c/∂n) = 6.78 μeV, corresponding to ∂ln T_c/∂ln n = 0.74.
- At T = 0.95 T_c with κ = 1.5 and B = 0.06 B_c2, the vortex cores are well separated and the superconducting fraction between them reaches its non-magnetic value with less than 1% difference.
- For the triangular lattice, the Abrikosov coefficient β_A = 1.16.
- The mean surface potential near B_c2 is ⟨eφ₀⟩ = (ε_con/n)(1−t²)²(1/β_A)(1−b)², where b = B/B_c2.
- For pure Niobium (κ = 0.78) at T = 0.95 T_c and B = 0.7818 B_c2, the superconducting fraction is suppressed to less than 1/3 of its non-magnetic value.

## Context

This work extends the earlier proposal by Blatter et al. (1996) to observe the vortex lattice via the electrostatic potential, by including the surface dipole that was previously neglected. The surface dipole was shown experimentally by Morris and Brown (1971) to cancel pairing-force contributions in the Meissner state. The present theory predicts that the surface potential scales as |ψ|⁴ rather than |ψ|², leading to different temperature and magnetic field dependencies that can be tested experimentally. The results enable experimental design for scanning force microscopy or Kelvin probe measurements on conventional superconductors, with the surface dipole providing a distinct signature through its quadratic suppression near T_c and B_c2.
