# Present status of nuclear cluster physics and experimental perspectives

> Knowledge on nuclear cluster physics has increased considerably as nuclear clustering remains one of the most fruitful domains of nuclear physics, facing some of the greatest challenges and opportunities in the years ahead. The occurrence of "exotic" shapes in light N=Z alpha-like nuclei and the evolution of clustering from stability to the drip-lines are being investigated more and more accurately both theoretically and experimentally. Experimental progresses in understanding these questions were recently examined and will be further revisited in this introductory talk: clustering aspects are, in particular, discussed for light exotic nuclei with a large neutron excess such as neutron-rich Oxygen isotopes with their complete spectrocopy.

## Key claim

Nuclear clustering remains a fruitful domain of nuclear physics, but the structure of key states such as the "Hoyle" state in \(^{12}\)C remains controversial because experimental results on its direct three-alpha decay are in disagreement, and the existence of a 4\(\alpha\)-chain state in \(^{16}\)O has not been confirmed by recent phase-shift analysis.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental and theoretical progress in nuclear cluster physics, focusing on \(\alpha\)-like \(N=Z\) nuclei and neutron-rich oxygen isotopes. It discusses results from \(\gamma\)-ray spectroscopy, charged-particle reactions (e.g., \(^{12}\)C(\(\gamma\),\(\alpha\))\(^8\)Be at the HIGS facility, \(^{12}\)C(\(\alpha\),\(\alpha\))\(^{12}\)C\(^*\) at the Birmingham cyclotron), and binary reaction measurements with the Binary Reaction Spectrometer at the Vivitron Tandem facility. Rotational band structures are analyzed via excitation-energy systematics as a function of \(J(J+1)\) and compared with Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) and generator-coordinate-method calculations.

## Key numbers

- The 2\(^{+}_{2}\) "Hoyle" rotational excitation in \(^{12}\)C appears at around 10 MeV, consistent with an L=2 angular distribution pattern from the \(^{12}\)C(\(\gamma\),\(\alpha\))\(^8\)Be reaction at the HIGS facility.
- A predicted 0\(^{+}_{6}\) state in \(^{16}\)O, analogous to the "Hoyle" state, lies at about 15.1 MeV, approximately 700 keV above the 4\(\alpha\)-particle breakup threshold.
- Superdeformed shapes in light nuclei correspond to a quadrupole deformation parameter \(\beta_2 \approx 0.6\) (major-to-minor axis ratio 2:1); hyperdeformed shapes correspond to \(\beta_2 \approx 1.0\) (3:1 ratio).
- In \(^{20}\)O, the first doublet (K=0\(^{\pm}_{2}\)) has a slightly larger moment of inertia (smaller slope parameter) than in \(^{18}\)O, consistent with \(^{14}\)C–\(^{6}\)He or \(^{16}\)C–\(^{4}\)He molecular structures starting well below thresholds of 16.8 MeV and 12.32 MeV, respectively.

## Context

This review extends the classic Ikeda-Diagram (Ikeda, 1968) for \(N=Z\) nuclei and its extension by von Oertzen (2001) for neutron-rich nuclei, now further including \(^{14}\)C cluster cores alongside \(^{16}\)O cores. It discusses connections between \(\alpha\)-clustering, quasimolecular resonances, and extreme deformations (superdeformation and hyperdeformation) in light to medium-mass nuclei, as investigated by groups such as Itoh (2004), Zimmerman (2013), and Marin (2014). The work enables future searches for Bose-Einstein condensate states in \(^{16}\)O and heavier \(\alpha\)-conjugate nuclei, and motivates combined \(\gamma\)-ray and charged-particle spectroscopy to study hyperdeformed states that decay by particle emission.
