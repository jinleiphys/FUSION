# The Virial Equation of State of Low-Density Neutron Matter

> We present a model-independent description of low-density neutron matter based on the virial expansion. The virial equation of state provides a benchmark for all nuclear equations of state at densities and temperatures where the interparticle separation is large compared to the thermal wavelength. We calculate the second virial coefficient directly from the nucleon-nucleon scattering phase shifts. Our results for the pressure, energy, entropy and the free energy correctly include the physics of the large neutron-neutron scattering length. We find that, as in the universal regime, thermodynamic properties of neutron matter scale over a wide range of temperatures, but with a significantly reduced interaction coefficient compared to the unitary limit.

## Key claim

The virial equation of state of low-density neutron matter scales over a wide range of temperatures, similar to the universal regime, but with a second virial coefficient \(b_n \approx 0.31\) that is reduced by 40% compared to the unitary limit value of 0.53, due to the combined effects of the effective range and higher partial waves.

## Method

The authors calculate the second virial coefficient \(b_n\) directly from the isospin-triplet nucleon-nucleon scattering phase shifts obtained from the Nijmegen partial wave analysis, including all partial waves with \(L \leq 6\). The virial expansion is truncated at second order in the fugacity \(z = e^{\mu/T}\), and the pressure, energy, entropy, and free energy are derived from the virial equation of state using thermodynamic relations. The range of validity is set by the fugacity \(z \lesssim 0.5\), corresponding to densities \(n \lesssim 4 \cdot 10^{11} \, (T/\text{MeV})^{3/2} \, \text{g/cm}^3\).

## Key numbers

- Second virial coefficient for neutron matter: \(b_n \approx 0.31\) (temperature-independent to within a few percent over 1–24 MeV), compared to the unitary limit \(b_2 = 3/2^{5/2} = 0.53\).
- Neutron-neutron scattering length: \(a_{nn} = -18.5 \pm 0.3 \, \text{fm}\); effective range: \(r_{nn} = 2.7 \, \text{fm}\).
- Universal factor from cold atom experiments: \(\xi = 0.51 \pm 0.04\) (Thomas2, 2002), \(\xi = 0.44 \pm 0.01\) (Carlson, fixed-node GFMC), \(\xi = 0.42 \pm 0.01\) (Astra, fixed-node GFMC).
- Density range for virial validity: \(n \lesssim 4 \cdot 10^{11} \, (T/\text{MeV})^{3/2} \, \text{g/cm}^3\); for \(T = 4 \, \text{MeV}\), this is \(n \sim 10^{11} \, \text{g/cm}^3 \sim 1/1000 \, n_0\) where \(n_0 = 0.16 \, \text{fm}^{-3}\).
- Charge-independence breaking (CIB) reduces \(b_n\) by about 10% for \(T < 5 \, \text{MeV}\) (using \(a_{nn} = -18.5 \, \text{fm}\) instead of \(a_{np} = -23.768 \, \text{fm}\)).
- Entropy per particle at the validity boundary: \(S/N \gtrsim 2.9\) for \(b_n = 0.31\) and \(z \lesssim 0.5\).

## Context

This work extends the virial expansion approach previously applied to low-density nuclear matter (Horowitz and Schwenk, 2004) to pure neutron matter, providing a model-independent benchmark for all nuclear equations of state at low densities. The results are compared to the Fermi hyper-netted-chain (FHNC) calculations of Friedman and Pandharipande (1981), finding very good agreement, in contrast to the authors' earlier findings for nuclear matter where clustering caused disagreement. The virial equation of state enables constraints on the neutrinosphere in supernovae and provides a check for lattice simulations of neutron matter using effective field theory (Dean, 2005).
