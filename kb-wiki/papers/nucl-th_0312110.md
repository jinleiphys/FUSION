# Proton-tetraneutron elastic scattering

> We analyze the elastic scattering of protons on a 4n system. This was used as part of the detection technique of a recent experiment [1] to search for the 4n (tetraneutron) as a bound particle. We show that it is unlikely that this process alone could yield the events reported in ref. [1], unless the 4n has an anomalously large backward elastic scattering amplitude.

## Key claim

The authors show that elastic proton-tetraneutron scattering is unlikely to explain the events reported in Marques et al. (2002) as evidence for a bound tetraneutron, unless the tetraneutron has an anomalously large backward elastic scattering amplitude.

## Method

The authors calculate the proton-tetraneutron elastic scattering cross section using the M3Y interaction to construct a real optical potential, with the imaginary part taken as equal in magnitude to the real part. They assume the tetraneutron has a one-body density distribution with rms radii of 7 fm and 10 fm to simulate its loosely-bound character, and compute the cross section at a proton energy of 14 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For proton recoil energies in the interval \(E_p/E_n = 1.4\)–2.6, the total elastic cross section is about 3.5 \(\mu\)b assuming a tetraneutron rms radius of 7 fm; a 10 fm radius yields a 10 times smaller value.
- With a \(^{14}\)Be beam intensity of 130 particles per second, a tetraneutron production cross section of 1 mb, and a carbon target of 275 mg/cm\(^2\), the estimated tetraneutron rate is \(1.8 \times 10^{-2}\) per second.
- For a proton target density of \(10^{24}\) /cm\(^2\) and a cross section of 3.5 \(\mu\)b, the counting rate is \(10^{-9}\) scattered protons per second; with 10% detector efficiency this yields approximately \(10^{-4}\) events per week, compared to the 6 events claimed in Marques et al. (2002) in a one-week run.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical discussion of the tetraneutron, following prior studies by Timofeyuk (2002) and Bertulani and Zelevinsky (2003) that argued against a bound tetraneutron, and Pieper (2003) who predicted a loosely-bound system with large rms radii. The authors directly challenge the experimental interpretation of Marques et al. (2002), showing that elastic proton-tetraneutron scattering cannot account for the observed events, and suggest that other processes or an anomalously large backward scattering amplitude would be required to sustain the bound tetraneutron interpretation.
