# Tensor-decomposition techniques for ab initio nuclear structure calculations. From chiral nuclear potentials to ground-state energies

> The impact of applying state-of-the-art tensor factorization techniques to modern nuclear Hamiltonians derived from chiral effective field theory is investigated. Subsequently, the error induced by the tensor decomposition of the input Hamiltonian on ground-state energies of closed-shell nuclei calculated via second-order many-body perturbation theory is benchmarked. With the aid of the factorized Hamiltonian, the second-order perturbative correction to ground-state energies is decomposed and the scaling properties of the underlying tensor network are discussed. The employed tensor formats are found to lead to an efficient data compression of two-body matrix elements of the nuclear Hamiltonian. In particular, the sophisticated \emph{tensor hypercontraction} (THC) scheme yields low tensor ranks with respect to both harmonic-oscillator and Hartree-Fock single-particle bases. It is found that the tensor rank depends on the two-body total angular momentum $J$ for which one performs the decomposition, which is itself directly related to the sparsity the corresponding tensor. Furthermore, including normal-ordered two-body contributions originating from three-body interactions does not compromise the efficient data compression. Ultimately, the use of factorized matrix elements authorizes controlled approximations of the exact second-order ground-state energy corrections. In particular, a small enough error is obtained from low-rank factorizations in $^{4}$He, $^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca.

## Key claim

Tensor hypercontraction (THC) and canonical polyadic decomposition (CPD) can efficiently compress the two-body matrix elements of chiral nuclear Hamiltonians, with the compression quality depending on the two-body total angular momentum J and the associated sparsity of the tensor; the error induced by low-rank factorization on second-order ground-state energy corrections is small enough for 4He, 16O, and 40Ca.

## Method

The authors applied CPD and THC tensor factorization techniques to the two-body interaction tensor of a chiral effective field theory Hamiltonian (2N at N3LO with cutoff 500 MeV, 3N at N2LO with cutoff 400 MeV, softened via SRG transformation) represented in a JT-coupled harmonic-oscillator basis with emax=4. They benchmarked the relative approximation error of the factorized Hamiltonian as a function of tensor rank for different (J,T,MT) blocks, and then used the factorized Hamiltonian to compute second-order many-body perturbation theory (MBPT2) ground-state energy corrections for closed-shell nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For the worst-case J=2 channel, reaching a relative error of ΔV = 10⁻¹ in the CPD format required a rank r_CPD ≈ N²·⁵, where N=15 is the one-body index size in the emax=4 model space.
- For the THC format, a relative error below ε ≈ 10⁻² was achieved with rank r_THC = N¹·⁸ in the worst-case scenario, and for the highest J values the same accuracy required only r_THC = N¹·³.
- The THC ranks corresponding to ε = 10⁻² remained below the critical value of N² for all channels, ensuring proper data compression.
- The SVD preprocessing step for the natural index grouping showed that for J=4, the maximum singular value index K_max reached about one third of the matrix size, while for other J values it dropped to one tenth.

## Context

This work extends tensor decomposition techniques previously applied to the electron repulsion tensor in quantum chemistry (e.g., Hohenstein et al. 2012, Parrish et al. 2013, Schutski et al. 2017) to the nuclear many-body problem, where the Hamiltonian includes both two-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions from chiral effective field theory. The results demonstrate that factorized Hamiltonians can enable controlled approximations in ab initio nuclear structure calculations, potentially reducing storage and computational scaling for methods such as coupled cluster theory and in-medium similarity renormalization group, and may eventually allow extension to heavier nuclei and open-shell systems.
