# Recent progress on understanding "pasta" phases in dense stars

> In cores of supernovae and crusts of neutron stars, nuclei can adopt interesting shapes, such as rods or slabs, etc., which are referred to as nuclear "pasta." Recently, we have been studying the pasta phases focusing on their dynamical aspects with quantum molecular dynamic (QMD) approach. We review our findings on the following topics: dynamical formation of the pasta phases by cooling down the hot uniform nuclear matter; a phase diagram on the density versus temperature plane; structural transitions between the pasta phases induced by compression and their mechanism. Properties of the nuclear interaction used in our works are also discussed.

## Key claim

The authors use quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations to show that nuclear pasta phases (rodlike, slablike, and bubble structures) can form dynamically from hot uniform matter within a finite time scale of order \(10^{3}-10^{4}\) fm/\(c\), and that structural transitions between pasta phases can occur under compression, both of which had been uncertain in earlier equilibrium studies.

## Method

The authors performed QMD simulations of nucleon many-body systems in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using a model Hamiltonian that includes Skyrme, Pauli, symmetry, momentum-dependent, and Coulomb potentials. They cooled hot uniform nuclear matter from \(T \sim 20\) MeV to \(\sim 0.1\) MeV or less over \(10^{3}-10^{4}\) fm/\(c\) to form pasta phases, and separately adiabatically compressed samples of rodlike and slablike nuclei at rates of \(\simeq 1.3\times 10^{-5} \rho_0\)/(fm/\(c\)) and \(\simeq 7.1\times 10^{-6} \rho_0\)/(fm/\(c\)) to induce structural transitions, classifying nuclear shapes using the integral mean curvature and Euler characteristic of the nuclear surface.

## Key numbers

* The normal nuclear density used in the model is \(\rho_0 = 0.165\) fm\(^{-3}\), with a binding energy of 16 MeV per nucleon for symmetric nuclear matter, incompressibility 280 MeV, and symmetry energy 34.6 MeV.
* The exchange energy between two identical nucleons bound in different nuclei is estimated as \(\sim 5 \times 10^{-6}\) MeV for an internuclear distance \(R \simeq 10\) fm and binding energy \(E_i \simeq 8\) MeV, which is much smaller than the typical energy difference per nucleon between pasta phases of order 0.1 keV (neutron star matter) to 10 keV (supernova matter).
* The transition from rodlike to slablike nuclei occurred at a final density of \(0.405 \rho_0\) with a final temperature of \(\simeq 1.35\) MeV; the transition from slablike nuclei to cylindrical holes occurred at a final density of \(0.490 \rho_0\) with a final temperature of \(\simeq 1.3\) MeV.
* The compression rates used were \(\simeq 1.3\times 10^{-5} \rho_0\)/(fm/\(c\)) for the columnar phase and \(\simeq 7.1\times 10^{-6} \rho_0\)/(fm/\(c\)) for the laminar phase, with the connecting stage of each transition taking \(\simeq 3000\)–4000 fm/\(c\) and the relaxation stage taking more than 8000 fm/\(c\).
* The transition from spherical nuclei to rodlike nuclei was simulated with a compression rate of \(\simeq 4.4\times 10^{-6} \rho_0\)/(fm/\(c\)) and completed within \(\simeq 2500\) fm/\(c\).

## Context

This work extends earlier equilibrium studies of pasta phases by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Hashimoto et al. (1984), which used liquid drop models or Thomas-Fermi approximations with assumed nuclear shapes, and by Lassaut et al. (1987) for finite temperatures. The QMD approach allows the authors to address dynamical formation and transitions without assuming nuclear shapes, and the results suggest that pasta phases could significantly increase neutrino opacity in supernova cores, as pointed out by Horowitz et al. (2004) and others, potentially affecting shock revival mechanisms.
