# Non-perturbative quarkonium dissociation rates in strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma

> Heavy quarks and quarkonia are versatile probes of the transport properties of the hot QCD medium produced in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions (URHICs). A robust description of heavy-flavor transport coefficients requires a microscopic approach that treats the open and hidden heavy-flavor sectors on the same footing. Here, we employ the quantum many-body $T$-matrix formalism to evaluate the dissociation rates of heavy quarkonia in the quark-gluon plasma (QGP). The basic ingredient is the heavy-light $T$-matrix, which utilizes a nonperturbative driving kernel constrained by lattice-QCD data. Its resummation in a ladder series provides a much enhanced interaction strength compared to previously used perturbative coupling to the quasiparticle partons in the QGP. The in-medium quarkonium properties, particularly their temperature-dependent binding energies, are evaluated self-consistently using the same interaction kernel, including interference effects (also referred to as the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential) as well as off-shell parton spectral functions. We systematically investigate the interplay of these effects and elaborate on the connections to the dipole approximation used in effective field theory.

## Key claim

The authors use a non-perturbative quantum many-body T-matrix formalism to compute quarkonium dissociation rates, finding that the interaction strength is much enhanced compared to previously used perturbative couplings, with the specific numerical results depending on which lattice-QCD constraint (HQ free energies or Wilson line correlators) is used to fix the in-medium potential.

## Method

The authors solve a three-dimensional Bethe-Salpeter equation reduced from the four-dimensional form using the suppression of energy transfer in heavy-particle scattering, with a partial-wave expansion yielding a one-dimensional integral equation for the T-matrix. The heavy-light T-matrices are computed using a nonperturbative driving kernel (a screened in-medium potential with color-Coulomb and confining string terms) constrained by fitting to lattice-QCD data for HQ free energies and Wilson line correlators, and the single-particle spectral functions are evaluated self-consistently by closing the T-matrices with thermal parton propagators. The quarkonium dissociation rates are then computed from these heavy-light scattering amplitudes, including off-shell parton spectral functions and interference effects (the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential), with comparisons to the quasifree approximation and the dipole expansion.

## Key numbers

- In the strongly coupled scenario (SCS), the collisional widths of heavy quarks exceed 0.5 GeV at low momenta
- The screening mass of the string interaction in the SCS is about a factor of 2-3 lower than the screening mass of the color-Coulomb potential
- Bare charm and bottom quark masses in the SCS are 1.264 GeV and 4.662 GeV, respectively
- Bare charm and bottom quark masses in the WLC scenario are 1.359 GeV and 4.681 GeV, respectively
- The WLC-constrained potential is comparable to the SCS at low temperatures but much stronger at higher temperatures, with the screening mass of the confining potential essentially constant with temperature

## Context

This work extends the T-matrix approach previously applied to open heavy-flavor transport (Rapp 2018) and quarkonium spectroscopy (Liu 2017, Tang 2023) by systematically computing inelastic dissociation rates for both charmonia and bottomonia at finite three-momentum. The non-perturbative treatment addresses the need for enhanced dissociation rates compared to perturbative couplings, which phenomenological studies (Du 2015, Du 2019) found required multiplicative factors of K ~ 3-5 to match experimental suppression of psi(2S) and bottomonium states. The results enable more realistic transport simulations of quarkonium production in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions and provide a microscopic connection to the dipole approximation used in effective field theory approaches.
