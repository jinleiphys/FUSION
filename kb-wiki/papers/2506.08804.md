# Modern approach to muonic x-ray spectroscopy demonstrated through the measurement of stable Cl radii

> Recent advances in muonic x-ray experiments have reinvigorated efforts in measurements of absolute nuclear charge radii. Here, a modern approach is presented, and demonstrated through determination of the charge radii of the two stable chlorine nuclides $^{35}$Cl and $^{37}$Cl. Knowledge of these radii has implications for fundamental studies in nuclear and atomic physics. For this purpose, a state-of-the-art experiment was performed at the $π$E1 beamline in the Paul Scherrer Institute (Switzerland), using a large-scale HPGe detector array in order to extract precise energies of the muonic $^{35}$Cl and $^{37}$Cl $np1s$ transitions. The nuclear charge radius extraction relies on modern calculations for QED effects and nuclear polarization with rigorous uncertainty quantification, including effects that were not accounted for in older studies. Additionally, we established a new method for applying the nuclear shape correction directly from energy density functionals, which are amenable to isotopes for which no high-quality electron scattering experiments are available. The resulting charge radii are $3.3335(23) fm$ for $^{35}$Cl and $3.3445(23) fm$ for $^{37}$Cl, thus improving the uncertainty of the available electron scattering values by a factor of seven. The correlation of several observables was evaluated between the different isotopes in order to produce a more precise value of the differential mean square charge radius $δ\langle r^2 \rangle^{37, 35}=+0.0771(66) fm^{2}$. In this case, improvement of the uncertainty by more than one order of magnitude was achieved compared to the literature value. This precision is sufficient to use this differential as input for isotope shift factor determination.

## Key claim

The authors present a modern approach to muonic x-ray spectroscopy that improves the precision of absolute nuclear charge radii for stable chlorine isotopes by a factor of seven compared to previous electron scattering values, while also establishing a new method for nuclear shape correction using energy density functionals that can be applied to isotopes lacking high-quality electron scattering data.

## Method

Muonic x-ray spectra were measured at the πE1 beamline of the Paul Scherrer Institute using a large array of HPGe detectors (14 detectors, 19 crystals) with digital data acquisition, targeting enriched 35Cl (as NaCl) and 37Cl (as AgCl) samples. The transition energies of the muonic np1s transitions were extracted using prompt time cuts and local energy calibration from radioactive sources (133Ba, 110mAg, 60Co). The charge radii were then derived by combining these experimental energies with modern calculations of QED effects (vacuum polarization, self-energy, nuclear recoil) and nuclear polarization, using the Barrett radius formalism and converting to RMS radii via a nuclear shape correction obtained from Brussels-Skyrme-on-a-grid (BSkG) energy density functional calculations.

## Key numbers

- Charge radius of 35Cl: 3.3335(23) fm, improving uncertainty of available electron scattering values by a factor of seven
- Charge radius of 37Cl: 3.3445(23) fm, improving uncertainty of available electron scattering values by a factor of seven
- Differential mean square charge radius δ⟨r²⟩^{37,35} = +0.0771(66) fm², improving uncertainty by more than one order of magnitude compared to literature
- Muon beam momentum optimized at 32 MeV/c for 35Cl and 30 MeV/c for 37Cl
- HPGe detector array achieved ~3% total detection efficiency at 1332 keV, energy resolution (FWHM) ~2.6 keV at 1332 keV, and time resolution (FWHM) ~14 ns at approximately 700 keV
- Measurement times: ~5.5 hours for 35Cl, ~8.5 hours for 37Cl

## Context

This work revives the practice of muonic x-ray spectroscopy for absolute charge radius determination, which had largely faded after the compilations of Angeli and Marinova (2013) and Fricke and Heilig (2004), with the last measurements dating from 1993. The approach improves upon the recent work on stable Pd isotopes by Saito et al. (2025) by including rigorous uncertainty quantification for QED and nuclear polarization effects and by introducing a nuclear shape correction from energy density functionals rather than relying solely on standard Fermi distributions. The improved chlorine radii serve as critical benchmarks for future laser spectroscopy of exotic chlorine isotopes, support studies of the Vud element of the CKM matrix via superallowed beta decays in 34Cl, and provide input for investigations of the symmetry energy slope in the equation of state of nuclear matter through mirror nuclei analysis.
