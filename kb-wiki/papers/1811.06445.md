# Can generalized TOV equations explain neutron star macroscopic properties?

> In the quest for describing massive neutron stars with radii compatible with recent expectations, we take a different approach in the present paper and, instead of tuning the equations of state, we use an appropriate parameterization of the TOV equations that result in increasing the maximum mass and decreasing the respective radius and consequently, also the radius of canonical stars.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that modifying the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations with free parameters, rather than tuning the equation of state, can simultaneously produce neutron stars with high maximum masses and small radii, potentially reconciling observations of massive pulsars with recent radius constraints. The authors hedge that this approach is a "more standard" alternative to braneworld scenarios, but the parameters themselves lack direct physical justification beyond being fitted to reproduce desired mass-radius relations.

## Method

The authors introduce a parameterized version of the TOV equations (PTOV) with five free parameters (α, β, γ, χ, σ) that modify the gravitational coupling, inertial pressure, self-gravity pressure, curvature term, and mass function respectively, following the formalism of Velten (2016). They then propose a further generalization (GTOV) that adds a density-dependent correction term Γ to the mass continuity equation. These modified equations are solved using hadronic equations of state from the GM1 and IU-FSU relativistic mean-field parameterizations (with and without hyperons) and the MIT bag model for quark matter, producing mass-radius relations for neutron stars and quark stars.

## Key numbers

- For hadronic stars with the GM1 parameterization and GTOV with Γ=4.0, the maximum mass is 2.02 M⊙ with radius 11.46 km, and the canonical 1.4 M⊙ star has radius 11.78 km
- For hadronic stars with the IU-FSU parameterization and GTOV with Γ=4.0, the maximum mass is 2.05 M⊙ with radius 11.42 km, and the canonical 1.4 M⊙ star has radius 11.72 km
- For quark stars with the MIT bag model (B=158 MeV/fm³) and GTOV with Γ=4.0, the maximum mass is 2.01 M⊙ with radius 10.83 km, and the canonical 1.4 M⊙ star has radius 11.12 km
- The best-fit value for the α parameter was found to be α=1.3, tested over a range from -2.0 to 2.0
- The best-fit value for the σ parameter was found to be σ=1/6, tested over a range from 0 to 3.0
- The Γ parameter was varied from 0 to 4.0, with larger values producing smaller radii and maximum masses

## Context

This work extends the parameterized TOV approach of Velten (2016) by adding a new correction term to the mass continuity equation, and contrasts with the braneworld approach of Benito (2014) that required solving four coupled equations with two extra parameters. The paper addresses the tension between the discovery of massive neutron stars PSR J1614-2230 (1.97±0.04 M⊙) and PSR J0348+0432 (2.01±0.04 M⊙) and radius constraints from Bayesian analyses (9.7-13.9 km from Hebeler (2013), 10.4-12.9 km from Steiner (2013), 10.1-11.1 km from Ozel (2016)). The results enable future work to constrain the Γ parameter using gravitational wave observations such as GW170817, which provided an upper limit on neutron star masses of 2.2-2.3 M⊙.
