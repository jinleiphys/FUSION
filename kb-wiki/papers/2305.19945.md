# Transfer reactions of exotic nuclei including core deformations: $^{11}$Be and $^{17}$C

> Background: Reactions with halo nuclei from deformed regions exhibit important deviations from the inert core+valence picture. Structure and reaction formalisms have recently been extended or adapted to explore the possibility of exciting the underlying core. Purpose: We will study up to what extent transfer reactions involving halo nuclei $^{11}$Be and $^{17}$C can be reproduced with two different models that have previously shown a good success reproducing the role of the core in light halo nuclei. Methods: We focus on the structure of $^{11}$Be and $^{17}$C with two core+valence models: Nilsson and a semi-microscopic particle-rotor model using Antisymmetrized Molecular Dinamic calculations of the cores. These models are later used to study $^{16}$C(d,p)$^{17}$C and $^{11}$Be(p,d)$^{10}$Be transfer reactions within the Adiabatic Distorted Wave Approximation. Results are compared with three different experimental data sets. Results: A good reproduction of both the structure and transfer reactions of $^{10}$Be and $^{17}$C is found. The Nilsson model provides an overall better agreement for the spectrum and reactions involving $^{17}$C while the semi-microscopic model is more adequate for $^{11}$Be, as expected, since the $^{17}$C core is closer to an ideal rotor. Conclusions: Both models show promising results for the study of transfer reactions with halo nuclei. We expect that including microscopic information in the Nilsson model, following the spirit of the semi-microscopic model, can provide a useful, yet simple framework for studying newly discovered halo nuclei.

## Key claim
The paper tests whether transfer reactions involving the halo nuclei 11Be and 17C can be reproduced by two core+valence models (Nilsson and semi-microscopic PAMD) that include core deformation, finding that the Nilsson model works better for 17C (whose core is closer to an ideal rotor) while the PAMD model is more adequate for 11Be.

## Method
The authors constructed two-body core+valence models for 11Be and 17C: a Nilsson model (strong-coupling limit) using a deformed Woods-Saxon potential with a pseudo-state method based on transformed harmonic oscillator (THO) functions, and a semi-microscopic particle-rotor model (PAMD) using Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) transition densities for the cores. These structure models were then used to compute transfer reaction cross sections for 16C(d,p)17C and 11Be(p,d)10Be within the Adiabatic Distorted Wave Approximation (ADWA), and results were compared with three experimental data sets.

## Key numbers
- For 17C: deformation parameter β = 0.34, central Woods-Saxon potential strength 44.27 MeV, spin-orbit strength 8.825 MeV, core moment of inertia ℏ/2J = 0.3 MeV (compatible with 16C 2+ excitation at 1.766 MeV)
- For 11Be: deformation parameter β = 0.67, central potential strengths 52.43 MeV (positive parity) and 49.62 MeV (negative parity), spin-orbit strength 8.5 MeV, core moment of inertia ℏ/2J = 0.56 MeV (compatible with 10Be 2+ excitation at 3.368 MeV)
- Mean square radius of 17C first excited state: 6.55 fm (Nilsson) and 5.24 fm (PAMD), versus ~4 fm for the ground state in both models
- Spectroscopic factor for 17C 1/2+1 state |s1/2⊗0+⟩ configuration: 0.67 (Nilsson), 0.51 (PAMD), compared to experimental 0.80±0.22
- Spectroscopic factor for 17C 5/2+1 state |d5/2⊗0+⟩ configuration: 0.33 (Nilsson), 0.32 (PAMD), compared to experimental 0.62±0.13
- 16C(d,p)17C reaction measured at 17.2 MeV/nucleon (GANIL)
- 11Be(p,d)10Be reaction measured at 26.9 MeV/nucleon (RCNP)

## Context
This work extends previous studies of core deformation effects in halo nuclei, building on the particle-rotor model applied by Moro (2012), Lay (2016), and others, and on the PAMD model introduced by Lay (2014) for 11Be and 19C. The Nilsson model had been applied to exotic nuclei by Hamamoto (2007) and by Maccormick (2017, 2018), but only to extract spectroscopic factors; this is the first application where deformation is included in the radial form factor of transfer reactions for exotic nuclei. The results enable future work incorporating microscopic information into the Nilsson model to create a simple framework for studying newly discovered halo nuclei.
