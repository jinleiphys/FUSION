# Experimental indication of anomalous sensitivity in many-body systems: Deterministic randomness in complex quantum collisions?

> We have experimentally tested a recently suggested possibility for anomalous sensitivity of the cross sections of dissipative heavy ion collisions. Cross sections for the $^{19}$F+$^{27}$Al dissipative collisions were measured at the fixed energy 118.75 MeV of the $^{19}$F for the 12 different beam spots on the same target foil. The data demonstrate dramatic differences between the cross sections for the different beam spots. The effect may indicate deterministic randomness in complex quantum collisions. New experiments are highly desirable in a view of the fundamental importance of the problem.

## Key claim

Cross sections for dissipative heavy ion collisions at a fixed beam energy show dramatic, statistically significant non-reproducibility when measured at 12 different beam spots on the same target foil, which may indicate deterministic randomness in complex quantum collisions. The effect is interpreted as arising from an anomalous sensitivity to infinitesimally small perturbations of the intermediate complex, with effective perturbation strengths on the order of \(K \sim \pm 10^{-6} D\) (about \(10^{-7}\) eV), roughly 13 orders of magnitude smaller than typical nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Method

Differential cross sections for B, C, N, and O fragments produced in dissipative heavy ion collisions of \(^{19}\)F + \(^{27}\)Al were measured at a fixed beam energy of 118.75 MeV using two gas-solid \(\Delta\)E-E telescopes at laboratory angles of 57° and 31°. The target was a rectangular \(^{27}\)Al foil of average thickness \(\simeq 67\ \mu\)g/cm\(^2\); 12 different beam spots on the same foil were bombarded by moving the target in 2 mm steps perpendicular to the reaction plane, without opening the reaction chamber. Cross sections were normalized either by monitor counts or by target thickness and Faraday cup charge, and the 12 resulting relative cross sections for each fragment and angle were scaled so their average was unity.

## Key numbers

- The probability for deviations greater than three standard deviations from the average cross section was 50% for 5 of the 8 fragment-angle combinations, and 33.33%, 25%, and 8.33% for the remaining 3 cases; the corresponding probability for a Gaussian distribution is 0.27%.
- For cross sections summed over all fragments and angles, the probability for deviations greater than four standard deviations was 67%, compared to 0.006% expected from Gaussian statistics.
- Measured cross sections for different beam spots differed by up to a factor of 1.5–2.
- The intrinsic excitation energy of the intermediate complex was \(E^* \simeq 20\) MeV, with average level spacing \(D \simeq 10^{-7}\) MeV.
- The effective perturbation strength was estimated as \(K \sim \pm 10^{-6} D \simeq \pm 10^{-7}\) eV.
- The beam energy spread (45 keV) and energy loss in the target (180 keV) gave a total energy spread of about 225 keV, comparable to the characteristic energy length of excitation function oscillations.

## Context

This work experimentally tests a theoretical prediction by Kun (2004) of anomalous sensitivity of cross sections in dissipative heavy ion collisions to extremely small perturbations, and extends earlier measurements by Wang et al. (2005) that suggested non-reproducibility between independent experiments. The results contradict the conventional expectation that cross sections should be reproducible under nominally identical conditions, and call for new independent experiments at other tandem accelerators to confirm the effect, which if real would imply deterministic randomness in complex quantum many-body systems.
