# Effective Spectral Function for Quasielastic Scattering on Nuclei

> Spectral functions that are used in neutrino event generators to model quasielastic (QE) scattering from nuclear targets include Fermi gas, Local Thomas Fermi gas (LTF), Bodek-Ritchie Fermi gas with high momentum tail, and the Benhar-Fantoni two dimensional spectral function. We find that the $ν$ dependence of predictions of these spectral functions for the QE differential cross sections (${d^2σ}/{dQ^2 dν}$) are in disagreement with the prediction of the $ψ'$ superscaling function which is extracted from fits to quasielastic electron scattering data on nuclear targets. It is known that spectral functions do not fully describe quasielastic scattering because they only model the initial state. Final state interactions distort the shape of the differential cross section at the peak and increase the cross section at the tails of the distribution. We show that the kinematic distributions predicted by the $ψ'$ superscaling formalism can be well described with a modified {\it {effective spectral function}} (ESF). By construction, models using ESF in combination with the transverse enhancement contribution correctly predict electron QE scattering data.

# Effective Spectral Function for Quasielastic Scattering on Nuclei

## Key claim

The authors show that the kinematic distributions predicted by the ψ' superscaling formalism for quasielastic scattering can be well described by a modified effective spectral function (ESF), but only when combined with a Q²-dependent correction factor V(Q²) that accounts for final state interactions at low Q². The ESF is constructed to reproduce the ψ' superscaling predictions at Q² values of 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7 GeV², rather than from first-principles nuclear theory.

## Method

The authors parametrize a momentum distribution P(k) for nucleons in carbon using a functional form with three components (s-wave, p-wave, and tail) and define two scattering processes: 1p1h (one proton one hole) with probability f₁ₚ₁ₕ and 2p2h (two protons two holes) with probability 1−f₁ₚ₁ₕ. They fit the parameters of the effective spectral function (including the binding energy Δ, the fraction f₁ₚ₁ₕ, and the momentum distribution shape parameters) so that the predicted normalized differential cross section (1/σ)(dσ/dν)(Q²,ν) matches the ψ' superscaling function at four specific Q² values. The off-shell energy of bound nucleons is computed separately for the 1p1h and 2p2h processes, with a Q²-dependent factor V(Q²)=1−e^{−xQ²} (x=12.04 GeV⁻²) that reduces the removal energy at low Q².

## Key numbers

- The effective spectral function for carbon uses a binding energy parameter Δ = 12.5 MeV for the 1p1h process
- The fraction of scattering via the 1p1h process is f₁ₚ₁ₕ = 0.808; the 2p2h fraction is 0.192
- The Q²-dependent correction factor is V(Q²) = 1 − e^{−xQ²} with x = 12.04 GeV⁻²
- The ψ' superscaling function for carbon uses Fermi broadening parameter K_F = 0.228 GeV and energy shift E_shift = 0.0165 GeV (for nuclei with 7 < A < 17)
- The 2014 ψ' superscaling function is given by F(ψ') = 1.3429 / [K_F (1 + 1.7119²(ψ' + 0.19525)²)(1 + e^{−1.69 ψ'})]
- The ESF momentum distribution is normalized to 1.0 with normalization factor N = 29.64 for carbon
- The momentum distribution is defined for k < 0.65 GeV; for k > 0.65 GeV, P(k) = 0
- The Pauli suppression factor for low Q² is K_Pauli = (3/4)(|q|/K_F)(1 − (1/12)(|q|/K_F)²) for |q| < 2K_F

## Context

This work extends the Bodek-Ritchie Fermi gas model with high momentum tail (Bodek and Ritchie, prior work) and the Benhar-Fantoni two-dimensional spectral function (Benhar and Fantoni, prior work) by incorporating final state interaction effects that these spectral functions omit. The authors show that existing spectral function models (global Fermi gas, Local Thomas Fermi gas, Benhar-Fantoni) disagree with the ψ' superscaling formalism of Bosted and Mamyan (2012) and an updated fit (2014), which is directly extracted from electron scattering data. The effective spectral function enables neutrino Monte Carlo generators (such as GENIE, NEUT, NuWro, and GiBUU) to reproduce electron scattering data without requiring full implementation of the ψ' superscaling formalism, which does not provide detailed hadronic final state composition.
