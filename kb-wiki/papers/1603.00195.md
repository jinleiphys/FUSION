# A nonstiff solution for the stochastic neutron point kinetics equations

> We propose an approach to solve the stochastic neutron point kinetics equations using an adaptation of the diagonalization-decomposition method (DDM). This new approach (Double-DDM) yields a nonstiff solution for the stochastic formulation, allowing the calculation of the neutron and precursor densities at any time of interest without the need of using progressive time steps. We use Double-DDM to compute results for stochastic problems with constant, linear, and sinusoidal reactivities. We show that these results strongly agree with those obtained by other approaches established in the literature. We also compute and analyze the first four statistical moments of the solutions.

# A nonstiff solution for the stochastic neutron point kinetics equations

## Key claim
The Double-DDM (diagonalization-decomposition method) yields a nonstiff solution for the stochastic neutron point kinetics equations, allowing calculation of neutron and precursor densities at any time of interest without progressive time steps; however, convergence of DDM is yet to be proven, so a Lyapunov criterion is used to guarantee convergence.

## Method
The authors adapt the deterministic diagonalization-decomposition method (DDM) to solve the stochastic formulation of the neutron point kinetics equations. First, the deterministic problem is solved with DDM using Laplace transforms and a recursive scheme with Gauss-Legendre integration; then the deterministic solution is used to build the stochastic component matrix; finally, a second decomposition scheme analogous to DDM is applied to solve the full stochastic system. The total number of stochastic components is determined by the Central Limit Theorem to guarantee a statistical error smaller than 0.05% with 95% confidence.

## Key numbers
- For a step-reactivity insertion with one precursor group ($\rho(t)=-1/3$, $\Lambda=2/3$, $\beta=0.05$, $\lambda_1=0.1$, $q=200$, $n(0)=400$, $C_1(0)=300$): Double-DDM gives $E(n(2))=402.35$, $\sigma(n(2))=28.610$, $E(C_1(2))=305.84$, $\sigma(C_1(2))=7.9240$ with $K=1,000$ histories; skewness $\gamma_1(n(2))=-1\times 10^{-10}$ and excess kurtosis $\gamma_2(n(2))=3\times 10^{-11}$.
- For a subcritical step-reactivity insertion with six precursor groups ($\rho(t)=0.003$, $\Lambda=0.00002$, $\beta=7\times 10^{-3}$, $q=0$): Double-DDM gives $E(n(0.1))=187.05$, $\sigma(n(0.1))=167.83$, $E(C(0.1))=4.488\times 10^5$, $\sigma(C(0.1))=1475.6$ with $K=1,000$ histories; skewness $\gamma_1(n(0.1))=-1\times 10^{-7}$, excess kurtosis $\gamma_2(n(0.1))=1.3\times 10^{-9}$.
- For a prompt critical step-reactivity insertion ($\rho(t)=0.007$, same parameters): Double-DDM gives $E(n(0.001))=135.86$, $\sigma(n(0.001))=93.210$, $E(C(0.001))=4.463\times 10^5$, $\sigma(C(0.001))=17.845$ with $K=5,000$ histories; skewness $\gamma_1(n(0.001))=-1.02\times 10^{-7}$, excess kurtosis $\gamma_2(n(0.001))=1.15\times 10^{-8}$.
- For linear reactivity $\rho(t)=0.25t$ with six precursor groups ($\Lambda=0.00001$, $\beta=6.403\times 10^{-3}$): Double-DDM gives $n(1.0)=1.403561$ with $K=15,738$ histories; $\sigma(n(1))=0.978$, $\gamma_1(n(1))=-3.02\times 10^{-7}$, $\gamma_2(n(1))=-1\times 10^{-7}$.
- For linear reactivity $\rho(t)=0.5t$: Double-DDM gives $n(1.0)=2.263278$ with $K=27,523$ histories; $\sigma(n(1))=1.11345$, $\gamma_1(n(1))=-2.15\times 10^{-6}$, $\gamma_2(n(1))=-3\times 10^{-8}$.

## Context
This work extends the deterministic diagonalization-decomposition method (DDM) introduced by Wollmann da Silva (2014) to the stochastic neutron point kinetics equations derived by Hayes (2005). The stochastic formulation had previously been solved using Monte Carlo, stochastic piecewise constant approximation (Stochastic PCA) by Hayes (2005), and order 1.5 strong Taylor and Euler-Maruyama methods by Saha (2012), but those approaches remained stiff except for cases with zero or one precursor group. The Double-DDM enables nonstiff solutions for problems with constant, linear, and sinusoidal reactivities, and provides the first analysis of third and fourth statistical moments for these stochastic solutions. Future work requires identifying a reference scale to obtain moment results independent of sample size and frequency of stochastic fluctuations.
