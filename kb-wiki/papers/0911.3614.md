# Proton emission off nuclei induced by kaons in flight

> We study the (K-,p) reaction on nuclei with a 1 GeV/c momentum kaon beam, paying a special attention at the region of emitted protons having kinetic energy above 600 MeV, which was used to claim a deeply attractive kaon nucleus optical potential. Our model describes the nuclear reaction in the framework of a local density approach and the calculations are performed following two different procedures: one is based on a many-body method using the Lindhard function and the other one is based on a Monte Carlo simulation. While both procedures coincide when it comes to consider the contribution of kaon quasi-elastic scattering, the simulation method offers more flexibility since it allows us to account for other processes which also contribute to the proton spectra, such as K- absorption by one and two nucleons producing hyperons. The simulation also considers final state interactions in terms of multiple scattering of the K-, p and all other primary and secondary particles on their way out of the nucleus, as well as the weak decay of the produced hyperons into (pi N). We find that this kaon in-flight reaction is not well suited to determine the kaon optical potential due, essentially, to the limited sensitivity of the cross section to its strength, but also to unavoidable uncertainties in the contribution from other processes. We also simulate the experimental requirement of having, together with the energetic proton, at least one charged particle detected in the decay counter surrounding the target, and find that the shape of the original cross section is appreciably distorted. We conclude that the new mechanisms, not considered in the analysis of the original experiment, allow us to explain the observed spectrum with the shallow kaon nucleus optical potential obtained in chiral models.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the (K-,p) reaction on nuclei with a 1 GeV/c kaon beam is not well suited to determine the kaon-nucleus optical potential because the cross section has limited sensitivity to the potential strength, and because previously neglected reaction mechanisms (kaon absorption by one and two nucleons producing hyperons, plus final state interactions) can explain the observed proton spectrum using the shallow kaon-nucleus optical potential from chiral models, contrary to the original claim of a deeply attractive potential.

## Method
The authors model the (K-,p) reaction on 12C using two complementary approaches: a many-body method based on the Lindhard function for quasi-elastic K- p scattering, and a Monte Carlo simulation that additionally includes K- absorption by one nucleon (K- N → π Λ, π Σ) and two nucleons (K- NN → Λ N, Σ N), followed by weak hyperon decay into π N. The Monte Carlo simulation also accounts for final state interactions of all primary and secondary particles through multiple scattering, and implements a minimal coincidence requirement simulating the experimental condition of detecting at least one charged particle in a decay counter surrounding the target.

## Key numbers
- The elementary K- p → K- p backward differential cross section at 1 GeV/c is taken as 8.8 mb/sr from experimental data.
- The total K- p cross section is 51.7 mb, and the total K- n cross section is 38 mb, giving an average ⟨σtot K-N⟩ of 45 mb.
- The two-nucleon absorption rate is set to 20% of the one-body absorption rate at nuclear matter density, implemented via a coefficient Cabs ≈ 6 fm5.
- The kaon optical potential depths tested are -60 ρ/ρ0 MeV (chiral model), -200 ρ/ρ0 MeV, -400 ρ/ρ0 MeV, and -600 ρ/ρ0 MeV, all with an imaginary part of -60 ρ/ρ0 MeV.
- The distortion from incoming kaon and outgoing proton absorption reduces the nuclear cross section by about a factor of 3.5.

## Context
This work extends the debate on the depth of the K- -nucleus optical potential, challenging the claim of Kishimoto (2007) that the (K-,p) reaction on 12C at 1 GeV/c requires a deeply attractive potential of about 200 MeV. The authors show that previously neglected mechanisms, similar to those used to reinterpret KEK and FINUDA kaonic atom peaks (Magas 2006, 2008), can reproduce the data with the shallow potential (~60 MeV) from chiral dynamics (Lutz 1998, Ramos 2000, Tolos 2006). The paper enables a more complete analysis of in-flight kaon reactions by demonstrating that coincidence requirements significantly distort the observed spectrum, and that multiple reaction channels must be included before drawing conclusions about kaon-nucleus binding.
