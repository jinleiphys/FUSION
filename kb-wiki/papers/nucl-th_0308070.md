# Extracting a value of the slope of the neutron form factor $G_{En}(q^2)$ at $q^2=0$ by reanalysis of the experimental data

> A new value $b=0.0205\pm0.0017$ fm$^2$ of the slope of the neutron form factor $G_{En}(q^2)$ at $q^2=0$ compatible with deuteron properties has been extracted by using a linear relation between $b$ and $A^2_S(1+η^2)$ we found for a class of nonlocal potential models having the experimental values of both $r_D$ and $Q$. Another {\it model dependent} value $b^{\rm MHKZ}=0.0206\pm0.0014$ fm$^2$, which is also compatible with deuteron properties, has been determined by applying "constrained" unitary transformations to the local MHKZ potential model. The sensitivity of a small changes in the experimental values used for $r_D$ and $Q$ on the value obtained for $b$ is also investigated.

## Key claim

The paper extracts a new value of the slope of the neutron electric form factor at zero momentum transfer, \(b = 0.0205 \pm 0.0017\) fm\(^2\), that is compatible with experimental deuteron properties (asymptotic S-state amplitude, asymptotic D/S ratio, rms radius, quadrupole moment, and binding energy) by using only nonlocal potential models that reproduce the experimental values of both the deuteron rms radius \(r_D\) and quadrupole moment \(Q\); the abstract glosses over the fact that this value is obtained from a linear relation with a negative slope between \(b\) and \(A_S^2(1+\eta^2)\) that holds only for a specific subset of twelve nonlocal potential models, excluding five Glendenning-Kramer models that lie on a separate line.

## Method

The authors computed the deuteron electric charge form factor \(C_E(q^2)\) from the radial S- and D-wave functions of various potential models, using a numerical integration up to 16 fm plus an analytic asymptotic correction. They then used the experimental ratio \(R(q^2)\) of the deuteron to proton electric form factors from Simon et al. (1980) to extract \(G_{En}(q^2)\) via the nonrelativistic impulse approximation, and fitted a cubic polynomial in \(q^2\) to obtain the slope \(b\). To enforce compatibility with both \(r_D\) and \(Q\), they applied "constrained" short-range unitary transformations to the wave functions of local potential models, adjusting the nonlocality parameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) until the transformed wave functions reproduced the experimental values of both \(r_D\) and \(Q\); only seventeen out of forty-nine potential models yielded such transformed wave functions, and twelve were used for the final extraction.

## Key numbers

*   Slope of the neutron form factor \(G_{En}(q^2)\) at \(q^2=0\) from nonlocal potential models: \(b = 0.0205 \pm 0.0017\) fm\(^2\).
*   Model-dependent slope from the MHKZ potential model with constrained unitary transformations: \(b^{\rm MHKZ} = 0.0206 \pm 0.0014\) fm\(^2\).
*   Slope from local potential models (not compatible with \(r_D\) and \(Q\)): \(b = 0.0296 \pm 0.0118\) fm\(^2\).
*   Sensitivity of \(b\) to changes in the deuteron rms radius: \(\delta b / \delta r_D = 0.6744\) fm.
*   Sensitivity of \(b\) to changes in the deuteron quadrupole moment: \(\delta b / \delta Q = -0.0052\).
*   When using the lower experimental limit for \(r_D\) (1.947 fm from Akimov et al.), the extracted slope becomes \(b = 0.0176 \pm 0.0013\) fm\(^2\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Berard et al. (1973), Allen et al. (1980), and Mustafa et al. (1995), who used linear correlations between \(b\) and \(r_D^2\) for local potential models to determine \(r_D\). Here, the authors instead use a correlation between \(b\) and \(A_S^2(1+\eta^2)\) for nonlocal, phase-equivalent potential models that simultaneously reproduce the experimental \(r_D\) and \(Q\), yielding a value of \(b\) consistent with the earlier measurements of Krohn and Ringo (1966) and Koester et al. (1976) but with a larger uncertainty. The results enable a more reliable extraction of the neutron electric form factor slope that is consistent with both asymptotic and interior deuteron properties, and the sensitivity analysis shows that the extracted \(b\) is most strongly correlated with the assumed value of \(r_D\).
