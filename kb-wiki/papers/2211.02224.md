# Long Range Plan: Dense matter theory for heavy-ion collisions and neutron stars

> Since the release of the 2015 Long Range Plan in Nuclear Physics, major events have occurred that reshaped our understanding of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) and nuclear matter at large densities, in and out of equilibrium. The US nuclear community has an opportunity to capitalize on advances in astrophysical observations and nuclear experiments and engage in an interdisciplinary effort in the theory of dense baryonic matter that connects low- and high-energy nuclear physics, astrophysics, gravitational waves physics, and data science

## Key claim
This white paper argues that the US nuclear physics community has a unique opportunity to capitalize on recent advances in astrophysical observations (gravitational wave detections, NICER radius measurements) and nuclear experiments (BESII, PREX-II/CREX, FRIB) through an interdisciplinary theory effort in dense baryonic matter, but the interpretation of these data requires significant coordinated theoretical development beyond current capabilities.

## Method
The paper reviews the current status and future challenges across multiple subfields: lattice QCD at finite baryon chemical potential (using Taylor expansion, analytic continuation from imaginary chemical potential, complex Langevin methods, and reweighting schemes), chiral effective field theory (χEFT) combined with many-body methods (Quantum Monte Carlo, many-body perturbation theory) for the nuclear equation of state, and dynamical modeling of heavy-ion collisions and neutron star mergers. It identifies specific theoretical tools needed, including Bayesian uncertainty quantification, multidimensional equations of state, transport coefficients, and quantum simulation algorithms.

## Key numbers
- The QCD crossover temperature at zero baryon chemical potential is T ≃ 156 MeV, known from lattice QCD to a few percent
- Nuclear saturation density is n_sat = 0.16 fm^(-3)
- χEFT calculations of neutron-rich dense matter may be valid up to approximately 2 n_sat (twice nuclear saturation density)
- The paper discusses neutron stars in excess of 2 M_⊙ (two solar masses)
- Beam Energy Scan II data covers collision energies √s_NN = 3, 7.7, and 27 GeV
- The paper references a freeze-out estimate for √s_NN = 2.4 GeV from HADES

## Context
This white paper updates the 2015 Long Range Plan in Nuclear Physics, responding to major developments including the first gravitational wave detection from a binary neutron star merger (LIGO/Virgo, 2017), NICER radius measurements of a two-solar-mass neutron star (Riley 2021, Miller 2021, Fonseca 2021), and new experimental results from STAR BESII and HADES possibly indicative of a QCD critical point. It extends the work of the Beam Energy Scan Theory (BEST) Collaboration (An 2021) and builds on Bayesian uncertainty quantification methods developed for χEFT (Furnstahl 2014, Wesolowski 2015, Drischler 2020). The paper enables a coordinated interdisciplinary approach to connect low-energy nuclear physics, high-energy heavy-ion physics, astrophysics, and gravitational wave physics for interpreting upcoming data from FRIB, RHIC, FAIR, LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA, and NICER.
