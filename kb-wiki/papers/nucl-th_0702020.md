# Resonances and fluctuations at SPS and RHIC

> We perform an analysis of preliminary data on hadron yields and fluctuations within the Statistical hadronization ansatz. We describe the theoretical disagreements between different statistical models currently on the market, and show how the simultaneous analysis of yields and fluctuations can be used to determine if one of them can be connected to underlying physics. We perform such an analysis on preliminary RHIC and SPS A-A data that includes particle yields, ratios and event by event fluctuations. We show that the equilibrium statistical model can not describe the $K/π$ fluctuation measured at RHIC and SPS, unless an unrealistically small volume is assumed. Such small volume then makes it impossible to describe the total particle multiplicity. The non-equilibrium model,on the other hand, describes both the $K/π$ fluctuation and yields acceptably due to the extra boost to the $π$ fluctuation provided by the high pion chemical potential. We show, however, that both models significantly over-estimate the $p/π$ fluctuation measured at the SPS, and speculate for the reason behind this.

## Key claim

The equilibrium statistical hadronization model cannot simultaneously describe both particle yields and the $K/\pi$ event-by-event fluctuation at RHIC and SPS unless an unrealistically small volume is assumed, which then fails to describe total particle multiplicity; the non-equilibrium model (with fitted $\gamma_q$) can describe both yields and the $K/\pi$ fluctuation, but both models significantly over-estimate the $p/\pi$ fluctuation measured at the SPS.

## Method

The author performed grand-canonical ensemble fits to preliminary heavy-ion data from RHIC Au-Au at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV and SPS Pb-Pb at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV, using publicly available statistical hadronization software (SHARE). The fits included particle yields, ratios, and event-by-event dynamical fluctuations ($\sigma^{dyn}$) for $K/\pi$ and $p/\pi$, with parameters for temperature $T$, baryochemical potential $\mu_B$, strangeness chemical potential $\mu_s$, isospin chemical potential $\mu_{I3}$, phase-space occupancy factors $\gamma_q$ and $\gamma_s$, and a normalization (volume). Strangeness, charge, and baryon number conservation were enforced as constraints.

## Key numbers

* At RHIC Au-Au $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV, the non-equilibrium fit ($\gamma_q \ne 1$) gives $T = 143.3 \pm 2.5$ MeV, $\mu_B = 20.8 \pm 3.9$ MeV, $\gamma_q = 1.541 \pm 0.001$, $\gamma_s = 1.980 \pm 0.158$, and normalization $1356 \pm 395$ fm$^3$.
* At RHIC Au-Au $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV, the equilibrium fit ($\gamma_q = 1$) gives $T = 159.6 \pm 6.9$ MeV, $\mu_B = 23.2 \pm 3.4$ MeV, $\gamma_s = 1.083 \pm 0.112$, and normalization $1689 \pm 488$ fm$^3$.
* At SPS Pb-Pb $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV, the non-equilibrium fit gives $T = 138.2 \pm 2.3$ MeV, $\mu_B = 231.2 \pm 19.1$ MeV, $\gamma_q = 1.645 \pm 0.003$, $\gamma_s = 1.577 \pm 0.116$, and normalization $2540 \pm 658$ fm$^3$.
* At SPS Pb-Pb $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV, the equilibrium fit gives $T = 151.3 \pm 3.8$ MeV, $\mu_B = 248.0 \pm 25.1$ MeV, $\gamma_s = 0.815 \pm 0.067$, and normalization $3746 \pm 444$ fm$^3$.
* Fitting the $K/\pi$ fluctuation alone with the equilibrium model forces an unrealistically small volume of $\sim 500$ fm$^3$ at RHIC and $\sim 1000$ fm$^3$ at SPS, under-predicting particle yields by several standard deviations.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing statistical hadronization approach (Hagedorn 1965, and later reviews by Braun-Munzinger et al., Cleymans et al., Becattini et al.) by adding event-by-event fluctuations as a discriminating observable. It directly tests the equilibrium model (with $\gamma_q=1$, typical of fits by Cleymans et al. and Becattini et al.) against the non-equilibrium model (with fitted $\gamma_q$, as in the work of Rafelski and Letessier), using preliminary RHIC and SPS data. The results enable future studies to use ratio fluctuations to probe the nature of the hadronization phase transition and the duration of the hadronic reinteraction phase, and call for more precise data on resonances such as $\Sigma^*$, $\Delta$, and $\rho$ to resolve the remaining discrepancies.
