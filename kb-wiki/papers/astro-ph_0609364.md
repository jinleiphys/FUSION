# Magnetar oscillations pose challenges for strange stars

> Compact relativistic stars allow us to study the nature of matter under extreme conditions, probing regions of parameter space that are otherwise inaccessible. Nuclear theory in this regime is not well constrained: one key issue is whether neutron stars are in fact composed primarily of strange quark matter. Distinguishing the two possibilities, however, has been difficult. The recent detection of seismic vibrations in the aftermath of giant flares from two magnetars (highly magnetized compact stars) is a major breakthrough. The oscillations excited seem likely to involve the stellar crust, the properties of which differ dramatically for strange stars. We show that the resulting mode frequencies cannot be reconciled with the observations for reasonable magnetar parameters. Ruling out strange star models would place a strong constraint on models of dense quark matter.

# Magnetar oscillations pose challenges for strange stars

## Key claim
The frequencies of torsional shear modes in strange star crusts cannot be reconciled with the observed quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) from magnetar giant flares for reasonable magnetar parameters, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that the observed QPOs are indeed crustal shear modes and that the coupling between crust and core does not significantly alter the frequencies.

## Method
The authors computed torsional shear mode frequencies for two types of strange star crust models (thin nuclear crust and nugget crust) using a plane-parallel geometry with constant gravitational acceleration, solving the perturbation equations for horizontal displacement with zero traction boundary conditions. They varied stellar mass (1.2–2.5 M⊙), radius (8–15 km), magnetic field (10¹²–10¹⁵ G), temperature (10⁷–10⁹ K), and for nugget crusts the strange quark mass (150–250 MeV), then compared the resulting mode frequencies to QPOs observed from SGR 1806-20 (18, 26, 30, 92, 150, 625 Hz) and SGR 1900+14 (28, 53, 84, 155 Hz).

## Key numbers
- For thin nuclear crust models, the n=0, l=2 fundamental mode lies in the range 26–54 Hz; obtaining a fundamental ≤30 Hz requires M ≥ 2.4 M⊙ for all radii, or M ≥ 2.2 M⊙ with R ≥ 14 km
- For thin nuclear crust at B = 10¹² G, the lowest overtone frequency is ≈725 Hz (for the thickest crust model with M = 1.2 M⊙, R = 15 km, ΔR/R = 8%); at B > 10¹⁴ G, the lower limit on overtone frequency is ≈1100 Hz
- For nugget crust models, the n=0, l=2 fundamental lies in the range ≈1–21 Hz, with no model permitting a fundamental in the 28–30 Hz range
- For nugget crust at B = 10¹² G, the n=1 overtone can be as low as ≈260 Hz; at B = 10¹⁴ G, only extreme models (M = 1.2 M⊙, R = 15 km, m_s ≈ 250 MeV, T ≥ 9×10⁸ K) permit an overtone frequency as low as 625 Hz
- Thin nuclear crust thickness ΔR/R = 5.4% at T = 10⁸ K, falling to 2.1% at T = 10⁹ K; nugget crust ΔR/R ranges from 0.7% (m_s = 150 MeV) to 6.1% (m_s = 250 MeV) for M = 1.4 M⊙, R = 12 km

## Context
This work extends the magnetar seismology approach of Piro (2005), Duncan (1998), and McDermott (1988), who showed that neutron star crustal shear modes match observed QPO frequencies, to test the strange star hypothesis of Witten (1984). The authors find that strange star models cannot simultaneously explain both the fundamental and overtone QPO frequencies, in contrast to neutron star models. If confirmed by more sophisticated models including realistic geometry and general relativistic corrections, this would provide a robust means of distinguishing strange stars from neutron stars and constrain the QCD phase diagram at finite chemical potential, with implications for the stability of terrestrial matter and the likelihood of multiply strange hadronic states like the H-dibaryon.
