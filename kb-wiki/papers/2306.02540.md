# Normal single-spin asymmetries in electron-proton scattering: two-photon exchange with intermediate state resonances

> We calculate the beam ($B_n$) and target ($A_n$) normal single-spin asymmetries in electron-proton elastic scattering from two-photon exchange amplitudes with resonance intermediate states of spin-parity $1/2^\pm$ and $3/2^\pm$ and mass $W \lesssim 1.8$ GeV. The latest CLAS exclusive meson electroproduction data are used as input for the transition amplitudes from the proton to the excited resonance states. For $B_n$, the spin 3/2 resonances dominate by an order of magnitude over the spin 1/2 states. In general we observe cancellations between the negative contributions of the $Δ(1232)$ and $N(1520)$ across both beam energy and scattering angle, and the positive contributions of the $Δ(1700)$ and $N(1720)$, leading to a rather large overall uncertainty band in the total $B_n$. At forward angles and beam energies $E_\textrm{lab}<1$ GeV, where the $Δ(1232)$ dominates, the calculated $B_n$ tend to overshoot the A4 and SAMPLE data. The calculated $B_n$ compare well with the measured values from the A4 and $Q_{\textrm{weak}}$ experiments with $E_\textrm{lab}>1$ GeV.

## Key claim

The beam normal single-spin asymmetry $B_n$ calculated from two-photon exchange amplitudes with resonance intermediate states shows large cancellations between negative contributions from the $\Delta(1232)$ and $N(1520)$ and positive contributions from the $\Delta(1700)$ and $N(1720)$, leading to a rather large overall uncertainty band; the calculation overshoots A4 and SAMPLE data at forward angles and beam energies below 1 GeV where the $\Delta(1232)$ dominates, but compares well with A4 and $Q_{\rm weak}$ data at beam energies above 1 GeV.

## Method

The authors computed the beam ($B_n$) and target ($A_n$) normal single-spin asymmetries in elastic electron-proton scattering by evaluating the imaginary part of the two-photon exchange amplitude using the Cutkosky cutting rules, with intermediate hadronic states consisting of spin-parity $1/2^\pm$ and $3/2^\pm$ resonances up to invariant mass $W \lesssim 1.8$ GeV. Transition amplitudes from the proton to excited resonance states were taken from the latest CLAS exclusive meson electroproduction data parametrization of resonance electrocouplings $A_{1/2}$, $A_{3/2}$, and $S_{1/2}$. The phase space integral over the intermediate electron momentum was performed numerically, with an analytic treatment applied in the quasi-real Compton scattering region where the intermediate electron three-momentum approaches zero.

## Key numbers

- Beam normal asymmetry $B_n$ is of order $10^{-6}$ to $10^{-5}$ for beam energies in the GeV range
- Target normal asymmetry $A_n$ is anticipated to be of order $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-2}$ for the same beam energy range
- Spin 3/2 resonances dominate $B_n$ by an order of magnitude over spin 1/2 states
- Laboratory threshold energies for resonance excitation range from 0.34 GeV for $\Delta(1232)$ to 1.11 GeV for $N(1720)$ in the zero width approximation
- Higher energy experiments ($1 \lesssim E_{\rm lab} \lesssim 3$ GeV) at small scattering angles give $B_n$ consistently in the range of approximately -7 to -4 ppm
- The SAMPLE measurement at $E_{\rm lab}=0.2$ GeV and backward angle shows relative tension with G0 and A4 measurements at similar kinematics

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical estimates of beam normal asymmetries by Pasquini and Vanderhaeghen (2004, 2005) and Gorchtein (2004, 2005), which used hadronic approximations with $\pi N$ intermediate states or generalized parton distribution approaches but showed significant disagreements with each other and with data. The calculation uses the latest CLAS electroproduction data (Hiller Blin 2019) for resonance electrocouplings, improving on earlier MAID-based inputs. The results enable better systematic error control for parity-violating experiments like $Q_{\rm weak}$ (2020) and support the proposed Jefferson Lab Hall A measurement of $A_n$ in both $e^-p$ and $e^+p$ scattering at beam energies of 2.2, 4.4, and 6.6 GeV.
