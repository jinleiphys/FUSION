# Heat can flow from cold to hot in Microcanonical Thermodynamics of finite systems. The microscopic origin of condensation and phase separations

> Microcanonical Thermodynamics allows the application of Statistical Mechanics on one hand to closed finite and even small systems and on the other to the largest,self-gravitating ones. However, one has to reconsider the fundamental principles of Statistical Mechanics especially its key quantity, entropy. Whereas in conventional Thermostatistics the homogeneity and extensivity of the system and the concavity of its entropy S(E) are central conditions, these fail for the systems considered here. E.g. at phase separation the entropy S(E) is necessarily convex to make e^{S(E)-E/T} bimodal in E (the two coexisting phases). This is so even for normal macroscopic systems with short-range coupling. As inhomogeneities and surface effects in particular cannot be scaled away,one has to be careful with the standard arguments of splitting a system into two or bringing two systems into thermal contact. Not only the volume part of the entropy must be considered. When removing an external constraint in regions of a negative heat capacity, the system may even relax under a flow of heat (energy) against the temperature slope. Thus Clausius formulation of the Second Law: "Heat always flows from hot to cold" can be violated. Temperature is not a necessary or fundamental control parameter of Thermostatistics. In the final sections of this paper the general microscopic mechanism leading to condensation and to the convexity of the microcanonical entropy S(E) at phase separation is sketched. Also the microscopic conditions for the existence or non-existence of a critical end-point of the phase-separation are discussed. This is explained for the liquid--gas and the solid--liquid transition.

## Key claim

Microcanonical thermodynamics, using the Boltzmann-Planck definition of entropy, shows that at phase separation the entropy \(S(E)\) is necessarily convex, which can cause heat to flow from cold to hot when constraints are removed in systems with negative heat capacity, thereby violating Clausius's formulation of the Second Law while still satisfying the Second Law via total entropy increase.

## Method

The paper derives the microcanonical partition sum for a classical system with Hamiltonian \(H = \sum_i p_i^2/(2m) + \Phi^{\text{int}}[\{\vec{r}\}]\) and separates it into an ideal-gas factor \(W_{\text{id-gas}}\) and an interaction factor \(W_{\text{int}}\) that depends on the accessible \(3N\)-dimensional configuration space volume. It analyzes the convexity of the microcanonical entropy \(S(E)\) by examining how the system's population of the \(3N\)-dimensional configuration space changes with energy, particularly through the opening of new configurational regions (e.g., droplet fission or evaporation) that cause upward jumps in \(S_{\text{int}}(E)\). The paper also applies a simplified square-well model to derive criteria for convexity and bimodality.

## Key numbers

* For a single-phase system (mono-dispersed sum), the heat capacity equals the ideal-gas value \(Nc(E) = (3N-2)/2\), independent of whether the system is condensed or not.
* At the ground state (\(E \to E_0\)), the interaction entropy \(S_{\text{int}}\) approaches \(\ln\{[{\cal V}_{0N}/{\cal V}]^{N-1}\} < 0\), where \({\cal V}_{0N}\) is the condensate eigen-volume and \({\cal V}\) is the container volume.
* In the gas phase (\(E \gg \Phi^{\text{int}}\)), \(S_{\text{int}} \to 0\) and the system behaves like an ideal gas filling the volume \({\cal V}\).
* For the Alder-Wainwright transition of hard disks, the heat capacity is everywhere the ideal-gas value \(Nc(E) = (3N-2)/2\), and there is no bimodality versus energy or cluster number, indicating no first-order phase separation.

## Context

This work extends the author's prior development of microcanonical thermodynamics (Gross 2001, 2004) to finite and self-gravitating systems, contradicting the conventional assumption of entropy concavity and extensivity used by Hertz (1910) and Berdichevsky (1997). It provides a microscopic mechanism for the convex intruder in \(S(E)\) that signals first-order phase transitions, which was previously treated only via the Yang-Lee singularity in the canonical ensemble. The results enable the study of phase separations in small systems like atomic clusters and hot nuclei, where surface effects cannot be scaled away, and clarify the conditions for the existence of a critical end-point.
