# Hybrid stars among mass gap objects are excluded by twin stars at $1.4\,M_\odot$

> We test whether compact objects in the so called mass gap ($2.5 < M_{\rm max}/M_\odot < 5.0$) can be hybrid stars. Using a generic hybrid equation of state with a first-order deconfinement transition, we map the allowed parameter space in a Seidov-type diagram and confront it with modern mass--radius constraints. We find that mass-gap hybrid stars require an extremely early onset of deconfinement and very stiff quark matter. The Bayesian analysis, however, favors equations of state with deconfinement at typical neutron-star masses around $1.4\,M_\odot$ with mass-twin stars that, if confirmed, would rule out hybrid stars as candidates for observed mass-gap compact objects.

## Key claim

Mass-gap objects (2.5–5.0 solar masses) can in principle be hybrid stars, but only if deconfinement begins at extremely low densities (onset below about 1.2 times nuclear saturation density) and quark matter is very stiff; however, Bayesian analysis favors equations of state where deconfinement occurs near 1.4 solar masses with mass-twin stars, which would rule out hybrid stars as candidates for observed mass-gap compact objects if those twins are confirmed.

## Method

The authors constructed a generic hybrid equation of state combining a Walecka-type relativistic density functional for nuclear matter with a constant-speed-of-sound quark matter phase, connected by a first-order deconfinement transition. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for three values of the squared sound speed (0.50, 0.75, 1.00) and mapped the allowed parameter space in Seidov-type diagrams of density jump versus onset density. They then applied Bayesian analysis incorporating modern mass–radius constraints from NICER and other observations to identify the most probable parameter regions.

## Key numbers

* The mass-gap region is defined as 2.5 < M_max/M_sun < 5.0, with specific observed objects: GW170817 total mass 2.74 (+0.04/-0.01) M_sun, GW190814 lighter component 2.59 (+0.08/-0.09) M_sun, and GW230529 mass 2.5–4.5 M_sun.
* For squared sound speed c_s^2 = 0.5 (typical for color-superconducting quark matter), mass-gap objects exist for all onset densities below 2.5 n_0, but third-family members among them are marginal only at n_onset = n_0 and Delta n = 0.6 n_0.
* For c_s^2 = 0.75 (the maximum plausible value 0.781 from relativistic hydrodynamic transport), third-family members among mass-gap objects exist for n_onset <= 1.5 n_0, but the mass defect in twin-star transitions is only about 10^(-4) M_sun, too small to explain observed eccentric millisecond pulsar orbits.
* The Bayesian posterior favors two solution classes: early onset (1.0 <= n_onset/n_0 <= 1.5, onset masses below 0.5 M_sun) that can reach the mass gap for c_s^2 = 0.75, and twin-star solutions around 1.4 M_sun where maximum masses reach just beyond 2 M_sun but cannot enter the mass gap.
* If PSR J0614-3329 and PSR J0437-4715 are confirmed as mass twins at 1.4 M_sun, the maximum mass for such a hybrid equation of state would be limited to M_max < 2.2 M_sun.

## Context

This work extends the classification of hybrid neutron star equations of state in Seidov-type diagrams introduced by Alford (2013) and applies it to the specific question of whether mass-gap objects (such as those detected in GW170817, GW190814, and GW230529) could be hybrid stars rather than black holes. The results contradict the possibility that such objects are hybrid stars if the twin-star interpretation of recent NICER measurements (Miller 2025 for PSR J0437-4715, Mauviard 2025 for PSR J0614-3329) is confirmed. The paper also connects to the eccentric millisecond pulsar puzzle studied by Chanlaridis (2024), where accretion-induced deconfinement transitions with mass defects of about 0.01 M_sun could explain observed orbital eccentricities, but finds that the required mass defects are not achieved for mass-gap objects.
