# 511 KeV Photons From Color Superconducting Dark Matter

> We discuss the possibility that the recent detection of 511 keV gamma rays from the galactic bulge, as observed by INTEGRAL, can be naturally explained by the supermassive very dense droplets (strangelets) of dark matter. These droplets are assumed to be made of ordinary light quarks (or antiquarks) condensed in non-hadronic color superconducting phase. The droplets can carry electrons (or positrons) in the bulk or/and on the surface. The e^+e^- annihilation events take place due to the collisions of electrons from the visible matter with positrons from dark matter droplets which may result in the bright 511 KeV gamma-ray line from the bulge of the Galaxy.

## Key claim

The observed 511 keV gamma-ray line from the galactic bulge, measured by INTEGRAL, can be naturally explained by collisions between electrons from ordinary visible matter and positrons carried by supermassive dense droplets of dark matter (strangelets) made of light quarks or antiquarks condensed in a non-hadronic color superconducting phase, though the authors note that their estimates span five orders of magnitude depending on assumptions about visible and dark matter distributions.

## Method

The authors model dark matter as macroscopic droplets (QCD balls) of color-superconducting quark matter, which can carry positrons on their surface or in their bulk. They estimate the annihilation rate by calculating the probability that an electron from visible matter hits such a droplet and forms positronium, which then decays into two 511 keV photons, using the geometrical cross-section of the droplet. Two estimates are performed: one assuming visible matter follows the dark matter distribution with a fixed cosmological ratio, and a second using a more realistic peaked visible matter distribution scaling as r^{-1.8} near the galactic center.

## Key numbers

- The observed flux of 511 keV photons is 9.9^{+4.7}_{-2.1} x 10^{-4} photons cm^{-2} s^{-1}, with an angular distribution half-maximum at 9 degrees (6 to 18 degrees at 2 sigma confidence)
- The first rough estimate yields a flux of 10^{-3} cm^{-2} s^{-1} multiplied by (10^{18}/B)^{1/3}, where B is the baryon number of a typical droplet
- The second estimate, using a visible matter distribution scaling as r^{-1.8} and dark matter as r^{-0.6}, yields a flux of 10^{-3} cm^{-2} s^{-1} multiplied by (10^{33}/B)^{1/3}
- The two estimates differ by almost five orders of magnitude due to different assumptions about matter distributions
- The typical width of the 511 keV line is estimated as Gamma/(511 keV) ~ v/c ~ alpha ~ 10^{-2}, corresponding to a few keV

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Boehm, Hooper, Silk, and Casse (2004) that light dark matter particles annihilating into electron-positron pairs could explain the INTEGRAL 511 keV signal, but places it in the specific framework of color-superconducting dark matter droplets (QCD balls) previously introduced by Zhitnitsky (2003) and Oaknin and Zhitnitsky (2004). The scenario enables a testable prediction that distinguishes it from other dark matter explanations: the local flux of 511 keV photons should be proportional to both visible and dark matter densities, not just dark matter density, and the effect should not be observed in directions not pointing to the galactic center.
