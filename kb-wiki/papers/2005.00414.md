# Precision measurement of the magnetic octupole moment in $^{45}$Sc as a test for state-of-the-art atomic- and nuclear-structure theory

> We report on measurements of the hyperfine $A, B$ and $C$-constants of the $3d4s^2 ~^2D_{5/2}$ and $3d4s^2 ~^2D_{3/2}$ atomic states in $^{45}$Sc. High-precision atomic calculations of the hyperfine fields of these states and second-order corrections are performed, and are used to extract $C_{5/2}=-0.06(6)$ kHz and $C_{3/2}=+0.04(3)$ kHz from the data. These results are one order of magnitude more precise than the available literature. From the combined analysis of both atomic states, we infer the nuclear magnetic octupole moment $Ω= -0.07(53) μ_N b$, including experimental and atomic structure-related uncertainties. With a single valence proton outside of a magic calcium core, scandium is ideally suited to test a variety of nuclear models, and to investigate in-depth the many intriguing nuclear structure phenomena observed within the neighboring isotopes of calcium. We perform nuclear shell-model calculations of $Ω$, and furthermore explore the use of Density Functional Theory for evaluating $Ω$. From this, mutually consistent theoretical values of $Ω$ are obtained, which are in agreement with the experimental value. This confirms atomic structure calculations possess the accuracy and precision required for magnetic octupole moment measurements, and shows that modern nuclear theory is capable of providing meaningful insight into this largely unexplored observable.

# Precision measurement of the magnetic octupole moment in $^{45}$Sc as a test for state-of-the-art atomic- and nuclear-structure theory

## Key claim

The magnetic octupole moment of $^{45}$Sc is measured as $\Omega = -0.07(53) \mu_N b$, which agrees with both shell-model and Density Functional Theory calculations, but the experimental uncertainty is too large by roughly an order of magnitude to provide a stringent test of the different theoretical approaches.

## Method

The experiment combines resonance laser ionization spectroscopy with radio-frequency (RF) spectroscopy to measure hyperfine $A$, $B$, and $C$ constants of the $3d4s^2 \ ^2D_{5/2}$ and $3d4s^2 \ ^2D_{3/2}$ atomic states in neutral $^{45}$Sc. Relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) theory calculations are performed to evaluate the hyperfine field factors $C/\Omega$ and second-order hyperfine shifts, which are essential to extract the nuclear magnetic octupole moment from the measured $C$ constants. Nuclear shell-model calculations using seven different Hamiltonians and DFT calculations with ten different energy density functionals are performed and compared to the experimental result.

## Key numbers

- Hyperfine constant $C_{5/2} = -0.062(59)[17]$ kHz (corrected for second-order shifts) for the $3d4s^2 \ ^2D_{5/2}$ state
- Hyperfine constant $C_{3/2} = +0.039(28)[2]$ kHz (corrected for second-order shifts) for the $3d4s^2 \ ^2D_{3/2}$ state
- Nuclear magnetic octupole moment $\Omega = -0.07(53) \mu_N b$ from combined analysis of both atomic states
- Shell-model $\Omega$ values range from 0.41 to 0.49 $\mu_N b$ with free $g$-factors and 0.28 to 0.35 $\mu_N b$ with spin-quenching factor 0.6
- DFT result $\Omega_{\text{DFT}} = +0.245(17) \mu_N b$ averaged over eight Skyrme and two finite-range functionals
- DFT magnetic dipole moment $\mu_{\text{DFT}} = +4.74(6) \mu_N$ compared to literature value $\mu = +4.75400(2) \mu_N$
- Electric quadrupole moment $Q = -0.220(9)$ b from literature, with DFT reaching experimental value near intrinsic quadrupole moment $Q_{20} = -1$ b

## Context

This work extends the sparse set of magnetic octupole moment measurements, which exist for only 18 stable isotopes (Childs 1971, Daly 1954, Brown 1966, and others), and addresses discrepancies such as the unexpectedly large $\Omega$ reported for $^{133}$Cs (Gerginov 2003) and $^{173}$Yb (Singh 2013). The combination of RF spectroscopy with resonance ionization provides a method applicable to radioactive scandium isotopes, which could help illuminate nuclear structure phenomena observed in neighboring calcium isotopes (Steppenbeck 2013, Wienholtz 2013, Ruiz 2016). The agreement between experiment and both shell-model and DFT calculations demonstrates that modern nuclear theory can provide meaningful predictions for this largely unexplored observable, though further improvements in experimental precision by roughly a factor of 10 would be required to discriminate between theoretical approaches.
