# Constraining scalar dark matter with Big Bang nucleosynthesis and atomic spectroscopy

> Scalar dark matter can interact with Standard Model (SM) particles, altering the fundamental constants of Nature in the process. Changes in the fundamental constants during and prior to Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) produce changes in the primordial abundances of the light elements. By comparing the measured and calculated (within the SM) primordial abundance of $^{4}$He, which is predominantly determined by the ratio of the neutron-proton mass difference to freeze-out temperature at the time of weak interaction freeze-out prior to BBN, we are able to derive stringent constraints on the mass of a scalar dark matter particle $ϕ$ together with its interactions with the photon, light quarks and massive vector bosons via quadratic couplings in $ϕ$, as well as its interactions with massive vector bosons via linear couplings in $ϕ$. We also derive a stringent constraint on the quadratic interaction of $ϕ$ with the photon from recent atomic dysprosium spectroscopy measurements.

## Key claim

By comparing the measured and Standard Model-predicted primordial abundance of $^{4}$He, the authors derive constraints on scalar dark matter particle mass and its interactions, but these constraints apply only to specific combinations of coupling parameters and assume that scalar dark matter saturates the present-day dark matter energy density.

## Method

The authors compare the measured and Standard Model-calculated primordial abundance of $^{4}$He, which depends on the neutron-to-proton ratio at weak interaction freeze-out, itself determined by the ratio of the neutron-proton mass difference to the freeze-out temperature. They express variations in this ratio in terms of scalar dark matter field couplings to photons, light quarks, and massive vector bosons via both linear and quadratic interactions, using the energy density evolution of the scalar field during and after Big Bang nucleosynthesis. For quadratic photon couplings, they also derive constraints from atomic dysprosium spectroscopy measurements of oscillating variations in the fine-structure constant.

## Key numbers

- Measured primordial $^{4}$He abundance: $Y_p^{\textrm{exp}} (^4 \textrm{He}) = 0.2474 \pm 0.0028$ (Peimbert 2007); Standard Model prediction: $Y_p^{\textrm{theor}} (^4 \textrm{He}) = 0.2486 \pm 0.0002$ (Berengut 2010)
- Measured neutron-proton ratio at weak interaction freeze-out: $(n/p)_{\textrm{weak}}^{\textrm{exp}} = 0.1801 \pm 0.0026$; predicted: $(n/p)_{\textrm{weak}}^{\textrm{theor}} = 0.1811 \pm 0.0002$
- Measured $Q_{np}/T_{\textrm{F}}$ ratio: $1.714 \pm 0.015$; predicted: $1.709 \pm 0.001$
- Relative difference in $Q_{np}/T_{\textrm{F}}$: $0.0033 \pm 0.0085$
- Weak interaction freeze-out temperature: $T_\textrm{F} \approx 0.75$ MeV; freeze-out time: $t_{\textrm{F}} \approx 1.1$ s
- Transition mass between oscillating and non-oscillating regimes: $m_\phi \approx 10^{-16}$ eV at $t_{\textrm{F}}$
- Present mean dark matter energy density: $1.3 \times 10^{-6}$ GeV/cm$^3$ from WMAP measurements

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Stadnik and Flambaum 2015) on cosmological evolution of fundamental constants due to oscillating scalar dark matter fields with quadratic couplings. It provides new constraints on scalar dark matter parameter space that were previously unconstrained, complementing existing limits from equivalence principle tests (Adelberger 2008, 2012), supernova energy loss bounds (Olive 2008), and lunar laser ranging (Turyshev 2004, 2012). The results enable future searches for scalar dark matter across a wide mass range, from $m_\phi \ll 10^{-16}$ eV to $m_\phi \gg 10^{-16}$ eV, and can be refined with improved measurements of primordial $^{4}$He abundances and atomic spectroscopy.
