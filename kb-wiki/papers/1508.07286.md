# New physics effects on neutrinoless double beta decay from right-handed current

> We study the impact of new physics contributions to neutrinoless double beta decay arising from right-handed current in comparison with the standard mechanism. If the light neutrinos obtain their masses from Type-II seesaw within left-right symmetric model, where the Type-I contribution is suppressed to negligible extent, the right-handed PMNS matrix is the same as its left-handed counterpart, making it highly predictable and can be tested at next-generation experiments. It is very attractive, especially with recent cosmological constraint favoring the normal hierarchy under which the neutrinoless double beta decay is too small to be observed unless new physics appears as indicated by the recent diboson excess observed at ATLAS. The relative contributions from left- and right-handed currents can be reconstructed with the ratio between lifetimes of two different isotopes as well as the ratio of nuclear matrix elements. In this way, the theoretical uncertainties in the calculation of nuclear matrix elements can be essentially avoided. We also discuss the interplay of neutrinoless double beta decay measurements with cosmology, beta decay, and neutrino oscillation.

## Key claim

If neutrinoless double beta decay is observed but cannot be explained by the standard light-neutrino mechanism, it would indicate new physics; the authors show that in a left-right symmetric model with Type-II seesaw dominance, the right-handed current contribution can dominate over the standard mechanism and saturate current experimental bounds even under the normal hierarchy, which is otherwise inaccessible to next-generation experiments.

## Method

The authors analyze neutrinoless double beta decay within a left-right symmetric model (LRSM) based on the gauge group SU(2)L × SU(2)R × U(1)B−L, assuming Type-II seesaw dominance where the Dirac mass term is suppressed so that the left- and right-handed neutrino mass matrices are proportional and share the same PMNS mixing matrix. They compute the effective Majorana mass parameters from both the standard light-neutrino exchange and the heavy-neutrino exchange via right-handed currents, using oscillation parameters from global fits and projected JUNO precision, and derive lower limits on the lightest neutrino mass from current experimental bounds. They also develop a method using ratios of half-lives and nuclear matrix elements between two different isotopes to separate the standard and new physics contributions while avoiding theoretical uncertainties in nuclear matrix element calculations.

## Key numbers

- Current lower limit on neutrinoless double beta decay half-life for 76Ge: T0ν1/2 > 2.1 × 10^25 yrs at 90% C.L. from GERDA, combined bound T0ν1/2 > 3.0 × 10^25 yrs
- Future GERDA Phase-II sensitivity: T0ν1/2 > 2.0 × 10^26 yrs
- Current lower limit for 136Xe: T0ν1/2 > 1.9 × 10^26 yrs from KamLAND-Zen, combined limit T0ν1/2 > 3.4 × 10^26 yrs at 90% C.L.
- Derived lower limit on lightest neutrino mass from current 0νββ bounds: m1 > 2.3 meV for normal hierarchy, m3 > 0.075 meV for inverted hierarchy
- Cosmological constraint on sum of light neutrino masses: mΣ < 84 meV at 1σ C.L., mΣ < 146 meV at 2σ C.L., mΣ < 208 meV at 3σ C.L.
- Assumed W_R mass: MW_R ≈ 2 TeV, with gR ≈ 2/3 gL, and heavy neutrino mass MN = 1 TeV, motivated by ATLAS diboson excess
- Typical momentum transfer in nuclear matrix elements: ⟨p⟩ ≈ 100 MeV
- Phase space factor for 76Ge: G0ν01 = 5.77 × 10−15 yr−1; for 136Xe: G0ν01 = 3.56 × 10−14 yr−1
- Nuclear matrix element ranges for 76Ge: M0νν = 2.58–6.64, M0νN = 233–412; for 136Xe: M0νν = 1.57–3.85, M0νN = 164–172

## Context

This work extends the study of neutrinoless double beta decay in left-right symmetric models, particularly following the Type-II seesaw dominance scenario explored by Tello et al. (2010), Deppisch et al. (2014), and Barry et al. (2013). It is motivated by the ATLAS diboson excess at 2 TeV (Aad 2015) and cosmological constraints favoring normal hierarchy (Dell'Oro 2015), which together suggest that observable 0νββ decay would require new physics beyond the standard light-neutrino mechanism. The ratio method for separating contributions using two isotopes builds on earlier work by Faessler et al. (2011) and Meroni et al. (2012), and the authors show that JUNO (An 2015) can reduce oscillation parameter uncertainties to below 1%, enabling precise reconstruction of the relative contributions from left- and right-handed currents.
