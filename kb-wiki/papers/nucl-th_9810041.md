# Quantum and statistical fluctuations in dynamical symmetry breaking

> Dynamical symmetry breaking in an expanding nuclear system is investigated in semi-classical and quantum framework by employing a collective transport model which is constructed to mimic the collective behavior of expanding systems. It is shown that the fluctuations in collective coordinates during the expansion are developed mainly by the enhancement of the initial fluctuations by the driving force, and that statistical and quantum fluctuations have similar consequences. It is pointed out that the quantal fluctuations may play an important role in the development of instabilities by reducing the time needed to break the symmetry, and the possible role of quantal fluctuations in spinodal decomposition of nuclei is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper shows that during the expansion of a hot nuclear system, fluctuations in the collective quadrupole coordinate are developed mainly by the enhancement of initial fluctuations (statistical or quantal) by the driving force, and that quantal fluctuations can reduce the time needed to break the symmetry by up to a factor of two compared to classical predictions, particularly at low temperatures.

## Method

The authors constructed a phenomenological collective transport model for the quadrupole moment of density fluctuations in an expanding spherical nuclear system. They performed classical Langevin simulations (with friction, diffusion, and a white-noise random force) and a quantal transport calculation (solving the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for the collective Hamiltonian, neglecting the collision term) for a system of A=64 nucleons, parameterizing the time-dependent spring constant and potential according to finite-temperature TDHF and RPA calculations. The evolution of the probability distribution n(Q,t) and the variances σ_Q(t) and σ_P(t) were computed for initial temperatures of 5 and 10 MeV and expansion times around 50 fm/c.

## Key numbers

- The initial frequency of the quadrupole mode at normal density is ω ≃ 65 A^{-1/3} MeV/ħ, which becomes imaginary (unstable) at maximum dilution, reaching about i20 A^{-1/3} MeV/ħ.
- The system takes between 30 fm/c and 75 fm/c to reach maximum dilution, which occurs typically around 1/3 of normal nuclear matter density; a characteristic expansion time t_0 = 50 fm/c is used in the calculations.
- The damping width of the giant quadrupole excitation is taken as Γ = 85 A^{-2/3} MeV.
- For initial temperatures T_0 = 5 MeV and T_0 = 10 MeV, the quantal fluctuations reduce the critical time τ to reach σ_Q = 0.2 by up to a factor of two compared to classical calculations without friction and stochastic forces.
- The initial variances in the quantal case are given by σ_Q(0) ≃ (ħω/2K_0) coth(ħω/2T_0) and σ_P(0) ≃ (Mħω/2) coth(ħω/2T_0), which reduce to the classical expressions σ_Q(0) ≃ T_0/K_0 and σ_P(0) ≃ MT_0 when T_0 ≫ ħω/2.

## Context

This work extends the linear-response treatment of unstable collective modes in expanding finite nuclear systems by Jacquot et al. (1998), who identified the softening of collective modes using temperature-dependent quantal RPA. It also builds on the stochastic one-body transport models (Boltzmann-Langevin) of Ayik and Gregoire (1988), Randrup and Remaud (1990), and Abe (1991), and on the non-Markovian quantal fluctuation-dissipation relations developed by Ayik and Randrup (1998). The results suggest that semi-classical simulations of spinodal decomposition and fragmentation in heavy-ion collisions may need to incorporate quantal fluctuations to correctly predict the time scale of symmetry breaking.
