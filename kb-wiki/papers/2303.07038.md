# Compositeness of $T_{cc}$ and $X(3872)$ by considering decay and coupled-channels effects

> The compositeness of weakly bound states is discussed using the effective field theory from the viewpoint of the low-energy universality. We introduce a model with coupling of the single-channel scattering to the bare state, and study the compositeness of the bound state by varying the bare state energy. In contrast to the naive expectation that the near-threshold states are dominated by the molecular structure, we demonstrate that a non-composite state can always be realized even with a small binding energy. At the same time, however, it is shown that a fine tuning is necessary to obtain the non-composite weakly bound state. In other words, the probability of finding a model with the composite dominant state becomes larger with the decrease of the binding energy in accordance with the low-energy universality. For the application to exotic hadrons, we then discuss the modification of the compositeness due to the decay and coupled-channels effects. We quantitatively show that these contributions suppress the compositeness, because of the increase of the fraction of other components. Finally, as examples of near-threshold exotic hadrons, the structures of $T_{cc}$ and $X(3872)$ are studied by evaluating the compositeness. We find the importance of the coupled-channels and decay contributions for the structures of $T_{cc}$ and $X(3872)$, respectively.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a weakly bound state near threshold can be elementary-dominant (non-composite) only with fine tuning of the bare state energy, but that the probability of finding a composite-dominant state increases as binding energy decreases, consistent with low-energy universality. For the specific cases of \(T_{cc}\) and \(X(3872)\), decay and coupled-channels effects suppress the compositeness, with coupled-channels being important for \(T_{cc}\) and decay contributions being important for \(X(3872)\).

## Method

The authors construct a nonrelativistic effective field theory model with single-channel scattering of two particles (\(\psi_1\) and \(\psi_2\)) coupled to a bare discrete state \(\phi\) via a contact three-point interaction, without direct \(\psi_1\psi_2\) interactions. They compute the on-shell T-matrix from the Lippmann-Schwinger equation, obtain the scattering amplitude, and evaluate the compositeness \(X\) (the probability of the hadronic molecule component) from the residue of the bound state pole. They then extend the model to include decay and coupled-channels effects and apply it to the \(T_{cc}\) and \(X(3872)\) systems.

## Key numbers

- The critical binding energy at which composite-dominant and elementary-dominant regions are equal is \(B_{\rm cr} = 0.243 E_{\rm typ}\), where \(E_{\rm typ} = \Lambda^2/(2\mu)\) is the typical energy scale of the model.
- For a typical bound state with \(B = E_{\rm typ}\), the fraction of the parameter space giving composite-dominant states is \(P_{\rm comp} = 0.25\).
- For a weakly bound state with \(B = 0.01 E_{\rm typ}\), the fraction is \(P_{\rm comp} = 0.88\).
- In the \(B \to 0\) limit, the compositeness \(X = 1\) holds for the entire allowed region of the bare state energy \(\nu_0/E_{\rm typ}\) (the compositeness theorem).
- The effective range in the single-channel model has an upper bound \(r_e \leq 4/(\pi \Lambda)\), which is the Wigner bound for finite-range interactions.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of compositeness for near-threshold states from the low-energy universality framework (Braaten 2004, Naidon 2016) and the compositeness theorem (Hyodo 2014) to include the effects of decay width and coupled channels. It builds on the resonance model without direct interactions (Braaten 2007, Kinugawa 2022) and the separable potential model (Hanhart 2014). The results enable quantitative evaluation of the molecular versus compact multiquark nature of exotic hadrons like \(T_{cc}\) and \(X(3872)\), showing that the naive expectation of molecular dominance for near-threshold states must be modified when decay and coupled-channels effects are significant.
