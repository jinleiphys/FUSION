# Triaxial bifurcations of rapidly rotating spheroids

> A rotating system, such as a star, liquid drop, or atomic nucleus, may rotate as an oblate spheroid about its symmetry axis or, if the angular velocity is greater than a critical value, as a triaxial ellipsoid about a principal axis. The oblate and triaxial equilibrium configurations minimize the total energy, a sum of the rotational kinetic energy plus the potential energy. For a star or galaxy the potential is the self-gravitating potential, for a liquid drop, the surface tension energy, and for a nucleus, the potential is the sum of the repulsive Coulomb energy plus the attractive surface energy. A simple, but accurate, Padé approximation to the potential function is used for the energy minimization problem that permits closed analytic expressions to be derived. In particular, the critical deformation and angular velocity for bifurcation from MacLaurin spheroids to Jacobi ellipsoids is determined analytically in the approximation.

# Triaxial bifurcations of rapidly rotating spheroids

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a simple Padé approximation to the exact gravitational self-energy of a triaxial ellipsoid yields closed-form analytic expressions for the MacLaurin-to-Jacobi bifurcation, with errors of about 1.5% in the critical eccentricity, smaller than the error inherent in assuming uniform density for real stars or galaxies.

## Method
The authors approximate the exact elliptic integral for the gravitational self-energy of a uniform-density triaxial ellipsoid by a two-term rational (Padé) function that respects symmetry, homogeneity, and the reference-sphere limit. They then minimize the total energy (rotational kinetic plus potential) as a function of the ellipsoid's semi-axes to find equilibrium shapes, solving the bifurcation condition analytically in the limit where the two unequal axes become equal. The same approach is applied to irrotational liquid drops (using a Padé approximation for surface area) and to the nuclear liquid-drop model (including Coulomb energy).

## Key numbers
- The exact MacLaurin spheroid angular velocity attains a global maximum at eccentricity \(e \approx 0.93\); the Padé approximation gives \(e \approx 0.929\) (one-tenth of one percent error).
- The bifurcation from MacLaurin spheroids to Jacobi ellipsoids occurs at eccentricity \(e = 0.826\) in the approximation; the exact value (Mayer, 1842) is \(e = 0.813\) (error about 1.5%).
- For the Padé approximation to the oblate spheroid gravitational energy, the coefficient of \(e^6\) in the series expansion is \(7/324 \approx 0.0216\), compared to the exact coefficient \(62/2835 \approx 0.0219\) (difference about 1%).
- In the flat-pancake limit (\(e \rightarrow 1\)), the Padé gravitational energy coefficient is \(8/5 = 1.60\), compared to the exact \(\pi/2 \approx 1.57\) (difference about 2%).
- For the surface-area Padé approximation of an oblate spheroid, the \(e^6\) coefficient is \(19/405 \approx 0.0469\), versus the exact \(136/2835 \approx 0.0480\) (difference about 2%); in the crêpe limit the coefficient is \(8/15 \approx 0.53\) versus the exact \(0.50\) (difference about 7%).
- For the nuclear liquid-drop model, the upper bound on the spheroid's angular velocity is \(\lim_{c\rightarrow\infty} (\mathcal{I}_0 / V_0^S) \, \omega^2 = 16/15\).

## Context
This work extends the classical results of MacLaurin (1742) and Jacobi (1834) on rotating self-gravitating fluids, and the exact bifurcation analysis of Chandrasekhar (1969) and Lebovitz (1967), by providing a simpler analytic derivation using Padé approximants. The same technique is applied to the nuclear liquid-drop model, following Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and Cohen and collaborators (1974), and to irrotational liquid drops relevant to microgravity experiments (Wang, 1996). The paper enables rapid analytic estimates of bifurcation points for rotating systems where exact elliptic integrals are cumbersome, and it clarifies that the approximation error is smaller than the physical modeling error from assuming uniform density.
