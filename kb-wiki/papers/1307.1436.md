# Imprint of the symmetry energy on the inner crust and strangeness content of neutron stars

> In this work we study the effect of the symmetry energy on several properties of neutron stars. First, we discuss its effect on the density, proton fraction and pressure of the neutron star crust-core transition. We show that whereas the first two quantities present a clear correlation with the slope parameter $L$ of the symmetry energy, no satisfactory correlation is seen between the transition pressure and $L$. However, a linear combination of the slope and curvature parameters at $ρ=0.1$ fm$^{-3}$ is well correlated with the transition pressure. In the second part we analyze the effect of the symmetry energy on the pasta phase. It is shown that the size of the pasta clusters, number of nucleons and the cluster proton fraction depend on the density dependence of the symmetry energy: a small $L$ gives rise to larger clusters. The influence of the equation of state at subsaturation densities on the extension of the inner crust of the neutron star is also discussed. Finally, the effect of the effect of the density dependence of the symmetry energy on the strangeness content of neutron stars is studied in the last part of the work. It is found that charged (neutral) hyperons appear at smaller (larger) densities for smaller values of the slope parameter $L$. A linear correlation between the radius and the strangeness content of a star with a fixed mass is also found.

## Key claim

The paper shows that while the transition density and proton fraction at the neutron star crust-core boundary are clearly correlated with the symmetry energy slope parameter \(L\), the transition pressure shows no such simple correlation; instead, a linear combination of the slope and curvature parameters of the symmetry energy evaluated at a subsaturation density of \(\rho = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) is well correlated with the transition pressure.

## Method

The authors used a combination of microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations with the Argonne V18 nucleon-nucleon interaction plus a three-body force, together with 27 Skyrme phenomenological models and several relativistic mean field (RMF) models (including NL3, GM1, TM1, FSU, IU-FSU, TW, DD-ME2, and DD-ME\(\delta\)) to study the equation of state of asymmetric nuclear matter. They introduced a generalized liquid-drop model (GLDM) that expands the energy around a reference density to identify correlations between the symmetry energy coefficients and the crust-core transition properties, and they compared thermodynamical spinodal calculations with dynamical spinodal calculations that include Coulomb and finite-size effects.

## Key numbers

- The transition density \(\rho_t\) and proton fraction \(Y_{p,t}\) show a clear correlation with the slope parameter \(L\) of the symmetry energy, as shown in Fig. 3 (left and middle panels).
- No satisfactory correlation is seen between the transition pressure \(P_t\) and \(L\) when different model families are compared (Fig. 3, right panel).
- A linear combination of the slope and curvature parameters at \(\rho = 0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) gives the correlation: \(P_{td}(L_{01}, K_{\rm sym,01}) = 9.59 \times 10^{-3} \times [L_{01} - 0.343 \times K_{\rm sym,01}] - 0.328\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\) (Eq. 7).
- Many nuclear models converge to a symmetry energy close to 25 MeV at \(\rho \simeq 0.12\) fm\(^{-3}\) and to a symmetry energy slope close to 100 MeV at \(\rho \simeq 0.06\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- A small \(L\) gives rise to larger pasta clusters, and a smaller \(L\) favors a wider slab phase in the inner crust.
- Charged hyperons appear at smaller densities and neutral hyperons at larger densities for smaller values of the slope parameter \(L\).
- A linear correlation exists between the radius and the strangeness content of a star with a fixed mass.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Ducoin et al. (2010, 2011) on correlations between symmetry energy coefficients and crust-core transition properties, and it complements studies of the pasta phase by Avancini et al. (2010) and Grill et al. (2012). The results enable improved predictions for the neutron star crust-core transition pressure, which is essential for interpreting pulsar glitch observations as discussed by Link et al. (1999), and they provide constraints on the role of hyperons in neutron star interiors, an open issue addressed by Providencia et al. (2013).
