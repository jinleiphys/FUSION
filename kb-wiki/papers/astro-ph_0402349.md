# Self-bound CFL stars in binary systems : are they "hidden" among the black hole candidates?

> The identification of black holes is one of the most important tasks of modern astrophysics. Candidates have been selected among binary stars based on a high mass function, and seriously considered when the lower mass limit exceeds ~ 3 solar masses. More recently the absence of (Type I) thermonuclear bursts has been advanced as an additional criterion in favor of the black hole interpretation, since the absence of a solid surface naturally precludes the accumulation and ignition of accreting material. We discuss in this Letter the possibility that self-bound stars made of CFL-paired quarks mimic the behavior of at least the low-mass end black holes as a result of a) higher maximum masses than ordinary neutron stars b) low steady luminosities due to the bare surface properties c) impossibility of generating Type I bursts because of the complete absence of normal matter crusts at their surfaces. These features caution about a positive identification of event horizons based on the lack of bursts.

# Self-bound CFL stars in binary systems: are they "hidden" among the black hole candidates?

## Key claim

Self-bound stars made of color-flavor locked (CFL) quark matter could mimic the behavior of low-mass black hole candidates in soft X-ray transients (SXTs) because they combine high maximum masses, low steady luminosities from bare surfaces, and a complete absence of Type I thermonuclear bursts due to lacking a normal matter crust. The authors caution that the absence of bursts, which has been advanced as evidence for event horizons, cannot be taken as a positive proof of black holes if such exotic stars exist in nature.

## Method

The authors constructed stellar sequences for self-bound CFL quark stars using equations of state derived from a model of electrically neutral, colorless CFL quark matter at zero temperature, with the bag constant B, strange quark mass m_s, and pairing gap Δ as free parameters. They calculated maximum masses and compactness for configurations where CFL matter is absolutely stable (energy per baryon below 939 MeV) and explored the parameter space to identify regimes yielding very massive stars. They then analyzed the surface properties of CFL stars, showing that the electrically neutral CFL phase does not allow electrons or a normal matter crust, and estimated the resulting photon and neutrino emission from accreting material.

## Key numbers

- Maximum masses of CFL stars can reach ~4 M⊙ for pairing gaps Δ ≥ 250 MeV, compared to ~2 M⊙ for unpaired strange quark matter.
- For the simplified case with a constant effective bag constant, the maximum mass scales as M_max ≈ 1.96 M⊙ / √(B_60) × (1 + δ), where δ = 0.15 × (Δ/100 MeV)² × (60 MeV fm⁻³ / B) and B_60 ≡ B / (60 MeV fm⁻³).
- The gravitational redshift of the maximum mass configuration has an asymptotic value z_max ≈ 0.35.
- The plasma frequency at the CFL star surface is of order ~20 MeV, similar to unpaired quark matter, suppressing equilibrium photon radiation.
- Accretion onto a bare CFL surface releases an additional energy term of ~30% over the normal gravitational release, due to exothermic fusion of incoming protons with the quark liquid.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of exotic compact objects as black hole impostors, following the proposal by Narayan and coworkers (Narayan, 2003) that the absence of Type I thermonuclear bursts in SXTs indicates event horizons, and the critique by Abramowicz, Kluźniak and Lasota (2003) that any star without a normal crust would also lack bursts. The authors build on their own previous work on CFL strange matter stability (Horvath and Lugones, 2003) and stellar models (Horvath and Lugones, 2004), and on the broader strange star literature (Zdunik, 2000; Page and Usov, 2002). The paper shows that CFL stars provide a concrete counterexample to the burst-absence criterion, and suggests that systems like SXT A0620-00 (with mass function f(M) ≥ 3 M⊙) could host such objects rather than black holes. Further work is needed to elaborate or rule out this association.
