# Theory of nuclear collective vibrations

> We review the theory of nuclear collective vibrations evolved over decades from phenomenological quasiclassical picture to sophisticated microscopic approaches. The major focus is put on the underlying microscopic mechanisms of emergent effects, which define the properties of giant resonances and soft modes. The response of atomic nuclei to electromagnetic and weak fields is discussed in detail. Astrophysical implications of the giant resonances and soft modes are outlined.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that the theory of nuclear collective vibrations can be derived from a single exact equation of motion for the nuclear response function, which then yields a hierarchy of approximations including random phase approximation (RPA), second RPA, particle-vibration coupling (PVC), and quasiparticle-phonon model (QPM) as special cases, rather than treating these as separate phenomenological approaches.

## Method

The authors derive the exact equation of motion for the two-time particle-hole response function from the many-body Hamiltonian with two-body interactions, then decompose the interaction kernel into static and dynamical (time-dependent) parts. They show how different levels of approximation for the correlated 2p2h propagator, from completely uncorrelated factorization (second RPA) through singly correlated (PVC) to doubly correlated (QPM/multiphonon), produce the major theoretical frameworks for nuclear collective vibrations.

## Key numbers

- The giant dipole resonance (GDR) centroid was observed above 10 MeV in early photoabsorption experiments by Baldwin and Klaiber (1947)
- The paper uses the convention ℏ = 1 throughout the formalism
- The spectral expansion of the response function runs over exact excited states |ν⟩ with energies ων = Eν − E0 measured from the ground-state energy

## Context

This review extends the theoretical lineage from Migdal (1945) and Goldhaber and Teller (1948) through the shell model of Mayer and Jensen (1955), RPA developed by Bohm and Pines (1951) and applied to nuclei by Elliott and Flowers (1957) and Brown and Bolsterli (1959), to modern beyond-mean-field approaches. It connects the phenomenological nuclear field theory of Bertsch, Bortignon, and Broglia (1983) and the quasiparticle-phonon model of Soloviev (1992) to the exact fermionic equations of motion, enabling a unified framework for describing giant resonances and soft modes with applications to astrophysics.
