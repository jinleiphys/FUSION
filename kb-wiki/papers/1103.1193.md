# A Long, Cold, Early r-process? Neutrino-induced Nucleosynthesis in He Shells Revisited

> We revisit a neutrino-driven r-process mechanism in the He shell of a core-collapse supernova, finding that it could succeed in early stars of metallicity < solar/1000, at relatively low temperatures and neutron densities, producing A ~ 130 and 195 abundance peaks over ~ 10-20 s. The mechanism is sensitive to the neutrino emission model and to neutrino oscillations. We discuss the implications of an r-process that could alter interpretations of abundance data from metal-poor stars, and point out the need for further calculations that include effects of the supernova shock.

# A Long, Cold, Early r-process? Neutrino-induced Nucleosynthesis in He Shells Revisited

## Key claim

The neutrino-driven r-process in the He shell of core-collapse supernovae could succeed in early stars of metallicity Z less than or equal to 10^{-3} solar metallicity, but only under specific conditions: an inverted neutrino mass hierarchy with full antineutrino_e to antineutrino_x conversion, antineutrino_e temperatures near 8 MeV, and progenitor masses in the ranges 11-16 or 49-75 solar masses. The mechanism produces the A ~ 130 and 195 abundance peaks over 10-20 seconds at relatively low temperatures and neutron densities, and turns off as progenitor metallicity increases.

## Method

The authors used stellar models u11-u75 of 11-75 solar mass stars with initial metallicity Z = 10^{-4} solar metallicity from Woosley, Heger, and Weaver (2002), focusing on the outer He shells at radii ~10^{10} cm. They evaluated nucleosynthesis using a modified big bang nucleosynthesis network for light nuclei (A < 16) combined with the Torch network for heavy-element flow from ^{56}Fe seeds, incorporating both neutral-current and charged-current neutrino reactions on ^{4}He with cross sections from Gazit (2009). Neutrino emission was modeled with Fermi-Dirac spectra assuming nominal temperatures of 4, 5.33, and 8 MeV for nu_e, antinu_e, and nu_x respectively, with a luminosity of 1.67 x 10^{52} erg/s per flavor and decay time of 3 seconds, and the effects of neutrino oscillations were included for both normal and inverted mass hierarchies.

## Key numbers

- Neutron number density in zone 597 of model u11: n_n ~ 10^{19} /cm^3, sufficient to drive an r-process
- Neutron capture rate on ^{56}Fe: ~10/s, with rate coefficient <v sigma_{n,gamma}(Fe)> ~ 10^{-18} cm^3/s
- Number of neutron captures per ^{56}Fe seed: N_cap = 88 at t=7 s, N_cap = 226 at t=20 s
- Time to reach A ~ 130 peak: ~10 s; time to reach A ~ 195 peak: ~18-20 s
- Total yield of heavy r-elements per supernova: Delta M_r ~ 10^{-8} solar masses, comparable to ~4 x 10^{-8} solar masses in the Sun
- Metallicity threshold: r-process conditions not found beyond Z ~ 10^{-3} solar metallicity
- Charged-current reaction rate per ^{4}He nucleus: lambda_{antinu_e alpha}^{CC}(0) = 8.35 x 10^{-7} /s for T_{antinu_e} = 8 MeV (inverted hierarchy)
- Collapse time for He shell material: tau_coll ~ 102 s for r_{10}=1, M=1 solar mass, alpha=0.6
- Shock arrival time: tau_sh ~ 21.8 s for typical parameters

## Context

This work revisits and extends the Epstein, Colgate, and Haxton (ECH) mechanism from 1988, which proposed neutrino-induced neutron production in He zones of low-metallicity supernovae but was previously critiqued by Woosley, Hartmann, Hoffman, and Haxton (1990) for being viable only in low-metallicity, compact supernovae. The key advance is the inclusion of the charged-current reaction ^{4}He(antinu_e, e^+ n)^{3}H as an efficient neutron source, which earlier re-examinations focusing only on neutral-current reactions had not found sufficient for producing nuclei above A=80. The mechanism enables a possible multiple-r-process explanation of Galactic chemical evolution, where this cold, early r-process operates at very low metallicities and could merge into other r-processes such as neutron star mergers that may only be viable at higher metallicities (Qian, 2000). The authors identify the need for further calculations including supernova shock effects and resolution of discrepancies in ^{12}C and ^{16}O neutron capture cross sections between nuclear data libraries.
