# Nuclear Dipole Response in the Finite-Temperature Relativistic Time Blocking Approximation

> The radiative neutron capture reaction rates of the r-process nucleosynthesis are immensely affected by the microscopic structure of the low-energy spectra of compound nuclei. The relativistic (quasiparticle) time blocking approximation (R(Q)TBA) has successfully provided a good description of the low-energy strength, in particular, the strength associated with pygmy dipole resonance, describing transitions from and to the nuclear ground state. The finite-temperature generalization of this method is designed for thermally excited compound nuclei and has the potential to enrich the fine structure of the dipole strength, especially in the low-energy region. The finite-temperature RTBA equations are derived using the Matsubara Green's function formalism. We show that with the help of a temperature-dependent projection operator on the subspace of the imaginary time it is possible to reduce the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the nuclear response function to a single frequency variable equation also at finite temperatures. The approach is implemented self-consistently in the framework of quantum hadrodynamics and keeps the ability of connecting the high-energy scale of heavy mesons and the low-energy domain of nuclear medium polarization effects in a parameter-free way. The presented calculations of the dipole response within a self-consistent relativistic framework reveal that, although the Landau damping plays the leading role in the temperature evolution of the strength distribution, (i) at moderate temperatures the PVC effects remain almost as strong as at $T=0$ and (ii) at high temperatures they are tremendously reinforced because of the formation of the new collective low-energy modes. In the dipole channel, the latter effect is responsible for the "disappearance" of the high-frequency GDR or, in other words, brings the GDR to the low-energy domain.

## Key claim

The finite-temperature generalization of the relativistic time blocking approximation (FT-RTBA) reveals that, although Landau damping dominates the temperature evolution of the dipole strength distribution, particle-vibration coupling (PVC) effects remain almost as strong as at T=0 at moderate temperatures and are tremendously reinforced at high temperatures due to the formation of new collective low-energy modes, which can bring the giant dipole resonance (GDR) into the low-energy domain.

## Method

The authors derived the finite-temperature relativistic time blocking approximation (FT-RTBA) equations using the Matsubara Green's function formalism, employing a temperature-dependent projection operator on the subspace of imaginary time to reduce the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the nuclear response function to a single frequency variable equation. The approach was implemented self-consistently in the framework of quantum hadrodynamics, connecting the high-energy scale of heavy mesons with the low-energy domain of nuclear medium polarization effects in a parameter-free way. The method was applied to the medium-light nuclei ⁴⁸Ca and ⁶⁸Ni, and the medium-heavy nuclei ¹⁰⁰,¹²⁰,¹³²Sn.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energies E* of the considered compound nuclei were found to increase quickly starting from temperatures 0.5 ≤ T ≤ 1.0 MeV.
- The low-energy part of the dipole strength distribution is moderately enhanced at temperatures T ≤ 4.0 MeV and increases dramatically above this temperature range.
- The width of the strength distribution grows rapidly with temperature at T ≥ 1.0 MeV.
- The traditional view of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) as an oscillation of the weakly bound neutron excess against the isospin-saturated core is nearly maintained up to T = 5.0 MeV and changes to a GDR-like pattern above this temperature.
- The collective behavior of the PDR disappears in the range of temperatures 1.0 ≤ T ≤ 5.0 MeV and restores beyond this range.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic time blocking approximation (RTBA) developed by Litvinova, Ring, and Tselyaev (2007-2013) from zero temperature to finite temperature, building on earlier finite-temperature approaches such as FT-RPA (Goodman 1981, Sagawa 1984) and FT-QRPA (Litvinova, Kamerdzhiev, Tselyaev 2003; Khan, Van Giai, Grasso 2004). The method enables systematic studies of both low-energy pygmy dipole resonance and high-energy giant dipole resonance in compound nuclei relevant for r-process nucleosynthesis, addressing limitations of earlier approaches that used simplified interactions or lacked self-consistency.
