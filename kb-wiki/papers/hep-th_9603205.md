# The Vacuum in Light-Cone Field Theory

> This is an overview of the problem of the vacuum in light-cone field theory, stressing its close connection to other puzzles regarding light-cone quantization. I explain the sense in which the light-cone vacuum is ``trivial,'' and describe a way of setting up a quantum field theory on null planes so that it is equivalent to the usual equal-time formulation. This construction is quite helpful in resolving the puzzling aspects of the light-cone formalism. It furthermore allows the extraction of effective Hamiltonians that incorporate vacuum physics, but that act in a Hilbert space in which the vacuum state is simple. The discussion is fairly informal, and focuses mainly on the conceptual issues. [Talk presented at {\sc Orbis Scientiae 1996}, Miami Beach, FL, January 25--28, 1996. To appear in the proceedings.]

## Key claim

The light-cone vacuum is not truly trivial; the apparent triviality is an artifact of infrared regulators that remove the vacuum structure from the outset, and a properly regulated light-cone formulation that includes boundary degrees of freedom is equivalent to the equal-time formulation and yields a complicated vacuum state.

## Method

The paper constructs a quantum field theory on null planes by using a finite-volume box as an infrared regulator and treating the initial-value problem on two intersecting null surfaces (constant \(x^+\) and constant \(x^-\)) rather than on a single null plane. For scalar and Fermi fields in 1+1 dimensions, the fields are expanded in Fourier modes on both surfaces, with commutation relations determined by requiring consistency with the Heisenberg equations and the Poincaré algebra. The conserved charges (including the Hamiltonian) receive contributions from both wings of the initial-value surface, ensuring equivalence to the equal-time formulation.

## Key numbers

* For a free massless scalar in 1+1 dimensions in a box of length \(2L\), the zero-momentum mode operators satisfy \([\psi, \phi_0] = i/4\), leading to a Fock space built from \(a_q\), \(\widetilde{a}_q\), and \(b\) operators.
* For a free massless Dirac fermion in 1+1 dimensions, the anticommutation relations are \(\{b_n, b^\dagger_m\} = \{d_n, d^\dagger_m\} = \{\beta_n, \beta^\dagger_m\} = \{\delta_n, \delta^\dagger_m\} = \delta_{n,m}\) with \(k^\pm_n = n\pi/L\) for odd \(n\).
* For a massive Dirac fermion, the mixed anticommutator is \(\{\psi_+(-L, x^-), \psi_-^\dagger(-L, y^-)\} = -(im/4)\epsilon(x^- - y^-)\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of McCartor (1995) on missing degrees of freedom in light-cone quantization of massless fermions, and addresses the "classic puzzles" of light-cone field theory including the Cauchy problem on null planes, boundary conditions, and the discontinuous treatment of massless versus massive fields. The construction enables the extraction of effective Hamiltonians that incorporate vacuum physics while acting in a Hilbert space with a simple vacuum state, and it provides a framework for understanding why certain calculations (e.g., the Schwinger model spectrum) succeed despite ignoring these subtleties.
