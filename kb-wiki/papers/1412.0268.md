# Impact of the phonon coupling on the photon strength function

> The pygmy dipole resonances and photon strength functions in stable and unstable Ni and Sn isotopes are calculated within the microscopic self-consistent version of the extended theory of finite fermi systems which includes phonon coupling effects.The Skyrme forces SLy4 is used. A pygmy dipole resonance in $^{72}$Ni is predicted at the mean energy of 12.4 MeV exhausting 25.7\% of the total energy-weighted sum rule. The microscopically obtained photon E1 strength functions are compared with available experimental data and used to calculate nuclear reaction properties. Average radiative widths and radiative neutron capture cross sections have been calculated taking the phonon coupling into account as well as the uncertainties caused by various microscopic level density models. In all three quantities considered, the contribution of phonon coupling turned out to be significant and is found necessary to explain available experimental data.

# Impact of the phonon coupling on the photon strength function

## Key claim
Phonon coupling (PC) effects, included via the self-consistent extended theory of finite Fermi systems (ETFFS) in the quasiparticle time blocking approximation (QTBA), are necessary to reproduce experimental photon strength functions, average radiative widths, and radiative neutron capture cross sections in stable and unstable Ni and Sn isotopes, but the agreement depends on the choice of nuclear level density model and is not perfect for all isotopes.

## Method
The authors performed self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the SLy4 Skyrme force to obtain ground states, then applied the QTBA method which includes both quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) and phonon coupling effects, with the single-particle continuum treated in discrete form. The resulting E1 photon strength functions were used as input to the Hauser-Feshbach reaction codes TALYS and EMPIRE to compute radiative neutron capture cross sections and average radiative widths for 13 Sn and Ni isotopes, with uncertainties estimated by using three different nuclear level density models (back-shifted Fermi gas, Generalized Superfluid model, and HFB plus Combinatorial model).

## Key numbers
- A pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) in $^{72}$Ni is predicted at mean energy 12.4 MeV exhausting 25.7% of the total energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) in the QTBA calculation, compared to 14.7% in QRPA.
- For $^{68}$Ni, the PDR centroid energy is 10.8 MeV exhausting 8.7% of EWSR (QTBA), in good agreement with experimental data of approximately 11 MeV and about 5% of EWSR.
- The average radiative width $\Gamma_{\gamma}$ for $^{62}$Ni calculated with QTBA and the HFB plus Combinatorial nuclear level density model is 2117 meV, compared to the experimental value of 2000 (300) meV.
- For $^{118}$Sn, the QTBA $\Gamma_{\gamma}$ value with the HFB plus Combinatorial model is 58.1 meV, compared to the experimental value of 117 (20) meV from Muhab (year not specified) and 80 (20) meV from RIPL-2 (year not specified).
- The PC effect increases the QRPA contribution to $\Gamma_{\gamma}$ by about 50 to 200% for stable nuclei, except for $^{122}$Sn and $^{124}$Sn where the increase is limited.

## Context
This work extends the self-consistent QRPA approach by including phonon coupling effects, which were previously considered in the quasiparticle-phonon model (Soloviev, 1976; Voronov and Guseva, year not specified) and in relativistic QTBA calculations for $^{68}$Ni (Litvinova et al., 2010). The results show that standard phenomenological models like the Enhanced Generalized Lorentzian (EGLO) model (RIPL-2, year not specified) and QRPA without PC fail to reproduce experimental data from the Oslo method (Toft et al., year not specified) and radiative width compilations (Muhab, year not specified; RIPL-2, year not specified). The QTBA method enables systematic predictions for unstable neutron-rich nuclei relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis (Arnould et al., 2007) and nuclear data applications, but remains to be applied to the full set of nuclei of astrophysical interest and compared with alternative PC approaches.
