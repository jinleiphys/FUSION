# Symmetry energy of dilute warm nuclear matter

> The symmetry energy of nuclear matter is a fundamental ingredient in the investigation of exotic nuclei, heavy-ion collisions and astrophysical phenomena. New data from heavy-ion collisions can be used to extract the free symmetry energy and the internal symmetry energy at subsaturation densities and temperatures below 10 MeV. Conventional theoretical calculations of the symmetry energy based on mean-field approaches fail to give the correct low-temperature, low-density limit that is governed by correlations, in particular by the appearance of bound states. A recently developed quantum statistical (QS) approach that takes the formation of clusters into account predicts symmetry energies that are in very good agreement with the experimental data. A consistent description of the symmetry energy is given that joins the correct low-density limit with quasiparticle approaches valid near the saturation density.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a quantum statistical (QS) approach which includes cluster correlations in the medium can reproduce the experimentally extracted symmetry energy at subsaturation densities and temperatures below 10 MeV, whereas conventional mean-field approaches fail in this regime because they do not account for bound-state formation.

## Method

The authors extracted the free symmetry energy from heavy-ion collision data (64Zn on 92Mo and 197Au at 35 MeV per nucleon) using an isoscaling analysis of Z=1 ejectile yields, with temperatures determined via the Albergo H-He double yield ratio method. They then derived the internal symmetry energy by combining the measured free symmetry energy with symmetry entropy values from a nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) model. These experimental results were compared to predictions from a quantum statistical (QS) approach that includes medium-modified light clusters (A ≤ 4) within a relativistic mean-field (RMF) framework with density-dependent couplings, as well as to standard RMF calculations without clusters.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of the underlying RMF model: n0 = 0.149 fm−3, with symmetry energy Esym(n0) = 32.73 MeV
- Experimental densities range from 0.00165 fm−3 to 0.00636 fm−3 (approximately 1/100th to 1/20th of saturation density)
- Experimental temperatures range from 3.31 MeV to 7.54 MeV
- Experimental free symmetry energies Fsym range from 5.64 MeV to 11.83 MeV across the measured density-temperature points
- Experimental internal symmetry energies Esym range from 7.465 MeV to 8.858 MeV
- Self-consistent temperatures Tsc are about 10% lower than the Albergo-method temperatures
- Self-consistent internal symmetry energies Escsym are 15 to 20% higher than the QS model values at the original T and n

## Context

This work extends the quantum statistical approach of Typel (2009) and Ropke (2008) to compare directly with experimental data from heavy-ion collisions, specifically the earlier analysis by Kowalski (2006). It contradicts mean-field and Skyrme Hartree-Fock approaches (such as the MDI parametrization of Chen (2005) and Li (2008)) that predict the symmetry energy goes to zero at low density, whereas the data and QS model show a finite value. The results enable improved modeling of nuclear matter for supernova simulations and neutron star structure, and the authors note that future work should extend the approach to include heavier clusters (A > 4) and explore the very low-temperature regime where new phases may occur.
