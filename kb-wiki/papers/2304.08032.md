# Revisiting the Lithium abundance problem in Big-Bang nucleosynthesis

> One of the three testaments in favor of the big bang theory is the prediction of the primordial elemental abundances in the big-bang nucleosynthesis (BBN). The Standard BBN is a parameter-free theory due to the precise knowledge of the baryon-to-photon ratio of the Universe obtained from studies of the anisotropies of cosmic microwave background radiation. Although the computed abundances of light elements during primordial nucleosynthesis and those determined from observations are in good agreement throughout a range of nine orders of magnitude, there is still a disparity of $^7$Li abundance overestimated by a factor of $\sim 2.5$ when calculated theoretically. The number of light neutrino flavors, the neutron lifetime and the baryon-to-photon ratio in addition to the astrophysical nuclear reaction rates determine the primordial abundances. We previously looked into the impact of updating baryon-to-photon ratio and neutron lifetime and changing quite a few reaction rates on the yields of light element abundances in BBN. In this work, calculations are performed using new reaction rates for $^3$H(p,$γ$)$^4$He, $^6$Li(p,$γ$)$^7$Be, $^7$Be(p,$γ$)$^8$B, $^{13}$N(p,$γ$)$^{14}$O, $^7$Li(n,$γ$)$^8$Li and $^{11}$B(n,$γ$)$^{12}$B along with the latest measured value of neutron lifetime. We observe from theoretical calculations that these changes result in improvement by causing further reduction in the abundance of $^7$Li than calculated earlier.

# Revisiting the Lithium abundance problem in Big-Bang nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

Incorporating six new reaction rates and the most recent neutron lifetime measurement (877.75 s) into standard BBN calculations yields only a marginal further reduction in the predicted primordial 7Li abundance, which remains overestimated by a factor of approximately 2.25 relative to the highest observational limit. The authors conclude that even with these updates, the majority of the nuclear reactions considered have no significant impact on the lithium abundance problem.

## Method

The authors used the Kawano/Wagoner BBN code, updating the Maxwellian-averaged thermonuclear reaction rates for six reactions (3H(p,γ)4He, 6Li(p,γ)7Be, 7Be(p,γ)8B, 13N(p,γ)14O, 7Li(n,γ)8Li, and 11B(n,γ)12B) with new parametrizations based on refined astrophysical S-factor calculations. They combined these with the latest experimental neutron lifetime of 877.75 s and the Planck/WMAP baryon-to-photon ratio η = 6.0914 × 10−10, then computed primordial abundances and compared them to their own earlier results (Singh 2019) and other recent BBN calculations (Coc 2014, Coc 2015, Cyburt 2016, Iliadis 2020, Pisanti 2021).

## Key numbers

- 7Li/H relative abundance from this work: (4.421 ± 0.066) × 10−10, compared to the observed plateau value of (1.58+0.35−0.28) × 10−10 from Sbordone (2010)
- 4He mass fraction from this work: 0.2461 ± 0.0002, compared to the observed value 0.2453 ± 0.0034 from Aver (2020)
- D/H relative abundance from this work: (2.620 ± 0.031) × 10−5, compared to the observed value (2.527 ± 0.030) × 10−5 from Cooke (2018)
- 3He/H relative abundance from this work: (1.066 ± 0.005) × 10−5, compared to the observational upper limit (<1.1 ± 0.2) × 10−5 from Bania (2002)
- Neutron lifetime used: 877.75 +0.28/−0.16 (stat+syst) s from Gonzalez (2021), a decrease of about 2.55 s (0.29%) from the previous value of 880.3 ± 1.1 s
- Shortening the neutron lifetime by 2.55 s reduces the 4He mass fraction by 0.24% and the 7Li relative abundance by 0.14%
- The new reaction rates alone (with the old neutron lifetime) reduce 7Li/H from 4.447 × 10−10 to 4.427 × 10−10
- The lowest limit of the current theoretical 7Li estimate is still larger by a factor of 2.25 compared to the highest limit of the observed value

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Singh 2019, Mitra 2012) that updated thirty-five reaction rates and the baryon-to-photon ratio, achieving a 12% reduction in 7Li abundance. The present results show that adding six more reaction rates produces only marginal further improvement, consistent with findings from other recent BBN codes (Coc 2014, Coc 2015, Cyburt 2016) that also found little impact from expanding the reaction network. The paper notes that alternative approaches involving charged massive particles (CHAMPs) or metastable particles during BBN have been explored (Jedamzik 2008, Kusakabe 2008) but do not appear to offer a simultaneous solution to the 6,7Li problems. The authors suggest that further observational improvements in detecting primordial lithium in very metal-poor galactic halo stars may be needed to resolve the remaining discrepancy.
