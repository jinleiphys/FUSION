# Coupling dynamical and statistical mechanisms for baryonic cluster production in nucleus collisions of intermediate and high energies

> Central nucleus-nucleus collisions produce many new baryons and the nuclear clusters can be formed from these species. The phenomenological coalescence models were sufficiently good for description of light nuclei yields in a very broad range of collision energies. We demonstrate that in reality the coalescence process can be considered as 1) the formation of primary diluted excited baryon clusters and 2) their following statistical decay leading to the final cold fragment production. We argue that the formation of such excited systems from the interacting baryons is a natural consequence of the nuclear interaction at subnuclear densities resulting in the nuclear liquid-gas type phase transition in finite systems. In this way one can provide a consistent interpretation of the experimental fragment yields (FOPI data) including the important collision energy dependence in relativistic ion reactions. We investigate the regularities of this new kind of fragment production, for example, their yield, isospin, and kinetic energy characteristics. A generalization of such a clusterization mechanism for hypernuclear matter is suggested. The isotope yields and particle correlations should be adequate for studying these phenomena.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the coalescence process in nucleus-nucleus collisions should be understood not as direct formation of cold nuclei from baryons, but as a two-step mechanism: first, the formation of primary diluted excited baryon clusters, followed by their statistical decay into final cold fragments. This interpretation is presented as a natural consequence of nuclear interactions at subnuclear densities leading to a liquid-gas type phase transition in finite systems, and the authors claim it provides a consistent interpretation of experimental fragment yields including the collision energy dependence, but the claim is hedged by noting that the excitation energy of the coalescent clusters must be limited to around 10 MeV per nucleon for the statistical description to be valid.

## Method

The authors used the Dubna Cascade model (DCM) to generate baryon parameters after the dynamical stage of relativistic nucleus collisions, and also employed two alternative generators (G2 and G3) for the phase-space distribution of baryons: G2 uses microcanonical momentum phase-space generation with total momentum and energy conservation, while G3 assumes an explosive hydrodynamical-like process with velocities proportional to coordinate distances from the center of mass. They applied a coalescence of baryon (CB) model with a velocity coalescence parameter \(v_c\) to form primary clusters, then used the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) to describe the de-excitation and break-up of these hot clusters into final cold fragments.

## Key numbers

- The subnuclear density at which baryons can form clusters is around \(0.1\rho_0\), where \(\rho_0 \approx 0.15\) fm\(^{-3}\) is the normal nuclear density.
- The lifetime of excited nuclear systems in multifragmentation is 50–100 fm/c, and the densities before break-up are around 0.1–0.3\(\rho_0\).
- The excitation energy of finite thermalized nuclear systems is limited to around 8–10 MeV per nucleon, with values close to the binding energies of the systems.
- The coalescence parameter values used are \(v_c = 0.07\), 0.14, 0.22, and 0.28 \(c\), where \(c\) is the speed of light.
- Source energies considered for the baryon generators are 20, 50, and 200 A MeV (MeV per nucleon).
- The source sizes used in simulations include \(A_0 = 100\), \(Z_0 = 40\) and \(A_0 = 400\), \(Z_0 = 160\).
- The freeze-out volume size factor is \(F \approx 3\) for the expanded volume where nucleons can still interact.
- The cluster density assumed for subsequent statistical decay is \(\rho_c \approx \frac{1}{6} \rho_0\), corresponding to an average distance of around 2 fm between neighboring nucleons.

## Context

This work extends the coalescence model approach (previously applied by Tonczak (1983), Botvina (2017), and others) by incorporating the statistical multifragmentation framework developed for peripheral collisions by the ALADIN, EOS, ISIS, and FASA collaborations. The authors build on their own prior work on hypernuclei production (Botvina 2011, 2013, 2016, 2017) and generalize the mechanism to hypernuclear matter. The paper enables future experimental studies at facilities such as STAR at RHIC, ALICE at LHC, CBM at FAIR, and BM@N and MPD at NICA, by providing predictions for light nuclei and hypernuclei production in central high-energy collisions, and suggests that isotope yields and particle correlations are adequate for studying these phenomena.
