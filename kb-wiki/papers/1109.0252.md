# Light clusters in nuclear matter: Excluded volume versus quantum many-body approaches

> The formation of clusters in nuclear matter is investigated, which occurs e.g. in low energy heavy ion collisions or core-collapse supernovae. In astrophysical applications, the excluded volume concept is commonly used for the description of light clusters. Here we compare a phenomenological excluded volume approach to two quantum many-body models, the quantum statistical model and the generalized relativistic mean field model. All three models contain bound states of nuclei with mass number A <= 4. It is explored to which extent the complex medium effects can be mimicked by the simpler excluded volume model, regarding the chemical composition and thermodynamic variables. Furthermore, the role of heavy nuclei and excited states is investigated by use of the excluded volume model. At temperatures of a few MeV the excluded volume model gives a poor description of the medium effects on the light clusters, but there the composition is actually dominated by heavy nuclei. At larger temperatures there is a rather good agreement, whereas some smaller differences and model dependencies remain.

## Key claim

The excluded volume model can reasonably mimic the quantum many-body medium effects on light clusters at temperatures around 10 MeV, but gives a poor description at temperatures of a few MeV; however, at those lower temperatures the composition is actually dominated by heavy nuclei rather than light clusters, making the deficiencies less relevant for the overall equation of state.

## Method

The authors compared three models for symmetric nuclear matter at fixed temperatures: a phenomenological excluded volume (ExV) model based on nuclear statistical equilibrium with hard-sphere repulsion, the generalized relativistic mean-field (gRMF) model where light clusters contribute as sources for meson fields, and the quantum statistical (QS) model based on thermodynamic Green's functions with Pauli blocking and self-energy shifts. All three models used the same DD2 relativistic mean-field parametrization for nucleon interactions and included bound states of nuclei with mass number A ≤ 4, with the ExV model also extended to include all available heavy nuclei and temperature-dependent internal partition functions.

## Key numbers

- At T = 4 MeV, the ExV model without heavy nuclei shows a sudden turnover in composition at n_B ~ 0.036 fm⁻³, whereas the QS and gRMF models show continuous dissolution of clusters above n_B ~ 5 × 10⁻³ fm⁻³
- At T = 4 MeV, heavy nuclei (A > 4) in the ExV model exceed a mass fraction X' > 0.1 for densities above 1.4 × 10⁻³ fm⁻³ and exceed X' > 0.9 above 0.016 fm⁻³
- At T = 10 MeV, heavy nuclei mass fraction exceeds 0.1 at n_B ~ 0.013 fm⁻³ and reaches a maximum of ~0.55 at n_B ~ 0.052 fm⁻³
- The internal energy per baryon at T = 10 MeV differs by up to 5 MeV between models in the density region where heavy nuclei are not dominant
- The saturation density used in the ExV model is n_B⁰ = 0.149 fm⁻³ (from the DD2 parametrization)

## Context

This work extends the comparison of nuclear matter models initiated by Typel et al. (2009) using the gRMF model and Röpke (2009, 2011) using the QS model, and directly tests the excluded volume approach used in the HS equation of state tables (Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich, 2010) that are already available for supernova simulations. The results enable assessment of whether the computationally simpler excluded volume model can replace the more detailed quantum many-body treatments for astrophysical applications, and identify the density-temperature regimes where heavy nuclei dominate the composition and where model differences matter most for supernova dynamics.
