# How should one formulate, extract, and interpret `non-observables' for nuclei?

> Nuclear observables such as binding energies and cross sections can be directly measured. Other physically useful quantities, such as spectroscopic factors, are related to measured quantities by a convolution whose decomposition is not unique. Can a framework for these nuclear structure `non-observables' be formulated systematically so that they can be extracted from experiment with known uncertainties and calculated with consistent theory? Parton distribution functions in hadrons serve as an illustrative example of how this can be done. A systematic framework is also needed to address questions of interpretation, such as whether short-range correlations are important for nuclear structure.

# How should one formulate, extract, and interpret 'non-observables' for nuclei?

## Key claim
Nuclear quantities such as spectroscopic factors and momentum distributions are "non-observables" because their extraction from measured cross sections involves a convolution whose decomposition is not unique; a systematic framework analogous to parton distribution functions in hadrons is needed to control the scale and scheme dependence of these quantities, but the necessary ingredients (corresponding to asymptotic freedom, infra-red safety, and factorization for PDFs) may not all be present for nuclei, imposing intrinsic limitations that must be quantified.

## Method
The paper examines the theoretical ambiguity of non-observables by considering unitary transformations that modify short-range physics while leaving matrix elements of operators invariant, and uses the deuteron D-state probability as a concrete example of how spectroscopic factors change under such transformations. It then draws a detailed analogy to parton distribution functions in QCD, where factorization, renormalization group evolution, and scheme dependence are rigorously controlled, and argues that analogous frameworks (based on chiral effective field theory and renormalization group methods) are needed for nuclear structure quantities.

## Key numbers
- The D-state probability of the deuteron shows a theoretical uncertainty of roughly a factor of two under unitary transformations that change the short-range tensor strength.
- The S-state probability of the deuteron shows a theoretical uncertainty of order 10% under the same transformations.
- For systems with a large separation of scale, unitary transformations that change spectroscopic factors lead to shifts of order (kR)^n with kR << 1, where k is a typical momentum scale and R denotes the range of interactions.
- The momentum distribution for strongly interacting cold atom gases near the unitarity limit follows a universal power law 1/k^4 as k approaches infinity as long as kR << 1.

## Context
This work extends the discussion of unitary transformations and resolution dependence in nuclear forces from Bogner (2009) to the specific problem of non-observables like spectroscopic factors, and draws on the rigorous framework developed for parton distribution functions as described by Collins (2003) and Campbell (2006). The paper enables a systematic approach to questions such as whether short-range correlations are important for nuclear structure, and whether the extraction of spectroscopic factors from experiments like (e,e'p) can be made as controlled as PDF extractions from deep-inelastic scattering.
