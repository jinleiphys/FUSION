# Computational approaches to many-body dynamics of unstable nuclear systems

> The goal of this presentation is to highlight various computational techniques used to study dynamics of quantum many-body systems. We examine the projection and variable phase methods being applied to multi-channel problems of scattering and tunneling; here the virtual, energy-forbidden channels and their treatment are of particular importance. The direct time-dependent solutions using Trotter-Suzuki propagator expansion provide yet another approach to exploring the complex dynamics of unstable systems. While presenting computational tools, we briefly revisit the general theory of the quantum decay of unstable states. The list of questions here includes those of the internal dynamics in decaying systems, formation and evolution of the radiating state, and low-energy background that dominates at remote times. Mathematical formulations and numerical approaches to time-dependent problems are discussed using the quasi-stationary methods involving effective Non-Hermitian Hamiltonian formulation.

## Key claim

This presentation argues that standard projection methods for multi-channel scattering problems suffer from fundamental numerical instability and lack of convergence, particularly when virtual (energy-forbidden) channels are involved; the Variable Phase Method (VPM) and time-dependent Trotter-Suzuki propagator expansion are offered as computationally stable alternatives that preserve unitarity.

## Method

The author examines the projection method applied to a one-dimensional two-particle scattering problem with an infinite wall, where the intrinsic Hamiltonian uses a harmonic oscillator confinement; the resulting D-matrix equation is solved with increasing truncation N of the channel space. The Variable Phase Method reformulates the coupled-channel Schrödinger equation into first-order differential equations for dynamic reflection and transmission amplitudes. A time-dependent approach using the Trotter-Suzuki expansion propagates the wave function in small time steps via separate application of potential and kinetic operators in coordinate and momentum space, connected by Fast Fourier Transform. For the Time Dependent Continuum Shell Model, the evolution operator is factorized using a Chebyshev polynomial expansion, allowing evaluation of the Green's function for all energies simultaneously.

## Key numbers

- For the projection method, the phase shift (defined as \(e^{2i\delta}=-R_{00}\)) shows oscillatory and non-convergent behavior as truncation N increases; the problem is "particularly severe when the non-interacting particle of mass \(\mu_1\) is heavy" (mass ratios \(\mu_1/\mu_2 = 1, 2, 3, 5, 10\) are shown).
- In the time-dependent Trotter-Suzuki approach, the computational cost per time step is \(N \log(N)\) for a coordinate space discretized into N points.
- For the Chebyshev expansion, the number of terms needed for convergence at time \(t=\pi\tau\) is \(n_{\text{max}}(\tau) \approx e\pi\tau/2 \approx 4\tau\); for energy resolution \(\Delta E/N\) the propagation extends to \(\approx \tau N\) requiring \(n_{\text{max}} \approx 4N\) and thus \(2N\) matrix-vector multiplications.
- In Winter's model decay, the mean lifetime \(\tau = 0.65\) (in units where \(\hbar=2m=1\)) is known from the poles of the scattering matrix; the survival probability shows three regimes: pre-exponential, exponential, and post-exponential power-law decay \(S(t) \sim 1/t^{2\ell+3}\).

## Context

This work extends the author's prior development of the Continuum Shell Model (Volya 2005, 2006) and its time-dependent version (Volya 2009), addressing the computational challenges of coupling discrete shell-model states to the continuum of reaction states. The projection method failure is documented in earlier studies by Ahsan (2010, 2011) and Sakharuk (1999). The Variable Phase Method follows Babikov (1968), and the Trotter-Suzuki approach follows Trotter (1959) and Suzuki (1990). The time-dependent Chebyshev expansion method builds on techniques by Loh (2001) and Ikegami (2002). The non-exponential decay features in Winter's model are discussed with reference to Peshkin (2014). This work enables efficient computation of scattering observables and strength functions across all energies simultaneously, with particular relevance to weakly-bound and unbound nuclear systems.
