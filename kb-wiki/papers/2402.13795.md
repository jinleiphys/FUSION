# Estimating the dark matter halo velocity and surface temperature of some known pulsars due to dark matter capture

> Considering four known pulsars J1906+0746, J1933-6211, J2043+1711 and the Vela pulsar, we study the scenario of dark matter (DM) capture in neutron stars (NSs). For the purpose we choose four well-known relativistic mean field models to obtain the radius corresponding to the observed mass of these pulsars and consequently the scattering cross-section of DM with the different particles of the $β$ stable NS matter. The estimated DM-electron scattering cross-section in this work is stringent compared to the current direct detection experimental probe. We then compute the lower limit on the halo velocity of DM for the four pulsars from the knowledge of the upper limit on effective temperature of the individual pulsars. We also extend our work to calculate the value of the effective temperature with the different models using the fitted values of the halo velocity of DM of the four pulsars with respect to their distances from the galactic center. Our findings are consistent with the analysis of the observed data.

## Key claim

The authors derive lower limits on the dark matter halo velocity for four pulsars by combining the observed upper limits on their effective surface temperatures with neutron star radii obtained from four relativistic mean field models, and show that these lower limits are consistent with (i.e., less than) fitted halo velocity values from independent observational data.

## Method

The authors use four relativistic mean field (RMF) models (TM1, GM1, DD2, DD-MEX) to compute the equation of state of beta-stable neutron star matter, then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain the radius corresponding to the observed mass of each pulsar. From the upper limit on effective temperature for each pulsar, they compute the upper limit on surface temperature and the energy deposited per unit time by captured dark matter, which yields a lower limit on the dark matter halo velocity. They also invert the calculation: using fitted halo velocity values from Bhattacharjee (2013), they compute the effective temperature for each pulsar and compare it to the observed upper limits.

## Key numbers

* For pulsar J1906+0746 (mass 1.29 solar masses, distance 7.4 kpc), the lower limit on halo velocity ranges from 3.434e-9 to 3.572e-9 across the four RMF models, using an upper limit effective temperature of log10(T_infinity) = 5.93.
* For pulsar J1933-6211 (mass 1.4 solar masses, distance 0.65 kpc), the lower limit on halo velocity ranges from 1.058e-8 to 1.094e-8 across the four models, using log10(T_infinity) = 5.64.
* For pulsar J2043+1711 (mass 1.38 solar masses, distance 1.389 kpc), the lower limit on halo velocity ranges from 3.039e-9 to 3.143e-9 across the four models, using log10(T_infinity) = 5.91.
* For the Vela pulsar (mass 1.88 solar masses, distance 0.28 kpc), the lower limit on halo velocity ranges from 4.815e-9 to 4.844e-9 across the four models, using log10(T_infinity) = 5.82.
* The threshold scattering cross-section for dark matter with neutrons, protons, electrons, and muons is of order 10^{-39} for J1906+0746, J1933-6211, and J2043+1711, and slightly above 10^{-40} for the Vela pulsar.
* The dark matter-electron scattering cross-section obtained is several orders of magnitude smaller than current direct detection experimental bounds from SENSEI, EDELWEISS, PandaX-II, XENON1T, SuperCDMS, DAMIC, and DarkSide-50.

## Context

This work extends the kinetic heating formalism of Bell (2019) and Joglekar (2020) to four specific pulsars with well-measured masses and positions, using four different RMF nuclear models to account for the uncertainty in neutron star radii. The derived lower limits on halo velocity are consistent with the fitted halo velocity data from Bhattacharjee (2013), and the predicted effective temperatures from those fitted velocities are all below the observational upper limits from Prinz (2015). The stringent dark matter-electron scattering cross-section estimates provide a target for future direct detection experiments beyond current probes.
