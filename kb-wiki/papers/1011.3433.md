# Recurrence and differential relations for spherical spinors

> We present a comprehensive table of recurrence and differential relations obeyed by spin one-half spherical spinors (spinor spherical harmonics) $Ω_{κμ}(\mathbf{n})$ used in relativistic atomic, molecular, and solid state physics, as well as in relativistic quantum chemistry. First, we list finite expansions in the spherical spinor basis of the expressions $\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}\,Ω_{κμ}(\mathbf{n})$ and {$\mathbf{A}\cdot(\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{C})\, Ω_{κμ}(\mathbf{n})$}, where $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, and $\mathbf{C}$ are either of the following vectors or vector operators: $\mathbf{n}=\mathbf{r}/r$ (the radial unit vector), $\mathbf{e}_{0}$, $\mathbf{e}_{\pm1}$ (the spherical, or cyclic, versors), $\boldsymbolσ$ (the $2\times2$ Pauli matrix vector), $\hat{\mathbf{L}}=-i\mathbf{r}\times\boldsymbol{\nabla}I$ (the dimensionless orbital angular momentum operator; $I$ is the $2\times2$ unit matrix), $\hat{\mathbf{J}}=\hat{\mathbf{L}}+1/2\boldsymbolσ$ (the dimensionless total angular momentum operator). Then, we list finite expansions in the spherical spinor basis of the expressions $\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B}\,F(r)Ω_{κμ}(\mathbf{n})$ and $\mathbf{A}\cdot(\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{C})\, F(r)Ω_{κμ}(\mathbf{n})$, where at least one of the objects $\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{B}$, $\mathbf{C}$ is the nabla operator $\boldsymbol{\nabla}$, while the remaining ones are chosen from the set $\mathbf{n}$, $\mathbf{e}_{0}$, $\mathbf{e}_{\pm1}$, $\boldsymbolσ$, $\hat{\mathbf{L}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{J}}$.

## Key claim

This paper provides a comprehensive, systematically organized table of recurrence and differential relations for spin one-half spherical spinors, filling a gap in the literature where these functions had been treated less exhaustively than scalar or vector spherical harmonics. The author notes that the specific signs in many of the listed relations depend on the phase conventions chosen in the defining equations (Eqs. 2.1.7, 2.1.9, 2.1.10) and the sign of the Pauli matrix sigma_y, so users working with different conventions may need to adjust signs accordingly.

## Method

The author defines spin one-half spherical spinors Omega_{kappa mu}(n) as two-component functions of the unit vector n, labeled by the indices kappa (nonzero integer) and mu (half-integer), with the orbital angular momentum quantum number l determined by kappa. The paper then lists finite expansions of various operator expressions acting on these spinors, where the operators are combinations of the radial unit vector n, cyclic versors e_0 and e_{pm1}, the Pauli matrix vector sigma, the orbital angular momentum operator hat{L}, and the total angular momentum operator hat{J}. The relations are organized into three subsections: algebraic recurrence relations (Section III.1), differential relations of the first kind involving hat{L} and hat{J} (Section III.2), and differential relations of the second kind involving the nabla operator acting on F(r)Omega_{kappa mu}(n) (Section III.3).

## Key numbers

- The spherical spinors are defined for kappa in {pm1, pm2, ...} and mu in {-|kappa|+1/2, -|kappa|+3/2, ..., |kappa|-1/2}
- The relation between kappa and the orbital quantum number l is: l = |kappa+1/2| - 1/2, which equals kappa for kappa>0 and -kappa-1 for kappa<0 (Eq. 2.1.8)
- The total angular momentum quantum number j relates to kappa as: j = |kappa| - 1/2 (Eq. 2.1.11)
- The operator hat{L}^2 acting on a spherical spinor yields eigenvalue kappa(kappa+1) (Eq. 3.2.8)
- The operator hat{J}^2 acting on a spherical spinor yields eigenvalue kappa^2 - 1/4 (Eq. 3.2.9)
- The operator n dot sigma acting on Omega_{kappa mu}(n) yields -Omega_{-kappa mu}(n) (Eq. 3.1.3)
- The operator sigma dot hat{L} acting on Omega_{kappa mu}(n) yields -(kappa+1)Omega_{kappa mu}(n) (Eq. 3.2.3)

## Context

This work extends the systematic treatment of spherical harmonics to spin one-half spherical spinors, which had previously been treated only superficially in standard angular momentum theory textbooks such as Rose (1957), Edmonds (1960), Biedenharn and Louck (1981), and even in the comprehensive treatise by Varshalovich et al. (1975). The paper is part of the author's research program (see also Szmytkowski, 2005) aimed at providing a complete reference for these functions, which are essential for separating spherical variables in the central-field Dirac problem and for constructing relativistic atomic, molecular, and solid-state wave functions. The tabulated relations enable researchers to avoid ad hoc derivations of particular properties, as had been necessary in works such as Bechler (1993), Rutkowski (1997), Szmytkowski (2002, 2004), and Mielnik-Pyszczorski (2006).
