# Three-boson stability for boosted interactions towards the zero-range limit

> We study the three-boson bound-state mass and wave functions for ground and excited states within the three-body relativistic framework with Kamada and Glöcke boosted potentials in the limit of a zero-range interaction. We adopt a nonrelativistic short-range separable potential, with Yamaguchi and Gaussian form factors, and drive them towards the zero-range limit by letting the form factors' momentum scales go to large values while keeping the two-body binding fixed. We show that the three-boson relativistic masses and wave functions are model-independent towards the zero-range limit, and the Thomas collapse is avoided, while the nonrelativistic limit kept the Efimov effect. Furthermore, the stability in the zero-range limit is a result of the reduction of boosted potential with the increase of the virtual pair center of mass momentum within the three-boson system. Finally, we compare the present results with Light-Front and Euclidean calculations.

## Key claim

The three-boson system with Kamada and Glöckle boosted potentials remains stable in the zero-range limit, avoiding the Thomas collapse, but only because the boosted potential is damped by the spectator momentum, providing an effective three-body repulsion that counterbalances the singular attraction; the nonrelativistic limit of the same framework retains the Efimov effect.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Faddeev integral equation in momentum space for three identical bosons, using boosted two-body potentials constructed from nonrelativistic separable potentials with Yamaguchi and Gaussian form factors. The boosted potentials were obtained by solving a quadratic integral equation via an iterative scheme (Kamada and Glöckle, 2007), and the form factors were driven toward the zero-range limit by increasing their momentum-scale parameters (beta for Yamaguchi, Lambda for Gaussian) to large values while keeping the two-body binding energy fixed at -2.225 MeV. The resulting three-body binding energies and Faddeev wave-function components were computed for ground and first excited states.

## Key numbers

- Two-body binding energy fixed at -2.225 MeV (deuteron binding energy) throughout the study.
- For Yamaguchi potential with beta = 200 fm^-1, ground-state binding energy E_t^(0) = -454.1 MeV; first excited state E_t^(1) = -6.076 MeV.
- For Yamaguchi potential with beta = 2000 fm^-1, ground-state binding energy E_t^(0) = -353.7 MeV; first excited state E_t^(1) = -4.891 MeV.
- For Gaussian potential with Lambda = 200 fm^-1, ground-state binding energy E_t^(0) = -465.2 MeV; first excited state E_t^(1) = -6.176 MeV.
- For Gaussian potential with Lambda = 2000 fm^-1, ground-state binding energy E_t^(0) = -357.7 MeV; first excited state E_t^(1) = -4.914 MeV.
- Power-law scaling of the Faddeev component in the region m less than k less than beta (or Lambda): psi^(0)(p, k=0) ~ p^{-1.9} and psi^(0)(p=0, k) ~ k^{-3.7}.
- Boosted potential matrix elements decrease as ~ 1/k for large spectator momentum k, while the two-body amplitude in Light-Front and Bethe-Salpeter approaches decays as ~ 1/log(k).

## Context

This work extends the study of relativistic three-boson stability to the instant-form dynamics framework with boosted potentials, complementing earlier results from Light-Front (Carbonell and Karmanov, 2002; Karmanov and Maris, 2003) and Euclidean Bethe-Salpeter (Ydrefors and Frederico, 2017) calculations, which also found stability but with stronger attraction. The results show that the boosted-potential approach yields weaker three-body binding than those field-theory-based methods, and the wave functions exhibit a universal power-law decay rather than the log-periodic behavior characteristic of Thomas-collapsed nonrelativistic states (Skornyakov and Ter-Martirosyan, 1956; Danilov, 1961). The framework may be applied beyond hadron physics to condensed-matter systems with hyperbolic dispersion relations, such as transition metal dichalcogenides (Wang et al., 2018) and hexagonal boron nitride (Zhang et al., 2017).
