# Dominance of Nuclear Processes in the Dissociation of 8B

## Key claim
Nuclear excitation processes dominate the break-up of 8B in collisions with heavy-ions even at energies well below the Coulomb barrier, contrary to the conventional assumption that Coulomb dissociation is the primary mechanism for weakly-bound systems. This dominance arises because the weak binding energy of 8B causes nuclear transition densities to extend to unusually large radial ranges (15–25 fm), making nuclear couplings comparable to or larger than Coulomb couplings at distances where standard treatments would consider nuclear forces negligible.

## Method
The authors constructed microscopic formfactors for inelastic transitions of the valence proton in 8B from a bound p3/2 state to continuum s and d states, treating nuclear and Coulomb excitation fields on equal footing via a folding of transition densities with Woods-Saxon nuclear potentials and Coulomb potentials. They employed a semiclassical coupled-channel formalism to calculate reaction amplitudes and cross sections for 8B break-up on 208Pb at 46 MeV/nucleon and on 58Ni at 25.3 MeV, including dipole and octupole multipolarities and accounting for absorption via an optical potential.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy of 8B: −0.14 MeV (experimentally known value used to tune the single-particle potential)
- Nuclear transition densities extend to radial ranges of 15–25 fm for weakly-bound systems
- For 8B + 208Pb at 46 MeV/nucleon: nuclear coupling dominates at center-of-mass distances from 25 fm inwards; safe to ignore nuclear effects for impact parameters larger than b ≈ 30 fm
- For 8B + 58Ni at 25.3 MeV (subbarrier): nuclear processes peak at θ ≈ 80°, corresponding to a distance of closest approach of 12 fm; Coulomb and nuclear contributions are equal at θ = 40°, corresponding to a distance of closest approach of about 20 fm
- For the dipole p3/2 → s1/2 transition at 46 MeV/nucleon: the nuclear component builds its contribution mostly from impact parameters between 10 and 20 fm, where it dominates over the Coulomb part
- For octupole transitions: using the standard r^λ-response instead of the full Coulomb formfactor overestimates couplings by orders of magnitude

## Context
This work extends earlier investigations of single-particle response in weakly-bound systems near the drip-line (refs. 1–3 in the paper) and directly challenges the conventional wisdom that Coulomb dissociation is the dominant break-up mechanism for systems like 8B. The authors explicitly compare with the experimental studies of 8B break-up on lead at 46 MeV/nucleon (ref. 4) and on nickel at 25.3 MeV (ref. 5), arguing that previous analyses relying solely on Coulomb dissociation may need to be dismissed because nuclear contributions are substantial. The paper enables future work to incorporate both nuclear and Coulomb couplings properly in break-up studies of drip-line nuclei, and suggests that an alternative formulation treating dissociation as proton transfer to the target (ref. 12) could provide complementary insight.
