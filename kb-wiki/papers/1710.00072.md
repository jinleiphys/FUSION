# Nuclear deformation in the laboratory frame

> We develop a formalism for calculating the distribution of the axial quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame within the rotationally invariant framework of the configuration-interaction shell model. The calculation is carried out using a finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo method. We apply this formalism to isotope chains of even-mass samarium and neodymium nuclei, and show that the quadrupole distribution provides a model-independent signature of nuclear deformation. Two technical advances are described that greatly facilitate the calculations. The first is to exploit the rotational invariance of the underlying Hamiltonian to reduce the statistical fluctuations in the Monte Carlo calculations. The second is to determine quadruple invariants from the distribution of the axial quadrupole operator. This allows us to extract effective values of the intrinsic quadrupole shape parameters without invoking an intrinsic frame or a mean-field approximation.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the distribution of the axial quadrupole operator in the laboratory frame, calculated within a rotationally invariant configuration-interaction shell model framework, provides a model-independent signature of nuclear deformation, even though the exact expectation value of the quadrupole operator is always zero for a rotationally invariant Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors used a finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) method to calculate the distribution of the axial quadrupole operator \(\hat{Q}_{20}\) in the laboratory frame for even-mass samarium and neodymium isotope chains. They employed a Fourier representation of the delta function to project onto eigenvalues of \(\hat{Q}_{20}\) and introduced an angle-averaging technique using sets of 6, 10, or 21 rotation angles to reduce statistical fluctuations and improve thermalization of the Monte Carlo sampling. Quadrupole invariants were then extracted from the moments of the laboratory-frame distribution to determine effective intrinsic quadrupole shape parameters without invoking a mean-field approximation.

## Key numbers

*   For the deformed nucleus \(^{154}\)Sm, the second moment \(\langle \hat{Q}_{20}^2 \rangle\) and third moment \(\langle \hat{Q}_{20}^3 \rangle\) showed improved thermalization and decorrelation when using 6-angle and 21-angle averaging, respectively, compared to no averaging (Fig. 2).
*   The low-temperature (\(T=0.1\) MeV) distribution for \(^{162}\)Dy was qualitatively similar to a rigid-rotor ground-state distribution with an intrinsic quadrupole moment \(q_0\) taken from the HFB value (Fig. 3).
*   The distribution for the spherical nucleus \(^{144}\)Nd was close to Gaussian at all temperatures, with only a slight skew at low temperatures (Fig. 4).
*   The second-order quadrupole invariant \(\langle \hat{Q} \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle\) was compared between AFMC and finite-temperature HFB for Sm and Nd isotopes, showing a smooth crossover in AFMC versus a sharp phase transition in HFB (Figs. 6 and 7).
*   The relations between moments of \(\hat{Q}_{20}\) and quadrupole invariants are given by: \(\langle \hat{Q} \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle = 5 \langle \hat{Q}_{20}^2 \rangle\), \(\langle (\hat{Q} \times \hat{Q})^{(2)} \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle = -5 \sqrt{7/2} \langle \hat{Q}_{20}^3 \rangle\), and \(\langle (\hat{Q} \cdot \hat{Q})^2 \rangle = (35/3) \langle \hat{Q}_{20}^4 \rangle\) (Eqs. 12-14).
*   Effective deformation parameters are defined as \(\beta = \frac{\sqrt{5 \pi}}{3 r_0^2 A^{5/3}} \langle \hat{Q} \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle^{1/2}\) and \(\cos 3\gamma = -\sqrt{7/2} \langle (\hat{Q} \times \hat{Q}) \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle / \langle \hat{Q} \cdot \hat{Q} \rangle^{3/2}\) (Eq. 17).

## Context

This work extends the method introduced by Alhassid et al. (2014) for extracting deformation signatures from AFMC simulations, previously demonstrated for \(^{148}\)Sm and \(^{154}\)Sm, to isotope chains of even-mass samarium and neodymium nuclei. It contrasts with the finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) mean-field approximation (Goodman 1981, Tanabe 1981), which exhibits a sharp phase transition from spherical to deformed shapes, whereas the AFMC results show a smooth crossover. The use of quadrupole invariants (Kumar 1972, Cline 1986) allows extraction of effective intrinsic quadrupole deformation parameters within the rotationally invariant configuration-interaction shell model, without resorting to a mean-field approximation, and enables future studies of shape coexistence and fluctuations in heavy nuclei.
