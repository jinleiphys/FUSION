# Consequences of temperature fluctuations in observables measured in high energy collisions

> We review the consequences of intrinsic, nonstatistical temperature fluctuations as seen in observables measured in high energy collisions. We do this from the point of view of nonextensive statistics and Tsallis distributions. Particular attention is paid to multiplicity fluctuations as a first consequence of temperature fluctuations, to the equivalence of temperature and volume fluctuations, to the generalized thermodynamic fluctuations relations allowing us to compare fluctuations observed in different parts of phase space, and to the problem of the relation between Tsallis entropy and Tsallis distributions. We also discuss the possible influence of conservation laws on these distributions and provide some examples of how one can get them without considering temperature fluctuations.

# Consequences of temperature fluctuations in observables measured in high energy collisions

## Key claim

Temperature fluctuations in the hadronizing fireball, modeled via nonextensive Tsallis statistics, are the source of power-law tails in single-particle distributions and broadening of multiplicity distributions; however, the same Tsallis distribution can also arise from conservation laws (yielding $q<1$) or from other mechanisms without any temperature fluctuations at all, meaning that observation of a Tsallis distribution does not uniquely imply the presence of intrinsic temperature fluctuations.

## Method

The authors review the superstatistics framework in which a local temperature $T$ fluctuates around an equilibrium value $T_0$ according to a gamma distribution derived from a Fokker-Planck equation; convoluting the Boltzmann-Gibbs factor $\exp(-E/T)$ with this gamma distribution yields the Tsallis distribution $h_q(E)$. They then examine multiplicity distributions (showing that temperature fluctuations convert Poisson distributions into negative binomial distributions), the equivalence of temperature and volume fluctuations via $E \sim VT^4$, generalized thermodynamic fluctuation relations connecting energy, temperature and multiplicity fluctuations, and the influence of conservation laws (conditional probabilities) which produce Tsallis distributions with $q<1$ even when $\lambda$ does not fluctuate.

## Key numbers

- The nonextensivity parameter $q$ is related to temperature fluctuations by $q = 1 + \omega_T^2$ where $\omega_T^2 = \text{Var}(T)/\langle T\rangle^2$ (Eq. 3).
- For multiplicity distributions, the negative binomial parameter $k = 1/(q-1)$; for $q\rightarrow 1$ the distribution becomes Poissonian, for $q\rightarrow 2$ it becomes geometric.
- From UA5 data at $\sqrt{s}=546$ GeV: $q = 1.27$ from $P(N)$, $q_T = 1.09$ from $p_T$ distributions, and $q_L = 1.36$ (corrected to $q_L = 1.14$) from rapidity distributions.
- The energy dependence of $1/k$ from multiplicity distributions is $k^{-1} = -0.104 + 0.058\ln\sqrt{s}$.
- For nuclear collisions, $q-1 = \alpha/N$ where $\alpha$ is a constant of order unity.
- The conditional probability from conservation laws yields $q' = (N-3)/(N-2) < 1$; for $N=30$, $q' = 0.964$.

## Context

This review extends the authors' previous review (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2009) on Tsallis statistics in multiparticle production, covering developments not included there. It builds on the superstatistics concept (Beck and Cohen, 2003) and the connection between temperature fluctuations and the nonextensivity parameter $q$ established in earlier work (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2000). The paper addresses critical remarks about the consistency of Tsallis statistics with thermodynamics (referenced as "debate") and provides generalized thermodynamic fluctuation relations that generalize Lindhard's uncertainty relation (Lindhard, 1990). The results enable comparisons of fluctuations measured in different parts of phase space (longitudinal vs. transverse) and allow extraction of the correlation coefficient $\rho$ between energy and temperature fluctuations, which carries information about the production process (e.g., whether large inelasticity yields many low-energy secondaries or few high-energy ones).
