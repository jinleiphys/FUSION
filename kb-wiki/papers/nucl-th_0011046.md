# The structure of lambda hypernuclei

> The density dependent relativistic hadron field theory is extended to also describe strange systems. It is seen that the application to hypernuclei works extremely well. Important spin orbit effects in lambda hypernuclei are studied and their experimental relevance is pointed out.

## Key claim

The density dependent relativistic hadron field theory (DDRH) is extended to describe strange systems, and the application to lambda hypernuclei works extremely well; however, the model requires a least-squares fit of two relative coupling constants (R_Λσ and R_Λω) because the quark-counting choice of 2/3 for both is ruled out, and the fit yields a degenerate "valley" of equally good parameter pairs rather than a unique solution.

## Method

The authors extend the DDRH field theory, which treats baryons interacting via meson exchange with density-dependent coupling functionals derived from Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) calculations in infinite nuclear matter using the Bonn A NN potential, to include the Λ hyperon. Since DBHF calculations for the full baryon octet are unavailable, they assume the functional shape of the coupling functionals is approximately equal for nucleons and hyperons, with a relative scaling factor given by the ratio of free-space couplings. The two scaling factors R_Λσ and R_Λω are then determined by a χ² fit to experimental Λ single-particle spectra, with R_Λσ fixed from a T-matrix calculation by Haidenbauer et al. (1998) and R_Λω determined from the fit.

## Key numbers

- The Λ central potential depth is roughly -30 MeV, in good agreement with experimental analyses.
- In ⁴⁰_ΛCa, the rms radius of the Λ d-shell is 3.6 fm, compared to the whole nucleus rms radius of 3.3 fm.
- The spin-orbit splitting decreases for nuclei below ⁴⁰_ΛCa due to continuum threshold effects, and also decreases when going to large mass numbers as a finite-size effect.

## Context

This work extends the DDRH field theory, previously applied successfully to isospin nuclei by Fuchs (1995), into the hypernuclear sector. It provides a microscopic alternative to conventional relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory as described by Serot (1986), which uses self-interactions rather than density-dependent couplings. The model enables future extraction of information on nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon interactions from upcoming high-precision experimental data, and has also been applied to neutron star matter including hyperons by Hofmann (2000).
