# Phase transitions in neutron star and magnetars and their connection with high energetic bursts in astrophysics

> The phase transition from normal hadronic matter to quark matter in neutron stars (NS) could give rise to several interesting phenomena. Compact stars can have such exotic states up to their surface (called strange stars (SS)) or they can have quark core surrounded by hadronic matter, known as hybrid stars (HS). As the state of matter of the resultant SS/HS is different from the initial hadronic matter, their masses also differ. Therefore, such conversion leads to huge energy release, sometimes of the order of $10^{53}$ ergs. In the present work we study the qualitative energy released by such conversion. Recent observations reveal huge surface magnetic field in certain stars, termed magnetars. Such huge magnetic fields can modify the equations of state (EOS) of the matter describing the star. Therefore, the mass of magnetars are different from normal NS. The energy released during the conversion process from neutron magnetar (NM) to strange magnetar/hybrid magnetar (SS/HS) is different from normal NS to SS/HS conversion. In this work we calculate the energy release during the phase transition in magnetars. The energy released during NS to SS/HS conversion exceeds the energy released during NM to SM/HM conversion. The energy released during the conversion of NS to SS is always of the order of $10^{53}$ ergs. The amount of energy released during such conversion can only be compared to the energy observed during the gamma ray bursts (GRB). The energy liberated during NM to HM conversion is few times lesser, and is not likely to power GRB at cosmological distances. However, the magnetars are more likely to lose their energy from the magnetic poles and can produce giant flares, which are usually associated with magnetars.

# Phase transitions in neutron star and magnetars and their connection with high energetic bursts in astrophysics

## Key claim

The energy released during the conversion of a neutron star to a strange star or hybrid star can reach approximately 10^53 ergs, which is sufficient to power gamma-ray bursts at cosmological distances, but only if the initial star is a normal neutron star rather than a magnetar; the conversion of a neutron magnetar to a strange magnetar or hybrid magnetar releases significantly less energy and is unlikely to power cosmological GRBs, though it may explain giant flares observed from magnetars.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for non-rotating compact stars using two hadronic equation of state parametrizations (TM1 and PL-Z) within the relativistic mean field model with hyperons, and a MIT bag model for quark matter with bag constants of 150 MeV (for strange stars) and 160 MeV (for hybrid stars). They compute the gravitational mass, proper mass, and baryonic mass of initial neutron stars and final quark stars, then calculate the energy release as the difference in binding energy between the two configurations, both with and without a 0.2 solar mass matter ejection scenario. For magnetars, they incorporate a density-dependent magnetic field (surface field 10^14 G, central field 10^17 G) that modifies the equation of state via Landau quantization of charged particles.

## Key numbers

- Energy released in neutron star to strange star conversion (no magnetic field, no matter ejection): 2.43 × 10^53 ergs (TM1 hadronic EOS) and 1.73 × 10^53 ergs (PL-Z hadronic EOS)
- Energy released in neutron star to hybrid star conversion (Maxwell construction, no magnetic field, no matter ejection): 0.80 × 10^53 ergs (TM1) and 0.57 × 10^53 ergs (PL-Z)
- Energy released in neutron magnetar to strange magnetar conversion (no matter ejection): 1.79 × 10^53 ergs (TM1) and 1.32 × 10^53 ergs (PL-Z)
- Energy released in neutron magnetar to hybrid magnetar conversion (Maxwell construction, no matter ejection): 0.43 × 10^53 ergs (TM1) and 0.41 × 10^53 ergs (PL-Z)
- Baryonic mass of initial neutron star: 1.71 solar masses (TM1) and 1.945 solar masses (PL-Z)
- Matter ejection assumed: 0.2 solar masses of baryonic mass
- Surface magnetic field of magnetars: 10^14 G; central magnetic field: 10^17 G
- Density jump for Maxwell construction (TM1, no magnetic field): from 0.34 fm^-3 to 0.56 fm^-3
- Mixed phase density range for Gibbs construction (TM1, no magnetic field): 0.13 fm^-3 to 0.56 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Bombaci and Datta (2000) who estimated energy release of order 10^53 ergs from neutron star to quark star conversion assuming baryonic mass conservation, and by Alcock et al. (1986) and Olinto (1987) who first proposed such conversion as an energy source. The authors incorporate matter ejection as suggested by Fryer and Woosley (2000), who showed that up to 0.2 solar masses could be ejected during the phase transition, breaking strict baryonic mass conservation. The inclusion of magnetic field effects on the equation of state follows work by Chakrabarty et al. (1997) and others on Landau quantization in strong magnetic fields. The results enable a distinction between normal neutron star conversions that can power cosmological gamma-ray bursts and magnetar conversions that are more likely associated with giant flares observed in soft gamma repeaters such as SGR 0526-66, SGR 1900+14, and SGR 1806-20.
