# Foundation of Hydrodynamics of Strongly Interacting Systems

> Hydrodynamics and quantum mechanics have many elements in common, as the density field and velocity fields are common variables that can be constructed in both descriptions. Starting with the Schroedinger equation and the Klein-Gordon for a single particle in hydrodynamical form, we examine the basic assumptions under which a quantum system of particles interacting through their mean fields can be described by hydrodynamics.

## Key claim

Hydrodynamics can describe a quantum many-body system only when the system has reached thermal equilibrium (where occupation numbers become quasi-stationary and \(\partial_t n_a \sim 0\)), or when the quantum stress tensor and mean-field stress tensor dominate over the thermal stress tensor, allowing quasi-hydrodynamic behavior even without full thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The author starts from the single-particle Schrödinger equation and Klein-Gordon equation, rewriting each in hydrodynamical form using the Madelung-Bohm decomposition of the wave function into amplitude and phase. For a many-body system in the extended mean-field approximation, the equations of continuity and Euler-type equations are derived by summing over single-particle states weighted by occupation numbers, with additional source terms arising from residual interactions that change occupation probabilities. The relativistic case is treated via the Feshbach-Villars decomposition of the Klein-Gordon equation into coupled first-order equations for particle and antiparticle components, from which continuity and Euler equations are obtained after neglecting pair production and zitterbewegung on time scales long compared to \(\hbar/2m_0\).

## Key numbers

- For a plane wave \(\phi(x)=A\cos(kx+B)\), the quantum pressure is \(p_{xx}=A^2 \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} k^2\), constant inside a spatially flat region.
- For a square well with \(n\) nodes in a very deep potential, the quantum pressure scales as \(p_{xx} \sim \frac{\hbar^2}{ma^3}\frac{(2n+1)^2\pi^2}{8}\), where \(a\) is the geometrical width.
- For the Skyrme I nucleon-nucleon interaction, the two-body interaction strength is \(t_0 = -1057\) MeV/fm\(^3\) and the three-body interaction strength is \(t_3 = +14463\) MeV/fm\(^6\).
- In the Thomas-Fermi approximation for a large fermion system, the quantum stress tensor is \(p_{ij}^{(q)} = \frac{\hbar^2}{5m} \left( \frac{6\pi^2}{4} \right)^{2/3} \rho^{5/3} \delta_{ij}\).
- The thermal stress tensor for a thermally equilibrated fermion gas is \(p_{ij}^{(t)} = \frac{\hbar^2}{5m} \left( \frac{6\pi^2}{4} \right)^{2/3} \rho^{5/3} \left[ \frac{2mkT}{\hbar^2 m (6\pi^2 \rho/4)^{2/3}} \right]^2 \delta_{ij}\).

## Context

This work extends the Madelung (1926) and Bohm (1952) formulation of quantum mechanics in hydrodynamical form to many-body systems with mean-field interactions, building on the author's earlier development of the quantum stress tensor (Wong 1976) and extended mean-field approximation (Wong 1977). It provides a formal foundation for understanding when a quantum many-body system, such as an atomic nucleus, a finite quark-gluon plasma, or a Bose-Einstein condensate, can be described by hydrodynamics, clarifying that thermal equilibrium is a key requirement but that quantum and mean-field stresses can sustain quasi-hydrodynamic behavior at low and moderate temperatures even without full thermal equilibrium. The relativistic treatment using the Feshbach-Villars (1958) decomposition opens the way to describe relativistic bosonic systems with particle-antiparticle degrees of freedom in a hydrodynamic framework.
