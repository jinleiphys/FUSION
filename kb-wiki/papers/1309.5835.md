# Dominant Secondary Nuclear Photoexcitation with the X-ray Free Electron Laser

> The new regime of resonant nuclear photoexcitation rendered possible by x-ray free electron laser beams interacting with solid state targets is investigated theoretically. Our results unexpectedly show that secondary processes coupling nuclei to the atomic shell in the created cold high-density plasma can dominate direct photoexcitation. As an example we discuss the case of $^{93m}$Mo isomer depletion for which nuclear excitation by electron capture as secondary process is shown to be orders of magnitude more efficient than the direct laser-nucleus interaction. General arguments revisiting the role of the x-ray free electron laser in nuclear experiments involving solid-state targets are further deduced.

## Key claim

Secondary nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) in the cold high-density plasma created by an X-ray free electron laser (XFEL) pulse on a solid-state target can exceed direct resonant photoexcitation by orders of magnitude, even when the laser photons are tuned to the nuclear transition energy. This dominance arises because the NEEC process operates over the much longer plasma lifetime (approximately 100 ps) compared to the XFEL pulse duration (approximately 100 fs).

## Method

The authors modeled the interaction of XFEL pulses with solid-state nuclear targets using a density matrix formalism and Bloch equations for direct photoexcitation, incorporating decoherence from the laser's limited coherence time. For the secondary process, they simulated plasma generation from XFEL-induced photoionization using mass photoabsorption coefficients and the FLYCHK collisional-radiative code to obtain electron temperature, electron density, and charge state distributions, then calculated total NEEC rates by summing over charge states and capture levels using relativistic multi-configurational Dirac-Fock wave functions from GRASP92.

## Key numbers

- For direct photoexcitation of $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo with LCLS parameters, the triggering occupation number per pulse is $\rho_{\mathrm{trig}} = 1.8 \times 10^{-20}$.
- For direct photoexcitation with European XFEL parameters, $\rho_{\mathrm{trig}} = 2.4 \times 10^{-21}$.
- For a seeded XFEL oscillator (XFELO) with 1 ps pulse duration, the calculated $\rho_{\mathrm{trig}}$ reaches $4.4 \times 10^{-14}$.
- For NEEC in the plasma at $T_e = 350$ eV, the triggering occupation number per pulse is $\rho_{\mathrm{trig}} = 5.5 \times 10^{-14}$, approximately 6 orders of magnitude higher than direct photoexcitation with available XFEL parameters.
- The estimated electron temperature for the Mo/Nb target is $T_e = 350$ eV, with electron density $n_e = 1.3 \times 10^{24}$ cm$^{-3}$.
- For the European XFEL parameters, the calculated signal rates are $9.6 \times 10^{-12}$ signal photons/s for direct photoexcitation and $2.2 \times 10^{-4}$ signal photons/s for NEEC.
- For LCLS parameters, signal rates are $5.6 \times 10^{-14}$ signal photons/s for direct photoexcitation and $1.7 \times 10^{-7}$ signal photons/s for NEEC.

## Context

This work extends theoretical studies of resonant nuclear photoexcitation from synchrotron radiation experiments (Gerdau 1999, Shvydko 1996) and optical-laser-generated plasmas (Harston 1999) into the new regime of XFEL sources. The authors show that for low-lying nuclear transitions (below approximately 25 keV), the secondary NEEC mechanism dominates over direct photoexcitation, reversing the conventional expectation from synchrotron radiation experiments where electronic processes were only background. This finding enables experimental strategies for isomer triggering in $^{93\mathrm{m}}$Mo and similar nuclei (a list is provided in Junker 2012), where the high uncertainty of nuclear transition energies (80 eV for the 4.85 keV transition in $^{93}$Mo) makes direct resonant photoexcitation challenging, while NEEC is robust against such uncertainties due to the broad electron energy distribution in the plasma.
