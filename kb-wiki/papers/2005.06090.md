# On the inversion of isobaric-analogue states in nuclei

> Isospin is an approximate symmetry in atomic nuclei, arising from the rather similar properties of protons and neutrons. Perhaps the clearest manifestation of isospin within nuclei is in the near-identical structure of excited states in mirror nuclei: nuclei with inverted numbers of protons and neutrons. Isospin symmetry, and therefore mirror-symmetry, is broken by electromagnetic interactions and the difference in the masses of the up and down quarks. A recent study by Hoff and collaborators presented evidence that the ground-state spin of $^{73}$Sr is different from that of its mirror, $^{73}$Br, due to an inversion of the ground- and first-excited states, separated by only 27 keV in the $^{73}$Br system. In this brief note, we place this inversion within the necessary context of the past half-century of experimental and theoretical work, and show that it is entirely consistent with normal behaviour, and affords no new insight into isospin-symmetry breaking. The essential point is that isospin-breaking effects due to the Coulomb interaction frequently vary from level to level within a given medium-mass nucleus by as much as 200 keV. Any level splitting smaller than this is liable to manifest a level inversion in the mirror partner which, absent disagreement with an appropriate nuclear model, does not challenge our understanding. While we note the novelty of an inversion in nuclear ground states, we emphasize that in the context of isospin there is nothing specifically illuminating about the ground state, or a level inversion.

# On the inversion of isobaric-analogue states in mirror nuclei

## Key claim

The inversion of ground- and first-excited states observed in the ⁷³Sr-⁷³Br mirror pair is entirely consistent with normal Coulombic isospin-symmetry-breaking behavior and affords no new insight into isospin-symmetry breaking, provided the level splitting (27 keV in ⁷³Br) is smaller than the typical 100-200 keV variation in isospin-breaking effects from level to level within medium-mass nuclei.

## Method

The authors analyzed experimental data for odd-odd and odd-mass mirror pairs between A=19 and A=59, computing the difference δE₁₂ = E₁₂^{N>Z} - E₁₂^{N<Z} between energy spacings of pairs of isobaric analogue states in mirror nuclei. They compared these experimental results to shell-model calculations using the codes NuShellX with USDB and GXPF1A interactions, evaluating the Coulomb potential in first-order perturbation theory with two choices for single-particle wave functions: harmonic oscillator (HO) and Woods-Saxon (WS) potentials. States at or beyond the proton-separation energy were excluded from the analysis.

## Key numbers

- 24 of 508 state combinations satisfy |δE₁₂| > E₁₂^{N>Z}, of which 9 undergo an inversion between the N>Z and N<Z systems
- Isospin-breaking effects due to the Coulomb interaction frequently vary from level to level within a given medium-mass nucleus by as much as 200 keV
- The ground- and first-excited states in ⁷³Br are separated by only 27 keV
- Approximately 35% of like-parity IAS-pairs separated by 27 keV would be expected to invert
- The Woods-Saxon choice has a root-mean-squared deviation from experiment of 53 keV, compared to 88 keV for the harmonic oscillator choice
- The rms deviation between the two theoretical methods (HO and WS) is 74 keV
- Positive and negative δE₁₂ values are approximately equally likely, with a characteristic scale of 100-200 keV

## Context

This work responds to a recent study by Hoff and collaborators (2020) that presented evidence for a ground-state spin difference in the ⁷³Sr-⁷³Br mirror pair due to a level inversion. The authors place this finding within the context of a half-century of experimental work on mirror nuclei, including data from the Evaluated Nuclear Structure Data File (ENSDF) and studies by Spieker (2019), Ong (2017), Milne (2016), and Davies (2013). The analysis demonstrates that the observed inversion does not challenge existing understanding of isospin symmetry, and the authors emphasize that state inversions provide no more information about isospin-symmetry breaking than any other mirror-energy shift, of which many hundreds have been observed. The work also references two famous cases of additional isospin-symmetry-breaking phenomena in unbound states: the 2⁻ and 1⁻ states in ¹⁶F-¹⁶N, and the 1/2⁺ and 1/2⁻ states in the ¹³N-¹³C mirror pair, originally studied by Thomas (1952) and Ehrman (1951).
