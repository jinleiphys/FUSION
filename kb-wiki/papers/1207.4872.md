# Maximum mass of a hybrid star having a mixed phase region in the light of pulsar PSR J1614-2230

> Recent observation of pulsar PSR J1614-2230 with mass about 2 solar masses poses a severe constraint on the equations of state (EOS) of matter describing stars under extreme conditions. Neutron stars (NS) can reach the mass limits set by PSR J1614-2230. But stars having hyperons or quark stars (QS) having boson condensates, with softer EOS can barely reach such limits and are ruled out. QS with pure strange matter also cannot have such high mass unless the effect of strong coupling constant or colour superconductivity are taken into account. In this work I try to calculate the upper mass limit for a hybrid stars (HS) having a quark-hadron mixed phase. The hadronic matter (having hyperons) EOS is described by relativistic mean field theory and for the quark phase I use the simple MIT bag model. I construct the intermediate mixed phase using Glendenning construction. HS with a mixed phase cannot reach the mass limit set by PSR J1614-2230 unless I assume a density dependent bag constant. For such case the mixed phase region is small. The maximum mass of a mixed hybrid star obtained with such mixed phase region is $2.01 M_{\odot}$.

## Key claim

Hybrid stars with a quark-hadron mixed phase constructed using a simple MIT bag model and relativistic mean-field hadronic EOS can reach the mass limit set by PSR J1614-2230 only if a density-dependent bag constant is assumed, and even then the mixed phase region is small; the maximum mass of 2.01 solar masses is achieved only with a specific choice of parameters (bag pressure 150 MeV and strange quark mass 300 MeV).

## Method

The hadronic phase (including hyperons) was described using the relativistic mean-field model with TM1 parametrization, and the quark phase was modeled with the three-flavor MIT bag model. The intermediate mixed phase was constructed using the Glendenning construction, which enforces mechanical and chemical equilibrium between the two phases while maintaining global charge neutrality. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations were solved to obtain the mass-radius relation, with crust EOS added from Negele and Vautherin for the inner crust and Feynman-Metropolis-Teller and Baym-Pethick-Sutherland for the outer crust.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of a mixed hybrid star with density-dependent bag constant: 2.01 solar masses (M_sun), achieved with B_g = 150 MeV and m_s = 300 MeV
- Maximum mass with B_g = 160 MeV and m_s = 150 MeV: 1.84 M_sun
- With constant bag pressure of 170 or 180 MeV (m_s = 150 MeV), the maximum mass is about 1.5 M_sun
- For constant bag pressure 170 MeV, the mixed phase region extends from baryon density 0.2 fm^{-3} to 0.76 fm^{-3}
- For constant bag pressure 180 MeV, the mixed phase region extends from 0.22 fm^{-3} to 0.89 fm^{-3}
- For varying bag pressure with B_g = 170 MeV, the mixed phase region ends at 0.53 fm^{-3}, a reduction of about 30%
- For varying bag pressure with B_g = 160 MeV, the mixed phase region starts at 0.15 fm^{-3} and ends at 0.36 fm^{-3}
- For varying bag pressure with B_g = 150 MeV, the mixed phase region starts at 0.13 fm^{-3} and ends at 0.3 fm^{-3}
- The radius corresponding to the maximum mass is about 10 km
- The asymptotic value of the density-dependent bag constant at high density was fixed at 130 MeV

## Context

This work extends studies following the precise mass measurement of PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010) at 1.97 +/- 0.04 M_sun, which ruled out many softer EOS with hyperons or quark matter. Prior work by Ozel (2010) and Lattimer (2010) examined implications for quark and hybrid stars in the bag model, while Bonanno and Sedrakian (2012) obtained massive hybrid stars using color-superconducting quark cores and stiff hadronic EOS. The present work shows that even with a simple MIT bag model and hyperonic RMF EOS, the mass limit can be reached with a density-dependent bag constant, though the mixed phase region becomes small. This enables future observational radius measurements to distinguish between neutron stars, strange stars, and hybrid stars, as their mass-radius relationships differ significantly.
