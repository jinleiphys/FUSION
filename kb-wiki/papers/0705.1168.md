# Going beyond perturbation theory: Parametric Perturbation Theory

> We devise a {\sl non--perturbative} method, called {\sl Parametric Perturbation Theory} (PPT), which is alternative to the ordinary perturbation theory. The method relies on a principle of simplicity for the observable solutions, which are constrained to be linear in a certain (unphysical) parameter. The perturbative expansion is carried out in this parameter and not in the physical coupling (as in ordinary perturbation theory). We provide a number of nontrivial examples, where our method is capable to resum the divergent perturbative series, {\sl extract} the leading asymptotic (strong coupling) behavior and {\sl predict} with high accuracy the coefficients of the perturbative series. In the case of a zero dimensional field theory we prove that PPT can be used to provide the imaginary part of the solution, when the problem is analytically continued to negative couplings. In the case of a $ϕ^4$ lattice model 1+1 and of elastic theory we have shown that the observables resummed with PPT display a branch point at a finite value of the coupling, signaling the transition from a stable to a metastable state. We have also applied the method to the prediction of the virial coefficients for a hard sphere gas in two and three dimensions; in this example we have also found that the solution resummed with PPT has a singularity at finite density. Predictions for the unknown virial coefficients are made.

# Parametric Perturbation Theory (PPT)

## Key claim

Parametric Perturbation Theory (PPT) is a non-perturbative method that replaces expansion in the physical coupling with expansion in an unphysical parameter, requiring the observable to be linear in that parameter (the "Principle of Absolute Simplicity"). The method can resum divergent perturbative series and extract strong-coupling asymptotic behavior, but its accuracy depends on the choice of the functional relation between the physical coupling and the unphysical parameter, and the method cannot reproduce exponential behavior (e.g., for negative couplings in zero-dimensional field theory).

## Method

The method introduces an unphysical parameter ρ and expands the observable in ρ rather than in the physical coupling g. A functional relation g = g(ρ) is constructed (typically as a ratio of polynomials in ρ) with unknown coefficients determined order by order by imposing that the observable be linear in ρ (the Principle of Absolute Simplicity). The perturbative calculation is carried out in ρ, and the coefficients in g(ρ) are fixed to eliminate nonlinear terms in ρ from the observable at each order. The method is applied to the Duffing oscillator using the Lindstedt-Poincaré technique, to the quantum anharmonic oscillator, a PT-symmetric Hamiltonian, zero-dimensional φ⁴ theory, QED effective action, one-plaquette SU(2) lattice gauge theory, and a 1+1 dimensional lattice φ⁴ model.

## Key numbers

- Duffing oscillator: PPT to order ρ³ yields maximum error Σ ≈ 5.27 × 10⁻⁴ for the squared frequency Ω² as gA² → ∞, compared to Σ_LDE ≈ 1.36 × 10⁻² for the Linear Delta Expansion at the same order (Amore 2003)
- Quantum anharmonic oscillator ground state: PPT using first 6 perturbative coefficients (N̄=3) predicts the strong-coupling coefficient α₀ ≈ 0.624458, compared to the precise value α₀ = 0.66798625915577710827096
- Anharmonic oscillator: PPT predicts the g⁷ coefficient with 1.399% error, g⁸ with 6.19% error, g⁹ with 15.61% error, and g¹⁰ with 29.03% error
- Zero-dimensional φ⁴ theory: PPT with N̄=3 gives the imaginary part of E(g) at g=-1 as Im E(g) = -0.3767931291206198 (exact), with PPT prediction Im E(g)₁ = -0.36488641384088155 from the first complex root
- Zero-dimensional φ⁴ theory: the denominator of g(ρ) has a zero at ρ₀ = -0.0593, signaling a branch point
- 1+1 dimensional lattice φ⁴ model: PPT with set [3,2] (using coefficients up to g⁶) predicts the asymptotic behavior E ∝ √g for g → ∞

## Context

This work extends the class of non-perturbative methods that include the Linear Delta Expansion (LDE) and Variational Perturbation Theory (VPT), which also introduce artificial parameters and use principles like the Principle of Minimal Sensitivity (Stevenson 1981). The method is applied to problems with divergent perturbative series, including the quantum anharmonic oscillator studied by Bender and Wu, a PT-symmetric Hamiltonian studied by Bender and Dunne (1998) and Bender and Weniger (2001), zero-dimensional φ⁴ theory previously treated with LDE by Jentschura, Zinn-Justin, and collaborators, and a 1+1 lattice φ⁴ model studied by Nishiyama (2001) using linked cluster expansion and DMRG. The method enables prediction of unknown perturbative coefficients and extraction of strong-coupling behavior from a finite number of perturbative coefficients, and can identify branch points signaling transitions from stable to metastable states.
