# Neutrino Mass, Electron Capture and the Shake-off Contributions

> Electron capture can determine the electron neutrino mass, while the beta decay of Tritium measures the electron antineutrino mass and the neutrinoless double beta decay observes the Majorana neutrino mass. Electron capture e. g. on 163Ho plus bound electron to 163Dy* plus neutrino can determine the electron neutrino mass from the upper end of the decay spectrum of the excited Dy*, which is given by the Q-Value minus the neutrino mass. The Dy* states decay by X-ray and Auger electron emissions. The total decay energy is measured in a bolometer. These excitations have been studied by Robertson and by Faessler et al.. In addition the daughter atom Dy can also be excited by moving in the capture process one electron into the continuum. The escape of these continuum electrons is automatically included in the experimental bolometer spectrum. Recently a method developed by Intemann and Pollock was used by DeRujula and Lusignoli for a rough estimate of this shake-off process for "s" wave electrons in capture on 163Ho. The purpose of the present work is to give a more reliable description of "s" wave shake-off in electron capture on Holmium. For that one needs very accurate atomic wave functions of Ho in its ground state and excited atomic wave functions of Dy* including a description of the continuum electrons. In the present approach the wave functions of Ho and Dy* are determined selfconsistently with the antisymmetrized relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock approach. The relativistic continuum electron wave functions for the ionized Dy* are obtained in the corresponding selfconsistent Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Potential. In this improved approach shake-off can hardly be seen after electron capture in 163Ho and thus can probably not affect the determination of the electron neutrino mass.

## Key claim

The shake-off process in electron capture on 163Ho, when calculated with self-consistent relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock wave functions instead of first-order perturbation theory, produces a negligible contribution to the bolometer spectrum and therefore cannot affect the determination of the electron neutrino mass.

## Method

The authors calculated the probability of shake-off (ejection of a bound electron into the continuum during electron capture) using the sudden approximation with self-consistent relativistic Dirac-Hartree-Fock wave functions for both the parent 163Ho atom and the daughter 163Dy* atom, including continuum electron wave functions obtained in the self-consistent Dirac-Hartree-Fock potential. They compared these results with the earlier approach of DeRujula and Lusignoli (2012), who used screened non-relativistic Coulomb wave functions and first-order perturbation theory following the method of Intemann and Pollock (1970). The shake-off probability was computed by integrating over continuum electron energies up to the Q-value of 2.8 keV, using 417 mesh points and testing numerical stability with multiple integration rules.

## Key numbers

- Overlap of 3s wave function between Ho and Dy: 0.99940 (Dirac-Hartree-Fock), yielding a 2-hole plus shake-off probability of 0.24% relative to 1-hole states
- Overlap of 4s wave function between Ho and Dy: 0.99909 (Dirac-Hartree-Fock), yielding a 2-hole plus shake-off probability of 0.36% relative to 1-hole states
- Q-value for electron capture on 163Ho: (2.8 ± 0.08) keV, taken from the ECHo collaboration
- Binding energy of 3s1/2 electron in 163Ho: 2.128 keV
- Binding energy of 4s1/2 electron in 163Ho: 0.4324 keV
- Maximum energy available for shake-off electron from 3s capture: 0.758 keV (Q-value minus 2-hole excitation energy)
- Maximum energy available for shake-off electron from 4s capture: 1.959 keV
- With a 10% error in single-electron overlaps and using the Vatai approximation, the shake-off probability could be as large as 34% of 1-hole states, but the correct Dirac-Hartree-Fock value is about 0.4%

## Context

This work extends the study of electron capture on 163Ho for neutrino mass determination, previously investigated by Robertson (2015) and Faessler et al. (2015), and specifically addresses the shake-off contribution estimated by DeRujula and Lusignoli (2012) using the method of Intemann and Pollock (1970). The authors show that the earlier perturbation-theory approach overestimates shake-off by about two orders of magnitude due to the sensitivity of the Vatai approximation to small errors in single-electron overlaps. The improved calculation demonstrates that shake-off is negligible for the ECHo experiment, which aims to determine the electron neutrino mass from the endpoint of the deexcitation spectrum of 163Dy*.
