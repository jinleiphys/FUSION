# A connection between angular dependent phase ambiguities and the uniqueness of the partial wave decomposition

> Unconstrained partial-wave amplitudes obtained at discrete energies from fits to complete sets of experimental data may not vary smoothly with energy, and are in principle non-unique. We demonstrate how this behavior can be ascribed to the continuum ambiguity. Starting from the spinless scattering case, we demonstrate how an unknown overall phase depending on energy and angle mixes the structures seen in the associated partial-wave amplitudes making the partial wave decomposition non-unique, and illustrate it on a simple toy model. We then apply these principles to pseudo-scalar meson photoproduction and show that the non-uniqueness effect can be removed through a phase rotation, allowing a consistent comparison with model amplitudes. The effect of this phase ambiguity is also considered for Legendre expansions of experimental observables. 5 pages,

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the well-known continuum ambiguity (an overall energy- and angle-dependent phase that leaves all observables invariant) is the root cause of the non-uniqueness and energy-discontinuity observed in unconstrained single-energy partial-wave analyses, and that this non-uniqueness can be removed by applying a phase rotation that fixes one helicity amplitude phase to a continuous arbitrary value, but the resulting multipoles remain phase-dependent and thus quantum-number identification still requires identifying the true continuum ambiguity phase.

## Method

The authors first derive the formal connection between angle-dependent phase rotations and partial-wave mixing using Legendre expansions and Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, showing that an angle-dependent phase mixes multipoles while an angle-independent phase does not. They then perform unconstrained single-energy partial-wave analyses (truncated at Lmax=5) on a complete set of eight observables for eta photoproduction, generated as numeric data from the ETA-MAID15a model, and compare the extracted multipoles to the known input. Finally, they apply a phase rotation to the helicity amplitudes, setting the phase of one helicity amplitude to zero at all energies and angles, and demonstrate that this yields continuous multipoles.

## Key numbers

- Toy model resonance parameters: S-wave mass M_S = 1.535 GeV, width Gamma_S = 0.15 GeV, residue a_S = (0.005 + i 0.004) GeV; P-wave mass M_P = 1.440 GeV, width Gamma_P = 0.10 GeV, residue a_P = (0.004 + i 0.003) GeV.
- For the toy model at resonance energies, |T_S(W=M_S)|^2 = 0.0073 and |T_P(W=M_P)|^2 = 0.01, both negligible compared to the space available from elastic unitarity (order 0.1).
- The single-energy analysis uses numeric data at W = 1769.8 MeV at 18 angles, with Lmax=5 truncation.
- The phase rotation Legendre expansion for the toy model rotation e^{i(2.0 + 0.5 x)} is effectively truncated at L'=5, with all Legendre coefficients for L' >= 6 having moduli within 10^{-7} or less.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of continuum ambiguities from elastic scattering (Atkinson 1973, Bowcock 1975, Atkinson 1985) and the angle-independent phase rotations used in multipole analyses (Tiator 2011, Sandorfi 2011) to the case of strongly inelastic photoproduction reactions. The authors show that while elastic unitarity nearly eliminates the continuum ambiguity in elastic scattering, and inelastic unitarity constrains it for near-threshold inelastic elastic scattering (Gibbs 1978, 2008), for photoproduction the ambiguity is essentially unconstrained. The paper enables a method to obtain continuous single-energy multipoles for comparison with model amplitudes, but notes that full quantum-number identification still requires determining the unknown continuum ambiguity phase.
