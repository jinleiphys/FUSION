# New method of studying slow strange meson properties in nuclear matter

> We suggest the new experimental method to explore the properties of slow strange mesons at normal nuclear matter density. We show that the $K^{+}$ and $K^{-}$ mesons with extremely small momenta relative to the surrounding medium rest frame can be produced in nucleus-nucleon collisions and their production cross sections are experimentally measurable. The experiments on study of the momentum dependence of meson-nuclear potentials are discussed.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the properties of slow K+ and K- mesons (with extremely small momenta relative to the surrounding medium rest frame) can be studied experimentally in nucleus-nucleon collisions using inverse kinematics, rather than in the more difficult direct proton-nucleus reactions, and that the resulting production cross sections are experimentally measurable.

## Method

The method uses inverse kinematics: a projectile nucleus beam collides with a hydrogen target. A slow meson produced at rest inside the projectile nucleus appears fast in the laboratory frame due to the Lorentz boost, shifting its momentum upward and reducing decay losses. The double differential cross section in the inverse kinematics is related to that in direct kinematics by a kinematic factor (p^2/E)^Ap * (E/p^2)^pA, which enhances the cross section for low-momentum mesons relative to the nuclear matter rest frame. The authors calculate expected cross sections for K+ and K- production by transforming existing data and model calculations from proton-nucleus reactions into the inverse kinematics frame.

## Key numbers

*   For K+ production at 2 AGeV beam energy, kaons produced with momenta from zero to 0.3 GeV/c in the projectile nucleus rest frame appear in the laboratory within longitudinal momentum ranges 0.78 to 1.47 GeV/c (backward emission) and 1.47 to 2.66 GeV/c (forward emission), with transverse momenta 0.0 to 0.3 GeV/c.
*   For K- production at 2.5 AGeV beam energy, antikaons produced backward with momenta up to 0.3 GeV/c in the projectile rest frame appear in the laboratory with longitudinal momenta 0.94 to 1.74 GeV/c and transverse momenta 0.0 to 0.3 GeV/c.
*   At 1 AGeV beam energy, a K+ meson with momentum 0.1 GeV/c relative to the projectile nucleus corresponds to a laboratory momentum of 0.7 GeV/c, and the differential cross section in Ap collisions is enhanced by a factor of 30 relative to pA collisions.
*   At the same energy, the K+ event rate in inverse kinematics exceeds that in traditional kinematics by about a factor of 400, accounting for both cross section enhancement and reduced decay losses over a 2.5 meter flight path.
*   For K- mesons at 2 AGeV beam energy, the corresponding event rate enhancement is a factor of 800.
*   All kaons and antikaons produced with momenta less than 300 MeV/c relative to the projectile nucleus rest frame are concentrated in the laboratory within a narrow cone of 10-20 degrees, corresponding to a solid angle of 1-3% of 4π.

## Context

This work extends the study of in-medium strange meson properties, motivated by theoretical predictions of a reduced K- mass and enhanced K+ mass in nuclear matter (effective chiral Lagrangians, mean-field models). It addresses the discrepancy between the strong attractive antikaon potential of -(180-200) MeV extracted from kaonic atom data (zero momentum) and the weaker potential of -(100-120) MeV inferred from heavy-ion collisions at GSI (momenta above 300 MeV/c), which suggests a momentum-dependent potential. The proposed method enables experiments at normal nuclear density in proton-nucleus collisions, avoiding the complex dynamics of heavy-ion collisions, and could clarify the reaction mechanism of subthreshold kaon production, where prior work by the ANKE Collaboration and others has yielded conflicting A-dependence exponents (alpha = 1.04 ± 0.03 versus 0.74 ± 0.05).
