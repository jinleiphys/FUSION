# Can Deuterium Formation Be Measured?

> The fundamental PP reaction has never been attempted in the laboratory as theory states it cannot be measured within the human life span. This forms the foundation of stellar nucleosynthesis and the standard solar model. Yet numerous observations including the Sun's obvious variability argue the theory is flawed and the reaction occurs in times many orders of magnitude shorter than prediction. Considering the consequence to accepted models and controlled fusion, testing is warranted.

# Can Deuterium Formation Be Measured?

## Key claim
The paper argues that the standard theoretical prediction for the proton-proton (PP) reaction rate, approximately 14 billion years, is likely incorrect by 12 to 13 orders of magnitude or more, based on observational discrepancies in deuterium abundance, solar neutrino measurements, and the unexpected detection of beryllium-7 on the Long Duration Exposure Facility (LDEF). The authors propose that deuterium formation may occur near the solar surface rather than exclusively in the core, and that this could be tested experimentally.

## Method
The authors review and critique the standard solar model (SSM) by comparing theoretical predictions against multiple observational datasets: the deuterium-to-hydrogen ratio observed in nature (10^-4 to 10^-5) versus the SSM prediction (10^-17); the detection of beryllium-7 on LDEF at 310 km altitude after 69 months of exposure; solar neutrino measurements from Homestake, Super Kamiokande, and SNO; and observations of solar flares, gamma-ray lines (0.511 MeV and 2.2 MeV), and short-period stellar luminosity variations. They propose that interstellar meteoroids impacting the solar surface at near solar escape velocity (618 km/s) could provide the conditions for prompt near-surface PP fusion.

## Key numbers
- The theoretical PP reaction rate predicted by Bethe and Critchfield (1938) is 14 × 10^9 years.
- Parker and Rolfs (1991) estimated a total cross section of 10^-47 cm^2 at E_p(lab) = 1 MeV for a 1 mA proton beam on a thick hydrogen target, yielding one reaction per 10^6 years.
- The observed deuterium-to-hydrogen ratio in nature is 10^-4 to 10^-5, while the SSM predicts 10^-17, a discrepancy of 12 orders of magnitude.
- LDEF orbited at 310 km altitude for 69 months and collected beryllium-7 (53-day half-life) on leading surfaces; cosmic ray spallation was ruled out as the source.
- Chappell et al. (1987) showed that only about 10^-3 of the solar wind penetrates the terrestrial bow shock.
- Homestake run number 117, which occurred during several solar flares including the largest ever recorded, yielded a neutrino count about five times those preceding and following and six times the long term mean, corresponding to 19 argon-37 atoms at 3 sigma above the mean (approximately 0.5 counts per day).
- The solar escape velocity is 618 km/s, equivalent to a thermal temperature of 15 × 10^6 K.

## Context
This paper extends a long-standing critique of the standard solar model and the Bethe-Critchfield (1938) prediction for the PP reaction rate, which has never been tested in the laboratory. The authors build on observations by Terekov et al. (1993) of the 2.2 MeV gamma ray from hydrogen neutron capture in solar flares, and on the LDEF beryllium-7 findings by Fishman et al. (1991) and Phillips et al. (1992). They directly challenge the Parker and Rolfs (1991) estimate and the Clayton (1968) textbook treatment. The work enables a proposed experimental test: searching for the 0.511 MeV positron-electron annihilation gamma ray from deuterium formation, which could be observed within hours rather than billions of years, and could be applied to cavitation fusion experiments such as those by Taleyarkhan et al. (2002).
