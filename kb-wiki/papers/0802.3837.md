# Search for long lived heaviest nuclei beyond the valley of stability

> The existence of long lived superheavy nuclei (SHN) is controlled mainly by spontaneous fission and $α$-decay processes. According to microscopic nuclear theory, spherical shell effects at Z=114, 120, 126 and N=184 provide the extra stability to such SHN to have long enough lifetime to be observed. To investigate whether the so-called "stability island" could really exist around the above Z, N values, the $α$-decay half lives along with the spontaneous fission and $β$-decay half lives of such nuclei are studied. The $α$-decay half lives of SHN with Z=102-120 are calculated in a quantum tunneling model with DDM3Y effective nuclear interaction using $Q_α$ values from three different mass formulae prescribed by Koura, Uno, Tachibana, Yamada (KUTY), Myers, Swiatecki (MS) and Muntian, Hofmann, Patyk, Sobiczewski (MMM). Calculation of spontaneous fission (SF) half lives for the same SHN are carried out using a phenomenological formula and compared with SF half lives predicted by Smolanczuk {\it et al}. Possible source of discrepancy between the calculated $α$-decay half lives of some nuclei and the experimental data of GSI, JINR-FLNR, RIKEN are discussed. In the region of Z=106-108 with N$\sim$ 160-164, the $β$-stable SHN $^{268}_{106}Sg_{162}$ is predicted to have highest $α$-decay half life ($T_α\sim 3.2hrs$) using $Q_α$ value from MMM. Interestingly, it is much greater than the recently measured $T_α$ ($\sim 22s$) of deformed doubly magic $^{270}_{108}Hs_{162}$ nucleus. A few fission-survived long-lived SHN which are either $β$-stable or having large $β$-decay half lives are predicted to exist near $^{294}110_{184}$, $^{293}110_{183}$, $^{296}112_{184}$ and $^{298}114_{184}$. These nuclei might decay predominantly through $α$-particle emission.

# Search for long lived heaviest nuclei beyond the valley of stability

## Key claim
Long-lived superheavy nuclei (SHN) may exist near specific proton and neutron numbers, but their survival depends on the competition between α-decay and spontaneous fission (SF) half-lives; the so-called "stability island" is not a single region but rather a set of possible "magic islands" or peninsulas, with the most promising candidates being β-stable or near-β-stable nuclei that have α-decay half-lives shorter than their SF half-lives.

## Method
The authors calculated α-decay half-lives for SHN with Z=102-120 using a quantum tunneling model with DDM3Y effective nuclear interaction, employing Qα values from three different mass formulae: KUTY (Koura, Uno, Tachibana, Yamada), Myers-Swiatecki (MS), and Muntian-Hofmann-Patyk-Sobiczewski (MMM). Spontaneous fission half-lives were calculated using a phenomenological formula from Ren and Xu and compared with microscopic SF predictions from Smolanczuk et al. β-decay half-lives from Möller, Nix, and Kratz were also considered to identify nuclei that could survive all decay modes.

## Key numbers
- The β-stable nucleus 268Sg162 is predicted to have the highest α-decay half-life (Tα ∼ 3.2 hrs) using Qα from MMM, which is much greater than the measured Tα (∼22 s) of the deformed doubly magic 270Hs162.
- For 290Sg184, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 1.18×10^8 yrs and TSF ∼ 1.3×10^6 yrs, giving a lifetime τ ∼ 1.27×10^6 yrs (still three orders of magnitude smaller than the age of Earth ∼4.5×10^9 yrs).
- For 294110184, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 311 yrs and TSF ∼ 4.48×10^4 yrs.
- For 296112184, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 3.10 yrs and TSF ∼ 3.09×10^5 yrs.
- For 298114184, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 17 days and TSF ∼ 4.38×10^5 yrs.
- For 293110183, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 352 yrs.
- For 292108184, using QKUTY, Tα ∼ 9.6×10^4 yrs and TSF ∼ 3.2×10^4 yrs.
- For 266Rf162, Tα ∼ 4.1×10^6 s (∼47.5 days) using QM, but TSF ∼ 23 s from Smolanczuk et al., making it unstable against SF.
- For 270Hs162, calculated Tα using QM is 28 s, while TSF ∼ 1.8 hrs; using experimental Qα gives Tα ∼ 9.53 s.
- For 277112, the calculated Tα with ℓ=0 is ∼93 μs, but with ℓ=4 it becomes 0.59 ms, matching the experimental value of 0.69 ms.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous calculations of α-decay half-lives for SHN (Chowdhury et al., 2006, 2007) by adding systematic comparison with spontaneous fission and β-decay half-lives, which were not checked earlier. It builds on the macroscopic-microscopic model (MMM) of Muntian et al. (2001) and the microscopic SF calculations of Smolanczuk et al. (1995, 1997). The study addresses the long-standing question of whether the "island of stability" predicted in the 1960s around 298114184 actually exists, and identifies specific nuclei (294110184, 293110183, 296112184, 298114184) that could be pursued experimentally via α-decay chains. The results suggest that the deformed neutron shell closure at N=162 becomes insignificant beyond Z=108, and that spherical shell effects at N=184 may produce only modest stability for Z≥114.
