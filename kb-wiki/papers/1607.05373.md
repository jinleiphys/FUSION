# Nuclear constraints on gravitational waves from deformed pulsars

> The recent direct detection of gravitational waves (GWs) from binary black hole mergers (2016, Phys. Rev. Lett. 116, no. 6, 061102; no. 24, 241103) opens up an entirely new non-electromagnetic window into the Universe making it possible to probe physics that has been hidden or dark to electromagnetic observations. In addition to cataclysmic events involving black holes, GWs can be triggered by physical processes and systems involving neutron stars. Properties of neutron stars are largely determined by the equation of state (EOS) of neutron-rich matter, which is the major ingredient in calculating the stellar structure and properties of related phenomena, such as gravitational wave emission from elliptically deformed pulsars and neutron star binaries. Although the EOS of neutron-rich matter is still rather uncertain mainly due to the poorly known density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy at high densities, significant progress has been made recently in constraining the symmetry energy using data from terrestrial nuclear laboratories. These constraints could provide useful information on the limits of GWs expected from neutron stars. Here after briefly reviewing our previous work on constraining gravitational radiation from elliptically deformed pulsars with terrestrial nuclear laboratory data in light of the recent gravitational wave detection, we estimate the maximum gravitational wave strain amplitude, using an optimistic value for the breaking strain of the neutron star crust, for 15 pulsars at distances 0.16 kpc to 0.91 kpc from Earth, and find it to be in the range of $\sim[0.2-31.1]\times 10^{-24}$, depending on the details of the EOS used to compute the neutron star properties. Implications are discussed.

## Key claim

The maximum gravitational wave strain amplitude from 15 nearby pulsars is estimated to be in the range of approximately [0.2-31.1] × 10⁻²⁴, but this represents an optimistic upper limit because it uses a breaking strain of the neutron star crust of σ_max = 0.1, whereas the breaking strain could be as low as 10⁻⁵.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to compute neutron star mass and radius for several nucleonic equations of state (EOS) constrained by terrestrial nuclear laboratory data, including the MDI interaction with two values of the parameter x (x=0 and x=-1) that bracket the allowed density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy. They then compute the quadrupole moment and ellipticity using empirical relations for slowly rotating stars, and calculate the gravitational wave strain amplitude h₀ for 15 pulsars with rotational frequencies below 300 Hz and distances less than 1 kpc, assuming a canonical mass of 1.4 M⊙.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational wave strain amplitude range: ∼[0.2-31.1] × 10⁻²⁴ for the 15 pulsars studied
- Pulsar distances from Earth: 0.16 kpc to 0.91 kpc
- Breaking strain of neutron star crust used: σ_max = 0.1 (optimistic value); earlier estimates range from 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻²
- For pulsar J0437-4715 (ν = 173.91 Hz, r = 0.16 kpc): h₀ ranges from 5.937 × 10⁻²⁴ (APR EOS) to 16.582 × 10⁻²⁴ (MDI x=-1 EOS)
- For pulsar J1744-1134 (ν = 245.43 Hz, r = 0.17 kpc): h₀ ranges from 11.128 × 10⁻²⁴ (APR EOS) to 31.082 × 10⁻²⁴ (MDI x=-1 EOS)
- For pulsar J1022+1001 (ν = 62.50 Hz, r = 0.60 kpc): h₀ ranges from 0.204 × 10⁻²⁴ (APR EOS) to 0.571 × 10⁻²⁴ (MDI x=-1 EOS)
- Saturation density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ for MDI and APR EOS; ρ₀ = 0.185 fm⁻³ for DBHF+Bonn B EOS
- Symmetry energy at saturation: 31.62 MeV (MDI), 32.60 MeV (APR), 33.71 MeV (DBHF+Bonn B)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Krastev, 2008) on constraining gravitational radiation from elliptically deformed pulsars using terrestrial nuclear laboratory data, updated in light of the direct gravitational wave detections by LIGO (Abbott, 2016). The results demonstrate that the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy significantly affects the predicted gravitational wave strain amplitude, with stiffer symmetry energy (MDI x=-1) producing larger deformations and stronger gravitational waves. The findings enable comparison with current LIGO and VIRGO sensitivity limits and motivate further refinement of neutron star crust properties and EOS constraints from both terrestrial experiments and future gravitational wave observations.
