# Gaussian Anisotropy In Strange Quark Stars

> In this paper for studying the anisotropic strange quark stars, we assume that the radial pressure inside the anisotropic star is a superposition of pressure in an isotropic case plus a Gaussian perturbation term. Considering a proportionality between electric charge density and the density of matter, we solve the TOV equation for different cases numerically. Our results indicate that anisotropy increases the maximum mass $M_{max}$ and also its corresponding radius $R$ for a typical strange quark star. According to our calculations, an anisotropy amplitude of $A=3\times10^{33}Nm^{-2}$ with a standard deviation of $σ=3\times10^{3}m$ leads to a neutron star of 1.97$M_{\odot}$. Furthermore, electric charge not only increases the maximum mass and its corresponding radius, but also raises up the anisotropy factor. We can see that the tangential pressure $p_{t}$ and anisotropy factor $Δ$ unlike the radial pressure $p_{r}$ have a maximum on the surface and this maximum increases by adding electric charge effect. However, we show that anisotropy can be more effective than electric charge in rasing maximum mass of strange quark stars.

## Key claim

The paper claims that adding a Gaussian perturbation to the radial pressure profile of a strange quark star, representing anisotropy, increases the maximum mass and corresponding radius, and that this anisotropy effect is more effective than electric charge in raising the maximum mass, but the specific result of 1.97 solar masses is achieved only for a particular choice of Gaussian amplitude and width with uncertainties of 1% at the origin and 10% on the surface.

## Method

The authors numerically solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation for a static, spherically symmetric strange quark star using the MIT Bag Model equation of state (SQM2 parameters: \(\rho_s = 3.056 \times 10^{17} \, \text{kg} \, \text{m}^{-3}\), \(\alpha = 0.324\)). They first obtain the isotropic, uncharged radial pressure profile, then treat the anisotropic case by adding a Gaussian perturbation term \(\delta p(r) = A \exp\left( -(r - \mu)^2 / (2\sigma^2) \right)\) with \(\mu = 2R/3\) to the isotropic pressure. For charged cases, they assume a proportionality between electric charge density and matter density (\(\rho_{ch} = f \times \rho\)) with \(f \leq 10^{-5} \, \text{esu} \, \text{g}^{-1}\), and solve the coupled system using the Runge-Kutta Fehlberg fourth-fifth order (RKF45) method for four scenarios: isotropic uncharged, isotropic charged, anisotropic uncharged, and anisotropic charged.

## Key numbers

- An anisotropy amplitude of \(A = 3 \times 10^{33} \, \text{N} \, \text{m}^{-2}\) with a standard deviation of \(\sigma = 3 \times 10^{3} \, \text{m}\) and \(\mu = 2R/3\) yields a strange quark star of mass \(1.97 \, M_{\odot}\).
- The MIT Bag Model parameters used are \(\rho_s = 3.056 \times 10^{17} \, \text{kg} \, \text{m}^{-3}\) and \(\alpha = 0.324\) for the SQM2 model.
- The charge fraction considered is \(f = 5 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{esu} \, \text{g}^{-1}\) for charged cases.
- The central density used in some figures is \(\rho_c = 1 \times 10^{18} \, \text{kg} \, \text{m}^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends numerical approaches to anisotropic strange quark stars, contrasting with analytical solutions such as those by Harko (2003) and Maharaj (2007) that assumed specific density profiles or metric functions. The authors show that a Gaussian perturbation to the isotropic pressure can reproduce the observed mass of the pulsar \(1.97 \pm 0.04 \, M_{\odot}\) reported by Demorest (2010), which rules out many equations of state. The results suggest that anisotropy, rather than electric charge, is a more plausible mechanism for explaining massive neutron stars within current equations of state, and the method enables future studies of alternative perturbation functions (e.g., Lorentzian) for anisotropic stars.
