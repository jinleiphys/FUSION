# Order-by-order uncertainties of nucleon-nucleon Wolfenstein amplitudes in chiral effective field theory

> Quantum mechanical invariance principles dictate the most general operator structure that can be present in the nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction. Five independent operators appear in the on-shell NN amplitude together with five corresponding coefficient functions. The usual choice for these coefficient functions is known as the NN Wolfenstein amplitudes. We analyze the order-by-order convergence of each of the five NN Wolfenstein amplitudes predicted by a semi-local coordinate space potential implementation of chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT). We do this at laboratory kinetic energies between 25 and 200 MeV for both neutron-proton and proton-proton scattering. Our analysis uses the Gaussian-Process methods developed by the BUQEYE collaboration to describe the contributions of each $χ$EFT order, and so yields truncation uncertainties for each Wolfenstein amplitude that are correlated across scattering angles. We combine information on the size of different orders in the EFT to infer the $χ$EFT breakdown scale for each amplitude, finding, on average, $Λ_b$ between 750 and 800 MeV. With this choice of $Λ_b$, the EFT truncation uncertainties cover both higher-order results and empirical Wolfenstein amplitudes well for all orders other than the leading order.

## Key claim

The order-by-order convergence of chiral effective field theory (χEFT) predictions for nucleon-nucleon Wolfenstein amplitudes is overall consistent with the BUQEYE Gaussian-process statistical model, provided that the expansion parameter Q is chosen to depend on momentum transfer and the reference amplitude y_ref is taken as the imaginary part of the np scalar amplitude. However, the leading-order (LO) prediction is a poor starting point for this convergence pattern, especially for proton-proton scattering, and the vector amplitude C requires special treatment because it has no LO contribution.

## Method

The authors analyzed the five complex Wolfenstein amplitudes (A, C, M, G, H) for both neutron-proton and proton-proton scattering, computed using the semi-local coordinate-space (SCS) χEFT NN potential of Epelbaum, Krebs, and Meißner with a cutoff of R = 0.9 fm at five chiral orders from LO to N⁴LO. They expressed the order-by-order predictions as dimensionless coefficient functions c_n and modeled these as independent draws from a Gaussian process with a squared exponential correlation kernel, following the BUQEYE methodology. The analysis was performed at laboratory kinetic energies of 25, 50, 100, 150, and 200 MeV, treating the coefficient curves as functions of momentum transfer q, with the expansion parameter Q defined using a smooth maximum of the relative momentum p and momentum transfer q.

## Key numbers

- The inferred χEFT breakdown scale Λ_b averages between 750 and 800 MeV across the Wolfenstein amplitudes.
- The momentum correlation length extracted from the Gaussian process analysis averages about 140 MeV for np scattering and about 160 MeV for pp scattering.
- The analysis covered laboratory kinetic energies from 25 to 200 MeV, with the highest energy corresponding to an expansion parameter of order 0.5.
- The difference of roughly 50 MeV in center-of-mass momentum between adjacent analyzed energies is slightly less than the momentum correlation length ℓ_p ≈ 60 MeV found by Millican et al. in a two-dimensional GP analysis of NN observables.

## Context

This work extends the BUQEYE Bayesian uncertainty quantification framework, previously applied to NN observables by Furnstahl (2015), Melendez (2017, 2019), and Millican (2024), to the invariant operator structure of the NN amplitude itself rather than to scattering observables. By analyzing the Wolfenstein amplitudes directly, the authors provide a more straightforward view of the χEFT convergence pattern than is possible through observables, which are bilinear in these amplitudes. The results enable future work to incorporate correlated truncation uncertainties from the NN interaction into few-body and many-body calculations that use the SCS χEFT potential, and the extracted breakdown scales and correlation lengths provide input for optimizing the EFT expansion parameter.
