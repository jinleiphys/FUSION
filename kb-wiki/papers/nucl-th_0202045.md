# Superfluidity with dressed nucleons

> The gap equation with dressed propagators is solved in symmetric nuclear matter. Nucleon self-energies are obtained within the self-consistent in medium T matrix approximation. The off-shell gap equation is compared to an effective quasiparticle gap equation with reduced interaction. At normal density, we find a reduction of the superfluid gap from 6.5MeV to .45MeV when self-energy effects are included.

## Key claim

When nucleon self-energy effects from the self-consistent in-medium T matrix approximation are included, the superfluid gap in symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density is reduced from 6.5 MeV to 0.45 MeV, but the gap remains nonzero, contrary to suggestions that normal nuclear matter is not superfluid.

## Method

The author solved the gap equation with dressed propagators in symmetric nuclear matter, obtaining nucleon self-energies from the self-consistent in-medium T matrix approximation using the separable Paris interaction. The off-shell gap equation was compared to an effective quasiparticle gap equation with a reduced interaction renormalized by the quasiparticle strength factor Z_k^2. The diagonal self-energies were calculated assuming a normal (nonsuperfluid) state of nuclear matter as a first approximation.

## Key numbers

- Superfluid gap at saturation density with dressed propagators: 0.45 MeV
- Superfluid gap at saturation density with bare interaction (BCS): 6.5 MeV
- Maximum superfluid gap with dressed propagators: 2.2 MeV at 0.5 times saturation density
- Density range studied: 0.1 to 2.0 times saturation density

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of pairing in nuclear matter that used the induced interaction (Clark 1976, Wambach 1993, Schulze 1996, Polls 1998) and studies of nucleon dressing effects (Bozek 2000, Baldo 2000, Lombardo 2001). It resolves the paradox that BCS calculations with bare interactions gave a large neutron-proton gap in the 3S1-3D1 channel incompatible with finite-nuclei data, and it justifies standard nuclear matter approaches that neglect the superfluid transition. The effective quasiparticle gap equation with reduced interaction Z_k^2 V(p,k) provides a starting point for including induced interaction effects (Schwenk 2001, Friman 2001, Friman 2002).
