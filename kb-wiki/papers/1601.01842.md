# Low density nuclear matter with quantum molecular dynamics : The role of the symmetry energy

> We study the effect of isospin-dependent nuclear forces on the pasta phase in the inner crust of neutron stars. To this end we model the crust within the framework of quantum molecular dynamics (QMD). For maximizing the numerical performance, a newly developed code has been implemented on GPU processors. As a first application of the crust studies we investigate the dependence of the particular pasta phases on the isospin dependence of the interaction, including non-linear terms in this sector of the interactions. Our results indicate that in contrast to earlier studies the phase diagram of the pasta phase is not very sensitive to isospin effects. We show that the extraction of the isospin parameters like asymmetry energy and slope from numerical data is affected by higher-order terms in the asymmetry dependence of the energies per particle. Furthermore, a rapid transition from the pasta to a homogeneous phase is observed even for proton-to-neutron ratios typical for a supernova environment.

# Low density nuclear matter with quantum molecular dynamics: The role of the symmetry energy

## Key claim
Contrary to earlier studies, the phase diagram of nuclear pasta in the inner crust of neutron stars is not very sensitive to isospin effects, including the symmetry energy and its slope L, when studied consistently within a single quantum molecular dynamics model. However, the extraction of symmetry energy parameters from numerical data is significantly affected by higher-order terms in the asymmetry dependence of the energies per particle, meaning the commonly used parabolic approximation is inadequate for proton fractions typical of supernova environments.

## Method
The authors performed quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations of 2048 nucleons (protons and neutrons) confined in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using a newly developed GPU-accelerated code. They extended the effective interaction of Maruyama et al. (1998) by adding a non-linear term to the isospin-dependent potential, allowing them to tune the symmetry energy and its slope L while keeping all other nuclear matter parameters fixed. Three parameter sets were prepared, yielding different symmetry energy behaviors, and the system was equilibrated at T=20 MeV then cooled to below 1 keV to obtain ground state configurations. Minkowski functionals and two-point correlation functions were used to characterize the pasta shapes and the transition to uniform matter.

## Key numbers
- 2048 nucleons were used in the simulation, with 608 protons and 1440 neutrons for a proton fraction Yp = 0.3
- The three parameter sets produced symmetry energy at saturation density esym(ρ0) values of 34.6, 34.3, and 34.2 MeV under the parabolic approximation, with corresponding slope L values of 76.8, 91.8, and 114.4 MeV
- When including the fourth-order term, esym(ρ0) ranged from 24.5 to 32.1 MeV and L ranged from 76.1 to 102.2 MeV across the three sets
- The lower boundary of the pasta phase lay in the range 0.1-0.125 ρ0 for all three parameter sets
- Long-range correlations in the two-point correlation function vanished between 0.6 ρ0 and 0.625 ρ0 for all three sets, indicating the transition to uniform matter
- The incompressibility K0 of symmetric nuclear matter was fixed at 280 MeV

## Context
This work extends the QMD approach of Maruyama et al. (1998) and Watanabe et al. (2003, 2004) by adding non-linear isospin terms to study the dependence of pasta phases on symmetry energy within a single consistent model. It contradicts earlier findings from static models such as the liquid-drop model of Bao et al. (2014), Thomas-Fermi approximations by Oyamatsu et al. (2007) and Grill et al. (2012), and the QMD study of Sonoda et al. (2008), all of which found that the width of the pasta phase decreases with increasing L. The results enable future studies of transport properties in neutron star crusts and can be extended to larger systems to investigate finite-size effects as discussed by Molinelli et al. (2015).
