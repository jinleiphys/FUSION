# Dynamical diquarks in the ${\boldsymbol{γ^{(\ast)} p\to N(1535)\tfrac{1}{2}^-}}$ transition

> The $γ^{(\ast)}+p \to N(1535) \tfrac{1}{2}^-$ transition is studied using a symmetry-preserving regularisation of a vector$\,\otimes\,$vector contact interaction (SCI). The framework employs a Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation to describe the initial and final state baryons as quark+di\-quark composites, wherein the diquark correlations are fully dynamical, interacting with the photon as allowed by their quantum numbers and continually engaging in breakup and recombination as required by the Faddeev kernel. The presence of such correlations owes largely to the mechanisms responsible for the emergence of hadron mass; and whereas the nucleon Faddeev amplitude is dominated by scalar and axial-vector diquark correlations, the amplitude of its parity partner, the $N(1535) \tfrac{1}{2}^-$, also contains sizeable pseudoscalar and vector diquark components. It is found that the $γ^{(\ast)}+p \to N(1535) \tfrac{1}{2}^-$ helicity amplitudes and related Dirac and Pauli form factors are keenly sensitive to the relative strengths of these diquark components in the baryon amplitudes, indicating that such resonance electrocouplings possess great sensitivity to baryon structural details. Whilst SCI analyses have their limitations, they also have the virtue of algebraic simplicity and a proven ability to reveal insights that can be used to inform more sophisticated studies in frameworks with closer ties to quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The transition form factors and helicity amplitudes for $\gamma^{(\ast)} p\to N(1535)\tfrac{1}{2}^-$ are highly sensitive to the relative strengths of scalar, pseudoscalar, axial-vector, and vector diquark components in the baryon Faddeev amplitudes, but the results are obtained using a symmetry-preserving regularisation of a vector$\otimes$vector contact interaction (SCI), which has limitations compared to frameworks with closer ties to quantum chromodynamics.

## Method

The authors employed a Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation to describe both the proton and the $N(1535)\tfrac{1}{2}^-$ as quark+diquark composites, using a symmetry-preserving regularisation of a vector$\otimes$vector contact interaction (SCI). The diquark correlations were treated as fully dynamical, interacting with the photon as allowed by their quantum numbers and undergoing breakup and recombination within the Faddeev kernel. The electromagnetic transition current was constructed from three distinct contributions: photon coupling directly to the dressed-quark, photon coupling to a diquark in elastic scattering, and photon inducing transitions between different diquark types.

## Key numbers

- Dressed-quark core masses: $m_{N(940)} = 1.14$ GeV and $m_{N(1535)} = 1.73$ GeV, obtained with interaction strength $\alpha_{\rm IR} = 0.93\pi$, infrared cutoff $\Lambda_{\rm ir} = 0.24$ GeV, and ultraviolet cutoff $\Lambda_{\rm uv} = 0.905$ GeV.
- Diquark mass-scales (in GeV): $m_{[ud]_{0^+}} = 0.78$, $m_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} = 1.06$, $m_{[ud]_{0^-}} = 1.15$, $m_{[ud]_{1-}} = 1.33$.
- Faddeev amplitude components for $N(1535)\tfrac{1}{2}^-$: scalar diquark coefficient $s = 0.66$, pseudoscalar diquark coefficient $p = 0.68$, axial-vector coefficients $a_1^1 = 0.20$, $a_2^1 = 0.14$, vector coefficients $v_1 = 0.11$, $v_2 = 0.09$.
- Static properties for $N^+(1535)$: Dirac radius $r_1 m_+ = 3.20(22)$, Pauli radius $r_2 m_+ = 3.52(76)$, anomalous magnetic moment $\kappa = -1.18(46)$.

## Context

This work extends the study of baryon structure using Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equations with dynamical diquark correlations, following earlier calculations of the nucleon and Roper resonance electroproduction (Wilson 2011, Segovia 2015). The results contrast with constituent-quark models (Isgur 1978) that picture the $N(1535)\tfrac{1}{2}^-$ as a simple $P$-wave excitation, and address the failure of quark models to reproduce the sign of the longitudinal helicity amplitude $S_{1/2}$ observed in pion electroproduction data (Dugger 2009, Aznauryan 2009). The findings enable more sophisticated studies using interactions with closer ties to quantum chromodynamics, informed by the sensitivity patterns identified here.
