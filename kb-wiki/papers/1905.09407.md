# Discovery of an Exceptionally Strong $β$-Decay Transition of $^{20}$F and Implications for the Fate of Intermediate-Mass Stars

> A significant fraction of stars between 7-11 solar masses are thought to become supernovae, but the explosion mechanism is unclear. The answer depends critically on the rate of electron capture on $^{20}$Ne in the degenerate oxygen-neon stellar core. However, due to the unknown strength of the transition between the ground states of $^{20}$Ne and $^{20}$F, it has not previously been possible to fully constrain the rate. By measuring the transition, we have established that its strength is exceptionally large and enhances the capture rate by several orders of magnitude. This has a decisive impact on the evolution of the core, increasing the likelihood that the star is (partially) disrupted by a thermonuclear explosion rather than collapsing to form a neutron star. Importantly, our measurement resolves the last remaining nuclear physics uncertainty in the final evolution of degenerate oxygen-neon stellar cores, allowing future studies to address the critical role of convection, which at present is poorly understood.

## Key claim

The second-forbidden, non-unique ground-state-to-ground-state transition in the beta decay of $^{20}$F is exceptionally strong ($\log ft = 10.89(11)$), enhancing the electron-capture rate on $^{20}$Ne by up to eight orders of magnitude in the density range $\rho_9 \simeq 4.5$–$9.5$ and thereby making thermonuclear explosion more likely than gravitational collapse for intermediate-mass stars of 7–11 solar masses.

## Method

The branching ratio of the second-forbidden ground-state transition in the $\beta^-$ decay of $^{20}$F was measured at the JYFL Accelerator Laboratory using a low-energy radioactive $^{20}$F beam from the IGISOL-4 facility. A Siegbahn-Slätis type intermediate-image magnetic electron transporter focused high-energy electrons from the forbidden transition into a segmented plastic-scintillator detector while suppressing the intense allowed transition and cosmic-ray background; a LaBr$_3$(Ce) detector measured the 1.63-MeV $\gamma$ ray from the allowed transition for normalization. The electron-capture rate on $^{20}$Ne was then calculated using shell-model matrix elements (USDB interaction) constrained by the measured transition strength, and the impact on stellar evolution was simulated with the MESA code and 3D hydrodynamical simulations using the LEAFS code.

## Key numbers

- Branching ratio of the forbidden ground-state transition: $0.41(11) \times 10^{-5}$ (statistical and systematic uncertainties added in quadrature).
- Transition strength: $\log ft = 10.89(11)$, making it three orders of magnitude stronger than the analogous transition in $^{36}\mathrm{Cl}\rightarrow{}^{36}\mathrm{Ar}$ ($\log ft = 13.321(3)$).
- Electron-capture rate on $^{20}$Ne increases by up to eight orders of magnitude in the density range $\rho_9 \simeq 4.5$–$9.5$ ($\log_{10}[\rho Y_e(\mathrm{g\;cm}^{-3})] \simeq 9.35$–$9.68$) at $T=0.4$ GK.
- Half-life of $^{20}$F: 11.0062(80) s.
- 3D hydrodynamical simulations produced bound remnant masses of 0.245–0.438 $M_\odot$ and ejecta masses of 0.951–1.150 $M_\odot$ for central densities at ignition of $7.943 \times 10^9$ to $9.772 \times 10^9$ g cm$^{-3}$.
- $^{60}$Fe production in the thermonuclear explosion: $3.63 \times 10^{-3}$ $M_\odot$.

## Context

This work resolves the last remaining nuclear physics uncertainty in the evolution of degenerate oxygen-neon stellar cores, which had been identified by Martínez-Pinedo et al. (2014) as the unknown strength of the ground-state transition between $^{20}$Ne and $^{20}$F. Previous studies by Miyaji et al. (1980), Nomoto (1982, 1984), Isern et al. (1991), Canal et al. (1992), Gutiérrez et al. (1996), and Schwab et al. (2015, 2017) had considered only the allowed transition to the first $1^+$ state in $^{20}$F, requiring a central density of $\rho_9 \approx 9.8$ for electron capture to begin. The new rate reduces the ignition density below the critical value of $\rho_9^\mathrm{crit}=8.9$ and favors off-center ignition, making thermonuclear explosion more likely than collapse to a neutron star. Future studies can now focus on the role of convection in the progenitor evolution, which remains poorly understood.
