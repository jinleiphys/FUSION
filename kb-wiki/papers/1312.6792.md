# Intermediate energy four-body breakup calculations for 22C

> The heaviest particle-bound carbon isotope, $^{22}$C, is thought to have a Borromean three-body structure. We discuss and compare four-body, i.e. three-body projectile plus target, reaction model calculations of reaction cross sections for such systems that use the fast adiabatic approximation. These methods are efficient and well-suited for quantitative analyses of reactions of neutron-rich nuclei with light target nuclei at secondary beam energies of $\approx$300 MeV/nucleon, as are now becoming available. We compare the predictions of the adiabatic model of the reaction both without and when including the additional eikonal approximation that has been used extensively. We show that the reaction cross section calculations have only limited sensitivity to the size and structure of $^{22}$C and that the differences arising from use of the eikonal approximation of the reaction mechanism are of a similar magnitude.

# Intermediate energy four-body breakup calculations for 22C

## Key claim

The reaction cross section calculations for 22C on a carbon target at 300 MeV/nucleon have only limited sensitivity to the size and structure of the projectile, and the differences between the full adiabatic model and the computationally simpler adiabatic-plus-eikonal approximation are small (approximately 2%), but this conclusion is conditional on the relatively narrow range of root-mean-squared radii (approximately 6%) produced by the three-body model wave functions used.

## Method

The authors performed four-body reaction model calculations for 22C incident on a 12C target at 300 MeV/nucleon, treating the projectile as a 20C core plus two valence neutrons in a Borromean three-body structure. They solved the scattering problem using the coupled-channels adiabatic approach of Christley et al., systematically expanding the breakup model space to include s-wave, p-wave, and d-wave configurations, and compared these results with calculations using the additional eikonal (Glauber) approximation. The three-body wave functions for 22C were generated using the code efaddy with Woods-Saxon neutron-core potentials and the Gogny-Pires-De Tourreil interaction for the neutron-neutron force, varying the s-wave potential depth and a three-body force to produce a range of two-neutron separation energies consistent with experimental uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- The 22C point-nucleon rms matter radii from the five model wave functions range from 3.505 fm (model k1) to 3.719 fm (model k4), a variation of approximately 6%
- The two-neutron separation energies for the models range from -0.046 MeV (model k4) to -0.442 MeV (model k1)
- The n+20C 2s1/2 virtual state scattering lengths range from -16.1 fm (model k4) to -333.3 fm (models k1 and k5)
- The most complete adiabatic model reaction cross sections (sigma_R Ad) range from 1328.0 mb (model k1) to 1391.3 mb (model k4) on a carbon target at 300 MeV/nucleon
- The Glauber few-body model cross sections (sigma_R Gl) range from 1299.1 mb (model k1) to 1353.3 mb (model k4), consistently approximately 2% smaller than the adiabatic results
- The dominant ground-state configuration is the [2s1/2]^2 component, with probabilities between 0.919 (model k4) and 0.939 (model k5)
- The 1D (nn) component contributes 0.174 (model k4) to 0.234 (model k1) of the wave function probability
- The 1G (nn) component contributes approximately 0.041-0.047 across all models
- The 20C core rms radius was taken as 2.913 fm from a Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculation with the SkX interaction
- The carbon target point-nucleon rms radius was taken as 2.32 fm

## Context

This work extends the four-body adiabatic reaction model developed by Christley et al. for 11Li elastic scattering to the Borromean system 22C, and compares it with the eikonal (Glauber) few-body approach used by Jakubassa-Amundsen (1995) and Johnson (1997) for reaction cross sections. The calculations are motivated by recent measurements at RIKEN at energies above 200 MeV/nucleon, including inclusive Coulomb dissociation on a Pb target (Nakamura, 2013) and fast nucleon removal on a C target (Kobayashi, 2012), as well as a low-energy reaction cross section measurement on a proton target (Tanaka, 2010) that suggested a very large rms radius of 5.4(9) fm. The results enable quantitative assessment of the accuracy of the computationally efficient eikonal method for intermediate-energy reactions of neutron-rich nuclei, and clarify the limited sensitivity of inclusive reaction cross sections to the detailed structure of 22C, suggesting that more exclusive observables may be needed to constrain the halo structure.
