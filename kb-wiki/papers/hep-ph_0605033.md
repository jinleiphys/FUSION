# Topical Review on "Beta-beams"

> Neutrino physics is traversing an exciting period, after the important discovery that neutrinos are massive particles, that has implications from high-energy physics to cosmology. A new method for the production of intense and pure neutrino beams has been proposed recently: the ``beta-beam''. It exploits boosted radioactive ions decaying through beta-decay. This novel concept has been the starting point for a new possible future facility. Its main goal is to address the crucial issue of the existence of CP violation in the lepton sector. Here we review the status and the recent developments with beta-beams. We discuss the original, the medium and high-energy scenarios as well as mono-chromatic neutrino beams produced through ion electron-capture. The issue of the degeneracies is mentioned. An overview of low energy beta-beams is also presented. These beams can be used to perform experiments of interest for nuclear structure, for the study of fundamental interactions and for nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim

Beta-beams offer a novel method for producing intense, pure, and collimated neutrino beams using boosted radioactive ions decaying through beta-decay, with the primary goal of investigating CP violation in the lepton sector, though the achievable sensitivity depends critically on achievable ion intensities and the specific accelerator scenario (original, medium-energy, or high-energy) that can be realized.

## Method

The paper reviews the beta-beam concept where radioactive ions are produced, collected, accelerated to relativistic energies (with Lorentz gamma factors ranging from approximately 5-14 for low-energy applications to 150-1000 for CP violation studies), and stored in a storage ring with straight sections. Neutrinos emitted from beta-decay along the straight sections are collimated in the forward direction due to the Lorentz boost, with the flux determined analytically from the known beta-decay spectrum and the ion beam parameters. The original scenario envisions using the CERN PS and SPS accelerators, a 7.5 km storage ring with 2.5 km straight sections, and a detector at the Frejus Underground Laboratory.

## Key numbers

- The original beta-beam scenario uses ion Lorentz gamma factors of approximately 100 (from acceleration to several tens of GeV per nucleon via PS and SPS accelerators)
- Low-energy beta-beams use gamma factors of 5-14 to produce neutrino beams in the 100 MeV energy range
- The storage ring in the original scenario has a total length of 7.5 km with straight sections of 2.5 km
- Radioactive ion beam facilities are expected to reach ion intensities of 10^11-10^13 per second
- The present upper limit on the third neutrino mixing angle is sin^2 2θ13 < 0.1 from the CHOOZ reactor experiment (Apollonio 1999)
- The solar oscillation parameters are Δm^2_12 = 7.9 × 10^(-5) eV^2 and sin^2 θ12 = 0.3
- The atmospheric oscillation parameters are Δm^2_23 = 2.4 × 10^(-3) eV^2 and sin^2 θ23 = 0.5

## Context

This review extends the original beta-beam proposal by Zucchelli (2002) and the subsequent feasibility study by Autin et al. (2003), covering developments through 2006. The work connects to the broader neutrino physics program including super-beams and neutrino factories (Albright et al. 2004), and discusses how beta-beams can complement these approaches by providing pure electron neutrino and antineutrino beams with well-known fluxes. The review also covers low-energy beta-beams (Volpe 2004) for nuclear structure and astrophysics applications, medium and high-energy scenarios for CP violation studies, and monochromatic neutrino beams from electron capture (Bernabeu et al. 2005a).
