# Multi-channel experimental and theoretical constraints for the $^{116}$Cd($^{20}$Ne,$^{20}$F)$^{116}$In charge exchange reaction at 306 MeV

> Charge exchange (CE) reactions offer a major opportunity to excite nuclear isovector modes, providing clues about the nuclear interaction in the medium. Moreover, double charge exchange (DCE) reactions are proving to be a tempting tool to access nuclear transition matrix elements (NME) related to double beta-decay processes. Through a multi-channel experimental analysis and a consistent theoretical approach of the $^{116}$Cd($^{20}$Ne,$^{20}$F)$^{116}$In single charge exchange (SCE) reaction at 306 MeV, we aim at disentangling from the experimental cross section the contribution of the competing mechanisms, associated with second or higher order sequential transfer and inelastic processes. We measured excitation energy spectra and absolute cross sections for elastic + inelastic, one-proton transfer and SCE channels, using the MAGNEX large acceptance magnetic spectrometer to detect the ejectiles. For the first two channels, we also extracted the experimental cross section angular distributions. The experimental data are compared with theoretical predictions obtained by performing two-step distorted wave Born approximation and coupled reaction channel calculations. We employ spectroscopic amplitudes for single-particle transitions derived within a large-scale shell model approach and different optical potentials for modeling the initial and the final state interactions. The present study significantly mitigates the possible model dependence existing in the description of these complex reaction mechanisms, thanks to the reproduction of several channels at once. In particular, our work demonstrates that the two-step transfer mechanisms produce a non negligible contribution to the total cross section of the $^{116}$Cd($^{20}$Ne,$^{20}$F)$^{116}$In reaction channel, although a relevant fraction is still missing, being ascribable to the direct SCE mechanism, which is not addressed here.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two-step sequential transfer mechanisms produce a non-negligible contribution to the total cross section of the 116Cd(20Ne,20F)116In single charge exchange reaction at 306 MeV, but a relevant fraction of the cross section remains unaccounted for and is ascribable to the direct SCE mechanism, which is not addressed in this work.

## Method

The authors measured excitation energy spectra and absolute cross sections for elastic plus inelastic scattering, one-proton transfer, and single charge exchange channels using the MAGNEX large acceptance magnetic spectrometer at INFN-LNS in Catania. They compared experimental data with theoretical predictions from two-step distorted wave Born approximation and coupled reaction channel calculations, employing spectroscopic amplitudes from large-scale shell model calculations and different optical potentials for initial and final state interactions.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 306 MeV for 20Ne4+ incident on 116Cd targets
- Target thicknesses: (1370 ± 70) μg/cm2 for one-proton transfer, (1080 ± 60) μg/cm2 for SCE, and (1330 ± 70) μg/cm2 for scattering measurements
- Angular range for elastic and inelastic scattering: 3° < θlab < 26°, measured at three optical axis settings (θopt = 8°, 13°, and 20°)
- Angular range for one-proton transfer: 3° < θlab < 14° at θopt = 8°
- Angular range for SCE: 4° < θlab < 15° at θopt = 9°
- Solid angle for SCE measurement: approximately 1.3 msr
- Systematic uncertainty of approximately 10% common to all experimental data, originating from target thickness

## Context

This work extends the multi-channel approach previously applied to other systems (Cavallaro et al., 2021; Akis et al.) by simultaneously measuring several reaction channels under identical experimental conditions for the 116Cd(20Ne,20F)116In system. The study is part of the NUMEN and NURE projects at LNS-INFN, which aim to exploit double charge exchange reactions to access nuclear transition matrix elements related to double beta decay. The results enable future isolation of the direct SCE contribution, which is necessary to extract structure information on the corresponding nuclear matrix elements, and provide a foundation for analyzing SCE and DCE reactions on lighter target nuclei using the same methods.
