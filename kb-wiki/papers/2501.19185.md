# Deviations from the Porter-Thomas Distribution due to Nonstatistical $γ$ Decay below the $^{150}$Nd Neutron Separation Threshold

> We introduce a new method for the study of fluctuations of partial transition widths based on nuclear resonance fluorescence experiments with quasimonochromatic linearly polarized photon beams below particle separation thresholds. It is based on the average branching of decays of $J=1$ states of an even-even nucleus to the $2^+_1$ state in comparison to the ground state. Between 5 and 7 MeV, a constant average branching ratio for $γ$ decays from $1^-$ states of 0.490(16) is observed for the nuclide $^{150}$Nd. Assuming $χ^2$-distributed partial transition widths, this average branching ratio is related to a degree of freedom of $ν= 1.93(12)$, rejecting the validity of the Porter-Thomas distribution, requiring $ν=1$. The observed deviation can be explained by nonstatistical effects in the $γ$-decay behavior with contributions in the range of 9.4(10)% up to 94(10)%.

# Deviations from the Porter-Thomas Distribution due to Nonstatistical γ Decay below the ¹⁵⁰Nd Neutron Separation Threshold

## Key claim

The authors report that the average branching ratio for γ decays from 1⁻ states in ¹⁵⁰Nd between 5 and 7 MeV is 0.490(16), which corresponds to a χ² distribution degree of freedom ν = 1.93(12), rejecting the Porter-Thomas distribution (ν = 1) by 7 standard deviations, but this deviation can be explained by nonstatistical contributions as small as 9.4(10)% or as large as 94(10)% of the total ground-state decay cross section, depending on whether the nonstatistical component is attributed to K = 0 or K = 1 states.

## Method

Nuclear resonance fluorescence experiments were performed at the Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory using the High Intensity γ-ray Source (HIγS) operated in a new high-resolution mode with a spectral bandwidth of about 1.8%. Quasimonochromatic linearly polarized photon beams between 3 and 7 MeV were scattered off a 11.583(1) g target of Nd₂O₃ enriched to 93.60(2)% in ¹⁵⁰Nd, and the resulting γ-ray spectra were decomposed via a fit-based method to separate decays to the 0⁺₁ ground state and the 2⁺₁ state at 130 keV. The average branching ratio was related to the internal fluctuation ratio s of χ²-distributed partial transition widths, which was then converted to the degree of freedom ν.

## Key numbers

- Average branching ratio for γ decays from 1⁻ states between 5 and 7 MeV: 0.490(16)
- Degree of freedom of the χ² distribution derived from the branching ratio: ν = 1.93(12)
- Statistical model simulation (Dicebox code) yields average branching ratio ≈ 0.31, consistent with s = 1/3 for Porter-Thomas distribution (ν = 1)
- Lower limit of nonstatistical contribution from K = 0 states: 9.4(10)% of total ground-state decay cross section
- Upper limit of nonstatistical contribution from K = 1 states: 94(10)% of total ground-state decay cross section
- Thomson scattering cross section correction for ¹⁵⁰Nd: 0.115 mb
- Energy of the 2⁺₁ state in ¹⁵⁰Nd: 130 keV

## Context

This work extends studies of partial width fluctuations previously conducted in thermal neutron capture experiments, where analyses of the nuclear data ensemble (NDE) by Haq (1982) found agreement with GOE predictions but reanalyses by Adams (1998), Shriner (2000), Koehler (2010), and Koehler (2011) revealed deviations with ν values ranging from about 0.5 to 2.7(6). The present study provides the first experimental data on width fluctuations below neutron separation thresholds in deformed nuclei, specifically in the pygmy dipole resonance region. The results contradict the assumption that the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model with Porter-Thomas distributed widths applies to E1 decays in this energy regime, and they support earlier evidence from Angell (2012), Isaak (2013), Isaak (2019), Papst (2020), and Sieja (2023) that decay properties of photoexcited 1⁻ states in the quasicontinuum do not follow statistical model expectations. The findings imply that photon strength function extractions via the ratio or shape method must account for the internal fluctuation ratio s.
