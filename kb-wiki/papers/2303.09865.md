# Nuclear decay anomalies as a signature of axion dark matter

> A number of nuclear decay anomalies have been reported in the literature, which purport to show periodic variations in the decay rates of certain radioisotopes. If these reports reflect reality, they would necessitate a seismic shift in our understanding of fundamental physics. We provide the first mechanism to explain these findings, via the misalignment mechanism of QCD axion dark matter, wherein oscillations of the effective $θ$ angle induce periodic variation in nuclear binding energies and hence decay rates. As we expect this effect to be most pronounced in low-$Q$ systems, we analyse 12 years of tritium decay data ($Q\simeq$ 18.6 keV) taken at the European Commission's Joint Research Centre. Finding no statistically significant excess, we exclude axion decay constants below $9.4\times10^{12} - 1.8\times10^{10}$ GeV (95% confidence level) for masses in the $1.7\times{10}^{-23} - 8.7\times 10^{-21}$ eV range.

# Nuclear decay anomalies as a signature of axion dark matter

## Key claim
If the reported periodic variations in nuclear decay rates are real, they could be explained by oscillations of the QCD axion dark matter field via the misalignment mechanism, which induces periodic variation in nuclear binding energies and hence decay rates. However, the authors find no statistically significant excess in 12 years of tritium decay data, and therefore the mechanism is not confirmed but instead used to set exclusion limits on axion parameters.

## Method
The authors calculate the fractional change in beta decay rate as a function of the QCD theta angle, using the theta-dependence of nuclear binding energies for light nuclei derived from Wigner SU(4) symmetry relations and numerical coefficients from Lee (2020). They then analyze 12 years of tritium decay data from the European Commission's Joint Research Centre using Least Squares Spectral Analysis (Lomb-Scargle periodograms) with Monte Carlo simulations to determine false positive thresholds, searching for oscillatory signals at trial frequencies corresponding to axion masses in the range of interest.

## Key numbers
- Tritium decay Q-value: approximately 18.6 keV
- Fractional change in tritium decay rate: I_0(theta) for tritium is approximately 0.18 times (delta E(theta) / keV), compared to 1.16 times (delta E(theta) / keV) for Re-187 (Q approximately 2.6 keV)
- Excluded axion decay constants: below 9.4 x 10^12 to 1.8 x 10^10 GeV (95% confidence level) for axion masses in the 1.7 x 10^-23 to 8.7 x 10^-21 eV range
- The shift in decay energy for excluded parameters: approximately 3.5 x 10^-2 eV times (rho_DM / 0.45 GeV/cm^3) times (10^-21 eV / m_a)^2 times (10^13 GeV / f_a)^2
- The fractional variation I(theta) for tritium: approximately 1.3 x 10^-5 times (rho_DM / 0.45 GeV/cm^3) times (10^-21 eV / m_a)^2 times (10^13 GeV / f_a)^2 times cos(2(omega t + p dot x + phi))
- Frequency range analyzed: 8.0 x 10^-9 Hz (period approximately 4.0 years) to 4.2 x 10^-6 Hz (period approximately 2.8 days), after excluding the lowest 15 trial frequencies

## Context
This work provides the first concrete mechanism to explain reported nuclear decay anomalies, which have been previously attributed to systematic effects by Pomme (2016, 2018, 2019, 2020) and others. The mechanism connects these anomalies to QCD axion dark matter, which also addresses the Strong CP problem via the Peccei-Quinn mechanism. The analysis extends the approach used in oscillating neutron electric dipole moment searches by Abel (2017), Wu (2019), and Aybas (2021) to nuclear decay data, and complements astrophysical constraints from ultra-faint dwarf galaxies and Lyman-alpha forest observations. The results enable future dedicated nuclear decay experiments optimized to search for this effect, potentially probing previously unexplored regions of axion parameter space.
