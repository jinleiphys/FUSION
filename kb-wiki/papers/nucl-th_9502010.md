# Study of non-equilibrium effects and thermal properties of heavy ion collisions using a covariant approach

> Non-equilibrium effects are studied using a full Lorentz-invariant formalism. Our analysis shows that in reactions considered here, no global or local equilibrium is reached. The heavier masses are found to be equilibrated more than the lighter systems. The local temperature is extracted using hot Thomas Fermi formalism generalized for the case of two interpenetrating pieces of nuclear matter. The temperature is found to vary linearly with bombarding energy and impact parameter whereas it is nearly independent of the mass of the colliding nuclei. This indicates that the study of temperature with medium size nuclei is also reliable. The maximum temperatures obtained in our approach are in a nice agreement with earlier calculations of other approaches. A simple parametrization of maximal temperature as a function of the bombarding energy is also given.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in heavy ion collisions from 40 MeV/nucleon to 500 MeV/nucleon, no global or local equilibrium is reached, contradicting the assumption of equilibrium used in hydrodynamical models. The maximum temperature extracted is nearly independent of the mass of the colliding nuclei, depending only on bombarding energy and impact parameter, which the abstract presents as a clean linear relation but which the full text qualifies as holding only for a fixed scaled impact parameter of b = 0.25 b_max.

## Method

The authors couple a covariant Relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (RQMD) model, which propagates baryons in a Lorentz-invariant fashion using constraint Hamiltonian dynamics, with a Generalized Hot Thomas Fermi Formalism (GHTFF) to extract local temperature from the matter and kinetic energy densities. The RQMD simulation includes stochastic scattering, Pauli-blocking, and Gaussian wave-packets for nucleons, with the collision part treated covariantly using Cugnon cross-sections. Temperature is extracted in each local volume element by fitting a deformed Fermi distribution consisting of two colliding Fermi spheres, under a local density approximation that is not itself made relativistic.

## Key numbers

- Anisotropy ratio <R_a> rises from about 0.5 to about 0.8 at 60 fm/c for 50 MeV/nucleon 40Ca-40Ca collisions at impact parameter 2 fm, indicating no global equilibrium (value less than 1).
- For 1 GeV/nucleon 40Ca-40Ca at impact parameter 2 fm, the anisotropy ratio shows a sudden increase upon nuclear contact, but the Vlasov-mode (no collisions) shows no such change.
- Maximum temperature parametrization for b = 0.25 b_max: T^max = 0.09466 * E_lab + 4.934, valid for bombarding energies between 40 MeV/nucleon and 500 MeV/nucleon (units: T in MeV, E_lab in MeV/nucleon).
- The product of boost velocity beta_cm and time when temperature and density reach maximum is nearly the same for all bombarding energies studied (125, 250, 375, 500 MeV/nucleon).
- At 500 MeV/nucleon for 40Ca-40Ca, central collisions (b=0 fm) yield higher temperature and density than peripheral collisions (b=5 fm), with the soft equation of state producing "far more temperatures" than the hard equation of state.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous non-covariant QMD studies of thermal properties (Khoa 1992) by using a fully covariant RQMD model (Lehmann 1994, Puri 1994) to avoid assumptions about the smallness of relativistic corrections. It contradicts the assumption of local equilibrium used in hydrodynamical models (Clare 1986) and agrees with earlier temperature extractions from measured pion yields by Hahn and Stocker (1986). The results enable future studies of temperature using medium-size nuclei and provide a simple parametrization for maximum temperature as a function of bombarding energy, though the authors note that using in-medium cross-sections or one-boson exchange cross-sections would produce significant enhancement in temperature (Khoa 1992, Puri 1994).
