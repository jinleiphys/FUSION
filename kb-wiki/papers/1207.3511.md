# From Exact to Partial Dynamical Symmetries: Lessons From the Interacting Boson Model

> We exploit the rich algebraic structure of the interacting boson model to explain the notion of partial dynamical symmetry (PDS), and present a procedure for constructing Hamiltonians with this property. We demonstrate the relevance of PDS to various topics in nuclear spectroscopy, including K-band splitting, odd-even staggering in the gamma-band and anharmonicity of excited vibrational bands. Special emphasis in this construction is paid to the role of higher-order terms.

# From Exact to Partial Dynamical Symmetries: Lessons From the Interacting Boson Model

## Key claim

Partial dynamical symmetries (PDS) provide a framework where a prescribed symmetry is neither exact nor completely broken, but is selectively preserved for only some states while others mix and lose the symmetry character; this intermediate structure can be constructed algorithmically by identifying n-body annihilation operators that vanish on specific irreducible representations of the symmetry group.

## Method

The author exploits the algebraic structure of the interacting boson model (IBM) to construct Hamiltonians with partial dynamical symmetry (PDS) by identifying n-boson annihilation operators that satisfy the property of annihilating all states in a given irreducible representation of the symmetry group. For SU(3)-PDS, two-boson operators P0 and P2m are identified that annihilate states in the (2N,0) ground band, while three-body operators Wℓm annihilate states in both the ground and β bands. For O(6)-PDS, three-boson operators involving P0 and s or d bosons are constructed that annihilate all states in the Σ=N ground band. The resulting Hamiltonians add products of these annihilation operators and their Hermitian conjugates to the dynamical symmetry Hamiltonian, preserving solvability for selected states while breaking symmetry for others.

## Context

This work extends the concept of dynamical symmetries pioneered by Iachello (1987) in the IBM framework, which has three dynamical symmetry limits (U(5), SU(3), O(6)) corresponding to vibrational, rotational, and γ-unstable collective spectra. The PDS approach addresses the common empirical situation where symmetry predictions are fulfilled by only some states but not others, as seen in K-band splitting in 168Er, odd-even staggering in the γ-band of 156Gd, and anharmonicity of excited vibrational bands in 196Pt. The construction enables controlled symmetry breaking that preserves previously successful dynamical symmetry descriptions for selected states while improving agreement for mixed states, and is applicable beyond bosonic systems to fermionic systems (Escher 2000, Rowe 2001, Isacker 2008) and quantum phase transitions (Leviatan 2007).
