# The overdamped chiral magnetic wave

> About eight years ago it was predicted theoretically that a charged chiral plasma could support the propagation of the so-called chiral magnetic waves, which are driven by the anomalous chiral magnetic and chiral separation effects. This prompted intensive experimental efforts in search of signatures of such waves in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. In fact, several experiments have already reported a tentative detection of the predicted signal, albeit with a significant background contribution. Here, we critically reanalyze the theoretical foundations for the existence of the chiral magnetic waves. We find that the commonly used background-field approximation is not sufficient for treating the waves in hot chiral plasmas in the long-wavelength limit. Indeed, the back-reaction from dynamically induced electromagnetic fields turns the chiral magnetic wave into a diffusive mode. While the situation is slightly better in the strongly-coupled near-critical regime of quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions, the chiral magnetic wave is still strongly overdamped due to the effects of electrical conductivity and charge diffusion.

## Key claim

The chiral magnetic wave (CMW) is not a propagating wave but a diffusive mode in hot chiral plasmas when dynamical electromagnetism is properly accounted for, and even in the strongly-coupled near-critical regime of quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions, the CMW is strongly overdamped due to electrical conductivity and charge diffusion.

## Method

The authors performed a hydrodynamic analysis of collective modes in a chiral plasma using linearized chiral hydrodynamic equations supplemented by Maxwell equations, treating the electromagnetic fields dynamically rather than as a fixed background. They derived the dispersion relations for the CMW in the high-temperature regime with vanishing chemical potentials, then extended the analysis to the nonperturbative regime of two-flavor quark-gluon plasma using lattice results for transport coefficients (electrical conductivity, susceptibilities, and diffusion coefficients) from Aarts (2014).

## Key numbers

- The electrical conductivity in deconfined quark-gluon plasma ranges from \(c_\sigma \approx 0.111\) at \(T = 200\ \text{MeV}\) to \(c_\sigma \approx 0.316\) at \(T = 350\ \text{MeV}\), with total conductivity \(\sigma_E = c_\sigma C_{\text{em}}^\ell T\) where \(C_{\text{em}}^\ell = 5e^2/9 \approx 5.1 \times 10^{-2}\)
- The fermion number susceptibility is \(\chi_f = c_\chi \chi_f^{(SB)}\) with \(\chi_f^{(SB)} = T^2/3\), where \(c_\chi = 0.804\) at \(T = 200\ \text{MeV}\), \(0.885\) at \(T = 235\ \text{MeV}\), and \(0.871\) at \(T = 350\ \text{MeV}\)
- The diffusion coefficient is \(D_f = c_D/(2\pi T)\) with \(c_D = 0.758\) at \(T = 200\ \text{MeV}\), \(1.394\) at \(T = 235\ \text{MeV}\), and \(1.826\) at \(T = 350\ \text{MeV}\)
- The system size in heavy-ion collisions is taken between about \(12\ \text{fm}\) and \(24\ \text{fm}\), giving an infrared cutoff for wave vectors of about \(100\ \text{MeV}\) at \(R \approx 12\ \text{fm}\) and \(50\ \text{MeV}\) at \(R \approx 24\ \text{fm}\)
- Numerical results are presented for magnetic fields \(eB = (50\ \text{MeV})^2\), \((100\ \text{MeV})^2\), and \((200\ \text{MeV})^2\) at temperatures \(T = 200\ \text{MeV}\), \(235\ \text{MeV}\), and \(350\ \text{MeV}\)

## Context

This work critically reanalyzes the theoretical foundations of the chiral magnetic wave originally proposed by Kharzeev (2010), which predicted a propagating collective mode in chiral plasmas driven by the chiral magnetic effect and chiral separation effect. The authors extend their earlier analysis (Rybalka, 2018) by incorporating lattice results for transport coefficients from Aarts (2014) to study the nonperturbative regime of quark-gluon plasma. The finding that the CMW is overdamped contradicts the background-field approximation used in prior studies and suggests that experimental signatures previously attributed to the CMW in heavy-ion collisions (reported by Ke (2012), Adamczyk (2013, 2015), Adam (2015), and Sirunyan (2017)) may require reinterpretation.
