# A dynamical implementation of canonical second quantization on a quantum computer

> We develop theoretical methods for the implementation of creation and destruction operators in separate registers of a quantum computer, allowing for a transparent and dynamical creation and destruction of particle modes in second quantization in problems with variable particle number. We establish theorems for the commutation (anticommutation) relations on a finite memory bank and provide the needed symmetrizing and antisymmetrizing operators. Finally, we provide formulae in terms of these operators for unitary evolution under conventional two- and four-body Hamiltonian terms, as well as terms varying the particle number. In this formalism, the number of qubits needed to codify $n$ particles with $N_p$ modes each is of order $n\log_2 N_p$. Such scaling is more efficient than the Jordan-Wigner transformation which requires $O(N_p)$ qubits, whenever there are a modest number of particles with a large number of states available to each (and less advantageous for a large number of particles with few states available to each). And although less efficient, it is also less cumbersome than compact encoding.

## Key claim

The paper presents a theoretical framework for implementing canonical second quantization on a quantum computer using separate particle registers, where creation and annihilation operators exactly fulfill (anti)commutation relations on a finite memory bank, except for a boundary term that appears when the number of particles saturates the available qubits.

## Method

The authors assign a dedicated register of qubits to each particle, consisting of a presence/absence qubit plus qubits encoding the particle's quantum numbers (momentum, spin, color). They construct creation and annihilation operators from set and scrap operators acting on individual registers, combined with control operators and symmetrizers that enforce Bose or Fermi statistics. The formalism is developed for one-particle, two-particle, and n-particle cases, with explicit verification of the canonical commutation relations on the finite memory space.

## Key numbers

- The number of qubits needed to encode n particles with N_p modes each scales as n log₂ N_p
- The Jordan-Wigner transformation requires O(N_p) qubits for comparison
- For momentum encoding, n_p = ⌈log₂ N_p⌉ qubits are needed (or (⌈log₂ N_p⌉)³ in three dimensions)
- One qubit is required for spin encoding
- Two qubits are required for color encoding

## Context

This work extends the register-particle codification first introduced by Barata (2020) for boson theories and is similar to compact encoding by Kirby (2021). The formalism is designed for quantum field theory applications where particle number varies, such as jet broadening studies by Barata (2022, 2023), heavy quarkonia by Glazek (2017), and light-front gauge formulations. The approach is less efficient than the Jordan-Wigner transformation for systems with many particles and few states per particle, but more efficient when there are few particles with many available states, and is less cumbersome than compact encoding.
