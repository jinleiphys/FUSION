# Estimating model bias over the complete nuclide chart with sparse Gaussian processes at the example of INCL/ABLA and double-differential neutron spectra

> Predictions of nuclear models guide the design of nuclear facilities to ensure their safe and efficient operation. Because nuclear models often do not perfectly reproduce available experimental data, decisions based on their predictions may not be optimal. Awareness about systematic deviations between models and experimental data helps to alleviate this problem. This paper shows how a sparse approximation to Gaussian processes can be used to estimate the model bias over the complete nuclide chart at the example of inclusive double-differential neutron spectra for incident protons above 100\,MeV. A powerful feature of the presented approach is the ability to predict the model bias for energies, angles, and isotopes where data are missing. The number of experimental data points that can be taken into account is at least in the order of magnitude of~$10^4$ thanks to the sparse approximation. The approach is applied to the Liège Intranuclear Cascade Model (INCL) coupled to the evaporation code ABLA. The results suggest that sparse Gaussian process regression is a viable candidate to perform global and quantitative assessments of models. Limitations of a philosophical nature of this (and any other) approach are also discussed.

## Key claim

Sparse Gaussian process regression can estimate model bias over the complete nuclide chart for inclusive double-differential neutron spectra with incident protons above 100 MeV, handling at least 10^4 data points, but the approach has philosophical limitations and currently neglects systematic experimental errors by using only a diagonal experimental covariance matrix.

## Method

The author applied sparse Gaussian process regression using the approximation of Snelson (2006) to estimate the model bias of the Liege Intranuclear Cascade Model (INCL) coupled to the evaporation code ABLA07. The covariance function was designed as a sum of two product covariance functions for low and high emission energy domains, connected by a logistic transition function, operating on a five-dimensional input space of incident energy, mass number, charge number, emission angle, and emission energy. The 300 pseudo-input locations and hyperparameters were jointly adjusted via marginal likelihood maximization using the L-BFGS-B algorithm, with the regression performed on a transformed quantity of relative and absolute differences between experimental data and model predictions.

## Key numbers

* 9287 experimental data points from the EXFOR database were taken into account for the analysis
* 300 pseudo-inputs were used for the sparse approximation, giving 1500 associated parameters for optimization
* 15 hyperparameters were jointly optimized with the pseudo-input locations
* The covariance function operates in a five-dimensional input space (incident energy, mass number, charge number, emission angle, emission energy)
* Incident proton energies ranged from 318 MeV to 3000 MeV across isotopes from carbon to thorium-232
* A 10% statistical uncertainty variance was assumed for all experimental data points via the diagonal matrix B

## Context

This work extends previous applications of Gaussian processes to nuclear data evaluation, such as Pigni (2003) and Schnabel (2015, 2016), by scaling to a dataset covering the complete nuclide chart through sparse approximations. The approach enables predictions of model bias for isotopes, energies, and angles where no experimental data exist, and could potentially be combined with existing evaluation methods such as those of Muir (2007), Leeb (2008), and Herman (2008). The method is applied to INCL/ABLA because this model combination performed very well in an IAEA benchmark of spallation models and is used in transport codes such as MCNPX and GEANT4.
