# Quantum Qualifiers for Neural Network Model Selection in Hadronic Physics

> As quantum machine-learning architectures mature, a central challenge is no longer their construction, but identifying the regimes in which they offer practical advantages over classical approaches. In this work, we introduce a framework for addressing this question in data-driven hadronic physics problems by developing diagnostic tools - centered on a quantitative quantum qualifier - that guide model selection between classical and quantum deep neural networks based on intrinsic properties of the data. Using controlled classification and regression studies, we show how relative model performance follows systematic trends in complexity, noise, and dimensionality, and how these trends can be distilled into a predictive criterion. We then demonstrate the utility of this approach through an application to Compton form factor extraction from deeply virtual Compton scattering, where the quantum qualifier identifies kinematic regimes favorable to quantum models. Together, these results establish a principled framework for deploying quantum machine-learning tools in precision hadronic physics.

# Quantum Qualifiers for Neural Network Model Selection in Hadronic Physics

## Key claim

The paper introduces a "quantum qualifier" framework that can predict, based on intrinsic properties of the data, whether a quantum deep neural network (QDNN) will outperform a classical deep neural network (CDNN) for a given extraction task in hadronic physics, but the authors explicitly state this is not a proof of quantum advantage but rather a practical decision tool for model selection.

## Method

The authors constructed synthetic classification and regression datasets with controlled variations in training set size, data complexity, number of input features, and noise level, then compared CDNN and QDNN performance using classification efficiency and a regression metric M_reg. They computed five complexity and information-theoretic measures (nonlinearity, frequency complexity, fractal dimension, mutual information, and Fourier transform complexity) for each target function, performed linear regressions to quantify correlations with quantum outperformance at fixed training epochs, and constructed a weighted-sum quantum qualifier predictor. The framework was then applied to pseudodata constructed using the exact kinematics and uncertainty structure of DVCS measurements from Jefferson Lab Hall A and Hall B experiments, using helicity-independent cross sections.

## Key numbers

- QDNN classification efficiency for 1 function data with 0.2 sigma noise and 8 features: 0.8998, compared to CDNN efficiency of 0.8144
- QDNN outperformance metric Xi = 1.378 for regression on target function y = cos(4x) with 1 sigma noise trained for 50 epochs
- Sign agreement between predicted and observed quantum outperformance across kinematic plane: approximately 0.84 to 0.90 for noise levels 0.5 sigma, 1 sigma, and 2 sigma
- Observed fraction Area(Xi_DVCS > 0) increases from 0.24 at 0.5 sigma noise to 0.83 at 2 sigma noise
- Xi_DVCS reaches approximately 0.3 near t = -1.3 GeV^2 and approximately -0.6 near t = -0.2 GeV^2 for 1 sigma noise
- Exponential prefactor alpha = 0.0101 for the quantum qualifier expression
- Total of 3,885 data points from JLab DVCS experiments spanning beam energies 3.355 to 10.992 GeV, Q^2 from 1.11 to 8.51 GeV^2, and x_B from 0.126 to 0.617

## Context

This work extends prior studies on quantum machine learning advantages in classification and regression (Cong 2019, Huang 2022, Senokosov 2024, Pesah 2022, Alvarez 2025) by developing a predictive framework rather than performing post hoc comparisons. It builds on classical deep learning approaches to Compton form factor extraction from DVCS (Kumericki 2011, Moutarde 2019) and applies quantum machine learning to the inverse problem of extracting Generalized Parton Distributions from experimental data (Ji 1997, Diehl 2003, Belitsky 2002, Belitsky 2010). The framework enables principled model selection between classical and quantum architectures for precision hadronic physics, with the DVCS-specific qualifier providing experimentally interpretable decision boundaries in kinematic space that can be adapted to other observables, formalisms, and experimental settings.
