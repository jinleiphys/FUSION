# The Fate of the Initial State Fluctuations in Heavy Ion Collisions. II The Fluctuations and Sounds

> Heavy ion collisions at RHIC are well described by the (nearly ideal) hydrodynamics for average events. In the present paper we study initial state fluctuations appearing on event-by-event basis, and the propagation of perturbations induced by them. We found that (i) fluctuations of several lowest harmonics have comparable magnitudes, (ii) that at least all odd harmonics are correlated in phase, (iii) thus indicating the local nature of fluctuations. We argue that such local perturbation should be the source of the "Tiny Bang", a pulse of sound propagating from it. We identify its two fundamental scales as (i) the "sound horizon" (analogous to the absolute ruler in cosmic microwave background and galaxy distribution) and (ii) the "viscous horizon", separating damped and undamped harmonics. We then qualitatively describe how one can determine them from the data, and thus determine two fundamental parameters of the matter, the (average) {\em speed of sound} and {\em viscosity}.

## Key claim

The paper argues that initial-state fluctuations in heavy ion collisions should be understood as local perturbations that generate propagating sound waves ("Tiny Bangs"), rather than as independent noise in different angular harmonics; the authors claim that at least all odd harmonics are correlated in phase, indicating the local nature of fluctuations, and that two fundamental scales, the sound horizon and the viscous horizon, can be extracted from data to determine the speed of sound and viscosity.

## Method

The authors use a Glauber model with fluctuating nucleon-nucleon cross sections (variance w_NN ≈ 0.25) to simulate an ensemble of heavy ion collisions, calculating participant anisotropies ε_n and their angles ψ_n for harmonics n=1 through 6 via Fourier expansion of the single-particle distribution in the transverse plane. They then argue that these initial perturbations propagate as sound waves on top of the expanding fireball, and derive the viscous damping of Fourier modes using the dispersion relation ω = c_s k − (i/2)(4η/3s)(k²/T), leading to a viscous horizon scale k_v that separates damped from undamped harmonics.

## Key numbers

- The variance of the fluctuating NN cross section is w_NN ≈ 0.25.
- For AuAu collisions at full RHIC energy, the microscopic scale l changes from about 0.5 fm to about 1 fm from initial to hadronization time.
- The large parameter R/l = O(10) in the region where hydrodynamics is used.
- The viscous horizon scale is estimated as k_v ≈ 200 MeV, corresponding to R_v ≈ 2π/k_v ≈ 2 fm, using η/s = 1/4π, T ≈ 200 MeV, τ_f ≈ 10 fm/c.
- Representative scales for central AuAu collisions at RHIC are R ≈ 8 fm, H_s ≈ 4 fm, R_v ≈ 2 fm, l ≈ 0.3 fm.
- The number of participant nucleons N_p is O(100), limited from above by 2A ≈ 400.
- The total hadron multiplicity is N_hadrons ≈ 10⁴ for RHIC and LHC.
- For near-central collisions (N_part > 300), the elliptic deformation is no longer dominant and is also due to fluctuations.
- All deformations except ε₁ range from O(1/10) for central collisions to 0.3–0.5 for most peripheral ones.
- The sound horizon angle is Δφ ≈ 2H_s/R, and the angular width is δφ = R_v/R, so harmonics larger than m > R/R_v are dissipated.

## Context

This work extends the study of sound propagation in heavy ion collisions from Casalderrey-Solana and Shuryak (2005) and Shuryak (2009), which modeled the fireball expansion with a Big-Bang-like metric and found that sound propagation should produce characteristic two-peak events. It also relates to the event-by-event hydrodynamical studies by the Brazilian group (Takahashi 2009, Andrade 2009) and the triangular flow work by Alver and Roland (2010), but differs by treating fluctuations as local perturbations rather than independent harmonic noise, and by including viscosity as a key phenomenon. The paper enables experimental determination of the speed of sound and viscosity by measuring the sound horizon and viscous horizon scales from angular correlations and harmonic amplitudes as functions of centrality and system size.
