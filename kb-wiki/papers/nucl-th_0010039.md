# On the free volume in nuclear multifragmentation

> In many statistical multifragmentation models the volume available to the $N$ nonoverlapping fragments forming a given partition is a basic ingredient serving to the simplification of the density of states formula. One therefore needs accurate techniques for calculating this quantity. While the direct Monte-Carlo procedure consisting of randomly generating the fragments into the freeze-out volume and counting the events with no overlapped fragments is numerically affordable only for partitions with small $N$, the present paper proposes a Metropolis - type simulation which allows accurate evaluations of the free volume even for cases with large $N$. This procedure is used for calculating the available volume for various situations. Though globally this quantity has an exponential dependence on $N$, variations of orders of magnitude for partitions with the same $N$ may be identified. A parametrization based on the virial approximation adjusted with a calibration function, describing very well the variations of the free volume for different partitions having the same $N$ is proposed. This parametrization was successfully tested within the microcanonical multifragmentation model from [Al. H. Raduta and Ad. R. Raduta, Phys. Rev. C {\bf 55}, 1344 (1997); {\it ibid.}, {\bf 56}, 2059 (1997)]. Finally, it is proven that parametrizations of the free volume solely dependent on $N$ are rather inadequate for multifragmentation studies producing important deviations from the exact results.

# On the free volume in nuclear multifragmentation

## Key claim
The paper proposes a Metropolis-type simulation that allows accurate evaluations of the free volume (the volume available to nonoverlapping fragments in a freeze-out volume) even for partitions with large numbers of fragments, whereas direct Monte Carlo methods become numerically prohibitive for large N. The author demonstrates that parametrizations of the free volume depending solely on the number of fragments N are inadequate for multifragmentation studies, because variations of orders of magnitude exist for partitions with the same N but different fragment size distributions.

## Method
The author develops a Metropolis-type simulation that evaluates the free volume ratio chi = Omega_eff / Omega^N by computing recurrence factors f_k for each successive fragment added to the system, where f_k is the average probability that the k-th fragment does not overlap any of the previous k-1 fragments. The simulation generates a Markovian walk in the configuration space of k fragments, randomly repositioning one fragment at a time while enforcing non-overlap constraints, and calculates f_k as the average of the product of step functions over accepted configurations. The free volume ratio is then obtained as chi = product_{k=1}^N g_k f_k, where g_k accounts for the wall constraint. The method is tested against direct Monte Carlo for small N and then applied to calculate chi for various freeze-out volumes and fragment partitions, leading to a parametrization based on the virial approximation adjusted with a calibration function f(N) = (a N + b) / (N^c + d N^e).

## Key numbers
- For A=100, n=10 (freeze-out volume = 10 Omega_0), chi values were calculated for N from 2 to 17 using both direct Monte Carlo and the Metropolis-type simulation, with N_s = 10^4 successful events, showing results "practically identical" within statistical errors smaller than the plotted points.
- For A=200 and n=4, 6, 8, 10, 14, 20, chi was calculated for N from 2 to 30 with 5 randomly chosen partitions per N; globally log_10 chi shows linear behavior, but variations of "orders of magnitude" occur for partitions with the same N, strongly increasing with decreasing n.
- For A=300 and n=4, 6, 8, 10, the ratio ln chi / ln chi_v (where chi_v is the virial approximation) was fitted with the function f(N) = (a N + b) / (N^c + d N^e); fitting parameters are tabulated for each n (e.g., for n=4: a=2.189191, b=1.096606, c=1.042162, d=-0.934725, e=0.711251).
- For the source (70,32) at excitation energies 2.5 and 6 MeV/nucleon, mass distributions calculated with the full simulation (including fragment positioning) and with the adjusted virial parametrization showed "very good agreement" for both cases.
- The SMM parametrization (with n=3, d=1.4 fm) produced a flatter ln chi(N) curve than the exact calculations for n=6, with "important discrepancies" noted.

## Context
This work extends the statistical multifragmentation modeling approach by providing a practical method to compute the free volume for large fragment multiplicities, a quantity that enters the density of states formula in models such as those by Bondorf, Gross, Randrup, and Fai and Randrup. The direct Monte Carlo method previously used by Cole, Heuer, and Charvet (Cole) and Das Gupta (DasG) was limited to small N due to exponentially decreasing chi values. The virial approximation of Randrup, Robinson, and Sneppen (Ran) provides a simple two-body estimate but overestimates chi because it neglects higher-order interactions and wall constraints. The proposed parametrization, tested within the microcanonical multifragmentation model of Raduta and Raduta (1997), enables accurate statistical calculations without explicit fragment positioning, and the demonstration that N-only parametrizations produce significant deviations from exact results has implications for existing multifragmentation models that rely on such approximations. The work also connects to ongoing debates about the role of freeze-out volume in determining caloric curves and phase transitions, referencing studies by Gulminelli, Gross, Bondorf, and LeFevre, as well as experimental freeze-out volume estimates from the ALADIN collaboration ranging from n=2.5 to 12.5.
