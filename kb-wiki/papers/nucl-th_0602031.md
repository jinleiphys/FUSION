# The Nuclear Scissors Mode from Various Aspects

> Three methods to describe collective motion, Random Phase Approximation (RPA), Wigner Function Moments (WFM) and the Green's Function (GF) method are compared in detail and their physical content analyzed on an example of a simple model, the harmonic oscillator with quadrupole--quadrupole residual interaction. It is shown that they give identical formulae for eigenfrequencies and transition probabilities of all collective excitations of the model, including the scissors mode, which is the subject of our special attention. The exact relation between the RPA and WFM variables and the respective dynamical equations is established. The transformation of the RPA spectrum into the one of WFM is explained. The very close connection of the WFM method with the GF one is demonstrated. The normalization factor of the ``synthetic'' scissors state and its overlap with physical states are calculated analytically. The orthogonality of the spurious state to all physical states is proved rigorously. A differential equation describing the current lines of RPA modes is established and the current lines of the scissors mode analyzed as a superposition of rotational and irrotational components.

## Key claim

The three methods (Random Phase Approximation, Wigner Function Moments, and Green's Function) yield identical analytical formulae for eigenfrequencies and transition probabilities of all collective excitations in the harmonic oscillator plus quadrupole-quadrupole model, but only under the specific approximation that equilibrium deformation and mean square radius of neutrons equal those of protons (approximation A4), which decouples isoscalar and isovector systems.

## Method

The authors compare three theoretical approaches to collective nuclear motion using a solvable model: a harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian with separable quadrupole-quadrupole residual interaction. The Wigner Function Moments method integrates the TDHF equation over phase space with tensor weights to obtain dynamical equations for collective variables. The RPA equations are solved analytically for the same model using the deformed oscillator basis, separating transitions within the same major shell (ΔN=0) and between different major shells (ΔN=2). The Green's Function method is employed to derive explicit analytical expressions for current distributions of the scissors mode and isovector giant quadrupole resonance.

## Key numbers

- Scissors mode energy in the small deformation limit: \(E_{sc} \approx \sqrt{3/2}\,\hbar\omega\,\delta\)
- Scissors mode energy expressed with \(\hbar\omega = 45.2/A^{1/3}\) MeV: \(E_{sc} \approx 55.4\,\delta A^{-1/3}\) MeV, which practically coincides with the result of Lipparini and Stringari (56 δ A^{-1/3} MeV)
- Experimental value cited: \(E_{sc} \approx 66\delta A^{-1/3}\) MeV (Richter)
- B(M1)↑ transition probability estimate: \(0.042 A^{4/3}\delta\,\mu_N^2\), practically coinciding with the result of Lipparini and Stringari (0.043 A^{4/3}δ μ_N^2)
- Contribution of the spurious mode to the energy weighted sum rule is approximately 16 times larger than that of the scissors mode
- For α = -2, the isovector GQR frequency squared: \(\Omega^2_{iv}=4\bar\omega^2\left(1+\frac{\delta}{3}+\sqrt{(1+\frac{\delta}{3})^2-\frac{3}{4}\delta^2}\right)\)
- For α = -2, the scissors mode frequency squared: \(\Omega^2_{sc}=4\bar\omega^2\left(1+\frac{\delta}{3}-\sqrt{(1+\frac{\delta}{3})^2-\frac{3}{4}\delta^2}\right)\)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the scissors mode in the HO+QQ model from Balbutsev and Schuck (2005) by establishing the exact equivalence between RPA, Wigner Function Moments, and Green's Function methods for this solvable system. The WFM results for energies were previously shown to be identical to those of Hamamoto and Nazarewicz (1994) using RPA, and the present paper demonstrates that transition probability formulas are also identical. The analytical expressions for current distributions enable direct comparison of the rotational and irrotational components of the scissors mode, while the explicit demonstration that omitting the Fermi surface deformation variable (P21) yields zero energy for the scissors mode confirms the conclusion of Bertsch (1974) that nuclear elasticity is the sole origin of the restoring force, consistent with the classical result of Lipparini and Stringari (1989) and Zawischa and Speth (1990) that the scissors mode is a zero energy mode without Fermi surface deformation.
