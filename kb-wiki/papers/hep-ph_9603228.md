# How Can Strange Quark Matter Occur Deeply in the Atmosphere?

> Motivated by some recent cosmic ray experiments we study the properties of strange quark matter near flavour equilibrium. Using Fermi-gas model we argue that, contrary to some claims, the geometrical radii of quark matter strangelets are not smaller but rather comparable to those of ordinary nuclei. We propose therefore a scenario of propagation of strangelets through the atmosphere which still allows for their deep penetration.

# How Can Strange Quark Matter Occur Deeply in the Atmosphere?

## Key claim

Contrary to earlier claims that strangelets must have much smaller geometrical radii than ordinary nuclei to explain deep atmospheric penetration, the authors argue that strangelet radii are comparable to those of ordinary nuclei (within 10-30%). They propose instead that deeply-penetrating strangelets are fragments of much heavier initial strangelets that lose mass through successive collisions with air nuclei until they reach a critical size near A = 300-400.

## Method

The authors model strange quark matter as a Fermi gas of up, down, and strange quarks confined in a spherical volume, calculating the rescaled radius r0 from the number density using thermodynamic potentials with QCD O(αc) corrections renormalized at mN/3 = 313 MeV. They then develop a scenario where an incoming strangelet of initial mass A0 loses mass Aair in each collision with an air nucleus, propagating until its mass reaches a critical value Acrit where neutron evaporation begins.

## Key numbers

- For commonly accepted SQM parameters (m ≈ 150 MeV, μ ≈ 300 MeV), strangelet radii are comparable to ordinary nuclear matter, with difference not exceeding 10-20%
- QCD corrections lead to slight increase of r0 not exceeding 30%
- The interaction mean free path for strangelets in atmosphere is estimated as λS = 124 g/cm² for A = 100, decreasing to λS = 59 g/cm² for A = 1000
- For comparison, proton mean free path is λproton = 60-70 g/cm², while normal nucleus mean free path is λnucleus ≈ 3.8 g/cm² for A = 100
- Critical mass number for strangelet stability against neutron emission is Acrit = 300 to 400
- Energy per baryon in strange matter is ε = 919 MeV
- Number densities corresponding to nuclear matter are n = (110 MeV)³
- Strangelet starts emitting neutrons for A ≤ 320, where dE/dA exceeds mN
- Observed candidate strangelets have A = 350 and 450 (Saito event), A = 460 (Exotic Track), and A = 1000 (Price event)
- Strangelets observed at depth 200 g/cm² would originate from A0 = 900 at top of atmosphere; Centauro events at mountain altitudes would require A0 = 1800

## Context

This work addresses the puzzle of deep atmospheric penetration by strangelets, motivated by cosmic ray experiments including the Exotic Track event (Ichimura, 1993), Price's event (Price, 1978), and Saito's anomalous events (Saito, 1990). It contradicts the interpretation of JACEE (1995) and Capdevielle (1995) that strangelets must have much smaller geometrical cross sections than ordinary nuclei. The proposed fragmentation scenario enables future searches for very heavy strangelets (A0 ~ 900-1800) entering the atmosphere, and suggests that the observed mass numbers near Acrit = 300-400 are not coincidental but reflect the threshold where strangelets become detectable before decaying by neutron evaporation.
