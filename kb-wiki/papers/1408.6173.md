# Level densities and thermodynamical properties of Pt and Au isotopes

> The nuclear level densities of $^{194-196}$Pt and $^{197,198}$Au below the neutron separation energy have been measured using transfer and scattering reactions. All the level density distributions follow the constant-temperature description. Each group of isotopes is characterized by the same temperature above the energy threshold corresponding to the breaking of the first Cooper pair. A constant entropy excess $ΔS=1.9$ and $1.1$ $k_B$ is observed in $^{195}$Pt and $^{198}$Au with respect to $^{196}$Pt and $^{197}$Au, respectively, giving information on the available single-particle level space for the last unpaired valence neutron. The breaking of nucleon Cooper pairs is revealed by sequential peaks in the microcanonical caloric curve.

## Key claim

The level densities of $^{194-196}$Pt and $^{197,198}$Au all follow a constant-temperature description, with each isotopic group characterized by the same temperature above the energy threshold for breaking the first Cooper pair, but the constant-temperature formula best represents the data only above approximately 2 MeV excitation energy, and the microscopic HFB-plus-combinatorial model predicts a concave curvature not seen in the experimental data.

## Method

The Oslo method was applied to particle-$\gamma$ coincidence data from the $^{195}$Pt($d,p$), $^{195}$Pt($p,p'$), $^{195}$Pt($p,d$), $^{197}$Au($d,p$), and $^{197}$Au($^{3}$He,$\alpha$) reactions, using the SiRi particle detector system and the CACTUS NaI gamma-ray detector array at the Oslo Cyclotron Laboratory. First-generation gamma-ray spectra were extracted via an iterative subtraction technique, and the nuclear level density and gamma-ray transmission coefficient were simultaneously fitted using the factorization $P(E,E_{\gamma}) \propto \rho(E-E_{\gamma})\mathcal{T}(E_{\gamma})$. The resulting relative level densities were normalized to known discrete levels at low excitation energy and to the total level density at the neutron separation energy calculated from measured s-wave neutron resonance spacings $D_0$.

## Key numbers

- $^{195}$Pt: neutron separation energy $S_n = 6.105$ MeV, s-wave resonance spacing $D_0 = 71.8(29)$ eV, total level density at $S_n$ $\rho(S_n) = 69(14) \times 10^4$ MeV$^{-1}$, constant temperature $T_{CT} = 0.63$ MeV
- $^{196}$Pt: $S_n = 7.922$ MeV, $D_0 = 15.93(41)$ eV, $\rho(S_n) = 165(32) \times 10^4$ MeV$^{-1}$, $T_{CT} = 0.63$ MeV
- $^{194}$Pt: $S_n = 8.357$ MeV, $\rho(S_n) = 512(154) \times 10^4$ MeV$^{-1}$ (estimated from systematics), $T_{CT} = 0.63$ MeV
- $^{197}$Au: $S_n = 8.072$ MeV, $\rho(S_n) = 400(120) \times 10^4$ MeV$^{-1}$ (estimated from systematics), $T_{CT} = 0.68$ MeV
- $^{198}$Au: $S_n = 6.512$ MeV, $D_0 = 15.5(8)$ eV, $\rho(S_n) = 91(17) \times 10^4$ MeV$^{-1}$, $T_{CT} = 0.67$ MeV
- Entropy excess $\Delta S = 1.9$ $k_B$ for $^{195}$Pt relative to $^{196}$Pt, and $\Delta S = 1.1$ $k_B$ for $^{198}$Au relative to $^{197}$Au, above 1.8 MeV excitation energy
- Shift along excitation energy axis between $^{195,196}$Pt pair: 1.25 MeV; between $^{197,198}$Au pair: 0.54 MeV
- Neutron pairing gap $\Delta_n = 1.02$ MeV ($^{195}$Pt), 0.97 MeV ($^{196}$Pt), 0.75 MeV ($^{197}$Au), 0.66 MeV ($^{198}$Au)
- Proton pairing gap $\Delta_p = 0.76$ MeV ($^{195}$Pt), 1.04 MeV ($^{196}$Pt), 0.94 MeV ($^{197}$Au), 0.66 MeV ($^{198}$Au)

## Context

This work extends the constant-temperature description of nuclear level densities, previously established for well-deformed rare-earth and actinide nuclei (Moretto, 2000s), to the transitional Pt and Au isotopes near the boundary between strongly-deformed and spherical shapes. The observation that each isotopic group shares the same temperature above the pairing threshold mirrors findings in actinides (Guttormsen, 2013) and lanthanides (Guttormsen, 2001). The entropy excess values provide quantitative information on single-particle level space for unpaired valence neutrons, and the sequential peaks in the microcanonical caloric curve reveal the successive breaking of nucleon Cooper pairs, analogous to the behavior seen in BCS theory for finite Fermi systems. The results enable improved modeling of reaction rates for nuclear astrophysics and reactor science, and provide experimental benchmarks for microscopic level-density calculations such as the HFB-plus-combinatorial approach (Goriely, 2008).
