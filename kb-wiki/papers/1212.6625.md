# Improved Calculation of Thermal Fission Energy

> Thermal fission energy is one of the basic parameters needed in the calculation of antineutrino flux for reactor neutrino experiments. It is useful to improve the precision of the thermal fission energy calculation for current and future reactor neutrino experiments, which are aimed at more precise determination of neutrino oscillation parameters. In this article, we give new values for thermal fission energies of some common thermal reactor fuel isotopes, with improvements on three aspects. One is more recent input data acquired from updated nuclear databases. the second one is a consideration of the production yields of fission fragments from both thermal and fast incident neutrons for each of the four main fuel isotopes. The last one is more carefully calculation of the average energy taken away by antineutrinos in thermal fission with the comparison of antineutrino spectrum from different models. The change in calculated antineutrino flux due to the new values of thermal fission energy is about 0.32%, and the uncertainties of the new values are about 50% smaller.

## Key claim
The paper provides new values for the thermal fission energy of four common reactor fuel isotopes (²³⁵U, ²³⁸U, ²³⁹Pu, ²⁴¹Pu) that are systematically slightly larger than prior results from Kopeikin (2004), with uncertainties reduced by about 50%, but the authors note that the incident neutron kinetic energy is excluded from their definition of fission energy used for antineutrino flux calculations.

## Method
The authors calculate the total fission energy \(E_{total}\) using mass excess values from the ATE2003 database and fission yields from the INDC database, weighting thermal and fast neutron-induced fission yields separately for each isotope based on reactor core simulation data from the Daya Bay Nuclear Power Plant. The average antineutrino energy \(<E_\nu>\) is obtained by fitting exponential functions to antineutrino spectra from the ILL, Huber, and Mueller models (and Vogel for ²³⁸U), averaging results from different models, and summing contributions above and below 2.0 MeV. The correction for incomplete beta and gamma decay \(\Delta E_{\beta\gamma}\) is taken from Kopeikin (2004), and the neutron capture energy \(E_{nc}\) is calculated using the mean energy per capture from Kopeikin (2004) and mean fission neutron numbers from the INDC database.

## Key numbers
- Total fission energy \(E_{total}\) for ²³⁵U: 203.19 ± 0.06 MeV; for ²³⁸U: 206.32 ± 0.17 MeV; for ²³⁹Pu: 207.58 ± 0.07 MeV; for ²⁴¹Pu: 211.33 ± 0.08 MeV
- Average antineutrino energy \(<E_\nu>\) for ²³⁵U: 9.06 ± 0.13 MeV; for ²³⁸U: 10.85 ± 0.39 MeV; for ²³⁹Pu: 7.41 ± 0.18 MeV; for ²⁴¹Pu: 8.42 ± 0.12 MeV
- Final fission energy \(E_f\) (excluding incident neutron kinetic energy) for ²³⁵U: 202.36 ± 0.26 MeV; for ²³⁸U: 205.99 ± 0.52 MeV; for ²³⁹Pu: 211.12 ± 0.34 MeV; for ²⁴¹Pu: 214.26 ± 0.33 MeV
- The change in calculated antineutrino flux due to the new fission energy values is about 0.32%, with the new flux being slightly smaller
- Uncertainties of the new fission energy values are about 50% smaller than those from Kopeikin (2004)

## Context
This work extends the calculations of Kopeikin (2004) by using updated nuclear databases (ATE2003 for mass excess, INDC for fission yields and neutron numbers) and by separately weighting thermal and fast neutron-induced fission yields for ²³⁵U, ²³⁹Pu, and ²⁴¹Pu, which previous calculations had treated as purely thermal. The improved precision in fission energy values directly benefits reactor neutrino experiments such as Daya Bay, KamLAND, and future experiments aiming for more precise determinations of neutrino oscillation parameters, by reducing the systematic uncertainty in antineutrino flux predictions.
