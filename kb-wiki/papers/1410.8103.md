# Energy, contact, and density profiles of one-dimensional fermions in a harmonic trap via non-uniform lattice Monte Carlo

> We determine the ground-state energy and Tan's contact of attractively interacting few-fermion systems in a one-dimensional harmonic trap, for a range of couplings and particle numbers. Complementing those results, we show the corresponding density profiles. The calculations were performed with a new lattice Monte Carlo approach based on a non-uniform discretization of space, defined via Gauss-Hermite quadrature points and weights. This particular coordinate basis is natural for systems in harmonic traps, and can be generalized to traps of other shapes. In all cases, it yields a position-dependent coupling and a corresponding non-uniform Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation. The resulting path integral is performed with hybrid Monte Carlo as a proof of principle for calculations at finite temperature and in higher dimensions. We present results for N=4,...,20 particles (although the method can be extended beyond that) to cover the range from few- to many-particle systems. This method is also exact up to statistical and systematic uncertainties, which we account for -- and thus also represents the first ab initio calculation of this system, providing a benchmark for other methods and a prediction for ultracold-atom experiments.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first *ab initio* calculation of ground-state energy, Tan's contact, and density profiles for attractively interacting unpolarized spin-1/2 fermions in a one-dimensional harmonic trap, covering the transition from few- to many-body regimes (N=4 to 20 particles). The method is exact up to statistical and systematic uncertainties, which the authors account for, but the results are limited to the range of couplings studied and show increasing noise and systematic effects at stronger couplings.

## Method

The authors implement a lattice Monte Carlo approach using a non-uniform spatial discretization defined by Gauss-Hermite quadrature points and weights, which naturally accommodates the harmonic trap potential. They use a Suzuki-Trotter decomposition of the imaginary-time evolution operator, followed by a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to handle the attractive zero-range interaction, and employ hybrid Monte Carlo to sample the resulting path integral. The bare coupling constant is tuned by matching the two-body ground-state energy to the exact continuum solution, and ground-state properties are obtained via projection Monte Carlo using a Slater determinant of harmonic oscillator single-particle orbitals as the trial wavefunction.

## Key numbers

* Ground-state energy per particle computed for N = 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 16, and 20 particles, in units of hbar*omega, for a range of couplings across the 1D BEC-BCS crossover; statistical uncertainty of order 1% at weak coupling, up to 5% at strongest couplings
* Temporal discretization parameter tau = 0.05 in lattice units used for the Suzuki-Trotter decomposition
* Lattice sizes N_x = 10, 20, 40, and 80 points used to assess systematic effects; infrared cutoff E_IR = (N_x - 1)^(-1) * hbar*omega and ultraviolet cutoff E_UV = (N_x - 1) * hbar*omega
* Monte Carlo estimates obtained by averaging over 10^4 de-correlated samples of the auxiliary field
* For the two-body problem, the exact solution from Busch et al. is reproduced as a renormalization condition
* Contact per particle shows essentially no dependence on particle number for the couplings studied, in contrast to the energy which shows clear N-dependence when displayed in units of the non-interacting ground-state energy
* Density profiles shown for N = 4, 8, 12, 16, 20 particles at couplings 2*a_HO/a_0 = 0 (non-interacting), 1.67, and 5.16

## Context

This work extends previous approaches that considered the homogeneous system (solvable via Bethe ansatz, reviewed by Guan et al.) combined with local density approximation, and exact diagonalization for 2 and 3 particles by Blume and numerically for larger systems. It complements Monte Carlo work by Casula et al. that focused on large particle number and polarized systems. The results provide benchmarks for few- and many-body methods and predictions for ultracold-atom experiments in highly constrained traps, and the non-uniform lattice technique can be generalized to other trap shapes, higher dimensions, and finite temperature.
