# Current in the light-front Bethe-Salpeter formalism I: Replacement of non-wave function vertices

> We apply the light-front reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter equation to matrix elements of the electromagnetic current between bound states. Using a simple (1+1)-dimensional model to calculate form factors, we focus on two cases. In one case, the interaction is dominated by a term instantaneous in light-cone time. Here effects of higher Fock states are negligible and the form factor can be effectively expressed using non-wave function vertices and crossed interactions. If the interaction is not instantaneous, non-wave function vertices are replaced by contributions from higher Fock states. These higher Fock components arise from the covariant formalism via the energy poles of the Bethe-Salpeter vertex and the electromagnetic vertex. The replacement of non-wave function vertices in time-ordered perturbation theory is a theorem which directly extends to generalized parton distributions, e.g., in (3+1) dimensions.

## Key claim

Non-wave function vertices in the light-front Bethe-Salpeter formalism are replaced by contributions from higher Fock states in light-front time-ordered perturbation theory, provided the interaction has light-cone time dependence; this replacement is a theorem that extends to generalized parton distributions in (3+1) dimensions, but non-wave function vertices can only be used efficiently when the interaction is approximately instantaneous in light-cone time.

## Method

The authors apply the light-front reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter equation to matrix elements of the electromagnetic current between bound states, using a (1+1)-dimensional ladder model of two scalar particles exchanging a massive scalar boson. They compare two cases: one where the interaction is dominated by a term instantaneous in light-cone time, and one where the interaction is not instantaneous (the full ladder exchange). The form factor is calculated using the reduced formalism, with the bound-state vertex function and current constructed via the gauging method of Kvinikhidze and Miller (1998), working to first order in the difference between the full propagator and the auxiliary Green's function.

## Key numbers

- The Weinberg propagator is defined as \( \dw (x|M^2) = 1 / (M^2 - m^2 / [x(1-x)]) \), where \( m \) is the scalar particle mass and \( M \) is the bound state mass.
- The one-boson exchange potential in the reduced formalism is given by \( w(x,y|M^2) = (-g^2 / (x-y)) \theta[x(1-x)] \theta[y(1-y)] [ \theta(x-y) D(x,y|M^2) - \{x \leftrightarrow y\} ] \), with \( D^{-1}(x,y|M^2) = M^2 - m^2/y - \mu^2/(x-y) - m^2/(1-x) \), where \( \mu \) is the boson mass and \( g \) the coupling constant.
- In the instantaneous limit \( \mu^2 \gg m^2, M^2 \), both the full ladder and instantaneous approximation wave functions approach \( \sim \dw(x,4m^2) \).

## Context

This work extends the light-front reduction scheme of Sales et al. (1999) to current matrix elements, superseding the authors' own earlier investigation (Tiburzi and Miller, 2002). It addresses the problem of non-wave function vertices previously identified by Bakker et al. (2000) and the treatment of Z-graph contributions in light-front dynamics, with applications to generalized parton distributions as formulated by Ji (1998). The replacement theorem enables future calculations of generalized parton distributions in (3+1) dimensions without modeling non-wave function vertices explicitly.
