# On possibility of synthesizing superheavy elements in nuclear explosions

> The possibility to produce superheavy elements in the course of low-yield nuclear explosions is analyzed within a simple kinetic model which includes neutron capture, gamma-emission, fission and particle evaporation from excited nuclei. We have calculated average numbers of absorbed neutrons as well as mass distributions of U and Cm nuclei exposed to an impulsive neutron flux, as functions of its duration. It is demonstrated that detectable amounts of heavy nuclei absorbing from 20 to 60 neutrons may be produced in this process. According to an optimistic scenario, after multiple beta-decay such nuclei may reach the long-living elements of the predicted ``island of stability''.

# On possibility of synthesizing superheavy elements in nuclear explosions

## Key claim
The authors propose that low-yield nuclear explosions can produce detectable amounts of neutron-rich heavy nuclei absorbing from 20 to 60 neutrons, which after multiple beta-decay may reach the predicted "island of stability", but only under an optimistic scenario where exposure times exceed 1 microsecond and neutron densities remain high (around 4×10²² cm⁻³), and the final survival of superheavy nuclei depends on whether they can avoid spontaneous fission and alpha-decay.

## Method
The authors developed a simple kinetic model that includes neutron capture, gamma-emission, fission, and particle evaporation from excited nuclei, using Monte Carlo simulations to track the stochastic evolution of individual target nuclei (²³⁸U and ²⁴⁸Cm) embedded in the active zone of a nuclear explosion. They calculated average numbers of absorbed neutrons and mass distributions as functions of exposure time, assuming fixed neutron densities between 4×10²⁰ cm⁻³ and 4×10²² cm⁻³ and temperatures around 10 keV. For practical abundance estimates, they solved coupled rate equations for the densities of nuclei with increasing mass numbers under constant neutron flux.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron density estimated for a 100 kt explosion: 6×10²² cm⁻³, derived from 1.45×10²⁵ fissioning nuclei of ²³⁵U (total mass 5.6 kg) producing about 2×10²⁵ free neutrons in a volume of about 300 cm³.
- Neutron capture cross sections used: 1 barn for U isotopes and 2 barn for Cm isotopes, independent of neutron energy and mass number.
- U and Cm nuclei can absorb 10-15 neutrons on average during 1 microsecond at neutron density 4×10²² cm⁻³.
- With exposure time of 3 microseconds, the fraction of nuclei capturing 50 neutrons would be about 10⁻⁸.
- For a 1 gram ²⁴⁸Cm target, concentration of nuclides with A≈300 would be 10⁻¹² after 3 microseconds exposure.
- Integrated neutron flux required for observable isotopes with more than 20 absorbed neutrons: about 2×10²⁴ n/cm², corresponding to τ ≈ 0.3 microseconds at neutron density 4×10²² cm⁻³.
- Typical integrated neutron fluxes reached in low-yield nuclear explosions: 3×10²⁴ to 10²⁵ n/cm².
- For comparison: modern spallation sources supply thermal neutron densities around 10¹² cm⁻³, nuclear reactors around 10¹¹ cm⁻³.

## Context
This work extends the earlier experimental efforts by Seaborg (1960s) and collaborators, who used underground nuclear explosions (Par 1964, Barbel 1964, Tweed 1965, Vulcan 1966, Cyclamen 1966) to produce elements up to ²⁵⁷Fm via neutron capture, with up to 20 neutrons absorbed by U targets. The authors argue that with optimized exposure times and neutron densities, this method could reach the neutron drip-line and potentially produce superheavy elements near the predicted island of stability (N≈184, Z=114, 120, 126), complementing heavy-ion fusion reactions (which produce neutron-deficient nuclei) and multi-nucleon transfer processes. The work enables future experimental searches for long-lived superheavy elements in explosion debris using radiochemical and mass-spectroscopic methods, and may help understand r-process nucleosynthesis in supernova explosions.
