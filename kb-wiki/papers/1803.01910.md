# First Measurement of the Ti$(e,e^\prime){\rm X}$ Cross Section at Jefferson Lab

> To probe CP violation in the leptonic sector using GeV energy neutrino beams in current and future experiments using argon detectors, precise models of the complex underlying neutrino and antineutrino interactions are needed. The E12-14-012 experiment at Jefferson Lab Hall A was designed to perform a combined analysis of inclusive and exclusive electron scatterings on both argon ($N = 22$) and titanium ($Z = 22$) nuclei using GeV energy electron beams. The measurement on titanium nucleus provides essential information to understand the neutrino scattering on argon, large contribution to which comes from scattering off neutrons. Here we report the first experimental study of electron-titanium scattering as double differential cross section at beam energy $E=2.222$ GeV and electron scattering angle $θ= 15.541$ deg, measured over a broad range of energy transfer, spanning the kinematical regions in which quasielastic scattering and delta production are the dominant reaction mechanisms. The data provide valuable new information needed to develop accurate theoretical models of the electromagnetic and weak cross sections of these complex nuclei in the kinematic regime of interest to neutrino experiments.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first measurement of the inclusive electron-titanium double differential cross section at beam energy 2.222 GeV and scattering angle 15.541 deg, but the cross section is extracted by normalizing the titanium yield ratio to published radiatively unfolded carbon cross sections rather than by direct absolute measurement, and the titanium data cannot yet be compared to the spectral-function-based theoretical model because the titanium spectral function is not available as an input.

## Method

The E12-14-012 experiment at Jefferson Lab Hall A used the Continuous Electron Beam Accelerator Facility (CEBAF) to deliver a 2.222 GeV electron beam onto solid foil targets of natural titanium (0.729 g/cm^2) and natural carbon (0.167 g/cm^2). Scattered electrons were detected in the Left High-Resolution Spectrometer at a fixed angle of 15.541 deg, with particle identification provided by gas threshold Cerenkov counters and lead-glass calorimeters. The cross section for each energy-transfer bin was computed as the product of a Monte Carlo cross section (a fit to existing data including radiative and Coulomb corrections) times the ratio of data to simulation yields; the titanium cross section was then obtained by multiplying the measured carbon cross section by the luminosity-normalized yield ratio of titanium to carbon.

## Key numbers

* Beam energy: 2.222 GeV; electron scattering angle: 15.541 deg.
* Titanium target thickness: 0.729 +/- 0.001 g/cm^2; carbon target thickness: 0.167 +/- 0.001 g/cm^2.
* Total uncertainty on the titanium cross section: up to approximately 2.75%, composed of statistical uncertainty (1.65%) and systematic uncertainty (2.2%) summed in quadrature.
* Total uncertainty on the carbon cross section: up to approximately 2.5%, composed of statistical uncertainty (1.2%) and systematic uncertainty (2.2%) summed in quadrature.
* Fermi momentum extracted for titanium from superscaling analysis: approximately 240 MeV, compared to 220 MeV for carbon (Moniz et al.).
* Momentum transfer magnitude at the quasielastic peak: approximately 600 MeV.

## Context

This work provides the first inclusive electron-titanium cross section data in the kinematic regime relevant for GeV-energy neutrino experiments, extending the sparse existing measurements on titanium (Heisenberg 1971, Selig 1988, Romberg 1971). The titanium data are specifically designed to mirror the neutron spectral function of argon, enabling future extraction of the argon neutron spectral function via the Ti(e,e'p) reaction, which is critical for modeling neutrino-argon interactions in experiments such as DUNE and the Short-Baseline Neutrino program. The carbon data validate the spectral-function-based theoretical framework (Benhar 2005, Ankowski 2015, Rocco 2016) and provide high-precision cross sections usable by carbon-target neutrino experiments including T2K, NOvA, and MINERvA.
