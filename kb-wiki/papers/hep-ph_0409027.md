# Describing relativistic fermions with two-component field

> With a non-unitary transformation, the Lagrangian of a Dirac fermion is decomposed into two decoupled sectors. We propose to describe massive relativistic fermions in gauge theories in a two-component form. All relations between the Green's functions in this form and those in the conventional four-component form are derived. It is shown that the S-matrix elements in both forms are exactly the same. The description of the fermion in the new form simplifies significantly the gamma-matrix algebra in the four-component form. In particular, in perturbative calculations the propagator of the fermion is a scalar function. The advantages of the two-component form make it very useful in practical applications.

# Describing relativistic fermions with two-component field

## Key claim
Massive relativistic fermions in gauge theories can be described using a single two-component field rather than the conventional four-component Dirac spinor, with the claim that the S-matrix elements in both forms are exactly the same. The hedge is that this equivalence holds only after a specific non-unitary transformation is applied, and the two-component description requires a nonlocal Lagrangian similar in form to the unexpanded Heavy Quark Effective Theory (HQET) Lagrangian.

## Method
The author applies a non-unitary triangular matrix transformation to the Dirac Lagrangian, decomposing it into two decoupled sectors each described by a two-component field. Relations between the Green's functions in the two-component form and those in the conventional four-component form are derived using the path integral method, and the equivalence of S-matrix elements for both fermion scattering and fermion-antifermion annihilation processes is demonstrated by showing that the two-component wavefunctions (projected Pauli spinors) and the scalar propagator reproduce the same on-shell amplitudes as the four-component Dirac formalism.

## Key numbers
- The free fermion propagator in the two-component form is a scalar function: $\widetilde{G}(p) = i (E+m)/(p^2 - m^2 + i\epsilon)$, where $E \equiv p_0$
- The two-component wavefunction for a fermion is $\tilde{u}_s(p) = \sqrt{E+m} \;\xi_s$, where $\xi_s$ are Pauli two-component spinors
- The two-component wavefunction for an antifermion is $\tilde{v}_s(p) = (\mathbf{p}\cdot\boldsymbol{\sigma})/\sqrt{E+m} \;\xi_s$
- The propagator decomposition shows two poles at $E = \pm \omega$ where $\omega \equiv \sqrt{\mathbf{p}^2 + m^2}$

## Context
This work extends the two-component approach used in nonrelativistic contexts (Pauli's two-component theory for nonrelativistic fermions) and in effective field theories such as HQET (Isgur and Wise, 1989; Politzer and Wise, 1988; Eichten and Hill, 1990) and nonrelativistic QCD (Caswell and Lepage, 1986) to the fully relativistic massive case without approximation. The formalism enables simplification of gamma-matrix algebra in perturbative calculations (replacing 4x4 gamma matrices with 2x2 sigma matrices), offers a scalar propagator for fermions, and may be applied to bound state problems and lattice formulations of chiral fermions to address the doubling problem.
