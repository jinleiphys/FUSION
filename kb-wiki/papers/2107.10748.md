# Self-consistent $O(4)$ model spectral functions from analytically continued FRG flows

> In this paper we explore practicable ways for self-consistent calculations of spectral functions from analytically continued functional renormalization group (aFRG) flow equations. As a particularly straightforward one we propose to include parametrizations of self-energies based on explicit analytic one-loop expressions. To exemplify this scheme we calculate the spectral functions of pion and sigma meson of the $O(4)$ model at vanishing temperature in the broken phase. Comparing the results with those from previous aFRG calculations, we explicitly demonstrate how self-consistency at all momenta fixes the tight relation between particle masses and decay thresholds. In addition, the two-point functions from our new semi-analytic FRG scheme have the desired domain of holomorphy built in and can readily be studied in the entire cut-complex frequency plane, on physical as well as other Riemann sheets. This is very illustrative and allows, for example, to trace the flow of the resonance pole of the sigma meson across an unphysical sheet. In order to assess the limitations due to the underlying one-loop structure, we also introduce a fully self-consistent numerical scheme based on spectral representations with scale-dependent spectral functions. The most notable improvement of this numerically involved calculation is that it describes the three-particle resonance decay of an off-shell pion, $π^* \to σπ\to3π$. Apart from this further conceptual improvement, overall agreement with the results from the considerably simpler semi-analytic one-loop scheme is very encouraging, however. The latter can therefore provide a sound and practicable basis for self-consistent calculations of spectral functions in more realistic effective theories for warm and dense matter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that self-consistent inclusion of momentum-dependent self-energies in analytically continued functional renormalization group (aFRG) flows, using explicit one-loop parametrizations, removes the mismatch between physical pole masses and decay thresholds that plagued earlier LPA-based calculations, while a fully self-consistent numerical scheme additionally captures three-particle resonance decay contributions beyond one-loop structure.

## Method

The authors propose a semi-analytic self-consistent one-loop (SC1L) scheme where the sigma and pion two-point functions are parametrized by analytic one-loop self-energy expressions with scale-dependent coupling constants and pole masses, solved self-consistently at each RG scale. For comparison, they also implement a fully self-consistent numerical scheme using spectral representations with scale-dependent spectral functions recomputed at every integration step. Both schemes are applied to the O(4) model at zero temperature in the broken phase, using the Wetterich equation for the effective potential with the four-dimensional Litim regulator.

## Key numbers

- The pion pole mass at the infrared fixed point is approximately 135 MeV, matching the physical pion mass.
- The sigma meson pole mass is found to be around 450-500 MeV, with a width of about 250-300 MeV from the SC1L calculation.
- The three-particle decay threshold for the process pi* -> sigma pi -> 3pi is located at 3 times the pion mass, approximately 405 MeV, which is correctly reproduced only in the fully self-consistent numerical scheme.
- The energy-weighted sum rule integral from 0 to infinity of rho(omega^2) d(omega^2) equals 1, verified numerically for the SC1L spectral functions.

## Context

This work extends the earlier aFRG calculations of Kamikado (2013) and Strodthoff (2016) for the O(4) model, which suffered from a mismatch between pole masses and decay thresholds due to missing self-consistency beyond zero momentum. The new SC1L scheme resolves this inconsistency while remaining analytically tractable, and the fully self-consistent numerical scheme goes beyond one-loop structure to describe three-particle decays. The approach enables future applications to more realistic effective theories for warm and dense matter, such as QCD low-energy models at finite temperature and chemical potential.
