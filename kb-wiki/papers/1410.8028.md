# QCD in magnetic fields: from Hofstadter's butterfly to the phase diagram

> I revisit the problem of a charged particle on a two-dimensional lattice immersed in a constant (electro)magnetic field, and discuss the energy spectrum - Hofstadter's butterfly - from a new, quantum field theoretical perspective. In particular, I point out that there is an intricate interplay between a) the structure of the butterfly at low magnetic flux, b) the absence of asymptotic freedom in QED and c) the enhancement of the quark condensate by a magnetic field at zero temperature. I proceed to discuss the response of the QCD condensate to the magnetic field at nonzero temperatures in four space-time dimensions, present the resulting phase diagram and compare it to low-energy model predictions.

# QCD in magnetic fields: from Hofstadter's butterfly to the phase diagram

## Key claim

The fractal energy spectrum of a charged particle on a lattice (Hofstadter's butterfly) is a lattice artefact that disappears in the continuum limit, but the low-magnetic-flux behavior of the eigenvalues does represent continuum physics and establishes a three-way correspondence between the butterfly's wing structure, the absence of asymptotic freedom in QED, and the leading-order magnetic catalysis of the quark condensate. At nonzero temperature, however, gluonic interactions reverse this enhancement and produce inverse magnetic catalysis, causing the chiral transition temperature to decrease with increasing magnetic field.

## Method

The author diagonalized the free lattice Dirac operator (staggered fermions) and the lattice Klein-Gordon operator on a two-dimensional lattice of size N_x = N_y = 40 in a constant U(1) magnetic field, obtaining the eigenvalue spectra (Hofstadter butterflies) for both bosonic and fermionic cases. For full QCD, the author performed large-scale lattice simulations with staggered quarks at physical masses, including continuum extrapolation, to compute the quark condensate and Polyakov loop as functions of temperature and magnetic field, and compared these results to chiral perturbation theory and the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model.

## Key numbers

- The magnetic flux through an elementary plaquette is given by alpha = a^2 qB/(2 pi) = N_b/(N_x N_y), with 0 <= N_b < N_x N_y.
- The leading-order quadratic contribution to the condensate in two dimensions is Delta bar-psi-psi^(2D) = (qB)^2 * beta_1 * 4 pi / m^3 + O(B^4), where beta_1 = 1/(12 pi^2) is the one-loop QED beta-function coefficient.
- For asymptotically high quark masses, the quadratic coefficient of the condensate is proportional to N_c * beta_1 = 3/(12 pi^2); for m approaching zero, chiral perturbation theory gives the scalar QED beta-function coefficient beta_1^(scalar) = 1/(48 pi^2).
- The lattice results for the condensate at zero temperature show quantitative agreement with chiral perturbation theory and the NJL model for low magnetic fields.
- The transition temperature T_c decreases with increasing magnetic field, as shown by the inflection points of both the quark condensate and the strange quark number susceptibility.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous lattice studies (Bali et al., 2011, 2012) that first demonstrated inverse magnetic catalysis in QCD with physical quark masses, contradicting earlier model predictions from the NJL model (Schramm, 1991; Gusynin, 1995) and quenched lattice simulations (Buividovich, 2008) that found only magnetic catalysis. The results also contradict earlier dynamical simulations with larger-than-physical pion masses (D'Elia, 2010, 2011) that predicted an increasing T_c with B. The correspondence between the butterfly spectrum and the QED beta-function provides a new analytical understanding of magnetic catalysis at zero temperature, while the inverse catalysis mechanism is explained by the indirect coupling of the magnetic field to gluonic degrees of freedom, particularly the Polyakov loop, via charged sea quark loops (Bruckmann, 2013). These findings enable improved low-energy models of QCD that incorporate inverse catalysis, with ongoing work by multiple groups (Fukushima, 2012; Fraga, 2013; Farias, 2014; Ayala, 2014; Andersen, 2014) to implement the effect in various theoretical frameworks.
