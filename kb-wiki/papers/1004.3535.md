# Quantifying the nucleon's pion cloud with transverse charge densities

> The transverse densities in a fast-moving nucleon offer a model-independent framework for analyzing the spatial structure of the pion cloud and its role in current matrix elements. We calculate the chiral large-distance component of the charge density using a dispersion representation of the form factor and discuss its partonic interpretation. The non-chiral core is dominant up to surprisingly large distances ~2 fm. The chiral component can be probed in precision low-Q^2 elastic eN scattering or in peripheral deep-inelastic processes which resolve its quark/gluon content.

## Key claim
The paper quantifies the nucleon's pion cloud using transverse charge densities, finding that the non-chiral core dominates up to surprisingly large distances of about 2 fm, contrary to naive expectations that the pion cloud region begins beyond 1 fm.

## Method
The authors calculate the chiral large-distance component of the transverse charge density using a dispersion representation of the Dirac form factor, relating the large-distance behavior to singularities in the timelike region. They compute the imaginary part of the isovector form factor from two-pion cuts using leading-order relativistic chiral Lagrangian amplitudes with pointlike pions and nucleons, then transform this to transverse coordinate space via an integral over modified Bessel functions. The chiral component is compared to the non-chiral component from rho meson exchange.

## Key numbers
- The non-chiral core is numerically dominant up to distances of about 2 fm
- The chiral component's contribution to the mean square transverse radius is ⟨b²⟩_chiral = 0.08 fm², which is only 14% of the experimental value ⟨b²⟩_exp = 0.57 fm²
- The fourth moment of the chiral component is ⟨b⁴⟩_chiral = 0.3 fm⁴, approximately equal to ⟨b²⟩²_exp
- The rho meson mass used is M_V = 770 MeV
- The pion decay constant is F_π = 93 MeV
- The nucleon isovector axial coupling is g_A = 1.26

## Context
This work extends the transverse density framework of Miller (2007) to quantify the chiral component of nucleon structure, addressing a long-standing difficulty in separating the nucleon's "core" from its "pion cloud" that had previously relied on chiral perturbation theory (Bernard, 1995) or chiral soliton models (Brown, 2010). The approach enables connections between low-energy elastic electron-nucleon scattering and peripheral deep-inelastic processes that can resolve the partonic content of the pion cloud, as discussed in the context of Sullivan processes (Sullivan, 1971) and earlier work by Strikman and Weiss (2003, 2009).
