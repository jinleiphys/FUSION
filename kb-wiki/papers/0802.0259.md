# Thermalization in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We propose a model for isotropization and corresponding thermalization in a nucleon system created in the collision of two nuclei. The model is based on the assumption: during the fireball evolution, two-particle elastic and inelastic collisions give rise to the randomization of the nucleon-momentum transfer which is driven by a proper distribution. As a first approximation, we assume a homogeneous distribution where the values of the momentum transfer is bounded from above. These features have been shown to result in a smearing of the particle momenta about their initial values and, as a consequence, in their partial isotropization and thermalization. The nonequilibrium single-particle distribution function and single-particle spectrum which carry a memory about initial state of nuclei have been obtained.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that isotropization and thermalization in heavy-ion collisions can arise from the randomization of nucleon-momentum transfer after a finite number of two-particle collisions, with the system retaining memory of the initial nuclear state rather than reaching full thermodynamic equilibrium. The model assumes a homogeneous distribution of momentum transfer bounded from above as a first approximation, which yields only partial isotropization and thermalization.

## Method

The authors develop the "maximal isotropization model" (MIM), a transport-like model where the evolution of the nucleon system is parametrized by the number of effective collisions per particle rather than by time. They assume that after every three sequential elastic collisions (or fewer for inelastic collisions), the momentum transfer becomes a random quantity driven by a homogeneous distribution bounded by a maximum value \(p_{\mathrm{max}}\). The single-particle distribution function is derived by integrating over all random momentum transfers while enforcing conservation of total energy and total momentum of the \(2N\)-nucleon system after freeze-out, using a micro-canonical-like approach that is then transformed to a canonical ensemble via Laplace transformation.

## Key numbers

- The maximum allowed momentum transfer is taken as \(p_{\mathrm{max}} \sim 1\) GeV/c, which implies nucleon-nucleon reactions cannot occur in a spatial volume with size less than 0.2 fm.
- The parameter \(\alpha\) governing the Gaussian width in the distribution is defined as \(\alpha = 3/(2M p_{\mathrm{max}}^2)\) for Cartesian coordinates, where \(M\) is the effective number of collisions per particle.
- For inelastic collisions producing \(n_\pi\) pions, the number of random degrees of freedom per particle is \(d_p = 3 - 4/(2 + n_\pi)\); for \(n_\pi = 2\), this gives \(d_p = 2\).
- The effective number of collisions is determined as \(M = d_{\mathrm{tot}}/3\), where \(d_{\mathrm{tot}}\) is the total sum of random degrees of freedom gained by a particle during all reactions before freeze-out.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of thermalization in heavy-ion collisions by proposing a mechanism based on momentum-transfer randomization from sequential two-particle collisions, in contrast to approaches based on quantum chromodynamics external color fields (Dokshitzer) or the Hawking-Unruh effect (Kharzeev). The model provides a nonequilibrium "two-source" distribution function that retains memory of the initial nuclear state, which can be tested experimentally in nucleus-nucleus collisions. The approach enables future calculations of single-particle spectra and correlation functions that carry information about the initial conditions of the colliding nuclei.
