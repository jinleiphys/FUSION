# Underlying structure of collective bands and self-organization in quantum systems

> The underlying structure of low-lying collective bands of atomic nuclei is discussed from a novel perspective on the interplay between single-particle and collective degrees of freedom, by utilizing state-of-the-art configuration interaction calculations on heavy nuclei. Besides the multipole components of the nucleon-nucleon interaction that drive collective modes forming those bands, the monopole component is shown to control the resistance against such modes. The calculated structure of 154Sm corresponds to coexistence between prolate and triaxial shapes, while that of 166Er exhibits a deformed shape with a strong triaxial instability. Both findings differ from traditional views based on beta/gamma vibrations. The formation of collective bands is shown to be facilitated from a self-organization mechanism.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the monopole component of the nucleon-nucleon interaction acts as a "resistance-control force" that optimizes effective single-particle energies for specific nuclear shapes, enabling collective bands to form through a self-organization mechanism; this contradicts the traditional interpretation of beta/gamma vibrations in deformed nuclei.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale Configuration Interaction (CI) calculations using the Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) for 154Sm and 166Er, solving the many-body Schrodinger equation without any assumptions about collective modes or shapes. They used a model space of one-and-a-half harmonic oscillator shells on top of a 110Zr inert core, with the V_MU effective interaction for the proton-neutron channel and interactions from Brown for like-particle channels. They then conducted "monopole-frozen" analyses where the monopole interaction was removed and average effective single-particle energies were used as constant inputs, to isolate the monopole effect.

## Key numbers

- For 154Sm: the energy gain of the prolate minimum measured from the spherical limit is ~8 MeV in the original calculation, compared to only ~5 MeV in the monopole-frozen analysis with spherical limit, showing the ESPE optimization lowers the prolate state energy by ~3 MeV
- For 154Sm: the 0+2 state at 1.1 MeV shows T-plot circles concentrated at gamma ~20 degrees (triaxial shape), while the ground state concentrates at beta2 = 0.28 and gamma = 0 degrees (prolate shape)
- For 166Er: the B(E2; 2+2 -> 0+1) value is 5.17 (21) W.u. experimentally, reproduced by the MCSM calculation as 5.3 W.u.
- For 166Er: the quadrupole invariant analysis yields gamma = 9 degrees for electric matrix elements and gamma = 10 degrees for mass matrix elements
- For 154Sm: in the monopole-frozen analysis using average ESPEs of the 0+1 state, the 0+2 energy is raised by 50% compared to the original calculation

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo Shell Model approach previously applied to Zr, Sn, and Hg isotopes (Togashi 2016, Kremer 2016, Marsh 2018, Sels 2019) to the textbook deformed nuclei 154Sm and 166Er. The results contradict the traditional interpretation of Bohr and Mottelson (1975) that the excited bands in these nuclei correspond to beta and gamma vibrations; instead, the calculations reveal shape coexistence between prolate and triaxial minima in 154Sm and a gamma-soft deformed shape with strong triaxial instability in 166Er. The work enables a new understanding of collective bands in heavy nuclei including superheavy elements, and suggests relevance to fission processes in uranium where new local minima or widening of minima in the gamma direction may appear at larger deformations.
