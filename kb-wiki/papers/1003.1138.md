# Towards the Gravity Dual of Quarkonium in the Strongly Coupled QCD Plasma

> We build a "bottom-up" holographic model of charmonium by matching the essential spectral data. We argue that this data must include not only the masses but also the decay constants of the J/psi and psi' mesons. Relative to the "soft-wall" models for light mesons, such a matching requires two new features in the holographic potential: an overall upward shift as well as a narrow "dip" near the holographic boundary. We calculate the spectral function as well as the position of the complex singularities (quasinormal frequencies) of the retarded correlator of the charm current at finite temperatures. We further extend this analysis by showing that the residues associated with these singularities are given by the boundary derivative of the appropriately normalized quasinormal mode. We find that the "melting" of the J/psi spectral peak occurs at a temperature of about 540 MeV, or 2.8 T_c, in good agreement with lattice results.

# Towards the Gravity Dual of Quarkonium in the Strongly Coupled QCD Plasma

## Key claim

The authors build a "bottom-up" holographic model of charmonium that matches not only the masses but also the decay constants of the J/psi and psi' mesons, requiring two new features in the holographic potential: an overall upward shift and a narrow "dip" near the holographic boundary. They find that the J/psi spectral peak "melts" at a temperature of about 540 MeV, or 2.8 T_c, which is substantially higher than the 1.2 T_c predicted by earlier holographic models that only matched masses.

## Method

The authors construct a 5D holographic dual for the charm current correlator using a "bottom-up" approach, where the holographic Schrodinger potential is built piecewise to match four experimental inputs: the masses and decay constants of both J/psi and psi'. The potential consists of a conformal term (3/4z^2) for small z, a shifted soft-wall term ((a^2 z)^2 + c^2) for large z, and a negative Dirac delta function at an intermediate position z_d to enhance the decay constants. At finite temperature, they introduce an AdS-Schwarzschild black hole metric with a horizon at z_h = (pi T)^{-1}, keeping the zero-temperature background B(z) unchanged, and solve the resulting equation of motion to extract the retarded correlator and spectral function.

## Key numbers

- J/psi mass: 3096 MeV (used as input, matched to experiment)
- psi' mass: 3685 MeV (used as input, matched to experiment)
- J/psi decay constant: 416 MeV (experimental value, matched by the model)
- psi' decay constant: 296 MeV (experimental value, matched by the model)
- Model parameters: a = 0.970 GeV, c = 2.781 GeV, alpha = 1.876 GeV, z_d = 2.211 GeV
- J/psi "melting" temperature: approximately 540 MeV, or 2.8 T_c (where T_c is approximately 190 MeV)
- In contrast, the scaled soft-wall model of Fujita (2009) predicts melting at approximately 1.2 T_c
- The shifted soft-wall potential (without the dip) underpredicts f_{J/psi} to 145 MeV and f_{psi'} to 173 MeV, compared to experimental values of 416 MeV and 296 MeV respectively
- The scaled soft-wall model (as in Fujita 2009) gives f_{J/psi} = 348 MeV and f_{psi'} = 348 MeV

## Context

This work extends the "bottom-up" holographic approach of Erlich (2005), Da Rold (2005), and Karch (2006) for light mesons to the charmonium system, correcting a drawback of the earlier holographic model by Fujita (2009) which scaled the soft-wall model to match only the J/psi mass but predicted melting at too low a temperature (1.2 T_c). The authors also contrast their results with non-relativistic thermal potential models (Mocsy 2007) that typically predict charmonium dissociation at 1.2-1.5 T_c, which is lower than lattice results. The improved agreement with lattice studies (which find the J/psi peak prominent up to nearly 2.5 T_c) suggests that matching both masses and decay constants is essential for correct finite-temperature predictions. This work enables more quantitative holographic studies of heavy quarkonium suppression in the quark-gluon plasma and provides a framework for incorporating operator product expansion constraints beyond leading order in future work.
