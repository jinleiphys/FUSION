# On the distinct observation of the moving triangle singularity

> It is shown that nonzero orbital momentum in the vertex of secondary interaction in the triangle graph leads to a more clear picture corresponding to the moving complex singularity compared with the case of constant vertex. The peak in the distribution of the final particles invariant mass becomes narrower and its shift with momentum transfer more distinct. It opens the possibility to observe this picture experimentally.

## Key claim

Nonzero orbital momentum in the secondary interaction vertex of the triangle graph produces a narrower peak in the invariant mass distribution of final particles and a more distinct shift with momentum transfer, making the moving triangle singularity experimentally observable, whereas with constant (s-wave) vertices the picture is too smooth and difficult to detect.

## Method

The author derived analytic expressions for the triangle graph amplitude in coordinate space, incorporating arbitrary orbital momenta in both the nuclear vertex (A → 1+3) and the secondary interaction vertex. Numerical calculations were performed using deuteron (Paris potential and Gaussian) wave functions for s-wave nuclear vertices and Gaussian and quasi-Hulthen p-wave functions for nuclear p-wave cases, with the lower vertex amplitude taken proportional to k^L for s-wave (L=0) and p-wave (L=1) cases.

## Key numbers

- For the deuteron with Paris wave function and p-wave lower vertex, the maximum of |M|^2 moves systematically to larger values of ξ with increasing λ, producing a distinctive pattern (Fig. 2b), unlike the s-wave lower vertex case (Fig. 2a).
- For a p-wave Gaussian nuclear function with p₀ = 0.75 κ (corresponding to ¹²C), the p-wave lower vertex again gives a more distinct moving peak pattern than the s-wave case (Fig. 3).
- The widths of maxima in M distributions for processes with intermediate Δ-isobar are of the order of 100 to 200 MeV, which are too broad for clear observation.
- The dimensionless variables ξ and λ are defined in terms of the invariant mass M and momentum transfer q, with the triangle graph having a square root branching point at ξ = 0 and logarithmic singularities at ξ = λ − 1 ± i√λ.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of the triangle mechanism, previously studied by Kolybasov (1997) and others, by showing that nonzero orbital momenta in the secondary interaction vertex dramatically improve the observability of the moving triangle singularity. Prior attempts to identify the triangle mechanism experimentally were limited to a few cases such as the peak in pion spectrum from K⁻d → pΛπ⁻ (with NΣ intermediate state) and anomalies in photonuclear interactions. The results enable experimental identification of the triangle mechanism in reactions where p-wave amplitudes dominate, such as antinucleon-nucleon interactions at low energies (where p-wave is greatly enhanced) and pion-nucleon interactions (dominated by p-wave from small energies).
