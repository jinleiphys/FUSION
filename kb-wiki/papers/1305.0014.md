# Framework for maximum likelihood analysis of neutron beta decay observables to resolve the limits of the V-A law

> We assess the ability of future neutron beta decay measurements of up to O(10^{-4}) precision to falsify the standard model, particularly the V-A law, and to identify the dynamics beyond it. To do this, we employ a maximum likelihood statistical framework which incorporates both experimental and theoretical uncertainties. Using illustrative combined global fits to Monte Carlo pseudodata, we also quantify the importance of experimental measurements of the energy dependence of the angular correlation coefficients as input to such efforts, and we determine the precision to which ill-known "second-class" hadronic matrix elements must be determined in order to exact such tests.

## Key claim

The paper assesses whether future neutron beta decay measurements reaching O(10^{-4}) precision can falsify the standard model's V-A law, but the actual test depends critically on simultaneously constraining poorly known "second-class" hadronic matrix elements (the induced scalar coupling f₃ and induced tensor coupling g₂) whose theoretical uncertainties currently limit the sensitivity of such tests.

## Method

The authors construct a maximum likelihood statistical framework (termed "nFitter") that incorporates both experimental and theoretical uncertainties, using the Rfit frequentist statistical procedure. They perform combined global fits to Monte Carlo pseudodata for the neutron lifetime τ and the energy-dependent angular correlation coefficients a(Eₑ) and A(Eₑ), including recoil-order corrections and contributions from scalar and tensor interactions beyond the standard model. The framework maps quark-level effective field theory operators to nucleon-level observables, allowing simultaneous treatment of standard model parameters (λ = g_A/g_V, weak magnetism f₂, second-class currents f₃ and g₂) and beyond-standard-model couplings (ε_S, ε_T).

## Key numbers

- The recoil expansion parameter R ≈ 1.37 × 10⁻³ characterizes the efficacy of the recoil expansion in neutron beta decay
- The CVC hypothesis for g_V is tested to 1.3 × 10⁻⁴ at 68% CL in superallowed 0⁺ → 0⁺ nuclear beta decays, with constraint mₑ f₃/M g_V = -(0.0011 ± 0.0013)
- The weak magnetism coupling f₂ is tested to approximately 6% at 68% CL from decay correlation measurements
- The second-class current g₂ is constrained to |g₂/f₂| < 0.1 at 90% CL from nuclear beta decay data, yielding |g₂| < 0.2 at 90% CL
- A QCD sum rule estimate gives g₂/g_A = -0.0152 ± 0.0053 for the nucleon sector
- The neutron lifetime relation is τ = 4908.7(1.9) s / [|V_ud|² Ξ], where Ξ = 1 + 3λ² in the absence of new physics, yielding τ = 885.6 s for V_ud = 0.97425 and λ = 1.2701
- The contribution of g₂ to the lifetime is given by C_{g_A g₂} = -6.21 × 10⁻³ (λ g₂/Ξ)
- The contribution of f₃ to the lifetime is given by C_{g_V f₃} = 7.12 × 10⁻⁴ (f₃/Ξ)

## Context

This work extends the global fitting methodology pioneered by the CKMfitter Group (Hocker, 2001; Charles, 2005) and the UTfit Collaboration (Ciuchini, 2000; Bona, 2005) from flavor physics to neutron beta decay observables. It builds upon prior global fits of Lee-Yang coefficients to beta decay observables by Severijns (2006) and Konrad (2010), but adds the capability to include theoretical uncertainties directly in the optimization and to fit the energy dependence of angular correlation coefficients rather than only phase-space-integrated values. The framework enables future experiments like PERC (Dubbers, 2007) to test the V-A law at 10⁻⁴ precision, and identifies the precision to which second-class currents must be determined (e.g., via lattice QCD calculations of g_S and g_T as in Bhattacharya, 2012) to avoid limiting such tests.
