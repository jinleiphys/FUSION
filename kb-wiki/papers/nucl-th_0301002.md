# Diquark condensation effects on hot quark star configurations

> The equation of state for quark matter is derived for a nonlocal, chiral quark model within the mean field approximation.We investigate the effects of a variation of the formfactors of the interaction on the phase diagram of quark matter. Special emphasis is on the occurrence of a diquark condensate which signals a phase transition to color superconductivity and its effects on the equation of state under the condition of beta- equilibrium and charge neutrality. We calculate the quark star configurations by solving the Tolman- Oppenheimer- Volkoff equations and obtain for the transition from a hot, normal quark matter core of a protoneutron star to a cool diquark condensed one a release of binding energy of the order of Delta M c^2 ~ 10^{53} erg. We find that this energy could not serve as an engine for explosive phenomena since the phase transition is not first order. Contrary to naive expectations the mass defect increases when for a given temperature we neglect the possibility of diquark condensation.

## Key claim

The release of binding energy from a transition to color-superconducting quark matter in a cooling protoneutron star is of order 10^53 erg, but this energy cannot serve as an engine for explosive phenomena because the phase transition is not first order; contrary to naive expectations, the mass defect is smaller when diquark condensation occurs than when it is neglected.

## Method

The authors derive the equation of state for two-flavor quark matter in beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality using a nonlocal, chiral quark model in the mean-field approximation, including both chiral and diquark gap parameters. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain static, spherically symmetric quark star configurations for different temperatures (0, 40, 60 MeV) and for different formfactors of the nonlocal interaction (Gaussian, Lorentzian, NJL-type). They compare cooling scenarios with and without diquark condensation, conserving total baryon number, to compute the mass defect between hot initial and cold final configurations.

## Key numbers

- The diquark gap reaches Δ ~ 200 MeV in the density range relevant for compact stars (μ_q ≤ 450 MeV).
- The critical temperature for the superconducting phase transition follows the BCS relation T_c ≈ 0.57 Δ(T=0, μ_q) g(μ_q).
- For the Gaussian formfactor, the mass defect in cooling with diquark condensation is ΔM(A) = 0.06 M_⊙, while without it is ΔM(B) = 0.09 M_⊙; for the Lorentzian formfactor, ΔM(A) = 0.05 M_⊙ and ΔM(B) = 0.07 M_⊙.
- The difference in mass defect between scenarios with and without diquark condensation is about 0.02 M_⊙ for initial configurations with masses of about 1.4–1.5 M_⊙.
- Maximum quark star masses are up to 1.8 M_⊙ for the Gaussian formfactor and up to 1.6 M_⊙ for the Lorentzian formfactor.
- Quark core radii with Lorentzian formfactors are up to 9 km, while for Gaussian models they extend up to 11 km.
- The electron fraction under beta-equilibrium with diquark condensation does not exceed n_e / n_total ≃ 0.01.

## Context

This work extends earlier estimates by Hong, Hsu and Sannino (2001) and Ouyed and Sannino (2001) of binding energy release from Cooper pairing in quark matter, which did not account for changes in gravitational binding energy due to the altered equation of state. The authors use a dynamical nonlocal chiral quark model, in contrast to earlier non-dynamical bag-model approaches by Lugones and Horvath (2002) and Alford and Reddy (2002) that depended on arbitrary choices of the strange quark mass, diquark gap, and bag constant. The results enable more realistic calculations of protoneutron star cooling evolution and show that general relativistic effects on the star's structure overcompensate the naive pairing-energy estimate, ruling out diquark condensation as an engine for supernovae or gamma-ray bursts.
