# Scoping of material response under DEMO neutron irradiation: comparison with fission and influence of nuclear library selection

> Predictions of material activation inventories will be a key input to virtually all aspects of the operation, safety and environmental assessment of future fusion nuclear plants. Additionally, the neutron-induced transmutation (change) of material composition (inventory) with time, and the creation and evolution of configurational damage from atomic displacements, require precise quantification because they can lead to significant changes in material properties, and thus influence reactor-component lifetime. A comprehensive scoping study has been performed to quantify the activation, transmutation (depletion and build-up) and immediate damage response under neutron irradiation for all naturally occurring elements from hydrogen to bismuth. The resulting database provides a global picture of the response of a material, covering the majority of nuclear technological space, but focussing specifically on typical conditions expected for a demonstration fusion power plant (DEMO). Results from fusion are compared against typical fission conditions for selected fusion relevant materials, demonstrating that the latter cannot be relied upon to give accurate scalable experimental predictions of material response in a future fusion reactor. Results from different nuclear data libraries are also compared, highlighting the variations and deficiencies.

## Key claim

Fission reactor irradiation cannot be relied upon to give accurate scalable predictions of material response in a future DEMO fusion reactor, because gas production rates (He and H) are significantly higher under fusion conditions for most elements, and the He-to-dpa and H-to-dpa ratios are also much larger, meaning fission experiments cannot simultaneously match the damage dose and gas production levels expected in DEMO.

## Method

The authors used the FISPACT-II inventory code with the TENDL-2014 nuclear cross section library to simulate neutron irradiation of all naturally occurring elements from hydrogen to bismuth under a typical DEMO first wall neutron spectrum (total integrated flux of 5.9×10¹⁴ n cm⁻² s⁻¹). They calculated activation, transmutation (depletion and build-up), gas production, displacements per atom (dpa) via the NRT formula, and primary knock-on atom (PKA) distributions for each element. For selected fusion-relevant elements, they compared these DEMO results against three fission spectra (PWR, HFR, and FBR) and also repeated the DEMO calculations using three other nuclear data libraries: ENDF/B-VII.1, JEFF-3.2, and JENDL-4.0.

## Key numbers

* Helium production in Fe under DEMO conditions is around 100 appm per fpy, compared to 1 appm or less per fpy in the three fission environments.
* Aluminium shows more than 400 appm He per fpy in DEMO, but less than 10 appm per fpy in fission.
* Hydrogen production in Al is almost 1500 appm per fpy in DEMO, compared to less than 50 appm per fpy in all three fission systems.
* For the row-three transition metals (Ta, W, Re), He production under DEMO is very low at about 2 appm per fpy, with no fission scenario producing above 1 appm.
* The gamma dose rate from pure elements 12 days after shutdown following a 2 fpy DEMO-FW irradiation shows that only Si is predicted to be below the 10 mSv hr⁻¹ remote recycling limit.
* For Fe, the range of He production values across the four nuclear libraries is within about 20 appm of a 130 appm average (TENDL-2014 predicts 110 appm), a variation of about 16%.
* For H production in Fe, the range across libraries is within about 30 appm of a 650 appm average, a variation of less than 5%.

## Context

This work extends earlier scoping studies of material response under fusion and fission conditions (Forrest 1998, Gilbert et al. 2014) by using the modern TENDL-2014 nuclear data library and the FISPACT-II inventory code, replacing obsolete data sets. The automated computational platform developed here enables rapid updates as nuclear data libraries and DEMO design concepts evolve. The results enable material selection studies for DEMO, highlight the need for a dedicated fusion materials test facility, and are now being routinely applied to validation and verification of new TENDL library releases.
