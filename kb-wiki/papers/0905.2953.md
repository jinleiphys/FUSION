# On Units for B(E2)'s

## Key claim
The paper clarifies that different conventions for defining B(E2) values exist in the literature, with the key distinction being whether the squared electron charge \(e^{2}\) is included inside or outside the B(E2) quantity; the numerical equivalence between conventions is maintained by adjusting the prefactor in the transition probability formula, but the notation can be confusing because authors sometimes use the same symbol for different definitions.

## Method
The author compares two formulations of the gamma-decay transition probability from a \(2_{1}^{+}\) state to the \(0_{1}^{+}\) ground state in even-even nuclei: one where B(E2) has dimensions of \(e^{2}L^{4}\) (Blatt and Weisskopf, Jackson) and one where B(E2) has dimensions of \(L^{4}\) (Heyde, Bohr and Mottelson, Lawson). Using the numerical constants \(\hbar c = 197.32\) MeV fm and \(e^{2} = 1.44\) MeV fm, the author derives the numerical prefactors for both conventions and then shows that the widely used Raman et al. tables, which quote B(E2) in units of \((e\ \text{barn})^{2}\), actually use the convention where \(e^{2}\) is outside the B(E2) (i.e., the \(w_{2}\) formula), despite listing the quantity as \(B_{1}/e^{2}\) in their lifetime formula.

## Key numbers
- \(\hbar c = 197.32\) MeV fm and \(e^{2} = 1.44\) MeV fm (numerical constants used for conversion).
- Transition probability formula \(w_{1} = 0.85 \times 10^{9} E^{5} B_{1}(E2)\) when B(E2) includes \(e^{2}\) (units of \(e^{2}\) fm\(^{4}\)).
- Transition probability formula \(w_{2} = 1.23 \times 10^{9} E^{5} B_{2}(E2)\) when B(E2) excludes \(e^{2}\) (units of fm\(^{4}\)).
- Raman et al. lifetime formula: \(\tau (1+\alpha) = 40.82 \times 10^{13} E^{-5} / (B(E2, 0\rightarrow 2))\) with units of keV, barns, and picoseconds.
- 1 barn = 100 fm\(^{2}\) and 1 keV = \(10^{-3}\) MeV (conversion factors used to verify equivalence).

## Context
This work extends the discussion of B(E2) conventions found in the compilation of Raman et al. and the earlier compilation of Stelson and Grodzins (who used cm\(^{4}\) units). The paper resolves apparent discrepancies between textbooks: Heyde (Table 4) clearly states B(E2) in units of fm\(^{4}\), while Bohr and Mottelson (eq. 3C-18) and Lawson (table 5-1, eq. 5.12) give the numerical coefficient 1.23 but actually use the 0.85 convention, creating potential confusion. This clarification enables practitioners to correctly interpret and compare B(E2) values across different sources and to properly convert between lifetime measurements and transition strengths.
