# Why can hadronic stars convert into strange quark stars with larger radii

> The total binding energy of compact stars is the sum of the gravitational binding energy $(BE)_g$ and the nuclear binding energy $(BE)_n$, the last being related to the microphysics of the interactions. While the first is positive (binding) both for hadronic stars and for strange quark stars, the second is large and negative for hadronic stars (anti-binding) and either small and negative (anti-binding) or positive (binding) for strange quark stars. A hadronic star can convert into a strange quark star with a larger radius because the consequent reduction of $(BE)_g$ is over-compensated by the large increase in $(BE)_n$. Thus, the total binding energy increases due to the conversion and the process is exothermic. Depending on the equations of state of hadronic matter and quark matter and on the baryonic mass of the star, the contrary is obviously also possible, namely the conversion of hadronic stars into strange quark stars having smaller radii, a situation more often discussed in the literature. We provide a condition that is sufficient and in most of the phenomenologically relevant cases also necessary in order to form strange quark stars with larger radii while satisfying the exothermicity request. Finally, we compare the two schemes in which quark stars are produced (one having large quark stars and the other having small quark stars) among themselves and with the third-family scenario and we discuss how present and future data can discriminate among them.

## Key claim

A hadronic star can convert into a strange quark star with a larger radius while the process remains exothermic, because the reduction in gravitational binding energy from the larger radius is over-compensated by a large increase in nuclear binding energy. This is possible only for strange quark stars (self-bound objects) and not for hybrid stars with a crust, and requires that the baryonic mass of the progenitor exceeds the crossing point of the mass-baryonic mass relations and that the crossing point of the mass-radius relations satisfies a specific inequality.

## Method

The authors decompose the total binding energy of compact stars into gravitational binding energy (BE)_g and nuclear binding energy (BE)_n, where (BE)_g is positive for both hadronic stars and strange quark stars, while (BE)_n is large and negative for hadronic stars but can be positive or small and negative for strange quark stars. They compare the M_g-R and M_b-R relations for hadronic stars (using the SFHo-HD equation of state containing hyperons and deltas, and the SFHo equation of state with only nucleons) and for strange quark stars (using a constant speed-of-sound equation of state with two parameter sets, QS1 and QS2), identifying intersection points C (from M_b-R curves) and D (from M_g-R curves) to determine whether conversion to a larger-radius quark star is exothermic.

## Key numbers

- For the SFHo-HD hadronic equation of state combined with QS1 or QS2, the crossing point C corresponds to a baryonic mass M_C ~ 1.6 M_sun, and the gravitational mass of the corresponding hadronic star is ~ 1.4 M_sun.
- For the SFHo hadronic equation of state (nucleons only), M_C ~ 2.2 M_sun and the gravitational mass of the corresponding hadronic star is ~ (1.9-2.0) M_sun.
- For QS1: e_0/n_0 = 860 MeV, n_0 = 0.27 fm^-3; for QS2: e_0/n_0 = 930 MeV, n_0 = 0.25 fm^-3.
- For the SFHo-HD case with QS1: R_C = 10.73 km, R_D = 10.86 km; with QS2: R_C = 10.82 km, R_D = 10.85 km.
- For the SFHo case with QS1: R_C = 11.33 km, R_D = 11.33 km; with QS2: R_C = 11.35 km, R_D = 11.32 km.
- The thin outer crust of a quark star contributes at most ~ 10^-5 M_sun and a few hundred meters to the radius.

## Context

This work extends the two-families scenario proposed by Drago (2013), where massive compact stars are interpreted as strange quark stars and less massive ones as hadronic stars containing hyperons and deltas, contrasting with the third-family scenario of hybrid stars (Alford 2015) which requires the speed of sound in quark matter to be near the speed of light to produce small radii. The analysis provides a criterion (R_C < R_D) for determining when exothermic conversion to larger-radius quark stars is possible, which enables discrimination between the two-families scenario with large quark stars, the two-families scenario with small quark stars (Bhattacharyya 2017), and the third-family scenario (Schertler 2000, Benic 2014) using future observations of masses, radii, tidal deformabilities, and merger signals.
