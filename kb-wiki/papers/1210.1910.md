# Structure of Quark Stars

> This paper gives an brief overview of the structure of hypothetical strange quarks stars (quark stars, for short), which are made of absolutely stable 3-flavor strange quark matter. Such objects can be either bare or enveloped in thin nuclear crusts, which consist of heavy ions immersed in an electron gas. In contrast to neutron stars, the structure of quark stars is determined by two (rather than one) parameters, the central star density and the density at the base of the crust. If bare, quark stars possess ultra-high electric fields on the order of 10^{18} to 10^{19} V/cm. These features render the properties of quark stars more multifaceted than those of neutron stars and may allow one to observationally distinguish quark stars from neutron stars.

## Key claim

Quark stars, if they exist, are more multifaceted than neutron stars because their structure depends on two parameters (central density and crust-base density) rather than one, and bare quark stars possess ultra-high electric fields on the order of 10^18 to 10^19 V/cm, which may allow observational distinction from neutron stars.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical structure of hypothetical strange quark stars made of absolutely stable 3-flavor strange quark matter, comparing bare stars (no crust) with dressed stars (enveloped in thin nuclear crusts of heavy ions in an electron gas). It examines the quark-lepton composition, mass-radius relationships, rotational properties, glitch behavior, and possible connections to observed compact objects such as central compact objects (CCOs), soft gamma repeaters (SGRs), anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs), and X-ray dim isolated neutron stars (XDINs).

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of quark stars: ~2 solar masses (same as neutron stars)
- Radii of quark stars: less than or about 10-12 km
- Electric surface fields: ~10^18 to ~10^19 V/cm for bare quark stars
- Neutron drip density at crust base: ~4.3 x 10^11 g/cm^3
- Kepler (mass-shedding) period for a 1.45 solar mass quark star: 0.55 to 0.8 milliseconds
- Crustal moment of inertia ratio: 10^-5 to 10^-3 of total moment of inertia
- Observed glitch frequency changes: Delta Omega / Omega = 10^-9 to 10^-6
- Pair plasma luminosity for hot bare strange star at ~10^11 K: ~3 x 10^51 erg/s
- Electric field energy density alters quark star masses by ~15% and radii by ~5%
- CCOs may be quark stars with radii less than ~3 km

## Context

This review extends the strange matter hypothesis originally proposed by Bodmer (1971), Terazawa (1979), and Witten (1984), and builds on prior work by Alcock and Farhi (1986), Alcock and Olinto (1988), and Weber (1999, 2005). It enables observational tests to distinguish quark stars from neutron stars using rapid rotation, glitch behavior, ultra-high electric fields, and thermal evolution of compact objects like CCOs, SGRs, AXPs, and XDINs.
