# Structure of hadron resonances with a nearby zero of the amplitude

> We discuss the relation between the analytic structure of the scattering amplitude and the origin of an eigenstate represented by a pole of the amplitude.If the eigenstate is not dynamically generated by the interaction in the channel of interest, the residue of the pole vanishes in the zero coupling limit. Based on the topological nature of the phase of the scattering amplitude, we show that the pole must encounter with the Castillejo-Dalitz-Dyson (CDD) zero in this limit. It is concluded that the dynamical component of the eigenstate is small if a CDD zero exists near the eigenstate pole. We show that the line shape of the resonance is distorted from the Breit-Wigner form as an observable consequence of the nearby CDD zero. Finally, studying the positions of poles and CDD zeros of the KbarN-piSigma amplitude, we discuss the origin of the eigenstates in the Lambda(1405) region.

## Key claim

The dynamical origin of an eigenstate (whether it is generated by the interaction in a given channel or by a hidden channel) can be determined by the distance between the eigenstate pole and a Castillejo-Dalitz-Dyson (CDD) zero in the same Riemann sheet: if a CDD zero lies near the pole, the eigenstate is not dynamically generated in that channel, whereas the absence of a nearby CDD zero indicates the state originates in that channel's dynamics.

## Method

The authors analyze the analytic structure of the partial-wave scattering amplitude by considering the zero coupling limit (ZCL), where off-diagonal couplings between channels are switched off while diagonal interactions are kept fixed. They use the argument principle to show that a pole which decouples from a channel amplitude in the ZCL must be accompanied by a CDD zero that annihilates with it, establishing a topological invariant. The method is applied to the coupled-channel \(\bar{K}N\)-\(\pi\Sigma\) system using the effective Tomozawa-Weinberg (ETW) model of Ikeda (2012) to study the \(\Lambda(1405)\) resonance.

## Key numbers

- Low-mass pole of \(\Lambda(1405)\): \(W^{\mathrm{Low}}_\mathrm{pole} = 1375 - 65i\ \mathrm{MeV}\); high-mass pole: \(W^{\mathrm{High}}_{\mathrm{pole}} = 1423 - 22i\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (from ETW model with isospin-symmetric hadron masses).
- CDD zero in the \(\bar{K}N\) amplitude: \(W_{\mathrm{CDD}}^{\mathrm{Low}} = 1381 - 108i\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (near the low-mass pole).
- CDD zero in the \(\pi\Sigma\) amplitude: \(W_{\mathrm{CDD}}^{\mathrm{High}} = 1428 - 0i\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (near the high-mass pole).
- In the ZCL, the high-mass pole moves to \(W = 1415\ \mathrm{MeV}\) on the real axis; the low-mass pole moves to \(W = 1367 - 91i\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- Model parameters for the \(N\Xi\)-\(\Lambda\Lambda\) example: \(v_{0,11} = -8.93\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}\), \(v_{0,12}=1.20\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}\), \(v_{0,22}=-3.53\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{MeV}^{-2}\), \(\Lambda=300\ \mathrm{MeV}\); resulting pole at \(E_{\mathrm{pole}}=-5-1i\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (relative to \(N\Xi\) threshold) and CDD zero at \(E_{\mathrm{CDD}}=-4\ \mathrm{MeV}\) in the \(\Lambda\Lambda\) amplitude.

## Context

This work extends the pole counting method of Morgan (1990, 1992) by using CDD zeros (originally introduced by Castillejo, Dalitz, and Dyson in 1955) rather than shadow poles to determine the origin of resonances, offering the advantage that CDD zeros lie in the same Riemann sheet as the physical pole and can sometimes be observed directly in phase shift data (e.g., \(\delta = \pi\) in \(\pi\pi(I=0)\) scattering near the \(\bar{K}K\) threshold, as noted by Pelaez in 2015). The method is complementary to Weinberg's compositeness approach (1965) and its extensions by Baru (2003) and Hyodo (2011), which give quantitative but model-dependent and complex-valued results for unstable states. The application to \(\Lambda(1405)\) confirms the two-pole picture of Jido (2003) and the dominance of \(\bar{K}N\) (\(\pi\Sigma\)) dynamics in the high-mass (low-mass) pole, consistent with the ZCL analysis of Cieply (2016).
