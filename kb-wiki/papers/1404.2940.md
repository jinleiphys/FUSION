# Relativistic corrections to the central force problem in a generalized potential approach

> We present a novel technique to obtain relativistic corrections to the central force problem in the Lagrangian formulation, using a generalized potential energy function. We derive a general expression for a generalized potential energy function for all powers of the velocity, which when made a part of the regular classical Lagrangian can reproduce the correct (relativistic) force equation. We then go on to derive the Hamiltonian and estimate the corrections to the total energy of the system up to the fourth power in $|\vec{v}|/c$. We found that our work is able to provide a more comprehensive understanding of relativistic corrections to the central force results and provides corrections to both the kinetic and potential energy of the system. We employ our methodology to calculate relativistic corrections to the circular orbit under the gravitational force and also the first-order corrections to the ground state energy of the hydrogen atom using a semi-classical approach. Our predictions in both problems give reasonable agreement with the known results. Thus we feel that this work has pedagogical value and can be used by undergraduate students to better understand the central force and the relativistic corrections to it.

## Key claim

The authors present a method to incorporate relativistic corrections into the classical central force problem by using a generalized velocity-dependent potential in a non-relativistic Lagrangian framework, claiming that this approach reproduces the correct relativistic force equation up to fourth order in \(|\vec{v}|/c\) while avoiding the complexity of covariant formulations. The hedge is that the off-diagonal term in the relativistic mass tensor is neglected based on an argument that it is negligibly small for nearly circular orbits, which limits the validity of the method to systems with small eccentricity.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic force equation for a particle of rest mass \(m_0\) and expand the Lorentz factor in powers of \(|\vec{v}|/c\), neglecting the off-diagonal term of the relativistic mass tensor. They then construct a generalized potential energy function \(U(r,v)\) as a power series in even powers of the radial velocity \(v = \dot{r}\), with expansion coefficients \(h_{2n}(r)\) determined by matching the series expansion of the generalized force. The Lagrangian is taken as the non-relativistic kinetic energy minus this generalized potential, and the Hamiltonian is obtained via Legendre transformation, yielding expressions for the total energy that include corrections up to quartic order in \(|\vec{v}|/c\).

## Key numbers

* For Pluto's orbit, the off-diagonal contribution of the relativistic force is estimated to be \(\sim \mathcal{O}(10^{-17})\) smaller than the diagonal term.
* The interaction parameter \(\Gamma = k/(\mu c^2)\) for the hydrogen atom is \(\Gamma \simeq 2.81 \times 10^{-15}\) m, while the Bohr radius is \(a_0 \simeq 5.29 \times 10^{-11}\) m, giving \(\Gamma / a_0 \ll 1\).
* The non-relativistic ground state energy of the hydrogen atom is \(E^{(0)}\), and the first-order relativistic correction \(E^{(1)}_r\) is computed using expectation values of operators involving \(\hat{r}^{-3}\) and \(\hat{r}^{-5}\).
* The relativistic correction to the circular orbit radius yields \(r_* > r_0\), where \(r_0 = L^2/(\mu k)\) is the non-relativistic circular radius, and the energy of the relativistic circular orbit is given by \(E_* = E_0/\delta + \text{corrections}\), with \(\delta = \frac{1}{2}[1 + (1 + 2k^2/(L^2 c^2))^{1/2}] > 1\).

## Context

This work extends the standard non-relativistic central force problem, as treated in textbooks such as Rana and Joag (Classical Mechanics) and Griffiths (Quantum Mechanics), by incorporating relativistic corrections without using the full covariant Lagrangian or Dirac equation. The method provides a pedagogical bridge for undergraduate students to understand relativistic effects in systems like planetary orbits and the hydrogen atom, and it yields a term resembling the spin-orbit coupling term in the hydrogen atom Hamiltonian, which the authors note is a natural consequence of the generalized potential approach. The results are compared to known relativistic corrections from standard expansions of the relativistic kinetic energy, and the authors state that their predictions give "reasonable agreement" with known results, though they note that the numerical values differ because the two approaches retain different terms in their expansions.
