# Pseudo SU(3) Model and Abnormal Parity States

> The most important features of the pseudo SU(3) model are reviewed. This description of heavy deformed nuclei is based on pseudo spin symmetry, and is able to correctly predict the collective spectra and their transition amplitudes, using appropriate effective charges. It also gives a very good description of the exotic double beta decay, a hard test of any nuclear model. The simplest pseudo SU(3) assumptions describe nucleons in the abnormal parity states as having seniority zero. This idea is very useful as a mathematical simplification, and is also able to give a nice description of the backbending phenomena, but has been challenged in a recent study about effective charges and $BE2(0^+_1 \rightarrow 2^+_1)$ systematics. We carefully checked this statement and conclude that the seniority zero approach is a formal way of assign abnormal parity nucleons a passive role. The contribution of the abnormal parity nucleons to the nuclear quadrupole moments is finite but only a fraction of their asymptotic Nilsson values.

## Key claim

The pseudo SU(3) model's assumption that nucleons in abnormal parity states have seniority zero is a formal way to assign them a passive role, and while it is a useful mathematical simplification, the contribution of these abnormal parity nucleons to nuclear quadrupole moments is finite but only a fraction of their asymptotic Nilsson values, requiring renormalized effective charges.

## Method

The paper reviews the pseudo SU(3) model for heavy deformed nuclei, which maps normal parity orbitals from higher major shells onto a pseudo oscillator shell with restored SU(3) symmetry. The authors compare the pseudo SU(3) model's predictions for quadrupole moments and BE2 transition strengths with those of the Single Shell Asymptotic Nilsson Model (SSANM), analyzing the role of abnormal parity states described as seniority zero configurations versus higher seniority states. They also examine the backbending phenomenon in nuclei like 126Ba using a product space of normal and abnormal parity subspaces coupled to good angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- For 168Er, using parametrization (nos), the effective charges that reproduce experimental data are: SSANM: e_eff(pi) = 1.7e, e_eff(nu) = 0.7e; pseudo SU(3): e_eff(pi) = 2.2e, e_eff(nu) = 1.2e
- For the abnormal parity states, the quadrupole moment of the Omega = 1/2 state in the single-j approach is q_0(single j) = eta(eta+2)/(2eta+1), while in the asymptotic limit it is q_0(asym) = 2eta, which is 3-4 times greater
- The effective charges in the pseudo SU(3) model must be renormalized by nearly 25% to compensate for the lack of quadrupole moment associated with nucleons in abnormal parity states
- For deformations appropriate to well-deformed nuclei, epsilon is approximately 0.3 (with epsilon = 0.95 beta)
- For 126Ba, the backbending of the yrast band occurs between spins I=10-14

## Context

This work extends the pseudo SU(3) model developed by Ratna Raju, Draayer, and Hecht (1973) and Draayer et al. (1984), which successfully predicted collective spectra and transition amplitudes for rare earth and actinide nuclei. The paper directly addresses a challenge from Bhalerao et al. (1992) who claimed that the seniority zero approach for abnormal parity states leads to a 50% underestimation of quadrupole moments in the pseudo SU(3) model. The authors show that with an alternative parametrization of effective charges, both the pseudo SU(3) model and the SSANM can reproduce experimental data, though the pseudo SU(3) requires larger effective charges. This enables a more nuanced understanding of how abnormal parity nucleons contribute to nuclear deformation and suggests that higher seniority states beyond the seniority zero approximation are needed for a complete description.
