# Light Axial Vectors, Nuclear Transitions, and the $^8$Be Anomaly

> New hidden particles could potentially be emitted and discovered in rare nuclear transitions. In this work we investigate the production of hidden vector bosons with primarily axial couplings to light quarks in nuclear transitions, and we apply our results to the recent anomaly seen in $^8$Be decays. The relevant matrix elements for $^8{\rm Be}^*(1^+) \rightarrow ^8{\rm Be} (0^+)$ transitions are calculated using ${\it ab \, initio}$ methods with inter-nucleon forces derived from chiral effective field theory and the in-medium similarity renormalization group. We find that the emission of a light axial vector with mass $m_X \simeq 17\, {\rm MeV}$ can account for the anomaly seen in the $1^+\to 0^+$ isoscalar transition together with the absence of a significant anomaly in the corresponding isovector transition. We also show that such an axial vector can be derived from an anomaly-free ultraviolet-complete theory that is consistent with current experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors find that a light axial vector boson with mass approximately 17 MeV can explain the anomalous electron-positron pair emission seen in the isoscalar $^8$Be$^*(1^+) \to ^8$Be$(0^+)$ transition, while simultaneously being consistent with the absence of a similar anomaly in the corresponding isovector transition, provided the nuclear matrix elements are computed using state-of-the-art ab initio methods.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio nuclear structure calculations for $^8$Be using the in-medium similarity renormalization group (IM-SRG) method with chiral effective field theory interactions, including two-nucleon forces at N$^3$LO and three-nucleon forces at N$^2$LO. They computed the reduced matrix elements for the proton and neutron spin operators connecting the $0^+$ ground state to the two lowest $1^+$ excited states, and used the correlation between these matrix elements and the known M1 transition strengths to extract predictions for the axial vector decay widths.

## Key numbers

- The axial vector mass required to explain the anomaly is $m_X \simeq 17$ MeV
- The predicted reduced matrix element for the isovector-like state: $\langle 0^+ \| \sigma_p \| \mathcal{V} \rangle = 0.102 (28)$
- The predicted reduced matrix element for the isovector-like state: $\langle 0^+ \| \sigma_n \| \mathcal{V} \rangle = -0.073 (29)$
- The predicted reduced matrix element for the isoscalar-like state: $\langle 0^+ \| \sigma_p \| \mathcal{S} \rangle = -0.047 (29)$
- The predicted reduced matrix element for the isoscalar-like state: $\langle 0^+ \| \sigma_n \| \mathcal{S} \rangle = -0.132 (33)$
- The predicted M1 matrix element for the isovector-like state is $0.76 (12)~\mu_N$, consistent with the experimental value $0.84(7)~\mu_N$

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Feng et al. (2016) who studied a protophobic vector boson explanation of the $^8$Be anomaly, but could not systematically evaluate the axial vector case due to the difficulty of computing the relevant nuclear matrix elements. The authors apply ab initio methods previously used for nuclear structure calculations (Hagen 2016, Calci 2016) to provide controlled estimates of the axial vector transition rates, enabling a test of whether an axial vector can explain the Atomki anomaly observed by Krasznahorkay et al. (2015).
