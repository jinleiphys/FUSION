# The fourth dimension of the nucleon structure: spacetime analysis of the timelike electromagnetic proton form factors

> As well known, spacelike proton form factors expressed in the Breit frame may be interpreted as the Fourier transform of static space distributions of electric charge and current. In particular, the electric form factor is simply the Fourier transform of the charge distribution $F(q)=\int e^{i\vec q \cdot \vec r} ρ(r)d^3r$. We don't have an intuitive interpretation of the same level of simplicity for the proton timelike form factor appearing in the reactions $e^+e^-\leftrightarrow \bar{p}p$. However, one may suggest that in the center of mass (CM) frame, where $q_μx^μ=qt$, a timelike electric form factor is the Fourier transform $F(q) =\int e^{iqt} R(t)dt$ of a function $R(t)$ expressing how the electric properties of the forming (or annihilating) proton-antiproton pair evolve in time. Here we analyze in depth this idea, show that the functions $ρ(r)$ and $R(t)$ can be formally written as the time and space integrals of a unique correlation function depending on both time and space coordinates.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the timelike electromagnetic proton form factor, measured in $e^+e^- \leftrightarrow \bar{p}p$ reactions, can be interpreted as the Fourier transform of a time-dependent function $R(t)$ describing how the electric properties of the forming or annihilating proton-antiproton pair evolve in time, analogous to how the spacelike form factor in the Breit frame is the Fourier transform of a static charge distribution $\rho(r)$.

## Method

The authors formalize the relation between the spacelike charge density $\rho(r)$ and the timelike time density $R(t)$ by introducing a four-dimensional correlation function $F(x) = F(t,\vec{x})$ whose Fourier transform gives the form factor $F(q)$. They show that $\rho(r)$ and $R(t)$ are orthogonal projections of this same underlying function: $\rho(|\vec{x}|) = \int dt F(t,\vec{x})$ and $R(t) = \int d^3\vec{x} F(t,\vec{x})$. The analysis uses a model where the virtual photon converts into a quark-antiquark pair at a four-point $x_1$, and the form factor emerges from integrating over all internal coordinates of the hadron Fock states.

## Key numbers

- The effective timelike form factor $|F_p|$ has been measured for $q^2$ ranging from the threshold $(2 M_N)^2$ to about 36 GeV$^2$, with most recent precise results from BABAR and BESIII collaborations.
- Four parameterizations are compared: $|F_{scaling}(q^2)| = \mathcal{A}/(q^2)^2 \log^2(q^2/\Lambda^2)$ with $\mathcal{A}=40$ GeV$^{-4}$, $\Lambda=0.45$ GeV$^2$; $|F_{scaling+corr}(q^2)| = \mathcal{A}/(q^2)^2[\log^2(q^2/\Lambda^2)+\pi^2]$ with $\mathcal{A}=72$ GeV$^{-4}$, $\Lambda=0.52$ GeV$^2$; $|F_{T3}(q^2)| = \mathcal{A}/(1-q^2/m_1^2)(2-q^2/m_2^2)$ with $\mathcal{A}=1.56$, $m_1^2=1.5$ GeV$^2$, $m_2^2=0.77$ GeV$^2$; $|F_{BABAR}(q^2)| = \mathcal{A}/(1+q^2/m_a^2)[1-q^2/0.71]^2$ with $\mathcal{A}=7.7$ GeV$^{-4}$, $m_a^2=14.8$ GeV$^2$.
- The oscillatory modulation term $F_{osc}(p) = A e^{-Bp} \cos(C p + D)$ has magnitude $A \simeq 0.1$ and $D\approx 0$, where $p$ is the relative three-momentum of the final hadron pair.

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of form factors from the spacelike region, where the Breit-frame Fourier transform gives static charge distributions (a standard result in nucleon structure), to the timelike region where no such intuitive picture previously existed. The approach builds on a suggestion from Kuraev (2011) about spacetime analysis of form factors, and connects to the authors' previous work (Bianconi 2015, 2016) on oscillatory modulations in timelike form factor data from BABAR. The analysis enables a unified spacetime description of both spacelike and timelike form factors, potentially clarifying the physical meaning of the threshold enhancement and oscillations observed in $e^+e^- \rightarrow \bar{p}p$ data, and may help distinguish between competing models such as vector meson dominance (Bijker 2004, Adamuscin 2005), dispersion relations (Belushkin 2006, Lomon 2012), and the AdS/QCD approach (Brodsky 2007).
