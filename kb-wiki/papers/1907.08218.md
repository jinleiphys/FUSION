# Pion and Kaon Structure at the Electron-Ion Collider

> Understanding the origin and dynamics of hadron structure and in turn that of atomic nuclei is a central goal of nuclear physics. This challenge entails the questions of how does the roughly 1 GeV mass-scale that characterizes atomic nuclei appear; why does it have the observed value; and, enigmatically, why are the composite Nambu-Goldstone (NG) bosons in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) abnormally light in comparison? In this perspective, we provide an analysis of the mass budget of the pion and proton in QCD; discuss the special role of the kaon, which lies near the boundary between dominance of strong and Higgs mass-generation mechanisms; and explain the need for a coherent effort in QCD phenomenology and continuum calculations, in exa-scale computing as provided by lattice QCD, and in experiments to make progress in understanding the origins of hadron masses and the distribution of that mass within them. We compare the unique capabilities foreseen at the electron-ion collider (EIC) with those at the hadron-electron ring accelerator (HERA), the only previous electron-proton collider; and describe five key experimental measurements, enabled by the EIC and aimed at delivering fundamental insights that will generate concrete answers to the questions of how mass and structure arise in the pion and kaon, the Standard Model's NG modes, whose surprisingly low mass is critical to the evolution of our Universe.

## Key claim

The paper argues that understanding the origin of visible mass in the Universe requires simultaneously explaining both the proton's roughly 1 GeV mass and the pion's anomalously low mass, with the kaon lying at the boundary between strong (dynamical chiral symmetry breaking) and Higgs-driven mass generation mechanisms; the authors claim that the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) can deliver fundamental insights into these questions through five specific measurements, but note that theoretical guidance from continuum calculations and lattice QCD is needed to properly interpret the results.

## Method

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the mass budget of the pion and proton in quantum chromodynamics (QCD), using the trace anomaly of the energy-momentum tensor and the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation to decompose mass contributions. It compares the capabilities of the planned EIC with those of the hadron-electron ring accelerator (HERA), the only previous electron-proton collider, and describes five key experimental measurements aimed at probing parton distribution functions, form factors, generalized parton distributions, open charm production, and fragmentation functions for pions and kaons.

## Key numbers

- Proton mass m_N ~ 1 GeV, approximately 2000 times the electron mass
- Pion mass m_pi ~ 0.15 m_N (approximately 140 MeV empirically)
- Kaon mass m_K ~ 0.5 GeV
- Rho meson mass m_rho > 5 m_pi
- Lattice QCD calculation reports proton mass decomposition: trace anomaly = 23(1)%, quark current-mass term = 9(2)%, gluon kinetic + potential energy = 36(6)%, quark kinetic + potential energy = 32(6)%
- Existing Drell-Yan data indicate about 40% of the pion's momentum is carried by gluons at a scale Q^2 = 5 GeV^2
- For the rho meson, just 6% of its mass-squared is directly tied to the current-quark mass term
- The scale of the Higgs effect for light quarks is ~ 1 MeV, and ~ 100 MeV for the s-quark

## Context

This perspective extends the theoretical framework of dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) developed by Nambu (1961) and the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner relation (1968), building on contemporary lattice QCD calculations by Yang (2018) and continuum studies by Maris (1997), Qin (2014), and Binosi (2016). The paper compares the EIC's capabilities with those of HERA and outlines measurements that could resolve the dichotomy between the proton's large mass and the pion's near-masslessness, potentially enabling experimental tests of the DCSB paradigm through studies of pion and kaon structure functions, form factors, and parton distribution amplitudes.
