# Case study: calculation of a narrow resonance with the LIT method

> The possibility to resolve narrow structures in reaction cross sections in calculations with the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method is studied. To this end we consider a fictitious two-nucleon problem with a low-lying and narrow resonance in the $^3P_1$ nucleon-nucleon partial wave and calculate the corresponding ``deuteron photoabsorption cross section''. In the LIT method the use of continuum wave functions is avoided and one works instead with a localized function \tildeΨ. In this case study it is investigated how far into the asymptotic region \tildeΨhas to be determined in order to obtain a precise resolution of the artificially introduced E1 resonance. Comparing with the results of a conventional calculation with explicit neutron-proton continuum wave functions it is shown that the LIT approach leads to an excellent reproduction of the cross section in the resonance region and of further finer cross section details at higher energies. To this end, however, for \tildeΨone has to take into account two-nucleon distances up to at least 30 fm.

# Case study: calculation of a narrow resonance with the LIT method

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method can resolve a narrow resonance with a width of 270 KeV in a deuteron photoabsorption cross section, provided the localized function $\tilde\Psi$ is calculated to two-nucleon distances of at least 30 fm and the LIT resolution parameter $\sigma_I$ is chosen between 1 and 2 MeV (about 4-8 times larger than the resonance width). The abstract's claim of "excellent reproduction" is conditional on these specific parameter choices; with $\sigma_I$ = 10 MeV the resonance cannot be resolved at all, and with $\sigma_I$ = 0.1 MeV the calculation becomes unreliable even with $R_{\rm max}$ = 300 fm.

## Method

A fictitious two-nucleon problem was constructed by modifying the $^3P_1$ partial wave of the AV18 nucleon-nucleon interaction with an added attractive potential to create a low-lying narrow resonance. The deuteron photoabsorption cross section was calculated in two ways: (i) conventionally, using explicit $^3P_1$ neutron-proton continuum wave functions, and (ii) via the LIT method, where the Lorentz integral transform of the response function was computed by solving a bound-state-like equation for the localized function $\tilde\Psi$ with various asymptotic boundary conditions (imposed at a maximum radius $R_{\rm max}$), followed by inversion using an extended basis set that includes a Lorentzian resonance term. The dependence of the results on $R_{\rm max}$ (from 10 to 300 fm) and on the LIT resolution parameter $\sigma_I$ (from 0.1 to 10 MeV) was systematically studied.

## Key numbers

- The fictitious $^3P_1$ resonance has a width $\Gamma$ of 270 KeV at a photon energy $\omega$ = 2.65 MeV (0.48 MeV above the deuteron break-up threshold).
- For $\sigma_I$ = 1 MeV with $R_{\rm max}$ = 30 fm, the LIT inversion reproduces the low-energy resonance peak with excellent agreement; deviations from the converged LIT are about 1% at $R_{\rm max}$ = 30 fm, reducing to 0.5% at $R_{\rm max}$ = 40 fm and 0.1% at $R_{\rm max}$ = 50 fm.
- For $\sigma_I$ = 0.5 MeV with $R_{\rm max}$ = 30 fm, deviations from the converged LIT reach up to 8%; $R_{\rm max}$ = 50 fm still shows visible oscillations.
- For $\sigma_I$ = 0.1 MeV, convergence requires $R_{\rm max}$ up to 300 fm; at $R_{\rm max}$ = 80 fm the result is not converged.
- With $\sigma_I$ = 5 MeV and $R_{\rm max}$ = 80 fm, the resonance peak cross section is underestimated by about 5%.
- A second resonance at about 10.5 MeV above threshold has a width of about 5 MeV and is four orders of magnitude weaker than the first resonance.
- A third broad maximum appears at about 60 MeV photon energy, about 25 times stronger than the second resonance peak.

## Context

This work extends the LIT method, previously applied mainly to broad structures such as giant dipole resonances and quasielastic peaks (e.g., in photoabsorption of A=3 to A=7 nuclei by Efros et al. (2000) and Doron et al. (2006)), to the regime of narrow resonances. It was motivated by a recent LIT calculation of $^4$He (e,e') form factors by Sonia (2007) where a resonance in the Coulomb monopole transition was observed but its width could not be determined. The study demonstrates that with proper choice of the asymptotic range of $\tilde\Psi$ and the resolution parameter $\sigma_I$, the LIT method can resolve structures with widths as small as 270 KeV, enabling future ab initio calculations of narrow resonances in few-body nuclear systems.
