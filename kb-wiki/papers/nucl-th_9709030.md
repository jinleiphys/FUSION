# Dibaryons in nuclear matter

> The possibility for occurrence of a Bose condensate of dibaryons in nuclear matter is investigated within the framework of the Walecka model in the mean-field approximation. Constraints for the omega - and sigma - meson coupling constants with dibaryons following from the requirement of stability of dibaryon matter against compression are derived and the effect of sigma - and pi - meson exchange current contributions to the sigma - dibaryon coupling constant is discussed. The mean-field solutions of the model are constructed. The effective nucleon mass vanishes when the density of dibaryons approaches a critical value about 0.15/fm^3. The Green's functions of the equilibrium binary mixture of nucleons and dibaryons are constructed by solving the Gorkov-Dyson system of equations in the no-loop approximation. We find that when the square of the sound velocity is positive, the dispersion laws for all elementary excitations of the system are real functions. This indicates stability of the ground state of the heterophase nucleon-dibaryon mixture. In the model considered, production of dibaryons becomes energetically favorable at higher densities as compared to estimates based on a model of non-interacting nucleons and dibaryons.

## Key claim

Within the Walecka model in the mean-field approximation, a Bose condensate of dibaryons in nuclear matter is possible, but the stability of the heterophase nucleon-dibaryon mixture depends on the sign of the square of the sound velocity; the effective nucleon mass vanishes when the dibaryon density approaches a critical value of about 0.15 fm^{-3}, and dibaryon production becomes energetically favorable only at higher densities compared to estimates from non-interacting models.

## Method

The authors extended the Walecka model by adding dibaryon fields interacting with nucleons and each other via omega- and sigma-meson exchanges, then solved the mean-field equations for the heterophase system. They derived constraints on the meson-dibaryon coupling constants from the requirement of stability of dibaryon matter against compression, and estimated exchange current contributions from sigma- and pi-meson diagrams to the sigma-dibaryon coupling constant. The Green's functions of the equilibrium binary mixture were constructed by solving the Gorkov-Dyson system of equations in the no-loop approximation.

## Key numbers

- Critical dibaryon density at which the effective nucleon mass vanishes: rho_DV^{c,max} approx 0.15 fm^{-3}
- Lower bound on dibaryon mass in nuclei from chemical equilibrium: m_D > 2 mu_N = 1.96 GeV, where epsilon_F = 40 MeV is the Fermi energy of nucleons in nuclei
- Lower bounds on dibaryon mass from neutron star stability (no interaction): m_D > 2.18 GeV (tensor interaction model) and m_D > 2.03 GeV (Reid model), corresponding to neutron chemical potentials of 1090 MeV and 1015 MeV respectively in a 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- Standard Walecka model parameters used: m_sigma = 520 MeV, g_omega^2 = 190.4, g_sigma^2 = 109.6
- Quark cluster model values for H-particle meson couplings: h_omega^2 = 603.7, h_sigma^2 = 279.2
- Ratio of H-particle couplings to additive estimates: h_omega/(2g_omega) = 0.89, h_sigma/(2g_sigma) = 0.80
- Critical density formula: rho_DV^{c,max} = (m_N m_sigma^2)/(g_sigma h_sigma) = 0.1507 (2g_sigma/h_sigma) fm^{-3}
- Threshold dibaryon mass for transition behavior: m_D = 1.89 GeV

## Context

This work extends the Walecka model (Walecka 1974, Chin 1977) to include dibaryon fields, building on earlier studies of heterophase nucleon-dibaryon matter by Baldin et al., Chizhov et al., and Mrowczynski. The paper connects to prior work on the H-particle by Jaffe (1977) and subsequent bag model, quark cluster model, and lattice calculations, as well as to studies of dibaryon effects on neutron star structure by Tamagaki and Olinto et al. The results enable further investigation of the equation of state of dense nuclear matter and neutron star stability, particularly regarding the role of exotic dibaryons such as the d' (2063 MeV) and d_1(1920) resonances.
