# Finite-Size Effects in Heavy Halo Nuclei from Effective Field Theory

> Halo/Cluster Effective Field Theory describes halo/cluster nuclei in an expansion in the small ratio of the size of the core(s) to the size of the system. Even in the point-particle limit, neutron halo nuclei have a finite charge radius, because their center of mass does not coincide with their center of charge. This point-particle contribution decreases as $1/A_c$, where $A_c$ is the mass number of the core, and diminishes in importance compared to other effects, e.g., the size of the core to which the neutrons are bound. Here we propose that for heavy cores the EFT expansion should account for the small factors of $1/A_c$. As a specific example, we discuss the implications of this organizational scheme for the inclusion of finite-size effects in expressions for the charge radii of halo nuclei. We show in particular that a short-range operator could be the dominant effect in the charge radius of one-neutron halos bound by a P-wave interaction. The point-particle contribution remains the leading piece of the charge radius for one-proton halos, and so Halo EFT has more predictive power in that case.

## Key claim

For heavy-core halo nuclei, the effective field theory expansion should incorporate factors of \(1/A_c\) (where \(A_c\) is the core mass number) as an additional small parameter alongside the usual ratio of core size to halo size; this reorganization can cause a short-range operator to become the dominant contribution to the charge radius of one-neutron halos bound by a P-wave interaction, whereas the point-particle contribution remains leading for one-proton halos.

## Method

The authors construct a Halo/Cluster Effective Field Theory (HEFT) Lagrangian for one-nucleon halo systems with a spin-0 core, including S-wave and P-wave nucleon-core interactions, and incorporate finite-size effects through derivative couplings to the photon field. They compute the charge form factor from diagrams involving photon couplings to the core, nucleon, and dicluster fields, and organize contributions according to powers of both \(k_{\rm lo}/k_{\rm hi}\) (the ratio of low to high momentum scales) and \(1/A_c\) (the inverse core mass number). The charge radius is extracted from the momentum expansion of the form factor, and the scaling of each contribution is estimated using naive dimensional analysis and the kinematic suppression from core recoil.

## Key numbers

* For S-wave neutron halos, the point-particle contribution to the charge radius squared scales as \(r_{\rm pt}^2 \sim 1/(A_c^2 k_{\rm lo}^2)\), where \(A_c\) is the core mass number and \(k_{\rm lo}\) is the low-momentum scale.
* For P-wave neutron halos, the point-particle contribution scales as \(r_{\rm pt}^2 \sim 1/(A_c^2 k_{\rm lo} k_{\rm hi})\), where \(k_{\rm hi}\) is the high-momentum scale.
* The core finite-size contribution to the charge radius squared scales as \(\rho_c^2 \sim 1/k_{\rm hi}^2\) for both S-wave and P-wave halos.
* The nucleon finite-size contribution scales as \(\rho_N^2/Z_h \sim R_N^2/Z_h\), where \(R_N\) is the nucleon size and \(Z_h\) is the halo charge; for a canonical core \(R_c \sim A_c^{1/3} R_N\), this gives \(\rho_N^2/(Z_h \rho_c^2) \sim 1/(Z_h A_c^{2/3})\).
* The short-range operator contribution scales as \(\gamma_0 r_0 \rho_\sigma^2 \sim k_{\rm lo}/k_{\rm hi}^3\) for S-wave halos and as \(\rho_\pi^2 \sim 1/k_{\rm hi}^2\) for P-wave halos.
* The proton charge radius squared is \(\rho_p^2 = 0.766\ \mathrm{fm}^2\) (Mohr 2015), and the neutron charge radius squared is \(\rho_n^2 = -0.116\ \mathrm{fm}^2\) (Olive 2016).

## Context

This work extends the HEFT framework developed by Bertulani (2002), Bedaque (2003), and Hammer (2011) for halo nuclei by explicitly incorporating the kinematic suppression factor \(1/A_c\) that arises from the core recoil, which was not accounted for in the power counting of previous treatments. The authors show that for heavy cores, the point-particle contribution to the charge radius of neutron halos is suppressed by \(1/A_c^2\), making finite-size and short-range contributions relatively more important; this is analogous to the need to distinguish relativistic corrections suppressed by \(1/m_N\) from other corrections in chiral EFT (van Kolck 1998, Chen 1999, Zhang 2017). The results enable systematic inclusion of finite-size effects in charge radius calculations for halo nuclei and highlight that for P-wave one-neutron halos (such as the excited state of \(^{11}\mathrm{Be}\)), a short-range operator may be as important as the long-distance contributions previously computed by Hammer and Phillips (2011), reducing predictive power compared to proton halos.
