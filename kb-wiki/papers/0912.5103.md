# Do we understand the incompressibility of neutron-rich matter?

> The ``breathing mode'' of neutron-rich nuclei is our window into the incompressibility of neutron-rich matter. After much confusion on the interpretation of the experimental data, consistency was finally reached between different models that predicted both the distribution of isoscalar monopole strength in finite nuclei and the compression modulus of infinite matter. However, a very recent experiment on the Tin isotopes at the Research Center for Nuclear Physics(RCNP) in Japan has again muddled the waters. Self-consistent models that were successful in reproducing the energy of the giant monopole resonance (GMR) in nuclei with various nucleon asymmetries (such as 90Zr, 144Sm, and 208Pb) overestimate the GMR energies in the Tin isotopes. As important, the discrepancy between theory and experiment appears to grow with neutron excess. This is particularly problematic as models artificially tuned to reproduce the rapid softening of the GMR in the Tin isotopes become inconsistent with the behavior of dilute neutron matter. Thus, we regard the question of ``why is Tin so soft?'' as an important open problem in nuclear structure.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the giant monopole resonance (GMR) energies measured in the Tin isotopes at RCNP are significantly lower than predictions from accurately calibrated models that successfully reproduce GMR energies in other nuclei (90Zr, 144Sm, 208Pb), and that this discrepancy grows with neutron excess. The author regards the question of "why is Tin so soft?" as an important open problem, noting that models tuned to reproduce this softening become inconsistent with the behavior of dilute neutron matter.

## Method

The author compares experimental GMR centroid energies for neutron-even 112Sn-124Sn isotopes against theoretical predictions from three relativistic mean-field models (FSUGold, NL3, and a Hybrid model) using consistent Mean-Field plus Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations. The incompressibility coefficient of neutron-rich matter is expressed as K0(α)=K0+Kτα2+O(α4), where α=(N−Z)/A is the nucleon asymmetry, and Kτ is related to the slope (L) and curvature (Ksym) of the symmetry energy at saturation density. The author also compares the equation of state of pure neutron matter from these models against microscopic calculations from various many-body approaches.

## Key numbers

- The incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter is placed at K0=240±10 MeV, representing a consensus between nonrelativistic and relativistic models
- The GMR in 208Pb was measured at EGMR=14.17±0.28 MeV (Youngblood 1999)
- FSUGold model predicts K0=230.0 MeV, L=60.5 MeV, and Kτ=−276.8 MeV; it overestimates experimental GMR energies by about 0.2 MeV for 112Sn and 0.7 MeV for 124Sn
- NL3 model predicts K0=271.5 MeV, L=118.2 MeV, and Kτ=−697.4 MeV
- Hybrid model predicts K0=230.0 MeV, L=118.6 MeV, and Kτ=−563.9 MeV; it falls within 0.1 MeV of experimental data for the full Tin isotopic chain but underestimates the GMR centroid energy in 208Pb by almost 1 MeV
- The liquid-drop inspired fits give EGMR=64.5A−0.29 for FSUGold, 102.6A−0.38 for NL3, and 112.7A−0.41 for Hybrid, compared to the liquid-drop prediction of λ=−1/3
- The neutron-neutron scattering length is |a|=18.5 fm and effective range is re=+2.7 fm

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to extract the nuclear incompressibility from the isoscalar giant monopole resonance, following earlier studies by Blaizot (1980, 1995), Youngblood (1977, 1981, 1999), and the reconciliation of nonrelativistic and relativistic predictions by Piekarewicz (2002, 2003) and others (Vretenar 2003, Agrawal 2003, Colo 2004). The paper directly addresses the discrepancy revealed by the RCNP experiment on Tin isotopes (Garg 2006, Li 2007), which contradicts the consensus value of K0=240±10 MeV. The author connects this problem to the equation of state of dilute neutron matter, referencing microscopic calculations by Friedman and Pandharipande (1981), Schwenk (2005), Hebeler (2009), Gandolfi (2008), and Gezerlis (2009). The work enables future studies that must simultaneously describe ground-state observables, collective modes, and the low-density neutron matter equation of state.
