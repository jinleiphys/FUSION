# Breakup of $^{9}$Be on $^{209}$Bi above and near the Coulomb barrier as a molecular single-particle effect: its influence on complete fusion and scattering

> The breakup of the $^{9}$Be projectile on the $^{209}$Bi target at bombarding energies above and near the Coulomb barrier is studied in the adiabatic two-center shell model approach. The effect of $^{9}$Be$% \to $n$+2α$ breakup channel on complete fusion, elastic and inelastic cross sections is investigated. Results show that the breakup of the projectile $^{9}$Be could be due to a molecular single-particle effect shortly before the colliding nuclei reach the Coulomb barrier.

## Key claim

The breakup of \(^{9}\)Be on \(^{209}\)Bi above and near the Coulomb barrier could be due to a molecular single-particle effect, specifically, the diabatic motion of the last neutron of \(^{9}\)Be into the continuum or its transfer to \(^{209}\)Bi, occurring shortly before the colliding nuclei reach the Coulomb barrier, rather than being a purely Coulomb or nuclear breakup process.

## Method

The authors used the adiabatic two-center shell model (TCSM) to calculate neutron single-particle level diagrams for the \(^{9}\)Be + \(^{209}\)Bi system as a function of internuclear distance. Classical trajectories were computed with a surface friction model (radial friction coefficient \(c_r^0 = 4 \times 10^{-23}\) MeV\(^{-1}\)s, tangential \(c_\varphi^0 = 0.01 \times 10^{-23}\) MeV\(^{-1}\)s), and the Landau-Zener mechanism was applied at pseudo-crossings of adiabatic levels to obtain breakup probabilities. Complete fusion cross sections were then factorised using a Glas-Mosel model modified by an average breakup probability, while elastic and inelastic scattering cross sections were derived from classical scattering formulas with a breakup hindrance factor.

## Key numbers

- The neutron separation energy of \(^{9}\)Be is \(S_n = 1.67\) MeV.
- The Q-value for the breakup channel \(^{9}\)Be \(\rightarrow\) n + \(2\alpha\) is \(Q = -1.57\) MeV.
- The Coulomb barrier height for the system is about 26 MeV, with barrier frequency \(\hbar\omega \approx 2\) MeV and barrier position \(r_B = 11.4\) fm (spherical case) or \(r_B \approx 11.5\) fm (deformed case).
- The touching configuration distance is \(r_t = 9.82\) fm; the critical distance from Galin's formula is \(r_{cr} = 8.01\) fm.
- At pseudo-crossings 1 and 2, the coupling matrix elements are \(|H'_{12}| = 0.1073\) MeV and 0.1250 MeV, respectively, with energy differences \(\frac{d}{dr}(\epsilon_1 - \epsilon_2)\) of 0.0184 MeV fm\(^{-1}\) and 0.0352 MeV fm\(^{-1}\).
- For prolate deformed nuclei (pole-to-pole), the barrier decreases by about 1.3 MeV relative to the spherical case.
- At bombarding energy \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 46\) MeV (1.15 \(V_B\)), the radial kinetic energy loss before reaching the Coulomb barrier is \(\leq 0.1\) MeV for orbital angular momenta \(0 \leq l \leq l_{cr}\).

## Context

This work extends the molecular single-particle approach of the TCSM (Greiner, 1970s) to the breakup of weakly bound \(^{9}\)Be, building on earlier studies of breakup effects on fusion using time-independent and time-dependent semiclassical coupled channels (TH2, TH4, TH1) and three-body Schrödinger equation methods (Yabana, 1999). It addresses contradictory experimental findings: suppression of complete fusion above the barrier observed for \(^{9}\)Be+\(^{209}\)Bi (Signorini, 1998; Dasgupta, 1999) and \(^{9}\)Be+\(^{208}\)Pb, but not for \(^{9}\)Be+\(^{64}\)Zn (Lubian, 2000). The results enable future coupled-channels calculations that incorporate molecular single-particle effects explicitly, and suggest that the presence or absence of fusion suppression depends on whether molecular orbitals and neutron promotion develop during the collision, which in turn depends on target structure.
