# Quantum Phase Transitions in the Interacting Boson Model: Integrability, level repulsion and level crossing

> We study the quantum phase transition mechanisms that arise in the Interacting Boson Model. We show that the second-order nature of the phase transition from U(5) to O(6) may be attributed to quantum integrability, whereas all the first-order phase transitions of the model are due to level repulsion with one singular point of level crossing. We propose a model Hamiltonian with a true first-order phase transition for finite systems due to level crossings.

## Key claim

The second-order quantum phase transition from U(5) to O(6) in the Interacting Boson Model arises because the Hamiltonian along this leg is quantum integrable, which ensures the ground-state energy is an analytic function of the control parameter and precludes level crossings; in contrast, all first-order phase transitions in the model are driven by level repulsion between the ground state and first excited state, except for one singular point at the O(6) critical symmetry where level crossings are allowed due to the realization of O(5) symmetry.

## Method

The authors studied the Consistent-Q Hamiltonian of the Interacting Boson Model, which depends on two control parameters (x and chi) and interpolates between the U(5), SU(3), O(6), and anti-SU(3) dynamical symmetries. For the integrable U(5)-O(6) leg (chi=0), they solved the exact Richardson equations for the Hamiltonian eigenvalues for up to 1000 bosons and compared with mean-field results. For the non-integrable transitions (U(5)-SU(3) and anti-SU(3)-SU(3)), they analyzed the ten lowest 0+ states for 50 and 9 bosons respectively, using perturbation theory to demonstrate level repulsion versus level crossing, and constructed a modified Hamiltonian that cancels the O(5) term to realize a first-order phase transition for finite systems.

## Key numbers

- For the U(5)-O(6) transition with 1000 bosons: the ground-state energy, expectation value of n_d, and derivative of the energy are continuous at the transition point, satisfying both Landau and Ehrenfest definitions of a second-order phase transition
- For the U(5)-SU(3) transition with 50 bosons: the ten lowest 0+ states show level repulsion at points of closest approach, with the gap derivative governed by the perturbation theory equations involving V_12^2/Delta
- For the anti-SU(3)-SU(3) transition with 9 bosons using the modified Hamiltonian: four 0+ levels with sigma=9 cross at chi=0, while away from this point level repulsion sets in
- The O(6) Hamiltonian eigenvalues are E_sigma_tau = [-sigma(sigma+4) + tau(tau+3)]/(2N), with the lowest O(6) band corresponding to sigma=N and tau=0,3,...

## Context

This work extends the mean-field studies of the IBM phase diagram using Catastrophe theory (Lopez-Moreno and Castanos, 1996) and Landau theory (Jolie et al., 2002) by going beyond the mean-field description to examine the microscopic mechanisms of quantum phase transitions. The finding that the U(5)-O(6) transition is integrable builds on earlier work by Alhassid and Whelan (1991) showing Poisson level statistics along this leg, and on the exact solvability of boson pairing Hamiltonians (Dukelsky and Pittel, 2001). The level repulsion mechanism for first-order transitions was earlier recognized by Zamfir, von Brentano, Casten, and Jolie (2002). The modified Hamiltonian that realizes a first-order phase transition for finite systems provides a model to explore universal properties of first-order phase transitions beyond the thermodynamic limit.
