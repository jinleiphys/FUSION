# Finite Volume Effect of Nucleons and the Formation of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> We study a thermodynamically consistent implementation of the nucleon volume in the mean field theory, and find that this volume has large consequences on the properties of hadronic matter under extreme conditions such as in astrophysical objects and high energy heavy-ion collisions. It is shown that we can reproduce the critical temperature $T_{c}\simeq 200$ MeV predicted by lattice QCD calculations for the phase transition from hadronic matter to quark-gluon plasma, by using parameters which are adjusted to fit all empirical data for normal nuclear matter.

# Finite Volume Effect of Nucleons and the Formation of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim
The authors show that including a finite nucleon volume in a thermodynamically consistent mean field theory can reproduce a critical temperature of approximately 200 MeV for the hadron-to-quark-gluon plasma phase transition, as predicted by lattice QCD, using parameters that simultaneously fit all empirical normal nuclear matter data. However, the actual computed transition temperature for vanishing net baryon density is 170 MeV for a bag constant of B^(1/4) = 200 MeV, with the value varying between about 120 and 240 MeV depending on the chosen bag constant.

## Method
The authors implement a thermodynamically consistent finite-volume correction for nucleons and antinucleons within the relativistic mean field theory framework of Bodmer and Boguta (1977). The hadronic phase is described by nucleons and antinucleons with an effective nucleon mass determined by minimizing the energy density, while the quark-gluon plasma phase is treated as a gas of relativistic massless quarks, antiquarks, and gluons with first-order QCD corrections and a bag constant. Phase coexistence is determined via Gibbs' conditions, and the mean field parameters are adjusted to fit normal nuclear matter properties including equilibrium density, binding energy, asymmetry energy, and compression modulus.

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature for vanishing net baryon density: T_c = 170 MeV (for B^(1/4) = 200 MeV)
- T_c varies from about 120 to 240 MeV when B^(1/4) is changed from 125 MeV to 300 MeV
- Normal nuclear matter parameters used: equilibrium density with r = 1.175 fm, binding energy B/A = 15.986 MeV, asymmetry energy E_as = 36.5 MeV, compression modulus K = 240 MeV
- Nucleon finite volume parameter: a = 0.62 fm (with τ = 4πa^3/3)
- Changing a from 0.62 fm to 0.74 fm changes T_c by less than 1 MeV
- Turning off O(α_s) corrections gives T_c = 140 MeV, a 20% change from the ideal quark-gluon gas result
- For a large baryon number N_B = 128, a phase transition still occurs with T_c ≈ 260 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass χ ≥ 0.6m for the simple case and χ ≥ 0.5m for the large N_B case

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous successful application of finite nucleon volume to neutron star mass limits (Ma, Zhang, and Greiner, 1992) by now addressing the quark-gluon plasma phase transition. It directly addresses the difficulty identified by Hagedorn, Stocker, and Greiner (1986) and Rischke, Gorenstein, Stoecker, and Greiner (1991) that point-like hadronic models with many resonances cannot reproduce a phase transition to quark-gluon plasma at high temperature. The approach enables a unified description of normal nuclear matter, neutron star properties, and the hadron-to-quark-gluon plasma transition, and suggests that further studies should include a realistic mass spectrum of baryonic resonances and strange degrees of freedom.
