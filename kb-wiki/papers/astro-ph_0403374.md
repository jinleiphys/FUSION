# Mergers of binary stars: The ultimate heavy-ion experience

> The mergers of black hole-neutron star binaries are calcuated using a pseudo-general relativistic potential that incorporates ${\mathcal O}(v^2/c^2)^3$ post-Newtonian corrections. Both normal matter neutron stars and self-bound strange quark matter stars are considered as black hole partners. As long as the neutron stars are not too massive relative to the black hole mass, orbital decay terminates in stable mass transfer rather than an actual merger. For a normal neutron star, mass transfer results in a widening of the orbit but the stable transfer ends before the minimum neutron star mass is reached. For a strange star, mass transfer does not result in an appreciable enlargement of the orbital separation, and the stable transfer continues until the strange star essentially disappears. These differences might be observable through their respective gravitational wave signatures.

# Mergers of binary stars: The ultimate heavy-ion experience

## Key claim
The qualitative differences in mass-radius relations between normal neutron stars and self-bound strange quark matter stars produce significant changes in the outcomes of gravitational mergers with black holes, but only as long as the neutron stars are not too massive relative to the black hole mass. The authors use a pseudo-general relativistic potential with post-Newtonian corrections up to order (v²/c²)³, which speeds up the evolution relative to Newtonian calculations but does not alter the qualitative differences between the two star types.

## Method
The authors calculated black hole-neutron star binary mergers using a pseudo-general relativistic potential incorporating post-Newtonian corrections up to order (v²/c²)³, as evaluated by Blanchet (2002). They considered both normal matter neutron stars and self-bound strange quark matter stars as black hole partners, using prototype equations of state from Lattimer and Prakash (2001). The evolution was followed from wide separation through orbital decay via gravitational radiation reaction, Roche lobe overflow, and stable mass transfer, with corrections applied to both the gravitational radiation reaction and the effective Roche lobe radius.

## Key numbers
- Initial masses employed: M₁ = 1.33 M☉ (neutron star) and M₂ = 2.67 M☉ (black hole)
- For normal neutron stars, stable mass transfer ends before the minimum neutron star mass is reached, which is about 0.09 M☉
- For self-bound strange stars, mass transfer continues until the star essentially disappears, with the smallest mass being that of a strange quark nugget
- The logarithmic derivative α ≡ d ln R / d ln M is ≤ 0 for normal neutron stars and ≥ 0 for self-bound strange quark matter stars (α ≅ 1/3 for low-mass self-bound stars)

## Context
This work extends earlier merger studies by Lee (2001), Prakash (2003), and Shibata (2003) by incorporating post-Newtonian corrections to the gravitational potential for asymmetric mass ratios (q ≠ 1). The authors contrast the behavior of normal neutron stars, which may contain hyperons, Bose condensates, or quark matter, with self-bound strange quark matter stars as proposed by Alcock (1986). The results suggest that gravitational wave signatures could distinguish between the two types of compact objects, and the work enables future studies incorporating non-conservative mass transfer, tidal synchronization, and accretion disk effects.
