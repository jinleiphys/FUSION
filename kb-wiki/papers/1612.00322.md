# Integral transform methods: a critical review of various kernels

> Some general remarks about integral transform approaches to response functions are made. Their advantage for calculating cross sections at energies in the continuum is stressed. In particular we discuss the class of kernels that allow calculations of the transform by matrix diagonalization. A particular set of such kernels, namely the wavelets, is tested in a model study.

# Integral transform methods: a critical review of various kernels

## Key claim

The authors argue that integral transform methods with positive-definite kernels allow calculation of response functions in the continuum via Hamiltonian diagonalization on finite-norm basis functions, but caution that the practical resolution of the reconstructed spectrum is limited by the density of eigenvalues obtained from the diagonalization, not just by the kernel properties.

## Method

The paper reviews the formal conditions under which an integral transform of a response function S(ω) can be computed by diagonalizing the Hamiltonian represented on a finite-norm basis, then inverts the transform to recover S(ω). As a test case, the authors apply Haar wavelets as the kernel to a model S(ω) consisting of a narrow resonance and a broader peak, and examine the quality of the reconstruction as a function of the maximum resolution index j_max.

## Key numbers

- The model S(ω) used for testing contains a narrow resonance and a larger peak, a situation described as "often problematic for common inversion methods."
- Reconstruction at j_max = 4 and j_max = 5 is shown in Figures 2a and 2b; the text states that a maximum resolution "slightly smaller than the width of the resonance allows a rather good description of both the resonance and the larger peak."
- The wavelet kernel is defined with integer parameters j (resolution, ranging from -∞ to ∞) and k (position, with k_max = 2^j - 1).

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method (Ref1 in the paper), which has been widely applied to electroweak interactions with few-nucleon systems, by examining alternative kernels that also satisfy the calculability and invertibility conditions. The authors contrast wavelets with the Laplace kernel (used in imaginary-time Monte Carlo calculations) and the Stieltjes kernel (Ref3), noting that the latter is useful for polarizabilities (Ref4). The paper enables future application of wavelet kernels to realistic many-body systems, where the eigenvalue density from diagonalization will set the achievable resolution.
