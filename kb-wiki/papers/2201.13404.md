# Modern approaches to optical potentials

> This chapter presents an overview of the optical model description of nucleon-nucleus scattering and reactions based on fundamental nuclear two-body and many-body forces. The chapter begins with a historical review followed by a discussion of several commonly used theoretical techniques for deriving nucleon-nucleus optical model potentials that account for antisymmetry, Pauli blocking, and multiple scattering. This is followed by a summary of current efforts to derive microscopic nuclear forces consistent with the fundamental theory of strong interactions, quantum chromodynamics, and the use of such interactions in the construction of microscopic optical potentials. The chapter will also outline the dispersive optical potential approach, which despite being primarily phenomenological, has its origins in formal Green's function theory. Finally, the results from modern optical potentials will be benchmarked to experimental data.

## Key claim

This chapter reviews modern approaches to constructing nucleon-nucleus optical potentials, arguing that microscopic methods based on chiral effective field theory and many-body theory can provide a more consistent description of nuclear structure and reactions than purely phenomenological potentials, though the dispersive optical potential approach, despite being primarily phenomenological, also offers a unified framework for scattering and structure data.

## Method

The authors survey several theoretical frameworks for deriving optical potentials: Feshbach's projection formalism, which formally connects the optical potential to quantum many-body theory by projecting out inelastic channels; Watson's multiple scattering theory, which approximates the projectile-nucleus interaction as a superposition of two-body nucleon-nucleon scattering amplitudes at sufficiently high energies (E ≳ 70 MeV); and the Green's function approach, which describes single-particle motion via the one-body Green's function and its Lehmann representation. They also discuss the dispersive optical model, a phenomenological approach rooted in formal Green's function theory, and benchmark results from these modern potentials against experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The complex square well potential with parameters V₀ = 42 MeV, ζ = 0.05, and R = 1.45 × 10⁻¹³ A^(1/3) cm reproduces experimental neutron total cross sections.
- The Koning-Delaroche global phenomenological optical potential has 35 adjustable parameters.
- The chiral effective field theory expansion is ordered in powers of q/Λ, where Λ ∼ 1 GeV is the chiral symmetry breaking scale.
- Three-body forces in chiral effective field theory arise at next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO), (q/Λ)³, without explicit Δ-isobar degrees of freedom, and one order lower, (q/Λ)², with explicit Δs.
- Multiple scattering theory is applicable for projectile energies E ≳ 70 MeV, where the projectile wavelength is small compared to interparticle separation.
- Differential elastic scattering cross sections for protons on ¹²C are shown at energies E = 122 MeV and 200 MeV, with theory results using N4LO chiral interactions and N2LO three-body forces.
- Elastic scattering cross section for antiprotons on ¹²C is shown at energy E = 180 MeV, using N3LO chiral antiproton-nucleon interactions.

## Context

This chapter extends the long history of optical model development from early phenomenological work by Fernbach, Serber, and Taylor (1949), LeLevier and Saxon (1952), and Feshbach, Porter, and Weisskopf (1954) to modern microscopic approaches. It builds on formal foundations established by Feshbach (1958), Watson (1953), Kerman, McManus, and Thaler (1959), and Bell and Squires (1959), as well as the dispersive optical model of Mahaux and Sartor (1986). The work enables future applications to rare-isotope beam experiments at facilities like FRIB, where microscopic optical potentials derived from chiral effective field theory can provide predictive power for nucleon-nucleus scattering on exotic nuclei far from stability.
