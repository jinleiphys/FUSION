# Low-mass neutron stars: universal relations, the nuclear symmetry energy and gravitational radiation

> The lowest neutron star masses currently measured are in the range $1.0-1.1~M_\odot$, but these measurement have either large uncertainties or refer to isolated neutron stars. The recent claim of a precisely measured mass $M/M_{\odot} = 1.174 \pm 0.004$ by Martinez et al [Astrophys. J. 812, 143 (2015)] in a double neutron star system suggests that low-mass neutron stars may be an interesting target for gravitational-wave detectors. Furthermore, Sotani et al [PTEP 2014, 051E01 (2014)] recently found empirical formulas relating the mass and surface redshift of nonrotating neutron stars to the star's central density and to the parameter $η\equiv (K_0 L^2)^{1/3}$, where $K_0$ is the incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter and $L$ is the slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density. Motivated by these considerations, we extend the work by Sotani et al to slowly rotating and tidally deformed neutron stars. We compute the moment of inertia, quadrupole moment, quadrupole ellipticity, tidal and rotational Love number and apsidal constant of slowly rotating neutron stars by integrating the Hartle-Thorne equations at second order in rotation, and we fit all of these quantities as functions of $η$ and of the central density. These fits may be used to constrain $η$, either via observations of binary pulsars in the electromagnetic spectrum, or via near-future observations of inspiralling compact binaries in the gravitational-wave spectrum.

## Key claim

The authors extend previous work by Sotani (2014) to show that the bulk properties of slowly rotating and tidally deformed low-mass neutron stars (moment of inertia, quadrupole moment, ellipticity, Love numbers, and apsidal constant) can be expressed as simple functions of the central density and the nuclear parameter η ≡ (K₀ L²)¹/³, where K₀ is the incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter and L is the slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density, provided the neutron star mass is low enough that the I-Love-Q universality relations begin to break down.

## Method

The authors integrate the Hartle-Thorne equations at second order in rotation to compute the moment of inertia, quadrupole moment, quadrupole ellipticity, tidal and rotational Love numbers, and apsidal constant for 14 unified equation-of-state models spanning central densities from ρc/ρ₀ = 1.0 to 2.0 in increments of 0.1. They then fit all computed quantities as functions of the central density ratio u_c and the nuclear parameter η using quadratic polynomials in u_c with coefficients linear in η.

## Key numbers

- The recent precise mass measurement of M/M⊙ = 1.174 ± 0.004 in a double neutron star system by Martinez (2015) motivates the study of low-mass neutron stars
- The nuclear saturation density is ρ₀/c² ≡ 2.68 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- For the 14 EOS models, K₀ values range from 180 MeV to 360 MeV and L values range from 31.9 MeV to 146 MeV, yielding η values from 55.7 MeV to 197 MeV
- The plausible range for η is 67 < η < 120 MeV, based on K₀ = 230 ± 40 MeV and L = 60 ± 20 MeV
- The rms relative error σ for the mass fit is 0.086 (including BSk21) or 0.076 (excluding BSk21)
- The rms relative error σ for the dimensionless moment of inertia I̅ = I/M³ fit is 0.122 (including BSk21) or 0.083 (excluding BSk21)
- The rms relative error σ for the dimensionless tidal Love number λ̅^(tid) = λ^(tid)/M⁵ fit is 0.462 (including BSk21) or 0.263 (excluding BSk21)
- The I-Love-Q relations hold within accuracy < 2% for masses at or above the minimum mass of 0.89 M⊙
- The improved Love-Love relation fit is accurate within < 1% for M/M⊙ ≥ 0.89

## Context

This work extends the empirical relations found by Sotani (2014) for nonrotating neutron stars to slowly rotating and tidally deformed stars, building on the Hartle-Thorne formalism developed by Hartle (1967, 1968). The authors show that the universal I-Love-Q relations discovered by Yagi (2013) hold within a few percent for low-mass stars but break down noticeably at very low masses, motivating the need for the new η-dependent fits. These fits may enable constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy parameter η through either electromagnetic observations of binary pulsars (such as moment of inertia measurements via spin-orbit coupling) or gravitational-wave observations of inspiralling compact binaries with Advanced LIGO, Advanced Virgo, KAGRA, and the Einstein Telescope.
