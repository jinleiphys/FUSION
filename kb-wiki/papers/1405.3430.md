# Evidence for a secondary bow in Newton's zero-order nuclear rainbow

> Rainbows are generally considered to be caused by static refraction and reflection. A primary and a secondary rainbow appear due to refraction and internal reflection in a raindrop as explained by Newton. The quantum nuclear rainbow, which is generated by refraction in the nucleus droplet, only has a "primary" rainbow. Here we show for the first time evidence for the existence of a secondary nuclear rainbow generated dynamically by coupling to an excited state without internal reflection. This has been demonstrated for experimental $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering using the coupled channel method with an extended double folding potential derived from microscopic realistic wave functions for $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O.

## Key claim

The paper presents evidence for a secondary nuclear rainbow in $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C scattering, generated dynamically by coupling to an excited state rather than by internal reflection. The Airy minimum previously attributed to the primary nuclear rainbow at large angles is actually a first-order Airy minimum of a secondary bow, resolving the mystery of why the global potential failed to reproduce the data at $E_L=281$ MeV.

## Method

The authors performed coupled channel (CC) calculations for $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C elastic scattering using an extended double folding (EDF) potential derived from microscopic realistic wave functions for $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O with a density-dependent nucleon-nucleon force (DDM3Y-FR). They included coupling to the $2^+$ (4.44 MeV) and $3^-$ (9.64 MeV) states of $^{12}$C, and in some calculations also the $3^-$ (6.13 MeV) and $2^+$ (6.92 MeV) states of $^{16}$O, with a phenomenological Woods-Saxon imaginary potential. The deflection functions from the CC calculations were compared with those from conventional single-channel double folding optical model calculations.

## Key numbers

- At $E_L=281$ MeV, the puzzling Airy minimum appears at $\theta \approx 70^\circ$, whereas the global potential predicted it at $\theta \approx 45^\circ$.
- The CC calculation at $E_L=330$ MeV yields a deflection function with three extrema: $L_{R0}=31$ with $\theta_{R0}=18^\circ$, $L_{R1}=34$ with $\theta_{R1}=43^\circ$ (primary rainbow), and $L_{R2}=26$ with $\theta_{R2}=95^\circ$ (secondary rainbow).
- The volume integral per nucleon pair $J_V$ of the ground state diagonal potential ranges from 301 MeV fm$^3$ at $E_L=200$ MeV to 229 MeV fm$^3$ at $E_L=608$ MeV.
- The imaginary potential strength parameters used in CC calculations range from 17.0 to 20.5 MeV for $^{12}$C excitation only, with radius fixed at 5.6 fm and diffuseness at 0.75 fm.

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear rainbow scattering in the $^{16}$O+$^{12}$C system, which had been described by a global potential reproducing data from $E_L=62$ to 1503 MeV (Ogloblin 1998, Nicoli 2000, Szilner 2001, Khoa 1994) but failed at $E_L=281$ MeV (Ogloblin 2003). The discovery of a dynamically generated secondary rainbow, distinct from the meteorological secondary rainbow requiring internal reflection (Newton, Nussenzveig 1969, 1977), solves this dilemma and changes the traditional view that nuclear rainbows are solely due to static refraction in a Luneburg lens (Michel 2002). The finding enables the search for secondary rainbows in other nuclear systems and predicts a clear Airy minimum around $E_L=400$ MeV for experimental verification.
