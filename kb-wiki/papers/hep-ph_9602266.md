# What does a change in the quark condensate say about restoration of chiral symmetry in matter?

> The contribution of nucleons to the quark condensate in nuclear matter includes a piece of first order in $m_π$, arising from the contribution of low-momentum virtual pions to the $πN$ sigma commutator. Chiral symmetry requires that no term of this order appears in the $NN$ interaction. The mass of a nucleon in matter thus cannot depend in any simple way on the quark condensate alone. More generally, pieces of the quark condensate that arise from low-momentum pions should not be associated with partial restoration of chiral symmetry.

# What does a change in the quark condensate say about restoration of chiral symmetry in matter?

## Key claim
The change in the quark condensate in nuclear matter includes a piece of first order in the pion mass \(m_\pi\) arising from low-momentum virtual pions, but chiral symmetry forbids this piece from contributing to the nucleon mass in matter or to the \(NN\) interaction. Therefore, a reduction in the quark condensate does not necessarily imply partial restoration of chiral symmetry, because contributions from low-momentum pions should not be interpreted as a signal of symmetry restoration.

## Method
The author uses chiral perturbation theory and Weinberg's dimensional counting rules to analyze the chiral expansion of the pion-nucleon sigma commutator \(\sigma_{\pi N}\) and the \(NN\) interaction. The leading nonanalytic term in \(\sigma_{\pi N}\) is of order \(m_\pi^3\), arising from the long-range pion cloud, but the corresponding term in the \(NN\) potential would be of order \(m_\pi\) and is forbidden by chiral symmetry. The argument is illustrated using the linear sigma model, where cancellations between diagrams involving sigma exchange and direct pion couplings remove the offending term.

## Key numbers
- The current best determination of \(\sigma_{\pi N}\) from \(\pi N\) scattering is \(45 \pm 7\) MeV.
- Using \(\sigma_{\pi N} = 45\) MeV in the linear density formula suggests a \(\sim 30\%\) reduction in the quark condensate at normal nuclear matter density.
- The scalar quark density of a pion, estimated via the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, is \(\frac{1}{2} m_\pi \simeq 70\) MeV for a unit-normalized pion state.
- The \({\cal O}(m_\pi^3)\) term in \(\sigma_{\pi N}\) is \(-25\) MeV, about half of the coefficient of the density in the linear condensate formula.

## Context
This work extends the debate on whether the quark condensate in matter signals partial chiral restoration, following model estimates by Cohen (1995), Furnstahl et al. (1995), and Birse (1995), and relativistic BHF calculations by Li and Ko (1995) and Brockmann and Weise (1995). It contradicts the naive interpretation that a reduced condensate directly implies symmetry restoration, as assumed in many QCD sum-rule approaches. The paper enables a more careful treatment of the \(\pi N\) continuum in matter, which would remove the spurious \({\cal O}(m_\pi)\) term from sum rules for nucleons in matter, and clarifies that low-momentum pions contribute to the condensate but not to hadron masses.
