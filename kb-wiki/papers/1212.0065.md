# Temperature determined by isobaric yield ratio in heavy-ion collisions

> This work focuses on the study of temperature associated with the final heavy fragments in reactions induced by both the neutron-proton symmetric and the neutron-rich projectiles, and with incident energy ranges from 60$A$ MeV to 1$A$ GeV. Isobaric yield ratio (IYR) is used to determine the temperature of heavy fragments. Cross sections of measured fragment in reactions are analyzed, and a modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model is used to calculate the yield of fragment in 140$A$ MeV $^{64}$Ni + $^{9}$Be and 1$A$ GeV $^{136}$Xe + $^{208}$Pb reactions. Relatively low $T$ of heavy fragments are obtained in different reactions ($T$ ranges from 1 to 3MeV). $T$ is also found to depend on the neutron-richness of the projectile. The incident energy affects $T$ very little. $Δμ/T$ (the ratio of the difference between the chemical potential of neutron and proton to temperature) is found to increase linearly as $N/Z$ of projectile increases. It is found that $T$ of the $^{48}$Ca reaction, for which IYRs are of $A<50$ isobars, is affected greatly by the temperature-corrected $ΔB(T)$. But $T$ of reactions using IYRs of heavier fragments are only slightly affected by the temperature-corrected $ΔB(T)$. The SAA model analysis gives a consistent overview of the results extracted in this work. $T$ from IYR, which is for secondary fragment, is different from that of the hot emitting source. $T$ and $Δμ$ are essentially governed by the sequential decay process.

# Temperature determined by isobaric yield ratio in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The temperature extracted from isobaric yield ratios of heavy fragments in heavy-ion collisions ranges from 1 to 3 MeV, which is significantly lower than the temperature of the hot emitting source and is essentially governed by the sequential decay process rather than the initial collision conditions. The incident energy (from 60A MeV to 1A GeV) affects this temperature very little, while the neutron-richness of the projectile does influence it.

## Method

The authors applied the modified Fisher model to experimental isobaric yield ratios (IYR) of fragments with neutron-excess I greater than or equal to 5, using the zero-temperature binding energy difference between isobars as a substitute for the free energy difference. They fitted the logarithmic IYR minus the mixing entropy term against the binding energy difference to extract both temperature T and the difference in chemical potentials between neutrons and protons (Delta-mu). A modified statistical abrasion-ablation (SAA) model was also used to calculate fragment yields for the 140A MeV 64Ni + 9Be and 1A GeV 136Xe + 208Pb reactions, comparing pre-fragment and final fragment temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Temperature T extracted from IYR ranges from 1 to 3 MeV across all reactions studied.
- The average temperature from all reactions is T = 2.23 MeV.
- For the 64A MeV 86Kr + 9Be reaction, T = 1.97 +/- 0.10 MeV and Delta-mu = -3.16 +/- 0.26 MeV.
- For the 48Ca reaction using temperature-corrected binding energies, T decreases to 1.56 +/- 0.12 MeV at T=2 MeV correction and 1.27 +/- 0.10 MeV at T=3 MeV correction.
- The linear fit between Delta-mu and projectile N/Z gives: y = (15.21 +/- 5.82)x + (-24.16 +/- 7.97).
- For isobars with A less than 50 at T greater than or equal to 3 MeV, the fractional difference between Delta-B(T) and Delta-B(0) exceeds 10 percent (Delta-T0 less than -0.1).

## Context

This work extends the modified Fisher model approach previously used for studying critical phenomena in nuclear liquid-gas transitions (YgMa 1999, EOS 2002, Das 2002) and symmetry energy studies (Huang 2010, Ma 2011) to systematically determine temperatures of heavy fragments across a wide range of projectile masses and incident energies. The results are consistent with the T = 2.2 MeV used by Tsang (2007) for estimating binding energies of neutron-rich copper isotopes. The SAA model analysis demonstrates that the extracted temperatures reflect the sequential decay process rather than the initial hot source, which is crucial for interpreting fragment yield data in studies of neutron-rich nuclei and symmetry energy. The work enables future studies to properly account for secondary decay effects when using fragment yields to extract nuclear properties.
