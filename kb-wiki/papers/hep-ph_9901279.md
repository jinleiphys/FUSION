# Baryons As Hyperspherical O(4) Partial Waves -- Is This The Message From The Spectra?

> It is argued that the baryon excitations group to four-dimensional partial waves described by means of the three Rarita-Schwinger (RS) fields [(σ-1 )/2 ,(σ-1)/2]* [(1/2, 0)+(0,1/2)] with σ=2,4 and 6, where all components happen to be occupied. In the O(4) decomposition of the πN scattering amplitudes, the RS spin- and parity clusters appear as poles on the complex energy plane, socalled H"ohler poles. This phenomenon indicates that the symmetry of the πN scattering amplitude is O(4) and thereby the space-time version of chiral symmetry, rather than O(3). Accordingly, the baryon spectrum generating algebra is su(2)_I*su(3)_c*o(1,3)_{ls} rather than su(6)_{sf} * su(3)_c * o(3)_l. The nucleon and Δspectra below \sim 2500 MeV are complete up to only 5 `missing' resonances. The three O(4) poles are distributed over two distinct Fock spaces of opposite vacuum parities thus defining the energy scale of the chiral phase transition for baryons. Within this new symmetry scenario, the covariant description of the RS baryon clusters is straightforward and their averaged masses are fitted by a Balmer-series like formula emerging from a simple quark-diquark model in the O(4) basis with Coulomb potential and a four-dimensional rigid rotator.

# Baryons As Hyperspherical O(4) Partial Waves

## Key claim

Baryon excitations group into four-dimensional hyperspherical O(4) partial waves (Rarita-Schwinger fields with σ = 2, 4, and 6) rather than into ordinary O(3) partial waves, and the non-strange baryon spectra below ~2500 MeV are complete up to only five "missing" resonances, a substantial reduction from the ten or more missing states predicted by the traditional SU(6)ₛ𝒻 ⊗ O(3)ₗ classification.

## Method

The author reanalyzes the pole positions of baryon resonances on the complex energy plane from πN scattering amplitude decompositions (Höhler poles) and identifies them with O(4) hyperspherical partial waves labeled by principal quantum number σ = 2, 4, 6 and parity η = ±1. A quark-hyperquark model is constructed in which diquarks are treated as pointlike bosonic degrees of freedom grouped into O(4) families with a parity selection rule, and the baryon clusters are described by totally symmetric traceless rank-(σ-1) Lorentz tensors with Dirac spinor components satisfying both Dirac and Proca equations.

## Key numbers

- The three O(4) partial waves have σ = 2, 4, and 6, with the first (σ = 2, η = +1) containing the lowest P, S, and D resonances
- The nucleon and Δ spectra below ~2500 MeV are complete with only 5 "missing" resonances in the O(4) scheme, compared to at least 10 missing in the traditional SU(6)ₛ𝒻 ⊗ O(3)ₗ scheme
- The F₁₇ resonance is the only non-strange state below 2000 MeV missing from the new scheme
- The mass centers of O(4) multiplets show approximately equidistant spacing of about 200 MeV to 300 MeV
- Maximal mass splitting within the 2₁₊, 2₃₊, 4₁₋, and 4₃₋ partial waves is 50-70 MeV
- The averaged masses of the Lorentz multiplets are fitted by a Balmer-series-like formula with parameters m₁ = 600 MeV and m₂ = 70 MeV
- The parameter 1/m₂ = 2.82 fm corresponds to an effective inertial moment with mass M = 1085 MeV and radius R = 1.13 fm
- The S₁₁(1535) and D₁₃(1520) states are separated by only 15 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Höhler and Sabba-Stefanescu (1970s) who discovered pole clustering in baryon spectra, and challenges the traditional SU(6)ₛ𝒻 ⊗ O(3)ₗ classification scheme reviewed by Particle Data Group and Bhaduri (1990s). The O(4) clustering was previously noticed by Hosaka (1990s) using a deformed harmonic oscillator quark model, but that scheme left several states unaccounted for. The quark-hyperquark model differs from customary quark-diquark models reviewed by Anselmino (1990s) by assuming O(4) clustering and a parity selection rule. The work enables straightforward covariant description of baryon clusters via Rarita-Schwinger propagators and suggests that the baryon spectrum generating algebra is su(2)𝐼 ⊗ su(3)𝑐 ⊗ o(1,3)ₗₛ rather than su(6)ₛ𝒻 ⊗ su(3)𝑐 ⊗ o(3)ₗ.
