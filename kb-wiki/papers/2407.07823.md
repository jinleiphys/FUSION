# Bayesian Inference of Fine-Features of Nuclear Equation of State from Future Neutron Star Radius Measurements to 0.1km Accuracy

> To more precisely constrain the Equation of State (EOS) of supradense neutron-rich nuclear matter, future high-precision X-ray and gravitational wave observatories are proposed to measure the radii of neutron stars (NSs) with an accuracy better than about 0.1 km. However, it remains unclear what particular aspects (other than the stiffness generally spoken of in the literature) of the EOS and to what precision they will be better constrained. In this work, within a Bayesian framework using a meta-model EOS for NSs, we infer the posterior probability distribution functions (PDFs) of incompressibility $K_{0}$ and skewness $J_{0}$ of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) as well as the slope $L$, curvature $K_{\rm{sym}}$, and skewness $J_{\rm{sym}}$ characterizing the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$, respectively, from mean values of NS radii consistent with existing observations and an expected accuracy $ΔR$ ranging from about 1.0 km to 0.1 km. We found that (1) the $ΔR$ has little effect on inferring the stiffness of SNM at suprasaturation densities, (2) smaller $ΔR$ reveals more accurately not only the PDFs but also pairwise correlations among parameters characterizing high-density $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$, (3) a double-peak feature of the PDF($K_{\rm{sym}}$) corresponding to the strong $K_{\rm{sym}}-J_{\rm{sym}}$ and $K_{\rm{sym}}-L$ anti-correlations is revealed when $ΔR$ is less than about 0.2 km, and the locations of the two peaks are sensitive to the maximum value of $J_{\rm{sym}}$ reflecting the stiffness of $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ above about 3 times the saturation density $ρ_0$ of SNM, (4) the high-precision radius measurement for canonical NSs is more useful than that for massive ones for constraining the EOS of nucleonic matter around $(2-3)ρ_0$.

## Key claim

Future neutron star radius measurements with accuracy down to 0.1 km will primarily improve constraints on the high-density behavior of nuclear symmetry energy (specifically the curvature $K_{\rm{sym}}$ and skewness $J_{\rm{sym}}$) rather than the stiffness of symmetric nuclear matter, and a double-peak feature in the posterior of $K_{\rm{sym}}$ emerges only when radius accuracy $\Delta R$ is better than about 0.2 km.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian inference using a meta-model equation of state for neutron stars composed of neutrons, protons, electrons, and muons at beta-equilibrium, parameterized by six nuclear matter properties: incompressibility $K_0$ and skewness $J_0$ of symmetric nuclear matter, plus the magnitude $E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)$, slope $L$, curvature $K_{\rm{sym}}$, and skewness $J_{\rm{sym}}$ of the symmetry energy. They generated posterior probability distribution functions for these parameters from existing neutron star radius data combined with simulated future measurements having accuracy $\Delta R$ varying from 1.0 km down to 0.1 km, using Markov-Chain Monte Carlo sampling with uniform priors over ranges listed in Table 1 of the paper.

## Key numbers

- The mean radius of a canonical (1.4 solar mass) neutron star from current literature is $R_{1.4}=12.0\pm 1.13$ km at 68% credibility
- The prior range for $K_0$ is 220 to 260 MeV; for $J_0$ is -400 to 400 MeV; for $K_{\rm{sym}}$ is -400 to 100 MeV; for $J_{\rm{sym}}$ is -200 to 800 MeV; for $L$ is 30 to 90 MeV; for $E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)$ is 28.5 to 34.9 MeV
- The double-peak feature of the posterior PDF($K_{\rm{sym}}$) is revealed when $\Delta R$ is less than about 0.2 km
- The transition density $\rho_t$ is most sensitive to the variation of $K_{\rm{sym}}$ among the EOS parameters
- The crust thickness uncertainty contributes 0.1 to 0.7 km to radius prediction uncertainty depending on the crust model

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian analyses of neutron star radii from the GW170817 era (Abbott 2018, De 2018) and NICER observations (Riley 2019, Miller 2019, Fonseca 2021) by systematically investigating how improved radius precision down to 0.1 km will refine constraints on specific high-density EOS parameters. The study builds on earlier findings by Lattimer (2000) that canonical neutron star radii scale with pressure around (1-2)$\rho_0$, and by Jake (2020) using machine learning that the most important EOS parameters for $R_{1.4}$ are $K_{\rm{sym}}$, $L$, $J_{\rm{sym}}$, $J_0$, $K_0$, and $E_{\rm{sym}}(\rho_0)$ in decreasing order. The results provide guidance for next-generation X-ray observatories (eXTP, STROBE-X) and gravitational-wave detectors (Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer) as well as for laboratory experiments with high-energy radioactive beams.
