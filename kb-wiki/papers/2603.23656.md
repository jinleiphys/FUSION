# Information-Geometric Quantum Process Tomography of Single Qubit Systems

> We establish an exact information-geometric inequality that remains valid regardless of the underlying dynamics, encompassing both Markovian and non-Markovian evolutions within the mixed-state domain. This inequality can be viewed as an extension of thermodynamic speed limits, which are typically formulated as inequalities. For single qubits, we show that this inequality saturates into a strict equality because the density matrix belongs to the quantum exponential family with the Pauli matrices serving as sufficient statistics. From a practical perspective, this identity enables a non-iterative linear regression approach to continuous-time quantum process tomography, bypassing the local minima issues common in non-linear optimization. We demonstrate the efficiency of this method by estimating the Hamiltonian and dissipation parameters of the Gorini-Kossakowski-Sudarshan-Lindblad (GKSL) master equation. Numerical simulations confirm the validity of this geometric estimator and highlight the necessity of error mitigation near the pure-state boundary where the inverse metric becomes singular.

## Key claim

The paper establishes an exact information-geometric equality for single-qubit systems that replaces the usual inequality form of thermodynamic speed limits, but this equality holds only when the density matrix remains strictly mixed (full-rank) and the dynamics produce a differentiable trajectory confined to the interior of the Bloch sphere.

## Method

The authors project the total score operator (logarithmic time derivative of the density matrix) onto the subspace spanned by fluctuations of the Pauli matrices, which serve as sufficient statistics for the quantum exponential family of a single qubit. Using the Bogoliubov-Kubo-Mori (BKM) metric and the canonical correlation inner product, they derive an orthogonal decomposition that becomes an exact equality for qubits because the Pauli matrices capture all information about the state evolution. This geometric identity is then applied to the Gorini-Kossakowski-Sudarshan-Lindblad (GKSL) master equation, where the linear structure of the Bloch-vector equation of motion enables a non-iterative linear regression approach to estimate the Hamiltonian vector and dissipation matrix parameters from time-series data.

## Key numbers

* The BKM metric for a single qubit is given by \( g_{\mu\nu}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{|\boldsymbol{a}|}{\text{arctanh}(|\boldsymbol{a}|)} \left( \delta_{\mu\nu} - \frac{a_\mu a_\nu}{|\boldsymbol{a}|^2} \right) + (1-|\boldsymbol{a}|^2) \frac{a_\mu a_\nu}{|\boldsymbol{a}|^2} \), where \(|\boldsymbol{a}|\) is the Bloch vector magnitude (Eq. 4.5 in the text).
* The inverse BKM metric is \( g^{-1}_{\mu\nu}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{\text{arctanh}(|\boldsymbol{a}|)}{|\boldsymbol{a}|} \left( \delta_{\mu\nu} - \frac{a_\mu a_\nu}{|\boldsymbol{a}|^2} \right) + \frac{1}{1-|\boldsymbol{a}|^2} \frac{a_\mu a_\nu}{|\boldsymbol{a}|^2} \).
* The universal geometric identity for a single qubit is \( \frac{1}{1-|\boldsymbol{a}|^2}\left(\frac{d|\boldsymbol{a}|}{dt}\right)^2 + \frac{\text{arctanh}(|\boldsymbol{a}|)}{|\boldsymbol{a}|^3} |\boldsymbol{a} \times \dot{\boldsymbol{a}}|^2 = \lim_{dt\to 0} \frac{2 D_{RE}[\hat{\rho}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)) || \hat{\rho}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t+dt))]}{(dt)^2} \) (Eq. 4.6 in the text).
* The GKSL Bloch-vector equation is \( \dot{\boldsymbol{a}} = 2\boldsymbol{e} \times \boldsymbol{a} - 2D_r \boldsymbol{a} \), where \( D_r = \operatorname{diag}(d_1, d_2, d_3) \) is the dissipation constant matrix (Eq. 5.1 in the text).

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic speed limits literature, which typically formulates inequalities bounding the rate of state evolution (Ito and Dechant, 2020; Shiraishi and colleagues, 2018; Nicholson and colleagues, 2020), by showing that for single qubits the inequality saturates to a strict equality due to the exponential-family structure of the density matrix. The result enables a linear regression approach to continuous-time quantum process tomography that avoids the local minima problems of maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) methods used in prior work (Fiurasek, 2001; Sacchi, 2001; Childs and colleagues, 2001; Jezek and colleagues, 2003; Altepeter and colleagues, 2003; O'Brien and colleagues, 2004; Ziman and colleagues, 2005; Mohseni and colleagues, 2008; Anis and colleagues, 2012; Merkel and colleagues, 2013; Samach and colleagues, 2022). The method specifically exploits the BKM metric rather than the symmetric logarithmic derivative (SLD) metric standard in quantum estimation theory (Helstrom, 1976), because the BKM metric's Hessian structure linearizes the inverse problem. This approach can be applied to estimate Hamiltonian and dissipation parameters of the GKSL master equation from time-series data, with numerical simulations demonstrating the need for error mitigation near the pure-state boundary where the inverse metric becomes singular.
