# The sign of the overlap of HFB wave functions

> The problem of how to compute accurately and efficiently the sign of the overlap between two general HFB wave functions is addressed. The results obtained can easily be extrapolated to the evaluation of the sign of the trace of a density operator exponential of one body operators.

## Key claim

The sign of the overlap between two general HFB wave functions can be computed unambiguously using a pfaffian of a skew-symmetric matrix, avoiding the sign ambiguity present in the standard Onishi formula and the numerical difficulties of Neergard's method based on pairwise degenerate eigenvalues of non-hermitian matrices.

## Method

The author uses fermion coherent states and Grassmann algebra to express the overlap integral as a Gaussian integral over Grassmann variables. The integral is evaluated by transforming the associated skew-symmetric matrix to canonical form, yielding an expression in terms of its pfaffian rather than the square root of a determinant. The same technique is extended to evaluate the trace of statistical density operators of the exponential-of-one-body-operator form.

## Key numbers

- The overlap formula is given by ⟨φ₀|φ₁⟩ = s_N pf(M), where s_N = (-1)^{N(N+1)/2} and M is the 2N×2N skew-symmetric matrix [[M^(1), -1], [1, -M^(0)*]].
- For HFB states sharing a discrete symmetry (simplex or time reversal), the overlap reduces to ⟨φ₀|φ₁⟩ = det(1 + M̅^(0)† M̅^(1)).
- The trace of a statistical density operator D̂ is expressed as Tr(D̂) = s_N (|det T₂₂|^{1/2} / det T₂₂) pf([[T₁₂T₂₂⁻¹, -(T₂₂ᵀ+1)], [(T₂₂+1), T₂₁T₂₂ᵀ]]).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Neergard (1983) for computing HFB overlap signs, which required handling pairwise degenerate eigenvalues of non-hermitian matrices and was difficult to implement numerically for wave functions breaking simplex symmetry. The pfaffian method provides a simpler numerical alternative that avoids both the eigenvalue difficulties and the uncertainties of continuity arguments used in prior calculations by Hara (1982), Oi (2005), and Bender (2008). The result enables practical implementation of triaxial angular momentum projection and symmetry restoration at finite temperature, and can help locate zeros of HFB overlaps to address the "pole problem" in beyond-mean-field calculations.
