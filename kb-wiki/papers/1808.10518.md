# Addressing the Majorana vs. Dirac Question with Neutrino Decays

> The Majorana versus Dirac nature of neutrinos remains an open question. This is due, in part, to the fact that virtually all the experimentally accessible neutrinos are ultra-relativistic. Noting that Majorana neutrinos can behave quite differently from Dirac ones when they are non-relativistic, we show that, at leading order, the angular distribution of the daughters in the decay of a heavy neutrino into a lighter one and a self-conjugate boson is isotropic in the parent's rest frame if the neutrinos are Majorana, independent of the parent's polarization. If the neutrinos are Dirac fermions, this is, in general, not the case. This result follows from CPT invariance and is independent of the details of the physics responsible for the decay. We explore the feasibility of using these angular distributions -- or, equivalently, the energy distributions of the daughters in the laboratory frame -- in order to address the Majorana versus Dirac nature of neutrinos if a fourth, heavier neutrino mass eigenstate reveals itself in the current or next-generation of high-energy colliders, intense meson facilities, or neutrino beam experiments.

# Addressing the Majorana vs. Dirac Question with Neutrino Decays

## Key claim

The angular distribution of the daughters in the decay of a heavy neutrino into a lighter one and a self-conjugate boson is isotropic in the parent's rest frame if the neutrinos are Majorana fermions, independent of the parent's polarization, while this isotropy does not generally hold for Dirac fermions. This result follows from CPT invariance and holds at leading order in perturbation theory, independent of the details of the physics responsible for the decay.

## Method

The authors derive the angular distribution of the decay products in the two-body decay N → ν_l + X, where X is a self-conjugate boson (e.g., γ, π⁰, ρ⁰, Z⁰, H⁰), using rotational invariance and CPT invariance of the interaction Hamiltonian at leading order. They compute the asymmetry parameter α for different decay modes assuming Standard Model weak interactions, and translate the rest-frame angular distributions into laboratory-frame energy distributions of the daughter boson X. They also consider the related decays N → ℓ_α^∓ + X^± for detectors without charge discrimination.

## Key numbers

- For the decay ν₄ → ν_l + π⁰, the asymmetry parameter α = 1 if neutrinos are Dirac fermions, yielding a (1+cosθ) angular distribution in the parent rest frame.
- For ν₄ → ν_l + H⁰, the asymmetry parameter α = 1 if neutrinos are Dirac fermions.
- For ν₄ → ν_l + ρ⁰, the asymmetry parameter α = (m₄² − 2m_ρ²)/(m₄² + 2m_ρ²) if neutrinos are Dirac fermions, vanishing when m₄ = √2 m_ρ.
- For ν₄ → ν_l + Z⁰, the asymmetry parameter α = (m₄² − 2m_Z²)/(m₄² + 2m_Z²) if neutrinos are Dirac fermions.
- For ν₄ → ν_l + γ, the asymmetry parameter α = 2Im(μd*)/(|μ|²+|d|²) if neutrinos are Dirac fermions, where μ and d are magnetic and electric transition dipole moments.
- For a heavy neutrino with m₄ = 300 MeV, m_X = 100 MeV, and a flat parent energy distribution from 500 MeV to 1000 MeV, the laboratory energy distribution of X is flat for A=0 (Majorana case) between 132 MeV and 456 MeV, while for A=±1 (Dirac case) the distribution has positive or negative slope.
- Estimated heavy neutrino lifetimes: cτ₄ ≳ 10⁹ m for m₄ = 10 MeV, cτ₄ ≳ 10³ m for m₄ = 100 MeV, and cτ₄ ≳ 10 cm for m₄ = 500 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier observations by Li and Wilczek (1981) and Shrock (1982) regarding isotropy in the special case where X = γ, generalizing the result to any self-conjugate boson and showing it follows from CPT and rotational invariance alone. The paper contrasts with the traditional approach of searching for neutrinoless double beta decay (the most promising current method) and with proposals to use relic neutrino capture on tritium (Long, 2014), low-energy eγ → eνν̅ scattering (Berryman, 2018), and neutrino pair emission from excited atoms (Yoshimura, 2011; Fukumi, 2012; Dinh, 2012; Song, 2015), which suffer from exceedingly small rates. The proposed method enables distinguishing Majorana from Dirac neutrinos using heavy neutrino decays at current or next-generation colliders, intense meson facilities, or neutrino beam experiments, even in detectors without charge discrimination.
