# Symmetries and Algebraic Methods in Neutrino Physics

> Symmetry properties associated with neutrino propagation with or without a background of other particles, including neutrinos, is reviewed. The utility of symmetries is illustrated with examples chosen from the see-saw mechanism and both matter-enhanced and collective neutrino oscillations. The role of symmetries in neutrino astrophysics is highlighted.

# Symmetries and Algebraic Methods in Neutrino Physics

## Key claim
The paper reviews how algebraic structures, particularly SO(5) and SU(2) algebras, provide a unifying mathematical framework for understanding neutrino mass generation, mixing, and collective oscillations, but emphasizes that many results (such as the factorization of the CP-violating phase) hold only for three active flavors and break down when sterile neutrinos are introduced.

## Method
The author demonstrates that the Dirac mass term in the neutrino Hamiltonian is mathematically analogous to the linearized BCS Hamiltonian of superconductivity, with the mass term arising from a pairing interaction between left- and right-handed spinor components. The see-saw mechanism is shown to correspond to a Pauli-Gürsey SU(2) rotation within an SO(5) algebraic structure. For collective neutrino oscillations, the Hamiltonian in the single-angle approximation is mapped to a BCS-like form whose eigenvalues can be found via Bethe ansatz, with conserved quantities constructed from the generators of the SU(2) algebras associated with each neutrino momentum mode.

- The two experimentally measured neutrino mass-squared differences differ by two orders of magnitude: δm²₂₁ and δm²₃₂

## Context
This review extends the algebraic treatment of neutrino physics previously explored by Balantekin (2000) on the SO(5) structure of the see-saw mechanism and by Pehlivan (2011) on conserved quantities in collective oscillations. The work connects the mathematical analogy between particle mass and the pairing gap in BCS theory originally noted by Nambu and Jona-Lasinio (1961). The algebraic methods presented enable exact solutions for collective neutrino oscillations via Bethe ansatz techniques and provide a framework for understanding spectral splits and swappings observed in mean-field calculations by Raffelt (2007) and Duan (2008).
