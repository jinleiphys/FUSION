# Microscopic-macroscopic level densities for low excitation energies

> Level density $ρ(E,{\bf Q})$ is derived within the micro-macroscopic approximation (MMA) for a system of strongly interacting Fermi particles with the energy $E$ and additional integrals of motion ${\bf Q}$, in line with several topics of the universal and fruitful activity of A.S. Davydov. Within the extended Thomas Fermi and semiclassical periodic orbit theory beyond the Fermi-gas saddle-point method we obtain $ρ\propto I_ν(S)/S^ν$, where $I_ν(S)$ is the modified Bessel function of the entropy $S$. For small shell-structure contribution one finds $ν=κ/2+1$, where $κ$ is the number of additional integrals of motion. This integer number is a dimension of ${\bf Q}$, ${\bf Q}=\{N, Z, ...\}$ for the case of two-component atomic nuclei, where $N$ and $Z$ are the numbers of neutron and protons, respectively. For much larger shell structure contributions, one obtains, $ν=κ/2+2$. The MMA level density $ρ$ reaches the well-known Fermi gas asymptote for large excitation energies, and the finite micro-canonical combinatoric limit for low excitation energies. The additional integrals of motion can be also the projection of the angular momentum of a nuclear system for nuclear rotations of deformed nuclei, number of excitons for collective dynamics, and so on. Fitting the MMA total level density, $ρ(E,{\bf Q})$, for a set of the integrals of motion ${\bf Q}=\{N, Z\}$, to experimental data on a long nuclear isotope chain for low excitation energies, one obtains the results for the inverse level-density parameter $K$, which differs significantly from those of neutron resonances, due to shell, isotopic asymmetry, and pairing effects.

## Key claim

The paper derives a microscopic-macroscopic (MMA) level density formula that removes the singularity of the standard Fermi-gas saddle-point method at low excitation energies, yielding a nonsingular analytical expression that reduces to the Fermi-gas asymptote at high energies and to the finite micro-canonical combinatoric limit at low energies, with the exponent parameter ν depending on both the number of additional integrals of motion κ and the magnitude of shell-structure contributions.

## Method

The authors use the extended Thomas-Fermi and semiclassical periodic-orbit theory (POT) to decompose the single-particle level density into smooth and oscillating shell components, then derive the level density ρ(E,Q) through an inverse Laplace transformation of the partition function that goes beyond the standard saddle-point method. For the integration over the Lagrange multiplier β (conjugate to energy), they employ a more accurate treatment that avoids the saddle-point approximation, while applying the saddle-point method for the other integrals of motion (neutron number N, proton number Z, and angular momentum projection M). The resulting MMA level density is expressed in terms of modified Bessel functions Iν(S) of the entropy S, and is fitted to experimental data for a long chain of deformed isotopes to extract the inverse level-density parameter K.

## Key numbers

* The level density exponent ν = κ/2 + 1 for small shell-structure contributions, and ν = κ/2 + 2 for much larger shell-structure contributions, where κ is the number of additional integrals of motion (e.g., κ = 3 for Q = {N, Z, M}).
* The smooth (extended Thomas-Fermi) level density parameter component is given by ãτ ≈ (π²/6) g^(τ)_ETF(λτ), and the oscillating shell correction is δaτ ≈ (π²/6) δg^(τ)_scl(λτ).
* The Jacobian determinant for the saddle-point integration over λn and λp yields J0 = gn(λn) gp(λp) ≈ g²/4 for small asymmetry, and J2 = a''p gn(λn) + a''n gp(λp) ≈ (π²/12) g'' g.
* The parameter ξ = ξ̄/β² with ξ̄τ ≈ - (2π⁴ δgτ)/(3 ĝτ D²τ), where Dτ = λτ/A^(1/3) is approximately the distance between major shells.
* The energy shell correction δEτ is related to the oscillating level density component by δE^(τ)_scl ≈ (Dτ/(2π))² δgτ(λτ).

## Context

This work extends the MMA approach originally suggested by Kolomietz, Magner, and collaborators (1979) for describing level densities in finite Fermi systems, building on the Strutinsky shell correction method and the Landau-Migdal quasiparticle theory. The formalism generalizes previous treatments (Magner et al., NPA, PRC, IJMPE) to include shell, isotopic asymmetry, and pairing effects for low excitation energies, and provides an analytical alternative to the constant temperature model (CTM) of Gilbert and Cameron (1965) and to microscopic approaches such as the Monte-Carlo Shell Model (Otsuka et al., 1997) and the moments method (Karataglidis et al., 2016). The results enable systematic fitting of the inverse level-density parameter K across long isotope chains, with applications to nuclear fission and high-spin rotational states.
