# Phenomenological study of the anisotropic quark matter in the 2-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model

> With the two flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model we carry out a phenomenological study on the chiral phase structure, mesonic properties and transport properties in a momentum-space anisotropic quark matter. To calculate transport coefficients we have utilized the kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, where the momentum anisotropy is embedded in the estimation of both distribution function and the relaxation time. It is shown that an increase of the anisotropy parameter $ξ$ may results in a catalysis of chiral symmetry breaking. The critical endpoint (CEP) is shifted to smaller temperatures and larger quark chemical potentials as $ξ$ increases, the impact of momentum anisotropy on temperature of CEP is almost the same as that on the quark chemical potential of CEP. The meson masses and the associated decay widths also exhibit a significant $ξ$ dependence. It is observed that the temperature behavior of scaled shear viscosity $η/T^3$ and scaled electrical conductivity $σ_{el}/T$ exhibit a similar dip structure, with the minima of both $η/T^3$ and $σ_{el}/T$ shifting toward higher temperatures with increasing $ξ$. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the Seebeck coefficient $S$ decreases when temperature goes up and its sign is positive, indicating the dominant carriers for converting the temperature gradient to the electric field are up-quarks. The Seebeck coefficient $S$ is significantly enhanced with a large $ξ$ for the temperature below the critical temperature.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a small momentum-space anisotropy parameter ξ (with |ξ|≪1) in the 2-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model catalyzes chiral symmetry breaking and shifts the critical endpoint (CEP) to lower temperatures and larger quark chemical potentials, with the caveat that the analysis is restricted to weakly anisotropic matter close to equilibrium where the distribution function is expanded to linear order in ξ.

## Method

The authors employed the SU(2) NJL model in the mean-field approximation, incorporating momentum-space anisotropy via the Romatschke-Strickland parametrization where the isotropic Fermi-Dirac distribution is deformed by rescaling one preferred direction. Transport coefficients (shear viscosity, electrical conductivity, Seebeck coefficient) were calculated using kinetic theory within the relaxation time approximation, with the momentum anisotropy embedded in both the distribution function and the relaxation time estimation. The meson masses and decay widths were obtained from the pole of the meson propagator in the random phase approximation, using the anisotropic distribution function in the polarization functions.

## Key numbers

- The anisotropy parameter ξ ≈ 0.3 is estimated at RHIC energy with critical temperature Tc ≈ 160 MeV, τ ≈ 6 fm/c, and η/s = 1/4π
- The critical endpoint shifts to smaller temperatures and larger quark chemical potentials as ξ increases, with the impact on temperature being almost the same as on quark chemical potential
- The minima of both scaled shear viscosity η/T³ and scaled electrical conductivity σ_el/T shift toward higher temperatures with increasing ξ
- The Seebeck coefficient S is positive (indicating up-quarks as dominant carriers) and decreases when temperature increases, with significant enhancement for large ξ at temperatures below the critical temperature

## Context

This work extends previous NJL model studies of QCD phase structure and transport properties (Zhuang 1995, Rehberg 1996) by incorporating momentum-space anisotropy relevant to heavy-ion collisions where rapid longitudinal expansion creates local momentum anisotropy. The Romatschke-Strickland parametrization (Romatschke and Strickland 2003) is used, which has been extensively applied to study anisotropic effects on photon production, parton self-energy, heavy-quark potential, and transport coefficients. The results enable future self-consistent studies solving the Boltzmann-Vlasov transport equation together with the gap equation for space-time dependent quark distributions, and provide input for relativistic anisotropic hydrodynamics models.
