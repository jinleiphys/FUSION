# SMASH -- A new hadronic transport approach

> Microscopic transport approaches are the tool to describe the non-equilibrium evolution in low energy collisions as well as in the late dilute stages of high-energy collisions. Here, a newly developed hadronic transport approach, SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) is introduced. The overall bulk dynamics in low energy heavy ion collisions is shown including the excitation function of elliptic flow employing several equations of state. The implications of this new approach for dilepton production are discussed and preliminary results for afterburner calculations at the highest RHIC energy are presented and compared to previous UrQMD results. A detailed understanding of a hadron gas with vacuum properties is required to establish the baseline for the exploration of the transition to the quark-gluon plasma in heavy ion collisions at high net baryon densities.

## Key claim

SMASH is a new hadronic transport approach that describes bulk observables and dilepton emission at low beam energies and can be employed for the late stage hadronic rescattering at high energies, with results comparable to the established UrQMD transport approach; however, SMASH does not reach the same magnitude of effects as UrQMD for hadronic rescattering at the highest RHIC energy due to missing additional baryon-antibaryon annihilation processes and cross-sections from the additive quark model for exotic combinations of hadrons.

## Method

The SMASH approach incorporates well-established mesons and baryons up to a mass of 2 GeV as degrees of freedom, with binary interactions occurring via resonance excitation and decay or string excitation and fragmentation, using a geometric collision criterion. The approach was validated by reproducing an analytic solution of the Boltzmann equation within a Friedmann-Robertson-Walker metric, and by comparing cross-sections for elementary reactions to experimental data and checking detailed balance in infinite matter calculations. Bulk dynamics were studied by calculating the excitation function of elliptic flow employing several equations of state, including a Skyrme mean field corresponding to two different compressibilities.

## Key numbers

*   Pion production in Au+Au collisions at kinetic energies of 1-2 A GeV describes experimental data reasonably, with nuclear potentials slightly reducing pion production, Fermi motion increasing it, and Pauli blocking reducing particle production due to forbidden reactions.
*   The excitation function of elliptic flow was calculated over a large energy range, showing that a harder equation of state reproduces the flow at low energies, with default values chosen according to Xu (2016).
*   Dilepton production as a function of invariant mass was studied in carbon-carbon collisions at a laboratory energy of 2 A GeV, where branching ratios contributing to the rho meson peak were adjusted and dilepton emission by vector mesons below the hadronic threshold was included, improving agreement in the low mass region.
*   For hadronic rescattering in Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, protons receive an increase of transverse momentum and the mass splitting of elliptic flow is increased significantly, but SMASH does not reach the same magnitude of effects as UrQMD.

## Context

This work extends the line of hadronic transport approaches such as UrQMD (Bass 1998, Bleicher 1999) and others (Buss 2011, Nara 2016, Cassing 2009) by providing a flexible, modular approach that condenses knowledge from existing approaches and incorporates new experimental data for elementary cross-sections and branching ratios. The shear viscosity over entropy ratio as a function of temperature and net baryon chemical potential agrees well with the previous UrQMD result (Rose 2017). The approach enables baseline calculations with hadronic vacuum properties to identify signals of the phase transition to the quark-gluon plasma in heavy ion collisions at high net baryon densities, and can be used for forced canonical thermalization in certain phase-space regions as a proxy for multi-particle collisions (Oliinychenko 2016).
