# Global microscopic calculations of ground-state spin and parity for odd-mass nuclei

> Systematic calculations of ground-state spin and parity of odd-mass nuclei have been performed within the Hartree--Fock--BCS (HFBCS) approach and the Finite-Range Droplet Model for nuclei for which experimental data are available. The unpaired nucleon has been treated perturbatively, and axial and left-right reflection symmetries have been assumed. As for the HFBCS approach, three different Skyrme forces have been used in the particle-hole channel, whereas the particle-particle matrix elements have been approximated by a seniority force. The calculations have been done for the 621 nuclei for which the Nubase 2003 data set give assignments of spin and parity with strong arguments. The agreement of both spin and parity in the self-consistent model reaches about 80% for spherical nuclei, and about 40% for well-deformed nuclei regardless of the Skyrme force used. As for the macroscopic-microscopic approach, the agreement for spherical nuclei is about 90% and about 40% for well-deformed nuclei, with different sets of spherical and deformed nuclei found in each model.

## Key claim

Systematic calculations of ground-state spin and parity for 621 odd-mass nuclei using the Hartree-Fock-BCS (HFBCS) approach with three Skyrme forces achieve about 80% agreement for spherical nuclei and about 40% for well-deformed nuclei, while the Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM) achieves about 90% for spherical nuclei and about 40% for deformed nuclei, but with different sets of spherical and deformed nuclei found in each model.

## Method

The authors performed global calculations of ground-state spin and parity for odd-mass nuclei using two approaches: the Hartree-Fock-BCS (HFBCS) self-consistent mean-field method with three Skyrme forces (SIII, SkM*, SLy4) in the particle-hole channel and a seniority force for pairing, and the macroscopic-microscopic Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM). The unpaired nucleon was treated perturbatively, axial and left-right reflection symmetries were assumed, and for deformed nuclei the rotor-plus-quasiparticle approximation was used with the equal-filling approximation to obtain time-even states. The calculations were compared against the 621 nuclei in the Nubase 2003 database with strong spin and parity assignments, excluding nuclei with transitional deformations (0.01 < β₂ < 0.1) or shape coexistence (energy difference between minima less than 2 MeV).

## Key numbers

- HFBCS with SIII: 83.9% agreement for spherical nuclei (183/218), 40.5% for well-deformed nuclei (60/148), 66.4% total (243/366)
- HFBCS with SkM*: 76.2% agreement for spherical nuclei (218/286), 37.5% for well-deformed nuclei (54/144), 63.3% total (272/430)
- HFBCS with SLy4: 77.8% agreement for spherical nuclei (186/239), 39.3% for well-deformed nuclei (57/140), 64.1% total (243/379)
- FRDM: 90.9% agreement for spherical nuclei (90/99), 43.1% for well-deformed nuclei (137/318), 54.4% total (227/417)
- When considering either of the two lowest quasiparticle excitations in HFBCS, agreement for SIII reaches 90.8% for spherical and 61.5% for deformed nuclei
- Pairing strengths used: g_n = 17 MeV/(11 + N) and g_p = 17 MeV/(11 + Z) as starting values
- Basis size N₀ linearly interpolated between 12 for A=10 and 18 for A=260, with oscillator parameter b₀ = 0.475 fm⁻¹

## Context

This work extends earlier local studies of ground-state spectroscopic properties in limited mass regions (Daniere; Libert, Meyer, and Quentin) to a global systematic comparison across the entire nuclear chart. The HFBCS approach builds on previous fission studies for even-even heavy and light nuclei using the SkM* interaction, while the FRDM comparison follows the earlier spin and parity calculations by Moller (1990). The results enable confidence in the predictive power of these mean-field approaches for applications such as studies of exotic neutron-rich nuclei, though the authors note that improvements should include more sophisticated treatment of the coupling between single-particle and collective degrees of freedom.
