# The Microscopic Approach to Nuclear Matter and Neutron Star Matter

> We review a variety of theoretical and experimental investigations aimed at improving our knowledge of the nuclear matter equation of state. Of particular interest are nuclear matter extreme states in terms of density and/or isospin asymmetry. The equation of state of matter with unequal concentrations of protons and neutrons has numerous applications. These include heavy-ion collisions, the physics of rare, short-lived nuclei and, on a dramatically different scale, the physics of neutron stars. The "common denominator" among these (seemingly) very different systems is the symmetry energy, which plays a crucial role in both the formation of the neutron skin in neutron-rich nuclei and the radius of a neutron star (a system 18 orders of magnitude larger and 55 orders of magnitude heavier). The details of the density dependence of the symmetry energy are not yet sufficiently constrained. Throughout this article, our emphasis will be on the importance of adopting a microscopic approach to the many-body problem, which we believe to be the one with true predictive power.

# The Microscopic Approach to Nuclear Matter and Neutron Star Matter

## Key claim

The Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF) approach, which uses realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials with parameters fixed from free-space scattering and never readjusted in the medium, can describe nuclear matter saturation properties and the equation of state across a wide density range, but the density dependence of the symmetry energy remains insufficiently constrained, especially at high densities.

## Method

The author reviews the DBHF approach to symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter, where the in-medium reaction matrix (G-matrix) is solved self-consistently from the Thompson relativistic three-dimensional reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter equation using the Bonn B one-boson-exchange potential with pseudovector coupling. The single-particle potential for neutrons and protons is parametrized in terms of scalar and vector fields (U_S and U_V^0), which are determined iteratively by fitting the G-matrix integrated over the Fermi sea. The energy per nucleon is then calculated from the kinetic and potential contributions, with the equation of state obtained as a function of density and asymmetry parameter alpha.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density for symmetric nuclear matter from the Idaho DBHF calculation: 0.185 fm^{-3}
- Saturation energy for symmetric nuclear matter from the Idaho DBHF calculation: -16.14 MeV
- Compression modulus from the Idaho DBHF calculation: 252 MeV
- Saturation density from the other DBHF model (Fuchs et al.): 0.181 fm^{-3}
- Saturation energy from the other DBHF model (Fuchs et al.): -16.15 MeV
- Compression modulus from the other DBHF model (Fuchs et al.): 230 MeV
- Typical value of the symmetry energy at nuclear matter density: 30 MeV, with theoretical predictions spreading between approximately 26 and 35 MeV
- Parametrization of the symmetry energy: e_sym(rho) = 12.5 MeV (rho/rho_0)^{2/3} + 17.5 MeV (rho/rho_0)^{gamma_i}, where gamma_i is found to be between 0.4 and 1.0
- The repulsive effect from the Z-diagram (three-body force) on the energy per particle in symmetric nuclear matter scales approximately as (rho/rho_0)^{8/3}

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the nuclear matter equation of state using microscopic many-body methods, building on earlier Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory (Brueckner and others, 1950s-1960s) and the Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach developed in the 1980s (Brockmann and Machleidt, 1984). The author compares DBHF predictions with relativistic mean-field models using density-dependent couplings (DD-RMF) and non-linear self-interactions (NL-RMF), and with constraints from heavy-ion flow data (Danielewicz et al., 2002). The work enables improved predictions for neutron star structure and for the interpretation of heavy-ion collision experiments at facilities such as FRIB, GSI, and MSU, particularly regarding the density dependence of the symmetry energy.
