# Neutron Star Equation of State in light of GW190814

> The observation of gravitational waves from an asymmetric binary opens the possibility for heavy neutron stars, but these pose challenges to models of the neutron star equation of state. We construct heavy neutron stars by introducing non-trivial structure in the speed of sound sourced by deconfined QCD matter, which cannot be well recovered by spectral representations. Their moment of inertia, Love number and quadrupole moment are very small, so a tenfold increase in sensitivity may be needed to test this possibility with gravitational waves, which is feasible with third generation detectors.

## Key claim

If GW190814 involved a black hole and a neutron star, the neutron star equation of state must contain non-trivial structure in the speed of sound between about 1.5 and 3 times nuclear saturation density, but this structure cannot be well recovered by the spectral parameterizations typically used in gravitational wave data analysis.

## Method

The authors constructed an infinite family of neutron star equations of state by parameterizing the speed of sound \(c_s^2\) with a wide variety of functional forms (linear, quadratic, hyperbolic tangent, oscillatory, double-peak) above a fixed SLy crust up to \(2 n_{sat}\). They imposed three restrictions: causality (\(c_s^2 \leq 1\)), maximum mass \(M_{\max} > 2.5 M_\odot\), and consistency with existing radius and tidal deformability constraints from LVC and NICER. They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for mass-radius relations and the Einstein equations in the Hartle-Thorne slow-rotation approximation to compute moment of inertia, Love number, and rotational quadrupole moment.

## Key numbers

- The compact object in GW190814 has mass \(M \approx (2.5-2.67) M_\odot\).
- All constructed equations of state reach a maximum mass of at least \(M_{\max} \geq 2.5 M_\odot\).
- For a \(2.5 M_\odot\) star: moment of inertia \(I/M^3 \in (4.95, 6.4)\); rotational quadrupole moment \(Q/(M^3 a^2) \in (1.43, 2.16)\); tidal deformability \(\Lambda \in (3.23, 18.88)\).
- The tidal deformability drops to \(\Lambda < 35\) for \(M > 2.5 M_\odot\).
- Current gravitational wave measurements resolve \(\Lambda\) for a \(1.4 M_\odot\) star to roughly \(\delta \Lambda \approx (100-400)\) at 90% confidence.
- The relative fractional variability in I-Love-Q relations across the EOS family is \(\lesssim 1\%\).
- Maximum central baryon densities of the EOS family lie in the range \(n_B/n_{sat} \sim 2-4\), with extremes down to 1.5 and up to 5.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of heavy neutron stars following the GW190814 detection by the LIGO/Virgo Collaboration (Abbott 2020), challenging the assumption that the secondary object must be a black hole based on maximum mass estimates of \((2.3-2.4) M_\odot\) from earlier work (Margalit 2017, Rezzolla 2017). The authors build on prior parameterized speed-of-sound approaches (Tews 2018, Annala 2019) and show that spectral parameterizations (Lindblom 2010, 2012, 2013) cannot capture the kink-prone structure needed for such massive stars. The results enable future tests with third-generation detectors like Cosmic Explorer or Einstein Telescope, which could achieve the tenfold increase in sensitivity needed to measure the very small tidal deformabilities of these heavy neutron stars.
