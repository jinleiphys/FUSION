# Coordinate-Space Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov Solvers for Superfluid Fermi Systems in Large Boxes

> The self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov problem in large boxes can be solved accurately in the coordinate space with the recently developed solvers HFB-AX (2D) and MADNESS-HFB (3D). This is essential for the description of superfluid Fermi systems with complicated topologies and significant spatial extend, such as fissioning nuclei, weakly-bound nuclei, nuclear matter in the neutron star rust, and ultracold Fermi atoms in elongated traps. The HFB-AX solver based on B-spline techniques uses a hybrid MPI and OpenMP programming model for parallel computation for distributed parallel computation, within a node multi-threaded LAPACK and BLAS libraries are used to further enable parallel calculations of large eigensystems. The MADNESS-HFB solver uses a novel multi-resolution analysis based adaptive pseudo-spectral techniques to enable fully parallel 3D calculations of very large systems. In this work we present benchmark results for HFB-AX and MADNESS-HFB on ultracold trapped fermions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two newly developed coordinate-space Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov solvers, HFB-AX (2D) and MADNESS-HFB (3D), can accurately solve the self-consistent HFB problem in large boxes for superfluid Fermi systems, but the benchmark results are specifically for ultracold trapped fermions rather than for nuclear systems directly.

## Method

The HFB-AX solver discretizes wave functions on a 2D grid using B-splines and diagonalizes the Hamiltonian matrix with a hybrid MPI and OpenMP parallel programming model, using threaded LAPACK and BLAS libraries. The MADNESS-HFB solver uses multiresolution analysis with adaptive pseudo-spectral techniques based on Alpert's multiwavelets and low-separation rank representations of Green's functions, employing a combined MPI and pthread parallel computing model with task graph scheduling. Both solvers were benchmarked by calculating pairing densities for 100 ultracold fermionic atoms in elongated traps described by the superfluid density functional SLDA.

## Key numbers

- For a heavy nucleus calculation with mesh size 0.6 fm, 10th order B-splines, and box size 24 fm, the rank of the Hamiltonian matrix is N=4608
- Threaded LAPACK library speeds up diagonalization by a factor of 3 with 6 threads
- MADNESS-HFB takes about 5 minutes per iteration on 5000 cores for both eta=5 and eta=16 trap aspect ratios
- HFB-AX takes about 10 minutes per iteration for eta=5 on 400 cores and 25 minutes for eta=16 on 800 cores
- In MADNESS-HFB, very large boxes were used: (x, y, z)[-100, 100] for eta=5 and [-160, 160] for eta=16
- In HFB-AX, 2D box sizes were 10.5x35 for eta=5 and 9.1x70 for eta=16
- Energy cutoff was taken as 6.75 for eta=5 and 5.05 for eta=16
- Trap aspect ratio eta up to 50 is used in experiments (cited from Par06a)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous development of HFB solvers for complicated geometries (Pei et al., earlier work) and provides a direct comparison between two complementary coordinate-space approaches. The solvers enable precise calculations for weakly-bound nuclei, fissioning nuclei, nuclear matter in neutron star crusts (referencing Newton et al.), and ultracold Fermi atoms in elongated traps, including the prediction of rapidly oscillating pairing potentials in highly elongated traps (Pei et al., 2010) indicative of the Larkin-Ovchinnikov phase. The paper also notes that many existing nuclear HFB solvers use harmonic oscillator basis expansion methods which offer poor accuracy for weakly-bound systems and large deformations (discussed in HFB-AX reference).
