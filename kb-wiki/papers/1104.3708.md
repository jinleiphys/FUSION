# Transport Properties of the Quark-Gluon Plasma -- A Lattice QCD Perspective

> Transport properties of a thermal medium determine how its conserved charge densities (for instance the electric charge, energy or momentum) evolve as a function of time and eventually relax back to their equilibrium values. Here the transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma are reviewed from a theoretical perspective. The latter play a key role in the description of heavy-ion collisions, and are an important ingredient in constraining particle production processes in the early universe. We place particular emphasis on lattice QCD calculations of conserved current correlators. These Euclidean correlators are related by an integral transform to spectral functions, whose small-frequency form determines the transport properties via Kubo formulae. The universal hydrodynamic predictions for the small-frequency pole structure of spectral functions are summarized. The viability of a quasiparticle description implies the presence of additional characteristic features in the spectral functions. These features are in stark contrast with the functional form that is found in strongly coupled plasmas via the gauge/gravity duality. A central goal is therefore to determine which of these dynamical regimes the quark-gluon plasma is qualitatively closer to as a function of temperature. We review the analysis of lattice correlators in relation to transport properties, and tentatively estimate what computational effort is required to make decisive progress in this field.

## Key claim

The quark-gluon plasma's transport properties, such as shear viscosity and relaxation times, can be extracted from lattice QCD Euclidean correlators via spectral functions, but the reconstruction is inherently ill-posed and requires strong prior analytic information; at temperatures relevant to heavy-ion collisions (a few times T_c), the relaxation times are not as long as weak-coupling kinetic theory predicts (1/g^4 T) nor as short as the strong-coupling N=4 SYM limit (τ_Π ≈ 1.3/2π T), with lattice fits suggesting intermediate values such as τ_Π ≈ 3.1/2π T at 1.58 T_c.

## Method

The paper reviews the determination of transport coefficients (shear viscosity η, bulk viscosity ζ, relaxation time τ_Π) from lattice QCD by computing Euclidean two-point correlators of conserved currents (vector current, energy-momentum tensor) and relating them via integral transforms to spectral functions ρ(ω), whose low-frequency behavior gives transport coefficients through Kubo formulas. The analysis combines analytic information from hydrodynamics (pole structure at low ω), perturbation theory and operator-product expansion (UV tail at ω ≫ T), and lattice Monte-Carlo data, using fit ansätze with a small number of parameters (e.g., 7 parameters fitted to 48 data points at T=2.3 T_c) or methods like maximum entropy, singular value decomposition, and Padé approximation to perform the analytic continuation from Euclidean to real frequencies.

- Lattice result for (η + 3ζ/4)/s at 2.32 T_c: 0.26(3)

- Static susceptibility in N=4 SYM: χ_s = N_c^2 T^2 / 8

- Entropy density in N=4 SYM: s = (π^2/2) N_c^2 T^3
- Estimated CPU time to achieve 10^{-3} relative accuracy on UV-subtracted force-force correlator at N_t=22: half a month on a 100 Tflops machine

## Context

This review extends the program of extracting transport coefficients from lattice QCD, building on the foundational work of Karsch and Wyld (1986) on analytic continuation and the maximum entropy method introduced by Gubernatis et al. (1991). It contrasts the spectral function structure found in weakly coupled kinetic theory (with narrow transport peaks) against the smooth, featureless spectral functions of strongly coupled N=4 SYM theory via the gauge/gravity duality (Policastro, Son, and Starinets 2001; Kovtun, Son, and Starinets 2006), and presents lattice results from Meyer (2009, 2010) that suggest the QGP at a few T_c lies between these regimes. The work enables future model-independent reconstructions of spectral functions with sufficiently precise lattice data, and provides benchmarks for the computational effort required (e.g., 2 Gflop-years for N_t=16 at 6 T_c), guiding the design of next-generation lattice calculations that could determine transport coefficients at the 30% level within five to ten years.
