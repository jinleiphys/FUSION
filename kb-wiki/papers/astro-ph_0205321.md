# Delayed Nickel Decay in Gamma Ray Bursts

> Recently observed emission lines in the X-ray afterglow of gamma ray bursts suggest that iron group elements are either produced in the gamma ray burst, or are present nearby. If this material is the product of a thermonuclear burn, then such material would be expected to be rich in Nickel-56. If the nickel remains partially ionized, this prevents the electron capture reaction normally associated with the decay of Nickel-56, dramatically increasing the decay timescale. Here we examine the consequences of rapid ejection of a fraction of a solar mass of iron group material from the center of a collapsar/hypernova. The exact rate of decay then depends on the details of the ionization and therefore the ejection process. Future observations of iron, nickel and cobalt lines can be used to diagnose the origin of these elements and to better understand the astrophysical site of gamma ray bursts. In this model, the X-ray lines of these iron-group elements could be detected in suspected hypernovae that did not produce an observable gamma ray burst due to beaming.

## Key claim

If nickel-56 ejected from a collapsar/hypernova remains partially ionized by the accretion disk's ionizing flux, its electron-capture decay is suppressed and the decay timescale increases dramatically; the exact decay rate depends on the details of the ionization front and the time-dependence of the ionizing source.

## Method

The authors model a homologous expansion of nickel-56-rich ejecta (mass ~0.1 solar masses, velocity beta ~0.2) from the accretion disk of a collapsar. They compute the ionization rate from a power-law X-ray flux and the recombination rate from standard formulae, then solve for the position of an ionization front moving through the expanding material. They combine this front motion with the nuclear physics of nickel-56 and cobalt-56 decay (electron capture vs. beta-plus decay) to predict the time-dependent abundances of nickel, cobalt, and iron as functions of position within the ejecta.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of nickel-56: 6.075 days for electron capture decay; experimental lower limit on beta-plus decay half-life of fully ionized nickel-56 is > 2.9 x 10^4 years
- Half-life of cobalt-56: 77.2 days; 19% branch for positron emission (I_beta+ = 0.19) which proceeds even when fully ionized
- Ionization front passage time: 6 days for constant ionizing flux (alpha = 0) and 17 days for flux decaying as t_d^{-1} (alpha = 1)
- Recombination rate coefficient C_rec = 1.1 x 10^6 (T/1 keV)^{-1/2} (M/0.1 M_sun) (0.2/beta)^3 ((1-cos 20 deg)/(1-cos theta)) (Z/28)^3 (A/56) day^{-1}
- Ionization rate coefficient C_i ~ 6 N_dot_{I,53} ((1-cos theta)/(1-cos 20 deg)) (beta/0.2) (0.1 M_sun/M) (T/1 keV)^{1/2} (28/Z)^5 (56/A) zeta
- Direct X-ray line flux from the model is detectable out to redshift z ~ 1; scattered off-axis flux detectable out to z ~ 0.2

## Context

This work extends the collapsar/hypernova model of Woosley (1993) and MacFadyen & Woosley (1999) by considering the ionization state of freshly synthesized nickel-56 ejected from the accretion disk. It builds on the observation of iron K-alpha lines in GRB afterglows by Piro (1999, 2000) and Antonelli (2000), and the detection of nickel lines by Reeves (2002). The model predicts that delayed nickel decay could be observed with Chandra, and that off-axis scattered X-ray line flux could test for hypernovae even when no gamma-ray burst is seen due to beaming.
