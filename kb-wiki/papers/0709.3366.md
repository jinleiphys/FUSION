# Jozso's Legacy: Chemical and Kinetic Freeze-out in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We review J. Zimanyi's key contributions to the theoretical understanding of dynamical freeze-out in nuclear collisions and their subsequent applications to ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, leading to the discovery of a freeze-out hierarchy where chemical freeze-out of hadron yields precedes the thermal decoupling of their momentum spectra. Following Zimanyi's lines of reasoning we show that kinetic freeze-out necessarily leads to a dependence of the corresponding freeze-out temperature on collision centrality. This centrality dependence can be predicted within hydrodynamic models, and for Au+Au collisions at RHIC this prediction is shown to reproduce the experimentally observed centrality dependence of the thermal decoupling temperature, extracted from hadron momentum spectra. The fact that no such centrality dependence is observed for the chemical decoupling temperature, extracted from the hadron yields measured in these collisions, excludes a similar kinetic interpretation of the chemical decoupling process. We argue that the chemical decoupling data from Au+Au collisions at RHIC can only be consistently understood if the chemical freeze-out process is driven by a phase transition, and that the measured chemical decoupling temperature therefore measures the critical temperature of the quark-hadron phase transition. We propose additional experiments to further test this interpretation.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the observed centrality independence of the chemical freeze-out temperature in Au+Au collisions at RHIC cannot be explained by a kinetic decoupling process from inelastic hadronic scattering, and instead must be driven by a phase transition, with the chemical decoupling temperature measuring the critical temperature of the quark-hadron phase transition.

## Method
The authors used a (2+1)-dimensional longitudinally boost-invariant hydrodynamic code (AZHYDRO) with standard initial conditions to generate flow patterns for 200 A GeV Au+Au collisions. They implemented a kinetic freeze-out criterion based on the competition between the local expansion rate and the scattering rate, using a parametrized pion scattering rate proportional to T^3.45, and computed the centrality dependence of the average kinetic decoupling temperature and radial flow velocity. They then compared these predictions with experimental data from STAR and contrasted the resulting centrality dependence with the observed centrality independence of the chemical freeze-out temperature.

## Key numbers
- Chemical freeze-out temperature from central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s)=200 A GeV: T_chem = 163 +/- 4 MeV, with baryon chemical potential mu_B = 24 +/- 4 MeV and strangeness saturation factor gamma_s = 0.99 +/- 0.07
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature in most central Au+Au collisions: T_kin = 89 +/- 12 MeV, increasing to T_kin = 127 +/- 13 MeV in most peripheral collisions
- Average radial flow velocity in most central collisions: <beta> = 0.59 +/- 0.05, decreasing to <beta> = 0.24 +/- 0.08 in most peripheral collisions
- The hydrodynamic calculation with kinetic freeze-out criterion used xi = 0.295 (xi^{-1} = 3.4) to match STAR data, with the scattering rate parametrized as 1/tau^pi_scatt = (59.5 fm^{-1}) (T/1 GeV)^3.45

## Context
This work extends the dynamical freeze-out concept pioneered by Zimanyi, Bondorf, and Garpman (1978), who introduced the competition between expansion rate and scattering rate as drivers of freeze-out. It addresses a controversy between two camps: Camp I (Becattini 1997, Heinz 1999, Stock 1999) holds that hadron production is a statistical process associated with a phase transition, while Camp II (Rapp and Shuryak 2001, Bass and合作者 2004) holds that chemical freeze-out is a kinetic process within the hadronic phase. The paper resolves this controversy by showing that the centrality independence of T_chem at RHIC excludes a kinetic interpretation, and proposes additional experiments to test the phase transition interpretation.
