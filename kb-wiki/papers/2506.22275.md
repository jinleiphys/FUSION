# Excited States from ADAPT-VQE convergence path in Many-Body Problems: application to nuclear pairing problem and $H_4$ molecule dissociation

> A quantum computing algorithm is proposed to obtain low-lying excited states in many-body interacting systems. The approximate eigenstates are obtained by using a quantum space diagonalization method in a subspace of states selected from the convergence path of the ADAPT-VQE (adaptive derivative-assembled pseudo-Trotter Ansatz variational quantum eigensolver) towards the ground state of the many-body problem. This method is shown to be accurate with only a small overhead in terms of quantum resources required to get the ground state. We also show that the quantum algorithm might be used to facilitate the convergence of the ADAPT-VQE method itself. Successful applications of the technique are made to like-particle pairing as well as neutron-proton pairing. Finally, the $H_4$ molecule's dissociation also illustrates the technique, demonstrating its accuracy and versatility.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that intermediate states generated during the ADAPT-VQE convergence path toward the ground state contain sufficient information about low-lying excited states, and that performing quantum subspace diagonalization (QSD) in this subspace can accurately reproduce the low-energy spectrum with only a small overhead in quantum resources beyond what is required for the ground state alone.

## Method

The authors first run ADAPT-VQE (adaptive derivative-assembled pseudo-Trotter Ansatz variational quantum eigensolver) to iteratively approximate the ground state of a many-body Hamiltonian, using either full parameter optimization (FPO) or single parameter optimization (SPO). They then collect the intermediate states generated at each iteration of the ADAPT-VQE convergence path and perform a generalized eigenvalue problem (quantum subspace diagonalization) in this subspace, computing Hamiltonian and overlap matrix elements via Hadamard test circuits. For systems with multiple symmetry sectors, they combine this approach with a state-averaged ADAPT-VQE strategy that simultaneously optimizes toward several low-lying states from orthogonal initial states.

## Key numbers

* For the like-particle pairing problem with 5 pairs (10 particles) on 10 doubly degenerate equidistant levels and coupling strength g/Delta-epsilon = 1, the QSD spectrum converges to the exact low-lying spectrum once the subspace dimension n_f exceeds approximately 30, which is much smaller than the Full Configuration Interaction dimension of C(10,5) = 252 in the seniority-zero subspace.
* For the like-particle pairing problem, the mean relative error (MRE) for the ground state (Lambda=1) improves by an order of magnitude when combining QSD with SPO compared to SPO alone.
* For the neutron-proton pairing problem with 2 neutrons and 2 protons on two blocks, the threshold for retaining eigenstates of the overlap matrix was set to epsilon = 10^{-6}.
* For the H4 molecule dissociation, approximately 12 ADAPT-VQE iterations per bond length r value were used, and the state-averaged strategy used K=4 initial states to target the four lowest states with symmetries A_g, B_{1g}, B_{2u}, and B_{3u}.

## Context

This work extends the ADAPT-VQE approach, previously applied to nuclear pairing problems by Zhang and Lacroix (2024), to access excited states rather than only the ground state. It builds on quantum subspace diagonalization methods reviewed by Motta (2024) and is related to the generator coordinate method (GCM) in nuclear physics, which Ring (1980) described, and its quantum algorithm version developed by Zheng and collaborators (2023, 2024). The method enables nuclear structure studies to access both ground states and low-lying spectroscopy on quantum computers, and the authors demonstrate compatibility with the state-averaged strategy of Grimsley and collaborators (2025) for handling degenerate symmetry sectors.
