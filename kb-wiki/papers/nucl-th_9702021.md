# General Relativistic Mean Field Theory for Rotating Nuclei

## Key claim
The authors formulate a general relativistic mean field theory for rotating nuclei using the tetrad formalism, arguing that previous work by Koepf and Ring (1989) based on Lorentz transformations is formally inadequate because the rotating frame is accelerated, not inertial. However, they show that the earlier approach yields the same final equations of motion because the incorrect transformation of the spinor field was compensated by an ad hoc transformation of the gamma matrices.

## Method
Starting from the special relativistic σ-ω model Lagrangian, the authors adopt the tetrad formalism to generalize the theory to a uniformly rotating (non-inertial) frame. They derive the equations of motion for the nucleon, σ meson, and ω meson fields in the rotating frame using two different tetrad choices: the "fundamental choice" (which yields time-dependent equations) and the "canonical choice" (which yields stationary equations analogous to the non-relativistic cranking model). The canonical choice corresponds to selecting the instantaneously rest frame as the locally inertial frame and produces a connection term Γμ = (-iΩΣx, 0) for the spinor field.

## Key numbers
- The metric tensor for the uniformly rotating frame is given explicitly with off-diagonal elements Ωz̃ and -Ωỹ, where Ω is the angular velocity.
- The canonical tetrad choice yields a connection Γμ(ẋ) = (-iΩΣx, 0) for the spinor field, where Σx = (1/4)[γ², γ³].
- The resulting single-particle equation contains the term -Ω(L̃x + Σx), combining orbital and spin contributions to the Coriolis coupling.

## Context
This work extends the relativistic mean field theory of Chin and Walecka (1974) to rotating nuclei by addressing a formal deficiency in the earlier formulation of Koepf and Ring (1989), who used a Lorentz transformation for the spinor field that is only valid for constant angle shifts. The authors clarify that the fermion field transforms as a scalar under general coordinate transformations, as known from quantum gravity (Weinberg, 1972). The formalism enables systematic numerical calculations of yrast states for both stable and unstable nuclei, with applications to superdeformed nuclei where pairing correlations are assumed unimportant (Koepf and Ring, 1993). A possible extension beyond mean field using the method of Kaneko, Nakano, and one of the present authors (1993) is noted but not yet numerically implemented.
