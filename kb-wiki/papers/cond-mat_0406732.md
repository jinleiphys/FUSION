# Dynamical Correlations as Origin of Nonextensive Entropy

> We present a simple and general argument showing that a class of dynamical correlations give rise to the so-called Tsallis nonextensive statistics. An example of a system having such a dynamics is given, exhibiting a non-Boltzmann energy distribution. A relation with prethermalization processes is discussed.

## Key claim

Dynamical correlations among particles, specifically the formation of strongly correlated clusters of size \(q\), can produce a stationary state described by Tsallis nonextensive statistics, where the system exhibits thermodynamic relations long before reaching true Boltzmann equilibrium; the authors emphasize that this state corresponds to a "pseudo-entropy" of the correlated subspace, not the full system entropy, and that the exact form of the entropy (Renyi vs. Tsallis) is not uniquely determined by the mechanism.

## Method

The authors derive the Tsallis distribution by assuming that in a dilute system of \(N\) particles with single-particle energies \(E_\alpha\) and occupation numbers \(N_\alpha\), strong correlations exist among any \(q\) particles in each state \(\alpha\). The number of ways to form such \(q\)-clusters is \(M_\alpha \approx N_\alpha^q / \Gamma(q+1)\). Minimizing the average energy per cluster \(\langle E \rangle_q\) under constraints of fixed total particle number and fixed total number of clusters \(M\) yields the Tsallis distribution \(p_\alpha \propto [1 + (q-1)\beta E_\alpha]^{1/(1-q)}\). They then illustrate the mechanism with a toy model of particles on equally spaced energy levels, where random pairwise collisions either set both particles to the lower energy (cluster-forming, with frequency ratio \(r\)) or exchange a fixed energy quantum \(\Delta E\) (thermalizing), and show that the resulting stationary energy spectra fit the Tsallis form.

## Key numbers

- For the toy model with \(r=0.01\), the distribution converges 10 times faster than for \(r=0\) (Boltzmann case).
- The Tsallis distribution in the one-dimensional toy model is valid only for \(q < 3/2\); for larger \(r\), the fitted \(q\) tends to this limiting value.
- The toy model simulations used \(r\) values ranging from \(0.00001\) to \(0.1\), starting from a distribution peaked at \(\langle E \rangle = 22.5\) (arbitrary units).
- The fitted Tsallis distribution parameters are given by \(\beta = 1/(3-2q)\langle E \rangle\) and \(C = (2-q)/(3-2q)\langle E \rangle\), where \(\langle E \rangle\) is the average energy.

## Context

This work extends the prethermalization concept of Berges et al. (2004), who showed that a quantum scalar field exhibits thermodynamic relations before true thermal equilibrium, by providing a dynamical mechanism based on particle correlations that yields Tsallis nonextensive statistics. It also connects to the long-standing Tsallis formalism (Tsallis, 1988; Tsallis, 1999) by offering a physical origin for the \(q\)-entropy and \(q\)-biased averages, which had previously lacked a clear dynamical basis. The toy model's mechanism is compared to the kinematical approach of Rafelski et al., and the authors suggest their scheme can interpret many existing applications of Tsallis statistics in biological systems, high-energy physics, and cosmology, while calling for future work based on realistic field-theoretical models.
