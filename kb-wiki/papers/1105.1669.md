# Quadrupole collectivity in random two-body ensembles

> We conduct a systematic investigation of the nuclear collective dynamics that emerges in systems with random two-body interactions. We explore the development of the mean field and study its geometry. We investigate multipole collectivities in the many-body spectra and their dependence on the underlying two-body interaction Hamiltonian. The quadrupole-quadrupole interaction component appears to be dynamically dominating in two-body random ensembles. This quadrupole coherence leads to rotational spectral features and thus suggests the formation of the deformed mean-field of a specific geometry.

## Key claim

The quadrupole-quadrupole (QQ) interaction component dynamically dominates in random two-body ensembles, generating rotational spectral features and deformed mean-field geometries; however, the emergence of collective rotational structure is conditional on the presence of the quadrupole component in the Hamiltonian, and the excitation energy ratios often deviate from the rotational limit due to non-collective features and fluctuations.

## Method

The authors adopt a shell-model approach with degenerate single-particle energies and define two-body random ensembles (TBRE) where two-body matrix elements are drawn from a Gaussian distribution consistent with the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble. They analyze spectral observables including fractional collectivity, intrinsic quadrupole moments, relative transition strengths, and energy ratios for systems with single and multiple j-levels, using between 10^5 and 10^7 random realizations per ensemble. The multipole structure of the Hamiltonian is examined by projecting out specific particle-hole channel components (monopole, dipole, quadrupole) via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization to identify which interaction terms drive collective behavior.

## Key numbers

- In the (19/2)^6 system, 10.4% of random realizations have the 0_gs followed by 2_1 state; 7.8% of total samples are collective (b > 0.7), while the statistical expectation for this spin sequence is only 0.015%.
- For collective realizations in (19/2)^6, oblate cases appear with probability 5.2% and prolate cases with 1.3% relative to the total ensemble.
- The deexcitation ratio B_42 for collective oblate realizations in (19/2)^6 peaks near the rotational limit of 1.4; for prolate realizations the peak is near 0.8.
- In the (19/2)^8 system, 7.5% of random spectra have 0_gs and 2_1 states, with 4.6% being collective (b > 0.7) and only oblate shapes observed.
- Removing both the J^2 term (K=1) and the QQ term (K=2) from the Hamiltonian reduces the fraction of realizations with 0_gs and 2_1 from 7.6% to 4.7% in the (19/2)^8 system.

## Context

This work extends the study of emergent collectivity in random two-body ensembles, building on the discovery by Johnson (1998) that ground state spin zero appears with high probability in TBRE, and on subsequent observations of pairing, deformation, and vibrational features in random ensembles reviewed by Zelevinsky (2004), Johnson (2007), Papenbrock (2007), Zhao (2004), and Bijker (2000). The paper systematically identifies the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction as the specific component responsible for rotational collectivity, which enables future studies to isolate how deformation geometry emerges from random interactions and to connect TBRE results to realistic nuclear structure models such as Elliott's SU(3) model.
