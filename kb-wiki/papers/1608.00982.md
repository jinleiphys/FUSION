# Chiral Magnetic Effect Task Force Report

> In this report, we briefly examine the current status of the study of the chiral magnetic effect including theory and experimental progress. We recommend future strategies for resolving uncertainties in interpretation including recommendations for theoretical work, recommendations for measurements based on data collected in the past five years, and recommendations for beam use in the coming years of RHIC. We have specifically investigated the case for colliding nuclear isobars (nuclei with the same mass but different charge) and find the case compelling. We recommend that a program of nuclear isobar collisions to isolate the chiral magnetic effect from background sources be placed as a high priority item in the strategy for completing the RHIC mission.

## Key claim

The report concludes that while many measurements of charge separation in heavy-ion collisions are generally consistent with expectations from the chiral magnetic effect (CME), background models can also account for much of the data, and based on current understanding, backgrounds may account for all of the observed charge separation; the most unambiguous evidence is likely to come from collisions of nuclear isobars, which could reduce the uncertainty on the magnetic field dependent contribution from 100% to plus or minus 6.7%.

## Method

The task force reviewed theoretical progress on the CME and associated effects, examined experimental status including recent results on central U+U and Au+Au collisions from the STAR collaboration at RHIC, and assessed the viability of using nuclear isobar collisions (nuclei with the same mass but different charge) to isolate the CME signal from background sources. They evaluated the gamma correlator observable, which measures event-by-event correlations of charge separation with respect to the reaction plane, and considered how background contributions from parity-even processes such as flow fluctuations and momentum conservation can mimic the CME signal.

## Key numbers

* The typical difference between positive and negative charges in the dipole term a_± is less than 10^{-4} in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV, based on STAR measurements.
* A program of nuclear isobar collisions can reduce the uncertainty on the magnetic field dependent contribution to the charge separation signal from 100% to plus or minus 6.7%.
* If the CME is responsible for at least approximately 20% of the experimental charge separation signal, the isobar program will enable a statistically significant measurement of the CME.

## Context

This report extends the work reviewed in Kharzeev, Liao, Voloshin, and Wang (2016) and builds on the foundational theoretical framework of the CME established by Fukushima, Kharzeev, and Warringa (2008) and earlier work by Vilenkin (1980). The report addresses the persistent ambiguity in interpreting charge separation measurements first reported by the STAR collaboration in 2009, where subsequent model studies of background effects and discoveries about fluctuations in initial geometry have shown that much or all of the original signal could arise from effects unrelated to the CME. The recommended isobar program would enable a definitive test by independently manipulating the magnetic field while keeping nuclear geometry nearly identical, a strategy that goes beyond what can be achieved with existing data from U+U and Au+Au collisions.
