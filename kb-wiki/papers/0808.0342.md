# Resonance signals in pion-nucleon scattering from speed plots and time delay

> We compare a number of methods used to locate resonances. These include the speed plot, the time-delay method of Eisenbud and Wigner, the time-delay matrix of Smith, and a modification by Ohmura. Numerical results show a consistency not previously reported. One time delay method gives the most consistent results, supporting the conclusions of a recent theoretical study.

## Key claim

The speed-plot, the Eisenbud single-channel time-delay, the Smith S-matrix time-delay, and the Ohmura-modified time-delay methods generally produce peaks at energies consistent with the real part of the resonance pole position for most well-established pion-nucleon resonances, but the method based on Eq. (4) (the energy derivative of the channel phase shift) fails for inelastic resonances, producing a time-advance dip instead of a peak.

## Method

The authors compared four resonance-location methods, speed plot, Eisenbud single-channel time delay, Smith's S-matrix time delay, and Ohmura's modified S-matrix time delay, by applying them to partial-wave decomposed S-matrices obtained from a multi-channel Chew-Mandelstam K-matrix fit to elastic pion-nucleon scattering and eta-N production data. They examined the S11, P11, D13, P33, F37, and S31 partial waves, comparing the energy at which each method produced a peak to the real part of the known resonance pole positions.

## Key numbers

- For the S11 channel, peaks from both Smith and Ohmura time-delay methods are evident at the N(1535) and N(1650) resonances, with the N(1535) peak clearly separated from the eta-N threshold cusp.
- For the P33 partial wave and the Delta(1232) resonance, all methods produce a peak corresponding to the pole position.
- For the 4-star Delta(1950) resonance in the F37 partial wave, all methods except Eq. (4) produce peaks at the real part of the pole position; Eq. (4) produces a sharp dip closer to the Breit-Wigner mass.
- For the 4-star Delta(1620) resonance in the S31 partial wave, both matrix methods and the speed plot produce peaks near the resonance energy, but the Ohmura result is shifted from the pole position, and both single-channel time-delay methods fail to peak at the resonance.

## Context

This work extends comparisons of resonance-location methods previously examined theoretically by Kelkar and Nowakowski (2004) and by Tshlee (2004), providing numerical confirmation of their conclusions. The results contradict an earlier study by Kelkar (2003) that found no prominent peaks for most isospin 3/2 resonances using Eq. (4). The findings enable more reliable identification of resonances in partial-wave analyses where only numerical amplitudes are available, and motivate further investigations into the theoretical basis of these methods.
