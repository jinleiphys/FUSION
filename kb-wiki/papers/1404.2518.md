# Density and Temperature in Heavy Ion Collisions: A Test of Classical and Quantum Approaches

> Different methods to extract the temperature and density in heavy ion collisions are compared using a statistical model tailored to reproduce many experimental features at low excitation energy. The model assumes a sequential decay of an excited nucleus and a Fermi gas entropy. We first generate statistical events as function of excitation energy but stopping the decay chain at the first step. In such a condition the 'exact' model temperature is determined from the Fermi gas relation to the excitation energy. From these events, using quantum and classical fluctuation methods for protons and neutrons, we derive temperature and density (quantum case only) of the system under consideration. Additionally, the same quantities are also extracted using the double ratio method for different particle combinations. A very good agreement between the "exact" temperatures and quantum fluctuation temperatures is obtained, the role of the density is discussed. Classical methods give a reasonable estimate of the temperature when the density is very low as expected. The effects of secondary decays of the excited fragments are discussed as well.

## Key claim

The quantum fluctuation method reproduces the "exact" Fermi-gas temperature of the statistical model very well, while classical double-ratio methods fail because they rely on classical assumptions; however, the apparent disagreement between different double-ratio combinations disappears when temperature rather than excitation energy is used as the control parameter.

## Method

The authors used the GEMINI statistical model to generate one million events for each excitation energy, assuming a sequential decay of a hot source with A=80, Z=40, and zero angular momentum. They stopped the decay chain at the first step to obtain an "exact" temperature from the Fermi gas relation E* = aT², then applied quantum fluctuation (QF) methods for protons and neutrons, classical fluctuation (CF) methods, and double-ratio (DR) methods using deuteron-triton-³He-alpha (dthα) and proton-neutron-triton-³He (pnth) combinations to extract temperature and density from the same events. The analysis was repeated for the full multi-step decay chain to study the effects of secondary decays.

## Key numbers

- Source mass A=80 and charge Z=40 were fixed for all simulations.
- Two values of the Fermi gas level-density parameter k₀ were used: 7.3 MeV and 15 MeV.
- For k₀=15 MeV, the implied density is ρ/ρ₀=1.0 (nuclear ground state density); for k₀=7.3 MeV, the implied density is ρ/ρ₀=0.34.
- The effective radius assumed in GEMINI for A=80 is R=7.6 fm (from R=1.16A^{1/3}+2.6 fm), equivalent to a density less than one third of normal nuclear density.
- Ground state densities used for normalization: ρₚ₀=ρₙ₀=0.08 fm⁻³, ρ₀=0.16 fm⁻³.
- One million events were generated for each excitation energy (or initial temperature).

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons of temperature and density extraction methods in heavy-ion collisions, particularly building on studies using the Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) model by Zheng et al. (2012-2013) and the fluctuation method introduced by Wuenschel et al. (2010). The paper directly tests the quantum fluctuation method against the classical double-ratio method of Albergo (1985) and the slope temperature method, using the GEMINI statistical model as a controlled benchmark. The results enable a clearer interpretation of experimental data from the Texas A&M Cyclotron Institute and support the use of quantum fluctuation methods over classical approaches for extracting thermodynamic quantities from heavy-ion collision data.
