# Strangeness Production in Neutron Stars

> Production of strange quarks in neutron stars is investigated in this work. Three cases, one in which the energy and neutrinos produced in the strangeness production reactions are retained in the reaction region, second in which the neutrinos are allowed to escape the reaction region but the energy is retained and the third in which both the energy and neutrinos escape the reaction region are considered. It is shown that the nonleptonic weak process dominates strange quark production while semileptonic weak processes, which produce neutrinos, lead to the cooling if the neutrinos escape the reaction region. It is found that the time required for the saturation of the strangeness fraction is between $10^{-7}$ and $10^{-5}$ sec, with the shorter time corresponding to the first two cases. About 0.2 neutrinos/baryon are emitted during the process in the first two cases where as the neutrino emission is somewhat suppressed in the last case. The average energy of the neutrinos produced in all the three cases is found to be several hundred $MeV$. We also find that a large amount of energy is released during the strangeness production in the first two cases and this leads to the heating of the reaction region. Implications of the neutrino production are investigated.

# Strangeness Production in Neutron Stars

## Key claim

The nonleptonic weak process dominates strange quark production in neutron star matter, while semileptonic weak processes produce neutrinos that can lead to cooling if they escape the reaction region; the time required for strangeness fraction saturation depends critically on whether energy and neutrinos are retained or escape, ranging from 10⁻⁷ to 10⁻⁵ seconds.

## Method

The authors calculated weak interaction reaction rates for strange quark production in quark matter using the standard weak interaction Lagrangian, including nonleptonic (u + d ↔ u + s) and semileptonic processes involving electrons and positrons. They numerically integrated the rate equations for three scenarios: (1) energy and neutrinos retained, (2) energy retained but neutrinos escaping, and (3) both energy and neutrinos escaping (constant temperature). The calculations were performed for baryon densities of 0.4–0.8 fm⁻³, strange quark masses of 150–200 MeV, and strong coupling constants of 0.1–0.5, assuming thermal equilibrium among quarks and electrons at all times.

## Key numbers

- Strangeness fraction saturation time: between 10⁻⁷ and 10⁻⁵ sec, with the shorter time (∼10⁻⁷ sec) for cases where energy is retained and the longer time (∼10⁻⁵ sec) for the constant temperature case
- Neutrino emission: approximately 0.2 neutrinos/baryon emitted in the first two cases (energy retained), with 0.07 to 0.25 neutrinos/baryon across all parameter sets
- Average neutrino energy: between 150 and 200 MeV for all three cases
- Temperature rise in the reaction region: reaches about 50 MeV in case 1 (energy and neutrinos retained) and a peak of about 45 MeV in case 2 (neutrinos escape, energy retained)
- Energy released: 130 MeV/baryon liberated during strangeness production for the parameter set with n_B = 0.4 fm⁻³, strange quark mass = 150 MeV, strong coupling constant = 0.1, and B¹/⁴ = 150 MeV
- For a 1.4 M⊙ star converted to a strange star: about 10⁵⁶ neutrinos produced carrying about 2.5 × 10⁵² ergs of energy
- For a 0.7 M⊙ strange quark matter region: about 2 × 10⁵⁶ neutrinos emitted in 10⁻⁷ sec carrying about 5 × 10⁵² ergs

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Dai et al. (1995), which the authors argue used incorrect reaction rates (Iwamoto-like formulae proportional to T⁵) and did not consider trapping of energy and neutrinos in the reaction region. The paper builds on the Witten (1984) conjecture that strange quark matter may be the ground state of dense matter, and on prior work by Alcock et al. (1986) on strange matter seeding, Olinto (1987), Olesen and Madsen (1991), and Heiselberg et al. (1993) on conversion front speeds, and Glendenning (1992) on mixed phase formation. The results enable quantitative estimates of neutrino production during neutron star to strange star conversion, with implications for supernova explosion mechanisms as proposed by Benvenuto and Hovarth (1994), suggesting that neutrinos from strangeness production could enhance the delayed shock mechanism.
