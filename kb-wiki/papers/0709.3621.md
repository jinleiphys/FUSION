# Constraining properties of rapidly rotating neutron stars using data from heavy-ion collisions

> Properties, structure, and thermal evolution of neutron stars are determined by the equation of state of stellar matter. Recent data on isospin-diffusion and isoscaling in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies as well as the size of neutron skin in $^{208}Pb$ have constrained considerably the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy and, in turn, the equation of state of neutron-rich nucleonic matter. These constraints could provide useful information about the global properties of rapidly rotating neutron stars. Models of rapidly rotating neutron stars are constructed applying several nucleonic equations of state. Particular emphasis is placed on configurations rotating rigidly at $716$ and $1122Hz$. The range of allowed hydrostatic equilibrium solutions is determined and tested for stability. The effect of rotation on the internal composition and thermal properties of neutron stars is also examined. At a given rotational frequency, each equation of state yields a range of possible neutron stars configurations restricted by the Keplerian (mass-shedding) limit, corresponding to the maximal circumferential radius, and the limit due to the onset of instabilities with respect to axial-symmetric perturbations, corresponding to the minimal equatorial radius of a stable neutron star model. We show that the mass of a neutron star rotating uniformly at $1122Hz$ is between $1.7$ and $2.1M_{\sun}$. Central stellar density and proton fraction decrease with increasing rotational frequency with respect to static models, and depending on the exact stellar mass and angular velocity, can drop below the Direct Urca threshold thus closing the fast cooling channel.

## Key claim
The mass of a neutron star rotating uniformly at 1122 Hz (the frequency of XTE J1739-285) is constrained to be between 1.7 and 2.1 solar masses, based on equations of state whose symmetry energy is consistent with terrestrial nuclear laboratory data (isospin diffusion, isoscaling, and neutron skin measurements). This range is narrower than the full range allowed by static models, and the paper emphasizes that this result depends on the specific equations of state (MDI with x=0 and x=-1) that bracket the nuclear data constraints.

## Method
The authors constructed two-dimensional stationary models of rigidly rotating neutron stars in general relativity using the RNS code, solving the hydrostatic and Einstein field equations under assumptions of stationarity, axial symmetry, and reflection symmetry about the equatorial plane. They applied four nucleonic equations of state (MDI with x=0 and x=-1, APR, and DBHF+Bonn B), with the MDI EOSs chosen because their symmetry energy is constrained by heavy-ion collision data (isospin diffusion and isoscaling) and neutron skin measurements of 208Pb. For each EOS, they computed sequences of equilibrium configurations parameterized by central energy density, tested stability against mass-shedding (Keplerian limit) and axial-symmetric perturbations, and examined the effect of rotation on central density and proton fraction.

## Key numbers
- The mass of a neutron star rotating uniformly at 1122 Hz is between 1.7 and 2.1 solar masses (based on the MDI x=0 and x=-1 EOSs, which represent the limits consistent with nuclear data).
- For the MDI (x=0) EOS, the maximum gravitational mass of a static (nonrotating) neutron star is 1.91 solar masses, with radius 9.89 km and central energy density 3.02 x 10^15 g cm^-3.
- For the MDI (x=-1) EOS, the maximum static mass is 1.97 solar masses, with radius 10.85 km and central energy density 2.57 x 10^15 g cm^-3.
- For the APR EOS, the maximum static mass is 2.19 solar masses, with radius 9.98 km and central energy density 2.73 x 10^15 g cm^-3.
- For the DBHF+Bonn B EOS, the maximum static mass is 2.24 solar masses, with radius 10.88 km and central energy density 2.36 x 10^15 g cm^-3.
- Rapid rotation at the Kepler frequency increases the maximum mass by up to 17% (APR and DBHF+Bonn B) relative to static models; the MDI (x=0) increase is 15%, and MDI (x=-1) is 14%.
- The Kepler (mass-shedding) frequencies for the maximum-mass rotating models are: 1742 Hz (MDI x=0), 1963 Hz (APR), 1512 Hz (MDI x=-1), and 1685 Hz (DBHF+Bonn B).
- For the MDI (x=0) EOS, a neutron star of mass approximately 1.9 solar masses has its direct Urca channel close at a rotational frequency of about 470 Hz.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of rotating neutron stars (e.g., Hartle 1967, Friedman 1986, Stergioulas 1994) by incorporating recent constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy from heavy-ion collision data (isospin diffusion by Tsang 2004, isoscaling by Tsang 2001 and Shetty 2007) and neutron skin measurements (Horowitz 2000, 2002). It directly builds on the MDI equation of state work by Li (2005) that used these data to constrain neutron star radii. The results enable future comparisons with observations of millisecond pulsars like XTE J1739-285 (Kaaret 2006) and PSR J1748-2446ad (Hessels 2006), and provide a framework for interpreting neutron star cooling data by showing that rapid rotation can close the fast direct Urca cooling channel.
