# Solution of Integral Equations by a Chebyshev Expansion Method

> A new spectral type method for solving the one dimensional quantum-mechanical Lippmann-Schwinger integral equation in configuration space is described. The radial interval is divided into partitions, not necessarily of equal length. Two independent local solutions of the integral equation are obtained in each interval via Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature in terms of Chebyshev Polynomials. The local solutions are then combined into a global solution by solving a matrix equation for the coefficients. This matrix is sparse and the equation is easily soluble. The method shows excellent numerical stability, as is demonstrated by several numerical examples.

## Key claim

The paper presents a spectral method for solving the one-dimensional quantum-mechanical Lippmann-Schwinger integral equation in configuration space that achieves excellent numerical stability and high accuracy, particularly for cases with long-range potentials or many oscillations, where conventional finite difference methods like Numerov experience difficulties due to roundoff error accumulation and loss of linear independence.

## Method

The radial interval [0, R_Max] is divided into partitions of arbitrary size, and in each partition two independent local solutions (Y and Z) of the integral equation are obtained via Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature using Chebyshev polynomial expansions with 16 Chebyshev points per partition. These local solutions are then combined into a global solution by solving a sparse block-tridiagonal "Big Matrix" equation of dimension 2m x 2m (where m is the number of partitions) for the stitching coefficients A and B, which produces a seamless continuation of the wave function across partitions.

## Key numbers

- For the Riccati-Bessel function test with k = 1 fm^-1, R_Max = 50 fm, and L = 6, the IEM error reaches machine accuracy with approximately 50 points per local wavelength, while the Numerov method requires 1260 points per local wavelength for comparable accuracy.
- For the more taxing case with k = 40 fm^-1, L = 8, and R_Max = 50 fm (580 nodes in the wave function), the IEM achieves best accuracy with 40 points per half wavelength (N = 12800 total points, 800 partitions), while the Numerov method requires 1280 points per half wavelength, a factor of 30 larger.
- For two coupled channels with exponential potentials (|V_0| = 5/sqrt(2) fm^-2, a = 4 fm), the IEM error in the R-matrix elements w_1 and w_2 is nearly independent of wave number k and much smaller than the Numerov error, which increases as k decreases.
- For the case with one negative-energy channel, the IEM yields values of w_1 and w_2 stable to 13 significant figures, while the Numerov method gives w_2 correct to less than 4 significant figures for kR_Max > 25.

## Context

This work extends the spectral method approach of Greengard and Rokhlin (1991) but differs substantially in how local solutions in each partition are combined into the global solution. The method is particularly suited for applications such as photoassociation in cold atom collisions, as reviewed by Julienne (1995), where very long-range interactions and many coupled channels pose difficulties for conventional approaches. The authors plan to generalize the method to other types of integral equations and higher-dimensional problems, including three-body reactions in coordinate space.
