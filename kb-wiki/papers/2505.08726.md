# An algebraic solution of Dirac equation on a static curved space-time

> We present exact solutions of the Dirac equation in static curved space-time using two distinct algebraic approaches. The first method employs $su(1,1)$ algebra operators together with the tilting transformation, enabling the derivation of the energy spectrum and eigenfunctions for both the Hydrogen atom and the Dirac-Morse oscillator. The second approach, based on the Schrödinger factorization method, extends the analysis to three representative potentials: the hydrogen atom, the Dirac-Morse oscillator, and a linear radial potential. Although structurally different from those obtained in the first method, the resulting operators in this approach also close the $su(1,1)$ algebra and, through representation theory, yield the corresponding energy spectra and eigenfunctions.

## Key claim

The paper presents exact solutions of the Dirac equation in static curved space-time using two algebraic approaches, but the solutions are obtained only under the specific metric condition \( e^{f(r)} = e^{g(r)} = 1 + \alpha^2 U(r) \), where \( \alpha \) is the fine-structure constant and \( U(r) \) is a well-behaved scalar potential, meaning the results are conditional on this particular choice of metric functions.

## Method

The authors first decouple the radial and angular components of the Dirac equation in a static spherically symmetric curved space-time with an external electromagnetic field. They then apply two distinct algebraic methods: (1) constructing \( su(1,1) \) Lie algebra generators together with a tilting transformation (Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula) to derive energy spectra and eigenfunctions, and (2) using the Schrödinger factorization method to factorize the second-order radial differential equations, yielding operators that also close the \( su(1,1) \) algebra. The energy spectra are obtained from the representation theory of \( su(1,1) \) via the Bargmann index quantization condition.

## Key numbers

- Hydrogen atom energy spectrum (first method): \( \epsilon_{nHT} = \frac{ -\alpha^2 \mu z - \left( n + 1 + \frac{\alpha (Z \cos(2\eta) - b)}{\sin(2\eta)} \right) \sqrt{ \alpha^2 (\mu^2 - z^2) + \left( n + 1 + \frac{\alpha (Z \cos(2\eta) - b)}{\sin(2\eta)} \right)^2 } }{ \alpha^2 z^2 - \left( n + 1 + \frac{\alpha (Z \cos(2\eta) - b)}{\sin(2\eta)} \right)^2 } \), where \( n \) is the radial quantum number and \( \eta \) is the tilting transformation parameter.
- Dirac-Morse oscillator energy spectrum: \( \epsilon_{MT} = \frac{2 \Gamma a (\Gamma b + n \alpha \delta) \pm 2 \sqrt{ \Gamma^2 a^2 - \Gamma^2 b^2 - 2 n \alpha \delta \Gamma b - n^2 \alpha^2 \delta^2 + 1 }}{2 (\Gamma^2 a^2 + 1)} \), with \( \Gamma = \frac{\sin(2\eta)}{a \cos(2\eta) + b} \) and \( \delta \) the Morse oscillator range parameter.
- Linear radial potential energy spectrum: \( \epsilon_{LS} = \frac{ -\alpha^{2} ab + \sqrt{ \beta^{2} \left[ 2\alpha^{2} \beta (2n + 1)(\alpha^{2}a^{2} + \beta^{2}) + \alpha^{2}(a^{2} - b^{2}) + \beta^{2} \right] } }{ \alpha^{2}a^{2} + \beta^{2} } \), where \( \beta = \frac{\alpha(b - a\cos(2\eta))}{\sin(2\eta)} \).
- Bargmann index for hydrogen atom: \( k_{HT} = \frac{\alpha}{\sin(2\eta)}(Z\cos(2\eta) - b) + 1 \).
- Bargmann index for Dirac-Morse oscillator: \( k_{MT} = \frac{1}{2} + \sqrt{\frac{1 - \epsilon^2}{\alpha^2 \delta^2}} \).
- Bargmann index for linear radial potential: \( k_{LS} = 1/4 \).

## Context

This work extends the algebraic treatment of the Dirac equation in curved space-time previously developed by De Oliveira and Schmidt (2023), who derived exact analytical solutions for the hydrogen atom, Dirac-Morse oscillator, and linear confining potential by decoupling radial and angular components. The paper also builds on Parker's (1980) pioneering analysis of one-electron atoms in curved space-time and on Barut's (1980) algebraic approach using \( su(1,1) \) generators for central potentials. The results enable further studies of relativistic quantum systems in curved backgrounds, particularly for comparing how space-time curvature modifies energy spectra and eigenfunctions relative to flat-space solutions.
