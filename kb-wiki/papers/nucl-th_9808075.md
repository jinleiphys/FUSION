# Asymptotic expansion in the fission process

> Asymptotic expansion of the Fokker-Planck equation in terms of the strength of the fluctuation has been carried out. The mean and the variance of the total kinetic energies of the fission fragments have been calculated and compared with the experimental values.

# Asymptotic expansion in the fission process

## Key claim

The Fokker-Planck equation for fission dynamics can be solved via an asymptotic expansion in the strength of the fluctuation, provided the relative fluctuation of the collective variable (e.g., the root-mean-square deviation of kinetic energy divided by the mean kinetic energy) is small (approximately 0.1). The method reproduces the extracted total kinetic energy (TKE) variances from experimental data only after subtracting the contribution from the variation of mean TKE with fragment mass asymmetry, which the abstract does not mention as a necessary correction.

## Method

The authors generalize van Kampen's asymptotic expansion method to the case where the diffusion coefficient depends explicitly on the stochastic variables, as occurs in fission when the friction coefficient depends on the nuclear shape. They expand the collective variables (elongation r and its velocity) as deterministic values plus fluctuating parts scaled by the square root of the diffusion coefficient, then derive a hierarchy of equations from the Fokker-Planck equation. The deterministic equations of motion (Euler-Lagrange equations) are solved simultaneously with coupled first-order differential equations for the variances g(t), G(t), and the covariance c(t) of position and velocity, using the Einstein fluctuation-dissipation theorem to evaluate the diffusion coefficient. The probability distribution function is then constructed by integrating over initial conditions, where each fissioning nucleus starts from a fixed initial position but with different partitioning of initial excitation energy via a random number.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square deviation of kinetic energy is always very small compared to the mean kinetic energy, approximately 0.1 (ratio).
- For the system 18O + 154Sm at 158.8 MeV laboratory energy: experimental variance σ_E²(exp) = 112.3 MeV², extracted σ_E = 10.1 MeV, theoretical σ_E(th) = 9.7 MeV.
- For 18O + 197Au at 158.8 MeV: experimental variance σ_E²(exp) = 190.4 MeV², extracted σ_E = 13.2 MeV, theoretical σ_E(th) = 11.9 MeV.
- For 16O + 197Au at 288.0 MeV: experimental variance σ_E²(exp) = 331.2 MeV², extracted σ_E = 16.7 MeV, theoretical σ_E(th) = 13.3 MeV.
- For 16O + 109Ag at 288.0 MeV: experimental variance σ_E²(exp) = 225.0 MeV², extracted σ_E = 9.0 MeV, theoretical σ_E(th) = 8.3 MeV.
- The deterministic value of total fragment kinetic energy (TKE) after scission is approximately 100–200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous deterministic calculations (Dhara et al., 1993, 1996, 1998) where dissipation was included via a nonconservative Rayleigh function but fluctuations were absent, by adding stochastic fluctuations through an asymptotic expansion of the Fokker-Planck equation. It contrasts with full Langevin equation approaches (Abe et al., 1986; Frobrich et al., 1993) or multidimensional Fokker-Planck equation solutions (Kramers, 1940; Grange et al., 1983) by exploiting the smallness of relative fluctuations to avoid solving the full stochastic equations in detail. The method enables calculation of TKE variances for fission fragments without solving the Fokker-Planck equation directly, and the authors emphasize that for proper comparison with experiment, experimental variances must be corrected for the contribution from the variation of mean kinetic energy over fragment mass asymmetry, using Viola systematics (Viola et al., 1985) and mass yield data (Hinde et al., 1992).
