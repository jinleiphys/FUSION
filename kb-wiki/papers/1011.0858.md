# Proposal for a Nuclear Gamma-Ray Laser of Optical Range

> A possibility of amplification of the 7.6 eV $γ$-radiation by the stimulated $γ$-emission of the ensemble of the $^{229m}$Th isomeric nuclei in a host dielectric crystal is proved theoretically. This amplification is a result of: 1) the excitation of a large number of the $^{229m}$Th isomers by laser radiation; 2) the creation of the inverse population of nuclear levels in a cooled sample owing to the interaction of thorium nuclei with the crystal electric field or with an external magnetic field; 3) the emissions/absorption of the optical photons by thorium nuclei in the crystal without recoil; 4) the nuclear spin relaxation through the conduction electrons of the metallic covering.

## Key claim

The paper theoretically proves the possibility of amplifying 7.6 eV gamma radiation via stimulated emission from an ensemble of 229mTh isomeric nuclei in a host dielectric crystal, but only under specific conditions: the creation of an effective inverse population through Zeeman or quadrupole splitting in a cooled sample, combined with recoil-free emission and absorption in the crystal.

## Method

The author calculates the amplification coefficient χ for the M1 transition between the 3/2+ isomeric level and the 5/2+ ground state of 229Th in a Th:LiCaAlF6 crystal. The analysis uses the standard laser amplification formula incorporating the radiative width (derived from experimental data on higher-energy M1 transitions via Alaga rules), the total line width (dominated by nuclear magnetic moment interactions, reducible to 1 kHz), and the linear attenuation coefficient. Three schemes for achieving effective inverse population are examined: Zeeman splitting in a 100 T magnetic field, electric quadrupole splitting from the crystal field gradient, and a time-sequenced scheme exploiting long spin-lattice relaxation times with metallic covering to accelerate relaxation.

## Key numbers

- Isomeric transition energy: 7.6 ± 0.5 eV, corresponding to wavelength λ_is = 163 ± 11 nm
- Radiative width of the isomeric transition: Γ_rad ≈ 3 × 10⁻¹⁹ eV
- Total line width of the transition: Δω_tot ≤ 7 × 10⁻¹³ eV (reducible to 1 kHz)
- Reduced M1 transition probability in Weisskopf units: B_W.u.(M1) ≈ 0.032 (enhanced average value)
- Ground state magnetic moment: μ_gr = 0.45 μ_N; isomeric state magnetic moment: μ_is ≈ -0.08 μ_N
- Initial density of 229Th nuclei in Th:LiCaAlF6: n_0 = 10¹⁸ cm⁻³
- Amplification coefficient for Zeeman scheme: χ ≈ 3 cm⁻¹
- Amplification coefficient for quadrupole splitting scheme: χ ≈ 2 cm⁻¹ (for the red transition)
- Amplification coefficient for the time-sequenced scheme: χ ≈ 3 cm⁻¹
- Energy gap of Th:LiCaAlF6 crystal: Δ ≈ 10 eV
- Recoil energy loss: E_R ≈ 1.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ eV (negligible, giving Mössbauer probability f = 1)
- Ground state spectroscopic quadrupole moment: Q_gr = 3.15 e b; isomeric state quadrupole moment: Q_is ≈ 1.8 e b
- Electric field gradient at Th⁴⁺ site: φ_zz ≈ -10¹⁸ V cm⁻²; with Sternheimer antishielding factor γ_∞ ≈ 100-200, EFG at nucleus can exceed -10²⁰ V cm⁻²
- Quadrupole interaction energy: E_QI ≈ -10⁻⁴ eV
- Duration of gamma-ray laser emission for L = 5 cm: τ ≈ 10² s
- Mean power of gamma-ray laser: ≈ 10⁻⁷ W
- Repetition frequency: f_rep ≈ 10⁵-10⁴ s⁻¹
- Nuclear spin relaxation time via metallic covering: ≲ 50 days
- Required photon flux density for pumping: φ ≈ 10²⁰ cm⁻² s⁻¹

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical and experimental efforts on nuclear gamma-ray lasers reviewed by Baldwin (1997) and Rivlin (2007), and builds on the identification of Th:LiCaAlF6 as a suitable host crystal by Rellergert (2010). The paper specifically addresses the long-standing challenges of accumulating sufficient isomeric nuclei and narrowing the emission line to its natural width, proposing that the 229Th isomer's uniquely low energy (7.6 eV) enables recoil-free Mössbauer emission in the optical range. The results enable practical studies of nuclear ensembles with high inverse population density, cooperative spontaneous emission as described by Dicke (1954), and potential applications including a nuclear clock as proposed by Peik (2003) and tests of fundamental constant variation as discussed by Flambaum (2006).
