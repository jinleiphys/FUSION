# The scenario of two families of compact stars 1. Equations of state, mass-radius relations and binary systems

> We present several arguments which favor the scenario of two coexisting families of compact stars: hadronic stars and quark stars. Besides the well known hyperon puzzle of the physics of compact stars, a similar puzzle exists also when considering delta resonances. We show that these particles appear at densities close to twice saturation density and must be therefore included in the calculations of the hadronic equation of state. Such an early appearance is strictly related to the value of the L parameter of the symmetry energy that has been found, in recent phenomenological studies, to lie in the range $40<L<62$ MeV. We discuss also the threshold for the formation of deltas and hyperons for hot and lepton rich hadronic matter. Similarly to the case of hyperons, also delta resonances cause a softening of the equation of state which makes it difficult to obtain massive hadronic stars. Quark stars, on the other hand, can reach masses up to $2.75 M_{\odot}$ as predicted by perturbative QCD calculations. We then discuss the observational constraints on the masses and the radii of compact stars. The tension between the precise measurements of high masses and the indications of the existence of very compact stellar objects (with radii of the order of $10$ km) is relieved when assuming that very massive compact stars are quark stars and very compact stars are hadronic stars. Finally, we discuss recent interesting measurements of the eccentricities of the orbits of millisecond pulsars in low mass X-ray binaries. The high values of the eccentricities found in some cases could be explained by assuming that the hadronic star, initially present in the binary system, converts to a quark star due to the increase of its central density.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the tension between observations of very massive compact stars (around 2 solar masses) and very compact stars (radii around 10 km) can be resolved by assuming two coexisting families of compact stars, hadronic stars and quark stars, rather than a single family, with the caveat that this scenario depends on the value of the symmetry energy parameter L being in the range 40 < L < 62 MeV, which forces the early appearance of delta resonances and hyperons in hadronic matter.

## Method

The authors compute hadronic equations of state using relativistic mean-field models (GM3 and SFHo parametrizations) including the full baryon octet (nucleons and hyperons) and delta isobar resonances, with beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions at zero temperature, and also at finite entropy per baryon (s=1) with trapped neutrinos for protoneutron star conditions. For quark matter, they adopt a parametrization of perturbative QCD calculations at finite chemical potential with a scale parameter X=3.5. They then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and compare these with observational constraints from pulsar mass measurements and X-ray radius estimates.

## Key numbers

- The L parameter of the symmetry energy is constrained to lie in the range 40 < L < 62 MeV, based on recent phenomenological studies (Lattimer 2012)
- In the SFHo model, L = 47 MeV and the symmetry energy at saturation S = 32 MeV
- Quark stars can reach masses up to 2.75 solar masses as predicted by perturbative QCD calculations
- With the chosen parametrization (X=3.5), the maximum mass of quark stars is 2.53 solar masses
- PSR J1614-2230 has mass M = 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses (Demorest 2010)
- PSR J0348+0432 has mass M = 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses (Antoniadis 2013)
- The black widow pulsar PSR B1957+20 could have mass 2.4 ± 0.12 solar masses, with a lower limit of 1.66 solar masses
- Analysis of short GRB plateaus gives a peak mass for the post-merger remnant of M = 2.46 (+0.13, -0.15) solar masses (Lasky 2013, Lu 2015)
- Fits on thermal emission of 6 quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries give radius R = 9.4 ± 1.2 km (Guillot 2013, 2014)
- Analysis of X-ray bursts indicates radii of about 9.5 ± 1.5 km and masses of about M = 1.6 ± 0.2 solar masses (Ozel 2010)
- For Cyg X-2, radius of about 9 ± 0.5 km is inferred for mass 1.44 ± 0.06 solar masses (Titarchuk 2002)
- For 4U 1728-34, suggested ranges are 8.7-9.7 km for radius and 1.2-1.6 solar masses for mass (Shaposhnikov 2003)
- Hadronic stars in this scenario have maximum masses of about 1.5-1.6 solar masses due to softening from deltas and hyperons

## Context

This work extends the two-families scenario proposed by Drago (2013) and contrasts with the single-family hybrid star scenario (Chen 2011, Bonanno 2011, Zdunik 2012, Kurkela 2014) and the twin compact stars scenario (Benic 2014). The paper introduces a "delta puzzle" analogous to the well-known hyperon puzzle, showing that delta resonances appear at densities close to twice saturation density when L is in the range 40-62 MeV, and that their inclusion softens the hadronic equation of state similarly to hyperons. The scenario enables future tests through observations of binary systems, particularly the eccentricities of millisecond pulsar orbits in low-mass X-ray binaries, where conversion of hadronic stars to quark stars could explain high eccentricity values.
