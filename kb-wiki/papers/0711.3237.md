# Supernova Relic Electron Neutrinos and anti-Neutrinos in future Large-scale Observatories

> We investigate the signal from supernova relic neutrinos in future large scale observatories, such as MEMPHYS (UNO, Hyper-K), LENA and GLACIER, at present under study. We discuss that complementary information might be gained from the observation of supernova relic electron anti-neutrinos and neutrinos using the scattering on protons on one hand, and on nuclei such as oxygen, carbon or argon on the other hand. When determining the relic neutrino fluxes we also include, for the first time, the coupling of the neutrino magnetic moment to magnetic fields within the core-collapse supernova. We present numerical results on both the relic electron neutrino and anti-neutrino fluxes and on the number of events for electron neutrinos on carbon, oxygen and argon, as well as electron anti-neutrinos on protons, for various oscillation scenarios. The observation of supernova relic neutrinos might provide us with unique information on core-collapse supernova explosions, on the star formation history and on neutrino properties, that still remain unknown.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether the coupling of the neutrino magnetic moment to magnetic fields within core-collapse supernovae can produce observable effects in the supernova relic neutrino signal at future large-scale detectors, while acknowledging that present understanding of supernova magnetic fields is still limited and the calculations assume a static density profile without shock wave effects.

## Method

The authors solve the three-flavor neutrino evolution equations including both the Mikheev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein matter effect and the resonant spin-flavor conversion due to neutrino magnetic moments coupling to a power-law magnetic field profile in a static 13.2 solar mass progenitor. They then integrate the resulting neutrino spectra from a single supernova over redshift using a star-formation-rate-based supernova rate function to obtain the relic neutrino fluxes, and multiply these by cross sections for detection channels on protons, oxygen, carbon, and argon to predict event rates in MEMPHYS, LENA, and GLACIER detectors.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino temperatures at the neutrinosphere: T_nu_e = 3.5 MeV, T_nu_bar_e = 5.1 MeV, T_nu_x = 7.0 MeV, giving average energies of 11 MeV, 16 MeV, and 22 MeV respectively
- Total supernova luminosity fixed at L_0 = 3 x 10^53 ergs, equally shared among all flavors
- Supernova rate function: R_SN(z) = 1.22 x 10^-2 x 0.3 f_star h_65 exp(3.4z)/(exp(3.8z)+45) yr^-1 Mpc^-3 with f_star = 1
- Best upper limits on SRN fluxes: 6.8 x 10^3 nu_e cm^-2 s^-1 (25-50 MeV) from LSD and 1.2 nu_bar_e cm^-2 s^-1 (E > 19.3 MeV) from Super-Kamiokande at 90% C.L.
- Relic nu_bar_e flux above 19.3 MeV ranges from 0.4-0.7 cm^-2 s^-1 depending on neutrino parameters, compatible with the Super-Kamiokande limit of 1.2 cm^-2 s^-1
- Relic nu_e number flux integrated 1-50 MeV ranges from 14.8-15.7 cm^-2 s^-1 across oscillation scenarios (MSW case)
- Relic nu_bar_e number flux integrated 1-50 MeV ranges from 10.3-10.8 cm^-2 s^-1 across oscillation scenarios (MSW case)
- Magnetic field profile: B(r) = B_0 (r_B/r)^n with r_B = 1.4 x 10^8 cm and n = 2 or 3
- Product mu_ij B_0 values considered: 0.1 for n=2, 1 for n=3 (denoted (mu B)_large)
- Oscillation parameters: Delta m^2_12 = 8 x 10^-5 eV^2, sin^2 2theta_12 = 0.86, Delta m^2_23 = 3 x 10^-3 eV^2, sin^2 2theta_23 = 0.96
- Two values for theta_13: sin^2 2theta_13 = 0.19 (upper limit) or 10^-4 (small)
- Detector fiducial masses: 440 kton for MEMPHYS (3 shafts), 50 kton for LENA, 100 kton for GLACIER
- In the inverted hierarchy with large theta_13 (IH-L), anti-neutrino events are larger by 50-60% compared to other cases without magnetic fields
- With strong magnetic fields in normal hierarchy, anti-neutrino event rates can increase by 30-50%

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of supernova relic neutrino fluxes by Ando (2002), Strigari (2003), and others by including for the first time the coupling of the neutrino magnetic moment to supernova magnetic fields via resonant spin-flavor conversion. The paper considers the three detector technologies (water Cerenkov, scintillator, liquid argon) being studied for MEMPHYS/UNO/Hyper-K, LENA, and GLACIER as described by Autiero (2007). The results enable future studies of how observations of supernova relic neutrinos might constrain neutrino magnetic moments, supernova magnetic field strengths, the neutrino mass hierarchy, and the value of theta_13, while also providing event rate predictions that can guide detector design and background rejection strategies.
