# Large Sound Speed in Dense Matter and the Deformability of Neutron Stars

> The historic first detection of the binary neutron star merger GW170817 by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration has set a limit on the gravitational deformability of neutron stars. In contrast, radio observations of PSR J0740+6620 find a very massive neutron star. Tension between the small deformability and the large maximum mass may suggest that the pressure rises rapidly with density and thus the speed of sound in dense matter is likely a large fraction of the speed of light. We use these observations and simple constant sound-speed model equations of state to set a lower bound on the maximum speed of sound in neutron stars. If the tidal deformability of a 1.4$M_\odot$ neutron star is less than 600, as is suggested by subsequent analyses of GW170817, we find that the sound speed in the cores of neutron stars is likely larger than the conformal limit of $c/\sqrt{3}$. Implications of this for our understanding of both hadronic and quark-gluon descriptions of dense matter are discussed.

## Key claim

The tension between the small tidal deformability of neutron stars inferred from GW170817 and the large maximum mass from PSR J0740+6620 suggests that the speed of sound in dense matter likely exceeds the conformal limit of \(c/\sqrt{3}\), provided the tidal deformability of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star is less than 600.

## Method

The authors modify twelve representative nuclear equations of state (EOS) by introducing a first-order phase transition at nuclear density \(\rho_0 = 150\) MeV/fm\(^3\), above which the EOS has a constant sound speed squared \(C_s^2\). For each model EOS, they choose a transition density \(\rho_2\) and adjust \(C_s^2\) so that the maximum neutron star mass is fixed to 2.1 \(M_\odot\), consistent with PSR J0740+6620. They then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations and compute the tidal deformability \(\Lambda_{1.4}\) of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star, treating the discontinuity in sound speed at the phase transition with a Taylor expansion of the dimensionless quantity \(y(r)\).

## Key numbers

- The central value of the LIGO GW170817 observation for the tidal deformability of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) neutron star is \(\Lambda_{1.4} = 190^{+390}_{-120}\).
- If \(\Lambda_{1.4} \approx 190\), the band of sound speed squared values from the model EOSs gives \(C_s^2 > 0.55\), corresponding to a maximum sound speed exceeding \(0.74c\).
- The absolute lower limit on deformability across all twelve EOSs, when the sound speed reaches the causal limit \(C_s^2 = 1\), is \(\Lambda_{1.4} \ge 77.35\).
- For the IUFSU model at \(\Lambda_{1.4} = 190\), the sound speed squared is \(C_s^2 = 0.588\), the radius of a 1.4 \(M_\odot\) star is \(R_{1.4} = 10.58\) km, and the transition density is \(\rho_2 = 382.98\) MeV/fm\(^3\).

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions of sound speed in dense matter by Bedaque and Steiner and by Moustakidis et al. (2017), which predated the deformability constraints from GW170817. It also builds on the constant sound-speed EOS approach used by Alford, Han, and Prakash (2013) and by Han and Steiner (2019). The results enable tighter constraints on the nature of dense matter, suggesting that QCD matter at neutron star densities is not asymptotically free but instead involves strong interactions, consistent with relativistic meson-baryon field theory models such as those of Walecka (1974) and with the nucleon hard-core picture from Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998).
