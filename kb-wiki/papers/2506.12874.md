# Sign-Problem-Free Nuclear Quantum Monte Carlo Simulation

> Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods offer exact solutions for quantum many-body systems but face severe limitations in fermionic systems like atomic nuclei due to the sign problem. While sign-problem-free QMC algorithms exist and provide valuable insights across disciplines, they have been restricted to simple models with limited quantitative predictive power. Here we overcome this barrier by developing a novel lattice nuclear force that is rigorously sign-problem-free for even-even nuclei. This interaction achieves a standard deviation of $σ= 2.932$ MeV from experimental binding energies for 76 even-even nuclei ($N,Z \leq 28$), matching state-of-the-art phenomenological mean-field models. Key innovations include the first sign-problem-free implementation of spin-orbit coupling for shell evolutions and an efficient QMC-optimized framework for global parameter fitting. Using this approach, we compute binding energies from $^4$He to $^{132}$Sn with unprecedented one-thousandth level numerical precision, reproduce symmetric nuclear matter saturation, and reveal novel spin-orbit-driven clustering in light nuclei. This work transforms sign-problem-free QMC into a scalable and predictive nuclear structure tool, while establishing a high-fidelity, non-perturbative foundation for \textit{ab initio} calculations of heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors claim to have developed the first sign-problem-free nuclear quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) interaction that quantitatively reproduces experimental binding energies across light to heavy even-even nuclei, but this claim is qualified by the restriction to even-even nuclei only (odd-even and odd-odd nuclei retain a mild sign problem) and by systematic overbinding at large neutron numbers that indicates missing physics such as pion-exchange potentials.

## Method

The authors constructed a minimal nuclear force on a three-dimensional cubic lattice, comprising a Wigner-SU(4) interaction (spin- and isospin-independent two- and three-body contact terms) plus a newly introduced spin-orbit coupling term, with local and non-local smearing parameters controlling the interaction range. They solved the Hamiltonian using auxiliary-field Monte Carlo with imaginary-time projection, where the trial wave function for even-even nuclei is a Slater determinant of time-reversal-paired nucleon wave functions, ensuring a positive-definite fermion determinant and thus a sign-problem-free simulation. The four free parameters (C2, C3, Cs, sL) were optimized against experimental binding energies of six nuclei (4He, 16O, 24Mg, 28Si, 32S, 40Ca) using a steepest-descent method with derivatives computed via the Feynman-Hellmann theorem simultaneously in the QMC runs.

## Key numbers

- Standard deviation from experimental binding energies: σ = 2.932 MeV for 76 even-even nuclei with N, Z ≤ 28
- Lattice spacing: a = 1.32 fm
- Nucleon mass: M = 938.92 MeV
- Temporal step: a_t = (1000 MeV)^(-1)
- Optimal parameters: C2 = -4.410 × 10^(-7) MeV^(-2), C3 = 1.561 × 10^(-15) MeV^(-5), Cs = 8.590 × 10^(-12) MeV^(-4), sL = 8.082 × 10^(-2); sNL fixed to 0.45
- Saturation point of symmetric nuclear matter: (ρ0, E/A) = (0.175 fm^(-3), -15.4 MeV)
- Binding energy of 100Sn: -824.7(8) MeV (experimental: -825.2 MeV)
- Binding energy of 132Sn: -1134.2(27) MeV (experimental: -1102.8 MeV)
- Relative errors below 3% for 4He and 132Sn, and below 1% for other representative nuclei
- Spin-orbit contribution E_sl accounts for 5-10% of binding energies on average; for 56Ni, E_sl/E_bind = 0.156; for 100Sn, E_sl/E_bind = 0.125; for 132Sn, E_sl/E_bind = 0.098

## Context

This work extends the sign-problem-free Wigner-SU(4) interaction previously studied by the same group (Niu and Lu, 2019) by adding a spin-orbit coupling term that enables shell evolution, achieving accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art phenomenological mean-field models (which typically give σ ≈ 2-3 MeV in this mass region). The approach enables direct, unbiased QMC simulations of heavy nuclei up to 132Sn with one-thousandth-level numerical precision, whereas existing nuclear QMC approaches using high-fidelity chiral forces remain confined to light and medium-mass systems and require error mitigation techniques. The framework establishes a non-perturbative foundation for perturbative incorporation of missing physics (such as pion-exchange potentials) and provides an exactly solvable benchmark for evaluating advanced algorithms including artificial neural networks and quantum computing.
