# Nuclear Astrophysics: CIPANP 2006

> I review progress that has been made in nuclear astrophysics over the past few years and summarize some of the questions that remain. Topics selected include solar neutrinos, supernovae (the explosion and associated nucleosynthesis), laboratory astrophysics, and neutron star structure.

# Nuclear Astrophysics: CIPANP 2006

## Key claim
This review paper summarizes progress in nuclear astrophysics across several subfields but does not present a single new result; instead it argues that the field is entering an era where multi-dimensional simulations, new laboratory measurements, and neutrino discoveries will together resolve long-standing problems in supernova mechanisms, nucleosynthesis, and neutron star structure, though many of these connections remain qualitative rather than quantitatively established.

## Method
The paper is a conference review talk covering four main topics: solar neutrinos and the CNO cycle, core-collapse supernova mechanisms, nucleosynthesis including the r-process and neutrino process, and neutron star structure. For each topic, the author summarizes recent experimental and theoretical results from the literature, identifies open questions, and discusses connections between laboratory measurements, astrophysical observations, and computational modeling.

- S-factor for 14N(p,γ): S14+1(0) = 1.61 ± 0.08 keV-b, measured by LUNA down to 70 keV center-of-mass energy, almost a factor of two below the former best value

- Cosmological upper bound on neutrino mass sum: Σ mν(i) ≤ 0.5 eV from CMB, large-scale structure, and Type Ia supernova observations
- Neutron star masses: 1.2-2.0 M⊙, radii 7-15 km
- Fastest pulsar Terzan5: 1.396 ms (716 Hz)
- Redshift claim for EXO 0748-676: 0.35 from Fe lines
- Keplerian velocity relation: vKepler ∼ 1045 (M/M⊙)^(1/2) (10 km/R)^(3/2) Hz
- Standard solar model pp flux uncertainty: ≤ 1%
- CNO cycle contributes to solar energy generation at about the 1% level
- LUNA measurement extends to 70 keV center-of-mass energy, the region of the Gamow peak
- Age increase for oldest globular cluster stars: about 0.8 Gyr from revised CNO rate
- CNO solar neutrino flux reduction: by half from revised 14N(p,γ) rate
- Super-Kamiokande relic neutrino limit: within about a factor of three of theoretical predictions
- KATRIN tritium experiment target: push 2.2 eV bound down to about 0.2 eV
- Future ββ-decay experiments may probe masses at 10-50 meV
- Anticipated Planck/LSST upper bound on neutrino mass sum: could drop to 50 meV
- Neutron star moment of inertia expected accuracy: about 10% after five years of observations of PSRJ0737-3039

- Shock wave stalls 200 to 300 km from the star's center
- Time relevant to explosion mechanism: about 1 second
- Neutrino light curve measurement requires detectors to follow out to ≥ 20 seconds
- Galactic supernova opportunity: may arise only once every 30 years

## Context
This review extends the tradition of nuclear astrophysics summaries at CIPANP conferences and covers work building on the standard solar model of Bahcall (2004), the core-collapse supernova simulations of Mezzacappa (2005) and 3D modelers, and the r-process abundance studies of Cowan (2005) and Qian (2005). It discusses the implications of new LUNA measurements for stellar evolution models and the connection between neutron star observations and the nuclear equation of state work of Lattimer and Prakash (2001). The paper enables future work by identifying specific open questions: the need for multi-D supernova simulations with full neutrino transport, the measurement of θ13 for understanding flavor conversion in supernovae, and the development of underground accelerators like the proposed DUSEL facility for nuclear astrophysics.
