# Separation of elastic and inelastic processes in the relaxation time approximation for collision integral

> We introduce a generalised relaxation-time-approximation form of the collision term in the Boltzmann kinetic equation that allows for using different relaxation times for elastic and inelastic collisions. The efficacy of the proposed framework is demonstrated with the numerical calculations that describe systems with different relations between the two relaxation times and the evolution time of the system.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a generalized relaxation-time-approximation (RTA) collision term for the Boltzmann kinetic equation that uses separate relaxation times for elastic and inelastic collisions, but the framework is restricted to constant relaxation times and massless particles, with the demonstration limited to one-dimensional boost-invariant expansion.

## Method

The authors propose a modified collision term of the form \(C[f] = p \cdot u \left[ \frac{f_{eq}(\mu,T)-f}{\tau_{el}} + \frac{f_{eq}(0,T)-f}{\tau_{in}} \right]\), where \(\tau_{el}\) and \(\tau_{in}\) are the relaxation times for elastic and inelastic processes respectively. They supplement this with generalized Landau matching conditions for energy density and particle number density, then solve the resulting kinetic equation analytically for the case of one-dimensional boost-invariant expansion using an iterative numerical method to determine the effective temperature \(T(\tau)\) and chemical potential \(\mu(\tau)\).

## Key numbers

- Initial conditions: proper time \(\tau_0 = 0.5\) fm, initial temperature \(T_0 = 600\) MeV, initial chemical potential \(\mu_0 = 0\)
- For \(\tau_{el} = \tau_{in} = 0.5\) fm: the ratio \(\mu/T\) reaches a minimum of approximately -0.03
- For \(\tau_{el} = 0.25\) fm and \(\tau_{in} = 100\) fm: the ratio \(\mu/T\) reaches a minimum of approximately -0.36
- Evolution time studied: from \(\tau = 0.5\) fm to \(\tau = 5\) fm

## Context

This work extends the standard RTA framework of Bhatnagar, Gross, and Krook (1954) and Anderson (1974) by allowing separate treatment of elastic and inelastic collision timescales, motivated by recent discussions in Blaizot (2016) and Huang (2013) about the need for different relaxation times for different interaction types. The framework enables future studies of Bose-Einstein condensation in heavy-ion collisions, as discussed by Blaizot (2011-2015) and Begun (2013, 2015), and can serve as a basis for deriving new equations of dissipative and anisotropic hydrodynamics.
