# Neural-Network-Based Variational Method in Nuclear Density Functional Theory: Application to the Extended Thomas-Fermi Model

> We propose a neural-network-based variational framework for nuclear Density Functional Theory based on the extended Thomas--Fermi (ETF) model, in which proton and neutron number densities are represented by multilayer perceptrons and determined by direct minimization of a Skyrme-type energy density functional. We clarify the mathematical connection to the conventional Euler--Lagrange formulation, showing that stationarity in parameter space corresponds to a projected Euler--Lagrange condition on the neural-network trial-density manifold. The basic validity of the framework is examined through three sets of calculations: a Woods--Saxon potential benchmark, ground-state calculations of finite nuclei ($^{40}$Ca, $^{90}$Zr, and $^{208}$Pb), and nuclear pasta phases. The binding energies of finite nuclei agree with existing ETF calculations to within $0.5\%$, and representative pasta structures including spheres, rods, and slabs are reproduced. We also find that single-precision arithmetic yields results comparable to double precision, suggesting that the present framework is well suited to GPU environments in which low-precision computation is advantageous.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a neural-network-based variational framework for nuclear Density Functional Theory using the Extended Thomas-Fermi model, where the key hedge is that the method achieves stationarity in parameter space corresponding to a *projected* Euler-Lagrange condition on the neural-network trial-density manifold, not the exact Euler-Lagrange condition itself.

## Method

The proton and neutron number densities are represented by multilayer perceptrons (MLPs) taking spatial coordinates as input, with nonnegativity enforced by an exponential output and particle-number constraints satisfied by explicit normalization. The Skyrme-type energy density functional is used directly as the loss function, and the network parameters are optimized via gradient-based methods (Adam optimizer) with automatic differentiation, without analytically deriving or solving the Euler-Lagrange equations. An auxiliary guiding potential is used during early training stages to stabilize the density profile, then removed for the final optimization.

- For ²⁰⁸Pb: binding energy −1630.8 MeV, neutron radius 5.614 fm, proton radius 5.467 fm (reference: −1631.9 MeV, 5.62 fm, 5.47 fm)
- Woods-Saxon benchmark reference energy: −1323.2 MeV; NN results deviate at about 10⁻¹% level
- Typical computational grid: dx=dy=dz=0.5 fm, Nx=Ny=Nz=64, box size 32 fm
- Typical optimization steps: 50000 for finite nuclei, 100000 for pasta phases
- Learning rate: η = 10⁻³
- Single-precision arithmetic yields results comparable to double precision

## Context

This work extends the conventional Euler-Lagrange formulation of the Extended Thomas-Fermi model (Bartel 1985, Centelles 1990) by replacing analytic derivation and solution of the mean-field equations with neural-network-based direct minimization. It builds on prior ETF calculations for finite nuclei and nuclear pasta phases (Centelles 1990, Nakamura) and connects to the broader AI-for-science movement where frameworks like FermiNet (Pfau 2020) and PauliNet (Hermann 2020) have demonstrated neural-network variational methods in electronic structure. The approach enables future extensions to higher-order ETF corrections, finite-temperature effects (Brack 1985), and potentially to Skyrme Hartree-Fock and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov levels, while being naturally suited to GPU environments where low-precision computation is advantageous.
