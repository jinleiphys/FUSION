# Classical and relavistic simple wave problems solved with Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics

> To simulate the expansion of the matter created in relativistic nuclear collisions, codes in 3+1 dimensions are used and we are developing a new one. To benchmark such codes, the Sod's shock tube is often used. A closely related problem is the one-dimensional expansion of a gas into vacuum. In this paper, we study this problem classically and relativistically with the Smoothed Particle Method and test various techniques to improve the precision and speed of the solution.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) method can reproduce the exact analytical solutions for both classical and relativistic one-dimensional gas expansion into vacuum (the "simple wave" problem), but the precision depends strongly on the number of SPH particles and the choice of fixed versus variable smoothing length, with the relativistic case reaching the maximum velocity even at modest particle numbers unlike the classical case.

## Method

The authors solve the one-dimensional Euler equations for a polytropic gas expanding into vacuum using the Lagrangian SPH method, with a cubic spline kernel for density interpolation and the standard SPH equation of motion that conserves total linear and angular momenta. For the relativistic case, they use entropy density as the conserved quantity and evolve a time derivative of the momentum variable \(P = (\epsilon + p)\gamma^2 v / s^*\) with a fixed speed of sound \(c_s = 1/\sqrt{3}\). They compare numerical results against the known analytical solutions for both classical and relativistic simple wave problems, testing fixed smoothing length with varying particle numbers \(N\) and variable smoothing length approaches.

## Key numbers

- Classical case: For \(z = -c_0 t\), density \(\rho = \rho_0\) and velocity \(v = 0\); for \(z = 2c_0/(\gamma-1) t\), density \(\rho = 0\) and velocity \(v = 2c_0/(\gamma-1)\); for \(z = 0\), density \(\rho = \rho_0 (2c_0/(\gamma+1))^{2/(\gamma-1)}\) and velocity \(v = 2c_0/(\gamma+1)\).

- Relativistic case: For \(z = -c_0 t\), velocity \(v = 0\) and energy density \(\epsilon = \epsilon_0\); for \(z = t\), velocity \(v = 1\) and energy density \(\epsilon = 0\); for \(z = 0\), velocity \(v = c_s\) and energy density \(\epsilon = \epsilon_0 (e^{-c_s \tanh^{-1} c_s})^{(1+c_s^2)/c_s^2}\).

- Relativistic SPH simulations used \(N = 2000\), \(N = 15000\), and \(N = 40000\) particles with fixed smoothing length; increasing to 40000 led to little improvement over 15000.

- Classical SPH with variable smoothing length and small \(N\) gave a precise solution but the velocity did not reach the maximum value \(2c_0/(\gamma-1)\).

## Context

This work extends the benchmarking of SPH codes beyond the standard Sod shock tube problem (commonly used for code validation) to the simple wave problem of gas expansion into vacuum, which is directly relevant to relativistic nuclear collisions where matter expands into vacuum. The authors cite the original SPH developments by Lucy (1977) and Gingold and Monaghan (1977), as well as the relativistic SPH formulation by Aguiar and the analytical solutions for simple waves from Rischke and others. The results enable future benchmarking of the authors' three-dimensional hydrodynamic code for simulating relativistic nuclear collisions, with planned improvements including variable smoothing length with a different method, particle splitting, and imaginary particles.
