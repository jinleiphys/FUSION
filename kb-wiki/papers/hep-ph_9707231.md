# Higher Resonance Contamination of pi NN Couplings Obtained Via the Three-Point Function Method in QCD Sum Rules

> We investigate the size of potential higher pseudoscalar resonance contaminations of the estimates of isospin-conserving and isospin-violating $πNN$ couplings obtained using the 3-point function method in QCD sum rules. For the isospin-conserving case it is shown that conventional models of the isovector pseudoscalar spectral function imply resonance decay constants large enough to create significant contaminations, and that assuming these models are incorrect, and that the decay constants are actually much smaller, implies physically implausible values for the flavor-breaking quark condensate ratios. For the isospin-violating case it is shown explicitly that such resonance contamination is unavoidably present and precludes using the 3-point function method as a means of estimating the at present unmeasured isospin-violating $πNN$ couplings.

# Higher Resonance Contamination of πNN Couplings Obtained Via the Three-Point Function Method in QCD Sum Rules

## Key claim

The three-point function QCD sum rule method for extracting πNN couplings is unreliable because higher pseudoscalar resonance contributions are unavoidably large; for the isospin-conserving case, conventional spectral function models imply resonance decay constants that create significant contamination, while for the isospin-violating case, the contamination is shown explicitly to preclude any reliable estimate of the unmeasured couplings.

## Method

The author analyzes the three-point vacuum correlator of two nucleon interpolating fields (Ioffe currents) and a pseudoscalar current, comparing the operator product expansion (OPE) side with a hadronic model that includes the pion and excited pseudoscalar resonances such as π(1300) and π(1800). For the isospin-conserving case, the decay constants of excited resonances are taken from finite energy sum rule models of the isovector pseudoscalar spectral function (Bijnens, Prades, de Rafael 1995). For the isospin-violating case, the author generalizes the Meissner-Henley interpolating field for the π⁰ to a family of fields parameterized by α, and examines the dependence of the extracted coupling difference δg = g_{π⁰nn} − g_{π⁰pp} on this parameter, using next-to-leading order chiral perturbation theory results for the mixing parameters.

## Key numbers

- For the model spectral function with the best duality fit, the decay constants are f_{π(1300)} = 2.2 MeV and f_{π(1800)} = 1.0 MeV.
- At Q² = 1 GeV², the product f_M m²_M / (Q² + m²_M) equals 1.8 MeV for the π, 2.7 MeV for the π(1300), and 0.76 MeV for the π(1800).
- The leading-order π-η mixing angle is θ₀ = (1.1 ± 0.2) × 10⁻².
- For the Meissner-Henley choice α = −1/3, the contribution to −δg/g_{πNN} from the isoscalar component required to remove η contamination is 1.1 × 10⁻²; for α = −1, this contribution is 3.1 × 10⁻².
- The range of values for −δg/g_{πNN} extracted by Meissner and Henley (1997) was (1.7–3.0) × 10⁻².
- Assuming extremely small excited resonance decay constants (a factor 60 smaller than the model) forces the strange-to-up quark condensate ratio above 1.38 and the isospin-breaking condensate ratio γ above 1.5 × 10⁻³, contradicting extractions that obtain γ < 0.

## Context

This work extends the critique by Birse and Krippa (1996) of the three-point function method for πNN couplings, providing quantitative evidence that higher resonance contamination is unavoidable. The author contrasts the three-point function approach with the two-point function method (Reinders, Rubinstein, Yazaki 1983; Birse and Krippa 1996; Shiomi and Hatsuda 1995), which avoids resonance contamination but suffers from uncertainties in higher-dimension operator matrix elements. The analysis uses finite energy sum rule results from Bijnens, Prades, and de Rafael (1995) and chiral perturbation theory from Gasser and Leutwyler (1985). The conclusion that the three-point function method cannot reliably estimate the isospin-violating πNN couplings leaves the two-point function method as the remaining viable QCD sum rule approach for this quantity, though it requires improved knowledge of higher-dimension operator matrix elements.
