# Neutrino oscillations: measuring $θ_{13}$ including its sign

> In neutrino phenomenology, terms in the oscillation probabilities linear in $\sin θ_{13}$ lead naturally to the question ``How can one measure $θ_{13}$ including its sign?'' Here we demonstrate analytically and with a simulation of neutrino data that ${\mathcal P}_{eμ}$ and ${\mathcal {P}_{μμ}$ at $L/E = 2π/Δ_{21}$ exhibit significant linear dependence on $θ_{13}$ in the limit of vacuum oscillations. Measurements at this particular value of $L/E$ can thus determine not only $θ_{13}$ but also its sign, if CP violation is small.

# Neutrino oscillations: measuring $\theta_{13}$ including its sign

## Key claim
The mixing angle $\theta_{13}$ appears linearly in the oscillation probabilities ${\mathcal P}_{e\mu}$ and ${\mathcal P}_{\mu\mu}$ at $L/E = 2\pi/\Delta_{21}$ in vacuum oscillations, and this linear term dominates the quadratic term at that specific $L/E$, enabling determination of both the magnitude and sign of $\theta_{13}$, provided that CP violation is small (i.e., $\delta$ near zero or $\pi$).

## Method
The authors analytically expanded the three-flavor neutrino oscillation probabilities in the limit where oscillations due to the atmospheric mass-squared difference ($\Delta_{32} \sim 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$) are incoherent while those due to the solar mass-squared difference ($\Delta_{21} \sim 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$) remain coherent. They isolated terms odd in $\theta_{13}$ and identified $L/E = 2(2n+1)\pi/\Delta_{12}$ as the optimal baseline-to-energy ratio where $\sin^2\varphi_{12}$ is maximal and CP-violating terms vanish. They then performed a simulation of existing neutrino oscillation data (solar, reactor, atmospheric, and K2K beam-stop data) using a $\chi^2$ analysis, fixing all parameters except $\theta_{13}$ to values from a fit with $\theta_{13}=0$.

## Key numbers
- The optimal $L/E$ for measuring the sign of $\theta_{13}$ is $L/E = 2\pi/\Delta_{21}$, corresponding to $L/E \approx 1.6 \times 10^4$ m/MeV for the first peak.
- At the one-standard-deviation bounds from the global fit, $\theta_{13} \in [-0.17, 0.22]$ (radians), with two minima at $\theta_{13} = 0.11$ and $-0.04$.
- For $\theta_{13} = -0.17$ and $0.22$, the oscillation probabilities at the optimal $L/E$ are: ${\mathcal P}_{e\mu}(-0.17) = 0.35$, ${\mathcal P}_{e\mu}(0.22) = 0.50$; ${\mathcal P}_{\mu\mu}(-0.17) = 0.37$, ${\mathcal P}_{\mu\mu}(0.22) = 0.22$.
- The relative difference for ${\mathcal P}_{\mu\mu}$ between the two $\theta_{13}$ extremes is $-0.25$, and for ${\mathcal P}_{e\mu}$ it is $+0.17$.
- The best-fit parameters at the absolute minimum ($\theta_{13}=0.11$) are: $\theta_{12}=0.48$, $\theta_{23}=0.80$, $\Delta_{21}=7.7\times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$, $\Delta_{32}=2.6\times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$.
- The ideal experimental baseline is $L = 80$ km with neutrino energies in the range 5 to 50 MeV (below the MSW resonance energy of about 100 MeV in the Earth's mantle).

## Context
This work extends the authors' earlier analysis (Latimer and Ernst, 2004) showing that the $\chi^2$ space for $\theta_{13}$ is asymmetric, and builds on the parameterization convention proposed in their previous work (Latimer and Ernst, 2004) where $\theta_{13}$ is allowed to range from $-\pi/2$ to $+\pi/2$ rather than the conventional $[0, \pi/2]$ range. It contradicts the common "one mass-squared dominance" approximation that yields probabilities depending only on $\sin^2\theta_{13}$, which the authors show is inadequate. The work enables future long-baseline experiments to design measurements specifically targeting $L/E = 2\pi/\Delta_{21}$ to determine both the magnitude and sign of $\theta_{13}$, and raises open questions about the correlation between $\theta_{13}$ and $\theta_{23}$, the role of CP violation, and the impact of matter effects (MSW effect) at higher energies.
