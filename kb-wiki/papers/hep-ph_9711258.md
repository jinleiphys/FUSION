# Non-equilibrium evolution of a `Tsunami': Dynamical Symmetry Breaking

> We propose to study the non-equilibrium features of heavy-ion collisions by following the evolution of an initial state with a large number of quanta with a distribution around a momentum |\vec k_0| corresponding to a thin spherical shell in momentum space, a `tsunami'. An O(N); ({\vec Φ}^2)^2 model field theory in the large N limit is used as a framework to study the non-perturbative aspects of the non-equilibrium dynamics including a resummation of the effects of the medium (the initial particle distribution). In a theory where the symmetry is spontaneously broken in the absence of the medium, when the initial number of particles per correlation volume is chosen to be larger than a critical value the medium effects can restore the symmetry of the initial state. We show that if one begins with such a symmetry-restored, non-thermal, initial state, non-perturbative effects automatically induce spinodal instabilities leading to a dynamical breaking of the symmetry. As a result there is explosive particle production and a redistribution of the particles towards low momentum due to the nonlinearity of the dynamics. The asymptotic behavior displays the onset of Bose condensation of pions and the equation of state at long times is that of an ultrarelativistic gas although the momentum distribution is non-thermal.

## Key claim

In an O(N) scalar field theory where the symmetry is spontaneously broken in vacuum, an initial non-thermal state with a high density of particles concentrated on a thin spherical shell in momentum space (a "tsunami") can have its symmetry restored by medium effects if the initial particle number per correlation volume exceeds a critical value; subsequently, non-perturbative spinodal instabilities automatically induce dynamical symmetry breaking, leading to explosive particle production and redistribution toward low momentum, with the asymptotic equation of state being ultrarelativistic despite a non-thermal momentum distribution.

## Method

The authors study the non-equilibrium evolution of an O(N) (Φ²)² model field theory in the large N limit, using a Gaussian wave-functional ansatz for the initial state with a particle distribution sharply peaked around a momentum |k₀| on a thin spherical shell in momentum space. The dynamics are solved self-consistently via the functional Schrödinger equation, yielding coupled equations for the order parameter φ(t) and the mode functions φ_k(t), with the self-consistent condition that the effective mass depends on the time-dependent expectation value ⟨π²⟩(t); renormalization is performed by absorbing quadratic and logarithmic divergences into the mass and coupling constant.

## Key numbers

- The initial particle distribution is taken as a Gaussian peaked at q₀ = k₀/M_R with width ξ, where the total number of particles in a correlation volume M_R⁻³ is N₀; the high-density regime corresponds to N₀ >> 1.
- The effective mass at the initial time, M_R² = ℳ²_{R π}(t=0), is obtained from a gap equation that includes a non-equilibrium contribution from the particle distribution, analogous to the hard-thermal-loop resummed mass.
- The dimensionless coupling is defined as g = λ_R/(8π²), and the dimensionless time is τ = M_R t.
- The initial occupation number for modes near the peak is n_q(0) ≈ N₀/I exp[-(q-q₀)²/ξ²], with N₀ the number of particles per correlation volume.

## Context

This work extends non-equilibrium field theory methods previously applied to phase transitions and particle production (e.g., Boyanovsky et al. (1995) on spinodal instabilities, and the large N studies by Cooper et al. (1994-1996)) to a novel initial condition, a "tsunami" configuration, relevant for heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. The approach contrasts with parton cascade models (Wang, Geiger, Eskola, 1990s) and hydrodynamic descriptions (Bjorken, 1983) by addressing non-perturbative dynamics from a strongly out-of-equilibrium initial state without assuming local thermodynamic equilibrium; it enables future study of disoriented chiral condensate formation (Rajagopal and Wilczek, 1993) and Bose condensation of pions in a non-thermal setting.
