# Fragments in Gaussian Wave-Packet Dynamics with and without correlations

> Generalization of Gaussian trial wave functions in quantum molecular dynamics models is introduced, which allows for long-range correlations characteristic for composite nuclear fragments. We demonstrate a significant improvement in the description of light fragments with correlations. Utilizing either type of Gaussian wave functions, with or without correlations, however, we find that we cannot describe fragment formation in a dynamic situation. Composite fragments are only produced in simulations if they are present as clusters in the substructure of original nuclei. The difficulty is traced to the delocalization of wave functions during emission. Composite fragments are produced abundantly in the Gaussian molecular dynamics in the limit $\hbar \rightarrow 0$.

# Fragments in Gaussian Wave-Packet Dynamics with and without Correlations

## Key claim

While introducing correlated Gaussian trial wave functions significantly improves the description of light fragments compared to uncorrelated wave functions, neither correlated nor uncorrelated Gaussian wave-packet dynamics can describe fragment formation in dynamic situations; composite fragments are only produced in simulations if they are already present as clusters in the substructure of the original nuclei, and the difficulty is traced to the delocalization of wave functions during emission.

## Method

The authors generalized the Gaussian trial wave functions used in Fermionic Molecular Dynamics (FMD) by introducing off-diagonal elements in the width matrix A, allowing for correlations between nucleons within fragments and decoupling of the fragment center-of-mass motion. They derived equations of motion from the time-dependent variational principle using a Volkov-type interaction and a Pauli potential to approximately account for antisymmetrization. They then simulated the time evolution of isolated light fragments (deuterons and alpha particles), excited nuclear systems expanding into vacuum (A=20-80), and symmetric heavy-ion collisions (12C+12C and 40Ca+40Ca), comparing dynamics with correlated wave functions, uncorrelated wave functions, and static (classical) widths.

## Key numbers

- For 12C ground state with diagonal width matrix and B1 interaction: minimum internal energy E_int = -87.1 MeV at Re A_ii = 0.33 fm^-2, giving R_rms = 2.32 fm
- For 12C ground state with off-diagonal width matrix: lower minimum E_int = -95.4 MeV at Re A_ii = 0.30 fm^-2 and Re A_ij = -0.038 fm^-2 for particles within the same cluster
- Three alpha particles in 12C have ground state energy E_int = -28.1 MeV
- Coulomb energy for 12C estimated at E_C = 11.4 MeV
- For the deuteron with uncorrelated dynamics, at t ~ 12 fm/c the deuteron becomes unbound
- For the excited system studies (A=20-80), initial temperatures T = 5-12 MeV and collective flow energies E_col = 0-25 MeV/nucleon were used
- For the 12C+12C reaction, beam energy of 29 MeV/nucleon was used
- For the 40Ca+40Ca reaction, beam energy of 35 MeV/nucleon was used
- Optimal Pauli potential strength u_3 = 70 MeV for A=12 and u_3 = 200 MeV for A=80
- When taking hbar reduced by a factor of 5-10, cluster production began to be observed
- At t = 300 fm/c for the excited system simulations, all relative energies of emitted particles were positive

## Context

This work extends the FMD approach of Feldmeier (1995) by introducing correlated trial wave functions to address the problem that in standard FMD, the center-of-mass localization energy for light fragments (A=2-4, Delta E ~ 10-30 MeV) can produce significant thermal penalty factors for emission given typical nuclear temperatures of 5-10 MeV. The results contradict expectations from QMD calculations by Aichelin (1986) and Maruyama (1990), where fragment production has been observed, and from FMD calculations by Feldmeier (1995) where multifragmentation events were reported; however, the authors argue that clusters in those FMD calculations were present in initial states and survived reactions rather than being formed dynamically. The finding that fragment formation fails due to wave-packet delocalization during emission suggests that Gaussian wave-packet dynamics, even with correlations, cannot describe the formation of new clusters in expanding nuclear systems, and that classical dynamics (hbar -> 0) or alternative approaches are needed for such processes.
