# The intermediate neutron capture process II.Nuclear uncertainties

> Carbon-enhanced metal-poor (CEMP) r/s-stars show surface-abundance distributions characteristic of the so-called intermediate neutron capture process (i-process) of nucleosynthesis. We previously showed that the ingestion of protons in the convective helium-burning region of a low-mass low-metallicity star can explain the surface abundance distribution observed in CEMP r/s stars relatively well. Such an i-process requires detailed reaction network calculations involving hundreds of nuclei for which reaction rates have not yet been determined experimentally. We investigate the nuclear physics uncertainties affecting the i-process during the AGB phase of low-metallicity low-mass stars by propagating the theoretical uncertainties in the radiative neutron capture cross sections, as well as the 13C(a,n)16O reaction rate, and estimating their impact on the surface-abundance distribution. It is found that considering systematic uncertainties on the various nuclear ingredients affecting the radiative neutron capture rates, surface elemental abundances are typically predicted within +/-0.4 dex. The 56 < Z < 59 region of the spectroscopically relevant heavy-s elements of Ba-La-Ce-Pr as well as the r-dominated Eu element remain relatively unaffected by nuclear uncertainties. In contrast, the inclusion of the direct capture contribution impacts the rates in the neutron-rich A~45, 100, 160, and 200 regions, and the i-process production of the Z~45 and 65-70 elements. Uncertainties in the photon strength function also impact the overabundance factors by typically 0.2-0.4 dex. Nuclear level densities tend to affect abundance predictions mainly in the Z=74-79 regions. The uncertainties associated with the neutron-producing reaction 13C(a,n)16O and the unknown beta-decay rates are found to have a low impact on the overall surface enrichment

## Key claim
The paper finds that surface elemental abundances from the i-process in low-metallicity AGB stars are typically predicted within ±0.4 dex when considering systematic uncertainties in theoretical radiative neutron capture rates, but this uncertainty range is conditional on the specific nuclear models adopted and does not represent a rigorous statistical bound.

## Method
The authors used the STAREVOL stellar evolution code to model a 1 solar mass, [Fe/H] = -2.5 star during the proton ingestion event at the beginning of the AGB phase. They coupled a nuclear network of 1160 species to transport processes and computed radiative neutron capture cross sections for 793 experimentally unknown nuclei using the TALYS reaction code, systematically varying the photon strength function, nuclear level density model, and direct capture contribution to propagate correlated theoretical uncertainties.

## Key numbers
- Surface elemental abundances predicted within ±0.4 dex when considering systematic uncertainties on nuclear ingredients affecting radiative neutron capture rates
- The 13C(α,n)16O reaction rate uncertainty is ±27% at T = 2.5×10^8 K (NACRE II evaluation), with a newer analysis giving ±16% at this temperature
- Increasing the 13C(α,n)16O rate from lower to upper NACRE II limits increases Z>55 surface overabundances by a maximum of 0.2 dex
- Varying the 13C(α,n)16O rate by a factor of two changes surface Z>35 [X/Fe] by 0.3-0.4 dex on average
- Photon strength function uncertainties impact overabundance factors by typically 0.2-0.4 dex
- Nuclear level density uncertainties affect abundance predictions mainly in the Z=74-79 region, with deviations up to 0.4 dex
- Direct capture contribution impacts rates in neutron-rich A~45, 100, 160, and 200 regions, affecting Z~45 and 65-70 elements
- The D1M+QRPA+0lim photon strength model gives (n,γ) rates up to a factor of ten higher than the generalized Lorentzian model
- Maximum neutron densities reach approximately 10^15 cm^-3 during the i-process

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous study (Choplin 2021) on i-process development in low-metallicity low-mass AGB stars by systematically analyzing nuclear uncertainties. It contrasts with one-zone Monte Carlo studies such as McKay (2019), which identified 66Ni(n,γ) as a major bottleneck; the present multi-zone simulation shows that modifying this reaction by a factor of 100 does not affect the nucleosynthesis flow. The results enable prioritization of future experimental and theoretical work, specifically calling for improved direct capture descriptions based on shell model calculations or (d,p) reaction constraints, and for Oslo-method measurements of photon strength functions and nuclear level densities in the neutron-rich A~100 and A~160 regions.
