# Reference Energies for Valence Ionizations and Satellite Transitions

> Upon ionization of an atom or a molecule, another electron (or more) can be simultaneously excited. These concurrently generated states are called "satellites" (or shake-up transitions) as they appear in ionization spectra as higher-energy peaks with weaker intensity and larger width than the main peaks associated with single-particle ionizations. Satellites, which correspond to electronically excited states of the cationic species, are notoriously challenging to model using conventional single-reference methods due to their high excitation degree compared to the neutral reference state. This work reports 42 satellite transition energies and 58 valence ionization potentials of full configuration interaction (FCI) quality computed in small molecular systems. Following the protocol developed for the quest database [https://doi.org/10.1002/wcms.1517, Véril et al. Wiley Interdiscip. Rev.: Comput. Mol. Sci. 2021, 11, e1517], these reference energies are computed using the configuration interaction using a perturbative selection made iteratively (CIPSI) method. In addition, the accuracy of the well-known coupled-cluster (CC) hierarchy (CC2, CCSD, CC3, CCSDT, CC4, and CCSDTQ) is gauged against these new accurate references. The performances of various approximations based on many-body Green's functions ($GW$, GF2, and $T$-matrix) for ionization potentials are also analyzed. Their limitations in correctly modeling satellite transitions are discussed.

## Key claim

This work provides 42 satellite transition energies and 58 valence ionization potentials of full configuration interaction (FCI) quality for small molecular systems, filling a gap where no established theoretical benchmarks for satellite energies in molecules previously existed; the accuracy of coupled-cluster and Green's function methods is gauged against these references, with the caveat that the reported FCI values are extrapolated from selected CI calculations and carry associated error bars rather than being exact.

## Method

The authors computed reference energies using the configuration interaction using a perturbative selection made iteratively (CIPSI) method, following the protocol developed for the QUEST database by Veril et al. (2021). For each system, single-state CIPSI calculations for the N- and (N-1)-electron ground states yielded principal ionization potentials, while multi-state CIPSI calculations provided excitation energies of the cationic species, which were combined to obtain satellite transition energies. The accuracy of the coupled-cluster hierarchy (CC2, CCSD, CC3, CCSDT, CC4, CCSDTQ) and Green's function methods (GW, GF2, T-matrix) was then benchmarked against these FCI-quality references.

## Key numbers

* 42 satellite transition energies and 58 valence ionization potentials of FCI quality were computed for small molecular systems.
* For water, the FCI principal ionization potential (1 2B1 state, (1b1)-1) is 12.309 eV with the 6-31+G* basis and 12.737 eV with aug-cc-pVQZ.
* For water, the FCI satellite transition (1 2A1 state, (3a1)-1) is 14.636 eV with 6-31+G* and 14.962 eV with aug-cc-pVQZ.
* For ammonia, the FCI principal IP (1 2A1 state, (3a1)-1) is 10.463 eV with 6-31+G* and 10.945 eV with aug-cc-pVQZ.
* For ammonia, the FCI inner-valence IP (3 2A1 state, (2a1)-1) is 26.683 eV with 6-31+G* and 26.833(1) eV with aug-cc-pVQZ.
* For methane, the FCI principal IP (1 2T2 state, (1t2)-1) is 14.028 eV with aug-cc-pVTZ and 14.079 eV with aug-cc-pVQZ.
* The mean absolute error of IP-EOM-CCSDT with respect to CCSDTQ was previously reported as 0.03 eV for a set of 42 IPs, while IP-EOM-CCSD had a MAE of 0.2 eV (Ranasinghe, 2019).

## Context

This work extends the QUEST database of highly accurate vertical excitation energies (Loos et al., 2018-2024) to include charged excitations, specifically satellite transitions which are the equivalent of double excitations in the realm of neutral excitations. It provides the first established theoretical benchmark set for satellite energies in molecules, building on prior work by Ranasinghe et al. (2019) who provided CCSDTQ-quality IPs for 42 states, and by Chatterjee and Sokolov (2019) who reported FCI-quality energies for the four lowest satellite states of the carbon dimer. The new reference data enable systematic benchmarking of approximate methods for satellite transitions, which are notoriously challenging for single-reference methods due to their high excitation degree compared to the neutral reference state.
