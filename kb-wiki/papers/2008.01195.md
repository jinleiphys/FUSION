# Accelerator Based Production of $^{225}$Ac for Cancer Treatment

> Targeted Alpha Therapy potentially offers a more specific action in killing tumor cells and less damage to neighbouring normal cells compared to $β$-emitters. $^{225}$Ac along with three other $α$-emitting daughter nuclei $^{221}$Fr, $^{217}$At and $^{213}$Po have significant potency for clinical use. Monte carlo simulation has been carried out using MONC code for production routes of $^{225}$Ac for its possible generation using proton accelerators with Thorium-232 and Radium-226 targets. All the calculations has been carried out using global model parameters without adjusting for individual target. The study reveals that proton beam of 20-30MeV energy with 5$μ$A current on radium-226 target can give rise to 1Ci of $^{225}$Ac activity in a week of irradiation. Thorium irradiation at low proton energy produces smaller $^{225}$Ac activity but one can benefit from high current, stable proton accelerators. Proton energy of 100MeV or above with hundreds of $μ$A current can produce reasonable $^{225}$Ac activity for practical use. However, the contamination of other co-produced radio-isotopes is much less at low proton energy hence low energy high current proton accelerators are also viable alternative to generate Actinium-225 radio isotope.

## Key claim

Monte Carlo simulations using global model parameters (not adjusted for individual targets) indicate that a proton beam of 20-30 MeV with 5 μA current on a radium-226 target can produce 1 Ci of ²²⁵Ac activity in one week of irradiation, but the authors note that availability and handling of radium-226 targets remain a challenge.

## Method

The authors used the MONC (Monte Carlo Nucleon Transport Code) to simulate proton-induced spallation reactions on thorium-232 and radium-226 targets. The code incorporates intra-nuclear cascade, pre-equilibrium, evaporation, and fission models, and solves Bateman's equations for isotope buildup and decay using ENDF decay data libraries. Cross-sections for individual reaction channels were calculated and compared with experimental data from the EXFOR database, and thick-target yields were estimated for various proton energies and irradiation times.

## Key numbers

- Proton beam of 20-30 MeV with 5 μA current on radium-226 target can produce 1 Ci of ²²⁵Ac activity in one week of irradiation
- For p+²³²Th at 30 MeV with 1 μA, combined activity after 10 days irradiation is approximately 20 MBq per μA, scalable to 2 GBq for 100 μA current
- For p+²³²Th at 30 MeV with 1 μA and 1 mm target thickness: 5.2 MBq/μA after 10 days, 9 MBq/μA after 20 days, 11 MBq/μA after 30 days
- For p+²³²Th at 100 MeV with 1 μA and 1 mm target thickness: 83 MBq/μA after 10 days
- For p+²²⁶Ra at 20 MeV with 1 μA and 1 mm target thickness: 1643 MBq/μA after 10 days, 2465 MBq/μA after 20 days, 2876 MBq/μA after 30 days
- For p+²²⁶Ra at 30 MeV with 1 μA and 1 mm target thickness: 1709 MBq/μA after 10 days
- The ²²⁶Ra(p,2n)²²⁵Ac reaction cross-section peaks at approximately 700 mb around 17 MeV proton energy
- The ²³²Th(p,4n)²²⁹Pa reaction has a threshold of approximately 19.5 MeV
- ²²⁹Pa decays to ²²⁵Ac via alpha decay with only 0.48% branching ratio; 99.52% decays to ²²⁹Th
- Temperature rise of a thorium metal target (10 cm diameter, 2 mm thickness) after one hour irradiation with 30 MeV, 1 μA proton beam is approximately 5000 K without cooling
- Proton range in thorium target: approximately 2 mm at 30 MeV, approximately 16 mm at 100 MeV
- Gain factor for ²²⁵Ac activity at 100 MeV compared to 30 MeV is approximately 100 for full beam dump; gain factor for 300 MeV compared to 100 MeV is approximately 80

## Context

This work extends prior experimental cross-section measurements for ²²⁶Ra(p,2n)²²⁵Ac by Apostolidis (2005) and for p+²³²Th reactions by Jost (2013), Lefort (1961), Ermolaev (2012), Weidner, Mashnik, Griswold (2016), and Gauvin. The study addresses the global shortage of ²²⁵Ac, which is currently produced primarily by milking ²²⁹Th at facilities including ORNL (USA), IPPE (Russia), and ITU (Germany) with a combined inventory of approximately 1.8 Ci/year (IAEA, 2013). The results enable evaluation of accelerator-based production as an alternative to reactor-based methods, with the ²²⁶Ra route appearing particularly promising for practical and economical use, though radium handling remains a challenge.
