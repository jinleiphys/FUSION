# R-mode astronomy

> Next generation gravitational wave detectors will start taking data in the near future. Here we discuss the chances to detect the continuous emission from r-mode oscillations in compact stars and study which properties of compact stars we can infer from such novel data. In particular we show that the combination of the gravitational wave data with electromagnetic multi-messenger observations could give us detailed insight into compact star properties, ranging from precise mass-radius measurements to the determination of the equation of state and the phase structure of dense matter.

## Key claim

The paper argues that combining gravitational wave data from r-mode oscillations with electromagnetic multi-messenger observations could yield detailed insight into compact star properties, but this depends on the specific r-mode emission scenario realized in nature, whether standard neutron matter with minimal damping or exotic matter with enhanced damping is present, and on the unknown r-mode saturation amplitude.

## Method

The authors perform a theoretical survey of r-mode gravitational wave emission from compact stars, deriving generalized expressions for the gravitational wave strain, luminosity, and rotational energy loss that incorporate a general r-mode spectrum parametrized by a correction factor χ(Ω). They analyze four classes of sources (newly-formed stars, young sources, LMXBs, and millisecond pulsars) and study their coupled thermal and rotational evolution under different damping scenarios, using the resulting steady-state curves to determine detectability with next-generation gravitational wave detectors.

## Key numbers

- The fundamental l=2 r-mode in Newtonian approximation has oscillation angular velocity ω = -4/3 Ω, with relativistic corrections altering this by a factor χ(Ω) ≈ 1; the Newtonian expression agrees with relativistic results to about 1% accuracy for the mode eigenfunctions
- R-modes can be unstable in sources spinning down to below 100 Hz in the minimal damping scenario for standard neutron stars
- Temperature measurements in LMXBs set bounds on the r-mode saturation amplitude α ≲ 10⁻⁸–10⁻⁷ in these sources
- For young sources with saturation amplitude α_sat ≳ 10⁻³, the gravitational wave strain becomes independent of the saturation amplitude and takes the form h₀(t) = √(5/8 × 𝒞GI/(D²t)), where 𝒞 ≈ 1 for constant saturation amplitude
- Newly-formed sources with α = 0.1 could be detected up to a distance of 1 Mpc with next-generation detectors
- Millisecond pulsars with saturated r-modes would emit gravitational waves for billions of years if the saturation amplitude is not below α < 10⁻¹⁰

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for r-mode gravitational wave astronomy pioneered by Owen et al. (1998) and builds on recent analyses of r-mode detectability by Alford et al. (2012), Mahmoodifar and Schwenzer (2013), and Alford et al. (2014). It contrasts with previous searches that focused exclusively on star deformations parametrized by ellipticity, and it addresses the tension between observational temperature bounds from LMXBs and proposed r-mode saturation mechanisms. The paper enables future gravitational wave searches to discriminate between different r-mode emission scenarios and to combine gravitational wave data with electromagnetic observations for precision measurements of compact star properties.
