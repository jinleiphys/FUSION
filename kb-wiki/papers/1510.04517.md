# Modification of magicity towards the dripline and its impact on electron-capture rates for stellar core-collapse

> The importance of microphysical inputs from laboratory nuclear experiments and theoretical nuclear structure calculations in the understanding of the core collapse dynamics, and the subsequent supernova explosion, is largely recognized in the recent literature. In this work, we analyze the impact of the masses of very neutron rich nuclei on the matter composition during collapse, and the corresponding electron capture rate. To this aim, we introduce an empirical modification of the popular Duflo-Zuker mass model to account for possible shell quenching far from stability, and study the effect of the quenching on the average electron capture rate. We show that the preeminence of the $N=50$ and $N=82$ closed shells in the collapse dynamics is considerably decreased if the shell gaps are reduced in the region of $^{78}$Ni and beyond. As a consequence, local modifications of the overall electron capture rate up to 30\% can be expected, with integrated values strongly dependent on the stiffness of magicity quenching and progenitor mass and potential important consequences on the entropy generation, the neutrino emissivity, and the mass of the core at bounce. Our work underlines the importance of new experimental measurements in this region of the nuclear chart, the most crucial information being the nuclear mass and the Gamow-Teller strength. Reliable microscopic calculations of the associated elementary rate, in a wide range of temperatures and electron densities, optimized on these new empirical information, will be additionally needed to get quantitative predictions of the collapse dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper shows that if shell gaps at N=50 and N=82 are reduced in the region of ⁷⁸Ni and beyond (shell quenching far from stability), the preeminence of these closed shells in core-collapse dynamics is considerably decreased, leading to local modifications of the overall electron capture rate up to 30%, with integrated values strongly dependent on the stiffness of magicity quenching and progenitor mass.

## Method

The authors introduced an empirical modification of the Duflo-Zuker mass model to simulate possible shell quenching far from stability, then used this modified mass model within a Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium (NSE) framework to compute the nuclear composition and average electron capture rates along two representative core-collapse trajectories (15 M⊙ and 25 M⊙ progenitors). Individual electron capture rates were calculated using the analytic L03 parametrization of Langanke et al., which expresses the rate as a function of the ground-state-to-ground-state Q-value, with fitted parameters B=4.6 MeV and ΔE=2.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the sensitivity study of Sullivan (2015), who showed that variations in electron capture rates on medium-heavy nuclei produce variations in inner core mass at bounce five times larger than those from progenitor model uncertainty, and identified the N=50 shell closure region (74≤A≤84) as most sensitive. The present paper addresses the complementary aspect of uncertainties associated with the nuclear distribution rather than individual rates, and demonstrates that shell quenching far from stability, as suggested by the more microscopic BSK22 mass model compared to phenomenological models like DZ10 and FRDM, can significantly alter the composition and electron capture rates during collapse. The results underline the need for new experimental mass measurements and Gamow-Teller strength determinations in the ⁷⁸Ni region, as well as microscopic calculations of weak rates optimized on these empirical data.
