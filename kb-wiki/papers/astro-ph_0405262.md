# The Physics of Neutron Stars

> Neutron stars are some of the densest manifestations of massive objects in the universe. They are ideal astrophysical laboratories for testing theories of dense matter physics and provide connections among nuclear physics, particle physics and astrophysics. Neutron stars may exhibit conditions and phenomena not observed elsewhere, such as hyperon-dominated matter, deconfined quark matter, superfluidity and superconductivity with critical temperatures near ${10^{10}}$ kelvin, opaqueness to neutrinos, and magnetic fields in excess of $10^{13}$ Gauss. Here, we describe the formation, structure, internal composition and evolution of neutron stars. Observations that include studies of binary pulsars, thermal emission from isolated neutron stars, glitches from pulsars and quasi-periodic oscillations from accreting neutron stars provide information about neutron star masses, radii, temperatures, ages and internal compositions.

## Key claim

Neutron stars are ideal astrophysical laboratories for testing theories of dense matter physics, but the properties of matter near and above nuclear saturation density remain highly uncertain, leading to up to a factor of 6 variation in predicted pressure near nuclear density and nearly 50% variation in predicted radii.

## Method

The paper reviews the formation, structure, internal composition, and evolution of neutron stars by synthesizing theoretical models of dense matter equations of state (EOS) with observational data from binary pulsars, thermal emission from isolated neutron stars, pulsar glitches, and quasi-periodic oscillations from accreting neutron stars. The authors describe how the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations of hydrostatic equilibrium in general relativity are solved numerically with different EOS inputs to obtain mass-radius relations, and how cooling curves are computed using Urca processes (direct and modified) with corrections for superfluidity and envelope composition.

## Key numbers

- Typical neutron star mass: ~1.5 solar masses (M☉), radius: ~12 km, central density: 5 to 10 times nuclear equilibrium density n₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³
- Most accurately measured pulsar mass: 1.4408 ± 0.0003 M☉ (binary pulsar PSR 1913+16)
- Maximum mass range: 1.44 to 3 M☉ (lower bound from PSR 1913+16, upper bound from causality)
- Minimum stable neutron star mass: ~0.1 M☉; lepton-rich proto-neutron stars unbound below ~1 M☉
- Neutrino-matter cross section: σ ≈ 10⁻⁴⁰ cm², mean free path λ ≈ 10 cm at baryon density n ≈ 2 to 3 n₀
- Gravitational binding energy released in core collapse: ~3 × 10⁵³ erg (~10% of total mass energy Mc²)
- Kinetic energy of expanding remnant: ~1 × 10⁵¹ to 2 × 10⁵¹ erg
- Neutrino diffusion time from proto-neutron star: τ ≈ 10 s (confirmed by SN 1987A observations)
- Core temperature during stage III: ~50 MeV (6 × 10¹¹ K)
- Highest observed pulsar spin rate: 641 Hz (PSR B1937+21), implying radius limit of 15.5 km for 1.4 M☉
- Neutron drip density in crust: 4 × 10¹¹ g cm⁻³
- Direct Urca process requires proton fraction x ≥ 1/9; typical x near n₀: x ≈ 0.048 (S_v(n₀) ≈ 30 MeV)
- Modified Urca rate reduced by factor (T/μ_n)² ≲ 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁵ compared to direct Urca
- Surface temperatures of thermally-emitting neutron stars: 3 × 10⁵ to 10⁶ K
- Radiation radius R∞ estimates from quiescent x-ray sources in globular clusters: 13 to 16 km
- Vela pulsar glitch implies at least 1.4% of star's moment of inertia resides in crust
- Twin high-frequency QPO peaks near 1 kHz, separated by ~300 Hz

## Context

This review extends earlier work on neutron star structure and dense matter physics, including foundational studies by Baym (1975, 1979), Heiselberg (2000), and Glendenning (1985, 1997) on exotic matter, and builds on observations of binary pulsars by Weisberg (2003) and thermal emission studies by Page (2004). The paper synthesizes theoretical and observational knowledge to identify key uncertainties, particularly the nuclear symmetry energy function S_v(n), that must be resolved by future accelerator experiments (rare-isotope facilities, parity-violating electron scattering on Pb²⁰⁸), neutrino observatories, and gravitational wave detectors (LIGO, VIRGO, GEO600, TAMA) to constrain neutron star maximum masses, radii, and internal compositions.
