# Artificial Intelligence Supported Shell-Model Calculations for Light Sn Isotopes

> The region around the doubly magic nuclide $^{100}Sn$ is very interesting for nuclear physics studies in terms of structure, reaction and nuclear astrophysics. The main ingredients in nuclear structure studies using the shell model are the single-particle energies and the two-body matrix elements. To obtain the former, experimental data of $^{101}Sn$ isotope spectrum are necessary. Since there is not enough experimental data, different approaches are used in the literature to obtain single-particle energies. In sn100pn interaction, the hole excitation spectrum was used in $^{131}Sn$ to determine neutron single-particle energies. The other approach is the use of the lightest isotope, $^{107}Sn$, which figures the model space orbitals. In this study, we estimated the spectrum of the $^{101}Sn$ isotope by artificial neural network method in order to obtain neutron single-particle energies. After the training was carried out by using the experimental spectra of the nuclei around $^{100}Sn$ isotope, the $^{101}Sn$ spectrum was obtained. Subsequently, neutron SPEs of the model space orbitals are defined. Shell model calculations for $^{102-108}Sn$ isotopes are carried out and results are compared to the experimental data and results obtained using the widely used interaction in the region, sn100pn. According to the results, it is seen that the Sn isotope spectra obtained with the new SPE values are more compatible with the experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors claim that using artificial neural networks (ANN) to estimate the excited energy spectrum of the $^{101}Sn$ isotope, and then deriving neutron single-particle energies from those estimates, yields shell-model calculations for $^{102-108}Sn$ isotopes that are more compatible with experimental data than calculations using the widely used sn100pn interaction. The hedge is that the ANN results depend on the choice of training data set, and the best-performing set varies by isotope and excited state.

## Method

The authors used a feed-forward artificial neural network with 4 or 5 input neurons (mass number, neutron number, proton number, orbital angular momentum, total spin), 6 hidden neurons, and 1 output neuron to estimate the $1/2^{+}$, $3/2^{+}$, and $11/2^{-}$ excited energy levels of $^{101}Sn$. They trained the network on three different data sets: set-1 (only odd Sn isotopes, 56 training points), set-2 (odd-N, even-Z isotopes including Sn, Te, Xe, Ba, 172 training points), and set-3 (odd-N isotopes with both even and odd Z including Sn, Sb, Te, I, Xe, Cs, Ba, 293 training points). From the ANN-estimated $^{101}Sn$ spectrum, they derived neutron single-particle energies for the $d_{5/2}$, $g_{7/2}$, $s_{1/2}$, $d_{3/2}$, and $h_{11/2}$ orbitals, then performed shell-model calculations for $^{102-108}Sn$ using the Kshell code with the Lanczos method.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to determine neutron single-particle energies for shell-model calculations in the $^{100}Sn$ region, where experimental data for $^{101}Sn$ are lacking. Prior approaches include using the hole excitation spectrum in $^{131}Sn$ (Brown et al.), using the spectrum of $^{107}Sn$ (Yakhelef and Bouldjedri), and modifying single-particle energies based on the single known $7/2^{+}$ level of $^{101}Sn$ (Trivedi et al.). The authors introduce artificial neural networks as a novel tool for this problem, building on their previous ANN work predicting ground-state energies, first $2^{+}$ energies, and nuclear radii. The results enable improved shell-model calculations for light Sn isotopes and suggest that ANN methods can supplement experimental data for nuclei near the proton drip line.
