# The Hyperspherical Harmonics method: a tool for testing and improving nuclear interaction models

> The Hyperspherical Harmonics (HH) method is one of the most accurate techniques to solve the quantum mechanical problem for nuclear systems with $A\le 4$. In particular, by applying the Rayleigh-Ritz or Kohn variational principle, both bound and scattering states can be addressed, using either local or non-local interactions. Thanks to this versatility, the method can be used to test the two- and three-nucleon components of the nuclear interaction. In the present review we introduce the formalism of the HH method, both for bound and scattering states. In particular, we describe the implementation of the method to study the $A=3$ and $4$ scattering problem. Second, we present a selected choice of results of the last decade, most representative of the latest achievements. Finally, we conclude with a discussion of what we believe will be the most significant developments within the HH method for the next five-to-ten years.

## Key claim

The Hyperspherical Harmonics (HH) method is presented as one of the most accurate techniques for solving the quantum mechanical problem of nuclear systems with A ≤ 4, capable of testing both two- and three-nucleon components of the nuclear interaction; however, the method currently works only with local three-nucleon interactions, though its extension to non-local three-nucleon interactions does not present conceptual difficulties.

## Method

The HH method expands the A-body wave function in a basis of hyperspherical harmonic functions constructed from Jacobi coordinates, hyperangular coordinates, and hyperradial functions expanded in Laguerre polynomials. For bound states, the Rayleigh-Ritz variational principle is applied to solve a generalized eigenvalue problem for the expansion coefficients. For scattering states describing elastic N+Y processes (where Y is a bound nucleus with A = 3 or 4), the wave function is split into a core part expanded in the HH basis and an asymptotic part describing the relative N-Y motion, with the Kohn variational principle used to determine the scattering parameters.

## Key numbers

- Triton binding energy calculated with only two-nucleon interactions is underestimated by 0.5–1 MeV, depending on the potential model considered
- Accuracy of ab-initio methods for A = 3 bound systems has reached approximately 1 keV or better
- The optimal nonlinear parameter γ for the Laguerre polynomial expansion is typically in the interval 2.5–4.5 fm⁻¹ for local potentials and 4–8 fm⁻¹ for non-local potentials
- The HH basis can reach up to about 10,000 terms for A = 3 and about one order of magnitude larger (approximately 100,000 terms) for A = 4

## Context

This review extends the HH method developments since 2008, building on the earlier work summarized in Kievsky (2008) which described the correlated HH (PHH/CHH) and pure HH methods for A = 3 and 4 bound states and zero-energy scattering. The paper contrasts the HH method with other ab-initio approaches such as the Faddeev (Faddeev-Yakubovsky) equations in coordinate or momentum space, the Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) method, variational Monte Carlo, Green's function Monte Carlo, and the no-core shell model, noting that the HH method is particularly well-suited for low-energy scattering states of astrophysical interest. The work enables further testing and refinement of nuclear interaction models, with ongoing efforts to extend the method to A = 5 and 6 systems and to treat breakup channels such as n + d → n + n + p.
