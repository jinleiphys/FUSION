# The electron screening puzzle and nuclear clustering

> Accurate measurements of nuclear reactions of astrophysical interest within, or close to, the Gamow peak, show evidence of an unexpected effect attributed to the presence of atomic electrons in the target. The experiments need to include an effective "screening" potential to explain the enhancement of the cross sections at the lowest measurable energies. Despite various theoretical studies conducted over the past 20 years and numerous experimental measurements, a theory has not yet been found that can explain the cause of the exceedingly high values of the screening potential needed to explain the data. In this letter we show that instead of an atomic physics solution of the "electron screening puzzle", the reason for the large screening potential values is in fact due to clusterization effects in nuclear reactions, in particular for reaction involving light nuclei.

## Key claim

The anomalously large electron screening potentials observed in low-energy nuclear reactions are not due to atomic physics effects but instead arise from nuclear clustering, where the cluster structure of light nuclei (e.g., \(^6\)Li as d+\(\alpha\)) enhances tunneling probabilities at ultra-low energies, even when the spectroscopic amplitudes for cluster configurations are small.

## Method

The authors model the fusion of two \(^6\)Li nuclei by decomposing each into a deuteron and an alpha cluster separated by a fixed distance of \(\sim 3.85\) fm, then calculate the total ion-ion potential as the sum of Coulomb and nuclear potentials between each pair of clusters. They compute the WKB tunneling probability averaged over all relative orientations of the two dicluster systems and compare it to the standard sphere-on-sphere optical model calculation.

## Key numbers

* The inter-cluster distance in \(^6\)Li is kept constant at \(\sim 3.85\) fm, obtained from cluster model formula and nuclear radii data.
* For the reaction \(^6\)Li(d,\(\alpha\))\(^4\)He, the experimental screening potential is \(330 \pm 120\) eV (H gas target) and \(330 \pm 49\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 175 eV.
* For the reaction \(^6\)Li(p,\(\alpha\))\(^3\)He, the experimental screening potential is \(440 \pm 150\) eV (H gas target) and \(355 \pm 67\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 175 eV.
* For the reaction \(^7\)Li(p,\(\alpha\))\(^4\)He, the experimental screening potential is \(300 \pm 160\) eV (H gas target) and \(363 \pm 52\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 175 eV.
* For the reaction \(^9\)Be(p,\(\alpha_0\))\(^6\)Li, the experimental screening potential is \(788 \pm 70\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 240 eV.
* For the reaction \(^{10}\)B(p,\(\alpha_0\))\(^7\)Be, the experimental screening potential is \(376 \pm 75\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 340 eV.
* For the reaction \(^{11}\)B(p,\(\alpha_0\))\(^8\)Be, the experimental screening potential is \(447 \pm 67\) eV, compared to the adiabatic limit of 340 eV.
* The ratio of experimental to adiabatic screening potential increases with cluster structure: for Z=1 nuclei the ratio is near unity; for Z=3 (Li isotopes) it is about 1.5-2; for Z=4-5 (Be, B) it reaches 2-3.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing "electron screening puzzle" identified in experiments by Rolfs (1988) and others, where measured screening potentials for reactions like d+d, d+\(^3\)He, and \(^3\)He+\(^3\)He exceed the adiabatic atomic limit calculated by Assenbaum (1987). The authors propose that nuclear clustering, rather than unknown atomic physics, explains the discrepancy, building on earlier observations of enhanced \(^6\)Li+\(^6\)Li fusion by Lattuada (1988). This reinterpretation implies that astrophysical S-factors extracted from low-energy data must be corrected for nuclear structure effects, and it motivates new experiments on reactions such as \(^6\)Li+\(^6\)Li, \(^7\)Li+\(^7\)Li, and \(^9\)Be+\(^3\)He to test the clustering hypothesis.
