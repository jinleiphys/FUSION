# Probing surface diffuseness of nucleus-nucleus potential with quasielastic scattering at deep sub-barrier energies

> We perform a systematic study on the surface property of nucleus-nucleus potential in heavy-ion reactions using large-angle quasielastic scattering at energies well below the Coulomb barrier. At these energies, the quasielastic scattering can be well described by a single-channel potential model. Exploiting this fact, we point out that systems which involve spherical nuclei require the diffuseness parameter of around 0.60 fm in order to fit the experimental data, while systems with a deformed target between 0.8 fm and 1.1 fm.

# Probing surface diffuseness of nucleus-nucleus potential with quasielastic scattering at deep sub-barrier energies

## Key claim

For systems involving spherical nuclei, the surface diffuseness parameter of the nucleus-nucleus potential extracted from large-angle quasielastic scattering at deep sub-barrier energies is around 0.60 fm, consistent with the conventional value; however, for systems with a deformed target, the required diffuseness parameter is significantly larger, between 0.8 fm and 1.1 fm. This conclusion holds only when the analysis is restricted to energies where the quasielastic cross section exceeds 0.94 of the Rutherford cross section, ensuring that channel coupling effects are negligible.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic analysis of experimental quasielastic scattering excitation functions for six heavy-ion systems at deep sub-barrier energies, using a one-dimensional Woods-Saxon optical potential with a fixed depth of 100 MeV and an imaginary part to simulate absorption. For spherical systems, they fitted the surface diffuseness parameter to data points where the ratio of quasielastic to Rutherford cross sections was at least 0.94, ensuring negligible channel coupling effects. For deformed systems, they included the deformation effect via an orientation average formula and extended the fitting region to somewhat higher energies due to sparse data at deep sub-barrier energies.

## Key numbers

- For the $^{32}$S + $^{197}$Au reaction (Coulomb barrier height 141.2 MeV), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.57 \pm 0.04$ fm
- For the $^{34}$S + $^{197}$Au reaction (Coulomb barrier height 140.2 MeV), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.53 \pm 0.03$ fm
- For the $^{32}$S + $^{208}$Pb reaction (Coulomb barrier height 145.1 MeV), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.60 \pm 0.04$ fm
- For the $^{34}$S + $^{208}$Pb reaction (Coulomb barrier height 144.1 MeV), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.63 \pm 0.04$ fm
- For the $^{16}$O + $^{208}$Pb reaction (Coulomb barrier height 76.1 MeV), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.59 \pm 0.10$ fm
- For the $^{16}$O + $^{154}$Sm reaction (deformed target, $\beta_2 = 0.306$, $\beta_4 = 0.05$), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 1.14 \pm 0.03$ fm
- For the $^{16}$O + $^{186}$W reaction (deformed target, $\beta_2 = 0.29$, $\beta_4 = -0.03$), best fitted surface diffuseness parameter: $a = 0.79 \pm 0.04$ fm
- A $\pm 1\%$ variation in the Coulomb barrier height for the $^{32}$S + $^{208}$Pb system changed the extracted diffuseness parameter by $\pm 0.04$ fm

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Hagino et al. (2005) on the $^{16}$O + $^{154}$Sm system and directly addresses the discrepancy between the conventional surface diffuseness parameter of around 0.63 fm used for elastic and inelastic scattering analyses (Broglia and Winther, 1991; Christensen and Winther, 1976) and the larger values (0.75 to 1.5 fm) required by fusion reaction analyses (Newton et al., 2004). The results show that for spherical systems, quasielastic scattering favors the conventional diffuseness, suggesting the double folding potential (Satchler and Love, 1979) is valid in the surface region for such systems, while the discrepancy with fusion data must arise from dynamics inside the Coulomb barrier. For deformed systems, the larger diffuseness values extracted from quasielastic scattering are consistent with those from fusion, but the origin of the difference between spherical and deformed systems remains unclear and requires further precision measurements at deep sub-barrier energies.
