# Hydrodynamic fluctuations of entropy in one-dimensionally expanding system

> The fluctuation-dissipation relation tells that dissipation always accompanies with thermal fluctuations. Relativistic fluctuating hydrodynamics is used to study the effects of the thermal fluctuations in the hydrodynamic expansion of the quark-gluon plasma created in the high-energy nuclear collisions. We show that the thermal noise obeys the steady-state fluctuation theorem when (i) the time scales of the evolution of thermodynamic quantities are sufficiently longer than the relaxation time, and (ii) the thermal fluctuations of temperature are sufficiently small. The steady-state fluctuation theorem describes the distribution of the entropy which can be related to the multiplicity observed in high-energy nuclear collisions. As a consequence, we propose an upper bound to the multiplicity fluctuations which is useful to test the initial state models. We also numerically investigate breaking of the steady-state fluctuation theorem due to the non-vanishing relaxation time in real nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The authors show that the steady-state fluctuation theorem (SSFT) holds exactly for entropy production in a one-dimensionally expanding (Bjorken) system described by relativistic fluctuating hydrodynamics, but only under two idealized conditions: (i) the evolution time scales of thermodynamic quantities are sufficiently longer than the relaxation time, and (ii) thermal fluctuations of temperature are sufficiently small. They further propose that the SSFT provides an upper bound on multiplicity fluctuations that can be used to test initial state models, while acknowledging that the theorem is broken in realistic nuclear collisions due to non-vanishing relaxation time.

## Method

The authors employ relativistic second-order fluctuating hydrodynamics applied to a longitudinally boost-invariant (Bjorken) expansion of quark-gluon plasma. They derive the entropy production rate from the stochastic constitutive equations for shear stress and bulk pressure, incorporating noise terms whose intensities are given by the fluctuation-dissipation relation. Under the Navier-Stokes limit (negligible relaxation time) and the assumption of negligible temperature fluctuations, they analytically derive the SSFT for the Gaussian distribution of the average entropy production rate. They then perform numerical simulations using a second-order stochastic Runge-Kutta method with a parametrized crossover equation of state and specified transport coefficients to study the breaking of the SSFT due to finite relaxation time.

## Key numbers

- Initial time: tau_i = 1.0 fm; initial temperature: T_i = 0.22 GeV
- Crossover temperature: T_c = 170 MeV; crossover region size: d = T_c/50
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: eta/s = 1/(4 pi)
- Default relaxation time: tau_R = 3 eta / (2 p)
- Constant relaxation time model: tau_R = 3.0 fm
- Time step for numerical integration: Delta tau = 0.1 fm
- Fluid element size: Delta eta_s = 1, Delta x = Delta y = 1 fm
- Temperature difference between dissipative and fluctuating hydrodynamics is of order a few percent after the crossover region at tau ~ 50 fm

## Context

This work extends the application of the fluctuation theorem, previously established in non-equilibrium statistical mechanics by Evans, Cohen, and Morriss (1993) and Gallavotti and Cohen (1995), to the context of high-energy nuclear collisions for the first time. It builds upon relativistic fluctuating hydrodynamics developed by Murase (2013) and the Bjorken expansion model (1982) to connect thermal fluctuations of entropy to experimentally observable multiplicity fluctuations. The proposed upper bound on multiplicity fluctuations enables tests of initial state models without relying on specific modeling of intermediate dynamics, complementing existing work on initial fluctuations of the transverse profile by Alver and Roland (2010) and studies of conserved charge fluctuations for the QCD critical point by Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak (1998, 1999).
