# Axion-Polaritons in quark stars: a possible solution to the missing pulsar problem

> This paper proposes an alternative mechanism to solve the so-called missing pulsar problem, a standing paradox between the theoretical expectations about the number of pulsars that should exist in the galaxy center of the Milky Way and their absence in the observations. The mechanism is based on the transformation of incident $γ$ rays into hybridized modes, known as axion-polaritons, which can exist inside highly magnetized quark stars with a quark matter phase known as the magnetic dual chiral density wave phase. This phase, which is favored over several other dense matter phases candidates at densities a few times nuclear saturation density, has already passed several important astrophysical tests. In the proposed mechanism, the absence of young magnetars occurs because as electromagnetic waves inside the star can only propagate through the hybridized modes, incident photons coming from a $γ$-ray burst get transformed into massless and massive axion polaritons by the Primakoff effect. Once thermalized, the massive axion-polaritons can self-gravitate up to a situation where their total mass overpasses the Chandrasekhar limit for these bosons, producing a mini blackhole that collapses the star.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the missing pulsar problem in the Galactic Center can be explained if young magnetars are quark stars in the magnetic dual chiral density wave (MDCDW) phase, where incident gamma rays from gamma-ray bursts are converted into massive axion-polaritons that accumulate and eventually cause the star to collapse into a mini black hole. The mechanism relies on the specific condition that the quark stars are bare (without a crust) and have inner magnetic fields of order 10^17 G or higher.

## Method

The authors analyze the attenuation of gamma rays through Compton scattering in two types of neutron stars (hybrid stars and quark stars) to determine whether incident photons can reach the quark matter core. For quark stars, they calculate the number of axion-polaritons that can be produced via the Primakoff effect, then compare the self-gravitation threshold and the Chandrasekhar limit for these bosons to determine if a mini black hole can form and collapse the star.

## Key numbers

- The axion-polariton gap is given by δ = αB₀/πm; for B ≃ 10^17 G, δ is in the range [0.06, 0.5] MeV with m ∈ [23.5, 2.8] MeV at intermediate baryonic densities ρ ∼ 3n_s
- The Chandrasekhar limit for axion-polaritons is N_AP^Ch = 1.5 × 10^44 (MeV/c²δ)², yielding N_AP^Ch ∼ 10^45-10^46 for the δ range considered
- For a quark star, gamma-ray intensity attenuation is minimal: I/I₀ = 0.89 after 300 fm, 0.83 after 500 fm, 0.77 after 700 fm, and 0.71 after 900 fm
- For a hybrid star, gamma rays decay to I/I₀ = 10^-30 after only 64.2 fm penetration
- The number of self-gravitating axion-polaritons at T = 10^7 K is N_AP^sg ≥ 6.9 × 10^57, which exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit
- A single gamma-ray burst can produce at least 10^55-10^58 axion-polaritons inside a bare strange star

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of axion electrodynamics in the MDCDW phase developed by Ferrer and de la Incera (2020) to address the missing pulsar problem, a paradox noted by Dexter and O'Leary (2014) where theoretical predictions of over 10^3 active radio pulsars within 10 pc of the Galactic Center conflict with observations. The mechanism differs from dark matter collapse scenarios proposed by Bramante and Linden (2016) and others, which require a very restrictive dark matter density profile; instead, the axion-polariton mechanism relies on the high gamma-ray burst activity in the Galactic Center and the properties of the MDCDW phase, which has already passed several astrophysical tests including compatibility with observed stellar masses M ≳ 2M_⊙ (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) and sound speed constraints.
