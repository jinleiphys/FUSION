# Comments on methods of analyses of nucleon-nucleus scattering data

> Methods of analysis of nucleon-nucleus scattering data have progressed markedly over the past 50 years, yet many analyses of scattering data still use prescriptions specified at various stages of that progress. But the assumptions made or inherent in those analyses are poorly justified and most are no longer necessary to facilitate evaluations. We investigate some of those assumptions and highlight deficiencies and the uncertainties they may make in data analyses.

## Key claim

Many analyses of nucleon-nucleus scattering data still use outdated prescriptions whose underlying assumptions are poorly justified and no longer necessary, yet the authors do not claim that any single modern method is fully correct; rather they demonstrate that even excellent fits to elastic scattering data do not guarantee a unique or physically correct optical potential.

## Method

The authors compare phenomenological optical model potentials (Woods-Saxon forms), semi-phenomenological JLM folding models, and fully microscopic g-folding optical potentials built from non-relativistic multiple scattering theory with density-dependent NN effective interactions. They use the modified Newton-Sabatier inverse scattering scheme to generate local potentials from phase shifts obtained from g-folding models, and compare the resulting relative motion wave functions from both microscopic and phenomenological potentials for nucleon scattering on 12C, 40Ca, and 208Pb at energies from 40 to 250 MeV.

## Key numbers

- For 200 MeV protons on 12C, two distinct inversion potentials gave fits with chi-squared per degree of freedom values of 1.006 and 1.018, yet the potentials differ markedly in shape and magnitude.
- The bands of confidence on the inversion potentials for 200 MeV protons on 12C indicate a 60% chance that another potential fitting the data with the same chi-squared/F would lie within the band.
- For 65 MeV protons on 40Ca and 208Pb, g-folding potentials using density-dependent NN g-matrices (solid curves) gave significantly better agreement with analyzing power data than potentials using free NN t-matrices (dashed curves).
- For 40 MeV neutrons on 208Pb, the s-wave relative motion wave function from the nonlocal g-folding potential shows a markedly decreased real part through the nuclear volume compared to the phase-equivalent local phenomenological potential, with the reduction not being the overall 10-15% often assumed for the Perey effect.
- The phenomenological optical model parameters for 40 MeV protons on 208Pb include V0 = 51.8 MeV, r0 = 1.159 fm, a0 = 0.784 fm; for 40 MeV neutrons on 208Pb they include V0 = 36.73 MeV, r0 = 1.205 fm, a0 = 0.685 fm.

## Context

This work extends the critical assessment of optical model methods dating back to Bethe (1940) and the Kerman-McManus-Thaler approach (1959), and builds on the g-folding model review by Amos (2000). It directly challenges the common assumption that a good fit to elastic scattering data validates a particular optical potential, and demonstrates that local phenomenological potentials and even inverse-scattering-derived potentials can be non-unique. The paper enables more cautious interpretation of nucleon-induced reaction analyses, particularly for extracting nuclear structure information, and highlights the need for microscopic treatments that include Pauli blocking, exchange amplitudes, and medium effects rather than relying on local phenomenological forms.
