# Phase Structure and Dynamics of QCD-- A Functional Perspective

> The understanding of the phase structure and the fundamental properties of QCD matter from its microscopic description requires appropriate first-principle approaches. Here I review the progress towards a quantitative first-principle continuum approach within the framework of the Functional Renormalization group established by the fQCD collaboration. I focus on recent quantitative results for quenched QCD and Yang-Mills theory in the vacuum before addressing the calculation of dynamical quantities such as spectral functions and transport coefficients in this framework.

## Key claim

The paper reviews progress toward a quantitative first-principle continuum approach to QCD using the Functional Renormalization Group (FRG), but the results shown are limited to quenched QCD and Yang-Mills theory in the vacuum; the extension to full QCD with dynamical quarks at finite temperature and density is stated as work in progress, not yet achieved.

## Method

The author reviews the FRG framework, where a scale-dependent effective action \(\Gamma_k\) is governed by a one-loop flow equation. For Yang-Mills theory, the system of propagators and primitively divergent vertex functions was solved self-consistently in Landau gauge. For quenched QCD, gluon and ghost propagators were used as input to compute the quark propagator, employing a full tensor basis for the quark-gluon vertex and non-classical higher-order vertices via computer-algebraic tools. For real-time observables, analytic continuation is performed directly on the level of the flow equations, avoiding numerical methods like the Maximum Entropy Method.

## Context

This work extends the FRG approach to QCD pursued by the fQCD collaboration, building on prior studies of Yang-Mills theory by Cyrol (2016) and quenched QCD by Mitter (2014). It contrasts with Lattice QCD, which is reliable at vanishing and small chemical potentials but suffers from the fermion sign problem at \(\mu_B \gtrsim T\). The results enable future calculations of fluctuation observables (e.g., cumulants of net-baryon distributions) at finite chemical potential, and direct computation of spectral functions and transport coefficients in full QCD, which are needed as input for hydrodynamic simulations.
