# Alpha Decay Energies of Superheavy Nuclei: Systematic Trends

> New superheavy nuclei are often identified through their characteristic $α$-decay energies, which requires accurate calculations of $Q_α$ values. While many $Q_α$ predictions are available, little is known about their uncertainties, and this makes it difficult to carry out extrapolations to yet-unknown systems. This work aims to analyze several models, compare their predictions to available experimental data, and study their performance for the unobserved $α$-decay chains of $^{296}$120 and $^{298}$120, which are of current experimental interest. Our quantified results will also serve as a benchmark for future, more sophisticated statistical studies. We use nuclear superfluid Density Functional Theory (DFT) with several Skyrme energy density functionals (EDFs). To estimate systematic model uncertainties we employ uniform model averaging. We evaluated the $Q_α$ values for even-even nuclei from Fm to $Z=120$. For well deformed nuclei between Fm and Ds, we find excellent consistency between different model predictions, and a good agreement with experiment. For transitional nuclei beyond Ds, inter-model differences grow, resulting in an appreciable systematic error. In particular, our models underestimate $Q_α$ for the heaviest nucleus $^{294}$Og. The robustness of DFT predictions for well deformed superheavy nuclei supports the idea of using experimental $Q_α$ values, together with theoretical predictions, as reasonable $(Z,A)$ indicators. Unfortunately, this identification method is not expected to work well in the region of deformed-to-spherical shape transition as one approaches $N=184$. The use of $Q_α$ values in the identification of new superheavy nuclei will benefit greatly from both progress in developing new spectroscopic-quality EDFs and more sophisticated statistical techniques of uncertainty quantification.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that nuclear Density Functional Theory (DFT) predictions for alpha-decay energies are robust and consistent for well-deformed superheavy nuclei, but systematic uncertainties grow substantially for shape-transitional nuclei near the deformed-to-spherical transition approaching N=184, where the method of using Q_alpha values for nuclide identification is not expected to work well.

## Method

The authors solved Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations using seven Skyrme energy density functionals (SkM*, SLy4, SV-min, UNEDF0, UNEDF1, UNEDF2, and UNEDF1SO) within nuclear superfluid DFT. They computed ground-state binding energies for even-even nuclei with proton numbers Z=98 to Z=120, extracting Q_alpha values from the binding energy differences. To estimate systematic model uncertainties, they employed uniform model averaging over the four most recent global functionals (SV-min, UNEDF0, UNEDF1, UNEDF2).

## Key numbers

- UNEDF1 achieved the best individual performance with a root-mean-square (rms) deviation from experimental Q_alpha values of 0.31 MeV
- SkM* gave the largest rms deviation among the functionals at 0.81 MeV
- The model-averaged results (SV-min, UNEDF0, UNEDF1, UNEDF2) yielded an rms deviation of 0.35 MeV from experimental data
- For the decay chain of 270Ds, the spread of calculated Q_alpha values (excluding SkM*) was less than 1 MeV
- For 266Hs, the SkM* result was 25 keV above the closest result from SV-min
- For 294Og, every EDF underestimated the experimental Q_alpha value, with UNEDF1SO being closest

## Context

This work extends previous DFT studies of superheavy nuclei (Agbemava 2015, Heenen 2015) by systematically comparing multiple Skyrme functionals and quantifying uncertainties through model averaging, rather than focusing on a single model's performance. The results benchmark the reliability of Q_alpha predictions for nuclide identification in superheavy element searches, and the authors plan to apply Bayesian machine learning techniques (Neufcourt 2018) for more sophisticated uncertainty quantification in future work.
