# Ab initio symmetry-adapted approaches to nuclear reactions

> In this review, we discuss recent applications of the ab initio symmetry-adapted no-core shell-model (SA-NCSM) theory for study and prediction of structure and reactions of stable and unstable nuclei from light to medium mass range. We explore structure properties of neutron-rich He, Mg, and Li isotopes, with a focus on nuclear collectivity, clustering, and spectroscopic factors, as well as multi-particle excitations of utmost significance in the proximity of the drip lines. In addition, we present extensions of the SA-NCSM with continuum for determining the microscopic structure of reaction fragments, which enables calculations of reaction cross sections for targets from the lightest $^{4,6}$He to $^{40}$Ca, rooted in first principles. We illustrate this for neutron and proton elastic scattering, deuteron and alpha capture reactions, and alpha knock-out reactions. Furthermore, we discuss microscopic optical potentials with uncertainty quantification, a critical ingredient in many reaction models, and reaction observables with uncertainties that stem from the underlying chiral potential. We also discuss the impact of alpha clustering on reactions of significance to nuclear astrophysics, as well as on beta decays and beyond-the-standard-model physics.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the *ab initio* symmetry-adapted no-core shell model (SA-NCSM) can describe both structure and reactions of stable and unstable nuclei from light to medium mass, but the actual reach of the method is limited to nuclei up to approximately mass A ≈ 50 (around the Calcium isotopes), and the continuum extensions are demonstrated for targets from the lightest ⁴,⁶He to ⁴⁰Ca.

## Method

The SA-NCSM enumerates all possible many-body configurations of protons and neutrons inside a nucleus using a symmetry-adapted basis that respects the inherent symmetries of nuclei, with the nuclear force modeled by chiral effective field theory. For reactions, the framework is extended with continuum treatments through three approaches: the Green's function (GF) technique, the resonating group method (RGM), and multiple scattering theory (MST). These extensions enable calculations of reaction observables such as elastic scattering cross sections, capture reaction cross sections, and knock-out reaction cross sections, as well as microscopic optical potentials with uncertainty quantification.

## Key numbers

- The SA-NCSM can reach medium-mass nuclei around the Calcium isotopes and mass A ≈ 50, with calculations for ²⁰Ne requiring 3.64 × 10¹² basis states in a full basis but becoming feasible in the SA-NCSM
- Calculations for ²⁰Ne use a model space of 145 million basis states (13 harmonic oscillator shells)
- The approach reproduces electric quadrupole (E2) transitions and moments using the bare proton charge and zero neutron charge, eliminating the need for fitted charge parameters
- The SA-NCSM with continuum enables calculations of reaction cross sections for targets from the lightest ⁴,⁶He to ⁴⁰Ca
- Low-energy nuclear reaction applications are relevant at projectile energies less than approximately 20 MeV per nucleon for the astrophysical regime
- Multiple scattering theory applications are relevant at projectile energies greater than approximately 65 MeV per nucleon
- The approach has been applied to neutron and proton elastic scattering, deuteron and alpha capture reactions, and alpha knock-out reactions

## Context

This review extends the successful concepts of the symplectic shell model (Rosensteel and Rowe, 1977) and the no-core shell model (Navratil, Vary, and Barrett, 2000) by using a symmetry-adapted basis that expands the reach of practically exact models to the medium-mass region. The work enables unified descriptions of deformation, rotations, vibrations, and clustering within a single *ab initio* framework, and provides microscopic inputs for coupled reaction channels (CRC) calculations and Hauser-Feshbach statistical reaction theory that can potentially bridge between light nuclei and heavier species.
