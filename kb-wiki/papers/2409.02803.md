# Configurational entropy and stability conditions of fermion and boson stars

> In a remarkable study by M. Gleiser and N. Jiang [Phys. Rev. D {\bf 92}, 044046, 2015], the authors demonstrated that the stability regions of neutron stars, within the framework of the simple Fermi gas model, and self-gravitating configurations of complex scalar field (boson stars) with various self couplings, obtained through traditional perturbation methods, correlate with critical points of the configurational entropy with an accuracy of a few percent. Recently, P. Koliogiannis \textit{et al.} [Phys. Rev. D {\bf 107}, 044069 2023] found that while the minimization of the configurational entropy generally anticipates qualitatively the stability point for neutron stars and quark stars, this approach lacks universal validity. In this work, we aim to further elucidate this issue by seeking to reconcile these seemingly contradictory findings. Specifically, we calculate the configurational entropy of bosonic and fermionic systems, described by interacting Fermi and boson gases, respectively, that form compact objects stabilized by gravity. We investigate whether the minimization of configurational entropy coincides with the stability point of the corresponding compact objects. Our results indicate a strong correlation between the stability points predicted by configurational entropy and those obtained through traditional methods, with the accuracy of this correlation showing a slight dependence on the interaction strength. Consequently, the stability of compact objects, composed of components obeying Fermi or boson statistics, can alternatively be assessed using the concept of configurational entropy.

## Key claim

The authors find that the minimization of configurational entropy correlates strongly with the traditional stability point (maximum mass configuration) for both fermion and boson stars, but the accuracy of this correlation depends on the interaction strength and the choice of momentum cut-off scheme, resolving apparent contradictions between earlier work by Gleiser and Jiang (2015) and Koliogiannis et al. (2023).

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for compact objects described by interacting Fermi gas equations of state and three different parametrizations of interacting boson gas equations of state. They compute the configurational entropy in momentum space from the Fourier transform of the energy density profile for each configuration, then compare the central density at which the configurational entropy reaches a minimum with the central density at the maximum mass (the traditional stability point). The analysis includes two analytical TOV solutions (Schwarzschild constant-density and Tolman-VII) and examines the effect of different momentum cut-off schemes.

## Key numbers

* For the interacting Fermi gas, the percentage error in gravitational mass between the configurational entropy method and the traditional method ranges from 0.022% (for interaction parameter y=0.001 MeV^-1) to 4.433% (for y=0.3 MeV^-1), while the error in central density ranges from 6.014% to 98.601%.
* For the boson gas case BG-C1, the percentage error in gravitational mass ranges from 1.750% (for w=0.5 MeV^-1 fm^3) to 2.252% (for w=0.3 MeV^-1 fm^3), while the error in central density ranges from 55.327% to 69.804%.
* For the boson gas case BG-C2, the percentage error in gravitational mass ranges from 3.237% (for z=0.05 MeV^-1 fm^3) to 4.161% (for z=0.3 MeV^-1 fm^3), while the error in central density ranges from 80.190% to 94.215%.
* For the boson gas case BG-C3 case (a) with n=4, the percentage error in gravitational mass is 0.418% and in central density is 14.332%; for case (b) with n=3, the error in gravitational mass is 4.318% and in central density is 100.000%.
* Using a cut-off at k_min=pi/R for the BG-C1 (w=0.001) case reduces the error in gravitational mass from 1.776% (no cut-off) to 0.068%, and the error in central density from 58.881% to 9.083%.

## Context

This work extends the investigation by Gleiser and Jiang (2015), who found that configurational entropy minima correlate with stability points for neutron stars (using a simple Fermi gas model) and boson stars with a few percent accuracy, and addresses the contradictory findings of Koliogiannis et al. (2023), who found that the approach lacks universal validity for neutron stars and quark stars with realistic equations of state. The results show that while configurational entropy can predict the maximum gravitational mass with errors typically under 5%, it fails to accurately predict the corresponding central density (errors up to 100%), and the choice of momentum cut-off scheme significantly affects the convergence. This enables future work to refine configurational entropy as a stability diagnostic for exotic compact objects such as dark matter stars.
