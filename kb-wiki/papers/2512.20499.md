# Shear viscosity at finite magnetic field for graphene, non-relativistic and ultra-relativistic cases

> The present article has addressed the finite magnetic field extension of the previous work by Cho et al. (Phys. Rev. B 108, 235172, 2023) on microscopic calculation of shear viscosity for electron fluid in graphene system. Our calculation is based on the kinetic theory approach in the relaxation time approximation. In the absence of a magnetic field, transport is governed by a single shear viscosity coefficient, whereas the application of a finite magnetic field induces anisotropy, giving rise to five independent shear viscosity coefficients associated with distinct velocity gradient tensors. These coefficients can be physically categorized into perpendicular, parallel, and Hall components relative to the magnetic field direction. When the scattering time equals the cyclotron time, the perpendicular component is suppressed by 80% and the parallel component by 50% and the Hall effect can reach maximum. Corresponding magnetic field strength for electron fluid in graphene is around 0.01-0.1 Tesla, and the same for non-relativistic electron fluid and ultra-relativistic quark fluid are around 10 Tesla and 10^{14} Tesla respectively. They may be considered as the required magnetic field strength in three different fluid systems to observe noticeable magnetic field response in their shear viscosity coefficients.

## Key claim
The paper extends a previous zero-field calculation of shear viscosity in graphene (Cho et al., Phys. Rev. B 108, 235172, 2023) to finite magnetic fields, showing that the magnetic field strength required to observe a noticeable response in the shear viscosity coefficients differs by many orders of magnitude across three systems: approximately 0.01-0.1 Tesla for graphene, 10 Tesla for non-relativistic electron fluids, and 10^14 Tesla for ultra-relativistic quark fluids.

## Method
The authors use kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, solving the Boltzmann transport equation with a Lorentz force term to obtain five independent shear viscosity coefficients from the viscous stress tensor. These coefficients are categorized into perpendicular, parallel, and Hall components relative to the magnetic field direction, and are expressed in terms of effective relaxation times that depend on both the scattering time tau_c and the cyclotron time tau_B. The formalism is applied to three dispersion relations: the graphene case (E = p v with v ≈ 10^6 m/s), the non-relativistic case (E = p^2/2m), and the ultra-relativistic case (E = p).

## Key numbers
- When the scattering time equals the cyclotron time (tau_c = tau_B), the perpendicular shear viscosity component is suppressed by 80% and the parallel component by 50%, while the Hall component reaches its maximum.
- The magnetic field strength required for this condition in graphene electron fluid is around 0.01-0.1 Tesla.
- The corresponding magnetic field for non-relativistic electron fluid is around 10 Tesla.
- The corresponding magnetic field for ultra-relativistic quark fluid is around 10^14 Tesla.
- The lower bound of eta/s for graphene near charge neutrality (mu → 0) is 5/(4 pi), which is five times the KSS bound of 1/(4 pi).

## Context
This work extends the zero-field microscopic calculation of shear viscosity for graphene by Cho et al. (2023) to finite magnetic fields, building on prior theoretical frameworks for magnetohydrodynamics in graphene by Narozhny (2019) and Alekseev (2016). The formalism parallels earlier finite-field shear viscosity calculations in relativistic hydrodynamics by Tuchin (2011), Ghosh (2018), and others, but applies it to the unconventional "graphene hydrodynamics" case which differs from both non-relativistic and relativistic hydrodynamics. The comparison across three systems (graphene, non-relativistic electron fluids, and ultra-relativistic quark fluids) enables identifying the vastly different magnetic field scales needed to observe anisotropic viscosity effects in each system.
