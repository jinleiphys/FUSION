# Non-Hermitian Quantum Mechanics Approach for Extracting and Emulating Continuum Physics Based on Bound-State-Like Calculations

> This work introduces a unified emulation framework for studying continuum physics in finite quantum systems. Using a reduced basis method, we construct powerful emulators for the inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation that operate in a combined parameter space of complex energy ($E$) and other inputs ($\bmθ$). Within the space, the emulators simultaneously perform analytical continuation in $E$ -- extracting continuum physics from numerically simpler bound-state-like calculations -- and interpolate this entire process across $\bmθ$. This yields a small, non-Hermitian system whose properties (e.g., resonances and scattering observables) can be rapidly predicted for any $\bmθ$. Crucially, the complex-$E$ emulation provides a pathway to compute continuum observables for complex systems where advanced bound-state methods exist but direct continuum calculations are yet to be developed, while the $\bmθ$-emulation enables rapid parameter-space exploration and can be adapted to accelerate other existing continuum calculations. Demonstrations with two- and three-body systems highlight the method's effectiveness and suggest its connection to (near-)optimal rational approximation. This Letter presents the key results, with further details reserved for a companion paper.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a reduced basis method (RBM) emulator that simultaneously performs analytical continuation in complex energy \(E\), extracting continuum physics from bound-state-like calculations, and interpolates this entire process across other input parameters \(\bm{\theta}\), but the method's effectiveness depends on breaking the mirror symmetry of training energy locations with respect to the real axis to force branch cuts away from the real axis.

## Method

The method solves the inhomogeneous Schrödinger equation at a sample of training points \((E_\alpha^\mathrm{tr}, \bm{\theta}_\alpha^\mathrm{tr})\) with \(\Im E_\alpha^\mathrm{tr} > 0\), where the solutions satisfy bound-state-like boundary conditions that are numerically simpler than oscillating asymptotics at real energies. These solutions are used as a subspace basis to construct a small non-Hermitian linear system via a variational approach, projecting the full operator onto the reduced basis. The resulting reduced-order model allows rapid emulation of the resolvent matrix element \(\mathcal{A}(E,\bm{\theta})\) for arbitrary \(E\) and \(\bm{\theta}\) by solving a small \(N_b\)-dimensional system.

## Key numbers

- Training energies for two-body demonstration: \(N_b = 23\) points evenly spaced with \(\Im E^\mathrm{tr}_\alpha = 10\) MeV, effective basis size \(N_\mathrm{eff} \approx 10\)
- Three-body spectrum emulation: \(N_b = 48\) training energies with \(\Im E^\mathrm{tr}_\alpha = 3\) MeV and \(\Re E^\mathrm{tr}_\alpha \in [-20, 50]\) MeV, \(N_\mathrm{eff} \approx 15\) for both bound-dimer and resonance cases
- Three-body parameter-space emulation: \(N_b = 60\) training points sampled via Latin hypercube sampling in \((\Re E, \lambda_4, \lambda_2)\) space with \(\Re E \in [-20, 50]\) MeV, \(\lambda_4 \in [-0.5, 0.5]\), \(\lambda_2 \in [-2.15, 1]\), \(N_\mathrm{eff} \approx 43\)
- Particle-dimer scattering emulation: \(N_b = 128\) training points sampled via Latin hypercube sampling in \((\Re E, E_\mathrm{rel}, \lambda_2, \lambda_4)\) space with \(E_\mathrm{rel} \in [0, 60]\) MeV and \(\lambda_2\) range giving dimer binding energy \(B_2 \in [2, 10]\) MeV

## Context

This work extends the complex energy (CE) method of Schlessinger (1966) and the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method of Efros (1985), which both exploit bound-state-like calculations to access continuum physics but use different inference techniques (continued-fraction extrapolation and integral transforms, respectively). The RBM-based approach provides a unified framework that can also accelerate existing continuum calculations from CE and LIT methods, and shows connections to (near-)optimal rational approximation as studied by Nakatsukasa (2018) and Trefethen (2021).
