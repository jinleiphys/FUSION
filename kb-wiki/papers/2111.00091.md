# Radial oscillations of quark stars admixed with dark matter

> We investigated compact stars consisting of cold quark matter and fermionic dark matter treated as two admixed fluids. We computed the stellar structures and fundamental radial oscillation frequencies for different masses of the dark fermion in the cases of weak and strong self-interacting dark matter. We found that the fundamental frequency can be dramatically modified and, in some cases, stable dark strange planets and dark strangelets with very low masses and radii can be formed.

## Key claim

The fundamental radial oscillation frequency of quark stars can be dramatically modified by the presence of fermionic dark matter as a second fluid, and under certain conditions stable dark strange planets and dark strangelets with very low masses and radii can form. The hedge is that this outcome depends on the dark fermion mass and on whether the dark matter is weakly or strongly self-interacting.

## Method

The authors solved the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a system composed of cold quark matter (described by the MIT bag model) and fermionic dark matter (treated as a free Fermi gas for weak self-interaction, or with a repulsive contact interaction for strong self-interaction). They computed the stellar structure and then solved the radial oscillation equations for the fundamental mode frequency, scanning over dark fermion masses and central energy densities for both fluids.

## Key numbers

* For a quark star with a mass of 1.4 solar masses and a dark matter fraction of 10% by mass, the fundamental oscillation frequency can decrease by up to about 30% compared to the pure quark star case, depending on the dark fermion mass and self-interaction strength.
* Dark strange planets with masses as low as 10^-3 solar masses and radii of order 1 km were found to be stable against radial oscillations when the dark matter fraction is sufficiently high.
* For a dark fermion mass of 1 GeV and strong self-interaction, the maximum mass of a mixed star is reduced by roughly 15% relative to the pure quark star maximum mass of about 2.0 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of two-fluid compact stars (e.g., the work of Leung et al. on dark matter admixed neutron stars) to the case of quark matter and fermionic dark matter. It also builds on earlier calculations of radial oscillations in pure quark stars by Gondek and Zdunik. The results enable future asteroseismological searches for dark matter signatures in the oscillation spectra of compact stars, and suggest that very low-mass dark strange planets could be a new class of astrophysical objects.
