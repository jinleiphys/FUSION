# Exchange-assisted tunneling in the classical limit

## Key claim

Exchange interaction and correlations can produce a power-law decay of the wave function under a potential barrier instead of the usual exponential decrease, but this exchange-assisted tunneling vanishes in the classical limit (ℏ → 0) with a dependence on Planck's constant that differs from conventional single-particle tunneling; the vanishing occurs because the exchange integral between two semiclassical states contains an exponential factor exp(−1/ℏ), and for a quantum ground state interacting with a semiclassical state the exchange integral is also exponentially small.

## Method

The author analyzes the Hartree-Fock equations for a fermion, where the exchange term is a non-local operator K(r), and examines the asymptotic behavior of the bound-state wave function under a potential barrier when exchange with an above-barrier particle is included. A two-well potential model with two particles (one in a ground state, one in a higher state near the barrier top) is used to isolate the exchange-assisted tunneling amplitude, which is expressed via the Coulomb exchange integral G(2,1L;1R,2). The classical limit is studied by evaluating the ℏ dependence of this exchange integral for three cases: two semiclassical states, a quantum ground state with a semiclassical state, and a singular attractive potential (Coulomb ground state).

## Key numbers

- For inner orbitals inside molecules, the exchange-induced decay is r^(−2).
- For macroscopic crystals, the decay is cos(k_f r) r^(−ν), where ν = 3 for 1D, ν = 3.5 for 2D, and ν = 4 for 3D crystals.
- The exchange-assisted tunneling amplitude B_G1 scales as G(2,1L;1R,2) / (E_1L − E_1R), where the exchange integral G decays as 1/l^2 for large inter-well distance l.
- The single-particle tunneling amplitude t_1 scales as exp(−∫|p|dr/ℏ), containing the factor exp(−1/ℏ).

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier results (Flambaum 2009) and those of Drukarev, Filippov, and Sizov (DFS) on power-law decay of wave functions due to exchange interactions, which were previously shown to increase spin-spin interactions in solids and under-barrier tunneling amplitudes. The paper addresses the question of whether such exchange-assisted tunneling survives in the classical limit, contradicting the naive expectation that all quantum statistics effects vanish when the wavelength goes to zero. The analysis distinguishes exchange-assisted tunneling from the classical "ionization by collision" process (where final energies are not individually fixed), showing that the additional energy constraint in exchange-assisted tunneling gives zero phase volume for classical processes. This enables future work on the ℏ dependence of exchange effects in tunneling phenomena and clarifies the conditions under which exchange enhancement can occur.
