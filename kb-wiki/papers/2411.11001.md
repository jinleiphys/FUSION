# Simultaneous Estimation of Elliptic Flow Coefficient and Impact Parameter in Heavy-Ion Collisions using CNN

> A deep learning based method with Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) algorithm is developed for simultaneous determination of the Elliptic Flow coefficient ($v_{2}$) and the Impact Parameter in Heavy-Ion Collisions at relativistic energies. The proposed CNN is trained on Pb$-$Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV with minimum biased events simulated with the AMPT event generator. A total of twelve models were built on different input and output combinations and their performances were evaluated. The predictions of the CNN models were compared to the estimations of the simulated and experimental data. The deep learning model seems to preserve the centrality and $p_{T}$ dependence of $v_{2}$ at the LHC energy together with predicting successfully the impact parameter with low margins of error. This is the first time a CNN is built to predict both $v_{2}$ and the impact parameter simultaneously in heavy-ion system.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a single Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) can simultaneously estimate both the elliptic flow coefficient (v₂) and the impact parameter (b) from the η-φ distribution of charged particles in Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV, with the hedge that the model was trained exclusively on AMPT-simulated events and its performance was evaluated against both simulated and experimental data, with the best results requiring both pT and mass-weighted input layers.

## Method

The authors generated 50K minimum-bias Pb-Pb events at √sNN = 5.02 TeV using the string-melting mode of the AMPT event generator, selecting charged particles with 0.2 < pT < 5.0 GeV/c and |η| < 0.8. They constructed binned (η, φ) images with (16×16) and (32×32) bin settings, weighted by pT, mass, transverse mass (mT), or both pT and mass, and trained a CNN with four convolutional layers (16, 16, 32, 32 filters), two dense layers (64 and 32 neurons), and a linear activation function using mean-squared error loss and the Adam optimizer. Twelve separate models were built covering four input options and three output options (v₂ only, b only, or both), with 80% of events used for training/validation and 20% for testing.

## Key numbers

- The CNN was trained on 50K minimum-bias Pb-Pb events at √sNN = 5.02 TeV, with 40K events for training/validation and 10K for testing
- Best-performing model (both pT and mass-weighted input, both v₂ and b output, 32×32 bins) achieved MAE of 4.37% for simultaneous prediction
- For v₂ prediction, all models achieved relative error (Δv₂/v₂^true) of 5% even in low-statistics regions where v₂ > 0.4
- For impact parameter prediction, the worst-performing centrality class (0-10%) deviated by only 0.25 fm with relative error of 7.1%, while the best case showed relative error as low as 0.5%
- All 24 models (12 combinations × 2 bin settings) showed maximum error less than 6%
- The pT-weighted input alone achieved MAE of 4.93% for v₂ and 3.83% for b with 32×32 bins
- The mT-weighted input achieved MAE of 3.84% for b with 16×16 bins (best single-output b prediction)

## Context

This work extends previous machine learning approaches to heavy-ion collisions, such as DNN-based flow estimation (DNNflow) and impact parameter estimation (impact1, impact2), by being the first to simultaneously predict both v₂ and impact parameter using a single CNN model. The authors compare their v₂ predictions against ALICE experimental data for Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 5.02 TeV (AliceFlow), showing good agreement across centrality classes. The method enables direct application to LHC experimental data and suggests that training on high-quality datasets from other event generators like HYDJET++ could further improve performance, while also providing a tool to test and improve Monte Carlo event generators.
