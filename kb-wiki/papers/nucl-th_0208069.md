# Theory of the Trojan-Horse Method

> The Trojan-Horse method is an indirect approach to determine the energy dependence of S-factors of astrophysically relevant two-body reactions. This is accomplished by studying closely related three-body reactions under quasi-free scattering conditions. The basic theory of the Trojan-Horse method is developed starting from a post-form distorted wave Born approximation of the T-matrix element. In the surface approximation the cross section of the three-body reaction can be related to the S-matrix elements of the two-body reaction. The essential feature of the Trojan-Horse method is the effective suppression of the Coulomb barrier at low energies for the astrophysical reaction leading to finite cross sections at the threshold of the two-body reaction. In a modified plane wave approximation the relation between the two-body and three-body cross sections becomes very transparent. The appearing Trojan-Horse integrals are studied in detail.

## Key claim

The Trojan-Horse Method (THM) can extract the energy dependence of astrophysical S-factors for two-body reactions by studying three-body reactions under quasi-free scattering conditions, with the essential feature being the effective suppression of the Coulomb barrier at low energies leading to finite cross sections at the threshold of the two-body reaction. The authors develop the theory starting from a post-form distorted wave Born approximation and show that in the surface approximation the three-body cross section can be related to the S-matrix elements of the two-body reaction, though the evaluation of actual Trojan-Horse experiments with the present theory is beyond the scope of this paper.

## Method

The authors derive the T-matrix element for the three-body reaction A + a → C + c + b using a post-form distorted wave Born approximation, where the Trojan Horse a is composed of clusters x and b. They apply the surface approximation, replacing the full scattering wave function of the C+c system with its asymptotic form for radii larger than a cutoff radius R, and introduce a plane wave approximation for the relative motions of A-a and B-b to obtain a factorized expression relating the three-body cross section to the S-matrix elements of the two-body reaction A + x → C + c. The resulting Trojan-Horse integrals, which describe the off-shell behavior and the effective removal of the Coulomb barrier, are studied in detail using Coulomb wave functions and Riccati-Bessel functions.

## Key numbers

- The cutoff radius R for the surface approximation is typically in the range of the sum of the radii of the two colliding nuclei, with the validity checked for the (d,p) reaction at E_d = 26 MeV.
- The binding energy ε_a of the Trojan Horse a with respect to the x+b threshold is given by ε_a = (m_x + m_b - m_a)c^2 = Q_2 - Q_3 > 0.
- The TH integrals J_l^(±) are defined with the dimensionless form J_l^(±)(R,η_Ax,k_Ax,Q_Aa) = k_Ax Q_Aa ∫_R^∞ dr_Ax u_l^(±)(η_Ax;k_Ax r_Ax) r_Ax j_l(Q_Aa r_Ax), where the Coulomb wave functions u_l^(±) and spherical Bessel functions j_l appear.
- The relation J_l^(H)(R,η,k,Q) = k/(k^2 - Q^2) [2ηk I_l^(H)(R,η,k,Q) + k H_l'(η;kR) z_l(QR) - Q H_l(η;kR) z_l'(QR)] is derived, valid for k ≠ Q, with k < Q from kinematical considerations.

## Context

This work extends earlier theoretical developments for the Trojan-Horse Method by Typel (2000) that were not directly applicable to experiments, and provides a more complete framework than the plane-wave impulse approximation (PWIA) used by Jain (1970) which did not make the Coulomb barrier suppression explicit. The THM complements other indirect methods such as the Coulomb dissociation method (Baur, 1994) and the asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) method (Azhari, 2001; Xu, 1994), which are limited to radiative capture reactions, whereas the THM can be applied to reactions where no photon is involved. The theory enables future extraction of two-body S-matrix elements from three-body cross section measurements, with applications to reactions such as those studied experimentally by Musumarra (2001), Spitaleri (2001), and others.
