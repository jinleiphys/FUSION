# Nonclassical trajectories in head-on collisions

> Rutherford scattering is usually described by treating the projectile either classically or as quantum mechanical plane waves. Here we treat them as wave packets and study their head-on collisions with the stationary target nuclei. We simulate the quantum dynamics of this one-dimensional system and study deviations of the average quantum solution from the classical one. These deviations are traced back to the convexity properties of Coulomb potential. Finally, we sketch how these theoretical findings could be tested in experiments looking for the onset of nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The average quantum trajectory of a projectile in a head-on Coulomb collision does not recover the classical solution, even in the asymptotic limit after the collision, because the Ehrenfest theorem equates quantum and classical dynamics only for potentials that are at most quadratic in position; for the convex Coulomb potential, Jensen's inequality guarantees that the average quantum force differs from the classical force at the same average position.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for a Gaussian wave packet representing an alpha particle incident head-on on a stationary gold nucleus, using the Cayley form of the evolution operator to maintain unitarity. They compared the resulting average position, momentum, and force (Ehrenfest trajectories) with the classical trajectories obtained from energy conservation and Hamilton's equations for the same Coulomb potential. The tunneling probability through a truncated Coulomb barrier was computed from the time-evolved wave function and compared with the classical barrier-crossing probability (from a Gaussian momentum ensemble) and with the WKB approximation.

## Key numbers

- For an alpha particle with kinetic energy \(T_0 = 5\) MeV launched from \(L = 10\) pm, the optimal initial wave-packet width that minimizes the difference between quantum and classical distances of closest approach is \(\sigma_0 = \sqrt{\hbar L / 2P} \approx 50\) fm.
- The quantum distance of closest approach satisfies \(d_{\mathrm{cl}} < d_{\mathrm{qm}} < d_{\mathrm{cl}} + \sqrt{\hbar L / (2P)}\) for the energies considered.
- For a launch distance \(L = 50\) pm and \(T_0 = 5\) MeV, the return journey of the quantum projectile is longer than the onward journey by approximately \(1.3 \times 10^{-22}\) s for \(\sigma = 159.85\) fm, and by approximately \(1.1 \times 10^{-21}\) s for \(\sigma = 20\) fm.
- At \(t=0\) for an alpha particle with \(T_0 = 5\) MeV launched from \(L = 50\) pm, the average quantum force is \(\ev{F} \approx 1.000031 \, F_{\mathrm{cl}}\).
- At \(\sigma = 10\) fm, the classical barrier-crossing probability is more than three orders of magnitude smaller than the quantum tunneling probability, which is at the measurable level of \(10^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the standard treatment of Rutherford scattering, which uses either classical point particles or quantum plane waves (Joachain, 1975), by modeling the projectile as a Gaussian wave packet and computing the full time-dependent dynamics. It directly contradicts the textbook statement (Eisberg, 1961) that the average quantum trajectory recovers classical motion, and it generalizes the Ehrenfest-theorem analysis of cubic potentials to potentials with fixed convexity (Jensen, 1906). The results enable experimental tests of quantum deviations via the onset of nuclear reactions, where the tunneling probability depends on the initial wave-packet spread, and they provide conditions under which the WKB approximation (Wentzel, 1926; Kramers, 1926; Brillouin, 1926) fails for low-energy projectiles.
