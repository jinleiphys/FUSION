# Long-range correlations in finite nuclei: comparison of two self-consistent treatments

> Long-range correlations, which are partially responsible for the observed fragmentation and depletion of low-lying single-particle strength, are studied in the Green's function formalism. The self-energy is expanded up to second order in the residual interaction. We compare two methods of implementing self-consistency in the solution of the Dyson equation beyond Hartree-Fock, for the case of the 16O nucleus. It is found that the energy-bin method and the BAGEL method lead to globally equivalent results. In both methods the final single-particle strength functions are characterized by exponential tails at energies far from the Fermi level.

## Key claim

Long-range correlations in finite nuclei can be treated self-consistently using either the energy-bin (BIN) method or the BAGEL method, and these two approaches yield globally equivalent results for the single-particle spectral functions of 16O, but the BIN method allows for more fragmentation into discrete states at intermediate energies.

## Method

The authors solved the Dyson equation for the single-particle Green's function in 16O with the self-energy expanded to second order in the residual interaction, using a G-matrix derived from the Bonn-C potential. They compared two iterative self-consistency schemes: the BIN method, which discretizes the energy axis into equidistant bins (5460 bins of width 0.05 MeV) and uses Fast Fourier Transform for the triple convolution in the self-energy, and the BAGEL(6,6) method, which represents the Green's function by a small set of poles that reproduce the lowest-order moments of the strength distribution via a truncated Lanczos tridiagonalization. Both methods were iterated until convergence, starting from renormalized Hartree-Fock single-particle energies.

## Context

This work extends earlier self-consistent Green's function calculations by Van Neck et al. (1991, 1993) using the BIN method and by Müther et al. (1993) using the BAGEL approach, providing the first direct comparison of the two methods for a finite nucleus (16O). The results confirm that both methods give equivalent global quantities (occupation probabilities, binding energies) and reveal a surprising exponential tail in the spectral functions at high excitation energies, which the authors suggest is a genuine consequence of self-consistency. This enables future applications of self-consistent Green's function methods beyond second order, and opens the possibility of studying the connection between many-body damping and stochastization in complex wavefunctions, as also noted in a recent investigation by Frazier (1996) on statistical properties of sd-shell states.
