# Correlations in Nuclei: Self-Consistent Treatment and the BAGEL Approach

> An approach is presented which allows a self-consistent description of the fragmentation of single-particle strength for nucleons in finite nuclei employing the Greens function formalism. The self-energy to be considered in the Dyson equation for the single-particle Greens function contains all terms of first (Hartree-Fock) and second order in the residual interaction. It is demonstrated that the fragmentation of the single-particle strength originating from the terms of second order can efficiently be described in terms of the so-called BAGEL approximation. Employing this approximation the self-energy can be evaluated in a self-consistent way, i.e. the correlations contained in the Greens function are taken into account for the evaluation of the self-energy. As an example this scheme is applied to the nucleus $^{16}O$, using a realistic nucleon nucleon interaction. The effects of the correlations on the occupation probabilities and the binding energy are evaluated.

# Correlations in Nuclei: Self-Consistent Treatment and the BAGEL Approach

## Key claim

The paper presents a self-consistent Green's function formalism for finite nuclei in which the self-energy includes all first-order (Hartree-Fock) and second-order terms in the residual interaction, and demonstrates that the BAGEL (BAsis GEnerated by Lanczos method) approximation can efficiently represent the single-particle Green's function with a small number of poles, enabling iterative self-consistency. The authors show that already the simplest BAGEL(0,1) approximation yields results very close to the most sophisticated BAGEL(6,6) calculation for the test nucleus ¹⁶O.

## Method

The irreducible self-energy for nucleons is constructed from first-order (Hartree-Fock) and second-order terms in an effective interaction derived from a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential via the Bethe-Goldstone equation. The Dyson equation for the single-particle Green's function is solved by diagonalizing a matrix that couples the single-particle state to 2-particle-1-hole (2p1h) and 2-hole-1-particle (2h1p) configurations. The BAGEL procedure transforms this matrix to tridiagonal form using the Lanczos method, allowing truncation to a small number of representative poles (p for 2p1h and q for 2h1p configurations). This truncated Green's function is then used to recompute the self-energy in an iterative scheme until convergence is achieved.

- Occupation probability for the 0s₁/₂ hole state in ¹⁶O: 0.978 (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6)), reduced from 1.000 in Hartree-Fock
- Occupation probability for the 0p₃/₂ hole state in ¹⁶O: 0.965 (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6))
- Occupation probability for the 0p₁/₂ hole state in ¹⁶O: 0.951 (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6))
- Occupation probability for the 0d₅/₂ particle state in ¹⁶O: 0.017 (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6)), increased from 0.000 in Hartree-Fock
- Single-particle energy for 0s₁/₂ in ¹⁶O: -45.09 MeV (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6)), compared to -44.15 MeV in Hartree-Fock
- Single-particle energy for 0p₃/₂ in ¹⁶O: -22.17 MeV (self-consistent BAGEL(6,6)), compared to -20.82 MeV in Hartree-Fock

## Context

This work extends the Green's function approach to finite nuclei by introducing a self-consistent treatment of correlations beyond the independent particle model, building on earlier studies of short-range correlations in nuclear matter by Pandharipande (1984), Ramos (1989), Benhar (1989), and Kohler (1992). The BAGEL method, previously developed by Muther and Skouras (1993, in press) and Muther, Taigel, and Kuo (1988), provides a systematic truncation scheme that avoids the "explosion of basis configurations" encountered when using the full spectral distribution of the Green's function, as an alternative to the energy-bin approach of Van Neck, Waroquier, and Ryckebusch (1991). The scheme enables calculations for heavier nuclei and larger model spaces, and the authors note that a similar approach could be developed for infinite systems like nuclear matter.
