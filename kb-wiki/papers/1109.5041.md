# Crustal failure during binary inspiral

> We present the first fully relativistic calculations of the crustal strain induced in a neutron star by a binary companion at the late stages of inspiral, employing realistic equations of state for the fluid core and the solid crust. We show that while the deep crust is likely to fail only shortly before coalescence, there is a large variation in elastic strain, with the outermost layers failing relatively early on in the inspiral. We discuss the significance of the results for both electromagnetic and gravitational-wave astronomy.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first fully relativistic calculations of crustal strain in a neutron star during binary inspiral, showing that while the deep crust fails only shortly before coalescence, the outermost layers fail relatively early in the inspiral. The key hedge is that these results depend on the assumed breaking strain of u_break ≈ 0.1 from molecular dynamics simulations, and the calculations do not account for the likely presence of nuclear pasta in the inner crust.

## Method

The authors extended the relativistic elastic perturbation scheme of Penner et al. (2011) to compute the tidal deformation of a neutron star with a solid crust and fluid core, using the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall equation of state for the core and the Douchin-Haensel equation of state for the crust. They calculated the Love number k₂ and the von Mises stress throughout the crust for a 1.4 M⊙ star in an equal-mass binary, then compared the strain to the breaking strain from molecular dynamics simulations to determine the gravitational-wave frequency at failure for different crustal layers.

## Key numbers

- The outer crust (up to neutron drip at r ≈ 11.15 km) fails at f_GW ≈ 200 Hz
- The bulk of the inner crust fails at f_GW ≈ 600-800 Hz, a factor of two or so below the ISCO frequency of 1.6 kHz for a 1.4 M⊙ equal-mass binary
- The breaking strain used is u_break ≈ 0.1 from molecular dynamics simulations
- The crustal strain energy release is estimated at up to 10⁴⁶ erg
- The released heat does not melt the crust: the required strain to melt would be u ≳ 0.5, exceeding the breaking strain of 0.1
- The 1.4 M⊙ star has a radius in the range 11-12 km, consistent with X-ray burst source constraints

## Context

This work resolves a discrepancy between earlier estimates by Kochanek (1992), who found crust failure only shortly before merger, and more recent work by Piro and collaborators (2010) and Troja and collaborators, who predicted much earlier failure. The authors show that the earlier-failure estimates incorrectly used the strain mapping appropriate for mountains rather than tidal deformations, overestimating the strain by a factor of ~10⁵. The results enable future studies of plastic deformation and dissipation during inspiral, and suggest that crustal failure could produce precursor electromagnetic signals comparable to the largest magnetar flares, though such events would be rare (roughly one per year of advanced LIGO/Virgo observation).
