# Nucleon Form Factors in QCD

> We calculate the electromagnetic and the axial form factors of the nucleon within the framework of light cone sum rules (LCSR) to leading order in QCD and including higher twist corrections. In particular we motivate a certain choice for the interpolating nucleon field. We find that a simple model of the nucleon distribution amplitudes which deviate from their asymptotic shape, but much less compared to the QCD sum rule estimates, allows one to describe the data remarkably well.

## Key claim

The authors calculate nucleon electromagnetic and axial form factors using light-cone sum rules (LCSR) to leading order in QCD with higher-twist corrections, and find that a simple model of nucleon distribution amplitudes that deviate from their asymptotic shape, but much less than previous QCD sum rule estimates, describes experimental data remarkably well, with the Ioffe current identified as the optimal interpolating nucleon field.

## Method

The authors construct light-cone sum rules for correlation functions involving an interpolating nucleon current (either the Ioffe current or a twist-3 current) and an electromagnetic or weak probe current, expanding the operator product on the light cone to twist-5 accuracy. They calculate the correlation functions to tree-level accuracy in QCD, extract the nucleon pole contribution via dispersion relations with a duality cutoff, and determine the form factors from the invariant amplitudes projected onto specific Lorentz structures. The analysis uses updated parameters for higher-twist nucleon distribution amplitudes and compares results obtained with three different interpolating currents to select the optimal one.

## Context

This work extends the LCSR approach for baryon form factors introduced by Braun et al. (2001) by using different interpolating currents, calculating both electromagnetic and weak form factors, and updating higher-twist distribution amplitude parameters. It contrasts with the hard perturbative QCD approach of Chernyak (1977) and Lepage & Brodsky (1979), where the leading contribution scales as 1/Q⁴ but is numerically suppressed by α_s/π factors, and with the QCD sum rule approach of Belyaev & Kogan (1992) which only works for Q² < 1 GeV². The results enable determination of baryon distribution amplitudes from experimental form factor data at large momentum transfers, with the soft contributions now calculable in terms of the same distribution amplitudes that enter the pQCD calculation, avoiding double counting.
