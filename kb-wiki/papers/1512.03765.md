# Nucleon Polarizabilities: from Compton Scattering to Hydrogen Atom

> We review the current state of knowledge of the nucleon polarizabilities and of their role in nucleon Compton scattering and in hydrogen spectrum. We discuss the basic concepts, the recent lattice QCD calculations and advances in chiral effective-field theory. On the experimental side, we review the ongoing programs aimed to measure the nucleon (scalar and spin) polarizabilities via the Compton scattering processes, with real and virtual photons. A great part of the review is devoted to the general constraints based on unitarity, causality, discrete and continuous symmetries, which result in model-independent relations involving nucleon polarizabilities. We (re-)derive a variety of such relations and discuss their empirical value. The proton polarizability effects are presently the major sources of uncertainty in the assessment of the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift and hyperfine structure. Recent calculations of these effects are reviewed here in the context of the "proton-radius puzzle". We conclude with summary plots of the recent results and prospects for the near-future work.

# Nucleon Polarizabilities: from Compton Scattering to Hydrogen Atom

## Key claim

The proton polarizability effects are presently the major sources of uncertainty in the assessment of the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift and hyperfine structure, and this review examines these effects in the context of the "proton-radius puzzle" while noting that the scalar dipole polarizabilities are measured in units of $10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$ and the critical electric field strength needed to induce any appreciable polarizability of the nucleon is approximately $10^{23}\,\mathrm{Volt}/\mathrm{m}$, which is only achievable through Compton scattering rather than static fields.

## Method

The authors review the current state of nucleon polarizabilities by examining lattice QCD calculations using the background-field method (measuring shifts in the mass spectrum upon applying classical electromagnetic fields) and chiral effective-field theory ($\chi$EFT) calculations at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) using the $\delta$-expansion power-counting scheme. They also review experimental programs measuring nucleon scalar and spin polarizabilities via Compton scattering with real and virtual photons, and re-derive model-independent relations based on unitarity, causality, and discrete and continuous symmetries.

## Key numbers

- Proton dipole polarizabilities from B$\chi$PT NNLO prediction: $\alpha_{E1}^{(p)} = 11.2 \pm 0.7 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$, $\beta_{M1}^{(p)} = 3.9 \pm 0.7 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$
- Proton dipole polarizabilities from PDG (2014): $\alpha_{E1} = 11.2 \pm 0.4 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$, $\beta_{M1} = 2.5 \pm 0.4 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$
- Neutron magnetic polarizability from lattice QCD (Adelaide CSSM group): $\beta_{M1}^{(n)} = 1.93 \pm 0.11_{\mathrm{stat}} \pm 0.11_{\mathrm{sys}} \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$
- $\Delta(1232)$-resonance excitation energy: $\varDelta = M_\Delta - M_N \simeq 293\,\mathrm{MeV}$
- Proton quadrupole polarizabilities from B$\chi$PT NNLO: $\alpha_{E2} = 17.3 \pm 3.9 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^5$, $\beta_{M2} = -15.5 \pm 3.5 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^5$
- Proton dispersive polarizabilities from B$\chi$PT NNLO: $\alpha_{E1\nu} = -1.3 \pm 1.0 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^5$, $\beta_{M1\nu} = 7.1 \pm 2.5 \times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^5$

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the understanding of nucleon polarizabilities from earlier work on Compton scattering and chiral perturbation theory, including the heavy-baryon $\chi$PT calculations of Hemmert (1996) and the baryon $\chi$PT framework of Lensky (2010, 2015). It addresses the discrepancy between the $\epsilon$-expansion and $\delta$-expansion counting schemes for $\Delta(1232)$ contributions, as seen in the forward spin polarizabilities calculations by Bernard (2012) versus Lensky (2014) and Alarcon (2015). The review enables future extractions of polarizabilities from ongoing Compton scattering experiments and improved calculations of two-photon-exchange effects in muonic hydrogen, which are critical for resolving the proton-radius puzzle.
