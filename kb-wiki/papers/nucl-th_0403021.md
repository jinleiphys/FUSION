# Magic numbers for superheavy nuclei in relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov theory

> The magic proton and neutron numbers are searched in the superheavy region with proton number $Z$=100 - 140 and neutron number $N$= ($Z$+30) - (2$Z$+32) by the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory with interactions NL1, NL3, NLSH, TM1, TW99, DD-ME1, PK1, and PK1R. Based on the two-nucleon separation energies $S_{2p}$ and $S_{2n}$, the two-nucleon gaps $δ_{2p}$ and $δ_{2n}$, the shell correction energies $E_{shell}^{p}$ and $E_{shell}^{n}$, the pairing energies $E_{pair}^{p}$ and $E_{pair}^{n}$, and the pairing gaps $Δ_{p}$ and $Δ_{n}$, $Z$=120, 132, and 138 and $N$=172, 184, 198, 228, 238, and 258 are suggested to be the magic numbers within the present approach. The $α$-decay half-lives are also discussed. In addition, the potential energy surfaces of possible doubly magic nuclei are obtained by the deformation-constrained relativistic mean field (RMF) theory, and the shell effects stabilizing the nuclei are investigated. Furthermore, the formation cross sections of $^{292}_{172}$120 and $^{304}_{184}$120 at the optimal excitation energy are estimated by a phenomenological cold fusion reactions model with the structure information extracted from the constrained RMF calculation.

## Key claim

Based on the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) theory with eight different effective interactions, the authors suggest that the magic proton numbers in the superheavy region are Z=120, 132, and 138, and the magic neutron numbers are N=172, 184, 198, 228, 238, and 258, but these magic numbers are subject to shell quenching effects and depend on the neutron or proton number of the nucleus, meaning the shell closures are not universal across all isotopes.

## Method

The authors performed RCHB calculations for over 1200 even-even nuclei with proton numbers Z=100-140 and neutron numbers N=130-312, using eight effective interactions (NL1, NL3, NLSH, TM1, TW99, DD-ME1, PK1, and PK1R). They analyzed the two-nucleon separation energies (S2p and S2n), two-nucleon gaps (δ2p and δ2n), shell correction energies from the Strutinsky procedure, pairing energies, and effective pairing gaps to identify shell closures. For candidate doubly magic nuclei, they computed potential energy surfaces using deformation-constrained relativistic mean field (RMF) theory and estimated formation cross sections using a phenomenological cold fusion reactions model.

## Key numbers

- Z=120, 132, and 138 are identified as common magic proton numbers across all eight effective interactions
- N=172, 184, 198, 228, 238, and 258 are identified as common magic neutron numbers across all eight effective interactions
- The RCHB equations were solved in a spherical box with radius R=25 fm and step size 0.1 fm
- The pairing force strength V0 was fixed at -650 MeV·fm^{-3} with nuclear matter density ρ0=0.152 fm^{-3}
- The smoothing range for Strutinsky shell correction calculations was γ=1.2, with the unit given as 41 A^{-1/3} (1 ± (1/3)(N-Z)/A) MeV
- Formation cross sections were estimated for the doubly magic candidates 292120 and 304120 at optimal excitation energy using a cold fusion model

## Context

This work extends previous microscopic calculations of superheavy nuclei, including relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov calculations with the Gogny interaction D1 and NLSH by Lalazissis (1996), Skyrme Hartree-Fock predictions by Cwiok (1996), and relativistic mean field studies by Rutz (1997) which gave doubly magic spherical nuclei at 114184, 120172, and 126184. The results differ from the finite-range droplet model (FRDM) predictions by Moller (1994) of shell closures at Z=114 and N=184, instead suggesting higher proton magic numbers. The work enables experimental searches for superheavy nuclei with Z=120 and N=172 or 184, and provides structure information for estimating formation cross sections in cold fusion reactions.
