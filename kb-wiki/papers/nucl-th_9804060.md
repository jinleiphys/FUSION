# Odd-Even Staggering of Nuclear Masses: Pairing or Shape Effect?

> The odd-even staggering of nuclear masses was recognized in the early days of nuclear physics. Recently, a similar effect was discovered in other finite fermion systems, such as ultrasmall metallic grains and metal clusters. It is believed that the staggering in nuclei and grains is primarily due to pairing correlations (superconductivity), while in clusters it is caused by the Jahn-Teller effect. We find that, for light and medium-mass nuclei, the staggering has two components. The first one originates from pairing while the second, comparable in magnitude, has its roots in the deformed mean field.

## Key claim

The odd-even staggering (OES) of nuclear masses in light and medium-mass nuclei has two comparable components: one from pairing correlations and one from the deformed mean field (Jahn-Teller effect), contradicting the recent suggestion that OES in light nuclei is merely a deformation effect.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock (HF) calculations without pairing for nuclei with Z=9-28 and N-Z=-2 to 6 using the HFODD code with two Skyrme parametrizations (SIII and SLy4). They analyzed the three-point difference Δ^(3)(N) and four-point difference Δ^(4)(N) of binding energies to separate pairing and mean-field contributions, and compared these to experimental binding energies.

## Key numbers

- Theoretical values of Δ^(4)_ν from HF without pairing reach 30% to 50% of the empirical OES
- The nuclear liquid-drop curvature contribution to the second difference is 38/A MeV (using symmetry energy coefficient a_I = 38 MeV)
- The smoothed-energy curvature contribution is approximately -36/A MeV, nearly canceling the liquid-drop contribution
- The effective mass factors are m*/m = 0.76 for SIII and 0.7 for SLy4
- For alkali-metal clusters, the liquid-drop contribution is approximately -0.15/N^(4/3) eV
- The level density for alkali-metal clusters gives 1/g(λ) ≈ 1.4/N eV (or ≈ 2.15/N eV from Fermi-gas estimate)
- Typical single-particle splitting due to cluster deformation is about 0.2 eV
- The average pairing gap trend is given by ̃Δ = 12/√A (fitted to Δ^(4), not Δ)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of OES from early nuclear physics (Heisenberg 1932) and the BCS theory (Bardeen, Cooper, Schrieffer 1957) to separate pairing and mean-field contributions. It directly contradicts the conclusion of Hakkinen et al. (1997) that OES in light nuclei is purely a deformation effect. The paper enables a method to extract true pairing gaps from experimental binding energies using the three-point filter at odd particle numbers, rather than the commonly used four-point difference which mixes pairing and mean-field effects.
