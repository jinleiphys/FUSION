# Impact of bulk viscosity on the post-merger gravitational-wave signal from merging neutron stars

> In the violent post-merger of binary neutron-star mergers strong oscillations are present that impact the emitted gravitational-wave (GW) signal. The frequencies, temperatures and densities involved in these oscillations allow for violations of the chemical equilibrium promoted by weak-interactions, thus leading to a nonzero bulk viscosity that can impact dynamics and GW signals. We present the first simulations of binary neutron-star mergers employing the self-consistent and second-order formulation of the equations of relativistic hydrodynamics for dissipative fluids proposed by Müller, Israel and Stewart. With the spirit of obtaining a first assessment of the impact of bulk viscosity on the structure and radiative efficiency of the merger remnant we adopt a simplified but realistic approach for the viscosity, which we assume to be determined by direct and modified Urca reactions and hence to vary within the stars. At the same time, to compensate for the lack of a precise knowledge about the strength of bulk viscosity, we explore the possible behaviours by considering three different scenarios of low, medium, and high bulk viscosity. In this way, we find that large values of the bulk viscosities damp the collision-and-bounce oscillations that characterize the dynamics of the stellar cores right after the merger. At the same time, large viscosities tend to preserve the $m=2$ deformations in the remnant, thus leading to a comparatively more efficient GW emission and to changes in the post-merger spectrum that can be up to $100\,{\rm Hz}$ in the case of the most extreme configurations. Overall, our self-consistent results indicate that bulk viscosity increases the energy radiated in GWs soon after the merger by $\lesssim 2\%$ in the (realistic) scenario of small viscosity, and by $\lesssim 30\%$ in the (unrealistic) scenario of large viscosity.

## Key claim

Bulk viscosity from Urca reactions can shift the dominant post-merger gravitational-wave frequency by up to 100 Hz and increase radiated GW energy by up to 30% in the unrealistic high-viscosity scenario, but only by less than 2% in the realistic low-viscosity scenario, indicating that bulk-viscous effects are modest under physically plausible conditions.

## Method

The authors performed the first general-relativistic simulations of binary neutron-star mergers using the second-order Müller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) formulation of dissipative hydrodynamics, solving the Einstein equations with the high-order code FIL. Bulk viscosity was modeled via direct and modified Urca reaction rates, with the resonant maximum bulk-viscosity coefficient systematically varied across four values spanning from negligible to large. The simulations used an equilibrated hybrid equation of state (TNTYST cold part plus ideal-fluid thermal part) for equal-mass irrotational binaries with total ADM mass ~2.55 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Bulk-viscosity models: resonant maximum coefficient at 2 nuclear saturation density and 1 kHz frequency, hat(zeta) in zeta_0 [10^{-4}, 0.4, 1.0, 2.4], where zeta_0 = 10^{30} g cm^{-1} s^{-1}
- Corresponding resonant temperatures: hat(T) in [3.6, 2.2, 1.3, 1.2] MeV
- Total ADM mass of binaries: ~2.55 solar masses at separation ~30 solar masses (~44 km)
- Reference grid resolution: Delta x ~ 0.17 solar masses (~260 m) on the sixth refinement level
- Bulk viscosity increases radiated GW energy by less than 2% in the realistic low-viscosity scenario (hat(zeta) = 0.4 zeta_0) and by up to ~30% in the unrealistic high-viscosity scenario (hat(zeta) = 2.4 zeta_0)
- Largest peak of the post-merger power spectral density (f_2) shifts by up to ~100 Hz (~4%) for the most extreme viscosity configuration
- Signal-to-noise ratio of post-merger GW signals increases by at most ~10% due to viscosity
- HMNS remnant dimensionless spin can be up to ~8% smaller with large bulk viscosity

## Context

This work extends prior studies of bulk viscosity in neutron-star mergers by Alford (2018, 2020, 2021, 2022), Most (2022), and Hammond (2023) by providing the first self-consistent simulations using the causal second-order MIS framework rather than simplified treatments. The results contradict the suggestion from Radice (2022) that out-of-thermodynamic-equilibrium effects are negligible, while confirming that bulk-viscous effects are modest for realistic viscosities. The observed frequency shifts are comparable to those induced by magnetic fields (Giacomazzo 2011) and must be accounted for in quasi-universal relations for the post-merger spectrum (Bauswein 2012, Takami 2014, Bernuzzi 2015, Rezzolla 2017) to avoid degeneracies when constraining the equation of state.
