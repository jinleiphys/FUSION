# New Power to Measure Supernova $ν_e$ with Large Liquid Scintillator Detectors

> We examine the prospects for detecting supernova $ν_e$ in JUNO, RENO-50, LENA, or other approved or proposed large liquid scintillator detectors. The main detection channels for supernova $ν_e$ in a liquid scintillator are its elastic scattering with electrons and its charged-current interaction with the $^{12}$C nucleus. In existing scintillator detectors, the numbers of events from these interactions are too small to be very useful. However, at the 20-kton scale planned for the new detectors, these channels become powerful tools for probing the $ν_e$ emission. We find that the $ν_e$ spectrum can be well measured, to better than $\sim 40\%$ precision for the total energy and better than $\sim 25\%$ precision for the average energy. This is adequate to distinguish even close average energies, e.g., 11 MeV and 14 MeV, which will test the predictions of supernova models. In addition, it will help set constraints on neutrino mixing effects in supernovae by testing non-thermal spectra. Without such large liquid scintillator detectors (or Super-Kamiokande with added gadolinium, which has similar capabilities), supernova $ν_e$ will be measured poorly, holding back progress on understanding supernovae, neutrinos, and possible new physics.

## Key claim

The authors claim that future 20-kton-scale liquid scintillator detectors (JUNO, RENO-50, LENA) can measure the supernova νe spectrum with better than ∼40% precision for total energy and better than ∼25% precision for average energy, adequate to distinguish average energies as close as 11 MeV and 14 MeV, but these precisions depend on the specific detection channels used and the assumed neutron detection efficiency.

## Method

The authors simulated supernova neutrino detection in a 20 kton liquid scintillator detector using a modified Maxwell-Boltzmann spectrum for each neutrino flavor, assuming a total emitted energy of 3×10^53 erg equally divided among six flavors at a distance of 10 kpc. They calculated event rates for νe elastic scattering on electrons and νe charged-current interactions on 12C, along with backgrounds from other channels, and performed χ2 analyses to determine the precision with which the νe average energy and total energy could be constrained.

## Key numbers

- For a 20 kton detector at 10 kpc, expected νe + e− elastic scattering events: 159 (for ⟨Eνe⟩ = 12 MeV), 160 (15 MeV), 160 (18 MeV)
- For νe + 12C → e− + 12Ng.s. charged-current events: 44 (⟨Eνe⟩ = 12 MeV), 114 (15 MeV), 214 (18 MeV)
- For νe + 12C charged-current events with ⟨Eνe⟩ = 18 MeV (oscillated case): 214 events
- The νe spectrum can be measured to better than ∼40% precision for total energy and better than ∼25% precision for average energy
- The 12Ng.s. decay half-life is 11 msec; the 12Bg.s. decay half-life is 20.2 msec
- Neutron capture on protons yields a 2.2 MeV gamma ray with mean capture lifetime ∼207 μsec in KamLAND
- The energy resolution of JUNO and RENO-50 is expected to be ∼3%/√(E/MeV)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis for Super-Kamiokande with gadolinium (Laha, Beacom 2013) to large liquid scintillator detectors, showing that JUNO, RENO-50, and LENA can achieve similar or better sensitivity to νe than water Cherenkov detectors. The paper enables future tests of supernova models by distinguishing between thermal and non-thermal νe spectra, which would reveal neutrino mixing effects in supernovae via the MSW mechanism and collective oscillations. The results complement existing capabilities for measuring ν̄e via inverse beta decay (Vogel 1999, Strumia 2003) and νx via neutrino-proton elastic scattering (Beacom 2002, Dasgupta 2011).
