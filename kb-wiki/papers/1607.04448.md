# High Gluon Densities in Heavy Ions Collisions

> The early stages of heavy ion collisions are dominated by high density systems of gluons that carry each a small fraction $x$ of the momenta of the colliding nucleons. A distinguishing feature of such systems is the phenomenon of "saturation" which tames the expected growth of the gluon density as the energy of the collision increases. The onset of saturation occurs at a particular transverse momentum scale, the "saturation momentum", that emerges dynamically and that marks the onset of non-linear gluon interactions. At high energy, and for large nuclei, the saturation momentum is large compared to the typical hadronic scale, making high density gluons amenable to a description with weak coupling techniques. This paper reviews some of the challenges faced in the study of such dense systems of small $x$ gluons, and of the progress made in addressing them. The focus is on conceptual issues, and the presentation is both pedagogical, and critical. Examples where high gluon density could play a visible role in heavy ion collisions are briefly discussed at the end, for illustration purpose.

## Key claim

This review argues that while saturation of gluon densities at small momentum fraction $x$ is theoretically inevitable and characterized by a dynamically generated saturation momentum $Q_s$, direct and compelling experimental evidence for this phenomenon remains a challenge, and the microscopic mechanisms leading to saturation are not yet fully understood.

## Method

The paper provides a pedagogical and critical review of the conceptual issues in high-density small-$x$ gluon systems, covering the parton model, linear evolution equations (DGLAP and BFKL), the eikonal approximation and color dipole formalism, the McLerran-Venugopalan model for classical color fields, and nonlinear evolution equations for gluon densities. It discusses the Color Glass Condensate effective theory and examines selected examples of particle production in heavy ion collisions where high gluon density effects may be visible.

## Key numbers

- Saturation momentum squared for large nuclei: $Q_s^2 \simeq 2\ {\rm GeV}^2$ at RHIC and $Q_s^2 \simeq 5\ {\rm GeV}^2$ at the LHC
- Typical momentum fraction $x$ of partons involved in particle production: $x \sim 10^{-2} - 10^{-3}$ at RHIC and $x \sim 10^{-4} - 10^{-6}$ at the LHC
- Lorentz-contracted thickness of nuclei in the center-of-mass frame: approximately $10^{-3}$ fm at the LHC, compared to a Pb nucleus diameter of about 12 fm
- Leading-order BFKL growth exponent: $\omega = 4 \ln 2$ (in leading order)

## Context

This review extends the line of work on high-density QCD and saturation physics that began with the Gribov-Levin-Ryskin (1984) proposal of parton recombination and the McLerran-Venugopalan model (1993, 1994) for classical color fields in large nuclei. It provides a critical perspective on developments including the Color Glass Condensate effective theory and nonlinear evolution equations, complementing existing reviews by Iancu (2002, 2003), Jalilian-Marian (2005), Weigert (2005), Gelis (2010, 2012), Lappi (2010), Albacete (2013, 2014), and the book by Kovchegov and Levin (2012). The paper aims to clarify conceptual issues for non-experts and distinguish what is solidly established from heuristic assumptions.
