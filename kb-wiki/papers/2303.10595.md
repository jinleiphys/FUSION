# Systematic analysis of $t$ and $^3$He breakup reactions

> Systematic measurement of $t$ and $^3$He knockout processes is planned. The weakly-bound nature of these nuclei may affect the interpretation of forthcoming knockout reaction data. Purpose: We aim at clarifying breakup properties of $t$ and $^3$He by investigating their elastic and breakup cross sections. We employ the four-body continuum-discretized coupled-channels method with the eikonal approximation to describe the $t$ and $^3$He reactions. The breakup cross section of $t$ is found to be almost the same as that of $^3$He and is about one-third of that of $d$. Coulomb breakup plays negligible role in the breakup of $t$ and $^3$He, in contrast to in the deuteron breakup reaction. It is found that $t$ and $^3$He tend to breakup into three nucleons rather than $d$ and a nucleon. It is shown that the breakup cross sections of $t$ and $^3$He are not as large as those of d but non-negligible. Because about 80% of them corresponds to the three-nucleon breakup process, a four-body breakup reaction model is necessary to quantitatively describe the breakup of $t$ and $^3$He.

## Key claim

The breakup cross sections of triton ($t$) and helium-3 ($^3$He) are nearly identical and about one-third that of deuteron ($d$), but contrary to what the abstract's "non-negligible" might suggest, the authors find that Coulomb breakup plays a negligible role for both $t$ and $^3$He, unlike in deuteron breakup, and that about 80% of the breakup proceeds through the three-nucleon channel rather than the two-body $d$+nucleon channel.

## Method

The authors employed the four-body continuum-discretized coupled-channels method with the eikonal approximation (E-CDCC) to describe $t$ and $^3$He as three-nucleon systems interacting with a target nucleus. They used the Gaussian expansion method (GEM) with the Minnesota nucleon-nucleon interaction plus a phenomenological three-body force to obtain ground and discretized continuum states of the projectiles, and constructed optical potentials by folding the Melbourne $g$-matrix with target densities. The P-separation method was applied to decompose breakup cross sections into $d$+nucleon and three-nucleon contributions.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of $t$: calculated $-8.45$ MeV, experimental $-8.48$ MeV (from Purcell 2010)
- Ground-state energy of $^3$He: calculated $-7.77$ MeV, experimental $-7.71$ MeV (from Purcell 2010)
- Root-mean-square radius of $t$: calculated 1.68 fm, experimental 1.84 fm
- Root-mean-square radius of $^3$He: calculated 1.70 fm, experimental 1.99 fm
- Total breakup cross section of $^3$He with $^{40}$Ca target: 62 mb at 40 MeV/nucleon, 48 mb at 70 MeV/nucleon, 20 mb at 150 MeV/nucleon
- Total breakup cross section of $d$ with $^{40}$Ca target: 148 mb at 40 MeV/nucleon, 114 mb at 70 MeV/nucleon, 47 mb at 150 MeV/nucleon
- Total breakup cross section of $^3$He with $^{90}$Zr target: 71 mb at 40 MeV/nucleon, 64 mb at 70 MeV/nucleon, 33 mb at 150 MeV/nucleon
- Total breakup cross section of $d$ with $^{90}$Zr target: 228 mb at 40 MeV/nucleon, 205 mb at 70 MeV/nucleon, 108 mb at 150 MeV/nucleon
- Probability of $d$ existence in the $^3$He ground state: $P_0 \approx 0.9$, consistent with 90% from Brida (2011) quantum Monte Carlo calculation
- Three-body force parameters: $V_3 = 9.7$ MeV, $\nu = 0.1$ fm$^{-2}$

## Context

This work extends the four-body CDCC method developed by Matsumoto (2004, 2006) and the eikonal CDCC approach by Ogata (2003, 2009, 2010) to systematically analyze $t$ and $^3$He breakup for the first time. It contrasts with the earlier three-body treatment of $^3$He as a $d+p$ system by Iseri (1986), showing that the four-body model gives total breakup cross sections twice as large as the three-body model due to the dominant three-nucleon breakup channel. The results enable proper interpretation of planned knockout reaction measurements for $t$ and $^3$He cluster states in nuclei, which are relevant for determining the symmetry energy term in the nuclear equation of state as discussed by Gaidarov (2021), Chen (2004), and Ono (2014).
