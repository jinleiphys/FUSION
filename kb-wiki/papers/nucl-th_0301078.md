# Bound Nucleon Form Factors, Quark-Hadron Duality, and Nuclear EMC Effect

> We discuss the electromagnetic form factors, axial form factors, and structure functions of a bound nucleon in the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model. Free space nucleon form factors are calculated using the improved cloudy bag model (ICBM). After describing finite nuclei and nuclear matter in the quark-based QMC model, we compute the in-medium modification of the bound nucleon form factors in the same framework. Finally, limits on the medium modification of the bound nucleon $F_2$ structure function are obtained using the calculated in-medium electromagnetic form factors and local quark-hadron duality.

## Key claim

The observed ~10% deviation in the polarization transfer ratio for protons bound in $^4$He compared to free protons, which cannot be explained by conventional nuclear physics models (including the best optical potentials, bound state wave functions, relativistic corrections, meson exchange currents, isobar contributions, and final state interactions), can only be accounted for by supplementing these descriptions with medium modifications of the nucleon's internal structure calculated within the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, although the error bars may still be too large to draw a definite conclusion.

## Method

The authors treat finite nuclei and nuclear matter in the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model using a Hartree, mean-field approximation with static, spherically symmetric nuclei, where quasi-particles moving in single-particle orbits are three-quark clusters with nucleon quantum numbers. Free space nucleon electromagnetic form factors are calculated using the improved cloudy bag model (ICBM), which includes a Peierls-Thouless projection for center-of-mass and recoil corrections and Lorentz contraction of internal quark wave functions. In-medium electromagnetic and axial form factors of bound nucleons are then computed within the same QMC framework, and local quark-hadron duality is used to relate the observed form factor modification to the expected modification of the deep-inelastic $F_2$ structure function at large Bjorken-$x$.

## Key numbers

- The polarization ratio in $^4$He was found to differ by approximately 10% from that in $^1$H at $Q^2 = 0.5, 1.0, 1.6$ and $2.6$ GeV$^2$ (Jefferson Lab) and at $Q^2 \approx 0.4$ GeV$^2$ (MAMI).
- The electric to magnetic form factor ratio for a bound proton is approximately 5% smaller than in free space at half nuclear matter density ($\rho = \frac{1}{2}\rho_0$) and approximately 10% smaller at normal nuclear matter density ($\rho = \rho_0 = 0.15$ fm$^{-3}$) at $Q^2 = 0$.
- The bound/free ratio of electric to magnetic form factors deviates by approximately 20% from unity at $Q^2 \sim 2$ GeV$^2$ for nuclear matter densities.
- The space component of the axial form factor at $Q^2 = 0$, $g_A^*$, is quenched by about 10% at normal nuclear matter density ($\rho_0 = 0.15$ fm$^{-3}$).
- The QMC model uses a free space nucleon bag radius $R_N = 0.8$ fm, current quark masses $m_{u,d} = 5$ MeV, and hadronic level parameters: $m_\sigma = 418$ MeV, $m_\omega = 783$ MeV, $m_\rho = 770$ MeV, $g^2_\sigma/4\pi = 3.12$, $g^2_\omega/4\pi = 5.31$, $g^2_\rho/4\pi = 6.93$.

## Context

This work extends the quark-meson coupling model treatment of finite nuclei and nuclear matter developed by Guichon (1995), Guichon et al. (1996), and Saito et al. (1997) to compute in-medium modifications of nucleon electromagnetic and axial form factors, motivated by polarization transfer experiments on $^4$He (Strauch et al., 2003) and $^{16}$O (O16 experiment) that conventional nuclear physics models (Laget, 2002; Kelly, 2002; Udias et al., 2002; Forest, 2002) could not explain. The use of local quark-hadron duality, originally observed by Bloom and Gilman (1970) and dramatically confirmed in high-precision Jefferson Lab measurements, provides a model-independent relation between the in-medium form factors and the $F_2$ structure function at large $x$, enabling constraints on models that attribute the nuclear EMC effect to deformation of intrinsic nucleon structure (Frankfurt and Strikman, 1988).
