# Variational Methods for Path Integral Scattering

> In this master thesis, a new approximation scheme to non-relativistic potential scattering is developed and discussed. The starting points are two exact path integral representations of the T-matrix, which permit the application of the Feynman-Jensen variational method. A simple Ansatz for the trial action is made, and, in both cases, the variational procedure singles out a particular one-particle classical equation of motion, given in integral form. While the first is real, in the second representation this trajectory is complex and evolves according to an effective, time dependent potential. Using a cumulant expansion, the first correction to the variational approximation is also evaluated. The high energy behavior of the approximation is investigated, and is shown to contain exactly the leading and next-to-leading order of the eikonal expansion, and parts of higher terms. Our results are then numerically tested in two particular situations where others approximations turned out to be unsatisfactory. Substantial improvements are found.

# Variational Methods for Path Integral Scattering

## Key claim

The Feynman-Jensen variational principle applied to two exact path integral representations of the T-matrix yields a variational approximation that singles out a classical equation of motion for a one-particle trajectory; the approximation contains exactly the leading and next-to-leading order of the eikonal expansion and parts of higher terms, and provides substantial improvements over other approximations in cases where those proved unsatisfactory.

## Method

The author starts from two exact path integral representations of the T-matrix (the eikonal or "3-3" representation and the ray or "3-1" representation) and applies the Feynman-Jensen variational method with a simple quadratic trial action. The variational procedure yields integral equations for the trial functions that reduce to a classical Newtonian equation of motion for the reference trajectory in the eikonal case, and to a complex trajectory evolving under an effective time-dependent potential in the ray case. A cumulant expansion is used to evaluate the first correction (second cumulant) to the variational approximation, and the high-energy behavior is analyzed analytically. Numerical tests are performed for two specific scattering situations where other approximations had failed.

## Key numbers

- For the Gaussian potential \( V(\vecx) = V_0 \exp(-\alpha \vecx^2) \), the second cumulant in the eikonal representation is given by an expression involving \( \gamma_\parallel(t_1,t_2) = \frac{\alpha}{m} |t_1 - t_2| \), with the prefactor \( (1 + \gamma_\parallel^2)^{-3/2} \).
- For the ray representation of the same Gaussian potential, the second cumulant involves \( \gamma_{1,2} = \frac{\alpha}{m} |t_{1,2}| \) and \( \gamma_3 = \gamma_\parallel - \gamma_1 - \gamma_2 \), with a prefactor \( [(1+2i\gamma_1)(1+2i\gamma_2) + \gamma_3^2]^{-1} \).
- Numerical evaluation of the second cumulant for the ray representation at \( K = 2\ \mathrm{fm}^{-1} \) showed that adaptive integration (DCUHRE) gained two orders of magnitude in precision over Gauss-Legendre integration, but still leveled off at large impact parameter \( b \).
- The first-order phase \( \chi_1(b) \) for spherically symmetric potentials reduces to \( \chi_1(b) = -\frac{1}{v^2 K} (1 + b \frac{d}{db}) \int_0^\infty dZ\ V^2(r) \).
- The second-order phase \( \tau_2(b) \) for spherically symmetric potentials contains terms proportional to \( \frac{1}{v^3 K^2} (1 + \frac{5}{3} b \frac{d}{db} + \frac{1}{3} b^2 \frac{d^2}{db^2}) \int_0^\infty dZ\ V^3(r) \) plus additional terms involving \( \chi_0 \) and its derivatives.

## Context

This master thesis extends the path integral approach to non-relativistic potential scattering developed by Abarbanel and Itzykson (1971), who derived the eikonal representation of the T-matrix. The variational method based on the Feynman-Jensen principle had been applied to bound state problems but not systematically to scattering. The work enables improved calculations of differential cross sections in regimes where the standard eikonal approximation and its corrections fail, particularly for potentials where other approximations proved unsatisfactory. The numerical results for Gaussian and other potentials demonstrate that the variational trajectory captures classical deflection effects that straight-line eikonal trajectories miss.
