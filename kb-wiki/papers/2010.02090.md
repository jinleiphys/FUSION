# GW190814: On the properties of the secondary component of the binary

> We show that the odds of the mass-gap (secondary) object in GW190814 being a neutron star (NS) improve if one allows for a stiff high-density equation of state (EoS) or a large spin. Since its mass is $\in (2.50,2.67) M_{\odot}$, establishing its true nature will make it either the heaviest NS or the lightest black hole (BH), and can have far-reaching implications on NS EoS and compact object formation channels. When limiting oneself to the NS hypothesis, we deduce the secondary's properties by using a Bayesian framework with a hybrid EoS formulation that employs a parabolic expansion-based nuclear empirical parameterization around the nuclear saturation density augmented by a generic 3-segment piecewise polytrope (PP) model at higher densities and combining a variety of astrophysical observations. For the slow-rotation scenario, GW190814 implies a very stiff EoS and a stringent constraint on the EoS specially in the high-density region. On the other hand assuming the secondary object is a rapidly rotating NS, we constrain its rotational frequency to be $f=1170^{+389}_{-495}$ Hz, within a $90\%$ confidence interval. In this scenario, the secondary object in GW190814 would qualify as the fastest rotating NS ever observed. However, for this scenario to be viable, rotational instabilities would have to be suppressed both during formation and the subsequent evolution until merger, otherwise the secondary of GW190814 is more likely to be a BH.

# GW190814: On the properties of the secondary component of the binary

## Key claim

The odds that the mass-gap secondary object in GW190814 (mass 2.50–2.67 solar masses) is a neutron star improve if one allows for a stiff high-density equation of state or a large spin, but the probability remains small (approximately 1% for a nonrotating neutron star and approximately 8% for a neutron star rotating at 716 Hz). For the rapidly rotating scenario to be viable, rotational instabilities would have to be suppressed both during formation and subsequent evolution until merger, otherwise the secondary is more likely to be a black hole.

## Method

The authors used a Bayesian framework with a hybrid equation of state formulation that combines a parabolic expansion-based nuclear empirical parameterization around nuclear saturation density with a generic 3-segment piecewise polytrope model at higher densities. They combined astrophysical observations including PSR J0740+6620, GW170817, GW190425, and NICER data, and applied the GW190814 secondary mass distribution as either a maximum-mass constraint (for nonrotating stars) or a maximum-mass threshold for uniformly rotating stars using a universal relation from Breu (2016). For the rapidly rotating scenario, they used the RNS code to compute stellar properties and constrained the spin via the universal relation.

## Key numbers

- Secondary mass of GW190814: m2 = 2.59 (+0.08, -0.09) solar masses, within a 90% confidence interval
- Probability that the secondary is a nonrotating neutron star: P(m2 > M_max) = 0.99, implying approximately 1% probability of being a neutron star
- Probability that the secondary is a rapidly rotating neutron star at 716 Hz: approximately 8%
- For the slow-rotation scenario: R_1.4 = 13.3 (+0.5, -0.6) km and Lambda_1.4 = 795 (+151, -194) at 90% confidence
- For the rapidly rotating scenario: rotational frequency f = 1170 (+389, -495) Hz at 90% confidence
- For the rapidly rotating scenario: dimensionless spin chi = 0.57 (+0.09, -0.26) at 90% confidence
- For the rapidly rotating scenario: equatorial radius R_e = 14.1 (+1.5, -2.0) km at 90% confidence
- For the rapidly rotating scenario: ellipticity e = 0.60 (+0.07, -0.23) at 90% confidence
- If the secondary is a black hole (assuming non-overlapping mass distributions): M_max = 2.22 (+0.19, -0.21) solar masses at 90% confidence
- Probability that the primary of GW190425 is a black hole under the BH hypothesis: approximately 40%

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Bayesian equation of state analysis (Biswas 2020) by applying it to the GW190814 secondary object. It builds on the LIGO-Virgo discovery paper (Abbott 2020) which reported approximately 3% probability for a neutron star using spectral EoS parameterization, and on Essick (2020) who found less than approximately 6% probability using nonparametric EoS. The study also connects to Most (2020) and Tsokaros (2020) who suggested rapid uniform rotation as a possibility, and to Breu (2016) whose universal relation for rotating maximum masses is employed. The results enable future work on constraining superfluid mutual friction in neutron star interiors and on the formation channels of compact objects in the mass gap.
