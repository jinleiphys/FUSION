# Dynamics of Hot Bulk QCD Matter: from the Quark-Gluon Plasma to Hadronic Freeze-Out

> We introduce a combined macroscopic/microscopic transport approach employing relativistic hydrodynamics for the early, dense, deconfined stage of the reaction and a microscopic non-equilibrium model for the later hadronic stage where the equilibrium assumptions are not valid anymore. Within this approach we study the dynamics of hot, bulk QCD matter, which is expected to be created in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions at the SPS, the RHIC and the LHC. Our approach is capable of self-consistently calculating the freeze-out of the hadronic system, while accounting for the collective flow on the hadronization hypersurface generated by the QGP expansion. In particular, we perform a detailed analysis of the reaction dynamics, hadronic freeze-out, and transverse flow.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a hybrid approach that combines relativistic ideal hydrodynamics for the early, dense quark-gluon plasma stage with a microscopic non-equilibrium transport model (UrQMD) for the later hadronic stage, claiming that this method can self-consistently calculate hadronic freeze-out without imposing a decoupling hypersurface by hand, provided the switch from fluid dynamics to transport occurs on or near the hadronization hypersurface.

## Method

The authors model the early stage of ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions using (2+1)-dimensional scaling hydrodynamics with cylindrical symmetry and longitudinal boost-invariance, assuming a first-order QCD phase transition described by an MIT bag model equation of state (bag constant B = 380 MeV/fm³) for the quark-gluon plasma and an ideal hadron gas for the low-temperature phase. On the hadronization hypersurface, they convert the fluid-dynamical solution into particle distributions using the Cooper-Frye formula and feed these into the UrQMD microscopic transport model, which then propagates individual hadrons with binary collision approximations through the hadronic stage until freeze-out. The initial conditions are specified on a proper-time hyperbola τᵢ = 1 fm/c for SPS and τᵢ = 0.6 fm for RHIC, with transverse density profiles following a wounded-nucleon distribution.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature T_C ≈ 160 MeV at zero net baryon density (from bag constant B = 380 MeV/fm³)
- For SPS Pb+Pb at √s = 17A GeV: initial energy density ε̄ᵢ = 6.1 GeV/fm³, net baryon density ρ̄ᵢ = 4.5 ρ₀, initial temperature Tᵢ ≈ 220 MeV, quark-chemical potential μq ≈ 150 MeV, specific entropy s̄/ρ̄_B = 45 ± 5, net baryon rapidity density dN_B/dy = 80, transverse radius R_T = 6 fm
- For RHIC Au+Au at √s = 200A GeV: initial energy density ε̄ᵢ = 20 GeV/fm³, net baryon density ρ̄ᵢ = 2.3 ρ₀, initial temperature Tᵢ ≈ 300 MeV, quark-chemical potential μq ≈ 45 MeV, specific entropy s̄/ρ̄_B = 205, net baryon rapidity density dN_B/dy = 25, transverse energy on τᵢ hyperbola dE_T/dy ≈ 1.3 TeV, decreasing to dE_T/dy ≈ 720 GeV on hadronization hypersurface
- For LHC: assumed initial time τ₀ ~ 1/p₀ with p₀ ~ 2 GeV

## Context

This work extends prior hybrid approaches that combined hydrodynamics with microscopic transport, addressing limitations of purely fluid-dynamical models (such as those by B. Schlei, J. Sollfrank, A. Dumitru, and collaborators) which imposed freeze-out by hand on guessed temperature hypersurfaces. The approach enables self-consistent calculation of freeze-out conditions and can explain why chemical freeze-out temperatures differ between heavy-ion collisions (140-160 MeV at SPS) and the early universe (~50 MeV), as noted by A. Dumitru (1999). The paper specifically addresses the problem of non-universal freeze-out for different hadron species, using the example of Ω baryons measured by the WA97 collaboration at SPS, which show less collective flow than pions and nucleons due to their small transport cross-sections.
