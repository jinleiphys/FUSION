# Phase-shift calculation using continuum-discretized states

> We present a method for calculating scattering phase shifts which utilizes continuum-discretized states obtained in a bound-state type calculation. The wrong asymptotic behavior of the discretized state is remedied by means of the Green's function formalism. Test examples confirm the accuracy of the method. The $α+n$ scattering is described using realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials. The $3/2^-$ and $1/2^-$ phase shifts obtained in a single-channel calculation are too small in comparison with experiment. The $1/2^+$ phase shifts are in reasonable agreement with experiment, and gain contributions both from the tensor and central components of the nucleon-nucleon potential.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for calculating scattering phase shifts using continuum-discretized states obtained from bound-state-type calculations, where the incorrect asymptotic behavior of the discretized state is corrected via a Green's function formalism. The method's accuracy depends on a suitable choice of the local potential \(U(r)\) and on how precisely the constant \(\lambda\) is determined, with the authors noting that the phase-shift calculation requires no channel radius unlike the \(R\)-matrix theory.

## Method

The authors compute the spectroscopic amplitude (SA) \(y(r)\) from a bound-state-type wave function expanded in correlated Gaussian basis functions, then solve an inhomogeneous differential equation for \(y(r)\) using a Green's function constructed from regular and irregular solutions of a homogeneous equation with a chosen local potential \(U(r)\). The phase shift \(\delta_\ell\) is extracted from the asymptotic form of the SA via the formula \(\tan \delta_\ell = \tan \delta_\ell^{(0)} - (2\mu k / \hbar^2 \lambda) p(\infty)\), where \(p(\infty)\) involves integrals of the residual interaction \(V_c - U\) and the constant \(\lambda\) is determined by least-squares fitting of the SA to the Green's-function-corrected form over an interval typically \([1,5]\) fm. The method is tested on \(n+p\) scattering with the Minnesota potential and on \(\alpha+n\) scattering with both the Minnesota potential and realistic AV8′ and G3RS nucleon-nucleon potentials.

## Key numbers

- For \(^3S_1\) \(n+p\) scattering with the Minnesota potential, the SAGF method reproduces the Numerov phase shift of 147.7° at \(E = 0.4986\) MeV to within 0.1° across all tested energies up to 77.31 MeV.
- The \(\alpha\) particle binding energy used in the effective-potential calculation is 29.90 MeV, with a root-mean-square matter radius of 1.41 fm.
- For \(\alpha+n\) scattering with realistic potentials, the \(\alpha\) particle binding energy is 25.09 MeV for AV8′ and 25.29 MeV for G3RS.
- The \(3/2^-\) phase shift shows a sharp resonance around \(E = 0.9\) MeV experimentally, but the single-channel calculation with realistic potentials produces phase shifts "by far smaller than experiment" for both \(3/2^-\) and \(1/2^-\) states.
- The threshold energy difference between \(\alpha+n\) and \(^3\)H+\(d\) is 17.6 MeV experimentally, but the theoretical value is about 15 MeV for both AV8′ and G3RS potentials.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Ivanov (2002), who used stochastic variational methods with stabilization ideas to calculate low-energy phase shifts for positronium-atom scattering, by instead employing a Green's function formalism to correct the asymptotic behavior of the spectroscopic amplitude. The method enables phase-shift calculations using bound-state techniques and is applicable to coupled-channel problems, in contrast to the resonating group method (RGM) which requires solving integro-differential equations for the relative motion function. The results for \(\alpha+n\) scattering with realistic potentials show that single-channel calculations fail to reproduce the \(P\)-wave phase shifts, pointing to the need for including distorted configurations such as \(^3\)H+\(d\) and \(^3\)He+\(2n\) channels, as well as three-body forces as in the Quantum Monte Carlo calculation of Nollett (2007) using the Illinois-2 three-body force.
