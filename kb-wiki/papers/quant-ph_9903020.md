# Quon Statistics for Composite Systems and a Limit on the Violation of the Pauli Principle for Nucleons and Quarks

> The quon algebra gives a description of particles, ``quons,'' that are neither fermions nor bosons. The parameter $q$ attached to a quon labels a smooth interpolation between bosons, for which $q = +1$, and fermions, for which $q = -1$. Wigner and Ehrenfest and Oppenheimer showed that a composite system of identical bosons and fermions is a fermion if it contains an odd number of fermions and is a boson otherwise. Here we generalize this result to composite systems of identical quons. We find $q_{composite}=q_{constituent}^{n^2}$ for a system of $n$ identical quons. This result reduces to the earlier result for bosons and fermions. Using this generalization we find bounds on possible violations of the Pauli exclusion principle for nucleons and quarks based on such bounds for nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes the Wigner–Ehrenfest–Oppenheimer rule for composite statistics to quons (particles obeying a smooth interpolation between Bose and Fermi statistics), finding that a composite of \(n\) identical quons with parameter \(q\) behaves as a quon with parameter \(q^{n^2}\), provided all composites are in the same internal state and interactions between composites are negligible compared to internal energy differences.

## Method

The authors construct bound-state creation operators as linear combinations of products of constituent quon creation operators, with coefficients chosen to project onto a single irreducible representation of the symmetric group. They then compute the scalar product of two-composite states using a generalized Wick's theorem where each crossing of contraction lines contributes a factor of \(q\), and show that interchanging two composites yields a relative factor of \(q^{n^2}\) from the \(n^2\) crossings between the two groups of \(n\) constituent lines. This result is then applied to nuclei and nucleons to translate experimental bounds on violations of the Pauli principle for composite nuclei into bounds on violations for their constituent nucleons and quarks.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{16}\text{O}\) nuclei, the probability of finding the two nuclei in a permutation antisymmetric state is less than \(5 \times 10^{-9}\) (Modugno, Ingusicio, and Tino, 1998).
- From the \(^{16}\text{O}\) bound, the limit on violation of Fermi statistics for nucleons is \(1 + q_{\text{nucleon}} \leq 2 \times 10^{-11}\).
- Extending to quarks within the model of nucleons as bound states of 3 quarks gives \(1 + q_{\text{quark}} \leq 2 \times 10^{-12}\).
- For comparison, the Ramberg–Snow experiment gives a limit for electrons of \(1 + q < 10^{-26}\).

## Context

This work extends the classic result of Wigner (1929) and Ehrenfest and Oppenheimer (1931) on the statistics of composite bosons and fermions to the quon formalism introduced by Greenberg (1991), which allows smooth interpolation between Bose and Fermi statistics via a parameter \(q\). The composite statistics rule enables experimental bounds on violations of the Pauli principle for nuclei, such as the \(^{16}\text{O}\) limit from Modugno, Ingusicio, and Tino (1998), to be converted into the first direct precision limits on violations for nucleons and quarks. The authors note that other limits on nucleon statistics, such as those from the solar p-p cycle (Plaga, 1995) or supernova core collapse models (Baron, 1995), rely on model-dependent arguments, whereas the present approach is directly linked to experimental measurement.
