# Beyond $ρ^{2/3}$ Scaling: Microscopic Origins and Multimessengers of High-Density Nuclear Symmetry Energy

> Nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(ρ)$ encoding the cost to make nuclear matter more neutron rich has been the most uncertain component of the EOS of dense neutron-rich nucleonic matter. It affects significantly the radii, tidal deformations, cooling rates and frequencies of various oscillation modes of isolated neutron stars as well as the strain amplitude and frequencies of gravitational waves from their mergers, besides its many effects on structures of nuclei as well as the dynamics and observables of their collisions. Siemens (1970s) observed that $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(ρ)$ scales as $(ρ/ρ_0)^{2/3}$ near the saturation density $ρ_0$ of nuclear matter, since both the kinetic part and the potential contribution (quadratic in momentum) exhibit this dependence. The scaling holds if: (1) the nucleon isoscalar potential is quadratic in momentum, and (2) the isovector interaction is weakly density dependent. After examining many empirical evidences and understanding theoretical findings in the literature we conclude that: (1) Siemens' $ρ^{2/3}$ scaling is robust and serves as a valuable benchmark for both nuclear theories and experiments up to $2ρ_0$ but breaks down at higher densities, (2) Experimental and theoretical findings about $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(ρ)$ up to $2ρ_0$ are broadly consistent, but uncertainties remain large for its curvature $K_{\mathrm{sym}}$ and higher-order parameters, (3) Above $2ρ_0$, uncertainties grow due to poorly constrained spin-isospin dependent tensor and three-body forces as well as the resulting nucleon short-range correlations. Looking forward, combining multimessengers from both observations of neutron stars and terrestrial heavy-ion reaction experiments is the most promising path to finally constraining precisely the high-density $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(ρ)$ and the EOS of supradense neutron-rich matter.

## Key claim

Siemens' $\rho^{2/3}$ scaling of the nuclear symmetry energy is robust and serves as a valuable benchmark up to twice the saturation density ($2\rho_0$) but breaks down at higher densities, where uncertainties grow due to poorly constrained spin-isospin dependent tensor and three-body forces as well as nucleon short-range correlations.

## Method

The paper reviews the microscopic origins of the nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho)$ by applying the Hugenholtz-Van Hove (HVH) theorem to asymmetric nucleonic matter, decomposing the symmetry energy into contributions from the kinetic energy, the isoscalar single-particle potential, and the isovector Lane potential. It then examines empirical evidence for Siemens' $\rho^{2/3}$ scaling by comparing the predicted relationships among the symmetry energy magnitude $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0)$, slope $L$, curvature $K_{\mathrm{sym}}$, and skewness $J_{\mathrm{sym}}$ at saturation density against results from multiple independent analyses of terrestrial nuclear experiments and neutron star observations.

## Key numbers

- $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31.6 \pm 2.7$ MeV and $L = 58.9 \pm 16$ MeV from a 2013 survey of 29 independent analyses
- $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31.7 \pm 3.2$ MeV and $L = 58.7 \pm 28.1$ MeV from a 2016 survey of 53 analyses
- Chiral effective field theory (2020) predicts $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = 31.7 \pm 1.1$ MeV and $L = 59.8 \pm 4.1$ MeV
- From 12 independent analyses of neutron star data after GW170817, the average curvature is $K_{\mathrm{sym}} \approx -103 \pm 101$ MeV at 68% confidence level
- Siemens' $\rho^{2/3}$ scaling predicts $L(\rho_0) = 2E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) \approx 56$ to 68 MeV, $K_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = -2E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) \approx -(56$ to $68)$ MeV, and $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(2\rho_0) = 1.58E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) \approx 44$ to 54 MeV using $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) \approx 31 \pm 3$ MeV
- Analyses of the ASY-EOS data from GSI using the UrQMD model give $E_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = 34$ MeV, $L = 72 \pm 13$ MeV, and $K_{\mathrm{sym}}(\rho_0) = -(40$ to $70)$ MeV

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Siemens (1970s) who first observed the $\rho^{2/3}$ scaling of the symmetry energy using Brueckner theory, and builds upon earlier reviews by Li (1998-2017), Lattimer (2004), Steiner (2005), Baran (2005), and others. The paper synthesizes constraints from both terrestrial heavy-ion experiments and multimessenger neutron star observations, including post-GW170817 analyses, to assess the current status of the high-density symmetry energy. It identifies the need to combine future high-precision neutron star observations with heavy-ion collision experiments involving high-energy rare isotopes to finally constrain the symmetry energy above $2\rho_0$, where the $\rho^{2/3}$ scaling breaks down.
