# Next-to-next-to-leading-order epsilon expansion for a Fermi gas at infinite scattering length

> We extend previous work on applying the epsilon-expansion to universal properties of a cold, dilute Fermi gas in the unitary regime of infinite scattering length. We compute the ratio xi = mu/epsilon_F of chemical potential to ideal gas Fermi energy to next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) in epsilon=4-d, where d is the number of spatial dimensions. We also explore the nature of corrections from the order after NNLO.

## Key claim

The authors compute the ratio ξ = μ/ε_F (chemical potential to ideal gas Fermi energy) for a cold, dilute Fermi gas at infinite scattering length to next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) in ε = 4 − d, where d is the number of spatial dimensions, and find that the NNLO correction is anomalously large, raising questions about the reliability of naive extrapolation to d = 3.

## Method

The authors use the ε-expansion method developed by Nishida and Son (2006), formulating the problem in d = 4 − ε spatial dimensions where a perturbative solution exists for ε ≪ 1. They evaluate the effective potential V(φ₀) for the superfluid condensate φ₀ through three-loop diagrams, using dimensional regularization and a decomposition of fermion propagators into retarded and advanced parts to simplify frequency integrals, then performing the remaining momentum integrals numerically via adaptive Monte Carlo integration.

## Key numbers

* ξ = (1/2) ε^(ε/2d) [ ε^(3/2) − 0.04916 ε^(5/2) − 0.95961 ε^(7/2) − (3/8) ε^(9/2) ln ε + O(ε^(9/2)) ] for the ratio of chemical potential to Fermi energy in d = 4 − ε dimensions
* The NNLO coefficient −0.95961 is approximately 20 times larger than the NLO coefficient −0.04916, indicating poor convergence of the ε expansion at ε = 1
* The cross diagram contribution K₃^(⊗) ≃ 0.15101 (dimensionless numerical constant from three-loop evaluation in exactly four dimensions)

## Context

This work extends the next-to-leading-order ε-expansion results of Nishida and Son (2006) for the unitary Fermi gas, which gave ξ ≃ 0.475 at NLO. The large NNLO correction suggests that simple truncation of the asymptotic ε series at ε = 1 may be unreliable, motivating more sophisticated Borel-Padé extrapolation methods similar to those used for critical exponents in phase transitions. The authors also show that new logarithmic corrections (ε^(9/2) ln ε) appear at the order beyond NNLO, indicating infrared issues that require modified power counting for higher orders.
