# Nucleon form factors and the pion-nucleon sigma term

> This talk summarizes the progress made since Lattice 2021 in understanding and controlling the contributions of towers of multihadron excited states with mass gaps starting lower than of radial excitations, and in increasing our confidence in the extraction of ground state nucleon matrix elements. The most clear evidence for multihadron excited state contributions (ESC) is in axial/pseudoscalar form factors that are required to satisfy the PCAC relation between them. The talk examines the broader question--which and how many of the theoretically allowed positive parity states $N(\textbf p)π(-\textbf p)$, $N(\textbf 0)π(\textbf 0)π(\textbf 0)$, $N(\textbf p)π(\textbf 0)$, $N(\textbf 0)π(\textbf p),\ \ldots$ make significant contributions to a given nucleon matrix element? New data for the axial, electric and magnetic form factors are presented. They continue to show trends observed in Ref[1]. The N${}^2$LO $χ$PT analysis of the ESC to the pion-nucleon sigma term, $σ_{πN}$, has been extended to include the $Δ$ as an explicit degree of freedom [2]. The conclusion reached in Ref [3] that $N π$ and $N ππ$ states each contribute about 10 MeV to $σ_{πN}$, and the consistency between the lattice result with $N π$ state included and the phenomenological estimate is not changed by this improvement.

## Key claim

The talk claims that including multihadron excited states (specifically \(N\pi\) and \(N\pi\pi\) states) in the analysis of lattice QCD correlation functions is necessary to extract reliable ground-state nucleon matrix elements, but the authors hedge that current data cannot statistically distinguish between fits with different excited-state spectra, requiring additional physics constraints such as the PCAC relation or chiral perturbation theory to select the correct analysis.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD simulations using 2+1-flavor Wilson-clover ensembles (extended from 7 to 13 ensembles with 11 distinct lattice spacings and pion masses) and computed nucleon 2-point and 3-point correlation functions for axial, vector, and scalar currents. They applied spectral decomposition fits with varying numbers of states, including explicit non-interacting \(N\pi\) and \(N\pi\pi\) states via narrow priors, and tested consistency using the PCAC relation between axial and pseudoscalar form factors and a N\(^2\)LO chiral perturbation theory analysis of the pion-nucleon sigma term extended to include the \(\Delta\) resonance.

## Key numbers

- The \(N\pi\) and \(N\pi\pi\) states each contribute about 10 MeV to the pion-nucleon sigma term \(\sigma_{\pi N}\), bringing the lattice result from approximately 40 MeV to approximately 60 MeV, consistent with phenomenological estimates.
- The axial charge \(g_A^{u-d}\) extracted with the standard analysis is about 5% smaller than the experimental value of 1.276(2), while the analysis including the \(N(0,0,1)\pi(0,0,-1)\) state gives a value about 6% larger than the standard analysis and consistent with experiment.
- The PCAC relation shows a significant (\(\sim 12\%\)) drop in the \(a070m130\) ensemble data as \(Q^2 \to 0\) when including the \(N\pi\) state, compared to approximately 50% without it.
- The energy of the lowest multihadron state \(N(0,0,1)\pi(0,0,-1)\) is approximately 1230 MeV on the ensembles studied.
- A state with an energy gap \(\Delta E_1 = 300\) MeV contributes 20% at source-sink separation \(\tau/a = 11\) (1 fm) and 5% at \(\tau/a = 22\) (2 fm).

## Context

This work extends the lattice QCD calculations of nucleon form factors and the sigma term reported by the same collaboration in Park (2021) and Gupta (2021), addressing the challenge of multihadron excited state contributions that were identified as the cause of PCAC violation in earlier work by Gupta (2017). The analysis builds on chiral perturbation theory predictions by Bar (2018) for enhanced \(N\pi\) contributions to axial matrix elements and by Bar (2021) for pion-loop effects in electromagnetic form factors. The results enable improved extraction of nucleon ground-state matrix elements needed for dark matter direct detection, lepton flavor violation studies, and electric dipole moment calculations, while highlighting the need for significantly higher statistics (estimated at a factor of 10 or more) to discriminate between competing fit models at physical pion mass.
