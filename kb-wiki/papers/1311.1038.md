# Ambiguities of the Rate of Oxygen Formation During Stellar Helium Burning in the 12C(a,g) Reaction

> The rate of oxygen formation determines the C/O ratio during stellar helium burning. It is the single most important nuclear input of stellar evolution theory including the evolution of Type II and Type Ia supernova. Yet the low energy cross section of the fusion of 4He + 12C denoted as the 12C(a,g)16O reaction still remains uncertain after forty years of extensive work. We analyze and critically review the most recent measurements of complete angular distributions of the outgoing gamma-rays at very low energies (Ecm > 1.0 MeV). Our analysis of the angular distribution measured with the EUROGAM/GANDI arrays lead us to conclude considerably larger error bars than published hence they are excluded from the current sample of "world data". We show that the current sample of "world data" of the measured E2 cross section factors below 1.7 MeV cluster to two distinct groups that lead to two distinct extrapolations of SE2(300) ~ 60 or ~ 154 keVb. We point to a much neglected discrepancy between the measured E1-E2 phase difference (phi_12) and unitarity as required by the Watson theorem, suggesting systematic problem(s) of some of the measured gamma-ray angular distributions. The ambiguity of the extrapolated SE2(300) together with a previously observed ambiguity of SE1(300) represent the current state of the art of the field and they must be resolved by future measurements of complete and detailed angular distributions of the 12C(a,g) reaction at very low energies (Ecm < 1.0 MeV).

# Ambiguities of the Rate of Oxygen Formation During Stellar Helium Burning in the 12C(a,g) Reaction

## Key claim

The measured E2 cross section factors (S_E2) from angular distributions below 1.7 MeV cluster into two distinct groups that extrapolate to S_E2(300) ≈ 60 keVb or ≈ 154 keVb, and neither solution can be favored or ruled out by current world data. Additionally, the measured E1-E2 relative phase angles (φ_12) in some experiments violate unitarity as required by the Watson theorem, indicating systematic problems in some measured gamma-ray angular distributions.

## Method

The author analyzed and critically reviewed published complete angular distributions of gamma-rays from the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction measured at center-of-mass energies approaching 1.0 MeV, using data from the EUROGAM/GANDI arrays at Stuttgart and other experiments (Kunz 2001, Plag 2012, Redder 1987, Ouellet 1996). The analysis employed standard Legendre polynomial expansions with published angular attenuation coefficients, fixing the relative phase angle φ_12 at the value predicted by unitarity and varying only the S_E2/S_E1 ratio and overall normalization to extract cross section factors.

## Key numbers

- The S_E2 values measured below 1.7 MeV cluster into two groups that extrapolate to S_E2(300) = 60 ± 12 keVb or S_E2(300) = 154 ± 31 keVb
- The two groups of data differ by an average factor of 2.6
- At E_cm = 1.342 MeV, the extracted S_E2/S_E1 ratio is 1.4^{+1.6}_{-0.6} with φ_12 fixed at 54 degrees, demonstrating that the ratio can vary by a factor as large as six while yielding similar fit quality (χ²/ν from 1.8 to 2.4)
- The R-matrix fit of Plag (2012) to the lower group yields χ²/N = 2.0 (N=10) for data from Plag (2012) and Redder (1987), but χ²/N = 6.0 (N=5) for data from Kunz (2001) and Ouellet (1996)
- The same fit multiplied by 2.57 yields χ²/N = 0.75 for the Kunz (2001) and Ouellet (1996) data, but χ²/N = 26.7 for the Plag (2012) and Redder (1987) data
- At E_cm = 1.254 MeV, Kunz (2001) measured E2/E1 = 1.28(32), while at the nearby energy of E_cm = 1.308 MeV, Plag (2012) measured E2/E1 = 0.44(20), a factor of almost 3 difference

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate on the 12C(α,γ)16O reaction cross section, which has remained uncertain after forty years of study despite being the single most important nuclear input for stellar evolution theory, including Type II and Type Ia supernova evolution. The ambiguity in S_E2(300) reported here for the first time resembles the previously observed ambiguity in S_E1(300), where the small value solution of approximately 10 keVb cannot be ruled out in favor of the approximately 80 keVb solution (Hale 1997, Gialanella 2001). The analysis excludes the Stuttgart EUROGAM/GANDI data (Hammer 2005, Assuncao 2006) from the world data sample, in agreement with Brune and Sayre (2013) but in conflict with Schuermann (2012), and points to the need for future measurements of complete and detailed angular distributions at energies below 1.0 MeV, such as those proposed by Gai (2012) and the LUNA-MV collaboration.
