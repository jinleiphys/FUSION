# The End of Cheap Uranium

> Historic data from many countries demonstrate that on average no more than 50-70% of the uranium in a deposit could be mined. An analysis of more recent data from Canada and Australia leads to a mining model with an average deposit extraction lifetime of 10+- 2 years. This simple model provides an accurate description of the extractable amount of uranium for the recent mining operations. Using this model for all larger existing and planned uranium mines up to 2030, a global uranium mining peak of at most 58 +- 4 ktons around the year 2015 is obtained. Thereafter we predict that uranium mine production will decline to at most 54 +- 5 ktons by 2025 and, with the decline steepening, to at most 41 +- 5 ktons around 2030. This amount will not be sufficient to fuel the existing and planned nuclear power plants during the next 10-20 years. In fact, we find that it will be difficult to avoid supply shortages even under a slow 1%/year worldwide nuclear energy phase-out scenario up to 2025. We thus suggest that a worldwide nuclear energy phase-out is in order. If such a slow global phase-out is not voluntarily effected, the end of the present cheap uranium supply situation will be unavoidable. The result will be that some countries will simply be unable to afford sufficient uranium fuel at that point, which implies involuntary and perhaps chaotic nuclear phase-outs in those countries involving brownouts, blackouts, and worse.

## Key claim

The paper argues that global uranium mine production will peak at most at 58 ± 4 ktons around 2015 and then decline to at most 41 ± 5 ktons by 2030, which will be insufficient to fuel existing and planned nuclear power plants even under a slow 1%/year worldwide nuclear energy phase-out scenario up to 2025. The hedge is that this model likely overestimates future production because planned plateau values for new mines have historically been larger than achieved values.

## Method

The author analyzed historic uranium mining data from depleted mines in Europe and Africa, showing that only 50-70% of originally estimated uranium in deposits could be extracted. Using more recent production data from individual deposits in Canada and Australia, the author formulated a "ten-year production model" where a deposit's production plateau can be sustained for 10 ± 2 years, and the total extractable uranium equals the annual plateau value multiplied by 10. This model was then applied to all larger existing and planned uranium mines worldwide to forecast production through 2030.

## Key numbers

- Global uranium mining peak of at most 58 ± 4 ktons around the year 2015
- Production decline to at most 54 ± 5 ktons by 2025 and to at most 41 ± 5 ktons around 2030
- Present global uranium usage is about 68 ktons/year
- Historic data show that on average no more than 50-70% of uranium in a deposit could be mined
- Average deposit extraction lifetime of 10 ± 2 years from analysis of Canadian and Australian data
- Total of about 2.5 million tons of uranium mined worldwide including 2010
- Kazakhstan production rose from 4.4 ktons in 2005 to almost 18 ktons in 2010
- McArthur deposit in Canada has an average ore grade of 15.24%, about 100 times larger than essentially all other deposits

## Context

This work extends the Energy Watch Group (EWG 2006) analysis of uranium supply limits, which predicted a production peak around 2025 based on Red Book resource numbers, but the author argues that EWG's reliance on claimed RAR numbers is inconsistent with actual production declines in the USA and South Africa. The paper contradicts the World Nuclear Association (WNA 2009) forecast of a production peak of 85 ktons/year around 2025, arguing that the WNA assumption of 20-year mine lifetimes is inconsistent with historic data. The analysis enables a more realistic assessment of uranium supply constraints for nuclear energy planning, suggesting that even maintaining current nuclear capacity will face supply shortages within 10-20 years.
