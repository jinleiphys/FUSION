# Parallelization techniques for quantum simulation of fermionic systems

> Mapping fermionic operators to qubit operators is an essential step for simulating fermionic systems on a quantum computer. We investigate how the choice of such a mapping interacts with the underlying qubit connectivity of the quantum processor to enable (or impede) parallelization of the resulting Hamiltonian-simulation algorithm. It is shown that this problem can be mapped to a path coloring problem on a graph constructed from the particular choice of encoding fermions onto qubits and the fermionic interactions onto paths. The basic version of this problem is called the weak coloring problem. Taking into account the fine-grained details of the mapping yields what is called the strong coloring problem, which leads to improved parallelization performance. A variety of illustrative analytical and numerical examples are presented to demonstrate the amount of improvement for both weak and strong coloring-based parallelizations. Our results are particularly important for implementation on near-term quantum processors where minimizing circuit depth is necessary for algorithmic feasibility.

## Key claim

The parallelization of fermionic Hamiltonian simulation on a quantum computer can be mapped to a path coloring problem on a graph constructed from the fermion-to-qubit encoding and the fermionic interactions, and by considering fine-grained details of the encoding (the strong coloring problem) one can obtain improved parallelization performance compared to the more abstracted weak coloring problem, though both approaches provide large reductions in circuit depth relative to a naive sequential approach.

## Method

The authors formalize the parallelization problem using custom fermionic codes, where a system graph determines the encoding of fermionic modes onto qubits and the paths through this graph implement the required fermionic edge operators. They define two versions of a conflict graph: the weak coloring problem considers whether paths share vertices in the system graph, while the strong coloring problem considers whether paths share individual qubits within those vertices, accounting for the internal qubit structure determined by the Jordan-Wigner encoding of local Majoranas at each vertex. Heuristic classical algorithms for graph coloring are then applied to determine the minimum number of parallel steps needed to implement all interaction terms.

## Key numbers

- For a star system graph and a complete system graph, strong coloring asymptotically provides up to a factor of two improvement over weak coloring.
- The number of qubits required for a system graph Sigma is given by Q(Sigma) = sum over vertices v of ceil(d(v)/2), where d(v) is the degree of vertex v.
- The chromatic number chi(G) of a graph G satisfies chi(G) <= max d(v) + 1 for any graph, and chi(G) <= max d(v) for simple connected graphs that are neither complete nor odd cycles (Brooks' theorem bounds).
- The clique number omega(G) provides a lower bound on the chromatic number: chi(G) >= omega(G).

## Context

This work extends the framework of custom fermionic codes developed by Chien and Whitfield (2020), which generalized the Bravyi-Kitaev superfast encoding and allowed flexible, architecture-aware fermion-to-qubit mappings. Prior work by Hastings (2014), Babbush et al. (2017), and Kivlichan et al. (2018) had explored term ordering and SWAP networks to reduce circuit depth for Jordan-Wigner transformed Hamiltonians, while Chiew et al. (2021) studied optimal fermion-qubit mappings using qubit enumeration. The present paper adds parallelizability as a new optimization axis within the custom fermionic code framework, enabling heuristic classical algorithms to inform efficient implementations of fermionic simulations on near-term quantum processors where minimizing circuit depth is critical.
