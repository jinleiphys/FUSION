# Multiplicity fluctuations and correlations in 5.02 TeV p+Pb collisions at zero impact parameter

> We present a Bayesian method to reconstruct event-by-event multiplicity fluctuations and rapidity correlations in p+Pb collisions at zero impact parameter from minimum-bias data, without assuming any model of the collision dynamics. We test it on Monte Carlo simulations with the Angantyr model, then apply it to ATLAS data on the distribution of charged multiplicity and transverse energy in p+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=5.02$ TeV. Fluctuations in $b=0$ collisions are quantum fluctuations which originate mostly from the proton wave function, and therefore have the potential to constrain the subnucleonic structure of the proton. The Angantyr model is found to overestimate fluctuations. In addition, we find that as the rapidity increases (towards the Pb-going side), not only the multiplicity density increases, but also its relative event-by-event fluctuation. This counter-intuitive phenomenon is also observed in simulations with Angantyr, and with the QCD dipole model, where its origin can be traced back to the branching process through which gluons are produced.

# Multiplicity fluctuations and correlations in 5.02 TeV p+Pb collisions at zero impact parameter

## Key claim

The authors present a Bayesian method to reconstruct event-by-event multiplicity fluctuations and rapidity correlations in p+Pb collisions at zero impact parameter from minimum-bias data, without assuming any model of the collision dynamics. The method is validated on Angantyr simulations and applied to ATLAS data, finding that the Angantyr model overestimates fluctuations and that relative event-by-event fluctuations increase with rapidity toward the Pb-going side, a counter-intuitive phenomenon also seen in the QCD dipole model.

## Method

The authors construct a correlated gamma distribution parametrization of fluctuations at fixed impact parameter, extending the Gaussian kernel approach used in nucleus-nucleus collisions. They perform a 15-parameter Bayesian fit of the minimum-bias joint distribution of charged multiplicity (Nch) in |eta|<2.5 and transverse energy (ET) in 3.2<eta<4.9, integrating over impact parameter using a smooth polynomial dependence. The method is validated by comparing reconstructed b=0 quantities against direct Angantyr simulations at b=0, then applied to ATLAS minimum-bias data at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Mean charged multiplicity at b=0 reconstructed from ATLAS data: Nch = 94 (with ~3% accuracy based on validation)
- Mean transverse energy at b=0 reconstructed from ATLAS data: ET = 60 GeV (with ~3% accuracy)
- Standard deviation of Nch at b=0 from ATLAS: sigma_Nch = 32
- Standard deviation of ET at b=0 from ATLAS: sigma_ET = 24 GeV
- Pearson correlation coefficient between Nch and ET at b=0 from ATLAS: r = 0.65
- Relative covariance matrix for ATLAS at b=0: sigma_11 = 0.101 (Nch), sigma_22 = 0.147 (ET), sigma_12 = 0.084
- Relative covariance matrix for Angantyr at b=0: sigma_11 = 0.182, sigma_22 = 0.244, sigma_12 = 0.187
- RMS error of correlated gamma fit to Angantyr b=0 distribution: 12.3% (vs 46.0% for Gaussian fit)
- RMS error of correlated gamma fit to Pb+Pb b=0 distribution: 5.9% (vs 6.3% for Gaussian fit)

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian impact parameter reconstruction method developed for nucleus-nucleus collisions by Das (2017) and Yousefnia (2021) to proton-nucleus collisions, following the approach of Rogly (2018). The method isolates quantum fluctuations at fixed impact parameter, which originate mostly from the proton wave function, providing new constraints on subnucleonic proton structure beyond what is accessible from traditional correlation analyses using ratios or nu_dyn combinations (Pruneau, 2002; Gorenstein, 2011). The results enable direct comparison with QCD-based models such as the dipole model and the fluctuating-string model, and suggest that the observed centrality dependence of multiplicity in p+Pb collisions can be explained by the interplay of proton size fluctuations and the number of wounded nucleons.
