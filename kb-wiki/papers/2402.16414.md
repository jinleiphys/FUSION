# Cumulant Green's function methods for molecules

> The cumulant expansion of the Green's function is a computationally efficient beyond-$GW$ approach renowned for its significant enhancement of satellite features in materials. In contrast to the ubiquitous $GW$ approximation of many-body perturbation theory, \textit{ab initio} cumulant expansions performed on top of $GW$ ($GW$+C) have demonstrated the capability to handle multi-particle processes by incorporating higher-order correlation effects or vertex corrections, yielding better agreements between experiment and theory for satellite structures. While widely employed in condensed matter physics, very few applications of $GW$+C have been published on molecular systems. Here, we assess the performance of this scheme on a series of 10-electron molecular systems (\ce{Ne}, \ce{HF}, \ce{H2O}, \ce{NH3}, and \ce{CH4}) where full configuration interaction estimates of the outer-valence quasiparticle and satellite energies are available.

## Key claim

The cumulant expansion on top of $GW$ ($GW$+C) systematically improves satellite descriptions in molecular systems compared to $GW$ alone, but the improvement is modest for outer-valence quasiparticle energies, and the method's performance depends on the starting point (e.g., $G_0W_0$ vs. qs$GW$) and the specific molecular system.

## Method

The authors performed $GW$+C calculations on a series of 10-electron molecules (Ne, HF, H2O, NH3, CH4) using the retarded Green's function formalism with a Hartree-Fock starting point. The cumulant was constructed from the diagonal elements of the $GW$ self-energy within the linear response approximation, and both one-shot ($G_0W_0$+C) and quasiparticle self-consistent (qs$GW$+C) variants were tested. Quasiparticle energies and satellite positions were extracted from the spectral function and compared to full configuration interaction (FCI) reference data.

## Key numbers

* For Ne, the $G_0W_0$+C quasiparticle energy of the $(2p)^{-1}$ state is 20.983 eV (spectral weight 0.942), compared to the FCI reference of 21.426 eV; qs$GW$+C gives 20.733 eV (0.930).
* For H2O, the $G_0W_0$+C quasiparticle energy of the $(1b_1)^{-1}$ state is 12.384 eV (0.927), versus FCI 12.540 eV; qs$GW$+C gives 12.228 eV (0.912).
* For NH3, the $G_0W_0$+C quasiparticle energy of the $(3a_1)^{-1}$ state is 10.993 eV (0.927), versus FCI 11.020 eV; qs$GW$+C gives 10.848 eV (0.914).
* For CH4, the $G_0W_0$+C quasiparticle energy of the $(1t_2)^{-1}$ state is 14.253 eV (0.936), versus FCI 14.410 eV; qs$GW$+C gives 14.086 eV (0.926).
* The $GW$+C renormalization factor $Z_p^{QP}$ is systematically smaller than the $GW$ factor $Z_p^{GW}$, indicating redistribution of weight from the quasiparticle peak to satellites.

## Context

This work extends the $GW$+C approach, previously applied mainly in condensed matter physics (e.g., to sodium by Aryasetiawan (1996) and silicon by Guzzo (2011)), to small molecular systems where FCI reference data are available from Marie (2024). It complements prior molecular applications by Vlcek (2018) on NH3, PH3, and C2H2, and by Vila (2020, 2021) on core excitations. The results enable benchmarking of cumulant methods for molecular satellites and highlight the need for improved treatment of satellite intensities and higher-order terms beyond linear response.
