# Probing impact-parameter dependent nuclear parton densities from double parton scatterings in heavy-ion collisions

> We propose a new method to determine the spatially or impact-parameter dependent nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs) using the double parton scattering (DPS) processes in high-energy heavy-ion (proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus) collisions. We derive a simple generic DPS formula in nuclear collisions by accommodating both the nuclear collision geometry and the spatially dependent nuclear modification effect, under the assumption that the impact-parameter dependence of nPDFs is only related to the nuclear thickness function. While the geometric effect is widely adopted, the impact of the spatially dependent nuclear modification on DPS cross sections has been overlooked so far, which can, however, be significant when the initial nuclear modification is large. In turn, the DPS cross sections in heavy-ion collisions can provide useful information on the spatial dependence of nPDFs. They can be, in general, obtained in minimum-bias nuclear collisions, featuring the virtue of independence of Glauber modeling.

# Probing impact-parameter dependent nuclear parton densities from double parton scatterings in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The paper proposes that double parton scattering (DPS) cross sections in minimum-bias heavy-ion collisions can determine the spatially (impact-parameter) dependent nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs), under the assumption that the impact-parameter dependence of nPDFs is only related to the nuclear thickness function. The author acknowledges that this method works only when the initial nuclear modification is large enough to produce a measurable effect, and that the existing DPS formula in heavy-ion collisions has overlooked this spatially dependent nuclear modification.

## Method
The author derives a generic DPS formula for nucleus-nucleus (AB) and proton-nucleus (pA) collisions by incorporating both the nuclear collision geometry (via thickness functions) and the spatially dependent nuclear modification effect (via a function G relating the impact-parameter dependent nPDF to the spatially averaged nPDF). The derivation uses a factorized ansatz for nucleon generalized double parton distributions and generalized single parton distributions, assuming the transverse parton profile is independent of nucleon type and unaffected by surrounding nucleons. The resulting expressions separate contributions from partons belonging to the same nucleon pair versus different nucleons in the colliding nuclei.

## Key numbers
- DPS cross sections in pA collisions scale by 3 times the number of nucleons A in a nucleus, while single parton scattering (SPS) cross sections scale by A (modulo other nuclear matter effects).
- DPS cross sections in AA collisions scale as A^{3.3}/5, while SPS cross sections scale as A^2.
- For lead (Pb) with A=208, R_A=6.624 fm, and sigma_eff,pp=15 mb, using a monomial G(x) proportional to x^a with a=0,1,2,3 predicts R_{pA to f1f2}^{DPS} values of 1.27, 1.22, 1.07, and 7.26 respectively for R_{pA}^f=0.6 (representative of open charm or J/psi at LHC forward rapidity).
- The hard-sphere model thickness function is T_A(b) = (3A)/(2 pi R_A^2) sqrt(1 - |b|^2/R_A^2) theta(R_A - |b|).

## Context
This work extends the pioneering study by Strikman and Treleani (2001) on DPS enhancement in nuclear collisions, and addresses a gap in existing DPS phenomenological applications in heavy-ion collisions (Strikman 2010, Blok 2012, d'Enterria 2012-2017, Cazaroto 2016, Blok 2017, Alvioli 2018-2019, Helenius 2019, Fedkevych 2019) that did not consider spatially dependent nuclear modification. The method provides an alternative to centrality-based measurements that rely on Glauber modeling (Vogt 1999, Miller 2007), which suffer from large uncertainties particularly in proton-nucleus collisions. The approach enables extraction of impact-parameter dependent nPDFs from minimum-bias data, complementing the only existing spatially dependent nPDFs from Helenius (2012) which were derived from A dependencies of spatially averaged nPDFs.
