# Particle Physics and Gravitational Waves as complementary windows on the Universe

> Particle physics and gravitational waves provide complementary probes of the deep structure of the Universe. Gravitational waves from the mergers of neutron stars and black holes are sensitive to the structure of dense quark matter and to different dark matter scenarios. Measurements of stochastic gravitational waves backgrounds can teach us about possible first order phase transitions in the early Universe, including providing sensitivity to the TeV scale which is of key interest to future particle collider experiments. Gravitational waves measurements will also give new probes of the evolution and expansion of the Universe, complementary to measurements with electromagnetic radiation. This Perspectives article explores the physics synergies between the science opportunities provided by next generation gravitational waves measurements and particle physics experiments. Gravitational waves can also probe deep into the early Universe reaching physics much above possible collider energies if the signals can be detected.

## Key claim

This Perspectives article argues that next-generation gravitational wave measurements and particle physics experiments provide complementary probes of the Universe's deep structure, but the synergy is conditional: gravitational waves can probe energy scales much above possible collider energies only if the signals can actually be detected, and the energy scale of possible new physics beyond the Standard Model is so far not known from present experiments.

## Method

The authors review the science programmes of current and future gravitational wave detectors (aLIGO, Virgo, KAGRA, LISA, Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer) alongside particle physics experiments (HL-LHC, future e+e- colliders, direct and indirect dark matter searches, axion experiments). They identify specific overlapping observables where the two fields can cross-constrain physics: neutron star mergers probing QCD matter at high density, black hole environments testing dark matter scenarios, stochastic gravitational wave backgrounds from first-order phase transitions at the TeV scale, and measurements of the Hubble parameter and dark energy.

## Key numbers

- The first direct GW detection event GW150914 involved two black holes of 29 and 35 solar masses, with about 3 solar masses (approximately 5% of the initial mass) released as gravitational waves in the frequency range 35-250 Hz
- More than 200 binary merger events have been recorded since 2015, plus one multimessenger event (GW170817) from a binary neutron star merger at distance 40 Mpc and redshift z=0.0099
- The GW170817 total initial mass was 2.74 (+0.04/-0.01) solar masses, with a 90% chance that the heavier neutron star had mass between 1.36 and 1.62 solar masses and the lighter between 1.15 and 1.36 solar masses
- LISA will detect gravitational waves in the frequency range between 10 microHz and 1 Hz, with three satellites 2.5 million km apart and an initial lifetime of 4.5 years
- The Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer will increase sensitivity by more than an order of magnitude in the frequency range between a few Hz and 2000 Hz, detecting approximately 100,000 mergers per year compared to about 200 from current detectors
- The Capers-lrd-z9 galaxy at redshift z=9.288 (Universe age 500 million years) has an estimated supermassive black hole mass between 4.5 x 10^6 and 3.2 x 10^8 solar masses
- The speed of gravitational waves and light are equal to within one part in 10^15, as determined from GW170817
- Dark matter makes up 27% of the energy budget of the Universe, dark energy 68%
- The postmerger phase of neutron star mergers radiates up to 0.1 solar masses over approximately 10-20 milliseconds, with a broad spectral peak at frequencies 2-4 kHz

## Context

This Perspectives article synthesises the science opportunities identified in the European Strategy for Particle Physics Physics Briefing Book (deBlas, 2026) and the Gravitational Wave Roadmap (Bailes, 2021). It extends the discussion of synergies between particle physics and gravitational wave astronomy beyond earlier work by explicitly connecting specific observables: neutron star equation of state constraints from GW170817 (LIGO Scientific, 2018) with future GSI/FAIR laboratory studies of dense QCD matter, and TeV-scale phase transitions detectable by LISA with Higgs physics at the HL-LHC. The paper enables a coordinated experimental strategy where gravitational wave detections of stochastic backgrounds, black hole environments, and neutron star mergers can inform particle physics searches for dark matter candidates (WIMPs, axions, primordial black holes) and first-order phase transitions, while collider and direct detection results can anchor the interpretation of gravitational wave signals.
