# Quadrupole Phonons in the Cadmium Isotopes

> A key question concerning the spherical-vibrator attributes of states in Cadmium isotopes is addressed by means of a boson Hamiltonian encompassing U(5) partial dynamical symmetry. The U(5) symmetry is preserved in a segment of the spectrum and is broken in particular non-yrast states, and the resulting mixing with the intruder states is small. The vibrational character is thus maintained in the majority of low-lying normal states, as observed in $^{110}$Cd.

## Key claim

The vibrational interpretation of the cadmium isotopes can be resurrected by mixing particular phonon states (not by mixing vibrational and intruder states as attempted previously), using a U(5) partial dynamical symmetry in which the U(5) symmetry is preserved in a subset of states (class A) but broken in specific non-yrast states (classes B and C), with the resulting mixing with intruder states remaining small.

## Method

The authors constructed a boson Hamiltonian combining a U(5) partial dynamical symmetry (PDS) Hamiltonian in the normal sector (with N=7 bosons) and an SO(6)-type Hamiltonian in the intruder sector (with N+2=9 bosons), plus a mixing term between the two boson spaces. The U(5)-PDS Hamiltonian was built by adding to the standard U(5) dynamical symmetry Hamiltonian an interaction term containing cubic boson operators that annihilate states with n_d = τ and n_Δ = 0 (class A states), thereby preserving exact U(5) quantum numbers for those states while mixing others. The model was applied to ¹¹⁰Cd, with parameters fitted to reproduce experimental energies and E2 transition rates.

## Key numbers

- For ¹¹⁰Cd, the U(5)-PDS calculation yields B(E2; 0⁺₃ → 2⁺₁) = 0.25 W.u., consistent with the experimental upper limit < 7.9 W.u.
- B(E2; 2⁺₅ → 4⁺₁) = 0.19 W.u., consistent with the experimental upper limit < 5 W.u.
- B(E2; 2⁺₅ → 2⁺₂) = 0.12 W.u., consistent with the experimental value 0.7⁺⁰·⁵₋₀·₆ W.u.
- The mixing with intruder states (b² in Eq. Psi) is 0.9% for 0⁺₁, 2.2% for 2⁺₁, 3.6% for 2⁺₂, 5.9% for 4⁺₁, 4.6% for 3⁺₁, 6.1% for 4⁺₂, and 17.3% for 6⁺₁.
- For the problematic non-yrast states, intruder mixing is 5.1% (0⁺₃), 2.9% (2⁺₅), 4.4% (2⁺₆), and 18% (0⁺₄).
- The U(5)-DS calculation (without PDS) gives B(E2; 0⁺₃ → 2⁺₁) = 46.29 W.u., B(E2; 2⁺₅ → 4⁺₁) = 19.84 W.u., and B(E2; 2⁺₅ → 2⁺₂) = 11.02 W.u., all far above experimental limits.
- Monopole transition strengths: ρ²(E0; 0⁺₂,ᵢ → 0⁺₁)·10³ < 31 (experimental), calculated 0.75; ρ²(E0; 2⁺₃,ᵢ → 2⁺₁)·10³ = 9(8) (experimental), calculated 10; ρ²(E0; 4⁺₃,ᵢ → 4⁺₁)·10³ = 106⁺⁹⁸₋₉₁ (experimental), calculated 36.

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing "Cd problem", the apparent breakdown of the spherical vibrational interpretation in cadmium isotopes, which was highlighted by Garrett (2008, 2012) and described by Heyde (2011) as an unsolved problem. Previous attempts to explain the anomalous decay patterns through strong mixing between normal vibrational states and intruder deformed states (as in the work of Heyde (1982) and Deleze (1993)) were found unsatisfactory because they required ~50% mixing for the 0⁺₃ state while failing to reproduce three-phonon yrast decays. The present approach instead uses a U(5) partial dynamical symmetry, extending earlier PDS applications to the SU(3) and SO(6) chains of the interacting boson model (IBM) by Leviatan (1996), Van Isacker (1999), and Garcia-Ramos (2009). The results enable a revived vibrational interpretation for ¹¹⁰Cd and suggest applicability to other neutron-rich cadmium isotopes.
