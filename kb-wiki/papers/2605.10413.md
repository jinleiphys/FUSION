# Light-front Hamiltonian jet evolution in the Glasma

> We develop a light-front Hamiltonian formalism to study the real-time quantum evolution of a high-energy quark propagating through the Glasma phase of a heavy-ion collision. In this work, the quark Fock space is truncated to the $\ket{q}$ sector and the wavefunction is expanded in a discrete basis representation, following the time-dependent Basis Light-Front Quantization (tBLFQ) framework. The classical Glasma background fields enter as a time-dependent external potential, and physical observables are extracted as expectation values of quantum operators over the time-evolved state. We compute the transverse momentum broadening and the jet quenching parameter, finding results consistent with classical estimates, including the expected scaling with respect to the saturation momentum, and use them to perform phenomenological estimations for different collision systems. We also study the color rotation of the quark state induced by the Glasma fields, and examine its dependence on the saturation scale and the gauge choice. This formalism allows systematic improvements to include, in particular, non-eikonal propagation and parton splittings that will be considered in forthcoming publications.

## Key claim

The authors develop a light-front Hamiltonian formalism (tBLFQ) to simulate the real-time quantum evolution of a high-energy quark propagating through the Glasma phase of a heavy-ion collision, but only in the single-quark Fock sector (no gluon radiation) and in the eikonal limit at mid-rapidity, with the Glasma fields treated as a classical time-dependent background.

## Method

The quark state is evolved using the time-dependent Basis Light-Front Quantization (tBLFQ) framework, where the wavefunction is expanded in a discrete basis representation and the Hamiltonian is derived from the Dirac Lagrangian with minimal coupling to a classical external gluon field. The Glasma background fields are obtained by solving the classical Yang-Mills equations on a transverse lattice in Milne coordinates, using the McLerran-Venugopalan model for the nuclear color charge distributions, and then mapped onto the jet light-cone coordinates via a first-order Taylor expansion in the z-coordinate around mid-rapidity. Physical observables are extracted as expectation values of quantum operators over the time-evolved state, with event averaging over multiple color charge configurations.

## Key numbers

- The jet quenching parameter $\hat{q}$ in the Glasma phase is found to be consistent with classical estimates, including the expected scaling with the saturation momentum $Q_s$.
- The saturation scale $Q_s$ is related to the color charge density parameter $g^2\mu$ via the dipole correlator of Wilson lines, with the proportionality depending on the infrared regulator $m_g$ and lattice spacing $a_\perp$.
- The transverse plane of side length $L_\perp$ is discretized into $N_\perp$ lattice points along each direction, giving a lattice spacing $a_\perp = L_\perp / N_\perp$.
- The color charge correlation in the MV model is given by $\langle \rho^a(\vec{x}_\perp) \rho^b(\vec{y}_\perp) \rangle = g^2\mu^2 \, \delta^{ab} \delta^{(2)}(\vec{x}_\perp - \vec{y}_\perp)$.
- The infrared regulator $m_g$ appears in the Poisson equation $(\Delta_\perp - m_g^2) \mathcal{A}^{\bullet,*}_\text{cov} = -\rho_{(1,2)}$ used to solve for the single-nucleus gauge fields.

## Context

This work extends the classical treatment of jet propagation in the Glasma, previously studied using Wong's equations and Fokker-Planck transport by Ipp et al. (2020) and Carrington et al. (2021), by introducing a fully quantum treatment of the quark state at the amplitude level. It builds on the tBLFQ framework developed by Li et al. (2020, 2021, 2023, 2025) for simulating quark jets through classical color background fields, and on the companion work by Avramescu et al. (2026) that established the connection between classical and quantum formalisms for computing broadening with Glasma fields. The formalism enables systematic future improvements to include non-eikonal propagation and parton splittings through higher Fock states.
