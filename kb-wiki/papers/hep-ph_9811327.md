# One Theorist's Perspective on Four Eras of Electron-Proton Scattering

## Key claim

The paper argues that the history of electron-proton scattering can be organized into four distinct eras (Archaic, Classical, Hellenistic, and Renaissance), and that the proton's structure functions exhibit approximate Bjorken scaling at surprisingly low momentum transfers (around 1 GeV^2), suggesting that asymptopia is "so close" and that the proton behaves like a bag of confined but otherwise free quarks with only weakly correlated constituents.

## Method

The author presents a personal, historically-organized narrative of theoretical developments in electron-proton scattering from 1954 through the late 1990s, drawing on conference proceedings, key papers, and personal recollections from his time at SLAC. The talk traces the evolution from elastic form factor measurements (Hofstadter, 1955) through the discovery of deep inelastic scaling (MIT-SLAC experiments, 1968), the formulation of QCD, and the subsequent renaissance in spin-dependent and nuclear deep inelastic scattering.

## Key numbers

- The proton elastic form factor falls like a dipole, O(1/Q^4), rather than a monopole as predicted by vector dominance, at momentum transfers of order 1 GeV
- The Bjorken sum rule (1966) predicted that inelastic electron-nucleon scattering cross sections must be greater than something that falls like a power of four-momentum transfer
- The Gross-Llewellyn Smith sum rule integral of the chiral-odd neutrino structure function F3 asymptotes to three at Q^2 values well below 1 GeV
- The parity violation experiment at SLAC (Prescott et al., mid-1970s) determined sin^2(theta_W) to be near 1/4
- QCD predicts asymptotic equipartition of momentum between quarks and gluons at a ratio of 3n_f/16 (with n_f = 4 in early days, now 5), which works "pretty well at 1 GeV, excellently at 10 GeV"

## Context

This talk was presented to honor Sid Drell on his retirement and extends the historical perspective of Drell and Zachariasen's 1961 monograph "Electromagnetic Structure of Nucleons" and the work of Bjorken (1966), Johnson and Low (1966), and the MIT-SLAC collaboration (Friedman, Kendall, Taylor). The paper enables a modern understanding of why the proton appears so composite yet its constituents are only weakly correlated, a puzzle that connects to the earlier work of Bloom and Gilman (1970) on early scaling and to the European Muon Collaboration's 1982 discovery of the nuclear EMC effect.
