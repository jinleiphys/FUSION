# Can magnetic fields stabilize or destabilize twin stars?

> Sharp phase transitions described by stiff equations of state allow for the existence of a third family of stable compact stars (besides white dwarfs and neutron stars), twin stars. In this work, we investigate for the first time the role of strong magnetic fields on non-magnetic twin stars sequences and the case in which magnetic fields themselves give rise to a third family of stable stars. We use three sets of equations of state to study such effects from a more general point of view: the Quark-Hadron Chiral Parity-Doublet (Q$χ$P) model for both hadronic and quark phases, and the Many-Body Forces (MBF) model connected to either the MIT Bag model with vector interaction (MIT) or to the Constant-Sound-Speed (CSS) approximation for the quark phase, through a Maxwell construction. Magnetic field effects are introduced in the structure of stars through the solution of the Einstein-Maxwell equations, assuming a poloidal magnetic field configuration and a metric that allows for the description of deformed stars. We show that strong magnetic fields can destabilize twin star sequences, with the threshold intensity being model dependent. On the other hand, magnetic fields can also give rise to twin stars in models that did not predict these sequences, up to some point when they are again destabilized. In this sense, magnetic fields can play an important role on the evolution of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Strong magnetic fields can either destabilize existing twin-star sequences (by erasing the mass-radius degeneracy) or create new twin-star configurations that do not exist without magnetic fields, depending on the equation-of-state model and the field strength. The effect is not universal: the threshold for destabilization is model-dependent, and for some models (Sets 2 and 3) twin stars survive up to the highest physically accessible central magnetic fields of about 1e18 G.

## Method

The authors constructed three sets of non-magnetic twin-star sequences using different combinations of hadronic and quark matter equations of state: the Many-Body Forces (MBF) model connected via Maxwell construction to either the vector MIT bag model (Set 1) or the Constant-Sound-Speed (CSS) approximation (Set 2), and the Quark-Hadron Chiral Parity-Doublet (QχP) model (Set 3). They then solved the Einstein-Maxwell equations for deformed stars with a poloidal magnetic field configuration using the LORENE numerical library, fixing the central magnetic field for each star in a sequence by adjusting the dipole magnetic moment. For a fourth set (Set 4), they used a different parametrization of the MBF+MIT combination that initially produced no twin stars, then introduced magnetic fields to see if twins could be generated.

## Key numbers

* Set 1 non-magnetic twin stars: hadronic maximum mass M1 = 1.969 Msun at radius R1 = 14.23 km; hybrid maximum mass M2 = 1.978 Msun at R2 = 12.47 km; radius interval of third family ΔR2 = 1.33 km.
* Set 1: twin stars destabilize completely at a central magnetic field Bc ~ 5e17 G.
* Set 1: for the hadronic maximum mass star, increasing Bc from zero to 4e17 G increases radius from 14.23 km to 14.49 km and mass from 1.969 Msun to 2.016 Msun.
* Set 2 non-magnetic twin stars: M1 = 2.061 Msun at R1 = 13.61 km; M2 = 2.108 Msun at R2 = 10.9 km; ΔR2 = 1.97 km.
* Set 2: for Bc ~ 1e18 G, hadronic maximum mass star radius increases from 13.61 km to 14.173 km and mass from 2.061 Msun to 2.208 Msun; twin stars survive.
* Set 3 non-magnetic twin stars: M1 = 1.971 Msun at R1 = 13.63 km; M2 = 1.680 Msun at R2 = 9.70 km; ΔR2 = 0.45 km.
* Set 3: for Bc ~ 1e18 G, hadronic maximum mass star radius increases from 13.63 km to 14.65 km and mass from 1.971 Msun to 2.159 Msun; twin stars survive.
* Set 4 (magnetic twin stars): non-magnetic hybrid maximum mass M2 = 1.966 Msun at R2 = 12.00 km; twin stars appear at Bc = 3e17 G and disappear again at Bc ~ 7e17 G.
* Set 4: at Bc = 3e17 G, the new hadronic maximum mass star has M1 = 1.939 Msun at R1 = 14.19 km, with third-family radius interval ΔR2 = 1.69 km (from 13.73 km to 12.04 km).

## Context

This work extends the study of twin stars (a third family of compact stars first suggested by Gerlach (1968) and later by Kampfer (1981) in the context of hybrid stars with a quark core) by adding the previously unexplored effect of strong magnetic fields. It builds on prior work showing that magnetic fields can decrease central density and turn hybrid/hyperonic stars into nucleonic stars (Franzon 2015, Franzon 2016), and that twin-star sequences can be generated by sharp phase transitions in non-magnetic models (Alford 2013, Benic 2014, Alvarez-Castillo 2013). The results enable future studies of how magnetic field decay during magnetar evolution could create or destroy twin-star configurations, and motivate more model-independent relations between magnetic field strength, compactness, and the mass minimum between branches.
