# Time-Dependent Response Calculations of Nuclear Resonances

> A new alternate method for evaluating linear response theory is formally developed, and results are presented. This method involves the time-evolution of the system using TDHF and is constructed directly on top of a static Hartree-Fock calculation. By Fourier transforming the time-dependent result the response function and the total probability amplitude are extracted. This method allows for a coherent description of static properties of nuclei, such as binding energies and deformations, while also providing a method for calculating collective modes and reaction rates. A full 3-D Cartesian Basis-Spline collocation representation is used with several Skyrme interactions. Sample results are presented for the giant multipole resonances of 16O, 40Ca, and 32S and compared to other calculations.

# Time-Dependent Response Calculations of Nuclear Resonances

## Key claim
The paper presents a method for evaluating linear response theory by time-evolving a static Hartree-Fock solution using time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and Fourier transforming the result, but the method's accuracy depends on the choice of Skyrme interaction and numerical convergence criteria; the SkM* interaction yields excellent results satisfying the energy-weighted sum rule, while SgII and SkII show poorer convergence.

## Method
The authors start from a static Hartree-Fock calculation and add a Gaussian-shaped time-dependent external perturbation to the Hamiltonian. They then evolve the system using TDHF with infinitesimal time steps, compute the density fluctuation as a function of time, and Fourier transform the result to extract the linear response structure function and total transition probability amplitude. The calculations use a 3-D Cartesian Basis-Spline collocation representation on a lattice grid with periodic boundary conditions, employing several Skyrme interactions (SkM*, SgII, SkII).

## Key numbers
- Time step of 0.4 fm/c used for the TDHF evolution, with up to 32768 time steps for a maximum time of approximately 6550 fm/c
- Perturbation strength parameter ε chosen in the range 2.0×10⁻⁴ ≤ ε ≤ 2×10⁻⁷ to remain in the linear regime
- Gaussian perturbation parameter α set to 1.0 c²/fm², allowing perturbation of collective energies up to approximately 150 MeV
- For ¹⁶O, the SkM* isoscalar quadrupole resonance peaks at about 20 MeV, compared to the experimental giant quadrupole resonance at 20.7 MeV
- The SkM* result gives 92% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR); SgII gives 66% and SkII gives 36%
- Imposing time-reversal symmetry causes small shifts in collective mode positions of approximately 0.3 MeV
- For ⁴⁰Ca, the SkM* and SgII quadrupole response shows a peak at about 17 MeV
- For the SkII parametrization in ¹⁶O, the difference between the authors' calculations and continuum RPA calculations by Krewald is less than 2% for L=2, 3, and 4 modes; closest agreement is for the hexadecupole mode where both calculations give a peak energy of 28.2 MeV
- Static HF solutions require energy fluctuation less than about 1.0×10⁻⁵ for adequate starting points
- Norm preservation accuracy typically required to ≤ 1.0×10⁻⁸ to 5.0×10⁻¹⁰

## Context
This work extends prior TDHF-based studies of collective vibrations by Umar (1994), Umar and Oberacker (1995), and others (BF79, SCF01, SC03), as well as continuum RPA calculations by Krewald (1977). The method enables consistent predictions of both static nuclear properties (binding energies, deformations) and dynamical properties (collective modes, reaction rates) using the same Hamiltonian, which is important for β-decay calculations of exotic nuclei where deformation sensitivity is critical, as noted by Sorlin (2001). The approach allows future studies of coupling between different multipole excitation modes and can be extended to larger box sizes for better continuum representation.
