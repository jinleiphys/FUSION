# Formation of Selfbound States in a One-Dimensional Nuclear Model -- A Renormalization Group based Density Functional Study

> In nuclear physics, Density Functional Theory (DFT) provides the basis for state-of-the art studies of ground-state properties of heavy nuclei. However, the direct relation of the density functional underlying these calculations and the microscopic nuclear forces is not yet fully understood. We present a combination of DFT and Renormalization Group (RG) techniques which allows to study selfbound many-body systems from microscopic interactions. We discuss its application with the aid of systems of identical fermions interacting via a long-range attractive and short-range repulsive two-body force in one dimension. We compute ground-state energies, intrinsic densities, and density correlation functions of these systems and compare our results to those obtained from other methods. In particular, we show how energies of excited states as well as the absolute square of the ground-state wave function can be extracted from the correlation functions within our approach. The relation between many-body perturbation theory and our DFT-RG approach is discussed and illustrated with the aid of the calculation of the second-order energy correction for a system of $N$ identical fermions interacting via a general two-body interaction. Moreover, we discuss the control of spuriously emerging fermion self-interactions in DFT studies within our framework. In general, our approach may help to guide the development of energy functionals for future quantitative DFT studies of heavy nuclei from microscopic interactions.

## Key claim

The authors present a renormalization-group (RG) based density functional theory (DFT) approach that allows the energy density functional to be constructed systematically from microscopic two-body interactions, but only for a one-dimensional toy model of identical fermions; the method is not yet applied to realistic three-dimensional nuclear systems, and the authors state that the result in the physical limit does not depend on the regulator choice only if the RG equation is solved exactly.

## Method

The authors derive a flow equation for the 2PPI (two-particle-point-irreducible) effective action, which is identified with the Hohenberg-Kohn energy density functional, by introducing a dimensionless control parameter λ that gradually switches on the two-body interaction from zero (non-interacting fermions in a box) to fully interacting. The flow is computed by expanding the effective action about its ground-state density and using the density correlation functions of the non-interacting system (computed from one-particle propagators via Wick's theorem) as initial conditions. The approach is applied to a one-dimensional system of identical fermions with a two-body potential consisting of a repulsive Gaussian at short range and an attractive Gaussian at long range, with parameters chosen to mimic nuclear saturation properties.

## Key numbers

* The two-body interaction potential parameters are set to \(\bar{g} \equiv g L_0 = 2.4\) and \(\bar{\sigma}_2 \equiv \sigma_2 L_0^{-1} = 4.0\), where the length scale \(L_0 \equiv \sigma_1 = 0.2\) sets the scale for all dimensionful quantities.
* The fermion mass is set to \(m = 1\), so energy is measured in units of inverse length squared and \(g\) in units of inverse length.
* The box extent \(L\) is used to confine the fermions, with periodic boundary conditions for odd particle numbers and antiperiodic boundary conditions for even particle numbers to avoid degeneracy of the free ground state.

## Context

This work extends the DFT-RG approach previously developed by the same group (Kemler 2013) and builds on the identification of the Hohenberg-Kohn functional with the 2PPI effective action (Furnstahl 2007, Drut 2009, Braun 2011). The one-dimensional nuclear model was originally introduced to test Monte Carlo calculations (Alexandrou 1988) and has been used to benchmark other methods (Jurgenson 2008). The approach is intended to complement existing efforts to construct microscopic energy density functionals, such as density-matrix expansions (Carlsson 2008, Stoitsov 2010) and direct optimization of functionals (Schunck 2009, Kortelainen 2010), and may help guide the development of functionals for future quantitative DFT studies of heavy nuclei from microscopic interactions.
