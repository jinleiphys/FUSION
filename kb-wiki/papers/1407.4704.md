# Massive Compact Stars as Quark Stars

> High massive compact stars have been reported recently in the literature, providing strong constraints on the properties of the ultradense matter beyond the saturation nuclear density. In view of these results, the calculations of quark star or hybrid star equilibrium structure must be compatible with the provided observational data. But, since the used equations of state describing quark matter are in general too soft, in comparison with the equation of states used to describe the hadronic or nuclear matter, the calculated quark star models presented in the literature are in general not suitable to explain the stability of high compact massive objects.

# Massive Compact Stars as Quark Stars

## Key claim

Massive compact stars with masses up to 2.73 solar masses can be interpreted as quark stars composed entirely of deconfined quarks, provided the equation of state is stiffened by including the Color-Flavor Locked (CFL) superconducting phase and a density-dependent quark mass model. However, this interpretation depends on the specific choice of model parameters (the confinement parameter C, the strange quark current mass, and the CFL gap parameter), and not all observed compact objects are naturally explained as pure quark stars.

## Method

The authors solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for spherically symmetric quark star models using an equation of state derived from the density-dependent quark mass model, which incorporates asymptotic freedom and confinement phenomenologically. The CFL superconducting phase is included via a gap term in the thermodynamic potential, with the gap parameterized using an approximate expression from the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model. For stars with a nuclear crust, the crust equation of state from Baym et al. (1971) is used, matched to the CFL quark matter core at the pressure where neutron drip occurs.

## Key numbers

- Maximum quark star mass: 2.73 solar masses with radius 13.3 km, for model D1 (C=70 MeV.fm^-3, m_s0=150 MeV, mu*=100 MeV)
- Maximum quark star mass for model A4: 2.25 solar masses with radius 11.4 km (C=100 MeV.fm^-3, m_s0=50 MeV, mu*=250 MeV)
- Phase transition between CFL and unpaired quark matter occurs at baryon density approximately 5.5 times normal nuclear density (rho_0 = 0.15 fm^-3) for model D4
- Nuclear crust thickness: 196 m for model D1 and 231 m for model D4 at central density 1.0e15 g cm^-3; 150 m and 169 m respectively at central density 3.0e15 g cm^-3
- Quark star masses with crust: 2.69 solar masses (model D1) and 2.37 solar masses (model D4) at central density 1.0e15 g cm^-3; 2.56 and 2.28 solar masses at central density 3.0e15 g cm^-3

## Context

This work extends calculations by Alford et al. (2005) and Kurkela et al. (2010) on color superconducting quark matter, and contrasts with softer equations of state from the simple MIT bag model that typically yield maximum masses around 1.6 solar masses for hybrid stars. The results are compared to observational mass-radius constraints from Ozel (2006) for EXO 0748-676, EXO 1745-248, and 4U 1608-52, showing that pure quark stars can match the more massive objects but not the smaller-radius candidates like EXO 1745-248, which may instead be hybrid stars. The density-dependent quark mass model of Chakrabarty et al. (1997) provides a phenomenological framework that enables future parameter constraints from observational data.
