# Lattice Improvement in Lattice Effective Field Theory

> Lattice calculations using the framework of effective field theory have been applied to a wide range few-body and many-body systems. One of the challenges of these calculations is to remove systematic errors arising from the nonzero lattice spacing. Fortunately, the lattice improvement program pioneered by Symanzik provides a formalism for doing this. While lattice improvement has already been utilized in lattice effective field theory calculations, the effectiveness of the improvement program has not been systematically benchmarked. In this work we use lattice improvement to remove lattice errors for a one-dimensional system of bosons with zero-range interactions. We construct the improved lattice action up to next-to-next-to-leading order and verify that the remaining errors scale as the fourth power of the lattice spacing for observables involving as many as five particles. Our results provide a guide for increasing the accuracy of future calculations in lattice effective field theory with improved lattice actions.

## Key claim

The paper systematically benchmarks the Symanzik lattice improvement program for lattice effective field theory, demonstrating that for a one-dimensional system of bosons with zero-range interactions, the remaining lattice errors scale as the fourth power of the lattice spacing at next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO) for observables involving up to five particles, but the authors note that this analysis is for a simple system and that in general the renormalization group will be needed to compute anomalous dimensions that modify the naive dimensional analysis.

## Method

The authors constructed a lattice action for one-dimensional bosons with zero-range interactions at leading order (LO), next-to-leading order (NLO), and next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO) using the Symanzik improvement program. They computed bound-state energies, root-mean-square radii, effective masses, and inverse scattering lengths for up to five bosons on lattices with varying lattice spacing, fitting the results to an asymptotic function O(a) = O_c + A_O * a^{b_O} to extract the error exponent b_O. The lattice parameters at N2LO were fit to simultaneously reproduce the continuum two-boson bound-state energy, two-boson effective range, two-boson effective mass, and three-boson bound-state energy.

## Key numbers

*   LO lattice errors for bound-state energies and radii of up to five bosons have exponent b_O consistent with 1 (e.g., two-boson energy exponent 0.974(6), five-boson energy exponent 0.94(2)).
*   NLO lattice errors for bound-state energies and radii have exponent b_O about 3 (e.g., two-boson radius exponent 3.05(2), five-boson energy exponent 3.21(4)).
*   N2LO lattice errors for bound-state energies and radii have exponent b_O about 4 (e.g., two-boson radius exponent 4.1(3), five-boson energy exponent 3.9(2)).
*   The two-boson inverse scattering length 1/a_2 has LO exponent 0.978(6) and NLO exponent 2.99(6).
*   The dimer-boson inverse scattering length 1/a_3 has LO exponent 2.80(3), NLO exponent 2.93(6), and N2LO exponent 4.08(3).
*   The continuum two-boson bound-state energy is -2.347 MeV, and the five-boson bound-state energy is -46.946 MeV, computed with C_0 = -0.1 and m = 938.92 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Symanzik improvement program, widely used in lattice quantum chromodynamics (Luscher 1984, Sheikholeslami 1985), to lattice effective field theory calculations of few-body and many-body systems. It systematically benchmarks the effectiveness of lattice improvement, which had not been done previously, and provides a guide for increasing accuracy in future lattice effective field theory calculations, particularly for nuclear forces and structure (Epelbaum 2011, Elhatisari 2016). The results enable more precise lattice simulations of systems with compact bound states, where lattice spacing errors grow with particle number, and suggest that four-boson contact interactions could be useful for removing lattice errors in systems with four or more bosons.
