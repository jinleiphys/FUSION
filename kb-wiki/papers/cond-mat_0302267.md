# A New Thermodynamics, From Nuclei to Stars

> Equilibrium statistics of Hamiltonian systems is correctly described by the microcanonical ensemble. Classically this is the manifold of all points in the $N-$body phase space with the given total energy. Due to Boltzmann's principle, $e^S=tr(δ(E-H))$, its geometrical size is related to the entropy $S(E,N,...)$. This definition does not invoke any information theory, no thermodynamic limit, no extensivity, and no homogeneity assumption, as are needed in conventional (canonical) thermo-statistics. Therefore, it describes the equilibrium statistics of extensive as well of non-extensive systems. Due to this fact it is the {\em fundamental} definition of any classical equilibrium statistics. It can address nuclei and astrophysical objects as well. All kind of phase transitions can be distinguished sharply and uniquely for even small systems. For transitions in nuclear physics the scaling to an hypothetical uncharged nuclear matter with an $N/Z-$ ratio like realistic nuclei is not needed.

## Key claim

The microcanonical ensemble, defined directly via Boltzmann's principle \(e^S = \text{tr}(\delta(E-H))\), is the fundamental definition of equilibrium statistics and correctly describes both extensive and non-extensive systems without requiring the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or homogeneity assumptions that are necessary in conventional canonical thermo-statistics.

## Method

The paper develops a geometric foundation for thermodynamics based on the topology of the Hessian (determinant of curvatures) of the microcanonical entropy \(S(E,N,\cdots)\) as a function of energy and particle number. Phase transitions are identified by analyzing the curvature eigenvalues: a positive largest eigenvalue \(\lambda_1 > 0\) indicates first-order phase separation with a convex intruder in the entropy surface, while \(\lambda_1 = 0\) with an extremum in the direction of the order parameter signals a continuous (second-order) transition. The method is demonstrated on the Potts-gas model on a \(50 \times 50\) lattice, on sodium clusters via MMMC simulations, and on self-gravitating rotating systems.

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 940\) K for \(N_0 = 200\) atoms, rising to \(T_{tr} = 1095\) K for \(N_0 = 3000\) atoms, compared to the bulk value of 1156 K.
- Latent heat per atom for sodium clusters: \(q_{lat} = 0.82\) eV for \(N_0 = 200\), \(q_{lat} = 0.94\) eV for \(N_0 = 3000\), compared to the bulk value of 0.923 eV.
- Surface tension parameter \(\sigma/T_{tr}\) for sodium clusters: 2.75 for \(N_0 = 200\), rising to 7.07 for \(N_0 = 3000\), compared to the bulk value of 7.41.
- The experimental excitation energy per nucleon \(e^*\) versus apparent temperature \(T_{app}\) is shown for 701 MeV \(^{28}\text{Si} + ^{100}\text{Mo}\) reactions, with Fermi-gas caloric curves for level-density parameters \(a = 6\) to 12.

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical approach to thermodynamics beyond the conventional canonical framework that requires the thermodynamic limit, as established by Yang-Lee theory and the Gibbs canonical ensemble. It directly addresses the problem that conventional scaling theory for nuclear phase transitions requires extrapolation to hypothetical uncharged nuclear matter with fixed \(N/Z\) ratio, which does not exist in reality due to infinite Coulomb self-energy. The paper enables the treatment of non-extensive systems including atomic clusters (extending work by Brechignac et al. (1995) on sodium clusters), nuclear multifragmentation (referencing d'Agostino et al. (2000) and Chbihi et al. (1999)), and self-gravitating astrophysical systems (referencing Votyakov, Hidmi, De Martino, Gross (2002) on rotating self-gravitating systems).
