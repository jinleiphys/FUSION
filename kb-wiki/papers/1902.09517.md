# Relativistic Hydrodynamic Fluctuations

> We present a general systematic formalism for describing dynamics of fluctuations in an arbitrary relativistic hydrodynamic flow, including their feedback (known as long-time hydrodynamic tails). The fluctuations are described by two-point equal-time correlation functions. We introduce a definition of equal time in a situation where the local rest frame is determined by the local flow velocity, and a method of taking derivatives and Wigner transforms of such equal-time correlation functions, which we call confluent. We find that the equations for confluent Wigner functions not only resemble kinetic equations, but that the kinetic equation for phonons propagating on an arbitrary background nontrivially matches the equations for Wigner functions, including relativistic inertial and Coriolis forces due to acceleration and vorticity of the flow. We also describe the procedure of renormalization of short-distance singularities which eliminates cutoff dependence, allowing efficient numerical implementation of these equations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a general systematic formalism for describing the dynamics of fluctuations in arbitrary relativistic hydrodynamic flows, including their feedback (long-time hydrodynamic tails), by introducing "confluent" derivatives and Wigner transforms that account for the changing local rest frame of the fluid. The key advance is the ability to cleanly separate short-range singularities due to "infinite noise" and absorb them into renormalization of variables, equation of state, and transport coefficients, which the authors contrast with the stochastic approach also pursued in the literature.

## Method

The authors derive coupled evolution equations for hydrodynamic variables (energy density and flow velocity) and the two-point equal-time correlation functions of their fluctuations, expressed as Wigner transforms. They introduce a "confluent" derivative that accounts for the changing local rest frame when taking derivatives of equal-time correlators, and define confluent Wigner functions by integrating over the 3-dimensional hyperplane orthogonal to the local flow velocity. The formalism is derived from linearized stochastic hydrodynamic equations with noise terms governed by the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, and the resulting equations for Wigner functions resemble kinetic equations, with the longitudinal mode fluctuations exactly matching kinetic equations for phonon gas on an arbitrary non-uniform flow background.

## Key numbers

- The range of correlations in hydrodynamics is of order $\sqrt{\gamma L/c_s}$, where $L$ is the homogeneity scale and $c_s$ is the speed of sound, with the corresponding "hard" momentum $p \sim 1/\sqrt{\gamma L/c_s}$ being much larger than the "soft" momentum $k = 1/L$
- The shear viscosity parameter is defined as $\gamma_\eta = \eta / w$ and the bulk viscosity parameter as $\gamma_\zeta = \zeta / w$, where $w$ is enthalpy density
- The noise strength is set by the fluctuation-dissipation theorem: $\langle S^{\mu\nu}(x) S^{\lambda\kappa}(x') \rangle = 2T [\eta (\Delta^{\mu\kappa}\Delta^{\nu\lambda} + \Delta^{\mu\lambda}\Delta^{\nu\kappa}) + (\zeta - \frac{2}{3}\eta) \Delta^{\mu\nu}\Delta^{\lambda\kappa}] \delta^{(4)}(x-x')$

## Context

This work extends the non-relativistic approach of Andreev (1978) to the relativistic regime, addressing the quintessentially relativistic issue of frame-dependence of simultaneity in defining equal-time correlators. The authors derive kinetic equations for phonon distribution functions that Andreev (1978) had postulated without derivation, and show that while the collision/relaxation term for longitudinal modes takes the simplest form assumed by Andreev, the kinetic equation for transverse modes contains flow gradient terms that were assumed absent in that prior work. The formalism enables numerical simulations of heavy-ion collisions or other relativistic many-body systems where fluctuations are important, including effects of vorticity that are absent in Bjorken boost-invariant flow but important in heavy-ion collisions as studied by STAR (2017). The approach also connects to the Hydro+ formalism for critical fluctuations developed by Stephanov (2018) and to Schwinger-Keldysh effective field theory approaches to hydrodynamics.
