# Theoretical considerations about heavy-ion fusion in potential scattering

> We carefully compare the one-dimensional WKB barrier tunneling model, and the one-channel Schödinger equation with a complex optical potential calculation of heavy-ion fusion, for a light and a heavy system. It is found that the major difference between the two approaches occurs around the critical energy, above which the effective potential for the grazing angular momentum ceases to exhibit a pocket. The value of this critical energy is shown to be strongly dependent on the nuclear potential at short distances, on the inside region of the Coulomb barrier, and this dependence is much more important for heavy systems. Therefore the nuclear fusion process is expected to provide information on the nuclear potential in this inner region. We compare calculations with available data to show that the results are consistent with this expectation.

# Theoretical considerations about heavy-ion fusion in potential scattering

## Key claim

The major difference between one-dimensional WKB barrier tunneling and one-channel Schrödinger equation with a complex optical potential for heavy-ion fusion occurs around the critical energy, above which the effective potential for the grazing angular momentum ceases to exhibit a pocket; this critical energy depends strongly on the nuclear potential at short distances inside the Coulomb barrier, with a much more important dependence for heavy systems.

## Method

The authors compared fusion cross sections calculated using the one-dimensional WKB barrier tunneling model (with Kemble's transmission coefficient and the Hill-Wheeler approximation) against those from solving the one-channel Schrödinger equation with a complex optical potential, for both a light and a heavy system. They employed two versions of the folding model for the real nuclear potential: the São Paulo potential (SPP) and the Akyüz-Winther potential (AW). Absorption was treated either through a Woods-Saxon imaginary potential with short-range parameters (W0 = -50 MeV, r0i = 1.0 fm, ai = 0.20 fm) or through ingoing wave boundary conditions (IWBC) at the inner turning point.

## Key numbers

- The critical angular momentum lcr is defined by the condition ωl = sqrt[-Vl''(Rl)/μ] = 0, where the pocket in the l-dependent potential disappears.
- The critical energy Ecr is given by Ecr = B(lcr), the barrier height for the critical partial wave.
- For the imaginary potential representing fusion absorption, the authors used a Woods-Saxon form with strength W0 = -50 MeV, radius parameter r0i = 1.0 fm, and diffusivity ai = 0.20 fm.
- For the imaginary potential representing total reaction absorption (including direct reactions), the authors used λ = 0.78 times the real São Paulo potential, following Gasques et al. (2006).
- The Hill-Wheeler transmission coefficient is T_HW(l,E) = 1 / [1 + exp(2π(Bl - E)/ħωl)].
- The classical approximation for fusion cross section above Ecr gives σF(E) ≈ σ0 × (Ecr/E), with σ0 = πħ²(lcr+1)²/(2μE).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical comparison between WKB tunneling and optical model treatments of heavy-ion fusion, building on the Feshbach formalism for the optical potential and the concept of compound nucleus formation. The authors reference the CCFULL code (Hagino, Rowley, Kruppa, 1999) which uses IWBC, and the work of Gasques et al. (2006) on the São Paulo potential with λ = 0.78. The paper clarifies that the Moldauer-Simonius formula (Moldauer, 1967; Simonius, 1974) governs the compound nucleus formation probability, and that for collisions above Ecr with l > lcr, absorption occurs without compound nucleus formation. This enables future work to extract information about the nuclear potential in the inner region of the Coulomb barrier from fusion data, particularly for heavy systems.
