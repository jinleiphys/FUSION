# Toward a Global Dispersive Optical Model for the Driplines

> A dispersive-optical-model analysis has been performed for both protons and neutrons on 40,42,44,48Ca isotopes. The fitted potentials describe accurately both scattering and bound quantities and extrapolate well to other stable nuclei. Further experimental information will be gathered to constrain extrapolations toward the driplines.

## Key claim

A dispersive-optical-model (DOM) analysis fitted to calcium isotopes (40,42,44,48Ca) can describe both scattering and bound-state data with 25 free parameters across 81 data sets, but extrapolations to neutron-rich dripline isotopes require better constraints on the isospin asymmetry dependence of the neutron imaginary surface potential, which the available n+40Ca data alone cannot determine.

## Method

The authors performed a local DOM parametrization of the nucleon self-energy, separating the real Hartree-Fock-like potential at the Fermi surface from the dynamic real and imaginary components linked by a subtracted dispersion relation. They fitted this potential to published elastic scattering data for p+40,42,44,48Ca and n+40Ca up to 200 MeV, total reaction cross sections, and bound-state properties (single-particle levels, spectroscopic factors, radii) from (e,e'p) experiments. The fit used 25 free parameters to describe 3569 data points across 81 data sets.

## Key numbers

- 25 free parameters used to fit 3569 data points from 81 data sets for Ca isotopes
- Elastic scattering data included for p+40,42,44,48Ca and n+40Ca with energies up to 200 MeV
- Only one data point could not be reproduced: the s1/2 proton hole state in 40Ca (later corrected by an independent re-analysis of (e,e'p) data)
- The asymmetry dependence of the surface imaginary potential is parameterized as W_s(E) = W_s^0(E) + ε (N-Z)/A W_s^1(E), with ε_p = +1 for protons; for neutrons, ε_n = 0 was found consistent with (d,p) spectroscopic factors but other values are not ruled out

## Context

This work extends the DOM approach of Mahaux and collaborators (1991) for individual magic nuclei to a chain of calcium isotopes, building on the authors' own prior studies (Charity 2006, 2007) that analyzed the world data on 40-48Ca. The fitted potential extrapolates well to stable nickel isotopes (preliminary comparison shown), suggesting potential for global fits. The key open question is constraining the neutron asymmetry parameter ε_n, which requires new n+48Ca scattering data (recently taken) to improve predictions for neutron-rich dripline isotopes.
