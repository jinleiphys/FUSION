# An Undergraduate Approach to the Quantum Hadrodynamics and the Physics of Neutron Stars

> In this tutorial, I discuss how to model a neutron star from the Quantum Hadrodynamics microscopic approach. After a brief discussion about hydrostatic equilibrium, I discuss the role of each meson of the model and how to calculate the corresponding equation of state and the expected values. Each meson is introduced individually. Its effects are analyzed from both an analytical and a numerical point of view. To explicitly show the effects of a given meson, the coupling constant is varied in an arbitrary range before being fixed to reproduce well-known constraints. This work is intended for late undergraduate students as well as early graduate students. The equation of states is obtained from the statistical mechanics formalism, which is more familiar to students at this stage of their research career, instead of the traditional quantum field theory formalism.

# An Undergraduate Approach to the Quantum Hadrodynamics and the Physics of Neutron Stars

## Key claim

This tutorial demonstrates that a neutron star model built from Quantum Hadrodynamics can be made accessible to late undergraduate and early graduate students by using statistical mechanics formalism instead of quantum field theory, but the coupling constants for each meson are initially varied in an arbitrary range before being fixed to reproduce well-known constraints, meaning the pedagogical presentation prioritizes conceptual clarity over predictive accuracy until constraints are applied.

## Method

The author constructs the equation of state (EOS) for neutron star matter by incrementally introducing mesons in the Quantum Hadrodynamics mean-field approximation, starting with free neutron matter, then adding the scalar sigma meson for attraction, and finally the vector omega meson for repulsion in the sigma-omega model. Each meson's coupling constant is varied in an arbitrary range to demonstrate its individual effect on the EOS and the resulting mass-radius relation from solving the Oppenheimer-Volkoff (OV) equations. The Lagrangian formalism is used throughout, with mesonic fields replaced by their classical expectation values in the mean-field approximation, and the energy density is minimized at fixed baryon density to determine the field values self-consistently.

## Key numbers

- Free neutron matter maximum mass: 0.71 solar masses, matching the original Oppenheimer-Volkoff result from 1939
- Neutron mass used in calculations: approximately 939 MeV
- Sigma meson mass: 512 MeV (based on the f0(500) resonance)
- Omega meson mass: 783 MeV
- The most massive well-measured pulsar PSR J0740+6620 has a mass of 2.08 ± 0.07 solar masses and a radius in the range of 11.41 km to 13.69 km (Riley 2021, Miller 2021)
- Neutron star interior temperatures are below 0.1 MeV, justifying the T = 0 K approximation
- Fermi energy of neutrons in neutron star interiors can reach values above 1 GeV

## Context

This tutorial fills a pedagogical gap between advanced Quantum Hadrodynamics texts such as Serot (1992) and introductory neutron star texts such as Silbar (2004) and Sagert (2006), which do not cover QHD. The work extends the original Oppenheimer-Volkoff calculation from 1939 by incorporating nuclear interactions through the sigma-omega model introduced by Walecka (1974), and enables students to understand how modern neutron star observations constrain nuclear matter models. The step-by-step approach, introducing one meson at a time, allows students to see how the attractive sigma meson softens the EOS and reduces maximum mass, while the repulsive omega meson stiffens it, ultimately enabling realistic EOSs that can reach the observed 2 solar mass neutron stars.
