# Direct dark matter detection: The diurnal variation in directional experiments

> We present some theoretical results relevant to the direct dark matter detection experiments, paying particular attention to directional experiments, i.e. experiments in which, not only the energy but the direction of the recoiling nucleus is observed. In directional experiments the detection rate depends on the angle between the line observation and the sun's direction of motion. Since, however, the direction of observation is fixed with respect the earth, while the Earth is rotating around its axis, in a directional experiment the angle between the direction of observation and the Sun's direction of motion will change during the day. So the observed signal in such experiments will exhibit a very interesting and characteristic periodic diurnal variation.

## Key claim

Directional dark matter experiments can exhibit a characteristic diurnal variation in the event rate, with a period of 24 hours, due to the Earth's rotation changing the angle between the fixed direction of observation and the Sun's direction of motion; this variation is independent of the Earth's rotational velocity and its amplitude depends on the inclination of observation (the angle between the direction of observation and the Earth's rotation axis).

## Method

The authors compute the differential and total event rates for WIMP-nucleus elastic scattering, first for standard (non-directional) experiments and then for directional experiments where the recoil direction is measured. They assume a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution for WIMPs in the galactic frame and fold it with the Earth's motion around the Sun (annual modulation) and the Earth's rotation (diurnal effect). The directional rate is expressed in terms of a parameter κ (the ratio of the directional to non-directional time-independent rate) and modulation parameters h_c and h_s, which depend on the observation angles (Θ, Φ) in the galactic frame; these are then transformed to the equatorial coordinate system (right ascension ᾶ, inclination δ̃) to compute the diurnal variation.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of Earth's orbital velocity to the Sun's velocity around the galaxy is δ = υ₁/υ₀ = 0.136.
- The escape velocity in the galactic frame is υ_esc = 2.84 υ₀ (y_esc = 2.84).
- The energy transfer unit is Q₀ = 40 A⁻⁴/³ MeV.
- The coherent total event rate is computed assuming a nucleon cross section σ_n = 10⁻⁴³ cm² = 10⁻⁷ pb for targets including ³²S and ¹³¹Xe, with results shown as a function of WIMP mass in GeV (figures 2a and 2b).
- The galactic axis unit vector components in the equatorial system are: ŷ = -0.868 î - 0.198 ĵ + 0.456 k̂.
- The Sun's direction of motion unit vector components are: ẑ = 0.494 î - 0.445 ĵ + 0.747 k̂.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Vergados 2003, 2004; Vergados and Faessler 2007) on the angular dependence of directional event rates, and builds on the suggestion (Cygnus 2009) that diurnal variation could be observable. The paper provides a theoretical framework for directional dark matter experiments (such as those using CS₂ or iodine targets) to exploit the diurnal variation as a background discrimination tool, complementing the annual modulation effect already known from non-directional experiments. The results enable experimentalists to optimize the inclination of observation (negative inclinations are preferred) and target choice (lighter targets show larger time variation) for future directional detectors.
