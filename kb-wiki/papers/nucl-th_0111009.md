# Mean-field analysis of interacting boson models with random interactions

> We investigate the origin of the regular features observed in numerical studies of the interacting boson model with random interactions, in particular the dominance of L=0 ground states and the occurrence of vibrational and rotational band structures. It is shown that all of these properties can be interpreted and explained in terms of a Hartree-Bose mean-field analysis, in which different regions of the parameter space are associated with geometric shapes. The same conclusions hold for the vibron model.

# Mean-field analysis of interacting boson models with random interactions

## Key claim
The regular features observed in numerical studies of the interacting boson model (IBM) with random interactions, specifically the dominance of L=0 ground states and the occurrence of vibrational and rotational band structures, can be explained by a Hartree-Bose mean-field analysis that associates different regions of the parameter space with distinct geometric shapes (spherical, deformed, and d-boson condensate), rather than requiring fine-tuned nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Method
The authors performed a Hartree-Bose mean-field analysis using axially symmetric coherent states to compute potential energy surfaces for the most general one- and two-body IBM Hamiltonian, where the nine Hamiltonian coefficients were chosen independently from a Gaussian distribution with zero mean. The equilibrium configuration for each Hamiltonian was determined by finding the minimum of the energy surface, classifying the resulting shapes into three classes: spherical (s-boson condensate), deformed (axially symmetric rotor), and d-boson condensate. The angular momentum of the ground state for each configuration was then obtained from the rotational structure combined with the Thouless-Valatin formula for moments of inertia.

## Key numbers
- Spherical condensate occurs in 39.4% of cases, always giving L=0 ground states
- Deformed condensate occurs in 36.8% of cases: 23.7% give L=0 ground states (positive moment of inertia), 13.1% give L=2N ground states (negative moment of inertia)
- d-boson condensate occurs in 23.8% of cases, with ground state angular momentum depending on N and signs of two moments of inertia
- Total L=0 ground state dominance ranges from approximately 63% to 77% depending on boson number N
- For N=3k, there is an enhancement of L=0 ground states and a corresponding decrease in L=2 ground states
- The probability distribution P(R) of the energy ratio R = (E(4_1)-E(0_1))/(E(2_1)-E(0_1)) shows peaks at the vibrational value R=2 and the rotational value R=10/3
- For the vibron model: spherical condensate probability = 0.396, deformed condensate probability = 0.216, p-boson condensate probability = 0.388
- Vibron model L=0 ground state dominance oscillates between approximately 53% (odd N) and 71% (even N)

## Context
This work extends previous numerical studies of the IBM with random interactions by Bijker and Frank (2001) and others, which had shown surprising regular features despite random interactions. The authors provide an intuitive geometric explanation that contrasts with earlier attempts to explain these phenomena through time-reversal invariance, width of energy distributions, or geometric chaoticity of angular momentum coupling as studied by Mulhall, Volya, and Zelevinsky (2000). The analysis demonstrates that the regular features arise from the occurrence of robust geometric phases (spherical and deformed shapes) rather than from details of angular momentum coupling, and the same conclusions hold for the vibron model used in molecular and cluster physics. The authors note that these results cannot be directly applied to fermion systems and are exploring nucleon-pair truncation schemes to address that case.
