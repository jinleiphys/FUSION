# Axion-induced effects in atoms, molecules and nuclei: Parity nonconservation, anapole moments, electric dipole moments, and spin-gravity and spin-axion momentum couplings

> We show that the interaction of an axion field, or in general a pseudoscalar field, with the axial-vector current generated by an electron through a derivative-type coupling can give rise to a time-dependent mixing of opposite-parity states in atomic and molecular systems. Likewise, the analogous interaction of an axion field with the axial-vector current generated by a nucleon can give rise to time-dependent mixing of opposite-parity states in nuclear systems. This mixing can induce oscillating electric dipole moments, oscillating parity non-conservation effects and oscillating anapole moments in such systems. By adjusting the energy separation between the opposite-parity states of interest to match the axion mass energy, axion-induced experimental observables can be enhanced by many orders of magnitude. Oscillating atomic electric dipole moments can also be generated by axions through hadronic mechanisms, namely the P,T-violating nucleon-nucleon interaction and through the axion-induced electric dipole moments of valence nucleons, which comprise the nuclei. The axion field is modified by the Earth's gravitational field. The interaction of the spin of either an electron or nucleon with this modified axion field leads to axion-induced observable effects. These effects, which are of the form $\mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbfσ$, differ from the axion-wind effect, which has the form $\mathbf{p}_{\textrm{a}} \cdot \mathbfσ$.

## Key claim

The interaction of a background axion (or pseudoscalar) field with the axial-vector current of electrons or nucleons via a derivative-type coupling can induce time-dependent mixing of opposite-parity states in atoms, molecules, and nuclei, producing oscillating electric dipole moments, parity nonconservation effects, and anapole moments; these effects can be resonantly enhanced by many orders of magnitude when the energy separation between opposite-parity states is tuned to match the axion mass energy.

## Method

The authors apply first-order time-dependent perturbation theory to a two-level subspace of opposite-parity eigenstates in atomic, molecular, or nuclear systems, using the non-relativistic limit of the axion-fermion interaction Lagrangian. They derive the time-dependent perturbed wavefunctions for both limiting regimes (axion mass much smaller or much larger than the energy splitting) and obtain expressions for the oscillating electric dipole moment in terms of the dynamic polarisability. For hadronic mechanisms, they extend the analysis to nuclear systems, specifically considering the P,T-violating nucleon-nucleon interaction and axion-induced electric dipole moments of valence nucleons.

## Key numbers

- Local cold dark matter density taken as ρ_CDM ≈ 7.6 × 10⁻⁴ eV⁴ (0.4 GeV/cm³), giving axion field amplitude a₀ ≈ 4 × 10² eV for m_a ≈ 10⁻⁴ eV and f_a ≈ 10²⁰ eV
- Estimated oscillating EDM magnitude for hydrogen: |d_a| ≈ 7 × 10⁻⁴⁰ e·cm × sin(m_a t)
- Estimated oscillating EDM magnitude for lithium: |d_a| ≈ 3 × 10⁻³⁸ e·cm × sin(m_a t)
- Solar System galactic rotation speed: v_rot ≈ 240 km/s, giving axion root-mean-squared velocity v_rms ≈ 290 km/s
- Axion mass range considered: 10⁻⁶ – 1 eV

## Context

This work extends the axion detection schemes proposed by Graham et al. (2013) and Victor et al. (2013), which focused on energy level shifts from the axion-wind effect (p_a·σ coupling), by identifying additional observable signatures: oscillating electric dipole moments, parity nonconservation, and anapole moments. The authors show that the temporal component of the axion-fermion interaction (∂_t φ term) produces a P-odd mixing analogous to the neutral weak interaction studied by Bouchiat (1974) and Khriplovich (1984). The results enable searches for axionic dark matter using existing experimental setups for static electric dipole moment measurements, particularly in ¹⁹⁹Hg (currently the most sensitive diamagnetic atom, per Romalis 2009, 2013) and ²²⁵Ra (which offers several orders-of-magnitude enhancement), as well as in alkali atoms like caesium.
