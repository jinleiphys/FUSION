# Gluonic excitations in lattice QCD: A brief survey

> Our current knowledge about glueballs and hybrid mesons from lattice QCD simulations is briefly reviewed.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD simulations have provided a well-determined glueball spectrum in the pure gauge theory and validated a leading-order Born-Oppenheimer picture for heavy-quark hybrid mesons, but the inclusion of light sea quark effects remains a major open question and may significantly alter the spectrum of hybrid states.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD calculations of glueball masses using improved gauge actions on anisotropic lattices with variational techniques applied to correlation function matrices, and heavy-quark hybrid meson spectra obtained from static quark potentials in the leading Born-Oppenheimer (LBO) approximation. For heavy-quark systems, the validity of the LBO picture is tested by comparing its level splittings with those from non-relativistic QCD (NRQCD) simulations that include higher-order spin interactions.

## Key numbers

- Scalar glueball total width for decays to two pseudoscalars: 108(29) MeV, obtained in the quenched approximation at β=5.7 (Wilson gauge action)
- Continuum limit mixing energy of scalar glueball with quarkonium at the strange quark mass: 43(31) MeV, consistent with zero mixing
- LBO predictions for conventional bottomonium states agree with spin-averaged experimental measurements below the BB̅ threshold
- NRQCD level splittings for conventional 2S, 1P and four hybrid states agree with LBO predictions within 10%
- Hybrid admixture probability in the Υ from NRQCD simulations: 0.0035(1)c_B² where c_B² ~ 1.5–3
- Light-quark 1⁻⁺ hybrid meson mass estimates from multiple lattice groups: 1.87(20) GeV (UKQCD 97), 1.97(9)(30) GeV (MILC 97), 2.11(10) GeV (MILC 99), 1.9(2) GeV (LaSch 99 with N_f=2)
- Charmonium 1⁻⁺ – 1S mass splittings: 1.34(8)(20) GeV (MILC 97), 1.22(15) GeV (MILC 99), 1.323(13) GeV (CP-PACS 99), 1.19 GeV (JKM 99 LBO)

## Context

This survey extends the quenched glueball calculations of Bali (2000) and the GF11 collaboration, and the SU(N) scaling studies of Teper (1999). The heavy-quark hybrid results build on the Born-Oppenheimer approach of Hasenfratz (1980) and the static potential calculations of Juge, Kuti, and Morningstar (1999). The paper identifies the critical open question of whether light sea quark effects, which cause string breaking at separations around 1 fm (shown by SESAM and the string-breaking collaboration), will spoil the Born-Oppenheimer picture for heavy-quark hybrids, and notes that all light-quark hybrid mass estimates near 2.0 GeV are well above experimental candidates at 1.4–1.6 GeV.
