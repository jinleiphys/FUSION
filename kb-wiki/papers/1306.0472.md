# Numerical integration of thermal noise in relativistic hydrodynamics

> Thermal fluctuations affect the dynamics of systems near critical points, the evolution of the early universe, and two-particle correlations in heavy-ion collisions. For the latter, numerical simulations of nearly-ideal, relativistic fluids are necessary. The correlation functions of noise in relativistic fluids are calculated, stochastic integration of the noise in 3+1-dimensional viscous hydrodynamics is implemented, and the effect of noise on observables in heavy-ion collisions is discussed. Thermal fluctuations will cause significant variance in the event-by-event distributions of integrated v2 while changing average values even when using the same initial conditions, suggesting that including thermal noise will lead to refitting of the hydrodynamical parameters with implications for understanding the physics of hot QCD.

## Key claim

Thermal fluctuations in relativistic hydrodynamics cause significant variance in event-by-event distributions of integrated elliptic flow \(v_2\) and change average values even when using identical initial conditions, which will require refitting of hydrodynamical parameters; however, the averaged noise diverges as \(1/\sqrt{\Delta V \Delta \tau}\) with decreasing cell size, making exceedingly fine resolutions nonsensical, and the continuum limit does not converge as it does in dissipative hydrodynamics without fluctuations.

## Method

The fluctuation-dissipation relation is used to derive the autocorrelation function for thermal noise in Israel-Stewart causal hydrodynamics, showing that the noise can be implemented as white noise in the Israel-Stewart equations. The stochastic differential equations with multiplicative noise are integrated numerically using Heun's method, which approximates the Stratonovich integral without requiring drift-correction terms, within the 3+1-dimensional viscous hydrodynamical code music. The noise is sampled as a traceless symmetric tensor with Gaussian distribution in each cell, with the correlation function \(\left \langle \xi^{\mu \nu}(x) \xi^{\rho \sigma}(x^{\prime}) \right \rangle = [2\eta T(\Delta^{\mu \rho}\Delta^{\nu \sigma}+\Delta^{\mu \sigma} \Delta^{\nu \rho}) + 2(\zeta-2\eta/3)T\Delta^{\mu \nu} \Delta^{\rho \sigma}] \delta^4(x-x^{\prime})\).

## Key numbers

* For a central lead-lead collision at \(\sqrt{s}/A = 2.76\;{\rm TeV}\) at the LHC, the energy density at the center of the transverse plane is approximately \(132\;{\rm GeV/fm^3}\), entropy density \(s=337\;{\rm fm^{-3}}\), pressure \(p=41\;{\rm GeV/fm^3}\), and temperature \(T=513\;{\rm GeV}\).
* With shear viscosity \(\eta_s/s = 0.08\), thermalization time approximately \(0.5\;{\rm fm}\), and cell resolutions of about \(0.5\;{\rm fm}\) in transverse directions, each component of the cell-averaged \(\Xi^{ij}\) has a root mean square of \(\approx \sqrt{2\eta_s T/(\Delta V \Delta \tau)} = 6.6 \;{\rm GeV/fm^3}\).
* Comparing this noise magnitude to the pressure \(p = 41\;{\rm GeV/fm^3}\) suggests the variance of flow and \(v_2\) caused by thermal noise in the most central class of lead-lead collisions at the LHC may be on the order of 15\%.
* In 1+1-dimensional boost-invariant hydrodynamics with parameters appropriate for the LHC, a small but non-zero two-particle correlation \(K(\Delta y)\) was found up to pseudorapidity gap \(\Delta y=4\).

## Context

This work extends the analytic calculation of thermal fluctuations in relativistic fluids by Kapusta (2011), who found the autocorrelation of the energy-momentum tensor in the Landau-Lifshitz frame and applied it to 1+1-dimensional boost-invariant hydrodynamics. It also builds on the work of Kovtun (2011), who showed that thermal fluctuations lead to an effective viscosity with a term proportional to \(1/\eta_{\rm cl}^2\) causing a lower bound. The paper enables numerical simulations of thermal noise in 3+1-dimensional viscous hydrodynamics for heavy-ion collisions, allowing comparisons with experimental data from RHIC and the LHC, and suggests that thermal fluctuations can provide an independent measurement of viscosity complementary to collective flow measurements.
