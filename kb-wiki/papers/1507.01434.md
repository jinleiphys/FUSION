# The Physics Of Supernova Neutrino Oscillations

> On February 23, 1987 we collected 24 neutrinos from the explosion of a blue super-giant star in the Large Magellanic Cloud confirming the basic paradigm of core-collapse supernova. During the many years we have been waiting for a repeat of that momentous day, the number and size of neutrino detectors around the world has grown considerably. If the neutrinos from the next supernova in our Galaxy arrive tomorrow we shall collect upwards of tens of thousands of events and next generation detectors will increase the amount of data we collect by more than an order of magnitude. But it is also now apparent that the message is much more complex than previously thought because many time, energy and neutrino flavor dependent features are imprinted upon the signal either at emission or by the passage through the outer layers of the star. These features arise due to the explosion dynamics, the physics of nuclei at high temperatures and densities, and the properties of neutrinos. In this proceedings I will present some aspects of the physics of supernova neutrino oscillations and what we should expect to observe when the neutrinos from the next Galactic supernova (eventually) arrive.

## Key claim

The neutrino signal from the next Galactic supernova will be much more complex than previously thought because many time-, energy-, and flavor-dependent features are imprinted upon the signal by explosion dynamics, nuclear physics, and neutrino properties; however, the ability to decode this signal depends on understanding multiple effects including neutrino self interactions, turbulence, and potential beyond-Standard-Model physics, many of which remain theoretically challenging.

## Method

The paper reviews the four epochs of core-collapse supernova neutrino emission (precollapse, collapse, accretion, and cooling) and the flavor transformation mechanisms that distort the emitted spectra as neutrinos propagate to Earth. For the specific case of turbulence, the author describes a method where density profiles from turbulence-free 1D hydrodynamical simulations are multiplied by a random field \(1+F(r)\), with \(F(r)\) realized as a Fourier series with Gaussian statistics, rms amplitude \(C_\star\), and an inverse power-law power spectrum. The Rotating Wave Approximation is then applied to treat the turbulent component as a perturbation, yielding an analytic solution for the two-flavor transition probability \(P_{12} = \frac{|\kappa|^2}{Q^2} \sin^2(Qr)\).

## Key numbers

- A core-collapse supernova at 10 kpc distance will produce of order \(10^4\) neutrino events in the SuperKamiokande detector.
- Approximately \(10^{58}\) neutrinos are emitted from a core-collapse supernova over \(\sim 10\) seconds.
- The gravitational binding energy released during collapse is of order \(10^{53}\) erg.
- The core contracts from \(r \sim 1000\) km down to \(r \sim 20\) km in \(\sim 200\) milliseconds.
- The standing accretion shock instability (SASI) introduces luminosity fluctuations of order \(10\%\) with periodicity of \(\sim 10\) milliseconds.
- The Lepton-number Emission Self-sustained Asymmetry (LESA) produces a dipole of order \(10\%\) in the \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar{\nu}_e\) fluxes from 3D simulations.
- The IceCube detector has time resolution of order 1 ms, enabling observation of SASI in the neutrino signal.
- The cooling phase of the proto-neutron star lasts \(10-20\) seconds.

## Context

This proceedings paper from CIPANP2015 reviews the state of supernova neutrino physics following the 1987 detection of 24 neutrinos from SN 1987A, extending the work reviewed by Scholberg (2012). It highlights recent developments including the LESA phenomenon found by Tamborra et al. in 3D simulations, the analytic treatment of turbulence by Patton, Kneller and McLaughlin (2014, 2015), and the possibility of matter-neutrino resonances (MNR) with non-standard interactions first studied in neutron star merger contexts by Malkus et al. (2012, 2014). The paper enables future work by identifying the need for high-resolution, long-duration multi-dimensional simulations to calibrate turbulence expectations and by opening the investigation of NSI effects on supernova neutrino signals and nucleosynthesis.
