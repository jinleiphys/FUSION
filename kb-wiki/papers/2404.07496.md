# Multifractal Dimension Spectrum Analysis for Nuclear Density Distribution

> We present an integral density method for calculating the multifractal dimension spectrum for the nucleon distribution in atomic nuclei. This method is then applied to analyze the non-uniformity of the density distribution in several typical types of nuclear matter distributions, including the Woods-Saxon distribution, the halo structure and the tetrahedral $α$ clustering. The subsequent discussion provides a comprehensive and detailed exploration of the results obtained. The multifractal dimension spectrum shows remarkable sensitivity to the density distribution, establishing it as an effective tool for studying the distribution of nucleons in nuclear multibody systems.

# Multifractal Dimension Spectrum Analysis for Nuclear Density Distribution

## Key claim
The multifractal dimension spectrum, computed via an integral density method, is remarkably sensitive to the non-uniformity of nucleon density distributions in atomic nuclei, but the method's ability to distinguish different structures depends on the choice of sampling ball radius and center position relative to the nuclear radius.

## Method
The authors developed an integral density method based on the cumulative mass multifractal formalism, where the generalized dimensions D_q are extracted from the slopes of log-log plots of a scaling relation involving mass enclosed in balls of varying radius R. They applied this method to Woods-Saxon density distributions for 16O, 40Ca, and 208Pb, to a halo density distribution for 11Li, and to a tetrahedral alpha-cluster (4α) Gaussian density distribution for 16O, computing D_q for q values ranging from -10 to 10 at multiple radial positions.

## Key numbers
- For 208Pb at the central region (r_g=0), D_q is approximately 2.996 for a uniform-like distribution, with values around 2.997 for smaller diffuseness and around 2.987 for larger diffuseness.
- For 208Pb, the root-mean-square radius R_rms is defined as sqrt((Z/A)R_0p^2 + (N/A)R_0n^2), with proton radius parameter R_0p = 1.81 Z^(1/3) - 1.12 and neutron radius parameter R_0n = 1.49 N^(1/3) - 0.79.
- The diffuseness parameters are a_p = 0.47 - 0.00083 Z and a_n = 0.47 + 0.00046 N for protons and neutrons respectively.
- For the tetrahedral 16O cluster, the Gaussian width parameter δ_α = 0.52 and the alpha cluster positions are defined by edge-length λ with β = (√6/4)λ.
- The sampling ball radius R_G is set as R_G = 3(R_rms - 1.2)/5 + 1.2 for the seven radial positions at r_g = 0, R_rms/6, R_rms/3, R_rms/2, 2R_rms/3, 5R_rms/6, and R_rms.

## Context
This work extends the cumulative mass multifractal method previously introduced by Mandelbrot (1967) and applied to mass distributions in Refs. [bibitem10-bibitem15], now adapted for continuous nuclear density distributions. It builds on prior studies of nuclear clustering and halo structures, including tetrahedral symmetry in 16O suggested by Ref. [bibitem18] and the 4α cluster model from Ref. [bibitem19], as well as the halo nucleus 11Li density from Ref. [bibitem17]. The method enables quantitative characterization of non-uniformity in nuclear density distributions, potentially serving as a feature variable for distinguishing between stable, halo, and cluster nuclei in future nuclear structure studies.
