# Linear mode analysis from spin transport equation

> We provide a linear analysis on normal modes of the spin Boltzmann equation proposed in \cite{Weickgenannt:2021cuo}, where the non-diagonal or polarized part of the transition rate is neglected to ensure the Hermitian property of linearized collision operator. As an instrumental element of spin kinetic theory, the conservation of total angular momentum is explicitly considered, thus our analysis is relevant to the recent investigation on the issue of local spin polarization. By treating the linearized collision operator as an evolution operator, solving the normal modes turns out a degenerate perturbation problem in quantum mechanics. The dispersion relations of spinless modes are in accordance with well-known calculations, while the frequencies of spin modes are also determined up to second-order in wave vector and the second order expressions are only formal solutions to be further determined. Moreover, the relaxation of spin density is related to our linear mode analysis, which shall play a big role in investigating the issues of the local spin polarization in the relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

# Linear mode analysis from spin transport equation

## Key claim

The paper provides a linear analysis of normal modes in the spin Boltzmann equation proposed by Weickgenannt (2021), but the second-order expressions for spin mode frequencies are only formal solutions that require further determination, and the analysis explicitly neglects the polarized part of the transition rate and the spin-exchange collision term to ensure Hermiticity of the linearized collision operator.

## Method

The authors linearize the spin Boltzmann equation around a global equilibrium state with zero spin potential and fluid velocity \(u^\mu=(1,0,0,0)\), splitting the linearized collision operator into a local Hermitian part \(\mathcal{L}_1\) and a nonlocal part \(\mathcal{L}_2\). They treat the linearized collision operator as an evolution operator and solve the normal mode problem using degenerate perturbation theory in quantum mechanics, where the non-uniformity \(\kappa\) (wave vector) is taken as a small perturbation. The eleven zero modes corresponding to conservation laws (particle number, energy-momentum, and total angular momentum) are identified, and the secular equation is solved to obtain dispersion relations up to second order in wave vector.

## Key numbers

- Eleven zero modes (collision invariants) are identified: \(1\), \(p^\mu\), and \(J^{\mu\nu}\), corresponding to conservation of particle number, energy-momentum, and total angular momentum.
- First-order dispersion relations for spinless modes include two sound modes with \(\omega^{(1)}_1 = -\omega^{(1)}_2 = \sqrt{H^2_{2,3}+H^2_{1,3}}\), three zero-frequency modes \(\omega^{(1)}_3=\omega^{(1)}_4=\omega^{(1)}_5=0\), and spin modes with \(\omega^{(1)}_7=\omega^{(1)}_8=H_{7,9}\) and \(\omega^{(1)}_9=\omega^{(1)}_{10}=-H_{7,9}\).
- Second-order spinless mode frequencies are expressed in terms of transport coefficients: shear viscosity \(\eta\), bulk viscosity \(\zeta\), and diffusion coefficient \(\lambda\), with damping rates proportional to \(\kappa^2\).
- Spin-related modes include four propagating transverse spin modes with speed \(H_{7,9}/\kappa\) and two purely decaying longitudinal modes.

## Context

This work extends the spin Boltzmann equation framework of Weickgenannt (2021) by providing a linear mode analysis analogous to the standard treatment of hydrodynamic modes in DeGroot (1980). The results for spinless modes reproduce the well-known sound, heat, and shear modes from ordinary hydrodynamics, confirming that spin effects decouple from spinless modes at this order. The analysis differs from Hattori (2019), where spin modes were non-conservative and purely decaying, whereas here the spin modes are protected by total angular momentum conservation and include propagating modes. This work enables the construction of causal and stable spin hydrodynamics from microscopic kinetic theory, relevant for understanding the spin sign puzzle in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.
