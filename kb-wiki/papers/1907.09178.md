# Impact of Uncertainties in Nuclear Reaction Cross Sections on p-Nucleosynthesis in Thermonuclear Supernovae

> The propagation of uncertainties in reaction cross sections and rates of neutron-, proton-, and alpha-induced reactions into the final isotopic abundances obtained in nucleosynthesis models is an important issue in studies of nucleosynthesis and Galactic Chemical Evolution. We developed a Monte Carlo method to allow large-scale postprocessing studies of the impact of nuclear uncertainties on nucleosynthesis. Temperature-dependent rate uncertainties combining realistic experimental and theoretical uncertainties are used. From detailed statistical analyses uncertainties in the final abundances are derived as probability density distributions. Furthermore, based on rate and abundance correlations an automated procedure identifies the most important reactions in complex flow patterns from superposition of many zones or tracers. The method so far was already applied to a number of nucleosynthesis processes. Here we focus on the production of p-nuclei in white dwarfs exploding as thermonuclear (type Ia) supernovae. We find generally small uncertainties in the final abundances despite of the dominance of theoretical nuclear uncertainties. A separate analysis of low- and high-density regions indicates that the total uncertainties are dominated by the high-density regions.

# Impact of Uncertainties in Nuclear Reaction Cross Sections on p-Nucleosynthesis in Thermonuclear Supernovae

## Key claim
Despite the dominance of theoretical nuclear uncertainties (with unmeasured rates carrying up to factor-of-10 uncertainties for alpha-induced reactions), the final p-nucleus abundances from thermonuclear supernova models show total uncertainties well below a factor of two, except for a few isotopes of Gd, Dy, Er, and Pt. The total uncertainties are dominated by high-density regions originating from the inner part of the white dwarf, and the uncertainties from reaction rates are small compared to uncertainties from the choice of site, explosion model, and numerical treatment of hydrodynamics.

## Method
The authors used the Monte Carlo framework PizBuin to perform postprocessing of 4624 tracer trajectories extracted from a 2D explosion simulation of a single-degenerate type Ia supernova, running a reaction network of 1342 nuclides with several tens of thousands of reactions. Temperature-dependent rate uncertainties were constructed by combining experimental ground-state uncertainties with theoretical uncertainties for excited-state contributions, with each of the 10000 Monte Carlo runs randomly varying each standard stellar rate within its assigned uncertainty interval. The Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient was used to identify key reactions, defined as those with |r| >= 0.65 between rate variation and abundance change.

## Key numbers
- 51200 tracer particles were extracted from the 2D explosion simulation; among these, 4624 tracers experienced conditions supporting p-nuclide production.
- The reaction network included 1342 nuclides (around stability and towards the proton-rich side).
- 10000 Monte Carlo runs were performed, requiring the full reaction network to be run several 10^7 times.
- Theory uncertainties ranged from a factor of 2 for neutron captures to a factor of 10 for reactions involving alpha particles.
- The only key rate found was 145Eu(p,gamma)146Gd, affecting the production of 146Sm.
- Total uncertainties were well below a factor of two for most p-nuclides, with 162Er approaching a factor of two at the upper limit; exceptions included isotopes of Gd, Dy, Er, and Pt in the high-density tracer group.

## Context
This work extends the authors' Monte Carlo method previously applied to the gamma-process (p-process) in core-collapse supernovae (Rauscher et al. 2016), the nu-p process in core-collapse supernovae, the weak s-process in massive stars, and the main s-process in AGB stars. The study addresses the production of p-nuclides in thermonuclear supernovae as suggested by Travaglio et al. (2011), finding that the uncertainties from reaction rates are small compared to uncertainties from astrophysical modeling. The results enable astronomers to interpret observational data and disentangle nuclear physics uncertainties from astrophysical modeling uncertainties in studies of Galactic Chemical Evolution, though the authors note that the tracer distribution may be specific to the 2D explosion model used and that 3D models may yield different relative weights of low- and high-density regions.
