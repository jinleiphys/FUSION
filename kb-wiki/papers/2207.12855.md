# Efficient Learning of Accurate Surrogates for Simulations of Complex Systems

> Machine learning methods are increasingly used to build computationally inexpensive surrogates for complex physical models. The predictive capability of these surrogates suffers when data are noisy, sparse, or time-dependent. As we are interested in finding a surrogate that provides valid predictions of any potential future model evaluations, we introduce an online learning method empowered by optimizer-driven sampling. The method has two advantages over current approaches. First, it ensures that all turning points on the model response surface are included in the training data. Second, after any new model evaluations, surrogates are tested and "retrained" (updated) if the "score" drops below a validity threshold. Tests on benchmark functions reveal that optimizer-directed sampling generally outperforms traditional sampling methods in terms of accuracy around local extrema, even when the scoring metric favors overall accuracy. We apply our method to simulations of nuclear matter to demonstrate that highly accurate surrogates for the nuclear equation of state can be reliably auto-generated from expensive calculations using a few model evaluations.

## Key claim

The paper introduces an online learning method for building surrogates that are "asymptotically" valid for any future model evaluations, but the authors hedge that this claim depends on the training set including all first- and second-order critical points (including endpoints) of the model response surface, and that the surrogate is only guaranteed valid for the chosen quality metric.

## Method

The method uses an iterative online learning workflow with three components: optimizer-driven sampling to generate training and test data, radial basis function (RBF) interpolation as the surrogate estimator, and a validation metric to evaluate candidate surrogates against test data. The optimizer-directed sampler uses ensembles of Nelder-Mead solvers to find critical points of the response surface, and surrogates are retrained when their test score falls below a validity threshold. The numerical implementation uses the open-source *mystic* optimization toolkit.

## Key numbers

* For the 2-dimensional Rastrigin function benchmark, the test validity criteria used tolerances of tol_ave = 10^-5 and tol_max = 10^-4 for "loose" settings, and tol_ave = 10^-7 and tol_max = 10^-6 for "strict" settings.
* For the nuclear equation of state application, a valid surrogate was found after 6846 model evaluations using an ensemble of 40 Nelder-Mead solvers with tol_max = 10^-6 and tol_sum = 10^-3.
* The nuclear matter study used baryon number densities in the range 0.04 fm^-3 to 1.6 fm^-3 and proton fractions y_p from 0.01 to 0.6.
* The bag constant for the MIT bag model quark matter EOS was taken as B^1/4 = 170 MeV (or B ~ 109 MeV/fm^3), with strange quark mass m_strange = 150 MeV.
* For the 2-D Michalewicz function benchmark, approximately 30,000 model evaluations were needed with loose tolerances to visually reproduce ground truth.

## Context

This work extends active learning strategies previously used by Lubbers et al. (2020) and Diaw et al. (2020) for developing surrogates of fine-scale material responses, and by Roehm et al. (2015) who used kriging for constructing surrogates of stress fields. The authors identify a critical limitation in prior approaches: the lack of guaranteed validity of surrogates for future data sets. The method enables auto-generation of accurate surrogates for expensive nuclear equation of state calculations, which is relevant for heavy-ion experiments and neutron-star research that require EOS tables over large density and temperature ranges.
