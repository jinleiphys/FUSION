# Heavy quarks and jets as probes of the QGP

> Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP), a QCD state of matter created in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, has remarkable properties, including, for example, a low shear viscosity over entropy ratio. By detecting the collection of low-momentum particles that arise from the collision, it is possible to gain quantitative insight into the created matter. However, its fast evolution and thermalization properties remain elusive. Only using high momentum objects as probes of QGP can unveil its constituents at different wavelengths. In this review, we attempt to provide a comprehensive picture of what was, so far, possible to infer about QGP given our current theoretical understanding of jets, heavy-flavor, and quarkonia. We will bridge the resulting qualitative picture to the experimental observations done at the LHC and RHIC. We will focus on the phenomenological description of experimental observations, provide a brief analytical summary of the description of hard probes, and an outlook on the main difficulties we will need to surpass in the following years. To benchmark QGP-related effects, we will also address nuclear modifications to the initial state and hadronization effects.

# Heavy quarks and jets as probes of the QGP

## Key claim
The paper argues that while hard probes (jets, heavy quarks, quarkonia) can provide complementary constraints on QGP properties beyond what soft probes offer, a quantitative extraction of transport coefficients like the jet quenching parameter $\hat{q}$ and the heavy quark diffusion coefficient $D_s$ remains limited by large theoretical uncertainties, the central value of $\hat{q}/T^3$ can vary by a factor of up to 6 across different models. The extracted transport properties from hard probes are qualitatively consistent with those from soft probes, but the precision is not yet sufficient for a definitive determination.

## Method
The authors review the phenomenological extraction of QGP transport coefficients from experimental data at RHIC and the LHC, focusing on three classes of hard probes: inclusive charged hadron and jet spectra, heavy-flavor hadron measurements (charm and beauty), and quarkonium (J/$\psi$ and $\Upsilon$) production. They compare results from multiple theoretical frameworks, including Higher-Twist, MARTINI, McGill-AMY, JETSCAPE, LIDO, and Bayesian analyses, that relate observables like nuclear modification factors and elliptic flow to medium properties such as $\hat{q}$, $D_s$, and $\eta/s$. The review also discusses how model assumptions about initial state, medium evolution, pre-equilibrium dynamics, and hadronization affect the extracted parameters.

## Key numbers
- Initial energy density in heavy-ion collisions exceeds 12 GeV/fm$^3$, based on charged hadron $dN/d\eta$, $\langle p_T\rangle$, and transverse energy distribution $dE_t/d\eta$ measurements.
- The extracted $\hat{q}/T^3$ central value can vary by a factor of up to 6 between different theoretical approaches.
- The charm-quark spatial diffusion coefficient $2\pi T D_s$ is extracted to be between 1 and 3 close to the critical temperature, significantly below perturbative QCD calculations.
- The specific shear viscosity $\eta/s$ extracted from soft probes is a factor of 10-20 smaller than water and helium, and a factor of around five smaller than calculations of Fermi gases.
- The relation $\eta/s \approx 1.25 T^3/\hat{q}$ is derived under the assumption of a gas of massless thermal particles.
- The ratio $D_s(2\pi T)/(\eta/4\pi s)$ is approximately 2.5 in a weakly coupled QGP and 1 in the strong coupling limit.
- QGP initialization time assumptions range from $\tau_0 = 0.6$ fm/$c$ up to $\tau_0 = 1.0$ fm/$c$ across different models.

## Context
This review extends the program of extracting QGP properties from soft probes, which have provided constraints on energy density, low specific shear viscosity, and thermalization via Bayesian analyses of relativistic hydrodynamics, to the hard sector. It builds on prior work including the JET Collaboration (2013) extraction of $\hat{q}$ from five different approaches using high-$p_T$ single hadron measurements, and the Duke hydro/transport model Bayesian analysis of heavy quark diffusion (Xu et al., 2017). The paper connects to lattice QCD results (HotQCD Collaboration, 2014; Banerjee et al., 2011; Ding et al., 2012; Kaczmarek et al., 2014; Brambilla et al., 2020; Altenkort et al., 2020) and AdS/CFT calculations (Policastro et al., 2002; Kovtun et al., 2004). The review identifies that future progress requires: (1) a unified description of coupled jet-medium evolution, (2) systematic tests of factorization assumptions, pre-equilibrium dynamics, and transport equation approximations, and (3) improved constraints on initial state and hadronization from upcoming LHC Run 3+4 and sPHENIX at RHIC data.
