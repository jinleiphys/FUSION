# Charge Symmetry Breaking in Pion-Nucleon Coupling Constants Induced by Axial Anomaly

> The contribution of axial anomaly to charge symmetry breaking in pion-nucleon coupling constants is calculated within instanton model for QCD vacuum. It has been demonstrated that the contribution is large and allows to explain Nolen-Schiffer anomaly.

# Charge Symmetry Breaking in Pion-Nucleon Coupling Constants Induced by Axial Anomaly

## Key claim
The axial anomaly contribution to charge symmetry breaking (CSB) in pion-nucleon coupling constants is large enough to explain the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly, but the result depends on the instanton model parameters and the negative sign of the quark condensate, which determines the sign of the CSB parameter.

## Method
The author calculated the contribution of the axial anomaly to CSB using the instanton liquid model for the QCD vacuum. Effective quark-gluon and quark-quark interactions induced by instantons were derived, and their matrix elements for neutral pion coupling to nucleons were evaluated using vacuum dominance, the axial anomaly relation for the topological charge matrix element, and PCAC relations.

## Key numbers
- The CSB parameter α = (g²_π⁰nn − g²_π⁰pp)/g²_π⁰NN ≈ 4%, calculated using m_d − m_u = 4.0 MeV, quark condensate ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ = −(260 MeV)³, ρ_c⁻¹ = 600 MeV, and 2π/α_s ≈ 12.
- The topological charge matrix element ⟨0|(α_s/4π)G_ãG_a|π⁰⟩ = [(m_d − m_u)/(m_d + m_u)] f_π m²_π, with f_π = 93 MeV and (m_d − m_u)/(m_d + m_u) ≈ 0.4.
- The axial couplings used were g_A³ = 1.267 and g_A⁸ = 0.585.
- For nuclei with A = 41, the CSB parameter α must be larger than 2% to explain the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly.

## Context
This work extends the search for QCD mechanisms behind charge symmetry breaking in strong interactions, specifically addressing the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly in mirror nuclei mass differences (Nolen and Schiffer, 1969). Prior explanations based on π⁰−η−η′ mixing gave small contributions due to large meson mass differences. The calculation uses the instanton liquid model of Shuryak (1982) and Diakonov and Petrov (1986), and the result enables a resolution of a longstanding problem in nuclear physics, as the estimated 4% CSB exceeds the 2% threshold required from nuclear structure calculations (Auerbach, 1983).
