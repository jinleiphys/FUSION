# The shell model Monte Carlo approach to level densities: recent developments and perspectives

> We review recent advances in the shell model Monte Carlo approach for the microscopic calculation of statistical and collective properties of nuclei. We discuss applications to the calculation of (i) level densities in nickel isotopes, implementing a recent method to circumvent the odd-particle sign problem; (ii) state densities in heavy nuclei; (iii) spin distributions of nuclear levels; and (iv) finite-temperature quadrupole distributions.

# Shell Model Monte Carlo Approach to Level Densities: Recent Developments and Perspectives

## Key claim

The shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method can calculate nuclear level densities microscopically in model spaces many orders of magnitude larger than conventional diagonalization, including for odd-mass nuclei where a sign problem previously prevented accurate calculations. The method demonstrates that a spherical configuration-interaction shell model approach within a truncated single-particle space can describe both vibrational and rotational collectivity in heavy nuclei, provided the Hamiltonian and model space are chosen appropriately for each mass region.

## Method

The SMMC method uses a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to express the Gibbs ensemble e^{-βH} as a functional integral over one-body ensembles of non-interacting nucleons moving in time-dependent auxiliary fields. Thermal expectation values are computed by Monte Carlo sampling of auxiliary field configurations, with canonical ensemble fixed-particle-number projections achieved through Fourier sums. For odd-mass nuclei, the sign problem is circumvented by computing single-particle Green's functions in imaginary time for an even-even reference nucleus and extracting ground-state energy differences from their asymptotic exponential decay. Level densities are obtained from the canonical partition function via inverse Laplace transform evaluated in the saddle-point approximation.

## Key numbers

- Model space for rare-earth nuclei: 50-82 shell plus 1f_{7/2} orbital for protons, 82-126 shell plus 0h_{11/2}, 1g_{9/2} orbitals for neutrons; many-particle model space for 162Dy is of order ~10^29
- Level densities calculated for nickel isotopes 59-64Ni using the fpg_{9/2} shell Hamiltonian
- State densities calculated for even-mass samarium isotopes 148-154Sm and neodymium isotopes 144-152Nd, and odd-mass samarium 149-155Sm and neodymium 143-149Nd
- Quadrupole distributions computed for 154Sm at temperatures T = 0.1 MeV, intermediate temperature, and T = 4 MeV
- Spin distributions calculated for 55Fe, 56Fe, and 60Co at multiple excitation energies

## Context

This work extends the SMMC approach developed by Lang (1993), Alhassid (1994), Koonin (1997), and Alhassid (2001) for microscopic level density calculations, which previously were limited to even-mass nuclei due to the odd-particle sign problem. The odd-particle sign problem solution was introduced by Mukherjee (2012) and first applied to level densities by Bonett-Matiz (2013). The method enables comparison with experimental level counting data, neutron resonance data from RIPL-3, and proton evaporation spectra from Voinov (2012). The quadrupole projection technique, introduced by Alhassid (2014), provides a path toward calculating level densities as a function of deformation, which is essential input for fission rate calculations. Future work aims to derive effective shell model Hamiltonians from density functional theory and extend calculations to actinides and unstable nuclei.
