# Clustering effects for explaining an anomalous JLab result on the Be-9 structure function

> An anomalous nuclear modification was reported by JLab measurements on the beryllium-9 structure function F_2. It is unexpected in the sense that a nuclear modification slope is too large to be expected from its average nuclear density. We investigated whether it is explained by a nuclear clustering configuration in Be-9 with two αnuclei and surrounding neutron clouds. Such clustering aspects are studied by using antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) and also by a simple shell model for comparison. We consider that nuclear structure functions F_2^A consist of a mean conventional part and a remaining one depending on the maximum local density. The first mean part does not show a significant cluster effect on F_2. However, we propose that the remaining one could explain the anonymous JLab slope, and it is associated with high densities created by the cluster formation in Be-9. The JLab measurement is possibly the first signature of clustering effects in high-energy nuclear reactions. A responsible physics could be an internal nucleon modification, which is caused by the high densities due to the cluster configuration.

## Key claim

The anomalous JLab nuclear modification slope for beryllium-9, which is too large to be explained by its average nuclear density, can be explained by a clustering configuration where the nucleus consists of two alpha particles with surrounding neutron clouds. The authors propose that the effect arises not from the mean conventional part of the structure function but from a remaining part associated with high local densities created by cluster formation, which could indicate an internal nucleon modification.

## Method

The authors calculated nuclear structure functions F_2^A using a convolution model with nucleon momentum distributions obtained from two approaches: a simple harmonic-oscillator shell model and antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD), a variational method that does not assume any specific cluster or shell configuration. They computed the momentum densities for ^4He and ^9Be, then evaluated F_2^A at Q^2=5 GeV^2 and compared the ratios F_2^A/F_2^D with JLab data. The nuclear modification slopes d(F_2^A/F_2^D)/dx were plotted against maximum local densities from both models to test whether clustering could resolve the anomaly.

## Key numbers

- The JLab nuclear modification slope for ^9Be was measured by approximating F_2^A/F_2^D data by straight lines in the range 0.35 < x < 0.7
- The structure function calculations were performed at Q^2 = 5 GeV^2
- When the ^9Be slope is plotted against the maximum local density from the AMD cluster model, it falls on the same curve as other nuclei; when plotted against the shell-model maximum density, it remains anomalously large

## Context

This work extends the study of nuclear modifications of structure functions (the EMC effect) discovered by the European muon collaboration, where nuclear effects at medium x are attributed to binding and possible internal nucleon modification. The authors had previously proposed in Hirai et al. (2010) that the anomalous JLab result could be interpreted by clustering in ^9Be. Unlike earlier studies that considered small quark clusters such as six-quark bag models in the early EMC-effect work, this paper uses realistic nuclear clustering configurations from low-energy nuclear physics. The work enables future experimental studies of cluster structure in deep inelastic scattering at JLab and calls for more elaborated theoretical models for comparison with upcoming data.
