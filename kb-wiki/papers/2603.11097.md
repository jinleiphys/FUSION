# Searching for New Particles Hidden under Known Resonances: A Heavy-Ion Diagnostic

> Searches for new physics typically rely on proton-proton collisions, where isolated mass bumps are the primary signatures. However, when a new particle is nearly degenerate in mass with a known Standard Model resonance, it can be partially or fully absorbed into the primary signal template. We investigate this generic loophole by proposing that heavy-ion collisions can provide complementary diagnostics for such hidden states. By utilizing observables sensitive to the quark-gluon plasma, such as the nuclear modification factor ($R_{AA}$) and elliptic flow ($v_2$), a hidden component can manifest as correlated biases in the extracted kinematics. We formulate a model-independent two-component framework, focusing on quarkonium peaks and using the $Υ(1S)$ mass region as a concrete stress-test example. The same template-level issue can in principle arise in other precision dimuon resonances, including electroweak channels such as $Z\toμ^+μ^-$, although those cases involve different intrinsic widths, backgrounds, and systematic uncertainties. Treating the mass resolution as an experimental nuisance parameter, we present sensitivity maps identifying the measurements required to constrain or reveal such hidden components.

## Key claim

Heavy-ion collisions can provide complementary diagnostics for new particles that are nearly degenerate in mass with known Standard Model resonances and thus partially or fully absorbed into the primary signal template in conventional proton-proton bump-hunt searches; the diagnostic relies on correlated biases in quark-gluon-plasma-sensitive observables such as the nuclear modification factor ($R_{AA}$) and elliptic flow ($v_2$), but the paper does not claim that existing data prefer such a hidden state.

## Method

The author formulates a model-independent two-component framework for precision dimuon resonance searches, where the invariant-mass distribution is a mixture of a known quarkonium state and a hidden narrow dimuon resonance with intrinsic width much smaller than the detector resolution. A toy Monte Carlo with 50,000 events per grid point is used to generate pseudo-data from the two-component distribution, and a forced single-peak Crystal Ball fit is performed with floating normalization, centroid, width, and tail parameters to extract the bias in reconstructed mass and yield. The response is mapped as a function of mass splitting $\Delta m$ and local mass resolution $\sigma_m$, focusing on the $\Upsilon(1S)$ mass region as a stress-test example.

## Key numbers

- Benchmark hidden-state mass $m_X \simeq 9.40$ GeV, corresponding to $\Delta m = 60$ MeV under the $\Upsilon(1S)$ peak at 9.46 GeV.
- Benchmark contamination fraction $f_X = 14\%$ (illustrative order-10% contamination).
- Resolution scan range $\sigma_m = 40$–120 MeV.
- For the benchmark point ($\Delta m = 60$ MeV, $f_X = 14\%$), the residual centroid bias reaches the $-5$ to $-10$ MeV range depending on $\sigma_m$.
- For a medium-transparent hidden state with $R_{AA}^X = 1.0$ and a medium-modified quarkonium with $R_{AA}^\mathcal{Q} = 0.3$, a hidden component with $r_{pp} = 0.05$ shifts the fitted $R_{AA}$ by approximately 0.035, while $r_{pp} = 0.10$ shifts it by approximately 0.07.

## Context

This work extends the generic blind spot in standard bump-hunt strategies (used by CMS, ATLAS, LHCb) where a new particle degenerate with a known resonance can be masked by the fitting procedure, and builds on prior discussions of heavy-ion collisions as complementary environments for BSM searches (Bruce et al., Drewes et al.). The proposed diagnostic differs from earlier heavy-ion BSM proposals by not relying on enhanced production or displaced signatures, but instead using the correlated response of fitted yield, mass lineshape, $R_{AA}$, and $v_2$ when an unresolved component is embedded in a quark-gluon-plasma environment. The formalism is applicable to any narrow quarkonium peak, with charmonium states and electroweak channels such as $Z\to\mu^+\mu^-$ left for future work due to different systematics.
