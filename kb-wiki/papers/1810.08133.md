# Using excitation-energy dependent fission yields to identify key fissioning nuclei in r-process nucleosynthesis

> We evaluate the impact of using sets of fission yields given by the GEF code for spontaneous (sf), neutron-induced ((n,f)), and beta-delayed (betadf) fission processes which take into account the approximate initial excitation energy of the fissioning compound nucleus. We further explore energy-dependent fission dynamics in the r process by considering the sensitivity of our results to the treatment of the energy sharing and de-excitation of the fission fragments using the FREYA code. We show that the asymmetric-to-symmetric yield trends predicted by GEF can reproduce the high-mass edge of the second r-process peak seen in solar data and examine the sensitivity of this result to the mass model and astrophysical conditions applied. We consider the effect of fission yields and barrier heights on the nuclear heating rates used to predict kilonova light curves. We find that fission barriers influence the contribution of 254Cf spontaneous fission to the heating at ~100 days, such that a light curve observation consistent with such late-time heating would both confirm that actinides were produced in the event and imply the fission barriers are relatively high along the 254Cf beta-feeding path. We lastly determine the key nuclei responsible for setting the r-process abundance pattern by averaging over thirty trajectories from a 1.2--1.4 M_odot neutron star merger simulation. We show it is largely the odd-N nuclei undergoing (Z,N)(n,f) and (Z,N)betadf that control the relative abundances near the second peak. We find the "hot spots" for beta-delayed and neutron-induced fission given all mass models considered and show most of these nuclei lie between the predicted N=184 shell closure and the location of currently available experimental decay data.

## Key claim

Using excitation-energy-dependent fission yields from the GEF code for spontaneous, neutron-induced, and beta-delayed fission can reproduce the high-mass edge of the second r-process peak seen in solar data, but this result is sensitive to the choice of mass model and astrophysical conditions; the asymmetric-to-symmetric yield trends predicted by GEF 2016 are the key feature enabling this reproduction.

## Method

The authors used the GEF 2016 code to generate fission fragment yields for spontaneous, neutron-induced (at 0.1 MeV incident neutron energy), and beta-delayed fission, treating each process with its distinct initial excitation energy. They then modified the FREYA code to accept GEF 2016 pre-neutron emission yields as input, allowing comparison of the two different treatments of fragment de-excitation and neutron emission. The r-process impact was evaluated by averaging over thirty trajectories from a 1.2-1.4 solar mass neutron star merger simulation, using four different mass models (FRDM2012, Thomas-Fermi, HFB-17, and ETFSI) with their corresponding fission barriers.

## Key numbers

- The asymmetric GEF 2016 yields for r-process nuclei tend to prefer daughters near A ~ 150, while the Kodama and Takahashi yields center near A ~ 144 for both asymmetric and symmetric fission.
- For neutron-induced fission in the r process, incident neutron energies are between ~0.01 - 0.2 MeV, with most fission taking place at ~0.1 MeV.
- The excitation energy for beta-delayed fission can be as large as 9 MeV, typically higher than the few MeV excitation energies of neutron-induced fission relevant for the r process.
- For very neutron-rich nuclei, some nuclei emit 8-10 neutrons per fission, with FREYA predicting as many as ~3 more neutrons emitted per fission than GEF in the most neutron-rich region with N > 184.
- The average neutron multiplicity in beta-delayed fission tends to be higher than that of neutron-induced fission, with 1-2 more neutrons emitted for neutron-induced fission compared to spontaneous fission.

## Context

This work extends prior studies using phenomenological fission descriptions such as GEF, ABLA, and Wahl in r-process simulations (Goriely 2013, Goriely GEF, Goriely GMP GEF) by explicitly treating the excitation energy dependence of fission yields for the three main fission processes occurring in a fission cycling r process. It also builds on the work of Kodama and Takahashi, whose parameterized double-Gaussian fission yields are widely used in r-process calculations but show different preferred daughter masses than GEF 2016. The results enable identification of key fissioning nuclei between the predicted N=184 shell closure and the location of currently available experimental decay data, potentially guiding future experimental facilities toward the most important nuclei for r-process nucleosynthesis.
