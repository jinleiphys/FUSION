# Parameter Counting in Relativistic Mean-Field Models

> Power counting is applied to relativistic mean-field energy functionals to estimate contributions to the energy from individual terms. New estimates for isovector, tensor, and gradient terms in finite nuclei are shown to be consistent with direct, high-quality fits. The estimates establish a hierarchy of model parameters and identify how many parameters are well constrained by bulk nuclear observables. We conclude that four (possibly five) isoscalar, non-gradient parameters, one gradient parameter, and one isovector parameter are well determined by the usual bulk nuclear observables.

## Key claim

Power counting applied to relativistic mean-field energy functionals shows that only a limited number of parameters are well constrained by bulk nuclear observables: four (possibly five) isoscalar non-gradient parameters, one gradient parameter, and one isovector parameter, with the hedge that these estimates are consistent with direct high-quality fits but do not prove uniqueness.

## Method

The authors construct a point-coupling energy functional expanded in powers of nucleon densities (scalar, vector, isovector-vector, tensor, and isovector-tensor) scaled according to Naive Dimensional Analysis (NDA) using the pion decay constant f_pi and a mass scale Lambda approximately 600 MeV. They apply power counting to estimate contributions from individual terms in the functional, then compare these estimates to results from direct parameter fits to bulk and single-particle nuclear properties of closed-shell nuclei.

## Key numbers

* Four (possibly five) isoscalar non-gradient parameters are well determined by bulk nuclear observables.
* One gradient parameter is well determined by bulk nuclear observables.
* One isovector parameter is well determined by bulk nuclear observables.
* The mass scale Lambda is approximately 600 MeV, consistent with existing empirical studies.
* The pion decay constant f_pi is approximately 94 MeV, used as a primary scale in NDA.

## Context

This work extends the modern Effective Field Theory (EFT) and Density Functional Theory (DFT) framework for relativistic mean-field models, building on earlier studies by Furnstahl (1993, 1996, 1997) and Serot (1997) that established connections between RMF parameters and nuclear observables. It contradicts the earlier assumption that adding more parameters (such as quartic vector self-couplings introduced by Gmuca in 1992) would improve fits, showing instead that only a limited number of parameters are constrained by the data. The results enable systematic truncation of RMF energy functionals and identify which parameters can be reliably extracted from bulk nuclear properties, guiding future fits to finite nuclei.
