# Neutrinoless double beta decay experiments

> The study of neutrinoless double beta decay is of outmost importance for neutrino physics. It is considered to be the gold plated channel to probe the fundamental character of neutrinos and to determine the neutrino mass. From the experimental point about nine different isotopes are explored for the search. After a general introduction follows a short discussion on nuclear matrix element calculations and supportive measurements. The current experimental status of double beta searches is presented followed by a short discussion of the ideas and proposals for large scale experiments.

## Key claim

Neutrinoless double beta decay is the only way to explore neutrino masses below 100 meV, but the conversion of measured half-lives into neutrino masses depends on nuclear matrix element calculations that still carry an uncertainty of a factor 2-3, and the claimed evidence for a signal in germanium-76 remains controversial.

## Method

The paper reviews the experimental status of neutrinoless double beta decay searches across approximately nine different isotopes, focusing on active detector techniques (where source and detector are identical, such as germanium semiconductors and cryogenic bolometers) and passive detector techniques (where source and detector are separate, such as time projection chambers). The author describes the current limits from the Heidelberg-Moscow and IGEX germanium experiments, the CUORICINO bolometer experiment using tellurium dioxide crystals, and the NEMO-3 time projection chamber experiment using molybdenum and selenium foils, along with the COBRA CdZnTe semiconductor approach and various proposals for future large-scale experiments.

## Key numbers

- Heidelberg-Moscow experiment: half-life limit for germanium-76 neutrinoless double beta decay is greater than 1.9 × 10^25 years (90% CL), corresponding to an upper bound on the effective Majorana neutrino mass of less than 0.35 eV
- A subgroup of the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration reported a possible signal with half-life between 0.7 and 4.2 × 10^25 years, implying a neutrino mass range of 0.2-0.6 eV
- CUORICINO experiment: half-life limit for tellurium-130 is greater than 2.2 × 10^24 years (90% CL), yielding an upper neutrino mass bound of 0.2-1.1 eV depending on matrix elements
- NEMO-3 experiment: half-life limit for molybdenum-100 is greater than 5.6 × 10^23 years (90% CL), resulting in an upper neutrino mass bound of 0.6-2 eV
- The tritium beta decay limit on electron neutrino mass is less than 2.2 eV
- To observe one decay per year at a half-life of about 6 × 10^26 years, approximately 100 kg of the isotope of interest is required
- The germanium-76 half-life limit corresponds to limits on right-handed current parameters of ⟨η⟩ less than 6.5 × 10^-9 and ⟨λ⟩ less than 8.2 × 10^-7

## Context

This review extends the discussion of neutrinoless double beta decay experiments following the major neutrino oscillation discoveries of the previous decade, which established that neutrinos have non-zero mass but cannot determine the absolute mass scale. The paper builds on the work of the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration (Kladior et al., 2001-2004) and the IGEX experiment, while also discussing the controversial claimed evidence for a signal in germanium-76. The review enables future large-scale experiments by outlining the required sensitivity (half-lives of 10^26-10^27 years for 50 meV mass sensitivity) and the need for improved nuclear matrix element calculations through charge exchange reactions, atomic trap measurements, and other supportive measurements as proposed by Zuber (2005).
