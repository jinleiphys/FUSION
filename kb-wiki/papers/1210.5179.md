# Hydrodynamic fluctuations and two-point correlations

> We examine correlations of energy density induced by initial state fluctuations, which are localized in both transverse and longitudinal extent. The hotspots are evolved according to hydrodynamics in a background which includes radial flow. Two-point energy density correlations from these hotspots are computed as a function of the difference in azimuthal angle and rapidity. Such localized perturbations occur naturally in the theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations and may provide insight into some features of the two-particle correlation data from RHIC and the LHC.

## Key claim
Localized hydrodynamic fluctuations, which are not boost-invariant but localized in both transverse and longitudinal extent, can produce two-point energy density correlations that may explain features of two-particle correlation data from RHIC and the LHC, but the authors defer full freeze-out analysis and note that the Gubser flow idealization requires azimuthal symmetry (restricting to central collisions) and conformal symmetry (only approximate for T >> T_c).

## Method
The authors consider an exact solution to ideal hydrodynamics (Gubser flow) as a background with radial flow, then add small perturbations of energy density and fluid velocity. They linearize the hydrodynamic equations, expand perturbations in spherical harmonics in the angular coordinates and Fourier modes in longitudinal rapidity, and solve analytically for the evolution of an initially localized Gaussian hotspot. The two-point energy density correlation function is computed by integrating over all hotspot positions and radial distance at a fixed final proper time.

## Key numbers
- Initial proper time: τ₀ = 0.5 fm/c
- Final proper time: τ_f = 10 fm/c
- Transverse size scale parameter: q⁻¹ = 4.3 fm
- Initial hotspot width: Σ = 0.1 (dimensionless)
- Initial hotspot amplitude: A = 1/2
- Initial radial position of hotspot: r₀ = 4.1 fm
- Spherical harmonic expansion summed up to l = 25

## Context
This work extends the approach of Staig and Shuryak (2011), who showed that initial-state hotspots placed at a particular transverse position could reproduce features of two-particle azimuthal correlations, by additionally examining the rapidity extent of correlations. It is a first step toward extending the results of Kapusta, Muller, and Stephanov (2011) on long-range rapidity correlations from hydrodynamic fluctuations to include angular dependence. The authors note that a proper Cooper-Frye freeze-out procedure and inclusion of stochastic noise (rather than hand-placed hotspots) are necessary next steps for comparison with experiment.
