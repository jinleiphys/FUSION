# The Nuclear Equation of State and Neutron Star Masses

> Neutron stars are valuable laboratories for the study of dense matter. Recent observations have uncovered both massive and low-mass neutron stars and have also set constraints on neutron star radii. The largest mass measurements are powerfully influencing the high-density equation of state because of the existence of the neutron star maximum mass. The smallest mass measurements, and the distributions of masses, have implications for the progenitors and formation mechanisms of neutron stars. The ensemble of mass and radius observations can realistically restrict the properties of dense matter, and, in particular, the behavior of the nuclear symmetry energy near the nuclear saturation density. Simultaneously, various nuclear experiments are progressively restricting the ranges of parameters describing the symmetry properties of the nuclear equation of state. In addition, new theoretical studies of pure neutron matter are providing insights. These observational, experimental and theoretical constraints of dense matter, when combined, are now revealing a remarkable convergence.

## Key claim

The paper argues that astrophysical observations of neutron star masses and radii, combined with laboratory nuclear experiments and theoretical studies of pure neutron matter, are converging to restrict the properties of the nuclear equation of state, particularly the symmetry energy near saturation density. The abstract's claim of "remarkable convergence" is hedged by the fact that precision measurements of both mass and radius for individual neutron stars do not yet exist, and the constraints rely on combining multiple imperfect observational techniques.

## Method

The paper reviews the general relativistic equations of hydrostatic equilibrium (Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations) for neutron star structure, using polytropic and maximally compact equations of state to derive scaling relations between mass, radius, and central density. Mass measurements are obtained from pulsar timing in binary systems using Keplerian parameters and relativistic effects including periastron advance, gravitational redshift, orbital decay from gravitational radiation, and Shapiro time delay, with inclination angles constrained by multiple relativistic observables or empirical binary period-companion mass relations.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: \(n_s \simeq 0.16\) baryons fm\(^{-3}\), mass density \(\rho_s \simeq 2.7 \times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), energy density \(\varepsilon_s \simeq 150\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\)
- Minimum stable neutron star mass: approximately 0.09 M\(_\odot\) with radius 200-300 km
- Maximum compactness limit from causality: \(R \ge 2.824 GM/c^2\), corresponding to maximum surface redshift \(z \le 0.8512\)
- Maximum mass from maximally compact equation of state: \(M_{max} \simeq 4.09 \sqrt{\varepsilon_s/\varepsilon_0}\) M\(_\odot\), with causality limiting maximum mass to approximately 3 M\(_\odot\)
- Largest well-measured neutron star mass: \(1.97 \pm 0.04\) M\(_\odot\) for PSR J1614-2230, determined via Shapiro time delay with \(\sin i = 0.99989\) and delay amplitude 48.8 \(\mu\)s
- Minimum spin period from causality: \(P_{min} = 0.20 (M_{max}/{\rm M}_\odot)\) ms
- Maximum fractional binding energy: 25.2%, maximum baryon chemical potential: \(\mu_{B,max} = 2.093\) GeV
- For a 1.4 M\(_\odot\) neutron star with \(M_{max} \gtrsim 2\) M\(_\odot\), minimum radius \(R \gtrsim 8.25\) km

## Context

This review extends the work of Lattimer and Prakash (2001, 2005, 2011) on neutron star structure and equation of state constraints, and updates the mass measurement compilation from those references. The paper synthesizes observational results including the precise mass measurement of PSR J1614-2230 by Demorest et al. (2010) and mass measurements from globular cluster pulsars by Freire et al. (2008) and Kiziltan et al. (2011), alongside laboratory constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy. The convergence of astrophysical, experimental, and theoretical constraints enables narrowing the range of equation of state properties needed for simulations of supernovae and neutron star mergers, and sets the stage for future gravitational wave observations from merging neutron star binaries.
