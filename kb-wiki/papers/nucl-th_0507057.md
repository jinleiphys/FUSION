# Hydrodynamic source with continuous emission in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV

> We analyze single particle momentum spectra and interferometry radii in central Au+Au collisions at RHIC within a hydro-inspired parametrization accounting for continuous hadron emission through the whole lifetime of hydrodynamically expanding fireball. We found that a satisfactory description of the data is achieved for a physically reasonable set of parameters when the emission from non space-like sectors of the enclosed freeze-out hypersurface is fairly long: $ 9$ fm/c. This protracted surface emission is compensated in outward interferometry radii by positive $r_{out} - t$ correlations that are the result of an intensive transverse expansion. The main features of the experimental data are reproduced: in particular, the obtained ratio of the outward to sideward interferometry radii is less than unity and decreases with increasing transverse momenta of pion pairs. The extracted value of the temperature of emission from the surface of hydrodynamic tube approximately coincides with one found at chemical freeze-out in RHIC Au+Au collisions. A significant contribution of the surface emission to the spectra and to the correlation functions at relatively large transverse momenta should be taken into account in advanced hydrodynamic models of ultrarelativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim
A hydro-inspired parametrization with an enclosed freeze-out hypersurface can simultaneously describe single-particle spectra and HBT radii in central Au+Au collisions at RHIC, provided that surface emission from non-space-like sectors lasts about 9 fm/c and is compensated by positive \(r_{out} - t\) correlations from intensive transverse expansion. The abstract's claim of a "satisfactory description" is hedged by the condition that the surface emission temperature must be close to the chemical freeze-out temperature (150 MeV) rather than the typical kinetic freeze-out range.

## Method
The authors generalized the blast-wave model to include continuous hadron emission by using an enclosed freeze-out hypersurface with two sectors: a volume sector (space-like, at constant proper time \(\tau_f\)) and a surface sector (non-space-like, with proper time linearly increasing with radius \(\tau_s(r) = a r + b\)). Particle emission was computed using a modified Cooper-Frye prescription with a step function \(\theta(p_\mu n^\mu(x))\) to restrict emission to particles with positive \(p_\mu n^\mu\), and the source was assumed boost-invariant in the longitudinal direction with a linear transverse rapidity profile for volume emission and a coupled profile for surface emission. The model was fitted to \(\pi^-\), \(K^-\), and \(p\) transverse momentum spectra and \(\pi^-\) HBT radii from central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV.

## Key numbers
- Duration of surface emission: \(\tau_f - \tau_i \approx 9\) fm/c (with \(\tau_i = 1\) fm/c and \(\tau_f = 10\) fm/c).
- Volume freeze-out temperature: \(T_v = 110\) MeV.
- Surface freeze-out temperature: \(T_s = 150\) MeV, close to the chemical freeze-out temperature of \(157 \pm 3\) MeV from Braun-Munzinger et al. (2004).
- Maximal transverse rapidity: \(\eta_T^{\text{max}} = 0.73\), corresponding to a maximal transverse velocity \(v^{\text{max}} \approx 0.6c\).
- Initial transverse velocity at \(\tau_i = 1\) fm/c: \(v(R_i, \tau_i) \approx 0.34c\).
- Final transverse radius: \(R_f = 11.3\) fm; initial transverse radius: \(R_i = 7\) fm.
- Chemical potentials: \(\mu_{s,\pi} = 0\) MeV, \(\mu_{s,K} = 0\) MeV, \(\mu_{s,p} = 40\) MeV; \(\mu_{v,\pi} = 53\) MeV, \(\mu_{v,K} = 45\) MeV, \(\mu_{v,p} = 280\) MeV.
- Ratio of detected to thermal (primary) particles: 2.2 for pions and kaons, 3.5 for protons.

## Context
This work extends the blast-wave model (used in, e.g., the "blast-wave" parametrization by Schnedermann et al.) by incorporating continuous emission from an enclosed freeze-out hypersurface, addressing the "HBT puzzle" where earlier models predicted \(R_{out}/R_{side} > 1\) in contradiction with RHIC data (e.g., from the STAR and PHENIX collaborations). The results support the hydro-kinetic approach of Akkelin and Sinyukov (2005) and suggest that surface emission at high temperature (close to chemical freeze-out) is essential for describing high-\(p_T\) spectra and HBT radii, enabling more realistic hydrodynamic models of ultrarelativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions.
