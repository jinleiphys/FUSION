# Analytic Solution for the Revised Helicity Evolution at Small $x$ and Large $N_c$: New Resummed Gluon-Gluon Polarized Anomalous Dimension and Intercept

> We construct an exact analytic solution of the revised small-$x$ helicity evolution equations derived recently. The equations we solve are obtained in the large-$N_c$ limit (with $N_c$ the number of quark colors) and are double-logarithmic (summing powers of $α_s \ln^2(1/x)$ with $α_s$ the strong coupling constant and $x$ the Bjorken $x$ variable). Our solution provides small-$x$, large-$N_c$ expressions for the flavor-singlet quark and gluon helicity parton distribution functions (PDFs) and for the $g_1$ structure function, with their leading small-$x$ asymptotics given by \begin{align} ΔΣ(x, Q^2) \sim ΔG (x, Q^2) \sim g_1 (x, Q^2) \sim \left( \frac{1}{x} \right)^{α_h} , \notag \end{align} where the exact analytic expression we obtain for the intercept $α_h$ can be approximated by $α_h = 3.66074 \, \sqrt{\frac{α_s \, N_c}{2 π}}$. Our solution also yields an all-order (in $α_s$) resummed small-$x$ anomalous dimension $Δγ_{GG} (ω)$ which agrees with all the existing fixed-order calculations (to three loops). Notably, our anomalous dimension is different from that obtained in the infrared evolution equation framework developed earlier by Bartels, Ermolaev, and Ryskin (BER), with the disagreement starting at four loops. Despite the previously reported agreement at two decimal points based on the numerical solution of the same equations, the intercept of our large-$N_c$ helicity evolution and that of BER disagree beyond that precision, with the BER intercept at large $N_c$ given by a different analytic expression from ours with the numerical value of $α_h^{BER} = 3.66394 \, \sqrt{\frac{α_s \, N_c}{2 π}}$. We speculate on the origin of this disagreement.

## Key claim

The paper presents an exact analytic solution to the revised small-x helicity evolution equations at large N_c, yielding a resummed gluon-gluon polarized anomalous dimension that agrees with all existing fixed-order calculations to three loops but disagrees with the earlier Bartels-Ermolaev-Ryskin (BER) result starting at four loops, with the intercept difference being smaller than the numerical precision of prior work.

## Method

The authors solve the large-N_c version of the KPS-CTT double-logarithmic helicity evolution equations using a double inverse Laplace transform method over the variables η−s_10 and s_10, where η and s_10 are logarithmic variables encoding energy and dipole size dependence. They derive partial differential equations for the polarized dipole amplitudes G, G_2, Γ, and Γ_2, construct homogeneous and particular solutions, and impose boundary conditions from the original integral equations to determine the unknown functions. The solution yields analytic expressions for the flavor-singlet quark and gluon helicity PDFs and the g_1 structure function.

## Key numbers

- The small-x asymptotic intercept α_h = (4/3^(1/3)) * sqrt(Re[(-9 + i sqrt(111))^(1/3)]) * sqrt(α_s N_c/(2π)) ≈ 3.66074 * sqrt(α_s N_c/(2π))
- The BER intercept α_h^BER = sqrt((17 + sqrt(97))/2) * sqrt(α_s N_c/(2π)) ≈ 3.66394 * sqrt(α_s N_c/(2π))
- The flavor-singlet helicity PDFs and g_1 structure function all have leading small-x asymptotics ~ (1/x)^α_h
- The disagreement between the analytic intercept and the BER intercept arises at four-loop order in the anomalous dimension expansion

## Context

This work extends the revised small-x helicity evolution equations derived by Cougoulic, Kovchegov, Tawabutr, and Tarasov (2022), which corrected earlier KPS equations, by providing the first exact analytic solution rather than the numerical solution reported in that same work. The result enables precise comparison with the infrared evolution equation framework of Bartels, Ermolaev, and Ryskin (1996), showing that despite numerical agreement to two decimal places, the analytic intercepts differ, with the disagreement traced to the four-loop level of the anomalous dimension. The analytic solution also provides all-order resummation that can be used for future phenomenological studies at the Electron-Ion Collider and for cross-checking fixed-order calculations beyond three loops.
