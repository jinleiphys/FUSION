# R-Process Freezeout, Nuclear Deformation, and the Rare-Earth Element Peak

> We use network calculations of r-process nucleosynthesis to explore the origin of the peak in the solar r-process abundance distribution near nuclear mass number A = 160. The peak is due to a subtle interplay of nuclear deformation and beta decay, and forms not in the steady phase of the r-process, but only just prior to freezeout, as the free neutrons rapidly disappear. Its existence should therefore help constrain the conditions under which the r-process occurs and freezes out.

## Key claim

The rare-earth element peak near mass number A ≈ 160 in the solar r-process abundance distribution forms not during the steady phase of the r-process, but only just prior to freezeout as free neutrons rapidly disappear, due to a subtle interplay of nuclear deformation and beta decay that funnels nuclei into the deformation maximum region.

## Method

The authors performed network calculations of r-process nucleosynthesis using a computer code that solves the differential equations determining time-development of nucleosynthesis, parameterizing the dependence of temperature on time (with ρ ∝ T³) to match results from more detailed supernova simulations. They ran simulations through freezeout until only stable nuclei remained, treating freezeout fully dynamically with neutron capture and photodisintegration continuing to compete with beta decay throughout the entire process. To confirm their mechanism, they also ran simulations with a simplified semi-empirical mass formula and beta-decay lifetimes fitted to the form T₁/₂ = a Q^α, introducing a kink in the separation energies at N = 104.

## Key numbers

- Initial neutron-to-seed ratio R = 57 in the sample simulation, implying a seed captured on average 57 neutrons
- The REE peak forms only after R falls below about 1, when steady beta flow is destroyed
- The simplified model used beta-decay lifetimes fitted with a = 250 and α = -3.80 in the function T₁/₂ = a Q^α
- The simplified model used Gaussian abundances of width 1.05 centered at N_max for each Z along the equilibrium path between N = 82 and N = 126
- When beta-decay rates were modified to be constant along curves of constant separation energy, the peak shrank by a factor of about 2 relative to the surrounding abundance level

## Context

This work extends earlier speculation by Burbidge et al. (1957) that deformation of neutron-rich rare-earth nuclei causes the peak, and calculations by Seeger, Fowler, and Clayton (1965) that produced a much broader abundance hump. It contradicts the alternative explanation by Cameron (1957) and Schramm and Fowler (1971) that the peak arises from mass-asymmetric fission of very heavy r-process nuclei, which Marti and Zeh (1985) argued is not compelling because the r-process should terminate by beta-delayed fission before A ≈ 160 fission fragments can be produced. The work builds on high-entropy neutrino-driven bubble simulations by Meyer et al. (1992) and Woosley et al. (1994) that produced a nice REE peak while following the r-process through freezeout, and it enables constraints on r-process freezeout conditions, confirming that freezeout is prompted by exhaustion of free neutrons rather than a rapid drop in density or temperature.
