# The incompleteness of complete pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction

> [Background] A complete set is a minimum set of observables which allows one to determine the underlying reaction amplitudes unambiguously. Pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction from the nucleon is characterized by four such amplitudes and complete sets involve single- and double-polarization observables. [Purpose] Identify complete sets of observables, and study how measurements with finite error bars impact their potential to determine the reaction amplitudes unambiguously. [Method] The authors provide arguments to employ the transversity representation in order to determine the amplitudes in pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction. It is studied whether the amplitudes in the transversity basis for the $γp \to K^+Λ$ reaction can be estimated without ambiguity. To this end, data from the GRAAL collaboration and mock data from a realistic model are analyzed. [Results] It is illustrated that the moduli of normalized transversity amplitudes can be determined from precise single-polarization data. Starting from mock data with achievable experimental resolution, it is quite likely to obtain imaginary solutions for the relative phases of the amplitudes. Also the real solutions face a discrete phase ambiguity which makes it impossible to obtain a statistically significant solution for the relative phases at realistic experimental conditions. [Conclusions] Single polarization observables are effective in determining the moduli of the amplitudes in a transversity basis. Determining the relative phases of the amplitudes from double-polarization observables is far less evident. The availability of a complete set of observables does not allow one to unambiguously determine the reaction amplitudes with statistical significance.

## Key claim

Even when a complete set of eight observables (three single-polarization and four double-polarization) is measured with realistic finite precision, the relative phases of the transversity amplitudes for pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction cannot be determined unambiguously with statistical significance, contrary to the expectation from ideal no-error analyses.

## Method

The authors expressed all single- and double-polarization observables for pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction in the transversity basis, where single-polarization observables (beam asymmetry Σ, target asymmetry T, recoil asymmetry P) are linear in the squared moduli of the normalized transversity amplitudes. They then applied the formalism of Chiang and Tabakin (1996) for solving complete sets to the γp → K+Λ reaction, using both real data from the GRAAL collaboration and mock data generated from a realistic model with simulated experimental resolution, to test whether the relative phases could be extracted without ambiguity.

## Key numbers

- The four normalized transversity amplitudes satisfy r1² + r2² + r3² + r4² = 1, leaving three independent moduli to be determined from single-polarization data.
- The moduli are given by r1 = ½√(1 + Σ + T + P), r2 = ½√(1 + Σ − T − P), r3 = ½√(1 − Σ − T + P), r4 = ½√(1 − Σ + T − P) from the three single asymmetries.
- A complete set requires three single and four double asymmetries, yielding eight observables for seven real unknowns (four moduli and three independent relative phases).
- For complete sets of the first kind (e.g., {Cx, Ox, E, F}) there are four possible solutions for the phases; for the second kind (e.g., {E, H}) there is an eightfold ambiguity.

## Context

This work extends the formal complete-set analysis of Barker, Donnachie, and Storrow (1975) and Chiang and Tabakin (1996) by incorporating the effect of finite experimental precision, which was previously noted as a concern by Ireland (2010). The transversity representation is shown to be particularly advantageous because single-polarization observables give linear equations for the moduli. The results demonstrate that even for the γp → K+Λ reaction, where the self-analyzing decay of the Λ provides access to recoil polarization, achieving truly complete measurements with statistical significance remains problematic under realistic experimental conditions.
