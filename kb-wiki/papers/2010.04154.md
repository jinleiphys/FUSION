# Quasielastic Electromagnetic Scattering Cross Sections and World Data Comparisons in the {\fontfamily{qcr}\selectfont GENIE} Monte Carlo Event Generator

> The usage of Monte Carlo neutrino event generators (MC$ν$EGs) is a norm within the high-energy $ν$ scattering community. The relevance of quasielastic (QE) energy regimes to $ν$ oscillation experiments implies that accurate calculations of $νA$ cross sections in this regime will be a key contributor to reducing the systematic uncertainties affecting the extraction of oscillation parameters. In spite of this, many MC$ν$EGs utilize highly phenomenological, parameterized models of QE scattering cross sections. Moreover, a culture of validation of MC$ν$EGs against prolific electron ($e$) scattering data has been historically lacking. In this work, we implement new $e A$ cross sections obtained from nuclear ab initio approaches in GENIE, the primary MC$ν$EG utilized by the FNAL community. In particular, we utilize results from Quantum MC methods which solve the many-body nuclear problem in the Short-Time Approximation (STA), allowing consistent retention of two-nucleon dynamics which are crucial to explain available nuclear electromagnetic (electroweak) data over a wide range of energy and momentum transfers. This new implementation in GENIE is fully tested against the world QE electromagnetic data, finding agreement with available data below $\sim2\,$GeV of beam energy with the aid of a scaling function formalism. The STA is currently limited to study $A\leq12$ nuclei, however, its semi-inclusive multibody identity components are exportable to other many-body computational techniques such as Auxiliary Field Diffusion MC which can reach $A\leq40$ systems while continuing to realize the factorization contained within the STA's multinucleon dynamics. Together, these developments promise to make future experiments such as DUNE more accurate in their assessment of MC$ν$EG systematics, $ν$ properties, and potentially empower the discovery of physics beyond the Standard Model.

## Key claim

The authors implement electron-nucleus cross sections from quantum Monte Carlo Short-Time Approximation (STA) calculations into the GENIE Monte Carlo event generator, finding agreement with world quasielastic electromagnetic data below approximately 2 GeV of beam energy when using a scaling function formalism, but note that the averaging technique used to interpolate response functions reduces cross section strength at low momentum transfers and high outgoing angles.

## Method

The authors pre-compute nuclear electromagnetic response functions for ⁴He using the Short-Time Approximation (STA) within quantum Monte Carlo methods, which solve the many-body nuclear problem with the Argonne-v₁₈ two-nucleon potential and Illinois-7 three-nucleon force. They then implement these tabulated response functions into GENIE via a hadron tensor interface that uses bilinear interpolation on a grid with 2 MeV spacing in energy transfer ω and 1 MeV spacing in three-momentum transfer |q|, covering 1 MeV ≤ |q| ≤ 2000 MeV and 2 MeV ≤ ω ≤ 1800 MeV. To achieve fine grid spacing from sparse STA calculations, they construct an averaged nonrelativistic scaling function from response functions at momentum transfers in the set {400, 450, ..., 750, 800, 1000} MeV, which is then used to generate interpolated response surfaces for cross section calculations.

## Key numbers

* The STA calculations use the Argonne-v₁₈ two-nucleon potential fitted to approximately 4300 data points in the Nijmegen pn and pp scattering database from 0–350 MeV, with a χ²/datum close to one.
* The input response tables for ⁴He use a grid with 2 MeV spacing between ω grid points and 1 MeV spacing between |q| grid points, covering 1 MeV ≤ |q| ≤ 2000 MeV and 2 MeV ≤ ω ≤ 1800 MeV.
* The set of known computed momentum transfers used for scaling function construction is |q| ∈ {400, 450, 500, 550, 600, 650, 700, 750, 800, 1000} MeV.
* Two-body electromagnetic currents contribute approximately 20–40% to electromagnetic transverse response functions and enhance the magnetic moment of ⁹C by approximately 40%.

## Context

This work extends the validation of ab initio nuclear response calculations within Monte Carlo event generators, following similar recent studies for existing GENIE cross section models by PhysRevD.102.053001 and Papadopolou (2020). The STA approach builds on Green's function Monte Carlo methods (Carlson, 2014) that have been applied to nuclei up to A=12, while the STA framework is designed to be exportable to Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo methods that can reach A≤40 systems relevant for future experiments such as DUNE. The implementation enables direct comparison of competing nuclear models within GENIE and provides a foundation for future inclusion of neutrino-nucleus responses using the same microscopic framework.
