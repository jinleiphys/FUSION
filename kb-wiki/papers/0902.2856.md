# Heavy Quark Thermalization in Classical Lattice Gauge Theory: Lessons for Strongly-Coupled QCD

> Thermalization of a heavy quark near rest is controlled by the correlator of two electric fields along a temporal Wilson line. We address this correlator within real-time, classical lattice Yang-Mills theory, and elaborate on the analogies that exist with the dynamics of hot QCD. In the weak-coupling limit, it can be shown analytically that the dynamics on the two sides are closely related to each other. For intermediate couplings, we carry out non-perturbative simulations within the classical theory, showing that the leading term in the weak-coupling expansion significantly underestimates the heavy quark thermalization rate. Our analytic and numerical results also yield a general understanding concerning the overall shape of the spectral function corresponding to the electric field correlator, which may be helpful in subsequent efforts to reconstruct it from Euclidean lattice Monte Carlo simulations.

## Key claim

The heavy quark thermalization rate in strongly-coupled QCD is significantly underestimated by the leading-order weak-coupling expansion, and classical lattice gauge theory can capture the infrared dynamics responsible for this failure at intermediate couplings, though the analogy between the two theories is only qualitative and involves an ambiguity in matching the lattice spacing to the temperature.

## Method

The authors computed the electric field correlator along a temporal Wilson line, whose zero-frequency limit gives the heavy quark momentum diffusion coefficient, using real-time classical lattice Yang-Mills theory for SU(2) and SU(3) gauge groups. They performed non-perturbative numerical simulations of the classical thermal ensemble, discretized on a spatial lattice with a much finer temporal spacing, and compared the results to analytic leading-order weak-coupling calculations in both the continuum quantum theory and the classical lattice theory. The matching between the lattice spacing and the temperature was performed by equating Debye screening lengths, though the authors note this matching is ambiguous for dynamical quantities.

## Key numbers

* For the classical lattice theory at leading-logarithmic order, the heavy quark momentum diffusion coefficient is given by $\kappa_{\rm{latt}} \approx \frac{g^2 C_F T m_{\rm{D,latt}}^2}{6\pi} \frac{\Sigma_{v^{-1}}}{\Sigma} \times (\ln\frac{1}{a m_{\rm{D,latt}}} + 1.831)$, where $\Sigma_{v^{-1}} = 5.5079614967$ and $\Sigma = 3.1759115356$ for the standard lattice action, giving a factor $\Sigma_{v^{-1}}/\Sigma \sim 5/3$ larger than the continuum quantum result.
* For the continuum quantum theory at leading-logarithmic order, $\kappa_{\rm{cont}} \simeq \frac{g^2 C_F T m_{\rm{D}}^2}{6\pi} (\ln\frac{T}{m_{\rm{D}}} + ...)$, where $m_{\rm{D,cont}}^2 = \frac{2 N_c + N_f}{6} g^2 T^2$.
* The Debye mass in the classical lattice theory is $m_{\rm{D,latt}}^2 = \frac{2 N_c \Sigma}{4\pi} \frac{g^2 T}{a}$ with $\Sigma = 3.1759115356$.
* At strong coupling on the lattice ($\beta_L \ll 1$), the dimensionless quantity $a^3 \kappa_{\rm{latt}}$ scales as $1/\beta_L^{5/2}$, while at weak coupling ($\beta_L \gg 1$) it scales as $(1/\beta_L^2) \ln \beta_L$.
* The non-perturbative numerical results deviate from the leading-order weak-coupling prediction for $\beta_L \lesssim 100$ and are always larger than the perturbative estimate, with a crossover in behavior occurring for $\beta_L = 1$ to $10$.

## Context

This work extends the use of classical lattice gauge theory, previously applied to Chern-Simons number diffusion (Ambjorn et al.) and the imaginary part of the heavy-quark potential (Laine, Philipsen, Tassler 2007), to the heavy quark thermalization problem. The authors argue that this analogue theory is an alternative to two other widely used frameworks: weak-coupling QCD truncated at first non-trivial order, which shows very large ${\cal O}(g)$ corrections for dynamical quantities (Caron-Huot, Moore 2008; Arnold, Moore, Yaffe 2003), and ${\cal N}=4$ Super-Yang-Mills theory in the large coupling limit via AdS/CFT, where matching to QCD is ambiguous. The results provide guidance for subsequent efforts to reconstruct the electric field spectral function from Euclidean lattice Monte Carlo simulations, and suggest that the leading-order weak-coupling expansion significantly underestimates the heavy quark thermalization rate at realistic couplings.
