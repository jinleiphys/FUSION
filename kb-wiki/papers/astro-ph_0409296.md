# Nonuniform Neutron-Rich Matter and Coherent Neutrino Scattering

> Nonuniform neutron-rich matter present in both core-collapse supernovae and neutron-star crusts is described in terms of a semiclassical model that reproduces nuclear-matter properties and includes long-range Coulomb interactions. The neutron-neutron correlation function and the corresponding static structure factor are calculated from molecular dynamics simulations involving 40,000 to 100,000 nucleons. The static structure factor describes coherent neutrino scattering which is expected to dominate the neutrino opacity. At low momentum transfers the static structure factor is found to be small because of ion screening. In contrast, at intermediate momentum transfers the static structure factor displays a large peak due to coherent scattering from all the neutrons in a cluster. This peak moves to higher momentum transfers and decreases in amplitude as the density increases. A large static structure factor at zero momentum transfer, indicative of large density fluctuations during a first-order phase transition, may increase the neutrino opacity. However, no evidence of such an increase has been found. Therefore, it is unlikely that the system undergoes a simple first-order phase transition. It is found that corrections to the commonly used single heavy nucleus approximation first appear at a density of the order of $10^{13}$ g/cm$^3$ and increase rapidly with increasing density. Thus, neutrino opacities are overestimated in the single heavy nucleus approximation relative to the complete molecular dynamics simulations.

## Key claim

The authors find that the static structure factor at zero momentum transfer shows no evidence of large density fluctuations that would indicate a simple first-order phase transition, despite such fluctuations potentially increasing neutrino opacity. Corrections to the commonly used single heavy nucleus approximation first appear at a density of approximately 10^13 g/cm^3 and increase rapidly with density, meaning that neutrino opacities are overestimated in that approximation relative to the full molecular dynamics simulations.

## Method

Molecular dynamics simulations with 40,000 to 100,000 nucleons were performed using a semiclassical model with two-body nuclear interactions (intermediate-range attraction and short-range repulsion) plus screened Coulomb interactions, with parameters adjusted to reproduce saturation density and binding energy of symmetric nuclear matter. The neutron-neutron correlation function g(r) was computed from the particle trajectories, and the static structure factor S(q) was obtained via Fourier transform of g(r), with an analytic extrapolation for distances beyond half the simulation box length to control finite-size effects. A cluster algorithm was also introduced to determine cluster composition for comparison with the single heavy nucleus approximation.

## Key numbers

- At baryon density ρ = 0.01 fm⁻³ (approximately 2×10¹³ g/cm³), the potential energy per particle is −5.377(1) MeV, the pressure is 6.9×10⁻³ MeV/fm³, and S(0) ≈ 0.790.
- At ρ = 0.025 fm⁻³, the potential energy per particle is −5.145(1) MeV, the pressure is 3.2×10⁻² MeV/fm³, and S(0) ≈ 0.344.
- At ρ = 0.050 fm⁻³, the potential energy per particle is −4.463(1) MeV, the pressure is 0.13 MeV/fm³, and S(0) ≈ 0.139.
- At ρ = 0.075 fm⁻³, the potential energy per particle is −3.686(1) MeV, the pressure is 0.33 MeV/fm³, and S(0) ≈ 0.077.
- The simulation at ρ = 0.01 fm⁻³ used 40,000 nucleons and a total evolution time of 1,287,000 fm/c; simulations at 0.025 and 0.050 fm⁻³ used 100,000 nucleons.
- The wavelength of a 10 MeV neutrino is approximately 120 fm.
- The screening length was arbitrarily decreased to λ = 10 fm to control finite-size effects.
- The model parameters are a = 110 MeV, b = −26 MeV, c = 24 MeV, and Λ = 1.25 fm².

## Context

This work extends earlier Monte Carlo simulations with 4,000 particles by Horowitz et al. (2004) to larger systems (up to 100,000 nucleons) using molecular dynamics, enabling more accurate calculation of the static structure factor at low momentum transfers. The results are compared to the single heavy nucleus approximation used in most current supernova simulations, showing that this approximation overestimates neutrino opacities at densities above approximately 10¹³ g/cm³. The study also relates to work by Watanabe et al. (2003) on quantum molecular dynamics simulations of pasta phases, and to the broader question of nucleon/nuclear duality analogous to quark/hadron duality studied at Jefferson Laboratory (Jeschonnek, 2002). The findings enable improved neutrino transport calculations in core-collapse supernova models.
