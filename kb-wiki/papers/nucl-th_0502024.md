# Distribution of the largest fragment in the Lattice Gas Model

> The distribution of the largest fragment is studied in different regions of the Lattice Gas model phase diagram. We show that first and second order transitions can be clearly distinguished in the grancanonical ensemble, while signals typical of a continuous transition are seen inside the coexistence region if a mass conservation constraint is applied. Some possible implications of these findings for heavy ion multifragmentation experiments are discussed.

## Key claim

The distribution of the largest fragment can serve as an order parameter for the liquid-gas phase transition in the Lattice Gas model, but its ability to distinguish first-order from continuous transitions depends critically on the statistical ensemble: bimodal distributions appear in the grandcanonical ensemble, while mass conservation in the canonical ensemble deforms the distribution and can mimic signals of a continuous transition even inside the coexistence region of a first-order transition.

## Method

The authors performed Metropolis Monte Carlo simulations of the Lattice Gas model on cubic lattices (linear sizes L=5,6,8,12,16) in grandcanonical, canonical, and microcanonical ensembles. They analyzed the distribution of the largest cluster size A_M using the Coniglio-Klein cluster definition, studying its behavior as a function of temperature and density across the phase diagram, and tested Delta-scaling laws by examining the correlation between the mean and variance of A_M distributions.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature of the Lattice Gas model: T_c ≈ 1.22ϵ (where ϵ is the nearest-neighbor coupling constant)
- Lattice sizes used: L=5,6,8,12,16 (cubic lattices)
- System size at the critical density: ⟨A_t⟩ = 256 particles for the 8x8x8 lattice at ρ/ρ₀=1/2
- Temperatures explored range from T=0.3ϵ to T=2.3ϵ for the constant-density scaling analysis
- Densities studied: ρ/ρ₀ = 1/4, 1/2, and 3/4

## Context

This work extends the analysis of largest fragment distributions in finite systems, building on the Ising Model with Fixed Magnetization (IMFM) study by Carmona et al. (2002), which showed approximate first scaling law behavior at subcritical densities. The paper addresses contradictory experimental findings: bimodal distributions in Au+Au collisions from the Indra-Aladin collaboration (Pichon et al.) versus scaling law changes in Xe+Cu collisions (Frankland et al.). The results enable interpretation of heavy-ion multifragmentation experiments by showing that conservation laws, not finite-size effects alone, can produce apparent continuous-transition signals in first-order phase transitions.
