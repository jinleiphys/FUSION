# Unified semiclassical approach to isoscalar collective modes in heavy nuclei

> A semiclassical model based on the solution of the Vlasov equation for finite systems with a sharp moving surface has been used to study the isoscalar quadrupole and octupole collective modes in heavy spherical nuclei. Within this model, a unified description of both low-energy surface modes and higher-energy giant resonances has been achieved by introducing a coupling between surface vibrations and the motion of single nucleons. Analytical expressions for the collective response functions of different multipolarity can be derived by using a separable approximation for the residual interaction between nucleons. The response functions obtained in this way give a good qualitative description of the quadrupole and octupole response in heavy nuclei. Although shell effects are not explicitly included in the theory, our semiclassical response functions are very similar to the quantum ones. This happens because of the well known close relation between classical trajectories and shell structure. The role played by particular nucleon trajectories and their connection with various features of the nuclear response is displayed most clearly in the present approach, we discuss in some detail the damping of low-energy octupole vibrations and give an explicit expression showing that only nucleons moving on triangular orbits can contribute to this damping.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a unified semiclassical description of both low-energy surface modes and high-energy giant resonances for isoscalar quadrupole and octupole collective modes in heavy spherical nuclei can be achieved by solving the Vlasov equation with moving-surface boundary conditions, but the model provides only a *qualitative* description and does not explicitly include shell effects, although the semiclassical response functions are very similar to quantum ones.

## Method

The authors solved the linearized Vlasov kinetic equation for a finite system with a sharp moving surface, treating the nuclear surface as a collective dynamical variable like in the liquid drop model. A separable approximation for the residual interaction between nucleons, of the form \(\kappa_L r_1^L r_2^L\), was employed to derive analytical expressions for the collective response functions. The coupling between single-particle motion and surface vibrations was introduced through boundary conditions that enforce mirror reflection of particles in the reference frame of the moving nuclear surface, with a self-consistency condition involving surface tension to determine the time dependence of the surface variables.

## Key numbers

* For a hypothetical nucleus of \(A=208\) nucleons, the quadrupole strength function shows a giant quadrupole peak and a broad low-energy bump when moving-surface boundary conditions are used, while the fixed-surface response shows only the giant peak.
* For the octupole strength function in the same \(A=208\) system, the zero-order response concentrates strength around 8 MeV and 24 MeV, the fixed-surface collective response has peaks around 6-7 MeV and 20 MeV, and the moving-surface response shows a large double hump at low energy and a novel resonance-like structure at about 13 MeV.
* The residual interaction strength \(\kappa_L\) was determined phenomenologically to be approximately \(2\kappa_{BM}\) for \(L=2,3\), where \(\kappa_{BM} = -\frac{4\pi}{3}\frac{m\omega_0^2}{A R^{2L-2}}\) with \(\omega_0 \approx 41 A^{-1/3}\) MeV.
* The equilibrium nuclear radius was taken as \(R = 1.2 A^{1/3}\) fm, and the surface tension parameter was \(\sigma \approx 1\) MeV fm\(^{-2}\).
* For octupole vibrations, the friction coefficient \(\gamma_{L=3}\) receives contributions only from the term with \(n=1\) and \(N=3\), meaning only nucleons moving on closed triangular trajectories contribute to the damping.

## Context

This work extends semiclassical models based on fluid dynamics (e.g., Holz (year not given)), which could explain giant resonances but failed to describe low-energy collective modes, by explicitly including single-particle degrees of freedom and their coupling to surface motion. The approach contrasts with quantum studies (e.g., Bortignon (year not given), Bertsch and Borridge (year not given), Lac (year not given)) that showed the importance of this coupling for low-energy modes. The model enables a unified analytical description of both low- and high-energy isoscalar response in heavy nuclei, and the explicit expression for the damping of octupole vibrations in terms of triangular nucleon trajectories provides a clear classical interpretation of a quantum damping mechanism.
