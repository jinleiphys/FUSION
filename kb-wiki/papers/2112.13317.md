# Microscopic collective inertial masses for nuclear reaction in the presence of nucleonic effective mass

> Collective inertial mass coefficients with respect to translational, relative, and rotational motions are microscopically calculated, along the collective reaction path self-consistently determined, based on the adiabatic self-consistent collective coordinate (ASCC) method. The impact of the time-odd component of the mean-field potential on the inertial masses are investigated. The results are compared with those calculated with the cranking formulae. The inertial masses based on the ASCC method reproduce the exact total nuclear mass for the translational motion as well as the exact reduced masses as the asymptotic values for the relative and rotational motions. In contrast, the cranking formulae fail to do so. This is due to the fact that the (local) Galilean invariance is properly restored in the ASCC method, but violated in the cranking formulae. A model Hamiltonian for low-energy nuclear reaction is constructed with the microscopically derived potentials and inertial masses. The astrophysical S-factors are calculated, which indicates the importance of microscopic calculation of proper inertial masses.

## Key claim

The ASCC method reproduces the exact total nuclear mass for translational motion and the exact reduced masses for relative and rotational motions as asymptotic values, even when the mean-field potential has velocity dependence (nucleonic effective mass $m^*/m \neq 1$), whereas the cranking formulae fail to do so because they violate local Galilean invariance.

## Method

The authors applied the adiabatic self-consistent collective coordinate (ASCC) method to a modified BKN energy density functional that includes a $\rho\tau - \mathbf{j}^2$ term to introduce velocity dependence and nucleonic effective mass. They solved the ASCC equations using the finite amplitude method (FAM) on a 3D Cartesian mesh to determine the collective reaction path and the local generators $\hat{Q}(q)$ and $\hat{P}(q)$, from which the inertial mass $M(R)$ was obtained via coordinate transformation from the collective coordinate $q$ to the relative distance $R$. For comparison, they also calculated inertial masses using both the non-perturbative and perturbative cranking formulae on constrained Hartree-Fock (CHF) states.

## Key numbers

- For the $\alpha+\alpha \rightarrow {}^8\text{Be}$ system, the model space was $12 \times 12 \times 18$ fm$^3$ with a mesh size of 1.0 fm.
- For the ${}^{16}\text{O}+\alpha \rightarrow {}^{20}\text{Ne}$ system, the model space was $12 \times 12 \times 18$ fm$^3$ with a mesh size of 1.1 fm.
- For the ${}^{16}\text{O}+{}^{16}\text{O} \rightarrow {}^{32}\text{S}$ system, the model space was $12 \times 12 \times 24$ fm$^3$ with a mesh size of 1.1 fm.
- The effective mass in the modified BKN functional is given by $1/(2m^*(\mathbf{r})) = 1/(2m) + B_3 \rho(\mathbf{r})$, where $B_3$ controls the deviation from the bare nucleon mass.
- The cranking formula for translation yields $M_{\text{cr}}^{\text{np}} = Am$ only when the mean-field Hamiltonian satisfies Galilean invariance $[\hat{h}_{\text{HF}}, \hat{x}_k] = (-i/m)\hat{p}_k$; otherwise it gives a wrong mass $M_{\text{cr}} \neq Am$.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous ASCC calculations (Wen and Nakatsukasa, 2016, 2017) from velocity-independent potentials to velocity-dependent potentials with nucleonic effective mass, addressing a known failure of the cranking formula (Bohr and Mottelson, 1975; Ring and Schuck, 1980) to reproduce the total mass for translational motion when the mean-field has velocity dependence. The ASCC method provides a microscopic foundation for constructing collective Hamiltonians for low-energy nuclear reactions, enabling calculation of astrophysical $S$-factors with proper inertial masses that respect Galilean invariance, unlike the cranking approach of Baran (2011) or the original Inglis formula (1954).
