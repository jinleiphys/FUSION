# Interpolating between small- and large-$g$ expansions using Bayesian Model Mixing

> Bayesian Model Mixing (BMM) is a statistical technique that can be used to combine models that are predictive in different input domains into a composite distribution that has improved predictive power over the entire input space. We explore the application of BMM to the mixing of two expansions of a function of a coupling constant $g$ that are valid at small and large values of $g$ respectively. This type of problem is quite common in nuclear physics, where physical properties are straightforwardly calculable in strong and weak interaction limits or at low and high densities or momentum transfers, but difficult to calculate in between. Interpolation between these limits is often accomplished by a suitable interpolating function, e.g., Padé approximants, but it is then unclear how to quantify the uncertainty of the interpolant. We address this problem in the simple context of the partition function of zero-dimensional $ϕ^4$ theory, for which the (asymptotic) expansion at small $g$ and the (convergent) expansion at large $g$ are both known. We consider three mixing methods: linear mixture BMM, localized bivariate BMM, and localized multivariate BMM with Gaussian processes. We find that employing a Gaussian process in the intermediate region between the two predictive models leads to the best results of the three methods. The methods and validation strategies we present here should be generalizable to other nuclear physics settings.

## Key claim

Bayesian Model Mixing (BMM) can produce reliable interpolants with principled uncertainty quantification between small- and large-coupling expansions, but the method requires careful choice of mixing strategy; employing a Gaussian process in the intermediate region between the two predictive models yields the best results among the three mixing methods tested, though the authors note the methods and validation strategies should be generalizable to other nuclear physics settings rather than claiming universal applicability.

## Method

The authors applied three Bayesian Model Mixing approaches to combine the asymptotic small-g expansion and the convergent large-g expansion of the zero-dimensional phi^4 theory partition function: linear mixture BMM with input-independent weights, localized bivariate BMM with precision-weighted combination of the two expansions, and localized multivariate BMM where a Gaussian process was introduced as a third model in the intermediate region. Each expansion was treated as a probability distribution with Gaussian truncation errors, where the standard deviations were modeled using either uninformative or informative forms that account for the factorial growth or decay of the series coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The small-g expansion coefficients follow s_{2k} = sqrt(2) * Gamma(2k + 1/2) / k! * (-4)^k, with odd coefficients s_{2k+1} = 0
- The large-g expansion coefficients follow l_k = Gamma(k/2 + 1/4) / (2 * k!) * (-1/2)^k
- The uninformative truncation error for small-g at even N_s is sigma_{N_s}(g) = Gamma(N_s + 3) * g^{N_s + 2} * cbar
- The informative truncation error for small-g at even N_s is sigma_{N_s}(g) = Gamma(N_s/2 + 1) * (4g)^{N_s + 2} * cbar
- The uninformative truncation error for large-g is sigma_{N_l}(g) = 1 / Gamma(N_l + 2) * 1 / g^{N_l + 3/2} * dbar
- The informative truncation error for large-g is sigma_{N_l}(g) = (1/(4g))^{N_l + 3/2} * 1 / Gamma(N_l/2 + 3/2) * dbar

## Context

This work extends the use of Bayesian methods for combining models predictive in different input domains, a problem common in nuclear physics where physical properties are calculable in strong and weak interaction limits or at low and high densities but difficult to calculate in between. The authors build on prior work using Padé approximants for interpolation (Honda 2014) and constrained extrapolations with order-dependent mappings (Wellenhofer 2020), addressing the key limitation that those methods lack principled uncertainty quantification. The results enable future application of BMM techniques to more complex nuclear physics problems such as quark-hadron phase transitions, cold atom expansions, and momentum transfer limits.
