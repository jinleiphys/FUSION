# Effects of Neutrino Trapping on Thermodynamic Properties of Nuclear ``Pasta''

> Geometrical structure of matter at subnuclear densities is investigated in the presence of a degenerate gas of neutrinos as encountered in stellar collapse. The crystalline phases with spherical, cylindrical and planar nuclei as well as with spherical and cylindrical nuclear bubbles are considered by using a compressible liquid-drop model. This model allows for uncertainties in the lepton fraction $Y_{\rm L}$ in addition to those in the nuclear surface tension $E_{\rm surf}$ and in the proton chemical potential in bulk neutron matter $μ_{\rm p}^{(0)}$. The phase diagrams obtained at zero temperature show that only the phases with rod-like and slab-like nuclei occur at typical values of $Y_{\rm L}$, $E_{\rm surf}$ and $μ_{\rm p}^{(0)}$, whereas the bubble phases, especially with spherical bubbles, are at best expected at hypothetically low values of $Y_{\rm L}$ and/or $E_{\rm surf}$. For the rod-like and slab-like nuclei, thermally induced displacements are calculated from their respective elastic constants. It is found that at temperatures appropriate to supernova cores, thermal fluctuations would destroy the layered lattice of slab-like nuclei almost independently of the nuclear models and of the degree of the neutrino degeneracy.

## Key claim

The paper finds that in supernova matter with trapped neutrinos, only rod-like and slab-like nuclear "pasta" phases occur at typical values of lepton fraction, surface tension, and proton chemical potential, while bubble phases require hypothetically low values of these parameters. At supernova-core temperatures, thermal fluctuations would destroy the layered lattice of slab-like nuclei almost independently of the nuclear model or neutrino degeneracy.

## Method

The authors used a compressible liquid-drop model extended from Baym, Bethe, and Pethick (1971) to describe zero-temperature supernova matter at subnuclear densities, incorporating a degenerate neutrino gas as an ideal ultrarelativistic Fermi gas. They calculated equilibrium phase diagrams by minimizing the total energy density with respect to nuclear size, neutron drip, beta equilibrium, and pressure at fixed baryon density and lepton fraction, then evaluated thermally induced displacements of rod-like and slab-like nuclei from elastic constants derived by Pethick and Potekhin (1999).

## Key numbers

- Lepton fraction range considered: 0.2 ≤ Y_L ≤ 0.4, wider than the usually adopted 0.3–0.38
- Melting density ρ_m approaches saturation density ρ_s ≈ 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^−3 as Y_L increases
- Energy difference between successive pasta phases: 1–10 keV per nucleon, an order of magnitude larger than for neutron star matter
- Curvature thermodynamic potential ω_c required for bubble phases: at least ~0.5 MeV fm^−1, compared to estimates of ~0.1–0.2 MeV fm^−1 at x~0.3 from Kolehmainen et al.
- Crossover temperature T_0 from quantum to thermal fluctuations: ~10^7 K for planar nuclei, ~10^9 K for cylindrical nuclei
- Thermal fluctuation calculations performed at T = 10 MeV and L = 10 km, appropriate to supernova cores

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis of neutron star matter (Watanabe, Iida, Sato, 2000) to include neutrino trapping effects relevant to stellar collapse, building on the liquid-drop model of Baym, Bethe, and Pethick (1971) and the elastic constant formalism of Pethick and Potekhin (1999). The results contradict the three-dimensional Thomas-Fermi calculations of Lassaut et al. (1987), who found all pasta phases including bubbles occur for a finite pressure range; the authors attribute this difference to the adopted bulk nuclear matter properties at proton fraction ~0.3. The finding that thermal fluctuations destroy the slab-like lattice in supernova cores, unlike the marginal instability found for neutron star crusts, has implications for neutrino transport and hydrodynamics in collapse-driven supernova simulations.
