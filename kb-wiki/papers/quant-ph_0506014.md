# Reconstruction of the optical potential from scattering data

> We propose a method for reconstruction of the optical potential from scattering data. The algorithm is a two-step procedure. In the first step the real part of the potential is determined analytically via solution of the Marchenko equation. At this point we use a diagonal Padé approximant of the corresponding unitary $S$-matrix. In the second step the imaginary part of the potential is determined via the phase equation of the variable phase approach. We assume that the real and the imaginary parts of the optical potential are proportional. We use the phase equation to calculate the proportionality coefficient. A numerical algorithm is developed for a single and for coupled partial waves. The developed procedure is applied to analysis of $^{1}S_{0}$ $NN$, $^{3}SD_{1}$ $NN$, $P31$ $π^{-} N$ and $S01$ $K^{+}N$ data.

# Reconstruction of the optical potential from scattering data

## Key claim

The authors propose a two-step inversion method that reconstructs a complex optical potential from scattering data by first solving the Marchenko equation for the real part using a diagonal Pade approximant of the unitary S-matrix, then determining the imaginary part via the phase equation of the variable phase approach under the assumption that the real and imaginary parts of the optical potential are proportional. The method is claimed to be free of the shortcomings of traditional parameter-fitting approaches, but its validity depends on the assumption that the real and imaginary parts are proportional and that the integrals involving the phase functions remain approximately unchanged when the potential becomes complex.

## Method

The algorithm proceeds in two steps: first, the real part of the optical potential is reconstructed analytically by solving the Marchenko integral equation, using a diagonal Pade approximant of the unitary S-matrix to obtain a degenerate kernel that permits an analytical solution; second, the imaginary part is determined via the phase equation of the variable phase approach, where the proportionality coefficient between the real and imaginary parts is calculated from the known inelasticity parameter. The method is developed for both single partial waves and coupled partial waves, with the coupled-channel case requiring a more complex Pade approximant for the S-matrix and a generalized Marchenko equation involving 2x2 matrix functions.

## Key numbers

- For the single-channel case, the proportionality relation gives cos^2(rho) approximately equal to exp(-2*alpha*delta^(0)), where rho is the inelasticity parameter, alpha is the proportionality coefficient, and delta^(0) is the phase shift from the real potential.
- The method is applied to four systems: 1S0 NN, 3SD1 NN, P31 pi- N, and S01 K+ N scattering data.
- The input data for the Marchenko inversion include the S-matrix S(q) for 0 < q < +infinity, bound state momenta q_j, and normalization matrices M_j for j = 1,...,n_b.
- The Pade approximant for single-channel S-matrix takes the form S(q) = (f_2(q) - i f_1(q))/(f_2(q) + i f_1(q)), where f_1 and f_2 are odd and even polynomials of q, with the degree of f_1 being one less than the degree of f_2.

## Context

This work extends the classical inverse-scattering approaches of Gelfand, Levitan, and Marchenko to construct complex optical potentials, which is not possible with standard energy-independent potential inversion methods. The authors note that direct application of these classical approaches to constructing local two-body potentials that are phase-equivalent to effective potentials in composite-particle reactions is impossible because such potentials must be complex above the inelastic threshold but real below it, requirements that are incompatible for energy-independent potentials. The method addresses shortcomings of traditional empirical fitting approaches used by Green and Geramb, which involve fitting many nonlinear parameters simultaneously and lack correlation of parameters obtained at various energies. The approach enables reconstruction of energy-dependent optical potentials from scattering data without assuming a specific analytic form for the potential.
