# Plasmon mass scale in classical nonequilibrium gauge theory

> Classical lattice Yang-Mills calculations provide a good way to understand different nonequilibrium phenomena in nonperturbatively overoccupied systems. Above the Debye scale the classical theory can be matched smoothly to kinetic theory. The aim of this work is to study the limits of this quasiparticle picture by determining the plasmon mass in classical real time Yang-Mills theory on a lattice in 3 spatial dimensions. We compare three methods to determine the plasmon mass: a hard thermal loop expression in terms of the particle distribution, an effective dispersion relation constructed from fields and their time derivatives, and by measuring oscillations between electric and magnetic field modes after artificially introducing a homogeneous color electric field. We find that a version of the dispersion relation that uses electric fields and their time derivatives agrees with the other methods within 50%.

## Key claim
The paper finds that a version of the dispersion relation using electric fields and their time derivatives agrees with the other methods within 50%, but the dispersion relation method consistently gives a plasmon mass about 50% higher than the uniform electric field and HTL methods, suggesting that some electric field energy in the infrared resides in nonplasmon modes.

## Method
The authors performed classical real-time SU(2) Yang-Mills simulations on a three-dimensional lattice with a Gaussian initial condition characterized by a dominant momentum scale Δ and occupation number n₀. They compared three methods to extract the plasmon mass: (1) a hard-thermal-loop (HTL) expression integrating over the quasiparticle distribution, (2) an effective dispersion relation constructed from Coulomb-gauge fields and their time derivatives, and (3) measuring oscillations between electric and magnetic field modes after artificially introducing a homogeneous color electric field.

## Key numbers
- The dispersion relation method yields ω²/Δ² = 0.256 when extrapolated to zero momentum (from Fig. 5 caption)
- The uniform electric field method gives ω²/Δ² ≈ 0.14 with a damping rate γ²/Δ² ≈ 0.002 (from Fig. 4 caption)
- The HTL method approaches the uniform electric field result in the continuum limit (from Fig. 7 analysis)
- The plasmon mass squared scales as t^{-2/7} at late times, consistent with the power law proposed by Kurkela (2012) (from Fig. 10)
- Changing the added uniform electric field energy between 3% and 30% of total energy changes ω²/Δ² by approximately 5% and the damping rate by approximately 25%
- The HTL method shows non-negligible ultraviolet cutoff dependence, while the uniform electric field method is ultraviolet safe

## Context
This work extends previous classical Yang-Mills simulations by Krasnitz (2000), Romatschke (2005, 2006), and Berges (2008, 2012) that studied plasma instabilities and thermalization in overoccupied gauge theories. The paper systematically compares three methods for extracting the plasmon mass, finding that the uniform electric field method is most reliable but computationally expensive. The results enable future studies of anisotropic systems exhibiting plasma instabilities and help establish the limits of the quasiparticle picture in classical gauge theory.
