# Internal heating and thermal emission from old neutron stars: Constraints on dense-matter and gravitational physics

> The equilibrium composition of neutron star matter is achieved through weak interactions (direct and inverse beta decays), which proceed on relatively long time scales. If the density of a matter element is perturbed, it will relax to the new chemical equilibrium through non-equilibrium reactions, which produce entropy that is partly released through neutrino emission, while a similar fraction heats the matter and is eventually radiated as thermal photons. We examined two possible mechanisms causing such density perturbations: 1) the reduction in centrifugal force caused by spin-down (particularly in millisecond pulsars), leading to "rotochemical heating", and 2) a hypothetical time-variation of the gravitational constant, as predicted by some theories of gravity and current cosmological models, leading to "gravitochemical heating". If only slow weak interactions are allowed in the neutron star (modified Urca reactions, with or without Cooper pairing), rotochemical heating can account for the observed ultraviolet emission from the closest millisecond pulsar, PSR J0437-4715, which also provides a constraint on |dG/dt| of the same order as the best available in the literature.

## Key claim

If only slow weak interactions (modified Urca reactions, with or without Cooper pairing) are allowed in the neutron star, rotochemical heating can account for the observed ultraviolet emission from the closest millisecond pulsar, PSR J0437-4715, and also provides a constraint on the time variation of the gravitational constant of the same order as the best available in the literature.

## Method

The authors modeled the thermal evolution of neutron stars by solving coupled differential equations for internal temperature and chemical imbalances (deviations from beta equilibrium) driven by two mechanisms: spin-down reducing centrifugal force (rotochemical heating) and a hypothetical time-varying gravitational constant (gravitochemical heating). They computed stationary-state solutions where heating balances cooling and chemical forcing balances reaction rates, then compared predicted surface temperatures to observational constraints from the millisecond pulsar PSR J0437-4715 for multiple equations of state and stellar masses.

## Key numbers

- Rotochemical heating predicts effective temperature for PSR J0437-4715 in the range T_s = (6.9 - 7.9) x 10^4 K, about 20% lower than the blackbody fit of Kargaltsev (2004)
- Stationary photon luminosity: L_gamma^st ~ 10^(30-31) |(P_dot_{-20} / P_ms^3) + (G_dot/G / 3 x 10^(-11) yr^(-1))|^(8/7) erg s^(-1)
- Stationary surface temperature: T_s^st ~ (2-3) x 10^5 |(P_dot_{-20} / P_ms^3) + (G_dot/G / 3 x 10^(-11) yr^(-1))|^(2/7) K
- Equilibration timescale: tau_st ~ 2 x 10^7 |(P_dot_{-20} / P_ms^3) + (G_dot/G / 3 x 10^(-11) yr^(-1))|^(-6/7) yr
- Upper limit on |G_dot/G| from gravitochemical heating: < 2 x 10^(-10) yr^(-1) for general case including direct Urca reactions
- Restrictive upper limit on |G_dot/G| for equations of state allowing only modified Urca reactions: < 4 x 10^(-12) yr^(-1)
- Spin-down age of PSR J0437-4715: tau_sd ~ 5 x 10^9 yr
- White dwarf companion cooling age: tau_WD ~ (2.5 - 5.3) x 10^9 yr
- Upper limit on surface temperature for pulsar J0108-1431: T_s < 8.8 x 10^4 K

## Context

This work extends the rotochemical heating formalism developed by Reisenegger (1995, 1997) and Fernandez (2005) and the gravitochemical heating analysis by Jofre (2006), applying both mechanisms to explain thermal emission from old neutron stars. The results contradict standard cooling models that predict much lower temperatures for old pulsars, and the gravitochemical constraint on |G_dot/G| is competitive with other methods probing similar timescales such as binary neutron star masses (Thorsett 1996) and helioseismology (Guenther 1998), while probing shorter timescales (~10^8 yr) than most cosmological methods. The work enables future constraints on dense matter physics and gravitational constant variation through an increased sample of neutron stars with measured thermal emission or good upper limits.
