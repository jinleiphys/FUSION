# Some forgotten features of the Bose Einstein Correlations

> Notwithstanding the visible maturity of the subject of Bose-Einstein Correlations (BEC), as witnessed nowadays, we would like to bring to ones attention two points, which apparently did not received attention they deserve: the problem of the choice of the form of $C_2(Q)$ correlation function when effects of partial coherence of the hadronizing source are to be included and the feasibility to model effects of Bose-Einstein statistics, in particular the BEC, by direct numerical simulations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard form of the two-particle Bose-Einstein correlation function \(C_2(Q)\) implicitly assumes a specific structure of the hadronizing source (independent coherent and chaotic subsources), and that an alternative form is required when the source is partially coherent due to an external field; additionally, the authors claim that direct numerical modeling of Bose-Einstein statistics via particle bunching is feasible and preferable to using afterburners, though still far from completion.

## Method

The authors compare two competing forms of the two-particle correlation function \(C_2(Q)\) for identical bosons: the standard form \(C_2(Q) = 1 + \lambda \cdot \Omega(Q\cdot r)\) and an alternative form \(C_2(Q) = 1 + 2p(1-p)\sqrt{\Omega(Q\cdot r)} + p^2 \Omega(Q\cdot r)^2\), showing that each encodes different assumptions about the hadronizing source. They then review existing numerical approaches to modeling Bose-Einstein correlations in Monte Carlo event generators, including the Metropolis method with fully symmetrized wave functions (Zajc 1999), the rejection method (Cramer 2005), and the cell-based approach (OMT 2000), and propose their own "Quantum Clan Model" based on forming bunches of particles called Elementary Emitting Cells (EECs) with a Bose-Einstein (geometrical) distribution within each cell.

## Key numbers

- The chaoticity parameter \(\lambda\) ranges from 0 (totally coherent source) to 1 (totally chaotic source), with the alternative parameter \(p\) in \((0,1)\) retaining essentially the same meaning.
- For extremely high multiplicity cases, the strength of BEC as given by \(\lambda\) exceeds 2 (even for \(C_2(Q)\)), a fact not noticed before.
- The proposed Quantum Clan Model yields a final multiplicity distribution in the form of the Polya-Aeppli (geometric-Poisson) distribution, which differs from the Negative Binomial distribution only for very small multiplicities.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing discussion on the proper form of the Bose-Einstein correlation function, contrasting the standard approach advocated by Andersson, Lonnblad, and Sjostrand (1996) with the alternative form proposed by Weiner (2000). The authors' own prior work (Kozlov, Utyuzh, Wilk, and Wlodarczyk 2005) showed that both forms are correct but describe different source structures. The paper also addresses the practical challenge of incorporating quantum Bose-Einstein statistics into classical Monte Carlo event generators, building on earlier numerical attempts by Zajc (1999), Cramer (2005), and the OMT collaboration (2000), and proposes the Quantum Clan Model as a more efficient alternative that enables future simulations to include Bose-Einstein effects from the start rather than via afterburners.
