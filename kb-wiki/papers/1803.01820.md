# Optical model potential analysis of $\bar nA$ and $nA$ interactions

> We use a momentum-dependent optical model potential to analyze the annihilation cross sections of antineutron $\bar n$ on C, Al, Fe, Cu, Ag, Sn, and Pb nuclei for projectile momenta $p_{\rm lab}$ $\lesssim$ 500 MeV/$c$. We obtain good description of annihilation cross sections data of Barbina {\it et al.} [Nucl.~Phys.~A {\bf 612}, ~346~(1997)] and of Astrua {\it et al.} [Nucl.~Phys.~A {\bf 697},~209~(2002)] which exhibit an interesting dependence of the cross sections on the $p_{\rm lab}$ as well as on the target atomic mass number $A$. We also obtain the neutron ($n$) non-elastic reaction cross sections for the same targets. Contrasting the $nA$ reaction cross sections $σ^{nA}_{\rm rec}$ to the $\bar nA$ annihilation cross sections $σ^{\bar nA}_{\rm ann}$, we find the $σ^{\bar nA}_{\rm ann}$ is significantly larger than the $σ^{nA}_{\rm rec}$, that is, the $σ^{\bar nA}_{\rm ann}$/$σ^{nA}_{\rm rec}$ cross section ratio lies between the values of order 1.8 and 3.8 in the momentum region where comparison is possible. The dependence of the annihilation cross section on the projectile charge is also examined in comparison with antiproton $\bar p$. Here we predict the $\bar pA$ annihilation cross section on the simplest assumption that both $\bar pA$ and $\bar nA$ interactions have the same nuclear part of the optical model potential but differs only on the electrostatic Coulomb interaction. Deviation from such simple model extrapolation in measurements will provide new information on the difference between $\bar nA$ and $\bar pA$ potentials.

## Key claim
The authors use a momentum-dependent optical model potential (POMP) to describe antineutron-nucleus annihilation cross sections, finding that the ratio of antineutron annihilation to neutron reaction cross sections lies between about 1.5 and 3.8 across the momentum region where comparison is possible, with the exact range depending on target and momentum.

## Method
The authors constructed a phenomenological optical model potential (POMP) with a momentum-dependent real part and momentum-independent imaginary volume and surface terms, fitted to reproduce experimental antineutron annihilation cross sections on C, Al, Fe, Cu, Ag, Sn, and Pb targets for projectile momenta up to 500 MeV/c. They solved the Schrödinger equation using the ECIS97 distorted-wave code to obtain reaction cross sections, and compared results with the Koning-Delaroche optical model potential (KD-OMP) for neutron-nucleus reactions and with experimental data from Barbina et al. (1997) and Astrua et al. (2002). For antiproton predictions, they assumed the same nuclear optical potential as for antineutrons but added the electrostatic Coulomb interaction.

## Key numbers
- The σᵅⁿᵅⁿᵅⁿ/σⁿᴬ ratio for carbon is about 1.5 at p_lab ≈ 160 MeV/c and about 2.3 at p_lab ≈ 400 MeV/c.
- The σᵅⁿᵅⁿᵅⁿ/σⁿᴬ ratio for iron is about 1.4 to 1.6 between p_lab values of 120 and 400 MeV/c.
- For all targets, the σᵅⁿᵅⁿᵅⁿ/σⁿᴬ ratio varies between about 1.5 and 3.8 in the momentum region where comparison is possible.
- The real potential depth V'_o for antineutron ranges from 52.00 MeV for ¹²C to 110.00 MeV for ²⁰⁶Pb.
- The antineutron volume absorption W_o ranges from 12.00 MeV for ¹²C to 2.80 MeV for ²⁰⁶Pb.
- The antineutron surface absorption W_oD is a constant 5.98 MeV for all targets.
- The antineutron radius parameter r_V for ²⁰⁶Pb is 1.785 fm, about 45% larger than the neutron r_V of 1.235 fm.
- At p_lab = 76 MeV/c, the calculations underestimate the experimental annihilation cross section by about 15-20% for Ag, Sn, and Pb targets.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of antinucleon-nucleus interactions, particularly addressing discrepancies found in earlier Glauber model calculations by Lee and Wong (2016) where antineutron cross sections were too flat and low below 200 MeV/c. It contrasts with the density-folded optical model of Friedman (2015), which predicted antineutron cross sections that decrease and shift to lower momenta as nuclear size increases, a feature not observed in the present analysis. The results enable future comparisons with antiproton data to test whether the nuclear parts of the antineutron and antiproton optical potentials are identical, as assumed here, and provide a baseline for upcoming experiments at facilities such as FAIR.
