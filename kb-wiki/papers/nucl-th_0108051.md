# Flow effects on the freeze-out phase-space density in heavy ion collisions

> The strong longitudinal expansion of the reaction zone formed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is found to significantly reduce the spatially averaged pion phase-space density, compared to naive estimates based on thermal distributions. This has important implications for data interpretation and leads to larger values for the extracted pion chemical potential at kinetic freeze-out.

# Flow effects on the freeze-out phase-space density in heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The spatially averaged pion phase-space density at freeze-out is significantly reduced by longitudinal expansion of the fireball, compared to naive estimates based on thermal distributions in the local rest frame. This means that previous comparisons of measured phase-space densities with Bose-Einstein distributions have likely underestimated the pion chemical potential at kinetic freeze-out.

## Method

The authors model a thermalized, longitudinally boost-invariant fireball with sharp freeze-out at proper time τ_f, using a source emission function with transverse flow rapidity ζ(r) and an r-dependent chemical potential μ(r). They evaluate the spatially averaged phase-space density ⟨f⟩(p_T) at midrapidity (y=0) by expanding the Bose-Einstein distribution as a sum of exponentials, integrating analytically over space-time rapidity and azimuthal angle to obtain Bessel functions, and performing the radial integration numerically. Two transverse density profiles are considered: a box-like profile and a Gaussian profile.

## Key numbers

- At low p_T, the spatially averaged phase-space density of a longitudinally expanding source is about 35% lower than its value in the local rest frame (for T=120 MeV, Boltzmann approximation, no transverse flow).
- For a longitudinally expanding source with T=120 MeV, an average pion fugacity of approximately 1.55 (corresponding to μ_B=50 MeV) is needed to produce an ⟨f⟩(p_T) that looks like a Boltzmann distribution with vanishing chemical potential.
- The kinetic freeze-out temperature used in the model calculations is 120 MeV.
- The chemical freeze-out temperature is around 170 MeV, while kinetic freeze-out occurs at temperatures around 100-120 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses of heavy-ion data at the AGS by the E877 Collaboration (1997) and at the SPS by Ferenc et al. (1999) and Tomasik, Wiedemann, and Heinz (1999), which found no evidence for unusually large pion freeze-out phase-space densities. Those studies compared the measured ⟨f⟩ with Bose-Einstein distributions using kinetic freeze-out temperatures of 100-120 MeV and small or vanishing pion chemical potentials, sometimes including transverse flow effects as in Cramer (2001). The present paper shows that these comparisons neglected the dominant reduction effect from longitudinal expansion, and that properly accounting for this effect will likely increase the extracted pion chemical potential. The results enable a more accurate extraction of the pion chemical potential at kinetic freeze-out by comparing data with the model calculations in Eqs. (E.results) rather than with simple thermal distributions.
