# Shell effects and the neutron emission within the multi-dimensional Langevin model for nuclear fission

> We solve the Langevin equations for the time evolution of parameters that describe the shape of fissioning system. On each integration step, we calculate the probability of neutron emission and estimate whether a neutron would be emitted or not. If yes, we decrease the excitation energy of the nucleus by the neutron separation energy plus the average energy of the emitted neutron, switch to the layer of potential energy surface with a smaller number of neutrons and continue the process of integration. If the trajectory reaches the scission point, we check how many neutrons were emitted along this trajectory. The pre-scission neutron multiplicity $M_{pre}$ is defined by the ratio of the total number of emitted neutrons to the total number of fission trajectories. Besides $M_{pre}$, the mass distribution of fission fragments, the distribution of emitted neutrons with respect to the fission stage (deformation of system) and the distribution of emitted neutrons with respect to their energies are calculated. The calculated quantities are compared with the available experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that incorporating neutron emission during the fission process (multi-chance fission) into a four-dimensional Langevin model significantly improves the agreement between calculated and experimental fission fragment mass distributions for \(^{236}\)U, particularly by enhancing the impact of shell effects at excitation energies between 10 and 40 MeV.

## Method

The authors solve four-dimensional Langevin equations for the time evolution of collective shape variables (based on the two-center shell model parametrization) for fissioning \(^{236}\)U nuclei. At each integration step, they calculate the instantaneous neutron emission rate using a Fermi-gas model and a surface integral that depends on the nuclear deformation; if a random number test indicates neutron emission, they reduce the local excitation energy by the neutron separation energy plus a Maxwellian-distributed kinetic energy and switch to the potential energy surface with one fewer neutron. The calculation is repeated for many trajectories until they reach the scission point (neck radius below 2 fm), and the pre-scission neutron multiplicity is defined as the ratio of total emitted neutrons to total fission trajectories.

## Key numbers

* Pre-scission neutron multiplicity \(M_{pre}\) for \(^{236}\)U is calculated as a function of excitation energy from \(E^* = 10\) MeV to \(E^* = 40\) MeV; the multiplicity becomes stable against variation of the number of fission trajectories when \(N_{fiss}\) is sufficiently large (shown for \(E^* = 20\) MeV).
* The critical neck radius defining the scission point is set to \(r_{neck}^{crit} = 2\) fm.
* The time step used in the Langevin integration is \(\Delta t = 0.05\) fm/c, where \(c\) is the Fermi velocity.
* The effective temperature parameter \(\hbar\varpi\) in the modified Einstein relation is set to 2 MeV, independent of deformation.
* The surface integral for a spherical shape is \(S_{int} = \pi R_0^2\); for prolate shapes the surface integral becomes smaller than this value.

## Context

This work extends previous Langevin-based fission models (e.g., the five-dimensional calculations of Sierk and the three-dimensional model of Aritomo (2025) and the four-dimensional model of Pomorski et al. (2024)) by explicitly coupling neutron emission to the shape evolution at each integration step, rather than treating first-chance fission only. The authors compare their calculated mass distributions to experimental data from Zeinalov, Shibata, and Nishio (2020), and their pre-scission neutron multiplicities to measurements from Kavita (2024) and Strecker (1990) for the \(\alpha+^{232}\)Th reaction. The approach enables detailed study of the deformation stage at which neutrons are emitted and provides a framework for future extension to five-dimensional calculations.
