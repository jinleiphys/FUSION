# Strongly Intensive Measures for Particle Number Fluctuations: Effects of Hadronic Resonances

> Strongly intensive measures $Δ$ and $Σ$ are used to study event-by-event fluctuations of hadron multiplicities in nucleus-nucleus collisions. The effects of resonance decays are investigated within statistical model and relativistic transport model. Two specific examples are considered: resonance decays to two positively charged particles (e.g., $Δ^{++}\rightarrow p+ π^+$) and to $π^+π^-$-pairs. (e.g., $ρ^0\rightarrow π^-+π^+$). It is shown that resonance abundances at the chemical freeze-out can be estimated by measuring the fluctuations of the number of stable hadrons. These model results are compared to the full hadron-resonance gas analysis within both the grand canonical and canonical ensemble. The ultra-relativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model of nucleus-nucleus collisions is used to illustrate the role of global charge conservation, centrality selection, and limited experimental acceptance.

## Key claim

Resonance abundances at chemical freeze-out can be estimated by measuring event-by-event fluctuations of stable hadron multiplicities using the strongly intensive measures Δ and Σ, provided that the system volume fluctuations are canceled out and the approximations ω[n_+] ≅ ω[N_-] and ω[R] ≅ 1 hold within the grand canonical ensemble.

## Method

The authors calculated the strongly intensive fluctuation measures Δ[N₁,N₂] and Σ[N₁,N₂] for two specific resonance decay scenarios: decays into two positively charged hadrons (e.g., Δ⁺⁺ → p + π⁺) and decays into π⁺π⁻ pairs (e.g., ρ⁰ → π⁺ + π⁻). Analytical expressions were derived within the grand canonical ensemble for an ideal Boltzmann gas of particles and resonances, and these were compared to full hadron-resonance gas model calculations using the THERMUS package. The ultra-relativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) transport model was used to illustrate the effects of global charge conservation, centrality selection, and limited experimental acceptance.

## Key numbers

- At √s_NN = 6.27 GeV (T = 130.8 MeV, μ_B = 482.4 MeV), the GCE HGM gives Δ[N₋,N₊] = 0.807, ω[N₊] = 1.237, ω[N₋] = 1.071, and ⟨N₊⟩/⟨N₋⟩ = 1.624.
- At √s_NN = 17.3 GeV (T = 158.6 MeV, μ_B = 228.6 MeV), the GCE HGM gives Δ[N₋,N₊] = 0.730, ω[N₊] = 1.195, ω[N₋] = 1.114, and ⟨N₊⟩/⟨N₋⟩ = 1.213.
- At √s_NN = 200 GeV (T = 165.9 MeV, μ_B = 23.5 MeV), the GCE HGM gives Σ[π⁺,π⁻] = 0.624, ω[π⁺] = 1.116, ω[π⁻] = 1.117, and ρ[π⁺,π⁻] = 0.246.
- At √s_NN = 5500 GeV (T = 166.0 MeV, μ_B = 0.87 MeV), the GCE HGM gives Σ[π⁺,π⁻] = 0.624, ω[π⁺] = 1.117, ω[π⁻] = 1.117, and ρ[π⁺,π⁻] = 0.246.
- The fraction ⟨R_ππ⟩/(⟨π⁻⟩ + ⟨π⁺⟩) from the full HGM ranges from 0.11 at √s_NN = 6.27 GeV to 0.17 at √s_NN = 200 GeV and 5500 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the use of strongly intensive measures Δ and Σ, introduced by Gorenstein and Gazdzicki (2011), to the problem of estimating resonance abundances from final-state hadron fluctuations, an idea first suggested by Jeon and Koch. The analytical results are tested against the full hadron-resonance gas model (HGM) using the THERMUS package and against the UrQMD transport model for Pb+Pb and p+p collisions at SPS energies. The approach enables experimental extraction of resonance yields (such as Δ⁺⁺ and ρ⁰) that are difficult to measure directly, and provides a tool for studying the chemical freeze-out conditions in heavy-ion collisions at SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies.
