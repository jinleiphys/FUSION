# A Bayesian mixture model approach to quantifying the empirical nuclear saturation point

> The equation of state (EOS) in the limit of infinite symmetric nuclear matter exhibits an equilibrium density, $n_0 \approx 0.16 \, \mathrm{fm}^{-3}$, at which the pressure vanishes and the energy per particle attains its minimum, $E_0 \approx -16 \, \mathrm{MeV}$. Although not directly measurable, the saturation point $(n_0,E_0)$ can be extrapolated by density functional theory (DFT), providing tight constraints for microscopic interactions derived from chiral effective field theory (EFT). However, when considering several DFT predictions for $(n_0,E_0)$ from Skyrme and Relativistic Mean Field models together, a discrepancy between these model classes emerges at high confidence levels that each model prediction's uncertainty cannot explain. How can we leverage these DFT constraints to rigorously benchmark saturation properties of chiral interactions? To address this question, we present a Bayesian mixture model that combines multiple DFT predictions for $(n_0,E_0)$ using an efficient conjugate prior approach. The inferred posterior for the saturation point's mean and covariance matrix follows a Normal-inverse-Wishart class, resulting in posterior predictives in the form of correlated, bivariate $t$-distributions. The DFT uncertainty reports are then used to mix these posteriors using an ordinary Monte Carlo approach. At the 95\% credibility level, we estimate $n_0 \approx 0.157 \pm 0.010 \, \mathrm{fm}^{-3}$ and $E_0 \approx -15.97 \pm 0.40 \, \mathrm{MeV}$ for the marginal (univariate) $t$-distributions. Combined with chiral EFT calculations of the pure neutron matter EOS, we obtain bivariate normal distributions for the symmetry energy and its slope parameter at $n_0$: $S_v \approx 32.0 \pm 1.1 \, \mathrm{MeV}$ and $L\approx 52.6\pm 8.1 \, \mathrm{MeV}$ (95\%), respectively. Our Bayesian framework is publicly available, so practitioners can readily use and extend our results.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Bayesian mixture model that combines multiple density functional theory (DFT) predictions for the nuclear saturation point (n₀, E₀) while accounting for discrepancies between Skyrme and Relativistic Mean Field model classes that individual model uncertainties cannot explain at high confidence levels. The authors claim their framework can rigorously benchmark saturation properties of chiral effective field theory interactions by leveraging these combined DFT constraints.

## Method

The authors employed a Bayesian hierarchical model using a Normal-inverse-Wishart conjugate prior to combine multiple DFT predictions for the saturation point (n₀, E₀) from Skyrme and Relativistic Mean Field models. They used an ordinary Monte Carlo approach with 10⁶ samples from developer-reported uncertainties and 10² samples from the resulting t-distributions per uncertainty sample, yielding 10⁸ total independent samples. The posterior predictive distribution for the saturation point follows a correlated bivariate t-distribution, which was then combined with chiral EFT calculations of pure neutron matter to constrain the symmetry energy and its slope parameter.

## Key numbers

- n₀ ≈ 0.157 ± 0.010 fm⁻³ at 95% credibility level (marginal univariate t-distribution)
- E₀ ≈ -15.97 ± 0.40 MeV at 95% credibility level (marginal univariate t-distribution)
- S_v ≈ 32.0 ± 1.1 MeV at 95% credibility level (symmetry energy at n₀)
- L ≈ 52.6 ± 8.1 MeV at 95% credibility level (slope parameter of symmetry energy at n₀)
- The posterior t-distribution had 9 degrees of freedom, while the pre-mixing degrees of freedom were ν_n = 17

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to constrain nuclear saturation properties from DFT predictions, specifically addressing the discrepancy between Skyrme and Relativistic Mean Field model classes that was noted in prior studies. The Bayesian mixture model approach enables rigorous combination of multiple model predictions with their reported uncertainties, providing a statistically principled way to benchmark chiral effective field theory interactions against empirical saturation constraints. The publicly available framework allows practitioners to extend these results to additional nuclear matter observables and to incorporate new DFT predictions as they become available.
