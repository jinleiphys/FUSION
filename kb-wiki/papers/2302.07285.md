# Second-Order Perturbation Theory in Continuum Quantum Monte Carlo Calculations

> We report on the first results for the second-order perturbation theory correction to the ground-state energy of a nuclear many-body system in a continuum quantum Monte Carlo calculation. Second-order (and higher) perturbative corrections are notoriously difficult to compute in most ab initio many-body methods, where the focus is usually on obtaining the ground-state energy. By mapping our calculation of the second-order energy correction to an evolution in imaginary time using the diffusion Monte Carlo method, we are able to calculate these nuclear corrections for the first time. After benchmarking our method in the few-body sector, we explore the effect of charge-independence-breaking terms in the nuclear Hamiltonian. We then employ that approach to investigate the many-body, perturbative, order-by-order convergence that is fundamental in modern theories of the nucleon-nucleon interaction derived from chiral effective field theory. We find cutoff-dependent perturbativeness between potentials at higher chiral order and also that the difference between leading order and next-to-leading order potentials is nonperturbative; both of these results have important implications for future nuclear many-body calculations. Our approach is quite general and promises to be of wide applicability.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first calculation of the second-order perturbation theory correction to the ground-state energy of a nuclear many-body system using a continuum quantum Monte Carlo method, achieved by mapping the correction to an imaginary-time evolution. The authors find that the perturbativeness between chiral effective field theory potentials at higher chiral order is cutoff-dependent, and that the difference between leading-order and next-to-leading-order potentials is nonperturbative.

## Method

The authors compute the second-order energy correction by evaluating an integral over imaginary time of the ground-state expectation value of the perturbation operator evolved with the unperturbed Hamiltonian, using the diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) method. They perform two DMC runs, one starting from walkers distributed according to the trial wave function and a second starting from walkers distributed according to the projected ground-state wave function, then compute an extrapolated estimate between them. The method is benchmarked on few-body systems in a harmonic oscillator trap and then applied to pure neutron matter with 66 particles using chiral EFT interactions at NLO and N$^2$LO.

## Key numbers

- For two noninteracting particles in a harmonic trap with $\hbar\omega = 1$ MeV perturbed by a Gaussian interaction, the second-order correction gives a ground-state energy of 3.1769(3) $\hbar\omega$, matching the nonperturbative result of 3.1768(2) $\hbar\omega$.
- For a perturbation from neutron-neutron to neutron-proton interaction in a 6-particle system with $\hbar\omega = 1$ MeV, the second-order corrected energy is 10.0885(6) $\hbar\omega$, compared to the nonperturbative result of 10.0876(4) $\hbar\omega$.
- For pure neutron matter with $N=66$ particles, when the core of the potential is soft ($R_0 \geq 1.1$ fm), the second-order perturbation theory results for treating the difference between N$^2$LO and NLO as a perturbation match the nonperturbative N$^2$LO results to within 1% error.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering use of second-order perturbation theory in nuclear-physics quantum Monte Carlo by Lu et al. (2022), which was limited to a single cutoff, by enabling the study of how perturbative different nuclear interactions are at the many-body level across multiple cutoffs. The method is benchmarked against exact diagonalization approaches such as those of Roth and Navratil (2007) and builds on the DMC framework of Wiringa and Pieper (2002) and the local chiral EFT interactions of Gezerlis et al. (2014). The approach enables future investigations of order-by-order convergence in chiral EFT and could be applied to other many-body techniques such as auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo or Green's Function Monte Carlo.
