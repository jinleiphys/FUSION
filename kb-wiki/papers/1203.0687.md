# Magnetic States at Short Distances

> The magnetic interactions between a fermion and an antifermion of opposite electric or color charges in the 1S0 and 3P0 states are very attractive and singular near the origin and may allow the formation of new bound and resonance states at short distances. In the two body Dirac equations formulated in constraint dynamics, the short-distance attraction for these states for point particles leads to a quasipotential that behaves near the origin as -α^2/r^2. Both 1S0 and 3P0 states admit two types of eigenstates with drastically different behaviors for the radial wave function u=rψ. One type of states, with u growing as r^{1+\sqrt(1-4*α^2)/2} at small r, will be called usual states. The other type of states with u growing as r^{(1-\sqrt(1-4*α^2))/2} will be called peculiar states. Both of the usual and peculiar eigenstates have admissible behaviors at short distances. The usual bound 1S0 states possess attributes the same as those one usually encounters in QED and QCD. In contrast, the peculiar bound 1S0 states, yet to be observed, have distinctly different bound state properties and scattering phase shifts. For the 3P0 states, the usual solutions lead to the standard bound state energies and no resonance, but resonances have been found for the peculiar states whose energies depend on the description of the internal structure of the charges, the mass of the constituent, and the coupling constant. The existence of both usual and peculiar eigenstates in the same system leads to the non-self-adjoint property of the mass operator and two non-orthogonal complete sets. The mass operator can be made self-adjoint with a single complete set of admissible states by introducing a new peculiarity quantum number and an enlarged Hilbert space.

# Magnetic States at Short Distances

## Key claim

The magnetic interactions in the 1S0 and 3P0 states of fermion-antifermion systems produce a quasipotential that behaves as -α²/r² near the origin, leading to two distinct classes of quantum-mechanically admissible eigenstates (usual and peculiar), where the peculiar states have not yet been observed and their physical existence remains to be further investigated.

## Method

The authors used the two-body Dirac equations (TBDE) formulated in Dirac's constraint dynamics to obtain a Pauli-reduced Schrödinger-like equation with an effective quasipotential containing all spin, orbital angular momentum, and tensor dependencies. They solved this equation analytically for the 1S0 states and numerically for the 3P0 states, using the variable phase shift formalism of Calogero to compute phase shifts and identify resonances, with the Coulomb potential A(r) = -α/r as the invariant interaction.

## Key numbers

- The short-distance quasipotential behaves as -α²/r², where α is the coupling constant
- For the peculiar 1S0 ground state of a fermion-antifermion pair with fermion rest mass m: root-mean-square radius is approximately 1/m, binding energy is approximately (2-√2)m, and rest mass is approximately √2m
- The (n+1)th 1S0 peculiar state is nearly degenerate in energy and approximately equal in size with the nth 1S0 usual state
- The usual bound 1S0 states agree with standard perturbative results through order α⁴

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Wong (1986) of the (e⁺e⁻) system using the Kemmer-Fermi-Yang equation, which investigated possible 3P0 magnetic resonances around 1.579 MeV in connection with anomalous positron peaks in heavy-ion collisions (Schäfer 1983). The authors previously found no such resonance in the TBDE framework (Crater and Wong, earlier work), but here they identify an overlooked peculiar solution. The results contradict predictions by Spence and collaborators (1991) based on three-dimensional truncations of the Bethe-Salpeter equation. The existence of both usual and peculiar eigenstates leads to a non-self-adjoint mass operator, which the authors resolve by introducing a peculiarity quantum number and an enlarged Hilbert space. Whether these peculiar states correspond to physical states remains an open question requiring further investigation.
