# Electro-disintegration following beta-decay

> I show that the disintegration of weakly-bound nuclei and the ionization of weakly-bound atomic electrons due to their interaction with leptons from beta decay is a negligible effect.

## Key claim

The disintegration of weakly-bound nuclei and the ionization of weakly-bound atomic electrons due to their interaction with leptons from beta decay is a negligible effect, based on a sequential two-step model that assumes the outgoing electron wave is spherically symmetric and favors monopole transitions.

## Method

The author calculates the probability of nuclear dissociation following beta-decay using first-order time-dependent perturbation theory, modeling the daughter nucleus with a Hulthén s-wave wavefunction for the initial bound state and an outgoing spherical wave for the final continuum state. The beta-particle is treated as a spherically symmetric outgoing wave with its charge smeared over its Compton wavelength, and the dissociation probability is integrated over the density of final states of the nucleon-nucleus system. The same formalism is adapted to atomic ionization by replacing the nucleon mass with the electron mass and using a loosely bound electron with a separation energy of 1 eV.

## Key numbers

- For a nucleus with neutron separation energy \(S_n = 10\) keV and an electron energy \(E_e = 10\) MeV, the dissociation probability is \(9.3 \times 10^{-7}\).
- For a nucleus with \(S_n = 70\) keV and \(E_e = 10\) MeV, the dissociation probability is \(4.8 \times 10^{-7}\).
- For a nucleus with \(S_n = 160\) keV and \(E_e = 10\) MeV, the dissociation probability is \(1.9 \times 10^{-7}\).
- For a nucleus with \(S_n = 310\) keV and \(E_e = 10\) MeV, the dissociation probability is \(7.3 \times 10^{-8}\).
- For atomic ionization with electron separation energy \(S_e = 1\) eV and beta-particle energy \(E_e = 10\) eV, the ionization probability is \(4.7 \times 10^{-8}\).
- For atomic ionization with \(S_e = 1\) eV and \(E_e = 50\) keV, the ionization probability is \(6.3 \times 10^{-10}\).
- For atomic ionization with \(S_e = 1\) eV and \(E_e = 1\) MeV, the ionization probability is \(1.09 \times 10^{-10}\).
- For atomic ionization with \(S_e = 1\) eV and \(E_e = 5\) MeV, the ionization probability is \(1.97 \times 10^{-11}\).

## Context

This work extends the discussion of limits on the existence of nuclei far from the line of stability, where beta-decay already sets stringent constraints (see, e.g., Ri02). The calculation rules out nuclear dissociation following beta-decay as a relevant effect near the drip line, and also shows that atomic ionization by the beta-particle is negligible. The author notes that a full quantum mechanical calculation using Feynman diagram techniques with proper account of relativistic effects and energy conservation is very unlikely to change these conclusions, and that the same line of thought applies to higher multipole interactions.
