# The Initial Flow of Classical Gluon Fields in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Using analytic solutions of the Yang-Mills equations we calculate the initial flow of energy of the classical gluon field created in collisions of large nuclei at high energies. We find radial and elliptic flow which follows gradients in the initial energy density, similar to a simple hydrodynamic behavior. In addition we find a rapidity-odd transverse flow field which implies the presence of angular momentum and should lead to directed flow in final particle spectra. We trace those energy flow terms to transverse fields from the non-abelian generalization of Gauss' Law and Ampere's and Faraday's Laws.

# The Initial Flow of Classical Gluon Fields in Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim
Using analytic solutions of the Yang-Mills equations expanded in powers of proper time œÑ, the authors calculate that the classical gluon field created in high-energy nuclear collisions develops transverse energy flow (radial, elliptic, and rapidity-odd directed flow) linearly in time, valid only up to roughly œÑ ~ 1/Q_s before the field decoheres and thermalizes.

## Method
The authors solve the classical Yang-Mills equations for SU(3) gauge fields in the forward light cone using a power series expansion in proper time œÑ, with boundary conditions from the McLerran-Venugopalan model. They compute the transverse Poynting vector S^i = T^{0i} (the energy flow) at order O(œÑ^1) from the transverse electric and magnetic fields, then average over color charge distributions to obtain event-averaged flow fields for symmetric and asymmetric nuclear collisions.

## Context
This work extends the analytic recursive solution of the Yang-Mills equations first described by Fries (2006) and builds on the color glass condensate framework of McLerran and Venugopalan (1993, 1994). The rapidity-odd flow term Œ≤^i is qualitatively consistent with experimental directed flow v_1 results, but the authors note that a quantitative comparison requires follow-up 3+1-D viscous hydrodynamic simulations. The results enable future work coupling pre-equilibrium flow into hydrodynamic calculations, with preliminary results showing that key features like angular momentum translate into hydrodynamic fields in a rapid thermalization scenario.
