# Decay of Nuclear Giant Resonances: Quantum Self-similar Fragmentation

> Scaling analysis of nuclear giant resonance transition probabilities with increasing level of complexity in the background states is performed. It is found that the background characteristics, typical for chaotic systems lead to nontrivial multifractal scaling properties.

# Decay of Nuclear Giant Resonances: Quantum Self-similar Fragmentation

## Key claim

The transition probabilities of nuclear giant resonances, when coupled to a chaotic background of more complex configurations, exhibit nontrivial multifractal scaling properties; this scaling is considerably poorer for regular (Poisson-distributed) backgrounds, suggesting that the self-similar fragmentation pattern is a signature of chaotic dynamics rather than a universal feature of all background types.

## Method

The authors performed a multifractal scaling analysis of the isovector quadrupole strength distribution for the giant quadrupole resonance of ⁴⁰Ca, using a truncated model space of 26 1p–1h states coupled to 3014 2p–2h states. They constructed a normalized measure from the set of energy eigenvalues weighted by their transition probabilities, then computed the generalized fractal dimensions D_q for two cases: one with no residual interaction in the 2p–2h subspace (producing Poisson level fluctuations, associated with regular dynamics) and one with full residual interaction (producing Wigner fluctuations, associated with chaotic dynamics).

## Key numbers

- The model space consisted of 26 1p–1h states and 3014 2p–2h states for the giant quadrupole resonance of ⁴⁰Ca.
- The input data comprised approximately 2¹¹ data points, sufficient to display exponential scaling.
- For the chaotic case, a fairly good scaling was obtained over about 8 points in the log–log plot for the generalized dimensions D_q.
- For the capacity dimension D₀, the value was approximately 1 in both cases, as this dimension is determined solely by the energy distribution.
- For the regular case, scaling was very poor for q > 2, and linear fits were not plotted for those values.

## Context

This work extends the application of random-matrix theory (RMT) and quantum chaology, as formulated by Bohigas (1984) and others, from stationary spectral fluctuations to the time-dependent fragmentation of giant resonances. The authors connect the Nakajima–Zwanzig rate equations for the giant resonance decay to a binary, self-similar, conservative random fragmentation process studied by Botet and Ploszajczak (1990), and they build on earlier calculations by Drozdz et al. (1995) for the ⁴⁰Ca giant quadrupole resonance. The results suggest that multifractal analysis of strength distributions can distinguish chaotic from regular background dynamics, and they open the possibility of using the fragmentation process as a unifying framework for both microscopic and macroscopic statistical phenomena in quantum systems.
