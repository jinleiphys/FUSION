# Magnetic collapse of a neutron gas: Can magnetars indeed be formed

> A relativistic degenerate neutron gas in equilibrium with a background of electrons and protons in a magnetic field exerts its pressure anisotropically, having a smaller value perpendicular than along the magnetic field. For critical fields the magnetic pressure may produce the vanishing of the equatorial pressure of the neutron gas. Taking it as a model for neutron stars, the outcome could be a transverse collapse of the star. This fixes a limit to the fields to be observable in stable neutron star pulsars as a function of their density. The final structure left over after the implosion might be a mixed phase of nucleons and meson condensate, a strange star, or a highly distorted black hole or black "cigar", but no any magnetar, if viewed as a super strongly magnetized neutron star. However, we do not exclude the possibility of a supersotrong magnetic fields arising in supernova explosions which lead directly to strange stars. In other words, if any magnetars exist, they cannot be neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a neutron star permeated by a super-strong magnetic field (surface fields above approximately 10^16 G) is unstable and must undergo a transverse collapse, which would preclude the formation of magnetars if they are defined as super-strongly magnetized neutron stars; however, the authors do not exclude the possibility that super-strong magnetic fields arising in supernova explosions could lead directly to strange stars instead.

## Method

The authors model a relativistic degenerate neutron gas in beta equilibrium with a background of electrons and protons, placed in an external magnetic field, using the one-loop approximation for the thermodynamic potential derived from the Dirac equation for neutral particles with anomalous magnetic moment. They compute the anisotropic pressure components (parallel and perpendicular to the magnetic field) from the energy-momentum tensor, which takes the form of a sum of an isotropic pressure term, the Maxwell stress tensor of the microscopic field B, and the Minkowski tensor for the relativistic nonlinear medium. The condition for transverse collapse is identified as the vanishing of the perpendicular pressure P_perp = -Omega - B*M, where Omega is the thermodynamic potential and M is the magnetization.

## Key numbers

* Surface magnetic fields B_surf >= 10^16 G are required for the transverse pressure to vanish and trigger collapse in a neutron gas.
* Core magnetic fields may reach values up to approximately 10^20 G, where the magnetic pressure from a rho meson condensate overwhelms the kinetic pressure (of order 10^36 dynes/cm^2), leading to P_perp < 0 and definite instability.
* The critical field for the rho meson condensate contribution is B_c_rho = m_rho^2/e approximately 10^20 G.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis of magnetic collapse in charged particle gases (Chaichian et al.) to neutral particles with anomalous magnetic moments, and is a more elaborated version of their earlier conference report (Hugo). The results contradict the classical electrodynamic expectation (Landau) that collapse would occur along the magnetic field, instead finding transverse collapse; this also disagrees with Khalilov's conclusion about the collapse direction, though both theories agree that collapse occurs. The paper builds on the magnetar model of Duncan and Thompson (1992) and addresses the open problem of anisotropic pressures in strongly magnetized compact stars, which was left unresolved by Herrera and Santos and by Mak and Harko. The outcome enables the possibility that the remnant of such a collapse could be a mixed phase of nucleons and meson condensate, a strange star, or a highly distorted black hole, but not a magnetar as a neutron star.
