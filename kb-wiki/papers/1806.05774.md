# Kubo formulas for thermodynamic transport coefficients

> Uncharged relativistic fluids in 3+1 dimensions have three independent thermodynamic transport coefficients at second order in the derivative expansion. Fluids with a single global $U(1)$ current have nine, out of which seven are parity preserving. We derive the Kubo formulas for all nine thermodynamic transport coefficients in terms of equilibrium correlation functions of the energy-momentum tensor and the current. All parity-preserving coefficients can be expressed in terms of two-point functions in flat space without external sources, while the parity-violating coefficients require three-point functions. We use the Kubo formulas to compute the thermodynamic coefficients in several examples of free field theories.

# Kubo formulas for thermodynamic transport coefficients

## Key claim

The paper derives Kubo formulas for all nine second-order thermodynamic transport coefficients in relativistic fluids with a global U(1) charge, showing that the seven parity-preserving coefficients can be expressed using only two-point correlation functions in flat space without external sources, contrary to previous approaches that required three-point functions for some of these coefficients.

## Method

The authors construct an equilibrium generating functional for thermodynamic correlation functions in terms of external metric and gauge field sources, expanding it to second order in derivatives with nine independent invariants and their associated susceptibility coefficients f_n(T,Ој). They then compute the energy-momentum tensor and U(1) current by functional differentiation of the generating functional, and extract Kubo formulas by expanding these equilibrium one-point functions to linear order in small metric and gauge field perturbations around flat space.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of thermodynamic transport coefficients from uncharged fluids, first noticed by Baier et al. (2007) and Bhattacharyya et al. (2008) at second order in the derivative expansion, to charged fluids with a global U(1) current. The connection between these coefficients and thermodynamics was established by Banerjee et al. (2012) and Jensen et al. (2012), who showed they follow from the equilibrium partition function. The paper generalizes the results of Jensen et al. (2012) for second-order transport to charged fluids, and provides Kubo formulas that enable evaluation of these coefficients on the lattice and in holography, using two-point functions rather than the three-point functions employed by Moore et al. (2012) and Buzzegoli et al. (2017).
