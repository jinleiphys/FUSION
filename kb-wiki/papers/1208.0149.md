# Second random-phase approximation with the Gogny force. First applications

> We present the first applications of the second random-phase-approximation model with the finite-range Gogny interaction. We discuss the advantages of using such an interaction in this type of calculations where 2 particle-2 hole configurations are included. The results found in the present work confirm the well known general features of the second random-phase approximation spectra: we find a large shift, several MeV, of the response centroids to lower energies with respect to the corresponding random-phase-approximation values. As known, these results indicate that the effects of the 1 particle-1 hole/2 particle-2 hole and 2 particle-2 hole/2 particle-2 hole couplings are important. It has been found that the changes of the strength distributions with respect to the standard random-phase-approximation results are particularly large in the present case. This important effect is due to some large neutron-proton matrix elements of the interaction and indicates that these matrix elements (which do not contribute in the mean-field calculations employed in the conventional fit procedures of the force parameters) should be carefully constrained to perform calculations

## Key claim

The paper presents the first application of the second random-phase approximation (SRPA) with the finite-range Gogny interaction, but the results are strongly affected by large neutron-proton matrix elements of the residual interaction that are not constrained by standard mean-field fitting procedures. The authors find that these matrix elements, which couple 1 particle-1 hole and 2 particle-2 hole configurations, produce an excessively large downward shift of the response centroids (more than 10 MeV) that is not stable with respect to the energy cutoff in the 2 particle-2 hole space.

## Method

The authors performed SRPA calculations for the nucleus 16O using the D1S parametrization of the Gogny interaction, including 2 particle-2 hole configurations in the excitation space. They compared full SRPA calculations (including all neutron-proton matrix elements) with a truncated version (SRPA*) that omitted the large neutron-proton matrix elements, and studied the numerical stability of the isoscalar monopole response as a function of the energy cutoff in the 2 particle-2 hole space. The calculations were performed in spherical symmetry using a harmonic oscillator basis, with single-particle states up to 60 MeV and 1 particle-1 hole configurations up to 100 MeV excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- The centroid energy of the isoscalar monopole response in 16O shifts from 20.37 MeV (cutoff 60 MeV) to 15.30 MeV (cutoff 80 MeV) in full SRPA, a deviation of 25%
- In the truncated SRPA* scheme, the centroid shifts from 23.97 MeV (cutoff 60 MeV) to 22.37 MeV (cutoff 80 MeV), a deviation of 7%
- For SRPA* with cutoff 100 MeV, the centroid is 21.32 MeV; with cutoff 120 MeV, the centroid is 20.49 MeV
- The experimental centroid for the giant monopole resonance in 16O is 21.13 MeV
- The RPA centroid energy is 23.88 MeV; the SRPA* centroid (with cutoff 120 MeV) is 20.49 MeV
- Some neutron-proton matrix elements in the 1 particle-1 hole/2 particle-2 hole coupling are 5 to 10 times larger than all other matrix elements
- The energy-weighted sum rule violation is 5% in both RPA and SRPA (with cutoff 60 MeV) due to neglecting Coulomb and spin-orbit terms
- For cutoff 80 MeV, 85% of the energy-weighted sum rule is found up to 40 MeV excitation energy

## Context

This work extends previous SRPA calculations using the Skyrme zero-range interaction (Gambacurta 2010, Gambacurta 2011a, Gambacurta 2011b) to the finite-range Gogny force, which is better suited for handling the particle-particle matrix elements that appear in beyond-RPA theories. The results show that the large neutron-proton matrix elements produce effects similar to but stronger than those found in the Skyrme case, and analogous effects have been found in variational multiparticle-multihole configuration interaction calculations for 30Si (Pillet 2012). The paper suggests that charge-exchange RPA calculations and complementary multiparticle-multihole models could help constrain these matrix elements, which are not tested by standard mean-field fitting procedures.
