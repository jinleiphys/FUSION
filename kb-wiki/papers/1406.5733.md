# A unified study of the production of all identified hadrons over wide ranges of transverse momenta at LHC

> The production of all identified hadrons at the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is studied with emphasis on the $p_T$ distributions up to 20 GeV/c in central collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV. The parton recombination model is used to determine the hadronic \ppt\ spectra from the quark \dis s. From the heavy hyperon spectra it is known from earlier studies that the $u, d, s$ thermal \dis s in \ppt\ are exponential with large inverse slopes that cannot be identified with any temperature in conventional fluid models. They are used as inputs in our model together with shower partons determined from our treatment of momentum degradation that uses high-\ppt\ pion data as input. Those thermal and shower partons are used to calculate the $p_T$ \dis s of all observed hadrons ($π, K, p, Λ$, $Ξ$, $Ω$ and $ϕ$) over wide ranges of \ppt, so the system is highly constrained. We show how well the LHC data can be reproduced with only a few parameters to adjust. Centrality dependence has not been studied. What is learned is that minijets are important, not only in giving rise to abundant shower partons, but also in the conversion of semihard partons in the medium to soft partons that enhance the thermal partons. Since the conversion process can occur throughout the expansion phase of the high-density medium, this work provides the basis for questioning the validity of the assumption of rapid equilibration.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the production of all identified hadrons (π, K, p, Λ, Ξ, Ω, φ) over a wide transverse momentum range (0.5 to 20 GeV/c) in central Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV can be described by a unified parton recombination model, but only if minijets are recognized as a crucial source of soft shower partons that enhance the thermal parton population, thereby challenging the assumption of rapid thermal equilibration in hydrodynamical models.

## Method

The authors use the parton recombination model, where hadron spectra are computed as convolutions of parton distributions with recombination functions for mesons and baryons. Thermal parton distributions are taken as exponential functions with inverse slopes Tq = 0.39 GeV/c and Ts = 0.51 GeV/c, determined phenomenologically from baryon spectra. Shower parton distributions are derived from hard and semihard partons that undergo momentum degradation in the medium, parametrized by a momentum-dependent degradation parameter γg(q) = γ0/(1 + (q/q0)²) with γ0 = 2.8 and q0 = 7 GeV/c, fitted to reproduce the high-pT pion data. The formalism includes recombination of thermal-thermal, thermal-shower, and shower-shower partons for mesons, and all combinations for baryons.

## Key numbers

- Inverse slopes for thermal partons: Tq = 0.39 GeV/c for light quarks (from proton data) and Ts = 0.51 GeV/c for strange quarks (from Ω data) at √sNN = 2.76 TeV
- Momentum degradation parameters: γ0 = 2.8 and q0 = 7 GeV/c for gluons, with γq = γg/2 for quarks
- The ratio ρchPb-Pb(√sNN=2.76 TeV) is more than twice the value at √sNN=200 GeV, and more than 30% higher than a logarithmic extrapolation from lower energies
- The Ω/p ratio at LHC rises exponentially with pT up to the highest measured pT around 7 GeV/c, increasing by a factor of 10
- The ratio of LHC to RHIC yields for π, p, and Ω increases by two orders of magnitude as pT increases up to 10 GeV/c
- The nuclear modification factor RAA for Pb-Pb at 0-5% centrality reaches a minimum of 0.13 at pT = 6-7 GeV/c, then rises to about 0.4 for pT > 30 GeV/c

## Context

This work extends the recombination model previously applied to Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV (Hwa and Yang, 2004; Hwa and Zhu, 2012) to LHC energies, superseding an earlier study by Hwa and Zhu (2013) that covered only pT < 5 GeV/c. The authors contrast their approach with conventional hydrodynamical models (Heinz and Kolb, 2002; Teaney, 2003) and perturbative QCD jet fragmentation approaches (Wang and Gyulassy, 1992; Baier et al., 2001), arguing that the large inverse slopes and exponential behavior of baryon spectra cannot be explained by thermal temperatures in fluid models. The work enables future studies of azimuthal dependence in non-central collisions and questions the validity of rapid equilibration assumptions in heavy-ion physics.
