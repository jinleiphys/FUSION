# Suspicion on Engrafting HBT From Astronomy to Heavy Ion Collision

> HBT method in astronomy and heavy ion collision is contrasted in present article.Some differences are found and validity of using HBT in heavy ion collision is suspected.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Hanbury Brown–Twiss (HBT) method, as used in astronomy, cannot be validly applied to heavy-ion collisions because the detector positions and the distance from source to detectors (variables \(d\) and \(L\) in the formalism) cannot be experimentally determined or integrated over in the collision context, contrary to the astronomical case.

## Method

The author contrasts the theoretical derivation of the HBT intensity correlation function for two point sources (astronomy) with the experimental procedure used in heavy-ion collisions, specifically referencing the STAR collaboration's analysis of two-pion correlations. The paper examines the variables entering the correlation function, source separation \(R\), detector separation \(d\), particle momenta \(p_1, p_2\), and source-to-detector distance \(L\), and argues that in heavy-ion experiments, particles are effectively "detected" at the source surface (where they are emitted), making \(L = 0\) and \(d \sim 0\), which invalidates the astronomical derivation that requires \(L \gg R, d\). The author then proposes an alternative interpretation of the experimentally measured correlation function as arising from a mechanism \(n_{c,i}(q)\) that modifies the number of particle pairs with small momentum difference relative to a completely random source.

## Key numbers

- The correlation function is defined as \(C_2({\bf q}) = A({\bf q})/B({\bf q})\), normalized to unity at large \({\bf q}\), where \(A({\bf q})\) is the measured two-pion distribution of pair momentum difference \({\bf q = p_2 - p_1}\) and \(B({\bf q})\) is the mixed background distribution from different events.
- The Lorentz-invariant variable used in correlation analyses is \(Q_{inv} = \sqrt{({\bf p}_1 - {\bf p}_2)^2 - (E_1 - E_2)^2}\).
- For a completely random source (e.g., computer-generated), the average same-event and mixed-event pair counts satisfy \(\bar{n}_{s,i}(q,q+dq) = \bar{n}_{m,i}(q,q+dq)\), yielding \(C_2(q) = 1\).

## Context

This work questions the foundational assumptions of applying the HBT intensity interferometry method, originally developed by Hanbury Brown and Twiss (1950s) for measuring stellar angular diameters, to heavy-ion collision source imaging, as proposed by authors such as Heinz (1999), Pratt, Rischke, Gyulassy, and Shuryak. The paper argues that the experimental procedure used by the STAR collaboration (2003) and others, which reconstructs particle tracks back to the source surface, does not satisfy the geometric conditions (\(L \gg R, d\)) required for the standard HBT derivation, and instead suggests that the observed correlation peak at small momentum difference may arise from production mechanisms (e.g., Pauli blocking or interactions) rather than source geometry. This critique challenges the standard interpretation of HBT radii extracted from heavy-ion data and calls for a re-examination of what the measured correlation function actually represents.
