# On the strong local potential limit

> Finite, bound, many-body systems, where the interaction operator, $V=\sum v_{ij}$, is local and happens to strongly dominate the kinetic energy operator, $T=\sum t_i$, display a classical limit from diagonalizing $V$ alone, hence a clear picture of interparticle correlations, such as steric blocking emerges. This limit exhibits intrinsic symmetries and also fluctuations from mass formulae. The present work investigates how such emergent symmetries and fluctuations might be extrapolated to physical situations where $T$ is reinstated.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in the strong local potential limit (where the two-body interaction \(V\) dominates the kinetic energy \(T\)), diagonalizing \(V\) alone yields a classical picture of shell structure and interparticle correlations; the authors investigate whether these emergent symmetries and mass fluctuations might survive when \(T\) is reinstated as a perturbation, but they do not prove this survival for realistic nuclear systems.

## Method

The authors treat a finite many-body system with Hamiltonian \(H = V + \lambda T\) and take the limit \(\lambda \to 0\), diagonalizing only the local two-body potential \(V\) (a difference of two Gaussians, denoted 2G or 2G3) by searching for classical optimal configurations that minimize the potential energy. They perform classical energy minimization for systems of \(A\) identical particles in two dimensions (2d) and three dimensions (3d), starting from random initial positions and allowing the system to converge to the minimal energy configuration, then analyze the resulting shell structures and symmetries.

## Context

This work extends the concept of steric blocking introduced by Giraud (2012) and builds on earlier studies of positive-definite operators as perturbations by Giraud (1978, 1982, 1984). It contrasts with the standard nuclear shell model approach, which assumes harmonic oscillator single-particle states and spin-orbit splitting to explain magic numbers, as in the work of Vogt (1965), Gottfried (1970), and others. The results suggest that shell structure can emerge from bare two-body interactions without a one-body centered potential, potentially providing a more microscopic basis for understanding magic numbers and correlations in nuclear physics and cluster physics, as studied by de Heer (1993) and Iñiguez (1986).
