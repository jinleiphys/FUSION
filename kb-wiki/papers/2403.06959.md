# Gluon Double-Spin Asymmetry in the Longitudinally Polarized $p+p$ Collisions

> We derive the first-ever small-$x$ expression for the inclusive gluon production cross section in the central rapidity region of the longitudinally polarized proton-proton collisions. The cross section depends on the polarizations of both protons, therefore comprising the numerator of the longitudinal double-spin asymmetry $A_{LL}$ for the produced gluons. The cross section is calculated in the shock wave formalism and is expressed in terms of the polarized dipole scattering amplitudes on the projectile and target protons. We show that the small-$x$ evolution corrections are included into our cross section expression if one evolves these polarized dipole amplitudes using the double-logarithmic helicity evolution derived in \cite{Kovchegov:2015pbl, Kovchegov:2016zex, Kovchegov:2018znm, Cougoulic:2022gbk}. Our calculation is performed for the gluon sector only, with the quark contribution left for future work. When that work is complete, the resulting formula will be applicable to longitudinally polarized proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions, as well as to polarized semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (SIDIS) on a proton or a nucleus. Our results should allow one to extend the small-$x$ helicity phenomenology analysis of \cite{Adamiak:2023yhz} to the jet/hadron production data reported for the longitudinally polarized proton-proton collisions at RHIC and to polarized SIDIS measurements at central rapidities to be performed at the EIC.

## Key claim

The paper derives the first small-\(x\) expression for the inclusive gluon production cross section in the central rapidity region of longitudinally polarized proton-proton collisions, but only for the gluon sector; the quark contribution is left for future work, and the resulting formula will only be fully applicable to polarized proton-proton, proton-nucleus collisions, and polarized SIDIS once that quark contribution is included.

## Method

The authors calculate the sub-eikonal (order-\(1/s\)) helicity-dependent correction to the inclusive gluon production cross section using the shock wave formalism and the light-cone operator treatment (LCOT) framework, treating one proton as a fast-moving projectile and the other as a target shock wave. The cross section is expressed in terms of polarized dipole scattering amplitudes on the projectile and target protons, and the small-\(x\) evolution corrections are included by evolving these polarized dipole amplitudes using the double-logarithmic helicity evolution (KPS-CTT) in the large-\(N_c\) approximation. The calculation is performed in the pure glue picture, with the incoming particle taken as a longitudinally polarized gluon and the produced particle also a gluon.

## Key numbers

* The sub-eikonal expansion of the gluon \(S\)-matrix is kept up to and including order-\(1/s\) terms in the expansion in inverse powers of the center-of-mass energy squared \(s\).
* The leading-order helicity evolution sums up powers of \(\alpha_s \, \ln^2 (1/x)\) in the double-logarithmic approximation (DLA).
* The intercepts driving the small-\(x\) asymptotics of helicity parton distribution functions from KPS-CTT evolution differ from those found by Bartels, Ermolaev and Ryskin at the \(< 1\%\) level at large \(N_c\) and \(< 3\%\) level at large \(N_c \& N_f\).
* The phenomenological analysis of Adamiak (2023) used polarized DIS and SIDIS data for \(x < 0.1\) and with photon virtuality \(Q^2 > 1.69\) GeV\(^2\).

## Context

This work extends the small-\(x\) helicity formalism developed by Kovchegov, Pitonyak, and Sievert (2015-2018) and modified by Cougoulic, Tarasov, and Tawabutr (2022) from deep inelastic scattering to inclusive particle production in polarized proton-proton collisions. The result enables extension of the helicity phenomenology analysis of Adamiak (2023) to the jet and hadron production data from longitudinally polarized proton-proton collisions at RHIC and to polarized SIDIS measurements at central rapidities to be performed at the EIC, potentially reducing the spread of predictions for helicity parton distribution functions at small \(x\).
