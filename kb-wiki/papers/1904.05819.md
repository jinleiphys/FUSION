# A New Approach to Determine Radiative Capture Reaction Rates at Astrophysical Energies

> Radiative capture reactions play a crucial role in stellar nucleosynthesis but have proved challenging to determine experimentally. In particular, the large uncertainty ($\sim$100%) in the measured rate of the $^{12}$C$(α,γ)^{16}$O reaction is the largest source of uncertainty in any stellar evolution model. With development of new high current energy-recovery linear accelerators (ERLs) and high density gas targets, measurement of the $^{16}$O$(e,e^\prime α)^{12}$C reaction close to threshold using detailed balance opens up a new approach to determine the $^{12}$C$(α,γ)^{16}$O reaction rate with significantly increased precision ($<$20%). We present the formalism to relate photo- and electro-disintegration reactions and consider the design of an optimal experiment to deliver increased precision. Once the new ERLs come online, an experiment to validate the new approach we propose should be carried out. This new approach has broad applicability to radiative capture reactions in astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that measuring the electrodisintegration reaction \(^{16}\text{O}(e,e^\prime\alpha)^{12}\text{C}\) close to threshold using high-current energy-recovery linear accelerators (ERLs) and high-density gas targets can determine the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) radiative capture rate with significantly improved precision (uncertainty below 20%), but only if an initial validation experiment at higher \(\alpha\)-particle energies is first carried out once the new ERLs become operational.

## Method

The authors develop the theoretical formalism relating the electrodisintegration cross section for \(^{16}\text{O}(e,e^\prime\alpha)^{12}\text{C}\) to the photodisintegration cross section for \(^{16}\text{O}(\gamma,\alpha)^{12}\text{C}\) via detailed balance, using the one-photon-exchange approximation in the plane-wave Born approximation. They perform a multipole decomposition of the response functions (limiting to C0, C1/E1, and C2/E2 multipoles) and parametrize the momentum-transfer dependence of the cross section using a small number of constants that can be determined experimentally. The design of an optimal experiment is considered, including incident electron energy, gas target properties, scattered electron spectrometer, and low-energy \(\alpha\)-particle detection, with estimates of event rates and systematic uncertainties.

## Key numbers

* The uncertainty in the measured rate of the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction is approximately 100%, making it the largest source of uncertainty in any stellar evolution model.
* The proposed new approach aims to determine the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction rate with significantly increased precision of less than 20%.
* At the helium burning temperature of about \(2 \times 10^8\) K and corresponding Gamow energy of about 300 keV, the cross section for the \(^{12}\text{C}+\alpha\rightarrow\gamma+^{16}\text{O}\) reaction is approximately \(10^{-5}\) pb.
* The new generation of ERLs can deliver luminosities of approximately \(10^{36}\) cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) for experiment.
* The initial validation measurement at higher \(E_{\alpha}^{c.m.}\) would require running time of order a month, while a longer experiment to determine the rate in the astrophysical region would require of order 6 months.
* The momentum transfer scale for nuclear momenta is taken to be roughly of order 200–250 MeV/c.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing experimental effort to determine the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction rate, which has been pursued through direct measurements (Dyer and Barnes, 1974; Redder, 1987; Kremer, 1988; Ouellet, 1996; Roters, 1999; Kunz, 2001; Gialanella, 2001; and others), \(\beta\)-delayed \(\alpha\)-decay of \(^{16}\text{N}\) (Buchmann, 1993; Azuma, 1994; Tang, 2010), and elastic scattering \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\alpha)^{12}\text{C}\) (Plaga, 1987; Tischhauser, 2009). The approach builds on earlier proposals for using electrodisintegration (Tsentalovich, 2000; Friscic, 2017; Lunkenheimer, 2017) and the theoretical formalism relating electro- and photo-disintegration (Raskin, 1989). If successful, the method would enable precise determination of the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) rate and be broadly applicable to other radiative capture reactions in astrophysics.
