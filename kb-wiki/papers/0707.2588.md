# Formation of superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reactions

> Within the concept of the dinuclear system (DNS), a dynamical model is proposed for describing the formation of superheavy nuclei in complete fusion reactions by incorporating the coupling of the relative motion to the nucleon transfer process. The capture of two heavy colliding nuclei, the formation of the compound nucleus and the de-excitation process are calculated by using an empirical coupled channel model, solving a master equation numerically and applying statistical theory, respectively. Evaporation residue excitation functions in cold fusion reactions are investigated systematically and compared with available experimental data. Maximal production cross sections of superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reactions with stable neutron-rich projectiles are obtained. Isotopic trends in the production of the superheavy elements Z=110, 112, 114, 116, 118 and 120 are analyzed systematically. Optimal combinations and the corresponding excitation energies are proposed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a dynamical model within the dinuclear system (DNS) concept that can reproduce available experimental evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy elements Z=110-113 within error bars, but the authors note that the physical nature of synthesizing heavy fissile nuclei is very complicated and further work is needed on the coupling of dynamic deformation and nucleon transfer as well as on fission barrier heights.

## Method

The authors model the formation of superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reactions by dividing the process into three stages: capture of the two colliding nuclei (calculated with an empirical coupled channel model), formation of the compound nucleus via nucleon transfer described by numerically solving a master equation for the time evolution of the mass distribution in the dinuclear system, and de-excitation of the compound nucleus through neutron evaporation competing with fission (calculated with statistical theory). The evaporation residue cross section is expressed as a sum over partial waves of the product of the transmission probability, the fusion probability (obtained from the master equation solution), and the survival probability.

## Key numbers

* Maximal production cross section for the reaction $^{48}Ca+^{208}Pb$ is 0.2 $\mu b$ for producing element Z=102.
* Maximal production cross section for the reaction $^{70}Zn+^{208}Pb$ is 1 pb for producing element Z=112.
* Production cross sections for synthesizing Z$\geq$113 are around or below 0.1 pb.
* For the reaction $^{62}Ni+^{208}Pb\rightarrow ^{269}Ds+1n$, the maximal cross section is $3.5\pm^{2.7}_{1.8}$ pb.
* For the reaction $^{64}Ni+^{208}Pb\rightarrow ^{271}Ds+1n$, the maximal cross section is $15\pm^{9}_{6}$ pb.
* The inner fusion barrier for the reaction $^{70}Zn+^{208}Pb$ at tip-tip orientation is $B_{fus}=20.98$ MeV, and at belly-belly orientation is $B_{fus}=25.71$ MeV.
* Constant values used in the quasi-fission Kramers formula: $\Gamma=2.8$ MeV, $\hbar\omega^{B_{qf}}=2.0$ MeV, $\hbar\omega=3.0$ MeV.
* Constant values used in the fission Kramers formula: $\hbar\omega_{g.s.}=\hbar\omega_{f}=1.0$ MeV, $\Gamma_{0}=2$ MeV.
* Maximal angular momentum taken as $J_{max}=30$.

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system model approach previously developed by Adamian et al. (1997) and applied by Li et al. (2003) and Feng et al. (2006), by incorporating the coupling of relative motion to nucleon transfer and including barrier distributions. The model is compared with experimental data from GSI (Hofmann et al., 2000; Munzenberg et al., 1999) for elements Z=107-112 synthesized in cold fusion reactions, and with RIKEN results (Morita et al., 2004) for element 113. The isotopic trends are compared with calculations by Adamian et al. (2004). The results enable predictions of optimal projectile-target combinations and excitation energies for synthesizing superheavy elements up to Z=120, though the authors note that synthesizing Z$\geq$113 in cold fusion reactions appears difficult with present facilities.
