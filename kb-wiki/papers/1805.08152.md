# Two-body dissipation effect in nuclear fusion reactions

> Friction coefficients for the fusion reaction $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O $\rightarrow$ $^{32}$S are extracted based on both the time-dependent Hartree-Fock and the time-dependent density matrix methods. The latter goes beyond the mean-field approximation by taking into account the effect of two-body correlations, but in practical simulations of fusion reactions we find that the total energy is not conserved. We analyze this problem and propose a solution that allows for a clear quantification of dissipative effects in the dynamics. Compared to mean-field simulations, friction coefficients in the density-matrix approach are enhanced by about $20 \, \%$. An energy-dependence of the dissipative mechanism is also demonstrated, indicating that two-body collisions are more efficient at generating friction at low incident energies.

## Key claim

Friction coefficients in the density-matrix approach (TDDM) are enhanced by about 20% compared to mean-field (TDHF) simulations, but this result depends on a newly developed numerical procedure to restore energy conservation that is broken in practical TDDM calculations due to basis incompleteness.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and time-dependent density matrix (TDDM) calculations for the head-on fusion reaction 16O+16O → 32S, using the TDDMP approximation where only time-reversed pair states are retained. To overcome energy non-conservation in TDDM arising from a finite moving basis, they introduced a correction term v'i(t) proportional to a time-dependent parameter β(t) that is adjusted at each time step to enforce total energy conservation. Friction coefficients were extracted from the microscopic dynamics by mapping the collective motion onto a one-dimensional Langevin equation, computing the intrinsic energy as a function of the relative distance R, and applying the Rayleigh formula γ(R) = dEintr/dR divided by the collective momentum P(R).

## Key numbers

- Friction coefficients in the TDDM approach are enhanced by about 20% compared to TDHF mean-field simulations.
- At incident energy Ec.m. = 40 MeV, the total energy in an uncorrected TDDMP simulation increases by about 18 MeV (approximately 10% relative deviation) during the merging phase (30 fm/c < t < 80 fm/c).
- The correlated ground state of 16O is more bound by about 5.2 MeV compared to the Hartree-Fock state, with a two-body correlation contribution of about 14.7 MeV to the total energy.
- Occupation numbers for the 1p1/2 states are depleted by about 10% due to correlations, while 1d5/2 states gain 6-7% population.
- The collective potential difference between TDHF and TDDM is within 5 MeV for all positions and energies studied.
- Collective potential differences between incident energies Ec.m. = 20 MeV and Ec.m. = 40 MeV reach up to 20 MeV in the region 2.5 fm < R < 7.5 fm.

## Context

This work extends the Dissipative Dynamics TDHF (DD-TDHF) method developed by Washiyama, Lacroix, and Ayik (2009) to include two-body correlations beyond the mean-field approximation. The TDDM approach, originally formulated by Wang and Cassing (1985) and Gong (1990), is implemented here with a practical energy-conservation scheme that enables quantitative study of dissipation. The results demonstrate that two-body collisions are more efficient at generating friction at low incident energies, and the method enables future systematic studies of dissipative effects in nuclear fusion reactions beyond the one-body dissipation mechanism described by Blocki (1978) and Randrup (1980, 1984).
