# Threshold Pion Production at Large Momentum Transfers

> We consider threshold pion electroproduction on a proton target for photon virtualities in the region 1-10 GeV$^2$. The S-wave multipoles at threshold, $E_{0+}$ and $L_{0+}$, are calculated using light-cone sum rules.

## Key claim

The authors use light-cone sum rules (LCSR) to calculate the S-wave multipoles \(E_{0+}\) and \(L_{0+}\) for threshold pion electroproduction on a proton, covering photon virtualities \(Q^2\) from 1 to 10 GeV\(^2\). They claim that the low-energy theorem (LET) predictions are reproduced at \(Q^2 \sim 1\) GeV\(^2\) to the required accuracy \(\mathcal{O}(m_\pi)\), while the perturbative QCD (pQCD) contribution formally appears as a higher-order radiative correction, making their approach a realistic interpolation between the low-\(Q^2\) and high-\(Q^2\) limits.

## Method

The authors apply light-cone sum rules (LCSR) to the generalized form factors \(G_1^{\pi N}\) and \(G_2^{\pi N}\), which are related to the S-wave transverse \(E_{0+}\) and longitudinal \(L_{0+}\) multipoles at threshold. The LCSR technique calculates "soft" nonperturbative contributions using nucleon distribution amplitudes (DAs), avoiding double counting with pQCD contributions. To minimize dependence on poorly known DAs, the authors predict form factor ratios and normalize to experimental electromagnetic nucleon form factors.

## Key numbers

- The calculation covers photon virtualities in the range \(Q^2 \sim 1-10\) GeV\(^2\).
- The nucleon mass is taken as \(m = 939\) MeV.
- The dipole formula uses \(\mu_0^2 = 0.71\) GeV\(^2\) for normalization: \(G_D(Q^2) = 1/(1+Q^2/\mu_0^2)^2\).
- The LET results are reproduced at \(Q^2 \sim 1\) GeV\(^2\) with accuracy \(\mathcal{O}(m_\pi)\).

## Context

This work extends the LCSR technique previously developed by Braun et al. (2001) for baryon form factors to the case of threshold pion electroproduction. It addresses a gap in the literature: while chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) calculations by Bernard et al. (1992, 1995) apply at small \(Q^2\) (up to \(\sim 0.1\) GeV\(^2\)), and pQCD collinear factorization by Pire and Szymanowski (2001) applies at very large \(Q^2\), no dedicated analysis existed for the intermediate region \(Q^2 \sim 1\) GeV\(^2\). The results enable future comparisons with experiments at Jefferson Laboratory, HERMES, and MAMI, and can be improved by radiative corrections to the LCSRs and more precise lattice calculations of nucleon distribution amplitudes.
