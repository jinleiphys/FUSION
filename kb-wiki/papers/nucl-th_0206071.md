# Baryonic masses based on the NJL model

> We employ the Nambu Jona--Lasinio model to determine the vacuum pressure on the quarks in a baryon and hence their density inside. Then we estimate the baryonic masses by implementing the local density approximation for the mean field quark energies obtained in a uniform and isotropic system. We obtain a fair agreement with the experimental masses.

## Key claim

The authors use the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model to derive the effective vacuum pressure on a baryon and then, by implementing the local density approximation for quark energies, obtain baryonic masses in fair agreement with experiment, but only after taking into account the density dependence of the constituent quark masses, which is the key feature that distinguishes this work from earlier constituent quark model approaches.

## Method

The authors first compute the vacuum pressure inside and outside a nucleon using the NJL model in the mean field approximation, then equate the effective vacuum pressure to the pressure from three confined quarks described by a Gaussian wave function to fix the nucleon radius. They then implement the local density approximation (LDA) to obtain the spatial dependence of the dynamical quark masses inside the baryon, using the quark density from the Gaussian wave function to define a local Fermi momentum, and finally compute the baryon mass by integrating the local quark energy density over the baryon volume.

## Key numbers

* Nucleon radius from the Gaussian wave function: 0.860 fm (set 1) and 0.897 fm (set 2), compared to the experimental value of 0.81 fm.
* Effective vacuum pressure acting on the nucleon: 9.13 x 10^8 (MeV)^4 (set 1) and 7.71 x 10^8 (MeV)^4 (set 2).
* Vacuum pressure outside the nucleon: 2.091 x 10^10 (MeV)^4 (set 1) and 2.493 x 10^10 (MeV)^4 (set 2).
* Vacuum pressure inside the nucleon (from the Dirac sea): 2.00 x 10^10 (MeV)^4 (set 1) and 2.42 x 10^10 (MeV)^4 (set 2).
* Dynamical quark masses in vacuum: m*_u = m*_d = 367.7 MeV (set 1) and 335.5 MeV (set 2); m*_s = 549.5 MeV (set 1) and 527.6 MeV (set 2).
* Self-consistent Gaussian width parameter for u,d quarks in the proton: b = 0.878 fm (set 1) and 0.922 fm (set 2); for the s quark in the Lambda hyperon: b_s = 0.697 fm (set 1) and 0.691 fm (set 2).
* Calculated nucleon mass (with self-consistent b and no spin): 970.86 MeV (set 1) and 928.03 MeV (set 2), compared to the experimental 938.27 MeV.
* Calculated Lambda mass (with self-consistent b and no spin): 1096.34 MeV (set 1) and 1067.12 MeV (set 2), compared to the experimental 1115.68 MeV.
* Calculated Omega mass (with self-consistent b and no spin): 1493.10 MeV (set 1) and 1486.26 MeV (set 2), compared to the experimental 1672.45 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the application of the NJL model from meson properties to baryon masses, building on earlier NJL studies by Klevansky (1992), Vogl and Weise (1991), and Hatsuda and Kunihiro (1994). It contrasts with the MIT bag model approach of Chodos et al. (1974) and DeGrand et al. (1975), where the bag pressure is a free parameter, by deriving the effective bag pressure from the NJL model. The key advance over the relativistic Faddeev approach of Ishii et al. (1993, 1995) and Krewald et al. (1995) is the inclusion of the density dependence of the constituent quark masses, which the authors argue is crucial for realistic baryon masses. The results enable a more consistent connection between the NJL model and the constituent quark model for baryon spectroscopy.
