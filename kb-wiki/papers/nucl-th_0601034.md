# Nuclear models and neutrino cross sections

> Merits and faults of the effective theory Random Phase Approximations are discussed in the perspective of its use in the prediction of neutrino-nucleus cross sections.

# Nuclear models and neutrino cross sections

## Key claim
The Random Phase Approximation (RPA) is useful for predicting neutrino-nucleus cross sections, but its results are strongly dependent on the choice of effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, and for neutrinos below 100 MeV the cross sections remain very model dependent; the explicit inclusion of many-particle many-hole excitations cannot be substituted by simply readjusting effective interaction parameters.

## Method
The author performed RPA calculations for electron and neutrino scattering on ¹²C and ¹⁶O target nuclei, using four different effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (LM1, LM2, PP, and a newly defined NuInt05). Single-particle wave functions and energies were generated from a Woods-Saxon potential. The RPA secular equations were solved to obtain excited-state amplitudes, and results were compared to experimental data for magnetic form factors, excitation energies, and cross sections in the giant resonance and quasi-elastic regions.

## Key numbers
- Excitation energies of the isospin triplet 1⁺ states in ¹²C: LM1 gives 17.2 MeV (¹²C), 20.2 MeV (¹²N), 14.3 MeV (¹²B); experimental values are 15.1 MeV, 17.3 MeV, and 13.4 MeV respectively.
- The uncertainty on calculated excitation energies across different interactions is about 2 MeV.
- For neutrino energies above 50 MeV, inclusion of many-particle many-hole excitations reduces RPA cross sections by a 10-15% factor.
- In the quasi-elastic region, inclusion of Final State Interactions lowers total neutrino cross sections by a 10-15% factor.
- Electron and neutrino cross sections on ¹⁶O were calculated at an incoming lepton energy of 1 GeV and scattering angle of 30°.

## Context
This work extends the discussion of RPA limitations in nuclear physics, building on prior studies of effective interactions such as those by Rinker (1978) for muonic atom polarization in ²⁰⁸Pb, and by Bakers (1997) for spin responses in ¹²C. The author shows that while RPA is necessary for describing giant resonances and collective low-lying states, the strong dependence on the effective interaction and the need for many-particle many-hole degrees of freedom (as argued by Krewald (1980)) limits predictive power for neutrino cross sections, especially for low-energy neutrinos from supernovae or muon decay at rest. The work enables future efforts to develop more complete many-body treatments beyond RPA for neutrino-nucleus scattering.
