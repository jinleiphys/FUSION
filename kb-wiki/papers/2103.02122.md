# Actinide crystallization and fission reactions in cooling white dwarf stars

> The first solids that form as a cooling white dwarf (WD) starts to crystallize are expected to be greatly enriched in actinides. This is because the melting points of WD matter scale as $Z^{5/3}$ and actinides have the largest charge $Z$. We estimate that the solids may be so enriched in actinides that they could support a fission chain reaction. This reaction could ignite carbon burning and lead to the explosion of an isolated WD in a thermonuclear supernova (SN Ia). Our mechanism could potentially explain SN Ia with sub-Chandrasekhar ejecta masses and short delay times.

## Key claim

The first solids that form as a cooling white dwarf (WD) begins to crystallize may be so enriched in actinides, due to their high charge Z, that they could support a fission chain reaction; this reaction could ignite carbon burning and lead to the explosion of an isolated WD in a thermonuclear supernova (SN Ia), potentially explaining SN Ia with sub-Chandrasekhar ejecta masses and short delay times, though the authors emphasize this is a preliminary estimate requiring verification with detailed simulations.

## Method

The authors construct a phase diagram for a two-component N/U system using the Medin and Cumming free energy formalism, approximating the C/O background as a single component with average charge Z=7. They then build a multicomponent phase diagram using a linear mixing rule for free energies, starting from solar abundances, to determine the equilibrium composition of the solid phase. Finally, they calculate the neutron multiplication factor k∞ for the resulting solid composition using ENDF nuclear cross-section data, and estimate the minimum 235U enrichment needed for criticality, the diffusion-driven growth time of a uranium-rich crystal, and the time for a neutron from spontaneous fission to initiate a chain reaction.

## Key numbers

- The melting temperature of pure uranium (Z=92) is 95 times higher than the melting temperature of carbon.
- The critical mass of uranium at WD densities (ρ ≈ 10⁸ g/cm³) could be as small as M_c ≈ 10⁻⁶ g.
- The minimum 235U enrichment f₅ necessary for criticality ranges from 0.12 at neutron energy E=1.0 MeV to 0.26 at E=0.05 MeV.
- For a 1.1 M⊙ WD, the total delay time (main sequence lifetime plus WD cooling time) can be significantly less than 1 Gy; with an initial 235U enrichment f₅(0)=0.25, the enrichment after 0.7 Gy is f₅=0.16.
- The diffusion time for uranium to grow a crystal to critical mass is t_D ≈ 23 s, while the time for a single neutron from spontaneous fission of 238U in a critical mass is t_f ≈ 1 year.
- The crystal is estimated to grow to a mass M* ≈ 5 mg (about 5000 times the critical mass) before a fission chain reaction is initiated.
- The total energy released from fission of this crystal is E_tot ≈ 4 × 10¹⁵ ε ergs, where ε is the fraction of ions that fission.
- The final temperature from the fission reaction is estimated to be T ≥ 2.2 × 10¹⁰ K, exceeding the 5 × 10⁹ K needed for carbon ignition of a 5 mg trigger mass (Timmes and Woosley 1992).

## Context

This work proposes a completely new mechanism for SN Ia that does not require a binary companion, unlike conventional models involving a WD accreting matter from a companion star (Hillebrandt 2013, Ruiz-Lapuente 2014). It extends prior work on phase separation of high-Z elements in neutron stars (Mckinven 2016) and on 22Ne sedimentation in crystallizing WDs (Bildsten 2001, Bauer 2020), but is the first to apply these ideas to actinides with Z as high as 92. The mechanism could explain a fraction of SN Ia with short delay times (Mannucci 2006) and sub-Chandrasekhar ejecta masses, but the authors emphasize that many open issues remain, including the role of heat conduction losses, the exact composition of the solid phase, and the need for detailed astrophysical simulations.
