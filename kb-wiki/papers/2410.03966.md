# Impressions of Parton Distribution Functions

> Parton distribution functions (DFs) have long been recognised as key measures of hadron structure. Today, theoretical prediction of such DFs is becoming possible using diverse methods for continuum and lattice analyses of strong interaction (QCD) matrix elements. Recent developments include a demonstration that continuum and lattice analyses yield mutually consistent results for all pion DFs, with behaviour on the far valence domain of light-front momentum fraction that matches QCD expectations. Theory is also delivering an understanding of the distributions of proton mass and spin amongst its constituents, which varies, of course, with the resolving scale of the measuring probe. Aspects of the pion DF and proton spin developments are sketched herein, along with some novel perspectives on gluon and quark orbital angular momentum.

# Impressions of Parton Distribution Functions

## Key claim

Continuum and lattice QCD analyses of pion parton distribution functions are now mutually consistent across all parton species, with large-x behavior matching QCD expectations, but this consistency relies on the all-orders evolution scheme that extends DGLAP evolution into the nonperturbative domain and requires a hadron scale where only valence degrees-of-freedom carry all hadron properties.

## Method

The authors employ continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs) and lattice QCD (lQCD) to compute Mellin moments of parton distribution functions, then use the all-orders (AO) evolution scheme, which uses an effective charge to integrate leading-order DGLAP equations exactly, to evolve results between scales. For the pion, lQCD moments from four independent lattice studies are evolved to a common hadron scale and compared with CSM predictions; for the proton, a quark-plus-diquark Faddeev equation approach generates wave functions from which spin decompositions are derived and evolved to experimental scales.

## Key numbers

- Pion valence quark DF first moment from Gao (2022) even moments, completed via chi-squared minimization: M_G^1(zeta_2 = 2 GeV) = 0.271
- Hadron scale predicted using the process-independent charge: zeta_H = 0.331(2) GeV; lQCD studies yield consistent value zeta_H = 0.350(44) GeV
- Pion valence quark DF large-x exponent at zeta_5 = 5.2 GeV from lQCD: beta_valence = 2.55(36); effective exponent on x in [0.85,1.0]: beta_eff-lQCD_valence(zeta_5) = 2.36(31)
- Effective large-x exponents at zeta_5 for glue: beta_glue_eff-lQCD = 3.56(23); for sea: beta_sea_eff-lQCD = 4.62(26)
- Proton spin decomposition at hadron scale: quark helicity sum = 0.65 +/- 0.01; quark orbital angular momentum sum = 0.175 -/+ 0.01; glue helicity and glue OAM both zero
- Individual quark OAM at hadron scale: l_u = -0.134, l_d = 0.309
- Proton spin decomposition at COMPASS scale zeta_C = sqrt(3) GeV: quark OAM l_q = -0.027; glue helicity Delta G = 1.30; glue OAM l_g = -1.09

## Context

This review extends the line of work on parton distribution functions from continuum methods (Cui 2021, Chang 2022) and lattice QCD (Alexandrou 2021, Joo 2019, Sufian 2019, Gao 2022) by demonstrating their unification for pion DFs via the all-orders evolution scheme developed in Ding (2019) and Yin (2023). The proton spin analysis builds on the quark-diquark Faddeev equation approach (Barabanov 2020) and addresses the "proton spin crisis" first identified by the European Muon Collaboration (1987). The results enable future experiments at high-luminosity facilities (BESIII, EIC, JLab 12 GeV) to validate QCD predictions for meson and nucleon structure, particularly the large-x power-law behavior of parton distributions.
