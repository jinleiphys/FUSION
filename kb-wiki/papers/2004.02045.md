# Presupernova neutrinos: directional sensitivity and prospects for progenitor identification

> We explore the potential of current and future liquid scintillator neutrino detectors of O (10) kt mass to localize a pre-supernova neutrino signal in the sky. In the hours preceding the core collapse of a nearby star (at distance D < 1 kpc), tens to hundreds of inverse beta decay events will be recorded, and their reconstructed topology in the detector can be used to estimate the direction to the star. Although the directionality of inverse beta decay is weak (~8% forward-backward asymmetry for currently available liquid scintillators), we find that for a fiducial signal of 200 events (which is realistic for Betelgeuse), a positional error of ~60 degrees can be achieved, resulting in the possibility to narrow the list of potential stellar candidates to less than ten, typically. For a configuration with improved forward-backward asymmetry (~40%, as expected for a lithium-loaded liquid scintillator), the angular sensitivity improves to ~15 degrees, and - when a distance upper limit is obtained from the overall event rate - it is in principle possible to uniquely identify the progenitor star. Any localization information accompanying an early supernova alert will be useful to multi-messenger observations and to particle physics tests using collapsing stars.

## Key claim

For a fiducial signal of 200 inverse beta decay events (realistic for Betelgeuse), a current-generation liquid scintillator detector can achieve a positional error of about 60 degrees, narrowing the list of potential stellar progenitors to fewer than ten; with a lithium-loaded scintillator having about 40% forward-backward asymmetry, the angular sensitivity improves to about 15 degrees, and when combined with a distance upper limit from the event rate, unique progenitor identification becomes possible in principle.

## Method

The authors modeled presupernova neutrino emission from 15 and 30 solar mass progenitor stars using the IBD event rates from Patton et al. (2017) and considered a 17 kt liquid scintillator detector (representative of JUNO). They applied the CHOOZ collaboration pointing method, using the average vector of reconstructed positron-to-neutron-capture vectors from IBD events to estimate the source direction, and derived the angular uncertainty from the statistical distribution of this average vector as a function of event number and forward-backward asymmetry. The analysis considered two detector configurations: standard LAB-based liquid scintillator (LS) with about 8% forward-backward asymmetry, and a hypothetical lithium-loaded scintillator (LS-Li) with about 40% asymmetry, and included background rates from the JUNO design report.

## Key numbers

- Forward-backward asymmetry for standard liquid scintillator: a0 = 0.16 (about 8% asymmetry)
- Forward-backward asymmetry for lithium-loaded liquid scintillator: a0 = 0.78 (about 40% asymmetry)
- For LS with N=100 events: angular uncertainty beta = 70 degrees at 68% C.L.
- For LS with N=500 events: angular uncertainty beta = 40 degrees at 68% C.L.
- For LS-Li with N=100 events: angular uncertainty beta = 18 degrees at 68% C.L.
- For LS-Li with N=500 events: angular uncertainty beta = 10 degrees at 68% C.L.
- Average angular separation between nearest-neighbor candidate stars in the compiled list: 10.4 degrees
- 70% of candidate stars have nearest-neighbor separation less than 12.8 degrees
- Maximum detectable distance for presupernova signal: D_max = 1 kpc
- Background rates for JUNO: r_on_Bkg = 2.66 per hour (reactor-on), r_off_Bkg = 0.16 per hour (reactor-off)

## Context

This work extends the exploratory study by Li et al. (2020), which first showed that for optimistic presupernova flux predictions and Betelgeuse at 0.2 kpc, a signal could be discovered days before collapse with about 80 degree directional error. The present paper provides the first dedicated study of presupernova neutrino localization, incorporating a new compilation of 31 nearby core-collapse supernova candidates and examining the diverse stellar population of red and blue supergiants. The results enable improved early alerts for the SuperNova Early Warning System (SNEWS) and its successor SNEWS 2.0, potentially allowing multi-messenger observations and particle physics tests (such as axion searches) that require pointing to the collapsing star.
