# Dynamic density correlations in a baryon rich fluid using Mori-Zwanzig-Nakjima projection operator method

> In this work, we calculate the dynamic density correlations using Mori-Zwanzig-Nakajima projection operator method. With a judicious choice of slow variables we derive the evolution equations for these slow variables starting from generalised Langevin equation. We get the hydrodynamic form of density correlations function which consist of two acoustic peaks: also called Brillouin peaks, and one thermal peak: also called Rayleigh peak. We then estimate the dynamic density correlations near the critical point using critical exponents extracted from the statistical bootstrap model of hadronic matter. We find that the bulk viscosity contributes to the sound attenuation at leading order $\sim|t|^{-\frac{5}{4}}$ while the thermal conductivity contribute at sub-leading order $\sim|t|^{-\frac{3}{4}}$. On the other hand, only the thermal conductivity contributes at leading order $\sim|t|^{\frac{1}{4}}$ to the thermal diffusivity. We discuss the implications of these results in a search for the QCD critical point in the heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

Using the Mori-Zwanzig-Nakajima projection operator method with a specific choice of slow variables (baryon density, energy density, and fluid velocity fluctuations), the author derives the hydrodynamic form of the dynamic density correlation function for a baryon-rich fluid and finds that near the QCD critical point, the bulk viscosity contributes to sound attenuation at leading order as \(\sim|t|^{-5/4}\) while thermal conductivity contributes at sub-leading order \(\sim|t|^{-3/4}\), with only thermal conductivity contributing at leading order \(\sim|t|^{1/4}\) to thermal diffusivity, based on critical exponents from the statistical bootstrap model.

## Method

The author applies the time-independent Mori-Zwanzig-Nakajima projection operator formalism to derive generalized Langevin equations for the chosen slow variables (baryon number density, energy density, and fluid velocity fluctuations) in a baryon-rich fluid. From these equations, linearized hydrodynamic equations in the Landau frame are obtained, and the dynamic density correlation function is derived by inverting the Laplace-Fourier transformed matrix equation and taking thermal averages. The critical behavior of the density correlations near the QCD critical point is then estimated by substituting the singular behavior of thermodynamic quantities and transport coefficients using critical exponents extracted from the statistical bootstrap model (SBM) with exponent parameter \(a = -4\).

## Key numbers

* Bulk viscosity contributes to sound attenuation at leading order \(\sim |t|^{-5/4}\) near the critical point.
* Thermal conductivity contributes to sound attenuation at sub-leading order \(\sim |t|^{-3/4}\) near the critical point.
* Thermal conductivity contributes to thermal diffusivity at leading order \(\sim |t|^{1/4}\) near the critical point.
* For a massless ideal classical gas, the width of the Brillouin peak reduces to \(\Gamma_{S,R} = \eta / (6 n_{b,0} T_0)\).
* The critical exponents of the statistical bootstrap model used are \(\hat{\alpha} = 1/2\), \(\hat{\beta} = 0\), \(\hat{\gamma} = 3/2\), and \(\hat{\nu} = 1/2\).

## Context

This work extends the application of the Mori-Zwanzig-Nakajima projection operator method, traditionally used for non-relativistic systems, to relativistic fluids and specifically to the study of dynamic density correlations near the QCD critical point. It builds on prior work by Minami (2009) on relativistic corrections to Brillouin peaks and by Minami (2011) on critical dynamics using generalized Langevin equations with non-linear couplings, but differs by using the statistical bootstrap model rather than the 3D Ising model to extract critical exponents. The results enable predictions for the behavior of Rayleigh and Brillouin peaks in heavy-ion collision experiments searching for the QCD critical point, particularly through the Beam Energy Scan program at RHIC and SPS.
