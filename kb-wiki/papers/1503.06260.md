# The theory of the Bohr-Weisskopf effect in the hyperfine structure

> For twenty years research into the anomalies in the HF spectra was going in a wrong direction by fighting the related Bohr-Weisskopf effect. As a way out, the model-independent way is proposed of estimating the nuclear radii from the hyper-fine splitting. The way is based on analogy of HFS to internal conversion coefficients, and the Bohr-Weisskopf effect - to the anomalies in the internal conversion coefficients. This makes transparent It is shown that the parameters which can be extracted from the data are the even nuclear moments of the magnetization distribution. The radii $R_2$ and (for the first time) $R_4$ are thus obtained by analysis of the experimental HFS for the H- and Li-like ions of $^{209}$Bi. The critical prediction of the HFS for the $2p_{1/2}$ state is discussed. The moments may be determined in this way only if the higher QED effects are properly taken into account. Therefore, this set of the parameters form a basis of a strict QED test. Experimental prospects are discussed, aimed at retrieving data on the HFS values for a set of a few-electron configurations of the atom.

# The theory of the Bohr-Weisskopf effect in the hyperfine structure

## Key claim
The Bohr-Weisskopf effect in hyperfine structure should not be treated as an anomaly to be eliminated via specific differences, but rather as a source of nuclear structure information. The authors propose a model-independent method based on an analogy with internal conversion coefficients, which allows extraction of even multipole moments of the nuclear magnetization distribution, provided higher-order QED corrections are properly accounted for.

## Method
The hyperfine splitting (HFS) is treated as the zero-energy limit of an internal conversion (IC) transition, using the formalism of anomalous internal conversion theory. The nuclear transition current is decomposed into a surface-current (SC) model contribution and a penetration term, and the radial matrix element is expanded in a Taylor series to separate the dependence on even moments of the magnetization distribution. By solving coupled equations for HFS values from multiple electronic configurations (e.g., 1s in H-like and 2s in Li-like ions of 209Bi), the second and fourth radial moments R2 and R4 of the nuclear magnetization are extracted.

## Key numbers
- For the 1s state of H-like 209Bi, the calculated HFS including QED corrections is w_th = 5.0558 eV, compared to the experimental value of 5.0863(11) eV (from exli).
- The root-mean-square radius of nuclear magnetization extracted from the 1s level alone is R2^(1s) = 6.207 fm; from the 2s level alone it is R2^(2s) = 6.275 fm.
- Simultaneous analysis of both 1s and 2s levels yields R2 = 6.118 fm and R4 = 6.760 fm (with R0 = 7.121 fm), or R2 = 6.12 fm and R4 = 6.78 fm (with R0 = 6.20 fm, differing by 0.3%).
- The predicted HFS for the 2p1/2 state is w_2p = 0.25753 eV, compared to a theoretical value of 257.84(5) meV from shab2.
- The Bohr-Weisskopf effect (defined as (w_NP - w_exp)/w_NP) is 1.73% for the 1s level, 1.47% for the 2s level, and 0.29% for the 2p1/2 level.
- The specific difference parameter zeta obtained from the fit is 0.1746, which is 3% higher than the value of 0.16886 used in prior work (shab1, shab2).

## Context
This work extends the classical theory of anomalous internal conversion (Church and Weneser, 1956; anom) to the hyperfine structure problem, building on earlier proposals by Karpeshin (HF, echa, book) that HFS can be calculated as the limiting case of ICC at zero transition energy. It directly contradicts the approach of Shabaev et al. (shab1, shab2) and others who have spent two decades trying to cancel the Bohr-Weisskopf effect via specific differences, which the authors argue is model-dependent and loses nuclear structure information. The method enables extraction of not only the second moment R2 (which is the usual effective nuclear radius discussed in isotopic chain studies, e.g., Barzakh (barza) and Kara (kara)), but also the fourth moment R4 for the first time. This provides a stringent test of QED corrections and opens the way for future experiments at storage rings (GSI Darmstadt, Lawrence Livermore, Lanzhou) to measure HFS in few-electron configurations of various atoms.
