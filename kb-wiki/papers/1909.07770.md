# Fusion of halo nucleus $^{6}He$ on $^{238}U$: evidence for tennis-ball (bubble) structure of the core of the halo (even the giant-halo) nucleus

> In a decade-and-a-half old experiment, Raabe et al.(Nature 431 (2004) 823), had studied fusion of an incoming beam of halo nucleus $^{6}He$ with the target nucleus $^{238}U$. We extract a new interpretation of the experiment, different from the one that has been inferred so far. We show that their experiment is actually able to discriminate between the structures of the target nucleus (behaving as standard nucleus with density distribution described with canonical RMS radius r = $r_0 A^{\frac{1}{3}}$ with $r_0$ = 1.2 fm), and the {\bf "core"} of the halo nucleus, which surprisingly, does not follow the standard density distribution with the above RMS radius. In fact the core has the structure of a tennis-ball (bubble) like nucleus, with a "hole" at the centre of the density distribution. This novel interpretation of the fusion experiment provides an unambigous support to an almost two decades old model (Abbas, Mod. Phys. Lett. A 16 (2001) 755), of the halo nuclei. This Quantum Chromodyanamics based model, succeeds in identifyng all known halo nuclei and makes clear-cut and unique predictions for new and heavier halo nuclei. This model supports the existence of tennis-ball (bubble) like core, of even the giant-neutron halo nuclei. This should prove beneficial to the experimentalists, to go forward more confidently, in their study of exotic nuclei.

# Fusion of halo nucleus $^{6}He$ on $^{238}U$: evidence for tennis-ball (bubble) structure of the core of the halo (even the giant-halo) nucleus

## Key claim

The fusion experiment of Raabe et al. (Nature 2004) actually discriminates between the standard nuclear density distribution of the target $^{238}U$ and the core of the halo nucleus $^{6}He$, which does not follow the canonical RMS radius formula $r = r_0 A^{1/3}$ with $r_0 = 1.2$ fm; instead, the core has a tennis-ball (bubble) like structure with a hole at the center of its density distribution, providing unambiguous support to the author's QCD-based model of halo nuclei (Abbas, Mod. Phys. Lett. A 2001).

## Method

The author reinterprets the existing experimental data of Raabe et al. (Nature 2004), who fired both $^{4}He$ and the two-neutron halo nucleus $^{6}He$ onto a $^{238}U$ target and distinguished complete fusion from neutron transfer by detecting fission events in coincidence with or without an accompanying $^{4}He$ particle. Using Ockham's razor, the author argues that the observed fusion of only the projectile core with a neutron-fattened target nucleus implies the core must differ fundamentally from the target in its density distribution. The author then connects this to electron scattering data showing a central density depression in $^{4}He$ and $^{3}He$, and to group-theoretical calculations of hidden color components in 9-quark and 12-quark systems within SU(12) symmetry.

## Key numbers

- The central density of $^{4}He$ is about 2.5 times higher than that of heavy nuclei like bismuth and lead, as determined from electron scattering.
- The RMS radius of standard nuclei follows $r = r_0 A^{1/3}$ with $r_0 = 1.2$ fm.
- The hidden color component of the 9-quark system is 97.6%, and of the 12-quark system is 99.8%, as determined group-theoretically.
- The binding energy of $^{3}H$ is 8.48 MeV, with a size of 1.7 fm; $^{4}He$ has a size of 1.674 fm; $^{3}He$ has a size of 1.88 fm and binding energy 7.7 MeV.
- Inter-triton cluster bond energy is about 5.4 MeV, derived from binding energy systematics of neutron-rich nuclei (e.g., $^{9}Li$: 19.90 MeV above 3 x 8.48 MeV, giving C = 6.63 MeV; $^{12}Be$: 34.73 MeV above 4 x 8.48 MeV, giving C = 5.79 MeV; $^{18}C$: 64.78 MeV above 6 x 8.48 MeV, giving C = 5.40 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the author's QCD-based model of halo nuclei (Abbas, Mod. Phys. Lett. A 2001), which predicted that the cores of halo nuclei have tennis-ball (bubble) like density distributions with central holes, in contrast to the standard assumption that the core density distribution is identical to the bare ground state nucleus (Tanihata, Nucl. Phys. A 2001). The reinterpretation of the Raabe et al. (Nature 2004) fusion experiment contradicts the conventional picture that the core of the halo nucleus has the same density distribution as a free nucleus, and instead supports the existence of bubble-like cores even in giant-halo nuclei such as $^{124}Zr$ and $^{64}Ca$, as predicted by Meng and Ring (Phys. Rev. Lett. 1998) and Zhang, Meng, Zhou and Zeng (Chin. Phys. Lett. 2002). The work enables experimental searches for central density depressions in neutron-rich nuclei via electron scattering (Bertulani, J. Phys. G 2007) and motivates searches for heavier halo nuclei beyond the current heaviest known, $^{37}Mg$ (Kobayashi et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 2014).
