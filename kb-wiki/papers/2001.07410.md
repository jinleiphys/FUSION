# Microscopic study of the $^{7}$Li-nucleus potential

> The optical potential without any free parameters for $^7$Li-nucleus interaction system is studied in a microscopic approach. It is obtained by folding the microscopic optical potentials of the constituent nucleons of $^{7}$Li over their density distributions. We employ an isospin-dependent nucleon microscopic optical potential, which is based on the Skyrme nucleon-nucleon effective interaction and derived by using the Green's function method, to be the nucleon optical potential. Harmonic oscillator shell model is used to describe the internal wave function of $^7$Li and get the nucleon density distribution. The $^7$Li microscopic optical potential is used to predict the reaction cross sections and elastic scattering angular distributions for target range from $^{27}$Al to $^{208}$Pb and energy range below 450 MeV. Generally the results can reproduce the measured data reasonably well. In addition, the microscopic optical potential is comparable to a global phenomenological optical potential in fitting the presently existing measured data generally.

## Key claim

The authors construct a fully microscopic optical potential for the \(^{7}\)Li-nucleus system with no free parameters, and show that it reproduces measured elastic-scattering angular distributions and reaction cross sections reasonably well for targets from \(^{27}\)Al to \(^{208}\)Pb at incident energies below 450 MeV, but the model fails for light targets such as \(^{16}\)O and shows discrepancies at larger scattering angles that may be corrected by including breakup effects.

## Method

The microscopic optical potential (MOP) for \(^{7}\)Li is obtained by folding the isospin-dependent nucleon MOPs (based on the Skyrme effective interaction SKC16 and derived via the Green's function method) over the neutron and proton density distributions of \(^{7}\)Li. The internal wave function of \(^{7}\)Li is described by a harmonic-oscillator shell model, with the oscillator parameter \(\beta = 0.2743\ \text{fm}^{-2}\) fixed by the nuclear matter root-mean-square radius of 2.50 fm. The resulting MOP is used to predict elastic-scattering angular distributions and reaction cross sections for targets from \(^{27}\)Al to \(^{208}\)Pb at incident energies below 450 MeV, and the results are compared with experimental data and with a global phenomenological optical potential (GOP) from Xu et al. (2018).

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter root-mean-square radius of \(^{7}\)Li set to 2.50 fm, yielding oscillator parameter \(\beta = 0.2743\ \text{fm}^{-2}\).
- Density distribution parameters: \(a_n = 0.0921\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(a_p = 0.0621\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(b_n = 0.0081\ \text{fm}^{-5}\), \(b_p = 0.0076\ \text{fm}^{-5}\) for the analytical form \(\rho_{n(p)}(r) = (a_{n(p)} + b_{n(p)} r^2) \exp(-\frac{7}{6}\beta r^2)\).
- Elastic-scattering angular distributions calculated for targets \(^{27}\)Al (6.0–24.0 MeV), \(^{58}\)Ni (14.22–42.0 MeV), \(^{65}\)Cu (25.0 MeV), \(^{89}\)Y (60.0 MeV), \(^{116}\)Sn (18.0–35.0 MeV), \(^{138}\)Ba (28.0 MeV), and \(^{208}\)Pb (27.0–52.0 MeV).
- Reaction cross sections calculated for \(^{13}\)C, \(^{27}\)Al, \(^{28}\)Si, \(^{64}\)Zn, \(^{\text{nat}}\)Cu, \(^{116}\)Sn, \(^{138}\)Ba, and \(^{208}\)Pb up to 450 MeV.
- Notch perturbation analysis for \(^{138}\)Ba at 28.0 MeV: best fit obtained with \(N_R = 0.89\) (weaker real part) or \(N_I = 1.89\) (stronger imaginary part) in the surface region 6 fm < R < 12 fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic optical potentials for nucleons (Shen et al., 2009), deuterons (Guo et al., 2010), tritons (Guo et al., 2014), and helium isotopes (Guo et al., 2009, 2011, 2017) to the weakly bound projectile \(^{7}\)Li. It provides a parameter-free alternative to the semi-microscopic potential of Woods et al. (1982) and the global phenomenological potential of Xu et al. (2018), and is applicable where experimental data are scarce. The analysis identifies that including breakup effects (not accounted for in the folding model) could improve the MOP, particularly at larger scattering angles, which the authors state will be their next subject.
