# Investigation of evaluated nuclear data in the prediction of inherent neutron sources

> Quantifying inherent neutron sources in matter, particularly $(α, n)$ reactions and spontaneous fission, is important in nuclear engineering and other fields. The SOURCES code is a common tool for calculating the yield and spectrum of such neutrons. This paper critically examines all modelling assumptions and nuclear data in SOURCES and proposes alternative approaches where applicable. For $(α, n)$ reactions, we show that the alpha emission lines for $^{235}\mathrm{U}$ should be updated. Furthermore, we compare four different stopping power data sets for alpha particles slowing down and propose measurements to constrain mixed oxide nuclear fuel data. We use the computer code PHITS to show that energy and angular straggling during the slowing down of alpha particles in the material of interest is unimportant. Then, we compare the cross section and emission spectrum of $(α, n)$ reactions in SOURCES to recently evaluated data libraries. Importantly, the modelling of SOURCES for the emission spectrum seems too simple and may need to be updated. Finally, we compare data on spontaneous fission and show that while the neutron yield from SOURCES is reliable, some discrepancy is found with the neutron spectrum of evaluated data libraries. Complementing this work is an implementation of spontaneous fission in the Monte Carlo code OpenMC.

## Key claim

The paper critically examines all modelling assumptions and nuclear data in the SOURCES code for calculating inherent neutron sources, showing that while the code remains largely reliable for neutron yield from spontaneous fission, the modelling of the emission spectrum for (alpha,n) reactions appears too simple and may need updating, and that the alpha emission lines for uranium-235 should be updated to correct a 6-7% underestimate of alpha particle production.

## Method

The authors compared the alpha line data, stopping power data sets, (alpha,n) cross sections, and spontaneous fission data used in SOURCES-4C against more recent evaluated data libraries including ENDF/B-VIII.0, as well as against the stopping power codes ASTAR, ATIMA, and SRIM. They used the Monte Carlo code PHITS to simulate alpha particle transport through thin films of matter, comparing results with and without energy and angular straggling to test whether the deterministic stopping power model in SOURCES is adequate. The comparison of (alpha,n) cross sections and emission spectra used recently evaluated data libraries, and spontaneous fission data were compared between SOURCES and evaluated data libraries.

## Key numbers

- SOURCES contains 619 alpha lines in 96 nuclides; ENDF/B-VIII.0 contains 869 alpha lines for the same 96 nuclides, of which 798 are below 6.5 MeV
- For uranium-235, SOURCES contains 11 alpha lines while ENDF/B-VIII.0 contains 21 lines; the extra lines have a total intensity of 6.2%, and SOURCES alpha line intensities for uranium-235 only add up to 92.7%
- For 3% enriched UO2 fuel, the underestimate from uranium-235 alpha lines leads to about 1.2% underestimate in the number of alpha particles produced
- For thin films where thickness is one percent or less of the alpha particle range, the PHITS simulation reproduces the ATIMA stopping power with discrepancy of less than 0.15%

## Context

This work extends the analysis of SOURCES data performed by Simakov (2017), which focused on yields and did not analyse some underlying assumptions in the SOURCES transport coding, and by Cano-Ott and Parvu (2024-2025) which focused on (alpha,n) cross sections. The paper enables improved predictions of inherent neutron sources for nuclear engineering applications including radiation protection studies of MOX fuel elements, nuclear reactor startup calculations, and non-destructive assay measurements, while identifying that new measurements of stopping power in plutonium are needed to constrain mixed oxide nuclear fuel data.
