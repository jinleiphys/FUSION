# Quantum Energies of Interfaces

> We present a method for computing the one-loop, renormalized quantum energies of symmetrical interfaces of arbitrary dimension and codimension using elementary scattering data. Internal consistency requires finite-energy sum rules relating phase shifts to bound state energies.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method for computing the one-loop renormalized quantum energy of symmetrical interfaces of arbitrary dimension and codimension using elementary scattering data, but the method's internal consistency requires that finite-energy sum rules relating phase shifts to bound state energies hold for the renormalization to be well-defined.

## Method

The authors compute the one-loop quantum energy per unit volume of trivial dimensions for interfaces by expressing it as an integral over phase shifts from scattering theory in the non-trivial dimensions, combined with a sum over bound state energies. They regulate ultraviolet divergences using dimensional regularization, subtract Born approximations from the phase shifts to isolate divergent contributions, and then add back the corresponding Feynman diagrams which are renormalized by standard counterterms. The method requires finite-energy sum rules (generalizations of Levinson's theorem) to cancel spurious poles that appear when analytically continuing from the regulated regime to physical integer dimensions.

- For \(m+n<3\), subtraction of the tadpole graph is sufficient; for \(3<m+n<5\), the two-point function must also be subtracted.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous methods for computing one-loop quantum energies of solitons (the \(n=0\) case) to interfaces with arbitrary numbers of trivial and non-trivial dimensions. It contrasts with approaches by Yaffe (using one-dimensional functional determinants) and Bordag (using zeta function regularization and analytic continuation to imaginary momentum), which have not been generalized beyond \(m=1\) and which renormalize by subtracting local terms with arbitrary scales rather than the entire Feynman graph. The method enables practical numerical computation of interface tensions and induced cosmological constants for a wide class of backgrounds, and the required sum rules were first obtained by Puff (1975) within scattering theory.
