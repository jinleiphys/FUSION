# Light front approach to correlations in hot quark matter

> We investigate two-quark correlations in hot and dense quark matter. To this end we use the light front field theory extended to finite temperature $T$ and chemical potential $μ$. Therefore it is necessary to develop quantum statistics formulated on the light front plane. As a test case for light front quantization at finite $T$ and $μ$ we consider the NJL model. The solution of the in-medium gap equation leads to a constituent quark mass which depends on $T$ and $μ$. Two-quark systems are considered in the pionic and diquark channel. We compute the masses of the two-body system using a $T$-matrix approach.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that light front quantization can be extended to finite temperature and chemical potential, recovering known properties of the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model such as chiral symmetry restoration and the dissociation of pion and diquark states, but only within the limitations of the NJL model as a test case and using a specific regularization scheme (Lepage–Brodsky cut-off).

## Method

The authors formulate quantum statistics on the light front plane by deriving the grand canonical statistical operator from the von-Neumann equation for light front time, then compute the in-medium quark propagator and the gap equation for the constituent quark mass as a function of temperature \(T\) and chemical potential \(\mu\). Two-body correlations in the pionic and scalar diquark channels are evaluated using a \(T\)-matrix approach within the Bethe-Salpeter framework, with the pole condition determining the bound-state masses.

## Context

This work extends light front quantization techniques, previously applied to vacuum QCD by Dirac (1949) and Brodsky (1997), to finite temperature and density, using the NJL model as a test case. It recovers results from earlier finite-temperature NJL studies by Klevansky (1992) and provides a foundation for applying light front methods to explore the full QCD phase diagram, including color superconductivity via the Thouless criterion (not shown in the truncated text). The approach enables future work on nonperturbative QCD at all values of temperature and chemical potential.
