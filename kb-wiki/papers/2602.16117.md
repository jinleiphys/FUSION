# Solving BDNK diffusion using physics-informed neural networks

> In this work, we reformulate the relativistic BDNK (Bemfica-Disconzi-Noronha-Kovtun) diffusion equation in flux-conservative form, and solve the resulting equations in $(1+1)$D using both a second-order Kurganov-Tadmor finite volume scheme and physics-informed neural networks (PINNs). In particular, we introduce the SA-PINN-ACTO framework, which combines the self-adaptive PINN technique with an exact enforcement of initial and periodic boundary conditions through an algebraic transform of the network's raw output, allowing the network to focus solely on minimizing the PDE residual. We test both approaches on smooth and discontinuous initial data, for both trivial and dynamically evolving velocity and temperature BDNK backgrounds, and for two characteristic speeds. The SA-PINN-ACTO method matches the converged Kurganov-Tadmor solutions for smooth profiles, while for discontinuous profiles the errors increase, reflecting an expected limitation of PINNs near sharp gradients.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the SA-PINN-ACTO framework can match the converged Kurganov-Tadmor finite volume solutions for smooth BDNK diffusion profiles, but the authors acknowledge that errors increase for discontinuous profiles, reflecting an expected limitation of PINNs near sharp gradients.

## Method

The authors reformulate the relativistic BDNK diffusion equation in (1+1)D into a flux-conservative first-order system by introducing auxiliary fields N_mu = -partial_mu alpha, then solve it using both a second-order Kurganov-Tadmor finite volume scheme with TVD minmod limiter and SSP-RK2 time integration, and a novel SA-PINN-ACTO framework that combines self-adaptive PINN weights with exact enforcement of initial and periodic boundary conditions through an algebraic transform of the network's raw output. The probe approximation is used where temperature and fluid velocity are background fields, neglecting backreaction to the energy-momentum sector.

## Key numbers

* For smooth initial data (Gaussian profile), the SA-PINN-ACTO method matches the converged Kurganov-Tadmor solutions; the paper reports that all KT simulations used N=1000 spatial cells for continuous initial data and N=2000 for discontinuous initial data.
* The characteristic speed used in the KT scheme is c = sqrt(sigma/lambda), with the constraint that c_ch^2 = sigma/lambda is a constant in (0,1) for causality and stability.
* The conductivity sigma uses C_B = 1/(4pi) for the small fugacity limit and C_B = 0.4 for higher-fugacity simulations, with the expression sigma = (C_B n / T^2)[(1/3) coth(alpha) - nT/(epsilon+P)].
* The adaptive time step in the KT scheme uses Delta t = Delta x / (8 c_max(t)), where c_max(t) is the maximum local characteristic speed.

## Context

This work extends the flux-conservative formulation of BDNK hydrodynamics introduced by Clarisse (2025) specifically to the diffusion-only case, and applies physics-informed neural networks for the first time to relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations. The BDNK theory (Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, Kovtun 2017-2020) provides a causal, stable first-order formulation that contrasts with Israel-Stewart-type theories where causality must be checked through evolving nonlinear inequalities. The paper enables future work on solving BDNK equations in higher dimensions or with full coupling to the energy-momentum sector using neural network approaches, while noting that finite volume methods such as KT remain superior for problems with sharp gradients.
