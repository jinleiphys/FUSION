# Nuclear Stopping as A Probe to In-medium Nucleon-nucleon Cross Section in Intermediate Energy Heavy Ion Collisions

> Using an isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics, nuclear stopping in intermediate heavy ion collisions has been studied. The calculation has been done for colliding systems with different neutron-proton ratios in beam energy ranging from 15MeV/u to 150MeV/u. It is found that, in the energy region from above Fermi energy to 150MeV/u, nuclear stopping is very sensitive to the isospin dependence of in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section, but insensitive to symmetry potential. From this investigation, we propose that nuclear stopping can be used as a new probe to extract the information on the isospin dependence of in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions.

# Nuclear Stopping as A Probe to In-medium Nucleon-nucleon Cross Section in Intermediate Energy Heavy Ion Collisions

## Key claim
Nuclear stopping, quantified by the momentum quadrupole \(Q_{ZZ}\) and the transverse-parallel ratio \(R\), can serve as a probe to extract the isospin dependence of the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section in heavy ion collisions, but only in the beam energy region from above the Fermi energy to about 150 MeV/u; below the Fermi energy, stopping is sensitive to both the in-medium cross section and the symmetry potential, making the probe ambiguous in that lower energy regime.

## Method
The authors used an isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model, which includes a density-dependent Skyrme mean field, Yukawa, Coulomb, momentum-dependent, Pauli, and symmetry potentials, along with an empirical in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section \(\sigma_{NN}^{med} = (1 + \alpha \rho / \rho_0) \sigma_{NN}^{free}\) with \(\alpha \approx -0.2\). They simulated central and peripheral collisions for five symmetric systems (\(^{20}\text{Ne}+^{20}\text{Ne}\), \(^{40}\text{Ar}+^{40}\text{Ar}\), \(^{80}\text{Zn}+^{80}\text{Zn}\), \(^{112}\text{Sn}+^{112}\text{Sn}\), \(^{124}\text{Sn}+^{124}\text{Sn}\)) at beam energies from 15 MeV/u to 150 MeV/u, comparing cases with and without isospin-dependent cross sections and with three different forms of symmetry potential.

## Key numbers
- The free neutron-proton cross section is about 3 times larger than the free proton-proton or neutron-neutron cross section below 300 MeV.
- For the reaction \(^{112}\text{Sn}+^{112}\text{Sn}\) at 100 MeV/u and impact parameter \(b = 0.0\) fm, the asymptotic stopping ratio \(R\) is 0.82 with isospin-dependent cross section and symmetry potential \(U_1^{sym}\), 0.70 with isospin-independent cross section and \(U_1^{sym}\), and 0.84 with isospin-dependent cross section and no symmetry potential.
- For the same reaction at \(b = 5.0\) fm, the asymptotic \(R\) values converge to 0.50–0.51 across all three cases, showing the loss of sensitivity to isospin dependence at large impact parameters.
- The Skyrme potential parameters used are \(\alpha = -390.1\) MeV, \(\beta = 320.3\) MeV, and \(\gamma = 1.1667\); the momentum-dependent interaction parameters are \(t_4 = 1.57\) MeV and \(t_5 = 5 \times 10^{-4}\) MeV\(^{-2}\); the Pauli potential strength is \(V_p = 30\) MeV with \(p_0 = 400\) MeV/c and \(q_0 = 5.64\) fm.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Bauer et al. (1990s) on nuclear stopping power in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions, which used mean fields without symmetry potential, and by Bass, Yennello, Johnston, and Bao-An Li (late 1990s) who suggested isospin equilibration as a probe of stopping. The present paper systematically compares the roles of the isospin-dependent in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section and the symmetry potential, finding that above the Fermi energy the cross section dominates stopping, enabling a new method to extract its isospin dependence. This enables future experiments to use measured stopping observables (e.g., \(R\) or \(Q_{ZZ}\)) to constrain the in-medium cross section for neutron-proton versus like-nucleon collisions, which is critical for understanding nuclear matter transport properties and the equation of state.
