# Nuclear Scattering at Very High Energies

> We discuss the current understanding of nuclear scattering at very high energies. We point out several serious inconsistencies in nowadays models, which provide big problems for any interpretation of data at high energy nuclear collisions. We outline how to develop a fully self-consistent formalism, which in addition uses all the knowledge available from studying electron-positron annihilation and deep inelastic scattering, providing a solid basis for further developments concerning secondary interactions.

## Key claim
The paper argues that current models for high-energy nuclear scattering (above 100 GeV cms energy per nucleon) contain serious theoretical inconsistencies, particularly regarding energy conservation in multiple-scattering calculations, and that a fully self-consistent formalism must treat energy sharing identically in both cross-section calculations and particle production.

## Method
The authors develop a "Parton-based Gribov-Regge Theory" that combines elements of Gribov-Regge multiple-scattering theory with parton-model factorization. They define three types of elementary parton-parton scatterings (soft, hard, and semi-hard) and construct hadron-hadron and nucleus-nucleus scattering amplitudes by summing over multiple interactions between parton constituents, with energy conservation enforced through explicit momentum-sharing variables in both cut and uncut diagrams.

## Key numbers
- The energy threshold for "very high energies" is defined as above 100 GeV cms energy per nucleon.
- The cutoff for perturbative QCD applicability is set at Q0^2 approximately 1 GeV^2.
- The hard scattering slope R_hard^2 is considered negligible compared to the soft Pomeron slope.
- The factor K approximately 1.5 is used to account for higher-order QCD corrections in hard scattering cross sections.
- The characteristic hadronic mass scale s0 is approximately 1 GeV^2.

## Context
This work extends the Gribov-Regge theory (Gribov, 1968; Gribov, 1969) and the parton model approach (Sjostrand, 1987; Wang, 1996) by addressing the energy-conservation inconsistency first discussed by Abramovsky (1992) and Braun (1990). The formalism aims to provide a solid basis for interpreting data from SPS heavy-ion programs at CERN and for modeling cosmic-ray air showers, enabling consistent treatment of both cross sections and particle production in nuclear collisions.
