# Ground state of finite nuclei evaluated from realistic interactions

> Ground state properties of finite nuclei ($^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca) are evaluated from realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions. The calculations are based on the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation. Special attention is paid to the role of the energy spectrum for the particle states, in particular for those close to the Fermi energy. Additional binding energy is obtained from the inclusion of the hole-hole scattering term within the framework of the Green function approach. Results for the energy distribution of the single-particle strength and the sensitivity to the nucleon-nucleon interaction are investigated. For that purpose three modern nucleon-nucleon interactions are employed: the Argonne V18, the charge dependent Bonn potential and a realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction which is based on chiral perturbation theory and which has recently been fitted by the Idaho group.

## Key claim

Ground state properties of finite nuclei (¹⁶O and ⁴⁰Ca) can be reproduced within 0.5 MeV per nucleon of the experimental binding energy using realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, but only if the oscillator parameter defining the model-space basis is constrained to yield the empirical radius; without that constraint, the calculated minima yield radii that are too small and densities that are too large, especially for ⁴⁰Ca.

## Method

The authors performed Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations for ¹⁶O and ⁴⁰Ca using a double-partitioned Hilbert space: short-range correlations outside a finite shell-model space were treated via a G-matrix solution of the Bethe-Goldstone equation, while long-range correlations inside the model space were evaluated by solving a Dyson equation for the single-particle Green function, including both 2p1h and 2h1p self-energy contributions. Three realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions were employed: the Argonne V18, the charge-dependent Bonn (CDBonn) potential, and the chiral-perturbation-theory-based Idaho A potential.

## Key numbers

- For ¹⁶O using CDBonn, the BHF calculation with the conventional choice of particle spectrum yields a binding energy about 2 MeV per nucleon less than the continuous-choice BHF calculation.
- For ¹⁶O with CDBonn, the BHF(mod) approach (model-space treatment with continuous choice) gives single-particle energies 0.9 MeV (s₁/₂), 1.6 MeV (p₃/₂), and 1.8 MeV (p₁/₂) more attractive than the BHF(cont) approach.
- The spin-orbit splitting for protons in the p-shell of ¹⁶O is 3.4 MeV in BHF(cont), 3.1 MeV in BHF(mod), and slightly larger in the full Green function approach, all much smaller than the experimental value of 6.3 MeV.
- For ¹⁶O, all three interactions yield total binding energies within 1 MeV per nucleon of the experimental 7.98 MeV per nucleon; the quasiparticle spectroscopic factors for p₃/₂ and p₁/₂ range between 0.71 and 0.75.
- For ¹⁶O, the energy minimum occurs at oscillator parameter α = 0.475 fm⁻¹ (CDBonn), 0.45 fm⁻¹ (Idaho A), and 0.44 fm⁻¹ (Argonne V18), corresponding to proton radii of 2.29 fm, 2.41 fm, and 2.48 fm respectively, all smaller than the experimental radius.
- For ⁴⁰Ca with CDBonn, the inclusion of 2h1p contributions in the Green function approach reduces the discrepancy in the d₃/₂ removal energy by more than 3 MeV compared to the BHF(mod) approximation.
- For ⁴⁰Ca, the calculated binding energies agree with the experimental 8.55 MeV per nucleon when the oscillator constant is fixed to α = 0.35 fm⁻¹, but the minimum-energy radius for CDBonn is about 25 percent smaller than the experimental value.

## Context

This work extends the BHF approach for finite nuclei by incorporating a detailed treatment of low-lying particle-particle excitations and hole-hole scattering terms via the Green function method, following earlier techniques applied to nuclear matter by Kuo and collaborators and to finite nuclei by Skouras and Nili. The results confirm the sensitivity of BHF calculations to the single-particle spectrum near the Fermi energy, as previously noted for nuclear matter by Fiasconaro and by Gad and Müther. The over-binding and too-small radii obtained when the oscillator parameter is not constrained suggest that repulsive three-nucleon forces, such as those used by Grangé and collaborators or by Pudliner and collaborators to reproduce nuclear matter saturation, are needed to cure the deficiency in finite nuclei.
