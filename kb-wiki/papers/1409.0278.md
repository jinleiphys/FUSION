# Effects of equation of state on hydrodynamic expansion, spectra, flow harmonics and two-pion interferometry

> We perform an extensive study of the role played by the equation of state in the hydrodynamic evolution of the matter produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions. By using the same initial conditions and freeze-out scenario, the effects of different equations of state are compared by calculating their respective hydrodynamical evolution, particle spectra, harmonic flow coefficients $v_2$, $v_3$ and $v_4$ and two-pion interferometry radius parameters. The equations of state investigated contain distinct features, such as the nature of the phase transition, as well as strangeness and baryon density contents, which are expected to lead to different hydrodynamic responses. The results of our calculations are compared to the data recorded at two RHIC energies, 130 GeV and 200 GeV. The three equations of state used in the calculations are found to describe the data reasonably well. Differences can be observed among the studied observables, but they are quite small. In particular, the collective flow parameters are found not to be sensitive to the choice of the equation of state, whose implications are discussed.

## Key claim

The three equations of state (LQCD, CEP, and FOS) all describe the measured data reasonably well, but the differences among the studied observables are quite small; in particular, the collective flow parameters are found not to be sensitive to the choice of the equation of state, contrary to the expectation that distinct features such as the nature of the phase transition would lead to different hydrodynamic responses.

## Method

The authors performed full 3+1 dimensional ideal hydrodynamic simulations using the NEXSPheRIO code with event-by-event fluctuating initial conditions from the NeXuS event generator, employing three different equations of state: a lattice QCD parameterization (LQCD) with zero baryon chemical potential, a smooth crossover EoS with a critical end point (CEP) that includes finite baryon chemical potential, and an EoS with a first-order phase transition and strangeness neutrality (FOS). The same initial conditions and freeze-out scenario were used for all three EoS, and the resulting particle spectra, harmonic flow coefficients v2, v3, and v4, and two-pion interferometry radii were calculated and compared to RHIC data at 130 GeV and 200 GeV for various centrality windows.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperatures for Au+Au at 130 GeV range from 128 MeV (FOS, 0-5% centrality) to 149 MeV (LQCD, 40-60% centrality); for 200 GeV they range from 128 MeV (FOS, 0-6% centrality) to 149 MeV (LQCD, 45-55% centrality)
- The critical chemical potential used in the CEP EoS is mu_c = 0.4 GeV
- The deconfinement transition for LQCD occurs in the temperature region 170 MeV < T < 220 MeV
- The transition temperature from lattice QCD is cited as T_c = 154(9) MeV (HotQCD Collaboration) and T_c = 151(3)(3) MeV (Wuppertal-Budapest Collaboration)
- The initial time for hydrodynamic evolution is tau_0 = 1.0 in hyperbolic coordinates
- Nearly 8000 NeXuS events were used for each centrality window, with 200 events used for particle spectra and two-pion interferometry calculations
- For flow coefficient calculations, 1000 events were used for 0-10% centrality and 8000 events for 20-30% centrality, with the Monte Carlo generator invoked 500 times for each initial condition

## Context

This work extends previous hydrodynamic studies by comparing three distinct equations of state (LQCD from Huovinen and Petreczky (2009), CEP from Hama et al. (2005), and FOS from Socolowski et al.) within the same event-by-event 3D hydrodynamic framework (NEXSPheRIO), covering a broader range of collision energies and centrality windows than earlier studies. The finding that collective flow parameters are insensitive to the EoS choice contrasts with expectations from earlier work by Huovinen (2005) and others, and suggests that future studies should focus on observables more sensitive to the EoS, such as femtoscopic radii, or incorporate viscous effects to better discriminate between different equations of state.
