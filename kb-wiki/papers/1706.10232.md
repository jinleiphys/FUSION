# Relativistic Three-Dimensional Lippmann-Schwinger Cross Sections for Space Radiation Applications

> Radiation transport codes require accurate nuclear cross sections to compute particle fluences inside shielding materials. The Tripathi semi-empirical reaction cross section, which includes over 60 parameters tuned to nucleon-nucleus (NA) and nucleus-nucleus (AA) data, has been used in many of the world's best-known transport codes. Although this parameterization fits well to reaction cross section data, the predictive capability of any parameterization is questionable when it is used beyond the range of the data to which it was tuned. Using uncertainty analysis, it is shown that a relativistic Three-Dimensional Lippmann-Schwinger (LS3D) equation model based on Multiple Scattering Theory (MST) that uses 5 parameterizations---3 fundamental parameterizations to nucleon-nucleon (NN) data and 2 nuclear charge density parameterizations---predicts NA and AA reaction cross sections as well as the Tripathi cross section parameterization for reactions in which the kinetic energy of the projectile in the laboratory frame ($T_{\rm Lab}$) is greater than 220 MeV/n. The relativistic LS3D model has the additional advantage of being able to predict highly accurate total and elastic cross sections. Consequently, it is recommended that the relativistic LS3D model be used for space radiation applications in which $T_{\rm Lab}>$ 220 MeV/n.

## Key claim

The relativistic Three-Dimensional Lippmann-Schwinger (LS3D) model predicts nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus reaction cross sections as well as the Tripathi semi-empirical parameterization for projectile kinetic energies above 220 MeV/n, while also providing accurate total and elastic cross sections that the Tripathi model cannot produce. This conclusion is conditional on using a specific proton charge density parameterization (a Lorentzian form rather than a Gaussian) and on excluding reactions below 220 MeV/n where medium effects are needed.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic three-dimensional Lippmann-Schwinger equation in momentum space using Multiple Scattering Theory, with the optical potential constructed from three nucleon-nucleon parameterizations and two nuclear charge density parameterizations (Harmonic-Well for A less than or equal to 16, two-parameter Fermi for heavier nuclei). They implemented a new proton charge density correction of the form 1/[1 + (q^2 a_0^2)]^2 for heavy ions, evaluated entirely in momentum-space representation, rather than the previous Gauss-Hermite quadrature approach in position space. Uncertainty quantification metrics (absolute uncertainty and relative uncertainty) were used to compare model predictions against experimental data across energy bins from 220 MeV/n to above 5 GeV/n.

## Key numbers

- The relativistic LS3D model predictions are now roughly 50 mb closer to experimental measurements compared to previous results from Werneth (2017).
- The proton charge density parameter used was a_0 = 1.0/18.77 fm^2, taken from Wolfe (1983) and Picklesimer (1984).
- The model is recommended for space radiation applications with projectile kinetic energy in the laboratory frame (T_Lab) greater than 220 MeV/n.
- The energy range considered extends up to 100 GeV/n for nuclei with masses up to Nickel.
- The Tripathi parameterization uses over 60 parameters tuned to nucleon-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus data.

## Context

This work extends the validation study of Werneth (2017), which compared Eikonal, partial wave, and LS3D models against the Tripathi parameterization (Tripathi, 1999) and recommended using Tripathi for all reaction cross sections. The present analysis reverses that recommendation by showing that with an improved proton charge density treatment, the relativistic LS3D model matches Tripathi's reaction cross section accuracy while also providing total and elastic cross sections. This enables a unified theoretical framework for space radiation transport codes such as Geant4, PHITS, and the NASA HZETRN code, replacing the semi-empirical parameterization with a physically motivated model for energies above 220 MeV/n.
