# Refined theoretical values of field and mass isotope shifts in thallium to extract charge radii of Tl isotopes

> Electronic factors for the field and mass isotope shifts in the $6p\ ^{2}P_{3/2} \to 7s\ ^{2}S_{1/2}$ (535 nm), $6p\ ^{2}P_{1/2} \to 6d\ ^{2}D_{3/2}$ (277 nm) and $6p\ ^{2}P_{1/2} \to 7s\ ^{2}S_{1/2}$ (378~nm) transitions in the neutral thallium were calculated within the high-order relativistic coupled cluster approach. These factors were used to reinterpret previous experimental isotope shift measurements in terms of charge radii of a wide range of Tl isotopes. Good agreement between theoretical and experimental King-plot parameters was found for the $6p\ ^{2}P_{3/2} \to 7s\ ^{2}S_{1/2}$ and $6p\ ^{2}P_{1/2} \to 6d\ ^{2}D_{3/2}$ transitions. It was shown that the value of the specific mass shift factor for the $6p\ ^{2}P_{3/2} \to 7s\ ^{2}S_{1/2}$ transition is not negligible compared to the value of normal mass shift in contrary to what had been suggested previously. Theoretical uncertainties in mean square charge radii were estimated. They were substantially reduced compared to the previously ascribed ones and amounted to less than 2.6%. The achieved accuracy paves the way to a more reliable comparison of the charge radii trends in the lead region.

## Key claim

The authors calculate refined electronic factors for field and mass isotope shifts in three neutral thallium transitions using high-order relativistic coupled cluster theory, and show that the specific mass shift factor for the 535 nm transition is not negligible compared to the normal mass shift, contrary to previous assumptions. The theoretical uncertainties in mean square charge radii are reduced to less than 2.6%, substantially smaller than the previously ascribed uncertainties.

## Method

The authors performed relativistic coupled cluster calculations including single, double, triple, and perturbative quadruple cluster amplitudes [CCSD(T), CCSDT, CCSDT(Q)] within the Dirac-Coulomb Hamiltonian for neutral thallium. They computed field shift constants (F), normal mass shift constants (k_NMS), and specific mass shift constants (k_SMS) for three transitions: 6p 2P3/2 → 7s 2S1/2 (535 nm), 6p 2P1/2 → 6d 2D3/2 (277 nm), and 6p 2P1/2 → 7s 2S1/2 (378 nm). Corrections were applied for higher-order correlation effects, basis set incompleteness, Gaunt and Breit inter-electron interactions, nuclear charge distribution model, and quantum electrodynamics effects. The calculated factors were then used to reinterpret previous experimental isotope shift measurements for Tl isotopes with mass numbers 179–208.

## Key numbers

- Field shift constant for 535 nm transition: F = 16.15(32) GHz/fm²
- Field shift constant for 277 nm transition: F = 9.50(44) GHz/fm²
- Field shift constant for 378 nm transition: F = 15.22(30) GHz/fm²
- Total mass shift constant for 535 nm transition: k_MS = -135(36) GHz·u
- Total mass shift constant for 277 nm transition: k_MS = -575(71) GHz·u
- Total mass shift constant for 378 nm transition: k_MS = -275(54) GHz·u
- Theoretical ratio F_277nm/F_535nm = 0.588(29), in agreement with experimental King-plot value 0.577(9)
- Theoretical uncertainties in mean square charge radii are less than 2.6%
- For 203,205Tl, the mass shift contributions to isotope shift are -6.5 MHz (535 nm), -27.5 MHz (277 nm), and -13.2 MHz (378 nm)

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical efforts on thallium isotope shifts, where field shift factors from different approaches ranged between 15.65 and 20.75 GHz/fm² (a ~30% indeterminacy), and mass shift factors were often estimated qualitatively with ~100% uncertainty. The authors improve upon the single-configuration Dirac-Fock calculations of Hermann (1993) and Menges (1992), the many-body perturbation theory of Martensson-Pendrill (1991), and earlier coupled cluster calculations of Martensson-Pendrill (1995). The refined charge radii enable more reliable comparison of charge radii trends in the lead region, where there is striking diversity in the patterns of charge radii isotopic dependence across thallium, lead, and bismuth chains. The achieved accuracy calls for state-of-the-art treatment of nuclear-structure effects such as nuclear deformation and polarization, which were not considered here.
