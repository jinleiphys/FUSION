# Tetrahedral Symmetry in Ground- and Low-Lying States of Exotic A ~ 110 Nuclei

> Recent theoretical calculations predict a possible existence of nuclei with tetrahedral symmetry: more precisely, the mean-field hamiltonians of such nuclei are symmetric with respect to double point-group Td. In this paper, we focus on the neutron-rich Zirconium isotopes as an example and present realistic mean-field calculations which predict tetrahedral ground-state configurations in 108,110Zr and low-lying excited states of tetrahedral symmetry in a number of N > 66 isotopes. The motivations for focusing on these nuclei, as well as a discussion of the possible experimental signatures of tetrahedral symmetry are also presented.

## Key claim

Realistic mean-field calculations predict that the neutron-rich isotopes \(^{108,110}\)Zr may have tetrahedral ground-state configurations, and that low-lying excited states of tetrahedral symmetry exist in a number of Zr isotopes with \(N \leq 66\); however, the self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov (HFB) calculations with the SLy4 force predict a tetrahedral ground state only in \(^{110}\)Zr and \(^{112}\)Zr, whereas the macroscopic-microscopic (Strutinsky) method finds the tetrahedral minimum to be excited relative to a prolate ground state in those same nuclei.

## Method

The authors performed both macroscopic-microscopic (Strutinsky) calculations using a Woods-Saxon mean-field with universal parameters and self-consistent HFB calculations using the SLy4 Skyrme force and the HFODD code. The deformation space included all multipoles up to \(\lambda = 12\), but higher-order terms were found negligible; the dynamical minimization used 11 deformation parameters (octupole \(\alpha_{3\mu}\), hexadecapole \(\alpha_{4\mu}\), and the \(\gamma\) angle). Pairing correlations were treated with the particle-number projection (PNP) technique before variation.

## Key numbers

- The tetrahedral shell gap at \(Z=40\) is larger by \(\sim 3\) MeV than the spherical shell gap at \(Z=40\).
- The tetrahedral minimum in \(^{110}\)Zr appears at a deformation \(\alpha_{32} \sim 0.14\).
- Including the tetrahedral degree of freedom lowers the minimum at \(\beta_2 = 0\) in \(^{110}\)Zr by more than 1 MeV compared to the spherical minimum.
- The barrier between the tetrahedral minimum and the oblate minimum in \(^{110}\)Zr amounts to up to \(\sim 1.5\) MeV.
- HFB energies relative to the tetrahedral minimum (in MeV): for \(^{110}\)Zr, the spherical minimum is at +0.43, the oblate at +0.07, and the prolate at +0.27; for \(^{112}\)Zr, the spherical is at +0.03, oblate at +0.30, and prolate at +1.01.
- The zero-point quadrupole deformation in \(^{110}\)Zr is estimated to be \(\sim 0.05\).
- The zero-point motion mechanism introduces deviations in level degeneracy of the order of 2 keV.

## Context

This work extends the earlier prediction by Dudek (2002) that tetrahedral and octahedral symmetries can produce shell gaps comparable to spherical ones, and it provides realistic calculations for a specific mass region (\(A \sim 110\)) where experimental study is feasible. The neutron-rich Zr isotopes up to \(^{110}\)Zr have been synthesised via projectile fission at GSI (Becker, 1994), and the paper suggests that these nuclei are viable for future isomer spectroscopy or beta-delayed gamma-ray spectroscopy following projectile fission. The predicted characteristic E2 decay pattern (competition between stretched and non-stretched transitions, with no \(C_i \to C_i\) transitions) offers a clear experimental signature to distinguish tetrahedral symmetry from axial or triaxial quadrupole rotors.
