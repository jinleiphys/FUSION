# Some Considerations concerning the Fluctuation of the Ratios of two Observables

> We discuss several possible caveats which arise with the interpretation of measurements of fluctuations in heavy ion collisions. We especially focus on the ratios of particle yields, which has been advocated as a possible signature of a critical point in the QCD phase diagram. We conclude that current experimental observables are not well-defined and are without a proper quantitative meaning.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the commonly used measure of dynamical ratio fluctuations, \(\sigma^2_{dyn}\), is not well-defined and lacks proper quantitative meaning because it depends strongly on arbitrary experimental choices (centrality selection and particle acceptance) and because the mixed-event background subtraction does not reliably remove trivial correlations; the authors conclude that a non-monotonic behavior of such an observable cannot be straightforwardly taken as evidence for a critical point in the QCD phase diagram.

## Method

The authors analyze the statistical properties of event-by-event distributions of particle multiplicities in heavy ion collisions, focusing on the measure \(\sigma^2_{dyn} = \sigma^2_{data} - \sigma^2_{mix}\) for ratio fluctuations. They illustrate the dependence of this measure on centrality class construction and detector acceptance using a Bi-variate Normal Distribution (BND) model, showing that perfectly correlated data can yield negative \(\sigma^2_{dyn}\) and that uncorrelated data can produce a correlated mixed-event background. They also reference HSD transport simulations to demonstrate how different centrality triggers (number of wounded nucleons vs. charged particle multiplicity) create different sub-samples even for the same centrality class interval.

## Key numbers

* The paper uses a BND example with mean values \(\langle N_1\rangle_{data} = \langle N_2\rangle_{data} = 50\) and co-variance \(\langle \Delta N_1\Delta N_2 \rangle_{data} = 0\) to show that the mixed-event background develops non-zero co-variance \(\langle \Delta N_1\Delta N_2 \rangle_{mix} \neq 0\) depending on the variances.
* The centrality class interval of \(20-22\%\) is used in Fig. 1 to illustrate that two different trigger conditions (number of wounded nucleons vs. charged particle multiplicity) produce distinct sub-samples.
* The NA49 analysis used \(3.5\%\) most central collisions selected via projectile spectator nucleon signal, while STAR used \(5\%\) most central collisions selected via reference charged hadron multiplicity distribution.

## Context

This paper extends the critical discussion of fluctuation measures in heavy ion collisions, following the work of Stephanov (2009), Koch (2009), and Konchakovski (2010), who revived interest in event-by-event fluctuations as a signature of the QCD critical point. The authors directly challenge the interpretation of results from the STAR collaboration (Abelev 2009) and NA49 collaboration (Alt 2008), arguing that the apparent numerical similarity between their measurements of dynamical ratio fluctuations is misleading due to different experimental acceptances and centrality selection methods. The paper does not propose an improved observable but instead calls for systematic studies of biases introduced by specific experimental setups, referencing the PhD thesis of Konchakovski (2010) for further examples.
