# Simulating stochastic fluid dynamics

> We present simulations of the real time dynamics of a fluid in the vicinity of a critical endpoint in the phase diagram. The relevant hydrodynamic theory is known as model H, and it is expected to describe the long-distance dynamics of QCD matter in the vicinity of a possible critical endpoint in the QCD phase diagram.

## Key claim

The authors present the first numerical simulations of model H, the stochastic hydrodynamic theory describing the long-distance dynamics of QCD matter near a possible critical endpoint in the phase diagram, but only for a non-expanding fluid in a fixed volume, not yet for an expanding relativistic fluid relevant to heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors discretize the stochastic partial differential equations of model H on a spatial lattice and evolve them in real time using a Metropolis algorithm that combines dissipative and stochastic updates into a single conserving update step for the order parameter field and momentum density. The advection terms are discretized using skew-symmetrized derivatives developed for incompressible turbulence simulations. The bare parameters (mass squared, coupling, external field) are tuned to locate the critical point, and the physical shear viscosity is extracted from simulations at various bare viscosities.

## Key numbers

* Dynamical critical exponent z = 3.01 measured at the critical point in a three-dimensional fluid, consistent with epsilon-expansion predictions and clearly different from mean-field behavior.
* The renormalization of shear viscosity in a non-critical fluid is inversely proportional to the bare value of eta, an effect dominated by self-advection of the momentum density (the "stickiness of sound").
* Crossover from model B like scaling (z approximately 4) to model H scaling (z approximately 3) observed as the physical viscosity is reduced in finite systems.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of model H originally formulated by Hohenberg and Halperin (1977) and its application to QCD critical dynamics proposed by Son and Stephanov (2004). It provides the first numerical implementation of this theory, overcoming challenges of ultraviolet divergences and fluctuation-dissipation relation compatibility that had prevented previous simulations. The results enable future work on embedding this framework in an expanding relativistic fluid to model realistic heavy-ion collision systems, following the mapping of Ising model parameters to the QCD phase diagram described by Parotto et al. (2018).
