# Poisson baseline of net-charge fluctuations in the relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Taking doubly charged particles, positive-negative charge pair production and the effects of volume fluctuations into account, the Poisson baseline of the fluctuations of net-charge is studied. Within the Poisson baseline, the cumulants of net-charge are derived. Comparing to the Skellam baseline of net-charge, we infer that doubly charged particles broaden the distributions of net-charge, while positive-negative charge pairs narrow the distributions. Using the ratios of doubly charged particles and positive-negative charge pairs from neutral resonance decays to the total positive charges from THERMINATOR 2, the first four orders of moments and the corresponding moment products are calculated in the Poisson baseline for Au + Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV at RHIC/STAR. We find that the standard deviation is mainly influenced by the resonance decay, while the third and fourth order moments and corresponding moment products are mainly modified and fit the data of RHIC/STAR much better after including the effects of volume fluctuations.

# Poisson baseline of net-charge fluctuations in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
The Poisson baseline for net-charge fluctuations, which accounts for doubly charged particles and positive-negative charge pairs from resonance decays, provides a better description of RHIC/STAR data than the standard Skellam baseline, but only after including volume fluctuations does it reproduce the measured third and fourth order moments; the standard deviation remains primarily sensitive to resonance decay effects rather than volume fluctuations.

## Method
The authors derived analytic expressions for cumulants of net-charge distributions under a Poisson baseline where the total positive and negative charges are decomposed into three independent Poisson-distributed components: doubly charged particles, singly charged particles from positive-negative pair production (with equal numbers in each event), and remaining singly charged particles. Using THERMINATOR 2 simulations of Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, they extracted the ratios of doubly charged particles (r_2+ ≈ 1.06%, r_2- ≈ 0.73%) and pair-produced particles (r_p ≈ 19.86%) relative to total positive charges. These ratios were then used to compute cumulants and moment products for the Poisson baseline, both with and without incorporating volume fluctuations via a Glauber model approach where the second through fourth reduced cumulants of volume were approximated as v_2=1, v_3=2, v_4=6 for non-central collisions.

## Key numbers
- Ratio of doubly positive charged particles to total positive charges: r_2+ ≈ 1.06% across all centralities, with less than 0.02% variation
- Ratio of doubly negative charged particles to total positive charges: r_2- ≈ 0.73% across all centralities, with less than 0.01% variation except for 60%-70% centrality
- Ratio of singly charged particles from pair production to total positive charges: r_p ≈ 19.86% across all centralities
- Mean total positive charge in 0%-5% centrality: ⟨N_+⟩ = 256.859
- Mean total positive charge in 60%-70% centrality: ⟨N_+⟩ = 9.1492
- The Poisson baseline including volume fluctuations yields skewness and kurtosis values that are "very close to the data" from RHIC/STAR at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, while the Skellam baseline gives systematically smaller values

## Context
This work extends the standard Skellam baseline approach used by the STAR Collaboration (Adamczyk et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 113, 092301, 2014) for net-charge fluctuations by incorporating resonance decay effects that were previously neglected. It builds on earlier work by Pan et al. (Phys. Rev. C 89, 014904, 2014) on cumulants of net-charge and by Skokov et al. (Phys. Rev. C 88, 034911, 2013) on volume fluctuation effects. The results enable more precise quantification of non-critical background contributions to net-charge cumulants, which is necessary before interpreting deviations as signatures of QCD critical phenomena. The authors note that significant discrepancies remain in the σ²/M moment product, suggesting additional correlations beyond those considered here.
