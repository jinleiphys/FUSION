# Bose-Einstein condensation of diquark molecules in three-flavor quark matter

> We study the phase diagram of strongly interacting matter with three quark flavors at low and intermediate densities and non-zero temperatures in the framework of an NJL-type model with four-point interactions. At large densities, when the interactions are weak due to asymptotic freedom, quarks form loosely bound Cooper pairs. However, when the density decreases, interactions become stronger and quark Cooper pairs transform smoothly into tightly bound diquark molecules. We find that such molecules are stable at low density and temperature and that they dissociate above a temperature $T_{\rm diss}$ of the order of the chiral phase transition temperature $T_c \sim 170$ MeV. We also explore the conditions under which these molecules undergo Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC). We find that BEC is only possible if we increase the attractive interaction in the diquark channel to (probably unrealistically) large values.

## Key claim

The paper finds that Bose-Einstein condensation (BEC) of diquark molecules in three-flavor quark matter is only possible if the attractive interaction in the diquark channel is increased to values that are "probably unrealistically large," specifically requiring a diquark coupling strength ratio \(G_D/G_S\) greater than approximately 1.1, whereas the canonical value from a Fierz transformation is \(G_D/G_S = 0.75\).

## Method

The authors employed an NJL-type model with four-point interactions for three quark flavors (u, d, s) in the mean-field approximation, treating the diquark coupling strength \(G_D\) as a free parameter. They computed the thermodynamic potential and solved the gap equations to determine the phase diagram in the temperature-chemical potential (\(T\)-\(\mu\)) plane, and analyzed the poles of the diquark propagator in the random phase approximation to identify bound diquark states and their dissociation temperatures. The condition \(\mu < \bar{M}_c\) (where \(\bar{M}_c\) is the average constituent quark mass for the diquark's constituent flavors) was used to distinguish the BEC regime from the BCS regime.

## Key numbers

- For the canonical diquark coupling \(G_D/G_S = 0.75\) (from Fierz transformation), no BEC region exists inside the color-superconducting phases.
- For \(G_D/G_S = 1.1\), BEC appears inside the 2SC phase in a region where \(\mu < \bar{M}_3\).
- The dissociation temperature \(T_{\rm diss}\) at \(\mu = 0\) for \(G_D/G_S = 1.1\) is comparable to or larger than the lattice QCD critical temperature \(T_{c, \rm LQCD} \simeq 170\) MeV.
- For \(G_D/G_S = 1.5\), the BEC region becomes "rather wide" and the critical temperatures for the 2SC and CFL phases increase substantially; further increase would make even the vacuum at \(T = \mu = 0\) a BEC of diquark molecules.

## Context

This work extends the study of color superconductivity from the weak-coupling BCS regime at high densities (where Cooper pairs are loosely bound, as in the analyses by Son (1998), Schafer (1999), Pisarski (1999), and Hong (1999)) to the strong-coupling regime at lower densities where tightly bound diquark molecules form. The results agree with the findings of Abuki (2006) regarding the possibility of diquark BEC for large coupling strengths, but the authors conclude that the required coupling values are likely unrealistic for applications to compact stellar objects or heavy-ion collisions. The paper enables future studies of the crossover between BCS and BEC regimes in QCD-like models and raises questions about whether diquark molecules could exist in the quark-gluon plasma phase.
