# Large N limit of the exactly solvable BCS model: analytics versus numerics

> We have studied the numerical solutions of Richardson equations of the BCS model in the limit of large number of energy levels at half-filling, and compare them with the analytic results derived by Gaudin and Richardson, which in turn leads to the standard BCS solution. We focus on the location and density of the roots, the eigenvalues of the conserved quantities, and the scaling properties of the total energy for the equally spaced and the two-level models.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the numerical solutions of Richardson's equations for the BCS model converge to the analytic continuum-limit results derived by Gaudin and Richardson in the limit of a large number of energy levels \(N\), but only under the assumption that the roots organize into a single arc; for the equally spaced model this assumption fails at weak coupling, where only a fraction of roots form complex conjugate pairs.

## Method

The authors numerically solved Richardson's coupled algebraic equations (Eq. 6) for the ground state of the reduced BCS model at half-filling, for two specific level configurations: a two-level model with degeneracy \(N/2\) per level, and an equally spaced model with \(N\) levels uniformly distributed in \([-\omega, \omega]\). They compared the numerical root distributions, the location of the outermost complex root \(E_{\text{max}}\), and the total energy against the analytic expressions derived from Gaudin's electrostatic continuum limit, which yields the BCS gap and chemical potential equations (Eqs. 43-45).

## Key numbers

* Two-level model: the gap parameter \(\Delta = \sqrt{g^2 - \varepsilon_1^2}\) for \(g \geq \varepsilon_1\) and \(\Delta = 0\) for \(g \leq \varepsilon_1\), where \(g = G N\) and \(\varepsilon_1\) is the level energy (units of \(\varepsilon_1\)).
* Two-level model: the real part of the outermost root \(\varepsilon_0 = 0\) for \(g \geq \varepsilon_1\) and \(\varepsilon_0 = -\varepsilon_1 \sqrt{1 - g/\varepsilon_1}\) for \(g \leq \varepsilon_1\).
* Two-level model: the scaling exponent \(\theta\) for the approach of \(E_{\text{max}}\) to its continuum limit is \(2/3\) for \(g = 1.5\,\varepsilon_1\) and \(1\) for \(g = 1.0\,\varepsilon_1\) and \(g = 0.5\,\varepsilon_1\).
* Equally spaced model: the gap \(\Delta = \omega / \sinh(1/g)\) with dimensionless coupling \(g = G N / \omega\).
* Equally spaced model: the condensation energy in the weak-coupling limit is \(E_C \sim -\Delta_{\text{BCS}}^2 / (2d)\), where \(d = \omega/N\) is the single-particle level spacing and \(\Delta_{\text{BCS}} = \Delta/2\).
* Equally spaced model: the arc \(\Gamma\) does not touch the energy interval \([-\omega, \omega]\) for \(g > g_0 = 1.13459\) (where \(\sinh(1/g_0) = 1\)).

## Context

This work extends the analytic continuum-limit treatment of the exactly solvable BCS model originally developed by Gaudin (1995) and Richardson (1960s-70s), and provides the first systematic numerical verification of those analytic predictions for large \(N\). It builds on the integrability proof of Cambiaggio, Rivas, and Saraceno (1997) and the electrostatic analogy used by Gaudin and Richardson. The results enable precise benchmarking of numerical solutions of Richardson's equations for mesoscopic systems such as ultrasmall metallic grains and nuclei, and clarify the conditions under which the standard BCS gap equation emerges from the exact solution.
