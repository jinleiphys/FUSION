# Nuclear Radii Calculations in Various Theoretical Approaches for Nucleus-Nucleus Interactions

> The information about sizes and nuclear density distributions in unstable (radioactive) nuclei is usually extracted from the data on interaction of radioactive nuclear beams with a nuclear target. We show that in the case of nucleus-nucleus collisions the values of the parameters depend rather strongly on the considered theoretical approach and on the assumption about the parametrization of the nuclear density distribution. The obtained values of root-mean-square radii (R_{rms}) for stable nuclei with atomic weights A = 12-40 vary by approximately 0.1 fm when calculated in the optical approximation, in the rigid target approximation, and using the exact expression of Glauber Theory. We present several examples of R_{rms} radii calculations using these three theoretical approaches and compare these results with the data obtained from electron-nucleus scattering.

# Nuclear Radii Calculations in Various Theoretical Approaches for Nucleus-Nucleus Interactions

## Key claim
The root-mean-square radii (R_rms) extracted from nucleus-nucleus interaction cross sections for stable nuclei with atomic weights A = 12–40 vary by approximately 0.1 fm depending on whether the optical approximation, rigid target approximation, or exact Glauber Theory expression is used, and also depend on the assumed parametrization of the nuclear density distribution (Woods-Saxon vs. harmonic oscillator). The Glauber Theory results agree better with electron-nucleus scattering data than the other approximations.

## Method
The authors calculated reaction cross sections for collisions of stable projectile nuclei (A = 12–40) with a carbon target at energies of 800–1000 MeV per nucleon, using three theoretical approaches: the optical approximation, the rigid target approximation, and the exact Glauber Theory evaluated via Monte Carlo simulation with the Metropolis algorithm. Nuclear matter densities were parametrized either by a harmonic oscillator (HO) distribution or a Woods-Saxon distribution with fixed diffuseness parameter a = 0.54 fm. The R_rms values were extracted by fitting the calculated reaction cross sections to experimental interaction cross section data from Ozawa et al. (2001) and Ozawa et al. (2002).

## Key numbers
- For ¹²C: R_rms = 2.31 ± 0.02 fm (HO, optical, no NN range) vs. 2.49 ± 0.01 fm (Glauber Theory, Woods-Saxon), a difference of 0.18 fm.
- For ¹⁶O: R_rms = 2.54 ± 0.02 fm (HO, optical, no NN range) vs. 2.69 ± 0.02 fm (Glauber Theory, Woods-Saxon).
- For ⁴⁰Ar: R_rms = 3.282 ± 0.036 fm (HO, optical, no NN range) vs. 3.30 ± 0.03 fm (Glauber Theory, Woods-Saxon).
- Experimental interaction cross section for ¹²C–¹²C: 856 ± 9 mb at 790 MeV per nucleon and 853 ± 6 mb at 950 MeV per nucleon (Ozawa et al., 2001).
- Total NN cross section used: σ = 43 mb; slope parameter β = 6 GeV⁻².
- The difference between R_rms values from different approximations is approximately 0.1 fm for stable nuclei with A = 12–40.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Ozawa et al. (2001) and Ozawa et al. (2002) on extracting nuclear radii from interaction cross sections of radioactive beams, and by Alkhazov et al. (2002) and Garrido et al. (2001) for ⁶He and ¹¹Li scattering on ¹²C. It systematically compares three theoretical approximations (optical, rigid target, exact Glauber) for stable nuclei, demonstrating that the choice of approximation introduces systematic uncertainties of ~0.1 fm in extracted R_rms values. The results enable more reliable interpretation of radii extracted from radioactive beam experiments by quantifying the model-dependent uncertainties. The Glauber Theory results are compared to electron-nucleus scattering data from De Vries et al. (1987), showing better agreement than the other approximations, especially for nuclei with A > 20.
