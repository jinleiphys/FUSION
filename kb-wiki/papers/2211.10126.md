# Three-particle Lellouch-Lüscher formalism in moving frames

> A manifestly relativistic-invariant Lellouch-Lüscher formalism for the three-particle decays is proposed. Similarly to ref.[1], the formalism is based on the use of the non-relativistic effective Lagrangians. Manifest Lorentz invariance is guaranteed, as in ref.[2], by choosing the quantization axis along the total four-momentum of the three-particle system. A systematic inclusion of the higher-order derivative couplings, as well as higher partial waves is addressed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a manifestly relativistic-invariant Lellouch-Lüscher formalism for three-particle decays, extending the proof-of-principle derivation of Müller et al. (2020) to include higher-order derivative couplings and higher partial waves, but only for decays into three identical particles with no two-to-three transitions.

## Method

The authors merge the non-relativistic effective field theory (NREFT) derivation of the Lellouch-Lüscher framework with a manifestly invariant three-particle setting by choosing the quantization axis along the total four-momentum of the three-particle system. They construct a particle-dimer Lagrangian with tensor fields for the dimer, systematically include higher partial waves through angular momentum projections, and derive the Faddeev equation in both infinite and finite volume. The finite-volume quantization condition is obtained as det 𝒜 = 0, where 𝒜 is a matrix in spectator momenta and partial-wave indices.

## Context

This work extends the proof-of-principle derivation by Müller et al. (2020) which only treated leading order in the EFT expansion and S-wave contributions, and provides a comparison with the RFT-based derivation by Hansen et al. (2021). The formalism enables the extraction of three-body decay amplitudes from lattice QCD simulations using data from sectors with different total momenta, which was previously not possible without manifest Lorentz invariance. The approach builds on the Lorentz-invariant three-particle framework of Müller et al. (2021) and addresses the systematic inclusion of derivative couplings and higher partial waves needed for applications such as ω → 3π decay.
