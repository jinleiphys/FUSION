# Heavy hadronic molecules with pion exchange and quark core couplings: a guide for practitioners

> We discuss selected and important features of hadronic molecules as one of promising forms of exotic hadrons near thresholds. Using examples of $D \bar D^*$ systems such as $X(3872)$ and $Z_c$, emphasis is put on the roles of the one pion exchange interaction between them and their coupling to intrinsic quark states. Thus hadronic molecules emerge as admixtures of the dominant long-range hadron structure and short-range quark structure. For the pion exchange interaction, properties of the tensor force are analyzed in detail. More coupled channels supply more attractions, and heavier constituents suppress kinetic energies, providing more chances to form hadronic molecules of heavy hadrons. Throughout this article, we show details of basic ideas and methods.

## Key claim

Hadronic molecules near thresholds are admixtures of long-range hadron structure (dominated by one-pion exchange) and short-range quark structure (compact quark core), and the tensor force from pion exchange provides extra attraction that is essential for forming these loosely bound states.

## Method

The authors derive the one-pion exchange potential (OPEP) for heavy meson systems using heavy quark spin symmetry and chiral symmetry, with coupling constants determined from the quark model and from experimental decay widths of \(D^* \to D\pi\) and \(K^* \to K\pi\). They then apply this framework to the \(D\bar D^*\) system, specifically for \(X(3872)\) and \(Z_c\), incorporating both the long-range pion exchange and short-range \(s\)-channel couplings to compact \(c\bar c\) quark states.

## Context

This work extends the hadronic molecule concept originally proposed for \(\Lambda(1405)\) as a \(\bar K N\) molecule by Dalitz (1959) and later applied to charmonium systems after the discovery of \(J/\psi\) by De Rujula (1976). It provides a practical guide for analyzing heavy hadronic molecules using the one-pion exchange interaction combined with quark core couplings, enabling future studies of the compositeness of states like \(X(3872)\) and the role of tensor forces in heavy meson systems. The authors emphasize that more coupled channels supply more attraction and heavier constituents suppress kinetic energies, increasing the chance of forming hadronic molecules.
