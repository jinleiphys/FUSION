# Signals for fluctuating constituent numbers in small systems

> We propose an extension of the initial condition model TRENTo for sampling the number of partons inside the nucleons that participate in a heavy-ion collision. This sampling method is based on parton distribution functions (PDFs) and therefore has a natural dependence on the momentum transferred in the collision and the scale being probed during the collision. We examine the resulting distributions and their dependence on the momentum transfer. Additionally, we explore the sensitivity of different observables on the number of partons using the TRENTo framework and the estimators available therein for final-state observables.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a novel method for sampling a fluctuating number of partons inside each nucleon in the TRENTo initial condition model, based on parton distribution functions (PDFs). The authors find that the strongest signals of this fluctuating constituent number appear in small collision systems (such as oxygen or neon) and in the ratio of odd harmonic eccentricities, but they note that the actual momentum transfer \(Q^2\) in a heavy-ion collision is inaccessible, so the method relies on a parametric dependence on this scale.

## Method

The authors extend the TRENTo initial condition model by replacing its fixed number of sub-nucleonic hotspots with a fluctuating number of partons sampled from PDFs. The algorithm first draws the momenta of three valence quarks from their respective PDFs, then repeatedly samples sea quarks, anti-quarks, and gluons (with species determined randomly based on \(Q^2\)) until the total momentum sum exceeds unity; the minimal momentum fraction \(x_{\text{min}} = Q^2/s\) provides a kinematic cutoff that ensures a finite maximum parton number. To gauge sensitivity to the parton number, the authors use initial-state estimators (entropy, energy, eccentricities) for fixed values of \(m\) (the number of partons) rather than performing full hydrodynamic simulations.

## Key numbers

* The minimal momentum fraction for top LHC energies is \(x_{\text{min}} \approx 10^{-6}\), providing a kinematic cutoff that ensures a finite maximal parton number \(m_{\text{max}} = 1/x_{\text{min}}\).
* The average number of partons in each nucleon as a function of \(Q^2\) initially grows but then decreases for large \(Q^2\); the standard deviation of the parton number distribution decreases with increasing \(Q^2\).
* For a lead nucleus in a full head-on collision, the total number of average partons fluctuates between approximately 8300 and 12500, depending on the momentum transfer.
* In the TRENTo model calibrated to data, the fixed number of sub-nucleonic degrees of freedom was found to be between four and five (rounded from the maximum likelihood value of the posterior).

## Context

This work extends the TRENTo initial condition model (Moreland 2018, Soeder 2023) by replacing its fixed number of sub-nucleonic hotspots with a fluctuating number based on PDFs, motivated by deep inelastic scattering results showing that protons and neutrons have a rich substructure with event-by-event fluctuations. The authors build on prior Bayesian calibration studies (Nijs 2020, Nijs 2021) that found a fixed constituent number between four and five with large uncertainties, and they note that stronger constraints can arise in smaller collision systems (Huang 2025). The proposed ratio of odd eccentricities (\(\varepsilon_3/\varepsilon_5\)) in oxygen-oxygen and neon-neon collisions could be used to constrain the parton number distribution in upcoming LHC light-ion runs.
