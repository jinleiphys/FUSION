# Boost-invariant superfluid flows

> We present some exact solutions to the ideal hydrodynamics of a relativistic superfluid with an almost-conformal equation of state. The solutions have stress tensors which are invariant under Lorentz boosts in one direction, and represent superfluid generalisations of the Bjorken and Gubser flows. We also study corrections to the flows in first-order hydrodynamics, arguing that dissipation is dominated by the shear viscosity. We present some simple numerical solutions for these viscous corrections. Finally, we estimate the size of corrections to the flows arising when the spontaneously broken $\mathrm{U}(1)$ symmetry responsible for superfluidity is only approximate, giving the corresponding Goldstone boson a small non-zero mass. We find that the massless solutions can still provide good approximations at sufficiently small spatial rapidities.

## Key claim

The paper presents exact boost-invariant solutions to ideal superfluid hydrodynamics with an almost-conformal equation of state, but the solutions require the local chemical potential to be zero unless the susceptibilities \(\chi\) and \(f^2\) have the same temperature dependence (i.e., \(k = k'\)), which is not generally true except in special cases such as chiral perturbation theory at low temperatures.

## Method

The authors construct superfluid generalizations of Bjorken and Gubser flows by imposing Lorentz boost invariance along one spatial direction and solving the ideal hydrodynamic equations with a model equation of state where the energy density is \(\varepsilon = 3n T^4 + \frac{1}{2} k T^2 \xi^2 + \frac{1}{2} f_\pi^2 \xi^2\) and the charge susceptibility is \(\chi = f_\pi^2 - k T^2\). For the Bjorken-type flow, they enforce translational invariance in the transverse plane and boost invariance along the beam axis, reducing all observables to functions of Milne time \(\tau\) only. For the Gubser-type flow, they replace transverse translational invariance with conformal covariance under a particular conformal Killing vector, following the method of Gubser (2010). They also study first-order dissipative corrections, focusing on shear viscosity while setting bulk viscosities to zero based on conformal symmetry arguments.

## Key numbers

- The late-time energy density for ideal superfluid Bjorken flow decays as \(\varepsilon(\tau) = \frac{3n}{\tau_0^{8/3} \tau^{4/3}} + \frac{f_\pi^2 \xi^2}{2 \tau^2} - \frac{k \xi^2}{2 \tau_0^{4/3} \tau^{8/3}} + \dots\), where the first term is the usual Bjorken flow and the \(\tau^{-2}\) term is the leading superfluid correction.
- For viscous Bjorken flow with shear viscosity \(\eta = \bar{\eta} T^3\), the late-time energy density becomes \(\varepsilon(\tau) = \frac{3n}{\tau_0^{8/3}\tau^{4/3}} + \frac{\tau_0^2 f_\pi^2 \xi^2 - 4\bar{\eta}}{2\tau_0^2 \tau^2} - \frac{n k \xi^2 - \bar{\eta}^2}{2n \tau_0^{4/3} \tau^{8/3}} + \dots\), showing that viscous and superfluid corrections appear at the same order \(\tau^{-2}\).
- At early times \(\tau \to 0\) with non-zero \(\xi\), the temperature grows linearly as \(T(\tau) \approx 4n \tau / (k \xi^2 \tau_0^2)\), while the energy density decreases as \(\varepsilon(\tau) \approx f_\pi^2 \xi^2/(2\tau^2) + 8n^2/(k \xi^2 \tau_0^2) + \dots\).
- The numerical solutions use parameters \(n = \pi^2/30\), \(k = 1/6\), \(\xi = 1/10\), and \(\tau_0 = 1/f_\pi\), motivated by O(4) sigma models relevant for QCD.

## Context

This work extends the boost-invariant Bjorken and Gubser flows, previously known for normal fluids (Gubser, 2010), to the case of relativistic superfluids with a spontaneously broken U(1) symmetry. The superfluid Bjorken flow generalizes earlier work by Lallouet (2002) which considered a different regime with non-zero chemical potential but zero superfluid velocity. The solutions provide analytic benchmarks for numerical codes simulating superfluid hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions, though the authors note that the restriction to temperatures much smaller than \(f_\pi\) limits direct application to the freezeout regime. The results also enable future studies of second-order dissipative effects and transverse anisotropy in superfluid flows.
