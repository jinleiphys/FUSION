# A dynamical model calculation to reconcile the nuclear fission lifetime from different measurement techniques

> The pre-scission particle multiplicities suggest a lifetime of 10 20 s for the nuclear fission to occur which is in contrast to the fission lifetime 10 18 s as predicted by atomic probe. This long standing ambiguity, arising due to the orders of magnitude differences among the fission lifetime measured from the nuclear and atomic probes, has been addressed within a dynamical model which includes the contributions from the nuclear shell effects. We show that, at lower excitation energies, these two probes decouples as the fissioning system survives for a long time without any particle evaporation. We also consider a wide range of reactions to study the impact of the excitation energy of compound nucleus on the fission dynamics in general. Our model predicts the average fission life time of superheavy nucleus 302 120, to be more than 10 18 s which is in reasonable agreement with the recent experiments.

# A dynamical model calculation to reconcile the nuclear fission lifetime from different measurement techniques

## Key claim

The authors show that the orders-of-magnitude discrepancy between fission lifetimes measured by nuclear probes (pre-scission particle multiplicities, ~10⁻²⁰ s) and atomic probes (crystal blocking, ~10⁻¹⁸ s) can be resolved by a dynamical model that includes nuclear shell effects, but only at low excitation energies where the fissioning system survives for a long time without particle evaporation. The decoupling between the two measurement techniques occurs specifically when the excitation energy falls below the particle emission threshold, between 67 MeV and 37 MeV for the ²²⁴Th system.

## Method

The authors performed stochastic Langevin equation simulations of fission dynamics for excited compound nuclei, following trajectories from ground-state deformation to scission with Monte-Carlo sampling of neutron, proton, alpha, and statistical gamma-ray evaporation at each time step. The driving force was the Helmholtz free energy calculated from a Yukawa-plus-exponential liquid-drop model with Strutinsky shell corrections from a two-centered Woods-Saxon mean field and BCS pairing, using Werner-Wheeler collective inertia and chaos-weighted wall friction. For each of approximately 10⁶ Langevin trajectories per macrostate, the authors recorded the average neutron evaporation time, the time of the last neutron evaporation, and the scission time, allowing comparison between the neutron-clock method and the actual fission time.

## Key numbers

- For ²²⁴Th at initial excitation energy E* = 37 MeV, the peak of the τ_f distribution lies at τ_f > 10⁻¹⁸ s, while τ_n and τ_nl distributions remain almost unaffected, showing decoupling between the two probes
- The decoupling of τ_f from τ_nl and τ_n appears somewhere between E* = 67 MeV and E* = 37 MeV for the ²²⁴Th system
- For the ²³⁸U+⁶⁴Ni reaction forming ³⁰²120, the average fission lifetime ⟨τ_f⟩ ≥ 10⁻¹⁸ s is possible only if the excitation energy E* ≤ 10 MeV with full shell correction
- With reduced energy dependence in shell correction (50% of the unaffected value), the attosecond time can be reached at a higher E* ~ 30 MeV for the superheavy system
- The average fission barrier for Z=120 isotopes is approximately 9 MeV in the authors' calculation
- Each Langevin trajectory was allowed a maximum dynamical time of 10⁻¹⁵ s in steps of 3 × 10⁻²⁵ s
- For the ¹⁹F+¹⁸¹Ta reaction, ⟨τ_f⟩ remains more than 10⁻¹⁷ s even at very large E*, with substantial contribution between 10⁻¹⁷ and 10⁻¹⁵ s

## Context

This work addresses a long-standing discrepancy between fission lifetimes measured by nuclear techniques (pre-scission particle multiplicities, as studied by Hinde (1992) and Lestone (1991, 1993)) and atomic techniques (crystal blocking, as measured by Morjean (1998, 2008) and Andersen (2007, 2008)). The authors extend previous combined dynamical and statistical model (CDSM) calculations by Frobrich (1998) and Goncalves (2002) by including full dynamical evolution from ground state to scission with all evaporation channels and proper shell effects. The results enable reconciliation of the two measurement techniques and provide predictions for the survival of superheavy element Z=120, relevant to experiments by Morjean (2008) and Freudenberger (2012).
