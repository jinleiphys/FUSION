# Benchmarking Many-body Approaches for the Determination of Isotope Shift Constants: Application to the Li, Be$^+$ and Ar$^{15+}$ Isoelectronic Systems

> We have applied relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) theory to determine the isotope shift (IS) constants of the first eight low-lying states of the Li, Be$^+$ and Ar$^{15+}$ isoelectronic systems. Though the RCC theory with singles, doubles and triples approximation (RCCSDT method) is an exact method for these systems for a given set of basis functions, we notice large differences in the results from this method when various procedures in the RCC theory framework are adopted to estimate the IS constants. This has been demonstrated by presenting the IS constants of the aforementioned states from the finite-field, expectation value and analytical response (AR) approaches of the RCCSDT method. Contributions from valence triple excitations, Breit interaction and lower-order QED effects to the evaluation of these IS constants are also highlighted. Our results are compared with high-precision calculations reported using few-body methods wherever possible. We find that results from the AR procedure are more reliable than the other two approaches. This analysis is crucial for understanding the roles of electron correlation effects in the accurate determination of IS constants in the heavier atomic systems, where few-body methods cannot be applied.

## Key claim

Although the relativistic coupled-cluster theory with singles, doubles, and triples (RCCSDT) is formally exact for three-electron systems given a fixed basis set, the computed isotope shift constants show large discrepancies depending on whether the finite-field, expectation value, or analytical response procedure is used; the authors find that the analytical response approach yields the most reliable results.

## Method

The authors applied relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) theory at the singles, doubles, and triples (RCCSDT) level to compute the field-shift, normal mass-shift, and specific mass-shift constants for the first eight low-lying states of Li, Be\(^+\), and Ar\(^{15+}\). They implemented three distinct computational procedures within the RCC framework: the finite-field approach (adding a perturbative term to the Hamiltonian), the direct expectation value evaluation (using unperturbed wave functions), and the analytical response approach (solving linearized equations for the first-order wave function). Contributions from the Breit interaction and lower-order quantum electrodynamic effects were also included.

## Key numbers

- For the Li atom \(2s\ ^2S_{1/2}\) state, the field-shift constant from the analytical response approach using the DC Hamiltonian is \(-2.035\) MHz fm\(^{-2}\) (RCCSDT), compared to \(-1.732\) MHz fm\(^{-2}\) from the finite-field approach and \(-2.038\) MHz fm\(^{-2}\) from the expectation value approach.
- For the Be\(^+\) ion \(2s\ ^2S_{1/2}\) state, the normal mass-shift constant from the finite-field approach gives an anomalous value of 1013.71 GHz amu (RCCSDT), while the analytical response approach gives 2411.72 GHz amu.
- For the Ar\(^{15+}\) ion \(2s\ ^2S_{1/2}\) state, the final field-shift constant including DC, Breit, and QED contributions (DCBQ) is \(-18633(50)\) MHz fm\(^{-2}\).
- The calculated electron affinity for the \(2s\ ^2S_{1/2}\) state of \(^7\)Li is 43484(10) cm\(^{-1}\), agreeing with the experimental value 43487.1594(2) cm\(^{-1}\) to within 0.008%.
- For the D1 transition (\(2s\ ^2S_{1/2} - 2p\ ^2P_{1/2}\)) of Ar\(^{15+}\), the excitation energy from this work is 31.8635 eV, compared to 31.8681 eV from Sapirstein and 31.8673(5) eV from Kozhedub.

## Context

This work extends the benchmarking of many-body methods for isotope shift constants beyond the few-body approaches of Drake (2006), Pachucki (2008), and Yan (2008), which are limited to systems with few electrons. The authors demonstrate that even for three-electron systems where RCCSDT is formally exact, the choice of computational procedure (finite-field, expectation value, or analytical response) introduces significant systematic differences, contradicting the common assumption that any of these approaches yields equivalent results. This analysis enables future application of the analytical response RCC method to heavier atomic systems where few-body methods cannot be applied, and where accurate isotope shift constants are needed to extract nuclear charge radii from measurements.
