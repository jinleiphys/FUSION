# Traps in hadron spectroscopy: Thresholds, triangle singularities, etc

> The knowledge of hadron spectrum is based on experimental observations of hadronic resonances. The resonances are normally observed as peaks in certain invariant mass distributions. However, neither is a peak necessarily due to the presence of a resonance, nor does a resonance necessarily lead to a peak. Kinematic singularities can also produce peaks. Here, we discuss such possibilities and methods distinguishing genuine resonances from kinematic effects.

## Key claim
Kinematic singularities such as threshold cusps and triangle singularities can produce peaks in invariant mass distributions that mimic genuine resonances, but a narrow pronounced near-threshold peak in an elastic channel necessarily indicates the presence of a nearby S-matrix pole, not merely a threshold cusp.

## Method
The paper analyzes two-body threshold cusps using a two-channel model (e.g., \(J/\psi\pi\) and \(D\bar D^*\)) with perturbative one-loop amplitudes, then tests self-consistency by comparing one-loop and two-loop results. For triangle singularities, the author derives the kinematic conditions (Eqs. for \(q_{\rm on+}=q_{a-}\)) under which the singularity lies on the physical boundary, and applies these to the \(D_1\bar D D^*\) loop in \(e^+e^-\to J/\psi\pi\pi\) and the \(\Lambda(1890)\chi_{c1}p\) loop in \(\Lambda_b^0\to J/\psi p K^-\).

## Key numbers
- The best fit to BESIII data for \(e^+e^- \to \pi^+ (D\bar D^*)^-\) at \(E_{\rm cm}=4.26\) GeV yields a one-loop amplitude that, when extended to two loops, shows large deviation, indicating the interaction is nonperturbative with \(G_\Lambda(E)C_D\) of order 1 near threshold.
- The triangle singularity for the \(D_1\bar D D^*\) loop in \(e^+e^-\to J/\psi\pi\pi\) appears as an arc in the \(E_{\rm cm}\)–\(M_{J/\psi\pi}\) plane; at \(E_{\rm cm}=4.29\) GeV the loop function has a sharp peak, while at lower energies the peak becomes less pronounced.
- The \(P_c(4450)\) mass coincides with the \(\chi_{c1}p\) threshold, and the triangle singularity of the \(\Lambda(1890)\chi_{c1}p\) loop reproduces the LHCb peak structure around 4.45 GeV.
- The \(f_0(980)\) peak from the \(\eta(1405)\to\pi\pi\pi\) triangle singularity is as narrow as about 10 MeV \(\sim 2(M_{K^0}-M_{K^+})\).

## Context
This work extends discussions of kinematic singularities in hadron spectroscopy, building on earlier treatments of threshold cusps (e.g., the \(\pi\pi\) S-wave cusp in \(K^\pm\to\pi^\pm\pi^0\pi^0\) by NA48/2, analyzed by Cabibbo (2004) and Gasser (2011)) and triangle singularities (Landau (1959), Coleman and Norton (1965)). It directly addresses the interpretation of the \(Z_c(3900)\), \(Z_c(4020)\), \(Z_b(10610)\), \(Z_b(10650)\), and \(P_c(4450)\) as either genuine resonances or kinematic effects, and enables future experimental tests such as measuring \(\chi_{c1}p\) invariant mass distributions in \(\Lambda_b^0\) decays and searching for the \(P_c(4450)\) in photoproduction, pion-induced reactions, and heavy ion collisions.
