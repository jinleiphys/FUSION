# The delimiting/frontier lines of the constituents of matter

> Looking at the \emph{chart of nuclides} displayed at the URL of the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) \cite{IAEA} -- that contains all the known nuclides, the natural and those produced artificially in labs -- one verifies the existence of two, not quite regular, \emph{delimiting lines} between which dwell all the nuclides constituting matter. These lines are established by the highly unstable radionuclides located the most far away from those in the central locus, the\emph{valley of stability}. Here, making use of the "old" semi-empirical mass formula for stable nuclides together with the energy-time uncertainty relation of quantum mechanics, by a simple calculation we show that the obtained \emph{frontier lines}, for proton and neutron excesses, present an appreciable agreement with the delimiting lines. For the sake of presenting a somewhat comprehensive panorama of the matter in our Universe and their relation with the frontier lines, we narrate, in brief, what is currently known about the astrophysical nucleogenesis processes.

## Key claim

Using the semi-empirical mass formula together with the energy-time uncertainty relation, the authors show that the calculated frontier lines for proton and neutron excesses agree appreciably with the observed delimiting lines on the chart of nuclides, though the agreement is qualitative rather than exact due to the simplicity of the model.

## Method

The authors used the Weizsäcker-Bethe-Bacher semi-empirical mass formula with Wapstra's 1958 parameter values to calculate nuclear masses for given proton number Z and mass number A. They then applied the energy-time uncertainty relation to determine the conditions under which a nucleus becomes unbound to proton or neutron emission, defining the frontier lines as the boundaries where the last nucleon is no longer bound. The calculated frontier lines were compared with the observed delimiting lines from the IAEA chart of nuclides.

## Key numbers

- The semi-empirical mass formula parameters used: a_v = 15.835 MeV, a_s = 18.33 MeV, a_c = 0.72 MeV, a_ass = 23.20 MeV, a_p = 11.20 MeV
- Hydrogen atom mass: M_1H = 938.783 MeV; neutron mass: M_n = 939.565 MeV
- There exist about 252 stable nuclides and 34 primordial radionuclides with half-lives long enough to have existed since Earth's formation approximately 4.5 Gy ago
- The total number of known nuclides is approximately 3300
- The beryllium-8 nucleus has a half-life of approximately 10^{-16} s

## Context

This work extends the classic liquid-drop model approach of Weizsäcker (1935) and Bethe and Bacher (1936) to predict the boundaries of nuclear stability, commonly known as drip lines. The authors compare their simple calculation with the observed delimiting lines from the IAEA chart of nuclides, noting that more sophisticated approaches incorporating quantum shell effects (such as those by Seeger, Myers, Moller, and others) would not change their conclusions expressively. The work enables a pedagogical understanding of where nuclear matter becomes unbound, connecting to discussions of nucleosynthesis processes including the s-process, r-process, and rp-process described in the seminal works of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) and subsequent studies.
