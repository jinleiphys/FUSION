# New constraints on radii and tidal deformabilities of neutron stars from GW170817

> We explore in a parameterized manner a very large range of physically plausible equations of state (EOSs) for compact stars for matter that is either purely hadronic or that exhibits a phase transition. In particular, we produce two classes of EOSs with and without phase transitions, each containing one million EOSs. We then impose constraints on the maximum mass, ($M < 2.16 M_{\odot}$), and on the dimensionless tidal deformability ($\tildeΛ <800$) deduced from GW170817, together with recent suggestions of lower limits on $\tildeΛ$. Exploiting more than $10^9$ equilibrium models for each class of EOSs, we produce distribution functions of all the stellar properties and determine, among other quantities, the radius that is statistically most probable for any value of the stellar mass. In this way, we deduce that the radius of a purely hadronic neutron star with a representative mass of $1.4\,M_{\odot}$ is constrained to be $12.00\!<\!R_{1.4}/{\rm km}\!<\!13.45$ at a $2$-$σ$ confidence level, with a most likely value of $\bar{R}_{1.4}=12.39\,{\rm km}$; similarly, the smallest dimensionless tidal deformability is $\tildeΛ_{1.4}\!>\!375$, again at a $2$-$σ$ level. On the other hand, because EOSs with a phase transition allow for very compact stars on the so-called `twin-star' branch, small radii are possible with such EOSs although not probable, i.e. $8.53\!<\!R_{1.4}/{\rm km}\!<\!13.74$ and $\bar{R}_{1.4}=13.06\,{\rm km}$ at a $2$-$σ$ level, with $\tildeΛ_{1.4}\!>\!35.5$ at a $3$-$σ$ level. Finally, since these EOSs exhibit upper limits on $\tildeΛ$, the detection of a binary with total mass of $3.4\,M_{\odot}$ and $\tildeΛ_{1.7}\!>\!461$ can rule out twin-star solutions.

## Key claim

The radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is constrained to 12.00 km < R < 13.45 km at 2-sigma confidence for purely hadronic equations of state, but this range broadens to 8.53 km < R < 13.74 km when phase transitions are allowed, with the most likely radius shifting from 12.39 km to 13.06 km respectively.

## Method

The authors constructed two classes of parameterized equations of state (one million each for purely hadronic matter and matter with phase transitions) using piecewise polytropes that match nuclear-physics calculations for the crust, state-of-the-art descriptions near nuclear-saturation density from Drischler (2016), and perturbative QCD at high densities. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for over one billion equilibrium models per class, then imposed constraints from GW170817 on maximum mass (M < 2.16 solar masses) and dimensionless tidal deformability (Lambda < 800), plus suggested lower limits, to produce probability distribution functions of stellar properties.

## Key numbers

- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass purely hadronic neutron star: 12.00 km < R < 13.45 km at 2-sigma confidence, with most likely value 12.39 km
- Smallest dimensionless tidal deformability for 1.4 solar mass hadronic star: Lambda > 375 at 2-sigma level
- Radius of 1.4 solar mass star with phase transitions: 8.53 km < R < 13.74 km at 2-sigma, with most likely value 13.06 km
- Smallest dimensionless tidal deformability for 1.4 solar mass star with phase transition: Lambda > 35.5 at 3-sigma level
- Upper limit on tidal deformability for twin stars at 1.7 solar masses: Lambda < 461
- Twin-star branch represents only 5 percent of the total sample with phase transitions

## Context

This work extends the approach of Annala (2017), who constrained the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star to R < 13.6 km and the minimum tidal deformability to Lambda > 120, by using a much larger ensemble of two million equations of state and imposing the combined constraints from GW170817 on both maximum mass and tidal deformability simultaneously. The results enable future gravitational-wave detections to rule out twin-star solutions if a binary with total mass 3.4 solar masses shows Lambda > 461, and highlight that improved knowledge of the outer core from Drischler (2017) could reduce radius uncertainties to below 1 km.
