# Nuclear structure and direct reaction studies in particle-$γ$ coincidence experiments at the FSU John D. Fox Superconducting Linear Accelerator Laboratory

> Since its foundation in the 1960s, the John D. Fox Superconducting Linear Accelerator Laboratory at Florida State University (FSU) pursued research at the forefront of nuclear science. In this contribution, we present recent highlights from nuclear structure and reaction studies conducted at the John D. Fox Superconducting Linear Accelerator Laboratory, also featuring the general experimental capabilities at the laboratory for particle-$γ$ coincidence experiments. Specifically, we focus on light-ion induced reactions measured with the Super-Enge Split-Pole Spectrograph (SE-SPS) and the CATRiNA neutron detectors, respectively. Some results obtained with the CeBrA demonstrator for particle-$γ$ coincidence experiments at the SE-SPS are presented. A highlight from the first experimental campaigns with the combined CLARION2-TRINITY setup, showing that weak reaction channels can be selected, is discussed as well.

## Key claim

The paper presents recent experimental capabilities and highlights from the John D. Fox Superconducting Linear Accelerator Laboratory, demonstrating that particle-gamma coincidence experiments with multiple detector systems can resolve close-lying nuclear states and determine spin-parity assignments that are inaccessible to singles particle spectroscopy alone, though the authors note that for some spin-orbit partner pairs (e.g., 1f5/2 and 1f7/2) the proton-gamma angular correlations are not sufficiently different to discriminate between them.

## Method

Light-ion induced reactions (primarily (d,p), (d,t), and (d,n)) were measured using the Super-Enge Split-Pole Spectrograph (SE-SPS) for charged-particle detection and the CATRiNA deuterated-benzene liquid scintillator array for neutron detection, with gamma rays detected in coincidence by the CeBrA demonstrator (CeBr3 scintillators) and the CLARION2 array (Compton-suppressed Clover-type HPGe detectors). Particle-gamma coincidence matrices were constructed to select specific decay pathways, and proton-gamma angular correlations were measured to determine multipole mixing ratios and spin-parity assignments, with neutron energies extracted both by time-of-flight and by spectrum unfolding using the MLEM and ANUBIS algorithms.

## Key numbers

- The SE-SPS has a solid angle of 12.8 msr and achieved a design resolution of approximately 20 keV during a 12C(d,p)13C experiment in June 2019; in standard operation, a FWHM of 30-70 keV has been routinely achieved.
- The extended CeBrA demonstrator has a combined full energy peak efficiency of about 3.5% at 1.3 MeV, compared to 1.5% for the five-detector demonstrator.
- The SE-SPS focal-plane detector has an active length of about 60 cm, with two anode wires separated by about 4.3 cm, and can be operated at rates as high as 2 kilocounts/s.
- For the 49Ti(d,t)48Ti reaction, the magnetic field was 11.2 kG and the solid-angle acceptance was 4.6 msr.
- In the CATRiNA experiment, the deuteron beam was bunched to 2-ns width with intervals of 82.5 ns for time-of-flight measurements; detectors were placed at 1-m distance from a 400-microgram/cm2 thick CD2 target.
- A software threshold cut of around 2 MeV was placed on neutron energies to minimize background.
- At Ed = 5 MeV, neutrons from 12C(d,n)13N ground state had energies around 4 MeV; at Ed = 8 MeV, these neutrons had energies around 7.2 MeV, with the first excited state of 13N at Ex = 2.36 MeV producing neutrons at 4.8 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the capabilities of the John D. Fox Laboratory, which has been operating since the 1960s and previously achieved milestones such as the first acceleration of negatively-charged helium ions (Car63a) and identification of isobaric analogue resonances (Fox64a). The SE-SPS was moved from the Wright Nuclear Structure Laboratory at Yale University after it ceased operation, and the CeBrA array builds on earlier work by Con24a. The CLARION2-TRINITY setup was installed in collaboration with Oak Ridge National Laboratory (Gra22a). These combined detector systems enable detailed spectroscopy of stable nuclei near the valley of beta stability, providing complementary data for rare-isotope studies at facilities like FRIB and testing reaction theory for ab-initio nuclear structure calculations.
