# Balance functions in a thermal model with resonances

> The pi+ pi- balance function in rapidity is computed in a thermal model with resonances. It is found that the correlations from the neutral-resonance decays are important, yielding about a half of the total contribution, which in general consist of resonance and non-resonance parts. The model yields the pionic balance function a few per cent wider that what follows from the recent data for the Au+Au collisions at 130GeV.

# Balance functions in a thermal model with resonances

## Key claim

The thermal model with resonances yields a pionic balance function that is a few per cent wider than the STAR data for Au+Au collisions at 130 GeV, with neutral-resonance decays contributing about half of the total correlation; the model agrees best with the most peripheral data, and the centrality dependence of the width cannot be reproduced by varying transverse flow within limits consistent with single-particle spectra.

## Method

The authors compute the π⁺π⁻ balance function in rapidity using a thermal model with single freeze-out, separating the contribution into two parts: a resonance contribution from decays of neutral hadronic resonances (K_S, η, η′, ρ⁰, ω, σ, f₀) and a non-resonance contribution from pions emitted directly from the fire-cylinder or from decays of other resonances. The resonance contribution uses the phase-space distributions of pions from two-body and three-body decays of neutral resonances with thermal momentum distributions from the Cooper-Frye formula, while the non-resonance contribution assumes local charge balancing at the freeze-out surface with thermal velocities. The model includes kinematic cuts of |η_π| < 1.3 and p_⟂ > 0.1 GeV to simulate the STAR acceptance.

## Key numbers

- The resonance contribution to the balance function integral is ∫ dδ B_R(δ) = 0.25, and the non-resonance contribution is ∫ dδ B_NR(δ) = 0.29, with total reaching 0.53 (including kinematic cuts)
- The ratio of non-resonance pions to all charged particles (excluding resonance pions) is N^π_NR/(N_charged - N_R^π) = 0.68
- For ρ_max/τ = 0.9 (⟨β_⟂⟩ = 0.50), the total mean width ⟨δ⟩_tot = 0.66 (range 0.2 < δ < 2.6) and 0.52 (range 0 < δ < 2.6)
- For ρ_max/τ = 0.8 (⟨β_⟂⟩ = 0.46), the total mean width ⟨δ⟩_tot = 0.66 (range 0.2 < δ < 2.6) and 0.53 (range 0 < δ < 2.6)
- The experimental mean widths from STAR: 0.594 ± 0.019 (0-10% centrality), 0.622 ± 0.020 (10-40%), 0.633 ± 0.024 (40-70%), and 0.664 ± 0.029 (70-96%)
- The freeze-out temperature used is 165 MeV
- The normalization factors adjusted for comparison with STAR data range from 0.40 to 0.51 across centrality bins

## Context

This work extends the thermal model with single freeze-out (Broniowski and Florkowski, 2002) to compute charge balance functions, following the proposal by Bass, Danielewicz, and Pratt (2000) that balance function widths can discriminate between production mechanisms. The results are compared to STAR measurements (2003) which showed narrower balance functions than earlier model expectations. The approach is similar to the blast-wave model calculation by Pratt et al. (2003) and complements the coalescence model explanation by Bialas (2003). The finding that neutral-resonance decays contribute about half of the correlation enables future work to incorporate Bose-Einstein correlations (which cause dips at small δ not captured here) and to address the discrepancy in normalization due to detector efficiency.
