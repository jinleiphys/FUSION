# Switching Reciprocity On and Off in a Magneto-Optical X-Ray Scattering Experiment Using Nuclear Resonance of 57Fe Foils

> Reciprocity is when the scattering amplitude of wave propagation satisfies a symmetry property, connecting a scattering process with an appropriate reversed one. We report on an experiment using nuclear resonance scattering of synchrotron radiation, which demonstrates that magneto-optical materials do not necessarily violate reciprocity. The setting enables to switch easily between reciprocity and its violation. In the latter case, the exhibited reciprocity violation is orders of magnitude larger than achieved by previous wave scattering experiments.

## Key claim

Magneto-optical materials do not necessarily violate reciprocity; the experiment demonstrates that reciprocity can be switched on and off by adjusting the orientation of magnetic fields in two 57Fe foils, with the nonreciprocal case showing a violation that is orders of magnitude larger than achieved by previous wave scattering experiments.

## Method

The experiment used nuclear resonance scattering of synchrotron radiation in forward-scattering geometry, analogous to transmission Mössbauer spectroscopy but performed in the time domain. Two 6 μm thick ferromagnetic α-57Fe foils were mounted in a sample holder with permanent magnets providing 0.19 T magnetic fields in adjustable directions, and the time response of the scatterer to excitation by a synchrotron pulse was recorded. The interchange of source and detector position was realized by a 180° rotation of the whole sample holder around the x axis, and the measured time spectra for direct and reciprocal geometries were compared.

## Key numbers

- Foil thickness: 6 μm for each α-57Fe absorber
- Magnetic field strength: 0.19 T applied by permanent magnets
- Slit diameter: 0.5 mm, selecting homogeneous parts of the foils
- Distance between foils: 39.2 cm
- Nonreciprocity ratio: nearly 10³ in certain time intervals for the arrangement with V₂ and V₃ foils (Fig. 1c)
- Magnetic field orientations used: (θ₁, φ₁) = (90°, 90°); (θ₂, φ₂) = (90°, 45°); (θ₃, φ₃) = (135°, 0°)

## Context

This work extends the general reciprocity theorem formulated by Deák and Fülöp (2011), which covers wave phenomena representable by a Schrödinger equation, and demonstrates experimentally that magneto-optical materials do not automatically violate reciprocity. The experiment enables future applications in γ-optics, particularly nonreciprocal devices such as circulators and isolators, which were previously suggested by Kamal (2011) for on-chip integration.
