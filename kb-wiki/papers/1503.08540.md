# Can dark matter induce cosmological evolution of the fundamental constants of Nature?

> We demonstrate that massive fields, such as dark matter, can directly produce a cosmological evolution of the fundamental constants of Nature. We show that a scalar or pseudoscalar (axion-like) dark matter field $ϕ$, which forms a coherently oscillating classical field and interacts with Standard Model particles via quadratic couplings in $ϕ$, produces `slow' cosmological evolution and oscillating variations of the fundamental constants. We derive limits on the quadratic interactions of $ϕ$ with the photon, electron and light quarks from measurements of the primordial $^4$He abundance produced during Big Bang nucleosynthesis and recent atomic dysprosium spectroscopy measurements. These limits improve on existing constraints by up to 15 orders of magnitude. We also derive limits on the previously unconstrained linear and quadratic interactions of $ϕ$ with the massive vector bosons from measurements of the primordial $^4$He abundance.

## Key claim

Massive dark matter fields (scalar or pseudoscalar) can directly produce both a "slow" cosmological evolution and oscillating variations of the fundamental constants, provided the field interacts with Standard Model particles via quadratic couplings in the field \(\phi\) and forms a coherently oscillating classical field with \(\langle \phi^2 \rangle \neq 0\).

## Method

The authors consider a non-relativistic cold scalar or pseudoscalar (axion-like) dark matter field \(\phi\) that forms a coherently oscillating classical field, \(\phi = \phi_0 \cos(\omega t)\). They derive how quadratic couplings of \(\phi\) to photons, electrons, light quarks, and massive vector bosons alter the Standard Model particle masses and the fine-structure constant \(\alpha\). Limits on these couplings are obtained from the measured primordial \(^4\)He abundance from Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN), from cosmic microwave background (CMB) angular power spectrum data, and from atomic dysprosium spectroscopy measurements, using the known evolution of the dark matter energy density with redshift.

## Key numbers

- The present mean cold dark matter energy density is \(\bar{\rho}_{\text{DM}} = 1.3 \times 10^{-6} \, [1+z(t)]^3 \, \text{GeV/cm}^3\), determined from WMAP measurements.
- The present-day local cold dark matter energy density is \(\rho_{\text{CDM}}^{\text{local}} \approx 0.4 \, \text{GeV/cm}^3\).
- CMB measurements give bounds \((\Delta \alpha / \alpha)_{\text{CMB}} \lesssim 0.01\) and \((\Delta m_e / m_e)_{\text{CMB}} \lesssim 0.04\), yielding constraints \(\Lambda'_\gamma \gtrsim 1 \, \text{eV}^2 / m_\phi\) and \(\Lambda'_e \gtrsim 0.6 \, \text{eV}^2 / m_\phi\) for the quadratic couplings.
- From the measured primordial \(^4\)He abundance \(Y_p^{\text{exp}}(^4\text{He}) = 0.2477 \pm 0.0029\) and the theoretical value \(Y_p^{\text{theor}}(^4\text{He}) = 0.2486 \pm 0.0002\), the authors derive constraints on quadratic couplings for both the non-oscillating regime (\(m_\phi \ll 10^{-16}\) eV) and the oscillating regime (\(m_\phi \gg 10^{-16}\) eV), with the numerical results given in Eqs. (13), (14), and (16) of the paper.
- Existing supernova energy loss limits give \(\Lambda'_\gamma \gtrsim 3 \times 10^3 \, \text{GeV}\) and \(\Lambda'_p \gtrsim 15 \times 10^3 \, \text{GeV}\) for the quadratic couplings.
- The new limits from BBN and atomic spectroscopy improve on existing constraints by up to 15 orders of magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the study of cosmological variation of fundamental constants beyond the usual dark energy-type theories (such as Brans-Dicke models, string dilaton models, chameleon models, and Bekenstein models, e.g., Terazawa (1981), Damour (1994), Khoury (2004), Bekenstein (1982)) by showing that massive dark matter fields can directly produce such variations. The authors note that dark matter models are more amenable to theoretical and experimental investigation than their dark energy-type counterparts. The results enable future laboratory searches using atomic clocks, highly-charged ions, molecules, nuclear clocks, and laser interferometers (e.g., Flambaum (2008), Ong (2014), Stadnik (2014), Stadnik (2015)) to probe new regions of parameter space for dark matter interactions.
