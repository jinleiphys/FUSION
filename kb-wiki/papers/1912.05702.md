# A NICER View of PSR J0030+0451: Millisecond Pulsar Parameter Estimation

> We report on Bayesian parameter estimation of the mass and equatorial radius of the millisecond pulsar PSR J0030$+$0451, conditional on pulse-profile modeling of Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) X-ray spectral-timing event data. We perform relativistic ray-tracing of thermal emission from hot regions of the pulsar's surface. We assume two distinct hot regions based on two clear pulsed components in the phase-folded pulse-profile data; we explore a number of forms (morphologies and topologies) for each hot region, inferring their parameters in addition to the stellar mass and radius. For the family of models considered, the evidence (prior predictive probability of the data) strongly favors a model that permits both hot regions to be located in the same rotational hemisphere. Models wherein both hot regions are assumed to be simply-connected circular single-temperature spots, in particular those where the spots are assumed to be reflection-symmetric with respect to the stellar origin, are strongly disfavored. For the inferred configuration, one hot region subtends an angular extent of only a few degrees (in spherical coordinates with origin at the stellar center) and we are insensitive to other structural details; the second hot region is far more azimuthally extended in the form of a narrow arc, thus requiring a larger number of parameters to describe. The inferred mass $M$ and equatorial radius $R_\mathrm{eq}$ are, respectively, $1.34_{-0.16}^{+0.15}$ M$_{\odot}$ and $12.71_{-1.19}^{+1.14}$ km, whilst the compactness $GM/R_\mathrm{eq}c^2 = 0.156_{-0.010}^{+0.008}$ is more tightly constrained; the credible interval bounds reported here are approximately the $16\%$ and $84\%$ quantiles in marginal posterior mass.

## Key claim
The mass and equatorial radius of PSR J0030+0451 are inferred as 1.34 solar masses and 12.71 km, respectively, but these values are conditional on a specific family of models for the hot regions on the stellar surface; the evidence strongly favors a configuration where both hot regions lie in the same rotational hemisphere, and models with simple antipodal circular spots are strongly disfavored.

## Method
The authors performed Bayesian parameter estimation by modeling the pulse-profile of NICER X-ray spectral-timing event data from PSR J0030+0451, using relativistic ray-tracing of thermal emission from two distinct hot regions on the pulsar's surface. They explored multiple morphologies for the hot regions (circular spots, annuli, crescents) and used nested sampling to infer the stellar mass, equatorial radius, and hot region parameters, with the Oblate Schwarzschild plus Doppler approximation for the neutron star spacetime.

## Key numbers
- Mass M = 1.34 (+0.15, -0.16) solar masses (68% credible interval bounds are approximately the 16% and 84% quantiles in marginal posterior mass)
- Equatorial radius R_eq = 12.71 (+1.14, -1.19) km
- Compactness GM/R_eq c^2 = 0.156 (+0.008, -0.010)
- Spin frequency of PSR J0030+0451: 205 Hz
- Distance to PSR J0030+0451: 325 +/- 9 pc (from radio observations by nanograv11)
- Integrated exposure time: 1,936,864 seconds

## Context
This work presents the first pulse-profile modeling results from the NICER mission, extending prior efforts to constrain neutron star masses and radii using X-ray observations. It builds on earlier studies of PSR J0030+0451 using XMM-Newton (Bogdanov 2009) and complements independent analysis of the same NICER data by Miller et al. (2019) using different modeling choices. The results enable tighter constraints on the dense matter equation of state when combined with other neutron star mass and radius measurements, such as those from gravitational wave event GW170817 (Abbott et al. 2017, 2018) and radio pulsar timing of PSR J0348+0432 (Antoniadis 2013) and PSR J1614-2230 (nanograv11).
