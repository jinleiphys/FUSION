# Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC) for a Study of Oxygen Formation In Stellar Helium Burning

> We are developing an Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC) detector for the study of the 12C(a,g)16O reaction that determines the ratio of carbon to oxygen in helium burning. This ratio is crucial for understanding the final fate of a progenitor star and the nucleosynthesis of elements prior to a Type II supernova; an oxygen rich star is predicted to collapse to a black hole, and a carbon rich star to a neutron star. Type Ia supernovae (SNeIa) are used as standard candles for measuring cosmological distances with the use of an empirical light curve-luminosity stretching factor. It is essential to understand helium burning that yields the carbon/oxygen white dwarf and thus the initial stage of SNeIa. The O-TPC is intended for use with high intensity photon beams extracted from the HIgS/TUNL facility at Duke University to study the 16O(g,a)12C reaction, and thus the direct reaction at energies as low as 0.7 MeV. We are conducting a systematical study of the best oxygen containing gas with light emitting admixture(s) for use in such an O-TPC. Preliminary results with CO_2 + TEA mixture were obtained

# Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC) for a Study of Oxygen Formation In Stellar Helium Burning

## Key claim
The authors propose that the astrophysical S-factor for the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction can be measured directly at center-of-mass energies as low as 700 keV using the inverse 16O(gamma,alpha)12C reaction with a novel Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC) at the HIgammaS facility, rather than relying on extrapolations from higher-energy data or the inconclusive beta-decay of 16N. However, the paper presents only preliminary results with a CO2 + TEA gas mixture and simulated anticipated results, not actual measured cross sections.

## Method
The authors are developing an Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber (O-TPC) that uses a gas mixture of CO2 with triethylamine (TEA) as a light-emitting admixture, with cascaded Gas Electron Multipliers (GEMs) or Thick GEM-like multipliers for electron amplification and UV photon production. The detector will be used with the high-intensity, linearly polarized photon beam from the HIgammaS facility at Duke University to study the photodissociation of 16O, detecting back-to-back alpha particles and 12C nuclei from the 16O(gamma,alpha)12C reaction. The TPC provides tracking of both particles, measurement of scattering angles with better than two degrees accuracy, and energy resolution of approximately 5% for background discrimination.

## Key numbers
- The photodissociation cross section for 16O(gamma,alpha)12C at center-of-mass energies near 700 keV is approximately 1 nb, compared to the direct 12C(alpha,gamma)16O cross section of approximately 10 pb (enhancement factor of 80-160).
- The Q-value for the 16O(gamma,alpha)12C reaction is -7.162 MeV, requiring gamma-ray energies from 7.9 to 10 MeV.
- The proposed experiment uses a 30 cm long fiducial target with CO2 at 76 torr (100 mbar) and a photon beam intensity of 1 x 10^9 /sec, yielding a luminosity of 1.5 x 10^29 sec^-1 cm^-2 (15 nb^-1/day).
- The design goal is to measure the direct 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction with a cross section as low as 10 pb, corresponding to energies as low as 700 keV.
- The astrophysical S-factors must be known at the Gamow peak (300 keV) with accuracy of approximately 10% or better.
- The pulsed photon beam has 0.1 nsec pulses every 180 nsec with at most 500 gammas per pulse.
- Alpha particle range is approximately 8 cm at 100 mbar; carbon range is approximately 2 cm (factor of 4 difference).

## Context
This work addresses the long-standing problem of determining the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction rate, which Willie Fowler (1984) called the "holy grail" of stellar nuclear physics. Previous attempts using the beta-decay of 16N (Azuma 1994, Buchmann 1993) quoted the S-factor with 25% uncertainty, but Hale (1996) showed the interpretation was inconclusive by at least a factor of 4 and as much as 8. A measurement by Hammer (2001) at lower energies suggested a d-wave cross section factor at least twice larger than the accepted value. The proposed O-TPC approach extends work by the Weizmann-PTB collaboration (Titt et al., Nuclear Instruments and Methods) and parallels a similar research program with TPC at RCNP in Osaka, Japan (Shima). Successful measurements would enable determination of the carbon-to-oxygen ratio in white dwarfs, which affects the peak luminosity and light curve of Type Ia supernovae used as standard candles for cosmological distance measurements.
