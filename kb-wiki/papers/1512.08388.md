# Heavy ion collisions and cosmology

> There are interesting parallels between the physics of heavy ion collisions and cosmology. Both systems are out-of-equilibrium and relativistic fluid dynamics plays an important role for their theoretical description. From a comparison one can draw interesting conclusions for both sides. For heavy ion physics it could be rewarding to attempt a theoretical description of fluid perturbations similar to cosmological perturbation theory. In the context of late time cosmology, it could be interesting to study dissipative properties such as shear and bulk viscosity and corresponding relaxation times in more detail. Knowledge and experience from heavy ion physics could help to constrain the microscopic properties of dark matter from observational knowledge of the cosmological fluid properties.

## Key claim

There are useful parallels between heavy ion collisions and cosmology that can benefit both fields, but the analogy is most fruitful at an abstract level because cosmological and nuclear scales differ by many orders of magnitude, late-time cosmology is dominated by gravity and dark matter while heavy ion collisions are governed by QCD, and only a single event can be studied in cosmology versus many collisions in heavy ion physics.

## Method

The paper compares the theoretical frameworks of relativistic fluid dynamics as applied to heavy ion collisions and cosmology, examining how perturbations around a homogeneous background are treated in each field. It discusses cosmological perturbation theory as a potential template for describing fluid perturbations in heavy ion collisions, and conversely explores how knowledge of dissipative properties (shear and bulk viscosity) from heavy ion physics could inform cosmological models of dark matter and large-scale structure formation.

## Key numbers

- The spectral index of cosmological perturbations is given by \(n_s\) and the overall amplitude by \(c\) in the relation \(P(k) = c \, k^{n_s-1}\), which specifies initial conditions for many modes by just two numbers.
- Upper bounds on dark matter self-interaction cross section \(\sigma\) over dark matter particle mass \(m_\text{DM}\) indicate \(\sigma/m_\text{DM}\) must be less than about one cm\(^2\)/g from galaxy cluster observations.
- An analysis of galaxy cluster Abell 3827 found a small offset interpreted as \(\sigma/m_\text{DM} \approx 3\) cm/g, though this interpretation remains under debate.
- The backreaction term \(D\) becomes large enough to affect cosmological expansion when the dimensionless ratio \(4\pi G_\text{N} D/3H^3\) reaches order one.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between heavy ion collisions and cosmology previously discussed in the literature, drawing on the author's prior work (Floerchinger 2013, 2014) on Bessel-Fourier transforms for heavy ion perturbations and on cosmological perturbation theory as developed by Mukhanov (1990) and Weinberg (2008). The paper connects to recent studies of viscous effects in cosmology (Blas 2015) and to the heavy ion physics literature on cavitation (Torrieri 2007, Rajagopal 2009) and anomalous plasma viscosity (Asakawa 2006). It enables future development of a perturbative description of anisotropies in heavy ion collisions analogous to cosmological perturbation theory, and suggests that precision cosmology missions such as the Euclid satellite (planned for 2020) could measure shear stress of dark matter to constrain its microscopic properties.
