# Effective $β$-decay rates of $r$-process waiting points in realistic stellar environments

> Reliable nuclear weak rates are key inputs for understanding the origin of heavy elements and constraining the environments of the corresponding stellar nucleosynthesis. We present the effective stellar $β^-$-decay rates of the $N=50, 82, 126$ $r$-process waiting-point nuclei in realistic stellar environments with high temperature, high density and strong magnetic field. Both allowed and first-forbidden transitions are considered, and transitions from the low-lying states of parent nuclei due to the thermal population are taken into account properly. The stellar $β^-$-decay rates of the $N=50, 82$ waiting points are not sensitive to stellar temperature, while those of the $N=126$ waiting points increase rapidly with stellar temperature. With the increase of stellar density, the electron chemical potential increases accordingly, which leads to reduction of the stellar $β$-decay rates. Besides, the stellar $β$-decay rates are found to increase rapidly with the magnetic field $B$ when $B \gtrsim 10^{14}$ G. Depending on the stellar temperature, density and magnetic field, the rates may vary by several orders of magnitude, which indicates that dynamic $β$-decay rates for corresponding stellar conditions may be indispensable inputs for understanding the $r$-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first calculation of effective stellar beta-decay rates for N=50, 82, and 126 r-process waiting-point nuclei that simultaneously accounts for high temperature, high density, and strong magnetic fields, but the rates vary by up to seven orders of magnitude depending on the specific stellar conditions, meaning that static or terrestrial rates are insufficient for realistic r-process modeling.

## Method

The authors used the projected shell model (PSM) to compute nuclear shape factors for both allowed Gamow-Teller and first-forbidden transitions, with transitions from thermally populated low-lying states of parent nuclei included. The stellar beta-decay rate was then obtained by integrating over Landau levels in a magnetic field, with the electron chemical potential determined self-consistently from the electron density, temperature, and magnetic field strength via the Fermi-Dirac distribution.

## Key numbers

- For N=126 waiting points, when temperature increases from 1 to 3 GK, stellar rates of 192Dy, 194Er, and 198Hf increase by about five times, and those of 196Yb increase by one order of magnitude.
- For 80Zn at N=50, when electron density increases from 10^7 to 10^10 mol/cm^3, the rate decreases from 10/s to less than 0.1/s.
- For 198Hf at N=126, with electron density 10^9 mol/cm^3 and temperature 1 GK, when magnetic field increases from 10^15 G to 10^17 G, the rate increases from less than 10^-2/s to approximately 10^3/s, a change of about five orders of magnitude.
- The onset magnetic field for rate enhancement is approximately B_onset ~ Q_11^2 x 4.4 x 10^12 G, yielding about 10^15 G for 128Pd and about 4 x 10^14 G for 196Yb.
- The stellar beta-decay rates of N=50, 82, and 126 waiting points may vary by three to seven orders of magnitude depending on stellar temperature, density, and magnetic field.

## Context

This work extends previous shell-model calculations by Suzuki (2012) and Zhi (2013) and quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) calculations by Roben (2024) for first-forbidden transitions, but finds that first-forbidden transitions contribute less than 30% of total terrestrial rates, in contrast to those earlier predictions of several-fold reductions. The results enable dynamic r-process simulations that couple beta-decay and neutrino energy-loss rates with evolving stellar conditions, and can help constrain r-process sites and astrophysical conditions when combined with precise abundance distributions and neutron-capture rates.
