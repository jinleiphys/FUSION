# Catapult neutrons from neck snapping in fission

> Dynamical fission calculations show that the post-scission configurations resemble two collinear pear-shaped fragments whose juxtaposed surface bulges subside relatively quickly, as the fragments acquire smoother shapes. The associated rapid speed of the healing bulge surface may boost nucleons in the fragment to energies sufficient for emission. The present study explores this mechanism by following the fate of nucleons that are reflected off the inwards moving bulge surface. The simulations suggest that the mechanism may produce high-energy neutrons at the level of a few per cent.

## Key claim

Dynamical fission calculations show that the "catapult" mechanism, in which nucleons are reflected off the rapidly shrinking surface bulge left after neck rupture, may produce high-energy neutrons at the level of a few per cent of the total prompt neutron yield, but the actual emission probability depends sensitively on the initial bulge geometry (height and width) and the fragment deformation.

## Method

The authors model the post-scission fragment as a spheroidal reference shape with a Gaussian-shaped surface bulge that shrinks under the influence of surface tension, with the motion damped by one-body dissipation. They then sample test neutrons from a Fermi-Dirac distribution (temperature T=1 MeV) and follow their classical trajectories: neutrons reflected from the inwards-moving bulge surface receive an energy boost, and those whose total energy exceeds the escape threshold (E_esc ≈ 44 MeV) are tracked through the fragment until they either escape (if their normal energy at the surface exceeds E_esc) or are reflected back.

## Key numbers

- For standard bulge parameters (initial height h0=2.0 fm, width σ0=1.0 fm, fragment axis ratio c/a=1.8), the expected number of catapult neutrons per fragment is 3.8% with a mean kinetic energy of 9.1 MeV.
- For a spherical fragment (c/a=1.0), the catapult yield drops to 2.7% with mean energy 7.7 MeV; for a more deformed fragment (c/a=2.0), the yield rises to 3.3% with mean energy 9.66 MeV.
- For thermal neutron fission of 236U, combining a deformed fragment (c/a=1.8) and a spherical fragment gives 0.065 catapult neutrons per fission, corresponding to about 2.7% of the total prompt neutron multiplicity (ν̄ = 2.42).
- The initial central velocity of the bulge surface is approximately -0.2 v_F (where v_F ≈ 8.4 fm/10⁻²² s is the Fermi speed).
- The catapult neutrons dominate over evaporation neutrons above approximately 9.3 MeV for 235U(n_th,f).

## Context

This work extends the "catapult" mechanism first proposed by Mädler (1986) using one-dimensional Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock calculations, and complements recent Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) studies by Abdurrahman et al. (2024) on 252Cf(sf), 236U, and 240Pu, which found scission neutron multiplicities of 9-14% with mean energies around 3 MeV. The present classical-trajectory approach allows more detailed exploration of parameter dependence than the computationally demanding TDDFT method, and the results support the experimental findings of Schulc et al. (2023) that very energetic neutrons dominate the spectrum above about 10 MeV.
