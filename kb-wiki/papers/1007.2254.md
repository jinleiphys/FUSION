# Lowest eigenvalue of the nuclear shell model Hamiltonian

> In this paper we investigate regular patterns of matrix elements of the nuclear shell model Hamiltonian $H$, by sorting the diagonal matrix elements from the smaller to larger values. By using simple plots of non-zero matrix elements and lowest eigenvalues of artificially constructed "sub-matrices" $h$ of $H$, we propose a new and simple formula which predicts the lowest eigenvalue with remarkable precisions.

# Lowest eigenvalue of the nuclear shell model Hamiltonian

## Key claim

The paper proposes a simple formula that predicts the lowest eigenvalue of a nuclear shell model Hamiltonian matrix with remarkable precision, but only under the empirical assumptions that the critical dimension where the slope changes equals the distance at which off-diagonal matrix elements become zero (d0 = D0) and that the slope ratio k'/k equals 2/5 for all cases, with considerable fluctuations observed in the data.

## Method

The authors sort the diagonal matrix elements of the nuclear shell model Hamiltonian H^(I) from smallest to largest, then construct sub-matrices h of dimension d by truncating H^(I) to its first d rows and columns. They compute the lowest eigenvalue ε_d of each sub-matrix and plot ε_d versus ln d, observing two linear regions separated at a critical dimension D0. They also compute ρ(d), the probability that off-diagonal matrix elements at distance d from the diagonal are non-zero, and identify d0 where ρ(d0)=0. The lowest eigenvalue is then predicted using the formula E_min^(I) = (2k/5) ln D + (3k/5) ln d0 + b, where k and b are extracted from sub-matrices with d ≤ D/10.

## Key numbers

- For the 24Mg nucleus, ln d0 equals 6.75 for Iπ=0+ states and 8.22 for Iπ=2+ states.
- The overall root-mean-squared deviation for two-hundred shell model Hamiltonians of sd-shell nuclei is 0.38 MeV, with relative deviation about 0.003.
- The empirical slope ratio k'/k is approximately 2/5, with considerable fluctuations across 200 examples.
- The ratio ln(D0)/ln(d0) is very close to 1.0 for all 200 examples tested.
- The dimension D of the matrices studied ranges from 500 to 10000.
- Sub-matrices h are constructed with dimension d ≤ D/10 to extract k and b.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior approach (Shen, 2009) which used linear correlation between sorted diagonal matrix elements and eigenvalues but deviated for the lowest eigenvalues. It contrasts with statistical formulas by Ratcliff (1971), Margetan (1981), Papenbrock (2000), Yoshinaga (2002), and Shen (2008) that predict the lowest eigenvalue from the energy centroid and spectral moments but apply only to random ensemble averages, not to individual realistic Hamiltonians. The new formula enables prediction of the lowest eigenvalue for realistic shell model calculations using sub-matrices much smaller than the full Hamiltonian, potentially overcoming limitations of diagonalizing very large matrices.
