# Semi-analytic equations to the Cox-Thompson inverse scattering method at fixed energy for special cases

> Solution of the Cox-Thompson inverse scattering problem at fixed energy [1,2,3] is reformulated resulting in semi-analytic equations. The new set of equations for the normalization constants and the nonphysical (shifted) angular momenta are free of matrix inversion operations. This simplification is a result of treating only the input phase shifts of partial waves of a given parity. Therefore, the proposed method can be applied for identical particle scattering of the bosonic type (or for certain cases of identical fermionic scattering). The new formulae are expected to be numerically more efficient than the previous ones. Based on the semi-analytic equations an approximate method is proposed for the generic inverse scattering problem, when partial waves of arbitrary parity are considered.

# Semi-analytic equations to the Cox-Thompson inverse scattering method at fixed energy for special cases

## Key claim

The Cox-Thompson inverse scattering equations at fixed energy can be reformulated into semi-analytic forms that eliminate matrix inversion operations, but only when the input phase shifts correspond to partial waves of a single parity (all even or all odd). For the generic case with mixed parity partial waves, the authors propose an approximate method by summing separately computed even and odd parity potentials.

## Method

The authors start from the Cox-Thompson (CT) inverse scattering method at fixed energy, which uses a separable ansatz for the transformation kernel in the Gel'fand-Levitan-type integral equation. They derive explicit analytic solutions for the asymptotic expansion coefficients by applying two lemmas about rational function systems, which become tractable when the physical angular momenta are restricted to either even or odd values only. The resulting equations (Eqs. 18 and 19 in the paper) express the input phase shifts directly in terms of products over the unknown shifted angular momenta, without requiring matrix inversion. For the generic case, the authors propose approximating the full potential as the sum of separately computed even-parity and odd-parity potentials.

## Key numbers

- Input data: neutron scattering by $^{12}$C target nucleus at laboratory energy $E_{\rm{n}}^{\rm{lab}}=12$ MeV (center-of-mass energy $E_{\rm{n}}^{\rm{c.m.}}=11.08$ MeV, wave number $k=0.766$ fm$^{-1}$)
- For the $l=0$ partial wave: original phase shift $\delta_0^{\rm orig}=0.522$ rad, elasticity $\eta_0^{\rm orig}=0.580$; the general CT method reproduces this with absolute difference $\Delta_0^{\rm g}=0.042$ rad, the approximate method with $\Delta_0^{\rm a}=0.039$ rad
- For the $l=1$ partial wave: original phase shift $\delta_1^{\rm orig}=-0.737$ rad, elasticity $\eta_1^{\rm orig}=1.000$; general method gives $\Delta_1^{\rm g}=0.044$ rad, approximate method gives $\Delta_1^{\rm a}=0.303$ rad
- For the $l=4$ partial wave: original phase shift $\delta_4^{\rm orig}=0.021$ rad, elasticity $\eta_4^{\rm orig}=0.831$; general method gives $\Delta_4^{\rm g}=0.093$ rad, approximate method gives $\Delta_4^{\rm a}=0.018$ rad
- Normalization constants in both even and odd cases are $B_l=[\cos(\delta_l)]^{-1}$

## Context

This work extends the Cox-Thompson inverse scattering method at fixed energy, originally developed by Cox (1970), by providing simplified equations for the special case of identical particle scattering. The general CT method requires solving highly nonlinear equations involving matrix inversion, which previous implementations by Apagyi (2003) and Melchert (2006) addressed with sophisticated numerical techniques like simulated annealing (Schumayer, 2008). The new semi-analytic equations enable easier solution for bosonic scattering (even partial waves) and certain fermionic cases (odd partial waves). The approximate method for generic scattering, demonstrated on neutron-12C phase shift data from Chen and Thornow (2005), provides a computationally simpler alternative for obtaining a first orientation about the underlying interaction, though with reduced accuracy compared to the full CT method. The authors note that a fully general semi-analytic method for all partial angular momenta remains an open problem for future study.
