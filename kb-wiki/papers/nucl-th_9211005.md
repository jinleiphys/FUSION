# Self-consistent anisotropic oscillator with cranked angular and vortex velocities

> The Kelvin circulation is the kinematical Hermitian observable that measures the true character of nuclear rotation. For the anisotropic oscillator, mean field solutions with fixed angular momentum and Kelvin circulation are derived in analytic form. The cranking Lagrange multipliers corresponding to the two constraints are the angular and vortex velocities. Self-consistent solutions are reported with a constraint to constant volume.

## Key claim

For the anisotropic oscillator potential, mean-field solutions with fixed angular momentum and Kelvin circulation can be derived analytically, and at small cranking velocities these self-consistent quantum solutions correspond exactly to classical Riemann rotors, providing a physical interpretation for the angular and vortex velocities as cranking Lagrange multipliers.

## Method

The paper formulates a cranked anisotropic oscillator Hamiltonian with both angular velocity ω and vortex velocity λ as Lagrange multipliers constraining angular momentum L and Kelvin circulation C. Self-consistent solutions are obtained by minimizing the intrinsic energy in the rotating frame subject to constant volume and requiring the oscillator frequencies to satisfy ω_x N_x = ω_y N_y = ω_z N_z, where N_k are the total quanta in each direction. Exact analytic eigenvalues of the Routhian are derived via a canonical boson transformation, and expectations of position operators, angular momentum, and circulation are computed using Feynman's lemma.

- For 166Er: classical formula error is less than 0.03% up to L=8; axes lengths constant up to L=8
- For 156Gd: projected Hartree-Fock calculations give rigidity r ~ 0.12, compared to r ~ 0.15 from measured moment of inertia and deformation

## Context

This work extends the classical Riemann rotor model of Chandrasekhar (1969) and Lebovitz (1967) to quantum mean-field theory, building on the Inglis cranking formalism (1954) and the algebraic GCM(3) framework of Rosensteel (1988). The paper shows that GCM(3) symmetry is not realized in real nuclei because the Kelvin circulation Casimir is not constant across rotational bands, unlike the ROT(3) Casimirs that measure deformation. The results enable interpretation of transverse E2 form factors from inelastic electron scattering in terms of the rigidity parameter r, as proposed by Moya de Guerra (1986) and Vassanji and Rowe (1987), and provide a method to extract the Kelvin circulation from experimental data.
