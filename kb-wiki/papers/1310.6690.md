# Universality in Similarity Renormalization Group Evolved Potential Matrix Elements and T-Matrix Equivalence

> We examine how the universality of two-nucleon interactions evolved using similarity renormalization group (SRG) transformations correlates with T-matrix equivalence, with the ultimate goal of gaining insight into universality for three-nucleon forces. With sufficient running of the SRG flow equations, the low-energy matrix elements of different realistic potentials evolve to a universal form. Because these potentials are fit to low-energy data, they are (approximately) phase equivalent only up to a certain energy, and we find universality in evolved potentials up to the corresponding momentum. More generally we find universality in local energy regions, reflecting a local decoupling by the SRG. The further requirements for universality in evolved potential matrix elements are explored using two simple alternative potentials. We see evidence that in addition to predicting the same observables, common long-range potentials (i.e., explicit pion physics) is required for universality in the potential matrix elements after SRG flow. In agreement with observations made previously for Vlowk evolution, regions of universal potential matrix elements are restricted to where half-on-shell T-matrix equivalence holds.

## Key claim

The universality of two-nucleon potential matrix elements after similarity renormalization group (SRG) evolution is restricted to regions where half-on-shell T-matrix equivalence holds, and in addition to predicting the same observables, common long-range potentials (explicit pion physics) are required for universality in the potential matrix elements after SRG flow.

## Method

The authors evolved several realistic two-nucleon potentials (Argonne v18 and multiple N3LO chiral effective field theory potentials with various cutoffs) using SRG transformations with both the kinetic energy generator and the block-diagonal generator. They constructed rank-1 inverse scattering separable potentials (ISSP) from AV18 phase shifts to test whether phase equivalence alone is sufficient for universality, and also used a delta-shell plus one-pion exchange potential to test the role of explicit long-range physics. The spread of diagonal and off-diagonal potential matrix elements was compared across different potentials at various SRG cutoff values, and correlated with the regions of phase shift equivalence.

## Key numbers

- SRG evolution to lambda = 1.5 fm^-1 causes diagonal matrix elements of different realistic potentials to collapse to a universal form at low momentum
- Phase equivalence among realistic potentials ends in a region characterized by 0.03 < epsilon(k) < 0.1, where epsilon(k) is the normalized range of phase shifts at fixed momentum
- An ISSP with binding energy of 0 MeV (instead of the physical 2.224 MeV) shows substantial differences in low-energy diagonal matrix elements even after SRG evolution
- An ISSP with binding energy of 5 MeV reproduces this energy to better than 100 eV and shows forced lower lowest-momentum matrix elements compared to the physical deuteron binding energy case
- For the block-diagonal generator with Lambda less than or equal to 2 fm^-1, only diagonal matrix elements below Lambda become universal, with a different flow than matrix elements evolved with the T generator

## Context

This work extends the observations of Bogner and collaborators (2001) on the model independence of Vlowk potentials, who found that low-momentum matrix elements match in regions of phase equivalence while off-diagonal elements match in regions of half-on-shell T-matrix equivalence. The study uses SRG transformations (Glazek and Wilson, 1993; Wegner, 1994) rather than Lee-Suzuki transformations, and finds that universality requires not just phase equivalence but also correct binding energy and explicit long-range pion physics. This enables future investigation of whether three-nucleon forces also flow to a universal form under SRG evolution, which would simplify construction of accurate many-body potentials.
