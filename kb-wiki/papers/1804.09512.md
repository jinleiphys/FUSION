# Exploring the applicability of dissipative fluid dynamics to small systems by comparison to the Boltzmann equation

> [Background] Experimental data from heavy-ion experiments at RHIC-BNL and LHC-CERN are quantitatively described using relativistic fluid dynamics. Even p+A and p+p collisions show signs of collective behavior describable in the same manner. Nevertheless, small system sizes and large gradients strain the limits of applicability of fluid-dynamical methods. [Purpose] The range of applicability of fluid dynamics for the description of the collective behavior, and in particular of the elliptic flow, of small systems needs to be explored. [Method] Results of relativistic fluid-dynamical simulations are compared with solutions of the Boltzmann equation in a longitudinally boost-invariant picture. As initial condition, several different transverse energy-density profiles for equilibrated matter are investigated. [Results] While there is overall a fair agreement of energy- and particle-density profiles, components of the shear-stress tensor are more sensitive to details of the implementation. The highest sensitivity is exhibited by quantities influenced by properties of the medium at freeze-out. [Conclusions] For some quantities, like the shear-stress tensor, agreement between fluid dynamics and transport theory extends into regions of Knudsen numbers and inverse Reynolds numbers where relativistic fluid dynamics is believed to fail.

## Key claim

For certain quantities such as the shear-stress tensor, agreement between Israel-Stewart fluid dynamics and the full Boltzmann equation persists into regimes of Knudsen number and inverse Reynolds number where relativistic fluid dynamics is conventionally expected to break down, though the comparison is limited to a longitudinally boost-invariant picture with thermal initial conditions.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Boltzmann equation using the BAMPS (Boltzmann Approach to Multi-Particle Collisions) framework for a massless classical gas with constant isotropic cross sections, under a (2+1)-dimensional boost-invariant geometry. They compared these results to numerical solutions of the Israel-Stewart fluid-dynamical equations (14-moment approximation) solved via the SHASTA algorithm, using several different transverse energy-density profiles (Gaussian with widths 1 fm and 3 fm, and a binary-collision Glauber profile) initialized at proper time τ₀ = 0.2 fm with a maximum central temperature T₀ = 500 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Initialization proper time τ₀ = 0.2 fm; maximum central temperature T₀ = 500 MeV
- Gaussian width parameters w = 1 fm (p+p or p+A systems) and w = 3 fm (event-averaged A+A systems)
- Impact parameter b = 7.5 fm for the binary-collision Glauber profile (nBC scenario)
- Constant isotropic cross sections varied from σ = 1 mb to σ = 20 mb
- Degeneracy factor g = 16 for the ultrarelativistic Maxwell-Boltzmann gas
- Shear viscosity-to-entropy density ratio η/s = (4/3)(T/σ) / [e^(α₀)(4 - α₀)(g/π²)T³] with α₀ = 0
- Transport coefficients in the 14-moment approximation: shear viscosity η = 4/(3σβ₀), shear relaxation time τ_π/λ_mfp = 5/3, shear-viscosity coefficient κ_n = 3/(16σ), diffusion relaxation time τ_n/λ_mfp = 9/4

## Context

This work extends prior comparisons between fluid dynamics and the Boltzmann equation, which had been limited to (0+1)-dimensional boost-invariant expansion (Huovinen, 2008; Denicol, 2010; Kurkela, 2015) or (1+1)-dimensional shock waves (Bouras, 2009; Bouras, 2010; Denicol, 2012). By incorporating transverse expansion and azimuthal structure, the study enables direct access to experimental observables such as transverse-momentum spectra and elliptic flow coefficients, allowing tests of fluid-dynamical applicability for small systems like those formed in p+p and p+A collisions.
