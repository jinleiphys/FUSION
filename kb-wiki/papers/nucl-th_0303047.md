# Saturation of nuclear matter and short-range correlations

> A fully self-consistent treatment of short-range correlations in nuclear matter is presented. Different implementations of the determination of the nucleon spectral functions for different interactions are shown to be consistent with each other. The resulting saturation densities are closer to the empirical result when compared with (continuous-choice) Brueckner-Hartree-Fock values. Arguments for the dominance of short-range correlations in determining the nuclear-matter saturation density are presented. A further survey of the role of long-range correlations suggests that the inclusion of pionic contributions to ring diagrams in nuclear matter leads to higher saturation densities than empirically observed. A possible resolution of the nuclear-matter saturation problem is suggested.

# Saturation of nuclear matter and short-range correlations

## Key claim

A fully self-consistent treatment of short-range correlations (SRC) in nuclear matter yields saturation densities closer to the empirical value than continuous-choice Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (ccBHF) calculations, but the authors suggest that the long-standing saturation problem may be resolved by questioning whether coherent long-range pion-exchange contributions to the binding energy, which are present in infinite nuclear matter but absent in finite nuclei, should be included at all.

## Method

The authors solve the ladder equation for the effective in-medium interaction using a self-consistency loop that couples the two-particle propagator (built from spectral functions) to the Dyson equation for the single-particle propagator. Two numerical implementations are compared: a continuous scheme representing the imaginary part of the self-energy with four Gaussians (for the Reid68 potential) and a discrete scheme representing the propagator with three poles (for Reid93, NijmI, NijmII, and separable Paris interactions). The resulting spectral functions are used to compute the energy per particle via the energy sum rule.

## Key numbers

- Empirical saturation density corresponds to a central density of 0.16 nucleons/fm³ or Fermi momentum k_F = 1.33 fm⁻¹ with a binding energy of about 16 MeV.
- For the Argonne v14 interaction, ccBHF gives a saturation density corresponding to k_F = 1.565 fm⁻¹, overestimating the empirical density by about 60%.
- Binding energies from the discrete and continuous self-consistent schemes agree to within 5% over the relevant k_F range around the minimum, and the location of the minimum agrees to within 3%.
- Self-consistent SRC calculations yield 4-6 MeV less binding than ccBHF and reduced saturation densities closer to the empirical value.
- For the Reid93 interaction at k_F = 1.37 fm⁻¹, the quasiparticle strength at the Fermi momentum is z = 0.74, and the hole strength for p = 0 integrated up to 100 MeV missing energy equals 83%.
- The nuclear matter compression modulus K_nm from ccBHF is 154 MeV for Reid93 and 148 MeV for separable Paris; the self-consistent SRC values are 177 MeV and 216 MeV respectively, compared to the experimental estimate of 210 ± 30 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent treatment of short-range correlations pioneered by the authors' earlier work (Dewulf et al., 2002) and by Bozek (1999, 2002), and contrasts with converged three-hole-line Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone calculations (Day, 1981; Baldo et al., 1990) that overestimate saturation density. The authors argue that long-range pion-exchange ring diagrams (Dickhoff et al., 1981) provide attractive contributions in nuclear matter that are not present in finite nuclei, based on experimental evidence from (e,e'p) reactions on ²⁰⁸Pb (Lapikas et al., 1999) and spin-response measurements (Carey et al., 1984; Taddeucci et al., 1994). The results enable a possible resolution of the nuclear-matter saturation problem by questioning whether such long-range correlations should be included when comparing to empirical saturation from finite nuclei.
