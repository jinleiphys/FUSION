# Simultaneous fitting of statistical-model parameters to symmetric and asymmetric fission cross sections

> The de-excitation of compound nuclei has been successfully described for several decades by means of statistical models. However, accurate predictions require some fine-tuning of the model parameters. This task can be simplified by studying several entrance channels, which populate different regions of the parameter space of the compound nucleus. Fusion reactions play an important role in this strategy because they minimise the uncertainty on the entrance channel by fixing mass, charge and excitation energy of the compound nucleus. If incomplete fusion is negligible, the only uncertainty on the compound nucleus comes from the spin distribution. However, some de-excitation channels, such as fission, are quite sensitive to spin. Other entrance channels can then be used to discriminate between equivalent parameter sets. The focus of this work is on fission and intermediate-mass-fragment emission cross sections of compound nuclei with 70<=A<=240. The statistical de-excitation model is GEMINI++. The choice of the observables is natural in the framework of GEMINI++, which describes fragment emission using a fission-like formalism. Equivalent parameter sets for fusion reactions can be resolved using the spallation entrance channel. This promising strategy can lead to the identification of a minimal set of physical ingredients necessary for a unified quantitative description of nuclear de-excitation.

## Key claim

The degeneracy of statistical-model parameter sets that describe fusion-induced fission and intermediate-mass-fragment (IMF) emission can be largely lifted by simultaneously fitting the same parameters to spallation-induced reactions, but the value of the level-density-parameter ratio \(a_\text{IMF}/a_\text{n}\) for IMF emission cannot be uniquely fixed until the discrepancy between two existing measurements of 1-GeV p+\(^{136}\)Xe charge distributions is resolved.

## Method

The authors used the GEMINI++ statistical de-excitation model to calculate fission and IMF-emission cross sections for compound nuclei with \(70 \lesssim A \lesssim 240\). Fusion entrance channels were modeled with a triangular spin distribution, while spallation entrance channels were described by the Liège Intranuclear-Cascade model (INCL). Parameter sets (barrier shifts \(\Delta B\), level-density-parameter ratios \(a_\text{f}/a_\text{n}\) and \(a_\text{IMF}/a_\text{n}\), and tilting corrections) were fitted simultaneously to fusion and spallation data for fission and IMF charge distributions.

## Key numbers

* Fitted barrier shifts \(\Delta B\) for IMF emission from fusion reactions show a linear dependence on the fissility parameter \(Z^2/A\) of the compound nucleus; for \(^{76}\)Se a shift of 8.5 MeV to Sierk's barriers was required, and for \(^{98}\)Mo a shift of 7 MeV was required.
* The ratio of level-density parameters at the conditional saddle point and in the ground state for IMF emission, \(a_\text{IMF}/a_\text{n}\), was fitted to values ranging from 1.015 to 1.035, depending on which spallation data set was used.
* For fission, the combined fusion/spallation approach produced predictive parameter sets for compound nuclei in the mass range \(155 \lesssim A \lesssim 225\), with some overestimation of fission cross sections for \(A < 170\).
* The average excitation energy of events producing fragments with \(10 < Z < 30\) in 1-GeV p+\(^{136}\)Xe spallation was \(\langle E^*\rangle = 411\) MeV, with the distribution extending up to \(\sim 750\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the strategy of combining fusion and spallation entrance channels, previously applied to fission by Mancusi et al. (2012), to the study of intermediate-mass-fragment emission. The approach resolves degeneracies in parameter sets that arise when fitting only fusion data, as demonstrated by the four equivalent parameter sets for \(^{200}\)Pb fission that are distinguished by spallation data. The results enable a more constrained determination of the minimal physical ingredients needed for a unified description of nuclear de-excitation, though the physical interpretation of the required barrier shift (possibly related to Sierk's finite-range corrections or the Wigner energy) remains ambiguous.
