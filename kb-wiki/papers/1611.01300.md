# Symmetry-unrestricted Skyrme mean-field study of heavy nuclei

> In the light of recent experimental developments, increasing attention is devoted to nuclear phenomena related to rotational excitations of exotic intrinsic nuclear configurations that often lack symmetries present in the majority of nuclei. Examples include configurations with a non-vanishing octupole moment. In order to describe this kind of states, we have developed a new computer code to solve the self-consistent mean-field equations, able to use most of today's effective Skyrme interactions and working in coordinate-space. We report on the development of MOCCa, a code based on the same principles as EV8, but offering the user individual control on many symmetry assumptions. In addition, the HF+BCS pairing treatment of EV8 has been generalised to the full machinery of Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov transformations. We discuss as example the static fission barrier of $^{226}$Ra, prefacing extended studies in the region, using the recent series of Skyrme parameterizations SLy5s1 through SLy5s8.

## Key claim

The new code MOCCa enables symmetry-unrestricted Skyrme mean-field calculations with full Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov pairing, but the authors note that the excellent reproduction of the fission barrier height of $^{226}$Ra (8.31 MeV versus the experimental 8.5 MeV) requires not only the code's flexibility but also a properly fine-tuned energy density functional, specifically the SLy5s1 parameterization with its particular surface tension.

## Method

The authors developed MOCCa, a computer code that solves the self-consistent mean-field equations on a cubic Lagrange mesh in coordinate space, generalizing the earlier EV8 code. MOCCa allows the user to conserve any of 16 subgroups of the symmetry groups $D^{T}_{2h}$ (for even-A nuclei) or $D^{TD}_{2h}$ (for odd-A nuclei), and implements the full Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) pairing treatment with a density-dependent zero-range pairing interaction and the Lipkin-Nogami prescription. As a demonstration, the code was used to calculate the static fission barrier of $^{226}$Ra as a function of the deformation parameter $\beta_{20}$ for eight Skyrme parameterizations (SLy5s1 through SLy5s8) that differ only in their surface energy coefficient.

## Key numbers

- The fission barrier height of $^{226}$Ra calculated with SLy5s1 is 8.31 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 8.5 MeV from the RIPL-3 database.
- The pairing strengths used are $(V_n, V_p) = (1000, 1450)$ MeV fm$^3$, determined by adjusting to the three-point proton and neutron gap of $^{216}$Ra for SLy5s1.
- The surface energy coefficients $a_{\text{surf}}^{\text{MTF}}$ for the SLy5sX series range from 18.0 MeV (SLy5s1) to 19.4 MeV (SLy5s8) in steps of 0.2 MeV.
- Barrier heights for the eight functionals increase systematically: SLy5s1 (8.31 MeV at $\beta_{20}=1.19$), SLy5s2 (9.03 MeV at $\beta_{20}=1.19$), SLy5s3 (10.21 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.23$), SLy5s4 (11.59 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.29$), SLy5s5 (13.01 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.35$), SLy5s6 (14.47 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.41$), SLy5s7 (15.99 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.47$), SLy5s8 (17.53 MeV at $\beta_{20}=2.52$).
- The octupole deformation lowers the energy of the ground-state minimum of $^{226}$Ra by slightly more than 1 MeV relative to the parity-conserving configuration.
- The pairing cutoff was introduced symmetric around the Fermi energy at 5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the capabilities of the EV8 code (Bonche et al., 2005; Ryssens et al., 2015) by providing user-controlled symmetry breaking and full HFB pairing, complementing the harmonic-oscillator-basis code HFODD (Dobaczewski et al., 2000) which offers similar flexibility. The results for $^{226}$Ra are compared to earlier relativistic mean-field calculations by Rutz et al. (1995) that used HF+BCS pairing without triaxial degrees of freedom; both approaches find similar minima but differ in the relative energy of symmetric and asymmetric fission paths at large deformations. The systematic study with the SLy5sX family of functionals (Jodon et al., 2016) demonstrates that the surface tension must be carefully tuned to reproduce fission barriers at the mean-field level, and that the same qualitative variation of barrier heights with surface tension is found for other nuclei.
