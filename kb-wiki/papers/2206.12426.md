# Towards Powerful Probes of Neutrino Self-Interactions in Supernovae

> Neutrinos remain mysterious. As an example, enhanced self-interactions ($ν$SI), which would have broad implications, are allowed. At the high neutrino densities within core-collapse supernovae, $ν$SI should be important, but robust observables have been lacking. We show that $ν$SI make neutrinos form a tightly coupled fluid that expands under relativistic hydrodynamics. The outflow becomes either a burst or a steady-state wind; which occurs here is uncertain. Though the diffusive environment where neutrinos are produced may make a wind more likely, further work is needed to determine when each case is realized. In the burst-outflow case, $ν$SI increase the duration of the neutrino signal, and even a simple analysis of SN 1987A data has powerful sensitivity. For the wind-outflow case, we outline several promising ideas that may lead to new observables. Combined, these results are important steps towards solving the 35-year-old puzzle of how $ν$SI affect supernovae.

## Key claim

The paper shows that enhanced neutrino self-interactions (nuSI) cause neutrinos to form a tightly coupled fluid that expands under relativistic hydrodynamics, but which outflow case (burst or steady-state wind) actually occurs remains uncertain; although the diffusive environment where neutrinos are produced may make a wind more likely, further work is needed to determine when each case is realized.

## Method

The authors model neutrino self-interactions outside the proto-neutron star (PNS) using relativistic hydrodynamics, considering two possible outflow regimes: a burst-like homogeneous expansion and a steady-state wind analogous to a relativistic fireball. For the burst-outflow case, they compute the optical depth for neutrino-neutrino scattering as a function of the expanding neutrino ball radius, then relate the decoupling radius (where optical depth becomes small) to the observed neutrino signal duration. They compare this predicted duration to the SN 1987A neutrino data from Kamiokande-II and IMB, assuming a common start time for the detected events.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino number density in supernovae is greater than 10^36 cm^-3
- PNS mass is approximately 1.5 solar masses, radius approximately 10 km
- Gravitational binding energy released is approximately 3 x 10^53 ergs
- Average neutrino energy outside the PNS is approximately 10 MeV
- Without nuSI, neutrino signal duration is approximately 10 s, corresponding to shell thickness l0 approximately c times 10 s
- With strong nuSI in the burst-outflow case, the neutrino mean free path is initially on the micrometer scale
- Conservative analysis excludes nuSI leading to decoupling radius l_FS/c greater than 30 s
- Estimated sensitivity could probe down to l_FS/c approximately 3 s
- For resonant nuSI with mediator mass M_phi approximately 10 MeV, sensitivity to coupling g is approximately 6 x 10^-5 times (tau_FS/10)^(1/2) times (l_FS/c / 30 s) times (M_phi/10 MeV)
- For M_phi = 10 MeV and g approximately 10^-3, signal duration would be 10 minutes

## Context

This work re-examines a 35-year-old puzzle about how neutrino self-interactions affect supernovae, following the debate between Manohar (1987), who claimed nuSI would hinder neutrino escape, and Dicus et al. (1988), who argued a coupled fluid expands regardless of interaction strength. The paper extends prior work by showing that in the burst-outflow case, the observed signal duration depends on the nuSI cross section through the decoupling radius, enabling new sensitivity. This enables future detailed reanalysis of SN 1987A data and preparation for interpreting data from the next galactic supernova, as well as motivating further work on the wind-outflow case and effects inside or near the PNS.
