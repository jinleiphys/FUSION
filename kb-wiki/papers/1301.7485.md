# Evidence of alpha particle condensation in $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O and Nambu-Goldstone boson

> It is suggested that direct evidence of Bose-Einstein condensation of alpha particles is obtained by observing a phase mode (Nambu-Goldstone boson) with long wavelength even when characteristic features such as superfluidity is diffucult to observe. For the 7.65 MeV $0_2^+$ Hoyle state in $^{12}$C and 15.1 MeV $0^+$ state in $^{16}$O, which are candidates for an alpha particle condensate, it is suggested that the emergent band head $0^+$ state of the $K=0_2^+$ rotational band with a very large moment of inertia is considered to be a Nambu-Goldstone boson.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that direct evidence for alpha particle condensation in the Hoyle state of $^{12}$C and the 15.1 MeV $0^+$ state of $^{16}$O can be obtained by observing a Nambu-Goldstone (NG) boson collective zero mode, even when characteristic features such as superfluidity are difficult to observe. The claim is conditional: the NG boson interpretation requires that the band head $0^+$ state of the $K=0_2^+$ rotational band with a very large moment of inertia is considered to be the NG boson.

## Method

The author analyzes existing experimental data and theoretical calculations for $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O, comparing the rotational band structures built on candidate alpha condensate states. For $^{16}$O, the author examines the $K=0_2^+$ rotational band predicted by Ohkubo and Hirabayashi (2010) using a coupled-channel method for $\alpha$+$^{12}$C elastic and inelastic scattering, and compares it with newly observed $0^+$ states from Itoh et al. (2012). For $^{12}$C, the author reinterprets the $2_2^+$ (9.63 MeV) and $4_1^+$ (13.3 MeV) states as members of a rotational band built on a NG boson $0^+$ state, using results from ab initio calculations by Kanada-En'yo (2007) and three-body resonance calculations by Kurokawa and Kato (2005, 2007).

## Key numbers

- In $^{16}$O: the 15.1 MeV $0^+$ state is the candidate four-alpha condensate; the predicted NG boson $0^+$ state at 16.6 MeV (excitation energy 1.5 MeV above the condensate) has a calculated alpha width of 1.1 MeV, and the newly observed $0^+$ state at 16.8 MeV with width about 1 MeV agrees precisely with this prediction.
- In $^{12}$C: the Hoyle state is at $E_x$ = 7.65 MeV; the $2_2^+$ state is at 9.63 MeV and the $4_1^+$ state is at 13.3 MeV; the candidate NG boson $0_3^+$ state is observed at 9.04 MeV with width 1.45 MeV (excitation energy 1.28 MeV above the Hoyle state), and a $0_4^+$ state is observed at 10.56 MeV with width 1.42 MeV.
- For $^{12}$C ab initio calculations: intraband $B(E2)$ values are 600 $e^2$fm$^4$ for $4_1^+ \rightarrow 2_2^+$ and 310 $e^2$fm$^4$ for $2_2^+ \rightarrow 0_3^+$, while the $B(E2)$ from $0_3^+$ to the Hoyle state is 100 $e^2$fm$^4$.
- The estimated moment of inertia connecting the Hoyle state and the $2_2^+$ (9.63 MeV) state is about 80% of that estimated from the $2_2^+$ (9.63 MeV) and $4_1^+$ (13.3 MeV) states.

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of alpha cluster states in light nuclei as Bose-Einstein condensates, building on the theoretical framework of Tohsaki et al. (2001) who first proposed alpha condensation, and the coupled-channel calculations of Ohkubo and Hirabayashi (2010) for $^{16}$O. The paper directly addresses the experimental controversy between Raduta et al. (2011), who claimed observation of three simultaneous alpha particles from the Hoyle state, and Manfredi et al. (2012), who set an upper limit of 0.45% probability for such events, confirming the earlier null result of Freer et al. (1994). The NG boson interpretation provides a new experimental signature for alpha condensation that avoids the difficulties of observing superfluidity or vortices in finite nuclear systems, and the author suggests this mechanism should be explored in other nuclei.
