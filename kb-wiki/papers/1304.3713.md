# Computational Nuclear Quantum Many-Body Problem: The UNEDF Project

> The UNEDF project was a large-scale collaborative effort that applied high-performance computing to the nuclear quantum many-body problem. UNEDF demonstrated that close associations among nuclear physicists, mathematicians, and computer scientists can lead to novel physics outcomes built on algorithmic innovations and computational developments. This review showcases a wide range of UNEDF science results to illustrate this interplay.

## Key claim

The UNEDF project demonstrated that close collaborations among nuclear physicists, applied mathematicians, and computer scientists can produce novel physics results through algorithmic and computational innovations, but the review emphasizes that these outcomes required newly formed partnerships "across the divide" that were unique to the project and could not have been achieved without such couplings.

## Method

The UNEDF collaboration applied multiple ab initio methods (Green's function Monte Carlo, no-core shell model, coupled-cluster), configuration interaction techniques, and nuclear density functional theory to study nuclear structure across the chart of nuclides. The project integrated high-performance computing with algorithmic developments including asynchronous dynamic load balancing, scalable eigensolvers, and hybrid MPI/OpenMP parallelization, while implementing verification and validation through cross-checking of different theoretical methods and codes using the same Hamiltonians.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of $^{12}$C reproduced in GFMC calculations propagated to $\tau \approx 1.0\,\text{MeV}^{-1}$ imaginary time
- $^{14}$C beta decay lifetime of $5730 \pm 30$ years explained by 3-nucleon forces suppressing the $0p$-shell contributions to the Gamow-Teller matrix element
- MFDn calculations for $^{10}$B used 160 million basis dimensions with 5.2 trillion nonzero matrix elements, requiring at least 42 TB of memory and using between 30,624 and 261,120 cores
- Coupled-cluster model-space size increased from 10 to 20 oscillator shells during UNEDF, enabling problems requiring approximately 4,000 times more computational cycles
- ADLB library scaled GFMC to more than 32,000 processes on BG/P and more than 260,000 processes on BG/Q
- Coupled-cluster calculations for $^{40}$Ca in 12 oscillator shells showed hybrid MPI/OpenMP V2.0 scaling to thousands of cores versus a few hundred cores with MPI-only V1.0
- Predicted soft subshell closure at $N=32$ in $^{54}$Ca, with excitation energies of $J^\pi=2^+$ states agreeing with available data and later verified experimentally by Steppenbeck (2012)

## Context

This review summarizes the UNEDF project (2006-2012), which extended prior ab initio work by Pieper (2001) on light nuclei and Bertsch (2007) on the nuclear energy density functional vision, while enabling new capabilities for describing medium-mass and heavy nuclei. The project enabled first-principles coupled-cluster calculations of neutron-rich oxygen and calcium isotopes by Hagen (2012), NCSMC calculations of reactions important for solar models by Navratil (2011), and established a framework for uncertainty quantification and cross-method benchmarking that continues in follow-up projects.
