# Spectroscopic factor of the 1+, 25Al(p,g)26Si resonance at Ex=5.68 MeV

> Nuclear shell model predictions for the proton spectroscopic factor of the 1+, Ex = 5.68 MeV level in 26Si are about fifty times smaller than the value suggested by the measured (a,3He) cross section for the Ex = 5.69 MeV mirror level in 26Mg, assuming purely single-particle transfer. Given that the 5.69 MeV level has been very weakly, if it all, populated in previous studies of the simpler 25Mg(d,p) reaction, it is unclear if the (a,3He) result is a true single-particle spectroscopic factor. If we assume the (a,3He) result, the thermonuclear rate of the 25Al(p,g)26Si reaction would increase by factors of 6 - 50 over stellar temperatures of T = 0.05 - 0.2 GK. We examine the implications of this enhanced rate for model predictions of nucleosynthesis in classical nova explosions.

## Key claim

If the measured ($\alpha$,$^{3}$He) spectroscopic factor for the mirror state in $^{26}$Mg is adopted, the thermonuclear rate of the $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$)$^{26}$Si reaction would increase by factors of 6 to 50 at stellar temperatures of 0.05 to 0.2 GK; however, it is unclear whether the ($\alpha$,$^{3}$He) result represents a true single-particle spectroscopic factor, and even with this enhanced rate, the reaction does not compete effectively with the beta-decay of $^{25}$Al in classical nova models.

## Method

The authors compared shell model predictions for the proton spectroscopic factor of the 1+, 5.68 MeV resonance in $^{26}$Si with the experimental value inferred from the $^{25}$Mg($\alpha$,$^{3}$He)$^{26}$Mg measurement of Yasue et al. (1990) for the mirror state at 5.69 MeV in $^{26}$Mg. They then performed two hydrodynamic simulations of a classical nova explosion on a 1.25 solar mass oxygen-neon white dwarf, one using the standard tabulated rate from Wrede (2009) and the other using an enhanced rate based on the larger spectroscopic factor, to test the impact on nucleosynthesis yields.

## Key numbers

- Shell model calculations give $S_{l=2} = 0.0040$ (Iliadis 1996), $S_{l=2} = 0.0048$ (Matic 2010), and $S_{l=2} = 0.0035$ (Richter 2011) for the 1+, 5.68 MeV state in $^{26}$Si; Yasue et al. (1990) measured $S_{l=2} = 0.20$ for the mirror state at 5.69 MeV in $^{26}$Mg, a factor of about 50 larger.
- At T = 0.1 GK, the tabulated total rate from Wrede (2009) is $3.0 \times 10^{-11}$ cm$^{3}$ s$^{-1}$ mol$^{-1}$; using the Yasue et al. spectroscopic factor gives a total rate of $1.5 \times 10^{-9}$ cm$^{3}$ s$^{-1}$ mol$^{-1}$.
- At T = 0.1 GK, the enhanced rate gives a $^{25}$Al half-life for proton capture of about $10^{6}$ s; at T = 0.2 GK, about 200 s; compared to the beta-decay half-life of $^{25}$Al of 7.2 s.
- Predicted nucleosynthesis yields from the two nova simulations agreed to better than about 5%.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing effort to determine charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates for classical nova explosions, following compilations by Iliadis (2010) and Wrede (2009). The paper highlights a discrepancy between shell model predictions and the experimental result of Yasue et al. (1990) for the 1+ resonance, noting that the 5.69 MeV state in $^{26}$Mg was very weakly populated in simpler $^{25}$Mg(d,p) measurements (Arch 1984, Bur 1984). The authors conclude that the $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$) rate is not a major source of uncertainty for nova nucleosynthesis models and suggest that future experimental efforts should focus on constraining the 3+ resonance contribution instead.
