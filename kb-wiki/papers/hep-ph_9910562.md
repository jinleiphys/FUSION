# Stochastic treatment of Disoriented Chiral Condensates within a Langevin description

> Applying a microscopically motivated semi-classical Langevin description of the linear sigma model we investigate for various different scenarios the stochastic evolution of a disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in a rapidly expanding system. Some particular emphasize is put on the numerical realisation of colored noise in order to treat the underlying dissipative and nonmarkovian stochastic equations of motion. A comparison with an approximate markovian (i.e. instantaneous) treatment of dissipation and noise will be made in order to identify the possible influence of memory effects in the evolution of the chiral order parameter. Assuming a standard Rayleigh cooling term to simulate a D-dimensional scaling expansion we present the probability distribution in the low momentum pion number stemming from the relaxing zero mode component of the chiral field. The best DCC signal is expected for initial conditions centered around $<σ> \approx 0 $ as would be the case of effective light `pions' close to the phase transition. By choosing appropriate idealized global parameters for the expansion our findings show that an experimentally feasible DCC, if it does exist in nature, has to be a rare event with some finite probability following a nontrivial and nonpoissonian distribution on an event by event basis. DCCs might then be identified experimentally by inspecting higher order factorial cumulants $θ_m$ ($m\ge 3$) in the sampled distribution.

## Key claim

The paper argues that an experimentally observable disoriented chiral condensate (DCC), if it exists in nature, must be a rare event with finite probability, following a nontrivial and non-Poissonian distribution on an event-by-event basis, rather than a common occurrence; this conclusion is conditional on the choice of idealized global parameters for a three-dimensional spherical expansion and on initial conditions centered around ⟨σ⟩ ≈ 0.

## Method

The authors employed a microscopically motivated semi-classical Langevin description of the linear sigma model to simulate the stochastic evolution of a DCC in a rapidly expanding system, using a Rayleigh cooling term to model a D-dimensional scaling expansion. They implemented both non-Markovian (colored noise) and Markovian (instantaneous) treatments of dissipation and noise to compare memory effects, and calculated the probability distribution of the low-momentum pion number stemming from the relaxing zero-mode component of the chiral field. The damping coefficient was derived from the on-shell plasmon damping rate in the sunset diagram, with a maximum value near the critical temperature.

## Key numbers

- The linear sigma model parameters are: pion decay constant fπ = 93 MeV, vacuum pion mass mπ = 140 MeV, sigma meson mass mσ ≈ 600 MeV, coupling λ ≈ 20, v² = (87 MeV)², and H = (122 MeV)³.
- The critical temperature for the chiral phase transition (with zero vacuum pion mass) is Tc ≈ 125 MeV; using a large N-expansion thermal mass gives Tc ≈ 154 MeV.
- The damping coefficient η reaches a maximum value of approximately 100 MeV near the critical temperature, corresponding to relaxation times of roughly 2 fm/c.
- The effective transversal mass μ⊥ and longitudinal mass μ∥ are computed as functions of temperature; below T ≈ 100 MeV the longitudinal mass exceeds twice the transversal mass, enabling σ → ππ decay.

## Context

This work extends the quench scenario of Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993) and the annealing scenario of Gavin and Muller (1994) by incorporating a stochastic Langevin description that accounts for dissipation and fluctuations from the thermal pion bath, going beyond mean-field approximations. It builds on earlier Langevin studies by Biro and Greiner (1997) and Xu, Greiner, and Biro (1998) that investigated the stochastic formation of DCCs, and it provides a framework for identifying DCCs experimentally through higher-order factorial cumulants θm (m ≥ 3) in the sampled pion number distribution. The results enable quantitative predictions for the probability of DCC formation in ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC facilities.
