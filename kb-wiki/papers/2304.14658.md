# Conserved number fluctuations under global rotation in a hadron resonance gas model

> Net-baryon number, net-charge and net-strangeness fluctuations measured in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions may reveal details and insights into the quark-hadron transition, hadrochemical freeze-out and possibly aid in the search of the QCD critical point. By scanning in collision energy, current and upcoming heavy-ion facilities aim to explore the finite density regime where the critical point may lie. Effects due to rotation are also expected in case of peripheral collisions and we report on conserved number susceptibilities as calculated in the hadron resonance gas model augmented by a global angular velocity. Since these quantities are directly related to the experimentally measurable moments of the corresponding distributions our results show the possible impact of vorticity on the theoretical baseline and should be useful for referencing with experimental data and QCD-based calculations.

## Key claim

The authors report that global rotation (angular velocity omega) enhances the fluctuations (susceptibilities) of net-baryon number, net-charge, and net-strangeness in the hadron resonance gas model, but the effect is significant only at lower collision energies for baryon number fluctuations and is most pronounced for strangeness and electric charge in peripheral collisions; the results provide a theoretical baseline that may help interpret experimental data, but the authors note that the ideal HRG model omits repulsive interactions, finite longitudinal size, and inhomogeneity.

## Method

The authors augment the standard hadron resonance gas (HRG) model by imposing a rigid global rotation via a coordinate transformation to a rotating frame, using a vierbein formalism for fermions. The pressure is computed from the grand canonical partition function with single-particle energy levels modified by a term (l+s)omega that acts as an effective chemical potential, and a causality condition R*omega <= 1 is enforced by introducing an infrared cutoff on the radial momentum integration. Susceptibilities of baryon number, electric charge, and strangeness are obtained as derivatives of the scaled pressure with respect to the corresponding scaled chemical potentials, and are evaluated along a freeze-out curve defined by fixed entropy density s/T^3 = 7.0, using freeze-out parametrizations from Cleymans et al. (2006) and Karsch (2011) for central collisions and from STAR (2017) for peripheral collisions.

## Key numbers

* Angular velocity values considered: omega = 0.01, 0.015, and 0.02 GeV for central collisions (fireball radius R = 30 GeV^-1, corresponding to 6 fm); omega = 0.02, 0.03, and 0.04 GeV for peripheral collisions (R = 12.5 GeV^-1, corresponding to 2.5 fm).
* The causality condition requires R*omega <= 1; for the chosen parameters, R*omega ranges from 0.25 to 0.5.
* The ultraviolet mass cutoff for the hadron list is Lambda = 1.5 GeV.
* The freeze-out condition is fixed at entropy density s/T^3 = 7.0.
* For net-proton fluctuations at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.4 GeV, the kurtosis times variance (kappa*sigma^2)_p with omega = 0.02 GeV can explain the HADES data, while the standard HRG (omega = 0) cannot.
* The vorticity inferred from STAR Lambda polarization measurements is omega ~ (9 +/- 1) x 10^21 s^-1, approximately 0.05 m_pi.

## Context

This work extends the rotating HRG model of Fujimoto (2021) by computing higher-order susceptibilities (up to sixth order) and their ratios (kurtosis, skewness, hyper-kurtosis) for conserved charges, and by comparing the results with experimental moment data from STAR (2014, 2017, 2021) and HADES (2020). The study provides a theoretical baseline for interpreting conserved number fluctuations in rotating QCD matter, which is relevant for the search for the QCD critical point at facilities such as RHIC, NICA, FAIR, and J-PARC-HI. The authors note that future work could incorporate repulsive interactions (Pradhan 2023), inhomogeneity (Chernodub 2020), and simultaneous magnetic fields (Mukherjee 2023) to more realistically model the heavy-ion collision fireball.
