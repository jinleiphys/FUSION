# The Role of Mass Asymmetry and Shell Structure in the Evaporation Residues Production

> The effects of the entrance channel and shell structure of reacting nuclei on the experimental evaporation residues have been studied by analysing the 40Ar+176Hf, 86Kr+130,136Xe, 124Sn+92Zr and 48Ca+174Yb reactions leading to the 216Th* and 222Th* compound nuclei. The measured excitation function of evaporation residues for the 124Sn+92Zr reaction was larger than that for the 86Kr+130Xe reaction. The experimental values of evaporation residues in the 86Kr+136Xe reaction were about 500 times larger than that in the 86Kr+130Xe reaction. These results are explained by the initial angular momentum dependence of the fusion excitation functions calculated in framework of the dinuclear system concept and by the differences in survival probabilities calculated in framework of advanced statistical model. The dependencies of the fission barrier and the Γ_n / Γ_f ratio on the angular momentum of the excited compound nucleus are taken into account.

# The Role of Mass Asymmetry and Shell Structure in the Evaporation Residues Production

## Key claim

The measured evaporation residue cross sections for reactions leading to the same compound nucleus depend not only on mass asymmetry but also on shell structure effects in the binding energies of the reacting nuclei, which can reverse the trend expected from mass asymmetry alone. Specifically, the more mass-asymmetric 86Kr+130Xe reaction produces four times fewer evaporation residues than the more symmetric 124Sn+92Zr reaction, contradicting the macroscopic dynamical model prediction that asymmetric reactions should be more favorable.

## Method

The authors analyzed five reactions (40Ar+176Hf, 86Kr+130,136Xe, 124Sn+92Zr, and 48Ca+174Yb) leading to 216Th* and 222Th* compound nuclei. They calculated capture cross sections by solving equations of motion for the relative distance and orbital angular momentum with dissipation, then computed fusion cross sections using the dinuclear system (DNS) concept where competition between complete fusion and quasifission is treated statistically via level densities over intrinsic fusion and quasifission barriers. Survival probabilities of the excited compound nuclei were calculated using an advanced statistical model that includes angular-momentum-dependent fission barriers from the rotating droplet model, shell corrections with temperature and angular momentum fade-out, and Kramers' fission width with transient time corrections.

## Key numbers

- The maximum evaporation residue cross section for 40Ar+176Hf is twelve times larger than for 86Kr+130Xe and three times larger than for 124Sn+92Zr, all leading to 216Th*.
- The measured evaporation residue cross section for 86Kr+136Xe is about 500 times larger than for 86Kr+130Xe, both leading to 222Th*.
- For reactions leading to 216Th*, the intrinsic fusion barrier B*_fus is 2.31 MeV for 40Ar+176Hf, 12.31 MeV for 86Kr+130Xe, and 9.87 MeV for 124Sn+92Zr; the quasifission barrier B_qf is 5.62 MeV, 2.35 MeV, and 1.35 MeV respectively; the fusion factor P_CN is 0.121, 0.011, and 0.051 respectively.
- The reduced dissipation coefficient beta_dis for the investigated reactions lies in the range (6-7) x 10^21 s^-1.
- The thermal equilibration time of the DNS is tau_ther = 2.6/T_DNS^2 x 10^-22 s, where T_DNS is the effective temperature in MeV.

## Context

This work extends the study of entrance channel effects on fusion-fission reactions, following earlier observations by Back (1996) for systems leading to 214Th and by Hahn (1987) for reactions leading to 200Po. The authors use the DNS concept, previously developed by various groups (references in the text), to explain why the more symmetric 124Sn+92Zr reaction produces more evaporation residues than the more asymmetric 86Kr+130Xe reaction, a result that contradicts the macroscopic dynamical model of Blocki (1986). The analysis enables improved predictions for optimal reaction conditions to synthesize superheavy elements by demonstrating that shell structure in binding energies, not just mass asymmetry, determines fusion probabilities.
