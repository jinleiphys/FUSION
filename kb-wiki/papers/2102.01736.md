# Open Quantum Systems for Quarkonia

> I review recent applications of the open quantum system framework in the understanding of quarkonium suppression in heavy-ion collisions, which has been used as a probe of the quark-gluon plasma for decades. The derivation of the Lindblad equations for quarkonium in both the quantum Brownian motion and the quantum optical limits and their semiclassical counterparts is explained. The hierarchy of time scales assumed in the derivation is justified from the separation of energy scales in nonrelativistic effective field theories of QCD. Physical implications of the open quantum system approach are also discussed. Finally, I list some open questions for future studies.

## Key claim

The open quantum system framework provides a consistent theoretical approach to simultaneously account for both plasma screening effects and recombination of quarkonium in the quark-gluon plasma, overcoming the model dependence that plagues semiclassical transport approaches; however, the derivation of Lindblad equations relies on specific hierarchies of time scales (Markovian approximation and either the quantum Brownian motion or quantum optical limit) that must be justified from the separation of energy scales in nonrelativistic effective field theories of QCD.

## Method

The author derives Lindblad equations for quarkonium in both the quantum Brownian motion limit (where the subsystem intrinsic time is much larger than the environment correlation time, τ_S ≫ τ_E) and the quantum optical limit (where the relaxation time is much larger than the subsystem intrinsic time, τ_R ≫ τ_S), starting from a total Hamiltonian H = H_S + H_E + H_I with factorized initial conditions and a thermal environment. The derivation expands the time evolution operator to second order in the interaction Hamiltonian, applies Fourier transforms of environment correlators, and uses the Markovian approximation (τ_R ≫ τ_E) to convert finite-difference equations into differential equations. The hierarchy of time scales is justified from the separation of energy scales in nonrelativistic effective field theories of QCD, and the relation between the Lindblad equations and their semiclassical counterparts (Boltzmann equation and Fokker-Planck equation) is elucidated.

## Key numbers

- The QGP lifetime is about 10 fm/c in the laboratory frame and the temperature range currently achieved is about 150–600 MeV.
- The annihilation rate of the heavy quark-antiquark pair in quarkonium is on the order of 100 keV and can be neglected during the QGP lifetime of about 10 fm/c.
- The relaxation time scales as τ_R ∼ T/(H_I^(int))^2, where T is the environment temperature.
- The environment correlation time is τ_E ∼ 1/T.
- The subsystem intrinsic time is τ_S ∼ 1/H_S, where H_S is the typical eigenenergy gap of the subsystem.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on quarkonium suppression in heavy-ion collisions that began with the plasma screening idea of Matsui (1986) and Karsch (1987), and later incorporated dynamical dissociation from Landau damping (Laine 2006, Beraudo 2007, Brambilla 2008) and recombination (Thews 2000, Andronic 2003, Andronic 2007). It contrasts with phenomenological studies that solve a Schrödinger equation with a complex potential (Strickland 2011, Krouppa 2015) which lack recombination, and with semiclassical transport models (Grandchamp 2003, Yan 2006, Zhao 2010, Du 2015) that have large systematic uncertainties in implementing recombination. The open quantum system approach enables a consistent treatment of both dissociation and recombination, and the author discusses new physical insights and a proposed new experimental observable to test them.
