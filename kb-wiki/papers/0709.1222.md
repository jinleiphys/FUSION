# Energetic Electrons and Nuclear Transmutations in Exploding Wires

> Nuclear transmutations and fast neutrons have been observed to emerge from large electrical current pulses passing through wire filaments which are induced to explode. The nuclear reactions may be explained as inverse beta transitions of energetic electrons absorbed either directly by single protons in Hydrogen or by protons embedded in other more massive nuclei. The critical energy transformations to the electrons from the electromagnetic field and from the electrons to the nuclei are best understood in terms of coherent collective motions of the many flowing electrons within a wire filament. Energy transformation mechanisms have thus been found which settle a theoretical paradox in low energy nuclear reactions which has remained unresolved for over eight decades. It is presently clear that nuclear transmutations can occur under a much wider range of physical conditions than was heretofore thought possible.

# Energetic Electrons and Nuclear Transmutations in Exploding Wires

## Key claim

Nuclear transmutations and fast neutrons observed in exploding wire experiments can be explained by inverse beta transitions of energetic electrons absorbed by protons, where the electrons acquire sufficient energy through coherent collective motions within the wire filament rather than from individual electron acceleration. This resolves the eight-decade-old theoretical paradox raised by Rutherford (1922) regarding whether electrons flowing through a wire could carry enough energy to induce nuclear reactions, with the condition that the collective energy transfer mechanism requires the Coulomb repulsion between electrons to be screened by positive ions in the wire filament.

## Method

The authors derive a theoretical model showing that a rapidly changing current in a thin wire filament induces a Faraday law voltage that accelerates electrons, with the power delivered to a single electron given by dW/dt = -η mc² (1/I₀)(dI/dt)(v/c), where I₀ ≈ 1.7 × 10⁴ Amperes is the current scale corresponding to the electron rest energy. They apply this to the Wendt-Irion (1922) exploding wire experiment, calculating the magnetic energy contribution from many electrons acting cooperatively to enable inverse beta transitions, and contrast this with Rutherford's vacuum electron beam experiment where Coulomb repulsion prevents such collective energy transfer.

## Key numbers

- I₀ ≈ 1.704509 × 10⁴ Amperes: the current scale derived from expressing the electron rest energy mc² in units of current using the vacuum impedance
- N ∼ 2 × 10¹⁶ electrons: number of electrons sent from one capacitor plate to the other in the Wendt-Irion experiment
- W_Coulomb ∼ 15 KeV: initial energy per stored electron in the capacitor
- N* ∼ 10¹³ electrons: maximum number of electrons that could make inverse beta transitions causing nuclear transmutations, based on total energy balance
- I/I₀ as high as 200: peak current ratio in the Wendt-Irion experiment
- W_magnetic ∼ 200 MeV × v/c: magnetic energy contribution for the peak current ratio
- v/c ∼ 0.1: estimated electron velocity in the filament required for sufficient magnetic energy
- v̄/c ∼ 10⁻⁴: mean drift velocity of electrons from one capacitor plate to the other through the transformer coil and filament

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate between Wendt and Irion (1922), who first reported nuclear reactions in exploding wires, and Rutherford (1922), who doubted that electrons flowing through a wire could carry enough energy to induce nuclear reactions. The authors argue that more recent experiments by Stephanakis (1972), Young (1977), Bakshaev (2001, 2006), Velikovich (2007), and Coverdale (2007) have experimentally settled the debate in favor of Wendt, detecting fast neutrons even without deuterons initially present. The theoretical mechanism builds on the authors' prior work on inverse beta transitions in chemical cells (Widom, 2006), and enables understanding of nuclear transmutations under a wider range of physical conditions than previously thought possible, including in thin wire filaments where surface plasma polariton modes are destroyed by the explosion.
