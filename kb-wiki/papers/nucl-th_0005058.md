# Relativistic eikonal approximation in high-energy A(e,e'p) reactions

> A fully relativistic model for the description of exclusive (e,e'p) reactions off nuclear targets at high energies and momentum transfers is outlined. It is based on the eikonal approximation for the ejectile scattering wave function and a relativistic mean-field approximation to the Walecka model. Results for ^{12}C(e,e'p) and ^{16}O(e,e'p) differential cross sections and separated structure functions are presented for four-momenta in the range 0.8 \leq Q^{2} \leq 20 (GeV/c)^{2}. The regions of applicability of the eikonal approximation are studied and observed to be confined to proton knockout in a relatively small cone about the momentum transfer. A simple criterium defining the boundaries of this cone is determined. The Q^2 dependence of the effect of off-shell ambiguities on the different (e,e'p) structure functions is addressed. At sufficiently high values of Q^2 their impact on the cross sections is illustrated to become practically negligible. It is pointed out that for the whole range of Q^2 values studied here, the bulk of the relativistic effects arising from the coupling between the lower components in the wave functions, is manifesting itself in the longitudinal-transverse interference term.

# Relativistic eikonal approximation in high-energy A(e,e'p) reactions

## Key claim

The eikonal approximation for describing final-state interactions in high-energy (e,e'p) reactions is reliable only for proton knockout within a small cone about the momentum transfer direction, defined by the condition |q|·θ ≤ 250 MeV rad; outside this cone, eikonal calculations produce unrealistic results that overshoot both plane-wave calculations and experimental data, particularly at missing momenta above 250 MeV/c.

## Method

The authors developed a fully relativistic model based on the Dirac eikonal approximation for the ejectile scattering wave function, using relativistic mean-field potentials from the Walecka model (Horowitz and Serot parameterization) for both bound and scattering states. Bound state wave functions were computed by solving coupled Dirac equations iteratively with a shooting-point method, and the eikonal scattering wave functions were obtained by applying the small-angle approximation (p² → 2K·p - K²) to the Dirac equation for the upper component. The model was applied to calculate differential cross sections and separated structure functions for ¹²C(e,e'p) and ¹⁶O(e,e'p) reactions, comparing results with Jefferson Lab data at Q² = 0.8 (GeV/c)² and with relativistic plane-wave impulse approximation (RPWIA) calculations.

## Key numbers

- Four-momentum transfer range studied: 0.8 ≤ Q² ≤ 20 (GeV/c)²
- For ¹⁶O(e,e'p) at Q² = 0.8 (GeV/c)² and ω = 0.439 GeV, eikonal calculations used missing momenta p_m below 355 MeV/c, with a spectroscopic factor of 0.6 adopted for all bound levels
- The eikonal approximation validity condition: |q|·θ ≤ 250 MeV rad, where θ is the angle between the transferred momentum and the ejectile momentum
- Eikonal results become unreliable at missing momenta p_m ≥ 250 MeV/c, where the slope of the differential cross section deviates from RPWIA trends
- For fixed outgoing proton momentum k_f = 1 GeV/c with initial electron energy 2.4 GeV, eikonal calculations at recoil angle θ = 0° (parallel kinematics) remain valid up to p_m = 0.5 GeV/c
- Bound state calculations used a radial extension of 20 fm with 0.01 fm stepsize, convergence tolerance of 0.001 MeV on single-particle energy levels

## Context

This work extends non-relativistic Glauber theory approaches (Jeschonnek, 1999; Radici, 1995; Rinat, 1997) to a fully relativistic framework for high-energy (e,e'p) reactions, using the Walecka model mean-field approximation (Horowitz and Serot, 1981) for nuclear structure. The model enables systematic study of off-shell ambiguities in the electron-nucleon vertex and relativistic effects from coupling between upper and lower Dirac components, which become critical in the GeV energy domain. The results establish clear limitations on the eikonal approximation's applicability that constrain future Glauber-based analyses of high-energy (e,e'p) data from Jefferson Lab and other facilities, while the finding that relativistic effects manifest primarily in the longitudinal-transverse interference term provides guidance for experimental separation of structure functions.
