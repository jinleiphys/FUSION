# Tunneling half-lives in macroscopic-microscopic picture

> Tunneling half lives are obtained in a minimalistic deformation picture of nuclear decays. As widely documented in other deformation models, one finds that the effective mass of the nucleus changes with the deformation parameter. However, contrary to the approach used in literature, a position-dependant mass potentially makes using WKB tunneling probabilities unreliable for estimating nuclear lifetimes. We instead use a new approach, a combination of the Transmission Matrix and WKB methods, to estimate tunneling probabilities. Because of the simplistic nature of the model, the calculated lifetimes are not accurate, however, the relative trends in the lifetimes of isotopes of individual nuclei are found to be consistent. Using this, we develop an empirical scaling to obtain the actual half-lives, and find the primary scaling parameter to have remarkably consistent values for all nuclei considered. The new tunneling method proposed here, which produces very different probabilities as compared to the usual WKB approach, is another key result of this work, and can be utilized for arbitrary potentials and mass variations.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a new combined Transmission Matrix and WKB method can produce consistent relative trends in nuclear half-lives across isotopes, even though the absolute calculated lifetimes are inaccurate due to the simplistic deformation model. The key hedge is that the model is only valid for nuclei with significant alpha decay mode participation, not beta decays.

## Method

The authors construct a minimalistic macroscopic-microscopic nuclear deformation model using only the second-order deformation parameter a2, truncated to a cubic deformation potential. They derive a position-dependent effective mass for the nucleus and develop a hybrid tunneling method that uses the Transmission Matrix approach in the middle of the barrier (where it is accurate) and WKB near the crossing points (where the Transmission Matrix diverges). The tunneling probability is then used to calculate half-lives, and an empirical logarithmic scaling relation is fitted to match calculated to actual lifetimes.

## Key numbers

- The primary scaling parameter m in the empirical relation log(ta) = m log(tc) + p(N,Z) is found to be approximately 9.11 on average across all nuclei considered
- For Uranium, the fitted m = 9.413 with p2 = 146.7 and p3 = 144.7
- For Plutonium, the fitted m = 7.615 with p2 = 130.7 and p3 = 129.1
- For Protactinium, the fitted m = 10.22 with p0 = 148.8 and p1 = 146.9
- For Neptunium, the fitted m = 9.118 with p0 = 148.3 and p1 = 148.6
- The average values of the parity-dependent parameters are p0 approximately 148.6, p1 approximately 147, p2 approximately 139.25, p3 approximately 137.45
- The threshold parameter for the hybrid method is chosen as th approximately th0/2, where th0 = (V(dm) - E)/E

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic deformation model developed in a companion paper (Jain and Bhagwat, 2024) that mapped nuclear deformation potentials to the Fold Catastrophe from Thom's Catastrophe Theory. The approach contrasts with standard WKB treatments used in other deformation models (such as those by Nilsson and collaborators), which the authors argue are unreliable for position-dependent mass. The new tunneling method can be applied to arbitrary potentials and mass variations, and the empirical scaling relation enables prediction of relative half-life trends between isotopes of Uranium, Plutonium, Protactinium, and Neptunium.
