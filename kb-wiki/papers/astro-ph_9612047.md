# Neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis and the site of the r-process

> If the r-process occurs deep within a Type II supernova, probably the most popular of the proposed sites, abundances of r-process elements may be altered by the intense neutrino flux. We point out that the effects would be especially pronounced for 8 isotopes that can be efficiently synthesized by the neutrino reactions following r-process freeze-out. We show that the observed abundances of these isotopes are entirely consistent with neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis, strongly arguing for a supernova r-process site. The deduced fluences place stringent constraints on the freeze-out radius and dynamic timescale of the r-process.

# Neutrino-induced nucleosynthesis and the site of the r-process

## Key claim

The observed solar-system abundances of eight specific isotopes in the valleys below the A~130 and A~195 r-process abundance peaks are fully consistent with being produced entirely by neutrino-induced spallation reactions during postprocessing after r-process freeze-out, provided the neutrino fluence parameter F is close to 0.031 for the lower peak and 0.015 for the upper peak. This consistency strongly argues that the r-process occurs within the intense neutrino flux of a Type II supernova interior, but the claim depends on the assumption that the unpostprocessed freeze-out abundances of these eight window nuclei were negligible.

## Method

The authors computed neutrino reaction rates and average neutron spallation numbers for charged-current (ν_e,e^-) and neutral-current heavy-flavor (ν,ν') reactions on r-process progenitor nuclei in the A~80, 130, and 195 abundance peaks, using cross sections from shell-model and continuum random phase approximation calculations. They then applied a Poisson-statistics inversion procedure to the observed solar r-process abundance distribution: for a given neutrino fluence parameter F, they iteratively removed the effects of neutrino postprocessing to recover the unpostprocessed freeze-out abundance pattern, and compared the predicted postprocessed abundances of the eight window nuclei to observed values from Kaeppeler (1989).

## Key numbers

- Neutrino luminosity from cooling protoneutron star: ~3×10^53 erg total, roughly equi-partitioned among all species
- Average neutrino energies: ⟨E_νe⟩ ≈ 11 MeV, ⟨E_ν̄e⟩ ≈ 16 MeV, ⟨E_νμ(τ)⟩ ≈ ⟨E_ν̄μ(τ)⟩ ≈ 25 MeV
- Neutrino cooling timescale: τ_ν ∼ 3 s
- Total reaction rates on average nuclei at r=100 km with luminosity 10^51 erg s^-1 per species: ~9 s^-1 (A~80), 15 s^-1 (A~130), 20 s^-1 (A~195)
- Average number of spallation neutrons per reaction: ⟨n⟩ ∼ 2 (A~80 and A~130), 3 (A~195)
- Upper bound on fluence for A~130 peak: F ≲ 0.045; best-fit fluence: F = 0.031
- Upper bound on fluence for A~195 peak: F ≲ 0.030; best-fit fluence: F = 0.015
- At F=0.031, predicted abundances for A=124-126: 0.24 (¹²⁴Te), 0.45 (¹²⁵Te), 0.65 (¹²⁶Te); observed values from Kaeppeler (1989): 0.215±0.020, 0.269±0.042, 0.518±0.126
- At F=0.015, predicted abundances for A=183-187: 0.0053 (¹⁸³W), 0.0093 (¹⁸⁴W), 0.0160 (¹⁸⁵Re), 0.0274 (¹⁸⁶W), 0.0411 (¹⁸⁷Re); observed values from Kaeppeler (1989): 0.0067±0.0016, 0.0135±0.0035, 0.0127±0.0024, 0.0281±0.0024, 0.0373±0.0040
- Freeze-out radii in the Woosley (1994) model: 600-1000 km, at times ~10 s after core bounce, yielding F ∼ 0.01-0.03

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Woosley (1990) that neutrinos can induce nucleosynthesis in supernova shells, applying it specifically to r-process postprocessing. It builds on the authors' more technical paper Qian (1996) that examined neutrino physics issues for the r-process, and on the Woosley (1994) model of a supernova "hot bubble" r-process with α-particle freeze-out followed by neutron capture on seed nuclei near A~100. The results enable quantitative constraints on the freeze-out radius and dynamic timescale of the r-process, and motivate full incorporation of neutrino interactions into r-process network calculations. The finding that the A~195 peak requires a different fluence than the A~130 peak suggests either later freeze-out at lower luminosity (consistent with Woosley 1994) or larger radius with shorter dynamic timescale (as in the wind scenario of Qian 1996).
