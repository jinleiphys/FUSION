# Description of the RHIC p_T-spectra in a thermal model with expansion

> The assumption of simultaneous chemical and thermal freeze-outs of the hadron gas leads to a surprisingly accurate, albeit entirely conventional, explanation of the recently-measured RHIC pT spectra. The original thermal spectra are supplied with secondaries from decays of all resonances, and subsequently folded with a suitably parameterized expansion involving longitudinal and transverse flow. The predictions of this thermal approach, with various parametrizations for the expansion, are in a striking quantitative agreement with the data in the whole available range of 0 < pT < 3.5GeV.

# Description of the RHIC p_T-spectra in a thermal model with expansion

## Key claim
The assumption of simultaneous chemical and thermal freeze-outs of the hadron gas, combined with resonance decays and a parameterized hydrodynamic expansion involving longitudinal and transverse flow, can reproduce the RHIC p_T spectra across the entire measured range of 0 < p_T < 3.5 GeV, but only if the freeze-out temperature is first fixed by particle ratio fits rather than adjusted independently for each particle species.

## Method
The authors used a thermal model with simultaneous chemical and thermal freeze-out at temperature T = 165 ± 7 MeV and baryon chemical potential μ_B = 41 ± 5 MeV, as determined from particle ratio fits. They computed initial thermal distributions for all hadrons, added contributions from sequential two- and three-body decays of all heavier resonances, and then folded the resulting spectra with two different boost-invariant parameterizations of the freeze-out hypersurface and hydrodynamic expansion (model I with fixed invariant time τ and model II following the blast model). The Cooper-Frye-Schonberg formula was used to calculate the final p_T spectra at mid-rapidity.

## Key numbers
- Freeze-out temperature: T = 165 ± 7 MeV, baryon chemical potential: μ_B = 41 ± 5 MeV (from thermal analysis of particle ratios)
- For minimum-bias data: model I yields τ = 5.55 fm, ρ_max = 4.50 fm; model II yields volume parameter V = (6.48 fm)^3, β_⊥ = 0.52
- For most central collisions: model I yields τ = 7.66 fm, ρ_max = 6.69 fm; model II yields V = (9.81 fm)^3, β_⊥ = 0.52
- ρ_max = 6.69 fm for central collisions is very close to the Au nucleus radius of 6.22 fm
- Total charged particle multiplicity for most central events: 555 ± 12 ± 35 (PHOBOS)
- Inverse slope parameters T_eff for pions in PHENIX minimum-bias data: experimental 190 ± 17 MeV (positive) and 207 ± 20 MeV (negative); model I gives 203 MeV and 202 MeV respectively
- Inverse slope parameters T_eff for protons/antiprotons in PHENIX minimum-bias data: experimental 274 ± 11 MeV (positive) and 301 ± 14 MeV (negative); model I gives 292 MeV and 291 MeV respectively
- Average transverse momentum ⟨p_⊥⟩ for pions in PHENIX most central data: experimental 370 ± 70 MeV; model I gives 438 MeV (positive) and 434 MeV (negative)
- Average transverse momentum ⟨p_⊥⟩ for protons/antiprotons in PHENIX most central data: experimental 840 ± 50 MeV (positive) and 860 ± 50 MeV (negative); model I gives 853 MeV and 852 MeV respectively
- Resonance decays effectively cool the system by 35-40 MeV

## Context
This work extends the successful application of thermal models from particle ratios at AGS and SPS (Braun-Munzinger et al., 1995; Cleymans et al., 1997; Becattini et al., 2001) to the description of transverse momentum spectra at RHIC. The approach combines the Bjorken longitudinal expansion model (Bjorken, 1983) with transverse flow following the blast model formalism of Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) and the work of Csorgo and Lorstad (1996). The key advance is that the temperature is fixed by particle ratios rather than fitted independently for each particle species, as was common in earlier blast-model fits. The results provide freeze-out conditions that can serve as constraints for more involved hydrodynamic calculations (Teaney, Lauret, and Shuryak, 2001; Huovinen, Kolb, Heinz, Ruuskanen, and Voloshin, 2001). The model should be further verified against HBT pion interferometry data and extended to include centrality effects such as elliptic flow and rapidity dependence.
