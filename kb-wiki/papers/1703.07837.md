# Experimentally Study the Deep Dirac Levels with High-Intensity Lasers

> Various theories have predicted the deep Dirac levels (DDLs) in atoms for many years. However, the existence of the DDL is still under debating, and need to be confirmed experimentally. With the development of high intensive lasers, nowadays, electrons can been accelerated to relativistic energy by high intensive lasers, electron-positron pairs can be created, and nuclear reactions can been ignited, which provide a new tool to explore the DDL related fields. In this paper, we propose a new experimental method to study the DDL levels by monitoring nuclei's orbital electron capture life time in plasma induced by high intensive lasers. If a DDL exists, a nuclear electron capture rate could be enhanced by factor of over $10^7$, which makes it practically detectable in nowadays high intensive laser environments.

## Key claim

The authors propose that if deep Dirac levels (DDLs) exist, the electron capture (EC) rate of certain nuclei could be enhanced by a factor of over \(10^7\), making DDL detection practically feasible in current high-intensity laser environments. The claim is conditional: the enhancement depends on the DDL actually being populated via electron-positron pair production or Nuclear Excitation by Electron Transition (NEET) mechanisms in laser-induced plasmas.

## Method

The authors analytically solve the Klein-Gordon equation for a hydrogen-like atom to obtain the DDL wave function and binding energy, then compute the electron capture decay rate using Fermi's golden rule. They approximate the EC rate ratio between the DDL and the normal Bohr ground state as proportional to the electron probability density at the nuclear surface, and also perform numerical calculations with a finite nuclear charge distribution. They identify candidate nuclei that can be produced via one-nucleon-transfer reactions in high-intensity laser experiments and propose a detection time window of 100 ns to 1 minute to suppress background.

## Key numbers

- For hydrogen (\(Z=1\)), the DDL binding energy is approximately \(0.5073\) MeV, compared to \(13.6\) eV for the Bohr ground state.
- The DDL orbital radius is about \(390\) fm, compared to \(0.53\) Å for the Bohr orbit.
- For \(^{7}\)Be, the predicted EC half-life if DDL exists is \(21\) ms (analytic) or \(52\) ms (numerical), versus the normal half-life of \(53.2\) days.
- For \(^{62}\)Cu, the predicted EC half-life if DDL exists is \(2.2\) ms (analytic) or \(5.9\) ms (numerical), versus the normal half-life of \(9.67\) minutes.
- For \(^{64}\)Cu, the predicted EC half-life if DDL exists is \(0.28\) s (analytic) or \(0.7\) s (numerical), versus the normal half-life of \(12.7\) hours.
- The proposed detection time window is from \(100\) ns to \(1\) minute after the laser pulse.

## Context

This work extends theoretical debates on the existence of deep Dirac levels, which have been predicted by solutions to the Dirac and Klein-Gordon equations but traditionally discarded as "anomalous" (Schiff, textbook). Prior experimental claims of DDL observation (Mills 2003, Phillips 2004) have been questioned by others (Jovicevic 2009, Rathke 2005, Phelps 2005, Dombey 2006). The authors propose using high-intensity lasers (up to \(10^{22}\) W/cm\(^2\)) to populate DDLs via electron-positron pair production (observed in laser experiments by Chen et al. 2011) or NEET (Kishimoto et al. 2006), and monitoring characteristic EC decay gamma rays as a signature. This enables a new experimental test of a long-standing quantum mechanics puzzle.
