# Multipair approach to pairing in nuclei

> The ground state of a general pairing Hamiltonian for a finite nuclear system is constructed as a product of collective, real, distinct pairs. These are determined sequentially via an iterative variational procedure that resorts to diagonalizations of the Hamiltonian in restricted model spaces. Different applications of the method are provided that include comparisons with exact and projected BCS results. The quantities that are examined are correlation energies, occupation numbers and pair transfer matrix elements. In a first application within the picket-fence model, the method is seen to generate the exact ground state for pairing strengths confined in a given range. Further applications of the method concern pairing in spherically symmetric mean fields and include simple exactly solvable models as well as some realistic calculations for middle-shell Sn isotopes. In the latter applications, two different ways of defining the pairs are examined: either with J=0 or with no well-defined angular momentum. The second choice reveals to be more effective leading, under some circumstances, to solutions that are basically exact.

# Multipair approach to pairing in nuclei

## Key claim
The ground state of a general pairing Hamiltonian can be constructed as a product of collective, real, distinct pairs determined sequentially via an iterative variational procedure, but this construction yields the exact ground state only for pairing strengths below a critical value where the exact Richardson pairs remain real; for stronger pairing where exact pairs become complex, the method produces very small but nonzero errors.

## Method
The method constructs the ground state as a product of N distinct collective pair operators acting on the vacuum, where each pair is defined by real amplitudes. The pairs are determined sequentially through an iterative cycle: for each pair index ρ, the Hamiltonian is diagonalized in a restricted space of dimension Ω formed by acting with all uncorrelated pair operators on the product of all other pairs, generating an updated pair B^(new)_ρ. This cycle is repeated for all ρ until the ground state energy converges. The approach was applied to the picket-fence model (PFM) and to spherically symmetric mean fields including single j-shell, double j-shell, and realistic Sn isotope calculations, comparing with exact solutions and projected BCS (PBCS) results.

## Key numbers
- For a system of 2N=16 particles over Ω=16 levels in the PFM, the method generates the exact ground state for pairing strengths g < g_c ≈ 0.36 (in units of level spacing d); for g ≥ g_c, the relative error in correlation energy is up to three orders of magnitude smaller than the corresponding PBCS error.
- The PBCS approximation in the PFM shows a maximum relative error in correlation energy of about 6% at g ≈ 0.4.
- In the double j-shell (j=11/2) with energies ε₁=-1, ε₂=1 (arbitrary units), the method with no well-defined angular momentum pairs gives basically exact results for N ≥ Ω_j (where Ω_j = j+1/2 = 6), with the largest J² expectation value violations of 0.008 at V=0.3 and 0.002 at V=0.7 for N=5 pairs.
- For the single j-shell model, the method with initial δ_{kν} amplitudes reproduces the exact energy E_N = -gN(Ω - N + 1) in one iterative cycle.
- In Sn isotope calculations (¹¹²-¹¹⁸Sn), approach B (pairs with no well-defined angular momentum) is basically exact for ¹¹⁶-¹¹⁸Sn, with the discontinuity at A=114 corresponding to a partial subshell closure from filling g_{7/2} and d_{5/2} levels.

## Context
This work extends the Richardson exact solution for the picket-fence model (Richardson, 1963; Richardson and Sherman, 1964) to general pairing Hamiltonians where the Richardson formalism cannot be applied. It addresses limitations of BCS and projected BCS approaches identified by Sandulescu and Bertsch (2010), who showed that PBCS quality degrades with increasing model space size because the exact ground state contains pairs of very different nature. The method enables description of systems where pairs of very different character populate the ground state, and is proposed as a tool to investigate spatial properties of pairing correlations beyond the HFB/BCS framework used by Matsuo (2001), Pillet et al. (2007), and Pastore et al. (2011). The approach shares variational, particle-number-conserving features with the multiparticle-multihole configuration mixing method of Pillet et al. (2008) but differs in using a product of distinct pairs rather than a linear combination of Slater determinants.
