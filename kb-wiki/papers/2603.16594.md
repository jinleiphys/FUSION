# Finite size effects on critical correlations in momentum space

> The search for the QCD critical end point (CEP) is a major objective of contemporary heavy-ion physics, motivating the study of fluctuation observables that are sensitive to critical dynamics. In particular, baryon-number fluctuations provide a natural probe because the net-baryon density can serve as an effective order parameter in the vicinity of the CEP. Near criticality, long-range correlations and power-law scaling are expected to emerge in the real-space two-point function of the baryon density, yet the finite size and finite lifetime of the fireball created in heavy-ion collisions impose intrinsic cutoffs that regulate the growth of the correlation length. These finite-size constraints significantly modify the observable structure of fluctuations, especially in momentum space, where experiments perform measurements. In this work we present a theoretical analysis of the momentum-space two-point correlation function for a system of finite spatial extent. We show that finite size effects lead to an effective scaling exponent which coincides with that of the infinitely extended system only in a prescribed scaling region within the experimentally accessible momentum range.

# Finite size effects on critical correlations in momentum space

## Key claim

The paper shows that finite-size effects in a system of finite spatial extent lead to an effective scaling exponent for the momentum-space two-point correlation function that coincides with the infinite-system exponent only within a prescribed crossover window of momenta; outside this window, the correlations either saturate to a constant (at low momenta) or follow the infinite-system power law (at high momenta), with the window location shifting to smaller momenta for larger system sizes.

## Method

The authors analytically compute the Fourier transform of a power-law real-space density-density correlator (|x|^{-a}) restricted to a finite spatial domain of linear extent δ in d=2 dimensions, using an integral representation of the power law combined with error-function regularization of the finite boundary. They derive closed-form expressions for the momentum-space correlator in two asymptotic regimes (kδ ≫ 1 and kδ ≪ 1) and numerically evaluate the effective scaling exponent γ_eff across the crossover region. The analysis is extended to include a hard-core repulsion radius r_0 below which correlations vanish, modeling proton-proton interactions.

## Key numbers

- The real-space power-law exponent a is set to 1/3, corresponding to the 3D Ising model universality class conjectured for the QCD critical end point.
- The linear extent of correlated baryons δ is taken as 1 fm for the baseline calculation.
- For the hard-core case, the excluded radius r_0 is set to 0.3 fm.
- For a system with hard-core radius r_0 = 0.3 fm and system size r = 10 fm, the left boundary of the power-law window k^{HC}_left = 5.052 MeV and the right boundary k^{HC}_right = 27.93 MeV, with Δγ = 0.510.
- For r = 3 fm, k^{HC}_left = 15.44 MeV and k^{HC}_right = 38.37 MeV (Δγ = 0.455); for r = 13 fm, k^{HC}_left = 3.927 MeV and k^{HC}_right = 28.75 MeV (Δγ = 0.524).
- The crossover window for the baseline (no hard-core) case lies in the momentum region of order 10^2 MeV for δ ~ 1 fm.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Antoniou, Davis, and Diakonos on fractal structure and critical correlations near the QCD critical point, and builds on the intermittency approach of Antoniou (2006) for detecting critical fluctuations via factorial moments. The analysis addresses finite-size effects that modify the scaling predictions of Stephanov, Rajagopal, and Shuryak, and provides a quantitative basis for interpreting experimental searches at RHIC (STAR) and SPS (NA61/SHINE). The results enable more precise predictions for the momentum-range where critical scaling should be observable in heavy-ion collisions, and connect the saturation plateau at low momenta to the baryon number susceptibility χ_B, which can be used to identify the critical temperature through its freeze-out dependence.
