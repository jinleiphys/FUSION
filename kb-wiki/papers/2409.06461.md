# Strangeness in Astrophysics

> In this contribution the role of strangeness in astrophysics is discussed and, more precisely, strange hadronic matter in the interior of neutron stars. A special attention is payed to certain phenomena involving strange hadronic matter, such as the hyperon puzzle, kaon condensation and the thermal behaviour of hyperons in neutron star mergers.

## Key claim

This review argues that while strange hadronic matter (hyperons and/or antikaons) is expected in neutron star interiors, the "hyperon puzzle" remains unresolved: the softening of the equation of state from hyperon appearance may prevent neutron stars from reaching the observed 2 solar mass threshold, though several proposed solutions exist.

## Method

The paper reviews three phenomena related to strange hadronic matter in neutron stars: the hyperon puzzle (where hyperon onset at 2-3 times nuclear saturation density softens the equation of state, potentially conflicting with 2 solar mass observations), kaon condensation (where antikaons might form a condensate if their effective mass drops below the electron chemical potential), and the thermal behavior of hyperons in neutron star mergers (where numerical simulations compare gravitational-wave frequencies from fully temperature-dependent equations of state against those using an ideal-gas thermal treatment with fixed adiabatic index).

## Key numbers

- Hyperons are expected to appear at densities of approximately 2-3 times nuclear saturation density (ρ₀) in neutron star interiors
- Neutron stars typically have masses around 1-2 solar masses and radii about 10-12 km
- Hyperonic equations of state produce an increase in the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency by up to approximately 150 Hz compared to purely nucleonic models
- The ideal-gas thermal treatment used for comparison assumes an adiabatic index Γ_th = 1.75, mimicking purely nucleonic thermal behavior

## Context

This review extends the long-standing discussion of strangeness in neutron stars that began with the seminal work of Ambartsumyan and Saakyan (1960) on hyperons in stellar cores. It summarizes recent attempts to resolve the hyperon puzzle, including stiff hyperon interactions (Bednarek 2011, Weissenborn 2011, Oertel 2014, Maslov 2015), three-body forces (Haidenbauer 2016, Logoteta 2019, Gerstung 2020, Takatsuka 2002, Vidana 2010, Yamamoto 2013, Lonardoni 2014), and alternative degrees of freedom such as Delta baryons (Schurhoff 2010, Drago 2014, Ribes 2019) or early quark matter transitions (Weissenborn 2011, Bonanno 2011, Klahn 2013, Lastowiecki 2011, Zdunik 2012). The thermal behavior of hyperons as a gravitational-wave signature, proposed by Blacker (2023), offers a potential observational test, though caveats remain regarding statistical power, hyperon abundance dependence, and possible confusion with other exotic degrees of freedom.
