# "Smashing more than two": Deuteron production in relativistic heavy ion collisions via stochastic multi-particle reactions

> We study the deuteron production via the deuteron pion and nucleon catalysis reactions, $πp n \leftrightarrow πd$ and $N p n \leftrightarrow N d$, by employing stochastic multi-particle reactions in the hadronic transport approach SMASH for the first time. This is an improvement compared to previous studies, which introduced an artificial fake resonance $d'$ to simulate these $3 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions as a chain of $2\leftrightarrow 2$ reactions. The derivation of the stochastic criterion for multi-particle reactions is presented in a comprehensive fashion and its implementation is tested against an analytic expression for the scattering rate and the equilibrating particle yields in box calculations. We then study Au + Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 7.7$ GeV, where we find that multi-particle collisions substantially reduce the time required for deuterons to reach partial chemical equilibrium with nucleons. Subsequently, the final yield of $d$ is practically independent from the number of $d$ at particlization, confirming the results of previous studies. The mean transverse momentum and the integrated elliptic flow as a function of centrality are rather insensitive to the exact realization of the $2\leftrightarrow 3$ reactions.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that direct stochastic multi-particle $3 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions for deuteron catalysis ($\pi p n \leftrightarrow \pi d$ and $N p n \leftrightarrow N d$) produce substantially faster equilibration of deuterons with nucleons than the previously used artificial two-step $2 \leftrightarrow 2$ chain via a fake $d'$ resonance, but the final deuteron yield and flow observables remain practically unchanged.

## Method
The authors implemented a stochastic (probabilistic) collision criterion in the SMASH hadronic transport approach to directly simulate $3 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions, avoiding the previous artificial $d'$ resonance method. They validated the implementation against analytic rate equations in static box calculations with periodic boundary conditions at fixed temperature, then applied the approach to Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 7.7$ GeV using a hybrid setup where the earlier dense stage was modeled by the MUSIC hydrodynamic code with Cooper-Frye particlization.

## Key numbers
- Box validation at $T = 0.155$ GeV with volume $V = (10\ \mathrm{fm})^3$: initial multiplicities of 30 pions per species and 60 nucleons per species; direct $3 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions reached equilibrated deuteron yields significantly faster than the $2 \leftrightarrow 2$ chain with $d'$ resonance.
- Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 7.7$ GeV: particlization hypersurface at constant energy density $\epsilon = 0.26$ GeV/fm$^3$, corresponding to peak temperature $T = 141 \pm 4$ MeV and baryon chemical potential $\mu = 340 \pm 50$ MeV at mid-rapidity ($|\eta_s| < 0.5$).
- The final deuteron yield was practically independent of the number of deuterons at particlization, confirming previous results.
- Mean transverse momentum and integrated elliptic flow as a function of centrality were rather insensitive to the exact realization of the $2 \leftrightarrow 3$ reactions.

## Context
This work extends the pion-catalysis deuteron production approach introduced by Oliinychenko (2018) for LHC energies and further tested by Oliinychenko (2020) down to $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}} = 7.7$ GeV, which previously relied on an artificial $d'$ resonance to simulate $3 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions as a chain of $2 \leftrightarrow 2$ reactions. The direct multi-particle implementation alleviates criticism by Aichelin (2019) regarding the non-existent intermediate resonance. The stochastic criterion method follows earlier work by Danielewicz (1991) for $Npn \leftrightarrow Nd$ at low energies and by Cassing (2001) for multi-particle reactions, but now includes the pion catalysis channel essential at intermediate energies. The results enable more reliable microscopic calculations of light nuclei production in heavy-ion collisions without artificial resonances.
