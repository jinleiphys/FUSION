# What is the right formalism to search for resonances?

> Hadron decay chains constitute one of the main sources of information on the QCD spectrum. We discuss the differences between several partial wave analysis formalisms used in the literature to build the amplitudes. We match the helicity amplitudes to the covariant tensor basis. Hereby, we pay attention to the analytical properties of the amplitudes and separate singularities of kinematical and dynamical nature. We study the analytical properties of the spin-orbit (LS) formalism, and some of the covariant tensor approaches. In particular, we explicitly build the amplitudes for the B -> psi pi K and B -> Dbar pi pi decays, and show that the energy dependence of the covariant approach is model dependent. We also show that the usual recursive construction of covariant tensors explicitly violates crossing symmetry, which would lead to different resonance parameters extracted from scattering and decay processes.

## Key claim

The differences between partial wave analysis formalisms (helicity, spin-orbit, and covariant tensor approaches) are not due to relativistic versus nonrelativistic kinematics but rather have a dynamical origin; the covariant projection method (CPT) explicitly violates crossing symmetry, which would lead to different resonance parameters extracted from scattering and decay processes.

## Method

The authors compare the canonical helicity partial wave formalism with the covariant projection method (CPT) by explicitly constructing amplitudes for the decays B → ψ π K and B → D̅ π π. They match helicity amplitudes to covariant tensor bases, isolate kinematical singularities from dynamical ones, and examine the analytical properties of the spin-orbit (LS) formalism and several covariant tensor approaches. The matching between formalisms is performed by expressing scalar functions as sums over kinematical singularity-free helicity partial waves and comparing the resulting threshold behavior.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of partial wave analysis formalisms by Chung (1993), Filippini (1995), and Anisovich (2006) by clarifying that the so-called "relativistic corrections" between approaches are actually Lorentz invariant functions that can be absorbed into scalar amplitudes. The paper enables more rigorous extraction of resonance parameters from experiments such as LHCb, Belle, and COMPASS by demonstrating that the covariant projection method introduces model-dependent energy dependence and violates crossing symmetry, which is particularly relevant for analyses of exotic XYZ states in heavy meson decays and for connecting lattice QCD calculations to experimental data.
