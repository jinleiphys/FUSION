# Scaling Of Chiral Lagrangians And Landau Fermi Liquid Theory For Dense Hadronic Matter

> We discuss the Fermi-liquid properties of hadronic matter derived from a chiral Lagrangian field theory in which Brown-Rho (BR) scaling is incorporated. We identify the BR scaling as a contribution to Landau's Fermi liquid fixed-point quasiparticle parameter from "heavy" isoscalar meson degrees of freedom that are integrated out from a low-energy effective Lagrangian. We show that for the vector (convection) current, the result obtained in the chiral Lagrangian approach agrees precisely with that obtained in the semi-phenomenological Landau-Migdal approach. This precise agreement allows one to determine the Landau parameter that enters in the effective nucleon mass in terms of the constant that characterizes BR scaling. When applied to the weak axial current, however, these two approaches differ in a subtle way. While the difference is small numerically, the chiral Lagrangian approach implements current algebra and low-energy theorems associated with the axial response that the Landau method misses and hence is expected to be more predictive.

## Key claim

The paper establishes that Brown-Rho (BR) scaling, originally proposed as a precursor of the chiral phase transition, can be identified with a contribution to Landau's Fermi liquid fixed-point quasiparticle parameter from heavy isoscalar meson degrees of freedom integrated out from a low-energy effective Lagrangian; this identification holds precisely for the vector (convection) current but differs subtly for the weak axial current, where the chiral Lagrangian approach implements current algebra and low-energy theorems that the Landau method misses.

## Method

The authors derive the electromagnetic convection current for a nucleon at the Fermi surface using both Landau-Migdal Fermi liquid theory and a chiral Lagrangian field theory that incorporates BR scaling. They compute the pionic contributions to the Landau parameters \(F_1\) and \(F_1^\prime\) from one-pion exchange and the vector-meson contributions from effective four-Fermion interactions obtained by integrating out \(\omega\) and \(\rho\) mesons. By comparing the nucleon effective mass derived from the chiral Lagrangian with that from Fermi liquid theory, they extract a relation between the BR scaling factor \(\Phi\) and the Landau parameter \(F_1^\omega\) associated with the isoscalar vector meson.

## Key numbers

* The one-pion-exchange contribution to the Landau parameters relevant for the convection current is given by \(\frac{F_1(\pi)}{3} = -F_1^\prime(\pi) = -\frac{3f^2 m_N^\star}{8\pi^2 k_F} I_1\), where \(I_1 = -2 + (1 + \frac{m_\pi^2}{2k_F^2})\ln(1 + \frac{4k_F^2}{m_\pi^2})\) and \(f = g_{\pi NN} (m_\pi/2m_N) \approx 1\).
* The one-pion-exchange contribution to the gyromagnetic ratio is \(\delta g_l^\pi = \frac{m_N}{k_F} \frac{f^2}{4\pi^2} I_1 \tau_3\).
* The relation between the BR scaling factor \(\Phi\) and the Landau parameter from the \(\omega\) meson is \(\tilde{F}_1(\omega) = 3(1 - \Phi^{-1})\).
* The vector-meson contributions to the Landau parameters are \(F_1(\omega) = -C_\omega^2 \frac{2k_F^2}{\pi^2 \mu} \simeq -C_\omega^2 \frac{2k_F^2}{\pi^2 m_N}\) and \(F_1^\prime(\rho) = -C_\rho^2 \frac{2k_F^2}{\pi^2 \mu} \simeq -C_\rho^2 \frac{2k_F^2}{\pi^2 m_N}\), where \(\mu\) is the baryon chemical potential.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory strategy for two-nucleon systems (developed by Kaplan, Savage, and Wise; and by Phillips, Rupak, and others) to dense many-body systems by combining chiral Lagrangian field theory with Landau Fermi liquid theory. It builds on the earlier proposal of Brown-Rho scaling (Brown and Rho, 1991) and the demonstration that Walecka mean field theory is equivalent to a chiral Lagrangian mean-field theory with BR-scaling parameters (Brown and Rho, 1996). The result enables a connection between QCD-motivated scaling of hadron masses (via the quark condensate) and the Landau parameters that govern low-energy nuclear dynamics, and it provides a framework for extrapolating to higher densities relevant for heavy-ion collisions.
