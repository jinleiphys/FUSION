# Coarse graining of NN inelastic interactions up to 3 GeV:Repulsive vs Structural core

> The repulsive short distance core is one of the main paradigms of nuclear physics which even seems confirmed by QCD lattice calculations. On the other hand nuclear potentials at short distances are motivated by high energy behavior where inelasticities play an important role. We analyze NN interactions up to 3 GeV in terms of simple coarse grained complex and energy dependent interactions. We discuss two possible and conflicting scenarios which share the common feature of a vanishing wave function at the core location in the particular case of S- waves. We find that the optical potential with a repulsive core exhibits a strong energy dependence whereas the optical potential with the structural core is characterized by a rather adiabatic energy dependence which allows to treat inelasticity perturbatively. We discuss the possible implications for nuclear structure calculations of both alternatives.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the repulsive core traditionally assumed in nuclear potentials may not be a genuine feature of the NN interaction; instead, the repulsive core found in QCD lattice calculations represents an upper bound of the true static energy within the restricted two-nucleon Hilbert space, and the alternative "structural core" scenario (where the wave function has a node but the potential remains attractive at short distances) provides an equally valid description of scattering data up to 3 GeV.

## Method

The authors apply a coarse-graining approach to the NN interaction in the \(^1S_0\) channel, representing the short-range potential as a sequence of piecewise square wells or delta-shells with a grid spacing \(\Delta r \sim 1/p_{\rm CM}\). They fit the real and imaginary parts of these coarse-grained potentials to the SAID partial wave analysis phase shifts and inelasticities for np scattering up to \(T_{\rm LAB}=3\) GeV, assuming one-pion exchange (OPE) for distances beyond \(r_c=3\) fm. Two distinct families of solutions are obtained: one with a repulsive core and one with an attractive (structural) core, both producing a node in the zero-energy wave function.

## Key numbers

- The maximum laboratory energy considered is \(T_{\rm LAB}^{\rm max}=3\) GeV, corresponding to a center-of-mass momentum \(p_{\rm CM}^{\rm max}=1.2\) GeV.
- The coarse-graining grid spacing at the highest energy is \(\Delta r \sim 0.3\) fm, comparable to the traditional core radius \(a_c=0.5\) fm.
- The SAID fit uses 147 parameters to describe all partial waves up to \(J=7\), fitting 25,362 pp data points (\(\chi^2=48,780.934\)) and 13,033 np data points (\(\chi^2=26,261.000\)).
- For the elastic fit up to 300 MeV, \(N=5\) square wells are sufficient, yielding two solutions: an attractive solution with \(V_1^{\rm SW} = -661(48)\) MeV at \(r=0.6\) fm and a repulsive solution with \(V_1^{\rm SW} = +1175(536)\) MeV at the same radius.
- The pion mass is taken as \(m_\pi = 140\) MeV and the pion-nucleon coupling constant as \(f^2=0.075\).

## Context

This work extends the coarse-graining methodology previously developed by the Granada group for elastic NN scattering up to 350 MeV (Entem et al., 2007; Navarro Perez et al., 2011-2013) to the inelastic regime up to 3 GeV. It directly confronts two historical interpretations of the short-range NN interaction: the repulsive core paradigm originating from Jastrow (1951) and Hamada-Johnston (1962), and the structural core concept proposed by Otsuki, Tamagaki, and others (1965-1969) based on the composite nature of nucleons. The paper also critically reinterprets recent QCD lattice results (Aoki et al., 2011, 2013) as providing an upper bound rather than a definitive repulsive core. The findings enable future work on whether the structural core scenario, with its weaker energy dependence, may be more suitable for nuclear structure calculations involving inelasticities.
