# Hydro+ in Action: Understanding the Out-of-Equilibrium Dynamics Near a Critical Point in the QCD Phase Diagram

> Upcoming experimental programs will look for signatures of a possible critical point in the QCD phase diagram in fluctuation observables. To understand and predict these signatures, one must account for the fact that the dynamics of any critical fluctuations must be out-of-equilibrium: because of critical slowing down, the fluctuations cannot stay in equilibrium as the droplet of QGP produced in a collision expands and cools. Furthermore, their out-of-equilibrium dynamics must also influence the hydrodynamic evolution of the cooling droplet. The recently developed Hydro+ formalism allows for a consistent description of both the hydrodynamics and the out-of-equilibrium fluctuations, including the feedback between them. We shall explicitly demonstrate how this works, setting up a Hydro+ simulation in a simplified setting: a rapidity-independent fireball undergoing radial flow with an equation of state in which we imagine a critical point close to the $μ_B=0$ axis of the phase diagram. Within this setup, we show that we can quantitatively capture non-equilibrium phenomena, including critical fluctuations over a range of scales and memory effects. Furthermore, we illustrate the interplay between the dynamics of the fluctuations and the hydrodynamic flow of the fireball: as the fluid cools and flows, the dynamical fluctuations lag relative to how they would evolve if they stayed in equilibrium; there is then a backreaction on the flow itself due to the out-of-equilibrium fluctuations; and, in addition, the radial flow transports fluctuations outwards by advection. Within our model, we find that the backreaction from the out-of-equilibrium fluctuations does not yield dramatically large effects in the hydrodynamic variables. Further work will be needed in order to check this quantitative conclusion in other settings but, if it persists, this will considerably simplify future modelling.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the Hydro+ formalism can quantitatively capture out-of-equilibrium critical fluctuations and their backreaction on hydrodynamic flow in a simplified model of a QGP fireball, but they find that the backreaction from out-of-equilibrium fluctuations does not yield dramatically large effects in the hydrodynamic variables, a conclusion that requires further verification in more realistic settings.

## Method

The authors implement the Hydro+ formalism in a simplified setting: a boost-invariant, azimuthally symmetric fireball undergoing radial expansion at zero baryon chemical potential, with an equation of state that includes an imagined critical point near the mu_B=0 axis. They solve coupled deterministic equations for the hydrodynamic variables (energy density, fluid velocity) and for the Wigner transform of the equal-time two-point function of the order parameter fluctuations, which evolves according to a relaxation rate equation that incorporates critical slowing down, advection by radial flow, and memory effects.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is set to eta_plus/s = eta/s = 1/(4 pi), a reasonable value within the range of strong-coupling estimates.
- The shear relaxation time is chosen as T * tau_Pi = 4 eta_plus/s, lying between the weak-coupling value (tau_Pi T = 6 eta/s) and the strong-coupling value (tau_Pi T = 2.63 eta/s) from N=4 SYM.
- The numerical instability from terms proportional to 1/r in the shear tensor equation is handled by setting those terms to zero for r less than 0.12 fm.
- The simulation covers center-of-mass energies between 7.7 GeV and 20 GeV per nucleon, producing QGP droplets that freeze out with mu_B between approximately 200 MeV and 400 MeV (these are experimental context numbers, not simulation results).

## Context

This work extends the Hydro+ formalism developed by Stephanov and Yin (2017) by providing the first explicit numerical implementation of the coupled dynamics between out-of-equilibrium critical fluctuations and hydrodynamic evolution. It builds upon earlier work by Berdnikov and Rajagopal (1999) that established the necessity of treating critical fluctuations out of equilibrium due to critical slowing down, and complements stochastic approaches by Kapusta and collaborators (2012, 2017) and Nahrgang and collaborators (2018). The results enable future full 3D simulations with realistic initial conditions and freezeout that will be needed to interpret data from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program.
