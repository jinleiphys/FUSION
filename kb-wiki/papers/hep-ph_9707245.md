# Disoriented chiral condensate formation from tubes of hot quark plasma

> We investigate the time evolution of a system of quarks interacting with sigma and pion fields starting from an initial configuration consisting of a tube of hot quark plasma undergoing a boost-invariant longitudinal expansion. We work within the framework of the linear sigma model using classical transport equations for the quarks coupled to the mean-field equations for the meson fields. In certain cases we find strong amplifications of any initial pion fields. For large-radius tubes, starting from quark densities that are very close to critical, we find that a disoriented chiral condensate can form in the centre of the tube. Eventually the collapse of the tube drives this state back to the true vacuum. This process converts the disoriented condensate, dominated by long-wavelength pion modes, into a coherent excitation of the pion field that includes significant components with transverse momenta of around 400 MeV. In contrast, for narrow tubes or larger initial temperatures, amplification occurs only via the pion-laser-like mechanism found previously for spherical systems. In addition, we find that explicit chiral symmetry breaking significantly suppresses the formation of disoriented condensates.

# Disoriented chiral condensate formation from tubes of hot quark plasma

## Key claim

For large-radius tubes (r₀ > 5 fm) with initial quark densities very close to critical, a disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) can form in the centre of the tube, but this state is transient: the collapse of the tube drives it back to the true vacuum, converting the DCC into a coherent excitation of the pion field with significant components at transverse momenta around 400 MeV. The formation is significantly suppressed by explicit chiral symmetry breaking at the physical pion mass.

## Method

The authors solve the classical Euler-Lagrange equations for the sigma and pion fields of the linear sigma model, coupled to a relativistic Vlasov equation for quarks and antiquarks, using a test-particle method with 40,000 test particles. The system is initialized as a uniform tube of hot quark plasma at proper time τ₀ = 1 fm/c, undergoing boost-invariant longitudinal expansion, with cylindrical symmetry assumed. The evolution is studied for various tube radii (r₀ = 3–6 fm) and initial temperatures (T = 225–250 MeV), with and without explicit chiral symmetry breaking (m_π = 139 MeV).

## Key numbers

- Tube radius r₀ = 6 fm, initial temperature T = 250 MeV, τ₀ = 1 fm/c: DCC forms at τ ≈ 2.5 fm/c when the pion field at small radii rises rapidly and oscillates about a value close to f_π = 93 MeV.
- For the same tube (r₀ = 6 fm, T = 250 MeV, chiral limit): the total pion number per unit rapidity N_π is enhanced by a factor of about 2000 compared to initial conditions.
- For the same tube with explicit symmetry breaking (m_π = 139 MeV, T = 225 MeV): N_π is enhanced by a factor of about 200 by τ ≈ 10 fm/c.
- For spherical systems studied previously (Paper I): maximum enhancement factors were less than 100.
- The σ meson mass is set to m_σ = 1000 MeV, the quark mass to M_q = 300 MeV, and the critical temperature for the chiral phase transition is T₀ ≈ 235 MeV.
- Significant pion strength builds up in modes with transverse momenta in the range 300–500 MeV, with a smaller peak at 700–900 MeV.
- DCC formation occurs only for tubes with r₀ > 5 fm; for r₀ < 5 fm, only the pion-laser-like mechanism operates.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of spherical droplets of hot quark matter (Abada and Birse, 1997, referred to as Paper I) to the more realistic cylindrical geometry relevant to the central regions of ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. It builds on the linear sigma model approach used by Csernai and Mishustin (1994) for infinite matter undergoing one-dimensional expansion, and by Asakawa et al. (1995) for cylindrical systems. The results show that DCC formation in finite tubes is sensitive to the competition between longitudinal expansion (which can trigger DCC formation) and transverse collapse (which destroys it), unlike infinite systems where only the former operates. The work enables future studies of DCC formation with more realistic initial conditions, including thermal fluctuations and three-dimensional dynamics, and highlights the need for better models of the early stages of heavy-ion collisions to determine the freeze-out proper time and temperature.
