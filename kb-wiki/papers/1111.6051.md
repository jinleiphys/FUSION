# Development of a Cox-Thompson inverse scattering method to charged particles

> A Cox-Thompson fixed-energy quantum inverse scattering method is developed further to treat long-range Coulomb interaction. Depending on the reference potentials chosen, two methods have been formulated which produce inverse potentials with singular or finite value at the origin. Based on the quality of reproduction of input experimental phase shifts, it is guessed that the p-alpha interaction possesses an interesting repulsive hard core.

## Key claim

The Cox-Thompson fixed-energy inverse scattering method can be extended to treat long-range Coulomb interactions, producing inverse potentials that are either singular or finite at the origin depending on the chosen reference potential; based on the quality of phase shift reproduction, the authors "guess" that the p-alpha interaction may possess a repulsive hard core, rather than claiming this as a definitive conclusion.

## Method

Three extensions of the Cox-Thompson method were formulated: the phase transformation method (PCT), which uses a matching radius to create a short-ranged modified potential; the generalized Coulomb CT method (CCT), which employs the bare Coulomb potential as reference; and the modified Coulomb CT method (MCT), which uses a Coulomb potential that is constant inside a matching radius. All methods convert a finite set of nuclear phase shifts into a central potential by solving systems of nonlinear equations for shifted angular momenta, then constructing the potential via a Gel'fand-Levitan-type kernel. The methods were tested on synthetic alpha-alpha scattering data and applied to experimental p-alpha phase shifts from Ali, Ahmad and Ferdous.

## Key numbers

- For alpha-alpha scattering at E_cm = 25 MeV, the CCT method reproduced the original phase shifts with differences Delta_hat_delta_l ranging from 0.0001 to 0.0176 radians across partial waves l = 0 to 9.
- For alpha-alpha scattering at E_cm = 35 MeV, the CCT method reproduced phase shifts with differences from 0.0001 to 0.0079 radians across partial waves l = 0 to 12.
- For the singular model potential at E_cm = 25 MeV, the CCT method reproduced phase shifts with differences from 0.0002 to 0.0094 radians across partial waves l = 0 to 15.
- For p-alpha scattering at E_lab = 17.45 MeV (E_cm = 13.96 MeV, k = 0.731 fm^-1), the CCT method reproduced phase shifts with differences from 0.0139 to 0.0250 radians for partial waves l = 0 to 3, while the PCT method gave differences from 0.0108 to 0.0765 radians.
- The matching radius for PCT and MCT procedures was set to r_a = 10 fm for alpha-alpha cases and r_a = 7 fm for p-alpha cases.
- The p-alpha inverse potentials showed a range of 3-4 fm and strength of 50-70 MeV (PCT) and 50-160 MeV (CCT).

## Context

This work extends the Cox-Thompson inverse scattering method, previously applied only to uncharged particle scattering (Palmai et al., 2003; Melchert, 2005), to charged particle scattering with long-range Coulomb tails. It builds on the modified Newton-Sabatier method used by Apagyi et al. (1991, 1994) for systems like 12C-12C elastic scattering and p-alpha spin-orbit potentials (Alexander, 1996), but overcomes the Newton-Sabatier method's requirement for an infinite set of phase shifts and its tendency to produce oscillatory potentials. The new methods enable model-independent reconstruction of effective nuclear potentials from experimental phase shift data for charged-particle systems, with the CCT method being particularly suited for interactions involving repulsive cores or nonlocality.
