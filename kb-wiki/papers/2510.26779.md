# Determination of the initial condition for the Balitsky-Kovchegov equation with transformers

> In the high-energy limit of QCD, scattering off nucleons and nuclei can be described in terms of Wilson-line correlators whose energy dependence is perturbative. The energy dependence of the two-point correlator, called the dipole amplitude, is governed by the Balitsky-Kovchegov (BK) equation. The initial condition for the BK equation can be fitted to the experimental data, which requires evolving the dipole amplitude for a large set of different parameter values. In this work, we train a transformer model to learn the energy dependence of the dipole amplitude, skipping the time-consuming numerical evaluation of the BK equation. The transformer predicts the learned dipole amplitude and the leading order inclusive deep inelastic scattering cross section very accurately, allowing for efficient fitting of the initial condition to the experimental data. Using this setup, we fit the initial condition of the BK equation to the inclusive deep inelastic scattering data from HERA and consider two different starting points $x_0$ for the evolution. We find better agreement with the experimental data for a smaller $x_0$. This work paves the way for future studies involving global fits of the dipole amplitude at leading order and beyond.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a transformer-based emulator can accurately reproduce the Balitsky-Kovchegov (BK) evolution of the dipole amplitude and the leading-order inclusive deep inelastic scattering cross section, enabling efficient fitting of the initial condition to HERA data. They find better agreement with experimental data for a smaller starting point $x_0 = 0.01$ compared to $x_0 = 0.05$, though they emphasize the analysis is restricted to leading-order accuracy and primarily aims to demonstrate methodology rather than obtain the most precise phenomenological fit.

## Method

The authors generate a library of 10,000 BK equation solutions using Latin hypercube sampling over the parameter space of the initial condition (saturation scale $Q_{s0}$, anomalous dimension $\gamma$, infrared regulator $e_c$, and running-coupling scale $C^2$). They train a transformer encoder with four layers, eight attention heads, and feed-forward width 512 to emulate the dipole amplitude $N(r,x)$ as a function of the four parameters plus kinematic variables $(r,x)$, then train a second transformer to map directly from parameters plus $(x,Q^2)$ to the normalized DIS reduced cross section $\sigma_r/(\sigma_0/2)$. The best-fit parameters are determined by minimizing a chi-squared function against 403-425 data points from combined HERA neutral current $e^+p$ data, with uncertainties estimated using 200 replica fits.

## Key numbers

- Mean and median relative errors of the dipole amplitude emulator on the validation set: 0.09% and 0.05%, respectively
- 99.978% of validation points for the dipole amplitude fall within the +/-1% interval
- Mean and median relative errors of the DIS cross section emulator: 0.115% and 0.073%, respectively
- 99.755% of validation points for the DIS cross section fall within the +/-1% interval
- For $x_0 = 0.01$ with 5-parameter fit: $Q_{s,0}^2 = 0.068^{+0.024}_{-0.015}$ GeV$^2$, $\sigma_0/2 = 14.8^{+0.9}_{-2.4}$ mb, $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof} = 0.857$
- For $x_0 = 0.05$ with 5-parameter fit: $Q_{s,0}^2 = 0.045^{+0.0021}_{-0.0017}$ GeV$^2$, $\sigma_0/2 = 16.6^{+0.2}_{-0.4}$ mb, $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof} = 1.195$
- For $x_0 = 0.01$ with 4-parameter fit ($\gamma$ fixed to 1.0): $Q_{s,0}^2 = 0.063^{+0.001}_{-0.004}$ GeV$^2$, $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof} = 0.854$
- For $x_0 = 0.05$ with 4-parameter fit ($\gamma$ fixed to 1.0): $Q_{s,0}^2 = 0.025^{+0.0035}_{-0.0025}$ GeV$^2$, $\chi^2/\mathrm{dof} = 1.471$
- Training dataset contains approximately $4.7 \times 10^7$ values of $N(r,x)$ from 10,000 parameter sets
- The $r$ grid spans $10^{-6} \leq r/\mathrm{GeV}^{-1} \leq 10^{2}$ with 150 points; the rapidity grid spans $0 \leq \ln(x_0/x) \leq 16$ with step 0.2

## Context

This work extends previous extractions of the dipole amplitude from HERA data using the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model and its generalizations at leading order (Albacete 2009, Lappi 2013, Ducloue 2019, Beuf 2020, Dumitru 2023, Casuga 2023, Hanninen 2025) and next-to-leading order (Hanninen 2022, Casuga 2025). It introduces a transformer-based emulator as an alternative to Gaussian-process emulators previously applied in small-$x$ studies (Casuga 2023, Casuga 2025), offering the ability to accommodate larger training sets and capture correlations across parameter space. The methodology enables future global fits of the dipole amplitude at leading order and beyond, and paves the way for precision studies at the future Electron-Ion Collider.
