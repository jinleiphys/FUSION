# Overview of lattice calculations of the $x$-dependence of PDFs, GPDs and TMDs

> For a long time, lattice QCD was unable to address the $x$-dependence of partonic distributions, direct access to which is impossible in Euclidean spacetime. Recent years have brought a breakthrough for such calculations when it was realized that partonic light-cone correlations can be accessed through spatial correlations computable on the lattice. Appropriately devised observables can be factorized into physical PDFs via a perturbative procedure called matching, analogous to the standard factorization of experimental cross sections. In this short review, aimed at a broader high-energy and nuclear physics community, we discuss the recent highlights of this research program. Key concepts are outlined, followed by a case study illustrating the typical stage of current lattice extractions and by a brief review of the most recent explorations. We finalize with a number of messages for the prospects of lattice determinations of partonic structure.

# Overview of lattice calculations of the x-dependence of PDFs, GPDs and TMDs

## Key claim

Lattice QCD can now access the x-dependence of partonic distributions through spatial correlations computable on the lattice, but current extractions remain qualitative rather than quantitative because no complete calculation has robustly quantified all systematic effects. The paper argues that while the sources of systematics are known and can be addressed, present-day results should not yet be compared with experimental or phenomenological results.

## Method

The paper reviews the quasi-distribution and pseudo-distribution approaches, in which one computes matrix elements of the form M(z,P) = ⟨N(P3)| ψ(z) Γ A(z,0) ψ(0) |N(P3)⟩, where the hadron is boosted in the z-direction and a Wilson line of length z maintains gauge invariance. These Euclidean matrix elements are then factorized into physical parton distribution functions via perturbative matching, analogous to the standard factorization of experimental cross sections. The review covers calculations for twist-2 PDFs, flavor-singlet PDFs, gluon PDFs, twist-3 PDFs, GPDs, and TMDs, with a case study focusing on unpolarized isovector PDFs from the ETMC and HadStruc collaborations.

## Key numbers

- Typical lattice sizes employed: L/a ∈ [32, 128] and lattice spacings a ∈ [0.03, 0.15] fm
- Nucleon boosts up to approximately 2-2.5 GeV at non-physical pion masses and around 1.5 GeV at the physical point before signal-to-noise becomes severe
- ETMC calculation used a lattice spacing a = 0.094 fm and L/a = 48 at the physical point
- HadStruc calculation used three pion masses: mπ = 358, 278, 172 MeV with the same lattice spacing a = 0.094 fm and L/a = 32, 64
- For the ETMC unpolarized PDFs, the total uncertainty from combining statistical and plausible systematic effects (cyan band) led to agreement with global fits for the whole x-range, while statistical-only errors (purple bands) showed tension
- For twist-3 PDFs, Wandzura-Wilczek relations hold within statistical errors for x ≲ 0.5, with error magnitudes allowing violations up to around 40%
- First flavor-singlet PDFs were obtained at a non-physical pion mass mπ = 260 MeV
- First gluon PDF study used mπ = 358 MeV with clover fermions
- TMD explorations used mπ = 333 MeV (LPC) and mπ = 350 MeV (Beijing+ETMC)

## Context

This review extends the line of work initiated by Ji (2013) proposing quasi-distributions and the large momentum effective theory (LaMET), which revived earlier approaches by Liu (1993), Detmold (2005), and Braun (2007). It updates previous reviews by Cichy (2018), Ji (2020), Constantinou (2020), and Cichy (2021). The paper enables the broader high-energy and nuclear physics community to understand the current stage of lattice calculations, with the prospect that lattice QCD can provide complementary constraints for distributions where experimental data are sparse, particularly for helicity and transversity PDFs, GPDs, and TMDs, in preparation for data from the 12 GeV upgrade at Jefferson Lab and the future Electron-Ion Collider.
