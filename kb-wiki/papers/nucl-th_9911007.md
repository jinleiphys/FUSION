# Dynamical restriction for a growing neck due to mass parameters in a dinuclear system

> Mass parameters for collective variables of a dinuclear system and strongly deformed mononucleus are microscopically formulated with the linear response theory making use of the width of single particle states and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. For the relative motion of the nuclei and for the degree of freedom describing the neck between the nuclei, we calculate mass parameters with basis states of the adiabatic and diabatic two-center shell model. Microscopical mass parameters are found larger than the ones obtained with the hydrodynamical model and give a strong hindrance for a melting of the dinuclear system along the internuclear distance into a compound system. Therefore, the dinuclear system lives a long time enough comparable to the reaction time for fusion by nucleon transfer. Consequences of this effect for the complete fusion process are discussed.

# Dynamical restriction for a growing neck due to mass parameters in a dinuclear system

## Key claim

Microscopically calculated mass parameters for the neck degree of freedom in a dinuclear system are 20–30 times larger than those from the hydrodynamical Werner-Wheeler approximation, which dynamically hinders the melting of the dinuclear system into a compound nucleus and thereby justifies the fixed-neck assumption of the DNS model, but only when the finite width of single-particle states (due to residual interactions) is included in the linear response theory.

## Method

Mass parameters for the collective variables (elongation λ, neck parameter ε, mass asymmetry η, and fragment deformations β) were derived within the linear response theory using the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and a Lorentzian broadening of single-particle levels with width Γ. The diagonal part of the mass tensor, dominated by the diagonal matrix elements of the coupling operator, was evaluated numerically in both the adiabatic and diabatic two-center shell model for symmetric and asymmetric dinuclear systems such as ¹¹⁰Pd+¹¹⁰Pd and ⁴⁸Ca+¹⁷²Hf.

## Key numbers

- Microscopical neck mass parameter M_εε ≈ (20–30) × M_WW_εε, where M_WW_εε is the Werner-Wheeler hydrodynamical value, for symmetric systems at the touching configuration.
- M_λλ (mass for elongation) is approximately equal to the Werner-Wheeler value: M_λλ = M_WW_λλ.
- The nondiagonal mass M_λε ≈ 0.4 × M_WW_λε, and M_λε / sqrt(M_λλ M_εε) ≪ 1.
- For the system ¹¹⁰Pd+¹¹⁰Pd at λ = 1.6 (touching configuration), M_εε increases by a factor of 2.5 as the system moves into the fission-type valley (ε → 0).
- M_diag_εε(T₀ = 1 MeV) / M_diag_εε(T₀ = 1.5 MeV) ≈ 5, showing a strong temperature dependence (∼ T₀⁻⁴).
- The single-particle width Γ_k is parameterized with Γ₀⁻¹ = 0.045 MeV⁻¹ and c = 20 MeV; the mass parameters depend only weakly on c.

## Context

This work extends the linear response treatment of nuclear inertia (Hofmann, 1997; Ivanyuk, 1993) to the dinuclear system geometry, providing a microscopic foundation for the fixed-neck assumption in the DNS model of fusion (Volkov, 1978; Adamian et al., 1997). The results contradict the hydrodynamical Werner-Wheeler approximation (used in earlier fusion models) which predicts much faster neck growth, and instead support the picture that the dinuclear system lives long enough for thermal fluctuations in mass asymmetry to drive fusion via nucleon transfer. The findings also connect to studies of structural forbiddenness (Adamian et al., 1997; Diaz-Torres et al., 1998) and enable more realistic dynamical calculations of fusion and quasifission in heavy-ion reactions.
