# Particle production in strong electromagnetic fields in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> I review the origin and properties of electromagnetic fields produced in heavy ion collisions. The field strength immediately after a collision is proportional to the collision energy and reaches eB\sim(m_π)^2 at RHIC and eB\sim10 (m_π)^2 at LHC. I demonstrate by explicit analytical calculation that after dropping by about one-two orders of magnitude during the first fm/c of plasma expansion, it freezes out and lasts for as long as quark-gluon plasma exists as a consequence of finite electrical conductivity of the plasma. Magnetic field breaks spherical symmetry in the direction perpendicular to the reaction plane and therefore all kinetic coefficients are anisotropic. I examine viscosity of QGP and show that magnetic field induces azimuthal anisotropy on plasma flow even in spherically symmetric geometry. Very strong electromagnetic field has an important impact on particle production. I discuss the problem of energy loss and polarization of fast fermions due to synchrotron radiation, consider photon decay induced by magnetic field, elucidate J/Psi dissociation via Lorentz ionization mechanism and examine electromagnetic radiation by plasma. I conclude that all processes in QGP are affected by strong electromagnetic field and call for experimental investigation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the extremely strong electromagnetic fields produced in heavy-ion collisions (reaching \(eB \sim m_\pi^2\) at RHIC and \(eB \sim 10 m_\pi^2\) at LHC) persist for the entire lifetime of the quark-gluon plasma due to finite electrical conductivity, and that these fields affect all QGP processes, but the author notes that separating electromagnetic effects from strong color force effects in experimental observables remains an outstanding challenge.

## Method

The author derives the time evolution of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions using the Liénard-Wiechert potentials for protons traveling on straight-line trajectories (eikonal approximation), then extends the calculation to include medium effects by solving Maxwell's equations with a finite electrical conductivity \(\sigma\) in both static and expanding (Bjorken-scenario) quark-gluon plasma. The magnetic field relaxation is modeled via a diffusion equation, and the viscous pressure tensor in strong magnetic fields is calculated using a kinetic equation approach that expands the distribution function deviation in terms of five shear viscosity coefficients.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC (\(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, Au+Au), the initial magnetic field strength is \(eB \approx m_\pi^2 \sim 10^{18}\) G, corresponding to \(eB \approx (2.5 m_\pi)^2\) right after collision
- At LHC, the initial field reaches \(eB \sim 10 m_\pi^2\)
- The electrical conductivity of static QGP from lattice calculations is \(\sigma = (5.8 \pm 2.9) \, (T/T_c) \, \text{MeV}\)
- The magnetic field at RHIC decreases to \(eB \approx (m_\pi/4)^2\) at \(t \approx 5\) fm
- The maximum magnetic field in a conducting medium occurs at time \(t = b^2\sigma/8\) and has value \(eB_\text{max} = 32 e^{-2} \alpha_\text{em} / (b^3 \sigma)\)
- The ratio of maximum field in conducting medium to vacuum field is \(B_\text{max}/B_0 = 32 e^{-2} / (\sigma b \gamma)\), which at RHIC (\(\gamma = 100\), \(b = 7\) fm, \(\sigma = 5.8\) MeV) is much larger than the vacuum case
- The inhomogeneity parameter for Schwinger pair production in QGP is \(\tilde \gamma \sim 0.01\) (using \(m_e = 0.5\) MeV, \(\lambda \sim 0.5\) fm, \(eE \sim m_\pi^2\))
- The response time of Schwinger pair current density is \(\sim 10^4\) fm/c, much larger than the plasma lifetime \(\sim 10\) fm/c

## Context

This review extends earlier work on electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions by Kharzeev (2007) and Bzdak (2011), who first calculated the field strength using realistic nuclear charge distributions, and by Skokov (2009), Voronyuk (2011), and Deng (2012) who examined the fields in more elaborated approaches. The paper shows that finite electrical conductivity causes the magnetic field to freeze into the plasma (analogous to the skin effect in conductors), contradicting the naive expectation that the field would decay rapidly in vacuum. This enables future experimental searches for magnetic-field-induced phenomena such as azimuthal anisotropy of flow, synchrotron radiation, photon decay, and J/Psi dissociation via Lorentz ionization.
