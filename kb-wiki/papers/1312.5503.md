# Initial State Fluctuations and Final State Correlations in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We review the phenomenology and theory of bulk observables in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, focussing on recent developments involving event-by-event fluctuations in the initial stages of a heavy ion collision, and how they manifest in observed correlations. We first define the relevant observables and show how each measurement is related to underlying theoretical quantities. Then we review the prevailing picture of the various stages of a collision, including the state-of-the-art modeling of the initial stages of a collision and subsequent hydrodynamic evolution, as well as hadronic scattering and freeze-out in the later stages. We then discuss the recent results that have shaped our current understanding and identify the challenges that remain. Finally, we point out open issues and the potential for progress in the field.

## Key claim

Initial state fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, arising from quantum fluctuations in the earliest stages including the nucleonic structure of the nucleus, are essential for understanding bulk observables; the authors review how these fluctuations manifest in final-state correlations and argue that the odd Fourier harmonics (e.g., triangular flow) exist only because of event-by-event fluctuations and have little or no geometry component, in contrast to the even moments.

## Method

The paper reviews the phenomenology and theory of bulk observables in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, defining relevant observables such as anisotropic flow coefficients $v_n$ and their relation to underlying theoretical quantities. It describes the state-of-the-art modeling of the initial stages of a collision, subsequent hydrodynamic evolution, and hadronic scattering and freeze-out in the later stages, with a focus on event-by-event fluctuations and how they manifest in observed correlations.

## Key numbers

- Temperatures reached in nuclear collisions at RHIC (up to 200 GeV per nucleon pair) and LHC ($\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV) are approximately 200-500 MeV.
- The statistical uncertainty in reconstructing an anisotropy coefficient $v_n$ from $N$ samples of the distribution is approximately $1/\sqrt{2N}$.
- Anisotropies typically have a value of a few percent, while the number of particles detected in a single event is at most a few thousand, resulting in a typical relative uncertainty of 50% or more in any single-event measurement.
- The fully momentum-integrated two-particle cumulant measurement reduces to $\bar v_n\{2\} = \sqrt{\langle \bar v_n^2 \rangle + \langle \bar\delta_{2,n} \rangle}$.
- Assuming no event-by-event fluctuations introduces at least a ~10% error in any model with realistic fluctuations.

## Context

This review extends the understanding of anisotropic flow from earlier work on elliptic flow at RHIC (Huovinen 2001) and the discovery of the importance of initial state fluctuations in Cu+Cu collisions (Alver 2006). It builds on the realization that triangular flow and higher Fourier coefficients arise from event-by-event fluctuations (Alver 2010), and connects to the analogy with cosmic microwave background measurements (Mocsy 2010). The review enables quantitative extraction of transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma, such as the shear viscosity over entropy ratio, by constraining initial conditions from experimental data.
