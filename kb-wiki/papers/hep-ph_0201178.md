# Particle Astrophysics and Cosmology: Cosmic Laboratories for New Physics (Summary of the Snowmass 2001 P4 Working Group)

> The past few years have seen dramatic breakthroughs and spectacular and puzzling discoveries in astrophysics and cosmology. In many cases, the new observations can only be explained with the introduction of new fundamental physics. Here we summarize some of these recent advances. We then describe several problem in astrophysics and cosmology, ripe for major advances, whose resolution will likely require new physics.

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent astrophysical and cosmological observations (circa 2001) can only be explained by new fundamental physics beyond the Standard Model, but it hedges that many of these interpretations remain provisional pending further experimental confirmation and that the connection between observations and new physics is often indirect.

## Method

The Snowmass 2001 P4 Working Group convened over 200 scientists from particle physics and astrophysics into eight topical subgroups (dark matter, gamma rays and X-rays, CMB and inflation, structure formation, cosmic rays, gravitational radiation, neutrino astrophysics, and early Universe physics). The group reviewed recent observational breakthroughs, surveyed the next generation of experiments and their capabilities, and identified science questions at the interface of particle physics, astrophysics, and cosmology that could be addressed with future experiments.

## Key numbers

* Total energy density of the Universe Omega_tot is close to unity, with matter density Omega_m approximately 0.3, implying approximately 70% of the energy density is dark energy
* 25% of the density of the Universe must be in nonbaryonic dark matter, with only 4% due to baryons
* WIMP dark matter candidates have masses in the 10-1000 GeV/c^2 range
* KSVZ axion models corresponding to 2.9 < m_a < 3.3 micro-eV have been tested and ruled out
* The diffuse isotropic gamma-ray flux is approximately 1.5 x 10^-5 cm^-2 s^-1 sr^-1 for E > 100 MeV
* AGN flares are believed to reach 10^45 ergs/sec
* The energy scale of inflation is constrained to be less than 2 x 10^16 GeV from CMB temperature fluctuations
* Planck satellite (launch 2007) should detect CMB polarization curl if inflation energy scale is greater than roughly 5 x 10^15 GeV

## Context

This paper summarizes the activities of the Snowmass 2001 P4 Working Group, extending the work of the previous Snowmass96 meeting. It reviews dramatic advances since Snowmass96 including CMB evidence favoring inflation (BOOMERanG, DASI, MAXIMA), supernova evidence for dark energy, and solar/atmospheric neutrino oscillation results. The paper identifies opportunities for the next decade, including direct dark matter detection experiments (CDMS, DAMA, Edelweiss), next-generation gamma-ray instruments (GLAST, VERITAS, Milagro), and CMB polarization experiments to probe inflationary gravitational waves. It enables a coordinated approach between particle physics, astrophysics, and cosmology communities for future experimental programs.
