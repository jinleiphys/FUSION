# Quark model of nucleon based on an analogy with polaron

> We demonstrate that the polaron theory from solid state physics can serve as an interesting analogue model for non-perturbative QCD, at least in the description of nucleons and related low-energy physics of strong interactions. By drawing explicit analogies between polaron physics, arising for an electron moving in an ionic crystal, and physics of pion-nucleon interactions, certain rules for the "polaron/QCD correspondence" are proposed. In polaron theory, the effective fermion mass as a function of the coupling constant is known both in the weak and strong coupling limits. The conjectured "polaron/QCD correspondence" translates these results into strong interactions. It is then shown how application of these rules leads to unexpectedly good quantitative predictions for the nucleon mass and the pion-nucleon sigma term. The polaron approach also predicts that the quark degrees of freedom in the form of the constituent quark account for one-third of the nucleon mass, consistent with lattice predictions. We discuss possible physical reasons underlying the observed quantitative similarity between polaron physics and non-perturbative QCD.

## Key claim

The polaron theory from solid state physics can serve as an analogue model for non-perturbative QCD in the description of nucleons, provided one accepts a set of proposed correspondence rules that map electron-phonon interactions onto quark-pion interactions. The model yields quantitative predictions for the nucleon mass and the pion-nucleon sigma term that match experimental values, but the correspondence is conjectural and relies on a non-relativistic treatment of strong interactions.

## Method

The authors establish a "polaron/QCD correspondence" by mapping the longitudinal optical polaron Hamiltonian onto the pion-nucleon interaction Hamiltonian, identifying phonons with pions, electrons with quarks, and the polaron effective mass with the constituent quark mass. They then apply known asymptotic results from polaron theory for the ground state energy and effective mass in both weak and strong coupling limits, translating these via the correspondence rules into predictions for the nucleon mass and the pion-nucleon sigma term. The numerical predictions are obtained by inserting the pion mass (140 MeV), current quark masses (approximately 5.5 MeV), and the constituent quark mass (313 MeV, taken as one-third of the nucleon mass) into the polaron scaling relations.

## Key numbers

- The pion-nucleon coupling constant is given as alpha_piNN = g^2_piNN/(4pi) approximately equal to 13.9, with g_piNN approximately 13.2 (Epelbaum).
- The pion mass used as the phonon energy scale is m_pi = 140 MeV (Particle Data Group).
- The sum of current u and d quark masses at the pion mass scale is m_u + m_d approximately 11 MeV, with m_u approximately 4 MeV and m_d approximately 7 MeV (Gasser).
- The quark condensate value is given as <q_bar q> = -(247.4 MeV)^3 for m_pi = 140 MeV.
- The constituent quark mass is taken as m_q* = 313 MeV, derived from one-third of the mean nucleon mass (939 MeV).
- Using the polaron strong-coupling formulas, the predicted nucleon mass is M_N approximately 939 MeV, matching the mean nucleon mass.
- The effective quark-pion coupling constant extracted from the polaron mass formula is alpha_eff approximately 7.3.
- An alternative estimate using the scaling relation alpha = alpha_0 sqrt(m*/m) gives alpha_eff approximately 7.6, assuming a frozen QCD coupling alpha_s^(fr) approximately 1.
- In the weak coupling limit, the model predicts the pion-nucleon sigma term sigma_piN approximately 40-60 MeV, matching modern estimates.

## Context

This work extends the only previous attempt to apply polaron theory to strong interactions by Iwao (1976), who proposed the polaron mechanism for constituent quark mass generation but made a numerical error in the coupling estimate. The approach contrasts with the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (Nambu and Jona-Lasinio, 1961) which describes spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking through fermion condensate formation, and with the AdS/QCD correspondence from string theory. The polaron model offers the conceptual advantage of being based on a theory that describes real, experimentally verifiable physics in solid state systems. The results enable a potential semi-analytical connection between polaron theory and QCD via the energy-momentum tensor, as discussed in the appendix, and suggest that further development of the correspondence could provide a non-relativistic framework for understanding nucleon structure and the dynamical generation of hadron masses.
