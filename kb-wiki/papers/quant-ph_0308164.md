# Estimation of the Local Density of States on a Quantum Computer

> We report an efficient quantum algorithm for estimating the local density of states (LDOS) on a quantum computer. The LDOS describes the redistribution of energy levels of a quantum system under the influence of a perturbation. Sometimes known as the ``strength function'' from nuclear spectroscopy experiments, the shape of the LDOS is directly related to the survivial probability of unperturbed eigenstates, and has recently been related to the fidelity decay (or ``Loschmidt echo'') under imperfect motion-reversal. For quantum systems that can be simulated efficiently on a quantum computer, the LDOS estimation algorithm enables an exponential speed-up over direct classical computation.

## Key claim

The paper reports an efficient quantum algorithm for estimating the local density of states (LDOS) that achieves an exponential speed-up over classical computation, but only for quantum systems that admit Polylog(N) gate decompositions and only when the coarse-grained features of the LDOS (such as its width) are of interest, not the full distribution.

## Method

The algorithm uses two successive phase-estimation circuits on the unperturbed unitary operator U and the perturbed operator U(σ), with an upper register of log₂(M) ancillary qubits setting the precision and a lower register of O(log₂(N)) qubits implementing the maps. The initial state is chosen either as a known eigenstate of U (when available) or as the maximally mixed state, and the joint statistics of the phase-estimation outcomes from both registers are accumulated over repeated runs to estimate the coarse-grained transition probabilities between unperturbed and perturbed eigenstates.

## Key numbers

- The algorithm estimates the LDOS to 1/Polylog(N) accuracy using only Polylog(N) resources, compared to classical computation requiring resources growing at least as N².
- The upper register contains m_q = log₂(M) qubits, where M is chosen to be O(log(N)) to avoid measuring an exponential amount of information.
- The Breit-Wigner width Γ = 2πσ²ρ_E can be tested efficiently when 1/Polylog(N) ≪ Γ(σ) ≪ O(1), with the upper bound arising from the bandwidth b = N/2 for fully chaotic models.
- For distinguishing two candidate distributions, the required sample size is K = log(ε)/log(λ), where λ is bounded above by the fidelity between the distributions.

## Context

This work extends the program of Feynman (1982) and Lloyd (1996) for efficient quantum simulation by addressing the readout problem: while quantum simulation can be efficient, complete measurement of the final state requires O(N²) repetitions. The LDOS algorithm provides an efficient readout method for a quantity of central interest in nuclear spectroscopy (the "strength function"), many-body physics (Wigner, 1955; Bohr and Mottelson, 1969), and quantum chaos (Heller, 1984; Izrailev, 1990). The algorithm enables efficient testing of the Breit-Wigner hypothesis against alternative LDOS profiles, which previously required direct diagonalization of N×N matrices, and can be applied to study dynamical localization and fidelity decay (Loschmidt echo) in complex quantum systems.
