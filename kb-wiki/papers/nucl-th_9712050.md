# A Method to Study `Chemical' Fluctuations in Nucleus--Nucleus Collisions

> A method to study event--by--event fluctuations of the `chemical' (particle type) composition of the final state of high energy collisions is proposed.}

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to study event-by-event fluctuations of the particle-type composition ("chemical" fluctuations) in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions, with the expectation that it can determine whether chemical equilibrium is reached. The method is an adaptation of the previously proposed "thermal" fluctuation method (TF-method) and relies on the assumption that the statistical properties of the new $\Phi(h_0)$ variable are identical to those of the $\Phi$ variable used for transverse momentum fluctuations.

## Method

The method converts the existing TF-method for transverse momentum fluctuations into a CF-method by substituting the kinematic variable (e.g., $p_T$) with a delta-function $\delta(h^i, h_0)$ that equals 1 if particle $i$ is of type $h_0$ and 0 otherwise. For each event, the quantity $Z = \sum_{i=1}^N z_i$ is calculated, where $z_i = \delta(h^i, h_0) - \overline{\delta(h, h_0)}$ and $\overline{\delta(h, h_0)}$ is the inclusive mean probability that any particle is of type $h_0$. The chemical fluctuation measure $\Phi(h_0)$ is then defined as $\sqrt{\langle Z^2 \rangle / \langle N \rangle} - \sqrt{\overline{z^2}}$, where averages are taken over all events.

## Context

This work extends the TF-method proposed by Gazdzicki (1992) for studying event-by-event fluctuations of kinematic variables, which was recently applied by the NA49 Collaboration to central Pb+Pb collisions at 158 A$\cdot$GeV. The new CF-method is designed to test whether chemical equilibrium is reached in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions, complementing earlier thermal fluctuation analyses that helped reject purely non-equilibrium approaches like initial state scattering models (Gazdzicki, 1997). The method can be applied to study changes in chemical fluctuations across different collision systems (p+p, p+A, A+A) and collision energies.
