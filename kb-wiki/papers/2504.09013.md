# Quantifying uncertainty in machine learning on nuclear binding energy

> Techniques from artificial intelligence and machine learning are increasingly employed in nuclear theory, however, the uncertainties that arise from the complex parameter manifold encoded by the neural networks are often overlooked. Epistemic uncertainties arising from training the same network multiple times for an ensemble of initial weight sets offer a first insight into the confidence of machine learning predictions, but they often come with a high computational cost. Instead, we apply a single-model uncertainty quantification method called Δ-UQ that gives epistemic uncertainties with one-time training. We demonstrate our approach on a 2-feature model of nuclear binding energies per nucleon with proton and neutron number pairs as inputs. We show that Δ-UQ can produce reliable and self-consistent epistemic uncertainty estimates and can be used to assess the degree of confidence in predictions made with deep neural networks.

## Key claim

The Δ-UQ method can produce reliable and self-consistent epistemic uncertainty estimates from a single neural network training run, but the uncertainty bands it generates are generally smaller than those from ensemble methods because Δ-UQ only explores the hypothesis space near a good local minimum, whereas ensemble methods include poor local minima that greatly overestimate epistemic uncertainty.

## Method

The authors applied the Δ-UQ method to a deep neural network with four composite layers (three layers of linear-SiLU-batchnorm plus one linear output layer) and 32 nodes per hidden layer, using proton number Z and neutron number N as the two input features to predict binding energy per nucleon (E/A). The input dimension was doubled by combining each input vector with a set of constant bias vectors ("anchors") drawn from the training data, creating m copies of each input for m anchors. The network was trained on either the AME2020 experimental dataset (3099 nuclei) or a synthetic DFT dataset (10393 nuclei) using the SLy4 energy functional, with training on 90% of the data and validation on 10%, and the best model was selected at the epoch with lowest validation loss after up to 40000 epochs.

## Key numbers

- DFT dataset: 10393 nuclei with Z between 2 and 120, excluding nuclei with A < 18; AME dataset: 3099 nuclei with A ≥ 18
- Training set: 2789 data points; validation set: 310 data points
- Best DFT model: training loss 2.46×10⁻⁴ MeV², validation loss 4.65×10⁻⁴ MeV² at epoch 39858
- Best AME model: training loss 3.55×10⁻⁴ MeV², validation loss 5.51×10⁻⁴ MeV² at epoch 26792
- Single Δ-UQ run: approximately 5 GPU hours on NVIDIA V100, 2.4 GPU hours on NVIDIA H100
- Ensemble of 2789 runs: cumulatively 7530 GPU hours (approximately 2.7 GPU hours per run on V100)
- Absolute difference between DFT and AME E/A data is less than 0.4 MeV for A ≥ 18; average difference per isobar is less than 0.2 MeV

## Context

This work extends single-model uncertainty quantification methods (Thiagarajan 2022) to nuclear binding energy predictions, addressing the high computational cost of ensemble methods previously used in nuclear machine learning (such as the 500-run approach in PhysRevC.106.014305 and the 50-run probabilistic network in PhysRevC.106.014305). The Δ-UQ method enables efficient epistemic uncertainty estimation for deep neural network predictions of nuclear masses, which is critical for extrapolating to neutron-rich and superheavy nuclei where experimental data are sparse and theoretical mass models (such as those from Moller 2016, Duflo 1994, and Goriely 2013) show significant variations.
