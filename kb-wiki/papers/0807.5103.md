# Long Tail of Quantum Decay from Scattering Data

> Whereas the short time behaviour of an unstable quantum mechanical system is well understood from its theoretical as well as experimental side, the long time tail of the very same systems has neither been measured experimentally nor is there a theoretical agreement on how to handle it. We suggest a possible way out of this unsatisfactory state of art. Theoretically we suggest that the correct spectral function entering the Fock-Krylov method to calculate the survival amplitude is proportional to the density of states of a resonance. The latter is essentially the energy derivative of a phase shift. As a bonus, we can connect the survival probability to scattering data via the phase shift. The method then not only establishes the spectral function, but is per se a semi-empirical method to extract the large time behaviour from scattering data.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the correct spectral function for the Fock-Krylov method of calculating survival probability is proportional to the density of states of a resonance, which is essentially the energy derivative of a phase shift; this provides a semi-empirical method to extract the large-time behavior of quantum decay from scattering data, though the method requires modification for s-wave resonances due to threshold singularities.

## Method

The authors use the Fock-Krylov method, where the survival amplitude is a Fourier transform of the spectral function, and identify the spectral function with the Beth-Uhlenbeck density of states given by the energy derivative of the scattering phase shift. They then apply this to calculate survival probabilities and critical transition times for both narrow resonances (using a Breit-Wigner model) and broad resonances (using the sigma meson as an example with an energy-dependent width parameterization from Bugg). For s-wave resonances, they replace the standard time-delay density of states with a dwell-time density of states to remove threshold singularities.

## Key numbers

- For narrow resonances (e.g., ⁸Be with l=2), critical lifetimes are approximately 30 and 70 in units of lifetimes.
- For the broad sigma meson, the short transition time is approximately one lifetime, and the large transition time is approximately eight lifetimes.
- The spectral function for a Breit-Wigner resonance gives dδ/dE = (Γ_R/2)/[(E_R - E)² + Γ_R²/4].
- For l > 0, the threshold exponent γ = l - 1/2, giving a power-law tail P(t) ∝ 1/t^(γ+1).

## Context

This work extends the Fock-Krylov method (Fock and Krylov, 1947) and the Beth-Uhlenbeck density of states (Beth and Uhlenbeck, 1937) to provide a semi-empirical approach for studying the long-time non-exponential decay tail, which was first predicted by Khalfin (1958) but has never been experimentally observed. The method enables calculation of survival probabilities for both narrow and broad resonances directly from scattering phase shift data, and the authors identify threshold resonances as the best candidates for observing non-exponential decay due to their smaller critical times.
