# Diffusion as a possible mechanism controlling the production of superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reactions

> The fusion probability for the production of superheavy nuclei in cold fusion reactions was investigated and compared with recent experimental results for $^{48}$Ca, $^{50}$Ti, and $^{54}$Cr incident on a $^{208}$Pb target. Calculations were performed within the fusion-by-diffusion model (FbD) using new nuclear data tables by Jachimowicz et al. It is shown that the experimental data could be well explained within the framework of the FbD model. The saturation of the fusion probability at bombarding energies above the interaction barrier is reproduced. It emerges naturally from the physical effect of the suppression of contributions of higher partial waves in fusion reactions and is related to the critical angular momentum. The role of the difference in values of the rotational energies in the fusion saddle point and contact (sticking) configuration of the projectile-target system is discussed.

## Key claim

The fusion probability for superheavy nuclei production in cold fusion reactions can be well reproduced by a diffusion-based model (the fusion-by-diffusion model), contrary to a prior claim by Hinde et al. (2018) that the energy dependence of the fusion probability indicates cold fusion reactions are not driven by a diffusion process.

## Method

The authors applied the l-dependent fusion-by-diffusion (FbD) model, which solves the Smoluchowski diffusion equation to calculate the probability that the projectile-target system overcomes the internal fusion barrier after capture. They used new nuclear data tables by Jachimowicz et al. (2021) for ground-state and saddle-point properties of superheavy nuclei, and redefined the systematics of the injection point distance (the only adjustable parameter) by fitting to 27 experimentally measured 1n evaporation residue cross sections. The model calculates the capture cross section using a Gaussian barrier distribution parametrization and the survival probability using classical transition state theory.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{48}$Ca + $^{208}$Pb, the mean entrance channel barrier $B_0$ is 173.0 MeV; for $^{50}$Ti + $^{208}$Pb, $B_0$ = 191.2 MeV; for $^{54}$Cr + $^{208}$Pb, $B_0$ = 208.3 MeV.
- The injection point distance parametrization is $s_{inj} = 0.878~\text{fm} - 0.294 \times (E_{c.m.} - B_0)~\text{fm/MeV}$, with an error corridor of $\pm 1$ fm.
- Scaling factors to reproduce experimental capture cross sections above $B_0$ are 0.96 for $^{48}$Ca + $^{208}$Pb, 0.55 for $^{50}$Ti + $^{208}$Pb, and 0.24 for $^{54}$Cr + $^{208}$Pb.
- The fusion probability saturates when the injection point distance reaches zero at temperatures $T \approx 0.6$–$0.8$ MeV for all three reactions.

## Context

This work directly addresses the conclusion of Hinde et al. (2018), who measured fusion probabilities for $^{48}$Ca, $^{50}$Ti, and $^{54}$Cr on $^{208}$Pb and argued that the energy dependence rules out a diffusion mechanism. The authors show that the FbD model, originally developed by Siwek-Wilczynska et al. (2005, 2011), reproduces the experimental data including the saturation of fusion probability at energies above the barrier, which emerges naturally from the suppression of higher partial waves related to the critical angular momentum. The results enable improved predictions for the production of superheavy nuclei with atomic numbers up to $Z=113$ in cold fusion reactions and provide a framework for understanding the role of rotational energy differences between the saddle and contact configurations.
