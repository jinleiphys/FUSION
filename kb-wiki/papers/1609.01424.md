# Microscopic phase-space exploration modeling of $^{258}$Fm spontaneous fission

> We show that the total kinetic energy (TKE) of nuclei after the spontaneous fission of $^{258}$Fm can be well reproduced using simple assumptions on the quantum collective phase-space explored by the nucleus after passing the fission barrier. Assuming energy conservation and phase-space exploration according to the stochastic mean-field approach, a set of initial densities is generated. Each density is then evolved in time using the nuclear time-dependent density-functional theory. This approach goes beyond mean-field by allowing spontaneous symmetry breaking as well as a wider dynamical phase-space exploration leading to larger fluctuations in collective space. The total kinetic energy and mass distributions are calculated. New information on the fission process: fluctuations in scission time, strong correlation between TKE and collective deformation of daughter nuclei as well as pre- and post-scission particle emission, are obtained.

## Key claim

The total kinetic energy (TKE) distribution after spontaneous fission of \(^{258}\)Fm can be well reproduced by a fully microscopic theory that combines stochastic sampling of initial one-body densities with time-dependent density-functional theory, provided the initial phase-space is constrained by energy conservation; however, the mass asymmetry remains underestimated unless the average excitation energy is increased beyond what is strictly justified by the tunneling probability.

## Method

The authors assume the fissioning nucleus tunnels adiabatically through the fission barrier to a given initial quadrupole deformation \(Q_2^{\rm ini}\), with the excess energy above the ground state converted into internal excitation energy \(E^*\). They then generate an ensemble of initial one-body densities by applying the stochastic mean-field technique: Gaussian random fluctuations are added to the occupation numbers of single-particle states within an energy window around the Fermi level, with the window size adjusted so that the ensemble-averaged energy matches \(E^*\). Each initial density is evolved independently using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TDHF+BCS) code TDHF3D, which includes dynamical pairing, on a spatial grid of \(48.8 \times 48.8 \times 26.4\) fm\(^3\) with a mesh size of 0.8 fm and a time step of \(1.5 \times 10^{-24}\) s. Fission is deemed to have occurred when the fragment separation reaches 26 fm within 4500 fm/\(c\).

## Key numbers

- Mean TKE: \(\overline{E_{\rm TKE}} = 211\) MeV, with standard deviation \(\sigma_E = 19\) MeV, compared to experimental values of \(\overline{E_{\rm TKE}} = 215.5\) MeV and \(\sigma_E = 19.3\) MeV for \(^{258}\)Fm spontaneous fission.
- 350 TDDFT trajectories were performed for initial deformation \(Q_2^{\rm ini} = 160\) barn; 512 trajectories for \(Q_2^{\rm ini} = 125\) barn.
- The spatial mesh size was \(\Delta r = 0.8\) fm within a box of \(48.8 \times 48.8 \times 26.4\) fm\(^3\); the time step was \(\Delta t = 1.5 \times 10^{-24}\) s.
- The ground-state quadrupole deformation of \(^{258}\)Fm is \(Q_2^{\rm GS} = 32\) barn; the total energy is assumed to be 1 MeV above this ground state.

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent density-functional theory (TDDFT) approach to fission by incorporating quantum fluctuations via the stochastic mean-field method, which was previously applied to simple models (Lacroix 2012, 2013, 2014b; Yilmaz 2014) and to derive transport coefficients (Ayik 2009, 2010; Yilmaz 2011, 2014b; Lacroix 2015). It directly addresses the limitations of standard TDDFT, which underestimates fluctuations and lacks spontaneous symmetry breaking (Negele 1989), and of the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM), which struggles with dissipation and pre-scission particle emission (Abe 1996; Bertsch 2011). The present calculation reproduces the TKE distribution for \(^{258}\)Fm for the first time with a fully microscopic theory, and it enables future coupling with methods that compute tunneling probabilities (Sadhukhan 2016) to improve the description of mass asymmetry.
