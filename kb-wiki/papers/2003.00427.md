# Analysis of Bose-Einstein correlation at 7 TeV by LHCb collaboration based on stochastic approach

> The Bose-Einstein correlation (BEC) in forward region ($2.0<η<4.8$) measured at 7 TeV in the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) by the LHCb collaboration is analyzed using two conventional formulas of different types named CF$_{\rm I}$ and CF$_{\rm II}$. The first formula is well known and contains the degree of coherence ($λ$) and the exchange function $E_{\rm BE}^2$ from the BE statistics. The second formula is an extended formula (CF$_{\rm II}$) that contains the second degree of coherence $λ_2$ and the second exchange function $E_{\rm BE_2}^2$ in addition to CF$_{\rm I}$. To examine the physical meaning of the parameters estimated by CF$_{\rm II}$, we analyze the LHCb BEC data by using a stochastic approach of the three-negative binomial distribution and the three-generalized Glauber-Lachs formula. Our results reveal that the BEC at 7 TeV consisted of three activity intervals defined by the multiplicity $n$ ([8, 18], [19, 35], and [36, 96]) can be well explained by CF$_{\rm II}$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the Bose-Einstein correlation (BEC) data at 7 TeV from the LHCb collaboration, measured in the forward region (2.0 < η < 4.8) across three multiplicity intervals, can be well described by an extended formula (CF_II) containing two degrees of coherence (λ_1 and λ_2), and that the physical meaning of these parameters can be understood through a stochastic approach using the three-negative binomial distribution (T-NBD) and the three-generalized Glauber-Lachs (T-GGL) formula.

## Method

The authors analyzed LHCb BEC data using two conventional formulas: CF_I with a single degree of coherence λ and exchange function E_BE^2, and CF_II with two degrees of coherence λ_1 and λ_2 and two exchange functions E_BE1^2 and E_BE2^2. They performed 1,000 trials using the CERN MINUIT program with random starting variables for the four free parameters in CF_II (λ_1, λ_2, R_1, R_2). To interpret the coherence parameters physically, they analyzed the multiplicity distribution (MD) data from LHCb using the three-negative binomial distribution (T-NBD) and the three-generalized Glauber-Lachs (T-GGL) formula, calculating the degrees of coherence from the moments of these distributions.

## Key numbers

- Low activity interval [8, 18]: CF_II with [E + G] gave R_1 = 0.85 ± 0.02 fm (E), λ_1 = 0.63 ± 0.01, R_2 = 2.08 ± 0.15 fm (G), λ_2 = 0.22 ± 0.02, χ^2/ndf = 429/384 with degree of emergence 0.81.
- Medium activity interval [19, 35]: CF_II with [E + G] gave R_1 = 1.26 ± 0.03 fm (E), λ_1 = 0.50 ± 0.02, R_2 = 2.27 ± 0.13 fm (G), λ_2 = 0.19 ± 0.02, χ^2/ndf = 495/384 with degree of emergence 0.77.
- High activity interval [36, 96]: CF_II with [E + G] gave R_1 = 1.43 ± 0.04 fm (E), λ_1 = 0.38 ± 0.02, R_2 = 2.44 ± 0.12 fm (G), λ_2 = 0.24 ± 0.02, χ^2/ndf = 486/384 with degree of emergence 0.80.
- T-NBD analysis of the MD gave χ^2 = 0.044 with parameters α_1 = 0.648 ± 0.102, ⟨n_1⟩ = 10.32 ± 1.63, k_1 = 1.60 ± 0.44; α_2 = 0.180 ± 0.059, ⟨n_2⟩ = 4.42 ± 0.53, k_2 = 5.06 ± 3.78; α_3 = 0.173 ± 0.059, ⟨n_3⟩ = 22.1 ± 3.6, k_3 = 4.10 ± 1.61.
- From T-NBD, the calculated degrees of coherence for the low activity interval were λ_1^(T-N) = 0.831 and λ_2^(T-N) + λ_3^(T-N) = 0.157; for medium activity: λ_1 = 0.557, λ_2 = 0, λ_3 = 0.270; for high activity: λ_1 = 0.333, λ_2 = 0, λ_3 = 0.358.

## Context

This work extends previous analyses of BEC using stochastic approaches by Mizoguchi and Biyajima (2010, 2018, 2019) and Biyajima (2018, 2019), applying the T-NBD and T-GGL frameworks to LHCb data for the first time. The study contradicts the adequacy of the single-coherence formula CF_I for describing LHCb BEC data, as CF_I yielded large χ^2 values (e.g., 591/386 for low activity with exponential function). The results enable a physical interpretation of the two coherence parameters in terms of diffractive dissociation components (non-diffractive, single diffractive, double diffractive) and support the role of branching equations (including death, birth, and immigration terms) in LHC collisions, as previously discussed by Zborovsky (2013, 2018), Wilk (2016, 2018), and others.
