# Mass Ejection in Failed Supernovae: Variation with Stellar Progenitor

> We study the ejection of mass during stellar core-collapse when the stalled shock does not revive and a black hole forms. Neutrino emission during the protoneutron star phase causes a decrease in the gravitational mass of the core, resulting in an outward going sound pulse that steepens into a shock as it travels out through the star. We explore the properties of this mass ejection mechanism over a range of stellar progenitors using spherically-symmetric, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations that treat neutrino mass loss parametrically and follow the shock propagation over the entire star. We find that all types of stellar progenitor can eject mass through this mechanism. The ejected mass is a decreasing function of the surface gravity of the star, ranging from several $M_\odot$ for red supergiants to $\sim 0.1M_\odot$ for blue supergiants and $\sim 10^{-3} M_\odot $ for Wolf-Rayet stars. We find that the final shock energy at the surface is a decreasing function of the core-compactness, and is $\lesssim 10^{47}-10^{48}$ erg in all cases. In progenitors with a sufficiently large envelope, high core-compactness, or a combination of both, the sound pulse fails to unbind mass. Successful mass ejection is accompanied by significant fallback accretion that can last from hours to years. We predict the properties of shock breakout and thermal plateau emission produced by the ejection of the outer envelope of blue supergiant and Wolf-Rayet progenitors in otherwise failed supernovae.

## Key claim

All types of stellar progenitor can eject mass through the neutrino mass loss mechanism in failed supernovae, but the ejected mass is a decreasing function of the surface gravity of the star, ranging from several solar masses for red supergiants to approximately 0.1 solar masses for blue supergiants and approximately 10^{-3} solar masses for Wolf-Rayet stars; in progenitors with a sufficiently large envelope, high core-compactness, or a combination of both, the sound pulse fails to unbind mass entirely.

## Method

The authors performed spherically-symmetric, time-dependent hydrodynamic simulations using the FLASH3 code, treating neutrino mass loss from the protoneutron star parametrically with exponential, full loss, and maximum loss models following Lovegrove and Woosley (2013). They evolved MESA stellar progenitor models with zero-age main sequence masses from 12 to 100 solar masses at solar and 10^{-2} solar metallicity, following the shock propagation from the inner core at 2x10^8 cm out to 2x10^16 cm. The simulations used the Helmholtz equation of state, a logarithmic grid with 256 cells per decade in radius, and a remapping procedure to move the inner boundary outward as material became supersonic.

## Key numbers

- Ejected mass for red supergiant progenitor R15z00: 4.2 solar masses (exponential neutrino loss model)
- Ejected mass for blue supergiant progenitor B25z00: 4.9x10^{-2} solar masses (exponential model)
- Ejected mass for Wolf-Rayet progenitor W40z00: 5.0x10^{-4} solar masses (exponential model)
- Final shock energy at the surface: less than or equal to 10^{47}-10^{48} erg in all cases
- Gravitational mass lost for baseline progenitors: 0.30 solar masses (R15z00), 0.24 solar masses (B25z00), 0.22 solar masses (W40z00) in exponential models
- Analytic energy estimate at radius r_c for R15z00: 9.9x10^{47} erg (exponential model)
- Analytic ejecta mass estimate for R15z00: 4.8 solar masses (exponential model)
- Core compactness parameter xi_{2.5} ranges from 0.15 (R12z00) to 0.97 (B80z-2)
- Envelope compactness xi_{env} ranges from 0.009 (R12z00) to 27.1 (W40z00)
- Neutrino cooling timescale tau_c = 3 seconds used in all models
- Time to reach Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov mass: 6.1 seconds (R15z00), 3.1 seconds (B25z00), 2.6 seconds (W40z00) in exponential models

## Context

This work extends the numerical demonstration of neutrino-driven mass ejection in failed supernovae by Lovegrove and Woosley (2013), who studied only red supergiant progenitors, to a broader range of stellar types including blue supergiants and Wolf-Rayet stars. The results connect to the observed disappearing massive star survey by Adams et al. (2017a), which detected a weak optical transient from a red supergiant progenitor attributed to black hole formation, and to the core compactness parameter framework of O'Connor and Ott (2011) for predicting black hole formation. The analytic derivation of maximum kinetic energy imparted to the outflow, consistent with the more detailed treatment by Coughlin et al. (2017), enables predictions of shock breakout and thermal plateau emission from blue supergiant and Wolf-Rayet progenitors in otherwise failed supernovae.
