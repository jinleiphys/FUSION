# Propagation of Statistical Uncertainties of Skyrme Mass Models to Simulations of $r$-Process Nucleosynthesis

> Uncertainties in nuclear models have a major impact on simulations that aim at understanding the origin of heavy elements in the universe through the rapid neutron capture process ($r$ process) of nucleosynthesis. Within the framework of the nuclear density functional theory, we use results of Bayesian statistical analysis to propagate uncertainties in the parameters of energy density functionals to the predicted $r$-process abundance pattern, by way not only of the nuclear masses but also through the influence of the masses on $β$-decay and neutron capture rates. We additionally make the first identifications of specific parameters of Skyrme-like energy density functionals which are correlated with particular aspects of the $r$-process abundance pattern. While previous studies have explored the reduction in the abundance pattern uncertainties due to anticipated new measurements of neutron-rich nuclei, here we point out that an even larger reduction will occur when these new measurements are used to reduce the uncertainty of model predictions of masses, which are then propagated through to the abundance pattern. We make a quantitative prediction for how large this reduction will be.

# Propagation of Statistical Uncertainties of Skyrme Mass Models to Simulations of r-Process Nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

The authors perform the first rigorous propagation of statistical uncertainties from nuclear density functional theory mass models to r-process abundance patterns, and they identify specific Skyrme energy density functional parameters that correlate with features of the abundance pattern. They further predict that reducing mass model uncertainties through new measurements will produce an even larger reduction in abundance pattern uncertainties than the direct use of those measurements alone.

## Method

The authors sample the Bayesian posterior distribution of the UNEDF1 Skyrme energy density functional within the 90% confidence region to generate fifty parameter sets, then compute Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov ground-state binding energies for all even-even nuclei from Hydrogen to Z=120 for each set. For each of the fifty mass tables, they calculate self-consistent neutron capture rates using the Los Alamos Hauser-Feshbach code CoH and beta-decay half-lives using a QRPA+HF framework, then implement these datasets into the nuclear reaction network code PRISM to simulate nucleosynthesis for three distinct astrophysical conditions: a high-entropy wind, a low-entropy accretion disk wind, and a fission-recycling outflow from a neutron star merger.

## Key numbers

- The UNEDF1 parametrization has an r.m.s. deviation on nuclear binding energies of 1.8 MeV, which goes down to 0.45 MeV for two-neutron separation energies
- Each mass table contains approximately 2,000 even-even nuclei; computing 50 mass tables requires approximately 1 million HFB calculations
- The isovector surface coupling constant C1^{rho delta rho} has UNEDF1 value -145.382 +/- 52.169
- For the tin isotopes, UNEDF1 fits known masses to about sigma_rms ~ 1 MeV
- The location of the one-neutron drip line varies by more than ten neutron numbers across the fifty mass tables
- The correlation coefficient between the isovector surface coupling constant and the rare earth peak weighted average mass number is r=0.68 for the low-entropy wind conditions
- The correlation coefficient between the proton pairing strength V0^p and the ratio of summed abundances in the rare earth region to the A=195 region is r=0.66 for high-entropy, r=0.76 for low-entropy, and r=0.75 for fission recycling conditions

## Context

This work extends previous studies that compared abundance patterns between distinct mass models (e.g., Martin+2016) or used random, uncorrelated mass variations (Mumpower+2016, Surman+2016) by instead propagating statistically rigorous uncertainties from a single Bayesian-calibrated energy density functional. The authors build on Bayesian inference methods applied to UNEDF1 parameters by Higdon (2014) and McDonnell (2015), and their results enable quantitative predictions of how future mass measurements at facilities like FRIB will reduce uncertainties in r-process abundance patterns, particularly for low-entropy wind conditions where the rare earth peak can serve as a key diagnostic. For fission-recycling conditions, the authors note that further theoretical advances in nuclear EDF theory (e.g., Neufcourt+2018) and fission modeling (e.g., Giuliani+2018, Bulgac+2018) will be needed beyond what their approach captures.
