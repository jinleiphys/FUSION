# Heavy-ion interaction potential deduced from density-constrained TDHF calculation

> We present a new method for calculating the heavy-ion interaction potential from a density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculation.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new method for calculating heavy-ion interaction potentials directly from time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) time-evolution using a density constraint, which includes all collective dynamics without restricting the choice of collective variables. The method is only applicable for energies above the fusion barrier since TDHF is a semi-classical theory in this regard.

## Method

The authors perform unrestricted TDHF time-evolution of the colliding system with no restrictions. At certain times during the evolution, the instantaneous TDHF density is used to perform a static Hartree-Fock minimization while constraining the total density to be the instantaneous TDHF density, which yields the density-constrained energy E_DC(R). The internuclear potential V(R) is obtained by subtracting the binding energies of the individual nuclei from E_DC(R), using the Skyrme SLy5 effective interaction including all time-odd terms.

## Key numbers

- For the head-on collision of 16O+16O at E_c.m. = 34 MeV, the double-folding potential agrees almost perfectly with the DC-TDHF approach for distances R >= 6 fm.
- For 16O+16O at E_c.m. = 12 MeV, the potential is about 1.5 MeV deeper than the one at 34 MeV.
- Calculations for 16O+22Ne at E_c.m. = 50 MeV show that barrier height, barrier position, potential minimum, and minimum position are considerably different for the two orientations of the deformed 22Ne nucleus.

## Context

This work extends earlier density-constraint methods developed in the mid-1980s (Cusson 1985, Umar 1985) that were used to describe shape resonances in light systems. The approach contrasts with phenomenological models such as the Bass model, proximity potential (Blocki 1977, Randrup 1978, Swiatecki 1984), and double-folding method (Satchler 1979, Bertsch 1977), as well as with constrained Hartree-Fock calculations (Zmora 1976, Bonche 2004) that typically constrain only a single collective degree of freedom. The method enables calculation of ion-ion potentials from mean-field theory without free parameters and reveals a "potential ladder" effect characteristic of all fusing systems, where the system repeatedly falls into minima and climbs barriers until complete equilibration.
