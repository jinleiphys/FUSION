# A data-driven prediction for the primordial deuterium abundance

> We predict the primordial deuterium abundance using a novel, fully data-driven approach, where we use Gaussian process regression to fit experimental nuclear reaction data for $d$,($d$,$n$)$^3$He, $d$,($d$,$p$)$t$, and $d$($p$,$γ$)$^3$He, three reactions to which the primordial deuterium abundance is most sensitive. Using the Planck determination of the baryon density, we predict $10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.442\pm0.040$ in standard Big Bang Nucleosynthesis, $1.70σ$ below the Cooke et al. measurement. Our result is consistent with predictions relying on first principles calculations of the deuterium burning cross sections. With the inferred baryon density from a combined fit to Planck, ACT DR6, and SPT-3G D1, this discrepancy worsens to $1.98σ$. We validate our approach and confirm that Gaussian processes make unbiased D/H predictions with appropriately-sized uncertainties. We repeat our validation tests for low-degree polynomial fits, a technique used in previous analyses, and find that they systematically over-predict D/H. Our results highlight the need for improved measurements of the $d$,($d$,$n$)$^3$He and $d$,($d$,$p$)$t$ S-factors at energies between 0.1 and 0.6 MeV.

## Key claim
Using Gaussian process regression to fit experimental nuclear reaction data for the three most sensitive reactions, the authors predict a primordial deuterium abundance of \(10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.442\pm0.040\) when marginalizing over the Planck baryon density, which is \(1.70\sigma\) below the Cooke et al. measurement; this discrepancy worsens to \(1.98\sigma\) when using a combined CMB baryon density from Planck, ACT DR6, and SPT-3G D1.

## Method
The authors performed Gaussian process regression on experimental S-factor data for the \(d(d,n)^3\)He, \(d(d,p)t\), and \(d(p,\gamma)^3\)He reactions, using a leave-dataset-out cross-validation procedure to optimize kernel hyperparameters while weighting each dataset equally. They drew samples from the resulting GP posteriors, thermally averaged them to obtain reaction rates, and propagated these rates through the LINX BBN code to predict D/H, marginalizing over CMB-inferred baryon densities. The method was validated with Monte Carlo mock-data tests that demonstrated unbiased predictions and correct coverage, and was compared against low-degree polynomial fits used in previous analyses.

## Key numbers
- \(10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.442 \pm 0.040\) when marginalizing over the Planck 2018 baryon density \(\omega_b = 0.02237 \pm 0.00015\), giving a \(1.70\sigma\) discrepancy with the Cooke et al. measurement of \(2.527 \pm 0.030\)
- \(10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.437 \pm 0.034\) when using the combined Planck+ACT DR6+SPT-3G D1 baryon density \(\omega_b = 0.022398 \pm 0.000095\), giving a \(1.98\sigma\) discrepancy
- For the ACT DR6 baryon density \(\omega_b = 0.02259 \pm 0.00017\), the prediction is \(10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.403 \pm 0.042\), a \(2.40\sigma\) discrepancy
- For the SPT-3G D1 baryon density \(\omega_b = 0.02221 \pm 0.00020\), the prediction is \(10^5\times\mathrm{D/H} = 2.471 \pm 0.047\), a \(1.00\sigma\) discrepancy
- In Monte Carlo validation tests, the true D/H was contained within the \(1\sigma\) error bar for 66%–69% of realizations and within \(2\sigma\) for 95%–96% of realizations

## Context
This work extends the line of BBN deuterium predictions by introducing a fully data-driven Gaussian process approach, contrasting with the low-degree polynomial fits used in the PArthENoPE network (Serpico 2004, Pisanti 2021) and the first-principles calculations with overall scaling used in the PRIMAT network (Pitrou 2018). The authors find their prediction agrees with PRIMAT (\(2.444 \pm 0.037\)) but disagrees with PArthENoPE (\(2.512 \pm 0.042\)), and their Monte Carlo tests show that polynomial fits systematically over-predict D/H. This work enables robust consistency checks between BBN and CMB determinations of the baryon density, and highlights the need for improved measurements of the \(d(d,n)^3\)He and \(d(d,p)t\) S-factors at energies between 0.1 and 0.6 MeV.
