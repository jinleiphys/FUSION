# Enhanced low-energy $γ$-decay strength of $^{70}$Ni and its robustness within the shell model

> Neutron-capture reactions on very neutron-rich nuclei are essential for heavy-element nucleosynthesis through the rapid neutron-capture process, now shown to take place in neutron-star merger events. For these exotic nuclei, radiative neutron capture is extremely sensitive to their $γ$-emission probability at very low $γ$ energies. In this work, we present measurements of the $γ$-decay strength of $^{70}$Ni over the wide range $1.3 \leq E_γ \leq 8 $ MeV. A significant enhancement is found in the $γ$-decay strength for transitions with $E_γ< 3$ MeV. At present, this is the most neutron-rich nucleus displaying this feature, proving that this phenomenon is not restricted to stable nuclei. We have performed $E1$-strength calculations within the quasiparticle time-blocking approximation, which describe our data above $E_γ\simeq 5$ MeV very well. Moreover, large-scale shell-model calculations indicate an $M1$ nature of the low-energy $γ$ strength. This turns out to be remarkably robust with respect to the choice of interaction, truncation and model space, and we predict its presence in the whole isotopic chain, in particular the neutron-rich $^{72,74,76}\mathrm{Ni}$.

## Key claim

The paper reports a significant enhancement in the gamma-decay strength of the neutron-rich nucleus 70Ni for transitions with gamma energy below 3 MeV, but the abstract's claim that this "proves" the phenomenon is not restricted to stable nuclei is hedged by the experimental limitations: the data rely on the beta-Oslo method with approximately 72,000 total counts, a small (5-10%) contribution from a longer-lived beta-decaying state in 70Co, and the extraction technique may subtract too many gamma rays below 3 MeV due to spin differences between directly populated and underlying excitation-energy bins.

## Method

The experiment used the beta-Oslo method at the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory: 70Co fragments from a 140 MeV/A 86Kr beam on a Be target were implanted in a double-sided silicon strip detector inside the Summing NaI (SuN) total absorption spectrometer, and gamma-ray spectra gated on implantation-beta-decay events were unfolded along both the gamma-energy and excitation-energy axes using GEANT4 response functions. The distribution of primary gamma rays for each 200-keV excitation-energy bin was extracted by an iterative subtraction technique, and the nuclear level density and gamma-transmission coefficient were obtained by a least chi-squared fit of the product rho(E_f) * T(E_gamma) to the primary gamma-ray matrix. Theoretical calculations included E1-strength calculations within the quasiparticle time-blocking approximation (QTBA) using the BSk17 Skyrme force, and large-scale shell-model calculations with multiple interactions (jun45, ca48mh1g, ca48mh2, ca40fpg) using the codes KSHELL and NuShellX@MSU.

## Key numbers

- The gamma-decay strength was measured over the range 1.3 <= E_gamma <= 8 MeV, with a significant enhancement found for E_gamma < 3 MeV.
- The total number of counts in the raw gamma spectra versus excitation energy was about 72,000.
- The summing efficiency of SuN varies with gamma multiplicity and initial excitation energy, averaging approximately 25-30%.
- The Q_beta value for the 70Co decay is 12.3 MeV.
- The neutron separation energy S_n for 70Ni is 7.3 MeV.
- The experimental B(E2; 2+_1 -> 0+_1) for 70Ni is 172(28) e^2 fm^4; the ca48mh1g shell-model calculation gives 154.8 e^2 fm^4.
- The experimental B(E2; 6+_1 -> 4+_1) is 43(1) e^2 fm^4; ca48mh1g gives 120.0 e^2 fm^4.
- The experimental B(E2; 8+_1 -> 6+_1) is 19(4) e^2 fm^4; ca48mh1g gives 21.7 e^2 fm^4.
- The QTBA calculation describes the experimental data above E_gamma ~ 5 MeV very well, within experimental error bars.
- For the ca48mh1g and ca40fpg shell-model calculations, the exponential slope parameter T is approximately 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the observation of the low-energy enhancement (upbend) in the gamma-strength function, previously seen in iron isotopes (Larsen 2013), molybdenum isotopes (Wiedeking 2012, Renstrom 2016), and nickel isotopes including 60,64,65,69Ni (Crespo Campo 2016, 2017; Spyrou 2017), to the more neutron-rich 70Ni, making it the most neutron-rich nucleus displaying this feature. The shell-model calculations show the upbend is remarkably robust with respect to choice of interaction, truncation, and model space, and predict its presence in the whole isotopic chain, particularly 72,74,76Ni. The presence of an upbend could increase astrophysical (n,gamma) reaction rates by up to about two orders of magnitude for very neutron-rich nuclei (Larsen and Goriely), which is critical for understanding the r-process in neutron-star merger ejecta where (n,gamma)-(gamma,n) equilibrium is not established (Just 2015).
