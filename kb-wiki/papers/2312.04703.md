# Solving the Lipkin model using quantum computers with two qubits only with a hybrid quantum-classical technique based on the Generator Coordinate Method

> The possibility of using the generator coordinate method (GCM) using hybrid quantum-classical algorithms with reduced quantum resources is discussed. The task of preparing the basis states and calculating the various kernels involved in the GCM is assigned to the quantum computer, while the remaining tasks, such as finding the eigenvalues of a many-body problem, are delegated to classical computers for post-processing the generated kernels. This strategy reduces the quantum resources required to treat a quantum many-body problem. We apply the method to the Lipkin model. Using the permutation symmetry of the Hamiltonian, we show that, ultimately, only two qubits is enough to solve the problem regardless of the particle number. The classical computing post-processing leading to the full energy spectrum can be made using standard generalized eigenvalues techniques by diagonalizing the so-called Hill-Wheeler equation. As an alternative to this technique, we also explored how the quantum state deflation method can be adapted to the GCM problem. In this method, variational principles are iteratively designed to access the different excited states with increasing energies. The methodology proposed here is successfully applied to the Lipkin model with a minimal size of two qubits for the quantum register. The performances of the two classical post-processing approaches with respect to the statistical noise induced by the finite number of measurements and quantum devices noise are analyzed. Very satisfactory results for the full energy spectra are obtained once noise correction techniques are employed.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM) can be implemented on a quantum computer using only two qubits for the Lipkin model, regardless of particle number, by exploiting permutation symmetry and using a hybrid quantum-classical approach where the quantum processor estimates kernels and the classical processor solves the resulting eigenvalue problem.

## Method

The authors encode the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick Hamiltonian using the natural SU(2) encoding with one qubit per two-level system, then generate coherent states as tensor products of single-qubit Y-rotations parameterized by a single angle theta. They use Hadamard tests on a two-qubit quantum register (one ancillary qubit plus one system qubit) to estimate the four one-body kernels (I, X, Y, Z expectation values) for each pair of angles, then reconstruct the many-body norm and Hamiltonian kernels analytically using permutation symmetry and the tensor product structure. The resulting Hill-Wheeler equation is solved classically either by standard diagonalization (GCM-Diag) or by an iterative variational quantum deflation method (GCM-VQD) that avoids norm matrix inversion.

## Key numbers

- Only 2 qubits are required for the quantum register regardless of the number N of two-level systems, compared to q = 2N for Jordan-Wigner encoding, q = N for direct SU(2) encoding, or q = ceil(log2(N+1)) for compact encoding
- For N=4, L=5 generating states are sufficient to obtain all eigenstates in the limit of infinite measurements
- For N=8, L=9 generating states are sufficient to obtain all eigenstates
- With 100 shots per one-body kernel estimate, the method reproduces exact eigenvalues for N=4 with satisfactory accuracy
- For N=8, higher precision is required: 10^4, 10^5, and 10^6 shots are used for the three panels shown
- The standard deviation of one-body kernel estimates scales as 1/sqrt(N_sh) where N_sh is the number of shots
- The Hadamard test circuits use only 3 CNOT gates

## Context

This work extends the recent proposal by Zheng (2023) for using GCM-based approaches on quantum computers, demonstrating a dramatic reduction in quantum resources compared to prior quantum algorithms for the Lipkin model by Cerf (2021) and Hlatshwayo (2022, 2023). The method enables solving the Lipkin model with a fixed two-qubit register independent of system size, which is a significant improvement over the compact encoding approach that required 3 qubits for N=8. The results validate the hybrid quantum-classical GCM strategy as a viable path toward treating more complex many-body problems on near-term quantum devices, though the authors note that precision requirements become more stringent as particle number increases.
