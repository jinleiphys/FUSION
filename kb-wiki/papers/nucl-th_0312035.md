# Exponential enhancement of nuclear reactions in condensed matter environment

> A mechanism that uses the environment to enhance the probability of the nuclear reaction when a beam of accelerated nuclei collides with a target nucleus implanted in condensed matter is suggested. The effect considered is exponentially large for low collision energies. For t + p collision the mechanism becomes effective when the energy of the projectile tritium is below $\sim$ 1 Kev per nucleon. The gain in probability of the nuclear reaction is due to a redistribution of energy and momentum of the projectile in several ``preliminary'' elastic collisions with the target nucleus and the environmental nuclei in such a way that the final inelastic projectile-target collision takes place at a larger relative velocity, which is accompanied by a decrease of the center of mass energy. The gain of the relative velocity exponentially increases the penetration through the Coulomb barrier.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a "rescattering mechanism" in which two preliminary elastic collisions (projectile-target, then recoiled target-heavy environment nucleus) can increase the relative collision velocity for the final inelastic nuclear event, exponentially enhancing the reaction probability at low projectile energies, provided the projectile is heavier than the target and the geometry is approximately aligned.

## Method

The authors consider a projectile nucleus of mass \(M\) colliding with a lighter target nucleus of mass \(m\) implanted in a solid, with a heavy environmental nucleus (\(M_\mathrm{env} \gg m\)) nearby. They analyze a sequence where the projectile first elastically strikes the target, the recoiled target then backscatters off the heavy environment nucleus, and the projectile and target meet again at an increased relative velocity \(w\). The enhancement factor \(F\) is derived as the product of the flux factor \(J\) (containing elastic differential cross sections and a geometric factor \(b\)) times the ratio of Coulomb penetration factors \(P(w)/P(V)\).

## Key numbers

* For t + p collision, the rescattering mechanism becomes effective when the projectile tritium energy is below \(\sim 0.7-1\) KeV per nucleon (corresponding to \(V_\mathrm{proj}/Z_\mathrm{proj} \le 0.17-0.2\) atomic units).
* The relative collision velocity in the final inelastic event is enhanced to \(w = 2V\) for t + p (projectile tritium, target proton) and to \(w = 2.5V\) for \(^7\)Li + p.
* For a heavy projectile, the relative velocity after rescattering is approximately \(w \simeq 3V\).
* The geometric parameter \(b\) (distance from the final nuclear event to the environmental nucleus) is taken as 1 and 3 Bohr radii in the numerical estimates.

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals for enhancing low-energy nuclear reactions in solids, specifically the "carambole" mechanism of KAF (2002) which required three elastic collisions and was only effective below 0.5 KeV for deuterium. The present rescattering mechanism requires only two preliminary collisions and is argued to be more effective for asymmetric collisions where the projectile is heavier than the target. The mechanism is distinct from phonon-assisted or atomic-motion enhancements considered by AFKZ (2002) and Fiorentini et al. (2003), and the authors note it is unlikely to explain the longstanding discrepancy in astrophysical fusion data because those involve thermalized media. The mechanism could be tested experimentally in beam-target setups using t + p reactions in a palladium environment, and may also be relevant to non-thermalized astrophysical environments such as supernova vicinities or laser-induced fusion.
