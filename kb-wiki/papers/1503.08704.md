# Quasi-power laws in multiparticle production processes

> We review the ubiquitous presence in multiparticle production processes of quasi-power law distributions (i.e., distributions following pure power laws for large values of the argument but remaining finite, usually exponential, for small values). Special emphasis is placed on the conjecture that this reflects the presence in the produced hadronic systems of some intrinsic fluctuations. If described by parameter q they form, together with the scale parameter $T$ ("temperature"), basis of Tsallis distribution, ${f(X)\sim [1 - (1-q)X/T]^{1/(1-q)}}$, frequently used to describe the relevant distributions (the X being usually a transverse momentum). We discuss the origin of such quasi-power law behavior based on our experience with the description of multiparticle production processes. In particular, we discuss Tsallis distribution with complex nonextensivity parameter q and argue that it is needed to describe log-oscillations as apparently observed in recent data on large momentum distributions in very high energy p-p collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quasi-power law distributions (Tsallis distributions) are ubiquitous in multiparticle production processes and that the observed log-periodic oscillations in high-energy p-p collision data at very large transverse momenta cannot be eliminated by any suitable changes of the parameters (q,T) or (n,T), suggesting that the simple Tsallis formula is too simple and that these oscillations may signal a genuine dynamical effect requiring a complex nonextensivity parameter.

## Method

The authors review multiple derivations of Tsallis distributions in multiparticle production: from constrained systems with fixed variables (energy, multiplicity, temperature), conditional probability, statistical physics considerations of isolated systems, systems with fluctuating multiplicity (binomial, Poissonian, and negative binomial distributions), preferential attachment ("rich-get-richer") mechanisms, multiplicative noise described by Langevin equations, and from Shannon entropy with appropriate constraints. They then extend the Tsallis distribution by allowing the power index to be complex, which introduces a log-oscillating multiplicative factor to account for oscillations observed in the ratio of data to fit for transverse momentum distributions from CMS and ATLAS experiments at 0.9 and 7 TeV.

## Key numbers

* Transverse momentum distributions from CMS and ATLAS experiments at the LHC cover a range of approximately 14 orders of magnitude in cross section, with p_T up to 180 GeV.
* For CMS data at 0.9 TeV, the Tsallis fit parameters were T=0.135 and n=8; for 7 TeV, T=0.145 and n=6.7.
* The log-oscillating correction factor R(E) for 0.9 TeV data used parameters a=0.865 and c=2.1; for 7 TeV data, a=0.909 and c=1.86, with b=0.166, d=0.948, and f=-1.462 common to both energies.
* From pure QCD partonic picture, the power index n is approximately 4 to 4.5, while observed hadron distributions show n approximately 7 to 8 depending on collision energy.

## Context

This review extends the authors' previous work on Tsallis statistics in multiparticle production (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2009, 2012) and the observation of log-periodic oscillations in complex systems. The paper connects to the QCD-based Hagedorn formula (Hagedorn, 1965) and the Tsallis distribution (Tsallis, 1988) widely used in high-energy physics data analysis by CMS, ATLAS, ALICE, PHENIX, and STAR collaborations. The complex power index approach was first investigated by the authors (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2013) and generalizes the treatment of log-periodic oscillations previously known in dissipative maps and restricted random walks. This work enables future investigation of whether the observed oscillations in LHC data represent a genuine dynamical effect requiring a complex nonextensivity parameter rather than simple parameter optimization.
