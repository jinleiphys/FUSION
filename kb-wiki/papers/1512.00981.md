# Pion-induced production of the $Z_c(3900)$ off a nuclear target

> We investigate the possibility to study the charmoniumlike state $Z_c(3900)$ through the pion-induced production off a nuclear target. By using a high-energy pion beam, the $Z_c(3900)$ can be produced off a proton or nucleus though the Primakoff effect. The production amplitude is calculated in an effective Lagrangian approach combined with the vector dominance model. The total cross sections of the $p(π^-, Z^-_c(3900))$ and $p(π^-, Z^-_c(3900)\to J/ψπ^-)$ reactions are calculated, and their order of magnitude is about 0.1 and 0.01nb, respectively, with an assumption of branch ratio 10\% for the $Z_c(3900)$ decay in $J/ψπ$ channel. If the proton target is replaced by a nuclear target, the production of the $Z_c(3900)$ enhances obviously. The predicted total cross sections for the $A(π^-, Z^-_c(3900))$ and $A(π^-, Z^-_c(3900)\to J/ψπ^-)$ reactions with $A=^{12}$C or $^{208}$Pb are on the order of magnitude of 100 and 10 nb, respectively, which is about one thousand times larger than the cross sections off a proton target. Based on these the results, we suggest the experimental study of the $Z_c(3900)$ by using high-energy pion beams with a nuclear target at facilities such as COMPASS and J-PARC.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the charmoniumlike state Z_c(3900) can be produced via pion-induced Primakoff effect off a nuclear target, with cross sections roughly 1000 times larger than off a proton target, but the numerical predictions depend on an assumed 10% branching ratio for the Z_c(3900) decay into J/psi pi and on the choice of cutoff parameter Lambda in the form factors.

## Method

The production amplitude is calculated using an effective Lagrangian approach combined with the vector dominance model, where a high-energy pion beam interacts with a proton or nucleus through exchange of a virtual photon that converts to a J/psi meson via VMD, and the J/psi then couples to the pion to produce the Z_c(3900). For nuclear targets, the interaction is described using a Woods-Saxon charge density distribution and an eikonal distortion factor accounting for final-state interactions of the outgoing meson. Cross sections are computed for both direct Z_c(3900) production and the subsequent decay Z_c(3900) to J/psi pi.

## Key numbers

- Total cross section for p(pi-, Z_c^-(3900)) reaction: order of magnitude 0.1 nb, assuming 10% branching ratio for Z_c(3900) to J/psi pi.
- Total cross section for p(pi-, Z_c^-(3900) to J/psi pi-) reaction: order of magnitude 0.01 nb, assuming 10% branching ratio.
- Total cross section for A(pi-, Z_c^-(3900)) reaction with A = 12C or 208Pb: order of magnitude 100 nb, assuming 10% branching ratio.
- Total cross section for A(pi-, Z_c^-(3900) to J/psi pi-) reaction with A = 12C or 208Pb: order of magnitude 10 nb, assuming 10% branching ratio.
- The coupling constant g_{pi psi Z_c}/M_Z = 1.50 for Gamma_{Z_c- to J/psi pi-} = 29 MeV, and 1.90 for Gamma = 46 MeV.
- The parameter e/f_{J/psi} = 0.027, derived from Gamma_{J/psi to e+e-} = 5.55 keV.
- The dipole form factor parameter m_D^2 = 0.71 GeV^2 for the proton electromagnetic form factors.
- Nuclear charge radius R = 1.1 A^{1/3} fm and surface thickness d = 0.53 fm for the Woods-Saxon distribution.
- The pi p total cross section sigma_pi = 24.1 mb used in the eikonal distortion factor.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of Z_c(3900) photoproduction off a proton (Huang et al., 2013) which predicted cross sections of 50-100 nb but were not confirmed by COMPASS data, possibly because the J/psi pi decay channel is not dominant or the photoproduction cross section was overestimated. The authors build on the Primakoff effect formalism used in earlier meson production studies (Badier, 1983; Kaskulov, 2011) and apply it to pion-induced production of the Z_c(3900), which was first observed by BESIII and Belle in e+e- collisions. The results suggest that nuclear targets at COMPASS and J-PARC could enable experimental study of the Z_c(3900) and other XYZ particles, where previous photoproduction attempts with proton targets have been unsuccessful.
