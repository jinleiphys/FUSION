# From few to many body degrees of freedom

> Here, I focus on the use of microscopic, few-body techniques that are relevant in the many-body problem. These methods can be divided into indirect and direct. In particular, indirect methods are concerned with the simplification of the many-body problem by substituting the full, microscopic interactions by pseudopotentials which are designed to reproduce collisional information at specified energies, or binding energies in the few-body sector. These simplified interactions yield more tractable theories of the many-body problem, and are equivalent to effective field theory of interactions. Direct methods, which so far are most useful in one spatial dimension, have the goal of attacking the many-body problem at once by using few-body information only. Here, I will present non-perturbative direct methods to study one-dimensional fermionic and bosonic gases in one dimension.

## Key claim
The paper argues that few-body techniques can be used to directly extract many-body properties in one-dimensional systems without solving the full many-body problem, but this direct approach is currently limited mostly to one spatial dimension, while indirect methods (replacing microscopic interactions with pseudopotentials) are more broadly applicable but still require solving a simplified many-body theory.

## Method
The author reviews two categories of methods: indirect methods that replace realistic two-body interactions with pseudopotentials fitted to reproduce scattering lengths or binding energies (using effective field theory expansions, renormalisation, and Lüscher's finite-volume method), and direct methods that use few-body solutions to obtain many-body information without solving a many-body problem. For indirect methods, the paper demonstrates renormalisation of the zero-range pseudopotential using cutoff regularisation and minimal subtraction, and applies it to mean-field and Bogoliubov theories for bosons. For direct methods, the paper presents a constructive bosonisation approach for one-dimensional fermions that derives the Luttinger model from two-body scattering phase shifts.

## Key numbers
- The renormalised coupling constant for s-wave scattering is \( g_R = 4\pi\hbar^2 a/m \), where \( a \) is the scattering length.
- For a cubic cutoff, the regularised integral \( (2\pi)^3\mathcal{I}'_0(0) = -15.3482484734169\ldots \Lambda + \mathcal{O}(1/\Lambda) \) as \( \Lambda \to \infty \).
- The ground-state energy of a dilute Bose gas in mean-field theory is \( E_{\mathrm{MF}}/N = g_R\rho/2 \), with density \( \rho \) satisfying \( 0 < \rho a^{1/3} \ll 1 \).
- For one-dimensional fermions, the effective coupling constant for odd-wave interactions at the Fermi points is \( g_0 = -2\hbar v_F \tan\theta_{k_F} \), where \( \theta_{k_F} \) is the scattering phase shift at the Fermi momentum.
- The speed of sound in the Luttinger model is \( v = v_F\sqrt{1 + 2g_0/(\pi\hbar v_F)} \).

## Context
This review extends the line of work on effective field theory for nuclear interactions (Weinberg, 1990; Phillips et al., 1998; Kaplan et al., 1998) and pseudopotentials (Huang and Yang, 1957; Tan, 2005) to ultracold atomic gases. It also connects to Lüscher's finite-volume method (Lüscher, 1986) for extracting scattering parameters, and to Luttinger liquid theory (Haldane, 1981; Mattis and Lieb, 1965) for one-dimensional systems. The direct methods described enable non-perturbative estimates of many-body quantities like the speed of sound from few-body scattering data, without solving the full many-body problem.
