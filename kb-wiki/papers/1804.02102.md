# Towards Reliable Nuclear Matrix Elements for Neutrinoless $ββ$ Decay

> The calculated nuclear matrix elements for the neutrinoless double-beta ($0νββ$) decay suffer from several limitations. Predicted matrix-element values depend on the many-body method used to calculate them and, in addition, they may need to be "quenched", albeit by an unknown amount due to the relatively high momentum transferred in the decay. These uncertainties are hard to figure out from theoretical calculations alone because of the unique character of $0νββ$ decay, not clearly related to other nuclear structure observables. We present recent progress in the determination of the nuclear matrix elements. First, we report improved shell model calculations in an extended configuration space of two harmonic oscillator shells. Second, we note the relation between the $0νββ$ decay and double Gamow-Teller transitions that can in principle be measured in double charge-exchange reactions. These new insights pave the way for a more reliable estimation of the value of the nuclear matrix elements.

# Towards Reliable Nuclear Matrix Elements for Neutrinoless ββ Decay

## Key claim
Shell model calculations of neutrinoless double-beta decay nuclear matrix elements (NMEs) in an extended configuration space of two harmonic oscillator shells show only a modest enhancement (~30% for ⁴⁸Ca) compared to one-shell calculations, suggesting that standard shell model NMEs are reasonably converged; however, the NMEs may still require an unknown "quenching" due to the relatively high momentum transfer (~100 MeV) in the decay, and the necessity of such quenching remains unclear.

## Method
The author reports improved shell model calculations for ⁴⁸Ca in a configuration space of two harmonic oscillator shells (sd and pf shells), including up to 2ħω sd-pf excitations, validated by reproducing excitation spectra of ⁴⁸Ca and ⁴⁸Ti and by describing Gamow-Teller strength and the two-neutrino ββ decay matrix element of ⁴⁸Ca. For ⁷⁶Ge, a two-shell calculation using the generator-coordinate method (an approximate approach, not a full shell model diagonalization) is compared to one-shell shell model results. Additionally, the author establishes a linear correlation between 0νββ decay NMEs and double Gamow-Teller (DGT) matrix elements across nuclei from calcium to xenon (42 ≤ A ≤ 136) using shell model calculations, and compares these to energy-density functional (EDF) theory and quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) results.

## Key numbers
- The ⁴⁸Ca NME in the two-shell calculation was enhanced by only ~30% compared to the one-shell calculation.
- For ⁴⁸Ca, a "renormalization" or "quenching" factor of q = 0.71 for each spin-isospin στ term was required to reproduce experimental Gamow-Teller strength and the two-neutrino ββ decay matrix element.
- The momentum transfer relevant for 0νββ decay is about q ~ 100 MeV.
- The DGT transition to the ground state is a tiny 0.03 per mil piece of the DGT sum rule.
- The subleading Fermi and tensor parts of the 0νββ NME are less than 20% of the dominant Gamow-Teller piece.
- The 0νββ and DGT matrix elements are dominated by contributions from nucleons at internucleon distances r_ab ≲ 3 fm.

## Context
This work extends previous shell model calculations of 0νββ decay NMEs that were typically limited to one harmonic oscillator shell, a restriction that had been claimed insufficient for converged NMEs (Vogel, 2012). The two-shell calculation for ⁴⁸Ca (Iwata et al., 2016) and the generator-coordinate method calculation for ⁷⁶Ge (Jiao et al., 2017) both show small changes from one-shell results. The linear correlation between 0νββ NMEs and DGT matrix elements, predicted by shell model and EDF theory (Rodriguez, 2015) but not by QRPA (Simkovic, 2011), opens the possibility of constraining NMEs through double charge-exchange reactions (Takaki, Uesaka, Cappuzzello, Takahisa, 2017). The short-range dominance of both matrix elements is explained by the factorization theorem of Bogner et al. (2010, 2012), which predicts a linear relation for short-range operators in low-energy states.
