# Deep learning of phase transitions with minimal examples

> Over the past several years, there have been many studies demonstrating the ability of deep neural networks to identify phase transitions in many physical systems, notably in classical statistical physics systems. One often finds that the prediction of deep learning methods trained on many ensembles below and above the critical temperature $T_{\rm c}$ behaves similarly to an order parameter, and this analogy has been successfully used to locate $T_{\rm c}$ and estimate universal critical exponents. In this work, we pay particular attention to the ability of a convolutional neural network to capture these critical parameters for the 2-$d$ Ising model when the network is trained on configurations at $T=0$ and $T=\infty$ only. We directly compare its output to the same network trained at multiple temperatures below and above $T_{\rm c}$ to gain understanding of how this extreme restriction of training data can impact a neural network's ability to classify phases. We find that the network trained on two temperatures is still able to identify $T_{\rm c}$ and $ν$, while the extraction of $γ$ becomes more challenging.

## Key claim

A convolutional neural network trained on only two temperatures (T=0 and T=infinity) for the 2-d Ising model can still identify the critical temperature Tc and the critical exponent nu, but extracting the critical exponent gamma becomes more challenging and yields results with larger uncertainties compared to a network trained on 20 temperatures near Tc.

## Method

The authors trained a convolutional neural network (CNN) on 2-d Ising model configurations using two strategies: the "BAL20" model trained on 20 temperatures (10 above and 10 below Tc) from Monte Carlo simulations, and the "BAL" model trained only on exact T=0 configurations (with magnetization m=1 or m=-1) and T=infinity configurations (with m=0). They generated configurations on square lattices of sizes L=128, 200, 256, 360, 440, 512, and 640 (plus L=760 for the BAL model), with 2000 configurations per ensemble separated by 5L^2 sweeps. They extracted pseudocritical temperatures Tc(L) from the peak of the prediction susceptibility chi_P using histogram reweighting, then performed finite-size scaling fits to extract Tc, nu, and gamma/nu, employing Bayesian model averaging to assess systematic uncertainties from lattice-size cuts.

## Key numbers

- The BAL20 model yielded Tc = 2.26828(61), compared to the analytic Onsager value Tc = 2.269185
- The BAL model yielded Tc = 2.2664(29), with a relative uncertainty roughly 4.76 times larger than BAL20
- The BAL20 model yielded nu = 1.017(41), compared to the analytic value nu = 1
- The BAL model yielded nu = 1.21(12), with a relative uncertainty roughly 2.46 times larger than BAL20
- The BAL20 model yielded gamma/nu = 1.786(65) after Bayesian model averaging, compared to the analytic value gamma/nu = 1.75
- The BAL model yielded gamma/nu = 1.92(13) after Bayesian model averaging, with a relative uncertainty roughly 1.86 times larger than BAL20
- The BAL20 fit for gamma/nu gave 1.776(17) with chi^2 per degree of freedom = 8.60 using all lattice sizes
- The BAL fit for gamma/nu gave 2.069(24) with chi^2 per degree of freedom = 18.25 using all lattice sizes

## Context

This work extends the approach of Li (2017), who trained CNNs only on T=0 configurations for 2-d Potts models to distinguish first-order from second-order transitions, and of Bachtis (2020), who showed that CNNs trained on multiple temperatures near Tc could extract Tc, nu, and gamma for the 2-d Ising model. The authors directly compare the minimal-training approach to the multi-temperature approach on the same architecture, finding that while Tc and nu remain accessible, gamma extraction degrades significantly. This enables future application to crossover systems like the QCD crossover, where training at very high and very low temperatures could identify a range of pseudocritical temperatures for further refinement.
