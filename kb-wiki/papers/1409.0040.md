# Particle spectra and HBT radii for simulated central nuclear collisions of C+C, Al+Al, Cu+Cu, Au+Au, and Pb+Pb from Sqrt(s)=62.4-2760 GeV

> We study the temperature profile, pion spectra and HBT radii in central symmetric and boost-invariant nuclear collisions using a super hybrid model for heavy-ion collisions (SONIC) combining pre-equilibrium flow with viscous hydrodynamics and late-stage hadronic rescatterings. In particular, we simulate Pb+Pb collisions at Sqrt(s)=2.76 TeV, Au+Au, Cu+Cu, Al+Al, and C+C collisions at Sqrt(s)=200 GeV and Au+Au, Cu+Cu collisions at Sqrt(s)=62.4 GeV. We find that SONIC provides a good match to the pion spectra and HBT radii for all collision systems and energies, confirming earlier work that a combination of pre-equilibrium flow, viscosity and QCD equation of state can resolve the so-called HBT puzzle. For reference, we also show p+p collisions at Sqrt(s)=7 TeV. We make tabulated data for the 2+1 dimensional temperature evolution of all systems publicly available for the use in future jet energy loss or similar studies.

# Particle spectra and HBT radii for simulated central nuclear collisions of C+C, Al+Al, Cu+Cu, Au+Au, and Pb+Pb from Sqrt(s)=62.4-2760 GeV

## Key claim

The SONIC super hybrid model, combining pre-equilibrium flow, viscous hydrodynamics with eta/s=0.08 and zeta/s=0.01, and hadronic rescattering, provides a good match to pion spectra and HBT radii across all collision systems and energies from Sqrt(s)=62.4 GeV to 2.76 TeV, confirming that the combination of these ingredients can resolve the so-called HBT puzzle. However, the model does not properly reproduce experimentally measured proton spectra because baryon-antibaryon annihilation is not implemented, and the Pb+Pb pion spectra show a discrepancy above pT=1.5 GeV due to a corrected nuclear radius parameter.

## Method

The authors model central, symmetric, boost-invariant nuclear collisions using the SONIC hybrid model, which starts with pre-equilibrium radial flow derived from gauge/gravity duality, then evolves the system with the relativistic viscous hydrodynamics solver VH2+1 (version 1.7) using eta/s=0.08, zeta/s=0.01, and a lattice QCD equation of state at zero baryon density, and finally generates hadrons at the T=170 MeV isothermal hypersurface followed by hadronic rescattering using the B3D cascade code. The initial energy density is taken as the square of the nuclear overlap function, and the overall normalization epsilon_0 is fixed by matching experimental charged multiplicity where available, with 5000 B3D events generated per hydro event.

## Key numbers

- Pb+Pb at Sqrt(s)=2.76 TeV: model charged multiplicity dNch/dy = 1635, mean pion pT = 503 MeV (experiment: 1584 +/- 80, 517 +/- 19 MeV for 0-5% most central)
- Au+Au at Sqrt(s)=200 GeV: model dNch/dy = 677, mean pion pT = 424 MeV (experiment: 691 +/- 52, 453 +/- 33 MeV for 0-5% most central)
- Au+Au at Sqrt(s)=62.4 GeV: model dNch/dy = 508, mean pion pT = 402 MeV (experiment: 472 +/- 41, 405 +/- 11.0 MeV for 0-5% most central)
- Cu+Cu at Sqrt(s)=200 GeV: model dNch/dy = 193, mean pion pT = 421 MeV (experiment: 198 +/- 15, 420 +/- 20 MeV for 0-10% most central)
- Cu+Cu at Sqrt(s)=62.4 GeV: model dNch/dy = 144, mean pion pT = 403 MeV (experiment: 138 +/- 10, 379 +/- 20 MeV for 0-10% most central)
- C+C at Sqrt(s)=200 GeV: model dNch/dy = 21, mean pion pT = 396 MeV (no experimental comparison available)
- Al+Al at Sqrt(s)=200 GeV: model dNch/dy = 68, mean pion pT = 415 MeV (no experimental comparison available)
- p+p at Sqrt(s)=7 TeV: model dNch/dy = 7, mean pT = 599 MeV for pT>0.15 GeV (experiment: 6, 622 +/- 21 MeV minimum-bias)
- Central temperature at tau=0.5 fm: Pb+Pb 470 MeV, Au+Au at 200 GeV 370 MeV, Au+Au at 62.4 GeV 340 MeV, Cu+Cu at 200 GeV 327 MeV, Cu+Cu at 62.4 GeV 300 MeV, Al+Al 287 MeV, C+C 238 MeV, p+p 390 MeV
- Switching time tau_sw for hydrodynamics is constrained between approximately 0.35 fm/c and 0.6 fm/c

## Context

This work extends the SONIC hybrid model approach from van der Schee (2013) to multiple collision systems and energies, confirming that pre-equilibrium flow, viscosity, and a QCD equation of state together resolve the HBT puzzle previously noted by Pratt (2008). The authors provide publicly available 2+1 dimensional temperature evolution data for all simulated systems to enable future studies of jet energy loss, direct photon emission, and heavy quark diffusion, with the sPHENIX upgrade motivation cited as a key application for understanding the nature of the quark-gluon plasma near the transition temperature.
