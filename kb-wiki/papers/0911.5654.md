# Open problems in nuclear density functional theory

> This note describes five subjects of some interest for the density functional theory in nuclear physics. These are, respectively, i) the need for concave functionals, ii) the nature of the Kohn-Sham potential for the radial density theory, iii) a proper implementation of a density functional for an "intrinsic" rotational density, iv) the possible existence of a potential driving the square root of the density, and v) the existence of many models where a density functional can be explicitly constructed.

# Open problems in nuclear density functional theory

## Key claim
The paper identifies five open problems in nuclear density functional theory (DFT), arguing that while DFT is widely used, several foundational issues remain unresolved, particularly the need for concave functionals to avoid absurd energy minima from ensemble mixtures, and the tension between the scalar nature of the Hamiltonian and the three-dimensional mean-field approaches that break rotational symmetry.

## Method
The author examines five specific subjects: (i) the requirement that the energy as a function of neutron and proton numbers be concave (positive second differences), and how quadratic terms in N and Z can enforce this; (ii) the adaptation of Kohn-Sham theory to radial (monopolar) densities, using scalar mixtures of Slater determinants; (iii) the construction of density functionals for intrinsic rotational states via variation after projection; (iv) the existence of a local potential driving the square root of the density, analogous to a theorem from atomic physics; and (v) an algebraic, polynomial-based constructive method for explicit density functionals using truncated harmonic oscillator bases.

## Key numbers
- A rough inspection of the nuclear binding table indicates that eigenvalues of a few MeV at most for the matrix [[alpha, beta], [beta, gamma]] are enough to ensure concavity of the mass formula.
- The polynomial method for constructing density functionals involves constraints: the number of constraints is N_c = N_n + N_p + 1, where N_n and N_p are the orders of the neutron and proton density polynomials, respectively.
- The number of real parameters (c_i coefficients) in the constructive model is the dimension of the basis {Phi_i}, which is significantly larger than N_c in realistic models.

## Context
This paper extends the foundational work on density functional theory by Levy and Lieb (Levy and Lieb, 1980s) and Hohenberg and Kohn (Hohenberg and Kohn, 1960s), and the Kohn-Sham formulation (Kohn and Sham, 1965). It identifies open problems that challenge current nuclear energy density functional (EDF) approaches, such as those used by Schuck and collaborators (Schuck, 2000s) for spherical and deformed nuclei. The paper enables future work on rigorous foundations for nuclear DFT, including the need to reconcile three-dimensional EDF calculations with rotational invariance, and the development of polynomial-based constructive methods that may provide alternatives to direct diagonalization of huge Hamiltonian matrices.
