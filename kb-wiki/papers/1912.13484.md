# Describing Migdal effects in diamond crystal with atom-centered localized Wannier functions

> Recent studies have theoretically investigated the atomic excitation and ionization induced by the dark matter (DM)-nucleus scattering, and it is found that the suddenly recoiled atom is much more likely to excite or lose its electrons than expected. Such phenomenon is called the "Migdal effect". In this paper, we extend the established strategy to describe the Migdal effect in isolated atoms to the case in semiconductors under the framework of tight-binding (TB) approximation. Since the localized aspects of electrons are respected in form of the Wannier functions (WFs), the extension of the existing Migdal approach for isolated atoms is much more natural, while the extensive nature of electrons in solids is reflected in the hopping integrals. We take diamond target as a concrete proof of principle for the methodology, and calculate relevant energy spectra and projected sensitivity of such diamond detector. It turns out that our method as a preliminary attempt is practically effective.

## Key claim

The authors extend the established Migdal effect formalism for isolated atoms to crystalline semiconductors by using atom-centered localized Wannier functions within the tight-binding approximation, demonstrating that this approach is "theoretically self-consistent and practically effective" as a preliminary attempt for diamond targets.

## Method

The authors first re-express Bloch wavefunctions in terms of atom-centered Wannier functions (WFs) generated from density functional theory calculations using Quantum Espresso and Wannier90 codes. They then impose the Galilean boost operator exclusively onto the WFs associated with the struck atom, while the delocalized nature of electrons in the crystal is encoded in the hopping integrals between neighboring atoms. The transition amplitude between initial valence and final conduction band states is computed using the matrix elements of the position operator between WFs, and the total excitation event rate is calculated by integrating over momentum transfer, DM velocity distribution, and the first Brillouin zone.

## Key numbers

- Lattice constant of diamond: a = 3.560 Å obtained from relaxation
- Momentum reference value: q_ref = 2π/a ≈ 3.48 keV
- For m_χ = 500 MeV, w = 100 km/s, and σ_χn = 10⁻³⁸ cm²: excitation event rate = 0.027 /kg/yr
- For m_χ = 10 MeV, w = 800 km/s, and σ_χn = 10⁻³⁸ cm²: excitation event rate = 0.299 /kg/yr
- Energy bin width used in form factor computation: ΔE = 0.059 eV
- Frozen energy window: from bottom of valence band to 40 eV above valence band maximum
- Widest spread of generated WFs: approximately (1.4 Å)²
- Hopping terms within up to third neighbor unit cells sufficient for convergence

## Context

This work extends the Migdal effect formalism developed for isolated atoms by Ibe et al. (2018) to crystalline semiconductors, complementing the first investigation of Migdal effects in semiconductor targets by Essig et al. (2019). The approach provides a framework for calculating electronic excitation rates in diamond detectors for sub-GeV dark matter searches, where traditional experiments using xenon (XENON1T), LUX, and PandaX become insensitive. The method enables future calculations of projected sensitivities for diamond-based dark matter detectors and can be applied to other semiconductor targets.
