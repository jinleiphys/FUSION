# Cold neutron scattering in imperfect deuterium crystals

> The differential cross sections for cold neutron scattering in mosaic deuterium crystals have been calculated for various target temperatures. The theoretical results are compared with the recent experimental data for the neutron wavelengths $λ\approx$~1--9~Å. It is shown that the structures of observed Bragg peaks can be explained by the mosaic spread of about $3^{\circ}$ and contributions from a~limited number of crystal orientations. Such a~crystal structure should be also taken into account in ultracold neutron upscattering due to the coherent phonon annihilation in solid deuterium.

# Cold neutron scattering in imperfect deuterium crystals

## Key claim

The observed Bragg peak structures in cold neutron scattering from solid deuterium crystals can be explained by a mosaic spread of about 3° and contributions from a limited number of crystal orientations, rather than by a random polycrystalline structure; however, the kinematic diffraction theory does not fully reproduce the temperature dependence of the measured coherent elastic cross section, suggesting that multiple Bragg scattering and extinction effects may be present.

## Method

The differential cross sections for cold neutron scattering in solid molecular deuterium (sD₂) were calculated using the Fermi pseudopotential and Van Hove response function formalism, including rotational-vibrational transitions in D₂ molecules, phonon processes described in the incoherent approximation with the Debye model, and separate treatment of coherent elastic Bragg scattering. The coherent elastic cross section was computed for both random polycrystalline hcp structures and for oriented hcp monocrystals, with mosaic imperfections modeled by numerically averaging over a Gaussian distribution of angular deviations with standard deviation α₀ (the mosaic spread). The theoretical total cross sections were compared with experimental data from neutron-beam attenuation measurements at wavelengths λ ≈ 1–9 Å for targets grown from gaseous and liquid D₂.

## Key numbers

- Mosaic spread of about 3° is optimal for explaining experimental Bragg peak widths; an increase or decrease by 1° leads to worse agreement with experiment.
- Debye temperature Θ_D = 110 K for 8-K sD₂ and Θ_D = 104 K for 18-K sD₂, calculated from sound velocity and molar volume with a correction factor β = 1.085 to fit the experimental value of 114 K at zero temperature.
- Root-mean-square displacement of 0.50 Å for a 5-K sD₂ crystal, in excellent agreement with experimental results from Nielsen (1971).
- Bragg-cutoff energy ε_B ≈ 2 meV for sD₂ at low pressure, determined by the shortest reciprocal-lattice vector τ_min = [1,0,0].
- Experimental data normalized to 4.1 b at λ = 6.5 Å and T = 8 K.
- Coherent scattering length b_coh = 6.671 fm and incoherent scattering length b_inc = 4.04 fm for neutron scattering from a bound deuterium nucleus.
- For the "liquid 2" sample at 8 K, a superposition of two crystal orientations [5,0,2] (45%) and [5,5,2] (55%) with 3° mosaic spread reproduces Bragg peak locations, with differences between calculated and experimental peak magnitudes not exceeding 30%.
- For the "liquid 1" sample at 8 K, three orientations [1,0,3], [1,1,3], and [2,0,1] (each 33%) with 3° mosaic spread provide an approximate description.
- For the "gas" sample at 8 K, 69 orientations corresponding to the shortest inverse-lattice vectors (range [0,0,1]–[5,1,0]) with equal probability and 3° mosaic spread roughly reproduce the data.
- At 18 K, scaling the 8-K Bragg cross section by a factor of 0.5 gives qualitative agreement with experiment.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Kasprzak (2008) and Atchison (2009), who found that simple Bragg cross sections for random polycrystalline hcp structure or a few specifically oriented hcp polycrystals did not agree with experimental data, producing peaks that were too sharp and too large. A more advanced calculation by Granroth (2009) for random polycrystalline sD₂ also disagreed with experiment in the Bragg-scattering region. The present results show that real sD₂ targets are composed of limited numbers of mosaic monocrystals rather than random polycrystals, explaining the different Bragg patterns observed in separately grown samples. This finding is important for ultracold neutron (UCN) sources, since coherent phonon annihilation in ortho-D₂ limits UCN converter effectiveness, and the polycrystalline approach neglecting crystal imperfections may lead to strong disagreement between theory and experiment, as shown in Atchison (2005).
