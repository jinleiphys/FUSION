# Generational variance reduction in Monte Carlo criticality simulations as a way of mitigating unwanted correlations

> Monte Carlo criticality simulations are widely used in nuclear safety demonstrations, as they offer an arbitrarily precise estimation of global and local tallies while making very few assumptions. However, since the inception of such numerical approaches, it is well known that bias might affect both the estimation of errors on these tallies and the tallies themselves. In particular, stochastic modeling approaches developed in the past decade have shed light on the prominent role played by spatial correlations through a phenomenon called neutron clustering. This effect is particularly of great significance when simulating loosely coupled systems (i.e., with a high dominance ratio). In order to tackle this problem, this paper proposes to recast the power iteration technique of Monte Carlo criticality codes into a variance reduction technique called Adaptative Multilevel Splitting. The central idea is that iterating over neutron generations can be seen as pushing a sub-population of neutrons towards a generational detector (instead of a spatial detector as variance reduction techniques usually do). While both approaches allow for neutron population control, the former blindly removes or splits neutrons. In contrast, the latter optimizes spatial, generational, and spectral attributes of neutrons when they are removed or split through an adjoint flux estimation, hence tempering both generational and spatial correlations. This is illustrated in the present article with a simple case of a bare slab reactor in the one speed theory on which the Adaptive Multilevel Splitting was applied and compared to variations of the Monte Carlo power iteration method used in neutron transport. Besides looking at the resulting efficiency of the methods, this work also aims at highlighting the main mechanisms of the Adaptive Multilevel Splitting in criticality calculations.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that recasting the Monte Carlo power iteration method as an Adaptive Multilevel Splitting (AMS) variance reduction technique can mitigate both generational and spatial correlations (neutron clustering) in criticality simulations, but this claim is conditioned on using the branchless collision method to handle supercritical systems and on the availability of an importance function that is optimal for the system.

## Method

The authors extended the Adaptive Multilevel Splitting (AMS) algorithm, originally developed for fixed-source variance reduction, to Monte Carlo criticality calculations by defining a generational detector and using an importance function that depends on both neutron generation and spatial position. They tested the method on a one-dimensional homogeneous bare slab reactor with one-speed neutron theory, comparing AMS with branchless collisions against four variants of the power iteration method (analog or branchless collisions combined with sampling with replacement or combing population control). All calculations used 1000 neutrons per cycle over 1000 successive generations in 1000 independent runs, with a deliberately low number of particles per generation to facilitate clustering.

## Key numbers

- The slab reactor had a total size of 100 cm (from x_min = -50.0 cm to x_max = 50.0 cm) with macroscopic cross sections: fission 0.250 cm^-1, absorption 0.575 cm^-1, scattering 0.425 cm^-1, and total 1.00 cm^-1
- The system was slightly supercritical with k_eff = 1.03437
- The AMS with branchless collisions achieved a Figure of Merit (FoM) multiplied by 100 compared to an elementary power iteration, close in magnitude to the power iteration using combing and branchless collisions
- All calculations used 1000 neutrons per cycle (N = 1000 initial independent tracks for AMS) over G = 1000 successive generations in M = 1000 independent runs

## Context

This work extends the Adaptive Multilevel Splitting method previously applied to shielding calculations (Louvin 2017) to criticality calculations, addressing the neutron clustering phenomenon identified in prior work by Dumonteil (2014) and Zoia (2014) as a source of biases in Monte Carlo criticality simulations. The approach contrasts with recent investigations by Sutton (2022) and Cosgrove (2020) that modified the power iteration method to counter clustering through beneficial changes to population control. The results suggest that AMS could be extended to time-dependent kinetics calculations and loosely coupled systems where the importance function would have more significant effects.
