# The nuclear density of states and the role of the residual interaction

> We discuss the role of mean-field and moment methods in microscopic models for calculating the nuclear density of states (also known as the nuclear level density). Working in a shell-model framework, we use moments of the nuclear many-body Hamiltonian to illustrate the importance of the residual interaction for accurate representations.

# The nuclear density of states and the role of the residual interaction

## Key claim
Spectral distribution (moment) methods can systematically incorporate the residual interaction beyond the mean-field approximation for nuclear level densities, but practical application requires accurate computation of third and fourth moments, which remains computationally challenging and currently limits the approach to lower excitation energies.

## Method
The authors work within a finite shell-model space and compute configuration moments (centroids, widths, skewness, kurtosis) of the nuclear many-body Hamiltonian. They approximate the total level density as a sum of partial densities for each spherical shell-model configuration, using either Gaussian distributions (second moments only) or modified Breit-Wigner forms (fitted to first four moments). The results are compared against exact diagonalization in the sd-shell and lower pf-shell, and against experimental level densities.

## Key numbers
- For ³²S in the sd-shell with the USD interaction, the mean-field Fermi gas calculation works "extremely well" for a spherical nucleus (Fig. 1, left panel).
- For ²⁴Mg, which is deformed, neither spherical nor deformed mean-field Fermi gas calculations yield a good description (Fig. 1, right panel).
- The sum-of-Gaussians approximation (using only first and second configuration moments) improves results for ²⁴Mg over the mean-field approach but still performs poorly at low energy (Fig. 2).
- The modified Breit-Wigner sum (using first through fourth configuration moments) yields "significant improvements" over the Gaussian sum for both ³²S and ²⁴Mg (Fig. 2).
- For sd-shell nuclides, the moment-method calculations agree well with experimental state densities (Fig. 3).
- For pf-shell nuclides, the calculations work well for ⁴⁸Ti and ⁴⁵Sc but not for ⁵¹V and ⁵²Cr (Fig. 4).

## Context
This work extends spectral distribution methods (also called statistical spectroscopy) as reviewed by Wong (1986) and applies them to nuclear level densities, which are the most uncertain input for Hauser-Feshbach statistical neutron capture calculations (Rauscher, Thielemann, and Kratz, 1997). The authors contrast their approach with the widely used generalized Bethe Fermi gas models, including those based on Skyrme Hartree-Fock single-particle spectra (Goriely, 1996), which largely ignore the residual interaction except for pairing. The method enables systematic inclusion of the residual interaction's effects on level densities, but the authors note that the most significant remaining issue for large-scale application is the choice of shell-model interaction, particularly the need to include intruder configurations of opposite parity.
