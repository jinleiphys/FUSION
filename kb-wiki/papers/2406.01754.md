# Validating Automated Resonance Evaluation with Synthetic Data

> The integrity and precision of nuclear data are crucial for a broad spectrum of applications, from national security and nuclear reactor design to medical diagnostics, where the associated uncertainties can significantly impact outcomes. A substantial portion of uncertainty in nuclear data originates from the subjective biases in the evaluation process, a crucial phase in the nuclear data production pipeline. Recent advancements indicate that automation of certain routines can mitigate these biases, thereby standardizing the evaluation process, reducing uncertainty and enhancing reproducibility. This article contributes to developing a framework for automated evaluation techniques testing, emphasizing automated fitting methods that do not require the user to provide any prior information. This approach simplifies the process and reduces the manual effort needed in the initial evaluation stage. It highlights the capability of the framework to validate and optimize subroutines, targeting the performance analysis and optimization of the fitting procedure using high-fidelity synthetic data (labeled experimental data) and the concept of a fully controlled computational experiment. An error metric is introduced to provide a clear and intuitive measure of the fitting quality by quantifying the accuracy and performance across the specified energy. This metric sets a scale for comparison and optimization of routines or hyperparameter selection, improving the entire evaluation process methodology and increasing reproducibility and objectivity.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that using high-fidelity synthetic data (labeled experimental data) enables validation and hyperparameter optimization of automated resonance evaluation routines, but the resulting hyperparameters are only "locally-optimal" because their effectiveness is tailored to specific synthetic data that mimics particular experimental conditions, and they may not be universally optimal across different datasets.

## Method

The authors developed a framework that generates high-fidelity synthetic data statistically indistinguishable from real experimental observables, then uses this data to benchmark and optimize an automated resonance identification subroutine (ARIS) treated as a black-box tool. ARIS processes synthetic data and produces estimates for resonance parameters without requiring prior information about resonance locations or parameters, using a two-step approach: creating a redundant feature bank for regression followed by backward stepwise selection to remove excess resonances. The framework introduces error metrics based on cross-section space (mean squared deviation of fitted cross-section from true cross-section) and resonance parameter space (strength function differences) to quantify fitting quality and enable hyperparameter selection.

## Key numbers

- The choice of evaluated nuclear data for $^{35}$Cl could change a reactor's criticality by about 2200 pcm, demonstrating the importance of accurate nuclear data evaluation.
- The error metric $\varepsilon_{\sigma}$ is computed using a default energy grid density of 100 points per eV to ensure the summation closely approximates the intended integral.
- In the example case, the cross-section error metric $\varepsilon_{\sigma}$ improved from 13.16 barns$^2$ (initial spin group assignment) to 6.42 barns$^2$ (after spin group reassignment and refit).
- The partial strength function error metric $\varepsilon_{\text{PSF}}$ improved from $3.63 \times 10^{-4}$ (initial assignment) to $0.35 \times 10^{-4}$ (after reassignment).
- The strength function error metric $\varepsilon_{\text{SF}}$ improved from $4.94 \times 10^{-7}$ to $2.72 \times 10^{-7}$ after spin group reassignment.

## Context

This work extends the development of automated resonance evaluation methods, specifically building on the automated resonance identification subroutine (ARIS) described by Walton (2024) and the synthetic data generation methodology by Walton and Brown (2024). The framework addresses challenges identified by the Working Party on International Nuclear Data Evaluation Cooperation (WPEC) subgroup 49 regarding reproducibility in nuclear data evaluation, and responds to the need for reducing subjective biases in the evaluation process that currently relies heavily on expert judgment using codes like SAMMY or REFIT. The approach enables systematic testing of evaluation routines, hyperparameter optimization, and sensitivity studies that were previously difficult due to the lack of labeled data with known true solutions.
