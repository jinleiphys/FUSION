# Entanglement entropy, single-particle occupation probabilities, and short-range correlations

> For quantum many-body systems with short-range correlations (SRCs), the intimate relationship between their magnitude, the behavior of the single-particle occupation probabilities at momenta larger than the Fermi momentum, and the entanglement entropy is a new qualitative aspect not studied and exploited yet. A large body of recent condensed matter studies indicate that the time evolution of the entanglement entropy describes the non-equilibrium dynamics of isolated and strongly interacting many-body systems, in a manner similar to the Boltzmann entropy, which is strictly defined for dilute and weakly interacting many-body systems. Both theoretical and experimental studies in nuclei and cold atomic gases have shown that the fermion momentum distribution has a generic behavior $n(k)=C/k^4$ at momenta larger than the Fermi momentum, due to the presence of SRCs, with approximately 20\% of the particles having momenta larger than the Fermi momentum. The presence of the long momentum tails in the presence of SRCs changes the textbook relation between the single-particle kinetic energy and occupation probabilities, $n_\text{mf}(k) = {1}/\{ 1+\expβ[ε(k)-μ]\}$ for momenta very different form the Fermi momentum, particularly for dynamics processes. SRCs induced high-momentum tails of the single-particle occupation probabilities increase the entanglement entropy of fermionic systems, which in its turn affects the dynamics of many nuclear reactions, such as heavy-ion collisions and nuclear fission.

## Key claim

Short-range correlations (SRCs) induce high-momentum tails in the single-particle occupation probabilities that increase the entanglement entropy of fermionic systems, and this entanglement entropy plays a role analogous to Boltzmann entropy for the non-equilibrium dynamics of isolated, strongly interacting many-body systems, even though Boltzmann entropy is strictly defined only for dilute and weakly interacting systems.

## Method

The paper derives the relationship between the single-particle momentum distribution n(k), the entanglement entropy S, and short-range correlations by analyzing the canonical occupation probabilities from the one-body density matrix. It parametrizes the momentum distribution as a combination of a mean-field thermal component at low momenta and a power-law tail n(k) = C/k^4 at momenta above a threshold k0, following the Tan relations. The entanglement entropy is computed from the orbital entanglement spectrum using the formula S = -g Σ [nα ln nα + (1-nα) ln(1-nα)], and the results are illustrated using time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) calculations of induced fission of 235U(n,f).

## Key numbers

- Approximately 20% of particles have momenta larger than the Fermi momentum due to SRCs
- The momentum distribution follows n(k) = C/k^4 for momenta k > kF, with the contact C(k0) = η(k0) nmf(k0) k0^4
- In TDDFT calculations of 235U(n,f), nucleon momenta up to pcut = ℏπ/dx ≈ 600 MeV/c are present (spatial resolution dx = 1 fm)
- The saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is n0 ≈ 0.16 fm^-3
- For T = 0.25 MeV and k0/kF = 1.0, the ratio of entropy with SRCs to mean-field entropy is 124.6; for T = 4 MeV and k0/kF = 1.3, the ratio is 1.27
- The IR knee in canonical occupation probabilities appears at εα ≈ 40 MeV, a feature absent from the textbook mean-field distribution

## Context

This work extends the understanding of short-range correlations established by Sartor (1980), who first showed the 1/k^4 momentum tail in dilute Fermi systems, and Shina Tan (2008), who proved the universal contact relations. It connects these results to the entanglement entropy framework developed in condensed matter studies by Calabrese and Cardy (2005, 2006) and others. The paper enables the inclusion of SRC effects in time-dependent simulations of nuclear reactions such as heavy-ion collisions and nuclear fission, building on the TDDFT approach extended to superfluid systems by Bulgac (2013, 2019, 2022).
