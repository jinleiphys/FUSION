# Ignatyuk damping factor: A semiclassical formula

> Data on nuclear level densities extracted from transmission data or gamma energy spectrum store the basic statistical information about nuclei at various temperatures. Generally this extracted data goes through model fitting using computer codes like CASCADE. However, recently established semiclassical methods involving no adjustable parameters to determine the level density parameter for magic and semi-magic nuclei give a good agreement with the experimental values. One of the popular ways to paramaterize the level density parameter which includes the shell effects and its damping was given by Ignatyuk. This damping factor is usually fitted from the experimental data on nuclear level density and it comes around 0.05 $MeV^{-1}$. In this work we calculate the Ignatyuk damping factor for various nuclei using semiclassical methods.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the Ignatyuk damping factor gamma for Ni, Sn, and Pb isotopes using a semiclassical trace formula with no adjustable parameters, finding values that lie close to the experimentally accepted range for Pb isotopes, but with the caveat that the best agreement requires tuning the asymptotic level density parameter n between 7 and 8, rather than the harmonic oscillator value of 10.

## Method

The authors use a semiclassical trace formula for a spherically symmetric harmonic oscillator potential with spin-orbit interactions at finite temperature to compute the excitation-energy-dependent level density parameter a(E*) and excitation energy E* for each nucleus. They then invert the Ignatyuk formula a(E*) = a_tilde [1 + (delta W / E*) (1 - exp(-gamma E*))] to extract the damping coefficient gamma for excitation energies from 0 to 10 MeV, using shell correction energies delta W from experimental binding energies and the liquid drop model. The asymptotic level density parameter a_tilde = A/n MeV^-1 is treated as a free parameter, with n varied between 6 and 12 to study the dependence of gamma on the nuclear potential model.

## Key numbers

- For Ni-58 with a_tilde = A/8 MeV^-1, the average gamma over 0-10 MeV excitation energy is 0.03896 +/- 0.00004 MeV^-1.
- For Sn-116 with a_tilde = A/8 MeV^-1, the average gamma over 0-10 MeV excitation energy is 0.04858 +/- 0.00008 MeV^-1.
- For Pb-208 with a_tilde = A/8 MeV^-1, the average gamma over 0-10 MeV excitation energy is 0.027179 +/- 0.00015 MeV^-1.
- For Pb-182 with a_tilde = A/8 MeV^-1, the average gamma over 0-10 MeV excitation energy is 0.07194 +/- 0.00640 MeV^-1.
- The damping coefficient gamma for Ni-48 approaches zero at n = 8.4, and for Ni-58 at n = 9.5, indicating the vanishing of shell effects.
- The typical experimentally fitted value of gamma is around 0.05 MeV^-1, with a range of 0.04 to 0.07 MeV^-1.

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical approach of Kaur et al. (2017), which used trace formulae from Brack and Jain (1995) to compute level density parameters for magic and semi-magic nuclei with no adjustable parameters, achieving agreement within 10-15% of experimental values. The authors apply this method to calculate the Ignatyuk damping factor, which is normally fitted from experimental nuclear level density data using codes like CASCADE, and find that their semiclassical values for Pb isotopes lie close to the experimentally accepted range reported by Rout et al. (2013). This enables future studies of shape transitions and nuclear potential changes with excitation energy, and the authors note that including pairing correlations and deformation in the potential would improve agreement with experimental values.
