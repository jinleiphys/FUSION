# Decoding the nuclear symmetry energy event-by-event in heavy-ion collisions with machine learning

> Inferences of the nuclear symmetry energy from heavy-ion collisions are currently based on the comparison of measured observables and transport model simulations. Only the expectation values of observables over all considered events are used in these approaches, however, observables can be obtained event-by-event both in experiments and transport model simulations. By using the light gradient boosting machine (LightGBM), a modern machine-learning algorithm, we present a framework for inferring the density-dependent nuclear symmetry energy from observables in heavy-ion collisions on the event-by-event analysis. The ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model simulations are used as training data. The symmetry energy slope parameter extracted with LightGBM event-by-event from test data also by UrQMD has an average spread of approximately 30~MeV from the truth, and is found to be robust against variations in model parameters. In addition, LightGBM can identify features that have the greatest effect on the physics of interest, thereby offering valuable insights. Our study suggests that the present framework can be a powerful tool and may offer a new paradigm to study the underlying physics in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that the nuclear symmetry energy slope parameter can be inferred event-by-event from heavy-ion collision observables using the LightGBM machine learning algorithm, but the method yields an average spread of approximately 30 MeV from the truth when tested on simulated data, and performs less accurately for the most extreme (very soft or very stiff) symmetry energy parametrizations.

## Method
The authors used the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model to simulate Au+Au collisions at 0.4 GeV/nucleon with impact parameter b=5 fm, generating 360000 training events and 50000 test events for each of six Skyrme interactions (Skz4, SLy230a, SV-sym32, SV-sym34, SkI2, SkI1) that yield different density-dependent symmetry energies. They extracted 30 observables per event related to the momenta and flow harmonics of free protons and deuterons within two rapidity windows, then trained a LightGBM regression model to predict the symmetry energy slope parameter L(ρ₀) from these event-by-event features. The trained model was tested on data generated with the same UrQMD parameter set and on three additional test sets with varied model parameters (free cross section, cluster recognition parameters, and nuclear potential stiffness).

## Key numbers
- The mean absolute error (MAE) for L(ρ₀) prediction using 30 observables on Testdata1 is 29.6 MeV.
- Using only 4 observables (pt-dependent v2) yields an MAE of 38.1 MeV, with all predictions converging toward the global average of 75.6 MeV.
- For the six Skyrme interactions, the true L(ρ₀) values range from 5.8 MeV (Skz4) to 159.0 MeV (SkI1); the predicted mean values from Testdata1 range from 44.1 MeV to 114.9 MeV.
- The standard deviation σ of predicted L(ρ₀) for individual events ranges from 16.8 MeV (Skz4) to 29.7 MeV (SkI1) on Testdata1.
- MAE values for Testdata2, Testdata3, and Testdata4 (different UrQMD parameter sets) are 29.4 MeV, 29.4 MeV, and 27.8 MeV respectively.
- For mixed impact parameter ranges b=0-2 fm and b=2-4 fm, the MAE values are 32.7 MeV and 31.2 MeV respectively.
- When averaging observables over 100 events instead of using single events, the MAE drops to 13.4 MeV.

## Context
This work extends traditional approaches that use only averaged observables over many events to constrain the nuclear symmetry energy, as done in the Transport Model Evaluation Project (TMEP) and by authors such as Russotto (2016) and the SRIT collaboration (2021). The method addresses the limitation that event-by-event fluctuations from stochastic nucleon-nucleon collisions and initial state fluctuations can conceal symmetry energy effects, as noted by Zhang (2004) and Wang (2021). The framework enables future extraction of the symmetry energy from real experimental data and can be generalized to study other physics in heavy-ion collisions, including the nuclear equation of state, in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, and quark gluon plasma shear viscosity.
