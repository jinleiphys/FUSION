# Scrutinizing $ππ$ scattering in light of recent lattice phase shifts

> In this paper, the $IJ=00, 11, 20$ partial wave $ππ$ scattering phase shifts determined by the lattice QCD approach are analyzed by using a novel dispersive solution of the S-matrix, i.e. the PKU representation, in which the unitarity and analyticity of scattering amplitudes are automatically satisfied and the phase shifts are conveniently decomposed into the contributions of the cuts and various poles, including bound states, virtual states and resonances. The contribution of the left-hand cut is estimated by the $SU(2)$ chiral perturbation theory to $\mathcal{O}(p^4)$. The Balanchandran-Nuyts-Roskies relations are considered as constraints to meet the requirements of the crossing symmetry. It is found that the $IJ=00$ $ππ$ scattering phase shifts obtained at $m_π=391$ MeV by Hadron Spectrum Collaboration (HSC) reveal the presence of both a bound state pole and a virtual state pole below the $ππ$ threshold rather than only one bound state pole for the $σ$. To reproduce the lattice phase shifts at $m_π=391$ MeV, a virtual-state pole in the $IJ=20$ channel is found to be necessary in order to balance the left-hand cut effects from the chiral amplitudes. Similar discussions are also carried out for the lattice results with $m_π=236$ MeV from HSC. The observed behaviors of the pole positions with respect to the variation of the pion masses can provide deep insights into our understanding of the dynamical origin of $σ$ resonance.

## Key claim

The lattice QCD phase shifts for $IJ=00$ $\pi\pi$ scattering at $m_\pi=391$ MeV, previously interpreted as indicating a single bound state for the $\sigma$, actually reveal the presence of both a bound state pole and a virtual state pole below the $\pi\pi$ threshold when analyzed using the PKU dispersive representation.

## Method

The authors analyzed lattice QCD phase shifts for $IJ=00, 11, 20$ partial wave $\pi\pi$ scattering using the PKU representation, a dispersive solution of the S-matrix that automatically satisfies unitarity and analyticity. The left-hand cut contribution was estimated using $SU(2)$ chiral perturbation theory to $\mathcal{O}(p^4)$, and the Balanchandran-Nuyts-Roskies (BNR) relations were imposed as constraints to enforce crossing symmetry. Separate fits to each channel were performed first, followed by combined fits with BNR penalty functions.

## Key numbers

* For $m_\pi=391$ MeV, the $IJ=00$ bound state pole was found at $\sqrt{s_b}=781$ MeV and the virtual state pole at $\sqrt{s_v}=709$ MeV in the individual fit.
* For $m_\pi=391$ MeV, the $\rho$ resonance pole in $IJ=11$ was determined at $\sqrt{s_\rho}=862.9-\frac{10.1}{2}i$ MeV from the individual fit.
* For $m_\pi=236$ MeV, the $\rho$ resonance pole in $IJ=11$ was determined at $\sqrt{s_\rho}=782.2-\frac{96.3}{2}i$ MeV from the individual fit.
* In the combined fit with modified Data391, the $IJ=00$ bound state pole was at $\sqrt{s_{b,IJ=00}}=774\pm 6$ MeV and the virtual state pole at $\sqrt{s_{v,IJ=00}}=716\pm 28$ MeV.
* In the combined fit, the $\rho$ mass and width were $M_{\rho}=863.2\pm 0.6$ MeV and $\Gamma_{\rho}=11.5\pm 1.2$ MeV.
* In the combined fit, the $IJ=20$ virtual state pole was at $\sqrt{s_{v,IJ=20}}=247\pm 99$ MeV.
* The $IJ=00$ individual fit without a virtual state pole gave $\chi^2=32.2$ for 8 data points; including a virtual state pole improved it to $\chi^2=17.0$.

## Context

This work extends the application of the PKU representation, previously used to extract $\sigma$ and $\kappa$ poles at physical pion masses (Zhou 2004) and to predict the $N^*(890)$ resonance in $\pi N$ scattering (Wang 2017, Yao 2020), to lattice QCD data at unphysical pion masses. The finding of a bound-virtual pole pair for the $\sigma$ at $m_\pi=391$ MeV contrasts with the single bound state interpretation from the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration (Briceno 2016) and differs from results using other dispersive techniques (Danilkin 2020). The observed behavior of pole positions as functions of pion mass provides insights into the dynamical origin of the $\sigma$ resonance and can help distinguish between models such as the tetraquark model (Jaffe 1976, Maiani 2004) and the unitarized meson model (van Beveren 1982, 1986).
