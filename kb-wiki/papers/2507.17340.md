# Implications of the recent neutron decay measurements on the properties of compact objects -- a dark star with nucleonic shell ?

> Recent experimental observation suggests that neutron decay is always accompanied by emission of electron while in 1% of cases proton is not emitted. We develop a scenario kinematically compatible with experimental observation, where neutron decay results in production of two dark matter particles of about half the mass of neutron and test properties of neutron stars with admixture of such particles. Constraints on mass and coupling to vector dark boson are obtained. The structure of the compact object is modified to a dark star with a shell of nucleonic matter around the nuclear saturation density.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the 1% of neutron decays where a proton is not emitted (based on comparing beam and bottle experiments) can be explained by a multi-step decay producing two dark matter particles of about half the neutron mass plus a positron, and that neutron stars with an admixture of such dark matter particles can satisfy all current astrophysical constraints only for a narrow range of dark matter mass and coupling.

## Method

The authors construct a relativistic mean field (RMF) model for nucleonic matter with parameters from Kanakis-Petousis (2024) and add fermionic dark matter particles with repulsive interaction mediated by a dark vector boson. They impose chemical equilibrium conditions linking neutron, proton, electron, and dark matter chemical potentials, enforce charge neutrality, and solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equation to obtain mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities. The allowed parameter space is constrained by requiring maximum neutron star mass above 2 solar masses, radius at 1.4 solar masses consistent with NICER measurements, and tidal deformability compatible with the GW170817 binary neutron star merger.

## Key numbers

- Dark matter particle mass range satisfying all constraints: 370–400 MeV
- Vector coupling to dark boson mass ratio satisfying all constraints: g_vD/m_vD = 0.01–0.015 MeV^-1
- The strictest astronomical constraint of 4 fm^2 for g_vD/m_vD corresponds to 0.01 MeV^-1 in natural units, further restricting DM masses to 370–400 MeV
- The most successful case uses m_D = 400 MeV and g_vD/m_vD = 0.015 MeV^-1
- The neutron half-life from bottle experiments is 878.4 ± 0.5 s; from beam experiments counting protons it is 888.0 ± 2.0 s; from the J-PARC beam experiment counting electrons it is 877.2 ± 1.7 s

## Context

This work extends the theoretical exploration of the neutron decay puzzle, following earlier studies by Fornal (2020), Motta (2018), Husain (2022–2025), and others who considered dark neutron decay channels in neutron stars. The paper specifically responds to the recent J-PARC measurement (2024) showing that electron emission accompanies every neutron decay, while 1% of decays lack a proton. The scenario enables future experimental searches for coincident positrons in neutron decay experiments and provides testable predictions for the mass and coupling of dark matter particles that could be probed by direct detection experiments like DAMIC-M.
