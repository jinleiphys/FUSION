# Charm Production in Flux Tubes

> We argue that the non-perturbative Schwinger mechanism may play an important role in the hadronic production of charm. We present a flux tube model which assumes that the colliding hadrons become color charged because of gluon exchange, and that a single non-elementary flux tube is built up as they recede. The strong chromoelectric field inside this tube creates quark pairs (including charmed ones) and the ensuing color screening breaks the tube into excited hadronic clusters. On their turn these clusters, or `fireballs', decay statistically into the final hadrons. The model is able to account for the soft production of charmed, strange and lighter hadrons within a unified framework.

## Key claim

The non-perturbative Schwinger mechanism in a flux tube with a string constant significantly larger than the standard 1 GeV/fm of a quark-antiquark string can account for the observed charm production cross section in hadronic collisions, but the authors do not claim this mechanism is the only significant source of charmed particles, noting that soft and hard mechanisms may both be necessary.

## Method

The authors developed a "firetube" model in which colliding hadrons exchange soft gluons, become color-charged, and form a single non-elementary flux tube as they recede. The chromoelectric field inside this tube creates quark pairs (including charm) via the Schwinger mechanism, and the resulting color screening fragments the tube into excited hadronic clusters ("fireballs") which decay statistically into final hadrons. Monte Carlo simulations were performed for proton-proton collisions using parameters including an average gluon exchange number of 2.0, an elementary string constant of 1 GeV/fm, and a tube cross section of 1.5 fm^2.

## Key numbers

*   The average value of the chromoelectric field force \(q_oE\) is approximately 3 GeV/fm, compared to the standard string model value of about 1 GeV/fm.
*   The dominant exponential factor in the Schwinger formula for charm production becomes as large as \(10^{-4}\) with the average field, versus \(10^{-15}\) for the standard string tension.
*   The model reproduces charged particle rapidity distributions in proton-proton collisions at center-of-mass energies of 20 GeV and 53 GeV.
*   The total charm production cross section was calculated and compared to experimental data from NA27, E743, NA25, and E653, with the energy dependence arising from the space-time volume swept by the firetube, which increases asymptotically as \(\ln(s)\).
*   The model reproduces the Feynman \(x_F\) distribution of \(D\) mesons in proton-proton collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=27\) GeV and the transverse momentum distribution of charmed mesons from the same reaction.

## Context

This work extends the "firetube" model previously developed by Nazareth, Prado, and Kodama (1989) and Nazareth, Kodama, and Portes (1992) for pions and nucleons to describe charm and strangeness production. It contrasts with perturbative QCD calculations of heavy quark hadroproduction (Nason, Dawson, and Ellis, 1988; Altarelli et al., 1988; Beenakker et al., 1989) which show strong scale dependence for charm and fail to reproduce features like small back-to-back transverse momentum correlations (Kodama et al., 1991) and leading charm enhancement (Adamovich et al., 1993; Alves et al., 1994). The model enables a unified non-perturbative framework for soft production of charmed, strange, and lighter hadrons, and the authors note that work is in progress to investigate how this mechanism scales to heavy ion collisions.
