# Oscillations at low energies

> A concise summary of the "Oscillation at low energies" parallel session at the 2014 Neutrino Oscillation Workshop is provided. Plans to use man-made neutrinos and antineutrinos to determine the neutrino mass hierarchy, search for sterile neutrinos, and to observe coherent neutrino-nucleus scattering were discussed. Potential measurements of solar neutrinos, supernova neutrinos, and geoneutrinos are also summarized.

## Key claim

This paper summarizes the discussions of the "Oscillation at low energies" parallel session at the 2014 Neutrino Oscillation Workshop, covering plans and potential measurements using man-made and natural low-energy neutrinos and antineutrinos; it is a conference summary, not a presentation of new original results, and the claims about future experimental sensitivities are based on simulations and projections, not on data.

## Method

The paper is a written summary of presentations given during a parallel session of a workshop. It collects and briefly describes the status, plans, and projected sensitivities of multiple experiments and theoretical studies presented at that session, covering reactor antineutrino experiments (JUNO, RENO-50, NUCIFER, Stereo, PROSPECT), radioactive-source experiments (SOX), coherent neutrino-nucleus scattering efforts (CENNS at the SNS), solar neutrino measurements (Borexino, KamLAND, SNO+, Super-Kamiokande), supernova neutrino detection and theory, and geoneutrino measurements (Borexino, KamLAND).

## Key numbers

* JUNO experiment: 20 kton liquid scintillator target; 53 km from reactor facilities with total power of 26.6 GW_th by 2020; predicted ~40 reactor antineutrino inverse beta decay interactions per day; after six years, can discriminate mass hierarchy with Delta-chi-squared > 9 (improving to >16 with external constraint on dmSq_32); target energy resolution of 3%/sqrt(E [MeV]).
* Stereo experiment: 58 MW_th reactor core; six 0.3 m^3 Gd-loaded liquid scintillator cells; predicted 400 antineutrino events per day; with 300 days of data, sensitivity to 5% sterile mixing with dmSq_41 of 2 eV^2 at 95% confidence level.
* PROSPECT experiment (phase 1): 2.5 ton detector at ~7 m from 85 MW reactor; ~1000 antineutrino interactions per day; can test 5% sterile mixing with dmSq_41 ~ 2 eV^2 at 3-sigma confidence level in one year.
* SOX experiment (144Ce source): 100 kCi source; half-life 285 days; antineutrinos with maximum energy 3.0 MeV; sensitive to 5% sterile mixing at 95% confidence level after one year.
* Borexino: measured pp neutrinos with 11% precision; measured 7Be neutrino interaction rate with 5% precision; detected 14.3 +/- 4.4 geoneutrinos in (3.69 +/- 0.16) x 10^31 proton-year exposure.
* KamLAND: measured 7Be solar neutrino flux of (5.82 +/- 0.98) x 10^9 cm^-2 s^-1; detected 116 (+28, -27) geoneutrino events in (4.9 +/- 0.1) x 10^32 proton-year exposure.
* Super-Kamiokande: day-night asymmetry for 8B solar neutrinos of (-3.2 +/- 1.1 (stat) +/- 0.5 (syst))%, deviating from zero by 2.7 sigma; would detect ~7300 inverse beta decay interactions from a 10 kpc distant supernova.
* Geoneutrino signal rates: Borexino 38.8 +/- 12 TNU; KamLAND 30 +/- 7 TNU.
* Earth's surface heat flux: 47 +/- 2 TW; crustal radiogenic heat: 6.8 (+1.4, -1.1) TW.

## Context

This paper summarizes the status and plans presented at the 2014 Neutrino Oscillation Workshop, extending the work of many prior experiments and proposals. It covers the JUNO and RENO-50 experiments for mass hierarchy determination (building on the work of Petcov (2002) and Learned (2006)), multiple sterile neutrino searches (NUCIFER, Stereo, PROSPECT, SOX) motivated by the reactor antineutrino anomaly (Mention (2011)) and the gallium anomaly (Abdur (2006)), and the emerging field of coherent neutrino-nucleus scattering (CENNS). On the natural-source side, it discusses solar neutrino measurements testing the LMA-MSW solution (with results from Borexino, KamLAND, Super-Kamiokande, and SNO), supernova neutrino detection (including the SNEWS alarm system and the GADZOOKS! program for Super-Kamiokande), and geoneutrino measurements (Borexino and KamLAND) that enable estimates of Earth's radiogenic heat. The paper enables future work by documenting the projected sensitivities and timelines for these experiments, and by identifying open questions such as the solar metallicity problem, the need for better reactor antineutrino emission models, and the requirement for next-generation geoneutrino detectors at carefully selected locations.
