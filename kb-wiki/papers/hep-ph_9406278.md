# Issues in Light Meson Spectroscopy: The Case for Meson Spectroscopy at Cebaf

> I review some outstanding issues in meson spectroscopy. The most important qualitative issue is whether hadrons with explicit gluonic degrees of freedom exist. To answer this question requires a much better understanding of conventional $q\bar{q}$ mesons. I therefore begin by examining the status of conventional meson spectroscopy and how the situation can be improved. The expected properties of gluonic excitations are discussed with particular emphasis on hybrids to give guidance to experimental searches. Multiquark systems are commented upon as they are likely to be important in the mass region under study and will have to be understood better. In the final section I discuss the opportunities that CEBAF can offer for the study of meson spectroscopy.

## Key claim

The most important qualitative issue in meson spectroscopy is whether hadrons with explicit gluonic degrees of freedom exist, but answering this question requires a much better understanding of conventional \(q\bar{q}\) mesons first; the author argues that without completing at least some of the missing multiplets, we cannot say we understand the meson spectrum, and that the first priority is to sort out light meson spectroscopy as a template against which to compare exotic candidates.

## Method

The author reviews the status of conventional light meson spectroscopy by comparing experimental data to predictions from a relativized quark model with a linear confining potential and spin-dependent terms from one-gluon-exchange and Thomas precession. He then discusses the expected properties of gluonic excitations, glueballs and hybrids, using two theoretical frameworks: the bag model and the flux tube model, enumerating their predicted quantum numbers, masses, and decay modes to guide experimental searches. Finally, he comments on multiquark systems and outlines opportunities for meson spectroscopy at CEBAF.

## Key numbers

- The flux tube model predicts the lowest glueball is a \(0^{++}\) state at 1.5 GeV, with all other glueball states above 2 GeV and the lowest \(J^{PC}\) exotic glueball around 2.5 GeV.
- The bag model predicts glueball masses lower than the flux tube model: \(M_{0^{++},2^{++}} = 1\) GeV, \(M_{0^{++},2^{++}} = 1.6\) GeV, and \(M_{0^{-+},2^{-+}} = 1.3\) GeV.
- The flux tube model predicts hybrid meson masses of approximately \(1.9 \pm 0.1\) GeV for hybrids with no strange quark content, with eight nearly degenerate nonets including three exotic \(J^{PC}\) nonets (\(2^{+-}\), \(1^{-+}\), \(0^{+-}\)).
- For the missing L=2 mesons, the quark model predicts the \(\eta_2 (1^1D_2)\) mass at 1680 MeV with width \(\sim 400\) MeV, and the \(\eta_2' (1^1D_2)\) mass at 1890 MeV with width \(\sim 150\) MeV.
- For radially excited P-wave mesons, the quark model predicts the \(a_2 (2^3P_2)\) mass at 1820 MeV with width \(\sim 140\) MeV, and the \(f_2 (2^3P_2)\) mass at 1820 MeV with width \(\sim 90\) MeV.
- The observed \(f_2\) at 1810 MeV has width \(\sim 200\) MeV, which is at least a factor of two larger than the quark model prediction of \(\sim 90\) MeV.
- The observed \(K_2^*(1980)\) has experimental width \(\sim 240\) MeV compared to the quark model prediction of \(\sim 150\) MeV.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing constituent quark model tradition (dating back approximately 30 years at the time of writing) and addresses the incomplete mapping of light meson spectra, particularly for orbital and radial excitations. It builds on prior work by Barnes, Swanson, and Weinstein (mid-1990s) on coupling to decay channels and by Isgur and Paton on the flux tube model, and it contrasts bag model predictions from Barnes, Close, and deViron and from Chanowitz and Sharpe. The paper enables future experimental searches at CEBAF by providing specific predictions for missing conventional states and exotic hybrids, emphasizing that high-statistics partial wave analysis will be necessary to filter by \(J^{PC}\) quantum numbers.
