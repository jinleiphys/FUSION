# Chiral symmetry breaking and the masses of hadrons: a review

> The masses of hadrons in the vacuum, where the chiral symmetry is restored, and in the medium are in general different even when the changes in the order parameters of chiral symmetry are the same. Here, we first discuss the relation between the hadron masses and the chiral symmetry breaking in approaches based on operator product expansion (OPE). We then discuss what additional changes occur to the hadron masses when going from the chiral symmetry restored vacuum to nuclear medium and/or finite temperature. The work will highlight how we can identify the effects of chiral symmetry restoration from experimental observations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that isolating chiral symmetry breaking effects on hadron masses requires measuring the mass difference between chiral partners rather than individual masses, because medium effects unrelated to chiral symmetry (such as dispersion effects and vector repulsion) contaminate individual mass shifts even when the chiral order parameter changes.

## Method

The author reviews the operator product expansion (OPE) formalism to separate quark operators into chiral symmetry-breaking and chiral symmetric components by adding and subtracting their chiral rotated forms. This separation is applied to the four-quark condensates appearing in QCD sum rules for vector and axial-vector mesons, allowing the calculation of hadron masses in a hypothetical chiral-symmetry restored vacuum where only the breaking parts are set to zero. The method is then extended to discuss additional medium effects (dispersion, nucleon self-energy, vector meson polarization splitting) that complicate experimental extraction of chiral restoration signals.

## Key numbers

- The chiral order parameter in nuclear matter is known to decrease substantially around 30% (Lacour 2010, Meissner 2001)
- For the rho and a1 system (with a1 width 400 MeV), the common mass in the chiral-symmetry restored vacuum is 572.5 ± 27.5 MeV; varying the a1 width between 250 and 600 MeV changes this mass by about 27.5 MeV
- For omega and f1, the chiral-symmetry restored masses are 655 ± 15 MeV and 1060 ± 30 MeV respectively
- For K* and K1, the chiral-symmetry restored masses are 545 ∓ 5 MeV
- The mass shift in nuclear matter is predicted to be maximally -249 MeV for K1- and -35 MeV for K1+ (Song 2018)
- The momentum-dependent correction from the twist-4 operator contributes about 2% to the vector meson mass at momentum ~0.5 GeV at nuclear matter density (Lee 1997)
- The statistical hadronization model predicts the hadronization temperature at LHC energies to be around 156 MeV (Stachel 2013)

## Context

This review extends the QCD sum rule approach to chiral symmetry breaking originally applied to vector mesons in nuclear medium by Hatsuda (1991) and the subsequent experimental programs measuring dilepton spectra from heavy ion collisions (Hayano 2008, Ichikawa 2018). The work builds on the separation of chiral symmetry-breaking and symmetric quark operators developed by Kim (2020, 2021) and applies it to identify K1 and K* as the most promising chiral partners for experimental measurement, as proposed by Song (2018) and Lee (2019). The paper enables future nuclear target experiments at JPARC and GSI to isolate chiral symmetry restoration effects by measuring the mass difference between chiral partners rather than individual masses, and suggests that anomalously large K1 to K* production ratios in relativistic heavy ion collisions could signal chiral symmetry restoration at hadronization.
