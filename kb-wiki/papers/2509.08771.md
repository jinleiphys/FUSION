# NuLattice: Ab initio computations of atomic nuclei on lattices

> We introduce NuLattice, a Python software package for ab initio computations of atomic nuclei on lattices. The computational tools consist of Hartree Fock, the coupled cluster method, the in-medium similarity renormalization group, and full configuration interaction. At present, the employed interactions are from pion-less effective field theory at leading order and consist of two-body and three-body contacts. We present results for light nuclei $^{2}$H, $^{3,4}$He, $^{8}$Be, $^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O. NuLattice algorithms exploit the sparsity and locality of lattice interactions, and as a result computations can be run on laptops.

## Key claim

NuLattice demonstrates that *ab initio* nuclear structure computations using a spatial lattice basis can be performed on laptops, but only for Hamiltonians from pion-less effective field theory at leading order where the interactions are short-ranged contact terms; the approach is currently limited to light nuclei and small lattice sizes, with the IMSRG method restricted to lattices of size L=2 or L=3 due to memory constraints.

## Method

The authors implement Hartree-Fock, coupled-cluster with singles and doubles (CCSD), in-medium similarity renormalization group at the IMSRG(2) level, and full configuration interaction (FCI) using a cubic spatial lattice single-particle basis with periodic boundary conditions. The Hamiltonian is taken from pion-less effective field theory at leading order, consisting of a kinetic energy term coupling neighboring sites and on-site two-body and three-body contact interactions. The code exploits the sparsity of lattice interactions by storing nonzero matrix elements as Python lists and using sparse-dense and dense-dense tensor contractions via `opt_einsum` for the many-body solvers.

## Key numbers

- For $^{16}$O on a lattice with spacing $a=2.5$ fm and size $L=5$, CCSD computations require 15,900 MB of memory, 1,517 seconds runtime, and 23 iterations on a 12-core Intel i7-9750H CPU with 32 GB RAM
- For $^{16}$O on $L=4$ with $a=2.5$ fm, CCSD uses 4,100 MB memory and 330 seconds; for $L=3$, 600 MB and 34 seconds
- The single-particle basis dimension is $D=4L^3$; for $L=5$ this gives $D=500$ states, comparable to a harmonic oscillator basis with $N_{\rm max}=7$ giving $D_{\rm ho}=480$
- Calibrated ground-state energies for $^4$He: $-29.70$ MeV (lattice spacing $a=2.5$ fm, $v=-9.0$, $w=6.0$), $-29.45$ MeV ($a=2.0$ fm, $v=-8.0$, $w=5.5$), $-28.97$ MeV ($a=1.7$ fm, $v=-7.0$, $w=4.4$)
- Corresponding $^3$He energies: $-16.32$ MeV, $-14.84$ MeV, $-10.82$ MeV; $^2$H energies: $-2.48$ MeV, $-2.53$ MeV, $-2.33$ MeV
- The Hamiltonian contains $28L^3$ kinetic energy terms, $8L^3$ two-body terms, and $4L^3$ three-body terms
- At $L=3$, the IMSRG(2) storage cost for $\Gamma^{pq}_{rs}$ is about 1 GB, with peak memory around 20 GB

## Context

This work extends the lattice effective field theory approach of Lee (2009) and Lahde (2019) by implementing multiple many-body methods (coupled-cluster, IMSRG, Hartree-Fock, FCI) in a single open-source Python package, contrasting with traditional harmonic oscillator basis approaches that require tens of terabytes for three-nucleon force storage as noted by Miyagi (2022). The code enables *ab initio* computations on laptops for light nuclei, making such calculations accessible to a broader research and education community, and builds on prior open-source nuclear structure software including the IMSRG code of Stroberg (2018) and the coupled-cluster code of Hagen (2014). Future work could extend to larger lattices, more realistic interactions from chiral effective field theory, and importance-truncation techniques for IMSRG as explored by Hoppe (2022).
