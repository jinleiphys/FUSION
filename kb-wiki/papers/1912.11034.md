# Vortical effects in Dirac fluids with vector, chiral and helical charges

> Helicity of free massless Dirac fermions is a conserved, Lorentz-invariant quantity at the level of the classical equations of motion. For a generic ensemble consisting of particles and antiparticles, the helical and chiral charges are different conserved quantities. The flow of helicity can be modelled by the helicity current, which is again conserved in the absence of interactions. Similar to the axial vortical effect which generates an axial (chiral) current, the helicity current is induced by vorticity in a finite temperature medium with vector (electrical) charge imbalance via the helical vortical effects, leading to new nondissipative transport phenomena. These phenomena lead to the appearance of a new hydrodynamic excitation, the helical vortical wave. Our results suggest the existence of a new type of triangle anomalies in QED which involve the helicity currents in addition to the standard vector and axial currents. Further exploiting the conservation of the helical current, we show that a finite helical chemical potential may be used to characterise thermodynamic ensembles of fermions similarly to, but independently of, the vector charge and chirality. We derive the pressure $P$ for fermions at finite vector, axial and helical chemical potentials and show that the quantities arising in anomalous transport, including various vortical and circular conductivities and the shear-stress coefficients, can be obtained by differentiation of $P$ with respect to the appropriate chemical potentials. Finally, we calculate the helicity relaxation time in the quark-gluon plasma above the crossover and show that it is similar to that for the axial charge.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that, in addition to the well-known chiral vortical effect, a rotating fluid of massless Dirac fermions with a vector (electrical) charge imbalance also generates a helicity current via new "helical vortical effects," and that these effects lead to a new hydrodynamic excitation called the helical vortical wave; the authors suggest these phenomena imply a new type of triangle anomaly in QED involving helicity currents.

## Method

The authors first establish the classical conservation of the helicity current for free massless Dirac fermions and introduce a helical chemical potential μ_H alongside the familiar vector and axial chemical potentials. They then compute the expectation values of the vector, axial, and helical charge currents and the stress-energy tensor at finite temperature T, finite chemical potentials (including μ_H), and finite rotation parameter Ω using both a classical Fermi-Dirac distribution approach and finite-temperature quantum field theory. The pressure P for fermions at finite vector, axial, and helical chemical potentials is derived, and the vortical and circular conductivities as well as shear-stress coefficients are obtained by differentiating P with respect to the appropriate chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- The helicity relaxation time in the quark-gluon plasma above the crossover is calculated and shown to be similar to that for the axial charge (no explicit numerical value given in the truncated text).
- The relation between chirality χ_j and helicity λ_j for particle modes is χ_j = 2λ_j, where χ_j = ±1 and λ_j = ±1/2.
- The paper uses Planck units (ħ = c = k_B = 1) throughout, with the convention ε^{0123} = (-g)^{-1/2} and the (+---) metric signature.

## Context

This work extends the study of anomalous transport in rotating relativistic fluids, specifically building on the chiral vortical effect (CVE) studied by various authors (CVE:1, CVE:2, CVE:3, CVE:4, Yamamoto 2015) and the broader hydrodynamics of relativistic plasmas with nonzero vorticity (Jiang 2015, Boyarsky 2015, Pavlovic 2016, Hattori 2017, Rybalka 2018). The results suggest applications in noncentral ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions that create quark-gluon plasma, the "most vortical fluid ever known" (STAR 2017), and may enable experimental studies of helical transport in Dirac and Weyl semimetals.
