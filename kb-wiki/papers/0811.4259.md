# The harmonic hyperspherical basis for identical particles without permutational symmetry

> The hyperspherical harmonic basis is used to describe bound states in an $A$--body system. The approach presented here is based on the representation of the potential energy in terms of hyperspherical harmonic functions. Using this representation, the matrix elements between the basis elements are simple, and the potential energy is presented in a compact form, well suited for numerical implementation. The basis is neither symmetrized nor antisymmetrized, as required in the case of identical particles; however, after the diagonalization of the Hamiltonian matrix, the eigenvectors reflect the symmetries present in it, and the identification of the physical states is possible, as it will be shown in specific cases. We have in mind applications to atomic, molecular, and nuclear few-body systems in which symmetry breaking terms are present in the Hamiltonian; their inclusion is straightforward in the present method. As an example we solve the case of three and four particles interacting through a short-range central interaction and Coulomb potential.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that bound states of identical particles can be accurately computed using the harmonic hyperspherical (HH) basis *without* first constructing symmetrized or antisymmetrized basis functions; the correct permutational symmetry emerges from diagonalization of the Hamiltonian matrix, and physical states can be identified among the eigenvectors, provided the Hamiltonian commutes with the permutation group.

## Method

The authors expand the potential energy operator in terms of a "potential basis" (PB) subset of HH functions, which depends only on the coordinates of a given particle pair. They then transform each PB element into the reference-set HH basis using analytically known kinematic rotation coefficients, yielding a compact expression for the potential as a sum over hyperangular functions \( \mathcal{G}^l_n(\Omega) \). The Hamiltonian matrix is constructed as a tensor product of hyperradial (Laguerre polynomial) and hyperangular (HH) parts, and diagonalized directly without prior symmetrization; the resulting eigenvectors are classified by their irreducible representations of the symmetric group \( S_A \).

## Key numbers

- For the Volkov potential (\(\hbar^2/m = 41.47\ \text{MeV\,fm}^{-2}\)), the two-nucleon system has a binding energy of **0.54592 MeV**.
- For \(A=3\) at \(K_{\text{max}}=30\), the dimension of the unsymmetrized basis is **72**; the symmetrized basis dimension is **27**.
- For \(A=4\) at \(K_{\text{max}}=30\), the unsymmetrized basis dimension is **7872** (K-type Jacobi coordinates) or **4056** (H-type); the symmetrized basis dimension is approximately **600**.
- The Volkov potential parameters: \(V_R = 144.86\ \text{MeV}\), \(R_1 = 0.82\ \text{fm}\), \(V_A = -83.34\ \text{MeV}\), \(R_2 = 1.6\ \text{fm}\).

## Context

This work extends the HH method for few-body systems (previously applied to \(A=3,4\) nuclei as reviewed by Kievsky (2008)) by avoiding the difficult step of constructing basis functions with definite permutational symmetry, a task that becomes increasingly complex for larger \(A\) and higher \(K\). The approach builds on the potential basis formalism of Fabre (1983) and uses kinematic rotation coefficients; for \(A=3\) these are the Raynal-Revai coefficients, while for \(A>3\) the coefficients are computed numerically. The method enables straightforward inclusion of symmetry-breaking terms (e.g., Coulomb interactions) and is intended as a first step toward applying the HH basis to systems with \(A>4\) interacting through realistic potentials.
