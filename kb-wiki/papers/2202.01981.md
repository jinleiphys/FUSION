# In-flight production of an isomeric beam of $^{16}$N

> An in-flight beam of $^{16}$N was produced via the single-neutron adding ($d$,$p$) reaction in inverse kinematics at the recently upgraded Argonne Tandem Linear Accelerator System (ATLAS) in-flight system. The amount of the $^{16}$N beam which resided in its excited 0.120-MeV $J^π=0^-$ isomeric state (T$_{1/2}\approx5$ $μ$s) was determined to be 40(5)% at a reaction energy of 7.9(3) MeV/$u$, and 24(2)% at a reaction energy of 13.2(2) MeV/$u$. The isomer measurements took place at an experimental station $\approx30$ m downstream of the production target and utilized an Al beam-stopping foil and a HPGe Clover detector. Composite $^{16}$N beam rate determinations were made at the experimental station and the focal plane of the Argonne in-flight radioactive ion-beam separator (RAISOR) with Si $Δ$E-E telescopes. A Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) approach was coupled with the known spectroscopic information on $^{16}$N in order to estimate the relative $^{16}$N isomer yields and composite $^{16}$N beam rates. In addition to the observed reaction-energy dependence of the isomer fraction, a large sensitivity to angular acceptance of the recoils was also observed.

## Key claim

The fraction of an in-flight $^{16}$N beam residing in its 0.120-MeV $J^\pi=0^-$ isomeric state depends strongly on both the reaction energy and the angular acceptance of the recoils, with the isomer fraction increasing at lower reaction energies due to improved momentum matching for $\ell=0$ neutron transfer.

## Method

The $^{16}$N beam was produced via the $^{15}$N($d$,$p$)$^{16}$N single-neutron adding reaction in inverse kinematics at two reaction energies using the upgraded ATLAS in-flight system with the RAISOR separator. The isomer fraction was determined by comparing the number of 120-keV $\gamma$-ray transitions from the isomer decay to the number of 1755-keV and 2742-keV $\gamma$-ray transitions from the ground-state $\beta$-decay, measured with a HPGe Clover detector at an experimental station approximately 30 m downstream of the production target. Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA) calculations using the code PTOLEMY were performed to estimate the relative isomer yields and composite beam rates, incorporating known spectroscopic overlaps and $\gamma$-ray branching ratios.

## Key numbers

- Isomer fraction of 40(5)% at a reaction energy of 7.9(3) MeV/$u$
- Isomer fraction of 24(2)% at a reaction energy of 13.2(2) MeV/$u$
- $^{16}$N beam rate of 4-8$\times10^3$ pps/pnA at the experimental station for the low-energy setting (7.9 MeV/$u$)
- $^{16}$N beam rate of 1-3$\times10^3$ pps/pnA at the experimental station for the high-energy setting (13.2 MeV/$u$)
- Composite $^{16}$N rate of 25-30$\times10^3$ pps/pnA measured at the RAISOR focal plane for the high-energy setting
- Isomer half-life of 5.25(6) $\mu$s; ground-state half-life of 7.13(2) s
- RAISOR angular acceptance of 75 mrad and energy acceptance up to $\Delta$E/E = 10%
- Optimal magnetic rigidity offset of $\Delta$B$\rho$ = +2.5% relative to the primary degraded $^{15}$N beam

## Context

This work extends the technique of producing isomeric beams via single-nucleon transfer reactions, previously demonstrated at facilities worldwide and at ATLAS since the late 1990s (Harss, 2000). The $^{15}$N($d$,$p$) reaction had been studied at various bombarding energies for nuclear structure and astrophysics (Warburton, 1957; Bohne, 1972; Bardayan, 2008). The results enable future reaction and spectroscopic studies using the $^{16}$N isomeric beam, and demonstrate that both reaction energy and angular acceptance are key degrees of freedom for tuning the isomer content in in-flight radioactive beams.
