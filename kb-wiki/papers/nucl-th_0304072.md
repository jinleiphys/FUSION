# Kinematical classification of two-pion production on the nucleon

> We give a full kinematical classification of all the tree-level two-pion photoproduction processes on the nucleon, which consists of seventeen diagrams. It suggests a method of analysis of two-pion data with little model bias.

## Key claim
The paper provides a full kinematical classification of all seventeen tree-level two-pion photoproduction processes on the nucleon, but the author notes that a perturbative approach has limited applicability, especially above the second resonance region around 1.6 GeV, and that the widths and coupling strengths are highly correlated, requiring analysis beyond perturbation theory.

## Method
The author classifies all tree-level two-pion photoproduction diagrams into seven categories based on their invariant momentum variables: s-channel, t-channel, u-channel, cascade, π-π channel, π-photon interaction, and late photon absorption. For each channel, a single resonance propagator is inserted with archetypical masses and widths, and the invariant amplitude squared is calculated on a 100×100 grid for two angular cases (parallel and perpendicular kinematics) at three values of the invariant mass √s, producing Dalitz plots for all seventeen diagrams.

## Key numbers
- The seventeen tree-level production diagrams are classified into seven categories based on invariant momentum variables: S, T, U, C, R, P, and X.
- Archetypical resonance masses used: M_s = 1.6 GeV, M_c = 1.35 GeV, m_r = 0.77 GeV, m_π = 0.14 GeV.
- Widths set to √Γ = 0.14 GeV for all resonances except the pion with √ε = 0.003 GeV.
- The invariant amplitude squared σ_M is calculated on a 100×100 grid for each diagram.
- It takes about five seconds to calculate 102 Dalitz plots independently on a normal desktop computer.

## Context
This work extends the systematic classification of two-pion production processes, similar to the s-, t-, and u-channel distinction for 2-to-2 scattering, and addresses the limitation that most analyses (such as isobar models) focus only on s-channel processes. The paper contrasts with the more restricted approaches of Murphy and Laget (1996), who included only a subset of diagrams (C_Δ, C_N*, and combinations), and the chiral perturbation theory work of Gómez Tejedor and Oset (1995), who analyzed different isospin channels up to 1.6 GeV. The classification enables a method of data analysis with little model bias, though the author notes that proper analysis must go beyond perturbation theory to include final-state and intermediate-state interactions.
