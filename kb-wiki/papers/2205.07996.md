# Horizons: Nuclear Astrophysics in the 2020s and Beyond

> Nuclear Astrophysics is a field at the intersection of nuclear physics and astrophysics, which seeks to understand the nuclear engines of astronomical objects and the origin of the chemical elements. This white paper summarizes progress and status of the field, the new open questions that have emerged, and the tremendous scientific opportunities that have opened up with major advances in capabilities across an ever growing number of disciplines and subfields that need to be integrated. We take a holistic view of the field discussing the unique challenges and opportunities in nuclear astrophysics in regards to science, diversity, education, and the interdisciplinarity and breadth of the field. Clearly nuclear astrophysics is a dynamic field with a bright future that is entering a new era of discovery opportunities.

## Key claim

This white paper argues that nuclear astrophysics is entering a new era of discovery opportunities driven by transformational advances in rare isotope facilities, gravitational wave astronomy, multi-dimensional computational modeling, and interdisciplinary networks, but the full scientific potential of these capabilities will only be unlocked through continued investment in experimental facilities, theory, and community integration.

## Method

The paper summarizes the outcomes of the JINA Horizons community town meeting held virtually from November 30 to December 4, 2020, which brought together 575 scientists from across the world and from all relevant fields. The authors conducted a holistic review of progress and status across nuclear science, astronomy, computational modeling, and cosmochemistry, identifying new open questions and scientific opportunities in the era of multi-messenger astronomy and rare isotope nuclear science. The paper also addresses challenges in diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility, as well as early career researcher development.

## Context

This white paper is part of a series organized by the Joint Institute for Nuclear Astrophysics (JINA) over the last two decades, including the 1999 town meeting at the University of Notre Dame and the 2012 town meeting in Detroit. It complements community-driven activities within nuclear science and astronomy, particularly the recently published ASTRO2020 Decadal Survey, with which field-specific priorities are well aligned. The paper identifies pathways for improving diversity and work environment in the nuclear astrophysics community, treating these goals on equal footing with scientific goals, and emphasizes the need for center-based networks such as JINA and IReNA in the US, ChETEC and ChETEC-INFRA in Europe, and Ukakuren in Japan to integrate research efforts across experimental, observational, and theory communities.
