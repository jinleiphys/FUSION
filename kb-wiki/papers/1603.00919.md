# Nucleon Resonance Physics

> Recent results of meson photo-production at the existing electron machines with polarized real photon beams and the measurement of polarization observables of the final state baryons have provided high precision data that led to the discovery of new excited nucleon and $Δ$ states using multi-channel partial wave analyses procedures. The internal structure of several prominent excited states has been revealed employing meson electroproduction processes. On the theoretical front, lattice QCD is now predicting the baryon spectrum with very similar characteristics as the constituent quark model, and continuum QCD, such as is represented in the Dyson-Schwinger Equations approach and in light front relativistic quark models, describes the non-perturbative behavior of resonance excitations at photon virtuality of $Q^2 > 1.5GeV^2$. In this talk I discuss the need to continue a vigorous program of nucleon spectroscopy and the study of the internal structure of excited states as a way to reveal the effective degrees of freedom underlying the excited states and their dependence on the distance scale probed.

# Nucleon Resonance Physics

## Key claim

Recent high-precision meson photoproduction data with polarized beams and measurement of final-state baryon polarization observables have enabled the discovery of new excited nucleon and Δ states through multi-channel partial wave analyses, but the interpretation of these states depends critically on dynamical coupled-channel effects that can produce significant mass shifts from bare quark-model predictions, as demonstrated by the Roper resonance pole mass shift from 1736 MeV to 1365 MeV.

## Method

The experimental program used polarized real photon beams at Jefferson Lab (CLAS), ELSA, GRAAL, and MAMI to measure differential cross sections and polarization observables for exclusive meson photoproduction channels including γp → K⁺Λ, γp → K⁺Σ⁰, γp → pω, and γp → pφ. These data were incorporated into multi-channel partial wave analyses, particularly the Bonn-Gatchina framework, to extract resonance parameters. For internal structure studies, meson electroproduction data at varying photon virtuality Q² were compared with lattice QCD predictions, Dyson-Schwinger Equation calculations, and light-front relativistic quark models.

## Key numbers

- Roper resonance N(1440)1/2⁺ pole mass shifted downward to 1365 MeV from its bare core mass of 1736 MeV in dynamical coupled-channel calculations by the EBAC group at JLab
- Lattice QCD predictions of the nucleon spectrum use dynamical quarks with pion masses of 396 MeV
- Resonance-like structures observed in γp → K⁺Λ backward-angle data at invariant masses of 1.7 GeV, 1.9 GeV, and 2.2 GeV
- Five nucleon states found with nearly degenerate masses near 1.9 GeV
- Eight baryon states in the mass range from 1.85 to 2.15 GeV have been discovered or had evidence significantly strengthened over the past five years
- Continuum QCD calculations (DSE, light-front relativistic quark models) describe resonance excitation behavior at photon virtuality Q² > 1.5 GeV²
- CLAS data for γp → pφ cover a mass range up to nearly 3 GeV

## Context

This work extends the decades-long effort to map the nucleon excitation spectrum that began with Anderson (1952) and was critical to the quark model of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964) and the discovery of color by Greenberg (1964). The results build on earlier hadronic probe studies that found only a fraction of the states predicted by the SU(6)⊗O(3) constituent quark model, and the new photoproduction data from CLAS, combined with multi-channel analyses by the Bonn-Gatchina group, JLab, and Juelich groups, have begun to fill in the "missing" states. The findings enable future searches for hybrid excitations with gluonic admixtures and studies of the transition to bare quark core contributions at short distances using the upgraded CLAS12 detector at Jefferson Lab, and may also help resolve discrepancies between lattice QCD results and baryon models used to describe baryon densities in heavy ion collisions.
