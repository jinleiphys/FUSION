# The Equation of State of Hot, Dense Matter and Neutron Stars

> Recent developments in the theory of pure neutron matter and experiments concerning the symmetry energy of nuclear matter, coupled with recent measurements of high-mass neutron stars, now allow for relatively tight constraints on the equation of state of dense matter. We review how these constraints are formulated and describe the implications they have for neutron stars and core-collapse supernovae. We also examine thermal properties of dense matter, which are important for supernovae and neutron star mergers, but which cannot be nearly as well constrained at this time by experiment. In addition, we consider the role of the equation of state in medium-energy heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent theoretical calculations of pure neutron matter, combined with experimental constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy and measurements of high-mass neutron stars (around 2 solar masses), now allow relatively tight constraints on the equation of state of dense matter, but thermal properties relevant for supernovae and neutron star mergers remain poorly constrained by experiment.

## Method

The authors review how constraints on the dense matter equation of state are formulated from three parallel tracks: (1) theoretical calculations of pure neutron matter near saturation density, (2) nuclear experiments probing the symmetry energy through binding energies, neutron skin thicknesses, and giant resonances, and (3) pulsar timing measurements of neutron star masses. They then apply these constraints to neutron star structure calculations using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations of general relativity, and examine thermal properties using Landau Fermi liquid theory. The high-density equation of state is parameterized to quantify the implications for neutron star radii and maximum masses.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear saturation density is approximately n_s ~ 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The symmetry energy at saturation density S_v lies in the range 26 MeV to 34 MeV, with the exponent gamma between 0.3 and 0.7
- The largest accurately measured neutron star mass is approximately 1.97 solar masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013)
- The maximum possible neutron star mass from causality is about 4.1 solar masses, using the maximally compact equation of state
- The core-crust transition density occurs in the range 0.4 to 0.5 times nuclear saturation density
- The pressure at the core-crust boundary is p_0 = 0.243 MeV fm^{-3}
- The radius of a 1.4 solar mass star correlates with pressure as R_{1.4} = (9.52 ± 0.49)[p_beta(n_s)]^{1/4} km and R_{1.4} = (5.68 ± 0.14)[p_beta(2n_s)]^{1/4} km
- The incompressibility parameter of symmetric nuclear matter from giant monopole resonances is 220 ± 30 MeV (Blaizot 1980)
- The proton fraction at saturation density for S_v = 31 MeV is approximately x_beta ~ 0.040

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes work on the dense matter equation of state, building on earlier debates about whether the equation of state is soft or stiff that involved Brown (1985) and Blaizot (1980). It connects theoretical neutron matter calculations (Gandolfi 2012, Drischler 2014) with experimental constraints from giant resonances (Youngblood 1999, Garg 2004, Colo 2004) and neutron skin measurements. The paper enables improved modeling of neutron star structure, core-collapse supernovae, and neutron star mergers, while identifying thermal properties as a key uncertainty requiring further theoretical and experimental work.
