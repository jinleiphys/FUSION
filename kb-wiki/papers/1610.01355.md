# Time evolution of linearized gauge field fluctuations on a real-time lattice

> Classical real-time lattice simulations play an important role in understanding non-equilibrium phenomena in gauge theories and are used in particular to model the prethermal evolution of heavy-ion collisions. Due to instabilities, small quantum fluctuations on top of the classical background may significantly affect the dynamics of the system. In this paper we argue for the need for a numerical calculation of a system of classical gauge fields and small linearized fluctuations in a way that keeps the separation between the two manifest. We derive and test an explicit algorithm to solve these equations on the lattice, maintaining gauge invariance and Gauss's law.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the Classical Statistical Approximation (CSA) for studying quantum fluctuations on classical gauge field backgrounds is problematic because the UV-divergent spectrum of vacuum fluctuations can dominate the dynamics on fine lattices, preventing a continuum limit. They propose instead to solve the explicitly linearized equations of motion for the fluctuations, which maintains a well-defined weak-coupling expansion and avoids the backreaction issues of CSA, though it sacrifices the ability to resum late-time secular divergences.

## Method

The authors derive and implement a lattice discretization of the linearized equations of motion for small gauge field fluctuations \(\{a_i, e^i\}\) on top of a classical background gauge field \(\{A_i, E^i\}\) in SU(2) pure gauge theory. The algorithm uses a leapfrog time discretization with separate updates for the background and fluctuation fields, and is constructed to exactly conserve a discretized version of Gauss's law for the fluctuations at every time step, which requires a non-trivial update for the gauge field fluctuation \(a_i\) that reduces to the naive discretization only in the limit \(\ud t \to 0\). Numerical tests verify that the squared differences between the linearized evolution and a separate full evolution of the summed fields scale as \(\varepsilon^4\) with fluctuation amplitude \(\varepsilon\), and that Gauss's law violation is at the level of machine precision.

## Key numbers

- The squared differences \(\delta_E\) and \(\delta_A\) between the linearized evolution and the full evolution of the summed fields scale as \(\varepsilon^4\) for fluctuation amplitudes \(\varepsilon\) ranging from 0.5 down to 0.0001, confirming correct linearization.
- The numerical tests used a lattice with time step \(\ud t = 0.01\) and \(N_t = 200\) steps to reach a physical time of \(\ud t N_t = 2\).
- Gauss's law violation for both background and fluctuations is at the level of machine precision, with double precision giving violations orders of magnitude smaller than single precision.

## Context

This work extends the classical real-time lattice simulation approach used to model the prethermal evolution of heavy-ion collisions, where the "glasma" fields are initially boost-invariant and anisotropic. The authors argue that the CSA approach used by Gelis (2013) and others suffers from UV sensitivity that prevents a continuum limit, as noted by Moore (2001) and Epelbaum (2014). The proposed linearized method is intended to provide a clean numerical setup for studying plasma instabilities without the CSA's backreaction problems, and is formulated analogously to how quark pair production is calculated by solving the Dirac equation in a classical background. The authors note that the later-time hydrodynamization is better described by kinetic theory as in Epelbaum (2015) and Kurkela (2015).
