# Excitation spectra from angular momentum projection of Hartree-Fock states and the configuration-interaction shell-model

> We make numerical comparison of spectra from angular-momentum projection on Hartree-Fock states with spectra from configuration-interaction nuclear shell-model calculations, all carried out in the same model spaces (in this case the sd, lower pf, and p-sd_5/2 shells) and using the same input Hamiltonians. We find, unsurprisingly, that the low-lying excitation spectra for rotational nuclides are well reproduced, but the spectra for vibrational nuclides, and more generally the complex specta for odd-A and odd-odd nuclides are less well reproduced in detail.

## Key claim

The paper finds that angular-momentum projected Hartree-Fock (PHF) spectra reproduce exact configuration-interaction (CI) spectra well for rotational nuclides and simple particle-rotor coupled systems, but the agreement is significantly worse for vibrational nuclides and for odd-A and odd-odd nuclides, where the rms errors are larger and ground-state spin assignments are frequently incorrect.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock calculations allowing deformed (rotationally broken) Slater determinants, then projected states of good angular momentum using the standard projection operator with Wigner D-matrices. They compared the resulting PHF ground-state energies and excitation spectra against exact full configuration-interaction diagonalization using the BIGSTICK code, all within the same model spaces (sd, lower pf, and p-sd_5/2 shells) and using the same semi-realistic input Hamiltonians (USDB, KB3G, and a hybrid Cohen-Kurath/Wildenthal/Millener-Kurath interaction respectively). For each nucleus, they computed the rms error of the PHF excitation spectrum after shifting the PHF ground state to match the CI ground state.

## Key numbers

- For 24Mg in the sd shell: PHF ground state is 1.78 MeV above CI ground state; correlation energy is 6.62 MeV, with PHF accounting for 73% of it; after shifting, rms error in excitation spectrum is 0.10 MeV.
- For 30Al (odd-odd) in the sd shell: PHF ground state is 6.40 MeV above CI ground state; PHF accounts for only 25% of correlation energy; after shifting, rms error is 0.66 MeV.
- For 52Ti (vibrational) in the pf shell: PHF ground state is 3.16 MeV above CI ground state; PHF accounts for 42% of correlation energy; after shifting, rms error is 0.53 MeV.
- For 49Cr (particle-rotor coupled) in the pf shell: PHF ground state is 2.63 MeV above CI ground state; PHF accounts for 35% of correlation energy; after shifting, rms error is only 0.01 MeV.
- For 18C in the p-sd_5/2 space: PHF ground state is 3.84 MeV above CI ground state; PHF accounts for 44% of correlation energy; after shifting, rms error is 0.22 MeV.
- For 22F in the p-sd_5/2 space: PHF ground state is 1.41 MeV above CI ground state; PHF accounts for only 15% of correlation energy; after shifting, rms error is 0.08 MeV.
- Ground-state J-pi assignment success rates: even-even nuclei in sd and pf shells 100% (15/15 and 10/10); even-even in p-sd shell 71% (12/17); odd-A in sd shell 68% (17/25); odd-A in pf shell 55% (6/11); odd-A in p-sd shell 52% (13/25); odd-odd in sd shell 53% (8/15); odd-odd in pf and p-sd shells 33% (2/6 and 4/12).

## Context

This work extends the philosophy of previous comparisons between random phase approximation (RPA) and CI calculations by the same group (SHERPA code, Johnson 2002), but here the approximation tested is projected Hartree-Fock rather than RPA. It differs from much prior PHF work (such as the projected shell model using quasiparticle states and schematic interactions, or the MONSTER/VAMPIR codes using quasiparticle states with particle-hole mixing) by using a single deformed Hartree-Fock state with angular momentum projection, realistic interactions, and no quasiparticle or particle-hole mixing, while focusing on excitation spectra quality rather than just ground-state energies. The results enable assessment of PHF as a potential shortcut for nuclear structure calculations, particularly for rotational nuclei, while quantifying its limitations for vibrational and odd-mass systems.
