# Collisions of Deformed Nuclei and Superheavy-Element Production

> A detailed understanding of complete fusion cross sections in heavy-ion collisions requires a consideration of the effects of the deformation of the projectile and target. Our aim here is to show that deformation and orientation of the colliding nuclei have a very significant effect on the fusion-barrier height and on the compactness of the touching configuration. To facilitate discussions of fusion configurations of deformed nuclei, we develop a classification scheme and introduce a notation convention for these configurations. We discuss particular deformations and orientations that lead to compact touching configurations and to fusion-barrier heights that correspond to fairly low excitation energies of the compound systems. Such configurations should be the most favorable for producing superheavy elements. We analyse a few projectile-target combinations whose deformations allow favorable entrance-channel configurations and whose proton and neutron numbers lead to compound systems in a part of the superheavy region where alpha half-lives are calculated to be observable, that is, longer than 1 microsecond.

# Collisions of Deformed Nuclei and Superheavy-Element Production

## Key claim

Deformation and orientation of colliding projectile and target nuclei have a very significant effect on fusion-barrier height and the compactness of the touching configuration; specific configurations with large positive hexadecapole moments (negative $\epsilon_4$) can produce "hugging" configurations that are substantially more compact than spherical or gentle-fusion configurations, potentially enabling production of superheavy elements with observable alpha half-lives (longer than 1 microsecond) even for compound systems far from the center of the predicted superheavy island.

## Method

The authors develop a classification scheme and notation convention for 34 distinct limiting fusion configurations of deformed nuclei (52 when projectile and target masses are unequal), considering oblate, spherical, and prolate shapes with either positive or negative hexadecapole moments. They calculate fusion-barrier parameters using the sum of nuclear and Coulomb potentials from the Nilsson perturbed-spheroid parameterization, with deformation parameters obtained from a mass calculation, and compute fusion cross sections by integrating transmission coefficients obtained via WKB barrier penetrability over angle and angular momentum.

## Key numbers

- For $^{16}$O + $^{154}$Sm: spherical barrier height 62.21 MeV at 10.54 fm; equatorial orientation [s,p$^+$,\orie] gives barrier 63.29 MeV at 10.10 fm; polar orientation [s,p$^+$,\orip] gives barrier 57.18 MeV at 11.87 fm
- For $^{150}$Nd + $^{150}$Nd: spherical touching distance 12.33 fm with touching potential 379.10 MeV; gentle fusion [p$^+$,p$^+$,\oriec] gives touching distance 11.74 fm with touching potential 390.96 MeV; hugging fusion [p$^-$,p$^-$,\oriec] with realistic $^{186}$W deformations gives touching distance 10.86 fm with touching potential 392.38 MeV
- For $^{186}$W + $^{110}$Pd: hugging configuration [p$^-$,p$^-$,\oriec] gives barrier maximum at 11.69 fm with height 376.20 MeV and touching distance 10.99 fm with touching potential 374.14 MeV; spherical reference gives touching distance 12.18 fm with touching potential 361.10 MeV
- The $^{150}$Nd + $^{150}$Nd system leads to compound nuclei where calculated alpha-decay half-lives are less than about 0.01 microseconds
- Hugging configuration for $^{186}$W + $^{110}$Pd is 1.47 fm more compact than the spherical configuration and 0.88 fm more compact than a configuration with large negative $\epsilon_4$

## Context

This work extends the discussion of deformation effects in heavy-ion fusion, which had previously been recognized in sub-barrier fusion enhancement, to the specific problem of superheavy-element production. The authors directly address the "gentle fusion" suggestion of Norenberg (1980s) for two well-deformed rare-earth nuclei in equatorial-cross orientation, showing that this configuration is not particularly compact due to large negative $\epsilon_4$ (bulging equatorial regions). Instead, they propose "hugging fusion" using nuclei with large positive $\epsilon_4$ (neck formation), such as those near the end of the rare-earth region (e.g., $^{186}$W). The work enables systematic evaluation of specific projectile-target combinations whose deformations allow favorable entrance-channel configurations leading to compound systems in the distant superheavy region (proton number above 114), where alpha half-lives are calculated to exceed 1 microsecond based on mass calculations from Moller et al. (1995).
