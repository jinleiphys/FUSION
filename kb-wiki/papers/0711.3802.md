# N_pN_n dependence of empirical formula for the lowest excitation energy of the 2^+ states in even-even nuclei

> We examine the effects of the additional term of the type $\sim e^{- λ' N_pN_n}$ on the recently proposed empirical formula for the lowest excitation energy of the $2^+$ states in even-even nuclei. This study is motivated by the fact that this term carries the favorable dependence of the valence nucleon numbers dictated by the $N_pN_n$ scheme. We show explicitly that there is not any improvement in reproducing $E_x(2_1^+)$ by including the extra $N_pN_n$ term. However, our study also reveals that the excitation energies $E_x(2_1^+)$, when calculated by the $N_pN_n$ term alone (with the mass number $A$ dependent term), are quite comparable to those calculated by the original empirical formula.

## Key claim

Adding an explicit exponential term in the product \(N_p N_n\) to the authors' earlier empirical formula for the first \(2^+\) excitation energy in even-even nuclei does not significantly improve the fit to data, despite the \(N_p N_n\) scheme's success in correlating nuclear observables. However, a formula using only the \(N_p N_n\) term plus a mass-dependent term performs comparably to the original four-parameter formula, except near closed shells.

## Method

The authors extended their earlier four-parameter empirical formula \(E_x = \alpha A^{-\gamma} + \beta [e^{-\lambda N_p} + e^{-\lambda N_n}]\) by adding a term \(\beta' e^{-\lambda' N_p N_n}\) and allowing separate parameters for protons and neutrons. They performed least-squares fits to measured \(E_x(2_1^+)\) values from the compilation of Raman et al. (2001) under five different parameter restrictions, minimizing a dimensionless \(\chi^2\) defined from the logarithmic error between calculated and experimental energies.

## Key numbers

- The original four-parameter formula (Case I) gave \(\chi^2 = 0.151\).
- Adding the \(N_p N_n\) term to the four-parameter formula (Case II) reduced \(\chi^2\) to 0.128, a reduction of about 15%.
- Extending to six parameters without the \(N_p N_n\) term (Case III) gave \(\chi^2 = 0.126\).
- The full six-parameter formula plus the \(N_p N_n\) term (Case IV) gave \(\chi^2 = 0.117\) (best fit), a further reduction of about 6% over Case III.
- The formula using only the \(N_p N_n\) term plus the mass term (Case V, 2+2 parameters) gave \(\chi^2 = 0.140\), comparable to the original four-parameter formula's 0.151.
- For Case II, one local minimum gave \(\lambda_p = \lambda_n = \infty\), meaning the separate \(N_p\) and \(N_n\) terms contributed only at \(N_p=0\) or \(N_n=0\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier empirical formula (Ha and Cha, 2007) for \(E_x(2_1^+)\) in even-even nuclei, which was motivated by the \(N_p N_n\) scheme pioneered by Casten (1985) and Hamamoto (1965). The study tests whether an explicit \(N_p N_n\) dependence, as suggested by the scheme's success in correlating collective observables, improves the formula. The results show no significant improvement, but the comparable performance of the \(N_p N_n\)-only term raises open questions about which functional form best captures the underlying valence proton-neutron interaction. Further study is called for to resolve whether the \(N_p \times N_n\) or \(N_p + N_n\) dependence is more fundamental.
