# Three-dimensional equation of state extension of quark matter in Fermi-liquid theory

> The cold, dense matter equation of state (EoS) determines crucial global properties of neutron stars (NSs), including the mass, radius and tidal deformability. However, a one-dimensional (1D), cold, and $β$-equilibrated EoS is insufficient to fully describe the interactions or capture the dynamical processes of dense matter as realized in binary neutron star (BNS) mergers or core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe), where thermal and out-of-equilibrium effects play important roles. We develop a method to self-consistently extend a 1D cold and $β$-equilibrated EoS of quark matter to a full three-dimensional (3D) version, accounting for density, temperature, and electron fraction dependencies, within the framework of Fermi-liquid theory (FLT), incorporating both thermal and out-of-equilibrium contributions. We compare our FLT-extended EoS with the original bag model and find that our approach successfully reproduces the contributions of thermal and compositional dependencies of the 3D EoS. Furthermore, we construct a 3D EoS with a first-order phase transition (PT) by matching our 3D FLT-extended quark matter EoS to the hadronic DD2 EoS under Maxwell construction, and test it through the GRHD simulations of the TOV-star and CCSN explosion. Both simulations produce consistent results with previous studies, demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of our 3D EoS construction with PT.

## Key claim
The authors develop a method to self-consistently extend a one-dimensional cold and beta-equilibrated quark matter equation of state to a full three-dimensional version (dependent on density, temperature, and electron fraction) using Fermi-liquid theory, and they demonstrate that this approach reproduces the thermal and compositional contributions of the original bag model, but only under the low-temperature, high-density conditions where FLT remains valid.

## Method
The authors apply multi-component relativistic Fermi-liquid theory to quark matter, starting from an energy functional expanded near the ground state. They derive expressions for the entropy density, thermal energy density, and thermal pressure in terms of the Landau effective masses and Fermi momenta of up, down, strange quarks and electrons. Using a given cold beta-equilibrated bag model EoS, they solve the charge neutrality, baryon number conservation, and weak equilibrium conditions to determine the Fermi momenta and the vector potential, then compute the cold EoS at arbitrary electron fraction and add the thermal FLT contributions. They construct a full 3D EoS with a first-order phase transition by matching the FLT-extended quark matter EoS to the hadronic DD2 EoS under Maxwell construction, and test it in GRHD simulations of a TOV star and a core-collapse supernova.

## Key numbers
- The bag model parameter sets used are: B^(1/4) = 136 MeV with alpha_s = 0.5, and B^(1/4) = 140 MeV with alpha_s = 0.
- The strange quark mass is set to m_s = 100 MeV, while up and down quark masses are set to m_u = m_d = 0 MeV.
- The timescale for reactions producing s quarks is estimated as ~10^(-6) s, much shorter than the hydrodynamic timescale of ~ms.
- The entropy density from FLT is given by s_v = (gT/6) * sum_i (m_i* * p_Fi) + O(T^3), and the thermal energy density is epsilon_th = (g T^2 / 12) * sum_i (m_i* * p_Fi).

## Context
This work extends prior efforts to construct three-dimensional equations of state for dense matter used in simulations of binary neutron star mergers and core-collapse supernovae, where thermal and out-of-equilibrium effects are important. It builds on the Fermi-liquid theory framework previously applied to nuclear matter by Baym and Chin (1976) and to quark matter by various authors (2012, 2019), and it provides a method to consistently incorporate finite-temperature and composition-dependent effects into quark matter EoS tables. The approach enables more realistic hydrodynamic simulations of hybrid stars with a first-order phase transition, complementing earlier parameterized extensions such as those by Raithel et al. (2019) that used symmetry energy and Dirac effective mass fits.
