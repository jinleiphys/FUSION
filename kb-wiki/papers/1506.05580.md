# Kinetic theory of a longitudinally expanding system of scalar particles

> A simple kinematical argument suggests that the classical approximation may be inadequate to describe the evolution of a system with an anisotropic particle distribution. In order to verify this quantitatively, we study the Boltzmann equation for a longitudinally expanding system of scalar particles interacting with a $ϕ^4$ coupling, that mimics the kinematics of a heavy ion collision at very high energy. We consider only elastic $2\to 2$ scatterings, and we allow the formation of a Bose-Einstein condensate in overpopulated situations by solving the coupled equations for the particle distribution and the particle density in the zero mode. For generic CGC-like initial conditions with a large occupation number and a moderate coupling, the solutions of the full Boltzmann equation do not follow a classical attractor behavior.

## Key claim

For a longitudinally expanding system of scalar particles with a $\phi^4$ interaction, the classical approximation (keeping only cubic terms in the distribution function) may fail to describe isotropization even when the occupation number is large, because the classical terms vanish for scatterings that produce particles with large longitudinal momentum; for generic Color Glass Condensate-like initial conditions with moderate coupling, the solutions of the full Boltzmann equation appear never to follow a classical attractor solution.

## Method

The authors solved the Boltzmann equation for a boost-invariant, longitudinally expanding system of scalar particles with elastic $2\to 2$ scatterings from a $\phi^4$ coupling, using a discretized momentum grid in the variables $\omega_p$ and $p_z$ with constant spacing to exactly satisfy energy-momentum conservation from the delta functions. They included the possibility of Bose-Einstein condensate formation in overpopulated situations by solving coupled equations for the smooth particle distribution and the condensate density $n_c$, and performed each computation both with the full collision kernel and with only the cubic (classical) terms.

## Key numbers

* For a system with initial anisotropy characterized by $|p_z| < \delta Q$ (where $\delta \ll 1$), the classical approximation starts to fail for the purposes of longitudinal pressure when $f(p_z\sim 0,|p|\sim Q) \sim \delta^{-3}$, which corresponds to an angle-averaged occupancy of $1/\delta^2 \gg 1$.
* The phase space for "quantum" scatterings (those producing $|p_{z4}|\sim Q$) is larger than that for "classical" scatterings (producing $|p_{z4}|\sim 3\delta Q$) by a factor of $\sim 1/\delta$.
* Each "quantum" scattering contributes more to the longitudinal pressure than a classically-scattered particle by a factor of $\sim 1/\delta^2$, because the longitudinal pressure involves $p_z^2/p^2$.

## Context

This work extends the classical attractor scenario observed in scalar field theories and Yang-Mills theory by Berges et al. (2014), where the ratio $P_L/P_T$ was found to decrease as $\tau^{-2/3}$ in the classical approximation. The authors provide a kinematical argument that the classical approximation artificially suppresses large-angle scatterings in anisotropic systems, and they test this quantitatively by solving the full Boltzmann equation. The results challenge the three-stage picture (transient, classical attractor, quantum isotropization) proposed in earlier work, suggesting that for moderate coupling the classical attractor may not exist at all.
