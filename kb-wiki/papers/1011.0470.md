# Astronomy with Radioactivities: Chapter 9, Nuclear Reactions

> Nuclear reaction rates determine the abundances of isotopes in stellar burning processes. A multitude of reactions determine the reaction flow pattern which is described in terms of reaction network simulations. The reaction rates are determined by laboratory experiments supplemented by nuclear reaction and structure theory. We will discuss the experimental approach as well as the theoretical tools for obtaining the stellar reaction rates. A detailed analysis of a reaction is only possible for a few selected cases which will be highlighted in this section. The bulk of nuclear reaction processes is however described in terms of a statistical model approach, which relies on global nuclear structure and reaction parameters such as level density and mass and barrier penetration, respectively. We will discuss a variety of experimental facilities and techniques used in the field, this includes low energy stable beam experiments, measurements at radioactive beam accelerators, and neutron beam facilities.

# Nuclear Reactions

## Key claim

Nuclear reaction rates determine the abundances of isotopes in stellar burning processes, but a detailed analysis of a reaction is only possible for a few selected cases; the bulk of nuclear reaction processes must be described in terms of a statistical model approach that relies on global nuclear structure and reaction parameters such as level density, mass, and barrier penetration.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework for stellar reaction rates, including the formulation of reaction network equations using abundance variables, the treatment of Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions for two-body reactions, and the incorporation of stellar enhancement factors for reactions on thermally excited nuclei. They describe the Gamow window approximation for charged-particle reactions and discuss the conditions under which nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) or quasi-statistical equilibrium (QSE) can simplify network calculations. The chapter surveys experimental facilities and techniques including low-energy stable beam experiments, radioactive beam accelerators, and neutron beam facilities.

## Key numbers

- For partial waves with angular momentum ℓ > 0, the relevant energy range for neutron-induced reactions shifts to approximately E₀ ≈ 0.172 T₉ (ℓ + 1/2) MeV with width Δ₀ ≈ 0.194 T₉ √(ℓ + 1/2) MeV
- The Gamow peak for charged-particle reactions is given by E₀ = 0.12204 (Z₁² Z₂² μ)^(1/3) T₉^(2/3) MeV with width Δ₀ = 0.23682 (Z₁² Z₂² μ)^(1/6) T₉^(5/6) MeV
- Electron capture rates in stellar core collapse are enhanced at temperatures T > 1.5 MeV due to unblocking of low-lying neutron states by thermal excitation
- Full nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) is established for temperatures T > 4-5 GK, depending on density

## Context

This chapter from the volume "Astronomy with Radioactivities" provides a pedagogical overview of nuclear reaction theory for astrophysics, extending the foundational treatment of reaction rates by Fowler and Hoyle (1975) and Iliadis (2007). It enables practitioners to understand the limitations of laboratory measurements for astrophysical applications, particularly the need to correct for electron screening effects (Iliadis 2007; Salpeter and van Horn 1969) and thermal population of excited states when converting laboratory cross sections to stellar reaction rates. The discussion of waiting-point approximation in r-process calculations builds on the work of Rauscher and Thielemann (2000) and references therein on fission barriers for neutron-rich nuclei.
