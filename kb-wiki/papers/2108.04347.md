# Angular momentum removal by neutron and $γ$-ray emissions during fission fragment decays

> We investigate the angular momentum removal from fission fragments (FFs) through neutron and $γ$-ray emission, where we find that about half the neutrons are emitted with angular momenta $\ge 1.5\hbar$ and that the change in angular momentum after the emission of neutrons and statistical $γ$ rays is significant, contradicting usual assumptions. Per fission event, in our simulations, the neutron and statistical $γ$-ray emissions change the spin of the fragment by 3.5 -- 5~$\hbar$, with a large standard deviation comparable to the average value. Such wide angular momentum removal distributions can hide any underlying correlations in the fission fragment initial spin values. Within our model, we reproduce data on spin measurements from discrete transitions after neutron emissions, especially in the case of light FFs. The agreement further improves for the heavy fragments if one removes from the analysis the events that would produce isomeric states. Finally, we show that while in our model the initial FF spins do not follow a saw-tooth like behavior observed in recent measurements, the average FF spin computed after neutron and statistical $γ$ emissions exhibits a shape that resembles a saw tooth. This suggests that the average FF spin measured after statistical emissions is not necessarily connected with the scission mechanism as previously implied.

## Key claim

The authors show that neutron and statistical gamma-ray emissions remove a significant amount of angular momentum from fission fragments (3.5–5 ℏ per fission event), contradicting the common assumption that neutron emission does not significantly change fragment spin. They further demonstrate that the saw-tooth shape observed in measured average fragment spins after statistical emissions can arise from the decay process itself and does not necessarily reflect the initial spin distribution at scission.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach fission fragment decay (HF³D) model implemented in the Monte Carlo codes CGMF and BeoH to simulate the de-excitation of fission fragments via neutron and gamma-ray emission for thermal neutron-induced fission of ²³⁵U and ²³⁹Pu, 1.9 MeV neutron-induced fission of ²³⁸U, and spontaneous fission of ²⁵²Cf. They computed the angular momentum removed by each neutron and by the full cascade of neutrons and statistical gamma rays, and compared the resulting average fragment spins at the first discrete transition against experimental data from Wilson et al. (2021).

## Key numbers

- About half the neutrons are emitted with angular momenta ≥ 1.5 ℏ across all reactions studied (49.2% for ²⁵²Cf(sf) to 52.5% for ²³⁸U(n₁.₉ MeV,f))
- The average angular momentum removed by a single neutron is 1.13–1.34 ℏ depending on the reaction, with standard deviations of 1.71–1.97 ℏ
- Approximately 22–24% of neutron emissions increase the fragment angular momentum (j_rm < 0)
- After all neutrons are emitted, the average change in fragment spin is 1.84–2.20 ℏ with standard deviations of 2.35–2.51 ℏ
- After both neutron and statistical gamma-ray emission, the average angular momentum removed is 3.54–4.98 ℏ with standard deviations of 3.78–4.93 ℏ
- For ²³⁵U(n_th,f): average spin removed by neutrons and statistical gammas is 3.54 ℏ (Δ = 3.78 ℏ); for ²⁵²Cf(sf): 4.98 ℏ (Δ = 4.93 ℏ)

## Context

This work extends the HF³D model framework previously developed by the Los Alamos group (CGMF code) and directly contradicts the assumption used in the experimental analysis of Wilson et al. (2021) that neutrons are emitted predominantly as s-waves with negligible angular momentum removal. The results also differ from the classical treatment of neutron angular momentum in the FREYA model (Randrup, 2021), which uses an ad hoc parameterization for fragment spins. The finding that the saw-tooth shape in measured spins can arise from statistical decay rather than scission geometry challenges the interpretation of Wilson et al. (2021) and earlier work by Wilhelmy et al. (1972). This work enables more accurate extraction of initial fission fragment spin distributions from experimental data and suggests that correlations between fragment spins at scission may be obscured by the decay process.
