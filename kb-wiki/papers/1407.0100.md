# Moving Beyond Chi-Squared in Nuclei and Neutron Stars

> There are several assumptions made in a standard $χ^2$ analysis of data, including the frequent assumption that the likelihood function is well approximated by a multivariate Gaussian distribution. This article briefly reviews the standard approach and describes how Bayesian inference can be used to go beyond the assumption that the likelihood is Gaussian. Two separate types of analysis relevant to nuclear physics are used as test cases. The first is the determination of the equation of state of dense matter from neutron star mass and radius data. The second is the use of theoretical nuclear mass models to fit currently available data and predict the value of masses which have not yet been measured. For the problem of predicting nuclear masses, it is demonstrated that approximating the likelihood function with a Gaussian can produce biased predictions of unmeasured masses. Finally, the lessons learned from these fitting problems are used to propose a method for improving constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy.

## Key claim

Bayesian inference can improve on standard chi-squared analyses when the likelihood function is not well approximated by a multivariate Gaussian, and using the Gaussian approximation can produce biased predictions of unmeasured nuclear masses and incorrect correlations in neutron star parameter estimation.

## Method

The paper compares standard chi-squared minimization with Bayesian Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling for two test cases: determining the neutron star equation of state from mass-radius data, and fitting nuclear mass models to experimental masses. For the neutron star case, the likelihood function is constructed as a product of two-dimensional probability distributions for each star's mass and radius, and the correlation matrix is computed from Monte Carlo samples rather than from the second derivative of chi-squared. For nuclear masses, the Dieperink09 model is fit to UNEDF mass data, and predictions for the binding energy of 137Sn are compared using both the full likelihood and a Gaussian approximation.

## Key numbers

- For the neutron star analysis, the correlation matrix includes 17 parameters: 7 from the equation of state (compressibility, energy per baryon of neutron matter, derivative of energy per baryon, and four pressure differences at energy densities 2, 3, 5, and 7 fm^-4) plus 10 individual neutron star masses
- The PREX experiment measured the neutron skin thickness of lead as delta R = 0.33 (+0.16, -0.18) fm
- A photoproduction measurement at MAMI gave delta R = 0.15 (+0.04, -0.06) fm
- The Gaussian approximation for the likelihood systematically predicts less binding for 137Sn compared to the full Monte Carlo result
- When fitting all measured nuclear masses, systematic uncertainties for mass predictions are at least 3 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian analysis of neutron star data from Steiner (2010) and Steiner (2013) and the statistical analysis of nuclear masses from the UNEDF collaboration (Kortelainen 2012). It demonstrates that the common practice of reporting correlation matrices from the second derivative of chi-squared can be misleading when the likelihood is non-Gaussian, as seen in the neutron star analysis where parameters appear anti-correlated near the best fit but are actually uncorrelated. The paper proposes a method for improving constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy by fitting several models to a data set optimized to minimize both fit uncertainty and inter-model systematic variation, building on earlier work by Horowitz (2001) connecting the neutron skin thickness of lead to the symmetry energy derivative L.
