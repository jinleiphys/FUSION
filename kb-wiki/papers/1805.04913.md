# An anomalous propulsion mechanism using magnetic fields

> We consider a gas composed of a single family of standard model leptons which are approximately massless and trapped inside a charged rotating shell. Due to the magnetic vortical effect, the leptons gain momentum in the direction of the magnetic field induced by the rotating shell. We compute this momentum gain in a perturbative expansion and discuss the possible application of it to pulsar kicks.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a gas of approximately massless leptons trapped inside a charged rotating shell gains momentum in the direction of the magnetic field due to the magnetic vortical effect, and that this mechanism could potentially explain pulsar kicks, but only if the magnetic fields during core collapse are extremely high or the core radius is close to the Schwarzschild radius.

## Method

The author considers a hydrostatically equilibrated gas composed of a single family of standard model leptons (with massless neutrinos and electrons whose mass is suppressed relative to temperature) trapped inside a spherical rotating charged shell. Using the constitutive relations for the stress tensor in hydrostatic equilibrium expanded to first order in derivatives, which are completely characterized by triangle anomalies, the author computes the response of the stress tensor to the magnetic field generated by the rotating shell. The background metric and electromagnetic field are obtained by solving the Einstein-Maxwell equations perturbatively in the angular velocity, and the total momentum of the gas is computed by integrating the momentum density over the interior volume.

## Key numbers

* The kick velocity of the shell is given by \( v = -P_B^z / M \), where \( P_B^z \) is the momentum contribution from the magnetic field and \( M \) is the shell mass.
* Typical masses for cores that collapse into pulsars are in the range \( 1 \lesssim M/M_{\odot} \lesssim 2.5 \), typical radii are \( 8 \lesssim r_*/\text{km} \lesssim 16 \), and magnetic fields range from \( 10^{12} \) G for ordinary radio pulsars to over \( 10^{13} \) G in Magnetars.
* Theoretical estimates predict magnetic fields of order \( 10^{16} - 10^{17} \) G in cores of young pulsars shortly after their birth.
* The electron chemical potential and lepton chemical potential are thought to be of the order of \( 200 \) MeV, with \( \mu^- \sim 70 \) MeV.
* To observe a kick at velocities of hundreds of kilometers per second, magnetic fields and rotation rates of order \( 10^{17} \) Gauss \( \times \) MegaHertz are required.
* The ratio of the emission time scale to the core radius for a jet-like mechanism is \( t_e / r_* \simeq 3 \times 10^4 \), where \( t_e \sim 1 \) sec.

## Context

This work extends the anomalous propulsion mechanism proposed by Kaminski (2014) for pulsar kicks, which considered a jet-like mechanism where neutrinos are continuously created and emitted. The present paper instead models a balloon-like mechanism where a finite amount of trapped gas is released instantaneously, similar to propelling a balloon by punching a hole through it. The author finds that the balloon mechanism produces insufficient momentum to explain pulsar kicks, falling a couple of orders of magnitude short, whereas the earlier jet-like estimate by Kaminski (2014) allowed for higher kick values due to the large ratio of emission time to core radius. The work also builds on the general framework for anomalous hydrodynamic behavior developed by Son (2009), Jensen (2012, 2013), and Banerjee (2012).
