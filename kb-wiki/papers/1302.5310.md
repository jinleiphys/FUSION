# Flow Vorticity in Peripheral High Energy Heavy Ion Collisions

> The vorticity development is studied in the reaction plane of peripheral relativistic heavy ion reactions where the initial state has substantial angular momentum. The earlier predicted rotation effect and Kelvin Helmholtz Instability, lead to significant initial vorticity and circulation. In low viscosity QGP this vorticity remains still significant at the time of freeze out of the system, even if damping due to the explosive expansion and the dissipation decreases the vorticity and circulation. In the reaction plane the vorticity arises from the initial angular momentum, and it is stronger than in the transverse plane where vorticity is caused by random fluctuations only.

## Key claim

Vorticity in the reaction plane of peripheral heavy ion collisions arises from the initial angular momentum and is significantly stronger than vorticity in the transverse plane caused by random fluctuations, but the vorticity and circulation decrease rapidly due to explosive expansion and viscous dissipation in 3+1D, so the paper studies whether detectable vorticity remains at freeze-out.

## Method

The authors performed 3+1D computational fluid dynamics (CFD) calculations using the particle-in-cell (PIC) method for peripheral Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energy, with an initial state model where the incoming Lorentz-contracted nuclei interpenetrate and the leading charges in each streak are slowed down by string-rope tension over 3-5 fm/c. They computed both classical and relativistic weighted vorticity distributions in the reaction [x-z] plane and averaged over all y-layers, weighting each fluid cell by its local energy density normalized to unity. They evaluated the time evolution of circulation and compared configurations with impact parameters favoring or not favoring Kelvin-Helmholtz Instability (KHI).

## Key numbers

- Collision energy: sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, impact parameter b = 0.7 b_max (cell size dx=dy=dz=0.4375 fm) for the primary configuration studied
- Initial average classical weighted vorticity in the reaction plane at t=0.17 fm/c: 0.1434 c/fm; relativistic weighted vorticity: 0.1185 c/fm
- At t=3.56 fm/c, average classical weighted vorticity in the reaction plane: 0.04845 c/fm; relativistic: 0.07937 c/fm
- At t=6.94 fm/c, average classical weighted vorticity in the reaction plane: 0.01555 c/fm; relativistic: 0.05881 c/fm
- For all [x-z] layers at t=0.17 fm/c, average classical weighted vorticity: 0.1971 c/fm; relativistic: 0.19004 c/fm
- For all layers at t=3.56 fm/c, average classical weighted vorticity: 0.0538 c/fm; relativistic: 0.10685 c/fm
- For all layers at t=6.94 fm/c, average classical weighted vorticity: 0.0159 c/fm; relativistic: 0.05881 c/fm
- Initial peak vorticity exceeded 3 c/fm, more than 10 times larger than the 0.2 c/fm from random fluctuations in the transverse plane (Stefan)
- At b=0.5 b_max (cell size 0.585 fm), initial average relativistic weighted vorticity for all layers: 0.07241 c/fm; at b=0.7 b_max: 0.19004 c/fm
- Forward rotation angle of the vorticity peak: 37 degrees for KHI-favorable configuration, 33 degrees for less favorable configuration
- For the more peripheral case (b=0.7 b_max), initial classical circulation was about 15% larger than for b=0.5 b_max; the two cases became equal around t = 8-10 fm/c

## Context

This work extends earlier predictions of rotation and Kelvin-Helmholtz Instability in the reaction plane from 2+1D hydrodynamic models (hydro1, hydro2) to a realistic 3+1D CFD description that accounts for dissipation due to 3D expansion, which was neglected in idealized calculations. The initial state model follows the shear flow description of M2001-2 and is similar to the laminar shear flow assumed in studies of polarization by Becattini and by XNWang. The results enable experimental searches for rotation and KHI signatures in peripheral heavy ion collisions at LHC, particularly through the rapidity-odd component of directed flow v1(y) as suggested by Eyyubova, and through other correlation observables to be addressed in forthcoming publications (CV13).
