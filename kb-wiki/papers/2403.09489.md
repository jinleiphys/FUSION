# Gravitational waves from glitch-induced f-mode oscillations in quark and neutron stars

> Matter in compact stars is dense enough that transient events within the star could have sufficiently high energies to produce detectable gravitational waves (GWs). These GWs could be used to constrain the equation of state (EoS) for matter in the star and could reveal that there is more than one type of EoS at play in the population, implying that multiple types of compact stars exist. One of these types could be quark stars, composed almost entirely of stable quark matter, and observing GWs is a way to test for the strange matter EoS. Here we explore the possibility that, if fundamental (f-) mode oscillations in pulsars are induced by a pulsar glitch, then these oscillations might produce detectable GWs. We use the existing population of pulsars and their glitches, as well as a much larger synthesized population, along with 15 EoSs (8 for neutron stars and 7 for quark stars) to generate frequencies, damping times, and GW strengths for each. We find that of the EoSs examined, all quark star EoSs produce narrower distributions of f-mode frequency than neutron star EoSs. This result, along with other elements of the data, could be used to differentiate between GWs (or other signals from f-modes) originating from neutron stars and quark stars and thus could confirm the existence of quark stars. We also find that GW astronomy is a potentially viable method for detecting a larger population of pulsars which are not observable electromagnetically and that future GW observatories have the possibility to greatly expand this capability.

## Key claim

If glitch-induced f-mode oscillations in pulsars produce detectable gravitational waves, the f-mode frequency distributions from all seven quark star equations of state examined are universally narrower than those from any of the eight neutron star equations of state, which could allow differentiation between the two types of compact star. However, the calculated gravitational wave strains are upper limits because the model assumes 100% efficiency in transferring glitch energy into gravitational wave energy, and the actual efficiency is unknown.

## Method

The authors used three populations: Population 1 (650 recorded glitches across 195 real pulsars with Gaussian mass distribution, mean 1.4 solar masses, width 0.15 solar masses), Population 2 (same glitches with uniform mass distribution from 1 to 2 solar masses), and Population 3 (10,000 synthesized pulsars and glitches with randomly generated mass, spin frequency, glitch size, and distance). For each glitch in each population, they calculated f-mode frequency, damping time, and gravitational wave strain using 15 equations of state (8 for neutron stars and 7 for quark stars), with f-mode frequency and damping time determined as functions of mass and radius for each equation of state.

## Key numbers

- All 7 quark star equations of state produced f-mode frequency distribution widths (sigma_nu) below 0.019 kHz for Population 1, while the narrowest neutron star equation of state (SHT) produced sigma_nu = 0.029 kHz for the same population
- For the APR neutron star equation of state with Population 3, 0.11% of glitches were in range (SNR >= 1) for aLIGO, 0.21% for A+, 1.7% for Einstein Telescope, and 4.6% for Cosmic Explorer
- For the SHT neutron star equation of state with Population 3, 0.38% of glitches were in range for aLIGO, 1.5% for A+, 11% for Einstein Telescope, and 23% for Cosmic Explorer
- For the CMOT1 quark star equation of state with CFL fit and Population 3, 0.28% of glitches were in range for aLIGO, 0.89% for A+, 7.9% for Einstein Telescope, and 20% for Cosmic Explorer
- Based on an estimated 1.2 x 10^6 active radio pulsars in the Milky Way and an average glitch period of 10 years, the authors infer up to 40 detectable glitches per year by aLIGO, 300 per year by A+, and detections every 6 hours for Einstein Telescope and every 4 hours for Cosmic Explorer

## Context

This work extends Ho (2020), which considered only one neutron star equation of state (BSk24) and only the known population of pulsar glitches, by incorporating 15 equations of state and a synthesized population of 10,000 pulsars to correct for observational biases. The study also adds quark star equations of state to test the strange matter hypothesis proposed by Weber (2005). The results enable future gravitational wave observatories to potentially distinguish between neutron stars and quark stars through f-mode frequency distributions, and could reveal pulsars not observable electromagnetically.
