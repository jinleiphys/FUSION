# In-medium polarization tensor in strong magnetic fields (II): Axial Ward identity at finite temperature and density

> We investigate the axial Ward identity (AWI) for massive fermions in strong magnetic fields. The divergence of the axial-vector current is computed at finite temperature and/or density with the help of a relation between the polarization and anomaly diagrams in the effective (1+1) dimensions realized in the lowest Landau level (LLL). We discuss delicate interplay between the vacuum and medium contributions that determines patterns of the spectral flow in the adiabatic limit and, more generally, the diabatic chirality production rate. We also establish an explicit relation between the AWIs from the LLL approximation and from the familiar triangle diagrams in the naive perturbative series with respect to the coupling constant.

# In-medium polarization tensor in strong magnetic fields (II): Axial Ward identity at finite temperature and density

## Key claim

The axial Ward identity (AWI) for massive fermions in strong magnetic fields is not simply governed by the chiral anomaly term; instead, the mass-dependent part of the divergence of the axial-vector current receives finite temperature and density corrections that can be comparable to the anomalous term, leading to effective conservation of the axial current in some parameter regimes. The authors compute the full AWI including both vacuum and medium contributions, showing that the mass-dependent part arises from the pseudoscalar condensate and can dominate over the anomalous term in the adiabatic limit at high temperature or density.

## Method

The authors compute the divergence of the axial-vector current by relating the vector-axial-vector (VA) correlator to the vector-vector (VV) polarization tensor via an identity that holds in the lowest Landau level (LLL) approximation, exploiting the effective (1+1)-dimensional reduction and chirality-momentum locking in the LLL spin eigenstates. They evaluate the polarization tensor at one-loop order in the LLL approximation, separating vacuum contributions (expressed in terms of the function I(q_parallel^2/(4m^2))) and medium contributions (involving integrals over the longitudinal momentum p_z with Fermi-Dirac distribution functions at temperature T and chemical potential mu). The resulting AWI is expressed as a product of the anomaly coefficient times a dimensionless function w(omega, q_z) that quantifies deviations from the massless anomaly result.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum polarization function in the LLL approximation takes the form Pi^{munu} = m_B^2 exp(-|bq_perp|^2/(2|q_f B|)) * tilde_Pi_para * P_para^{munu}, where m_B^2 = |q_f B|/(2pi) * q_f^2/pi.
- The vacuum contribution to the polarization function is tilde_Pi_para^vac(q_para^2) = 1 - I(q_parallel^2/(4m^2)), with limiting behaviors I(0) = 1 and I(infinity) = 0.
- In the massless limit (q_para^2/m^2 -> infinity), the vacuum polarization function reduces to tilde_Pi_para^vac = 1, reproducing the chiral anomaly result.
- The medium contribution tilde_Pi_para^temp(omega, q_z) is proportional to the fermion mass m^2 and vanishes in the strictly massless case.
- The AWI in the massless case gives F.T. partial_mu j_A^mu = (q_f^2/(2pi^2)) * tilde_E(q) dot B * exp(-|bq_perp|^2/(2|q_f B|)).
- The threshold condition for fermion-antifermion pair creation in the LLL is q_para^2 >= (2m)^2.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous computation of the one-loop polarization tensor in strong magnetic fields (Paper I, Hattori and Itakura 2022) to the axial Ward identity, building on the relation between vector and axial-vector currents in the LLL approximation that is analogous to (1+1)-dimensional QED (Schwinger 1962). The results connect to the chiral magnetic effect (Kharzeev 2007, Fukushima 2008) and chiral plasma instability (Akamatsu 2013), with applications to relativistic heavy-ion collisions, neutron-star physics, and cosmology. The authors establish that the LLL approximation reproduces the triangle diagram results (Adler 1969, Bell 1969) up to a Gaussian factor that reduces to unity in the homogeneous electric-field limit or strong magnetic field limit, and they show that the anomalous term receives no temperature or density corrections (Itoyama 1982) while the mass-dependent part does.
