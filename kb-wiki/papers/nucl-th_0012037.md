# Negative heat capacities and first order phase transitions in nuclei and other mesoscopic systems

> The origin of predicted and observed anomalies in caloric curves of nuclei and other mesoscopic systems is investigated. It is shown that a straightforward thermodynamical treatment of an evaporating liquid drop leads to a backbending in the caloric curve and to negative specific heats in the two phase coexistence region. The cause is found not in the generation of additional surface, but in the progressive reduction of the drop's radius, and surface, with evaporation.

# Negative heat capacities and first order phase transitions in nuclei and other mesoscopic systems

## Key claim
The backbending caloric curve and negative heat capacities observed in mesoscopic systems arise not from the generation of additional surface area or from microcanonical statistics, but from the progressive reduction of the drop's radius and surface during evaporation, which is an accidental evolution in parameter space rather than a fundamental thermodynamic anomaly.

## Method
The authors treat an evaporating liquid drop using standard thermodynamics, modifying the Clapeyron equation to include a surface energy term proportional to the drop's radius. They construct a caloric curve at constant external pressure by integrating the enthalpy change as the drop evaporates, using the scaled variables p', T', and r' defined by the ratio of bulk enthalpy to surface energy. The resulting temperature and enthalpy are expressed parametrically as functions of the decreasing radius.

## Key numbers
- The scaled radius range shown, 1 ≤ r₀' ≤ 5.35, corresponds to a gold nucleus (A = 197) evaporating to a single nucleon.
- The liquid drop model achieves one percent accuracy in systems as small as 40 nucleons and possibly smaller.
- The surface energy correction to the molar enthalpy is given by ΔH_m = ΔH_m⁰ - 3c_s V_m^l / r, where c_s is the surface energy coefficient.

## Context
This work extends the liquid drop model of nuclear physics to explain anomalies previously attributed to microcanonical statistics or bubble formation in lattice gas models (Gulminelli, Chomaz, 1999) and multifragmentation models (Gross, 1997). The authors show that the same negative heat capacities observed experimentally in sodium clusters (Schmidt, 2001) and inferred from nuclear multifragmentation fluctuations (D'Agostino, 2000) can be reproduced by standard thermodynamics with a surface correction. This enables a unified thermodynamic treatment of phase transitions in mesoscopic systems without invoking new physics, and provides a benchmark for lattice gas and other numerical models to reproduce.
