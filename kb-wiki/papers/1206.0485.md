# Electromagnetic radiation by quark-gluon plasma in magnetic field

> The electromagnetic radiation by quark-gluon plasma in strong magnetic field is calculated. The contributing processes are synchrotron radiation and one--photon annihilation. It is shown that in relativistic heavy--ion collisions at RHIC and LHC synchrotron radiation dominates over the annihilation. Moreover, it constitutes a significant part of all photons produced by the plasma at low transverse momenta; its magnitude depends on the plasma temperature and the magnetic field strength. Electromagnetic radiation in magnetic field is probably the missing piece that resolves a discrepancy between the theoretical models and the experimental data. It is argued that electromagnetic radiation increases with the magnetic field strength and plasma temperature.

## Key claim

Synchrotron radiation from quarks and antiquarks in the strong magnetic field produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions likely constitutes a significant part of all photons emitted by the quark-gluon plasma at low transverse momenta, and may resolve the discrepancy between theoretical models without magnetic fields and experimental data from RHIC; the magnitude of this contribution depends on the plasma temperature and magnetic field strength.

## Method

The photon spectrum and angular distribution from quark-gluon plasma in a strong, spatially homogeneous external magnetic field were calculated in the ideal gas approximation, treating the fermion motion as quantized into Landau levels. Two processes were computed: synchrotron radiation (photon emission by a charged fermion transitioning between Landau levels) and one-photon pair annihilation (quark-antiquark annihilation into a single photon), using the conservation laws for energy and momentum along the magnetic field direction. The differential photon multiplicity was obtained by summing over initial and final Landau levels, quark flavors (u, d, s), and integrating over the initial quark momentum distribution at a given temperature, then compared to experimental data by dividing the measured yield by the estimated plasma volume and magnetic field relaxation time.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field shortly after collision reaches \(10^{18}-10^{19}\) G, exceeding the critical Schwinger field \(B_c = 4.41 \times 10^{13}\) G.
- At RHIC, about 3% of the plasma energy density resides in the magnetic field; at LHC, this fraction reaches as much as 40%.
- Synchrotron radiation contribution is about two orders of magnitude larger than that of pair annihilation in the relevant kinematic region.
- For \(eB = m_\pi^2\), \(T = 200\) MeV, \(y = 0\), \(\phi = \pi/3\), the cutoff frequency for the \(1 \to 0\) Landau level transition is \(\omega_{s,10} = \sqrt{2 e_f B} / \sin\theta\).
- At high photon energies, the synchrotron spectrum scales as \(\sqrt{B}\) and as \(\sqrt{T} e^{-\omega/T}\).
- For \(eB = m_\pi^2\), \(T = 200\) MeV, \(y = 0\), \(\phi = \pi/3\), the maximum Landau level \(j_{\text{max}}\) needed for 5% accuracy ranges from 30 (at \(k_\perp = 0.1\) GeV) to 150 (at \(k_\perp = 3\) GeV).
- For \(eB = 15 m_\pi^2\), \(T = 400\) MeV, \(y = 0\), \(\phi = \pi/3\), \(j_{\text{max}}\) ranges from 8 (at \(k_\perp = 1\) GeV) to 16 (at \(k_\perp = 1\) GeV for s quarks).

## Context

This work extends calculations of synchrotron radiation in strong magnetic fields, previously applied to gamma-ray bursts (Herold 1982, Harding 1987, Latal 1986, Baring 1988), to the quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. It builds on predictions of strong magnetic fields in such collisions (Kharzeev 2007, Skokov 2009, Voronyuk 2011, Deng 2012, Bzdak 2011) and on estimates of magnetic field relaxation time in the plasma (Tuchin 2010). The results suggest that synchrotron radiation can explain the photon excess observed by PHENIX (Adare 2008) and enable future studies of azimuthal anisotropy and elliptic flow coefficients in photon spectra, as well as improved treatment of time-dependent magnetic fields using quasi-classical approximations or computational techniques (Zhao 2011).
