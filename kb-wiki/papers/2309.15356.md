# On partition temperature of massless particles in high-energy collisions

> Although partition temperature derived using the Darwin-Fowler method is exact for simple scenarios, the derivation for complex systems might reside on specific approximations whose viability is not ensured if the thermodynamic limit is not attained. This work elaborates on a related problem relevant to relativistic high-energy collisions. On the one hand, it is simple enough that closed form expression can be obtained precisely for the one-particle distribution function. On the other hand, the resulting expression is not an exponential form, and therefore it is not straightforward that the notion of partition function could be implied. Specifically, we derive the one-particle distribution function for massless particles where the phase space integration is performed exactly for the underlying canonical ensemble consisting of a given number of particles. We discuss the viability of the partition temperature in this case. Possible implications of the obtained results regarding the observed Tsallis distribution in transverse momentum spectra in high-energy collisions are also addressed.

## Key claim

The authors derive an exact closed-form one-particle distribution function for massless particles in a canonical ensemble with fixed particle number, showing that even though the resulting expression is not an exponential form, the notion of partition temperature remains viable for low and intermediate momenta, but breaks down in the high-momentum region due to the bounded total energy.

## Method

The authors start from a microcanonical ensemble of N massless particles with conserved total energy and momentum, using the Darwin-Fowler method with Laplace and Fourier transforms to enforce conservation laws. They choose a specific probability density function f(p) = (β/2)exp(-β|p|) for longitudinal emission, which allows exact contour integration using the residue theorem without saddle-point approximation. The resulting one-particle distribution is a polynomial sum, which they then fit numerically to an exponential form A exp(-p/T_p) to extract the partition temperature for different multiplicities n = 5, 10, 50, and 100.

## Key numbers

* For n = 5, the extracted inverse partition temperature 1/T_p = 0.0523 with standard error 0.0006 and p-value 1.2 × 10^{-51}
* For n = 10, 1/T_p = 0.0794 with standard error 0.0005 and p-value 9.5 × 10^{-67}
* For n = 50, 1/T_p = 0.0962 with standard error 0.0001 and p-value 3.2 × 10^{-102}
* For n = 100, 1/T_p = 0.0981 with standard error 0.00005 and p-value 6.5 × 10^{-117}
* The partition temperature converges to T_p → p̅ in the limit n → ∞, where p̅ = 10 in natural units
* Numerical sampling used N_sample = 50 points evenly distributed on the interval p ∈ (0, 2p̅)

## Context

This work extends the Darwin-Fowler approach to partition temperature, which was previously applied to p̅p collisions at 540 GeV by the same group (jet-ph-05, 2005) using saddle-point approximations that showed discrepancies for small multiplicities. The exact analytic result for massless particles demonstrates that partition temperature remains feasible even when the thermodynamic limit is not attained, supporting the statistical interpretation of Tsallis distributions observed in transverse momentum spectra by ATLAS, ALICE, and CMS collaborations. The findings enable further quantitative comparisons between the statistical phase-space approach and the observed Tsallis-type distributions in high-energy collisions, particularly regarding the role of multiplicity fluctuations as discussed by Wilk and Wlodarczyk (jet-ph-10) and others (jet-ph-12, jet-ph-13).
