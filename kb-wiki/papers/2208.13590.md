# Bayesian inference of real-time dynamics from lattice QCD

> The computation of dynamical properties of nuclear matter, ranging from parton distribution functions of nucleons and nuclei to transport properties in the quark-gluon plasma, constitutes a central goal of modern theoretical physics. This real-time physics often defies a perturbative treatment and the most successful strategy so far is to deploy lattice QCD simulations. These numerical computations are based on Monte-Carlo sampling and formulated in an artificial Euclidean time. Real-time physics is most conveniently formulated in terms of spectral functions, which are hidden in lattice QCD behind an ill-posed inverse problem. I will discuss the current methods state-of-the art in the extraction of spectral functions from lattice QCD simulations, based on Bayesian inference and emphasize the importance of prior domain knowledge, vital to regularizing the otherwise ill-posed extraction task. With Bayesian inference allowing us to make explicit the uncertainty in both observations and in our prior knowledge, a systematic estimation of the total uncertainties in the extracted spectral functions is nowadays possible. Two implementations of the Bayesian Reconstruction (BR) method for spectral function extraction, one for MAP point estimates and one based on an open access Monte-Carlo sampler are provided.I will briefly touch on the use of machine learning for spectral function reconstruction and discuss some new insight it has brought to the Bayesian community.

## Key claim

Bayesian inference provides a systematic framework for extracting real-time spectral functions from Euclidean lattice QCD correlation functions, but the reconstruction remains an ill-posed inverse problem that requires explicit prior domain knowledge to regularize the extraction, and the total uncertainty can only be estimated when both observational and prior uncertainties are made explicit.

## Method

The author reviews the Bayesian Reconstruction (BR) method for spectral function extraction from lattice QCD, where Euclidean correlation functions obtained from Monte-Carlo simulations serve as input data. The method applies Bayes theorem to compute a posterior probability for the spectral function given the data and prior knowledge, using a likelihood constructed from the Gaussian-distributed correlator data and a prior probability that incorporates domain knowledge through regulator functionals such as Tikhonov regularization, total variation, or arc-length penalization. Two implementations of the BR method are provided: one for maximum-a-posteriori (MAP) point estimates and one based on an open-access Monte-Carlo sampler.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD simulations provide only around O(10–100) points along imaginary time tau, from which the spectral function must be reconstructed.
- The number of frequency bins N_mu is much larger than the number of Euclidean time points N_tau, making the inverse problem highly degenerate.
- The covariance matrix C_ij of the mean correlator data requires the number of configurations N_conf to be significantly larger than the number of data points along imaginary time to avoid exact zero eigenvalues.
- The error on the Euclidean correlator from Monte-Carlo sampling decreases as O(1/sqrt(N_conf)).

## Context

This work reviews and extends the Bayesian approach to spectral function reconstruction pioneered by Nakahara (1999) and Asakawa (2000), which was inspired by prior work in condensed matter physics by Jarrell (1996) and image reconstruction by Skilling (1991). The review covers applications to zero-temperature hadron spectra, parton distribution functions, in-medium hadrons, transport coefficients, and the complex in-medium heavy quark potential. The paper enables systematic uncertainty quantification in spectral reconstructions and discusses how machine learning approaches have provided new insights to the Bayesian community, while noting that competing non-Bayesian methods such as the Backus-Gilbert method and Pade reconstruction do not guarantee that the reconstructed spectral function reproduces the input data.
