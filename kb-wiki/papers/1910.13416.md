# Spectroscopy of short-lived radioactive molecules: A sensitive laboratory for new physics

> The study of molecular systems provides exceptional opportunities for the exploration of the fundamental laws of nature and for the search for physics beyond the Standard Model of particle physics. Measurements of molecules composed of naturally occurring nuclei have provided the most stringent upper bounds to the electron electric dipole moment to date, and offer a route to investigate the violation of fundamental symmetries with unprecedented sensitivity. Radioactive molecules - where one or more of their atoms possesses a radioactive nucleus - can contain heavy and deformed nuclei, offering superior sensitivity for EDM measurements as well as for other symmetry-violating effects. Radium monofluoride, RaF, is of particular interest as it is predicted to have an appropriate electronic structure for direct laser cooling. Furthermore, some Ra isotopes are known to be octupole deformed, thereby resulting in a large enhancement of their symmetry-violating nuclear moments. Until now,however, no experimental measurements of RaF have been performed, and their study is impeded by major experimental challenges, as no stable isotopes of radium exist. Here, we present a novel experimental approach to study short-lived radioactive molecules using the highly sensitive collinear resonance ionisation method. With this technique we have measured, for the first time, the energetically low-lying electronic states for each of the isotopically pure RaF molecules at the ISOLDE-CERN. Our results provide strong evidence of the existence of a suitable laser-cooling scheme for these molecules and constitute a pivotal step towards high-precision studies in these systems. Our findings open up new opportunities in the synthesis, manipulation and study of short-lived radioactive molecules, which will have a direct impact in many-body physics, astrophysics, nuclear structure, and fundamental physics research.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first experimental spectroscopic measurements of radium monofluoride (RaF) molecules, providing strong evidence that RaF possesses a suitable electronic structure for direct laser cooling, but the assignment of some vibronic transitions (particularly the $B\,^{2}\Delta_{3/2}$ state and the $\Delta v=-1$ band system) remains tentative due to congested spectral structure.

## Method

Radioactive RaF$^+$ molecular ions were produced at ISOLDE-CERN by reacting radium isotopes with CF$_4$ gas in a heated uranium carbide target, then mass-selected, cooled in a helium-filled radio-frequency quadrupole trap, and neutralized in-flight via charge exchange with sodium vapour. The neutral RaF molecules were overlapped with tunable pulsed laser beams in a collinear geometry within the Collinear Resonance Ionisation Spectroscopy (CRIS) setup, where resonant excitation followed by 355-nm ionization produced RaF$^+$ ions detected on a particle detector as a function of laser wavenumber.

## Key numbers

- $A\,^2\Pi_{1/2} - X\,^2\Sigma^+$ (0,0) band center measured at 13287.8(1) cm$^{-1}$, in agreement with the ab initio prediction of 13300(1200) cm$^{-1}$
- Upper limit on the lifetime of the $^2\Pi_{1/2}$ ($v'=0$) excited state: $T_{1/2} \leq 50$ ns, corresponding to a spontaneous decay rate $> 2\times10^{7}$ s$^{-1}$
- Upper limit of 0.03 for the peak intensity ratio $I(0,1)/I(0,0)$, indicating highly diagonal Franck-Condon factors
- Harmonic frequency for the $X\,^2\Sigma^+$ ground state: $\tilde{\omega}_e = 441.8(1)$ cm$^{-1}$; for the $A\,^2\Pi_{1/2}$ excited state: $\tilde{\omega}_e = 435.5(1)$ cm$^{-1}$
- Dissociation energy for the $X\,^2\Sigma^+$ ground state: $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}_e = 2.92(5)\times10^{4}$ cm$^{-1}$; for the $A\,^2\Pi_{1/2}$ state: $\tilde{\mathcal{D}}_e = 2.90(3)\times10^{4}$ cm$^{-1}$
- Vibronic spectra measured for five isotopologues: $^{223}$RaF, $^{224}$RaF ($T_{1/2}=3.6$ d), $^{225}$RaF, $^{226}$RaF, and $^{228}$RaF
- Molecules produced at rates below $10^{6}$ molecules/s; scanning covered a 1000 cm$^{-1}$ range in 5 hours at 0.06 cm$^{-1}$/s

## Context

This work extends theoretical predictions by Isaev et al. (2010, 2013) that RaF would be suitable for laser cooling, and provides the first experimental confirmation of those predictions. The collinear resonance ionisation method, previously developed for atomic spectroscopy at ISOLDE-CERN, was adapted here for short-lived radioactive molecules, enabling studies of species with lifetimes as short as a few milliseconds. The results enable future high-precision measurements of parity-violating and time-reversal-violating effects in RaF, particularly using octupole-deformed radium isotopes studied by Gaffney et al. (2013), and open the door to spectroscopic studies of other radioactive molecules of astrophysical interest such as $^{26}$AlF.
