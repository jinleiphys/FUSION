# Chiral Lagrangians and Quark Condensate in Nuclei

> We study the evolution with density of the quark condensate in the nuclear medium with interacting nucleons and including the short range correlations. We work with two chiral models, the linear sigma model and the non-linear one. For the last one we use two versions, one which does not satisfy PCAC, and another one which does. We show that the quark condensate, as other observables, is independent on the variant selected. The application to physical pions excludes the linear sigma model as a credible one. In the non-linear models our conclusions are: first there is no systematic reaction imposed by chiral symmetry against symmetry restoration, second, if one keeps only the s-wave pion-nucleon interaction, the quark condensate evolves essentially linearly with density, as if the nucleons were non interacting. The main correction arises from the p-wave pion-nucleon interaction. Last, in the s-wave optical potential, chiral symmetry tolerates but does not impose two body terms. On the other hand the effect of correlations linked to the isospin symmetric amplitude is negligible.

## Key claim

The quark condensate in nuclear matter evolves essentially linearly with density when only the s-wave pion-nucleon interaction is considered, and chiral symmetry does not impose a systematic reaction against symmetry restoration; the main correction arises from the p-wave pion-nucleon interaction, while the effect of correlations linked to the isospin symmetric amplitude is negligible.

## Method

The authors studied the density evolution of the quark condensate using two chiral models: the linear sigma model and the non-linear sigma model (with two versions, one satisfying PCAC and one not). They evaluated the nuclear sigma commutator up to second order in density using tree-level diagrams in the Hartree approximation, incorporating short-range correlations via a schematic correlation function with a cut-off parameter \(q_c = m_\omega\). The non-linear model calculations were performed using a Weinberg-type Lagrangian and a PCAC-satisfying representation, with the condensate obtained from the expectation value of the symmetry-breaking operator.

## Key numbers

- At normal nuclear matter density \(\rho_0 = 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\), the linear sigma model predicts a quark condensate value of 0.54 relative to vacuum, compared to 0.65 in the linear approximation (i.e., without \(\sigma\) exchange terms).
- The nucleon sigma commutator is taken as \(\Sigma_N = 45\) MeV, fixing the sigma mass at \(m_\sigma = 740\) MeV in the linear sigma model.
- The s-wave \(\pi\)N scattering length from the linear sigma model is \(a^+ = -0.059\; m_\pi^{-1}\), while the experimental value is \(a^+ = -0.010\; m_\pi^{-1}\).
- The r.m.s. radius of the condensate in the linear sigma model is \(r_s = \sqrt{6}/m_\sigma \approx 0.65\) fm, compared to the experimental value of \(r_s = 1.26\) fm.
- The \(\sigma\) exchange contribution to the s-wave optical potential at \(\rho \approx 0.7\rho_0\) is \(\Delta_{\sigma\, {\rm exch}} = -0.023\; m_\pi^{-1}\).
- The sum of known contributions to the optical potential parameter \((b_0)_{\rm eff}\) at \(\rho \approx 0.7\rho_0\) is \(-0.029\; m_\pi^{-1}\), matching the experimental value.
- In the non-linear model, the two-pion exchange correction from the s-wave contact term gives a relative correction to \(\Sigma_N\) of \(+3.6 \times 10^{-3}\) (from \(\Delta\Sigma_1^{2\pi}\)) and \(-1.4 \times 10^{-2}\) (from \(\Delta\Sigma_2^{2\pi}\)), both very small.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the quark condensate in nuclear matter by Chanfray and Ericson (1994) and Birse and McGovern (1992), who examined the role of \(\sigma\) meson exchange and distortion effects in the linear sigma model. The authors show that the linear sigma model is unrealistic for quantitative discussion due to its failure to reproduce the experimental s-wave \(\pi\)N scattering length and the scalar radius of the nucleon. The non-linear sigma model, following the effective Lagrangian approach popularized by Weinberg (1990) and Lynn (1992), provides a more credible framework where the condensate evolves nearly linearly with density and the effective pion mass is independent of the representation. The results enable future work on the in-medium pion mass and the s-wave optical potential, with the need to incorporate additional chirally invariant terms to reproduce the threshold cancellation of the non-Born \(\pi\)N amplitude.
