# Stability of the fragments and thermalization at peak center-of-mass energy

> We simulate the central reactions of nearly symmetric, and asymmetric systems, for the energies at which the maximum production of IMFs occurs (E$_{c.m.}^{peak}$).This study is carried out by using hard EOS along with cugnon cross section and employing MSTB method for clusterization. We study the various properties of fragments. The stability of fragments is checked through persistence coefficient and gain term. The information about the thermalization and stopping in heavy-ion collisions is obtained via relative momentum, anisotropy ratio, and rapidity distribution. We find that for a complete stopping of incoming nuclei very heavy systems are required. The mass dependence of various quantities (such as average and maximum central density, collision dynamics as well as the time zone for hot and dense nuclear matter) is also presented. In all cases (i.e., average and maximum central density, collision dynamics as well as the time zone for hot and dense nuclear matter) a power law dependence is obtained.

## Key claim
The paper claims that at the peak center-of-mass energy for intermediate mass fragment production, the degree of stopping and thermalization increases with system mass, with very heavy systems required for complete stopping; however, this conclusion is qualified by the use of a hard equation of state and energy-dependent Cugnon cross section within the quantum molecular dynamics model, and the results are specific to central collisions at b = 0.0 fm.

## Method
Central collisions (b = 0.0 fm) of seven symmetric and asymmetric systems from $^{20}$Ne+$^{20}$Ne to $^{197}$Au+$^{197}$Au were simulated using the quantum molecular dynamics model with a hard equation of state and energy-dependent Cugnon nucleon-nucleon cross section. The phase space was clusterized using the minimum spanning tree method with binding energy check (MSTB) with a binding energy threshold of -4.0 MeV for fragments with three or more nucleons. Fragment stability was quantified via the persistence coefficient and gain term, while thermalization was assessed through relative momentum, anisotropy ratio, and rapidity distribution.

## Key numbers
- Peak incident energies for maximal IMF production: approximately 24, 46, 69, 77, 96, 124, and 104 AMeV for $^{20}$Ne+$^{20}$Ne, $^{40}$Ar+$^{45}$Sc, $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni, $^{86}$Kr+$^{93}$Nb, $^{129}$Xe+$^{118}$Sn, $^{86}$Kr+$^{197}$Au, and $^{197}$Au+$^{197}$Au, respectively.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for maximal average density $\langle \rho^{avg} \rangle$ versus system mass: 0.08 $\pm$ 0.02.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for maximal maximum density $\langle \rho^{max} \rangle$ versus system mass: 0.034 $\pm$ 0.008.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for time of maximal collision rate versus system mass: -0.017 $\pm$ 0.038.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for time of maximal average density versus system mass: 0.015 $\pm$ 0.07.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for time interval with $\rho_{avg} \geq \rho_0$: 0.15 $\pm$ 0.05.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for time interval with $\rho_{avg} \geq \rho_0/2$: -0.04 $\pm$ 0.06.
- Power law exponent $\tau$ for total nucleon-nucleon collisions at 200 fm/c versus system mass: 1.28 $\pm$ 0.054.
- Persistence coefficient reaches an asymptotic value of 0.8 after approximately 130 fm/c.

## Context
This work extends the experimental study of Sisan et al. (2001) on IMF multiplicity at peak energy, which reported a linear increase of $E_{c.m.}^{max}$ with system mass and a power-law dependence for peak multiplicity with exponent 0.7. It also builds on the thermalization studies of Dhawan et al. (2006) and Sood and Puri (2006), who examined stopping in heavy-ion collisions using light charged particles and participant-spectator matter, respectively. The results enable systematic predictions of stopping power, thermalization timescales, and density evolution across a wide mass range at the energy where IMF production is maximal, providing benchmarks for future experimental tests with 4$\pi$ detector arrays.
