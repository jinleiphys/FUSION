# Measurement and microscopic description of odd-even staggering of charge radii of exotic copper isotopes

> The mesoscopic nature of the atomic nucleus gives rise to a wide array of macroscopic and microscopic phenomena. The size of the nucleus is a window into this duality: while the charge radii globally scale as $A^{1/3}$, their evolution across isotopic chains reveals unanticipated structural phenomena [1-3]. The most ubiquitous of these is perhaps the Odd-Even Staggering (OES) [4]: isotopes with an odd number of neutrons are usually smaller in size than the trend of their even-neutron neighbours suggests. This OES effect varies with the number of protons and neutrons and poses a significant challenge for nuclear theory [5-7]. Here, we examine this problem with new measurements of the charge radii of short-lived copper isotopes up to the very exotic $^{78}$Cu $(Z=29, N=49)$, produced at only 20 ions/s, using the highly-sensitive Collinear Resonance Ionisation Spectroscopy (CRIS) method at ISOLDE-CERN. Due to the presence of a single proton outside of the closed Z=28 shell, these measurements provide crucial insights into the single-particle proton structure and how this affects the charge radii. We observe an unexpected reduction in the OES for isotopes approaching the $N=50$ shell gap. To describe the data, we applied models based on nuclear Density Functional Theory [2,8] (DFT) and ab-initio Valence-Space In-Medium Similarity Renormalization Group (VS-IMSRG) theory [9,10]. Through these comparisons, we demonstrate a relation between the global behavior of charge radii and the saturation density of nuclear matter, and show that the local charge radii variations, which reflect the many-body polarization effects due to the odd neutron, naturally emerge from the VS-IMSRG calculations.

## Key claim
The odd-even staggering (OES) of charge radii in copper isotopes is unexpectedly reduced as the neutron number approaches the N=50 shell gap, and this local variation naturally emerges from ab-initio VS-IMSRG calculations using only NN+3N forces constrained by few-body data, while the global behavior of charge radii is linked to the nuclear saturation density.

## Method
Charge radii of copper isotopes from N=29 to N=49 were measured using Collinear Resonance Ionisation Spectroscopy (CRIS) at ISOLDE-CERN, with radioactive ions produced by neutron-induced fission of 238U and purified via laser ionisation, mass separation, and a radio-frequency Paul trap. The hyperfine spectra were recorded by scanning a narrowband pulsed laser across the 249 nm optical transition, and isotope shifts were extracted to determine changes in mean-squared charge radii. Theoretical calculations were performed using Fayans Density Functional Theory (DFT) and ab-initio Valence-Space In-Medium Similarity Renormalization Group (VS-IMSRG) theory with chiral NN+3N interactions.

## Key numbers
- Charge radius of 78Cu (Z=29, N=49) measured at a production rate of only 20 ions/s, with detection rates below 0.05 ions/s on resonance
- Isotope shift for 78Cu: -5623(10) MHz relative to 65Cu, yielding δ⟨r²⟩⁶⁵·⁷⁸ = +0.58(3)[11] fm²
- Reference charge radius for 65Cu: r₆₅ = 3.9022(14) fm
- Laser linewidths below 100 MHz achieved with CRIS, compared to typical fast-beam collinear laser spectroscopy
- Mass-shift factor for the 249 nm line: M₂₄₉ = 2284(24) GHz·u; field-shift factor: F₂₄₉ = 439(80) MHz·fm⁻²
- DFT uncertainty due to uniform blocking approximation: about 5 × 10⁻³ fm

## Context
This work extends previous charge radius measurements of copper isotopes up to 75Cu (Bissell 2016) by three additional neutron numbers, reaching 78Cu near the doubly magic 78Ni system (Taniuchi 2019). The results challenge DFT calculations using the Fayans functional, which had successfully described charge radii in calcium (Reinhard 2017) and tin (Gorges 2019) regions but overestimates OES in heavier odd-Z systems. The VS-IMSRG calculations, previously advanced for masses, spectroscopy, and electroweak transitions below A=100 (Henderson 2018), now demonstrate that OES of both radii and binding energies emerges naturally from chiral interactions constrained only by few-body data, enabling future comparisons with heavier odd-Z systems near 100Sn.
