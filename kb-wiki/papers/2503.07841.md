# Fingerprints of triaxiality in the charge radii of neutron-rich Ruthenium

> We present the first measurements with a new collinear laser spectroscopy setup at the Argonne Tandem Linac Accelerator System utilizing its unique capability to deliver neutron-rich refractory metal isotopes produced by the spontaneous fission of 252Cf. We measured isotope shifts from optical spectra for nine radioactive ruthenium isotopes 106-114Ru, reaching deep into the mid-shell region. The extracted charge radii are in excellent agreement with predictions from the Brussels-Skyrme-on-a-Grid models that account for the triaxial deformation of nuclear ground states in this region. We show that triaxial deformation impacts charge radii in models that feature shell effects, in contrast to what could be concluded from a liquid drop analysis. This indicates that this exotic type of deformation should not be neglected in regions where it is known to occur, even if its presence cannot be unambiguously inferred through laser spectroscopy.

## Key claim

Triaxial deformation impacts charge radii in nuclear models that include quantum mechanical shell effects, contrary to what would be concluded from a liquid drop analysis; however, the effect is model-dependent and charge radii alone cannot unambiguously determine whether a given nucleus exhibits triaxial deformation.

## Method

The authors installed a new collinear laser spectroscopy setup (ATLANTIS) at the Argonne Tandem Linac Accelerator System, using the CARIBU 252Cf fission source to deliver neutron-rich refractory ruthenium isotopes. They measured isotope shifts from optical spectra in the 350-nm ground-state transition of neutral ruthenium for nine radioactive isotopes 106-114Ru, using a magnesium-based charge-exchange cell for neutralization and an RFQ cooler-buncher for improved signal-to-noise ratio. The isotope shifts were converted to differential charge radii via a King plot procedure using muonic atom data from stable isotopes.

## Key numbers

* Isotope shifts δν102,A measured for nine radioactive ruthenium isotopes with total uncertainties between 3 and 9 MHz (e.g., 106Ru: -666.7(3.5) MHz; 114Ru: -1282.2(6.0) MHz)
* Absolute charge radii extracted for 106-114Ru, ranging from Rc = 4.5353(24) fm for 106Ru to Rc = 4.6011(61) fm for 114Ru
* Reference charge radius for stable 102Ru: ⟨rc2⟩1/2 = 4.481(2) fm from muonic atom spectroscopy
* Field shift parameter F = -2.13(46) GHz fm-2 and mass shift parameter K∞ = 954(564) u GHz from King plot analysis
* Production rate for the least abundant isotope 114Ru: only a few tens of ions per second
* Charge-exchange efficiency with magnesium: approximately 90% at about 400°C
* Buncher accumulation times up to 30 s with time spread of approximately 1 μs at 23,750 V

## Context

This work extends laser spectroscopy measurements into the neutron-rich refractory metal region previously inaccessible to ISOL-type facilities, specifically the ruthenium isotopic chain where triaxial deformation is established experimentally (Garrett 2022, Esmaylzadeh 2022) and supported by models (Moller 2006, Scamps 2021). The results are compared to the Brussels-Skyrme-on-a-Grid (BSkG) model series, which systematically allows for triaxial deformation; earlier studies with these models already demonstrated the relevance of triaxiality for binding energies and isomeric states in this region (Hukkanen 2023, Stryjczyk 2024). The work enables a consistent description of masses, spectroscopy, and charge radii within a triaxial framework, challenging the long-held assumption from liquid drop arguments (Hilberath 1992) that charge radii are insensitive to triaxial shape.
