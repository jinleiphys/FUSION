# A possible explanation why the Theta+ is seen in some experiments and not in others

> To understand the whole set of positive and null data on the Theta+(1530)-production, we suggest the hypothesis that multiquark hadrons are mainly generated from many-quark states, which emerge either as short-term hadron fluctuations, or as hadron remnants in hard processes. This approach allows us to describe both non-observation of the Theta+ in current null experiments and peculiar features of its production in positive experiments. Further, we are able to propose new experiments that might be decisive for the problem of the Theta+ existence. Distributions of the Theta+ in such experiments can give important information both on higher Fock components of conventional hadrons and about structure and hadronization properties of hadron remnants produced in hard processes. We also explain that description of multiquark hadrons may require a modified form of the constituent quark model, with quark masses and couplings being intermediate between their values for the familiar constituent quarks and the current ones.

## Key claim

The authors hypothesize that multiquark hadrons like the Θ⁺(1530) are primarily produced from many-quark states (short-term hadron fluctuations or hadron remnants in hard processes), rather than from few-quark configurations; this hypothesis is presented as a qualitative explanation, not a quantitative prediction, for why the Θ⁺ appears in some experiments but not others.

## Method

The paper reviews existing positive and null experimental data on Θ⁺(1530) production and interprets them through the lens of QCD's Fock column picture, where hadrons contain configurations with varying numbers of quark-antiquark pairs. The authors argue that exotic hadrons are preferentially generated from higher Fock components (many-quark states) of conventional hadrons or from hadron remnants in hard processes, and they apply this framework to analyze specific experimental results from ZEUS, SVD, HERMES, Belle, CLAS, COSY-TOF, and other collaborations.

## Key numbers

- Γ_Θ⁺ = (0.36 ± 0.11) MeV: experimental width of the Θ⁺(1530) from DIANA data (Bar 2004), cited as the most precise measured value
- Γ_Θ⁺ ≤ 15 MeV: upper bound from the Chiral Quark Soliton Approach (χQSA) (Diakonov 1997)
- Γ_Θ⁺ < 0.64 MeV: upper limit from Belle data (2006) on K⁺n → pK_S charge exchange
- Γ_Θ⁺ = (0.9 ± 0.3) MeV: width extracted by Cahn and Trilling (2004) from earlier DIANA analysis
- Θ⁺(1530)/Λ(1116) ratio ≈ 4%: relative yield in ZEUS data, approximately constant with Q² within uncertainties
- No Θ⁺ observed at |x_F| > 0.3 or p_T > 1.5 GeV in SVD data (preliminary)
- Q² > 1 GeV² with W < 125 GeV (x > 6.4×10⁻⁵) required for Θ⁺ observation in ZEUS data
- Theoretical χQSA calculation with relativistic corrections and mass difference gave Γ_Θ⁺ ≈ 0.43 MeV (Choi 2006), comparable to the experimental value

## Context

This work extends the discussion of Karliner and Lipkin (2005), who suggested the Θ⁺ is produced mainly through a multiquark resonance N(2400), by generalizing to any many-quark state rather than a specific resonance. The paper contradicts the view that all positive Θ⁺ results are statistical fluctuations (Hicks 2005) and provides a framework to reconcile positive data from ZEUS, SVD, and DIANA with null results from CLAS, Belle, and COSY-TOF. The hypothesis enables new experiments that could test whether Θ⁺ production scales with parton multiplicity in hard processes, and suggests that studying pentaquarks can reveal properties of higher Fock components in conventional baryons.
