# Study of the $^{25}$Mg(d,p)$^{26}$Mg reaction to constrain the $^{25}$Al(p,$╬│$)$^{26}$Si resonant reaction rates in nova burning conditions

> The rate of the $^{25}$Al($p$,$╬│$)$^{26}$Si reaction is one of the few key remaining nuclear uncertainties required for predicting the production of the cosmic $╬│$-ray emitter $^{26}$Al in explosive burning in novae. This reaction rate is dominated by three key resonances ($J^ŽĆ=0^{+}$, $1^{+}$ and $3^{+}$) in $^{26}$Si. Only the $3^{+}$ resonance strength has been directly constrained by experiment. A high resolution measurement of the $^{25}$Mg($d$,$p$) reaction was used to determine spectroscopic factors for analog states in the mirror nucleus, $^{26}$Mg. A first spectroscopic factor value is reported for the $0^{+}$ state at 6.256 MeV, and a strict upper limit is set on the value for the $1^{+}$ state at 5.691 MeV, that is incompatible with an earlier ($^{4}$He,$^{3}$He) study. These results are used to estimate proton partial widths, and resonance strengths of analog states in $^{26}$Si contributing to the $^{25}$Al($p$,$╬│$)$^{26}$Si reaction rate in nova burning conditions.

## Key claim

The paper uses a high-resolution measurement of the $^{25}$Mg(d,p)$^{26}$Mg reaction to indirectly constrain the $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$)$^{26}$Si reaction rate in nova burning conditions, but the results are limited by the assumption of isospin symmetry between mirror states in $^{26}$Mg and $^{26}$Si, and the analysis explicitly treats only the direct transfer mechanism while higher-order and multistep processes are not included.

## Method

A deuteron beam at 8 MeV from the TUNL FN tandem Van de Graaff accelerator was used to bombard $^{25}$Mg targets (99.2% enrichment, thicknesses 90 and 112 $\mu$g/cm$^2$). Reaction protons were momentum-analyzed using the Enge split-pole magnetic spectrograph at angles between 13 and 55 degrees, with particle identification via a $\Delta E/E$ detector combination. Spectroscopic factors were extracted by comparing measured differential cross sections to distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) calculations using the FRESCO code, with compound nuclear contributions estimated using the Hauser-Feshbach approach in TALYS for weakly populated states.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factor for the $0^{+}$ state at 6.256 MeV in $^{26}$Mg: $C^2S(\ell=2) = 0.042(10)$, a first reported value for the (d,p) reaction
- Upper limit on spectroscopic factor for the $1^{+}$ state at 5.691 MeV in $^{26}$Mg: $C^2S(\ell=2) < 5.7 \times 10^{-3}$ at 1$\sigma$ confidence, incompatible with the earlier value of 0.20(4) from Yasue et al. (1990)
- Spectroscopic factors for the $3^{+}$ state at 6.125 MeV in $^{26}$Mg: $C^2S(\ell=0) = 0.11(2)$ and $C^2S(\ell=2) = 0.27(6)$
- Derived resonance strength for the $0^{+}$ state in $^{26}$Si at $E_r = 0.3761$ MeV: $\omega\gamma = 2.4 \times 10^{-4}$ eV
- Upper limit on resonance strength for the $1^{+}$ state in $^{26}$Si at $E_r = 0.1622$ MeV: $\omega\gamma < 2.6 \times 10^{-9}$ eV
- Resonance strength for the $3^{+}$ state in $^{26}$Si at $E_r = 0.4154$ MeV: $\omega\gamma = 2.3 \times 10^{-2}$ eV (adopted from Bennett et al. 2013)

## Context

This work extends indirect studies of the $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$)$^{26}$Si reaction, which is a key nuclear uncertainty for predicting $^{26}$Al production in novae. The $3^{+}$ resonance strength was previously constrained by Peplowski et al. (2009) via $^{25}$Al(d,n) and by Bennett et al. (2013) via $^{26}$P $\beta$-decay, but the $0^{+}$ and $1^{+}$ resonances had no experimental constraints. The new upper limit on the $1^{+}$ spectroscopic factor is incompatible with the earlier $^{25}$Mg($^{4}$He,$^{3}$He) study by Yasue et al. (1990), and the authors suggest this discrepancy may arise from multistep reaction contributions or unresolved neighboring states in that work. The results enable improved reaction rate calculations for nova burning conditions, showing that below 0.2 GK the $^{25}$Al(p,$\gamma$)$^{26}$Si rate is likely dominated by $\beta$-decay, and that further efforts should focus on the $3^{+}$ resonance uncertainty.
