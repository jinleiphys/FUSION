# Neutrinos And Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

> According to the standard models of particle physics and cosmology, there should be a background of cosmic neutrinos in the present Universe, similar to the cosmic microwave photon background. The weakness of the weak interactions renders this neutrino background undetectable with current technology. The cosmic neutrino background can, however, be probed indirectly through its cosmological effects on big bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) and the cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation. In this BBN review, focused on neutrinos and, more generally on dark radiation, the BBN constraints on the number of "equivalent neutrinos" (dark radiation), on the baryon asymmetry (baryon density), and on a possible lepton asymmetry (neutrino degeneracy) are reviewed and updated. The BBN constraints on dark radiation and on the baryon density following from considerations of the primordial abundances of deuterium and helium-4 are in excellent agreement with the complementary results from the CMB, providing a suggestive, but currently inconclusive, hint of the presence of dark radiation and, they constrain any lepton asymmetry. For all the cases considered here there is a "lithium problem": the BBN-predicted lithium abundance exceeds the observationally inferred primordial value by a factor of ~3.

# Neutrinos And Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

BBN constraints on dark radiation (parameterized as the equivalent number of neutrinos, ΔNν) and on the baryon density from deuterium and helium-4 abundances are in excellent agreement with complementary CMB results, providing a suggestive but currently inconclusive hint of the presence of dark radiation. For all cases considered, the BBN-predicted lithium abundance exceeds the observationally inferred primordial value by a factor of approximately 3, constituting the "lithium problem."

## Method

The paper reviews and updates BBN constraints on three fundamental parameters: the baryon density parameter η10, the expansion rate parameter S (related to ΔNν, the equivalent number of additional neutrino species representing dark radiation), and the neutrino degeneracy parameter ξ (lepton asymmetry). These constraints are derived by comparing BBN-predicted primordial abundances of deuterium and helium-4, obtained from numerical BBN codes and fitted to analytic expressions, with observationally inferred primordial abundances from high-redshift QSO absorption-line systems for deuterium and from extragalactic HII regions for helium-4.

## Key numbers

- BBN-inferred baryon density (assuming standard model, ΔNν = 0 = ξ): η10 = 5.96 ± 0.28, corresponding to ΩBh² = 0.0218 ± 0.0010
- Observationally inferred primordial deuterium abundance: yDP ≡ 10⁵(D/H)P = 2.63 ± 0.12 (log yDP = 0.42 ± 0.02)
- Observationally inferred primordial helium-4 mass fraction: YP = 0.2565 ± 0.0060 (combining statistical error ±0.0010 and systematic error ±0.0050 linearly)
- BBN-predicted helium-4 mass fraction for standard model: YP = 0.2476 ± 0.0007
- BBN-predicted lithium abundance (for ηLi = 6): yLiP ≡ 10¹⁰(Li/H)P = 4.82(1 ± 0.10), corresponding to A(Li) ≈ 2.7
- Observed lithium plateau: A(Li) ≈ 2.2 ± 0.1, a factor of approximately 3 lower than BBN predictions
- Neutron lifetime adopted: τn = 881.5 ± 1.5 s (updated from Particle Data Group 2011)

## Context

This review extends the author's prior work (Steigman 2007, 2008, 2010) and builds on the BBN parameter fitting framework of Kneller and Steigman (2004) and Simha and Steigman (2008). It incorporates the updated neutron lifetime from the Particle Data Group (2011) and addresses the tension in the d(p,γ)³He cross section highlighted by Nollett and Holder (2011). The paper enables simultaneous constraints on baryon density, dark radiation, and lepton asymmetry using BBN and CMB data, while identifying the unresolved lithium problem as a key open question requiring further investigation of stellar structure and evolution or new physics.
