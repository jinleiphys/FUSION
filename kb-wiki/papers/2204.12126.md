# Symmetry restoration methods

> Symmetry techniques based on group theory play a prominent role in the analysis of nuclear phenomena, and in particular in the understanding of observed regular patterns in nuclear spectra and selection rules for electromagnetic transitions. A variety of symmetry-based nuclear models have been developed in nuclear physics, providing efficient tools of choice to interpret nuclear spectroscopic data. This chapter provides a pedagogical introduction to the basic idea of symmetry-breaking mechanism and symmetry-restoration methods in modeling atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a pedagogical introduction to symmetry-breaking mechanisms and symmetry-restoration methods in modeling atomic nuclei, with the caveat that spontaneous symmetry breaking in nuclear physics arises only as a result of approximations (mean-field approaches) rather than from the exact solution of the nuclear many-body problem, which would preserve all symmetries.

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of projection operators based on group theory, specifically using the orthogonality relations of irreducible representations (irreps) of symmetry groups to extract components with correct quantum numbers from symmetry-breaking mean-field wave functions. It describes the generator coordinate method (GCM) framework, where nuclear wave functions are built as linear superpositions of non-orthogonal basis functions projected from deformed mean-field states, with coefficients determined by solving the Hill-Wheeler-Griffin integral equation. The distinction is made between projection-after-variation (PAV) and variation-after-projection (VAP) schemes, and between simple symmetry restoration and the full symmetry-projected GCM (PGCM) that also mixes projected wave functions with different collective coordinates.

## Key numbers

* The dimension of the irreducible representation for the rotational group SO(3) is given as \(2\ell+1\) for spherical harmonics \(Y_{\ell m}\) with \(m=-\ell,\cdots,\ell\).
* The nuclear size is defined as \(R=1.2 A^{1/3}\) fm, where \(A\) is the mass number of the nucleus.
* The multipole deformation parameters are defined as \(\beta_{\lambda\mu} = \frac{4\pi}{3 A R^{\lambda}} \sum_{ij}(Q_{\lambda\mu})_{ij} \langle\Phi(\mathbf{q})| a^\dagger_i a_j |\Phi(\mathbf{q})\rangle\), with the multipole moment tensor operator \(Q_{\lambda\mu}=r^{\lambda} Y_{\lambda\mu}\) and \(\mu=-\lambda,-\lambda+1,\ldots,\lambda\).

## Context

This chapter extends the pedagogical tradition of textbooks by Ring (1980) and Blaizot (1986) and recent review papers by Sheikh (2021) and Yao (PPNP) on symmetry breaking and restoration in nuclear mean fields. It provides the conceptual foundation for advanced nuclear models including the multi-reference energy-density-functional (MR-EDF) method, Monte-Carlo shell model (MCSM), projected shell model (PSM), and recent implementations into ab initio methods such as symmetry broken and restored coupled-cluster theory by Duguet (2015), the in-medium generator coordinate method (IM-GCM) by Yao (2018, 2020), and many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) by Frosini (2021). The work enables the interpretation of nuclear spectroscopic data across different mass regions by recovering missing quantum numbers from symmetry-breaking mean-field wave functions.
