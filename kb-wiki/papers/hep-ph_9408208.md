# Astrophysical Implications of Neutrino Mass and Mixings

> Astrophysical implications of neutrino mass and mixings are discussed. The status of solar and atmospheric neutrino problems, and recent developments concerning nuclear physics input to solar models and solar opacities are reviewed. Implications of neutrino mass and mixings in supernova dynamics are explored. The effects of supernova density fluctuations in neutrino propagation is described.

## Key claim

Neutrino mass and mixings, particularly through the MSW effect, can have significant astrophysical implications for solar neutrino deficits, atmospheric neutrino anomalies, supernova dynamics, and heavy-element nucleosynthesis, but these effects depend on specific, currently unmeasured values of neutrino masses and mixing angles.

## Method

The paper reviews the status of solar and atmospheric neutrino experiments, comparing measured fluxes and ratios to standard solar model predictions. It then explores the MSW effect in supernova environments, calculating resonance densities between the neutrinosphere and stalled shock, and examines how density fluctuations in the sun and collapsing stellar cores affect neutrino flavor conversion probabilities.

## Key numbers

- Number of light neutrino flavors: \(N_{\nu} = 2.99 \pm 0.04\) from the invisible width of Z.
- Upper limit on tau neutrino mass: 35 MeV; a mass in the range of 1 to 10 eV could be a significant dark matter component.
- Homestake experiment: \(2.3 \pm 0.3\) SNU observed vs. \(8.0 \pm 1.0\) SNU (SSM1) and \(6.4 \pm 1.4\) SNU (SSM2).
- Kamioka experiment: observed flux ratio \(0.50 \pm 0.04 \pm 0.06\) relative to SSM1.
- SAGE: \(74 ^{+13}_{-12}\) (stat.)\(^{+5}_{-7}\) (sys.) SNU vs. \(131.5 ^{+7}_{-6}\) SNU (SSM1).
- Gallex: \(79 \pm 10\) (stat.) \(\pm 6\) (syst.) SNU vs. \(131.5 ^{+7}_{-6}\) SNU (SSM1).
- Non-adiabatic MSW solution parameters: \(\delta m^2 \sim 10^{-5}\) eV\(^2\) and \(\sin \theta \sim 0.1\).
- Atmospheric neutrino ratio of ratios R: Kamioka \(0.60 ^{+0.07}_{-0.06} \pm 0.05\), IMB \(0.54 \pm 0.05 \pm 0.12\), Soudan \(0.55 \pm 0.27\), Nusex \(0.99 ^{+0.35}_{-0.25}\), Frejus \(0.87 \pm 0.16 \pm 0.08\).
- For atmospheric neutrino oscillations, \(\delta m^2 > 10^{-3}\) eV\(^2\) is required for the oscillating term to average to zero.
- MSW resonance density: \(\rho_{\rm res} = 1.31 \times 10^7 (\delta m^2 / {\rm eV}^2) ({\rm MeV} / E_{\nu}) (0.5 / Y_e)\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
- Neutrinosphere density: \(\sim 10^{12}\) g cm\(^{-3}\); stalled shock density: \(\sim 2 \times 10^7\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
- Neutrino temperatures: \(\nu_{\tau}, \overline{\nu}_{\tau}, \nu_{\mu}, \overline{\nu}_{\mu}\) at about 8 MeV; \(\overline{\nu}_e\) at about 5 MeV; \(\nu_e\) at about 3.5 to 4 MeV.
- For small mixing angles between \(\nu_{\tau}\) and \(\nu_e\), a 60% increase in explosion energy is possible.
- In collapsing star simulations, fractional density fluctuations of \(5 \times 10^{-3}\) lead to complete depolarization (50% conversion) for heavy neutrino mass greater than \(\sim 30\) MeV.

## Context

This review extends the discussion of neutrino mass and mixings from the MSW effect, originally proposed by Mikheyev, Smirnov, and Wolfenstein, to astrophysical environments including the sun and supernovae. It builds on prior work by Bahcall (standard solar models), Gaisser (atmospheric neutrino fluxes), and Mayle (supernova dynamics), and addresses recent measurements of the S\(_{17}\) astrophysical S-factor from RIKEN and updated opacities from Livermore. The paper enables future searches for neutrino oscillations in long-baseline experiments and further studies of r-process nucleosynthesis constraints in supernovae.
