# Quantum Monte Carlo Calculations of Light Nuclei

> Variational Monte Carlo and Green's function Monte Carlo are powerful tools for calculations of properties of light nuclei using realistic two-nucleon and three-nucleon potentials. Recently the GFMC method has been extended to multiple states with the same quantum numbers. The combination of the Argonne v_18 two-nucleon and Illinois-2 three-nucleon potentials gives a good prediction of many energies of nuclei up to 12C. A number of other recent results are presented: comparison of binding energies with those obtained by the no-core shell model; the incompatibility of modern nuclear Hamiltonians with a bound tetra-neutron; difficulties in computing RMS radii of very weakly bound nuclei, such as 6He; center-of-mass effects on spectroscopic factors; and the possible use of an artificial external well in calculations of neutron-rich isotopes.

## Key claim

The combination of the Argonne v18 two-nucleon and Illinois-2 three-nucleon potentials gives a good prediction of many energies of nuclei up to 12C, but the RMS error in reproducing experimental energies of 39 narrow states (width less than 0.2 MeV) is 0.7 MeV, and the 12C result is marked preliminary due to a simplified trial wave function and approximate treatment of the three-nucleon potential in the Green's function Monte Carlo propagation.

## Method

Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) was used to construct sophisticated trial wave functions with two- and three-nucleon correlation operators, and Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) projected out the true ground state by propagating in imaginary time using a constrained-path algorithm with 10-20 unconstrained steps to control the Fermion sign problem. The Hamiltonian included the Argonne v18 two-nucleon potential and the Illinois-2 three-nucleon potential, with calculations performed for nuclei up to A=12 using parallel computers.

## Key numbers

- GFMC energy for 4He using an eight-operator NN potential had a statistical error of only 20 keV and agreed with other best results to less than 0.1% accuracy
- The AV18+IL2 Hamiltonian reproduces experimental energies of 39 states with width less than 0.2 MeV with an RMS error of 0.7 MeV
- VMC underbinds compared to GFMC by 4.3 MeV (13%) for 6Li and 25 MeV (38%) for 10B
- A=10 calculations require approximately 8,000 processor hours on the NERSC IBM SP (390 MFLOPS/processor) and 10^16 operations
- 12C calculations require approximately 150,000 processor hours on the Los Alamos qsc computer (360 MFLOPS/processor) and 2x10^17 operations
- Computed RMS charge radius for 4He is 1.660(10) fm compared to experimental 1.673(1) fm
- Computed RMS charge radius for 6He is 2.15(7) fm compared to experimental 2.054(14) fm
- Computed RMS charge radius for 8He is 1.98(4) fm (no experimental value given)
- The three-nucleon potential contributes 2-9% of total potential energy but 15-50% of binding energy in light nuclei

## Context

This work extends the GFMC method to multiple states with the same quantum numbers, building on earlier VMC and GFMC reviews up to A=8 (Pieper and Wiringa, 2001) and A=9,10 results (Pieper, Varga, and Wiringa, 2002). The Illinois-2 three-nucleon potential was developed (Pieper, Pandharipande, Wiringa, and Carlson, 2001) to address the need for additional binding in p-shell nuclei beyond what the Urbana series provided. The paper compares GFMC results with no-core shell model calculations (Navratil, Vary, and Barrett, 2000), finding that NCSM is much faster but less accurate when three-nucleon potentials are used due to basis size limitations. The tetraneutron study (Pieper, 2003) demonstrates that modern nuclear Hamiltonians strongly exclude a bound tetraneutron, contradicting an experimental claim. The work enables future calculations of neutron-rich isotopes using artificial external wells and highlights difficulties in computing RMS radii of weakly bound nuclei such as 6He.
