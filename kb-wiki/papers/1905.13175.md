# Towards Understanding Astrophysical Effects of Nuclear Symmetry Energy

> Determining the Equation of State (EOS) of dense neutron-rich nuclear matter is a shared goal of both nuclear physics and astrophysics. Except possible phase transitions, the density dependence of nuclear symmetry \esym is the most uncertain part of the EOS of neutron-rich nucleonic matter especially at supra-saturation densities. Much progresses have been made in recent years in predicting the symmetry energy and understanding why it is still very uncertain using various microscopic nuclear many-body theories and phenomenological models. Simultaneously, significant progresses have also been made in probing the symmetry energy in both terrestrial nuclear laboratories and astrophysical observatories. In light of the GW170817 event as well as ongoing or planned nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations probing the EOS of dense neutron-rich matter, we review recent progresses and identify new challenges to the best knowledge we have on several selected topics critical for understanding astrophysical effects of the nuclear symmetry energy.

## Key claim

The density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho)\) remains the most uncertain part of the equation of state of neutron-rich nucleonic matter, especially at supra-saturation densities, because of poor knowledge about the isospin-dependence of two-body forces and the spin-isospin dependence of three-body and tensor forces at short distances in dense matter.

## Method

The paper reviews recent progress in constraining the nuclear symmetry energy by combining microscopic nuclear many-body theories (e.g., Gogny functionals, Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, self-consistent Green's function approaches) with phenomenological models, and by analyzing data from terrestrial nuclear experiments (heavy-ion reactions, isospin diffusion, pion ratios) and astrophysical observations (neutron star radii, tidal deformability from GW170817, quasi-periodic oscillations). The authors decompose the symmetry energy and its density slope \(L(\rho)\) using the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem to identify contributions from the isoscalar effective mass, isovector single-nucleon potential, and momentum-dependent terms.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of nuclear matter: \(\rho_0 \approx 2.8 \times 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\) (0.16 fm\(^{-3}\)).
- Most probable incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter: \(K_0 = 230 \pm 20\) MeV.
- Fiducial values from 28 analyses: \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_0) = 31.6 \pm 2.7\) MeV and \(L(\rho_0) = 58.9 \pm 16\) MeV.
- More recent survey: \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_0) = 31.7 \pm 3.2\) MeV and \(L(\rho_0) = 58.7 \pm 28.1\) MeV.
- Skewness parameter of symmetric nuclear matter: \(-800 \leq J_0 \leq 400\) MeV.
- Predicted ranges for symmetry energy curvature and skewness: \(-400 \leq K_{\rm sym} \leq 100\) MeV, \(-200 \leq J_{\rm sym} \leq 800\) MeV.

## Context

This review extends earlier compilations such as Li (2013) and Oertel (2017) by incorporating constraints from the GW170817 event and recent analyses of neutron star observables. It identifies the tensor force in the isosinglet nucleon-nucleon channel and short-range correlations as key microscopic ingredients that cause divergent predictions at supra-saturation densities. The work enables future multi-messenger approaches combining nuclear theory, terrestrial experiments (e.g., rare isotope beam facilities), and astrophysical observations to resolve the high-density behavior of the symmetry energy.
