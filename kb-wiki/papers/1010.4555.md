# Determining pseudoscalar meson photo-production amplitudes from complete experiments

> We review the development of the most general analytic form of the cross section, dependent upon the three polarization vectors of the beam, target and recoil baryon, including all single, double and triple-polarization terms involving 16 spin-dependent observables. We examine the different conventions that have been used by different authors, and we present expressions that allow the direct numerical calculation of any pseudoscalar meson photo-production observables with arbitrary spin projections from the Chew-Goldberger-Low-Nambu amplitudes. We use this numerical tool to clarify apparent sign differences that exist in the literature. We also present analytic expressions that determine the recoil baryon polarization, together with examples of their potential use with quasi-4pi detectors to deduce observables. As an illustration of the use of the consistent machinery presented in this review, we carry out a multipole analysis of the gamma p -> K^+ Lambda reaction and examine the impact of recently published polarization measurements. In fitting multipoles, we use a combined Monte Carlo sampling of the amplitude space, with gradient minimization, and find a shallow chi^2 valley pitted with a very large number of local minima. This results in broad bands of multipole solutions that are experimentally indistinguishable. While these bands have been noticeably narrowed by the inclusion of new polarization measurements, many of the multipoles remain very poorly determined, even in sign, despite the inclusion of data on 8 different observables. We have compared multipoles from recent PWA codes with our model-independent solution bands. The potential accuracy of amplitudes that could be extracted from measurements of all 16 polarization observables has been studied with mock data using the statistical variations that are expected from ongoing experiments.

## Key claim

While mathematical proofs indicate that 8 carefully chosen observables are sufficient to determine pseudoscalar meson photo-production amplitudes free of ambiguities, experiments with realistically achievable uncertainties will require a significantly larger number of observables, as demonstrated by a multipole analysis of the gamma p -> K+ Lambda reaction where data on 8 different observables still left many multipoles very poorly determined, even in sign.

## Method

The authors developed a numerical tool based on the Chew-Goldberger-Low-Nambu (CGLN) amplitudes that allows direct calculation of any pseudoscalar meson photo-production observable with arbitrary spin projections, using this to resolve sign discrepancies in the literature for six double-polarization observables. They then carried out a model-independent multipole analysis of the gamma p -> K+ Lambda reaction using a combined Monte Carlo sampling of amplitude space with gradient minimization, fitting data on 8 different observables to extract multipole solutions. Finally, they used mock data with statistical variations expected from ongoing experiments to study the potential accuracy of amplitudes that could be extracted from measurements of all 16 polarization observables.

## Key numbers

- The general cross section involves 16 spin-dependent observables: the unpolarized differential cross section (d sigma_0), three single-polarization asymmetries (Sigma, T, P), and three sets of four double-polarization asymmetries (beam-target, beam-recoil, target-recoil)
- In the multipole analysis of gamma p -> K+ Lambda, data on 8 different observables still left many multipoles very poorly determined, even in sign
- The multipole expansion sums over orbital angular momentum l from 0 to a limiting value l_max that depends on the energy
- The density of state factor is given by rho_0 = k/q, where k is the meson c.m. momentum and q is the photon c.m. energy

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Fasano, Tabakin and Saghai (1992) by expanding their treatment to include the complete set of triple polarization cases, and resolves sign discrepancies in double-polarization observables that had appeared inconsistently across the literature (Barker, Donnachie and Storrow, 1975; Chiang and Tabakin, 1997). The analysis demonstrates that ongoing complete experiments at Jefferson Lab (FROST and HDice programs), as well as complementary experiments at GRAAL in Grenoble and electron facilities in Bonn and Mainz, will need to measure significantly more than the theoretical minimum of 8 observables to achieve model-independent amplitude extraction with realistic uncertainties.
