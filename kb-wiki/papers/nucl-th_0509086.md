# A consistent quasiparticle picture of Quark-Gluon plasma and the velocity of sound

## Key claim
A thermodynamically consistent density-dependent quark mass (DDQM) model, incorporating a background field to remove thermodynamic inconsistencies, reproduces the qualitative features of lattice data for the velocity of sound in a hot quark-gluon plasma, but the comparison is limited because the lattice results are for pure gluon plasma while the model results include quarks.

## Method
The authors use a two-flavor (u and d) DDQM model where the effective quark mass depends on temperature and quark density, with a self-consistent number density relation. They enforce thermodynamic consistency by introducing a background field Φ that satisfies conditions derived from Biró et al. (2000), then compute the total energy density and pressure of the QGP from quark and gluon contributions plus the background field. The velocity of sound is calculated as (∂P/∂E)_T rather than the adiabatic derivative, with the authors noting the difference does not exceed 10%.

## Key numbers
- The critical temperature increases by 29% when the background field Φ is included in the DDQM model.
- The bag constant parameter B^(1/4) is fixed at 145 MeV in the main calculations.
- Nuclear matter saturation density n₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³ is used as the reference density.
- The canonical value for sound velocity squared in an ideal QGP is u² = 1/3 at high temperatures.
- The gluonic sector energy density and pressure become zero for α_c ≥ 4π/15, interpreted as gluon condensate formation.

## Context
This work extends the DDQM model of Chakrabarty et al. (1989) and earlier studies by incorporating the thermodynamic consistency conditions derived by Biró et al. (2000). The results are compared with lattice QCD calculations of sound velocity for pure gluon plasma from lattice studies (2005) and with lattice data on quark number susceptibility. The model provides a phenomenological framework that captures non-perturbative to perturbative QCD transition features, and the finite baryon density results for sound velocity are presented as predictions for future lattice calculations.
