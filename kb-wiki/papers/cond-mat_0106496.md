# Non-extensive Hamiltonian systems follow Boltzmann's principle not Tsallis statistics. -- Phase Transitions, Second Law of Thermodynamics

> Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V)=k*ln W(E,N,V) relates the entropy to the geometric area e^{S(E,N,V)} of the manifold of constant energy in the N-body phase space. From the principle all thermodynamics and especially all phenomena of phase transitions and critical phenomena can be deduced. The topology of the curvature matrix C(E,N) (Hessian) of S(E,N) determines regions of pure phases, regions of phase separation, and (multi-)critical points and lines. Thus, C(E,N) describes all kind of phase-transitions with all their flavor. No assumptions of extensivity, concavity of S(E), or additivity have to be invoked. Thus Boltzmann's principle and not Tsallis statistics describes the equilibrium properties as well the approach to equilibrium of extensive and non-extensive Hamiltonian systems. No thermodynamic limit must be invoked.

# Non-extensive Hamiltonian systems follow Boltzmann's principle not Tsallis statistics

## Key claim

Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V) = k ln W(E,N,V) is the only axiomatic assumption necessary for thermo-statistics of Hamiltonian systems, whether extensive or non-extensive; Tsallis q-statistics reduces to Boltzmann entropy for closed Hamiltonian systems at equilibrium, and the physical temperature and pressure must be defined through Boltzmann's entropy to satisfy the zeroth law.

## Method

The author derives equilibrium thermodynamics directly from Boltzmann's principle by computing the geometric area W(E,N,V) of the constant-energy manifold in N-body phase space via the microcanonical partition sum, without invoking extensivity, additivity, concavity of S(E), or the thermodynamic limit. Phase transitions are classified by the topology of the curvature matrix (Hessian) of S(E,N) and its eigenvalues λ₁, λ₂, which determine regions of pure phases, phase separation, and critical points. The approach to equilibrium (Second Law) is analyzed through the box-counting measure of phase-space distributions under dynamical mixing, showing that the closure of the time-evolved manifold equals the larger microcanonical manifold.

## Key numbers

- The entropy is defined as S(E,N,V) = ln W(E,N,V) (with Boltzmann constant k = 1), where W(E,N,V) = tr[ε₀ δ(E − Ĥ_N)] is the area of the constant-energy manifold.
- A single stable phase is indicated by λ₁ < 0 (locally concave entropy).
- A first-order transition with phase separation is indicated by λ₁ > 0 (convex intruder in the direction of the largest curvature).
- A continuous (second-order) transition occurs where λ₁(E,N) = 0 and v₁·∇λ₁ = 0.
- A multi-critical point occurs where λ₁ = 0 and ∇λ₁ = 0.
- For a system expanding from volume V₁ to V₂, the Second Law is expressed as lim_{δ→0} lim_{t→∞} M_δ(t,t₀) = W(E,N,V₂) ≥ W(E,N,V₁).

## Context

This work extends Boltzmann's (1877) original principle and contrasts with the conventional Yang-Lee theory (1952) of phase transitions via grand-canonical partition sum zeros, as well as with the thermodynamic limit approach advocated by Lebowitz (1999) and Lieb (1997). The author argues against the Tsallis q-entropy formalism (Tsallis, 1988; Abe, 2001; Toral, 2001) for equilibrium Hamiltonian systems, showing that Boltzmann's principle suffices for both extensive and non-extensive systems. The work enables the study of phase transitions in "Small" systems (e.g., nuclei with ~100 nucleons) without requiring the thermodynamic limit, and provides a geometric classification of phase transitions via the Hessian of entropy.
