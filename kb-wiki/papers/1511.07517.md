# Microscopic Theory of Nuclear Fission: A Review

> This article reviews how nuclear fission is described within nuclear density functional theory. In spontaneous fission, half-lives are the main observables and quantum tunnelling the essential concept, while in induced fission the focus is on fragment properties and explicitly time-dependent approaches are needed. The cornerstone of the current microscopic theory of fission is the energy density functional formalism. Its basic tenets, including tools such as the HFB theory, effective two-body effective nuclear potentials, finite-temperature extensions and beyond mean-field corrections, are presented succinctly. The EDF approach is often combined with the hypothesis that the time-scale of the large amplitude collective motion driving the system to fission is slow compared to typical time-scales of nucleons inside the nucleus. In practice, this hypothesis of adiabaticity is implemented by introducing (a few) collective variables and mapping out the many-body Schrödinger equation into a collective Schrödinger-like equation for the nuclear wave-packet. Scission configurations indicate where the split occurs. This collective Schrödinger equation depends on an inertia tensor that includes the response of the system to small changes in the collective variables and also plays a special role in the determination of spontaneous fission half-lives. A trademark of the microscopic theory of fission is the tremendous amount of computing needed for practical applications. In particular, the successful implementation of the theories presented in this article requires a very precise numerical resolution of the HFB equations for large values of the collective variables. Finally, a selection of the most recent and representative results obtained for both spontaneous and induced fission is presented with the goal of emphasizing the coherence of the microscopic approaches employed.

## Key claim

This review argues that nuclear density functional theory (DFT), combined with the adiabatic hypothesis that collective motion is slow compared to nucleonic timescales, provides a coherent microscopic framework for describing both spontaneous and induced fission, but acknowledges that reaching predictive accuracy requires progress in nuclear energy functionals, technical approximations, and understanding of scission physics.

## Method

The paper reviews how fission is described within nuclear density functional theory by solving Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations to compute potential energy surfaces (PES) as functions of collective variables such as multipole moments or Funny Hills parameters. The adiabatic approximation maps the many-body Schrodinger equation onto a collective Schrodinger-like equation with an inertia tensor computed via adiabatic time-dependent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov or the generator coordinate method. Spontaneous fission half-lives are extracted from multi-dimensional quantum tunnelling probabilities using the WKB formula, while induced fission studies employ time-dependent approaches including semi-classical and fully quantum many-body methods.

## Key numbers

* Spontaneous fission half-lives range from 4.2 microseconds in $^{250}$No to over 1.4 x $10^{10}$ years for $^{232}$Th, spanning over 35 orders of magnitude
* The time from saddle to scission is typically of order $10^{-19}$ seconds, about two orders of magnitude slower than the timescale inferred from the average binding energy per nucleon of approximately 8 MeV
* Energy released during fission is typically about 200 MeV per fission event, with 10-20% being excitation energy
* The fissility parameter is given by $x \approx Z^{2}/50.88A(1-\eta I^2)$ with $I = (N-Z)/A$ and $\eta = 1.7826$
* Four known fissile elements ($^{239,241}$Pu and $^{233,235}$U) can undergo fission with thermal neutrons having kinetic energy of a few tens of meV

## Context

This review extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach pioneered by Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and later formalized by Strutinsky and collaborators, which combined liquid drop models with shell corrections. The paper situates DFT-based fission theory as distinct from configuration interaction methods such as ab initio techniques or the nuclear shell model, while noting that relativistic mean-field approaches (reviewed by Ring 1996 and Niksic 2011) are excluded for length. The work enables systematic microscopic predictions of fission observables for applications in nuclear energy, superheavy element research, and r-process nucleosynthesis simulations of neutron star mergers as studied by Goriely (2011), Korobkin (2012), and Just (2015).
