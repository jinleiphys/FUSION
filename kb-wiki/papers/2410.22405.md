# No-quenching baseline for energy loss signals in oxygen-oxygen collisions

> In this work, we perform computations of inclusive jet, and semi-inclusive jet-hadron cross sections for minimum bias oxygen-oxygen collisions at RHIC and LHC collision energies. We compute the no-quenching baseline for the jet nuclear modification factor $R_\mathrm{AA}$ and jet-, and hadron-triggered semi-inclusive nuclear modification factors $I_\mathrm{AA}$. We do this with state-of-the-art nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs), next-to-leading-order matrix elements, parton shower, and hadronization. We observe deviations from unity due to cold-nuclear matter effects, even without quenching. We demonstrate that the parton distribution uncertainties constitute a significant obstacle in detecting energy loss in small collision systems. Hadron-triggered observables are particularly sensitive to uncertainties due to correlations between the trigger and analyzed particles. For jet-triggered $I_\mathrm{AA}$, there exists a kinematic window in which nPDF and scale uncertainties cancel dramatically while showing little sensitivity to parton shower and hadronization models, addressing a major limiting factor for energy loss discovery in small systems.

## Key claim

The paper provides a no-quenching baseline for jet and semi-inclusive observables in oxygen-oxygen collisions, showing that cold-nuclear matter effects from nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs) cause deviations from unity even without any energy loss in a quark-gluon plasma. The authors demonstrate that for jet-triggered semi-inclusive nuclear modification factors, there exists a kinematic window where nPDF and scale uncertainties cancel dramatically, which could address a major limiting factor for energy loss discovery in small collision systems.

## Method

The authors performed next-to-leading-order (NLO) calculations of inclusive jet and semi-inclusive jet-hadron cross sections for minimum bias oxygen-oxygen collisions at RHIC and LHC energies, using NLO nuclear parton distribution functions (nPDFs) from EPPS21, TUJU21, and nNNPDF3.0, combined with NLO-matched parton showers and hadronization in Pythia8 and Herwig7 event generators. Jets were reconstructed using the anti-kT algorithm with R=0.4, and the nuclear modification factors R_AA and I_AA were computed without including any final-state medium interactions (the "no-quenching" baseline). The authors systematically evaluated nPDF uncertainties, scale uncertainties from 9-point variations of factorization and renormalization scales, and shower/hadronization model dependence.

## Key numbers

- Jet nuclear modification factor R_OO at sqrt(s_NN)=6.8 TeV: EPPS21 shows deviations from unity with nPDF uncertainty bands that are approximately four times larger than those from EPPS16 at low pT
- Jet-triggered semi-inclusive I_AA at sqrt(s_NN)=6.8 TeV: nPDF uncertainties are up to ten times smaller than those for inclusive jet R_OO in certain kinematic regions
- At sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV: the no-quenching baseline for jet-triggered I_AA is significantly suppressed below unity at higher hadron momenta due to nPDF effects
- Scale uncertainties in I_AA at sqrt(s_NN)=6.8 TeV: around a few percent across all pT, smaller than most nPDF uncertainties
- Shower and hadronization model differences between Pythia and Herwig for I_OO: up to 5% suppression in Herwig relative to Pythia
- Jet trigger thresholds studied: pT^j>30 GeV and pT^j>60 GeV at LHC; pT^j>15 GeV at RHIC

## Context

This work extends previous baseline calculations for oxygen-oxygen collisions by Huss (2020) and Brewer (2021) by using updated nPDF parametrizations (EPPS21, TUJU21, nNNPDF3.0) and providing systematic uncertainty estimates. The study is motivated by the planned LHC oxygen-oxygen run at sqrt(s_NN)=6.8 TeV in 2025 and existing RHIC data at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV, where previous measurements of inclusive and semi-inclusive nuclear modification factors in pPb collisions by CMS (2016), ATLAS (2014), and ALICE (2021, 2023) have set upper limits on energy loss. The results enable future experimental searches for energy loss signals in small collision systems by providing a controlled baseline against which any quenching signal must be distinguished.
