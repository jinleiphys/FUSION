# Second virial coefficients of light nuclear clusters and their chemical freeze-out in nuclear collisions

> Here we develop a new strategy to analyze the chemical freeze-out of light (anti)nuclei produced in high energy collisions of heavy atomic nuclei within an advanced version of the hadron resonance gas model. It is based on two different, but complementary approaches to model the hard-core repulsion between the light nuclei and hadrons. The first approach is based on an approximate treatment of the equivalent hard-core radius of a roomy nuclear cluster and pions, while the second approach is rigorously derived here using a self-consistent treatment of classical excluded volumes of light (anti)nuclei and hadrons. By construction, in a hadronic medium dominated by pions, both approaches should give the same results. Employing this strategy to the analysis of hadronic and light (anti)nuclei multiplicities measured by ALICE at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} =2.76$ TeV and by STAR at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} =200$ GeV, we got rid of the existing ambiguity in the description of light (anti)nuclei data and determined the chemical freeze-out parameters of nuclei with high accuracy and confidence. At ALICE energy the nuclei are frozen prior to the hadrons at the temperature $T = 175.1^{+2.3}_{-3.9}$ MeV, while at STAR energy there is a single freeze-out of hadrons and nuclei at the temperature $T = 167.2 \pm 3.9$ MeV. We argue that the found chemical freeze-out volumes of nuclei can be considered as the volumes of quark-gluon bags that produce the nuclei at the moment of hadronization.

## Key claim

The paper resolves the ambiguity in describing light (anti)nuclei chemical freeze-out by developing two complementary approaches to model hard-core repulsion between nuclei and hadrons, finding that at LHC energies nuclei freeze out before hadrons at a higher temperature, while at RHIC energies there is a single freeze-out for both.

## Method

The authors extended the hadron resonance gas model (HRGM) with multicomponent hard-core repulsion using the induced surface tension concept, deriving a self-consistent treatment of classical excluded volumes for light nuclei (deuteron, triton, helium-3, helium-4, hypertriton) and hadrons. They applied this framework to analyze measured multiplicities of hadrons and light (anti)nuclei from ALICE at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV and STAR at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, fitting the chemical freeze-out parameters.

## Key numbers

- At ALICE energy (sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV), the chemical freeze-out temperature for light nuclei is T = 175.1^{+2.3}_{-3.9} MeV, prior to hadron freeze-out.
- At STAR energy (sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV), there is a single freeze-out temperature for both hadrons and nuclei at T = 167.2 ± 3.9 MeV.
- The root-mean-square radii used for light nuclei are: deuteron 2.1421 ± 0.0088 fm, triton 1.7591 ± 0.0363 fm, helium-3 1.9661 ± 0.0030 fm, helium-4 1.6755 ± 0.0028 fm, and hypertriton 4.9 fm (estimate from Ref. HtritonR).
- The hard-core radius of baryons used is R_b = 0.365 fm, and the largest hard-core diameter of hadrons is 0.84 fm.

## Context

This work extends the multicomponent HRGM with induced surface tension previously developed by the same group (IST1, IST2, IST3) to include light nuclear clusters, overcoming the heuristic bag model approximation used in their earlier work (Grinyuk 2020). It addresses the puzzle of why light nuclei appear to freeze out at temperatures near 160 MeV despite their binding energies being an order of magnitude smaller, and provides a framework to distinguish between the coalescence and thermal statistical model explanations for light nuclei production in heavy-ion collisions.
