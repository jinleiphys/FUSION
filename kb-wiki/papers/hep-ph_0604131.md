# Can collisional energy loss explain nuclear suppression factor for light hadrons ?

> We argue that in the measured $p_T$ domain of RHIC, collisional rather than the radiative energy loss is the dominant mechanism for jet quenching. Accordingly we calculate nuclear suppression factor for light hadrons by taking only the elastic energy loss in sharp contrast with the previous calculations where only the radiative loss are considered.

## Key claim

The authors argue that in the measured transverse momentum domain of RHIC (p_T ~ 1-13 GeV), collisional energy loss, rather than radiative energy loss, is the dominant mechanism for jet quenching of light hadrons, contrary to the commonly held view that collisional loss can be ignored altogether.

## Method

The authors calculate the nuclear suppression factor (R_AA) for neutral pions using only collisional (elastic) energy loss, modeled via a Fokker-Planck equation that incorporates drag and diffusion coefficients derived from hard thermal loop (HTL) corrected gluon propagators in the small-angle scattering limit. The initial parton distributions are parametrized and evolved dynamically through an expanding quark-gluon plasma described by Bjorken hydrodynamics, and the resulting parton distributions at the transition temperature T_c = 190 MeV are convolved with fragmentation functions to obtain the pion spectra.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear suppression factor R_AA for neutral pions in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV is calculated and found to describe PHENIX data reasonably well, with the extracted initial parton density n_{g+q+qbar} = 49 fm^{-3} at this energy.
- At sqrt(s) = 17.3 GeV, the extracted initial parton density is 4 fm^{-3} with R_AA = 0.97; at 62.4 GeV, density is 15 fm^{-3} with R_AA = 0.59; at 130 GeV, density is 27 fm^{-3} with R_AA = 0.39; at 200 GeV, density is 49 fm^{-3} with R_AA = 0.27.
- The maximum average parton energy required to produce pions in the measured p_T range (1-13 GeV) at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV is about 26 GeV.
- For lower beam energies of 62.4 (130) AGeV, the maximum average parton energy required to produce a 13 GeV pion is 16 (22) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier studies (Alam et al. 2005, Roy et al. 2006) which first showed that collisional loss can be as important as radiative loss for light partons at RHIC energies, contradicting the standard jet tomography models of Gyulassy et al. (2003) and others that considered only radiative energy loss via induced bremsstrahlung. The finding that collisional loss dominates in the measured p_T range at RHIC challenges the assumptions underlying previous calculations by Wang (2004) and Salgado (2004), and suggests that the theory of jet tomography needs to be revised to include both mechanisms, with implications for interpreting the non-photonic single electron spectrum from heavy meson decays measured by PHENIX.
