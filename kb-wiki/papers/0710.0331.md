# The strength of crystalline color superconductors

> We present a study of the shear modulus of the crystalline color superconducting phase of quark matter, showing that this phase of dense, but not asymptotically dense, quark matter responds to shear stress as a very rigid solid. This phase is characterized by a gap parameter $Δ$ that is periodically modulated in space and therefore spontaneously breaks translational invariance. We derive the effective action for the phonon fields that describe space- and time-dependent fluctuations of the crystal structure formed by $Δ$, and obtain the shear modulus from the coefficients of the spatial derivative terms. Within a Ginzburg-Landau approximation, we find shear moduli which are 20 to 1000 times larger than those of neutron star crusts. This phase of matter is thus more rigid than any known material in the universe, but at the same time the crystalline color superconducting phase is also superfluid. These properties raise the possibility that the presence of this phase within neutron stars may have distinct implications for their phenomenology. For example, (some) pulsar glitches may originate in crystalline superconducting neutron star cores.

## Key claim

Within a Ginzburg-Landau approximation, the crystalline color superconducting phase of quark matter has a shear modulus 20 to 1000 times larger than that of neutron star crusts, making it more rigid than any known material in the universe while simultaneously being superfluid; however, the Ginzburg-Landau expansion parameter \((\Delta/\delta\mu)^2\) can be around a tenth to a fourth for the most favorable phases, meaning this approximation is at the edge of its validity.

## Method

The authors derived the low-energy effective action for phonon fields describing space- and time-dependent fluctuations of the periodically modulated gap parameter \(\Delta\) in an NJL model within mean-field, weak-coupling, and Ginzburg-Landau approximations. They integrated out fermion fields to obtain the phonon effective action to second order in derivatives, second order in the gap parameters \(\Delta_I\), and second order in the phonon fields, then extracted the shear modulus tensor from the coefficients of the spatial derivative terms by comparing to the general form of the elastic action. The analysis was performed for two specific crystal structures, CubeX and 2Cube45z, which had previously been identified as having the lowest free energy among analyzed patterns.

## Key numbers

- Shear modulus of crystalline quark matter: \(\nu_{\rm CQM} = 1.18\, \mu^2 \Delta^2 = 2.47\, \frac{\text{MeV}}{\text{fm}^3} \left(\frac{\Delta}{10~\text{MeV}}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\mu}{400~\text{MeV}}\right)^2\)
- The shear modulus is 20 to 1000 times larger than those of neutron star crusts
- The Ginzburg-Landau expansion parameter \((\Delta/\delta\mu)^2\) is around 0.1 to 0.25 for the most favorable phases
- The crystalline phases are favored over unpaired quark matter and the gapless CFL phase for the density range \(2.9\Delta_0 < M_s^2/\mu < 10.4\Delta_0\)
- For \(\Delta_0 = 25\) MeV and \(M_s = 250\) MeV, the window of densities extends from below the \(\mu\) at which nuclear matter supersedes quark matter to above \(\mu \sim 500\) MeV
- The magnitude of the crystal momentum vectors is \(q_I = \eta \delta\mu_I\) with \(\eta = 1.1997\)
- Transverse phonon velocities are \(\sqrt{1/3}\) and \(\sqrt{2/3}\) of the speed of light

## Context

This work extends the analysis of crystalline color superconducting phases previously studied by Rajagopal (2006) and Mannarelli (2006), who calculated free energies of various crystal structures and identified CubeX and 2Cube45z as the most favorable patterns. The paper provides the first calculation of the shear modulus for these phases, demonstrating that they are far more rigid than neutron star crusts studied by Strohmayer (1991). This rigidity, combined with the superfluidity of the phase, enables a new hypothesis for pulsar glitches originating in quark matter cores rather than in the neutron star crust, potentially resolving the conflict between vortex pinning in crusts and the observed precession of several pulsars reported by Stairs (2000).
