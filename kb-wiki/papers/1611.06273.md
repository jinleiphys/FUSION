# Merging symmetry projection methods with coupled cluster theory: Lessons from the Lipkin model Hamiltonian

> Coupled cluster and symmetry projected Hartree-Fock are two central paradigms in electronic structure theory. However, they are very different. Single reference coupled cluster is highly successful for treating weakly correlated systems, but fails under strong correlation unless one sacrifices good quantum numbers and works with broken-symmetry wave functions, which is unphysical for finite systems. Symmetry projection is effective for the treatment of strong correlation at the mean-field level through multireference non-orthogonal configuration interaction wavefunctions, but unlike coupled cluster, it is neither size extensive nor ideal for treating dynamic correlation. We here examine different scenarios for merging these two dissimilar theories. We carry out this exercise over the integrable Lipkin model Hamiltonian, which despite its simplicity, encompasses non-trivial physics for degenerate systems and can be solved via diagonalization for a very large number of particles. We show how symmetry projection and coupled cluster doubles individually fail over different correlation limits, whereas models that merge these two theories are highly successful over the entire phase diagram. Despite the simplicity of the Lipkin Hamiltonian, the lessons learned in this work will be useful for building an ab initio symmetry projected coupled cluster theory that we expect to be accurate over the weakly and strongly correlated limits, as well as the recoupling regime.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that merging symmetry projection (parity projection) with coupled cluster theory yields highly accurate results across the entire phase diagram of the Lipkin model Hamiltonian, whereas each method individually fails in different correlation limits. The authors caution that while the combined methods are promising, the practical implementation for realistic systems will likely require similarity-transformed approaches rather than variational ones, and that the choice of the broken-symmetry reference determinant is nontrivial.

## Method

The authors use the integrable Lipkin model Hamiltonian with 50 particles, where the parameter x controls the correlation strength (weak at x≈1, strong at x≈0). They compare several approaches: restricted coupled cluster doubles (RCCD) built on the symmetry-adapted reference, unrestricted coupled cluster singles and doubles (UCCSD) built on the parity-broken reference, projected Hartree-Fock (PHF), and various combinations including projected restricted coupled cluster (PRCC), projected unrestricted coupled cluster (PUCC), and their extended coupled cluster variants (PRECC and PUECC). The wave function parameters and the symmetry-breaking parameter κ are determined either variationally, via similarity-transformed equations, or through a Brueckner-style optimization.

## Key numbers

- The 50-particle Lipkin model is used throughout (n = 50, so each level is 50-fold degenerate).
- The mean-field spontaneously breaks parity symmetry near x = 2/3, and in the thermodynamic limit there is a second-order phase transition exactly at x = 2/3.
- RCCD delivers a complex energy for x ≲ 2/3, while UCCSD is virtually exact for all x except the intermediate coupling regime near x = 2/3.
- PRCCD is at least qualitatively reasonable everywhere, unlike RCCD which fails except for x ≳ 2/3.
- PUCCSD improves upon UCCSD results further, regardless of whether the Brueckner procedure or the PHF-optimized κ is used.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the Lipkin model as a benchmark for many-body methods, including time-dependent HF (Krieger 1974), extended coupled-cluster theory (Arponen 1983, Robinson 1989), and symmetry projected HF (Robledo 1992). The authors build on their recent work on polynomial similarity transformations (Degroote 2016) and spin-projected Hartree-Fock (Qiu 2016) to explore merging coupled cluster with symmetry projection. The results enable the development of an ab initio symmetry projected coupled cluster theory expected to be accurate in the weakly and strongly correlated limits, as well as the recoupling regime.
