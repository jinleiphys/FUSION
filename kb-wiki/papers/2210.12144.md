# Assessing the theory-data tension in neutrino-induced charged pion production: the effect of final-state nucleon distortion

> Pion production on nuclei constitutes a significant part of the total cross section in experiments involving few-GeV neutrinos. Combined analyses of data on deuterium and heavier nuclei points to tensions between the bubble chamber data and the data of the MINER$ν$A experiment, which are often ascribed to unspecified nuclear effects. To understand the origin of these tensions, a microscopic quantum mechanical framework is needed to compute nuclear matrix elements. We use the local approximation to the relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) to assess the role of final-state nucleon distortion. To perform this comparison under conditions relevant to neutrino experiments, we compute cross sections for the MINER$ν$A and T2K charged pion production datasets. The inclusion of nucleon distortion leads to a reduction of the cross section up to 10\%, but to no significant change in shape of the flux-averaged cross sections. Results with and without distortion compare favorably to experimental data, with the exception of the low-$Q^2$ MINER$ν$A $π^+$ data. We point out that hydrogen target data from BEBC is also overpredicted at low-$Q^2$, and that the discrepancy is similar in shape and magnitude to what is found in comparison to MINER$ν$A data. Including nucleon distortion alone cannot explain the overprediction of low-$Q^2$ cross sections measured by MINER$ν$A. The similar overprediction of BEBC data on hydrogen means that it is impossible to ascribe this discrepancy solely to a nuclear effect. Axial couplings and their $Q^2$ dependence should ideally be derived from more precise data on hydrogen and deuterium.

## Key claim

The inclusion of final-state nucleon distortion in a relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) framework reduces the charged pion production cross section by up to 10% but does not change the shape of flux-averaged cross sections; this distortion alone cannot explain the overprediction of low-\(Q^2\) cross sections measured by MINER\(\nu\)A, and the similar overprediction of hydrogen target data from BEBC means the discrepancy cannot be ascribed solely to a nuclear effect.

## Method

The authors used the local approximation to the relativistic distorted wave impulse approximation (RDWIA) to compute nuclear matrix elements for neutrino-induced charged pion production on carbon, including final-state nucleon distortion via an energy-dependent relativistic mean field (EDRMF) potential. They compared these RDWIA results with the relativistic plane wave impulse approximation (RPWIA), where the final-state nucleon is treated as a plane wave, and computed cross sections for the MINER\(\nu\)A and T2K datasets using a hybrid single-nucleon operator that combines non-resonant background with resonance exchanges and a Regge approach at high invariant mass.

## Key numbers

- Inclusion of nucleon distortion leads to a reduction of the cross section up to 10%.
- The BEBC data on hydrogen is overpredicted at low-\(Q^2\) by almost a factor 2 for \(W < 1.4~\mathrm{GeV}\) in the \(n\pi^+\) channel.
- The vector-vector contribution in the \(n\pi^+\) channel from the DCC and Hybrid models agrees well, while the total cross section is underpredicted by almost a factor 2 for \(W < 1.4~\mathrm{GeV}\).
- The DCC and Hybrid model results are integrated up to \(Q^2 = 2.5~\mathrm{GeV}^2\) for the \(W\)-dependence comparisons.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of tensions between bubble chamber data (ANL/BNL) and MINER\(\nu\)A data highlighted in Ref. ANLBNLMinerva (2020), which often ascribed discrepancies to unspecified nuclear effects. The authors use the RDWIA framework previously applied to nucleon knockout in electromagnetic interactions by Atkinson (2018), Kelly (2005), and Udias (1993), and the EDRMF potential introduced by Gonzalez-Jimenez (2019) that successfully describes \((e,e')\) data. The results show that nucleon distortion alone cannot resolve the low-\(Q^2\) tension, and the similar overprediction of BEBC hydrogen data implies that axial couplings and their \(Q^2\) dependence should be derived from more precise data on hydrogen and deuterium, rather than being attributed to nuclear effects.
