# Studies on the electromagnetic structure of the nucleon by free and quasi-free Compton scattering at MAMI (Mainz)

> Using hydrogen and deuterium targets, Compton scattering by the proton and neutron have been studied at the tagged photon beam of the MAMI (Mainz) accelerator using different experimental setups. The theoretical tools for the analysis of the experimental data have been investigated, as there are the nonsubtracted dispersion theory and the theory of quasi-free reactions on the proton and neutron bound in the deuteron. Experimental Compton scattering data are understood in the first and second resonance region with good precision. Precise electromagnetic polarizabilities and spin polarizabilities for the proton and neutron have been determined.

# Studies on the electromagnetic structure of the nucleon by free and quasi-free Compton scattering at MAMI (Mainz)

## Key claim
The nonsubtracted dispersion theory, including a sigma meson t-channel contribution, successfully describes Compton scattering data on the proton and neutron in the first and second resonance region, enabling precise extraction of electromagnetic polarizabilities and spin polarizabilities. The agreement between theory and experiment for the proton backward spin polarizability validates the corresponding prediction for the neutron, which is then used to extract the neutron polarizability difference from quasi-free scattering data.

## Method
Free and quasi-free Compton scattering experiments were performed on hydrogen and deuterium targets using the tagged photon beam at the MAMI (Mainz) accelerator, employing the TAPS apparatus and the Mainz 64 cm diameter × 48 cm NaI(Tl) detector in coincidence with the Göttingen segmented recoil detector SENECA. The data were analyzed using nonsubtracted dispersion theory, where the invariant amplitudes are decomposed into s-channel and t-channel contributions, with the t-channel part of A1 attributed to sigma meson exchange and the t-channel part of A2 described by pi, eta, and eta-prime meson exchanges. Quasi-free events from proton and neutron were discriminated through a thin plastic scintillator layer in front of SENECA, and the quasi-free proton data were evaluated in the same way as the neutron data to test the experimental method against free-proton results.

## Key numbers
- Proton polarizability sum: α_p + β_p = 14.0 ± 0.3 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (from Baldin sum rule using photoabsorption data, Levchuk 2000)
- Proton polarizability difference: α_p − β_p = 10.5 ± 0.9 (stat+syst) ± 0.7 (model) × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (TAPS apparatus, below pion threshold, Olmos 2001)
- Neutron polarizability sum: α_n + β_n = 15.2 ± 0.5 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (from photoabsorption cross section, Levchuk 2000)
- Neutron polarizability difference: α_n − β_n = 9.8 ± 3.6 (stat) +2.1/−1.1 (syst) ± 2.2 (model) × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (quasi-free Compton scattering, Kossert 2002)
- Proton electric polarizability: α = 12.2 ± 0.6 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Proton magnetic polarizability: β = 1.8 ∓ 0.6 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Neutron electric polarizability: α = 12.5 ± 2.3 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Neutron magnetic polarizability: β = 2.7 ∓ 2.3 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- Proton backward spin polarizability: γ_π = −38.7 ± 1.8 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁴
- Neutron backward spin polarizability: γ_π = +58.6 ± 4.0 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁴ (first experimental result for this quantity)
- Sigma meson mass giving optimum fit to large-angle proton Compton data: m_σ = 600 MeV

## Context
This work extends the application of nonsubtracted dispersion theory, as developed by Lvov (1997) and Babusci (1998), to extract nucleon polarizabilities from Compton scattering data. The sigma meson ansatz for the t-channel contribution to the invariant amplitude A1, which is responsible for the diamagnetism and enhancement of the electric polarizability over s-channel predictions, is tested against experimental data from Galler (2001) and Wolf (2001). The neutron polarizability difference result from quasi-free Compton scattering (Kossert 2002) agrees with the earlier neutron scattering in a Coulomb field experiment by Schmiedmayer (1991), but the quasi-free method is argued to be better tested through proton control experiments. The results enable improved tests of chiral perturbation theory predictions, which currently agree with data only at leading order (Bernard 1995) but disagree when the Delta degree of freedom is included (Griesshammer 2002).
