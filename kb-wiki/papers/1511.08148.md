# A Hydrodynamical Study on the Conversion of Hadronic Matter to Quark Matter: I. Shock-Induced Conversion

> We study transitions of hadronic matter (HM) to 3-flavor quark matter (3QM) locally, regarding the conversion processes as combustion and describing them hydrodynamically. Not only the jump condition on both sides of the conversion front but the structures inside the front are also considered by taking into account what happens during the conversion processes on the time scale of weak interactions as well as equations of state (EOS's) in the mixed phase. Under the assumption that HM is metastable with their free energies being larger than those of 3QM but smaller than those of 2-flavor quark matter (2QM), we consider the transition via 2QM triggered by a rapid density rise in a shock wave. Based on the results, we discuss which combustion modes (strong/weak detonation) may be realized. HM is described by an EOS based on the relativistic mean field theory and 2, 3QM's are approximated by the MIT bag model. We demonstrate for a wide range of bag constant and strong coupling constant in this combination of EOS's that the combustion may occur in the so-called endothermic regime, in which the Hugoniot curve for combustion runs below the one for the shock wave in P-V plane, and which has no terrestrial counter part. We find that strong detonation always occurs. Depending on the EOS of quark matter (QM) as well as the density of HM and the Mach number of the detonation front, deconfinement from HM to 2QM is either completed or not completed in the shock wave. In the latter case, which is more likely if the EOS of QM ensures that deconfinement occurs above the nuclear saturation density and that the maximum mass of cold quark stars is larger than two solar mass, the conversion continues further via the mixing state of HM and 3QM on the time scale of weak interactions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for a wide range of bag constant and strong coupling constant, the shock-induced conversion of hadronic matter to 3-flavor quark matter occurs in the so-called endothermic regime (where the Hugoniot curve for combustion runs below the one for the shock wave in the pressure-volume plane), which has no terrestrial counterpart, and that strong detonation always occurs in this scenario.

## Method

The authors model the conversion of hadronic matter to 3-flavor quark matter as a combustion process, solving the Rankine-Hugoniot jump conditions across the conversion front while also considering the internal structure of the front using equations of state for the mixed phase. Hadronic matter is described by an equation of state based on relativistic mean field theory, while 2-flavor and 3-flavor quark matter are approximated by the MIT bag model. The analysis is local, focusing only on the conversion front region, and considers the time scales of strong interactions for deconfinement and weak interactions for beta-equilibration to strange quarks.

## Key numbers

- Initial hadronic matter density for cold neutron star matter: 5.6 x 10^14 g/cm^3 at T = 0 MeV
- Initial hadronic matter density for proto-neutron star matter: 3.0 x 10^14 g/cm^3 at T = 10 MeV with lepton fraction Y_lep = 0.3
- Bag constant values considered: B^(1/4) = 125, 130, and 140 MeV
- Strong coupling constant values considered: alpha_s = 0.6 and 0.8
- The beta-equilibration time scale for conversion from 2-flavor to 3-flavor quark matter is approximately 10^-8 s
- The width of the conversion region is estimated to be no larger than approximately 10^2 cm
- The maximum mass of cold quark stars for which incomplete deconfinement is more likely is larger than 2 solar masses

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Olinto (1998), Heiselberg (1992), and Benvenuto et al. (1989) on the conversion of neutron stars to quark stars, but differs by considering the internal structure of the conversion front rather than only the jump conditions. The paper challenges the assumption made by Cho et al. (1994) and Lugones et al. (1994) that combustion can only occur in the exothermic regime, arguing that the endothermic regime is physically possible because the free energy of 3-flavor quark matter is always lower than that of hadronic matter. The results enable future global simulations of neutron star conversion that incorporate the detailed front structures identified here.
