# Thermally Fluctuating Second-Order Viscous Hydrodynamics and Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The fluctuation-dissipation theorem requires the presence of thermal noise in viscous fluids. The time and length scales of heavy ion collisions are small enough so that the thermal noise can have a measurable effect on observables. Thermal noise is included in numerical simulations of high energy lead-lead collisions, increasing average values of the momentum eccentricity and contributing to its event by event fluctuations.

## Key claim

Thermal noise in second-order viscous hydrodynamics, treated perturbatively, measurably shifts and broadens the distribution of the momentum eccentricity in Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies, but the effect on single-particle spectra vanishes when averaged over many events because the ensemble average of the fluctuating parts of the energy-momentum tensor is zero.

## Method

The authors extended the 3+1 dimensional second-order viscous code MUSIC by linearizing the Israel-Stewart equations around a noise-less background and adding a stochastic noise term with autocorrelation proportional to the shear and bulk viscosities and temperature. They solved the coupled equations for the fluctuating parts of the ideal and viscous energy-momentum tensors using the MacCormack predictor-corrector method, then extracted the momentum eccentricity as a proxy for elliptic flow from 200 events at fixed impact parameter.

## Key numbers

* For Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies with impact parameter b = 6.0 fm (10-20% centrality class), inclusion of thermal noise increased the average momentum eccentricity by about 5% relative to the noiseless case.
* The simulation used transverse cell dimensions of Δx = Δy = 45/128 fm ≈ 0.35 fm, with evolution starting at τ_i = 0.4 fm/c and freeze-out completed by τ_f = 14.9 fm/c.
* For ultra-central collisions with b = 0 fm, the average momentum eccentricity became non-zero due to thermal noise, whereas it would be exactly zero in the noiseless case.
* The variance of v_2 in mid-central event classes from ATLAS data is approximately 0.05, providing a scale for comparison with the thermal noise contribution.

## Context

This work extends the linearized fluctuating hydrodynamics approach first implemented in a 1+1 dimensional Bjorken model by Kapusta (2011) to a full 3+1 dimensional simulation using the MUSIC code of Schenke (2010). It contrasts with approaches that treat initial-state fluctuations as the dominant source of event-by-event flow harmonics, as in Schenke (2013). The results suggest that combining thermal fluctuations with initial-state fluctuations could help explain the ultra-central CMS 0-0.2% centrality class data, where average v_n values are comparable to the thermal-noise-only results, potentially enabling an independent measurement of the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio.
