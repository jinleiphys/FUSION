# Moscow-type NN-potentials and three-nucleon bound states

> A detailed description of Moscow-type (M-type) potential models for the NN interaction is given. The microscopic foundation of these models, which appear as a consequence of the composite quark structure of nucleons, is discussed. M-type models are shown to arise naturally in a coupled channel approach when compound or bag-like six-quark states, strongly coupled to the NN channel, are eliminated from the complete multiquark wave function. The role of the deep-lying bound states that appear in these models is elucidated. By introducing additional conditions of orthogonality to these compound six-quark states, a continuous series of almost on-shell equivalent nonlocal interaction models, characterized by a strong reduction or full absence of a local repulsive core (M-type models), is generated. The predictions of these interaction models for 3N systems are analyzed in detail. It is shown that M-type models give, under certain conditions, a stronger binding of the 3N system than the original phase-equivalent model with nodeless wave functions. An analysis of the 3N system with the new versions of the Moscow NN potential describing also the higher even partial waves is presented. Large deviations from conventional NN force models are found for the momentum distribution in the high momentum region. In particular, the Coulomb displacement energy for nuclei ^3He - ^3H displays a promising agreement with experiment when the ^3H binding energy is extrapolated to the experimental value.

## Key claim

Moscow-type (M-type) NN potential models, which arise from eliminating six-quark bag states coupled to the NN channel, produce three-nucleon bound states with stronger binding than conventional phase-equivalent models with nodeless wave functions, but only under the condition that the additional orthogonality constraints to compound six-quark states are properly imposed.

## Method

The authors derive M-type potentials by starting from a six-quark Hamiltonian with one-gluon exchange, confinement, and one-meson exchange, then separating the total antisymmetric six-quark wave function into mutually orthogonal bag-like (six-quark) and cluster (NN) components. They eliminate the bag-like states using the Feshbach projection method, which introduces orthogonality conditions that produce internal nodes in the NN relative wave function at small distances (approximately 0.6 fm for S-waves). The resulting effective NN potential has a deep attractive well (approximately 1 GeV) with strongly reduced or absent local repulsive core, and the authors test this model by solving the three-nucleon bound-state problem for the 3H and 3He systems.

## Key numbers

- The node position for relative S-waves is located at approximately 0.6 fm, and for P-waves at approximately 0.9 fm, determined by the deep attractive well depth of about 1 GeV
- The Coulomb displacement energy between 3He and 3H, when the 3H binding energy is extrapolated to the experimental value, yields ΔE_C approximately 740 keV, showing promising agreement with experiment
- The Malfliet-Tjon MT-V potential used as a test case has parameters: g_R = 1458.05 MeV, g_A = -578.09 MeV, μ_R = 3.11 fm, μ_A = 1.55 fm, giving a deuteron binding energy of -0.4136 MeV
- The orthogonality condition fit works for Gaussian function radii r_0 in the interval 0.15 to 0.5 fm, with the local core disappearing already at r_0 = 0.2 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on the Moscow NN potential (Kukulin 1994, 1992, 1986) and the quark compound bag model of Simonov (1981), providing a microscopic foundation based on six-quark permutational symmetries. The approach contrasts with conventional one-boson-exchange models (Machleidt 1989, 1993) that rely on a local repulsive core from vector meson exchange, and instead shows that M-type potentials are related to the supersymmetric partner of the Reid-Soft-Core potential (Michel 1988, Baye 1987). The results enable future studies of many-nucleon systems using a simple nonlocal potential that incorporates quark degrees of freedom without explicit six-quark calculations, and suggest that the description of three-nucleon systems must be supplemented with an additional attractive six-quark-plus-nucleon contribution.
