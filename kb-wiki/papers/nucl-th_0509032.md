# Few-Nucleon Forces and Systems in Chiral Effective Field Theory

> We outline the structure of the nuclear force in the framework of chiral effective field theory of QCD and review recent applications to processes involving few nucleons.

## Key claim

Chiral effective field theory provides a systematic, model-independent framework for deriving nuclear forces from QCD's approximate chiral symmetry, but the nonperturbative nature of few-nucleon systems requires applying the EFT to the kernel of scattering equations (the effective potential) rather than directly to scattering amplitudes, and the power counting becomes significantly more complicated than in the meson or single-nucleon sectors due to the presence of shallow bound states.

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of nuclear forces using chiral perturbation theory (CHPT) extended to few-nucleon systems via Weinberg's proposal: apply the EFT expansion to the effective nuclear potential (the kernel of the Lippmann-Schwinger or Schrödinger equation), then solve the resulting nonperturbative equation. The heavy-baryon formalism is employed to handle the large nucleon mass scale, with the chiral Lagrangian organized in powers of a soft scale Q (pion mass or external momenta) relative to the chiral symmetry breaking scale Λχ ∼ 770 MeV or 4πFπ.

## Key numbers

- The scalar quark condensate, an order parameter of spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking, is Σ = −(262 ± 12 MeV)³ (MS scheme at μ = 2 GeV)
- The isoscalar S-wave ππ scattering length from two-loop CHPT combined with Roy equations is a₀⁰ = 0.220 ± 0.005; the E865 experiment measured a₀⁰ = 0.216 ± 0.013(stat) ± 0.002(syst) ± 0.002(theor)
- The leading-order Weinberg prediction for a₀⁰ was 0.16; the next-to-leading order Gasser-Leutwyler value was 0.20
- The pion decay constant is Fπ = 92.4 MeV
- The up and down quark masses in the MS scheme at μ = 1 GeV are mu ∼ 5 MeV and md ∼ 9 MeV

## Context

This review extends the chiral EFT approach originally developed for meson and single-nucleon sectors by Gasser and Leutwyler (1983, 1984) and Weinberg (1978) to the few-nucleon sector following Weinberg's generalization (1990, 1991). It contrasts with phenomenological boson-exchange models (Yukawa, 1935) and modern phenomenological NN potentials by providing a systematic, improvable connection to QCD. The work enables consistent construction of many-body forces and nuclear current operators for electroweak probes, which cannot be achieved in phenomenological approaches lacking a systematic power counting.
