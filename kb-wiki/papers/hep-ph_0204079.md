# The Crystallography of Color Superconductivity

> We develop the Ginzburg-Landau approach to comparing different possible crystal structures for the crystalline color superconducting phase of QCD, the QCD incarnation of the Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell phase. In this phase, quarks of different flavor with differing Fermi momenta form Cooper pairs with nonzero total momentum, yielding a condensate that varies in space like a sum of plane waves. We work at zero temperature, as is relevant for compact star physics. The Ginzburg-Landau approach predicts a strong first-order phase transition (as a function of the chemical potential difference between quarks) and for this reason is not under quantitative control. Nevertheless, by organizing the comparison between different possible arrangements of plane waves (i.e. different crystal structures) it provides considerable qualitative insight into what makes a crystal structure favorable. Together, the qualitative insights and the quantitative, but not controlled, calculations make a compelling case that the favored pairing pattern yields a condensate which is a sum of eight plane waves forming a face-centered cubic structure. They also predict that the phase is quite robust, with gaps comparable in magnitude to the BCS gap that would form if the Fermi momenta were degenerate. These predictions may be tested in ultracold gases made of fermionic atoms. In a QCD context, our results lay the foundation for a calculation of vortex pinning in a crystalline color superconductor, and thus for the analysis of pulsar glitches that may originate within the core of a compact star.

## Key claim

The Ginzburg-Landau approach predicts a strong first-order phase transition (as a function of the chemical potential difference between quarks) and for this reason is not under quantitative control; nevertheless, the qualitative insights and quantitative but uncontrolled calculations make a compelling case that the favored pairing pattern yields a condensate which is a sum of eight plane waves forming a face-centered cubic structure.

## Method

The authors develop a Ginzburg-Landau expansion to order Δ⁶ for a crystal made up of the sum of arbitrarily many plane waves, all with the same |q| = q₀ but with different patterns of wavevector directions, working at zero temperature in a toy model with two massless flavors of quarks and a pointlike interaction mimicking single-gluon exchange. They compare different possible arrangements of plane waves (crystal structures) by evaluating the free energy expansion coefficients, focusing on the dependence of the condensate on position and momenta while simplifying the color-flavor pattern to involve only u and d quarks with Fermi momenta split by a chemical potential difference δμ.

## Key numbers

- The single-plane-wave LOFF phase is favored over BCS and over no pairing in the range δμ₁ = Δ₀/√2 ≈ 0.707 Δ₀ to δμ₂ ≈ 0.754 Δ₀.
- At the second-order phase transition at δμ₂, the wavevector magnitude is q₀ ≈ 0.90 Δ₀ ≈ 1.2 δμ₂.
- The opening angle characterizing the pairing rings on the Fermi surfaces is ψ₀ = 2 cos⁻¹(δμ/|q|) ≈ 67.1°.
- The maximal number of nonintersecting rings with opening angle 67.1° that fit on a sphere is nine.

## Context

This work extends the Larkin-Ovchinnikov-Fulde-Ferrell (LOFF) phase concept from condensed matter physics to QCD, building on prior studies of crystalline color superconductivity by Bowers (2001), and analyses at asymptotically high densities by others (2001, 2002). The results lay the foundation for calculating vortex pinning in a crystalline color superconductor, enabling analysis of pulsar glitches that may originate within the core of a compact star, and the predictions may also be tested in ultracold gases of fermionic atoms.
