# Detecting the third family of compact stars with normalizing flows

> We explore the anomaly detection framework based on Normalizing Flows (NF) models introduced in \cite{PhysRevC.106.065802} to detect the presence of a large (destabilising) dense matter phase transition in neutron star (NS) observations of masses and radii, and relate the feasibility of detection with parameters of the underlying mass-radius sequence, which is a functional of the dense matter equation of state. Once trained on simulated data featuring continuous $M(R)$ solutions (i.e., no phase transitions), NF is used to determine the likelihood of a first-order phase transition in a given set of $M(R)$ observations featuring a discontinuity, i.e., perform the anomaly detection. Different mock test sets, featuring two branch solutions in the $M(R)$ diagram, were parameterized by the NS mass at which the phase transition occurs, $M_c$, and the radius difference between the heaviest hadronic star and lightest hybrid star, $ΔR$. We analyze the impact of these parameters on the NF performance in detecting the presence of a first-order phase transition. Among the results, we report that given a set of 15 stars with radius uncertainty of $0.2$ km, a detection of a two-branch solution is possible with 95\% accuracy if $ΔR > 0.4$ km.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a Normalizing Flows (NF) model trained on simulated neutron star mass-radius data without phase transitions can detect the presence of a first-order phase transition (manifesting as a disconnected hybrid star branch) with 95% accuracy, but only when the radius gap between the heaviest hadronic star and the lightest hybrid star exceeds 0.4 km for a set of 15 stars with 0.2 km radius uncertainty; the detectability depends strongly on the critical mass at which the transition occurs and the observational noise level.

## Method

The authors constructed mock mass-radius datasets from 25,287 relativistic mean field hadronic equations of state, then artificially created two-branch solutions by shifting the upper portion of the mass-radius curve by a radius difference ΔR at a chosen critical mass Mc. They trained coupling neural spline flow models on single-branch (hadronic-only) data with varying noise levels (Set 0: no noise; Set 1: σR=0.2 km, σM=0.1 M⊙; Set 2: σR=0.626 km, σM=0.136 M⊙) and input sizes of N=5 or N=15 stars per observation set. After training, they evaluated anomaly detection by computing the negative log-likelihood of test samples containing two-branch solutions and classifying anomalies using a threshold set at 1% false positive rate on the hadronic test sets.

## Key numbers

- With 15 stars and radius uncertainty 0.2 km (Set 1), detection of a two-branch solution is possible with 95% accuracy if ΔR > 0.4 km (for Mc=1.5 M⊙)
- For Set 0 (no noise) with 15 stars and Mc=1.5 M⊙, 100% detection accuracy is achieved for ΔR > 0.4 km
- For Set 1 with 15 stars and Mc=1.5 M⊙, 100% detection accuracy requires ΔR > 0.6 km
- For Set 2 (largest noise) with 15 stars and Mc=1.5 M⊙, 100% detection accuracy requires ΔR > 0.8 km
- For Mc=1.8 M⊙ with 15 stars, 100% detection requires ΔR > 1 km (Set 0), ΔR > 1.6 km (Set 1), and ΔR > 2.4 km (Set 2)
- The training dataset consisted of 25,287 nuclear models based on relativistic mean field descriptions
- Mock observation sets contained ns=120 samples for training and ns=1 sample for testing
- The NF architecture used 7 coupling transformations for N=5 and 10 for N=15, with 3 hidden layers of 15 neurons each

## Context

This work extends the NF-based anomaly detection framework introduced by PhysRevC.106.065802 (2022) for detecting phase transitions in neutron star mass-radius observations, moving from specific hybrid equations of state to generic mass-radius diagrams parameterized by Mc and ΔR. The approach contrasts with the work of Brandes (2024) who combined NF with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo for full equation of state posterior inference. The results enable future studies to incorporate gravitational-wave tidal deformability measurements (as suggested by Soma (2023) and Raithel (2022)) and to quantify uncertainties using Bayesian neural networks within the NF framework, as explored in prior work by Carvalho (2023, 2024).
