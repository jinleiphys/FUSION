# A Further Measurement of the beta-Delayed alpha-Particle Emission of 16N

> We measured the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission spectrum of 16N with a sensitivity for beta-decay branching ratios of the order of 10-10. The 16N nuclei were produced using the d(15N,16N)p reaction with 70 MeV 15N beams and a deuterium gas target 7.5 cm long at a pressure of 1250 torr. The 16N nuclei were collected (over 10 s) using a thin aluminum foil with an areal density of 180 mu g/cm2 tilted at 7 Deg with respect to the beam. The activity was transferred to the counting area by means of a stepping motor in less than 3 s with the counting carried out over 8 s. The beta-delayed alpha-particles were measured using a time of flight method to achieve a sufficiently low background. Standard calibration sources (148Gd, 241Am, 208,209Po, and 227Ac) as well as alpha-particles and 7Li from the 10B(n,alpha)7Li reaction were used for an accurate energy calibration. The energy resolution of the catcher foil (180-220 keV) was calculated and the time of flight resolution (3-10 nsec) was measured using the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission from 8Li that was produced using the d(7Li,8Li)p reaction with the same setup. The line shape was corrected to account for the variation in the energy and time resolution and a high statistics spectrum of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N is reported. However, our data (as well as earlier Mainz data and unpublished Seattle data) do not agree with an earlier measurement of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N taken at TRIUMF after averaging over the energy resolution of our collection system. This disagreement, amongst other issues, prohibits accurate inclusion of the f-wave component in the R-matrix analysis.

## Key claim

The authors report a high-statistics measurement of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission spectrum of 16N, but their data (as well as earlier Mainz data and unpublished Seattle data) disagree with an earlier measurement taken at TRIUMF after averaging over the energy resolution of the collection system, and this disagreement prohibits accurate inclusion of the f-wave component in the R-matrix analysis.

## Method

The 16N nuclei were produced using the d(15N,16N)p reaction with 70 MeV 15N beams and a deuterium gas target 7.5 cm long at a pressure of 1250 torr, then collected over 10 s using a thin aluminum foil with an areal density of 180 microgram/cm2 tilted at 7 degrees with respect to the beam. The activity was transferred to the counting area by a stepping motor in less than 3 s with counting carried out over 8 s, and beta-delayed alpha-particles were measured using a time-of-flight method to achieve low background. The energy resolution of the catcher foil (180-220 keV) was calculated and the time-of-flight resolution (3-10 nsec) was measured using beta-delayed alpha-particle emission from 8Li produced using the d(7Li,8Li)p reaction with the same setup.

## Key numbers

* Sensitivity for beta-decay branching ratios of the order of 10^-10
* 16N produced with 70 MeV 15N beams and deuterium gas target at 1250 torr pressure
* Catcher foil areal density of 180 microgram/cm2 tilted at 7 degrees
* Collection time of 10 s, transfer time less than 3 s, counting time of 8 s
* Energy resolution of catcher foil: 180-220 keV
* Time-of-flight resolution: 3-10 nsec
* Approximately 1.3 million alpha-particles in singles obtained in the final spectrum
* Chi-squared per data point of 1.4 when comparing Yale data to Seattle averaged fit curve
* Chi-squared per data point of 7.2 when comparing Yale data to TRIUMF averaged fit curve
* Chi-squared per data point of 2.5 when comparing Mainz data to Seattle fit curve
* Chi-squared per data point of 123 when comparing Mainz data to TRIUMF fit curve

## Context

This work extends earlier measurements of the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N from Mainz (Neubeck et al., 1974), TRIUMF (Azuma et al., 1994), Yale (Zhao et al., 1993), and Seattle (Zhao et al., 1995, unpublished), and is a continuation and improvement of the original Yale-UConn experiment. The measurement aims to constrain the 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction cross section, which is critical for understanding the carbon/oxygen ratio at the end of stellar helium burning and its impact on type II and type Ia supernovae. The disagreement between data sets prevents precise determination of the f-wave component in the R-matrix analysis, which in turn limits the extraction of the p-wave contribution to the 16N spectrum that is relevant for the astrophysical reaction.
