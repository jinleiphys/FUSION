# Disentangling Jet Modification in Jet Simulations and in Z+Jet Data

> We study the impact of selection biases on jet structure and substructure observables and separate these effects from effects caused by jet quenching. We use the angular separation $ΔR$ of the hardest splitting in a jet as the primary example observable. We first conduct a simplified Monte Carlo study in which it is possible to identify the same jet after quenching in a heavy ion collision and as it would have been if it had formed in vacuum. We select a sample of jets by placing a cut on their quenched $p_T$ and, as is possible only in a Monte Carlo study, compare to the same jets unquenched, and see that the $ΔR$ distribution seems to be unmodified. However, if we select a sample of jets formed in vacuum by placing a cut on their unquenched $p_T$ and compare to those same jets after quenching, we see a significant enhancement in the number of jets with large $ΔR$, primarily due to the soft particles in the jet that originate from the wake in the droplet of quark-gluon plasma excited by the parton shower. We confirm that the jets contributing to this enhancement are those jets which lost the most energy, which were not included in the sample selected after quenching; jets selected after quenching are those which lose a small fraction of their energy. Next, we employ a method that is available to experimentalists: in a sample of jets with a recoiling $Z$ boson, we show that selecting jets based on the jet $p_T$ after quenching yields a $ΔR$ distribution that appears unmodified while selecting a sample of jets produced in association with a $Z$ boson whose (unmodified) $p_T$ is above some cut yields a significant enhancement in the number of jets with large $ΔR$. We again confirm that this is due to particles from the wake, and that the jets contributing to this enhancement are those which have lost a significant fraction of their energy.

### Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the apparent modification of jet substructure observables like the groomed splitting angle ΔR is dominated by selection bias: when jets are selected by their quenched transverse momentum (as in inclusive heavy-ion measurements), the sample is biased toward jets that lose little energy, making quenching effects nearly invisible; only when selection bias is removed, by selecting jets based on the unmodified pT of a recoiling Z boson, does a substantial enhancement at large ΔR appear, primarily from wake particles in the quark-gluon plasma.

### Method
The authors used the hybrid model of jet quenching, where Pythia8-generated parton showers are embedded in a boost-invariant hydrodynamic medium with a single free parameter κ_sc = 0.404 controlling energy loss. For inclusive jet samples (without nuclear PDFs), they matched each quenched jet to its unquenched counterpart by proximity in (η, φ), enabling a direct comparison of the same jet with and without medium effects. For the experimentally realizable analysis, they used Z+jet events (with nuclear PDFs included) and compared samples selected either by jet pT after quenching or by the Z boson pT (which is unmodified by the plasma). Jets were reconstructed with the anti-kT algorithm (R=0.4) and groomed using the soft drop algorithm with z_cut=0.1 and β=0.

### Key numbers
- Jet selection cut: pT_cut = 80 GeV for both quenched/unquenched and Z+jet samples.
- Inclusive jet sample: initial hard parton scattering with pT > 50 GeV; Z+jet sample generated for hard processes with pT > 5 GeV with a pT^{-5} weighting.
- Jet radius parameter: R = 0.4; jet pseudorapidity requirement: |η| < 1.8 (inclusive) or |η_Z| < 2.4 (Z+jet).
- Hybrid model parameter: κ_sc = 0.404, the best-fit value for describing hadron and jet suppression at T = 145 MeV.
- Collision energy: √s_NN = 5.02 TeV for PbPb collisions; hydrodynamic medium corresponds to 0–5% most central collisions.
- Matching requirement: ΔR between quenched and unquenched jet ≤ 0.4; all jets required to have pT > 30 GeV.

### Context
This work extends the study of selection biases in jet quenching phenomenology, building on prior analyses by Milhano (2015), Casalderrey-Solana (2016), and Brewer (2017, 2018) that showed how selecting jets by their quenched pT biases toward jets that lose little energy. It contrasts with inclusive jet measurements (e.g., by ATLAS and CMS) and dijet studies, where such biases are harder to remove. The paper shows that Z+jet events, as measured by CMS (2017, 2018) and ATLAS (2018, 2019), provide a practical way to eliminate this bias experimentally. The results enable future experimental comparisons of groomed jet observables in Z+jet events to disentangle genuine medium modification from selection effects, and suggest that varying grooming parameters can control the contribution of medium response.
