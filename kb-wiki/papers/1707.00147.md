# Equilibrium nuclear ensembles taking into account vaporization of hot nuclei in dense stellar matter

> We investigate the high-temperature effect on the nuclear matter that consists of mixture of nucleons and all nuclei in the dense and hot stellar environment. The individual nuclei are described within the compressible liquid-drop model that is based on Skyrme interactions for bulk energies and that takes into account modifications of the surface and Coulomb energies at finite temperatures and densities. The free-energy density is minimized with respect to the individual equilibrium densities of all heavy nuclei and the nuclear composition. We find that their optimized equilibrium densities become smaller and smaller at high temperatures because of the increase of thermal contributions to bulk free energies and the reduction of surface energies. The neutron-rich nuclei become unstable and disappear one after another at some temperatures. The calculations are performed for two sets of model parameters leading to different values of the slope parameter in the nuclear symmetry energy. It is found that the larger slope parameter reduces the equilibrium densities and the melting temperatures. We also compare the new model with some other approaches and find that the mass fractions of heavy nuclei in the previous calculations that omit vaporization are underestimated at $T \lesssim 10$MeV and overestimated at $T\gtrsim10$~MeV. The further sophistication of calculations of nuclear vaporization and of light clusters would be required to construct the equation of state for explosive astrophysical phenomena.

## Key claim

The authors find that at high temperatures (above approximately 5–10 MeV), neutron-rich nuclei in a dense stellar environment become unstable and disappear one by one due to vaporization, a process that previous multi-nucleus approximation (MNA) equations of state omitted; the mass fractions of heavy nuclei in those earlier calculations are underestimated at temperatures below about 10 MeV and overestimated above about 10 MeV.

## Method

The free-energy density of a mixture of nucleons and all nuclei (heavy nuclei with atomic numbers 6–2000 and light clusters with Z ≤ 5 or N ≤ 5) is minimized with respect to both the number densities of each species and the individual equilibrium densities of each heavy nucleus. Heavy nuclei are described by a compressible liquid-drop model using Skyrme-type interactions for bulk energies, with surface and Coulomb energies modified at finite temperatures and densities; two parameter sets (B and E) with different values of the slope parameter L in the nuclear symmetry energy are compared.

## Key numbers

- The calculations are performed for baryon densities of 0.1 n₀ and 0.3 n₀, and proton fractions Yp = 0.2 and 0.4, typical of supernova cores.
- For the parameter set E at Yp = 0.2, nB = 0.3 n₀, nuclei with (N,Z) = (60,20) and (30,10) are populated at T = 3 and 5 MeV but disappear at T = 10 MeV.
- The critical temperature Tc for bulk matter with charge fraction x = 0.25 is about 14 MeV, yet nuclei with that asymmetry vanish at T = 10 MeV due to surface-energy reductions.
- For the specific nucleus ⁵⁰Ca (Z/A ≈ 0.40), vaporization occurs around T = 13 MeV at nB = 0.1 n₀ and around T = 11–12 MeV at nB = 0.3 n₀.
- The average equilibrium density increases slightly around T = 3 MeV as lighter nuclei (A ≲ 100) replace heavier ones (A ≳ 100), then decreases above T ∼ 5 MeV.
- The larger slope parameter L (set B) gives smaller equilibrium densities and lower melting temperatures compared to set E.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous self-consistent multi-nucleus description (Furusawa and Mishustin, 2017) from low temperatures (below 3 MeV) to higher temperatures up to about 15 MeV, replacing a simple parametric bulk-energy formula with a Skyrme-type mean-field calculation. It contrasts with earlier MNA equations of state such as SMSM (Botvina et al., 2004, 2010), HS (Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich, 2010), and FYSS (Furusawa et al., 2011, 2013, 2017), which used incompressible liquid-drop models and omitted the optimization of individual nuclear equilibrium densities; it also differs from the work of Gulminelli and Oertel (2015), who studied nuclear composition in a multi-nucleus description without optimizing equilibrium densities. The results enable more realistic equations of state for explosive astrophysical phenomena such as core-collapse supernovae and compact-star mergers, though the authors note that further sophistication of nuclear vaporization and light-cluster calculations is required.
