# JOZSO, a renewed version of the computer code GAMOW for calculating broad neutron resonances in phenomenological nuclear potentials

> A renewed version of the computer code GAMOW ~\cite{[Ve82]} is given in which the difficulties in calculating broad neutron resonances are amended. New types of phenomenological neutron potentials with strict finite range are built in. Landscape of the S-matrix can be generated on a given domain of the complex wave number plane and S-matrix poles in the domain are localized. Normalized Gamow wave functions and trajectories of given poles can be calculated optionally.

# JOZSO, a renewed version of the computer code GAMOW for calculating broad neutron resonances in phenomenological nuclear potentials

## Key claim

The JOZSO program amends the difficulties in calculating broad neutron resonances that plagued its predecessor GAMOW, specifically by replacing the single-point matching of logarithmic derivatives with a summed modulus minimization over a radial interval, and by incorporating new strictly finite-range potential forms. The improvement is conditional on the user selecting appropriate potential types and matching intervals, and the program is restricted to the lower half of the complex wave number plane (decaying resonances), with poles in the upper half requiring the separate codes GAMOW or ANTI.

## Method

The program solves the radial Schrödinger equation for spherically symmetric, strictly finite-range neutron potentials using adaptive step-size Runge-Kutta-Fehlberg integration (Cash-Karp parameters). Internal solutions are propagated outward from the origin (using a power series expansion for the first points to handle centrifugal and spin-orbit singularities), and external solutions are propagated inward from the asymptotic region using outgoing Riccati-Hankel functions. The pole of the S-matrix is found by minimizing the sum of the moduli of the differences between internal and external logarithmic derivatives over a radial interval (rather than at a single matching point), using the Nelder-Mead algorithm. Normalized Gamow wave functions and pole trajectories as a function of potential strength can be calculated optionally.

## Key numbers

- The program calculates S-matrix poles in the complex wave number plane; pole positions agree with those given in Ixaru et al. (1995) to 3-4 decimal digits.
- The power series expansion for the internal solution near the origin uses up to 50 terms (j=0 to 50) in the form u_i(r,k) = sum a_j r^(l+1+j).
- The matching interval for the logarithmic derivative comparison is selected where the potential falls to approximately V_or/10 (lower bound r_1) and V_or/1000 (upper bound r_2), where V_or is the potential value close to the origin.
- The external contribution to the squared norm is given in closed form as N_e^2 = (-R_as F_c^2 / 2) [H_l^+(k_o R_as)^2 + H_{l+1}^+(k_o R_as)^2 - ((2l+1)/(k_o R_as)) H_l^+(k_o R_as) H_{l+1}^+(k_o R_as)].
- The conversion factor between energy in MeV and wave number squared in fm^{-2} is c_1 = 2μ/ħ^2, where μ is the reduced mass of the projectile-target system.

## Context

This work extends the capabilities of the GAMOW code (Vertse et al., 1982) and the ANTI code (Ixaru et al., 1995) by addressing their difficulties with broad neutron resonances, particularly for large real parts of the wave number. The new code incorporates strictly finite-range potential forms including the cut-off Woods-Saxon (CWS), the generalized Woods-Saxon (GWS), the SV potential (Sas and Vertse, 2008), and the SS potential (Sahu and Sahu, 2012), which have smooth compact support and avoid the cut-off radius dependence problems identified for broad resonances in CWS potentials (Andersson et al., 2011; Deltuva, 2012). The trajectory calculation method enables systematic study of how poles move in the complex k-plane as potential strength varies, as demonstrated in Sas et al. (2015) and Baran et al. (2015, 2017).
