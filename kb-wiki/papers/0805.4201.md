# Bulk viscosity and spectral functions in QCD

> We examine the behavior of the spectral function for the trace of the stress tensor in QCD in the two regimes where it is possible to make analytical progress; weak coupling, and close to a second order QCD phase transition. We determine the behavior of the bulk viscosity in each regime. We discuss the problem of analytic continuation of the (lattice) Euclidean correlation function to determine the spectral function. In each case the spectral function has a narrow peak at small frequency; its shape would be challenging to extract accurately from lattice data with error bars.

## Key claim

The spectral function for the trace of the stress tensor in QCD has a narrow peak at small frequency in both the weak-coupling regime and near a second-order phase transition; this shape would be challenging to extract accurately from lattice data with error bars, and the authors argue that the Kharzeev-Tuchin sum rule (2008) is inconsistent with perturbative results unless subtractions are applied on both sides.

## Method

The authors compute the spectral function \(\rho(\omega)\) for the \(T^\mu_\mu\) operator in QCD using two analytic approaches. At weak coupling (high temperature), they evaluate the leading-order two-gluon cut diagram and the pole contribution from thermal gluons with plasma dispersion, extending the zero-frequency bulk viscosity calculation of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2001) to nonzero frequencies via kinetic theory and a Boltzmann equation solved variationally. Near a second-order phase transition, they apply universality arguments and dynamical scaling from the liquid-gas system, adapting Onuki's (1997) analysis of bulk viscosity to QCD by mapping the Ising model variables to temperature and chemical potential.

## Key numbers

* At weak coupling, the height of the peak in \(\rho/\omega\) is of order \(\alpha_s^2 T^3\) and the area under the peak is \(\int d\omega \, \rho/\omega \sim \alpha_s^{7/2} T^4\).
* The width of the peak at weak coupling is parametrically \(\Gamma \sim g^4 T^3/p^2\) for hard particles, with the dominant contribution to the height coming from hard \(p \sim T\) particles.
* Near the critical point, the bulk viscosity diverges as \(\zeta \propto t^{-z\nu+\alpha}\) along the crossover line, where \(t\) is the distance in the \(T,\mu\) plane from the critical point, \(\nu = 0.630\) is the correlation length exponent, \(\alpha = 0.110\) is the heat capacity exponent, and \(z \simeq 3\) is the dynamical critical exponent.
* The Wightman function near the critical point scales as \(G^>(\omega) \sim T^3 (\omega/T)^{-1+\alpha/z\nu}\) for frequencies \(\omega > \xi^{-z} T^{1-z}\).

## Context

This work extends the perturbative bulk viscosity calculation of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2001) to nonzero frequencies and generalizes the dynamical critical analysis of Onuki (1997) from the liquid-gas system to QCD. It directly contradicts the sum-rule approach of Kharzeev and Tuchin (2008), showing that their claimed relation would give a parametrically different result (\(\mathcal{O}(g^6 T^4)\) versus \(\mathcal{O}(g^7 T^4)\)) at weak coupling. The results enable a better understanding of the challenges in extracting the bulk viscosity from lattice Euclidean correlation functions, as the narrow low-frequency peak in the spectral function is difficult to resolve with finite error bars.
