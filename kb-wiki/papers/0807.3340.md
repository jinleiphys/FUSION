# From AGS-SPS and Onwards to the LHC

> I review the history of the efforts using heavy ion collisions to make new forms of matter. I discuss both the development of the theoretical ideas about such new forms of matter, as well the past, present and planned experimental efforts. I also highlight the development of this activity in both India and China.

# From AGS-SPS and Onwards to the LHC

## Key claim
This review argues that the SPS experiments produced matter with energy density exceeding that of normal nuclear matter by more than a factor of 20, but the evidence that this matter was a Quark Gluon Plasma is only "modestly and weakly stated" in the CERN assessment document; the claim of discovery of a Quark Gluon Plasma is not supported by the SPS data alone.

## Method
The paper provides a historical review of theoretical developments (phase diagram of QCD, Color Glass Condensate, Glasma) and experimental programs (Bevalac, AGS, SPS, RHIC, and planned LHC) in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. It traces the evolution of ideas from Lee-Wick matter through lattice gauge theory computations to modern hydrodynamical models, and highlights the development of heavy ion physics communities in India and China.

## Key numbers
- Energy density achieved at SPS exceeded that of normal nuclear matter by more than a factor of 20
- RHIC hydrodynamical models require mean free paths lambda <= 0.5 Fm/c for small viscosities
- The ratio of physics excitement between LHC and RHIC is estimated as roughly equal to the ratio between RHIC and SPS
- At large N_c, energy densities scale as O(1) for hadronic phase, N_c for quarkyonic phase, and N_c^2 for deconfined phase

## Context
This review extends the historical narrative from the Bear Mountain workshop (1974) through the Bielefeld meeting (1980) and the SPS program assessed by Jacob and Heinz (2002). It contrasts the SPS results with later RHIC discoveries including large elliptic flow, jet suppression, and forward multiplicity suppression. The paper enables future work at LHC by framing open questions about the strongly interacting Quark Gluon Plasma, the Color Glass Condensate, and the phase diagram at finite baryon density. Prior work referenced includes Cabibbo and Parisi (1975) on the QCD phase diagram, Collins and Perry (1975) on asymptotic freedom in dense matter, and Bjorken (1983) on the hydrodynamical model.
