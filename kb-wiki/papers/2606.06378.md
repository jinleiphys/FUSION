# On the Possibility of a Strong First-Order Phase Transition in Neutron Stars

> Whether cold dense QCD matter undergoes a strong first-order phase transition remains an open question. In nature, neutron stars provide the most direct probe of cold dense QCD matter. Theoretically, chiral effective field theory constrains the equation of state of dense matter near nuclear saturation density, while perturbative QCD calculations constrain it at densities well beyond stable neutron-star interiors. We perform Bayesian inference with non-parametric Gaussian-process equation of state for $β$-equilibrated neutron-star matter under the assumption with and without a strong first-order phase transition, using the tidal deformability from GW170817, the NICER mass--radius measurements of PSR~J0740$+$6620, PSR~J0030$+$0451, PSR~J0437$-$4715, PSR~J0614$-$3329, chiral effective field theory, and perturbative QCD. Our results favor a strong first-order phase transition, with its onset most likely lying \emph{above} the central density of the most massive neutron star. Such an onset reconciles the stiffness required to support massive neutron stars with the softening favored by perturbative QCD from asymptotically high density.

## Key claim

The data moderately favor a strong first-order phase transition (FOPT) over a smooth equation of state, but the onset of this transition most likely lies *above* the central density of the most massive stable neutron star, meaning the transition does not occur inside any stable neutron star.

## Method

The authors performed Bayesian inference using a non-parametric Gaussian-process model for the squared sound speed of cold beta-equilibrated neutron-star matter, conditioned on chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) calculations below 1.5 times nuclear saturation density and perturbative QCD (pQCD) constraints at 25 times nuclear saturation density. They compared two hypotheses: a smooth equation of state (NPT) and one with a strong first-order phase transition (FOPT) where the sound speed vanishes over a finite density interval, using observational constraints from the GW170817 tidal deformability, NICER mass-radius measurements of four pulsars, and theoretical constraints from ChEFT and pQCD.

## Key numbers

- Bayes factor for FOPT versus NPT: 3.2 (moderate evidence on the Jeffreys scale) for termination density n_L = 25 n_0
- Bayes factor for FOPT-out (onset above central density) versus FOPT-in: 5.1 for n_L = 25 n_0
- Maximum mass under FOPT: 2.15^{+0.13}_{-0.11} solar masses (68% credible interval) for n_L = 25 n_0
- Maximum mass under NPT: 2.07^{+0.10}_{-0.08} solar masses (68% credible interval) for n_L = 25 n_0
- Radius of 1.4 solar mass neutron star under FOPT: 11.76^{+0.38}_{-0.38} km for n_L = 25 n_0
- Radius of 1.4 solar mass neutron star under NPT: 11.79^{+0.37}_{-0.41} km for n_L = 25 n_0
- Tidal deformability at 1.4 solar masses under FOPT: 309^{+88}_{-70} for n_L = 25 n_0
- Tidal deformability at 1.4 solar masses under NPT: 317^{+86}_{-75} for n_L = 25 n_0
- Median FOPT onset density: 7.35 n_0 (68% interval 5.30 to 11.85 n_0) for n_L = 25 n_0
- Median FOPT end density: 17.15 n_0 (68% interval 11.96 to 21.46 n_0) for n_L = 25 n_0
- Median central density of most massive neutron star: 6.13 n_0 under FOPT for n_L = 25 n_0
- Posterior probability of twin-star solution under FOPT: less than or equal to 0.1% for both n_L choices

## Context

This work extends Bayesian model selection studies by Pang (2021), Komoltsev (2024), Huang (2025), and Tang (2025) by allowing the FOPT onset density to range over the full ChEFT-pQCD window rather than restricting it to densities reached inside neutron stars. The finding that the FOPT onset lies above the central density of the most massive neutron star reconciles the stiffness required to support massive neutron stars with the softening favored by perturbative QCD at high density, and it agrees with earlier physics-agnostic inferences by Somasundaram (2021) and Brandes (2023) that found no sharp sound-speed drop within massive neutron-star cores. The result enables next-generation gravitational-wave observatories to test the FOPT through binary neutron-star post-merger signals, which briefly access densities beyond the central density of stable neutron stars.
