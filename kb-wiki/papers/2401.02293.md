# Shear viscosity of nucleonic matter

> The research status of the shear viscosity of nucleonic matter is reviewed. Some methods to calculate the shear viscosity of nucleonic matter are introduced, including mean free path, Green-Kubo, shear strain rate, Chapman-Enskog and relaxation time approximation. Based on these methods, results for infinite and finite nucleonic matter are discussed, which are attempts to investigate the universality of the ratio of shear viscosity over entropy density and transport characteristics like the liquid-gas phase transition in nucleonic matter. In addition, shear viscosity is also briefly discussed for the quantum chrodynamical matter produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

This review paper surveys the state of knowledge on shear viscosity in nucleonic matter, but it does not present a single new numerical result or a definitive claim; instead it compiles and compares multiple theoretical methods and their outcomes, noting that the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density may exhibit a minimum near the liquid-gas phase transition, analogous to behavior seen in water and other substances.

## Method

The paper reviews five principal methods for calculating shear viscosity in nucleonic matter: the mean free path approximation, the Green-Kubo formula (both classical and a generalized form for Fermi-Dirac systems), the shear strain rate method (SLLOD algorithm for non-equilibrium molecular dynamics), the Chapman-Enskog method (solving the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equation), and the relaxation time approximation. It discusses applications to both infinite nuclear matter and finite nuclei, using transport models such as the Improved Quantum Molecular Dynamics (ImQMD) model and the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) model, and compares results across different densities, temperatures, and isospin asymmetries.

## Key numbers

* For a unitary Fermi gas, a minimum value of the ratio \(\eta/s \simeq 0.44\) (in units of \(\hbar/k_B\)) was found at a temperature \(T/T_F \simeq 0.27\), where \(T_F\) is the Fermi temperature (Salasnich, 2011).
* The KSS bound gives a lower limit for the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density: \(\eta/s \geqslant \hbar/(4\pi k_B)\) (Kovtun, Son, and Starinets, 2005).
* In the Green-Kubo analysis of nucleonic matter using the ImQMD model without mean field, the autocorrelation function \(C(t)\) converges to zero within approximately 60 fm/c at a density of \(0.4\rho_0\) (where \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is the saturation density), and converges faster at a higher density of \(1.0\rho_0\) (Deng et al., 2021).
* For fermionic \(^3\)He, the shear viscosity decreases rapidly at the transition temperature \(T_A = 2.6\) mK (Alvesalo, 1975).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on transport coefficients in nuclear matter, such as the studies by Danielewicz (1984), Prakash et al. (1993), and Shternin et al. (2008, 2013, 2017) on neutron star crusts and cores, and by Csernai et al. (2006) on the behavior of \(\eta/s\) near phase transitions in various substances. It also connects to the extensive literature on the quark-gluon plasma from RHIC and LHC, where a near-perfect fluid with very low \(\eta/s\) was inferred (Song and Heinz, 2008; Shen et al., 2010). The paper enables future studies of the liquid-gas phase transition in nucleonic matter and the transport properties of dense baryonic matter accessible at facilities such as FAIR, GANIL, and HIAF.
