# New estimation for neutron-neutron scattering length: charge symmetry and charge independence breaking revisited

> Recent experimental results for neutron-neutron scattering length are reanalyzed from the point of view of three-nucleon force contribution. We found that the limiting value of $a_{nn}=- 15.8\pm 0.5$~fm must be free of any implicit three-body force contribution. We have also shown that the difference between the above experimental value of $a_{nn}$ and the well established value of neutron-proton scattering length $a_{np}$ can be explained by differences in the one-pion exchange potentials.

## Key claim

The authors claim that the limiting value of the neutron-neutron scattering length \(a_{nn} = -15.8 \pm 0.5\) fm, obtained by extrapolating experimental values to infinite separation distance between the neutron pair and the proton, is free of any implicit three-body force contribution. This value is significantly smaller than the widely accepted value of \(-18.9 \pm 0.4\) fm from pion capture experiments, and the authors argue that the difference between \(a_{nn}\) and the neutron-proton scattering length \(a_{np}\) can be explained solely by differences in the one-pion exchange potentials.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed eight values of \(a_{nn}\) extracted from kinematically complete breakup experiments (seven from \(n+d \to nn+p\) and one from \(d+d \to {}^2n_0+{}^2p_0\)) at different incident energies, plotting them against the distance \(R\) between the two-neutron pair and the proton (or diproton) in the intermediate state. They extrapolated the observed curve to \(R \to \infty\) to obtain an asymptotic value of \(a_{nn}\) that they argue is free of three-nucleon force contributions. They then corrected this value for the repulsive magnetic interaction between the two neutrons. Separately, they used two different \(np\) potential models (a dibaryon model and the Reid soft core potential) fitted to the well-known \(a_{np} = -23.74\) fm, replaced the \(np\) one-pion exchange potential with the \(nn\) one-pion exchange potential (accounting for differences in pion masses and coupling constants), and computed the resulting \(a_{nn}\) to test whether the difference between \(a_{nn}\) and \(a_{np}\) can be explained by charge independence breaking in the one-pion exchange potential.

## Key numbers

- The limiting value of the neutron-neutron scattering length, corrected for magnetic interaction, is \(a_{nn}^{\text{corr}} = -15.8 \pm 0.5\) fm, obtained from extrapolation of experimental data to infinite separation distance.
- The new measurement from the \(dd\) breakup reaction at 15 MeV deuteron beam energy gave \(a_{nn} = -22.2 \pm 0.6\) fm, corresponding to a virtual state energy \(\varepsilon_{nn} = 76 \pm 6\) keV.
- The well-established neutron-proton scattering length is \(a_{np} = -23.74\) fm.
- Using the dibaryon model, replacing the \(np\) one-pion exchange potential with the \(nn\) one-pion exchange potential yielded \(a_{nn} = -15.75\) fm, in good agreement with the extrapolated value.
- Using the Reid soft core potential, the same procedure gave \(a_{nn}^{\text{RSC}} = -15.57\) fm.
- The accepted "purely nuclear" proton-proton scattering length is \(a_{pp}^{\text{nuc}} = -17.3 \pm 0.3\) fm, leading to a charge-symmetry breaking difference \(a_{nn} - a_{pp}^{\text{nuc}} = -1.5\) fm (opposite sign to the conventional value).

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate on the correct value of the neutron-neutron scattering length, which has varied between \(-16\) and \(-19\) fm in different experiments (reviewed by Machner and Slaus, Tornow, and Chen). The authors challenge the widely accepted value of \(a_{nn} = -18.9 \pm 0.4\) fm from stopped pion capture experiments (Chen), arguing that it may be contaminated by three-body force effects or other unresolved issues. Their extrapolation method yields a value close to the average from old breakup experiments using the Migdal-Watson approximation (Kuhn, Tornow), which is less sensitive to three-body forces. The finding that the difference between \(a_{nn}\) and \(a_{np}\) can be explained by one-pion exchange differences implies that charge independence breaking in the \(NN\) interaction is largely due to the pion mass and coupling constant differences, and that charge symmetry breaking has the opposite sign compared to conventional treatments. The authors note that their low \(a_{nn}\) value is consistent with the Coulomb displacement energy between \(^3\)H and \(^3\)He within their dibaryon model (Konobeevski et al., JPhys2), but they acknowledge that the pion capture reaction results remain a challenge to be addressed in future work.
