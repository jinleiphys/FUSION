# Physics of many-body resonances with complex scaling and applications to light unstable nuclei

> We review the exotic phenomena in light unstable nuclei with a focus on many-body resonances, which can decay into more than two constituents, and are frequently observed in unstable nuclei above the three-body threshold energy. The complex scaling transformation of the Schrödinger equation is a powerful method for describing many-body resonances, because it separates the continuum spectra into resonant and non-resonant continuum ones. Since the asymptotic wave functions of the resonances are regularized in the complex scaling, many-body resonances are described using the $L^2$ basis functions in the eigenvalue problem. The properties of many-body resonances can then be discussed in the same way as those of the bound states. We apply the complex scaling to the system consisting of a stable nucleus and valence nucleons and investigate many-body resonances in neutron-rich and proton-rich light nuclei. Using the eigenstates obtained with the complex scaling, we construct the extended completeness relation and the Green's function. They are used to calculate the level densities and the general transition strengths into many-body unbound states. We also discuss the interpretation of the complex expectation values associated with resonances, which remains an open problem. We propose a possible scheme for it in terms of the complex-scaled Green's function.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how the complex scaling transformation of the Schrodinger equation provides a unified description of many-body resonances (states decaying into more than two constituents) and non-resonant continuum states in light unstable nuclei, but notes that the physical interpretation of complex expectation values for resonances remains an open problem, for which a scheme using the complex-scaled Green's function is proposed.

## Method

The complex scaling transformation rotates all spatial coordinates and momenta by a real angle theta, converting divergent resonance wave functions into square-integrable L2 basis functions. The complex-scaled Hamiltonian is diagonalized using a finite basis expansion (e.g., harmonic oscillator or Gaussian basis), yielding discrete complex eigenvalues that separate into bound states, resonances (with complex energies E_r - i Gamma/2), and non-resonant continuum states lying along rotated 2-theta lines in the complex energy plane. The extended completeness relation and complex-scaled Green's function are then constructed from these eigenstates to calculate level densities and transition strengths into many-body unbound states.

## Key numbers

- The average binding energy per nucleon for stable nuclei is 8 MeV, while in unstable nuclei a few extra nucleons are weakly bound with binding energies of around 1 MeV.
- For the Gaussian interaction exp(-a r^2), complex scaling is applicable only for |theta| < pi/4 because cos(2theta) must be positive to avoid divergence at large r.
- In the example of He-6 (2+ states), two three-body resonances are identified: 2+_1 and 2+_2, along with two types of continuum states (three-body He-4+n+n and two-body He-5(3/2-,1/2-)+n).
- The scaling angle theta must be greater than the specific angle theta_r = arctan(gamma_r/kappa_r) = |arg(k_R)| to regularize the divergent behavior of a resonance with momentum k_R = kappa_r - i gamma_r.

## Context

This review extends the complex scaling method originally developed by Aguilar, Combes, and Balslev (1971) and Nuttall and Cohen (1969) to many-body resonances in light unstable nuclei, particularly Borromean systems such as He-6 = alpha+n+n and Li-11 = Li-9+n+n where no two-body subsystems are bound. The approach enables the description of soft dipole resonances (SDR) in neutron-halo nuclei, which were proposed by Hansen and Jonson (1987) and Ikeda (1988, 1992), and allows calculation of strength functions and level densities for many-body unbound states. The work builds on Berggren's extended completeness relation (1968) and the Gamow shell model framework, and enables future unified descriptions of quantum many-body unbound states including both neutron-rich and proton-rich nuclei.
