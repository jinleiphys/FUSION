# Radiative proton capture cross sections in the mass range $40-55$

> Proton capture cross sections in the energy range of astrophysical interest for mass region 40-54 have been calculated in the Hauser-Feshbach formalism with reaction code TALYS1.6. The density dependent M3Y effective nucleon-nucleon interaction folded with target radial matter densities from relativistic mean field approach is used to obtain the semi-microscopic optical potential. A definite normalization of potential-well depths has been used over the entire mass region. The $(p,γ)$ rates of some reactions, important in the astrophysical scenario, are calculated using the potential in the relevant mass region.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a semimicroscopic optical potential constructed by folding the density-dependent M3Y interaction with relativistic mean field densities, using a single renormalization factor of 0.9 across the entire mass range A=40-54, can reproduce experimental radiative proton capture cross sections and astrophysical S-factors with reasonable accuracy, though the agreement varies significantly between nuclei and the method is not expected to work well near closed shells where level densities are low.

## Method

Proton capture cross sections for nuclei in the mass range A=40-54 were calculated using the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model implemented in the TALYS1.6 reaction code. The optical potential was constructed by folding the density-dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective nucleon-nucleon interaction with target radial matter densities obtained from relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations using the FSUGold Lagrangian, with a constant renormalization factor of 0.9 applied to both real and imaginary parts of the potential across the entire mass region. Nuclear level densities were taken from Goriely's microscopic results, and the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov model was used for the E1 gamma-ray strength function.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy differences between RMF theory and experiment are less than 1.5% for all stable nuclei in the mass range A=40-54 (e.g., $^{40}$Ca: theory 341.90 MeV vs. experiment 342.05 MeV; $^{54}$Fe: theory 464.91 MeV vs. experiment 471.76 MeV)
- Charge radius differences between theory and experiment are less than 2.1% (e.g., $^{40}$Ca: theory 3.43 fm vs. experiment 3.47 fm; $^{54}$Fe: theory 3.66 fm vs. experiment 3.69 fm)
- For $^{51}$V(p,$\gamma$)$^{52}$Cr, the present theory overestimates the measurement by a factor of approximately 2
- For $^{50}$Cr(p,$\gamma$)$^{51}$Mn, the present calculation underestimates the average data by a factor of approximately 4-5
- For $^{46}$Ti(p,$\gamma$)$^{47}$V, $^{47}$Ti(p,$\gamma$)$^{48}$V, and $^{48}$Ti(p,$\gamma$)$^{49}$V, the theory reproduces measurements within a factor of approximately 1.5 or less
- For $^{50}$Ti(p,$\gamma$)$^{51}$V, the results show significant overestimation by a factor of approximately 3
- The relevant Gamow energy window for this mass region lies between 1-2 MeV corresponding to a temperature of approximately 2 GK
- Q-values for proton capture in this mass range are within approximately 5 MeV to 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous semimicroscopic folding model calculations in the mass range A=55-80 (Gangopadhyay et al., Lahiri et al., Dutta et al., Chakraborty et al.) to the lower mass region A=40-54, using the same methodology and input parameters. The approach improves upon phenomenological global optical potentials such as those used by Rauscher et al. and Goriely et al. (2008), which require frequent variation of reaction rates and give rise to uncertainties away from the stability valley. The use of a single renormalization factor across the entire mass region enables extrapolation to nuclei where experimental data are not available, which is important for calculating reaction rates identified by Parikh et al. as significant in type I X-ray burst nucleosynthesis, and the results are compared with rates from the NON-SMOKER reaction code.
