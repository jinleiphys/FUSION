# Multi-task learning on nuclear masses and separation energies with the kernel ridge regression

> A multi-task learning (MTL) framework, called gradient kernel ridge regression, for nuclear masses and separation energies is developed by introducing gradient kernel functions to the kernel ridge regression (KRR) approach. By taking the WS4 mass model as an example, the gradient KRR network is trained with the mass model residuals, i.e., deviations between experimental and theoretical values of masses and one-nucleon separation energies, to improve the accuracy of theoretical predictions. Significant improvements are achieved by the gradient KRR approach in both the interpolation and the extrapolation predictions of nuclear masses and separation energies. This demonstrates the advantage of the present MTL framework that integrates the information of nuclear masses and separation energies and improves the predictions for both of them.

## Key claim

The gradient kernel ridge regression (GKRR) multi-task learning framework, when extended with odd-even effects (GKRRoe), improves both the interpolation and extrapolation predictions of nuclear masses and separation energies beyond what single-task KRR achieves, but the leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV) is not a valid test of its performance because the target values can be exactly deduced from neighboring nuclei in the training set.

## Method

The authors developed a gradient kernel ridge regression (GKRR) approach that introduces gradient kernel functions into the standard KRR framework, enabling simultaneous training on nuclear mass residuals and one-nucleon separation energy residuals relative to the WS4 mass model. The weight parameters are determined by minimizing a loss function that includes both mass and separation energy data, with regularization to prevent overfitting. The approach was extended to include odd-even effects (GKRRoe) using a remodulated kernel function, and evaluated using leave-one-plus-four-out cross-validation (LOFOCV) where the target nucleus and its four nearest neighbors are excluded from the training set.

## Key numbers

- The GKRRoe approach achieves a root-mean-square (rms) deviation of around 50 keV for masses and one-nucleon separation energies in LOOCV (though this is noted as not a valid test of predictive power).
- In LOFOCV, the WS4 mass model alone has a rms deviation of 290 keV for masses; KRRoe reduces this to 182 keV, and GKRRoe further improves the description, particularly for medium-mass and heavy nuclei.
- For extrapolation predictions, the KRRoe improves mass predictions for nuclei with extrapolation distance smaller than 6, with improvement up to 100 keV for small extrapolation distances; GKRRoe performs globally better than KRRoe in both the length of extrapolation distance and reduction of rms deviation at a given distance.

## Context

This work extends the single-task KRR approach for nuclear masses developed by Wu (2020) and Wu (2021), which previously achieved the most precise machine-learning mass model by incorporating odd-even effects. It also builds on the multi-task learning framework applied to giant dipole resonance parameters by Bai (2021). The gradient KRR approach is inspired by simultaneous training on functionals and their functional derivatives in density functional theory studies by Meyer (2020). The results enable more accurate predictions of nuclear masses and separation energies for both known and unknown nuclei, which are critical inputs for astrophysical nucleosynthesis studies.
