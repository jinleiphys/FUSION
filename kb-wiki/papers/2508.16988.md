# Probing Dynamical Electrical Conductivity via Dilepton Emission: A Kinetic theory approach

> Dileptons serve as a clean and penetrating probe of the Quark--Gluon Plasma created in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. In this work, we investigate thermal dilepton spectra and their elliptic flow through the dynamical conductivity that governs the production rate. The conductivity is obtained from the trace of the spectral function within relativistic kinetic theory using the Relaxation Time Approximation. This allows us to derive for the first time an analytical expression for the dilepton rate with explicit dependence on the relaxation time of quark-antiquark interactions. We find a non-monotonic dependence of the dilepton rate on the relaxation time and compare the resulting transverse momentum, invariant mass spectra and elliptic flow with previous quantum field theory results. The spectra and elliptic flow are obtained by integrating the rate over the full spacetime volume of the evolving medium, using temperature and flow profiles from realistic MUSIC hydrodynamic simulations without considering the effect of magnetic fields in the profiles itself. However, we study the role of an external space-time dependent magnetic field by making the conductivity anisotropic. At small relaxation times, magnetic fields have negligible impact, while for larger relaxation times and stronger initial fields, modifications of up to $\sim$20\% appear in both spectra and elliptic flow. Assuming instead a constant magnetic field of $\sim 1\,m_π^2$ at large relaxation times yields more modest effects, with changes of about 10\% in spectra and 5\% in elliptic flow.

## Key claim

The paper derives for the first time an analytical expression for the thermal dilepton production rate that explicitly depends on the relaxation time of quark-antiquark interactions, using the dynamical conductivity obtained from relativistic kinetic theory within the Relaxation Time Approximation. The authors find a non-monotonic (resonance-like) dependence of the dilepton rate on the relaxation time, which the abstract does not specify as a key qualitative feature.

## Method

The authors computed the dynamical electrical conductivity from the Boltzmann transport equation using the Relaxation Time Approximation, then expressed the thermal dilepton production rate in terms of this conductivity. They integrated the local rest-frame rate over the full spacetime volume of the evolving medium using temperature and flow profiles from (2+1)-dimensional boost-invariant MUSIC hydrodynamic simulations with Monte Carlo Glauber initial conditions for Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV. The effect of an external magnetic field was incorporated by making the conductivity anisotropic, splitting it into parallel and perpendicular components relative to the field direction, using a Gaussian-smeared spatial profile with a time-dependent decay.

## Key numbers

- The static (DC) conductivity was derived as \(\sigma_{\rm DC} = \frac{2e^{2}}{9}\tau_{c}T^{2}\), where \(\tau_{c}\) is the relaxation time and \(T\) the temperature.
- At small relaxation times, magnetic fields have negligible impact on spectra and elliptic flow; for larger relaxation times and stronger initial fields, modifications of up to approximately 20% appear in both spectra and elliptic flow.
- Assuming a constant magnetic field of approximately \(1\,m_{\pi}^{2}\) at large relaxation times yields changes of about 10% in spectra and 5% in elliptic flow.
- The hydrodynamic simulation used a grid of \(200 \times 200\) cells spanning \(20\,\text{fm} \times 20\,\text{fm}\), starting at proper time \(\tau_{0} = 0.4\,\text{fm}\) with time step \(\Delta\tau = 0.06\,\text{fm}\) up to \(\tau = 12\,\text{fm}\).
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio was fixed at \(\eta/s = 0.08\) with zero bulk viscosity.
- The integration over the medium was restricted to fluid cells with local temperature above \(155\,\text{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends previous quantum field theory calculations of dilepton production (such as those by McLerran and Toimela (1984) and later works by Rapp and collaborators) by providing a kinetic theory derivation that yields an explicit relaxation time dependence. It also builds on studies of magnetic field effects on dilepton emission by Bandyopadhyay et al. (2016), Sadooghi and Taghinavaz (2016), and Ghosh et al. (2018), among others, but differs by using a space-time-dependent magnetic field profile rather than a constant field. The results enable future comparisons between kinetic theory and QFT approaches to dilepton production and provide a framework for incorporating anisotropic conductivity into hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions.
