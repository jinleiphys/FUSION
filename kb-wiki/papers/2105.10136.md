# Quasi-elastic electron scattering with KIDS nuclear energy density functional

> Isoscalar and isovector effective masses of the nucleon in nuclear medium are explored in the quasi-elastic electron scattering of nuclei with KIDS (Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU) density functional model. Effective masses are varied in the range (0.7 ~ 1.0)$M$ where $M$ is the mass of the nucleon in free space. Parameters in the KIDS functional are adjusted to nuclear matter equation of state, energy and radius of selected nuclei, and effective mass of nucleons. Hartree-Fock equation is solved to obtain the wave functions of the nucleon in target nuclei, and they are plugged in the calculation of electron-nucleus scattering cross sections at the energies of incident electrons 300 MeV ~ 2.5 GeV. Theoretical prediction agrees well with measurement. Dependence on the effective mass is evident: cross section tends to increase with small isoscalar effective masses. However, effect of isovector effective mass is negligible. Spectroscopic factors are estimated for the protons in the outermost shells of $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb. Results are consistent with the values in the literature.

# Quasi-elastic electron scattering with KIDS nuclear energy density functional

## Key claim
The isoscalar effective mass of the nucleon in nuclear medium has a clear, systematic effect on quasi-elastic electron scattering cross sections, smaller isoscalar effective masses produce larger cross sections, but the isovector effective mass has negligible influence. This correlation holds across a wide range of mass numbers, though the effect is most pronounced in heavy nuclei like 208Pb and nearly invisible in light nuclei like 16O and 40Ca.

## Method
The authors solved non-relativistic Hartree-Fock equations using the KIDS (Korea-IBS-Daegu-SKKU) energy density functional, with parameters adjusted to reproduce nuclear matter saturation properties (density 0.16 fm−3, binding energy 16.0 MeV, incompressibility 240 MeV) and the binding energies and charge radii of 40Ca, 48Ca, and 208Pb. The resulting non-relativistic wave functions were transformed into relativistic form via a Darwin factor procedure, then used to calculate exclusive (e,e'p) reduced cross sections and response functions. Final-state interactions were included via a relativistic optical model, and electron Coulomb distortions were treated following the Ohio group method. Three KIDS variants were compared: KIDS0 (μS=0.99, μV=0.81), KIDS0-m*99 (μS=0.90, μV=0.90), and KIDS0-m*77 (μS=0.70, μV=0.70), alongside the standard Skyrme model SLy4 (μS=0.70, μV=0.80).

## Key numbers
- Effective masses were varied in the range (0.7–1.0)M, where M is the free nucleon mass.
- For 208Pb in parallel kinematics at Ei=412 MeV and Tp=100 MeV, models with μS=0.7 (Group2) gave cross sections 15–30% larger than models with μS≈1.0 (Group1).
- For 16O single-particle levels, Group2 models (μS=0.7) differed from experiment by 9.4–13.2%, while Group1 models (μS≈1.0) differed by 16.6–18.5%.
- For 208Pb single-particle levels, Group1 models gave differences of 2.5–5.7% (3s1/2 state) and 7.2–10.5% (2d3/2 state), while Group2 models gave 7.9–9.7% and 14.5–15.3%, respectively.
- Spectroscopic factors extracted for 40Ca were 0.72 (2s1/2) and 0.65–0.80 (1d3/2); for 208Pb they were 0.95 (3s1/2) and 0.90 (2d3/2).
- The fourth response function RLT for 16O showed up to about 45% difference between effective mass variants at the peak of the 1p1/2 orbit, and about 10% at the 1p3/2 orbit.

## Context
This work extends the KIDS density functional framework (Gil et al., 2019; Papakonstantinou et al., 2018) to quasi-elastic electron scattering, building on prior studies of nuclear matter and static nuclear properties with the same functional. The results contradict the notion that effective mass effects are uniformly important across all nuclei: light nuclei (16O, 40Ca) show negligible sensitivity in cross sections, while heavy nuclei (208Pb) show strong sensitivity. The finding that smaller isoscalar effective mass increases cross sections is consistent with the pattern seen in the UNEDF model (Kortelainen et al., 2010), which has μS=0.9 and shows better agreement for heavy nuclei than light ones. The work enables future constraints on the isoscalar effective mass from high-precision (e,e'p) data, particularly from JLab and NIKHEF, and demonstrates that the left-right asymmetry ALT provides a spectroscopic-factor-free observable for direct comparison with experiment.
