# Differential comparison of identified-hadron $\bf p_t$ spectra from high-energy A-B nuclear collisions based on a two-component model of hadron production

> Identified-hadron (PID) spectra from 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb and $p$-$p$ collisions are analyzed via a two-component (soft + hard) model (TCM) of hadron production in high-energy nuclear collisions. The PID TCM is adapted with minor changes from a recent analysis of PID hadron spectra from 5 TeV $p$-Pb collisions. Results from LHC data are compared with a PID TCM for 200 GeV Au-Au pion and proton spectra. 2.76 TeV proton spectra exhibit strong inefficiencies above 1 GeV/c estimated by comparing the $p$-$p$ spectrum with the corresponding TCM. After inefficiency correction Pb-Pb proton spectra are very similar to Au-Au proton spectra. PID A-A spectra are generally inconsistent with radial flow. Jet-related Pb-Pb and Au-Au spectrum hard components exhibit strong suppression at higher $p_t$ in more-central collisions corresponding to results from spectrum ratio $R_{AA}$ but also, for pions and kaons, exhibit dramatic enhancements below $p_t = 1$ GeV/c that are concealed by $R_{AA}$. In contrast, enhancements of proton hard components appear only above 1 GeV/c suggesting that the baryon/meson "puzzle" is a jet phenomenon. Modification of spectrum hard components in more-central A-A collisions is consistent with increased gluon splitting during jet formation but with approximate conservation of leading-parton energy within a jet via the lower-$p_t$ enhancements.

## Key claim

The two-component (soft + hard) model of hadron production applied to 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb identified-hadron spectra reveals that the baryon/meson "puzzle" is a jet phenomenon, with proton hard-component enhancements appearing only above 1 GeV/c while pion and kaon hard components show dramatic enhancements below 1 GeV/c that are concealed by the conventional ratio R_AA.

## Method

The author adapted a previously established two-component model (TCM) for identified-hadron spectra from 5 TeV p-Pb collisions, applying it with minor changes to 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb and p-p collision data from the ALICE collaboration. Normalized data spectra were compared with unit-normal soft-component model functions (Levy distributions on transverse mass) and hard-component model functions (Gaussians on transverse rapidity with exponential tails) to extract data hard components via Y_i(y_t) = [X_i(y_t) - S_0i(y_t)] / [(z_hi/z_si) x(nu) nu(nu)], using Glauber-model centrality parameters for the Pb-Pb system.

## Key numbers

- Soft-component model parameter T = 145 +/- 3 MeV for pions, universal over all A-B collision systems and collision energies
- Soft-component Levy exponent n = 8.5 +/- 0.5 for pions at 5 TeV
- Hard-component centroid y_t = 2.52 +/- 0.03 for pions, shifting to 2.92 +/- 0.02 for protons
- Hard-component width sigma_y_t = 0.56 +/- 0.03 for pions, narrowing to 0.47 for protons
- Soft-component mean p_t = 0.40 +/- 0.02 GeV/c for pions, increasing to 0.73 +/- 0.02 GeV/c for protons
- Hard-component mean p_t = 1.15 +/- 0.03 GeV/c for pions, increasing to 1.57 +/- 0.03 GeV/c for protons
- Central Pb-Pb collisions (0-5%): N_part = 383, N_bin = 1685, nu = 8.8
- Peripheral Pb-Pb collisions (60-80%): N_part = 22.5, N_bin = 26.7, nu = 2.4
- Soft-component charge density for 2.76 TeV p-p collisions: rho_sNN = 4.55

## Context

This work extends the two-component model analysis previously applied to 200 GeV Au-Au collisions (Trainor, 2015) and 5 TeV p-Pb collisions (Trainor, 2019) to 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb data from the ALICE collaboration. The results challenge the conventional interpretation of radial flow in A-A collisions and the blast-wave model fits (Schnedermann et al., 1993), instead suggesting that jet modification with increased gluon splitting and approximate conservation of leading-parton energy within a jet explains the observed spectrum evolution. The analysis enables direct comparison of hard-component modifications across collision systems and energies, revealing a sharp transition in jet modification trends similar to that observed in 200 GeV Au-Au collisions.
