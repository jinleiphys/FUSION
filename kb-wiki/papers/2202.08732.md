# In-medium hadronization of heavy quarks and its effect on charmed meson and baryon distributions in heavy-ion collisions

> We present a new model for the description of heavy-quark hadronization in relativistic heavy-ion collisions in the presence of a reservoir of lighter thermal particles with which recombination can occur leading to the formation of color-singlet clusters. Color neutralization is assumed to occur locally, within the same fluid cell occupied by the heavy quark and it proceeds via the recombination of the latter with light antiquarks (quarks) or diquarks (anti-diquarks), which are assumed to be present in the medium with thermal abundance as effective degrees of freedom around the QCD crossover temperature $T_c$. Typically the resulting color-singlet clusters have quite low invariant mass, in most of the cases below 4 GeV, and in this case they are assumed to undergo an isotropic 2-body decay in their local rest-frame. Heavier clusters are instead fragmented as Lund strings. The possibility of recombination with light diquarks enhances the yields of charmed baryons, in qualitative agreement with recent measurements. The assumption of local color neutralization leads to a strong space-momentum correlation, which provides a substantial enhancement of the collective flow of the final-state charmed hadrons, affecting both their momentum and their angular distributions

## Key claim
The paper presents a new model for heavy-quark hadronization in heavy-ion collisions where charm quarks recombine not only with light antiquarks but also with light diquarks from the thermal medium, enhancing charmed baryon yields. The key condition is that color neutralization occurs locally within the same fluid cell, producing a strong space-momentum correlation that enhances collective flow of final-state charmed hadrons.

## Method
The authors simulate charm quark propagation through a deconfined fireball using a relativistic Langevin equation, then hadronize the quarks at a freeze-out hypersurface at T=155 MeV by recombining them with thermally-distributed light antiquarks or diquarks from the same fluid cell. The resulting color-singlet clusters with invariant mass below a cutoff (set to 3.8 GeV) undergo isotropic two-body decay, while heavier clusters are fragmented as Lund strings using PYTHIA 6.4. The model is applied to Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV using two different sets of heavy-quark transport coefficients (HTL weak-coupling and lattice-QCD).

## Key numbers
- Hadronization temperature set to T_H = 155 MeV, matching lattice-QCD estimates of the pseudocritical temperature
- Charm quark mass explored at m_c = 1.3 GeV and m_c = 1.5 GeV, with final results using m_c = 1.5 GeV to minimize resampling uncertainties
- Cluster mass cutoff M_max = 3.8 GeV, close to the 4 GeV value used in HERWIG, separating two-body decay from string fragmentation
- Light quark masses: u/d quarks at 0.33000 GeV, s quark at 0.50000 GeV; diquark masses range from 0.57933 GeV for (ud)_0 to 1.09361 GeV for (ss)_1
- D* branching ratios applied for vector c-anti-q clusters up to cluster mass M_C = 2.8 GeV
- The Lambda_c+/D0 ratio in central Pb-Pb collisions is much larger than the value around 0.1 expected from e+e- collisions
- For m_c=1.3 GeV, hadronizing 20 million charm quarks requires sampling about 45 million light antiquarks or diquarks; for m_c=1.5 GeV, about 23 million

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous POWLANG transport setup (Beraudo 2014, Beraudo 2017, Beraudo 2021) which had described heavy-quark hadronization via recombination into color-singlet strings but could not reproduce the enhanced Lambda_c+/D0 ratio observed in heavy-ion collisions. The new model incorporates diquark recombination to address this shortcoming, building on the observation that the Catania group (Minissale 2020) successfully used a similar recombination approach for pp collisions. The model enables study of charmed baryon (Lambda_c, Xi_c, Omega_c) production and flow in heavy-ion collisions, and the authors plan to extend it to proton-proton collisions to understand how a hot expanding environment affects charm production even in small systems.
