# More is different: 50 years of nuclear BCS

> Many of the concepts which are at the basis of the development associated with a quantitative treatment of the variety of phenomena associated with the spontaneous breaking of gauge symmetry in nuclei have been instrumental in connection with novel studies of soft matter, namely of protein evolution and protein folding. Although the route to these subjects and associated development does not necessarily imply the nuclear physics connection, such a connection has proven qualitatively and quantitatively inspiring. In particular to model protein evolution in terms of the alignment of quasispins displaying twenty different projections, one for each of the twenty amino acids occurring in nature, and the associated symmetry breaking in information (sequence) space. Emergent properties of the corresponding phase transition are domain walls which stabilize local elementary structures (LES), few groups of 10-20 aminoacids which become structured already in the denatured state provide the molecular recognition directing protein folding. In fact, their docking is closely related to the transition state of the process. While the two-step, yes or no, folding process, does not provide direct information concerning LES, one can force LES from virtual to become real, observable final state entities. Getting again inspiration from the nuclear case (virtual processes contributing to pair correlations can be forced to become real with the help of a probe which itself changes particle number by two), one would expect that to make real virtual LES, that is segments of the protein which already at an early stage of the folding process flicker in and out of their native conformation, one needs a probe which itself displays a similar behaviour. Peptides displaying a sequence identical to LES are such probes.

# More is different: 50 years of nuclear BCS

## Key claim
The concepts of spontaneous breaking of gauge symmetry developed in nuclear BCS theory, particularly pairing correlations and the associated collective modes, provide a quantitatively and qualitatively inspiring framework for modeling protein evolution and protein folding, where the alignment of quasispins with twenty projections (one per amino acid) in information (sequence) space leads to emergent domain walls that stabilize local elementary structures (LES) directing folding.

## Method
The paper reviews Nuclear Field Theory (NFT) calculations of absolute differential cross sections for two-nucleon transfer reactions (specifically (p,t) and (t,p) reactions) on light exotic nuclei (e.g., 11Li, 9Li, 10Be, 12Be) and on open-shell superfluid Sn isotopes, comparing theoretical predictions with experimental data. It then extends the conceptual analogy between Cooper pair tunneling in nuclei and the use of peptides (p-LES) with sequences identical to LES segments to block protein folding, drawing on in vitro and ex vivo experimental tests of folding inhibition.

## Key numbers
- The neutron Cooper pair in 11Li is bound by approximately 300 keV from phonon exchange, with the experimental value being about 380 keV.
- The calculated absolute cross section for 11Li(p,t)9Li(gs) is 6.1 mb, compared to the experimental value of 5.7 ± 0.9 mb.
- The calculated cross section for 11Li(p,t)9Li(1/2-; 2.69 MeV) is 0.7 mb, compared to 1.0 ± 0.36 mb.
- For 112Sn(p,t)110Sn at ECM = 26 MeV, the calculated cross section is 1301 μb versus 1309 ± 200 (±14) μb.
- For 124Sn(p,t)122Sn at ECM = 25 MeV, the calculated cross section is 838 μb versus 958 ± 144 (±15) μb.
- For 206Pb(t,p)208Pb(gs), the calculated cross section is 0.52 mb versus 0.68 ± 0.21 mb.

## Context
This work extends the nuclear pairing concepts developed by Bohr, Mottelson, and Anderson (Anderson 1972) on spontaneous symmetry breaking in gauge space, and the NFT framework of Bes, Broglia, and collaborators (Bes 1976; Broglia 1976) for treating pairing correlations. It enables a new paradigm for protein folding inhibition using p-LES peptides as potential drug leads that do not elicit resistance, analogous to the anti-pairing effect in nuclei when blocking a hot orbital with an odd nucleon. The paper also connects to the Josephson effect discussion (Josephson 1962; Bardeen 1962; Cohen, Falicov, and Phillips 1962) and to planned experiments at TRIUMF (Kanungo and Tanihata) on pairing vibrations in 10Be.
