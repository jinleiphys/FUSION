# From binding and saturation to criticality in nuclear matter with lattice effective field theory

> We investigate the interaction dependence of the liquid-gas critical point of symmetric nuclear matter in finite-temperature lattice effective field theory. Building on the pinhole-trace algorithm, we benchmark a first-order perturbative treatment for representative Hamiltonian splittings and then compute the finite-temperature equation of state for a sequence of sign-friendly lattice Hamiltonians ranging from an SU(4)-symmetric interaction to Hamiltonians with physical ${}^{1}S_{0}$ and ${}^{3}S_{1}$ channel dependence and three improved leading-order Hamiltonians. The finite-temperature analysis is complemented by zero-temperature calculations of the symmetric-matter saturation point and the binding energies of selected nuclei within the same lattice framework. We find that the benchmarked perturbative strategy is quantitatively reliable in the thermodynamic regime studied. Across this Hamiltonian sequence, the LO Hamiltonians improve the overall description of finite-nucleus binding energies and move the zero-temperature saturation point toward the empirical region, while lowering the critical temperature from 15.33(6) MeV to 13.50(17)-13.71(19) MeV. These calculations show that finite-temperature criticality is not fixed by zero-temperature saturation and binding alone, and provide a complementary benchmark for future lattice interaction development.

## Key claim

The liquid-gas critical point of symmetric nuclear matter is not uniquely determined by zero-temperature saturation and binding properties alone; improving the Hamiltonian's description of finite-nucleus binding energies and the saturation point shifts the critical temperature downward, but the correlation between ground-state and finite-temperature observables is not one-to-one.

## Method

The authors used the pinhole-trace algorithm within lattice effective field theory on a periodic lattice with spacing 1.32 fm, treating an SU(4)-symmetric interaction nonperturbatively and adding spin-isospin dependent S-wave operators and three different leading-order Hamiltonians as first-order perturbative corrections. They computed the finite-temperature equation of state for symmetric nuclear matter across a sequence of sign-friendly lattice Hamiltonians, and complemented these with zero-temperature calculations of the saturation point and binding energies of selected nuclei from tritium to calcium-40.

## Key numbers

* Critical temperature for the SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian: 15.33(6) MeV
* Critical temperature range for the three LO Hamiltonians: 13.50(17) to 13.71(19) MeV
* Critical pressure for the SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian: 0.274(4) MeV/fm^3
* Critical pressure range for the three LO Hamiltonians: 0.166(9) to 0.181(11) MeV/fm^3
* Critical density for the SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian: 0.0884(30) fm^{-3}
* Critical density range for the three LO Hamiltonians: 0.0657(26) to 0.0720(46) fm^{-3}
* Saturation density for the SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian: 0.2188(2) fm^{-3}
* Saturation density range for the three LO Hamiltonians: 0.1717(5) to 0.1818(2) fm^{-3}
* Saturation energy per nucleon for the SU(4)-symmetric Hamiltonian: -17.0930(41) MeV
* Saturation energy per nucleon range for the three LO Hamiltonians: -15.9253(56) to -15.8317(261) MeV
* Experimental critical temperature estimate from Elliott (2013): 17.9(4) MeV

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature lattice thermodynamics program established by Lu (2019) and Ren (2023), who used a single sign-friendly SU(4)-symmetric interaction, by systematically varying the Hamiltonian toward more realistic forms. The results show that finite-temperature criticality probes different aspects of the nuclear interaction than zero-temperature saturation and binding, providing a complementary benchmark for future lattice interaction development and connecting to chiral many-body perturbation theory studies by Wellenhofer (2014) and Carbone (2018, 2019) as well as self-consistent Green's function calculations by Soma (2009).
