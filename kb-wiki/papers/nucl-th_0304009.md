# Relevance of pseudospin symmetry in proton-nucleus scattering

> The manifestation of pseudospin-symmetry in proton-nucleus scattering is discussed. Constraints on the pseudospin-symmetry violating scattering amplitude are given which require as input cross section and polarization data, but no measurements of the spin rotation function. Application of these constraints to p-58Ni and p-208Pb scattering data in the laboratory energy range of 200 MeV to 800 MeV, reveals a significant violation of the symmetry at lower energies and a weak one at higher energies. Using a schematic model within the Dirac phenomenology, the role of the Coulomb potential in proton-nucleus scattering with regard to pseudospin symmetry is studied. Our results indicate that the existence of pseudospin-symmetry in proton-nucleus scattering is questionable in the whole energy region considered and that the violation of this symmetry stems from the long range nature of the Coulomb interaction.

## Key claim

The paper argues that pseudospin symmetry is not relevant for proton-nucleus scattering in the energy range 200–800 MeV, contrary to earlier suggestions that it might hold at medium energies. The violation of the symmetry is attributed primarily to the long-range Coulomb interaction, not to the nuclear potential, and the apparent weak violation at 800 MeV is an artifact of the limited angular range of available data.

## Method

The authors derived constraints on the ratio \(R_{\rm ps} = \tilde B / \tilde A\) (pseudospin-dependent over pseudospin-independent scattering amplitude) that require only differential cross section and polarization data, avoiding the need for spin-rotation measurements. They applied these constraints to experimental p-\(^{58}\)Ni and p-\(^{208}\)Pb elastic scattering data at laboratory energies from 200 MeV to 800 MeV. Additionally, they performed schematic model calculations within Dirac phenomenology using Woods-Saxon nuclear potentials and a Coulomb potential from a uniformly charged sphere to isolate the Coulomb contribution to pseudospin symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers

* For p-\(^{58}\)Ni at \(E_{\rm Lab}=192\) MeV and 400 MeV, the extracted \(|R_{\rm ps}|^2\) values approach 1 at certain scattering angles, indicating the pseudospin-dependent and independent amplitudes are of comparable size.
* For p-\(^{208}\)Pb at \(E_{\rm Lab}=200\) MeV, complete data sets yield \(|R_{\rm ps}|^2\) values that indicate a significant contribution of the pseudospin-dependent scattering amplitude.
* For p-\(^{208}\)Pb at \(E_{\rm Lab}=800\) MeV, the analysis confirms a weak violation of pseudospin symmetry, consistent with earlier findings by Ginocchio (1999) and Leeb (2000).
* In pure Coulomb scattering calculations for proton-Pb, \(|R_{\rm ps}|^2\) exceeds 1 at 90 degrees and decreases with increasing energy (shown for 100, 200, 400, and 800 MeV).
* In schematic model calculations for p-\(^{208}\)Pb at 200 MeV with nuclear potentials satisfying exact pseudospin symmetry (\(V_0 = -W_0 = 300\) MeV), the ratio \(|R_{\rm ps}|\) at backward angles approaches the pure Coulomb values, exceeding 1.
* The nuclear part alone, in model calculations, yields \(|R_{\rm ps}|\) values not exceeding 0.15, indicating a small nuclear pseudospin-symmetry violating contribution.

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis by Ginocchio (1999) and Leeb (2000), who found weak pseudospin symmetry violation in p-\(^{208}\)Pb scattering at 800 MeV but could not make systematic statements due to scarce spin-rotation data. The present study confirms the earlier conjecture by Bowlin et al. (1988) that pseudospin symmetry is destroyed at low energies and only remnants might survive at high energies. The results question the relevance of pseudospin symmetry for proton-nucleus scattering across the entire 200–800 MeV range and show that the Coulomb potential, not the nuclear interaction, is the dominant source of symmetry breaking. This contrasts with the bound-state regime where Lisboa and Malheiro (2002) found only weak pseudospin violation due to cancellations between nuclear and Coulombic terms.
