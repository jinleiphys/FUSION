# Nuclear physics in the cosmos

> We observe photons and neutrinos from stars. Based on these observations, complemented by measurements of cosmic rays energies and composition, we have been able to constrain several models for the Big Bang and for stellar evolution. But that is not enough. We also need to help this effort with laboratory experiments. We are still far from being able to reproduce stellar environments in a terrestrial laboratory. But in many cases we can obtain accurate nuclear reaction rates needed for modeling primordial nucleosynthesis and hydrostatic burning in stars. The relevant reactions are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years indirect reaction methods have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors. These methods require a combination of new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, which are the subject of this short review.

## Key claim

This short review argues that while direct laboratory measurements of nuclear reaction rates at astrophysical energies remain extremely difficult, indirect reaction methods, combined with new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, can extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors needed for modeling primordial nucleosynthesis and hydrostatic burning in stars, though the author notes that the situation on electron screening of nuclear reactions "is confusing" and that either experimentalists are publishing wrong analysis or all aspects of theory might not have been considered yet.

## Method

The paper reviews several indirect nuclear physics methods for astrophysics: the Trojan Horse method (THM) for extracting low-energy reaction cross sections from breakup reactions, the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) technique for determining radiative capture cross sections from peripheral transfer reactions, and the Resonating Group Method (RGM) combined with No-Core-Shell-Model (NCSM) wavefunctions for calculating S-factors. The author also discusses coupled-channels calculations for fusion reactions, intermediate-energy Coulomb excitation with relativistic corrections, and the use of facilities like NIF and ITER for studying stellar plasma conditions and opacities.

## Key numbers

- The reaction $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B has a recommended astrophysical S-factor of $S_{17} = 20.8 \pm 0.7$ (expt) $\pm 1.4$ (theor) eV b, from a recent compilation
- A Padé approximant fit to the $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B S-factor is given by $S_{17}(E) = (22.109\ {\rm eV.b}) \times (1+5.30E+1.65E^2+0.857E^3)/(1+E/0.1375)$, where E is the center-of-mass energy in MeV
- ITER is designed to produce 500 MW of fusion power from 50 MW of input power, with a predicted building cost of 15 billion euros
- NIF costs roughly US$ 5 billion and uses 192 laser beams focused on a pellet containing a few milligrams of deuterium-tritium fuel
- The NIF X-ray opacity platform covers a photon energy range of 200 - 10,000 eV
- ITER heats deuterium-tritium plasma to temperatures of 150 million $^\circ$C
- Relativistic corrections to the Rutherford formula increase the scattering angle by up to 6% in nuclear collisions at 100 MeV/nucleon, and affect elastic scattering cross sections by up to 13% for center-of-mass scattering angles around 0-4 degrees
- The stopping power in proton+hydrogen collisions is proportional to $v^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha=1.35$, where $v$ is the projectile velocity
- Neutron-rich isotopes along the r-process path have lifetimes of less than one second, typically $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-1}$ s

## Context

This review extends the line of work on indirect nuclear reaction methods for astrophysics, building on the Trojan Horse method introduced by Baur (1986) and the ANC technique developed by Mukhamedzhanov (1990) and others. The paper discusses the long-standing discrepancy between experimental and theoretical electron screening in laboratory fusion reactions, referencing work by Assenbaum (1987), Rolfs (1995, 2001), and others, and notes that this problem remains unresolved. The review enables future work combining ab initio nuclear structure calculations (such as NCSM) with reaction theory (RGM/GCM) to calculate astrophysical S-factors, as demonstrated for the $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B reaction by Navratil (2005) and others.
