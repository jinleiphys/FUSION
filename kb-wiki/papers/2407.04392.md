# Nonlinear Schwinger mechanism in QCD, and Fredholm alternatives theorem

> We present a novel implementation of the Schwinger mechanism in QCD, which fixes uniquely the scale of the effective gluon mass and streamlines considerably the procedure of multiplicative renormalization. The key advantage of this method stems from the nonlinear nature of the dynamical equation that generates massless poles in the longitudinal sector of the three-gluon vertex. An exceptional feature of this approach is an extensive cancellation involving the components of the integral expression that determines the gluon mass; it is triggered once the Schwinger-Dyson equation of the pole-free part of the three-gluon vertex has been appropriately exploited. It turns out that this cancellation is driven by the so-called Fredholm alternatives theorem, which operates among the set of integral equations describing this system. Quite remarkably, in the linearized approximation this theorem enforces the exact masslessness of the gluon. Instead, the nonlinearity induced by the full treatment of the relevant kernel evades this theorem, allowing for the emergence of a nonvanishing mass. The numerical results obtained from the resulting equations are compatible with the lattice findings, and may be further refined through the inclusion of the remaining fundamental vertices of the theory.

## Key claim

The paper presents a novel implementation of the Schwinger mechanism in QCD where the nonlinearity of the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the bound-state pole amplitude, specifically a term cubic in the amplitude that reduces to a factor proportional to a constant omega, fixes the scale of the effective gluon mass uniquely; in the linearized approximation the Fredholm alternatives theorem enforces exact masslessness of the gluon, but the nonlinearity induced by the full treatment evades this theorem and allows a nonvanishing mass to emerge.

## Method

The authors constructed a nonlinear Bethe-Salpeter equation for the amplitude governing the formation of massless poles in the longitudinal sector of the three-gluon vertex, including a term cubic in the amplitude that had been omitted in previous linearized treatments. They then coupled this equation to the Schwinger-Dyson equation for the pole-free part of the three-gluon vertex and to the gluon propagator SDE, exploiting the Fredholm alternatives theorem to identify the cancellation mechanism that determines the gluon mass. The multiplicative renormalization of the mass equation was carried out exactly by introducing the SDE for the three-gluon vertex, which depends on the renormalization constant Z_3, and the resulting finite expression was found to be proportional to the parameter omega from the nonlinear BSE term.

## Key numbers

- The gluon mass m^2 is related to the transition amplitude I(0) by m^2 = g^2 I^2, where I := I(0) is the scalar form factor of the gluon-scalar transition amplitude at zero momentum.
- The scalar form factor I is given by I = -(3i C_A/2) integral_k k^2 Delta^2(k) B_fat(k), where C_A is the Casimir eigenvalue of the adjoint representation [N for SU(N)].
- The form factor L_s(r) of the three-gluon vertex in the soft-gluon limit satisfies L_s(r) = 1 + alpha_s integral_k k^2 Delta^2(k) K(r,k) L_s(k), with alpha_s = g^2/4pi.
- The gluon propagator at zero momentum satisfies Delta^{-1}(0) = m^2, where m^2 is the residue of the massless pole in the vacuum polarization.

## Context

This work extends the program of Cornwall (1981) and subsequent studies by Aguilar et al. (2011, 2015, 2017, 2021) on the Schwinger mechanism in Yang-Mills theories, where previous linearized treatments left the scale of the bound-state amplitude undetermined and required setting the renormalization constant Z_3 = 1 in numerical evaluations. The nonlinear BSE introduced here resolves the scale ambiguity and enables exact multiplicative renormalization of the mass equation, producing results compatible with lattice simulations by Athenodorou et al. (2016), Boucaud et al. (2017), and others; the approach can be further refined by including the remaining fundamental vertices of the theory.
