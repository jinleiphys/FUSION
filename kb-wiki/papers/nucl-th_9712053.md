# Can we see the hadron-quark transition happening in neutron stars?

> In order to actually see the hadron-quark transition happening in a neutron star, we point out and study two static conditions (the transition hadronic density must be lower than the neutron star maximum hadronic density; the neutron star mass at the transition hadronic density must be in the observed range, of order 1.4 solar mass) and one dynamical condition (nucleation must occur during the star lifetime). We find that the mini-colapse acompanying the transition from metastable hadronic matter to quark matter may be relevant to explain macro-glitches and gamma ray bursts, but that the mecanism increasing the star density must be relatively fast, e.g. accretion but not slowing down. This rules out a scenario for gamma ray bursts proposed recently.

## Key claim

The hadron-quark transition in neutron stars could be observable via a mini-collapse if the star's central density increases rapidly (e.g., by accretion but not by slowing down), which rules out a recently proposed gamma-ray burst scenario by Ma and Xie (1996) that relied on spin-down.

## Method

The author solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for hydrostatic equilibrium using the MIT bag model equation of state for quark matter (with bag constant B as a free parameter) and two hadronic equations of state: the soft Bethe-Johnson VN model and the stiff Walecka model. Nucleation times for quark droplet formation in metastable hadronic matter were calculated using a WKB tunneling formalism adapted from Lifshitz and Kagan (1972) and Haensel and Schaeffer (1982), treating the quark bubble as a quantum fluctuation with kinetic and potential energy terms.

## Key numbers

- For a BJVN hadronic equation of state with B=56 MeV fm$^{-3}$, the transition occurs at hadronic density $n_{H t}=1.46$ fm$^{-3}$; the nucleation time drops from $10^{11}$ yr at $n_H=1.62$ fm$^{-3}$ to $10^5$ yr at $n_H=1.63$ fm$^{-3}$
- For a pure BJVN neutron star (no quarks), maximum mass is $M_{max}=1.76 M_\odot$ at radius R=9.2 km and central density $n_c=1.49$ fm$^{-3}$
- For a pure Walecka neutron star (no quarks), maximum mass is $M_{max}=2.59 M_\odot$ at R=12.4 km and central density $n_c=0.76$ fm$^{-3}$
- For a Walecka star with B=73 MeV fm$^{-3}$, the transition mass is $M_t=1.4304 M_\odot$ at radius $R_t=13.2$ km
- Energy release estimates for the mini-collapse range from $2\times 10^{50}$ erg to $60\times 10^{50}$ erg depending on the degree of metastability (5% or 15% above transition density)
- Free-fall times for the collapse are of order $1-3\times 10^{-4}$ s
- For a BJVN star accreting at $10^{-9} M_\odot$ yr$^{-1}$, the central density increases from $n_{H t}$ to $n_H=1.63$ fm$^{-3}$ in approximately $10^6$ yr

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of first-order phase transitions in neutron stars, particularly the pion transition studied by Migdal et al. (1979), Haensel and Proszynski (1980, 1982), and Kampfer (1981), which were later ruled out by measurements of the spin-isospin repulsion coefficient. The paper contradicts the gamma-ray burst model of Ma and Xie (1996) by showing that spin-down increases central density by only $\sim 10^{-3}$, insufficient to trigger nucleation within a star's lifetime. The results enable future work on accretion-driven transitions and suggest that the mini-collapse could explain macro-glitches and gamma transients like the March 5, 1979 event, but not supernovae.
