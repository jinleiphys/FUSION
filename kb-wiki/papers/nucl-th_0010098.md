# Collective Modes of Tri-Nuclear Molecules

> A geometrical model for tri-nuclear molecules is presented. An analytical solution is obtained provided the nuclei, which are taken to be prolately deformed, are connected in line to each other. Furthermore, the tri-nuclear molecule is composed of two heavy and one light cluster, the later sandwiched between the two heavy clusters. A basis is constructed in which Hamiltonians of more general configurations can be diagonalized. In the calculation of the interaction between the clusters higher multipole deformations are taken into account, including the hexadecupole one. A repulsive nuclear core is introduced in the potential in order to insure a quasi-stable configuration of the system. The model is applied to three nuclear molecules, namely $^{96}$Sr + $^{10}$Be + $^{146}$Ba, $^{108}$Mo + $^{10}$Be + $^{134}$Te and $^{112}$Ru + $^{10}$Be + $^{130}$Sn.

## Key claim

The paper presents a geometrical model for tri-nuclear molecules that can be solved analytically only under the restrictive condition that the three prolately deformed nuclei are connected in a linear chain, with a light cluster sandwiched between two heavy clusters; the authors hedge that the experimental evidence for such molecules is "probable" and requires further confirmation and better statistics.

## Method

The authors construct a Hamiltonian for three aligned clusters using relative coordinates for the two heavy fragments and the light fragment, then quantize the kinetic energy including butterfly and belly-dancer modes, β and γ vibrations, and rotations of individual clusters. The nuclear and Coulomb interactions between clusters are computed via a double-folding procedure using M3Y effective nucleon-nucleon forces with a repulsive core added to prevent overlap, including multipole deformations up to hexadecupole. The Schrödinger equation is solved analytically by assuming small inclination angles and neglecting certain coupling terms, yielding a basis in which more general configurations can later be diagonalized.

## Key numbers

- The transition energy of 3368 keV in the ¹⁰Be nucleus interacting with ⁹⁶Sr and ¹⁴⁶Ba is probably about 6 keV smaller than for the free ¹⁰Be nucleus.
- The isoscalar M3Y force components are 7999 exp(-4r)/(4r) - 2134 exp(-2.5r)/(2.5r) MeV; the isovector components are -4885.5 exp(-4r)/(4r) + 1175.5 exp(-2.5r)/(2.5r) MeV.
- The isoscalar zero-range pseudo-exchange potential strength is -276 MeV·fm³; the isovector one is 217 MeV·fm³.
- For the light cluster (¹⁰Be), the radius prescription is R₀ = 1.04·A₃¹/³ and diffusivity a = 0.35 fm.
- The model is applied to three systems: ⁹⁶Sr + ¹⁰Be + ¹⁴⁶Ba, ¹⁰⁸Mo + ¹⁰Be + ¹³⁴Te, and ¹¹²Ru + ¹⁰Be + ¹³⁰Sn.

## Context

This work extends the two-cluster molecular model of Greiner and Scheid (prior work) to three clusters, motivated by the cold fission decay of ²⁵²Cf into three fragments observed by Hamilton (1990s). The model enables spectroscopy of giant nuclear molecules with half-lives larger than 10⁻¹³ seconds, and provides a theoretical framework for interpreting the observed 6 keV shift in the ¹⁰Be transition energy as due to modification of the shell model frequency by the surrounding heavy clusters.
