# Solution of the alpha-potential mystery in the gamma-process and its impact on the Nd/Sm ratio in meteorites

> The 146Sm/144Sm ratio in the early solar system has been constrained by Nd/Sm isotope ratios in meteoritic material. Predictions of 146Sm and 144Sm production in the gamma-process in massive stars are at odds with these constraints and this is partly due to deficiences in the prediction of the reaction rates involved. The production ratio depends almost exclusively on the (gamma,n)/(gamma,alpha) branching at 148Gd. A measurement of 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd at low energy had discovered considerable discrepancies between cross section predictions and the data. Although this reaction cross section mainly depends on the optical alpha+nucleus potential, no global optical potential has yet been found which can consistently describe the results of this and similar alpha-induced reactions at the low energies encountered in astrophysical environments. The untypically large deviation in 144Sm(alpha,gamma) and the unusual energy dependence can be explained, however, by low-energy Coulomb excitation which is competing with compound nucleus formation at very low energies. Considering this additional reaction channel, the cross sections can be described with the usual optical potential variations, compatible with findings for (n,alpha) reactions in this mass range. Low-energy (alpha,gamma) and (alpha,n) data on other nuclei can also be consistently explained in this way. Since Coulomb excitation does not affect alpha-emission, the 148Gd(gamma,alpha) rate is much higher than previously assumed. This leads to small 146Sm/144Sm stellar production ratios, in even more pronounced conflict with the meteorite data.

# Solution of the alpha-potential mystery in the gamma-process and its impact on the Nd/Sm ratio in meteorites

## Key claim

The long-standing discrepancy between predicted and measured cross sections for the 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd reaction at low energies can be resolved by including low-energy Coulomb excitation as an additional reaction channel that diverts alpha-particle flux away from compound nucleus formation, without requiring a modified optical alpha+nucleus potential. However, this resolution leads to a 148Gd(gamma,alpha) rate that is much higher than previously assumed, resulting in 146Sm/144Sm stellar production ratios that are in even more pronounced conflict with meteorite data than earlier predictions.

## Method

The author used the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model implemented in a newly developed code called SMARAGD to calculate reaction cross sections, incorporating Coulomb excitation (Coulex) as a competing channel that reduces the compound formation cross section. The Coulex cross section was computed using regular Coulomb wave functions for electric quadrupole (E2) transitions, and the modified compound formation cross section was obtained by multiplying the standard formation cross section by a factor f_l = sigma_form / (sigma_form + sigma_Coulex) for each partial wave. The approach was applied to reanalyze existing experimental data for 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd, 168Yb(alpha,n)171Hf, 168Yb(alpha,gamma)171Hf, 141Pr(alpha,n)144Pm, and 169Tm(alpha,n)172Lu.

## Key numbers

- The 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd S-factor data at 10.2 MeV showed a deviation from predictions of more than an order of magnitude, which could not be reproduced by any standard optical potential.
- After including Coulex correction and renormalizing the alpha-widths by a factor of 1/3, the standard Woods-Saxon potential (McFadden and Satchler, 1966) reproduced the 144Sm(alpha,gamma) data accurately.
- The stellar 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd reactivity at 2.5 GK obtained with the SMARAGD code using the standard Woods-Saxon potential with scaled alpha-width was 1.2 x 10^{-14} cm^3 s^{-1} mole^{-1}, which is two orders of magnitude higher than the value of 1.3 x 10^{-16} cm^3 s^{-1} mole^{-1} obtained by directly fitting the experimental data with an energy-dependent Woods-Saxon potential.
- The resulting 146Sm/144Sm production ratio R in a 25 solar mass core-collapse supernova model (ccSN-A) was 0.13 using the new potential, compared to 0.44 using the energy-dependent potential fit, and 0.19 using the standard Woods-Saxon potential without Coulex correction.
- The meteorite data permit a range of 0.2 <= R <= 0.23 for the 146Sm/144Sm ratio in the early solar system, assuming an open-box model for galactic chemical evolution.

## Context

This work addresses the "alpha-potential mystery" in gamma-process nucleosynthesis, where no global optical alpha+nucleus potential could consistently describe low-energy alpha-induced reaction data, particularly the extreme deviation seen in 144Sm(alpha,gamma)148Gd measured by Somorjai et al. (1998). The author shows that the previously puzzling energy dependence and magnitude of deviations can be explained by Coulomb excitation competing with compound nucleus formation, a mechanism previously assumed negligible at low energies. The finding that (n,alpha) experiments on 143Nd and 147Sm show overprediction by a factor of 3 (consistent with the required renormalization) supports this interpretation. The work challenges existing nucleosynthesis models for both core-collapse supernovae and type Ia supernovae, and calls for improved measurements of B(E2) values for odd nuclei and better hydrodynamic modelling of supernova explosions to resolve the conflict with meteoritic data.
