# The stability of relativistic stars and the role of the adiabatic index

> We study the stability of three analytical solutions of the Einstein's field equations for spheres of fluid. These solutions are suitable to describe compact objects including white dwarfs, neutron stars and supermassive stars and they have been extensively employed in the literature. We re-examine the range of stability of the Tolman VII solution, we focus on the stability of the Buchdahl solution which is under contradiction in the literature and we examine the stability of the Nariai IV solution. We found that all the mentioned solutions are stable in an extensive range of the compactness parameter. We also concentrate on the effect of the adiabatic index on the instability condition. We found that the critical adiabatic index, depends linearly on the ratio of central pressure over central energy density $P_c/{\cal E}_c$, up to high values of the compactness. Finally, we examine the possibility to impose constraints, via the adiabatic index, on realistic equations of state in order to ensure stable configurations of compact objects.

# The stability of relativistic stars and the role of the adiabatic index

## Key claim
All three analytical solutions (Tolman VII, Buchdahl, and Nariai IV) are stable over an extensive range of the compactness parameter, contradicting earlier claims that the Buchdahl solution is unstable. The critical adiabatic index depends linearly on the ratio of central pressure to central energy density up to high compactness values, but the stability analysis shows that solutions can remain stable even in regions where the causality condition (speed of sound less than speed of light) is violated.

## Method
The author employed Chandrasekhar's variational method for radial pulsations to examine the dynamical stability of three analytical solutions of Einstein's field equations for spherical fluid spheres: Tolman VII, Buchdahl, and Nariai IV. The stability condition was evaluated by computing the squared pulsation frequency sigma^2 from the variational integral, using trial functions for the Lagrangian displacement that satisfy the boundary conditions. The critical adiabatic index gamma_cr was computed from the condition sigma^2 = 0, and the averaged adiabatic index was compared to this critical value to determine stability.

## Key numbers
- The Tolman VII solution becomes unstable at compactness beta = 0.3428, and remains unstable up to beta = 0.3862 where the central pressure becomes infinite.
- The Nariai IV solution is stable up to beta = 0.4126, where the ratio P_c/E_c becomes infinite.
- The Buchdahl solution is stable up to beta = 0.2, beyond which the squared speed of sound becomes negative (v_s^2 < 0).
- For the Buchdahl solution, a Taylor expansion yields the approximation (sigma R)^2 approximately equal to (3 pi^2)/(2(pi^2 - 6)) times beta for low values of beta.
- The central pressure becomes infinite for the Tolman VII solution at beta = 0.3862, and for the Nariai IV solution at beta = 0.4126.
- The causality condition (speed of sound less than speed of light) is satisfied for Tolman VII only when beta < 0.2698, for Buchdahl when beta < 1/6, and for Nariai IV when beta < 0.2277.
- The Buchdahl solution requires beta < 0.4 to ensure positive energy density, and beta < 1/5 to satisfy v_s^2 > 0.

## Context
This work extends the stability analysis of analytical relativistic star solutions, resolving a contradiction in the literature regarding the Buchdahl solution: Knutsen (1988) found it unstable, while Negi (2007) found it stable, and the present results confirm Negi's conclusion. The study also provides the first stability analysis of the Nariai IV solution. The results enable constraints on realistic equations of state for neutron stars through the adiabatic index, building on the foundational work of Chandrasekhar (1964) on the variational method and the critical adiabatic index for the Schwarzschild constant-density interior solution. The Tolman VII solution stability was previously examined by Negi (1999, 2001), and the present work reexamines and compares it with the other solutions.
