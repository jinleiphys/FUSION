# Ambiguities in the partial-wave analysis of pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction

> Ambiguities in pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction, arising from incomplete experimental data, have analogs in pion-nucleon scattering. Amplitude ambiguities have important implications for the problems of amplitude extraction and resonance identification in partial-wave analysis. The effect of these ambiguities on observables is described. We compare our results with those found in earlier studies.

## Key claim

The paper shows that pseudoscalar-meson photoproduction amplitudes are not uniquely determined by cross-section and single-polarization measurements alone, and that the resulting ambiguities, one discrete and three continuous transformations of the amplitudes, have direct analogs in elastic pion-nucleon scattering but become more severe at higher energies where Watson's theorem no longer constrains the phases.

## Method

The authors generalize the discrete and continuous ambiguity transformations previously introduced for photoproduction amplitudes (Keaton and Workman, 1995) and express them in terms of transversity amplitudes, following the formalism of Omelaenko. They then demonstrate how these transformations alter the angular structure of observables by applying a continuous rotation with angle \(\phi = n\theta\) to the target-recoil observable \(L_Z\) for \(\gamma p \to p\pi^0\) at 500 MeV, using helicity amplitudes generated from a multipole analysis.

## Key numbers

- For \(\gamma p\to p\pi^0\) at 500 MeV, the target-recoil observable \(L_Z\) crossed zero three times; applying the transformation with \(\phi = \theta\) increased the number of zero crossings to 5, and with \(\phi = 2\theta\) to 7.
- The paper identifies the region near a center-of-mass energy of 1.9 GeV as promising for studying these ambiguities, because this is the energy of recent ITEP-PNPI spin-rotation measurements and is well separated from the threshold region.

## Context

This work extends the earlier discrete-ambiguity analysis of Keaton and Workman (1995) by generalizing the transformations to continuous forms and connecting them to the root-conjugation ambiguity found by Omelaenko. The results contradict the implicit assumption of earlier partial-wave analyses (Carnegie-Mellon-Berkeley and Karlsruhe-Helsinki groups) that dispersion relations and unitarity alone resolve all ambiguities, especially given that recent spin-rotation data disagree with those analyses' predictions. The paper enables future studies of nodal trajectories (Tabakin and collaborators) to account for the fact that the Legendre classes used in that method are not preserved by these ambiguity transformations, and it highlights that kaon and eta photoproduction analyses, lacking Watson's theorem constraints, are more susceptible to these ambiguities than pion photoproduction.
