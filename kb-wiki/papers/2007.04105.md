# A unified view of the first-excited $2^+$ and $3^-$ states of Cd, Sn and Te isotopes

> Symmetries are known to play an important role in the low lying level structure of Sn isotopes, mostly in terms of the seniority and generalized seniority schemes. In this paper, we revisit the multi-j generalized seniority approach for the first excited $2^+$ and $3^-$ states in the Cd ($Z=48$), Sn ($Z=50$) and Te ($Z=52$) isotopes, where the Cd and Te isotopes represent two-proton hole and two-proton particle nuclei, thus involving both kind of particles (protons and neutrons) in contrast to Sn isotopes. Interestingly, the approach based on neutron valence space alone is able to explain the B(E2) and B(E3) trends respectively for the $2^+$ and $3^-$ states in all the three Cd, Sn and Te isotopes. The new results on the inverted parabolic behavior of B(E3) values in Cd and Te isotopes are understood in a manner identical to that of Sn isotopes by using the generalized seniority scheme. No shell quenching is supported by these calculations; hence, the neutron magic numbers, $N=50$ and $N=82$, remain robust in these isotopic chains. It is quite surprising that the generalized seniority continues to be reasonably successful away from the semi-magic region, thus providing a unifying view of the $2^+$ and $3^-$ states.

## Key claim

The multi-j generalized seniority scheme, using only the neutron valence space, can explain the B(E2) and B(E3) trends for the first excited 2+ and 3- states in Cd, Sn, and Te isotopes, even though Cd and Te isotopes have both protons and neutrons in the valence space. The paper claims this is "quite surprising" because the generalized seniority approach continues to be "reasonably successful away from the semi-magic region."

## Method

The author applied the generalized seniority scheme with quasi-spin operators defined as S+ = Σj (-1)^lj S+j, using a simple pairing Hamiltonian in a multi-j space. For the 2+ states, calculations used pair degeneracies Ω=10 (before mid-shell, with orbitals g7/2, d5/2, d3/2, s1/2) and Ω=12 (after mid-shell, with orbitals d5/2, h11/2, d3/2, s1/2). For the 3- states, calculations used Ω=9 (d5/2 ⊗ h11/2) and Ω=11 (d5/2 ⊗ d3/2 ⊗ h11/2). The calculated B(EL) trends were compared with experimental data from the compilation of Pritychenko (2016) for B(E2) and Kibedi and Spear (2002) for B(E3).

## Key numbers

- B(E2) values for the first excited 2+ states in Cd, Sn, and Te isotopes show two asymmetric inverted parabolas with a dip around neutron number N=64-66, explained by a change in orbital filling from g7/2/d5/2 dominance to h11/2 dominance.
- B(E3) values for the first excited 3- states show an inverted parabolic trend in all three isotopic chains, with peaks at different neutron numbers: N=58-60 for Cd, N=66 for Sn, and N=70 for Te.
- The first excited 2+ states in Sn isotopes are observed at "nearly constant energy throughout the isotopic chain from N=52 to 80."
- The energies of the first excited 2+ states in Cd and Te isotopes are "consistently lower (nearly half) in comparison to the energies in Sn isotopes."

## Context

This work extends the author's previous generalized seniority studies of Sn isotopes (Maheshwari 2016, 2017, 2019) to the Cd and Te isotopic chains, which are two-proton hole and two-proton particle nuclei respectively. The paper contradicts the expectation that generalized seniority would fail away from semi-magic nuclei, and it argues against shell quenching at the neutron magic numbers N=50 and N=82. The results enable future studies of how generalized seniority might apply to other non-semi-magic nuclei and call for more precise experimental measurements, particularly in Cd and Te isotopes.
