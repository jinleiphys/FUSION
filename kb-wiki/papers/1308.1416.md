# Tensor force effects and high-momentum components in the nuclear symmetry energy

> We analyze microscopic many-body calculations of the nuclear symmetry energy and its density dependence. The calculations are performed in the framework of the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock and the Self-Consistent Green's Functions methods. Within Brueckner-Hartree-Fock, the Hellmann-Feynman theorem gives access to the kinetic energy contribution as well as the contributions of the different components of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. The tensor component gives the largest contribution to the symmetry energy. The decomposition of the symmetry energy in a kinetic part and a potential energy part provides physical insight on the correlated nature of the system, indicating that neutron matter is less correlated than symmetric nuclear matter. Within the Self-Consistent Green's Function approach, we compute the momentum distributions and we identify the effects of the high momentum components in the symmetry energy. The results are obtained for the realistic interaction Argonne V18 potential, supplemented by the Urbana IX three-body force in the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations.

# Tensor force effects and high-momentum components in the nuclear symmetry energy

## Key claim

The tensor component of the nucleon-nucleon interaction gives the largest contribution to the nuclear symmetry energy, but this conclusion is conditional on the use of the Argonne V18 potential with a three-body force in the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock framework; the isovector properties show only a small dependence on the three-body force despite the individual contributions to symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter not being small.

## Method

The authors performed Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations using the Argonne V18 realistic nucleon-nucleon potential supplemented by the Urbana IX three-body force, reduced to a density-dependent two-body force. They applied the Hellmann-Feynman theorem within BHF to decompose the total energy into kinetic and potential contributions and to isolate the contributions of different components of the nucleon-nucleon interaction (central, tensor, spin-orbit, L²). Additionally, they used the Self-Consistent Green's Functions (SCGF) method to compute momentum distributions and identify the effects of high-momentum components on the symmetry energy.

## Context

This work extends microscopic many-body calculations of the nuclear symmetry energy by Bombaci (1991), Lee (1998), and Frick (2005) using BHF and SCGF methods, and provides a detailed decomposition of the symmetry energy into kinetic and potential parts as well as spin-isospin channels. The results are compatible with experimental constraints reviewed by Tsang (2012) and Vinas (2012), and the finding that neutron matter is less correlated than symmetric nuclear matter enables improved modeling of neutron star structure and the core-crust transition, building on the work of Lattimer (2005) and Schulze (2006). The analysis of tensor force effects on the symmetry energy extends the earlier work of Vidaña (2009, 2011) and Rios (2012).
