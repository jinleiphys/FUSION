# Density dependence of the "symmetry energy" in the lattice gas model

> Isoscaling behavior of the statistical emission fragments from the equilibrated sources with $Z$ = 30 and $N$ = 30, 33, 36 and 39, resepectively, is investigated in the framework of isospin dependent lattice gas model. The dependences of isoscaling parameters $α$ on source isospin asymmetry, temperature and freeze-out density are studied and the "symmetry energy" is deduced from isoscaling parameters. Results show that "symmetry energy" $C_{sym}$ is insensitive to the change of temperature but follows the power-law dependence on the freeze-out density $ρ$. The later gives $C_{sym}$ = 30$(ρ/ρ_0)^{0.62}$ if the suitable asymmetric nucleon-nucleon potential is taken. The effect of strength of asymmetry of nucleon-nucleon interaction potential on the density dependence of the "symmetry energy" is dicussed.

# Density dependence of the "symmetry energy" in the lattice gas model

## Key claim

The symmetry energy coefficient $C_{sym}$ extracted from isoscaling parameters in the lattice gas model follows a power-law dependence on freeze-out density, $C_{sym} = 30(\rho/\rho_0)^{0.62}$, but only when a specific asymmetric nucleon-nucleon potential ($\epsilon_{pn} = -7$ MeV, $\epsilon_{pp} = 0$) is used; the exponent and coefficient both increase with the asymmetry of the nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Method

The authors performed lattice gas model simulations of equilibrated nuclear sources with fixed proton number $Z = 30$ and neutron numbers $N = 30, 33, 36, 39$ (giving $N/Z = 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.3$). Nucleons were placed on cubic lattices of sizes $5^3$ through $9^3$ to achieve freeze-out densities from $0.08\rho_0$ to $0.48\rho_0$, with momenta sampled from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution at temperatures between 1 and 6 MeV. Fragment clusters were identified using the Coniglio-Klein prescription, and isoscaling parameters $\alpha$ were extracted from yield ratios of light fragments ($Z \le 9$) for different source pairs. The symmetry energy $C_{sym}$ was then deduced via the grand-canonical relation $\alpha = (4C_{sym}/T)\Delta[(Z/A)^2]$, and its density dependence was fitted to $C_{sym} = C_0(\rho/\rho_0)^\gamma$ for five different choices of the nucleon-nucleon interaction parameters $(\epsilon_{pn}, \epsilon_{pp})$.

## Key numbers

- For the potential set $(\epsilon_{pn} = -7, \epsilon_{pp} = 0)$, the fitted density dependence gives $C_0 \sim 30$ MeV and $\gamma \sim 0.62$, consistent with recently suggested values from data and models.
- For the standard potential set $(\epsilon_{pn} = -5.33, \epsilon_{pp} = 0)$, the fit gives $C_0 \sim 24$ MeV and $\gamma \sim 0.6$.
- The binding energy per nucleon from the lattice gas model with $(\epsilon_{pn} = -5.33, \epsilon_{nn} = 0)$ is approximately -11.7 MeV, while with $(\epsilon_{pn} = -7, \epsilon_{nn} = 0)$ it becomes approximately -15.4 MeV/A, closer to the uniform nuclear matter value of -16 MeV at $T = 0$ and normal density.
- Freeze-out densities studied range from $0.08\rho_0$ (lattice $9^3$) to $0.48\rho_0$ (lattice $5^3$) for the $A = 60$ system.
- The exponent $\gamma$ from the literature is noted to lie in the range 0.55-0.69, and the coefficient $C_0$ in the range 31-33 MeV from recent data and models (Shetty).

## Context

This work extends the isoscaling method, originally proposed by Tsang (2001) for extracting the symmetry energy from experimental fragment yields, to a controlled lattice gas model where temperature and freeze-out density are well-defined input parameters. The results are consistent with the degenerate Fermi gas model analysis of Li (2006) showing little temperature dependence of the symmetry energy, and the extracted density dependence ($\gamma \sim 0.6-0.62$) falls within the range 0.55-0.69 proposed by Shetty from recent data and models. The study enables systematic comparison of how different choices of nucleon-nucleon interaction asymmetry affect the extracted symmetry energy, providing a framework for constraining the density dependence of the symmetry energy term in the nuclear equation of state by confronting model calculations with experimental isoscaling data.
