# $SO(3)$ quadratures in angular-momentum projection

> While the angular-momentum projection is a common tool for theoretical nuclear structure studies, a large amount of computations are required particularly for triaxially deformed states. In the present work, we clarify the conditions of the exactness of quadratures in the projection method. For efficient computation, the Lebedev quadrature and spherical $t$-design are introduced to the angular-momentum projection. The accuracy of the quadratures is discussed in comparison with the conventional Gauss-Legendre and trapezoidal quadratures. We found that the Lebedev quadrature is the most efficient among them and the necessary number of sampling points for the quadrature, which is often proportional to the computation time, is reduced by a factor 3/2 in comparison with the conventional method.

## Key claim

The angular-momentum projection can be performed exactly using a quadrature rule with degree of exactness \(t = J + I_\text{max}\), where \(J\) is the angular momentum of the projection and \(I_\text{max}\) is the maximum angular momentum contained in the wave function before projection; the Lebedev quadrature combined with the trapezoidal quadrature reduces the necessary number of sampling points by a factor of about 2/3 compared to the conventional Gauss-Legendre and trapezoidal method, but this efficiency gain is conditional on the wave function not being axis-aligned.

## Method

The authors derived the condition that a quadrature must have degree of exactness \(t = J + I_\text{max}\) to integrate the Wigner \(D\)-functions appearing in the angular-momentum projector exactly. They tested three quadrature schemes for the three-fold Euler-angle integral: the conventional product of trapezoidal (for \(\alpha\) and \(\gamma\)) and Gauss-Legendre (for \(\beta\)) quadratures (T+GL+T); the Lebedev quadrature for \((\alpha, \beta)\) combined with trapezoidal for \(\gamma\) (Lebedev+T); and the spherical \(t\)-design for \((\alpha, \beta)\) combined with trapezoidal for \(\gamma\) (SD2+T). Numerical benchmarks were performed using projected Hartree-Fock wave functions for \(^{57}\text{Fe}\) in the \(pf\)-shell with the GXPF1A interaction and for \(^{10}\text{Be}\) in a no-core shell-model approach with the JISP16 interaction.

## Key numbers

- For the \(J^\pi = 1/2^-\) state of \(^{57}\text{Fe}\), the effective maximum angular momentum in the wave function is \(I_\text{max} = 35/2\) (threshold \(f_I \geq 10^{-5}\)), requiring quadrature degree \(t = 18\); at \(t = 18\) the energy error is around \(10^{-5}\) MeV, and at \(t = 27\) the precision reaches machine epsilon.
- For the \(J^\pi = 21/2^-\) state of \(^{57}\text{Fe}\), the effective \(I_\text{max} = 37/2\) requires \(t = 29\); the energy error at \(t = 29\) is around \(10^{-5}\) MeV.
- For the ground state of \(^{10}\text{Be}\), the effective \(I_\text{max} = 12\) (threshold \(f_I \geq 10^{-5}\)) requires \(t = 12\); the energy error at \(t = 12\) is around \(10^{-3}\) MeV, and at \(t = 19\) the error is \(10^{-8}\) MeV.
- For degree \(t = 14\), the number of sampling points is 1800 for T+GL+T, 1290 for Lebedev+T, 1800 for SD2+T, and 960 for the Gauss-type \(SO(3)\) quadrature; the theoretical minimum number of points (efficiency = 1) is 1124.
- The number of points for the Lebedev quadrature on \(\mathbb{S}^2\) scales as \(N_\text{Lebedev} \simeq \frac{1}{3}(t+1)^2 + 2\), compared to \((t+1)\lceil (t+1)/2 \rceil\) for the trapezoidal+Gauss-Legendre method on \(\mathbb{S}^2\).

## Context

This work extends the mathematical analysis of quadrature exactness for angular-momentum projection, which is a standard technique in nuclear structure methods such as the projected shell model (PSM), the Monte Carlo shell model (MCSM), and the generator coordinate method (GCM). The conventional approach uses a product of trapezoidal and Gauss-Legendre quadratures as described in Bally (2019). The authors introduce the Lebedev quadrature, previously applied to spin projection in quantum chemistry, and spherical \(t\)-designs from combinatorial design theory, showing that the Lebedev+T method reduces the number of sampling points by roughly a factor of 2/3 relative to the conventional T+GL+T method. The Gauss-type \(SO(3)\) quadrature of Graef (2013) is identified as potentially more efficient but currently limited to low degrees (up to 14th degree with 960 points), restricting its practical application to small problems.
