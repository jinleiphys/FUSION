# Chiral transport equation from the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian and the on-shell effective field theory

> We derive the relativistic chiral transport equation for massless fermions and antifermions by performing a semiclassical Foldy-Wouthuysen diagonalization of the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian. The Berry connection naturally emerges in the diagonalization process to modify the classical equations of motion of a fermion in an electromagnetic field. We also see that the fermion and antifermion dispersion relations are corrected at first order in the Planck constant by the Berry curvature, as previously derived by Son and Yamamoto for the particular case of vanishing temperature. Our approach does not require knowledge of the state of the system, and thus it can also be applied at high temperature. We provide support for our result by an alternative computation using an effective field theory for fermions and antifermions: the on-shell effective field theory. In this formalism, the off-shell fermionic modes are integrated out to generate an effective Lagrangian for the quasi-on-shell fermions/antifermions. The dispersion relation at leading order exactly matches the result from the semiclassical diagonalization. From the transport equation, we explicitly show how the axial and gauge anomalies are not modified at finite temperature and density despite the incorporation of the new dispersion relation into the distribution function.

# Chiral transport equation from the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian and the on-shell effective field theory

## Key claim

The chiral transport equation of Son and Yamamoto is derived for both particles and antiparticles without requiring a well-defined Fermi surface or zero temperature, by performing a semiclassical Foldy-Wouthuysen diagonalization of the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian to order O(ℏ) and treating the resulting noncanonical Poisson brackets classically; the derivation shows that the Berry connection modifies both the equations of motion and the fermion/antifermion dispersion relations at first order in ℏ, and that the axial and gauge anomalies remain unchanged at finite temperature and density despite these modifications.

## Method

The authors perform a semiclassical Foldy-Wouthuysen diagonalization of the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian in powers of the Planck constant ℏ, obtaining the diagonalized Hamiltonian and rotated position/momentum operators at order O(ℏ) with their associated noncanonical commutators. For massless fermions, they derive the Poisson brackets from these commutators and obtain the classical equations of motion, from which they construct the chiral transport equation using Liouville's theorem. As an independent check, they develop an on-shell effective field theory (OSEFT) by integrating out off-shell fermionic modes, factorizing the Dirac field into particle and antiparticle projectors with a velocity decomposition, and deriving the dispersion relations at leading order.

## Key numbers

- The dispersion relation for particles is ε⁺_p = p (1 - eℏ λ (B·p)/(2p³)), where λ = ±1 is the helicity operator and p is the kinetic momentum.
- The dispersion relation for antiparticles is ε⁻_p = -p (1 - eℏ λ (B·p)/(2p³)).
- The Berry curvature for massless fermions is Ω = p/(2p³).
- The phase-space invariant measure is (1 + eℏ B·Ω) d³r d³p/(2πℏ)³.
- The axial anomaly equation derived is ∂_μ j^μ_A = e²/(2π²ℏ²) E·B = (2α)/(πℏ) E·B, with no thermal corrections.
- The validity condition for the semiclassical expansion requires eℏ B·p/p³ ≪ 1.

## Context

This work extends the derivation of the chiral kinetic theory by Son and Yamamoto (2012), which was only strictly valid at zero temperature with a well-defined Fermi surface, to finite temperature and density by using a semiclassical diagonalization of the quantum Dirac Hamiltonian. It also resolves discrepancies among previous proposals by Son and Yamamoto (2012), Stephanov and Yin (2012), and Chen et al. (2012), which differed in the fermion dispersion law and explicit terms in the kinetic equation. The OSEFT approach generalizes the high-density effective theory (HDET) of Hong (1998, 1999) and the large energy effective theory (LEET) of Charles et al. (1998), enabling systematic computation of next-to-leading-order corrections and thermal effects in relativistic plasmas.
