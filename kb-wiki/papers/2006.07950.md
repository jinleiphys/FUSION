# Anomalous scattering and transport in chiral matter

> Chiral anomaly modifies the scattering processes in chiral systems which can be computed using the Maxwell-Chern-Simons theory that couples electrodynamics to the pseudoscalar field $θ$ describing the topological charge induced be external sources. Assuming slow variation of the topological charge density, the fermion scattering cross section is computed in the Born approximation and is found to have a resonance at the scattering angles proportional to the chiral conductivity. As a result, the transport coefficients are suppressed at high temperatures. The anisotropy of the cross section arises due to the spatial variation of the topological charge; its effect on the electrical conductivity is discussed.

## Key claim

The chiral anomaly modifies fermion scattering in chiral systems, producing a resonance in the differential cross section at momentum transfer equal to the chiral conductivity \(b_0\), which in turn suppresses transport coefficients at high temperatures; however, this result depends on the assumption of slow variation of the topological charge density and on the static limit where the ion mass is much larger than the momentum transfer.

## Method

The scattering of light charged fermions ("electrons") off heavy charged fermions ("ions") was computed in the Born approximation using the Maxwell-Chern-Simons theory, which couples electrodynamics to a pseudoscalar field \(\theta\) describing the topological charge. The photon propagator in chiral matter was derived and used to obtain the scattering potential, with the topological charge gradient treated as a constant vector \(b^\mu = (b_0, -\mathbf{b})\). The cross sections were then averaged over random orientations of the ion magnetic moment and integrated to obtain transport cross sections, which were subsequently used in classical transport theory to compute electrical conductivity.

## Key numbers

- The resonance in the differential cross section occurs at momentum transfer \(|\mathbf{q}| = b_0\), corresponding to scattering angle \(\vartheta = b_0/|\mathbf{p}|\) in the static limit.
- At high momenta \(|\mathbf{p}| \gg b_0, \Gamma\), the transport cross section is \(\sigma_T \approx (e^2 \mu^2 / 6\pi) \ln(4\mathbf{p}^2 / b_0^2)\).
- For stationary matter with \(\mathbf{b} \neq 0\) and \(b_0 = 0\), the transport cross section at \(|\mathbf{p}| \gg |\mathbf{b}|\) is \(\sigma_T \approx (ee')^2 E^2 / (4\pi \mathbf{p}^4) \ln[\mathbf{p}^2 / (\mathbf{b}^2 (1+|\cos\chi|))]\).
- The electrical conductivity in homogeneous matter at high temperatures \(T \gg b_0, \Gamma\) is \(\sigma = \pi / (\mu^2 T \ln(T/b_0))\).
- In stationary matter at \(T \gg |\mathbf{b}|\), the conductivities are \(\sigma = 16\pi T / (e^2 \ln(T/|\mathbf{b}|))\) and \(\sigma' = 4\pi T / (e^2 \ln^2(T/|\mathbf{b}|))\).

## Context

This work extends the Maxwell-Chern-Simons theory of Carroll (1989) and Wilczek (1987) to scattering processes, building on the analysis of chiral matter by Qiu (2016) and Tuchin (2014). It provides a microscopic mechanism for the suppression of transport coefficients in chiral systems, potentially explaining the small shear viscosity to entropy density ratio observed in Quark-Gluon Plasma as noted by Heinz (2013). The results enable experimental investigation of the chiral anomaly through scattering measurements and suggest that the gluon propagator in QCD, which has the same form as the photon propagator in this theory, may produce analogous effects.
