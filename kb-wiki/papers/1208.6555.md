# Analysis of the Independent Particle Model approach to Nuclear Densities

> We present an analysis of the use of the Darwin-Fowler approximation in connection with the statistical IPM, by comparing the results of our recent studies with the occupation number approach (OCN) and some traditional statistical independent particle model (IPM) approaches. The analysis of level density works based on the statistical IPM reveals that the use of the the Darwin-Fowler approximation, in some of them, is theoretically inconsistent and some of their results should rather be considered as theoretical coincidences with other consistent approaches, than proofs of their validity. We conclude that, in general, the use of the Darwin-Fowler approximation with the statistical IPM should be used criteriously or, if possible, avoided altogether and suggest that the combinatorial IPM approaches have important advantages over the other models and formalisms analyzed in this paper, especially regarding the consistency of the microscopic description of the nuclear structure and dynamics of non highly excited systems.

## Key claim

The Darwin-Fowler approximation is theoretically inconsistent when used with the statistical independent particle model (IPM) for nuclear level density calculations, particularly for non-highly excited systems, and many results obtained with it should be considered coincidental rather than valid; the combinatorial IPM (IPMC) approach offers important advantages in consistency for microscopic descriptions of nuclear structure and dynamics.

## Method

The author compares three approaches to nuclear level density: the statistical approach (STA) of Bethe (1936), the occupation number approach (OCN) of Margenau (1959), and the combinatorial independent particle model (IPMC). The analysis focuses on the theoretical consistency of each approach with respect to the Darwin-Fowler method, examining whether the saddle-point approximation is valid for the excitation energies considered. The author evaluates the formalism of Bloch (1954, 1968) in detail, showing that it simultaneously assumes both low excitation (for the highly degenerate Fermi gas) and high excitation (for the saddle-point approximation), creating a logical contradiction.

## Key numbers

- The OCN model obtains the parameter gc with magnitude close to "2" for energies not greater than 10 MeV, while the author's calculations for atomic masses between 13 and 79 give gc between zero and "10", increasing with particle number A, for excitation varying from zero to the point of maximum of gc
- The parameter bc is nearly one order of magnitude greater than gc for all excitations and mass numbers in the author's calculations
- For intermediate size nuclei in the fundamental state, only nearly 10 occupied single particle levels need to be considered in the OCN approach
- The single particle level scheme consisting of 10 levels up to level "3s" has maximum occupation equal to 92 particles

## Context

This work extends the author's previous studies (Guimaraes, arXiv) on the limitations of the Darwin-Fowler method for nuclear level density calculations, and critiques the formalisms of Bloch (1954, 1968) which rely on the saddle-point approximation without specifying the excitation energy range of validity. The analysis contrasts with the STA of Bethe (1936), which consistently avoids the Darwin-Fowler method, and with the OCN of Margenau (1959), which uses direct summation over single particle levels. The paper suggests that the IPMC approach, as used by Williams (1969) and the author's own work, should be favored for consistent microscopic descriptions, especially for non-highly excited systems.
