# Correlations from generalized thermodynamics

> In order to account for possible nonstatistical fluctuations in a hadronizing system (leading to the characteristic power-like behavior of the respective single particle spectra and to the broadening of the corresponding multiparticle distributions) while using the statistical approach one has to resort to its nonextensive version. The new parameter q appearing there is directly connected to the variance of the particular variable X, q = 1+Var(X)/<X>^2 (with q = 1 for the usual statistical model). We shall demonstrate here how such an approach allows us to compose fluctuations of different observables (described by their respective parameters q) leading to a characteristic sum rule connecting q's deduced from measurements of these observables, which can be verified experimentally. We shall also discuss ensembles in which all relevant quantities, namely the energy (U), temperature (T) and multiplicity (N), can fluctuate. A specific relation connecting all these fluctuating variables is proposed. It generalizes the so called Lindhard thermodynamic uncertainty relations known in the literature, by introducing, in a natural way, a possibility of correlations between the fluctuating variables considered. This point is illustrated using an example from the multiparticle production processes. We show that fluctuations from different parts of phase space (characterized by different parameters q) are correlated and that the strength of these correlations is a function of these q's, Cov(U,T) = F({q}). These correlations can be tested experimentally. Some rough first estimates, using available data, are presented.

# Correlations from generalized thermodynamics

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that when all relevant event variables (energy U, temperature T, and multiplicity N) are allowed to fluctuate in a hadronizing system, a correlation coefficient ρ between U and T is necessary to fully describe the event; this correlation generalizes the Lindhard thermodynamic uncertainty relations and can in principle be extracted from experimental data, though current statistical uncertainties prevent a decisive conclusion on whether ρ is negative or zero.

## Method
The authors derive sum rules connecting the nonextensivity parameters q obtained from different observables (multiplicity distributions, transverse momentum distributions, and rapidity distributions) by considering an ensemble where energy U, temperature T, and multiplicity N all fluctuate. They introduce a correlation coefficient ρ = Cov(U,T)/√[Var(U)Var(T)] and express it in terms of the measured q-parameters from full phase space (q), transverse phase space (q_T), and longitudinal phase space (q_L), using a parameter α that specifies the fraction of temperature fluctuations going into the transverse direction. The formalism is applied to existing data from p̄p collisions at 546 GeV (UA5 experiment).

## Key numbers
- For p̄p collisions at 546 GeV: q = 1.27 from multiplicity distributions P(N), q_T = 1.09 from transverse momentum distributions, and q_L = 1.48 from rapidity distributions (corrected to q_L = 1.14 after accounting for simultaneous p_T fluctuations)
- The relation q = 1 + ω²_T connects the nonextensivity parameter to the relative variance of temperature, where ω²_T = Var(T)/⟨T⟩²
- The sum rule for uncorrelated fluctuations (ρ = 0) gives q - 1 = (q_L - 1) + (q_T - 1)
- The energy dependence of q from multiplicity distributions is fitted by 1/k = -0.104 + 0.029 ln s, where q = 1 + 1/k
- The energy dependence of q_T from transverse momentum distributions is fitted by q_T = 1.25 - 0.33 s^{-0.054}

## Context
This work extends the nonextensive (Tsallis) statistical approach to multiparticle production, which was developed to account for power-like single-particle spectra and broadened multiparticle distributions observed in high-energy collisions (Wilk and Wlodarczyk reviews, 2000s; Tsallis, 1988). The paper generalizes the Lindhard thermodynamic uncertainty relations (Lindhard, 1970s) by introducing correlations between fluctuating variables, and builds on earlier work by the authors (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2011) on covariance in such ensembles. The results enable experimental tests of how available energy is partitioned between particle production and particle energization, though the authors note that the notion of Tsallis statistics remains debated (e.g., recent discussions in the literature).
