# The Proton Radius Puzzle – Why We All Should Care

## Key claim
The proton radius puzzle may be resolved either by insufficient accuracy in electronic hydrogen experiments, by an improperly accounted two-photon exchange effect, or by new physics such as an electrophobic scalar boson; the author expects upcoming experiments to resolve the issue within the next year.

## Method
The paper reviews the status of the proton radius puzzle as of the 2018 CIPANP conference, comparing muonic and electronic hydrogen spectroscopy determinations of the proton charge radius. It examines two-photon exchange corrections using dispersion relations and the unmeasured subtraction function T1(0,Q^2), and explores the possibility of a new scalar boson coupling to muons and protons, constrained by data from muonic hydrogen Lamb shifts, muon anomalous magnetic moment, neutron scattering on lead-208, nuclear binding energies, and eta decay.

## Key numbers
- Muonic hydrogen proton radius: r_p = 0.84184(67) fm (Pohl 2010)
- Electronic hydrogen proton radius: r_p = 0.8768(69) fm (CODATA)
- Difference between muonic and electronic determinations: 7.9 standard deviations (R. Pohl, 2014 Mainz conference)
- Muonic hydrogen Lamb shift energy: 206.2942(32) meV
- Required new attractive effect on the 2S state to resolve the puzzle: 0.31 meV
- Two-photon exchange prediction for helium-4 Lamb shift from hadronic effects: 4.8 meV (ruled out by measurement)
- Muon anomalous magnetic moment discrepancy used: Delta a_mu = 287(80) x 10^{-11}
- Muonic hydrogen Lamb shift discrepancy used: delta E_L^{mu H} = -0.307(56) meV
- Limit on Lamb shift change in electronic hydrogen: delta E_L^H < 14 kHz (2 S.D.)
- Allowed scalar boson mass range from eta decay constraints: 200 keV to 3 MeV

## Context
This review extends earlier reviews by Pohl (2013) and Carlson (2015) and discusses recent experimental results including the Beyer et al. (2017) measurement of the 2s-4p transition finding the small radius, and the Fleurbaey et al. (2018) measurement of the 1s-3s transition finding the large radius. The paper also discusses the deuteron radius puzzle, where muonic deuterium measurements show a smaller radius than CODATA values, and notes that preliminary muonic helium-3 and helium-4 data show no puzzle for helium-4 with small uncertainties. The upcoming MUSE experiment (Gilman 2013) will measure electron and muon scattering on protons to constrain two-photon exchange effects.
