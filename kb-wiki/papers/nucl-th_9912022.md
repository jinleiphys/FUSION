# Meson-baryon interaction in the quark-gluon exchange framework

> We extend the Fock-Tani formalism to include the meson-baryon interaction. The Fock-Tani formalism is a first principle method which allows the use of field theoretic methods for treating systems of composite particles. An application of this general result can be the $K^{+}N$ system which is now reconized as much more suitable system for the investigation of the short range nature of the hadronic repulsion.

## Key claim

The Fock-Tani formalism, a first-principles method for treating composite particles with field-theoretic techniques, is extended to derive a general effective meson-baryon potential from quark-gluon exchange; the derivation is formal and no numerical results are presented, but the author states that the K+N system is a suitable application for investigating short-range hadronic repulsion.

## Method

The author extends the Fock-Tani formalism by constructing generators of a unitary transformation that maps composite meson and baryon operators (built from quark and antiquark creation operators with explicit internal wavefunctions) onto ideal particles obeying canonical (anti)commutation relations. He then derives Heisenberg-like equations of motion for the transformed operators and solves them order by order in the wavefunctions up to second order. Finally, he applies the transformed operators to a microscopic Hamiltonian containing quark-quark, antiquark-antiquark, and quark-antiquark interactions to obtain an effective meson-baryon potential expressed as a sum of four terms V1 through V4.

## Key numbers

- No numerical results are presented in this paper; the work is purely formal and methodological.

## Context

This work extends the Fock-Tani formalism, previously applied to meson-meson and baryon-baryon systems by Hadjimichef, Krein, Szpigel, and da Veiga (1996, 1998), to the meson-baryon sector. The author argues that the K+N system is more suitable than the NN system for studying short-range hadronic repulsion, following the argument of Buttgen, Holinde, Lohse, Muller-Groeling, Speth, and Wyborny (1990), because the K+N interaction lacks one-pion exchange and has weak two-pion exchanges. The derived potential enables future calculations of K+N scattering in higher partial waves (beyond the S-wave studied by groups such as Bender, Dosch, Pirner, and Kruse (1984) and Barnes and Swanson (1994)), which could help discriminate between different short-range repulsion mechanisms proposed in meson-exchange models like the Paris (Lacombe et al., 1980), Nijmegen (Nigels, Rijken, and deSwart, 1978), and Bonn (Machleidt, Holinde, and Elster, 1987) potentials.
