# Fragmentation of 14-N, 16-O, 20-Ne, and 24-Mg Nuclei at 290 to 1000 MeV/nucleon

> We report fragmentation cross sections measured at 0 deg for beams of 14-N, 16-O, 20-Ne, and 24-Mg ions, at energies ranging from 290 MeV/nucleon to 1000 MeV/nucleon. Beams were incident on targets of C, CH2, Al, Cu, Sn, and Pb, with the C and CH2 target data used to obtain hydrogen-target cross sections. Using methods established in earlier work, cross sections obtained with both large-acceptance and small-acceptance detectors are extracted from the data and when necessary corrected for acceptance effects. The large-acceptance data yield cross sections for fragments with charges approximately half of the beam charge and above, with minimal corrections. Cross sections for lighter fragments are obtained from small-acceptance spectra, with more significant, model-dependent corrections that account for the fragment angular distributions. Results for both charge-changing and fragment production cross sections are compared to the predictions of the Los Alamos version of the Quark Gluon String Model (LAQGSM) as well as the NUCFRG2 and PHITS models. For all beams and targets, cross sections for fragments as light as He are compared to the models. Estimates of multiplicity-weighted helium production cross sections are obtained from the data and compared to PHITS and LAQGSM predictions. Summary statistics show that the level of agreement between data and predictions is slightly better for PHITS than for either NUCFRG2 or LAQGSM.

## Key claim

The paper reports fragmentation cross sections for light beams (14-N, 16-O, 20-Ne, 24-Mg) on targets from carbon to lead at 290–1000 MeV/nucleon, but the light-fragment cross sections (below about half the beam charge) require model-dependent acceptance corrections that introduce larger relative errors, and the reported values are sensitive to the specific acceptance angles used in each experiment.

## Method

Beams of 14-N, 16-O, 20-Ne, and 24-Mg at energies from 290 to 1000 MeV/nucleon were incident on C, CH2, Al, Cu, Sn, and Pb targets. Energy-loss spectra from silicon detectors were used to identify fragments; large-acceptance detectors (half-angles 4.8°–9.8°) provided cross sections for fragments with charges approximately half the beam charge and above with minimal corrections, while small-acceptance detectors (half-angles 1.4°–3.8°) were used for lighter fragments, with acceptance corrections based on Goldhaber’s model of fragment angular distributions combined with Coulomb multiple scattering. Hydrogen-target cross sections were obtained by subtracting C-target results from CH2-target data.

## Key numbers

- Charge-changing cross section for 14-N at 290 MeV/nucleon on H target: 229 ± 11 mb; on C target: 750 ± 14 mb; on Pb target: 3092 ± 197 mb.
- Charge-changing cross section for 16-O at 1000 MeV/nucleon on C target: 856 ± 26 mb; on Pb target: 3307 ± 112 mb.
- Charge-changing cross section for 20-Ne at 400 MeV/nucleon on H target: 311 ± 15 mb; on C target: 1034 ± 21 mb; on Pb target: 3555 ± 129 mb.
- Charge-changing cross section for 24-Mg at 400 MeV/nucleon on C target: not given in the truncated text (only the table header is shown).
- For 400 MeV/nucleon 14-N on C, the small-acceptance charge spectrum shows resolved peaks at Z = 2.76 ± 0.013 and 2.96 ± 0.013, attributed to He pairs and Li fragments respectively.
- The systematic uncertainty assigned to acceptance corrections for light fragments is ±6%, added in quadrature with other uncertainties.

## Context

This work extends the authors’ previous fragmentation measurements (Zeitlin 1997, 2001, 2007a, 2007b, 2008) to lighter beam ions (N, O, Ne, Mg) and provides cross sections for fragments as light as He, enabling more stringent tests of nuclear models at large charge changes (small impact parameters). The data are compared to the LAQGSM, NUCFRG2, and PHITS models, with PHITS showing slightly better overall agreement. The results also allow comparison to the extensive cross-section database of Webber (1990a, 1990b), though discrepancies of up to 8% are noted for some carbon-target results. The improved database supports validation of transport codes used for space radiation shielding and hadron therapy applications.
