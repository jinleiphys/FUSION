# Multiplicity distributions and long range rapidity correlations

> The physics of the initial conditions of heavy ion collisions is dominated by the nonlinear gluonic interactions of QCD. These lead to the concepts of parton saturation and the Color Glass Condensate (CGC). We discuss recent progress in calculating multi-gluon correlations in this framework, prompted by the observation that these correlations are in fact easier to compute in a dense system (nucleus-nucleus) than a dilute one (proton-proton).

# Multiplicity distributions and long range rapidity correlations

## Key claim

Multi-gluon correlations in the Color Glass Condensate framework are actually easier to compute in dense nucleus-nucleus collisions than in dilute proton-proton collisions, because the dominant contributions come from diagrams that are disconnected for fixed color sources and become connected only after averaging over the color charge configurations, enhancing correlations by an additional factor of \(1/\alpha_s\) compared to quantum correlations.

## Method

The author computes multi-gluon correlations by first evaluating the single-gluon spectrum from the classical field configuration for a fixed configuration of color charges, then averaging over the probability distribution \(W_y[\rho(\mathbf{x}_\perp)]\) of those charges. For the multiplicity distribution, the calculation uses the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model Gaussian probability distribution and a linearized approximation where each gluon is produced from two Lipatov vertices, with the combinatorial factor obtained by counting the different ways of contracting the sources pairwise. The rapidity dependence of correlations is treated using the JIMWLK renormalization group evolution, formulated as a Langevin equation in the space of Wilson lines.

## Key numbers

- The gluon multiplicity is \(N \sim 1/\alpha_s\) for a fixed configuration of classical color sources, with \(\alpha_s\) the strong coupling constant.
- The parameter \(k\) of the negative binomial distribution is given by \(k \approx (N_c^2 - 1) Q_s^2 S_\perp / (2\pi)\), where \(N_c\) is the number of colors, \(Q_s\) is the saturation scale, and \(S_\perp\) is the transverse area of the system.
- The mean multiplicity is \(\bar{n} = f_N (1/\alpha_s) Q_s^2 S_\perp\).
- The number of independent glasma flux tubes is \(Q_s^2 S_\perp = N_{\text{FT}}\), and the total number of independent emitters is \(k \approx N_{\text{FT}} (N_c^2 - 1)\).
- The forward-backward correlation coefficient \(b\) reaches a maximum value of \(1/2\) in the limit of maximal correlation between multiplicities at different rapidities, under the condition of fixed reference multiplicity.

## Context

This work extends the glasma flux tube picture of Dumitru (2008) and Gavin (2008) for understanding correlations originating from the initial stage of heavy ion collisions, and builds on the formal discussion of Gelis (2008) regarding multigluon correlations. The calculation of the multiplicity distribution as a negative binomial distribution agrees with phenomenological observations in both pp and AA data from Arnison (1982), Alner (1985), Ansorge (1988), Adler (2007), and Adare (2008). The rapidity-dependent correlation calculation uses the JIMWLK evolution framework of Gelis (2008) and Lappi (2009), and is compared to PHOBOS data. The forward-backward correlation analysis follows the discussion in Lappi (2009) and addresses the STAR data of Abelev (2009), showing that the measured correlation coefficient saturating or exceeding the \(1/2\) limit makes interpretation in terms of any microscopic origin difficult.
