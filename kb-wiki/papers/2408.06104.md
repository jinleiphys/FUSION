# Quantum corrections at second order in derivatives to the dynamics of small non-relativistic fluids

> To capture the dynamics of macroscopic non-relativistic fluids consisting of very many atoms, it is typically sufficient to truncate the gradient expansion at order zero, leading to ideal fluid dynamics, or at order one, leading to the Navier-Stokes theory. For mesoscopic fluids consisting of a small number of atoms, second-order corrections can become significant. We investigate here specifically superfluids at vanishing temperature, and identify relevant second-order terms of quantum origin that contribute already in a static situation. The general form of these terms arises from an extension of the Gross-Pitaevskii theory. In the context of density functional theory, they are named after C. von Weizsäcker. We assess the influence of these terms on numerical solutions of second-order fluid dynamic equations for the expansion of a mesoscopic ultra-cold Fermi gas released from an anisotropic harmonic trap in two spatial dimensions.

# Quantum corrections at second order in derivatives to the dynamics of small non-relativistic fluids

## Key claim

Second-order gradient terms in the fluid dynamic description of mesoscopic superfluids at vanishing temperature, specifically those of quantum origin that contribute already in static situations, can be systematically derived from an extension of the Gross-Pitaevskii theory and are equivalent to the von Weizsacker correction in density functional theory; these terms become significant when the system size is small enough that spatial gradients of the density are large, but the paper assesses their influence only for the specific case of a two-dimensional ultra-cold Fermi gas released from an anisotropic harmonic trap.

## Method

The authors construct a derivative expansion of the stress-energy tensor up to second order in spatial gradients for non-relativistic superfluids, identifying the allowed second-order contributions to the bulk viscous pressure, shear stress tensor, and heat flux that are consistent with Galilean boost symmetry. They then derive the specific form of these second-order terms from a quantum effective action for the superfluid order parameter field, which generalizes the Gross-Pitaevskii equation by including a term proportional to the square of the density gradient (the Y-term). Numerical solutions of the resulting second-order fluid dynamic equations are obtained for the expansion dynamics of a mesoscopic ultra-cold Fermi gas in two spatial dimensions after release from an anisotropic harmonic trap, using an approximate equation of state for the strongly interacting BEC side of the BEC-BCS crossover.

## Key numbers

- The quantum pressure term in the Gross-Pitaevskii equation contributes to the bulk viscous pressure as $\Pi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{4 m D} n_s \boldsymbol{\nabla}^2 \ln(n_s)$ and to the shear stress as $\pi_{jk} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{4m} n_s \left[\partial_j \partial_k - \frac{1}{D}\delta_{jk} \boldsymbol{\nabla}^2 \right] \ln(n_s)$, where $D$ is the number of spatial dimensions.
- The generalized superfluid equation of motion (Eq. 19) includes a term $- 2 Y(n_s) [\boldsymbol{\nabla}^2 n_s] - Y^\prime(n_s) [\boldsymbol{\nabla} n_s][\boldsymbol{\nabla}n_s]$ multiplying the order parameter, where $Y(n_s)$ is a coefficient function from the effective action.
- In the kinetic energy functional of density functional theory (Eq. 26), the gradient correction appears as $\lambda \frac{\hbar^2}{8m^2} \frac{(\vec{\nabla} \rho)^2}{\rho}$ with $\lambda=1$ in the original work of C. von Weizsacker (1935).

## Context

This work extends the standard Gross-Pitaevskii theory for superfluids (Pitaevskii and Stringari, 2016) by including a generic Y-term in the effective action that modifies the coefficient of the quantum pressure term, connecting to the von Weizsacker correction in density functional theory (von Weizsacker, 1935; Jones and Gunnarsson, 1971). The second-order fluid dynamic framework builds on relativistic developments for causal fluid dynamics (Israel and Stewart; Romatschke, 2017) and on previous non-relativistic second-order treatments for cold Fermi gases (Chao and Schaefer, 2012; Kikuchi et al., 2016; Lewis and Crowell, 2017). The numerical application to mesoscopic Fermi gases is motivated by recent experimental observations of elliptic flow in few-particle ultra-cold atomic systems (Brandstetter et al., 2023; Holten et al., 2022), and the results enable a systematic assessment of whether second-order corrections can explain the observed fluid-like behavior in systems too small for conventional Navier-Stokes hydrodynamics.
