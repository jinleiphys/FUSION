# Decay Modes of the Hoyle State in $^{12}C$

> Recent experimental results give an upper limit less than 0.043\% (95\% C.L.) to the direct decay of the Hoyle state into 3$α$ respect to the sequential decay into $^8${Be}+$α$. We performed one and two-dimensional tunneling calculations to estimate such a ratio and found it to be more than one order of magnitude smaller than experiment depending on the range of the nuclear force. This is within high statistics experimental capabilities. Our results can also be tested by measuring the decay modes of high excitation energy states of $^{12}$C where the ratio of direct to sequential decay might reach 10\% at $E^*$($^{12}$C)=10.3 MeV. The link between a Bose Einstein Condensate (BEC) and the direct decay of the Hoyle state is also addressed. We discuss a hypothetical `Efimov state' at $E^*$($^{12}$C)=7.458 MeV, which would mainly {\it sequentially} decay with 3$α$ of {\it equal energies}: a counterintuitive result of tunneling. Such a state, if it would exist, is at least 8 orders of magnitude less probable than the Hoyle's, thus below the sensitivity of recent and past experiments.

## Key claim

The authors' tunneling calculations predict the ratio of direct decay (DD) to sequential decay (SD) for the Hoyle state to be at least one order of magnitude smaller than the current experimental upper limit of 0.043% (95% C.L.), with the precise value depending on the assumed range of the nuclear force parameter α_N.

## Method

The authors performed one-dimensional tunneling calculations for the sequential decay (12C → 8Be + α) and two-dimensional tunneling calculations for the direct decay (12C → 3α), using the action integral formalism with a Coulomb potential modified by a nuclear force parameter α_N that varies from 0 (Gamow limit) to 1 (touching spheres). For the direct decay, they assumed equal distances and momenta between all three α particles (r12 = r13 = r23), reducing the two-dimensional problem to an effective one-dimensional calculation. The characteristic assault time τ_B was fitted to reproduce the known lifetime of 8Be.

## Key numbers

- The experimental upper limit for the ratio of direct decay to sequential decay of the Hoyle state is less than 0.043% (95% C.L.), corresponding to about 1 out of 2500 events.
- The calculated ratio of DD/SD for the Hoyle state is 0.0012% (or 0.0036% after accounting for particle indistinguishability), which is more than a factor of 10 smaller than the experimental upper limit.
- For the hypothetical Efimov state at E* = 7.458 MeV, the probability is at least 8 orders of magnitude smaller than that of the Hoyle state.
- The ratio of direct to sequential decay might reach 10% at E*(12C) = 10.3 MeV.
- The width of the Hoyle state is 8.5 eV.
- The outer classical turning point for the 8Be ground state decay is R0 = 62.72 fm, derived from the Coulomb potential with Z1=Z2=2 and energy 0.09184 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the experimental efforts to measure the direct decay branch of the Hoyle state, which have progressively improved upper limits from 4% (Freer et al., 1994) to 0.2% (Itoh et al., 2014) and finally to 0.043% (Dell'Aquila et al., 2017; Smith et al., 2017). The authors' results are in surprising agreement with the three-body calculations of Ishikawa (2014), who found ratios of DDE/SD = 0.005% and DDL/SD = 0.03% using a completely different approach. The paper also discusses the hypothetical Efimov state at 7.458 MeV, which would decay sequentially but with three equal-energy α particles, a counterintuitive result of tunneling that could not be distinguished experimentally from direct decay. The work enables future high-statistics experiments to test the predicted ratio and potentially search for the Efimov state, which would require more than 28000 Hoyle state events (the current experimental statistics) to detect.
