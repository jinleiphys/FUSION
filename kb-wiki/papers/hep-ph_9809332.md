# Large Strangeness as a QGP Signal in an Isentropic Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

> Lattice QCD results reveal that the critical parameters and the order of the quark-hadron phase transition are quite sensitive to the number of dynamical flavours and their masses included in the theory. Motivated by this result we develop a phenomenological equation of state for the quark-gluon plasma consisting of $n_f$ flavours retaining the entropy per baryon ratio continuous across the quark-hadron phase boundary. We thus obtain a generalised expression for the temperature and baryon chemical potential dependent bag constant. The results are shown for the realistic case, i.e., involving u, d and s quarks only. We then obtain a phase boundary for an isentropic quark-hadron phase transition using Gibbs' criteria. Similarly another phase boundary is obtained for the transition to an ideal QGP from the solution of the condition $B(μ,T)=0$. The variation of critical temperature $T_c$ with the number of flavours included in the theory. Also the variation of ${(ε-4P)}/T^4$ with temperature are studied and compared with lattice results. Finally the strange particle ratios $\frac{\barΛ}Λ$, $\frac{\barΞ}Ξ$ and $\frac{K^+}{K^-}$ are obtained at both phase boundaries. We propose that their variations with the temperature and baryon chemical potential can be used in identifying the quark-gluon plasma in the recent as well as in future heavy-ion experiments.

# Large Strangeness as a QGP Signal in an Isentropic Quark-Hadron Phase Transition

## Key claim

The paper proposes that strange particle ratios (specifically \(\bar{\Lambda}/\Lambda\), \(\bar{\Xi}/\Xi\), and \(K^+/K^-\)) can serve as signals for quark-gluon plasma formation in heavy-ion collisions, but only in the midrapidity, nearly baryon-free region; the authors explicitly caution that their model breaks down at baryon chemical potentials above 300 MeV and that existing CERN and AGS data correspond to a thermal hadron gas rather than QGP, contradicting earlier claims by Asprouli and Panagiotou (1998).

## Method

The authors develop a phenomenological equation of state for the quark-gluon plasma with a temperature- and baryon-chemical-potential-dependent bag constant \(B(\mu,T)\) for \(n_f\) flavours, imposing continuity of the entropy-per-baryon ratio across the phase boundary. They use Gibbs' criteria to construct an isentropic, equilibrium phase boundary between QGP and hadron gas, and a second boundary from the condition \(B(\mu,T)=0\) representing the transition to an ideal, noninteracting QGP. Strange particle ratios are then calculated along both phase boundaries using a gluon-fragmentation model for quark production in the QGP phase.

## Key numbers

- The temperature-dependent bag constant \(B(\mu,T)\) vanishes at \(T = 210\) MeV when interactions among quarks and gluons are included, compared to \(T = 440\) MeV for three massless quarks without interactions.
- The critical temperature for the deconfining phase transition is \(T_c \simeq 160\) MeV.
- For \(\mu \leq 100\) MeV (the expected region at RHIC and LHC), the ratio \(\bar{\Lambda}/\Lambda \geq 0.56\) would indicate formation of an ideal QGP.
- For \(\mu < 100\) MeV, the ratio \(\bar{\Xi}/\Xi > 0.74\) would indicate QGP formation.
- For \(\mu < 100\) MeV, the ratio \(K^+/K^- \leq 1.3\) would indicate QGP formation.
- The present experimental value of \(\bar{\Lambda}/\Lambda\) is \(0.22 \pm 0.01\), which the authors interpret as signifying a hot and dense hadron gas rather than QGP.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Leonidov et al. (1995) by generalising the \(\mu\)- and \(T\)-dependent bag constant to \(n_f\) flavours and incorporating QCD perturbative corrections, building on the authors' own earlier work (Patra, Singh, and Singh, 1997-1998). The results are compared qualitatively with lattice QCD findings that the critical temperature drops with increasing flavour number (\(T_c \propto n_f^{-1/2}\)). The paper directly contradicts the analysis of Asprouli and Panagiotou (1998), who used arbitrarily fixed phase boundaries and concluded that existing CERN and AGS data indicated QGP formation; the present authors argue those data instead correspond to a thermalised hadron gas. The work aims to provide a basis for interpreting future RHIC and LHC experiments where nearly baryon-free QGP is expected.
