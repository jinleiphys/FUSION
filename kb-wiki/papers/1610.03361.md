# Equations of state for supernovae and compact stars

> We review various theoretical approaches for the equation of state (EoS) of dense matter, relevant for the description of core-collapse supernovae, compact stars and compact star mergers. The emphasis is put on models that are applicable to all of these scenarios. Such EoS models have to cover large ranges in baryon number density, temperature and isospin asymmetry. The characteristics of matter change dramatically within these ranges, from a mixture of nucleons, nuclei, and electrons to uniform, strongly interacting matter containing nucleons, and possibly other particles such as hyperons or quarks. As the development of an EoS requires joint efforts from many directions we consider different theoretical approaches and discuss relevant experimental and observational constraints which provide insights for future research. Finally, results from applications of the discussed EoS models are summarized.

## Key claim

This review argues that constructing a "general purpose" equation of state (EoS) covering the full thermodynamic range for core-collapse supernovae, neutron stars, and compact binary mergers requires joint efforts from many theoretical approaches, but the abstract's emphasis on applicability to all scenarios glosses over the fact that many existing EoS models can only provide properties for particular thermodynamic conditions and are not sufficient to describe all these astrophysical systems simultaneously.

## Method

The authors survey theoretical approaches for dense matter EoSs, dividing them into ab-initio many-body methods (starting from realistic few-body interactions) and phenomenological approaches (using effective interactions with 10-15 parameters fitted to nuclear properties). They discuss the relevant thermodynamic variables (temperature, baryon number density, electron fraction) and particle degrees of freedom that must be covered, then review experimental and observational constraints from terrestrial experiments, hypernuclear spectroscopy, and astrophysical observations. Finally, they summarize results from applications of the discussed EoS models to proto-neutron stars, binary mergers, core-collapse supernovae, and black hole formation.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n_sat ≈ 0.16 fm^{-3}
- Maximum baryon number density in neutron stars: approximately ten times nuclear saturation density (10 × n_sat)
- Temperature range for general purpose EoS: 0 MeV ≤ T < 150 MeV
- Baryon number density range for general purpose EoS: 10^{-11} fm^{-3} < n_B < 10 fm^{-3}
- Electron fraction range for general purpose EoS: 0 < Y_e < 0.6
- Observed compact star masses of 2 M_sun (demorest_10, Antoniadis_13, Fonseca:2016tux)
- Typical temperatures in post-merger remnant neutron star core: 20 to 60 MeV (bauswein10)
- Highest densities in hot rotating remnant neutron star: between 2 and 6 n_sat (hotokezaka13b)
- Electron fraction in dynamic ejecta of neutron star mergers: initially 0.0 to 0.2 (rosswog13, sekiguchi15)
- Final electron fraction values in merger ejecta: roughly 0.1 to 0.4 (Wanajo:2014wha, sekiguchi15)
- Temperature threshold for nuclear statistical equilibrium: approximately 0.5 MeV (iliadis07)
- Strangeness changing weak interaction timescales: order of 10^{-6} s or below (Brown92)
- Hyperon-nucleon scattering data points used in fitting: approximately 35 data points from the 1960s (Engelmann_66, Alexander_68, SechiZorn_69, Eisele_71)

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of dense matter EoSs by Glendenning (1997), Weber (2004), and Lattimer (2012) with a specific focus on models applicable simultaneously to core-collapse supernovae, neutron stars, and compact binary mergers. It provides a comprehensive overview of both formal approaches (ab-initio methods and phenomenological models) and available general purpose EoSs, enabling future work to identify which parts of the EoS and associated uncertainties relate to potential astrophysical observations. The paper also discusses open questions regarding the treatment of inhomogeneous matter, phase transitions, and the need for improved experimental and observational constraints.
