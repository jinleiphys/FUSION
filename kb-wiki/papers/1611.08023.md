# Equation of state dependence of directed flow in a microscopic transport model

> We study the sensitivities of the directed flow in Au+Au collisions on the equation of state (EoS), employing the transport theoretical model JAM. The EoS is modified by introducing a new collision term in order to control the pressure of a system by appropriately selecting an azimuthal angle in two-body collisions according to a given EoS. It is shown that this approach is an efficient method to modify the EoS in a transport model. The beam energy dependence of the directed flow of protons is examined with two different EoS, a first-order phase transition and crossover. It is found that our approach yields quite similar results as hydrodynamical predictions on the beam energy dependence of the directed flow; Transport theory predicts a minimum in the excitation function of the slope of proton directed flow and does indeed yield negative directed flow, if the EoS with a first-order phase transition is employed. Our result strongly suggests that the highest sensitivity for the critical point can be seen in the beam energy range of $4.7\leq\srtNN\leq11.5$ GeV.

## Key claim
The authors show that modifying the two-body collision term in the JAM transport model to control pressure according to a given equation of state (EoS) can reproduce hydrodynamical predictions for directed flow, including a minimum in the excitation function and negative directed flow for a first-order phase transition. However, the minimum from this approach is located at a beam energy much lower than the minimum observed by the STAR Collaboration, suggesting that the connection between the softest point of the EoS and the STAR data is not seen if the employed EoS is close to the true EoS in nature.

## Method
The authors modified the JAM microscopic transport model by introducing a new collision term that controls the pressure of the system by selecting the azimuthal angle in two-body collisions according to a given EoS, based on the virial theorem relation between pressure and momentum change. They tested two EoS types: a first-order phase transition (hadron resonance gas with bag constant B^{1/4}=220 MeV giving T_c=156 MeV at zero net baryon density) and a crossover EoS from a chiral model consistent with lattice QCD. The beam energy dependence of proton directed flow was computed for mid-central Au+Au collisions (impact parameter range 4.6<b<9.4 fm) and compared with STAR, NA49, and E895 data.

## Key numbers
- Beam energy range of highest sensitivity for the critical point: 4.7 ≤ √sNN ≤ 11.5 GeV
- First-order phase transition EoS uses bag constant B^{1/4}=220 MeV leading to phase transition temperature T_c=156 MeV at vanishing net baryon density
- Crossover EoS includes baryon density dependent vector repulsive mean field V(ρ_B)=½Kρ_B² with K=0.45 GeV fm³
- Softening in first-order phase transition occurs at energy density e≈1 GeV/fm³
- Slope of directed flow obtained by fitting v₁(y)=Fy+Cy³ in rapidity interval -0.8<y<0.8
- Local minimum for first-order phase transition located at slightly higher beam energy than one-fluid model prediction (√sNN≈4 GeV) and three-fluid model prediction (√sNN=6.5 GeV)
- For crossover EoS, local minimum at √sNN≈6 GeV but less pronounced
- Magnitude of minimum in JAM with first-order phase transition is about a factor 5 smaller than ideal hydrodynamical prediction

## Context
This work extends previous attempts to implement EoS effects in transport models, including the method of choosing attractive orbits in two-body collisions (Nara 2016) and the approach of Sorge (1998) for controlling pressure. It contrasts with hybrid models (Steinheimer 2014) and three-fluid models (Ivanov 2014) that have also studied directed flow excitation functions. The results support the idea that a large negative slope of proton directed flow could identify a strong softening due to first-order phase transition, but the minimum location differs from STAR data, suggesting the need for future measurements in the beam energy range 4.7<√sNN<11.5 GeV at facilities such as BES II at RHIC, FAIR, NICA, and J-PARC.
