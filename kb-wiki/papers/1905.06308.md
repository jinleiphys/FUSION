# Energy of the $^{229}$Th nuclear clock transition

> The first nuclear excited state of $^{229}$Th offers the unique opportunity for laser-based optical control of a nucleus. Its exceptional properties allow for the development of a nuclear optical clock which offers a complementary technology and is expected to outperform current electronic-shell based atomic clocks. The development of a nuclear clock was so far impeded by an imprecise knowledge of the energy of the $^{229}$Th nuclear excited state. In this letter we report a direct excitation energy measurement of this elusive state and constrain this to 8.28$\pm$0.17 eV. The energy is determined by spectroscopy of the internal conversion electrons emitted in-flight during the decay of the excited nucleus in neutral $^{229}$Th atoms. The nuclear excitation energy is measured via the valence electronic shell, thereby merging the fields of nuclear- and atomic physics to advance precision metrology. The transition energy between ground and excited state corresponds to a wavelength of 149.7$\pm$3.1 nm. These findings set the starting point for high-resolution nuclear laser spectroscopy and thus the development of a nuclear optical clock of unprecedented accuracy. A nuclear clock is expected to have a large variety of applications, ranging from relativistic geodesy over dark matter research to the observation of potential temporal variation of fundamental constants.

## Key claim

The first direct measurement of the $^{229}$Th nuclear isomer excitation energy constrains it to 8.28$\pm$0.17 eV, a value that agrees with the prior indirect measurement of 7.8$\pm$0.5 eV (Beck 2009) within its 1$\sigma$ statistical uncertainty, but the authors note that density functional theory calculations suggest a subset of excited states may be populated more dominantly, which would shift the energy to the lower end of the error bar.

## Method

The experiment generated $^{229\text{m}}$Th ions via the 2% decay branch in the $^{233}$U $\alpha$-decay, formed bunches of 400 $^{229\text{m}}$Th$^{3+}$ ions at 10 Hz, and neutralised them by passage through a dual graphene layer. The internal conversion electrons emitted in-flight during the isomeric decay of neutral $^{229}$Th atoms were collected with a magnetic bottle-type retarding field electron spectrometer, and the integrated electron spectrum was fitted with a complementary error function to extract the deflection point energy, which was then related to the isomeric energy via simulations that accounted for the unknown distribution of initial electronic excited states.

## Key numbers

- Isomeric excitation energy: $E_I = 8.28 \pm 0.17$ eV (statistical uncertainty $\pm 0.03$ eV, systematic uncertainty $\pm 0.16$ eV)
- Corresponding transition wavelength: $149.7 \pm 3.1$ nm
- Deflection point of the fitted error function: $E_{\text{defl}} = 1.77 \pm 0.03$ eV
- Reference energy from simulations: $E_0 = 6.51$ eV
- Prior indirect measurement value: $7.8 \pm 0.5$ eV (Beck 2009)
- Spectrometer FWHM resolution: about 3%
- Thorium ionisation potential: 6.31 eV (Kohler 1997)

## Context

This work provides the first direct energy measurement of the $^{229}$Th nuclear isomer, which had previously been inferred only from indirect gamma-ray measurements (Kroger and Reich 1976; Reich and Helmer 1990; Helmer and Reich 1994; Beck 2007; Beck 2009). The improved precision (0.17 eV versus 0.5 eV) reduces the time required for laser-based scanning to less than one day using the scheme proposed by von der Wense (2017), and the measured wavelength of 149.7 nm lies in the transparency window of VUV-transparent crystals such as MgF$_2$ or CaF$_2$, enabling the development of a nuclear optical clock with a predicted systematic frequency uncertainty of $1.5\times10^{-19}$ (Campbell 2012).
