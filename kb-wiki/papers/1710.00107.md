# Correlated Prompt Fission Data in Transport Simulations

> Detailed information on the fission process can be inferred from the observation, modeling and theoretical understanding of prompt fission neutron and $γ$-ray~observables. Beyond simple average quantities, the study of distributions and correlations in prompt data, e.g., multiplicity-dependent neutron and \gray~spectra, angular distributions of the emitted particles, $n$-$n$, $n$-$γ$, and $γ$-$γ$~correlations, can place stringent constraints on fission models and parameters that would otherwise be free to be tuned separately to represent individual fission observables. The FREYA~and CGMF~codes have been developed to follow the sequential emissions of prompt neutrons and $γ$-rays~from the initial excited fission fragments produced right after scission. Both codes implement Monte Carlo techniques to sample initial fission fragment configurations in mass, charge and kinetic energy and sample probabilities of neutron and $γ$~emission at each stage of the decay. This approach naturally leads to using simple but powerful statistical techniques to infer distributions and correlations among many observables and model parameters. The comparison of model calculations with experimental data provides a rich arena for testing various nuclear physics models such as those related to the nuclear structure and level densities of neutron-rich nuclei, the $γ$-ray~strength functions of dipole and quadrupole transitions, the mechanism for dividing the excitation energy between the two nascent fragments near scission, and the mechanisms behind the production of angular momentum in the fragments, etc. Beyond the obvious interest from a fundamental physics point of view, such studies are also important for addressing data needs in various nuclear applications. (See text for full abstract.)

## Key claim

The paper argues that while average fission observables are well known, detailed correlated data (e.g., neutron-neutron, neutron-gamma, and gamma-gamma correlations) are still lacking even for well-studied cases, and that the FREYA and CGMF Monte Carlo codes, when integrated into transport codes like MCNP6.2 and MCNPX-PoliMi, provide a new capability to simulate these correlations for applications in nonproliferation, safeguards, nuclear energy, and defense.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical basis of prompt fission neutron and gamma-ray emission from excited fission fragments, then describe the Monte Carlo event-by-event codes FREYA and CGMF, which sample initial fission fragment configurations in mass, charge, and kinetic energy and simulate sequential neutron and gamma emission. They discuss the integration of these codes into the MCNP6.2 and MCNPX-PoliMi transport codes, and compare model calculations with experimental data for a wide range of correlated fission observables including multiplicity distributions, angular distributions, and neutron-neutron and neutron-gamma correlations.

## Key numbers

- The current evaluated uncertainty on average prompt neutron multiplicity (nubar) for 252Cf(sf) is 0.13%
- The prompt neutron multiplicity distribution for spontaneous fission of many Pu, Cm, and Cf isotopes can be approximated by a Gaussian of width sigma_nu = 1.20
- Terrell used a Gaussian of width sigma_nu = 1.08 in his analysis of neutron multiplicity distributions
- Nifenecker inferred the relation between gamma-ray energy and neutron multiplicity for a given fragment: E_gamma(A,KE) = (0.75 * nu(A,KE) + 2) MeV
- The average spin of initial fission fragments is often estimated to be approximately 7-9 hbar

## Context

This review extends the work of the IAEA Coordinated Research Project "Prompt Fission Neutron Spectra of Actinides" (Capote, 2016), which contained little discussion of correlated fission data. The paper builds on the development of the FREYA code (Randrup and Vogt), the CGMF code (Talou et al.), and the FIFRELIN and GEF codes, and describes their integration into MCNP6.2 and MCNPX-PoliMi (Pozzi, 2012) for transport simulations. The work enables improved modeling of correlated fission events for applications where average quantities are insufficient, such as neutron multiplicity counting (Ensslin, 1998) and non-destructive assay methods.
