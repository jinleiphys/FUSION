# Modified two-potential approach to tunneling problems

> One-body quantum tunneling to continuum is treated via the two-potential approach, dividing the tunneling potential into external and internal parts. We show that corrections to this approach can be minimized by taking the separation radius inside the interval determined by simple expressions. The resulting two-potential approach reproduces the resonance energy and its width, both for narrow and wide resonances. We also demonstrate that, without losing its accuracy, the two-potential approach can be modified to a form resembling the R-matrix theory, yet without any uncertainties of the latter related to the choice of the matching radius.

## Key claim

The two-potential approach (TPA) can be made extremely accurate for both narrow and wide resonances by choosing the separation radius \(R\) inside a well-defined "window" determined by simple correction-term expressions, rather than at the barrier maximum; the resulting modified two-potential approach (MTPA) eliminates the explicit dependence on the separation radius and avoids the uncertainties of R-matrix theory related to the choice of matching radius.

## Method

The authors divide the tunneling potential into an inner potential \(U(r)\) (containing a bound state \(\Phi_0\)) and an outer potential \(\tilde W(r)\), then derive perturbative expressions for the resonance width \(\Gamma\) and energy shift \(\Delta\) in terms of bound and scattering wave functions. They evaluate the leading correction terms of two types: one from replacing the full Green's function by the outer-potential Green's function, and another from replacing the energy-dependent Green's function by its value at the bound-state energy. By minimizing these corrections, they identify a window for the separation radius \(R\) and then eliminate \(R\) entirely by matching the bound-state wave function to the irregular solution of the repulsive potential at a radius \(\bar r\) inside the barrier, yielding the MTPA formulas. The TPA and MTPA results are compared to exact Gamow-state calculations using the GAMOW code for proton and neutron resonances in a Woods-Saxon potential.

## Key numbers

- For a P-wave square-well resonance at \(E_{\rm res}=1\) keV with exact width \(\Gamma = 55.9\) eV, the TPA with \(R\) inside the barrier gives \(\Gamma = 55.3\) eV.
- For the \(0h_{11/2}\) proton resonance in a Woods-Saxon potential at \(E_{\rm res}=1.5\) MeV, the exact width is \(\Gamma = 4.918 \times 10^{-18}\) MeV; TPA with \(R-\bar R = 4.28\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm TPA} = 4.919 \times 10^{-18}\) MeV (0% correction).
- For the \(2s_{1/2}\) proton resonance at \(E_{\rm res}=1.5\) MeV, exact \(\Gamma = 6.695 \times 10^{-14}\) MeV; TPA with \(R-\bar R = 3.48\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm TPA} = 6.709 \times 10^{-14}\) MeV (-0.2% correction).
- For the \(0i_{13/2}\) neutron resonance at \(E_{\rm res}=1\) MeV, exact \(\Gamma = 1.834 \times 10^{-6}\) MeV; TPA with \(R-\bar R = 3.45\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm TPA} = 1.844 \times 10^{-6}\) MeV (-0.5% correction).
- For the \(1f_{5/2}\) neutron resonance at \(E_{\rm res}=1\) MeV, exact \(\Gamma = 9.271 \times 10^{-2}\) MeV; TPA with \(R-\bar R = 4.57\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm TPA} = 9.408 \times 10^{-2}\) MeV (-1.5% correction).
- MTPA for the \(0h_{11/2}\) resonance with \(\bar r-\bar R = 3.09\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm MTPA} = 4.909 \times 10^{-18}\) MeV (0.2% correction).
- MTPA for the \(2s_{1/2}\) resonance with \(\bar r-\bar R = 3.24\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm MTPA} = 6.692 \times 10^{-14}\) MeV (0% correction).
- MTPA for the \(1f_{5/2}\) resonance with \(\bar r-\bar R = 2.96\) fm gives \(\Gamma_{\rm MTPA} = 8.373 \times 10^{-2}\) MeV (10% correction).

## Context

This work extends the two-potential approach developed by Gurvitz and collaborators (Gurvitz 1988, 1990, 1991) by providing a systematic analysis of correction terms and a prescription for the optimal separation radius. It addresses the sensitivity of R-matrix theory (Lane 1960) to the matching radius, a problem noted by Humbel (1992) and Arima (1999). The MTPA enables accurate and parameter-free calculations of resonance widths for applications such as proton emission from exotic nuclei, previously treated with approximate formulas by Maglione (1998) and Ferreira (1997).
