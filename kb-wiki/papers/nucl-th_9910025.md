# Consequences of the center-of-mass correction in nuclear mean-field models

> We study the influence of the scheme for the correction for spurious center-of-mass motion on the fit of effective interactions for self-consistent nuclear mean-field calculations. We find that interactions with very simple center-of-mass correction have significantly larger surface coefficients than interactions for which the center-of-mass correction was calculated for the actual many-body state during the fit. The reason for that is that the effective interaction has to counteract the wrong trends with nucleon number of all simplified schemes for center-of-mass correction which puts a wrong trend with mass number into the effective interaction itself. The effect becomes clearly visible when looking at the deformation energy of largely deformed systems, e.g. superdeformed states or fission barriers of heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

The choice of approximation scheme for the center-of-mass correction during the fit of effective interactions systematically alters the surface coefficient of the interaction, with simpler schemes producing larger surface coefficients than schemes using the full microscopic correction, because the effective interaction must compensate for the incorrect mass-number trend of the simplified correction.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) and relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations for spherical and axially deformed nuclei, using five different schemes for the center-of-mass correction (ranging from a simple harmonic-oscillator estimate to the full microscopic correction included before variation). They compared nuclear matter properties (volume, symmetry, and surface coefficients) and deformation energy curves for 240Pu across pairs of interactions fitted identically except for the center-of-mass correction scheme.

## Key numbers

- Surface coefficient asurf for interactions using the microscopic center-of-mass correction is smaller by roughly 1.0 MeV compared to interactions using simpler schemes, as seen in the pairs Es (18.2 MeV, scheme C) vs Zs (16.9 MeV, scheme B) and SLy4 (18.2 MeV, scheme C) vs SLy6 (17.4 MeV, scheme A)
- The difference Delta Ecm = Ecm_dir - Ecm_mic for non-relativistic interactions fitted to the mass formula gives tilde_a_surf = 1.2 MeV for SkM*, Es, and SLy4, matching the actual difference Delta asurf between Es and Zs (1.3 MeV)
- For RMF forces, the difference Delta Ecm requires a curvature term; for NL1 the coefficients are tilde_a_vol = -0.12 MeV, tilde_a_surf = 1.2 MeV, and tilde_a_curv = -3.2 MeV, with Delta avol = -0.23 MeV and Delta asurf = 1.0 MeV between NL1 and NL-Z
- The harmonic oscillator estimate for the center-of-mass correction is Ecm_osc = -3/4 * 41 * A^{-1/3} MeV
- The fit approximation Ecm_fit = -17.2 * A^{-0.2} MeV is used for RMF forces NL1 and NL2

## Context

This work extends the analysis of center-of-mass correction schemes previously discussed by Ring and Schuck (1980) and by Butler (1984), and directly compares interactions such as the Skyrme forces SLy4 and SLy6 from Chabanat et al. (1997-1998) and the RMF forces NL1 and NL-Z from Reinhard et al. (1989). The findings explain why the Skyrme force SkM*, fitted to the fission barrier of 240Pu, gives a low surface coefficient despite using a simple center-of-mass correction, and demonstrate that interactions using the microscopic correction automatically yield reasonable surface properties without explicit surface fitting. The results enable improved understanding of extrapolations to superdeformed states and fission barriers in heavy nuclei.
