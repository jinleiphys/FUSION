# Causality Constraints on Hadron Production In High Energy Collisions

> For hadron production in high energy collisions, causality requirements lead to the counterpart of the cosmological horizon problem: the production occurs in a number of causally disconnected regions of finite space-time size. As a result, globally conserved quantum numbers (charge, strangeness, baryon number) must be conserved locally in spatially restricted correlation clusters. This provides a theoretical basis for the observed suppression of strangeness production in elementary interactions (pp, e^+e^-). In contrast, the space-time superposition of many collisions in heavy ion interactions largely removes these causality constraints, resulting in an ideal hadronic resonance gas in full equilibrium.

## Key claim

Causality constraints force hadron production in elementary high-energy collisions to occur in causally disconnected fireballs, which provides a theoretical basis for the observed strangeness suppression; in heavy-ion collisions, the superposition of many nucleon-nucleon interactions removes these constraints, allowing full hadronic equilibrium.

## Method

The authors model the space-time evolution of a high-energy collision as a boost-invariant inside-outside cascade, with partonisation time τ_q ≈ 1 fm and hadronisation time τ_h. They define a "causal fireball" by requiring that the most distant points of the fireball at formation and hadronisation can still exchange a light signal. Using this geometric criterion, they calculate the maximum rapidity interval over which fireballs remain causally connected, and then apply this to the statistical hadronisation model to explain strangeness suppression in elementary collisions versus its absence in nuclear collisions.

## Key numbers

- Partonisation time τ_q ≈ 1 fm (derived from string tension σ ≈ 0.2 GeV²)
- For τ_q = 1 fm and τ_h = 2 fm, fireballs with rapidity η > 0.7 are causally disconnected from the fireball at η = 0
- For τ_h = 7 fm, the rapidity horizon is only η_d = 2
- For a fireball at rest with τ_q = 1 fm and τ_h = 3 fm: velocity β = 0.50, rapidity η = 0.55, proper radius r = 1.7 fm
- For a fireball at rest with τ_q = 1 fm and τ_h = 5 fm: β = 0.67, η = 0.81, r = 4.5 fm
- The first moving fireball causally connected to the central one has β₁ = 0.80, η₁ = 1.10 for τ_h = 3 fm
- The universal hadronisation temperature from data is around 160 MeV
- Strangeness suppression factor γ_s is found to be 0.5 to 0.7 from about 20 GeV up to LHC energies in elementary collisions
- Fits to data at √s = 17.3 and 200 GeV require a strangeness correlation radius of about 1 fm
- At √s = 200 GeV, the separation between nucleons at opposite edges of colliding nuclei has decreased to 0.3 fm

## Context

This work extends the statistical hadronisation model of Becattini and others, which successfully describes hadron production using an ideal gas of resonances, by providing a causal explanation for the strangeness suppression factor γ_s introduced by Rafelski. The authors connect their causality analysis to the earlier strangeness correlation volume model of Hagedorn, Redlich, and Tounsi (HRT), showing that the correlation volume V_c is naturally the volume of a causally connected cluster. The paper also relates to the Unruh radiation approach of Castorina, Kharzeev, and Satz (CKS), which inherently contains local strangeness conservation. The results enable a unified understanding of why elementary collisions (pp, e⁺e⁻) show strangeness suppression while heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC approach full equilibrium.
