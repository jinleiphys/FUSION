# An accurate calculation of the nucleon axial charge with lattice QCD

> We report on a lattice QCD calculation of the nucleon axial charge, $g_A$, using Möbius Domain-Wall fermions solved on the dynamical $N_f=2+1+1$ HISQ ensembles after they are smeared using the gradient-flow algorithm. The calculation is performed with three pion masses, $m_π\sim\{310,220,130\}$ MeV. Three lattice spacings ($a\sim\{0.15,0.12,0.09\}$ fm) are used with the heaviest pion mass, while the coarsest two spacings are used on the middle pion mass and only the coarsest spacing is used with the near physical pion mass. On the $m_π\sim220$ MeV, $a\sim0.12$ fm point, a dedicated volume study is performed with $m_πL \sim \{3.22,4.29,5.36\}$. Using a new strategy motivated by the Feynman-Hellmann Theorem, we achieve a precise determination of $g_A$ with relatively low statistics, and demonstrable control over the excited state, continuum, infinite volume and chiral extrapolation systematic uncertainties, the latter of which remains the dominant uncertainty. Our final determination at 2.6\% total uncertainty is $g_A = 1.278(21)(26)$, with the first uncertainty including statistical and systematic uncertainties from fitting and the second including model selection systematics related to the chiral and continuum extrapolation. The largest reduction of the second uncertainty will come from a greater number of pion mass points as well as more precise lattice QCD results near the physical pion mass.

## Key claim

The paper reports a lattice QCD calculation of the nucleon axial charge \(g_A\) with a total uncertainty of 2.6%, yielding \(g_A = 1.278(21)(26)\), where the first uncertainty includes statistical and systematic uncertainties from fitting and the second includes model selection systematics from the chiral and continuum extrapolation. The result is compatible with the experimental PDG value of \(1.2723(23)\), but the chiral extrapolation remains the dominant systematic uncertainty, and the largest reduction of the second uncertainty will require more pion mass points and more precise results near the physical pion mass.

## Method

The calculation uses Möbius Domain-Wall fermions (MDWF) solved on dynamical \(N_f = 2+1+1\) HISQ gauge configurations generated by the MILC Collaboration, after smearing the gauge fields with the gradient-flow algorithm. A new strategy motivated by the Feynman-Hellmann theorem is employed to construct derivative correlation functions, which allows removal of excited-state contamination through extrapolation in a single time variable and eliminates the need to vary two time variables independently. Simultaneous fits are performed to the two-point correlation function together with the axial and vector effective derivatives, using three pion masses (\(m_\pi \sim \{310, 220, 130\}\) MeV), three lattice spacings (\(a \sim \{0.15, 0.12, 0.09\}\) fm), and a dedicated volume study on the \(m_\pi \sim 220\) MeV, \(a \sim 0.12\) fm ensemble with \(m_\pi L \sim \{3.22, 4.29, 5.36\}\).

## Key numbers

*   Final result: \(g_A = 1.278(21)(26)\), with a total uncertainty of 2.6%.
*   Experimental PDG value: \(g_A^{\text{exp}} = 1.2723(23)\).
*   Pion masses used: \(m_\pi \sim \{310, 220, 130\}\) MeV.
*   Lattice spacings used: \(a \sim \{0.15, 0.12, 0.09\}\) fm.
*   Volume study on the \(m_\pi \sim 220\) MeV, \(a \sim 0.12\) fm ensemble: \(m_\pi L \sim \{3.22, 4.29, 5.36\}\).
*   Previous LQCD result from Bhattacharya (2016): \(g_A^{\text{PNDME}} = 1.195(33)(20)\), which differs from the PDG value by 2 standard deviations.

## Context

This work extends the lattice QCD calculation of \(g_A\) by addressing the major systematic uncertainties that have historically made the quantity difficult to compute, particularly excited-state contamination, continuum and infinite volume limits, and chiral extrapolation. It improves upon the earlier calculation by Bhattacharya (2016), which reported \(g_A = 1.195(33)(20)\) and differed from experiment by 2 standard deviations, by using a Feynman-Hellmann inspired method that provides better control over excited-state systematics and reduces computational cost. The result enables future calculations of in-medium corrections to \(g_A\) in nuclei (the so-called \(g_A\) quenching effect), which is relevant for understanding potential signals for neutrinoless double-beta decay, and demonstrates that lattice QCD can achieve the precision needed to search for beyond-the-Standard-Model physics through comparisons with the experimental axial charge.
