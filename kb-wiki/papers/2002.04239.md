# Detection prospects for the second-order weak decays of $^{124}$Xe in multi-tonne xenon time projection chambers

> We investigate the detection prospects for two-neutrino and neutrinoless second order weak decays of $^{124}$Xe -- double electron capture ($0/2ν\text{ECEC}$), electron capture with positron emission ($0/2ν\text{EC}β^+$) and double-positron emission ($0/2νβ^+β^+$) -- in multi-tonne xenon time projection chambers. We simulate the decays in a liquid xenon medium and develop a reconstruction algorithm which uses the multi-particle coincidence in these decays to separate signal from background. This is used to compute the expected detection efficiencies as a function of position resolution and energy threshold for planned experiments. In addition, we consider an exhaustive list of possible background sources and find that they are either negligible in rate or can be greatly reduced using our topological reconstruction criteria. In particular, we draw two conclusions: First, with a half-life of $T_{1/2}^{2ν\text{EC}β^+} = (1.7 \pm 0.6)\cdot 10^{23}\,\text{yr}$, the $2ν\text{EC}β^+$ decay of $^{124}$Xe will likely be detected in upcoming Dark Matter experiments (e.g. XENONnT or LZ), and their major background will be from gamma rays in the detector construction materials. Second, searches for the $0ν\text{EC}β^+$ decay mode are likely to be background-free, and new parameter space may be within the reach. To this end we investigate three different scenarios of existing experimental constraints on the effective neutrino mass. The necessary 500 kg-year exposure of $^{124}$Xe could be achieved by the baseline design of the DARWIN observatory, or by extracting and using the $^{124}$Xe from the tailings of the nEXO experiment. We demonstrate how a combination of $^{124}$Xe results with those from $0νβ^-β^-$ searches in $^{136}$Xe could help to identify the neutrinoless decay mechanism.

# Detection prospects for the second-order weak decays of 124Xe in multi-tonne xenon time projection chambers

## Key claim

The two-neutrino electron capture with positron emission (2νECβ+) decay of 124Xe, with a half-life of (1.7 ± 0.6)·10^23 yr, will likely be detected in upcoming Generation 2 dark matter experiments such as XENONnT or LZ, provided that external gamma-ray backgrounds from detector construction materials can be sufficiently reduced via fiducial volume cuts. For the neutrinoless mode (0νECβ+), searches in both G2 and G3 experiments are expected to be background-free, and new parameter space may be within reach if a 500 kg-year exposure of 124Xe can be achieved.

## Method

The authors simulated the decay channels (2νECβ+, 0νECβ+, 2νβ+β+, 0νβ+β+, and 0νECEC) using the DECAY0 event generator, propagated particles through simplified liquid xenon detector models in Geant4, and developed a reconstruction algorithm that uses multi-particle coincidence and spatial clustering to separate signal from background. They computed detection efficiencies as functions of energy threshold, position resolution, and detector size for two classes of experiments: Generation 2 (G2) detectors modeled as 120 cm height/diameter cylinders and a Generation 3 (G3) detector modeled as a 250 cm cylinder. An exhaustive list of background sources was evaluated, including radiogenic gamma rays from detector materials, 222Rn, charged-current neutrino scattering, and neutron-induced backgrounds.

## Key numbers

- 2νECβ+ half-life of 124Xe: T1/2 = (1.7 ± 0.6)·10^23 yr
- Expected 2νECβ+ signal rate: 8.3 ± 2.9 decays per tonne-year of natural xenon (G2 experiment with 10 mm x-y-z resolution)
- Expected 0νECβ+ rate under light-neutrino exchange: less than 2.6·10^-2 decays per tonne-year of natural xenon
- Q-value of 124Xe decay: 2856.73(12) keV
- Energy available for positron in 0νECβ+: 1802.92(12) keV (after subtracting 1022.00 keV for 2mec^2 and 31.81 keV for K-shell hole)
- Energy available for two positrons in 0νβ+β+: 812.74(12) keV (after subtracting 2043.99 keV for 4mec^2)
- Resonance condition for 0νECEC: excitation energy 2792.27(13) keV, with nearest known state at 2790.41(9) keV (deviation of 1.86(15) keV)
- Detection efficiency for 2νECβ+ in G2 with 10 mm x-y resolution: 42%; in G3: 47%
- Detection efficiency for 0νECβ+ in G2 with 10 mm x-y resolution: 37%; in G3: 41%
- Detection efficiency for 0νECEC (three-gamma branch) in G2 with 10 mm x-y resolution: 28%; in G3: 33%
- Background from 214Bi in 238U chain passing 0νECβ+ cuts: 1.5·10^-6 events per primary decay
- Background from 208Tl in 232Th chain passing 0νECβ+ cuts: 4.5·10^-5 events per primary decay (after accounting for 35.9% branching)
- Background from 238U chain passing 2νECβ+ cuts: 6.9·10^-3 events per primary decay (no fiducial cut), reduced to 1.1·10^-4 with 20 cm fiducial cut
- Background from 232Th chain passing 2νECβ+ cuts: 7.3·10^-3 events per primary decay (no fiducial cut), reduced to 1.3·10^-4 with 20 cm fiducial cut
- Geoneutrino CC scattering rate on 129Xe: 5.0·10^-8 events per tonne-year of natural Xe; on 131Xe: 4.9·10^-6 events per tonne-year
- Reactor antineutrino CC scattering rate at SNOLAB on 129Xe: 9.1·10^-7 events per tonne-year; on 131Xe: 3.6·10^-6 events per tonne-year
- Production rate of 128Cs from solar neutrino CC scattering: 0.02 nuclei per tonne-year of natural Xe; of 130Cs: 0.07 nuclei per tonne-year
- Energy resolution model: σE/E = a/√E + b with a = 31 keV^1/2 and b = 0.37 (combined signal), giving ~1% at the Q-value
- Charge-only resolution: b scaled to 4.4, giving ~6% at ~500 keV
- 124Xe abundance: approximately 1 kg per tonne of natural xenon
- G2 dark matter experiments: ~50-100 kg-year 124Xe exposure in 10 years
- G3 DARWIN experiment: ~500 kg-year 124Xe exposure
- nEXO tailings: approximately 50 kg of 124Xe available

## Context

This work extends the study of second-order weak decays of 124Xe, building on previous simulations by Barros (2014) and the DECAY0 event generator by Tretyak. The authors provide the first detailed detection feasibility study for these decay modes in multi-tonne xenon time projection chambers, specifically targeting the upcoming XENONnT, LZ, nEXO, and DARWIN experiments. The results enable future experimental searches for both two-neutrino and neutrinoless modes, and the authors demonstrate how combining 124Xe results with 0νβ-β- searches in 136Xe (as pursued by EXO-200 and nEXO) could help identify the neutrinoless decay mechanism. The background analysis extends the material screening and simulation work reported by the nEXO collaboration (2018) and the XENON collaboration (2019).
