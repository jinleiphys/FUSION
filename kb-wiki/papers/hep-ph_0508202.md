# New Test of Supernova Electron Neutrino Emission using Sudbury Neutrino Observatory Sensitivity to the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background

> Supernovae are rare nearby, but they are not rare in the Universe, and all past core-collapse supernovae contributed to the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB), for which the near-term detection prospects are very good. The Super-Kamiokande limit on the DSNB electron {\it antineutrino} flux, $ϕ(E_ν> 19.3 {\rm MeV}) < 1.2$ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$, is just above the range of recent theoretical predictions based on the measured star formation rate history. We show that the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory should be able to test the corresponding DSNB electron {\it neutrino} flux with a sensitivity as low as $ϕ(22.5 < E_ν< 32.5 {\rm MeV}) \simeq 6 $ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$, improving the existing Mont Blanc limit by about three orders of magnitude. While conventional supernova models predict comparable electron neutrino and antineutrino fluxes, it is often considered that the first (and forward-directed) SN 1987A event in the Kamiokande-II detector should be attributed to electron-neutrino scattering with an electron, which would require a substantially enhanced electron neutrino flux. We show that with the required enhancements in either the burst or thermal phase $ν_e$ fluxes, the DSNB electron neutrino flux would generally be detectable in the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory. A direct experimental test could then resolve one of the enduring mysteries of SN 1987A: whether the first Kamiokande-II event reveals a serious misunderstanding of supernova physics, or was simply an unlikely statistical fluctuation. Thus the electron neutrino sensitivity of the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory is an important complement to the electron antineutrino sensitivity of Super-Kamiokande in the quest to understand the DSNB.

## Key claim

The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO) should be able to test the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB) electron neutrino flux with a sensitivity as low as approximately 6 cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$ in the energy range 22.5–32.5 MeV, provided that the required enhancements to the electron neutrino flux from SN 1987A (to explain the forward-directed first event in Kamiokande-II) are real rather than statistical fluctuations.

## Method

The authors estimate the SNO sensitivity to the DSNB electron neutrino flux using the charged-current reaction $\nu_e + d \rightarrow e^- + p + p$ on deuterons in 1 kton of heavy water. They calculate expected signal rates for a 5 kton-yr exposure, using theoretical DSNB spectra based on the Concordance Model for the star formation rate history convolved with supernova neutrino emission parameters, and estimate backgrounds from solar $hep$ neutrinos below 20 MeV and from invisible muon decays produced by atmospheric neutrinos above 30 MeV. The sensitivity criterion requires at least 1.6 signal events in the 20–30 MeV electron energy range, exceeding the square root of the estimated 2.5 background events.

## Key numbers

* Super-Kamiokande limit on DSNB electron antineutrino flux: $\phi(E_\nu > 19.3\ {\rm MeV}) < 1.2$ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$
* Projected SNO sensitivity to DSNB electron neutrino flux: $\phi(22.5 < E_\nu < 32.5\ {\rm MeV}) \simeq 6$ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$
* This SNO sensitivity improves the existing Mont Blanc limit ($\phi(25 < E_\nu < 50\ {\rm MeV}) < 6.8 \times 10^3$ cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$) by about three orders of magnitude
* Estimated invisible muon decay background rate in SNO: 0.5 per kton per year in the 20–30 MeV range
* For a 5 kton-yr exposure, estimated background events $N_B = 2.5$ and required signal events $N_S > \sqrt{N_B} = 1.6$
* Number of deuterons per kton of D$_2$O: $N_d = 6 \times 10^{31}$
* Predicted DSNB $\nu_e$ flux in the 22.5–32.5 MeV range for $T = 4$ MeV: 0.2 cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$; for $T = 8$ MeV: 0.9 cm$^{-2}$ s$^{-1}$
* Required flux enhancement factor relative to standard predictions ranges from 32 (for $T = 4$ MeV) to 6 (for $T = 8$ MeV)

## Context

This work extends the DSNB analysis framework developed by Strigari et al. (2005) in their Concordance Model, which used the most recent astronomical data on star formation rates from GALEX and other surveys to predict DSNB fluxes near the Super-Kamiokande limit. The paper directly addresses the unresolved mystery from SN 1987A observations by Kamiokande-II and IMB: whether the forward-directed first event and the generally forward angular distribution indicate enhanced electron neutrino emission (possibly from the neutronization burst or thermal phase) or are simply statistical fluctuations. The proposed SNO measurement would complement the Super-Kamiokande electron antineutrino sensitivity and could resolve whether standard supernova models are seriously incomplete, or whether the SN 1987A angular anomalies were statistical in nature.
