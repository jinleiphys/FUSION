# Quantum simulation of real-time current correlators and DIS-inspired observables in the Schwinger model

> Hadronic tensors encode the nonperturbative structure of hadrons probed in deep inelastic scattering (DIS), yet their direct evaluation requires real-time evolution that presents a challenge for traditional Euclidean lattice approaches. In this work, we present the first quantum simulation of real-time hadronic current-current correlators in a confining gauge theory, from which DIS-inspired structure functions are extracted as a proof-of-principle demonstration in the Schwinger model, i.e (1+1)-dimensional QED. Using two complementary quantum-simulation strategies -- quantum-circuit and tensor-network methods -- we compute the real-time current-current correlator directly on the lattice and validate our results against exact diagonalization where applicable. From this correlator, we compute the hadronic tensor and determine the longitudinal structure function, the sole nonvanishing DIS observable in two space-time dimensions. Our study demonstrates that quantum simulation offers a viable complementary pathway towards the evaluation of real-time observables relevant for hadronic structure. It also provides a foundation for extending the calculations from Schwinger model to other gauge theories.

## Key claim

The authors present the first quantum simulation of real-time hadronic current-current correlators in a confining gauge theory, from which DIS-inspired structure functions are extracted as a proof-of-principle demonstration in the Schwinger model. The longitudinal structure function computed here should be understood as a model observable probing the real-time response of the confined spectrum, rather than a phenomenological structure function of QCD, because in 1+1 dimensions the electromagnetic field carries no propagating dynamical degrees of freedom and the DIS kinematic picture does not apply literally.

## Method

The authors discretize the (1+1)-dimensional QED (Schwinger model) Hamiltonian using staggered fermions on a lattice, then map it to qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. They compute the hadronic tensor by evaluating the real-time current-current correlator matrix element between two spacetime points for a hadron state, using two complementary approaches: quantum-circuit simulations with Trotterized time evolution and tensor-network simulations using the time-dependent variational principle (TDVP) algorithm. The hadronic state is prepared as the first excited charge-neutral state using the density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) algorithm, and the longitudinal structure function is obtained from the hadronic tensor via discrete Fourier transforms.

## Key numbers

- Hadron mass at m/g=0.5 and ag=0.2 is 1.538, differing from the continuum-extrapolated value of 1.495 by about 3%
- In the massless limit, the exact analytical result for hadron mass is M_h/g = 1/sqrt(pi) approximately 0.564
- Tensor-network simulations used a maximum bond dimension of chi_max=800 in DMRG, with resulting MPS bond dimensions around chi less than or equal to 50 for the vacuum state and chi less than or equal to 150 for the hadron state
- Quantum-circuit simulations used 100,000 sampling shots with a time step of 0.1 on an ideal simulator
- The x_B values from the N=120 qubit simulation lie approximately in the range 0.5334 to 0.579
- A peak in the longitudinal structure function is observed at Q^2 approximately 15 GeV^2 with magnitude in the range 0.1 to 1

## Context

This work extends recent quantum simulation efforts for real-time observables in gauge theories, including calculations of hadronic vacuum polarization by Barata (2024) and parton distribution functions by Lamm (2019), Kreshchuk (2020), and Qian (2021). It contrasts with traditional Euclidean lattice QCD approaches that require solving an ill-conditioned inverse Laplace transform for real-time information, as discussed by Liu (1993, 1999) and Liang (2019). The results provide a foundation for extending calculations from the Schwinger model to other gauge theories, including QCD in higher dimensions, and enable future work on wave-packet states for broader kinematic coverage of x_B values.
