# In-medium effects on electromagnetic probes

> We discuss some of the aspects of the physics of relativistic nuclear collisions, in particular those having to do with the observation of electromagnetic radiation. We concentrate on what such measurements tell us about the local, in-medium properties of the environment from which they emerge. The contribution from different sources are considered: that from the partonic sector of QCD, and that from the confined hadronic phase. Specifically, we discuss the observation of real photons and of lepton pairs at the SPS and at RHIC, and make predictions for the LHC. The role of jets is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while low-mass dilepton and low-pT photon data from the SPS are consistent with modified in-medium vector spectral densities, the case for unambiguous observation of a quark-gluon plasma through electromagnetic probes at SPS energies "can't be made convincingly." At RHIC and LHC energies, however, jet-plasma interactions produce a significant photon source that outshines other contributions at intermediate transverse momentum.

## Method

The author reviews calculations of real photon and dilepton production rates using two complementary approaches: effective hadronic Lagrangians (massive Yang-Mills formalism with chiral U(3)L x U(3)R symmetry) and forward scattering amplitudes fitted to experimental data. These rates are integrated over space-time histories using either boost-invariant hydrodynamics or fireball models, with initial temperatures, critical temperatures, and freeze-out temperatures as parameters. For high-pT photons and jets at RHIC and LHC, the AMY (Arnold-Moore-Yaffe) formalism is used to evolve jet momentum distributions through the medium, accounting for energy loss via gluon bremsstrahlung.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature of 330 MeV, critical temperature of 180 MeV, and freeze-out temperature of 120 MeV used in the hydrodynamic calculation for intermediate-mass dileptons at SPS
- At RHIC, the jet-plasma photon source outshines other sources at pT ~ 4 GeV/c; at LHC, this occurs at pT ~ 8 GeV/c
- Strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.3 used in the AMY jet-quenching calculation
- Critical temperature of 160 MeV assumed for the first-order phase transition in the jet evolution model
- Broadening parameter for nuclear transverse momentum broadening: <Delta k_T^2> ~ 0.1 - 0.2 GeV^2 from p-A data analyses
- The intermediate-mass NA50 data yields a plasma radiation component of about 20% of the total

## Context

This review extends the line of work on electromagnetic probes of relativistic nuclear collisions, building on prior calculations of vector spectral densities by Rapp and Wambach (1999) and by Eletsky et al. (2001). The paper connects low-mass dilepton measurements from the CERES and Helios/3 collaborations at SPS with real photon data from WA98, showing they can be consistently described by hadronic degrees of freedom. At higher energies, the work applies the AMY jet-quenching formalism to predict photon yields at RHIC and LHC, enabling quantitative comparison with PHENIX data and providing a framework for interpreting future LHC measurements.
