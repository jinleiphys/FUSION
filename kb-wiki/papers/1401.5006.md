# Effects of AMM on the EoS of Magnetized Dense Systems

> We investigate the effects of the anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) in the EoS of a fermion system in the presence of a magnetic field. In the region of strong magnetic fields ($B>m^2$) the AMM is found from the one-loop fermion self-energy. In contrast to the weak-field AMM found by Schwinger, in the strong magnetic field case, the AMM depends on the Landau level (LL) and decreases with it. The effects of the AMM in the EoS at intermediate-to-large fields can be found introducing the one-loop, LL-dependent AMM in the effective Lagrangian that is then used to find the thermodynamic potential of the system. We compare the plots of the parallel and perpendicular pressures versus the magnetic field in the strong field region considering the LL-dependent AMM, the Schwinger AMM, and no AMM at all. The results clearly show a separation between the physical magnitudes found using the Schwinger AMM and the LL-dependent AMM. This is an indication of the inconsistency of considering the Schwinger AMM beyond the weak field region $B< m^2$ where it was originally found. The curves for the EoS, pressures and magnetization at different fields give rise to the well-known de Haas van Alphen oscillations, associated to the change in the number of LL contributing at different fields.

# Effects of AMM on the EoS of Magnetized Dense Systems

## Key claim
The one-loop, Landau-level-dependent anomalous magnetic moment (AMM) calculated in the strong-field region (B > m²) has a negligible effect on the equation of state and thermodynamic quantities of a charged fermion system, contrary to previous claims that the Schwinger AMM stiffens the EoS. Using the Schwinger AMM beyond its weak-field validity region (B < m²) overestimates the AMM's relevance and leads to incorrect physical interpretations.

## Method
The authors computed the one-loop fermion self-energy in a constant, uniform magnetic field to extract the AMM and mass corrections as functions of the Landau level (LL) for the strong-field region (m² ≪ B ≤ μ²). They incorporated these LL-dependent radiative corrections into the fermion dispersion relations and then into the thermodynamic potential at zero temperature, from which they derived the particle density, energy density, magnetization, and parallel/perpendicular pressures. For comparison, they repeated the calculations using the weak-field Schwinger AMM (linear in B) and with no AMM at all.

## Key numbers
- The threshold field for validity of the Schwinger AMM is B ≃ 10¹³ G for electrons and B ≃ 10¹⁵ G for u quarks with current mass of 5 MeV.
- Numerical results are presented in the region 0.1 < 2eB/μ² < 1, with μ = 300 MeV used for the energy density and pressure plots.
- The LLL rest energy in the strong-field approximation is ε⁰_LLL = |m + E⁰|, where E⁰ = (α/4π) m log²(m̂²) in the leading strong-field approximation.
- The Schwinger AMM is T_Sch = κ μ_B B, with κ = α/(2π) and μ_B = e/(2m).

## Context
This work extends the calculation of the AMM from the one-loop self-energy in strong magnetic fields, building on prior work by Ferrer et al. (various years) and Jancovici (year not specified), who pointed out limitations of the Schwinger AMM. The results contradict earlier studies by Chakrabarty et al., Lattimer et al., Dexheimer, and Strickland (various years), who reported that the Schwinger AMM stiffens the EoS of magnetized neutron star matter. The paper enables a correct treatment of AMM effects in dense magnetized systems by showing that the LL-dependent AMM is negligible at strong fields, and it identifies the incorrect use of the LLL dispersion relation with the Schwinger AMM as a likely cause of the previously reported EoS stiffening.
