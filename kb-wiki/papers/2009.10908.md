# On the Speed of Sound in Hyperonic Stars

> We build upon the remarkable, model independent constraints on the equation of state of dense baryonic matter established recently by Annala et al. [1]. Using the quark-meson coupling model, an approach to nuclear structure based upon the self-consistent adjustment of hadron structure to the local meson fields, we show that, once hyperons are allowed to appear in dense matter in \b{eta}-equilibrium, the equation of state is consistent with those constraints. As a result, while one cannot rule out the occurence of quark matter in the cores of massive neutron stars, the available constraints are also compatible with the presence of hyperons.

## Key claim

The model-independent constraints on the equation of state that Annala et al. (2020) interpreted as evidence for quark matter in massive neutron star cores are also fully consistent with the presence of hyperons, once hyperons are allowed to appear in dense matter under beta-equilibrium; the paper does not rule out quark matter but shows that hyperons provide an equally viable explanation.

## Method

The authors used the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, which describes nuclear matter through the self-consistent modification of baryon structure in relativistic mean fields generated by meson coupling to confined quarks, with baryon internal structure described by the MIT Bag model including one-gluon-exchange. They solved for the composition of dense matter in beta-equilibrium, allowing all baryons of the octet to appear, and computed the resulting equation of state, speed of sound, and polytropic index as functions of density. The model parameters were fitted to reproduce standard nuclear matter properties (saturation density, symmetry energy, binding energy) and free-space baryon masses, with no additional parameters for hyperon interactions.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: \(n_0 = 0.148\ \text{fm}^{-3}\)
- Symmetry energy: \(S = 30\ \text{MeV}\)
- Binding energy per nucleon: \(\mathcal{E} = -15.8\ \text{MeV}\)
- Maximum neutron star mass including hyperons: approximately \(1.95\ M_\odot\), compatible with the J1614 pulsar mass of \(1.908 \pm 0.016\ M_\odot\) (Arzoumanian) within one standard deviation
- Radius of a \(1.4\ M_\odot\) neutron star: \(12.8\ \text{km}\)
- Tidal deformability of a \(1.4\ M_\odot\) neutron star: \(420\), consistent with GW170817 constraints
- Core density of the largest mass stars in the model: roughly \(1\ \text{fm}^{-3}\)
- Polytropic index falls below \(1.75\) for energy densities above \(600\ \text{MeV/fm}^3\)
- Bag radius: \(R_B = 0.8\ \text{fm}\); sigma meson mass: \(m_\sigma = 700\ \text{MeV}\)

## Context

This work directly extends the model-independent analysis of Annala et al. (2020), who used speed-of-sound interpolation to generate equations of state and concluded that quark matter cores in massive neutron stars should be considered the standard scenario. The authors show that the same observational constraints (maximum mass above \(1.97\ M_\odot\), tidal deformability from GW170817) are also satisfied by the QMC model when hyperons are included, contradicting the interpretation that a polytropic index below 1.75 uniquely signals quark matter. This result builds on earlier work by Stone et al. (2007), who first showed that the QMC model could produce \(2\ M_\odot\) neutron stars with hyperons, and it challenges the community to find new observational tests to distinguish between hyperonic and quark matter cores.
