# How and How Much is ${g_A}$ {\it Fundamentally} Quenched in Nuclei?

> The superallowed Gamow-Teller transition in the doubly-magic-shell nucleus $^{100}$Sn and the high resolution spectral shape analysis in the fourth-forbidden nonunique transition in $^{115}$In indicate as much as $\sim 40\%$ {\it fundamental} quenching in the axial-current coupling constant $g_A$ in nuclei. This can be attributed to an effect of the trace anomaly in QCD "emerging" in nuclear medium. If confirmed, this would signal a major revamping to do in nuclear interactions consistent with chiral-scale symmetry in nuclear medium and a big impact on $0ö§$ and $ö§ö§$ double $öý$ decays for BSM. I present an argument that such a big anomaly-induced quenching is incompatible with how hidden scale symmetry manifests in nuclear medium, A possible means to resolve this issue is discussed in terms of hidden scale symmetry permeating in baryonic matter from normal nuclear matter to massive compact-star matter.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the approximately 40% "fundamental" quenching of the axial-current coupling constant gA observed in the superallowed Gamow-Teller transition in 100Sn and in spectral shape analyses of forbidden decays in 115In is incompatible with how hidden scale symmetry manifests in nuclear medium, and that the anomaly-induced quenching interpretation conflicts with established results from other nuclear weak processes such as first-forbidden transitions.

## Method

The author applies the Fermi-liquid fixed-point (FLFP) approximation within a nuclear effective field theory called GnEFT, which implements hidden local symmetry and hidden scale symmetry, to compute the Gamow-Teller strength for the superallowed transition in the doubly-magic-shell nucleus 100Sn. The FLFP calculation is mapped to the Extreme Single Particle Model (ESPM) in shell-model language, and the resulting prediction is compared with experimental data from the RIKEN and GSI measurements. The author also analyzes the spectral-shape method (SSM) for multiply forbidden nonunique beta decays in heavy nuclei and examines the first-forbidden axial-charge transitions in lead nuclei as a consistency check.

## Key numbers

- Free-space axial coupling constant: gA = 1.276 (from neutron beta decay)
- RIKEN measurement of Gamow-Teller strength in 100Sn: B_GT = 4.4 (+0.9 / -0.7), implying approximately 40% quenching relative to the FLFP prediction
- FLFP prediction for Gamow-Teller strength: B_Landau_GT approximately 11, corresponding to gA* approximately 1
- GSI measurement of 100Sn Gamow-Teller strength: B_GT approximately 10, consistent with the FLFP prediction
- Anomaly-induced quenching factor from RIKEN data: q_ssb approximately 0.6
- Anomaly-induced quenching factor from 115In spectral shape analysis: q_ssb approximately 0.65-0.75, giving quenched gA* approximately 0.83-0.96
- Nuclear correlation factor from Landau Fermi-liquid theory: q_Landau_snc approximately 0.8
- Experimental value for the axial-charge enhancement factor in lead nuclei: epsilon_MEC_exp = 2.01 +/- 0.05
- Predicted axial-charge enhancement factor without anomaly-induced quenching: epsilon_MEC(q_ssb=1) approximately 2.0
- Predicted axial-charge enhancement factor with anomaly-induced quenching: epsilon_MEC(q_ssb=0.6) approximately 1.5

## Context

This paper extends the author's previous work with Ma (2020) on quenched gA in nuclei and emergent scale symmetry, and directly addresses the tension between the RIKEN measurement (Lubos et al., 2019) and the GSI measurement (Hinke et al., 2012) of the 100Sn Gamow-Teller decay. The analysis contradicts the interpretation of Gysbers et al. (2019) that most quenching can be shifted to two-body exchange currents by changing the resolution scale, and challenges the spectral-shape method results of Suhonen and collaborators (2016, 2021, 2022) for forbidden decays. The paper enables a focused experimental and theoretical effort to identify the ESPM final state in 100In and to resolve whether the anomaly-induced quenching interpretation is correct, which has major implications for neutrinoless double-beta decay searches.
