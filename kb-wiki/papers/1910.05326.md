# X-ray assisted nuclear excitation by electron capture in optical laser-generated plasmas

> X-ray assisted nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) into inner-shell atomic holes in a plasma environment generated by strong optical lasers is investigated theoretically. The considered scenario involves the interaction of a strong optical laser with a solid-state nuclear target leading to the generation of a plasma. In addition, intense x-ray radiation from an X-ray Free Electron Laser (XFEL) produces inner-shell holes in the plasma ions, into which NEEC may occur. As case study we consider the $4.85$-keV transition starting from the 2.4 MeV long-lived $^{\mathrm{93m}}$Mo isomer that can be used to release the energy stored in this metastable nuclear state. We find that the recombination into $2p_{1/2}$ inner-shell holes is most efficient in driving the nuclear transition. Already at few hundred eV plasma temperature, the generation of inner-shell holes can allow optimal conditions for NEEC, otherwise reached for steady-state plasma conditions in thermodynamical equilibrium only at few keV. The combination of x-ray and optical lasers presents two advantages: first, NEEC rates can be maximized at plasma temperatures where the photoexcitation rate remains low. Second, with mJ-class optical lasers and an XFEL repetition rate of $10$ kHz, the NEEC excitation number can reach $\sim 1$ depleted isomer per second and is competitive with scenarios recently envisaged at petawatt-class lasers.

## Key claim

X-ray assisted nuclear excitation by electron capture (NEEC) into inner-shell atomic holes in optical laser-generated plasmas can achieve NEEC rates at few hundred eV plasma temperatures that are otherwise only reached at few keV in steady-state thermodynamical equilibrium, provided the plasma density is low enough (below approximately 10^23 cm^-3) that inner-shell hole lifetimes are not too short.

## Method

The authors model a two-stage scenario: first, a mJ-class optical laser generates a plasma from a solid-state nuclear target containing 93mMo isomers; second, an XFEL pulse produces L-shell holes (2s, 2p1/2, 2p3/2) via photoionization. NEEC rates are calculated by summing over charge states and capture channels using relativistic Dirac-Fock wave functions for bound states, Dirac continuum wave functions, and a relativistic Fermi-Dirac free-electron distribution, with charge state distributions from the FLYCHK radiative-collisional code. The number of depleted isomers is obtained by integrating the NEEC rate over the plasma volume and lifetime, with the inner-shell hole lifetime determined by the shorter of the XFEL pulse duration (100 fs), the atomic recombination timescale, or the plasma lifetime.

## Key numbers

- The 93mMo isomer has a half-life of 6.85 h and lies at 2.4 MeV; the triggering transition energy is 4.85 keV (E2).
- NEEC resonance strengths for capture into the L shell lie between approximately 10^-3 b eV and 10^-5 b eV; capture into other shells is less than or equal to 10^-5 b eV.
- Resonance energies for L-shell capture require free electron energies between 52 eV and 597 eV.
- For electron densities of 10^19 cm^-3 and 10^21 cm^-3, x-ray assisted NEEC enhances the excitation number compared to thermodynamical equilibrium conditions; at 10^23 cm^-3 the effect is negligible.
- With a 100 mJ or 10 mJ optical laser and an XFEL repetition rate of 10 kHz, the NEEC excitation number can reach approximately 1 depleted isomer per second.
- The plasma lifetime is on the order of 10 ps or longer; the XFEL pulse duration is 100 fs.
- The laser absorption coefficient lies between 0.1 and 0.2 for the intensity range considered.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of NEEC in XFEL-generated cold plasmas (Gunst 2014, Gunst 2015) and in optical-laser-generated plasmas (Wu 2018, Gunst 2018) by introducing a combined two-laser scheme that decouples the free electron energy condition from the capture state generation condition. The scenario enables NEEC at low plasma temperatures where competing nuclear photoexcitation from thermal photons (dominant above approximately 1.6 keV for n_e = 10^21 cm^-3) remains small, allowing unambiguous identification of the NEEC mechanism. The results are relevant for upcoming combined optical and XFEL facilities such as HIBEF at the European XFEL, and for other XFEL facilities (LCLS, SACLA, SwissFEL) paired with commercial mJ-class optical lasers.
