# Regarding proton form factors

> The proton's elastic electromagnetic form factors are calculated using an Ansatz for the nucleon's Poincare' covariant Faddeev amplitude that only retains scalar diquark correlations. A spectator approximation is employed for the current. On the domain of q^2 accessible in modern precision experiments these form factors are a sensitive probe of nonperturbative strong interaction dynamics. The ratio of Pauli and Dirac form factors can provide realistic constraints on models of the nucleon and thereby assist in developing an understanding of nucleon structure.

## Key claim

The proton's elastic electromagnetic form factors are calculated using a simplified model that retains only scalar diquark correlations, with the explicit acknowledgment that this model must be incomplete because it neglects axial-vector diquark correlations and the nucleon's pion cloud; the authors argue that even this rudimentary Ansatz can bracket experimental data on the ratio \(F_2^p/\kappa F_1^p\), but caution that reproducing that ratio alone does not guarantee a veracious description of the individual form factors.

## Method

The authors employ a Poincaré covariant product Ansatz for the nucleon's Faddeev amplitude that represents the proton as a composite of a dressed quark and a nonpointlike scalar diquark correlation, using algebraic parametrisations for the dressed-quark propagator, the diquark Bethe-Salpeter amplitude and propagator, and the quark-diquark correlation function. The dressed-quark-photon vertex is given by a Ball-Chiu Ansatz that satisfies the vector Ward-Takahashi identity, and the nucleon-photon vertex is constructed from a spectator approximation with five one-body current diagrams evaluated via Monte Carlo integration.

## Key numbers

* The Euclidean constituent-quark mass is \(M_{u,d}^E = 0.33\) GeV, defined as the solution of \(p^2 = M^2(p^2)\).
* The vacuum quark condensate is \(-\langle \bar q q \rangle_0^{1\,{\rm GeV}^2} = (0.221\,{\rm GeV})^3\).
* The dimensionless current-quark mass corresponds to \(m = 5.1\) MeV.
* For the parameter set with \({\rm R}=0.25\): the proton charge radius squared is \((0.65)^2\) fm\(^2\), the neutron charge radius squared is \(-(0.38)^2\) fm\(^2\), the proton magnetic moment is \(2.58\) \(\mu_N\), and the neutron magnetic moment is \(-1.39\) \(\mu_N\).
* For the parameter set with \({\rm R}=0.50\): the proton charge radius squared is \((0.61)^2\) fm\(^2\), the neutron charge radius squared is \(-(0.37)^2\) fm\(^2\), the proton magnetic moment is \(2.52\) \(\mu_N\), and the neutron magnetic moment is \(-1.37\) \(\mu_N\).
* The experimental values quoted for comparison are: proton charge radius squared \((0.87)^2\) fm\(^2\), neutron charge radius squared \(-(0.34)^2\) fm\(^2\), proton magnetic moment \(2.79\) \(\mu_N\), and neutron magnetic moment \(-1.91\) \(\mu_N\).
* The dipole mass used in the fit to \(G_E^p(q^2) = 1/(1+q^2/m_d^2)^2\) is \(m_d = 1.1\) GeV, corresponding to a proton charge radius of \(0.62\) fm.
* The length scales from the fitted parameters are: \(\lambda_{0^+}=1/m_{0^+} \sim 1/3\) fm, \(l_{0^+}=1/\omega_{0^+} \sim 1/7\) fm, and \(l_{q\{qq\}} = 1/\omega_{q\{qq\}} \sim 2/3\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the Dyson-Schwinger equation approach to nucleon form factors previously developed in Bloch (2000) and Bloch (2001), using a scalar-diquark-only truncation of the Poincaré covariant Faddeev equation. The study is motivated by contemporary experimental data from Jones (2000), Gayou (2002), and others that suggest a single dipole mass cannot simultaneously describe the proton's electric and magnetic form factors, a possibility already evident in the earlier calculations of Bloch (2000). The authors explicitly note that their model omits axial-vector diquark correlations and pion cloud effects, which Oettel (2000) and Leinweber (2001) have shown contribute significantly to nucleon electromagnetic properties; the present calculation therefore serves to illustrate the sensitivity of form factor ratios to nonperturbative dynamics and to provide constraints for more complete future calculations that include these missing contributions.
