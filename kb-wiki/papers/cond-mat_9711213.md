# Interaction-Driven Equilibrium and Statistical Laws in Small Systems. The Cerium Atom

> It is shown that statistical mechanics is applicable to isolated quantum systems with finite numbers of particles, such as complex atoms, atomic clusters, or quantum dots in solids, where the residual two-body interaction is sufficiently strong. This interaction mixes the unperturbed shell-model (Hartree-Fock) basis states and produces chaotic many-body eigenstates. As a result, an interaction-induced statistical equilibrium emerges in the system. This equilibrium is due to the off-diagonal matrix elements of the Hamiltonian. We show that it can be described by means of temperature introduced through the canonical-type distribution. However, the interaction between the particles can lead to prominent deviations of the equilibrium distribution of the occupation numbers from the Fermi-Dirac shape. Besides that, the off-diagonal part of the Hamiltonian gives rise to the increase of the effective temperature of the system (statistical effect of the interaction). For example, this takes place in the cerium atom which has four valence electrons and which is used in our work to compare the theory with realistic numerical calculations.

## Key claim

Statistical mechanics can be applied to isolated few-particle quantum systems (such as the cerium atom with only four valence electrons) when the residual two-body interaction is strong enough to produce chaotic compound eigenstates, but the resulting equilibrium distribution of occupation numbers can deviate prominently from the Fermi-Dirac shape due to strong fluctuations of the two-body matrix elements between different orbitals.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock-Dirac (HFD) and configuration interaction (CI) calculations for the cerium atom, using 14 relativistic subshells in the single-particle basis and exact diagonalization of an approximately 8000 x 8000 Hamiltonian matrix for even states with total angular momentum projection J_z = 0. They computed the exact eigenstates and eigenvalues, then averaged the occupation numbers over energy intervals to obtain n_s(E). Separately, they constructed a statistical model using the canonical ensemble with diagonal approximation energies E_k (including averaged two-body Coulomb matrix elements U_sp) and compared the thermally averaged occupation numbers n_s(T) to the dynamical CI results.

## Key numbers

- The cerium atom has four valence electrons and an ionization threshold of I = 5.539 eV.
- The Hilbert space dimension for the CI calculation was N ~ 8 x 10^3 basis states.
- The numbers of eigenstates with total angular momentum J = 0 through 10 are 343, 917, 1354, 1493, 1433, 1153, 826, 497, 262, 107, and 34, respectively.
- The density of energy levels with given J and parity reaches hundreds of levels per eV at excitation energies of just a few eV.
- The level density follows rho(E) proportional to exp(a sqrt(E - E_g)) at low energies.
- The occupation numbers were calculated at an excitation energy of 3.75 eV above the atomic ground state for comparison with the Fermi-Dirac distribution.

## Context

This work extends the study of statistical mechanics in finite quantum systems previously examined in rare-earth atoms (Ce), 12 nucleons in the s-d shell (Zel), and n = 4-7 particles with two-body random interaction (Flambaum and collaborators, 1996-1997). The paper shows that the strong Coulomb interaction in cerium, particularly the hierarchy where U_{4f4f} > U_{4fs} > U_{sp} due to the compact 4f orbital, causes breakdown of the standard Fermi-Dirac distribution and of the usual Migdal theory of normal finite Fermi systems (TFFS). The results enable a temperature-based statistical description for few-particle systems where the mean-field approximation fails, and establish criteria for when such a description is valid.
