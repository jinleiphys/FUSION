# Nuclear Reactions Important in Alpha-Rich Freezeouts

> The alpha-rich freezeout from equilibrium occurs during the core-collapse explosion of a massive star when the supernova shock wave passes through the Si-rich shell of the star. The nuclei are heated to high temperature and broken down into nucleons and alpha particles. These subsequently reassemble as the material expands and cools, thereby producing new heavy nuclei, including a number of important supernova observables. In this paper we introduce two web-based applications. The first displays the results of a reaction-rate sensitivity study of alpha-rich freezeout yields. The second allows the interested reader to run paramaterized explosive silicon burning calculations in which the user inputs his own parameters. These tools are intended to aid in the identification of nuclear reaction rates important for experimental study. We then analyze several iron-group isotopes (59Ni, 57Co, 56Co, and 55Fe) in terms of their roles as observables and examine the reaction rates that are important in their production.

## Key claim

The paper introduces two web-based applications for identifying nuclear reaction rates important in alpha-rich freezeout nucleosynthesis, but the analysis is limited to a specific set of expansion parameters (initial temperature T9 = 5.5, initial density ρ0 = 10^7 g/cm^3, density e-folding timescale τex = 0.141 s) and only three values of neutron excess (η = 0, 0.002, 0.006), with detailed results presented only for η = 0.006.

## Method

The authors performed 12,753 alpha-rich freezeout calculations using the Clemson nucleosynthesis code with a network of 376 species up to Z=35 (Bromine) and 2,125 reactions, employing NACRE and NON-SMOKER rate compilations. For each reaction, the reference rate was multiplied by factors of 0.1 and 10.0 at all temperatures (with corresponding reverse rates adjusted by detailed balance) for three values of neutron excess η, and the modified yields were compared to reference calculations. The calculations assumed an exponential density expansion with ρ(t) = ρ0 exp(-t/τex) and a radiation-dominated relation ρ ∝ T^3, starting from an initial composition of 28Si with enough 29Si to set η.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature: T9 = 5.5 (T/10^9 K) and initial density ρ0 = 10^7 g/cm^3 for all calculations
- Density e-folding timescale: τex = 0.141 s (from τex = 446 s/√ρ0)
- Total calculations performed: 2,125 reactions × 3 η values × 2 multiplicative factors + 3 reference calculations = 12,753
- For 55Fe at η = 0.006: a factor of 10 decrease in the 55Co(p,γ)56Ni reaction rate increases 55Co yield by 526%; a factor of 10 increase reduces yield by almost 80%
- For 55Fe at η = 0.006: the triple-α reaction has about a factor of two effect on yield when a factor of 10 change in the triple-α rate is made
- For 56Ni at η = 0.006: the largest effect from reaction rate changes was due to triple-α, producing about a 6% change in yield
- For 57Ni at η = 0.006: the 57Ni(n,p)57Co reaction produces at most a 17.7% change in yield for a factor of ten change in the rate
- For 59Cu at η = 0.006: a factor of 10 decrease in 59Cu(p,γ)60Zn produces about a 50% increase in yield; a factor of 10 increase drops yield by about 30%
- 55Fe half-life: τ1/2 = 2.73 years
- 56Ni half-life: τ1/2 = 6.075 days; 56Co half-life: τ1/2 = 77.2 days
- 59Ni half-life: 75,000 years
- Reference mass fraction of 56Ni at t=0 years for η=0.006: 0.767
- Reference mass fraction of 57Ni at t=0 years for η=0.006: 3.967 × 10^{-2}
- Reference mass fraction of 59Cu at t=0 years for η=0.006: 1.887 × 10^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the reaction-rate sensitivity methodology previously applied to 44Ti production by The et al. (1998) to a broader set of iron-group observables (59Ni, 57Co, 56Co, and 55Fe) in alpha-rich freezeout nucleosynthesis. The paper provides web-based tools that enable researchers to identify governing reactions for new observables as they become relevant, such as isotopes important for Galactic chemical evolution models (e.g., 40Ca, 48Ti, 52,53Cr) or potential measurements of 44Ca excesses in presolar silicon carbide X grains from live 44Ti decay as studied by Nittler et al. (1996). The authors intend these tools to serve as a template for similar sensitivity studies on other nucleosynthesis processes.
