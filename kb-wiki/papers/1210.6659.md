# Quantum Monte Carlo approaches to nuclear and atomic physics

> Quantum Monte Carlo methods have proven to be valuable in the study of strongly correlated quantum systems, particularly nuclear physics and cold atomic gases. Historically, such ab initio simulations have been used to study properties of light nuclei, including spectra and form factors, low-energy scattering, and high-momentum properties including inclusive scattering and one- and two-body momentum distributions. More recently they have been used to study the properties of homogeneous and inhomogeneous neutron matter and cold atomic gases. There are close analogies between these seemingly diverse systems, including the equation of state, superfluid pairing, and linear response to external probes. In this paper, we compare and contrast results found in nuclear and cold atom physics. We show updated lattice results for the energy of the homogeneous unitary Fermi gas and comparisons with neutron matter, as well as for the dependence of the cold atom energy on the mass ratio between paired particles, which yields insights on the structure of the ground state. We also provide new lattice and continuum results for the harmonically trapped unitary gas, again comparing neutron matter and cold atoms.

## Key claim

Quantum Monte Carlo methods provide accurate ab initio results for both nuclear and cold atomic systems, but the paper's central comparison reveals that while the equations of state of neutron matter and cold unitary Fermi gases agree at very low densities, they diverge at modest densities primarily due to the significant effective range of the neutron-neutron interaction, a detail the abstract's mention of "close analogies" does not fully emphasize.

## Method

The authors employed two complementary zero-temperature Quantum Monte Carlo approaches: Diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) in coordinate space and Auxiliary Field Monte Carlo (AFMC) on a lattice. Both methods propagate a trial wave function in imaginary time to project out the ground state, using either a short-time two-body propagator (DMC) or a Hubbard-like hopping Hamiltonian with auxiliary-field sampling (AFMC). The DMC calculations used a fixed-node algorithm to provide upper bounds on the energy, while the AFMC lattice simulations avoided the sign problem for purely attractive interactions by factorizing the spin-up and spin-down determinants.

## Key numbers

* Bertsch parameter for the unitary Fermi gas: AFMC lattice calculations extrapolate to ξ = 0.372(5), consistent with the most recent experimental value of ξ = 0.375(5) from Ku (2012).
* Best present DMC upper bound for the Bertsch parameter: ξ = 0.390(1) after extrapolation to zero effective range, from Forbes (2011) and Gandolfi (2011).
* Slope parameter S for the linear dependence of the unitary gas equation of state on k_F r_e: S = 0.12(3) from combined DMC and AFMC results in Carlson (2011), with a more recent DMC value of S = 0.127(4) from Forbes (2012).
* Pairing gap at unitarity for cold atoms: δ = Δ/E_F = 0.50(5) from DMC calculations, compared to experimental extractions of δ = 0.45(5) and δ = 0.44(3).
* Pairing gap in neutron matter reaches a maximum of nearly 0.3 E_F at k_F a = -5, considerably smaller than in cold atoms due to the effective range of the neutron-neutron interaction.
* Contact parameter ζ extracted from the equation of state: ζ = 0.901(2) at unitarity.

## Context

This paper extends the long history of Quantum Monte Carlo applications to strongly correlated systems, from early work on light nuclei by Carlson (1987, 1988) and Pieper (2001) to more recent studies of neutron matter by Gezerlis (2008, 2010) and Gandolfi (2009, 2011) and cold atomic gases by Carlson (2003, 2008) and Giorgini (2008). The results enable quantitative comparisons between neutron matter and cold atom experiments, particularly for the equation of state, pairing gap, and short-range physics governed by Tan's contact parameter. The findings also provide ab initio benchmarks for nuclear density functionals, showing that the isovector gradient term in Skyrme interactions like SLy4 needs adjustment to reproduce microscopic neutron drop calculations, which has implications for modeling neutron star crusts.
