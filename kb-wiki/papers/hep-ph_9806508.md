# Relativistic Bound States in 2+1 and 1+1 Dimensions in the Null-Plane

> The Relativistic formulation of the three-boson model interacting via a zero-range two-body force in the null-plane is given in 2+1 and 1+1 space-time dimension. The bound state energy is calculed as function of the two-body boson binding energy.

# Relativistic Bound States in 2+1 and 1+1 Dimensions in the Null-Plane

## Key claim

The three-boson bound state with zero-range two-body force can be formulated in null-plane dynamics for 1+1 and 2+1 space-time dimensions, with the ground-state binding energies approaching the well-known nonrelativistic values in the limit where the two-body binding energy goes to zero. The lower bound for the three-boson system mass is found to be M_{3B} > sqrt(2) M, the same limit previously obtained in 3+1 dimensions.

## Method

The authors derive Faddeev-like integral equations for the three-boson vertex component in null-plane coordinates, using a zero-range two-body interaction. The two-body scattering amplitude is obtained analytically in both 1+1 and 2+1 dimensions, and the bound-state equations are solved numerically for the ground state with the boson mass set to M = 1.

## Key numbers

- The lower bound for the three-boson system mass is M_{3B} > sqrt(2) M, where M is the single boson mass.
- In 1+1 dimensions, the momentum fraction y of the spectator boson is constrained to 1 > y > M^2 / M^2_{3B}.
- In 2+1 dimensions, the perpendicular momentum is bounded by q^2_perp < (1 - y)(M^2_{3B} y - M^2).
- The ground-state binding energy is defined as E_{3B} = M_{2B} + M - M_{3B}, where M_{2B} is the two-boson bound-state mass.

## Context

This work extends the null-plane formulation of relativistic three-body bound states, previously developed for 3+1 dimensions by Frederico (1992), to lower space-time dimensions. The nonrelativistic limit reproduces the well-known values from Dodd (1970) when the two-body binding energy approaches zero. The results provide a framework for studying relativistic few-body systems in reduced dimensions and enable comparisons between null-plane dynamics across different space-time dimensionalities.
