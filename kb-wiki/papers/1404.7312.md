# Spectral Functions from the Functional Renormalization Group

> In this article we wish to present a new method to obtain spectral functions at finite temperature and density from the Functional Renormalization Group (FRG). The FRG offers a powerful non-perturbative tool to deal with phase transitions in strong-interaction matter under extreme conditions and their fluctuation properties. Based on a thermodynamically consistent truncation we derive flow equations for pertinent two-point functions in Minkowski space-time. We demonstrate the feasibility of the method by calculating mesonic spectral functions in hot and dense hadronic matter using the quark-meson model as a simple example.

# Spectral Functions from the Functional Renormalization Group

## Key claim
The authors present a new method to obtain spectral functions at finite temperature and density from the Functional Renormalization Group (FRG) by analytically continuing flow equations for two-point functions from imaginary to real time, but the feasibility is demonstrated only within the quark-meson model as a simple low-energy effective model, not in full QCD.

## Method
The method derives flow equations for retarded two-point functions in Minkowski space-time from the Wetterich equation, using a thermodynamically consistent truncation (local potential approximation) with scale-dependent but momentum-independent vertices. The analytic continuation is performed in two steps: first treating the external energy as an imaginary discrete Matsubara frequency and exploiting periodicity of occupation numbers, then replacing the discrete imaginary energy by a continuous real energy to obtain retarded propagators. The flow equations for real and imaginary parts of the retarded two-point functions are solved at the global minimum of the effective potential in the infrared, and spectral functions are obtained from the imaginary part of the retarded inverse propagator.

## Key numbers
- For the O(4) model in vacuum with physical pion mass: pion screening mass = 137.2 MeV, sigma screening mass = 425.0 MeV, pion decay constant = 93.0 MeV (UV cutoff Lambda = 500 MeV)
- For the O(4) model near chiral limit: pion screening mass = 16.4 MeV, sigma screening mass = 299.8 MeV, pion decay constant = 93.1 MeV
- For the quark-meson model: pion decay constant = 93.5 MeV, pion screening mass = 138 MeV, sigma screening mass = 509 MeV, quark mass = 299 MeV (UV cutoff Lambda = 1000 MeV)
- Critical endpoint in the quark-meson model phase diagram located at mu = 293 MeV and T = 10 MeV
- At T = 150 MeV and mu = 0 MeV, a pronounced sigma meson peak appears at omega approximately 280 MeV
- At mu = 292 MeV and T = 10 MeV (about 1 MeV from the critical endpoint), the sigma screening mass drops to about half its vacuum value, with a pronounced sigma peak at omega approximately 290 MeV

## Context
This work extends the FRG-based analytic continuation method previously applied to the O(4) model in vacuum by Kamikado (2014) to finite temperature and density within the quark-meson model. The approach goes beyond mean-field theory (successfully employed by Brown and collaborators over several decades) by incorporating quantum fluctuations, which is particularly important for understanding chiral symmetry restoration in hadronic matter. The method enables computation of spectral functions across the entire (T, mu) phase diagram and opens the possibility to calculate transport coefficients, as well as extensions to other spectral functions such as those of the rho and a1 mesons relevant for electromagnetic probes in heavy-ion collisions.
