# Thermal Spectra Without Detailed Balance

> A thermal spectrum is often taken as a signature that the emitted probe has reached detailed balance with the surrounding medium. We show that this interpretation is not generally valid by studying how the microscopic emission kernel determines the macroscopic spectrum. In $3+1$ dimensions, a simple thermal spectrum can be generated without probe thermalization when the relevant kernel belongs to a thermally degenerate class. A representative case is realized when the differential cross section depends on the scattering angle but carries no additional dependence on the Mandelstam variable $s$, as in low-energy Thomson scattering. Our results provide a kernel-based criterion for distinguishing genuine probe--medium exchange equilibrium from thermal spectra produced by the structure of the emission kernel itself.

# Thermal Spectra Without Detailed Balance

## Key claim
A thermal (Boltzmann) emission spectrum from a probe escaping a thermal medium does **not** necessarily indicate that the probe has reached detailed balance or exchange equilibrium with the medium. The spectrum can be thermal simply because the microscopic emission kernel belongs to a "thermally degenerate" class, specifically when the angularly averaged kernel is proportional to the Mandelstam variable \(s\) (the \(h=2\) case in their power expansion), as realized in low-energy Thomson scattering.

## Method
The authors classify production kernels \(\mathcal{K}\) for \(2\to2\) scattering processes in an equilibrium medium by expanding the kernel in powers of \(\sqrt{s}\) (center-of-mass energy) and Legendre polynomials in \(\cos\Theta\) (scattering angle). They compute the momentum-differential production rate of the emitted probe in the classical (Maxwell-Boltzmann) limit, projecting the rate onto a Laguerre basis to decompose the spectrum into spectral modes. By evaluating the spectral coefficients for elementary kernels \((\sqrt{s})^{h}\), they identify which power \(h\) yields a pure Boltzmann spectrum (only the zeroth mode nonzero) and which yields nonthermal distortions.

## Key numbers
- For \(h=2\) (kernel \(\propto s\)), the spectral coefficients satisfy \(\mathcal{R}_{c,i}[\mathcal{K}_2] = 0\) for all \(i \geq 1\); only the zeroth mode contributes, producing a Boltzmann spectrum \(\mathcal{R}_c \propto (k/T)^2 e^{-k/T}\).
- For positive even \(h > 2\), the coefficients have finite support: \(\mathcal{R}_{c,i} \neq 0\) only for \(i < h/2\) (e.g., \(h=4\) gives nonzero only for \(i=0,1\); \(h=6\) for \(i=0,1,2\)).
- For odd \(h\), all coefficients \(\mathcal{R}_{c,i}\) are nonzero for all \(i\) (e.g., \(h=1\): normalized coefficients \(1, 1/6, 1/16, 1/32, \dots\)).
- For \(h=0\), all coefficients are nonzero (e.g., \(i=0:1\), \(i=1:1/3\), \(i=2:1/6\), \(i=3:1/10\), \(\dots\)).
- The low-energy Thomson scattering differential cross section is \(d\sigma/d\Omega_{\rm cm} = \alpha^2/(2m_e^2) (1+\cos^2\theta)\), yielding \(\mathcal{K} \propto s/m_e^2 (1+\cos^2\theta)\), which is the \(h=2\) case.

## Context
This work extends the understanding of thermal photon and dilepton production in high-energy nuclear collisions (e.g., Shen 2013, Rapp 2014, ALICE 2015) and neutrino emission from supernovae (Keil 2002, Tamborra 2012) by showing that a thermal spectrum does not guarantee probe thermalization. It contradicts the common interpretation that a thermal spectrum implies detailed balance (Planck 1901, Rybicki 2004). The authors introduce a kernel-based criterion to distinguish "thermally degenerate kernels" (which produce thermal spectra without equilibration) from "exchange-diagnostic kernels" (which require equilibration for a thermal spectrum). This enables cleaner diagnostics of medium properties: deviations from a thermal spectrum in processes with thermally degenerate kernels can signal medium nonequilibrium without contamination from kernel-induced spectral structure.
