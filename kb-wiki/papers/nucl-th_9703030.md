# Fluctuations and HBT Scales in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> Bose-Einstein correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions are examined in a general model containing the essential features of hydrodynamical, cascade as well as other models commonly employed for describing the particle freeze-out. In particular the effects of longitudinal and transverse expansion, emission from surfaces moving in time, the thickness of the emitting layer varying from surface to volume emission and other effects are studied. Model dependences of freeze-out sizes and times are discussed and compared to recent $Pb+Pb$ data at 160A$\cdot$GeV.

## Key claim

Bose-Einstein correlation (HBT) radius parameters can be interpreted as fluctuations in radial, temporal, angular, and emission-layer-thickness variables; the outward radius parameter \(R_o\) can become smaller than the sideward radius \(R_s\) when opacity (surface emission) and transverse flow are included, contrary to the expectation from transparent sources without flow.

## Method

The authors construct a general cylindrically symmetric source model with longitudinal Bjorken flow, transverse flow, and a Glauber absorption factor that interpolates between volume emission (transparent source, large mean free path \(\lambda_{mfp}\)) and surface emission (opaque source, \(\lambda_{mfp} \ll R\)). They compute the HBT radius parameters \(R_s\), \(R_o\), and \(R_l\) as variances of the emission function in the sideward, outward, and longitudinal directions, expanding to second order in the relative momentum and to leading order in \(\lambda_{mfp}/R\). Analytical expressions are derived for the surface-emission limit, and numerical results are shown for intermediate opacities and transverse flow strengths.

## Key numbers

* For central \(Pb+Pb\) collisions at 160 A\(\cdot\)GeV, the measured pion HBT radii are \(R_s \simeq R_o \sim 4.5\)–\(5.0\) fm and \(R_l \simeq 5\)–\(6\) fm, with average transverse momentum \(p_\perp \simeq 165\) MeV and \(\beta_o \simeq 0.76\).
* From transverse momentum slopes, the temperature is \(T \sim 120\) MeV and the transverse flow \(\langle v_s^2 \rangle^{1/2} \sim 0.6c\).
* Fitting the model to the data yields a freeze-out time \(\tau_f \sim 12\) fm/c, an initial transverse source size \({\cal R} \sim 11\) fm, and a mean free path \(\lambda_{mfp} \sim 2\)–\(3\) fm.
* The average emission time is \(\sqrt{\langle\tau^2\rangle} = \sqrt{2/5}\,\tau_f \simeq 7\) fm/c.
* For a static surface source without flow, \(R_s = {\cal R}/\sqrt{3}\); for volume emission of constant density, \(R_s = {\cal R}/2\).
* The longitudinal HBT radius parameter scales as \(R_l^2 \simeq (2/5)(T/m_\perp)\tau_f^2\) for constant emission per surface element.

## Context

This work extends earlier HBT analyses of hydrodynamical models (e.g., Csorgo and Heinz) and cascade codes (e.g., Humanic, RQMD, QGSM, Scott) by providing a unified framework that includes both volume and surface emission, longitudinal and transverse expansion, and moving freeze-out surfaces. It contradicts the simple expectation from transparent sources (Csorgo, Heinz) that \(R_o^2 = R_s^2 + \beta_o^2 \sigma(\tau)\) always holds, showing that opacity and transverse flow can make \(R_o < R_s\). The results enable extraction of freeze-out times and source sizes from NA44 data, and they motivate future studies of two-component sources and the breakdown of the Glauber picture when the inward-moving surface overtakes the emitted particles.
