# Practical solution to the Monte Carlo sign problem: Realistic calculations of 54Fe

> We present a practical solution to the "sign problem" in the auxiliary field Monte Carlo approach to the nuclear shell model. The method is based on extrapolation from a continuous family of problem-free Hamiltonians. To demonstrate the resultant ability to treat large shell-model problems, we present results for 54Fe in the full fp-shell basis using the Brown-Richter interaction. We find the Gamow-Teller beta^+ strength to be quenched by 58% relative to the single-particle estimate, in better agreement with experiment than previous estimates based on truncated bases.

# Practical solution to the Monte Carlo sign problem: Realistic calculations of 54Fe

## Key claim

The authors present a practical solution to the sign problem in auxiliary-field Monte Carlo shell model calculations by extrapolating observables from a family of Hamiltonians with negative-definite interaction eigenvalues (where the sign is identically positive) to the physical Hamiltonian, requiring only a modest factor-of-two variation in the isovector monopole pairing strength.

## Method

The Hamiltonian is decomposed into a "good" part (all two-body interactions with negative eigenvalues in the density decomposition) and a "bad" part (interactions with positive eigenvalues). A continuous family of Hamiltonians H_g = H_G + g H_B is defined, where for g ≤ 0 the integrand has positive definite sign and Monte Carlo sampling is sign-problem-free. Observables are calculated for g values from -1 to 0 using the Hubbard-Stratonovich representation of the imaginary-time propagator, then extrapolated to g=1 (the physical Hamiltonian) using polynomial fits. For 54Fe, calculations used β = 2 MeV⁻¹ with 32 time slices and approximately 3300 uncorrelated samples per g value on a 512-node parallel computer.

## Key numbers

- Gamow-Teller β⁺ strength ⟨(GT₊)²⟩ = 4.32 ± 0.24, quenched by 58% relative to the single-particle estimate of 10.28
- Total mass quadrupole strength ⟨Q²⟩ = 1482 ± 84 fm⁴, compared to the single-particle estimate of 380 fm⁴
- B(E2) for the first 2⁺ state decay = 96 ± 1 e²fm⁴ (free nucleon charges), versus experimental value of 126 e²fm⁴
- First moment of isoscalar quadrupole strength function = 1.25 ± 0.16 MeV, compared to empirical 2⁺ excitation energy of 1.408 MeV
- Total M1 strength ⟨(M1)²⟩ = 14.1 ± 0.4 μ²_N, quenched relative to single-particle estimate of 42.55 μ²_N
- Ground-state energy ⟨H⟩ = -55.5 ± 0.5 MeV
- Mean (p,n) excitation energy = 6.13 ± 0.17 MeV
- Mean (n,p) excitation energy (Coulomb-corrected) = 1.24 ± 0.2 MeV
- Proton occupation numbers: f₇/₂ = 4.92 ± 0.03, p₃/₂ = 0.56 ± 0.02, p₁/₂ = 0.11 ± 0.01, f₅/₂ = 0.41 ± 0.01
- Neutron occupation numbers: f₇/₂ = 6.35 ± 0.03, p₃/₂ = 0.86 ± 0.02, p₁/₂ = 0.17 ± 0.01, f₅/₂ = 0.61 ± 0.01

## Context

This work extends the auxiliary-field Monte Carlo shell model method developed by Johnson, Koonin, Lang, and Ormand (1992) and Lang, Johnson, Koonin, and Ormand (1993), which previously suffered from the sign problem for realistic interactions except a schematic class. The method was validated against direct diagonalization in the sd-shell using the Brown-Wildenthal interaction (Brown and Wildenthal, 1988) and in ⁴⁴Ti using the Brown-Richter interaction (Richter, Vandermerwe, Julies, and Brown, 1991). The full fp-shell calculation of ⁵⁴Fe (with approximately 5×10⁹ m-scheme Slater determinants) demonstrates feasibility for model spaces far beyond the largest treated by standard diagonalization (⁴⁸Ti, with approximately 7×10⁶ determinants, by Caurier, Poves, and Zuker, 1991). The 58% quenching of Gamow-Teller β⁺ strength is significantly larger than previous estimates of 6.40 or 6.70 from truncated-basis calculations by Auerbach, Bertsch, Brown, and Zhao (1993), and brings the result closer to the experimental value of 3.1 ± 0.6 from Vetterli et al. (1989). This enables systematic studies of temperature, nuclide, and interaction dependence, as well as determination of optimal effective interactions and operators in greatly enlarged model spaces.
