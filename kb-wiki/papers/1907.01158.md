# $^{16}O(p,α)^{13}N$ makes explosive oxygen burning sensitive to the metallicity of the progenitors of type Ia supernovae

> Even though the main nucleosynthetic products of type Ia supernovae belong to the iron-group, intermediate-mass alpha-nuclei (silicon, sulfur, argon, and calcium) stand out in their spectra up to several weeks past maximum brightness. Recent measurements of the abundances of calcium, argon, and sulfur in type Ia supernova remnants have been interpreted in terms of metallicity-dependent oxygen burning, in accordance with previous theoretical predictions. It is known that $α$-rich oxygen burning results from $^{16}$O$\rightarrow^{12}$C followed by efficient $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C fusion reaction, as compared to oxygen consumption by $^{16}$O fusion reactions, but the precise mechanism of dependence on the progenitor metallicity has remained unidentified so far. I show that the chain $^{16}$O(p,$α$)$^{13}$N($γ$,p)$^{12}$C boosts $α$-rich oxygen burning when the proton abundance is large, increasing the synthesis of argon and calcium with respect to sulfur and silicon. For high-metallicity progenitors, the presence of free neutrons leads to a drop in the proton abundance and the above chain is not efficient. Although the rate of $^{16}$O(p,$α$)$^{13}$N can be found in astrophysical reaction rate libraries, its uncertainty is unconstrained. Assuming that all reaction rates other than $^{16}$O(p,$α$)$^{13}$N retain their standard values, an increase by a factor of approximately seven of the $^{16}$O(p,$α$)$^{13}$N rate at temperatures in the order $3-5\times10^9$ K is enough to explain the whole range of calcium-to-sulfur mass ratios measured in Milky Way and LMC supernova remnants. These same measurements provide a lower limit to the $^{16}$O(p,$α$)$^{13}$N rate in the mentioned temperature range, on the order of a factor of 0.5 with respect to the rate reported in widely used literature tabulations.

# $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ makes explosive oxygen burning sensitive to the metallicity of the progenitors of type Ia supernovae

## Key claim

The chain $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N(\gamma,p)^{12}C$ is the specific mechanism that makes the calcium-to-sulfur mass ratio in type Ia supernova ejecta depend on progenitor metallicity, but only if the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ reaction rate is at least 0.5 times the rate reported by Caughlan and Fowler (1988) at temperatures of $3-5\times10^9$ K; an increase by a factor of approximately seven relative to that same rate is sufficient to explain the full observed range of calcium-to-sulfur ratios.

## Method

The author performed one-dimensional simulations of detonating white dwarfs with mass 1.06 $M_\odot$ using the JINA REACLIB compilation for thermonuclear reaction rates, varying the progenitor metallicity and the scaling factor $f_0$ applied to the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ reaction rate (and its inverse via detailed balance). The nucleosynthetic fluxes from the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ chain, the $^{16}O(\gamma,\alpha)^{12}C$ photodisintegration, and the $^{12}C+^{12}C$ fusion reaction were tracked in mass shells reaching peak temperatures of $4\times10^9$ K to determine the $\alpha$-efficiency of oxygen burning and the resulting elemental yields of calcium, sulfur, and argon.

## Key numbers

- The calcium-to-sulfur mass ratio $M_{Ca}/M_S$ spans the observed range $0.17 - 0.28$ (with uncertainty 0.04 in both limits) in Milky Way and LMC supernova remnants, as measured by Suzaku (2017).
- In a mass shell with peak temperature $4\times10^9$ K, the low-metallicity case ($Z=2.25\times10^{-4}$) yields $M_{Ca}/M_S = 0.45$, while the high-metallicity case ($Z=0.0225$) yields $M_{Ca}/M_S = 0.17$.
- An increase by a factor of approximately seven of the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ rate at temperatures $3-4\times10^9$ K is enough to explain the whole range of observed calcium-to-sulfur mass ratios.
- The lower limit on the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ rate from SNR measurements is a factor of 0.5 with respect to the rate reported by Caughlan and Fowler (1988).
- In the temperature range $3.5-5\times10^9$ K, the STARLIB rate is larger than the REACLIB rate by a factor that varies between 1.5 and 2.5.

## Context

This work identifies the specific nuclear reaction chain responsible for the metallicity dependence of calcium and sulfur yields in type Ia supernovae, which was previously noted by De (2014) and Miles (2016) but left unexplained. The results extend the work of Woosley (1973), who included the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N(\gamma,p)^{12}C$ chain in the $\alpha$-rich branch of explosive oxygen burning, and of Bravo (2012), who found this reaction among the most influential for $^{40}Ca$ abundance. The findings enable future X-ray observatories to constrain the $^{16}O(p,\alpha)^{13}N$ reaction rate through more accurate measurements of intermediate-mass element emission lines in supernova remnants, and they resolve the discrepancy noted by Martinez-Rodriguez (2017) between standard SNIa models and the observed calcium-to-sulfur ratios.
