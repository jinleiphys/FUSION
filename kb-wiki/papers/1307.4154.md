# Extended quark mean-field model for neutron stars

> We extend the quark mean-field (QMF) model to strangeness freedom to study the properties of hyperons ($Λ,Σ,Ξ$) in infinite baryon matter and neutron star properties. The baryon-scalar meson couplings in the QMF model are determined self-consistently from the quark level, where the quark confinement is taken into account in terms of a scalar-vector harmonic oscillator potential. The strength of such confinement potential for $u,d$ quarks is constrained by the properties of finite nuclei, while the one for $s$ quark is limited by the properties of nuclei with a $Λ$ hyperon. These two strengths are not same, which represents the SU(3) symmetry breaking effectively in the QMF model. Also, we use an enhanced $Σ$ coupling with the vector meson, and both $Σ$ and $Ξ$ hyperon potentials can be properly described in the model. The effects of the SU(3) symmetry breaking on the neutron star structures are then studied. We find that the SU(3) breaking shifts earlier the hyperon onset density and makes hyperons more abundant in the star, in comparisons with the results of the SU(3) symmetry case. However, it does not affect much the star's maximum mass. The maximum masses are found to be $1.62 M_{\odot}$ with hyperons and $1.88 M_{\odot}$ without hyperons. The present neutron star model is shown to have limitations on explaining the recently measured heavy pulsar.

## Key claim

The authors extend the quark mean-field (QMF) model to include strangeness by breaking SU(3) symmetry at the quark level, using different confinement strengths for u,d quarks and s quarks. This SU(3) breaking shifts the hyperon onset density earlier and makes hyperons more abundant in the star, but does not affect the maximum mass much; the model cannot explain the observed 2 solar mass pulsar.

## Method

The authors solve Dirac equations for constituent quarks in a scalar-vector harmonic oscillator confinement potential, with different potential strengths for u,d quarks (k^q = 700 MeV/fm^2, constrained by finite nuclei) and s quarks (k^s = 1100 MeV/fm^2, constrained by the Lambda hyperon potential in nuclear matter). The resulting density-dependent baryon effective masses are used in a relativistic mean-field Lagrangian for octet baryons (N, Lambda, Sigma, Xi) interacting via sigma, omega, and rho mesons, with an enhanced Sigma-omega coupling to reproduce the repulsive Sigma potential. The equations of state are then used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for neutron star structure.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass with hyperons: 1.62 solar masses
- Maximum neutron star mass without hyperons: 1.88 solar masses
- Single Lambda potential at saturation density: U_Lambda^(N) = -29.64 MeV (with k^s = 1100 MeV/fm^2)
- Single Lambda potential at saturation density with SU(3) symmetry (k^s = k^q = 700 MeV/fm^2): U_Lambda^(N) = -25 MeV
- Single Sigma potential at saturation density: U_Sigma^(N) = 30 MeV (used to determine enhanced Sigma-omega coupling)
- Saturation density of nuclear matter: rho_0 = 0.145 fm^{-3}
- Energy per particle at saturation: E/A = -16.3 MeV
- Incompressibility: K = 280 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation: M_n*/M_n = 0.63
- Symmetry energy: a_sym = 35 MeV
- Lambda hyperon onset density with SU(3) breaking: 0.34 fm^{-3}
- Lambda hyperon onset density with SU(3) symmetry: 0.40 fm^{-3}
- Xi^- hyperon onset density: 0.46 fm^{-3}
- Xi^0 hyperon onset density: 0.96 fm^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the quark mean-field model of Toki et al. (1998) and Shen et al. (2000, 2002) to include hyperons with broken SU(3) symmetry, contrasting with the chiral SU(3) QMF model of Wang et al. (2001, 2004, 2005) which used an explicit symmetry-breaking term in the Lagrangian. The results are consistent with conventional RMF calculations including hyperons by Schaffner et al. (1992, 2000) and Vidana et al. (2000), but cannot reach the 2 solar mass constraint from PSR J1614-2230. The authors suggest that future work could readjust QMF parameters from finite nuclei data or consider phase transitions to quark matter to achieve heavier neutron stars.
