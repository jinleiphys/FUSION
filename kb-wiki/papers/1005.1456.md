# Anatomy of neck configuration in fission decay

> The anatomy of neck configuration in the fission decay of Uranium and Thorium isotopes is investigated in a microscopic study using Relativistic mean field theory. The study includes $^{236}U$ and $^{232}Th$ in the valley of stability and exotic neutron rich isotopes $^{250}U$, $^{256}U$, $^{260}U$, $^{240}Th$, $^{250}Th$, $^{256}Th$ likely to play important role in the r-process nucleosynthesis in stellar evolution. Following the static fission path, the neck configurations are generated and their composition in terms of the number of neutrons and protons are obtained showing the progressive rise in the neutron component with the increase of mass number. Strong correlation between the neutron multiplicity in the fission decay and the number of neutrons in the neck is seen. The maximum neutron-proton ratio is about 5 for $^{260}$U and $^{256}$Th suggestive of the break down of liquid-drop picture and inhibition of the fission decay in still heavier isotopes. Neck as precursor of a new mode of fission decay like multi-fragmentation fission may also be inferred from this study.

# Anatomy of neck configuration in fission decay

## Key claim

The authors claim that the maximum neutron-to-proton ratio in the neck configuration reaches about 5 for the heaviest isotopes studied (260U and 256Th), and that this value represents a limit beyond which neck formation becomes unsustainable, signaling a breakdown of the liquid-drop picture and inhibition of fission in still heavier neutron-rich isotopes. This conclusion is conditional on the assumption that the neck's composition correlates with the known instability of hydrogen isotopes beyond 6H.

## Method

The authors performed constrained relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations using the NL3 interaction, following the static fission path along the quadrupole deformation coordinate for eight uranium and thorium isotopes. They solved the coupled Dirac and meson field equations self-consistently in an axially deformed harmonic oscillator basis with up to 28 oscillator shells, using constant-gap BCS pairing for open-shell nuclei. The neck configuration was identified by the criterion that the density falls to 15% of the central density and remains constant along the Z-coordinate, and the neutron and proton numbers in the neck were obtained by integrating the respective density distributions over the neck volume.

## Key numbers

- Fission barrier for 236U: 6.95 MeV (compared to experimental value of 5.75 MeV)
- Fission barrier for 250U: 4.05 MeV (compared to Howard-Moller value of 4.3 MeV)
- Ground-state binding energy of 236U: 1790.5 MeV (experimental: 1790.4 MeV)
- Ground-state binding energy of 232Th: 1767.1 MeV (experimental: 1766.7 MeV)
- Ground-state quadrupole deformation beta2 for 236U: 0.26 (experimental: 0.28)
- Ground-state quadrupole deformation beta2 for 232Th: 0.26 (experimental: 0.26)
- Neck configuration binding energy for 236U: 1808.5 MeV (20 MeV below ground state)
- Neck configuration binding energy for 260U: 1923.9 MeV
- Neck neutron number for 236U: 2.42; for 260U: 5.11
- Neck proton number for 236U: 0.95; for 260U: 1.03
- Neck N/Z ratio for 236U: 2.54; for 260U: 4.96; for 256Th: 5.55
- Average neutron-to-proton density ratio in neck: 1.70 for 236U, 3.00 for 260U; 1.51 for 232Th, 2.73 for 256Th
- Neck length for 236U: 6.28 fm; for 260U: 4.29 fm
- Tip-to-tip distance for 236U: 37.48 fm; for 256Th: 36.96 fm
- Kinetic energy from neck rupture for 236U: 139 MeV (compared to most probable 155 MeV for asymmetric fission)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Patra et al., 2009) identifying a valley of thermally fissile uranium and thorium isotopes with neutron numbers N=154-172, and provides microscopic support for the concept of "multifragmentation fission" proposed earlier. The RMF results are compared with constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov calculations by Dubray et al. (2008) using the Gogny interaction, which found elongated shapes without clear-cut necks in thorium and fermium isotopes, and with Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS results by Bonneau (2006) for fermium isotopes. The study enables predictions of enhanced neutron multiplicities (expected to exceed 4 for the heaviest isotopes) in fission of neutron-rich actinides relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis, and suggests that fission may be inhibited in uranium isotopes heavier than 260U in favor of beta-decay, analogous to the preference for alpha-decay over fission in superheavy elements.
