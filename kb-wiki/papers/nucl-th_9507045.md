# Collisions of Deformed Nuclei: A Path to the Far Side of the Superheavy Island

> A detailed understanding of complete fusion cross sections in heavy-ion collisions requires a consideration of the effects of the deformation of the projectile and target. Our aim here is to show that deformation and orientation of the colliding nuclei have a very significant effect on the fusion-barrier height and on the compactness of the touching configuration. To facilitate discussions of fusion configurations of deformed nuclei, we develop a classification scheme and introduce a notation convention for these configurations. We discuss particular deformations and orientations that lead to compact touching configurations and to fusion-barrier heights that correspond to fairly low excitation energies of the compound systems. Such configurations should be the most favorable for producing superheavy elements. We analyse a few projectile-target combinations whose deformations allow favorable entrance-channel configurations and whose proton and neutron numbers lead to compound systems in a part of the superheavy region where alpha half-lives are calculated to be observable, that is, longer than 1 microsecond.

## Key claim

Deformation and relative orientation of colliding nuclei have a very significant effect on fusion-barrier height and compactness of the touching configuration, and particular combinations of prolate shapes with large positive hexadecapole moments (negative epsilon_4) can produce neck-like touching configurations that are substantially more compact than spherical or gentle-fusion configurations, potentially enabling production of superheavy elements with observable alpha half-lives longer than 1 microsecond.

## Method

The authors developed a classification scheme and notation for 34 to 52 distinct limiting fusion configurations of deformed nuclei, considering oblate, spherical, and prolate shapes with either positive or negative hexadecapole moments, and five relative orientations (polar-transverse, polar-parallel, equatorial-transverse, equatorial-parallel, equatorial-cross). They calculated fusion potential-energy surfaces and barrier parameters using a macroscopic model for the potential between two arbitrarily oriented deformed heavy ions, with nuclear shapes described by the Nilsson perturbed-spheroid parameterization and the beta parameterization, and compared calculated fusion cross sections to measured data for the reaction 16O + 154Sm.

- For 186W + 110Pd: equatorial-parallel (oriep) barrier 375.12 MeV at 11.69 fm; equatorial-cross (oriec) barrier 376.20 MeV at 11.69 fm; spherical case barrier >361.10 MeV at 12.18 fm touching

- Difference between equatorial and polar fusion-barrier heights for 16O + 154Sm: 6.56 MeV in this calculation versus 8 MeV in Ref. [16]
- Element 107 production cross section: 167 pb; element 111 production cross section: 2-3 pb

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of deformation effects in heavy-ion fusion, particularly the "gentle fusion" concept proposed by Norenberg (1980s) for two well-deformed rare-earth nuclei in equatorial-cross orientation, and the authors' own prior model for macroscopic potential between deformed heavy ions (Moller et al., 1994). The paper shows that the gentle fusion configuration [p+,p+,oriec] is only 0.59 fm more compact than the spherical case for 150Nd + 150Nd, whereas configurations with large positive epsilon_4 (negative hexadecapole moments) produce neck-like hugging configurations that are substantially more compact. This enables systematic identification of projectile-target combinations whose deformations allow favorable entrance-channel configurations for superheavy element production, with compound systems in regions where calculated alpha half-lives exceed 1 microsecond.
