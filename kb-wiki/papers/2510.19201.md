# Resolving the spurious-state problem in Dirac equation by using the staggered-grid method

> Discretizing the Dirac equation on a uniform grid with the central difference formula often generates spurious states. We propose a staggered-grid scheme in the framework of the finite-difference method that suppresses these spurious states without introducing Wilson terms or ad-hoc filtering. In this approach, the large and small components of the Dirac equation are placed on interlaced nodes, and the first-order derivatives are evaluated between staggered points, yielding a Hamiltonian that breaks the unitary transformation between $H_κ$ and $H_{-κ}$. Benchmarks with the nuclear Woods-Saxon potentials demonstrate one-to-one agreement with the eigenvalues obtained from shooting method and asymmetric finite-difference method, rapid convergence for weakly bound states, and reduced box-size sensitivity. The method retains the simplicity of central differences and standard matrix diagonalization, while naturally extending to higher-order and multi-dimension systems. It provides a compact and efficient tool for relativistic bound-state and scattering calculations.

# Resolving the spurious-state problem in Dirac equation by using the staggered-grid method

## Key claim

The staggered-grid method (SGM) eliminates spurious states from central-difference discretizations of the Dirac equation without introducing Wilson terms or ad-hoc filtering, by placing the large and small components on interlaced nodes shifted by half a grid spacing, which breaks the unitary transformation symmetry between Hamiltonians $H_\kappa$ and $H_{-\kappa}$ that causes the spurious degeneracy.

## Method

The radial Dirac equation for a particle in scalar and vector Woods-Saxon potentials is discretized on a uniform grid of spacing $h$ from $r=0$ to $R_\text{box}=nh$, but with the large component $G(r)$ placed at integer nodes $ih$ and the small component $F(r)$ placed at half-integer nodes $(i-1/2)h$. First-order derivatives are evaluated between staggered points using a central-difference-like formula $df/dr \approx [f(r+h/2)-f(r-h/2)]/h$, and the centrifugal term $\kappa/r$ is treated via linear interpolation to preserve Hermiticity of the Hamiltonian matrix. The resulting $2n \times 2n$ Hamiltonian is diagonalized to obtain eigenvalues and wave functions.

## Key numbers

- For the $1s_{1/2}$ neutron state in $^{132}$Sn with Woods-Saxon potential, the three-point SGM (3PSGM) eigenvalue is $-55.006$ MeV, compared to $-55.004$ MeV from three-point asymmetric finite-difference (3PADF) and $-55.005$ MeV from the shooting method.
- For the $2s_{1/2}$ state, 3PSGM gives $-33.933$ MeV, closer to the shooting-method value of $-33.929$ MeV than the 3PADF value of $-33.915$ MeV.
- For the weakly bound $3p_{1/2}$ state ($\kappa=1$), 3PSGM yields $-0.306$ MeV, while 3PADF gives $-0.283$ MeV.
- Calculations used a spherical box of radius $R_\text{box}=20$ fm with $n=500$ lattice points.
- The Woods-Saxon potential parameters are: $V=-71.28$ MeV, $\lambda=11.12$, $\kappa=0.462$, $r_0=1.233$ fm, $r^{ls}=1.144$ fm, $a=0.615$ fm, $a^{ls}=0.648$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous asymmetric finite-difference (AFD) method (Zhang 2022) that also removes spurious states but requires alternating backward/forward difference formulas for different $\kappa$ values. The staggered-grid approach is inspired by the Marker-and-Cell (MAC) scheme in fluid dynamics (Harlow 1965) and the Yee scheme in electromagnetics (Yee 1966), and is conceptually related to Kogut-Susskind fermions in lattice field theory (Kogut and Susskind 1975). The method enables one-shot matrix diagonalization for relativistic bound-state calculations without the complexity of Wilson terms or domain-wall fermions, and naturally extends to higher-order differences and multi-dimensional systems.
