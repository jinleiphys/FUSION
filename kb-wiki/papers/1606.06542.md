# Flavor-dependent eigenvolume interactions in a hadron resonance gas

> Eigenvolume effects in the hadron resonance gas (HRG) model are studied for experimental hadronic yields in nucleus-nucleus collisions. If particle eigenvolumes are different for different hadron species, the excluded volume HRG (EV-HRG) improves fits to multiplicity data. In particular, using different mass~-~volume relations for strange and non-strange hadrons we observe a remarkable improvement in the quality of the fits. This effect appears to be rather insensitive to other details in the schemes employed in the EV-HRG. We show that the parameters found from fitting the data of the ALICE Collaboration in central Pb+Pb collisions at the collision energy $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 2.76$~TeV entail the same improvement for all centralities at the same collision energy, and for the RHIC and SPS data at lower collision energies. Our findings are put in the context of recent fits of lattice QCD results.

## Key claim

If particle eigenvolumes are made flavor-dependent, specifically using different mass–volume relations for strange and non-strange hadrons, the excluded-volume hadron resonance gas (EV-HRG) model yields a remarkable improvement in fits to experimental hadron multiplicity data, an effect that is rather insensitive to other details of the EV scheme employed.

## Method

The authors fit the EV-HRG model to mid-rapidity hadron yields (pions, kaons, protons, Lambdas, Xis, Omegas, and phi mesons) measured by the ALICE Collaboration in central Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, minimizing chi-squared. They test four eigenvolume parametrizations: a common linear mass–volume relation for all hadrons (1b); separate linear coefficients for strange and non-strange hadrons (2b); separate linear coefficients for four flavor–meson/baryon groups (4b); and a scheme where non-strange hadrons follow a linear mass–volume relation while strange hadrons follow an inverse mass–volume relation (s-inv). The best-fit parameters from the 0–5% centrality class are then applied without retuning to other centralities at the same energy and to lower-energy data from STAR (200 GeV) and NA49 (20–158 GeV per nucleon).

## Key numbers

- I-HRG fit to ALICE 0–5% data: chi²/N_dof = 22.2/9 ≈ 2.4, T = 154.1 ± 2.2 MeV, mu_B = 3.4 ± 6.9 MeV, V = 4993 ± 644 fm³.
- 2b scheme fit to ALICE 0–5%: chi²/N_dof = 12.5/8 ≈ 1.5, T = 159.6 ± 3.6 MeV, r_p = 0.374 fm (fixed), r_Lambda = 0.313 ± 0.042 fm.
- 4b scheme fit to ALICE 0–5%: chi²/N_dof = 0.86/5 ≈ 0.172, T = 150.8 ± 5.4 MeV, r_p = 0.402 ± 0.081 fm, r_pi = 0.179 ± 0.083 fm, r_Lambda = 0.106 ± 1.467 fm, r_K = 0.361 ± 0.08 fm.
- s-inv scheme fit to ALICE 0–5%: chi²/N_dof = 0.88/7 ≈ 0.098, T = 152.9 ± 1.8 MeV, r_p = 0.449 ± 0.044 fm, r_Lambda = 0.371 ± 0.044 fm.
- Energy density at freeze-out in s-inv scheme: 0.15–0.20 GeV/fm³ (compared to 0.25–0.40 GeV/fm³ for I-HRG); energy per particle E/N ≈ 0.83 GeV (s-inv) vs. 0.91 GeV (I-HRG).
- For the s-inv scheme applied to ALICE 5–10% centrality: chi²/N_dof = 1.022/7 ≈ 0.14, T = 154.3 ± 2.3 MeV, V = 7141 ± 633 fm³.
- For the s-inv scheme applied to STAR 200 GeV: chi²/N_dof = 6.5/8 ≈ 0.82, T = 158.1 ± 1.6 MeV, mu_B = 29.7 ± 8.8 MeV.
- For the s-inv scheme applied to NA49 30 GeV: chi²/N_dof = 7.08/7 ≈ 1.01, T = 138.5 ± 1.9 MeV, mu_B = 459.5 ± 5.5 MeV.
- Interpolated freeze-out curvature for s-inv scheme: kappa_2 = 0.0139 ± 0.0018, T_0 = 155.4 ± 1.4 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the excluded-volume hadron resonance gas approach of Rischke (1991) and Yen (1998) by introducing flavor-dependent eigenvolumes, motivated by earlier studies of EV effects in lattice QCD (Alba 2017). The results directly address the "proton anomaly" (the poor description of the p/pi ratio in the ideal HRG) that has been discussed by Stachel (2013), Floris (2014), and others. The s-inv scheme yields a chemical freeze-out curve whose curvature (kappa_2 = 0.0139) is compatible with the pseudocritical line curvature from lattice QCD (Bellwied 2015) and with the constant-physics curvature (Bazavov 2017). The findings enable future studies that include higher-order moments of conserved charge fluctuations and the addition of unmeasured strange baryons to the hadron list.
