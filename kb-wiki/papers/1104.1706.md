# Thermal evolution of massive compact objects with dense quark cores

> We examine the thermal evolution of a sequence of compact objects containing low-mass hadronic and high-mass quark-hadronic stars constructed from a microscopically motivated equation of state. The dependence of the cooling tracks in the temperature versus age plane is studied on the variations of the gaplessness parameter (the ratio of the pairing gap for red-green quarks to the electron chemical potential) and the magnitude of blue quark gap. The pairing in the red-green channel is modeled assuming an inhomogeneous superconducting phase to avoid tachionic instabilities and anomalies in the specific heat; the blue colored condensate is modeled as a Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS)-type color superconductor. We find that massive stars containing quark matter cool faster in the neutrino-cooling era if one of the colors (blue) is unpaired and/or the remaining colors (red-green) are paired in a inhomogeneous gapless superconducting state. The cooling curves show significant variations along the sequence, as the mass (or the central density) of the models is varied. This feature provides a handle for fine-tuning the models to fit the data on the surface temperatures of same-age neutron stars. In the late-time photon cooling era we observe inversion in the temperature arrangement of models, i.e., stars experiencing fast neutrino cooling are asymptotically hotter than their slowly cooling counterparts.

## Key claim

Massive compact objects containing quark matter cool faster during the neutrino-cooling era when one color (blue) is unpaired and/or the remaining colors (red-green) are paired in an inhomogeneous gapless superconducting state, but the cooling curves show significant variations along the mass sequence that provide a handle for fine-tuning models to fit observational data on same-age neutron stars.

## Method

The authors constructed a sequence of compact star models using a microscopically motivated equation of state, with low-density hadronic matter matched to high-density quark matter via a Maxwell construction. They solved the general relativistic structure equations to obtain stellar models, then evolved the thermal evolution equation in the isothermal-interior approximation, integrating neutrino luminosities and specific heats over the stellar volume at each time step. The pairing in red-green quarks was modeled using an interpolation formula parameterized by the gaplessness parameter ζ (the ratio of the pairing gap to the electron chemical potential), while blue quarks were treated with a BCS-type pairing model.

## Key numbers

- The sequence includes models with central densities ρc,14 = 5.1 (purely hadronic, 1.10 M⊙), 10.8 (1.91 M⊙ with quark core radius 0.68 km), 11.8 (1.93 M⊙ with quark core radius 3.41 km), and 21.0 (2.05 M⊙ with quark core radius 6.77 km, the maximum mass configuration)
- The quark-hadron transition occurs at 0.5ρ0, where ρ0 = 2.8 × 10^14 g cm^-3 is nuclear saturation density
- The isothermal interior extends down to density ρm = 10^10 g cm^-3
- The blue quark gap parameter Δb was varied between 0 and 0.1 MeV
- The gaplessness parameter ζ was set to either 0.9 (gapless regime) or 1.1 (fully gapped regime)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of quark-hadronic star cooling by Page (2000), Blaschke (2000), Alford (2004), and Anglani (2006) by using a realistic sequence of stellar models with masses up to 2 M⊙, rather than fixed 1.4 M⊙ toy models. The authors build on the equation of state and stellar configurations from Ippolito (2007) and Knippel (2009), and model the inhomogeneous superconducting phase to avoid the tachionic instabilities and anomalous specific heat behavior found in homogeneous gapless phases by Huang (2004) and Sedrakian (2006). The results enable future fine-tuning of model parameters (mass, gaplessness parameter, and blue quark gap) to fit observational surface temperature data from pulsars such as those compiled by Page (2009).
