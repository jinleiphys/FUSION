# What have we learned from antiproton proton scattering?

> From recent charge exchange measurements in the extreme forward direction, an independent and precise determination of the pion nucleon coupling constant is possible. This determination has reopened the debate on the value of this fundamental coupling constant of nuclear physics. Precise measurements of charge exchange observables at forward angles below 900 MeV/c would also give a better understanding of the long range part of the two-pion exchange potential. For example, the confirmation of the coherence of the tensor forces from the pion exchange and the isovector two-pion exchange would be very valuable. With the present data first attempts at an $\NbarN$ partial wave analysis have been made where, as in nucleon nucleon scattering, the antinucleon nucleon high J partial waves are mainly given by one-pion exchange. Finally a recent $\pbarp$ atomic cascade calculation and the fraction of P-state annihilation in gas targets is commented on.

## Key claim

The paper argues that recent antiproton-proton charge exchange measurements in the extreme forward direction provide a new, independent determination of the pion-nucleon coupling constant \(g_{\pi NN}^2/4\pi\), yielding a value approximately 5% smaller than the previously accepted value, thereby reopening the debate on this fundamental constant.

## Method

The author reviews the analysis of charge exchange data (\(\overline{p}p \rightarrow \overline{n}n\)) from the LEAR experiment PS206, where the differential cross section at forward angles (0 < \(-t\) < 1.5 \(m_\pi^2\)) is extrapolated to the pion pole at \(t = m_\pi^2\) using a polynomial expansion in \((m_\pi^2 - t)\). The residue of this pole gives the pion-nucleon coupling constant. The paper also discusses the use of meson-exchange potential models, partial wave analyses, and atomic cascade calculations to interpret \(\overline{N}N\) scattering data.

## Key numbers

- The pion-nucleon coupling constant from \(\overline{p}p \rightarrow \overline{n}n\) data is \(g_{\pi NN}^2/4\pi \approx 13.7\), about 5% smaller than the value from other methods (\(g_{\pi NN}^2/4\pi \approx 14.4\)).
- The Goldberger-Treiman relation gives a lowest-order value \(g_{\pi NN} \approx 12.7\) when \(m_u = m_d = m_\pi = 0\).
- The chiral symmetry scale \(\Lambda_\chi \sim 1\) GeV.
- The nucleon quark core radius is of order 0.8 fm.
- The two-pion exchange potential has a range of \((2 m_\pi)^{-1}\) or shorter.
- The forward angle measurements cover the range \(0 < -t < 1.5 m_\pi^2\).
- The author recommends measurements at laboratory momenta \(p_{lab} \sim 200 - 800\) MeV/c and below 400 MeV/c for further analysis.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the Nijmegen group (1990s), who advocated a smaller value for \(g_{\pi NN}^2/4\pi\) from both \(NN\) and \(\overline{N}N\) data within a one-boson-exchange potential model, and the Uppsala group (1990s), who reanalyzed \(np \rightarrow pn\) experiments. The paper also references the Paris and Nijmegen groups' first attempts at \(\overline{N}N\) partial wave analysis. The results enable better determination of the long-range two-pion exchange potential, which is the same in the \(NN\) potential and is crucial for understanding the stiffness of the nuclear equation of state, with implications for supernova explosions.
