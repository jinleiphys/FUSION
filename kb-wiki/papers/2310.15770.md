# Dynamical and finite-size effects on the criterion of first-order phase transition

> To identify first-order phase transitions in the dynamical process similar to the relativistic heavy-ion collisions, we investigate the dynamical behaviors of the first-order phase transition criterion in the Fokker-Planck framework. In the thermodynamic limit, the criterion can be expressed as combinations of cumulants or coefficients of an Ising-like effective potential. Our study reveals that factors such as phase transition scenarios, initial temperature, system volume, relaxation rate, and evolution trajectory have great impacts on the criterion, a larger initial temperature, a smaller volume, a larger relaxation rate, or bending of the trajectory will all lead to a reduction of the first-order phase transition signal, while volume expansion over time preserves signal integrity. Analysis along a hypothetical freezeout line shows that the signal is possibly preserved at relatively large chemical potentials.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the criterion Δ for first-order phase transitions, expressed in terms of cumulant ratios, is significantly suppressed or even eliminated by dynamical and finite-size effects in systems like heavy-ion collisions; the signal survives only for sufficiently large volumes, small relaxation rates, and initial temperatures close to the phase transition region, and is preserved at relatively large chemical potentials along a hypothetical freezeout line.

## Method

The authors adopt a parametric Landau free energy density of Ising form, parameterized in the temperature–baryon chemical potential plane, and compute equilibrium cumulants from the probability distribution P[σ] ∝ exp[−V Ω[σ]/T]. Dynamical evolution is modeled via a Fokker-Planck equation for the probability distribution of the σ field, truncated at fourth order, with a temperature-dependent relaxation rate τ_eff ∝ ξ^z (z=3). The coupled differential equations for the coefficients of the dynamical free energy are solved numerically along vertical or curved trajectories in the μ–T plane, and the criterion Δ = [8C₄/C₂ − 21(C₃/C₂)²] / [12(C₂/C₁)⁴] is evaluated from the resulting cumulants.

## Key numbers

- The critical endpoint (CEP) is placed at (T_c, μ_c) = (170, 240) MeV.
- The system volume is set to V = 10³ fm³ (typical QGP fireball size) for most calculations, with comparisons at V = 500 fm³ and V = 10⁵ fm³.
- The initial relaxation rate τ_rel is varied among 0.05, 0.1, and 0.2 fm.
- The initial temperature T₀ is varied among 175, 180, 185, and 190 MeV.
- The temperature evolution follows T(t) = T₀ [(t + t₀)/t₀]^(−λ) with t₀ = 10 fm and λ = 0.45.
- For μ = 250 MeV with V = 10³ fm³ and τ_rel = 0.1 fm, the positive Δ signal disappears during evolution; for μ = 260 and 270 MeV it survives at lower temperatures.
- At V = 500 fm³, the positive Δ signal disappears for all τ_rel values tested (0.05, 0.1, 0.2 fm); at V = 10⁵ fm³ it is preserved for τ_rel = 0.05 and 0.1 fm but fades for τ_rel = 0.2 fm.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical cumulant studies of Mukherjee (2015) and Jiang (2023) from the critical region into the first-order phase transition region, where discontinuities in the equation of state previously prevented such analysis. It also incorporates finite-size effects following the rounding of first-order transitions discussed by Brezin (1985) and Imry (1980). The criterion Δ, which in the thermodynamic limit agrees with the coexisting-phase condition of Lu (2025), is shown to be a practical observable for the Beam Energy Scan at RHIC, provided the dynamical and finite-size suppression factors are accounted for. The results enable quantitative modeling of whether first-order phase transition signals can survive the fireball expansion and be detected in net-proton cumulant measurements.
