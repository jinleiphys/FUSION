# The quest for the quark-gluon plasma from the perspective of dynamical models of relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> The physics of heavy-ion collisions is one of the most exciting and challenging directions of science for the last four decades. On the theoretical side one deals with a non-abelian field theory, while on the experimental side today's largest accelerators are needed to enable these studies. The discovery of a new stage of matter - called the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) - and the study of its properties is one of the major achievements of modern physics. In this contribution we briefly review the history of theoretical descriptions of heavy-ion collisions based on dynamical models, focusing on the personal experiences in this inspiring field.

## Key claim

This review argues that the successful description of experimental data from relativistic heavy-ion collisions requires the explicit inclusion of a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) phase in dynamical models, but the authors note that the influence of the QGP increases with bombarding energy and that at low collision energies (3-5 GeV) QGP droplets are tiny and their traces in observables are hardly visible since the dynamics is dominated by hadronic interactions.

## Method

The authors review the historical development of dynamical models for heavy-ion collisions, contrasting two main approaches: fully microscopic transport models (exemplified by the Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics or PHSD approach, which evolves partonic degrees of freedom via Kadanoff-Baym equations) and hybrid models (exemplified by the UrQMD hybrid approach, which couples a Boltzmann-equation initial stage to hydrodynamic evolution with a QCD equation of state and a hadronic afterburner). They compare model predictions to experimental data from AGS, SPS, RHIC, and LHC energies, focusing on observables such as transverse mass spectra, the K+/pi+ ratio, and elliptic flow.

## Key numbers

* The K+/pi+ ratio at 20 to 30 A·GeV (the "horn") is missed by approximately 40% in hadron-string transport models without a QGP phase, as shown by UrQMD and HSD calculations compared to NA49 data.
* The critical temperature for the QGP phase transition used in PHSD is Tc = 0.158 GeV.
* At high collision energies, approximately 90% of matter at midrapidity is in the QGP phase for a short time, as shown by PHSD calculations for Au+Au collisions.
* The QGP energy fraction at midrapidity (|y| < 0.5) in central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 19.6 GeV shows partonic interactions become dominant for about 10 fm/c.
* The inverse slope parameter for K+ spectra from central Au+Au reactions becomes significantly larger than pp results for sqrt(s) > 3.5 GeV, indicating missing pressure in hadronic-only models.

## Context

This review extends the authors' personal work on the UrQMD and PHSD transport approaches, which were developed to address discrepancies between hadron-string models and experimental data that emerged at RHIC energies. The paper contrasts with earlier parton cascade models such as VNI (Geiger, 1991), Zhang's Parton Cascade (Zhang, 1998), and BAMPS (Xu, 2004) that employed only ideal gas equations of state for the partonic phase. The work enables future Bayesian analyses to extract transport coefficients such as eta/s(T) and the charm diffusion coefficient D_s(T) from experimental data, as already begun by Bass (2017), Auvinen (2017), and Xu (2017).
