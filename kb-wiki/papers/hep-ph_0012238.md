# Key Issues in Hadronic Physics

> A group of fifty physicists met in Duck, NC, Nov. 6-9 to discuss the current status and future goals of hadronic physics. The main purpose of the meeting was to define the field by identifying its key issues, challenges, and opportunities. The conclusions, incorporating considerable input from the community at large, are presented in this white paper.

# Key Issues in Hadronic Physics

## Key claim

This white paper, produced by a group of fifty physicists meeting in Duck, NC in November 2000, argues that the focus of hadronic physics should no longer be on testing Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) but rather on understanding it, and that the combination of theoretical tools and experimental probes now available offers an unprecedented opportunity to make decisive progress on fundamental questions such as confinement, chiral symmetry breaking, and the quark-gluon structure of matter. The paper explicitly states it does not represent a response to the NSAC charge, a historical review, or an endorsement of any particular experimental effort, and notes that RHIC physics is being reviewed separately.

## Method

The paper synthesizes community input gathered at a workshop of fifty physicists to identify key issues, challenges, and opportunities in hadronic physics. It organizes the field into sections covering fundamental problems in strong interaction physics, hard scattering and form factors, spectroscopy, chiral structure, models, and experimental and theoretical tools. The document was prepared for presentation at a Town Meeting at Jefferson Lab and for the NSAC long range planning process.

## Key numbers

- Quark spins account for only about 30% of the nucleon's spin, based on precision measurements from polarized deep inelastic scattering over the past five years (relative to the year 2000).
- Gluons carry nearly 50% of the nucleon's momentum.
- The nuclear binding energy per particle is of the order of 10 MeV, compared to the natural QCD energy scale of hundreds of MeV.
- Ultra high energy cosmic rays have been observed with energies of the order of 10^11 GeV.
- Data from Drell-Yan and electroproduction experiments show a pronounced excess of dbar over ubar quarks at intermediate values of x.
- Polarized DIS data with the assumption of SU(3) quark flavor symmetry hint that s quarks may carry as much as -10% of the nucleon spin.
- Strange quarks account for only a few percent of the nucleon's total momentum based on DIS with neutrino beams and other experiments.

## Context

This white paper represents a community-wide effort to define the priorities and open questions in hadronic physics at the turn of the millennium, building on decades of work from the discovery of quarks through deeply inelastic scattering at SLAC to the EMC effect discovered by the European Muon Collaboration over a decade prior. It extends the long history of hadronic physics from phenomenological descriptions of hadron-hadron interactions and the hadron spectrum through to contemporary ideas on heavy quark symmetry, effective field theory, the quark-gluon plasma, and color superconducting phases. The paper identifies specific open questions including the origin of confinement, chiral symmetry breaking, the spin structure of the nucleon, the behavior of parton distributions at large x, and the role of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) as a new formalism bridging elastic and deeply inelastic scattering.
