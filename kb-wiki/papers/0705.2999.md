# Constraining properties of neutron stars with heavy-ion reactions in terrestrial laboratories

> Heavy-ion reactions provide a unique means to investigate the equation of state (EOS) of neutron-rich nuclear matter, especially the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy $E_{sym}(ρ)$. The latter plays an important role in understanding many key issues in both nuclear physics and astrophysics. Recent analyses of heavy-ion reactions have already put a stringent constraint on the $E_{sym}(ρ)$ around the saturation density. This subsequently allowed us to constrain significantly the radii and cooling mechanisms of neutron stars as well as the possible changing rate of the gravitational constant G.

# Constraining properties of neutron stars with heavy-ion reactions in terrestrial laboratories

## Key claim
Heavy-ion reaction data have placed stringent constraints on the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy \(E_{sym}(\rho)\) only at sub-saturation densities (up to about \(1.2\rho_0\)), which in turn allow constraints on neutron star radii, cooling mechanisms, and the possible time variation of the gravitational constant G, but predictions at supra-normal densities remain widely divergent.

## Method
The authors analyzed isospin diffusion in the reaction \(^{124}\)Sn + \(^{112}\)Sn at \(E_{beam}/A = 50\) MeV using a transport model with the MDI interaction, extracting constraints on the symmetry energy below \(1.2\rho_0\). They then applied these constrained symmetry energies (parameter sets \(x=0\) and \(x=-1\)) to calculate neutron star mass-radius relations and proton fractions, and used the gravitochemical heating formalism to derive an upper limit on the time variation of G.

## Key numbers
- Maximum density reached in the heavy-ion reaction studied is \(1.2\rho_0\) (normal nuclear matter density), so the symmetry energy constraints are only valid below this density.
- For the MDI interaction with \(x=0\), the minimum neutron star mass for direct URCA cooling is \(1.39\ \mathrm{M}_{\odot}\).
- Neutron star radii consistent with the terrestrial constraints lie between 11.5 and 13.6 km (radiation radii between 14.4 and 16.3 km) for a \(1.4\ \mathrm{M}_{\odot}\) neutron star.
- Upper limit on the relative changing rate of the gravitational constant: \(|\dot{G}/G| \le 4 \times 10^{-12}\ \mathrm{yr}^{-1}\).

## Context
This work extends the program of constraining the nuclear symmetry energy from heavy-ion reactions, building on prior transport model analyses by Li et al. (2003) and Chen et al. (2004). It connects these terrestrial constraints to neutron star properties, following the framework of Lattimer and Prakash (2001) and Steiner et al. (2005). The gravitochemical heating method was first suggested by Jofre, Reisenegger, and Fernandez (2006) for constraining \(\dot{G}/G\). The results enable tighter predictions for neutron star radii and cooling scenarios, and provide the best available laboratory-based limit on the time variation of G at the time of publication.
