# Topological Uncertainty for Anomaly Detection in the Neural-network EoS Inference with Neutron Star Data

> We study the performance of the Topological Uncertainty (TU) constructed with a trained feedforward neural network (FNN) for Anomaly Detection. Generally, meaningful information can be stored in the hidden layers of the trained FNN, and the TU implementation is one tractable recipe to extract buried information by means of the Topological Data Analysis. We explicate the concept of the TU and the numerical procedures. Then, for a concrete demonstration of the performance test, we employ the Neutron Star data used for inference of the equation of state (EoS). For the training dataset consisting of the input (Neutron Star data) and the output (EoS parameters), we can compare the inferred EoSs and the exact answers to classify the data with the label $k$. The subdataset with $k=0$ leads to the normal inference for which the inferred EoS approximates the answer well, while the subdataset with $k=1$ ends up with the unsuccessful inference. Once the TU is prepared based on the $k$-labled subdatasets, we introduce the cross-TU to quantify the uncertainty of characterizing the $k$-labeled data with the label $j$. The anomaly or unsuccessful inference is correctly detected if the cross-TU for $j=k=1$ is smaller than that for $j=0$ and $k=1$. In our numerical experiment, for various input data, we calculate the cross-TU and estimate the performance of Anomaly Detection. We find that performance depends on FNN hyperparameters, and the success rate of Anomaly Detection exceeds $90\%$ in the best case. We finally discuss further potential of the TU application to retrieve the information hidden in the trained FNN.

## Key claim
The Topological Uncertainty (TU) method, applied to a trained feedforward neural network (FNN) for equation-of-state (EoS) inference from neutron star data, can detect anomalous (unsuccessful) inferences with a success rate exceeding 90% in the best case, but this performance depends on the FNN hyperparameters and the tolerance parameter δ that defines the boundary between normal and anomalous inference.

## Method
The authors trained a feedforward neural network (FNNEoS) to map neutron star mass-radius data (input X) to EoS parameters (output Y), specifically the squared sound velocity at discretized energy densities. They introduced a label function K_δ that classifies each inference as successful (k=0) or unsuccessful (k=1) based on the L∞-norm of the logarithmic pressure difference between true and predicted EoS. For each layer of the trained FNN, they constructed a weighted bipartite graph from the weight matrices and neuron activations, computed the maximum spanning tree (MST) to obtain a persistence diagram for zero-dimensional homology, and defined the TU as the average Wasserstein-1 distance between the probability distribution of MST edge weights for a given input and the Fréchet mean distribution for each label class. The cross-TU was then used to quantify whether new data should be classified as normal or anomalous.

## Key numbers
- The training dataset consisted of N_data = 400,000 samples (N_MR = 20,000 M-R lines × N_sample = 20 sample points per line), with N_Star = 20 neutron stars and N_seg = 5 energy-density segments.
- The learning quality R_LQ(δ) exceeded 0.8 for δ > 0.2 for all tested FNN architectures, meaning more than 80% of training outputs were classified as correct inferences.
- The success rate of Anomaly Detection exceeded 90% in the best case, using the cross-TU with the tolerance parameter δ = 0.5 and truncation parameter N_trun = 3.
- The validation loss for the best-performing architecture F_(4,4,4,4) was 1.35 × 10^{-2}.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous FNNEoS framework (Fujimoto 2017, 2019, 2021, 2024) for inferring the dense-matter equation of state from neutron star observations, which relies on the one-to-one correspondence between the EoS and the mass-radius relation (Lindblom 1992). The TU method was originally proposed for classification problems (Gebhart 2019, Lacombe 2021) to detect out-of-distribution inputs where softmax confidence scores fail. This study provides the first demonstration of using TU for anomaly detection in a regression-based physics inference problem, enabling automated identification of unreliable EoS predictions without knowing the true answer, which is relevant for analyzing real observational data from x-ray and gravitational-wave experiments (Riley 2019, 2021; Miller 2019, 2021; LIGO Scientific 2017, 2018).
