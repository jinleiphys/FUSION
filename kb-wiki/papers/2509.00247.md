# Improving the Precision of First-Principles Calculation of Parton Physics from Lattice QCD

> Large Momentum Effective Theory (LaMET) provides a general framework for computing the multi-dimensional partonic structure of the proton from first principles using lattice quantum chromodynamics (QCD). In this effective field theory approach, LaMET predicts parton distributions through a power expansion and perturbative matching of a class of Euclidean observables -- quasi-distributions -- evaluated at large proton momenta. Recent advances in lattice renormalization, such as the hybrid scheme with leading-renormalon resummation, together with improved matching kernel that incorporates higher-loop corrections and resummations, have enhanced both the perturbative and power accuracy of LaMET, enabling a reliable quantification of theoretical uncertainties. Moreover, the Coulomb-gauge correlator approach further simplifies lattice analyses and improves the precision of transverse-momentum-dependent structures, particularly in the non-perturbative region. State-of-the-art LaMET calculations have already yielded certain parton observables with important phenomenological impact. In addition, the recently proposed kinematically enhanced lattice interpolation operators promise access to unprecedented proton momenta with greatly improved signal-to-noise ratios, which will extend the range of LaMET prediction and further suppress the power corrections. The remaining challenges, such as controlling excited-state contamination in lattice matrix elements and extracting gluonic distributions, are expected to benefit from emerging lattice techniques for ground-state isolation and noise reduction. Thus, lattice QCD studies of parton physics have entered an exciting stage of precision control and systematic improvement, which will have a broader impact for nuclear and particle experiments.

# Improving the Precision of First-Principles Calculation of Parton Physics from Lattice QCD

## Key claim
Large Momentum Effective Theory (LaMET) enables first-principles calculations of parton distributions from lattice QCD with systematically improvable uncertainties, but the precision is ultimately limited by the maximum attainable proton momentum and residual lattice systematics such as discretization, unphysical quark masses, and finite-volume effects. The paper argues that recent advances in renormalization, matching kernels, and Coulomb-gauge correlators have brought LaMET calculations to a stage where certain parton observables can reach 10% to 20% combined uncertainties in the valence region, though full systematic control remains an ongoing challenge.

## Method
The paper reviews the LaMET framework, which computes parton distributions through a power expansion and perturbative matching of Euclidean quasi-distributions evaluated at large proton momenta on the lattice. Key technical developments include the hybrid renormalization scheme with leading-renormalon resummation (LRR), higher-order perturbative matching at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) with renormalization group and threshold resummations, and the Coulomb-gauge correlator approach that eliminates Wilson-line linear divergences to improve signal-to-noise ratios at large transverse separations.

## Key numbers
- Pion valence PDF calculated at proton momentum $P^z = 1.94$ GeV using NNLO matching with LRR, RGR, and TR, on a lattice with spacing $a = 0.04$ fm and pion mass $m_\pi = 300$ MeV
- Combined uncertainties from statistics, scale variation, higher order and power corrections estimated to be from 10% to 20% across the range $0.2 \lesssim x \lesssim 0.8$
- LaMET predictions are valid for $x \in [x_0, 1-x_0]$ with $x_0 \sim \Lambda_{\rm QCD}/P^z$
- State-of-the-art calculation of the quark Collins-Soper kernel performed at physical quark masses with continuum limit and next-to-next-to-leading-logarithmic matching

## Context
This work reviews the development of LaMET, originally proposed by Ji (2013), as a framework for computing parton distribution functions (PDFs), transverse-momentum-dependent distributions (TMDs), and generalized parton distributions (GPDs) from lattice QCD. It extends earlier efforts focused on Mellin moments (Kronfeld, 1984; Martinelli, 1987) and other approaches for x-dependence (Liu, 1993; Detmold, 2005; Braun, 2007; Radyushkin, 2017). The advances described enable precision comparisons with global fits from groups such as GRVP1-92, ASV-10, xFitter-20, and JAM21NLO, and are expected to provide increasingly important inputs for experiments at Jefferson Lab, the Electron-Ion Collider, and CERN. Remaining challenges include controlling excited-state contamination and extracting gluonic distributions, which may benefit from emerging techniques such as the generalized eigenvalue problem approach and Lanczos methods for matrix-element extraction.
