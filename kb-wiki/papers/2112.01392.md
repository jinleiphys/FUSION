# Ab initio nuclear thermodynamics from lattice effective field theory

> We show that the {\it ab initio} calculations of nuclear thermodynamics can be performed efficiently using lattice effective field theory. The simulations use a new approach called the pinhole trace algorithm to calculate thermodynamic observables for a fixed number of protons and neutrons enclosed in a finite box. In this framework, we calculate the equation of state, the liquid-vapor coexistence line and the critical point of neutral symmetric nuclear matter with high precision. Since the algorithm uses a canonical ensemble with a fixed number of particles, it provides a sizable computational advantage over grand canonical ensemble simulations that can be a factor of several thousands to as much as several millions for large volume simulations.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that *ab initio* calculations of nuclear thermodynamics can be performed efficiently using lattice effective field theory with the pinhole trace algorithm, which uses a canonical ensemble with fixed particle numbers rather than a grand canonical ensemble. The computational advantage over grand canonical simulations can be a factor of several thousands to as much as several millions for large volume simulations, though the method is demonstrated only for neutral symmetric nuclear matter at the leading-order pionless EFT level.

## Method

The authors performed lattice effective field theory simulations using a leading-order SU(4)-invariant pionless interaction with two-body and three-body contact terms, with parameters fit to deuteron, triton, and medium-mass nuclei properties. They introduced the pinhole trace algorithm (PTA), which computes the canonical partition function by sampling over Slater determinant basis states of fixed nucleon number and over auxiliary fields, using a shuttle algorithm for auxiliary field updates and Metropolis updates for nucleon positions and spins. The chemical potential was extracted using a quantum adaptation of the Widom insertion method, and the pressure was obtained by integrating the Gibbs-Duhem equation.

## Key numbers

* Lattice spacing: a = 1.32 fm, corresponding to momentum cutoff Lambda = pi/a approximately 471 MeV.
* Saturation density of neutral symmetric nuclear matter at zero temperature: rho_0 = 0.205 fm^{-3}, with energy per nucleon E/A = -16.9 MeV.
* Two-body coupling: C_2 = -3.41 x 10^{-7} MeV^{-2}; three-body coupling: C_3 = -1.4 x 10^{-14} MeV^{-5}; smearing parameters s_L = 0.061 and s_NL = 0.5.
* Computational scaling of PTA: for large A and L, CPU time scales as A^2 L^3 (with L_t = 100 time steps).
* Speedup factor of PTA over grand canonical BSS algorithm: V/A, which can be as large as one thousand depending on lattice spacing and particle density.

## Context

This work extends earlier lattice EFT efforts for nuclear thermodynamics (Muller 1999, Lee 2004) which had made little progress due to the computational cost of grand canonical simulations in large volumes. The pinhole trace algorithm builds on the pinhole algorithm introduced by Elhatisari (2017) for sampling nucleon positions and spin/isospin, but adapts it to perform a full quantum mechanical trace over states for thermodynamic calculations. The approach enables first-principles computation of the equation of state, liquid-vapor coexistence line, and critical point of symmetric nuclear matter, and can be extended to asymmetric matter and neutron star physics.
