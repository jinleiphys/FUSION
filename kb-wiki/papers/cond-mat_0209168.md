# Nonextensivity: from low-dimensional maps to Hamiltonian systems

## Key claim

The authors argue that for long-range interacting systems (interaction range exponent 0 ≤ α/d ≤ 1), the standard Boltzmann-Gibbs equilibrium is reached only in the limit N→∞ followed by t→∞, whereas the physically relevant t→∞ followed by N→∞ limit generically yields a non-BG quasi-stationary state that may persist for times diverging with system size; the nonextensive statistical mechanics based on the q-entropy may describe these quasi-stationary states.

## Method

The paper reviews three classes of dynamical systems: (i) one-dimensional dissipative maps (the logistic map), where sensitivity to initial conditions and entropy production are analyzed at the edge of chaos using q-exponential functions; (ii) two-dimensional conservative maps (the standard map), where average sensitivity and entropy production are studied across the chaotic-to-regular transition; and (iii) classical many-body long-range-interacting Hamiltonians (the Hamiltonian Mean Field model), where microcanonical simulations with water-bag initial conditions are used to characterize quasi-stationary states and their lifetime scaling with N.

## Key numbers

- Logistic map at edge of chaos: q = 0.2445... and λ_q = ln α / ln 2 = 1.3236..., where α is the Feigenbaum constant
- Logistic map multifractal analysis: α_min = 0.380..., α_max = 0.755..., yielding q = 0.2445...
- Standard map: q ≈ 0.3 for the power-law sensitivity regime at small values of the control parameter a
- HMF model: critical energy density U_c = 0.75, corresponding to critical temperature T_c = 0.5
- HMF model: largest Lyapunov exponent in the quasi-stationary state λ_QSS scales as N^{-1/9}
- HMF model at U = 0.69: lifetime of the quasi-stationary state τ_QSS diverges with N

## Context

This review extends the nonextensive statistical mechanics framework introduced by Tsallis (1988) from low-dimensional maps to many-body Hamiltonian systems with long-range interactions. It builds on prior work on the logistic map (Schuster; Baranger et al.), the standard map (Chirikov), and the HMF model (Antoni and Ruffo, 1995; Latora et al., 1998-2001). The results enable the characterization of quasi-stationary states in long-range interacting systems, with implications for understanding systems where Lyapunov spectra approach zero and chaos becomes weak, such as self-gravitating systems and plasmas.
