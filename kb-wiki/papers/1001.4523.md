# Equivalent Hamiltonians

> I give a characterization of the conditions for two Hamiltonians to be equivalent, discuss the construction of the operators that relate equivalent Hamiltonians, and introduce variational methods that can select Hamiltonians with desirable features from the space of equivalent Hamiltonians.

## Key claim

The paper characterizes when two Hamiltonians are "equivalent" in a stronger sense than mere unitary equivalence: they must preserve the S-matrix and the description of free particles, which requires that the unitary transformation relating them satisfy specific asymptotic conditions (the Ekstein theorem) rather than just being a unitary map.

## Method

The author uses the two-Hilbert-space formulation of multichannel scattering theory to derive necessary and sufficient conditions for two Hamiltonians to yield identical scattering observables. A class of scattering-equivalent unitary transformations is constructed via the Cayley transform of a Hermitian operator Γ that has a cluster expansion, where each cluster component is compact after removing momentum-conserving delta functions. The construction is made computationally tractable by taking the cluster components of Γ to be finite-rank operators, reducing the problem to linear algebra and quadratures.

## Key numbers

- The Möbius function for the partial ordering of partitions is given explicitly: δ_{a⊇b}^{-1} = (-1)^{n_a} ∏_{i=1}^{n_a} (-)^{n_{b_i}} (n_{b_i}-1)! for a ⊇ b, and 0 otherwise.
- The coefficients relating the connected part of an operator to its cluster components are C_a = -δ_{1⊇a}^{-1} = (-)^{n_a} (n_a-1)!.
- For a three-body example with rank-one projections, the kernel of the integral equation for the resolvent R involves a 10-dimensional vector space (ten operators appear in the finite-rank kernel).

## Context

This work extends the formal scattering theory of Ekstein (1960) and provides a variational framework for constructing equivalent Hamiltonians, complementing renormalization group methods (Bogner 2003, Nogga 2004) and unitary transformation approaches (Okubo 1954, Suzuki 1980) that suppress off-diagonal couplings between high- and low-energy scales. The method enables independent control of two-, three-, and many-body forces in the transformed Hamiltonian, which is relevant for many-body calculations where the transformed interactions from other methods introduce uncontrolled many-body forces.
