# Elastic properties of phases with nonspherical nuclei in dense matter

> We consider the elastic constants of phases with nonspherical nuclei, so-called pasta phases, predicted to occur in the inner crust of a neutron star. First, we treat perfectly ordered phases and give numerical estimates for lasagna and spaghetti when the pasta elements are spatially uniform: the results are in order-of-magnitude agreement with the numerical simulations of Caplan, Schneider, and Horowitz, Phys. Rev. Lett. 121, 132701 (2018). We then turn to pasta phases without long-range order and calculate upper (Voigt) and lower (Reuss) bounds on the effective shear modulus and find that the lower bound is zero, but the upper bound is nonzero. To obtain better estimates, we then apply the self-consistent formalism and find that this predicts that the shear modulus of the phases without long-range order is zero if the pasta elements are spatially uniform. In numerical simulations, the pasta elements are found to be modulated spatially and we show that this modulation is crucial to obtaining a nonzero elastic moduli for pasta phases without long-range order. In the self-consistent formalism we find that, for lasagna, the effective shear modulus is linear in the elastic constants that do not vanish when the pasta elements are spatially uniform while, for spaghetti, it varies as the square root of these elastic constants. We also consider the behavior of the elastic constant associated with a homologous strain (hydrostatic compression) of the structure of the pasta phases without long-range order.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for pasta phases (nonspherical nuclei in neutron star crusts) without long-range order, the effective shear modulus is zero if the pasta elements are spatially uniform, but becomes nonzero when spatial modulation of the pasta elements is taken into account; the self-consistent formalism predicts that for lasagna the effective shear modulus is linear in the small elastic constants, while for spaghetti it varies as the square root of these constants.

## Method

The authors performed analytical calculations of elastic constants for perfectly ordered lasagna and spaghetti phases using a liquid drop model, then applied the Voigt (upper bound) and Reuss (lower bound) approximations to estimate effective elastic moduli for polycrystalline pasta phases without long-range order. They further employed the self-consistent formalism, solving coupled equations for the effective shear modulus and bulk modulus, and compared results with the Hill average and numerical simulations.

## Key numbers

- For lasagna without modulations, the elastic constant c33 ≈ 3 × 10^31 erg cm^{-3} for σ = 0.1 MeV fm^{-2}, n_pi ≈ 0.05 n_s, and u = 0.5
- For spaghetti without modulations, (c11 + c12)/2 ≈ 1 × 10^31 erg cm^{-3} for σ = 0.1 MeV fm^{-2}, n_pi = 0.1 n_s, and u = 0.3
- For lasagna, c33 = 3 E_surf, where E_surf is the surface energy per unit volume
- For spaghetti, (c11 + c12)/2 = (3/4) E_surf and c66 = E_Coul g(u) = (E_surf/2) g(u)
- The function g(u) for spaghetti is fitted by g(u) = 10^{2.1(u-0.3)} for the range where spaghetti is expected stable
- For lasagna, the Voigt bound for shear modulus μ_V ≈ c33/15 and for bulk modulus K^u_V ≈ c33/9
- For spaghetti, the Voigt bound μ_V ≈ (1/30)(7c11 - 5c12) and K^u_V ≈ (2/9)(c11 + c12)
- The self-consistent effective shear modulus for lasagna is linear in the small parameter p, while for spaghetti it varies as p^{1/2} for small p

## Context

This work extends the analytical framework of Pethick and Potekhin (1998) for elastic constants of ordered pasta phases and provides theoretical underpinning for the molecular dynamics simulations of Caplan, Schneider, and Horowitz (2018). The paper contradicts the common astrophysical practice of using the Voigt upper bound to predict elastic properties of polycrystals, showing this gives qualitatively incorrect results for pasta phases. The results enable calculations of collective mode frequencies in neutron star crusts and estimates of continuous gravitational wave emission from rotating neutron stars, and can be combined with superfluid density tensor calculations using analogous methods.
