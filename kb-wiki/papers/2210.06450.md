# Machine learning-based jet and event classification at the Electron-Ion Collider with applications to hadron structure and spin physics

> We explore machine learning-based jet and event identification at the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC). We study the effectiveness of machine learning-based classifiers at relatively low EIC energies, focusing on (i) identifying the flavor of the jet and (ii) identifying the underlying hard process of the event. We propose applications of our machine learning-based jet identification in the key research areas at the future EIC and current Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider program, including enhancing constraints on (transverse momentum dependent) parton distribution functions, improving experimental access to transverse spin asymmetries, studying photon structure, and quantifying the modification of hadrons and jets in the cold nuclear matter environment in electron-nucleus collisions. We establish first benchmarks and contrast the estimated performance of flavor tagging at the EIC with that at the Large Hadron Collider. We perform studies relevant to aspects of detector design including particle identification, charge information, and minimum transverse momentum capabilities. Additionally, we study the impact of using full event information instead of using only information associated with the identified jet. These methods can be deployed either on suitably accurate Monte Carlo event generators, or, for several applications, directly on experimental data. We provide an outlook for ultimately connecting these machine learning-based methods with first principles calculations in quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

Machine learning-based classifiers can effectively identify jet flavor and underlying hard processes at the relatively low energies of the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), where jet transverse momenta are predominantly 10-30 GeV, and these classifiers can enhance constraints on parton distribution functions and spin asymmetries, though the reduced energy and increased sparsity of jets compared to the LHC may affect performance and Monte Carlo biases must be considered.

## Method

The authors generated simulated events using PYTHIA6 with the eRHIC tune, producing two data sets: leading-order deep inelastic scattering (DIS) events for jet flavor tagging studies, and low-Q² photoproduction di-jet events for quark vs. gluon and direct vs. resolved photon classification. Jets were reconstructed using the anti-kT algorithm with radius parameter R=1.0 in the laboratory frame, requiring pTjet > 10 GeV for single jets and considering rapidity |ηlab| < 4. The machine learning classifiers were trained on particle-level information including four-momenta, particle identification, and charge information, with studies varying minimum particle pT capabilities and comparing performance when using only in-jet information versus full event information.

## Key numbers

- Jet transverse momenta at the EIC are predominantly 10-30 GeV, compared to O(100-1000 GeV) at the LHC
- For DIS events, photon virtuality was required in the range 25 < Q² < 1000 GeV² and inelasticity 0.1 < y < 0.85
- For photoproduction events, Q² was required in the range 10⁻⁵ < Q² < 1 GeV² with the same inelasticity cut 0.1 < y < 0.85
- A sample of 14 million DIS events satisfied the selection criteria for single jet studies
- Jet radius parameter R=1.0 was used for jet reconstruction
- Particles with lifetime cτ > 1 cm were included in the analysis
- The EIC center-of-mass energy reaches up to √s = 141 GeV

## Context

This work extends machine learning-based jet classification techniques, previously developed for high-energy LHC physics (Gallicchio 2011, deOliveira 2015, Komiske 2018), to the lower energy regime of the future EIC, where jets are more sparse and Monte Carlo modeling has larger non-perturbative contributions. The authors propose applications to enhance constraints on transverse momentum dependent parton distribution functions, improve measurements of transverse single spin asymmetries (building on STAR Collaboration measurements and proposals by Kang 2020), and study cold nuclear matter effects in electron-nucleus collisions. The work establishes first benchmarks for flavor tagging at the EIC and provides guidance for detector design considerations including particle identification capabilities and minimum transverse momentum thresholds.
