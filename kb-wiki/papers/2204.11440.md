# $ϕ$ meson properties in nuclear matter from QCD sum rules with chirally separated four-quark condensates

> The modification of the $ϕ$ meson spectrum in nuclear matter is studied in an updated QCD sum rule analysis, taking into account recent improvements in properly treating the chiral invariant and breaking components of four-quark condensates. Allowing both mass and decay width to change at finite density, the QCD sum rule analysis determines certain combinations of changes for these parameters that satisfy the sum rules equally well. A comprehensive error analysis, including uncertainties related to the behavior of various condensates at linear order in density, the employed renormalization scale and perturbative corrections of the Wilson coefficients, is used to compute the allowed ranges of these parameter combinations. We find that the $ϕ$ meson mass shift in nuclear matter is especially sensitive to the strange sigma term $σ_{sN}$, which determines the decrease of the strange quark condensate in nuclear matter. Specifically, we obtain a linear relation between the width $Γ_ϕ$ and mass shift $Δm_ϕ$ given as $ Γ_ϕ = aΔm_ϕ + bσ_{sN}+c$ with $a = (3.947^{+0.139}_{-0.130})$, $b = (0.936^{+0.180}_{-0.177} )$ and $c = -(7.707^{+4.791}_{-5.679}) \mathrm{MeV}$.

## Key claim

The QCD sum rule analysis shows that the $\phi$ meson mass shift and decay width in nuclear matter cannot be uniquely determined independently, but instead satisfy a linear relation that depends on the strange sigma term $\sigma_{sN}$; the mass shift is especially sensitive to this quantity, which determines the decrease of the strange quark condensate in nuclear matter.

## Method

The authors performed an updated QCD sum rule analysis of the $\phi$ meson in nuclear matter, incorporating two major improvements over previous work: allowing both the mass and decay width to vary in the phenomenological spectral function parametrization, and separating the four-quark condensates into chiral symmetric and chiral breaking components treated independently. The vacuum sum rules for the $\phi$ and its parity partner $f_1^{s\bar{s}}$ were first used to extract the four-quark condensate parameters via auxiliary parameters $\kappa$, after which the finite-density sum rules were solved using the linear density approximation for the strange quark and gluon condensates, with the strange sigma term $\sigma_{sN}$ as a key input parameter.

## Key numbers

* The linear relation between the $\phi$ meson width $\Gamma_\phi$ and mass shift $\Delta m_\phi$ in nuclear matter is $\Gamma_\phi = a\Delta m_\phi + b\sigma_{sN}+c$ with $a = (3.947^{+0.139}_{-0.130})$, $b = (0.936^{+0.180}_{-0.177})$, and $c = -(7.707^{+4.791}_{-5.679})\ \mathrm{MeV}$.
* The vacuum $\phi$ meson mass used as input is 1019.45 MeV with a width of 4.26 MeV; the $f_1^{s\bar{s}}$ meson mass is 1426.3 MeV with a width of 54.5 MeV.
* The vacuum saturation parameter $\kappa_\phi$ extracted from the vacuum sum rule is 2.0056 at $\mu = 1$ GeV (with $\sqrt{s_0}=1.438$ GeV) and 2.9390 at $\mu = 2$ GeV (with $\sqrt{s_0}=1.448$ GeV).
* The chiral symmetry breaking four-quark condensate $\alpha_s \langle B_{ss} \rangle_B$ is evaluated as $(0.2060\ \mathrm{GeV})^6$ at $\mu=1$ GeV and $(0.2375\ \mathrm{GeV})^6$ at $\mu=2$ GeV.
* The KEK E325 experimental result (negative mass shift and broadening at normal nuclear matter density) is consistent with $\sigma_{sN} \sim 125$ MeV, while lattice QCD calculations give $\sigma_{sN} \sim 50$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the QCD sum rule approach of Gubler (2014) by including broadening effects in the spectral function and applying the chirally separated four-quark condensate treatment developed by Kim (2020, 2021) for light vector and axial-vector mesons to the strange sector. The results enable experimental programs at J-PARC E16, HADES, and ALICE to interpret their measurements of the $\phi$ meson in nuclear matter in terms of the strange sigma term $\sigma_{sN}$, and highlight a tension between the KEK E325 result and lattice QCD calculations of $\sigma_{sN}$ by Alexandrou (2019), Borsanyi (2020), and Copeland (2021).
