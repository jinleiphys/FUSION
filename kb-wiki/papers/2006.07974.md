# Force and pressure in many-particle quantum dynamics

> The Newtonian concept of force may be useful in some aspects of the dynamics of many-particle quantum systems such as fissioning nuclei. Following Ehrenfest's method, we show that the quantum kinetic force between parts of an extended quantum system can be described by an operator acting on the boundary between the two subsystems. The contribution to the force due to a short-ranged particle interaction can also be treated in the same way. This includes interaction effects treated in density functional theory by local functionals. The force operators are applied to several simple models to demonstrate the method.

# Force and pressure in many-particle quantum dynamics

## Key claim
The quantum kinetic force between parts of an extended quantum system can be described by an operator acting only at the boundary between the two subsystems, provided the particle number in each subsystem is stationary at the time of force evaluation. This operator approach works for short-ranged interactions treated in density functional theory by local functionals, but long-range forces such as Coulomb require integration over the full density distribution.

## Method
The author derives force operators using the double-commutator Heisenberg representation method following Ehrenfest's theorem, applied to the kinetic Hamiltonian and to potential terms. For one-dimensional systems, the kinetic force operator is expressed in terms of derivatives of the wave function at the interface point x0, and the potential contribution reduces to an integral of density times potential gradient. The method is generalized to three dimensions via a stress tensor formalism, and to many-particle systems using the single-particle density matrix and Wigner representation.

## Key numbers
- For a particle in a box of length L, the force on the boundary is F = ℏ²π²n²/(M L³), and the kinetic force operator at any interior division point x0 reproduces this same value.
- For a Gaussian wave packet with width parameter b, the acceleration of the right-hand density distribution is d²⟨x̂_R⟩/dt² = (1/(2π^{1/2} M² b³)) (1 + (ℏ t/M b²)²)^{-3/2}.
- For a one-dimensional Fermi gas with N particles in a box, the total force on the wall is F_b = (ℏ² k_F²/(3 M)) (N/L) + (1/2) v₀ (N²/L²), where k_F ≈ πN/L.
- For a local density functional with interaction energy density V = (1/2) v₀ ρ²(x), the force at the interface is F_V = -(1/2) v₀ ρ²(x₀).
- For a general local energy density functional V(ρ) = Σ_m (1/m!) v_m ρ^m, the force is F_V = Σ_m (1/m!) (m-1) v_m ρ(x₀)^m.
- For diabatically deformed Fermi surfaces, the Lame coefficients of the kinetic stress tensor are λ = μ = k_f²/(5M).

## Context
This work extends the Ehrenfest theorem to subsystems of extended quantum systems, providing a formal framework for calculating forces relevant to nuclear fission dynamics where nascent fragments are joined by a neck region. The approach contrasts with the Feynman-Hellman method for constrained mean-field calculations, offering an independent check. The author notes that pairing effects, important in low-frequency nuclear dynamics via Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov theory (Bulgac 2016), produce forces proportional to pairing energy density at the division point but are typically negligible. The stress tensor formalism connects to earlier work on diabatic dynamics in Fermi systems including zero sound in liquid ³He (Wheatley 1967), plasmons in conductors (Jahnke 1967), and giant quadrupole resonances in nuclei (Lipparini and Stringari 1998).
