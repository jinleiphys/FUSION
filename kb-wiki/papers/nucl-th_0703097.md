# Determining neutron-capture cross sections via the surrogate reaction technique

> Indirect methods play an important role in the determination of nuclear reaction cross sections that are hard to measure directly. In this paper we investigate the feasibility of using the so-called surrogate method to extract neutron-capture cross sections for low energy compound-nuclear reactions in spherical and near-spherical nuclei. We present the surrogate method and develop a statistical nuclear-reaction simulation to explore different approaches to utilize surrogate reaction data. We assess the success of each approach by comparing the extracted cross sections with a predetermined benchmark. In particular, we employ regional systematics of nuclear properties in the 34 <= Z <= 46 region to calculate (n,gamma) cross sections for a series of Zr isotopes, and to simulate a surrogate experiment and the extraction of the desired cross section. We identify one particular approach that may provide very useful estimates of the cross section, and we discuss some of the limitations of the method. General recommendations for future (surrogate) experiments are also given.

## Key claim

The surrogate reaction method can provide useful estimates of low-energy neutron-capture cross sections for spherical and near-spherical nuclei in the mass 90-100 region, but only if the spin-parity population mismatch between the neutron-induced and surrogate reactions is properly accounted for; the Weisskopf-Ewing approximation is not valid for these low-energy capture reactions.

## Method

The authors developed a statistical nuclear-reaction simulation using a modified version of the Hauser-Feshbach code Stapre, which allows arbitrary spin-parity distributions for the compound nucleus to be read in from a file rather than calculated from entrance-channel transmission coefficients. They employed regional systematics for nuclear properties (level densities, gamma strength functions, and shell corrections) in the 34 ≤ Z ≤ 46 region to calculate (n,γ) cross sections for a series of Zr isotopes, and then simulated a surrogate experiment to test different approaches for extracting the desired cross section from surrogate data, comparing each extracted result against a predetermined benchmark.

## Key numbers

- Maxwellian-averaged (n,γ) cross sections (MACS) at k_B T = 30 keV were calculated for six Zr isotopes: ⁹⁰Zr, ⁹¹Zr, ⁹²Zr, ⁹³Zr, ⁹⁴Zr, and ⁹⁵Zr; results were compared to recommended values from Bao et al. (2000)
- For the (n,γ) reaction on ⁹¹Zr (producing ⁹²Zr), the authors performed detailed surrogate experiment simulations; this case served as the reference benchmark
- Width fluctuation corrections deplete the (n,γ) cross section by less than 10% below 1 MeV neutron energy, and are negligible above that
- The regional systematics for the shell correction δW(Z,N) were determined by least-squares quadratic fits for N ≤ 50 (coefficients: c₀ = -0.114399, c₁ = 9.44901, c₂ = -188.821) and for N > 50 (c₀ = -0.0421006, c₁ = 5.45665, c₂ = -171.285), valid for 34 ≤ Z ≤ 46
- The systematics for the average total s-wave radiation width ⟨Γ_γ⟩₀ (in meV) were fit as ⟨Γ_γ⟩₀^sys = [aA + b][c log₁₀(D₀) + d] with a = -5.928, b = 343.8, c = -42.43, d = 343.8, where D₀ is in keV

## Context

This work extends the surrogate method, previously applied to (n,f) cross sections in actinides by Cramer and Britt (1970), Britt and Wilhelmy (1979), Petit et al. (2004), Burke et al. (2006), Plettner et al. (2005), and Escher and Dietrich (2006), to the determination of (n,γ) cross sections on spherical and near-spherical nuclei in the mass 90-100 region. The study builds on early surrogate experiments in the mass-90 region by Britt et al. (1976) and on the work of Younes and Britt (2003) who demonstrated that accounting for the spin-parity population mismatch can significantly affect extracted results. The regional systematics developed here enable future surrogate experiments on s-process branch-point nuclei such as ⁹³Zr and ⁹⁵Zr, which are important for understanding nucleosynthesis in AGB stars and interpreting presolar grain measurements from Nicolussi et al. (1997).
