# Branching ratios of mesonic and nonmesonic antikaon absorptions in nuclear medium

> The branching ratios of K^- absorption at rest in nuclear matter are theoretically investigated in order to understand the mechanism of K^- absorption into nuclei. For this purpose mesonic and nonmesonic absorption potentials are evaluated as functions of nuclear density, the kaon momentum and energy from one- and two-body K^- self-energy, respectively. By using a chiral unitary approach for the s-wave Kbar N amplitude we find that both the mesonic and nonmesonic absorption potentials are dominated by the Lambda(1405) contributions. The fraction of the mesonic and nonmesonic absorptions are evaluated to be respectively about 70% and 30% at the saturation density almost independently on the kaon momentum. We also observe different behavior of the branching ratios to pi ^+ Sigma^- and pi^- Sigma^+ channels in mesonic absorption due to the interference between Lambda(1405) and the I=1 nonresonant background, which is consistent with experimental results. The nonmesonic absorption ratios [Lambda p]/[Sigma^0 p] and [Lambda n]/[Sigma^0 n] are about unity while [Sigma^+ n]/[Sigma^0 p] and [Sigma^- p]/[Sigma^0 n] are about two due to the Lambda(1405) dominance in absorption. Taking into account the kaon momenta and energies, the absorption potentials become weaker due to the downward shift of the initial K^-N two-body energy, but this does not drastirally change the nonmesonic fraction. The Sigma(1385) contribution in the p-wave Kbar N amplitude is examined and found to be very small compared to the Lambda(1405) contribution in slow K^- absorption.

## Key claim

The branching ratios of mesonic and nonmesonic antikaon absorption in nuclear matter are evaluated using a chiral unitary approach, finding that both absorption potentials are dominated by the Lambda(1405) resonance contributions, with the mesonic fraction about 70% and nonmesonic about 30% at saturation density, though this result depends on the assumption that in-medium effects on mesons, baryons, and hyperon resonances are neglected as a zeroth-order approximation.

## Method

The authors calculated the imaginary part of the K- self-energy in symmetric nuclear matter using the Thomas-Fermi approximation for bound nucleons, evaluating one-body (mesonic) and two-body (nonmesonic) absorption potentials from Feynman diagrams. The s-wave Kbar N to meson-baryon transition amplitudes were obtained from a chiral unitary approach that dynamically generates the Lambda(1405) resonance, while the nonmesonic absorption was modeled via one-meson exchange with short-range correlations and SU(3) coupling constants. The absorption potentials were computed as functions of nuclear density, kaon momentum, and kaon energy.

## Key numbers

- Mesonic absorption fraction: about 70% at saturation density (rho_0 = 0.17 fm^-3)
- Nonmesonic absorption fraction: about 30% at saturation density
- Nonmesonic absorption ratios: [Lambda p]/[Sigma^0 p] and [Lambda n]/[Sigma^0 n] are about unity
- Nonmesonic absorption ratios: [Sigma^+ n]/[Sigma^0 p] and [Sigma^- p]/[Sigma^0 n] are about two
- Lambda(1405) peak position in the chiral unitary approach: about 1420 MeV with a width of about 40 MeV
- Density range where Lambda(1405) energy is accessible: rho_N approximately 0.05 to 0.1 fm^-3
- Short-range correlation cutoff parameter: Lambda = 1.0 GeV, q_C = 780 MeV
- Pion decay constant used: f = f_pi = 93.0 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Sekihara 2009) which discussed nonmesonic decay of Lambda(1405) in nuclear matter using a one-meson exchange model, by now considering an explicit K- in the initial state rather than assuming perfect Lambda(1405) dominance. The results are relevant for interpreting stopped K- experiments on nuclei such as helium-4 (Katz 1970) and heavier nuclei (VanderVelde-Wilquet 1977), where nonpionic final states were observed at about 20% probability. The study enables more realistic calculations of kaonic atom properties and searches for kaonic nuclei, complementing theoretical analyses by Cieply (2011) and Friedman (2012) that use subthreshold in-medium Kbar N amplitudes.
