# The nature of near-threshold XYZ states

> We demonstrate that the recently observed $X$, $Y$, $Z$ states cannot be purely from kinematic effect. Especially the narrow near-threshold structures in elastic channels call for nearby poles of the $S$-matrix which are qualified as states. We propose a way to distinguish cusp effects from genuine states and demonstrate that (not all of) the recently observed $X$, $Y$, $Z$ states cannot be purely from kinematic effects. Especially, we show that the narrow near-threshold structures in elastic channels call for nearby poles of the $S$-matrix, since the normal kinematic cusp effect cannot produce that narrow structures in the elastic channels in contrast to genuine $S$-matrix poles. In addition, it is also discussed how spectra can be used to distinguish different scenarios proposed for the structure of those poles, such as hadro-quarkonia, tetraquarks and hadronic molecules. The basic tool employed is heavy quark spin symmetry.

# The nature of near-threshold XYZ states

## Key claim
Narrow near-threshold structures observed in elastic channels (such as the Zc(3900) in Y(4260) → π D D*) cannot be produced by purely kinematic cusp effects; they necessarily require nearby poles of the S-matrix, which qualify as genuine physical states. The authors argue that while some XYZ structures in inelastic channels might be explained as cusps, the narrow elastic-channel peaks demand nonperturbative rescattering or quark-level poles.

## Method
The authors analyze the Y(4260) → π D D* decay using a separable interaction Lagrangian with a Gaussian regulator, fitting the D D* invariant mass distribution to BESIII data. They compare perturbative (tree-level plus one-loop) calculations with full resummation of bubble diagrams to infinite order, demonstrating that the perturbative treatment is inconsistent because two-loop contributions exceed one-loop contributions. Heavy quark spin symmetry (HQSS) is then employed to compare predictions from three dynamical models: hadro-quarkonia, tetraquarks, and hadronic molecules.

## Key numbers
- The perturbative fit (tree + one-loop) yields parameters: g_Y = 40.01 GeV^{-3/2}, C = 187.37 GeV^{-2}, Λ = 0.257 GeV.
- The nonperturbative fit (summing all bubble loops) yields: g_Y = 33.85 GeV^{-3/2}, C = 20.09 GeV^{-2}, Λ = 0.89 GeV.
- The nonperturbative fit produces a pole below the D D* threshold with binding energy 0.59 MeV.
- The perturbative requirement (two-loop contribution at most half of one-loop) cannot produce the pronounced near-threshold structure.
- The full two-loop contribution deviates from the one-loop result by more than allowed in a perturbative scheme.
- The bound state from summing all bubbles has a binding energy of 9 MeV when using the perturbative-fit parameters.

## Context
This work extends the debate on whether XYZ states are kinematic effects (as argued by Swanson (2014, 2015)) or genuine states. The authors directly contradict Swanson (2015), noting that while that work explains inelastic channels as cusps, it attributes elastic-channel structures to form factors, which the authors deem unnatural. The paper builds on Guo (2014) with updated BESIII data and uses HQSS to distinguish between hadro-quarkonia (Voloshin 2007, Dubynskiy 2008), tetraquarks (Maiani 2014), and hadronic molecules (Guo 2013, Cleven 2015). The analysis enables experimental searches at BESIII or future super tau-charm factories to discriminate between these models based on predicted spin partner masses and quantum numbers.
