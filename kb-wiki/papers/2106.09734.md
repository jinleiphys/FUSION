# Equation of State Dependence of Gravitational Waves in Core-Collapse Supernovae

> Gravitational waves (GWs) provide unobscured insight into the birthplace of neutron stars (NSs) and black holes in core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe). The nuclear equation of state (EOS) describing these dense environments is yet uncertain, and variations in its prescription affect the proto-neutron star (PNS) and the post-bounce dynamics in CCSNe simulations, subsequently impacting the GW emission. We perform axisymmetric simulations of CCSNe with Skyrme-type EOSs to study how the GW signal and PNS convection zone are impacted by two experimentally accessible EOS parameters, (1) the effective mass of nucleons, $m^\star$, which is crucial in setting the thermal dependence of the EOS, and (2) the isoscalar incompressibility modulus, $K_{\rm{sat}}$. While $K_{\rm{sat}}$ shows little impact, the peak frequency of the GWs has a strong effective mass dependence due to faster contraction of the PNS for higher values of $m^\star$ owing to a decreased thermal pressure. These more compact PNSs also exhibit more neutrino heating which drives earlier explosions and correlates with the GW amplitude via accretion plumes striking the PNS, exciting the oscillations. We investigate the spatial origin of the GWs and show the agreement between a frequency-radial distribution of the GW emission and a perturbation analysis. We do not rule out overshoot from below via PNS convection as another moderately strong excitation mechanism in our simulations. We also study the combined effect of effective mass and rotation. In all our simulations we find evidence for a power gap near $\sim$1250 Hz, we investigate its origin and report its EOS dependence.

## Key claim

The peak frequency of gravitational waves from core-collapse supernovae has a strong dependence on the nucleon effective mass parameter of the nuclear equation of state, while the isoscalar incompressibility modulus shows little impact; this effective mass dependence arises because higher effective mass values reduce thermal pressure, causing faster proto-neutron star contraction and earlier explosions.

## Method

The authors performed axisymmetric (2D) simulations of core-collapse supernovae using the FLASH code with a 20 solar mass progenitor, incorporating M1 neutrino transport and a modified general relativistic effective potential. They employed five Skyrme-type equations of state from Schneider (2019) that systematically vary the nucleon effective mass and the isoscalar incompressibility modulus, and also studied the combined effect of rotation with these parameters.

## Key numbers

- Peak gravitational wave frequency shows strong dependence on effective mass, with higher effective mass values producing more compact proto-neutron stars and higher peak frequencies
- A power gap near approximately 1250 Hz is found in all simulations, with a narrow width of about 50 Hz, persisting for hundreds of milliseconds
- The isoscalar incompressibility modulus K_sat shows little impact on gravitational wave signals compared to the effective mass
- The minimum grid zone spacing is approximately 326 meters, with angular resolution of 0.82 degrees beyond 80 km
- Neutrino opacities are computed for 12 logarithmically-spaced energy groups from 1 MeV to approximately 315 MeV

## Context

This work extends the spherically-symmetric simulations of Schneider (2019), who reported an effective mass dependence of post-bounce dynamics, into two dimensions to study gravitational wave emission. It builds on studies by Kuroda (2017), Pan (2018), and Morozova (2018) exploring equation of state dependence of gravitational waves, and investigates the power gap first noted by Morozova (2018) near 1200 Hz. The results enable future multimessenger observations of nearby core-collapse supernovae to constrain the nuclear equation of state through gravitational wave signals.
