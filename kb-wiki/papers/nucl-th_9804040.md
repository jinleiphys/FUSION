# Regularization and the potential of effective field theory in nucleon-nucleon scattering

> This paper examines the role that regularization plays in the definition of the potential used in effective field theory (EFT) treatments of the nucleon-nucleon interaction. I consider $NN$ scattering in $S$-wave channels at momenta well below the pion mass. In these channels (quasi-)bound states are present at energies well below the scale $m_π^2/M$ expected from naturalness arguments. I ask whether, in the presence of such a shallow bound state, there is a regularization scheme which leads to an EFT potential that is both useful and systematic. In general, if a low-lying bound state is present then cutoff regularization leads to an EFT potential which is useful but not systematic, and dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction leads to one which is systematic but not useful. The recently-proposed technique of dimensional regularization with power-law divergence subtraction allows the definition of an EFT potential which is both useful and systematic.

## Key claim

The paper argues that for nucleon-nucleon scattering in S-wave channels where shallow (quasi-)bound states exist at energies well below the natural scale \(m_\pi^2/M\), no single regularization scheme yields an effective field theory potential that is both systematic and useful, except the recently-proposed technique of dimensional regularization with power-law divergence subtraction (PDS), which satisfies both criteria.

## Method

The author analyzes S-wave nucleon-nucleon scattering at momenta well below the pion mass using an effective Lagrangian of contact operators (Eq. 1) and iterates the resulting potential (Eq. 2) via the Lippmann-Schwinger equation. Three regularization schemes are compared: momentum-space cutoff regularization, dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction (MSbar), and dimensional regularization with power-law divergence subtraction (PDS). For each scheme, the coefficients in the potential are renormalized to reproduce the first two terms of the effective range expansion (scattering length \(a\) and effective range \(r_e\)), and the resulting on-shell T-matrix and phase shifts are evaluated.

## Key numbers

* Scattering length in the \(^1S_0\) channel is unnaturally large, with bound-state energy well below the natural scale \(m_\pi^2/M\) (no explicit numerical value given for \(a\) or \(r_e\) in the text).
* Cutoff regularization with \(\beta = 150\) MeV to \(\beta = \infty\) yields phase shifts that reproduce the Nijmegen phase shift analysis up to momenta \(k\) of order \(m_\pi\) (Fig. 1).
* In cutoff EFT, the coefficients scale as \(C_2 \sim 1/(M \beta^3)\) and \(C \sim 1/(M \beta)\) (Eq. 14), leading to the ratio \(C_2/C \sim 1/\beta^2\) (Eq. 15).
* For the bound-state wave function from zeroth-order cutoff EFT, the quantum average \(\langle \hat{p}^{2n} \rangle / \beta^{2n} = 4/((2n-1)\pi) \cdot \sqrt{MB}/\beta\) for \(n=1,2,\ldots\) (Eq. 18), showing all higher-order terms contribute equally.
* In DR with MSbar, the coefficients are \(C^{\overline{\rm MS}} = 4\pi a / M\) and \(C_2^{\overline{\rm MS}} = \pi a^2 r_e / M\) (Eq. 21), yielding a domain of validity \(k^2 \ll 2/(a r_e)\).
* In PDS, the subtracted loop integral gives \(\mathcal{I}^{\rm PDS} = -M/(4\pi)(ik + \mu)\) (Eq. 24), and the coefficients become \(C^{\rm PDS}(\mu) = (4\pi/M)(-\mu + 1/a)^{-1}\) and \(C_2^{\rm PDS}(\mu) = (4\pi/M)(-\mu + 1/a)^{-2} r_e/4\) (Eq. 26).
* For PDS with \(\Lambda \gg \mu \gg 1/a\), the coefficients scale as \(C_{2n} \sim 1/(M \mu^{n+1} \Lambda^n)\) (Eq. 27), enabling systematic truncation.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory approach to nucleon-nucleon scattering pioneered by Weinberg (1990, 1991, 1992), who proposed applying chiral perturbation theory power counting to an n-nucleon effective potential rather than directly to the S-matrix. The paper contrasts with the cutoff-based EFT calculations of Lepage (1997) and Steele and Furnstahl (1998), which yield useful but not systematic potentials, and with the dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction approach of Kaplan et al. (1996), which is systematic but not useful. The PDS scheme, proposed by Kaplan et al. (1998), resolves this tension by including part of the range effects through linear divergence subtraction, enabling a systematic and useful EFT for NN scattering. The work enables future applications of EFT to nuclear systems with shallow bound states while raising open questions about the relation of PDS coefficients to underlying NN potentials and the preservation of gauge and chiral symmetries.
