# Probing the QCD Critical Point with Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We utilize an event-by-event relativistic hydrodynamic calculation performed at a number of different incident beam energies to investigate the creation of hot and dense QCD matter near the critical point. Using state-of-the-art analysis and visualization tools we demonstrate that each collision event probes QCD matter characterized by a wide range of temperatures and baryo-chemical potentials, making a dynamical response of the system to the vicinity of the critical point very difficult to isolate above the background.

## Key claim

Event-by-event relativistic hydrodynamic simulations show that each heavy-ion collision probes a wide range of temperatures and baryo-chemical potentials simultaneously, making any dynamical response of the system to the QCD critical point very difficult to isolate above the background, even if the collision energy is chosen to place the system near the critical region.

## Method

The authors used a hybrid hydrodynamic model with fluctuating initial conditions generated by the Ultra-relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) approach, coupled to an equation of state that incorporates a critical end point (CEP) via a Polyakov-loop chiral hadronic SU(3)_f model. They simulated central Au+Au collisions at beam energies of 10, 25, and 40 GeV/u, and projected the time evolution of the QCD matter into the temperature versus quark-chemical potential phase diagram using the MADAI Workbench visualization package.

## Key numbers

- At a beam energy of 25A GeV, initial temperatures in the central transverse slice range from 120 to 210 MeV.
- Each event contains QCD matter spread over at least 50 MeV in quark-chemical potential (mu_q) at any given time.
- The three beam energies studied (10, 25, and 40 GeV/u) are separated by a similar difference in mu_q as the spread within a single event.
- For the equation of state used, an incident beam energy of 25 GeV/u provides the best opportunity for probing matter in the vicinity of the CEP.

## Context

This work extends event-by-event hydrodynamic studies by Petersen (2008) and others to explicitly map the range of temperatures and chemical potentials present in a single collision, rather than assuming a single sharp trajectory in the phase diagram. The results highlight a fundamental challenge for experimental searches for the QCD critical point at RHIC and FAIR: the broad spread in thermodynamic conditions within each event may overwhelm any signal from matter passing through the critical region. Future work must account for this spread, the width of the critical region, and the influence of the hadronic phase on observables.
