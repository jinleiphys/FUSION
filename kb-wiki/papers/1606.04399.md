# Double-step truncation procedure for large-scale shell-model calculations

> We present a procedure that is helpful to reduce the computational complexity of large-scale shell-model calculations, by preserving as much as possible the role of the rejected degrees of freedom in an effective approach. Our truncation is driven first by the analysis of the effective single-particle energies of the original large-scale shell-model hamiltonian, so to locate the relevant degrees of freedom to describe a class of isotopes or isotones, namely the single-particle orbitals that will constitute a new truncated model space. The second step is to perform an unitary transformation of the original hamiltonian from its model space into the truncated one. This transformation generates a new shell-model hamiltonian, defined in a smaller model space, that retains effectively the role of the excluded single-particle orbitals. As an application of this procedure, we have chosen a realistic shell-model hamiltonian defined in a large model space, set up by seven and five proton and neutron single-particle orbitals outside 88Sr, respectively. We study the dependence of shell-model results upon different truncations of the original model space for the Zr, Mo, Ru, Pd, Cd, and Sn isotopic chains, showing the reliability of this truncation procedure.

## Key claim

The authors propose a double-step truncation procedure that first identifies relevant single-particle orbitals by analyzing effective single-particle energies (ESPE) of the original large-scale shell-model hamiltonian, then performs a unitary transformation to generate a new hamiltonian in a smaller model space. The reliability of this approach depends on whether the ESPE analysis reveals a clear energy separation between the retained and discarded orbitals; when such a separation exists, the truncated hamiltonian reproduces the original results, but when the separation is small, the truncation may fail to capture the full collectivity.

## Method

The authors start from a realistic shell-model hamiltonian derived from the CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential via the Vlow-k approach and many-body perturbation theory, defined in a large model space of seven proton and five neutron orbitals outside 88Sr (denoted [75]). They calculate the proton and neutron ESPE as functions of valence particle number to identify which orbitals are most relevant, then split the original model space into a P-space (retained orbitals) and Q-space (discarded orbitals). A unitary transformation of the original hamiltonian is performed by solving the eigenvalue problem for two-valence-nucleon systems (90Zr, 90Sr, 90Y) and constructing effective hamiltonians Heff45 and Heff42 in the truncated model spaces [45] (four proton, five neutron orbitals) and [42] (four proton, two neutron orbitals), retaining only two-body matrix elements.

## Key numbers

- The original model space [75] contains seven proton orbitals (1p1/2, 0g9/2, 0g7/2, 1d5/2, 1d3/2, 2s1/2, 0h11/2) and five neutron orbitals (0g7/2, 1d5/2, 1d3/2, 2s1/2, 0h11/2) outside 88Sr.
- The neutron ESPE gap at Z=50 between the (1d5/2, 0g7/2) subspace and the (2s1/2, 1d3/2, 0h11/2) orbitals is approximately 2.4 MeV, attributed to the tensor component of the proton-neutron interaction.
- For 96Mo, the R4/2 ratio (excitation energy of 4+ versus 2+ states) equals 2.0 with H75 and Heff45, but drops to 1.6 when evaluated with Heff42.
- For Sn isotopes, the complete filling of the proton 0g9/2 orbital induces an energy gap of approximately 3 MeV at Z=50 between the (1d5/2, 0g7/2) subspace and the (2s1/2, 1d3/2, 0h11/2) orbitals.
- For 104Sn calculations with Heff45, the E2+1 excitation energy varies less than 1% and the B(E2) varies less than 2% when the truncation parameter t is increased from 5 to 6.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier application of the truncation procedure to tin isotopes (Coraggio 2015) and provides a systematic test across multiple isotopic chains (Zr, Mo, Ru, Pd, Cd, Sn). The method addresses computational limitations encountered in large-scale shell-model calculations such as those by Banu (2005) for light tin isotopes and Crawford (2013) for chromium and iron isotopic chains, where exact diagonalization was not feasible and ad-hoc truncations were employed. The approach enables shell-model studies of heavy-mass nuclei where model spaces become too large for direct diagonalization, and the authors plan to apply it to regions around doubly-magic 132Sn and 208Pb.
