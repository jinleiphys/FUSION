# Effect of running coupling on photons from jet - plasma interaction in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> We discuss the role of collisional energy loss on high $p_T$ photon data measured by PHENIX collaboration by calculating photon yield in jet-plasma interaction. The phase space distribution of the participating jet is dynamically evolved by solving Fokker-Planck equation. We treat the strong coupling constant ($α_s$) as function of momentum and temperature while calculating the drag and diffusion coefficients. It is observed that the quenching factor is substantially modified as compared to the case when $α_s$ is taken as constant. It is shown that the data is reasonably well reproduced when contributions from all the relevant sources are taken into account. Predictions at higher beam energies relevant for LHC experiment have been made.

# Effect of running coupling on photons from jet-plasma interaction in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The PHENIX high-$p_T$ photon data can be reasonably well reproduced when the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$ is treated as a function of both momentum and temperature (running coupling) in the collisional energy loss of jets, but the data are only reproduced in the low $p_T$ domain (below about 8 GeV) and the agreement depends on the choice of initial conditions such as temperature and formation time.

## Method

The authors calculate the photon yield from jet-plasma interaction by solving the Fokker-Planck equation to dynamically evolve the phase-space distribution of the jet parton, using drag and diffusion coefficients computed with a running strong coupling $\alpha_s(k,T)$ parameterized to respect both perturbative UV behavior and the 3D infrared fixed point. The collisional energy loss is computed from the drag coefficient, and an effective radiative energy loss is added separately; the space-time integrated photon yield is then obtained by integrating over a longitudinally expanding (Bjorken) plasma with a temperature profile, and the total photon spectrum includes contributions from hard scattering, bremsstrahlung, and thermal Compton/annihilation processes.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC energy with initial temperature $T_i = 0.446$ GeV and formation time $\tau_i = 0.147$ fm/c, the jet-photon yield with running coupling and collisional energy loss alone is depleted by a factor of **1.7 to 2** compared to the case with constant $\alpha_s$.
- At LHC energy ($T_i = 0.897$ GeV, $\tau_i = 0.073$ fm/c), the jet-photon yield with running coupling is suppressed by a factor of **3 to 4** compared to the constant-coupling case.
- For RHIC, the difference between the running-coupling and constant-coupling results for the photon $p_T$ spectra is a factor of **2 to 2.5** more depletion with running coupling.
- At LHC, the assumption made in earlier work (that $p_\gamma \sim p_q$) leads to a difference of a factor of **2 to 3** compared to the full calculation.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of jet-photon production by DKS (2000) by relaxing the assumption that the annihilating quark directly converts into a photon and by including collisional energy loss with a running coupling, following the formalism of Braun et al. (2007) who showed that collisional energy loss increases substantially when $\alpha_s$ is treated as a function of both temperature and momentum. The results are compared to PHENIX photon data and provide predictions for LHC energies; the authors note that the relative importance of collisional versus radiative energy loss in the context of RHIC data remains unsettled, with some groups (Gossiaux, 2008) finding collisional loss six times larger than constant-coupling estimates while others (2008) claim collisional loss is sub-leading.
