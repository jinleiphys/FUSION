# A Pedestrian Introduction to Gamow Vectors

> The Gamow vector description of resonances is compared with the S-matrix and the Green function descriptions using the example of the square barrier potential. By imposing different boundary conditions on the time independent Schrodinger equation, we obtain either eigenvectors corresponding to real eigenvalues and the physical spectrum or eigenvectors corresponding to complex eigenvalues (Gamow vectors) and the resonance spectrum. We show that the poles of the S matrix are the same as the poles of the Green function and are the complex eigenvalues of the Schrodinger equation subject to a purely outgoing boundary condition. The intrinsic time asymmetry of the purely outgoing boundary condition is discussed. Finally, we show that the probability of detecting the decay within a shell around the origin of the decaying state follows an exponential law if the Gamow vector (resonance) contribution to this probability is the only contribution that is taken into account.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Gamow vector description of resonances is equivalent to the S-matrix and Green function descriptions for the square barrier potential, but with the crucial qualification that this equivalence holds only when the purely outgoing boundary condition is imposed on the time-independent Schrödinger equation, which introduces an intrinsic time asymmetry.

## Method

The authors solve the three-dimensional radial Schrödinger equation for a square barrier potential of height V₀ with inner radius a and outer radius b, restricting to zero orbital angular momentum (l=0). They compare three approaches: (1) Dirac kets obtained with boundedness boundary conditions yielding real eigenvalues on [0,∞); (2) Gamow vectors obtained by imposing purely outgoing boundary conditions at infinity, yielding complex eigenvalues; and (3) the Green function constructed from solutions that vanish at the origin and satisfy outgoing boundary conditions. The poles of the S-matrix are computed from the ratio of outgoing to incoming wave amplitudes in the exterior region.

## Key numbers

- The square barrier potential has height V₀ > 0, inner radius a, and outer radius b, with V(r)=0 for 0<r<a, V(r)=V₀ for a<r<b, and V(r)=0 for b<r<∞.
- The wave number is k = √(2mE/ħ²) for energy E, and Q = √(k² - 2mV₀/ħ²) inside the barrier region.
- The S-matrix for l=0 is S(E) = -F₁(k)/F₂(k), where F₁ and F₂ are the outgoing and incoming wave amplitudes in the exterior region.
- The complex resonance eigenvalues come in pairs E_R ± iΓ_R/2, where Γ_R is the width and the lifetime τ = 1/Γ_R.
- The Wronskian of the regular solution χ and the outgoing solution ψ is W(χ,ψ)(k) = 2ikF₂(k).

## Context

This pedagogical paper extends the work of Gamow (1928) on α decay by providing a rigorous comparison of the Gamow vector, S-matrix, and Green function descriptions of resonances, using the square barrier potential as a concrete example. It builds on the Rigged Hilbert Space formalism developed by Bohm, Gadella, and others to give mathematical rigor to Gamow's heuristic treatment. The paper enables graduate students to understand how the purely outgoing boundary condition connects the complex eigenvalues of the Schrödinger equation to the poles of the S-matrix and the Green function, and how this boundary condition introduces time asymmetry through semigroup time evolution.
