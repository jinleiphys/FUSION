# Neutron stars and the dense matter equation of state: from microscopic theory to macroscopic observations

> The past years have witnessed tremendous progress in understanding the properties of neutron stars and of the dense matter in their cores, made possible by electromagnetic observations of neutron stars and the detection of gravitational waves from their mergers. These observations provided novel constraints on neutron-star structure, that is intimately related to the properties of dense neutron-rich matter described by the nuclear equation of state. Nevertheless, constraining the equation of state over the wide range of densities probed by astrophysical observations is still challenging, as the physics involved is very broad and the system spans many orders of magnitude in densities. Here, we review theoretical approaches to calculate and model the neutron-star equation of state in various regimes of densities, and discuss the related consequent properties of neutron stars. We describe how the equation of state can be calculated from nuclear interactions that are constrained and benchmarked by nuclear experiments. We review neutron-star observations, with particular emphasis on information provided by gravitational-wave signals and electromagnetic observations. Finally, we discuss future challenges and opportunities in the field.

## Key claim

This review paper consolidates the current understanding of neutron star structure and the dense matter equation of state, but emphasizes that constraining the equation of state over the wide range of densities probed by astrophysical observations remains challenging because the physics involved spans many orders of magnitude in density and cannot be fully realized in terrestrial experiments.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to calculate the neutron-star equation of state across different density regimes, from low-density neutron matter described by universal properties of ultracold Fermi gases, through the crustal regime with nuclear pasta phases, to the high-density core where hyperons or quark matter may appear. It describes how the equation of state can be calculated from microscopic nuclear interactions (including two- and three-body forces) that are constrained by nuclear experiments and scattering data, and reviews how electromagnetic observations and gravitational-wave signals from neutron star mergers provide observational constraints.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n0 ≈ 0.16 fm^-3, corresponding to a mass density of ≈ 2.7 × 10^14 g/cm^3
- Binding energy B of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation: B ≈ 16 MeV
- Nuclear incompressibility K = 230 ± 20 MeV (from recent models)
- Neutron-neutron scattering length a ≈ -18.5 fm and effective range re ≈ 2.7 fm
- Densities in neutron star centers can reach 6 to 8 times nuclear saturation density
- Neutron star typical radii around 10 km with masses up to 2 times the solar mass
- Inner crust extends up to mass density ρ ∼ 60 MeV/fm^3 (10^14 g/cm^3), about half nuclear saturation density
- Neutron drip occurs at mass density ρ ∼ 0.2 MeV/fm^3 (4 × 10^11 g/cm^3) in isolated neutron stars
- Outer crust begins at mass density near ρ ∼ 6 × 10^-7 MeV/fm^3 (10^6 g/cm^3)
- Superfluid pairing gap in neutron matter is of order 1 MeV
- First direct measurement of a pulsar exceeding 2 solar masses was reported in 2010 by Demorest et al.

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on neutron star structure and the nuclear equation of state, building on foundational calculations by Wiringa (1988) and Akmal (1998) that predicted two-solar-mass neutron stars before their observational discovery. It connects microscopic nuclear theory approaches (including chiral effective field theory and Quantum Monte Carlo methods) with observational constraints from radio pulsar timing, X-ray observations, and gravitational-wave detections of binary neutron star mergers (first detected by Abbott et al. in 2017). The paper enables future work by identifying open questions about the composition of neutron star cores, the nature of phase transitions at high density, and the need for improved constraints from both nuclear experiments and multi-messenger astrophysics.
