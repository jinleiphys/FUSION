# On the possible space-time fractality of the emitting source

> Using simple space-time implementation of the random cascade model we investigate numerically a conjecture made some time ago which was joining the intermittent behaviour of spectra of emitted particles with the possible fractal structure of the emitting source. We demonstrate that such details are seen, as expected, in the Bose-Einstein correlations between identical particles. \\

# On the possible space-time fractality of the emitting source

## Key claim

Using a simple space-time implementation of a random cascade model, the authors demonstrate numerically that a fractal (scale-invariant) structure of the emitting source, if present, would leave detectable signatures in Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) between identical particles; however, the effect is less pronounced than earlier conjectures suggested, and the power-law scaling of the source density is only approximately realized for long cascades (large source mass) and/or fast evolution (small lifetime parameter).

## Method

The authors model the emitting source as a one-dimensional or three-dimensional random cascade process in which an initial mass M decays sequentially into two daughter masses, with a fraction of the parent mass converted to kinetic energy at each vertex. Each vertex is assigned a finite lifetime drawn from an exponential distribution with parameter τ, and the space-time coordinates of each final particle are recorded. Bose-Einstein correlations are then imposed as an "afterburner" by weighting same-sign particle pairs with the factor 1 + cos[(r_i − r_j)·(p_i − p_j)], using the generated positions and momenta.

## Key numbers

- For one-dimensional cascades with constant τ = 0.2 fm, the density ρ(r) scales as ρ(r) ~ (1/r)^L with L = 1.89 (M = 100 GeV); for three-dimensional cascades with τ = 0.2/M, L = 2.8 (M = 100 GeV).
- Multiplicity distributions are shown for source masses M = 10, 40, and 100 GeV (with μ = 0.3 GeV for D=1): symmetric cascades yield mean multiplicities ⟨N⟩ = 7.39, 14.67, and 22.76, with dispersions σ = 2.37, 5.12, and 8.47, respectively.
- Asymmetric cascades yield ⟨N⟩ = 3.97, 4.88, and 5.48, with σ = 1.07, 1.27, and 1.41, for the same three masses.
- The scaled factorial moments F₂ and F₃ are computed for a one-dimensional cascade with M = 40 GeV and μ = 0.3 GeV as a function of number of bins n_bin.
- The intercept C₂(Q=0) is noticeably lower for three-dimensional cascades than for one-dimensional cascades, and the shape of C₂(Q) is more exponential-like for D=1 and more Gaussian-like for D=3.

## Context

This work extends earlier conjectures by Bialas (1990s) and by Peschanski and collaborators linking intermittent behavior of particle spectra to fractal structure of the emitting source, and specifically to the idea that the source size should fluctuate in a scale-invariant way. The authors implement numerically the space-time aspects that were not addressed in the earlier phase-space-only cascade model of Sarcevic and collaborators (1990s). The results enable future event-by-event analyses of BEC data with preselection on energy, centrality, or multiplicity to search for fractal signatures, though the authors caution that distinguishing fractal from conventional Gaussian or exponential source shapes via BEC alone will be difficult.
