# The $η{-}^3$He scattering length revisited

> The possible existence of $η$-mesic nuclei poses an interesting and still open issue of research. Since the occurence of such $η$-nucleus bound states is reflected in the corresponding $η$-nucleus scattering length, we critically review the present knowledge for the $η$$^3$He system. Specifically, we scrutinize the available experimental information for the reaction $p{+}d \to η{+} ^3{\rm He}$ which is commonly used to extract the $η$$^3$He scattering length. We point out several striking discrepancies between the various measurements. Subject to those inconsistencies we deduce a value of $a{=}|4.3{\pm}0.3|{+}i(0.5{\pm}0.5)$ fm.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the existing experimental data for the reaction \(p+d \to \eta + {}^3\mathrm{He}\) are internally inconsistent, and that the commonly quoted \(\eta{-}^3\mathrm{He}\) scattering length of \(a = |4.3 \pm 0.3| + i(0.5 \pm 0.5)\) fm is subject to those discrepancies, meaning the value is not a robust extraction but rather a conditional estimate that depends on which subset of data is used.

## Method

The authors critically reviewed all available experimental data for the reaction \(p+d \to {}^3\mathrm{He}+\eta\) near threshold, focusing on the final-state interaction (FSI) in the \(\eta{-}^3\mathrm{He}\) system. They applied the Watson-Migdal approximation to extract the complex \(\eta{-}^3\mathrm{He}\) scattering length from the energy dependence of the spin- and angle-averaged squared reaction amplitude, fitting the data with a model that assumes \(s\)-wave dominance and neglects the effective range term. They performed separate fits to the data of Mayer et al. (1996) alone and to the combined data of Mayer et al. and Berger et al. (1988), and compared the resulting confidence contours with predictions from several microscopic model calculations.

## Key numbers

* The combined fit to the low-energy data of Berger et al. (1988) and Mayer et al. (1996) yields a scattering length of \(a = |4.3 \pm 0.3| + i(0.5 \pm 0.5)\) fm, with a total \(\chi^2\) of 57.
* The fit to the data of Mayer et al. alone gives \(a = |3.8 \pm 0.6| + i(1.6 \pm 1.1)\) fm, with a total \(\chi^2\) of only 0.16.
* The analysis of Berger et al. using their own data and a different fitting function gave \(|a| = 3.4 \pm 0.1\) fm.
* The data used for the low-energy fits cover final momenta \(q \le 70\) MeV/c, corresponding to excess energies \(\varepsilon \le 6.11\) MeV.
* The imaginary part of the scattering length extracted from the combined fit is \(\Im a = 0.5 \pm 0.5\) fm; a lower bound from the optical theorem using the \(\pi^- p \to \eta n\) reaction gives \(\Im a_{\eta N} \ge 0.28\) fm, which would imply \(\Im a \ge 0.84\) fm for the \(\eta{-}^3\mathrm{He}\) system if scaled by a factor of 3.

## Context

This work extends the line of inquiry into the existence of \(\eta\)-mesic nuclei, which has been debated since the 1980s. It directly contradicts the earlier extraction by Wilkin (1993) who analyzed preliminary Saclay data and obtained a correlation between the real and imaginary parts of the scattering length without including the data of Berger et al. (1988). The paper also compares its results with microscopic model calculations by Wycech (1993), Rakityansky (1995), Fix (2001), and Green (2002), finding that only the most recent calculation by Green lies within the \(\chi^2+1\) confidence contour of the combined fit. The analysis enables future experiments at COSY and CELSIUS to resolve the data inconsistencies by providing specific recommendations for new measurements at final momenta \(q \le 70\) MeV/c and around \(q = 70\) MeV/c.
