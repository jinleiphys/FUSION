# Transport coefficients of a hot QCD medium and their relative significance in heavy-ion collisions

> The main focus of this article is to obtain various transport coefficients for a hot QCD medium that is produced while colliding two heavy nuclei ultra-relativistically. As the hot QCD medium follows dissipative hydrodynamics while undergoing space-time evolution, the knowledge of the transport coefficients such as thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, shear and bulk viscosities are essential to understand the underlying physics there. The approach adopted here is semi-classical transport theory. The determination of all these transport coefficients requires knowledge of the medium away from equilibrium. In this context, we setup the linearized transport equation employing the Chapman-Enskog technique from kinetic theory of many particle system with a collision term that includes the binary collisions of quarks/antiquarks and gluons. In order to include the effects of a strongly interacting, thermal medium, a quasi-particle description of realistic hot QCD equation of state has been employed through the equilibrium modeling of the momentum distributions of gluons and quarks with non-trivial dispersion relations while extending the model for finite but small quark chemical potential. The effective coupling for strong interaction has been redefined following the charge renormalization under the scheme of the quasiparticle model. The consolidated effects on transport coefficients are seen to have significant impact on their temperature dependence. The relative significances of momentum and heat transfer as well as charge diffusion processes in hot QCD have been investigated by studying the ratios of the respective transport coefficients.

## Key claim

The paper computes shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, thermal conductivity, and electrical conductivity for a hot QCD medium using semi-classical transport theory with a Chapman-Enskog approach, but the results depend on the specific choice of equation of state (EOS1, EOS2, or lattice EOS) and on the effective quasiparticle model employed, meaning the temperature dependence of the transport coefficients is not universal but rather modulated by the chosen EOS and the finite but small quark chemical potential (up to 0.1 GeV).

## Method

The authors employed the Chapman-Enskog method from kinetic theory to linearize the relativistic transport equation for a multicomponent system of quarks, antiquarks, and gluons, with a collision term including binary elastic scatterings. The equilibrium momentum distributions of the partons were modeled using the effective quasiparticle model (EQPM) of Chandra and Ravishankar, which maps hot QCD medium effects (from perturbative QCD at O(g^5) and O(g^6 ln(1/g)+δ), and from (2+1)-flavor lattice QCD) into temperature-dependent effective fugacities z_g and z_q, leading to non-trivial dispersion relations. The effective strong coupling was redefined via charge renormalization by equating the Debye mass computed from the quasiparticle distributions to the leading-order hard-thermal-loop expression, and thermal relaxation times for quarks and gluons were obtained from the interaction rates using QCD 2→2 scattering amplitudes with a transport weight factor (1−cosθ) to handle small-angle divergences.

## Key numbers

- The QCD transition temperature T_c was taken as 170 MeV throughout the analysis.
- The finite quark chemical potential μ_q was chosen as 0.0 GeV and 0.1 GeV for the calculations.
- The Debye mass squared m_D^2 is given by an expression involving PolyLog[2,z_g] and PolyLog[2,-z_q] with coefficients 2N_c/π^2 and 2N_f/π^2 respectively, plus a μ_q^2 term (N_f/π^2)(z_q/(1+z_q)), reducing to the HTL result m_D^2(HTL)=4πα_s T^2{(N_c/3+N_f/6)+μ̃_q^2 N_f/(2π^2)} in the ideal gas limit z_g,q→1.
- The effective coupling α_eff(T,μ_q) is defined as α_s(T,μ_q) multiplied by a ratio of the Debye mass expressions, with the numerator containing PolyLog[2,z_g] and PolyLog[2,-z_q] terms and the denominator containing (N_c/3+N_f/6)+μ̃_q^2 N_f/(2π^2).

## Context

This work extends prior quasiparticle-based estimates of shear and bulk viscosities for QGP (Chandra and Ravishankar, 2010-2012; Plumari, 2011; Bluhm, 2011) by simultaneously computing thermal conductivity and electrical conductivity within the same framework, and by studying their ratios (Wiedemann-Franz law, Prandtl number, and the ratio η/s over σ/T). It enables comparison with holographic predictions from AdS/CFT (Son, 2007; Braby, 2010) and with lattice QCD results for electrical conductivity (Amato, 2013; Aarts, 2015; Brandt, 2013), and provides input for dissipative hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies.
