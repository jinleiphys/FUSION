# Testing Lorentz invariance in orbital electron capture

> Searches for Lorentz violation were recently extended to the weak sector, in particular neutron and nuclear $β$ decay [1]. From experiments on forbidden $β$-decay transitions strong limits in the range of $10^{-6}$-$10^{-8}$ were obtained on Lorentz-violating components of the $W$-boson propagator [2]. In order to improve on these limits strong sources have to be considered. In this Brief Report we study isotopes that undergo orbital electron capture and allow experiments at high decay rates and low dose. We derive the expressions for the Lorentz-violating differential decay rate and discuss the options for competitive experiments and their required precision.

## Key claim

Orbital electron capture can provide competitive tests of Lorentz violation in the weak sector using high-intensity sources that deliver high decay rates at low radiation dose, potentially improving on existing limits of \(10^{-6}\)–\(10^{-8}\) on Lorentz-violating components of the \(W\)-boson propagator. The authors derive the Lorentz-violating differential decay rate for allowed \(K\)-orbital electron capture and identify that one set of parameters (\(\chi_i^{s0}\)) remains completely unconstrained by any previous experiment.

## Method

The authors derived the theoretical expression for the allowed \(K\)-orbital electron capture decay rate including Lorentz violation by adding a complex tensor \(\chi^{\mu\nu}\) to the Minkowski metric in the \(W\)-boson propagator, following the formalism of Noordmans et al. (2013). They expressed the Lorentz-violating contributions in terms of asymmetries measurable from polarized sources and recoil momentum detection, then surveyed candidate isotopes that decay exclusively by electron capture to identify those suitable for high-statistics experiments.

## Key numbers

- Existing bounds on Lorentz-violating components \(\chi^{\mu\nu}\) from forbidden \(\beta\)-decay experiments range from \(10^{-6}\) to \(10^{-8}\) on different linear combinations (Noordmans et al., 2013).
- To reach \(10^{-9}\) statistical accuracy, a source strength of order 1 Curie (1 Curie-year \(\simeq 10^{18}\) disintegrations) is required.
- \(^{37}\)Ar has a half-life of \(3.0 \times 10^6\) s and a \(Q\)-value of 814 keV; a 35 mCi source was previously produced from 0.4 g of CaCO\(_3\).
- \(^{131}\)Cs has a half-life of \(8.4 \times 10^5\) s and a \(Q\)-value of 355 keV; proton activation yields \(>10^7\) Bq/\(\mu\)Ah.
- \(^{163}\)Ho has a half-life of \(1.4 \times 10^{11}\) s and a \(Q\)-value of 2.6 keV; maximal production rate is projected at about \(10^4\) Bq/h, insufficient for competitive Lorentz-violation tests.
- \(^{165}\)Er has a half-life of \(3.7 \times 10^4\) s and a \(Q\)-value of 376 keV; proton activation yields \(10^8\) Bq/\(\mu\)Ah.
- A nuclear polarization of 56% was achieved for \(^{37}\)Ar via spin exchange with optically-pumped K atoms (Pitt, PhD thesis).

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz-violation search framework developed by Noordmans et al. (2013, 2013b) for neutron and nuclear \(\beta\) decay into the domain of orbital electron capture, where existing bounds from forbidden \(\beta\) decay (Newman et al., 1976; Ullman et al., 1978) are already at the \(10^{-6}\)–\(10^{-8}\) level. The paper identifies \(^{37}\)Ar and \(^{131}\)Cs as the only viable isotopes for competitive measurements and proposes that an experiment measuring the recoil from a polarized nucleus could immediately produce new limits on the unconstrained \(\chi_i^{s0}\) parameters, while also testing the feasibility of a high-statistics program aiming for \(10^{-9}\) precision.
