# Access to the kaon radius with kaonic atoms

> We put forward a method for determination of the kaon radius from the spectra of kaonic atoms. We analyze the few lowest transitions and their sensitivity to the size of the kaon for ions in the nuclear charge range Z = 1 - 100, taking into account finite-nuclear-size, finite-kaon-size, recoil and leading-order quantum-electrodynamic effects. Additionally, the opportunities of extracting the kaon mass and nuclear radii are demonstrated by examining the sensitivity of the transition energies in kaonic atoms.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that kaonic atom spectroscopy can determine the kaon radius, but the method's feasibility depends on accounting for strong interaction shifts, which can reduce transition energies by tens of MeV and must be included in future high-precision work.

## Method
The authors solved the Klein-Gordon equation for kaonic atoms with nuclear charges Z=1-100, incorporating finite-nuclear-size effects via a homogeneously charged sphere model and finite-kaon-size effects via a two-sphere potential. They included leading-order quantum-electrodynamic corrections through the Uehling potential and accounted for recoil using reduced-mass formalism. Sensitivity coefficients for the nuclear radius and kaon mass were computed by varying these parameters in the calculations.

## Key numbers
- For kaonic helium He², the 3d→2p transition energy is 6.465 keV, in agreement with experimental and theoretical values from Bazzi (2009).
- The finite-kaon-size effect reaches a maximum of 0.8% for the 2p→1s transition at Z≈22, 0.57% for 3d→2p at Z≈54, and 0.46% for 4f→3d at Z≈92.
- For kaonic lead Pb⁸², the 1s binding energy is -17 MeV without strong interaction, reduced to -7 MeV with hadronic contribution.
- The kaon radius used in the two-sphere model is 0.40 fm.

## Context
This work extends the approach used for muonic atoms, which proved highly sensitive to nuclear parameters (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2020), to the heavier kaon system. It builds on prior kaonic atom studies by the DAΦNE collaboration (Bazzi 2009, 2011, 2012) and earlier QED predictions (Santos 2005). The method enables extraction of the kaon radius, which was last measured by direct scattering on electrons (Amendolia 1986), and could improve the kaon mass determination last updated by Gall (1988).
