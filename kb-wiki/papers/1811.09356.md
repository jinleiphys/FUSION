# Model Dependence of the Pion Form Factor Extracted from Pion Electro-production

> In 2008 the Jefferson Laboratory $F_π$ Collaboration released results for the pion electromagnetic form factor, which they extracted from pion electro-production data. The measured values for the pion form factor are model dependent, and require the use of the Vanderhaeghen, Guidal and Laget Regge Model for their extraction. While agreement between this model and data is impressive, the theoretical implementation of gauge invariance is less satisfying. We would like to establish how well the extracted form factor corresponds to the true form factor. To do this, we use a simple toy model, which imposes gauge invariance in a more theoretically satisfying way. The model form factor is extracted from our model cross section using the method employed by the $F_π$ Collaboration to extract the experimental pion form factor. We conclude that the reconstructed model form factor is a reasonable representation of the true model form factor for the kinematics chosen, although we note that the extracted form factor is smaller than the true form factor. This suggests that current extracted values of pion form factor may be overestimated.

## Key claim

The authors use a toy model with a more theoretically satisfying implementation of gauge invariance to test the extraction procedure used by the Jefferson Laboratory $F_\pi$ Collaboration, finding that the reconstructed model form factor is a reasonable representation of the true form factor for the chosen kinematics, but the extracted form factor is smaller than the true form factor, suggesting that current experimentally extracted values of the pion form factor may be overestimated.

## Method

The authors construct a simple toy model of pion electro-production that imposes gauge invariance in a more theoretically satisfying way than the Vanderhaeghen, Guidal and Laget (VGL) Regge Model used by the $F_\pi$ Collaboration. They calculate the exact model form factor and cross section from this toy model, then apply the same extraction method used by the $F_\pi$ Collaboration, fitting a monopole form factor parameterization to the longitudinal differential cross section, to reconstruct the form factor from the model cross section. By comparing the reconstructed form factor to the true model form factor, they assess the systematic uncertainty introduced by the VGL Model's approach to gauge invariance.

## Key numbers

- The extracted model form factor is smaller than the true model form factor for the kinematics chosen, with the authors concluding the reconstructed form factor is a "reasonable representation" but noting the systematic offset.
- The monopole form factor parameterization used in the extraction is $F_\pi(Q^2) = (1 + Q^2/\Lambda_\pi^2)^{-1}$, where $\Lambda_\pi^2$ is the single free parameter fit to the longitudinal cross section data.
- For reference, the $F_\pi$ Collaboration's best fit value for one set of kinematics was $\Lambda_\pi^2 = 0.458 \pm 0.031_{-0.068}^{+0.255}$ GeV$^2$ (Huber, 2008).
- The asymptotic limit of the pion form factor from perturbative QCD is $Q^2 F_\pi(Q^2) \to 16\pi f_\pi^2 \alpha_s(Q^2)$, where $f_\pi \approx 0.132$ GeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the $F_\pi$ Collaboration's extraction of the pion electromagnetic form factor from electro-production data (Huber, 2008), which used the VGL Regge Model (Vanderhaeghen, Guidal and Laget, 1997). The authors address concerns about the VGL Model's implementation of gauge invariance, where the pion form factor multiplies the entire Reggeized amplitude rather than appearing naturally in the $t$-channel vertex. The study is motivated by earlier work using a Bethe-Salpeter approach (Qin, 2017) which found that the off-shell pion form factor increases in magnitude as the pion deviates further from its mass shell. The results suggest that future extractions should account for this model dependence, and the toy model approach provides a framework for quantifying systematic uncertainties in the extracted pion form factor.
