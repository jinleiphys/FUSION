# Spin-dependent observables in surrogate reactions

> Observables emitted from various spin states in compound U nuclei are investigated to validate usefulness of the surrogate reaction method. It was found that energy spectrum of cascading $γ$-rays and their multiplicities, spectrum of evaporated neutrons, and mass-distribution of fission fragments show clear dependence on the spin of decaying nuclei. The present results indicate that they can be used to infer populated spin distributions which significantly affect the decay branching ratio of the compound system produced by the surrogate reactions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that energy spectra of evaporated neutrons, cascading gamma-rays, and fission fragment mass distributions show clear spin dependence for compound U nuclei, but the authors hedge that the spin-dependence of fission fragment mass distributions is "not a phenomena confirmed yet" and requires special caution.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach theory implemented in the CCONE code system to calculate neutron and gamma-ray spectra from compound nucleus 238U at excitation energy 10 MeV for spin-parity states Jπ = 0+, 5+, 10+, and 20+. They also performed 3-dimensional Langevin calculations on a potential energy surface from the two-center shell model to compute fission fragment mass distributions from 240U, incorporating a rotating liquid-drop model with spin-dependent rotational energy and temperature-dependent shell corrections.

## Key numbers

- Neutron spectra from 238U at 10 MeV excitation: maximum neutron energy decreases from about 4 MeV for low spin to slightly less than 3.8 MeV at J=10, and to 3 MeV at J=20
- Gamma-ray multiplicity from 238U: about 2 for J=0, increases to 5 for J=10, and exceeds 10 for J=20
- Shell damping energy Ed chosen as 20 MeV for the fission calculations
- Spin values investigated in steps of approximately 10 hbar, with specific calculations at J=0, 5, 10, and 20

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Chiba and Iwamoto (2010) who established conditions for the surrogate ratio method to work, including the requirement that spin values populated by surrogate reactions not exceed about 10 hbar. The results enable experimental design for forthcoming surrogate reaction measurements by identifying observables that can signal the spin distribution of compound nuclei, which is critical for correctly inferring neutron cross sections of unstable nuclei such as minor actinides and long-lived fission products.
