# Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian with octic potential applied to the $^{106-116}$Cd isotopes

> The Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian, with an octic potential in the $β$-deformation variable, is numerically solved for a $γ$-unstable symmetry of the nuclear system. The analytical structure of the model allows the description of multiple phenomena of great interest for the nuclear structure such as ground-state shape phase transitions and their critical points, dynamical shape phase transitions, shape coexistence with and without mixing, anomalous in-band $E2$ transitions, large $E2$ intra-band transitions and large monopole transition between the first excited $0^+$ state and the ground state, respectively. As a first application of the present model is selected the $^{106-116}$Cd isotope chain known in literature to manifest shape phase transition, respectively shape coexistence and mixing.

## Key claim

The Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian with an octic potential in the β deformation variable, solved numerically for γ-unstable symmetry, can describe ground-state shape phase transitions, shape coexistence with and without mixing, and anomalous electromagnetic transitions; the paper presents a first application to the 106-116Cd isotope chain, where the sextic potential could not fully reproduce the experimental data due to constraints imposed by quasi-exactly solvable methods.

## Method

The γ-unstable Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian was numerically diagonalized using a basis of Bessel functions of the first kind (solutions of the infinite square well potential) for an octic potential v₁(β) = a₁β² + a₂β⁴ + a₃β⁶ + a₄β⁸. The separation constant ω = τ(τ+3) was used, with ν = τ + 3/2, and the total energy included an L(L+1) term to break degeneracy. The method extends the diagonalization approach previously applied to sextic potentials, using matrix elements calculated from integrals over the scaled variable x = β/β_w.

## Key numbers

- For the 106-116Cd isotopes, the model parameters b₂, b₃, b₄, and Ṽ were determined (values given in Table 1 of the paper) to fit the 0_g⁺, 2_g⁺, 4_g⁺, 6_g⁺ ground band states, the first excited 0_β⁺ state, and the 2_γ⁺, 3_γ⁺, 4_γ⁺ states of the first γ-band.
- The critical point of a second-order shape phase transition was simulated with parameters a₁=1, a₂=-3, a₃=1, a₄=2 (curve III in Fig. 1).
- For the first-order shape phase transition scenario (Fig. 2), the parameters were b₂=11.5, b₃=-6.72, b₄=1, with Ṽ_w=2.2.
- For shape coexistence with mixing (Fig. 3), the parameters were b₂=14.5, b₃=-7.63, b₄=1, with Ṽ_w=2.4.
- For shape coexistence without mixing (Fig. 7), the parameters were b₂=30.35, b₃=-11.1, b₄=1, with Ṽ_w=2.7.

## Context

This work extends previous applications of the Bohr-Mottelson Hamiltonian with sextic potential (Budaca 2020, 2021, 2022) to higher-order polynomial potentials, enabling description of more complex nuclear spectra. The model was applied to the 106-116Cd isotope chain, which was previously studied with a quasi-exactly solvable sextic potential by Lahbas (year not given) that could not cover shape coexistence and mixing; microscopic studies of these isotopes include work by Kumar (year not given), Garrett (year not given), Nomura (year not given), and Sharma (year not given). The results enable future investigation of nuclei where the sextic potential fails to reproduce experimental data, particularly for shape coexistence phenomena.
