# Critical Velocity in 3He-B Vibrating Wire Experiments as Analog of Vacuum Instability in a Slowly Oscillating Electric Field

> The Lancaster experiments with a cylindrical wire moving in superfluid 3He-B are discussed, where the measured critical velocity of pair creation is much below the Landau critical velocity. The phenomenon is shown to be analogous to the instability of the electron-positron vacuum in an adiabatically alternating strong electric potential of both signs, where the positive- and negative-root levels cross and thus the instability treshold is twice less than in the conventional case of a single static potential well.

# Critical Velocity in 3He-B Vibrating Wire Experiments as Analog of Vacuum Instability in a Slowly Oscillating Electric Field

## Key claim

The measured critical velocity for pair creation by a vibrating wire in superfluid 3He-B (approximately 0.25 times the Landau critical velocity) can be explained by a mechanism analogous to electron-positron pair production in an adiabatically alternating electric potential of both signs, where the instability threshold is twice lower than in the conventional static potential well case. This mechanism requires the presence of bound states near the wire surface, mixing of opposite-momentum states, and slow oscillation of the wire velocity.

## Method

The authors analyze the Lancaster experiments where a cylindrical wire (radius 2 to 50 micrometers) vibrates in superfluid 3He-B at frequencies 100-1000 Hz, which is much slower than the characteristic quasiparticle energy scale. They model the fermionic spectrum using a Bogoliubov-Nambu Hamiltonian with Dirac-like structure, incorporating the local enhancement of superfluid velocity near the wire surface (a factor of 2 at the surface) and the anisotropic suppression of the gap near the surface. The critical velocity for pair creation is derived from the condition that bound state energies cross zero and connect to the continuum, with the surface roughness providing momentum mixing between +p_F and -p_F states.

## Key numbers

- The measured critical velocity in Lancaster experiments was approximately 0.25 times the Landau critical velocity v_L, independent of the wire material and radius
- The Landau critical velocity is v_L = Delta_0 / p_F, where Delta_0 is the bulk superfluid gap and p_F is the Fermi momentum
- The parallel "speed of light" at the wire surface is c_parallel(r=R) approximately 0.4 c at T=0 for diffusive boundary conditions (from KSS)
- The critical velocity for filling surface bound states is v_0^* = (1/2) c_parallel(r=R) = v_L * Delta_parallel(r=R) / (2 Delta_0)
- The critical velocity for quasiparticle emission via the Zeldovich mechanism is v_1^* = (c + c_parallel(r=R)) / 3, giving at most 0.47 v_L
- The experimental result v_0^* approximately 0.25 v_L implies Delta_parallel(r=R) approximately 0.5 Delta_0
- For the Dirac model with two delta-function potentials, the critical strength for level crossing is U_1 = pi/2, compared to U_2 = pi for the conventional static case

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Lambert (1990s), who first suggested that adiabatic oscillations could explain subcritical dissipation in the Lancaster experiments (CCGMP, CGPS). The authors incorporate the anisotropic gap suppression near the surface (from KSS) and the quantum mechanical treatment of bound states (from Privorotskii), obtaining a modified critical velocity that depends on the parallel gap suppression. The mechanism differs from the Zeldovich mechanism (Gershtein and Zeldovich, 1970s) for positron production in static potentials, requiring instead an oscillating potential that allows positive- and negative-root levels to cross and return. The analogy connects to the broader program of using superfluid 3He as a laboratory for quantum field theory phenomena, including the Schwinger effect and event horizon physics (Jacobson, 1990s).
