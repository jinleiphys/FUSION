# Nucleosynthesis in Massive Stars - Including All Stable Isotopes

> We present the first calculations to follow the evolution of all stable isotopes (and their abundant radioactive progenitors) in a finely zoned stellar model computed from the onset of central hydrogen burning through explosion as a Type II supernova. The calculations were performed for a 15 solar mass Pop I star using the most recently available set of experimental and theoretical nuclear data, revised opacity tables, and taking into account mass loss due to stellar winds. We find the approximately solar production of proton-rich isotopes above a mass number of A=120 due to the gamma-process. We also find a weak s-process, which along with the gamma-process and explosive helium and carbon burning, produces nearly solar abundances of almost all nuclei from A=60 to 85. A few modifications of the abundances of heavy nuclei above mass 90 by the s-process are also noted and discussed. New weak rates lead to significant alteration of the properties of the presupernova core.

## Key claim

This paper presents the first self-consistent stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis calculation that follows all stable isotopes (and their radioactive progenitors) from central hydrogen burning through Type II supernova explosion for a 15 solar mass star, finding approximately solar production of proton-rich isotopes above A=120 via the gamma-process and nearly solar abundances for nuclei from A=60 to 85 from the weak s-process combined with explosive burning. The authors note that the results are for a single stellar mass and that the average nucleosynthetic yield of massive stars requires combining populations of different stars, each with its own peculiar yields.

## Method

The authors used a modified version of the KEPLER stellar evolution code to evolve a 15 solar mass Population I star from hydrogen ignition through core collapse and supernova explosion, co-processing nuclear reaction networks of increasing size: 617 isotopes during hydrogen and helium burning, 1482 isotopes during carbon burning, and 2439 isotopes during the supernova explosion. The explosion was simulated using a piston that moved inward for 0.45 seconds to a radius of 500 km then outward to produce a total kinetic energy of 1.2e51 ergs, with updated opacity tables (OPAL95), new weak rates from Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (2000), new strong and electromagnetic reaction rates from Rauscher and Thielemann (2000), and mass loss from stellar winds.

## Key numbers

- Stellar model: 15.08 solar masses initial mass, 12.64 solar masses final mass at core collapse
- Helium core mass: 4.16 solar masses (compared to 4.36 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Iron core mass: 1.55 solar masses (compared to 1.32 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Central electron fraction (Ye) at core collapse: 0.436 (compared to 0.422 in Woosley and Weaver 1995), a 2-3% increase
- Density in the mass range 1.5-2.0 solar masses increased by 30-50% relative to Woosley and Weaver (1995) models
- Yield of I-56Ni: 9.08e-2 solar masses (compared to 1.15e-1 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Yield of I-44Ti: 1.75e-5 solar masses (compared to 5.68e-5 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Yield of I-26Al: 4.66e-5 solar masses (compared to 4.30e-5 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Yield of I-60Fe: 7.16e-5 solar masses (compared to 2.66e-5 solar masses in Woosley and Weaver 1995)
- Supernova explosion kinetic energy: 1.2e51 ergs
- Proton-rich isotopes above A=123 produced within a factor of two relative to I-16O production
- Proton-rich isotopes between A=100 and A=123 underproduced by a factor of 3 to 4 relative to I-16O
- 80% of all ejecta (including winds) did not experience the s-process

## Context

This work extends the nucleosynthesis calculations of Woosley and Weaver (1995) by using a much larger nuclear reaction network (2439 isotopes versus 200 isotopes) and updated input physics including new weak rates from Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (2000) and new reaction rates from Rauscher and Thielemann (2000). The calculations enable future studies of additional stellar masses and metallicities, as well as the inclusion of rotationally induced mixing processes, to build a complete picture of nucleosynthesis from massive stars and to understand which stellar populations produce the solar abundance pattern.
