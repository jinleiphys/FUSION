# Evolving images of the proton: Hadron physics over the past 40 years

> Once upon a time, the world was simple: the proton contained three quarks, two {\it ups} and a {\it down}. How these give the proton its mass and its spin seemed obvious. Over the past forty years the proton has become more complicated, and how even these most obvious of its properties is explained in a universe of quarks, antiquarks and gluons remains a challenge. That this should be so should come as no surprise. Quantum Chromodynamics, the theory of the strong interaction, is seemingly simple, and its consequences are straightforward in the domain of hard scattering where perturbation theory applies. However, the beauty of the hadron world is its diversity. The existence of hadrons, their properties, and their binding into nuclei do not appear in the Lagrangian of QCD. They all emerge as a result of its strong coupling. Strong coupling QCD creates complex phenomena, much richer than known 40 years ago: a richness that ensures colour confinement and accounts for more than 95\% of the mass of the visible Universe. How strong coupling QCD really works requires a synergy between experiment and theory. A very personal view of these fascinating developments in cold QCD is presented.

## Key claim

This paper presents a personal historical overview of how the understanding of the proton's structure has evolved over 40 years, arguing that while perturbative Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) successfully describes hard scattering processes, the strong-coupling regime responsible for confinement, mass generation, and the full spectrum of hadrons remains a challenge that requires a synergy between experiment and theory.

## Method

The author traces the development of hadron physics from the quark model and S-matrix bootstrap approaches of the 1960s, through the discovery of asymptotic freedom and the formulation of QCD as an SU(3) gauge theory, to modern applications of perturbative QCD in deep inelastic scattering and the challenges posed by non-perturbative phenomena. The narrative covers the evolution of parton distribution functions (PDFs), the running coupling constant, heavy quarkonium spectroscopy, chiral perturbation theory, and the role of strong final-state interactions in precision electroweak measurements.

## Key numbers

- The proton's valence quarks carry less than 50% of the proton's longitudinal momentum (in the infinite momentum frame), with the valence distribution peaking around x ~ 0.15
- The QCD scale parameter Lambda_QCD is 100-200 MeV, setting the momentum scale at which the strong interaction becomes strong
- The current masses of up and down quarks in the renormalised Lagrangian are only a few MeV, much smaller than Lambda_QCD
- The neutron is just 1.3 MeV heavier than the proton, enabling the decay n -> p e- anti-nu_e
- Constituent quarks have masses of about 300-350 MeV, roughly one third of the proton mass
- Strong coupling QCD accounts for more than 95% of the mass of the visible Universe

## Context

This review extends the narrative from the early quark model work of Gell-Mann (1964) and Zweig (1964), through the development of QCD by Fritzsch, Gell-Mann and Leutwyler (1973), and the discovery of asymptotic freedom by Politzer, Gross and Wilczek (1973). It connects the DGLAP evolution formalism (Dokshitzer, Gribov, Lipatov, Altarelli, Parisi) for structure functions to modern PDF analyses such as CJ15 (Accardi et al.) and HERAPDF 2.0, while highlighting open questions about colour-glass condensates at very small x that could be addressed by a future Electron-Ion Collider.
