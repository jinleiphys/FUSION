# Resonances and Off-Shell Characteristics of Effective Interactions

> The importance of including experimental resonances in constructing effective inter-cluster interactions has been investigated. For this, we first address the question of how to obtain the analytical properties of the Jost function in regions of physical interest on the complex k-plane when the potential is given in a tabular form. We then employ the Marchenko inverse scattering method to construct, numerically, phase equivalent local potentials supporting the same bound state(s) but having different resonance spectra which affect the off-shell characteristics of the corresponding scattering amplitudes. This implies that the inclusion of the experimental resonances in constructing a potential would change its shape, strength, and range which in turn would modify the bound and scattering wave functions in the interior region. This is expected to have important consequences in calculations of transition amplitudes in nuclear reaction theories, which strongly depend on the behaviour of the wave functions at short distances. The influence of Supersymmetric Transformations on the position and movement of resonances has also been investigated.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that including experimental resonances in constructing effective inter-cluster potentials changes the potential's shape, strength, and range, which in turn modifies bound and scattering wave functions in the interior region; this implies that even broad resonances which are "unnoticed" by phase shifts still affect the off-shell characteristics of the scattering amplitude and therefore cannot be ignored when constructing realistic effective interactions.

## Method

The authors employ the Marchenko inverse scattering method to numerically construct phase-equivalent local potentials that support the same bound state(s) but have different resonance spectra, using a rational parametrization of the S-matrix. They extend an existing Jost function method based on complex coordinate rotation to handle potentials given in tabular form by fitting the tabulated potentials with analytic forms (sums of Gaussians) that allow analytic continuation into the complex r-plane. They also apply Supersymmetric (SUSY) transformations to remove unphysical Pauli Forbidden States and study how these transformations affect resonance positions.

## Key numbers

- For the nucleon-alpha potential of Dubovichenko and Dzhazairov-Kakhramanov (ℓ=0, V₀=55.774 MeV, α=0.292 fm⁻²), the asymptotic normalization constant for the unphysical Pauli Forbidden State at -9.73058 MeV was found to be A_s=6.1603 fm⁻¹/²
- For the alpha-alpha potential of Buck et al. (ℓ=0, V₀=122.6225 MeV, α=0.22 fm⁻², β=0.75 fm⁻¹), the original potential sustains two unphysical deep bound states at -72.78 MeV and -22.28 MeV and a resonance at 179.22 keV with Γ=0.94905 keV
- After SUSY transformation removing one unphysical bound state from the Buck et al. potential, the S-wave Jost function zero was found at k=0.10383 - i0.77289×10⁻⁵ fm⁻¹, corresponding to E=0.11261 - (i/2)0.33532×10⁻⁴ MeV
- The fitting accuracy for the analytic continuation of tabulated potentials was typically of order 10⁻⁴ (least square error on 120 points), and with such fits all resonances from a test potential were recovered within three to five decimal points

## Context

This work extends the Marchenko inverse scattering method (Marchenko, Chadan, Newton) and the Jost function method for complex k-plane calculations (Rakityansky et al., 1996-1998) to potentials given in numerical/tabular form. It addresses the non-uniqueness problem in inverse scattering when bound states are present, where the asymptotic normalization constants (not available from experiment) determine the distribution of resonances. The results enable more reliable construction of effective inter-cluster potentials for use in few-body calculations and reaction theories (such as electro-disintegration and photo-disintegration) that depend on wave function behavior at short distances, and relate to the Resonating Group Method (RGM) and the work of Baye (1987) on SUSY transformations connecting deep and shallow potentials.
