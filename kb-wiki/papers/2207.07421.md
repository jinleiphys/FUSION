# Impact of systematic nuclear uncertainties on composition and decay heat of dynamical and disk ejecta in compact binary mergers

> Theoretically predicted yields of elements created by the rapid neutron capture (r-) process carry potentially large uncertainties associated with incomplete knowledge of nuclear properties and approximative hydrodynamical modelling of the matter ejection processes. We present an in-depth study of the nuclear uncertainties by varying theoretical nuclear input models that describe the experimentally unknown neutron-rich nuclei. This includes two frameworks for calculating the radiative neutron capture rates and 14 different models for nuclear masses, $β$-decay rates and fission properties. Our r-process nuclear network calculations are based on detailed hydrodynamical simulations of dynamically ejected material from NS-NS or NS-BH binary mergers plus the secular ejecta from BH-torus systems. The impact of nuclear uncertainties on the r-process abundance distribution and the early radioactive heating rate is found to be modest (within a factor of $\sim20$ for individual $A>90$ abundances and a factor of 2 for the heating rate). However, the impact on the late-time heating rate is more significant and depends strongly on the contribution from fission. We witness significantly larger sensitivity to the nuclear physics input if only a single trajectory is used compared to considering ensembles of $\sim$200-300 trajectories, and the quantitative effects of the nuclear uncertainties strongly depend on the adopted conditions for the individual trajectory. We use the predicted Th/U ratio to estimate the cosmochronometric age of six metal-poor stars and find the impact of the nuclear uncertainties to be up to 2 Gyr.

## Key claim

The paper finds that systematic nuclear uncertainties have a modest impact on r-process abundance distributions and early radioactive heating rates (within a factor of ~20 for individual A>90 abundances and a factor of 2 for the heating rate), but the impact on late-time heating rates is more significant and depends strongly on the contribution from fission. The authors emphasize that the quantitative effects of nuclear uncertainties strongly depend on whether single trajectories or ensembles of ~200-300 trajectories are used, with significantly larger sensitivity observed for single trajectories.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nuclear network calculations based on detailed hydrodynamical simulations of dynamically ejected material from NS-NS and NS-BH binary mergers plus secular ejecta from BH-torus systems. They varied theoretical nuclear input models including two frameworks for calculating radiative neutron capture rates and 14 different models for nuclear masses, beta-decay rates, and fission properties, using ensembles of 150-300 trajectories rather than single trajectories to capture the full range of astrophysical conditions.

## Key numbers

- Individual abundances for A>90 vary within a factor of ~20 due to nuclear uncertainties across the ensemble of trajectories
- Early radioactive heating rate varies by a factor of 2 due to nuclear uncertainties
- The impact of nuclear uncertainties on cosmochronometric age estimates using Th/U ratios for six metal-poor stars is up to 2 Gyr
- The dynamical ejecta masses for NS-NS models are 3.3 x 10^-3 Msun (SFHo-135-135) and 8.7 x 10^-3 Msun (SFHo-125-145), while the NS-BH model SFHo-11-23 ejects 40.4 x 10^-3 Msun
- BH-torus ejecta masses are 24.7 x 10^-3 Msun (M3A8m1a5) and 70.1 x 10^-3 Msun (M3A8m3a5-v2)
- The mean electron fraction ranges from 0.04 (SFHo-11-23) to 0.26 (SFHo-135-135) across the models
- The mean initial neutron mass fraction ranges from 0.54 (SFHo-135-135) to 0.85 (SFHo-11-23)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear uncertainties in r-process nucleosynthesis (Goriely 2001, Schatz 2002, Mumpower 2016, Nikas 2020) by using ensembles of trajectories from state-of-the-art hydrodynamical simulations rather than single trajectories, and by systematically varying complete nuclear models rather than using Monte Carlo approaches that assume uncorrelated rates. The results enable more reliable predictions for kilonova light curve modelling and cosmochronometric age dating of metal-poor stars, while highlighting that the impact of nuclear uncertainties on late-time heating rates depends critically on whether the r-process reaches the fissile region.
