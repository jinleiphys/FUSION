# The Status and Future of Color Transparency and Nuclear Filtering

> 40 years after its introduction, the phenomenon of color transparency remains a domain of controversial interpretations of experimental data. We review present evidence for or against its manifestation in various exclusive hard scattering reactions. The nuclear transparency experiments reveal whether short distance processes dominate a scattering amplitude at some given kinematical point. We plead for a new round of nuclear transparency measurements in a variety of experimental set-ups, including near-forward exclusive reactions related to generalized parton distribution (GPD) physics and near-backward exclusive reactions related to transition distribution amplitudes (TDA) physics.

## Key claim
After 40 years, color transparency remains a domain of controversial interpretations of experimental data; the authors argue that new measurements are needed to resolve whether short-distance processes dominate scattering amplitudes at given kinematic points, rather than claiming definitive evidence for or against the phenomenon.

## Method
The authors review existing nuclear transparency measurements from exclusive hard scattering reactions, analyzing how the observed transparency ratios compare with quantum chromodynamics (QCD) predictions for point-like configurations. They then propose specific experimental set-ups for future measurements, including near-forward exclusive reactions related to generalized parton distribution (GPD) physics and near-backward exclusive reactions related to transition distribution amplitude (TDA) physics.

## Context
This review extends the 40-year debate on color transparency, which began with the original predictions of point-like configurations in QCD. The authors argue that previous experiments, such as those at Jefferson Lab, have produced ambiguous results that require clarification. The paper enables future experimental programs by identifying specific exclusive channels (GPD and TDA reactions) where new nuclear transparency measurements could resolve long-standing controversies.
