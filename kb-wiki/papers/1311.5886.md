# Shining LUX on Isospin-Violating Dark Matter Beyond Leading Order

> Isospin-violating dark matter (IVDM) has been proposed as a viable scenario to reconcile conflicting positive and null results from direct detection dark matter experiments. We show that the lowest-order dark matter-nucleus scattering rate can receive large and nucleus-dependent corrections at next-to-leading order (NLO) in the chiral expansion. The size of these corrections depends on the specific couplings of dark matter to quark flavors and gluons. In general the full NLO dark-matter-nucleus cross-section is not adequately described by just the zero-energy proton and neutron couplings. These statements are concretely illustrated in a scenario where the dark matter couples to quarks through scalar operators. We find the canonical IVDM scenario can reconcile the null XENON and LUX results and the recent CDMS-Si findings provided its couplings to second and third generation quarks either lie on a special line or are suppressed. Equally good fits with new values of the neutron-to-proton coupling ratio are found in the presence of nonzero heavy quark couplings. CDMS-Si remains in tension with LUX and XENON10/100 but is not excluded.

## Key claim

The paper shows that next-to-leading order (NLO) chiral corrections can produce large, nucleus-dependent shifts in the dark matter-nucleus scattering rate for isospin-violating dark matter (IVDM), meaning the canonical IVDM scenario with a fixed neutron-to-proton coupling ratio of approximately -0.7 is not unique; new regions of compatibility between conflicting direct detection experiments arise for other values of this ratio when strange quark or gluonic couplings are nonzero.

## Method

The authors compute NLO chiral perturbation theory corrections to scalar-mediated WIMP-nucleus scattering, including two-body contributions and recoil-energy-dependent terms in the amplitude. They express the NLO differential rate in terms of the standard leading-order parameters (WIMP-proton cross section sigma_p and neutron-to-proton coupling ratio r) plus two additional parameters representing rescaled strange quark and gluonic (heavy quark) couplings. Using shell model calculations for the two-body nuclear matrix elements, they then perform a phenomenological analysis comparing the predicted rates for xenon, silicon, and germanium targets against data from CDMS-Si, XENON10/100, and LUX experiments.

## Key numbers

- The canonical IVDM point has neutron-to-proton coupling ratio r = -0.7 with zero strange and gluonic couplings (benchmark A).
- For a xenon target with zero strange and gluonic couplings, the NLO corrections are at the percent level; turning on strange or gluonic couplings at the 10% level of light quark couplings produces order-one shifts in the r value where the degradation factor has a minimum.
- The degradation factor at the minimum reaches values near 10^{-4} for most values of r_min, indicating strong suppression of the xenon rate.
- The two-body coefficients from shell model calculations are t_u = -0.63 MeV, t_d = -1.27 MeV, and t_s = 0.070 MeV, using a conventional cutoff d_c = 0.5 fm.
- The nucleon sigma terms used are sigma_{pi N} = 45(15) MeV and sigma_s = 45(25) MeV from lattice QCD ranges.

## Context

This work extends the chiral EFT approach to WIMP-nucleus scattering developed by Cirigliano (2012) and Prezeau (2003), which previously found that NLO corrections are typically at the 10% level for isospin-conserving couplings but can wash out the leading-order cancellation for isospin-violating couplings. The paper directly addresses the tension between positive signals from CDMS-Si (Agnese 2013) and null results from XENON100 (Aprile 2012) and LUX (Akerib 2013), which previous studies by Frandsen (2013), Gresham (2013), and DelNobile (2013) identified as potentially resolvable only by IVDM or inelastic down-scattering. The results enable future work connecting direct detection constraints to collider searches and indirect detection by mapping phenomenological bounds onto the underlying quark-level couplings.
