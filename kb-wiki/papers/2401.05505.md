# Predicting nucleon-nucleus scattering observables using nuclear structure theory

> Developing a predictive capability for inelastic scattering will find applications in multiple areas. Experimental data for neutron-nucleus inelastic scattering is limited and thus one needs a robust theoretical framework to complement it. Charged-particle inelastic scattering can be used as a surrogate for $(n, γ)$ reactions to predict capture cross sections for unstable nuclei. Our work uses microscopic nuclear structure calculations for spherical nuclei to obtain nucleon-nucleus scattering potentials and calculate cross sections for these processes. We implement the Jeukenne, Lejeune, Mahaux (JLM) semi-microscopic folding approach, where the medium effects on nuclear interaction are parameterized in nuclear matter to obtain the nucleon-nucleon $(NN)$ interaction in a medium at positive energies. We solve for the nuclear ground state using the Hartree-Fock-Bogliubov (HFB) many-body method, assuming the nucleons within the nucleus interact via the Gogny-D1M potential. The vibrational excited states of the target nucleus are calculated using the quasi-particle random phase approximation (QRPA). We demonstrate our approach for spherical nuclei in the medium-mass region, showing scattering results for the $^{90}$Zr nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that combining Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) and quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) nuclear structure calculations with the Jeukenne-Lejeune-Mahaux (JLM) folding model can produce nucleon-nucleus scattering cross sections, but the inelastic scattering results are systematically underpredicted and the agreement with experimental data varies with beam energy, likely due to missing spin-orbit and Coulomb exchange contributions to the transition potentials.

## Method

The authors implemented the JLM-B semi-microscopic folding approach, where the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction in a medium is parameterized from nuclear matter calculations and folded with target densities to generate scattering potentials. The target ground state was calculated using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method with the Gogny-D1M interaction, and vibrational excited states were obtained from quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA). Scattering cross sections were computed using the coupled-channels code FRESCO in the Distorted-Wave Born Approximation (DWBA), with the folding procedure applied to both elastic and inelastic channels for the test nucleus $^{90}$Zr.

## Key numbers

- Elastic proton scattering on $^{90}$Zr was reproduced for incident energies from 14.7 MeV to 185 MeV, consistent with the JLM-B validity range of 1 keV to 200 MeV.
- HFB+QRPA calculation gave excitation energy $E(2_1^+) = 2.725$ MeV compared to experimental 2.186 MeV, and $B(E2: 0^+ \rightarrow 2_1^+) = 0.038$ $e^2 b^2$ compared to experimental 0.061 $e^2 b^2$.
- HFB+QRPA calculation gave excitation energy $E(3_1^-) = 2.858$ MeV compared to experimental 2.748 MeV, and $B(E3: 0^+ \rightarrow 3_1^-) = 0.078$ $e^2 b^3$ compared to experimental 0.098 $e^2 b^3$.
- Inelastic differential cross sections for the first $2^+$ and $3^-$ states were underpredicted at all beam energies between 14.7 MeV and 185 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the JLM folding approach previously applied to $^{208}$Pb and $^{209}$Bi by Dupuis (2019) to the medium-mass spherical nucleus $^{90}$Zr, combining it with state-of-the-art HFB+QRPA structure calculations using the Gogny-D1M interaction. The approach aims to provide a predictive capability for inelastic scattering on unstable nuclei, which is needed for surrogate reaction methods to determine neutron-capture cross sections for nuclei relevant to stellar nucleosynthesis. The next step is to include spin-orbit and Coulomb exchange contributions to the transition potentials, which the authors identify as necessary for improving agreement with experimental data, particularly at lower beam energies where Coulomb effects dominate and at higher energies where spin-orbit contributions become important.
