# More uses for Thermal Models

> We explore combinations of particle and anti-particle yields which can be used to test thermal models in a parameter free way. We also explore combinations which can be used to extract $μ_B/T$, $μ_S/T$ and $μ_Q/T$. We use experimentally measured particle-antiparticle specific ratios for proton $p$, Lambda $Λ$, and cascade $Ξ$, for $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=$ 7.7-39 GeV from RHIC BES phase-1 to extract the $μ_{B,S,Q}/T$. These compared well with published STAR freeze-out parameters. These combinations are verified to predict a similar combination of $Ω$ yields. We also extend this idea to predict (anti-)nuclei yields at energies where they are not measured. We also update parametrizations for the $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ dependence of freeze-out parameters $T$ and $μ_B$, and present for the first time a similar parametrization of $μ_S$.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that specific combinations of particle and antiparticle yields (the ratio \(R_h\) of arithmetic to geometric means) can be used to extract the chemical potential ratios \(\mu_B/T\), \(\mu_S/T\), and \(\mu_Q/T\) without performing a full thermal-model fit, provided that the system is in local thermal equilibrium and \(\mu_Q/T\) is small.

## Method

The authors construct the quantity \(R_h = (n_h + n_{\bar{h}})/(2\sqrt{n_h n_{\bar{h}}})\) for protons, Lambdas, and cascades using published mid-rapidity yields from the STAR experiment at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 7.7\)–39 GeV. They invert the analytic expressions for \(R_p\), \(R_\Lambda\), and \(R_\Xi\) to obtain \(\mu_B/T\), \(\mu_S/T\), and \(\mu_Q/T\) via Eqs. (6)–(8), then validate the extracted values by comparing with published STAR freeze-out parameters and by predicting \(R_\Omega\) from the extracted chemical potentials. The method is extended to light nuclei (deuterons and tritons) to predict anti-nuclei yields at energies where they are not measured.

## Key numbers

* Extracted \(\mu_B/T\) increases with centrality (number of participating nucleons \(\langle N_{\rm part}\rangle\)) at fixed beam energy, reflecting increasing baryon density in more central collisions.
* Extracted \(\mu_S/T\) is independent of \(\langle N_{\rm part}\rangle\) within uncertainties, as expected for a system with fixed strangeness.
* Extracted \(\mu_Q/T\) is small and has large relative errors; it tends to become more negative with increasing \(\langle N_{\rm part}\rangle\).
* New parametrizations for the energy dependence of freeze-out parameters: \(T = A - B\mu_B^2\) with \(A = 163.4 \pm 1.8\) MeV and \(B = (1.3 \pm 0.2) \times 10^{-4}\) MeV\(^{-1}\); \(\mu_B = C/(1 + D\sqrt{s_{NN}})\) with \(C = 1187 \pm 220\) MeV and \(D = 0.259 \pm 0.063\) MeV\(^{-1}\); \(\mu_S = E/(1 + F\sqrt{s_{NN}})\) with \(E = 239 \pm 104\) MeV and \(F = 0.218 \pm 0.131\) MeV\(^{-1}\).
* Extrapolated freeze-out parameters at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 4.85\) GeV: \(T_{\rm ch} = 127.0 \pm 6.5\) MeV, \(\mu_B = 526.4 \pm 27.9\) MeV, \(\mu_S = 116.2 \pm 15.6\) MeV.
* Extrapolated freeze-out parameters at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 6.27\) GeV: \(T_{\rm ch} = 136.4 \pm 3.9\) MeV, \(\mu_B = 452.7 \pm 17.9\) MeV, \(\mu_S = 101.0 \pm 10.2\) MeV.
* Extrapolated freeze-out parameters at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 14.5\) GeV: \(T_{\rm ch} = 154.9 \pm 1.8\) MeV, \(\mu_B = 249.8 \pm 5.7\) MeV, \(\mu_S = 57.5 \pm 3.5\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the statistical hadronization model (SHM) approach used by STAR (2017) and earlier parametrizations by Cleymans (2005), Manninen (2008), Cleymans (2011), and Andronic (2009) by providing a parameter-free method to extract chemical potential ratios from particle-antiparticle ratios without performing a full fit. The method enables quick tests of thermalization via double ratios (Eq. 3) and can be applied to higher-order cumulants as proposed by Gupta (2022). The new parametrization of \(\mu_S\) as a function of \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) is presented for the first time, and the predictions for anti-nuclei yields at unmeasured energies (e.g., anti-deuterons at 7.7 GeV and anti-tritons across a range) can be tested by future experiments such as STAR BES-II.
