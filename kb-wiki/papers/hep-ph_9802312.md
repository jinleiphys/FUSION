# Stochastic interpretation of Kadanoff-Baym equations and their relation to Langevin processes

> In this more pedagogical study we want to elucidate on stochastic aspects inherent to the (non-)equilibrium real time Green's function description (or `closed time path Green's function' -- CTPGF) of transport equations, the so called `Kadanoff-Baym equations'. As a toy model we couple a free scalar boson quantum field to an exemplaric heat bath with some given temperature T. It will be shown in detail that the emerging transport equations have to be understood as the ensemble average over stochastic equations of Langevin type. This corresponds to the equivalence of the influence functional approach by Feynman and Vernon and the CTP technique. The former, however, gives a more intuitive physical picture. In particular the physical role of (quantum) noise and the connection of its correlation kernel to the Kadanoff-Baym equations will be discussed. The inherent presence of noise and dissipation related by the fluctuation-dissipation theorem guarantees that the modes or particles become thermally populated on average in the long-time limit. For long wavelength modes with momenta much less than the temperature the emerging wave equation do behave nearly as classical. On the other hand, a kinetic transport description can be obtained in the semi-classical particle regime. Including fluctuations, its form resembles that of a phenomenological Boltzmann-Langevin description. However, we will point out some severe discrepancies in comparison to the Boltzmann- Langevin scheme. As a further byproduct we also note how the occurrence of so called pinch singularities is circumvented by a clear physical necessity of damping within the one-particle propagator.

## Key claim

The Kadanoff-Baym equations, which describe the ensemble-averaged evolution of non-equilibrium Green's functions, must be understood as the ensemble average over stochastic equations of Langevin type, where the noise correlation kernel is directly related to the self-energy components of the closed time path formalism.

## Method

The authors couple a free scalar boson quantum field to a heat bath at fixed temperature T, treating the bath's influence via self-energy kernels in the closed time path Green's function (CTPGF) formalism. They rewrite the CTP action in the form of a Feynman-Vernon influence functional, identifying the real symmetric kernel I(x₁,x₂) as a noise correlator and the antisymmetric kernel a(x₁,x₂) as the dissipative term. They then show that the retarded/advanced propagators are determined solely by the mass-shift and damping kernels (s and a), while the Kadanoff-Baym equation for D^< contains the noise kernel I, and demonstrate that averaging over a Gaussian noise distribution with correlation I reproduces the CTP propagator.

## Key numbers

* For long wavelength modes with momenta |k| ≪ T, the occupation number n(ω_p) = (e^{ω_p/T} − 1)^{-1} approaches the classical equipartition limit T/m^* ≫ 1.
* The fluctuation-dissipation relation for colored noise is I(t−t') = 2T Γ(t−t'), where Γ is the friction kernel and T is the bath temperature.
* In the long-time limit, the average squared momentum of a Brownian particle satisfies ⟨p²(t→∞)⟩ = T M, independent of the detailed form of the friction kernel.
* The on-shell damping rate in the weak coupling regime is γ(p) = −Im(Σ_ret(p, E_p))/(2E_p).

## Context

This pedagogical study extends the formal equivalence between the Feynman-Vernon influence functional approach and the closed time path technique, previously noted by Smith and Calzetta (1988) on a formal level, by providing a more physically intuitive demonstration using a scalar field coupled to a heat bath. The work connects to the derivation of effective stochastic equations for soft modes in φ⁴-theory by Greiner (1997) and to the Langevin description for long-wavelength modes in non-Abelian gauge theories proposed by Huet and Son (1997) for cosmological baryogenesis. The analysis also shows how pinch singularities are naturally circumvented by the physical necessity of damping in the one-particle propagator.
