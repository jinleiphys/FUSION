# Induced Fission of $^{240}$Pu within a Real-Time Microscopic Framework

> We describe the fissioning dynamics of Pu240 from a configuration in the proximity of the outer fission barrier to full scission and the formation of the fragments within an implementation of density functional theory extended to superfluid systems and real-time dynamics. The fission fragments emerge with properties similar to those determined experimentally, while the fission dynamics appears to be quite complex, with many excited shape and pairing modes. The evolution is found to be much slower than previously expected, and the ultimate role of the collective inertia is found to be negligible in this fully nonadiabatic treatment of nuclear dynamics, where all collective degrees of freedom (CDOF) are included (unlike adiabatic treatments with a small number of CDOF).

## Key claim

The time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) framework, applied to the induced fission of $^{240}$Pu from the outer fission barrier to scission, produces fission fragment properties similar to experimental data, but reveals that the saddle-to-scission evolution is much slower (by roughly an order of magnitude) than previously expected from hydrodynamic or Langevin approaches, and that collective inertia plays a negligible role in this fully nonadiabatic treatment where all collective degrees of freedom are included.

## Method

The authors solved the time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) equations for a $^{240}$Pu nucleus in a simulation box of $22.5^2 \times 40$ fm$^3$ with a momentum cutoff $p_c \approx 500$ MeV/c, using the Skyrme SLy4 energy density functional and a density-dependent pairing interaction parameterized by $\eta$ (mixing of volume and surface pairing). Starting from a constrained self-consistent configuration near the outer fission barrier at zero temperature, they followed the real-time dynamics for up to 120,000 time steps (0.119 fm/c each) using approximately 1,760 GPUs on the Titan supercomputer, allowing all collective degrees of freedom to evolve without restrictions on nuclear shape.

## Key numbers

- Saddle-to-scission times: 4,360 fm/c (S2, $\eta=0.5$), 14,010 fm/c (S3, $\eta=0$), 14,419 fm/c (S1, $\eta=0.75$), and 12,751 fm/c (S4, $\eta=0$, lower excitation)
- Total kinetic energy (TKE) of fission fragments: 182 MeV (S1), 183 MeV (S2), 180 MeV (S3), 181 MeV (S4), compared to systematic value of approximately 177.3 MeV for the given neutron energies
- Excitation energies of heavy fragment ($E^*_H$) and light fragment ($E^*_L$): S1: 5.26 MeV and 17.78 MeV; S2: 9.94 MeV and 11.57 MeV; S3: 3.35 MeV and 29.73 MeV; S4: 7.85 MeV and 9.59 MeV
- Light fragment mass numbers ($A_L$): 104.0 (S1), 106.3 (S2), 105.5 (S3), 103.9 (S4), compared to systematic value of approximately 100.55
- Estimated post-scission neutron numbers for light fragment ($\nu_L$): 1.9 (S1), 1 (S2), 2.9 (S3), 1 (S4), compared to evaluated average of about 3

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent density functional theory approach to fission, previously applied to lighter systems and simpler dynamics, to a heavy actinide nucleus ($^{240}$Pu) with full inclusion of pairing correlations and without restricting collective degrees of freedom. It contradicts the much shorter time scales (about 1,000 fm/c or less) predicted by hydrodynamic approaches, Langevin dynamics with various viscosities (PhysRevC_1976, PhysRevC_1978), and approximate time-dependent mean-field treatments, while also showing that the frozen-occupation approximation and naive TDHF treatments (Maruhn) fail because they do not allow the necessary transitions between single-particle states with different $m$-quantum numbers. The results enable future improvements in nuclear energy density functionals, particularly for large deformations and dynamic properties, and open the possibility of predicting fission observables for astrophysical environments where experiments are not feasible.
