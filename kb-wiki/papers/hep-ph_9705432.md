# Multi-Boson Correlations Using Wave-Packets

> Brooding over bosons, wave packets and Bose - Einstein correlations, we present a generic quantum mechanical system that contains arbitrary number of bosons characterized by wave-packets and that can undergo a Bose-Einstein condensation either by cooling, or increasing the number density of bosons, or by increasing the overlap of the multi-boson wave-packet states, achieved by changing the size of the single-particle wave-packets. We show that the n-particle correlations may mimic coherent or chaotic behaviour for certain limiting wave-packet sizes. Effects of complete n-particle symmetrization are included. The resulting weights which fluctuate between 1 and n! are summed up with the help of a formal analogy between the considered wave-packet system and an already explored multi-boson plane-wave system. We solve the model analytically in the highly condensed and in the rare gas limiting cases, numerically in the intermediate cases. The relevance of the model to multi-pion production in high energy heavy ion physics as well as to the Bose-Einstein condensation of atomic vapours is discussed. As a by-product, a new class of probability distribution functions is obtained, event-by-event fluctuations of single-particle momentum distributions are predicted and the critical density for the onset of pion-lasing in high energy heavy ion collisions is derived.

## Key claim

The paper presents a generic quantum mechanical model of multi-boson systems using wave-packets, showing that n-particle Bose-Einstein correlations can mimic either coherent or chaotic behavior depending on the wave-packet size, and that the transition to a Bose-Einstein condensate occurs when the overlap of single-particle wave-packets reaches a critical value, not only through cooling or increasing number density.

## Method

The authors construct n-boson states from single-particle Gaussian wave-packets with identical width σ, characterized by centers in coordinate and momentum space (ξᵢ, πᵢ). They define a density matrix that includes induced emission by weighting the product of single-particle distributions with the sum over permutations of wave-packet overlaps, then reduce the multi-particle wave-packet problem to an effective plane-wave problem using a formal analogy. The model is solved analytically in the rare gas limit and the highly condensed limit, and numerically for intermediate cases, using recurrence relations derived from the ring-algebra of multi-particle symmetrization.

## Key numbers

* The overlap between two wave-packets with identical width σ is given by γᵢⱼ = exp( -(πᵢ - πⱼ)²/(4σ²) ) × exp( -(ξᵢ - ξⱼ)² σ²/4 ) × phase factors, with γᵢᵢ = 1.
* The single-particle source distribution in coordinate space is a Gaussian with radius R: ρₓ(ξ) = (1/(2πR²)³/²) exp(-ξ²/(2R²)).
* The single-particle source distribution in momentum space is a Gaussian with temperature T: ρₚ(π) = (1/(2πmT)³/²) exp(-π²/(2mT)).
* For n particles emitted in the same wave-packet state, the density matrix weight is n!/𝒩(n); for negligible overlap, the weight is 1/𝒩(n), yielding weights fluctuating between 1 and n!.
* In a central Pb+Pb collision at SPS with 160 AGeV laboratory energy, approximately 500-600 π⁻ are produced per event.

## Context

This work extends the plane-wave treatment of Bose-Einstein correlations by introducing wave-packets to resolve the contradiction between classical source assumptions and quantum mechanics, building on the pion-laser concept of Pratt (1993) and the ring-algebra formalism developed in that work and by Chao (1995). The model is relevant to multi-pion production in high-energy heavy ion collisions (where the Quark-Gluon Plasma is sought) and to Bose-Einstein condensation of atomic vapors in magnetic traps. It enables analytic calculation of multiplicity distributions, single-particle spectra, and two-particle correlation functions for systems with up to hundreds of bosons, overcoming the factorial growth of computational cost that limits explicit symmetrization methods such as those of Cramer (1996).
