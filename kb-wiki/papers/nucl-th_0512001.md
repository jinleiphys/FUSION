# Parton cascade and coalescence

## Key claim

Parton coalescence is a promising mechanism for explaining the observed baryon/meson differences and constituent quark number scaling of elliptic flow at RHIC, but dynamical models (specifically covariant parton transport theory) reveal that simple parametrizations of quark phase space distributions are inconsistent with realistic dynamics, and the observed scaling requires a highly nontrivial interplay between spatial inhomogeneities and nonlinear couplings of large local flow anisotropies.

## Method

The paper reviews covariant parton transport theory based on the Boltzmann equation with elastic 2→2 and inelastic 2→2 scattering rates, implemented in the MPC (Molnar Parton Cascade) model. It then applies a dynamical coalescence approach that extends the Gyulassy-Frankel-Remler (GFR) formalism to account for diffuse 4D freezeout distributions, using box Wigner functions to combine coalescence with independent fragmentation of partons that do not find coalescence partners.

## Key numbers

- Elliptic flow from transport theory is reduced by 30–50% relative to the ideal fluid limit at RHIC opacities (Fig. 1b)
- The transport opacity corresponds to dN_g/dη(b=0) × σ_gg ≈ 1000 × 45 mb, about 15 times above the perturbative estimate
- Shear viscosity of the parton plasma is close to the conjectured lower bound η_min = s/(4π)
- In dynamical coalescence for Au+Au at √s_NN = 200 GeV with b = 8 fm and σ_gg = 10 mb, direct pions and protons from coalescence show modest flow reduction of about 20% for mesons and 30% for baryons relative to quark number scaling
- Coalescence enhances both pion and proton yields by up to a factor of three in the coalescence window 1.5 < p_T < 4.5 GeV
- Even with immediate freezeout at τ = 0.1 fm/c, the p/π ratio is enhanced only 1.5–1.7 times relative to p+p, much less than the factor ≈3 seen at RHIC
- Essentially all partons above p_T > 2.5 GeV fragment rather than coalesce

## Context

This invited plenary talk at Quark Matter 2005 reviews the parton cascade approach and its implications for coalescence at RHIC, extending earlier work on coalescence models by Voloshin (1999), Hwa and Yang (2003), and the Duke group (2003). It directly challenges the simplifying assumptions of spatially uniform parton densities or uniform flow anisotropies used in prior coalescence studies (coalv2, dukeCoal), showing that dynamical transport models from MPC (Molnar, 2004) and AMPT (Lin et al., 2004) produce spatially nonuniform local densities and flow anisotropies that break simple scaling. The work enables future studies combining realistic transport with coalescence to test whether the approach can quantitatively reproduce RHIC data, particularly precise measurements of deviations from quark number scaling and the transition to pure fragmentation at high p_T.
