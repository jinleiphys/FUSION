# FRIGA, A New Approach To Identify Isotopes and Hyper-nuclei In N-Body Transport Models

> We present a new approach to identify fragments in computer simulations of relativistic heavy ion collisions. It is based on the simulated annealing technique and can be applied to n-body transport models like the Quantum Molecular Dynamics. This new approach is able to predict isotope yields as well as hyper-nucleus production. In order to illustrate its predicting power, we confront this new method with experimental data and show the sensitivity on the parameters which govern the cluster formation.

## Key claim

FRIGA extends the earlier Simulated Annealing Clusterisation Algorithm (SACA) by adding asymmetry energy, pairing, and shell effects to the binding energy calculation, and by incorporating a secondary de-excitation step via GEMINI++, in order to predict isotope yields and hyper-nucleus production from n-body transport models like Quantum Molecular Dynamics; the authors hedge that the method's success depends on the assumption that the ratio of nuclear structure contributions to the calculated binding energy remains constant at the moderate temperatures and near-ground-state densities where primary fragments are identified.

## Method

The FRIGA algorithm starts from a minimum spanning tree (MST) pre-clustering of nucleon positions and momenta from a QMD transport simulation, then applies a simulated annealing (Metropolis) procedure to exchange nucleons between neighboring pre-clusters, seeking the partition with the most negative total binding energy. The binding energy for each cluster is computed using the same Skyrme, Yukawa, and Coulomb potentials as in the transport code, supplemented by an asymmetry energy term (from IQMD) and a structure term that scales the difference between experimental binding and the Bethe-Weizsäcker formula by the ratio of the calculated binding energy to the Bethe-Weizsäcker volume-surface-Coulomb binding. Primary fragments with excitation energy exceeding 1A MeV are then de-excited using the GEMINI++ code for sequential evaporation or fission.

## Key numbers

- For Au+Au collisions at 600A MeV and impact parameter 4 fm, FRIGA reaches asymptotic light-fragment (Z ≤ 2) multiplicity at around 50 fm/c, whereas MST needs at least 200 fm/c.
- The asymmetry energy potential term uses E₀ = 23.3 MeV and, by default, γ = 1 (linear density dependence); the kinetic part of the asymmetry energy at saturation density corresponds to about 9 MeV in IQMD.
- The pairing energy is taken from the Bethe-Weizsäcker formula as Δ_pairing = 11.2 A^{-1/2} MeV; for ⁴He and ³He the pairing energies are 12 MeV and 6.9 MeV, respectively.
- Primary fragments identified by FRIGA have average excitation energies that hardly exceed 1A MeV; secondary decays contribute up to 50% of the total α-particle yield in minimum bias Au+Au at 600A MeV.
- For the "Rise and Fall" curve in Au+Au at 600A MeV, FRIGA with a hard equation of state (K = 380 MeV) reproduces ALADiN experimental data, while a soft equation of state without momentum dependence (K = 200 MeV) does not.

## Context

This work extends the SACA approach of Dorso et al. (1993) and Puri et al. (2000), which had successfully reproduced experimental fragment charge distributions and bimodality, by adding isotopic and hyper-nuclear capabilities. It contrasts with simpler coalescence models (Gutbrod et al., 1976; Gosset et al., 1977) that require isotope-specific parameters and cannot handle heavy clusters, and with MST methods that only yield reliable fragments after 200-400 fm/c. The FRIGA method enables the study of isotope yields and hyper-nucleus production in transport models, and can be used to constrain the nuclear equation of state by comparing predictions with experimental data such as the ALADiN "Rise and Fall" curve.
