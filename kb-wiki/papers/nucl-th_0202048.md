# The Density Matrix Renormalization Group Method and Large-Scale Nuclear Shell-Model Calculations

> The particle-hole Density Matrix Renormalization Group (p-h DMRG) method is discussed as a possible new approach to large-scale nuclear shell-model calculations. Following a general description of the method, we apply it to a class of problems involving many identical nucleons constrained to move in a single large j-shell and to interact via a pairing plus quadrupole interaction. A single-particle term that splits the shell into degenerate doublets is included so as to accommodate the physics of a Fermi surface in the problem. We apply the p-h DMRG method to this test problem for two $j$ values, one for which the shell model can be solved exactly and one for which the size of the hamiltonian is much too large for exact treatment. In the former case, the method is able to reproduce the exact results for the ground state energy, the energies of low-lying excited states, and other observables with extreme precision. In the latter case, the results exhibit rapid exponential convergence, suggesting the great promise of this new methodology even for more realistic nuclear systems. We also compare the results of the test calculation with those from Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov approximation and address several other questions about the p-h DMRG method of relevance to its usefulness when treating more realistic nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The particle-hole Density Matrix Renormalization Group (p-h DMRG) method can reproduce exact shell-model results for ground-state energies, low-lying excited states, and occupation numbers with extreme precision in systems with competing pairing and quadrupole correlations, provided a well-defined Fermi surface exists; the method exhibits rapid exponential convergence even for systems far beyond exact diagonalization, though the test problems are restricted to identical nucleons in a single large-j shell.

## Method

The authors split a single large-j shell into doubly-degenerate single-particle levels via an axially-deformed Hartree-Fock calculation, separating them into particle levels (above the Fermi surface) and hole levels (below). They then iteratively add levels moving outward from the Fermi surface, at each step constructing a superblock hamiltonian from the enlarged particle and hole blocks, diagonalizing it, building reduced density matrices for particles and holes from the ground state (or lowest few states), and truncating to the p most important states in each block based on the largest density-matrix eigenvalues. The calculation is repeated with increasing p until the results converge exponentially.

## Key numbers

- For 10 particles in a j=25/2 shell with chi=1, g=0.1, epsilon=0.1: exact correlation energy is -0.70633; HFB correlation energy is -0.20641 (only 28% of exact); DMRG with p=200 achieves more than 99% of the exact correlation energy.
- For the same system at p=200, the largest superblock matrix diagonalized had dimension 2,886; at p=40, the maximum dimension was 232, yet DMRG still achieved over 95% of the full correlation energy.
- For the same system at p=200, all three lowest excitation energies are reproduced to 1% or better.
- For 40 particles in a j=99/2 shell (chi=1, g=0.1, epsilon=0.2): DMRG with p=100 gives ground-state correlation energy -2.8994; exponential fit yields asymptotic value -2.8990 +/- 0.0003 (accuracy to 1 part in 10^4); HFB correlation energy is -1.7902 (less than 62% of DMRG estimate).
- The exact hamiltonian matrix for the 40-particle, j=99/2 system would have dimension 3.84 x 10^25.

## Context

This work extends the p-h DMRG method, previously applied to pairing problems in ultrasmall superconducting grains (Dukelsky et al., 2001) and to a two-level pairing model (Dukelsky et al., 1998), into the nuclear shell-model regime with competing pairing and quadrupole correlations. The test problems use a single-j shell with pairing plus quadrupole interaction and a single-particle splitting term, comparing DMRG results against exact Lanczos diagonalization (for the 10-particle case) and against Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov approximation. The method enables large-scale shell-model calculations for systems far beyond the limits of exact diagonalization, such as the 40-particle j=99/2 case, and the authors suggest it can be extended to realistic neutron-proton systems with multiple shells.
