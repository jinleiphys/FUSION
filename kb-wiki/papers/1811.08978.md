# An Elementary End of the Periodic Table

> Using the Planck scale as an absolute bound of half-life, we give a quick estimate, in the manner of Feynman's fine-structure method, of the highest possible atomic number. We find, upon simple extrapolation, that element 168 would constitute the end of the Periodic Table and its isotope with atomic weight 411, being the most stable. These are remarkably close to current best estimates obtained from sophisticated and much more involved Hartree-Fock calculations.

## Key claim
Using the Planck time as an absolute lower bound on half-life, a simple log-linear extrapolation from known transuranic elements suggests that element 168 with atomic weight 411 is the heaviest possible element, but this bound depends on the assumption that the linear fit for maximal half-life versus atomic number holds up to the Planck scale.

## Method
The authors collected the longest observed half-lives and atomic weights for transuranic elements from uranium (Z=92) to oganesson (Z=118). They performed log-linear regression of half-life against atomic number and linear regression of atomic weight against atomic number, obtaining statistically significant fits. They then extrapolated the half-life fit to the Planck time (5.39 x 10^-44 seconds) to find the maximum atomic number, and used the weight fit to find the corresponding atomic mass.

## Key numbers
- Element 168 is predicted as the end of the Periodic Table, with its most stable isotope having atomic weight 411.
- The log-linear fit for half-life is ln(tau) ≈ 195.68 - 1.76 Z, with p-value 8.37 x 10^-16 and F-statistic 322.69.
- The linear fit for atomic weight is W ≈ 25.43 + 2.30 Z, with p-value 7.38 x 10^-27 and F-statistic 2633.02.
- Planck time used as the absolute lower bound: t_P = 5.39 x 10^-44 seconds.
- If the nuclear physics definition of existence (half-life around 10^-14 seconds) were used instead, the extrapolation gives Z_max ≈ 129.

## Context
This work extends the long history of theoretical upper bounds on atomic number, from Meitner and Frisch (1939) suggesting Z=100, through Feynman's estimate of Z≈137 based on the 1s electron speed not exceeding light speed, to more accurate Hartree-Fock-Slater calculations pushing Z_max to approximately 173 (Schwarz, 1978; Pyykkö, 2011). The novelty is combining nuclear data with the Planck scale from quantum gravity, which the authors claim is the first such estimate bridging these energy regimes. The result (Z=168) is close to but slightly below the current best theoretical bounds from sophisticated Hartree-Fock calculations, and the authors suggest it constitutes an absolute theoretical bound due to the fundamental nature of the Planck scale.
