# Analysis of the kinematic boundaries of the quasi-elastic neutrino-nucleus cross section in the superscaling model with a relativistic effective mass

> In this work we obtain the analytical expressions for the boundaries of the charged current quasi-elastic double differential cross section in terms of dimensionless energy and momentum transfers, for the Relativistic Fermi Gas (RFG) and the Super-Scaling approach with relativistic effective mass (SuSAM*) models, within the scaling formalism. In addition, we show that this double differential cross section in the scaling formalism has very good properties to be implemented in the Monte Carlo (MC) neutrino event generators, particularly because its peak is almost flat with the (anti)neutrino energy. This makes it especially well-suited for the event generation by the acceptance-rejection method usually used in the neutrino generators. Finally, we analyze the total charged current quasi-elastic (CCQE) cross section $σ(E_ν)$ for both models and attribute the enhancement observed in the SuSAM* total cross section to the high-momentum components which are present, in a phenomenological way, in its scaling function, while these are absent in the RFG model.

## Key claim

The paper derives analytical expressions for the kinematic boundaries of the charged-current quasi-elastic (CCQE) double differential cross section in the scaling formalism for both the Relativistic Fermi Gas (RFG) and SuSAM* models, and claims that the double differential cross section in terms of dimensionless variables (kappa, lambda) has an almost flat peak with respect to neutrino energy, making it particularly suitable for Monte Carlo event generation via the acceptance-rejection method. The enhancement observed in the SuSAM* total cross section relative to RFG is attributed to high-momentum components present in its phenomenological scaling function, which are absent in the RFG model.

## Method

The authors transform the standard CCQE double differential cross section from final lepton kinematic variables (muon kinetic energy and scattering angle) to dimensionless energy and momentum transfer variables (lambda = omega/(2m_N) and kappa = q/(2m_N)) within the scaling formalism. They derive the analytical boundaries of the allowed phase space in the (lambda, kappa) plane by combining constraints from the nuclear model (RFG scaling variable limits psi = +/-1 and Pauli blocking conditions) with constraints from lepton kinematics (physical muon scattering angle). The analysis is extended from the RFG model to the SuSAM* model, which uses a relativistic effective nucleon mass fitted to inclusive (e,e') data and a phenomenological scaling function extracted from a global fit.

## Key numbers

* Fermi momentum k_F = 225 MeV/c, corresponding to eta_F = 0.239, used for the RFG model calculations.
* Muon mass m_mu = 106 MeV/c^2 used for the lepton kinematic constraints.
* Fermi coupling constant G_F = 1.116 x 10^{-11} MeV^{-2} and cos(theta_c) = 0.975 for the Cabibbo angle.
* The maximum allowed value of lambda is given by lambda_max = (E_nu - m_mu)/(2m_N) = epsilon_nu - tilde{m}_mu, where epsilon_nu = E_nu/(2m_N) and tilde{m}_mu = m_mu/(2m_N).
* The QE peak position in the (lambda, kappa) plane is given by kappa_QE(lambda) = sqrt(lambda(lambda+1)).
* The NPB region boundaries are kappa^{NPB}_{pm}(lambda) = (1/2) sqrt((epsilon_F + 2lambda)^2 - 1) +/- eta_F/2.

## Context

This work extends the analytical treatment of kinematic boundaries previously developed for the RFG model (Alberico 1988, Barbaro 1998, 2003) to the SuSAM* model (Amaro 2015, 2017, 2018; Ruiz Simo 2018), which incorporates a relativistic effective mass fitted to inclusive electron scattering data. The derived boundaries enable efficient implementation of these nuclear models in Monte Carlo neutrino event generators, particularly for the acceptance-rejection method, and provide a framework for understanding the enhancement of the total CCQE cross section in SuSAM* relative to RFG due to high-momentum nucleon components. The work enables future comparisons with experimental CCQE cross section measurements from experiments such as MiniBooNE (Aguilar-Arevalo 2010, 2013, 2018) and T2K (Abe 2011, 2014, 2015).
