# Electrostatic mapping of nuclear pairing

> The traditional nuclear pairing problem is shown to be in one-to-one correspondence with a classical electrostatic problem in two dimensions. We make use of this analogy in a series of calculations in the Tin region, showing that the extremely rich phenomenology that appears in this classical problem can provide interesting new insights into nuclear superconductivity.

# Electrostatic mapping of nuclear pairing

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the nuclear pairing problem, which is exactly solvable via Richardson's equations, maps one-to-one onto a classical two-dimensional electrostatic problem. The authors claim that the geometrical transition from isolated "atoms" to a "cluster" of free charges (pairons) in the electrostatic picture directly reflects the superconducting phase transition in the quantum problem, even though that transition is softened and difficult to see in exact quantum results due to the finiteness of the Hilbert space.

## Method

The authors start from the Richardson solution of the pairing Hamiltonian, which yields a set of equations for unknown pair energies \(e_\alpha\). They construct an energy functional \(U\) whose derivatives with respect to the pair energies reproduce the Richardson equations, and identify this functional as the Coulomb energy of a 2D electrostatic system: fixed charges (orbitons) at positions \(2\varepsilon_i\) with charges \(k_i\) (related to orbital degeneracy and seniority), and free unit positive charges (pairons) at positions \(e_\alpha\), subject to a uniform electric field of intensity \(1/(2g)\). They then solve the Richardson equations for the ground states of \(^{114}\)Sn and \(^{116}\)Sn, tracking the pairon positions as the pairing strength \(g\) increases from the weak-coupling limit to the physical value.

## Key numbers

- The physical value of the pairing strength \(g\) for Tin isotopes is approximately \(-21.7\) MeV/A = \(-0.190\) MeV.
- For \(^{116}\)Sn, a critical value \(g_c = 0.19044\) is identified where the eighth pairon crosses the \(g_{7/2}\) orbiton, very near the physical value of \(0.187\).
- The transition from isolated atoms to a cluster in the electrostatic picture occurs at around \(g \sim 0.08\) for \(^{114}\)Sn.
- The orbiton charges for the Tin region are: \(d_{5/2}\) at position 0.0 with charge \(-1.5\); \(g_{7/2}\) at 0.44 with charge \(-2.0\); \(s_{1/2}\) at 3.80 with charge \(-0.5\); \(d_{3/2}\) at 4.40 with charge \(-1.0\); \(h_{11/2}\) at 5.60 with charge \(-3.0\).

## Context

This work extends the exact solvability of the pairing Hamiltonian first presented by Richardson (1960s) and later generalized by Dukelsky et al. (2001) to other pairing-governed systems. The electrostatic analogy builds on ideas first published by Richardson (1977) and has been recently connected to Conformal Field Theory by Sierra (2001). The mapping provides a pictorial way to understand nuclear superconductivity that is difficult to see in exact quantum calculations due to finite-size effects, and the authors suggest the Richardson solution could replace the BCS part of Skyrme HF+BCS codes to improve accuracy in large model spaces.
