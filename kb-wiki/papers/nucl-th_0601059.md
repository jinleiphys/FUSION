# Dilepton production at HADES: theoretical predictions

> Dileptons represent a unique probe for nuclear matter under extreme conditions reached in heavy-ion collisions. They allow to study meson properties, like mass and decay width, at various density and temperature regimes. Present days models allow generally a good description of dilepton spectra in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collision. For the energy regime of a few GeV/nucleon, important discrepancies between theory and experiment, known as the DLS puzzle, have been observed. Various models, including the one developed by the Tübingen group, have tried to address this problem, but have proven only partially successful. High precision spectra of dilepton emission in heavy-ion reactions at 1 and 2 GeV/nucleon will be released in the near future by the HADES Collaboration at GSI. Here we present the predictions for dilepton spectra in C+C reactions at 1 and 2 GeV/nucleon and investigate up to what degree possible scenarios for the in-medium modification of vector mesons properties are accessible by the HADES experiment.

## Key claim

The paper presents theoretical predictions for dilepton spectra in C+C collisions at 1 and 2 AGeV, arguing that the HADES experiment will be able to clearly discriminate between vacuum and in-medium scenarios for vector meson properties, but only in the mass region around the rho/omega peak; at low invariant masses the in-medium effects are at the 20-30% level and will be difficult to distinguish in the light C+C system.

## Method

The authors combine a resonance model for elementary dilepton sources (including direct decays, Dalitz decays, and baryon resonance decays via extended vector meson dominance) with the relativistic quantum molecular dynamics (RQMD) transport model for heavy-ion collisions. They compute dilepton spectra for C+C reactions at 1.0 and 2.0 AGeV under several in-medium scenarios: Brown-Rho scaling of vector meson masses, collisional broadening of the rho and omega widths, and quantum decoherence effects. The theoretical results are then filtered through the full HADES acceptance filter with smearing for the detector mass resolution.

## Key numbers

- Collisional broadening values adopted: Gamma_rho^coll = 100 MeV and Gamma_omega^coll = 116.6 MeV at rho = rho_0
- Full in-medium calculation uses Gamma_rho^tot = 250 MeV and Gamma_omega^tot = 125 MeV at rho = rho_0
- Variation of the rho meson width between Gamma_rho^tot = 150 to 250 MeV modifies the dilepton yield by a factor of 2 in the mass range 0.5-0.8 GeV
- Decoherence effects are responsible for at most a 50% change in the dilepton spectra at intermediate masses
- A moderate in-medium omega width of Gamma_omega^tot = 60 MeV (with Gamma_rho^tot = 200 MeV at rho_0) gives at most a 50% increase in yield relative to the full in-medium scenario in the 0.4-0.8 GeV mass region
- The ratio of dilepton spectra at 2.0 over 1.0 AGeV shows in-medium effects at the 20-30% level
- Brown-Rho scaling parameter alpha = 0.2 for the mass scaling law m*_V = m_V(1 - alpha rho/rho_0)
- HADES mass resolution is Delta M/M approximately 1% (sigma) over the entire spectrum

## Context

This work extends the Tübingen group's previous transport calculations (She03) that addressed the DLS puzzle, where discrepancies between theory and experiment for dilepton spectra at a few GeV/nucleon had been observed. The DLS Collaboration (Por97, DLS2) measured dilepton spectra at 1.0 AGeV in Ca+Ca and C+C reactions, and the low mass region was underestimated by transport calculations even when in-medium spectral functions or dropping mass scenarios were included (Bra98, Ern98). The predictions are designed for comparison with upcoming high-precision data from the HADES Collaboration at GSI (Had05, Fri99), which will measure dilepton spectra in A+A, p+A, and pi+A reactions. The results enable experimental tests of in-medium vector meson modifications, including Brown-Rho scaling (Bro91), collisional broadening, and decoherence effects, and suggest that heavier systems than C+C may be needed to clearly observe low-mass in-medium effects.
