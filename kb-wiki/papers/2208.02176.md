# Nuclear physics uncertainties in light hypernuclei

> The energy levels of light hypernuclei are experimentally accessible observables that contain valuable information about the interaction between hyperons and nucleons. In this work we study strangeness $S = -1$ systems $^{3,4}_Λ$H and $^{4,5}_Λ$He using the ab initio no-core shell model (NCSM) with realistic interactions obtained from chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT). In particular, we quantify the finite precision of theoretical predictions that can be attributed to nuclear physics uncertainties. We study both the convergence of the solution of the many-body problem (method uncertainty) and the regulator- and calibration data-dependence of the nuclear $χ$EFT Hamiltonian (model uncertainty). For the former, we implement infrared correction formulas and extrapolate finite-space NCSM results to infinite model space. We then use Bayesian parameter estimation to quantify the resulting method uncertainties. For the latter, we employ a family of 42 realistic Hamiltonians and measure the standard deviation of predictions while keeping the leading-order hyperon-nucleon interaction fixed. Following this procedure we find that model uncertainties of ground-state $Λ$ separation energies amount to $\sim 20(100)$ keV in $^3_Λ$H($^4_Λ$H,He) and $\sim 400$ keV in $^5_Λ$He. Method uncertainties are comparable in magnitude for the $^4_Λ$H,He $1^+$ excited states and $^5_Λ$He, which are computed in limited model spaces, but otherwise much smaller. This knowledge of expected theoretical precision is crucial for the use of binding energies of light hypernuclei to infer the elusive hyperon-nucleon interaction.

## Key claim

The authors quantify the finite precision of theoretical predictions for light hypernuclei that can be attributed to nuclear physics uncertainties, finding that model uncertainties from the nuclear Hamiltonian dominate over method uncertainties from the many-body solution for ground-state Λ separation energies, with the latter becoming comparable only for excited states and heavier systems computed in limited model spaces.

## Method

The authors employ the ab initio no-core shell model (NCSM) in a Jacobi-coordinate formulation to solve the many-body Schrödinger equation for the strangeness S = -1 systems ³ΛH, ⁴ΛH, ⁴ΛHe, and ⁵ΛHe. They quantify method uncertainty by implementing infrared correction formulas to extrapolate finite-space NCSM results to infinite model space, using Bayesian parameter estimation with a stochastic model for next-to-leading-order corrections. For model uncertainty, they employ a family of 42 realistic NNLOsim Hamiltonians from chiral effective field theory, varying the regulator cutoff and calibration data, while keeping the leading-order Bonn–Jülich hyperon-nucleon interaction fixed, and measure the standard deviation of predictions.

## Key numbers

- Model uncertainties of ground-state Λ separation energies amount to ~20 keV in ³ΛH and ~100 keV in ⁴ΛH,He.
- Model uncertainties of ground-state Λ separation energies amount to ~400 keV in ⁵ΛHe.
- Method uncertainties are comparable in magnitude for the ⁴ΛH,He 1⁺ excited states and ⁵ΛHe, which are computed in limited model spaces, but otherwise much smaller than model uncertainties.
- The family of Hamiltonians comprises 42 different parametrizations of the nuclear interaction at NNLO order, associated with seven different regulator cutoffs Λ = 450, 475, …, 575, 600 MeV and six different maximum scattering energies Tmax = 125, 158, …, 257, 290 MeV.
- The hyperon-nucleon interaction uses a momentum cutoff ΛYN ranging from 550 to 700 MeV, with the default value ΛYN = 600 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ab initio NCSM approach for hypernuclei developed by Wirth (2017) and applies it to quantify theoretical uncertainties, building on the infrared extrapolation formalism for nuclear systems established by Furnstahl (2012) and Forssén (2017). The study uses the family of NNLOsim nuclear Hamiltonians from Carlsson (2015) and the leading-order Bonn–Jülich hyperon-nucleon interaction from Polinder (2006). The results enable the use of binding energies of light hypernuclei to constrain the hyperon-nucleon interaction, as the quantified theoretical precision is crucial for inferring the elusive hyperon-nucleon interaction from experimental data.
