# A Microscopic Energy- and Density-Dependent Effective Interaction and its Test by Nucleus-Nucleus Scattering

> An effective nucleon-nucleon interaction calculated in nuclear matter from the Bonn potential has been parametrized in terms of a local density- and energy-dependent two-body interaction. This allows to calculate the real part of the nucleus-nucleus scattering potential and to test this effective interaction over a wide region of densities ($ρ\leq 3ρ_0$) produced dynamically in scattering experiments. Comparing our calculations with empirical potentials extracted from data on light and heavy ion scattering by model-unrestricted analysis methods, we find quantitative agreement with the exception of proton scattering. The failure in this case may be traced back to the properties of the effective interaction at low densities, for which the nuclear matter results are not reliable. The success of the interaction at high overlap densities confirms the empirical evidence for a soft equation of state for cold nuclear matter.

# A Microscopic Energy- and Density-Dependent Effective Interaction and its Test by Nucleus-Nucleus Scattering

## Key claim
The authors derive a local, energy- and density-dependent effective nucleon-nucleon interaction from the Bonn potential via G-matrix calculations in nuclear matter, and test it against empirical nucleus-nucleus scattering potentials without adjusting any free parameters. The interaction reproduces heavy-ion and alpha scattering potentials quantitatively, but fails for proton scattering because the nuclear-matter-derived interaction is unreliable at densities below saturation density (ρ < ρ₀).

## Method
The authors solved the Bethe-Goldstone equation in nuclear matter using the Bonn one-boson-exchange potential to obtain the G-matrix, then parametrized the G-matrix as a local two-body interaction with Yukawa terms for the scalar-isoscalar, isovector, spin-isospin, and spin-isovector channels. This local interaction was used in a double-folding model to compute the real part of the nucleus-nucleus optical potential, with antisymmetrisation treated in the finite-range approximation, and the results were compared to empirical potentials extracted from model-unrestricted analyses of proton, alpha, and ¹⁶O scattering data.

## Key numbers
- The effective interaction was tested over a density range ρ ≤ 3ρ₀, where ρ₀ = 0.17 fm⁻³ is the saturation density of nuclear matter.
- For ¹⁶O + ¹⁶O scattering at E = 704 MeV, ignoring the explicit energy dependence (using the T-matrix instead of the G-matrix) overestimates the real potential by up to 20%.
- The root-mean-square radius of the calculated potential for p + ⁴⁰Ca is larger by 30% than the experimental value, far beyond experimental uncertainty.
- The derived interaction yields a nuclear matter compressibility of K ≈ 190 MeV, consistent with a soft equation of state.
- The nuclear matter saturation properties from the interaction are E/A = -13 MeV and ρ₀ = 0.22 fm⁻³, slightly off the empirical values of -16 MeV and 0.17 fm⁻³ (the Coester-band problem).
- Small readjustment of the potential strength by a factor λ = 0.9 – 1.0 brings the calculations into full agreement with experimental results for heavy-ion and alpha scattering.

## Context
This work extends earlier double-folding analyses that used a factorized effective interaction with a phenomenological density dependence fitted to scattering data for light ions (Clement and collaborators, 1990s) and heavy ions (Bartnitzky and collaborators, 1990s). The authors replace the purely phenomenological density dependence with a microscopically derived interaction from nuclear matter G-matrix calculations, building on the approach of Müther and collaborators (1990s) for the Bonn potential. The success at high densities confirms the empirical evidence for a soft equation of state, while the failure at low densities points to the need for a theoretical treatment starting from finite nuclei rather than homogeneous nuclear matter. This enables more rigorous tests of nuclear matter properties from scattering experiments, but also highlights the limitations of nuclear-matter-based interactions for surface-sensitive reactions.
