# Effects of triaxiality and pairing interaction on fission barriers of actinide nuclei studied by density-dependence relativistic mean-field theory

> We employ density-dependent relativistic mean-field theory to study how the triaxiality and pairing interaction affect the inner fission barriers of actinide nuclei. It was found that triaxiality reduced the inner fission barriers and improved agreement with experimental values for many actinides. However, about 1-2 MeV discrepancy to the experimental values still remained for some of the considered nuclei. Such a discrepancy could be made further smaller by increasing the BCS pairing strength parameter. In this work, we demonstrated that adjusting the paring strength was effective to reproduce the experimental inner fission barriers as well as "pairing rotational energy" and binding energy in a consistent manner for nuclei where the effect of the triaxiality on the inner fission barriers was significant.

## Key claim

The authors find that including triaxiality reduces inner fission barriers by about 2 MeV and improves agreement with experimental values for many actinides, but a discrepancy of about 1-2 MeV remains for some nuclei; this residual discrepancy can be further reduced by increasing the BCS pairing strength parameter, and adjusting the pairing strength can simultaneously reproduce experimental inner fission barriers, pairing rotational energy, and binding energy for nuclei where triaxiality effects are significant.

## Method

The authors used density-dependent relativistic mean-field theory with two parameter sets (DD-ME2 and DD-PC1) and a non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus BCS pairing model (SkM*) for comparison. They imposed constraints on axial and triaxial mass quadrupole moments to map the total energy surface covering the inner fission barrier, and varied the BCS pairing strength by a multiplier α (with α=1.20 corresponding to a 20% increase) to study its effect on barrier heights and pairing rotational energies.

## Key numbers

- For 240Pu, the inner fission barrier height with axial symmetry was 9.40 MeV (DD-ME2), 8.61 MeV (DD-PC1), and 10.16 MeV (SkM*); with triaxiality it was 7.50 MeV (DD-ME2), 6.53 MeV (DD-PC1), and 7.93 MeV (SkM*)
- The experimental inner fission barrier height for 240Pu is 5.60 MeV (Bjørnholm and Lynn) or 6.05 MeV (RIPL-3)
- Triaxiality reduced inner fission barriers by about 2 MeV relative to axial symmetry across the actinide region
- Increasing the pairing strength by 20% (α=1.20) improved agreement with experimental pairing rotational energies for Pu isotopes in both DD-ME2 and DD-PC1 calculations
- The pairing strength parameter G was set to 728 MeV·fm³ to reproduce the pairing gap in symmetric nuclear matter

## Context

This work extends previous studies that found triaxiality suppresses inner barrier heights by about 2 MeV using relativistic mean-field models (Chinese group, using RMF + point coupling model) and non-relativistic energy density functionals (Skyrme and Gogny forces). The authors' prior study (Kouno et al., 2021) showed that increasing pairing strength could improve agreement with experimental inner fission barriers under axial symmetry. The present work demonstrates that the combination of triaxiality and adjusted pairing strength can systematically improve reproducibility of experimental barriers, and that pairing rotational energy provides a consistent observable to determine the pairing strength without requiring calculations for odd nuclei.
