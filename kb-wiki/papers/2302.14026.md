# Methods for systematic study of nuclear structure in high-energy collisions

> There is increasing interest in using high-energy collisions to probe the structure of nuclei, in particular with the high-precision data made possible by collisions performed with pairs of isobaric species. A systematic study requires a variation of parameters representing nuclear properties such as radius, skin thickness, angular deformation, and short-range correlations, to determine the sensitivity of the various observables on each of these properties. In this work we propose a method for efficiently carrying out such study, based on the shifting of positions of nucleons in Monte-Carlo samples. We show that by using this method, statistical demands can be dramatically reduced -- potentially reducing the required number of simulated events by orders of magnitude -- paving the way for systematic study of nuclear structure in high-energy collisions,

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to systematically study nuclear structure in high-energy collisions by shifting nucleon positions in pre-existing Monte Carlo samples, which can reduce the required number of simulated events by orders of magnitude compared to generating independent simulations for each set of nuclear parameters.

## Method

The authors generate a baseline set of nuclear configurations by sampling nucleon positions from a spherically-symmetric Woods-Saxon distribution, using an alternative sampling method that approximates the Woods-Saxon as a convolution of a spherical step function and a 3D Gaussian. To change nuclear properties such as radius, skin thickness, angular deformation (parameterized by multipole coefficients βℓ,m), or short-range correlations, they apply continuous shifts to nucleon positions derived from solving a continuity equation for the one-body density or from a pair-distance transformation for two-body correlations. For angular deformations, they use an irrotational flow ansatz and solve a second-order differential equation for the shift field, while for short-range correlations they shift pairs of nucleons radially from their midpoint to achieve a desired correlation function.

## Key numbers

- For the isobar pair ⁹⁶Ru and ⁹⁶Zr, the Woods-Saxon parameters used are R = 5.09 fm, a = 0.46 fm for ⁹⁶Ru and R = 5.02 fm, a = 0.52 fm for ⁹⁶Zr, with quadrupole deformation β₂ = 0.16 and γ = 30° for Ru, and β₂ = 0.06, β₃,₀ = 0.20 for Zr.
- For short-range correlations with C_str = -1 and C_len = 0.4 fm, the offset C_∞ is approximately 3 × 10⁻³.
- In a benchmark test adding short-range correlations (C_len = 0.2 fm, C_str = -1) to uncorrelated nucleons, the shift in eccentricity is approximately 0.1%, and the statistical uncertainty is reduced by a factor of approximately 32 using the shifting method, reducing the required number of events by a factor of approximately 2900.
- The nucleon-nucleon cross section used in the Glauber model benchmark is σ_NN = 0.62 fm².

## Context

This work extends the emerging direction of using high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions to infer nuclear properties such as deformation, as demonstrated in recent isobaric collision measurements of ⁹⁶Ru and ⁹⁶Zr at the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider by STAR (2021). Prior approaches, such as those by Jia (2022) requiring hundreds of millions of simulation events per parameter set, or the exclusion-distance method of Alver (2008) and Moreland (2014) which can distort the one-body distribution, are made more efficient by this shifting technique. The method enables systematic scans over nuclear structure parameters (radius, skin thickness, deformation, short-range correlations) with dramatically reduced computational cost, facilitating precise comparisons to experimental data.
