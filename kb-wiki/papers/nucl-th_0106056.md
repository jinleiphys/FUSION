# Phenomenological study of hadron interaction models

> We present a phenomenological study of three models with different effective degrees of freedom: a Goldstone Boson Exchange (GBE) model which is based on quark-meson couplings, the quark delocalization, color screening model (QDCSM) which is based on quark-gluon couplings with delocalized quark wavefunctions, and the Fujiwara-Nijmegen (FN) mixed model which includes both quark-meson and quark-gluon couplings. We find that for roughly two-thirds of 64 states consisting of pairs of octet and decuplet baryons, the three models predict similar effective baryon-baryon interactions. This suggests that the three very different models, based on different effective degrees of freedom, are nonetheless all compatible with respect to baryon spectra and baryon-baryon interactions. We also discuss the differences between the three models and their separate characteristics.

## Key claim

The three constituent quark models (GBE, QDCSM, FN) predict similar effective baryon-baryon interactions for roughly two-thirds of the 64 possible two-baryon channels consisting of octet and decuplet baryons, despite being based on different effective degrees of freedom (quark-meson, quark-gluon, and quark-meson-gluon couplings). However, for the remaining one-third of states, especially promising dibaryon candidates, the models give drastically different predictions that are sensitive to model details.

## Method

The authors performed a systematic calculation of adiabatic effective baryon-baryon interactions for all possible S-wave states of six-quark systems (u, d, s flavors) using three constituent quark models: the Goldstone Boson Exchange (GBE) model with two smearing versions (Gaussian and Yukawa), the Fujiwara-Nijmegen (FN) mixed model, and the quark delocalization, color screening model (QDCSM). The adiabatic approximation was used to obtain effective potentials as a function of baryon separation, and the six-body matrix elements were calculated using fractional parentage expansions and variational methods.

## Key numbers

- For the deuteron channel (SIJ=001), the minimum effective potential is -18 MeV (FN), -21 MeV (QDCSM), and -3 MeV (GBE), with equilibrium separations of 1.2 fm, 1.3 fm, and 2.0 fm respectively
- For the (001) deuteron channel, short-range repulsion is 2436 MeV for GBE with Gaussian smearing but only about 790 MeV for GBE with Yukawa smearing
- The QDCSM predicts a strong intermediate-range attraction for state (003) with V0 = -359 MeV, decreasing to V0 = -308 MeV for (-1,1/2,3), V0 = -184 MeV for (-202), and only several MeV for (-403)
- The FN model predicts for state (-3,3/2,0) V0 = -87 MeV, for (-410) V0 = -95 MeV, for (-5,1/2,1) V0 = -136 MeV, and for (-600) V0 = -281 MeV
- For the di-Omega state (-600), the FN model predicts binding energy B = -62 MeV with mass 3282 MeV (Omega-Omega threshold is 3345 MeV); the GBE model predicts B = -11 MeV
- For the H particle (-200), the QDCSM predicts B = -14 MeV with mass 2218 MeV; the FN model obtains mass 2312 MeV (threshold 2231 MeV); GBE(a) obtains 2207 MeV; GBE(b) obtains 2249 MeV
- For states with large angular momentum (J=3), the difference in |V0| between GBE(a) and GBE(b) is greater than 20 MeV but not more than 100 MeV
- For some states in GBE model (a), |V0| ranges from 200 to 400 MeV at small equilibrium separation (0.5-0.8 fm), while model (b) shows very small |V0| or no attraction

## Context

This work extends the debate over which effective degrees of freedom are proper for low-energy QCD, comparing the Glozman-Riska-Brown GBE model (Glozman, Riska, Brown, 1990s), the Fujiwara-Nijmegen hybrid model (Fujiwara, 1990s), and the quark delocalization, color screening model (Wang, Ping, Goldman, 1990s). The finding that two-thirds of states yield similar predictions across models suggests that quark delocalization and color screening in the QDCSM effectively represent meson exchange effects. The remaining one-third of states, particularly high-spin and high-strangeness dibaryon candidates such as the d* (003), di-Omega (-600), and H particle (-200), show model-dependent predictions that require experimental searches to distinguish which effective degrees of freedom best describe low-energy hadron physics.
