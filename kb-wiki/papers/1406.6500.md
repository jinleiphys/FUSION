# Universal short-time response and formation of correlations after quantum quenches

> The short-time evolution of two distinct systems, the pump and probe experiments with semiconductor and the sudden quench of cold atoms in an optical lattice, is found to be described by the same universal response function. This analytic formula at short time scales is derived from the quantum kinetic theory approach observing that correlations need time to be formed. The demand of density conservation leads to a reduction of the relaxation time by a factor of four in quench setups. The influence of finite trapping potential is derived and discussed as well as Singwi-Sjølander local field corrections.

## Key claim

The short-time evolution of two distinct systems, pump-probe experiments in semiconductors and sudden quenches of cold atoms in an optical lattice, is described by the same universal response function, derived from a mean-field kinetic theory with a conserving relaxation-time approximation, under the condition that correlations need time to form and that density conservation is explicitly imposed.

## Method

The author linearizes a kinetic equation with a relaxation-time approximation that conserves density (Mermin correction), starting from a mean-field level where higher-order correlations are neglected because they require time to build up. For the sudden quench of cold atoms in a Hubbard lattice, the Laplace-transformed kinetic equation is solved analytically for the time-dependent density, including the effects of a harmonic trapping potential as a perturbation. For the response to an external perturbation, the two-time density response function is derived from the same kinetic equation and shown to satisfy a differential equation whose solution yields a universal analytic form for both Coulomb and Hubbard systems.

## Key numbers

- Relaxation time used to fit experimental cold-atom data: \(\tau = 0.6 \, \hbar/J\) (common value for all data sets).
- Lattice constant for cold-atom experiments: \(a = \lambda/2 = 765\) nm, with wave vector \(q = \pi \hbar / a\) and initial density \(n = 1/(2a)\).
- Plasma frequency for GaAs: \(\omega_p = 14.4\) THz; relaxation time for GaAs: \(\tau = 85\) fs.
- The f-sum rule is time-independent: \(\int_0^\infty \omega \, \mathrm{Im} \, \varepsilon^{-1} = -\pi \omega_p^2\).
- The compressibility sum rule for the lattice gas in the long-time limit: \(\kappa = 1/[n (n a U - K a^2/2)]\).

## Context

This work extends the Mermin (1970) conserving relaxation-time approximation to time-dependent mean fields and applies it to both long-range Coulomb systems (semiconductors) and short-range Hubbard models (cold atoms in optical lattices). It provides an analytic formula that reproduces experimental pump-probe data in GaAs (Huber et al., 2001, 2002) and cold-atom quench data (Trotzky et al., 2012) better than the numerical renormalization-group calculations of Flesch et al. (2008) for longer times. The result enables quick estimation of short-time collective-mode formation without numerically demanding Green's function or renormalization-group simulations, and it provides a baseline from which higher-order correlation effects (e.g., Singwi-Sjølander local field corrections) can be extracted.
