# Comment on: "Nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths in QCD sum rules,"

> In a recent Physical Review Letter, Kondo and Morimatsu present a QCD sum rule calculation of nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths. They also relate the empirical scattering lengths to the nucleon mass shift in nuclear matter to cast doubt on the "linear density approximation." In this Comment, we point out flaws in both parts of their analysis and draw very different conclusions.

## Key claim

Kondo and Morimatsu's QCD sum rule calculation of nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths is flawed in both its derivation and its conclusions; the authors argue that the empirical scattering lengths have little to do with the nucleon mass shift in nuclear matter, and that the "linear density approximation" is not invalidated by the analysis presented in the original Letter.

## Method

The authors critique the impulse approximation used by Kondo and Morimatsu, where the zero-momentum limit is taken after the approximation, which drastically overestimates the mass shift when applied at momenta much smaller than the Fermi momentum. They also identify a missing asymmetric continuum contribution in the finite-density sum rules of Kondo and Morimatsu, which completely alters the results when corrected. The authors contrast this with a mean-field treatment of the nucleon optical potential, where the linear density approximation follows naturally from averaging the effective nucleon-nucleon interaction in medium.

## Key numbers

- Kondo and Morimatsu predicted Re V(0) ≈ -600 MeV at saturation density using scattering lengths; the authors argue the correct result from detailed calculations shows |Re V(0)| < 100 MeV.
- The authors note that the impulse approximation with |q| ≫ k_F is valid, but taking q=0 after the approximation when |q| ≪ k_F drastically overestimates the mass shift.

## Context

This Comment directly contradicts the conclusions of Kondo and Morimatsu (1993), who claimed that QCD sum rules overestimate nucleon mass shifts and cast doubt on the linear density approximation. The authors instead support the approach of Furnstahl, Griegel, and Cohen (1992), which focuses separately on Lorentz scalar and vector self-energies and finds that mean-field components dominate. The analysis also builds on the finite-density sum rules of Hatsuda, Høgaasen, and Prakash (1991) and the multiple scattering expansion formalism of Feshbach (1992). The corrected sum rules are expected to give results consistent with Furnstahl, Griegel, and Cohen (1992), but with greater sensitivity to details.
