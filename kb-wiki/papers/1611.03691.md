# Quantifying statistical uncertainties in ab initio nuclear physics using Lagrange multipliers

> Theoretical predictions need quantified uncertainties for a meaningful comparison to experimental results. This is an idea which presently permeates the field of theoretical nuclear physics. In light of the recent progress in estimating theoretical uncertainties in ab initio nuclear physics, we here present and compare methods for evaluating the statistical part of the uncertainties. A special focus is put on the (for the field) novel method of Lagrange multipliers (LM). Uncertainties from the fit of the nuclear interaction to experimental data are propagated to a few observables in light-mass nuclei to highlight any differences between the presented methods. The main conclusion is that the LM method is more robust, while covariance based methods are less demanding in their evaluation.

## Key claim
The Lagrange multiplier (LM) method for propagating statistical uncertainties from fits of nuclear interactions to observables is more robust than covariance-based methods when the chi-squared surface is non-quadratic or observables are nonlinear in the low-energy constants, but covariance-based methods are computationally less demanding and sufficient when statistical uncertainties are small.

## Method
The author compares six methods for propagating statistical uncertainties from the fit of chiral effective-field theory low-energy constants (LECs) to experimental data: four covariance-matrix-based methods (Monte Carlo sampling, quadratic approximation, linear approximation, and an approximate covariance matrix using only first derivatives) and two Lagrange multiplier methods (exact and approximate). All methods are applied to the NNLOsim potential with a cutoff of 500 MeV and maximum laboratory energy of 290 MeV, using automatic differentiation for derivatives except for the approximate covariance matrix where finite differences are used. The statistical uncertainties are propagated to observables in light nuclei (deuteron, helium-3, helium-4) and to neutron-proton scattering observables.

## Key numbers
- For helium-4 binding energy and point-proton radius, all six methods produce identical statistical uncertainties (specific numerical values not given in the truncated text).
- For deuteron binding energy, the Monte Carlo sampling and quadratic approximation produce larger uncertainties than the LM method due to quadratic dependence of the observable on LECs in directions not picked up by LM.
- For neutron-proton analyzing power at laboratory energy 175.26 MeV and center-of-mass angle 97.63 degrees, the linear uncertainty nearly vanishes because the derivative crosses zero, while other methods capture larger uncertainties from quadratic variations.
- When artificially scaling all uncertainties by a factor γ = 10, the deuteron point-proton radius shows discrepancies: among 26 eigen directions, nine have deviations exceeding 5% from the quadratic approximation, with ratios σ_calc,i/σ_quad,i ranging from 0.3 to 0.9.

## Context
This work extends recent efforts to quantify theoretical uncertainties in ab initio nuclear physics, particularly in chiral effective-field theory (χEFT), following statistical sensitivity analyses by Dobaczewski (2014) and Carlsson (2016), Bayesian methods by Furnstahl (2015) and Wesolowski (2016), and error propagation by Ekström (2015) and Navarro (2015). The Lagrange multiplier method, previously used in particle physics by Stump (2001), is introduced to nuclear physics as a more robust alternative when the chi-squared surface is not quadratic. The results enable practitioners to choose between computationally cheaper covariance methods and more accurate LM methods depending on the size of statistical uncertainties and the linearity of observables.
