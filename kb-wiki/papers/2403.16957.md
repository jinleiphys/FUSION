# Hydrodynamic fluctuations and topological susceptibility in chiral magnetohydrodynamics

> Chiral magnetohydrodynamics is devoted to understanding the late-time and long-distance behavior of a system with an Adler-Bell-Jackiw anomaly at finite temperatures. The non-conservation of the axial charge is determined by the topological density $\vec{E} \cdot \vec{B}$; in a classical hydrodynamic description this decay rate can be suppressed by tuning the background magnetic field to zero. However it is in principle possible for thermal fluctuations of $\vec{E} \cdot \vec{B}$ to result in a non-conservation of the charge even at vanishing $B$-field; this would invalidate the classical hydrodynamic effective theory. We investigate this by computing the real-time susceptibility of the topological density at one-loop level in magnetohydrodynamic fluctuations, relating its low-frequency limit to the decay rate of the axial charge. We find that the frequency-dependence of this susceptibility is sufficiently soft as to leave the axial decay rate unaffected, validating the classical hydrodynamic description. We show that the susceptibility contains non-analytic frequency-dependence which is universally determined by hydrodynamic data. We comment briefly on possible connections to the recent formulation of the ABJ anomaly in terms of non-invertible symmetry.

## Key claim

The authors compute the one-loop contribution to the topological susceptibility from magnetohydrodynamic fluctuations and find that the frequency-dependence is sufficiently soft that the axial charge decay rate vanishes at zero background magnetic field, validating the classical hydrodynamic description of chiral magnetohydrodynamics despite the possibility that thermal fluctuations of the topological density could invalidate it.

## Method

The authors compute the real-time retarded correlation function of the topological density operator Q = 8 E·B at one-loop order in magnetohydrodynamic fluctuations, using the Kubo formula that relates the low-frequency limit of this correlator to the axial charge decay rate. They express the Euclidean correlator in terms of spectral densities of electric and magnetic field two-point functions, then evaluate the resulting integrals using the magnetohydrodynamic form of the transverse correlator A(ω,|p|) = -iω²ρ/(ω+iDp²), where ρ is the resistivity and D the magnetic diffusion constant. The momentum integral is performed analytically, and the remaining frequency integral is separated into infrared and ultraviolet regions to extract the non-analytic frequency dependence.

## Key numbers

- The axial charge decay rate Γ_A is given by the formula Γ_A = lim_{Ω→0} (k²/(χ_A Ω)) Im G^R_{QQ}(Ω, p=0), where k = 1/(16π²) is the anomaly coefficient for a single Dirac fermion and χ_A is the axial charge susceptibility.
- The classical result for the decay rate with a background magnetic field B is Γ_A = 64 k² B² ρ / χ_A, where ρ is the plasma resistivity.
- The one-loop calculation yields Γ_A(Ω) = -(16 k² ρ²)/(2√2 π⁴ D^(5/2) χ_A) ∫ dω [f(ω+Ω)-f(ω)] [ω(ω+Ω)²/(Ω(2ω+Ω))] (√|ω+Ω| - √|ω|), where f(ω) is the Bose distribution function.
- In the limit Ω → 0, the infrared part of the integral gives I⁺(Ω)_IR = (-√Λ/(2β) + βΛ^(5/2)/120 - β³Λ^(9/2)/4320 + ...) Ω, which is proportional to Ω, leading to a vanishing decay rate at zero frequency.

## Context

This work extends the effective field theory treatment of chiral magnetohydrodynamics developed in Das (2022) and Landry (2022), which derived the classical formula Γ_A = 64 k² B² ρ/χ_A for the axial charge decay rate in a background magnetic field. The authors address a potential instability of that classical description: thermal fluctuations of the topological density E·B could produce a non-zero decay rate even at vanishing B-field, which would invalidate the hydrodynamic effective theory. The result validates the classical hydrodynamic description and suggests that the non-invertible symmetry structure of the chiral anomaly is protected against one-loop fluctuation effects. The calculation also connects to the memory matrix formalism and the work of Delacretaz on relating the decay rate to the topological susceptibility.
