# Towards a universal description of hadronic phase of QCD

> Mean-field model quantum field theories of hadrons were traditionally developed to describe cold and dense nuclear matter and are by now very well constrained from the recent neutron star merger observations. We show that when augmented with additional known hadrons and resonances but not included earlier, these mean-field models can be extended beyond its regime of applicability. Calculating some specific ratios of baryon number susceptibilities for finite temperature and moderate values of baryon densities within mean-field approximation, we show that these match consistently with the lattice QCD data available at lower densities, unlike the results obtained from a non-interacting hadron resonance gas model. We also estimate the curvature of the line of constant energy density, fixed at its corresponding value at the chiral crossover transition in QCD, in the temperature-density plane. The number density at low temperatures and high density is found to be about twice the nuclear saturation density along the line of constant energy density of $ε=348 \pm 41$ MeV/$\text{fm}^3$. Moreover from this line we can indirectly constrain the critical end-point of QCD to be beyond $μ_B=596$ MeV for temperature $\sim 125 $ MeV.

## Key claim

The authors show that relativistic mean-field nuclear models, traditionally designed for cold dense matter and well-constrained by neutron star observations, can be extended to describe QCD thermodynamics at finite temperature and moderate baryon densities (μ_B/T = 3–5) by augmenting them with additional hadrons and resonances from the QMHRG list, provided the couplings of these extra baryons to mesons are tuned to match lattice QCD data on a specific susceptibility ratio.

## Method

The authors start with a relativistic mean-field model (model 1) that includes nucleons, hyperons, and mesons (σ, ω, ρ, δ, σ*, φ) with couplings fixed from nuclear saturation properties and neutron star constraints. They extend this model (model 2) by adding all baryons from the QMHRG list, parametrizing the unknown couplings of non-strange baryons as a fraction α of nucleon couplings and strange baryons as a fraction α_S of Λ-hyperon couplings. They solve the mean-field equations self-consistently at finite temperature and baryon chemical potential, then compute ratios of baryon number susceptibilities (χ^{BS}_{31}/χ^{BS}_{11}, χ^{BQ}_{31}/χ^{BQ}_{11}, χ^{B}_{4}/χ^{B}_{2}) and compare to lattice QCD data to constrain α and α_S.

## Key numbers

- The energy density fixed at the chiral crossover value is ε = 348 ± 41 MeV/fm³.
- The number density at low temperatures and high density along the constant energy density line is about twice the nuclear saturation density.
- The critical end-point of QCD is constrained to be beyond μ_B = 596 MeV for temperature ∼ 125 MeV, giving a lower bound μ_B/T ∼ 4.76.
- For model 1, the curvature coefficients of the constant energy density line are κ₂ = 0.020(2), κ₄ = −0.0010(3), κ₆ = 0.000060(3).
- For model 2, the curvature coefficients are κ₂ = 0.020(2), κ₄ = −0.0005(1), κ₆ = 0.000010(2).
- The baryon densities obtained for neutron star conditions (n_Q/n_B = 0.05–0.2, n_S = 0) vary from 0.28 fm⁻³ to 0.35 fm⁻³ as energy density varies from ε = 307 to 389 MeV/fm³.
- The couplings for model 2 are fitted to lattice QCD data with α ranging from 0.15 to 0.2 and α_S ranging from 0.15 to 0.7.

## Context

This work extends relativistic mean-field models (Bunta 2004) that were previously constrained by neutron star merger observations (Nandi 2018) to finite temperature and moderate baryon densities, where they are compared to lattice QCD data (Borsanyi 2018) and the QMHRG model (Bazavov 2014). The results show that including repulsive interactions beyond the non-interacting hadron resonance gas model is necessary to explain lattice data, and the extended model preserves agreement with cold dense matter constraints like the nuclear liquid-gas transition. This enables a path toward a universal hadronic model valid across the entire QCD phase diagram, with future directions including beyond-mean-field effects and incorporation of chiral symmetry breaking.
