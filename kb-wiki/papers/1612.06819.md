# Deformation dependence of the isovector giant dipole resonance: The neodymium isotopic chain revisited

> Proton inelastic scattering experiments at energy E_p = 200 MeV and a spectrometer scattering angle of 0 degree were performed on 144,146,148,150Nd and 152Sm exciting the IsoVector Giant Dipole Resonance (IVGDR). Comparison with results from photo-absorption experiments reveals a shift of resonance maxima towards higher energies for vibrational and transitional nuclei. The extracted photo-absorption cross sections in the most deformed nuclei, 150Nd and 152Sm, exhibit a pronounced asymmetry rather than a distinct double-hump structure expected as a signature of K-splitting. This behaviour can be related to the proximity of these nuclei to the critical point of the phase shape transition from vibrators to rotors with a soft quadrupole deformation potential. Self-consistent random-phase approximation (RPA) calculations using the SLy6 Skyrme force provide a relevant description of the IVGDR shapes deduced from the present data

## Key claim

The paper claims that the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) in the most deformed neodymium isotopes (150Nd) and 152Sm does not exhibit the expected double-hump structure from K-splitting, but instead shows a pronounced asymmetry; this behavior is attributed to these nuclei lying near the critical point of a shape phase transition from vibrators to rotors with a soft quadrupole deformation potential, rather than being fully rigid rotors.

## Method

Proton inelastic scattering experiments at 200 MeV incident energy and a spectrometer scattering angle of 0 degrees were performed on 144,146,148,150Nd and 152Sm at iThemba LABS using the K600 magnetic spectrometer. The double-differential cross sections were converted to equivalent photo-absorption cross sections via the virtual photon method using the eikonal approximation, after subtracting contributions from the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR), isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance (ISGQR), and a phenomenological background. The resulting spectra were compared to previous (gamma,xn) data from Saclay and to self-consistent Skyrme separable random-phase approximation (SSRPA) calculations using the SLy6 force.

## Key numbers

- Incident proton energy: Ep = 200 MeV
- Spectrometer angular acceptance: theta_lab = ±1.91 degrees
- Target isotopic enrichments: >96% for all targets except 148Nd (90%)
- Target areal densities: 1.8 to 2.6 mg/cm^2
- Energy resolution: ΔE = 45 keV (FWHM)
- Statistical errors in the IVGDR region: 2-4%
- Systematic uncertainty on double-differential cross sections: ±7%
- For 144Nd: centroid energy 15.64 ± 0.01 MeV, width 4.93 ± 0.03 MeV (present work), compared to 15.05 ± 0.10 MeV and 5.30 ± 0.25 MeV (Carlos 1971)
- For 150Nd: K=0 component centroid 11.97 ± 0.10 MeV, width 2.91 ± 0.40 MeV; K=1 component centroid 15.67 ± 0.04 MeV, width 5.64 ± 0.09 MeV (present work)
- For 152Sm: K=0 component centroid 12.40 ± 0.20 MeV, width 4.73 ± 0.65 MeV; K=1 component centroid 16.36 ± 0.07 MeV, width 6.36 ± 0.14 MeV (present work)
- Ratio R of summed photo-absorption cross sections (10-14 MeV)/(14-18 MeV): 0.42 for 144Nd, 0.60 for 150Nd, 0.62 for 152Sm (present work)
- Ground-state deformation parameters beta2: 0.13 (144Nd), 0.15 (146Nd), 0.20 (148Nd), 0.28 (150Nd), 0.31 (152Sm)
- SSRPA calculations exhaust 98-100% of the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn energy-weighted sum rule

## Context

This work extends and contradicts the classic Saclay photo-absorption measurements of Carlos et al. (1971, 1974) on the neodymium and samarium isotopic chains, which had reported a clear double-hump structure in 150Nd and 152Sm interpreted as K-splitting from rigid quadrupole deformation. The present results are consistent with a critical reanalysis of the Saclay method by Varlamov et al. (2014) and with newer (gamma,n) data by Nyhus et al. (2015) and Filipescu et al. (2014) that find systematically lower cross sections. The findings enable future tests at facilities such as the NEPTUN tagger at the S-DALINAC and at ELI-NP, and suggest that the IVGDR shape may serve as a probe of the softness of the nuclear deformation potential near shape phase transitions.
