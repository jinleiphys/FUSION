# Report of the Snowmass 2021 Topical Group on Lattice Gauge Theory

> Lattice gauge theory continues to be a powerful theoretical and computational approach to simulating strongly interacting quantum field theories, whose applications permeate almost all disciplines of modern-day research in High-Energy Physics. Whether it is to enable precision quark- and lepton-flavor physics, to uncover signals of new physics in nucleons and nuclei, to elucidate hadron structure and spectrum, to serve as a numerical laboratory to reach beyond the Standard Model, or to invent and improve state-of-the-art computational paradigms, the lattice-gauge-theory program is in a prime position to impact the course of developments and enhance discovery potential of a vibrant experimental program in High-Energy Physics over the coming decade. This projection is based on abundant successful results that have emerged using lattice gauge theory over the years: on continued improvement in theoretical frameworks and algorithmic suits; on the forthcoming transition into the exascale era of high-performance computing; and on a skillful, dedicated, and organized community of lattice gauge theorists in the U.S. and worldwide. The prospects of this effort in pushing the frontiers of research in High-Energy Physics have recently been studied within the U.S. decadal Particle Physics Planning Exercise (Snowmass 2021), and the conclusions are summarized in this Topical Report.

## Key claim

Lattice gauge theory is positioned to provide essential nonperturbative theoretical inputs for a wide range of High-Energy Physics experiments over the coming decade, but achieving these goals requires not only the anticipated increase in exascale computing resources but also continued innovations in algorithms, methods, and workforce development.

## Method

The report synthesizes the findings of the Snowmass 2021 community planning exercise for lattice gauge theory, drawing on nearly 60 Letters of Interest, approximately 15 whitepapers, and multiple workshops and conferences organized within the Theory Frontier (TF05) and four other Frontiers. The conclusions are based on community assessment of the current state of lattice calculations across quark and lepton flavor physics, nucleon structure, beyond-Standard-Model phenomenology, and computational infrastructure.

## Key numbers

- The current statistical significance of the muon (g-2) tension with the Standard Model is 4.2 sigma, with lattice QCD projected to reach below 0.5% precision for hadronic vacuum polarization (HVP) and 10% for hadronic light-by-light scattering (HLbL) by 2025.
- The Flavor Lattice Averaging Group (FLAG) world average for the strong coupling constant alpha_s(M_Z) has a precision of about 0.7%, with future step-scaling methods aiming below 0.5%.
- Decay constants f_K/f_pi and the semileptonic vector form factor f_+(0) have been determined with about 0.2% precision, and the FLAG world average is in approximately 3 sigma tension with CKM unitarity.
- Lattice QCD calculations of epsilon'/epsilon for direct CP violation in kaon decays have achieved about 40% precision, consistent with experimental results from NA48 and KTeV.
- The Standard Model calculation of Delta M_K (the K_L-K_S mass difference, 3.482(5) x 10^{-12} MeV) has been reported with 40% precision.
- The estimated computational cost for reducing the uncertainty in epsilon'/epsilon by a factor of about 4 is approximately 2000 exaflop-hours.

## Context

This report summarizes the conclusions of the Snowmass 2021 decadal planning exercise for lattice gauge theory, extending and updating the community vision articulated in prior USQCD whitepapers (USQCD 2019, USQCD 2022) and the FLAG averaging reports (Aoki 2021). It enables a coordinated roadmap for lattice calculations to support the experimental programs at the LHC, Belle II, Fermilab Muon (g-2), neutrino oscillation experiments, and dark matter direct detection searches over the next decade, while also identifying the need for continued algorithm development and workforce retention.
