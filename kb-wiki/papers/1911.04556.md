# Production of hypernuclei and properties of hyper-nuclear matter

> The relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions can produce hypernuclei and low-temperature hyper-matter as a result of hyperon capture by nuclear residues and free nucleons. We use the transport, coalescence and statistical models to describe the whole process, and point at the important advantages of such reactions: A broad variety of formed hypernuclei in masses and isospin allows for investigating properties of exotic hypernuclei, as well as the hypermatter both at high and low temperatures. The abundant production of multi-strange nuclei that can give an access to multi-hyperon systems and strange nuclear matter. The de-excitation of hot hyper-cluster will allow for the hyperon correlation studies. There is a saturation of the hypernuclei production at high energies, therefore, the optimal way to pursue this experimental study is to use the accelerator facilities of intermediate energies.

## Key claim

Relativistic nucleus-nucleus collisions can produce hypernuclei and low-temperature hyper-matter via hyperon capture by nuclear residues and free nucleons, but the production of hypernuclei saturates at high energies, making intermediate-energy accelerator facilities the optimal experimental approach.

## Method

The authors combined the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) transport model with a coalescence of baryons (CB) model to simulate the dynamical stage of Au+Au and C+C collisions, followed by the Dubna Cascade Model (DCM) for participant baryon generation and a statistical Fermi-break-up (FB) model generalized for hypernuclei to describe the de-excitation of hot coalescence clusters. The yields and rapidity distributions of normal fragments and hyper-fragments with one captured Λ hyperon were calculated integrated over all impact parameters.

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies by Botvina and collaborators (2007, 2011, 2013, 2015, 2017) and Buyukcizmeci and collaborators (2013, 2018) that demonstrated the production of all kinds of hypernuclei including multi-strange and exotic ones in nuclear collisions. It also builds on experimental confirmations of light hypernuclei production by the STAR, ALICE, and Saito collaborations, and fission hypernuclei by Armstrong (1993) and Ohm (1997). The new development is the extension of the coalescence approach toward hot clusters and their de-excitation, which enables the investigation of hyperon correlations, multi-hyperon systems, and strange nuclear matter properties relevant for neutron star astrophysics.
