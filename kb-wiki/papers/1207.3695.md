# On some fundamental peculiarities of the traveling wave reactor

> On the basis of the condition for nuclear burning wave existence in the neutron-multiplicating media (U-Pu and Th-U cycles) we show the possibility of surmounting the so-called dpa-parameter problem, and suggest an algorithm of the optimal nuclear burning wave mode adjustment, which is supposed to yield the wave parameters (fluence/neutron flux, width and speed of nuclear burning wave) that satisfy the dpa-condition associated with the tolerable level of the reactor materials radioactive stability, in particular that of the cladding materials. It is shown for the first time that the capture and fission cross-sections of $^{238}$U and $^{239}$Pu increase with temperature within 1000-3000K range, which under certain conditions may lead to a global loss of the nuclear burning wave stability. Some variants of the possible stability loss due to the so-called blow-up modes (anomalous nuclear fuel temperature and neutron flow evolution) are discussed and are found to possibly become a reason for a trivial violation of the traveling wave reactor internal safety.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the dpa-parameter problem (damaging dose to cladding materials) in a traveling wave reactor can be overcome by adjusting the initial fuel composition to control wave speed, neutron flux, and fluence, but also warns that temperature-dependent increases in capture and fission cross-sections of ²³⁸U and ²³⁹Pu in the 1000–3000 K range can cause a global loss of nuclear burning wave stability, potentially violating internal safety.

## Method

The authors derive an analytical solution for a one-dimensional non-stationary reactor equation in one-group diffusion approximation with negative reactivity feedback (the Van Dam model), obtaining expressions for the amplitude, phase speed, and full width at half-maximum of the soliton-like nuclear burning wave. They then use these expressions to relate the neutron fluence and damaging dose to the wave parameters, and analyze the temperature dependence of neutron cross-sections for ²³⁸U and ²³⁹Pu to identify conditions for blow-up modes (anomalous temperature and neutron flux evolution).

## Key numbers

- Maximum phase velocity of the nuclear burning wave: 3.70 cm/day for the uranium-plutonium cycle and 0.31 cm/day for the thorium-uranium cycle, based on a fast neutron diffusion length of about 5 cm.
- Characteristic beta-decay time for the active fuel component: approximately 3.3 days for the uranium-plutonium cycle and 39.5 days for the thorium-uranium cycle.
- Damaging dose target for cladding materials: the paper aims to keep the fast neutron damaging dose below about 200 dpa, compared to 93 dpa for the Russian BN-800 reactor and a research target of 133 to 164 dpa by 2020.
- Temperature range for cross-section increase: 1000–3000 K, where capture and fission cross-sections of ²³⁸U and ²³⁹Pu are shown to increase.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of traveling wave reactors originally proposed by Feoktistov (1990s) and independently by Teller, Ishikawa, and Wood (1990s), and builds on the one-dimensional autowave model of Van Dam (2000) and the three-dimensional perturbation analysis of Chen and Maschek (2005). The paper addresses the practical technological challenge of high damaging dose (over 20% burn-up leading to about 500 dpa) that has been considered a principal obstacle to TWR realization, and introduces the concept of temperature-driven blow-up modes as a previously unrecognized threat to internal safety.
