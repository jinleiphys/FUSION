# Comparative Study of Quarkonium Transport in Hot QCD Matter

> This document summarizes the efforts of the EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force on "Suppression and (re)generation of quarkonium in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC", centered around their 2019 and 2022 meetings. It provides a review of existing experimental results and theoretical approaches, including lattice QCD calculations and semiclassical and quantum approaches for the dynamical evolution of quarkonia in the quark-gluon plasma as probed in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. The key ingredients of the transport models are itemized to facilitate comparisons of calculated quantities such as reaction rates, binding energies, and nuclear modification factors. A diagnostic assessment of the various results is attempted and coupled with an outlook for the future.

## Key claim

The paper provides a comparative review of several theoretical approaches to quarkonium transport in hot QCD matter, but it does not claim a single definitive model; instead, it itemizes the key ingredients of each approach (reaction rates, medium assumptions, regeneration mechanisms, etc.) to facilitate diagnostic comparisons of calculated quantities such as reaction rates, binding energies, and nuclear modification factors, while noting that no lattice QCD constraints are used in some models and that the range of applicability is established by effective field theory power counting or phenomenological fits.

## Method

The document reviews and compares four distinct theoretical frameworks for quarkonium transport in heavy-ion collisions: the Santiago comover interaction model (CIM), the Duke-MIT approach, the Munich-KSU approach, and the Nantes model. Each framework is described in terms of its underlying formalism (Boltzmann transport equations, open quantum systems with pNRQCD effective field theory, or quantum master equations in the quantum Brownian motion regime), its treatment of dissociation and recombination, its medium evolution model (Bjorken expansion, 2+1D viscous hydrodynamics, or 3+1D anisotropic hydrodynamics), and its constraints from lattice QCD, proton-nucleus data, and cold nuclear matter effects.

## Key numbers

- The Santiago CIM uses an effective temperature \(T_{\rm eff} \simeq 200\)-300 MeV and a phase space parameter \(n \simeq 0.5\)-2, fitted to CMS and ATLAS p-Pb data.
- The Munich-KSU approach uses a bottom quark mass \(M_b = M_{\Upsilon(1S)} / 2 = 4.73\) GeV, a Bohr radius \(a_0 = 0.678\) GeV\(^{-1}\), and a Coulombic binding energy \(|E| = 460\) MeV; the evolution is not extended below \(T_f = 190\) MeV or above \(T \sim 500\) MeV.
- The Nantes model reproduces the mass spectra of charmonium and bottomonium states with a precision of 20 MeV; it uses bare masses of 1.47 GeV for c quarks and 4.88 GeV for b quarks.
- The Duke-MIT approach uses a 2+1D viscous hydrodynamic evolution (VISHNU) calibrated against light particle production, and EPPS16 nPDF parameterization for cold nuclear matter effects.

## Context

This paper extends the work of the EMMI Rapid Reaction Task Force meetings in 2019 and 2022, providing a systematic comparison of existing theoretical approaches to quarkonium suppression and regeneration in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC. It builds on prior work by Capella et al. (1996-2005) for the comover interaction model, by Yao et al. (2018-2021) and Binder et al. (2021) for the Duke-MIT approach, by Brambilla et al. (2016-2023) for the Munich-KSU approach, and by Blaizot and Escobedo (2017) and Delorme et al. (2021-2023) for the Nantes model. The comparison enables future work to identify which ingredients are most critical for describing experimental data and to guide the development of more unified transport frameworks.
