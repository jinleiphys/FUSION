# Neutrino Emissivity of Dense Stars

> The neutrino emissivity of compact stars is investigated in this work. We consider stars consisting of nuclear as well as quark matter for this purpose. Different models are used to calculate the composition of nuclear and quark matter and the neutrino emissivity. Depending on the model under consideration, the neutrino emissivity of nuclear as well as quark matter varies over a wide range. We find that for nuclear matter, the direct URCA processes are allowed for most of the relativistic models without and with strange baryons, whereas for the nonrelativistic models this shows a strong dependence on the type of nuclear interaction employed. When the direct URCA processes are allowed, the neutrino emissivity of hadronic matter is larger than that of the quark matter by several orders of magnitude. We also find that the neutrino emissivity departs from $T^6$ behavior when the temperature is larger than the difference in the Fermi momenta of the particles, participating in the neutrino-producing reactions.

## Key claim

The neutrino emissivity of dense stars depends strongly on the nuclear model used, and the direct URCA processes are allowed for most relativistic models (nonlinear Walecka, derivative scalar coupling, chiral sigma) but show strong dependence on the type of nuclear interaction for nonrelativistic models; the emissivity departs from the standard T^6 behavior when the temperature is larger than the difference in Fermi momenta of the participating particles.

## Method

The authors calculated neutrino emissivity for both hadronic and quark matter using several nuclear models: nonlinear Walecka model, derivative scalar coupling model, chiral sigma model, and nonrelativistic models (AV14+UVII, UV14+UVII, UV14+TNI) for hadronic matter, and MIT bag model and chiral color dielectric (CCD) model for quark matter. For each model, they computed the composition (proton fractions, hyperon populations) and then evaluated the direct URCA process emissivity using exact phase space integration, comparing with the standard approximate formula that assumes T^6 scaling.

## Key numbers

- The critical proton fraction for direct URCA processes is 1/9 (from Pethick 1992), requiring proton Fermi momentum larger than 0.5 times neutron Fermi momentum.
- For relativistic models, the required proton fraction is attained above approximately 2 times nuclear matter density (rho_0, where rho_0 = 0.15 fm^{-3}).
- At T = 0.5 MeV, neutrino emissivity from DSC and CS model matter is higher by a factor of 2-4 than other hadronic models up to density 1.0 fm^{-3}.
- The neutrino emissivity from CCD model is approximately one order of magnitude higher than from the bag model, with the same strong coupling constant alpha_s = 0.08.
- The universal fitting function for the ratio of approximate to exact emissivity uses coefficients a = -2.5, b = 100, c = 30, with an overall multiplicative factor of 1.5 for hadronic matter relative to quark matter.
- The nonrelativistic model UV14+UVII is the only nonrelativistic combination that produces nonzero neutrino emissivity.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of neutrino cooling in neutron stars, particularly the work of Lattimer (1991) on direct URCA processes in nuclear matter and Iwamoto (1980) on quark matter neutrino emissivity. It also builds on the authors' previous work (Ghosh, Phatak, Sahu, 1995) on quark matter emissivity where they found the approximate T^6 formula fails for certain Fermi momenta. The paper enables a systematic comparison of relativistic versus nonrelativistic nuclear models and quark models for neutron star cooling, showing that direct URCA processes in hadronic matter can provide rapid cooling without requiring a phase transition to quark matter.
