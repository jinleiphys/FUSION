# On the normalisation of the cosmic star formation history

> Strong constraints on the cosmic star formation history (SFH) have recently been established using ultraviolet and far-infrared measurements, refining the results of numerous measurements over the past decade. Taken together, the most recent and robust data indicate a compellingly consistent picture of the SFH out to redshift z~6, with especially tight constraints for z<~1. We fit these data with simple analytical forms, and derive conservative bands to indicate possible variations from the best fits. Since the z<~1 SFH data are quite precise, we investigate the sequence of assumptions and corrections that together affect the SFH normalisation, to test their accuracy, both in this redshift range and beyond. As lower limits on this normalisation, we consider the evolution in stellar mass density, metal mass density, and supernova rate density, finding it unlikely that the SFH normalisation is much lower than indicated by our direct fit. Additionally, predictions from the SFH for supernova type Ia rate densities tentatively suggests delay times of ~3 Gyr. As a corresponding upper limit on the SFH normalisation, we consider the Super-Kamiokande (SK) limit on the electron antineutrino flux from past core-collapse supernovae, which applies primarily to z<~1. We find consistency with the SFH only if the neutrino temperatures from SN events are relatively modest. Constraints on the assumed initial mass function (IMF) also become apparent. The traditional Salpeter IMF, assumed for convenience by many authors, is known to be a poor representation at low stellar masses (<~ 1 solar mass), and we show that recently favoured IMFs are also constrained. In particular, somewhat shallow, or top-heavy, IMFs may be preferred, although they cannot be too top-heavy. (Abridged)

# On the normalisation of the cosmic star formation history

## Key claim

The cosmic star formation history (SFH) is tightly constrained out to redshift z~6, but the normalisation of the SFH at z<~1 is subject to a sequence of assumptions and corrections whose accuracy the authors test systematically; the SFH normalisation cannot be much lower than the direct fit indicates (based on lower limits from stellar mass density, metal mass density, and supernova rate density) and cannot be much higher (based on the Super-Kamiokande limit on the electron antineutrino flux from past core-collapse supernovae), with consistency requiring relatively modest neutrino temperatures and favouring somewhat top-heavy initial mass functions (IMFs) that are not too top-heavy.

## Method

The authors compiled SFH measurements from ultraviolet, far-infrared, H-alpha, and radio data out to z~6, applying obscuration corrections via a UV+FIR technique for z<~1 and a common obscuration correction at higher redshifts. They performed chi-squared fits of the parametric form of Cole (2001) to 58 selected star formation rate density measurements, then converted the fitted SFH into a predicted type II supernova rate history and diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB) flux for comparison with the Super-Kamiokande limit of 1.2 cm^-2 s^-1 (for E_nu > 19.3 MeV), exploring two extreme IMF assumptions (the Baldry-Glazebrook IMF with high-mass slope -1.15 and the modified Salpeter A IMF with slope -1.35) and three neutrino temperatures (4, 6, and 8 MeV).

## Key numbers

- The best-fitting Cole (2001) parameters for the SalA IMF are a=0.0170, b=0.13, c=3.3, d=5.3; for both the SalA and BG IMFs with T=4 MeV, the best-fitting chi-squared is 37.5 with 54 degrees of freedom.
- The scale factor to convert SFH measurements from a Salpeter IMF to the BG IMF is 0.50 (-0.305 dex); to the SalA IMF it is 0.77 (-0.114 dex).
- For the BG IMF, the SNII rate conversion factor is 0.0132/M_sun; for the SalA IMF it is 0.00915/M_sun; the combined effect gives BG IMF SNII rates that are a factor of 0.94 of those from the SalA IMF.
- The Super-Kamiokande limit on the electron antineutrino flux is 1.2 cm^-2 s^-1 for E_nu > 19.3 MeV.
- The obscuration correction to UV data increases from a factor of two at z~0 to a factor of five at z>~1.
- The SFH data at z<~1 are constrained to within approximately 30-50%; at z>~1 the SFH is determined within a factor of about 3 up to z~6.

## Context

This work extends the SFH data compilation of Hopkins (2004) with new measurements from SDSS, GALEX, COMBO17, Spitzer, and the Hubble Ultra Deep Field, and builds on the DSNB constraints from Fukugita & Kawasaki (2003), Ando (2004), Strigari (2004, 2005), and the Super-Kamiokande limit from Malek (2003). The analysis enables quantitative constraints on the IMF, favouring somewhat top-heavy IMFs over the traditional Salpeter (1955) IMF, and provides a framework for future work on the mass-dependence of the SFH and the reasons for the decline in the SFH to low redshifts, while calling for improved data on supernova rate density evolution, the stellar mass ranges producing core-collapse and type Ia supernovae, and the neutrino backgrounds from core-collapse supernovae.
