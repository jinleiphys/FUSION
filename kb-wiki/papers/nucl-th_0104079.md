# Quantum tunneling and the principle of relativity

> It is shown that the results of Buttiker and Landauer on the traversal time of quantum tunneling through a potential barrier are in agreement with the principle of relativity. Also, they are consistent with the data on the life-time of nuclear particles that decay in flight. PACS number: 03.30, Special Relativity

## Key claim
The paper argues that the Buttiker-Landauer traversal time for quantum tunneling is consistent with the principle of relativity, provided the tunneling velocity never exceeds the speed of light. The author further claims that this traversal time can be related to the lifetime of particles decaying in flight, but only under the assumption that the barrier width equals the particle's characteristic length.

## Method
The author starts from the Buttiker-Landauer traversal time formula for a rectangular potential barrier, given by an integral over the classically forbidden region. This expression is then transformed using a relativistic factor under the assumption that the tunneling velocity approaches the speed of light. The resulting equation is compared to the relativistic time dilation formula for particles decaying in flight, and the barrier width is estimated from known particle lifetimes. The analysis is extended to time-dependent barriers modulated at a frequency characteristic of strong interactions, estimating the resulting energy sidebands.

## Key numbers
- The estimated barrier width for muon decay (lifetime approximately 2.2 × 10⁻⁶ s) is about 6.6 × 10⁴ cm.
- The estimated barrier width for charged pion decay (lifetime approximately 2.6 × 10⁻⁸ s) is about 7.8 × 10² cm.
- The estimated barrier width for neutral pion decay (lifetime approximately 0.8 × 10⁻¹⁶ s) is about 2.4 × 10⁻⁶ cm.
- The estimated energy change for tunneling particles at n=1, using a modulation frequency of about 10²³ s⁻¹, is approximately 66 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the analysis of Buttiker and Landauer (1982) on traversal time in quantum tunneling, as well as the review by Landauer and Martin (1994) on different definitions of traversal time. The author also draws on prior estimates of the strong-interaction potential barrier height from Dermendjiev (1999, 2000). The paper suggests that particle decay in flight could be reinterpreted as a tunneling process, and that modulated barriers might provide a mechanism for accelerating strongly interacting particles, which could be tested experimentally.
