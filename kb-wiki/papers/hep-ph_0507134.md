# Thoughts on Non-Perturbative Thermalization and Jet Quenching in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We start by presenting physical arguments for the impossibility of perturbative thermalization leading to (non-viscous) Bjorken hydrodynamic description of heavy ion collisions. These arguments are complimentary to our more formal argument presented in [hep-ph/0503038]. We argue that the success of hydrodynamic models in describing the quark-gluon system produced in heavy ion collisions could only be due to non-perturbative strong coupling effects. We continue by studying non-perturbative effects in heavy ion collisions at high energies. We model non-perturbative phenomena by an instanton ensemble. We show that non-perturbative instanton vacuum fields may significantly contribute to jet quenching in nuclear collisions. At the same time, the instanton ensemble contribution to thermalization is likely to be rather weak, leading to non-perturbative thermalization time comparable to the time of hadronization. This example illustrates that jet quenching is not necessarily a signal of a thermalized medium. Indeed, since the instanton models do not capture all the effects of QCD vacuum (e.g. they do not account for confinement), there may be other non-perturbative effects facilitating thermalization of the system.

## Key claim

Perturbative QCD cannot produce the thermalized, hydrodynamically evolving quark-gluon medium assumed in ideal Bjorken hydrodynamics for heavy ion collisions; any successful thermalization must arise from non-perturbative strong-coupling effects, and jet quenching can occur without thermalization, as illustrated by an instanton-vacuum model.

## Method

The author presents two qualitative arguments against perturbative thermalization: (1) in Bjorken hydrodynamics with weak coupling, the perturbative mean free path remains smaller than the system size at all times, preventing the free-streaming final state required by Feynman diagrams; (2) the zero-coupling limit of QCD gives energy density scaling as epsilon ~ 1/tau, while the zero-coupling limit of ideal Bjorken hydrodynamics gives epsilon ~ 1/tau^(4/3), showing they are incompatible. The author then models non-perturbative effects using an instanton ensemble, calculating the instanton density modified by the strong classical gluon fields of the Color Glass Condensate, and estimates parton energy loss and thermalization time from parton-instanton interactions.

## Key numbers

* The thermalization time required by RHIC phenomenology is tau less than or approximately 0.5 fm/c.
* For an ideal gas equation of state (epsilon = 3p), Bjorken hydrodynamics gives energy density scaling as epsilon ~ 1/tau^(4/3).
* Free-streaming (non-interacting) quarks and gluons give energy density scaling as epsilon ~ 1/tau.
* The perturbative shear viscosity for QCD with two light flavors is eta = 86.473 T^3 / [g^4 ln(1/g)], which diverges in the g -> 0 limit.
* The vacuum instanton density n_0(rho) is proportional to 1/rho^5 times exp(-2 pi / alpha_s(rho)) and other factors given in Eq. (isize).

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier formal proof (Kovchegov, 2005, hep-ph/0503038) that perturbative thermalization is impossible in heavy ion collisions, providing complementary physical arguments. It challenges the "bottom-up" thermalization scenario (Baier, Mueller, Schiff, Son, 2001) and subsequent work on plasma instabilities (Arnold, Lenaghan, Moore, 2003; others), which attempted to achieve perturbative thermalization. The instanton-vacuum model used here follows the instanton ensemble approach of Shuryak, Diakonov, Petrov, and others; the energy-loss formula applied is that of Baier, Dokshitzer, Mueller, Peigne, and Schiff (1997) and Zakharov (1997). The paper enables future work to search for other non-perturbative mechanisms (such as QCD strings) that could provide both thermalization and jet quenching, while cautioning that jet quenching alone is not evidence of a thermalized medium.
