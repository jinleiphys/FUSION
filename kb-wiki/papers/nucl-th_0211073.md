# A National Underground Science Laboratory in the U.S.

## Key claim
A single deep multi-purpose underground laboratory in the U.S. is needed to meet the needs of diverse groups in physics, earth science, geomicrobiology, and applied science, but the choice between the Homestake and San Jacinto sites involves trade-offs in access, timing, and ownership that had not been resolved as of late 2002.

## Method
The paper reviews recent underground science results and summarizes the process of site evaluation by the Bahcall Committee, which compared U.S. candidate sites (Carlsbad/WIPP, Soudan, Homestake, San Jacinto, and greenfield sites) against international facilities. The committee identified Homestake and San Jacinto as the two primary deep candidates based on depth and scientific potential, and the paper describes the advantages and issues of each.

## Key numbers
- Carlsbad (WIPP) depth: 1600–2000 meters water equivalent (mwe)
- Soudan depth: about 2000 mwe
- Homestake depth: up to 7200 mwe
- San Jacinto depth: 5400–6300 mwe
- Greenfield sites (CA and NV) depth: 5000–8000 mwe
- The pattern of neutrino masses emerging from experiments implies new physics at an energy scale ~10^15 GeV

## Context
This paper extends the discussion following the 2000 Seattle meeting of neutrino physicists and the subsequent Bahcall Committee report, which rated creation of a deep underground laboratory as the highest priority for the community. It also references the National Research Council Committee on the Physics of the Universe (2002), which listed eleven questions where underground science is crucial. The paper enables future decisions by federal funding agencies, with an NRC committee expected to release its final report by the end of 2002.
