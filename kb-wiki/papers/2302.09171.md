# Light front synchronization and rest frame densities of the proton: Electromagnetic densities

> We clarify the physical origin and meaning of the two-dimensional relativistic densities of the light front formalism. The densities are shown to originate entirely from the use of light front time instead of instant form time, which physically corresponds to using an alternative synchronization convention. This is shown by using tilted light front coordinates, which consist of light front time and ordinary spatial coordinates, and which are also used to show that the obtained densities describe a system at rest rather than at infinite momentum. These coordinates allow all four components of the electromagnetic current density to be given clear physical meanings. We explicate the formalism for spin-half targets, obtaining charge and current densities of the proton and neutron using empirical form factor parametrizations, as well as up and down quark densities and currents. Angular modulations in the densities of transversely-polarized states are explained as originating from redshifts and blueshifts due to quarks moving in different longitudinal directions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the two-dimensional relativistic densities obtained from the light front formalism are not kinematic distortions from boosting to infinite momentum, but instead arise purely from choosing an alternative time synchronization convention (light front synchronization) rather than the standard Einstein synchronization. The densities describe a system at rest when using tilted light front coordinates, contrary to the widespread belief that they describe a system moving at the speed of light.

## Method

The authors introduce tilted light front coordinates, which use light front time \(x^+ = t + z\) together with ordinary spatial coordinates \((x, y, z)\), and show that these coordinates correspond to an anisotropic clock synchronization convention where light travels instantaneously in the \(-z\) direction. They derive the properties of these coordinates, including the metric, mass-shell relation, velocity formulas, and Lorentz transformation properties, then construct wave packets and probability currents for spin-half systems. Using these tools, they derive formulas for intrinsic electromagnetic densities and apply them to empirical proton and neutron form factor parametrizations to obtain charge and current densities, as well as up and down quark densities.

## Key numbers

- The one-way speed of light in the \(+z\) direction under light front synchronization is \(c/2\), while the two-way speed remains the invariant constant \(c\)
- A free particle is at rest in tilted coordinates when \(\tilde{p}_x = 0\), \(\tilde{p}_y = 0\), and \(\tilde{p}_z = m\)
- Under a longitudinal boost of rapidity \(\eta\), the tilted time transforms as \(\tilde{\tau}' = e^{-\eta} \tilde{\tau}\) and the \(z\) coordinate transforms as \(\tilde{z}' = e^{\eta} \tilde{z} - \sinh(\eta) \tilde{\tau}\)
- The longitudinal velocity of a boosted object is \(v_z = \frac{1}{2}(1 - e^{2\eta})\), bounded to \((-\infty, 1/2)\)

## Context

This work extends the light front density formalism originally developed by Burkardt (2002) and Miller (2010) by addressing two persistent criticisms: that light front densities describe systems at infinite momentum rather than at rest, and that the "minus" components of four-vector densities lack clear physical meaning. The authors build on the tilted coordinate approach first explored by Blunden, Burkardt, and Miller (1999) and provide a synchronization-based interpretation that resolves these issues. This enables future application to the energy-momentum tensor (planned as a sequel work) and supports the spatial tomography program of the upcoming Electron Ion Collider.
