# Incompressibility in finite nuclei and nuclear matter

> The incompressibility (compression modulus) $K_{\rm 0}$ of infinite symmetric nuclear matter at saturation density has become one of the major constraints on mean-field models of nuclear many-body systems as well as of models of high density matter in astrophysical objects and heavy-ion collisions. We present a comprehensive re-analysis of recent data on GMR energies in even-even $^{\rm 112-124}$Sn and $^{\rm 106,100-116}$Cd and earlier data on 58 $\le$ A $\le$ 208 nuclei. The incompressibility of finite nuclei $K_{\rm A}$ is expressed as a leptodermous expansion with volume, surface, isospin and Coulomb coefficients $K_{\rm vol}$, $K_{\rm surf}$, $K_τ$ and $K_{\rm coul}$. \textit{Assuming} that the volume coefficient $K_{\rm vol}$ is identified with $K_{\rm 0}$, the $K_{\rm coul}$ = -(5.2 $\pm$ 0.7) MeV and the contribution from the curvature term K$_{\rm curv}$A$^{\rm -2/3}$ in the expansion is neglected, compelling evidence is found for $K_{\rm 0}$ to be in the range 250 $ < K_{\rm 0} < $ 315 MeV, the ratio of the surface and volume coefficients $c = K_{\rm surf}/K_{\rm vol}$ to be between -2.4 and -1.6 and $K_{\rm τ}$ between -840 and -350 MeV. We show that the generally accepted value of $K_{\rm 0}$ = (240 $\pm$ 20) MeV can be obtained from the fits provided $c \sim$ -1, as predicted by the majority of mean-field models. However, the fits are significantly improved if $c$ is allowed to vary, leading to a range of $K_{\rm 0}$, extended to higher values. A self-consistent simple (toy) model has been developed, which shows that the density dependence of the surface diffuseness of a vibrating nucleus plays a major role in determination of the ratio K$_{\rm surf}/K_{\rm vol}$ and yields predictions consistent with our findings.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the generally accepted value of the incompressibility of infinite symmetric nuclear matter, K0 = (240 ± 20) MeV, can be obtained from fits to experimental giant monopole resonance (GMR) data only if the ratio of surface to volume coefficients c = Ksurf/Kvol is fixed near -1, as predicted by most mean-field models; however, allowing c to vary freely yields significantly improved fits and extends the allowed range of K0 to 250 < K0 < 315 MeV.

## Method

The authors extracted the incompressibility of finite nuclei KA from experimental GMR energies using the scaling approximation, which ensures a fast-converging leptodermous expansion in powers of A-1/3 and the asymmetry parameter β = (N-Z)/A. They performed a comprehensive re-analysis of GMR data for even-even 112-124Sn and 106,100-116Cd isotopes, combined with earlier data on nuclei with 58 ≤ A ≤ 208, fitting the volume, surface, isospin, and Coulomb coefficients (Kvol, Ksurf, Kτ, and Kcoul) while neglecting the curvature term. A self-consistent toy model was developed to study the density dependence of surface diffuseness in vibrating nuclei and its effect on the ratio Ksurf/Kvol.

## Key numbers

- K0 is found in the range 250 < K0 < 315 MeV when the ratio c = Ksurf/Kvol is allowed to vary between -2.4 and -1.6.
- The Coulomb coefficient is Kcoul = -(5.2 ± 0.7) MeV.
- The isospin incompressibility coefficient Kτ lies between -840 and -350 MeV.
- The generally accepted value K0 = (240 ± 20) MeV is reproduced only when c ≈ -1, as predicted by most mean-field models.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing effort to determine nuclear incompressibility from GMR data, which began with Blaizot et al. (1976) who obtained K0 = (210 ± 30) MeV using data on 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb. The paper directly addresses the tension between non-relativistic models (predicting K0 around 210-250 MeV, e.g., Blaizot et al. 1995, Colo et al. 2004) and relativistic models (predicting higher values, e.g., Sharma et al. 1988, Vretenar et al. 1997). The results enable future work to constrain effective interactions by requiring them to reproduce not only K0 but also the surface-to-volume ratio c and the isospin coefficient Kτ, and the toy model provides a framework for understanding the role of surface diffuseness in nuclear vibrations.
