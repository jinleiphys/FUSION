# Echos of the liquid-gas phase transition in multifragmentation

> A general discussion is made concerning the ways in which one can get signatures about a possible liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter. Microcanonical temperature, heat capacity and second order derivative of the entropy versus energy formulas have been deduced in a general case. These formulas are {\em exact}, simply applicable and do not depend on any model assumption. Therefore, they are suitable to be applied on experimental data. The formulas are tested in various situations. It is evidenced that when the freeze-out constraint is of fluctuating volume type the deduced (heat capacity and second order derivative of the entropy versus energy) formulas will prompt the spinodal region through specific signals. Finally, the same microcanonical formulas are deduced for the case when an incomplete number of fragments per event are available. These formulas could overcome the freeze-out backtracking deficiencies.

## Key claim

The paper presents exact, model-independent microcanonical formulas for temperature, heat capacity, and the second derivative of entropy with respect to energy, which can reveal the spinodal region of a liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear multifragmentation even when the experimental path in the (E,V) plane does not produce a backbending caloric curve, provided the freeze-out constraint is of the fluctuating volume (constant λ) type.

## Method

The authors derive microcanonical formulas for temperature, heat capacity, and ∂²S/∂E² starting from the density of states for a classical N-particle system with various conservation levels (energy only; energy and momentum; energy, momentum, and angular momentum). They test these formulas by comparing them with results extracted from the probability distributions of a canonical ensemble at constant pressure, using a sharp microcanonical multifragmentation model with two freeze-out hypotheses (hard-sphere and simplified Wigner-Seitz). The formulas are applied to systems with and without Coulomb interaction, under constant volume and constant λ constraints, and are also extended to the case where only an incomplete number of fragments per event are detected.

## Key numbers

- For the nucleus (50,23) with constant λ = 5 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁻³ and Coulomb interaction included (model version ii), the microcanonical formulas produce a negative branch in the heat capacity curve and a positive region in ∂²S/∂E², indicating a first-order phase transition.
- For the nucleus (200,82) with constant λ = 3 × 10⁻³ fm⁻³ and no Coulomb interaction (model version ii), the caloric curve shows a backbending, reflected in negative heat capacity and positive ∂²S/∂E².
- For the nucleus (200,82) with constant volume V = 10 V₀ and Coulomb interaction included (model version i), the caloric curve is monotonic with a "plateau-like" region; the heat capacity remains positive (with a peak) and ∂²S/∂E² is negative, yielding no conclusion about a phase transition.
- For the nucleus (200,82) without Coulomb interaction, using a λ(E) path corresponding to the microcanonical λ at V = 10 V₀, the caloric curve is monotonically increasing, yet the heat capacity shows a negative branch and ∂²S/∂E² shows a positive region, evidencing the spinodal region.
- The lowest excitation energy limit for which the microcanonical formulas work for the (200,82) system with Coulomb and V = 10 V₀ lies between 1 and 2 MeV/u, determined by the condition that the K probability distribution does not intersect K = 0 at a non-negligible value.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Chomaz (2000) and Chomaz (2001), who proposed that a negative heat capacity in a fluctuating volume ensemble signals the spinodal region of a liquid-gas phase transition, but whose formula relied on unevaluated terms (microcanonical temperature and partial heat capacity) requiring additional assumptions. The present paper provides exact, model-independent formulas that avoid those approximations, and tests them against the sharp microcanonical multifragmentation model of Raduta (1999). The results enable experimental application to multifragmentation data, such as those from the MULTICS-MINIBALL collaboration (D'Agostino, 2000) and INDRA (D'Agostino, 2002), by offering a robust method to identify the spinodal region even with incomplete fragment detection.
