# Effect of magnetic field on the strange star

> We study the effect of a magnetic field on the strage quark matter and apply to strange star. We found that the strange star becomes more compact in presence of strong magnetic field.

## Key claim

The presence of a strong magnetic field makes a strange star more compact, reducing both its mass and radius, but this conclusion depends on the magnetic field strength and whether the effect on s-quarks is included; the oscillatory behavior of the equation of state due to Landau levels does not affect the characteristic structure of the star.

## Method

The authors model strange quark matter using the MIT bag model with non-interacting quarks (u, d, s) and electrons, imposing charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium. They compute the thermodynamic potential and number density for each species in a constant magnetic field along the z-axis, using the Landau level formalism at zero temperature, and then derive the equation of state (pressure vs. energy density). The mass and radius of nonrotating strange stars are obtained by numerically integrating the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) structure equations for a relativistic spherical static star.

## Key numbers

- For a low magnetic field of \(H = 10^{14}\) G, the strangeness fraction (\(n_s/n_B\)) shows no oscillatory behavior; for a high field of \(H = 10^{18}\) G, it oscillates as consecutive Landau levels pass the Fermi level.
- The critical magnetic field for an electron (mass 0.5 MeV) is \(\sim 4.4 \times 10^{13}\) G; for a light quark (current mass 5 MeV) it is \(\sim 4.4 \times 10^{15}\) G; for an s-quark (current mass 150 MeV) it is \(\sim 10^{19}\) G.
- The bag pressure \(B\) is set to 56 MeV fm\(^{-3}\); current quark masses are 5 MeV for u and d quarks, and 150 MeV for the s-quark.
- With increasing magnetic field, the mass of the strange star decreases from 2.26 to 1.86 solar masses, and the radius decreases from 11 km to 10 km.
- The central density increases with magnetic field; surface red shift, moment of inertia, and fundamental period all decrease.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of strange quark matter stability by Farhi and Jaffe (1984) and Alcock et al. (1986) by incorporating the effect of strong magnetic fields, which had been considered for neutron stars but not systematically for strange stars. The authors note that previous work by Chakrabarty (1995) ignored the quantum effect of magnetic fields on s-quarks, but here they consider both cases. The results enable more realistic modeling of compact star structure under extreme magnetic fields, relevant to observations of pulsars and magnetars with surface fields up to \(10^{13}\) G and interior fields possibly reaching \(10^{18}\) G.
