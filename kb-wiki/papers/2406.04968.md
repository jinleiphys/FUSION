# Shear viscosity from perturbative Quantum Chromodynamics to the hadron resonance gas at finite baryon, strangeness, and electric charge densities

> Through model-to-data comparisons from heavy-ion collisions, it has been shown that the Quark Gluon Plasma has an extremely small shear viscosity at vanishing densities. At large baryon densities, significantly less is known about the nature of the shear viscosity from Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD). Within heavy-ion collisions, there are three conserved charges: baryon number (B), strangeness (S), and electric charge (Q). Here we calculate the shear viscosity in two limits using perturbative QCD and an excluded-volume hadron resonance gas at finite BSQ densities. We then develop a framework that interpolates between these two limits such that shear viscosity is possible to calculate across a wide range of finite BSQ densities. We find that the pQCD and hadron resonance gas calculations have different BSQ densities dependence such that a rather non-trivial shear viscosity appears at finite densities.

## Key claim

The authors calculate the shear viscosity normalized by temperature over enthalpy (ηT/w) at finite baryon, strangeness, and electric charge densities using two limiting calculations, perturbative QCD at high temperatures and an excluded-volume hadron resonance gas at low temperatures, and then interpolate between them, finding that the three chemical potentials produce qualitatively different dependencies that are not captured by considering baryon chemical potential alone.

## Method

The shear viscosity is computed in the high-temperature limit using leading-logarithmic-order perturbative QCD with massless u, d, and s quarks, extending the variational method of Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2000, 2003) and Danhoni and Moore (2022) to include separate chemical potentials for baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge. In the low-temperature limit, the shear viscosity is calculated from kinetic theory using an excluded-volume hadron resonance gas model with a single hard-core radius for all hadrons, employing the PDG2021+ particle list via the Thermal-FIST package. The two limits are connected by an interpolation framework that uses comparisons to lattice QCD to determine switching temperatures between the HRG, an intermediate regime, and pQCD.

## Key numbers

* η/s at vanishing baryon density from Bayesian analyses is approximately 0.08–0.2 (no units, ratio).
* For heavy nuclei used in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, Z/A ranges from 0.38 to 0.45 (dimensionless ratio).
* Under strangeness neutrality, μ_S ≈ μ_B/3 (dimensionless ratio of chemical potentials).
* Under electric charge conservation, μ_Q ≈ −μ_B/10 (dimensionless ratio of chemical potentials).
* The pQCD calculation shows that ηT/w increases with increasing μ_S/T but decreases with increasing μ_Q/T when the other chemical potentials are set to zero (no numerical values given for the magnitude of change).
* For the strangeness neutral trajectory with Z/A = 0.4, the non-monotonic behavior of ηT/w seen for finite μ_B alone disappears, and ηT/w only decreases with increasing μ_B (qualitative result, no numerical values).

## Context

This work extends previous pQCD calculations of shear viscosity at finite baryon density by Danhoni and Moore (2022) and HRG calculations by McLaughlin et al. (2021) to include all three conserved charges (baryon number, strangeness, electric charge) relevant for heavy-ion collisions. The results enable future hydrodynamic simulations that require a four-dimensional description of shear viscosity as a function of temperature and the three chemical potentials, which is necessary for modeling systems with local fluctuations of conserved charges as discussed by Carzon et al. (2019) and Plumberg et al. (2023, 2024). The framework is designed to accommodate either the presence or absence of a QCD critical point, unlike approaches that assume a specific critical behavior.
