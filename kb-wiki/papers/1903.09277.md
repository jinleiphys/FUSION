# Multimessenger Universe with Gravitational Waves from Binaries

> Future GW detector networks and EM observatories will provide a unique opportunity to observe the most luminous events in the Universe involving matter in extreme environs. They will address some of the key questions in physics and astronomy: formation and evolution of compact binaries, sites of formation of heavy elements and the physics of jets.

## Key claim

Future gravitational wave detector networks (3G) combined with electromagnetic observatories will enable multimessenger observations of binary neutron star and neutron star-black hole mergers out to high redshifts, but the ability to identify host galaxies and characterize kilonovae will be routine only out to z<0.5 without a gamma-ray burst jet pointing toward Earth.

## Method

The paper presents a forward-looking assessment of the scientific capabilities of next-generation gravitational wave detector networks (specifically a network of one Einstein Telescope and two Cosmic Explorer detectors, denoted 1ET+2CE) compared to current advanced detectors (HLV and HLVKI). It simulates expected detection rates and localization accuracies for binary neutron star mergers using a Madau-Dickinson star formation rate with an exponential time delay of 100 Myr e-fold time and a local co-moving merger rate of 1000 Gpc^-3 yr^-1. The analysis projects the number of events detectable per year and their localization precision in square degrees, then evaluates the scientific reach for demographics, nucleosynthesis, jet physics, cosmology, and multi-band gravitational wave astronomy with LISA.

## Key numbers

* 1ET+2CE network is projected to detect approximately 990,000 binary neutron star mergers per year, compared to 48 per year for the current HLV network
* Of those 990,000 events, about 14,000 would be localized to better than 1 square degree, 410,000 to better than 10 square degrees, and 970,000 to better than 100 square degrees
* The median localization error for 1ET+2CE is 12 square degrees, compared to 19 square degrees for HLV and 7 square degrees for HLVKI
* Optical kilonovae will be detectable by LSST out to 3 Gpc, while infrared characterization with JWST/GMT/TMT/E-ELT would be possible out to 1 Gpc (z<0.2)
* About 10 compact binary neutron star or neutron star-black hole mergers with electromagnetic counterparts are required to reach Hubble constant measurements at the 5% level, and 200 to reach 1%
* LISA would provide eccentricity measurements to a precision of Delta e < 0.001 and sky localization to 0.1 square degrees for stellar-mass black hole binaries

## Context

This white paper extends the science case established by the discovery of GW170817 (The LIGO Scientific Collaboration, 2017), which provided the first multimessenger observation of a binary neutron star merger and demonstrated connections to short gamma-ray bursts (Goldstein, 2017) and r-process nucleosynthesis (Abbott, 2017). The paper builds on prior work on gravitational wave cosmology (Schutz, 1986; Abbott, 2017; Sathyaprakash, 2009) and chemical evolution studies of r-process elements (Lattimer, 1974; Ji, 2015). It enables a roadmap for future detector networks to address open questions about compact binary formation channels, the role of neutron star mergers in heavy element production, jet physics, and multi-band gravitational wave astronomy with LISA.
