# What can we learn about the unstable equation-of-state branch from neutron-star mergers?

> The Equation of State (EOS) of dense strongly-interacting matter can be probed by astrophysical observations of neutron stars (NS), such as X-ray detections of pulsars or the measurement of the tidal deformability of NSs during the inspiral stage of NS mergers. These observations constrain the EOS at most up to the density of the maximum-mass configuration, $n_\textrm{TOV}$, which is the highest density that can be explored by stable NSs for a given EOS. However, under the right circumstances, binary neutron star (BNS) mergers can create a postmerger remnant that explores densities above $n_\textrm{TOV}$. In this work, we explore whether the EOS above $n_\textrm{TOV}$ can be measured from gravitational-wave or electromagnetic observations of the postmerger remnant. We perform a total of twenty-five numerical-relativity simulations of BNS mergers for a range of EOSs and find no case in which different descriptions of the matter above $n_{\rm TOV}$ have a detectable impact on postmerger observables. Hence, we conclude that the EOS above $n_\textrm{TOV}$ can likely not be probed through BNS merger observations for the current and next generation of detectors.

## Key claim

The paper systematically investigates whether the equation of state (EOS) at densities above the maximum stable neutron-star density (n_TOV) can be measured from binary neutron star merger observations, and concludes that it likely cannot be probed through current or next-generation detectors, because in all 25 numerical-relativity simulations performed, different descriptions of matter above n_TOV had no detectable impact on postmerger observables.

## Method

The authors performed 25 numerical-relativity simulations of binary neutron star mergers using the BAM code, employing three different EOSs (SLy, MPA1, H4) for the stable branch up to n_TOV, and attaching five different polytropic extensions with varying stiffness (polytropic index Gamma between 0.25 and 2.5) above n_TOV for each EOS. They analyzed the time evolution of maximum density, black hole formation time, gravitational-wave signals (extracting the Newman-Penrose scalar Psi_4 at 1200 km radius), and ejecta masses (using the geodesic criterion u_t less than -1 on a 600 km extraction sphere) to quantify the impact of the unstable EOS branch.

## Key numbers

- The three stable-branch EOSs (SLy, H4, MPA1) produce neutron star radii ranging from approximately 11.5 to 13.5 km for a typical 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- SLy and H4 EOSs have maximum masses of approximately 2 solar masses, while MPA1 has a maximum mass of approximately 2.5 solar masses
- For SLy simulations with total mass 2.73 solar masses, the difference in collapse time between different EOS extensions is about 100 microseconds, too small for detection
- For the MPA1 simulation with total mass 3.15 solar masses, the shift in gravitational-wave peak frequency between the most extreme scenarios (Gamma=0.25 and Gamma=2.50) is about 30 Hz, while previous studies show measurement uncertainties of 50 to 100 Hz for close binary neutron star mergers
- For H4 and MPA1 setups, differences in ejecta masses for various EOS extensions are below 10^{-4} solar masses
- The maximum density explored by stable neutron stars (n_TOV) is estimated at approximately 5 to 8 times nuclear saturation density

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Bauswein (2018), Most (2018), and Figura (2020) on measuring high-density EOS from neutron star mergers, but reaches a more pessimistic conclusion. The authors find that n_TOV represents a firm limit for probing the EOS with astrophysical data, and that accurate knowledge of the cold and hot EOS below n_TOV is sufficient for predicting postmerger observables such as ejecta mass and gravitational-wave waveform. The results contradict the possibility that binary neutron star mergers could probe densities beyond those already accessible in high-mass pulsars, though the authors note they cannot rule out particular combinations of source parameters or phase transitions that might produce observable effects.
