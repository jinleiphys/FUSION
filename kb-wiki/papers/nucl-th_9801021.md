# Electrons and Nuclei: Fundamental Interactions and Structure

> Examples are given of the usefulness of electrons in interaction with nuclei for probing fundamental interactions and structure

## Key claim

Electron-nucleus interactions remain a versatile tool for testing fundamental interactions (electroweak theory, CKM unitarity, time-reversal invariance) and extracting nuclear/hadron structure information (strangeness matrix elements, neutron radii, anapole moments), though many results are still limited by experimental precision or theoretical uncertainties.

## Method

The paper reviews several experimental approaches: superallowed Fermi beta decays (0+ to 0+ transitions) to test CVC and extract Vud; parity-violating electron scattering on hydrogen (SAMPLE experiment at MIT) at Q2 ≈ 0.1 GeV2 to measure the strange magnetic moment κs; atomic parity violation measurements in Cs to determine the weak charge QW and the anapole moment; and searches for time-reversal violation via beta-decay correlations and atomic electric dipole moments in 199Hg.

## Key numbers

- Vud = 0.9740 ± 0.0006 from superallowed Fermi decays; unitarity sum U = 0.9972 ± 0.0019 (or 0.9980 ± 0.0019 with smooth Z-dependent corrections)
- CVC holds to ∼ 4 × 10−4 from superallowed transition measurements
- Lower limit on 0νββ half-life for 76Ge: 3 × 1024 y
- Parity-violating asymmetry in SAMPLE at Q2 = 0.1 GeV2 yields κs Fs = 0.23 ± 0.37 (stat) ± 0.15 (syst) ± 0.19 (axial radiative corrections) n.m.
- Atomic Cs weak charge QW = −72.35 ± 0.27exp ± 0.54th
- Anapole moment in Cs: κa = 0.364 ± 0.062; derived π-N parity-violating coupling fπ = (9.5 ± 2.1exp ± 3.5th) × 10−7
- 199Hg electric dipole moment upper limit: |dE| ≤ 8.7 × 10−28 e-cm (95% CL); corresponding θ ≤ 10−10
- Limit on time-reversal-odd π-N coupling: fπT ≤ 10−11

## Context

This review extends the long tradition of using electrons to probe nuclear structure and fundamental symmetries, building on earlier tests of CVC in 12B/12N decays and the SLAC polarized electron scattering experiments that first confirmed electroweak neutral currents. The results enable future precision measurements at TJNAF and MIT to push strange magnetic moment uncertainties to ±0.1 n.m., and highlight a discrepancy between the anapole-derived fπ (∼9.5×10−7) and the 18F limit (≤1.5×10−7) that remains unresolved. The atomic parity violation work constrains possible Z' boson masses above ∼500 GeV.
