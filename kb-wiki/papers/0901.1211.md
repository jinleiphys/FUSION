# Nuclear and Particle Physics applications of the Bohm Picture of Quantum Mechanics

> Approximation methods for calculating individual particle/ field motions in spacetime at the quantum level of accuracy (a key feature of the Bohm Picture of Quantum Mechanics (BP)), are studied. Modern textbook presentations of Quantum Theory are used throughout, but only to provide the necessary, already existing, tested formalisms and calculational techniques. New coherent insights, reinterpretations of old solutions and results, and new (in principle testable) quantitative and qualitative predictions, can be obtained on the basis of the BP that complete the standard type of postdictions and predictions.

## Key claim

The Bohm Picture of Quantum Mechanics (BP) can provide new coherent insights, reinterpretations of old solutions, and in-principle testable quantitative and qualitative predictions that complete standard quantum postdictions and predictions, but the paper emphasizes that real differences from textbook quantum theory would be seen only for histories of individual systems (as distinct from ensembles), and that meaningful judgments on the status of BP require gathering sufficient data on individual particle histories.

## Method

The paper reformulates the time-dependent Schroedinger equation by writing the wavefunction as R exp(iS) and deriving coupled equations for the real functions R and S, introducing the Quantum Information Potential (QIP). It then defines Bohm's equations of motion for "beable" particles via the guidance condition p_k = gradient_k S, which yields deterministic trajectories for individual particles. The method is applied to three case studies: EPR-like correlations in alpha-decay channels of light nuclei, neutron interferometry under gravitational fields, and quantum transitions in nuclear beta-decay processes.

## Key numbers

- For the neutron interferometry case, the phase difference between two paths is given by Phi_12 = -(M_N/hbar)^2 * g * l_1 * l_2 * sin(delta) * lambda/(2*pi), where M_N is the neutron mass, g is gravitational acceleration, l_1 and l_2 are path lengths, delta is the inclination angle, and lambda is the de Broglie wavelength of the incoming wavefunction.
- For the two-alpha decay of Be-8, the Hamiltonian for t>0 is H = -(1/(2*M_alpha)) * sum_{k=1}^2 d^2/dx_k^2, with M_alpha being the alpha particle mass.
- For a single alpha particle guided by a wave-packet, the velocity at time t is given by dX/dt = (1/(1 + (sigma_A^2/(2*M_alpha)*t)^2)) * [p_A/M_alpha + (sigma_A^2/M_alpha)*(X(t) - x_A)*t], where sigma_A is the width parameter and p_A is the initial momentum.

## Context

This paper extends the Bohmian interpretation of quantum mechanics (Bohm 1952, Bohm and Hiley 1993) to applications in nuclear and particle physics, fields that the author states have not yet been thoroughly reinterpreted within the BP. The work builds on prior formulations by de Broglie and Bohm and their co-workers, and aims to draw attention to the BP by giving illustrative examples from contemporary nuclear and particle physics practices. The paper enables future experimental tests of individual particle trajectories in alpha-decay, neutron interferometry, and beta-decay processes, potentially distinguishing BP predictions from standard textbook quantum mechanics at the level of individual systems rather than statistical ensembles.
