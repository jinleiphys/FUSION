# Nuclear giant resonances from first principles

> This chapter presents an ab initio perspective on giant resonances in atomic nuclei and surveys the principal theoretical frameworks that aim to describe these collective excitations from first principles. While the study of nuclear giant resonances has traditionally been dominated by the energy density functional approach, recent years have witnessed the development of advanced many-body approaches grounded directly in realistic nuclear interactions, namely, Hamiltonians that reproduce nucleon-nucleon phase shifts and accurately describe the binding energies of light nuclei. Within this modern framework, we review the main many-body methods currently used to compute nuclear response functions. These include the random phase approximation, the Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster theory, the projected generator-coordinate method, and the self-consistent Green's functions approach. After giving a general conceptual and historical overview of giant-resonance phenomena, we outline the theoretical foundations and computational implementations of each method. We conclude with a critical comparison of their predictions for selected benchmark nuclei, $^{16}$O and $^{40}$Ca, emphasizing points of agreement and divergence, while maintaining a close connection to the relevant experimental observables.

# Nuclear giant resonances from first principles

## Key claim

Ab initio methods can now describe giant resonances in medium-mass nuclei starting directly from realistic nuclear interactions derived from chiral effective field theory, without parameters adjusted to specific nuclei; however, a full, comprehensive uncertainty quantification for ab initio computations of giant resonances is still missing, and further work is required to strengthen agreement among different methods.

## Method

The authors review four ab initio many-body methods used to compute nuclear response functions for giant resonances: the random phase approximation (RPA), the Lorentz integral transform coupled-cluster (LIT-CC) theory, the projected generator-coordinate method (PGCM), and the self-consistent Green's functions (SCGF) approach. Each method starts from a Hamiltonian containing nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces derived from chiral effective field theory, and solves the nuclear Schrodinger equation with controlled approximations. The response functions are connected to experimental observables such as photoabsorption cross sections and inelastic scattering cross sections through linear response theory.

## Key numbers

- The giant dipole resonance was first identified in uranium by Baldwin and Klaiber in 1947, located at around 10-20 MeV of excitation energy
- Benchmark calculations are performed for the nuclei 16O and 40Ca, comparing predictions across different ab initio methods
- The electric dipole polarizability was extracted in 208Pb (Tamii 2011) and in 40,48Ca (Birkhan 2017, Fearick 2023) from relativistic proton inelastic scattering experiments

## Context

This review extends the traditional energy-density functional (EDF) approach to giant resonances, which has been the dominant framework historically (Schunck 2019, Colo 2022 Handbook), by surveying recent ab initio developments. The work connects to earlier phenomenological models such as the Goldhaber-Teller (1948) and Steinwedel-Jensen (1950) models of the giant dipole resonance, and to self-consistent mean-field studies (Paar 2007, Erler 2011, Nakatsukasa 2012). The ab initio methods reviewed here build on advances in coupled-cluster theory (Miorelli 2018), the in-medium similarity renormalization group (Parzuchowski 2016), PGCM (Porro 2024), and SCGF theory (Raimondi 2019), and enable connections between laboratory nuclear observables and astrophysical properties such as neutron star incompressibility and symmetry energy.
