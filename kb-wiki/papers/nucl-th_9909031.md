# Muon-catalyzed fusion in "warm-fusion"

## Key claim
The paper postulates that in a heavy water cluster (D₂O)ₙ with specific energy of approximately 150 eV, an O–D bond transforms into an O–Dμ bond, creating a muonic molecule; this hypothesis is necessary to explain the "warm" fusion effect observed in a Brookhaven National Laboratory experiment where deuterated titanium was bombarded by 300 keV heavy water clusters.

## Method
The author proposes a model for muon-catalyzed fusion within the (D₂O)ₙ – TiD system, starting from the hypothesis that fusion reaction output is proportional to deuterium concentration in the cluster. The model then introduces a postulate that at specific cluster energies, ordinary D₂O molecules become muonic molecules via bond transformation, and applies standard muon-catalyzed fusion physics, including muonic atom ionization energy scaling (206 times the hydrogen atom ionization energy) and dissociation energy calculations, to explain the observed threshold behavior at cluster size N ≈ 110 and the vanishing of fusion for N > 500.

## Key numbers
- The specific energy required for the O–D bond to transform into an O–Dμ bond is approximately 150 eV.
- The ionization energy of a muonic atom Dμ is calculated as Tμ = 206 × T₀, where T₀ is the ionization energy of a hydrogen atom, yielding Tμ = 2.8 keV.
- The cluster size at which muonic atoms Dμ have exactly this ionization energy is N = 107, for a cluster energy of 300 keV.
- The dissociation energy Tdis for a muonic molecule D₂O is estimated as approximately 190 eV, based on the electron affinity for muon (185 eV) plus the ordinary molecule dissociation energy (5 eV).
- For cluster energy T = 300 keV and angle ϑ = 0°, the maximum cluster size N for dissociation is 1580; for ϑ = 52° (based on the 104° molecular bond angle in water), N ≈ 550.

## Context
This work attempts to explain the "warm" fusion experiment at Brookhaven National Laboratory (reported in Ref. p1), which the author notes contradicts standard concepts of these processes and is not convincingly explained by prior attempts that reduce the results to fusion by macroparticle collision (Ref. p4). The author's postulate is presented as a qualitative result of quantum theory interpretation developed in his previous paper (Ref. p6). The model enables a detailed explanation of the threshold behavior at N ≈ 110 and the sharp decrease in fusion for N > 500, without invoking the macroparticle collision idea.
