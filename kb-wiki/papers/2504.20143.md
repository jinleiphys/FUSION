# Neutrino flavor instabilities in a binary neutron star merger remnant: Roles of a long-lived hypermassive neutron star

> Understanding the post-merger evolution of binary neutron star merger (BNSM) requires accurate modeling of neutrino transport and microphysics including neutrino flavor conversions. Many previous studies have suggested that fast flavor instability (FFI) and collisional flavor instability (CFI) pervade inner regions of BNSM remnant, and they could impact on fluid dynamics and r-process nucleosynthesis. In this work, we re-examine prospects of occurrences of FFI and CFI using Boltzmann neutrino transport, assuming a frozen fluid background obtained from a numerical relativity simulation of BNSM. We pay special attention to a case involving a long-lived ($>1\,$ s) hypermassive neutron star (HMNS). Apart from confirming the claim that these flavor instabilities can occur in BNSM remnants, some new insights are revealed. We identify multiple mechanisms responsible for generating electron neutrino lepton number (ELN) angular crossings, corresponding to a key indicator of FFI onset, which differ notably from those in black hole (BH) accretion disk systems. We argue that the appearance of positive chemical potential of electron-type neutrinos plays important roles on generating ELN angular crossings. For CFI, their growth rates are generally lower than FFI, but they can persistently occur in most of the accretion disk up to $\sim 1\,$ s. We also find that neglecting contributions of heavy-leptonic neutrinos results in overestimating growth rate and area of unstable regions of CFI. Our result suggests that FFI (CFI) tends to occur transiently (persistently) and locally (widespread in the disk), and FFI is more sensitive to the central compact object (HMNS or BH) than CFI, though more self-consistent simulations with incorporating effects of flavor conversions are needed to confirm these claims.

## Key claim

The paper confirms that both fast flavor instability (FFI) and collisional flavor instability (CFI) can occur in binary neutron star merger remnants with a long-lived hypermassive neutron star, but reveals that FFI tends to be transient and local while CFI is persistent and widespread, and that neglecting heavy-leptonic neutrinos overestimates CFI growth rates and unstable regions.

## Method

The authors performed Boltzmann neutrino transport simulations on a frozen fluid background obtained from a numerical relativity simulation of a binary neutron star merger with a long-lived hypermassive neutron star (DD2 equation of state, equal-mass 1.35 solar mass binaries). They used a discrete ordinate method with multi-angle, multi-energy, and multi-species treatments to solve the Boltzmann equation in flat spacetime, then applied approximate stability analysis formulas to estimate growth rates of FFI and CFI at six time snapshots (0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, and 1.3 seconds).

## Key numbers

* The hypermassive neutron star in the simulation survived for more than 1 second without entering runaway gravitational collapse, up to the end of the simulation at approximately 1.3 seconds.
* The growth rate of FFI was typically above 10^7 per second in regions where it occurred, with no regions showing growth rates below this value except where ELN crossings were absent.
* CFI growth rates were generally lower than FFI growth rates, and CFI could persistently occur in most of the accretion disk up to approximately 1 second.
* The spatial domain for Boltzmann transport simulations extended from 0 to 250 km in radius, with 512 radial grid points and 96 zenith angle grid points covering 0 to pi/2.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of FFI in binary neutron star merger remnants by Wu and Tamborra (2017), Richers et al. (2022), and Just et al. (2022) that identified ELN angular crossings, and studies of CFI by Johns and Xiong (2023) that showed CFI can occur in merger remnants. The authors provide the first systematic survey of both FFI and CFI based on a long-term simulation of a binary neutron star merger remnant with a long-lived hypermassive neutron star, which has not been well studied previously. The results enable future self-consistent time-dependent radiation-hydrodynamic simulations incorporating flavor conversions to confirm the transient versus persistent nature of these instabilities.
