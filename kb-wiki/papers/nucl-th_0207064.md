# The Role of Surface Entropy in Statistical Emission of Massive Fragments from Equilibrated Nuclear Systems

> Statistical fragment emission from excited nuclear systems is studied within the framework of a schematic Fermi-gas model combined with Weisskopf's detailed balance approach. The formalism considers thermal expansion of finite nuclear systems and pays special attention to the role of the diffuse surface region in the decay of hot equilibrated systems. It is found that with increasing excitation energy, effects of surface entropy lead to a systematic and significant reduction of effective emission barriers for fragments and, eventually, to the vanishing of these barriers. The formalism provides a natural explanation for the occurrence of negative nuclear heat capacities reported in the literature. It also accounts for the observed linearity of pseudo-Arrhenius plots of the logarithm of the fragment emission probability {\it versus} the inverse square-root of the excitation energy, but does not predict true Arrhenius behavior of these emission probabilities.

## Key claim

The paper argues that surface entropy effects, arising from the diffuse nuclear surface region, cause a systematic and significant reduction of effective emission barriers for intermediate-mass fragments (IMFs) with increasing excitation energy, eventually leading to barrier vanishing. This provides a statistical explanation for high IMF yields without invoking a liquid-gas phase transition, but the formalism is schematic and does not predict true Arrhenius behavior for emission probabilities.

## Method

The authors combine a Fermi-gas model with Weisskopf's detailed balance approach to calculate fragment emission probabilities from equilibrated, thermally expanding nuclear systems. They model the entropy as having separate volume and surface contributions, with the surface level-density parameter coefficient set to four times the volume coefficient. The equilibrium density is found by maximizing entropy at fixed total excitation energy, and effective emission barriers are derived from the entropy difference between the equilibrium and saddle-point configurations.

## Key numbers

- The surface level-density parameter coefficient is set to $\alpha_\sigma = 4/14.6$ MeV$^{-1}$, four times the volume coefficient $\alpha_V = 1/14.6$ MeV$^{-1}$.
- The ground-state binding energy per nucleon is assumed to be $\epsilon_b = 8$ MeV.
- The caloric curve for $^{197}$Au shows a maximum temperature of approximately $T_{max} = 6$ MeV, above which no equilibrium density exists.
- At excitation energies per nucleon of about 3 MeV/nucleon, fragment emission begins to compete effectively with proton emission for $^{197}$Au.
- The Coulomb radius parameter used for saddle-point energies is $r_{Coul} = 1.3 (\rho/\rho_o)^{-1/3}$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier formalism (Toke et al., 2002) and directly addresses failures of prior statistical models such as the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), the Microcanonical Multifragmentation Model (MMMC), percolation models, Fisher's model, and the Expanding Emitting Source Model (EESM), which the authors argue cannot explain how IMF emission competes with nucleon evaporation. The formalism provides a natural explanation for negative nuclear heat capacities reported in the literature and accounts for observed linearity in pseudo-Arrhenius plots of fragment emission probability versus inverse square-root of excitation energy, enabling incorporation into existing statistical decay codes.
