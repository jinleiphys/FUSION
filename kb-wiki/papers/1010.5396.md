# Origin of the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb in nuclear mean-field models

> We study whether the neutron skin thickness (NST) of 208Pb originates from the bulk or from the surface of the nucleon density distributions, according to the mean-field models of nuclear structure, and find that it depends on the stiffness of the nuclear symmetry energy. The bulk contribution to NST arises from an extended sharp radius of neutrons, whereas the surface contribution arises from different widths of the neutron and proton surfaces. Nuclear models where the symmetry energy is stiff, as typical relativistic models, predict a bulk contribution in NST of 208Pb about twice as large as the surface contribution. In contrast, models with a soft symmetry energy like common nonrelativistic models predict that NST of 208Pb is divided similarly into bulk and surface parts. Indeed, if the symmetry energy is supersoft, the surface contribution becomes dominant. We note that the linear correlation of NST of 208Pb with the density derivative of the nuclear symmetry energy arises from the bulk part of NST. We also note that most models predict a mixed-type (between halo and skin) neutron distribution for 208Pb. Although the halo-type limit is actually found in the models with a supersoft symmetry energy, the skin-type limit is not supported by any mean-field model. Finally, we compute parity-violating electron scattering in the conditions of the 208Pb parity radius experiment (PREX) and obtain a pocket formula for the parity-violating asymmetry in terms of the parameters that characterize the shape of the 208Pb nucleon densities.

## Key claim

The neutron skin thickness of 208Pb originates from either bulk or surface contributions depending on the stiffness of the nuclear symmetry energy, a nuance the abstract presents as a finding rather than a conditional result: models with stiff symmetry energy predict a bulk contribution about twice as large as the surface contribution, while models with soft symmetry energy predict roughly equal bulk and surface parts, and supersoft models make the surface contribution dominant.

## Method

The authors decomposed the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb into bulk and surface components by fitting self-consistent mean-field densities (from Skyrme, Gogny, and relativistic mean-field interactions) with two-parameter Fermi distributions, extracting equivalent sharp radii and surface widths. They then computed the bulk contribution from the difference in equivalent sharp radii of neutrons and protons, and the surface contribution from the difference in surface widths, and correlated these with the density derivative L of the nuclear symmetry energy.

## Key numbers

- The neutron skin thickness of 208Pb ranges from 0.115 fm (HFB-8) to 0.321 fm (NL1) across mean-field models.
- The bulk contribution varies from 0.030 fm (MSk7) to 0.216 fm (NL1), while the surface contribution is confined to 0.069 fm (Sk-Rs) to 0.105 fm (NL1).
- The density derivative L of the symmetry energy ranges from 9.4 MeV (MSk7) to 140.1 MeV (NL1).
- Experimental determinations cited: antiprotonic atom data give 0.16 ± 0.02 (stat) ± 0.04 (syst) fm; hadron scattering compilation gives 0.165 ± 0.025 fm; pygmy dipole resonance gives 0.194 ± 0.024 fm; proton elastic scattering gives 0.211 +0.054 -0.063 fm.
- The surface contribution approximates to 3 A^{-1/3} (b_n - b_p) ≈ 0.1 fm for 208Pb, with b_n - b_p ≈ 0.2 fm on average.

## Context

This work extends the droplet model analysis of Myers and Swiatecki (1974, 1980) and the authors' own previous studies (Centelles et al., 2009) by explicitly separating bulk and surface contributions to the neutron skin thickness in mean-field models. It shows that the well-known linear correlation of the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb with the density derivative L of the symmetry energy arises primarily from the bulk part, not the surface part. The results provide a framework for interpreting upcoming PREX parity-violating electron scattering data, suggesting that such experiments could constrain not only the neutron rms radius but also the shape of the neutron density profile.
