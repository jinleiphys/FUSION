# Properties of nuclear pastas

> In this Review we study the nuclear pastas as they are expected to be formed in neutron star cores. We start with a study of the pastas formed in nuclear matter (composed of protons and neutrons), we follow with the role of the electron gas on the formation of pastas, and we then investigate the pastas in neutron star matter (nuclear matter embedded in an electron gas).

## Key claim

This review argues that nuclear pasta structures in neutron star crusts can be studied effectively using classical molecular dynamics (CMD) simulations, which capture both global and local energy minima that static methods miss; however, the authors note that the CMD pressure at low densities is not correct because the system should approximate a free nucleon gas, which is a quantum system.

## Method

The authors used classical molecular dynamics with Pandharipande potentials to simulate nuclear matter (protons and neutrons) and neutron star matter (nuclear matter embedded in an electron gas via a screened Coulomb potential). Systems of 2,000 nucleons were placed in cubic cells with periodic boundary conditions at various densities, temperatures, and proton fractions (x = Z/A from 0.3 to 0.5). The simulations were analyzed using cluster-recognition algorithms, radial distribution functions, Lindemann coefficients, Kolmogorov statistics, Minkowski functionals, and Maxwell constructions to determine phase boundaries and symmetry energies.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter at intermediate temperatures (1 MeV less than or equal to T less than or equal to 15 MeV) was studied at densities from 0.02 to 0.2 fm^-3
- The saturation density at near-zero temperature varies from 0.16 fm^-3 at x=0.5 to 0.04 fm^-3 at x=0.3
- The compressibility at saturation density for the medium Pandharipande potential is 283 MeV, comparable to the value of 250 MeV quoted by its creators
- The critical temperature for liquid-gas coexistence reaches T=0 at x=0.1324, indicating this is the lower proton fraction limit for the liquid-gas mixed region
- Pasta forms at around T approximately 1.5 MeV, and liquid-to-solid phase changes were detected at T approximately 0.5 MeV
- The compressibility reduces by approximately 30% as temperature increases from 1 to 5 MeV for x=0.4 and 0.5

## Context

This review extends earlier static studies of nuclear pasta from the 1980s that used liquid drop models (Hashimoto, 1980s), mean field theories (Page, 1980s), and Thomas-Fermi models (Koonin, 1980s), which worked only at zero temperature and missed non-traditional local minima. The CMD approach enables temperature-dependent dynamical studies that can achieve phase changes and identify local minima by cooling, building on prior work by Dorso (2014) and Horo (2004). The findings enable better understanding of neutron star crust properties relevant to star-quakes, pulsar frequencies, and thermal evolution, since neutrino opacity is enhanced by coherent scattering from pasta structures.
