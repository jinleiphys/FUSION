# Establishing the Range of Applicability of Hydrodynamics in High-Energy Collisions

> We simulate the space-time dynamics of high-energy collisions based on a microscopic kinetic description in the conformal relaxation time approximation, in order to determine the range of applicability of an effective description in relativistic viscous hydrodynamics. We find that hydrodynamics provides a quantitatively accurate description of collective flow when the average inverse Reynolds number is sufficiently small and the early pre-equilibrium stage is properly accounted for. We further discuss the implications of our findings for the (in)applicability of hydrodynamics in proton-proton, proton-nucleus and light nucleus collisions.

# Establishing the Range of Applicability of Hydrodynamics in High-Energy Collisions

## Key claim
Viscous hydrodynamics provides an accurate description of collective flow in high-energy collisions only when the average inverse Reynolds number Re⁻¹ is below approximately 0.75 and the early pre-equilibrium stage is properly accounted for; even in the limit of very large interaction strength, the early-time pre-equilibrium dynamics can never be described by ordinary viscous hydrodynamics, affecting collective flow observables at the few-percent level.

## Method
The authors simulate the space-time dynamics of high-energy collisions using the Boltzmann equation in the conformal relaxation time approximation (RTA), with a conformal relaxation time τ_R(x) = (η/s)/(5T(x)). They compare these microscopic kinetic theory results to macroscopic descriptions using relativistic viscous hydrodynamics (Mueller-Israel-Stewart type second-order hydrodynamics via the vHLLE Hydro code) and ideal hydrodynamics, for Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV. The comparison is quantified through the elliptic energy flow ε_p and the average inverse Reynolds number Re⁻¹, with the system's evolution characterized by a single dimensionless opacity parameter γ̂ that accounts for η/s, initial energy per unit rapidity, and system size.

## Key numbers
- A critical inverse Reynolds number Re⁻¹_c ≈ 0.75 is required for viscous hydrodynamics to describe the space-time dynamics with a few percent accuracy; switching at Re⁻¹ ≲ 0.75 keeps discrepancies below 5%.
- For opacities γ̂ ≳ 3–4, the system equilibrates before the onset of transverse expansion (τ_Hydro < τ_Exp), making hydrodynamics applicable; this corresponds to η/s ≲ 3/4π for 30–40% centrality Pb+Pb or ≲ 60% centrality for η/s = 2/4π.
- In minimum bias p+p collisions at LHC energies, opacities take values of γ̂ ≈ 0.7, making hydrodynamics not applicable for η/s = 2/4π.
- In p+Pb collisions, opacities range from γ̂ ≈ 1.5 in minimum bias events to γ̂ ≈ 2.7 in high-multiplicity events.
- In O+O collisions at LHC energies, estimated opacities reach from 1.4 in peripheral events to 3.1 in central events.
- The timescale for equilibration follows τ_Hydro/R ≈ 1.53 γ̂^{-4/3} [(Re⁻¹_c)^{-3/2} - 1.21 (Re⁻¹_c)^{0.7}].
- Changes in η/s by fifty percent result in changes of anisotropic flow of about 15% in the regime 4π η/s ≃ 1–3.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of hydrodynamic applicability in 0+1D Bjorken flow (Berges 2013, Heller 2015, Spalinski 2017, Strickland 2017, Giacalone 2019, Kurkela 2019, Denicol 2019) to the more complex space-time dynamics of real-world collisions. It builds on the opacity parameter framework from Kurkela (2019) and Ambrus (2021), and provides quantitative criteria for when viscous hydrodynamics can replace kinetic theory descriptions. The findings enable systematic determination of whether hydrodynamics is applicable in small collision systems (p+p, p+Pb, O+O) and provide a rescaling scheme (Eq. 4) to initialize hydrodynamic simulations at arbitrarily early times by compensating for the improper description of pre-equilibrium dynamics. The results challenge the hydrodynamic paradigm for small systems where collective flow has been observed (ALICE 2014, ATLAS 2017, CMS 2017), suggesting that non-equilibrium descriptions are required for γ̂ ≲ 3–4.
