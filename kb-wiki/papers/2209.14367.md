# Three-loop corrections to the quark and gluon decomposition of the QCD trace anomaly and their applications

> In the QCD energy-momentum tensor $T^{μν}$, the terms that contribute to physical matrix elements are expressed as the sum of the gauge-invariant quark part and gluon part. Each part undergoes the renormalization due to the interactions among quarks and gluons, although the total tensor $T^{μν}$ is not renormalized thanks to conservation of energy and momentum. We show that, through the renormalization, each of the quark and gluon parts of $T^{μν}$ receives a definite amount of anomalous trace contribution, such that their sum reproduces the well-known QCD trace anomaly. We provide a procedure to derive such anomalous trace contribution for each quark/gluon part to all orders in perturbation theory, and obtain the corresponding explicit formulas up to three-loop order in the $\overline{\rm MS}$ scheme in the dimensional regularization. We apply our three-loop formulas of the quark/gluon decomposition of the trace anomaly to calculate the anomaly-induced mass structure of nucleons as well as pions. Another application of our three-loop formulas is a quantitative analysis for the constraints on the twist-four gravitational form factors of the nucleon, $\bar{C}_{q,g}$.

# Three-loop corrections to the quark and gluon decomposition of the QCD trace anomaly and their applications

## Key claim

The quark and gluon parts of the QCD energy-momentum tensor each receive definite anomalous trace contributions through renormalization, such that their sum reproduces the well-known QCD trace anomaly; this decomposition is derived to three-loop order in the MS-bar scheme, and the author shows that the relative size of gluon versus quark loop effects in the nucleon mass is about five to one in the chiral limit, while for the pion the two contributions are roughly equal.

## Method

The author defines a basis of gauge-invariant twist-two and twist-four operators and introduces renormalization constants for the quark and gluon parts of the energy-momentum tensor, including mixing between quark and gluon operators. Six of the ten renormalization constants are determined from known DGLAP splitting functions (up to three-loop) and from the renormalization-group invariance of the total trace anomaly; the remaining four are fixed by requiring ultraviolet finiteness of the renormalized operators. The resulting three-loop formulas for the trace of each part are then applied to compute the anomaly-induced mass structure of nucleons and pions, and to constrain the twist-four gravitational form factors of the nucleon.

## Key numbers

- For the nucleon in the chiral limit with Nc=3, nf=3: the ratio of the quark-part trace to the gluon-part trace is -0.181818 - 0.0258682 α_s(μ) + 0.0424613 α_s^2(μ) (Eq. property).
- Using α_s(1 GeV) = 0.47358 (three-loop running coupling in MS-bar with α_s(M_Z)=0.1181), the nucleon matrix element of F^2 is ⟨N(p)|F^2(μ=1 GeV)|N(p)⟩ ≈ -8.61 m_N^2.
- For the pion at μ=1 GeV: the gluon-part trace contributes 0.521 × 2m_π^2 and the quark-part trace contributes 0.479 × 2m_π^2 (Eq. eq19), to O(m) accuracy.
- The three-loop anomalous dimension γ_m(g) = 0.63662 α_s + 0.768352 α_s^2 + 0.801141 α_s^3 ≈ 0.559.
- The asymptotic value of the nucleon's twist-four gravitational form factor C̅_q in the chiral limit is -0.146 (for Nc=3, nf=3).

## Context

This work extends the two-loop decomposition of the QCD trace anomaly by Hatta et al. (2018) to three-loop order, and builds on the author's own earlier three-loop calculation (Tanaka, 2018). The results enable quantitative separation of gluon and quark loop contributions to hadron masses, revealing that the nucleon mass is dominated by gluon effects while the pion mass is nearly equally shared, a distinction attributed to the pion's nature as a Nambu-Goldstone boson. The three-loop formulas also provide constraints on the twist-four gravitational form factors C̅_{q,g} of the nucleon, which are relevant to the force distribution inside the nucleon and the transverse spin sum rule (Hatta, 2012; Polyakov, 2018).
