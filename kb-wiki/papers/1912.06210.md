# Hierarchical Bayesian Thermonuclear Rate for the $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li Big Bang Nucleosynthesis Reaction

> Big bang nucleosynthesis provides the earliest probe of standard model physics, at a time when the universe was less than a thousand seconds old. It determines the abundances of the lightest nuclides, which give rise to the subsequent history of the visible matter in the Universe. This work derives new $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li thermonuclear reaction rates based on all available experimental information. This reaction sensitively impacts the primordial abundances of $^{7}$Be and $^7$Li during big bang nucleosynthesis. We critically evaluate all available data and disregard experimental results that are questionable. For the nuclear model, we adopt an incoherent sum of single-level, two-channel R-matrix approximation expressions, which are implemented into a hierarchical Bayesian model, to analyze the remaining six data sets we deem most reliable. In the fitting of the data, we consistently model all known sources of uncertainty, including discrepant absolute normalizations of different data sets, and also take the variation of the neutron and proton channel radii into account, hence providing less biased estimates of the $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li thermonuclear rates. From the resulting posteriors, we extract R-matrix parameters ($E_r$, $γ^2_n$, $γ^2_p$) and derive excitation energies, partial and total widths. Our fit is sensitive to the contributions of the first three levels above the neutron threshold. Reaction rates were computed by integrating 10,000 samples of the reduced cross section. Our $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li thermonuclear rates have uncertainties between 1.5% and 2.0% at temperatures of $\leq$1 GK. We compare our rates to previous results and find that the $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li rates most commonly used in big bang simulations have too optimistic uncertainties.

## Key claim

The authors derive new $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li thermonuclear reaction rates using a hierarchical Bayesian model that accounts for discrepant data sets, finding that previously used rates have "too optimistic uncertainties", the new rates have uncertainties between 1.5% and 2.0% at temperatures $\leq$1 GK, but the analysis reveals that the reduced cross sections measured by different groups differ by up to $\approx$30%, and the authors explicitly state they did not expect a factor of $\approx$3.5 increase in the rate that would be needed to resolve the cosmological lithium problem.

## Method

The authors adopted an incoherent sum of single-level, two-channel R-matrix approximation expressions implemented into a hierarchical Bayesian model, analyzing six data sets deemed most reliable after critical evaluation of all available experimental information. They modeled all known sources of uncertainty including discrepant absolute normalizations of different data sets and variation of neutron and proton channel radii, extracting R-matrix parameters ($E_r$, $\gamma^2_n$, $\gamma^2_p$) from the resulting posteriors and computing reaction rates by integrating 10,000 samples of the reduced cross section.

## Key numbers

- Thermonuclear rate uncertainties between 1.5% and 2.0% at temperatures of $\leq$1 GK
- Reduced cross sections from different groups differ by up to $\approx$30%
- Thermal neutron cross section of $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li is $\approx$ $4.5\times10^4$ barn, the largest thermal cross section known among light nuclides
- A reaction rate uncertainty of $\approx$5% at BBN temperatures translates to a $\approx$4% variation in predicted $^7$Li abundance
- Predicted $^7$Li/H ratio exceeds the observed one by a factor of $\approx$3 (the cosmological lithium problem)
- A factor of $\approx$3.5 increase in the $^7$Be(n,p)$^7$Li rate would reconcile predicted and observed primordial $^7$Li abundances
- The fit is sensitive to the first three levels above the neutron threshold in $^8$Be, with total widths ranging between 100 keV and 1 MeV
- Six absolute cross section normalizations were used with systematic uncertainties: Gibbons & Macklin (1959) 5.0%; Marganiec et al. (2019) 5.1%; Cerny et al. (1989) 8.5%; Tomandl et al. (2019) 3.2%; Koehler et al. (1988) 2.0%; Damone et al. (2018) 10%

## Context

This work extends the hierarchical Bayesian approach previously applied to BBN reactions by Iliadis et al. (2016) for $^2$H(p,$\gamma$)$^3$He and $^3$He($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^7$Be, by Gomes et al. (2017) for $^2$H(d,n)$^3$He and $^2$H(d,p)$^3$H, and by de Souza et al. (2019) for $^3$H(d,n)$^4$He and $^3$He(d,p)$^4$He. The authors critically evaluate previous R-matrix analyses by Koehler et al. (1988), Adelberger et al. (2003), and Descouvemont (2004), noting that recent work by Kuntz et al. (2016) and Damone et al. (2018) kept $^8$Be excitation energies fixed from ENSDF without accounting for their uncertainties. The new rates enable more reliable BBN predictions for $^7$Li abundance, which is particularly important given the unresolved cosmological lithium problem where predicted $^7$Li/H exceeds observations by a factor of $\approx$3.
