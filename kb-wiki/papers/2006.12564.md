# A Somewhat Random Walk Through Nuclear and Particle Physics

> These notes are an outgrowth of an advanced undergraduate course taught at the University of Maryland, College Park. They are intended as an introduction to various aspects of particle and nuclear physics with an emphasis on the role of symmetry. The basic philosophy is to introduce many of the fundamental ideas in nuclear and particle physics using relatively sophisticated mathematical tools -- but to do so in as a simplified a context to explain the underlying ideas. Thus, for example, the Higgs mechanism is discussed in terms of an Abelian Higgs model. The emphasis is largely, but not entirely theoretical in orientation. The goal is for readers to develop an understanding of many of the underlying issues in a relatively sophisticated way.

## Key claim

These notes present an advanced undergraduate-level introduction to nuclear and particle physics that emphasizes the role of symmetry, using simplified mathematical contexts (such as an Abelian Higgs model rather than the full electroweak theory) to make sophisticated ideas accessible, but the authors explicitly note that the material covers only a small fraction of modern fundamental physics and focuses on developments from the mid-1930s to the mid-1970s.

## Method

The paper is a set of lecture notes developed from an advanced undergraduate course at the University of Maryland, College Park, taught initially in Spring 2018 and revised with a teaching assistant in Spring 2019. The notes introduce quantum field theory concepts in simplified contexts, discuss symmetry principles including continuous spacetime symmetries and discrete symmetries (parity, time reversal), and cover topics such as the liquid drop model of the nucleus, Yukawa theory of the strong force, Fermi theory of beta decay, gauge theories, symmetry breaking, and the Higgs mechanism.

## Key numbers

- The neutron mass is approximately 0.1% heavier than the proton mass, establishing the near degeneracy that gave rise to the understanding of an underlying approximate symmetry of the nuclear force.
- The historical period covered by the notes spans from the mid-1930s to the mid-1970s, approximately forty years of development in nuclear and particle physics.
- The notes describe that by 1935, the strong and weak nuclear forces, electrons, protons, neutrons, nuclei, antimatter, and the pion (as a conjecture) were already well-established.

## Context

This work extends the tradition of pedagogical introductions to nuclear and particle physics that emphasize symmetry, similar in spirit to the approach of using simplified models to teach advanced concepts. The authors note that existing undergraduate textbooks were not suitable for the kind of course they wanted to teach, motivating the creation of these notes. The material enables students to understand fundamental ideas in quantum field theory and symmetry breaking without requiring graduate-level background, and includes problems for further study.
