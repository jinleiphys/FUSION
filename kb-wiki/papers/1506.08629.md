# Collective neutrino oscillations and spontaneous symmetry breaking

> Neutrino oscillations in a hot and dense astrophysical environment such as a core-collapse supernova pose a challenging, seven-dimensional flavor transport problem. To make the problem even more difficult (and interesting), neutrinos can experience collective oscillations through nonlinear refraction in the dense neutrino medium in this environment. Significant progress has been made in the last decade towards the understanding of collective neutrino oscillations in various simplified neutrino gas models with imposed symmetries and reduced dimensions. However, a series of recent studies seem to have "reset" this progress by showing that these models may not be compatible with collective neutrino oscillations because the latter can break the symmetries spontaneously if they are not imposed. We review some of the key concepts of collective neutrino oscillations by using a few simple toy models. We also elucidate the breaking of spatial and directional symmetries in these models because of collective oscillations.

## Key claim

Collective neutrino oscillations in dense astrophysical environments can spontaneously break spatial and directional symmetries that are often imposed in simplified models, meaning that results from symmetric toy models (like the bipolar model or the Bulb model) may not be compatible with the full, symmetry-unconstrained dynamics.

## Method

The paper reviews collective neutrino oscillations using the bipolar model (a homogeneous, isotropic system of single-energy \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar\nu_e\)) and the two-beam model (neutrinos emitted in two directions from the \(x\) axis, propagating in the \(x\)-\(z\) plane). It applies linear stability analysis to the equations of motion for neutrino flavor density matrices, expanding around initial weak-interaction states to find complex eigenvalues that indicate flavor instabilities. The analysis is extended to continuous energy spectra and to spherical harmonic decompositions to study directional symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers

* The bipolar model with \(\nu_e\) and \(\bar\nu_e\) initially has flavor instability only in the inverted mass hierarchy (IH), with the instability condition \(\frac{2\omega_0}{(1+\sqrt{\alpha})^2} < -\eta\mu < \frac{2\omega_0}{(1-\sqrt{\alpha})^2}\), where \(\mu = \sqrt{2} G_F n_\nu\) and \(\alpha = n_{\bar\nu}/n_{\nu}\).
* In the two-beam model, the minus modes \([\epsilon^-, \bar\epsilon^-]\) become unstable in the normal mass hierarchy (NH) over the same range of \(\mu\) as the plus modes are unstable in IH, breaking the left-right symmetry.
* A spectral split occurs at critical energy \(E_C = |\Delta m^2|/(2\Omega_0)\), where \(\Omega_0\) is the collective oscillation frequency at \(\mu=0\), such that \(\nu_e\) and \(\nu_\tau\) swap spectra for \(E > E_C\).
* A core-collapse supernova emits \(\sim 10^{58}\) neutrinos in all flavors within \(\sim 10\) seconds.

## Context

This review extends the understanding of collective neutrino oscillations beyond simplified models such as the bipolar model (Kostelecky 1994, Duan 2005, Hannestad 2006) and the Bulb model (Duan 2006), which assumed spatial and directional symmetries. A series of recent studies (Raffelt 2013, Mirizzi 2013, Duan 2013, Mangano 2014, Mirizzi 2015) showed that these symmetries can be broken spontaneously by collective oscillations, effectively "resetting" progress made under symmetry-imposed assumptions. The work enables future studies of neutrino flavor transport in core-collapse supernovae without artificial symmetry constraints, using the full seven-dimensional problem.
