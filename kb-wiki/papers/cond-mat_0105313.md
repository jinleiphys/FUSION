# Straight way to Thermo-Statistics, Phase Transitions, Second Law of Thermodynamics, but without Thermodynamic Limit

> Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V)=k\ln W relates the entropy to the geometric area e^{S(E,N,V)} of the manifold of constant energy in the N-body phase space. From the principle all thermodynamics and especially all phenomena of phase transitions and critical phenomena can be deduced. The topology of the curvature matrix C(E,N) (Hessian) of S(E,N) determines regions of pure phases, regions of phase separation, and (multi-)critical points and lines. Thus, C(E,N) describes all kind of phase-transitions with all their flavor. They are linked to convex (upwards bending) intruders of S(E,N), here the canonical ensemble defined by the Laplace transform to the intensive variables becomes non-local and violates the basic conservation laws (it mixes widely different conserved quantities). Thus Statistical Mechanics becomes a geometric theory addressing the whole ensemble or the manifold of all points in phase space which are consistent with the few macroscopic conserved control parameters. Moreover, this interpretation leads to a straight derivation of irreversibility and the Second Law of Thermodynamics out of the time-reversible microscopic mechanical dynamics. It is the whole ensemble that spreads irreversibly over the accessible phase space not the single N-body trajectory. This is all possible without invoking the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or concavity of S(E,N,V) and also without invoking any cosmological constraints. It is further shown that non-extensive Hamiltonian systems at equilibrium are described by Boltzmann's principle and not by Tsallis non-extensive statistics.

## Key claim
Boltzmann's principle $S(E,N,V)=k\ln W$, applied to the geometric area of the constant-energy manifold in phase space, can derive all thermodynamics, phase transitions, and the Second Law without invoking the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or concavity of the entropy. The paper argues that the microcanonical ensemble is fundamental and that the canonical ensemble fails at phase transitions and for non-extensive systems, where it becomes non-local and violates conservation laws.

## Method
The paper uses the microcanonical ensemble as the fundamental starting point, defining entropy via Boltzmann's principle $S(E,N,V)=k\ln W$, where $W$ is the phase-space volume of the constant-energy manifold. The topology of the curvature matrix (Hessian) $C(E,N)$ of the entropy $S(E,N)$ is analyzed to classify phase transitions: a negative largest eigenvalue $\lambda_1<0$ indicates a single stable phase, while a positive eigenvalue $\lambda_1>0$ signals a first-order transition with phase separation. The approach is applied to Monte Carlo simulations of sodium clusters (liquid-gas transition) and a 2D Potts lattice gas (multi-critical points).

## Key numbers
- For sodium clusters of $N_0=200$ atoms at 1 atm, the transition temperature $T_{tr}$ is 940 K, the latent heat $q_{lat}$ is 0.82 eV, and the boiling entropy $s_{boil}$ is 10.1 (units not specified, presumably per atom).
- For sodium clusters of $N_0=1000$ atoms, $T_{tr}=990$ K, $q_{lat}=0.91$ eV, and $s_{boil}=10.7$.
- For sodium clusters of $N_0=3000$ atoms, $T_{tr}=1095$ K, $q_{lat}=0.94$ eV, and $s_{boil}=9.9$.
- For bulk sodium, $T_{tr}=1156$ K, $q_{lat}=0.923$ eV, and $s_{boil}=9.267$.
- The surface tension over transition temperature $\sigma/T_{tr}$ for sodium clusters rises from 2.75 ($N_0=200$) to 5.68 ($N_0=1000$) to 7.07 ($N_0=3000$), approaching the bulk value of 7.41.
- For the 2D Potts-3 lattice gas on a $50\times50$ lattice, the critical energy of the continuous transition in the ordinary ($q=3$) Potts model (no vacancies) is $e_c \approx 1.58$ in the thermodynamic limit.

## Context
This work extends the geometric interpretation of statistical mechanics based on Boltzmann's principle, challenging the conventional view that the thermodynamic limit (as emphasized by Lieb (1997) and Lebowitz (1999)) is essential for phase transitions and the Second Law. It directly contradicts the applicability of Tsallis non-extensive statistics to Hamiltonian systems at equilibrium, arguing that Boltzmann's principle remains valid. The approach enables the study of phase transitions in "Small" (non-extensive) systems like atomic clusters and nuclei, where the canonical ensemble fails, and provides a framework for deriving irreversibility from reversible dynamics without invoking cosmological constraints.
