# Comparisons of Matrices with Different Elements but Identical Eigenvalues

> We show 2 matrices that have identical eigenvalues but different eigenfunctions. This shows that in obtaining two body nuclear matrix elements empirically, it is not sufficient to consider only energy levels. Other quantities like transitions must also be included.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that two different Hamiltonian matrices can have identical eigenvalues (energy levels) but different eigenfunctions, implying that fitting nuclear matrix elements solely to experimental energy levels is insufficient; transition rates and other observables must also be included to uniquely determine the interaction.

## Method

The authors construct 11×11 pentadiagonal matrices with diagonal elements E(n)=n and off-diagonal parameters v and w, comparing cases where the sign of v is uniform versus alternating. They compute eigenvalues and eigenfunctions numerically using Mathematica, then define a simulated transition operator O to calculate transition amplitudes and lifetime ratios between the two matrix variants. They also present a 3×3 shell-model example with two neutrons in the f7/2 and p3/2 shells to illustrate the same phenomenon in a nuclear-physics context.

## Key numbers

- For the tridiagonal case (v=1, w=0), the lowest eigenvalue is -7.46E-1 for both M1 and M2.
- The ratio of lifetimes (alternating-sign v versus same-sign v) for the transition from state 1 to state 0 is 3.34.
- The ratio of lifetimes for the transition from state 2 to state 1 is 0.75.
- For the pentadiagonal case (v=0, w=±1) in a 4×4 matrix, the transition amplitude from n=0 to n=1 is -6.464E-1 for w=+1 and 1.354 for w=-1.
- For the 3×3 shell-model example, the three eigenvalues are -3.48134, -0.214496, and 6.69584 for both matrices M'1 and M'2.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of tridiagonal matrices with identical eigenvalues by Zamick and collaborators (2018–2021). It directly addresses the empirical approach of McCullen, Bayman, and Zamick (1964) and Escuderos, Zamick, and Bayman (2005), who extracted two-body matrix elements in the f7/2 shell from excitation energies of 42Ca, 42S, and 42Ti. The results show that such fitting procedures are ambiguous unless transition data are also included, and they open the question of how many additional observables are needed to uniquely determine nuclear matrix elements in more complex configuration spaces.
