# Remembrance of things past: non-equilibrium effects and the evolution of critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point

> We report on recent progress in the study of the evolution of non-Gaussian cumulants of critical fluctuations. We explore the implications of non-equilibrium effects on the search for the QCD critical point.

## Key claim
Non-equilibrium effects due to critical slowing down can cause the skewness and kurtosis of critical fluctuations to differ significantly in both magnitude and sign from equilibrium expectations, meaning that the system's fluctuations "remember" earlier stages of its evolution through the critical regime.

## Method
The authors derived a set of evolution equations for the cumulants of critical modes starting from a Fokker-Planck equation describing the relaxation of the probability distribution of the space-averaged critical mode. They showed that when the equilibrium correlation length is much smaller than the system size, the evolution of lower cumulants decouples from higher cumulants, yielding closed-form equations. They then applied these equations along phenomenologically motivated trajectories through the QCD critical regime, using a linear mapping between QCD variables (T, μ) and Ising variables (r, h), with the relaxation time scaling as τ_eff ∼ ξ^3_eq.

## Key numbers
- The dynamical universal exponent for the QCD critical point is z ≈ 3, giving τ_eff ∼ ξ^3_eq.
- Results depend on the dimensionless ratio τ_rel/τ_I, with values shown for 0.005, 0.02, 0.05, and 0.2.
- The non-equilibrium correlation length is defined as ξ ≡ √(κ₂ T/V), where V is volume and T is temperature.

## Context
This work extends the study of critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point by incorporating non-equilibrium effects, building on earlier universality arguments by Hohenberg (1977) and the observation by Berdnikov (1999) that real-time critical fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions could differ from equilibrium values. The authors' approach provides a framework for interpreting beam energy scan data from the STAR experiment, showing that similar experimental curves can arise from different combinations of freeze-out curves and relaxation times, complicating the search for the critical point.
