# A New Proposal for Neutrino Mass and |V(ud)| Measurements

> We introduce a new method to detect the absolute neutrino mass scale. It uses a macroscopic mass of tritium source. We explain that the neutrino mass can be measured by scaling the mass difference of the source between initial and final state, and its heat value. This method is free from the electron energy resolution limit and the statistical error. We estimate the required accuracy to measure the neutrino mass. We also report that the {u,d} component of the CKM matrix, |V(ud)| may be determined in 10^-6 accuracy as an application of this work.

# A New Proposal for Neutrino Mass and |V(ud)| Measurements

## Key claim
The authors propose that the absolute neutrino mass can be determined by measuring the macroscopic mass difference and heat output of a tritium source, rather than by detecting electron energy spectra. They claim this method is free from the electron energy resolution limit and statistical error, but acknowledge that achieving sensitivity to a 0.2 eV neutrino mass requires measuring the mean neutrino energy with a relative accuracy of 10^{-11}, along with correspondingly high precision in several other parameters.

## Method
The method uses a macroscopic condensed tritium sample in a container, measuring the mass difference ΔM between initial and final states and the released thermal energy M_Q via a differential scanning calorimeter. The mean neutrino energy ⟨E_ν⟩ is derived from energy conservation as ⟨E_ν⟩ = (m_T - m_He)(1 - M_Q/ΔM), where m_T - m_He is the atomic mass difference between tritium and helium-3. The neutrino mass dependence of ⟨E_ν⟩ is computed from the full differential decay width including the exact m_ν effect, and the required accuracies for all input parameters are estimated by comparing the fractional change in ⟨E_ν⟩ caused by m_ν to that caused by uncertainties in C_A, m_He+, m_e, and the mass difference m_T - m_He+ - m_e.

## Key numbers
- The squared neutrino mass splittings from oscillation data are Δm_{12}^2 = (6-9)×10^{-5} eV^2 and Δm_{23}^2 = (1-3)×10^{-3} eV^2.
- The latest upper bound on the neutrino mass from tritium beta decay is m_ν < 2 eV (PDG).
- For a neutrino mass of 0.1 eV, the fractional change in mean neutrino energy is δ⟨E_ν⟩ ≈ 1.12×10^{-11}; for 1 eV, it is ≈ 1.12×10^{-9}.
- To detect a 0.2 eV neutrino mass (the KATRIN goal), the required accuracies are: δC_A/C_A < 10^{-4}, δm_He+/m_He+ < 10^{-5}, δm_e/m_e < 10^{-8}, δ(m_T - m_He+ - m_e)/(m_T - m_He+ - m_e) < 10^{-8}, and δΔM/ΔM, δM_Q/M_Q < 10^{-11}.
- For a 0.2 eV neutrino mass, the required accuracy on ⟨E_ν⟩ is 10^{-11}.
- The normalized standard deviation of ⟨E_ν⟩ for 1 mol of tritium decays is 3.91×10^{-13}, making statistical error negligible.
- The current experimental value of |V_ud| is 0.97418 ± 0.00027 (PDG); the method could potentially determine |V_ud| with 10^{-6} accuracy.

## Context
This work proposes an alternative to conventional tritium beta decay experiments like KATRIN (which will start in 2012 with sensitivity to m_ν > 0.2 eV), which are limited by electron energy resolution and multiple scattering in the source. The method extends the approach of using tritium beta decay for neutrino mass measurement (Fermi, 1935) but replaces electron spectroscopy with macroscopic mass and heat measurements. It also offers a potential route to improve the determination of |V_ud| beyond current precision, complementing measurements from superallowed nuclear beta decays. The required precision in the atomic mass difference m_T - m_He+ - m_e is noted to be achievable with expected improvements in mass spectrometry.
