# Nuclear applications of inverse scattering, present ... and future?

> There now exists a practical method (IP) for the routine inversion of $S$-matrix elements to produce the corresponding potential. It can be applied to spin-1/2 and spin-1 projectiles. We survey the ways that IP inversion can be applied in nuclear physics by inverting $S_{lj}$ derived from theory or from experiment. The IP inversion method can be extended to invert $S_{lj}(E)$ over a range of energies to produce a potential $V(r,E) + \vect{l}\vdot\gvectσ V_{\rm ls}(r,E)$. It also yields parity-dependent potentials between pairs of light nuclei and can be convoluted with a direct search on the $S$-matrix to produce `direct data $\to V$ inversion'. The last is an economical alternative form of optical model search to fit many observables (e.g. for polarized deuterons) for many energies, producing an energy-dependent potential with many parameters (e.g. $T_{\rm R}$ for deuterons).

## Key claim

The Iterative-Perturbative (IP) inverse scattering method can routinely invert S-matrix elements to produce potentials for spin-1/2 and spin-1 projectiles, but the method's reliability depends on controlling the innate ill-posedness of the inversion problem through demanding smooth potentials and using a suitable starting reference potential.

## Method

The IP method assumes a linear response of the elastic scattering S-matrix to small potential changes, using the relation ΔS_l = -(i m / ħ² k) ∫ (u_l(r))² ΔV(r) dr. Starting from a known reference potential, the method adds a basis expansion ∑ c_i v_i(r) and solves the over-determined linear equations via singular value decomposition to find coefficients c_i that bring the calculated S-matrix closer to the target S-matrix, iterating until convergence. The method can be extended to handle spin-dependent potentials, parity-dependent potentials, energy-dependent inversion, and direct inversion from experimental observables by convolving the S-matrix search with the S→V inversion.

## Key numbers

- For 15.6 MeV protons on ⁸He, the bare optical model potential has volume integral J_R = 704.14 MeV fm³, while the complete coupled-reaction-channel (CRC) inversion gives J_R = 653.94 MeV fm³ and the calculation omitting non-orthogonality (NONO) gives J_R = 571.28 MeV fm³
- For the same system, the bare potential has imaginary volume integral J_I = 55.37 MeV fm³, CRC gives J_I = 307.47 MeV fm³, and NONO gives J_I = 252.62 MeV fm³
- The bare potential rms radius for the real part is 3.092 fm, CRC gives 2.938 fm, and NONO gives 2.840 fm
- For ⁶He incident on ²⁰⁸Pb at 27 to 66 MeV, the dynamic polarization potential from Coulomb dipole interaction has an appreciable real part extending out to 60 fm
- The deuteron-⁴He multi-energy direct inversion fitted 1000 data points (five observables, wide angular range, many energies from 4 to 13 MeV)

## Context

This survey extends the work of Kukulin and Mackintosh (2004) on the IP inversion method and reviews applications including parity-dependent potentials for light nuclei from RGM calculations, dynamic polarization potentials from coupled-channel calculations, and direct data-to-potential inversion. The method enables systematic determination of local-equivalent potentials from theoretical S-matrices (such as those from RGM or coupled-reaction-channel calculations) and provides an economical alternative to conventional optical model searches for fitting many observables across multiple energies, as demonstrated for deuteron-⁴He scattering by Cooper and Mackintosh (2003).
