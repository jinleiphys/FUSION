# Anomaly induced transport from symmetry breaking in holography

> We study the transport properties of relativistic fluids induced by quantum anomalies in presence of explicit symmetry breaking. To this end we consider a holographic Einstein-Maxwell model in 5 dimensions with pure gauge and a mixed gauge-gravitational Chern-Simons terms, coupled with a scalar field. To study the chiral vortical effects and the energy transport sector, apart from the chiral magnetic effects, we have considered the full backreaction of the gauge field on the metric. We have studied the anomalous effects by using Kubo formulae involving correlators of the charged currents and the energy current. Our findings reveal that, in the presence of explicit symmetry breaking, anomaly-induced transport phenomena can extend beyond anomalous currents and affect non-anomalous sectors as well. In particular, we find that all the conductivities display a distinct sensitivity to the mass parameter controlling the symmetry breaking, thus reflecting the interplay between anomaly coefficients and explicit symmetry breaking terms. These findings highlight the role played by pure gauge and mixed gauge-gravitational anomalies in holographic transport, and their importance for strongly coupled systems with broken symmetries.

## Key claim

Anomaly-induced transport can extend beyond anomalous currents to affect non-anomalous sectors when explicit symmetry breaking is present; specifically, all conductivities display a distinct sensitivity to the mass parameter controlling the symmetry breaking, reflecting an interplay between anomaly coefficients and explicit symmetry breaking terms.

## Method

The authors constructed a holographic Einstein-Maxwell model in five dimensions with pure gauge and mixed gauge-gravitational Chern-Simons terms, coupled to a scalar field that introduces explicit symmetry breaking. They solved the fully backreacted background equations numerically using a shooting method, then computed retarded correlators via Kubo formulae at zero frequency and in the zero-momentum limit to extract the chiral magnetic and chiral vortical conductivities for vector, axial, non-anomalous, and energy currents.

## Key numbers

- The chiral magnetic conductivity for the vector current induced by the vector magnetic field at zero mass is $\sigma^{B}_{v v}(0) = \mu_a / (2\pi^2)$.
- The chiral magnetic conductivity for the axial current induced by the vector magnetic field at zero mass is $\sigma^{B}_{a v}(0) = \mu_v / (2\pi^2)$.
- The chiral magnetic conductivity for the energy current induced by the vector magnetic field at zero mass is $\sigma^{B}_{\varepsilon v}(0) = \mu_v \mu_a / (2\pi^2)$.
- The chiral magnetic conductivity for the axial current induced by the axial magnetic field at zero mass is $\sigma^{B}_{a a}(0) = \mu_a / (2\pi^2)$.
- The chiral magnetic conductivity for the energy current induced by the axial magnetic field at zero mass is $\sigma^{B}_{\varepsilon a}(0) = (\mu_v^2 + \mu_a^2)/2 + \pi^2 T^2 / 6$.
- The chiral vortical conductivity for the axial current at zero mass is $\sigma^{V}_{a}(0) = (\mu_v^2 + \mu_a^2)/2 + \pi^2 T^2 / 6$.
- The chiral vortical conductivity for the energy current at zero mass is $\sigma^{V}_{\varepsilon}(0) = (\mu_a/3)(3\mu_v^2 + \mu_a^2 + \pi^2 T^2)$.
- The Hawking temperature for the massless case is $T = (r_h / \pi \ell^2) \left( 1 - (\ell^2 / 6 r_h^2)(\mu_v^2 + \mu_a^2 + \mu_w^2) \right)$.

## Context

This work extends the holographic study of anomaly-induced transport by Megias (2016), which considered only a pure gauge Chern-Simons term and found that anomalies can induce transport in non-anomalous currents when symmetries are explicitly broken. The present paper adds the mixed gauge-gravitational Chern-Simons term and full backreaction of gauge fields on the metric, enabling the study of chiral vortical effects and energy transport. The results enable future investigations of how explicit symmetry breaking redistributes anomalous transport between axial and non-anomalous sectors in strongly coupled systems, with potential applications to heavy-ion collision phenomenology and Weyl semimetal transport.
