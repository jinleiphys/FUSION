# High-precision measurements of the atomic mass and electron-capture decay $Q$ value of $^{95}$Tc

> A direct measurement of the ground-state-to-ground-state electron-capture decay $Q$ value of $^{95}$Tc has been performed utilizing the double Penning trap mass spectrometer JYFLTRAP. The $Q$ value was determined to be 1695.92(13) keV by taking advantage of the high resolving power of the phase-imaging ion-cyclotron-resonance technique to resolve the low-lying isomeric state of $^{95}$Tc (excitation energy of 38.910(40) keV) from the ground state. The mass excess of $^{95}$Tc was measured to be $-$86015.95(18) keV/c$^2$, exhibiting a precision of about 28 times higher and in agreement with the value from the newest Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020). Combined with the nuclear energy-level data for the decay-daughter $^{95}$Mo, two potential ultra-low $Q$-value transitions are identified for future long-term neutrino-mass determination experiments. The atomic self-consistent many-electron Dirac--Hartree--Fock--Slater method and the nuclear shell model have been used to predict the partial half-lives and energy-release distributions for the two transitions. The dominant correction terms related to those processes are considered, including the exchange and overlap corrections, and the shake-up and shake-off effects. The normalized distribution of the released energy in the electron-capture decay of $^{95}$Tc to excited states of $^{95}$Mo is compared to that of $^{163}$Ho currently being used for electron-neutrino-mass determination.

## Key claim

The ground-state-to-ground-state electron-capture decay Q value of 95Tc was directly measured to be 1695.92(13) keV using the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap mass spectrometer, and when combined with nuclear energy-level data for the decay daughter 95Mo, two potential ultra-low Q-value transitions were identified for future neutrino-mass determination experiments, though the paper does not claim these transitions are definitively suitable without further study.

## Method

The experiment used the phase-imaging ion-cyclotron-resonance (PI-ICR) technique at the JYFLTRAP double Penning trap mass spectrometer to measure cyclotron frequency ratios between singly charged 95Tc+ and 95Mo+ ions. The 95Tc ions were produced via proton-induced fusion-evaporation reactions on a natural molybdenum target at 45 MeV from the K-130 cyclotron, while 95Mo+ reference ions were prepared using an offline glow-discharge ion source. The atomic self-consistent many-electron Dirac-Hartree-Fock-Slater method and the nuclear shell model were then used to predict partial half-lives and energy-release distributions for the two identified ultra-low Q-value transitions, including exchange and overlap corrections and shake-up and shake-off effects.

## Key numbers

- The ground-state-to-ground-state electron-capture Q value of 95Tc was measured to be 1695.92(13) keV.
- The mass excess of 95Tc was measured to be -86015.95(18) keV/c^2, with a precision about 28 times higher than the value from the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020).
- The excitation energy of the low-lying isomeric state of 95Tc was determined to be 38.910(40) keV.
- The mass uncertainty of the reference daughter 95Mo was 0.12 keV/c^2 from AME2020.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing search for isotopes with ultra-low Q-value beta or electron-capture decays for direct neutrino-mass determination, which has been actively pursued at Penning trap facilities including JYFLTRAP, LEBIT, CPT, ISOTRAP, and SHIPTRAP (Haaranen 2013, Suhonen 2014, Karthein 2019, De Roubin 2020, Ge 2021, Ge 2022, Ramalho 2022, Keblbeck 2023). The normalized distribution of released energy in the electron-capture decay of 95Tc to excited states of 95Mo was compared to that of 163Ho, which is currently being used for electron-neutrino-mass determination in the ECHo and HOLMES experiments. The identification of two potential ultra-low Q-value transitions in 95Tc enables future long-term experiments aimed at determining the electron-neutrino mass, complementing the existing 163Ho and tritium-based approaches.
