# Finite size effects and the mixed quark-hadron phase in neutron stars

> We demonstrate that the form and location (not the size or spacing) of the energetically preferred geometrical structure of the crystalline quark-hadron mixed phase in a neutron star is very sensitive to finite size terms beyond the surface term. We consider two independent approaches of including further finite size terms, namely the multiple reflection expansion of the bag model and an effective surface tension description. Thus care should be taken in any model requiring detailed knowledge of these crystalline structures.

# Finite size effects and the mixed quark-hadron phase in neutron stars

## Key claim

The form and location (but not the size or spacing) of the energetically preferred geometrical structure of the crystalline quark-hadron mixed phase in a neutron star is very sensitive to finite size terms beyond the surface term, such as curvature energy and zero-point energy; this sensitivity makes calculations involving detailed knowledge of the crystalline mixed phase unreliable at present, even for the three simple geometries of spheres, rods, and slabs.

## Method

The authors model the mixed phase of quark and hadronic matter in a neutron star by subdividing it into Wigner-Seitz cells containing one of three discrete geometric forms (drops, rods, or slabs) of the rarer phase immersed in the dominant phase. They minimize the sum of Coulomb energy and finite size energies (surface, curvature, and zero-point terms) for each geometry as a function of the quark volume fraction, using two independent approaches: the multiple reflection expansion of the MIT bag model (with explicit surface tension and curvature coefficients from Madsen (1994), plus a phenomenological zero-point parameter) and an effective surface tension description generalized from the cosmological quark-hadron transition of Kajantie, Potvin, and Rummukainen (1993). The hadronic equation of state uses coupling constants fit to bulk nuclear properties (binding energy -16.3 MeV, saturation density 0.153 fm^-3, symmetry energy 32.5 MeV, compression modulus 240 MeV, effective nucleon mass 0.78), and the quark phase uses the bag model with B^1/4 = 180 MeV, quark masses m_u=5 MeV, m_d=10 MeV, m_s=150 MeV, and alpha_s=0.

## Key numbers

- Hadronic equation of state parameters: binding energy B/A = -16.3 MeV, saturation density rho_0 = 0.153 fm^-3, symmetry energy coefficient a_sym = 32.5 MeV, compression modulus K = 240 MeV, effective nucleon mass m*/m = 0.78.
- Bag constant: B^1/4 = 180 MeV; quark masses: m_u = 5 MeV, m_d = 10 MeV, m_s = 150 MeV.
- Neutron star at mass limit: 1.454 solar masses, radius 10.32 km.
- Quark surface tension from bag model decreases from about 37 to 25 MeV/fm^2 as chemical potentials drop through the star; curvature coefficient decreases from 25 to 12 MeV/fm.
- Hadronic surface tension from nuclear liquid drop model is about 1 MeV/fm^2, negligible compared to quark contribution.
- Structure radii in the mixed phase lie between 2.5 and 7.5 fm; Debye screening lengths are about 5 fm.
- The sum of finite size and Coulomb energy densities is at most 7 MeV/fm^3 in the bag model approach; in the effective surface tension approach it is kept below 10 MeV/fm^3.
- In the effective surface tension model, the surface tension varies between 51 and 66 MeV/fm^3; the parameter delta varies between a fraction of 1 fm to a few fm, and can become negative near chi approximately 0.5.
- The curvature term from the bag model is reproduced in the effective surface tension model for delta approximately 0.6 fm.
- Pure quark matter core extends to R = 2.0 km; pure hadronic matter begins beyond R = 7.9 km.
- When u and d quarks are not assumed massless, structure sizes increase by about 5 percent.
- For the zero-point parameter Z_o, values above 1.5 are required for the model to be reliable; hadronic rods are only preferred in a very narrow range when Z_o is unacceptably small.
- When hadronic surface tension contribution is added (3-4 percent increase), the smallest acceptable Z_o is reduced to 1.3.

## Context

This work extends the calculation of Glendenning and Pei (1995), who first computed the form, size, spacing, and location of the crystalline mixed phase by minimizing Coulomb plus surface energies. It addresses concerns about the treatment of surface tension as a free parameter in Heiselberg, Pethick, and Staubo (1993), where the unstructured phase was found to be preferred for large surface tension; the authors argue that opening a degree of freedom should lower energy, not raise it, making that conclusion suspect. The results show that the location of geometric structures is highly sensitive to higher-order finite size terms, making detailed predictions unreliable, but also suggest that the wide range of glitch phenomena observed in pulsars may be closely related to restructuring in the solid crystalline region of the pulsar.
