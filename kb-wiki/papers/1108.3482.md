# Quantum Fields at Finite Temperature "from tera to nano Kelvin"

> These lectures introduce techniques that are used in the description of systems of particles and fields at high temperature (or density). These methods have a broad range of physical applications. We shall discuss two specific applications: one related to hot and dense matter composed of quarks and gluons, with temperatures in the tera Kelvin range, the other related to Bose-Einstein condensation in ultra-cold gases, with temperatures in the nano Kelvin range. As we shall see, in both systems, long wavelength collective phenomena lead to similar features, in spite of the huge difference in orders of magnitude of the respective energy scales.

## Key claim

These lectures demonstrate that the same quantum field theory techniques used to describe the quark-gluon plasma at tera-Kelvin temperatures can be applied to Bose-Einstein condensation in ultra-cold gases at nano-Kelvin temperatures, because in both systems long-wavelength collective phenomena lead to similar features despite the enormous difference in energy scales. The central paradox addressed is that interactions can have large effects even when the coupling between elementary constituents is small, due to infrared divergences in perturbation theory that require effective field theory techniques to resolve.

## Method

The lectures introduce finite-temperature quantum field theory techniques, beginning with the path integral formulation and perturbative evaluation of the partition function for scalar fields, then extending to QCD. The infrared divergences that plague perturbative calculations at finite temperature are identified, and a simple effective field theory is constructed to handle these divergences by integrating out the hard (high-momentum) modes. This effective theory is then applied to two distinct physical systems: the thermodynamics of the quark-gluon plasma and the shift of the Bose-Einstein condensation transition temperature due to weak repulsive interactions between atoms.

## Key numbers

- The conversion factor between temperature and energy: 300 K corresponds to approximately 1/40 eV, or equivalently 1 eV corresponds to about 120,000 K
- The QCD scale Lambda_QCD is of order 250 MeV
- The bag constant B has value approximately 175 MeV/fm^3, corresponding to B^(1/4) approximately 192 MeV
- The transition temperature T_c in the bag model is given by T_c = (45/(17*pi^2))^(1/4) * B^(1/4) approximately 0.72 * B^(1/4), yielding T_c approximately 150 MeV for B^(1/4) approximately 200 MeV
- The effective number of degrees of freedom in the quark-gluon plasma is 37 = 2*8 + (7/8)*2*2*2*3, accounting for gluons (8 colors, 2 spin states) and quarks (3 colors, 2 spins, 2 flavors, q and anti-q)
- The energy density of massless pions is epsilon = 3 * (pi^2/30) * T^4, and the pressure is P = 3 * (pi^2/90) * T^4
- The energy density of the quark-gluon plasma is epsilon = 37 * (pi^2/30) * T^4 + B, and the pressure is P = 37 * (pi^2/90) * T^4 - B

## Context

These lectures extend the pedagogical tradition of finite-temperature field theory textbooks such as Kapusta (1989), Kapusta and Gale (2006), and Le Bellac (1996), as well as earlier many-body physics texts by Kadanoff and Baym (1962), Abrikosov, Gorkov, and Dzyaloshinskii (1963), and Fetter and Walecka (1971). The lectures are based on earlier review articles by Blaizot (2001, 2002) and Blaizot, Iancu, and Rebhan (2003) for the QCD portion, and Blaizot (2008) for the Bose-Einstein condensation portion. The work enables a unified understanding of how effective field theories can overcome infrared divergences in weakly coupled systems at extreme temperature scales, and provides a bridge between high-energy nuclear physics and cold-atom physics.
