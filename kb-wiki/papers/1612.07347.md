# Deconfinement, gradient and cooling scales for pure SU(2) lattice gauge theory

> We investigate the approach of pure SU(2) lattice gauge theory with the Wilson action to its continuum limit using the deconfining phase transition, the gradient flow and the cooling flow to set the scale. For the gradient and cooling scales we explore three different energy observables and two distinct reference values for the flow time. When the aim is to follow scaling towards the continuum limit, one gains at least a factor of 100 in computational efficiency by relying on the gradient instead of the deconfinement scale. Using cooling instead of the gradient flow one gains another factor of at least 34 in computational efficiency on the gradient flow part without any significant loss in the accuracy of scale setting. Concerning our observables, the message is to keep it simple. The Wilson action itself performs as well as or even better than the other two observables explored. Two distinct fitting forms for scaling are compared of which one connects to asymptotic scaling. Differences of the obtained estimates show that systematic errors of length ratios, though only about 1%, can be considerably larger than statistical errors of the same observables.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for pure SU(2) lattice gauge theory, the gradient flow and cooling flow provide computationally much more efficient alternatives to the deconfining phase transition for setting the scale, with the caveat that systematic errors in length ratios, though only about 1%, can be considerably larger than statistical errors.

## Method

The authors performed Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulations of pure SU(2) lattice gauge theory with the Wilson action, using the Fabricius-Haan-Kennedy-Pendleton heatbath algorithm combined with overrelaxation updates. They extracted the deconfining scale from Polyakov loop susceptibility maxima on lattices up to \(N_\tau=12\), and computed six gradient scales and six cooling scales using three different energy observables (\(E_0\), \(E_1\), \(E_4\)) at two distinct reference flow times, then analyzed scaling and asymptotic scaling behavior.

## Key numbers

- Computational efficiency gain of at least a factor of 100 when using the gradient scale instead of the deconfinement scale.
- Using cooling instead of the gradient flow yields another factor of at least 34 in computational efficiency on the gradient flow part.
- Deconfining scale estimates: \(\beta_c(4) = 2.299188(61)\), \(\beta_c(6) = 2.426366(52)\), \(\beta_c(8) = 2.510363(71)\), \(\beta_c(10) = 2.57826(14)\), \(\beta_c(12) = 2.63625(35)\).
- Gradient scale \(L_1 = s_0^{01}\) at \(\beta=2.3\) on \(8^4\) lattice: 1.361(13); at \(\beta=2.875\) on \(52^4\) lattice: 8.510(64).
- Cooling scale \(L_7 = s_0^{01}\) at \(\beta=2.3\) on \(8^4\) lattice: 1.342(12); at \(\beta=2.875\) on \(52^4\) lattice: 8.514(60).
- Systematic errors of length ratios are about 1% but can be considerably larger than statistical errors.

## Context

This work extends the methodology of Luscher (2010) for the gradient flow scale to pure SU(2) lattice gauge theory, and quantitatively confirms the suggestion by Bonati and D'Elia (2014) that cooling can replace the gradient flow for scale setting with equivalent accuracy. The results enable efficient scale setting in SU(2) studies and provide a benchmark for more complex theories like QCD, while also comparing with earlier deconfinement transition results from Engels et al. (1996) and Lucini et al. (2004).
