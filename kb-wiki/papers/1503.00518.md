# Constraining supernova equations of state with equilibrium constants from heavy-ion collisions

> Cluster formation is a fundamental aspect of the equation of state (EOS) of warm and dense nuclear matter such as can be found in supernovae (SNe). Similar matter can be studied in heavy-ion collisions (HIC). We use the experimental data of Qin et al. [Phys. Rev. Lett. 108, 172701 (2012)] to test calculations of cluster formation and the role of in-medium modifications of cluster properties in SN EOSs. For the comparison between theory and experiment we use chemical equilibrium constants as the main observables. This reduces some of the systematic uncertainties and allows deviations from ideal gas behavior to be identified clearly. In the analysis, we carefully account for the differences between matter in SNe and HICs. We find that, at the lowest densities, the experiment and all theoretical models are consistent with the ideal gas behavior. At higher densities ideal behavior is clearly ruled out and interaction effects have to be considered. The contributions of continuum correlations are of relevance in the virial expansion and remain a difficult problem to solve at higher densities. We conclude that at the densities and temperatures discussed mean-field interactions of nucleons, inclusion of all relevant light clusters, and a suppression mechanism of clusters at high densities have to be incorporated in the SN EOS.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that chemical equilibrium constants extracted from heavy-ion collision data can effectively discriminate between different supernova equations of state, but only after carefully accounting for differences between supernova matter and heavy-ion collision matter, including finite source size, limited nuclear composition, and Coulomb screening effects.

## Method

The authors compared experimental data from Qin et al. (2012) on cluster production in 47A MeV 40Ar collisions with 112,124Sn and 64Zn with 112,124Sn against multiple theoretical supernova equation of state models (HS, STOS, LS, SHT, SHO). They used chemical equilibrium constants as the primary observable, calculated as the ratio of cluster number density to the product of free neutron and proton number densities raised to appropriate powers. The theoretical models were modified to match heavy-ion collision conditions by suppressing heavy nuclei formation, removing Coulomb screening, and constraining the composition to nuclei with Z ≤ 2.

## Key numbers

- Experimental density uncertainties are 17%, temperature uncertainties are 10% for densities below 0.01 fm^-3, increasing to approximately 15% at 0.03 fm^-3
- The emitting source contains roughly 30 to 40 nucleons, representing approximately 20% of the total system
- Lithium appears in less than 1/10 of events, contributing a fractional mass of approximately 2%
- The total proton fraction of the source is Y_p^tot = 0.41
- In the STOS EOS, heavy nuclei have masses ranging from 40 to 140, with approximately 50% or more of nucleons bound in heavy nuclei
- The HS(DD2) model with Z ≤ 2 composition and no Coulomb screening shows good agreement with experimental data, with most deviations within experimental error bars

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Qin et al. (2012) by correcting misinterpretations of the HS equation of state and achieving better agreement between theoretical models at low densities. It builds on the approach of Natowitz et al. (2010), who first demonstrated density effects in clusterized matter from experiments. The study enables more reliable use of heavy-ion collision data to constrain supernova equations of state, particularly regarding in-medium modifications of cluster properties, and identifies that mean-field interactions, inclusion of all relevant light clusters, and cluster suppression mechanisms at high densities must be incorporated in supernova equations of state.
