# Classification of Coupled-Channel Near-Threshold Structures

> Since 2003, plenty of resonant structures have been observed in the heavy quarkonium regime. Many of them are close to the thresholds of a few pairs of heavy hadrons. They are candidates of exotic hadrons and have attracted immense attentions. Based on a coupled-channel nonrelativistic effective field theory, we classify the near-threshold structures of a symmetry-related two-channel system by studying the evolution of the scattering amplitude line shapes and pole positions with the variation of the single-channel scattering length and channel coupling strength. We show that the evolution of the scattering amplitude line shapes can be understood from the pole trajectories in the complex energy plane, and the pole evolution can be traced back to the renormalization group fixed points. We provide a dictionary of correspondence between the evolution of line shapes and pole trajectories along with varying interaction and channel coupling strengths, which can be used to understand the experimental observations of the near-threshold structures.

## Key claim

The paper classifies near-threshold resonant structures in a two-channel system with symmetry-related channels, showing that the evolution of scattering amplitude line shapes can be understood from pole trajectories traced back to renormalization group fixed points. The classification applies specifically when the single-channel scattering lengths and channel coupling strengths are varied, and the authors provide a "dictionary" mapping line shapes to pole locations, but note that the results are at leading order in a zero-range effective field theory and may require generalization to three or more channels for complete treatment.

## Method

The authors use a coupled-channel nonrelativistic zero-range effective field theory (ZREFT) with dimensional regularization and the power divergence subtraction scheme, following the renormalization group treatment of Lensky (2011). They solve the Lippmann-Schwinger equation for S-wave scattering in a two-channel system with nearby thresholds, expanding the potential around renormalization group fixed points (trivial FP, one-pole FP, and two-pole FP). For symmetry-related channels (imposing a22 = a11), they compute the leading-order T-matrix elements and study the pole trajectories on the four Riemann sheets of the complex energy plane as functions of the single-channel scattering length a11 and the channel coupling strength a12, using the D0D*0 and D+D*- system (relevant for X(3872)) as a concrete example with threshold splitting delta = 0.64 fm-1.

## Key numbers

* Threshold splitting between the two channels: delta = 0.64 fm-1 (for the D0D*0 and D+D*- system)
* For case V1, the half-threshold-heights width of |T21| is Gamma = Delta [1 + O(a11-2 delta-2)]
* For case V2, the half-threshold-heights width of |T11| is Gamma = 4 Delta [1/(a11 delta)2 + 1/(a11 delta) + 1 + O(a112 delta2)]
* For case V2, the half-threshold-heights width of |T21| is Gamma = Delta [7/(4(a11 delta)2) + 1/(a11 delta) + 3 + O(a112 delta2)]
* For cases B3 and V3, the half-threshold-heights width of |Ti1| is Gamma = (17/8) Delta [1 + O(|a11| delta)]
* The X(3872) mass is about 8 MeV below the D+D*- threshold

## Context

This work extends the general explanation of near-threshold structures given by Dong (2020), which showed that a nontrivial peak or dip must appear near a heavy hadron threshold with S-wave attractive interaction, but did not address the complications from multiple nearby thresholds. The classification builds on the renormalization group fixed point analysis of Lensky (2011) for two-channel systems and provides a framework to interpret experimental observations of exotic hadron candidates such as X(3872), Zc(3900), Tcc+(3875), Pcs(4338), and X(3960), all of which are close to at least two nearby thresholds related by isospin, SU(3), or heavy quark spin symmetry. The results enable rough determination of pole locations from observed line shapes and can be applied to future experimental data, though the authors note that a three-channel treatment is needed for complete analysis when the lowest channel is weakly coupled to higher channels.
