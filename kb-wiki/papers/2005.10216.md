# Future of Nuclear Fission Theory

> There has been much recent interest in nuclear fission, due in part to a new appreciation of its relevance to astrophysics, stability of superheavy elements, and fundamental theory of neutrino interactions. At the same time, there have been important developments on a conceptual and computational level for the theory. The promising new theoretical avenues were the subject of a workshop held at the University of York in October 2019; this report summarises its findings and recommendations.

## Key claim

This workshop report argues that nuclear fission theory is "ripe for rapid progress" through modern microscopic frameworks and computational tools, but explicitly acknowledges that all quantitative nuclear models remain phenomenological at some level and that "fully microscopic" or "from first principles" descriptions are not yet a reality for fission.

## Method

The paper summarizes findings and recommendations from the Workshop on Future of Theory in Fission held at the University of York in October 2019. It reviews the current state of microscopic fission theory, including density functional theory (DFT), time-dependent DFT, the generator coordinate method, and macroscopic-microscopic approaches, and outlines challenges and possible solutions for future development rather than providing a detailed review.

## Key numbers

- Spontaneous fission half-lives span from "microseconds or smaller to billions of years"
- In odd-A nuclei, spontaneous fission is "typically hindered by ~3-5 orders of magnitude relative to their even-even neighbours"
- The paper mentions time scales for fission stages but provides no specific numerical values for these times

## Context

This report extends discussions from earlier reviews such as Schunck and Robledo (2016) on microscopic models and Andreyev et al. (2017), Talou et al. (2018), and Schmidt and Jurado (2018) on fission phenomenology. It identifies key open questions including whether a unified microscopic theory can cover the entire energy range from spontaneous fission to fission well above the barrier, and whether microscopic theory can justify successful phenomenological assumptions. The work enables future coordinated community efforts to develop microscopic fission theory using modern computational capabilities.
