# Unraveling the reaction mechanisms leading to partial fusion of weakly bound nuclei

> Collisions between complex nuclei may give rise to their total or partial fusion. The latter case is found experimentally to gain importance when one of the colliding nuclei is weakly bound. It has been commonly assumed that the partial fusion mechanism is a two-step process, whose first step is the dissociation of the weakly bound nucleus, followed by the capture of one of the fragments. To assess this interpretation, we present the first implementation of the three-body model of inclusive breakup proposed in the 1980s by Austern et al. [Phys. Rep. 154, 125 (1987)] that accounts for both the direct, one-step, partial fusion and the two-step mechanism proceeding via the projectile continuum states. Contrary to the widely assumed picture, we find that, at least for the investigated cases, the partial fusion is largely dominated by the direct capture from the projectile ground-state.

# Unraveling the reaction mechanisms leading to partial fusion of weakly bound nuclei

## Key claim
Contrary to the widely assumed breakup-fusion picture in which partial fusion proceeds as a two-step process (projectile dissociation followed by fragment capture), the authors find that for the investigated cases the incomplete fusion (ICF) is largely dominated by direct capture from the projectile ground state. The two-step mechanism, while not completely negligible, represents a minor contribution, with differences between the full calculation and the ground-state-only calculation remaining less than 12% for all explored incident and binding energies.

## Method
The authors implemented the three-body IAV (Ichimura-Austern-Vincent) model of inclusive breakup using a continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) expansion of the three-body scattering wavefunction, which is the first practical application of this theory. They compared the full IAV-CDCC calculation with two approximations: the IAV-DWBA (distorted-wave Born approximation) and the IAV-CDCC(gs) which retains only the projectile ground-state component, thereby isolating the direct one-step mechanism from the two-step breakup-fusion path. The method was applied to the deuteron-induced reaction 93Nb(d,pX) at 25.5 MeV and to the 6Li+209Bi reaction at several energies around the Coulomb barrier.

## Key numbers
- For the 93Nb(d,pX) reaction at Ed=25.5 MeV, the three calculations (IAV-DWBA, IAV-CDCC, IAV-CDCC(gs)) give very similar results for the NEB differential cross section at Ep=14 MeV.
- For the 6Li+209Bi reaction at 40 MeV incident energy, the IAV-DWBA differs from the full IAV-CDCC by only 7%, and the IAV-CDCC(gs) differs by 11%.
- For the 6Li+209Bi reaction at 36 MeV with artificially varied separation energy, the IAV-CDCC(gs) underestimates the full IAV-CDCC result by approximately 11% for the most weakly bound case considered (Sαd=1 MeV).
- The complete fusion suppression for 6Li+209Bi amounts to approximately 30%, as found in many experiments and confirmed by the calculations.

## Context
This work extends the IAV model previously applied in DWBA approximation by Lei and Moro (2019) to reactions such as 6,7Li+209Bi, which successfully reproduced observed complete fusion suppression but could not distinguish between one-step and two-step ICF mechanisms. The results contradict the breakup-fusion picture developed by Udagawa and Tamura (1981) and by Ichimura, Austern and Vincent (1985), and challenge the interpretation of recent experimental results by Cook (2019) that suggested a direct mechanism. The implementation enables future studies of the dependence of ICF on projectile binding energy and incident energy, and provides a benchmark for assessing the accuracy of the commonly used DWBA approximation.
