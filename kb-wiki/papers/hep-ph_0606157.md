# Color Superconductivity in Dense, but not Asymptotically Dense, Quark Matter

> At ultra-high density, matter is expected to form a degenerate Fermi gas of quarks in which there is a condensate of Cooper pairs of quarks near the Fermi surface: color superconductivity. In this chapter we review some of the underlying physics, and discuss outstanding questions about the phase structure of ultra-dense quark matter. We then focus on describing recent results on the crystalline color superconducting phase that may be the preferred form of cold, dense but not asymptotically dense, three-flavor quark matter. The gap parameter and free energy for this phase have recently been evaluated within a Ginzburg-Landau approximation for many candidate crystal structures. We describe the two that are most favorable. The robustness of these phases results in their being favored over wide ranges of density. However, it also implies that the Ginzburg-Landau approximation is not quantitatively reliable. We describe qualitative insights into what makes a crystal structure favorable which can be used to winnow the possibilities. We close with a look ahead at the calculations that remain to be done in order to make quantitative contact with observations of compact stars.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the phase structure of dense quark matter and argues that the crystalline color superconducting phase, evaluated within a Ginzburg-Landau approximation, is a strong candidate for the ground state of cold, dense but not asymptotically dense, three-flavor quark matter, although the Ginzburg-Landau approximation is not quantitatively reliable due to the robustness of these phases.

## Method

The authors review the underlying physics of color superconductivity and discuss outstanding questions about the phase structure of ultra-dense quark matter. They then describe recent results on the crystalline color superconducting phase, where the gap parameter and free energy were evaluated within a Ginzburg-Landau approximation for many candidate crystal structures, identifying the two most favorable ones. The analysis uses an NJL model with the interaction strength chosen such that the CFL gap parameter is 25 MeV at zero strange quark mass stress.

## Key numbers

* CFL gap parameter Δ₀ ~ 10-100 MeV at asymptotically high densities where the strange quark mass is negligible.
* Transition from CFL to gapless CFL (gCFL) phase occurs at μ ≈ ½ M_s²/Δ₀, with the CFL phase stable when M_s²/μ < 2Δ₀.
* The gCFL phase remains favored beyond M_s²/μ ≈ 4Δ₀, the value at which the CFL phase would become unfavored.
* The CFL gap parameter is set to 25 MeV at M_s²/μ = 0 in the NJL model used for the crystalline phase calculations.
* The critical temperatures for most phases discussed are expected to be of order 10 MeV or higher, while the core temperature of a neutron star drops below this value within minutes of its creation.

## Context

This review extends prior work on color superconductivity, including the CFL phase established by Alford (1998) and the gapless CFL phase studied by Alford (2003, 2004), which was later found to suffer from a magnetic instability as shown by Huang (2004), Casalbuoni (2004), and others. The paper focuses on the crystalline color superconducting phase, originally proposed by Alford (2000) and Bowers (2001), as a resolution to the gCFL instability, and it enables future quantitative calculations needed to make contact with observations of compact stars.
