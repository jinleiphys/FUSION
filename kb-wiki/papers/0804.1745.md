# Relativistic Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions without Hydrodynamics

> The partition function of nonequilibrium distribution which we recently obtained [arXiv:0802.0259] in the framework of the maximum isotropization model (MIM) is exploited to extract physical information from experimental data on the proton rapidity and transverse mass distributions. We propose to partition all interacting nucleons into ensembles in accordance with the number of collisions. We analyze experimental rapidity distribution and get the number of particles in every collision ensemble. We argue that even a large number of effective nucleon collisions cannot lead to thermalization of nucleon system; the thermal source which describes the proton distribution in central rapidity region arises as a result of fast thermalization of the parton degrees of freedom. The obtained number of nucleons which corresponds to the thermal contribution is treated as a ``nucleon power'' of the created quark-gluon plasma in a particular experiment.

## Key claim

Even a large number of effective nucleon collisions (up to M=12 or M=13) cannot lead to thermalization of the nucleon system; the thermal source that describes the proton distribution in the central rapidity region arises instead from fast thermalization of parton degrees of freedom, and the number of nucleons from this thermal contribution is treated as the "nucleon power" of the created quark-gluon plasma.

## Method

The authors partition all interacting nucleons into ensembles according to the number of collisions M each nucleon has undergone, using the maximum isotropization model (MIM) to derive partial distribution functions D_M(p) for each ensemble. They fit the sum of these partial contributions plus a thermal term to experimental proton rapidity distributions from NA49 (Pb+Pb at 158A GeV) and E802 (Au+Au at 11.6A GeV/c), extracting the coefficients C(M) and C_therm that represent the absolute number of protons in each collision ensemble and from the thermal source, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the maximum isotropization model (MIM) previously developed by the same authors (Anchishkin and Yezhov, 2007) and applies it to experimental data from NA49 (1999) and E802 (1999) collaborations. It contrasts with the standard relativistic hydrodynamics approach originating from Landau (1953) and later applied to perfect fluid descriptions of quark-gluon plasma (Gyulassy, 2004). The model enables extraction of the number of nucleons originating from a thermalized parton source (QGP) without solving hydrodynamic equations, and the authors find that QGP was created not only at SPS energies but also, with higher nucleon power, at AGS energies.
