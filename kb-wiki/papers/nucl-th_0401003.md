# A cluster version of the GGT sum rule

> We discuss the derivation of a ``cluster sum rule'' from the Gellmann-Goldberger-Thirring (GGT) sum rule as an alternative to the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule, which was used as the basis up to now. We compare differences in the assumptions and approximations. Some applications of the sum rule for halo nuclei, as well as, nuclei with a pronounced cluster structure are discussed.

# A cluster version of the GGT sum rule

## Key claim

The cluster sum rule, previously derived from the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule, can be derived equivalently from the Gellmann-Goldberger-Thirring (GGT) sum rule, yielding the same mathematical expression but with different underlying assumptions, specifically, the GGT derivation does not rely on the long-wavelength limit, Siegert's theorem, or restriction to dipole excitations, and it remains valid in both nonrelativistic and relativistic frameworks.

## Method

The authors start from the GGT sum rule, which is based on causality and analyticity of the forward elastic scattering amplitude, using a once-subtracted dispersion relation and the optical theorem to relate the real part of the amplitude to the total photoabsorption cross section. They decompose a composite system into two or more clusters and take the difference of the total photoabsorption cross sections of the composite system and its constituent clusters, assuming that the real parts of the forward scattering amplitudes at infinite energy cancel. Under a "spectator approximation", where the internal excitation of each cluster is unchanged by the presence of the other, this cross-section difference is identified with the breakup cross section into the specific cluster channel.

## Key numbers

- The classical TRK sum rule gives an integrated photoabsorption cross section of 60 (NZ/A) MeV mb.
- For a two-cluster system with charges Z_a, Z_b and mass numbers A_a, A_b, the GGT cluster sum rule yields an integrated cross section of [(Z_a A_b - Z_b A_a)^2 / (A_a A_b A_c)] × 60 MeV mb.
- For ^6He → ^4He + 2n, the GGT cluster sum rule predicts 20 MeV mb; the experimental result is 26 ± 5 MeV mb.
- For ^8He → ^6He + 2n, the GGT cluster sum rule predicts 10 MeV mb; the experimental result is 7.5 ± 1.4 MeV mb.
- For ^8He → ^4He + 4n, the GGT cluster sum rule predicts 30 MeV mb (no experimental value given).
- For ^9Be → 2α + n, the GGT cluster sum rule predicts 13.3 MeV mb; the experimental cross section below 2.2 MeV is only about 0.33 MeV mb.
- For ^6Li + γ → α + p + n, the sum rule prediction is 1/3 of the total integrated photo cross section.
- For ^16O + γ → ^14N + p + n, the sum rule prediction is 1/8 of the total integrated photo cross section.
- For ^16O + γ → ^15N + n, the sum rule prediction is 1/15 of the total integrated photo cross section.

## Context

This work extends the cluster sum rule approach previously derived from the TRK sum rule by Alhassid, Bertsch, and Gai (1982) and applied to halo nuclei by Ikeda (1992) and Bertsch (1998). The GGT-based derivation provides an independent foundation that is not restricted to dipole transitions or the long-wavelength limit, and it avoids assumptions about local potentials or exchange currents. The results enable quantitative tests of cluster configurations in exotic nuclei, such as ^6He, ^8He, and neutron-rich oxygen isotopes, by comparing measured breakup cross sections to the sum rule predictions, and they offer a framework for studying low-lying dipole strength (the "pigmy resonance") in nuclei far from stability.
