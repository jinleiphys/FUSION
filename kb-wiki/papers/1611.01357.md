# Neutron star properties and the equation of state for its core

> Few unified equations of state for neutron star matter where core and crust are described using the same nuclear model are available. However the use of non-unified equations of state with a simplified matching between the crust and the core has been shown to introduce uncertainties in the radius determination which can be larger than the expected precision of the next generation of X-ray satellites. We aim at eliminating the dependence of the radius and mass of neutron staron the detailed model for the crust and on the crust-core matching procedure. We solve the approximate equations of the hydrostatic equilibrium for the crust of neutron stars obtaining a precise formula for the radius which depends only on the core mass and radius, and on the baryon chemical potential at the core-crust interface and on the crust surface. For a fully accreted crust one needs additionally the value of the total deep crustal heating per one accreted nucleon. For typical neutron star masses the approximate approach allows to determine the neutron star radius with an error ~0.1% (~ 10 m, equivalent to a 1% inaccuracy in the crust thickness). The formalism applies to neutron stars with a catalyzed or a fully accreted crust. The difference in the neutron star radius between the two models is proportional to the total energy release due to deep crustal heating. For a given model of dense matter describing the neutron star core, the radius of a neutron star can be accurately determined independently of the crust model with a precision much better than the ~5% one expected from the next generation of X-ray satellites. This allows to circumvent the problem of the radius uncertainty which may arise when non-unified equations of state for the crust and the core are used.

# Neutron star properties and the equation of state for its core

## Key claim

The radius of a neutron star can be determined independently of the crust model with a precision much better than the ~5% expected from next-generation X-ray satellites, provided one knows only the core mass and radius, the baryon chemical potential at the core-crust interface, and the chemical potential at the surface. This eliminates the uncertainty introduced by non-unified equations of state where the crust and core are described using different nuclear models.

## Method

The authors solve the approximate Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations of hydrostatic equilibrium for the crust of neutron stars, deriving a precise formula for the radius that depends only on the core mass and radius, and on the baryon chemical potential at the core-crust interface and at the crust surface. For a fully accreted crust, the formula additionally requires the total deep crustal heating per one accreted nucleon. The approach is tested against exact unified equations of state for two dense matter models (DH and NL3ωρ) that satisfy the observational constraint of maximum mass above 2 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- For typical neutron star masses, the approximate approach determines the neutron star radius with an error ~0.1% (~10 m), equivalent to a 1% inaccuracy in the crust thickness.
- For the NL3ωρ equation of state, the difference between exact and approximate radii is 20 m at 1.0 solar masses, 8 m at 1.5 solar masses, and 3 m at 2.0 solar masses.
- For the DH equation of state, the difference between exact and approximate radii is 10 m at 1.0 solar masses and less than 4 m at 1.5-2.0 solar masses.
- The crust mass approximation is accurate up to 6% for masses above 1 solar mass, resulting in less than 0.3% error in total mass determination.
- The difference in neutron star radius between a catalyzed crust and an accreted crust is 80 m for a 1.4 solar mass star, and ranges from 330 m at 0.5 solar masses to 50 m at 1.8 solar masses.
- For the accreted crust model with total energy release Q_tot = 1.9 MeV, the approximate formula for the radius difference has an inaccuracy of about 1 m at 1.5 solar masses.
- The approximate formula for the crust thickness has a relative error less than 0.7% for the real core-crust boundary and less than 3.5% for an artificially deep boundary at nuclear density, equivalent to less than 10 m and less than 100 m inaccuracy respectively.
- The baryon chemical potential at the core-crust interface is 953.3 MeV for the DH equation of state and 954.6 MeV for the NL3ωρ equation of state.
- The chemical potential at zero pressure for cold catalyzed matter is 930.4 MeV (iron-56).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Lattimer (2007) and earlier studies by Zdunik (2002, 2008, 2011) on separating the TOV equation into factors dependent on stellar properties and the equation of state. It directly addresses the problem identified by Fortin (2016) that non-unified equations of state with simplified matching between crust and core introduce radius uncertainties of order 5%, which is as large as the precision expected from next-generation X-ray telescopes such as NICER, Athena, and potential LOFT-like missions. The formalism enables accurate determination of neutron star radii independently of the crust model, circumventing the need for unified equations of state and allowing reliable comparison with observational data from these upcoming missions.
