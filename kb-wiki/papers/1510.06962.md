# Measuring the basic parameters of neutron stars using model atmospheres

> Model spectra of neutron star atmospheres are nowadays widely used to fit the observed thermal X-ray spectra of neutron stars. This fitting is the key element in the method of the neutronstar radius determination. Here, we present the basic assumptions used for the neutron star atmosphere modeling as well as the main qualitative features of the stellar atmospheres leading to the deviations of the emergent model spectrum from blackbody. We describe the properties of two of our model atmosphere grids: (i) pure carbon atmospheres for relatively cool neutron stars (1--4 MK) and (ii) hot atmospheres with Compton scattering taken into account. The results obtained by applying these grids to model the X-ray spectra of the central compact object in supernova remnant HESS 1731-347, and two X-ray bursting neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries, 4U 1724-307 and 4U 1608-52, are presented. Possible systematic uncertainties associated with the obtained neutron star radii are discussed.

## Key claim

Model atmosphere spectra can be used to determine neutron star masses and radii, but the derived radii carry systematic uncertainties from the assumed chemical composition, the treatment of Compton scattering, and the distance to the source; the method yields constraints rather than unique values because the mass-radius relation curves from spectral fitting and Eddington flux measurements depend on the unknown hydrogen mass fraction and distance.

## Method

The authors computed two grids of neutron star model atmospheres: (i) pure carbon atmospheres for relatively cool neutron stars (effective temperatures 1–4 MK) using standard hydrostatic equilibrium, radiative transfer with bound-free, bound-bound, and free-free opacities, and the occupation probability formalism for pressure ionization; and (ii) hot atmospheres with Compton scattering taken into account for luminous neutron stars with effective temperatures above 10 MK. These model spectra were fitted to observed thermal X-ray spectra of the central compact object in supernova remnant HESS J1731-347 and of two X-ray bursting neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries (4U 1724-307 and 4U 1608-52) to obtain constraints on the apparent radius R_infinity and the Eddington flux, which together yield mass-radius curves.

## Key numbers

- Pure carbon atmosphere grid covers effective temperatures 1–4 MK (1–4 million Kelvin) for relatively cool neutron stars.
- For a pure hydrogen atmosphere with X = 1, the Eddington temperature T_Edd,infinity = 1.64 × 10^7 K is used to generate the mass-radius constraint curve.
- The observed Eddington flux F_Edd = 5.61 × 10^(-8) erg s^(-1) cm^(-2) and normalization K = 1.37 × 10^(-32) are shown for two distances: D = 5.3 kpc (thick curves) and D = 7.7 kpc (thin curves); at the larger distance the curves do not intersect, indicating no solution.
- The pressure-scale height in a neutron star atmosphere is approximately h_p ≈ 1.3 × T_6 × (g - g_rad)_14^(-1) cm, where T_6 is temperature in units of 10^6 K and (g - g_rad)_14 is the net surface gravity in units of 10^14 cm s^(-2).
- The sedimentation time for heavy elements in a neutron star atmosphere is t_sed ≈ 0.04 × (ρ^1.3) / (g_14^2 × T_6^0.3) seconds.
- The critical mass accretion rate that disrupts gravitational separation is approximately 3 × 10^16 × R_6^2 g/s, corresponding to about 5 × 10^(-10) × R_6^2 solar masses per year, which provides a luminosity of about 6 × 10^36 erg/s or about 0.03 L_Edd for a neutron star with R = 10 km and M = 1.5 solar masses.
- The surface temperature in cool hydrogen atmospheres can be as low as T_surf ≈ 0.2 T_eff.

## Context

This review extends the work of Ho and Heinke (2009), who first showed that pure carbon atmosphere models give reasonable neutron star radii (10–14 km) for the central compact object in Cassiopeia A, in contrast to hydrogen models that gave emitting-region sizes of only 4–5.5 km (Pavlov and Luna 2009). The authors' own prior grids (Suleimanov et al. 2011, 2012, 2014) provide the model spectra used here for the CCO in HESS J1731-347 (Klochkov et al. 2013, 2015) and for the cooling-tail method applied to 4U 1724-307 and 4U 1608-52 (Suleimanov et al. 2011, Poutanen et al. 2014). The work enables more robust radius measurements by quantifying how chemical composition, Compton scattering, and distance uncertainties affect the derived mass-radius relation, and it identifies the need for better constraints on the hydrogen mass fraction in accreting systems.
