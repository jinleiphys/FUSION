# The neutron skin effect in Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76A TeV at the LHC

> Collisions of lead nuclei at relativistic energies provide valuable insight into the properties of the quark gluon plasma formed in such collisions where the initial geometry and density profile play a crucial role in governing the subsequent evolution of the produced hot and dense fireball. The neutron skin thickness resulting from the difference between the neutron and proton density distributions in neutron rich lead nuclei plays an important role in nuclear structure studies. In this work we investigate the impact of neutron skin on the space time evolution of the fireball formed in Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76A TeV at the LHC and analyze how the presence of neutron skin affect bulk observables sensitive to the initial nuclear structure. The time evolution of initial profile along with the average $p_T$, particle spectra and anisotropic flow parameters are estimated to investigate the effect of neutron skin on these observables. The initial spatial anisotropy of the fireball is found to be affected by the neutron skin thickness significantly especially for the peripheral collisions. This leads to a substantial enhancement of the elliptic flow of hadrons with an even stronger effect observed for photons. In addition, the effect is found to be more pronounced for lower beam energy collisions of lead nuclei.

# The neutron skin effect in Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76A TeV at the LHC

## Key claim
The inclusion of a finite neutron skin thickness in lead nuclei, as measured by the PREX experiment, significantly enhances the elliptic flow of hadrons and especially thermal photons in peripheral Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76A TeV, while leaving particle spectra and average temperature nearly unchanged. The effect is more pronounced at lower beam energies and in peripheral collisions, but the authors note that a more detailed analysis incorporating event-by-event fluctuations and viscous effects is required for quantitative assessment.

## Method
The authors used a (2+1)-dimensional ideal relativistic hydrodynamic model (MUSIC) with Glauber model initial conditions, comparing two cases: a single Woods-Saxon nucleon density distribution (without neutron skin effect) and separate proton and neutron Woods-Saxon distributions (with neutron skin effect). The neutron skin parameters were taken from the PREX experiment and prior nuclear structure studies, with proton radius 6.680 fm and proton diffuseness 0.447 fm, and neutron radius 6.70 ± 0.03 fm with neutron diffuseness 0.55 ± 0.03 fm. The initial energy density was a linear combination of wounded nucleon and binary collision contributions, and the Cooper-Frye formalism was used for hadron production while thermal photon rates were integrated over the full space-time evolution.

## Key numbers
- Neutron skin thickness for 208Pb: Δnp ≈ 0.28 fm (from PREX experiment, cited in text)
- Nuclear radius for lead (single distribution): d_Pb = 6.624 fm; nuclear skin thickness: a_Pb = 0.549 fm
- Proton distribution parameters: d_Pb^p = 6.680 fm, a_Pb^p = 0.447 fm
- Neutron distribution parameters: d_Pb^n = 6.70 ± 0.03 fm, a_Pb^n = 0.55 ± 0.03 fm
- Initial formation time: τ0 = 0.14 fm/c; central energy density: 576 GeV/fm^3
- Freeze-out temperature: 145 MeV; total charged particle multiplicity for most central collisions: about 1600
- Nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section at 2.76A TeV: 64 mb; at 39A TeV: 80 mb
- Impact parameters used for centrality bins: b = 2.33 fm (0-5%), 5.85 fm (10-20%), 8.97 fm (30-40%), 12.25 fm (60-70%)

## Context
This work extends prior studies on neutron skin effects in relativistic nuclear collisions by Paukkunen (2015), Helenius (2017), and De (2016) by using a full hydrodynamic evolution to compute bulk observables including anisotropic flow. It builds on the PREX experiment's direct measurement of neutron skin thickness in 208Pb and addresses the long-standing discrepancy between theoretical model calculations of photon anisotropic flow and experimental data. The results suggest that including neutron skin effects can partially reduce this discrepancy for peripheral collisions, and the authors identify event-by-event fluctuations and viscous effects as necessary next steps for quantitative assessment.
