# Non-Central Heavy-Ion Collisions are the Place to Look for DCC

> We give two reasons why we believe that non-central ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions are the place to look for the disoriented chiral condensates (DCC). First, we argue that the most probable quench scenario for the formation of DCC requires non-central collisions. Second, we show by numerical simulations that strong electromagnetic fields of heavy ions can exert a surprisingly large effect on the DCC domain formation through the chiral anomaly. The effect again requires non-central collisions. Interestingly, the result of simulations is consistent with the formation of correlated two domains of the chiral condensate, which are aligned in space, perpendicular to the scattering plane, but misaligned in isospin space.

## Key claim

Non-central ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions are argued to be the optimal environment for observing disoriented chiral condensates (DCC), based on two reasons: the most probable quench scenario for DCC formation requires non-central collisions, and strong electromagnetic fields in such collisions can significantly affect DCC domain formation through the chiral anomaly.

## Method

The authors present theoretical arguments for the quench scenario in non-central collisions and perform numerical simulations of the chiral anomaly's effect on DCC domain formation in the presence of strong electromagnetic fields. The simulations examine how the electromagnetic fields of heavy ions influence the formation and alignment of DCC domains through the chiral anomaly mechanism.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for DCC formation in heavy-ion collisions by identifying non-central collisions as the most promising experimental configuration. The authors argue that previous searches for DCC in central collisions may have been suboptimal, and that the chiral anomaly effect in strong electromagnetic fields provides a new mechanism for DCC formation that has not been considered in prior work. The results suggest specific experimental signatures to look for in non-central collision data.
