# Particlization with local event-by-event conservation laws

> We present a sampling method for the transition from relativistic hydrodynamics to particle transport, commonly referred to as particlization, which preserves the local event-by-event conservation of energy, momentum, baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge. The proposed method is essential for studying fluctuations and correlations by means of stochastic hydrodynamics. It is also useful for studying small systems. The method is based on Metropolis sampling applied to particles within distinct patches of the hypersurface, where hydrodynamics and kinetic evolutions are matched.

## Key claim

The paper presents a particlization method that preserves local event-by-event conservation of energy, momentum, baryon number, strangeness, and electric charge microcanonically, i.e. in every sample, which is essential for studying fluctuations and correlations via stochastic hydrodynamics but has not been done previously in the context of relativistic hydrodynamics.

## Method

The method splits the switching hypersurface into independent patches of similar size (roughly 50-1000 computational cells) and enforces conservation laws over each patch using a Metropolis algorithm. The Markov chain proposes 2-to-3 and 3-to-2 stochastic "collisions" on the hypersurface that conserve energy, momentum, and quantum numbers, with acceptance probabilities computed from the Cooper-Frye distribution and phase-space integrals. The algorithm preserves local cell-by-cell variations of temperature, chemical potentials, and collective velocities within each patch.

## Key numbers

- Patch size requirement: patch should contain many particles such that \(b^3 n \gg 1\) and be much larger than thermal length: \(b \gg 1/T\), where \(T\) is temperature.
- In typical relativistic ion collision simulations, average number of particles per computational cell is of order \(10^{-3} - 10^{-1}\) given cell sizes between (0.2 fm)\(^3\) and (0.5 fm)\(^3\).
- A patch consists of roughly 50-1000 computational cells; a typical hypersurface of about \(10^6\) cells results in about \(10^3 - 10^4\) patches.
- In the test scenario with three cells, temperatures were \(T_1 = 0.155\) GeV, \(T_2 = 0.165\) GeV, \(T_3 = 0.175\) GeV; collective velocities were \(\vec{v}_1 = (0.2, 0.3, 0.4)\), \(\vec{v}_2 = (0.1, 0.5, 0.5)\), \(\vec{v}_3 = (0.3, 0.4, 0.2)\).
- Scaled variances of multiplicities in the patch agreed within 0.5% with the microcanonical expectation in the thermodynamic limit computed using analytic formulas from Hauer (2007).
- Runtime to generate \(10^5\) samples for the test scenario was around 2 minutes; for a realistic hypersurface at \(\sqrt{s} \simeq 10\) GeV with about 150 patches of \(\sim 10\) GeV, the implementation takes about 40 minutes to generate 10000 samples.

## Context

This work extends hybrid approaches that combine relativistic hydrodynamics with kinetic transport, where standard Cooper-Frye sampling is grand-canonical and produces scaled variances \(\omega = 1\) by construction. Previous attempts at canonical particlization include the SER algorithm by Schwarz (2017) and Oliinychenko (2016), which samples the correct canonical distribution but cannot be extended to the microcanonical ensemble, as well as ad hoc modifications by Bozek (2012) and Huovinen (2012). The method enables studies of correlations and fluctuations in combination with stochastic hydrodynamics, and may be applied to small systems where event-by-event conservation laws have large impact, as well as charge-dependent correlations relevant for the chiral magnetic effect.
