# Common Signatures of Statistical Coulomb Fragmentation of Highly Excited Nuclei and Phase Transitions in Confined Microcanonical Systems

> Characteristic signatures of statistical Coulomb fragmentation of highly excited nuclear systems were analyzed. It was found that in some important aspects, they coincide with perceived signatures of phase transitions in confined hypothetical pseudo-microcanonical systems, thus potentially giving rise to a false interpretation of experimental observations in terms of phase transitions. It is demonstrated that the heat capacity as derived based on experimental observations may show domains of faux negative heat capacity for the same fundamental reason a faux negative heat capacity appears in constrained numerical modeling of phase transitions in excited nuclear matter, the reason being an effective truncation of the microcanonical phase space. Similarly, selected experimental data may exhibit bimodality apparently in accordance with the truncated pseudo-microcanonical (but not the true microcanonical) calculations for confined systems.

# Common Signatures of Statistical Coulomb Fragmentation of Highly Excited Nuclei and Phase Transitions in Confined Microcanonical Systems

## Key claim

The signatures of statistical Coulomb fragmentation of highly excited nuclei (generalized fission) coincide in important aspects with perceived signatures of phase transitions in confined pseudo-microcanonical systems, potentially giving rise to false interpretation of experimental observations in terms of phase transitions. The heat capacity derived from experimental observations may show domains of faux negative heat capacity for the same fundamental reason that faux negative heat capacity appears in constrained numerical modeling: an effective truncation of the microcanonical phase space.

## Method

The authors constructed a schematic two-phase model allowing only mono-nuclear and symmetric di-nuclear configurations of a finite nuclear system, incorporating both surface energy and surface entropy from the diffuse nuclear surface domain using Thomas-Fermi approximation and liquid-drop model parameters. They calculated conditional entropies and weight functions for each configuration as functions of excitation energy, then examined how the apparent thermodynamic quantities (entropy, temperature, heat capacity) change when intermediate macroscopic configurations are included versus excluded from the model phase space. The model was then extended to interpret Coulomb fragmentation as a process where the system reaches multifragment saddle configurations and is pulled apart by Coulomb forces, with weight functions for different saddle shapes intersecting at characteristic excitation energies.

## Key numbers

- The crossing of conditional entropies for mono- and di-nuclear phases occurs at approximately 8.35 MeV/u excitation energy per nucleon (Fig. 1).
- Level density parameters are expressed as a_m = A/14 (1 + 4A^{-1/3}) MeV^{-1} for mono-nuclear and a_d = 2(A/28)[1 + (A/2)^{-1/3}] MeV^{-1} for di-nuclear configurations.
- The surface energy difference between di-nuclear and mono-nuclear configurations is given by E_d - E_m = c_Surf (F_2^d - 1) A^{2/3}, where F_2^d = 2^{1/3} is the ratio of surface area of the symmetric di-nucleus to that of a sphere of the same volume.
- Model calculations were performed for three system sizes: thermodynamic limit (TL, A approaching infinity), large system (A=200), and small system (A=80).
- The inter-phase configuration used to demonstrate healing of the convex intruder had a deformation parameter F_2 = 1.13, halfway between mono-nuclear (F_2=1) and di-nuclear (F_2=2^{1/3}) configurations.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous findings (Toke, 2006; Toke, 2007) that asymmetric fission with proper accounting for surface entropy and thermal expansion can explain intermediate-mass fragment yields without invoking phase transitions. It directly challenges the interpretation of multifragmentation as a phase transition as implemented in the SMM (Bondorf, 1995), MMMC (Gross, 1990), and lattice gas models, showing that the same signatures (negative heat capacity, bimodality) can arise trivially from truncation of phase space in numerical calculations. The paper demonstrates that apparent negative heat capacity can be eliminated by including just one intermediate macroscopic configuration between the two base phases, and argues that claims of negative heat capacity and bimodality from truncated model calculations (such as those by Chomaz, 2004 and Gross, 2001) do not provide a sound foundation for inferring true thermodynamic behavior of physical systems.
