# Explosive nucleosynthesis: nuclear physics impact using neutrino-driven wind simulations

> We present nucleosynthesis studies based on hydrodynamical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and their subsequent neutrino-driven winds. Although the conditions found in these simulations are not suitable for the rapid neutron capture (r-process) to produce elements heavier than A$\sim$130, this can be solved by artificially increasing the wind entropy. In this way one can mimic the general behavior of an ejecta where the r-process occurs. We study the impact of the long-time dynamical evolution and of the nuclear physics input on the final abundances and show that different nuclear mass models lead to significant variations in the abundances. These differences can be linked to the behavior of nuclear masses far from stability. In addition, we have analyzed in detail the effect of neutron capture and beta-delayed neutron emission when matter decays back to stability. In all our studied cases, freeze out effects are larger than previously estimated and produce substantial changes in the post freeze out abundances.

## Key claim

Although the neutrino-driven wind simulations used in this study do not naturally produce conditions suitable for the r-process to synthesize elements heavier than A~130, artificially increasing the wind entropy by a factor of two (equivalent to halving the density) allows the authors to mimic a high-entropy ejecta and study the impact of nuclear physics input and long-time dynamical evolution on the final r-process abundances.

## Method

The authors used long-time hydrodynamical trajectories from Arcones, Janka, and Scheck (2007) and artificially increased the entropy by a factor of two to enable r-process nucleosynthesis up to the third peak. They employed a full reaction network at high temperatures and a fully implicit network for the r-process phase that includes photodissociation, neutron capture, beta-decay, and fission. They systematically varied the nuclear mass model (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-17, Duflo-Zuker) and the treatment of the reverse shock to study the sensitivity of final abundances to both nuclear physics input and dynamical evolution, distinguishing between hot (T~1 GK) and cold (T<0.5 GK) r-process conditions.

## Key numbers

- The entropy was artificially increased by a factor of two (equivalent to dividing the density by a factor of two) to produce the third r-process peak.
- The reverse shock was moved to a temperature of T_rs ≈ 1 GK for one set of trajectories.
- A mass element ejected at 8 seconds after the explosion was used for the temperature evolution study.
- The largest differences in abundances between mass models appear in the region around A ~ 185, linked to the behavior of two-neutron separation energies before N=126.
- For the FRDM mass model, an anomalous behavior before N=90 produces accumulation of matter and peak formation around A ≈ 135 even for the cold r-process.

## Context

This work extends the nucleosynthesis studies of Arcones, Janka, and Scheck (2007) and Fischer et al. (2010), which found that conditions in modern long-time supernova simulations are not suitable for producing heavy r-process elements (A>130). The authors build on the classical r-process framework of Kratz, Bitouzet, et al. (1993) and the hot versus cold r-process distinction of Wanajo (2007). The results enable future experimental efforts to measure nuclear masses far from stability and to test theoretical neutron capture cross sections, as the freeze-out effects found here are larger than previously estimated and neutron captures during decay to stability are required to explain the rare earth peak, as suggested by Surman, Engel, et al. (1997).
