# The possibility of twin star solutions in a model based on lattice QCD thermodynamics

> The properties of compact stars and in particular the existence of twin star solutions are investigated within an effective model that is constrained by lattice QCD thermodynamics. The model is modified at large baryon densities to incorporate a large variety of scenarios of first order phase transitions to a phase of deconfined quarks. This is achieved by matching two different variants of the bag model equation of state, in order to estimate the role of the Bag model parameters on the appearance of a second family of neutron stars. The produced sequences of neutron stars are compared with modern constrains on stellar masses, radii, and tidal deformability from astrophysical observations and gravitational wave analyses. It is found that most of the possible scenarios disfavor a strong phase transition to quark matter and do not support the conjecture of a second family of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper finds that most scenarios of a first-order phase transition to deconfined quark matter, implemented via a Maxwell construction matching a chiral mean-field model to bag model equations of state, disfavor the existence of twin star solutions that satisfy the two-solar-mass constraint from astrophysical observations.

## Method

The authors use the SU(3)-flavor parity-doublet Polyakov-loop quark-hadron mean-field model (CMF), which is constrained by lattice QCD thermodynamics at vanishing baryon density, and match it via a Maxwell construction to two variants of the bag model at high densities: the standard MIT Bag model (with two or three massless quark flavors) and the vector-enhanced MIT Bag model (vMIT) with repulsive vector interactions among quarks. They vary the bag constant B and, for the vMIT model, the quark-omega coupling constant g_q^omega, then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities, comparing these to constraints from pulsar mass measurements and the GW170817 gravitational wave event.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter saturation properties reproduced by the CMF model: ground state density n0 = 0.16 fm^-3, binding energy E0/B = -15.2 MeV, asymmetry energy S0 = 31.9 MeV, compressibility K0 = 267 MeV.
- For the 3-flavor MIT Bag-CMF model, twin star solutions appear for bag constants B^{1/4} less than or approximately 200 MeV, with twin star masses M less than or approximately 1.5 solar masses.
- For the 2-flavor MIT Bag-CMF model, no twin star solutions appear; stable combined solutions exist for B^{1/4} less than or approximately 160 MeV.
- For the vMIT Bag-CMF model with B^{1/4} = 180 MeV, twin stars appear with masses below 1.5 solar masses.
- The dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda for 1.4 solar mass stars from the 2-flavor Bag-CMF model lies within the 2-sigma confidence bound 375 less than Lambda_{1.4 M_sun} less than 800 from GW170817; the 3-flavor model predicts Lambda values below this constraint for stars with masses greater than or approximately 1.2 solar masses.
- The maximum mass of pure Bag model stars scales as M_max^Bag approximately 1.78 (155 MeV / B^{1/4})^2 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends the CMF model of Motornenko (2019), which previously showed only a crossover transition to quarks and no second family of stars, by explicitly constructing first-order phase transitions to bag model quark matter. It contrasts with earlier studies such as Glendenning (1998), Schertler (2000), and Alford (2015) that found twin star solutions, and with the work of Klahn (2015) and Gomes (2018) that used the vMIT model to satisfy the two-solar-mass constraint. The results suggest that many twin star predictions may suffer from confirmation bias, and that well-established models constrained by lattice QCD thermodynamics do not support their existence under current astrophysical constraints. The findings enable future work to focus on density-dependent repulsive couplings or alternative transition constructions that might produce twin stars with higher masses.
