# Non-congruent Phase Transitions in Cosmic Matter and in the Laboratory

> Non-congruence appears to be the most general form of phase transition in cosmic matter and in the laboratory. In terrestrial applications noncongruencemeans coexistence of phases with different chemical composition in systems consisting of two (or more) chemical elements. It is just the case for all phase transitions in high-temperature chemically reactive mixtures, which are typical for uranium-bearing compounds in many nuclear energy devices, both contemporary and perspective. As for cosmic matter, most of real and hypothetical phase transitions without nuclear reactions, i.e., those in the interiors of giant planets (solar and extrasolar), those in brown dwarfs and other sub-stellar objects, as well as in the outer crust of compact stars, are very plausible candidates for such type of phase transformations. Two exotic phase transitions, the gas-liquid phase transition in dense nuclear matter and the quark-hadron transition occuring in the interior of compact stars as well as in high-energy heavy-ion collisions are under discussion as the most extreme example of hypothetical non-congruence for phase transformations in High Energy Density Matter.

# Non-congruent Phase Transitions in Cosmic Matter and in the Laboratory

## Key claim

Any phase transition in an equilibrium system containing two or more chemical elements must be non-congruent in general, with congruent phase transitions arising only as exceptions; this applies to both terrestrial chemically reactive mixtures and cosmic matter, including the gas-liquid phase transition in dense nuclear matter and the quark-hadron transition in compact stars and heavy-ion collisions, provided the system is treated under the appropriate thermodynamic conditions (e.g., Gibbs conditions rather than Maxwell conditions).

## Method

The paper develops a thermodynamic classification of phase transitions based on whether coexisting phases have fixed or variable chemical composition (congruent vs. non-congruent). It derives equilibrium conditions for neutral species (Gibbs conditions, equalities of chemical potentials for each species) and for charged species (Gibbs-Guggenheim conditions, equalities of electro-chemical potentials with a finite Galvani potential drop across the phase interface). The analysis is applied to the uranium-oxygen system using a "chemical picture" model that treats both liquid and vapor as equilibrium multi-component mixtures of atoms, molecules, ions, and electrons, with self-consistent calculation of chemical reactions, ionization, and non-ideality corrections (Coulomb interaction, short-range repulsion and attraction) over a wide range of temperature and pressure.

## Key numbers

- Uranium-oxygen system calculations cover temperatures up to 20 kK and pressures up to 2 GPa, including the vicinity of the true critical point of the non-congruent phase transition.
- The pressure-temperature phase diagram for non-congruent evaporation in U-O plasma (O/U = 2.0) shows a two-dimensional "banana-like" region replacing the one-dimensional saturation curve of ordinary congruent phase transitions.
- The Galvani potential drop across the phase interface in Coulomb systems is a thermodynamic quantity that tends to zero at the critical point of gas-liquid phase transition.

## Context

This work extends the concept of non-congruent phase transitions from terrestrial applications (uranium-bearing compounds in nuclear energy devices, studied in prior work by Iosilevskiy and collaborators in the 1980s and 1990s) to cosmic matter, including giant planet interiors, brown dwarfs, and compact stars. It contrasts the standard Maxwell construction (forced-congruent, one-dimensional phase boundaries) with the more general Gibbs conditions (non-congruent, two-dimensional phase boundaries) and the Gibbs-Guggenheim conditions for charged species. The paper specifically addresses the "mixed phase" scenario introduced by Glendenning (1992) and developed by many authors, where a charged emulsion of micro-fragments may be thermodynamically stable. The analysis enables future work to determine whether exotic phase transitions in compact stars and heavy-ion collisions (at RHIC, LHC, FAIR, and NICA) are non-congruent, and whether the structured mixed phase ("pasta plasma") scenarios are equivalent to non-congruent phase transitions.
