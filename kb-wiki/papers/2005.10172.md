# Compressional-mode resonances in the molybdenum isotopes: Emergence of softness in open-shell nuclei near A=90

> "Why are the tin isotopes soft?" has remained, for the past decade, an open problem in nuclear structure physics: models which reproduce the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) in the "doubly-closed shell" nuclei, $^{90}$Zr and $^{208}$Pb, overestimate the ISGMR energies of the open-shell tin and cadmium nuclei, by as much as 1 MeV. In an effort to shed some light onto this problem, we present results of detailed studies of the ISGMR in the molybdenum nuclei, with the goal of elucidating where--and how--the softness manifests itself between $^{90}$Zr and the cadmium and tin isotopes. The experiment was conducted using the $^{94,96,98,100}$Mo($α,α^\prime$) reaction at $E_α= 386$ MeV. A comparison of the results with relativistic, self-consistent Random-Phase Approximation calculations indicates that the ISGMR response begins to show softness in the molybdenum isotopes beginning with $A=92$.

## Key claim
The isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) in molybdenum isotopes begins to show "softness" (lower centroid energies than predicted by models that reproduce the doubly-closed-shell nuclei ⁹⁰Zr and ²⁰⁸Pb) starting at A=92, with the effect fully manifest in ⁹⁴Mo and ⁹⁶Mo. The authors conclude that this softness is qualitatively the same phenomenon previously documented in tin and cadmium isotopes, but the underlying cause remains unresolved.

## Method
The experiment used the ⁹⁴,⁹⁶,⁹⁸,¹⁰⁰Mo(α,α′) reaction at E_α = 386 MeV at the Research Center for Nuclear Physics (RCNP), Osaka University. Inelastically scattered α-particles were momentum-analyzed in the Grand Raiden spectrometer in vertical-focusing mode, enabling instrumental background subtraction via detected vertical positions. Multipole decomposition analysis (MDA) was performed on angular distributions extracted over 0°–10° and excitation energies 10–32 MeV, using DWBA calculations with optical-model parameters constrained by elastic scattering off ⁹⁸Mo. The extracted ISGMR strength distributions were compared with relativistic, self-consistent Random-Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations using the FSUGarnet interaction.

## Key numbers
- ISGMR centroid energies (m₁/m₀) for molybdenum isotopes: ⁹⁴Mo = 16.4 ± 0.2 MeV, ⁹⁶Mo = 16.3 ± 0.2 MeV, ⁹⁸Mo = 16.2 ± 0.2 MeV, ¹⁰⁰Mo = 15.6 ± 0.2 MeV
- For comparison: ⁹⁰Zr centroid = 16.9 ± 0.1 MeV, ¹¹⁶Sn centroid = 15.8 ± 0.1 MeV, ²⁰⁸Pb centroid = 13.9 ± 0.1 MeV
- FSUGarnet interaction has K_∞ = 229.6 ± 2.5 MeV, while the accepted value from doubly-closed-shell nuclei is K_∞ = 240 ± 20 MeV
- The low-energy peak (deformation-induced) in ¹⁰⁰Mo exhausts 32 ± 4% of the EWSR at E₀ = 13.2 ± 0.4 MeV
- Total EWSR exhausted up to 35 MeV: ⁹⁴Mo = 88 ± 4%, ⁹⁶Mo = 93 ± 4%, ⁹⁸Mo = 102 ± 6%, ¹⁰⁰Mo = 93 ± 6%

## Context
This work extends the puzzle of ISGMR "softness" previously documented in tin isotopes by Li et al. (2007, 2010) and cadmium isotopes by Patel et al. (2013) to the molybdenum isotopic chain (Z=42), bridging the gap between the doubly-magic ⁹⁰Zr (Z=40) and the soft cadmium (Z=48) and tin (Z=50) nuclei. The mutually-enhanced magicity (MEM) explanation proposed by Khan (2009) was refuted experimentally by Patel et al. (2015), and pairing effects have been deemed insufficient to account for the softening. The results enable further theoretical work: a recent quasiparticle-RPA analysis by Colo and Nesterenko (2020) using four Skyrme interactions found that the best reproduction of the molybdenum ISGMR data comes from the SkP^δ interaction with K_∞ = 202 MeV, significantly below the accepted range.
