# Gamma-Ray Bursts and Afterglows from Rotating Strange Stars and Neutron Stars

> We here discuss a new model of $γ$-ray bursts (GRBs) based on differentially rotating strange stars. Strange stars in this model and differentially rotating neutron stars in the Kluźniak-Ruderman model can produce extremely relativistic, variable fireballs required by GRBs and then become millisecond pulsars. The effect of such pulsars on expansion of the postburst fireballs through magnetic dipole radiation is studied. We show that these two models can explain naturally not only various features of GRBs but also light curves of afterglows.

# Gamma-Ray Bursts and Afterglows from Rotating Strange Stars and Neutron Stars

## Key claim

Differentially rotating strange stars (and differentially rotating neutron stars in the Kluźniak-Ruderman model) can produce the extremely relativistic, variable fireballs required for gamma-ray bursts, and the resulting millisecond pulsar's magnetic dipole radiation can explain the observed decline-rise-decline behavior of afterglow light curves, provided that the stellar period is submillisecond (approximately 0.6 ms) and the magnetic field is around 3 × 10^13 G, as inferred from the afterglow of GRB 970508.

## Method

The authors model a differentially rotating strange star (or neutron star) in which internal poloidal magnetic field is wound into a toroidal configuration and amplified until buoyancy forces cause it to break through the stellar surface, producing a series of sub-bursts. They then treat the post-burst millisecond pulsar as a central energy source that injects rotational energy via magnetic dipole radiation into the expanding relativistic fireball, and solve the energy-conservation equation for the blastwave evolution in a uniform interstellar medium to derive the synchrotron afterglow flux as a function of observer time.

## Key numbers

- Total available differential rotation energy: E = 5 × 10^51 ergs × (Î/10^44 g cm^2) × (Ω/10^4 s^−1)^2
- Critical magnetic field for buoyant escape: B_f ≈ 2 × 10^17 G × (ρ/4 × 10^14 g cm^−3)^(1/2)
- Time interval between sub-bursts: τ_i ≈ 10 s × (B/10^13 G)^(−1) × (Ω/10^4 s^−1)^(−1)
- Total GRB duration: τ_d ≈ 60 s × (B/10^13 G)^(−1) × (Ω/10^4 s^−1)
- For GRB 970508 afterglow: τ ≈ 0.4 days, T ≈ 2 days, leading to stellar period P ≈ 0.6 ms and magnetic field B_13 ≈ 3.1
- R-band flux evolution: F_ν ∝ t_⊕^(−1/4) for early times (p ≈ 1.0), then rising as F_ν ∝ t_⊕^((2−p)/2) (p ≈ 1.0), then decaying as F_ν ∝ t_⊕^((2−3p)/4) with p ≈ 2.2 giving F_ν ∝ t_⊕^(−1.2)
- Neutron star–strange star capture rate in globular clusters: ℜ ∼ 10^(−17) yr^(−1) × (M/M_⊙)^2 × (v/500 km s^−1)^(−3) × (R_g/10 kpc)^(−3) × (ξ/10)

## Context

This work extends the Kluźniak-Ruderman model (1998) for differentially rotating neutron stars as GRB progenitors to the case of strange stars, and builds on earlier strange-star GRB models by Haensel, Paczyński and Amsterdamski (1991) and Cheng and Dai (1996). It also incorporates the fireball-shock framework of Mészáros and Rees (1997) and Sari, Piran and Narayan (1998). The model enables a unified explanation of both the prompt GRB emission and the complex afterglow light curves (specifically the decline-rise-decline behavior observed in GRB 970508 by Djorgovski et al., 1997), and provides testable predictions for the period and magnetic field of the central pulsar.
