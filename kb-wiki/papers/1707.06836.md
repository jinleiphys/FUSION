# Temperature dependence of nuclear fission time in heavy-ion fusion-fission reactions

> Accounting for viscous damping within Fokker-Planck equations led to various improvements in the understanding and analysis of nuclear fission of heavy nuclei. Analytical expressions for the fission time are typically provided by Kramers' theory, which improves on the Bohr-Wheeler estimate by including the time-scale related to many-particle dissipative processes along the deformation coordinate. However, Kramers' formula breaks down for sufficiently high excitation energies where Kramers' assumption of a large barrier no longer holds. In the regime $T>1$ MeV, Kramers' theory should be replaced by a new theory based on the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck first-passage time method that is proposed here. The theory is applied to fission time data from fusion-fission experiments on $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb $\rightarrow$ $^{224}$Th. The proposed model provides an internally consistent one-parameter fitting of fission data with a constant nuclear friction as the fitting parameter, whereas Kramers' fitting requires a value of friction which falls out of the allowed range. The theory provides also an analytical formula that in future work can be easily implemented in numerical codes such as CASCADE or JOANNE4.

## Key claim

Kramers' formula for the fission time breaks down dramatically when the reduced barrier \(U_f/T < 5\), overestimating the fission time by up to a factor of 27, and the proposed Ornstein-Uhlenbeck first-passage time model provides an internally consistent one-parameter fit of fission data with a constant nuclear friction that falls within the allowed range, whereas Kramers' fitting requires a friction value that violates the overdamped regime bound.

## Method

The authors derive an analytical expression for the fission time using the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck mean first-passage time method with a reflecting boundary at the potential minimum and an absorbing boundary at the saddle point, linearizing the potential between these points. They apply this formula to fission time data from the fusion-fission reaction \(^{16}\text{O}+^{208}\text{Pb} \rightarrow ^{224}\text{Th}\), estimating the fission energy landscape using Lestone's fast method with the Sierk barrier correction for the liquid-drop model. The only adjustable parameter is the nuclear friction coefficient \(\beta\), which must satisfy the overdamped regime condition \(\beta \gg 4.2 \times 10^{20}\,\text{s}^{-1}\) for this system.

## Key numbers

* For the \(^{224}\text{Th}\) system with fission barrier \(U_f = 4.58\,\text{MeV}\), the best fit using the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck formula (Eq. 3) gives \(\beta = 2.2 \times 10^{21}\,\text{s}^{-1}\), and the analytical linearized formula (Eq. 6) gives \(\beta = 2.5 \times 10^{21}\,\text{s}^{-1}\); both satisfy the overdamped bound \(\beta \gg 4.2 \times 10^{20}\,\text{s}^{-1}\).
* The best fit using Kramers' formula requires \(\beta = 6.5 \times 10^{20}\,\text{s}^{-1}\), which violates the overdamped condition \(\beta \gg 4.2 \times 10^{20}\,\text{s}^{-1}\) derived from Eq. (10) for \(^{224}\text{Th}\) with \(T \approx 2\,\text{MeV}\), \(\mu \approx 224 m_{av}\), and \(\Delta q \approx 1\,\text{fm}\).
* For the Lennard-Jones dimer simulation, Kramers' formula deviates from simulation data below \(U_f/T \approx 6\) and is off by a factor greater than 20 when \(U_f/T \sim 1\), with errors up to 27-fold in the fission time.
* In the limit \(U_f/T \rightarrow 0\), the proposed model recovers the free diffusion limit \(\tau_f = \frac{1}{2} L^2 / D = \frac{1}{2} (\mu\beta/T) L^2\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of dissipative fission dynamics beyond Kramers' theory (Kramers 1940), which has been extensively applied by Weidenmüller and collaborators to nuclear fission. The paper shows that Kramers' assumptions of a large barrier and thermalization in the well fail for \(U_f/T < 5\), a regime increasingly relevant as heavy-ion fusion-fission experiments reach higher excitation energies and lower fission barriers due to large angular momentum. The proposed Ornstein-Uhlenbeck mean first-passage time model, with its analytical formula (Eq. 6), can be implemented in numerical codes such as CASCADE (Puehlhofer) and JOANNE4 (Lestone 1999), and future work should combine this approach with the temperature-dependent effective potential corrections of Lestone and McCalla (2008, 2009) to account for the deformation dependence of the level density parameter.
