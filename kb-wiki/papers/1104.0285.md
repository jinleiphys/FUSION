# Role of momentum correlations in the properties of fragments produced in heavy-ion collisions

> The role of momentum correlations is studied in the properties of light and medium mass fragments by imposing momentum cut in clustering the phase space. Our detailed investigation shows that momentum cut has major role to play in the properties of fragments.

## Key claim

The paper claims that imposing a momentum cut (relative momentum ≤ 150 MeV/c) during clusterization in the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model produces significant quantitative differences in fragment properties such as rapidity distribution, transverse momentum spectra, and energy ratio, compared to the standard spatial-only minimum spanning tree (MST) method, but the qualitative behavior remains similar.

## Method

The authors simulated reactions of ¹²C+¹²C and ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca at 100 MeV/nucleon using the QMD model with a soft equation of state and standard energy-dependent Cugon cross section. They applied two clusterization algorithms to the phase space: the minimum spanning tree (MST) method, which binds nucleons within a spatial distance of 4 fm, and the minimum spanning tree with momentum cut (MSTP) method, which additionally requires that the relative momentum of nucleons in a fragment be ≤ 150 MeV/c. Fragment properties were compared for central (b̂=0.0) and peripheral (b̂=0.8) colliding geometries.

## Key numbers

- The momentum cut value used in the MSTP method is p_min = 150 MeV/c.
- The spatial cut used in both MST and MSTP methods is r_min = 4 fm.
- Reactions were simulated at an incident energy of 100 MeV/nucleon for both ¹²C+¹²C and ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca systems.
- Colliding geometries studied were central (b̂ = 0.0) and peripheral (b̂ = 0.8).

## Context

This work extends prior studies by Puri et al. (Kumar et al.) who introduced the MSTP method, and by the authors' own recent work on the role of momentum cut on fragment structure and colliding geometry. The paper addresses a gap in the literature by examining how the momentum cut affects specific fragment properties (rapidity distribution, p_t spectra, and E_rat) that had not been studied before. The results enable a more realistic treatment of fragment formation in QMD simulations by filtering out unstable fragments that would decay in real collisions.
