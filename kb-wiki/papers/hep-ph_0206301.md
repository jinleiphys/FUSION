# What Do We Know About the Strange Magnetic Radius?

> We analyze the q^2-dependence of the strange magnetic form factor, \GMS(q^2), using heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT) and dispersion relations. We find that in HBChPT a significant cancellation occurs between the O(p^2) and O(p^3) loop contributions. Consequently, the slope of \GMS at the origin displays an enhanced sensitivity to an unknown O(p^3) low-energy constant. Using dispersion theory, we estimate the magnitude of this constant, show that it may have a natural size, and conclude that the low-q^2 behavior of \GMS could be dominated by nonperturbative physics. We also discuss the implications for the interpretation of parity-violating electron scattering measurements used to measure \GMS(q^2).

# What Do We Know About the Strange Magnetic Radius?

## Key claim
The slope of the strange magnetic form factor at the origin (the strange magnetic radius) is dominated by an unknown low-energy constant at order p^3 in heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory, because the leading O(p^2) and O(p^3) loop contributions largely cancel each other. Consequently, the low-momentum behavior of the strange magnetic form factor could be dominated by nonperturbative physics, and the strange magnetic moment cannot presently be extracted from experiment in a model-independent manner.

## Method
The authors compute the O(p^3) loop contributions to the strange magnetic form factor G_M^{(s)}(q^2) using heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory (HBChPT), including one-loop diagrams with kaon, pion, and eta intermediate states, as well as recoil corrections and symmetry-breaking operators. They also perform a dispersion relation analysis of G_M^{(s)} using the low-mass contributions from 3pi and K anti-K intermediate states, dominated by the omega(782) and phi(1020) resonances, to estimate the unknown low-energy constant b_s^r.

## Key numbers
- The O(p^2) HBChPT result for the strange magnetic radius is <r^2_s>_M = -0.16 fm^2 (arising entirely from the kaon loop diagram).
- After including O(p^3) contributions, the LEC-independent loop contribution to <r^2_s>_M is -0.04 fm^2, indicating a substantial cancellation with the O(p^2) term.
- The low-mass dispersion relation analysis (3pi and K anti-K contributions) gives <r^2_s>_M = -0.34 fm^2 and mu_s = -0.28.
- The quenched lattice QCD calculation of Liu et al. gives mu_s = -0.36 +/- 0.20, corresponding to b_s + 0.6 b_8 approximately -0.6 +/- 0.1.
- The dipole fit to the lattice data yields b_s^r(mu = 1 GeV) = 0.23 +/- 0.13.
- The dispersion relation analysis gives b_s^r approximately -1.1.
- An uncertainty in b_s^r of Delta b_s^r = +/- 1 corresponds to a +/- 0.3 fm^2 uncertainty in the strange magnetic radius.

## Context
This work extends the O(p^2) HBChPT analysis of Meissner et al. (1997) for the strange magnetic form factor by computing the previously unknown O(p^3) contributions, revealing that the leading-order parameter-free prediction for the magnetic radius is unreliable due to large cancellations. The authors use dispersion relations to estimate the unknown low-energy constant, building on their earlier work (Hammer et al., 2001) on kaon cloud contributions. The results have direct implications for interpreting parity-violating electron scattering measurements from the SAMPLE Collaboration at MIT-Bates, the HAPPEX and G0 experiments at Jefferson Lab, and the Mainz experiment, which aim to determine the strange magnetic form factor and extract the strange magnetic moment. Future low-momentum-transfer measurements below |q^2| < 0.05 (GeV/c)^2 could provide an unambiguous determination of the strange magnetic radius.
