# Perspectives on Nuclear Structure and Scattering with the Ab Initio No-Core Shell Model

> Nuclear structure and reaction theory are undergoing a major renaissance with advances in many-body methods, strong interactions with greatly improved links to Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), the advent of high performance computing, and improved computational algorithms. Predictive power, with well-quantified uncertainty, is emerging from non-perturbative approaches along with the potential for new discoveries such as predicting nuclear phenomena before they are measured. We present an overview of some recent developments and discuss challenges that lie ahead. Our focus is on explorations of alternative truncation schemes in the harmonic oscillator basis, of which our Japanese--United States collaborative work on the No-Core Monte-Carlo Shell Model is an example. Collaborations with Professor Takaharu Otsuka and his group have been instrumental in these developments.

# Perspectives on Nuclear Structure and Scattering with the Ab Initio No-Core Shell Model

## Key claim

The No-Core Monte Carlo Shell Model (NC-MCSM) and the No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) produce extrapolated ground state energies that agree with each other and with Full Configuration Interaction (FCI) results where available, but the NC-MCSM results lie above the NCSM results at comparable many-body basis sizes; the NC-MCSM shows superior computational scaling for heavier nuclei, while alternative truncation schemes using single-particle weights of the form W = (alpha n + l) can improve convergence for specific observables.

## Method

The authors benchmarked the NC-MCSM against the NCSM and FCI for light nuclei up to A=12 using the JISP16 nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction, comparing ground state energies and convergence rates as functions of many-body basis size. They then explored alternative truncation schemes within the NCSM framework by assigning weights W = (alpha n + l) to each harmonic oscillator single-particle orbital (where n is the radial quantum number and l is the orbital angular momentum) and truncating the many-body basis by a cutoff W_max on the sum of these weights, applying this to 8He with the Daejeon16 NN interaction.

## Key numbers

- For 12C with only four HO shells (Nshell=4), the FCI basis size is of the order of 10^16, even after symmetry constraints.
- The NC-MCSM typically keeps only a few hundred Monte Carlo basis states, though the underlying FCI basis can be of the order of 10^20.
- For 8He with Daejeon16, a cutoff W_max = 18 corresponds to Nmax=14 for the conventional truncation (alpha=2), producing matrix dimensions of about two billion.
- At a fixed basis parameter hbar-omega=15 MeV, the weight W = (1.0 n + l) gave the lowest variational upper bound for the ground state energy of 8He among the cases examined, even though it had the smallest dimension in the comparison set.
- For the point-proton RMS radius of 8He at hbar-omega=10 MeV, the case W = (n + l) provided results with the least sensitivity to many-body basis size at higher dimensions.

## Context

This work extends the benchmark studies of the Japan-United States collaboration on the No-Core Monte Carlo Shell Model (Abe et al., 2012-2014) and the No-Core Shell Model (Barrett, 2013; Maris, 2008). It builds on the JISP16 NN interaction (Shirokov, 2005) and the Daejeon16 NN interaction (Shirokov, 2016) to compare truncation schemes. The alternative truncation approach using weighted single-particle orbitals (Anderson, 2012) is explored as a way to improve convergence for observables like the RMS radius that converge slowly with conventional Nmax truncation. This work enables future investigations of heavier nuclei where the NCSM has limited utility, and opens the door to systematic studies of alternative truncation schemes with different single-particle bases, Hamiltonians including three-nucleon forces, and additional observables.
