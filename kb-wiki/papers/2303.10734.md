# QCD Phase Diagrams via QHD and MIT-Based Models

> In this paper the QCD phase diagram is obtained by the crossing of two effective models: the MIT bag based models are used to describe quark matter and QHD type models to describe hadronic matter, being the use of the former something new to this kind of approach and the latter used with improved parameterizations as compared with previous calculations. We use the Gibbs' conditions to establish the crossing points of the pressure in function of the chemical potential obtained in both phases. We first analyze two-flavour symmetric matter constrained to both the freeze-out and the liquid-gas phase transition at the hadronic phase. Later we analyse the results for $β$-stable and charge neutral stellar matter and compare two different prescriptions: one that assumes flavour conservation, so that the quark phase is completely determined from the hadronic phase, a prescription never applied to finite temperatures before, and the other based on the Maxwell construction, where the quark phase is also $β$-stable. At the end, we compute the latent heat to find a signature of the critical end point.

## Key claim

The QCD phase diagram is constructed by crossing two effective models (MIT bag for quark matter and QHD for hadronic matter) using Gibbs' conditions, but the results are model-dependent and the low-chemical-potential, high-temperature region should be regarded only as a guide due to limitations of extrapolating stellar matter conditions.

## Method

The authors used relativistic mean-field QHD models (parametrizations eL3ωρ and NL3*ωρ) for hadronic matter and MIT bag-based models (with a temperature-dependent bag constant and optional vector field) for quark matter, applying Gibbs' conditions (equal temperature, pressure, and chemical potential) to find phase transition points. For two-flavor symmetric matter, they imposed equal chemical potentials for nucleons and for up/down quarks; for stellar matter, they included β-equilibrium and charge neutrality, comparing two prescriptions: flavour conservation (quark phase determined from hadronic phase) and Maxwell construction (quark phase also β-stable). They computed latent heat to estimate the critical end point.

## Key numbers

- Maximum temperatures at low chemical potentials converge around T = 168 MeV, determined by the choice of T₀ = 131 MeV for B₀¹/⁴ = 148 MeV and T₀ = 155 MeV for B₀¹/⁴ = 165 MeV
- At T = 0, the phase transition chemical potential must be at least μ = 1050 MeV (from Polyakov loop formalism) and occurs in the range μ = 1080–1100 MeV (from NJL-based models), with a conservative upper limit of μ = 1400 MeV assumed
- The eL3ωρ parametrization yields saturation density n₀ = 0.156 fm⁻³, energy per particle E/A = 16.2 MeV, compressibility K = 256 MeV, effective nucleon mass M*/M = 0.69, symmetry energy E_sym = 32.1 MeV, and slope L = 66 MeV
- The NL3*ωρ parametrization yields n₀ = 0.150 fm⁻³, E/A = 16.3 MeV, K = 258 MeV, M*/M = 0.59, E_sym = 30.7 MeV, and L = 42 MeV
- Vector coupling values used for the MIT bag model: G_V = 0.0, 0.05 fm², and 0.1 fm²
- Quark masses fixed at m_u = m_d = 4 MeV and m_s = 95 MeV
- Hyperon potentials in strange nuclear matter: U_Λ(n₀) = -28 MeV, U_Σ(n₀) = +30 MeV, U_Ξ(n₀) = -4 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous calculations by Lopes et al. (2021) that used only MIT bag model extensions, and by Biesdorf et al. (2022), by combining two effective relativistic models (QHD and MIT) with improved parametrizations that satisfy nuclear matter saturation properties and astrophysical constraints (neutron star masses above 2 M☉). The flavour conservation prescription for finite temperatures is new, having previously been applied only at T = 0 by Bombaci (2017). The results are compared with the chemical freeze-out line from Cleymans (2006) and the liquid-gas phase transition from Finn (1982), and the approach enables estimation of the critical end point location through latent heat calculations.
