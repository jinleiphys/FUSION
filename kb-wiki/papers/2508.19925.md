# Measurement of the differential and total cross-sections of $γ$-ray emission induced by $14.1$ MeV neutrons for C, Al, Si, Ca, Ti, Cr, and Fe using the tagged neutron method

> In this work, differential cross sections of $γ$-ray emission produced in nuclear reactions induced by $14.1$~MeV neutrons are measured for the $4.439$~MeV line from carbon, as well as for $10$ individual $γ$-ray lines from aluminum, $6$ from silicon, $8$ from calcium, $16$ from titanium, $6$ from chromium, and $14$ from iron. The measurements were conducted using the tagged neutron method with four LaBr$_3$(Ce) scintillation detectors positioned at angles of $25^{\circ}$, $45^{\circ}$, $60^{\circ}$, and $70^{\circ}$ relative to the generator target -- sample center axis. A neutron generator capable of producing $16$ separate beams of tagged neutrons was employed, which, combined with the detector system, enabled the determination of differential cross-sections for $64$ distinct angle values in the range of $17^{\circ}$ to $89^{\circ}$. To simplify data visualization, the angular distributions were divided into $5^{\circ}$ intervals, with weighted mean values of the angle and differential cross-section calculated for each interval. Corrections for multiple neutron scattering and attenuation, $γ$-ray attenuation, and total detection efficiency, computed using GEANT4, were accounted for in the cross-section calculations. Additional measurements were performed to validate the correction calculations. The total $γ$-ray emission cross-sections were obtained by approximating the angular distributions with even-order Legendre polynomial expansions up to the $6$th degree, followed by integration over the full solid angle. The total systematic error for the obtained data was estimated as $9$\,\%.

## Key claim
The paper reports differential and total γ-ray emission cross-sections for 14.1 MeV neutrons on seven elements, claiming improved precision over prior work through optimized geometry and correction procedures, but the total systematic uncertainty is 9% and the multiple-scattering correction carries an additional 3-7% uncertainty depending on the specific γ-ray line.

## Method
Differential cross-sections were measured using the tagged neutron method with an ING-27 neutron generator producing 16 tagged neutron beams and four LaBr₃(Ce) scintillation detectors at 25°, 45°, 60°, and 70° relative to the sample center axis, yielding 64 distinct emission angles from 17° to 89°. Angular distributions were binned into 5° intervals with weighted mean values, and total cross-sections were obtained by fitting even-order Legendre polynomial expansions up to 6th degree and integrating over solid angle. Corrections for neutron attenuation in the generator, multiple neutron scattering, γ-ray attenuation, and detection efficiency were computed using GEANT4 simulations and validated with dedicated experiments using isotopic sources and a PuBe neutron source with NaCl solution.

## Key numbers
- Total cross-section for the 4.439 MeV γ-ray line from carbon (¹²C(n,n')¹²C): 186 ± 17 mb, with Legendre coefficients a₂ = 0.28 ± 0.01 and a₄ = -0.33 ± 0.02
- Total systematic uncertainty for all data: 9%
- Efficiency uncertainty: 5% (average deviation between GEANT4 simulation and experimental data)
- Multiple neutron scattering correction uncertainty: 7% (upper estimate for all lines)
- γ-ray attenuation correction uncertainty: 2% (from dedicated ¹³⁷Cs and ⁶⁰Co source measurements)
- Number of nuclei in sample uncertainty: 2%
- Statistical uncertainty for differential cross-sections: 0.5-1% for most intense lines, 20-30% for least intense lines
- Neutron beam attenuation in generator: 9% reduction in neutrons reaching sample
- Sample dimensions: 44 × 44 cm, thicknesses 0.7-2 cm, target-to-sample distance 24.8 cm

## Context
This work extends the TANGRA project's systematic measurements of γ-ray emission cross-sections at 14.1 MeV (Grozdanov 2018, Fedorov 2019, Fedorov 2021, Dashkov 2022, Kopatch 2023, Kopatch 2025) to carbon, aluminum, silicon, calcium, titanium, chromium, and iron, which are major constituents of geological materials. It addresses the fragmented angular distribution data noted in Simakov's compilation (1998), where most prior measurements had only 1-2 angular points, and improves upon recent work at GAINS in GELINA (Negret 2013, Olacel 2017, Negret 2014) which lacked angular distribution measurements. The results enable more accurate elemental composition analysis for applications in geology (Bolshakov 2020), metallurgy (Alexakhin 2022), and planetary nuclear spectroscopy.
