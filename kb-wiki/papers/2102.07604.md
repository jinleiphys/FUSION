# Some Aspects of the Theory of Heavy Ion Collisions

> We review the theoretical aspects relevant in the description of high energy heavy ion collisions, with an emphasis on the learnings about the underlying QCD phenomena that have emerged from these collisions.

## Key claim

This review argues that high-energy heavy ion collisions provide a unique laboratory for studying QCD phenomena, particularly the quark-gluon plasma, but emphasizes that the theoretical description requires going beyond collinear factorization due to gluon saturation effects at small momentum fraction \(x\), where the gluon density becomes large enough that multi-parton interactions dominate.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for heavy ion collisions by dividing the collision evolution into stages ordered by proper time \(\tau\), starting from the initial non-equilibrated gluon fields treated via the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective theory, through hydrodynamic expansion, to kinetic freezeout. It describes how the CGC treats incoming nuclei as classical color currents \(J^\mu\) coupled to Yang-Mills fields, with the statistical distribution of color charges encoded in a functional \(W[\rho]\), and explains how next-to-leading order corrections and the JIMWLK renormalization group equation resum large logarithms of the longitudinal momentum cutoff.

## Key numbers

* The deconfinement transition temperature for pure-glue QCD is approximately \(270\ \mathrm{MeV}\), while the physical crossover transition for light quarks occurs around \(155\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* The saturation momentum scales as \(Q_s^2 \sim A^{1/3} x^{-\lambda}\) with \(\lambda \approx 0.25\) determined phenomenologically from deep inelastic scattering data.
* The top quark mass is approximately \(175\ \mathrm{GeV}\), and the CERN SPS collided heavy ions at a center-of-mass energy of \(17\ \mathrm{GeV}\) per nucleon pair.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical understanding developed from experimental programs at RHIC (Brookhaven National Laboratory) and the LHC (CERN), which shifted focus from demonstrating quark-gluon plasma production to quantitatively measuring its properties, as summarized in works by Adams (2005), Adcox (2004), and Aamodt (2010). It builds on the McLerran-Venugopalan model (1993) for the CGC initial state and the JIMWLK evolution framework developed by Jalilian-Marian (1996) and Iancu (2000), enabling future calculations of multi-particle correlations and small-\(x\) physics at collider energies.
