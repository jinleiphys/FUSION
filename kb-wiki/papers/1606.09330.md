# New neutron-rich isotope production in $^{154}$Sm+$^{160}$Gd

> Deep inelastic scattering in $^{154}$Sm+$^{160}$Gd at energies above the Bass barrier is for the first time investigated with two different microscopic dynamics approaches: improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model and time dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory. No fusion is observed from both models. The capture pocket disappears for this reaction due to strong Coulomb repulsion and the contact time of the di-nuclear system formed in head-on collisions is about 700 fm/c at an incident energy of 440 MeV. The isotope distribution of fragments in the deep inelastic scattering process is predicted with the simulations of the latest ImQMD-v2.2 model together with a statistical code (GEMINI) for describing the secondary decay of fragments. More than 40 extremely neutron-rich unmeasured nuclei with $58 \le Z\le 76$ are observed and the production cross sections are at the order of ${\rm μb}$ to mb. The multi-nucleon transfer reaction of Sm+Gd could be an alternative way to synthesize new neutron-rich lanthanides which are difficult to be produced with traditional fusion reactions or fission of actinides.

# New neutron-rich isotope production in $^{154}$Sm+$^{160}$Gd

## Key claim
Deep inelastic scattering in $^{154}$Sm+$^{160}$Gd at energies above the Bass barrier can produce more than 40 extremely neutron-rich unmeasured nuclei with $58 \le Z\le 76$, but only if the capture pocket disappears due to strong Coulomb repulsion and the contact time remains short (about 700 fm/c), meaning fusion to superheavy nuclei is impossible for this system.

## Method
The authors used two microscopic dynamics models: the improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model with parameter set IQ3a and the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory with Skyrme energy density functional SLy5, both applied to head-on and peripheral collisions of $^{154}$Sm+$^{160}$Gd at $E_{\rm c.m.}=440$ MeV. For isotope production predictions, the ImQMD-v2.2 model was combined with the GEMINI statistical code to account for secondary decay of fragments, with 30000 events simulated per impact parameter.

## Key numbers
- Contact time of the di-nuclear system in head-on collisions: about 700 fm/c at $E_{\rm c.m.}=440$ MeV
- Bass barrier height: 393 MeV; incident energy $E_{\rm c.m.}=440$ MeV is 47 MeV above the Bass barrier
- Predicted Q-value for complete fusion: $Q=-410$ MeV from WS4 calculations
- More than 40 extremely neutron-rich unmeasured nuclei with $58 \le Z\le 76$ produced, with cross sections at the order of ${\rm \mu b}$ to mb
- Production cross section of $^{182}$Yb: about $46\ {\rm \mu b}$
- At $b=3$ fm, 0.6% of simulation events show the neck of the di-nuclear system remaining at $t=2000$ fm/c; at $b=5$ fm, 0.02%
- About 1% of simulation events at central collisions show ternary breakup
- Sum of the two emission angles of projectile-like and target-like nuclei: about $96^\circ$
- Suggested angular range for detection: $20^\circ <\Theta_{\rm lab}<60^\circ$

## Context
This work extends the investigation of multi-nucleon transfer reactions for producing neutron-rich heavy nuclei, following earlier studies by Zychor et al. (1984) on Hafnium and Lutetium production with various projectiles, and by Choudhury and Gupta (2014) and Cap et al. (2014) who gave contradictory predictions for fusion in symmetric $^{154}$Sm+$^{154}$Sm. The paper contradicts the possibility of synthesizing superheavy nuclei via this reaction, but enables a new pathway to produce neutron-rich lanthanides that are difficult to reach via traditional fusion reactions or fission of actinides, as noted in the AME2012 chart of nuclides. The results suggest that deep inelastic scattering between two lanthanides is an efficient alternative to projectile fragmentation methods such as those used at GSI by Shubina et al. (2003) with $^{197}$Au beams.
