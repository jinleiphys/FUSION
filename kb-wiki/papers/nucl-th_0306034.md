# Thermal analysis of production of resonances in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Production of resonances is considered in the framework of the single-freeze-out model of ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. The formalism involves the virial expansion, where the probability to form a resonance in a two-body channel is proportional to the derivative of the phase-shift with respect to the invariant mass. The thermal model incorporates longitudinal and transverse flow, as well as kinematic cuts of the STAR experiment at RHIC. We find that the shape of the pi+ pi- spectral line qualitatively reproduces the preliminary experimental data when the position of the rho peak is lowered. This confirms the need to include the medium effects in the description of the RHIC data. We also analyze the transverse-momentum spectra of rho, K*(892), and f_0(980), and find that the slopes agree with the observed values. Predictions are made for eta, eta', omega, phi, Lambda(1520), and Sigma(1385).

## Key claim
The single-freeze-out thermal model using vacuum phase shifts for the $\rho$ meson cannot reproduce the preliminary STAR data for the $\pi^+\pi^-$ invariant-mass spectrum; the model yields a $\rho$ peak position around 740-747 MeV, whereas the data suggest a peak near 700 MeV, confirming the need for in-medium modifications of the $\rho$ meson mass.

## Method
The authors apply the Dashen-Ma-Bernstein virial expansion formalism, where resonance production in two-body channels is proportional to the derivative of the scattering phase shift with respect to invariant mass. They incorporate experimental $\pi\pi$ phase shifts parameterized from data, longitudinal and transverse flow via a boost-invariant single-freeze-out model with freeze-out temperature $T \simeq 165$ MeV, and the full kinematic cuts of the STAR experiment (pion rapidity $|y_\pi| \leq 1$, pseudorapidity $|\eta_\pi| \leq 0.8$, transverse momentum $p_\perp^\pi \geq 0.2$ GeV). All resonances from the Particle Data Tables are included, with enhancement factors from decays of higher resonances computed from the thermal model.

## Key numbers
- The $\rho$ peak position in the isovector channel weight function $f d\delta/(\pi dM)$ is at 762.7 MeV, while the point where $\delta(M)=\pi/2$ is at 773.6 MeV (a 10 MeV "dropping" of the $\rho$ meson "mass").
- For a static thermal source at $T=165$ MeV, the $\rho$ peak in the $\pi^+\pi^-$ invariant-mass spectrum shifts to 752.2 MeV; at $T=110$ MeV it shifts to 744.0 MeV.
- In the full single-freeze-out model with flow and kinematic cuts, the $\rho$ peak position ranges between 740 and 747 MeV depending on the $p_T$ bin.
- Scaling the $\rho$ channel phase shift by a factor $s=0.91$ places the peak at 700 MeV, matching the preliminary STAR data.
- The thermal parameters fitted to stable hadron ratios are $T=165.6 \pm 4.5$ MeV and $\mu_B=28.5 \pm 3.7$ MeV for the vacuum $\rho$ mass case; for the modified $\rho$ mass (700 MeV) they are $T=167.6 \pm 4.6$ MeV and $\mu_B=28.9 \pm 3.8$ MeV.
- The model predicts $\rho^0/\pi^- = 0.114 \pm 0.002$ (vacuum $\rho$) and $0.135 \pm 0.001$ (modified $\rho$), compared to the experimental value $0.183 \pm 0.028$ (40-80% centrality).
- Enhancement factors from higher resonance decays include $d_\rho = 1.42$, $d_{K_S} = 1.98$, $d_\eta = 1.74$, $d_\omega = 1.43$.

## Context
This work extends the single-freeze-out model developed by Broniowski, Florkowski, and collaborators (2001-2002) for stable hadron yields and spectra to the production of resonances in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at RHIC. The analysis confirms earlier conclusions by Shuryak and Brown (2002) and Rapp (2002) that the observed lowering of the $\rho$ peak in STAR data requires genuine in-medium modifications of the $\rho$ meson mass, beyond thermal effects. The results enable future studies of medium modifications by providing a baseline thermal calculation against which in-medium effects can be quantified, and by showing that the shape of the $\pi^+\pi^-$ spectral line depends strongly on the thermal freeze-out temperature, offering an independent method to determine $T_{\rm therm}$.
