# Superscaling Predictions for Neutral Current Quasielastic Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering

> The application of superscaling ideas to predict neutral-current (NC) quasielastic (QE) neutrino cross sections is investigated. Results obtained within the relativistic impulse approximation (RIA) using the same relativistic mean field potential (RMF) for both initial and final nucleons -- a model that reproduces the experimental (e,e') scaling function -- are used to illustrate the ideas involved. While NC reactions are not so well suited for scaling analyses, to a large extent the RIA-RMF predictions do exhibit superscaling. Independence of the scaled response on the nuclear species is very well fulfilled. The RIA-RMF NC superscaling function is in good agreement with the experimental (e,e') one. The idea that electroweak processes can be described with a universal scaling function, provided that mild restrictions on the kinematics are assumed, is shown to be valid.

# Superscaling Predictions for Neutral Current Quasielastic Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering

## Key claim

Neutral-current quasielastic neutrino-nucleus cross sections exhibit superscaling properties even in the presence of strong final-state interactions, provided the kinematics are chosen such that the range of energies of the ejected nucleon is nearly independent of the incoming neutrino energy (e.g., nucleon detection angles larger than roughly 50 degrees). Under these conditions, a universal scaling function can describe inclusive electron, charged-current, and neutral-current neutrino scattering, with first-kind scaling respected at the 10% level.

## Method

The authors used the Relativistic Impulse Approximation with Relativistic Mean Field potentials (RIA-RMF) for both initial bound and final ejected nucleons, employing self-consistent Dirac-Hartree solutions from a Lagrangian containing sigma, omega, and rho mesons. They computed neutral-current quasielastic differential cross sections for the reaction A(nu,nu'N)X as a function of outgoing nucleon kinetic energy and detection angle, then divided by the single-nucleon cross section and plotted against the dimensionless scaling variable psi^u derived from the Relativistic Fermi Gas analysis in neutral-current kinematics. The resulting scaling functions were compared across different beam energies (first-kind scaling), different target nuclei (second-kind scaling), and against the experimental (e,e') scaling function.

## Key numbers

- First-kind scaling is respected at the 10% level for nucleon detection angles larger than roughly 50 degrees, even with strong final-state interactions present.
- The analysis applies for neutrino beam energies from approximately 500 MeV up to a few GeV, under quasielastic kinematics.
- Results are shown for beam energies of 1, 1.5, and 2 GeV, and for target nuclei including C-12 and others, with the outgoing nucleon detection angle fixed at 40 degrees and 60 degrees.
- The RIA-RMF neutral-current scaling function at 60 degrees and two beam energies follows closely the experimental (e,e') scaling function, while at 40 degrees clear departure from first-kind scaling is visible.

## Context

This work extends the SuperScaling Approach (SuSA), previously applied to charged-current neutrino-nucleus scattering by Amaro et al. (2006) and Caballero et al. (2005), to neutral-current processes. The authors build on the scaling analysis framework outlined for neutral-current reactions by Amaro et al. (2006) using the Relativistic Fermi Gas, which exhibits perfect superscaling by definition but does not reproduce the experimental scaling function shape or magnitude. The RIA-RMF model used here reproduces the experimental (e,e') scaling function, unlike the Relativistic Fermi Gas, and was previously shown by Caballero et al. (2007) to predict a universal scaling function for both electron and charged-current neutrino scattering. The results enable reliable, largely model-independent predictions for neutral-current quasielastic neutrino cross sections using the phenomenological SuSA approach, which is relevant for oscillation experiments such as MiniBooNE at Fermilab where neutral-current events are expected as the third most important event type.
