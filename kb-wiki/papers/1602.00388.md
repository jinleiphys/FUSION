# Infinitesimal cranking for triaxial angular-momentum-projected configuration-mixing calculation and its application to the gamma vibrational band

> Inclusion of time-odd components into the wave function is important for reliable description of rotational motion by the angular-momentum-projection method; the cranking procedure with infinitesimal rotational frequency is an efficient way to realize it. In the present work we investigate the effect of this infinitesimal cranking for triaxially deformed nucleus, where there are three independent cranking axes. It is found that the effects of cranking about three axes on the triaxial energy spectrum are quite different and inclusion of all of them considerably modify the resultant spectrum from the one obtained without cranking. Employing the Gogny D1S force as an effective interaction, we apply the method to the calculation of the multiple gamma vibrational bands in $^{164}$Er as a typical example, where the angular-momentum-projected configuration-mixing with respect to the triaxial shape degree of freedom is performed. With this method, both the $K=0$ and $K=4$ two-phonon gamma vibrational bands are obtained with considerable anharmonicity. Reasonably good agreement, though not perfect, is obtained for both the spectrum and transition probabilities with rather small average triaxial deformation $γ\approx 9^\circ$ for the ground state rotational band. The relation to the wobbling motion at high-spin states is also briefly discussed.

# Infinitesimal cranking for triaxial angular-momentum-projected configuration-mixing calculation and its application to the gamma vibrational band

## Key claim

The inclusion of all three independent cranking axes via infinitesimal cranking considerably modifies the triaxial energy spectrum obtained by angular-momentum projection compared to the non-cranked case, but the result is independent of the actual values of the infinitesimal frequencies within first-order perturbation theory. The method yields both K=0 and K=4 two-phonon gamma vibrational bands in 164Er with considerable anharmonicity, achieving reasonably good but not perfect agreement with experimental spectrum and transition probabilities at an average triaxial deformation of γ ≈ 9° for the ground state rotational band.

## Method

The authors perform constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Gogny D1S force, applying the quadrupole constraints (Q, γ) while keeping Q fixed at the ground-state value and varying γ. They then apply angular-momentum projection from the cranked HFB states using infinitesimal rotational frequencies (10 keV/ħ) about all three principal axes, followed by configuration mixing (generator coordinate method) with respect to the triaxiality parameter γ. The method is applied to 164Er with a model space of N_osc^max = 10, maximum angular momentum I_max = 40, and K_max = 30.

## Key numbers

- The ground state of 164Er has quadrupole deformation Q = 5.644 b and β₂ = 0.316, with average neutron pairing gap Δ̄_ν = 0.846 MeV and proton pairing gap Δ̄_π = 0.859 MeV
- The ground state energy gains about 1.2 MeV by angular-momentum projection at finite triaxial deformation γ ≈ 9.7°
- Members of the g-band take energy minima at γ ≈ 9.7°, while members of the γ-band take minima at γ ≈ 13.6°
- The experimental gamma vibrational 2⁺_γ state in 164Er is observed at excitation energy 0.860 MeV
- The γ value at which calculated 2⁺_γ energy agrees with experiment is γ ≈ 15.4°
- The infinitesimal cranking frequencies used are 10 keV/ħ, which guarantees independence of the excitation spectrum within about 1 keV for low-lying states
- The experimental E2 transition probability from ground state to the second 2⁺ state is 0.148 and 0.170 e²b² (from Ref. [RGH82])

## Context

This work extends the infinitesimal cranking method previously applied to axially symmetric systems (Tagami and Shimizu, 2012) and tetrahedrally-symmetric systems (Tagami, Shimizu, and collaborators, 2013, 2015) to the triaxial case, where three independent cranking axes exist. The study addresses the long-standing problem of whether rare-earth nuclei like 164Er are axially symmetric or triaxially deformed, which was revived by the triaxial projected shell model approach (Sun, Hara, and collaborators, 2000; Sheikh, Bhat, and collaborators, 2008). The results are compared with the asymmetric rotor model (Davydov and Filippov, 1958) and with random phase approximation calculations using schematic interactions (Dumitrescu and Hamamoto, 1982), showing that the overestimation of E2 transition probabilities in RPA approaches is greatly improved in this angular-momentum-projected configuration-mixing approach. The work also briefly discusses the relation between multiple gamma vibrational bands and wobbling motion at high-spin states.
