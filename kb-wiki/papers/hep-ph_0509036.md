# Mach cones in an evolving medium

> The energy and momentum lost by a hard parton propagating through hot and dense matter has to be redistributed in the nuclear medium. Apart from heating the medium, there is the possibility that collective modes are excited. We outline a formalism that can be used to track the propagation of such a mode through the evolving medium if its dispersion relation is known. Under the assumption that a sound wave is created, we track the jet energy loss as a function of spacetime and follow the resulting mach cone throughout the fireball evolution.We compare with the angular correlation pattern of hard hadrons as obtained by the PHENIX collaboration and find good agreement with the data provided that a substantial fraction of jet energy ($\sim$ 90%) is deposited into a propagating mode and that the hot matter can be characterized by an EOS with a soft point (not necessarily a mixed phase).

# Mach cones in an evolving medium

## Key claim

The observed splitting of the away-side peak in two-particle correlations at RHIC can be explained by the excitation and propagation of Mach cones through the evolving fireball, provided that a substantial fraction (approximately 90%) of the jet energy lost to the medium is deposited into a propagating sound mode and that the hot matter is characterized by an equation of state with a soft point (not necessarily a mixed phase).

## Method

The authors use a fireball evolution model tuned to describe transverse mass spectra, HBT correlation radii, and hadronic dN/dy distributions at RHIC. They calculate the average energy loss of a hard parton along its path through the medium using quenching weights derived from the plasma frequency and averaged momentum transfer, with the transport coefficient taken as proportional to the local energy density to the 3/4 power. Assuming a fraction f of the lost energy excites a colorless sound mode with a dispersion relation E = c_s^2 p, they propagate the shock front elements through the evolving medium, continuously adjusting the Mach angle using the local speed of sound determined from a quasiparticle equation of state fitted to lattice results, and including the effect of local flow velocity. At freeze-out, the shockwave energy is converted into kinetic energy and the resulting hadron spectra are computed using the Cooper-Frye formula with an additional boost from the shockwave.

## Key numbers

- The best fit to PHENIX data requires a fraction f approximately 0.9 of the lost jet energy to excite a shockwave.
- For an energy in the collective mode of about 5 GeV, the number of pions in the shockwave is approximately 20 for c_s at freeze-out of approximately sqrt(0.2), and approximately 50 if c_s = 0.3.
- A 10 GeV jet can potentially involve O(100) particles in the Mach cone.
- The speed of sound in the medium ranges between approximately 0.3 and 0.5, while flow velocities can be less than 0.7.
- The freeze-out temperature used is approximately 110 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the interpretation of the splitting of the away-side peak observed by the PHENIX collaboration (PHENIX, 2005) in terms of energy lost to propagating sound modes, as previously suggested by Shuryak (2005) and Stoecker (2005). It contrasts with the results of Chaudhuri (2005), where a Mach cone structure was demonstrated in photon emission using a different source term, noting that the energy densities shown in that work (more than 20 times above the surrounding medium) do not reflect the situation at RHIC. The formalism enables future calculations of three-particle correlations as measured by PHENIX and STAR, and opens the question of whether sound is the only collective mode compatible with the data and how colorful and colorless sound modes can be consistently included.
