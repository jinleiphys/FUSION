# Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking and Nambu-Goldstone Bosons in Quantum Many-Body Systems

> Spontaneous symmetry breaking is a general principle, that constitutes the underlying concept of a vast number of physical phenomena ranging from ferromagnetism and superconductivity in condensed matter physics to the Higgs mechanism in the standard model of elementary particles. I focus on manifestations of spontaneously broken symmetries in systems that are not Lorentz invariant, which include both, nonrelativistic systems as well as relativistic systems at nonzero density, providing a self-contained review of the properties of spontaneously broken symmetries specific to such theories. Topics covered include: (i) Introduction to the mathematics of spontaneous symmetry breaking and the Goldstone theorem. (ii) Minimization of Higgs-type potentials for higher-dimensional representations. (iii) Counting rules for Nambu-Goldstone bosons and their dispersion relations. (iv) Construction of effective Lagrangians. Specific examples in both relativistic and nonrelativistic physics are worked out in detail.

## Key claim

Spontaneous symmetry breaking in nonrelativistic systems and relativistic systems at nonzero density leads to a richer variety of Nambu-Goldstone boson counting rules and dispersion relations than in Lorentz-invariant theories, with the number of NG bosons generally not equal to the number of broken generators and their energy-momentum relation potentially being quadratic rather than linear.

## Method

The paper provides a pedagogical review that first establishes the mathematical foundations of spontaneous symmetry breaking, including the subtleties of implementing symmetry transformations in infinite volume and the concept of inequivalent ground states. It then develops general counting rules for Nambu-Goldstone bosons based on the commutator structure of broken generators, following the Nielsen-Chadha classification and more recent developments, and illustrates these rules with explicit examples including a free nonrelativistic field theory, a ferromagnet, and a spin-one color superconductor. The effective Lagrangian approach of Leutwyler (1993) is presented as a model-independent description of spontaneously broken symmetries in nonrelativistic systems.

## Key numbers

- The free nonrelativistic particle exhibits a single NG boson with dispersion relation \(E_{\mathbf{k}} = \mathbf{k}^2/2m\), making it the simplest example of a type-II NG boson.
- The scalar product of two distinct ground states in the free nonrelativistic theory is \(|\langle z'|z\rangle| = e^{-\Omega |z'-z|^2}\), demonstrating orthogonality in the infinite volume limit.
- The commutator of the two translation generators in the free nonrelativistic theory develops a central charge: \([Q_1, Q_2] = 2i\Omega\), which is proportional to the system volume.
- The expectation value of the particle number operator in a coherent ground state is \(\langle z|Q_3|z\rangle = \Omega |z|^2\), giving a finite density in the infinite volume limit.

## Context

This review extends the classic work on the Goldstone theorem by Guralnik (1968) and the counting rules for NG bosons established by Nielsen and Chadha (1975), providing a unified treatment applicable to both relativistic and nonrelativistic systems. The paper enables further development of effective field theory descriptions for systems with spontaneously broken symmetries at finite density, such as color superconductors and Bose-Einstein condensates, and identifies a novel example of a NG boson with noninteger power of momentum dispersion that precludes conventional effective field theory description.
