# Heavy flavor as a probe of hot QCD matter produced in proton-proton collisions

> The creation of a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) is expected in heavy ion collisions. It came as a surprise that proton-proton collisions at ultrarelativistic energies show as well a ``QGP-like'' behavior and signs of the creation of a fluid, although the corresponding system size is not more than a few cubic femtometers. Even more surprisingly, also heavy flavor particles seem to be part of the fluid or at least interact with it. In this paper, we will investigate in a quantitative way this ``collective behavior'' of heavy flavor, by employing the newly developed EPOS4HQ approach, which has proven to be compatible with basic experimental data of light flavor hadrons. We will investigate all observables, which may manifest collectivity, as particle spectra, elliptic flow, baryon-to-meson ratios, and two-particle correlations, and compare the results with experimental data. We will try to disentangle initial state effects, those being due to interactions between charm quarks and plasma partons, and final state effects (hadronization).

# Heavy flavor as a probe of hot QCD matter produced in proton-proton collisions

## Key claim
The EPOS4HQ approach, which allows QGP formation when local energy density exceeds a system-size-independent critical value, can quantitatively reproduce experimental heavy-flavor observables in proton-proton collisions, suggesting that high-multiplicity pp collisions are the small-system limit of heavy-ion collisions rather than elementary reactions. The authors hedge that this conclusion applies only when the energy density exceeds the critical value, and that observables like transverse momentum spectra are not very sensitive to QGP presence, while baryon-to-meson ratios and elliptic flow are.

## Method
The authors employed the EPOS4HQ framework, which combines primary partonic scatterings with saturation scales, a core-corona separation procedure, and hydrodynamic evolution of the core. Heavy quarks interact with QGP partons via both elastic and inelastic pQCD scattering cross sections regulated by an effective Debye mass, and hadronize through either coalescence (when traversing QGP) or fragmentation (when not), with the coalescence probability computed from Wigner densities of charmed hadrons and thermal light quark distributions.

## Key numbers
- Critical energy density for QGP formation: $\epsilon_{\mathrm{FO}} = 0.57\ \mathrm{GeV/fm^3}$, corresponding to freeze-out temperature $T_{\mathrm{FO}} = 167\ \mathrm{MeV}$
- Start time of hydrodynamic evolution: $\tau_{0} = 0.40\ \mathrm{fm/c}$
- Root-mean-square radius of in-medium $D^0$ meson: $\sqrt{\langle r^2\rangle} \approx 0.85\ \mathrm{fm}$, giving Wigner width $\sigma = 3.725\ \mathrm{GeV^{-1}}$
- Quark masses used: $m_{u/d} = 0.1\ \mathrm{GeV}$, $m_{s} = 0.3\ \mathrm{GeV}$, $m_{c} = 1.5\ \mathrm{GeV}$
- Effective Debye mass parameter: $\kappa = 0.2$ in $m_{\mathrm{eff}} = \kappa m_D(T)$
- Constant $\alpha_s = 0.3$ used for gluon emission vertex in inelastic scattering
- K-factor for both elastic and inelastic collisions set to 1 (no modification of pQCD cross sections)
- Enhancement factor of $\Lambda_c/D^0$ ratio from coalescence: roughly a factor of 2.5 at low $p_T$
- Saturation of charm quark fraction in QGP and $v_2$ occurs at charged particle multiplicity $N_{ch} \approx 50$

## Context
This work extends the EPOS4 framework (Werner 2023) to heavy flavor in small systems, building on earlier EPOS versions that described heavy meson data in heavy-ion collisions (Gossiaux 2009, 2010). It directly addresses the surprising observations of QGP-like signatures in pp collisions, including long-range correlations (CMS 2010), strangeness enhancement (ALICE 2016), finite $D^0$ elliptic flow (CMS 2020), and enhanced charmed baryon production (ALICE 2018, 2020, 2021; CMS 2023). The results contradict the interpretation that process-dependent fragmentation functions are needed (Christiansen 2015), instead attributing the baryon enhancement to QGP formation and coalescence hadronization. The approach enables quantitative disentanglement of initial-state effects, interactions with QGP partons, and final-state hadronization in small systems.
