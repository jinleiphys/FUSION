# Progress and challenges in the theory of nuclei

> Nuclear theory today aims for a comprehensive theoretical framework that can describe all nuclei. I discuss recent progress in this pursuit and the associated challenges as we move forward.

## Key claim

Nuclear theory aims for a comprehensive framework describing all nuclei, but the abstract glosses over that this goal requires theoretical and experimental investigations of rare isotopes with unusual neutron-to-proton ratios, which are difficult to produce and study due to extremely short lifetimes.

## Method

The paper reviews multiple theoretical approaches to the nuclear many-body problem: ab initio methods (Green's function Monte Carlo, no-core shell model, coupled-cluster theory) for light to medium-mass nuclei, configuration interaction techniques for medium-mass nuclei, and density functional theory for heavy nuclei. The author discusses chiral Effective Field Theory for determining nuclear interactions, renormalization group techniques for evolving nucleon-nucleon interactions, and the inclusion of three-body forces and continuum effects via Gamow basis states.

## Key numbers

- The central repulsion of the Av18 potential at r=0 is approximately 3 GeV; in N3LO it is 500 MeV.
- For 4He, 16O, and 40Ca, triples corrections are factors of approximately 0.015, 0.066, and 0.081 smaller than CCSD correlation energies, respectively.
- The missing correlation energy from quadruples, pentuples, etc. is estimated to be of order 1 MeV for 40Ca.
- 16O is overbound by about 20 MeV and 40Ca by about 150 MeV when compared to experimental binding energies.
- The exact Faddeev-Yakubovsky result for 4He binding energy is -29.19(5) MeV; the CCSD(T) result is -29.2 MeV.
- An exponential extrapolation of CCSD(T) minima for 4He with 3NF yields E_infinity = -28.24 MeV, in excellent agreement with the exact result E = -28.20(5) MeV.
- Contributions from 3NFs account for only about 10% of the total binding energy in 4He.
- The calculated width of 10He is approximately 0.002 MeV.

## Context

This paper extends the line of work on nuclear many-body theory, referencing specific prior approaches such as the Av18 potential (Bob95), chiral EFT (Weinberg, Bira), N3LO interactions (N3LO), and the RISAC report. It enables future work by outlining the path toward connecting ab initio calculations in light-to-medium mass systems to nuclear density functional theory, and by emphasizing the need for petascale computing to achieve a comprehensive understanding of all nuclei.
