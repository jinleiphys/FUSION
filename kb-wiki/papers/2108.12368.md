# Uncertainty limits on neutron star radius measurements with gravitational waves

> Upcoming observing campaigns with improved detectors will yield numerous detections of gravitational waves from neutron star binary inspirals. Rare loud signals together with numerous signals of moderate strength promise stringent constraints on the properties of neutron star matter, with a projected radius statistical uncertainty of $50-200$m with ${\cal{O}}(2000)$ sources. Given this precision we revisit all analysis assumptions and identify sources of systematic errors, quantify their impact on radius extraction, and discuss their relative importance and ways to mitigate them.

## Key claim

Gravitational-wave observations of neutron star binaries could achieve a radius statistical uncertainty of 50-200 meters with approximately 2000 sources, but reaching this precision requires that all sources of systematic error in the analysis be quantified and reduced below the statistical uncertainty level.

## Method

The paper examines each component of gravitational-wave parameter estimation, the data, noise power spectral density, waveform model, and assumptions of Gaussian and stationary noise, and simulates the impact of systematic errors on tidal deformability and radius extraction. For non-Gaussian noise, the author injects sine-gaussian glitches overlapping with simulated binary neutron star signals at signal-to-noise ratios of 140 and 420 and tests whether simultaneous glitch modeling recovers unbiased tidal parameters. For noise power spectral density misestimation, the author analyzes signals with the detector strain sensitivity altered by up to plus or minus 10 percent in the 400-1000 Hz frequency range. For waveform accuracy, the author computes frequency-domain phase differences between signals from neutron stars with different radii relative to 13 km using the IMRPhenomD_NRTidalv2 waveform model.

## Key numbers

- Projected radius statistical uncertainty of 50-200 meters with approximately 2000 sources
- GW170817 had signal-to-noise ratio of 32 and combined tidal deformability of 300 (+420, -230) at 90 percent credible level
- A GW170817-like event would have signal-to-noise ratio of 100 at design sensitivity, 200 with A+, 300 with Voyager, and 1000 with 3G detectors
- Estimated detections per year: approximately 10 at design sensitivity, approximately 100 for A+, approximately 500 for Voyager
- Total signal-to-noise ratio from catalogs: median of 60 with 10 sources, 200 with 100 sources, 450 with 500 sources, 650 with 1000 sources
- A total signal-to-noise ratio of 1000 gives tidal deformability uncertainty of 20 and radius uncertainty of 50-200 meters for different neutron star masses
- A total signal-to-noise ratio of 200 from approximately 100 sources gives tidal deformability uncertainty of 100 and radius uncertainty of 1 km at 1.6 solar masses
- Calibration uncertainty of no more than 4 degrees in phase at 68 percent level in LIGO detectors during first half of O3, corresponding to 7 degrees at 90 percent level
- A 0.5 km radius difference induces 3-4 radians of dephasing; waveform systematics must be kept below this level
- A 0.5 km change in radius leads to noise-weighted phase change of approximately 0.3 radians

## Context

This work extends projections from earlier studies such as Lackey (2014) and Landry (2020) on combining multiple gravitational-wave events to constrain neutron star properties, and responds to the improved detector sensitivity expected from A+, Voyager, and 3G detectors. The paper identifies that waveform model systematics become significant for signal-to-noise ratios above approximately 100, consistent with findings from Dietrich (2020) and Dudi (2018), and that calibration uncertainty is currently comparable to the target radius uncertainty of 100 meters. The analysis enables future work on hierarchical inference of the neutron star equation of state from multiple sources, which must account for correlations between tidal deformability and mass ratio, selection effects, and population models for neutron star masses and spins.
