# Lambda-hypernuclear production in stopped (K-,pion) reactions reexamined

> DWIA calculations of Lambda-hypernuclear production rates in stopped K- reactions on several p-shell targets used recently in experiments by the FINUDA Collaboration are reported. Chirally motivated K- + N -> pion + Lambda in-medium transition amplitudes are employed and the sensitivity of the calculated rates to the initial K- atomic wavefunctions and final pion distorted waves is studied. The calculated rates are compared with measured rates, wherever available, confirming earlier observations that (i) the calculated rates are generally lower than the measured rates, and (ii) the deeper the K- nuclear potential, the worse is the discrepancy. The A dependence of the calculated 1s Lambda hypernuclear production rates is discussed for the first time, providing a useful tool in resolving the issue of depth of the K- nuclear potential near threshold.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that DWIA calculations of Λ-hypernuclear production rates in stopped K⁻ reactions on p-shell targets are systematically lower than measured rates, and that the discrepancy worsens with deeper K⁻-nuclear potentials; the authors propose that the A-dependence of 1sΛ production rates, rather than absolute normalizations, may resolve the depth of the K⁻-nuclear potential near threshold.

## Method

The authors performed distorted wave impulse approximation (DWIA) calculations for stopped K⁻ reactions on ⁷Li, ⁹Be, ¹²C, ¹³C, and ¹⁶O targets, using chirally motivated K⁻N → πΛ in-medium transition amplitudes to generate branching ratios (BRs). Initial K⁻-atomic wave functions were obtained from three different K⁻-nuclear optical potentials (shallow chiral [Kχ], medium [Keff], and deep density-dependent [KDD]), and final pion distorted waves were generated from two pion-nuclear optical potentials (πb and πc). The capture rates were computed for 1pN → 1sΛ and 1pN → 1pΛ transitions, with sensitivity tests performed on baryon wave functions, BR choices, and meson wave functions.

## Key numbers

- Calculated 1sΛ capture rate for ¹²ΛC with [Kχ] potential and πb pion potential: 0.425 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻; with [KDD] potential: 0.125 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (Table IV)
- Measured summed 1sΛ capture rate for ¹²ΛC: (0.98 ± 0.12) × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (KEK, Tamura)
- Calculated 1sΛ capture rate for ¹⁶ΛO with [Kχ] potential: 0.219 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻; with [KDD] potential: 0.055 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (Table IV)
- Measured summed 1sΛ capture rate for ¹⁶ΛO: (0.43 ± 0.06) × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (KEK, Tamura)
- Ratio of 1sΛ capture rate in ¹⁶O to that in ⁷Li: 2.66 for [Kχ] and 5.27 for [KDD] (Section IV.B.1)
- Branching ratio R(K⁻n → π⁻Λ) at ρ = ρ₀/2: 10.54 × 10⁻²; at ρ = 0: 9.68 × 10⁻² (Table I)
- Branching ratio R(K⁻p → π⁰Λ) at ρ = ρ₀/2: 5.27 × 10⁻²; at ρ = 0: 4.84 × 10⁻² (Table I)
- Calculated 1sΛ capture rate for ¹²ΛB with [Kχ] potential: 0.203 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻; with [KDD] potential: 0.060 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (Table V)
- Measured summed 1sΛ capture rate for ¹²ΛB: (0.28 ± 0.08) × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (BNL, Ahmed)
- Calculated summed 1pΛ capture rate (0⁺ + 2⁺) for ¹²ΛC with [Kχ]: 1.268 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻; with [KDD]: 0.431 × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (Table V)
- Measured summed 1pΛ capture rate for ¹²ΛC: (2.3 ± 0.3) × 10⁻³ per stopped K⁻ (KEK, Tamura)

## Context

This work extends previous DWIA calculations by Gal and Klieb (1970s), Matsuyama and Yazaki (1980s), and Cieply and Friedman (2000s), none of which achieved satisfactory agreement with measured rates. The key novelty is the use of chirally motivated in-medium K⁻N → πΛ branching ratios (BR1, BR2) instead of emulsion-derived BRs (BR3) from Van Der Velde (1970s), and the first systematic study of the A-dependence of 1sΛ production rates across the p-shell. The results enable future experiments, particularly those by the FINUDA Collaboration, to discriminate between shallow (≈ −50 MeV) and deep (≈ −190 MeV) K⁻-nuclear potentials by comparing the measured A-dependence with the calculated rates in Table IV, rather than relying on absolute normalization which is experimentally delicate.
