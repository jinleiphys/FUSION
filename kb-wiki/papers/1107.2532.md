# Theoretical Studies of Rare-Earth Nuclei leading to $_{50}$Sn-Daughter Products and the Associated Shell Effects

> Cluster decays of rare-earth nuclei are studied with a view to look for neutron magic shells for the $_{50}$Sn nucleus as the daughter product always. The $^{100}$Sn and $^{132}$Sn radioactivities are studied to find the most probable cluster decays and the possibility, if any, of new neutron shells. For a wide range of parent nuclei considered here (from Ba to Pt) $^{12}$C from $^{112}$Ba and $^{78}$Ni from $^{210}$Pt parent are predicted to be the most probable clusters (minimum decay half-life) referring to $^{100}$Sn and $^{132}$Sn daughters, respectively. Also, $^{22}$Mg decay of $^{122}$Sm is indicated at the second best possibilty for $^{100}$Sn-daughter decay. In addition to these well known magic shells (Z=50, N=50 and 82), a new magic shell at Z=50, N=66 ($^{116}$Sn daughter) is indicated for the $^{64}$Ni decay from $^{180}$Pt parent.

## Key claim

The paper predicts that, in addition to the well-known neutron magic numbers N=50 and 82 for the Z=50 Sn daughter nucleus, a new magic shell may exist at Z=50, N=66 (corresponding to a \({}^{116}\)Sn daughter), based on the calculated minimum decay half-life for \({}^{64}\)Ni emission from \({}^{180}\)Pt. However, the predicted half-lives for this and the \({}^{132}\)Sn-daughter radioactivity are too large for present-day experiments, making the results of "more of an academic interest."

## Method

The author used the preformed cluster model (PCM), which treats cluster decay as a two-step process of cluster preformation and subsequent barrier penetration. The fragmentation potential was calculated from experimental binding energies (Audi and Wapstra 2003) supplemented by theoretical masses (Möller et al. 1995), including Coulomb and nuclear proximity potentials. Decay half-lives were computed for 329 parent nuclei from Ba to Pt (mass numbers A=110-210) emitting clusters from C to Ni, always leaving a Sn daughter, to identify minima in half-life that indicate shell closures.

## Key numbers

- The shortest half-life in the entire study is \(\log_{10}T_{1/2}(s)=2.27\) (Q-value = 22.16 MeV) for \({}^{12}\)C decay of \({}^{112}\)Ba, producing a \({}^{100}\)Sn daughter.
- For the \({}^{132}\)Sn daughter, the most probable cluster is \({}^{78}\)Ni from \({}^{210}\)Pt, with predicted \(\log_{10}T_{1/2}(s)=34.974\) (Q-value = 119.292 MeV).
- For the newly indicated \({}^{116}\)Sn daughter (N=66), the most probable cluster is \({}^{64}\)Ni from \({}^{180}\)Pt, with predicted \(\log_{10}T_{1/2}(s)=34.975\) (Q-value = 124.192 MeV).
- The second-best possibility for \({}^{100}\)Sn-daughter decay is \({}^{22}\)Mg from \({}^{122}\)Sm, with \(\log_{10}T_{1/2}(s)=9.735\).
- For \({}^{132}\)Sn-daughter decay, the second-best case is \({}^{50}\)Ca from \({}^{182}\)Yb.

## Context

This work extends earlier PCM predictions by Kumar et al. (1994) for \({}^{100}\)Sn and \({}^{132}\)Sn daughter radioactivities from rare-earth parents, and the QMFT-based predictions of Sandulescu, Poenaru, and Greiner (1980). It also follows the approach used by the author for sub-shell closed Zr daughters (Sushil 2003, 2009). The study enables future experimental searches for cluster radioactivity in the rare-earth region, particularly for the \({}^{100}\)Sn-daughter case (e.g., \({}^{12}\)C from \({}^{112}\)Ba), which has a half-life potentially within experimental reach, while the heavier cluster decays (\({}^{78}\)Ni, \({}^{64}\)Ni) remain beyond current detection capabilities.
