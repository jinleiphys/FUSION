# Calculation of Exclusive Cross Sections with the Lorentz Integral Transform Method

> The longitudinal structure function of the d(e,e'p) exclusive cross section is calculated with the Lorentz integral transform method. In this approach final state interaction is fully taken into account, but without using a final state wave function. Cross sections are obtained via the inversion of the transform. It is shown that the inversion results are very stable. The comparison to a conventional calculation with an explicit np final state wave function shows that the obtained results are also very precise. Thus the method opens up the possibility to obtain exclusive cross sections for reactions with more than two particles, where it is generally very difficult to calculate the exact final state wave function.

## Key claim

The Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method can be extended to calculate exclusive cross sections, not just inclusive ones, by computing transition matrix elements without explicit final state wave functions. The authors demonstrate that for the deuteron breakup test case, the inversion of the transform is stable and yields results with relative differences below 1% compared to conventional calculations, except in cases with very small final state interaction effects where differences may reach a few percent.

## Method

The authors calculate the longitudinal structure function of the d(e,e'p) exclusive cross section using the LIT method, solving inhomogeneous differential equations with bound-state-like boundary conditions to obtain the Lorentz transform, then inverting it to extract the transition matrix elements. They test the method on the deuteron breakup with a semi-realistic TN potential (central, no tensor force) for three different kinematics with strong final state interaction effects, comparing results to conventional calculations using explicit np final state wave functions. The inversion uses an expansion in basis functions with a regularization parameter N chosen within a stability region.

## Key numbers

- For kinematics (i) (E_np=120 MeV, q_cm^2=5 fm^-2): relative differences between LIT and conventional calculation for t^FSI are less than 1% for l=0,2,4 multipoles; the l=4 FSI matrix element is only -2.5% of the corresponding Born term, making the relative difference for the total matrix element of order 10^-4
- For kinematics (ii) (photon line, E_gamma=70 MeV): relative FSI effects compared to Born term are -100% (l=0), -25% (l=2), -1.5% (l=4); LIT results agree with conventional calculation within less than 1% for real parts
- For kinematics (iii) (E_np=1 MeV, q_cm^2=2 fm^-2): relative FSI effects compared to Born term are -230% (l=0), +2.5% (l=2); LIT results deviate from conventional calculation by about 1% for real parts
- The elastic monopole contribution R_00(E_0) is about 1.1 fm^(3/2) for kinematics (i), 14 fm^(3/2) for kinematics (ii), and 15 fm^(3/2) for kinematics (iii)
- Alternative methods for calculating the LIT (using double differential equations) yield relative deviations of order 1% compared to the standard scalar product method

## Context

This work extends the LIT method, previously applied successfully to inclusive breakup cross sections in few-body physics (Efros, Leidemann, Orlandini 1994; 1999), to exclusive reactions for the first time. It follows a theoretical proposal by Efros (1985) for applying integral transforms to exclusive reactions using the Stieltjes transform, which was found problematic for inclusive reactions due to inversion difficulties (Efros, Leidemann, Orlandini 1992). The method enables calculation of exclusive cross sections for reactions with more than two particles in the final state, where exact final state wave functions are generally impossible to compute. The authors note that for three-body final states, a different approach using double differential equations is necessary, and their test on the deuteron case suggests such calculations should be reliable.
