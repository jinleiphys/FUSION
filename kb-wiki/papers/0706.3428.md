# Aspects of causal viscous hydrodynamics

> We investigate the phenomenology of freely expanding fluids, with different material properties, evolving through the Israel-Stewart (IS) causal viscous hydrodynamics, and compare our results with those obtained in the relativistic Eckart-Landau-Navier-Stokes (ELNS) acausal viscous hydrodynamics. Through the analysis of scaling invariants we give a definition of thermalization time which can be self-consistently determined in viscous hydrodynamics. Next we construct the solutions for one-dimensional boost-invariant flows. Expansion of viscous fluids is slower than that of one-dimensional ideal fluids, resulting in entropy production. At late times, these flows are reasonably well approximated by solutions obtained in ELNS hydrodynamics. Estimates of initial energy densities from observed final values are strongly dependent on the dynamics one chooses. For the same material, and the same final state, IS hydrodynamics gives the smallest initial energy density. We also study fluctuations about these one-dimensional boost-invariant backgrounds; they are damped in ELNS hydrodynamics but can become sound waves in IS hydrodynamics. The difference is obvious in power spectra due to clear signals of wave-interference in IS hydrodynamics, which is completely absent in ELNS dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that Israel-Stewart (IS) causal viscous hydrodynamics gives the smallest initial energy density for a given final state compared to both ideal and Eckart-Landau-Navier-Stokes (ELNS) acausal viscous hydrodynamics, but this result is conditional on using the same material properties and final state; additionally, fluctuations around boost-invariant flow are damped in ELNS but can become propagating sound waves in IS hydrodynamics, a qualitative difference visible in power spectra.

## Method

The authors reduce the Israel-Stewart causal viscous hydrodynamic equations to three coupled scalar equations for longitudinal (boost-invariant) flow in curvilinear coordinates appropriate to heavy-ion collisions, neglecting baryon density, charge density, heat flux, and bulk viscosity. They analyze scaling solutions for three fluid models (simple fluid with constant material properties, Boltzmann fluid with fixed shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, and conformal fluids) and study linearized fluctuations around these boost-invariant backgrounds by Fourier transforming in spacetime rapidity. The ELNS limit is recovered by setting the relaxation time to zero.

## Key numbers

- For a Boltzmann fluid, the dimensionless combination \(\chi = \epsilon \tau_\pi / \eta = 9/2\) (derived from \(\chi = 3/2 c_s^2\) with \(c_s^2 = 1/3\)).
- For a conformal fluid with AdS/CFT prediction, \(\eta/s = 1/4\pi\) and \(a = (1 - \ln 2)/6\pi\), yielding \(\chi = (1 - \ln 2)/2 \approx 0.15\).
- The Bjorken scaling solution for ideal fluids gives \(\epsilon(\tau, \eta) = \epsilon_0 (\tau_0 / \tau)^B\) where \(B = 1 + c_s^2\).
- For ideal fluids, propagating sound waves exist only for wavenumbers \(k > k_0\) where \(k_0 = (1 - c_s^2)/(2 c_s)\); for \(c_s^2 = 1/3\), \(k_0 = 1/\sqrt{3}\).
- The damping exponent for ideal fluid fluctuations is \(\omega_d(k) = B + \frac12(1 - c_s^2) - c_s \sqrt{k_0^2 - k^2}\) for \(k < k_0\).

## Context

This work extends the study of causal viscous hydrodynamics initiated by Israel and Stewart (1979) and applies it to the boost-invariant geometry relevant to relativistic heavy-ion collisions, following earlier studies by Muronga (2002), Rischke (2004), Heinz (2005), and Baier (2006). The paper provides a systematic comparison between IS and ELNS formalisms for three fluid models, establishing scaling invariants (analogous to Reynolds and Knudsen numbers) that connect flows with different material properties. The analysis of fluctuations and power spectra enables future extraction of the equation of state or initial conditions from event-by-event measurements in heavy-ion experiments, and the self-consistent definition of thermalization time via the condition \(\varphi < 4/3\) offers a way to determine when IS hydrodynamics becomes applicable.
