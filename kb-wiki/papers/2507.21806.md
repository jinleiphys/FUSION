# Shape evolution in neutron-rich odd-even $^{105-109}$Nb isotopes

> Neutron-rich nuclei around $Z\sim40$ exhibit multiple shape transitions. This region shows one of the sharpest transitions in the nuclear chart, from a spherical vibrator at $N=58$ to a strongly deformed prolate shape at $N=60$, with largest deformations seen for $_{38}$Sr and $_{40}$Zr. Below $Z=36$, a spherical-to-oblate transition is predicted, while above $Z=42$ and $N\ge60$, the shape evolves from axial to triaxial. Even-$Z$ nuclei have been well studied, but odd-$Z$ isotopes such as Nb offer additional insights into these mechanisms. The Nb isotopes lie at the boundary between axially deformed Zr and triaxially deformed Mo nuclei. This work explores the structure of neutron-rich Nb nuclei up to $N=68$, aiming to understand shape evolution with isospin and the onset of triaxiality. Two complementary fission experiments were used: (i) $^{238}$U+$^9$Be at GANIL in inverse kinematics with AGATA, EXOGAM, and VAMOS++, allowing prompt and delayed $γ$-ray spectroscopy with isotopic identification; (ii) spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf with the Gammasphere array providing high-fold $γ$-coincidence data. The level scheme of $^{105}$Nb was significantly extended with two new negative-parity bands. A revised scheme is proposed for $^{107}$Nb, differing from previous results, and new structures are reported in $^{109}$Nb. The signature splitting analysis indicates triaxial deformation for positive-parity bands, while negative-parity bands show axial symmetry, similar to Zr. This reveals a shape coexistence in neutron-rich Nb nuclei.

## Key claim
The paper claims that shape coexistence exists in neutron-rich Nb isotopes, where the ground-state band exhibits triaxial deformation (attributed to a proton hole coupled to a triaxially deformed Mo core), while the negative-parity bands display an axially symmetric deformed structure similar to Zr. This conclusion is hedged by the fact that spin assignments for the new bands are based on systematic comparisons with lighter Nb isotopes rather than direct measurements, and the band-head energy for one negative-parity band in $^{105}$Nb remains unknown.

## Method
The structure of $^{105-109}$Nb was investigated using high-resolution gamma-ray spectroscopy of fission fragments from two complementary reactions: inverse-kinematics $^{9}$Be($^{238}$U,f) at GANIL (using AGATA, EXOGAM, and VAMOS++ for isotopic identification and prompt/delayed gamma detection) and spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf at LBNL (using the Gammasphere array for high-fold gamma-gamma coincidence data). Level schemes were constructed from isotopically gated gamma-ray spectra and coincidence analysis, with band assignments and triaxiality discussed via signature splitting analysis.

## Key numbers
- **$^{105}$Nb**: Half-life of the $(3/2^-)$ state measured as $T_{1/2}=94(12)$ ns from Gammasphere data.
- **$^{105}$Nb**: A new transition at 58.1(1) keV is identified as the $(3/2^-)$ band head to ground-state transition, detected using Gammasphere.
- **$^{105}$Nb**: The gamma-ray energy resolution achieved with AGATA was 5 keV (FWHM) at 1.2 MeV after Doppler correction.
- **$^{252}$Cf experiment**: Statistics included $5.7\times 10^{11}$ $\gamma-\gamma-\gamma$ events in 3D cubes and $1.9\times 10^{11}$ $\gamma-\gamma-\gamma-\gamma$ events in 4D hypercubes.
- **$^{99}$Nb**: A revised level scheme is proposed for the positive-parity band, with new transitions at 681.2(3) keV, 922.7(3) keV, and 361.2(5) keV.

## Context
This work extends the systematic study of shape evolution in the $Z\sim40$ region, where a sharp spherical-to-prolate transition occurs at $N=60$ (seen in Sr and Zr) and an axial-to-triaxial evolution is known for $Z\ge42$. It builds on prior studies of Nb isotopes by Luo (2005) and Hagen (2017), correcting the tentative level scheme for $^{107}$Nb that was contaminated by $^{103}$Nb. The results enable a more detailed comparison of shape coexistence mechanisms between odd-Z Nb and their even-Z neighbors (Zr and Mo), and provide new data for testing theoretical models of triaxial deformation and the role of the $\pi g_{9/2}$ orbital.
