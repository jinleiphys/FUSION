# Symmetry energy, unstable nuclei, and neutron star crusts

> Phenomenological approach to inhomogeneous nuclear matter is useful to describe fundamental properties of atomic nuclei and neutron star crusts in terms of the equation of state of uniform nuclear matter. We review a series of researches that we have developed by following this approach. We start with more than 200 equations of state that are consistent with empirical masses and charge radii of stable nuclei and then apply them to describe matter radii and masses of unstable nuclei, proton elastic scattering and total reaction cross sections off unstable nuclei, and nuclei in neutron star crusts including nuclear pasta. We finally discuss the possibility of constraining the density dependence of the symmetry energy from experiments on unstable nuclei and even observations of quasi-periodic oscillations in giant flares of soft gamma-ray repeaters.

## Key claim

The paper reviews a phenomenological approach that uses more than 200 equations of state consistent with empirical masses and charge radii of stable nuclei, but finds that the incompressibility \(K_0\) and the density symmetry coefficient \(L\) remain uncertain even after fitting to stable-nucleus data, and that constraining \(L\) requires future systematic measurements of matter radii or elastic scattering peak angles for unstable nuclei with large neutron excess.

## Method

The authors constructed a macroscopic nuclear model based on a Thomas-Fermi density functional that depends on the equation of state of uniform nuclear matter, parametrized by five EOS parameters (\(n_0\), \(w_0\), \(K_0\), \(S_0\), \(L\)) and a gradient coefficient \(F_0\). They fitted the calculated binding energies and root-mean-square charge radii to empirical data for stable nuclei on the smoothed beta stability line, obtaining about 200 acceptable sets of \(K_0\) and \(L\). They then applied these EOS sets to predict matter radii, proton elastic scattering differential cross sections (via the optical-limit Glauber model), total reaction cross sections, and two-proton separation energies for unstable nuclei, and compared the results with existing data where available.

## Key numbers

* The rms deviations of calculated masses from measured values for stable nuclei are of order 3 MeV, comparable to the Weizsäcker-Bethe mass formula.
* The rms deviations of calculated charge radii from measured values for stable nuclei are about 0.06 fm, comparable to the \(A^{1/3}\) law.
* Differences of order 0.1 fm occur in the predicted matter radii of very neutron-rich Ni and Sn isotopes due to uncertainties in \(L\) (varying \(L\) from 0 to 80 MeV at fixed \(K_0\)).
* For proton elastic scattering off \(^{80}\)Ni at \(T_p = 800\) MeV, the difference in the first diffraction peak angle between \(^{58}\)Ni and \(^{80}\)Ni shows a dependence on \(L\) large enough to enable extraction of \(L\) if the peak angle can be measured with accuracy of order 0.01 deg.
* The optimal gradient coefficient \(F_0\) ranges from approximately 60 to 70 MeV fm\(^5\) across the acceptable EOS sets.

## Context

This work extends the phenomenological approach of Oyamatsu (1993) and Oyamatsu and Iida (2003) that connects the nuclear equation of state to global nuclear properties via a macroscopic Thomas-Fermi model. It contrasts with mean-field model predictions (e.g., Skyrme-Hartree-Fock and Gogny models) and with constraints from heavy-ion data, pigmy dipole resonances, and isobaric analog states, which the authors note are not always consistent with each other (Tsang, 2012). The results enable future experiments on unstable nuclei at facilities such as RIKEN and GSI to constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy, and they also connect to neutron star crust properties including nuclear pasta and quasi-periodic oscillations in soft gamma-ray repeater giant flares.
