# The Temperature Dependence of Solar Neutrino Fluxes

> By comparing neutrino fluxes and central temperatures calculated from 1000 detailed numerical solar models, we derive improved scaling laws which show how each of the neutrino fluxes depends upon the central temperature (flux $\propto T^m$); we also estimate uncertainties for the temperature exponents. With the aid of a one-zone model of the sun, we derive expressions for the temperature exponents of the neutrino fluxes. For the most important neutrino fluxes, the exponents calculated with the one-zone model agree to within 20\% or better with the exponents extracted from the detailed numerical models. The one-zone model provides a physical understanding of the temperature dependence of the neutrino fluxes. For the $pp$ neutrino flux, the one-zone model explains the (initially-surprising) dependence of the flux upon a negative power of the temperature and suggests a new functional dependence. This new function makes explicit the strong anti-correlation between the $^7$Be and $pp$ neutrino fluxes. The one-zone model also predicts successfully the average linear relations between neutrino fluxes, but cannot predict the appreciable scatter in a $Δϕ_i/ϕ_i$ versus $Δϕ_j/ϕ_j$ diagram.

## Key claim

The paper derives improved scaling laws relating solar neutrino fluxes to central temperature (flux ∝ T^m) from 1000 detailed numerical solar models, but the authors acknowledge that the one-zone model they develop to explain these scalings agrees with the detailed models only to within about 20% for the most important neutrino fluxes, and that the one-zone model cannot predict the appreciable scatter in flux-flux correlation diagrams.

## Method

The authors analyzed 1000 detailed numerical solar models from the Monte Carlo study of Bahcall and Ulrich (1988), fitting power-law relations between each neutrino flux and the central temperature by minimizing residuals in logarithmic space. They then constructed a static one-zone model of the present-day sun with fixed temperature and density, deriving analytic expressions for the temperature exponents of each neutrino flux from the nuclear reaction rates and equilibrium abundances. The one-zone model predictions were compared to the numerical results, and the Fogli-Lisi sum rule (1995) was used to check consistency with the constant solar luminosity constraint.

## Key numbers

- Temperature exponent for pp neutrinos: m(pp) = -1.1 from detailed models (one-zone gives m = -0.9)
- Temperature exponent for ⁷Be neutrinos: m(⁷Be) = 10 from detailed models (one-zone gives m = 11)
- Temperature exponent for ⁸B neutrinos: m(⁸B) = 24 from detailed models (one-zone gives m = 25)
- Temperature exponent for pep neutrinos: m(pep) = -2.4 ± 0.9 from detailed models
- Temperature exponent for ¹³N neutrinos: m(¹³N) = 24 from detailed models
- Temperature exponent for ¹⁵O neutrinos: m(¹⁵O) = 27 from detailed models
- Temperature exponent for ¹⁷F neutrinos: m(¹⁷F) = 28 from detailed models
- One-zone model characteristic central temperature: T_c = 14 × 10⁶ K
- Standard solar model central temperature: T_{c,SSM} = 15.64 × 10⁶ K
- The pp flux is represented as φ(pp) ∝ [1 - 0.08 (T/T_{SSM})^m'] where the factor 0.08 equals R_{34}/2R_{33}
- One-zone model gives m'(pp) ≈ 11; detailed models yield m'(pp) = 13.0
- Slope of Δφ(⁷Be)/φ(⁷Be) versus Δφ(pp)/φ(pp): Monte Carlo slope ≈ -9, one-zone slope ≈ -13
- Slope of Δφ(⁸B)/φ(⁸B) versus Δφ(⁷Be)/φ(⁷Be): Monte Carlo slope ≈ 22, one-zone slope ≈ 28
- Slope of Δφ(⁸B)/φ(⁸B) versus Δφ(pp)/φ(pp): Monte Carlo slope ≈ -2.4, one-zone slope ≈ -2.3

## Context

This work extends the earlier temperature scaling relations from Bahcall and Ulrich (1988) by providing formally fitted exponents with uncertainty estimates and by adding exponents for the minor neutrino fluxes (pep, ¹³N, ¹⁵O, ¹⁷F) that were previously unavailable. The one-zone model provides a physical explanation for the counter-intuitive negative temperature dependence of the pp neutrino flux, which had been known since 1988 but not explained. The results enable quantitative study of non-standard solar models that alter the central temperature, supporting the conclusion from Castellani, Degl'Innocenti, and Fiorentini (1993) and others that changes in central temperature alone cannot reconcile the four operating solar neutrino experiments with theoretical predictions.
