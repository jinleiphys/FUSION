# Improvements to model of projectile fragmentation

> In a recent paper [Phys. Rev. C 044612 (2011)] we proposed a model for calculating cross-sections of various reaction products which arise from disintegration of projectile like fragment resulting from heavy ion collisions at intermediate or higher energy. The model has three parts: (1) abrasion, (2) disintegration of the hot abraded projectile like fragment (PLF) into nucleons and primary composites using a model of equilibrium statistical mechanics and (3) possible evaporation of hot primary composites. It was assumed that the PLF resulting from abrasion has one temperature T. Data suggested that while just one value of T seemed adequate for most cross-sections calculations, it failed when dealing with very peripheral collisions. We have now introduced a variable T=T(b) where b is the impact parameter of the collision. We argue there are data which not only show that T must be a function of b but, in addition, also point to an approximate value of T for a given b. We propose a very simple formula: T(b)=D_0+D_1(A_s(b)/A_0) where A_s(b) is the mass of the abraded PLF and A_0 is the mass of the projectile; D_0 and D_1 are constants. Using this model we compute cross-sections for several collisions and compare with data.

# Improvements to model of projectile fragmentation

## Key claim
The authors introduce an impact-parameter-dependent temperature T(b) for the projectile-like fragment (PLF) formed in abrasion, replacing the previous assumption of a single constant temperature. They propose that T(b) can be expressed as a simple linear function of the ratio A_s(b)/A_0, where A_s(b) is the mass of the abraded PLF and A_0 is the projectile mass, and that this dependence is necessary to reproduce experimental data for very peripheral collisions.

## Method
The model combines three stages: (1) abrasion using straight-line geometry to determine the PLF composition as a function of impact parameter b; (2) expansion of the hot PLF to one-third normal nuclear density followed by statistical breakup using the canonical thermodynamic model (CTM) at temperature T(b); and (3) sequential evaporation from the primary hot composites. The temperature T(b) is determined iteratively by matching calculated M_IMF versus Z_bound to experimental data, and then parametrized as T(b) = D_0 + D_1 (A_s(b)/A_0). Cross-sections are computed by discretizing the impact parameter range into bins, each with its own temperature, and summing contributions.

## Key numbers
- For 124Sn on 119Sn at 600 MeV/nucleon, extracted temperatures range from T = 6.398 MeV at b = 2.912 fm (Z_bound = 11.0) down to T = 4.190 MeV at b = 10.876 fm (Z_bound = 49.0).
- For 107Sn on 119Sn at 600 MeV/nucleon, extracted temperatures range from T = 6.200 MeV at b = 3.886 fm (Z_bound = 15.0) down to T = 4.000 MeV at b = 10.876 fm (Z_bound = 47.0).
- A universal parametrization T(b) = 7.5 MeV - (A_s(b)/A_0) * 4.5 MeV is used for most cross-section calculations; for Sn isotopes a slightly different fit T(b) = 7.2 MeV - (A_s(b)/A_0) * 3.2 MeV gives better agreement for M_IMF versus Z_bound.
- For 58Ni on 9Be at 140 MeV/nucleon, the PLF mass fraction at b = 0 is approximately A_s/A_0 ≈ 0.6, in contrast to symmetric collisions where A_s/A_0 ≈ 0 at b = 0.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous model (Mallik, 2011) which assumed a single constant temperature for the PLF and failed for very peripheral collisions. The new impact-parameter-dependent temperature approach is validated against experimental data from Ogul (for Sn+Sn reactions at 600 MeV/nucleon), Mocko (for Ni on Be and Ta at 140 MeV/nucleon), and Reinhold (for Xe on Al at 790 MeV/nucleon). The extracted temperatures are compared with those obtained from the Albergo formula (Albergo) and with results from the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) calculations by Botvina. The work enables systematic calculation of projectile fragmentation cross-sections across a wide range of systems and impact parameters, including very peripheral collisions that were previously inaccessible.
