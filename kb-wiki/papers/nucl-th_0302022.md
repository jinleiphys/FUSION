# Mean first passage time for nuclear fission and the emission of light particles

> The concept of a mean first passage time is used to study the time lapse over which a fissioning system may emit light particles. The influence of the "transient" and "saddle to scission times" on this emission are critically examined. It is argued that within the limits of Kramers' picture of fission no enhancement over that given by his rate formula need to be considered.

## Key claim

Within the limits of Kramers' picture of fission, there is no enhancement of light particle emission beyond that given by Kramers' rate formula; the transient effect and saddle-to-scission time do not provide additional emission time beyond the mean first passage time (MFPT), which is exactly half the total fission time to reach the saddle.

## Method

The authors use the concept of the mean first passage time (MFPT) from the Smoluchowski equation (the overdamped limit of Kramers' equation) to compute the average time for a fissioning system to reach a given exit point from the potential minimum. They compare analytic MFPT results with Langevin simulations for a cubic potential and for small-barrier cases, examining the dependence on the exit point and the role of initial conditions.

## Key numbers

- For the Langevin simulation parameters (T=3 MeV, E_b=8 MeV, ℏω_a=1 MeV, η_a=5), the transient delay is about 5-10 × 10⁻²¹ sec.
- For T/E_b = 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1.0, the MFPT to reach the saddle Q_b is exactly half the asymptotic value (the mean fission lifetime τ_f).
- For small barriers (E_b=1 MeV and vanishing barrier) at T=2, 3, 4 MeV, the MFPT-derived width Γ_mfpt = ℏ/τ_mfpt is of order 0.9 to 2.3 MeV, comparable to typical neutron widths of 1-3 MeV for medium-heavy nuclei.
- The friction coefficient γ/C is about 1 ℏ/MeV at T=2 MeV and about 3 ℏ/MeV at T=3 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the Kramers (1940) picture of fission by clarifying that the transient effect, previously interpreted by Grangé et al. (1980s) and others as allowing additional neutron emission beyond the Kramers rate, is not consistent with the MFPT formalism. The authors argue that statistical codes using static probabilities should use the ratio Γ_n/Γ_K rather than attempting to incorporate transient or saddle-to-scission times. The paper enables a re-examination of experimental analyses that have followed the conventional line of invoking transient effects, and suggests that deviations from Γ_n/Γ_K may instead indicate pre-equilibrium effects or more complicated potential landscapes.
