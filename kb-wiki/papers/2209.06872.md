# Snowmass Neutrino Frontier: Neutrino Interaction Cross Sections (NF06) Topical Group Report

> A thorough understanding of neutrino cross sections in a wide range of energies is crucial for the successful execution of the entire neutrino physics program. In order to extract neutrino properties, long-baseline experiments need an accurate determination of neutrino cross sections within their detector(s). Since very few of the needed neutrino cross sections across the energy spectrum are directly measured, we emphasize the need for theoretical input and indirect measurements such as electron scattering, which would complement direct measurements. In this report we briefly summarize the current status of our knowledge of the neutrino cross sections and articulate needs of the experiments, ongoing and planned, at energies ranging from CEvNS and supernova neutrino energies to the DUNE and atmospheric neutrino energies.

## Key claim

This Snowmass topical group report argues that the successful execution of the entire neutrino physics program requires a thorough understanding of neutrino cross sections across a wide energy range, but emphasizes that since very few needed cross sections are directly measured, theoretical input and indirect measurements such as electron scattering are necessary to complement direct measurements.

## Method

The report surveys the current status of neutrino cross section knowledge and articulates the needs of ongoing and planned experiments across multiple energy regimes, from coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering (CEvNS) and supernova neutrino energies through the DUNE and atmospheric neutrino energy ranges. The authors identify gaps between directly measured cross sections and those required for oscillation analyses, and recommend a program of theoretical calculations combined with electron scattering measurements to address these gaps.

## Key numbers

- The DUNE far detector modules are designed with a nominal 10 kt fiducial mass per module, with the single-phase module having 10 kt active mass and the dual-phase module having 12.096 kt active mass (10.643 kt fiducial)
- The DUNE experiment has a nominal operational lifetime of 20 years, with beam turn-on expected in 2026 and a planned beam power of 1.2 MW
- The single-phase TPC has a maximum drift length of 3.52 m with a drift time of 2.25 ms, while the dual-phase TPC has a maximum drift length of 12 m with a drift time of 7.5 ms
- The supernova neutrino burst readout window is 30 s, with a 10 s pre-trigger buffer period

## Context

This report was prepared for the Snowmass 2021 process as part of the Neutrino Frontier, specifically the NF06 topical group on neutrino interaction cross sections. It extends the discussions from prior community planning exercises and builds on the work of experiments such as DUNE, MicroBooNE, MINERvA, and COHERENT. The report enables future cross section measurement programs by identifying specific energy regimes where direct measurements are lacking and where electron scattering data could provide complementary constraints, particularly for the DUNE long-baseline oscillation program and supernova neutrino detection.
