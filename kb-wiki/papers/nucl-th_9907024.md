# Nuclear currents based on the integral form of the continuity equation

> We present an approach to obtain new forms of the nuclear electromagnetic current, which is based on an integral form of the continuity equation. The procedure can be used to restore current conservation in model calculations in which the continuity equation is not verified. Besides, it provides, as a particular result, the so-called Siegert's form of the nuclear current, first obtained by Friar and Fallieros by extending Siegert's theorem to arbitrary values of the momentum transfer. The new currents are explicitly conserved and permit a straightforward analysis of their behavior at both low and high momentum transfers. The results are illustrated with a simple nuclear model which includes a harmonic oscillator mean potential.

## Key claim

The paper presents a general family of conserved nuclear electromagnetic currents derived from an integral form of the continuity equation, which includes the Siegert current of Friar and Fallieros as a special case; however, the authors find that in a simple harmonic-oscillator model, neither the Siegert-type current nor their new alternative current produces better results than simply using the non-conserved current, casting doubt on the utility of "restoring" current conservation in model calculations.

## Method

The authors start from the differential continuity equation and derive an integral continuity equation (ICE) in both coordinate and momentum space by integrating along the radial direction or along a scaling parameter. They then define two families of conserved currents by choosing integration limits that set the boundary term to zero: one with the lower limit at zero momentum transfer (yielding the Siegert current of Friar and Fallieros) and one with the upper limit at infinity (yielding a new current). The asymptotic behavior of both currents and their multipoles is analyzed analytically, and the method is tested on electric multipole transitions in a single-particle harmonic-oscillator model of 16O and 39K, where the spin-orbit current is deliberately omitted to simulate a non-conserved current.

## Key numbers

- The harmonic oscillator model parameters for 16O: V0 = -53.6 MeV, b = 1.67 fm, VLS = -4.20 MeV.
- The harmonic oscillator model parameters for 40Ca: V0 = -55.7 MeV, b = 1.80 fm, VLS = -1.90 MeV.
- For q > 1 fm^-1, the new current J^(infinity) gives results closer to the "pseudo-data" than the Siegert current J^(0), while for q below 1 fm^-1 the Siegert current is more accurate.
- The Siegert current J^(0) diverges as O(1/r^2) near the origin in coordinate space, while J^(infinity) decays as O(1/r^2) at large r.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Friar and Fallieros (1984), who derived a Siegert current valid for arbitrary momentum transfer by extending Siegert's theorem beyond the long-wavelength limit. The authors show that the Friar-Fallieros current is a special case of a broader family of conserved currents obtainable from the integral continuity equation. The paper also relates to earlier work by Amaro, Ameziane and Lallena (1996) on models that do not satisfy the continuity equation, and to studies by Caparelli and de Passos (1997) and Friar and Haxton (1985) that found pathological high-momentum-transfer behavior in the Siegert current. The new current J^(infinity) provides a prescription that is well-behaved at high momentum transfer, though the authors caution that neither prescription outperforms the simple non-conserved current in their test cases, leaving open the question of how best to handle current conservation in model calculations.
