# Applications of a Global Nuclear-Structure Model to Studies of the Heaviest Elements

> We present some new results on heavy-element nuclear-structure properties calculated on the basis of the finite-range droplet model and folded-Yukawa single-particle potential. Specifically, we discuss calculations of nuclear ground-state masses and microscopic corrections, $α$-decay properties, $β$-decay properties, fission potential-energy surfaces, and spontaneous-fission half-lives. These results, obtained in a global nuclear-structure approach, are particularly reliable for describing the stability properties of the heaviest elements.

## Key claim

The finite-range droplet model (FRDM) combined with the folded-Yukawa single-particle potential provides particularly reliable predictions for the stability properties of the heaviest elements, but the authors caution that spontaneous-fission half-lives may be significantly different from their "ballpark" value of 1 ms due to general calculation uncertainties and the possibility of huge increases from specialization energies in odd systems.

## Method

The authors used the macroscopic-microscopic method, where the total nuclear potential energy is the sum of a macroscopic term (the finite-range droplet model, FRDM) and a microscopic term representing the shell-plus-pairing correction (calculated from the folded-Yukawa single-particle potential). Ground-state masses were calculated with the FRDM (1992) version, which includes a Lipkin-Nogami pairing model with improved effective-interaction pairing gap, minimization with respect to higher-multipole shape distortions, and Coulomb redistribution effects. Fission potential-energy surfaces were calculated using a shape parameterization that allows for both compact spherical fragments and elongated fragments, with spontaneous-fission half-lives obtained from integrals of inertia functions along fission paths.

## Key numbers

- The FRDM (1992) mass calculation shows a discrepancy of 0.448 MeV between measured and calculated masses for nuclei above N=65.
- When model constants were adjusted only to nuclei with Z,N >= 28 and A <= 208 (1110 known masses), the error for all known nuclei was 0.745 MeV, compared to 0.669 MeV with the standard model adjusted to 1654 nuclei.
- The mass excess of $^{272}$110 is 133.82 MeV in the standard model and 133.65 MeV in the restricted adjustment; for $^{288}$110 it is 165.68 MeV and 166.79 MeV, respectively.
- The microscopic correction minimum in the superheavy region is at Z=115, N=179 with an energy of -9.44 MeV; at Z=114, N=179 the energy is almost the same.
- The calculated $\alpha$-decay half-life of $^{272}$110 is about 70 ms; $^{288}$110 has 4 y; $^{290}$110 has 1565 y.
- A "ballpark" spontaneous-fission half-life for nuclei near $^{272}$110 is 1 ms, with possible increases of up to 10 orders of magnitude for odd systems.
- For $^{258}$Fm, the kinetic-energy distribution is represented by two Gaussians centered at about 200 and 235 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic approach developed in the 1981 nuclear mass model by Möller and Nix, which used the folded-Yukawa single-particle potential from 1972 and the Yukawa-plus-exponential finite-range model. The new FRDM (1992) incorporates Coulomb redistribution effects and improved pairing models, showing much improved agreement with measured $\alpha$-decay Q values compared to the 1988 FRLDM comparison by Münzenberg et al. (1989). The calculations predict the superheavy island is centered around $^{288}$110 and $^{290}$110, with $\alpha$-decay half-lives that rule out natural occurrence of superheavy elements, and propose a new mechanism for the short spontaneous-fission half-life of $^{258}$Fm based on low inertia in a new fission valley rather than barrier disappearance.
