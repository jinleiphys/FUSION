# Earth Regeneration of Solar Neutrinos at SNO and Super-Kamiokande

> We analyze the 1258-day Super-Kamiokande day and night solar neutrino energy spectra with various $χ^2$ definitions. The best-fit lies in the LMA region at $(Δm^2, tan^2 θ)=(5.01\times 10^{-5} eV^2, 0.60)$, independently of whether systematic errors are included in the $χ^2$-definition. We compare the exclusion and allowed regions from the different definitions and choose the most suitable definition to predict the regions from SNO at the end of three years of data accumulation. We first work under the assumption that Super-Kamiokande sees a flux-suppressed flat energy spectrum. Then, we consider the possibility of each one of the three MSW regions being the solution to the solar neutrino problem. We find that the exclusion and allowed regions for the flat spectrum hypothesis and the LMA and LOW solutions are alike. In three years, we expect SNO to find very similar regions to that obtained by Super-Kamiokande. We evaluate whether the zenith angle distribution at SNO with optimum binning will add anything to the analysis of the day and night spectra; for comparison, we show the results of our analysis of the 1258-day zenith angle distribution from Super-Kamiokande, for which the best-fit parameters are $(Δm^2, tan^2 θ)=(5.01\times 10^{-5} eV^2, 0.56)$.

# Earth Regeneration of Solar Neutrinos at SNO and Super-Kamiokande

## Key claim

The best-fit oscillation parameters from the 1258-day Super-Kamiokande day and night energy spectra lie in the LMA region at (Δm², tan²θ) = (5.01 × 10⁻⁵ eV², 0.60), independently of whether systematic errors are included in the χ²-definition; however, the exclusion and allowed regions for the flat spectrum hypothesis and the LMA and LOW solutions are alike, making them difficult to distinguish even with three years of SNO data.

## Method

The authors analyzed the 1258-day Super-Kamiokande day and night solar neutrino energy spectra using three different χ² definitions (including one with no systematic errors) and compared the resulting exclusion and allowed regions. They then simulated SNO charged-current event rates for three years of data accumulation, generating Monte Carlo data for the best-fit oscillation parameters in each of the three MSW solutions (LMA, SMA, LOW) and for a flat flux-suppressed spectrum, and computed expected exclusion and allowed regions. Finally, they evaluated whether the zenith angle distribution at SNO with optimized binning (one day bin and five non-uniform night bins) adds information beyond the day and night spectra.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit oscillation parameters from SK 1258-day day/night spectra: (Δm², tan²θ) = (5.01 × 10⁻⁵ eV², 0.60) using χ² definitions with and without systematic errors
- Best-fit parameters from SK 1258-day zenith angle distribution: (Δm², tan²θ) = (5.01 × 10⁻⁵ eV², 0.56)
- Measured SK flux suppression relative to SSM: Data_SK/SSM = 0.459⁺⁰·⁰¹⁸₋₀·₀₁₆
- SNO expected event rate without oscillations: approximately 9250 events per year

- ⁸B flux in BPB2000: 5.05 × 10⁶ (1⁺⁰·²⁰₋₀·₁₆) cm⁻² s⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Super-Kamiokande solar neutrino data (SK collaboration, 2000) by comparing different χ² definitions and projecting the capabilities of the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO). The authors build on prior global oscillation analyses (referred to as "analysis" and "sol" in the text) that identified three robust MSW solutions (LMA, SMA, LOW) to the solar neutrino problem, and on the CHOOZ limit (1999) for θ₁₃. The study enables predictions for what SNO can achieve with three years of charged-current data alone, finding that the LMA and LOW solutions will remain difficult to distinguish, while the SMA solution can be largely excluded.
