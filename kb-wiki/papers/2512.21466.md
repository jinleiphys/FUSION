# Probing gluon saturation with forward di-hadron correlations in proton-nucleus collisions

> We present a detailed numerical investigation of semi-inclusive forward di-hadron production in proton-nucleus collisions employing the Color Glass Condensate effective theory. We focus on the regime where di-hadrons are produced nearly back-to-back in the transverse plane, thereby justifying a transverse-momentum-dependent factorization approach in terms of small-$x$ gluon distributions. Our computation integrates several key elements: i) non-linear rapidity evolution via the Balitsky-Kovchegov equation with running coupling, ii) both perturbative and non-perturbative Sudakov resummation, and iii) a phenomenologically constrained model for the initial conditions for small-$x$ gluon distributions. We compare this phenomenological framework to experimental data from the STAR Collaboration on azimuthal correlations in forward di-pion production in both proton-proton and proton-gold collisions. We analyze the systematic theoretical uncertainties associated with the saturation scales of nuclei at the initial scale for rapidity evolution and with those associated with the hadronization process. Finally, we make predictions for the kinematics anticipated to be covered by the ALICE Forward Calorimeter (FoCal) upgrade at the Large Hadron Collider.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that including both perturbative and non-perturbative Sudakov resummation alongside small-$x$ evolution via the running-coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov equation can resolve the long-standing puzzle of missing nuclear broadening in forward di-hadron azimuthal correlations, while still capturing saturation-induced suppression of the away-side peak.

## Method
The authors compute the coincidence probability for forward di-pion production in proton-proton and proton-gold collisions using a transverse-momentum-dependent (TMD) factorization approach within the Color Glass Condensate effective theory. They evolve the dipole correlator via the running-coupling Balitsky-Kovchegov equation from initial conditions fitted to HERA data, relate all small-$x$ gluon TMDs to the dipole in a Gaussian large-$N_c$ approximation, and include both perturbative and non-perturbative Sudakov resummation with off-shell hard factors from the improved TMD formalism. The nuclear saturation scale is scaled from the proton value using a minimum-bias nuclear dipole model, and the non-perturbative Sudakov factor is tuned to reproduce the width in proton-proton collisions.

## Key numbers
- Initial proton saturation momentum at $x_0 = 0.01$: $Q^2_{s0,p} = 0.168 \, {\rm GeV}^2$, with anomalous dimension $\gamma = 1.119$ and IR cutoff $\Lambda_{\rm IR} = 0.241 \, {\rm GeV}$.
- Nuclear saturation scales at $x_0 = 0.01$: $Q_{s0, {\rm Au}}^2 = 2.67 \, Q^2_{s0,p}$ for gold and $Q_{s0, {\rm Pb}}^2 = 2.75 \, Q^2_{s0,p}$ for lead.
- Non-perturbative Sudakov parameters: $g_1 = 0.212$, $g_2 = 0.84$, $Q_0^2 = 2.4 \, {\rm GeV}^2$, with multiplicative factor $C_{NP} = 2.3$ tuned to proton-proton data.
- Matching scale for TMDs: $x_c = x_0 e^{-1} = 0.0037$; for $x > x_c$ a matching prescription $\Fcal^{(i)}_{\alpha}(x,\kt) = \left(\frac{1-x}{1-x_c} \right)^4 \Fcal^{(i)}_{\alpha}(x_c,\kt)$ is applied.
- The nuclear initial saturation scale is varied by approximately 20% to estimate systematic uncertainties from impact-parameter modeling.

## Context
This work extends the small-$x$ TMD framework of Dominguez et al. (2011) and Marquet et al. (2016) by incorporating Sudakov resummation alongside running-coupling BK evolution, addressing the discrepancy between earlier leading-order CGC calculations (Albacete et al., 2018) and STAR data (2021) which showed similar correlation widths in proton-proton and proton-gold collisions despite predicted nuclear broadening. The approach builds on the joint resummation formalism of Mueller and Xiao (2012, 2013) and the improved TMD framework of Kotko et al. (2015). The results enable quantitative predictions for the ALICE FoCal upgrade at the LHC and provide a framework for interpreting future measurements at RHIC, the LHC, and the Electron-Ion Collider.
