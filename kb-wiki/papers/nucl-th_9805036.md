# Unstable particles in matter at a finite temperature: the rho and omega mesons

> Unstable particles (such as the vector mesons) have an important role to play in low mass dilepton production resulting from heavy ion collisions and this has been a subject of several investigations. Yet subtleties, such as the implications of the generalization of the Breit-Wigner formula for nonzero temperature and density, e.g. the question of collisional broadening, the role of Bose enhancement, etc., the possibility of the kinematic opening (or closing) of decay channels due to environmental effects, the problem of double counting through resonant and direct contributions, are often given insufficient emphasis. The present study attempts to point out these features using the rho and omega mesons as illustrative examples. The difference between the two versions of the Vector Meson Dominance Model in the present context is also presented. Effects of non-zero temperature and density, through vector meson masses and decay widths, on dilepton spectra are studied, for concreteness within the framework of a Walecka-type model, though most of the basic issues highlighted apply to other scenarios as well.

## Key claim

The study demonstrates that the commonly held notion that the omega meson is too long-lived to convey information on the fireball in heavy ion collisions is contradicted under certain conditions; at a temperature of 200 MeV and normal nuclear density, the omega lifetime reduces to approximately 2.3 fm/c, comparable to that of the rho meson (approximately 2.1 fm/c), due to the kinematic opening of the decay channel omega to rho pi which is closed in free space.

## Method

The authors employed a Walecka-type mean field model to compute in-medium modifications of rho and omega meson masses and decay widths at finite temperature and baryon density, using the generalized Breit-Wigner formula for unstable particles in a thermal bath as formulated by Weldon. The dilepton production rates from rho decay and pion annihilation were calculated using both versions of the Vector Meson Dominance Model (VMD1 and VMD2), incorporating Bose enhancement factors and the distinction between elastic and inelastic contributions to the total width. The in-medium self energies were computed from nucleon loops with modified nucleon mass, including both particle-hole and Dirac sea contributions in the Relativistic Hartree Approximation.

## Key numbers

- The in-medium rho meson mass reduces to approximately 430 MeV at T = 200 MeV and normal nuclear matter density (n_B^0)
- The in-medium decay rate of the omega meson (including omega to rho pi, omega to 3pi, and omega pi to pi pi processes) reaches approximately 86 MeV at T = 200 MeV and normal nuclear density
- The omega meson lifetime at T = 200 MeV and normal nuclear density is approximately 2.3 fm/c, compared to the rho meson lifetime of approximately 2.1 fm/c under the same conditions
- The two-body decay channel omega to rho pi opens at a temperature of approximately 190 MeV at zero baryon density, while at normal nuclear density this channel remains open even at zero temperature
- The coupling constants used in the Walecka model are: g_omegaNN approximately 10, g_rhoNN approximately 2.6, kappa_rho approximately 6.1, kappa_omega = 0, and g_sigma approximately 7.4, with sigma mass m_sigma = 450 MeV

## Context

This work extends the generalized Breit-Wigner formalism of Weldon (1990) for unstable particles at nonzero temperature and density, applying it specifically to rho and omega mesons in hot and dense hadronic matter relevant to heavy ion collisions. The study contrasts with earlier work by Haglin (1995) by arguing that elastic scattering processes do not contribute to the total width Gamma_tot (the rate of approach to chemical equilibrium), and it demonstrates that the kinematic opening of the omega to rho pi decay channel contradicts the assumption by Heinz (1991) that the omega is too long-lived to probe the fireball. The results enable more accurate modeling of low-mass dilepton spectra from heavy ion collisions, particularly regarding the role of Bose enhancement, collisional broadening, and the choice between VMD1 and VMD2 formulations of vector meson dominance.
