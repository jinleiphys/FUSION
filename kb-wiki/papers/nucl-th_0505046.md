# Understanding low energy reaction with exotic nuclei

> Recent developments on the understanding of low energy reactions are highlighted. Emphasis is given to the CDCC framework where the breakup channels of the projectile are included explicitly. Properties of the breakup couplings are presented. Comments are given with regard to the separation between the nuclear and the Coulomb contributions to breakup cross sections as well as the dependence on the optical potentials. A discussion on the sensitivity of the CDCC basis is discussed, by comparing pure breakup results with transfer to the continuum calculations. Finally, some remaining controversies show the need to go beyond the single particle picture for the projectile.

## Key claim

The Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method is the best working alternative to exact Integral Faddeev Equations for low-energy reactions with loosely bound two-body projectiles, but the paper shows that the standard CDCC breakup approach (using projectile-Jacobi coordinates) is not universally valid; for some systems, a transfer-to-the-continuum representation in a different Jacobi set is more appropriate, and the choice depends on the average relative energy and angular momentum of the subsystems.

## Method

The authors performed CDCC calculations for the breakup of \(^8\)B into \(^7\)Be+p on a \(^{58}\)Ni target at 25.8 MeV, and for the breakup of \(^7\)Be on Pb and C targets. They compared standard CDCC breakup (BU) calculations with transfer-to-the-continuum (TR*) calculations for the \(^8\)B case, and tested the sensitivity of breakup cross sections to the choice of optical potentials for the fragment-target interactions. They also examined the properties of continuum-continuum couplings as a function of initial and final bin energies for s-wave and d-wave transitions.

## Key numbers

* For the \(^8\)B + \(^{58}\)Ni system at 25.8 MeV, the standard CDCC breakup calculation produces a pronounced Coulomb peak around 10-20 degrees in the \(^7\)Be angular distribution, which the transfer-to-the-continuum approach does not reproduce well.
* In tests of continuum coupling truncation for the \(^8\)B on Ni and \(^7\)Be on Pb experiments, up to 30% time gain was found when only the lower l=0,1,2 projectile partial waves were included.
* For the \(^8\)B breakup, the Notre Dame data and the NSCL/MSU data show a 60% inconsistency in the quadrupole excitation strength when assuming a single-particle \(^7\)Be(inert)+p description of the projectile.

## Context

This work extends the CDCC framework developed by Austern and collaborators, and builds on earlier studies of breakup reactions for dripline nuclei such as \(^8\)B (Nunes 1999) and \(^7\)Be (Summers 2004). The paper highlights that simple angular selection of "Coulomb dissociation" is insufficient to guarantee nuclear-free data, contradicting assumptions used in earlier astrophysical extractions (Davids 1998). The results motivate the need to go beyond the inert-core, single-particle picture of the projectile, particularly for cases like \(^{11}\)Be(p,p') inelastic scattering (Lapoux, unpublished GANIL data) and \(^{16}\)C knockout (Maddalena 2000), where core excitation in the continuum must be treated dynamically.
