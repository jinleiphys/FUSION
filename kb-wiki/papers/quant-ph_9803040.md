# Flow equations for band--matrices

> Continuous unitary transformations can be used to diagonalize or approximately diagonalize a given Hamiltonian. In the last four years, this method has been applied to a variety of models of condensed matter physics and field theory. With a new generator for the continuous unitary transformation proposed in this paper one can avoid some of the problems of former applications. General properties of the new generator are derived. It turns out that the new generator is especially useful for Hamiltonians with a banded structure. Two examples, the Lipkin model, and the spin--boson model are discussed in detail.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new generator for continuous unitary transformations (flow equations) that preserves the band-diagonal structure of a Hamiltonian during the flow, unlike Wegner's original generator which causes the Hamiltonian to lose its initially simple structure. The new generator is claimed to be especially useful for Hamiltonians with a banded matrix representation, but the author explicitly notes it does not provide a renormalization scheme and cannot treat Hamiltonians with continuous spectra.

## Method

The author introduces a generator defined by η_nm = sign(n-m) h_nm (with η_nn = 0) for a real symmetric matrix H = (h_nm). The flow equations dH/dℓ = [η, H] are derived, and it is shown analytically that the band-diagonal structure (h_nm = 0 for |n-m| > M) is preserved during the flow. The method is then applied to two models: the Lipkin model (finite Hilbert space, two tridiagonal matrices) and the spin-boson model (infinite Hilbert space, two tridiagonal matrices), where the flow equations reduce to coupled differential equations for the diagonal elements ε_n and off-diagonal elements δ_n.

## Key numbers

- Lipkin model: For large particle number J, the gap between ground state and first excited state is √(ξ₀² − 16 V₀² J²), valid for 4 J V₀ < ξ₀.
- Lipkin model: The approximate spectrum is ε_{n1,2} = √(4ξ₀² − 64 V₀² J²) (n + 1/2 ± 1/4) − (J + 1/2) ξ₀.
- Spin-boson model: For large n, the asymptotic eigenvalues are ε_n(ℓ=∞) = n ω − λ²/(4ω) ∓ (−1)ⁿ (Δ/2) J₀( (2λ/ω) √n ), where J₀ is the Bessel function of the first kind.
- Spin-boson model: The asymptotic expression for large n simplifies to ε_n(ℓ=∞) = n ω − λ²/(4ω) ∓ (−1)ⁿ (Δ/(2 n^{1/4})) √(ω/(πλ)) cos( (2λ/ω) √n − π/4 ).
- Spin-boson model: The condition for validity of the asymptotic expression is λ/(ω√n) ≪ 1 and (Δ/(2ω)) √(λ/(πω)) (1/n^{3/4}) < 1.
- Spin-boson model: For λ/ω = 4.0, the relative error of the asymptotic formula is up to 2% for n=10, and less than 0.1% when the condition (Δ/(2ω)) √(λ/(πω)) (1/n^{3/4}) < 0.1 and n ≳ λ²/ω².

## Context

This work extends Wegner's flow equation method (Wegner, 1994) by introducing a generator that preserves band-diagonal structure, addressing a limitation noted by Richter (1997) for the spin-boson model. The Lipkin model results reproduce those of Pirner and Friman (1998) and standard RPA, while the spin-boson asymptotic formula extends a first-order perturbative result by Graham et al. (1984) to a much wider range of validity. The new flow equations are complementary to the original approach: they cannot treat problems with continuous spectra or multiple energy scales (such as dissipative quantum systems treated by Kehrein and Mielke, 1997), but are suitable for single- or few-particle systems with banded Hamiltonians, potentially useful in nuclear or atomic physics.
