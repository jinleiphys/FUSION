# Using inverse scattering methods to study inter-nucleus potentials

> It is now straightforward to carry out S-matrix to potential inversion over a very wide range of energies and for a wide range of projectile-target combinations. Inversion is possible in many cases involving spin. IP inversion also permits direct scattering data-to-potential inversion and furnishes powerful tools for the phenomenological analysis of nuclear scattering. The resulting single particle potentials exhibit various generic properties which challenge fundamental reaction theories as well as yield information on densities, provide input for reaction calculations. S-matrix to potential inversion is also a powerful tool for directly investigating theoretical processes which contribute to inter-nuclear potentials. Various studies have given insight into contributions to the dynamic polarisation potential (DPP) due to breakup processes and due to collective and reaction channel coupling and have also illuminated the role played by exchange processes in leading to non-locality and parity dependence of the potentials. A case study involving d + He-4 is a model for ways in which inversion applied jointly to theory and experiment might illuminate the scattering of exotic nuclei.

# Using inverse scattering methods to study inter-nucleus potentials

## Key claim

The iterative perturbative (IP) inversion method can extract local inter-nucleus potentials from both theoretical S-matrices and experimental scattering data, but the resulting potentials are subject to profound ambiguities even when fits to data are essentially perfect, requiring judicious reinstatement of physical prejudices such as continuity with energy.

## Method

The IP method iteratively corrects a potential by adding basis functions, with amplitudes derived from linear equations based on the response of the elastic scattering S-matrix to small potential changes via the Born approximation integral. Inversion can be applied to fixed-energy S-matrices, energy-dependent S-matrices, or directly to experimental observables (cross sections and analyzing powers) by minimizing chi-squared at each iteration. For the d + ⁴He case study, the authors performed direct data-to-potential inversion fitting 539 data points across 19 energies between 2.935 and 11.475 MeV, using a starting potential derived from multi-configuration resonating group model (MCRGM) calculations at 29.4 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Volume integral per nucleon pair of the real Wigner central potential from MCRGM at 29.4 MeV: J_R = 499.72 MeV fm³, with rms radius 2.905 fm
- Volume integral per nucleon pair for the SG empirical potential: J_R = 567.8 MeV fm³, rms radius 3.01 fm
- Volume integral per nucleon pair for the J empirical potential: J_R = 556.5 MeV fm³, rms radius 2.86 fm
- Chi-squared per degree of freedom for SG potential fit: χ²/F = 5.84, with bound state energy E_bound = -1.611 MeV
- Chi-squared per degree of freedom for J potential fit: χ²/F = 5.95, with bound state energy E_bound = -1.8 MeV
- Experimental deuteron-⁴He bound state energy: -1.472 MeV
- The J potential fitted data at 5 deuteron laboratory energies between 6.24 and 10 MeV; the SG potential fitted data at 19 energies between 2.935 and 11.475 MeV

## Context

This work extends the IP inversion method developed by Mackintosh, Cooper, and collaborators (early 1990s) to directly fit experimental observables, building on earlier S-matrix-to-potential inversion techniques. The d + ⁴He case study compares MCRGM calculations by Kanada et al. (KKST) with experimental data compiled by Kuznetsova and Kukulin, showing that the RGM model gives a much better description of scattering than its mediocre fits to data would suggest. The method enables extraction of dynamic polarization potentials from coupled-channels calculations and provides a tool for studying exotic nuclei, where the authors note that their earlier work on ¹¹Li scattering from ¹²C and ²⁸Si revealed a long tail in the real potential that cannot be explained by current theories of ¹¹Li.
