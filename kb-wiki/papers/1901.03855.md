# Revisit to electrical and thermal conductivities, Lorenz number and Knudsen number in thermal QCD in a strong magnetic field

> We have explored how the electrical ($σ_{\rm el}$) and thermal ($κ$) conductivities in a thermal QCD medium get affected in weak-momentum anisotropies arising either due to a strong magnetic field or due to asymptotic expansion. This study facilitates to understand the longevity of strong magnetic field through $σ_{el}$, Lorenz number in Wiedemann-Franz law, and the validity of equilibrium by the Knudsen number. We calculate the conductivities by solving relativistic Boltzmann transport equation in relaxation-time approximation within quasiparticle model at finite T and strong B. We have found that $σ_{el}$ and $κ$ get enhanced in a magnetic field-driven anisotropy, but $σ_{el}$ decreases with temperature, opposite to its faster increase in expansion-driven anisotropy. Whereas $κ$ increases slowly with temperature, contrary to its rapid increase in expansion-driven anisotropy. The above findings are broadly attributed to three factors: the stretching and squeezing of distribution function in anisotropies generated by the magnetic field and asymptotic expansion, respectively, the dispersion relation and resulting phase-space factor, the relaxation-time in absence and presence of strong magnetic field. So $σ_{\rm el}$ extracts the time-dependence of magnetic field, which decays slower than in vacuum but expansion-driven anisotropy makes the decay faster. The variation in $κ$ transpires that Knudsen number decreases with T but expansion-driven anisotropy reduces its value and magnetic field-driven anisotropy raises its value but to less than one, thus the system can still be in equilibrium. The ratio, $κ/σ_{el}$ in magnetic field-driven anisotropy increases linearly with temperature but with a value smaller than in expansion-driven anisotropy. Thus the Lorenz number can make the distinction between different anisotropies.

## Key claim

The paper claims that electrical and thermal conductivities in a thermal QCD medium are enhanced by weak-momentum anisotropy arising from a strong magnetic field, but the electrical conductivity decreases with temperature in this case, opposite to its behavior in expansion-driven anisotropy; however, these results are obtained under the specific condition of weak anisotropy (ξ < 1) and within the quasiparticle model with relaxation-time approximation, and the strong magnetic field limit requires eB ≫ T².

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation in the relaxation-time approximation for a thermal QCD medium of quarks, antiquarks, and gluons, using a quasiparticle model where interactions are incorporated through temperature- and magnetic-field-dependent dispersion relations. They computed electrical and thermal conductivities for three cases: an isotropic medium, an expansion-driven anisotropic medium (with positive anisotropy parameter ξ), and a strong magnetic field-driven anisotropic medium (with negative ξ, using only the lowest Landau level). The conductivities were then used to evaluate the Lorenz number (κ/(σ_el T)) and the Knudsen number (Ω = λ/L) to assess the validity of local equilibrium and distinguish between anisotropy origins.

## Key numbers

- Electrical conductivity σ_el in the magnetic field-driven anisotropic medium is enhanced compared to the isotropic case, but decreases monotonically with temperature; in the expansion-driven anisotropic medium, σ_el increases with temperature.
- Thermal conductivity κ in the magnetic field-driven anisotropic medium increases very slowly with temperature, contrary to its rapid increase in the expansion-driven anisotropic medium.
- The Knudsen number Ω decreases with temperature; expansion-driven anisotropy reduces its magnitude, while strong magnetic field-driven anisotropy raises its value but keeps it less than one, indicating the system can still be in local equilibrium.
- The ratio κ/σ_el in the magnetic field-driven anisotropic medium increases linearly with temperature, with a magnitude smaller than in the expansion-driven anisotropic medium, yielding distinct Lorenz numbers.
- Magnetic field decay in the isotropic conducting medium is slower than in vacuum; for Au+Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 4 fm and Lorentz factor γ = 100, the magnetic field in the medium persists longer than in vacuum.
- For the expansion-driven anisotropic medium with ξ = 0.6, the lifetime of a nearly stable magnetic field is slightly less than in the isotropic thermal medium.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of electrical conductivity in quark matter under magnetic fields, including lattice QCD calculations by Buividovich (2010), the dilute instanton-liquid model by Nam (2012), and the effective fugacity approach by Kurian (2017), as well as earlier work by one of the authors on electrical conductivity in expansion-driven anisotropy (Srivastava, 2015). It also builds on studies of thermal conductivity and the Wiedemann-Franz law in QGP by Mitra (2017) and Harutyunyan (2017). The results enable distinguishing between anisotropies of different origins (magnetic field-driven vs. expansion-driven) through transport coefficients and their ratios, and provide input for phenomenological applications such as the lifetime of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions and the validity of hydrodynamic descriptions via the Knudsen number.
