# Quark deconfinement in neutron star cores: The effects of spin-down

> We study the role of spin-down in driving quark deconfinement in the high density core of isolated neutron stars. Assuming spin-down to be solely due to magnetic braking, we obtain typical timescales to quark deconfinement for neutron stars that are born with Keplerian frequencies. Employing different equations of state (EOS), we determine the minimum and maximum neutron star masses that will allow for deconfinement via spin-down only. We find that the time to reach deconfinement is strongly dependent on the magnetic field and that this time is least for EOS that support the largest minimum mass at zero spin, unless rotational effects on stellar structure are large. For a fiducial critical density of $5ρ_0$ for the transition to the quark phase ($ρ_0=2.5\times10^{14}$g/cm$^3$ is the saturation density of nuclear matter), we find that neutron stars lighter than $1.5M_{\odot}$ cannot reach a deconfined phase. Depending on the EOS, neutron stars of more than $1.5M_{\odot}$ can enter a quark phase only if they are spinning faster than about 3 milliseconds as observed now, whereas larger spin periods imply that they are either already quark stars or will never become one.

## Key claim
For a fiducial critical density of 5ρ₀ (where ρ₀ = 2.5×10¹⁴ g/cm³ is the saturation density of nuclear matter), neutron stars lighter than 1.5 M⊙ cannot reach a deconfined quark phase via spin-down alone; stars heavier than 1.5 M⊙ can enter a quark phase only if they are currently spinning faster than about 3 milliseconds, while larger spin periods imply they are either already quark stars or will never become one.

## Method
The authors used the RNS code to construct sequences of rotating neutron star models with constant baryon number for four different equations of state (BBB2, C, APR, and OBJ). They assumed spin-down is entirely due to magnetic dipole braking, integrated the spin-down equation to compute the time required for the central density to reach within 1% of the critical deconfinement density (5ρ₀), and determined the minimum and maximum gravitational masses that allow deconfinement via spin-down for each EOS.

## Key numbers
- Critical deconfinement density: ρ_crit = 5ρ₀, with ρ₀ = 2.5×10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Minimum gravitational mass for deconfinement via spin-down: 1.46 M⊙ (BBB2), 1.53 M⊙ (C), 1.78 M⊙ (APR), 2.8 M⊙ (OBJ)
- Maximum gravitational mass at Kepler rotation that can reach critical density: 1.79 M⊙ (BBB2), 1.83 M⊙ (C), 2.16 M⊙ (APR), 3.36 M⊙ (OBJ)
- Minimum central density at Kepler rotation to reach deconfinement: 3.92 ρ₀ (BBB2), 3.88 ρ₀ (C), 4.12 ρ₀ (APR), 2.52 ρ₀ (OBJ)
- Time to spin down to within 1% of ρ_crit for minimum mass stars with B=10¹² G: 183 years (BBB2), 170 years (C), 152 years (APR), 663 years (OBJ)
- Time to spin down to within 1% of ρ_crit for minimum mass stars with B=10¹⁵ G: 96 minutes (BBB2), 89 minutes (C), 73 minutes (APR), 348 minutes (OBJ)
- Limiting spin period beyond which central density changes little: P_max ≈ 3 ms for most EOS, about 6 ms for OBJ

## Context
This work extends studies of quark deconfinement in neutron stars (Heiselberg, 1995; Glendenning, 1997) by specifically quantifying the role of spin-down from magnetic braking as the driver of increasing central density. The results enable predictions for which observed isolated neutron stars could potentially undergo a Quark Nova transition (Ouyed, 2002; Ouyed, 2005) and constrain the deconfinement density to be no more than about 5ρ₀ for stars in the currently observed mass range (Stairs, 2004). The four equations of state employed span a range of stiffness, from the softer BBB2 (Baldo, 1997) to the very stiff OBJ (Oppenheimer, 1975).
