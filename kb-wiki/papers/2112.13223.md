# Analysis of identified particle transverse momentum spectra produced in pp, p--Pb and Pb--Pb collisions at the LHC using Tsallis--Pareto-type function

> In the framework of a multi-source thermal model at the partonic-level, we have analyzed transverse momentum spectra of hadrons measured by the ALICE Collaboration in proton-proton ($pp$ or $p$-$p$) collisions at the center-of-mass energy of $\sqrt{s}=7$ and 13 TeV, proton-lead ($p$-Pb) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=5.02$ TeV, and lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.76$ TeV. For meson(baryon), the contributions of two(three) constituent quarks are considered, in which each quark contributes to hadron transverse momentum to obey the revised phenomenological Tsallis transverse momentum distribution for the Maxwell-Boltzmann particles (the TP-like function in short) with isotropic random azimuthal angle. Three main parameters, namely, the revised index $a_0$, effective temperature $T$, and entropy-related index $n$ are obtained, which show the same tendency for small and large systems with respect to the centrality (or multiplicity) of events, rest mass of hadrons, and constituent mass of quarks.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the transverse momentum spectra of identified hadrons in pp, p-Pb, and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies can be described by a multi-source thermal model at the partonic level, where each constituent quark contributes to hadron transverse momentum according to a revised Tsallis-Pareto-type (TP-like) distribution with isotropic random azimuthal angles, and the three extracted parameters (revised index a0, effective temperature T, and entropy-related index n) show the same tendency for both small and large collision systems with respect to centrality, hadron rest mass, and constituent quark mass.

## Method

The authors used a Monte Carlo method to simulate the transverse momentum of hadrons by combining the transverse momentum vectors of their constituent quarks (two for mesons, three for baryons) with isotropic random azimuthal angles. Each quark's transverse momentum was sampled from a revised Tsallis-Pareto-type distribution (TP-like function) with parameters a0, T, and n, using constituent quark masses rather than current quark masses. The model was fitted to ALICE Collaboration data for pions, kaons, protons, K0S, K*, Lambda, Xi, and Omega particles across multiple centrality/multiplicity classes in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 and 13 TeV, p-Pb collisions at sqrt(sNN)=5.02 TeV, and Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(sNN)=2.76 TeV.

## Key numbers

- For pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV, pions (pi+ + pi-) in multiplicity class I (0-0.95%): a0 = -0.550 +/- 0.004, T = 0.345 +/- 0.002 GeV, n = 5.071 +/- 0.054
- For pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV, protons (p + anti-p) in multiplicity class I: a0 = 0.530 +/- 0.008, T = 0.214 +/- 0.002 GeV, n = 6.645 +/- 0.035
- For pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV, Omega- + anti-Omega+ in multiplicity classes I+II: a0 = 0.830 +/- 0.004, T = 0.312 +/- 0.002 GeV, n = 9.597 +/- 0.031
- For pp collisions at sqrt(s)=13 TeV, pions in multiplicity class I (0-0.92%): a0 = -0.550 +/- 0.003, T = 0.375 +/- 0.002 GeV, n = 4.996 +/- 0.045
- For pp collisions at sqrt(s)=13 TeV, Xi- + anti-Xi+ in multiplicity class I: a0 = 0.255 +/- 0.008, T = 0.431 +/- 0.002 GeV, n = 10.059 +/- 0.048
- The effective temperature T decreases systematically from the most central to the most peripheral multiplicity classes for all particle species in all collision systems

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Yang, Duan, Liu, Sahoo, 2020-2021) where they used the convolution of two or three revised Tsallis-Pareto-type distributions assuming parallel or vertical quark azimuths. The current work improves upon this by using Monte Carlo methods to allow isotropic random azimuthal angles between constituent quarks, which is more physically realistic. The analysis covers a wider range of identified particles (including strange and multi-strange hadrons) across small (pp, p-Pb) and large (Pb-Pb) collision systems, enabling systematic comparison of the extracted parameters. The results enable future studies of hadronization mechanisms and the transition between small and large collision systems at the LHC.
