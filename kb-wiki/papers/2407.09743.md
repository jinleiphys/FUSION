# Simultaneous Extraction of the Weak Radius and the Weak Mixing Angle from Parity-Violating Electron Scattering on $^{12}\mathrm{C}$

> We study the impact of nuclear structure uncertainties on a measurement of the weak charge of $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ at the future MESA facility in Mainz. Information from a large variety of nuclear models, accurately calibrated to the ground-state properties of selected nuclei, suggest that a $0.3$% precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry at forward angles will not be compromised by nuclear structure effects, thereby allowing a world-leading determination of the weak charge of $^{12}\mathrm{C}$. Furthermore, we show that a combination of measurements of the parity-violating asymmetry at forward and backward angles for the same electron beam energy can be used to extract information on the nuclear weak charge distribution. We conclude that a $0.34$% precision on the weak radius of $^{12}\mathrm{C}$ may be achieved by performing a $3$% precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry at backward angles.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a 0.3% precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry at forward angles on carbon-12 at the future MESA facility will not be significantly contaminated by nuclear structure uncertainties, provided the strong correlation between the weak radius and the charge radius is exploited; this allows a world-leading determination of the weak charge of carbon-12 without requiring an additional measurement at a larger momentum transfer as previously thought necessary by Koshchii (2020).

## Method

The authors computed ground-state proton and neutron densities for carbon-12 using a large set of 18 non-relativistic Skyrme and 3 covariant energy density functionals, all calibrated to binding energies and charge radii of magic and semi-magic nuclei not including carbon-12. They folded these point-nucleon densities with single-nucleon electromagnetic and weak form factors to obtain charge and weak-charge densities, then performed distorted wave Born approximation calculations using the DREPHA code to predict the parity-violating asymmetry at forward (29 degrees) and backward (145 degrees) angles for a fixed electron beam energy of 155 MeV. A linear fit to the model predictions, assigning a conservative 1% uncertainty to each model, established the correlation between weak radius and charge radius.

## Key numbers

- The weak charge of carbon-12 in the Standard Model including radiative corrections is Q_W(SM) = -5.499.
- The experimental charge radius of carbon-12 from electron scattering (sum-of-Gaussians parametrization) is R_ch = 2.469(5) fm.
- The extracted point-proton radius from the charge radius is R_p = 2.389(5) fm.
- The linear fit yields R_wk = 0.9942(15) x R_ch, corresponding to a weak skin of approximately -0.015 fm.
- A 0.3% precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry at forward angles is sufficient for a clean determination of the weak charge.
- A 0.34% precision on the weak radius of carbon-12 may be achieved by performing a 3% precision measurement of the parity-violating asymmetry at backward angles.
- The reference parity-violating asymmetry at forward angles is A_PV_ref_f = 0.517 ppm; at backward angles it is A_PV_ref_b = 7.92 ppm for a weak skin of -0.0143 fm.

## Context

This work extends the earlier feasibility study by Koshchii (2020) for carbon-12, which required hand-picking only five nuclear mean-field models that predicted the charge radius within approximately 1% of experiment and concluded that an additional measurement at larger momentum transfer was necessary. The present study uses a broader set of 21 energy density functionals and exploits the strong correlation between weak and charge radii to eliminate the need for model selection, enabling a simultaneous extraction of the weak mixing angle and the weak radius. This approach is complementary to coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering experiments and to the PREX and CREX measurements of neutron skins in lead-208 and calcium-48 at Jefferson Lab, and it will enable a precise low-energy test of the running of the weak mixing angle at the future MESA facility.
