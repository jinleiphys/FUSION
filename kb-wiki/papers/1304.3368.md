# Constraining the neutron-proton effective mass splitting using empirical constraints on the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy around normal density

> According to the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem, nuclear symmetry energy \esym and its slope \lr at an arbitrary density $ρ$ are determined by the nucleon isovector (symmetry) potential \usym and its momentum dependence $\frac{\partial U_{sym}}{\partial k}$. The latter determines uniquely the neutron-proton effective k-mass splitting $m^*_{n-p}(ρ,δ)\equiv (m_{\rm n}^*-m_{\rm p}^*)/m$ in neutron-rich nucleonic matter of isospin asymmetry $δ$. Using currently available constraints on the \es0 and \l0 at normal density $ρ_0$ of nuclear matter from 28 recent analyses of various terrestrial nuclear laboratory experiments and astrophysical observations, we try to infer the corresponding neutron-proton effective k-mass splitting $m^*_{n-p}(ρ_0,δ)$. While the mean values of the $m^*_{n-p}(ρ_0,δ)$ obtained from most of the studies are remarkably consistent with each other and scatter very closely around an empirical value of \emass$=0.27\cdotδ$, it is currently not possible to scientifically state surely that the \emass is positive within the present knowledge of the uncertainties. Quantifying, better understanding and then further reducing the uncertainties using modern statistical and computational techniques in extracting the \es0 and \l0 from analyzing the experimental data are much needed.

# Constraining the neutron-proton effective mass splitting using empirical constraints on the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy around normal density

## Key claim

Using 28 existing constraints on the symmetry energy $E_{sym}(\rho_0)$ and its slope $L(\rho_0)$ at normal nuclear density, the inferred neutron-proton effective k-mass splitting $m^*_{n-p}(\rho_0,\delta)$ clusters around $0.27\cdot\delta$, but the uncertainties are too large to state definitively that the splitting is positive.

## Method

The authors apply the Hugenholtz-Van Hove (HVH) theorem to relate the nuclear symmetry energy $E_{sym}(\rho)$ and its density slope $L(\rho)$ to the isovector (symmetry) potential $U_{sym}(\rho,k)$ and its momentum derivative $\partial U_{sym}/\partial k$. Using 28 published constraints on $E_{sym}(\rho_0)$ and $L(\rho_0)$ from terrestrial experiments and astrophysical observations, they invert the HVH relations to extract the corresponding $U_{sym}(\rho_0,k_F)$ and the neutron-proton effective k-mass splitting $m^*_{n-p}(\rho_0,\delta)$ at normal density, assuming the well-established isoscalar effective mass $m_0^*/m = 0.7 \pm 0.05$.

## Key numbers

- Global average of $E_{sym}(\rho_0)$ from 28 analyses: 31.6 MeV, with a "standard deviation" of 0.92 MeV and average "error bar" size of 2.66 MeV.
- Global average of $L(\rho_0)$ from 28 analyses: 58.9 MeV, with a "standard deviation" of 16.5 MeV and average "error bar" size of 16.0 MeV.
- Global average of the isovector potential $U_{sym}(\rho_0,k_F)$: 28.7 MeV, with a "standard deviation" of 1.82 MeV and average "error bar" size of 7.78 MeV.
- Global average of the neutron-proton effective mass splitting $m^*_{n-p}(\rho_0,\delta)$: $0.27\cdot\delta$, with a "standard deviation" of $0.25\cdot\delta$ and average "error bar" size of $0.35\cdot\delta$.
- Using $E_{sym}(\rho_0)=31$ MeV, $m_0^*/m=0.7$, and Fermi energy $E_F(\rho_0)=36$ MeV, a positive $m^*_{n-p}$ requires $L(\rho_0) \leq 76$ MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Xu, Li, and Chen (2010) and earlier HVH-based formulations (Dabrowski 1973) to systematically compare 28 independent constraints on symmetry energy parameters. The results address the longstanding controversy over the sign and magnitude of the neutron-proton effective mass splitting, which has implications for pairing and superfluidity in nuclei and neutron stars, isospin transport in heavy-ion reactions, and protoneutron star cooling. The authors call for improved statistical and computational techniques to reduce uncertainties in extracting $E_{sym}(\rho_0)$ and $L(\rho_0)$ from experimental data, noting that current uncertainties prevent a definitive statement about the sign of the mass splitting.
