# The Sounds of the QCD Phase Transition

> Hydrodynamic description of a fireball produced in high energy heavy ion collisions has been recently supplemented by a very successful study of acoustic perturbation created by the initial state perturbations. We discuss sound produced at later stages of the collision, as the temperature drops below critical, $T<T_c$, and originated from the Rayleigh-type collapse of the QGP clusters. In certain analytic approximation we study distorted "sound spheres" and calculate modifications of the particle spectra and two-particle correlators induced by them. Unlike for initial state perturbations studied previously, we propose to look for those late-time sounds using rapidity correlations, rather than the azimuthal angles of the particles. We then summarize known data on rapidity correlations from RHIC and LHC, suggesting that the widening of those can be the first signature of the late-time sounds.

# The Sounds of the QCD Phase Transition

## Key claim
The paper proposes that late-time sound waves emitted by collapsing QGP clusters during the QCD phase transition (at temperatures below T_c) can be observed through rapidity correlations of final-state particles, rather than through azimuthal correlations used for initial-state perturbations. The authors suggest that existing data from RHIC and LHC showing widening of rapidity correlations may be the first signature of these late-time sounds, but note that this interpretation depends on the cluster being located near the edge of the fireball for the effect to be observable.

## Method
The authors model the Rayleigh-type collapse of QGP clusters using the Euler hydrodynamic equations with viscosity, solving for the time evolution of the cluster radius R(t) and calculating the resulting sound radiation intensity. They then propagate the sound perturbations on top of an expanding fireball described by the Gubser-Yarom analytic flow solution, using small perturbations in (ρ, θ, φ, η) coordinates with separation of variables. Finally, they compute particle spectra and two-particle correlations via the Cooper-Frye formula on an isothermal freeze-out surface at T_fo = 120 MeV, integrating over the freeze-out cylinder and using a saddle-point approximation for the azimuthal integral.

## Key numbers
- The viscosity-to-entropy ratio η/s = (1.5-2)/4π is cited as consistent with acoustic damping in prior hydrodynamic studies
- The critical temperature is T_c ≈ 170 MeV and the kinetic freeze-out temperature is T_fo ≈ 100-120 MeV
- The time difference between the T_c surface and T_fo surface is about 2 fm/c for emission radii r < 6 fm, growing to 6 fm/c at r ≈ 8 fm
- The sound sphere radius for internal sources is R_s ∼ 1 fm, giving an expected angular size Δφ ≈ R_s/r* ∼ 1/7
- The transverse flow velocity at LHC reaches v_⊥ ∼ 0.8, giving a blue-shift factor of ∼ 1/3 in the Boltzmann exponent
- The charged multiplicity per cluster in AuAu collisions is ⟨N_ch⟩ ∼ 6 charged particles (up to 10 including neutrals), compared to ⟨N_ch⟩ ∼ 3 in pp collisions
- The width of cluster decay in pseudorapidity changes from about 0.8 in pp to about 1.4 at mid-central collisions
- The sound-induced peaks in rapidity are shifted by about ±1/2 unit of rapidity

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies of acoustic perturbations from initial-state fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions (Staig and Shuryak, 2011) and builds on the Gubser-Yarom analytic flow framework (Gubser and Yarom, 2010). It contrasts with the standard approach of studying critical fluctuations through the beam energy scan program at RHIC (Stephanov, 1998) by proposing a new observable: rapidity correlations rather than azimuthal harmonics. The paper also draws an analogy to sonoluminescence experiments (Brenner, 2002) where collapsing bubbles efficiently transfer energy into shocks and sound waves. The proposed interpretation of PHOBOS and ALICE rapidity correlation data as signatures of late-time sounds would enable a new method for studying the QCD phase transition region, though the authors note that distinguishing cluster-decay contributions from sound-induced contributions requires multi-body correlation measurements.
