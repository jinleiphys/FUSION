# Hidden pseudospin and spin symmetries and their origins in atomic nuclei

> Symmetry plays a fundamental role in physics. The quasi-degeneracy between single-particle orbitals $(n, l, j = l + 1/2)$ and $(n-1, l + 2, j = l + 3/2)$ indicates a hidden symmetry in atomic nuclei, the so-called pseudospin symmetry (PSS). Since the introduction of the concept of PSS in atomic nuclei, there have been comprehensive efforts to understand its origin. Both splittings of spin doublets and pseudospin doublets play critical roles in the evolution of magic numbers in exotic nuclei discovered by modern spectroscopic studies with radioactive ion beam facilities. Since the PSS was recognized as a relativistic symmetry in 1990s, many special features, including the spin symmetry (SS) for anti-nucleon, and many new concepts have been introduced. In the present Review, we focus on the recent progress on the PSS and SS in various systems and potentials, including extensions of the PSS study from stable to exotic nuclei, from non-confining to confining potentials, from local to non-local potentials, from central to tensor potentials, from bound to resonant states, from nucleon to anti-nucleon spectra, from nucleon to hyperon spectra, and from spherical to deformed nuclei. Open issues in this field are also discussed in detail, including the perturbative nature, the supersymmetric representation with similarity renormalization group, and the puzzle of intruder states.

## Key claim

This review argues that pseudospin symmetry (PSS) and spin symmetry (SS) are relativistic symmetries of the Dirac Hamiltonian, exactly conserved only under idealized conditions (e.g., Σ(r) = 0 or dΣ(r)/dr = 0) that cannot support bound states, making PSS a dynamical or non-perturbative symmetry that is always broken in realistic nuclei.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework of pseudospin symmetry and spin symmetry within relativistic mean-field (RMF) and relativistic Hartree-Fock (RHF) theories, examining the Dirac equation for nucleons and anti-nucleons. They survey extensions of PSS/SS studies across multiple dimensions: from stable to exotic nuclei, from bound to resonant states, from spherical to deformed nuclei, from nucleon to anti-nucleon and hyperon spectra, and across various potential forms including Woods-Saxon, harmonic oscillator, tensor, and non-local potentials.

## Key numbers

- The traditional magic numbers in stable nuclei are found to be 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, and 82 for both protons and neutrons, plus 126 for neutrons (Haxel 1949, Mayer 1949)
- The N=28 shell closure disappears due to quenching of the spin-orbit splitting for the ν1f spin doublets (Gaudefroy 2006, Bastin 2007)
- The Z=64 subshell closure is closely related to restoration of pseudospin symmetry for the π2p̃ and π1f̃ pseudospin doublets (Nagai 1981, Long 2007)
- Pseudospin symmetry is exactly conserved when Σ(r) ≡ S(r) + V(r) = 0 (Ginocchio 1997) or when dΣ(r)/dr = 0 (Meng 1998)

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Hecht and Adler (1969) and Arima, Harvey, and Shimizu (1969), who first identified the near degeneracy defining pseudospin symmetry, and Ginocchio (1997) who revealed PSS as a relativistic symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian. It also builds on the discovery by Zhou, Meng, and Ring (2003) that spin symmetry is well conserved in anti-nucleon spectra. The review enables future work on shell evolution in exotic nuclei studied at radioactive ion beam facilities, and opens questions about the perturbative nature of PSS, supersymmetric representations with similarity renormalization group methods, and the puzzle of intruder states.
