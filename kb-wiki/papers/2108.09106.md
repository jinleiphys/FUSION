# Energy and pairing dependence of dissipation in real-time fission dynamics

> This work presents a microscopic study of dissipation in fission dynamical evolutions with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS method in terms of energy dependence and pairing dependence. The friction coefficients and dissipation energies are extracted by mapping the symmetric fission process of $^{258}$Fm into a classical equation of motion. Density-constrained calculations are used to obtain the dynamical potential. The obtained friction coefficients have a strong dependence of deformations, and averagely match the coefficients adopted in statistical models. The dissipation indeed increase with increasing initial excitation energies or with decreasing pairings. It is also shown that post-scission dissipations play a significant role. The demonstrated characteristics of dissipations will be valuable for calibrations of various fission models.

## Key claim

The friction coefficients extracted from time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TD-BCS) simulations of symmetric fission of ²⁵⁸Fm increase with increasing initial excitation energy and decrease with increasing pairing strength, but the paper's claim that dissipation "increase with increasing initial excitation energies or with decreasing pairings" is qualified by the finding that post-scission dissipation plays a significant role and that the pairing dependence is less pronounced than the energy dependence.

## Method

The authors performed deformation-constrained Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations to prepare initial configurations of ²⁵⁸Fm at different finite temperatures and pairing strengths, then evolved these configurations using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TD-BCS) method. The dynamical potential was obtained via density-constrained HF-BCS calculations that reproduce the density distributions from TD-BCS at each time step. The friction coefficients and dissipation energies were extracted by mapping the one-dimensional quadrupole deformation dynamics onto a classical equation of motion (Langevin-type equation), with the collective inertia derived non-adiabatically from the dynamical trajectory.

## Key numbers

- Friction coefficients range from 1 to 6 × 10²¹ s⁻¹, deformation-dependent, and averagely match the dissipation coefficients used in Langevin and statistical models (e.g., a friction coefficient of 3 × 10²¹ s⁻¹ reproduces fission probabilities in prior work).
- At temperature T = 1.0 MeV (excitation energy E* = 20.5 MeV), the compound nucleus becomes highly dissipative with γ around 4–6 × 10²¹ s⁻¹.
- For the zero-temperature case (T = 0), the fission evolution time to scission is 804 fm/c, scission deformation Q₂₀ = 334.1 b, TKE = 239.8 MeV, pre-scission dissipated energy = 9.6 MeV, and total dissipated energy = 17.2 MeV.
- At T = 1.0 MeV, the evolution time increases to 1732 fm/c, scission deformation to 374.7 b, TKE decreases to 226.7 MeV, pre-scission dissipated energy = 21.9 MeV, and total dissipated energy = 35.1 MeV.
- With pairing strength at 80% of the nominal value, evolution time = 1236 fm/c, scission deformation = 331.3 b, TKE = 241.0 MeV; with pairing at 110%, evolution time = 762 fm/c, scission deformation = 340 b, TKE = 238.3 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the dissipative dynamics method previously applied to nuclear fusion reactions (DDTDHF1, DDTDHF2, DDTDHF3) to the fission context, using the quadrupole deformation as the collective coordinate rather than the relative distance between nuclei. The results provide microscopic validation for the friction coefficients used in statistical models (e.g., Mccalla, Krappe) and Langevin approaches (e.g., Randrup), and the demonstrated energy and pairing dependence of dissipation will inform calibration of fluctuation strengths in time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) for predictive fission modeling. The study also contrasts with the wall-and-window formula (BLOCKI), which gives very large dissipation parameters requiring reduction factors, and with the linear response theory (Hofman, Yamaji) predicting dissipation increase with temperature, while the two-body dissipation from a collision dominance model (annal) decreases as T⁻².
