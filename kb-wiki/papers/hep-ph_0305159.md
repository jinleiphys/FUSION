# A road map to solar neutrino fluxes, neutrino oscillation parameters, and tests for new physics

> We analyze all available solar and related reactor neutrino experiments, as well as simulated future 7Be, p-p, pep, and ^8B solar neutrino experiments. We treat all solar neutrino fluxes as free parameters subject to the condition that the total luminosity represented by the neutrinos equals the observed solar luminosity (the `luminosity constraint'). Existing experiments show that the p-p solar neutrino flux is 1.02 +- 0.02 (1 sigma) times the flux predicted by the BP00 standard solar model; the 7Be neutrino flux is 0.93^{+0.25}_{-0.63} the predicted flux; and the ^8B flux is 1.01 +- 0.04 the predicted flux. The neutrino oscillation parameters are: Delta m^2 = 7.3^{+0.4}_{-0.6}\times 10^{-5} eV^2 and tan^2 theta_{12} = 0.41 +- 0.04. We evaluate how accurate future experiments must be to determine more precisely neutrino oscillation parameters and solar neutrino fluxes, and to elucidate the transition from vacuum-dominated to matter-dominated oscillations at low energies. A future 7Be nu-e scattering experiment accurate to +- 10 % can reduce the uncertainty in the experimentally determined 7Be neutrino flux by a factor of four and the uncertainty in the p-p neutrino flux by a factor of 2.5 (to +- 0.8 %). A future p-p experiment must be accurate to better than +- 3 % to shrink the uncertainty in tan^2 theta_{12} by more than 15 %. The idea that the Sun shines because of nuclear fusion reactions can be tested accurately by comparing the observed photon luminosity of the Sun with the luminosity inferred from measurements of solar neutrino fluxes. Based upon quantitative analyses of present and simulated future experiments, we answer the question: Why perform low-energy solar neutrino experiments?

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that existing solar and reactor neutrino experiments, combined with the luminosity constraint (which requires that the total energy carried by neutrinos equals the observed solar luminosity), can determine the p-p solar neutrino flux to ±2% without assuming standard solar model flux predictions, and that future low-energy experiments can further improve these determinations and test the fundamental assumption that nuclear fusion powers the Sun.

## Method
The authors performed 152 global χ² analyses of all available solar neutrino data (chlorine, SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, Super-Kamiokande, SNO pure D₂O and salt phases) and KamLAND reactor data, treating the ⁸B, ⁷Be, p-p, and CNO neutrino fluxes as free parameters. They imposed the luminosity constraint as a linear relation between neutrino fluxes and the solar luminosity, and marginalized over the mixing angle θ₁₃ using constraints from atmospheric, K2K, and CHOOZ experiments. They also simulated future ⁷Be ν-e scattering experiments and p-p experiments to evaluate required precision for improving oscillation parameter determinations.

## Context
This work extends the approach of Bahcall and Krastev (1996), who found that the small mixing angle (SMA) solution was slightly preferred over LMA and vacuum oscillations with much larger flux uncertainties (factors of two for p-p and five for ⁸B at 95% C.L.). The present analysis uses the much richer data set from Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and KamLAND to show that the LMA MSW solution is correct and that fluxes can now be determined independently of standard solar model assumptions. The paper provides a quantitative roadmap for future low-energy solar neutrino experiments (⁷Be and p-p) to test the transition from vacuum-dominated to matter-dominated oscillations and to measure the solar luminosity via neutrinos, enabling a direct test of whether nuclear fusion powers the Sun.
