# Revisiting the proton-radius problem using constrained Gaussian processes

> Background: The "proton radius puzzle" refers to an eight-year old problem that highlights major inconsistencies in the extraction of the charge radius of the proton from muonic Lamb-shift experiments as compared against experiments using elastic electron scattering. For the latter, the determination of the charge radius involves an extrapolation of the experimental form factor to zero momentum transfer. Purpose: To estimate the proton radius by introducing a novel non-parametric approach to model the electric form factor of the proton. Methods: Within a Bayesian paradigm, we develop a model flexible enough to fit the data without any parametric assumptions on the form factor. The Bayesian estimation is guided by imposing only two physical constraints on the form factor: (a) its value at zero momentum transfer (normalization) and (b) its overall shape, assumed to be a monotonically decreasing function of the momentum transfer. Variants of these assumptions are explored to assess the impact of these constraints. Results: So far our results are inconclusive in regard to the proton puzzle, as they depend on both, the assumed constrains and the range of experimental data used. For example, if only low momentum-transfer data is used, adopting only the normalization constraint provides a value compatible with the smaller muonic result, while imposing only the shape constraint favors the larger electronic value. Conclusions: We have presented a novel technique to estimate the proton radius from electron scattering data based on a non-parametric Gaussian process. We have shown the impact of the physical constraints imposed on the form factor and of the range of experimental data used. In this regard, we are hopeful that as this technique is refined and with the anticipated new results from the PRad experiment, we will get closer to resolve of the puzzle.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a non-parametric Bayesian method using constrained Gaussian processes to extract the proton charge radius from electron scattering data, but the results remain inconclusive regarding the proton radius puzzle because the extracted radius depends on both the physical constraints imposed on the form factor and the range of experimental data used.

## Method

The authors model the electric form factor of the proton using a linear combination of basis functions derived from a grid of equally spaced points, with coefficients that correspond to the form factor's value, first derivative, and second derivatives at the grid points. They impose physical constraints including normalization at zero momentum transfer and monotonic decreasing shape, then place a truncated Gaussian process prior on the coefficients to enforce these constraints within a Bayesian framework. Posterior sampling is performed using Markov Chain Monte Carlo to estimate the proton radius and its uncertainty from the 1422 Mainz data points.

## Key numbers

- The muonic hydrogen result gives a proton radius of 0.84087(39) fm, while the CODATA value combining electron scattering and atomic spectroscopy gives 0.8775(51) fm
- A recent measurement of the 2S-4P transition in electronic hydrogen suggests a smaller proton radius of 0.8335(95) fm, consistent with the muonic result
- The charge radius of 208Pb is known to 5.5012(13) fm, or about two parts in 10,000
- The PRad experiment at JLab collected data in the momentum-transfer range of Q2 = 10^-4 to 10^-1 GeV^2

## Context

This work extends the statistical approach to the proton radius problem initiated by Higinbotham et al. (2015), who used linear extrapolations of low-Q2 data to obtain radii consistent with muonic results, and contrasts with parametric fitting methods such as monopole, dipole, polynomial, and Pade approximant fits that have generated controversy. The method aims to eliminate reliance on specific functional forms by using a nonparametric approach, and the authors anticipate that refinement of the technique combined with new results from the PRad experiment may help resolve the puzzle.
