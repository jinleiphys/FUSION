# Determination of the forward Compton scattering amplitudes for C and Pb

> The forward Compton scattering amplitude for carbon and lead have been calculated from total photoabsorption cross section data by using dispersion relations. The results show a large difference between the scattering amplitudes for nuclei and the free nucleon, above the $Δ$ region. The forward Compton scattering cross sections have been calculated and compared with the available data. The Weise sum rule is discussed together with the prediction of a recent theoretical model.

# Determination of the forward Compton scattering amplitudes for C and Pb

## Key claim

The forward Compton scattering amplitudes for carbon and lead, calculated from total photoabsorption cross section data via dispersion relations, show a large difference from the free-nucleon amplitude above the Δ-resonance region, with the high-mass nucleon resonances absent in the nuclear curves; this difference cannot be attributed solely to Fermi-motion smearing and may reflect the onset of shadowing.

## Method

The real part of the forward Compton amplitude was calculated using the Kramers-Kronig dispersion relation, with the imaginary part obtained from the optical theorem applied to total photoabsorption cross section data. The authors performed numerical integration of fits to the available photoabsorption data for carbon, lead, proton, and neutron from 0.14 to 5 GeV, using a parametrization that combined six Breit-Wigner resonances with a background contribution that included a quasi-deuteron component and a shadowing factor. The low-energy contribution below the pion photoproduction threshold was taken from experimental values of the integrated cross section Σ_μ.

## Key numbers

- Zero of the real part of the forward Compton amplitude for the proton: ω*_p = 0.317 ± 0.002 GeV
- Zero of the real part for carbon: ω*_C = 0.328 ± 0.002 GeV
- Zero of the real part for lead: ω*_Pb = 0.332 ± 0.002 GeV
- Reduced χ² values for the photoabsorption cross section fits: 0.37 for carbon, 0.13 for lead, 0.4 for proton, 0.22 for neutron
- The approximation δ = 0 in the dispersion relation introduces an error |δ_max(ω)| < 0.1 GeV μb for ω > 0.3 GeV
- Classical dipole sum value S = 60 GeV μb
- Experimental Σ_μ values: 1.91 NZ/A for carbon and 1.75 NZ/A for lead

## Context

This work extends the dispersion relation analysis of Ahrens et al. (1988), who had found a universal zero of the nuclear forward Compton amplitude at 327 ± 5 MeV, by using new photoabsorption data from Bianchi et al. and Mirazita et al. that cover the intermediate energy region up to about 80 GeV. The results contradict the model of Boffi et al., which predicted an antishadowing effect below 2 GeV and used the Weise sum rule to check consistency; the present calculation shows large disagreement with that model in the whole energy range. The derived forward Compton amplitudes enable testing of recent theoretical models for the damping of high-mass nucleon resonances in nuclear medium and for the shadowing effect at high energy.
