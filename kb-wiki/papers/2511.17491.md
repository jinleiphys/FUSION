# Exploring fixed points and eigenstates of quantum systems with reinforcement learning

> We introduce a reinforcement learning algorithm designed to identify the fixed points of a given quantum operation. The method iteratively constructs the unitary transformation that maps the computational basis onto the basis of fixed points through a reward-penalty scheme based on quantum measurements. In cases where the operation corresponds to a Hamiltonian evolution, this task reduces to determining the Hamiltonian eigenstates. The algorithm is first benchmarked on random Hamiltonians acting on two and three qubits and then applied to many-body systems of up to six qubits, including the transverse-field Ising model and the all-to-all pairing Hamiltonian. In both cases, the algorithm is demonstrated to perform successfully; in the pairing model, it can also reveal hidden symmetries, which can be exploited to restrict learning to specific symmetry sectors. Finally, we discuss the possibility of post-selecting high-fidelity states even when full convergence has not been reached.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a reinforcement learning algorithm that can identify all fixed points (or eigenstates) of a quantum operation simultaneously by learning the global unitary transformation connecting the computational basis to the fixed-point basis, rather than finding states sequentially. The method works for general quantum operations in principle, but the authors restrict their demonstration to unitary Hamiltonian evolution and acknowledge that the algorithm may converge to spurious invariant superpositions if the evolution time parameter is not carefully randomized across iterations.

## Method

The algorithm initializes a set of qudits in distinct computational basis states and iteratively applies a candidate unitary transformation \(D_k\), the quantum operation \(\mathcal{E}\) (here unitary time evolution under a Hamiltonian), and the inverse transformation \(D_k^\dagger\), followed by projective measurements in the computational basis. Based on whether each qudit's measurement outcome matches its original label, the algorithm applies rewards (reducing exploration parameters) or punishments (increasing exploration parameters and applying random rotations in the corresponding two-dimensional subspaces), updating the unitary transformation \(D_k\) via a product of pairwise rotation operators. A fine-tuning stage resets exploration parameters after initial convergence to allow further refinement, and the evolution time \(\tau\) is randomly sampled at each iteration to avoid convergence to spurious eigenstates that are invariant only for specific resonant times.

## Key numbers

- For random two-qubit Hamiltonians (\(d=4\)), the algorithm achieves mean fidelities \(F_{\mathrm{max}} \approx F_{\mathrm{min}} \approx 0.97\) without resets and \(F_{\mathrm{max}} \approx F_{\mathrm{min}} \approx 0.98\) with resets, using parameters \(r=0.9\), \(p=2/r\), \(w_{\mathrm{th}}=0.005\), \(w_{\mathrm{r}}=0.01\), and \(N_{\mathrm{r}}=100\) realizations.
- For random three-qubit Hamiltonians (\(d=8\)), fidelities improve from \(F_{\mathrm{min}} \approx 0.90\) and \(F_{\mathrm{max}} \approx 0.91\) without resets to \(F_{\mathrm{min}} \approx 0.97\) and \(F_{\mathrm{max}} \approx 0.98\) with resets, using identical parameters.
- The dimensionless evolution time \(\tilde{\tau}\) is uniformly sampled from the interval \([0, 100]\) in each iteration for the random Hamiltonian benchmarks.
- The algorithm is applied to many-body systems of up to six qubits, including the transverse-field Ising model and the all-to-all pairing Hamiltonian.

## Context

This work extends the sequential eigenstate-finding reinforcement learning approach of Albarran-Arriagada (2020) by learning all fixed points simultaneously through a global unitary transformation, achieving higher fidelities and faster convergence for two-qubit systems while also scaling to three qubits where the prior method struggled. The algorithm is benchmarked on random Hamiltonians and then applied to the transverse-field Ising model (relevant to condensed matter physics) and the Richardson pairing Hamiltonian (relevant to nuclear physics), demonstrating that it can reveal hidden symmetries in the pairing model that allow restriction to specific symmetry sectors. The authors position this as a non-variational alternative to hybrid quantum-classical variational methods (such as those by McClean 2016, Cerezo 2021, and Tilly 2022) that avoids the barren plateau problem, and they note that future work will extend the method to non-unitary operations including noise and dissipation.
