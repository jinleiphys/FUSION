# The $γ$-process nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae. I. A novel analysis of $γ$-process yields in massive stars

> The $γ$-process nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae is generally accepted as a feasible process for the synthesis of neutron-deficient isotopes beyond iron. However, crucial discrepancies between theory and observations still exist: the average production of $γ$-process yields from massive stars are too low to reproduce the solar distribution in galactic chemical evolution calculations, and the yields of the Mo and Ru isotopes are by a further factor of 10 lower than the yields of the other $γ$-process nuclei. We investigate the $γ$-process in 5 sets of core-collapse supernova models published in literature with initial masses 15, 20, and 25 M$_{\odot}$ at solar metallicity. We compared the $γ$-process overproduction factors from the different models. To highlight the possible effect of nuclear physics input, we also considered 23 ratios of two isotopes close to each other in mass, relative to their solar values. Further, we investigated the contribution of C-O shell mergers in the supernova progenitors as an additional site of the $γ$-process. Our analysis shows that a large scatter among the different models exists for both the $γ$-process integrated yields and the isotopic ratios. We found only 10 ratios that agree with their solar values, all the others differ by at least a factor of 3 from the solar values in all the considered sets of models. The $γ$-process within C-O shell mergers mostly influence the isotopic ratios that involve intermediate and heavy proton-rich isotopes with $\rm A>100$.

# The gamma-process nucleosynthesis in core-collapse supernovae. I. A novel analysis of gamma-process yields in massive stars

## Key claim

The gamma-process in core-collapse supernovae remains unable to reproduce solar p-nuclei abundances, with average production too low by roughly a factor of three and Mo-Ru isotopes underproduced by an additional factor of ten; however, the authors show that large scatter exists among different model sets and that C-O shell mergers can significantly boost production of heavier p-nuclei, though not enough to resolve the overall discrepancy.

## Method

The authors compared gamma-process overproduction factors and isotopic ratios from five sets of one-dimensional core-collapse supernova models (Rauscher+02, Pignatari+16, Ritter+18, Sieverding+18, Lawson+22) for 15, 20, and 25 solar mass progenitors at solar metallicity. They analyzed 23 ratios of two isotopes close in mass relative to their solar values to highlight nuclear physics input effects, and separately evaluated the contribution of C-O shell mergers in the supernova progenitors as an additional gamma-process site.

## Key numbers

- Average overproduction factor F0 varies by up to a factor of roughly five across different model sets for the same progenitor mass
- Only 10 out of 23 isotopic ratios agree with their solar values; all others differ by at least a factor of 3 from solar values in all considered model sets
- Mo and Ru isotopes are underproduced by a further factor of 10 compared to other gamma-process nuclei
- F0/O-16 overproduction ratio is approximately 0.5 in typical models, with only PGN15 reaching about 1.05, still far from the factor of roughly two required to reproduce solar abundances
- In C-O shell merger models (RIT15 and RAU20), the merger component dominates production for most p-isotopes with mass number greater than 110, contributing over 50% of yields for isotopes such as 132Ba (95.9% in RIT15), 162Er (97.2% in RIT15), and 180Ta (98.3% in RIT15)
- The 20 solar mass model from Rauscher+02 experienced a C-O shell merger about 1 day before core collapse
- The Ritter+18 25 solar mass model experienced so large fallback that the explosion ended in a failed supernova, locking all explosive nucleosynthesis material into a central remnant of 5.7 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the long-standing investigation of p-nuclei production in massive stars, following foundational studies by Burbidge (1957), Woosley (1978), and the first complete gamma-process computations by Prantzos (1990) and Rayet (1995). The authors confirm the persistent underproduction problem identified by Arnould (2003) and Rauscher (2013), and the specific Mo-Ru deficit noted by Pignatari (2016). The analysis of C-O shell mergers builds on earlier work by Rauscher (2002) and Ritter (2018), who showed such events can boost p-nuclei yields by orders of magnitude. The large scatter among models found here motivates future calculations with updated nuclear reaction rates, particularly since most models considered did not use the latest reaction rates for gamma-process nucleosynthesis. The role of C-O shell mergers requires further investigation as a potential partial solution to the missing p-nuclei production.
