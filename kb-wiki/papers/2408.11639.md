# Capture Rates of Highly Degenerate Neutrons

> At the low temperature and high density conditions of a neutron star crust neutrons are degenerate. In this work, we study the effect of this degeneracy on the capture rates of neutrons on neutron rich nuclei in accreted crusts. We use a statistical Hauser-Feshbach model to calculate neutron capture rates and find that neutron degeneracy can increase rates significantly. Changes increase from a factor of a few to many orders of magnitude near the neutron drip line. We also quantify uncertainties due to model inputs for masses, $γ$-strength functions, and level densities. We find that uncertainties increase dramatically away from stability and that degeneracy tends to increase these uncertainties further, except for cases near the neutron drip line where degeneracy leads to more robustness. As in the case of capture of classically distributed neutrons, variations in the mass model have the strongest impact. Corresponding variations in the reaction rates can be as high as 3 to 4 orders of magnitude, and be more than 5 times larger than under classical conditions. To ease the incorporation of neutron degeneracy in nucleosynthesis networks, we provide tabulated results of capture rates as well as analytical expressions as function of temperature and neutron chemical potential, for proton numbers between $3 \le Z \le 85$, derived from fits to our numerical results. Fits are based on a new parametrization that complements previously employed power law approximations with additional Lorentzian terms that account for low energy resonances, significantly improving accuracy.

## Key claim

Neutron degeneracy in accreting neutron star crusts can increase neutron capture rates from a factor of a few to many orders of magnitude near the neutron drip line, with the largest enhancements occurring for nuclei with negative Q-values where the neutron chemical potential exceeds the threshold energy.

## Method

The authors used the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model implemented in the TALYS 1.9 reaction code to calculate neutron capture cross sections for a broad range of neutron-rich nuclei (proton numbers Z between 3 and 85). They integrated these cross sections over Fermi-Dirac neutron energy distributions to obtain degenerate capture rates, and compared them to rates calculated with Maxwell-Boltzmann distributions. The baseline calculation used the HFB-Skyrme mass model, HFB+QRPA gamma-strength function, and HFS nuclear level density; variations in each input were explored separately to quantify uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- Ratio R between degenerate and classical capture rates can reach as large as 10^46 for nuclei like 74Ti (Z=22, N=52) at low temperature and high chemical potential
- Variations in reaction rates due to different nuclear model inputs can be as high as 3 to 4 orders of magnitude
- Under degenerate conditions, model-induced uncertainties can be more than 5 times larger than under classical Maxwell-Boltzmann conditions
- The new parametrization with Lorentzian terms improves the normalized root-mean-square error by about 30% compared to a power-law-only fit for exothermic reactions
- Calculations cover temperatures between 0.001 and 1 MeV and neutron chemical potentials between 0.2 and 2.0 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analytical approach of Shternin et al. (2012), who studied degenerate neutron capture for Mg and Ca isotopes using a power-law approximation for cross sections. The authors find significant differences from that pioneering work, motivating analogous studies to those of Lau et al. (2018) who incorporated degenerate capture rates using the Shternin methodology. The uncertainty analysis parallels studies by Liddick et al. (2016) for non-degenerate neutron captures, confirming that uncertainties grow dramatically away from stability and showing that degeneracy typically increases these uncertainties further, except near the neutron drip line where degeneracy provides more robustness. The tabulated results and analytical expressions enable incorporation of neutron degeneracy into nucleosynthesis networks for accreted neutron star crusts.
