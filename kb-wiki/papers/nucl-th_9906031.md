# Simple Applications of Effective Field Theory and Similarity Renormalization Group Methods

> We use two renormalization techniques, Effective Field Theory and the Similarity Renormalization Group, to solve simple Schr{ö}dinger equations with delta-function potentials in one and two dimensions. The familiar one-dimensional delta-function does not require renormalization, but it provides the simplest example of a local interaction that can be replaced by a sequence of effective cutoff interactions that produce controllable power-law errors. The two-dimensional delta-function leads to logarithmic divergences, dimensional transmutation and asymptotic freedom, providing an example of some of the most important renormalization problems in gauge field theories. We concentrate on the power-law analysis of errors in low-energy observables. The power-law suppression of the effects of irrelevant operators is critical to the success of field theory, and understanding them turns renormalization group techniques into powerful predictive tools for complicated problems where exact solutions are not available.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that both Effective Field Theory (EFT) and the Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) can produce effective Hamiltonians for delta-function potentials whose errors can be understood and controlled, but the SRG introduces additional inverse-logarithmic errors from approximate running couplings that are not present in EFT's power-law error structure.

## Method

The authors solve the Schrodinger equation with delta-function potentials in one and two dimensions using two renormalization techniques. For EFT, they introduce a Gaussian cutoff on momentum transfer, construct an effective potential as a series of local contact interactions with adjustable coefficients, and determine these coefficients by fitting the inverse on-shell K-matrix calculated from the exact theory. For the SRG, they apply unitary transformations that lower a cutoff on energy differences between states, using both the Glazek-Wilson formulation and Wegner's flow equation approach, with coupling coherence fixing the dependence of higher-order couplings on the fundamental running coupling.

## Key numbers

- In one dimension, the delta-function potential does not require renormalization and serves as the simplest example of replacing a local interaction with effective cutoff interactions that produce controllable power-law errors
- In two dimensions, the delta-function potential leads to logarithmic divergences, dimensional transmutation and asymptotic freedom, analogous to important renormalization problems in gauge field theories
- The EFT error analysis shows that errors in the inverse on-shell K-matrix scale as powers of k^2/lambda^2, where k is the momentum and lambda is the cutoff
- The SRG introduces errors that scale as inverse powers of logarithms of the cutoff in addition to power-law errors similar to those seen in EFT

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical literature on delta-function interactions (Zeldo, Phillips) and applies EFT methods developed by Lepage and Steele and Furnstahl (1998) to simple quantum mechanical systems. The paper connects to recent applications of EFT to nuclear physics, particularly the two-nucleon problem (Ordonez 1992, Cohen 1999), and to the SRG formalism invented by Glazek and Wilson and independently by Wegner. The results enable systematic comparison between EFT and SRG approaches for more complex problems where exact solutions are not available, including nuclear many-body systems.
