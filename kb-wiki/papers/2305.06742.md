# Screening of electric field and nuclear EDM in non-stationary states of atoms and molecules

> According to the Schiff theorem, an external electric field vanishes at atomic nucleus in a neutral atom in a stationary state, i.e. it is completely shielded by electrons. This makes a nuclear electric dipole moment (EDM) unobservable. We show that if atom or molecule is not in a stationary state (e.g. in a superposition of two stationary states), electric field on the nucleus is not zero and interaction with nuclear EDM does not vanish. In molecules this effect is enhanced by the ratio of nuclear mass to to electron mass, $M_n/m_e$ , since nuclei in a molecule are slow (compare to electrons) and do not provide efficient screening in a non-stationary environment. Electric field on the nucleus may also affect nuclear reactions.

# Screening of electric field and nuclear EDM in non-stationary states of atoms and molecules

## Key claim
The Schiff theorem, which states that an external electric field is completely shielded at the nucleus in a neutral atom in a stationary state, does not apply when the atom or molecule is in a non-stationary state (e.g., a superposition of two stationary states); in such states, the electric field on the nucleus is non-zero and can interact with a nuclear electric dipole moment (EDM), with the effect enhanced in molecules by the ratio of nuclear mass to electron mass.

## Method
The author derives quantum-mechanical expressions for the total electric field on the nucleus in a non-stationary state by starting from the Hamiltonian of a neutral atom in an external electric field and using the commutator of the total electron momentum with the Hamiltonian. For molecules, the derivation uses the relative nuclear momentum and reduced nuclear mass, subtracting center-of-mass motion, to obtain the field on a specific nucleus in a diatomic molecule. The analysis covers two cases: when the electric dipole matrix element between the two stationary states is non-zero (giving an oscillating field even without external field), and when it is zero (requiring perturbation theory with an external field).

## Key numbers
- For atoms, the amplitude of the oscillating electric field on the nucleus is estimated as |E^t_z| ~ (epsilon_a - epsilon_b)^2 / [Z (eV)^2] * 10^7 V/cm, assuming c_a c_b ~ 1; for Z ~ 1 and energy difference of a few eV, this field may exceed by three orders of magnitude electric fields used to measure neutron and atomic EDM.
- For atoms with zero dipole matrix element between states, the field on the nucleus from a constant external field is |E^t_z| ~ (epsilon_a - epsilon_b)^2 / [Z (27 eV)^2] * E^ext_z, so for energy differences smaller than 27 eV the field on the nucleus is smaller than the external field.
- In molecules, the electric field on the nucleus is proportional to the reduced nuclear mass mu rather than the electron mass, giving an enhancement factor of nuclear mass to electron mass (M_n/m_e) compared to atoms for equal energy differences.

## Context
This work extends the Schiff theorem (Schiff, 1963) beyond its original stationary-state formulation, building on prior studies of incomplete screening in oscillating fields (DFSS 1986, Flambaum 2018, DBGF 2018, Flambaum and Samsonov, Bao). The finite nuclear size effect was previously implemented as the nuclear Schiff moment (Sandars, Hinds, SFK, FKS 1986), and the suppression factor for atomic EDM relative to nuclear EDM is proportional to the squared nuclear radius over squared atomic radius (~10^-9) partly compensated by Z^2 R_S (SFK). The result enables possible measurement of nuclear EDM in non-stationary states and may affect nuclear reactions such as neutron capture enhanced by laser fields (LaserNucl, Ho), where electric field mixing of s-wave and p-wave resonances could produce effects similar to parity-violating interactions with enhancement factors up to 10^6 (SF 1980, SF 1982, Nucl1, Nucl2).
