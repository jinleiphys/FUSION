# Quantum Complexity Fluctuations from Nuclear and Hypernuclear Forces

> Toward an improved understanding of the role of quantum information in nuclei and exotic matter, we examine the quantum magic (non-stabilizerness) in low-energy strong interaction processes. As stabilizer states can be prepared efficiently using classical computers, and include classes of entangled states, it is quantum magic and fluctuations in quantum magic, together with entanglement, that determine computational resource requirements. As a measure of fluctuations in quantum magic, and hence the severity of the exponentially-scaling classical computing resource requirements, induced by scattering, the "magic power" of the S-matrix is introduced. This provides indirect experimental constraints on quantum resources required to model nuclei and dense matter using fault-tolerant quantum computers. Using experimentally-determined scattering phase shifts and mixing parameters, the magic power in nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon scattering, along with the magic in the deuteron, are found to exhibit interesting and distinct features. The $Σ^-$-baryon is identified as a potential candidate catalyst for enhanced spreading of magic and entanglement in dense matter, depending on in-medium decoherence.

## Key claim

The paper introduces the "magic power" of the S-matrix as a measure of fluctuations in quantum magic (non-stabilizerness) induced by low-energy nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon scattering, arguing that magic and its fluctuations, together with entanglement, determine the computational resource requirements for simulating nuclei and dense matter on fault-tolerant quantum computers. The authors hedge that these constraints are only abstractly related to the quantum resources required to perform QCD simulations of these systems, and that the results correspond specifically to the impact of two-body scattering processes on computational resources required to simulate many-body systems in terms of baryon degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors map the spin degrees of freedom in nucleon-nucleon and hyperon-nucleon scattering onto one-qubit and two-qubit systems, using experimentally-determined scattering phase shifts and mixing parameters from the Nijm93 potential for NN scattering and from next-to-next-to-leading order chiral effective field theory for YN scattering. They compute the magic power of the S-matrix by averaging the linear magic (1-Renyi entropy) induced by the S-matrix on all stabilizer states, and compare it with the entanglement power of the S-matrix computed over tensor-product stabilizer states.

## Key numbers

* The magic power of the S-matrix in the coupled $^3S_1$-$^3D_1$ np scattering channel reaches maxima of approximately 0.17 near laboratory momenta of 31, 110, and 330 MeV, compared to the maximum possible one-qubit magic of 1/3.
* The linear magic of the deuteron bound state is approximately 0.17, using a D-wave amplitude of 0.24 (5.8% probability) from the Nijm93 potential.
* The maximum magic power in the two-qubit NN spin-sector S-matrix is 0.3, occurring at phase shift difference $\Delta\delta = (k+1/2)\pi/2$, while the maximum entanglement power is 1/6 (approximately 0.167).
* In $\Sigma^- n$ scattering, the magic power and entanglement power remain approximately maximal over an extended energy range because the phase shift difference $\Delta\delta$ takes values close to $\pi/4$.
* Two successive $\Sigma^- n$ scatterings induce a linear magic of approximately 0.405 in the two neutrons over a significant kinematic range in a simplified pure-state model.

## Context

This work extends the study of entanglement in few-baryon scattering initiated by Beane et al. (2018, 2020, 2021) and builds on the definition of magic power for unitary operators by Leone, Oliviero and Hamma (2022). It connects to investigations of entanglement suppression and emergent symmetries of strong interactions, and to the observation that SU(4) symmetry emerges in the large-N$_c$ limit of QCD (Kaplan, 1995). The results enable future studies of how magic and entanglement evolve through the confinement scale, and provide a framework for estimating the computational complexity of simulating nuclear systems at different energies, suggesting that simulations at parameters corresponding to vanishing magic would be computationally less demanding than at points with local maxima in magic.
