# Quantifying Correlations Between Isovector Observables and the Density Dependence of Nuclear Symmetry Energy away from Saturation Density

> According to the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem, the nuclear symmetry energy $S(ρ)$ and its slope $L(ρ)$ at arbitrary densities can be decomposed in terms of the density and momentum dependence of the single-nucleon potentials in isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter which are potentially accessible to experiment. We quantify the correlations between several well-known isovector observables and $L(ρ)$ to locate the density range in which each isovector observable is most sensitive to the density dependence of the $S(ρ)$. We then study the correlation coefficients between those isovector observables and all the components of the $L(ρ)$. The neutron skin thickness of $^{208}$Pb is found to be strongly correlated with the $L(ρ)$ at a subsaturation density of $ρ= 0.59 ρ_0$ through the density dependence of the first-order symmetry potential. Neutron star radii are found to be strongly correlated with the $L(ρ)$ over a wide range of supra-saturation densities mainly through both the density and momentum dependence of the first-order symmetry potential. Finally, we find that although the crust-core transition pressure has a complex correlation with the $L(ρ)$, it is strongly correlated with the momentum derivative of the first-order symmetry potential, and the density dependence of the second-order symmetry potential.

## Key claim

The paper quantifies that the strongest correlation between the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and the density slope of the symmetry energy L(ρ) appears not at saturation density ρ0 but at a subsaturation density of ρ = 0.59 ρ0, and that neutron star radii correlate most strongly with L(ρ) at supra-saturation densities between 1.5 ρ0 and 2 ρ0 for a 1.4-solar-mass star, with the specific behavior depending on whether L(ρ) is monotonic or peaks and falls.

## Method

The authors performed a least-squares covariance analysis using two Skyrme energy density functionals (SLy4 and NRAPR) that have nearly identical symmetric nuclear matter equations of state but different density dependences of the symmetry energy at supra-saturation densities. They computed correlation coefficients between the density slope L(ρ) and three isovector observables (neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and 48Ca, neutron star radii for 1.0, 1.4, and 1.8 solar masses, and crust-core transition pressure) over a wide range of baryon densities. They further decomposed L(ρ) into five components based on the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem to identify which physical quantities drive each correlation.

## Key numbers

- The strongest correlation between the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and L(ρ) occurs at a subsaturation density of ρ = 0.59 ρ0 for both SLy4 and NRAPR models.
- For 48Ca, the strongest correlation with L(ρ) occurs at ρ = 0.63 ρ0 for SLy4 and at ρ = 0.39 ρ0 for NRAPR.
- For a 1.4-solar-mass neutron star, the strongest correlation with L(ρ) appears at 1.5 ρ0 for SLy4 and at approximately 2.5 ρ0 for NRAPR.
- For a 1.8-solar-mass neutron star, the strongest correlation with L(ρ) appears at 2.5 ρ0 for SLy4 and at approximately 3.5 ρ0 for NRAPR.
- The isoscalar properties of bulk nuclear matter (saturation density, binding energies, incompressibility, isoscalar effective mass, macroscopic gradient coefficient) were fixed at a 2% level, while the isovector effective mass and symmetry-gradient coefficient were allowed 20% theoretical error bars.

## Context

This work extends prior correlation studies between the symmetry energy slope L and isovector observables (Brown 2000, Horowitz 2000, Furnstahl 2001) by providing a proper statistical measure of correlations as a function of baryon density rather than only at saturation density. It builds on the decomposition of L(ρ) into single-nucleon potential components derived from the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem (Chen 2011). The results enable future experimental programs at Jefferson Lab (Horowitz 2013) and rare isotope beam facilities to constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy by targeting specific density regimes through combined measurements of neutron skins, neutron star radii, and crust-core transition properties.
