# Neutron - mirror neutron mixing and neutron stars

> The oscillation of neutrons $n$ into mirror neutrons $n'$, their mass degenerate partners from dark mirror sector, can have interesting implications for neutron stars: an ordinary neutron star could gradually transform into a mixed star consisting in part of mirror dark matter. Mixed stars can be detectable as twin partners of ordinary neutron stars: namely, there can exist compact stars with the same masses but having different radii. For a given equation of state (identical between the ordinary and mirror components), the mass and radius of a mixed star depend on the proportion between the ordinary and mirror components in its interior which in turn depends on its age. If $50 \% - 50\%$ proportion between two fractions can be reached asymptotically in time, then the maximum mass of such "maximally mixed stars" should be $\sqrt2$ times smaller than that of ordinary neutron star while the stars exceeding a critical mass value $M^{\rm max}_{NS}/\sqrt2$ should collapse in black holes after certain time. We evaluate the evolution time and discuss the implications of $n-n'$ transition for the pulsar observations as well as for the gravitational waves from the neutron star mergers and associated electromagnetic signals.

## Key claim
Neutron stars can gradually transform into mixed stars containing mirror neutrons via $n-n'$ oscillation, with the maximum mass of a maximally mixed star (50% ordinary and 50% mirror neutrons) being $\sqrt{2}$ times smaller than that of an ordinary neutron star, provided the transformation is adiabatic and the equation of state is identical between ordinary and mirror components.

## Method
The authors model the adiabatic transformation of a neutron star into a mixed star using a two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) framework, where the total energy density and pressure are sums of ordinary and mirror components with identical equations of state. They solve the coupled TOV equations for two concentric spheres of ordinary and mirror nuclear matter, using the SLy equation of state and a two-polytrope equation of state as examples, and estimate the $n-n'$ conversion rate in dense nuclear matter to determine the evolution timescale.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass of ordinary neutron star with SLy equation of state: $M^{\rm max}_{\rm NS} \simeq 2.05 \, M_{\odot}$, reached at central density $\rho_c^{\rm max} \simeq 2.86 \times 10^{15}$ g/cm$^3$ (baryon number density $n_c^{\rm max} \simeq 1.28$ fm$^{-3}$)
- Maximum mass of ordinary neutron star with two-polytrope equation of state: $M^{\rm max}_{\rm NS} = 2.57 \, M_{\odot}$
- Typical neutron star radius for $M \simeq 1.4 \, M_{\odot}$: about 12 km for both equations of state
- $n-n'$ mixing mass can be as large as $\eps \sim 10^{-15}$ eV, corresponding to oscillation time $\tau_{nn'} = \eps^{-1}$ as small as 1 second
- Maximum mass of maximally mixed star: $M^{\rm max}_{\rm NS} / \sqrt{2}$ (approximately 1.45 $M_{\odot}$ for SLy, 1.82 $M_{\odot}$ for two-polytrope)
- Total baryon number at maximum mass for SLy: $N^{\rm max}_{\rm NS} \simeq 2.91 \times 10^{57}$

## Context
This work extends the concept of neutron-mirror neutron mixing proposed by Berezhiani (2005) and subsequent studies, applying it to neutron star astrophysics. It builds on earlier discussions by the same authors (INT, Massimo) and recent work by Nussinov and Ciancarella. The paper enables observational tests through pulsar timing, gravitational wave signals from neutron star mergers, and electromagnetic counterparts, potentially explaining the narrow mass distribution of neutron stars in binaries around $1.4 \, M_{\odot}$ if $n-n'$ mixing is sufficiently large.
