# Essential elements for nuclear binding

> How does nuclear binding emerge from first principles? Our current best understanding of nuclear forces is based on a systematic low-energy expansion called chiral effective field theory. However, recent {\it ab initio} calculations of nuclear structure have found that not all chiral effective field theory interactions give accurate predictions with increasing nuclear density. In this letter we address the reason for this problem and the first steps toward a solution. Using nuclear lattice simulations, we deduce the minimal nuclear interaction that can reproduce the ground state properties of light nuclei, medium-mass nuclei, and neutron matter simultaneously with no more than a few percent error in the energies and charge radii. We find that only four parameters are needed. With these four parameters one can accurately describe neutron matter up to saturation density and the ground state properties of nuclei up to calcium. Given the absence of sign oscillations in these lattice Monte Carlo simulations and the mild scaling of computational effort scaling with nucleon number, this work provides a pathway to high-quality simulations in the future with as many as one or two hundred nucleons.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a minimal nuclear interaction with only four parameters, based on SU(4)-invariant local and nonlocal smeared contact interactions, can reproduce ground state properties of light and medium-mass nuclei up to calcium and neutron matter simultaneously with no more than a few percent error in energies and charge radii, but this agreement is achieved through additional fine-tuning of the balance between local and nonlocal interactions that goes beyond the standard chiral effective field theory expansion.

## Method

The authors constructed a leading-order SU(4)-invariant pion-less effective field theory on a periodic lattice with spacing 1.32 fm, using two-body and three-body contact interactions with local and nonlocal smearing parameters. They fixed the two-body interaction by fitting the SU(4)-averaged S-wave scattering length to 9.1 fm and effective range to 2.2 fm, and determined the three-body coupling by fitting the triton binding energy. They then performed auxiliary-field Monte Carlo lattice simulations with a shuttle algorithm to compute ground state energies and charge radii for nuclei up to A=48, using the pinhole algorithm for density distributions and treating the Coulomb interaction perturbatively.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing: a = 1.32 fm, corresponding to momentum cutoff Lambda = 465 MeV
- Optimal SU(4)-averaged scattering length: a0 = 9.1 fm
- SU(4)-averaged effective range: r0 = 2.2 fm
- Optimized parameters: C2 = -3.41 x 10^-7 MeV^-2, C3 = -1.4 x 10^-14 MeV^-5, sNL = 0.5, sL = 0.061
- Triton binding energy: 8.48(2)(0) MeV (fitted to experimental value 8.48 MeV)
- Helium-4 binding energy: 28.89(1)(1) MeV (experimental: 28.3 MeV), ratio 1.02
- Oxygen-16 binding energy: 121.9(1)(3) MeV (experimental: 127.6 MeV), ratio 0.96
- Calcium-40 binding energy: 346.8(6)(5) MeV (experimental: 342.1 MeV), ratio 1.01
- Largest relative error in binding energy: 4% for Oxygen-16
- Largest relative error in charge radius: 8% for tritium
- Temporal lattice spacing: 0.001 MeV^-1, projection time: 0.3 MeV^-1

## Context

This work extends the exploratory SU(4)-invariant lattice calculations of Elhatisari (2017) on nuclei up through oxygen and addresses the convergence problems of chiral effective field theory interactions at higher nuclear densities identified in many-body calculations by Hagen (2012), Binder (2013), and Epelbaum (2013). The results provide a pathway to realistic lattice simulations of heavy nuclei with up to 200 nucleons by demonstrating that SU(4)-invariant interactions with local and nonlocal smearing can serve as a starting point for improved chiral calculations up to N3LO, with ongoing work to include one-pion exchange and higher-order corrections.
