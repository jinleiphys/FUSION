# On the efficient computation of high-order derivatives for implicitly defined functions

> Scientific studies often require the precise calculation of derivatives. In many cases an analytical calculation is not feasible and one resorts to evaluating derivatives numerically. These are error-prone, especially for higher-order derivatives. A technique based on algorithmic differentiation is presented which allows for a precise calculation of higher-order derivatives. The method can be widely applied even for the case of only numerically solvable, implicit dependencies which totally hamper a semi-analytical calculation of the derivatives. As a demonstration the method is applied to a quantum field theoretical physical model. The results are compared with standard numerical derivative methods.

## Key claim

Algorithmic differentiation (AD) can compute higher-order derivatives of implicitly defined functions at machine precision, even when the implicit dependencies are only known numerically, which is a situation where semi-analytical derivative calculation is impossible.

## Method

The authors reformulate the implicit function relation \( \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{z}) = 0 \) as an inverse relation \( \mathbf{H}(\mathbf{z}) = \mathbf{S} \mathbf{x} \), where \( \mathbf{x} \) are the independent variables and \( \mathbf{z} \) includes both independent and dependent variables. They then apply the forward mode of algorithmic differentiation using Taylor arithmetic, computing Taylor coefficients of \( \mathbf{z} \) with respect to \( \mathbf{x} \) by iteratively solving linear systems involving the Jacobian \( \mathbf{H}_{\mathbf{z}} \). The method is demonstrated on the linear sigma model with quark degrees of freedom (LσM), a quantum field theoretical model for low-energy QCD, where the grand potential depends implicitly on temperature and chemical potential through condensates defined by equations of motion.

## Key numbers

* The forward mode of AD yields one column of the Jacobian at no more than three times the operation count \( O_{\mathbf{F}} \) of the function evaluation.
* The reverse mode of AD yields one row of the Jacobian at no more than four times \( O_{\mathbf{F}} \), independent of the number of input variables (the "cheap gradient result").
* The complexity of computing Taylor coefficients for arithmetic operations grows as \( d^2 \) for multiplications and \( \sim 2d \) for additions, where \( d \) is the highest derivative degree.
* For the exponential function, the Taylor coefficient computation requires \( \sim d^2 \) floating point operations.
* Using the higher-order reverse mode, the number of forward and reverse sweeps can be reduced to \( \log_2(d) \).

## Context

This work extends the algorithmic differentiation technique described in Griewank and Walther (2008) to the specific case of implicitly defined functions, building on initial remarks by Kedem (1980). The method enables the computation of high-order Taylor expansion coefficients for thermodynamic quantities in effective QCD models, such as the linear sigma model, where previous approaches using divided differences or symbolic differentiation become error-prone or intractable at high orders. The AD tool ADOL-C provides the implementation used in the numerical tests.
