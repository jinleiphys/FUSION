# $K^-$ - nucleus relativistic mean field potentials consistent with kaonic atoms

> $K^-$ atomic data are used to test several models of the $K^-$ nucleus interaction. The t($ρ$)$ρ$ optical potential, due to coupled channel models incorporating the $Λ$(1405) dynamics, fails to reproduce these data. A standard relativistic mean field (RMF) potential, disregarding the $Λ$(1405) dynamics at low densities, also fails. The only successful model is a hybrid of a theoretically motivated RMF approach in the nuclear interior and a completely phenomenological density dependent potential, which respects the low density theorem in the nuclear surface region. This best-fit $K^-$ optical potential is found to be strongly attractive, with a depth of 180 \pm 20 MeV at the nuclear interior, in agreement with previous phenomenological analyses.

## Key claim

The only successful model for reproducing kaonic atom data is a hybrid of a theoretically motivated relativistic mean field (RMF) approach in the nuclear interior and a completely phenomenological density dependent (DD) potential that respects the low density theorem in the nuclear surface region; purely microscopic coupled-channel models incorporating the Λ(1405) dynamics, as well as standard RMF potentials that ignore this resonance, both fail to fit the data.

## Method

The authors solved the Klein-Gordon equation for K⁻ atoms using several forms of the optical potential, testing them against a database of 65 data points across the periodic table. They compared a chirally motivated coupled-channel t(ρ)ρ potential (Weise and collaborators), a standard RMF potential with scalar and vector couplings motivated by SU(3) and the quark model, and a hybrid model that uses the RMF form for densities above about 0.2ρ₀ and the phenomenological DD form at lower densities. For the RMF and hybrid models, the imaginary part of the potential was treated phenomenologically, and the coupling constant ratios α_σ and α_ω were either fixed by theory or treated as free parameters in fits to the data.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit DD optical potential depth at nuclear interior: about 190 MeV (attractive).
- Best-fit RMF potential depth at nuclear interior: about 190 MeV (attractive), with a strongly attractive vector potential and a strongly repulsive scalar potential.
- Hybrid RMF+DD best-fit real potential depth at nuclear interior: about 185 MeV, very close to the purely phenomenological DD depth.
- Depth of the attractive real potential at ρ₀ for the chirally motivated coupled-channel model: about 120 MeV.
- χ² per point for the chirally motivated coupled-channel model: close to 15 (very poor fit).
- χ² per point for the best-fit tρ potential: about 2.0 (significantly inferior to DD).
- χ² per point for the best-fit DD potential: about 1.5 (good fit).
- χ² per point for the hybrid RMF+DD potentials: 1.4 – 1.5 (good fit).
- Imaginary part depth for the RMF-fit potential: about 60 MeV.
- Transition from repulsion to attraction for the DD potential occurs near ρ/ρ₀ = 0.15.
- Transition from repulsion to attraction for the chirally motivated model occurs near ρ/ρ₀ = 0.1.
- For the hybrid model, the matching radius R_M is chosen as R_M ≈ R_{1/2} + 0.1 fm, where ρ(R_{1/2}) = ρ₀/2.
- The depth U^(K̄) from Eq. (8) for the linear model L: 189 MeV (quark model) and 181 MeV (SU(3)).

## Context

This work extends previous phenomenological analyses by Friedman et al. (1993, 1994) that used a purely density-dependent optical potential to fit kaonic atom data, finding a depth of about 200 ± 20 MeV. It directly tests the chirally motivated coupled-channel model of Weise and collaborators (1995, 1996) and the RMF approach of Schaffner and Mishustin (1996), both of which fail the atomic data test. The hybrid model provides a theoretically motivated K⁻-nucleus potential that can be used for extrapolations to high-density matter, such as in studies of kaon condensation in neutron stars. The failure of the microscopic coupled-channel models poses a major theoretical challenge, suggesting that the multiple scattering expansion for the optical potential may break down for the K⁻-nucleus system unless self-consistency requirements (as considered by Lutz, 1998) are imposed.
