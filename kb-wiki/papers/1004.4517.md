# Charged-Particle Thermonuclear Reaction Rates: II. Tables and Graphs of Reaction Rates and Probability Density Functions

> Numerical values of charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates for nuclei in the A=14 to 40 region are tabulated. The results are obtained using a method, based on Monte Carlo techniques, that has been described in the preceding paper of this series (Paper I). We present a low rate, median rate and high rate which correspond to the 0.16, 0.50 and 0.84 quantiles, respectively, of the cumulative reaction rate distribution. The meaning of these quantities is in general different from the commonly reported, but statistically meaningless expressions, "lower limit", "nominal value" and "upper limit" of the total reaction rate. In addition, we approximate the Monte Carlo probability density function of the total reaction rate by a lognormal distribution and tabulate the lognormal parameters μ and σ at each temperature. We also provide a quantitative measure (Anderson-Darling test statistic) for the reliability of the lognormal approximation. The user can implement the approximate lognormal reaction rate probability density functions directly in a stellar model code for studies of stellar energy generation and nucleosynthesis. For each reaction, the Monte Carlo reaction rate probability density functions, together with their lognormal approximations, are displayed graphically for selected temperatures in order to provide a visual impression. Our new reaction rates are appropriate for bare nuclei in the laboratory. The nuclear physics input used to derive our reaction rates is presented in the subsequent paper of this series (Paper III). In the fourth paper of this series (Paper IV) we compare our new reaction rates to previous results.

## Key claim

The paper provides tabulated charged-particle thermonuclear reaction rates for A=14 to 40 nuclei based on Monte Carlo techniques, presenting statistically meaningful low, median, and high rates (0.16, 0.50, and 0.84 quantiles) rather than the statistically meaningless "lower limit," "nominal value," and "upper limit" used in previous work; the rates are appropriate for bare nuclei in the laboratory and must be corrected for electron screening and thermal excitations when used in stellar models.

## Method

The authors used the Monte Carlo method described in Paper I, where each nuclear input quantity (resonance energies, strengths, S-factors, partial widths) is assigned a specific probability density function (Gaussian, lognormal, Porter-Thomas, etc.) and random values are drawn accordingly to compute the total reaction rate. The procedure is repeated at least 5,000 times per reaction to estimate the probability density function of the total rate, from which the median (0.50 quantile) is taken as the recommended rate and the 0.16 and 0.84 quantiles as low and high rates. The Monte Carlo probability density functions are approximated by lognormal distributions, with parameters μ and σ tabulated at each temperature along with the Anderson-Darling test statistic for the reliability of this approximation.

## Key numbers

* For 28Si(p,γ)29P at T=0.03 GK: median rate = 5.57×10−25 cm3 mol−1 s−1, low rate = 3.79×10−25 cm3 mol−1 s−1, high rate = 8.18×10−25 cm3 mol−1 s−1; lognormal parameters μ = −55.85, σ = 0.386; Anderson-Darling statistic = 0.134.
* For 28Si(p,γ)29P at T=0.3 GK: lognormal σ = 0.104; Anderson-Darling statistic = 0.532; the rate is dominated by a single narrow resonance at Ercm = 357.1 keV with strength ωγ = 0.0020 eV.
* For 35Ar(p,γ)36K at T=0.03 GK: median rate = 7.65×10−31 cm3 mol−1 s−1, low rate = 5.16×10−31 cm3 mol−1 s−1, high rate = 1.17×10−30 cm3 mol−1 s−1; lognormal parameters μ = −69.11, σ = 1.436; Anderson-Darling statistic = 3377, indicating poor lognormal fit due to a resonance at Ercm = 2 keV with ±21 keV uncertainty.
* Reaction rates are tabulated for 62 reactions on a temperature grid from T=0.01 GK to 10 GK.
* The number of Monte Carlo samples per reaction always exceeded 5,000.

## Context

This work (Paper II of a series) extends the Monte Carlo method for reaction rate evaluation introduced in Paper I to nuclei in the A=14 to 40 mass region, providing statistically rigorous rates that replace the classical "lower limit/nominal value/upper limit" approach. The nuclear physics input used is detailed in Paper III, and comparisons to previous results are made in Paper IV. The rates are intended for use in stellar model calculations of energy generation and nucleosynthesis in environments such as red giants, AGB stars, classical novae, massive stars, and type I x-ray bursts, but must be corrected for electron screening at high densities and thermal excitations of target nuclei at high temperatures.
