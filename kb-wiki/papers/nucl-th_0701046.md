# Relativistic RPA plus phonon-coupling analysis of pygmy dipole resonances

> The relativistic random-phase approximation (RRPA) plus phonon-coupling (PC) model is applied in the analysis of E1 strength distributions in $^{208}$Pb and $^{132}$Sn, for which data on pygmy dipole resonances (PDR) have recently been reported. The covariant response theory is fully consistent: the effective nuclear interaction NL3 is used both to determine the spectrum of single-nucleon Dirac states, and as the residual interaction which determines the collective phonon states in the relativistic RPA. It is shown that the picture of the PDR as a resonant oscillation of the neutron skin against the isospin saturated proton-neutron core, and with the corresponding RRPA state characterized by a coherent superposition of many neutron particle-hole configurations, remains essentially unchanged when particle-vibration coupling is included. The effect of two-phonon admixtures is a weak fragmentation and a small shift of PDR states to lower excitation energy. Even though the PDR calculated in the extended model space of $ph \otimes$phonon configurations contains sizeable two-phonon admixtures, it basically retains a one-phonon character and its dynamics is not modified by the coupling to low-lying surface vibrations.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the picture of the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) as a resonant oscillation of the neutron skin against the isospin-saturated core, characterized by a coherent superposition of many neutron particle-hole configurations in the relativistic random-phase approximation (RRPA), remains essentially unchanged when particle-vibration coupling is included; the effect of two-phonon admixtures is only a weak fragmentation and a small shift of PDR states to lower excitation energy.

## Method

The authors applied the relativistic RPA plus phonon-coupling (RRPA-PC) model, using the covariant response theory in the time-blocking approximation (TBA) to calculate the E1 strength distributions in 208Pb and 132Sn. The effective nuclear interaction NL3 was used consistently to determine the spectrum of single-nucleon Dirac states, to calculate the collective phonon states in the relativistic RPA, and to determine the particle-phonon coupling amplitudes. The phonon space included states with spin and parity 2+, 3-, 4+, 5-, and 6+ with excitation energies below the neutron separation energy and reduced transition probabilities at least 10% of the maximal one for a given spin and parity.

## Key numbers

* For 208Pb, the RRPA PDR peak at 7.18 MeV becomes fragmented in the RRPA-PC model into states at 6.84 MeV and 6.34 MeV; the sum of neutron particle-hole configuration amplitudes for the RRPA peak is 80.2%, reduced to about 50% for the state at 6.34 MeV.
* For 132Sn, the RRPA PDR peak at 7.54 MeV shifts to 7.16 MeV in the RRPA-PC model, with the sum of neutron particle-hole configuration amplitudes reduced by less than 7%.
* The ratio of the integrated PDR cross section (below 8 MeV for 208Pb, below 9 MeV for 132Sn) to the GDR cross section (Bn - 25 MeV) is 0.053 for the RRPA and 0.044 for the RRPA-PC model, compared to the experimental value of 0.03(2) for 132Sn from Adrich et al. (2005).
* The dipole photoabsorption cross sections were calculated with a smearing parameter of 200 keV for the full spectra and 40 keV for the low-energy region.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic RPA analyses of PDR in 208Pb and 132Sn by Vretenar et al. (2001), Paar et al. (2003), and Paar et al. (2005) by including particle-vibration coupling effects that were previously omitted. It directly addresses the contrasting results from the non-relativistic QRPA plus phonon coupling model of Colo et al. (2001) and Sarchi et al. (2004), which predicted non-collective low-lying E1 strength in these nuclei, and the Quasiparticle Phonon Model of Tselyaev et al. (2004) which found PDR states retaining one-phonon character. The results enable further studies of how particle-vibration coupling affects the multipole response in neutron-rich nuclei, particularly regarding the collectivity of low-lying dipole states.
