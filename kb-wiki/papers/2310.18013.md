# Location and symmetry of superconductivity in neutron stars

> Earlier, it was a standard assumption that the entire core of neutron stars is superconducting. However, the matter contents in the inner core has been unknown even qualitatively, because the density of matter in that region is expected to be higher than the nuclear saturation density 0.16 $\mathrm{fm}^{-3}$. As a consequence, no reliable model exists that would describe the neutron star matter in the inner core of neutron stars. Thus, a possibility of presence of normal, nonsuperconducting, plasma in the inner core cannot be excluded as of today. This point is supported by the numerical calculations performed in [1]. The calculations are based on the equation of state and the proton Cooper pairing gap energy derived from the chiral effective field theory. The numerical results show that the superconducting gap goes to zero beyond the depth about 1 km below the crust-core boundary. Given that the stellar radius is of the order of 12 km, therefore the superconducting proton matter is expected to exist only in a thin layer at the tip of the outer core. Recently it has been realized that the symmetry of superconductor is anisotropic in the lasagna region of the pasta phases located at the bottom of the crust. However the question of whether this symmetry is continuous or discreet was unsolved. The numerical calculations performed in [1] have shown that the tunneling rate between the adjacent slabs in the entire range of the corresponding densities is negligibly small. Thus, a discreet model is necessary for the description of the lasagna region. Uncertainties and future directions of the research are discussed.

## Key claim

The entire core of neutron stars is not necessarily superconducting; instead, superconducting proton matter is expected to exist only in a thin layer at the tip of the outer core, because the superconducting gap goes to zero beyond about 1 km below the crust-core boundary. Additionally, the symmetry of the superconductor in the lasagna region of the pasta phases is discreet rather than continuous, as the tunneling rate between adjacent slabs is negligibly small.

## Method

The author solved the equations of force balance between gravitational force and pressure gradient using an equation of state (EoS) composed of three parts: the solid crust EoS from the Barcelona-Catania-Paris-Madrid model, the outer core EoS derived from chiral effective field theory, and a generalized polytropic EoS for the inner core. The proton Cooper pairing gap energy from chiral effective field theory was used to determine where the superconducting gap vanishes. Quantum mechanical tunneling probabilities between adjacent slabs in the lasagna region were calculated to determine whether the symmetry is continuous or discreet.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: 0.16 fm\(^{-3}\), above which the matter contents of the inner core are qualitatively unknown.
- Superconducting gap goes to zero beyond a depth of about 1 km below the crust-core boundary.
- Stellar radius is of the order of 12 km, so the superconducting layer is a thin shell at the tip of the outer core.
- Two parameter sets for the outer core EoS yield: (K, S0, L) = (236 MeV, 32.3 MeV, 20.1 MeV) and (K, S0, L) = (261 MeV, 33.4 MeV, 46.4 MeV), where K is incompressibility, S0 is symmetry energy, and L is its slope.
- The energy per baryon normalization constant \(\varepsilon_0 = 36.84\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the earlier standard assumption from Baym, Bethe, and Pethick (1969) that the entire neutron star core is filled with isotropic proton superconductor, by showing that superconductivity is confined to a thin layer. It also builds on the discovery by Kobyakov (2018) and Kobyakov and Pethick (2018) that the ground state at the crust-core boundary has anisotropic symmetry due to slab-like proton structures. The results enable future models of magnetic torque from stellar magnetic fields and studies of hydromagnetic waves at the boundary between superconducting and normal plasmas.
