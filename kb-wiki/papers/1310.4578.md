# Systematic and correlated nuclear uncertainties in the i-process at the neutron shell closure N = 82

> Nuclear astrophysics simulations aiming to study the origin of the elements in stars require a multitude of nuclear physics input. Both systematic model dependent and statistically correlated uncertainties need to be considered. An application where realistic uncertainty assessments are especially important is the intermediate neutron capture process or i process: a neutron capture regime with neutron densities intermediate between the slow and rapid processes. Accordingly, the main network flux proceeds on the neutron-rich unstable isotopes up to 4-5 species off the valley of stability. The i process has been clearly identified to be active in post-AGB stars during the Very Late Thermal Pulse H-ingestion event, and a recent work infers about its important role in early generations of stars. Here we demonstrate the effect of propagating systematic nuclear uncertainties from different theoretical models to final abundances for a region around the 2$^\mathrm{nd}$ peak at $A-Z=80$ for elemental ratio predictions involving Ba, La and Eu in i-process conditions. These elements are used to distinguish different n-capture contributions observed in low-metallicity stars. For the simple 1-zone model adopted here, predictions vary as much as a factor of 22 in on possible observational plane ([La/Eu] vs. [Ba/La]). To consider statistically correlated uncertainties, we similarly perform a full nuclear physics uncertainty study within a given Hauser-Feshbach model and demonstrate the role of correlations on the final stellar abundance uncertainties. We show that in i-process conditions the main result of neglecting correlations is to underestimate the impact of nuclear uncertainties on the final nucleosynthesis yields by as much as two orders of magnitude. ## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that propagating systematic nuclear uncertainties from different Hauser-Feshbach models to final i-process abundances can change elemental ratio predictions by up to a factor of 22 in the [La/Eu] versus [Ba/La] observational plane, and that neglecting correlations within a single model underestimates the impact of nuclear uncertainties on final nucleosynthesis yields by as much as two orders of magnitude.

## Method

The authors used a 1-zone i-process trajectory with constant temperature of 2x10^8 K and density of 10^4 g/cm^3 to simulate intermediate neutron capture conditions. They compared neutron capture rates from three Hauser-Feshbach codes (CoH_3, TALYS 1.4, and NON-SMOKER) for 22 unstable fission-product nuclei around the N=82 neutron shell closure, and performed a Monte Carlo covariance analysis within the CoH_3 model to study the effect of correlated uncertainties on final abundances.

## Key numbers

- Predictions vary by as much as a factor of 22 in the [La/Eu] vs. [Ba/La] observational plane when using different Hauser-Feshbach models for the 22 nuclei around N=82
- Neglecting correlations underestimates the impact of nuclear uncertainties on final nucleosynthesis yields by as much as two orders of magnitude
- Cs final abundances are the most affected by correlated nuclear uncertainties with an uncertainty of about a factor of 3.5, compared to a factor of 6x10^-3 when uncorrelated nuclear uncertainties are used
- Between NON-SMOKER and CoH_3, differences in capture rates lead to as much as a factor of 22 in the [Ba/La] ratio and 1.3 in the [La/Eu] ratio
- Between NON-SMOKER and TALYS 1.4, differences in capture rates lead to as much as a factor of 47 in the [Ba/La] ratio and 19 in the [La/Eu] ratio
- CoH_3 over-estimates the MACS from NON-SMOKER on average by a factor of about 1.4 in this region
- TALYS 1.4 under-estimates the MACS from NON-SMOKER on average by 4% in this region
- The i-process 1-zone trajectory provides a neutron density around N_n ~ 10^15 cm^-3

## Context

This work extends the investigation of intermediate neutron capture process (i-process) nucleosynthesis proposed by Herwig et al. (2011) and further developed by Herwig et al. (2013) for super-AGB stars, which may explain the elemental signatures observed in CEMP-r/s stars. The study focuses on the region around the N=82 neutron shell closure, particularly the bottleneck nucleus I-135, which determines nucleosynthesis predictions in the La-Eu-Ba plot used to distinguish between CEMP-s and CEMP-r/s stars. The paper demonstrates that previous sensitivity studies considering only single reaction rate changes (such as those by Mumpower et al. 2012, Beun et al. 2009, and Surman et al. 2009) are insufficient, and that proper treatment of both systematic model differences and correlated uncertainties within a single model is essential for realistic astrophysical uncertainty assessments.
