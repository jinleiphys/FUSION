# Indications for the Nonexistence of Three-Neutron Resonances near the Physical Region

> The pending question of the existence of three-neutron resonances near the physical energy region is reconsidered. Finite rank neutron-neutron forces are used in Faddeev equations, which are analytically continued into the unphysical energy sheet below the positive real energy axis. The trajectories of the three-neutron S-matrix poles in the states of total angular momenta and parity J^π=1/2 +- and J^π= 3/2 +- are traced out as a function of artificial enhancement factors of the neutron-neutron forces. The final positions of the S-matrix poles removing the artificial factors are found in all cases to be far away from the positive real energy axis, which provides a strong indication for the nonexistence of nearby three-neutron resonances. The pole trajectories close to the threshold E=0 are also predicted out of auxiliary generated three-neutron bound state energies using the Padé method and agree very well with the directly calculated ones.

## Key claim

The paper provides a strong indication that three-neutron resonances do not exist near the physical energy region, but the claim is qualified by the use of simplified finite-rank neutron-neutron forces rather than the most modern high-precision nucleon-nucleon potentials with three-nucleon forces; the final S-matrix pole positions for all studied states (J^π = 1/2^± and 3/2^±) were found to be far away from the positive real energy axis after removing artificial enhancement factors.

## Method

The authors solved the Faddeev equations for three neutrons using finite-rank (rank 2) neutron-neutron forces in the partial wave states ^1S_0, ^3P_0, ^3P_2, and ^1D_2, with the ^3P_2-^3F_2 coupling neglected. They artificially enhanced the neutron-neutron force strengths to create three-neutron bound states, then analytically continued the Faddeev equations into the unphysical energy sheet below the positive real energy axis by deforming the integration path into the lower half plane, tracing the trajectories of the S-matrix poles as the enhancement factors were reduced to their physical values.

## Key numbers

- For J^π = 3/2^- with 10 channels, the final resonance position at physical force strength was -12.13 - i 37.96 MeV
- For J^π = 3/2^+ with 10 channels, the final resonance position at physical force strength was -10.30 - i 33.20 MeV
- For J^π = 1/2^- with 5 channels, the final resonance position at physical force strength was -20.38 - i 45.27 MeV
- For J^π = 1/2^+ with 6 channels, the final resonance position at physical force strength was -14.29 - i 48.34 MeV
- The enhancement factor needed for a 3n bound state at zero energy in the 3/2^- state was λ_{^3P_2} = 3.23 (10 channel calculation)
- The enhancement factor needed for a 3n bound state at zero energy in the 3/2^+ state was λ_{^3P_2} = 3.2339687436 and λ_{^3P_0} = 4.7339687436
- Including the repulsive ^3P_1 force shifted the 3/2^- final resonance by -1.79 - i 2.87 MeV and the 3/2^+ final resonance by -0.80 - i 0.42 MeV

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Sofianos et al. (1997) and Lazauskas and Carbonell (2002) that used more realistic forces but could not follow resonance trajectories to physical force strengths due to numerical instabilities. It also revisits the earlier finding by Gloeckle (1978) that used only a ^1S_0 Yamaguchi force, where three-neutron resonances appeared to approach the physical region; the present work shows that including additional partial wave components (P- and D-waves) pushes the final pole positions far from the real axis. The results contradict the claim by Pieper et al. (2001) of a possible 3/2^+ resonance at E = 14 - i 13 MeV using the Minnesota force and complex scaling. The paper enables future work using more realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials and three-nucleon forces to confirm or refine these findings.
