# On the Response Function Technique for Calculating the Random-Phase Approximation Correlation Energy

> We develop a scheme to exactly evaluate the correlation energy in the random-phase approximation, based on linear response theory. It is demonstrated that our formula is completely equivalent to a contour integral representation recently proposed by Donau et al. being numerically more efficient for realistic calculations. Numerical examples are presented for pairing correlations in rapidly rotating nuclei.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that their previously developed response-function method for calculating the RPA correlation energy (PairRMP 1999) is exactly equivalent to a contour integral representation proposed by Donau et al. (1999), but their approach is numerically more efficient because it uses an integration path along the imaginary axis where the integrand decays rapidly, rather than along the real axis where it oscillates.

## Method

The RPA correlation energy is expressed as an integral of the logarithm of the determinant of the RPA response matrix, which has logarithmic singularities at the RPA eigenfrequencies and unperturbed two-quasiparticle energies. By deforming the integration contour from the real axis to the imaginary axis, the authors obtain an expression involving the real part of the response function evaluated on the imaginary frequency axis, which converges rapidly and avoids the need for a finite imaginary part epsilon. The same technique is applied to calculate the non-energy-weighted sum rule for the RPA pairing gap.

## Key numbers

- For the nucleus 164Er, the static (mean-field) pairing gap Delta vanishes at a rotational frequency of hbar*omega_rot = 0.34 MeV (the pairing phase-transition point)
- The approximate calculations used finite values of Im(omega) = 100 keV and 200 keV for comparison with the exact (Im(omega) = 0) results
- A low-energy cutoff of hbar*omega_cut = 400 keV was used for the approximate calculations of the RPA pairing gap
- The g-band and s-band crossing occurs at hbar*omega_rot = 0.25 MeV, producing a cusp in the correlation energy

## Context

This work extends the response function technique for calculating RPA correlation energies developed by PairRMP (1999) and addresses a criticism by Donau et al. (1999) that the original method did not properly handle spurious Nambu-Goldstone modes. The authors show both methods are equivalent and provide a numerically superior integration scheme. The technique enables exact calculation of pairing correlations in rapidly rotating nuclei, including the RPA pairing gap and correlation energy, and provides a basis for analyzing the pairing phase transition in strongly rotating nuclei, complementing number-projection methods (RS 1965) that give smoother behavior at transition points.
