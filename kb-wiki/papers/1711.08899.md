# Exploring manifestation and nature of a dineutron in two-neutron emission using a dynamical dineutron model

> Emission of two neutrons or two protons in reactions and decays is often discussed in terms of "dineutron" or "diproton" emission. The discussion often leans intuitively on something described by Migdal-Watson approximation. In this work we propose a way to formalize situations of dineutron emission. It is demonstrated that properly formally defined dineutron emission may reveal properties which are drastically different from those traditionally expected, and properties which are actually observed in three-body decays.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a formally defined "dineutron emission" does not necessarily produce a single low-energy peak in the neutron-neutron energy distribution, contrary to common expectations based on the Migdal-Watson approximation; the actual correlation pattern depends critically on the internal quantum configuration (e.g., [s²], [p²], [d²]) and the geometry of the emitting source, not just on the final-state interaction.

## Method
The authors develop a Dynamic Dineutron Model (D2nM) that solves a three-body Schrödinger equation with purely outgoing wave boundary conditions and a complex energy, using the Hyperspherical Harmonics method and an iterative correction procedure based on an analytic Green's function. They isolate the effect of the neutron-neutron final-state interaction by constructing a short-range three-body potential that controls the dominant hyperspherical component (K=0, 2, or 4), thereby producing pure [s²], [p²], or [d²] configurations in the nuclear interior. The model is applied to the ground-state decay of ²⁶O, and results are compared with a static dineutron model (S2nM) and the Migdal-Watson approximation.

## Key numbers
- The neutron-neutron scattering length used in the potential is a = -18.7 fm, consistent with the experimental value a_S = 18.9 ± 0.4 fm.
- For the [s²] configuration in D2nM, the peak in the E_nn distribution stabilizes at 80–90 keV for total decay energies E_T ~ 500 keV, and requires E_T exceeding 5 MeV to push the peak above 100 keV.
- For the Migdal-Watson approximation, the peak in the n-n energy correlation spectrum is located at about 115–120 keV.
- In the trivial two-body model, the peak for emission off a static source lies at 70–100 keV for source rms radii b_nn = 3–7 fm.
- The condition for the S2nM results to coincide with the Migdal-Watson approximation is ⟨Y⟩ ≲ ⟨X⟩ ≲ 2⟨Y⟩, where ⟨X⟩ is the mean distance between neutrons and ⟨Y⟩ is the mean distance from the n-n center of mass to the core.
- For the [p²] configuration, a "antidineutron" peak at ε > 0.5 appears at E_T ~ 500 keV, and a dineutron low-ε peak arises at E_T ~ 1 MeV, with equal intensities at E_T > 2–3 MeV.
- For the [d²] configuration, no pronounced low-energy n-n peak is obtained in the whole considered E_T domain (up to at least several MeV).

## Context
This work extends the theoretical discussion of dineutron emission beyond the Migdal-Watson approximation (Watson 1952, Migdal 1955), which has been the standard tool for interpreting low-energy two-neutron spectra. It directly addresses the controversy surrounding the claimed discovery of dineutron emission in ¹⁶Be decay (Spyrou 2012, Marques 2012) and provides a framework to clarify the interplay between nuclear structure (pairing correlations) and final-state interactions. The results enable a more rigorous interpretation of two-neutron decay data from light exotic nuclei such as ²⁶O, and they caution against simplistic use of the dineutron concept in femtoscopy-like studies of neutron halos (Marques 2000, 2001, 2002).
