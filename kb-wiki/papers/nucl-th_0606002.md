# Muon capture on nuclei: random phase approximation evaluation versus data for 6 $\le$ Z $\le$ 94 nuclei

> We use the random phase approximation to systematically describe the total muon capture rates on all nuclei where they have been measured. We reproduce the experimental values on these nuclei to better than 15% accuracy using the free nucleon weak form factors and residual interactions with a mild $A$ dependency. The isospin dependence and the effects associated with shell closures are fairly well reproduced as well. However, the calculated rates for the same residual interactions would be significantly lower than the data if the in-medium quenching of the axial-vector coupling constant is employed to other than the true Gamow-Teller amplitudes. Our calculation thus suggests that no quenching is needed in the description of semileptonic weak processes involving higher multipole transitions and momentum transfer $\sim m_μ$, with obvious importance to analogous weak processes.

## Key claim

The authors use the random phase approximation (RPA) to systematically calculate total muon capture rates across all nuclei with measured data, reproducing experimental values to better than 15% accuracy. However, the calculated rates would be significantly lower than the data if in-medium quenching of the axial-vector coupling constant were applied to transitions other than the true Gamow-Teller amplitudes, suggesting that no quenching is needed for semileptonic weak processes involving higher multipole transitions and momentum transfer near the muon mass.

## Method

The authors employed the standard random phase approximation (SRPA) with a phenomenological Landau-Migdal residual interaction, using a spin-isospin coupling constant g' that varies with A^(1/3) from 0.7 in 16O to 0.96 in 208Pb. Single-particle states were obtained from a Woods-Saxon potential diagonalized in a harmonic oscillator basis, with the last proton and neutron levels fixed to experimental separation energies. For open-shell nuclei, BCS equations were solved to determine occupation numbers, and the muon wavefunction was treated fully relativistically by solving the Dirac equation in the finite-size nuclear field, including both large and small components of the Dirac bi-spinor for the first time.

## Key numbers

* Total muon capture rates were calculated for nuclei with atomic numbers 6 ≤ Z ≤ 94 and reproduced experimental values to better than 15% accuracy, with exceptions at Z=74 and Z=80.
* The spin-isospin coupling constant g' was interpolated as g' = c1 + c2 * A^(1/3), yielding g'=0.7 in 16O and g'=0.96 in 208Pb; the change in total capture rate between these values is less than 10%.
* For 12C, the calculated rate is 0.042 × 10^6 s^-1 versus the experimental 0.039 × 10^6 s^-1; for 208Pb, the calculated rate is 14.97 × 10^6 s^-1 versus the experimental 13.45 × 10^6 s^-1.
* The Gamow-Teller strength in lighter nuclei (N and Z less than 40) was quenched by a common factor of (0.8)^2 = 0.64 following Wildenthal (1983).
* The muon mass is m_μ = 105.6 MeV, and the mean excitation energy in muon capture is in the giant resonance region around 15 MeV, slowly decreasing with A or Z.

## Context

This work extends previous RPA calculations by Kolbe et al. (2000, 2001) that studied muon capture on selected nuclei (12C, 16O, 40Ca, 44,48Ca, 56Fe, 90Zr, 208Pb) and by Zinner et al. (2004) on calcium and tin isotope chains. It provides the first fully comprehensive theoretical evaluation of total muon capture rates over the entire range of nuclei with available experimental data, superseding earlier limited calculations by Fearing (1976) and the Hartree-Fock RPA approach of Auerbach and collaborators. The findings enable improved evaluation of semileptonic weak processes at similar momentum transfer, including supernova neutrino detection and nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double beta decay.
