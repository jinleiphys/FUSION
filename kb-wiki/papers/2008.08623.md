# Charge Conservation and Higher Moments of Charge Fluctuations

> Higher moments of distributions of net charge and baryon number in heavy-ion collisions have been proposed as signals of fundamental QCD phase transitions. In order to better understand background processes for these observables, models are presented which enable one to gauge the effects of local charge conservation, decays of resonances and clusters, Bose symmetrization, and volume fluctuations. Monte Carlo methods for generating samplings of particles consistent with local charge conservation are presented, and are followed by a review of simple analytic models involving a single type of charge with a constant experimental efficiency. The main model consists of thermal emission superimposed onto a simple parameterization of collective flow, known as a blast-wave, with emission being consistent with individual canonical ensembles. The spatial extent of local charge conservation is parameterized by the size and extent over which charge is conserved. The sensitivity of third and fourth order moments, skewness and kurtosis, to these parameters, and to beam energy and baryon density is explored. Comparisons with STAR data show that a significant part of the observed non-Poissonian fluctuations in net-proton fluctuations are explained by charge and baryon-number conservation, but that measurements of the STAR collaboration for fluctuations of net electric charge significantly differ from expectations of the models presented here.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that local charge conservation, resonance decays, and volume fluctuations can explain a significant part of the observed non-Poissonian fluctuations in net-proton distributions measured by STAR, but the same models fail to reproduce STAR's net electric charge fluctuation measurements, which differ qualitatively from model expectations.

## Method

The authors construct a blast-wave model where thermal emission occurs from independent sub-volumes (patches), each treated as a canonical ensemble with fixed baryon number, electric charge, and strangeness. They use recursive techniques to exactly calculate the canonical partition function for a multi-component hadron gas, then generate statistically independent Monte Carlo events by sampling particles from each patch according to thermal weights. The generated particles are subjected to resonance decays and filtered through the STAR detector acceptance, with Bose symmetrization effects included for pions.

## Key numbers

- For a hadron gas at temperature 150 MeV, the maximum number of hadrons A_max = 250 was sufficient for patch volumes less than approximately 700 fm^3
- The zero-momentum pion Bose factor at 150 MeV is e^{-m/T} ≈ 0.4, while for characteristic thermal momentum the factor is ≈ 0.1
- The integrated strength of the charge balance function for electric charge in the full STAR TPC acceptance is approximately 0.35
- For a Skellam (uncorrelated) distribution, the ratios C_3/C_1 = 1 and C_4/C_2 = 1
- For fixed charge Q and uniform efficiency α, the ratio C_3/C_1 = (1-α)(1-2α) and C_4/C_2 = 1 + 3α(1-α)(ω_M - 2), where ω_M is the relative variance of the base multiplicity distribution

## Context

This work extends earlier analytic models by Savchuk (2019) that treated a single type of charge with uniform efficiency, generalizing to three conserved charges (baryon number, electric charge, strangeness) with realistic detector acceptance. The patch method builds on prior charge balance function calculations by Schlichting (2010) and Pratt (2010), and the recursive partition function techniques extend methods used by Cheng (2002) for isospin fluctuations and Pratt (1999) for nuclear fragmentation. The results enable experimental searches for QCD critical point signals by providing a baseline for background contributions from charge conservation, though the discrepancy with STAR net charge data indicates either missing physics in the models or systematic issues in the measurements.
