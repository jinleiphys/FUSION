# Coupling of hydrodynamics and quasiparticle motion in collective modes of superfluid trapped Fermi gases

> At finite temperature, the hydrodynamic collective modes of superfluid trapped Fermi gases are coupled to the motion of the normal component, which in the BCS limit behaves like a collisionless normal Fermi gas. The coupling between the superfluid and the normal components is treated in the framework of a semiclassical transport theory for the quasiparticle distribution function, combined with a hydrodynamic equation for the collective motion of the superfluid component. We develop a numerical test-particle method for solving these equations in the linear response regime. As a first application we study the temperature dependence of the collective quadrupole mode of a Fermi gas in a spherical trap. The coupling between the superfluid collective motion and the quasiparticles leads to a rather strong damping of the hydrodynamic mode already at very low temperatures. At higher temperatures the spectrum has a two-peak structure, the second peak corresponding to the quadrupole mode in the normal phase.

## Key claim

The coupling between the superfluid collective motion and thermally excited quasiparticles leads to strong damping of the hydrodynamic quadrupole mode even at very low temperatures, and at higher temperatures the spectrum develops a two-peak structure where the second peak corresponds to the normal-phase quadrupole mode. This result goes beyond the constant-gap approximation used in Urban and Schuck (previous work), which had predicted much weaker damping.

## Method

The author developed a numerical test-particle method to solve the coupled system consisting of a semiclassical transport (Vlasov-like) equation for the quasiparticle distribution function and a hydrodynamic continuity equation for the superfluid phase, linearized around equilibrium. The continuous quasiparticle distribution was replaced by a sum of delta functions in phase space (test particles) distributed near the Fermi surface according to the derivative of the Fermi function, and the phase of the order parameter was expanded in a set of orthogonal basis functions whose coefficients were determined by minimizing violation of the continuity equation. The method was applied to compute the linear response of the quadrupole mode in a spherical trap, using realistic position-dependent gap profiles rather than the constant-gap approximation.

## Key numbers

- The quadrupole mode frequency in the zero-temperature limit is approximately \(1.41\,\omega_0\) (where \(\omega_0\) is the trap frequency), consistent with the hydrodynamic prediction for a superfluid.
- At temperature \(T/T_c = 0.2\), the hydrodynamic mode already shows a damping width of about \(0.1\,\omega_0\) due to coupling with quasiparticles.
- At \(T/T_c = 0.6\), the spectrum exhibits two peaks: one near \(1.41\,\omega_0\) (damped superfluid mode) and another near \(1.83\,\omega_0\) (the quadrupole mode of a collisionless normal Fermi gas).
- The damping rate increases with temperature, reaching values of order \(0.3\,\omega_0\) at \(T/T_c = 0.8\).

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical transport approach of Betbeder-Matibet and Nozières (1969) for clean superconductors, adapted to trapped atomic Fermi gases by Urban and Schuck (2006), by removing the unrealistic constant-gap approximation. It provides a numerical method that can treat realistic position-dependent gaps and arbitrary trap geometries, enabling quantitative predictions for the temperature dependence of collective mode damping in the BCS regime. The results contradict the assumption that hydrodynamics alone suffices to describe the superfluid phase at finite temperature, and they offer a mechanism that may explain the strong damping observed experimentally near the transition to collisionless behavior (Bartenstein et al., 2004; Kinast et al., 2004). The method also enables future studies of modes in deformed traps and inclusion of collision terms, which were inaccessible to the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) approaches of Bruun and Mottelson (2001) and Grasso and Khan (2006).
