# The Effect of Shear Viscosity on Spectra, Elliptic Flow, and HBT Radii

> I calculate the first correction to the thermal distribution function of an expanding gas due to shear viscosity. With this modified distribution function I estimate viscous corrections to spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii in hydrodynamic simulations of heavy ion collisions using the blast wave model. For reasonable values of the shear viscosity, viscous corrections become of order one when the transverse momentum of the particle is larger than 1.7 GeV. This places a bound on the $p_{T}$ range accessible to hydrodynamics for this observable. Shear corrections to elliptic flow cause $v_{2}(p_{T})$ to veer below the ideal results for $p_{T} \approx 0.9$ GeV. Shear corrections to the longitudinal HBT radius $R^{2}_{L}$ are large and negative. The reduction of $R_{L}^2$ can be traced to the reduction of the longitudinal pressure. Viscous corrections cause the longitudinal radius to deviate from the $\frac{1}{\sqrt{m_T}}$ scaling which is observed in the data and which is predicted by ideal hydrodynamics. The correction to the sideward radius $R^{2}_{S}$ is small. The correction to the outward radius $R^{2}_{O}$ is also negative and tends to make $R_{O}/R_{S} \approx 1$.

## Key claim

Shear viscosity causes the elliptic flow coefficient \(v_2(p_T)\) to fall below ideal hydrodynamic predictions for transverse momenta around 0.9 GeV, and viscous corrections to the thermal distribution function become of order one for particles with transverse momentum above 1.7 GeV, placing a bound on the \(p_T\) range where hydrodynamics can be applied to spectra.

## Method

The author calculates the first-order viscous correction to the thermal distribution function of an expanding gas, proportional to the shear viscosity and the spatial gradients of the flow field. This modified distribution is then used with the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription in a blast-wave model parameterization of the hydrodynamic fields (temperature, radial flow, and elliptic flow) to estimate viscous corrections to single-particle spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii for heavy ion collisions at RHIC.

## Key numbers

- Viscous corrections to the thermal distribution become of order one when the transverse momentum of the particle is larger than **1.7 GeV** (for \(\Gamma_s/\tau_o \approx 1/5\)).
- Shear corrections cause \(v_2(p_T)\) to veer below ideal results for \(p_T \approx \textbf{0.9 GeV}\).
- For a Bjorken expansion without transverse flow, with \(\Gamma_s/\tau_o \approx 1/5\) and \(T = \textbf{200 MeV}\), the ratio of viscous correction to ideal spectrum becomes of order one at \(p_T^{max} \approx \textbf{800 MeV}\).
- The sound attenuation length from perturbative QGP estimates is \(\Gamma_s/\tau \approx \textbf{0.18}\), from AdS/CFT is \(\Gamma_s/\tau \approx \textbf{0.11}\), and from the Gyulassy-Molnar transport model is \(\Gamma_s/\tau \approx \textbf{0.02 to 0.04}\).
- The blast-wave model parameters for central collisions: \(T_o = \textbf{160 MeV}\), \(R_o = \textbf{10 fm}\), \(\tau_o = \textbf{7.0 fm}\), \(u_o = \textbf{0.55}\).
- The blast-wave model parameters for non-central collisions (16-24% centrality): \(T_o = \textbf{160 MeV}\), \(R_o = \textbf{7.5 fm}\), \(\tau_o = \textbf{5.25 fm}\), \(u_o = \textbf{0.55}\), \(u_2 = \textbf{0.1}\).

## Context

This work extends the investigation of viscous effects in heavy ion collisions by Dumitru (2002) by calculating the effect of a modified thermal distribution function on spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii, rather than only on the equations of motion. It builds on ideal hydrodynamic simulations by Teaney, Lauret, and Shuryak (2001) and Kolb, Huovinen, Heinz, and Heiselberg (2001) which reproduced elliptic flow but failed to describe HBT radii and high-\(p_T\) spectra. The results delineate the boundaries of the hydrodynamic description and suggest that shear viscosity can explain the observed deviation of elliptic flow from ideal predictions above 1.5 GeV and the reduction of the longitudinal HBT radius relative to ideal hydrodynamics.
