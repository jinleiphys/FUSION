# Calculating n-Point Charge Correlations in Evolving Systems

> In dynamic systems, charge susceptibilities and local charge correlations change with time. These changes are accompanied by non-local correlations which spread diffusively with time and are constrained by local charge conservation. Assuming the local features of the correlation, which for a gas would be the correlation of charges within the same particle, are equilibrated, a diagrammatic formalism is presented for calculating the evolution of the associated non-local correlations. These provide correlations of $n$ density operators at different positions for arbitrary $n$. The techniques were developed with an eye towards relativistic heavy-ion collisions, and can account for correlations indexed by up, down and strange charges. Understanding the evolution of such correlations is crucial if one is to interpret measurements of charge fluctuations from the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider.

## Key claim

Assuming that the local (same-particle) features of charge correlations are equilibrated, the evolution of non-local (different-particle) correlations of arbitrary order \(n\) can be calculated using a diagrammatic formalism that generalizes the treatment of two-point functions to three-point and higher-order correlations, with the key condition that the local susceptibility \(\chi^{(n)}\) is known from chemical equilibrium.

## Method

The total \(n\)-point correlation function is split into contributions where charges reside on the same particle (local part, given by the equilibrium susceptibility \(\chi^{(n)}\)) and contributions where charges are on different particles (non-local part). For three-point correlations, the non-local part is further split into cases where two charges are on one particle and the third on another (\(C^{(2;1)}\)), and where all three are on different particles (\(C^{(1;1;1)}\)). The evolution of the non-local correlations is derived from local charge conservation using a co-moving derivative \(D_t\) that includes fluid expansion, with source terms arising from the time rate of change of the local susceptibilities. The formalism is extended to arbitrary \(n\) via a diagrammatic representation where vertices represent sources (e.g., \(-D_t\chi^{(n)}\)) and lines represent Green's functions for diffusive charge transport.

## Key numbers

* The transition from hadronic gas to quark-gluon plasma occurs at temperatures above approximately 160 MeV, with a smooth crossover in a narrow window \(150 \lesssim T \lesssim 180\) MeV.
* For temperatures below 150 MeV, lattice charge susceptibilities are consistent with a weakly interacting hadron gas; above 180 MeV, they are consistent with a weakly interacting gas of light up, down, and strange quarks.
* The source term for two-point correlations is given by \(S^{(2)}_{ab}(\bm{r}_{12},t) = -D_t \chi^{(2)}_{ab}(\bm{r}_{12},t)\), and the three-point source term includes contributions such as \(S^{(3)}_{abc}(\bm{r},t) = -D_t \chi^{(3)}_{abc}(\bm{r},t) - L^{(2)}_{ab,d} S^{(2)}_{cd}(\bm{r},t) - \dots\) (three additional terms).
* The vertex for generating two-point correlations is \(V^{(0\rightarrow 2)}_{ab}(\bm{r},t) = -D_t \chi^{(2)}_{ab}(\bm{r},t)\), and the vertex for generating three-point correlations is \(V^{(0\rightarrow 3)}_{abc}(\bm{r},t) = -D_t \chi^{(3)}_{abc}(\bm{r},t) - L^{(2)}_{ab,d} V^{(0\rightarrow 2)}_{cd}(\bm{r},t) - \dots\) (three additional terms).

## Context

This work extends prior treatments of two-point charge correlations in evolving systems (Pratt 2017, Pratt 2018) to three-point and higher-order correlations, which are needed to interpret measurements of higher-order charge fluctuations (e.g., \(\langle \delta Q^3 \rangle\), \(\langle \delta Q^4 \rangle\)) from the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider. The formalism assumes local chemical equilibrium and diffusive charge transport, and it enables the calculation of \(n\)-point correlations in coordinate space, which can later be projected into momentum space using techniques from Pratt (2011, 2015). The approach is designed to be combined with hydrodynamic models and can account for correlations indexed by up, down, and strange charges.
