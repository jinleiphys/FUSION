# Strong magnetic fields and pasta phases revisited

> In this work, we compute the structure and composition of the inner crust of a neutron star in the presence of a strong magnetic field, such as it can be found in magnetars. To determine the geometry and characteristics of the crust inhomogeneities, we consider the compressible liquid drop model, where surface and Coulomb terms are included in the variational equations, and we compare our results with previous calculations based on more approximate treatments. For the equation of state (EoS), we consider two non-linear relativistic mean-field models with different slopes of the symmetry energy, and we show that the extension of the inhomogeneous region inside the star core due to the magnetic field strongly depends on the behavior of the symmetry energy in the crustal EoS. Finally, we argue that the extended spinodal instability observed in previous calculations can be related to the presence of small amplitude density fluctuations in the magnetar outer core, rather than to a thicker solid crust. The compressible liquid drop model formalism, while in overall agreement with the previous calculations, leads to a systematic suppression of the metastable solutions, thus allowing a more precise estimation of the crust-core transition density and pressure, and therefore a better estimation of the crustal radius.

## Key claim

The compressible liquid drop model (CLD) systematically suppresses the metastable solutions that appear in the simpler coexistence phase (CP) approximation, allowing a more precise estimation of the crust-core transition density and pressure; the extended spinodal instability observed in previous calculations is likely due to small-amplitude density fluctuations in the magnetar outer core rather than to a thicker solid crust.

## Method

The authors employed the compressible liquid drop model (CLD) to compute the structure and composition of the inner crust of a neutron star under strong magnetic fields, explicitly including surface and Coulomb terms in the variational equations for the Wigner-Seitz cell. They compared these results with the simpler coexistence phase (CP) approximation used in previous works, using two non-linear relativistic mean-field (RMF) models (NL3 and NL3ωρ) that have different slopes of the symmetry energy at saturation (L = 118 MeV and L = 55 MeV, respectively). The calculations were performed for magnetized β-equilibrium matter at magnetic field strengths of B* = 5×10³ and B* = 10⁴ (corresponding to B = 2.2×10¹⁷ G and B = 4.4×10¹⁷ G), and the results were compared with dynamical spinodal calculations from the Vlasov approach.

## Key numbers

- NL3 model: symmetry energy slope L = 118 MeV; NL3ωρ model: L = 55 MeV (both models have saturation density ρ₀ = 0.148 fm⁻³ and incompressibility K = 270 MeV)
- Magnetic field values considered: B* = 5×10³ (B = 2.2×10¹⁷ G) and B* = 10⁴ (B = 4.4×10¹⁷ G), where B^c_e = 4.414×10¹³ G
- For NL3 at B=0: crust-core transition density ρ_cc = 0.06780 fm⁻³ (CLD); at B*=5×10³: ρ_cc = 0.07369 fm⁻³ (CLD); at B*=10⁴: ρ_cc = 0.08007 fm⁻³ (CLD)
- For NL3 at B*=5×10³: transition from solid to liquid region ρ_{1→2} = 0.06627 fm⁻³ (CLD); at B*=10⁴: ρ_{1→2} = 0.06421 fm⁻³ (CLD)
- For NL3ωρ at B=0: ρ_cc = 0.08751 fm⁻³ (CLD); at B*=5×10³: ρ_cc = 0.08682 fm⁻³ (CLD); at B*=10⁴: ρ_cc = 0.08568 fm⁻³ (CLD)
- In the extended region for NL3: density fluctuation (ρ_clus - ρ_gas)/(ρ_clus + ρ_gas) in the range 1.3-5.5%; proton density fluctuation in the range 21-57%

## Context

This work extends the coexistence phase calculations of Pais (2021) and Wang (2022) and the dynamical spinodal analysis of Fang (2016, 2017) by using the more self-consistent compressible liquid drop model, which includes surface and Coulomb terms directly in the variational equations. The results clarify an apparent contradiction between the Thomas-Fermi calculations of Bao (who found weak magnetic field effects on the crust-core transition) and the spinodal/CP calculations that predicted an extended crust; the CLD model shows that the "extended crust" is better interpreted as a slightly inhomogeneous liquid portion of the outer core with small-amplitude density fluctuations, rather than a thicker solid crust. The work also demonstrates that the effect of the magnetic field on the crust extension depends strongly on the symmetry energy slope L, with the stiffer NL3 (L=118 MeV) showing a qualitatively different behavior from the softer NL3ωρ (L=55 MeV).
