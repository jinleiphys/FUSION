# Magnetic dipole excitations in nuclei: elementary modes of nucleonic motion

> The nucleus is one of the most multi-faceted many-body systems in the universe. It exhibits a multitude of responses depending on the way one 'probes' it. With increasing technical advancements of beams at the various accelerators and of detection systems the nucleus has, over and over again, surprised us by expressing always new ways of 'organized' structures and layers of complexity. Nuclear magnetism is one of those fascinating faces of the atomic nucleus we discuss in the present review. We shall not just limit ourselves to presenting the by now very large data set that has been obtained in the last two decades using various probes, electromagnetic and hadronic alike and that presents ample evidence for a low-lying orbital scissors mode around 3 MeV, albeit fragmented over an energy interval of the order of 1.5 MeV, and higher-lying spin-flip strength in the energy region 5 - 9 MeV in deformed nuclei, nor to the presently discovered evidence for low-lying proton-neutron isovector quadrupole excitations in spherical nuclei. To the contrary, we put the experimental evidence in the perspectives of understanding the atomic nucleus and its various structures of well-organized modes of motion and thus enlarge our discussion to more general fermion and bosonic many-body systems.

## Key claim

This review argues that magnetic dipole excitations in nuclei reveal a rich spectrum of collective modes, including the low-lying orbital scissors mode around 3 MeV (fragmented over about 1.5 MeV) and higher-lying spin-flip strength in the 5–9 MeV region in deformed nuclei, as well as low-lying proton-neutron isovector quadrupole excitations in spherical nuclei, but the authors emphasize that the experimental evidence must be placed in the broader context of understanding organized nucleonic motion and extended to general fermion and bosonic many-body systems.

## Method

The paper is a comprehensive review of experimental and theoretical work on magnetic dipole (M1) excitations in nuclei, covering data from high-resolution inelastic electron scattering at the DALINAC and S-DALINAC, nuclear resonance fluorescence (NRF) with real photons at the DYNAMITRON accelerator and other facilities, and hadronic probes such as proton scattering and neutron-induced compound reactions. Theoretical frameworks discussed include the two-rotor model (TRM), the interacting boson model (IBM-2), the Quasi-Particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), and shell-model calculations, with comparisons to experimental form factors, branching ratios, and parity assignments.

## Key numbers

- The scissors mode in heavy deformed nuclei has a mean excitation energy scaling roughly as 66 δ A^{-1/3} MeV, placing its center of gravity at about 3 MeV in rare-earth nuclei.
- The total summed M1 transition strength from the ground state into J^π = 1^+ states in heavy deformed nuclei is Σ B(M1)↑ ≈ 3 μ_N^2, with the strongest individual state carrying about 1.5 μ_N^2.
- In the nuclear transition current, the orbital part dominates over the spin part with a typical ratio B_l(M1)/B_σ(M1) ≈ 10.
- The summed experimental M1 transition strength up to about 4 MeV varies quadratically with the quadrupole ground state deformation parameter.
- In the deformed nucleus ¹⁵⁶Gd, the first discovered scissors mode state at E_x = 3.075 MeV has a transition strength B(M1)↑ of about 1.5 μ_N^2.
- In ¹⁵⁶Gd, combined (e,e′) and (γ,γ′) experiments revealed five more weakly excited 1^+ states, bringing the total M1 strength to about 2.4 μ_N^2.
- In present-day NRF experiments, reduced transition strengths as low as B(M1)↑ ≈ 0.01 μ_N^2 at about 3 MeV can be detected.
- The scissors mode has been studied in 42 isotopes from Nd to Pt in the rare-earth region, plus in the actinide mass region.
- For the deformed nucleus ¹⁵⁰Nd, three strongly excited states near 3 MeV were directly and model-independently shown to have positive parity (J^π = 1^+).
- Branching ratio analysis of about 200 J=1 levels in rare-earth nuclei shows maxima at 0.5 (for K=1 states) and 2.0 (for K=0 states), consistent with Alaga rules.

## Context

This review extends and systematizes the experimental and theoretical work on magnetic dipole excitations that began with the theoretical predictions of the scissors mode by Lo Iudice and Palumbo (1978, 1979) in the two-rotor model and by Iachello (1981) and Dieperink (1983) in the interacting boson model (IBM-2), and its experimental discovery in ¹⁵⁶Gd by Richter, Bohle, and collaborators (1983, 1984). It builds on earlier reviews by Richter (1990, 1995), Kneissl (1996), and Lo Iudice (1997, 2000), and on the systematic data compilation by Enders (2005). The review enables future work by connecting nuclear scissors modes to analogous rotational-oscillatory motion in other many-body systems such as metallic clusters, elliptically deformed quantum dots, and trapped Bose-Einstein condensates, and by identifying open questions such as the missing strength in odd-mass nuclei and the need for better theoretical descriptions of fragmented transition strength.
