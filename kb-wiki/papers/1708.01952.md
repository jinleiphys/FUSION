# The anatomy of atomic nuclei: illuminating many-body wave functions through group-theoretical decomposition

> With modern computers we can compute nuclear many-body wave functions with an astounding number of component, $ > 10^{10}$. But, aside from reproducing and/or predicting experiments, what do we learn from vectors with tens of billions of components? One way to characterize wavefunctions is through irreducible representations of groups. I discuss briefly the history of group-theoretical characterization of nuclear wavefunctions, with an emphasis of using Lanczos-type methods to efficiently dissect arbitrary wavefunctions into group irreps. Although the resulting decompositions are often fragmented over many irreps, one nonetheless finds powerful patterns. First, group decompositions along rotational bands show coherent commonalities, supporting the picture of a shared "intrinsic shape;" this is also called \textit{quasi-dynamical symmetry}. Second, group decompositions for wave functions using both phenomenological and \textit{ab initio} forces are often very similar, despite vastly different origins and dimensionalities. Both of these results suggest a group theoretical decomposition can provide a robust "anatomy" of many nuclear wave functions. This in turn supports the idea of using symmetry-based many-body frameworks for calculations.

## Key claim

Group-theoretical decomposition of nuclear many-body wave functions reveals robust patterns, specifically, quasi-dynamical symmetry in rotational bands and striking similarity between phenomenological and *ab initio* calculations, even though the decompositions are often fragmented over many irreducible representations, suggesting that symmetry-based many-body frameworks can provide meaningful insight beyond merely reproducing experimental data.

## Method

The author uses the Lanczos algorithm to efficiently decompose nuclear wave functions into irreducible representations of groups (SU(2) for spin, SU(3) for quadrupole deformation, SU(4) for spin-isospin) by computing the fraction of each eigenstate in subspaces labeled by Casimir operator eigenvalues. Calculations are performed with the BIGSTICK configuration-interaction code, using both phenomenological interactions (Cohen-Kurath for the 0p shell, USDB for the 1s0d shell, GXPF1 for the 1p0f shell) and *ab initio* interactions from chiral effective field theory softened by similarity renormalization group, in no-core shell model spaces defined by harmonic oscillator frequency and Nmax truncation.

## Key numbers

- Largest configuration interaction calculations at the time of writing had dimensions of roughly 20 billion M-scheme basis states.
- A vector of 2 × 10^10 components requires 80 Gbytes of storage in single precision.
- M-scheme sparsity of 10^-6 would require roughly 5 Petabytes for nonzero matrix elements.
- One matrix-vector multiplication would require approximately 3 trillion floating-point operations.
- The ground state typically converges in 50-70 Lanczos iterations; the lowest ten states converge in less than 300 iterations.
- For 11B in the 0p shell, the phenomenological Cohen-Kurath calculation has a basis dimension of 62 M-scheme states; the NCSM calculation uses Nmax=6 with harmonic oscillator frequency ℏΩ=22 MeV.
- For 9Be, the NCSM calculation uses Nmax=6 with ℏΩ=20 MeV, dimension of 20 million states.
- For 20Ne, the NCSM calculation uses Nmax=4 with ℏΩ=16 MeV, dimension of 75 million basis states.
- The phenomenological USDB calculation for 20Ne in the 1s0d space has an M-scheme dimension of 640 states.

## Context

This work extends the long history of group-theoretical characterization of nuclear wave functions, from Wigner's SU(4) supermultiplet theory (Wigner, 1937) and Elliott's SU(3) model (Elliott, 1958) through the concept of quasi-dynamical symmetry (Rowe, 1999; Bahri, 2003). The Lanczos decomposition technique was originally introduced for strength functions (Caurier, 1990) and later adapted for symmetry-guided decompositions (Johnson, 2001; PhysRevC.91.034313). The results support using symmetry-adapted many-body frameworks for more efficient calculations and suggest that group-theoretical decomposition provides a robust "anatomy" of nuclear wave functions that is largely independent of the specific interaction or model space size.
