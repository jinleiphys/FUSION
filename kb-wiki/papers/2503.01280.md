# The nuclear ground-state properties and stellar electron emission rates of 76Fe, 78Ni, 80Zn, 126Ru, 128Pd and 130Cd using RMF and pn-QRPA models

> Our study consists of investigations of nuclear ground state properties and weak transition rates of even even waiting point nuclei. The calculation was performed for N = 50 and N = 82 nuclei. The Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model was used to explore the nuclear ground state properties of selected nuclei. The proton neutron quasi particle random phase (pnQRPA) model was used for the computation of allowed Gamow Teller (GT) and unique first forbidden (U1F) transitions of the selected waiting point nuclei. The RMF approach with different density dependent interactions, DD ME2 and DD PC1, was used to compute potential energy curves and surfaces, quadrupole moments, deformation parameters, binding energies, proton neutron separation energies, charge, and radii. The RMF computed deformation parameters were used in the pn QRPA model, as a free parameter, for the computation of GT and U1F weak transitions. We investigated three different sets of deformation parameter for the calculation of electron emission rates. The rates changed considerably with change in deformation parameter. We later investigated contribution of allowed GT and U1F rates and competition between positron capture and electron emission rates at high stellar temperatures. The computed positron capture rates were significant especially at low densities and high temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper claims that including unique first-forbidden (U1F) transitions alongside allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) transitions in pn-QRPA calculations significantly reduces the total calculated half-lives of waiting-point nuclei, and that the computed positron capture rates become significant at high stellar temperatures and low densities, but the actual agreement with experimental data depends on the choice of deformation parameter and the inclusion of both transition types.

## Method

The authors used the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with density-dependent DD-ME2 and DD-PC1 interactions to compute ground-state properties (binding energies, deformation parameters, charge radii, separation energies) of six even-even waiting-point nuclei with N=50 (76Fe, 78Ni, 80Zn) and N=82 (126Ru, 128Pd, 130Cd). The deformation parameters from RMF and from the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM) were then used as input to the proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (pn-QRPA) model with a deformed Nilsson potential basis to compute allowed Gamow-Teller and unique first-forbidden stellar electron emission and positron capture rates across a range of stellar temperatures and densities.

## Key numbers

- Maximum deviations of binding energies from experimental data for RMF[DD-ME2] are between 0.024 and 0.049 MeV, for RMF[DD-PC1] between 0.004 and 0.027 MeV, for RMF[NL3*] between 0.011 and 0.027 MeV, for HFB[Sly4] between 0.006 and 0.037 MeV, and for FRDM between 0.012 and 0.025 MeV.
- The maximum deviation between experimental data and RMF model results for one-neutron and one-proton separation energies is under 1 MeV.
- Pairing gap values were set to Δn = Δp = 12/√A MeV according to global systematics.
- At T9 = 30, the computed U1F positron capture rates contribute almost 90% for most waiting point isotones.
- The value of D in the positron capture rate formula was taken as 6143 s, and the ratio gA/gV was taken as -1.2694.

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of weak interaction rates for waiting-point nuclei by Klapdor et al. (1984), Staudt et al. (1989, 1990), and Hirsh et al. (1993), and builds on the deformed pn-QRPA approach developed by Nabi and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (1999, 2004). It specifically addresses the need for including first-forbidden transitions, following the efforts of Homma et al. (1996), Borzov (2006), and the large-scale shell model calculations of Zhi et al. (2013). The results enable more accurate r-process simulation codes by providing electron emission and positron capture rates for these closed-shell nuclei, with the authors noting that future work will extend to open-shell nuclei for complete r-process coverage.
