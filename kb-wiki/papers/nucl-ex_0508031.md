# Breakup of loosely bound nuclei as indirect method in nuclear astrophysics: 8B, 9C, 23Al

> We discuss the use of one-nucleon breakup reactions of loosely bound nuclei at intermediate energies as an indirect method in nuclear astrophysics. These are peripheral processes, therefore we can extract asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANC) from which reaction rates of astrophysical interest can be inferred. To show the usefulness of the method, three different cases are discussed. In the first, existing experimental data for the breakup of 8B at energies from 30 to 1000 MeV/u and of 9C at 285 MeV/u on light through heavy targets are analyzed. Glauber model calculations in the eikonal approximation and in the optical limit using different effective interactions give consistent, though slightly different results, showing the limits of the precision of the method. The results lead to the astrophysical factor S_17(0)=18.7+/-1.9 eVb for the key reaction for solar neutrino production 7Be(p,γ)8B. It is consistent with the values from other indirect methods and most direct measurements, but one. Breakup reactions can be measured with radioactive beams as weak as a few particles per second, and therefore can be used for cases where no direct measurements or other indirect methods for nuclear astrophysics can be applied. We discuss a proposed use of the breakup of the proton drip line nucleus 23Al to obtain spectroscopic information and the stellar reaction rate for 22Mg(p,γ)23Al.

## Key claim

One-nucleon breakup reactions of loosely bound nuclei at intermediate energies can be used as an indirect method to extract asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) and thereby astrophysical S-factors, but the precision of the method is limited to about 10% by the ability to compute absolute cross sections using different effective nucleon-nucleon interactions.

## Method

The authors analyze one-proton-removal (breakup) cross sections and momentum distributions for loosely bound projectiles incident on light through heavy targets at intermediate energies (30–1000 MeV/nucleon). They employ an extended Glauber model in the eikonal approximation and optical limit, using several effective nucleon-nucleon interactions (JLM, standard, Ray) to calculate breakup cross sections and extract ANCs from the peripheral part of the reaction. For the case of 23Al, they perform Glauber model calculations assuming either a 2s1/2 or 1d5/2 orbital for the ground state to predict cross sections and momentum distributions that can distinguish the spin-parity assignment.

## Key numbers

- For 8B breakup, the total ANC extracted from 31 determinations is C_tot^2(JLM) = 0.483 ± 0.050 fm^-1, leading to the astrophysical factor S_17(0) = 18.7 ± 1.9 eV·b for the reaction 7Be(p,γ)8B.
- For 9C breakup at 285 MeV/nucleon on C, Al, Sn, and Pb targets, the total ANC is C_p3/2^2 + C_p1/2^2 = 1.26 ± 0.13 fm^-1, leading to S_18(0) = 46 ± 6 eV·b for the reaction 8B(p,γ)9C.
- For 23Al, the predicted one-proton-removal cross sections on a 12C target at 60 MeV/nucleon are 97 mb for a 2s1/2 ground state and 42 mb for a 1d5/2 ground state, with corresponding momentum distribution widths (FWHM) of 60 MeV/c and 180 MeV/c, respectively.
- The proton separation energy of 23Al is S_p = 0.123(19) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposals (Trache 2001, 2002, 2004) to extract astrophysical S-factors from one-nucleon-removal reactions, using 8B as a benchmark case. The method offers an alternative to extracting ANCs from transfer reactions (Mukhamedzhanov 2001) and to direct radiative capture measurements, which are often impossible for unstable nuclei. The results for S_17(0) are consistent with values from Coulomb dissociation data (Davids 2003) and most direct measurements, but disagree with one direct measurement (Junghans 2003). For 23Al, the proposed experiment would resolve the spin-parity ambiguity of the ground state, which is critical for calculating the 22Mg(p,γ)23Al reaction rate in ONe novae, where current estimates (Wiescher 1988) may be too low by a factor of 30–50 if a level inversion exists as suggested by Cai (2002) and Zhang (2002).
