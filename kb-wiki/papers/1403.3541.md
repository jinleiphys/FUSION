# Hawking-Unruh Hadronization and Strangeness Production in High Energy Collisions

> The thermal multihadron production observed in different high energy collisions poses many basic problems: why do even elementary, $e^+e^-$ and hadron-hadron, collisions show thermal behaviour? Why is there in such interactions a suppression of strange particle production? Why does the strangeness suppression almost disappear in relativistic heavy ion collisions? Why in these collisions is the thermalization time less than $\simeq 0.5$ fm/c? We show that the recently proposed mechanism of thermal hadron production through Hawking-Unruh radiation can naturally answer the previous questions. Indeed, the interpretation of quark- antiquark pairs production, by the sequential string breaking, as tunneling through the event horizon of colour confinement leads to thermal behavior with a universal temperature, $T \simeq 170$ Mev,related to the quark acceleration, a, by $T=a/2π$. The resulting temperature depends on the quark mass and then on the content of the produced hadrons, causing a deviation from full equilibrium and hence a suppression of strange particle production in elementary collisions. In nucleus-nucleus collisions, where the quark density is much bigger, one has to introduce an average temperature (acceleration) which dilutes the quark mass effect and the strangeness suppression almost disappears.

# Hawking-Unruh Hadronization and Strangeness Production in High Energy Collisions

## Key claim

The thermal hadron production observed in high energy collisions arises from Hawking-Unruh radiation at the event horizon of color confinement, where the hadronization temperature depends on the quark mass; this mass dependence naturally produces strangeness suppression in elementary collisions, but in nucleus-nucleus collisions the high quark density forces an average temperature that dilutes the mass effect and restores near-equilibrium strangeness production.

## Method

The authors model string breaking in QCD as quark-antiquark pair production via tunneling through the event horizon of color confinement, analogous to Hawking-Unruh radiation. They compute the Unruh temperature T = a/2π from the quark acceleration a = σ/w_q, where σ is the string tension and w_q is the effective mass of the produced quark, then derive species-dependent hadronization temperatures for mesons and baryons based on their strangeness content. For heavy ion collisions, they introduce an average temperature obtained by weighting the accelerations of light and strange quarks by their respective abundances, and solve for self-consistency between the average temperature and the resulting hadron ratios.

## Key numbers

- Universal hadronization temperature for massless quarks: T(00) ≈ 0.178 GeV (178 MeV), from σ ≈ 0.2 GeV² using T = √(σ/2π)
- For a strange quark mass m_s = 0.100 GeV, the temperature for a strange meson (one light, one strange quark) is T(0s) ≈ 0.167 GeV (167 MeV), about 6% lower than the massless case
- For a φ meson (strange quark-antiquark pair) with m_s = 0.100 GeV, T(ss) ≈ 0.157 GeV (157 MeV), about 12% lower than the massless case
- The effective strangeness suppression factor γ_s derived from the temperature difference is γ_s ≈ 0.73 for σ = 0.2 GeV² and m_s = 0.1 GeV
- The self-consistent average temperature in a high-density system (heavy ion collisions) is T̄ ≈ 174 MeV for σ = 0.2 GeV² and m_s = 0.1 GeV
- The Wroblewski factor λ (ratio of strange to light quarks) is λ ≈ 0.26 in elementary collisions and increases to λ ≈ 0.33 when using the equilibrium temperature T̄ in a simplified model, compared to the experimental value λ ≈ 0.5 for nucleus-nucleus scattering

## Context

This work extends the Hawking-Unruh hadronization mechanism proposed by Castorina, Kharzeev, and Satz (2007) to explain both strangeness suppression in elementary e⁺e⁻ and hadron-hadron collisions and its near-disappearance in relativistic heavy ion collisions. The approach provides a dynamical alternative to the phenomenological strangeness suppression factor γ_s used in the statistical hadronization model of Becattini et al. (various years) and Rafelski (1980s). The mechanism also explains the extremely fast thermalization time (< 0.5 fm/c) observed in heavy ion collisions by replacing kinetic equilibration with a stochastic process inherent to quantum tunneling through color horizons. The next step is a full statistical model calculation incorporating all resonances to quantitatively reproduce the Wroblewski factor in nucleus-nucleus collisions.
