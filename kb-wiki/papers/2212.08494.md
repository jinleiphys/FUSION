# The proxy-SU(3) symmetry in atomic nuclei

> The microscopic origins and the up-to-now predictions of the proxy-SU(3) symmetry model of atomic nuclei are reviewed. Starting from the experimental evidence for the special role played by nucleon pairs with maximal spatial overlap, the proxy-SU(3) approximation scheme is introduced and its validity is demonstrated through Nilsson model calculations, as well as through its connection to the spherical shell model. The major role played by highest weight irreducible representations of SU(3) in shaping up the nuclear properties is pointed out, resulting in parameter-free predictions of the collective variables beta and gamma for even-even nuclei, in the explanation of the dominance of prolate over oblate shapes in the ground states of even-even nuclei, in the prediction of a shape/phase transition from prolate to oblate shapes below closed shells, as well as in the prediction of specific islands on the nuclear chart in which shape coexistence is confined. Further developments within the proxy-SU(3) scheme are outlined.

# The proxy-SU(3) symmetry in atomic nuclei

## Key claim

The proxy-SU(3) symmetry model reestablishes the SU(3) symmetry of the three-dimensional isotropic harmonic oscillator in medium-mass and heavy nuclei by replacing intruder orbitals with their Nilsson 0[110] counterparts, under the condition that the changes inflicted on the physical system are minimal and that the 11/2[505] Nilsson orbital (which has no 0[110] partner) lies at the very top of the shell and is therefore empty for most nuclei.

## Method

The proxy-SU(3) approximation replaces intruder orbitals (which have descended from the shell above due to the spin-orbit interaction) by their Nilsson 0[110] partners, which are the deserting orbitals that have fled to the shell below, based on the experimental observation that 0[110] Nilsson pairs maximize the proton-neutron interaction due to large spatial overlaps. The validity of this replacement was tested using Nilsson model calculations comparing matrix elements and single-particle energy levels with and without the proxy replacement, and was further corroborated by translating the Nilsson 0[110] replacements into the spherical shell model basis through unitary transformations connecting the cartesian Elliott basis to the j-j coupling basis.

## Key numbers

- The 0[110] Nilsson pairs correspond to spherical shell model |0 1 1 0⟩ pairs, which are identical to the de Shalit-Goldhaber pairs and the Federman-Pittel pairs identified in earlier work.
- For the collective variable β, the proxy-SU(3) predictions use the rescaling factor A/(Sπ+Sν) where Sπ (Sν) is the size of the proton (neutron) valence shell, with the constant r0 = 0.87 determined from a fit over a wide range of nuclei.
- The shape/phase transition from prolate to oblate shapes occurs at N = 116 for rare earths with valence protons in the 50-82 shell and valence neutrons in the 82-126 shell.
- The highest weight irreps and the highest C2 irreps are identical up to midshell, but differ in the upper half of each shell, with the exception of the last 5 particle numbers (corresponding to states with up to 4 holes at the top of the shell).

## Context

This review extends the Elliott SU(3) symmetry, which was previously applicable only to light nuclei in the sd shell, to medium-mass and heavy nuclei where the spin-orbit force breaks the SU(3) symmetry. The proxy-SU(3) approach is distinct from the pseudo-SU(3) symmetry introduced by Adler (1973) and the quasi-SU(3) symmetry introduced by Zuker (1995), though it is compatible with both; recent work by Cseh (2021) and Hess (2022) has shown that proxy-SU(3) and pseudo-SU(3) lead to the same physical conclusions within the semimicroscopic algebraic quartet model. The parameter-free predictions for β and γ enable comparisons with the D1S-Gogny interaction and relativistic mean field theory, and the predicted prolate-to-oblate shape/phase transition has been corroborated by experimental evidence from Namenson (1976), Alkhomashi (2009), and others, as well as by microscopic theoretical considerations from Kumar (1972) and Sarriguren (2007).
