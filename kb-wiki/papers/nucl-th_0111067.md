# Reliable calculations of level densities using statistical spectroscopy

> Nucleosynthesis calculations require nuclear level densities for hundreds or even thousands of nuclides. Ideally one would like to constrain these level densities by microscopically motivated yet computationally cheap models. A statistical approach suggests that low moments of the Hamiltonian might be sufficient. Recently Zuker proposed a simple combinatoric formula for level densities based upon the binomial distribution. We rigorously test the binomial formula against full scale shell-model diagonalization calculations for selected $sd$- and $pf$-shell nuclides and also against Monte Carlo path integration calculations. We find that the fourth moment is as important as the third moment to a good description of the level density, as well as partitioning of the model space into subspaces.

# Reliable calculations of level densities using statistical spectroscopy

## Key claim

The binomial distribution approximates shell-model level densities better than Gaussians, but the fourth moment of the Hamiltonian is as important as the third moment for a good description; partitioning the model space into configuration subspaces and using fourth-moment-scaled binomials (SUPARB-FMS) can reveal low-lying structure and achieve factor-of-two accuracy, though the standard binomial often overestimates the fourth moment.

## Method

The authors computed exact shell-model level densities via full diagonalization using OXBASH for selected sd-shell nuclides (with the Wildenthal USD interaction) and pf-shell nuclides 44,45Ti (with the Brown-Richter interaction), and for larger dimensions used Monte Carlo path-integral calculations with a schematic multipole-multipole interaction fitted to the T=1 matrix elements. They calculated exact spectroscopic moments up to fourth order using the formulas of Ayi (1974) and compared Gaussian, standard binomial, and fourth-moment-scaled (FMS) binomial distributions against the exact densities. They then partitioned the model space by single-particle configurations, computed configuration moments up to fourth order, modeled each partial density as a binomial (standard or FMS), and summed them to produce SUPARB (sum of partitioned binomials) level densities.

## Key numbers

- The scaled fourth moment m4 for most binomials and shell-model diagonalizations is typically around 2.8, compared to 3 for a Gaussian.
- A scaled third moment with magnitude >= 0.05 is regarded as a significant asymmetry.
- The maximum m3 allowed by the standard binomial for a dimension d=500 is 0.16; for exact m3 > 0.16 the standard binomial fails.
- Gaussians give a good estimate of the exact level density within a factor of 5.
- The SUPARB-FMS approach models the exact level density within a factor of 2.
- For 24Mg and 32S, the standard binomial systematically overestimates the configuration scaled fourth moments compared to exact values.

## Context

This work extends the statistical spectroscopy approach of French (1971) and Wong (1986), who argued that low moments of the Hamiltonian control nuclear properties, and the binomial distribution proposal of Zuker (1999). It improves upon earlier Gaussian-based models by Grimes (1979, 1983) and the Gram-Charlier expansions that can yield negative densities. The method enables microscopically motivated level densities for nucleosynthesis calculations, where previous empirical approaches such as Gilbert and Cameron (1965) had uncertainties beyond a factor of 10, and later works by Woosley (1978) and Thielemann (1987) had uncertainties of factors of 8 and 5 respectively. The approach can be generalized to spin-cutoff factors and nuclear heat capacities.
