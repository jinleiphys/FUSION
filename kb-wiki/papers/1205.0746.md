# Higher-order Brunnian structures and possible physical realizations

> We consider few-body bound state systems and provide precise definitions of Borromean and Brunnian systems. The initial concepts are more than a hundred years old and originated in mathematical knot-theory as purely geometric considerations. About thirty years ago they were generalized and applied to the binding of systems in nature. It now appears that recent generalization to higher order Brunnian structures may potentially be realized as laboratory made or naturally occurring systems. With the binding energy as measure, we discuss possibilities of physical realization in nuclei, cold atoms, and condensed matter systems. Appearance is not excluded. However, both the form and the strengths of the interactions must be rather special. The most promising subfields for present searches would be in cold atoms because of external control of effective interactions, or perhaps in condensed-matter systems with non-local interactions. In nuclei, it would only be by sheer luck due to a lack of tunability.

# Higher-order Brunnian structures and possible physical realizations

## Key claim
Higher-order Brunnian systems (specifically second-order structures built from nine particles arranged as three Borromean three-body clusters) may be realizable in physical systems, but only if both the form and strengths of the interactions are rather special; the most promising subfields for present searches are cold atoms (due to external control of effective interactions) or possibly condensed-matter systems with non-local interactions, while in nuclei such structures would appear only by sheer luck due to a lack of tunability.

## Method
The authors define Brunnian systems of order n and type B(n,k) in terms of binding energy rather than geometric linking, then construct a Hamiltonian for a nine-particle second-order Brunnian system with three species (a,b,c) and two-body interactions V_aa, V_bb, V_cc, V_ab, V_bc, V_ac. They analyze the conditions needed: (i) each three-body subsystem must be Borromean (bound as a three-body system with no two-body bound state), and (ii) the effective three-cluster system must itself be Borromean. They survey possible realizations in ultracold non-polar atomic gases, dipolar molecules in layered two-dimensional geometries, condensed-matter systems with non-local correlations, and nuclear cluster states.

## Key numbers
- The three Hoyle state threshold for a second-order Brunnian state lies at 52.06 MeV in 36Ar (9-alpha threshold).
- The three 9Be threshold is at 48.63 MeV in 27Mg.
- The three 6He threshold is at 27.86 MeV in 18C.
- The three 11Li threshold lies more than 50 MeV above the unbound ground state resonance in 33F.
- The Hoyle state in 12C is at 7.65 MeV excitation energy and is unbound.
- The 12Be system with a "two 6He structure" has a threshold of only 10.1 MeV.
- The only known four-body Brunnian system in nuclei is 10C (Cur08).

## Context
This work extends the concept of Borromean systems introduced in nuclear physics by Zhukov et al. (1992) and the mathematical generalization to higher-order Brunnian links by Baas (2009, 2012). It builds on the established understanding of Borromean halo nuclei such as 6He and 11Li (Jensen et al., 2004) and the Ikeda picture of alpha-cluster states (Ikeda, 1968; Oertzen et al., 2006). The paper enables future experimental searches in cold atomic gases using Feshbach resonances (Chin et al., 2010) and dipolar molecule setups (Lahaye et al., 2009; Ye et al., 2009), and suggests that condensed-matter systems with topological properties (Hasan and Kane, 2011; Zhang, 2012) may provide alternative venues.
