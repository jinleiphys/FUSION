# Precise determination of lattice phase shifts and mixing angles

> We introduce a general and accurate method for determining lattice phase shifts and mixing angles, which is applicable to arbitrary, non-cubic lattices. Our method combines angular momentum projection, spherical wall boundaries and an adjustable auxiliary potential. This allows us to construct radial lattice wave functions and to determine phase shifts at arbitrary energies. For coupled partial waves, we use a complex-valued auxiliary potential that breaks time-reversal invariance. We benchmark our method using a system of two spin-1/2 particles interacting through a finite-range potential with a strong tensor component. We are able to extract phase shifts and mixing angles for all angular momenta and energies, with precision greater than that of extant methods. We discuss a wide range of applications from nuclear lattice simulations to optical lattice experiments.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method for determining lattice phase shifts and mixing angles that is more precise than extant methods, applicable to arbitrary non-cubic lattices, and works for all angular momenta and energies; the key qualifier is that the method is benchmarked on a specific finite-range potential with a strong tensor component, and the precision claim is relative to the specific prior method of Borasoy et al. (2007).

## Method

The method combines angular momentum projection onto states of total angular momentum (J, J_z) using spherical harmonics and Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, with a hard spherical wall boundary condition at radius R_W to remove rotational symmetry breaking from the lattice. An adjustable real auxiliary Gaussian potential in the region between R_O and R_W allows continuous tuning of energy eigenvalues, while for coupled partial waves a complex-valued auxiliary potential that breaks time-reversal symmetry is added in region III to obtain two linearly independent solutions for the S-matrix. Phase shifts and mixing angles are extracted by fitting the radial wave function in the asymptotic region (between R_I and R_O) to spherical Bessel functions.

## Key numbers

- Lattice spacing a = 100 MeV^{-1} (π/a = 314 MeV), box size 35a, with R_I = 9.02a, R_O = 12.02a, R_W = 15.02a.
- For the benchmark system, the interaction potential uses C = -2.00 MeV and R_0 = 2.00 × 10^{-2} MeV^{-1}, with nucleon mass m_N ≡ 2μ = 938.92 MeV.
- The hard wall constant Λ = 10^8 MeV; for coupled channels, the complex auxiliary potential uses U_0 = 20.0 MeV with r_0 ≃ R_W.
- Lattice phase shifts and mixing angles agree with continuum results from threshold up to relative center-of-mass momentum p_CM ≡ k = 140 MeV for J ≤ 4 and S = 1.
- For anisotropic rectangular lattices, good agreement with continuum is found up to a_z ≃ 1.4a (with unit cell volume 100^3 MeV^{-3}), but breaks down when a_z becomes comparable to the interaction range.

## Context

This work extends the spherical wall method previously used by Borasoy et al. (2007) for two-nucleon scattering in lattice effective field theory, and by Carlson et al. (1984) for nuclear scattering, by adding angular momentum projection and an auxiliary potential to overcome limitations in precision and energy range. The method is designed to complement the adiabatic projection method of Pine et al. (2013) and Elhatisari et al. (2014) for nucleus-nucleus scattering, and is applicable to arbitrary lattice geometries relevant for optical lattice experiments emulating condensed matter systems and quantum field theories.
