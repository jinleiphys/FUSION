# Refractive Distortions of Two-Particle Correlations from Classical Trajectory Calculations

> Calculations of two-particle correlations usually assume particles interact only pair-wise after their final collisions with third bodies. By considering classical trajectories, we show that interactions with the mean field can alter the spatial dimensions of the outgoing phase-space-density profiles by tens of percent, consistent with more complicated quantum complications.

# Refractive Distortions of Two-Particle Correlations from Classical Trajectory Calculations

## Key claim

Interactions with the mean field after a particle's last collision can alter the spatial dimensions of the outgoing phase-space-density profiles by tens of percent, and these distortions can be accurately modeled using classical trajectory calculations rather than full quantum distorted-wave approximations, except at very low transverse momentum (below approximately 50 MeV/c) or when the mean field has a very sharp discontinuity.

## Method

The author calculates two-particle correlation functions by first computing the asymptotic phase-space density distribution using classical trajectories through a scalar mean field, then applying the standard relation between the correlation function and the spatial variance of the phase-space density. For validation, quantum calculations are performed by solving the Klein-Gordon equation for outgoing cylindrical partial waves in a complex optical potential with a Fermi-Dirac form, and the resulting sideward source-size distortions are compared directly to the classical trajectory results for a static cylindrical source. The classical approach is then extended to a more realistic expanding cylindrical geometry with boost-invariant longitudinal flow and radial collective expansion, where particles are emitted from a moving breakup surface with a temperature of 120 MeV and a collective transverse rapidity of 0.8.

## Key numbers

- The in-medium pion mass used in the static cylinder model is 50 MeV/c^2, with the vacuum mass being the free pion mass.
- For the static cylinder with radius R = 10 fm and diffuseness a = 3 fm, classical and quantum calculations of the sideward dimension ratio agree within 1-2% for transverse momentum p_t > 100 MeV/c, and within 10% even at p_t = 40 MeV/c.
- When the diffuseness parameter is reduced to a = 0.5 fm, discrepancies between classical and quantum calculations increase to a few percent for p_t > 100 MeV/c and become notably higher near p_t = 50 MeV/c.
- For the expanding cylindrical geometry with the breakup surface moving outward at rapidity y_b = 0.4, radius R = 9 fm, and time tau = 9 fm/c, the distortions to R_side and R_long are significantly larger than for the static case, with the sideward distortion exceeding tens of percent at p_t near 100 MeV/c.
- For the static case at maximum expansion (y_b = 0, R = 12 fm, tau = 12 fm/c), the sideward distortion closely mimics the static cylinder results, while longitudinal distortion is small.
- For the collapsing case (y_b = -0.4, R = 9 fm, tau = 15 fm/c), distortions are smaller than for the expanding or static cases.
- The escape probability for particles from the static cylinder with r_0 = 5 fm is small at low p_t, rising with increasing p_t.

## Context

This work extends the quantum distorted-wave calculations of Cramer (2004) and Miller (2005), who showed that refractive effects from a time-independent optical potential could distort extracted source dimensions by a few tens of percent, and the Coulomb mean-field calculations of Barz (1996, 1998) which explained different apparent source sizes for positive and negative pions at AGS energies. The paper demonstrates that classical trajectory calculations are a viable and simpler alternative to quantum calculations for modeling these distortions, enabling future incorporation of time-varying potentials, non-spherical geometries, and three-body effects that are difficult to handle quantum mechanically. The results underscore the importance of including mean-field effects in hadronic Boltzmann descriptions of the breakup stage, particularly given that the pion dispersion relation may be modified by several tens of MeV at breakup densities near normal nuclear density (approximately 0.15 hadrons/fm^3).
