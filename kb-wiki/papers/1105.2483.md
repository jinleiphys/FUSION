# Shear viscosity, Bulk viscosity and Relaxation Times of Causal Dissipative Relativistic Fluid-Dynamics at Finite Temperature and Chemical Potential

> The microscopic formulas for the shear viscosity $η$, the bulk viscosity $ζ$, and the corresponding relaxation times $τ_π$ and $τ_Π$ of causal dissipative relativistic fluid-dynamics are obtained at finite temperature and chemical potential by using the projection operator method. The non-triviality of the finite chemical potential calculation is attributed to the arbitrariness of the operator definition for the bulk viscous pressure.We show that, when the operator definition for the bulk viscous pressure $Π$ is appropriately chosen, the leading-order result of the ratio, $ζ$ over $τ_Π$, coincides with the same ratio obtained at vanishing chemical potential. We further discuss the physical meaning of the time-convolutionless (TCL) approximation to the memory function, which is adopted to derive the main formulas. We show that the TCL approximation violates the time reversal symmetry appropriately and leads results consistent with the quantum master equation obtained by van Hove. Furthermore, this approximation can reproduce an exact relation for transport coefficients obtained by using the f-sum rule derived by Kadanoff and Martin. Our approach can reproduce also the result in Baier et al.(2008) Ref. \cite{con} by taking into account the next-order correction to the TCL approximation, although this correction causes several problems.

# Shear viscosity, Bulk viscosity and Relaxation Times of Causal Dissipative Relativistic Fluid-Dynamics at Finite Temperature and Chemical Potential

## Key claim

The microscopic formulas for shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, and their corresponding relaxation times in causal dissipative relativistic fluid-dynamics can be extended to finite chemical potential using the projection operator method, provided the bulk viscous pressure operator is defined with an additional term proportional to the number density fluctuation. When this appropriate operator definition is chosen, the leading-order ratio of bulk viscosity to its relaxation time at finite chemical potential coincides with the same ratio obtained at vanishing chemical potential.

## Method

The authors applied the Mori projection operator method to derive microscopic expressions for transport coefficients, using Kubo's canonical correlation as the inner product with the statistical ensemble defined by K = H - μN. They employed the time-convolutionless (TCL) approximation to the memory function to violate time reversal symmetry, and demonstrated that this approximation reproduces the van Hove quantum master equation and satisfies the f-sum rule derived by Kadanoff and Martin. The microscopic formulas were then evaluated at leading order using a non-interacting charged scalar boson model with the improved energy-momentum tensor.

## Key numbers

- For a charged scalar boson with pion mass m = 140 MeV, the η-τπ ratio η/[τπ(ε+P)] equals P/(ε+P), converging to 0.25 for massless bosons at high temperature.
- The η-τπ ratio monotonically increases with temperature and decreases slowly as chemical potential grows, as shown for μ = 50, 100, and 130 MeV.
- The ζ-τΠ ratio ζ/[τΠ(ε+P)] equals [(1/3 - c_s²)(ε+P) - (2/9)(ε-3P)]/(ε+P), which vanishes in the massless limit.
- The ratio D/τD for the generalized diffusion equation satisfies D/τD = ΩD, where ΩD = ∫d³x(J(x,0),J(0,0)) / ∫d³x(δn(x,0),δn(0,0)).

## Context

This work extends the projection operator approach previously developed at vanishing chemical potential by the same group (Koide et al., 2009; Huang et al., 2010) to include finite chemical potential, which introduces non-trivial complications due to the arbitrariness of the bulk viscous pressure operator definition. The results are consistent with the 14-moment approximation of the Boltzmann equation at vanishing chemical potential as shown by Denicol et al. (2010), and the approach can reproduce the results of Baier et al. (2008) by including next-order corrections to the TCL approximation, though the authors argue those corrections cause several problems including inconsistency with the f-sum rule. The derived formulas enable calculation of transport coefficients for causal dissipative hydrodynamics at finite density, relevant for heavy-ion collisions and neutron star mergers.
