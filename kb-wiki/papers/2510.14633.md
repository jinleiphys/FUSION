# Effect of Isolation Criteria on Prompt Photon Production in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> Prompt photon measurements in relativistic nuclear collisions serve as an essential comparative basis for heavy ion studies enabling the separation of medium induced effects. However, the identification of prompt photons is experimentally challenging due to substantial backgrounds from photons produced in hadron decays and jet fragmentation. Appropriate isolation criteria are applied to suppress these background contributions. We analyze prompt photon spectra using the JETPHOX framework to quantify the relative contributions of fragmentation and direct production mechanisms to the total photon yield. We perform a systematic study of the impact of isolation criteria on prompt photon production in relativistic nuclear collisions with emphasis on their dependence on beam energy and photon transverse momentum. The fragmentation contribution is found to be substantially large particularly for $p_T < 15$ GeV and the isolation criterion plays a crucial role in the analysis of prompt photons in that $p_T$ region. A dynamical isolation criterion suppresses the fragmentation component more effectively than a fixed one in this region. Furthermore, the isolation criterion shows a stronger dependence on beam energy and photon $p_T$ than on system size. These observations emphasize the importance of employing carefully selected and consistent isolation criteria when comparing experimental data with theoretical calculations especially for observables sensitive to fragmentation.

# Effect of Isolation Criteria on Prompt Photon Production in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

## Key claim
The fragmentation contribution to prompt photon production is substantially large for photon transverse momentum below 15 GeV, and the choice of isolation criterion (fixed versus dynamical) significantly affects the relative contributions of direct and fragmentation components. The isolation criterion shows a stronger dependence on beam energy and photon transverse momentum than on collision system size, meaning that comparisons between experimental data and theoretical calculations require carefully matched isolation criteria.

## Method
The authors used the JETPHOX framework, a parton-level Monte Carlo event generator implementing next-to-leading order perturbative QCD, to compute prompt photon production cross sections in proton-proton and heavy ion collisions. They systematically varied the isolation criterion between a fixed transverse momentum threshold of 2 GeV and a dynamical threshold of 0.1 times the photon transverse momentum, both applied within a cone radius of 0.4 in the pseudorapidity-azimuth plane, and compared the resulting direct and fragmentation contributions across beam energies from 200 GeV to 13 TeV and collision systems including pp, Au+Au, Pb+Pb, O+O, and Ru+Ru.

## Key numbers
- The fragmentation contribution is substantially large for photon transverse momentum below 15 GeV, with the direct component contributing about 60-90% of the yield in the range 4 to 15 GeV for 13 TeV pp collisions with the fixed isolation cut of 2 GeV.
- At 13 TeV pp collisions, the fragmentation contribution with the fixed isolation criterion of 2 GeV is about 2.5-3 times larger than that with the dynamical criterion of 0.1 times photon transverse momentum at photon transverse momentum of 4 GeV.
- For 200 GeV pp collisions, the fragmentation contribution shows a factor of 2-2.5 difference between the two isolation criteria at 4 GeV photon transverse momentum.
- For Pb+Pb collisions at 5.02 TeV per nucleon, the difference between the two isolation criteria amounts to roughly a factor of 3 at photon transverse momentum of 4 GeV.
- For Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV per nucleon, the difference between the two isolation criteria is about a factor of 2.5 at photon transverse momentum of 4 GeV.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of prompt photon production using the JETPHOX framework (Aurenche et al.) and addresses the need for systematic comparison between theoretical calculations and experimental data from ALICE, CMS, and PHENIX collaborations. The results are relevant to the "direct photon puzzle" regarding azimuthal anisotropy, as fragmentation photons contribute to elliptic flow (Chatterjee et al., 2013) while direct photons do not, and the findings enable more precise interpretation of the nuclear modification factor R_AA for photons and the separation of thermal photon contributions from the quark-gluon plasma.
