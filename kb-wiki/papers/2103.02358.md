# Pseudo spin doublet bands and Gallagher Moszkowski doublet bands in $^{100}$Y

> New transitions in neutron rich $^{100}$Y have been identified in a $^9$Be+$^{238}$U experiment with mass- and Z- gates to provide full fragment identification. These transitions and high spin levels of $^{100}$Y have been investigated by analyzing the high statistics $纬$-$纬$-$纬$ and $纬$-$纬$-$纬$-$纬$ coincidence data from the spontaneous fission of $^{252}$Cf at the Gammasphere detector array. Two new bands, 14 new levels and 23 new transitions have been identified. The $K^蟺=4^+$ new band decaying to an 1s isomeric state is assigned to be the high-$K$ Gallagher-Moszkowski (GM) partner of the known $K^蟺=1^+$ band, with the $蟺5/2[522] \otimes 谓3/2[411]$ configuration. This 4$^+$ band is also proposed to be the pseudo spin partner of the new $K^蟺=5^+$ band with a 5$^{+}$ $蟺5/2[422] \otimes 谓5/2[413]$ configuration, to form a $蟺5/2[422] \otimes 谓[312$ $5/2,3/2]$ neutron pseudospin doublet. Constrained triaxial covariant density functional theory and quantal particle rotor model calculations have been applied to interpret the band structure and available electromagnetic transition probabilities and are found in good agreement with experimental values.

# Pseudo spin doublet bands and Gallagher Moszkowski doublet bands in $^{100}$Y

## Key claim

The paper claims that the newly identified $K^\pi=4^+$ band in $^{100}$Y is simultaneously the high-$K$ Gallagher-Moszkowski (GM) partner of the known $K^\pi=1^+$ band and the pseudo spin partner of the new $K^\pi=5^+$ band, but this assignment depends on the specific Nilsson configurations ($\pi 5/2[522] \otimes \nu 3/2[411]$ for the GM doublet and $\pi 5/2[422] \otimes \nu 5/2[413]$ for the pseudo spin doublet) and is supported by theoretical calculations that assume a large prolate deformation with no triaxiality.

## Method

The authors identified new $\gamma$-ray transitions in $^{100}$Y using two complementary experimental approaches: (i) a $^9$Be+$^{238}$U reaction in inverse kinematics at GANIL with mass and charge identification via the VAMOS++ spectrometer and EXOGAM detector array, and (ii) high-fold $\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$ and $\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$-$\gamma$ coincidence analysis of $^{252}$Cf spontaneous fission data measured with the Gammasphere detector array. Constrained triaxial covariant density functional theory (CDFT) calculations with the PC-PK1 functional and quantal particle rotor model (PRM) calculations were performed to interpret the band structures and electromagnetic transition probabilities.

## Key numbers

- The ground state minimum of $^{100}$Y from CDFT calculations is at $\beta_2 = 0.45$, $\gamma = 0^\circ$, corresponding to a large prolate shape.
- A local minimum exists at ($\beta_2 = 0.32$, $\gamma = 60^\circ$) but lies about 1.0 MeV above the ground state, much larger than the bandhead energy of band (3) at 334 keV.
- The moments of inertia for all three bands are close to rigid body, with the PRM calculation using $\mathcal{J} = 20.0~\hbar^2/\text{MeV}$.
- The $B(M1)$ value for the $2^+$ state in band (1) is about $1.2~\mu_N^2$, in agreement with the experimental value from $\beta$ decay.
- Bands (1) and (2) are separated by a nearly constant energy of about 150 keV over the whole spin region.
- Bands (2) and (3) have energy differences within 20 keV.

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron-rich nuclei in the $A\sim 100$ mass region, where a sudden onset of large ground-state deformation occurs for $N \geq 60$ in Sr, Y, and Zr isotopes (Hamilton 1995). The paper builds on prior identification of the $1^+$ $\pi 5/2[422] \otimes \nu 3/2[411]$ band in $^{100}$Y by Wohr and Hwang (1998) and on laser spectroscopy measurements of deformation by Cheal and Baczynska. The observation of both GM doublet and pseudo spin doublet bands in the same nucleus provides experimental evidence for concepts previously reported in neighboring nuclei such as $^{98}$Sr, $^{99}$Y (Li, Meyer, Luo 2005), and $^{108}$Tc (Xu). The results enable future angular distribution and polarization asymmetry measurements to confirm the predicted $E2$ dominance in $\Delta I=1$ transitions of bands (2) and (3).
