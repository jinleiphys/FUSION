# Strong and Electromagnetic Interactions

> Elements of a new model of strong interactions are described. The model is based on an extension of electrodynamics that is derived from a regular Lagrangian density. Here the electric and magnetic fields of Maxwell equations play a symmetric role. It is shown that results are in accordance with general properties of nuclear and nucleon systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that strong interactions arise from magnetic monopole charges carried by quarks (treated as dyons), based on a new classical electrodynamics of charges and monopoles derived from a regular Lagrangian density; the model is presented as a qualitative first attempt that is consistent with known properties of hadrons and nuclei, but the author explicitly states that detailed quantitative calculations are not yet performed.

## Method

The author constructs a classical field theory of electric charges and magnetic monopoles from a Lagrangian density that treats electric and magnetic fields symmetrically, yielding equations of motion where charges do not interact directly with monopole fields but only indirectly through real photons. This framework is then applied to hadronic systems by identifying quarks as dyons (particles with both electric and magnetic charge), postulating that baryons have a core with magnetic charge +3g₀ to which three valence quarks of magnetic charge -g₀ are attracted, and examining qualitative consequences for nucleon structure, nuclear forces, and scattering experiments.

## Key numbers

- The elementary magnetic charge is given by g₀² = 1/4e² ≈ 34 in units where ℏ=c=1, but the author notes this value is not imposed on calculations and the theory leaves g₀ as a free parameter
- The neutron/proton structure function ratio is described by F₂^{en}/F₂^{ep} = 0.96 - 0.75x
- Typical nuclear binding energy is about 8 MeV per nucleon, while excited states of nucleons and mesons are measured in hundreds of MeV
- Molecular binding energies are a fraction of an eV, while excited states of electrons are of order 10 eV

## Context

This work extends earlier proposals by Dirac (1948), Schwinger (1968-1969), and Sawada (1973-1984) that quarks may be dyons and that strong interactions are associated with magnetic monopoles. The author's specific contribution is a new classical charge-monopole theory (Comay, 1984) that removes the restriction on the strength of the elementary magnetic charge and predicts that charges do not interact with monopole fields. The paper aims to show this framework can qualitatively explain the EMC effect (Aubert et al., 1983; Bodek et al., 1983), the nuclear tensor force, the similarity between nuclear and van der Waals forces, and the absence of exotic strongly-bound hadron states, while also resolving the apparent contradiction with the measured upper bound on the neutron's electric dipole moment.
