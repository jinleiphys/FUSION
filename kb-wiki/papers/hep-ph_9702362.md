# How pi0 -> gamma gamma changes with temperature

> At zero temperature, in the chiral limit the amplitude for pi0 to decay into two photons is directly related to the coefficient of the axial anomaly. At any nonzero temperature, this direct relationship is lost: while the coefficient of the axial anomaly is independent of temperature, in a thermal bath the anomalous Ward identities do not uniquely constrain the amplitude for pi0 -> gamma gamma. Explicit calculation shows that to lowest order about zero temperature, this amplitude decreases.

## Key claim

At any nonzero temperature, the direct relationship between the amplitude for π⁰ → γγ and the coefficient of the axial anomaly is lost; while the anomaly coefficient itself remains temperature-independent, the anomalous Ward identities do not uniquely constrain the decay amplitude, and explicit calculation to lowest order about zero temperature shows the amplitude decreases rather than increases.

## Method

The authors compute the π⁰ → γγ amplitude using a gauged nonlinear sigma model that incorporates anomalies via the Wess-Zumino-Witten term, working with two flavors in the chiral limit (m_π = 0). They evaluate one-loop diagrams at zero temperature and then extend the calculation to low temperature (T ≪ f_π) using the imaginary time formalism, retaining only the leading T²/f_π² corrections. They also perform a general analysis of the anomalous Ward identity and the Sutherland-Veltman theorem at nonzero temperature, and verify the Adler-Bardeen theorem by computing the correlator between one axial and two vector currents to one-loop order.

## Key numbers

- f_π ≈ 93 MeV is the pion decay constant at zero temperature
- The temperature-dependent pion decay constant to leading order: f_π(T) = (1 − 1/12 × T²/f_π²) f_π
- The π⁰ → γγ coupling at low temperature to order T²/f_π²: g_{πγγ}(T) = (1 − 1/12 × T²/f_π²) g_{πγγ}
- N_c = 3 is the number of colors in the anomaly coefficient e²N_c/(12π²)
- The thermal tadpole integral gives ⟨π²⟩_T = T²/12

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the axial anomaly and π⁰ decay to finite temperature, contradicting the natural supposition (Estrada 1990, Estrada 1991) that the amplitude should be temperature-independent because the anomaly itself is not. The authors show that the Sutherland-Veltman theorem, which at zero temperature forces the relation between the amplitude and the anomaly, fails at any nonzero temperature due to the preferred rest frame of the heat bath, following an analysis by Itoyama and Mueller (1983). The results enable further study of how anomalous processes behave in thermal environments and clarify why the Adler-Bardeen theorem remains valid even when the direct connection to the decay amplitude is lost.
