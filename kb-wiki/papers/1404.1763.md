# Anisotropic flow in transport+hydrodynamics hybrid approaches

> This contribution to the focus issue covers anisotropic flow in hybrid approaches. The historical development of hybrid approaches and their impact on the interpretation of flow measurements is reviewed. The major ingredients of a hybrid approach and the transition criteria between transport and hydrodynamics are discussed. The results for anisotropic flow in (event-by-event) hybrid approaches are presented. Some hybrid approaches rely on hadronic transport for the late stages for the reaction (so called afterburner) and others employ transport approaches for the early non equilibrium evolution. In addition, there are 'full' hybrid calculations where a fluid evolution is dynamically embedded in a transport simulation. After demonstrating the success of hybrid approaches at high RHIC and LHC energies, existing hybrid caluclations for collective flow observables at lower beam energies are discussed and remaining challenges outlined.

## Key claim

Hybrid approaches that combine hydrodynamics with hadronic transport have become the "standard model" for describing high-energy heavy-ion collisions, but the hadronic rescattering stage contributes more to the suppression of elliptic flow than the value of shear viscosity in the quark-gluon plasma phase, making it essential to include transport for quantitative comparisons to experimental data.

## Method

The paper reviews hybrid approaches that match a hydrodynamic evolution (ideal or viscous, 2+1D or 3+1D) to a hadronic transport cascade (UrQMD, JAM, or RQMD) via a Cooper-Frye particlization hypersurface at a switching temperature typically around 160-165 MeV. Some approaches also use transport for the early non-equilibrium stage (NeXus, UrQMD, AMPT) to generate fluctuating initial conditions for the fluid evolution, while "full" hybrid approaches embed hydrodynamics between transport stages for both early and late phases.

## Key numbers

* Elliptic flow suppression ratio v2^supp. = (v2^A - v2^B)/v2^A: the contribution of hadronic rescattering (ideal hydro + UrQMD vs. pure ideal hydro) is larger than the contribution of minimal QGP shear viscosity (eta/s = 0.08) at RHIC energies, as shown in Fig. 6 of the paper.
* Switching temperature of 160 MeV was used in the first hybrid approach (Bass 2000) for the transition from fluid dynamics to microscopic description.
* Starting time t_start around 0.5 fm and Gaussian kernel width sigma around 1 fm are necessary to simultaneously describe pion multiplicity and elliptic flow in Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV.
* Elliptic flow of protons is increased by roughly 30% during the hadronic stage of the reaction at RHIC energies (Hirano 2007).
* Negative contributions to particle spectra at midrapidity from the Cooper-Frye sampling are rather small (5% or even smaller), but may increase at forward rapidities and with event-by-event fluctuations.

## Context

This review extends the pioneering hybrid approach work of Bass (2000) and Teaney (2000), who first combined hydrodynamics with hadronic transport to improve upon pure hydrodynamic calculations with sudden Cooper-Frye freeze-out. It covers subsequent developments including the 3+1D hybrid of Nonaka (2006) matching to UrQMD, the Hirano (2005, 2007) approach with JAM transport, and the viscous hybrid VISHNU of Song (2010-2013). The paper enables future work on extracting transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma more quantitatively, particularly through 3+1D viscous hybrid simulations that can address longitudinal dynamics and the equilibration process using dynamical Yang-Mills simulations as reviewed by Luzum (2013).
