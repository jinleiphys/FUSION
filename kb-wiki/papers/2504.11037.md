# Statistical complexity as a probe of mass and phase structure in compact objects

> In this work, we present a comprehensive and systematic study of the statistical complexity, originally introduced by López-Ruiz, Mancini, and Calbet [Phys. Lett. A 209, 321-326 (1995)], across a broad range of compact star models. We explore how complexity correlates not only with macroscopic observables such as mass and radius, but also with the microscopic characteristics of the underlying equation of state. By incorporating both realistic equations of state and analytical solutions to Einstein's field equations, we demonstrate that gravitational mass plays a dominant role in determining the behavior of complexity. Furthermore, we show that strong phase transitions within the stellar interior, such as those hypothesized in hybrid stars, can manifest as distinct features in the complexity profile, offering a potential informational signature of such transitions. This work offers new insights into the link between information theory and compact object physics, highlighting complexity's potential as a diagnostic tool in astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that statistical complexity (SC), defined via the López-Ruiz-Mancini-Calbet formalism, correlates strongly with gravitational mass across a broad range of compact object models, and that strong phase transitions in hybrid stars produce distinct features in the SC profile, but this correlation is conditional on the specific form of the mass-radius relation dictated by the equation of state (EoS), with the SC maximum not universally coinciding with the stability point.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for hydrostatic equilibrium using a wide set of EoSs: interacting Fermi gas (FG) with variable repulsion parameter y, two types of interacting boson gas (BG-C1 and BG-C2), realistic hadronic EoSs for neutron stars, quark EoSs with bag constant B_eff = 150 MeV fm^{-3} and parameter lambda, hybrid EoSs using the Maxwell construction with a speed of sound c_s = 1, and white dwarf EoSs with A/Z = 2. They also employed the analytical Schwarzschild constant-density solution and Tolman-VII solution. For each stellar model, they computed the Shannon information entropy S and disequilibrium D from the dimensionless energy density profile, then combined them as C = exp(S) * D to obtain the statistical complexity.

## Key numbers

- For the Schwarzschild analytical solution with R = 10 km, the mass that maximizes SC is xi = 0.359 (where xi = M/M_sun), derived from dC_S/dM = 0.
- For the Tolman-VII analytical solution with R = 10 km, the mass that maximizes SC is xi = 0.347.
- The dimensionless constant delta in the Schwarzschild solution is delta = 2.685 x 10^5.
- For the Tolman-VII solution, the disequilibrium is D_T = 3.84 x 10^5 xi^2 R_bar^{-3}, where R_bar = R/km.
- For quark stars, the bag constant used is B_eff = 150 MeV fm^{-3}, with lambda values of (1, 2, 5, 16).
- For the interacting Fermi gas, the particle mass is taken as m_chi = 939.565 MeV/c^2 throughout the study.
- For white dwarfs, the ratio A/Z = 2 is used.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of statistical complexity in compact objects by Sanudo and Pacheco (2009) and Moustakidis (2009), which focused on white dwarfs and neutron stars respectively, and by de Avellar (2012, 2014) and Adhitya (2020). It is the first study to investigate SC in hybrid stars with pronounced phase transformations. The results connect to the configurational entropy (CE) approach of Gleiser and Stamatopoulos (2012) and Gleiser and Jiang (2015), which linked CE minima to maximum mass stability; here SC provides complementary information about internal spatial organization. The findings enable using SC as a diagnostic tool to identify phase transitions in compact objects and to probe the interplay between macroscopic observables and microscopic EoS characteristics.
