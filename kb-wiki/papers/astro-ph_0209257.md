# Gamma Ray Bursts from delayed collapse of neutron stars to quark matter stars

> We propose a model to explain how a Gamma Rays Burst can take place days or years after a supernova explosion. Our model is based on the conversion of a pure hadronic star (neutron star) into a star made at least in part of deconfined quark matter. The conversion process can be delayed if the surface tension at the interface between hadronic and deconfined-quark-matter phases is taken into account. The nucleation time (i.e. the time to form a critical-size drop of quark matter) can be extremely long if the mass of the star is small. Via mass accretion the nucleation time can be dramaticaly reduced and the star is finally converted into the stable configuration. A huge amount of energy, of the order of 10$^{52}$--10$^{53}$ erg, is released during the conversion process and can produce a powerful Gamma Ray Burst. The delay between the supernova explosion generating the metastable neutron star and the new collapse can explain the delay proposed in GRB990705 and in GRB011211.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that some gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) occurring days to years after a supernova explosion are produced by the delayed conversion of a metastable pure hadronic neutron star into a more compact star containing deconfined quark matter, with the delay regulated by mass accretion rather than by the mass and spin of the metastable star itself.

## Method

The authors calculate the nucleation time for formation of a critical-size droplet of deconfined quark matter inside a hadronic star using a semiclassical (WKB) tunneling approximation, incorporating a non-zero surface tension at the hadron-quark interface. They compute the energy released as the difference between the gravitational mass of the metastable hadronic star and that of the final stable hybrid star or quark star having the same baryonic mass, using the GM3 model with hyperons for the hadronic phase and a bag model for the quark phase.

## Key numbers

- Energy released in the hadron-to-quark conversion: (3–5) × 10^52 erg for all sensible choices of equation-of-state parameters
- For a hadronic star with initial mass 1.32 M⊙ and bag constant B^(1/4) = 170 MeV (B = 109 MeV/fm^3), the lifetime is about 10^12 years
- Accretion of about 0.01 M⊙ onto a 1.32 M⊙ hadronic star reduces its lifetime to a few years
- The "critical mass" M_cr of a metastable hadronic star with lifetime τ = 1 year is in the range (0.9–1.4) M⊙
- The metastable hadronic star (mass 1.3 M⊙, radius ~13 km) can collapse into a hybrid star (radius ~10.5 km) or a quark star (radius ~9 km)
- Surface tension values used: 10 to 50 MeV/fm^2

## Context

This model extends the "supranova" model of Vietri (1998) by replacing the collapse to a black hole with a hadron-to-quark matter conversion, addressing the problem that black hole collapse ejects too much baryonic material to produce GRBs (Fryer 1998). The work builds on earlier discussions of neutron star to quark star conversion (Cheng and Dai 1996, Bombaci and Datta 2000, Wang 2000, Ouyed 2002) and introduces surface tension as the key mechanism producing metastability. The model can explain the delays inferred for GRB990705 (Amati 2000, ~10 years) and GRB011211 (Reeves 2002, ~4 days), and predicts that all GRBs from this mechanism should have approximately the same isotropic energy and a duration of at least 10 seconds.
