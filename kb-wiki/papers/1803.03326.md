# Quantum-Classical Computation of Schwinger Model Dynamics using Quantum Computers

> We present a quantum-classical algorithm to study the dynamics of the two-spatial-site Schwinger model on IBM's quantum computers. Using rotational symmetries, total charge, and parity, the number of qubits needed to perform computation is reduced by a factor of $\sim 5$, removing exponentially-large unphysical sectors from the Hilbert space. Our work opens an avenue for exploration of other lattice quantum field theories, such as quantum chromodynamics, where classical computation is used to find symmetry sectors in which the quantum computer evaluates the dynamics of quantum fluctuations.

## Key claim

The authors present a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm for simulating the dynamics of the two-spatial-site Schwinger model on IBM's quantum computers, where classical computation is used to project the Hilbert space into symmetry sectors (momentum, charge, and parity) before the quantum computer evaluates time evolution within those reduced sectors. The abstract's claim of reducing qubit requirements by a factor of ~5 applies specifically to the two-spatial-site system with a cutoff of |l_n| ≤ 1 on electric flux links, and the reduction from 12 qubits to 3 qubits (a factor of 4) is achieved only after also imposing a second energy cutoff (tilde-Λ = 3) that introduces systematic errors at the ~1% level.

## Method

The authors discretize the Schwinger model on one and two spatial lattice sites using the Kogut-Susskind staggered fermion action with periodic boundary conditions, mapping the Hamiltonian to qubits via the Jordan-Wigner transformation. They impose Gauss's law, project onto zero-momentum and definite-parity sectors using classical computation, and further truncate the electric field energy to |l_n| ≤ 1 with an additional cutoff tilde-Λ = 3 on total electric field energy. Ground state energies are obtained using the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) with Bayesian global optimization on IBM's ibmqx2 and ibmqx5 quantum processors, while time evolution is implemented both via exact SU(4) decomposition of the evolution operator and via first-order Trotterization, with readout-error mitigation and zero-noise extrapolation applied to both methods.

## Key numbers

- The naive Hilbert space for the two-spatial-site system (4 fermion sites, 4 flux links with |l_n| ≤ 1) contains 1296 quantum states requiring 12 qubits; imposing Gauss's law reduces this to 13 physical states with Q=0.
- After momentum projection to k=0 and parity classification, the k=0, P=+1 sector contains 5 states and the k=0, P=-1 sector contains 4 states, reducing the qubit requirement from 12 to 3 qubits (a factor of 4).
- With the additional cutoff tilde-Λ = 3 on total electric field energy, the k=0, P=+1 sector fits in 2 qubits, introducing systematic errors at the ~1% level for x = 0.6 and μ = 0.1.
- VQE ground state energies for the k=0, P=+1 sector with tilde-Λ = 1, 2, 3 were measured as ⟨H⟩ = -0.91(1) MeV, -1.01(4) MeV, and -1.01(2) MeV respectively, using 8192 measurement shots per circuit.
- The VQE calculation for the tilde-Λ = 3 sector required 6.4 QPU-seconds and 2.4 CPU-seconds with a total run time of 4 hours.
- The Trotterized time evolution with δt = 0.1 lost quantum coherence after approximately 10 time steps, limited by the T₂ coherence time of ibmqx2.

## Context

This work extends previous quantum simulations of the Schwinger model in trapped ion systems by Martinez (2016) and Muschik (2017), which used open boundary conditions and long-range interactions, by instead employing periodic boundary conditions with local interactions and classical symmetry projection to reduce qubit requirements. The approach enables exploration of real-time dynamics on superconducting quantum hardware that cannot natively implement the long-range gates used in trapped ion systems, and opens an avenue toward simulating lattice quantum field theories such as quantum chromodynamics on near-term quantum computers, though the classical pre-processing required for symmetry projection does not yet provide a quantum advantage in Hilbert space dimensionality.
