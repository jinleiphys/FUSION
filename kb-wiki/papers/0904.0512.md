# Formation of Nuclear "Pasta" in Supernovae

> In supernova cores, nuclear "pasta" phases such as triangular lattice of rod-like nuclei and layered structure of slab-like nuclei are considered to exist. However, it is still unclear whether or not they are actually formed in collapsing supernova cores. Using {\it ab-initio} numerical simulations called the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD), we here solve this problem by demonstrating that a lattice of rod-like nuclei is formed from a bcc lattice by compression. We also find that, in the transition process, the system undergoes zigzag configuration of elongated nuclei, which are formed by a fusion of two original spherical nuclei.

## Key claim
Using Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) simulations, the authors demonstrate that a triangular lattice of rod-like nuclei (nuclear "pasta") can be formed by compressing a bcc lattice of spherical nuclei, establishing that pasta phases can actually form in collapsing supernova cores. The formation is triggered by spontaneous breaking of the bcc structure due to internuclear attraction from overlapping nucleon density tails, not by the fission instability of individual nuclei as previously assumed.

## Method
The authors performed QMD simulations of a system of protons, neutrons, and a uniform background of relativistic degenerate electrons in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using the Ewald sum for Coulomb interactions. Starting from a bcc lattice of spherical nuclei at various temperatures (0.25 MeV to 1.5 MeV) and proton fractions (x ≈ 0.39 and 0.49), they applied adiabatic and isothermal compression at rates below 10⁻⁶ ρ₀/(fm/c) and observed the dynamical transition to rod-like nuclei. They also performed a simplified model simulation treating nuclei as point charged particles with a Woods-Saxon potential to verify the mechanism.

## Key numbers
- Central density in supernova cores increases from ~10⁹ g cm⁻³ to ~3×10¹⁴ g cm⁻³ (normal nuclear density, corresponding to nucleon number density ρ₀ = 0.165 fm⁻³) during collapse.
- The QMD simulation used total nucleon number N = 3328 (1312 protons and 2016 neutrons) at initial nucleon number density ρ = 0.15 ρ₀ (box size L = 51.23 fm).
- The first pair of nuclei started to touch and fuse at t ≈ 57080 fm/c and ρ ≈ 0.243 ρ₀ for the adiabatic compression starting at T = 0.25 MeV.
- All nuclei fused to form rod-like nuclei at t < 72700 fm/c and ρ < 0.267 ρ₀.
- The compression was stopped at t = 76570 fm/c and ρ = 0.275 ρ₀ (T ≈ 0.5 MeV at this stage).
- The simplified model simulation used 128 nuclei of ²⁰⁸Pb, with Woods-Saxon potential parameters V₀ = -11.5 MeV, R = 17 fm, and a = 0.5 fm.

## Context
This work extends equilibrium phase diagram studies of nuclear pasta (Bonche 1985, Lassaut 1987, Watanabe 2003, Newton 2009, Avancini 2008) and static perturbative calculations (Iida 2001) by providing the first ab-initio dynamical demonstration that pasta phases can form during supernova collapse. The finding that nuclei connect before deforming due to fission instability contradicts the generally accepted picture described in the review by Pethick and Ravenhall (1995) and is consistent with results from Brandt (2005) using an incompressible liquid-drop model. The discovery of a zigzag pairing mechanism during the transition is a new result not seen in previous work. This enables future core collapse simulations incorporating the drastically different neutrino transport properties in pasta phases, as pointed out by Watanabe (2003) and Horowitz (2005).
