# BPS Skyrmions as neutron stars

> The BPS Skyrme model has been demonstrated already to provide a physically intriguing and quantitatively reliable description of nuclear matter. Indeed, the model has both the symmetries and the energy-momentum tensor of a perfect fluid, and thus represents a field theoretic realization of the "liquid droplet" model of nuclear matter. In addition, the classical soliton solutions together with some obvious corrections (spin-isospin quantization, Coulomb energy, proton-neutron mass difference) provide an accurate modeling of nuclear binding energies for heavier nuclei. These results lead to the rather natural proposal to try to describe also neutron stars by the BPS Skyrme model coupled to gravity. We find that the resulting self-gravitating BPS Skyrmions provide excellent results as well as some new perspectives for the description of bulk properties of neutron stars when the parameter values of the model are extracted from nuclear physics. Specifically, the maximum possible mass of a neutron star before black-hole formation sets in is a few solar masses, the precise value depending on the precise values of the model parameters, and the resulting neutron star radius is of the order of 10 km.

# BPS Skyrmions as neutron stars

## Key claim
Self-gravitating BPS Skyrmions provide an excellent description of neutron star bulk properties when model parameters are extracted from nuclear physics, but the results are not yet final predictions because they depend on the specific potential chosen and the precise value of the length parameter, which could deviate by about 20-30% in either direction.

## Method
The authors couple the BPS Skyrme model (a submodel of the near-BPS Skyrme model containing only the sextic term and potential term) to gravity, then solve the resulting Einstein equations numerically for static, spherically symmetric metrics using an axially symmetric ansatz for the Skyrme field. The two model parameters (mass scale m and length scale l) are fitted to the helium-4 nuclear mass (931.75 MeV per nucleon) and the nucleon radius (1.25 fm) respectively, and the system is integrated via shooting from the center with boundary conditions at the compacton surface.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass for the pion mass potential: M_max = 3.734 M_sun, with radius R_max = 18.458 km
- Maximum neutron star mass for the pion mass potential squared: M_max = 2.4388 M_sun, with radius R_max = 16.801 km
- Maximum baryon number in solar units for the pion mass potential: n_max = 5.005; for the pion mass potential squared: n_max = 3.271
- Mass parameter fit: m = 49.15 MeV (pion mass potential) and m = 47.20 MeV (pion mass potential squared)
- Length parameter fit: l = 0.884 fm (pion mass potential) and l = 0.746 fm (pion mass potential squared)
- Central energy density without gravity for the pion mass potential: 285 MeV fm^{-3}; for the pion mass potential squared: 909 MeV fm^{-3}
- Central energy density at maximum mass is about 2.7 times (pion mass potential) and 2.2 times (pion mass potential squared) the non-gravitational value
- Gravitational coupling constant: kappa^2 = 16 pi G = 6.654 x 10^{-41} fm MeV^{-1}
- Solar baryon number: B_sun = 1.198 x 10^57

## Context
This work extends the BPS Skyrme model, previously shown to accurately describe nuclear binding energies for heavier nuclei (Adam et al., 2013; Marleau, 2012), to the gravitational regime for neutron star description. It contrasts with earlier attempts using the original Skyrme model, such as the hedgehog ansatz by Bizon (which proved unstable for higher baryon number) and Skyrmion crystal approaches by Walhout, Piette, and collaborators (which assume crystalline structure at odds with the expected superfluid core). The results enable a unified description from nucleons to neutron stars and suggest that the effective on-shell equation of state stiffens with increasing baryon number due to self-gravitation, a feature that could motivate going beyond mean-field theory in traditional nuclear physics approaches like Quantum Hadron Dynamics (Walecka).
