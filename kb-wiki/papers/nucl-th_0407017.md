# Electromagnetic form factors of hyperons in a relativistic quark model

> The relativistically covariant constituent quark model developed by the Bonn group is used to compute the EM form factors of strange baryons. We present form-factor results for the ground-state and some excited hyperons. The computed magnetic moments agree well with the experimental values and the magnetic form factors follow a dipole $Q^2$ dependence.

# Electromagnetic Form Factors of Hyperons in a Relativistic Quark Model

## Key claim

The Bonn group’s relativistically covariant constituent quark model reproduces ground-state hyperon magnetic moments in good agreement with experiment, and the magnetic form factors follow a dipole Q² dependence, but the model badly reproduces the decay width of the Λ(1405) resonance, which may indicate the special nature of this resonance.

## Method

The Bethe-Salpeter equation for three constituent quarks is solved using an instantaneous linear confinement potential for the three-particle interaction and the ’t Hooft instanton-induced interaction for the two-particle interaction. Electromagnetic form factors are computed from the current matrix element in the center-of-mass frame of the incoming baryon using a structureless constituent quark current operator j^E_μ = Ψ̄ q̂ γ_μ Ψ. The Sachs electric and magnetic form factors are extracted from the Dirac and Pauli form factors for ground-state hyperons, while helicity amplitudes are computed for hyperon resonances.

## Context

This work extends the Bonn group’s relativistic constituent quark model (Merten et al., 2002) from nucleons to strange baryons, providing form factors needed for isobar-model descriptions of kaon electroproduction, for which Jefferson Lab data were recently published (Mohring et al., 2003). The calculated Σ⁻ electric mean-square radius is compared to experimental values from Adamovich et al. (1999) and Eschrich et al. (2001). The poor reproduction of the Λ(1405) decay width points to the special nature of this resonance, as discussed by Hyodo et al. (2004). The results enable improved treatment of hyperon resonance contributions in electroproduction reactions, particularly for resonances that are minor in photoproduction but may become important at finite Q².
