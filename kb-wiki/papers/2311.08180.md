# Systematics of neutron emission

> Neutron physics is one of the oldest branches of the experimental nuclear physics,but the investigation of the spontaneous neutron emission from the ground state along the neutron dripline is still at its beginning, in spite of the crucial importance for nuclear astrophysics. The proton dripline is much better investigated and a systematics of spontaneous proton half lives corected by the centrifugal barrier (monopole transitions) is given by the Geiger-Nuttall law $\log_{10}T\simχ$, where $χ\sim ZQ^{-1/2}$ is the Coulomb parameter characterizing the outgoing Coulomb-Hankel wave in terms of the daughter charge $Z$ and Q-value. Our purpose is to propose a similar simple systematics of spontaneous neutron half lives, but in terms of the nuclear reduced radius $ρ=κR\sim A^{1/3}Q^{1/2}$, characterizing the "neutral" outgoing spherical Hankel wave. It turns out that the half life in emission of neutral particles is governed by the scaling law $T\simρ^{-2}\sim A^{-2/3}Q^{-1}$ for monopole transitions. We evidence the important role of the angular momentum carried by the emitted neutron. The influence of the neutron wave function generated by a Woods-Saxon nuclear mean field is also analyzed.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a simple systematics for spontaneous neutron half-lives analogous to the Geiger-Nuttall law for proton emitters, but using the nuclear reduced radius ρ ∼ A^(1/3) Q^(1/2) instead of the Coulomb parameter. The authors hedge that the available experimental data are very limited and have relatively large errors, and that the angular momentum assignment for several nuclei is not experimentally determined but assumed from the standard spherical shell model.

## Method

The authors collected all available experimental data on spontaneous neutron emission from ground states for light nuclei with A ≤ 28, including Q-values, half-lives, and assigned angular momenta. They fitted the experimental half-lives to the spherical Neumann function squared y_l^2(ρ), using a single common parameter for all angular momenta, and then divided out the centrifugal factor C_l^2(ρ) to obtain reduced half-lives. They also analyzed the role of the neutron wave function by computing the experimental formation probability and compared their results to resonant states generated in a spherical Woods-Saxon mean field with universal parametrization.

## Key numbers

- The fitting formula for neutron half-lives is T_l = 0.1 y_l^2(ρ), where ρ = κR_0 ≈ 0.263 Q^(1/2) A^(1/3) with Q in MeV.
- The experimental data span Q-values from 82 keV (⁵B, l=1*) to 1800 keV (⁴Be, l=2) and half-lives from 2.28×10^(-22) s (⁸O, l=2) to 4.56×10^(-20) s (⁹F, l=2).
- The rms error of the experimental formation probability fit is σ = 0.492, corresponding to a factor of about three in half-life.
- For ²⁸F, the theoretical half-life for l=1 is about two orders of magnitude shorter than for l=2 at the experimental Q-value of 219-220 keV.
- The largest experimental half-life is only about two orders of magnitude above the characteristic nuclear time of 10^(-22) s.

## Context

This work extends the systematics of spontaneous proton emission half-lives proposed by Delion (2006) to the neutron dripline, where the Coulomb barrier is absent and only the centrifugal barrier binds neutrons. The authors note that the neutron dripline has been investigated experimentally up to argon by Cottle (2012) and Nowacki (2021), and that the standard shell model closure at N=20 appears to disappear with increasing asymmetry, as shown by Umeno (2016). The proposed systematics enables rough estimates of neutron emission half-lives and provides a tool to assign angular momenta of unbound neutrons, which is particularly relevant for resolving questions about the island of inversion around N=20 and the magicity of ²⁸O.
