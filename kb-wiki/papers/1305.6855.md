# Continuum TDHF calculation of Isoscalar and Isovector Giant Monopole Resonances

> We motivate and summarise some recent results in the application of formally exact boundary conditions in nuclear time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations, making use of Laplace transformations to calculate the values of the wave functions at the boundaries. We have realised the method in the case of giant monopole resonances of spherically-symmetric nuclei, and present strength functions of O-16 and Ca-40 using a simplified version of the Skyrme force, showing that no artefacts from discretisation occur as contaminants

## Key claim

The authors present a formally exact boundary condition method for time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations that avoids artefacts from wave function reflection at the computational box edge, but they note that the simplified Skyrme force used cannot be expected to give quantitatively correct results for giant monopole resonances.

## Method

The method applies Laplace transformations to the time-dependent Schrodinger equation outside the nuclear potential region, obtaining an expression for the wave function at the spatial boundary that is non-local in time and requires integration from the start of the simulation. The authors implement this for spherically-symmetric nuclei using a zero-range Skyrme interaction with parameters t0 = -1090.0 MeV fm^3 and t3 = 17288.0 MeV fm^6, calculating strength functions for O-16 and Ca-40 from Fourier transforms of the time-dependent expectation values of the monopole operator.

## Key numbers

* O-16 and Ca-40 strength functions calculated with energy resolution of 0.7 MeV
* Computational box radius of 30 fm used for the calculations
* Skyrme force parameters: t0 = -1090.0 MeV fm^3 and t3 = 17288.0 MeV fm^6

## Context

This work extends prior time-dependent Hartree-Fock calculations that used masking functions (Reinhard 2006) or complex optical potentials (Nakatsukasa 2005) to handle boundary conditions, which the authors note can become large and do not absorb equally at all energies. The method enables longer time iterations than reflecting boundary conditions allow, giving finer energy resolution through the Nyquist theorem, and the authors report that extension to full Skyrme force and deformed nuclear resonances is in progress.
