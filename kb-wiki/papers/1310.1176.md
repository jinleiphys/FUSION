# Structure and compositeness of hadron resonances

> The structure of the hadron resonances attracts much attention, in association with the recent observations of various exotic hadrons which do not fit well in the conventional picture. These findings urge us to consider various new configurations such as the multiquark states and the hadronic molecules. However, it is a subtle problem to define a proper classification scheme for the hadron structure, and the nonzero decay width of the hadron resonances makes the analysis complicated. In this paper, we summarize the recent developments in the studies of the structure of the hadron resonances, focusing on the notion of the compositeness in terms of the hadronic degrees of freedom.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the structure of hadron resonances should be classified in terms of hadronic degrees of freedom using a model-independent measure, and that the compositeness (field renormalization constant) provides such a measure for stable bound states, though its generalization to resonances with nonzero decay width remains incomplete and requires further development.

## Method

The author reviews the formal definition of compositeness using the field renormalization constant Z, which represents the probability of finding the elementary component in a physical bound state, with the compositeness X = 1 - Z representing the probability of the composite (two-hadron) component. For stable s-wave bound states, the compositeness is expressed exactly in terms of the binding energy and the transition form factor, and in the weak-binding limit it is related model-independently to the scattering length and effective range parameters following the approach of Weinberg (1965). The paper then discusses the difficulties of extending this framework to resonance states with complex eigenvalues, where the Gamow vectors in the rigged Hilbert space lead to complex-valued expectation values.

## Key numbers

* The compositeness X and elementariness Z satisfy the normalization condition 1 = Z + X, with both quantities bounded between 0 and 1 for stable bound states.
* For the deuteron, the analysis using the weak-binding relation shows it is dominated by the two-nucleon composite structure, as originally demonstrated by Weinberg (1965).
* The binding energy B of a bound state is defined as positive (E = -B) in the formalism, with the bound state pole located on the real axis of the first (physical) Riemann sheet.
* The resonance pole position has a real part interpreted as the mass and an imaginary part interpreted as half the decay width, but the mean squared radii and form factors of the Lambda(1405) resonance are obtained as complex numbers when treated as a resonance in the scattering amplitude, as shown by Sekihara (2008, 2010).

## Context

This review extends the compositeness approach originally developed by Weinberg (1965) for distinguishing elementary and composite particles, which was applied to the deuteron as a weakly-bound two-nucleon system. The paper addresses the need for a model-independent classification scheme for exotic hadrons such as the Lambda(1405) resonance, which has been described by multiple competing configurations including three-quark states (Isgur 1978), meson-baryon molecules (Dalitz 1967), and five-quark states (Strottman 1979). The work enables future applications of compositeness to resonance states and provides a framework for comparing results from different models and lattice QCD calculations, though a complete treatment of resonances remains an open problem.
