# Constituent-counting rule in photoproduction of hyperon resonances

> We analyze the CLAS data on the photoproduction of hyperon resonances, as well as the available data for the ground state $Λ$ and $Σ^{0}$ of the CLAS and SLAC-E84 collaborations, by considering constituent-counting rule suggested by perturbative QCD. The counting rule emerges as a scaling behavior of cross sections in hard exclusive reactions with large scattering angles, and it enables us to determine the number of elementary constituents inside hadrons. Therefore, it could be used as a new method for identifying internal constituents of exotic-hadron candidates. From the analyses of the $γ\, p \to K^{+} Λ$ and $K^{+} Σ^{0}$ reactions, we find that the number of the elementary constituents is consistent with $n_γ = 1$, $n_{p} = 3$, $n_{K^{+}} = 2$, and $n_Λ = n_{Σ^{0}} = 3$. Then, the analysis is made for the photoproductions of the hyperon resonances $Λ(1405)$, $Σ(1385)^{0}$, and $Λ(1520)$, where $Λ(1405)$ is considered to be a $\bar K N$ molecule and hence its constituent number could be five. However, we find that the current data are not enough to conclude the numbers of their constituent. It is necessary to investigate the higher-energy region at $\sqrt{s} > 2.8$ GeV experimentally beyond the energy of the available CLAS data for counting the number of constituents clearly. We also mention that our results indicate energy dependence in the constituent number, especially for $Λ(1405)$. If an excited hyperon is a mixture of three-quark and five-quark states, the energy dependence of the scaling behavior could be valuable for finding its composition and mixture.

## Key claim

The constituent-counting rule, a perturbative QCD scaling law for hard exclusive reactions at large scattering angles, can be used to determine the number of elementary constituents inside hadrons and thereby identify exotic-hadron candidates; however, the current CLAS data for the hyperon resonances Λ(1405), Σ(1385)⁰, and Λ(1520) are insufficient to conclude their constituent numbers, and data at center-of-mass energies above 2.8 GeV are needed.

## Method

The authors analyzed differential cross sections dσ/dt for the reactions γ p → K⁺ Y(∗) (Y = Λ, Σ⁰, Λ(1405), Σ(1385)⁰, Λ(1520)) from CLAS and SLAC-E84 collaborations, fitting the data to the scaling form dσ/dt = f(θ_cm)/s^(n−2) with the scaling exponent n as a free parameter. They performed fits for various choices of the minimum center-of-mass energy √s_min (from 2.3 GeV to 2.7 GeV) and for different angular bins around θ_cm = 90°, extracting n and its uncertainty for each reaction.

## Key numbers

- For γ p → K⁺ Λ with √s_min = 2.6 GeV and one angular bin (−0.05 < cosθ_cm < 0.05): n = 9.6 ± 0.5 (χ²/N_d.o.f. = 22/22)
- For γ p → K⁺ Σ⁰ with √s_min = 2.6 GeV and one angular bin: n = 9.1 ± 0.4 (χ²/N_d.o.f. = 10/22)
- For γ p → K⁺ Λ(1405) with √s_min = 2.5 GeV and six angular bins: n = 10.6 ± 0.6 (χ²/N_d.o.f. = 20/17)
- For γ p → K⁺ Σ(1385)⁰ with √s_min = 2.5 GeV and six angular bins: n = 11.4 ± 0.4 (χ²/N_d.o.f. = 34/17)
- For γ p → K⁺ Λ(1520) with √s_min = 2.5 GeV and six angular bins: n = 9.8 ± 0.5 (χ²/N_d.o.f. = 40/17)
- The maximum center-of-mass energy available in CLAS data for hyperon resonances is √s = 2.8 GeV, compared to 3.5 GeV for ground-state hyperons from SLAC-E84

## Context

This work extends the constituent-counting rule analyses previously applied to ground-state hyperons by Anderson (1976) and to the γ p → π⁺ n reaction by the JLab Hall-A and E94-104 collaborations (Zhu 2002, Zhu 2004), as well as the CLAS analysis of Λ photoproduction by Schumacher (2010). The authors apply the method to hyperon resonances for the first time, particularly targeting the exotic candidate Λ(1405), which is expected to be a K̅ N molecule with five constituents. The results motivate future measurements at JLab after the 12-GeV upgrade and at LEPS II to reach √s > 2.8 GeV, enabling clear determination of constituent numbers and potentially revealing energy-dependent scaling behavior that could distinguish mixtures of three-quark and five-quark components in excited hyperons.
