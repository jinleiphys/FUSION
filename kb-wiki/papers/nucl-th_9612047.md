# Deuteron electrodisintegration near threshold in the Bethe-Salpeter approach

> In the framework of the covariant Bethe-Salpeter formalism we calculate the amplitude of electrodisintegration of the deuteron near the threshold energy. Due to the small relative momentum of the final np-pair, the deuteron break-up process may be sufficiently described by a transition to the dominant 1S0 channel into the final state. Therefore only one unknown structure function (form factor) exists. The explicit formula for this form factor is given in terms of partial Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes. We compare our result to the usual non-relativistic calculations. The essential role of P-states as well as their possible link to mesonic exchange currents is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that deuteron electrodisintegration near threshold can be adequately described by a transition to the dominant \(^1S_0\) final state, reducing the hadronic matrix element to a single unknown structure function \(V(s,q^2)\); this simplification relies on the assumption that the relative momentum of the final neutron-proton pair is small, and the result is derived within the covariant Bethe-Salpeter formalism rather than the usual non-relativistic impulse approximation.

## Method

The authors compute the electromagnetic transition amplitude for deuteron breakup using the Bethe-Salpeter (BS) equation for the two-nucleon system, expressing the deuteron and final \(^1S_0\) state BS amplitudes in a partial-wave decomposition with \(\rho\)-spin and spin operators. They evaluate the hadronic current matrix element in the relativistic impulse approximation via the Mandelstam procedure, perform the trace over Dirac matrices, and reduce the resulting integrals to a scalar form factor \(V(s,q^2)\) that depends on the squared total energy \(s\) and momentum transfer \(q^2\). The final state is approximated by a plane wave for the \(^1S_0\) channel, and the calculation is carried out in the laboratory system.

## Key numbers

- The relative energy of the outgoing neutron-proton pair is \(E_{np} = 0-3\) MeV for Saclay experiments and \(E_{np} = 0-10\) MeV for SLAC experiments.
- The differential cross section for unpolarized electrons and deuterons is given by \(\frac{d^2\sigma}{dE'_e d\Omega'_e} = \left(\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega}\right)_M \frac{M |\mathbf{p}^*| m}{12\pi^2} [(E_e+E'_e)^2 - 2E_e E'_e \cos^2(\theta_e/2)] \tan^2(\theta_e/2) |V(s,q^2)|^2\), where \(|\mathbf{p}^*| = \sqrt{s/4 - m^2}\).
- For vector-polarized deuterons with polarization parallel to the \(Z\)-axis, the asymmetry at backward scattering (\(\theta_e = 180^\circ\)) simplifies to \(A_\parallel = \frac{3}{2}\kappa\), where \(\kappa\) is the degree of polarization.
- For tensor-polarized deuterons with alignment component \(p_{D\,zz}\), the tensor analyzing power at backward scattering is \(A_{zz} = 1\).
- The form factor at threshold \(s = 4m^2\) is expressed as \(V(4m^2,q^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi}} \left\{ \frac{1}{2Mm} (F_1^{(V)}(q^2)+F_2^{(V)}(q^2)) \frac{g_S(k_0^*,|\mathbf{q}|/2) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} g_D(k_0^*,|\mathbf{q}|/2)}{M-2\sqrt{m^2+\mathbf{q}^2/4}} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2M^2|\mathbf{q}|} \left(F_1^{(V)}(q^2) + \left[1-\frac{M}{2m}\sqrt{1+\frac{\mathbf{q}^2}{4m^2}}\right]F_2^{(V)}(q^2)\right) (g_t^e(k_0^*,|\mathbf{q}|/2) - g_t^o(k_0^*,|\mathbf{q}|/2)) \right\}\).

## Context

This work extends the relativistic description of deuteron electrodisintegration beyond the non-relativistic impulse approximation, which fails to reproduce experimental differential cross sections even at small momentum transfers (Saclay and SLAC data). The authors note that only one prior relativistic investigation exists, using light front dynamics (Karmanov, year not given), and that mesonic exchange currents (MEC), particularly pion exchange (Mathiot, year not given; Tamura, year not given), are needed to fill the minimum in the non-relativistic cross section. The covariant Bethe-Salpeter approach enables a consistent treatment of relativistic effects and P-state contributions, which the authors link to pair currents that partially incorporate two-body currents, and it provides explicit formulas for polarization observables that can be tested in future experiments.
