# Emulation of large-scale qubit registers with a phase-space approach

> A phase-space approach is used and benchmarked for the simulation of the continuous-time evolution of large registers of qubits. It is based on a statistical ensemble of independent mean-field trajectories, where mean field is introduced at the level of the qubits, substituting quantum fluctuations/correlations with classical ones. The approach only involves at worse a quadratic cost in the system size, allowing to simulate up to several thousands of qubits on a classical computer. It provides qualitatively accurate description of one-qubit observables evolutions, making it a useful reference in comparison to techniques limited to small qubit numbers. The predictive power is, however, less robust for multi-qubits observables. We benchmark the method on the $k$-local transverse-field Ising model, considering a large variety of systems ranging from local to all-to-all interactions, and from weak to strong coupling regimes, with up to 2000 qubits. To showcase the versatility of the approach, simulations on 2D and 3D Ising models are also made.

# Emulation of large-scale qubit registers with a phase-space approach

## Key claim

The Phase-Space Approximation (PSA) provides qualitatively accurate descriptions of one-qubit observables for up to several thousand qubits at quadratic computational cost, but its predictive power is less robust for multi-qubit observables and degrades in the strong-coupling regime (η ≥ 1) where the approximation of independent trajectories breaks down.

## Method

The authors benchmark the PSA on the k-local transverse-field Ising model (TFIM) by evolving a statistical ensemble of independent mean-field trajectories, where each trajectory is a product state evolved with mean-field equations of motion and the initial sampling uses a biased Rademacher distribution to reproduce the correct quantum moments at t₀. They systematically compare the PSA against exact dynamics for systems with up to 2000 qubits, varying the interaction strength η, connectivity k, and initial state, and also benchmark against matrix product state (MPS) evolution.

## Key numbers

- Systems with up to 2000 qubits are simulated on a classical computer using the PSA approach.
- The computational complexity scales as O(N_traj L²), where N_traj = 10⁴ trajectories are used for convergence.
- The MF critical threshold occurs at η = 1, where η = (2J/h)(k − k(k+1)/(2L)).
- Simulations use a time interval T = 10 h⁻¹ with time step dt = 10⁻² h⁻¹.
- The deviation D_r(T) is computed over 400 different (θ, φ) initial states on the Bloch sphere for L = 10, k = 1, η = 0.5.
- For the k-local TFIM, k ranges from 1 (nearest neighbor) to k = L−1 (all-to-all interaction).
- The entanglement entropy is computed for subsystems of m = 1, 2, and 3 qubits in a chain of L = 10 sites.

## Context

This work extends the PSA, originally developed for fermionic many-body systems in nuclear physics by Ayik (2008) and Lacroix (2012, 2013, 2014), to qubit systems with varying interaction ranges from nearest-neighbor to all-to-all. The technique coincides with the discrete truncated Wigner approximation (dTWA) used in condensed matter physics by Schachenmayer (2015) and Kunimi (2021), but the authors provide a formulation emphasizing the density matrix that is more adapted to the quantum computing community. The benchmark enables classical validation of quantum computations for large qubit registers, serving as a comparison point for techniques like tensor network methods (MPS) that are limited to smaller qubit numbers or specific entanglement structures.
