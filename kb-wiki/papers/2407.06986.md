# Quantum Monte Carlo calculations of electron scattering from $^{12}\text{C}$ in the Short-Time Approximation

> The Short-Time approximation is a method introduced to evaluate electroweak nuclear response for systems with $A\geq12$, extending the reach of first-principle many-body Quantum Monte Carlo calculations. Using realistic two- and three-body nuclear interactions and consistent one- and two-body electromagnetic currents, we calculate longitudinal and transverse response densities and response functions of $^{12}\text{C}$. We compare the resulting cross sections with experimental data for electron-nucleus scattering, finding good agreement.

## Key claim

The Short-Time Approximation (STA) reproduces electron scattering cross sections from \(^{12}\text{C}\) in good agreement with experimental data, but the method is valid only when the momentum transfer \(|\mathbf{q}|\) is much larger than the Fermi momentum \(k_F \approx 270\) MeV/\(c\); for \(|\mathbf{q}| \lesssim 300\) MeV/\(c\) the STA does not reproduce the correct threshold behavior and requires a redistribution of strength via a Gaussian smearing procedure.

## Method

The authors implemented the STA within the Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) method, using the Argonne \(v_{18}\) (AV18) two-nucleon potential and the Urbana-X three-nucleon interaction, together with consistent one- and two-body electromagnetic currents. The STA factorizes the real-time propagator to retain only two-nucleon dynamics in the final state, reducing the computational cost by a factor of \(\sim 10\) compared to exact Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations for \(^{12}\text{C}\). Response densities were computed at five values of momentum transfer (300, 450, 570, 650, and 800 MeV/\(c\)), and an interpolation scheme based on normalized cumulative integrals was developed to generate response functions at arbitrary momentum transfer while conserving sum rules.

## Key numbers

* Longitudinal sum rule \(S_L^{\rm int}\) for \(^{12}\text{C}\) at \(|\mathbf{q}| = 300\) MeV/\(c\): 0.84 (including two-body currents), compared to GFMC value 0.94.
* Transverse sum rule \(S_T^{\rm int}\) for \(^{12}\text{C}\) at \(|\mathbf{q}| = 300\) MeV/\(c\): 1.23 (including two-body currents), compared to GFMC value 1.37.
* Longitudinal sum rule \(S_L^{\rm cc}\) for \(^{12}\text{C}\) at \(|\mathbf{q}| = 570\) MeV/\(c\): 0.87 (including two-body currents), compared to GFMC value 0.91.
* Transverse sum rule \(S_T^{\rm cc}\) for \(^{12}\text{C}\) at \(|\mathbf{q}| = 570\) MeV/\(c\): 1.38 (including two-body currents), compared to GFMC value 1.51.
* The STA computational cost for \(^{12}\text{C}\) is reduced by a factor of \(\sim 10\) relative to exact GFMC calculations.
* The threshold smearing parameters used are \(\omega_{\text{th}} = 25\) MeV and \(\overline{\omega} = 15\) MeV.
* Response densities were evaluated on grids with 10 MeV spacing up to \(e \sim E_{\rm cm} \sim 500\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the STA, previously applied to \(^4\text{He}\) (Pastore 2019), \(^3\text{H}\), and \(^3\text{He}\) (Andreoli 2022), to the \(A=12\) system \(^{12}\text{C}\) for the first time. The STA results are compared with GFMC calculations from Lovato (2016) and experimental data from Barreau (1983) and Jourdan (1997). The approach enables first-principle calculations of electroweak response functions for nuclei with \(A \geq 12\) using the Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method, providing improved inputs for neutrino event generators such as GENIE (Andreopoulos 2009) used in DUNE and Hyper-Kamiokande analyses.
