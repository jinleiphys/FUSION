# Self-consistent Green's function theory for atomic nuclei

> Nuclear structure theory has recently gone through a major renewal with the development of ab initio techniques that can be applied to a large number of atomic nuclei, well beyond the light sector that had been traditionally targeted in the past. Self-consistent Green's function theory is one among these techniques. The present work aims to give an overview of the self-consistent Green's function approach for atomic nuclei, including examples of recent applications and a discussion on the perspectives for extending the method to nuclear reactions, doubly open-shell systems and heavy nuclei.

# Self-consistent Green's function theory for atomic nuclei

## Key claim
Self-consistent Green's function theory provides a systematically improvable, ab initio solution of the many-body Schrödinger equation for atomic nuclei that can be applied well beyond the light sector, but the accuracy of the method depends on the truncation scheme (ADC(n) order) and the softness of the nuclear interaction, with estimated errors of about 2% for ground-state energies and less than 0.5% for radii when using soft potentials.

## Method
The method reformulates the A-body Schrödinger equation in terms of one-body Green's functions (propagators) and solves the Dyson equation (or Gorkov equation for open-shell systems) iteratively, with the irreducible self-energy approximated via the algebraic diagrammatic construction (ADC) at orders n=1, 2, or 3. The energy-dependent Dyson equation is transformed into an energy-independent eigenvalue problem using the Lehmann representation of the self-energy, which is solved iteratively with Krylov projection techniques (Lanczos algorithm) to manage the growing dimensionality. Calculations employ a spherical harmonic oscillator basis with typical parameters e_max = 12-15 for the one-body basis and e_3max = 14-18 for three-body matrix elements.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energies computed with ADC(3) in 16O differ from no-core shell model results by less than 1% for any tested interaction
- For soft potentials (NN+3N(lnl) and NN+3N(400)), estimated theoretical error is about 2% for ground-state energies and less than 0.5% for charge radii of medium-mass nuclei
- For harder potentials (NNLO_sat), estimated errors rise to 4% for energies and 1% for radii
- The approximation of the three-body density matrix by an antisymmetrised product of one-body density matrices introduces errors smaller than 250 keV for binding energies of oxygen isotopes
- Typical basis parameters used: e_max = 12-15, e_2max = 2*e_max, e_3max = 14-18
- ADC(2) calculations show that e_max = 13 model space guarantees sufficiently converged results for 36Ca

## Context
This review extends the line of ab initio nuclear structure methods that have been developed since the 1990s, when SCGF techniques were reimported into nuclear physics from quantum chemistry and solid-state physics (Dickhoff, 1992; Kowalski, 2004). The work builds on formal developments including the generalisation to Gorkov (particle-number-breaking) framework (Soma, 2011), inclusion of three-body interactions in Dyson theory (Carbone, 2013), and derivation of ADC(3) working equations (Raimondi, 2018). The method enables applications to medium-mass nuclei up to tin and xenon isotopes (Arthuis, 2020), computation of ab initio optical potentials for elastic scattering (Idini, 2019), and studies of lepton-nucleus scattering (Barbieri, 2019), with future directions including extension to nuclear reactions, doubly open-shell systems, and heavy nuclei.
