# Structures of the Vela pulsar and the glitch crisis from the Brueckner theory

> Detailed structures of the Vela pulsar (PSR B0833-45, with a period of $89.33$ milliseconds) are predicted by adopting a recently-constructed unified treatment of all parts of neutron stars: the outer crust, the inner crust and the core based on modern microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations. To take the pulsar mass ranging from $1.0M_{\odot}$ to $2.0M_{\odot}$, we calculate the central density, the core/crust radii, the core/crust mass, the core/crustal thickness, the moment of inertia, and the crustal moment of inertia. Among them, the crustal moment of inertia could be effectively constrained from the accumulated glitch observations, which has been a great debate recently, known as "glitch crisis". Namely, superfluid neutrons contained in the inner crust, which are regarded as the origin of the glitch in the standard two-component model, could be largely entrained in the nuclei lattices, then there may not be enough superfluid neutrons ($\sim 4/5$ less than the previous value) to trigger the large glitches ($Δν/ν_0 \sim 10^{-6}$) in the Vela pulsar. We then provide the first analysis of the crisis based on the microscopic basis, by confronting the glitch observations with the theoretical calculations for the crustal moment of inertia. We find that despite some recent opposition to the crisis argument, the glitch crisis is still present, which means that besides the crust superfluid neutrons, core neutrons might be necessary for explaining the large glitches of the Vela pulsar.

# Structures of the Vela pulsar and the glitch crisis from the Brueckner theory

## Key claim

Despite recent opposition to the glitch crisis argument, the crisis is still present when analyzed using a microscopic unified neutron star equation of state: the crustal moment of inertia fraction from the BCPM, BSk21, and BSk20 models is no more than 6.6%, which falls short of the 7% threshold required by glitch observations if only crustal superfluid neutrons are responsible, implying that core neutrons may be necessary to explain the large glitches of the Vela pulsar.

## Method

The authors adopt the Barcelona-Catania-Paris-Madrid (BCPM) nuclear energy density functional, derived from microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) calculations using the Argonne v18 potential plus three-body forces, to construct a unified equation of state covering the outer crust, inner crust, and core of neutron stars. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations in the slow-rotation approximation to compute structural properties (central density, radii, masses, thicknesses, moments of inertia) for the Vela pulsar over a mass range of 1.0 to 2.0 solar masses, and compare results with three other equations of state: BSk21, BSk20, and a matched Av18* EoS.

## Key numbers

- Vela pulsar spin period: 89.33 milliseconds
- For a 1.4 solar mass star with the BCPM EoS: radius = 11.75 km, central number density = 0.548 fm^-3 (about 3 times nuclear saturation density 0.17 fm^-3)
- Inner crust thickness for a 1.4 solar mass star with unified EoSs: 0.53 km; ratio R_icrust/R = 0.045
- Inner crust thickness range across unified EoSs (BCPM, BSk21, BSk20): 0.2 - 0.84 km; ratio R_icrust/R range: 0.07 - 0.02
- Crustal moment of inertia magnitude: 10^43 g cm^2; total moment of inertia magnitude: 10^45 g cm^2
- Maximum fractional crustal moment of inertia from unified EoSs: 6.6%, which is below the glitch-required threshold of 7%
- Previous glitch constraint (without entrainment): I_c/I >= 1.6%; updated constraint (with entrainment): I_c/I >= 7%
- Superfluid neutron reduction due to entrainment: approximately 4/5 less than the previous value

## Context

This work extends the unified equation of state approach of the Barcelona-Catania-Paris-Madrid group (Li et al. 2015) to the specific case of the Vela pulsar, and directly addresses the "glitch crisis" first proposed independently by Andersson et al. (2012) and Chamel (2013). The crisis arises because crustal entrainment reduces the available superfluid neutrons, making it difficult to explain Vela's large glitches (Delta nu/nu_0 ~ 10^-6) with only crustal superfluid. The authors contrast their microscopic unified treatment with earlier combined EoS approaches (e.g., Negele and Vautherin for the inner crust, Feynman-Metropolis-Teller and Baym-Pethick-Sutherland for the outer crust) and with phenomenological models such as those of Douchin and Haensel (2001) and the Brussels-Montreal BSk family. The results contradict studies by Piekarewicz et al. (2014) and Li et al. (2015a) that argued large transition pressures could resolve the crisis, and instead support the need for core neutron participation in glitch mechanisms.
