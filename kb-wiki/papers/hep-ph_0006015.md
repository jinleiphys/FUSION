# Technique for Direct eV-Scale Measurements of the Mu and Tau Neutrino Masses Using Supernova Neutrinos

> Early black hole formation in a core-collapse supernova will abruptly truncate the neutrino fluxes. The sharp cutoff can be used to make model-independent time-of-flight neutrino mass tests. Assuming a neutrino luminosity of $10^{52}$ erg/s per flavor at cutoff and a distance of 10 kpc, SuperKamiokande can detect an electron neutrino mass as small as 1.8 eV, and the proposed OMNIS detector can detect mu and tau neutrino masses as small as 6 eV. This {\it Letter} presents the first technique with direct sensitivity to eV-scale mu and tau neutrino masses.

# Technique for Direct eV-Scale Measurements of the Mu and Tau Neutrino Masses Using Supernova Neutrinos

## Key claim
If a core-collapse supernova forms a black hole early enough to abruptly truncate the neutrino signal, the sharp cutoff enables model-independent time-of-flight neutrino mass measurements. The technique can detect mu and tau neutrino masses as small as 6 eV in the proposed OMNIS detector, assuming a neutrino luminosity of 10^52 erg/s per flavor at cutoff and a distance of 10 kpc, but only if the mu and tau neutrino masses are nearly degenerate as suggested by atmospheric neutrino results.

## Method
The authors analyze the time-of-flight delay of massive neutrinos after the abrupt termination of neutrino fluxes caused by black hole formation in a core-collapse supernova. For SuperKamiokande, they split the inverse beta decay events into different neutrino energy ranges to simultaneously measure the cutoff time and the electron neutrino mass. For OMNIS, they count the number of neutral-current single-neutron spallation events on lead that arrive after the measured cutoff time, using the known cutoff time from SuperKamiokande and the known distance to the supernova to infer the mu and tau neutrino masses from the delayed event count.

## Key numbers
- SuperKamiokande can detect an electron neutrino mass as small as 1.8 eV, assuming a neutrino luminosity of 10^52 erg/s per flavor at cutoff and a distance of 10 kpc.
- The proposed OMNIS detector can detect mu and tau neutrino masses as small as 6 eV (90% confidence level), assuming degenerate masses and the same luminosity and distance.
- For a 6.1 eV mu/tau neutrino mass, OMNIS would expect 2.3 delayed events in the tail after the cutoff.
- The event rate in SuperKamiokande before cutoff is approximately 1500 s^-1 for the inverse beta decay reaction.
- The thermally-averaged cross section for mu and tau neutrinos on 208Pb (1-neutron spallation) is 760 x 10^-42 cm^2 at T = 8 MeV.
- The neutrino energy-dependent delay is given by Δt(s) = 0.515 (m/E)^2 D, where m is in eV, E in MeV, and D in units of 10 kpc.

## Context
This work extends the earlier time-of-flight technique of Beacom and Vogel (1998), which used average arrival times and achieved sensitivity to mu/tau neutrino masses down to 45 eV in SuperKamiokande and 30 eV in SNO, but scaled poorly with detector mass (as 1/M_D^1/4). The new technique exploits the sharp cutoff from early black hole formation, studied by Burrows (1988) and Mezzacappa and Bruenn (1999), and achieves much better sensitivity with a more favorable scaling as sqrt(D/L_BH M_D). This enables the first direct technique with eV-scale sensitivity to mu and tau neutrino masses, which is crucial because laboratory bounds are much weaker (170 keV for mu neutrinos, 18 MeV for tau neutrinos) and because the see-saw mechanism suggests these masses could be much larger than the electron neutrino mass.
