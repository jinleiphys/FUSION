# Precision neutron interferometric measurements of the n-p, n-d, and n-3He zero-energy coherent neutron scattering amplitudes

> We have performed high precision measurements of the zero-energy neutron scattering amplitudes of gas phase molecular hydrogen, deuterium, and $^{3}$He using neutron interferometry. We find $b_{\mathit{np}}=(-3.7384 \pm 0.0020)$ fm\cite{Schoen03}, $b_{\mathit{nd}}=(6.6649 \pm 0.0040)$ fm\cite{Black03,Schoen03}, and $b_{n^{3}\textrm{He}} = (5.8572 \pm 0.0072)$ fm\cite{Huffman04}. When combined with the previous world data, properly corrected for small multiple scattering, radiative corrections, and local field effects from the theory of neutron optics and combined by the prescriptions of the Particle Data Group, the zero-energy scattering amplitudes are: $b_{\mathit{np}}=(-3.7389 \pm 0.0010)$ fm, $b_{\mathit{nd}}=(6.6683 \pm 0.0030)$ fm, and $b_{n^{3}\textrm{He}} = (5.853 \pm .007)$ fm. The precision of these measurements is now high enough to severely constrain NN few-body models. The n-d and n-$^{3}$He coherent neutron scattering amplitudes are both now in disagreement with the best current theories. The new values can be used as input for precision calculations of few body processes. This precision data is sensitive to small effects such as nuclear three-body forces, charge-symmetry breaking in the strong interaction, and residual electromagnetic effects not yet fully included in current models.

# Precision neutron interferometric measurements of the n-p, n-d, and n-3He zero-energy coherent neutron scattering amplitudes

## Key claim

The measured zero-energy coherent neutron scattering amplitudes for n-d and n-3He are both in disagreement with the best current theoretical calculations, even when state-of-the-art NN potentials and three-nucleon force models are used. The precision is now sufficient to severely constrain few-body models, but only theories that correctly account for nuclear three-body forces, charge-symmetry breaking, and residual electromagnetic effects have the possibility to successfully confront the data.

## Method

Neutron interferometry was performed at the NIST Center for Neutron Research using a perfect silicon crystal interferometer with a cold monochromatic neutron beam (E = 11.1 meV). The phase shift induced by gas-phase molecular hydrogen, deuterium, and 3He samples was measured using a secondary sampling method with a rotatable quartz phase shifter, with the atom density determined from the ideal gas law with virial coefficient corrections, temperature monitored to 0.023% accuracy, pressure measured to better than 0.01%, and neutron wavelength determined using a pyrolytic graphite crystal calibrated against a silicon standard.

## Key numbers

- n-p bound zero-energy coherent scattering amplitude: b_np = (-3.7384 ± 0.0020) fm (from this experiment alone)
- n-d bound zero-energy coherent scattering amplitude: b_nd = (6.6649 ± 0.0040) fm (from this experiment alone)
- n-3He bound zero-energy coherent scattering amplitude: b_n3He = (5.8572 ± 0.0072) fm (from this experiment alone)
- Combined world data value for b_np: (-3.7389 ± 0.0010) fm, after corrections for multiple scattering, radiative corrections, and local field effects
- Combined world data value for b_nd: (6.6683 ± 0.0030) fm
- Combined world data value for b_n3He: (5.853 ± 0.007) fm
- Ratio b_n3He / b_np = (-1.5668 ± 0.0021)
- Ratio b_nd / b_np = (-1.7828 ± 0.0014)
- Triton binding energy used for comparison: (8.481855 ± 0.000013) MeV

## Context

This work extends precision neutron interferometric measurements of coherent scattering amplitudes in the two-, three-, and four-body systems, providing data that can be used to fix parameters in chiral effective field theory expansions and test modern NN potentials such as AV18 (Wiringa 1995) and CD Bonn 2000. The n-d results disagree with calculations by Witala et al. (2003) using high-precision NN forces with 3N force models, and the n-3He results disagree with resonating group method calculations by Hofmann and Hale (2003) using AV18 with 3N forces. These measurements enable improved determination of the doublet n-d scattering amplitude (which fixes one of two low-energy constants in EFT at NNLO) and motivate future measurements of the neutron-neutron scattering amplitude and the neutron-electron scattering amplitude.
