# Multimessenger study of baryon-charged QCD matter in heavy-ion collisions

> Multimessenger studies of heavy-ion collisions, using hadrons and electromagnetic probes, can reveal the properties of the created QCD matter from different perspectives. This study calculates the thermal dilepton invariant mass spectra and thermal photon transverse momentum spectra in Au+Au collisions at low beam energies from the Beam Energy Scan program, using a (3+1)-dimensional multistage hydrodynamic model calibrated by rapidity-dependent hadronic distributions. The effects of thermodynamic rapidity variation, baryon chemical potential, and fluid expansion on the spectra are explored. Methods for extracting temperature from photon and dilepton spectra are examined by comparison with the underlying hydrodynamic temperature. The possibility of combining photon and dilepton spectra to extract radial flow is also investigated. This study provides insights into measuring the thermodynamic properties of the created systems in heavy-ion collisions using multiple messengers through two fundamental interactions within the same framework.

## Key claim

This study demonstrates that temperatures extracted from thermal dilepton and photon spectra in heavy-ion collisions are consistent with each other and with the underlying hydrodynamic temperature evolution, but only when the intrinsic discrepancies between these probes are acknowledged and the photon Doppler shift is removed; the extracted temperatures and radial flow deviate from the true hydrodynamic values but can still be used to estimate them if correlations are quantified through realistic simulations.

## Method

The authors used a (3+1)-dimensional multistage hydrodynamic model (music for hydrodynamics, isd for initial conditions, urqmd for hadronic afterburner) to simulate Au+Au collisions at 7.7 and 14.5 GeV from the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program. The model was calibrated using rapidity-dependent hadronic distributions constructed from STAR Collaboration measurements, including newly calibrated results for 14.5 GeV collisions. Thermal dilepton invariant mass spectra and thermal photon transverse momentum spectra were calculated by convoluting emission rates (including baryon chemical potential dependence) with hydrodynamic fluid cells above the Cleymans freeze-out line, and temperatures were extracted by fitting the spectra with exponential functional forms.

## Key numbers

- At 14.5 GeV, the energy density-weighted average temperature varies by approximately 4% and the baryon chemical potential by approximately 80% across spacetime rapidity on the freeze-out surface
- At 7.7 GeV, the energy density-weighted average temperature varies by approximately 25% and the baryon chemical potential by approximately 115% across spacetime rapidity on the freeze-out surface
- The dilepton invariant mass spectra at midrapidity (y=0) are enhanced by approximately 50% at 7.7 GeV and about 10% at 14.5 GeV at M=3 GeV compared to spectra within |y|<1
- Setting baryon chemical potential to zero in photon emission rates suppresses the photon spectra by approximately 20% at lower center-of-mass energies
- Turning off flow reduces the photon pT spectrum by around 20% at pT approximately 3 GeV
- The dilepton temperature (T_ll) differs from the hydrodynamic average temperature by about 1% for most of the evolution
- The photon temperature without transverse flow (T_gz) differs from the hydrodynamic average temperature by about 1% for most of the evolution
- The constant offset between T_ll and T_gz is 4 MeV at 7.7 GeV and 5 MeV at 14.5 GeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Churchill (2023) on dilepton emission and Du (2023) on hadron production at BES energies, bridging the gap between collisions at 7.7 GeV and 19.6 GeV by newly calibrating the model for 14.5 GeV collisions. The study builds on the approach of Shen (2013) and Paquet (2015) for photon and dilepton calculations, and investigates the same region of the QCD phase diagram relevant to neutron star physics. The results enable future work to quantify relationships between effective extracted properties and underlying hydrodynamic quantities across various center-of-mass energies and centralities, which could help interpret experimental measurements from facilities such as GSI/FAIR, HIAF, and NICA.
