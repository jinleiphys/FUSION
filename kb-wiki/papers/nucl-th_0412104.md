# Description of the two neutrino double beta decay in deformed nuclei with projected spherical single particle states

> Using an angular momentum projected single particle basis, a pnQRPA approach is used to study the $2νββ$ properties of ten isotopes, exhibiting various quadrupole deformations. The mother and daughter nuclei exhibit different quadrupole deformations. Since the projected basis enables a unified description of deformed and spherical nuclei, situations where the nuclei involved in the double beta decay process are both spherical, both deformed or one spherical and another deformed, can be treated through a sole formalism. Dependence of single $β^-$ and $β^+$ strength distribution on atomic mass number and nuclear deformation is analyzed. For the double beta decay process, the Gamow-Teller transition amplitudes and half lives are calculated. Results are compared with the experimental data as well as with the predictions of other theoretical approaches. The agreement between the present results and experimental data is fairly good.

## Key claim

The paper claims that using an angular momentum projected single particle basis within a proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pnQRPA) provides a unified formalism for describing two-neutrino double beta decay in nuclei with different shapes (spherical, deformed-prolate, deformed-oblate), but the agreement with experimental half-lives depends on carefully tuning the particle-hole and particle-particle interaction strengths for each isotope, and for some cases the best fit requires setting the particle-particle strength to zero.

## Method

The authors constructed an angular momentum projected single particle basis from a particle-core Hamiltonian, then applied a pnQRPA approach with separable pairing, dipole-dipole particle-hole, and particle-particle interactions to calculate Gamow-Teller transition amplitudes and half-lives for ten double beta emitters. The BCS and pnQRPA equations were solved separately for mother and daughter nuclei, which can have different quadrupole deformations, and the overlap between phonon states of the intermediate odd-odd nucleus was computed. The interaction strengths were fixed by fitting experimental log ft values for single beta transitions and GT resonance centroids where available, or using systematic A-dependent formulas otherwise.

## Key numbers

* For $^{48}$Ca, the calculated half-life with $\chi=0.180$ MeV and $g_{pp}=0.0$ is $4.3\times 10^{19}$ years, compared to the experimental value of $4.3^{+2.4}_{-1.1}\times 10^{19}$ years.
* For $^{100}$Mo, the calculated half-life is $1.0\times 10^{19}$ years, compared to the experimental value of $0.95\pm 0.04\times 10^{19}$ years.
* For $^{128}$Te, the calculated half-life is $2.8\times 10^{24}$ years, compared to the experimental value of $2.5\pm 0.3\times 10^{24}$ years.
* For $^{130}$Te, the calculated half-life is $1.0\times 10^{21}$ years, compared to the experimental value of $0.9\pm 0.1\times 10^{21}$ years.
* The particle-hole strength $\chi$ for $^{128}$Te and $^{130}$Te was set to 0.268 MeV to reproduce the GT resonance centroid at 13.7 MeV and 14.1 MeV respectively.
* The single particle space dimensions for the pnQRPA matrix range from 118 ($^{48}$Ca mother) to 166 ($^{116}$Cd mother).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous formalism (Raduta 2004) by applying it to a broader set of ten isotopes spanning six different shape transition types, including spherical-to-spherical, spherical-to-deformed, and deformed-to-deformed cases. It builds on earlier pnQRPA approaches using Nilsson or deformed Woods-Saxon bases (Homma 1996, Sarigiannis 1997, Engel 1998, Simkovic 1999) and a self-consistent deformed method with Skyrme interactions (Sarigiannis 1997). The results enable systematic comparison with experimental double beta decay half-lives and provide nuclear matrix elements that could be used to evaluate neutrinoless double beta decay rates, though the formalism requires further testing for nuclei where no experimental data exist (such as $^{104}$Ru, $^{110}$Pd, and $^{134}$Xe).
