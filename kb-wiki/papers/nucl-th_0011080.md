# Light Front Nuclear Theory and the HERMES Effect

> I discuss the use of light cone variables to compute the nucleonic and mesonic components of nuclear wave functions. A Lagrangian and its energy-momentum tensor $T^{^+μ}$ is used to define the total momentum operators $P^μ$. The aim is to use wave functions, expressed in terms of plus-momentum variables, which are used to analyze high energy experiments such as deep inelastic scattering, Drell-Yan production, (e,e') and (p,p') reactions. We discuss infinite nuclear matter within the mean field approximation; finite nuclei using the mean field approximation; nucleon-nucleon scattering, within the one boson exchange approximation; and, infinite nuclear matter including the effects of two-nucleon correlations. Standard good results for nuclear saturation properties are obtained, with a possible improvement in the lowered value, 180 MeV, of the computed nuclear compressibility. In our approach, manifest rotational invariance emerges at the end of the calculation. Thus nuclear physics can be done in a manner in which modern nuclear dynamics can be implemented and symmetries are respected. A salient feature is that $ω,σ$ and $π$ mesons are obtained as important constituents. These can contribute coherently to enhance nuclear electroproduction cross sections for longitudinal virtual photons at low $Q^2$, while depleting the cross section for transverse photons. Thus the recent HERMES inelastic lepton-nucleus scattering data at low $Q^2$ and small $x$ can be described using photon-meson and meson-nucleus couplings which are consistent with constraints obtained from meson decay widths, nuclear structure, deep inelastic scattering, and lepton pair production data. Our model makes a variety of testable predictions.

# Light Front Nuclear Theory and the HERMES Effect

## Key claim

Light front dynamics applied to nuclear structure can describe the HERMES effect, a large enhancement of the longitudinal-to-transverse cross section ratio in lepton-nucleus scattering at low $Q^2$ and small $x$, using photon-meson and meson-nucleus couplings that are consistent with constraints from meson decay widths, nuclear structure, deep inelastic scattering, and lepton pair production data, though the couplings are not uniquely determined by those constraints.

## Method

The author applies light front quantization to nuclear Lagrangians (the Walecka model and a chiral Lagrangian with $N,\pi,\sigma,\omega,\rho,\eta,\delta$ fields) to compute nuclear wave functions in terms of plus-momentum variables. Three studies are performed: infinite nuclear matter in the mean field approximation, finite nuclei in the mean field approximation, and infinite nuclear matter including two-nucleon correlations via a light front version of Brueckner theory. The resulting mesonic components of the nuclear wave function are then used to compute coherent contributions to electroproduction cross sections for longitudinal and transverse virtual photons on nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear compressibility computed as 180 MeV, a possible improvement over standard values
- In infinite nuclear matter mean field (Walecka model with Chin & Walecka 1974 parameters: $g_v^2 M_N^2/m_v^2=195.9$, $g_s^2 M_N^2/m_s^2=267.1$), vector mesons carry 35% of total nuclear plus-momentum $P^+$, nucleons carry only 65%
- In finite nuclei mean field (Walecka model), nucleons carry about 70% of total $P^+$
- In infinite nuclear matter with two-nucleon correlations, nucleons carry at least 84% of nuclear plus-momentum (Fermi gas part only), with potential increase to about 90% including two-particle two-hole states
- Excess pions per nucleon about 5%
- HERMES collaboration measured $( \sigma_L(A)/\sigma_L(D) ) / ( \sigma_T(A)/\sigma_T(D) ) \approx 5$ at $x=0.01$, $Q^2=0.5$ GeV$^2$
- Upper limit on $\gamma\omega\sigma$ coupling from $\omega\to\gamma\sigma$ decay: $g_{UL}^2 \alpha = 0.013 \approx 2\alpha$

## Context

This work extends the light front approach to nuclear physics, motivated by the EMC effect (discovered by the European Muon Collaboration) and the subsequent crisis in nuclear theory when Drell-Yan experiments (e.g., the E772 experiment) failed to find the enhanced anti-quark distributions predicted by conventional meson-exchange models of the EMC effect. The author's earlier work with collaborators (Blunden, Burkardt, Machleidt) developed light front Brueckner theory and mean field calculations. The HERMES effect explanation builds on the idea that the $\omega$, $\sigma$, and $\pi$ mesons responsible for nuclear binding can be directly observed as effective constituents at low $Q^2$ and small $x$, enabling future tests via exclusive mesonic states in the current fragmentation region and measurements at different $Q^2$ values.
