# Strangeness-Conserving Hadronic Parity Violation at Low Energies

> The parity-violating nucleon-nucleon interaction is the key to understanding the strangeness-conserving hadronic weak interaction at low energies. In this brief talk, I review the past accomplishments in and current status of this subject, and outline a new joint effort between experiment and theory that tries to address the potential problems in the past by focusing on parity violation in few-nucleon systems and using the language of effective field theory.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the parity-violating nucleon-nucleon interaction, despite decades of study, has not yielded a consistent picture between experiment and theory, and that a new joint effort using effective field theory (EFT) and few-nucleon systems can address the potential problems in the older meson-exchange and S-P amplitude frameworks.

## Method

The paper reviews the two traditional frameworks for describing the parity-violating nucleon-nucleon interaction: the S-P amplitude formalism (with five Danilov parameters) and the meson-exchange model (with seven PV meson-nucleon coupling constants, notably the DDH predictions). It then outlines a new EFT-based formulation by Zhu et al. (2004) that organizes the PV potential to order Q, containing short-range contact operators (10 low-energy constants reducible to 5), a long-range one-pion-exchange term, and a medium-range two-pion-exchange term, for a total of 6 undetermined parameters (5 LECs plus h_pi^1). The paper describes a "hybrid" EFT approach that marries the general EFT potential with state-of-the-art phenomenological nuclear wave functions to re-analyze PV observables in few-nucleon systems.

## Key numbers

- The first evidence of parity violation in hadronic systems was a circular polarization P_gamma = -(6 +/- 1) x 10^{-6} in the gamma-decay of ^{181}Ta (Lobashov, 1967)
- The radiative thermal neutron capture on proton gave P_gamma = -(1.30 +/- 0.45) x 10^{-6} (Lobashov, 1972), later revised to P_gamma = (1.8 +/- 1.8) x 10^{-7} (Knyaz'kov, 1984)
- The DDH "best" value for the pion coupling h_pi^1 is 4.6 x 10^{-7}, with an allowed range of 0.0 to 11.4 x 10^{-7}
- The DDH "best" value for h_rho^0 is -11.4 x 10^{-7}, with an allowed range of -30.8 to 11.4 x 10^{-7}
- The DDH "best" value for h_omega^0 is -1.9 x 10^{-7}, with an allowed range of -10.3 to 5.7 x 10^{-7}
- The pp asymmetry at 13.6 MeV is A_L = (-0.93 +/- 0.21) x 10^{-7} (Bonn)
- The pp asymmetry at 45 MeV is A_L = (-1.57 +/- 0.23) x 10^{-7} (SIN)
- The p-alpha asymmetry at 46 MeV is A_L = (-3.3 +/- 0.9) x 10^{-7} (SIN)
- The chiral symmetry breaking scale Lambda_chi = 4 pi F_pi is approximately 1.161 GeV
- The pion mass m_pi is approximately 140 MeV, defining the energy scale E << 10 MeV for the pionless theory

## Context

This review extends the line of work on hadronic parity violation that began with Lee and Yang (1956) and the first experimental evidence by Lobashov (1967). It contrasts the older meson-exchange framework, standardized by Desplanques, Donoghue, and Holstein (DDH, 1980) with seven PV coupling constants, against the newer EFT formulation by Zhu et al. (2004) that contains 6 parameters. The paper identifies two major puzzles: the anapole moments of ^{133}Cs and ^{205}Tl contradict each other, and the pp asymmetry data give h_rho consistent with DDH but h_omega only marginally consistent. The new direction enables a systematic program combining high-precision measurements in few-nucleon systems (at LANSCE, NIST, and the Spallation Neutron Source) with reliable few-body calculations and model-independent EFT to resolve these inconsistencies.
