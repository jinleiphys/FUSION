# Breakup of three particles within the adiabatic expansion method

> General expressions for the breakup cross sections in the lab frame for $1+2$ reactions are given in terms of the hyperspherical adiabatic basis. The three-body wave function is expanded in this basis and the corresponding hyperradial functions are obtained by solving a set of second order differential equations. The ${\cal S}$-matrix is computed by using two recently derived integral relations. Even though the method is shown to be well suited to describe $1+2$ processes, there are nevertheless particular configurations in the breakup channel (for example those in which two particles move away close to each other in a relative zero-energy state) that need a huge number of basis states. This pathology manifests itself in the extremely slow convergence of the breakup amplitude in terms of the hyperspherical harmonic basis used to construct the adiabatic channels. To overcome this difficulty the breakup amplitude is extracted from an integral relation as well. For the sake of illustration, we consider neutron-deuteron scattering. The results are compared to the available benchmark calculations.

## Key claim

The adiabatic expansion method can be used to compute breakup amplitudes for 1+2 reactions, but the convergence of the breakup amplitude in terms of the hyperspherical harmonic basis becomes extremely slow for particular kinematic configurations, specifically when two particles move away close to each other in a relative zero-energy state, requiring a huge number of basis states; this difficulty is overcome by extracting the breakup amplitude from an integral relation.

## Method

The three-body wave function was expanded in the hyperspherical adiabatic (HA) basis, and the hyperradial functions were obtained by solving a set of second-order coupled differential equations. The S-matrix was computed using two integral relations derived from the Kohn variational principle, and the breakup amplitude was extracted from an additional integral relation to avoid convergence problems in the hyperspherical harmonic expansion. The method was applied to neutron-deuteron scattering using a semi-realistic s-wave force, and the results were compared to available benchmark calculations.

## Key numbers

- For the n-d case with total angular momentum J=3/2, the function \(\tilde{u}^{s_x=1}(r_{np})\) obtained from the lowest breakup angular eigenfunction \(\Phi_2^{J=1/2}(\rho,\Omega_\rho)\) was shown for fixed Jacobi coordinate y values of 57 fm, 85 fm, and 114 fm, matching the zero-energy neutron-proton wave function up to almost 20 fm for y=114 fm.
- The incident energy in the center of mass frame for n-d is given by \(E_{in}=2E_{in}^{(lab)}/3\), where \(E_{in}^{(lab)}\) is the lab frame incident energy.
- The projectile-dimer reduced mass for n-d is \(\mu_y=2m/3\), where m is the nucleon mass.
- The three-body momentum \(\kappa\) is related to total energy E by \(\kappa=\sqrt{2mE}/\hbar\).
- The incident momentum in the lab frame is \(p_y^{(lab)}=\sqrt{2mE_{in}^{(lab)}}/\hbar\).

## Context

This work extends the hyperspherical adiabatic expansion method, previously applied to elastic, inelastic, and recombination processes below the breakup threshold (Romero-Redondo et al., 2011; Kievsky et al., 2010; Garrido et al., 2012), to explicitly compute breakup amplitudes above the dimer breakup threshold. The method addresses convergence difficulties noted in earlier HH-based variational calculations of breakup amplitudes (Kievsky et al., 1997, 1999; Viviani et al., 2001) and provides an alternative to Faddeev equation approaches (Witała et al., 1998; Glöckle et al., 1996; Deltuva et al., 2005). The n-d benchmark calculations by Friar et al. (1995) are used for validation, and the method is intended as a first step toward describing N-d scattering with realistic two- and three-body forces including Coulomb interaction.
