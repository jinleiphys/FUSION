# Information theory in high energy physics (extensive and nonextensive approach)

## Key claim
The information theory (MaxEnt) approach, in both its extensive and nonextensive (Tsallis) forms, can describe single-particle rapidity distributions across all types of high-energy collisions (e+e- annihilations, pp and ppbar scatterings, and heavy-ion collisions) using only two parameters, inelasticity K and nonextensivity q, but the fit for e+e- annihilations requires q < 1, which the authors interpret as a sign that conservation laws prevent a stationary final temperature from developing, rather than as a failure of the method.

## Method
The authors apply the MaxEnt principle with Tsallis entropy to derive single-particle rapidity distributions p(y) = (1/N) dN/dy = (1/Z_q) exp_q(-beta_q * mu_T * cosh y), where the parameters Z_q and beta_q are fixed by normalization and energy conservation constraints rather than fitted freely. They then compare these distributions to experimental rapidity spectra for charged pions in pp and ppbar collisions at various energies, in central Au+Au collisions at 19.6, 130, and 200 GeV, and in e+e- annihilations at 91.2 GeV.

## Key numbers
- For central Au+Au collisions (impact parameter 0-6%): q = 1.29 at 19.6 GeV, q = 1.26 at 130 GeV, and q = 1.27 at 200 GeV; the q-inelasticity kappa_q was 0.42, 0.48, and 0.46 respectively.
- For e+e- annihilations at 91.2 GeV: the fit required q = 0.6 with K_q = 1.
- The nonextensivity parameter q is related to the Negative Binomial parameter k by q = 1 + 1/k.
- The physically meaningful inelasticity parameter is given by K_q = kappa_q / (3 - 2q).

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous application of nonextensive MaxEnt to pp and ppbar collisions (qMaxEnt, 2004) by demonstrating that the same formalism also describes heavy-ion and e+e- data. It contrasts with standard thermal models (e.g., those reviewed in STAT, HAG) that treat temperature and chemical potential as free fit parameters, and with earlier information-theory approaches (Chao, 1997) that used only extensive Shannon entropy. The results enable model-independent extraction of inelasticity and fluctuation parameters from rapidity distributions, and the authors suggest that future work should focus on discrepancies at small and large rapidities by introducing additional physical mechanisms one-by-one (Next, 2004).
