# On freeze-out problem in relativistic hydrodynamics

> A finite unbound system which is equilibrium in one reference frame is in general nonequilibrium in another frame. This is a consequence of the relative character of the time synchronization in the relativistic physics. This puzzle was a prime motivation of the Cooper--Frye approach to the freeze-out in relativistic hydrodynamics. Solution of the puzzle reveals that the Cooper--Frye recipe is far not a unique phenomenological method that meets requirements of energy-momentum conservation. Alternative freeze-out recipes are considered and discussed.

# On freeze-out problem in relativistic hydrodynamics

## Key claim

The Cooper–Frye freeze-out recipe is not a unique phenomenological method that satisfies energy-momentum conservation; alternative freeze-out prescriptions exist and are viable, but all sharp freeze-out methods are questionable because the freeze-out process is extended in space and time rather than occurring on a 3-dimensional hypersurface.

## Method

The authors analyze the Lorentz transformation properties of an equilibrium distribution function for a finite unbound system (a droplet of nucleons) and demonstrate that a system which is equilibrium in one reference frame is generally nonequilibrium in another frame due to the relativity of simultaneity. They compare three freeze-out recipes: freeze-out in the computation frame, a modified Milekhin method (freeze-out in the local-rest frame), and the Cooper–Frye method, evaluating each against requirements of energy-momentum conservation and consistency with microscopic treatments based on the Boltzmann equation (Sinyukov 2002, 2008) and Kadanoff–Baym equations (Knoll 2008).

## Key numbers

- The freeze-out criterion used in the modified Milekhin method sets the local energy density threshold at ε = 0.4 GeV/fm³.
- The initial energy density for the 1D step-like slab calculation is ε₀ ≈ 3 GeV/fm³.
- Characteristic curves for ε = 0.7 GeV/fm³ remain fairly unaffected by freeze-out removal, while ε = 0.4 GeV/fm³ curves differ between calculations with and without removal of frozen-out matter.
- Spectra calculated with the modified Milekhin method plus entropy conservation coincide with those without the entropy conservation requirement within 1%.

## Context

This work extends the foundational discussion of freeze-out in relativistic hydrodynamics that began with Milekhin (1959) in the context of the Landau hydrodynamic model, and was later criticized by Cooper and Frye (1974) for not conserving energy. The paper resolves a puzzle about the Lorentz transformation of equilibrium distributions that motivated the Cooper–Frye approach, showing that the Cooper–Frye recipe is not unique. The analysis enables comparison of phenomenological freeze-out methods with microscopic treatments from Sinyukov (2002, 2008) and Knoll (2008), which approximately justify the Cooper–Frye formula only on space-like parts of the freeze-out hypersurface but not on time-like parts where negative spectra can occur. The work identifies the open question of which phenomenological method most closely approximates the microscopic results, given that numerical implementation of microscopic methods in 3D hydrodynamic simulations remains highly complicated.
