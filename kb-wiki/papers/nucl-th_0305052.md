# Flow Equations for Hamiltonians from Continuous Unitary Transformations

> This thesis presents an overview of the flow equations recently introduced by Wegner. The little known mathematical framework of the flow in the manifold of unitarily equivalent matrices, as discovered in the mathematical literature before Wegner's paper, is established in the initial chapter and used as a background for the entire presentation. The application of flow equations to the Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation and to the elimination of the electron-phonon coupling in a solid is reviewed. Recent flow equations approaches to the Lipkin model are examined thoroughly, paying special attention to their utility near the phase change boundary. We present more robust schemes by requiring that expectation values be flow dependent; either through a variational or self-consistent calculation. The similarity renormalization group equations recently developed by Glazek and Wilson are also reviewed. Their relationship to Wegner's flow equations is investigated through the aid of an instructive model.

## Key claim

This thesis argues that Wegner's flow equations, which continuously diagonalize a Hamiltonian via a unitary transformation, can be made more robust by requiring that expectation values be flow-dependent through variational or self-consistent calculations, particularly near phase change boundaries where standard approaches struggle.

## Method

The author reviews the mathematical framework of flow equations as continuous unitary transformations on Hamiltonians, establishing that Wegner's generator choice η(ℓ) = [Diag(H(ℓ)), H(ℓ)] produces steepest descent curves toward diagonality in matrix space. The method is applied to the Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation, the elimination of electron-phonon coupling in solids, and the Lipkin model, with perturbative solutions derived to second order in the coupling constant. The relationship between Wegner's flow equations and the similarity renormalization group equations of Glazek and Wilson is investigated through an instructive model.

## Key numbers

- For the perturbative solution of the flow equations, diagonal elements ε_i(ℓ) = E_i + g² Σ_k (V_ik²/Δ_ik)(1 - e^{-2Δ_ik²ℓ}) + ... to second order in g, where Δ_ij = E_i - E_j
- Off-diagonal elements v_ij(ℓ) = gV_ij e^{-Δ_ij²ℓ} + ... to first order in g, decaying exponentially to zero
- The manifold of n×n Hermitian matrices unitarily equivalent to H_0 has dimension n²-n when eigenvalues are distinct
- For 3×3 real matrices, the manifold of unitarily equivalent matrices is a sphere with 2 handles
- For 3×3 real tridiagonal matrices with distinct eigenvalues λ₁ > λ₂ > λ₃, the manifold breaks into four closed components (++, +-, -+, --), with the ++ component homeomorphic to a hexagon

## Context

This work extends the flow equations introduced by Wegner (1994) and connects them to earlier mathematical results by Deift (1983) on isospectral flows. It reviews applications to the Foldy-Wouthuysen transformation and electron-phonon coupling elimination, and thoroughly examines flow equation approaches to the Lipkin model by Meshkov, Lipkin and Glick (1965). The thesis also reviews the similarity renormalization group equations developed by Glazek and Wilson (1993, 1994) and investigates their relationship to Wegner's flow equations. This work enables more robust schemes for treating Hamiltonians near phase transitions and provides a unified mathematical framework connecting flow equations to steepest descent optimization on matrix manifolds.
