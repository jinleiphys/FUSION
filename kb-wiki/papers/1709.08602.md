# Dynamical vs geometric anisotropy in relativistic heavy-ion collisions: which one prevails?

> We study the influence of geometric and dynamical anisotropies on the development of flow harmonics and, simultaneously, on the second- and third-order oscillations of femtoscopy radii. The analysis is done within the Monte Carlo event generator HYDJET++, which was extended to dynamical triangular deformations. It is shown that the merely geometric anisotropy provides the results which anticorrelate with the experimental observations of either $v_2$ (or $v_3$) or second-order (or third-order) oscillations of the femtoscopy radii. Decays of resonances significantly increase the emitting areas but do not change the phases of the radii oscillations. In contrast to the spatial deformations, the dynamical anisotropy alone provides the correct qualitative description of the flow and the femtoscopy observables simultaneously. However, one needs both types of the anisotropy to match quantitatively the experimental data.

## Key claim

Geometric anisotropy alone (spatial deformation of the freeze-out hypersurface) cannot simultaneously reproduce the correct sign of elliptic or triangular flow and the correct phase of femtoscopy radii oscillations; dynamical anisotropy (momentum-space modulation of the flow velocity profile) alone gives the correct qualitative behavior for both observables, but a combination of both types of anisotropy is required for quantitative agreement with experimental data.

## Method

The authors used the Monte Carlo event generator HYDJET++, extended to include dynamical triangular deformations via a new parameter ρ₃(b) modulating the transverse flow rapidity, to simulate Pb+Pb collisions at √s = 2.76 TeV. They independently varied the geometric anisotropy parameters (ε₂ for elliptic, ε₃ for triangular spatial deformations) and the dynamical anisotropy parameters (δ₂ for elliptic, ρ₃ for triangular velocity-profile modulations), switching each on or off to isolate their separate effects on the flow harmonics v₂ and v₃ and on the second- and third-order azimuthal oscillations of the femtoscopy radii R_out, R_side, and R_long.

## Key numbers

- Elliptic flow v₂(p_T) is negative for p_T ≤ 5 GeV/c when only geometric anisotropy ε₂ = 0.3 is used, contradicting experimental observations; switching to ε₂ = −0.3 gives positive v₂ but shifts the phases of R²_side and R²_out oscillations by π/2.
- Dynamical anisotropy δ₂ = 0.3 (with ε₂ = 0) produces positive differential elliptic flow v₂(p_T) and the correct qualitative pattern: local minimum of R²_side and local maximum of R²_out at Δφ₂ ≈ π/2.
- For triangular flow, geometric anisotropy ε₃ = 0.3 yields negative v₃(p_T) for all transverse momenta, while ε₃ = −0.3 gives positive v₃(p_T) but with R²_side and R²_out extrema at positions inconsistent with ALICE data at LHC.
- Dynamical triangular anisotropy ρ₃ = 0.3 (with ε₃ = 0) reproduces the experimentally observed pattern: both R²_out and R²_side have maxima at Δφ₃ ≈ π/3, and v₃(p_T) is positive.
- The HYDJET++ model reproduces the measured k_T-dependencies of R_out and R_long in 5% most central collisions within about 8% for R_long.

## Context

This work extends previous studies using ideal hydrodynamic models, toy Gaussian-source models, the Buda-Lund model, and the blast-wave model (e.g., Wiedemann (1997), Heinz and Kolb (2002), Retiere and Lisa (2004), and the Cracow model THERMINATOR) that investigated the sensitivity of femtoscopy correlations and flow harmonics to source anisotropy. The authors show that earlier conclusions attributing second-order radii oscillations to geometric anisotropy and third-order oscillations to triangular flow anisotropy are incomplete, because geometric anisotropy alone anticorrelates with experimental observations of either v₂/v₃ or the radii oscillations. The results enable future quantitative extraction of the relative contributions of geometric and dynamical anisotropies from combined flow and femtoscopy measurements at RHIC and LHC.
