# Scatter-Limited Hybrid Monte Carlo, Deterministic Transport with Quasi-Monte Carlo Sampling

> We present a hybrid method for time-dependent particle transport that combines Monte Carlo (MC) estimation with a deterministic discrete ordinates (\(S_N\)) solve, augmented by quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) sampling. For spatial discretizations, the MC component computes a piecewise-constant (cell-averaged) solution, while the \(S_N\) stage employs bilinear discontinuous finite elements. By hybridizing the formulation, the MC subproblem after a prescribed scatter limit becomes scattering-free, yielding a simple and efficient streaming/attenuation procedure. Between time steps, a simple scatter-free MC step is run to relabel the $S_N$ solution as an MC solution. A key feature of the approach is a tunable parameter \(N_{s}\) that controls how many material collisions are handled in the (Q)MC leg before handing off to the deterministic \(S_N\) solve; \(N_s=0\) recovers a purely uncollided MC leg, while \(N_s>0\) produces multi-scatter hybrids. QMC replaces pseudorandom draws with low-discrepancy points in the existing MC sampling maps, enabling a plug-in adoption within the standard MC code with modest, localized changes. We observe significant accuracy and convergence rate improvements through the use of QMC and practically no additional computational cost, which are generally not seen in comparable non-hybrid solves. We believe the multi-scatter approach provides additional flexibility in terms of parallelization and the choice of deterministic solver.

# Scatter-Limited Hybrid Monte Carlo, Deterministic Transport with Quasi-Monte Carlo Sampling

## Key claim

The hybrid method achieves significant accuracy and convergence rate improvements through the use of quasi-Monte Carlo (QMC) sampling at practically no additional computational cost, but this benefit is contingent on the specific algorithmic formulation that uses a relabeling step (step 3 in the algorithm) rather than the alternative additive approach (steps 3' and 4'), which does not benefit from QMC.

## Method

The method splits the time-dependent linear transport equation into two coupled subproblems: a scatter-limited Monte Carlo (MC) leg that handles particles up to a prescribed number of material collisions \(N_s\), and a deterministic discrete ordinates (\(S_N\)) solve with discontinuous Galerkin spatial discretization for the remaining collided flux. Between time steps, a scatter-free MC step relabels the \(S_N\) solution back into an MC representation to prevent exponential depletion of low-scatter particles. Quasi-Monte Carlo replaces pseudorandom draws with Halton low-discrepancy points in the existing MC sampling maps, requiring only modest localized code changes.

## Key numbers

- Convergence rate \(\alpha_{QMC} = -0.69\) for Reed's problem with \(N_s = 0\), compared to \(\alpha_{MC} = -0.50\) for the same case, measured via least-square fit of \(c N_p^{\alpha}\) to the \(L^2\) error.
- Convergence rate \(\alpha_{QMC} = -0.61\) for the Dogleg problem with \(N_s = 0\), compared to \(\alpha_{MC} = -0.49\) for the same case.
- For the non-hybrid case (\(N_s = \infty\)), convergence rates were \(\alpha_{QMC} = -0.56\) and \(\alpha_{MC} = -0.46\) for Reed's problem, and \(\alpha_{QMC} = -0.53\) and \(\alpha_{MC} = -0.47\) for the Dogleg problem.
- Number of \(S_N\) iterations decreased from 31 at \(N_s = 0\) to 12.2 at \(N_s = 20\) for Reed's problem, and from 7 at \(N_s = 0\) to 1 at \(N_s = 10\) and \(N_s = 20\) for the Dogleg problem.
- Spatial resolution was \(N_x = 80\) for Reed's problem and \(N_x \times N_y = 30 \times 50\) for the Dogleg benchmark.

## Context

This work extends the streaming-scattering splitting approach previously developed by Krotz (2024) and related collision-source splitting methods by Alcouffe (2006), introducing a tunable multi-scatter parameter \(N_s\) that controls how many collisions are handled by the MC leg before transfer to the deterministic solver. The method builds on prior hybrid strategies including regional-coupling frameworks by Mosher (2006) and Wagner (2014), and high-order/low-order closures by Park (2014). The multi-scatter capability enables flexible parallelization and the potential to use cheaper deterministic solvers (e.g., diffusion) for the collided component, while the QMC integration provides a plug-in pathway to variance reduction without substantial changes to existing MC sampling procedures.
