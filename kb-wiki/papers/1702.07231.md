# An overview of experimental results from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at the CERN LHC: hard probes

> The first collisions of lead nuclei, delivered by the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at the end of 2010, at a centre-of-mass energy per nucleon pair $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV, marked the beginning of a new era in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion physics. The study of the properties of the produced hot and dense strongly-interacting matter at these unprecedented energies is currently experimentally pursued by all four big LHC experiments, ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb. The more than a factor 10 increase of collision energy at LHC, relative to the previously achieved maximal energy at other collider facilities, results in an increase of production rates of hard probes. This review presents selected experimental results focusing on observables probing hard processes in heavy-ion collisions delivered during the first three years of the LHC operation. It also presents the first results from Run 2 heavy-ion data at the highest energy, as well as from the studies of the reference pp and pPb systems, which are an integral part of the heavy-ion programme.

# An overview of experimental results from ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions at the CERN LHC: hard probes

## Key claim

The paper reviews experimental results on hard probes from the first three years of LHC heavy-ion operation, showing that the factor-of-ten increase in collision energy relative to previous facilities enables precision studies of jet quenching, heavy-flavour energy loss, and quarkonium suppression; however, the observed suppression patterns require disentangling competing mechanisms such as parton energy loss, cold nuclear matter effects, and (re)combination, with the predicted mass hierarchy of energy loss (gluon > light quark > charm > bottom) not yet conclusively confirmed for light versus charm hadrons at intermediate transverse momentum.

## Method

The review compiles measurements from the ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb collaborations of hard probes in lead-lead (PbPb) collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV and 5.02 TeV, together with reference proton-proton (pp) and proton-lead (pPb) collisions at the same nucleon-nucleon energies. The nuclear modification factor R_AA and elliptic flow coefficient v_2 are used to quantify medium-induced modifications of high-transverse-momentum particles, reconstructed jets, heavy-flavour mesons, and quarkonia, with comparisons to perturbative QCD models incorporating radiative and collisional energy loss.

## Key numbers

- D meson yield in central (0-10%) PbPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV is suppressed by a factor of about four at pT around 10 GeV/c
- D meson R_AA measured up to pT = 36 GeV/c for two centrality intervals (0-10% and 30-50%) in PbPb at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- Non-prompt J/psi R_AA shows significant suppression of beauty for all measured centralities in the range 6.5 < pT < 30 GeV/c
- b-jet suppression observed in the range 80 < pT < 250 GeV/c in PbPb at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- D meson v_2 measured in centrality range 30-50% for pT range 2-8 GeV/c, compatible with charged-particle v_2
- Number of c-cbar pairs per event increases from 0.2 at SPS sqrt(s_NN) = 17.3 GeV to 10 at RHIC sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, and up to 85 at LHC sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV
- J/psi binding energy in vacuum is 0.64 GeV; Upsilon(1S) binding energy is 1.10 GeV
- Upsilon(1S) radius is 0.14 fm; J/psi radius is 0.25 fm

## Context

This review extends the heavy-ion physics programme from RHIC (where jet quenching was first established in AuAu collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV by experiments such as PHENIX and STAR) to the higher energies of the LHC, where the increased production rates of hard probes allow systematic studies of energy loss mechanisms proposed by Bjorken (1982) and subsequent theoretical developments. The simultaneous measurement of open heavy flavour and quarkonia at LHC addresses questions left open by SPS experiments (where open charm was not accessible) and enables tests of sequential quarkonium melting as a QGP thermometer, with lattice QCD calculations predicting dissociation temperatures for different bound states relative to the critical temperature T_c.
