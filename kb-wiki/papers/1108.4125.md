# On the Cooling of the Neutron Star in Cassiopeia A

> We demonstrate that the high-quality cooling data observed for the young neutron star in the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A over the past 10 years--as well as all other reliably known temperature data of neutron stars--can be comfortably explained within the "nuclear medium cooling" scenario. The cooling rates of this scenario account for medium-modified one-pion exchange in dense matter and polarization effects in the pair-breaking formations of superfluid neutrons and protons. Crucial for the successful description of the observed data is a substantial reduction of the thermal conductivity, resulting from a suppression of both the electron and nucleon contributions to it by medium effects. We also find that possibly in as little as about ten years of continued observation, the data may tell whether or not fast cooling processes are active in this neutron star.

## Key claim

The "nuclear medium cooling" scenario can explain the observed rapid cooling of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A over the past 10 years, as well as all other reliably known neutron star temperature data, provided the thermal conductivity is substantially reduced by medium effects (by a factor of about 0.265 for the best-fit model). The authors note that within about ten more years of continued observation, the data may distinguish whether fast cooling processes (pion condensation) are active in this neutron star.

## Method

The authors computed neutron star cooling curves using the "nuclear medium cooling scenario," which incorporates medium-modified one-pion exchange (MOPE) for the nucleon-nucleon interaction, polarization effects in pair-breaking formation (PBF) processes for superfluid neutrons and protons, and a suppressed thermal conductivity. They used a relativistic version of the Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall (APR) equation of state, adopted tiny values for the neutron \(^3P_2\) pairing gap following Schwenk and Friman (2003), and tested two models for the proton \(^1S_0\) gap. The thermal conductivity was reduced by a factor \(\zeta_\kappa\) to fit the observed surface temperature decline of Cas A from 2000 to 2009.

## Key numbers

- Surface temperature of Cas A decreased from \(2.12 \times 10^6\) K to \(2.04 \times 10^6\) K over the 10-year period from 2000 to 2009.
- Best-fit model: neutron star mass \(M = 1.463~M_\odot\) with thermal conductivity suppression factor \(\zeta_\kappa = 0.265\).
- For a lower mass of \(M = 1.390~M_\odot\), the cooling curve covers the whole set of available neutron star cooling data.
- Without pion condensation, the Cas A data can be reproduced with \(\zeta_\kappa = 0.220\) and mass \(M = 1.506~M_\odot\).
- For proton gap model II, Cas A data can be fitted for \(\zeta_\kappa \leq 0.015\) and masses \(M \geq 1.73~M_\odot\).
- The critical temperature for the neutron \(^3P_2\) pairing gap in the alternative minimal cooling scenario is around \(0.5 \times 10^9\) K (Page et al. 2010) or \((0.7-0.9) \times 10^9\) K (Yakovlev et al. 2010).
- The ratio of medium-modified Urca (MMU) emissivity to standard modified Urca (MU) emissivity scales as \(\sim 10^3 (n/n_0)^{10/3} \Gamma^6(n) / [\omega^*(n)/m_\pi]^8\), where \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is nuclear saturation density.

## Context

This work extends the nuclear medium cooling scenario previously developed by Voskresensky (1986, 1987), Migdal (1990), and applied by Schaab (1996), Blaschke (2004), and Grigorian (2005). It provides an alternative to the "minimal cooling" interpretations of Page et al. (2010) and Yakovlev et al. (2010), which explained the Cas A data using strong \(^3P_2\) neutron pairing and PBF processes with free one-pion exchange. The present scenario instead attributes the rapid cooling to efficient medium-modified Urca and bremsstrahlung processes, a very small neutron \(^3P_2\) gap, a large proton gap, and suppressed thermal conductivity. Continued monitoring of Cas A over the next decade may discriminate between models with and without fast cooling processes such as pion condensation.
