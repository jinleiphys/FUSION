# Quark Coalescence with Quark Number Conservation and the Effect on Quark-Hadron Scaling

> We develop a new formulation of the quark coalescence model by including the quark number conservation in order to describe the hadronization of the bulk of the quark-gluon plasma. The scalings between hadron and quark phase space distributions are shown to depend on the transverse momentum. For hard quarks, our general scalings reproduce the usual quadratic scaling relation for mesons and the cubic scaling relation for baryons. For softer quarks, however, the inclusion of the quark number conservation leads to a linear scaling for the hadron species that dominates the quark number of each flavor, while the scalings of non-dominant hadrons depend on the coalescence dynamics. For charm mesons, we find that the distribution of soft $D$ mesons does not depend on the light quark distribution but the distribution of soft $J/ψ$ mesons is inversely correlated to the light quark distribution.

## Key claim

The paper develops a new formulation of the quark coalescence model that includes quark number conservation (unitarity), showing that the scaling relations between hadron and quark phase space distributions depend on transverse momentum: for hard quarks the usual quadratic (meson) and cubic (baryon) scalings are recovered, but for soft quarks the inclusion of quark number conservation leads to a linear scaling for the hadron species that dominates the quark number of each flavor, while the scalings of non-dominant hadrons depend on the coalescence dynamics.

## Method

The author formulates rate equations for the time evolution of quark, meson, and baryon phase space distributions during coalescence, incorporating quark number conservation equations for each flavor. Analytical solutions are obtained for the cases of one meson and one baryon species (in the limits where mesons or baryons dominate) and for charm mesons (D and J/psi), assuming that coalescence coefficients have the same time-dependence and that internal momenta of coalescing quarks can be neglected. The coalescence probability is defined, and a scale p_perp_0 is introduced to separate hard quarks (low coalescence probability) from soft quarks (coalescence probability near one).

## Key numbers

* The scale p_perp_0 is defined as the quark transverse momentum where the coalescence probability equals 1/2, corresponding to f_0(p_q) I_M(p_M,t_F) = 1 for light quarks in the meson-dominated case.
* For hard quarks, the coalescence probability is proportional to the quark distribution function: p_coal_hard_q(p_q) approximately equals f_0(p_q) I_M(p_M,t_F), which is much less than 1.
* For soft quarks in the meson-dominated case, the final meson distribution is f_soft_M(p_M,t_F) approximately equals f_0(p_q)/2^3 (where 2^3 = n_qM^3 = 8), giving a linear scaling with the quark distribution.
* For soft quarks in the baryon-dominated case, the final baryon distribution is f_soft_B(p_B,t_F) approximately equals f_0(p_q)/(3 * 3^3) = f_0(p_q)/81, giving a linear scaling for baryons.
* For charm mesons, the scale for charm quarks p_perp_0,c equals p'_perp_0 * m_c/m_q, where p'_perp_0 is the light quark scale corresponding to f_0(p_q) I_M(p_M,t_F) = 2^(1/r_D) - 1.
* For soft D mesons, the distribution f_soft_D(p_D,t_F) approximately equals f_c0(p_c) * z_cD^3, where z_cD = m_c/(m_c+m_q), meaning it depends only on the charm quark distribution.
* For soft J/psi mesons with r_D >= 1, the distribution is proportional to f_c0^2(p_c) / f_0(p_q), showing an inverse correlation with the light quark distribution.

## Context

This work extends the standard quark coalescence model used to describe hadronization in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, which previously violated unitarity by not conserving quark numbers. The new formulation addresses the bulk of soft partons in the quark-gluon plasma, where coalescence is the dominant hadronization process, unlike earlier approaches such as the algebraic coalescence rehadronization (ALCOR) model by Zimanyi and collaborators (2000s) that used normalization constants to enforce quark number conservation, and the study by Yang (2005) that addressed total multiplicities. The results enable future quantitative predictions for hadron elliptic flow scaling at low transverse momentum and for heavy flavor observables, particularly the inverse correlation between soft J/psi and light quark distributions, though the author notes that numerical estimates of coalescence coefficients and momentum scales are needed for quantitative model predictions.
