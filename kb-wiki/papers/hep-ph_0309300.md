# GADZOOKS! Antineutrino Spectroscopy with Large Water Cerenkov Detectors

> We propose modifying large water Čerenkov detectors by the addition of 0.2% gadolinium trichloride, which is highly soluble, newly inexpensive, and transparent in solution. Since Gd has an enormous cross section for radiative neutron capture, with $\sum E_γ= 8$ MeV, this would make neutrons visible for the first time in such detectors, allowing antineutrino tagging by the coincidence detection reaction $\barν_e + p \to e^+ + n$ (similarly for $\barν_μ$). Taking Super-Kamiokande as a working example, dramatic consequences for reactor neutrino measurements, first observation of the diffuse supernova neutrino background, Galactic supernova detection, and other topics are discussed.

## Key claim

Adding 0.2% gadolinium trichloride to large water Cerenkov detectors like Super-Kamiokande would make neutrons visible for the first time in such detectors, enabling antineutrino tagging via the coincidence reaction \(\bar{\nu}_e + p \to e^+ + n\), but the feasibility depends on achieving adequate radiopurity of the additive and verifying that light attenuation remains acceptable over the detector's scale.

## Method

The authors propose dissolving 0.2% by mass of gadolinium trichloride (GdCl\(_3\)) in the water of large Cerenkov detectors, exploiting the 49000 barn thermal neutron capture cross section of natural gadolinium to produce an 8 MeV gamma cascade. They calculate that 90% of neutron captures would occur on Gd within about 20 \(\mu\)s after thermalization, and evaluate the resulting coincidence signal for antineutrino detection using Super-Kamiokande as a concrete example. The analysis includes estimates of ambient neutron backgrounds from cosmic ray muon spallation, radioactive contaminants, and the detector's trigger capabilities, as well as projections for reactor neutrino monitoring, diffuse supernova neutrino background detection, and Galactic supernova observations.

## Key numbers

- 0.2%: proposed mass fraction of GdCl\(_3\) to be added to water in the detector.
- 49000 barns: thermal neutron capture cross section on natural Gd, compared to 0.3 barns on free protons.
- 90%: fraction of neutron captures expected on Gd with the proposed admixture.
- 20 \(\mu\)s: approximate capture time after thermalization, about 10 times faster than in pure water.
- 8 MeV: total gamma energy released in neutron capture on Gd.
- 5 MeV: peak equivalent single electron energy from the Gd capture gamma cascade in water Cerenkov detectors.
- 30/day: expected rate of \(\bar{\nu}_e + p \to e^+ + n\) interactions from nuclear reactors in Super-Kamiokande after oscillations.
- 2-6 per year: expected number of diffuse supernova neutrino background events above 10 MeV in GADZOOKS!.
- 3-8 MeV: range of equivalent single electron energy from Gd capture gammas.
- 2.5 MeV: lowest positron energy at which coincidence detection should be possible.
- 10\(^5\) neutrons/day: approximate rate of cosmic ray muon spallation neutrons produced in Super-Kamiokande.
- 10\(^{10}\)/day: decay rate of \(^{152}\)Gd alpha decays from 100 tons of GdCl\(_3\) in Super-Kamiokande.
- 1 neutron/day: estimated neutron production from \(^{152}\)Gd alpha decays via \(^{17}\)O(\(\alpha\),n) and \(^{18}\)O(\(\alpha\),n) reactions.
- 4.5 m: thickness of water shielding surrounding the fiducial volume that stops neutrons from the surrounding rock.
- 2\(^\circ\): improved pointing accuracy to a Galactic supernova by isolating inverse beta events.

## Context

This paper extends the concept of neutron detection via gadolinium from smaller scintillator detectors (where Gd is used routinely) and from the SNO heavy water Cerenkov detector (which used Gd in thin foils) to large light water Cerenkov detectors for the first time. The proposal directly enables the first detection of the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), which previous limits from Super-Kamiokande (Malek, 2003) would require about 40 years to achieve without this technique. The work also builds on reactor neutrino measurements from KamLAND (2002) and supernova neutrino studies following SN 1987A, and opens the path to megaton-scale detectors that could observe hundreds of DSNB events per year.
