# Chiral crossover characterized by Mott transition at finite temperature

> We discuss the proper definition for the chiral crossover at finite temperature, based on the Goldstone's theorem. Different from the usually used maximum change of chiral condensate, we propose to define the crossover temperature by the Mott transition of pseudo-Goldstone bosons, which, by definition, guarantees the Goldstone's theorem. We analytically and numerically demonstrate this property in frame of a Pauli-Villars regularized NJL model. In external magnetic field, we find that the Mott transition temperature shows an inverse magnetic catalysis effect.

# Chiral crossover characterized by Mott transition at finite temperature

## Key claim
The chiral crossover temperature should be defined by the Mott transition of pseudo-Goldstone bosons (where the pion mass equals twice the quark mass) rather than by the maximum change of the chiral condensate, because the latter breaks Goldstone's theorem in the physical case with nonzero current quark mass. In the Pauli-Villars regularized NJL model, the Mott transition temperature differs from the pseudo-critical temperature and shows inverse magnetic catalysis, regardless of whether the pseudo-critical temperature exhibits magnetic catalysis or inverse magnetic catalysis.

## Method
The author calculates the dynamical quark mass (order parameter) in mean-field approximation and the pion mass (pseudo-Goldstone mode) in random phase approximation (RPA) within a two-flavor Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model regularized by the Pauli-Villars scheme. The Mott transition temperature is defined by the threshold condition m_pi(T_m) = 2 m_q(T_m), and the pseudo-critical temperature is defined by the inflection point of the quark mass, d^2 m_q / dT^2 = 0. Calculations are performed both in chiral limit (m_0 = 0) and in the physical case (m_0 = 6.4 MeV), with and without an external magnetic field.

## Key numbers
- In chiral limit at B = 0, the critical temperature is T_c = 163 MeV, and the Mott transition temperature coincides with it: T_m = T_c = 163 MeV.
- In the physical case at B = 0, the pseudo-critical temperature is T_pc = 162 MeV, while the Mott transition temperature is T_m = 174 MeV, showing T_m > T_pc.
- The NJL model parameters are: current quark mass m_0 = 6.4 MeV (physical case) or 0 (chiral limit); coupling constant G = 4.9 GeV^{-2} (physical) or 5.03 GeV^{-2} (chiral limit); Pauli-Villars mass parameter Lambda = 977.3 MeV; vacuum chiral condensate <psi-bar psi> = -(230 MeV)^3; vacuum pion mass m_pi = 134 MeV; vacuum pion decay constant f_pi = 93 MeV.
- When modeling lattice QCD results with a magnetic-field-dependent coupling, at eB/m_pi^2 = 10: G(B)/G(0) = 0.97, T_pc(B)/T_pc(0) = 0.99, T_m(B)/T_m(0) = 0.88; at eB/m_pi^2 = 20: G(B)/G(0) = 0.9, T_pc(B)/T_pc(0) = 0.97, T_m(B)/T_m(0) = 0.39.

## Context
This work extends the study of chiral symmetry restoration in QCD by proposing a new definition for the crossover temperature, motivated by the Goldstone theorem. It contrasts with the standard approach used in lattice QCD simulations (which give T_pc about 156 MeV) and in effective models, where the pseudo-critical temperature is defined via the inflection point of the chiral condensate. The author shows that in the NJL model, the Mott transition temperature exhibits inverse magnetic catalysis, while the pseudo-critical temperature shows magnetic catalysis, offering a potential resolution to the long-standing discrepancy between lattice QCD (which finds inverse magnetic catalysis for T_pc) and mean-field effective models (which find magnetic catalysis for T_pc). The work calls for lattice QCD calculations of the pion mass at finite temperature and magnetic field to precisely determine the Mott transition temperature.
