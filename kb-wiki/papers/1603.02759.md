# Improvement on Fermionic properties and new isotope production in molecular dynamics simulations

> By considering momentum transfer in the Fermi constraint procedure, the stability of the initial nuclei and fragments produced in heavy-ion collisions can be further improved in the quantum molecular dynamics simulations. The case of the phase space occupation probability larger than one is effectively reduced with the proposed procedure. Simultaneously, the energy conservation can be better described for both individual nuclei and heavy-ion reactions. With the revised version of the improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model, the fusion excitation functions of $^{16}$O+$^{186}$W and the central collisions of Au+Au at 35 AMeV are re-examined. The fusion cross sections at sub-barrier energies and the charge distribution of fragments are relatively better reproduced due to the reduction of spurious nucleon emission. The charge and isotope distribution of fragments in Xe+Sn, U+U and Zr+Sn at intermediate energies are also predicted. More unmeasured extremely neutron-rich fragments with $Z=16-28$ are observed in the central collisions of $^{238}$U+$^{238}$U than that of $^{96}$Zr+$^{124}$Sn, which indicates that multi-fragmentation of U+U may offer a fruitful pathway to new neutron-rich isotopes.

## Key claim
By adding a momentum-transfer step (two-body "inelastic scattering") to the Fermi constraint procedure in the improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model, the stability of initial nuclei and fragments is further improved and the number of "pseudo" nucleons with phase-space occupation probability greater than one is reduced, but the improvement is conditional on a small transfer factor (f_t = 5e-6) that must be tuned to preserve energy conservation.

## Method
The authors modified the ImQMD model (version v2.2) by introducing a momentum-transfer step between neighboring nucleons when their relative momentum exceeds the Fermi momentum (p_F = 260 MeV/c), in addition to the existing two-body "elastic scattering" constraint. They then simulated the time evolution of individual nuclei (132Sn, 197Au), fusion reactions (16O+186W), and central collisions of 197Au+197Au at 35 AMeV, as well as multi-fragmentation reactions for 129Xe+120Sn, 238U+238U, and 96Zr+124Sn at intermediate energies. The primary fragments were analyzed directly from the transport simulation, and for Au+Au the statistical decay code GEMINI was also applied to account for secondary de-excitation.

## Key numbers
- For 92Zr at t=2000 fm/c, the average number of spurious emitted nucleons was reduced by 50% (to 0.56) with v2.2 compared to v2.1.
- For 132Sn at t=2000 fm/c, the average number of spurious emitted nucleons was reduced by 33% (to 1.75) with v2.2 compared to v2.1.
- About 12% of nucleons in 132Sn and 16% in 197Au had occupation probability >1 with v2.1; these fractions were "evidently reduced" with v2.2 (exact reduced values not given in text).
- The momentum transfer factor used was f_t = 5e-6 for the IQ3a parameter set.
- The Fermi momentum cutoff for the momentum-transfer condition was p_F = 260 MeV/c.
- For 238U+238U at 35 AMeV, the estimated production probability for the unmeasured nuclei 60Ca and 78Ni was smaller than 5e-5 in central collisions.
- The incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter for the IQ3a parameter set is about 225 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the ImQMD model (previously developed by the same group, e.g., ImQMD 2002, 2004, 2006, 2010, 2014) by improving the Fermi constraint procedure, which was originally adopted from the CoMD model (constrain, 2002). The revision addresses residual spurious nucleon emission and energy drift that persisted in earlier versions (v2.1). The improved model enables more reliable predictions of fusion cross sections at sub-barrier energies and of neutron-rich fragment production in multi-fragmentation reactions, suggesting that central collisions of 238U+238U at 35 AMeV may produce unmeasured extremely neutron-rich isotopes with Z=16-28, whereas the neutron-rich system 96Zr+124Sn does not reach beyond known masses.
