# Pre-hydrodynamic evolution and its impact on quark-gluon plasma signatures

> State-of-the-art hydrodynamic models of heavy-ion collisions have considerable theoretical model uncertainties in the description of the very early pre-hydrodynamic stage. We add a new computational module, K$_\mathrm{T}$Iso, that describes the pre-hydrodynamic evolution kinetically, based on the relativistic Boltzmann equation with collisions treated in the Isotropization Time Approximation. As a novelty, K$_\mathrm{T}$Iso allows for the inclusion and evolution of initial-state momentum anisotropies. To maintain computational efficiency K$_\mathrm{T}$Iso assumes strict longitudinal boost invariance and allows collisions to isotropize only the transverse momenta. We use it to explore the sensitivity of hadronic observables measured in relativistic heavy-ion collisions to initial-state momentum anisotropies and microscopic scattering during the pre-hydrodynamic stage.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a computationally efficient pre-hydrodynamic module, K$_\mathrm{T}$Iso, that uses a simplified kinetic description based on the Isotropization Time Approximation but restricts collisions to isotropize only transverse momenta while assuming strict longitudinal boost invariance, thereby limiting its ability to study longitudinal pressure isotropization but enabling event-by-event simulations with fluctuating initial transverse density profiles.

## Method

The authors implement the relativistic Boltzmann equation with the Isotropization Time Approximation collision term, reducing the momentum-space dimensionality by assuming the distribution function remains proportional to $\delta(v_z)$ throughout the pre-hydrodynamic evolution. They use operator splitting to separately handle transverse advection via the MacCormack scheme, longitudinal Bjorken expansion via exact integration, and collisions via fourth-order Runge-Kutta propagation with adaptive time steps. Initial-state momentum anisotropies are introduced by parametrizing the transverse momentum distribution with a Bessel-Gaussian random field for the elliptic flow coefficient $\tilde{v}_2$ and a uniform random field for the flow angle $\tilde{\psi}_2$.

## Key numbers

- The proper time step-size is set to $\Delta \tau = \min(\Delta x / 8, \tauiso^{\rm min} / 8)$, where $\tauiso^{\rm min}$ is the minimal isotropization time across the grid.
- For the MacCormack scheme, the spatial grid spacing must satisfy $\Delta x < \sigma / 6$ where $\sigma$ is the smallest physical scale in the transverse plane, and the proper time spacing must obey $\Delta \tau < \Delta x / 8$.
- The isotropization time follows the conformal relation $T \tauiso = 5 (\eta/s)$, where $T$ is temperature and $\eta/s$ is the specific shear viscosity.
- For free-streaming validation, the initial temperature profile width is $b = 1$ fm, and the analytic solution for energy density after time $t$ involves the modified Bessel function $I_0$ with argument $-\frac{3}{4}\frac{|\vec{x}_\perp|t}{4b^2}$.
- For the Bessel-Gaussian random field, the mean $m$ is varied between 0 and 0.3, the variance $\sigma$ between 0 and 0.05, and correlation lengths $l_{bg}$ and $l_u$ are set to 1 fm or 2 fm.
- The comparison with the free-streaming code \texttt{freestream-milne} shows better than 5% agreement for energy density and shear stress components inside the fireball region after 1.5 fm/$c$ of evolution.

## Context

This work extends the pre-hydrodynamic modeling approach of Kurkela et al. (2018, 2019, 2020) who developed the Isotropization Time Approximation, and contrasts with the K$\emptyset$MP$\emptyset$ST module of Kurkela et al. (2018) which uses linear response theory but cannot handle large transverse gradients in small systems. The approximation of restricting collisions to transverse momenta differs from Ambrus et al. (2021) who incorporate all $v_z$-dependent terms. The module is designed for integration into Bayesian parameter estimation frameworks such as those used by the JETSCAPE collaboration (2020) and Everett et al. (2020), and enables systematic study of how initial-state momentum anisotropies from models like IP-Glasma (Schenke et al., 2012) affect final-state observables.
