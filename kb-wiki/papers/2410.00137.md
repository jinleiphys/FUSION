# Accessing universal relations of binary neutron star waveforms in massive scalar-tensor theory

> We investigate how the quasi-universal relations connecting tidal deformability with gravitational waveform characteristics and/or properties of individual neutron stars that were proposed in the literature within general relativity would be influenced in the massive Damour-Esposito-Farese-type scalar-tensor gravity. For this purpose, we systematically perform numerical relativity simulations of ~120 binary neutron star mergers with varying scalar coupling constants. Although only three neutron-star equations of state are adopted, a clear breach of universality can be observed in the data sets. In addition to presenting difficulties in constructing quasi-universal relations in alternative gravity theories, we also briefly compare the impacts of non-general-relativity physics on the waveform features and those due to the first order or cross-over quantum chromodynamical phase transition.

## Key claim

The quasi-universal relations connecting binary tidal deformability to gravitational-wave characteristics that hold in general relativity are systematically violated in massive Damour-Esposito-Farese-type scalar-tensor gravity, particularly when scalarization occurs before merger; this violation can mimic the effects of a first-order or cross-over quantum chromodynamical phase transition, meaning a future disagreement between detected signals and quasi-universal predictions cannot serve as a smoking-gun for either modified gravity or phase transitions alone.

## Method

The authors performed approximately 120 numerical relativity simulations of equal-mass binary neutron star mergers in both general relativity and massive Damour-Esposito-Farese scalar-tensor theory, using three piecewise-polytropic equations of state (APR4, MPA1, H4) and varying the scalar coupling constant B while fixing the scalar mass to m_phi = 1.33 x 10^{-11} eV. Quasi-equilibrium initial data were prepared at fewer than 5 orbits before merger, and the simulations focused on post-merger evolution to extract the gravitational-wave frequency at merger (f_peak), the dominant post-merger peak frequency (f_2), and the peak gravitational-wave amplitude (h_peak), which were then compared against existing quasi-universal fitting formulas from Kiuchi (2019).

## Key numbers

* Scalar mass fixed to m_phi = 1.33 x 10^{-11} eV (Compton wavelength approximately 15 km)
* For the f_peak versus binary tidal deformability relation, the largest deviation from the Kiuchi (2019) fitting formula in general relativity was less than 5.8 percent, while scalarized mergers with the H4 equation of state showed f_peak roughly constant for tilde-Lambda^{1/5} greater than or approximately equal to 3.4
* For the f_2 versus binary tidal deformability relation, general relativity data with APR4 and MPA1 equations of state deviated by less than 1 percent from the Kiuchi (2019) formula, while scalarized cases with soft equations of state showed a systematic reduction in f_2 when tilde-Lambda^{1/5} less than approximately 3.4
* For the h_peak versus binary tidal deformability relation, general relativity results exceeded the Kiuchi (2019) fitting formula by up to 3.3 percent for low tilde-Lambda values
* For the f_2 versus R_{1.6} relation from Bauswein (2015), the formula overestimated f_2 by greater than or approximately equal to 10 percent for APR4 and MPA1 equations of state even in general relativity

## Context

This work extends the numerical relativity studies of binary neutron star mergers in massive scalar-tensor gravity by Lam (2024) and Kuan (2023), and systematically tests the quasi-universal relations previously established in general relativity by Kiuchi (2019), Bauswein (2015), Bernuzzi (2014), and Takami (2014). The results show that scalarization effects can produce violations in these relations that mimic the signatures of first-order or cross-over quantum chromodynamical phase transitions discussed by Most (2018), Bauswein (2018), and Huang (2022), complicating future efforts to distinguish between modified gravity and nuclear phase transitions using gravitational-wave observations. The authors note that further work is needed to discriminate between these effects, and that thermal effects in post-merger signals remain to be explored.
