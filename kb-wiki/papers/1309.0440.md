# The nuclear energy density functional formalism

> The present document focuses on the theoretical foundations of the nuclear energy density functional (EDF) method. As such, it does not aim at reviewing the status of the field, at covering all possible ramifications of the approach or at presenting recent achievements and applications. The objective is to provide a modern account of the nuclear EDF formalism that is at variance with traditional presentations that rely, at one point or another, on a {\it Hamiltonian-based} picture. The latter is not general enough to encompass what the nuclear EDF method represents as of today. Specifically, the traditional Hamiltonian-based picture does not allow one to grasp the difficulties associated with the fact that currently available parametrizations of the energy kernel $E[g',g]$ at play in the method do not derive from a genuine Hamilton operator, would the latter be effective. The method is formulated from the outset through the most general multi-reference, i.e. beyond mean-field, implementation such that the single-reference, i.e. "mean-field", derives as a particular case. As such, a key point of the presentation provided here is to demonstrate that the multi-reference EDF method can indeed be formulated in a {\it mathematically} meaningful fashion even if $E[g',g]$ does {\it not} derive from a genuine Hamilton operator. In particular, the restoration of symmetries can be entirely formulated without making {\it any} reference to a projected state, i.e. within a genuine EDF framework. However, and as is illustrated in the present document, a mathematically meaningful formulation does not guarantee that the formalism is sound from a {\it physical} standpoint. The price at which the latter can be enforced as well in the future is eventually alluded to.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the nuclear energy density functional (EDF) method can be formulated in a mathematically meaningful way even when the energy kernel \(E[g',g]\) does not derive from a genuine Hamilton operator, but that such a mathematically consistent formulation does not guarantee physical soundness; the price at which physical consistency can be enforced is alluded to but not fully resolved.

## Method

The author provides a modern theoretical account of the nuclear EDF formalism from the outset through the most general multi-reference (beyond mean-field) implementation, with the single-reference (mean-field) case derived as a particular limit. The presentation is limited to the non-relativistic framework, uses the Skyrme family of parametrizations for illustration, and demonstrates that symmetry restoration can be formulated without reference to a projected state, i.e., entirely within a genuine EDF framework. The formalism relies on product states of Bogoliubov type (generalized Slater determinants) that break symmetries such as particle-number (U(1)) and rotational (SO(3)) symmetry, with order parameters tracking the extent of symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers

* The paper discusses nuclei with masses typically between 10 and 350 as intermediates between few- and many-body systems.
* Nearly 3100 observed nuclei are referenced, with thousands more still to be discovered.
* In the sd shell, about 30 combinations of two-body matrix elements need to be refitted to reach about 140 keV root mean square error on nearly 600 pieces of spectroscopic data.
* The volume of the SO(3) group is given as \(16\pi^2\) and the volume of the U(1) group as \(2\pi\).
* The dimension of irreducible representations of SO(3) is \(2J+1\), and for U(1) it is 1.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical foundations of the nuclear EDF method beyond traditional Hamiltonian-based presentations (e.g., those reviewed by Bender (2003) and Niksic (2011)), which are not general enough to encompass modern EDF parametrizations that do not derive from a genuine Hamilton operator. The paper explicitly contrasts its approach with the traditional formulation that relies on a Hamiltonian-based picture and a projected-state framework. It enables future work to address the physical pathologies identified in recent studies (e.g., Dobaczewski (2007), Lacroix (2008), Bender (2008), Duguet (2008)) by providing a mathematically rigorous foundation upon which physically sound formulations can eventually be built.
