# Bulk and neutron-proton asymmetry coefficients of the semi-empirical mass formula tuned to ground state mass excess of AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012)

> Davidson et al. has extended Seeger's mass formula to non-zero excitation energies by introducing temperature-dependent coefficients in the liquid drop energy part of the semi-empirical mass formula, without considering the shell effects. The semi-empirical mass formula of Davidson et al. is applicable for the compound nucleus temperatures less than or equal to 4 MeV. The mass excess calculated using this mass formula including shell effects/corrections does not reproduce the ground state mass excesses of the new atomic mass evaluation data AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012) with its coefficients at zero temperature. So, the coefficients of the semi-empirical mass formula are needed to be tuned to reproduce the ground state mass excess of the nuclei in the recent atomic mass evaluation data AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012). The bulk and neutron-proton asymmetry coefficients of the semi-empirical mass formula of Davidson et al. with shell effects have been tuned to reproduce the mass excess data for all the nuclei of AME2020 (Z=1-118 and A=1-295) and the nuclei of FRDM(2012) (Z=8-136 and A=16-339, except 3456 nuclei which are also available in the AME2020 data) at zero temperature, i.e., the coefficients are tuned for 9420 nuclei known at present. The tuned bulk and neutron-proton asymmetry coefficients reproduce the mass excess of the new atomic mass evaluation data AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012) within a difference of less than 1 MeV and can be used for the applications/investigations in the areas of physics where high energies are experienced or nuclei involved are in excited states, e.g., fusion-evaporation and fusion-fission processes in heavy-ion reactions.

## Key claim

The bulk and neutron-proton asymmetry coefficients of Davidson et al.'s temperature-dependent semi-empirical mass formula, when tuned at zero temperature, can reproduce the ground state mass excess of all 9420 nuclei in AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012) within a difference of less than 1 MeV, enabling the formula's use for excited-state applications such as heavy-ion fusion-evaporation and fusion-fission processes.

## Method

The authors took the temperature-dependent semi-empirical mass formula of Davidson et al., which extends Seeger's mass formula by introducing temperature-dependent coefficients in the liquid drop energy part, and included shell corrections via the Myers and Swiatecki formalism with temperature dependence from Jensen et al. They then adjusted only the bulk coefficient α(0) and the neutron-proton asymmetry coefficient a_a at zero temperature to minimize the deviation between the calculated ground state mass excess and the experimental data from AME2020 (Z=1-118, A=1-295) and theoretical data from FRDM(2012) (Z=8-136, A=16-339, excluding 3456 nuclei already in AME2020), covering a total of 9420 nuclei.

## Key numbers

* The tuned coefficients reproduce the mass excess of AME2020 and/or FRDM(2012) within a difference of less than 1 MeV for all 9420 nuclei.
* The dataset includes 3558 nuclei from AME2020 (Z=1-118, A=1-295) and 5862 nuclei from FRDM(2012) (Z=8-136, A=16-339) not present in AME2020.
* The temperature-dependent formula of Davidson et al. is applicable for compound nucleus temperatures less than or equal to 4 MeV.
* The shell correction temperature damping parameter T0 is 1.5 MeV, and the level density parameter a is taken as A/9 for heavy mass region and A/10 for superheavy mass region.

## Context

This work extends prior coefficient adjustments by Gupta et al. (2003) (up to Z=28), Gupta et al. (2003) (up to Z=56), Birbikram et al. (2008) (up to Z=97), and Birbikram et al. (2008a) (up to Z=118), which were based on older mass evaluations (AME1995, AME2003, FRDM(1992)). The new tuning incorporates 122 new nuclei from AME2020 and 339 new nuclei from FRDM(2012) compared to the previous generation of mass tables, enabling the use of the temperature-dependent mass formula for studying decay and formation of hot compound nuclei in heavy-ion reactions and astrophysics.
