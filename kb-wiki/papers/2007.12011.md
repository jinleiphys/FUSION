# Constraining the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section from the width of nuclear giant dipole resonance

> We develop a new lattice Hamiltonian method for solving the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equation. Adopting the stochastic approach to treat the collision term and using the GPU parallel computing to carry out the calculations allows for a rather high accuracy in evaluating the collision term, especially its Pauli blocking, leading thus to a new level of precision in solving the BUU equation. Applying this lattice BUU method to study the width of giant dipole resonance (GDR) in nuclei, where the accurate treatment of the collision term is crucial, we find that the obtained GDR width of $^{208}{\rm Pb}$ shows a strong dependence on the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section $σ_{\rm NN}^*$. A very large medium reduction of $σ_{\rm NN}^*$ is needed to reproduce the measured value of the GDR width of $^{208}{\rm Pb}$ at the Research Center for Nuclear Physics in Osaka, Japan.

## Key claim
The width of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) in 208Pb, calculated with a new high-precision lattice Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) method, depends strongly on the in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section σ*NN; reproducing the experimental GDR width of 4.0 MeV requires a very large medium reduction of σ*NN, with a reduction factor parameter α ≈ 1.8, which is significantly larger than the value of 0.6 from the microscopic Rostock cross section.

## Method
The authors developed a lattice Hamiltonian method to solve the BUU equation, using a stochastic approach for the collision term and GPU parallel computing to achieve high accuracy in evaluating the collision term and its Pauli blocking. They applied this method to calculate the isovector dipole response of 208Pb by adding a small perturbation to the ground state and extracting the GDR width from the strength function via Fourier transform of the time-dependent response function. The in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section was parameterized as σ*NN = σfreeNN exp[-α (ρ/ρnuc) / (1 + (Tc.m./T0)²)] with ρnuc = 0.16 fm⁻³ and T0 = 0.015 GeV, treating α as a free parameter.

## Key numbers
- The GDR width of 208Pb from the LBUU calculation with the free NN cross section is 6.5 MeV, while the Vlasov calculation (no NN scatterings) gives 1.5 MeV.
- The experimental GDR width of 208Pb from the (p,p') reaction at RCNP is 4.0 MeV.
- Reproducing the experimental GDR width requires α ≈ 1.8 in the exponential reduction factor for σ*NN.
- The GDR width calculated with the FU4FP6 parameterization is 4.32 MeV, consistent with the experimental data.
- The GDR widths calculated with the Fuchs cross section and the Rostock cross section (α = 0.6) are 5.39 MeV and 5.59 MeV, respectively, both overestimating the experimental value.
- In the LBUU calculation with the free NN cross section, an average of 97.93% of attempted collisions in the ground state of 208Pb are blocked by the Pauli principle, resulting in an average of 1.30 successful collisions of physical nucleons per fm/c.
- The root mean square radius and ground-state energy of 208Pb vary by less than 3.6% (0.2 fm) and 3.2% (50 MeV), respectively, during the time evolution of 0-500 fm/c.
- The lattice spacing used is l = 0.5 fm, the momentum-space averaging parameter Δp = 0.05 GeV, and the number of ensembles NE = 30000.

## Context
This work extends previous lattice Hamiltonian Vlasov calculations (Wang et al., 2019) by including a stochastic elastic NN collision term, enabling precise calculation of the collisional damping width of the GDR. The results contradict the early study of balance energy by Westfall et al. (1991) that favored a small medium reduction, but are consistent with more recent analyses of collective flow and nuclear stopping by Lopez et al. (1990) and of nucleon-induced reaction cross sections by Oertzen et al. (2006). The constraint on σ*NN from the GDR width raises challenges for microscopic calculations based on realistic NN interactions, such as the Brueckner theory calculations by Alm et al. (1993) and the Dirac-Brueckner T-matrix calculations by Fuchs et al. (2001), and enables more reliable extraction of the nuclear equation of state from heavy-ion collision data.
