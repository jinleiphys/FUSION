# Spectral control over $γ$-ray echo using a nuclear frequency comb system

> Two kinds of spectral control over $γ$-ray echo using a nuclear frequency comb system are theoretically investigated. A nuclear frequency comb system is composed of multiple nuclear targets under magnetization (hyperfine splitting), mechanical motion (Doppler shift) or both, namely, moving and magnetized targets. In frequency domain the unperturbed single absorption line of $γ$-ray therefore splits into multiple lines with equal spacing and becomes a nuclear frequency comb structure. We introduce spectral shaping and dynamical splitting to the frequency comb structure respectively to optimize the use of a medium and to break the theoretical maximum of echo efficiency, i.e., 54\%. Spectral shaping scheme leads to the reduction of required sample resonant thickness for achieving high echo efficiency of especially a broadband input. Dynamical splitting method significantly advances the echo efficiency up to 67\% revealed by two equivalent nuclear frequency comb systems. We also show that using only few targets is enough to obtain good echo performance, which significantly eases the complexity of implementation. Our results extend quantum optics to 10keV regime and lay the foundation of the development of $γ$-ray memory.

## Key claim
The paper theoretically demonstrates that spectral shaping of a nuclear frequency comb (NFC) can reduce the required sample resonant thickness for high echo efficiency, and that dynamical splitting of the comb can break the previously established theoretical maximum echo efficiency of 54%, achieving up to 67% under specific conditions.

## Method
The authors model a system of multiple nuclear targets (using the 14.4 keV transition in 57Fe) whose absorption lines are shifted via hyperfine splitting, Doppler shift, or both to form a nuclear frequency comb. They solve the Maxwell-Bloch equations in the perturbation regime for two spectral control schemes: (1) spectral shaping, where the resonant thickness of each target is weighted by a Gaussian distribution to match the input pulse spectrum; and (2) dynamical splitting, where targets are accelerated to create time-dependent Doppler shifts that modify the comb structure during the echo process.

## Key numbers
- Theoretical maximum echo efficiency for a stationary flat comb is 54% when average resonant thickness and comb spacing approach infinity.
- Dynamical splitting method achieves echo efficiency up to 67% with fidelity 96% for a 7 ns input pulse using six targets with hybrid shift (hyperfine splitting plus Doppler shift) and average resonant thickness 11.2.
- For a 5 ns input pulse, dynamical splitting yields echo efficiency 65% with fidelity 87%.
- Spectral shaping reduces required total resonant thickness by up to 22% for a 1 ns input pulse (bandwidth 141 Gamma) and up to 53% for a 5 ns input pulse (bandwidth 28 Gamma).
- With spectral shaping, echo efficiency of 51% and fidelity 99% is achieved using only 3 targets for a 5 ns input pulse.
- The spontaneous decay rate Gamma of 57Fe excited states is 1/141.1 GHz.
- Target acceleration velocities are on the order of a few mm/s over 100 ns, with terminal velocity 4.83 mm/s for comb spacing S=50.

## Context
This work extends the atomic frequency comb (AFC) echo memory concept from optical quantum optics (Sangouard 2007, Afzelius 2009) into the 10 keV gamma-ray regime, building on prior proposals for gamma-ray echo memory using nuclear frequency combs (Zhang 2018). The dynamical splitting method is analogous to gradient echo memory (Hetet 2008) but uses temporal variation rather than spatial gradient, and improves upon the stepwise gradient echo memory proposal (Zhang 2018) by requiring fewer targets. The results enable development of gamma-ray photon memory and tunable delay lines for Mossbauer spectroscopy.
