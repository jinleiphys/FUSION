# Emergent nucleosynthesis from a 1.2 second long simulation of a black-hole accretion disk

> We simulate a black-hole accretion disk system with full-transport general relativistic neutrino radiation magnetohydrodynamics (GR$ν$RMHD) for 1.2 seconds. This system is likely to form after the merger of two compact objects and is thought to be a robust site of $r$-process nucleosynthesis. We consider the case of a black-hole accretion disk arising from the merger of two neutron stars. Our simulation time coincides with the nucleosynthesis timescale of the $r$ process ($\sim$ 1 second). Because these simulations are time consuming, it is common practice to run for `short' duration of approximately 0.1 to 0.3 seconds. We analyze the nucleosynthetic outflow from this system and compare the results between stopping at 0.12 and 1.2 seconds respectively. We find that the addition of mass ejected in the longer simulation as well as more favorable thermodynamic conditions from emergent viscous ejecta greatly impacts the nucleosynthetic outcome. We quantify the error in nucleosynthetic outcomes between short and long cuts.

## Key claim

Running a full-transport GRνRMHD simulation of a black-hole accretion disk for 1.2 seconds instead of the typical 0.1–0.3 seconds reveals that emergent viscous ejecta from later times drastically alters the nucleosynthetic outcome, producing a complete r-process through the actinides rather than only a first-peak pattern; however, the total unbound mass has not yet fully asymptoted at 1.2 seconds, so even longer simulations may be needed.

## Method

The authors extended a previous GRνRMHD simulation of a black-hole accretion disk (Miller 2019b) using the νbhlight code, evolving a torus of mass 0.12 M⊙ around a 2.58 M⊙ black hole with spin 0.69 for 1.2 seconds. They tracked approximately 1.5×10⁶ Lagrangian tracer particles, selected unbound tracers using a 250 gravitational radii distance criterion and positive Bernoulli parameter, and post-processed the resulting 461,690 long-duration trajectories with the PRISM nucleosynthesis code using FRDM2012 nuclear masses and CoH₃ reaction rates.

## Key numbers

- Simulation duration: 1.2 seconds (long cut) versus 0.12 seconds (short cut)
- Black hole mass: 2.58 M⊙, dimensionless spin: 0.69
- Initial disk mass: 0.12 M⊙, initial electron fraction: 0.1, initial entropy: 4 k_B/baryon
- Number of unbound tracers: 79,556 (short) and 461,690 (long); ejected mass increases by a factor of 18.5 between cuts
- Total ejected mass: 3.557×10³⁰ g (short) versus 6.567×10³¹ g (long)
- Average electron fraction: 0.247 (short) versus 0.146 (long)
- Average entropy: 19.67 k_B/baryon (short) versus 15.49 k_B/baryon (long)
- Average density at tracer endpoint: 2.05×10⁵ g/cm³ (short) versus 7.75×10⁵ g/cm³ (long)
- Average percent error in final mass fractions between short and long cuts: 450–500%
- For nuclei with Z ≤ 50, error in population is roughly a factor of 6; for Z > 50, error is roughly a factor of 2
- At 1.2 seconds, 65% of accreted mass (6.46×10³¹ g) is unbound, about 27% of total disk mass

## Context

This work extends the earlier GRνRMHD simulation of Miller (2019b) to a duration that matches the r-process nucleosynthesis timescale, addressing the common practice of running such simulations for only 0.1–0.3 seconds. The results contrast with shorter simulations (e.g., Siegel 2017, Christie 2019) that capture only early fast outflows, showing that late-time viscous ejecta dominates the mass budget and produces a complete r-process through the actinides. The findings enable future nuclear sensitivity studies (Li 2022) to cover the wider range of thermodynamic conditions revealed by long-duration simulations, and highlight that even longer simulations are needed to fully capture the unbound mass asymptote.
