# Topics in Lattice QCD and Effective Field Theory

> Effective field theories provide a formalism for categorizing low-energy effects of a high-energy fundamental theory in terms of the low-energy degrees of freedom. This process has been well established in mapping the fundamental theory of QCD in terms of the hadronic degrees of freedom, which allows for quantitative connections and predictions between hardronic observables. A more direct approach to performing the non-perturbative QCD calculations is through lattice QCD. These computationally intensive calculations approximate continuum physics with a discretized lattice to extract hadronic phenomena from first principles. However, as in any approximation, there are multiple systematic errors between lattice QCD calculation and actual hardronic phenomena. To account for these systematic effects in terms of hadronic interactions, effective field theory proves to be useful. However, the fundamental theory of interest here is lattice QCD, as opposed to the usual continuum QCD. In this work, the basics of this process are outlined, and multiple original calculations are presented: effective field theory for anisotropic lattices, I=2 $ππ$ scattering for isotropic, anisotropic, and twisted mass lattices. Additionally, a usage of effective field theories and the employment of an isospin chemical potential on the lattice is proposed to extract several computationally difficult scattering parameters. Lastly, recently proposed local, chiral lattice actions are analyzed in the framework of effective field theory, which illuminates various challenges in simulating such actions.

## Key claim

This work constructs an effective field theory (EFT) for anisotropic Wilson lattice actions, extending the standard chiral perturbation theory framework to include lattice spacing artifacts specific to anisotropic lattices where the temporal and spatial lattice spacings differ by a factor ξ = a_s / a_t. The key hedge is that the formalism is valid only for a specific power counting where lattice spacing effects are on the same order as quark mass contributions (p² ∼ B m_q ∼ W a), and that at a fixed anisotropy, the two distinct O(a) operators become indistinguishable and can be combined into one.

## Method

The author constructs the Symanzik action for both unimproved and O(a)-improved anisotropic Wilson lattice actions, then builds the corresponding chiral Lagrangians for mesons and baryons using a spurion analysis that promotes lattice spacing coefficients to spurion fields transforming under chiral symmetry. The analysis classifies new anisotropic operators into categories: those that do not break continuum symmetries, those that break chiral symmetry, and those that break Lorentz/hypercubic symmetry, with particular focus on operators that break the accidental O(4) symmetry down to O(3) at O(a) rather than O(a²) as in the isotropic case. Extrapolation formulae for hadron masses and modified dispersion relations are provided, and the Aoki-phase diagram for anisotropic actions is discussed.

## Key numbers

- The anisotropy ratio is defined as ξ = a_s / a_t, where a_s is the spatial lattice spacing and a_t is the temporal lattice spacing
- At tree level, the O(a) improvement coefficients are c_t = 1/2 (1/ξ + ν) and c_r = ν, where ν is the speed-of-light parameter
- The pion decay constant convention used is f ∼ 132 MeV
- The power counting assumed is m_q ∼ a Λ², where Λ is the QCD scale
- For unimproved anisotropic Wilson fermions, hypercubic-to-cubic symmetry breaking occurs at O(a), one order lower in the lattice spacing than the O(4) breaking in the isotropic action (which occurs at O(a²))

## Context

This work extends the established program of using chiral perturbation theory to parameterize lattice spacing artifacts, previously carried out to O(a²) for mesons by Sharpe (1998), Rupak (2002), and Bar (2003), and for baryons by Beane (2003) and Tiburzi (2005). The formalism enables systematic removal of anisotropic lattice artifacts from correlation functions generated with the dynamical anisotropic Wilson gauge configurations currently being produced by groups such as Lin (2009) and Beane (2009). The work also highlights that anisotropic actions may enter the Aoki-phase even when the equivalent isotropic action is in the QCD-phase, as first discussed by Aoki (1983).
