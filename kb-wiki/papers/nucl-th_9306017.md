# Thermo Field Dynamics and Kinetic Coefficients of a Charged Boson Gas

> Thermo Field Dynamics for inhomogeneous systems is generalized to quantum fields with a continuous single-particle mass spectrum. The modification of the hamiltonian in states with a local thermal Bogoliubov symmetry is used to calculate thermal conductivity and diffusion coefficient of pions interacting with hot, compressed nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper claims that kinetic coefficients (thermal conductivity and diffusion coefficient) of a charged boson gas can be calculated directly from the response of the quantum system to gradients in temperature and chemical potential, without solving a transport equation, provided the system is close enough to equilibrium that corrections to the spectral function are of second order in the gradients.

## Method

The author generalizes Thermo Field Dynamics (TFD) to inhomogeneous systems by introducing a local Bogoliubov symmetry, which mixes momentum modes through a generalized Bogoliubov transformation parameterized by a space-dependent occupation number density. The gradient part of the Hamiltonian is extracted from the time derivative of the field operators, and the Kubo formula is used to compute the response of particle and energy currents to spatial inhomogeneities. The calculation is performed for a complex scalar field representing charged pions, using a spectral decomposition that accounts for a continuous single-particle mass spectrum.

## Key numbers

* The free on-shell energy is defined as ω_k = sqrt(k^2 + m^2), where m is the physical particle mass.
* The Bose-Einstein distribution functions are n_±(k) = 1/(exp(β(ω_k ∓ μ)) - 1), with β the inverse temperature and μ the chemical potential.
* The spectral function normalization integrals are ∫_0^∞ dE E ρ_l(E,k) = 1/2 and ∫_0^∞ dE ρ_l(E,k) = Z_kl, where Z_kl is a momentum-dependent wave-function renormalization factor.
* The energy Ω_kl is defined as Ω_kl = (1/Z_kl) ∫_0^∞ dE E ρ_l(E,k) = 1/(2Z_kl).

## Context

This work extends the TFD formalism developed by Umezawa (1993) and others to systems with continuous mass spectra, building on the observation by Landsman (1988) that interacting thermal fields require a spectral function broader than a delta function. The approach provides an alternative to solving transport equations directly, as done in standard relativistic transport theory, and enables calculation of transport coefficients for pions in hot, compressed nuclear matter without the pathologies of perturbation theory noted by Tjon, Botermans, and collaborators (1991).
