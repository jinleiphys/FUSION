# First experimental test of the ratio method for nuclear-reaction analysis

> Nuclear halos are very exotic quantal structures observed far from stability. Because of their short lifetime, they are mostly studied through reactions. The ratio method offers a new observable: the ratio of angular differential cross sections for breakup and scattering. It is predicted to be much more sensitive to the projectile structure than individual cross sections thanks to its independence of the reaction process. We test this new observable experimentally for the first time considering the collision of 11Be on C at 22.8 MeV/nucleon. We extend this analysis to similar data recently measured on Pb at 19.1 MeV/nucleon. Both analyses confirm the theoretical predictions, which opens the door to a new era in the study of nuclear structure near the neutron dripline. This should prove invaluable in conjunction with the start of FRIB. The ratio method could also be extended to other fields of quantum physics beyond nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The ratio of angular differential cross sections for breakup and scattering is experimentally shown to be nearly independent of the optical potential used in reaction calculations and to cancel systematic uncertainties, confirming theoretical predictions that it should provide a more robust observable for extracting halo-nucleus structure than individual cross sections.

## Method

The authors performed the first simultaneous measurement of scattering and inclusive breakup angular distributions for 11Be on a natural carbon target at 22.8 MeV/nucleon using the BlueSTEAl detector system at Texas A&M University. They analyzed these data together with existing 11Be-Pb data at 19.1 MeV/nucleon from Duan et al. (2022) using the dynamical eikonal approximation (DEA) with a three-body model of the projectile and three different optical potentials for the core-target interaction.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 22.8 ± 0.3 MeV/nucleon for the 11Be-C measurement; 19.1 MeV/nucleon for the 11Be-Pb data from Duan et al. (2022)
- 11Be beam purity: about 60% with 6% from 8Li; beam intensity typically 1.2 × 10^4 particles per second
- Carbon target thickness: 8.7 (3) mg/cm^2 (natural carbon, 99.8% purity)
- Measured cross section uncertainties: 7–19% (1 sigma), including statistical (1–17%), beam intensity (<0.1%), target thickness (3.5%), and solid angle (5.4%)
- At 25 degrees, the ratios differ by only 15% across different optical potentials, whereas the individual cross sections change by more than an order of magnitude

## Context

This work provides the first experimental test of the ratio method proposed theoretically by Capel, Johnson, and Nunes (2011, 2013, 2020), which was developed within the Recoil Excitation and Breakup model (REB) of Johnson, Al-Khalili, and Tostevin (1997). The results confirm that the ratio removes the strong dependence on optical potentials that has hampered traditional reaction analyses (Crespo, Gomez-Camacho, and Baldo (2004); Singh, Nunes, and Potel (2022); Hebborn, Whitehead, and Liddick (2023)). The method opens the door to studying single-particle structure of unstable nuclei at facilities like FRIB, with a recently approved experiment on 19C planned to test the method in exclusive conditions.
