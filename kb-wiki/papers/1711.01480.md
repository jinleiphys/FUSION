# Photo- and Electro-excitation of Bound Neutrons and Protons

> Data for pion photo-productions off the neutron ($γn\to πN$) have been primarily extracted from deuteron-target data ($γd\to πNN$) by applying kinematical cuts thereby isolating the quasi-free samples. We critically examine if the neutron-target data obtained through this conventional procedure can be contaminated by final state interactions (FSI) and/or the kinematical cuts. The analysis is conducted with a theoretical model for $γd\to πNN$ that takes account of the impulse mechanisms supplemented by the $NN$ and $πN$ rescattering mechanisms. We show that the FSI effects still visibly remain in the extracted $γ$`$n$'$\toπN$ unpolarized cross sections and polarization asymmetries $E$ even after the kinematical cuts are applied. We also find the FSI effects on $γ$`$n$'$\toπ^0n$ can be somewhat different from those on $γ$`$p$'$\toπ^0p$.

## Key claim

Even after applying the conventional kinematical cuts designed to isolate quasi-free samples, final state interaction (FSI) effects still visibly contaminate the extracted neutron-target unpolarized cross sections and polarization asymmetries \(E\) for \(\gamma n \to \pi N\) from deuteron-target data, and the FSI effects on \(\gamma n \to \pi^0 n\) can be somewhat different from those on \(\gamma p \to \pi^0 p\).

## Method

The author constructed a theoretical model for \(\gamma d \to \pi NN\) that includes impulse mechanisms supplemented by \(NN\) rescattering and \(\pi N\) rescattering mechanisms, using elementary \(\gamma N \to \pi N\) and \(\pi N \to \pi N\) amplitudes from a dynamical coupled-channels (DCC) model developed through analyzing \(\sim 27,000\) data points up to \(W \lesssim 2.1\) GeV. The model was then used to generate \(\gamma d \to \pi NN\) cross sections, to which the same kinematical cuts used in recent experimental analyses (e.g., from CLAS at JLab) were applied, followed by a Fermi-unsmearing correction. The extracted quasi-free \(\gamma n \to \pi N\) observables were compared with the corresponding free ones calculated from the same elementary amplitudes to quantify distortions from FSI and the cuts.

## Key numbers

*   For \(\gamma d \to \pi^0 pn\), a large reduction of the cross sections due to \(NN\) rescattering is essential for good agreement with data; the reduction is mainly caused by orthogonality between the deuteron wave function and the \(^3S_1\) partial wave in the final \(NN\) state.
*   For \(\gamma n \to \pi^- p\) extracted from \(\gamma d \to \pi^- pp\) at backward pion kinematics, a \(\sim 9\%\) reduction of \(d\sigma/d\Omega_\pi\) at \(W = 1500\) MeV is found due to \(\pi N\) rescattering.
*   The FSI effects on the extracted quasi-free cross sections are somewhat different between \(\gamma n \to \pi^0 n\) and \(\gamma p \to \pi^0 p\), with generally a few percent difference in the reduction rates.
*   The polarization asymmetry \(E\) for \(\gamma n \to \pi^0 n\) extracted from \(\gamma d \to \pi^0 pn\) shows nonnegligible FSI effects at some \(W\), although the magnitude is smaller than for \(d\sigma/d\Omega_\pi\) due to partial cancellation in the ratio.

## Context

This work extends the line of critical examination of the conventional quasi-free extraction method for neutron-target data from deuteron targets, which has been a persistent concern in the field. The study uses a DCC-based model that has been developed through analyzing \(\sim 27,000\) data points of \(\pi N, \gamma N \to \pi N, \eta N, K\Lambda, K\Sigma\) from thresholds up to \(W \lesssim 2.1\) GeV, and the model predictions for \(\gamma d \to \eta pn\) cross sections are noted to be in excellent agreement with data. The findings contradict the assumption made in some experimental analyses (e.g., MAMI experiments) that FSI effects are the same for neutron and proton targets or that they cancel in the polarization asymmetry \(E\). The paper enables a practical iterative procedure for correctly extracting \(\gamma n \to \pi N\) amplitudes by using a \(\gamma d \to \pi NN\) reaction model to compute FSI correction factors, which can then be applied to experimental data.
