# Atmospheric Effects on Neutron Star Parameter Constraints with NICER

> We present an analysis of the effects of uncertainties in the atmosphere models on the radius, mass, and other neutron star parameter constraints for the NICER observations of rotation-powered millisecond pulsars. To date, NICER has applied the X-ray pulse profile modeling technique to two millisecond-period pulsars: PSR J0030+0451 and the high-mass pulsar PSR J0740+6620. These studies have commonly assumed a deep-heated, fully ionized hydrogen atmosphere model, although they have explored the effects of partial-ionization and helium composition in some cases. Here, we extend that exploration and also include new models with partially ionized carbon composition, externally heated hydrogen, and an empirical atmospheric beaming parameterization to explore deviations in the expected anisotropy of the emitted radiation. None of the studied atmosphere cases have any significant influence on the inferred radius of PSR J0740+6620, possibly due to its X-ray faintness, tighter external constraints, and/or viewing geometry. In the case of PSR J0030+0451, both the composition and ionization state could significantly alter the inferred radius. However, based on the evidence (prior predictive probability of the data), partially ionized hydrogen and carbon atmospheres are disfavored. The difference in the evidence for ionized hydrogen and helium atmospheres is too small to be decisive for most cases, but the inferred radius for helium models trends to larger sizes around or above 14-15 km. External heating or deviations in the beaming that are less than $5\,\%$ at emission angles smaller than 60 degrees, on the other hand, have no significant effect on the inferred radius.

## Key claim

None of the studied atmosphere model variations (composition, ionization state, external heating, or beaming parameterization) significantly affect the inferred radius of PSR J0740+6620, but for PSR J0030+0451 the composition and ionization state can alter the inferred radius, although partially ionized hydrogen and carbon atmospheres are disfavored by the evidence.

## Method

The authors performed pulse profile modeling of NICER observations for two rotation-powered millisecond pulsars (PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620) using multiple atmosphere models: deep-heated fully ionized hydrogen and helium, partially ionized hydrogen and carbon, externally heated hydrogen, and an empirical beaming parameterization. They compared posterior distributions of neutron star parameters, particularly radius, across these different atmosphere assumptions and evaluated model evidence using prior predictive probability.

## Key numbers

- PSR J0030+0451: For the ST-U hot region model (circular hot regions), helium atmosphere shifts the inferred radius from around 10.5 km to 14 km compared to hydrogen; for the ST+PST model (circle and crescent), the shift is from 13 km to 15 km
- PSR J0030+0451: The partially ionized hydrogen case with ST-U model and joint NICER+XMM data predicts a significantly smaller radius around 11 km
- PSR J0030+0451: Total counts in the data set are 1.5 x 10^6 versus 0.6 x 10^6 for PSR J0740+6620
- PSR J0030+0451: Evidence for carbon atmospheres is worse by more than ~200 in ln-units compared to hydrogen; for PSR J0740+6620 the difference is more than ~10 ln-units
- PSR J0030+0451: Evidence for helium is around 20 ln-units larger than for hydrogen in the joint NICER+XMM analysis
- PSR J0030+0451: Inferred effective temperature is around log10(Teff/K) ≈ 6.1 for both hot regions
- Beaming deviations less than 5% at emission angles smaller than 60 degrees have no significant effect on inferred radius

## Context

This work extends the NICER pulse profile modeling studies of PSR J0030+0451 (Miller et al. 2019, Riley et al. 2019, Vinciguerra et al. 2023) and PSR J0740+6620 (Miller et al. 2021, Riley et al. 2021, Salmi et al. 2022), which previously assumed deep-heated fully ionized hydrogen atmospheres with limited tests of helium and partial ionization. The study quantifies systematic uncertainties from atmosphere model choices and enables more robust neutron star radius constraints, while also identifying that the helium atmosphere results for PSR J0030+0451 differ from those in Miller et al. (2019) due to different prior assumptions, particularly the 16 km upper limit on radius.
