# Motivations for Early High-Profile FRIB Experiments

> This white paper is the result of a collaboration by those that attended a workshop at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), organized by the FRIB Theory Alliance (FRIB-TA), on Theoretical Justifications and Motivations for Early High-Profile FRIB Experiments. It covers a wide range of topics related to the science that will be explored at FRIB. After a brief introduction, the sections address: (II) Overview of theoretical methods, (III) Experimental capabilities, (IV) Structure, (V) Near-threshold Physics, (VI) Reaction mechanisms, (VII) Nuclear equations of state, (VIII) Nuclear astrophysics, (IX) Fundamental symmetries, and (X) Experimental design and uncertainty quantification.

## Key claim
This white paper argues that early high-profile experiments at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) can achieve transformative nuclear-physics results across structure, reactions, astrophysics, and fundamental symmetries, but the actual feasibility depends on the staged availability of detector systems and beam intensities, with many flagship capabilities (such as the High Rigidity Spectrometer and GRETA full array) not coming online until after 2026.

## Method
The paper surveys the theoretical justifications and experimental capabilities for early FRIB science across eight topical sections: theoretical methods, experimental capabilities, nuclear structure, near-threshold physics, reaction mechanisms, nuclear equations of state, nuclear astrophysics, fundamental symmetries, and experimental design with uncertainty quantification. It describes specific detector systems (BECOLA, LEBIT, S800, GRETA, GRETINA, SeGA, CAESAR, MoNA-LISA, FDSi, SOLARIS, SECAR, JANUS, HiRA, LANA) and their current performance metrics, then projects how these will be applied to key measurements in the first years of FRIB operation.

## Key numbers
- Mass measurements at FRIB will reach precision of delta m/m approximately 10^-6 to 10^-8 for over 1300 isotopes with mass uncertainties greater than 50 keV/c^2
- TOF-Brho technique can measure rare isotopes with half-lives down to 100s of nanoseconds and mass precision down to 100 keV/c^2
- BECOLA has achieved beam rates as low as approximately 10/s for 36Ca ions and 54Ni atoms
- RISE instrument aims for approximately 1 ion/s sensitivity for laser spectroscopy
- Tritium beam intensities at FRIB for charge-exchange reactions are more than 30 times higher than at NSCL
- CAESAR offers efficiency of approximately 40% at 1 MeV with 192 CsI(Na) crystals
- GRETA will provide high-resolution (approximately 0.2% FWHM), high efficiency (greater than or equal to 30% at 1.3 MeV), and peak-to-total greater than or equal to 50% at 1.3 MeV, covering approximately 80% solid angle with 120 HPGe detectors
- MoNA-LISA single neutron detection efficiency is approximately 70%
- SECAR is designed for primary beam rejection of 10^-13, with additional 10^-4 rejection at focal plane, enabling measurement of resonance strengths down to 1 micro-eV
- ReA linear accelerator accelerates beams from 0.3 to 10 MeV/u, with planned upgrade to approximately 20 MeV/u
- SOLARIS Silicon array mode provides missing mass resolution approaching 100 keV and works at beam intensities of 10^4 pps and above; AT-TPC mode extends reach to 100 pps

## Context
This white paper extends the planning efforts of the FRIB Theory Alliance (FRIB-TA) workshop and builds on decades of experimental work at NSCL, RIKEN RIBF, and ISOL-type facilities. It directly references prior techniques such as the TOF-Brho method (Matos 2012, Meisel 2013), Penning trap mass measurements at LEBIT (Ringle 2013), collinear laser spectroscopy at BECOLA (Minamisono 2013, Rossi 2014), charge-exchange reactions using the S800 spectrometer (Bazin 2003), and the SπRIT experiment at RIKEN (2021, 2020). The paper enables a coordinated early science program by mapping which detector systems and beam capabilities will be available in each phase of FRIB operation, and identifies open questions about how to extract Gamow-Teller strengths without beta-decay calibrations, how to handle multi-neutron cross-talk in invariant-mass measurements, and how to design new detectors for the HRS and FRIB400 upgrades.
