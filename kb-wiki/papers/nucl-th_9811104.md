# A first order transition and parity violation in a color superconductor

## Key claim

In cold, dense quark matter with three colors and either two or three flavors, assuming condensation occurs in the total spin J=0 channel, the transition to a color superconducting phase where strange quarks condense with either up or down quarks is driven first order by the Coleman-Weinberg mechanism. At densities sufficiently high that the axial U(1) symmetry is effectively restored, the condensate of up with down quarks spontaneously violates parity relative to the ordinary vacuum, though the authors cannot specify how strongly first order the transition is nor how dense matter must be for parity violation to occur.

## Method

The authors analyze the effective field theory of quark-quark condensate fields in four dimensions, ignoring gluons on the grounds that Debye screening generates a gluon mass m_D ~ g μ, causing gluons to decouple over large distances. They classify the possible color and flavor representations of J=0 condensates under SU(3)_c × SU(N)_f for two, three, and two-plus-one flavors, then compute the renormalization group behavior of the scalar field theories to determine whether the phase transition is first or second order via the Coleman-Weinberg mechanism.

## Key numbers

- The Debye mass for gluons at nonzero density is m_D ~ g μ, where g is the SU(3)_c coupling constant.
- For three massless flavors at large chemical potential μ, the density of instantons falls off like ~ 1/μ^9.
- The pseudo Goldstone boson from spontaneous breaking of axial U(1) symmetry, arising primarily from the up-down condensate, has a mass of order ~ 5 MeV, not ~ 100 MeV as for pions.
- The gap in QCD depends on the coupling as φ ~ μ exp(-c_QCD/√g^2), where c_QCD is a constant.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering analysis of color superconductivity by Bailin and Love (1984) and the recent suggestions by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998) regarding color-flavor locking for three flavors. The authors build on model calculations by Alford, Rajagopal, and Wilczek (1998), Rapp, Schäfer, Shuryak, and Velkovsky (1998), and others, but provide general arguments independent of specific models. The results enable future work on the nature of the chiral phase transition at nonzero density, the possibility of large parity violation in heavy ion collisions and quark stars, and the properties of pseudo Goldstone bosons in color superconducting phases.
