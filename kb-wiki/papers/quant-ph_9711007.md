# Symmetries in nuclear, atomic and molecular spectroscopy

> This paper addresses the three following questions. (i) How the structures of group and of chain of groups enter nuclear, atomic and molecular spectroscopy? (ii) How these structures can be exploited, in a quantum- mechanical framework, in the problems of state labelling and (external) symmetry breaking? (iii) How it is possible to associate a Wigner-Racah algebra to a group or a chain of groups for making easier the calculation of quantum-mechanical matrix elements? Numerous examples illustrate the philosophy of qualitative and quantitative applications to spectroscopy.

## Key claim

The paper argues that group theory and chains of groups can be systematically applied to nuclear, atomic, and molecular spectroscopy for classifying wavefunctions and interactions and for calculating matrix elements, but it emphasizes that classification groups need not correspond to actual symmetries of the Hamiltonian and that accidental degeneracies with respect to geometrical symmetry groups can be resolved by larger degeneracy groups.

## Method

The paper presents a pedagogical exposition of how finite or compact groups and chains of groups are introduced in spectroscopy, either as symmetry groups (invariance groups of the Hamiltonian) or as classification groups (used for labelling state vectors even when they are not symmetries). It reviews Wigner's theorem, the Wigner-Eckart theorem, and Racah's lemma as the formal links between group theory and quantum mechanics, and illustrates these concepts with worked examples from atomic physics (hydrogen atom, lanthanide/actinide ions), nuclear physics (Wigner-Hund SU(4) model, Elliott SU(3) rotation model), and molecular physics (octahedral symmetry for Ti(H2O)6^3+).

## Key numbers

* The degeneracy degree for the hydrogen atom discrete level E_n is n^2 = sum_{ell=0}^{n-1} (2 ell + 1), with n in N* and E_1 = -1/2 Z^2.
* For the nf^3 configuration (ell=3, N=3), the number of wavefunctions is C^{N}_{2(2 ell+1)} = 14!/(3! 11!) = 364.
* The isotropic harmonic oscillator in 3 dimensions has energy levels E_n = (n + 3/2) omega with degeneracy (n+1)(n+2)/2.
* The first nuclear magic numbers A = 2, 8, and 20 correspond to the groupings 1s, 1s+1p, and 1s+1p+2s1d shells from the SU(3) decomposition of the 3D oscillator.

## Context

This lecture extends the group-theoretic approach to spectroscopy pioneered by Wigner (1927) and Racah (1942), particularly Racah's use of the chain U(7) superset SO(7) superset G_2 superset SO(3) for classifying lanthanide and actinide ion configurations. It also connects to the Elliott SU(3) rotation model in nuclear physics and the Wigner-Hund SU(4) model for nucleon-nucleon forces. The paper enables future work by providing a unified framework for state labelling and matrix element calculation across nuclear, atomic, and molecular systems, while noting that open questions remain regarding missing labels in chains such as SU(4) superset SU(2)_T tensor SU(2)_S.
