# Light clusters as a possible source of crustal impurities: a quasi-particle approach

> The presence of impurities in the neutron star crust is known to affect in an important way the thermal and electrical conductivity of the star. In this work, we explore the possibility that such impurities might arise from the simultaneous presence of heavy ions together with Hydrogen and Helium isotopes formed during the cooling process of the star. We consider an equilibrium population of such light particles at temperatures close to the crystallization of the crust within an effective quasi-particle approach including in-medium binding energy shifts, and using different versions of the relativistic mean field approach for the crustal modeling. Thermal effects are consistently included also in the dominant ion species present in each crustal layer described in the compressible liquid drop approximation. We find that the impurity factor associated to light clusters is comparatively very small and can be neglected in transport calculations, even if a strong model dependence is observed.

# Light clusters as a possible source of crustal impurities: a quasi-particle approach

## Key claim

The impurity factor associated with light clusters (Hydrogen and Helium isotopes) in the neutron star crust is comparatively very small and can be neglected in transport calculations, even though a strong model dependence is observed across different relativistic mean-field functionals. This result represents a lower limit to crustal impurities because fluctuations of the dominant ion species and possible exotic neutron-rich clusters are neglected.

## Method

The authors model each layer of the inner neutron star crust as a collection of identical neutral Wigner-Seitz cells containing a single heavy spherical nucleus (treated with the compressible liquid drop model including surface, Coulomb, and translational terms) plus a uniform distribution of dripped neutrons and point-like light clusters (deuterons, tritons, helium-3, alpha particles, and helium-6). Light clusters are included as explicit degrees of freedom within an effective quasi-particle approach using relativistic mean-field theory, with in-medium binding energy shifts and modified scalar meson couplings calibrated on ab-initio calculations and experimental constraints. The equilibrium composition is determined by minimizing the total free energy with respect to the nuclear droplet size, baryonic density, proton density, and volume fraction, using seven different nuclear interactions (FSU, FSU2R, NL3ωρ, NLset21, DDME2, DD2, and TW).

## Key numbers

- Impurity parameter values from light clusters are found to be negligible compared to values between Q_imp ≈ 5 and Q_imp ≈ 100 obtained in prior work (Fantina 2019, Dinh-Thi 2023) for the inner crust from simultaneous presence of light (Z ≈ 2–3) and heavy (Z ≈ 20–30) ions
- The heavy ion atomic numbers considered are of order Z ≈ 20–40 for the ground-state composition
- The surface tension parameters for each model are fitted to the Atomic Mass Evaluation 2016; for example, FSU has σ₀ = 1.17734 MeV/fm², b_s = 21.18512, p = 3.0
- Saturation densities ρ₀ range from 0.148 fm⁻³ (FSU, NL3ωρ) to 0.156 fm⁻³ (NLset21)
- Symmetry energy slope L_sym,0 ranges from 42 MeV (NLset21) to 60 MeV (FSU)
- The critical temperature for the surface tension temperature dependence is given by T_c = 87.76 (K₀/375)^(1/2) (0.155/ρ₀)^(1/3) y_p^I (1 − y_p^I) MeV

## Context

This work extends the multi-component approach of Fantina (2019) and Dinh-Thi (2023), who computed the impurity factor at finite temperature using a single energy functional and the compressible liquid drop model for all nuclei, which gives a poor description of the lightest nuclei. The present study improves upon that by using a more accurate microscopic description of light cluster free energies via the relativistic mean-field quasi-particle approach previously developed by Pais (2019) and calibrated on ab-initio calculations by Röpke (2014, 2020) and experimental constraints. The results suggest that light clusters alone cannot explain the high impurity parameters (Q_imp ≈ 5–100) needed to match observational cooling data from isolated X-ray pulsars and binary systems (Pons 2013, Newton 2013, Deibel 2016, Hambaryan 2017, Tan 2018), implying that other sources such as non-adiabatic cooling dynamics, pasta phases, or multi-modal charge distributions from heavy species must be responsible.
