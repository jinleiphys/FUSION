# Californium-254 and kilonova light curves

> Neutron star mergers offer unique conditions for the creation of the heavy elements and additionally provide a testbed for our understanding of this synthesis known as the $r$-process. We have performed dynamical nucleosynthesis calculations and identified a single isotope, $^{254}$Cf, which has a particularly high impact on the brightness of electromagnetic transients associated with mergers on the order of 15 to 250 days. This is due to the anomalously long half-life of this isotope and the efficiency of fission thermalization compared to other nuclear channels. We estimate the fission fragment yield of this nucleus and outline the astrophysical conditions under which $^{254}$Cf has the greatest impact to the light curve. Future observations in the middle-IR which are bright during this regime could indicate the production of actinide nucleosynthesis.

## Key claim

The paper identifies a single isotope, Californium-254 (²⁵⁴Cf), whose spontaneous fission decay can dominate the heating of kilonova ejecta at timescales of 15 to 250 days, but only under specific astrophysical conditions: outflows with low electron fraction (Yₑ ≈ 0.2) that produce a complete r-process pattern including actinides, and where the red component of the ejecta is slow and/or massive enough for the thermalization efficiency to be significant.

## Method

The authors performed dynamical r-process nucleosynthesis calculations using the PRISM reaction network (version 2.0) with wind conditions of entropy per baryon s/k = 40, outflow timescale τ = 20, and electron fraction Yₑ = 0.2. They included all relevant nuclear reaction channels including charged particle reactions, neutron capture, photodissociation, β-decay, delayed neutron emission, and fission from neutron-induced, β-delayed, and spontaneous channels, using nuclear properties based on the FRDM2012 theoretical model with evaluated data from NUBASE2016 where available. They constructed fission fragment yields for ²⁵⁴Cf(sf) using a hybrid method combining experimental data for ²⁵²Cf(sf) with calculations for neutron-induced fission of ²⁵¹,²⁵³Cf, and applied thermalization efficiencies from Barnes et al. (2016) to compute effective heating rates for kilonova light curve models.

## Key numbers

- ²⁵⁴Cf has an experimentally measured half-life of 60.5 ± 0.2 days with spontaneous fission as the dominant decay mode and an α-decay branching of 0.31 ± 0.016%
- The inclusion of spontaneous fission from ²⁵⁴Cf produces approximately one order of magnitude higher heating at around 20 days, and almost a factor of 100 higher heating at 100-300 days
- At 25 days after explosion, inclusion of ²⁵⁴Cf increases the near-infrared brightness by almost 2 magnitudes in the JHK-bands for an ejecta mass of 0.05 M⊙ and median velocity of 0.1 c
- In the mid-infrared at 50-250 days, the effect translates to approximately 4 magnitudes brighter transients
- The thermalization timescale for fission fragments is τ_fis = 150.0 × (m_ej/0.05 M⊙)^(1/2) × (v_ej/0.1 c)^(-3/2) × (125 MeV/⟨E_fis⟩)^(1/2) days
- The nucleus ²⁵⁸Fm has a half-life of 370 ± 14 μs with approximately 100% spontaneous fission branching

## Context

This work extends earlier suggestions by Wanajo et al. (2014) that ²⁵⁴Cf could dominate late-time heating, but provides the first detailed nucleosynthesis and light curve calculations incorporating experimentally known spontaneous fission decays. The paper builds on the historical speculation by Baade et al. (1956) and Fields et al. (1956) that ²⁵⁴Cf might power supernovae, now applied to neutron star merger kilonovae. The results enable future observations with JWST in the mid-infrared to potentially detect actinide nucleosynthesis in mergers, distinguishing between scenarios with and without a slow, lanthanide-rich red component as modeled by Kasen et al. (2017).
