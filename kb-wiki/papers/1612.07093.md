# Compact bifluid hybrid stars: Hadronic Matter mixed with self-interacting fermionic Asymmetric Dark Matter

> The masses and radii of non-rotating and rotating configurations of pure hadronic stars mixed with self-interacting fermionic Asymmetric Dark Matter are calculated within the two-fluid formalism of stellar structure equations in general relativity. The Equation of State (EoS) of nuclear matter is obtained from the density dependent M3Y effective nucleon-nucleon interaction. We consider dark matter particle mass of 1 GeV. The EoS of self-interacting dark matter is taken from two-body repulsive interactions of the scale of strong interactions. We explore the conditions of equal and different rotational frequencies of nuclear matter and dark matter and find that the maximum mass of differentially rotating stars with self-interacting dark matter to be $\sim 1.94 M_\odot$ with radius $\sim 10.4$ kms.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a hybrid star composed of hadronic matter mixed with self-interacting fermionic Asymmetric Dark Matter (ADM) can reach a maximum mass of approximately 1.94 solar masses with a radius of about 10.4 km under differential rotation, but this result is conditional on the dark matter being the dominant component (with central enthalpy fixed for nuclear matter and dark matter central enthalpy varied) and on the two fluids having different rotational frequencies (700 Hz for dark matter, 300 Hz for nuclear matter).

## Method

The authors use the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations within general relativity to model non-rotating, rigidly rotating, and differentially rotating configurations of hybrid stars. The nuclear matter equation of state (EoS) is derived from the density-dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective nucleon-nucleon interaction, while the self-interacting fermionic ADM EoS is obtained from a massive vector field theory with a mediator mass of 100 MeV and a dark matter particle mass of 1 GeV. The stellar structure calculations are performed using the LORENE code, with both EoSs fitted to polytropic forms.

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass of a pure dark matter star (non-rotating, self-interacting ADM, particle mass 1 GeV): 3.0279 solar masses with radius 16.2349 km
- Maximum mass of a pure dark matter star (rotating, self-interacting ADM, particle mass 1 GeV): 3.1460 solar masses with equatorial radius 19.2173 km
- Maximum mass of a hybrid star with fixed dark matter central enthalpy (0.24c^2) and varying nuclear matter central enthalpy, under differential rotation (dark matter 300 Hz, nuclear matter 700 Hz): 1.3640 solar masses with radius 6.7523 km and central baryonic number density 2.1060 fm^{-3}
- Maximum mass of a hybrid star with fixed nuclear matter central enthalpy (0.24c^2) and varying dark matter central enthalpy, under differential rotation (dark matter 700 Hz, nuclear matter 300 Hz): 1.9355 solar masses with radius 10.3717 km and central dark baryonic number density 1.1605 fm^{-3}
- For the 1.9355 solar mass differentially rotating hybrid star: matter mass is 2.1105 solar masses, consisting of 0.1179 solar masses of nuclear matter and 1.9926 solar masses of dark matter
- Polytropic index for interacting nuclear matter: gamma = 2.03; polytropic constant K = 5.65283 x 10^35 in C.G.S. units
- Polytropic index for interacting dark matter: gamma = 1.97562; polytropic constant K = 1.33404 x 10^36 in C.G.S. units
- Symmetric nuclear matter incompressibility K_infinity: 274.7 +/- 7.4 MeV
- Nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density E_sym(rho_0): 30.71 +/- 0.26 MeV
- Slope L of the symmetry energy: 45.11 +/- 0.02 MeV
- Isospin dependent part K_tau of the isobaric incompressibility: -408.97 +/- 3.01 MeV

## Context

This work extends the two-fluid formalism previously applied to non-self-annihilating dark matter admixed neutron stars by Leung et al. (2011) and to asymmetric fermionic dark matter by Goldman et al. (2013), by incorporating strong self-interactions between dark matter particles via a massive vector field. The study enables future investigations into the effects of dark matter on hybrid stars containing a quark core, which the authors note cannot be predicted without full calculations and leave for future work.
