# On the Wong cross section and fusion oscillations

> We re-examine the well-known Wong formula for heavy-ion fusion cross sections. Although this celebrated formula yields almost exact results for single-channel calculations for relatively heavy systems such as $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Sm, it tends to overestimate the cross section for light systems such as $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C. We generalise the formula to take account of the energy dependence of the barrier parameters and show that the energy-dependent version gives results practically indistinguishable from a full quantal calculation. We then examine the deviations arising from the discrete nature of the intervening angular momenta, whose effect can lead to an oscillatory contribution to the excitation function. We recall some compact, analytic expressions for these oscillations, and highlight the important physical parameters that give rise to them. Oscillations in symmetric systems are discussed, as are systems where the target and projectile identities can be exchanged via a strong transfer channel.

## Key claim

The standard Wong formula, which treats barrier parameters as energy-independent, overestimates fusion cross sections for light systems like \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C; an energy-dependent version that uses barrier parameters evaluated at the grazing angular momentum yields results practically indistinguishable from full quantal calculations, and the oscillatory structure in symmetric systems arises from the discrete addition of partial waves, with an analytic expression that depends on the barrier curvature and grazing angular momentum.

## Method

The authors derived an energy-dependent Wong formula by replacing the fixed \(l=0\) barrier parameters \([B,R_B,\hbar\omega]\) with energy-dependent values \([V_E,R_E,\omega_E]\) evaluated at the grazing angular momentum \(l_g\) for each incident energy. They applied the Poisson summation formula to the Hill-Wheeler transmission expression to obtain an analytic oscillatory term, and compared the resulting cross sections with full quantal optical-model calculations using an exponential nuclear potential with a surface diffuseness parameter. They also analyzed experimental data for \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C and \(^{28}\)Si+\(^{28}\)Si systems, including the effects of Bose symmetry and elastic transfer.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C with an exponential potential (\(a=0.8\) fm), the \(l=0\) barrier parameters are \(B=6.22\) MeV, \(R_B=7.42\) fm, and \(\hbar\omega=2.52\) MeV.
- The energy-dependent Wong formula reproduces full quantal calculations to within oscillations of about 1 mb for the non-symmetrised \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C system.
- For the symmetrised \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C system (even \(l\) only), the oscillatory amplitude is given by \(E\sigma_{\rm osc}=2\pi R_E^2\hbar\omega_E\,{\rm exp}(-\xi)\,{\rm sin}(\pi l_g)\), where \(\xi\) is defined in Eq. (13).
- For \(^{28}\)Si+\(^{28}\)Si with \([B,a]=[28.8~{\rm MeV},0.8~{\rm fm}]\), the \(\xi\) parameter is larger by a factor of about 2 than for \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C, damping oscillations dramatically.
- A parametrised fit to \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C data using energy-independent parameters \([B,R_B,\hbar\omega]=[5.6,6.3,3.0]\) corresponds to an unphysically large diffuseness \(a=2.0\) fm.
- For \(^{12}\)C+\(^{12}\)C above about 25 MeV, the data require that the transmission of \(l=14\) be reduced by a factor of 2 and all higher partial waves be removed.

## Context

This work extends the original Wong formula (Wong, 1973) and the earlier analysis of fusion oscillations by Poffé, Rowley and Lindsay (1983), who first proposed the interpretation of oscillations as due to successive addition of partial waves and gave a compact analytic expression. The paper also builds on recent discussions by Esbensen (2012) and Wong (2013) on the same topic. The energy-dependent generalization enables more accurate modeling of light systems and clarifies that oscillations in heavier systems like \(^{28}\)Si+\(^{28}\)Si are too small to observe, though recent data by Stefanini et al. (2014) show unexpected structures that may arise from channel couplings rather than symmetrization effects.
