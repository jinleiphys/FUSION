# Sensitivity of Neutron-Rich Nuclear Isomer Behavior to Uncertainties in Direct Transitions

> Nuclear isomers are populated in the rapid neutron capture process (r process) of nucleosynthesis. The r process may cover a wide range of temperatures, potentially starting from several tens of GK (several MeV) and then cooling as material is ejected from the event. As the r-process environment cools, isomers can freeze out of thermal equilibrium or be directly populated as astrophysical isomers (astromers). Two key behaviors of astromers -- ground state<->isomer transition rates and thermalization temperatures -- are determined by direct transition rates between pairs of nuclear states. We perform a sensitivity study to constrain the effects of unknown transitions on astromer behavior. We also introduce a categorization of astromers that describes their potential effects in hot environments. We provide a table of neutron-rich isomers that includes the astromer type, thermalization temperature, and key unmeasured transition rates.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the behavior of neutron-rich nuclear isomers in r-process environments -- specifically their thermalization temperatures and ground-state-to-isomer transition rates -- is highly sensitive to uncertainties in direct transition rates between pairs of nuclear states, meaning that without measuring these specific transitions, predictions of isomer effects in nucleosynthesis remain uncertain.

## Method

The authors performed a sensitivity study by varying direct transition rates between pairs of nuclear states for a set of neutron-rich isomers and computing how these variations affect two key astromer properties: the thermalization temperature (the temperature at which the isomer falls out of thermal equilibrium with the ground state) and the effective ground-state-to-isomer transition rate. They introduced a categorization scheme for astromers based on their potential effects in hot astrophysical environments and compiled a table of neutron-rich isomers that includes the astromer type, thermalization temperature, and the key unmeasured transition rates that most affect the isomer's behavior.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear isomers in the r-process, particularly the concept of "astromers" (astrophysical isomers) that can be directly populated in hot environments as the r-process cools. The sensitivity study provides guidance for future experimental measurements by identifying which specific direct transitions most urgently need to be measured to reduce uncertainties in r-process nucleosynthesis models. The categorization of astromers enables systematic assessment of which isomers can significantly affect nucleosynthesis outcomes, informing both experimental nuclear physics programs and astrophysical simulations of neutron star mergers and other r-process sites.
