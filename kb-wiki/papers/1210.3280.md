# New Properties of High Momentum Distribution of Nucleons in Asymmetric Nuclei

> Based on the recent experimental observations of the dominance of tensor interaction in the ~250-600~MeV/c momentum range of nucleons in nuclei, the existence of two new properties for high-momentum distribution of nucleons in asymmetric nuclei is suggested. The first property is the approximate scaling relation between proton and neutron high-momentum distributions weighted by their relative fractions in the nucleus. The second property is the inverse proportionality of the strength of the high-momentum distribution of protons and neutrons to the same relative fractions. Based on these two properties the high-momentum distribution function for asymmetric nuclei has been modeled and demonstrated that it describes reasonably well the high-momentum characteristics of light nuclei. However, the most surprising result is obtained for neutron rich nuclei with large A, for which a substantial relative abundance of high-momentum protons as compared to neutrons is predicted. For example, the model predicts that in Au the relative fraction of protons with momenta above $k_{F} \sim 260$~MeV/c is 50% more than that of neutrons. Such a situation may have many implications for different observations in nuclear physics related to the properties of a proton in neutron rich nuclei.

## Key claim

Based on the dominance of tensor interactions in the ~250–600 MeV/c momentum range, two new properties are predicted for high-momentum nucleon distributions in asymmetric nuclei: (1) an approximate scaling relation between proton and neutron high-momentum distributions weighted by their relative fractions, and (2) an inverse proportionality of the strength of the high-momentum distribution to the nucleon's relative fraction. The most surprising result is that for neutron-rich heavy nuclei, the model predicts a substantial relative abundance of high-momentum protons compared to neutrons, for example, in Au the fraction of protons with momenta above ~260 MeV/c is 50% more than that of neutrons.

## Method

The author models the high-momentum nucleon distribution in asymmetric nuclei by assuming that only proton-neutron (pn) short-range correlations (SRCs) contribute in the ~k_F–600 MeV/c range, neglecting pp and nn SRCs due to tensor force dominance. Using the factorization of the A-nucleon wave function into a correlated NN pair and an A-2 mean-field component, the momentum distribution is related to the deuteron momentum distribution via a parameter a_2(A,y) that depends on nuclear asymmetry y = |x_n - x_p|. The model is tested against realistic calculations for light nuclei (A ≤ 12) using Faddeev equations, Correlated Gaussian Basis approach, and Variational Monte Carlo methods with the Argonne V18 potential.

## Key numbers

- For Au (A=197), the relative fraction of protons with momenta above k_F ~ 260 MeV/c is 50% more than that of neutrons.
- For ^3He (y=0.33), the kinetic energy of protons is 14.71 MeV and of neutrons is 19.35 MeV (difference -4.64 MeV); for ^3H (y=0.33), proton kinetic energy is 19.61 MeV and neutron is 14.96 MeV (difference +4.65 MeV).
- For ^8He (y=0.50), proton kinetic energy is 30.13 MeV and neutron kinetic energy is 18.60 MeV (difference 11.53 MeV).
- For ^10Be (y=0.20), proton kinetic energy is 30.20 MeV and neutron kinetic energy is 25.95 MeV (difference 4.25 MeV).
- For ^11B (y=0.09), proton kinetic energy is 33.40 MeV and neutron kinetic energy is 31.75 MeV (difference 1.65 MeV).

## Context

This work extends the experimental observation of strong (factor of 20) dominance of pn SRCs over pp and nn SRCs in the ~250–600 MeV/c range (see references isosrc, EIPsc), understood through tensor force dominance. It builds on the standard SRC factorization model of Frankfurt and Strikman (1981) and the relation between nuclear momentum distributions and deuteron momentum distributions. The predictions enable new experimental tests via semi-inclusive A(e,e'N)X reactions at Jefferson Lab (currently underway for ^56Fe and ^208Pb) and have implications for the EMC effect in neutron-rich nuclei, the NuTeV anomaly, and neutron star properties such as cooling through direct URCA processes. The author notes that center-of-mass motion of pn SRCs and contributions from pp and nn SRCs will diminish the predicted imbalance for heavy nuclei, leading to a modified scaling relation with an exponent γ ≤ 1 that decreases with A.
