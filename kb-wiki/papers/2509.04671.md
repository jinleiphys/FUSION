# New phases in QCD at finite temperature and chemical potential

> Understanding the phases of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at finite temperature and baryon density is crucial for describing matter in heavy-ion collisions and the interior of neutron stars. While the transition from the confined phase to the deconfined phase has been extensively studied at vanishing chemical potential, recent theoretical work suggests the existence of intermediate phases with partial deconfinement, where only a subset of the color degrees of freedom is deconfined. In this paper, we study the generalization of partial deconfinement in QCD in the Veneziano large-$N_{\rm c}$ limit ($N_{\rm f}/N_{\rm c}$ fixed) to finite baryon chemical potential. We find that the partially deconfined phase has finer structure, and hence that there are four phases in QCD at finite temperature and moderately large chemical potential: complete confinement, complete deconfinement, and two kinds of partial deconfinement. A key ingredient is the refined understanding of the meaning of the 'Gross-Witten-Wadia point', i.e., the opening of a gap in the distribution of Polyakov line phases: either string condensation or baryon condensation causes the GWW transition. As a by-product, we observe the emergence of the QCD critical point from the interplay between baryon condensation and partial deconfinement. While our approach is necessarily qualitative due to the fermion sign problem, it provides a unified theoretical framework for understanding the rich phase structure of dense QCD matter and offers new perspectives on the location and nature of the QCD critical point.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that at finite baryon chemical potential, the partially deconfined phase of QCD splits into two distinct phases (PD-1 and PD-2) separated by a Gross-Witten-Wadia transition, leading to a total of four phases in the Veneziano large-\(N_{\rm c}\) limit; however, the authors emphasize that their analysis is necessarily qualitative due to the fermion sign problem and cannot provide precise quantitative predictions.

## Method

The authors study QCD in the Veneziano large-\(N_{\rm c}\) limit (where \(N_{\rm f}/N_{\rm c}\) is fixed) with degenerate quark mass, using the Polyakov loop eigenvalue distribution as the key diagnostic. They analyze the Gross-Witten-Wadia (GWW) transition, the opening of a gap in the distribution of Polyakov line phases, and argue that either string condensation or baryon condensation can cause this transition. Based on general symmetry principles and the gauge-invariant Hilbert space construction, they classify phases by the number of "active" color degrees of freedom \(M_{\rm active}\) and the size of the deconfined sector \(M_{\rm deconf}\), without solving the strongly-coupled dynamics directly.

## Key numbers

* Transition temperatures at zero chemical potential are estimated as \(T_{{\rm CC}\to{\rm PD}} \sim 175\) MeV and \(T_{{\rm PD}\to{\rm CD}} \sim 350\) MeV, based on lattice QCD analysis of \(N_{\rm f}=2+1\) QCD configurations from the WHOT-QCD collaboration.
* Lattice data show that at \(T = 174\) MeV (lattice \(16 \times 32^3\)) the system is either complete confinement or partial deconfinement, while at \(T = 348\) MeV (lattice \(8 \times 32^3\)) it is either partial deconfinement or complete deconfinement.
* At \(T = 697\) MeV (lattice \(4 \times 32^3\)) the system is in the complete deconfinement phase, and at \(T = 279\) MeV (lattice \(10 \times 32^3\)) it is in the partial deconfinement phase.

## Context

This work extends the partial deconfinement framework previously developed for zero chemical potential (Hanada 2016, Hanada 2018, Hanada 2019) to finite baryon density, and connects to the "Spaghetti of Quarks with Glueballs" (SQGB) phase proposed by Fujimoto, Fukushima, Hidaka, and McLerran (2025). The paper also relates the CC-2 phase to the quarkyonic phase of McLerran and Pisarski (2007), and provides a framework for understanding the QCD critical point as emerging from the interplay between baryon condensation and partial deconfinement. The approach is limited to qualitative analysis because the fermion sign problem prevents direct numerical or analytic solution at finite chemical potential.
