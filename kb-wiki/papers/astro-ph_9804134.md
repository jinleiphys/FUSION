# Resonant production of gamma rays in jolted cold neutron stars

> Acoustic shock waves passing through colliding cold neutron stars can cause repetitive superconducting phase transitions in which the proton condensate relaxes to its equilibrium value via coherent oscillations. As a result, a resonant non-thermal production of gamma rays in the MeV energy range with power up to 10^(52) erg/s can take place during the short period of time before the nuclear matter is heated by the shock waves.

# Resonant production of gamma rays in jolted cold neutron stars

## Key claim

Acoustic shock waves from colliding cold neutron stars can cause repetitive superconducting phase transitions whose coherent oscillations of the proton condensate produce gamma rays via parametric resonance, but this mechanism operates only during the short period *before* the nuclear matter is heated by the shock waves, and the duration of the pulse is uncertain depending on heat transport details.

## Method

The author applies time-dependent Ginzburg-Landau theory to describe the relaxation of the proton condensate order parameter after density variations from acoustic shock waves drive it through the superconducting phase transition. The equation of motion for the order parameter is coupled to the electromagnetic field, and the resulting Mathieu equation for photon modes shows exponentially growing solutions (parametric resonance) that produce copious non-thermal gamma rays in the MeV range. The power available from the condensate relaxation is estimated from the energy gap dependence on density and the acoustic wave period.

## Key numbers

- Power output up to $10^{52 \pm 1}$ erg/s from the resonant gamma-ray production
- Acoustic wave time scale $\tau_a$ ranges from $10^{-2}$ $\mu$s to $0.1$ s for magnetic field $\sim 10^{12}$ G
- Proton energy gap $\Delta_0 \lesssim 0.8$ MeV at densities $\rho \sim 10^{14}$ to $10^{15}$ g cm$^{-3}$
- Fermi momentum $p_F \sim 10^2$ MeV for the same density range
- Energy density from condensation $\delta E/V \sim 10^3$ (MeV)$^4$
- Neutron star radius $R \approx 10$ km
- Superconducting transition temperature $T_c \sim 0.5$ MeV
- Order parameter equilibrium value $\phi_0 \approx 20$ MeV
- Oscillation frequency $\omega \approx 3$–$5$ MeV for $\Delta_0 = 0.4$–$0.8$ MeV
- Effective proton mass $m_* \approx 0.6 m_p$
- Proton fraction $Y_p \approx 0.03$ of all baryons
- Resonance parameter $q \approx 2$–$20$ for oscillation amplitude $\Phi$ between 1 and 10 MeV
- Conservative lower bound on duration of resonant production: $1$ ms

## Context

This work extends the study of parametric resonance from quantum field theory and cosmology (as applied to oscillating scalar fields) to the astrophysical setting of neutron star mergers. It addresses the long-standing problem of reconciling the optical thickness of a fireball with the short duration and high energy of gamma-ray bursts (GRB), which were recently shown to originate at redshift $z \sim 1$ from events releasing $\sim 10^{52}$ erg in photons. The mechanism relies on the density dependence of the proton energy gap (based on calculations of the gap as a function of density) and on the oscillation spectra of neutron stars. The paper suggests that observed GRB, or at least the initial milliseconds of some bursts, may be explained by this resonant emission before the fireball erupts.
