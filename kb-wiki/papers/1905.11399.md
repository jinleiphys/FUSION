# Nuclear-matter distribution in the proton-rich nuclei $^7$Be and $^8$B from intermediate energy proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics

> Absolute differential cross sections for elastic $p^7$Be and $p^8$B small-angle scattering were measured in inverse kinematics at an energy of 0.7 GeV/u at GSI Darmstadt. The hydrogen-filled ionization chamber IKAR was used as an active target to detect the recoil protons. The projectile tracking and isotope identification were performed with multi-wire proportional chambers and scintillation detectors. The measured cross sections were analysed using the Glauber multiple-scattering theory. The root-mean-square (rms) nuclear matter radii $R_{\rm m} = 2.42 (4)$ fm for $^7$Be and $R_{\rm m} = 2.58 (6)$ fm for $^8$B were obtained. The radial density distribution deduced for $^8$B exhibits a proton halo structure with the rms halo radius $R_{\rm h} = 4.24 (25)$ fm. A comparison of the deduced experimental radii is displayed with existing experimental and theoretical data.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first simultaneous determination of nuclear matter radii for both \(^7\)Be and \(^8\)B from a single experiment using intermediate-energy proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics, and provides evidence for a proton halo in \(^8\)B based on the measured curvature of the differential cross section and the extracted halo radius, with the caveat that the halo interpretation depends on the specific density parameterization used (GG or GO) and the assumption that \(^8\)B consists of a \(^7\)Be core plus a valence proton.

## Method

Absolute differential cross sections for elastic \(p^7\)Be and \(p^8\)B scattering were measured at 0.7 GeV/u in inverse kinematics at GSI Darmstadt using the hydrogen-filled ionization chamber IKAR as an active target to detect recoil protons, with projectile tracking via multi-wire proportional chambers and isotope identification via scintillation detectors. The measured cross sections were analyzed within the Glauber multiple-scattering theory using four phenomenological density parameterizations (SF, GH, GG, GO) to extract root-mean-square (rms) nuclear matter radii and, for \(^8\)B, the core and halo radii assuming a core-plus-valence-proton structure.

## Key numbers

*   Rms nuclear matter radius for \(^7\)Be: \(R_{\rm m} = 2.42 (4)\) fm (averaged over four density parameterizations, including statistical and systematic uncertainties).
*   Rms nuclear matter radius for \(^8\)B: \(R_{\rm m} = 2.58 (6)\) fm (averaged over four density parameterizations, including statistical and systematic uncertainties).
*   Rms halo radius for \(^8\)B: \(R_{\rm h} = 4.24 (25)\) fm (derived from the GG and GO parameterizations using the relation between \(R_{\rm m}\), core radius \(R_{\rm c}\), and halo radius).
*   Rms core radius for \(^8\)B: \(R_{\rm c} = 2.25 (3)\) fm (mean from GG and GO parameterizations).
*   Rms neutron radius for \(^7\)Be: \(R_{\rm n} = (2.27 \pm 0.10)\) fm (derived by combining the measured \(R_{\rm m}\) with the proton radius \(R_{\rm p} = 2.507(17)\) fm from Nortershauser (2009)).
*   Proton skin thickness for \(^7\)Be: \(\delta_{\rm pn} = 0.23 (10)\) fm (difference between \(R_{\rm p}\) and \(R_{\rm n}\)).
*   Beam energies: 701 MeV/u for \(^7\)Be and 702 MeV/u for \(^8\)B, with an energy spread of 1.3%.
*   Angular resolution: \(\sigma_{\Theta} = 0.74\) mrad for \(^7\)Be and \(\sigma_{\Theta} = 1.00\) mrad for \(^8\)B.

## Context

This work extends the method of intermediate-energy proton elastic scattering in inverse kinematics, previously applied by the PNPI-GSI Collaboration to stable and neutron-rich nuclei such as \(^6\)He, \(^8\)He, \(^{11}\)Li, and \(^{14}\)Be (Alkhazov 2002, Neumaier 2002, Dobrovolsky 2006, Ilieva 2012), to the proton-rich isotopes \(^7\)Be and \(^8\)B. The results resolve the wide scatter of previous \(R_{\rm m}\) values for \(^8\)B obtained from interaction and reaction cross sections (ranging from 2.38(2) fm to 2.61(8) fm in works by Negoita (1996), Fukuda (1999), Tanihata (1988), Warner (1995), and others) and provide a direct measurement of the halo radius. The deduced matter radius for \(^7\)Be agrees with Warner (2001) but exceeds the value from Tanihata (1988). The findings enable more reliable calculations of the \(^7\)Be(p,\(\gamma\))\(^8\)B reaction rate, which is critical for modeling solar neutrino production.
