# Breakdown of the impulse approximation and its consequences: the low-Q^2 problem

> Neutrino scattering data and the standard calculations of the cross section show a discrepancy in the low-Q^2 (four-momentum transfer squared) region. The calculations rely on the assumption, called the impulse approximation, that the nucleus the neutrino scatters off can be described as a collection of independent nucleons and therefore only one nucleon takes part in the interaction. It is known from electron scattering that such picture is valid only when transferred momentum |q|>400 MeV/c. For lower |q|'s, the nucleus is probed with a lower spatial resolution and a few nucleons are involved in the scattering, so the use of the impulse approximation is unjustified. It means that the standard calculations of the cross sections are unreliable for |q|<400 MeV/c. I show that the contribution of low-momentum interactions to the quasielastic neutrino cross section cannot be reduced below ~19% by any selection of the beam energy in the few-GeV region. Moreover, low-Q^2 neutrino events happen only when transferred momentum is low too. Hence the discrepancy in the low-Q^2 region is caused by the inappropriate description of low-|q| interactions in the models commonly used in Monte Carlo simulations.

## Key claim
The standard impulse approximation (IA) used in neutrino-nucleus cross-section calculations is unreliable for momentum transfers below |q| < 400 MeV/c, and the contribution of such low-momentum interactions to the quasielastic neutrino cross section cannot be reduced below approximately 19% by any choice of beam energy in the few-GeV region.

## Method
The author calculated quasielastic muon-neutrino scattering on oxygen-16 using a spectral function from Benhar, Fabrocini, Fantoni, and Sick (2000) to describe the nuclear ground state, neglecting final-state interactions. The differential cross section dσ/d|q| and dσ/dQ² were computed for neutrino energies from 0.2 to 5.0 GeV, and the fractional contribution from momentum transfers below 300 MeV/c and below 400 MeV/c was tabulated to quantify the region where the impulse approximation breaks down.

## Key numbers
- For neutrino energy 0.2 GeV, 97.2% of the cross section comes from |q| ≤ 300 MeV/c; at 0.4 GeV, 18.9% comes from |q| ≤ 300 MeV/c and 43.3% from |q| ≤ 400 MeV/c.
- The minimum contribution of |q| ≤ 300 MeV/c to the total cross section is 9.0% at neutrino energy 1.4 GeV; the minimum contribution of |q| ≤ 400 MeV/c is 18.8% at 1.4 and 2.0 GeV.
- For neutrino energies from 1.0 to 5.0 GeV, the contribution from |q| ≤ 400 MeV/c remains between 18.8% and 19.4%, never falling below approximately 19%.
- The impulse approximation is known from electron scattering to be valid only for |q| ≥ 400 MeV/c and fails below 300 MeV/c.

## Context
This work extends the known breakdown of the impulse approximation from electron scattering (Ankowski and Sobczyk, 2008) to neutrino scattering, identifying it as the cause of the low-Q² discrepancy observed in K2K and MiniBooNE data. The author shows that standard Monte Carlo models relying on the impulse approximation cannot describe low-|q| interactions, and suggests that a correct description using the random phase approximation should reduce the cross section in the low-Q² region. This enables improved neutrino-nucleus cross-section models for oscillation experiments.
