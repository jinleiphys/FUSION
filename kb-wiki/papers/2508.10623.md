# Momentum expansions in finite-density perturbative calculations

> Complex-valued Feynman integrals in the imaginary time formalism and zero-temperature limit suffer from particular types of infrared divergences that can not be regulated by integration dimension alone. Related problems leading to integration order dependent results are even further pronounced in the presence of additional scales such as external momenta. This plays a noticeable role in systems featuring fermionic degrees of freedom such as cold Quantum Chromodynamics, where loop integrals are complexified by chemical potential(s). Working in the limit of vanishing temperature, we utilize novel complex-valued extensions to bubble Feynman integrals and study momentum expansions of fermionic loop integrals. The expansions are then used to illustrate the mechanisms of manifested discrepancies between orders of integration, associated with the residue theorem. Finally, we address the issues by introducing a representation avoiding the observed ambiguity and briefly overview classes of integrals insensitive to problems from external momenta.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in the zero-temperature limit of the imaginary-time formalism, the order of integration (temporal vs. spatial) for fermionic one-loop bubble integrals with external momenta can yield different results, even for first-order fermionic poles, when the external momentum has a non-zero temporal component; this discrepancy is distinct from the previously known issue associated with fermionic poles having only chemical potential as a scale.

## Method

The authors study the fermionic one-loop bubble integral with two propagators, \(\mathcal{J}_{11}(\mu, \vec{s}, s_0)\), in the limit of vanishing temperature and with a hierarchy \(|s_0|, |\vec{s}| \ll \mu\). They compare two integration orders: first performing the temporal integral via the residue theorem (strict \(T=0\) limit) and then the spatial integral, versus first performing the Matsubara sum (using Euler-Maclaurin formalism) and then the spatial integral. The analysis is carried out using momentum expansions in the external momenta, and the results are compared to identify discrepancies. The authors also generalize the known master formulas for bubble integrals to complex-valued chemical potentials to facilitate the expansions.

## Key numbers

* The discrepancy between integration orders is shown to occur even at leading order in the momentum expansion, where the residue-based result for the integral with external momenta does not equal the known result for the zero-momentum bubble integral \(\mathcal{I}_2(\mu)\) (the limit of \(\mathcal{J}_{11}\) as \(|S| \to 0^+\)).
* The master formula for the residue-based bubble integral with a complex chemical potential \(\mu \in \mathbb{C}\) is given as \(\text{Res}[\mathcal{I}_\alpha](\mu) = -\left(\frac{e^\gamma \Lambda^2}{4\pi}\right)^{\frac{3-d}{2}} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha-\frac{1}{2})}{\sqrt{\pi} (4\pi)^{d/2} \Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(d/2)} \frac{|\text{Re}\mu|^{d+1-2\alpha}}{d+1-2\alpha}\).
* The master formula for the Euler-Maclaurin-based bubble integral with a complex chemical potential is \(\mathcal{I}_\alpha(\mu) = -\left(\frac{e^\gamma \Lambda^2}{4\pi}\right)^{\frac{3-d}{2}} \frac{1}{(4\pi)^{d/2} \Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(d/2+1-\alpha)} \frac{|\text{Re}\mu|^{d+1-2\alpha}}{d+1-2\alpha}\), which equals \(\mathcal{I}_\alpha(|\text{Re}\mu|)\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of integration-order discrepancies in finite-density perturbative calculations, previously identified for fermionic bubble integrals with only a chemical potential scale by Gorda (2022) and Österman (2023). The paper shows that a new type of discrepancy arises when external momenta are present, which is relevant for the ongoing computation of the \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^3)\) coefficient of the QCD pressure at high density (e.g., Gorda 2018, 2023; Kärkkäinen 2025). The results enable a systematic understanding of how to correctly evaluate the non-factorizing four-loop Feynman integrals that constitute the remaining unresolved contributions to this coefficient.
