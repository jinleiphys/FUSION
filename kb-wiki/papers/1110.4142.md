# Connecting Neutron Star Observations to Three-Body Forces in Neutron Matter and to the Nuclear Symmetry Energy

> Using a phenomenological form of the equation of state of neutron matter near the saturation density which has been previously demonstrated to be a good characterization of quantum Monte Carlo simulations, we show that currently available neutron star mass and radius measurements provide a significant constraint on the equation of state of neutron matter. At higher densities we model the equation of state using polytropes and a quark matter model, and we show that our results do not change strongly upon variation of the lower boundary density where these polytropes begin. Neutron star observations offer an important constraint on a coefficient which is directly connected to the strength of the three-body force in neutron matter, and thus some theoretical models of the three-body may be ruled out by currently available astrophysical data. In addition, we obtain an estimate of the symmetry energy of nuclear matter and its slope that can be directly compared to the experiment and other theoretical calculations.

# Connecting Neutron Star Observations to Three-Body Forces in Neutron Matter and to the Nuclear Symmetry Energy

## Key claim

Neutron star mass and radius measurements can constrain the strength and density dependence of the three-body force in neutron matter, but only under the condition that the equation of state near saturation density is described by a specific phenomenological form that faithfully reproduces quantum Monte Carlo simulations. The constraints on the three-body force parameters are nearly independent of the high-density equation of state model used, whether polytropes or quark matter.

## Method

The authors constructed a phenomenological equation of state for neutron matter near saturation density using a power-law parametrization with four free parameters, which had been previously shown to accurately fit quantum Monte Carlo calculations. At higher densities (above 0.40 fm^-3), they modeled the equation of state using either a sequence of two piecewise-continuous polytropes or a quark matter model from Alford et al. (2005). They performed Bayesian analysis with uniform priors on equation of state parameters, using neutron star mass and radius measurements including a recent measurement from the transiently accreting neutron star U24 and the 1-sigma lower limit of 1.93 solar masses from Demorest et al. (2010).

## Key numbers

- The posterior probability distributions constrain the three-body force parameter b to the range 3.3 < b < 4.8 MeV and the exponent beta to 2.28 < beta < 2.5, independent of the high-density equation of state model.
- The nuclear symmetry energy E_sym is constrained to 32 < E_sym < 34 MeV at 68% confidence.
- The density derivative of the symmetry energy L is constrained to 43 < L < 52 MeV at 68% confidence.
- Neutron star radii lie between about 11 and 12.3 km regardless of mass to within 68% confidence.
- The matching density between the neutron matter parametrization and polytropes was varied between 0.32 fm^-3 and 0.48 fm^-3 with small effect on results.
- The correction to the pressure ratio of neutron matter to neutron star matter is 0.86 plus or minus 0.03 at saturation density.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Steiner et al. (2010) by connecting neutron star observations directly to the parameters of three-body forces in neutron matter, building on quantum Monte Carlo calculations by Gandolfi et al. (2009, 2011). The results rule out more extreme models for the three-neutron force from the range considered in Gandolfi et al. (2011), and are consistent with constraints from heavy-ion collisions (Tsang et al., 2009), nuclear masses (Liu et al., 2010), pygmy dipole resonances (Carbone et al., 2010), isobaric analog states (Danielewicz et al., 2009), and antiprotonic atoms (Warda et al., 2009). The findings enable future work to test specific three-body force models against astrophysical data, though corrections from four-body forces, relativistic effects, and hyperons below 0.32 fm^-3 would require revisiting these constraints.
