# Complex-Scaling Calculation of Three-Body Resonances Using Complex-Range Gaussian Basis Functions --- Application to 3$Î±$ resonances in 12C ---

> We propose to use the complex-range Gaussian basis functions, {r^l e^{-(1 \pm iĎ‰)(r/r_n)^2}Y_{lm}(\hat{r}); r_n in a geometric progression}, in the calculation of three-body resonances with the complex-scaling method (CSM) in which use is often made of the real-range Gaussian basis functions, {r^l e^{-(r/r_n)^2}Y_{lm}(\hat{r})}, that are suitable for describing the short-distance structure and the asymptotic decaying behavior of few-body systems. The former basis set is more powerful than the latter when describing the resonant and nonresonant continuum states with highly oscillating amplitude at large scaling angles Î¸. We applied the new basis functions to the CSM calculation of the 3Î±resonances with J=0^+, 2^+ and 4^+ in 12C. The eigenvalue distribution of the complex scaled Hamiltonian becomes more precise and the maximum scaling angle becomes drastically larger (Î¸_{max}=16 deg. \arrow 36 deg.) than those given by the use of the real-range Gaussians. Owing to these advantages, we were able to confirm the prediction by Kurokawa and Kato [Phys. Rev. C 71, 021301 (2005)] on the appearance of the new broad 0^+_3 state; we show it as an explicit resonance pole isolated from the 3$Î±$ continuum.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that complex-range Gaussian basis functions enable direct isolation of the broad $0^+_3$ resonance pole from the $3\alpha$ continuum in $^{12}$C at a scaling angle of $36^\circ$, whereas previous work by Kurokawa and Kato (2005) using real-range Gaussians could only reach $16^\circ$ and required extrapolation via the analytic continuation of the coupling constant (ACCC) method to predict this state.

## Method

The authors implement the complex-scaling method (CSM) for the $3\alpha$ system in $^{12}$C using the orthogonality condition model (OCM), expanding the three-body wave function in complex-range Gaussian basis functions of the form $r^l e^{-(1 \pm i\omega)(r/r_n)^2}Y_{lm}(\hat{r})$ with ranges $r_n$ in a geometric progression. They solve the complex-scaled SchrĂ¶dinger equation $[H(\theta)-E(\theta)]\Psi(\theta)=0$ for total angular momenta $J=0^+, 2^+, 4^+$, using the same $\alpha$-$\alpha$ potential and $3\alpha$ potential as Kurokawa and Kato (2005), with the Pauli principle enforced via the OCM projection operator.

## Key numbers

- Maximum scaling angle increased from $\theta_{\text{max}}=16^\circ$ (real-range Gaussians) to $\theta_{\text{max}}=36^\circ$ (complex-range Gaussians)
- The $0^+_3$ resonance appears at $E_{res}=0.79 - i\,0.84$ MeV (resonance energy $E_r=0.79$ MeV, half-width $\Gamma/2=0.84$ MeV) relative to the $3\alpha$ breakup threshold
- Total basis size for the $0^+_3$ calculation: $\gamma_{\text{max}}=4448$ basis functions with $\omega=\pi/2$
- For the $0^+$ states at $\theta=16^\circ$ and $26^\circ$: $\gamma_{\text{max}}=3200$ basis functions with $r_1=0.3$ fm, $r_{n_{\text{max}}}=30.0$ fm for $l=0$; $R_1=0.5$ fm, $R_{N_{\text{max}}}=40.0$ fm for $L=0$
- The $3\alpha$ potential strengths: $V_{3\alpha}^{0^+}=31.7$ MeV, $V_{3\alpha}^{2^+}=63.0$ MeV, $V_{3\alpha}^{4^+}=150.0$ MeV with $\mu=0.15$ fm$^{-2}$
- In the harmonic oscillator test: complex-range Gaussians reproduced the 36-quanta state energy to 36.0004 (exact: 36), while real-range Gaussians gave 45.2

## Context

This work extends the CSM calculations of $3\alpha$ resonances in $^{12}$C previously performed by Kurokawa and Kato (2005), who used real-range Gaussian basis functions and could only reach $\theta=16^\circ$, requiring ACCC extrapolation to predict the broad $0^+_3$ state. The new complex-range basis functions allow direct observation of this resonance pole at $\theta=36^\circ$, confirming the Kurokawa and Kato (2005) prediction and addressing the question raised by Arai (2008) who reported the $0^+_3$ state missing from his microscopic $R$-matrix calculation. The method enables future CSM studies of three-body resonances with large decay widths that were previously inaccessible with real-range Gaussian bases.
