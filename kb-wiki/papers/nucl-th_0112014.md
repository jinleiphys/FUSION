# Randomness and collectivity in nuclear structure: Three theoretical puzzles

> We show and interpret three examples of nontrivial results obtained in numerical simulations of many-body systems: exponential convergence of low-lying energy eigenvalues in the process of progressive truncation of huge shell-model matrices, apparently ordered spectra generated by random interactions, and regular behavior of complex many-body energies in a system with single-particle orbitals in continuum. The possible practical applications and new approaches are suggested.

# Randomness and collectivity in nuclear structure: Three theoretical puzzles

## Key claim
The paper presents three numerical puzzles, exponential convergence of low-lying eigenvalues under shell-model truncation, dominance of spin-zero ground states from random interactions, and regular ordering of complex energies in continuum-coupled systems, but emphasizes that full theoretical understanding of these effects remains incomplete, with only plausible explanations offered rather than rigorous proofs.

## Method
The authors performed numerical shell-model diagonalizations for nuclei such as ⁴⁹Cr using the FPD6 interaction, progressively truncating the basis ordered by energy centroids and fitting the ground-state energy as an exponential function of dimension. For the random-interaction puzzle, they generated ensembles of rotationally invariant two-body interactions with randomly chosen matrix elements in a single-j shell (j=15/2, N=6 particles) and computed the fraction of ground states with each total spin J. For the continuum puzzle, they placed 3 fermions on 8 equidistant single-particle orbitals, assigned a complex energy (decay width γ) to the uppermost orbital, and tracked the trajectories of the many-body complex eigenvalues as γ increased.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energy of ⁴⁹Cr converges exponentially with truncated dimension; the full JT-dimension is 232514.
- Mean square deviation of calculated ground-state energies from experimental data for nuclei from ⁴²Ca to ⁵⁶Ni is 0.27 MeV, after Coulomb and monopole corrections.
- In the single-j model (j=15/2, N=6 particles), the fraction f_J of ground states with spin J=0 exceeds 50% for random two-body interactions, despite the low statistical multiplicity of J=0 states.
- In the continuum model (3 particles on 8 orbitals), 21 of 56 many-body states move nearly parallel into the complex plane as γ increases, while 35 states acquire very small widths.
- The exponential convergence regime sets in at an energy distance of (3–4)σ̄ from the original centroid.

## Context
This work extends the authors' earlier discussions of quantum chaos in many-body systems (Zelevinsky, 1998) and builds on statistical spectroscopy methods (Wong, 1986) and shell-model studies of quantum chaos (Big, 1994; Frazier, 1995). The exponential convergence method was developed for fp-shell calculations (Mihunhub, 2001) and is related to exponential localization in disordered solids (Rammer, 1998). The random-interaction puzzle was triggered by Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (1998) and sparked extensive theoretical discussion (Johnson, Bertsch, Dean, and Talmi, 2000). The continuum coupling analysis connects to earlier work on superradiance (Krot, 1996; Sokolov, 1997; Sokolov and Annenkov, 1999) and the Dicke effect in optics (Dicke, 1954). The results enable practical truncation schemes for shell-model calculations where full diagonalization is infeasible, and suggest new approaches for treating weakly bound nuclei and reactions far from stability.
