# Using Steady State Behavior to Assess Treatments of Nuclear Isomers in Astrophysical Environments

> Differing reaction rates of long-lived nuclear states can force the level occupations out of thermal equilibrium, causing calculations of overall rates which rely on thermal equilibrium to be inaccurate. Therefore, nucleosynthesis calculations which include nuclei with isomers must use techniques that do not assume thermal equilibrium, and it is imperative that such techniques appropriately account for transitions between the ground and isomeric states via higher-lying levels. We develop a formalism to compute the steady state occupations of nuclear levels and apply it to the examples 26Al, 34Cl, and 85Kr. We show that this approach is useful both for assessing the required number of nuclear levels and for determining the temperature above which thermal equilibrium rates are appropriate.

## Key claim

The paper develops a steady-state formalism for computing nuclear level occupations that accounts for the slow internal transitions of isomers, and shows that this approach can determine both the minimum number of nuclear levels needed for accurate nucleosynthesis calculations and the temperature above which the simpler thermal equilibrium assumption becomes valid.

## Method

The authors constructed coupled differential equations for nuclear state abundances including internal electromagnetic transitions (spontaneous and thermally stimulated), external destruction rates (e.g., beta-decay), and production channels, then solved for the steady-state occupation fractions n_i and total destruction rate lambda^D under both zero-production and constant-production conditions. They applied this formalism to three nuclei with long-lived isomers (26Al, 34Cl, and 85Kr) using experimental gamma-transition and beta-decay rates where available, supplemented by Weisskopf approximations for unmeasured transitions and assumed log(ft)=5.0 for unknown beta-decays.

## Key numbers

- 26Al ground state beta-decay rate: lambda_0^beta = 3.1 x 10^{-14} s^{-1}; isomer at 228 keV has lambda_{228}^beta = 0.11 s^{-1}
- 26Al isomer dominates total beta-decay rate above T = 15 keV in the no-production steady state
- 26Al steady-state calculations converge with thermal equilibrium at T approximately 40 keV
- 85Kr ground state beta-decay rate: lambda_0^beta = 2.0 x 10^{-9} s^{-1}; isomer at 305 keV has lambda_{305}^beta = 3.4 x 10^{-5} s^{-1}
- 85Kr isomer has a 21% branch to the ground state via internal transition at T=0
- 85Kr equilibration temperature is approximately 25 keV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of 26Al beta-decay in thermal baths by Coc (2000), Runkle (2001), Gupta (2001), Iliadis (2011), and Reifarth, which considered differing numbers of nuclear levels and generally agreed that higher-lying levels facilitate communication between ground and isomeric states. The formalism enables nucleosynthesis network calculations to determine the minimum number of levels required for accurate effective rates and identifies when the thermal equilibrium assumption of Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1982) and Oda et al. (1994) is appropriate. The method is broadly applicable beyond beta-decay to other nuclear reaction channels where isomers affect nucleosynthesis, such as the rp-process studied by Sun et al. (2005).
