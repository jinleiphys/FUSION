# Systematic study of the validity of the eikonal model including uncertainties

> Nuclear reactions at intermediate beam energies are often interpreted using the eikonal model. In the analysis of complex reaction probes, where few-body reaction methods are needed, the eikonal method may be used as an efficient way for describing the fragment-target reaction process. In this work, we perform a systematic study to test the validity of the eikonal approximation for nucleon-nucleus reactions. We also quantify uncertainties due to the nucleon optical potential on reaction observables. We inspect the validity of the eikonal model and its semiclassical correction by comparing it to exact solutions (obtained from solving the optical model equation with a finite differences method) for a wide range of reactions. We also study the effect of relativistic corrections, both kinematic and dynamic, by effectively incorporating the relativistic effects at intermediate energies. The uncertainties from a Bayesian global optical potential (KDUQ) are propagated to the observables of interest. Our study includes neutron and proton reactions on $^{27}$Al, $^{40}$Ca, $^{90}$Zr and $^{208}$Pb, for a wide range of energies $E_{lab}=0-400$ MeV. Our results show that for the proton absorption cross section, the eikonal model can be used down to around $60$ MeV and the semiclassical correction extends its use to $30$ MeV. However, the validity of the eikonal model for the neutron total cross section only goes down to $\approx120$ MeV, a range extended to $\approx 50$ MeV when using the semiclassical correction. We find the semi-classical correction to the eikonal model to be less effective in describing the angular distributions. The $1σ$ uncertainty intervals on the observables we studied is less than $5$% for most of the energies considered, but increases rapidly for higher energies, namely energies outside the range of KDUQ ($E_{lab}>200$ MeV).

## Key claim

The eikonal model's validity for nucleon-nucleus reactions depends strongly on the observable and target, with the model usable down to approximately 60 MeV for proton absorption on heavy targets but only down to approximately 120 MeV for neutron total cross sections; the semiclassical correction extends these ranges to 30 MeV and 50 MeV respectively, but offers little improvement for angular distributions.

## Method

The authors compared eikonal model predictions and eikonal predictions with a semiclassical correction (shifting impact parameter to the classical distance of closest approach) against exact solutions of the Schrödinger equation obtained via partial-wave expansion using the finite-differences code FRESCO. They used the Bayesian global optical potential KDUQ, which provides 416 posterior samples, to propagate uncertainties to reaction observables for neutron and proton reactions on 27Al, 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb at laboratory energies from 0 to 400 MeV, including relativistic kinematic and dynamic corrections.

## Key numbers

- For proton absorption on 208Pb, the eikonal model is valid down to 60 MeV; the semiclassical correction extends validity to 25 MeV.
- For neutron total cross section on 208Pb, the eikonal model is valid down to 110 MeV; the semiclassical correction extends validity to 50 MeV.
- For neutron total cross section on 90Zr, the eikonal model is valid down to 160 MeV; the semiclassical correction extends validity to 40 MeV.
- The 1-sigma uncertainty intervals on integrated cross sections are less than 5% for most energies below 200 MeV, but increase rapidly above 200 MeV, reaching approximately 25% for neutron elastic cross section at 400 MeV.
- Relativistic corrections become significant above 100 MeV laboratory energy.

## Context

This work provides the first comprehensive systematic study of eikonal model validity across multiple targets and observables, extending prior specific studies such as those by PhysRevC.96.054607 and PhysRevC.98.044610. It uses the KDUQ global optical potential (KDUQ, 2021) which was calibrated on data up to 200 MeV, and the results enable concrete energy thresholds for transitioning between eikonal and exact methods in few-body reaction codes, as well as opening the possibility of Bayesian model mixing to smoothly handle the transition with quantified uncertainties.
