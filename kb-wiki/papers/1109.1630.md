# 2+1 dimensional hydrodynamics including bulk viscosity: a systematics study

> We have studied the effect of nonzero bulk viscosity with peak near the lattice QCD predicted crossover temperature $T_{co}\sim 175 MeV$ on charged particle transverse momentum spectra and elliptic flow. The Israel-Stewart theory of 2nd order causal dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics is used to simulate the space time evolution of the matter formed in Au-Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=200 GeV assuming longitudinal boost invariance. A systematic comparison of temperature, transverse velocity, spatial and momentum anisotropy evolution of the ideal, bulk and shear viscous fluid has been carried out. Two different temperature dependent forms of $ζ/s$ and a constant $η/s$ was used. Both the bulk and shear viscous correction to the ideal freezeout distribution function are included. The dissipative correction to the freezeout distribution for bulk viscosity was calculated using Grad's fourteen moment method. From our simulation we show that the method is applicable only for $ζ/s < 0.005\timesη/s|_{KSS}$ for freezeout temperatures 130 and 160 MeV.

## Key claim

The Israel-Stewart second-order causal dissipative hydrodynamics with bulk viscosity is applicable to Au-Au collisions at 200 GeV only when the ratio of bulk viscosity to entropy density is less than 0.005 times the KSS bound for shear viscosity (η/s = 1/4π), for freezeout temperatures of 130 and 160 MeV; this condition limits the range of validity of the Grad's fourteen-moment method used for the bulk viscous correction to the freezeout distribution.

## Method

The authors extended the 2+1D Israel-Stewart viscous hydrodynamics code AZHYDRO-KOLKATA to include bulk viscosity, solving relaxation equations for both bulk and shear stress tensors simultaneously with energy-momentum conservation, assuming longitudinal boost invariance. Two different temperature-dependent forms of ζ/s (bulk viscosity to entropy density ratio) were used, both peaking near the lattice QCD crossover temperature of 175 MeV, along with a constant η/s = 1/4π (the AdS/CFT minimal value). The initial energy density profile was parameterized using a two-component Glauber model with central energy density 30 GeV/fm³ at initial time τ_i = 0.6 fm/c, and the Cooper-Frye algorithm was used to compute particle spectra from the freezeout surface, including both shear and bulk viscous corrections to the ideal freezeout distribution function calculated via Grad's fourteen-moment method.

## Key numbers

- Central energy density: ε₀ = 30 GeV/fm³ at initial time τ_i = 0.6 fm/c for Au-Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV
- Crossover temperature: T_co ∼ 175 MeV from lattice QCD
- Impact parameter: b = 7.4 fm for non-central Au-Au collisions
- Hard scattering fraction: x = 0.13 in the two-component Glauber model
- Freezeout temperatures studied: T_F = 130 MeV and 160 MeV
- Peak value of ζ/s in form-2 is approximately 10 times larger than the peak value in form-1
- Relative correction to p_T spectra at p_T ∼ 2 GeV: ∼ 10% with form-2 of ζ/s, less for form-1
- Relative correction to v_2 due to form-1 of ζ/s: within ∼ 4% for p_T range 0-3 GeV
- Relative correction to v_2 due to form-2 of ζ/s: within ∼ 8% for p_T range 0-3 GeV
- Applicability condition: ζ/s < 0.005 × (η/s)|_KSS for freezeout temperatures 130 and 160 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of dissipative hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions by including bulk viscosity alongside shear viscosity, using the Israel-Stewart formalism as developed by Israel (1976) and Chaudhuri (2008). The study builds on lattice QCD calculations of bulk viscosity near the crossover temperature by Meyer (2007) and others, and on the temperature-dependent ζ/s parametrizations from Noronha-Hostler (2008) and Denicol (2009). The results enable systematic comparison of bulk versus shear viscous effects on observables and establish the range of validity for the Grad's fourteen-moment method used in freezeout corrections, informing future phenomenological extractions of transport coefficients from experimental data.
