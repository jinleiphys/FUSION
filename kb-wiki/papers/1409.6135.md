# On the nuclear robustness of the r process in neutron-star mergers

> We have performed r-process calculations for matter ejected dynamically in neutron star mergers based on a complete set of trajectories from a three-dimensional relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamic simulation. Our calculations consider an extended nuclear network, including spontaneous, $β$- and neutron-induced fission and adopting fission yield distributions from the ABLA code. We have studied the sensitivity of the r-process abundances to nuclear masses by using different models. Most of the trajectories, corresponding to 90% of the ejected mass, follow a relatively slow expansion allowing for all neutrons to be captured. The resulting abundances are very similar to each other and reproduce the general features of the observed r-process abundance (the second and third peaks, the rare-earth peak and the lead peak) for all mass models as they are mainly determined by the fission yields. We find distinct differences in the abundance yields at and just above the third peak, which can be traced back to different predictions of neutron separation energies for r-process nuclei around neutron number $N=130$. The remaining trajectories, which contribute 10% by mass to the total integrated abundances, follow such a fast expansion that the r process does not use all the neutrons. This also leads to a larger variation of abundances among trajectories as fission does not dominate the r-process dynamics. The total integrated abundances are dominated by contributions from the slow abundances and hence reproduce the general features of the observed r-process abundances. We find that at timescales of weeks relevant for kilonova light curve calculations, the abundance of actinides is larger than the one of lanthanides. Hence actinides can be even more important than lanthanides to determine the photon opacities under kilonova conditions. (Abridged)

## Key claim

The r-process abundance pattern from neutron-star merger dynamical ejecta is robust against variations in nuclear mass models for the 90% of ejected mass that expands slowly enough to capture all neutrons, because fission cycling dominates the dynamics; however, distinct differences appear at and just above the third peak (around A ~ 195) that trace back to different predictions of neutron separation energies for nuclei near neutron number N=130.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations for 528 trajectories from a three-dimensional relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamic simulation of a 1.35-1.35 M_sun neutron-star merger, using a nuclear network of over 7300 nuclei. They adopted four different mass models (FRDM, WS3, DZ31, HFB21) to compute neutron capture rates consistently via the statistical model code MOD-Smoker, while keeping fission yields fixed from the ABLA code and beta-decay rates from Möller et al. (2003). The trajectories were classified into "slow ejecta" (484 trajectories, ~1.57 × 10^{-3} M_sun) where all neutrons are captured, and "fast ejecta" (44 trajectories, ~1.28 × 10^{-4} M_sun) where free neutrons remain at the end of the r process.

## Key numbers

- Total ejected mass in the simulation: ~1.7 × 10^{-3} M_sun
- Slow ejecta: 484 trajectories with total mass ~1.57 × 10^{-3} M_sun (90% of ejected mass)
- Fast ejecta: 44 trajectories with total mass ~1.28 × 10^{-4} M_sun (10% of ejected mass)
- Initial neutron-to-seed ratios (R_n/s) range from 400 to 2000 across trajectories
- Density threshold for complete neutron capture: ρ_th ≈ 0.5 g cm^{-3} at t ~ 1 s
- Initial temperatures for r-process calculations: T = 6 GK, with densities from ~10^7 to ~3 × 10^{13} g cm^{-3}
- Neutron star radius for 1.35 M_sun: 14.49 km; maximum gravitational mass: 2.21 M_sun

## Context

This work extends studies by Korobkin et al. (2012) and Bauswein et al. (2013), who found nearly identical abundance distributions between the second and third peaks across different merger conditions but strong sensitivity to fission treatment. It complements simultaneous independent studies by Eichler et al. (2015) and Caballero et al. (2014), who showed that the ABLA fission code and faster beta-decay rates (from energy density functional calculations) resolve abundance deficiencies near the second peak. The results enable more reliable predictions for kilonova light curves by establishing that actinide abundances exceed lanthanide abundances at weeks timescales, making actinides potentially more important for photon opacities in kilonova conditions.
