# The Breaking Strain of Neutron Star Crust and Gravitational Waves

> Mountains on rapidly rotating neutron stars efficiently radiate gravitational waves. The maximum possible size of these mountains depends on the breaking strain of neutron star crust. With multi-million ion molecular dynamics simulations of Coulomb solids representing the crust, we show that the breaking strain of pure single crystals is very large and that impurities, defects, and grain boundaries only modestly reduce the breaking strain to around 0.1. Due to the collective behavior of the ions during failure found in our simulations, the neutron star crust is likely very strong and can support mountains large enough so that their gravitational wave radiation could limit the spin periods of some stars and might be detectable in large scale interferometers. Furthermore, our microscopic modeling of neutron star crust material can help analyze mechanisms relevant in magnetar giant and micro flares.

# The Breaking Strain of Neutron Star Crust and Gravitational Waves

## Key claim
The breaking strain of neutron star crust is around 0.1, even when impurities, defects, and grain boundaries are present, which is large enough to support mountains that could radiate detectable gravitational waves. This result depends on the collective, non-dislocation-based failure mode observed in molecular dynamics simulations of Coulomb solids, and the authors note that their impurity sample may represent an upper limit on impurity effects because it contains more impurities than allowed by crust cooling observations.

## Method
The authors performed large-scale molecular dynamics simulations of up to 12.8 million ions interacting via a screened Coulomb (Yukawa) potential, representing neutron star crust material at a reference density of 1e13 g/cm^3. They applied shear deformation to perfect single crystals (bcc and fcc), polycrystalline samples with randomly oriented grains, and crystals containing impurities from nucleosynthesis ash, measuring the shear stress as a function of strain to determine the breaking strain. Two independent MD codes were used (YukawaMD and SPaSM), with strain rates of 4e-7 c/fm and temperatures of 0.1 MeV (Gamma=834).

## Key numbers
- Breaking strain of pure single crystals: well above 0.1 for all investigated crystallographic shear systems
- Breaking strain of defective/polycrystalline material: around 0.1 (reduced only modestly from pure crystals)
- Polycrystalline sample: 12.8 million ions with 8 randomly oriented grains, average grain diameter 3962 fm
- Reference density: n = 7.18e-5 fm^-3 (1e13 g/cm^3) with Z=29.4, A=88
- Temperature: T = 0.1 MeV, corresponding to Coulomb parameter Gamma = 834
- Impurity parameter Q = 22.3 for the nucleosynthesis ash sample (27648 ions, Z from 8 to 47)
- Maximum stress reduced by less than 45% in impure crystal compared to pure crystal
- Breaking strain of steel: around 0.005 (approximately 20 times smaller than neutron star crust)
- Estimated maximum ellipticity epsilon <= 4e-6 for a 1.4 solar mass, 10 km radius neutron star
- Tripling temperature reduces maximum stress by about 25% without significantly altering breaking strain
- Void healing simulation: cylindrical hole with diameter 2.5 times nearest neighbor distance healed under pressure, maximum stress reduced by only 25%

## Context
This work provides the first detailed molecular dynamics calculation of neutron star crust breaking strain, replacing earlier estimates that varied by orders of magnitude and were based on loose analogies with conventional materials (breakingestimate1, breakingestimate2). The results directly impact gravitational wave searches by LIGO (ligo, ligo2) and the interpretation of spin equilibrium in accreting neutron stars (watts2008). The findings also inform models of magnetar giant flares as starquakes (duncan) and the interpretation of quasiperiodic oscillations in giant flare tails (qpo1, qpo2, QPOinterp3). The impurity constraints are compared to crust cooling observations by Shternin et al. (shternin) and Brown and Cumming (cumming), who concluded that the impurity parameter Q must be less than 10.
