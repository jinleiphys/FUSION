# Mathematical surprises and Dirac's formalism in quantum mechanics

> By a series of simple examples, we illustrate how the lack of mathematical concern can readily lead to surprising mathematical contradictions in wave mechanics. The basic mathematical notions allowing for a precise formulation of the theory are then summarized and it is shown how they lead to an elucidation and deeper understanding of the aforementioned problems. After stressing the equivalence between wave mechanics and the other formulations of quantum mechanics, i.e. matrix mechanics and Dirac's abstract Hilbert space formulation, we devote the second part of our paper to the latter approach: we discuss the problems and shortcomings of this formalism as well as those of the bra and ket notation introduced by Dirac in this context. In conclusion, we indicate how all of these problems can be solved or at least avoided.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the mathematical contradictions that arise in quantum mechanics are not merely pedagogical curiosities but stem from a systematic lack of mathematical rigor, particularly the neglect of operator domains; these problems are inherent in Dirac's bra-ket formalism and cannot be resolved within that framework without adopting the precise mathematical tools of functional analysis.

## Method

The paper first presents a series of simple counterexamples in wave mechanics that lead to apparent contradictions (e.g., taking the trace of the canonical commutation relation, integration by parts for momentum, and eigenvalue problems for composite operators). It then reviews the rigorous mathematical formalism of Hilbert spaces, densely defined operators, domains of definition, and the distinction between Hermitian and self-adjoint operators. Finally, it applies this formalism to resolve each contradiction and critically analyzes Dirac's abstract Hilbert space approach and bra-ket notation, identifying its mathematical shortcomings.

*   Example 4: The momentum operator \(P = (\hbar / i) d/dx\) on \(L^2([0,1], dx)\) with Dirichlet boundary conditions \(\psi(0)=0=\psi(1)\) has no eigenvalues, yet its spectrum is the entire complex plane.

## Context

This work extends the critical analysis of Dirac's formalism by Jauch (1968) and the mathematical foundations laid by Reed and Simon (1972) and Richtmyer (1978). It contradicts the common textbook presentation that treats Hermitian and self-adjoint operators as synonyms and that applies Dirac's bra-ket calculus without regard to domain issues. The paper enables a more rigorous approach to teaching quantum mechanics and provides a framework for resolving apparent paradoxes in the study of the Aharonov-Bohm effect and in quantum optics (e.g., phase and number operators).
