# The Width of an Electron-Capture Neutrino Wave Packet

> We discuss the size of an electron neutrino wave packet emerging from an electron capture decay using the formalism of open quantum systems. This quantitative result is based on methodology that we have previously used to predict the width of an electron antineutrino wave packet from a beta-decaying nucleus, adapted for the different initial states in electron capture and beta decay. These predictions are now becoming relevant to experimental probes of the neutrino width, for example indirectly through precision spectroscopic studies of the nuclear recoil. We provide a translation between a recent Beryllium Electron capture in Superconducting Tunnel junctions Experiment (BeEST) measurement of the daughter nuclear recoil spectrum from $\mathrm{^{7}Be}$ electron capture decay ($e^- + \mathrm{^{7}Be}\rightarrow\mathrm{^{7}Li+ν_e}$) and a constraint on the outgoing neutrino width. According to our analysis, the direct limit on the neutrino wave packet width from electron capture decay using the recent BeEST result should map to $σ_{ν,x}>6.2\,\mathrm{pm}$. We determine that a hard upper limit on the wave packet width exists at $σ_{ν,x}\sim2.7\,\mathrm{nm}$, based on the relative momentum between the electron and nucleus. However, we find that the localization is most likely driven by the embedding of the decaying atom in the tantalum crystal lattice resulting in a width scale of approximately $σ_{ν,x}\sim10$~pm, motivated by data from X-ray diffraction and M$\ddot{\mathrm{o}}$ssbauer spectroscopy, which lies intriguingly close to the current bound.

# The Width of an Electron-Capture Neutrino Wave Packet

## Key claim

The electron neutrino wave packet width from electron capture decay has a hard upper limit of approximately 2.7 nm based on the relative momentum between the electron and nucleus, but the actual width is most likely driven by the embedding of the decaying atom in the tantalum crystal lattice, yielding a scale of approximately 10 pm that lies close to the current experimental bound of 6.2 pm.

## Method

The authors apply their previously developed open quantum systems formalism for calculating neutrino wave packet widths, adapted from beta decay to electron capture by accounting for both initial-state particles (electron and nucleus) and conserving energy and momentum in the decay. They write down the initial state momentum-space wave function for the electron-nucleus system, apply the decay operator that conserves center-of-mass momentum, trace out the nuclear recoil degrees of freedom to form the neutrino reduced density matrix, and extract the off-diagonal width in position space as the coherent wave packet width. For the specific case of 7Be electron capture, they use the 1S electron wave function with atomic number Z=4 and estimate the relative momentum width from the Fourier transform of the hydrogenic orbital.

## Key numbers

- The direct lower limit on the neutrino wave packet width from BeEST data maps to sigma_nu,x > 6.2 pm, derived from the measured recoil energy spectrum width of 2.9 eV and the Heisenberg uncertainty principle
- The hard upper limit on the wave packet width is sigma_nu,x ~ 2.7 nm, based on the relative momentum between the electron and nucleus in 7Be electron capture
- The likely width scale from lattice localization is approximately sigma_nu,x ~ 10 pm, motivated by X-ray diffraction and Mossbauer spectroscopy data in tantalum metal
- For 51Cr electron capture in a solid source, the coherence distance is 6400 m for ground state decay and 2100 m for excited state decay, assuming Delta m_32^2 ~ 2.5 x 10^-3 eV^2
- For 37Ar electron capture in a gas source, the atomically limited width gives sigma_nu ~ 0.13 nm, leading to a coherence distance of 97 km

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous methodology for predicting antineutrino wave packet widths from beta decay (Jones 2023) to the electron capture case, accounting for the different initial states. The predictions are directly relevant to the BeEST experiment (Smolsky 2024), which provides the first direct limits on the neutrino wave packet width from electron capture via precision spectroscopy of the daughter nuclear recoil. The results enable experimental probes of neutrino coherence through precision spectroscopic studies of nuclear recoils, and the authors use their predictions to argue that wave packet effects cannot explain the gallium anomaly observed in SAGE (Abdurashitov 1996), GALLEX (Anselmann 1995), and BEST (Barinov 2022), since the predicted coherence distances for 51Cr and 37Ar sources are far too large to produce observable disappearance at the relevant baselines.
