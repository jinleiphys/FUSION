# Strangelets accelerated by pulsars in galactic cosmic rays

> It is shown that nuggets of strange quark matter may be extracted from the surface of pulsars and accelerated by strong electric fields to high energies if pulsars are strange stars with the crusts, comprised of nuggets embedded in a uniform electron background. Such high energy nuggets called usually strangelets give an observable contribution into galactic cosmic rays and may be detected by the upcoming cosmic ray experiment Alpha Magnetic Spectrometer AMS-02 on the International Space Station.

## Key claim

If pulsars are actually strange stars with crusts composed of strangelets embedded in an electron background, then strangelets can be extracted from the pulsar surface and accelerated by strong electric fields to high energies, giving an observable contribution to galactic cosmic rays that may be detected by AMS-02. The authors estimate the mean production rate and typical energies within a factor of 2-3, but the entire scenario depends on the assumption that strange quark matter is absolutely stable and that all compact objects identified as neutron stars are actually strange stars.

## Method

The authors calculate the rate of strangelet ejection from a pulsar's polar cap using the standard Goldreich-Julian charge density required to screen the electric field component along the magnetic field, combined with the pulsar spin-down law. They integrate this rate over the pulsar lifetime from initial angular velocity (~10^3-10^4 s^-1) to final angular velocity (~10^-1 s^-1) to obtain the total number of strangelets ejected. They then estimate the typical energies of strangelets accelerated in polar gaps and outer gaps using the known electric potentials in those regions, and compute the expected flux at Earth using the galactic escape time for charged particles.

## Key numbers

- Total number of strangelets ejected from a single pulsar during its lifetime: N_str ~ 5 c^2 I / (Z e R^3 B_p), where I ~ 10^45 g cm^2, R ~ 10^6 cm, B_p ~ 10^12 G
- Mean galactic production rate of strangelets by pulsars: ~ 1.3 x 10^42 Lambda yr^-1, where Lambda is near unity for typical parameters (Z=10^2, I=10^45 g cm^2, R=10^6 cm, B_p=10^12 G)
- Typical energy of strangelets accelerated in polar gaps: E_str^p ~ 3 x 10^14 (Z/10^2) eV
- Typical energy of strangelets accelerated in outer gaps: E_str^out ~ 3 x 10^16 (Z/10^2) eV
- Expected flux of polar-gap-accelerated strangelets at ~10^14-10^15 eV: F_str^p ~ 25 Lambda (m^2 sterad yr)^-1
- Expected flux of outer-gap-accelerated strangelets at ~10^16-10^17 eV: F_str^out ~ 0.1 Lambda m^-2 yr^-1 sterad^-1
- Production rate of mass in strangelets with A~10^3: ~ 10^-12 Lambda M_sun yr^-1, which is a hundred times smaller than the value suggested by Madsen (2005)

## Context

This work proposes pulsars as a new source of high-energy strangelets in the Galaxy, in contrast to previous work that assumed strangelets are produced only in strange star collisions (Madsen 1988) or Type II supernova explosions (Benvenuto and Horvath 1989). The authors note that earlier calculations of the strangelet flux in cosmic rays used production rates that differed by several orders of magnitude and relied on Fermi acceleration in supernova shocks, which has a well-known particle injection problem. The predicted strangelet flux from polar gaps is high enough to be detected by the AMS-02 experiment on the International Space Station, which has an acceptance of about 0.5 m^2 sterad and will analyze cosmic rays for three years.
