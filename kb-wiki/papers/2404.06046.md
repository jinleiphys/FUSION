# Nuclear charge radii of germanium isotopes around $N$ = 40

> Collinear laser spectroscopy measurements were performed on $^{68-74}$Ge isotopes ($Z = 32$) at ISOLDE-CERN, by probing the $4s^2 4p^2 \, ^3\!P_1 \rightarrow 4s^2 4p 5s \, ^3\!P_1^o$ atomic transition (269~nm) of germanium. Nuclear charge radii are determined via the measured isotope shifts, revealing a larger local variation than the neighboring isotopic chains. Nuclear density functional theory with the Fayans functionals Fy($Δr$,HFB) and Fy(IVP), and the SV-min Skyrme describes the experimental data for the differential charge radii $δ\langle r^{2} \rangle$ and charge radii $R_{\rm c}$ within the theoretical uncertainties. The observed large variation in the charge radii of germanium isotopes is better accounted for by theoretical models incorporating ground state quadrupole correlations. This suggests that the polarization effects due to pairing and deformation contribute to the observed large odd-even staggering in the charge radii of the Ge isotopic chain.

## Key claim

The charge radii of germanium isotopes around \(N=40\) exhibit a larger local variation and enhanced odd-even staggering compared to neighboring isotopic chains, which is better accounted for by theoretical models that incorporate ground-state quadrupole correlations, suggesting that both pairing and shape polarization effects contribute to the observed behavior.

## Method

Collinear laser spectroscopy was performed on \(^{68-74}\)Ge isotopes at the COLLAPS setup at ISOLDE-CERN, probing the \(4s^2 4p^2 \, ^3\!P_1 \rightarrow 4s^2 4p 5s \, ^3\!P_1^o\) atomic transition at 269 nm. Isotope shifts were measured relative to the stable reference isotope \(^{72}\)Ge, and nuclear charge radii were extracted using a King-plot analysis with experimentally calibrated atomic mass-shift and field-shift factors. Nuclear density functional theory calculations using the Fayans functionals Fy(\(\Delta r\),HFB) and Fy(IVP), and the Skyrme parametrization SV-min were performed, including treatments at spherical, deformed, and ground-state quadrupole correlation (GSC) levels.

## Key numbers

- Differential charge radii \(\delta\langle r^{2}\rangle^{72,A}\) for \(^{68-74}\)Ge range from \(-0.184(13)[67]\) fm\(^2\) for \(^{68}\)Ge to \(+0.174(6)[41]\) fm\(^2\) for \(^{74}\)Ge, with statistical errors in curved brackets and systematic errors in square brackets.
- Absolute charge radii \(R_{\rm ch}\) for \(^{68-74}\)Ge range from 4.029(2)[8] fm for \(^{69}\)Ge to 4.080(2)[5] fm for \(^{74}\)Ge, with the \(^{72}\)Ge reference value taken as 4.059(2)[0] fm from electron scattering and muonic atom data.
- The calibrated atomic field-shift factor is \(F = +317(58)\) MHz/fm\(^2\) and the mass-shift factor is \(K_{\rm MS} = -9(22)\) GHz\(\cdot\)u, obtained from a King-plot analysis using known radius parameters from electron scattering and muonic atom spectroscopy.
- The odd-even staggering parameter \(\Delta^{3}(\langle r^{2}\rangle,N)\) shows model-dependent behavior, with the Fy(\(\Delta r\),HFB) functional capturing the large OES in all three calculation variants, and the SV-min GSC variant describing it very well.

## Context

This work extends previous laser spectroscopy measurements of charge radii in the \(Z=28-31\) region (nickel, copper, zinc, gallium) by providing the first data for radioactive germanium isotopes, which were previously scarce even for stable isotopes. The results are compared with nuclear density functional theory calculations using the Fayans functionals Fy(\(\Delta r\),HFB) (calibrated on calcium radii in Fayan (2017)) and Fy(IVP) (developed in In-radii to address isovector pairing), and the SV-min Skyrme functional. The enhanced odd-even staggering in germanium is attributed to both pairing and shape polarization effects, highlighting the transitional character of these nuclei with soft potential energy surfaces and shape coexistence, as previously discussed by Heyde (2011) and Sugawara (2003). This work enables further tests of quantified DFT calculations for deformed open-shell nuclei and motivates studies of more neutron-rich germanium isotopes.
