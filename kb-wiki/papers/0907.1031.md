# The QCD confinement transition: hadron formation

> We review the foundations and the applications of the statistical and the quark recombination model as hadronization models.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the statistical hadronization model (SHM) and the quark recombination/coalescence model as effective phenomenological descriptions of hadron formation, noting that hadronization is not yet calculable from QCD first principles and that these models, while limited, provide relevant information about QCD in the confinement regime.

## Method

The authors review the foundations and formalism of the Statistical Hadronization Model (SHM), which postulates that every multihadronic state localized within a cluster and compatible with conservation laws is equally likely, with clusters treated as finite-sized colorless objects. They then review the quark recombination/coalescence model, which extends the concept of single parton fragmentation functions used in elementary collisions since the 1970s to describe hadron formation in relativistic heavy ion collisions. The paper compares the two models and discusses their successes in describing hadronic multiplicities in both elementary and heavy ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- The QCD confinement scale is of order 1 fm, with perturbative QCD applicable only for momentum transfers much greater than 1 GeV
- The pion's Compton wavelength is approximately 1.4 fm, below which finite size effects become important in the SHM
- The ideal hadron-resonance gas picture is estimated to hold for temperatures above approximately 100 MeV

## Context

This review extends the long history of statistical approaches to multiparticle production dating back to Fermi (1950) and Hagedorn (1960s), and the quark recombination model which builds on fragmentation functions used since the 1970s. The paper discusses how the SHM's recent success in describing particle multiplicities in elementary collisions (where particle numbers are of order 10) challenges the belief that hadronic thermalization would require long timescales, and how the quark recombination model has found success specifically in relativistic heavy ion collisions. The work enables further investigation into why non-thermal systems exhibit thermal behavior and how these models relate to the fundamental QCD scale Lambda_QCD.
