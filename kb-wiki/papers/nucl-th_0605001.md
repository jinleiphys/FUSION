# Isospin asymmetry in nuclei and nuclear symmetry energy

> The volume and surface symmetry parts of the nuclear symmetry energy and other coefficients of the liquid droplet model are determined from the measured atomic masses by the maximum likelihood estimator. The volume symmetry energy coefficient extracted from finite nuclei provides a constraint on the nuclear symmetry energy. This approach also yields the neutron skin of a finite nucleus through its relationship with the volume and surface symmetry terms and the Coulomb energy coefficient. The description of nuclear matter from the isoscalar and isovector components of the density dependent M3Y effective interaction provide a value of the symmetry energy that is consistent with the empirical value of the symmetry energy extracted from measured atomic masses and with other modern theoretical descriptions of nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The volume symmetry energy coefficient extracted from finite nuclei using the maximum likelihood estimator provides a constraint on the nuclear symmetry energy, and the value obtained from the density dependent M3Y effective interaction is consistent with this empirical value, but the extracted volume symmetry energy coefficient depends on whether lighter nuclei (A < 16) are included in the fit, changing from 30.048 MeV to 29.687 MeV.

## Method

The authors fitted the liquid droplet model mass formula (with volume, surface, Coulomb, pairing, and volume plus surface symmetry terms) to measured and extrapolated atomic mass excesses from the Audi-Wapstra-Thibault atomic mass table using the maximum likelihood estimator method, which simultaneously solves for model parameters, theoretical error standard deviation, and theoretical error mean. Separately, they performed a mean field calculation of nuclear matter using the density dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) effective nucleon-nucleon interaction to compute the nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density from both the standard expansion definition and the alternative definition as the energy difference between pure neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter.

## Key numbers

- Volume symmetry energy coefficient \(S_v = 30.048 \pm 0.004\) MeV from fitting all 2228 experimentally measured atomic mass excesses
- Volume symmetry energy coefficient \(S_v = 29.687 \pm 0.010\) MeV from fitting 2168 measured atomic mass excesses for A \(\ge\) 16
- Surface symmetry energy coefficient \(S_s = 16.674 \pm 0.007\) MeV from all 2228 measured data; \(S_s = 17.680 \pm 0.022\) MeV for A \(\ge\) 16
- Nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density from DDM3Y mean field calculation: \(S(\rho_0) = 31.18 \pm 0.29\) MeV using the standard definition (Eq. 1) and \(31.86 \pm 0.29\) MeV using the alternative definition (Eq. 7)
- Theoretical model error \(\sigma_{th} = 2.880\) and mean \(\mu_{th} = 0.029\) for 2228 measured data; \(\sigma_{th} = 2.782\) and \(\mu_{th} = 0.037\) for 2168 data with A \(\ge\) 16
- Ratio \(S_v / S_s \approx 1.8\) from the present fit

## Context

This work extends the liquid droplet model approach of Danielewicz (2003) by using the maximum likelihood estimator method to decouple theoretical and experimental errors, yielding a volume symmetry energy coefficient consistent with the range 29.10-32.67 MeV found in that prior work. The DDM3Y mean field result (31.18 MeV) agrees with the variational calculation of Akmal et al. (1998) using the Argonne Av18 potential with relativistic boost correction and UIX* three-nucleon interaction (30.1 MeV), while being higher than other variational results such as Av14 (24.90 MeV) or Av18+UIX (29.23 MeV). The extracted volume symmetry energy enables constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density and provides a relationship to neutron skin thickness in finite nuclei.
