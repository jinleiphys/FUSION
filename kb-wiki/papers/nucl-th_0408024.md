# Triaxiality and the determination of the cubic shape parameter K3 from five observables

> The absolute and the relative quadrupole shape invariants q3 and K3 provide a model independent measure of triaxiality for beta-rigid nuclei. We will show that one can obtain q3 and K3 from a small number of observables. The approximations which are made will be shown to hold within a few percent both in the rigid triaxial rotor model and the interacting boson model. The shape parameter K3 is given for an exemplary set of nuclei and is translated into effective values of the geometrical deformation parameters beta and gamma.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the cubic shape parameter \(K_3\), a model-independent measure of triaxiality for beta-rigid nuclei, can be obtained with good accuracy (within a few percent) from only five observables: four B(E2) values and the sign of the quadrupole moment of the \(2^+_1\) state, using a second-order Q-phonon approximation that is necessary for transitional nuclei where the first-order approximation deviates by up to 30%.

## Method

The authors derive a second-order approximation for the cubic shape invariant \(q_3\) within the Q-phonon scheme, allowing one Q-forbidden matrix element with \(\Delta Q = 2\) in each term of the sum over E2 matrix elements. This yields an expression for \(K_3^{appr.}\) in terms of four B(E2) values: \(B(E2;2^+_1\rightarrow 0^+_1)\), \(B(E2;2^+_2\rightarrow 0^+_1)\), \(B(E2;2^+_2\rightarrow 2^+_1)\), and \(B(E2;2^+_1\rightarrow 2^+_1)\), plus the sign of the quadrupole moment of the \(2^+_1\) state. The approximation is tested against exact calculations in the rigid triaxial rotor model (Davydov-Fillipov) and the interacting boson model (IBM-1) across their full parameter ranges.

## Key numbers

* In the rigid triaxial rotor model, the ratio \(R_{geo}^{K_3} = (1+|K_3^{appr.}|)/(1+|K_3|)\) deviates from unity by at most 8% for all \(\gamma\) values between 0 and 30 degrees; the corresponding absolute deviation of \(\gamma^{appr.}\) from the true \(\gamma\) is below 3.5 degrees at \(\gamma \approx 15\) degrees.
* In the IBM-1 with \(N=10\) bosons, the ratio \(R_{IBM}^{K_3} = (1+|K_3^{appr.}|)/(1+|K_3|)\) deviates from unity by less than 7% over the full parameter space; the deviation of \(\gamma_{eff}^{appr.}\) from \(\gamma_{eff}\) is always smaller than 2.5 degrees.
* For the first-order approximation in the rigid triaxial rotor model, the deviation from unity reaches a maximum of about 30%, demonstrating the necessity of the second-order approximation.
* For \(^{190}\)Os, the first-order approximation gives \(K_3^{(1)} = -0.7(1)\), while the second-order approximation gives \(K_3^{appr.} = -0.35(9)\), showing a significant correction for transitional nuclei.
* For \(^{152}\)Sm and \(^{154}\)Gd, the \(K_4^{appr.}\) values are 1.02(3) and 1.088(26), respectively, indicating near-beta-rigidity.

## Context

This work extends the use of quadrupole shape invariants introduced by Kumar (1972) and widely applied by Cline and co-workers (1986), who obtained \(q_2\) and \(q_3\) from extensive multiple Coulomb excitation data sets. The paper provides a practical method to determine the cubic shape parameter \(K_3\) from a much smaller set of observables, making it accessible for nuclei where complete Coulomb excitation data are unavailable. This enables systematic studies of triaxiality across the nuclear chart, particularly for transitional nuclei near shape/phase transitions between spherical and deformed shapes, and between prolate and oblate deformations, as discussed by Iachello (2000) and Casten (2000). The method also allows extraction of effective \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) deformation parameters from data, though the authors caution that for vibrational nuclei the geometrical parameters are not well-defined while \(K_3\) remains a valid observable.
