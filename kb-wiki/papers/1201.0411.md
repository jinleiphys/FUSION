# Study of reaction and decay using densities from relativistic mean field theory

> Relativistic mean field calculations have been performed to obtain nuclear density pro- file. Microscopic interactions have been folded with the calculated densities of finite nuclei to obtain a semi-microscopic potential. Life time values for the emission of proton, alpha particles and complex clusters have been calculated in the WKB approach assum- ing a tunneling process through the potential barrier. Elastic scattering cross sections have been estimated for proton-nucleus scattering in light neutron rich nuclei. Low en- ergy proton reactions have been studied and their astrophysical implications have been discussed. The success of the semi-microscopic potentials obtained in the folding model with RMF densities in explaining nuclear decays and reactions has been emphasized.

## Key claim

Semi-microscopic optical potentials constructed by folding relativistic mean field (RMF) densities with effective nucleon-nucleon interactions can successfully describe nuclear decays, elastic scattering, and low-energy proton reactions across the entire mass region, without requiring normalization of the optical potential for decay studies, though the real and imaginary parts of the potential must be normalized by factors of 0.7 and 0.1 respectively to reproduce experimental S-factors for low-energy proton capture reactions.

## Method

The author performed relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations using the FSU Gold Lagrangian density, solving the equations in coordinate space with BCS pairing (pairing strength 300 MeV-fm for both protons and neutrons). Theoretical nuclear densities were folded with either the density-dependent M3Y (DDM3Y) interaction or the Jeukenne-Lejeune-Mahaux (JLM) interaction to construct semi-microscopic nucleon-nucleus potentials. Decay lifetimes for proton, alpha, and cluster emission were calculated in the WKB approximation assuming tunneling through the potential barrier, while elastic scattering cross sections and low-energy proton reaction rates were computed using the optical model and the TALYS 1.2 code with Hauser-Feshbach statistical theory.

## Key numbers

- Proton decay half-lives for 28 spherical proton emitters with even neutron number were calculated using the DDM3Y interaction; for example, log10 T(s) for 105Sb was 2.27(46) (experimental: 2.049(+0.058/-0.067)), for 109I was -4.03(4) (experimental: -3.987(+0.020/-0.022)), and for 185Bi was -5.33(13) (experimental: -4.229(+0.068/-0.081)).
- For alpha decay in superheavy nuclei, a constant spectroscopic factor of 1.4 x 10^-2 was used for all decays, obtained from a fit of half-life values.
- Elastic proton scattering cross sections for 6He and 8He in inverse kinematics at 71 MeV/A and 72 MeV/A respectively were reproduced using the DDM3Y potential with the real part scaled by 0.8 and the imaginary part by 0.2.
- For low-energy proton reactions in the A=60-80 mass region, the real part of the folded DDM3Y potential was normalized by a factor of 0.7 and the imaginary part by 0.1 to explain experimental S-factors.
- At 1.2 GK, the population reaching A=80 or beyond after 100 seconds was more than 1.5 times the corresponding quantity at 1.5 GK in rp-process network calculations starting from 56Ni.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Basu (who used phenomenological densities with the M3Y interaction) and Bhagwat and Gambhir (who used RMF densities) by applying RMF-based folding potentials consistently to decays, elastic scattering, and astrophysical reactions. The study addresses uncertainties in rp-process reaction rates previously highlighted by Schatz, who varied rates by a factor of 100, and provides a consistent microscopic framework for calculating rates at waiting-point nuclei (64Ge, 68Se, 72Kr, 76Sr) that are critical for X-ray burst models. The results enable more reliable predictions for nucleosynthesis in explosive hydrogen-rich environments where experimental data on exotic nuclei are unavailable.
