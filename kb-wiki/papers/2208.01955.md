# Relativistic dissipative spin hydrodynamics from kinetic theory with a nonlocal collision term

> We derive relativistic dissipative spin hydrodynamics from kinetic theory featuring a nonlocal collision term using the method of moments. In this framework, the components of the spin tensor are dynamical variables which obey relaxation-type equations. We find that the corresponding relaxation times are determined by the local part of the collision term, while the nonlocal part contributes to the Navier-Stokes terms in these equations of motion. The spin relaxation time scales are comparable to those of the usual dissipative currents. Finally, the Navier-Stokes limit of the Pauli-Lubanski vector receives contributions proportional to the shear tensor of the fluid, which implies that the polarization of hadrons observed in heavy-ion collisions is influenced by dissipative effects.

## Key claim

The authors derive relativistic dissipative spin hydrodynamics from kinetic theory with a nonlocal collision term, showing that spin relaxation times are comparable to those of standard dissipative currents and that the Navier-Stokes limit of the Pauli-Lubanski vector receives contributions proportional to the fluid shear tensor, implying that hadron polarization in heavy-ion collisions is influenced by dissipative effects, but only under the condition that the Knudsen number for spin degrees of freedom is of order one, which occurs when spin dynamics happens on similar or larger time scales than the bulk collision dynamics.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic Boltzmann equation with a nonlocal collision term derived from quantum field theory for spin-1/2 Dirac particles, where the space-time shift in the collision term is of order the Compton wavelength. They apply the method of moments generalized to spin degrees of freedom, expanding the spin-dependent distribution function in irreducible moments of the spin vector, and derive relaxation-type equations for the dynamical spin moments. The collision term is linearized and separated into local and nonlocal parts, with the local part determining relaxation times and the nonlocal part contributing to Navier-Stokes terms; the system is truncated in a 14+24-moment approximation (14 moments from Israel-Stewart theory plus 24 spin tensor components).

## Key numbers

- Spin relaxation times for the moments \(\mathfrak{p}^\mu\), \(\mathfrak{z}^{\mu\nu}\), and \(\mathfrak{q}^{\lambda\mu\nu}\) are computed as functions of \(m\beta_0\) (mass over temperature) and shown in Fig. 1; they are of the same order of magnitude but slightly smaller than relaxation times for standard dissipative quantities \(\Pi\), \(n^\mu\), and \(\pi^{\mu\nu}\) from the standard 14-moment approximation.
- The space-time shift \(\Delta^\mu\) in the nonlocal collision term is of order \(\hbar/m\), the Compton wavelength of the particles.
- The condition for the expansion in powers of \(\hbar\) to remain meaningful is \(\Delta/\ell_\text{vort} \ll 1\), where \(\ell_\text{vort}\) characterizes the fluid vorticity scale; the authors assume \(\Delta/\ell_\text{vort} \sim \text{Kn}\) (Knudsen number).
- The collision integrals \(g_r^{(0)}\) and \(h_r^{(2)}\) contain factors of \(\hbar/m\) and involve integrations over momentum space with the transition rate \(\mathcal{W}_0\).

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to establish relativistic spin hydrodynamics (Florkowski 2017, Hattori 2019, Speranza 2020) by providing a derivation of second-order dissipative spin hydrodynamics from a microscopic kinetic theory with a nonlocal collision term, which was missing in the literature. It builds on spin kinetic theory derived from quantum field theory (Weickgenannt 2020, 2021) and the method of moments (Denicol 2012). The results enable phenomenological studies of how dissipative effects influence hadron polarization in heavy-ion collisions, particularly addressing whether spin degrees of freedom evolve on time scales comparable to the bulk fluid, as suggested by perturbative QCD estimates (Kapusta 2019) and effective model calculations (Ayala 2019, 2020).
