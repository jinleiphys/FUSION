# Initial state of Heavy-Ion Collisions: Isotropization and thermalization

> I discuss how local thermal equilibrium and hydrodynamical flow are reached in heavy-ion collisions in the weak coupling limit.

## Key claim
The paper argues that in the weak coupling limit of QCD, the prethermal evolution of heavy-ion collisions proceeds through two distinct stages (overoccupied and underoccupied) that must be described by different effective theories (classical Yang-Mills and kinetic theory respectively), and that this combined approach can smoothly and automatically approach hydrodynamical flow without dependence on an unphysical initialization time.

## Method
The author reviews recent simulations using classical Yang-Mills (CYM) theory for the early overoccupied stage and effective kinetic theory (EKT) for the later underoccupied stage, with a parametrically large region of overlapping validity where both descriptions are equivalent at leading order. The CYM simulations start from Color-Glass-Condensate initial conditions and are evolved in 3+1D, while the EKT simulations use a parametrization of the CYM output at a switching time τ_EKT = 1/Q_s and are evolved with different values of the coupling constant α_s.

## Key numbers
- The initial gluon occupancy at the saturation scale Q_s is f ∼ 1/α_s (parametrically overoccupied)
- For the small coupling α_s ≈ 0.03, viscous hydrodynamics agrees with the kinetic theory simulation at very late times τ ∼ 5000/Q_s
- For the intermediate coupling α_s ≈ 0.3, the system follows the hydrodynamical line already around τ ∼ 10/Q_s
- Converting to physical units using Q_s ≈ 2 GeV gives hydrodynamization times of ∼ 1 fm/c
- At hydrodynamization, the anisotropy is still quite large with P_L/P_T ∼ 0.2

## Context
This work extends the weak coupling thermalization picture originally proposed by Baier (2000), who first realized that under longitudinal expansion an initially overoccupied system becomes underoccupied before reaching thermal equilibrium. It contrasts with holographic methods applied to N=4 SYM by Chesler (2008, 2010) and van der Schee (2013), which cannot be applied to QCD. The paper enables a quantitative leading-order description of prethermal evolution that can be matched to hydrodynamical simulations, and opens the possibility of interpolating between weak and strong coupling results as initiated by Keegan (2015).
