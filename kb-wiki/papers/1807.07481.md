# Distributions of the largest fragment size in multifragmentation: Traces of a phase transition

> Distributions of the largest fragment charge are studied using the ALADIN data on fragmentation of $^{197}$Au projectiles at relativistic energies. The statistical measures skewness and kurtosis of higher-order fluctuations provide a robust indication of the transition point, linked to a phase transition in the thermodynamic limit. Extensive comparisons with predictions of a bond percolation model corroborate the high accuracy of this model in reproducing the distributions as well as the whole fragmentation pattern as represented by the measured charge correlations. In analogy to percolation, the pseudocritical and critical points are identified in the fragmentation data. Questions concerning the distinction between different models and between first- and second-order phase transitions are discussed.

## Key claim

The statistical measures skewness and kurtosis of the largest fragment charge distribution provide a robust indication of the transition point in nuclear multifragmentation, but the distinction between first- and second-order phase transitions remains ambiguous because both types exhibit similar cumulant signatures in finite systems, with the pseudocritical point characterized by zero skewness and a kurtosis minimum near -1.

## Method

The authors analyzed ALADIN data from fragmentation of 197Au projectiles on Cu, In, and Au targets at incident energies of 600, 800, and 1000 MeV per nucleon, studying the probability distributions of the largest fragment charge Zmax as a function of the sorting variable Zbound (sum of charges of fragments with Z >= 2). They computed the dimensionless cumulant ratios K2 (normalized variance), K3 (skewness), and K4 (kurtosis excess) of the Zmax distributions and compared them with predictions from a three-dimensional bond percolation model on simple cubic lattices with free boundary conditions, using Monte Carlo event generation and the Hoshen-Kopelman algorithm for cluster recognition.

## Key numbers

- The pseudocritical point in the experimental data is identified at Zbound = 54, corresponding to a mean system size Z0 of approximately 64 (derived from Zbound/Z0 = 0.84).
- The critical point in the percolation sense is located near Zbound = 36, with an estimated system size Z0 around 52 (from Zbound/Z0 = 0.69).
- At the pseudocritical point (Zbound = 54), the fragment size distribution for Z < 15 follows a power-law with exponent tau = 2.189 and normalization constant q0 = 0.173.
- At Zbound = 36, Zipf's law behavior is observed with exponent lambda = 0.93 from a fit to percolation results.
- For the 197Au + 197Au system at 1000 MeV/nucleon, statistical uncertainties at Zbound = 31 are 0.0091 for K2, 0.077 for K3, and 0.347 for K4.
- The transition point at Zbound = 54 corresponds to an excitation energy around 6 MeV per nucleon, associated with temperatures in the range 5-7 MeV.
- Near-critical events at Zbound = 36 correspond to excitation energy about 10 MeV per nucleon and temperatures in the range 6-7 MeV.
- For the thermodynamic model, transition temperatures for systems with 64, 216, and 1000 nucleons are 6.09, 6.55, and 7.11 MeV respectively, with the thermodynamic limit expected at T = 8 MeV and critical temperature Tc = 18 MeV.

## Context

This work extends percolation studies by Brzychczyk (2015) that proposed cumulant ratios as signatures of critical behavior in finite systems, applying them to experimental nuclear multifragmentation data from the ALADIN Collaboration (Schuettauf et al., 1996). The analysis demonstrates that the bond percolation model reproduces not only the largest fragment distributions but also the full fragmentation pattern including charge correlations, as previously noted by Kreutz et al. (1993). The results enable quantitative comparisons with other models such as the lattice-gas model and the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM), which have been shown by Wieloch et al. and Pietrzak et al. to exhibit similar cumulant signatures despite containing first-order phase transitions in the thermodynamic limit, highlighting the difficulty of distinguishing transition order in finite nuclear systems.
