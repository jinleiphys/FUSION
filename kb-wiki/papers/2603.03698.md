# Empirical Universal Scaling of Neutron-Skin Curvature Across the Nuclear Chart

> Neutron skins encode essential information about nuclear geometry, surface structure, and isovector response, yet a compact description across the nuclear chart remains elusive. We present an empirical analysis of neutron-excess surface systematics using a mass-normalized, charge-radius-derived proxy ("neutron-skin curvature") built from evaluated experimental charge radii. By normalizing radii to the reduced Compton length $r_B=\hbar/(mc)$, we form a dimensionless curvature ratio that enables comparison across isotopic chains of widely varying mass. When expressed versus normalized neutron excess, data for more than 800 nuclei spanning 88 elements collapse onto a single empirical curve without element-specific rescaling or interaction-model tuning; the curve is used only as a fixed baseline for residual analysis. The collapse accounts for approximately 88% of the variance and is substantially tighter than droplet-style baselines fit to the same dataset. Residuals show structured deviations: three finite-size regimes (skin formation, relaxation toward bulk geometry, and saturation) and a distinct few-body domain for very light nuclei ($Z\le 4$). Stratifying residuals by periodic-table families reveals tighter submanifolds for several groups, suggesting additional geometric constraints layered on the global trend. These results are obtained directly from evaluated experimental data and physical constants, without introducing new interaction terms, and motivate further study of geometric correlations with other nuclear and atomic observables.

## Key claim
The paper claims that when nuclear charge radii are normalized by the relativistic quantum length scale \(\hbar/(mc)\) and expressed as a dimensionless curvature ratio versus normalized neutron excess, data for over 800 nuclei across 88 elements collapse onto a single empirical curve without element-specific rescaling or interaction-model tuning, accounting for approximately 88% of the variance. The hedge is that this "neutron-skin curvature" is explicitly an operational, charge-radius-derived proxy for neutron-excess surface growth and should not be conflated with the neutron-skin thickness \(\Delta r_{np}\) extracted from neutron-sensitive probes.

## Method
The author constructed a mass-normalized curvature framework by dividing experimental charge radii by the reduced Compton length \(r_B = \hbar/(mc)\) to form a dimensionless curvature ratio \(K_R = r_{\text{exp}}/r_B\). A core-skin decomposition was performed to isolate the neutron-excess surface component, and the resulting skin-curvature proxy was expressed as a function of normalized neutron excess \(x = N_{\text{excess}}/Z\). The global backbone was established using a fixed quadratic baseline fitted to the full dataset, and residuals were analyzed for structured deviations stratified by curvature families and periodic-table groupings.

## Key numbers
- Data from more than 800 nuclei spanning 88 elements were analyzed.
- The universal curve collapse accounts for approximately 88% of the observed variance.
- In curvature-normalized coordinates, the coefficient of determination is \(R^2 \approx 0.97\), compared to \(R^2 \approx 0.63\) for the radius-based representation.
- The curvature-normalized representation achieves a reduction in normalized residual scatter by more than a factor of three under matched quadratic baselines.
- Family-conditioned residual collapse ratios relative to global residual MAD: C1 family \(\approx 0.00\), Transitional family 0.12, C2 family 0.24, \(\alpha\)-trough family 0.34, C3 onset family 0.50.
- Branching effect sizes (Cohen's \(d\)): Transitional family \(\sim 3.0\), C2 family \(\sim 5.5\).
- Parity gap RMS values: C1 family 0.0156, Transitional family 0.0106, C2 family 0.0269, \(\alpha\)-trough family 0.0165.

## Context
This work extends empirical systematics of nuclear charge radii from the Angeli and Marinova (2013) compilation by introducing a mass-normalized curvature framework that achieves tighter cross-element collapse than standard droplet-style baselines fitted to the same dataset. The observed universal scaling establishes a robust empirical foundation for charge-radius-derived isovector surface geometry and motivates further investigation of geometric correlations with other nuclear and atomic observables, including potential connections to spectroscopic manifestations analyzed in a companion paper.
