# Reply to Comment on "Is a Trineutron Resonance Lower in Energy than a Tetraneutron Resonance?"

> We reply to a Comment on our Letter [Phys. Rev. Lett. 118, 232501 (2017), arXiv:1612.01502] by A. Deltuva and R. Lazauskas [Phys. Rev. Lett 123, 069201 (2019), arXiv:1904.00925].

# Reply to Comment on "Is a Trineutron Resonance Lower in Energy than a Tetraneutron Resonance?"

## Key claim

The authors defend their earlier claim that extrapolations of trapped three-neutron and four-neutron calculations point to a common positive energy scale independent of trap geometry, suggesting a possible tetraneutron resonance and indications of a trineutron resonance at lower energy. However, they explicitly acknowledge that they did not claim a resonance definitely exists, cannot quantify its width, and cannot distinguish between a resonance and a virtual state.

## Method

The authors used auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) to compute the lowest-energy eigenstates of three- and four-neutron systems confined in external Woods-Saxon trapping potentials of varying depth and radius. They extrapolated the energies to the limit of zero trap depth, following the procedure of Pieper (2003), and validated this extrapolation method on two-body S-wave resonances where the correct resonance energy is reproduced. They also examined the imaginary-time evolution of the four-neutron energy to check for signs of decay into clusters.

## Key numbers

- The four-neutron resonance is identified at approximately 2.5 MeV from extrapolations using only points where the four-neutron energy is below the two-neutron energy for a Woods-Saxon radius of 7.5 fm.
- For the four-neutron system in a Woods-Saxon well with depth V0 = -1.25 MeV and radius R_WS = 6.0 fm, the condition E_2n < E_4n holds, and no decay in energy is observed over a very long imaginary time evolution.
- Additional two-body calculations for two S-wave resonances show that the linear extrapolation to zero well depth correctly gives the position of the resonance within fit uncertainties.

## Context

This reply addresses a Comment by Deltuva and Lazauskas (2019) on the authors' original Letter (Gandolfi et al., 2017), which itself followed experimental evidence for a tetraneutron resonance from Kisamori (2016). The work extends a long line of conflicting theoretical results on few-neutron resonances, including calculations by Pieper (2003), Lazauskas (2005), Hiyama (2016), Shirokov (2016), Fossez (2016), and Greene (2018). The authors note that Deltuva (2018) already put forward arguments similar to those in the Comment, and they acknowledge that their extrapolation cannot distinguish between a resonance and a virtual state, leaving the question of few-neutron structures for experiments to decide.
