# Constraining Hamiltonians from chiral effective field theory with neutron-star data

> Multi-messenger observations of neutron stars (NSs) and their mergers have placed strong constraints on the dense-matter equation of state (EOS). The EOS, in turn, depends on microscopic nuclear interactions that are described by nuclear Hamiltonians. These Hamiltonians are commonly derived within chiral effective field theory (EFT). Ideally, multi-messenger observations of NSs could be used to directly inform our understanding of EFT interactions, but such a direct inference necessitates millions of model evaluations. This is computationally prohibitive because each evaluation requires us to calculate the EOS from a Hamiltonian by solving the quantum many-body problem with methods such as auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC), which provides very accurate and precise solutions but at a significant computational cost. Additionally, we need to solve the stellar structure equations for each EOS which further slows down each model evaluation by a few seconds. In this work, we combine emulators for AFDMC calculations of neutron matter, built using parametric matrix models, and for the stellar structure equations, built using multilayer perceptron neural networks, with the \texttt{PyCBC} data-analysis framework to enable a direct inference of coupling constants in an EFT Hamiltonian using multi-messenger observations of NSs. We find that astrophysical data can provide informative constraints on two-nucleon couplings despite the high densities probed in NS interiors.

# Constraining Hamiltonians from chiral effective field theory with neutron-star data

## Key claim
Present multi-messenger neutron-star observations provide modest additional constraints on two-nucleon low-energy couplings (LECs) beyond those already obtained from NN scattering data, but only when the nuclear Hamiltonian is applied up to twice nuclear saturation density (2n₀). Next-generation gravitational-wave detectors could provide strong complementary constraints on these LECs.

## Method
The authors combined two emulators, a parametric matrix model (PMM) for auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) calculations of pure neutron matter and multilayer perceptron (MLP) neural networks for the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations, within the PyCBC Bayesian inference framework. They sampled over six spectral LECs from an N²LO chiral EFT Hamiltonian, using priors from NN scattering analyses, and analyzed data from GW170817, pulsar mass measurements, and NICER radius observations. The EOS was constructed by combining the PMM neutron-matter output with a nuclear meta-model and a speed-of-sound extension above 2n₀.

## Key numbers
- Radius of a 1.4 M⊙ neutron star: 11.6⁺⁰·⁷₋₀·₅ km at 90% credible level
- AFDMC emulator speedup factor: 10⁸ relative to full AFDMC calculations
- AFDMC emulator errors: approximately 1%, comparable to AFDMC statistical uncertainty
- TOV emulator errors: approximately 0.01% for maximum mass, radii, and tidal deformabilities
- Transition density for speed-of-sound extension: n_tr = 2n₀ (with n₀ ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³)
- Training set: 200,000 EOS; validation set: 70,000 EOS
- PMM training: approximately 30 training LEC sets
- Simulated next-generation detector events: SNR ~2300 for 1.4-1.4 M⊙ binary; SNR ~1730 for 1.0-1.0 M⊙ binary
- GW170817 tidal deformability constraint for 1.4 M⊙ NS: 190⁺³⁹⁰₋₁₂₀ at 90% credible level (from prior literature)

## Context
This work extends earlier efforts by Maselli et al. (2020) and Sabatucci et al. (2022) that constrained simplified Hamiltonians with a single free 3N-force parameter, and by Somasundaram et al. (2024) who studied two pion-nucleon LECs with broad uniform priors. The present work overcomes those limitations by studying all six spectral LECs in an N²LO Hamiltonian using informative priors from NN scattering analyses (Somasundaram et al., 2023). The pipeline enables direct inference of microscopic nuclear couplings from astrophysical data, paving the way to measure LECs that are difficult to extract from laboratory experiments.
