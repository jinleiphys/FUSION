# Neutrino scattering and flavor transformation in supernovae

> We argue that the small fraction of neutrinos that undergo direction-changing scattering outside of the neutrinosphere could have significant influence on neutrino flavor transformation in core-collapse supernova environments. We show that the standard treatment for collective neutrino flavor transformation is adequate at late times, but could be inadequate in the crucial shock revival/explosion epoch of core-collapse supernovae, where the potentials that govern neutrino flavor evolution are affected by the scattered neutrinos. Taking account of this effect, and the way it couples to entropy and composition, will require a new paradigm in supernova modeling.

## Key claim

The standard Neutrino Bulb model for collective neutrino flavor transformation is adequate at late times (e.g., neutrino-driven wind phase), but could be inadequate during the crucial shock revival/explosion epoch of core-collapse supernovae, because the small fraction of neutrinos that undergo direction-changing scattering outside the neutrinosphere can contribute significantly to the neutrino-neutrino interaction potential despite their low number density.

## Method

The authors compare the maximum magnitude of the neutrino-neutrino Hamiltonian contributed by neutrinos that have undergone direction-changing scattering (the "halo") to that contributed by neutrinos that stream directly from the neutrinosphere (the "bulb"). They derive a safety criterion (Equation labeled Safety) relating matter density to radius, below which the halo contribution is less than 1% of the bulb contribution. They then evaluate this criterion against 1D density profiles from various supernova environments and perform a 3D calculation using a 2D matter density distribution from a 15 solar mass progenitor core-collapse supernova model at 500 ms after core bounce, computing the single-scattering halo contribution to the neutrino-neutrino potential using energy-dependent neutral current neutrino-nucleon scattering cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The safety criterion for the Neutrino Bulb model to be adequate (halo contribution < 1%) is: ρ(r) < 0.01 × ρ_{τ=1} (R_ν / r)^3, where ρ_{τ=1} ~ 10^(11 to 12) g cm^{-3} and R_ν ~ 30-60 km for the transport sphere early in the explosion epoch.
- In the 2D Fe-core-collapse model at 500 ms after core bounce, the scattered halo nowhere contributes a maximum magnitude less than 14% of the neutrino-neutrino potential magnitude, and in many places contributes 90% or more of the total.
- The criterion for matter suppression of collective oscillations, Δ|H_e| ~ Δ|H_V|, is not met where matter densities drop below ~ 10^(7 to 8) g cm^{-3}.
- For a matter density profile ρ(r') ∝ r'^{-3}, each logarithmically spaced shell makes an equal contribution of neutrino number density at radius r, causing the ratio |H_νν^{halo}|/|H_νν^{bulb}| to remain fixed with increasing radius.

## Context

This work extends the standard Neutrino Bulb model used in all collective neutrino flavor transformation calculations (Duan 2006, Hannestad 2006, Dasgupta 2009, Cherry 2010, 2011) by identifying a previously overlooked contribution from direction-scattered neutrinos. The paper shows that the Fe-core-collapse shock revival environment (Bruenn 2009) and O-Ne-Mg core-collapse models (Nomoto 1984, 1987) will have significant scattered halos, while late-time neutrino-driven wind models (Duan 2006, Kitaura 2006, Fogli 2007) avoid this complication. This finding necessitates a new paradigm in supernova modeling that couples neutrino flavor transformation to entropy, composition, and multi-dimensional flow geometries, potentially affecting understanding of the supernova explosion mechanism and nucleosynthesis.
