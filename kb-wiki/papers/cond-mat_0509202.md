# On the foundation of thermodynamics by microcanonical thermostatistics. The microscopic origin of condensation and phase separations

> Conventional thermo-statistics address infinite homogeneous systems within the canonical ensemble. However, some 170 years ago the original motivation of thermodynamics was the description of steam engines, i.e. boiling water. Its essential physics is the separation of the gas phase from the liquid. Of course, boiling water is inhomogeneous and as such cannot be treated by conventional thermo-statistics. Then it is not astonishing, that a phase transition of first order is signaled canonically by a Yang-Lee singularity. Thus it is only treated correctly by microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck statistics. This is elaborated in the present paper. It turns out that the Boltzmann-Planck statistics is much richer and gives fundamental insight into statistical mechanics and especially into entropy. This can be done to a far extend rigorously and analytically. The deep and essential difference between ``extensive'' and ``intensive'' control parameters, i.e. microcanonical and canonical statistics, is exemplified by rotating, self-gravitating systems. In this paper the necessary appearance of a convex entropy $S(E)$ and the negative heat capacity at phase separation in small as well macroscopic systems independently of the range of the force is pointed out. The appearance of a critical end-point for the liquid-gas transition in the $p-E$ or $V-E$ phase diagram can be easily explained as well the non-existence of a critical end-point of the solid-liquid transition.

## Key claim

The paper argues that first-order phase transitions and phase separations (such as boiling water) cannot be correctly described by canonical Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics, which requires homogeneous infinite systems; instead, they require microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck statistics, where a convex entropy S(E) and negative heat capacity appear as necessary and generic signals of phase separation, even in macroscopic systems and independently of the range of the force.

## Method

The author derives the microcanonical partition sum for a classical N-body system with a Hamiltonian containing kinetic energy and a pairwise interaction potential, separating it into an ideal-gas factor and a configuration factor. The configuration factor is analyzed to show how the accessible volume in 3N-dimensional configuration space changes with energy, producing jumps when the system can form droplets, evaporate particles, or fission into multiple clusters. These jumps create a convex intruder in the entropy S(E) and a negative heat capacity, which are demonstrated analytically and via microcanonical Monte Carlo simulations for sodium clusters of sizes N=200, 1000, and 3000 atoms.

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters at the liquid-gas transition: transition temperature T_tr = 940 K (N=200), 990 K (N=1000), 1095 K (N=3000), compared to bulk value 1156 K.
- Latent heat per atom q_lat = 0.82 eV (N=200), 0.91 eV (N=1000), 0.94 eV (N=3000), compared to bulk value 0.923 eV.
- Surface entropy per atom Delta s_surf = 0.55 (N=200), 0.56 (N=1000), 0.44 (N=3000).
- Surface tension over transition temperature sigma/T_tr = 2.75 (N=200), 5.68 (N=1000), 7.07 (N=3000), compared to bulk value 7.41.
- The depth of the convex intruder in entropy scales as Delta S_surf(E_min) proportional to N^{2/3}, going to infinity in the thermodynamic limit.

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical thermostatistics approach developed by Gross in earlier papers (Gross 2001, 2002, 2004) and contrasts with conventional chemical thermodynamics as presented by Guggenheim (1967), which treats phase separation by artificially splitting the system into homogeneous macroscopic phases. The paper resolves the Antonov paradox in astrophysics (Lynden-Bell 1968) by showing that negative heat capacity does not lead to gravo-thermal catastrophe when properly treated microcanonically. It enables the description of phase separations in small systems like fragmenting nuclei (experimentally observed by D'Agostino 2000), atomic clusters (Schmidt 2001), and rotating self-gravitating systems, where intensive control parameters like temperature or angular velocity are inappropriate.
