# The shell effects in the scission-point configuration of fissioning nuclei

> In the present work the formal definition of the scission point - the maximal elongation at which the nucleus splits into two fragments - is given. The shape and the deformation energy at the scission point are calculated within the macroscopic-microscopic model. Three minima in the scission point deformation energy are found corresponding to the "standard", "supershort" and "superlong" fission modes. The contribution of each fission mode to the mass distribution of the fission fragments and total kinetic energy is discussed and compared with the experimental results. On the example of the fission process of U-235 by thermal neutrons it is shown that the present approach reproduces correctly the position of the peaks of the mass distribution of the fission fragments, the value and the fine details of the total kinetic energy distribution and the magnitude of the total excitation energy of the fission fragments.

## Key claim

The scission point can be formally defined as the maximal elongation at which a mononuclear shape ceases to exist, and the deformation energy at this point shows three minima corresponding to "standard", "supershort", and "superlong" fission modes; however, the relative magnitude of the resulting mass-distribution peaks deviates substantially from experimental data, indicating that the quasi-static scission-point model with a Boltzmann distribution at T_coll = 1.5 MeV provides only qualitative agreement.

## Method

The shape of the fissioning nucleus at the scission point was calculated using the macroscopic-microscopic method, where the liquid-drop energy was minimized under constraints on volume, elongation (R12), mass asymmetry (δ), and quadrupole moments of the left and right fragments (Q2L, Q2R). The optimal shapes were obtained by solving the Euler-Lagrange equation for the profile function ρ(z), with a smoothing parameter Δz = 0.25 R0 to interpolate between small-deformation and separated-fragment definitions of elongation and asymmetry. The total deformation energy (liquid-drop plus shell correction) was then minimized with respect to fragment deformations for each mass asymmetry, and fission observables were computed assuming a Boltzmann distribution with collective temperature T_coll = 1.5 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The smoothing width Δz was chosen as 0.25 R0; results do not change much for 0.1 R0 ≲ Δz ≲ 0.5 R0.
- The collective temperature T_coll used in the Boltzmann distribution was 1.5 MeV.
- The experimental mass distribution for thermal-neutron fission of 235U shows three peaks: main peak at A_H = 141, a three-times-lower peak at A_H = 134, and a much smaller peak (~10⁻³ of the main peak) at symmetric splitting.
- The total kinetic energy shows a sharp drop of 10–15 MeV around symmetric fission.
- The radius parameter r0 used in the Coulomb energy was 1.225 fm, fixed by Pashkevich (1971) from fission barrier heights.
- The calculated total excitation energy deviates from experiment by 10–20%.

## Context

This work extends the optimal-shape approach of Strutinsky (1968) by incorporating mass asymmetry and separate fragment quadrupole deformations, building on earlier developments by Ivanyuk et al. (2008, 2012) and the author's previous work (2012). The scission-point model used here is similar to that of Brosa et al. (1990), who parametrized fission modes by Gaussians fitted to experiment, but the present work attempts to calculate the distributions from the potential energy surface rather than fitting them. The results enable future dynamical calculations using Langevin equations, which are currently too time-consuming for routine use.
