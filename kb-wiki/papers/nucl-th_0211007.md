# Meson exchange currents in electromagnetic one-nucleon emission

> The role of meson exchange currents (MEC) in electron- and photon-induced one-nucleon emission processes is studied in a nonrelativistic model including correlations and final state interactions. The nuclear current is the sum of a one-body and of a two-body part. The two-body current includes pion seagull, pion-in-flight and the isobar current contributions. Numerical results are presented for the exclusive 16O(e,e'p)15N and 16O(γ,p)15N reactions. MEC effects are in general rather small in (e,e'p), while in (γ,p) they are always large and important to obtain a consistent description of (e,e'p) and (γ,p) data, with the same spectroscopic factors. The calculated (γ,p) cross sections are sensitive to short-range correlations at high values of the recoil momentum, where MEC effects are larger and overwhelm the contribution of correlations.

## Key claim

Meson exchange currents (MEC) produce only small effects in exclusive (e,e'p) reactions, but are always large and essential in (γ,p) reactions to achieve a consistent description of both reaction channels using the same spectroscopic factors; the calculated (γ,p) cross sections are sensitive to short-range correlations at high recoil momentum, but MEC effects there overwhelm the correlation contribution.

## Method

The authors employ a nonrelativistic direct knockout model with final state interactions, where the nuclear current is the sum of a one-body part (convective and spin terms) and a two-body part that includes pion seagull, pion-in-flight, and Δ-isobar current contributions from one-pion-exchange diagrams. The transition matrix elements are computed using distorted waves for the outgoing nucleon, shell-model single-particle wave functions for bound states, and a Jastrow-type correlation function for the two-nucleon wave function when short-range correlations are included. Numerical calculations are performed for exclusive 16O(e,e'p)15N and 16O(γ,p)15N reactions.

## Key numbers

- For 16O(e,e'p)15N at E0 = 520 MeV and outgoing proton kinetic energy Tp = 90 MeV in parallel kinematics, a reduction factor of 0.8 is applied to theoretical reduced cross sections to reproduce experimental data; MEC effects are negligible, with the Δ-isobar current practically canceling the seagull contribution.
- For 16O(γ,p)15N at Eγ = 60 MeV, the seagull current gives a significant enhancement of the one-body result, but adding pion-in-flight reduces the cross section; the Δ current is not very important at this energy.
- For 16O(γ,p)15N at Eγ = 100 and 196 MeV, MEC effects are always large; at 196 MeV the Δ current dominates at small proton emission angles (up to ~70 degrees), while pion-in-flight dominates at larger angles where recoil momenta reach 700-800 MeV/c.
- Short-range correlations produce a strong enhancement of the one-body current contribution at 196 MeV for emission angles above 70 degrees, but this effect is overwhelmed by MEC in the final result.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of MEC in electromagnetic knockout reactions by Benenti et al. (1997) and Giusti et al. (2000), which included only the pion-seagull term with simplified spin treatment, by now including all one-pion-exchange diagrams (seagull, pion-in-flight, Δ-isobar) with full spin coupling and spin-orbit optical potential. The results confirm the validity of the Distorted Wave Impulse Approximation (DWIA) for (e,e'p) reactions as used in analyses from NIKHEF and Oxford, while showing that consistent description of (γ,p) data requires two-body currents. The findings differ quantitatively from earlier work by Boffi et al. (1993) and Sluys et al. (1995), but are similar in magnitude to results of Amaro et al. (1998). The model enables future consistent analyses of both electron- and photon-induced nucleon emission with the same spectroscopic factors, addressing the longstanding problem of spectroscopic factor discrepancies between independent-particle shell model predictions (~60-70% of predicted values) and experimental data.
