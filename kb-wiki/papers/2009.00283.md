# Statistical and Dynamical Model Studies of Nuclear Multifragmentation Reactions at Intermediate Energies

> Nuclear multifragmentation is an important phenomenon, the study of which can throw light on reaction mechanism in heavy ion collisions at intermediate and high energies. Based on statistical and dynamical model studies, this thesis is concentrated mainly on, the following three aspects of nuclear multifragmentation reactions namely (i) production of exotic nuclei which are normally not available in the laboratory (ii) nuclear symmetry energy from heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies and (iii) Nuclear liquid-gas phase transition. In addition to these equivalence of statistical ensembles under different conditions is also studied in the framework of multifragmentation.

## Key claim

This thesis investigates nuclear multifragmentation reactions at intermediate energies using both statistical and dynamical models, with the claim that these approaches can collectively describe the production of exotic nuclei, extract the nuclear symmetry energy, and characterize the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition, but the applicability of statistical models is limited to the freeze-out condition, while dynamical models are necessary to describe the full time evolution of the system.

## Method

The author employs the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) dynamical model, where each nucleon is represented by many point-like test particles whose time evolution is studied, incorporating nuclear mean field, Fermi momenta, nucleon-nucleon collisions, and Pauli blocking. For the statistical description, the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) is used, which calculates partition functions analytically using recursion relations without Monte Carlo sampling, assuming equilibrium at freeze-out. The Thomas-Fermi method is applied to compute ground state densities and Fermi momenta for finite nuclei, with initial positions and momenta of test particles assigned via Monte Carlo sampling from the density profile.

## Key numbers

- The multifragmentation regime is identified for excitation energies greater than approximately 3 to 10 MeV/nucleon, with central heavy ion collisions between 25 MeV/nucleon to 200 MeV/nucleon and peripheral collisions from 25 MeV/nucleon to 2 GeV/nucleon.
- For a system of mass A0=168 and charge Z0=75 (representing 112Sn + 112Sn central collisions after pre-equilibrium emission), at T=3 MeV fission is the dominant channel with total fragment multiplicity about 2; at T=5 MeV multifragmentation dominates; at T=7 MeV the system breaks into a larger number of smaller mass fragments.
- The Thomas-Fermi method uses a guess density with parameters ρM=1.18A^(1/3) fm and a=1/√2 fm for the equivalent sharp radius and surface width.
- In the collision algorithm, elastic scattering is checked against σ^e_nn→nn(√s)/55; inelastic channels are considered for √s ≤ 2.015 GeV for Δ production, and Δ absorption is checked via n+Δ→n+n.

## Context

This work extends the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) of Bondorf et al. and the BUU model of Bertsch and Das Gupta (1988) by applying both frameworks to the same physical problems, exotic nuclei production, symmetry energy extraction, and phase transition, within a single thesis. It enables future comparisons between the equilibrium-based CTM predictions and dynamical BUU simulations for the same reaction systems, and provides a foundation for interpreting upcoming multifragmentation experiments at the Variable Energy Cyclotron Centre in Kolkata. The study also contrasts with percolation models (Campi, Bauer) and lattice gas models (Pan) by using a transport-theory approach with explicit mean-field and collision dynamics.
