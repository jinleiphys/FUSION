# Coupled-cluster computations of atomic nuclei

> In the past decade, coupled-cluster theory has seen a renaissance in nuclear physics, with computations of neutron-rich and medium-mass nuclei. The method is efficient for nuclei with product-state references, and it describes many aspects of weakly bound and unbound nuclei. This report reviews the technical and conceptual developments of this method in nuclear physics, and the results of coupled-cluster calculations for nucleonic matter, and for exotic isotopes of helium, oxygen, calcium, and some of their neighbors.

## Key claim

Coupled-cluster theory is an efficient and accurate first-principles method for computing properties of neutron-rich and medium-mass nuclei, particularly those with product-state references such as doubly magic or closed-subshell nuclei, but its accuracy depends on sufficient shell gaps and the inclusion of three-nucleon forces, and it is not yet competitive with phenomenological approaches for reproducing experimental data across the full nuclear chart.

## Method

The coupled-cluster method uses an exponential ansatz for the wave function, starting from a product-state reference (typically a Hartree-Fock state) and including particle-hole excitations via cluster operators, most commonly truncated at singles and doubles (CCSD) with non-iterative triples corrections (CCSD(T) or Lambda-CCSD(T)). The method employs similarity-transformed Hamiltonians and operators, and for weakly bound or unbound nuclei, a Gamow-Hartree-Fock basis in the complex-energy plane (Berggren basis) is used to treat bound, resonant, and scattering states on equal footing. Calculations are performed with soft interactions obtained via renormalization group transformations, and three-nucleon forces are included either explicitly or as in-medium corrections to two-nucleon forces.

## Key numbers

- For quantum dots with 6, 12, and 20 electrons at oscillator frequencies omega = 0.28, 0.5, and 1.0 atomic units, CCSD recovers 90-97% of the correlation energy, while Lambda-CCSD(T) recovers 99-100% (Table in Section on quantum dots).
- For neutron matter with the chiral NNLO_opt interaction and three-nucleon forces, coupled-cluster CCD results are close to second-order many-body perturbation theory, with triples corrections small; three-nucleon forces act repulsively (Section on neutron matter and nuclear matter).
- For calcium isotopes, the first excited 2+ state in 52Ca is at about 2.5 MeV excitation energy, compared to 3.8 MeV in 48Ca; for 54Ca, coupled-cluster calculations yield E(2+) approximately 2 MeV, suggesting a soft subshell closure (Section on neutron-rich isotopes of calcium).
- The coupled-cluster prediction for the neutron separation energy of 52Ca is S_n approximately 6.6 MeV, close to the measured value of about 6 MeV (Section on neutron-rich isotopes of calcium).
- For 61Ca, the ground state is very close to threshold with spin and parity 1/2+, dominated by s-waves; the 5/2+ and 9/2+ states are resonances at approximately 1 MeV and 2 MeV above threshold (Section on neutron-rich isotopes of calcium).
- For helium isotopes 3-10He, coupled-cluster calculations with a low-momentum NN interaction (cutoff lambda = 1.9 fm^-1) and a Gamow-Hartree-Fock basis yield semi-quantitative agreement with data for particle decay widths (Section on helium isotopes).

## Context

This review extends the earlier reviews of coupled-cluster theory in nuclear physics by Kuemmel (1978) and Bishop (1991), covering developments since then including applications to neutron-rich oxygen and calcium isotopes. The method complements other first-principles approaches such as Green's function Monte Carlo (Carlson 1987, Pudliner 1997, Pieper 2001), no-core shell model (Navratil 2000, 2009; Barrett 2013), self-consistent Green's function method (Dickhoff 2004, Barbieri 2009), lattice simulations (Lee 2009), and the in-medium similarity renormalization group (Tsukiyama 2011, Hergert 2013). The results enable predictions for exotic nuclei near the drip line, such as the possibility of Efimov physics in 62Ca (Hagen 2013), and provide input for Halo Effective Field Theory. Open questions include the need to include three-nucleon forces consistently, the treatment of continuum effects for nuclei near the drip line, and the challenge of computing symmetric nuclear matter where the method is less perturbative than for neutron matter.
