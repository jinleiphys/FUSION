# Counting the number of correlated pairs in a nucleus

> We suggest that the number of correlated nucleon pairs in an arbitrary nucleus can be estimated by counting the number of proton-neutron, proton-proton, and neutron-neutron pairs residing in a relative $S$ state. We present numerical calculations of those amounts for the nuclei $^{4}$He, $^{9}$Be, $ ^{12}$C, $ ^{27}$Al, $ ^{40}$Ca, $ ^{48}$Ca, $ ^{56}$Fe, $ ^{63}$Cu, $ ^{108}$Ag, and $ ^{197}$Au. The results are used to predict the values of the ratios of the per-nucleon electron-nucleus inelastic scattering cross section to the deuteron in the kinematic regime where correlations dominate.

## Key claim

The number of correlated nucleon pairs in a nucleus can be estimated by counting the number of proton-neutron, proton-proton, and neutron-neutron pairs that reside in a relative $S$ state, with the caveat that only $pn$ pairs in the $^{3}S_{1}$ (T=0) state with principal quantum number $n=0$ are assumed to contribute to the measured $a_{2}(A/D)$ scaling coefficients.

## Method

The authors computed the weights of relative orbital angular momentum quantum numbers for all possible two-nucleon pairs in a harmonic-oscillator (HO) basis by performing a coordinate transformation from single-particle coordinates to relative and center-of-mass coordinates. The Slater determinant was constructed by filling shell-model single-particle states up to the Fermi level for each nucleus. The number of pairs in relative $S$ states was then extracted for ten nuclei ranging from $^{4}$He to $^{197}$Au, and these counts were used to predict $a_{2}(A/D)$ values via a scaling relation that assumes the elementary cross section for electron scattering from a correlated $pn$ pair equals that for the deuteron.

## Key numbers

- The number of pairs prone to correlation effects follows a power law $\sim A^{1.44 \pm 0.01}$, with $d=0.39 \pm 0.02$ for T=0 $pn$ pairs and $d=0.13 \pm 0.01$ for T=1 $pn$ pairs.
- For $^{12}$C, about 50% of the $pn$ pairs have $l=0$; for $^{197}$Au, this fraction drops to about 10%.
- The predicted $a_{2}(A/D)$ values from the model are: $^{4}$He = 2.4, $^{9}$Be = 2.8, $^{12}$C = 3.3, $^{27}$Al = 4.4, $^{40}$Ca = 5.2, $^{48}$Ca = 5.2, $^{56}$Fe = 5.4, $^{63}$Cu = 5.6, $^{108}$Ag = 6.3, $^{197}$Au = 7.0.
- For $^{197}$Au, the naive $\frac{N(N-1)}{Z(Z-1)}$ combinatorics predicts a $nn$ to $pp$ ratio of 2.24, but the computed $S$-state count gives 1.77.
- For $A>4$, about 25% of correlated pairs are excluded from the experimentally scanned phase space due to center-of-mass motion of the pair.

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Weinstein (2010) that the magnitude of the EMC effect can be predicted from measured $a_{2}(A/D)$ coefficients, which are interpreted as a measure of short-range correlations (SRC) relative to the deuteron. The authors provide a microscopic method to compute the number of SRC-prone pairs, in contrast to earlier phenomenological extractions. The results enable predictions for $a_{2}$ coefficients for nuclei where data are sparse, and suggest that the $a_{2}$ coefficient saturates for heavy nuclei, motivating further experimental measurements at large $A$ to clarify the trend.
