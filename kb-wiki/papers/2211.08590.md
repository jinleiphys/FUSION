# Dark Matter or Regular Matter in Neutron Stars? How to tell the difference from the coalescence of compact objects

> The mirror twin Higgs model is a candidate for (strongly-interacting) complex dark matter, which mirrors SM interactions with heavier quark masses. A consequence of this model are mirror neutron stars -- exotic stars made entirely of mirror matter, which are significantly smaller than neutron stars and electromagnetically dark. This makes mergers of two mirror neutron stars detectable and distinguishable in gravitational wave observations, but can we observationally distinguish between regular neutron stars and those that may contain some mirror matter? This is the question we study in this paper, focusing on two possible realizations of mirror matter coupled to standard model matter within a compact object: (i) mirror matter captured by a neutron star and (ii) mirror neutron star-neutron star coalescences. Regarding (i), we find that (non-rotating) mirror-matter-admixed neutron stars no longer have a single mass-radius sequence, but rather exist in a two-dimensional mass-radius plane. Regarding (ii), we find that binary systems with mirror neutron stars would span a much wider range of chirp masses and completely different binary Love relations, allowing merger remnants to be very light black holes. The implications of this are that gravitational wave observations with advanced LIGO and Virgo, and X-ray observations with NICER, could detect or constrain the existence of mirror matter through searches with wider model and parameter priors.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether gravitational wave and X-ray observations can distinguish between regular neutron stars, mirror neutron stars (MNSs), and mirror-matter admixed neutron stars (MANSs) within the mirror twin Higgs model, finding that MANSs occupy a two-dimensional mass-radius plane rather than a one-dimensional curve, and that binary systems involving mirror neutron stars produce distinct chirp mass ranges and binary Love relations that are unreachable by standard neutron star binaries.

## Method

The authors construct two-fluid solutions to the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations describing non-rotating stars in general relativity, using a relativistic mean-field nuclear model for standard model matter and scaling the same model by the ratio f/v (between 3 and 7) for mirror matter, with scalings extracted from lattice QCD and chiral perturbation theory. They compute stellar structure, stability criteria via the determinant of the baryon number variation matrix, and tidal deformabilities by solving the first-order Einstein field equations for tidal perturbations in two-fluid systems. The inspiral analysis uses post-Newtonian theory to compute chirp masses and symmetric mass ratios, while merger remnants are estimated from mass conservation and stability conditions.

## Key numbers

- MNS masses range from approximately 0.5 to 1 solar masses with radii between 4 and 8 km
- The symmetric mass ratio for NS-MNS binaries can be as low as 0.16, corresponding to a mass ratio of 0.3
- The chirp mass for NS-MNS binaries can be as low as 0.5 solar masses, corresponding to a total mass of 1 solar mass
- Merger remnants of NS-MNS coalescence produce black holes with masses between approximately 1.3 and 3.7 solar masses
- Merger remnants of MNS binaries can produce black holes as small as 0.8 solar masses
- Stable MANS remnants from NS-MNS mergers have masses between approximately 1.2 and 1.6 solar masses and radii between 8 and 9.5 km
- The admixed mirror matter core in a stable MANS remnant can have masses up to approximately 0.4 solar masses and radii as large as 4.5 km, making up about one third of the star's mass
- The tidal deformability plane for MANSs extends to a minimum Lambda of approximately 10 for objects with masses as low as about 0.75 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of mirror neutron star binaries (Hippert 2021) to include mixed NS-MNS binaries and admixed stars, using first-principle lattice QCD and chiral perturbation theory results to scale the equation of state with f/v, unlike prior studies such as Sandin (2008), Ciarcelluti (2010), and Kain (2021) that considered mirror matter identical to standard model particles. The results enable gravitational wave searches with LIGO and Virgo and X-ray observations with NICER to detect or constrain mirror matter by using wider model and parameter priors, and motivate future development of two-fluid numerical relativity simulations for the merger stage.
