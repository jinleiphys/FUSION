# SMASH: Results from hadronic transport for heavy-ion collisions at high densities

> This mini-review summarizes the general setup and some highlight results from the hadronic transport approach SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons). We start by laying out the software development structures as well as the particle properties and how they are determined by elementary collisions. The different ways to produce light clusters in SMASH, either by coalescence or dynamic multi-particle reactions, are explained. The constraints on nuclear mean fields and the corresponding equation of state from collective flow observables are discussed. In addition, we show how fluctuations associated with a potential critical endpoint survive through the hadronic rescattering stage. Besides hadronic observables, electromagnetic probes offer nice possibilities to study the properties of matter. We present results on collisional broadening of resonances and elliptic flow of dileptons. Last but not least, we review how SMASH can be employed as part of a hybrid approach including a Bayesian analysis for transport coefficients as a function of temperature and chemical potential. We end with an outlook how the hybrid approach has been recently extended to lower collision energies by dynamical fluidization initial conditions.

## Key claim

This mini-review summarizes the general setup and highlight results from the hadronic transport approach SMASH, covering recent developments including light cluster production, nuclear mean fields, critical fluctuations, electromagnetic probes, and hybrid approaches; the authors present these as ongoing work with the caveat that many results depend on specific model choices (e.g., momentum-dependent potentials, coalescence versus dynamic production) and that the approach is still under active development for the intermediate energy range relevant to the future CBM program at FAIR.

## Method

The SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) transport approach uses vacuum degrees of freedom with over 150 hadronic species, treating inelastic reactions via resonance excitation and decay at low energies and a string model at high energies, with cross-sections following experimental data or extrapolated using the additive quark model. Light nuclei are produced either by coalescence (identifying clusters based on distance in coordinate and momentum space after backward propagation) or dynamically via multi-particle interactions using stochastic rates with testparticles in cells. For higher beam energies, SMASH is coupled to the 3+1 dimensional viscous hydrodynamics code vHLLE in a hybrid approach, with initial conditions from SMASH, hydrodynamic evolution, and subsequent hadronic rescattering after Cooper-Frye sampling.

## Key numbers

- Over 150 hadronic species are included in SMASH, covering the corresponding multiplets and antiparticles
- The codebase is covered by about 100 unit tests to ensure proper running when interfaces change
- For AuAu collisions at \(E_{\rm lab}=0.4A\) GeV, the soft momentum dependent potential leads to the best agreement with FOPI directed flow data
- For AuAu collisions at \(E_{\rm lab}=1.23A\) GeV, the Bayesian analysis considered directed and elliptic flow for protons and deuterons in the intermediate transverse momentum range \(1.0 < p_\perp < 1.5\) GeV
- Dilepton invariant mass spectra in Ar+KCl collisions at \(E_{\rm lab} = 1.76A\) GeV require medium-modified spectral functions for best description of HADES data
- Ag+Ag collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.55\) GeV show that \(\Delta\to N e^+e^-\) and \(\eta\to \gamma e^+e^-\) decays have opposing contributions to the total dilepton elliptic flow
- The effective width of the \(\rho\) meson shows a quasi-universal scaling with local hadron density across different collision systems studied by HADES

## Context

This work extends the hadronic transport approach SMASH, which builds on prior transport models such as JAM, PHSD, and UrQMD (Nara 1999, Cassing 2009, Bass 1998, Bleicher 1999), and is part of the broader effort to constrain the nuclear equation of state from heavy-ion collisions including systematic uncertainty evaluations (TMEP 2022, LeFevre 2015). The results enable future comparisons with the RHIC Beam Energy Scan data from STAR (2025) and will inform the upcoming CBM program at FAIR, particularly through the dynamical hybrid approach that extends to lower collision energies by fluidizing SMASH hadrons based on local energy density, similar to prior work by Akamatsu (2018).
