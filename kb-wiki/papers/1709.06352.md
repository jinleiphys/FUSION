# Dissipative Properties and Isothermal Compressibility of Hot and Dense Hadron Gas using Non-extensive Statistics

> We evaluate the transport properties such as shear viscosity ($η$), bulk viscosity ($ζ$) and their ratios over entropy density ($s$) for hadronic matter using relativistic non-extensive Boltzmann transport equation (NBTE) in relaxation time approximation (RTA). In NBTE, we argue that the system far from equilibrium may not reach to an equilibrium described by extensive (Boltzmann-Gibbs (BG)) statistics but to a $q$-equilibrium defined by Tsallis non-extensive statistics after subsequent evolution, where $q$ denotes the degree of non-extensivity. We observe that $η/s$ and $ζ/s$ decrease rapidly with temperature ($T$) for various $q$-values. As $q$ increases, the magnitudes of $η/s$ and $ζ/s$ decrease with $T$. We also show the upper mass cutoff dependence of these ratios for a particular $q$ and find that they decrease with the increase in mass cutoff of hadrons. Further, we present the first estimation of isothermal compressibility ($κ_T$) using non-extensive Tsallis statistics at finite baryon chemical potential ($μ_B$). It is observed that, $κ_T$ changes significantly with the degree of non-extensivity. We also study the squared speed of sound ($c_{s}^{2}$) as a function of temperature at finite baryon chemical potential for various $q$ and upper mass cutoffs. It is noticed that there is a strong impact of $q$ and mass cutoff on the behaviour of $c_{s}^{2}$.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a hadronic system far from equilibrium relaxes not to a Boltzmann-Gibbs equilibrium but to a "q-equilibrium" described by Tsallis non-extensive statistics, and that the degree of non-extensivity (parameter q) significantly affects transport coefficients and thermodynamic properties, with the caveat that the KSS bound on η/s can be violated at higher temperatures for q > 1.

## Method

The authors solve the relativistic non-extensive Boltzmann transport equation (NBTE) in the relaxation time approximation (RTA), using the thermodynamically consistent Tsallis distribution as the equilibrium distribution function. They calculate shear viscosity (η) and bulk viscosity (ζ) for a multi-component hadron resonance gas with a constant hadronic cross-section of 11.3 mb, considering all hadrons and resonances up to a mass cutoff of 2.0 GeV. Isothermal compressibility (κ_T) and squared speed of sound (c_s^2) are derived from thermodynamic relations using Tsallis statistics at finite baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- η/s decreases with temperature and goes below the KSS bound (1/4π in natural units) at higher T for q > 1; the temperature at which this occurs decreases with increasing q.
- For q = 1.07, η/s decreases as the upper mass cutoff of hadrons increases, due to contributions of heavier resonances to entropy density.
- ζ/s decreases rapidly with temperature for all q-values, with lower values for higher q; the temperature at which ζ/s changes rapidly is lower for higher q.
- κ_T at μ_B = 0 decreases with increasing q; at μ_B = 0.5 GeV, κ_T shows similar behavior but with smaller values compared to μ_B = 0.
- For q = 1.07 at μ_B = 0.5 GeV, κ_T decreases as the upper mass cutoff increases, with the effect most pronounced up to a mass cutoff of 1 GeV.
- c_s^2 at μ_B = 0.5 GeV initially increases with temperature, reaches a maximum, then decreases; the criticality in c_s^2 (q-dependent peak) disappears after q = 1.13.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Gavin (1985) and the extensive-statistics BTE calculations of Ref. [ref1] (which used excluded-volume corrections with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics) by incorporating Tsallis non-extensive statistics. It provides the first estimation of isothermal compressibility for hadronic matter using non-extensive statistics, building on the work of Mukherjee (2017) who estimated κ_T in extensive HRG models. The results enable studies of dissipative properties in high-multiplicity p+p collisions at the LHC, where the q-value approaches 1 with increasing multiplicity as shown by Khuntia (2017), and are relevant for understanding the equation of state and critical point location in QCD phase transitions.
