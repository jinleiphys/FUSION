# Adiabatic Quantum Computation with the Fermionic Position Space Schrödinger Equation

> The efficient encoding of the fermionic Schrödinger equation as a spin system Hamiltonian is a long-term problem. I describe an encoding for the fermionic position space Schrödinger equation on a finite-volume periodic lattice with a local potential. The challenging part of the construction is the implementation of the kinetic energy operator, which is essentially the Laplacian. The finite difference implementation on the lattice combines contributions from neighboring lattice sites, which is complicated by fermionic exchange symmetry. Two independently useful techniques developed here are operator filtering and entanglement gadgets. Operator filtering is useful when a simple operator acting on a subspace of the full Hilbert space has a desired set of interactions. Occupation suppression of the complement of the subspace then filters away unwanted contributions of the operator. Entanglement gadgets encode the same information differently in two sets of qubits. We may then independently choose the most efficient encoding for operators acting on the qubits. The construction for the Laplacian described here has $\mathcal{O}\left(An 2^D\right)$ cost in bounded Pauli weight terms where $A$ is the number of identical spinless fermions, $N=2^n$ is the number of lattice points in each direction, and $D$ is the number of dimensions. The finite volume context protects the gap between the ground state and the first excited state, yielding polynomial time complexity with the box size.

## Key claim

The paper presents an encoding of the fermionic position space Schrödinger equation on a finite-volume periodic lattice that achieves polynomial scaling in the log of the number of lattice points and linear scaling in the number of particles, but the construction requires that the finite volume protects the gap between ground and first excited state to yield polynomial time complexity with box size.

## Method

The author encodes the kinetic energy operator (Laplacian) for identical spinless fermions on a lattice using a combination of binary reflected Gray code (BRGC) or Hamming distance 2 Gray code (H2GC) for position encoding, then builds the fermionic Laplacian on top of a distinguishable-particle Laplacian. Two new techniques are introduced: operator filtering, which suppresses unwanted contributions of an operator by penalizing occupation of the complement of a desired subspace, and entanglement gadgets, which encode the same information in two sets of qubits to independently choose the most efficient encoding for different operators. The fermionic Laplacian is constructed by applying the distinguishable-particle Laplacian together with swap operators and rotation operators to handle fermionic exchange symmetry and periodic boundary conditions.

## Key numbers

* The Laplacian construction has cost O(A n 2^D) in bounded Pauli weight terms, where A is the number of identical spinless fermions, N = 2^n is the number of lattice points in each direction, and D is the number of dimensions.
* For the BRGC encoding in one dimension, the Laplacian cost is O(A n) in bounded Pauli-weight operators.
* For the H2GC encoding, the overall cost is O(A n^2 D) in bounded-weight Pauli operators, set by the penalty implementation for excluded basis states.
* The number of basis states for A spinless fermions in D dimensions with n qubits per axis is given by the combination C(2^(n D), A).
* The number of ancillary qubits required for swap and rotation operators is O(A n).

## Context

This work extends the encoding of the finite-volume periodic position space Schrödinger equation for distinguishable particles presented in PRXQuantum (2022) to identical fermions, building on the BRGC and H2GC Laplacian implementations from that work. The paper also references the Bravyi-Kitaev transform (Bravyi, 2002) and Jordan-Wigner transform (Jordan and Wigner, 1928) as alternative fermion-to-spin encodings, noting their overhead for fixed particle number. The construction enables adiabatic quantum computation of fermionic systems with exponential advantage over classical computing, with the finite volume protecting the spectral gap as argued by Aharonov et al. (2008).
