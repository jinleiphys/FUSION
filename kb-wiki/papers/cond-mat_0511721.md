# Vanishing bulk viscosities and conformal invariance of unitary Fermi gas

> By requiring general-coordinate and conformal invariance of the hydrodynamic equations, we show that the unitary Fermi gas has zero bulk viscosity, zeta=0, in the normal phase. In the superfluid phase, two of the bulks viscosities have to vanish, zeta_1=zeta_2=0, while the third one zeta_3 is allowed to be nonzero.

# Vanishing bulk viscosities and conformal invariance of unitary Fermi gas

## Key claim
The unitary Fermi gas has exactly zero bulk viscosity in the normal phase, and two of the three bulk viscosities vanish in the superfluid phase, as a consequence of conformal invariance of the hydrodynamic equations, not merely from scale invariance, which alone would only constrain the scaling form of the viscosities but not force them to zero.

## Method
The author constructs a microscopic Lagrangian for fermions interacting via a Yukawa potential tuned to infinite scattering length, then places the system in a curved 3D spatial metric and background gauge field to expose its symmetries. By requiring that the hydrodynamic equations (continuity, momentum conservation, entropy production) inherit the general-coordinate and conformal invariance of the microscopic theory, the dissipative terms in the stress tensor and entropy production function are shown to produce unwanted terms proportional to the second time derivative of the conformal parameter unless the bulk viscosity coefficients vanish.

## Key numbers
- Normal phase: bulk viscosity ζ = 0 exactly at unitarity (infinite scattering length).
- Superfluid phase: ζ₁ = ζ₂ = 0 exactly; ζ₃ is allowed to be nonzero.
- The result holds for all ratios of temperature T to chemical potential μ, including regimes where the Boltzmann equation is not reliable (T ∼ μ).

## Context
This work extends the use of scale invariance in the unitary Fermi gas, previously applied to thermodynamic observables by Ho (2004), to kinetic coefficients. It contradicts the generic expectation from the Boltzmann equation with three-body collisions, which yields nonzero bulk viscosity (Landau and Lifshitz, 1981). The result enables interpretation of the observed dip in damping rates of radial breathing modes near Feshbach resonances (Thomas, 2004; Grimm, 2004) as partially due to vanishing bulk viscosities, and suggests that two-fluid dissipative hydrodynamics could extract kinetic coefficients from experimental data.
