# Generalized seniority from random Hamiltonians

> We investigate the generic pairing properties of shell-model many-body Hamiltonians drawn from ensembles of random two-body matrix elements. Many features of pairing that are commonly attributed to the interaction are in fact seen in a large part of the ensemble space. Not only do the spectra show evidence of pairing with favored J=0 ground states and an energy gap, but the relationship between ground state wave functions of neighboring nuclei show signatures of pairing as well. Matrix elements of pair creation/annihilation operators between ground states tend to be strongly enhanced. Furthermore, the same or similar pair operators connect several ground states along an isotopic chain. This algebraic structure is reminiscent of the generalized seniority model. Thus pairing may be encoded to a certain extent in the Fock space connectivity of the interacting shell model even without specific features of the interaction required.

## Key claim

Pairing features commonly attributed to specific attractive interactions in nuclear shell models, such as J=0 ground states, energy gaps, enhanced pair-transfer amplitudes, and generalized seniority structure, arise generically from a large class of random two-body Hamiltonians and may be encoded in Fock space connectivity rather than requiring specific interaction details.

## Method

The authors performed shell-model calculations in the sd space (j=1/2, 3/2, 5/2; up to 12 identical particles) and pf space (including j=7/2; up to 20 identical particles) using ensembles of random two-body matrix elements with Gaussian distributions. They defined four ensembles: RQE (Random Quasiparticle Interaction, with variance proportional to (2J+1)^{-1}), TBRE (Two-Body Random Ensemble, constant variance), RQE-NP (RQE with all J=0 matrix elements set to zero), and RQE-SPE (RQE with realistic single-particle energies). For each ensemble they computed at least 1000 samples for systems of 4, 6, and 8 neutrons in sd space (labeled 20O, 22O, 24O) and 4, 6, 8, and 10 neutrons in pf space (labeled 44Ca, 46Ca, 48Ca, 50Ca), plus systems with protons and neutrons (24,26,28Mg).

- Fraction of RQE-NP ensemble (J=0 matrix elements removed) yielding J=0 ground states: 50% for 20O, 68% for 22O, 51% for 24O; 46% for 44Ca, 59% for 46Ca, 53% for 48Ca, 45% for 50Ca (Table I)
- Average scaled gap between J=0 ground state and first excited state for RQE: 2.7 for 20O, 3.2 for 22O, 2.9 for 24O; 3.1 for 44Ca, 3.8 for 46Ca, 3.4 for 48Ca, 3.5 for 50Ca (Table II)
- Average pair-transfer fractional collectivity for RQE: 0.77 for 24O to 22O, 0.65 for 22O to 20O; 0.57 for 50Ca to 48Ca, 0.51 for 48Ca to 46Ca, 0.48 for 46Ca to 44Ca (Table III)
- Average pair-transfer fractional collectivity for RQE-NP: 0.75 for 24O to 22O, 0.59 for 22O to 20O; 0.42 for 50Ca to 48Ca, 0.34 for 48Ca to 46Ca, 0.28 for 46Ca to 44Ca (Table III)
- For realistic interactions, pair-transfer fractional collectivity: 0.99 for 24O to 22O, 0.86 for 22O to 20O; 0.98 for 50Ca to 48Ca, 0.86 for 48Ca to 46Ca, 0.94 for 46Ca to 44Ca (Table III)
- Correlation of pair-transfer amplitude vectors (cos theta) for O and Ca chains with RQE shows sharp peak at 1, at least a factor of 10 above average bin height; for Mg chain the peak is a factor of 4 above average bin height

## Context

This work extends the earlier finding by Johnson (1998) that spectral signatures of pairing (J=0 ground states and energy gaps) appear generically in random Hamiltonians, and also the observation by Bickers (1999) that time-reversal invariance is not necessary for these effects. The paper goes beyond spectral features to demonstrate wave-function signatures of pairing: enhanced pair-transfer amplitudes and persistence of the same pair operator along isotopic chains, reminiscent of the generalized seniority model of Talmi. The results contrast with rotational bands, which are not favored by random Hamiltonians, suggesting that random ensembles describe only Fermi liquid behavior while the T=0 part of the nuclear interaction (with strong quadrupole component) is needed for collective rotational spectra. This enables future work to explore whether adding simple physical components (like an attractive surface delta interaction) to random ensembles could produce the richer dynamical symmetries observed in nuclear spectroscopy.
