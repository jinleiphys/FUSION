# Inhomogeneous nucleation in quark hadron phase transition

> The effect of subcritical hadron bubbles on a first-order quark-hadron phase transition is studied. These subcritical hadron bubbles are created due to thermal fluctuations, and can introduce a finite amount of phase mixing (quark phase mixed with hadron phase) even at and above the critical temperature. For reasonable choices of surface tension and correlation length, as obtained from the lattice QCD calculations, we show that the amount of phase mixing at the critical temperature remains below the percolation threshold. Thus, as the system cools below the critical temperature, the transition proceeds through the nucleation of critical-size hadron bubbles from a metastable quark-gluon phase (QGP), within an inhomogeneous background populated by an equilibrium distribution of subcritical hadron bubbles. The inhomogeneity of the medium results in a substantial reduction of the nucleation barrier for critical bubbles. Using the corrected nucleation barrier, we estimate the amount of supercooling for different parameters controlling the phase transition, and briefly discuss its implications to cosmology and heavy-ion collisions.

# Inhomogeneous nucleation in quark hadron phase transition

## Key claim

The authors show that for a first-order quark-hadron phase transition with surface tension values between 2 and 10 MeV/fm² (as obtained from lattice QCD), the amount of phase mixing from subcritical hadron bubbles at the critical temperature remains below the percolation threshold of 30%, so the transition proceeds via nucleation of critical-size bubbles from an inhomogeneous background rather than through percolation. The inhomogeneous background substantially reduces the nucleation barrier, leading to less supercooling than predicted by homogeneous nucleation theory.

## Method

The authors model subcritical hadron bubbles as Gaussian fluctuations with amplitude and radius, using a quartic double-well effective potential parameterized by surface tension, correlation length, and critical temperature obtained from lattice QCD. They compute the equilibrium density distribution of subcritical bubbles by solving the Boltzmann equation with shrinking and nucleation terms, then calculate the extra free energy density from these fluctuations to obtain a corrected nucleation barrier. Using this corrected barrier, they solve the coupled equations for bubble nucleation, growth, and hydrodynamic expansion in 1+1 dimensions to determine the temperature evolution and supercooling.

## Key numbers

- Surface tension range from lattice QCD: 2 MeV/fm² to 10 MeV/fm², producing 15% to 30% phase mixing at T_C (below percolation threshold of 30%)
- Correlation length fixed at ξ_q = 0.5 fm at T = T_C
- Critical temperature taken as T_C = 160 MeV
- For σ = 50 MeV/fm²: homogeneous nucleation supercools to 0.945 T_C; inhomogeneous correction reduces supercooling to 0.95 T_C (about 10% reduction)
- For σ = 30 MeV/fm²: homogeneous nucleation supercools to 0.98 T_C; inhomogeneous correction reduces supercooling to 0.984 T_C (about 20% reduction)
- For σ = 10 MeV/fm²: supercooling reduced further to approximately 0.997 T_C
- Initial conditions: T_0 = 250 MeV, τ_0 = 1 fm/c, yielding τ_c = 3.8 fm/c for cooling to T_C
- Bubble growth velocity: v(T) = 3[1 - T/T_c]^(3/2)

## Context

This work extends the homogeneous nucleation theory of Langer (1967, 1973) and its application to quark-hadron phase transitions by Csernai and Kapusta (1992) and Ignatius et al. (1994), incorporating pre-transitional phenomena previously studied in condensed matter physics (isotropic to nematic transitions) and cosmological electroweak phase transitions by Gleiser and Kolb (1992). The formalism for subcritical bubbles follows the approach of Gleiser, Gelmini, and Kolb (1993) and Gleiser, Kolb, and Watkins (1991). The results disagree with Agarwal et al. (1994), who found large hadron fractions at temperatures up to twice T_C, likely due to different potential parameterization. The findings enable predictions for reduced baryon number fluctuations in cosmological nucleosynthesis and modified hadron multiplicity distributions in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
