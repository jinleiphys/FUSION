# Are strange stars distinguishable from neutron stars by their cooling behaviour?

> The general statement that strange stars cool more rapidly than neutron stars is investigated in greater detail. It is found that the direct Urca process could be forbidden not only in neutron stars but also in strange stars. If so, strange stars would be slowly cooling and their surface temperatures would be more or less indistinguishable from those of slowly cooling neutron stars. The case of enhanced cooling is reinvestigated as well. It is found that strange stars cool significantly more rapidly than neutron stars within the first $\sim 30$ years after birth. This feature could become particularly interesting if continued observation of SN 1987A would reveal the temperature of the possibly existing pulsar at its centre.

## Key claim

The paper qualifies the general statement that strange stars cool more rapidly than neutron stars, finding that if the direct Urca process is forbidden in both types of stars (due to low electron fraction), their surface temperatures become more or less indistinguishable. The enhanced cooling scenario only produces a significant difference within the first ~30 years after birth, which could be used to identify a strange star if a very young pulsar like the one possibly at the centre of SN 1987A is observed.

## Method

The authors used the MIT bag model with O(α_s) corrections to model strange quark matter, determining its equation of state and composition for parameter ranges where strange matter is absolutely stable. They simulated the thermal evolution of both strange stars and neutron stars using a numerical cooling code, considering two complementary parameter sets for strange matter (SM-1 with high electron fraction and SM-2 with zero electron fraction) and comparing standard and enhanced cooling scenarios with and without superfluid cores. The calculations were performed for a gravitational star mass of M = 1.4 M_⊙, and the results were compared with ROSAT and ASCA observations of five pulsars.

## Key numbers

- The direct Urca process in strange stars has emissivity ~10^24 × T_9^6 erg cm^−3 s^−1 (fast), while the modified Urca process has emissivity ~10^20 × T_9^8 erg cm^−3 s^−1 (slow), and bremsstrahlung has emissivity ~10^19 × T_9^8 erg cm^−3 s^−1 (slow)
- The quark direct Urca process is only possible if the electron fraction Y_e > 0; for SM-2 (Y_e = 0) both direct and modified Urca processes become unimportant
- Strange stars cool significantly more rapidly than neutron stars within the first ~30 years after birth
- The superfluid gap energy for strange matter was chosen as Δ = 0.4 MeV, comparable to the proton ^1S_0 gap in nuclear matter (0.2–1.0 MeV)
- The densest possible nuclear crust on a strange star is about 0.2 km thick
- The transition density from inner crust to core in neutron stars is ρ_tr = 1.7 × 10^14 g cm^−3
- Observed surface temperatures for five pulsars (PSRs 0002+62, 0833-45, 0656+14, 0630+18, 1055-52) range from log T_s^∞ ≈ 5.42 to 6.24 K depending on the atmosphere model used

## Context

This work extends earlier investigations by Alcock (1988), Pizzochero (1991), Page (1991), and Schaab (1995) that did not incorporate the standard cooling scenario now shown to be possible in both neutron star and strange star matter. The paper contradicts the common claim that strange stars always cool faster, showing that when the direct Urca process is forbidden in both types of stars, their cooling curves become indistinguishable. The results enable future identification of strange stars through observation of very young pulsars (within ~30 years of birth), particularly if continued observation of SN 1987A reveals the temperature of a possible pulsar at its centre.
