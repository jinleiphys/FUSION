# Nucleon internal degrees of freedom and the uniqueness of the Gamow-Teller state

> The Gamow-Teller strength in nuclei can be strongly affected by the internal degrees of freedom of the nucleon. It is demonstrated that this feature is unique to the Gamow-Teller. Excitation modes that involve spatial degrees of freedom are much less influenced by the internal excitation of nucleons. The fact that the observed Gamow-Teller strength is quenched by 30-40 percent in all nuclei suggests that indeed this is due to the nucleon excitation in nuclei.

## Key claim

The observed 30–40% quenching of Gamow-Teller (GT) strength in all nuclei is plausibly due to mixing with nucleon internal excitations (specifically the \(\Delta_{33}\) resonance), and this mechanism is unique to GT and M1 transitions because they lack a spatial coordinate; excitations that depend on the nucleon's spatial coordinate \(\bm{r}\) are much less affected.

## Method

The author constructs a theoretical framework in which a one-body operator \(\hat{O}\) is split into a part acting on nucleon degrees of freedom (\(\hat{O}_N\)) and a part acting on internal nucleon degrees of freedom (\(\hat{O}_q\)), with mixing between a coherent nucleon particle-hole state \(|\lambda\rangle\) and a coherent \(N^{-1}N^*\) state \(|\lambda^*\rangle\). A quenching factor \(\gamma\) is derived from the mixing amplitude and the transition matrix elements, and is estimated separately for operators that depend on the spatial coordinate \(\bm{r}\) (e.g., dipole, monopole) and for the spin-isospin-only GT operator. For the GT case, the mixing with the \(\Delta_{33}\) resonance is evaluated using a quark-model estimate for the transition strength and a coupling constant ratio \(G = 4/3\).

## Key numbers

- Observed GT strength quenching is 30–40% in all nuclei studied.
- For the dipole resonance in \(^{90}\text{Zr}\), the reduction factor \(f\) is about 1/2000 and the quenching factor \(\gamma\) is \(4 \times 10^{-3}\).
- For the dipole resonance in \(^{208}\text{Pb}\), the quenching factor \(\gamma\) is \(1.5 \times 10^{-3}\).
- For the GT strength in \(^{208}\text{Pb}\), the quenching factor \(\gamma = 0.53\).
- For the GT strength in \(^{90}\text{Zr}\), the quenching factor \(\gamma = 0.51\).
- The \(\Delta_{33}\) resonance mass is 1232 MeV, with excitation energy \(E_{\lambda^*} \simeq 300\) MeV above the nucleon mass.
- The matrix element squared for the nucleon-to-\(\Delta\) transition in a nucleus is \(\frac{32}{25}A\) (quark model estimate).
- The GT strength matrix element squared \(\langle GT | \hat{O}_N| 0 \rangle^2\) equals \(N-Z\).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Brown (1981), Auerbach (1983), and Zamick (1982), who proposed that internal nucleon degrees of freedom (especially the \(\Delta_{33}\) resonance) remove GT strength to high excitation energies (about 300 MeV). It contrasts with the alternative explanation of Bertsch (1982), Klein (1985), and Waka (1997), which attributes quenching to fragmentation of strength via 2p-2h mixing at tens of MeV above the main peak. The paper suggests that the unique absence of a spatial coordinate in the GT operator makes the internal-excitation mechanism dominant, and that this has implications for double Gamow-Teller transitions and double-beta decay calculations (Auerbach and Zamick, 1989; Engel, 2017; Iachello, 2017).
