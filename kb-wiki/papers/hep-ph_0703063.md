# Low-energy neutrinos at off-axis from a standard beta-beam

> We discuss a scenario to extract up to 150 MeV neutrinos at a standard beta-beam facility using one and two detectors off-axis. In particular we show that the high-energy component of the neutrino fluxes can be subtracted through a specific combination of the response of two off-axis detectors. A systematic analysis of the neutrino fluxes using different detector geometries is presented, as well as a comparison with the expected fluxes at a low-energy beta-beam facility. The presented option could offer an alternative way to perform low-energy neutrino experiments.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that low-energy neutrinos (up to 150 MeV) can be extracted from a standard beta-beam facility using one or two off-axis detectors, but the single-detector option yields fluxes 20-100 times weaker than a dedicated low-energy beta-beam, making it impractical unless higher ion intensities are achieved. The two-detector subtraction technique can increase intensity by about an order of magnitude, but introduces significant statistical errors.

## Method
The authors calculated neutrino flux profiles by integrating over the storage ring straight sections and detector volume using the standard formalism for beta-beam fluxes. They considered three scenarios: a reference low-energy beta-beam with 6He ions at gamma=7 and gamma=14; a single off-axis detector placed at a distance y from the straight section determined by a desired maximum neutrino energy E_cut; and two off-axis detectors where the response of one detector is subtracted from the other using a geometric scaling factor to remove the high-energy component. Fluxes were evaluated for ion boosts of gamma=60 and gamma=100 with energy cuts of 100 and 150 MeV, using both point detectors and finite-size cylindrical, spherical, and disc-shaped detectors of 954 m^3 volume.

## Key numbers
- Low-energy beta-beam (gamma=7): average energy 22.8 MeV, peak flux 24.5 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 5782 events/year for antineutrino-proton scattering in water
- Low-energy beta-beam (gamma=14): average energy 42.6 MeV, peak flux 28.4 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 42393 events/year
- Single off-axis detector (gamma=100, E_cut=100 MeV): average energy 32.7 MeV, peak flux 0.64 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 626 events/year, detector distance y=61.4 m
- Single off-axis detector (gamma=100, E_cut=150 MeV): average energy 49.6 MeV, peak flux 1.03 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 2998 events/year, y=48.0 m
- Single off-axis detector (gamma=60, E_cut=100 MeV): average energy 32.9 MeV, peak flux 0.70 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 693 events/year, y=74.7 m
- Single off-axis detector (gamma=60, E_cut=150 MeV): average energy 50.2 MeV, peak flux 1.20 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 3512 events/year, y=56.0 m
- Two-detector subtraction with d1 geometry (gamma=60, E_cut=100 MeV): average energy 28.7 MeV, peak flux 6.09 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 4013 subtracted events/year, statistical error 1334
- Two-detector subtraction with d1 geometry (gamma=60, E_cut=150 MeV): average energy 41.27 MeV, peak flux 7.63 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 13240 subtracted events/year, statistical error 1334
- Two-detector subtraction with d1 geometry (gamma=100, E_cut=100 MeV): average energy 21.3 MeV, peak flux 4.42 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 2487 subtracted events/year, statistical error 1949
- Two-detector subtraction with d1 geometry (gamma=100, E_cut=150 MeV): average energy 39.0 MeV, peak flux 5.44 x 10^9 m MeV^-1 s^-1, 8162 subtracted events/year, statistical error 1949

## Context
This work extends the concept of beta-beam facilities originally proposed by Zucchelli (2002) and the low-energy beta-beam idea first suggested by Volpe (2003). It provides an alternative to dedicated low-energy beta-beam facilities discussed by Serreau (2004), McLaughlin (2004), Balantekin (2005-2006), Bueno (2006), Barranco (2007), and Jachowicz (2005). The off-axis technique was first proposed for neutrino beams by the E889 collaboration. The paper is complementary to a contemporaneous study by Amanik (2007) on how neutrino spectral shapes change at low-energy beta-beams depending on detector geometry. The results enable future feasibility studies for low-energy neutrino experiments at standard beta-beam facilities, particularly if improved ion production methods (such as proposed by Rubbia in 2006) yield higher intensities.
