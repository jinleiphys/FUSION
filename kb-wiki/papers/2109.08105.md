# Density Functional Equation of State and Its Application to the Phenomenology of Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We present a mean-field model of the dense nuclear matter equation of state designed for use in computationally demanding hadronic transport simulations. Our approach, based on the relativistic Landau Fermi-liquid theory, allows us to construct a family of equations of state spanning a wide range of possible bulk properties of dense QCD matter. We implement the developed model in the hadronic transport code SMASH, and show that the resulting dynamic behavior reproduces theoretical expectations for the thermodynamic properties of the system based on the underlying equation of state. In particular, we show that pair distribution functions calculated from hadronic transport simulation data are consistent with theoretical expectations based on the second-order cumulant ratio, and can be used as a signature of crossing the phase diagram in the vicinity of a critical point. We additionally present a novel method that may enable a measurement of the speed of sound and its derivative with respect to the baryon number density in heavy-ion collisions. Application of our approach to available experimental data implies that the derivative of the speed of sound is non-monotonic in systems created in collisions at intermediate to low energies, which in turn may be connected to non-trivial features in the underlying equation of state.

## Key claim

A mean-field model of the dense nuclear matter equation of state, based on relativistic Landau Fermi-liquid theory, can be implemented in hadronic transport simulations to reproduce theoretical expectations for thermodynamic properties, and the derivative of the speed of sound extracted from experimental data using a novel method appears non-monotonic in systems created at intermediate to low collision energies, which may indicate non-trivial features in the underlying equation of state.

## Method

The author constructs a family of equations of state using relativistic Landau Fermi-liquid theory, then implements the model in the SMASH hadronic transport code. For simulations of heavy-ion collisions at intermediate beam energies, parametrizations are developed that describe dense nuclear matter with two phase transitions: the known nuclear liquid-gas phase transition and a postulated high-temperature, high-density phase transition meant to model the QCD transition from hadronic to quark and gluon degrees of freedom. The author validates the transport simulations by showing that pair distribution functions calculated from simulation data are consistent with theoretical expectations based on the second-order cumulant ratio, and presents a method connecting the speed of sound to cumulants of the net baryon number.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: n0 approximately 0.160 fm^{-3}
- Nuclear matter critical point identified at critical temperature Tc^{(N)} approximately 18 MeV and critical baryon number density nc^{(N)} approximately 0.4 n0
- Pseudocritical temperature of the crossover QGP-hadron transition at zero baryon chemical potential: Tpc = 156.6 +/- 1.5 MeV (from HotQCD collaboration, 2018)
- Central densities of neutron stars can reach about nB approximately 5-10 n0
- Low-temperature regime for ordinary nuclear matter: T less than or approximately 30 MeV, with baryon chemical potential muB approximately 800-1000 MeV or baryon densities nB approximately 0.1-1.5 n0

## Context

This work extends the use of mean-field equations of state in hadronic transport simulations, building on the SMASH code developed by Weil et al. (2016) and the UrQMD approach of Bass et al. (1998) and Bleicher et al. (1999). The method for extracting the speed of sound from baryon cumulants connects to the Beam Energy Scan Theory (BEST) collaboration's search for the QCD critical point, and the validation of pair distribution functions against the second-order cumulant ratio enables using hadronic transport codes to study phase transition signals in dense nuclear matter. The approach is designed for computationally demanding simulations relevant to the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program.
