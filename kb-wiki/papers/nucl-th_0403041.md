# Origin of chaos in the spherical nuclear shell model: role of symmetries

> To elucidate the mechanism by which chaos is generated in the shell model, we compare three random-matrix ensembles: the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble, French's two-body embedded ensemble, and the two-body random ensemble (TBRE) of the shell model. Of these, the last two take account of the two-body nature of the residual interaction, and only the last, of the existence of conserved quantum numbers like spin, isospin, and parity. While the number of independent random variables decreases drastically as we follow this sequence, the complexity of the (fixed) matrices which support the random variables, increases even more. In that sense we can say that in the TBRE, chaos is largely due to the existence of (an incomplete set of) symmetries.

## Key claim

Chaos in the spherical nuclear shell model is largely due to the existence of an incomplete set of symmetries (spin, isospin, parity) rather than solely to the two-body nature of the residual interaction; the matrices that transport the random two-body matrix elements into the many-body space become dense precisely because of these symmetries, compensating for the drastically reduced number of independent random variables compared to the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble.

## Method

The authors compare three random-matrix ensembles: the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE), French's two-body embedded ensemble (EGOE(2)), and the two-body random ensemble (TBRE) of the shell model. For the TBRE, they analyze the structural matrices \(C_{\mu\nu}^{J}(\alpha)\) that couple many-body states with fixed total spin \(J\) via the two-body matrix elements \(V_\alpha\), both for a single \(j=19/2\) shell with 6 fermions and for the \(sd\)-shell with 12 nucleons in the \(J=T=0\) sector. They compute the second moments of the Hamiltonian and the number of principal components (NPC) of the eigenstates of the individual operators \(X(\alpha)\) to quantify the mixing induced by symmetry alone.

## Key numbers

- For 6 fermions in a single \(j=19/2\) shell, the number of two-body matrix elements is \(a = j + 1/2 = 10\), while the number of many-body states with fixed total spin \(J\) grows as \(j^{m-3}\) (for \(j \gg m \gg 1\)).
- For the \(sd\)-shell with \(l=24\) single-particle states, \(m=12\) nucleons, and \(q=3\) subshells, the number of independent two-body matrix elements is \(a = 63\) (28 diagonal, 22 off-diagonal transferring one particle, 13 transferring two particles), while the Hamiltonian matrix in the \(J=T=0\) sector has dimension \(839\).
- In the single \(j=19/2\) shell with \(m=6\) fermions, the number of principal components normalized by the total dimension \(D=1242\) ranges from \(NPC/D = 0.13\) (for \(J=42\)) to \(0.27\) (for \(J=0,1,2,22\)), compared to the GOE expectation of \(1/3\).
- For the \(sd\)-shell, within the \((6,4,2)\) partition (dimension \(D=103\)), the average \(NPC/D = 0.10\); within the \((5,4,3)\) partition (\(D=56\)), \(NPC/D = 0.18\); within the \((4,4,4)\) partition (\(D=35\)), \(NPC/D = 0.20\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of chaos in nuclear shell-model calculations by Zelevinsky et al. (1996) and the embedded ensemble studies by Kota (2001) and by Brody et al. (1981). It contrasts the TBRE with the EGOE(2) to isolate the role of conserved quantum numbers (spin, isospin, parity) in generating dense Hamiltonian matrices from a small number of random two-body matrix elements. The findings enable a structural understanding of why chaos is generic in the spherical shell model without relying on specific choices of the two-body interaction, and they clarify the mechanism of "geometric chaoticity" previously noted by Zelevinsky et al. (1996).
