# Diagonalization of full finite temperature Green's function by quasi-particles

> For thermal systems, standard perturbation theory breaks down because of the absence of stable, observable asymptotic states. We show, how the introduction of {\it statistical} quasi-particles (stable, but not observable) gives rise to a consistent description. Statistical and spectral information can be cleanly separated also for interacting systems.

## Key claim
The paper shows that the thermal instability of observable states in interacting quantum field theories at finite temperature can be absorbed into a Bogoliubov transformation, defining stable but non-observable "statistical" quasi-particles that serve as a basis for perturbation theory. The key condition is that this approach sacrifices the observability aspect of asymptotic states rather than their stability.

## Method
The authors employ Thermo Field Dynamics (TFD) with a doubled Hilbert space and two disjoint representations of canonical commutation relations. They define a Bogoliubov transformation that maps physical particle operators to statistical quasi-particle operators that annihilate the thermal equilibrium state vectors, and then generalize this to interacting bosonic fields by decomposing the field into energy-momentum eigenmodes weighted by a spectral function. The full finite-temperature propagator is diagonalized by expressing it in terms of a time-dependent fluctuating particle number parameter that appears linearly in the Bogoliubov matrix.

## Key numbers
- The Bogoliubov matrix is parameterized as B = [[1+n, -n], [-1, 1]] where n = f/(1-f) is the number density of physical particles and f = exp(-beta(omega-mu)) is the statistical weight.
- The spectral weight function rho(E,k) satisfies the normalization integral over dE^2 of rho(E,k) = 1.
- The equal-time limit of the fluctuating particle number gives the time-independent equilibrium Heisenberg density N^H_k = (integral dE rho(E,k) n_{Ek}) / (integral dE rho(E,k)).

## Context
This work extends the Thermo Field Dynamics formalism of Arimitsu and Umezawa (1987) and the textbook treatment by Umezawa, and contrasts with the Schwinger-Keldysh Closed-Time-Path method where the matrix structure of the propagator remains obscure. The approach builds on the concept of statistical quasi-particles from Bogoliubov and Dzyub (1971) and on earlier work by Henning and Umezawa (1992). The diagonalization enables a clean separation of statistical information (boundary conditions, thermal excitations) from spectral information contained in the weight function, and can be extended to non-equilibrium systems to derive transport equations.
