# Study of nuclear pairing with Configuration-Space Monte-Carlo approach

> Pairing correlations in nuclei play a decisive role in determining nuclear drip-lines, binding energies, and many collective properties. In this work a new Configuration-Space Monte-Carlo (CSMC) method for treating nuclear pairing correlations is developed, implemented, and demonstrated. In CSMC the Hamiltonian matrix is stochastically generated in Krylov subspace, resulting in the Monte-Carlo version of Lanczos-like diagonalization. The advantages of this approach over other techniques are discussed; the absence of the fermionic sign problem, probabilistic interpretation of quantum-mechanical amplitudes, and ability to handle truly large-scale problems with defined precision and error control, are noteworthy merits of CSMC. The features of our CSMC approach are shown using models and realistic examples. Special attention is given to difficult limits: situations with non-constant pairing strengths, cases with nearly degenerate excited states, limits when pairing correlations in finite systems are weak, and problems when the relevant configuration space is large.

## Key claim

The Configuration-Space Monte-Carlo (CSMC) method avoids the fermionic sign problem and the Trotter-Suzuki decomposition used in prior imaginary-time approaches, but its practical effectiveness depends on the pairing interaction having all positive matrix elements and on the use of importance sampling to control the exponential growth of variance with path length.

## Method

The CSMC method generates a Krylov subspace by repeatedly applying the Hamiltonian operator (defined as \(V = -H\)) to an initial random state, with each application represented as a sum over all possible connected paths in the configuration space of paired nucleons. These path sums are evaluated via Markov-chain Monte-Carlo sampling using an ensemble of independent walkers, where each walker carries a "bag" amplitude that is updated recursively based on the matrix elements and the chosen step probabilities. The ground state energy is extracted either from the ratio of linear norms of successive Krylov vectors or from a full reconstruction of the wave function, with the linear-norm method requiring no wave-function storage.

## Key numbers

- For a half-occupied ladder model with \(\omega = 18\) levels, \(n = 9\) nucleon pairs, and constant pairing strength \(G = 1\) (in units of single-particle level spacing \(\epsilon\)), the linear-norm-based energy estimate converges to the exact ground state energy from below, while the BCS ground state energy lies above the exact value.
- The critical BCS pairing strength for the half-occupied ladder model is approximately \(G_{\text{cr}} = \epsilon / \ln(2\omega)\).
- The statistical error in the energy estimate scales as \(\Delta E_0(L) / E_0(L) \approx (1/\sqrt{N}) \, \text{Cv}\{B_{L+1}\}\), where \(N\) is the number of walkers and \(\text{Cv}\{B_{L+1}\}\) is the coefficient of variation of the bag amplitudes, which grows exponentially with path length \(L\).
- For the pairing Hamiltonian, each configuration has exactly \(n(\omega - n + 1)\) possible one-step transitions (including the diagonal move), making the number of connected configurations independent of the specific state.

## Context

This work extends the exact treatment of nuclear pairing correlations beyond the limitations of BCS theory (Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer, 1957) and its particle-number non-conservation issues, as well as beyond the Richardson exact solution (Richardson, 1964) and direct diagonalization methods (Whitehead, 1972; Volya, 2001) that become computationally impractical for large configuration spaces. The CSMC method improves upon the imaginary-time Monte-Carlo approach of Cerf and Martin (1993), which relied on Trotter-Suzuki decomposition and was mainly applied to constant pairing strengths; CSMC avoids the Trotter-Suzuki breakup entirely and can handle non-constant pairing strengths, nearly degenerate excited states, weak pairing limits, and large configuration spaces with controlled precision.
