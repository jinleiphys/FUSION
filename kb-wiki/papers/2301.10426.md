# Deep learning predicted elliptic flow of identified particles in heavy-ion collisions at the RHIC and LHC energies

> Recent developments on a deep learning feed-forward network for estimating elliptic flow ($v_2$) coefficients in heavy-ion collisions have shown us the prediction power of this technique. The success of the model is mainly the estimation of $v_2$ from final state particle kinematic information and learning the centrality and the transverse momentum ($p_{\rm T}$) dependence of $v_2$. The deep learning model is trained with Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02$ TeV minimum bias events simulated with a multiphase transport model (AMPT). We extend this work to estimate $v_2$ for light-flavor identified particles such as $π^{\pm}$, $\rm K^{\pm}$, and $\rm p+\bar{p}$ in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies. The number of constituent quark (NCQ) scaling is also shown. The evolution of $p_{\rm T}$-crossing point of $v_2(p_{\rm T})$, depicting a change in meson-baryon elliptic flow at intermediate-$p_{\rm T}$, is studied for various collision systems and energies. The model is further evaluated by training it for different $p_{\rm T}$ regions. These results are compared with the available experimental data wherever possible.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a deep neural network trained on minimum-bias Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV from the AMPT model can predict elliptic flow coefficients for identified light-flavor hadrons across different collision systems and energies, but the predictions deviate from AMPT values for transverse momenta above approximately 4-6 GeV/c due to limited training statistics in that region.

## Method

The authors used a deep feed-forward neural network with one input layer (3072 features), four hidden dense layers (128, 256, 256, and 256 nodes with ReLU activation), and one output layer with linear activation, trained on AMPT-simulated minimum-bias Pb-Pb events at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV. The input features consisted of 32×32 binned \((\eta-\phi)\) distributions weighted by transverse momentum, particle mass, and \(\log\sqrt{(s_{\rm NN}/s_{0})}\) for charged tracks with \(|\eta|<0.8\) and \(p_{\rm T} \geq 0.15\) GeV/c. The model was then applied to predict \(v_2\) for \(\pi^{\pm}\), \(\rm K^{\pm}\), and \(\rm p+\bar{p}\) in Au-Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 200\) GeV, Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 2.76\) and 5.02 TeV, and Xe-Xe collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.44\) TeV for (0-10)%, (40-50)%, and (60-70)% centrality classes.

## Key numbers

* Mean absolute error (\(\Delta v_2\)) for the DNN model was smallest with no L2 regularization and no dropout, as shown for Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV.
* The DNN used 32×32 binning in \((\eta-\phi)\) space, giving 3072 features per event (32×32×3 for \(p_{\rm T}\), mass, and \(\log\sqrt{(s_{\rm NN}/s_{0})}\) weighting).
* The DNN predictions agreed with AMPT values up to \(p_{\rm T} \lesssim 4.0-6.0\) GeV/c; beyond this, mismatch occurred due to fewer than 100 counts per \(p_{\rm T}\)-bin for \(p_{\rm T} \gtrsim 6.0\) GeV/c.
* The \(v_2(p_{\rm T})\) maximum occurred around \(p_{\rm T} \approx 2.0\) GeV/c for all three particle species in (40-50)% central Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV.
* The NCQ scaling of \(v_2/n_q\) versus \(KE_{\rm T}/n_q\) showed scaling at low \(KE_{\rm T}/n_q\) for all particle types, with proton breaking the scaling at higher \(KE_{\rm T}/n_q\).

## Context

This work extends the deep learning approach for elliptic flow estimation introduced by Mallick (2022) from charged hadrons to identified light-flavor particles (\(\pi^{\pm}\), \(\rm K^{\pm}\), \(\rm p+\bar{p}\)) across multiple collision systems and energies. The results are compared with experimental data from ALICE (2018, 2014, 2021) and show that the DNN preserves the mass ordering at low \(p_{\rm T}\) and the baryon-meson separation at intermediate \(p_{\rm T}\) characteristic of quark coalescence, as seen in AMPT simulations. The study enables faster computation of flow coefficients compared to traditional multi-particle cumulant methods and demonstrates that a single DNN trained at one energy can generalize to other systems, though with reduced accuracy in extreme centrality bins due to statistical limitations.
