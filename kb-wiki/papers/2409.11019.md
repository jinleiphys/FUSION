# Iso-scalar liquid drop model

> New liquid drop model with the isospin-square dependence of the volume and surface energy terms is applied to reproduce experimentally known masses of nuclei with number of protons and neutrons larger or equal to twenty. The ground-state microscopic energy corrections are taken into account. In spite of the fact that the model contains only six adjustable parameters, the quality of mass reproduction is good, and it is comparable with other contemporary mass models. Also, the fission barrier heights of actinide nuclei evaluated using the topographical theorem of Myers and Swiatecki are close to the data.

# Iso-scalar liquid drop model

## Key claim

The new Iso-Scalar Liquid Drop (ISLD) model, with only six adjustable parameters and an isospin-square dependence in the volume and surface terms, reproduces experimental nuclear masses with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.517 MeV for 2259 measured nuclei, which is comparable to or better than more parameter-rich models like FRDM (0.536 MeV) and LSD (0.523 MeV). However, this good performance relies on using microscopic energy corrections taken from the FRDM tables of Möller et al. (2016), and the model's fission barrier heights estimated via the topographical theorem of Myers and Swiatecki systematically underestimate experimental values.

## Method

The authors fit six adjustable parameters (volume energy coefficient a_vol, volume isospin coefficient kappa_vol, surface energy coefficient a_sur, surface isospin coefficient kappa_sur, nuclear radius parameter r_0, and odd-even energy parameter D) of the ISLD formula to the experimental and estimated atomic masses from the 2021 mass table of Wang et al., for nuclei with Z,N >= 20. The macroscopic energy includes volume and surface terms with a T(T+1) isospin dependence (where T = |N-Z|/2), a Coulomb term, a Coulomb exchange term, and an odd-even energy term; microscopic ground-state energy corrections (shell, pairing, and deformation) are taken from the FRDM tables of Möller et al. (2016). Fission barrier heights for actinide nuclei are then estimated using the Myers and Swiatecki topographical theorem, which equates the barrier height to the difference between the macroscopic mass at the saddle point and the experimental ground-state mass, with saddle-point energies computed using the Fourier-over-Spheroid shape parametrization.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation of ISLD model (parameter set a) from 2259 experimental masses: **0.517 MeV** (compared to FRDM: 0.536 MeV, LSD: 0.523 MeV, Thomas-Fermi: 0.669 MeV)
- Root-mean-square deviation of ISLD model (parameter set b) from 3165 experimental plus estimated masses: **0.745 MeV** (compared to FRDM: 0.956 MeV, LSD: 0.817 MeV, Thomas-Fermi: 0.874 MeV)
- ISLD model has only **6 adjustable parameters** (compared to FRDM: 9+4, LSD: 8+4, Thomas-Fermi: 8+4)
- Volume energy coefficient a_vol = **-15.381912 MeV** (set a) or **-15.499610 MeV** (set b)
- Surface energy coefficient a_sur = **17.27931 MeV** (set a) or **17.64004 MeV** (set b)
- Nuclear radius parameter r_0 = **1.229286 fm** (set a) or **1.214608 fm** (set b)
- Odd-even energy parameter D = **4.305 MeV** (set a) or **4.234 MeV** (set b)
- Saddle-point energy for 250Cf in both LSD and ISLD models: approximately **1.3 MeV** at deformation c ≈ 1.35 and a_4 ≈ 0.06
- Scission point elongation for 250Cf: LSD at c = **2.62**, ISLD at c = **2.70**, with ISLD exit about **4 MeV** lower than LSD exit

## Context

This work extends the line of liquid-drop models with quadratic isospin dependence, following the ideas of Bohr and Mottelson (1969) and the models of Duflo and Zuker (1995) and Moretto et al. (2012). It updates the Lublin-Strasbourg Drop (LSD) model of Pomorski and Dudek (2003) and the Thomas-Fermi model of Myers and Swiatecki (1996) by fitting to the most recent mass table of Wang et al. (2021). The ISLD model's well-defined deformation dependence of the linear isospin term (Wigner/congruence energy) offers an advantage over the FRDM of Möller et al. (2016), which requires additional phenomenological deformation dependence at scission. The model enables improved predictions for superheavy nuclei masses (where differences with LSD reach up to 1.5 MeV), for astrophysical r-process calculations, and for fission fragment properties such as total kinetic energy and isotopic yields, with planned dynamical calculations using the new ISLD potential energy surfaces.
