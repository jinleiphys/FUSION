# Rotation of pear-shaped $^{100}$Ru nucleus

> Atomic nuclei in general can have deformed shapes and nearly all these shapes are symmetric with respect to reflection. Only a few Actinide nuclei have stable reflection asymmetric pear shapes in their ground state and exhibit characteristic rotational bands. In this article, we report on the observation of two alternate parity rotational bands in 100Ru, which are connected by seven interleaved electric dipole transitions and their rates are found to be enhanced. In addition, the moments of inertia associated with these two opposite parity rotational bands have been found to be similar. These experimental observations indicate the rotation of a stable pear-shaped 100Ru nucleus, which is the first such observation outside the Actinide mass region. This shape is built on an excited configuration and originates from the rotational alignment of the angular momenta of a pair of neutrons. This unique observation establishes an alternate mechanism by which an atomic nucleus can assume a pear shape.

## Key claim

The paper reports the observation of two alternating-parity rotational bands in \(^{100}\)Ru connected by seven interleaved electric dipole (E1) transitions with enhanced rates, and nearly identical moments of inertia for the two bands, which together indicate the rotation of a stable pear-shaped (octupole-deformed) nucleus. This is the first such observation outside the Actinide mass region, but the pear shape is built on an excited configuration (not the ground state) and originates from the rotational alignment of a pair of neutrons, establishing an alternate mechanism for stable reflection asymmetry.

## Method

Excited states of \(^{100}\)Ru were populated via a fusion-evaporation reaction using a 50 MeV alpha beam on a \(^{100}\)Mo target at the K-130 cyclotron at VECC, India. Gamma rays were detected with an array of 11 HPGe clover detectors, with six placed at 90° to the beam direction to maximize dipole emission sensitivity. About 5 billion two-fold gamma-gamma coincidence events were recorded using a PIXIE-16 digitizer system and sorted into matrices with a 200 ns coincidence window; the symmetric matrix was analyzed with the RADWARE program ESCL8R to build the partial level scheme.

## Key numbers

- Seven interleaved E1 transitions were observed connecting the two opposite-parity bands (Band2 and Band3) in \(^{100}\)Ru.
- The weighted mean of B(E1)/B(E2) for spins I \(\geq\) 12\(\hbar\) is 8.93 \(\pm\) 0.87 fm\(^{-2}\), significantly larger than the low-spin weighted mean of 2.26 \(\pm\) 0.15 fm\(^{-2}\).
- The estimated average retardation of the E1 transition rate is 10\(^5\) (relative to the Weisskopf single-particle estimate), based on an assumed quadrupole moment Q\(_0\) \(\approx\) 200 efm\(^2\).
- The alternating parity structure is observed from I = 12\(\hbar\) up to I = 22\(\hbar\); beyond I = 22\(\hbar\) (rotational frequency \(\hbar\omega\) = 0.66 MeV), Band3 terminates and a new band with moment of inertia \(\approx\) 10% larger appears.
- The moments of inertia of the two parity bands become identical beyond I = 16\(\hbar\) (\(\hbar\omega\) = 0.5 MeV), and parity splitting vanishes.

## Context

This work extends the search for stable octupole deformation beyond the Actinide region (Z \(\approx\) 88, N \(\approx\) 134), where it was previously known in nuclei such as \(^{226}\)Ra (PhysRevLett.78.2920) and \(^{224}\)Ra (PhysRevC.49.R605), and beyond the \(^{144}\)Ba case (PhysRevLett.124.032501). It contradicts a recent theoretical study (PhysRevC.102.024311) that concluded nuclei in the A \(\approx\) 90 region are unlikely to possess stable octupole deformation. The observation enables further study of an alternate mechanism for pear shapes via rotational alignment of a neutron pair, and suggests that similar phenomena may occur in other mass regions such as \(^{66}\)Zn (PhysRevC.104.044302, PhysRevC.102.064313).
