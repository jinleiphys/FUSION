# General Charge Balance Functions, A Tool for Studying the Chemical Evolution of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> In the canonical picture of the evolution of the quark-gluon plasma during a high-energy heavy-ion collision, quarks are produced in two waves. The first is during the first fm/c of the collision, when gluons thermalize into the QGP. After a roughly isentropic expansion that roughly conserves the number of quarks, a second wave ensues at hadronization, 5-10 fm/c into the collision. Since each hadron contains at least two quarks, the majority of quark production occurs at this later time. For each quark produced in a heavy-ion collision, an anti-quark of the same flavor is created at the same point in space-time. Charge balance functions identify, on a statistical basis, the location of balancing charges for a given hadron, and given the picture above one expects the distribution in relative rapidity of balancing charges to be characterized by two scales. After first demonstrating how charge balance functions can be created using any pair of hadronic states, it will be shown how one can identify and study both processes of quark production. By considering balance functions of several hadronic species, and by performing illustrative calculations, this class of measurement appears to hold the prospect of providing the field's most stringent insight into the chemical evolution of the QGP.

# General Charge Balance Functions, A Tool for Studying the Chemical Evolution of the Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim

Charge balance functions constructed from any pair of hadronic species can statistically separate two distinct waves of quark production in heavy-ion collisions: an early wave during QGP thermalization and a later wave at hadronization, provided the balance functions are analyzed in relative rapidity and the hadronization component is distinguished from the QGP component by its narrow width. The paper hedges that the separation relies on model-dependent assumptions about quark densities before hadronization and baryon yields, which are experimentally uncertain at the 25% level.

## Method

The author derives a formal relation between charge-charge correlation functions in the QGP phase and balance functions for arbitrary hadronic species after hadronization, using a thermal ansatz where local hadron populations are determined by a grand canonical ensemble constrained by the charge correlation. The correlation function after hadronization is decomposed into a QGP component (from correlations present before hadronization) and a hadronization component (a narrow peak at zero relative rapidity from pairs created at hadronization), with weights for each species pair calculated from the charge susceptibility matrices of the QGP and hadronic phases. Illustrative predictions for balance functions in relative rapidity are generated by convolving the spatial-rapidity balance functions with a thermal blast-wave model to map to momentum-space rapidity.

## Key numbers

- The majority of quark production occurs at hadronization, 5-10 fm/c into the collision, because each hadron contains at least two quarks and entropy conservation requires the number of quasi-particles to stay roughly equal.
- For the default calculation (baryon suppression factor f_B=0.6, n_quarks/n_had=0.85), the hadronization component of the pi+ pi- balance function integrates to 0.636, while the QGP component integrates to 0.239.
- The K+ K- balance function has a small hadronization component because few additional strange quarks are produced during hadronization.
- The p pbar balance function can have a negative hadronization component when the number of quarks is more than nine times the number of baryons, which occurs for the default parameters.
- The characteristic width of the QGP component is estimated as sigma_(QGP) >= 0.5 units of rapidity, while the hadronization component width is estimated as sigma_(had) <= 0.2 units of rapidity.
- Three ratios of n_quarks/n_had were explored: 0.7, 0.85, and 1.0.
- Three values of baryon suppression factor f_B were used: 0.5, 0.6, and 0.7, spanning the range of experimental uncertainty from PHENIX and STAR measurements.
- Hadronic yields used in the thermal model: pions 268 per unit rapidity, kaons 54, protons/neutrons 22.5, lambdas 8.5, sigmas 3.4, xis 1.95, omegas 0.35.

## Context

This work extends the charge balance function formalism originally proposed by Bass et al. (2000) for heavy-ion collisions, which was motivated by the desire to distinguish early versus late production of charges. Balance functions were previously used at the CERN ISR to study hadronization dynamics in pp and e+e- collisions in the 1980s, and have been observed to narrow significantly in central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC (Aggarwal 2010, Adams 2003, Westfall 2004), consistent with delayed hadronization from a long-lived QGP (Schlichting 2010). The paper enables experimental extraction of both the QGP and hadronization components of quark production by measuring balance functions for multiple hadronic species, particularly the p K- balance function which the author argues would be difficult to explain without a two-component picture. The approach requires improved experimental measurements of baryon yields, which currently differ between collaborations by several tens of percent.
