# Effects of Strong Interaction on the Electromagnetic Dissociation

> We investigate effects of strong interactions on the electromagnetic dissociation of nuclei in heavy ion collisions. We start from the eikonal approach to the equivalent photon method to describe the electromagnetic contributions to the cross section of peripheral collisions. We summarize some results of this approach and characterize recent experiments in "universal plots". In the second part of this work, we give a straight forward method to include transitions induced by the strong interactions between the ions. We introduce different methods to obtain the nuclear transition potential, and study the behavior of the resulting nuclear contributions.

## Key claim

The paper provides a method to include nuclear (strong-interaction) contributions to the electromagnetic dissociation cross section in peripheral heavy-ion collisions, showing that for many experiments the semiclassical equivalent-photon method already describes the data sufficiently well, but that nuclear contributions can become significant depending on the choice of optical potential parameters.

## Method

The authors start from the eikonal (Glauber) approach to the equivalent photon method for Coulomb excitation, then introduce nuclear transition potentials using both the folding formalism with Gaussian charge densities and the deformed optical potential model (Bohr-Tassie and Bohr-Mottelson collective models). They derive expressions for the nuclear inelastic scattering amplitude and compare the Glauber result to the Distorted Wave Born Approximation (DWBA), showing equivalence for large angular momenta and small scattering angles. They also introduce a simplified "ramp" potential model to clarify the role of optical potential parameters.

## Context

This work extends the equivalent photon method (Weizsacker 1934, Williams 1935) for Coulomb dissociation by incorporating nuclear excitation effects, building on the Glauber approach (Glauber 1959) and the collective model of Bohr and Tassie (Tassie 1956). The method enables estimation of nuclear contributions to electromagnetic dissociation experiments, particularly relevant for Coulomb dissociation studies of astrophysical reactions such as \( ^{7}\text{Be}(p,\gamma)^{8}\text{B} \) (Motobayashi 1994). The connection to DWBA (Blair 1966, Alder 1972) provides a bridge between the eikonal and distorted-wave formalisms for heavy-ion collisions.
