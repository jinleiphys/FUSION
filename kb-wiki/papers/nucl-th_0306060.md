# Study of Equilibrium Using Collision Dynamics

> We discuss the possibility of equilibrium (and thermalization) in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies within a transport model. This was achieved by dividing the nuclear matter into different collision zones. We find that those nucleons which experience at least ten collisions are close to complete equilibrium whereas others never achieve any equlilbrium.

# Study of Equilibrium Using Collision Dynamics

## Key claim
Within the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) model, nucleons that experience at least ten collisions are close to complete equilibrium, whereas nucleons with fewer collisions never achieve full equilibrium. The degree of equilibrium depends on both the reaction geometry and the number of collisions each individual nucleon suffers, with central collisions producing better thermalized matter than peripheral ones.

## Method
The authors simulated heavy-ion collisions of Ca-Ca, Xe-Sn, and Au-Au at intermediate energies using the QMD transport model, which treats each nucleon as a Gaussian wave packet propagating under classical equations of motion with Skyrme, Yukawa, Coulomb, and momentum-dependent interactions. They divided the final-state rapidity distribution into collision zones: spectator matter (SM, zero collisions), low collision matter (LCM, 1-4 collisions), moderate collision matter (MCM, 5-9 collisions), and high collision matter (HCM, 10 or more collisions). The degree of equilibrium was assessed via the rapidity distribution shape and the anisotropy ratio.

## Key numbers
- For Xe-Sn at 400 MeV/nucleon, the final anisotropy ratio values are 0.87 for central (b=0 fm), 0.67 for semi-central (b=4 fm), and 0.37 for peripheral (b=8 fm) impact parameters; a value of 1.0 would indicate complete equilibrium.
- The maximal nucleon-nucleon collision rate for Xe-Sn is 34 at 400 MeV/nucleon and 56 at 1 GeV/nucleon.
- Nucleons that suffer at least ten collisions exhibit a Gaussian rapidity distribution peaked at mid-rapidity, indicating near-complete equilibrium, at both 400 MeV/nucleon and 1 GeV/nucleon.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of thermalization in heavy-ion collisions using transport models such as Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) and QMD, as referenced by the authors (QMD model papers from 1991-1992). The finding that a threshold of approximately ten collisions is required for equilibrium provides a quantitative criterion for when local equilibrium assumptions in hydrodynamical models are justified. The results also connect to studies of collective flow and balance energy, showing that transverse flow increases with collision rate and system mass, consistent with prior observations by Hartnack (1990s).
