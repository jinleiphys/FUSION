# Correction methods for finite-acceptance effects in two-particle correlation analyses

> Two-particle angular correlations have been widely used as a tool to explore particle production mechanisms in heavy-ion collisions. The mixed-event technique is generally used as a standard method to correct for finite-acceptance effects. We demonstrate that event mixing only provides an approximate acceptance correction, and propose new methods for finite-acceptance corrections. Starting from discussions about 2-dimensional correction procedures, new methods are derived for specific assumptions on the properties of the signal, such as uniform signal distribution or $δ$-function-like trigger particle distribution, and suitable for two-particle correlation analyses from particles at mid-rapidity and jet-hadron or high $p_{\text{T}}$-triggered hadron-hadron correlations. Per-trigger associated particle yields from the mixed-event method and the new methods are compared through Monte Carlo simulations containing well-defined correlation signals. Significant differences are observed at large pseudorapidity differences in general and especially for asymmetric particle distribution like that produced in proton--nucleus collisions. The applicability and validity of the new methods are discussed in detail.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the standard mixed-event technique provides only an approximate correction for finite-acceptance effects in two-particle correlation analyses, and proposes new exact correction formulas that are valid under specific assumptions about the signal properties (uniform signal distribution or delta-function-like trigger particle distribution), with the caveat that their applicability depends on how closely the real data match these assumed conditions.

## Method

The authors derive exact mathematical relations between the measured per-trigger yield with finite acceptance and the ideal per-trigger yield with infinite acceptance, starting from a general formalism in one-dimensional coordinate space. They obtain two specific correction formulas: one assuming a constant distribution of the correlated signal reference point, and another assuming a delta-function-like trigger particle distribution (relevant for jet-hadron or high-pT-triggered correlations). These methods are tested by comparing per-trigger associated particle yields from the standard mixed-event method and the new methods using Monte Carlo simulations containing well-defined correlation signals generated with PYTHIA and a toy model with a common symmetry plane.

## Key numbers

- The correction factor for the constant signal distribution case is given by (A_a star A_t)(Delta x) / Delta_t, where Delta_t = a_{2,t} - a_{1,t} is the size of the trigger particle acceptance.
- The correction factor for the delta-function trigger case is given by (A_a star n_trig A_t)(Delta x) / N_trig, where n_trig(x) is the measured trigger particle distribution within acceptance.
- Significant differences between the mixed-event method and the new methods are observed at large pseudorapidity differences, especially for asymmetric particle distributions like those produced in proton-nucleus collisions.

## Context

This work extends earlier critiques of the mixed-event technique found in recent papers that proposed corrections using single-particle efficiency times acceptance functions (Wang, 2013) and multi-dimensional weights (Pruneau, 2015). The new methods provide mathematically exact corrections under specific assumptions, enabling more accurate extraction of correlation signals in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, particularly for studies of the near-side ridge in pp collisions (Khachatryan, 2010) and double-ridge structures in p-Pb collisions (ALICE, 2013; ATLAS, 2013; CMS, 2013). The formalism also allows explicit checking of the translational invariance assumption when working in (x_t, Delta x) space.
