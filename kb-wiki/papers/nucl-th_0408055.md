# A Calculation of the Viscosity to Entropy Ratio of a Hadronic Gas

> We calculate thermodynamic quantities and transport coefficients of a hadronic gas using a Monte Carlo hadronic collision event generator URASiMA. The obtained shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of the hadronic gas is approximately 0.3 -- 0.6 in the range $T$ = 100 -- 180MeV and is quite insensitive to the baryon number density.

## Key claim

The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of a hadronic gas is approximately 0.3 to 0.6 in the temperature range 100 to 180 MeV, and this ratio is quite insensitive to the baryon number density; the abstract glosses over that this result is obtained from a specific Monte Carlo event generator (URASiMA) that includes only ordinary hadronic collisions with no special strong correlations such as strings or potentials.

## Method

The authors performed hadro-molecular dynamics simulations using the Monte Carlo collision event generator URASiMA, which includes baryons (nucleons and Delta resonances) and mesons (pion, eta, sigma, rho) but ignores anti-baryons and strangeness. They placed hadrons in a fixed box of 10^3 fm^3 and simulated the time evolution to create a statistical ensemble at temperatures from 100 to 180 MeV and baryon densities up to twice normal nuclear density. Transport coefficients were extracted from the correlation functions of the stress tensor and heat current using linear response theory, with the correlation integrals cut off at t = 20 fm.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio (η_s/s) is approximately 0.3 to 0.6 in the temperature range T = 100 to 180 MeV.
- Shear viscosity η_s is approximately 0.2 to 0.6 GeV/fm^2 and is almost independent of baryon number density.
- The temperature dependence of shear viscosity is approximately T^5, stronger than the naive dimensional analysis result of T^3.
- Normal nuclear density n_b0 is 0.157 fm^{-3}.
- The box size used in simulations is 10^3 fm^3.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of transport coefficients for hadronic matter, notably Gavin's calculation of shear viscosity for a pion gas (approximately 0.05 to 0.15 GeV/fm^2) and Muronga's calculation for a pure meson gas using UrQMD (which gave about half the present result). The authors note that Dobado and Llanes-Estrada obtained shear viscosity values for a pion gas ranging from about 0.1 to 10 GeV/fm^2 depending on the model used. The finding that η_s/s is of order unity or smaller for a hadronic gas enables comparison with the peculiarly small ratio identified as a possible feature of the quark-gluon plasma, and suggests that such small values may be a common feature of relativistic gases rather than unique to QGP.
