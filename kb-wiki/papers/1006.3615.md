# The significance of using the Newcomb-Benford law as a test of nuclear half-life calculations

> Half-life number sequences collected from nuclear data charts are found to obey the Newcomb-Benford law. Based on this fact, it has been suggested recently, that this law should be used to test the quality of nuclear decay models. In this paper we briefly recall how, when and why the Newcomb-Benford law can be observed in a set of numbers with a given probability distribution. We investigate the special case of nuclear half-lives, and show that the law provides no additional clue in understanding decay half-lives. Thus, it can play no significant role in testing nuclear decay theories.

## Key claim

The Newcomb-Benford law provides no additional clue in understanding nuclear decay half-lives beyond what is already known from the probability distribution of the half-lives themselves, and therefore it can play no significant role in testing nuclear decay theories, contrary to recent suggestions.

## Method

The authors analyzed 2298 nuclear half-life values from the NUBASE2003 database, rejecting nuclei for which only upper or lower limits were known. They computed the distribution of first significant digits and compared it to the Newcomb-Benford law prediction using binomial-distribution errors. They also performed a ones scaling test (OST) by repeatedly multiplying the data set by a scaling constant of 1.01 and tracking the fraction of numbers beginning with digit 1. The authors then examined the mathematical condition for a data set to obey the law: the Fourier transform of the probability density function of the numbers (on a logarithmic scale) must vanish at all nonzero integer frequencies. They fitted a log-normal distribution to the half-life data and computed its Fourier transform to show that this condition is automatically satisfied.

## Key numbers

- 2298 half-life values from NUBASE2003 were analyzed; the observed counts for first digits 1 through 9 all fell within two standard deviations of the Newcomb-Benford law predictions (e.g., digit 1: observed 701, expected 692 ± 22; digit 2: observed 405, expected 405 ± 18).
- The half-life values span 54 decimal orders of magnitude.
- The fitted Gaussian on the decimal logarithmic scale has a mean of 1.0503 ± 0.0051 and a half-width at half-maximum (HWHM) of 3.0139 ± 0.0060, corresponding to a standard deviation of approximately 2.56 on the decimal logarithmic scale.
- The standard deviation of the Fourier transform of the fitted Gaussian is approximately 0.062, making the transform very small at all integer frequencies.
- The ones scaling test gave a relative number of ones scattering around the expected value of 0.301 with an error of 0.0096.

## Context

This paper directly contradicts the conclusions of Buck (1993), Ni and Ren (2008), and Ni and Ren (2009), who had suggested that the Newcomb-Benford law could be used to test nuclear decay models. The authors show mathematically that any distribution whose Fourier transform decays sufficiently before the first integer frequency will automatically satisfy the law, and that the known log-normal-like distribution of nuclear half-lives meets this criterion. This work enables future researchers to focus on explaining the actual half-life distribution itself rather than testing whether models reproduce the Benford law, which the authors argue is an artifact of the digit-sampling procedure rather than a physical phenomenon.
