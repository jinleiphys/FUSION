# Recent Progress in QCD Condensate Evaluations and Sum Rules

> We review the recent status of the QCD sum rule approach to study the properties of hadrons in vacuum and in hot or dense matter. Special focus is laid on the progress made in the evaluation of the QCD condensates, which are the input of all QCD sum rule calculations, and for which much new information has become available through high precision lattice QCD calculations, chiral perturbation theory and experimental measurements. Furthermore, we critically examine common analysis methods for QCD sum rules and contrast them with potential alternative strategies. The status of QCD sum rule studies investigating the modification of hadrons at finite density as well as recent derivations of exact sum rules applicable to finite temperature spectral functions, are also reviewed.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that QCD sum rules remain a useful tool for studying hadron properties despite their typical 10-20% precision limitations, particularly for finite-density systems where lattice QCD faces the sign problem, but the reliability of the method depends critically on improved determinations of QCD condensates from lattice QCD, chiral perturbation theory, and experiment.

## Method

The authors review the QCD sum rule formalism, which combines dispersion relations relating the correlator in the deep Euclidean region to an integral over its imaginary part (the spectral function) with the operator product expansion (OPE) that systematically incorporates both perturbative and non-perturbative contributions. They examine the status of QCD condensate evaluations in vacuum, at finite temperature, finite density, and in constant magnetic fields, cataloging operators up to mass dimension 6 and discussing higher-order alpha_s corrections to Wilson coefficients for mesonic and baryonic correlators. The review also covers analysis techniques including the conventional "pole + continuum" assumption and the maximum entropy method for extracting spectral functions without strong assumptions about their form.

## Key numbers

- QCD sum rule predictions are typically limited to about 10% to 20% precision
- Up to mass dimension 6, vacuum condensates include: dimension 3 chiral condensate ⟨0|q̅q|0⟩, dimension 4 gluon condensate ⟨0|G^a_{μν}G^{aμν}|0⟩, dimension 5 mixed condensate ⟨0|q̅σ_{μν}t^a G^{aμν}q|0⟩, and dimension 6 four-quark and triple-gluon condensates
- At finite density, new Lorentz-violating condensates appear including the dimension 3 vector condensate ⟨Ω|q̅γ^μ q|Ω⟩ whose 0-component equals the quark number density ⟨Ω|q^† q|Ω⟩
- Finite-density QCD sum rules can be applied at least up to densities of order normal nuclear matter density

## Context

This review extends the long history of QCD sum rule methodology originating from Shifman, Vainshtein and Zakharov (1978) and updates earlier reviews by Reinders (1984), Nielsen (2009), and others. It contrasts with lattice QCD as described by Aoki (2013), which can precisely compute many hadronic observables with physical pion masses but cannot yet handle finite-density calculations due to the sign problem. The review enables future applications to exotic hadron studies (popularized by Nielsen, 2009), finite-density hadron modification studies relevant to experimental facilities such as FAIR, NICA, HIAF and J-PARC, and provides exact sum rules at finite temperature that can constrain spectral function fits to lattice data or determine condensate values and hydrodynamic transport coefficients.
