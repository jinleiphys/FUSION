# Black Dwarf Supernova in the Far Future

> In the far future long after star formation has ceased the universe will be populated by sparse degenerate remnants, mostly white dwarfs, though their ultimate fate is an open question. These white dwarfs will cool and freeze solid into black dwarfs while pycnonuclear fusion will slowly process their composition to iron-56. However, due to the declining electron fraction the Chandrasekhar limit of these stars will be decreasing and will eventually be below that of the most massive black dwarfs. As such, isolated dwarf stars with masses greater than $\sim 1.2 M_\odot$ will collapse in the far future due to the slow accumulation of iron-56 in their cores. If proton decay does not occur then this is the ultimate fate of about $10^{21}$ stars, approximately one percent of all stars in the observable universe. We present calculations of the internal structure of black dwarfs with iron cores as a model for progenitors. From pycnonuclear fusion rates we estimate their lifetime and thus delay time to be $10^{1100}$ years. We speculate that high mass black dwarf supernovae resemble accretion induced collapse of O/Ne/Mg white dwarfs while later low mass transients will be similar to stripped-envelope core-collapse supernova, and may be the last interesting astrophysical transients to occur prior to heat death.

# Black Dwarf Supernova in the Far Future

## Key claim

Isolated white dwarfs with masses greater than approximately 1.2 solar masses will collapse and explode as supernovae in the far future (after ~10^1100 years) due to pycnonuclear fusion slowly converting their composition to iron-56, which reduces the electron fraction and thus the Chandrasekhar mass below the dwarf's actual mass. This fate applies only if proton decay does not occur, and the collapse is triggered when the core density reaches the threshold for electron capture onto iron-56.

## Method

The authors calculate the internal structure of black dwarf progenitors using the relativistic electron equation of state, with a piecewise model having an iron-56 core (Ye = 0.464) and an outer layer of lighter nuclei (Ye = 0.5), integrating the classical equations of stellar structure with the central density fixed at the critical density for electron capture onto iron-56 (1.19 × 10^9 g/cm^3). They estimate the number of progenitors by combining the initial-final mass relation for white dwarfs from Cummings (2016) with a Salpeter initial mass function, and compute black dwarf lifetimes from pycnonuclear fusion rates for silicon-28 at the burning front using the Wigner-Seitz approximation for a bcc crystal lattice following Schramm and Koonin (1990).

## Key numbers

- Progenitor mass range: 1.16 to 1.35 solar masses (minimum and maximum, respectively)
- Minimum progenitor radius: 0.347 Earth radii (for fully iron black dwarf at 1.167 solar masses)
- Maximum progenitor radius: 0.374 Earth radii (for progenitor with infinitesimal iron core at 1.355 solar masses)
- Critical central density for electron capture onto iron-56: 1.19 × 10^9 g/cm^3
- Threshold electron Fermi energy for electron capture onto iron-56: 4.207 MeV
- Corresponding pressure at threshold: 5.57 × 10^26 dyn/cm^2
- Number of progenitors: approximately 10^21 stars (about one percent of all stars in the observable universe)
- Lifetime for most massive black dwarfs (1.35 solar masses): 10^1100 years
- Lifetime for intermediate mass black dwarfs (1.24 solar masses): 10^1600 years
- Lifetime for least massive black dwarfs (1.16 solar masses): 10^32000 years
- Electron fraction of iron-56: 0.464 (compared to 0.5 for canonical white dwarfs)
- Ratio of Chandrasekhar mass for iron core versus canonical composition: approximately 0.86

## Context

This work extends the far-future astrophysics program of Dyson (1979) and Adams and Laughlin (1997), who first proposed that degenerate remnants would be processed to iron via pycnonuclear fusion, by providing detailed stellar structure calculations and more precise lifetime estimates. The authors note that their most massive progenitors may resemble accretion-induced collapse of O/Ne/Mg white dwarfs as studied by Nomoto (1984, 1987) and Isern (1991), while lower-mass transients may be similar to stripped-envelope core-collapse supernovae. The work enables future simulations with stellar evolution codes such as MESA using pycnonuclear reaction networks, and suggests that black dwarf supernovae may be the last astrophysical transients to occur before heat death, occurring after all objects have become causally disconnected due to cosmic expansion.
