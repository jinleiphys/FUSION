# Systematics of strength function sum rules

> Sum rules provide useful insights into transition strength functions and are often expressed as expectation values of an operator. In this letter I demonstrate that non-energy-weighted transition sum rules have strong secular dependences on the energy of the initial state. Such non-trivial systematics have consequences: the simplification suggested by the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, for example, does not hold for most cases, though it weakly holds in at least some cases for electric dipole transitions. Furthermore, I show the systematics can be understood through spectral distribution theory, calculated via traces of operators and of products of operators. Seen through this lens, violation of the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis is unsurprising: one \textit{expects} sum rules to evolve with excitation energy. Furthermore, to lowest order the slope of the secular evolution can be traced to a component of the Hamiltonian being positive (repulsive) or negative (attractive).

# Systematics of strength function sum rules

## Key claim

The non-energy-weighted sum rule (NEWSR) for transition operators exhibits strong secular dependence on the excitation energy of the initial state, meaning the generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which assumes strength functions are independent of initial state energy, does not hold for most transitions, though it weakly holds in at least some cases for electric dipole (E1) transitions. This energy dependence is expected from spectral distribution theory and can be traced to whether the relevant component of the Hamiltonian is attractive (negative slope) or repulsive (positive slope).

## Method

The author performed configuration-interaction (CI) shell model calculations using the BIGSTICK code in the sd shell (using the USDB interaction) and in the p-sd5/2 space (using Cohen-Kurath, USD, and Millener-Kurath interactions) to compute non-energy-weighted sum rules as expectation values of the operator R = O-dagger O for M1, E2, Gamow-Teller, and E1 transitions. The secular behavior of the sum rules as a function of initial energy was then analyzed using spectral distribution theory, which computes traces of operators and products of operators to derive linear and quadratic approximations to the energy dependence.

## Key numbers

- For 33P in the sd space: isoscalar E2 sum rules fall sharply with initial energy; isovector M1 and Gamow-Teller sum rules grow with initial energy; isoscalar M1 shows large fluctuations (units: M1 in mu_N^2, E2 in e^2-fm^4)
- Gamow-Teller sum rules were computed for several even-even, odd-odd, and odd-A nuclides in the sd space, showing relatively insensitive behavior across nuclides
- For E1 transitions in the p-sd5/2 space: the first 3- state was placed at approximately 6.1 MeV above the ground state by shifting sd single-particle energies relative to p-shell energies
- Sum rules were binned into 2 MeV bins for analysis
- Harmonic oscillator wavefunctions used an oscillator length of 2.5 fm
- Gamow-Teller calculations used a quenched value of g_A approximately equal to 1
- Isovector sum rules were divided by 3 to roughly average over charge-changing transitions

## Context

This work extends and provides a theoretical framework for understanding previous experimental and theoretical evidence against the Brink-Axel hypothesis for E1 (see work by Rubehn et al. 1993, Behr et al. 1995, Lui et al. 2007, Angell et al. 2012, Larsen and Goriely 2013), M1 (Lui et al. 2007, Simon et al. 2013, Brown and Larsen 2014), E2 (Lui et al. 2007, Schwengner 2014), and Gamow-Teller transitions (Frisk et al. 1997, Ney et al. 2007, Martinez-Pinedo et al. 2014). It also contrasts with a recent ab initio calculation by Kvaal et al. (2015) that supported the Brink-Axel hypothesis for E1 transitions from low-lying states, and with the Oslo method's reliance on M1 strengths following the hypothesis (Schiller et al. 2000, Schiller et al. 2003, Voinov et al. 2006, Simon et al. 2009). The work enables future studies to use spectral distribution theory to predict which transitions will show strong energy dependence and to parameterize sum rule evolution using quantities relatively easy to calculate.
