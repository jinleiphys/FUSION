# Future of superheavy element research: Which nuclei could be synthesized within the next few years?

> Low values of the fusion cross sections and very short half-lives of nuclei with Z$>$120 put obstacles in synthesis of new elements. Different nuclear reactions (fusion of stable and radioactive nuclei, multi-nucleon transfers and neutron capture), which could be used for the production of new isotopes of superheavy (SH) elements, are discussed in the paper. The gap of unknown SH nuclei, located between the isotopes which were produced earlier in the cold and hot fusion reactions, can be filled in fusion reactions of $^{48}$Ca with available lighter isotopes of Pu, Am, and Cm. Cross sections for the production of these nuclei are predicted to be rather large, and the corresponding experiments can be easily performed at existing facilities. For the first time, a narrow pathway is found to the middle of the island of stability owing to possible $β^+$-decay of SH isotopes which can be formed in ordinary fusion reactions of stable nuclei. Multi-nucleon transfer processes at near barrier collisions of heavy (and very heavy, U-like) ions are shown to be quite realistic reaction mechanism allowing us to produce new neutron enriched heavy nuclei located in the unexplored upper part of the nuclear map. Neutron capture reactions can be also used for the production of the long-living neutron rich SH nuclei. Strong neutron fluxes might be provided by pulsed nuclear reactors and by nuclear explosions in laboratory conditions and by supernova explosions in nature. All these possibilities are discussed in the paper.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, despite the low cross sections and short half-lives that hinder synthesis of elements with Z > 120, several realistic reaction pathways exist to produce new neutron-rich superheavy nuclei within the next few years; crucially, the authors claim that a narrow pathway to the middle of the island of stability may exist via β⁺-decay of certain superheavy isotopes formed in ordinary fusion reactions of stable nuclei, but this depends on unconfirmed decay properties.

## Method

The authors used the two-center shell model to calculate adiabatic multi-dimensional potential energy surfaces for heavy nuclear systems, and solved Langevin-type dynamical equations to simultaneously describe deep inelastic scattering, quasi-fission, and fusion processes. They also applied a statistical model to calculate survival probabilities of compound nuclei, and solved a set of differential equations for neutron capture and β⁻ decay chains to model production of superheavy nuclei via multiple neutron captures.

## Key numbers

- Cross section for element 113 production in the cold fusion reaction ⁷⁰Zn + ²⁰⁹Bi was 0.03 pb (Morita et al., 2007)
- Cross section for element 118 production in the hot fusion reaction ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴⁹Cf was about 1 pb (Oganessian et al., 2006)
- Predicted cross section for element 119 in the ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁵⁴Es fusion reaction is about 0.3 pb (Zagrebaev et al., 2012)
- Predicted cross sections for elements 119 and 120 in the ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁴⁹Bk and ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁴⁹Cf fusion reactions are about 0.04 pb (Zagrebaev et al., 2008)
- Predicted cross sections for new isotopes of element 115 (²⁸⁴⁻²⁸⁶115) in the ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴¹Am fusion reaction are about 0.1 pb, 2 pb, and 4 pb, respectively
- Predicted cross section for the 3n evaporation channel of the ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁵⁰Cm fusion reaction is about 0.8 pb
- In nuclear explosions, the neutron capture time τₙ is about 1 μs, allowing more than 20 neutrons to be captured before decay
- In nuclear reactors, typical τₙ is about 1 year

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on fusion dynamics and superheavy element production (Zagrebaev et al., 2003, 2004, 2008, 2011, 2012) and builds on experimental results from the Dubna group (Oganessian et al., 2007) and cold fusion experiments (Hofmann et al., 2000; Morita et al., 2007). The paper proposes specific experiments to fill the gap between isotopes produced in cold and hot fusion reactions, and suggests that multi-nucleon transfer reactions in collisions of actinide nuclei (e.g., ²³⁸U + ²⁴⁸Cm) could produce new neutron-rich superheavy nuclei, a mechanism previously explored by Schädel et al. (1982) and others. The work also revives the idea of using multiple nuclear explosions to bypass the fermium gap in neutron capture synthesis, as originally discussed by Meldner (1972).
