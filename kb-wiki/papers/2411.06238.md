# Imprints of high-density nuclear symmetry energy on the crustal fraction of neutron star moment of inertia

> The density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$ remains the most uncertain aspect of the equation of state (EOS) of supradense neutron-rich nucleonic matter. Implications of observational crustal fraction of neutron star (NS) moment of inertia $ΔI/I$ on the $E_{\rm sym}(ρ)$ are examined in the present work, utilizing an isospin-dependent parameterization of NS EOS. We find that symmetry energy parameters significantly influence the $ΔI/I$, while the EOS of symmetric nuclear matter has a negligible effect. An increase in the slope $L$ and skewness $J_{\rm sym}$ of symmetry energy results in a larger $ΔI/I$, whereas an increase in the curvature $K_{\rm sym}$ leads to a reduction in $ΔI/I$. Additionally, we discuss the imprints of observational $ΔI/I$ on the symmetry energy for NSs with masses of 1.0 M$_\odot$ or 1.4 M$_\odot$. Our results indicate that the $ΔI/I$ has the potential to set a lower limit of symmetry energy at densities exceeding $3ρ_0$, particularly when $L$ is constrained to values less than $60$ MeV, thereby enhancing our understanding of supradense NS matter.

# Imprints of High-Density Nuclear Symmetry Energy on the Crustal Fraction of Neutron Star Moment of Inertia

## Key claim
The crustal fraction of neutron star moment of inertia (ΔI/I) can set a lower limit on the nuclear symmetry energy at densities exceeding 3ρ₀, but only when the slope parameter L is constrained to values less than 60 MeV; for larger L values, the constraint becomes ineffective. The paper demonstrates that symmetry energy parameters significantly influence ΔI/I while the symmetric nuclear matter equation of state has negligible effect.

## Method
The authors used an isospin-dependent meta-model parameterization of the neutron star equation of state, expanding the symmetry energy E_sym(ρ) around saturation density ρ₀ in terms of its slope L, curvature K_sym, and skewness J_sym. They calculated the crust-core transition density and pressure using the thermodynamical condition of vanishing incompressibility (K_μ = 0), then computed ΔI/I for neutron stars of 1.0 M_⊙, 1.4 M_⊙, and 2.0 M_⊙ using the formalism of Link (1999). The Negele-Vautherin equation of state was used for the inner crust and the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state for the outer crust.

## Key numbers
- The slope L of symmetry energy at ρ₀ is constrained to 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV from surveys of 53 analyses (Li 2013, Oertel 2017)
- The curvature K_sym is around -100 ± 100 MeV (Li 2021, Grams 2022, Margueron 2017b, Mondal 2017, Somasundaram 2021)
- The skewness J_sym is roughly known to be between -200 and 800 MeV (Cai 2017, Zhang 2017, Tews 2017), with a lower boundary of 200 MeV adopted in this work
- For a 1.0 M_⊙ neutron star with L = 30 MeV, J_sym must exceed 294 MeV to satisfy ΔI/I ≥ 7%
- For a 1.4 M_⊙ neutron star with L = 60 MeV, the upper limit of K_sym is constrained to -46 MeV and the lower limit of J_sym to 289 MeV
- For L = 60 MeV with a 1.0 M_⊙ neutron star, the upper limit of K_sym is constrained to 41 MeV
- The binding energy E₀(ρ₀) of symmetric nuclear matter is -15.9 ± 0.4 MeV (Garg 2018, Shlomo 2006)
- The incompressibility K₀ of symmetric nuclear matter is 240 ± 20 MeV (Garg 2018, Shlomo 2006)
- The skewness J₀ of symmetric nuclear matter is -190 ± 100 MeV (Zhang 2019, Xie 2019, Xie 2020)

## Context
This work extends previous studies by Zhang and Li (2021) that used maximum observed mass, mass-radius measurements, and tidal deformability to constrain the equation of state, finding that symmetry energy above 3ρ₀ remained uncertain. It builds on the finding by Link (1999) that ΔI/I must exceed 1.4% from glitch data, later raised to 7% when accounting for superfluid neutron entrainment (Andersson 2012, Chamel 2012). The results enable future observational constraints on high-density symmetry energy from pulsar glitch measurements, particularly for neutron stars with masses around 1.4 M_⊙, and complement constraints from NICER (Miller 2021, Riley 2021) and LIGO/Virgo (2018) that are limited to densities below approximately 3ρ₀.
