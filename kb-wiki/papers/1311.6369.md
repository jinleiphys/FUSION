# How fast can an unstable particle decay into two final states and be observed

> We investigate unstable hadrons (resonances) which mainly decay into two final states in a very short time. We estimate how far at most these two final states can travel away from each other in the half-life of the initial unstable hadron. As examples, this distance is about 2.5 fm for rho -> pi pi, 1.8 fm for Delta -> N pi, and 0.6 fm for f_0(500) -> pi pi. We calculate this distance for altogether 181 hadrons, among all the 324 ones listed in PDG2012 together with Zc(3900) and Zc(4025). We find it is around one femtometer for many hadrons, which is of the same order of magnitude as the hadronic radii. We also estimate this distance for altogether 15 unstable nuclei. We find it is about several femtometers for some of them, which is of the same order of magnitude as the nuclear radii. For example, it is about six femtometers for O12 -> N11 p.

## Key claim

The paper estimates the maximum distance that two decay products can separate during the half-life of an unstable parent hadron or nucleus, finding that for many hadrons this distance is around one femtometer, which is of the same order as hadronic radii; this suggests that for some short-lived resonances the two final-state particles may not separate geometrically even after the decay process is complete, potentially affecting experimental observability.

## Method

The author uses relativistic kinematics to compute the speeds of the two decay products from energy and momentum conservation, assuming the parent particle is initially at rest and that the mass difference is entirely converted into kinetic energy. The maximum separation distance \(d_X\) is then defined as \(d_X = (\hbar / \Gamma_X) (|v_A| + |v_B|)\), where \(\Gamma_X\) is the decay width, and the theoretical uncertainty \(\Delta d_X\) is estimated from the uncertainty principle. This calculation is performed for 181 hadrons selected from the 324 listed in PDG2012 (plus Zc(3900) and Zc(4025)) and for 15 unstable nuclei, using published masses, widths, and dominant two-body decay modes.

## Key numbers

* For \(\rho(770) \rightarrow \pi\pi\): \(d_X = 2.39\)–\(2.51\) fm, with \(\Delta d = 0.14\) fm.
* For \(\Delta(1232) \rightarrow N\pi\): \(d_X = 1.8\) fm (from abstract; table gives 1.8 fm as example), with \(\Delta d = 0.22\) fm.
* For \(f_0(500) \rightarrow \pi\pi\): \(d_X = 0.41\)–\(0.85\) fm, with \(\Delta d = 0.34\) fm.
* For \(\Upsilon(10580) \rightarrow BB\): \(d_X = 1.06\)–\(1.43\) fm.
* For \(^{12}\mathrm{O} \rightarrow ^{11}\mathrm{N}p\): \(d_X = 5.26\)–\(6.08\) fm, with \(\Delta d = 1.80\) fm.
* For \(K^*(892) \rightarrow \pi K\): \(d_X = 5.35\)–\(6.27\) fm, with \(\Delta d = 0.17\) fm.
* For \(\phi(1020) \rightarrow KK\): \(d_X = 21.2\)–\(21.6\) fm, with \(\Delta d = 0.42\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the study of resonance lifetimes from a geometric, relativistic-motion perspective rather than using field theory or scattering theory. It builds on the PDG2012 compilation (Beringer et al., 2012) and includes the newly observed Zc(3900) and Zc(4025) states (Ablikim et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2013; Xiao et al., 2013). The results enable a discussion of whether short-lived resonances can be geometrically resolved as distinct particles, and the author compares the computed distances to hadronic radii from chiral perturbation theory (Albaladejo and Oller, 2012) and other measurements.
