# Precision calculation for nucleon capture by deuteron with Effective Field Theory

> We calculate the cross section for radiative capture of neutron by deuteron $n+d \to 3H+γ$ using Effective Field Theory(EFT). The calculation includes N2LO order and we compare our results with available calculated data below E=0.2 Mev.

## Key claim
The cross section for neutron radiative capture by deuteron (n+d → ³H+γ) can be calculated using Effective Field Theory (EFT) at next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO) with 1% accuracy for energies around 50-70 keV, when compared to the Evaluated Nuclear Data File (ENDF/B-VI).

## Method
The authors performed an EFT calculation for the three-nucleon system in the triton channel, including contributions from M1 multipole transitions and four-nucleon operators coupling to magnetic fields. The kernel was expanded in powers of Q, iterated by inserting into an integral equation, and regularized at N2LO to obtain the cross section. The coefficient L1 in the Lagrangian was determined from two-body capture experimental data.

## Key numbers
- At 50 keV: N2LO cross section = 1.38 μb, compared to ENDF value of 1.39(0) μb
- At 60 keV: N2LO cross section = 1.50 μb, compared to ENDF value of 1.50(0) μb
- At 70 keV: N2LO cross section = 1.61 μb, compared to ENDF value of 1.61(0) μb
- At 100 keV: N2LO cross section = 1.93 μb, compared to ENDF value of 1.94(0) μb
- At 140 keV: N2LO cross section = 2.30 μb, compared to ENDF value of 2.22(9) μb
- The calculation achieves 1% accuracy at N2LO for energies ~50-70 keV, compared to 10-40% at LO and <10% at NLO

## Context
This work extends the EFT approach for two-body radiative capture (n+p → d+γ) by Savage and Chen (1999) and Rupak (2000) to the three-body radiative capture process n+d → ³H+γ. The results address significant theoretical uncertainties in Big Bang Nucleosynthesis reactions at energies between 0.02 and 0.2 MeV, where experimental measurements are limited. The calculation enables more precise predictions for primordial light element abundances and can be compared with earlier evaluations from ENDF/B-VI and the Smith, Kawano and Malaney (SKM) model.
