# Expanding Nuclear Physics Horizons with the Gamma Factory

> The Gamma Factory (GF) is an ambitious proposal, currently explored within the CERN Physics Beyond Colliders program, for a source of photons with energies up to $\approx 400\,$MeV and photon fluxes (up to $\approx 10^{17}$ photons per second) exceeding those of the currently available gamma sources by orders of magnitude. The high-energy (secondary) photons are produced via resonant scattering of the primary laser photons by highly relativistic partially-stripped ions circulating in the accelerator. The secondary photons are emitted in a narrow cone and the energy of the beam can be monochromatized, eventually down to the $\approx1$ ppm level, via collimation, at the expense of the photon flux. This paper surveys the new opportunities that may be afforded by the GF in nuclear physics and related fields.

## Key claim

The Gamma Factory (GF) is a proposed photon source that could produce gamma-ray beams with energies up to approximately 400 MeV and fluxes up to approximately 10^17 photons per second, exceeding existing sources by orders of magnitude, but the paper surveys potential opportunities rather than reporting experimental results, and the actual realization depends on overcoming challenges such as ensuring sufficient ion beam intensity and addressing low excitation rates for narrow nuclear transitions.

## Method

The GF concept uses highly relativistic partially-stripped ions circulating in a storage ring (such as the LHC at CERN) as targets for primary laser photons; the laser photons resonantly scatter off bound electrons in the ions, and the scattered (secondary) photons are boosted in energy by a factor of up to 4γ^2 relative to the initial laser photons, where γ is the Lorentz factor of the ions. The secondary photons are emitted in a narrow cone with opening angle approximately 1/γ, and the beam energy can be monochromatized down to approximately 1 ppm via collimation at the expense of flux. The paper surveys opportunities for nuclear spectroscopy using primary beams, fixed-target experiments with secondary beams, and production of tertiary beams.

## Key numbers

- Photon energies up to approximately 400 MeV
- Photon fluxes up to approximately 10^17 photons per second
- Energy monochromatization down to approximately 1 ppm via collimation
- Lorentz factor γ of ions ranges from 10 (in SPS) to 2900 (in LHC)
- Number of ions per bunch: 10^8 for Pb ions (up to 3×10^9 for He-like Ca)
- Effective repetition rate: 10 MHz (20 MHz planned after LHC injector upgrade)
- Normalized emittance of ion beam: 1.6 μm (for LHC)
- Ion energy spread: 10^-4
- For hydrogen-like calcium ions at γ = 2048, with σ_x = 0.44 mm and σ_x' = 2.2 μrad, the uncorrelated energy spread of the secondary gamma beam is on the order of 10^-3
- Laser photons with energy up to 10 eV, boosted in the ion rest frame to up to 60 keV
- Examples of low-lying nuclear transitions accessible include: 229Th at 8 eV (M1, radiative half-life 5.19×10^3 s), 235U at 76 eV (E3, radiative half-life 7.03×10^23 s), 57Fe at 14.413 keV (M1, radiative half-life 9.32×10^-7 s), 238U at 44.916 keV (E2, radiative half-life 1.26×10^-7 s)

## Context

This paper surveys the nuclear physics opportunities of the Gamma Factory proposal, which was originally proposed by Krasny (2015) and developed within the CERN Physics Beyond Colliders program (Jaeckel et al., 2020). It extends earlier discussions of nuclear quantum optics using X-ray free electron lasers (Buervenich, 2006) and laser spectroscopy in ion storage rings (Nortershauser, 2015; Ullmann, 2017). The paper identifies potential applications including spectroscopy of nuclear transitions on par with atomic laser spectroscopy, direct measurements of astrophysical S-factors, precision parity violation studies, and production of medical isotopes and nuclear isomers, while noting that key components such as production and storage of partially stripped ions have already been demonstrated at the SPS and LHC.
