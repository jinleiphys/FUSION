# Double Beta Decay: Theory, Experiment, and Implications

## Key claim

Neutrinoless double beta decay has not been observed, and the upper limits on its rate can be interpreted as independent limits on each possible decay amplitude, including the effective Majorana neutrino mass, provided one dismisses as unnatural the possibility of complete cancellation between amplitudes. The observation of the decay would necessarily imply that neutrinos have a nonvanishing Majorana mass, independent of the actual mechanism mediating the decay.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for double beta decay, including the particle-physics formalism relating decay rates to neutrino mass parameters and right-handed current couplings, and the nuclear-structure problem of evaluating nuclear matrix elements using the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and shell model methods. Experimental results from geochemical, radiochemical, and direct counting experiments are compiled, with half-life limits and derived constraints on the effective Majorana neutrino mass ⟨m_ν⟩ and the Majoron coupling ⟨g_ν,χ⟩.

## Context

This chapter reviews the field of double beta decay as it stood around 2000, extending the classic reviews by Primakoff and Rosen, Haxton and Stephenson, and Doi, Kotani, and Takasugi. It connects the search for neutrinoless double beta decay to neutrino oscillation experiments and direct mass measurements, noting that the 0νββ sensitivity to ⟨m_ν⟩ ∼ 0.2–0.4 eV was already competitive with other probes of neutrino mass. The paper enables future experimental programs by clarifying the relationship between measured half-life limits and fundamental parameters, while emphasizing that the uncertainty in nuclear matrix elements, particularly for the 0ν mode, remains the dominant limitation in extracting neutrino mass bounds.
