# In-medium thermal conductivity and diffusion coefficients of a hot hadronic gas mixture

> The relativistic kinetic theory approach has been employed to study four well-known transport coefficients that characterize heat flow and diffusion for the case of a hot mixture constituting of nucleons and pions. Medium effects on the cross-section for binary collisions ($Nπ$,$ππ$) have been taken into consideration by incorporating self-energy corrections to modify the propagator of the exchanged $Δ$ baryon in $Nπ$ interaction and the $ρ$ and $σ$ meson propagators for the case of $ππ$ interaction. The temperature dependence of the four coefficients have been investigated for several values of the baryon chemical potential.

## Key claim

The paper claims that medium effects on binary collision cross-sections (ππ and πN), incorporated via self-energy corrections to the propagators of exchanged resonances (Δ, ρ, σ), significantly modify the temperature and baryon chemical potential dependence of thermal conductivity and diffusion coefficients in a hot hadronic gas mixture, compared to calculations using vacuum cross-sections.

## Method

The relativistic kinetic theory approach with the relaxation time approximation was used to derive expressions for four transport coefficients (thermal conductivity λ, Dufour coefficient D'_T, diffusion coefficient D_F, and thermal diffusion coefficient D_T) for a binary mixture of nucleons and pions. Medium-modified cross-sections for πN scattering were obtained by incorporating self-energy corrections to the Δ baryon propagator, while ππ scattering cross-sections were modified using self-energy corrections to the ρ and σ meson propagators, all evaluated using thermal field theory methods. The relaxation times for pions and nucleons were then computed from these in-medium cross-sections and used to evaluate the transport coefficients via momentum integrals over equilibrium distribution functions.

- The decay width of the ρ meson at μ_π = 80 MeV increases from approximately 150 MeV at T = 100 MeV to about 350 MeV at T = 180 MeV (Fig. 3, decay_width.eps)
- The in-medium πN cross-section at T = 150 MeV, μ_π = 80 MeV, μ_N = 500 MeV is significantly suppressed relative to the vacuum cross-section across the center-of-mass energy range 1.1–1.6 GeV (Fig. 4a)
- The in-medium ππ cross-section at T = 150 MeV, μ_π = 80 MeV shows suppression relative to vacuum across the center-of-mass energy range 0.3–1.2 GeV (Fig. 4b)
- The mean relaxation time of pions at μ_N = 500 MeV and μ_π = 80 MeV decreases from approximately 8 fm/c at T = 100 MeV to about 2 fm/c at T = 180 MeV when using in-medium cross-sections (Fig. 5a)
- The mean relaxation time of nucleons at μ_N = 500 MeV and μ_π = 80 MeV decreases from approximately 6 fm/c at T = 100 MeV to about 2 fm/c at T = 180 MeV when using in-medium cross-sections (Fig. 5a)
- The thermal conductivity λ at μ_N = 500 MeV and μ_π = 80 MeV increases from approximately 0.005 GeV²/fm at T = 100 MeV to about 0.025 GeV²/fm at T = 180 MeV when using in-medium cross-sections (Fig. 6a)
- The Dufour coefficient D'_T at μ_N = 500 MeV and μ_π = 80 MeV increases from approximately 0.001 fm⁻¹ at T = 100 MeV to about 0.008 fm⁻¹ at T = 180 MeV when using in-medium cross-sections (Fig. 7a)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of transport coefficients in hot hadronic matter, which had primarily focused on shear and bulk viscosities using vacuum cross-sections (Prakash 1993, Itakura 2007, Davesne 1996) or chiral perturbation theory (Dobado 2002, Chen 2007). The authors had previously incorporated medium effects on ππ cross-sections to study viscosities and thermal conductivity of a pure pion gas (Sukanya 2010, 2012, 2013) and on πN cross-sections to study viscous coefficients of a hadronic gas mixture (Utsab 2016). The present work enables more realistic dissipative hydrodynamic simulations for heavy-ion collisions at FAIR energies or the RHIC Beam Energy Scan program, where baryon chemical potential is significant and baryon number conservation plays an important role in thermal and diffusion transport.
