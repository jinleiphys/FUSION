# Particle-number projection method in time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory: Properties of reaction products

> Background: The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory has been successful in describing low-energy heavy ion collisions. Recently, we have shown that multinucleon transfer processes can be reasonably described in the TDHF theory combined with the particle-number projection technique. Purpose: In this work, we propose a theoretical framework to analyze properties of reaction products in TDHF calculations. Methods: TDHF calculation in three-dimensional Cartesian grid representation combined with particle number projection method. Results: We develop a theoretical framework to calculate expectation values of operators in the TDHF wave function after collision with the particle-number projection. To show how our method works in practice, the method is applied to $^{24}$O+$^{16}$O collisions for two quantities, angular momentum and excitation energy. The analyses revealed following features of the reaction: The nucleon removal proceeds gently, leaving small values of angular momentum and excitation energy in nucleon removed nuclei. Contrarily, nuclei receiving nucleons show expectation values of angular momentum and excitation energy which increase as the incident energy increases. Conclusions: We have developed a formalism to analyze properties of fragment nuclei in the TDHF theory combined with the particle-number projection technique. The method will be useful for microscopic investigations of reaction mechanisms in low-energy heavy ion collisions as well as for evaluating effects of particle evaporation on multinucleon transfer cross sections.

## Key claim

The paper develops a formalism to calculate expectation values of one- and two-body operators in particle-number projected TDHF wave functions after collision, but the illustrative application to \(^{24}\)O+\(^{16}\)O collisions ignores pairing correlations and is restricted to the Skyrme SLyIII.0.8 parameter set.

## Method

The authors extend the particle-number projection (PNP) technique to compute expectation values of operators (angular momentum and excitation energy) for fragment nuclei in TDHF calculations. They apply the method to \(^{24}\)O+\(^{16}\)O collisions using a three-dimensional Cartesian grid representation with 0.8-fm mesh spacing, the Skyrme SLyIII.0.8 interaction, and a spatial division into spheres of radius 16 fm around each fragment.

## Key numbers

- Fusion occurs at a distance of closest approach \(d = 9.4\) fm for head-on collisions (\(b=0\)), corresponding to \(E_\mathrm{lab} \sim 24.5\) MeV; for non-central collisions at \(E_\mathrm{lab} = 2\), 4, and 8 MeV/nucleon, fusion occurs at \(d = 8.7\), 8.3, and 7.5 fm, respectively.
- The PNP integral over \(\theta\) uses \(M = 30\) grid points, which is sufficient for the \(^{24}\)O+\(^{16}\)O system.
- Spatial grid points of \(N_x \times N_y \times N_z = 90 \times 80 \times 26\) are used with 0.8-fm mesh spacing; initial nuclei are placed 32 fm apart.
- At the highest incident energy of \(E_\mathrm{lab} = 8\) MeV/nucleon, the proton transfer probability is maximum around \(d = 8\) fm.
- For nucleon removal channels, expectation values of angular momentum are very small; for nucleon addition channels, angular momentum values increase with incident energy and are roughly proportional to the square root of energy, with one-neutron addition to \(^{16}\)O carrying angular momentum \(l_z = R m v_\mathrm{rel}\) where \(R = r_0 A^{1/3}\) with \(r_0 = 1.2\) fm and \(A = 16\).

## Context

This work extends the particle-number projection technique introduced by Simenel (2009) for calculating transfer probabilities in TDHF to also compute expectation values of operators in projected wave functions. The method enables microscopic investigation of reaction mechanisms in low-energy heavy ion collisions and provides inputs (excitation energy and angular momentum) for statistical models of particle evaporation, which are needed to compare with measured multinucleon transfer cross sections from experiments such as those by Corradi and collaborators.
