# Nuclear symmetry energy effects on neutron stars properties

> We construct a class of nuclear equations of state based on a schematic potential model, that originates from the work of Prakash et. al. \cite{Prakash-88}, which reproduce the results of most microscopic calculations. The equations of state are used as input for solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations for corresponding neutron stars. The potential part contribution of the symmetry energy to the total energy is parameterized in a generalized form both for low and high values of the baryon density. Special attention is devoted to the construction of the symmetry energy in order to reproduce the results of most microscopic calculations of dense nuclear matter. The obtained nuclear equations of state are applied for the systematic study of the global properties of a neutron star (masses, radii and composition). The calculated masses and radii of the neutron stars are plotted as a function of the potential part parameters of the symmetry energy. A linear relation between these parameters, the radius and the maximum mass of the neutron star is obtained. In addition, a linear relation between the radius and the derivative of the symmetry energy near the saturation density is found. We also address on the problem of the existence of correlation between the pressure near the saturation density and the radius.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a generalized parameterization of the potential part of the nuclear symmetry energy, using separate functional forms for densities below and above saturation, can reproduce the results of microscopic calculations of dense matter (specifically the A18+delta+UIX* variational calculation of Akmal (1998)), and that this parameterization yields linear relations between the potential parameters and neutron star properties such as radius and maximum mass.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state for neutron-rich matter using a schematic potential model based on Prakash (1988), where the symmetric nuclear matter energy is parameterized with a density-dependent potential and the symmetry energy is separated into kinetic and potential contributions. The potential part of the symmetry energy was parameterized with two forms: a simple power law F(u)=u^c, and a more flexible two-parameter form F(u)=u^{c1} for u<=1 and F(u)=u^{c2}e^{1-u}+(u-1)(c1+1-c2) for u>=1. These equations of state were used as input to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for neutron star structure, assuming beta-stable matter composed of neutrons, protons, and electrons at zero temperature.

## Key numbers

* Saturation density n_s = 0.16 fm^{-3}, with energy per baryon E/A - m_N c^2 = -16 MeV and incompressibility K_0 = 240 MeV
* Symmetry energy at saturation S_0 = 30 MeV, with kinetic contribution 13 u^{2/3} and potential contribution 17 F(u) (in MeV)
* For the simple parameterization F(u)=u^c, the linear fit for radius at M=1.4 M_sun is R_{1.4} = 10.52114 + 3.56746 c (in km), with c ranging from 0.4 to 1.5
* For the simple parameterization, maximum mass fit: M_max = 1.87442 + 0.12344 c (in M_sun)
* For the two-parameter form reproducing the A18+delta+UIX* case: c1=0.77, c2=1.09 yields R_{1.4}=12.76 km, R_max=10.59 km, M_max=1.974 M_sun
* Pressure at saturation for the A18+delta+UIX* case: P_s = 3.48107 MeV fm^{-3}
* Equilibrium proton fraction at saturation: x_s approximately 0.04

## Context

This work extends the schematic potential model of Prakash (1988) by introducing a more flexible parameterization of the symmetry energy that can reproduce the density dependence found in microscopic variational calculations such as Akmal (1998) (the A18+delta+UIX* case). The linear relations found between neutron star radius and the derivative of the symmetry energy near saturation density connect to the work of Lattimer (2001) on correlations between pressure and radius. The results enable systematic study of how the uncertain density dependence of the symmetry energy affects neutron star observables, and provide analytic relations that could allow radius measurements to constrain the symmetry energy derivative.
