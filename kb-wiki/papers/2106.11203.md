# Antistars or antimatter cores in mirror neutron stars?

> The oscillation of the neutron $n$ into mirror neutron $n'$, its partner from dark mirror sector, can gradually transform an ordinary neutron star into a mixed star consisting in part of mirror dark matter. The implications of the reverse process taking place in the mirror neutron stars depend on the sign of baryon asymmetry in mirror sector. Namely, if it is negative, as predicted by certain baryogenesis scenarios, then $\bar{n}'-\bar{n}$ transitions create a core of our antimatter gravitationally trapped in the mirror star interior. The annihilation of accreted gas on such antimatter cores could explain the origin $γ$-source candidates, with unusual spectrum compatible to baryon-antibaryon annihilation, recently identified in the Fermi LAT catalog, In addition, some part of this antimatter escaping after the mergers of mirror neutron stars can produce the flux of cosmic antihelium and also heavier antinuclei which are hunted in the AMS-02 experiment.

## Key claim

The oscillation of neutrons into mirror neutrons can gradually transform ordinary neutron stars into mixed stars containing mirror dark matter, and the reverse process in mirror neutron stars could create antimatter cores that explain certain gamma-ray source candidates from the Fermi LAT catalog, provided the mirror sector has a negative baryon asymmetry as predicted by certain co-baryogenesis scenarios.

## Method

The paper analyzes the implications of neutron-mirror neutron ($n-n'$) oscillations in dense nuclear matter, calculating the conversion rate using the Schrödinger equation with an effective Hamiltonian that includes the medium-induced energy splitting from coherent scattering. The production rate of mirror neutrons via the $nn \to n'n$ scattering process is estimated accounting for Pauli blocking, and the resulting evolution equations are integrated to determine the fraction of mirror baryons produced in neutron stars over cosmic timescales. The same formalism is then applied in reverse to mirror neutron stars, where $\bar{n}' \to \bar{n}$ transitions would create antimatter cores.

## Key numbers

- The $n-n'$ mixing mass can be as large as $\epsilon \sim 10^{-15}$ eV, corresponding to a characteristic oscillation time $\tau = 1/\epsilon$ of a few seconds for free neutrons.
- The effective conversion time for a neutron star is $\tau_\epsilon = (a_R / \epsilon_{15}^2) \times (M / 1.5 M_\odot)^{2/3} \times 10^{15}$ yr, where $\epsilon_{15} = \epsilon \times 10^{15}$ eV$^{-1}$ and $a_R \simeq 1$.
- For $\epsilon \sim 10^{-15}$ eV, the fraction of mirror baryons produced in a 10 Gyr old neutron star is $N'/(N+N') = 10^{-5} \times (\epsilon_{15}^2 / a_R) \times (1.5 M_\odot / M)^{2/3}$.
- The energy production rate from $n-n'$ conversion is $\dot{\mathcal{E}} \simeq \epsilon_{15}^2 \times (M / 1.5 M_\odot) \times 10^{31}$ erg/s.
- The mirror photosphere temperature is estimated as $T = \epsilon_{15}^{1/2} \times (x \kappa M / 1.5 M_\odot)^{1/4} \times (1\ \text{km} / R_\text{ph})^{1/2} \times 10^6$ K.
- The mass loss rate from $n-n'$ conversion is $\dot{M}/M \simeq -\epsilon_{15}^2 \times 10^{-16}$ yr$^{-1}$.
- Observational data on the Hulse-Taylor pulsar B1913+16 yield an upper bound $\epsilon < 10^{-13}$ eV from orbital period decay.
- Surface temperature measurements of old pulsars like PSR J2144-3933 ($T_\text{surf} < 4 \times 10^4$ K) could approach $\epsilon < 10^{-16}$ eV.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous analysis in Berezhiani (2021) on $n-n'$ oscillations in neutron stars, and builds on the co-baryogenesis models of Berezhiani (2016, 2018) that predict opposite signs of baryon asymmetry in ordinary and mirror sectors. The paper directly addresses the 14 gamma-ray source candidates identified by Dupourque (2021) in the Fermi LAT catalog with spectra compatible to baryon-antibaryon annihilation, and connects to the search for cosmic antihelium and heavier antinuclei in the AMS-02 experiment. The scenario enables future observational tests through gamma-ray surveys, cosmic ray antinuclei detection, and pulsar timing measurements.
