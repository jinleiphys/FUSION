# Hydrodynamic description of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Recent theoretical developments of relativistic hydrodynamics applied to ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions are briefly reviewed. In particular, the concept of a formal gradient expansion is discussed, which is a tool to compare different hydrodynamic models with underlying microscopic theories.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that relativistic hydrodynamics can be treated as an effective theory describing the approach of physical systems toward local equilibrium, with the formal gradient expansion serving as a tool to compare different hydrodynamic models with underlying microscopic theories. The abstract's claim of a "brief review" glosses over the paper's specific focus on the distinction between hydrodynamic and non-hydrodynamic modes, and the condition that genuine hydrodynamic behavior is only revealed when the modeling is not sensitive to transient modes.

## Method

The paper reviews the standard model of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, separating the space-time evolution into three stages: an initial out-of-equilibrium stage lasting about 1-2 fm/c, a hydrodynamic expansion stage lasting about 10 fm/c for central collisions of large nuclei, and a freeze-out stage. It introduces the formal gradient expansion as a method to compare different formulations of relativistic hydrodynamics, including perfect-fluid, Navier-Stokes, and anisotropic hydrodynamics, and discusses how these models relate to microscopic theories such as AdS/CFT correspondence and kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation.

## Key numbers

- The first (out-of-equilibrium) stage of heavy-ion collisions lasts for about 1-2 fm/c after the initial impact
- The second (hydrodynamic expansion) stage lasts for about 10 fm/c for central collisions of large nuclei
- The paper discusses the Landau model (1953) and Bjorken model (1982) as the two famous perfect-fluid hydrodynamic models of particle production

## Context

This review extends the line of work on relativistic hydrodynamics applied to heavy-ion collisions, building on the classical text by Landau and Lifshitz (fluid mechanics) and the modern treatment by Rezzolla and Zanotti (2013). It connects to recent reviews by Romatschke (2009), Jeon and Heinz (2015), Jaiswal and Roy (2016), Alqahtani et al. (2017), and Yan (2017), and discusses the insights from AdS/CFT correspondence (Casalderrey-Solana et al., 2011; DeWolfe et al., 2013; Heller, 2016) and kinetic theory. The paper enables further work on the concept of "hydrodynamization" as a replacement for the earlier idea of early thermalization, and provides a framework for comparing different hydrodynamic formulations through the gradient expansion.
