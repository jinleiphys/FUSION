# The Quark Propagator in the NJL Model in a self-consistent 1/Nc Expansion

> The quark propagator is calculated in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model in a self-consistent 1/Nc-expansion at next-to-leading order. The calculations are carried out iteratively in Euclidean space. The chiral quark condensate and its dependence on temperature and chemical potential is calculated directly and compared with the mean-field results. In the chiral limit, we find a second-order phase transition at finite temperature and zero chemical potential, in agreement with universality arguments. At zero temperature and finite chemical potential, the phase transition is first order. In comparison with the mean-field results, the critical temperature and chemical potential are slightly reduced. We determine spectral functions from the Euclidean propagators by employing the Maximum-Entropy-Method (MEM). Thereby quark and meson masses are estimated and decay channels identified. For testing this method, we also apply it to evaluate perturbative spectral functions, which can be calculated directly in Minkowski space. In most cases we find that MEM is able to reproduce the rough features of the spectral functions, but not the details.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a self-consistent next-to-leading-order (NLO) 1/Nc expansion of the NJL model, derived from a Φ-derivable (thermodynamically consistent) approach, yields a second-order chiral phase transition at finite temperature and zero chemical potential in the chiral limit, in agreement with universality arguments; however, the intermediate RPA mesons that enter the NLO gap equation are not massless, so the correct low-temperature behavior of the quark condensate (the T² term from chiral perturbation theory) is not reproduced.

## Method

The authors solve the NLO gap equation for the quark propagator iteratively in Euclidean (Matsubara) space, using a Φ-derivable (2PI) expansion of the thermodynamic potential truncated at NLO in 1/Nc. The NLO self-energy consists of a quark loop dressed by an RPA meson propagator built from the self-consistent quark propagator, leading to nonlocal, energy- and momentum-dependent dressing functions A, B, and C. All loops are regularized with sharp 3-momentum cutoffs (Λ = 664.3 MeV for quarks, Λ_M = 500 MeV for meson loops), and the chiral quark condensate is computed directly from the Euclidean propagator trace. Spectral functions are extracted from the Euclidean propagators using the Maximum-Entropy Method (MEM) to estimate quark and meson masses and identify decay channels.

## Key numbers

*   Quark condensate in leading order (Hartree): ⟨ūu⟩^(1/3) = −250.8 MeV, corresponding to a constituent quark mass m_H = 300 MeV.
*   Pion mass in Hartree + RPA: m_π ≈ 137 MeV (pole fit from Matsubara data), consistent with the input vacuum value of 135.0 MeV.
*   Intermediate RPA pion mass in the NLO scheme (at T = 10 MeV, μ = 0): m_π ≈ 340 MeV from a pole fit.
*   Sigma meson mass in Hartree + RPA: m_σ ≈ 670 MeV from a pole fit.
*   Intermediate RPA sigma and pion masses in the chiral limit at the critical temperature T_c = 131 MeV: m_π = m_σ ≈ 270 MeV.
*   Critical temperature for chiral restoration in the chiral limit at μ = 0: reduced in NLO relative to the Hartree result; the NLO value is T_c = 131 MeV.
*   Critical chemical potential for chiral restoration in the chiral limit at T = 0: slightly reduced in NLO relative to the Hartree result.
*   Model parameters (fixed from leading order): Λ = 664.3 MeV, GΛ² = 2.06, m_0 = 5.0 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the NJL model beyond the standard mean-field (Hartree) and random-phase approximation (RPA) by including the back-reaction of mesons on the quark propagator via a self-consistent 1/Nc expansion. It corrects earlier simplified NLO attempts (e.g., DSTL95, FB96, Oer00) that neglected nonlocal self-energy contributions and found a first-order phase transition at finite temperature, in contradiction with universality arguments (Pisarski and Wilczek, 1984). The self-consistent scheme enables a meaningful study of the phase transition, but the authors note that the perturbative 1/Nc approach (e.g., Oer00, BBRV08) correctly reproduces the low-temperature T² term of the quark condensate from chiral perturbation theory (Gasser and Leutwyler, 1987), whereas the self-consistent scheme does not. The MEM analysis of spectral functions is tested against perturbative Minkowski-space results and is found to reproduce rough features but not details.
