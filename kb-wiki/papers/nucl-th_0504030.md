# Antiproton-nucleus potentials from global fits to antiprotonic X-rays and radiochemical data

> We report on global fits of optical-model parameters to 90 data points for $\bar p$ X-rays and 17 data points of radiochemical data put together. With the help of separate fits to the two kinds of data it is possible to determine phenomenologically the radial region where the absorption of antiprotons takes place and to obtain neutron densities which represent the average behaviour over the periodic table. A finite-range attractive and absorptive $\bar p$-nuclear isoscalar potential fits the data well. Self-consistent dynamical calculations within the RMF model demonstrate that the polarization of the nucleus by the {\it atomic} antiproton is negligible.

# Antiproton-nucleus potentials from global fits to antiprotonic X-rays and radiochemical data

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a finite-range attractive and absorptive isoscalar antiproton-nucleus optical potential fits the combined data well, but the isovector component of the potential cannot be determined from global fits and is consistent with zero. The authors caution that the extrapolation of the potential into nuclear-matter densities is highly model dependent, and that well-defined nuclear bound states of antiprotons are unlikely due to large expected widths.

## Method
The authors performed global fits of optical-model parameters to 90 data points from antiprotonic X-ray measurements (strong-interaction level shifts and widths) and 17 data points from radiochemical absorption-ratio measurements, using a Klein-Gordon equation with a finite-range folding model (range parameter beta = 0.85 fm). Neutron density distributions were parameterized by the rms radius difference r_n - r_p, expressed as a linear function of (N-Z)/A, with three shape models (skin, halo, and average) tested. Self-consistent dynamical calculations within the relativistic mean-field (RMF) model were also performed to check whether nuclear polarization by the atomic antiproton affects the results.

## Key numbers
- 90 X-ray data points and 17 radiochemical data points were used in the global fits, for a total of 107 data points.
- The best-fit optical potential parameters (finite-range folding model, beta = 0.85 fm) are: Re b_0 = 1.3 +/- 0.1 fm, Im b_0 = 1.9 +/- 0.1 fm.
- The isovector parameter Im b_1 was found to be consistent with zero at the best-fit minima for the acceptable neutron-density models.
- The reduction parameter epsilon (deviation from G-parity couplings) needed to fit atomic data ranged from 0.15 (for Ca-40) to 0.35 (for Pb-208).
- Dynamical RMF calculations showed polarization effects of less than 1 eV for the 3d state in O-16, the 4f state in Ca-40, and the 9k state in Pb-208, confirming that static calculations are adequate.
- For deeply bound 1s and 2p states in Ca, dynamical vs. static differences were a few eV, deemed experimentally unobservable.
- The radial region where most antiproton absorption occurs was confirmed phenomenologically to be about 2.5 fm beyond the charge half-density radius, consistent with the theoretical prediction of Wycech et al. (1996).
- Extrapolated to nuclear center, the potential is attractive at about 110 MeV depth with an absorptive part near 160 MeV depth.

## Context
This work extends previous global analyses of antiprotonic atom data by Friedman, Gal, and Batty (1995, 1997), which used about 50 data points, by incorporating the larger and more accurate data set from the PS209 collaboration (Trzcinska et al., 2001) and radiochemical data from Lubinski et al. (1998) and Schmidt et al. (1999). The paper also builds on the finite-range folding approach introduced by Friedman and Gal (2004). The radiochemical method was originally developed by Wycech et al. (1996), who predicted that absorption occurs about 2.5 fm beyond the charge half-density radius. The present results enable improved determination of average neutron densities across the periodic table and confirm that static (non-dynamical) calculations of antiprotonic atoms are valid, in contrast to the large polarization effects found for antiproton-nuclear states by Mares et al. (2005).
