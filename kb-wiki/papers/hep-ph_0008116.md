# Couplings of a Light Dilaton and Violations of the Equivalence Principle

> Experimental discovery of the dilaton would provide strong evidence for string theory. A light dilaton could show up in current tests of the inverse square law for gravity at sub-millimeter distances. In the large extra dimension scenario, Kaluza-Klein excitations of the dilaton can also contribute to the cooling of supernovae. In order to quantify these effects we compute the couplings of a low energy dilaton to matter. These predominantly arise from the fundamental dilaton coupling to the gluon field strength, which receives a sizable enhancement from QCD scaling. We show that detection of the dilaton will give a direct measurement of the QCD coupling constant at the string scale. Particular attention is paid to the size of equivalence principle violating dilaton couplings.

## Key claim

The dilaton's coupling to matter is dominated by its coupling to gluons, which receives a large enhancement factor \(K = (4\pi/9)/\alpha_3(\Lambda_s)\) from QCD scaling; this enhancement applies to both the equivalence-principle-conserving and equivalence-principle-violating parts of the force, contrary to earlier claims that the violating part was only of order unity.

## Method

The authors start from a string-inspired effective Lagrangian at the string scale \(\Lambda_s\) containing the dilaton, gluons, and quarks (three light flavors plus heavy quarks). They use the operator product expansion and renormalization-group techniques of Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1978) to rewrite the dilaton coupling in terms of the divergence of the scale current and quark mass operators, then integrate out heavy quarks to obtain the low-energy coupling. The nucleon and atomic matrix elements are evaluated using chiral perturbation theory to leading order in quark masses, with the strange quark content of the nucleon determined from the pion-nucleon sigma term and hyperon masses.

## Key numbers

* Enhancement factor for dilaton coupling to matter: \(K = (4\pi/9)/\alpha_3(\Lambda_s)\); for the Standard Model with \(\Lambda_s = M_{PL}\), \(K \simeq 73\); for the MSSM with \(\Lambda_s = M_{PL}\), \(K \simeq 38\).
* Isospin-conserving nucleon matrix element: \(\hat\chi_+ = (2.7 \pm 0.8) \times 10^{-1}\).
* Isospin-violating nucleon matrix element: \(\hat\chi_- = -(1.5 \pm 0.5) \times 10^{-3}\).
* For the Standard Model with \(\Lambda_s = M_{PL}\), the dilaton force between nucleons is approximately \(2.6 \times 10^3\) times stronger than gravity, and the equivalence-principle-violating component is approximately 8 times stronger than gravity.
* For two large extra dimensions with \(\Lambda_s = 10\) TeV, the enhancement factor is \(K_2 \simeq 15\), and the supernova cooling constraint on the size of the extra dimensions becomes \(R < 2.5 \times 10^{-4}\) mm (a factor \(\sqrt{8}\) stronger than the bound from graviton emission alone).

## Context

This work extends the earlier calculations of dilaton couplings by Taylor and Veneziano (1988) and Ellis et al. (1989), correcting both the enhancement factor (using \(b_0^\ell\) instead of \(b_0^s\)) and finding that equivalence-principle-violating terms receive the same \(1/\alpha_3(\Lambda_s)\) enhancement as the leading coupling. The results enable quantitative predictions for sub-millimeter tests of gravity and for supernova cooling rates in large-extra-dimension scenarios, and show that dilaton detection would provide a direct measurement of the QCD coupling at the string scale.
