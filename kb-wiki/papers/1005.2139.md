# Magnetic Domains in Magnetar Matter as an Engine for Soft Gamma-ray Repeaters and Anomalous X-ray Pulsars

> Magnetars have been suggested as the most promising site for the origin of observed soft gamma-ray repeaters (SGRs) and anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs). In this work we investigate the possibility that SGRs and AXPs might be observational evidence for a magnetic phase separation in magnetars. We study magnetic domain formation as a new mechanism for SGRs and AXPs in which magnetar-matter separates into two phases containing different flux densities. We identify the parameter space in matter density and magnetic field strength at which there is an instability for magnetic domain formation. We conclude that such instabilities will likely occur in the deep outer crust for the magnetic Baym, Pethick, and Sutherland (BPS) model and in the inner crust and core for magnetars described in relativistic Hartree theory. Moreover, we estimate that the energy released by the onset of this instability is comparable with the energy emitted by SGRs.

# Magnetic Domains in Magnetar Matter as an Engine for Soft Gamma-ray Repeaters and Anomalous X-ray Pulsars

## Key claim

Magnetic domain formation in magnetar matter, driven by the Schoenberg effect where the differential susceptibility exceeds 1/4π, can provide a new mechanism for SGRs and AXPs, but only in specific density and magnetic field regimes: the deep outer crust for the magnetic BPS model, and the inner crust and core for relativistic Hartree theory. The authors hedge that this proposal "warrants further investigation" and that detailed dynamical numerical studies are needed.

## Method

The authors calculated the magnetization and differential magnetic susceptibility for magnetar matter using three different equations of state: an ideal n-p-e gas, relativistic Hartree mean field theory (including effective baryon masses from σ, ω, and ρ meson exchange), and the magnetic Baym-Pethick-Sutherland (BPS) model for matter below neutron drip density. They identified regions in the density-magnetic field parameter space where the differential susceptibility η exceeds 1/4π, indicating instability to magnetic domain formation, and estimated the resulting magnetostrictive stresses, crustal strain, and energy release.

## Key numbers

- Neutron drip density: ρ_drip ≈ 4.3 × 10^11 g/cm³
- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_nuc ≈ 2.8 × 10^14 g/cm³
- For the ideal npe gas, magnetic domains cannot form below density ~10^13 g/cm³ for magnetic fields of ~10^14 – 10^15 G
- For relativistic Hartree theory, domain formation occurs above density ρ ~ 10^14 g/cm³ and field strength B ~ 10^16 G; no domain formation for B ≲ 10^18 G
- For the magnetic BPS model, magnetic domains cannot form if density is less than ρ ~ 5 × 10^10 g/cm³ for a surface magnetic field of B ~ 10^14 – 10^15 G; domain formation occurs at low density ≳ 10^11 g/cm³ and field ≳ 10^13 G
- Estimated spacing between domain interfaces: Δz ~ 100 m × (g/10^14 cm/s²)^(-1) × (μ_e/2)^(-1) × (ε_e/5)^(-1) × (B/10^15 G)
- Horizontal magnetic field variation: ΔB/B ~ 50 × (B/10^15 G) × 1/(ε_e² – 1)
- Ohmic dissipation timescale: τ_D ~ σ A / 4π c² ~ 10^4 yr
- Cracking timescale in outer crust: τ_C ~ Δz / v_s ≈ 0.1 ms
- Released elastic energy: ΔE_D ~ 6 × 10^42 χ² θ_m (R_D/10^4 cm)² (B/10^15 G)² erg
- Length change due to domain formation: δl/l ≈ 0.02, corresponding to 2 m length change for a 100 m characteristic domain size

## Context

This work extends the magnetic domain concept from Blandford and Hernquist (1982) for magnetic white dwarfs and the crackling noise model of Kondratyev (2002) for magnetic avalanches in neutron star crusts. It contrasts with the starquake model of Thompson and Duncan (1995) and the statistical earthquake analogies of Cheng et al. (1996) by proposing that magnetic domain formation and sudden readjustment, rather than purely crustal fractures, can drive SGR giant flares and AXP outbursts. The paper enables future dynamical simulations of domain evolution in neutron star crusts and suggests that gravitational wave detection from SGRs could verify the model.
