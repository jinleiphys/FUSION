# Electromagnetic Meson Production in the Nucleon Resonance Region

> Recent experimental and theoretical advances in investigating electromagnetic meson production reactions in the nucleon resonance region are reviewed.

## Key claim

This review paper summarizes the status of experimental and theoretical work on electromagnetic meson production in the nucleon resonance region, but it does not present a single new experimental result or theoretical prediction; instead it surveys the field and describes the need for close collaboration between experiment and theory to resolve long-standing problems such as the "missing resonance problem" and the large uncertainties in partial decay widths listed by the Particle Data Group.

## Method

The authors review experimental facilities (JLab, MAMI-B, MIT-Bates, GRAAL, LEGS, LEPS, ELSA) and their detector systems, then present a unified derivation of the relativistic quantum field theory formalism for single-meson electroproduction, including the one-photon exchange mechanism and the expression for the differential cross section in terms of the hadron current and kinematic factors. They then discuss the phenomenological reaction models used to extract resonance parameters from data, focusing on analyses of pion and eta production.

## Key numbers

- The CLAS detector achieves timing resolutions of Delta T = 120-200 psec (rms) depending on scintillator bar length (30 cm to 350 cm).
- The Hall A HRS spectrometers have a momentum resolution of Delta p/p ~ 2 x 10^-4.
- The MAMI-B microtron reaches a maximum beam energy of 850 MeV.
- The GRAAL facility produces a laser backscattered polarized photon beam up to 1470 MeV energy.
- The LEGS facility produces a laser backscattered real photon beam with energy up to 470 MeV.
- The LEPS facility at SPring-8 produces a laser-backscattered polarized photon beam with energy up to 2.4 GeV.
- The ELSA accelerator reaches an energy of 3 GeV.
- The Hall C HMS reaches a maximum momentum of 7 GeV/c, while the SOS is limited to about 1.8 GeV/c.
- The well-determined empirical helicity amplitudes for the Delta(1232) to gamma N transition are about 40% larger than predictions from practically all hadron models.

## Context

This review extends the long history of hadron model development for nucleon resonances, including the constituent quark model refined by Isgur and Karl (1979) and Capstick and Isgur (1986), as well as bag models, chiral bag models, algebraic models, soliton models, Skyrme models, and covariant models based on Dyson-Schwinger equations. It addresses the "missing resonance problem" first noted by Koniuk and Isgur (1980), where some resonances predicted by the constituent quark model are not seen in pi N elastic scattering amplitude analyses. The paper enables future work by describing the experimental and theoretical infrastructure needed to resolve discrepancies between model predictions and empirical values, particularly for the Delta(1232) transition form factors where Lattice QCD calculations have recently been attempted by Alexandrou et al. (2003).
