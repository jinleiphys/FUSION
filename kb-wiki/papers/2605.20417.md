# Quantum Simulation of Gauge Theories for Particle and Nuclear Physics

> Lattice field theory, along with its algorithmic and hardware ecosystems, has been at the forefront of computational particle and nuclear physics. It continues to deliver impressive results on the hadronic spectrum, structure, decays, and reactions. Yet, this vigorous campaign has fallen short in addressing a range of problems involving dense matter and general dynamical phenomena. The reason is that such problems require an exponential scaling of computing time and space in system size. Quantum simulation, enabled by quantum-computing algorithms and hardware technology, promises a way forward by offering several polynomially efficient algorithms compared with their inefficient classical counterparts. Lattice gauge theorists have engaged in a multi-pronged program to leverage such new possibilities, and have steadily advanced the state of theory, algorithm, and hardware implementations and co-design. In this talk, I motivate the quantum-computational lattice-field-theory program; introduce the questions such a program is expected to address and the strategies it involves; report on recent progress; and end with a note on challenges and opportunities ahead.

## Key claim
Quantum simulation offers polynomially efficient algorithms for lattice gauge theory problems that are classically intractable due to exponential scaling of computing time and space in system size, but the full resource estimates for simulating QCD dynamics remain an open problem and depend on state preparation, observable estimation, and control of multiple error sources.

## Method
The paper reviews the quantum-computational lattice-field-theory program, which involves formulating gauge theories in a Hamiltonian framework (with truncation of infinite-dimensional gauge-field Hilbert spaces), developing digital and hybrid analog-digital quantum algorithms for time evolution, state preparation, and measurement, and implementing these algorithms on current quantum hardware platforms such as trapped ions, superconducting circuits, and Rydberg arrays. The author surveys theoretical questions about Hamiltonian formulations and truncation errors, algorithmic progress including product-formula and post-Trotter methods with resource estimates, and experimental demonstrations on quantum processors.

## Key numbers
- For a Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian with cutoff Lambda=4 on the irrep quantum number, the naive Pauli expansion for an SU(3) theory contains roughly 4^64 ~ 10^38 Pauli strings per plaquette.
- A representative second-order product-formula gate-count scaling for QCD is O(nu^(3/2) V^(3/2) T^(3/2) epsilon^(-1/2) Lambda polylog(V^(3/2)T^(3/2)Lambda epsilon^(-3/2))), where V is spatial volume, T evolution time, epsilon target accuracy, and nu number of implemented terms.
- For a spatial volume V = (10 fm)^3, lattice spacing 10^(-2) fm, cutoff Lambda = 10, and evolution time comparable to the lattice extent, the required number of qubits and T gates are roughly 10^11 and 10^49 to 10^50, respectively.
- Using a singular-value-decomposition-based algorithm, the number of terms to be implemented can be reduced by almost 9 orders of magnitude, resulting in a reduction of almost 14 orders of magnitude in the overall cost of the second-order product formula at fixed accuracy.
- A qubitization algorithm for the plaquette contribution scales roughly as O(N_c^4) terms (rather than exponentially in N_c^2), with gate complexity O(N_c^4 V T polylog(V T epsilon^(-1))).
- For the same representative setting, the qubitization time-evolution cost involves roughly the same qubit count (10^11) but only 10^27 T gates.
- Some of the largest QCD computations require fractions of exascale high-performance computing machines per year (amounting to roughly 10^24 floating-point operations per calculation) and petabytes of storage (amounting to roughly 10^16 classical bits).

## Context
This talk reviews the state of theory, algorithm, and hardware implementation for quantum simulation of gauge theories, extending the pioneering work of Byrnes and Yamamoto (2005) on SU(N_c) lattice gauge theories and of Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2012) on end-to-end algorithms for scattering in scalar field theory. The program aims to address classically intractable problems in particle and nuclear physics including finite-density phases of matter (where Monte-Carlo methods suffer a fermion sign problem), real-time evolution and equilibration, and dynamical structure functions. The work enables future fault-tolerant quantum computations of QCD dynamics, with current experimental highlights including quench dynamics on IBM processors, thermalization studies on trapped-ion systems at Duke University, and scattering simulations on IonQ and Quantinuum quantum computers.
