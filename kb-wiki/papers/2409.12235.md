# $ν$-point energy correletors with FastEEC: small-$x$ physics from LHC jets

> In recent years, energy correlators have emerged as a powerful tool for studying jet substructure, with promising applications such as probing the hadronization transition, analyzing the quark-gluon plasma, and improving the precision of top quark mass measurements. The projected $N$-point correlator measures correlations between $N$ final-state particles by tracking the largest separation between them, showing a scaling behavior related to DGLAP splitting functions. These correlators can be analytically continued in $N$, commonly referred to as $ν$-correlators, allowing access to non-integer moments of the splitting functions. Of particular interest is the $ν\to 0$ limit, where the small momentum fraction behavior of the splitting functions requires resummation. Originally, the computational complexity of evaluating $ν$-correlators for $M$ particles scaled as $2^{2M}$, making it impractical for real-world analyses. However, by using recursion, we reduce this to $M 2^M$, and through the FastEEC method of dynamically resolving subjets, $M$ is replaced by the number of subjets. This breakthrough enables, for the first time, the computation of $ν$-correlators for LHC data. In practice, limiting the number of subjets to 16 is sufficient to achieve percent-level precision, which we validate using known integer-$ν$ results and convergence tests for non-integer $ν$. We have implemented this in an update to FastEEC and conducted an initial study of power-law scaling in the perturbative regime as a function of $ν$, using CMS Open Data on jets. The results agree with DGLAP evolution, except at small $ν$, where the anomalous dimension saturates to a value that matches the BFKL anomalous dimension.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the computational complexity of evaluating ν-point energy correlators (ν-correlators) can be reduced from 2^(2M) to M 2^M through recursion, and further improved by replacing the number of particles M with the number of subjets via the FastEEC method, enabling for the first time the computation of ν-correlators on LHC data. The key hedge is that limiting the number of subjets to 16 is sufficient to achieve percent-level precision, which is validated using known integer-ν results and convergence tests for non-integer ν.

## Method

The authors implement a recursive algorithm for computing ν-correlators based on a new recursion relation (Eq. 6 in the paper) that expresses the weight function as a sum over subsets with alternating signs. They combine this with the FastEEC method of dynamically resolving subjets using the Cambridge/Aachen clustering algorithm, traversing the clustering tree recursively and restricting to contributions involving both branches of each splitting. The implementation limits the maximum number of subjets to 16 (8 per branch) and is applied to CMS 2011A Open Data on jets with transverse momenta between 500 and 550 GeV and pseudorapidity |η| < 1.9.

## Key numbers

- Computational complexity reduced from 2^(2M) to M 2^M, where M is the number of particles or subjets
- Limiting subjets to 16 achieves percent-level precision: approximately 1% relative error for N=3 and 2-3% for N=6 in the perturbative regime
- Computation time approximately 0.01 seconds per jet on a single core of an M2 MacBook Air with n_sub = 16
- Fit region for extracting scaling exponent chosen as 0.07 < R_L < 0.3 to avoid non-perturbative and jet boundary effects
- Effective scale μ* = 10 GeV used for α_s evaluation, with α_s(m_Z) = 0.118 and two-loop running giving α_s(μ*) = 0.178

## Context

This work extends the projected N-point energy correlators proposed by Chen et al. (2020) by enabling their analytic continuation to non-integer ν (ν-correlators) on real LHC data, which was previously impractical due to exponential computational scaling. The method builds on the FastEEC algorithm developed by Budhraja et al. (2024) for integer N-point correlators and enables access to small-x physics through the ν → 0 limit, where the anomalous dimension saturates to a value matching the BFKL anomalous dimension rather than diverging as the DGLAP anomalous dimension does. This opens the possibility of probing BFKL physics and the analytic Regge trajectory from collider data, complementing approaches such as the soft-drop momentum sharing variable which explicitly excludes the small-x region.
