# A Vision for the Science of Rare Isotopes

> The field of nuclear science has considerably advanced since its beginning just over a century ago. Today, the science of rare isotopes is on the cusp of a new era with theoretical and computing advances complementing experimental capabilities at new facilities internationally. In this article we present a vision for the science of rare isotope beams (RIBs). We do not attempt to cover the full breadth of the field, but rather provide a perspective and address a selection of topics that reflect our own interests and expertise. We focus in particular on systems near the drip lines, where one often finds nuclei that are referred to as "exotic," and where the role of the "nuclear continuum" is only just starting to be explored. An important aspect of this article is the attempt to highlight the crucial connections between nuclear structure and nuclear reactions required to fully interpret and leverage the rich data to be collected in the next years at RIB facilities. Further, we connect the efforts in structure and reactions to key questions of nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim

This paper presents a forward-looking perspective on rare isotope science, arguing that the field is on the cusp of a new era driven by experimental facilities like FRIB and theoretical advances in effective field theory and ab initio methods, but explicitly states it does not attempt to cover the full breadth of the field and instead reflects the specific topics and expertise of the authors.

## Method

The authors provide a review-style vision paper that surveys emergent phenomena in exotic nuclei near the drip lines, including shell evolution, halo structures, open quantum system effects, and clustering. They connect these structural phenomena to nuclear reactions and astrophysics, discussing how effective field theories and renormalization group methods bridge from QCD to observable nuclear properties, and highlight the interplay between continuum couplings and emergent phenomena that becomes critical near the drip lines.

## Key numbers

- FRIB will ultimately provide access to thousands of previously unstudied isotopes, and the planned FRIB 400 MeV/u energy upgrade will triple or even quadruple the number of isotopic chains for which the neutron drip line is experimentally accessible, extending knowledge from Z=10 (Ne) up to Z=30-60
- Ab initio computations of 208Pb were published in 2022, while in 2015 the state of the art was limited to closed shells below N,Z = 30
- The neutron drip line is confirmed up to Z=10, while the proton drip line is stringently defined as far as Z=13 and locally at heavier masses
- The number of isotopes experimentally accessible, currently estimated at about 3000, is expected to roughly double with new RIB facilities
- The one-neutron or one-proton separation energy defining the weakly bound region falls below approximately 1.0 MeV, about one order of magnitude smaller than the average binding energy per nucleon B(A,Z)/A approximately 8.5 MeV
- The largest isotope ever created, 294Og, has Z=118

## Context

This vision paper extends the line of work on rare isotope science reviewed by Thoennessen (2004) and builds on decades of developments in shell model calculations (Brown 2001, Caurier 2005), halo physics (Tanihata 2013, Jensen 2004), and open quantum system descriptions (Michel 2021, Okolowicz 2003). It connects to the recent operation of FRIB beginning in 2022 and other international facilities (RIBF in Japan, SPIRAL2 in France, FAIR in Germany, ARIEL at TRIUMF in Canada), and discusses how upcoming data will test nuclear forces derived from chiral effective field theory (Weinberg 1990, Epelbaum 2009) and constrain the nuclear equation of state relevant for neutron star physics (Thiel 2019, Lattimer 2021).
