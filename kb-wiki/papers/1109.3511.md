# Nuclear pasta in supernovae and neutron stars

> In supernova cores and neutron star crusts, nuclei with exotic shapes such as rod-like and slab-like nuclei are expected to exist. These nuclei are collectively called nuclear "pasta". For the past decades, existence of the pasta phases in the equilibrium state has been studied using various methods. Recently, the formation process of the pasta phases, which has been a long-standing problem, has been unveiled using molecular dynamics simulations. In this review, we first provide the astrophysical background of supernovae and neutron stars and overview the history of the study of the pasta phases. We then focus on the recent study on the formation process of the pasta phases. Finally, we discuss future important issues related to the pasta phases: their astrophysical evidence and consequences.

## Key claim

This review argues that nuclear pasta phases (non-spherical nuclei such as rods and slabs) are expected to exist in supernova cores and neutron star crusts, and that recent molecular dynamics simulations have finally unveiled the formation process of these phases, which had been a long-standing problem. The authors note that while existence of pasta phases in equilibrium has been confirmed by many nuclear models, their actual dynamical formation in astrophysical environments remained unclear until their own QMD simulations.

## Method

The authors review the use of quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations with a Pauli potential to study the dynamical formation of pasta phases, without assuming any particular nuclear shape. They simulate systems of neutrons, protons, and electrons in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using the Ewald summation method for Coulomb interactions and a QMD Hamiltonian with parameters fitted to reproduce nuclear matter ground state properties (energy -16 MeV per nucleon, density 0.165 fm^-3). The simulations start from hot uniform nuclear gas at T ~ 20 MeV and cool it slowly over 10^3-10^4 fm/c using a frictional relaxation method to maintain quasi-thermal equilibrium.

## Context

This review extends the pioneering work of Baym, Bethe, and Pethick (1971) who first predicted nuclei turning inside out near normal nuclear density, and the independent work of Ravenhall et al. and Hashimoto et al. (early 1980s) who identified the five typical pasta phases using liquid-drop models. The authors' own QMD simulations address the dynamical formation process that had been completely open in prior static studies (liquid-drop models, Thomas-Fermi approximations, Hartree-Fock calculations). The review enables future work on astrophysical consequences of pasta phases, including their effects on neutrino scattering in supernovae, direct URCA cooling in neutron stars, elastic properties affecting r-mode instabilities and torsional shear modes, and glitch phenomena in pulsars.
