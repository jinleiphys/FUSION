# Fluctuations, correlations and the nonextensivity

> Examples of joint probability distributions are studied in terms of Tsallis' nonextensive statistics both for correlated and uncorrelated variables, in particular it is explicitely shown how correlations in the system can make Tsallis entropy additive and that the effective nonextensivity parameter $q_N$ decreases towards unity when the number of variables $N$ increases. We demonstrate that Tsallis distribution of energies of particles in a system leads in natural way to the Negative Binomial multiplicity distribution in this system.

# Fluctuations, correlations and the nonextensivity

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that fluctuations in a multicomponent system, when they affect the whole system jointly (rather than each component independently), introduce correlations between components and lead to Tsallis distributions; however, these same fluctuations cause the effective nonextensivity parameter \(q_N\) to approach unity as the number of components \(N\) increases, making the system appear extensive for large \(N\). The authors also show that the Tsallis energy distribution for particles in a system naturally yields a Negative Binomial multiplicity distribution, not a Poissonian one.

## Method
The authors start from exponential single-variable distributions and introduce fluctuations in the scale parameter \(\lambda\) via a gamma distribution, producing Tsallis (power-law) distributions. They then construct joint probability distributions for two and \(N\) random variables under two scenarios: independent fluctuations per variable (uncorrelated case) and a common fluctuation affecting all variables jointly (correlated case). For the \(N\)-variable correlated case, they derive the joint Tsallis distribution and compute its marginal distributions, moments, and correlation coefficient. They apply the same formalism to energy distributions of particles in a high-energy collision, deriving the resulting multiplicity distribution by integrating over the sum of energies and comparing with the Poissonian case obtained from exponential energy distributions.

## Key numbers
- For the two-variable correlated case, the correlation coefficient \(\rho = (q-1)/(2-q)\); equivalently, \(q = 1 + \rho/(\rho+1)\).
- For \(N\) variables with common fluctuations, the effective nonextensivity parameter \(q_N = 1 + (q_1-1)/[1 + (q_1-1)(N-1)]\), which tends to 1 as \(N \to \infty\).
- The Negative Binomial multiplicity distribution derived from Tsallis energy distributions has mean \(\langle N \rangle = E/\lambda\) and variance \(Var(N) = \langle N \rangle + \langle N \rangle^2 (q-1)\).
- The parameter \(k\) of the Negative Binomial distribution equals \(k = 1/(q-1)\); for \(q \to 1\), \(k \to \infty\) and the distribution becomes Poissonian; for \(q \to 2\), \(k \to 1\) and it becomes geometric.
- For RHIC data (STAR experiment, centrality 30-40%, \(N_{part} \sim 100\)), the relative variance \(\omega = 4 \times 10^{-4}\); PHENIX data give \(\omega\) values of \(2.2 \times 10^{-4}\), \(2.4 \times 10^{-4}\), \(3.6 \times 10^{-4}\), and \(4.9 \times 10^{-4}\) for centralities 0-5%, 0-10%, 10-20%, and 20-30% respectively.

## Context
This work extends the superstatistics framework of Beck and Cohen (2003) and the authors' own earlier work (Wilk and Wlodarczyk, 2000) connecting fluctuations to Tsallis statistics. It clarifies that correlations induced by common fluctuations can cancel pre-existing correlations, and that the effective nonextensivity decreases with system size, a point also discussed by Parvan (2005) in a thermodynamic context. The derivation of Negative Binomial multiplicity distributions from Tsallis energy distributions provides a microscopic foundation for the widely observed NBD in high-energy multiparticle production (reviewed by the authors as "NB" in the text), and connects the NBD parameter \(k\) to the correlation coefficient \(\rho\) of two-particle energy correlations. The results enable interpretation of event-by-event transverse momentum fluctuations measured at RHIC (STAR and PHENIX collaborations) as temperature fluctuations of the hadronizing system, relevant for searches for the Quark-Gluon Plasma.
