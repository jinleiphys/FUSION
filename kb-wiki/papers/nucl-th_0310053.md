# Unified Scheme for Describing Time Delay and Time Advance in the Interpolation of Rotational Bands of Resonances

> In this paper we show how rotational bands of resonances can be described by using trajectories of poles of the scattering amplitude in the complex angular momentum plane: each band of resonances is represented by the evolution of a single pole lying in the first quadrant of the plane. The main result of the paper consists in showing that also the antiresonances (or echoes) can be described by trajectories of the scattering amplitude poles, instead of using the hard-sphere potential scattering as prescribed by the classical Breit-Wigner theory. The antiresonance poles lie in the fourth quadrant of the complex angular momentum plane, and are associated with non-local potentials which take into account the exchange forces; it derives a clear-cut separation between resonance and antiresonance poles. The evolution of these latter poles describes the passage from quantum to semi-classical physics. The theory is tested on the rotational band produced by $α$-$α$ elastic scattering and on the hadronic rotational bands in $π^+$-p elastic scattering.

## Key claim

The paper claims that rotational bands of resonances and their associated antiresonances (echoes) can both be described by pole trajectories in the complex angular momentum plane, with resonance poles in the first quadrant and antiresonance poles in the fourth quadrant, thereby providing a unified scheme for time delay and time advance. This replaces the classical Breit-Wigner approach where antiresonances were described by hard-sphere potential scattering, and the authors emphasize that the pure resonance width \(\Gamma_R\) cannot be identified with the observed peak width \(\Gamma\) due to distorting effects of the antiresonance.

## Method

The authors start from an integro-differential Schrodinger-type equation for non-local potentials that account for exchange forces between clusters, derived in a previous paper (De Micheli, referred to as I). They perform a Watson-type resummation of the partial-wave expansion of the scattering amplitude, deforming the integration contour in the complex angular momentum (\(\lambda\)) plane to isolate pole contributions. The phase-shifts are then expressed in terms of the pole parameters \(\alpha_R + i\beta_R\) (first quadrant, resonances) and \(\alpha_A - i\beta_A\) (fourth quadrant, antiresonances), with separate formulas for identical spinless bosons (e.g., \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) scattering) and non-identical particles (e.g., \(\pi^+\)-p scattering).

## Key numbers

* For \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) elastic scattering: the rotational band of resonances is tested, with the symmetrized phase-shift formula \(\delta_\ell = \sin^{-1}\left\{\frac{1+(-1)^\ell}{2} \frac{\beta_R(2\alpha_R+1)}{\{[(\ell-\alpha_R)^2+\beta_R^2][(\ell+\alpha_R+1)^2+\beta_R^2]\}^{1/2}}\right\}\) used to interpolate resonances at integer \(\ell\) values.
* For \(\pi^+\)-p elastic scattering: the hadronic rotational bands are tested, with the unsymmetrized phase-shift formula \(\delta_\ell = \sin^{-1}\frac{\beta_R(2\alpha_R+1)}{\{[(\ell-\alpha_R)^2+\beta_R^2][(\ell+\alpha_R+1)^2+\beta_R^2]\}^{1/2}}\) applied.
* The pure resonance width is given by \(\Gamma_R = \frac{2\beta_R(d\alpha_R/dE)}{(d\alpha_R/dE)^2 + (d\beta_R/dE)^2}\), derived from a Taylor expansion of \(\lambda(E)\) near the resonance energy.
* The total cross-section is computed as \(\sigma_\text{tot} = \frac{4\pi}{k^2} \sum_{\ell=0}^\infty (2\ell+1) \sin^2\delta_\ell\), with \(k^2 = E\) in units where \(\hbar = 2\mu = 1\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analyses (Fioravanti, Viano1, Viano2) where rotational resonances were fitted using first-quadrant poles but antiresonances were still described by hard-sphere potential scattering, similar to classical Breit-Wigner theory. The new unified scheme using fourth-quadrant poles for antiresonances provides a clear separation between time delay (resonances) and time advance (antiresonances), and enables the description of the evolution from quantum resonances into semi-classical surface waves at higher energies. The theory is tested on \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) elastic scattering (previously studied in Viano1) and \(\pi^+\)-p elastic scattering (previously studied in Viano2), and the authors note that the interference between resonance and antiresonance contributions appears negligible in the phenomenological analysis.
