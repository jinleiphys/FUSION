# Attractive and repulsive contributions of medium fluctuations to nuclear superfluidity

> Oscillations of mainly surface character (S=0 modes) give rise, in atomic nuclei, to an attractive (induced) pairing interaction, while spin (S=1) modes of mainly volume character generate a repulsive interaction, the net effect being an attraction which accounts for a sizeable fraction of the experimental pairing gap. Suppressing the particle-vibration coupling mediated by the proton degrees of freedom, i.e., mimicking neutron matter, the total surface plus spin-induced pairing interaction becomes repulsive.

# Attractive and repulsive contributions of medium fluctuations to nuclear superfluidity

## Key claim

The net induced pairing interaction from medium fluctuations in atomic nuclei is attractive and accounts for a sizeable fraction of the experimental pairing gap, but only because surface density modes (S=0) provide attraction that outweighs repulsion from volume spin modes (S=1). When the proton degrees of freedom are suppressed to mimic neutron matter, the total induced interaction becomes repulsive, reconciling the seemingly contradictory behavior between finite nuclei and neutron-star crusts.

## Method

The authors performed Hartree-Fock plus BCS calculations for the open-shell nucleus 120Sn using the SkM* Skyrme force, then computed polarization quanta within the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) for multipolarities J^pi = 1+, 2+, 2-, 3+, 3-, 4+, 4-, 5+, and 5- up to 30 MeV. They constructed the induced pairing interaction from particle-vibration coupling vertices, separating contributions from natural parity modes (spin-independent, attractive) and unnatural parity modes (spin-dependent, repulsive), and solved the BCS gap equation to obtain state-dependent pairing gaps.

## Key numbers

- The exchange of S=1 spin modes quenches the pairing gap arising from exchange of only S=0 density modes by roughly 30% in 120Sn.
- The experimental pairing gap for 120Sn is 1.4 MeV; the induced interaction accounts for approximately 50% of this value, with the other half coming from the bare nucleon-nucleon force.
- The most attractive diagonal matrix element is associated with the 1h^2_{11/2}(0) configuration at single-particle energy epsilon = -8.07 MeV (Fermi energy epsilon_F = -8.50 MeV), arising mainly from exchange of a 2+ phonon at 1.5 MeV.
- A large repulsive matrix element is associated with the 2d^2_{3/2}(0) configuration at epsilon = -8.52 MeV, dominated by exchange of a 3+ vibration at 4.35 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of medium-induced pairing in finite nuclei from the authors' prior work (PRL, 2004) and reconciles it with calculations in neutron-star crusts by Ainsworth et al. (1989) and Lombardo et al. (2001), where polarization effects quench the pairing gap. The key insight is that in finite nuclei, surface-localized high-j orbitals near the Fermi energy couple efficiently to collective surface vibrations (natural parity), while the proton-neutron part of the particle-hole interaction provides the dominant attractive contribution; both features are absent in neutron matter, leading to net repulsion. The results enable a unified understanding of pairing in finite nuclei and infinite neutron systems, and call for further study with improved Skyrme parametrizations such as SkO' that better constrain the spin-dependent channel.
