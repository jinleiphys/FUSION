# Implications of the discrepancy between proton form factor measurements

> Recent polarization transfer measurements of the proton electromagnetic form factors yield very different results from previous Rosenbluth extractions. This inconsistency implies uncertainties in our knowledge of the form factors and raises questions about how to best combine data from these two techniques. If the discrepancy is due to missing correction to the cross section data, as has been suggested, then different applications will require the use of different form factors. We present two extractions of the form factors: a global fit to the world's cross section data, and a combined extraction from polarization transfer and cross section data. The former provides a parametrization of the elastic electron-proton cross section, while the latter provides a consistent extraction of the underlying form factors, under the assumption that missing terms in the radiative correction explain the difference between the cross section and polarization transfer results.

## Key claim

If the discrepancy between Rosenbluth and polarization transfer measurements is due to missing two-photon exchange corrections to the cross section data, then the true proton form factors (related to proton structure) differ from the form factors that parametrize the elastic electron-proton cross section, and different applications require different form factor sets.

## Method

The author performed a global fit to 470 elastic electron-proton cross section data points (443 measurements from 27 experiments) over Q^2 from 0.005 to 30 GeV^2, extracting "Rosenbluth form factors" using a polynomial parametrization. A second "Polarization form factors" extraction combined the same cross section data with 26 polarization transfer data points from Jones (2000), Gayou (2001), and Gayou (2002), applying a minimal linear epsilon-dependent correction of 6% to the cross sections to resolve the discrepancy.

## Key numbers

- The Rosenbluth fit yields total chi^2 = 326.7 for 431 degrees of freedom (reduced chi^2 = 0.758), indicating quoted uncertainties are too large
- The Polarization fit yields total chi^2 = 391.6 for 457 degrees of freedom (reduced chi^2 = 0.857)
- A 5-7% epsilon-dependent correction, roughly linear in epsilon, is required to resolve the discrepancy for Q^2 between 1 and 6 GeV^2
- Typical uncertainties on G_M from the Rosenbluth fit are approximately 1% at low Q^2, increasing to about 2% at Q^2 = 10 GeV^2 and 8% at Q^2 = 30 GeV^2
- For G_E, uncertainties are 1-2% at low Q^2, 5-10% for intermediate Q^2 values (2-4 GeV^2), growing rapidly at higher Q^2
- Absolute cross sections from the Rosenbluth fit are known to better than 2% up to Q^2 approximately 4 GeV^2, reaching 10% at Q^2 = 25 GeV^2
- The two-photon exchange correction calculated by Blunden (2003) is only about 2%, less than half the size needed to explain the discrepancy

## Context

This work extends the global analysis of Arrington (2003) by including additional Jefferson Lab measurements from Christy (2003) and Dutta (2003), as well as low Q^2 data from Borkowski (1974), Murphy (1974), Simon (1980), and Simon (1981), and extends the fit range to 30 GeV^2. The paper directly addresses the discrepancy between Rosenbluth extractions and polarization transfer measurements from Jones (2000), Gayou (2001), and Gayou (2002), and provides two separate form factor parametrizations for use depending on whether the application requires the elastic cross section or the underlying proton structure. The work enables future experiments such as the "Super-Rosenbluth" measurement at Jefferson Lab (E01-001) and a new polarization transfer experiment (E01-109) to resolve the origin of the discrepancy.
