# Rapidly spinning dark matter-admixed neutron stars

> Millisecond pulsars, representing the older neutron star population, are believed to have undergone a prolonged period of dark matter accumulation, resulting in a higher dark matter content. Their extreme rotation makes them unique laboratories for studying rapidly rotating neutron stars admixed with dark matter. In this work, we model uniformly rotating neutron stars with a dark matter component that rotates independently from the baryon matter, allowing for the investigation of both co-rotating and counter-rotating scenarios. We examine the impact of dark matter rotation on the macroscopic properties of neutron stars, including the mass-radius relation, the mass-shedding Keplerian limit, and moments of inertia, for various dark matter particle masses and total fractions, considering both core and halo distributions. Our findings provide a more comprehensive understanding of how dark matter influences the equilibrium properties of rotating neutron stars, offering new insights into the astrophysical implications of self-interacting dark matter.

## Key claim

The paper models rapidly rotating neutron stars with a dark matter component that can rotate independently from the baryonic matter, finding that the dark matter distribution (core vs. halo) and its rotation state (co-rotating or counter-rotating) significantly alter the mass-radius relation, Keplerian limit, and moment of inertia, but only when the dark matter fraction is sufficiently high (e.g., 15%) or the particle mass is chosen to produce extended halos.

## Method

The authors solve the Einstein field equations for a two-fluid system (baryonic matter and self-interacting bosonic dark matter) in axial symmetry using a modified version of the RNS code that implements the Komatsu-Eriguchi-Hachisu scheme. They consider uniformly rotating configurations where the two fluids can have independent angular velocities, exploring both co-rotating and counter-rotating scenarios. The baryonic matter is modeled with three equations of state (DD2npY-T, DD2, and IST), while the dark matter is described as a Bose-Einstein condensate with a fixed self-interaction strength λ = 24π and particle masses of 1 GeV (core configurations) or 250 MeV (halo configurations).

## Key numbers

- Maximum mass increase due to rotation: approximately 20% for pure baryonic neutron stars and for dark matter core configurations; approximately 20% for halo configurations with 5% dark matter fraction
- Radius increase due to rotation: approximately 40% for pure baryonic neutron stars and for dark matter core configurations; approximately 40% for halo configurations with 5% dark matter fraction
- Keplerian angular velocity for a core configuration with total mass 2 M⊙, dark matter fraction 5%, and dark matter particle mass 1 GeV: ΩKBM = 8068 Hz
- Keplerian angular velocity for a halo configuration with total mass 2.08 M⊙, dark matter fraction 5%, and dark matter particle mass 250 MeV: ΩKBM = 6841 Hz
- Dark matter deformation near the last stable configuration: equatorial radius exceeds polar radius by at most approximately 2% for non-rotating dark matter
- Baryonic matter deformation: equatorial radius exceeds polar radius by approximately 50% even for low-mass systems
- Transition mass for mixed configuration (RBM_e > RDM_e but RDM_p > RBM_p) in halo case with 15% dark matter fraction: 1.44 M⊙
- Peak oblateness occurs at ΩBM ≈ 6 kHz for all configurations

## Context

This work extends previous studies of non-rotating dark matter-admixed neutron stars (Ivanytskyi 2019, Sagun 2021, Karkevandi 2021) and slowly rotating configurations (Routaray 2024, Cronin 2023) by systematically exploring configurations up to the mass-shedding Keplerian limit. Unlike Konstantinou (2024), the authors include both core and halo dark matter distributions. The results enable more realistic modeling of millisecond pulsars for comparison with NICER observations (Miao 2022, Rutherford 2022, Shakeri 2022) and gravitational wave detections from binary neutron star mergers.
