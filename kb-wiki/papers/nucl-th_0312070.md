# Solving the Richardson equations for Fermions

> Forty years ago Richardson showed that the eigenstates of the pairing Hamiltonian with constant interaction strength can be calculated by solving a set of non-linear coupled equations. However, in the case of Fermions these equations lead to singularities which made them very hard to solve. This letter explains how these singularities can be avoided through a change of variables making the Fermionic pairing problem numerically solvable for arbitrary single particle energies and degeneracies.

# Solving the Richardson equations for Fermions

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that the singularities in Richardson's equations for fermionic pairing, which had prevented numerical solution for over forty years, can be avoided through a change of variables to symmetric sums $S_p$ combined with a cluster decomposition and adiabatic continuation in the pairing strength $g$. The method works for arbitrary single-particle energies and degeneracies, but requires limiting cluster sizes to at most $n_k = -2d_k + 1$ variables and using $gS_{-1}$ as an unknown variable instead of $S_{N_k}$ when singularities occur.

## Method
The Richardson equations are solved by first obtaining an approximate solution in the weak-interaction limit ($g \to 0$), then evolving this solution adiabatically by gradually increasing $g$. Variables $x_\alpha$ are grouped into clusters around single-particle levels $2e_j$, and the equations are transformed to new variables $S_p = \sum_{\alpha \in C_k} (2e_k - x_\alpha)^p$, which remain smooth through singularities. A Newton-Raphson gradient method is applied to the transformed equations, with linear extrapolation in the $S_p$ variables used to pass through critical values of $g$ where singularities occur.

## Key numbers
- The critical cluster size for singularities is $n_k = -2d_k + 1$, where $d_k = (\nu_j - \Omega_j)/2$ for fermions; for the $1d_{3/2}$ level with $\Omega_j = 2$ and seniority $\nu_j = 0$, this gives $n_k = 3$
- A singularity occurs around the $1d_{3/2}$ level at $g = 0.245$ in the example system
- The example system (neutrons in $^{56}$Fe) has a full many-body space of dimension $\sim 10^{15}$, while the zero-seniority basis has dimension 14894
- The method solves the equations for all interaction strengths in "a matter of seconds" using a Fortran-95 program, compared to Lanczos diagonalization which required 50 iterations and was less accurate for excited states
- The ground state could be solved with the original equations only up to $g \simeq 0.2$ before singularities appeared

## Context
This work resolves a forty-year-old problem in exactly solvable pairing models, first formulated by Richardson (1963) for fermions and later extended to bosons. Previous attempts to handle the singularities were limited to specific cases, such as Richardson (1966) for doubly degenerate equidistant levels in the ground state, or required manual tuning of solutions. The method extends to the recently developed exactly solvable pairing models of Dukelsky et al. (2001) and the $O(5)$ pairing models of Pan et al., which suffer from the same singularities. This enables practical applications of exactly solvable pairing models to nanometallic grains, Bose-Einstein condensates, and nuclear physics, and may be applicable to other exactly solvable pairing and spin models.
