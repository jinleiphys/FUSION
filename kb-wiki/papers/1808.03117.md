# Collective Effects: the viewpoint of HEP MC codes

> Collective effects are observed in high-multiplicity pp events, similar to the signals traditionally attributed to the formation of a Quark Gluon Plasma in heavy ion collisions. In core--corona models it is assumed that a partial plasma formation is indeed possible also in pp, but here the focus is on several recent models that attempt to explain pp data without invoking plasma formation. These attempts are partly successful, but there is still not a unified framework.

## Key claim

Collective effects observed in high-multiplicity pp events can be partially explained by several recent models that do not invoke quark-gluon plasma formation, but there is still no unified framework that fully accounts for all observations.

## Method

The paper reviews modifications to the standard string and cluster hadronization models used in the PYTHIA, Herwig, and SHERPA event generators, including colour reconnection, rope hadronization, junction baryon formation, string shoving, and variable string tension. These models are compared to experimental data on strangeness enhancement, particle yields, and flow coefficients in pp collisions at LHC energies.

## Key numbers

- Standard string tension is approximately 1 GeV/fm, with strangeness suppression treated as a free parameter of order 0.2–0.25
- A typical LHC value ⟨dn_charged/dy⟩ ≈ 6 corresponds to approximately six strings being pulled out in a pp event
- For two parallel strings, the effective string tension in the rope model becomes κ_eff = (3/2)κ for the first break
- The p/π yield ratio peaks at around 2–3 GeV in transverse momentum
- The v_2{2} signal from colour reconnection is reduced appreciably when a gap is added to the extraction, while string shoving still gives a small positive contribution

## Context

This work extends the discussion of collective effects beyond the core–corona model implemented in EPOS (Pierog 2013, Werner 2013), which assumes partial QGP formation in pp collisions. The paper reviews alternative mechanisms proposed within the PYTHIA, Herwig, and SHERPA frameworks that attempt to explain ALICE data on strangeness enhancement (ALICE 2017) and flow measurements (Khachatryan 2010, 2015, 2016; Aad 2015) without invoking a phase transition. It enables further development of unified models that can describe pp, pA, and AA collisions within a single framework, such as the Angantyr model for PYTHIA.
