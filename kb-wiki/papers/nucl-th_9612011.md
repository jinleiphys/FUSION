# Quantum Diffusion and Tunneling with Parametric Banded Random Matrix Hamiltonians

> The microscopic origin of dissipation of a driven quantum many body system is addressed in the framework of a parametric banded random matrix approach. We find noticeable violations of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and we observe also that the energy diffusion has a markedly non-Gaussian character. Within the Feynman-Vernon path integral formalism and in the Markovian limit, we further consider the time evolution of a slow subsystem coupled to such a ``bath'' of intrinsic degrees of freedom. We show that dissipation leads to qualitative modifications of the time evolution of the density matrix of the slow subsystem. In either the spatial, momentum or energy representation the density distribution acquires very long tails and tunneling is greatly enhanced.

# Quantum Diffusion and Tunneling with Parametric Banded Random Matrix Hamiltonians

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that energy diffusion in a driven quantum many-body system described by a parametric banded random matrix Hamiltonian is markedly non-Gaussian and violates the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, leading to density distributions with very long tails and greatly enhanced tunneling in the slow subsystem, but these results are derived under the assumption of a Markovian limit and an adiabatic approximation for the slow degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors model the intrinsic degrees of freedom of a quantum many-body system using a parametric banded random matrix Hamiltonian whose matrix elements depend on a slow "shape" variable X(t). They compute the time evolution of the driven intrinsic subsystem by solving the time-dependent Schrödinger equation with a time-dependent Hamiltonian H₁(X(t)) = h₀ + h₁(X(t)), where h₀ is diagonal and defines the average density of states, and h₁(X) is a Gaussian random matrix with a specified correlation function. For the case where the slow variable becomes dynamical, they employ the Feynman-Vernon path integral formalism to derive an evolution equation for the reduced density matrix of the slow subsystem, obtaining a Markovian master equation in the adiabatic limit.

## Key numbers

- The spreading width Γ↓ characterizes the intrinsic subsystem; in the adiabatic limit, the occupation probabilities reach a Lorentzian distribution n_k = (1/π) × (Γ↓/2) / (ε_k² + (Γ↓/2)²)
- In the adiabatic limit (τ_slow ≫ τ_fast), the energy diffusion constant D(V₀) = [ħΓ↓/(2X₀²)] × V₀², showing quadratic velocity dependence
- In the diabatic limit (τ_slow ≪ τ_fast), the diffusion constant D(V₀) = [Γ↓X₀κ₀³(β²κ₀²+4)/(8ħ²)] × exp(β²κ₀²/8) × (1/V₀), showing inverse velocity dependence
- The fluctuation-dissipation theorem is violated: βD = γ(1 + β²κ₀²/4) instead of the Gaussian result βD = γ, where γt is the first cumulant
- In the energy distribution tails, P(ε) ∝ exp(-α|ε| ln^{1/2}|ε|) for some time-dependent constant α

## Context

This work extends the parametric banded random matrix approach previously developed by the same authors (Bulgac, Do Dang, and Kusnezov, 1995-1996) to study dissipation in finite quantum many-body systems. It contrasts with the Caldeira-Leggett treatment of dissipative tunneling (Caldeira and Leggett, 1983) and the quantum Langevin equation approach (Ford, Kac, and Mazur, 1965), which assume Gaussian diffusive processes. The paper shows that a complex quantum environment with realistic spectral properties produces non-Markovian energy transfer with long tails, enabling future studies of dissipation in systems such as atomic nuclei and complex molecules where the intrinsic subsystem has a finite number of degrees of freedom and an exponentially increasing density of states.
