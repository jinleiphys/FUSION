# Impact of new data for neutron-rich heavy nuclei on theoretical models for $r$-process nucleosynthesis

> Current models for the $r$ process are summarized with an emphasis on the key constraints from both nuclear physics measurements and astronomical observations. In particular, we analyze the importance of nuclear physics input such as beta-decay rates; nuclear masses; neutron-capture cross sections; beta-delayed neutron emission; probability of spontaneous fission, beta- and neutron-induced fission, fission fragment mass distributions; neutrino-induced reaction cross sections, etc. We highlight the effects on models for $r$-process nucleosynthesis of newly measured $β$-decay half-lives, masses, and spectroscopy of neutron-rich nuclei near the $r$-process path. We overview r-process nucleosynthesis in the neutrino driven wind above the proto-neutron star in core collapse supernovae along with the possibility of magneto-hydrodynamic jets from rotating supernova explosion models. We also consider the possibility of neutron star mergers as an r-process environment. A key outcome of newly measured nuclear properties far from stability is the degree of shell quenching for neutron rich isotopes near the closed neutron shells. This leads to important constraints on the sites for $r$-process nucleosynthesis in which freezeout occurs on a rapid timescale.

## Key claim

New measurements of nuclear masses and beta-decay half-lives near the r-process path, particularly around the N=82 closed shell, show conflicting evidence between mass measurements (which indicate shell quenching) and nuclear spectroscopy (which suggests robust shell closures), and this tension has important implications for identifying the astrophysical sites of r-process nucleosynthesis.

## Method

The paper reviews the impact of recent experimental nuclear data, including beta-decay half-lives, masses, and spectroscopy of neutron-rich nuclei, on theoretical r-process nucleosynthesis models. It examines how these data constrain the nuclear physics input (masses, neutron capture rates, beta-decay rates, fission properties) used in reaction network calculations for candidate r-process sites including the neutrino-driven wind in core-collapse supernovae, magneto-hydrodynamic jets, and neutron star mergers.

## Key numbers

- Approximately half of the elements heavier than iron are produced by the r-process, and it is the only means to produce naturally occurring radioactive actinides such as Th and U.
- Up to 110 neutron-rich isotopes of elements from Rb to Sn have been measured at the RIKEN Radioactive Isotope Beam Factory, encompassing the neutron closed shell at N=82.
- Mass measurements of nuclei adjacent to the waiting-point nuclide 130Cd using ISOLTRAP at ISOLDE/CERN showed a significant deviation of approximately 400 keV from earlier mass estimates, indicating a reduction of the N=82 shell gap below the doubly magic nucleus 132Sn.
- Observations of seven stars in the dwarf galaxy Reticulum II show evidence of a single event that ejected approximately 0.2 solar masses of r-process elements.
- The r-process path in the model of Shibagaki (2016) proceeded to nuclei with A approximately 320, whereas the path in Goriely (2013) terminates at A approximately 278.
- In the Shibagaki (2016) model, only about 10% of the final yield comes from termination of the r-process path at N=212 and Z=111, while almost 90% of the A=160 yield came from fission of more than 200 different parent nuclei mostly via beta-delayed fission.

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to identify r-process sites, building on foundational work by Burbidge (1957) and more recent reviews by Arnould (2007), Martinez-Pinedo (2008), and Thielemann (2011). The paper highlights how new measurements from facilities like RIKEN, CERN, and JYFL are constraining nuclear models, particularly the finite range droplet model (FRDM) of Moller (1995, 2012) and various Hartree-Fock-Bogolyubov mass models. The conflicting evidence on shell quenching near N=82 enables future work to discriminate between r-process sites such as neutron star mergers versus core-collapse supernovae, and informs the interpretation of kilonova observations from short gamma-ray bursts as reported by Tanvir (2013) and Berger (2013).
