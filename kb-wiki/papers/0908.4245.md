# Systematic variation in the apparent burning area of thermonuclear bursts and its implication for neutron star radius measurement

> Precision measurements of neutron star radii can provide a powerful probe of the properties of cold matter beyond nuclear density. Beginning in the late 1970s it was proposed that the radius could be obtained from the apparent or inferred emitting area during the decay portions of thermonuclear (type I) X-ray bursts. However, this apparent area is generally not constant, preventing reliable measurement of the source radius. Here we report for the first time a correlation between the variation of the inferred area and the burst properties, measured in a sample of almost 900 bursts from 43 sources. We found that the rate of change of the inferred area during decay is anticorrelated with the burst decay duration. A Spearman rank correlation test shows that this relation is significant at the <10^{-45} level for our entire sample, and at the 7x10^{-37} level for the 625 bursts without photospheric radius expansion. This anticorrelation is also highly significant for individual sources exhibiting a wide range of burst durations, such as 4U 1636-536 and Aql X-1. We suggest that variations in the colour factor, which relates the colour temperature resulted from the scattering in the neutron star atmosphere to the effective temperature of the burning layer, may explain the correlation. This in turn implies significant variations in the composition of the atmosphere between bursts with long and short durations.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first discovery of a pattern in the otherwise erratic evolution of the inferred emission area during thermonuclear X-ray burst decay: the rate of change of the inferred area is anticorrelated with burst decay duration, but the authors caution that this correlation may be driven by variations in the colour factor rather than changes in the actual burning area, and that substantial spread around the trend suggests multiple factors are at work.

## Method

The authors analyzed 877 thermonuclear (type I) X-ray bursts from 43 sources observed with the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE) proportional counter array (PCA), using spectral fits from Galloway et al. (2008). For each burst, they normalized the time axis from 0 to 1 between the start (peak flux for non-PRE bursts, touchdown for PRE bursts) and the stop time when flux dropped below 15% of maximum, then computed the best-fit linear slope of the normalized squared inferred radius versus normalized time, and correlated this slope with the logarithm of burst duration using a Spearman rank test.

## Key numbers

- The anticorrelation between the rate of change of inferred area and burst decay duration is significant at the less than 10^{-45} level for the full sample of 877 bursts from 43 sources.
- For the 625 bursts without photospheric radius expansion (PRE), the anticorrelation is significant at the 7 x 10^{-37} level.
- For the individual source 4U 1636-536, the correlation significance is 5 x 10^{-14}; for Aql X-1, it is 2 x 10^{-7}.
- The temperature slope correlation with burst duration is significant at the 1.5 x 10^{-38} level for the full sample.
- The theoretical colour factor ratio f_ratio (f_2/f_1) ranges from 0.67 to 1.5 for the chemical compositions considered, which implies a range of -1.34 to 1.34 for the normalized radius-squared slope R^2_sl.

## Context

This work extends the burst sample analysis of Galloway et al. (2008) by identifying a systematic pattern in the previously unexplained variation of inferred emission area during burst decay, which had prevented reliable neutron star radius measurements since the method was proposed in the late 1970s. The correlation suggests that colour factor variations related to atmospheric composition differences between short (helium-rich) and long (mixed hydrogen-helium) bursts may explain the trend, building on theoretical calculations of spectral hardening by Madej et al. (2004) and Majczyna et al. (2005). If confirmed, this pattern could enable more precise neutron star radius measurements with future missions such as Astrosat, providing constraints on the equation of state of cold matter beyond nuclear density.
