# Lambda-proton correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The prospect of using lambda-proton correlations to extract source sizes in relativistic heavy ion collisions is investigated. It is found that the strong interaction induces a large peak in the correlation function that provides more sensitive source size measurements than two-proton correlations under some circumstances. The prospect of using lambda-proton correlations to measure the time lag between lambda and proton emissions is also studied.

# Lambda-proton correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The strong interaction produces a large peak in the $\Lambda p$ correlation function that can provide more sensitive source size measurements than two-proton correlations for sources larger than approximately 6 fm, but the authors note that deuteron coalescence should statistically provide the most accurate determination of source parameters of all baryonic probes.

## Method

The authors calculate $\Lambda p$ correlation functions using a Gaussian source model with an Urbana-type potential motivated by low-energy $\Lambda p$ scattering and hypernuclei binding energy data, generating relative wave functions from this potential. They compare these correlations to two-proton correlations for the same source sizes, and also study the effect of a spatial displacement between lambda and proton emission sources by binning correlation functions according to the sign of the projected relative momentum.

## Key numbers

- The Urbana-type potential parameters are $\bar{V} = 6.2 \pm 0.05$ MeV (spin-independent) and $V_{\sigma} = 0.25 \pm 0.25$ MeV (spin-dependent), with a repulsive core $W_C = 2137$ MeV, $R = 0.5$ fm, $d = 0.2$ fm.
- The scattering length and effective range for the spin singlet are $-2.88$ fm and $2.92$ fm respectively; for the spin triplet they are $-1.66$ fm and $3.78$ fm respectively.
- For a thermal temperature of 300 MeV, 20 million $\Lambda p$ pairs yield 10 pairs in $0 < k < 5$ MeV/$c$ (30% statistical uncertainty), 70 pairs in $5 < k < 10$ MeV/$c$ (12%), 800 pairs in $25 < k < 30$ MeV/$c$ (4%), and 7000 pairs in $0 < k < 50$ MeV/$c$.
- The difference in correlation functions between $T = 3$ MeV and $T = 300$ MeV is less than 5%.
- Uncertainties in the potential parameters translate to an approximately $\pm 0.5$ fm uncertainty in the source size extracted from the correlation function at $k < 25$ MeV/$c$.

## Context

This work extends the use of two-particle correlations, previously applied to pions, kaons, and protons in relativistic heavy ion collisions (Boson HBT studies), to the $\Lambda p$ system as a probe of source sizes and emission time lags. The authors build on the work of Lednicky et al. (1990s) showing that non-identical particle correlations can reveal whether particles are emitted simultaneously, and they compare their proposed method to deuteron coalescence measurements (Llope, 1990s) and neutron-proton correlations (Lyuboshits, 1980s). The results enable experimental tests of sequential freeze-out scenarios for strange versus non-strange baryons at RHIC and other relativistic heavy ion facilities.
