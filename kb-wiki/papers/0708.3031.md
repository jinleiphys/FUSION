# Bounds to binding energies from the concavity of thermodynamical functions

> Sequences of experimental ground-state energies are mapped onto concave patterns cured from convexities due to pairing and/or shell effects. The same patterns, completed by a list of excitation energies, can be used to give numerical estimates of the grand potential $Ω(β,μ)$ for a mixture of nuclei at low or moderate temperatures $T=β^{-1}$ and at many chemical potentials $μ.$ The average nucleon number $<{\bf A} >(β,μ)$ then becomes a continuous variable, allowing extrapolations towards nuclear masses closer to drip lines. We study the possible concavity of several thermodynamical functions, such as the free energy and the average energy, as functions of $<{\bf A} >.$ Concavity, when present in such functions, allows trivial interpolations and extrapolations providing upper and lower bounds, respectively, to binding energies. Such bounds define an error bar for the prediction of binding energies. An extrapolation scheme for such concave functions is tested. We conclude with numerical estimates of the binding energies of a few nuclei closer to drip lines.

# Bounds to binding energies from the concavity of thermodynamical functions

## Key claim

The authors show that experimental ground-state binding energies, after removal of pairing and shell-effect convexities via empirical tuning parameters, can be embedded in a finite-temperature thermodynamic framework where the free energy is provably concave; this concavity then provides rigorous upper and lower bounds for binding energies, with the caveat that the average energy itself is not guaranteed to be concave at all temperatures, and the method's predictive power degrades when too few excited states are known for the nuclei of interest.

## Method

The authors take sequences of experimental ground-state binding energies (e.g., Sn isotopes from A=110 to 137, Sm isotopes from A=128 to 144) and apply two empirical corrections: a pairing correction of 1050 keV (Sn) or 1250 keV (Sm) added to even-A nuclei, and a parabolic correction of 300 keV×(A-118)^2 (Sn) or 36 keV×(A-139)^2 (Sm) to enforce concavity of the tuned energies. They then construct the grand partition function Z(μ,β) using the tuned ground-state energies plus the lowest ten excited states per nucleus, compute the grand potential Ω, the average nucleon number ⟨A⟩, the average energy ⟨H⟩, and the free energy F = ⟨H⟩ - TS as functions of chemical potential μ at fixed temperatures T = β^{-1} (typically 100 keV to 2 MeV). By inverting ⟨A⟩(μ) to obtain μ(⟨A⟩), they plot ⟨H⟩ and F versus the continuous variable ⟨A⟩ and test concavity numerically via second derivatives.

## Key numbers

- For Sn isotopes: pairing correction p = 1050 keV added to even-A nuclei; parabolic coefficient P = 300 keV with minimum at A=118; after tuning, all 26 second differences become positive (range 6 to 3385 keV).
- For Sm isotopes: pairing correction p = 1250 keV; parabolic coefficient P = 36 keV with minimum at A=139; after tuning, second differences become positive (range 0 to 653 keV).
- At T = 100 keV, the ratio |TS' / ⟨H⟩'| for tuned Sn data is smaller than 4×10^{-4}; at T = 300 keV the ratio is ~10^{-3} or less; at T = 2 MeV the ratio is ~10^{-2}.
- For Sm isotopes, the band width between ⟨H⟩' at T=300 keV and F' at T=150 keV is approximately 800 keV, yielding a ±400 keV error estimate for predictions.
- Predicted binding energies for Sm isotopes: E_pred(133Sm) = -1081622 keV (error +466 keV relative to table value -1082088 keV); E_pred(134Sm) = -1094280 keV (error +232 keV relative to -1094512 keV); E_pred(135Sm) = -1104086 keV (error -110 keV relative to -1103976 keV); E_pred(144Sm) = -1196422 keV (error -686 keV relative to -1195736 keV).

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature concavity analysis of Jennings, Giraud, and Barrett (2005) to finite temperature by introducing thermodynamic functions (grand potential, free energy, average energy) as tools for extrapolating binding energies toward the drip lines. The method contrasts with traditional mass formulae (e.g., Bethe-Weizsäcker liquid-drop model with shell and pairing corrections) by exploiting the provable concavity of the grand potential and free energy rather than fitting parametric forms. The approach enables error-bar estimates for predicted masses of exotic nuclei, but its reliability depends on the availability of excited-state data for the nuclei used in the thermodynamic sums; the authors note that extrapolations become risky when too few excited states are known, as occurs for Sm isotopes lighter than 136Sm.
