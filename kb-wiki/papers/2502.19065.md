# Rescattering effects on spin-interference for $ρ^{0}$ photoproduction in heavy-ion collisions

> Recent measurements by various experiments in ultra-peripheral collisions have observed spin-interference in $ρ^{0}$ photoproduction, marking a breakthrough in Fermi-scale quantum interference experiments. Building on this, STAR extended the measurement to hadronic heavy-ion collisions, where significant rescattering effects on $ρ^{0}$ mesons were expected. In this study, we investigate how these rescattering effects influence the measurement of spin-interference. By embedding $ρ^{0}$ mesons produced via photoproduction, modeled by the Vector Meson Dominance model, into the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics framework, we estimate the impact on the $\cos2ϕ$ and $\cos4ϕ$ modulations, where $ϕ$ is the angle between $ρ^{0}$ and one of the daughters' ($π^{\pm}$) transverse momentum. The results indicate a significant suppression of the $\cos2ϕ$ modulation, while the $\cos4ϕ$ modulation remains largely unaffected, which provides insight for understanding the difference due to rescattering effects between experimental measurements and theoretical predictions for $ρ^{0}$ photoproduction in heavy-ion collisions.

# Rescattering effects on spin-interference for ρ⁰ photoproduction in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The rescattering of decay pions from photoproduced ρ⁰ mesons in the hadronic medium of heavy-ion collisions significantly suppresses the cos2φ modulation of the decay angular distribution, while the cos4φ modulation remains largely unaffected. This suppression is centrality-dependent and provides a mechanism to explain the difference between experimental measurements and theoretical predictions for spin-interference in hadronic heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors embed ρ⁰ mesons produced via photoproduction (modeled using the Vector Meson Dominance model and Equivalent Photon Approximation) into the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) framework to simulate Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV. They classify ρ⁰ mesons into "reconstructable" (both decay pions unscattered) and "scattered" categories, then analyze the cos2φ and cos4φ modulations of the decay angular distribution as functions of transverse momentum, embedding time, and collision centrality. Linear polarization is implemented by applying Monte Carlo weights to the decay angles based on the polarization degree calculated from the photoproduction framework.

## Key numbers

- The ρ⁰ meson lifetime is approximately 1.3 fm/c, making it sensitive to hadronic medium interactions before decay
- The Woods-Saxon nuclear radius for gold is RWS = 6.38 fm with skin depth d = 0.535 fm
- The Breit-Wigner width uses p0 = 358 GeV/c as the pion momentum at the ρ⁰ pole mass
- The invariant mass range for ρ⁰ is cut to 2mπ < Mππ < Mρ + 5Γ0
- Coherent photoproduced ρ⁰ mesons have pT < 0.3 GeV/c, where hadronization yield is much lower
- The cos2φ modulation for reconstructable photoproduced ρ⁰ shows two distinct dips near pT ≈ 0.12 GeV/c and 0.20 GeV/c
- For embedding times shorter than 1 fm/c, the cos2φ modulation remains largely unchanged; it approaches zero around 10 fm/c
- The rescattering effect on cos2φ is more significant in central collisions (0-20%) and diminishes in peripheral collisions (80-100%)

## Context

This work extends the spin-interference measurements originally proposed by Xing et al. and Zha et al., which were first observed in ultra-peripheral collisions by STAR and ALICE collaborations. The STAR collaboration subsequently extended these measurements to hadronic heavy-ion collisions, where significant rescattering effects were expected. The study uses the UrQMD transport model (based on string fragmentation and hadronic scattering) to quantify how the dense hadronic medium modifies the polarization-dependent decay angular distributions. The results enable interpretation of the difference between experimental measurements and theoretical predictions for ρ⁰ photoproduction spin-interference in heavy-ion collisions, and suggest that peripheral collisions with a pT cut below 0.3 GeV/c are optimal for measuring the photoproduction signal.
