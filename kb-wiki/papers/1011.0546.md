# Exploring universality in nuclear clusters with Halo EFT

> I present results and highlight aspects of halo EFT to loosely bound systems composed of nucleons and alpha particles, with emphasis on Coulomb interactions.

## Key claim

Halo EFT can describe low-energy resonances and Coulomb interactions in loosely bound nuclear clusters like αα and pα systems, but the αα system exhibits an unexpected and not fully understood fine-tuning between strong and electromagnetic forces that leads to an exponentially suppressed resonance width and a scattering length of order 2000 fm.

## Method

The author applies halo effective field theory (halo EFT) to αα and pα systems, introducing an auxiliary dimer field to describe S-wave interactions and using minimal substitution to incorporate Coulomb forces. For αα scattering, Coulomb photons are summed to all orders using the Kong-Ravndal technique with Coulomb Green's functions, and the amplitude takes the form of a Coulomb-modified effective range expansion. For pα scattering, the resonance envelope is dominated by the angular momentum barrier with Coulomb as a correction, and the LO amplitude includes both S1/2 and P3/2 contributions.

## Key numbers

- αα scattering length at LO: a0 = -1.80 × 10^3 fm
- αα effective range at LO: r0 = 1.083 fm
- αα scattering length at NLO: a0 = (-1.92 ± 0.09) × 10^3 fm
- αα effective range at NLO: r0 = 1.098 ± 0.005 fm
- αα shape parameter at NLO: P0 = -1.46 ± 0.08 fm^3
- αα resonance energy: ER ≈ 92 keV
- Low-momentum scale for αα: Mlo ≈ 20 MeV (from √(mαER))
- High-momentum scale for αα: Mhi ≈ 140 MeV (from pion mass or alpha excitation energy)
- Expansion parameter for αα: approximately 1/7

## Context

This work extends the halo EFT framework developed by Bertulani, Hammer, and van Kolck (2002) for low-energy narrow resonances, and builds on the Coulomb treatment of Kong and Ravndal (2000). The αα results improve upon earlier effective range determinations by Rasche (1970s) with better accuracy. The study suggests a connection between the fine-tuning in the αα system and the Hoyle state in 12C, which is essential for understanding carbon production in stars and remains an almost unexplored subject.
