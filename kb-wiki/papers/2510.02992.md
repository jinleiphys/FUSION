# Production of spectator neutrons, protons and light fragments on fixed targets at NICA

> The Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model was connected to a set of models called Ablation Monte Carlo (AMC) to identify excited spectator fragments on completion of the UrQMD modeling of nucleus-nucleus collisions and then simulate spectator decays. The UrQMD-AMC approach combines the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) clustering algorithm, which associates individual nucleons with excited clusters termed prefragments, with decay models of excited nuclei from the Geant4 toolkit. The relevant decay model for the prefragments decay is selected based on their mass and excitation energy, and includes nuclear evaporation, statistical multifragmentation, and Fermi breakup models. The UrQMD-AMC approach was validated by comparing its results with data on the rapidity and transverse momentum distributions of neutrons and light fragments from 600A MeV Sn + Sn and 10.6A GeV Au + Ag collisions. This approach supplements the evolution of individual nucleons during a nucleus-nucleus collision simulated with UrQMD with nucleon clustering to simulate also the production of spectator nuclear fragments, which are not produced in the UrQMD model alone.

## Key claim

The hybrid UrQMD-AMC approach, which supplements UrQMD modeling of nucleus-nucleus collisions with nucleon clustering and statistical decay models, can describe spectator fragment production but underestimates yields of fragments with high transverse momenta and, for semi-central collisions, average fragment multiplicities by 20-25%, likely due to neglecting clusterization of elastically scattered nucleons.

## Method

The authors connected version 3.4 of the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model to the Ablation Monte Carlo (AMC) set of models. After UrQMD simulates the first 100 fm/c of a nucleus-nucleus collision, the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) clustering algorithm identifies spectator nucleons and groups them into excited prefragments. The excitation energy of each prefragment is calculated using a combination of the Ericson formula and the parabolic ALADIN approximation, and the prefragment decay is simulated using nuclear evaporation, statistical multifragmentation, and Fermi breakup models from the Geant4 toolkit, selected based on prefragment mass and excitation energy.

## Key numbers

- For 10.6A GeV 197Au collisions with Ag nuclei, the model overestimates the average multiplicity of fragments with Z >= 3 by 20-25% for Z_bound between 45 and 55, in both cascade and Skyrme modes.
- For 10.6A GeV 197Au collisions with Ag nuclei, calculations with both UrQMD-AMC options underestimate the production of Z >= 3 fragments with transverse momentum above 75 MeV/c.
- For 10.6A GeV 197Au collisions with Ag nuclei, the average multiplicity of helium fragments (Z=2) is underestimated by 20-25% for Z_bound between 15 and 40.
- For 600A MeV 124Sn collisions with 124Sn, the UrQMD-AMC calculation with Skyrme forces well describes the measured average multiplicity of projectile spectator neutrons, while the cascade mode systematically underestimates it.
- For 600A MeV 124Sn collisions with 124Sn, both calculation modes underestimate yields of very forward neutrons with y_n - y_0 > 0.3.
- For 3.8A GeV 124Xe collisions with 130Xe and 184W targets, approximately 30% of spectator alpha-particles are expected to propagate through the beam hole and remain undetected by forward calorimeters.

## Context

This work extends the AAMCC-MST model (Svetlichnyi 2020, Nepeivoda 2022, Vasyagina 2025) by connecting it to the widely used UrQMD transport model (Bass 1998, Bleicher 1999), which has been applied to BM@N experiment data (BMN 2023). The approach is validated against experimental data from the EMU01 Collaboration (Adamovich 1999), Jain et al. (1995), Cherry et al. (1994), and Pawlowski et al. (2023). The results provide predictions for spectator nucleon and light fragment production in fixed-target collisions at NICA, enabling systematic uncertainty estimates for detector efficiency and acceptance corrections in the BM@N and MPD experiments.
