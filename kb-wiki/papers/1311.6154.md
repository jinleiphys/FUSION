# Strangeness and charm in quark-gluon hadronization

> This dissertation presents a theoretical study of soft hadron production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We explore the principles governing the hadronization of the expanding QGP fireball, and to understand its properties. Much of the ongoing effort is to demonstrate the validity of a QGP hadronization model which describes the particle production data accurately. We begin with a centrality study of multistrange hadrons from Au-Au collisions at 62.4 GeV at RHIC. We show that the statistical hadronization model (SHM) well describes particle production in QGP hadronization. For all centralities, the physical properties are compatible with the earlier proposed critical hadronization pressure suggesting universal hadronization conditions of QGP. Heavy-ion collisions at LHC present a new challenge for SHM in describing particle production at TeV energy scales. We show that the chemical non-equilibrium model gives a good description of the hadron production in Pb--Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV consistently as a function of centrality. Moreover, the model parameters assume expected values suggested by previous studies at lower energies. The quark-gluon plasma fireball hadronizes at the same universal hadronization conditions, that is a common critical pressure, entropy and energy density. At LHC energies, a significant amount of charm is expected to be produced and it is therefore crucial to incorporate charm into the present description of particle production. We present an upgraded SHARE with CHARM program, that quantifies the effect of charm on the yield of lighter hadrons and physical properties of the hadronizing fireball. In addition to light flavors ($u,d,s$), SHARE with CHARM describes charm hadron production and decays. We observe, that according to present experimental results, charm decays mainly affect the yields of multistrange particles.

## Key claim
The paper claims that the statistical hadronization model (SHM) in its chemical non-equilibrium variant (allowing phase space occupancies γ_q and γ_s to differ from unity) consistently describes hadron production in both RHIC Au-Au collisions at 62.4 GeV and LHC Pb-Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV, with the caveat that the semi-equilibrium model (γ_q=1) fails at LHC energies, particularly for the proton-to-pion ratio.

## Method
The author performed global fits of the statistical hadronization model to experimental hadron yields (π, K, p, φ, Λ, Ξ, Ω) as a function of collision centrality, using both semi-equilibrium (γ_q=1, γ_s free) and non-equilibrium (γ_q and γ_s both free) variants. For RHIC 62.4 GeV data, yields were interpolated across centrality bins using a power-law function of the number of participating nucleons. For LHC 2.76 TeV data, the SHARE with CHARM program was used to incorporate charm hadron production and decays, with charm abundance treated as a model parameter.

## Key numbers
- Critical hadronization pressure P = 82 MeV/fm³ found at both RHIC 62.4 GeV and LHC 2.76 TeV
- Energy density ε = 0.50 GeV/fm³ and entropy density σ = 3.3 fm⁻³ at hadronization for RHIC 62.4 GeV
- Light quark phase space occupancy γ_q ≈ 1.6 at both RHIC 62.4 GeV and LHC 2.76 TeV
- Strangeness phase space occupancy γ_s = 2.2 for most central Au-Au collisions at 62.4 GeV
- Hadronization temperature T ≈ 160 MeV for semi-equilibrium fit at RHIC 62.4 GeV
- Volume per unit rapidity at LHC is 4 times larger than at RHIC 62.4 GeV
- Estimated total charm abundance at RHIC: N_c\bar{c} ≈ 8.6 for central Au-Au at 200 GeV
- Estimated charm abundance at LHC: N_c\bar{c} ∈ (6,45) from D⁰ yield extrapolation, or N_c\bar{c} = 246 ± 154 from pp cross-section scaling
- D⁰ meson yield at LHC: dN_D⁰/dy ∈ (1.3, 9.0) from extrapolated p_T spectrum
- Proton-to-pion ratio at LHC: p/π = 0.046

## Context
This work extends the statistical hadronization model approach of Rafelski (1991) and Letessier (2002) to LHC energies, confirming the universal hadronization conditions (critical pressure, energy density, entropy density) previously proposed for SPS energies by Rafelski (2009). The analysis contradicts the semi-equilibrium SHM fit presented by Ivanov (2013) for LHC data, which suggested SHM fails at TeV energies. The upgraded SHARE with CHARM program enables future studies of charm quark effects on lighter hadron yields and entropy production, particularly the unexplained steeper-than-linear entropy growth with centrality at LHC.
