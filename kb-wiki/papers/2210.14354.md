# Bayesian Estimation of the $S$ Factor and Thermonuclear Reaction Rate for $^{16}$O(p,$γ$)$^{17}$F

> The $^{16}$O(p,$γ$)$^{17}$F reaction is the slowest hydrogen-burning process in the CNO mass region. Its thermonuclear rate sensitively impacts predictions of oxygen isotopic ratios in a number of astrophysical sites, including AGB stars. The reaction has been measured several times at low bombarding energies using a variety of techniques. The most recent evaluated experimental rates have a reported uncertainty of about 7.5\% below $1$~GK. However, the previous rate estimate represents a best guess only and was not based on rigorous statistical methods. We apply a Bayesian model to fit all reliable $^{16}$O(p,$γ$)$^{17}$F cross section data, and take into account independent contributions of statistical and systematic uncertainties. The nuclear reaction model employed is a single-particle potential model involving a Woods-Saxon potential for generating the radial bound state wave function. The model has three physical parameters, the radius and diffuseness of the Woods-Saxon potential, and the asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) of the final bound state in $^{17}$F. We find that performing the Bayesian $S$ factor fit using ANCs as scaling parameters has a distinct advantage over adopting spectroscopic factors instead. Based on these results, we present the first statistically rigorous estimation of experimental $^{16}$O(p,$γ$)$^{17}$F reaction rates, with uncertainties ($\pm 4.2$\%) of about half the previously reported values.

## Key claim

The paper presents the first statistically rigorous estimation of experimental $^{16}$O(p,$\gamma$)$^{17}$F reaction rates using Bayesian methods, achieving uncertainties of $\pm 4.2\%$, about half the previously reported 7.5% uncertainty, but this result depends on using asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) rather than spectroscopic factors as scaling parameters in the single-particle potential model.

## Method

The authors applied a hierarchical Bayesian model to fit all reliable $^{16}$O(p,$\gamma$)$^{17}$F cross section data from four experiments (Hester et al. 1958; Chow et al. 1975; Becker et al. 1982; Morlock et al. 1997), separately accounting for statistical and systematic uncertainties. The nuclear reaction model employed a single-particle potential model with a Woods-Saxon potential for generating radial bound state wave functions, treating three adjustable parameters: the radius and diffuseness of the Woods-Saxon potential, and the asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) of the final bound state in $^{17}$F. The Bayesian framework allowed simultaneous fitting of total cross section data and individual transition data, including data sets previously excluded from analyses.

## Key numbers

- Thermonuclear reaction rate uncertainty of $\pm 4.2\%$ below 1 GK, compared to the previous reported uncertainty of about 7.5%
- The $^{16}$O(p,$\gamma$)$^{17}$F reaction has a Q-value of $600.27 \pm 0.25$ keV
- The lowest-lying resonance is located at approximately 2.7 MeV laboratory energy
- Hester et al. (1958) measured total cross sections at six center-of-mass energies between 132 and 160 keV, with statistical uncertainties ranging from 14% to 40% and a total systematic uncertainty of 14%
- Chow et al. (1975) measured cross sections at center-of-mass energies between 795 and 2404 keV, with statistical uncertainties from 3% to 12% and a combined systematic uncertainty of 5%
- Becker et al. (1982) measured at a single center-of-mass energy of 853 keV with 13% statistical uncertainty and 5% systematic uncertainty
- Morlock et al. (1997) reported cross sections below 3.5 MeV center-of-mass energy, with statistical errors between 1.5% and 3% and a systematic uncertainty of 10%

## Context

This work extends the hierarchical Bayesian method first presented by Iliadis et al. (2016) for thermonuclear reaction rate estimation, which was previously applied to Big Bang Nucleosynthesis reactions and reactions using one-level R-matrix approximations. The study addresses limitations of previous $^{16}$O(p,$\gamma$)$^{17}$F rate evaluations by Iliadis (2008) and the NACRE compilation, which used conventional $\chi^2$ methods with ad hoc assumptions for combining data sets and handling statistical versus systematic uncertainties. The results enable more precise predictions of oxygen isotopic ratios ($^{17}$O/$^{16}$O and $^{18}$O/$^{16}$O) in astrophysical contexts including AGB stars and interpretation of presolar stardust grain measurements.
