# Superfluid fraction in the crystal phase of the inner crust of neutron stars

> In the most extended layer of the inner crust of neutron stars, nuclear matter is believed to form a crystal of clusters immersed in a superfluid neutron gas. Here we analyze this phase of matter within fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations using Skyrme-type energy density functionals for the mean field and a separable interaction in the pairing channel. The periodicity of the lattice is taken into account using Bloch boundary conditions, in order to describe the interplay between band structure and superfluidity. A relative flow between the clusters and the surrounding neutron gas is introduced in a time-independent way. As a consequence, the complex order parameter develops a phase, and in the rest frame of the superfluid one finds a counterflow between neutrons inside and outside the clusters. The neutron superfluid fraction is computed from the resulting current. Our results indicate that at densities above 0.03 fm$^{-3}$, more than 90% of the neutrons are effectively superfluid, independently of the detailed choice of the interaction, cluster charge, and lattice geometry. This fraction is only slightly lower than the one obtained recently within linear response theory on top of the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer approximation, and it approaches the hydrodynamic limit for strong pairing. As a consequence, it is likely that the inner crust alone can provide a sufficient superfluid angular momentum reservoir to explain pulsar glitches.

## Key claim

The authors claim that at baryon densities above 0.03 fm\(^{-3}\), more than 90% of neutrons in the inner crust crystal phase are effectively superfluid, and this result is independent of the detailed choice of interaction, cluster charge, and lattice geometry. This conclusion is hedged by the condition that the superfluid fraction is computed from fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations that include the phase of the pairing gap induced by relative flow, which the authors argue was missing in prior linear-response treatments.

## Method

The authors performed fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations for neutrons and Hartree-Fock (HF) calculations for protons in a periodic lattice, using Skyrme-type energy density functionals (SLy4 and BSk24) for the mean field and a separable Gaussian interaction in the pairing channel. Bloch boundary conditions were imposed to account for the lattice periodicity, and a relative flow between clusters and the surrounding neutron gas was introduced via a Galilean boost, allowing the complex order parameter to develop a phase from which the neutron superfluid fraction was computed from the averaged current.

## Key numbers

* At baryon densities above 0.03 fm\(^{-3}\), more than 90% of neutrons are effectively superfluid, independent of interaction choice, cluster charge, and lattice geometry.
* For the SLy4 interaction at baryon density \(\rho_b = 0.033\) fm\(^{-3}\) with proton number \(Z=20\) and body-centered cubic cell extension \(L=33\) fm, the average pairing gap is about 2 MeV.
* For the SLy4 interaction at \(\rho_b = 0.043\) fm\(^{-3}\) with \(Z=20\) and simple cubic cell extension \(L=24\) fm, the coherence length in the gas is \(\xi_{\text{SLy}} \simeq 8\) fm and in the cluster \(\xi_{\text{SLy}} \simeq 50\) fm; for BSk24 under the same conditions, \(\xi_{\text{BSk}} \simeq 5\) fm in the gas and \(\xi_{\text{BSk}} \simeq 25\) fm in the cluster.
* The HFB pairing gap in the cluster for SLy4 is about 0.5 MeV, while the Local-Density Approximation (LDA) predicts only about 0.05 MeV, a difference of one order of magnitude.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous HFB studies of the rod (spaghetti) and slab (lasagna) pasta phases (Almirante 2024, Almirante 2024A) to the three-dimensional crystal phase of the inner crust. It contradicts earlier band-structure calculations (Chamel 2012) that predicted very strong entrainment and thus a small superfluid fraction, and it resolves a discrepancy with superfluid hydrodynamics (Martin 2016) by showing that HFB results approach the hydrodynamic limit for strong pairing. The finding that the inner crust alone can provide a sufficient superfluid angular momentum reservoir to explain pulsar glitches enables more realistic modeling of glitch mechanisms without requiring contributions from the core.
