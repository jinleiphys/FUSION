# The Tucson-Melbourne Three-Nucleon Force in the automatized Partial Wave Decomposition

> A recently developed procedure for a partial wave decomposition of a three-nucleon force is applied to the pi-pi, pi-rho and rho-rho components of the Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon potential. The resulting matrix elements for the pi-pi and pi-rho components are compared with the values obtained using the standard approach to the partial wave decomposition, in which the pi-rho expressions for the matrix elements are also derived and presented. Several numerical tests and results for the triton binding energy and the correlation function prove the reliability and efficiency of the new method.

## Key claim

The automatized partial wave decomposition (aPWD) method reliably reproduces the standard partial wave decomposition results for the pi-pi and pi-rho components of the Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon force, but the authors note that the standard approach requires a large number of intermediate partial waves (up to j_max=5) to achieve convergence for some channel combinations, and even that may be insufficient for certain small matrix elements.

## Method

The authors apply their previously developed aPWD procedure to the pi-pi, pi-rho, and rho-rho components of the Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon potential. The method uses symbolic computation (Mathematica) to generate Fortran code for the spin-momentum and isospin parts of the interaction, then performs five-dimensional numerical integrations over angular variables to obtain partial-wave matrix elements in the LS-coupling basis, which are subsequently recoupled to the jJ-coupling basis used in Faddeev equations. The aPWD approach also directly computes matrix elements of V^(1)(1+P) without separately decomposing the permutation operator, avoiding the need for intermediate partial-wave summations.

## Key numbers

- The pi-pi, pi-rho, and rho-rho components of the TM 3NF are evaluated using parameters from Coon (1993): a=1.03 mu^-1, b=-2.62 mu^-3, c=0.91 mu^-3, d=-0.753 mu^-3, with mu=139.6 MeV
- Matrix elements are shown for momenta p'=0.132 fm^-1, q'=0.132 fm^-1, q=0.132 fm^-1 and for p'=0.711 fm^-1, q'=0.132 fm^-1, q=2.84 fm^-1
- The aPWD calculation of V^(1)(1+P) matrix elements requires about 1.5 times the computing time needed for V^(1) alone
- For the pi-rho force, the "Kroll-Ruderman" term dominates for momenta p below 2 fm^-1, while the two "Delta" terms become larger for p>2 fm^-1 but have opposite signs
- Convergence tests for the pi-rho and full TM forces show that for channel combination (1,1) the smallest number of partial waves already gives the aPWD limit, while for other combinations partial waves up to j_max=5 are needed; for one case (Fig. f10c) even j_max=5 is insufficient to reach the aPWD limit

## Context

This work extends the earlier aPWD method introduced by Golak (2010) to all components of the Tucson-Melbourne three-nucleon force, which was originally developed by Coon (1979) and Ellis (1985). Previous calculations by Stadler (1995) and Witala (1995) studied the TM force effects on triton binding energy and scattering observables but were restricted to total angular momenta j <= 2 in the two-nucleon subsystem. The new method enables reliable inclusion of shorter-range pi-rho and rho-rho components in higher partial waves, which is needed to address discrepancies between calculations using only the pi-pi part and experimental data at higher energies. The approach is also designed to handle the numerous momentum-spin-isospin structures expected from chiral perturbation theory three-nucleon forces at higher orders.
