# Evaluation of reactor neutrino flux: issues and uncertainties

> Evaluation of the reactor $\barν_e$ flux and spectrum is an essential ingredient of their application in the neutrino oscillation studies. Two anomalies, i.e. discrepancies between the observed and expected count rates, are widely discussed at the resent time. The total rate is $\sim$ 6\% lower than the expectation at all distances $>$ 10 m from the reactor. And there is a shoulder (often referred to as "bump") at neutrino energies 5-7 MeV, not predicted in the calculated spectrum. I review the ways the flux and spectrum is evaluated and concentrate on the error budget. I argue that far reaching conclusions based on these anomalies should await a thorough understanding of the uncertainties of the spectrum, and point out possible standard physics sources of the anomalies.

## Key claim

The two widely-discussed reactor neutrino anomalies, a ~6% deficit in total rate and a spectral "bump" at 5–7 MeV, may have standard physics explanations rooted in the uncertainties of the predicted reactor antineutrino spectrum, and far-reaching conclusions (such as invoking sterile neutrinos) should await a thorough understanding of those uncertainties.

## Method

The paper reviews the two principal methods for determining reactor antineutrino spectra: the "ab initio" summation method, which sums the beta-decay contributions of all fission fragments using fission yields and decay databases, and the conversion method, which starts from experimentally measured aggregate electron fission spectra (from ILL, Grenoble for ²³⁵U, ²³⁹Pu, ²⁴¹Pu; and later for ²³⁸U) and converts them to antineutrino spectra by fitting the electron spectrum to a sum of virtual beta-decay branches. The author evaluates the error budget for each method, focusing on uncertainties from fission yields, branching ratios, forbidden transitions, and corrections from weak magnetism, finite nuclear size, and radiative effects.

## Key numbers

- The global fit of measured reactor antineutrino capture rates, normalized to the Huber-Mueller prediction and corrected for three-flavor oscillations, gives a ratio R = 0.942 ± 0.009(exp) ± 0.025(model), indicating a ~6% deficit at distances >10 m.
- The experimental uncertainty on R is ~1%, while the model uncertainty is ~2.5%.
- When different assumptions about weak magnetism and shape factors were applied to the non-uniquely forbidden component in the conversion method, the antineutrino spectrum varied by as much as 4%.
- The two standard fission-yield libraries (JEFF-3.1.1 and corrected ENDF/B-VII.1) agree within 6% at all energies after corrections.
- About 5% of the relevant nuclei are sufficiently far from stability that no measured spectra are available, requiring modeling.
- The weak magnetism correction uncertainty is estimated at ~20% for allowed decays and ~30% for forbidden decays.
- The fission yield uncertainty contribution to the summation method is tentatively ~10%.
- A 5% uncertainty on the conversion method is tentatively assigned in the absence of a detailed multi-parameter sensitivity study.

## Context

This review extends the discussion of the "reactor anomaly" (first highlighted by Mention et al. and related to LSND, MiniBooNE, GALLEX, and SAGE indications) and the spectral "bump" observed by Daya-Bay, RENO, and Double Chooz. It builds on the Huber-Mueller model (Huber 2011, Mueller et al. 2011) and the earlier ILL electron spectrum measurements (Schreckenbach et al. 1980s), and references the forthcoming review by the author. The paper identifies specific needed experiments, particularly better measurements of the ²³⁸U spectrum and studies of the effect of reactor neutron spectrum hardness, and calls for reducing systematic uncertainties before drawing conclusions about physics beyond the Standard Model.
