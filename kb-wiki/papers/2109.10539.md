# Nuclear charge radii of Na isotopes: A tale of two theories

> The accuracy of atomic theory calculations limits the extraction of nuclear charge radii from isotope shift measurements of odd-proton nuclei. For Na isotopes, though precise spectroscopic measurements have existed since more than half a century, calculations by different methods offer a wide range of values. Here, we present accurate atomic calculations to reliably extract the Na charge radii. By combining experimental matter radii with nuclear coupled-cluster calculations based on nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces, we constrain the parameters obtained from the atomic calculations. Therefore, this study guides atomic theory and highlights the importance of using accurate atomic and nuclear computations in our understanding of the size of light nuclei.

# Nuclear charge radii of Na isotopes: A tale of two theories

## Key claim
By combining experimental matter radii with nuclear coupled-cluster calculations based on nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces, the authors constrain atomic parameters to extract Na charge radii; however, the extracted charge radii and neutron skin uncertainties remain dominated by the calculated atomic parameters, and the nuclear theory predictions for charge radii agree well with experiment for Ne and Mg but the preferred K_SMS value for Na sits at the lower edge of the atomic calculation confidence interval.

## Method
The authors performed relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) atomic calculations using the analytic response method with singles, doubles, and full triples excitations (AR-RCCSDT) to compute isotope shift constants (field shift F and specific mass shift K_SMS) for Na and Mg+. For nuclear structure, they employed single-reference coupled-cluster calculations with the DeltaNNLO_GO interaction (450 MeV cutoff) starting from axially symmetric Hartree-Fock reference states, computing ground-state properties of odd-mass Na isotopes. The atomic calculations were benchmarked against precise isotope shift measurements in Mg+ and against a semi-empirical fit using experimental matter radii combined with nuclear theory predictions of neutron skin thickness.

## Key numbers
- Field shift constant F for Na D1 and D2 lines: -39.2(3) MHz/fm^2 from AR-RCCSDT
- Specific mass shift constant K_SMS for Na D1 and D2 lines: 109(3) GHz amu from AR-RCCSDT
- Semi-empirical fit from neutron skin comparison yields K_SMS = 105.3(1.3) GHz amu for Na
- Field shift constant F for Mg+ D1 and D2 lines: -126.3(7) MHz/fm^2 from AR-RCCSDT
- Specific mass shift constant K_SMS for Mg+ D1 and D2 lines: 374(7) GHz amu from AR-RCCSDT
- Experimental K_SMS for Mg+ derived from measurements: 369.3(3) GHz amu (D1) and 367.7(3) GHz amu (D2)
- Charge radius of Na-23: 2.9935(38) fm
- Charge radius of Na-31: 3.339(13)[137] fm (statistical and systematic uncertainties)
- Charge radius of Na-19: 2.99(3) fm (nuclear theory prediction)
- Neutron skin for Na-31 from nuclear theory: 0.37(3) fm
- Neutron skin for Na-33 from nuclear theory: 0.45(3) fm

## Context
This work extends the analytic response RCC method (previously applied to indium by Sahoo (2020) and to lithium-like systems) to include full triples excitations for many-electron systems, enabling charge radius extraction for Na isotopes where prior atomic calculations by Tupitsyn (2003), Berengut (2003), Korol (2007), Safronova (2001), and Sahoo (2010) gave a wide range of K_SMS values. The nuclear calculations follow the approach of Sun (2018) and Samadder (2020) for Ne and Mg isotopes, using the DeltaNNLO_GO interaction from Jiang (2020). The results enable future experiments at facilities like FRIB to test nuclear theory predictions of a kink in charge radii at N=20 and monotonous neutron skin increase in neutron-rich Na isotopes, and similar calculations are needed for other odd-Z elements where atomic theory input is critical for charge radius extraction from isotope shift data.
