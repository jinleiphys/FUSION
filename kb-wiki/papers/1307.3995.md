# On the Maximum Mass of Neutron Stars

> One of the most intringuing questions about neutron stars concerns their maximum mass. The answer is intimately related to the properties of matter at densities far beyond that found in heavy atomic nuclei. The current view on the internal constitution of neutron stars and on their maximum mass, both from theoretical and observational studies, are briefly reviewed.

## Key claim

This review paper summarizes the current theoretical and observational understanding of the maximum mass of neutron stars, but emphasizes that the actual value remains highly uncertain because it depends on the poorly known equation of state of matter at densities up to about ten times normal nuclear density, and that different theoretical models predict maximum masses ranging from about 1.3 to 2.8 solar masses.

## Method

The authors review the historical development of the concept of a maximum mass for degenerate stars, from Chandrasekhar's work on white dwarfs through Landau's method and the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. They then survey modern theoretical predictions for the maximum mass based on different assumptions about the composition of neutron star cores (purely nucleonic, hyperonic, mesonic, or quark matter) and different computational approaches (microscopic many-body calculations, relativistic mean-field models, perturbative QCD, and effective quark matter models). The review also summarizes observational mass measurements from binary pulsar systems.

## Key numbers

- Normal nuclear density: $\rho_0 = 2.8 \times 10^{14}~\mathrm{g~cm^{-3}}$ (corresponding baryon number density $n_0 = 0.16~\mathrm{fm^{-3}}$)
- Chandrasekhar mass limit for white dwarfs: $M_{\mathrm{Ch}} = 1.46 (2Y_e)^2~M_\odot$
- Landau's maximum mass estimate for neutron stars from Newtonian ideal Fermi gas: approximately $1.8~M_\odot$
- Oppenheimer and Volkoff's 1939 maximum mass for ideal neutron Fermi gas: approximately $0.7~M_\odot$
- Zwicky's 1938-1939 estimate using incompressible matter: approximately $11~M_\odot$
- Purely nucleonic core microscopic calculations predict maximum masses in the range $1.8~M_\odot$ to $2.5~M_\odot$
- Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations with nucleons only: $2.0-2.5~M_\odot$
- Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations with nucleons and hyperons: $1.3-1.6~M_\odot$
- Dirac Brueckner Hartree-Fock (nucleons only): $2.0-2.5~M_\odot$
- Variational chain summation (nucleons only): $2.0-2.2~M_\odot$
- Perturbative QCD (nucleons and quarks): $2.0~M_\odot$
- Relativistic Mean Field (nucleons only): $2.1-2.8~M_\odot$
- Relativistic Mean Field (nucleons and hyperons): $2.0-2.3~M_\odot$
- RMF/NJL (nucleons and quarks): $2.0-2.2~M_\odot$
- RMF/Modified Bag Model (nucleons and quarks): $2.0-2.5~M_\odot$
- Maximum density at center of most massive neutron stars: $\lesssim 5\times 10^{15}~\mathrm{g~cm^{-3}}$
- Density at bottom of outer crust: approximately $4\times 10^{11}~\mathrm{g~cm^{-3}}$
- Density at bottom of inner crust: approximately $\rho_0/2$
- Density range of outer core: approximately $\rho_0/2$ to $2\rho_0$
- Density range of inner core: up to approximately $10\rho_0$
- Measured mass of GRO J0422+32: $3.97 \pm 0.95~M_\odot$ (but possibly substantially lower due to systematic errors)

## Context

This review extends the long line of theoretical work on neutron star maximum mass that began with Landau (1932), Oppenheimer and Volkoff (1939), and Zwicky (1938-1939), and continues through modern many-body calculations such as those by Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998) and various relativistic mean-field models. The paper highlights the "hyperon puzzle" where Brueckner-Hartree-Fock calculations including hyperons predict maximum masses below 1.6 solar masses, contradicting observations of massive pulsars like PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010). The review enables future work by clarifying which theoretical approaches can and cannot accommodate the observed 2-solar-mass pulsars, and by identifying the need for better constraints on the high-density equation of state from both nuclear physics experiments and additional neutron star mass measurements.
