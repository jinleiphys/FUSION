# Particle and pair spectra for strongly correlated Fermi gases: A real-frequency solver

> The strongly attractive Fermi gas in the BCS-BEC crossover is efficiently described in terms of coupled fermions and fermion pairs, or molecules. We compute the spectral functions of both fermions and pairs in the normal state near the superfluid transition using a Keldysh formulation in real frequency. The mutual influence between fermions and pairs is captured by solving the self-consistent Luttinger-Ward equations: these include both the damping of fermions by scattering off dressed pairs, as well as the decay of pair states by dissociation into two dressed fermions. The pair spectra encode contact correlations between fermions and form the basis for computing dynamical response functions and transport properties.

# Particle and pair spectra for strongly correlated Fermi gases: A real-frequency solver

## Key claim

The paper presents a real-frequency solver for the self-consistent Luttinger-Ward equations that computes fermion and pair spectral functions directly in real frequency, circumventing the ill-defined analytical continuation from imaginary frequency. The method works reliably with standard numerical precision even for sharp spectral features narrower than the grid spacing, provided the self-energy varies slowly on the scale of the temperature.

## Method

The coupled integral equations for fermion and pair Green functions (Dyson equations with self-energies from one-loop diagrams) are solved iteratively on a real-frequency and momentum grid, using a Keldysh formulation in equilibrium. The key technical innovation is an "inverse" integrator that interpolates the denominator of the Green function linearly between grid points, allowing analytical integration of the momentum and frequency integrals via a logarithmic formula. The imaginary parts of the self-energies are computed from convolution integrals of spectral functions weighted by Fermi and Bose occupation factors, and the real parts are obtained by Kramers-Kronig transformation to ensure exact analyticity.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature at unitarity: $T_c \simeq 0.16 T_F$ from experiment and quantum Monte Carlo, reproduced by self-consistent Luttinger-Ward theory with $(\beta\mu)_c \approx 2.5$
- Non-self-consistent calculation with bare propagators at unitarity yields $(\beta\mu)_c \approx 1.5$
- Grid spacing used for lowest temperature $T/T_F = 0.16$: $\Delta\varepsilon = 0.08\varepsilon_F$ and $\Delta p = 0.08 k_F$
- Frequency grid domain: $\varepsilon = -100T$ to $100T$ with spacing $\Delta\varepsilon = 0.5T$; momentum grid: $p = 0$ to $10\sqrt{2mT}$ with spacing $\Delta p = 0.2\sqrt{2mT}$
- Convergence typically reached after a dozen iterations, slower near $T_c$
- Multi-loop contributions modify the density equation of state by less than 10% even at $T_c$ (Diagrammatic Monte Carlo, Van Houcke et al. 2012)

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent Luttinger-Ward (or self-consistent T-matrix) approach developed by Haussmann (1993, 2007) and others (Hanai 2014, Frank 2018, Pini 2019) for the BCS-BEC crossover of strongly attractive Fermi gases. Previous numerical solutions were performed in imaginary (Matsubara) frequency, requiring analytical continuation to obtain real-frequency spectra. The new real-frequency solver enables direct computation of dynamical response functions and transport properties without the ill-defined continuation step. The results at unitarity show good agreement with spectral data from Johansen (2024) and reproduce the thermodynamic density and contact from earlier imaginary-frequency computations.
