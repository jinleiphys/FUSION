# Semiclassical theory of surface plasmons in spheroidal clusters

> A microscopic theory of linear response based on the Vlasov equation is extended to systems having spheroidal equilibrium shape. The solution of the linearized Vlasov equation, which gives a semiclassical version of the random phase approximation, is studied for electrons moving in a deformed equilibrium mean field. The deformed field has been approximated by a cavity of spheroidal shape, both prolate and oblate. Contrary to spherical systems, there is now a coupling among excitations of different multipolarity induced by the interaction among constituents. Explicit calculations are performed for the dipole response of deformed clusters of different size. In all cases studied here the photoabsorption strength for prolate clusters always displays a typical double-peaked structure. For oblate clusters we find that the high--frequency component of the plasmon doublet can get fragmented in the medium size region ($N \sim 250$). This fragmentation is related to the presence of two kinds of three-dimensional electron orbits in oblate cavities. The possible scaling of our semiclassical equations with the valence electron number and density is investigated.

## Key claim

The paper extends a semiclassical Vlasov-equation theory of linear response (a semiclassical version of RPA) from spherical to spheroidal (both prolate and oblate) clusters, finding that for oblate clusters the high-frequency component of the plasmon doublet can become fragmented in medium-size clusters (N ~ 250) due to the existence of two distinct types of three-dimensional electron orbits (W-type and B-type) in oblate cavities, whereas prolate clusters always display a typical double-peaked structure without such fragmentation.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Vlasov equation for electrons moving in a deformed equilibrium mean field approximated by a spheroidal cavity (both prolate and oblate), using action-angle variables and spheroidal coordinates to compute the semiclassical propagator D^0 and then solving the coupled one-dimensional integral equations (Eq. 2.13) for the collective propagator D. The residual interaction is a Coulomb-type force with a local-field correction G(k) appropriate for sodium, and calculations are performed for sodium clusters with N=254 valence electrons and deformation parameters η ranging from 1.0 to 2.0.

## Key numbers

- Cluster size: N = 254 valence electrons (sodium), with Wigner-Seitz parameter r_s = 4.0 (in units of Bohr radius)
- Deformation parameter η = R_>/R_< studied for range 1 ≤ η ≤ 2, with spherical limit taken at η = 1.001
- For prolate clusters at η = 1.5: classical Mie theory predicts ω_x,y = 1.073 ω_Mie and ω_z = 0.836 ω_Mie; the calculated collective plasmon peaks are blue-shifted by ~7% relative to these classical values
- For prolate clusters at η = 2.0: classical Mie theory predicts ω_x,y = 1.113 ω_Mie and ω_z = 0.722 ω_Mie
- For oblate clusters at η = 1.5: classical Mie theory predicts ω_x,y = 0.912 ω_Mie and ω_z = 1.157 ω_Mie; M=1 collective plasmons are blue-shifted by ~7%, while M=0 collective peaks are blue-shifted by ~10-15%
- For oblate clusters at η = 2.0: classical Mie theory predicts ω_x,y = 0.842 ω_Mie and ω_z = 1.258 ω_Mie
- The fraction of electrons in B-type orbits in oblate cavities reaches 50% at η ≈ 1.7
- The energy-weighted sum rule (area under photoabsorption curves) is conserved within 2%
- A finite broadening parameter ε = 0.002 ω_Mie was used in calculations, modifying the width by ~5%
- Upper integration limit k_max = 4 p_F used when solving the coupled integral equations

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical Vlasov-equation approach of Dellafiore, Matera, and collaborators (1990s) from spherical systems to spheroidal geometry, building on the analogy between the nuclear giant dipole resonance (first described by Danos) and cluster surface plasmons. The spheroidal cavity model follows the suggestion of Strutinsky et al. that a spheroidal cavity gives a more realistic description than the oscillator potential (used by Clemenger, inspired by the Nilsson model), while remaining integrable unlike deformed Saxon-Woods potentials studied by Arvieu et al. which become partially chaotic. The work enables semiclassical calculations of plasmon response in deformed clusters without the numerical complexity of fully quantum RPA calculations, which Ekardt and Penzar (1990s) had previously performed only for prolate clusters.
