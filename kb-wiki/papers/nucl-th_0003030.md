# How unique is the Asymptotic Normalisation Coefficient (ANC) method?

> The asymptotic normalisation coefficients (ANC) for the vertex $^{10}$B $\to$ $^9$Be + p is deduced from a set of different proton transfer reactions at different energies. This set should ensure the peripheral character of the reaction and availability of data for the elastic channels. The problems associated with the characteristics of the data and the analysis are discussed. For a subgroup of the set of available data, the uniqueness property of the extracted ANC is fulfilled. However, more measurements are needed before a definite conclusion can be drawn.

## Key claim

For a subgroup of the available proton transfer data that are clearly peripheral and have quotable normalisation errors, the extracted asymptotic normalisation coefficient (ANC) for the vertex \(^{10}\text{B} \rightarrow ^{9}\text{Be} + p\) is unique; however, with the present data, a definite conclusion on the uniqueness property of the ANC method cannot be drawn, and more measurements are needed.

## Method

The authors extracted the ANC \(C^2_{19}\) for the \(^{10}\text{B} \rightarrow ^{9}\text{Be} + p\) vertex by analysing differential cross sections from a set of proton transfer reactions at different energies: \(^{9}\text{Be}(^{10}\text{B},^{9}\text{Be})^{10}\text{B}\) at 100 MeV, \(^{9}\text{Be}(d,n)^{10}\text{B}\) at 7 and 15 MeV, \(^{10}\text{B}(d,^{3}\text{He})^{9}\text{Be}\) at 11.8 MeV, and \(^{12}\text{C}(^{10}\text{B},^{9}\text{Be})^{13}\text{N}\) at 100 MeV combined with \(^{12}\text{C}(d,n)^{13}\text{N}\) at 9 and 12.4 MeV. For each reaction, optical model potentials were fitted to elastic scattering data, and the DWBA (Distorted Wave Born Approximation) was used to calculate transfer cross sections; the experimental spectroscopic factor was obtained by a \(\chi^2\) fit of the DWBA calculation to the forward-angle transfer data, and the ANC was derived from the relation \(C_{AB \ell j} = \mathcal{S}^{1/2} \, b_{AB \ell j}\), where \(b_{AB \ell j}\) is the asymptotic normalisation of the single-particle wave function.

## Key numbers

*   \(C^2_{19} = 4.9 \pm 0.25\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) from \(^{9}\text{Be}(^{10}\text{B},^{9}\text{Be})^{10}\text{B}\) at 100 MeV, with a systematic error of 7%.
*   \(C^2_{19} = 4.8 \pm 0.35\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) from \(^{9}\text{Be}(d,n)^{10}\text{B}\) at 7 MeV, with a systematic error of 20%.
*   \(C^2_{19} = 4.92 \pm 0.54\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) from \(^{10}\text{B}(d,^{3}\text{He})^{9}\text{Be}\) at 11.8 MeV, using \(C^2_{12} = 3.9 \pm 0.06\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) for the \(^{3}\text{He} \rightarrow d + p\) vertex, with a systematic error of 25%.
*   \(C^2_{19} = 6.09 \pm 0.54\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) from \(^{9}\text{Be}(d,n)^{10}\text{B}\) at 15 MeV, but this reaction was found not to be peripheral and thus not useful for the ANC method.
*   \(C^2_{19} = 2.98 \pm 0.63\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) and \(2.30 \pm 0.47\ \text{fm}^{-1}\) from \(^{12}\text{C}(^{10}\text{B},^{9}\text{Be})^{13}\text{N}\) at 100 MeV combined with \(^{12}\text{C}(d,n)^{13}\text{N}\) at 9 MeV using data from Schelin (1984) and Davis (1972), respectively; these values were considered unreliable due to large systematic uncertainties and reaction mechanism issues.

## Context

This work extends tests of the ANC method, previously applied to the \(^{16}\text{O}(^{3}\text{He},d)^{17}\text{F}\) reaction by Gagliardi (1999) and to the \(^{7}\text{Be}(d,n)^{8}\text{B}\) reaction by Liu (1996), by systematically checking the uniqueness of the ANC for the \(^{10}\text{B} \rightarrow ^{9}\text{Be} + p\) vertex using multiple transfer reactions. The results highlight that while the method appears consistent for a subset of peripheral reactions with well-characterised data, the large systematic errors and non-peripheral contributions in other datasets prevent a definitive validation. The authors conclude that further measurements with high-resolution, carefully normalised cross sections for both transfer and elastic channels are crucial to unambiguously test the uniqueness of ANCs, and that the reaction mechanism, particularly for deuteron-induced reactions, must be better understood before the method can be reliably applied.
