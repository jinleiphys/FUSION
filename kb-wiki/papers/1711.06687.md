# Causality of fluid dynamics for high-energy nuclear collisions

> Dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics is not always causal and can favor superluminal signal propagation under certain circumstances. On the other hand, high-energy nuclear collisions have a microscopic description in terms of QCD and are expected to follow the causality principle of special relativity. We discuss under which conditions the fluid evolutions for a radial expansion are hyperbolic and how the properties of the solutions are encoded in the associated characteristic curves. The expansion dynamics is causal in relativistic sense if the characteristic velocities are smaller than the speed of light. We obtain a concrete inequality from this constraint and discuss how it can be violated for certain initial conditions. We argue that causality poses a bound to the applicability of relativistic fluid dynamics. }

## Key claim

Dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics, as applied to high-energy nuclear collisions, is not automatically causal; the expansion dynamics is causal only if the characteristic velocities derived from the equations of motion are smaller than the speed of light, and this condition can be violated for certain initial conditions, thereby posing a bound on the applicability of relativistic fluid dynamics.

## Method

The authors analyze the causality of relativistic fluid dynamics for a radial expansion geometry relevant to heavy-ion collisions, using generalized Bjorken coordinates to describe both longitudinal and transverse expansion. They derive the full set of partial differential equations for energy density, fluid velocity, shear stress, and bulk viscous pressure from energy-momentum conservation and the DNMR (Denicol-Niemi-Molnar-Rischke) relaxation equations. Causality is then studied by examining the characteristic curves of the hyperbolic system and deriving an energy inequality that bounds the growth of perturbations, with the condition that all characteristic velocities must be subluminal.

## Key numbers

- The causality condition is expressed as a concrete inequality requiring that the characteristic velocities (the eigenvalues of the matrix \(M = A^{-1}B\)) be smaller than the speed of light; no explicit numerical bound is given in the truncated text.
- The energy inequality for linearized perturbations takes the form \(0 \le E(t) \le e^{2\mu t} E(0)\), where \(\mu\) is a positive constant chosen to make a quadratic form negative definite.
- The domain of dependence of a point \(P=(x^0,x^1)\) is bounded by the two extreme characteristic curves with maximal and minimal velocities, and the inequality \(\frac{1}{2}\int_{P_l}^{P_r} dx^1 (\overline{\delta J},\overline{\delta J}) \le \frac{1}{2}\int_{A_l}^{A_r} dx^1 (\overline{\delta J},\overline{\delta J})\) is derived.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of causality in dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics, building on the foundational formulations of Muller (1967) and Israel and Stewart (1979), as well as the DNMR derivation from the Boltzmann equation by Denicol, Niemi, Molnar, and Rischke (2012). It also connects to holographic approaches such as those of Baier, Romatschke, Son, Starinets, and Stephanov (2008) and the AdS/CFT correspondence of Maldacena (1998). The results enable a systematic check of whether a given fluid-dynamical simulation of heavy-ion collisions respects relativistic causality, and provide a criterion for when the fluid-dynamical description itself breaks down.
