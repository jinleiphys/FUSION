# Probing Quark Gluon Liquid Using Transverse Momentum Fluctuations

> The onset of equilibration in nuclear collisions can lead to related trends in the centrality dependence of the average transverse momentum, pt fluctuations and net charge fluctuations. We extend the transport description of S. Gavin (Phys. Rev. Lett. 92, 162301 (2004)) to include radial flow.

# Probing Quark Gluon Liquid Using Transverse Momentum Fluctuations

## Key claim
The onset of equilibration in nuclear collisions can explain the centrality dependence of transverse momentum fluctuations, but radial flow must be included to describe the most central collisions; the combined thermalization and flow contributions are essentially independent of beam energy.

## Method
The authors extend a Boltzmann transport description (Gavin, 2004) that uses a survival probability S to model thermalization, adding radial flow via an average parton drift velocity v that blue-shifts the effective temperature. They compute dynamic transverse momentum fluctuations from two-body correlations using Langevin noise added to the Boltzmann equation, and compare to CERES, STAR, and PHENIX data.

## Key numbers
- The ratio ⟨δp_t1δp_t2⟩^(1/2)/⟨p_t⟩ for central collisions is roughly independent of beam energy, as compiled from CERES and STAR data.
- For massless partons, the effective temperature including flow is T_eff = T[(1+v)/(1-v)]^(1/2), with ⟨p_t⟩_e ∝ [(1+v)/(1-v)]^(1/2) and ⟨δp_t1δp_t2⟩_e ∝ (1+v)/(1-v).
- The observable F_(p_t) ≈ N⟨δp_t1δp_t2⟩/2σ^2 is proportional to NR⟨p_t⟩^2/σ^2 ∼ NR, where R is the scaled variance from Eq. (1).
- The scaled variance R is independent of the transverse momentum acceptance p_(t,max) for a binomial detection process.

## Context
This work extends the transport description of Gavin (2004) that addressed longitudinal expansion but omitted radial flow, which was needed to match STAR data for central collisions. The results contradict jet-based explanations for p_t fluctuations, as the energy independence of the data constrains any jet contribution. The framework enables future work to describe thermalization, flow, and jet effects in a common transport framework, with open questions about how the correlation length changes from liquid to gas values.
