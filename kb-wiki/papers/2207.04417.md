# A general, scale-independent description of the sound speed in neutron stars

> Using more than a million randomly generated equations of state that satisfy theoretical and observational constraints we construct a novel, scale-independent description of the sound speed in neutron stars where the latter is expressed in a unit-cube spanning the normalised radius, $r/R$, and the mass normalized to the maximum one, $M/M_{\rm TOV}$. From this generic representation, a number of interesting and surprising results can be deduced. In particular, we find that light (heavy) stars have stiff (soft) cores and soft (stiff) outer layers, respectively, or that the maximum of the sound speed is located at the center of light stars but moves to the outer layers for stars with $M/M_{\rm TOV}\gtrsim0.7$, reaching a constant value of $c_s^2=1/2$ as $M\to M_{\rm TOV}$. We also show that the sound speed decreases below the conformal limit $c_s^2=1/3$ at the center of stars with $M=M_{\rm TOV}$. Finally, we construct an analytic expression that accurately describes the radial dependence of the sound speed as a function of the neutron-star mass, thus providing an estimate of the maximum sound speed expected in a neutron star.

## Key claim

Using more than a million randomly generated equations of state that satisfy theoretical and observational constraints, the authors construct a scale-independent description of the sound speed in neutron stars expressed in a unit-cube of normalized radius and normalized mass. The hedge is that this description is statistical (median and 95% credibility intervals from a frequentist approach with hard-cutoff constraints) rather than a deterministic prediction, and the results depend on the specific set of imposed constraints (mass, radius, and tidal deformability bounds).

## Method

The authors generate 8 million equations of state by patching together the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland crust model at low densities, randomly sampled polytropes in the intermediate range, piecewise-linear segments for the sound speed as a function of chemical potential using 7 segments, and perturbative QCD constraints at high densities (above 40 times nuclear saturation density). They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for each equation of state and retain only those satisfying observational constraints: maximum mass above 2.0 solar masses, radius lower bounds from NICER measurements of PSR J0740+6620 and PSR J0030+0451, and binary tidal deformability below 720 from GW170817, reducing the ensemble to approximately 1 million viable equations of state.

## Key numbers

- Maximum sound speed squared reaches a constant value of c_s^2 = 1/2 for stars with M/M_TOV greater than approximately 0.7
- Sound speed at the center of maximum-mass stars (M = M_TOV) is c_s^2 approximately 0.3, below the conformal limit of 1/3
- The radial location of maximum sound speed moves to r/R approximately 0.5-0.7 for heavy stars with M/M_TOV greater than approximately 0.7
- The maximum sound speed squared at the center of intermediate-mass stars (M/M_TOV approximately 0.75) reaches c_s^2 approximately 1/2
- For a neutron star with maximum mass approximately 2.2 solar masses and radius approximately 12 km, the maximum sound speed is c_s approximately 0.7 located at approximately 8 km from the center

## Context

This work extends the statistical equation-of-state sampling approach of Altiparmak (2022) and the piecewise-linear sound-speed parametrization of Annala (2019) to produce a scale-independent description of sound speed throughout the neutron-star interior. The results contradict the natural expectation that maximum sound speed always occurs at the stellar center, showing instead that heavy stars develop soft cores and stiff outer layers. The finding that traditional nuclear-theory equations of state such as HS(DD2) (Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich, 2009), BHB-Lambda (Banik, Hempel, and Bandyopadhyay, 2014), and V-QCD (Demircik, Ecker, and Järvinen, 2021) cannot reproduce the predicted softening of heavy-star cores points to the possible appearance of new degrees of freedom such as hyperons or deconfined quarks. The analytic fitting formula provided for the sound speed as a function of normalized radius can be used to constrain nuclear-theory calculations in regions where predictions have large uncertainties.
