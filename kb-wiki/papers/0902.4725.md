# Mapping the Two-Component Atomic Fermi Gas to the Nuclear Shell-Model

> The physics of a two-component cold fermi gas is now frequently addressed in laboratories. Usually this is done for large samples of tens to hundreds of thousands of particles. However, it is now possible to produce few-body systems (1-100 particles) in very tight traps where the shell structure of the external potential becomes important. A system of two-species fermionic cold atoms with an attractive zero-range interaction is analogous to a simple model of nucleus in which neutrons and protons interact only through a residual pairing interaction. In this article, we discuss how the problem of a two-component atomic fermi gas in a tight external trap can be mapped to the nuclear shell model so that readily available many-body techniques in nuclear physics, such as the Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method, can be directly applied to the study of these systems. We demonstrate an application of the SMMC method by estimating the pairing correlations in a small two-component Fermi system with moderate-to-strong short-range two-body interactions in a three-dimensional harmonic external trapping potential.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a two-component atomic Fermi gas in a tight harmonic trap with an attractive zero-range interaction can be mapped onto the nuclear shell model, enabling the direct application of the Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method. The mapping is valid specifically for moderate-to-strong short-range two-body interactions, and the authors show that the zero-range interaction produces no sign problem in the SMMC approach, which is a nontrivial result because the atomic interaction is more general than the typical pairing force used in nuclear physics.

## Method

The authors map the two-component ultracold Fermi gas in a three-dimensional isotropic harmonic trap onto the nuclear shell model by identifying the two internal hyperfine states of the atoms with the spin states of a single nucleon species, and replacing the nuclear mean-field with the harmonic oscillator potential. They then apply the Shell Model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method, which uses a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation to linearize the two-body interaction and performs Monte Carlo integration over auxiliary fields, to compute pairing correlations in small systems with up to 20 particles. The pairing strength is evaluated as the sum over matrix elements of a number-conserving BCS-like pair operator, with mean-field values subtracted to isolate genuine pairing correlations.

## Key numbers

- Interaction strengths used: \( g = 10 \) and \( g = 20 \), where \( g = -V_0/(\hbar\omega b^3) \) and \( b = \sqrt{\hbar/m\omega} \) is the oscillator length.
- For \( g = 10 \), the corresponding two-body scattering length ratio is \( a/b = -1.0 \) (on the BCS side); for \( g = 20 \), \( a/b = 11 \) (on the BEC side near unitarity).
- Shell closures occur at particle numbers \( N = 2, 8, \) and 20 in the isotropic three-dimensional oscillator potential.
- The model space consists of the major shells \( s+p+sd \) for the calculations.
- Pairing strength shows odd-even staggering, with strong dips at \( N = 7, 9, \) and 19 connected to shell closures.
- For \( g = 10 \), typical matrix element magnitude is of order 1 in units of the single-particle level spacing \( \hbar\omega \); for \( g = 20 \), the system is pairing dominated.

## Context

This work extends the connection between cold atomic gas physics and nuclear many-body methods, building on the seminal nuclear pairing problem described by Bohr, Mottelson, and Pines (1958) and on the exact two-body solution in a harmonic trap by Busch et al. (1998). The mapping enables the use of the SMMC method, previously successful in nuclear physics (Lang et al., 1993; Alhassid, 1994; Koonin et al., 1997), for studying small ultracold Fermi systems where mean-field approaches such as BCS break down due to large quantum fluctuations. The results enable future studies of pairing correlations in few-body Fermi systems that are now experimentally accessible in optical microtraps (Jochim group, Heidelberg), and the authors note that the approach could be extended to multi-component Fermi systems by mapping onto the isospin degree of freedom.
