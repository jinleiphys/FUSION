# Progress in Constraining Nuclear Symmetry Energy Using Neutron Star Observables Since GW170817

> New observational data of neutron stars since GW170817 have helped improve our knowledge about nuclear symmetry energy especially at high densities. We have learned particularly: (1) The slope parameter $L$ of nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density $ρ_0$ of nuclear matter from 24 new analyses is about $L\approx 57.7\pm 19$ MeV at 68\% confidence level consistent with its fiducial value, (2) The curvature $K_{\rm{sym}}$ from 16 new analyses is about $K_{\rm{sym}}\approx -107\pm 88$ MeV, (3) The magnitude of nuclear symmetry energy at $2ρ_0$, i.e. $E_{\rm{sym}}(2ρ_0)\approx 51\pm 13$ MeV at 68\% confidence level, has been extracted from 9 new analyses of neutron star observables consistent with results from earlier analyses of heavy-ion reactions and the latest predictions of the state-of-the-art nuclear many-body theories, (4) while the available data from canonical neutron stars do not provide tight constraints on nuclear symmetry energy at densities above about $2ρ_0$, the lower radius boundary $R_{2.01}=12.2$ km from NICER's very recent observation of PSR J0740+6620 of mass $2.08\pm 0.07$ $M_{\odot}$ and radius $R=12.2-16.3$ km at 68\% confidence level sets a tight lower limit for nuclear symmetry energy at densities above $2ρ_0$, (5) Bayesian inferences of nuclear symmetry energy using models encapsulating a first-order hadron-quark phase transition from observables of canonical neutron stars indicate that the phase transition shift appreciably both the $L$ and $K_{\rm{sym}}$ to higher values but with larger uncertaintie , (6) The high-density behavior of nuclear symmetry energy affects significantly the minimum frequency necessary to rotationally support GW190814's secondary component of mass (2.50-2.67) $M_{\odot}$ as the fastest and most massive pulsar discovered so far.

## Key claim

Based on 24 new analyses of neutron star observables since GW170817, the slope parameter of nuclear symmetry energy at saturation density is about L ≈ 57.7 ± 19 MeV at 68% confidence level, consistent with earlier fiducial values from surveys of over 50 analyses of both terrestrial and astrophysical data, but the error bars have not been significantly reduced despite the new data.

## Method

The authors compiled and compared results from 24 new analyses of neutron star observables (primarily tidal deformability and radii) published since the GW170817 detection, extracting posterior probability distribution functions for the symmetry energy parameters L (slope), K_sym (curvature), and E_sym(2ρ₀) from Bayesian inferences and other model analyses of neutron star data. They also examined the effects of including a first-order hadron-quark phase transition in Bayesian analyses and studied the impact of high-density symmetry energy on the rotational stability of GW190814's secondary component.

## Key numbers

- Slope parameter L ≈ 57.7 ± 19 MeV at 68% confidence level from 24 new analyses of neutron star observables since GW170817
- Curvature K_sym ≈ -107 ± 88 MeV at 68% confidence level from 16 new analyses of neutron star observables
- Symmetry energy at twice saturation density E_sym(2ρ₀) ≈ 51 ± 13 MeV at 68% confidence level from 9 new analyses
- NICER observation of PSR J0740+6620: mass 2.08 ± 0.07 M⊙, radius R = 12.2-16.3 km at 68% confidence level, with lower radius boundary R_{2.01} = 12.2 km
- GW190814 secondary component mass range: (2.50-2.67) M⊙
- PREX-II experiment: neutron skin in ²⁰⁸Pb of size R_n - R_p = 0.283 ± 0.071 fm, implying L = 106 ± 37 MeV based on Relativistic Mean Field model calculations

## Context

This review updates the systematics of symmetry energy parameters from earlier surveys by Li et al. (2013) which found L = 58.9 ± 16 MeV from 29 analyses, and Oertel et al. (2017) which found L = 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV from 53 analyses. The new results are consistent with chiral Effective Field Theory predictions from Drischler et al. (2020) giving L = 59.8 ± 4.1 MeV, but show tension with the PREX-II experiment's implication of L = 106 ± 37 MeV. The work enables future high-precision constraints from advanced X-ray observatories and gravitational wave detectors, and highlights the need to simultaneously determine the high-density symmetry energy and hadron-quark phase transition properties using combined astrophysical and nuclear experimental data.
