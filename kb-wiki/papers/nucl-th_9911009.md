> The synthesis of superheavy elements is analysed within the dinuclear system concept of compound nucleus formation. The perspectives for using radioactive beams in complete fusion reactions are discussed.

# Dinuclear concept - cluster model of fusion

## Key claim
The dinuclear system (DNS) concept, in which compound nucleus formation proceeds via sequential nucleon transfer rather than a melting of the nuclei along the relative distance, can reproduce the optimal excitation energies of compound nuclei formed in cold fusion reactions and explain the strong decrease of cold fusion cross sections with increasing atomic number. The authors hedge that the DNS model's explanation of fusion probability trends relies on taking deformation and shell effects into account in the potential energy dependence on mass asymmetry.

## Method
The DNS model treats compound nucleus formation as a combined diffusion process in the mass asymmetry degree of freedom η = (A₁ − A₂)/(A₁ + A₂) and the relative distance, with competition between complete fusion and quasifission. The evaporation residue cross section is calculated as σ_ER = Σ_J σ_c P_CN W_sur, where σ_c is the capture cross section for angular momentum J, P_CN is the fusion probability, and W_sur is the survival probability against fission during de-excitation. The model incorporates nuclear structure effects through the dependence of the DNS potential energy on η, including deformation and shell effects.

## Key numbers
- In the reaction ⁸⁶Kr+¹³⁰Xe versus ⁸⁶Kr+¹³⁶Xe, the difference in σ_ER is about 3 orders of magnitude, with larger P_CN and W_sur for the ¹³⁶Xe reaction.
- For ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu → ²⁸⁹114+3n, P_CN = 6×10⁻⁴, which is about 10⁵ times larger than in ⁷⁶Ge+²⁰⁸Pb → ²⁸³114+1n.
- The σ_ER for ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu reaction is σ_3n = 1.5 pb, larger than the formation cross section of element Z=114 in Pb-based reactions.
- The experimental σ_ER of the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁴Pu reaction is 1 pb, while the ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁸Cm and ⁴⁸Ca+²⁴⁹Cf reactions yield smaller σ_ER values.
- For reactions ⁷⁶,⁷⁴Ge+²⁰⁸Pb → ²⁸³,²⁸¹114+1n, the expected σ_ER is smaller than 0.2 pb.

## Context
This work extends the DNS concept developed by the authors and collaborators (Adamian, Antonenko, Nasirov, Volkov, and Scheid) to the synthesis of superheavy elements, contrasting with adiabatic fusion models that assume melting along the relative distance, which overestimate fusion probability P_CN and give incorrect isotopic trends. The DNS model explains experimental fusion probability trends observed in reactions such as ⁹⁰Zr+⁹⁰,⁹²,⁹⁶Zr, ⁸⁶Kr+⁹⁹,¹⁰²,¹⁰⁴Ru, and ⁸⁶Kr+¹³⁰,¹³⁶Xe, where fusion probabilities decrease when neutron numbers deviate from magic numbers. The work enables predictions for future experiments with neutron-rich projectiles (⁷⁰,⁷⁴,⁷⁸Ni, ⁸⁰Zn, ⁸⁶Ge, ⁹²Se) on ²⁰⁸Pb targets and for actinide-based reactions with ⁴⁸Ca beams, and discusses the potential of radioactive beams for producing superheavy elements with longer lifetimes.
