# Time Delay and Time Advance in Resonance Theory

> We propose a theory of the resonance-antiresonance scattering process which differs considerably from the classical one (the Breit-Wigner theory), which is commonly used in the phenomenological analysis. Here both resonances and antiresonances are described in terms of poles of the scattering amplitude: the resonances by poles in the first quadrant while the antiresonances by poles in the fourth quadrant of the complex angular momentum plane. The latter poles are produced by non-local potentials, which derive from the Pauli exchange forces acting among the nucleons or the quarks composing the colliding particles.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that both time delay (resonances) and time advance (antiresonances) can be described symmetrically by pole singularities of the scattering amplitude in the complex angular momentum plane, with resonances corresponding to poles in the first quadrant and antiresonances to poles in the fourth quadrant, provided the class of potentials is extended to include non-local potentials arising from Pauli exchange forces.

## Method

The authors analyze elastic scattering of composite particles (α–α and π⁺–p) using partial wave phase-shift data, and develop a formalism where the scattering amplitude is continued to complex angular momenta. They show that non-local potentials, derived from the antisymmetrization of fermionic constituents via the resonating group method, produce integro-differential equations whose angular momentum dependence allows poles in both the first and fourth quadrants of the complex angular momentum plane. The resonance and antiresonance contributions are then fitted to experimental phase-shifts and cross-sections using pole terms that act at different energy ranges.

## Context

This work extends the Breit-Wigner resonance theory by addressing its inability to describe asymmetric resonance peaks and the time advance associated with antiresonances. The authors build on the Wigner-Eisenbud causality analysis and the Levinson sum rule, which relates time delay and time advance, and they contrast their approach with the standard method of adding a hard-sphere potential scattering term (Blatt and Weisskopf, 1952; Mott and Massey, 1965). The theory enables a unified treatment of resonances and antiresonances in collisions of composite particles where Pauli exchange forces (via non-local potentials) are significant, with potential applications to cluster scattering and hadron-hadron scattering.
