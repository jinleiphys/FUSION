# Universal charge-mass relation: From black holes to atomic nuclei

> The cosmic censorship hypothesis, introduced by Penrose forty years ago, is one of the corner stones of general relativity. This conjecture asserts that spacetime singularities that arise in gravitational collapse are always hidden inside of black holes. The elimination of a black-hole horizon is ruled out by this principle because that would expose naked singularities to distant observers. We test the consistency of this prediction in a gedanken experiment in which a charged object is swallowed by a charged black hole. We find that the validity of the cosmic censorship conjecture requires the existence of a charge-mass bound of the form $q\leqμ^{2/3}E^{-1/3}_c$, where $q$ and $μ$ are the charge and mass of the physical system respectively, and $E_c$ is the critical electric field for pair-production. Applying this bound to charged atomic nuclei, one finds an upper limit on the number $Z$ of protons in a nucleus of given mass number $A$: $Z\leq Z^*=α^{-1/3}A^{2/3}$, where $α=e^2/\hbar$ is the fine structure constant. We test the validity of this novel bound against the $(Z,A)$-relation of atomic nuclei as deduced from the Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula.

## Key claim

The cosmic censorship conjecture, applied to a gedanken experiment where a charged object is swallowed by a charged black hole, implies a universal charge-mass bound \(q \leq \mu^{2/3} E_c^{-1/3}\) for any spherically symmetric object with negligible self-gravity; for atomic nuclei, this translates to \(Z \leq \alpha^{-1/3} A^{2/3}\), a bound that is stronger than the classical limit \(q \leq (2\mu R)^{1/2}\) and is tested against the Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula.

## Method

The author considers a charged test body of rest mass \(\mu\), charge \(q\), and proper radius \(R\) slowly lowered toward a near-extremal Reissner-Nordström black hole of mass \(M\) and charge \(Q\). The total energy-at-infinity of the body, including gravitational redshift, electrostatic interaction, and gravitationally induced self-energy, is minimized to find the minimal energy required to overcome the potential barrier. The condition for the black hole to preserve its horizon after assimilation is then derived, and the Schwinger discharge limit on the black-hole electric field (\(E_+ \leq E_c\)) is used to translate this into a universal bound on \(q\) and \(\mu\). This bound is then applied to atomic nuclei by substituting nuclear charge \(q = Z|e|\) and mass \(\mu \simeq A m_p\), yielding the \((Z,A)\)-inequality, which is compared to the most stable nuclei from the Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula.

## Key numbers

- The universal charge-mass bound derived is \(q \leq \mu^{2/3} E_c^{-1/3}\), where \(E_c = m_l^2/(e\hbar)\) is the critical electric field for pair-production.
- For atomic nuclei, the bound becomes \(Z \leq Z^* = \alpha^{-1/3} A^{2/3}\), with the fine structure constant \(\alpha = e^2/\hbar \simeq 1/137\).
- The largest known completely stable nucleus, lead-208 (82 protons, 126 neutrons), satisfies \(Z/A^{2/3} \simeq 2.33\), conforming to the bound by a factor of \(\sim 2.2\).
- The largest known artificially made nucleus (118 protons, 294 nucleons) satisfies \(Z/A^{2/3} \simeq 2.67\), conforming to the bound by a factor of \(\sim 1.9\).
- For the most stable nuclei given by the semi-empirical mass formula, the ratio \(Z(A)/Z^*(A)\) reaches a maximal value of \(\simeq 0.56\).
- The experimentally measured value of \(\beta \equiv a_C/(4a_A)\) is \(\sim 7.7 \times 10^{-3}\), and with this value the inequality \(2\beta A^{2/3} - (\alpha A)^{1/3} + 2 \geq 0\) holds for all \(A\).

## Context

This work extends the cosmic censorship conjecture, introduced by Penrose (1969), from a purely gravitational principle to a universal constraint on the charge and mass of any weakly self-gravitating object, including atomic nuclei. It contrasts with the classical bound \(q \leq (2\mu R)^{1/2}\) derived from electrodynamics (Levich, 1960; Erber, 1961), which is shown to be much looser for nuclei. The new bound is tested against the Weizsäcker semi-empirical mass formula (Rohlf, 1994; Segre, 1977; Cook, 1967; Goo1, 1967) and is found to be satisfied by all known and theoretically possible nuclei, with the ratio \(Z(A)/Z^*(A)\) reaching a maximum of 0.56 for stable nuclei and up to 0.85 for maximally charged nuclei. This enables future tests of the bound against hypothetical superheavy nuclei and quark nuggets (strangelets), where the charge-mass relation \(Z \propto A^{2/3}\) is expected.
