# The magnetic properties of a deuteron $r_m$ from the AdS/QCD hard-wall model

> The deuteron is a spin1 particle and due to current conservation and the P and C invariance of the EM interaction, it has three EM form factors in the one photon exchange (OPE) approximation, which include the charge GC(Q2), quadrupole GQ(Q2) and magnetic GM(Q2) form factors and was calculated in [1, 2, 3] at a zero temperature within soft-wall and hard-wall models AdS/QCD. In this work, we numerically calculated the deuteron magnetic radius RM in the framework of the hard-wall model of AdS/QCD and compare our results with the experimental data and soft-wall model results [2]

## Key claim

The hard-wall model of AdS/QCD yields a deuteron magnetic radius of 1.48996 fm, which is closer to the experimental value than the soft-wall model result, but still deviates from the experimental central value of 1.90 fm.

## Method

The authors constructed an effective action in the bulk of AdS space containing minimal, magnetic dipole, and non-minimal gauge interaction terms for the deuteron and electromagnetic fields. They computed the deuteron magnetic form factor \(G_M(Q^2)\) from the bulk-to-boundary propagator and profile function within the hard-wall model, then extracted the magnetic radius \(R_M\) by evaluating the derivative of \(G_M(Q^2)\) at \(Q^2=0\) using MATHEMATICA.

## Key numbers

- Hard-wall model deuteron magnetic radius: \(R_M^{h.w.} = 1.48996\) fm
- Experimental deuteron magnetic radius: \(R_M^{exp} = 1.90 \pm 0.14\) fm
- Soft-wall model deuteron magnetic radius: \(R_M^{s.w.} = 2.26\) fm
- Deuteron magnetic moment used: \(\mu_D = 0.8574\)
- Normalization factor: \(G_M(0) = m_D \mu_D / m_N = 1.714\)

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of deuteron electromagnetic form factors within the soft-wall and hard-wall AdS/QCD models at zero temperature, as performed by the same group (Allahverdiyeva et al., prior work). The hard-wall result (1.49 fm) is closer to the experimental value (1.90 fm) than the soft-wall result (2.26 fm), but still underestimates it; this suggests that further refinements to the AdS/QCD approach, such as including finite-temperature effects or additional interaction terms, may be needed to achieve better agreement with experiment.
