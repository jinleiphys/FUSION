# Gamma strength functions and the Brink-Axel hypothesis

> Experimental tests of the Brink-Axel hypothesis relating gamma strength functions (GSF) deduced from absorption and emission experiments are discussed. High-resolution inelastic proton scattering at energies of a few hundred MeV and at very forwrd angles including $0^\circ$ presents a new approach to test the validity of the BA hypothesis in the energy region of the pygmy dipole resonance. Such data not only provide the GSF but also the level density and thus permit an independent test of their model-dependent decomposition in the Oslo method.

## Key claim

The generalized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which states that the gamma strength function (GSF) is independent of the properties of initial and final states and should be the same in emission and absorption experiments, is tested using a new method of high-resolution inelastic proton scattering. The paper finds that while the hypothesis holds in some cases (e.g., \(^{96}\)Mo), it appears violated in others (e.g., \(^{120}\)Sn), and the results for \(^{208}\)Pb are inconclusive due to anomalously low level densities.

## Method

The author uses high-resolution inelastic proton scattering at beam energies of a few hundred MeV and scattering angles close to \(0^\circ\), including \(0^\circ\), to extract E1 and M1 strength distributions via multipole decomposition analysis (MDA) of angular distributions and, for M1, spin transfer observables with a polarized beam. The E1 cross sections are converted to B(E1) strengths or photoabsorption cross sections using the virtual photon method, while M1 cross sections are converted to spin-M1 matrix elements using the unit cross section method. Additionally, level densities (LD) are extracted from the fine structure of the giant dipole resonance using a fluctuation analysis of the autocorrelation function, allowing an independent test of the model-dependent decomposition of GSF and LD in the Oslo method.

## Key numbers

*   For \(^{208}\)Pb, the M1 contribution to the GSF does not exceed 10% at the maximum of the resonance.
*   For \(^{208}\)Pb, the level density is extracted in the excitation energy region 9.5–12.5 MeV.
*   For \(^{96}\)Mo, the GSF from the (p,p\(^\prime\)) experiment lies between the Oslo and NRF results for \(\gamma\) energies between 6 and 8 MeV.
*   For \(^{120}\)Sn, the GSF exhibits two pronounced resonance-like structures around 6.5 and 8 MeV, indicated by arrows.
*   For \(^{120}\)Sn, the GSF from the (p,p\(^\prime\)) experiment covers \(\gamma\) energies from 5 to about 7.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends tests of the Brink-Axel hypothesis, originally formulated for the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) by Brink (1955) and Axel (1962), to lower energies including the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) and spinflip M1 resonance. It directly addresses conflicting results between Oslo-type experiments (e.g., Guttormsen (2005), Larsen (2010)) and nuclear resonance fluorescence (NRF) experiments (e.g., Rusev (2009)) for nuclei like \(^{96}\)Mo. The new method enables future systematic studies of the role of deformation and other nuclear properties in the validity of the Brink-Axel hypothesis.
