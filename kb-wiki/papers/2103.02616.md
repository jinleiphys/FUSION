# Neutrino Fast Flavor Conversions in Neutron-star Post-Merger Accretion Disks

> A compact accretion disk may be formed in the merger of two neutron stars or of a neutron star and a stellar-mass black hole. Outflows from such accretion disks have been identified as a major site of rapid neutron-capture (r-process) nucleosynthesis and as the source of 'red' kilonova emission following the first observed neutron-star merger GW170817. We present long-term general-relativistic radiation magnetohydrodynamic simulations of a typical post-merger accretion disk at initial accretion rates of $\dot{M}\sim 1\,M_\odot\,\text{s}^{-1}$ over 400ms post-merger. We include neutrino radiation transport that accounts for effects of neutrino fast flavor conversions dynamically. We find ubiquitous flavor oscillations that result in a significantly more neutron-rich outflow, providing lanthanide and 3rd-peak r-process abundances similar to solar abundances. This provides strong evidence that post-merger accretion disks are a major production site of heavy r-process elements. A similar flavor effect may allow for increased lanthanide production in collapsars. The formalism presented here may also be used in simulations of core-collapse supernovae to explore whether fast conversions strengthen or weaken the explosion.

## Key claim

Neutrino fast flavor conversions are ubiquitous in high-accretion-rate post-merger accretion disks (initial accretion rate ~1 solar mass per second) and result in significantly more neutron-rich outflows that produce lanthanide and third-peak r-process abundances comparable to solar abundances, providing strong evidence that such disks are a major production site of heavy r-process elements.

## Method

The authors performed 3D general-relativistic radiation magnetohydrodynamic (GRMHD) simulations of a typical post-merger accretion disk around a spinning black hole over 400 ms, using a multi-group two-moment (M1) neutrino transport scheme with six energy bins. They dynamically included neutrino fast flavor conversions by solving a general-relativistic dispersion relation at each grid point; when the instability growth rate exceeded a critical threshold (10^7 s^-1), they assumed full flavor equipartition between electron, muon, and tau neutrinos and anti-neutrinos. Two simulations were compared: one with fast conversions (FC) and one without (NFC), with outflow properties recorded by 10^5 passive tracer particles and post-processed with the SkyNet nuclear reaction network.

## Key numbers

- Initial accretion rate: ~1 solar mass per year (M_dot ~ 1 M_sun s^-1)
- Black hole mass: 3 M_sun with dimensionless spin 0.8
- Initial torus mass: 0.07 M_sun, extending from 22 km to 190 km
- Fast flavor instability growth times: up to ~0.1 ns at early times, increasing to 1-100 ns at ~300 ms
- Total unbound ejecta mass: ~0.026 M_sun (FC run) and ~0.03 M_sun (NFC run)
- Mass-averaged ejecta velocity: ~0.1c for both runs
- Lanthanide mass fraction: 0.097 (FC) vs 0.049 (NFC) vs 0.139 (solar r-process)
- Third r-process peak mass fraction: 0.134 (FC) vs 0.023 (NFC) vs 0.183 (solar r-process)
- Second r-process peak mass fraction: 0.631 (FC) vs 0.709 (NFC) vs 0.347 (solar r-process)

## Context

This work extends previous post-merger disk simulations by Siegel et al. (2017, 2018) and Fernandez et al. (2019) that operated at lower accretion rates (few x 10^-2 M_sun s^-1) and did not include fast flavor conversions dynamically. It also goes beyond earlier studies of fast conversions in neutron-star mergers by Wu et al. (2017) that used post-processing analysis, and by Stapleford et al. (2020) that restricted calculations to one spatial dimension. The results enable more realistic predictions for kilonova emission from events like GW170817 and suggest that similar flavor effects may operate in collapsars and core-collapse supernovae, though the authors note that the assumption of maximal mixing may overestimate the impact on ejecta composition and requires further investigation.
