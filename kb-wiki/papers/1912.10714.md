# Lepton kinematics in low energy neutrino-Argon interactions

> Background: Neutrinos in the low-energy regime provide a gateway to a wealth of interesting physics. While plenty of literature exists on detailing the calculation and measurement of total reaction strengths, relatively little attention is paid to the measurement and modeling of the final lepton through differential cross sections at low energies, despite the experimental importance. Purpose: We calculate differential cross sections for low-energy neutrino-nucleus scattering. We examine the role played by forbidden transitions in these distributions and how this differs across different energies and nuclear target masses. Attention is also paid to predictions for typical experimental neutrino spectra. Method: The differential cross sections are calculated within a Continuum Random Phase Approximation framework, which allows us to include collective excitations induced by long-range correlations. The Coulomb interaction of the final lepton in charged current events is treated in an effective way. Results: Kinematic distributions are calculated for $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ar and $^{208}$Pb. $^{40}$Ar model results are compared for CC ($ν_e,e^-$) reactions to events generated by the MARLEY event generator, with noticeable discrepancies. Conclusion: Forbidden transitions have a marked effect on the kinematic distributions of the final lepton at low-energy kinematics, such as for DAR neutrinos or for a Fermi-Dirac spectrum at low temperature. This could introduce biases in experimental analyses. Backwards scattering is noticeably more prominent than with MARLEY.

## Key claim

Forbidden transitions (multipoles beyond the allowed Fermi and Gamow-Teller operators) have a marked effect on the kinematic distributions of the final lepton in low-energy neutrino-argon interactions, causing backward scattering to be noticeably more prominent than predicted by the MARLEY event generator, which lacks forbidden transition modeling.

## Method

The differential cross sections were calculated within a Continuum Random Phase Approximation (CRPA) framework, which includes collective excitations induced by long-range correlations. The Coulomb interaction of the final lepton in charged current events was treated by interpolating between the Fermi function (for low outgoing lepton momentum) and the modified effective momentum approximation (MEMA) (for higher outgoing lepton momentum). The nuclear response functions were decomposed into multipole moments, with contributions from allowed (0+ and 1+) and forbidden (higher-order) transitions separated and compared.

## Key numbers

- Differential cross sections were calculated for incoming neutrino energies of 30, 50, and 70 MeV for targets of 16O, 40Ar, and 208Pb.
- The axial coupling constant used was the free-nucleon value g_A = 1.27.
- The cross section was folded with a Lorentzian of width 3 MeV to account for the finite width of nuclear excitations.
- For a Fermi-Dirac spectrum at temperatures of 6 K and 10 K, the CRPA model and MARLEY results matched favorably for the low-temperature spectrum, but the higher temperature showed discrepancies due to forbidden transitions.
- At 25 MeV, the MARLEY MC distribution differed significantly from CRPA predictions, attributed to discrete excitations not included in the CRPA spectrum.

## Context

This work extends prior CRPA calculations for low-energy neutrino-nucleus interactions (Van Dessel 2019, Pandey 2014, Pandey 2016) by focusing on differential cross sections and lepton kinematics rather than total cross sections, which have been the primary focus of most prior literature (McLaughlin 2004, Engel 2002, Volpe 2001, Kolbe 2000, among others). The results are compared directly to the MARLEY event generator (Gardiner 2017), which is used for simulating low-energy neutrino-argon reactions at DUNE. The findings enable improved modeling of angular and energy distributions for experimental analyses at DUNE, JUNO, and Hyper-Kamiokande, and highlight the need to include forbidden transitions in event generators to avoid biases in energy reconstruction.
