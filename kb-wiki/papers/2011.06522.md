# From soft to hard radiation: the role of multiple scatterings in medium-induced gluon emissions

> A proper understanding of the physics of medium-induced gluon emissions is known to be of critical importance to describe the properties of strongly interacting matter under extreme conditions. In this regard, many theoretical efforts have been directed towards obtaining analytical calculations which might help us discerning the underlying physical picture and the dominant dynamics for different regimes. These analytical approaches rely on approximations whose validity is analyzed here by comparing their results with a recently developed numerical evaluation which includes all-order resummation of multiple scatterings. More specifically, by quantitatively comparing the energy spectrum and rates, we observe that three different regimes -- each with its corresponding physical picture -- emerge naturally from the equations: the high-energy regime where the emission process is dominated by a single hard scattering, the intermediate-energy regime where coherence effects among multiple scatterings become fundamental, and the low-energy regime where the dynamics is again dominated by a single scattering but where one must include the suppression factor due to the probability of not having any further scatterings (which is obtained through the resummation of virtual terms).

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that medium-induced gluon emission spectra separate into three distinct regimes (high-energy, intermediate-energy, and low-energy) each dominated by different physical mechanisms, but the validity of the harmonic oscillator approximation for the intermediate regime depends on the medium being sufficiently dense, with the HO+NLO expansion only becoming stable for \(\chi \sim 14\) or larger.

## Method

The authors numerically evaluate the full BDMPS-Z medium-induced gluon emission spectrum with all-order resummation of multiple scatterings using a Yukawa-type interaction model and a hard thermal loop (HTL) interaction model, then compare these results to the first-order opacity expansion (GLV or single hard scattering result) and to the harmonic oscillator approximation with next-to-leading order corrections from Mehtar-Tani (2019) and Barata (2020). The energy spectrum integrated over all transverse momenta is computed for a brick medium with constant density \(n_0\) and length \(L\), expressed in terms of the dimensionless variable \(x = \omega / \bar\omega_c = 2\omega / (\mu^2 L)\).

## Key numbers

- The low-energy asymptotic formula matches the all-order result for sufficiently low energy, with the suppression factor from virtual resummation being essential; the \(N=1\) result departs greatly from the full evaluation in this regime.
- The HO+NLO expansion becomes stable with respect to the matching scale \(a\) only for \(\chi \sim 14\) or larger, where \(\chi = n_0 L / C\) with \(C = e^{-1+2\gamma_E}/4 \approx 0.29\).
- The HO+NLO approximation has a minimum energy for which it holds: \(\hat x > 2e\) from the transcendental equation \(\Lambda = \frac{1}{2} \ln(\Lambda \hat x)\), and in practice results cannot be trusted for \(\hat x < 10\).
- For the Yukawa collision rate, the mapping between parameters gives \(\hat q_0 = n_0 \mu^2\) and \(\mu^{\star 2} = C \mu^2\) with \(C \approx 0.29\).
- The strong coupling is fixed to \(\alpha_s = 0.3\) and the Casimir factor for a quark emitter is \(C_R = C_F = 4/3\).

## Context

This work extends the numerical framework developed by Andres (2020) for all-order resummation of multiple scatterings in medium-induced gluon radiation, using it to test the validity of analytical approximations commonly employed in jet quenching phenomenology. The paper quantitatively compares the full spectrum to the harmonic oscillator approximation and its extensions from Mehtar-Tani (2019) and Barata (2020), and to the single hard scattering (GLV) result from Gyulassy (2000). The findings enable improved phenomenological tools for describing jet suppression in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, and help resolve questions about which approximations are valid in different energy regimes for different medium densities.
