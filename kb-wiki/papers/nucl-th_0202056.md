# Comment on "Spectroscopy with Random and Displaced Random Ensembles"

## Key claim
Time reversal invariance, as interpreted by Velazquez et al. through the behaviors of the centroid $E_{cI}$ and variance $\sigma_I^2$, cannot be the origin of the 0+ ground state dominance in even fermion systems; the results using a displaced two-body random ensemble (TBRE) are much more complicated than claimed and are not generally true.

## Method
The authors analyze the statistical properties of energy levels from TBRE calculations for fermion systems in single-$j$ and two-$j$ shells, examining the relationship between the variance $\sigma_I$ of states with a given total angular momentum $I$ and the probability $P(I)$ that a state of that angular momentum becomes the ground state. They test the effect of positive and negative displacements of the TBRE on the ground-state probabilities for several fermion configurations.

## Key numbers
- For a 5-fermion system in a single-$j$ shell, $\sigma_I$ with $I = I_{min} = 1/2$ is very large, yet $P(1/2)$ is always close to zero.
- For 4 fermions in a two-$j$ shell ($j_1 = 13/2$, $j_2 = 9/2$) using a TBRE $\pm 5$, $P(0) \sim 100\%$ for both negative and positive displacements.
- For 4 fermions in a two-$j$ shell ($j_1 = 11/2$, $j_2 = 3/2$) using a TBRE $\pm 5$, $P(0) \sim 0\%$ for both negative and positive displacements.
- For 4 fermions in a two-$j$ shell ($j_1 = 9/2$, $j_2 = 5/2$) using a TBRE $\pm 5$, the displacement plays only a minor role, with very slight changes in $P(I)$.

## Context
This Comment directly addresses the work of Velazquez et al. (Ref. [2]), who proposed time reversal invariance as the origin of 0+ ground state dominance based on centroid and variance arguments. The authors note that this idea was already found inapplicable to fermions in a single-$j$ shell by Bijker et al. (Ref. [3]), and that a different explanation involving correlation between states was proposed by Zhao and Arima (Ref. [4] and Zhao). The results show that displaced TBRE behavior is system-dependent, contradicting the general claims of Velazquez et al., and that correlation between states, not time reversal invariance, is essential for understanding ground-state probabilities in random-interaction models.
