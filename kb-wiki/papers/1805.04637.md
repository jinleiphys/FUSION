# r-Process Nucleosynthesis: Connecting Rare-Isotope Beam Facilities with the Cosmos

> This is an exciting time for the study of r-process nucleosynthesis. Recently, a neutron star merger GW170817 was observed in extraordinary detail with gravitational waves and electromagnetic radiation from radio to gamma rays. The very red color of the associated kilonova suggests that neutron star mergers are an important r-process site. Astrophysical simulations of neutron star mergers and core collapse supernovae are making rapid progress. Detection of both, electron neutrinos and antineutrinos from the next galactic supernova will constrain the composition of neutrino-driven winds and provide unique nucleosynthesis information. Finally FRIB and other rare-isotope beam facilities will soon have dramatic new capabilities to synthesize many neutron-rich nuclei that are involved in the r-process. The new capabilities can significantly improve our understanding of the r-process and likely resolve one of the main outstanding problems in classical nuclear astrophysics. However, to make best use of the new experimental capabilities and to fully interpret the results, a great deal of infrastructure is needed in many related areas of astrophysics, astronomy, and nuclear theory. We will place these experiments in context by discussing astrophysical simulations and observations of r-process sites, observations of stellar abundances, galactic chemical evolution, and nuclear theory for the structure and reactions of very neutron-rich nuclei. This review paper was initiated at a three-week International Collaborations in Nuclear Theory program in June 2016 where we explored promising r-process experiments and discussed their likely impact, and their astrophysical, astronomical, and nuclear theory context.

## Key claim

This review argues that the recent multi-messenger observation of the neutron star merger GW170817 provides strong evidence that neutron star mergers are an important r-process site, but that fully interpreting future experimental data from FRIB and other rare-isotope beam facilities will require extensive supporting infrastructure in astrophysics, astronomy, and nuclear theory.

## Method

The paper reviews the current state of r-process nucleosynthesis research by synthesizing findings across multiple disciplines: stellar abundance observations of metal-poor stars and ultra-faint dwarf galaxies, multi-messenger observations including gravitational waves and kilonovae, galactic chemical evolution simulations, astrophysical simulations of neutron star mergers and core collapse supernovae, nuclear sensitivity studies, experimental methods for measuring properties of neutron-rich nuclei, and nuclear theory predictions for nuclear structure and reactions.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 30 stars have been confirmed with [Eu/Fe] > +1.0, where Eu is taken as a representative r-process element
- Only about 3% of metal-poor stars are highly enhanced in r-process elements
- More than 30 elements produced by the r-process can be detected in a single star when UV and optical spectra are obtainable
- The star HD 108317 shows 34 elements heavier than Zn (Z = 30) detected, with [Eu/Fe] = +0.5
- For field stars with high r-process enhancement, average differences in light element abundances compared to stars without such enhancement are constrained to be approximately 3.5% or less
- The very red color of the kilonova associated with GW170817 was observed two or more days after the merger, peaking in the infrared

## Context

This review extends the long line of r-process research dating back to Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957), and was initiated at a three-week International Collaborations in Nuclear Theory program in June 2016. It builds on the recent multi-messenger observation of GW170817 and the discovery of strongly r-process enriched stars in the ultra-faint dwarf galaxy Reticulum II by Ji (2016). The paper enables future work by identifying the nuclear physics measurements needed at FRIB and other facilities, and by establishing the iterative cycle between nuclear experiment, nuclear theory, astrophysical simulations, and astronomical observations needed to resolve the r-process site question.
