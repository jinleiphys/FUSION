# Initial state and thermalization

> We report recent results on the role of instabilities in the isotropization and thermalization of a longitudinally expanding system of quantum fields.

## Key claim

Instabilities in the classical Yang-Mills fields produced in heavy-ion collisions cause secular divergences in fixed-order loop calculations; resumming these unstable contributions via a Gaussian average over fluctuating initial conditions can cure these divergences and lead to isotropization and thermalization of the expanding system.

## Method

The authors study a scalar \(\phi^4\) field theory as a simpler proxy for Yang-Mills theory, first in a fixed volume and then with a longitudinally expanding boost-invariant background. They compute the energy-momentum tensor at leading order (LO) and next-to-leading order (NLO), identify secular divergences from unstable modes, and perform a resummation by exponentiating the operator that generates shifts of the initial classical field, which is equivalent to a Gaussian average over initial conditions. The occupation number and pressure components are then evolved in time.

## Key numbers

- In the fixed-volume scalar theory, the resummed pressure relaxes to the equilibrium value \(P = \epsilon/3\) (no explicit time or coupling given in the text for this result).
- For the longitudinally expanding scalar system, the time dependence of the energy density changes from \(\tau^{-1}\) to \(\tau^{-4/3}\), indicating a transition from very small longitudinal pressure to a longitudinal pressure equal to \(\epsilon/3\).
- The longitudinal pressure, after a rapid initial decrease due to redshifting, grows again due to instabilities and becomes very close to the transverse pressure (no explicit numerical values for the pressures or times are provided in the text).

## Context

This work extends the Color Glass Condensate (CGC) framework by addressing the long-standing problem of isotropization and thermalization in the early stages of ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions. It builds on prior studies of instabilities in classical Yang-Mills solutions (insta) and on the JIMWLK factorization of cutoff logarithms (JIMWLK). The results for the scalar model pave the way for performing the same resummation in full Yang-Mills theory, using the spectrum of fluctuations over a boost-invariant background worked out in DusliGV1, to enable a first-principles description of the approach to hydrodynamics.
