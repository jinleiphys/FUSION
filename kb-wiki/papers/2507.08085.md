# Conformal prediction for uncertainties in nucleon-nucleon scattering

> Conformal prediction is a distribution-free and model-agnostic uncertainty-quantification method that provides finite-sample prediction intervals with guaranteed coverage. In this work, for the first time, we apply conformal-prediction to generate uncertainty bands for physical observables in nuclear physics, such as the total cross section and nucleon-nucleon phase shifts. We demonstrate the method's flexibility by considering three scenarios: (i) a pointwise model, where expansion coefficients in chiral effective field theory are treated as random variables; (ii) a Gaussian-process model for the coefficients; and (iii) phase shifts at various energies and partial waves calculated using local interactions from chiral effective field theory. In each case, conformal-prediction intervals are constructed and validated empirically. Our results show that conformal prediction provides reliable and adaptive uncertainty bands even in the presence of non-Gaussian behavior, such as skewness and heavy tails. These findings highlight conformal prediction as a robust and practical framework for quantifying theoretical uncertainties.

## Key claim

Conformal prediction provides reliable and adaptive uncertainty bands for nucleon-nucleon scattering observables that maintain guaranteed finite-sample coverage even when the underlying distributions exhibit non-Gaussian behavior such as skewness and heavy tails, but this guarantee depends on the exchangeability assumption and the method is applied as a postprocessing step after Bayesian inference rather than replacing it.

## Method

The authors applied split conformal prediction and conformalized quantile regression (CQR) to posterior samples of nucleon-nucleon total cross sections and phase shifts derived from chiral effective field theory. They considered three scenarios: (i) a pointwise model where expansion coefficients are treated as independent Student-t random variables; (ii) a Gaussian-process model for the expansion coefficients that captures correlations across energies; and (iii) phase shifts at various energies and partial waves calculated using local chiral EFT interactions from Gezerlis et al. (2013, 2014). For each case, they split data into training and calibration sets, computed conformity scores (absolute residuals or quantile-based scores), and constructed prediction intervals with target coverage levels of 68% and 95%.

## Key numbers

* 95% CQR intervals for the total cross section at N2LO using the EKM potential with R0 = 0.9 fm were constructed across a range of energies, with empirical coverage of 0.9499 achieved on toy chi-squared data with 4 degrees of freedom
* For the pointwise model at 50 MeV and 200 MeV, posterior distributions of the expansion coefficient c_n were generated using 10000 samples with prior hyperparameters nu0 = 1 and tau0 = 1
* Empirical coverage distributions for the total cross section at N2LO and E = 50 MeV were computed using 2000 samples from the GP model, with n_calib = 900, n_train = 900, and n_valid = 200, repeated over n_trial = 1000 trials
* For n_split = 100, 1000, and 10000, the spread of empirical coverage for CQR narrowed and converged toward the target coverage of 0.9 as n_split increased

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian uncertainty quantification framework developed by the BUQEYE Collaboration (Melendez et al., 2017, 2019) for chiral EFT truncation errors, which treated expansion coefficients via Student-t distributions or Gaussian processes. It introduces conformal prediction as a complementary postprocessing step that provides finite-sample frequentist coverage guarantees without relying on prior distributions or model correctness assumptions. The method enables future applications to fitting local nuclear interactions used in quantum Monte Carlo calculations, such as those from Gezerlis et al. (2013, 2014) and Somasundaram et al. (2024), and could be extended to handle nonexchangeable data through weighted conformity scores as discussed by Barber et al. (2023).
