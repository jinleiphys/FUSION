# Connecting small-scale to large-scale structures of fast neutrino-flavor conversion

> We present a systematic study of fast neutrino-flavor conversion (FFC) with both small-scale and large-scale numerical simulations in spherical symmetry. We find that FFCs can, in general, reach a quasi-steady state, and these features in the non-linear phase are not characterized by the growth rate of FFC instability but rather angular structures of electron neutrino lepton number (ELN) and heavy one (XLN). Our result suggests that neutrinos can almost reach a flavor equipartition even in cases with low growth rate of instability (e.g., shallow ELN crossing) and narrow angular regions (in momentum space) where flavor conversions occur vigorously. This exhibits that ELN and XLN angular distributions can not provide a sufficient information to determine total amount of flavor conversion in neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors. Based on the results of our numerical simulations, we provide a new approximate scheme of FFC that is designed so that one can easily incorporate effects of FFCs in existing classical neutrino transport codes for the study of core-collapse supernova (CCSN) and binary neutron star merger (BNSM). The scheme has an ability to capture key features of quasi-steady state of FFCs without solving quantum kinetic neutrino transport, which will serve to facilitate access to FFCs for CCSN and BNSM theorists.

## Key claim

Fast neutrino-flavor conversions can reach a quasi-steady state even in cases with low growth rate of instability (e.g., shallow ELN crossing) and narrow angular regions where flavor conversions occur vigorously, and the total amount of flavor conversion cannot be determined solely from the angular distributions of electron neutrino lepton number (ELN) and heavy lepton number (XLN).

## Method

The authors performed 17 quantum kinetic equation (QKE) simulations in spherical symmetry using the GRQKNT code with a discrete-ordinate Sn method, 5th-order WENO scheme, and fourth-order TVD Runge-Kutta time integration. They employed an attenuating Hamiltonian parameter (xi) to make large-scale simulations (50 km spatial scales) tractable, and complemented these with local simulations (without attenuation) in the vicinity of the inner boundary. The simulations used two-flavor approximation with monochromatic energy (12 MeV), fixed neutrino number density at the inner boundary, and systematically varied parameters controlling the depth and crossing point of ELN angular distributions.

## Key numbers

- Neutrino number density at inner boundary (R = 50 km): 6 x 10^32 cm^-3 for nu_e
- Vacuum potential parameters: Delta m^2 = 2.5 x 10^-6 eV^2, mixing angle = 10^-6, neutrino energy = 12 MeV
- Ratio of vacuum potential to self-interaction potentials: approximately 10^-8
- Spatial width of linear growth regime in local simulations (xi = 1): approximately 20 cm
- Global simulation box: 50 km <= R <= 100 km (Delta R = 50 km)
- Physical time for global simulations: T_sim = 0.5 ms (approximately 3 times the light-crossing time)
- ELN crossing point for reference model (alpha = 1, beta_ee = 0, bar_beta_ee = 1): cos(theta_nu) = 0.5
- ELN crossing depth for shallowest crossing studied: approximately 1% (bar_beta_ee = 10^-2)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Nagakura and Zaizen 2022, referred to as NZv1) which introduced the attenuating Hamiltonian approach for large-scale FFC simulations but used fixed initial angular distributions. The present systematic study covers various initial conditions to capture generic features of FFCs, and provides an approximate scheme to determine quasi-steady states without solving QKE, designed for implementation in existing classical neutrino transport codes for core-collapse supernova (CCSN) and binary neutron star merger (BNSM) simulations. This contrasts with previous approaches that either performed local simulations (e.g., Delfan Azari et al. 2020, Bhatt et al. 2020, Richers et al. 2021) or used phenomenological models with parametric flavor equipartition assumptions (e.g., Li and Siegel 2021, Just et al. 2022).
