# Nuclear matrix elements of double beta decay from beta decay

> The evaluation of the nuclear matrix elements (NME) of the two-neutrino double beta ($2νββ$) decay and neutrinoless double beta ($0νββ$) decay using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) is addressed. In particular, the extraction of a proper value of the proton-neutron particle-particle interaction parameter, $g_{\rm pp}$, of this theory is analyzed in detail. Evidence is shown, that it can be misleading to use the experimental half-life of the $2νββ$ decay to extract a value for $g_{\rm pp}$. Rather, arguments are given in favour of using the available data on single beta decay for this purpose.

## Key claim
The paper argues that fitting the proton-neutron particle-particle interaction parameter \(g_{\rm pp}\) of the pnQRPA to the experimental half-life of the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) decay can be misleading, because compensating errors in the two single-beta-decay branches (EC and \(\beta^-\)) can conspire to produce a correct \(2\nu\beta\beta\) decay rate. Instead, the author advocates fitting \(g_{\rm pp}\) to available single-beta-decay data.

## Method
The author uses the spherical proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) with a Woods-Saxon single-particle potential and a realistic Bonn-type G-matrix two-body interaction. The particle-hole \(J^\pi=1^+\) matrix elements are fixed to reproduce the Gamow-Teller giant resonance, while the particle-particle strength \(g_{\rm pp}\) is treated as a free parameter. The analysis compares \(g_{\rm pp}\) values extracted from fitting the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life with those obtained from fitting single \(\beta^-\) decay half-lives for the decays of \(^{116}\)Cd, \(^{128}\)Te, \(^{76}\)Ge, \(^{82}\)Se, and \(^{100}\)Mo.

## Key numbers
- For \(^{116}\)Cd: fitting the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta\beta)\simeq 1.03\), yielding \(M_{\rm EC}\simeq 1.4\) and \(M_{\beta^-}\simeq 0.24\); the ratio of experimental to theoretical half-lives is \(t_{1/2}^{(\rm EC)}({\rm exp.})/t_{1/2}^{(\rm EC)}({\rm th.})\simeq 2.6\) and \(t_{1/2}^{(\beta^-)}({\rm exp.})/t_{1/2}^{(\beta^-)}({\rm th.})\simeq 0.16\). Fitting the \(\beta^-\) decay gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta^-)\simeq 0.85\) and \(M_{\rm EC}\simeq 1.2\) (experimental \(M_{\rm EC}({\rm exp})\simeq 0.8\)).
- For \(^{128}\)Te: fitting the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta\beta)\simeq 0.82\), yielding \(M_{\rm EC}\simeq 1.19\) and \(M_{\beta^-}\simeq 0.05\); the half-life ratios are \(t_{1/2}^{(\rm EC)}({\rm exp.})/t_{1/2}^{(\rm EC)}({\rm th.})\simeq 9.7\) and \(t_{1/2}^{(\beta^-)}({\rm exp.})/t_{1/2}^{(\beta^-)}({\rm th.})\simeq 0.17\). Fitting the \(\beta^-\) decay gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta^-)\simeq 0.755\) and \(M_{\rm EC}\simeq 1.15\) (experimental \(M_{\rm EC}({\rm exp})\simeq 0.38\) for \(g_{\rm A}=1.0\)).
- For \(^{76}\)Ge: fitting the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta\beta)\simeq 0.94\), yielding \(\log ft({\rm EC})\simeq 3.9\) and \(\log ft(\beta^-)\simeq 6.4\) for \(g_{\rm A}=1.0\).
- For \(^{82}\)Se: fitting the \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta\beta)\simeq 1.07\), yielding \(\log ft({\rm EC})\simeq 4.0\) and \(\log ft(\beta^-)\simeq 6.2\) for \(g_{\rm A}=1.0\).
- For \(^{100}\)Mo: the computed total NME never reaches the experimental NME (using \(g_{\rm A}=1.0\)); fitting the \(\beta^-\) decay gives \(g_{\rm pp}(\beta^-)\simeq 1.02\), yielding \(M_{\rm EC}\simeq 1.97\) and \(\log ft({\rm EC})\simeq 3.7\) for \(g_{\rm A}=1.0\).

## Context
This work extends the long-standing discussion of the "\(g_{\rm pp}\) problem" in pnQRPA calculations of double beta decay, first noted by Vogel and Zirnbauer (1986) and Civitarese et al. (1987). It directly challenges the method proposed by Rodin et al. (2003) and Stoica et al. (2001) of using \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-lives to fix \(g_{\rm pp}\) for \(0\nu\beta\beta\) matrix elements. The paper enables a more reliable extraction of \(0\nu\beta\beta\) nuclear matrix elements by advocating the use of single-beta-decay data, which can also be extended to study forbidden contributions (e.g., \(2^-\) multipoles) and to access \(\beta\beta\) decays where no \(2\nu\beta\beta\) data exist.
