# Core polarization, Brown-Rho scaling and a memory of Gerry's Princeton years

> Core-polarization (CP) and Brown-Rho (BR) scaling were among Gerry's most favorite topics. In this contribution, we will discuss some of the early history as well as more recent work associated with these two fascinating phenomena. I (TTSK) will begin with some recollections of Princeton, where I met Gerry for the first time in 1964 and worked as his postdoc. Core polarization was in fact the first topic he assigned to me. JWH started working with Gerry at Stony Brook from 2003 and was Gerry's last student in nuclear physics. We three had teamed up well, working closely on both CP and BR scaling, particularly on the latter's connection to the anomalously-long beta-decay lifetime of carbon-14. We shall here briefly review these topics, including a recently developed new Brown-Rho scaling based on a Skyrmion half-Skyrmion two-phase model.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how core-polarization (CP) and Brown-Rho (BR) scaling, two topics favored by Gerry Brown, can be connected to explain nuclear structure phenomena, with the hedge that BR scaling provides an alternative to three-body forces for describing density-dependent nuclear interactions, and that the new BR scaling based on a Skyrmion half-Skyrmion two-phase model is only "inferred" by the model rather than rigorously derived.

## Method

The authors review the historical development of the Kuo-Brown (KB) matrix elements, which used a Brueckner G-matrix plus second-order core-polarization diagrams (G_{3p1h}) to calculate effective shell-model interactions. For the core-polarization study, they employed the renormalization group low-momentum nucleon-nucleon interaction V_{low-k}, summed folded diagrams to all orders, and used the induced-interaction approach of Kirson and Babu and Brown (KBB) to generate core-polarization diagrams self-consistently to all orders. For Brown-Rho scaling, they implemented density-dependent scaling of meson masses (rho, omega, sigma) and cutoff parameters in one-boson-exchange potentials (BonnA and BonnS), with a new two-region scaling function based on a Skyrmion half-Skyrmion transition at density n_{1/2}.

## Key numbers

- The all-order KBB core-polarization matrix elements are about 10% weaker than the second-order CP matrix elements for the sd-shell
- The Brown-Rho scaling parameter C has the value 0.15-0.20 in the linear scaling relation Phi_BR(n) = 1 - C n/n_0
- Nuclear matter saturation density n_0 is 0.16 fm^{-3}
- With new-BR scaling, the equation of state gives energy per nucleon E_0/A = -15 MeV, saturation momentum k_F = 1.30 fm^{-1}, and compression modulus K = 208 MeV
- The carbon-14 beta-decay lifetime of approximately 5800 years was reproduced using BR scaling
- The new-BR scaling uses transition densities n_{1/2} of 1.5 n_0 and 2.0 n_0
- Scaling parameters for region I: C_rho = 0.13, C_sigma = 0.121, C_omega = 0.139, C_N = 0.13
- In region II the nucleon mass is fixed at m*_N/m_N = 0.77
- Calculated neutron-star maximum masses are 2.39 M_sun (with radius 10.90 km) for n_{1/2} = 2.0 n_0 and 2.38 M_sun (with radius 10.89 km) for n_{1/2} = 1.5 n_0
- The four EOS curves using V_{low-k} plus Skyrme density-dependent force all give E_0/A approximately -15 MeV, k_F approximately 1.40 fm^{-1}, and K approximately 150 MeV

## Context

This paper extends the foundational work on Kuo-Brown matrix elements from Kuo and Brown (1966, 1967, 1968) and the Brown-Rho scaling from Brown and Rho (1991, 2004), connecting these to modern renormalization group methods and Skyrmion models. The work enables microscopic foundations for the empirical Skyrme density-dependent effective interaction, as the authors show that BR-scaled interactions and unscaled V_{NN} plus Skyrme force give similar saturation properties. The new-BR scaling based on a half-Skyrmion model from Dong et al. (2013) provides a framework for calculating nuclear symmetry energy up to high densities and neutron-star properties, though the scaling at densities above approximately 1.5 n_0 remains an open question.
