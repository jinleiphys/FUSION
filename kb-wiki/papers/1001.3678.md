# Synoptic Sky Surveys and the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background: Removing Astrophysical Uncertainties and Revealing Invisible Supernovae

> The cumulative (anti)neutrino production from all core-collapse supernovae within our cosmic horizon gives rise to the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB), which is on the verge of detectability. The observed flux depends on supernova physics, but also on the cosmic history of supernova explosions; currently, the cosmic supernova rate introduces a substantial (+/-40%) uncertainty, largely through its absolute normalization. However, a new class of wide-field, repeated-scan (synoptic) optical sky surveys is coming online, and will map the sky in the time domain with unprecedented depth, completeness, and dynamic range. We show that these surveys will obtain the cosmic supernova rate by direct counting, in an unbiased way and with high statistics, and thus will allow for precise predictions of the DSNB. Upcoming sky surveys will substantially reduce the uncertainties in the DSNB source history to an anticipated +/-5% that is dominated by systematics, so that the observed high-energy flux thus will test supernova neutrino physics. The portion of the universe (z < 1) accessible to upcoming sky surveys includes the progenitors of a large fraction (~ 87%) of the expected 10-26 MeV DSNB event rate. We show that precision determination of the (optically detected) cosmic supernova history will also make the DSNB into a strong probe of an extra flux of neutrinos from optically invisible supernovae, which may be unseen either due to unexpected large dust obscuration in host galaxies, or because some core-collapse events proceed directly to black hole formation and fail to give an optical outburst.

## Key claim

Synoptic sky surveys will reduce the uncertainty in the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB) source history from the current ±40% to an anticipated ±5% dominated by systematics, but only if the cosmic supernova rate normalization can be precisely determined via direct counting of supernovae out to redshift z ~ 1.

## Method

The authors calculate the DSNB flux using a line-of-sight integral of neutrino sources out to cosmic horizon, combining the cosmic supernova rate (CSNR) with neutrino energy spectra modeled as Fermi-Dirac distributions. They forecast CSNR measurements from upcoming synoptic surveys (LSST, Pan-STARRS, DES) by modeling supernova detection rates as a function of redshift, survey depth, and sky coverage, then compare the redshift reach of these surveys with the redshift distribution of DSNB neutrino sources.

## Key numbers

- Current CSNR normalization uncertainty: ±40%, leading to substantial uncertainty in DSNB predictions
- Future CSNR normalization uncertainty anticipated: ±5% (dominated by systematics) from one year of synoptic survey observations
- Fraction of expected 10-26 MeV DSNB event rate from sources within z < 1: approximately 87% (1.5 neutrinos/year out of 1.8 neutrinos/year total)
- Fraction from sources within z < 0.5: approximately 54% (1.0 neutrinos/year)
- Super-Kamiokande current upper limit on DSNB flux: 1.2 cm⁻² s⁻¹ above 19.3 MeV neutrino energy
- LSST expected detection rate: more than 10⁴ core-collapse supernovae per year in each Δz = 0.05 bin at z ~ 0.1-0.5, with detections out to z ~ 0.9
- For a Salpeter initial mass function (dN/dm ∝ m⁻²·³⁵) over 8-100 M⊙, approximately 90% of collapse events are core-collapse (optically luminous) and approximately 10% are direct-collapse (optically invisible)

## Context

This work extends previous DSNB predictions by Horiuchi (2009) and others, who found the DSNB is no more than a factor 2-4 below the 2003 Super-K limit, and who identified the CSNR normalization as the dominant uncertainty. The paper shows that upcoming synoptic surveys (LSST, Pan-STARRS, DES) will measure the CSNR by direct counting with high statistics, enabling precise DSNB predictions that can distinguish between different supernova neutrino models (with effective temperatures Tν = 4, 6, or 8 MeV). The improved precision will also allow the DSNB to probe optically invisible supernovae (direct-collapse events forming black holes without optical outbursts), which current estimates suggest may constitute ~10% of collapse events but could be significantly higher if fallback black hole formation events are also invisible.
