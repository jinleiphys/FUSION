# A small parameter approach for few-body problems

> A procedure to solve few-body problems is developed which is based on an expansion over a small parameter. The parameter is the ratio of potential energy to kinetic energy for states having not small hyperspherical quantum numbers, K>K_0. Dynamic equations are reduced perturbatively to equations in the finite-dimension subspace with K\le K_0. Contributions from states with K>K_0 are taken into account in a closed form, i.e. without an expansion over basis functions. Estimates on efficiency of the approach are presented.

## Key claim

The paper develops a perturbative method for solving few-body problems by exploiting a small parameter: the ratio of potential energy to kinetic energy for states with hyperspherical quantum numbers \(K > K_0\), where \(K_0\) is a chosen cutoff. The method reduces the dynamic equations to a finite-dimensional subspace (\(K \le K_0\)) and treats contributions from higher \(K\) states in closed form without basis expansion, with the hedge that the approach is applicable when kinetic energy dominates potential energy for the high-\(K\) components.

## Method

The method splits the Hilbert space into a low-hypermomentum subspace (\(K \le K_0\)) and a high-hypermomentum subspace (\(K > K_0\)). Using projectors \(P_{K_0}\) and \(Q_{K_0}\), the Schrödinger equation is rewritten to express the high-\(K\) component \(\Psi_i^h\) in terms of the low-\(K\) component \(\Psi_i^l\) via a Green function \(G_{K_0} = (T + \delta H - E_i^{(0)})^{-1} Q_{K_0}\), where \(\delta H\) is a subsidiary interaction. A perturbative expansion in powers of \(G_{K_0}U\) and \(G_{K_0}V\) yields equations for \(\Psi_i^l\) in the \(K \le K_0\) subspace only, with the effective interaction \(-V\Gamma V\) accounting for high-\(K\) effects; the high-\(K\) component is reconstructed as a quadrature. The method is extended to reaction problems using a localized state ansatz and complex energies.

## Key numbers

- For the \(^4\)He 0\(^+\) bound state with a strong-repulsive-core NN interaction at \(K_0 = 14\), the difference between the approximate and accurate binding energies is 0.23 MeV (from Table 4 of Ref. [4]).
- For the reaction estimate in the same system using the AV4 potential (central component of AV18) at \(K_0 = 14\) and \(\sigma_I = 10\) MeV, the integral transform \(\Phi(E)\) is computed in a two-channel model with \(K=0\) and \(K=16\) harmonics; the central force has a repulsive core height of 2.7 GeV.
- The number of hyperspherical harmonics with \(K \le 14\) is "several hundreds," and a swift rise in the number of harmonics with the same \(K\) starts at \(K\) values about 10 (from Table 3 of Ref. [4]).
- The condition for applicability is roughly \(\frac{\hbar^2}{2m \bar{\rho}^2} (K_0 + \frac{n-2}{2})^2 \gg |\langle V - \bar{V} \rangle|\), where \(\bar{\rho} \simeq A^{1/2} \langle r^2 \rangle^{1/2}\) is the range of the state.

## Context

This work extends the perturbation method of Ref. [2] for solving large systems of bound-state linear equations in the hyperspherical-hyperradial expansion, which was efficient for A=3 but faced computational difficulties for A>4 due to the rapid increase in the number of hyperspherical harmonics with \(K\). The method contrasts with the Green Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) method, which is limited to bound states and does not provide wave functions in a simple form, and with Faddeev-Yakubovsky calculations for A=4 (Ref. [8]), which require too much numerical effort. It also differs from effective interaction methods (Refs. [9, 10]) that disregard higher excitations and cannot reproduce correlation effects. The approach enables testing realistic nuclear forces from effective field theory in four-nucleon reaction problems and scattering below three-body breakup thresholds in \(^5\)He, \(^7\)Li, \(^7\)Be, and \(^8\)Be systems.
