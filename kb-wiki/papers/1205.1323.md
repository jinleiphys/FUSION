# Single-Particle Distributions in Multi-Chain Model with successive collision for High-Energy Nucleus-Nucleus collisions

> High-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions are studied in multi-chain model with successive collision. Analytic forms for single-particle distribution are derived.

## Key claim

The authors derive analytic forms for single-particle distributions in nucleus-nucleus (A-A) collisions using a multi-chain model (MCM) with successive collisions, under the assumption that the dynamics scales with energy and that transverse momentum distributions are independent of incident energy and longitudinal momentum.

## Method

The authors extend the MCM with successive collision from nucleon-nucleus (N-A) to nucleus-nucleus (A-B) collisions by introducing a vector-operator formalism in moment space using Mellin transformations. They assume three recurrence equations for the operators and impose a sum decomposition rule that restricts the maximum number of collisions of one nucleon inside the projectile nucleus to the mass number of the target nucleus. The full combinations of chain configurations are resolved, and the resulting single-particle distributions are expressed in terms of Glauber probabilities combined with hypergeometric distributions.

## Key numbers

- The inelastic cross section of N-A collisions is given by sigma^{NA}_{inel} = integral d b sum_{m=1}^{A} P_m(A;b), where P_m(A;b) is the Glauber probability.
- The averaged collision number for N-A collisions is bar n = A sigma^{NN}_{inel} / sigma^{NA}_{inel}.
- The inelastic cross section of A-B collisions is given by sigma^{AB}_{inel} = integral d b sum_{m=1}^{AB} P_m(AB;b), where P_m(AB;b) is the Glauber probability for A-B collisions.
- The averaged collision number for A-B collisions is bar n = AB sigma^{NN}_{inel} / sigma^{AB}_{inel}.
- The maximum number of collisions of one nucleon inside projectile nucleus A is restricted to the mass number B of the target nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the multi-chain model (MCM) previously applied to nucleon-nucleus collisions by Noda and collaborators (Refs. 6-9) to the more complex case of nucleus-nucleus collisions. The authors note that earlier dynamical simulation models were developed because analytical calculation for A-A collisions was difficult due to the complexity of resolving full chain configurations. The derived analytic forms enable calculation of single-particle distributions for inclusive processes A+B to C+X without requiring simulation, and the authors suggest this formalism could be applied to polarization phenomena in A-A collisions, which are being investigated experimentally.
