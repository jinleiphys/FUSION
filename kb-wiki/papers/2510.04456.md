# $β$-Delayed Proton Pandemonium: A first look at the $^{31}$Cl($βp γ$)$^{30}$P decay scheme

> Positron decays of proton-rich nuclides exhibit large $Q$ values, producing complex cascades which often involve various radiations, including protons and $γ$ rays. Often, only one of the two are measured in a single experiment, limiting the accuracy and completeness of the decay scheme. An example is $^{31}$Cl, for which protons and $γ$ rays have been measured in detail individually but never with substantial sensitivity to proton-$γ$ coincidences. The purpose of this work is to provide detailed measurements of $^{31}$Cl $β$-delayed proton decay including $β$-$p$-$γ$ sequences, extract spectroscopic information on $^{31}$S excited states as well as their $β$ feeding, and compare to shell-model calculations. A fast, fragmented beam of $^{31}$Cl provided was deposited in the Gaseous Detector with Germanium Tagging (GADGET) system. GADGET's gas-filled Proton Detector was used to detect $β$-delayed protons, and the Segmented Germanium Array (SeGA) was used to detect $β$-delayed $γ$ rays. Up to 20 previously unobserved $β$-delayed proton transitions have been discovered, most of which populate excited states of $^{30}$P. Here present the first detailed $^{31}$Cl($βp γ$)$^{30}$P decay scheme and find improved agreement with theoretical calculations of the Gamow-Teller strength distribution for $^{31}$S excitation energies $7.5 < E_x < 9.5$ MeV. The present work demonstrates that the capability to detect $β$-delayed protons and $γ$ rays in coincidence is essential to construct accurate positron decay schemes for comparison to theoretical nuclear structure calculations. In some respects, this phenomenon for $β$-delayed protons resembles the pandemonium effect originally introduced for $β$-delayed $γ$ rays.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that many previously assumed ground-state $\beta$-delayed proton transitions from $^{31}$Cl actually populate excited states of $^{30}$P, revealing a "proton pandemonium" effect analogous to the well-known pandemonium effect for $\beta$-delayed $\gamma$ rays, and that proton-$\gamma$ coincidence measurements are essential for constructing accurate decay schemes.

## Method

A fast fragmented beam of $^{31}$Cl was implanted in the GADGET system at NSCL, which combines a gas-filled Proton Detector (MICROMEGAS) for detecting $\beta$-delayed protons with the Segmented Germanium Array (SeGA) for detecting $\beta$-delayed $\gamma$ rays. Proton-$\gamma$ coincidence data were analyzed by gating on $\gamma$ rays from known excited states of $^{30}$P to extract proton spectra populating each final state, with careful background subtraction using both random coincidence scaling and off-peak sampling. Proton detection efficiencies were modeled using a Simplified Geometric Monte Carlo (SGMC) approach incorporating electron diffusion effects in the P10 gas, validated against detailed Geant4 simulations.

## Key numbers

- Absolute detection efficiencies of 4.5(5)% and 2.7(6)% for the 1248- and 2234-keV $\gamma$ rays, respectively, corresponding to ground-state transitions from the two lowest-lying excited states in $^{31}$S
- Revised proton energy of $E_{p_0}=258(4)$ keV for the astrophysically relevant resonance in the $^{30}$P($p,\gamma$)$^{31}$S reaction, with absolute intensity $I_p$(abs) = $9.0(10) \times 10^{-6}$
- Proton branching ratio $\Gamma_p/\Gamma = 2.4(3) \times 10^{-4}$ and resonance strength $\omega\gamma=77(45)$ $\mu$eV for the 258-keV resonance
- New $^{31}$S level at 8418(3) keV identified from a $\beta$-delayed proton decay at $E_{p_3}=834(3)$ keV populating the 1454-keV level of $^{30}$P
- At least 20 previously unobserved $\beta$-delayed proton transitions discovered, most populating excited states of $^{30}$P
- Two new $^{31}$S proton-unbound levels identified (denoted with asterisks in the data table)

## Context

This work extends previous measurements of $^{31}$Cl $\beta^+$ decay that only provided substantial information on either proton singles spectra (Saastamoinen 2011, Saastamoinen 2016, Batchelder 2020) or $\gamma$-ray emission from $^{31}$S excited states (Bennett 2018), but never with proton-$\gamma$ coincidence sensitivity. The results demonstrate that the GADGET system, previously used for astrophysical resonance studies (Budner 2022), can serve as a sensitive probe of nuclear structure. The recent upgrade to GADGET II, integrating with the FRIB Decay Station Initiator (Mahajan 2024), enables even more detailed measurements including discrimination between $\beta$-delayed proton and $\alpha$ events.
