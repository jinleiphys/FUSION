# Thermal photons from $πρ\ra πγ$ revisited

> We evaluate the photon spectra from the reaction $πρ\ra πγ$ for the exchange of $π$, $ρ$, $ω$, $ϕ$ and $a_1$ as intermediary mesons. It is found that the contributions from the intermediary $a_1$ is more than any other meson exchange processes up to photon energies 2.5 GeV.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for the reaction π ρ → π γ, the a₁ meson exchange process is the dominant contributor to thermal photon production for photon energies up to 2.5 GeV, contradicting a prior claim that ω exchange in the t-channel is the single most important process above 2 GeV.

## Method

The authors evaluated photon emission rates from the reaction π ρ → π γ by considering all possible diagrams with π, ρ, ω, φ, and a₁ mesons in the intermediate state, including s-channel, t-channel, and four-point interactions. Coupling constants were fixed using vector meson dominance to reproduce known decay widths (ω → π⁰ γ, φ → π⁰ γ, ρ → π π, and a₁ → π ρ and a₁ → π γ), and coherent sums were performed for diagrams of the same class without introducing form factors at the vertices.

## Key numbers

- At a temperature T = 200 MeV, the a₁ exchange process is the dominant contributor to the photon emission rate for photon energies up to E ∼ 2.5 GeV.
- Beyond E ∼ 2.5 GeV, the contributions from ω exchange processes in the s and t channels become comparable to the a₁ exchange processes.
- The φ exchange contribution is negligibly small compared to the other meson exchange processes.

## Context

This work extends the study of thermal photon production from hadronic matter by revisiting the reaction π ρ → π γ, which is the dominant photon production process for photon energies above 0.5 GeV. It directly contradicts the claim of Turbide (2003) that t-channel ω exchange is the single most dominant process for E > 2 GeV, and also disagrees with Xiong (2003) regarding the relative importance of s-channel versus t-channel a₁ exchange. The results enable more accurate subtraction of hadronic photon backgrounds when searching for quark-gluon plasma signals in heavy ion collisions.
