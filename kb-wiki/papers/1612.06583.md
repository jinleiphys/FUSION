# Collinear cluster tri-partition: Kinematics constraints and stability of collinearity

> A new mode of nuclear fission has been proposed by the FOBOS collaboration, called Collinear Cluster Tri-partition (CCT), suggesting that three heavy fission fragments can be emitted perfectly collinearly in low-energy fission. It is surprising that CCT escaped observation for so long given the relatively high reported yield, of roughly 0.5% relative to binary fission. These claims call for an independent verification with a different experimental technique. Verification experiments based on direct observation of CCT fragments with fission fragment spectrometers require guidance with respect to the allowed kinetic energy range, which we present in this paper. We discuss corresponding model calculations which, if CCT is found in such verification experiments, could indicate how the breakups proceed. We also study the intrinsic stability of collinearity. Three different decay models are used, which span together the timescales of three-body fission. These models are used to calculate the possible kinetic energy ranges of CCT fragments in 235U(n,f) and 252Cf(sf). We use semi-classical trajectory calculations with a Monte-Carlo method to study the intrinsic stability of collinearity. CCT has a high net Q-value, but in a sequential decay, the intermediate steps are energetically and geometrically unfavorable or even forbidden. Moreover, perfect collinearity is extremely unstable, and broken by the slightest perturbation. According to our results, the central fragment would be very difficult to detect due to its low kinetic energy, raising the question of why previous experiments could not detect a missing-mass signature corresponding to CCT. We find that a realization of CCT would require an unphysical fine-tuning of the initial conditions. Our results enable independent experimental verification and encourage further critical theoretical studies of CCT.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Collinear Cluster Tri-partition (CCT), a proposed ternary fission mode with three heavy fragments emitted perfectly collinearly, would require unphysical fine-tuning of initial conditions to be realized, and that perfect collinearity is extremely unstable against the slightest perturbation, despite the high reported yield of roughly 0.5% relative to binary fission.

## Method

The authors used three decay models spanning the timescales of three-body fission: a sequential decay model (long timescales between scissions), an "almost sequential" decay model (intermediate timescales), and a true ternary decay model (infinitesimal timescales). Semi-classical trajectory calculations with a point charge approximation and a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method were employed to compute final kinetic energies, while a Monte-Carlo method sampled perturbations in the ternary particle position and momentum perpendicular to the fission axis to study the intrinsic stability of collinearity. Calculations were performed for the systems 235U(nth,f) and 252Cf(sf) with fragment masses corresponding to isotopes of Sn, Ni, Si, and Ca.

## Key numbers

- The reported yield of CCT is roughly 0.5% relative to binary fission
- In 90% of all ternary fission events, the third particle is a 4He nucleus; in only 1% of events does the ternary particle have Z>2, with yields rapidly dropping with increased Z
- Ternary particles up to Z=16 have been observed at yields of the order of 10^{-9} per fission
- Dedicated counting experiments gave upper yield limits below 10^{-8} for true ternary fission
- A similar experiment by Kravtsov (1999) showed no indication of CCT down to a level of 7.5×10^{-6} per fission in 252Cf(sf)
- The most energetically favorable masses in the FOBOS analysis were ASn≈132 and ANi≈68-72 with ν≈0-4, with missing masses ASi≈34-36 and ACa≈48-52
- The most favorable mass splits for the intermediate fragments were found to be AMo=104 and ACd=120 with neutron multiplicity ν1=0
- In the decay of 104Mo, the most favorable mass split is ANi=70 and ASi=34 with ν2=0; in the decay of 120Cd, it is ANi=70 and ACa=50 with ν2=0
- Any neutron multiplicity ν2>0 lowers the IF Q-value and the final total kinetic energy of the TP and LF by up to 8 MeV per neutron
- The tip distance for physically reasonable scission configurations is less than 4 fm
- The trajectory calculations used a time step of 10^{-25} s, continuing until more than 99% of the potential energy was converted into kinetic energy

## Context

This work extends the controversial claims by the FOBOS collaboration (Pyatkov 2010, 2012) regarding Collinear Cluster Tri-partition, which suggested a new mode of nuclear fission with three heavy fragments emitted collinearly at yields of 5×10^{-3} per fission. The paper contradicts the CCT interpretation by showing that sequential decay models require intermediate steps with extremely low or negative Q-values and high fission barriers, and that collinearity is intrinsically unstable. The results enable independent experimental verification using direct observation methods such as the LOHENGRIN fission fragment recoil separator, and encourage further critical theoretical studies of CCT.
