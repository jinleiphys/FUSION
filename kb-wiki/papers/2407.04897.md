# QCD Masterclass Lectures on Jet Physics and Machine Learning

> These lectures were presented at the 2024 QCD Masterclass in Saint-Jacut-de-la-Mer, France. They introduce and review fundamental theorems and principles of machine learning within the context of collider particle physics, focused on application to jet identification and discrimination. Numerous examples of binary discrimination in jet physics are studied in detail, including $H\to b\bar b$ identification in fixed-order perturbation theory, generic one-versus two-prong discrimination with parametric power counting techniques, and up versus down quark jet classification by assuming the central limit theorem, isospin conservation, and a convergent moment expansion of the single particle energy distribution. Quark versus gluon jet discrimination is considered in multiple contexts, from using additive, infrared and collinear safe observables, to using hadronic multiplicity, and to including measurements of the jet charge. While many of the results presented here are well known, some novel results are presented, the most prominent being a parametrized expression for the likelihood ratio of quark versus gluon discrimination for jets on which hadronic multiplicity and jet charge are simultaneously measured. End-of-lecture exercises are also provided.

## Key claim

These lectures argue that by treating the machine learning architecture as a black box and applying fundamental theorems (universal approximation theorem, Neyman-Pearson lemma, central limit theorem) together with QCD principles, one can analytically determine the optimal binary discriminant for jet classification problems without needing to know the internal workings of the machine; the most prominent novel result is a parametrized expression for the likelihood ratio of quark versus gluon discrimination when hadronic multiplicity and jet charge are simultaneously measured.

## Method

The author reviews fundamental theorems of machine learning (universal approximation theorem, Neyman-Pearson lemma, central limit theorem) and QCD principles (infrared and collinear safety, collinear and soft factorization, jet algorithms) and then applies them to four binary discrimination problems in jet physics: quark versus gluon jet discrimination using scale-invariant emission pictures, H to b bbar identification using fixed-order perturbation theory, quark versus gluon discrimination using hadronic multiplicity, and up versus down quark jet classification using the central limit theorem, isospin conservation, and a convergent moment expansion of the single particle energy distribution. The analysis proceeds by determining signal and background probability distributions on an appropriate data space and then computing the likelihood ratio as the optimal discriminant.

## Key numbers

- The universal approximation theorem states that for any epsilon greater than 0, there exist weights, biases, and coefficients such that |f(x) - G(x)| is less than epsilon, where G(x) is a linear combination of sigmoids with N terms (N finite but likely enormous for arbitrary accuracy)
- The AUC (area under the ROC curve) ranges from 0 (perfect discrimination) to 1/2 (completely random discrimination), with the definition AUC = integral from 0 to 1 of dx Sigma_s(Sigma_b^{-1}(x))
- The mean of the background distribution of the likelihood ratio is unity: integral from 0 to infinity of dL L p_b(L) = 1

## Context

These lectures extend the author's previous pedagogical work on jets (Larkoski 2017, 2020, 2021) and textbooks on particle physics (Larkoski 2019) and quantum mechanics (Larkoski 2022), while situating machine learning in particle physics within the broader explosion of ML applications that began around the mid-2010s with the rise of GPUs and deep neural networks. The work enables theorists to calculate the absolute best possible discrimination performance any machine could achieve for jet classification problems, using the Neyman-Pearson lemma as the foundation for optimal binary discrimination, and builds on the historical development of jet substructure from the discovery of the gluon (TASSO 1979, Barber 1979, PLUTO 1979, JADE 1979) through modern applications like H to b bbar identification that jump-started the field.
