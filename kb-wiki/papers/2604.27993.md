# Hadron properties at finite temperature

> This review provides an overview of thermal effects on hadron properties, focusing on the theoretical frameworks used to describe in-medium modifications of masses, decay widths, and spectral functions. We examine the application of finite-temperature quantum field theory -- specifically the imaginary-time formalism (ITF) -- to analyze both light- and heavy-hadron sectors. For light hadrons, we discuss the role of chiral symmetry restoration and the different definitions of thermal masses in effective field theories, like chiral perturbation theory. In the heavy-flavor sector, we review recent progress in describing open-heavy mesons and quarkonia using self-consistent unitarized approaches and nonrelativistic effective field theories. All these results are complemented by analyses of recent lattice-QCD calculations using the Euclidean formulation of QCD at finite temperature, relevant to extract screening masses and reconstructed spectral functions. Finally, we discuss the phenomenological impact of the thermal modifications on experimental observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, including numerical simulations, dilepton spectra, transport coefficients, and hadron femtoscopy. By combining phenomenological considerations with robust theoretical tools, this review provides a coherent picture of how thermal effects emerge in the hadronic phase and how they can be systematically studied within controlled frameworks. Ultimately, the discussion serves as a bridge between experimental observations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions and fundamental developments in finite-temperature QCD and effective field theories for hadronic systems.

## Key claim

This review synthesizes theoretical frameworks for thermal modifications of hadron properties, arguing that finite-temperature effects on masses, decay widths, and spectral functions can be systematically studied within controlled effective field theories and lattice QCD, but the quantitative reliability of purely hadronic approaches becomes increasingly limited near the chiral restoration region where deconfined degrees of freedom emerge.

## Method

The authors review the imaginary-time formalism (ITF) of finite-temperature quantum field theory, where the temporal direction is compactified to a circle of circumference β = 1/T, and bosonic fields satisfy periodic boundary conditions while fermionic fields obey antiperiodic boundary conditions. They examine how thermal corrections enter hadronic propagators through Matsubara frequency sums, with the self-energy Π(ω, p; T) encoding interactions with the thermal bath: its real part contributes to mass shifts while its imaginary part is associated with thermal width. The review covers applications to light hadrons using chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) and the linear sigma model, and to heavy-flavor hadrons using nonrelativistic effective field theories and self-consistent unitarized approaches, complemented by lattice-QCD calculations using the Euclidean formulation.

## Key numbers

* The chiral crossover transition at zero baryochemical potential occurs around Tc ≃ 160 MeV, as established by lattice QCD (Aoki 2006).
* Chemical freeze-out temperature in heavy-ion collisions is Tch ≃ 150 MeV, where inelastic interactions effectively cease (Braun-Munzinger 2003, Andronic 2017).
* Kinetic freeze-out temperature is Tkin ≃ 110 MeV, when momentum distributions decouple from the medium.
* In ChPT calculations (Song 1993), the relative difference between pole mass and screening mass for pions reaches approximately 19% at T = 200 MeV.
* The average collision time for pions from leading-order ChPT scattering rate is τ ≃ 12 Fπ⁴/T⁵, where Fπ is the pion decay constant at leading order (Goity 1989).
* For the pion damping coefficient at moderate temperatures (T = 40–100 MeV), inclusion of phenomenological interactions leads to a correction of about 30% with respect to leading-order ChPT (Schenk 1991).

## Context

This review extends the line of work on finite-temperature QCD and hadronic effective field theories, building on foundational thermal field theory references (Weldon 1983, Kapusta 2006, Bellac 2011, Laine 2016) and lattice-QCD results for the equation of state (Borsanyi 2013, HotQCD 2014) and screening masses (Bazavov 2019). It enables systematic connections between theoretical predictions for in-medium hadron modifications and experimental observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, including dilepton spectra, transport coefficients, and hadron femtoscopy, while identifying open questions about the quantitative reliability of hadronic models near the chiral restoration temperature.
