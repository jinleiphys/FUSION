# Hadron Physics Opportunities at FAIR

> This White Paper outlines a coordinated, decade-spanning programme of hadron and QCD studies anchored at the GSI/FAIR accelerator complex. Profiting from intense deuteron, proton and pion beams coupled with high-rate capable detectors and an international theory effort, the initiative addresses fundamental questions related to the strong interaction featuring confinement and dynamical mass generation. This includes our understanding of hadron-hadron interactions and the composition of hadrons through mapping the baryon and meson spectra, including exotic states, and quantifying hadron structure. This interdisciplinary research connects topics in the fields of nuclear, heavy-ion, and (nuclear) astro (particle) physics, linking, for example, terrestrial data to constraints on neutron star structure. A phased roadmap with SIS100 accelerator start-up and envisaged detector upgrades will yield precision cross sections, transition form factors, in-medium spectral functions, and validated theory inputs. Synergies with external programmes at international accelerator facilities worldwide are anticipated. The programme is expected to deliver decisive advances in our understanding of non-perturbative (strong) QCD and astrophysics, and high-rate detector and data-science technology.

## Key claim

This White Paper outlines a coordinated, decade-spanning programme of hadron and QCD studies anchored at the GSI/FAIR accelerator complex, with the claim that the SIS100 accelerator start-up (planned for 2028) and envisaged detector upgrades will yield precision cross sections, transition form factors, in-medium spectral functions, and validated theory inputs, though the actual delivery of these results depends on the phased roadmap and continued international funding.

## Method

The paper surveys the planned experimental programme at the GSI/FAIR complex, which will use intense deuteron, proton, and pion beams from the SIS100 synchrotron (protons up to 29 GeV) coupled with high-rate capable detectors including CBM, HADES, and PANDA. It compares the FAIR capabilities with those of other international facilities (BEPC-BESIII, CERN-SPS and LHC, EIC, ELSA, HIAF, JLab, J-PARC, MESA, NICA, SuperKEKB) by tabulating beam types, momenta or center-of-mass energies, intensities or luminosities, and operational status. The document also describes the planned detector upgrades and the theory effort supporting the experimental programme.

## Key numbers

* FAIR-SIS100 will provide proton beams of 5–30 GeV/c with intensity of 10^13/s, with first operation planned for 2028.
* The SPS at CERN delivers secondary pion beams of 10–380 GeV with intensity 5×10^8/s and secondary kaon beams of 10–380 GeV with intensity 2.5×10^7/s.
* JLab CEBAF delivers electron beams up to 12 GeV with intensities of 10^32–10^35/cm^2/s across its experimental halls.
* SuperKEKB operates with 7 GeV electrons and 4 GeV positrons, targeting a luminosity of 6×10^35/cm^2/s, about 30 times higher than its predecessor KEKB.
* BESIII has already collected 10^10 J/ψ events, 2.7×10^9 ψ(2S) events, and 20 fb^-1 on the ψ(3770) resonance.
* The EIC will cover a center-of-mass energy range of 20–140 GeV with expected luminosity of 10^33–10^34/cm^2/s, with first operation expected around 2035.
* J-PARC high-p beam line delivers proton beams of 31 GeV/c with intensity up to 10^10 per pulse (2 s pulse every 4.2 s).

## Context

This White Paper extends the planning efforts for hadron physics at FAIR, building on the prior work of the COMPASS, HADES, and PANDA collaborations, and situates the FAIR programme within the global landscape of facilities including JLab (GlueX, CLAS12), J-PARC, BESIII, and the future EIC. The document enables a coordinated roadmap for the next decade of hadron physics at FAIR, connecting terrestrial data to constraints on neutron star structure and linking topics in nuclear, heavy-ion, and astroparticle physics.
