# Progress on reactions with exotic nuclei

> Modelling breakup reactions with exotic nuclei represents a challenge in several ways. The CDCC method (continuum discretized coupled channel) has been very successful in its various applications. Here, we briefly mention a few developments that have contributed to the progress in this field as well as some pertinent problems that remain to be answered.

## Key claim

The Continuum Discretized Coupled Channel (CDCC) method has been successful for modeling breakup reactions with exotic nuclei, but significant problems remain unresolved, including a 60% inconsistency in the quadrupole excitation strength for \(^8\)B breakup between low-energy and high-energy data, and the need to address orthogonality issues when including both projectile and target continua.

## Method

The authors review developments in the CDCC method, where scattering states of the projectile are binned in energy or momentum and coupled via continuum-continuum interactions. They analyze the long-range behavior of these couplings (scaling as \(1/R^2\) for dipole and \(1/R^3\) for higher multipoles) and compare standard CDCC breakup with an alternative "transfer to the continuum" approach that expands wavefunctions in the target continuum rather than the projectile continuum.

## Key numbers

- Coulomb multistep effects in \(^8\)B breakup around the Coulomb barrier reduced the cross section by up to 20%, with the nuclear peak at larger angles disappearing through continuum-continuum couplings.
- The binding energy of \(^8\)B is 0.137 MeV, making its continuum very important in reactions such as \(^7\)Be(d,n)\(^8\)B.
- Low-energy Notre Dame data and high-energy NSCL/MSU data show a 60% inconsistency in the quadrupole excitation strength for \(^8\)B breakup.
- Continuum-continuum couplings behave as \(1/R^2\) for dipole transitions and \(1/R^3\) for all higher multipoles.

## Context

This work extends the CDCC method developed by earlier authors (e.g., the original CDCC formulation) and addresses specific discrepancies in \(^8\)B breakup data relevant to the \(S_{17}\) astrophysical factor. The paper identifies the need to bridge different breakup models (standard CDCC versus transfer to the continuum) and highlights the potential role of core excitation, which is better treated in microscopic approaches such as the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum (SMEC), to resolve the 60% quadrupole strength inconsistency.
