# Experimental review on the chiral magnetic effect in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The chiral magnetic effect (CME) refers to a predicted phenomena in quantum chromodynamics that manifests as a charge separation along an external magnetic field, driven by an imbalance of quark chirality. Searches for the CME has been carried out by azimuthal particle correlations in relativistic heavy ion collisions where such a chirality imbalance is anticipated and a strong magnetic field is created in the initial stage. No conclusive experimental evidence on the CME has been established so far because of large background contributions to azimuthal correlation observables. We review the status of the experimental search for the CME, covering the observables used, the techniques to mitigate backgrounds, and the strengths and limitations of various experimental approaches, and outline a future prospect of the CME search in high-energy nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

The chiral magnetic effect (CME) remains experimentally unconfirmed because all azimuthal correlation observables used to search for it are contaminated by large, charge-dependent background contributions that cannot be fully separated from any potential CME signal, despite two decades of measurements at RHIC and the LHC.

## Method

The review surveys experimental measurements of the charge-dependent three-point azimuthal correlator $\gamma_{\alpha\beta} \equiv \langle \cos(\phi_\alpha +\phi_\beta -2\psi_{\mathrm{RP}})\rangle$ and its opposite-sign minus same-sign difference $\Delta\gamma$, which is the primary CME observable. It describes multiple background-mitigation strategies: event-shape engineering (ESE) to vary elliptic flow $v_2$ while keeping the magnetic field fixed; isobar collisions ($^{96}_{44}$Ru vs $^{96}_{40}$Zr) to compare systems with different magnetic fields but similar flow; comparative measurements using spectator planes versus participant planes; and mixed-harmonic correlators such as $\gamma_{123}$ that are sensitive only to backgrounds.

## Key numbers

- The magnetic field created in non-central heavy ion collisions is estimated to be on the order of $B\sim m_\pi^2/e \sim 10^{18}$ Gauss
- The elliptic flow values measured with respect to participant planes ($v_2^{\mathrm{PP}}$) and spectator planes ($v_2^{\mathrm{SP}}$) differ by an appreciable 10-20% depending on collision centrality
- The double ratio $(\Delta\gamma/v_2)_{\mathrm{Ru}}/(\Delta\gamma/v_2)_{\mathrm{Zr}}$ from the isobar program did not exceed unity as naively expected, indicating non-negligible background differences between the two systems
- The $\kappa_2$ parameter defined as $\kappa_2 = \Delta\gamma_{112}/(v_2\Delta\delta)$ cannot be theoretically calculated or experimentally measured in a background-only scenario, leading to large systematic uncertainties in any extracted CME signal fraction

## Context

This review extends the experimental search program initiated by the STAR Collaboration (2009) with the first Au+Au measurements of the $\gamma$ correlator, and covers subsequent work by ALICE and CMS at the LHC. It builds on the theoretical framework of Kharzeev (2007) and Fukushima (2008) predicting the CME from QCD topological charge fluctuations in strong magnetic fields. The review identifies the isobar collision results from STAR (2021) as a key recent development showing that background differences between Ru+Ru and Zr+Zr collisions are larger than anticipated, and points to the spectator/participant plane method proposed by Xu (2017) and Voloshin (2018) as a promising path forward that does not depend on specific background models.
