# Bose-Einstein condensation phenomenology in systems with repulsive interactions

> The role of repulsive interactions in statistical systems of Bose particles is investigated. Three different phenomenological frameworks are considered: a mean field model, an excluded volume model, and a model with a medium dependent effective mass. All three models are tuned to yield similar equations of state, with only minor deviations from the ideal Bose gas at small chemical potentials. Our analysis indicates, however, that these models lead to qualitatively different results for the Bose-Einstein condensation phenomenon. We discuss the different aspects of this phenomenon, namely, an onset of the Bose-Einstein condensation, particle number fluctuations, and a behavior of the Bose condensate. The obtained results can be helpful for interpreting the lattice QCD data at small temperature and large isospin chemical potential and the data on multiple pion production in high energy nuclear collisions.

# Bose-Einstein condensation phenomenology in systems with repulsive interactions

## Key claim

While repulsive interactions in Bose systems are often assumed to universally increase the critical temperature for Bose-Einstein condensation relative to the ideal Bose gas, the authors show that this conclusion is not of general validity: three different phenomenological models (mean field, excluded volume, and effective mass) tuned to produce nearly identical equations of state at zero chemical potential yield qualitatively different predictions for the BEC onset temperature, with the effective mass model actually predicting a *decrease* in critical temperature compared to the ideal gas.

## Method

The authors compare three phenomenological frameworks for repulsive interactions in a relativistic Bose gas (specifically pions): a mean field model with a density-dependent potential U(n)=an, an excluded volume model with eigenvolume parameter b, and an effective mass model where the particle mass increases with scalar density via m* = m + c n_s. All three model parameters are tuned so that at zero chemical potential and T=150 MeV, the pressure is suppressed to approximately 98% of the ideal Bose gas value, ensuring only minor deviations from ideality at μ=0. The BEC onset lines, condensate fractions, particle number fluctuations (scaled variance ω), and zero-temperature properties are then computed and compared across models and against the ideal Bose gas baseline.

## Key numbers

- Model parameters fixed at m_π = 135 MeV, T = 150 MeV, requiring p(T,μ=0)/p_id(T,μ=0) ≈ 0.98: a = 0.15 m_π^{-2} (MF), b = 0.145 m_π^{-3} (EV), c = 2.21 m_π^{-2} (EM)
- The excluded volume parameter b corresponds to a pion hard-core radius r ≈ 0.3 fm
- At T = 110 MeV and n = 0.06 fm^{-3}, the ratio p(T,n)/p_id(T,n) equals approximately 1.02 (MF), 1.02 (EV), and 1.15 (EM)
- The EM model exhibits a maximal temperature T_c^{max} above which BEC does not occur; this is comparable to lattice QCD results where T_c^{max} ≈ 160 MeV ≈ 1.2 m_π
- Non-relativistic and ultra-relativistic analytic expressions for T_c^{max} in the EM model are given in terms of the parameter c

## Context

This work extends the long-standing discussion of how repulsive interactions modify BEC, which previously found that repulsion increases the critical temperature (e.g., Baym (1999), Holzmann (1999), PhysRevLett.83.1703). The authors show this conclusion is model-dependent by comparing three frameworks that yield identical behavior at μ=0. The results are relevant for interpreting lattice QCD data at finite isospin chemical potential (Brandt 2017, Brandt 2018) and for pion BEC in heavy-ion collisions (Begun 2006, Begun 2008). The EM model's prediction of a maximal BEC temperature, similar to lattice QCD findings, suggests it may be useful for parameterizing the QCD equation of state at finite isospin density for temperatures below approximately 160 MeV, even though the physical mechanism in QCD involves a transition to partonic degrees of freedom not present in the EM model.
