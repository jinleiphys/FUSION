# Polarization in relativistic heavy ion collisions: a theoretical perspective

> We review the theoretical framework for the calculation of particle polarization in relativistic heavy ion collisions within the hydrodynamical model. The covariant decomposition of the mean spin vector is presented and open theoretical issues addressed.

## Key claim
The paper reviews the theoretical framework for calculating particle polarization in relativistic heavy ion collisions using the hydrodynamic model, asserting that polarization is proportional to thermal vorticity (the antisymmetric gradient of the four-temperature field) under local thermodynamic equilibrium, but notes that the exact solution for polarization at global thermodynamic equilibrium with constant non-zero thermal vorticity remains an open theoretical issue.

## Method
The author derives the mean spin vector for spin-1/2 particles from the local thermodynamic equilibrium density operator, expanding it to first order in thermal vorticity. The spin vector is then decomposed into three contributions: a temperature gradient term, a vorticity term, and an acceleration term, with the final polarization obtained by integrating over the freeze-out hypersurface using a Cooper-Frye-like formula extended to particles with spin.

## Key numbers
- Global polarization of Λ hyperons observed by STAR over center-of-mass energy range 7.7 to 200 GeV
- Polarization decreases from few percent at sqrt(s_NN) = O(10) GeV to few permille at sqrt(s_NN) = O(100) GeV
- At TeV energy range (LHC), global polarization along angular momentum direction is not seen, likely below experimental sensitivity
- Plasma acceleration at outset of hydrodynamical stage is estimated at A ~ 10^30 g

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Becattini et al. (2013, 2015) that predicted polarization from thermal vorticity, which was confirmed by STAR (2017) measurements. It builds on earlier estimates by X.N. Wang (1991) and provides the basis for hydrodynamic calculations of polarization that were performed by Becattini et al. (2015, 2016) and X.N. Wang et al. (2017). The review identifies open issues including whether the spin tensor has full physical meaning and whether relativistic hydrodynamics must be extended to fluids with spin, as suggested by Florkowski et al. (2017).
