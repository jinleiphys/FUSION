# Strangelets at finite temperature: nucleon emission rates, interface and shell effects

> We investigate the properties of strangelets at finite temperature $T$, where an equivparticle model is adopted with both the linear confinement and leading-order perturbative interactions accounted for using density-dependent quark masses. The shell effects are examined by solving the Dirac equations for quarks in the mean-field approximation, which diminish with temperature as the occupation probability of each single-particle levels fixed by the Fermi-Dirac statistics, i.e., shell dampening. Consequently, instead of decreasing with temperature, the surface tension extracted from a liquid-drop formula increases with $T$ until reaching its peak at $T\approx 20$-40 MeV with vanishing shell corrections, where the formula roughly reproduces the free energy per baryon of all strangelets. The curvature term, nevertheless, decreases with $T$ despite the presence of shell effects. The neutron and proton emission rates are fixed microscopically according to the external nucleon gas densities that are in equilibrium with strangelets, which generally increase with $T$ ($\lesssim 50$ MeV) for stable strangelets but decrease for those that are unstable against nucleon emission at $T=0$. The energy, free energy, entropy, charge-to-mass ratio, strangeness per baryon, and root-mean-square radius of $β$-stable strangelets obtained with various parameter sets are presented as well. The results indicated in this work are useful for understanding the products of binary compact star mergers and heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The surface tension of strange quark matter, extracted from a liquid-drop fit to the free energy per baryon of strangelets, increases with temperature up to a peak at T ≈ 20–40 MeV, contrary to the usual expectation that it decreases with temperature; this counterintuitive behavior is attributed to the dampening of shell effects as temperature rises.

## Method

The authors adopt an equivparticle model with density-dependent quark masses that incorporate linear confinement and leading-order perturbative interactions. They solve the Dirac equations for quarks in the mean-field approximation for spherically symmetric strangelets, obtaining single-particle levels self-consistently with the mean fields and quark wave functions. The occupation probability of each level follows the Fermi-Dirac distribution at a given temperature, and neutron and proton emission rates are fixed from the densities of external nucleon gases in equilibrium with the strangelet.

## Key numbers

- Surface tension σ increases with T, peaking at T ≈ 20–40 MeV (e.g., for parameter set C = −0.5, √D = 180 MeV, σ rises from ~10 MeV/fm² at T = 0 to ~15 MeV/fm² at T = 40 MeV).
- Curvature term λ decreases monotonically with T (e.g., from ~2.5 MeV at T = 0 to ~1.0 MeV at T = 80 MeV for the same parameter set).
- For β-stable strangelets at T = 10 MeV, magic numbers for quarks are identified at N_i = 6, 24, 60, 96, with robust magic numbers 6 and 24.
- The entropy per baryon S/A approaches its bulk limit S₀/A already at A ≥ 4 (e.g., S₀/A ≈ 0.5 at T = 10 MeV for C = −0.5, √D = 180 MeV).
- The charge-to-mass ratio f_z and strangeness per baryon f_s fluctuate with A at small T due to shell effects, becoming smooth at larger A or T; for the adopted parameter set, f_z and f_s are generally insensitive to T other than shell dampening.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of strangelets at zero temperature using the equivparticle model (Xia 2018) and finite-temperature investigations using the MIT bag model (Mustafa 1996, Mustafa 1997) or the multiple reflection expansion method (Zhang 2003, Wen 2005, Chen 2022). By directly solving the Dirac equations rather than using the MRE method, the authors capture shell effects and their dampening with temperature, which leads to the unexpected increase in surface tension. The results are relevant for understanding the products of binary compact star mergers and heavy-ion collisions, and they enable more realistic modeling of strangelet formation and survival in those environments.
