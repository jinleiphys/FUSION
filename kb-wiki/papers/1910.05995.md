# Precision Measurements of Fundamental Interactions with (Anti)Neutrinos

> We discuss the main limitations of past neutrino scattering experiments and possible ways to address them in a next-generation program of precision measurements of fundamental interactions with (anti)neutrinos. A reduction of the longstanding precision gap with respect to electron scattering experiments could provide interesting synergies with the existing efforts in the fixed-target, collider, and nuclear physics communities.

# Precision Measurements of Fundamental Interactions with (Anti)Neutrinos

## Key claim
Past neutrino scattering experiments have been limited by four intrinsic bottlenecks (statistics versus resolution, target control, flux uncertainties, and nuclear effects) that can be largely overcome using a Straw Tube Tracker (STT) detector at the Long-Baseline Neutrino Facility (LBNF), reducing the precision gap with respect to electron scattering experiments. The paper argues this is achievable specifically through a compact detector with physically separated, configurable targets and a solid hydrogen target for flux calibration, but the full program depends on dedicated running with a high-energy beam option after nominal oscillation data taking.

## Method
The author proposes a Straw Tube Tracker (STT) detector design in which thin layers (1-2% radiation length) of chemically pure target materials are alternated with low-mass straw tracking layers, with the target mass representing about 97% of the total detector mass. A solid hydrogen target is implemented by subtracting measurements on dedicated graphite (pure carbon) targets from those on CH₂ plastic targets, with kinematic identification of hydrogen interactions using transverse plane balance to achieve efficiencies exceeding 90% and purities of 80-95%. The relative νμ and ν̅μ fluxes are determined to better than 1% accuracy using exclusive processes on hydrogen with small energy transfer ν, while the absolute ν̅μ flux is obtained from ν̅μ p → μ⁺ n interactions at small momentum transfer Q using the known cross-section from neutron β decay.

## Key numbers
- LBNF nominal beam power: 1.2 MW, with planned upgrades to 2.4 MW
- Expected statistics: a 5-ton fiducial mass detector would collect more than 10⁸ CC interactions at the near site
- Muon energy resolution target: ΔEμ ≤ 0.2%
- STT average density tunable up to ~0.18 g/cm³, with pure tracking module density of ~0.005 g/cm³
- Target layers: 1-2% radiation length each; radiator consists of 150 foils 15 μm thick
- Hydrogen selection efficiency: exceeding 90%; purity: 80-95%
- Past experiments achieved ΔEμ from 0.2% (NOMAD) to 2-4% (MINOS ND); ΔEH from 0.43% (NuTeV) to 5.6% (MINOS ND)
- Past flux uncertainties: 5-10% systematic uncertainties on neutrino fluxes
- Relative flux determination accuracy: better than 1% using exclusive processes on hydrogen

## Context
This work extends the discussion of neutrino scattering limitations identified in reviews such as Alvarez-Ruso (2017) and Mosel (2019), and proposes a detector concept building on earlier STT designs described by Petti (2004) and in the DUNE CDR (Mishra 2008, Acciarri 2016). The proposed program would enable precision tests of electroweak physics, structure functions and parton distributions, sum rules (Adler 1964, Gross and Llewellyn Smith 1969), and measurements of the free neutron structure function and d/u quark ratio (Alekhin 2017), while also providing sensitivity to new physics beyond the Standard Model. The approach aims to create synergies with existing efforts in the fixed-target, collider, and nuclear physics communities.
