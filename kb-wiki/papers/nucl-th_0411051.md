# Quark deconfinement and neutrino trapping in compact stars

> We study the role played by neutrino trapping on the hadron star (HS) to quark star (QS) conversion mechanism proposed recently by Berezhiani and collaborators. We find that the nucleation of quark matter drops inside hadron matter, and therefore the conversion of a HS into a QS, is strongly inhibit by the presence of neutrinos.

## Key claim
Neutrino trapping strongly inhibits the quantum nucleation of a quark matter drop inside hadron matter, and therefore the conversion of a hadron star into a quark star, by raising the static transition pressure and requiring much larger central pressures to achieve comparable nucleation times.

## Method
The authors computed the quantum nucleation time of a quark matter drop in the stellar center using a semiclassical WKB approach, modeling the potential well with volume and surface terms. The hadron phase was described by a relativistic mean-field model fitted to nuclear saturation, and the quark phase by the MIT bag model with a bag constant of 85.29 MeV/fm³, strange quark mass 150 MeV, and zero QCD structure constant. They compared the neutrino-free and neutrino-trapped cases by including neutrinos in the equation of state for both phases.

## Key numbers
- For a star with a mean-life time of 1 year, the baryonic mass with neutrinos trapped is M_B ~ 2.12 M_sun, whereas without neutrinos it is M_B ~ 1.61 M_sun.
- The bag constant used was B = 85.29 MeV/fm³.
- The surface tension was taken as sigma = 30 MeV/fm².
- The strange quark mass was set to m_s = 150 MeV; up and down quark masses were set to zero.
- The number of virtual centers of droplet formation was estimated as N_c ~ 10^48.

## Context
This work extends the hadron-star to quark-star conversion mechanism proposed by Berezhiani et al. (2003), which considered finite-size effects at the phase interface but did not include neutrino trapping. The results show that neutrino trapping, relevant for the early stages of a protoneutron star or for accreting systems, can prevent the conversion entirely or push it to much higher masses, implying that the observable signatures of quark deconfinement depend strongly on the thermal and neutrino evolution of the star. This enables future studies to incorporate realistic cooling and neutrino diffusion timescales into models of hybrid star formation.
