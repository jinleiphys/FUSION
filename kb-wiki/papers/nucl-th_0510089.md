# Invisible nuclear system

> A consecutive formalism and analysis of exactly solvable radial reflectionless potentials with barriers, which in the spatial semiaxis of radial coordinate $r$ have one hole and one barrier, after which they fall down monotonously to zero with increasing of $r$, is presented. It has shown, that at their shape such potentials look qualitatively like radial scattering potentials in two-partial description of collision between particles and nuclei or radial decay potentials in the two-partial description of decay of compound spherical nuclear systems. An analysis shows, that the particle propagates without the smallest reflection and without change of an angle of motion (or tunneling) during its scattering inside the spherically symmetric field of the nucleus with such radial potential of interaction, i. e. the nuclear system with such interacting potential shows itself as invisible for the incident particle with any kinetic energy. An approach for construction of a hierarchy such reflectionless potentials is proposed, wave functions of the first potentials of this hierarchy are found.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a class of exactly solvable radial reflectionless potentials with one hole and one barrier can be constructed, such that a particle scattering in the spherically symmetric field of a nucleus with such a potential propagates without any reflection and without change of angle of motion (or tunneling) for any kinetic energy, making the nuclear system "invisible" to the incident particle. The hedge is that this invisibility holds only for potentials that are reflectionless in the sense defined, and the construction relies on supersymmetric quantum mechanics (SUSY QM) with continuous energy spectra and zero lowest energy levels.

## Method

The author uses Darboux transformations from supersymmetric quantum mechanics to construct a hierarchy of radial reflectionless potentials. Starting from a zero potential (reflectionless by definition), the formalism generates partner potentials via superpotentials; the condition for reflectionlessness is enforced by requiring the first potential in the hierarchy to be zero, which forces the coefficients γ_n in the inverse-power potential family to become integer numbers (0, 2, 6, 12, 20, 30, 42...). The resulting second potential V₂(r) has one hole and one barrier, falls monotonically to zero at large r, and is shown to be reflectionless by analyzing the radial wave functions and their associated probability flows.

## Key numbers

- The reflectionless inverse-power potential has the form V₂(r) = 2α² / (r + r₀ + 1/(Cα))², where α = ℏ/√(2m), C > 0 is an arbitrary real positive constant, and r₀ ≥ 0 is a positive number close to zero.
- The hierarchy of reflectionless inverse-power potentials has coefficients γ_n = 0, 2, 6, 12, 20, 30, 42... (integer numbers) in the expression V_n(r) = γ_n α² / (r + r₀)².
- The superpotential for the construction is W(r) = (2β - α)/f(r̄) - β/r̄, with f(r̄) = C(2β - α) r̄^(2β/α) + r̄ and r̄ = r + r₀; the condition β = α makes the first potential zero and reflectionless.
- The partial S-matrix components satisfy S_l^(1)(k) = -S_l^(2)(k) and δ_l^(1)(k) = δ_l^(2)(k) + π/2 for the two partner potentials.
- The radial probability flows for convergent and divergent waves in the reflectionless inverse-power potential are j^±(k,r) = ±(ℏk/m) |N̄₂|², independent of r.

## Context

This work extends the methods of supersymmetric quantum mechanics (Cooper 1995) and the formalism of Darboux transformations to radial scattering problems with continuous energy spectra, specifically building on the author's prior construction of one-dimensional reflectionless potentials (Maydanyuk 2005). It enables the construction of exactly solvable radial potentials that mimic realistic nuclear scattering and decay potentials (with a hole and a barrier) but are completely transparent to incident particles, which could be used as test models in nuclear reaction theory or to study the limits of invisibility in quantum scattering. The paper also connects to the inverse problem of quantum mechanics (Chadan 1977) and to shape-invariant potential methods (Gendenshtein 1983, Dutt 1986).
