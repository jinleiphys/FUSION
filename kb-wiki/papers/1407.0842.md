# The nucleon and the two solar mass neutron star

> The existence of a star with such a large mass means that the equation of state is stiff enough to provide a high enough pressure up to a fairly large central densities,. Such a stiff equation of state is possible if the ground state has nucleons as its constituents. This further implies that a purely nucleon ground state may exist till about four times nuclear density which indicates that quarks in the nucleon are strongly bound and that the nucleon nucleon potential is strongly repulsive. We find this to be so in a chiral soliton model for the nucleon which has bound state quarks. We point out that this has important implications for the strong interaction $ μ_B$ vs T phase diagram.

## Key claim

The existence of a two-solar-mass neutron star (PSR J1614-2230) implies that a purely nucleon ground state may persist up to about four times nuclear density, provided the nucleon-nucleon potential is strongly repulsive and quarks are strongly bound inside nucleons; the authors find this to be the case in a chiral soliton model, but note that the central density of a rotating 1.97 solar mass star is approximately that of a static 1.8 solar mass star, which is very close to the density where a Maxwell phase transition to quark matter would begin.

## Method

The authors use a mean-field chiral soliton model with constituent quarks coupled to sigma and pion fields, fitting the quark-meson coupling to the nucleon mass (960 MeV) and the sigma mass to pi-pi scattering (tree level ~800 MeV). They perform a Maxwell construction between the APR98 nuclear equation of state (A18+dv+UIX) and a three-flavor pion-condensed quark matter phase, plotting energy per baryon versus inverse baryon density to find the phase transition density. They then compute the binding energy of a quark in the nucleon soliton by comparing the energy of a three-quark bound state to a two-quark bound state plus an unbound quark, and estimate the density at which quarks become unbound from the soliton radius.

## Key numbers

- The central density of a static 1.8 solar mass APR star is n_B ~ 0.62/fm^3, which is very close to the initial density of the Maxwell phase transition (n_B ~ 0.57/fm^3) for sigma mass ~850 MeV.
- The binding energy of a quark in the nucleon is ~745 MeV, obtained as the difference between the energy of a two-quark bound state plus an unbound quark (1707 MeV) and the three-quark bound state nucleon (960 MeV).
- Quarks become unbound when the soliton radius R ~ 0.6 fm, corresponding to a nucleon density of ~0.77/fm^3 (from 1/(6 R^3) assuming hexagonal close packing).
- For the tree-level sigma mass ~850 MeV, the Maxwell common tangent starts at 1/n_B ~ 1.75 fm^3 (n_B ~ 0.57/fm^3) in the nuclear phase and ends at 1/n_B ~ 1.25 fm^3 (n_B ~ 0.8/fm^3) in the quark matter phase.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Soni and Bhattacharya (2011) on the Maxwell construction between nuclear and quark matter, and directly addresses the constraint from the discovery of the ~2 solar mass neutron star PSR J1614-2230 by Shapiro delay (Demorest et al. 2010). The authors argue that the internal structure of the nucleon, specifically the high quark binding energy and strong nucleon-nucleon repulsion found in chiral soliton models, can push the nuclear-to-quark phase transition to higher densities than the Maxwell construction predicts, enabling purely nucleon stars up to two solar masses. This scenario contradicts conventional hybrid star models with soft quark matter cores, which typically give maximum masses of only ~1.6 solar masses (Lattimer and Prakash, Soni and Bhattacharya 2011), and suggests that at finite temperature and small baryon density, a mixed phase of nucleon bound states in a chirally restored sea may appear, opposite to the bag model picture.
