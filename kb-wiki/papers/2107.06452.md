# Nucleon-nucleon potentials from Delta-full chiral effective-field-theory and implications

> We closely investigate NN potentials based upon the Delta-full version of chiral effective field theory. We find that recently constructed NN potentials of this kind, which (when applied together with three-nucleon forces) were presented as predicting accurate binding energies and radii for a range of nuclei from A=16 to A=132 and providing accurate equations of state for nuclear matter, yield a chi^2/datum of 60 for the reproduction of the pp data below 100 MeV laboratory energy. This chi^2 is more than three times what the Hamada-Johnston potential of the year of 1962 achieved already some 60 years ago. We perceive this historical fact as concerning in view of the current emphasis on precision. We are able to trace the very large chi^2 as well as the apparent success of the potentials in nuclear structure to unrealistic predictions for P-wave states, in which the Delta-full NNLO potentials are off by up to 40 times the NNLO truncation errors. In fact, we show that, the worse the description of the P-wave states, the better the predictions in nuclear structure. Thus, these potentials cannot be seen as the solution to the outstanding problems in current miscroscopic nuclear structure physics.

## Key claim

The Delta-full NNLO potentials recently constructed by the Goteberg-Oak Ridge group, which were presented as predicting accurate binding energies and radii for nuclei from A=16 to A=132 and accurate nuclear matter equations of state, yield a chi-squared per datum of 60 for pp data below 100 MeV laboratory energy, more than three times worse than the Hamada-Johnston potential from 1962, and the apparent success in nuclear structure is traced to unrealistic P-wave predictions that are off by up to 40 times the NNLO truncation errors.

## Method

The authors computed chi-squared per datum for the Goteberg-Oak Ridge DeltaNNLO potentials against the 2016 NN database for pp and np scattering in energy bins 0-100 MeV and 0-200 MeV, and compared these to the Hamada-Johnston potential and to their own refitted DeltaNNLO potentials. They also calculated nuclear matter energy per nucleon as a function of density using the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (particle-particle ladder) approximation, and compared phase shifts for P-wave states below 100 MeV against the Nijmegen and Granada phase-shift analyses.

## Key numbers

- chi-squared/datum for pp data 0-100 MeV: DeltaNNLO(450)_GO = 60.7, DeltaNNLO(394)_GO = 34.3, Hamada-Johnston (1962) = 19.6, DeltaNNLO(450)_Rf = 2.07, DeltaNNLO(394)_Rf = 1.87
- chi-squared/datum for np data 0-100 MeV: DeltaNNLO(450)_GO = 5.87, DeltaNNLO(394)_GO = 8.58, DeltaNNLO(450)_Rf = 1.27, DeltaNNLO(394)_Rf = 1.20
- chi-squared/datum for pp plus np 0-100 MeV: DeltaNNLO(450)_GO = 28.8, DeltaNNLO(394)_GO = 19.3, DeltaNNLO(450)_Rf = 1.59, DeltaNNLO(394)_Rf = 1.47
- chi-squared/datum for pp plus np 0-200 MeV: DeltaNNLO(450)_GO = 29.6, DeltaNNLO(394)_GO = 32.6, DeltaNNLO(450)_Rf = 3.71, DeltaNNLO(394)_Rf = 10.1
- P-wave phase shifts from GO potentials are off by up to 40 times the NNLO truncation errors
- Nuclear matter energy per nucleon from GO potentials lies on the Magic 1.8/2.0(EM) curve, while refitted potentials produce less attraction similar to the 2NFs used in Hop19

## Context

This work extends the critique of Delta-full chiral EFT potentials developed by the Goteberg-Oak Ridge group (Eks18, Jia20), which claimed simultaneous accurate predictions for medium-mass nuclei and nuclear matter. The authors show that the GO potentials achieve their nuclear structure success only because of unrealistic P-wave predictions, and that properly refitted DeltaNNLO potentials (consistent with truncation errors) produce nuclear matter underbinding similar to the underbinding seen in Hop19 for intermediate-mass nuclei. This contradicts the claim that Delta-full NNLO potentials solve the outstanding problems in microscopic nuclear structure physics, and instead suggests that the Magic 1.8/2.0(EM) potential (Heb11) achieves its success through a different mechanism related to softness and non-locality.
