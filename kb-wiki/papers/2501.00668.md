# General features of the stellar matter equation of state from microscopic theory, new maximum-mass constraints, and causality

> The profile of a neutron star probes a very large range of densities, from the density of iron up to several times the density of saturated nuclear matter, and thus no theory of hadrons can be considered reliable if extended to those regions. We emphasize the importance of taking contemporary ab initio theories of nuclear and neutron matter as the baseline for any extension method, which will unavoidably involve some degree of phenomenology. We discuss how microscopic theory, on the one end, with causality and maximum-mass constraints, on the other, set strong boundaries to the high-density equation of state. We present our latest neutron star predictions where we combine polytropic extensions and parametrizations guided by speed of sound considerations. The predictions we show include our baseline neutron star cooling curves.

## Key claim
Microscopic chiral EFT predictions at normal densities are soft, so the high-density equation of state must first be extended with a stiff polytrope to support current maximum-mass constraints (at least 2.2 solar masses), then followed by a softer segment or a causality-preserving speed-of-sound parametrization to avoid violating causality at central densities.

## Method
The authors construct the equation of state (EoS) for beta-stable matter using chiral EFT at N2LO and N3LO, including three-nucleon forces. Above the density where chiral EFT is reliable (up to 0.277 fm^-3), they extend the EoS with piecewise polytropes and with a parametrization of the speed of sound that enforces causality by construction. They then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius relations and perform neutron star cooling simulations using the NSCool package with two different envelope models.

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass constraint from PSR J0952-0607: 2.35 ± 0.17 solar masses (Romani et al. 2022)
- Lower limit for J0740+6620 pulsar: 2.08 ± 0.07 solar masses, with radius estimate 12.35 ± 0.75 km (Miller et al. 2021)
- For the N3LO EoS with Gamma = 3.8: maximum mass 2.49 solar masses, radius at maximum mass 11.31 km, central density 0.88 fm^-3, radius of 1.4 solar mass star 12.30 km
- For the N2LO EoS with Gamma = 3.8: maximum mass 2.42 solar masses, radius at maximum mass 10.99 km, central density 0.94 fm^-3, radius of 1.4 solar mass star 12.09 km
- For the N3LO EoS with Gamma = 3.3: maximum mass 2.26 solar masses, radius at maximum mass 10.70 km, central density 1.01 fm^-3, radius of 1.4 solar mass star 12.11 km
- For the N2LO EoS with Gamma = 3.3: maximum mass 2.18 solar masses, radius at maximum mass 10.34 km, central density 1.12 fm^-3, radius of 1.4 solar mass star 11.84 km
- Matching densities for the combined polytrope-plus-speed-of-sound extension: 0.277 fm^-3 and 0.563 fm^-3
- Chiral expansion parameter Q at the first matching density: 51 percent

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous chiral EFT-based EoS studies (Sammarruca and Millerson 2021a, 2021b, 2022) by incorporating the higher maximum-mass constraint from PSR J0952-0607 (Romani et al. 2022) and comparing with Bayesian analyses such as Huth et al. (2022) that use phenomenological density functionals. The cooling simulations provide baseline results for comparison with observational data from thermally emitting isolated neutron stars (Potekhin et al. 2020), and the authors plan a future comprehensive analysis including superfluid gaps and medium effects. The work also contrasts with relativistic mean-field models (Kumar 2023) that lack constraints from free-space few-nucleon data.
