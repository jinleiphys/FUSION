# Digital quantum simulations of scattering in quantum field theories using W states

> High-energy particle collisions can convert energy into matter through the inelastic production of new particles. Quantum computers are an ideal platform for simulating the out-of-equilibrium dynamics of collisions and the formation of subsequent many-particle states. In this work, evidence for inelastic particle production is observed in one-dimensional Ising field theory using IBM's quantum computers. The scattering experiment is performed on 104 qubits of ibm_marrakesh and uses up to 5,589 two-qubit gates to access the post-collision dynamics. An outgoing heavy particle produced in the collision is identified from the skewness of the measured energy density. Integral to this computation is a new quantum algorithm for preparing the initial state (wavepackets) of a quantum field theory scattering simulation. This method efficiently prepares wavepackets by extending recent protocols for creating W states with mid-circuit measurement and feedforward. The required circuit depth is independent of wavepacket size and spatial dimension, representing a superexponential improvement over previous methods. Our wavepacket preparation algorithm can be applied to a wide range of lattice models and is demonstrated in one-dimensional Ising field theory, scalar field theory, the Schwinger model and two-dimensional Ising field theory.

## Key claim

Evidence for inelastic particle production is observed in one-dimensional Ising field theory using IBM's quantum computers, but only at simulation times up to t=24.75 due to cumulative device noise, and the identification of the heavy particle relies on comparing skewness of the post-collision energy density to that of single-particle propagation to isolate inelastic effects from systematic errors.

## Method

The authors simulate scattering of two single-particle wavepackets in one-dimensional Ising field theory on 104 qubits of IBM's ibm_marrakesh quantum processor. They prepare the initial wavepacket states using a two-step algorithm: first initializing a W-like state |W(k0)⟩ with a constant-depth circuit, then applying symmetry-preserving energy minimization circuits (determined via ADAPT-VQE on a classical MPS simulator) to project onto single-particle eigenstates. Time evolution is performed with second-order Trotterization using up to 45 Trotter steps (5,589 two-qubit gates), and the vacuum-subtracted energy density is measured to track particle trajectories.

## Key numbers

- Simulation performed on 104 qubits of ibm_marrakesh using up to 5,589 two-qubit gates
- Two-qubit gate depth of 130 for the latest simulation time
- Trotter step size of δt=0.55 used to balance Trotter error and circuit depth
- Wavepackets have momentum k0=0.32π and momentum spread σ=0.13, supported on d≳21 sites
- Total energy Etot/Ethr=1.2, where the inelastic threshold Ethr=m1+m2=4.57
- Light particle mass m1=1.59 and heavy particle mass m2=2.98 in lattice units
- Hamiltonian parameters: gx=1.25, gz=0.15
- Simulation times: t0=0, t1=8.25, t2=16.5, t3=24.75
- The skewness of the post-collision state is 0.6σ larger than the single-particle state at t2, and 1σ larger at t3

## Context

This work extends classical MPS simulations of scattering in Ising field theory by Jha (2024), which demonstrated elastic scattering, scattering near resonances, and inelastic particle production. The quantum simulation approach enables access to regimes with higher entanglement that challenge classical methods. The wavepacket preparation algorithm improves upon prior methods by Zemlevskiy (2024) and Farrell (2024) that incurred exponentially-scaling classical overhead, and methods by Davoudi (2024) and Chai (2023) requiring polynomial circuit depth, by achieving circuit depth independent of wavepacket size. The results demonstrate a path toward near-term quantum advantage in simulations of scattering, though current hardware noise limits simulation times to before fully asymptotic particles form.
