# Effective Field Theory for Bound State Reflection

> Elastic quantum bound-state reflection from a hard-wall boundary provides direct information regarding the structure and compressibility of quantum bound states. We discuss elastic quantum bound-state reflection and derive a general theory for elastic reflection of shallow dimers from hard-wall surfaces using effective field theory. We show that there is a small expansion parameter for analytic calculations of the reflection scattering length. We present a calculation up to second order in the effective Hamiltonian in one, two, and three dimensions. We also provide numerical lattice results for all three cases as a comparison with our effective field theory results. Finally, we provide an analysis of the compressibility of the alpha particle confined to a cubic lattice with vanishing Dirichlet boundaries.

## Key claim

Elastic reflection of shallow two-body bound states from a hard-wall boundary can be described analytically using effective field theory, with an expansion parameter of \(e^{-2\kappa_B a_R}\) that controls convergence of the reflection scattering length calculation up to second order in the effective Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors derive an effective Hamiltonian for the center-of-mass motion of a shallow dimer scattering from a hard-wall boundary using an adiabatic approximation, treating the relative coordinate wavefunction as the ground state of the internal Hamiltonian with Dirichlet boundary conditions. They construct asymptotic expansions in powers of \(e^{-\kappa_B r_+(X)}\) and \(e^{-\kappa_B r_-(X)}\) using a method-of-images ansatz with generalized \(d\)-dimensional Yukawa functions, then compute the effective potential and diagonal adiabatic correction to first and second order in one, two, and three spatial dimensions for arbitrary mass ratio \(m_2/m_1\). Numerical lattice results are provided for comparison with the effective field theory calculations in all three dimensional cases.

## Key numbers

- The expansion parameter controlling convergence is \(e^{-2\kappa_B a_R}\), where \(\kappa_B\) is the binding momentum and \(a_R\) is the reflection scattering length.
- In one dimension at first order, the effective potential is \(V^{(1)}(X)+T^{(1)}(X) = \frac{\kappa_B^2 M^2}{m_1 m_2}\left[\frac{e^{-\kappa_B r_+(X)}}{m_1}+\frac{e^{-\kappa_B r_-(X)}}{m_2}\right]\).
- In three dimensions at first order, the effective potential is \(T^{(1)}(X)+V^{(1)}(X) = \frac{\kappa_B M}{2 m_1 m_2 X}\left[e^{-\kappa_B r_+(X)}+e^{-\kappa_B r_-(X)}\right]\).
- The effective potential crosses zero at \(X\kappa_B \sim 0.5\) for mass ratio \(m_2/m_1 = 4\) in one, two, and three dimensions, with good convergence for \(X\kappa_B \gtrsim 0.5\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous letter publication (Lee 2010) by providing full details of the effective field theory calculations for dimer-wall reflection. The method complements Luscher's periodic boundary analysis (Luscher 1985, 1986) for bound states and scattering states, and is motivated by recent lattice effective field theory calculations of carbon-12 (Epelbaum 2011) that indicated compressed alpha clusters. The results enable extraction of nuclear radii and quantities analogous to bulk and shear modulus from hard-wall boundary calculations, and provide a universal result for Wannier exciton energies in direct-band gap semiconductors as a function of binding energy, effective masses, and nanostructure geometry.
