# Interplay between the weak-coupling results and the lattice data in dense QCD

> We discuss the interplay between two first-principles calculations of QCD at high density: perturbative results in the weak-coupling regime and the recent lattice-QCD result at finite isospin density. By comparing these two results, we verify empirically that the weak-coupling calculations of the bulk thermodynamics and the gap parameter for Cooper pairing between quarks can be applicable down to the quark chemical potential $μ\sim 1$ GeV. Having verified the validity of the weak-coupling results in QCD at finite isospin density, we discuss possible effects on QCD at finite baryon density, which is relevant for the application to realistic environments such as neutron stars, by using the fact that QCD at finite baryon and isospin density have the common weak-coupling expansions. First, we show the size of the color-superconducting gap at finite baryon density is as small as a few MeV at $μ= 1$ GeV, which implies that the color-flavor locked phase may be unstable against unpairing up to $μ\sim 1.4$ GeV even in the weak-coupling regime. We also introduce a prescription to reduce the ambiguity arising from the undetermined renormalization scale in the weak-coupling calculation by matching with the lattice-QCD data. We demonstrate the effect of such reduction on neutron-star phenomenology by performing the Bayesian analysis.

# Interplay between the weak-coupling results and the lattice data in dense QCD

## Key claim

The weak-coupling perturbative QCD calculations of bulk thermodynamics and the Cooper pairing gap parameter are empirically valid down to quark chemical potential μ ∼ 0.8 GeV, despite the longstanding belief that such calculations are only reliable at much higher chemical potentials where the coupling constant g < 1.

## Method

The author compares the weak-coupling perturbative QCD pressure (including the Cooper pairing condensation energy) to recent lattice QCD results at finite isospin density in the continuum limit, using the 5-loop running coupling constant in the MSbar scheme with Λ_MSbar ≈ 338 MeV. By performing a nonlinear least-squares fit of the weak-coupling pressure to the upper bound of the lattice data in the intermediate window μ ∈ [1.0, 1.4] GeV, the author determines an optimal range for the renormalization scale parameter X = Λbar/(2μ) ∈ [1.22, 2.0], replacing the conventional ad-hoc range X ∈ [0.5, 2.0]. This lattice-informed scale is then applied to QCD at finite baryon density using the universality of weak-coupling expansions between the two theories.

## Key numbers

- The weak-coupling formulas are applicable down to μ = 0.8 GeV, corresponding to baryon chemical potential μ_B = 2.4 GeV
- The color-superconducting gap in the color-flavor locked phase at μ = 0.8 GeV is Δ_CFL ≃ 0.7–1.5 MeV
- The CFL phase may be unstable against unpairing up to μ ∼ 1.4–2.4 GeV (μ_B ∼ 4–7 GeV) because the gap is smaller than the stress from the strange quark mass m_s(2 GeV) = 93.4 MeV
- The condensation energy contribution δP/P_id is less than ∼ 0.01% at μ = 0.8 GeV
- The relative range of P/P_id shrinks from ±31% (conventional scale) to ±4.2% (lattice-informed scale)
- The Bayes factor for positive trace anomaly increases from 3.53 (conventional) to 20.3 (lattice-informed), giving strong evidence for a soft equation of state in neutron star cores

## Context

This work extends the comparison between perturbative QCD and lattice QCD at finite isospin density, building on the lattice calculations by Abbott et al. (2023, 2024) that determined the equation of state in the continuum limit up to μ ∼ 1.6 GeV. The author uses the QCD inequality approach of Cohen (2003) and the bound derived by Fujimoto (2023) and Abbott et al. (2024) to relate isospin and baryon density results. The Bayesian analysis follows the constant-likelihood approach of Altiparmak et al. (2022) and Jiang et al. (2022). This work enables more precise constraints on neutron star equations of state from perturbative QCD inputs, and suggests that the color-flavor locked phase may not be the ground state at densities relevant for neutron star cores, contrary to some earlier expectations.
