# Torqued fireballs in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We show that the fluctuations in the wounded-nucleon model of the initial stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, together with the natural assumption that the forward (backward) moving wounded nucleons emit particles preferably in the forward (backward) direction, lead to an event-by-event torqued fireball. The principal axes associated with the transverse shape are rotated in the forward region in the opposite direction than in the backward region. On the average, the standard deviation of the relative torque angle between the forward and backward rapidity regions is about 20deg for the central and 10deg for the mid-peripheral collisions. The hydrodynamic expansion of a torqued fireball leads to a torqued collective flow, yielding, in turn, torqued principal axes of the transverse-momentum distributions at different rapidities. We propose experimental measures, based on cumulants involving particles in different rapidity regions, which should allow for a quantitative determination of the effect from the data. To estimate the non-flow contributions from resonance decays we run Monte Carlo simulations with THERMINATOR. If the event-by-event torque effect is found in the data, it will support the assumptions concerning the fluctuations in the early stage of the fireball formation, as well as the hypothesis of the asymmetric rapidity shape of the emission functions of the moving sources in the nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that event-by-event fluctuations in the wounded-nucleon model, combined with the assumption that forward-moving wounded nucleons emit particles preferentially forward and backward-moving ones preferentially backward, produce a torqued fireball where the principal axes of the transverse shape rotate in opposite directions in forward versus backward rapidity regions; the effect is purely statistical and vanishes on average, so it can only be observed through event-by-event fluctuation studies.

## Method

The authors use the wounded-nucleon model with an admixture of binary collisions (mixed model, α=0.145) implemented in the GLISSANDO Monte Carlo code for Au+Au collisions at √sNN=200 GeV. They introduce asymmetric rapidity-dependent emission functions for forward and backward moving wounded nucleons, then compute the event-by-event distribution of the difference between principal axis angles at forward and backward space-time rapidities. They also perform 3+1 dimensional perfect-fluid hydrodynamic evolution of a torqued initial condition and simulate the final hadronization stage with THERMINATOR to estimate non-flow contributions from resonance decays.

## Key numbers

- Standard deviation of the relative torque angle between forward and backward rapidity regions: about 20° for central collisions and 10° for mid-peripheral collisions (from the abstract)
- For centrality 20-30%, rms width of ΔFB distribution: 2° at η∥=0.5 and 7° at η∥=2.5 (for elliptic deformation)
- For centrality 0-10%, rms width of ΔFB distribution: 4° at η∥=0.5 and 16° at η∥=2.5 (for elliptic deformation)
- For centrality 20-30% and rank-3 (triangular) case, rms width: 4° at η∥=0.5 and 16° at η∥=2.5
- Mixed model parameter α=0.145, corresponding to collisions at √sNN=200 GeV
- Hydrodynamic evolution initial proper time τ0=0.25 fm/c
- Freeze-out temperature: 150 MeV
- Impact parameter for hydrodynamic demonstration: b=6.6 fm (centrality 20-25%)
- Input torque angle for hydrodynamic test: 5° (one-sided rotation), yielding forward-backward difference of approximately 8° at freeze-out

## Context

This work extends the wounded-nucleon approach of Bialas and Czyz (2004) and the tilted fireball model of Bozek (2010) that successfully reproduced directed flow at RHIC. It builds on earlier studies of event-by-event fluctuations in the initial state that explained enhanced elliptic flow (Aguiar 2001) and triangular flow (Alver 2010). The paper proposes experimental measures based on cumulants, similar to flow analysis methods of Ollitrault (1997) and Borghini (2000), that could allow observation of the torque effect in existing RHIC data, which would support the asymmetric rapidity emission hypothesis and the fluctuation picture of the early fireball stage.
