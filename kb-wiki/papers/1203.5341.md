# Strong QCD and Dyson-Schwinger Equations

> The real-world properties of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) - the strongly-interacting piece of the Standard Model - are dominated by two emergent phenomena: confinement; namely, the theory's elementary degrees-of-freedom - quarks and gluons - have never been detected in isolation; and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), which is a remarkably effective mass generating mechanism, responsible for the mass of more than 98% of visible matter in the Universe. These phenomena are not apparent in the formulae that define QCD, yet they play a principal role in determining Nature's observable characteristics. Much remains to be learnt before confinement can properly be understood. On the other hand,the last decade has seen important progress in the use of relativistic quantum field theory, so that we can now explain the origin of DCSB and are beginning to demonstrate its far-reaching consequences. Dyson-Schwinger equations have played a critical role in these advances. These lecture notes provide an introduction to Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs), QCD and hadron physics, and illustrate the use of DSEs to predict phenomena that are truly observable.

## Key claim

Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) provide the only extant continuum framework that can simultaneously connect both meson and baryon observables with the momentum-dependent dressed-quark mass function that is the keystone of dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB), though the lecture notes acknowledge that much remains to be learnt before confinement can properly be understood.

## Method

The author presents a Poincaré covariant Faddeev equation for baryons, founded on the observation that an interaction describing colour-singlet mesons also generates nonpointlike quark-quark (diquark) correlations in the colour-antitriplet channel. The dominant correlations for ground state octet and decuplet baryons are scalar (0+) and axial-vector (1+) diquarks, which are retained in approximating the quark-quark scattering matrix within the Faddeev equation. The approach specifies an interaction kernel for the gap equation, solves for the dressed-quark propagator, computes masses and amplitudes for diquark correlations via the Bethe-Salpeter kernel, then solves the Faddeev equation for baryon masses and amplitudes, and finally computes electromagnetic form factors using a current constrained by vector Ward-Takahashi identities.

## Key numbers

- The DSE study produces an excitation of the nucleon (Roper resonance) at 1.82 ± 0.07 GeV, described as predominantly a radial excitation of the quark-diquark system
- In the nucleon's rest frame, just 37% of the total spin of the nucleon is contained within components of the Faddeev amplitude which possess zero quark orbital angular momentum
- The computation simultaneously correlating meson and baryon ground- and excited-state masses produces an rms relative error per degree of freedom of 13%
- The nucleon's Faddeev amplitude describes a ground-state dominated by its scalar diquark component at 78%, with the axial-vector component significantly smaller
- The Roper resonance is constituted almost entirely (81%) from the axial-vector diquark correlation
- For the ratio d_v/u_v at x=1, the momentum-dependent mass function yields 0.28 (F2^n/F2^p = 0.49), while the constant mass function yields 0.18 (F2^n/F2^p = 0.41)
- Both results are consistent with the experimentally inferred d_v/u_v = 0.23 ± 0.09 (90% confidence level, F2^n/F2^p = 0.45 ± 0.08)

## Context

These lecture notes extend the DSE approach to baryons, building on prior work that used DSEs to study meson properties and the momentum-dependent dressed-quark mass function. The author shows that DSE-computed baryon masses agree with the bare masses employed in modern coupled-channels models of pion-nucleon reactions by Suzuki (2009) and Gasparyan (2003), and that the Roper resonance is the proton's first radial excitation, with its mass made lighter by a dense cloud of pions and other mesons. The work enables future experiments at JLab in the 12 GeV era to establish conclusively whether the ratio G_E^p/G_M^p possesses a zero, which would assist in refining understanding of the dressed-quark mass function and QCD's beta-function.
