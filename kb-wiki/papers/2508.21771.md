# Conflation of Ensemble-Learned Nuclear Mass Models for Enhanced Precision

> Ensemble learning algorithms, the gradient boosting and bagging regressors, are employed to correct the residuals of nuclear mass excess for a diverse set of six nuclear mass models. The weighted average of these corrected residuals reduces due to their partial cancellation, yielding a significant improvement in nuclear mass predictions. Our conflated model, which integrates ensemble learning and model averaging (ELMA), achieves a root mean square error of approximately 65 keV, well below the critical threshold of 100 keV, for the complete data set of Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020). The validity of ELMA is demonstrated through the evaluation of $Q$ values for $α$ decay, showing a marked decrease in deviations from experimental data relative to predictions from individual nuclear mass models. We have also compiled a table of nuclear mass excesses and binding energies for about 6,300 nuclei, which serves as a valuable resource for various nuclear physics applications and is publicly accessible via the ELMA web interface (https://ddnp.in).

# Conflation of Ensemble-Learned Nuclear Mass Models for Enhanced Precision

## Key claim
The authors claim that combining gradient boosting and bagging regressors to correct residuals from six different nuclear mass models, then taking a weighted average of the corrected residuals, yields a conflated model (ELMA) with a root mean square error of approximately 65 keV for the complete AME2020 dataset. This result depends on the specific choice of 12 physics-informed features, the particular hyperparameter settings for each machine learning model, and the weighted averaging procedure where weights are determined from test-set performance.

## Method
The authors applied Gradient Boosting Regressor (GBR) and Bootstrap Aggregation (BAR, using Support Vector Regression as the base estimator) to correct the residuals of six nuclear mass models: WS4, WS4+, FRDM, DZ(28), UNEDF1, and RMF. They trained these machine learning models on 2,028 nuclei from AME2016 using 12 physics-informed features (including proton number, neutron number, mass number, isospin asymmetry, even-odd indicators, magic gap distances, promiscuity factor, and shell numbers). The corrected residuals from all 12 machine-learning-corrected models (six for GBR, six for BAR) were then combined via a weighted average, with weights computed from test-set performance, to produce the final ELMA predictions.

## Key numbers
- The conflated ELMA model achieves a root mean square error of approximately 65 keV (0.065 MeV) for the complete AME2020 dataset of 2,457 nuclei, below the critical threshold of 100 keV.
- The training set consisted of 2,028 nuclei from AME2016; the test set contained 358 nuclei from AME2016; the extrapolation set contained 71 nuclei newly added in AME2020; a new-data set contained 31 nuclei measured after AME2020; the full dataset comprised 2,488 nuclei.
- GBR-corrected WS4+ achieved a test RMSE of 0.1216 MeV; BAR-corrected WS4+ achieved a test RMSE of 0.1165 MeV.
- Raw residuals for the six models ranged from 0.1840 MeV (WS4+, training set) to 2.0756 MeV (RMF, training set).
- The authors compiled a table of nuclear mass excesses and binding energies for approximately 6,300 nuclei, publicly accessible via the ELMA web interface.

## Context
This work extends the approach of using machine learning to correct nuclear mass model residuals, building on prior studies such as Yuksel (2024) that employed physics-informed features for mass modeling. The ELMA method improves upon individual mass models (WS4, WS4+, FRDM, DZ(28), UNEDF1, RMF) by combining ensemble learning corrections with model averaging, achieving accuracy below the 100 keV threshold often considered necessary for reliable nuclear astrophysics applications. The publicly available table of mass excesses and binding energies for about 6,300 nuclei enables further studies in nuclear structure, astrophysical nucleosynthesis, and related fields, while the demonstrated improvement in alpha-decay Q-values validates the method for practical nuclear physics applications.
