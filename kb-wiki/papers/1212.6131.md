# Numerical modeling of core-collapse supernovae and compact objects

> Massive stars (M> 10Msun) end their lives with spectacular explosions due to gravitational collapse. The collapse turns the stars into compact objects such as neutron stars and black holes with the ejection of cosmic rays and heavy elements. Despite the importance of these astrophysical events, the mechanism of supernova explosions has been an unsolved issue in astrophysics. This is because clarification of the supernova dynamics requires the full knowledge of nuclear and neutrino physics at extreme conditions, and large-scale numerical simulations of neutrino radiation hydrodynamics in multi-dimensions. This article is a brief overview of the understanding (with difficulty) of the supernova mechanism through the recent advance of numerical modeling at supercomputing facilities. Numerical studies with the progress of nuclear physics are applied to follow the evolution of compact objects with neutrino emissions in order to reveal the birth of pulsars/black holes from the massive stars.

## Key claim
Despite decades of effort, reproducing supernova explosions in numerical modeling remains difficult; the paper argues that the mechanism is still an unsolved issue because it requires full knowledge of nuclear and neutrino physics at extreme conditions and large-scale multi-dimensional simulations of neutrino radiation hydrodynamics, with the hedge that even state-of-the-art 2D simulations produce explosions only with approximate neutrino transfer and that 3D results are still premature.

## Method
The paper reviews numerical modeling of core-collapse supernovae using neutrino radiation hydrodynamics in one, two, and three dimensions, incorporating nuclear equations of state (EOS) and neutrino reaction rates. It describes simulations that follow the collapse of a massive star's iron core, core bounce, shock wave propagation, and the delayed neutrino heating mechanism, with particular attention to multi-dimensional hydrodynamic instabilities such as convection and standing accretion shock instabilities (SASI). The author also discusses the use of supercomputing facilities, including the K-computer, to solve the 6D Boltzmann equation for neutrino distributions in 3D.

## Key numbers
- Gravitational energy release from core collapse: ~10^53 erg, of which ~10^51 erg (about 1%) goes into the explosion energy (kinetic, internal, and potential).
- The initial shock wave energy after core bounce: several 10^51 erg, but energy loss from iron dissociation amounts to minus several 10^51 erg, causing the shock to stall.
- Neutrino heating energy behind the stalled shock: several 10^51 erg, comparable to the other energy factors.
- Massive stars of ~20 solar masses evolve for ~10^7 years, with the core collapse to explosion occurring in ~1 second, followed by proto-neutron star cooling via neutrino emission for ~20 seconds, then further cooling as a cold neutron star for ~10^2 to 10^5 years.
- The observation of a massive neutron star of ~2 solar masses (Demorest 2010) provides a strong constraint on EOS sets for simulations.
- Pulsar kick velocities from parameterized explosion simulations: ~300 km/s; spin periods: ~600 ms (Wongwathanarat 2010).

## Context
This review extends the line of work on core-collapse supernova modeling that began with Bethe (1990) and the delayed explosion mechanism proposed by Bethe and Wilson (1985). It builds on prior numerical studies in 1D by Rampp et al. (2000), Liebendoerfer et al. (2001), and Sumiyoshi et al. (2005), which found no explosions for typical progenitors, and on 2D simulations that have produced a dozen explosion cases (Kotake 2012b). The paper contrasts with earlier EOS tables by Lattimer and Swesty (1991) and Shen et al. (1998a, 1998b, 2011), and discusses recent extensions to include hyperons and quarks (Ishizuka et al. 2008, Sagert et al. 2009) and nuclear mixtures at low densities (Hempel et al. 2012). The work enables future full 3D simulations of neutrino radiation hydrodynamics on exascale supercomputers, which are needed to determine the dominant explosion mechanism and to connect progenitor properties to neutron star birth.
