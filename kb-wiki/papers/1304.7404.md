# Separation of proton polarizabilities with the beam asymmetry of Compton scattering

> We propose to determine the magnetic dipole polarizability of the proton directly from the beam asymmetry of low-energy Compton scattering based on the fact that the leading non-Born contribution to the asymmetry is due to the magnetic polarizability alone; the electric polarizability cancels out. The beam asymmetry thus provides the simplest observable with a clean separation of the magnetic polarizability from the electric one. Introducing polarizabilities in a Lorentz-invariant fashion we compute the higher-order (recoil) effects of polarizabilities on beam asymmetry and show that these effects are most suppressed in forward kinematics. With the prospects of precision Compton experiments at the MAMI and HIGS facilities in mind, we argue why the beam asymmetry could be the best way to measure the elusive magnetic polarizability of the proton.

## Key claim

The beam asymmetry Σ₃ of low-energy Compton scattering can be used to extract the magnetic dipole polarizability β_M1 of the proton independently of the electric polarizability α_E1, because the leading non-Born contribution of α_E1 cancels out in this observable. However, this clean separation holds only at leading order in the low-energy expansion; at the energies of planned experiments (80–150 MeV), higher-order corrections become substantial and must be accounted for, though they are suppressed in forward kinematics.

## Method

The authors compute the beam asymmetry Σ₃ = (dσ_|| − dσ_⊥)/(dσ_|| + dσ_⊥) for proton Compton scattering using a low-energy expansion (LEX) and a Lorentz-invariant effective Lagrangian that introduces polarizabilities. They compare the leading-order LEX result to next-next-to-leading order (NNLO) baryon chiral perturbation theory (BχPT) calculations from Lensky (2009) for photon energies of 100 MeV and 135 MeV at various scattering angles. The higher-order (recoil) effects of polarizabilities on the asymmetry are computed analytically up to O(ω⁴), including contributions from the anomalous magnetic moment and spin polarizabilities.

## Key numbers

- PDG values: α_E1 = (12.0 ± 0.6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β_M1 = (1.9 ± 0.5) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
- The central value of the BχPT calculation for β_M1 is a factor of 2 larger than the PDG value
- Planned experiments at MAMI and HIGS are for photon-beam energies between 80 and 150 MeV
- At 100 MeV and forward angles (60°), the leading LEX result is in good agreement with the NNLO BχPT result
- At 135 MeV, the leading LEX result does not apply; differences between LEX and BχPT are mainly due to α_E1 (about 11 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ in BχPT) and the π⁰-anomaly contribution
- The conventional observable (cos²θ dσ_⊥ − dσ_||)/dΩ is small (a few nb/sr) compared to the unpolarized cross section (a few tens of nb/sr)
- The scattering angle should be chosen near 60 degrees to limit the impact of higher-order terms on the extraction of β_M1

## Context

This work addresses the significant disagreement between the PDG value of β_M1 and recent chiral effective field theory calculations by Lensky (2009) and McGovern (2012), where the BχPT central value is a factor of 2 larger than the PDG value. The authors argue that model dependence in extracting polarizabilities from unpolarized Compton scattering data is the likely source, analogous to the Rosenbluth separation problem for proton form factors where polarization measurements provided a breakthrough. The proposed beam asymmetry measurement, planned at MAMI and HIGS facilities, could enable a model-independent determination of β_M1 and, when combined with unpolarized cross sections, provide exclusive access to α_E1.
