# The Spin Orbit term in the Nuclear Shell Model

> Quasi-nuclear systems, representing nuclei with variable size, are studied to investigate the occurrence of the spin-orbit term in the nuclear mean field in the transition from infinite nuclear matter to finite nuclei. Relativistic as well as non-relativistic mean field calculations based on models for the nucleon-nucleon ($NN$) interaction, which fit the $NN$ scattering data, are considered. A very strong correlation between the strength of the spin-orbit term and radius of the nuclear system is observed. The origin of the spin-orbit term is analyzed by inspecting the contributions of the different partial waves and various mesons in a One-Boson-Exchange model of the $NN$ interaction. The influence of correlation effects and the enhancement of the small component of Dirac spinors for nucleons in the nuclear medium is discussed.

## Key claim

The spin-orbit splitting in the nuclear mean field shows a very strong correlation with the radius of the nuclear system, disappearing for larger radii and appearing only for sufficient localization of the nuclear structure; this dependence is observed across both relativistic and non-relativistic approaches, though the abstract does not specify that the relativistic results are typically around 30 percent smaller than non-relativistic results for a given radius.

## Method

The study constructs quasi-nuclear systems for 16O and 40Ca using Woods-Saxon potentials with varying radius parameters, adjusting the depth to localize specific single-particle states at zero energy. Relativistic calculations use the Improved Local Density Approximation (ILDA) based on Dirac Brueckner Hartree Fock (DBHF) results from the Bonn potential, while non-relativistic calculations employ the low-momentum interaction Vlowk derived from the Bonn A potential via the Unitary-Model-Operator approach, supplemented by a zero-range three-nucleon force. The spin-orbit splitting is evaluated from single-particle energy differences and from expectation values of the Schrodinger-equivalent spin-orbit potential.

## Key numbers

- For quasi-nuclear 16O, the spin-orbit splitting in the p-shell (ΔE_rel = E_0p1/2 - E_0p3/2) varies strongly with the radius of the nucleon density distribution, disappearing at larger radii
- The effective range parameter for the ILDA folding is t = 1.3528 - 0.1322 A^(1/3) fm, depending on mass number A
- Experimental spin-orbit splittings (in MeV): 16O 0p-shell 6.30, 16O 0d-shell 5.08, 40Ca 0f-shell 5.64, 40Ca 1p-shell 1.67, 12C 0p-shell (2.83), 28Si 0d-shell (0.80), 56Ni 0f-shell (2.23)
- ILDA calculations yield (MeV): 16O 0p-shell 5.44, 16O 0d-shell 4.85, 40Ca 0f-shell 6.83, 40Ca 1p-shell 1.96, 12C 0p-shell 4.18, 28Si 0d-shell 5.85, 56Ni 0f-shell 6.79
- Non-relativistic HF+3N calculations yield (MeV): 16O 0p-shell 3.91, 16O 0d-shell 4.57, 40Ca 0f-shell 4.96, 40Ca 1p-shell 1.30, 12C 0p-shell -2.34, 28Si 0d-shell -0.69, 56Ni 0f-shell 0.11
- Relativistic results are typically around 30 percent smaller than corresponding non-relativistic results for a given radius of the nucleon distribution
- Ignoring contributions from partial waves with L=1 and S=1 (3P_J waves) in Vlowk reduces the spin-orbit splitting to values very close to zero

## Context

This work extends the study of the spin-orbit term from infinite nuclear matter to finite nuclei using quasi-nuclear systems, building on the shell model of Goeppert-Mayer (1949) and Haxel, Jensen and Suess (1949) who first incorporated the spin-orbit term to reproduce magic numbers. The relativistic approach follows the Walecka model (Walecka 1974, 1975) and the ILDA method of Sun et al. (2016), while the non-relativistic approach uses the Vlowk renormalization technique of Bogner et al. (2003) and chiral effective field theory interactions of Machleidt and Entem (2011). The results enable systematic comparison of relativistic and non-relativistic predictions for spin-orbit splitting across nuclear sizes, and show that for nuclei with closed subshells (12C, 28Si, 56Ni) the non-relativistic HF+3N approach yields negative spin-orbit splittings, indicating that deformed solutions or configuration mixing must be considered.
