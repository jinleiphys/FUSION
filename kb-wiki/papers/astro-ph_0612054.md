# Pulsars as Astrophysical Laboratories for Nuclear and Particle Physics

> A forefront area of research concerns the exploration of the properties of hadronic matter under extreme conditions of temperature and density, and the determination of the equation of state--the relation between pressure, temperature and density--of such matter. Experimentally, relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments enable physicists to cast a brief glance at hot and ultra-dense matter for times as little as about $10^{-22}$ seconds. Complementary to this, the matter that exists in the cores of neutron stars, observed as radio pulsars, X-ray pulsars, and magnetars, is at low temperatures but compressed permanently to ultra-high densities that may be more than an order of magnitude higher than the density of atomic nuclei. This makes pulsars superb astrophysical laboratories for medium and high-energy nuclear physics, as discussed in this paper.

## Key claim

Pulsars serve as permanent astrophysical laboratories for studying hadronic matter under extreme conditions, complementing terrestrial heavy-ion collision experiments that can only probe such matter for times as short as about \(10^{-22}\) seconds; the paper reviews how observations of pulsars can constrain the equation of state of matter at densities more than an order of magnitude higher than the density of atomic nuclei, but the actual constraints depend on the specific nuclear many-body model used and on whether exotic matter (such as hyperons, meson condensates, or quark matter) exists in neutron star cores.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models for the equation of state of neutron star matter, including Thomas-Fermi based models, Schroedinger-based many-body approaches, relativistic mean-field (RMF) and relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) treatments, the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model for quark matter, and other phenomenological quark models. These equations of state are used to compute neutron star mass-radius relationships and compositions, which are then compared to observational data on pulsar masses, rotational frequencies, and cooling behavior.

## Key numbers

- The baryon number density of normal nuclear matter is \(n_0 = 0.15\) nucleons/fm\(^3\), corresponding to a mass density of \(2.5 \times 10^{14}\) g/cm\(^3\).
- The fastest known neutron star, PSR J1748-2446ad, rotates at a period of 1.39 ms (719 Hz); PSR B1937+21 rotates at 1.58 ms (633 Hz); PSR B1957+20 rotates at 1.61 ms (621 Hz).
- The average neutron star mass derived from radio pulsar observations is \(1.350 \pm 0.004\, M_{\odot}\) (Thorsett and Chakrabarty, 1999).
- Observed high neutron star masses include \(M_{\rm J0751+1807} = 2.1 \pm 0.2\, M_{\odot}\), \(M_{\rm 4U\,1636+536} = 2.0 \pm 0.1\, M_{\odot}\), \(M_{\rm Vela\, X-1} = 1.86 \pm 0.16\, M_{\odot}\), and \(M_{\rm 4U\,1700-37} = 2.44 \pm 0.27\, M_{\odot}\).
- The maximum possible mass of a stable neutron star is theoretically estimated at \(\sim 3\, M_{\odot}\) (Rhoades and Ruffini, 1974).
- The \(K^-\) meson mass in nuclear matter at densities \(\sim 3\, n_0\) is estimated to be \(m^*_{K^-} \simeq 200\) MeV; the relation \(m^*_{K^-} / m_{K^-} \simeq 1 - 0.2\, n / n_0\) holds for neutron-rich matter, with vacuum mass \(m_K = 495\) MeV.
- The maximum mass of neutron stars with \(K^-\) condensation has been calculated to be around \(1.5\, M_{\odot}\) (Thorsson, Prakash, and Lattimer, 1994).
- The pentaquark \(\Theta^+\) has a predicted mass of 1540 MeV.
- The strange quark mass is around 150 MeV.
- Quark deconfinement could occur at densities between about \(2-10\, n_0\).

## Context

This review extends the long-standing line of work connecting neutron star observations to nuclear physics, building on foundational studies such as the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff calculations (Oppenheimer and Volkoff, 1939; Tolman, 1939) and subsequent work on hyperons (Glendenning, 1985), meson condensation (Kaplan and Nelson, 1986; Brown, 1987), and quark matter (Ivanenko and Kurdgelaidze, 1965; Itoh, 1970; Baym and Chin, 1976). The paper enables future work by identifying open questions, such as whether exotic matter (boson condensates, color-superconducting quark matter, H-dibaryons, pentaquarks) exists in pulsar cores, whether rotationally-driven phase transitions occur, and how gravitational-wave detectors may provide new observational constraints.
