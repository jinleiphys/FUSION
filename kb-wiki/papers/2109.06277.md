# Machine-learning-based identification for initial clustering structure in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> $α$-clustering structure is a significant topic in light nuclei. A Bayesian convolutional neural network (BCNN) is applied to classify initial non-clustered and clustered configurations, namely Woods-Saxon distribution and three-$α$ triangular (four-$α$ tetrahedral) structure for $^{12}$C ($^{16}$O), from heavy-ion collision events generated within a multi-phase transport (AMPT) model. Azimuthal angle and transverse momentum distributions of charged pions are taken as inputs to train the classifier. On multiple-event basis, the overall classification accuracy can reach $95\%$ for $^{12}$C/$^{16}$O + $^{197}$Au events at $\sqrt{S_{NN}} =$ 200 GeV. With proper constructions of samples, the predicted deviations on mixed samples with different proportions of both configurations could be within $5\%$. In addition, setting a simple confidence threshold can further improve the predictions on the mixed dataset. Our results indicate promising and extensive possibilities of application of machine-learning-based techniques to real data and some other problems in physics of heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

A Bayesian convolutional neural network (BCNN) can classify initial clustered versus non-clustered configurations of light nuclei in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, but only on a multiple-event basis (not event-by-event), because event-by-event fluctuations in such small systems prevent discrimination even though the overall classification accuracy can reach 95% or higher when enough events are merged.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT (a multi-phase transport) model to simulate central \(^{12}\)C/\(^{16}\)O + \(^{197}\)Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, initializing the light nuclei either with a Woods-Saxon distribution (non-clustered) or with a three-\(\alpha\) triangular (for \(^{12}\)C) or four-\(\alpha\) tetrahedral (for \(^{16}\)O) clustered configuration. They constructed 2D histograms of azimuthal angle and transverse momentum of charged pions (with rapidity from -1 to 1 and transverse momentum from 0 to 2 GeV/\(c\)) as inputs, and trained a Bayesian convolutional neural network (BCNN) with four convolutional layers and three Bayesian fully connected layers to classify the two configurations. Because event-by-event classification failed (accuracy just above 50%), events were merged after rotation to the third-order participant plane, with the number of merged events \(N_{Event}\) set to 1000, 2000, or 4000.

## Key numbers

* Overall classification accuracy reached 95% for \(^{12}\)C + \(^{197}\)Au at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV with \(N_{Event} = 4000\) merged events.
* Validation accuracy reached 97% for \(^{16}\)O + \(^{197}\)Au with \(N_{Event} = 2000\) and 99% for \(^{16}\)O + \(^{197}\)Au with \(N_{Event} = 4000\).
* On mixed datasets with different proportions of Woods-Saxon and clustered configurations (20%/80%, 40%/60%, 60%/40%, 80%/20%), the deviations between true proportions and final predictions were within 5% for \(^{12}\)C with \(N_{Event} = 4000\) and \(^{16}\)O with \(N_{Event} = 2000\).
* After applying a confidence threshold of 0.99, deviations were within 4% for all three best cases.
* The side length of the equilateral triangle for clustered \(^{12}\)C was set to 3.103 fm to match the measured rms charge radius of 2.47 fm; for \(^{16}\)O tetrahedral configuration the edge length was 3.416 fm.
* The network had approximately 0.58 million parameters and was trained on \(8 \times 10^5\) events per configuration, with \(4 \times 10^4\) samples per \(N_{Event}\) and configuration.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Zhang (2017), Zhang (2018), and He (2020) that investigated initial geometry effects on elliptic and triangular flows, HBT correlations, and electromagnetic fields in \(^{12}\)C + \(^{197}\)Au collisions, where differences among spherical, clustered triangle, and clustered chain structures were observed but mixture proportions could not be determined. The authors note that earlier probes proposed by Broniowski (2014) and Bozek (2014) for \(\alpha\)-clustering evidence in relativistic heavy-ion collisions were limited. This machine-learning approach enables quantitative estimation of the mixture of clustered and non-clustered configurations in light nuclei, and the authors suggest it could be applied to experimental data from RHIC-STAR and LHC-ALICE for small collision systems such as \(^{16}\)O + \(^{16}\)O.
