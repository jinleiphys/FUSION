# Influence of Cluster Configurations and Nucleon--Nucleon Scattering Cross-Section on Stopping Power in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We investigate the impacts of nuclear $α$-clustering structures and nucleon--nucleon cross-section on nuclear stopping power for ${}^{16}\text{O}$ + ${}^{40}\text{Ca}$ collisions below 300 MeV/nucleon using an extended quantum molecular dynamics (EQMD) model. Our results show that the specific $α$-clustering configurations of ${}^{16}\text{O}$--including chain, square, kite, and tetrahedron--have a significant effect on collision dynamics. Among them, the tightly bound tetrahedral structure exhibits the highest stopping power. Moreover, the repulsive Coulomb interaction is found to reduce the stopping power of protons in the Fermi-energy domain. At higher energies, the decreasing trend is influenced by both the nucleon--nucleon cross-section and the mean field.

## Key claim
The stopping power in central 16O + 40Ca collisions below 300 MeV/nucleon is sensitive to the specific alpha-clustering configuration of the projectile, with the tetrahedral structure yielding the highest stopping power; however, the authors do not identify any single configuration as the ground state of 16O, noting that their binding energies are all close to each other (within about 0.13 MeV/nucleon), and the hierarchy of stopping power (tetrahedron > square > kite > chain) is opposite to the order of fragment fluctuations reported in prior work.

## Method
The authors used the extended quantum molecular dynamics (EQMD) model, which allows dynamically varying Gaussian wave-packet widths, to simulate central collisions of 16O projectiles with four different alpha-clustering configurations (chain, square, kite, tetrahedron) against spherical 40Ca targets. The stopping power was quantified using the isotropy ratio R_E (ratio of transverse to twice the parallel kinetic energy in the center-of-mass frame) and the transverse-to-longitudinal rapidity variance ratio (vartl). Hot fragments with excitation energy above zero and mass number greater than 4 were subsequently deexcited using the GEMINI++ code based on the Hauser-Feshbach formalism.

## Key numbers
- The binding energies per nucleon for the four 16O configurations after frictional cooling were: chain -7.82 MeV, kite -7.79 MeV, square -7.92 MeV, and tetrahedron -7.82 MeV, all close to the experimental ground-state value of -7.98 MeV.
- The Skyrme potential parameters used were alpha = -124.3 MeV, beta = 70.5 MeV, gamma = 2, giving a saturation density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3, binding energy per nucleon E/A = -16.5 MeV, and incompressibility K = 380 MeV.
- The symmetry energy coefficient was taken as c_s = 25 MeV, and the Pauli potential parameters were c_P = 15 MeV, f_0 = 1.0, and mu = 1.3.
- The default nucleon–nucleon cross-section was parametrized as sigma_NN = 100 / (1 + epsilon' / 200 MeV) mb, where epsilon' = p_rel^2 / (2m).
- The stopping power hierarchy tetrahedron > square > kite > chain was observed across the studied energy range, with tetrahedron and square configurations exceeding R_E = 1 (indicating strong squeeze-out) at intermediate energies.
- Neutron R_E decreased in the 20–50 MeV/nucleon range, consistent with experimental results from Lehaut (2010).

## Context
This work extends prior studies of nuclear stopping power in heavy-ion collisions, which have used observables such as R_E and vartl to probe the in-medium nucleon–nucleon cross-section and the equation of state (Lehaut 2010, Lopez 2014, Reisdorf from the FOPI Collaboration). It builds on the capability of the EQMD model to describe alpha-particle clustering (He, Ma, and Cao) and connects to recent interest in initial geometric fluctuations in intermediate-energy reactions (Ma review, Guo 2017, Cao 2023) as well as relativistic collisions at RHIC (Giacalone, Jia). The finding that the stopping power hierarchy is opposite to the order of fragment fluctuations (Sun, Deng, Ko) suggests that these two observables provide complementary probes of nuclear clustering structure. The results enable future experimental tests using stopping power measurements to distinguish between compact and diffuse initial configurations of light nuclei.
