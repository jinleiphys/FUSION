# Ab Initio Simulations of Light Nuclear Systems Using Eigenvector Continuation and Auxiliary Field Monte Carlo

> In this work, we discuss a new method for calculation of extremal eigenvectors and eigenvalues in systems or regions of parameter space where direct calculation is problematic. This technique relies on the analytic continuation of the power series expansion for the eigenvector around a point in the complex plane. We start this document by introducing the background material relevant to understand the basics of quantum mechanics and quantum field theories on the lattice, how we perform our numerical simulations, and how this relates to the nuclear physics we probe. We then move to the mathematical formalism of the eigenvector continuation, which is rooted in analytic function theory and linear algebra. We then discuss how these techniques are implemented numerically, with a discussion about the computational costs. Finally, we discuss applications of this method to full, quantum many-body systems. These include neutron matter, the Bose-Hubbard model, the Lipkin model, and the Coulomb interaction in light nuclei with LO chiral forces. These systems cover two categories of interest to the field: systems with a substantial sign problem, or systems that exhibit quantum phase transitions.

## Key claim

Eigenvector continuation (EC) is a new method for calculating extremal eigenvectors and eigenvalues in systems where direct calculation is problematic, relying on analytic continuation of the power series expansion for the eigenvector around a point in the complex plane; the method is demonstrated for systems with a substantial sign problem or systems that exhibit quantum phase transitions, but the accuracy depends on constructing a projection subspace from eigenvectors computed at multiple small coupling values.

## Method

The Hamiltonian is written as H = H₀ + c H₁ with tunable complex coupling c. Eigenvectors are computed at several small values of c using finite differences as numerical analogues of derivatives, then formed into a projection operator P. The Hamiltonian at a target coupling C⊙ is projected into this lower-dimensional subspace, yielding a projected Hamiltonian M = P†HP and norm matrix N = P†P; the generalized eigenvalue problem M v = λᵢ N v is solved, with the lowest eigenvalue giving the EC estimate for the ground state energy. Applications include the Bose-Hubbard model, neutron matter, the Lipkin model, and Coulomb interactions in light nuclei with leading-order (LO) chiral forces, using auxiliary field Monte Carlo with Hubbard-Stratonovich transformations.

## Key numbers

- Bose-Hubbard model: phase transition occurs around U/t = -3.8, from a weakly-bound Bose gas to a strongly-bound cluster.
- Lipkin model: for small NV/ε, energy levels are ordered by m; for larger NV/ε, energy levels pair up into even and odd parity states with the same energy.
- Coulomb interaction: simulations use LO chiral EFT with the fine-structure constant α_em as the tunable parameter; the EC method estimates ground state energy at the physical value of α_em.

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation method introduced by Frame (2018) to full quantum many-body systems, addressing two categories of difficulty: the Monte Carlo sign problem (as in neutron matter and Coulomb interactions in light nuclei) and quantum phase transitions (as in the Bose-Hubbard model and Lipkin model). The approach builds on lattice effective field theory methods using chiral EFT (Machleidt, 2011; Scherer, 2003) and auxiliary field Monte Carlo techniques, with low-energy constants constrained by nucleon-nucleon scattering data and deuteron properties (Li, 2018; Lu, 2018; Lu, 2015). The method enables calculations in parameter regions where direct simulation is infeasible, and the author plans to investigate applications to many other systems.
