# Pre-Supernova (Anti)Neutrino Emission Due to Weak-Interaction Reactions with Hot Nuclei

> Reliable predictions of (anti)neutrino spectra and luminosities are essential for assessing the feasibility of detecting pre-supernova neutrinos. Using the stellar evolution code MESA, we calculate the (anti)neutrino spectra and luminosities under realistic conditions of temperature, density, and electron fraction. Our study includes (anti)neutrinos produced by both thermal processes and nuclear weak-interaction reactions. By comparing the results of the thermal quasiparticle random-phase approximation with the standard technique based on the effective $Q$-value method, we investigate how thermal effects influence the spectra and luminosities of emitted (anti)neutrinos. Our findings show that a thermodynamically consistent treatment of Gamow--Teller transitions in hot nuclei enhances both the energy luminosity and the average energies of the emitted (anti)neutrinos.

## Key claim

A thermodynamically consistent treatment of Gamow-Teller transitions in hot nuclei, using the thermal quasiparticle random-phase approximation (TQRPA), enhances both the energy luminosity and the average energies of emitted (anti)neutrinos compared to the standard effective Q-value method, particularly when downward transitions from thermally excited states and neutral-current nuclear de-excitation are included.

## Method

The authors used the stellar evolution code MESA to simulate a 14 solar mass progenitor star and extract temperature, density, electron fraction, and isotopic composition as a function of time during the final day before core collapse. They computed (anti)neutrino spectra and luminosities from both thermal processes (pair annihilation) and nuclear weak-interaction reactions (electron/positron capture, beta decay, and nuclear de-excitation) using the TQRPA framework, which treats Gamow-Teller strength functions at finite temperature via the superoperator formalism in Liouville space. The TQRPA results were compared with those from the standard effective Q-value method, which approximates each nucleus as undergoing a single transition using an average neutrino energy from prior shell-model calculations.

## Key numbers

- At core collapse (t=0), the electron chemical potential is approximately 8–9 MeV, and electron capture on iron-group nuclei occurs primarily through GT+ resonance excitation.
- The survival probability for electron neutrinos in the normal hierarchy is p = sin²θ₁₃ ≈ 0.024; for the inverted hierarchy, p = sin²θ₁₂ cos²θ₁₃ ≈ 0.300.
- The survival probability for electron antineutrinos in the normal hierarchy is p̄ = cos²θ₁₂ cos²θ₁₃ ≈ 0.676; for the inverted hierarchy, p̄ = sin²θ₁₃ ≈ 0.024.
- The neutral-current de-excitation process enhances the electron antineutrino luminosity by approximately an order of magnitude compared to calculations without this process.
- At t ≈ 0 and t ≈ 10 hours, the TQRPA predicts slightly greater heavy-lepton flavor (anti)neutrino luminosities than the Q_eff method when ND and PA luminosities become comparable.
- In the 7–9 MeV energy range, the high-energy ND component of the electron antineutrino spectrum becomes comparable to or surpasses the pair annihilation contribution.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Dzhioev et al. (2023, 2024) that demonstrated the TQRPA framework accurately evaluates contributions of thermally excited states to (anti)neutrino production, and it directly compares with the effective Q-value method used by Patton et al. (2017) and based on shell-model calculations by Langanke (2001). The results enable more reliable predictions for detecting pre-supernova neutrinos in terrestrial experiments, particularly through channels sensitive to electron neutrinos, and the authors plan to investigate how the hardening of the neutrino spectrum predicted by TQRPA influences detectability via elastic scattering on electrons, charged- and neutral-current interactions with nuclei, and coherent elastic neutrino-nucleus scattering.
