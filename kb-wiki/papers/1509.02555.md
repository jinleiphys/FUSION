# Impressions of the Meson Spectrum: Hybrids & Exotics, present and future

> It has long been expected that the spectrum of hadrons in QCD would be far richer and extensive than experiment has so far revealed. While there have been experimental hints of this richness for some time, it is really only in the last few years that dramatic progress has been seen in the exploration both experimentally and in calculations on the lattice. Precision studies enabled by new technology both with detectors and high performance computations are converging on an understanding of the spectrum in strong coupling QCD. These methodologies are laying the foundation for a decade of potential discovery that electro and photoproduction experiments at Jefferson Lab, which when combined with key results on $B$ and charmonium decays from both $e^+e^-$ and $pp$ colliders, should turn mere impressions of the light meson spectrum into a high definition picture.

## Key claim

The light meson spectrum is far richer than the simple quark-antiquark picture, but definitive identification of hybrid and exotic states (such as the $\pi_1$ with $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$) requires high-statistics data with near-perfect angular coverage and robust amplitude analysis that can separate small partial waves from dominant ones, a condition only now being met by experiments like COMPASS, GlueX, and CLAS12.

## Method

The paper reviews the current experimental and theoretical status of light meson spectroscopy, focusing on states beyond the simple quark-antiquark picture: glueballs, tetraquarks, hadronic molecules, and hybrids. It discusses results from hadroproduction experiments (COMPASS at CERN, BNL-E852), heavy-flavor decays (BaBar, Belle, BESIII, LHCb), and lattice QCD calculations by the Hadron Spectrum Collaboration, which compute meson spectra using quark-antiquark and gluonic operators at a pion mass of 396 MeV. The paper also describes the need for analyticity, crossing, and unitarity constraints (e.g., Khuri-Treiman equations) in amplitude analysis, as pursued by the Joint Physics Analysis Center (JPAC).

## Key numbers

- The lightest glueball is predicted by lattice QCD to be a scalar around 1.6-1.7 GeV, with a tensor above 2 or 2.2 GeV.
- The $X(3872)$ is 140 MeV above $\overline{D}D$ threshold, has a width less than 1.2 MeV, and sits within a "whisker" of $\overline{D^0}D^{*0}$ threshold, 8 MeV below the corresponding charged $D$ threshold.
- The Hadron Spectrum Collaboration computed the meson spectrum at a pion mass of 396 MeV; the lightest hybrid with exotic quantum numbers ($\pi_1$, $J^{PC}=1^{-+}$) appears in the mass range around 2 GeV.
- COMPASS at 190 GeV/c has almost 100 million three-pion events, 400 times the statistics of the BNL-E852 experiment at 18 GeV/c.
- COMPASS uses a set of 88 partial waves, deliberately "over-complete," to avoid truncation artifacts seen in earlier analyses.

## Context

This paper reviews the long-standing search for gluonic and multi-quark states in the light meson spectrum, extending earlier experimental hints from GAMS (Alde 1988), BNL-E852 (Adams 2000), and VES (Beladidze 1993, Zaitsev 2000), and lattice predictions by Sexton (1995), Morningstar (1999), and Michael (1988). It sets the stage for the next decade of discovery with GlueX and CLAS12 at Jefferson Lab, combined with results from COMPASS, BaBar, Belle, BESIII, and LHCb, and emphasizes that the same amplitude analysis tools needed for hadron spectroscopy also reduce uncertainties in CKM matrix parameters from multi-body weak decays.
