# Coexistence and evolution of shapes: mean-field-based interacting boson model

> A method of deriving the Hamiltonian of the interacting boson model, that is based on the microscopic framework of the nuclear energy density functional, is presented. The constrained self-consistent mean-field calculation with a given energy density functional provides potential energy surface within the relevant collective coordinates, which is subsequently mapped onto the expectation value of the interacting-boson Hamiltonian in the boson condensate state. This procedure completely determines the strength parameters of the IBM, and the diagonalization of the mapped Hamiltonian yields excitation spectra and transition rates for a given nucleus. Two recent applications of the method are discussed, that is, the descriptions of the intruder states in Cadmium isotopes and the octupole correlations in neutron-rich odd-mass Barium isotopes.

## Key claim

The method of deriving the interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian from nuclear energy density functional (EDF) mean-field calculations can describe shape coexistence, octupole correlations, and odd-mass nuclei, but the mapping is only approximate within a limited range of the potential energy surface (PES) near the global minimum, and the resulting spectroscopic predictions show systematic discrepancies for certain states and transitions, particularly for intruder-normal mixing and B(E2) values.

## Method

Constrained self-consistent mean-field (SCMF) calculations using a given relativistic or non-relativistic EDF produce a potential energy surface (PES) in terms of collective coordinates (β,γ for quadrupole; β₂,β₃ for octupole). This SCMF PES is then mapped onto the expectation value of an IBM Hamiltonian in the boson coherent state, determining the IBM strength parameters by fitting the topology of the SCMF surface near the global minimum. The resulting IBM Hamiltonian is diagonalized in the laboratory frame to obtain excitation spectra and electromagnetic transition rates; for odd-mass nuclei, the interacting boson-fermion model (IBFM) is used with single-particle energies and occupation numbers from SCMF calculations and three free coupling constants fitted to experimental levels.

## Key numbers

- For Cd isotopes: prolate global minimum at β ≈ 0.15 for ¹¹²Cd, with an oblate local minimum between β = 0.2 and 0.3
- For Cd isotopes: boson numbers N_π = 1 (normal) and 3 (intruder 2p-2h) for protons, 5 ≤ N_ν ≤ 8 for neutrons
- For ¹¹⁴Cd: the lowest 0⁺₃ energy occurs at mid-shell, with the 0⁺₂ state assigned to intruder configuration in phenomenological calculations
- For ¹¹²,¹¹⁴Cd: B(E2; 0⁺₂ → 2⁺₁) is underpredicted by a factor of ten; for ¹¹⁶Cd it is overpredicted by a factor of ten
- For Ba isotopes: ¹⁴⁴,¹⁴⁶Ba show equilibrium minima with non-zero octupole deformation β₃ ≈ 0.1
- For ¹⁴⁴Ba: predicted B(E3; 3⁻ → 0⁺) is 23 W.u., but considerably smaller than the experimental value (which has large uncertainty)
- For ¹⁴⁵Ba: theoretical negative-parity band built on 15/2⁻₄ state at 1167 keV, close to experimental 15/2⁻ state at 1226 keV
- For ¹⁴⁵Ba: predicted B(E3; 15/2⁻₄ → 9/2⁺₁) = 25 W.u. and B(E3; 19/2⁻₂ → 13/2⁺₁) = 31 W.u.

## Context

This work extends the SCMF-to-IBM mapping method developed by Nomura (2008) and applied to rotational nuclei (Nomura 2011), γ-soft nuclei (Nomura 2012), octupole shapes (Nomura 2014), and shape coexistence (Nomura 2016). The two highlighted applications address the long-standing problem of intruder states in even-even Cd isotopes (Nomura 2018) and the onset of octupole deformation in neutron-rich odd-mass Ba isotopes (Nomura 2018), which is relevant to searches for CP violation beyond the Standard Model. The method enables systematic spectroscopic predictions for heavy exotic nuclei accessible at radioactive-ion beam facilities, complementing conventional IBM studies that relied on phenomenological fitting.
