# Impact of the equation-of-state -- gravity degeneracy on constraining the nuclear symmetry energy from astrophysical observables

> There is a degeneracy between the equation of state (EOS) of superdense neutron-rich nuclear matter and the strong-field gravity in understanding properties of neutron stars. While the EOS is still poorly known, there are also longstanding ambiguities in choosing General Relativity or alternative gravity theories in the not-so-well tested strong-field regime. Besides possible appearance of hyperons and new phases, the most uncertain part of the nucleonic EOS is currently the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy. To provide information that may help break the EOS-gravity degeneracy, we investigate effects of symmetry energy within its uncertain range determined by terrestrial nuclear laboratory experiments on the gravitational binding energy and spacetime curvature of neutron stars within GR and the scalar-tensor (ST) theory of gravity. In particular, we focus on effects of the following parameters characterizing the EOS of neutron-rich nucleonic matter: (1) the incompressibility $K_0$ of symmetric nuclear matter, (2) the slope $L$ of symmetry energy at saturation density and (3) the high-density behavior of symmetry energy. We find that the variation of either the density slope $L$ or the high-density behavior of symmetry energy leads to large changes in both the binding energy and curvature of neutron stars while effects of varying the more constrained $K_0$ are negligibly small. The difference in predictions using the GR and the ST theory appears only for massive neutron stars, and is significantly smaller than the differences resulting from variations in the symmetry energy. We conclude that within the ST theory of gravity, the EOS-gravity degeneracy has been broken by the recent relativistic pulsar measurements, and that measurements of neutron star properties sensitive to the compactness constrain mainly the density dependence of the symmetry energy.

## Key claim

The degeneracy between the nuclear equation of state (EOS) and strong-field gravity can be broken within scalar-tensor (ST) theories of gravity using recent relativistic pulsar measurements, but only because the variation in neutron star properties due to the uncertain symmetry energy is significantly larger than the variation due to switching between General Relativity (GR) and ST gravity; the EOS-gravity degeneracy remains unresolved for massive neutron stars where the gravity-model differences are largest.

## Method

The authors constructed neutron star models using the Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations within both GR and scalar-tensor gravity, employing a set of relativistic mean-field (IU-FSU) and Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SkIU-FSU) equations of state. They systematically varied three nuclear EOS parameters within experimentally constrained ranges: the incompressibility K0 of symmetric nuclear matter (220-260 MeV), the slope L of the symmetry energy at saturation density (47.2-100 MeV), and the high-density behavior of the symmetry energy. They then computed the fractional gravitational binding energy and the full contraction of the Riemann curvature tensor (Kretschmann invariant) for neutron stars of different masses.

## Key numbers

- The incompressibility K0 of symmetric nuclear matter is constrained to 240 ± 20 MeV, and varying K0 between 220 MeV and 260 MeV changes the fractional binding energy by less than 1% for all neutron star masses considered.
- For a 1.4 solar-mass neutron star, changing the symmetry energy slope L from 47.2 MeV (IU-FSU) to 100 MeV (IU-FSU²) changes the fractional binding energy by -8.85% and the surface curvature by -24.81%.
- For a 1.9 solar-mass neutron star, the SkIU-FSU model (softer high-density symmetry energy) gives a fractional binding energy of -0.1639 and surface curvature of 1.5226 × 10⁻² km⁻², compared to -0.1491 and 1.2051 × 10⁻² km⁻² for the IU-FSU model, a relative change of +9.89% in binding energy and +26.35% in curvature.
- The difference between GR and scalar-tensor gravity predictions for a 1.9 solar-mass neutron star using the IU-FSU EOS is only +1.21% in fractional binding energy, much smaller than the changes from varying the symmetry energy.
- The surface curvature of neutron stars ranges from approximately 2.0 × 10¹⁴ times the solar surface curvature for 1.0-1.5 solar-mass stars to 4.0 × 10¹⁴ times solar for 1.72-1.95 solar-mass stars, depending on the symmetry energy.
- The fractional binding energy of a 1.9 solar-mass neutron star lies between -0.1380 and -0.1639 across the EOS models studied.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Eksi (2014) and Psaltis (2008) on using curvature as a measure of gravitational field strength, and builds on the binding energy constraints from the double pulsar PSR J0737-3039B studied by Podsiadlowski (2005) and Newton (2009). The authors show that the binding energy of low-mass neutron stars primarily constrains the slope L of the symmetry energy at saturation density, while massive neutron stars like PSR J0348+0432 (Antoniadis, 2013) probe the high-density behavior of the symmetry energy. This enables future work to use astrophysical observables such as surface gravitational redshift and maximum spin frequency to determine the density dependence of the symmetry energy, provided the EOS-gravity degeneracy is resolved.
