# Cluster production and the chemical freeze-out in expanding hot dense matter

> We discuss medium effects on light cluster production in the QCD phase diagram by relating Mott transition lines to those for chemical freeze-out. In heavy-ion collisions at highest energies provided by the LHC, light cluster abundances should follow the statistical model because of low baryon densities. Chemical freeze-out in this domain is correlated with the QCD crossover transition. At low energies, in the nuclear fragmentation region, where the freeze-out interferes with the liquid-gas phase transition, self-energy and Pauli blocking effects are important. We demonstrate that at intermediate energies the chemical freeze-out line correlates with the maximum mass fraction of nuclear bound states, in particular $α$ particles. In this domain, the HADES, FAIR and NICA experiments can give new insights.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that chemical freeze-out in expanding hot dense matter is driven by the formation of nuclear bound states (clusters) and their dissolution via the Mott effect, rather than being a purely statistical process. The authors specifically claim that at intermediate energies, the chemical freeze-out line correlates with the maximum mass fraction of alpha particles, with the caveat that this correlation holds only when in-medium effects such as Pauli blocking and self-energy shifts are properly accounted for.

## Method
The authors use a quantum statistical approach based on the nonequilibrium statistical operator (NSO) method to describe cluster formation in nuclear matter. They calculate the in-medium binding energy shifts and Pauli blocking effects for light clusters (²H, ³H, ³He, ⁴He) using a relativistic mean-field (RMF-DD2) approximation, and determine the Mott density where bound states dissolve. The freeze-out line is computed by combining this quantum statistical treatment of the nucleon subsystem with a hadron resonance gas (HRG) description for mesons and heavier baryons, including excluded volume effects.

## Key numbers
- LHC freeze-out temperature: T_cf = 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV, coinciding with the lattice QCD pseudocritical temperature for chiral crossover at μ = 0 (HotQCD 2018)
- Lowest freeze-out temperature from heavy-ion collisions: T_cf = 48.3 MeV at E_lab = 0.8 A GeV
- Low-energy HIC freeze-out temperatures: range 4 MeV < T_cf < 10 MeV (Natowitz 2010, Qin 2011)
- Liquid-gas critical point: T_liquid-gas = 12.1 MeV, μ_liquid-gas = 915.61 MeV (Typel 2009)
- Maximum freeze-out density from HRG with excluded volume: 0.144 fm⁻³ at T = 130 MeV (using baryon radius r_B = 0.365 fm)
- Maximum freeze-out density from ideal HRG: 0.175 fm⁻³ at T = 130 MeV
- Mott line fit for alpha particles (30-130 MeV range): T_Mott,α(n) [MeV] = 2.6 + 731.9 × n [fm⁻³]
- Freeze-out interpolation formula: T_cf(μ) [MeV] = 156.5 - 76.68 × (μ [GeV])² - 139.7 × (μ [GeV])⁴

## Context
This work extends the unified description of chemical freeze-out across all collision energies first attempted by Ropke (2017) and later by Poberezhnyuk (2019) using a quantum van der Waals hadron resonance gas model, but now includes nucleonic correlations and cluster formation that were missing in the low-temperature region. The paper connects the ultrarelativistic regime described by Andronic (2017) with the low-energy fragmentation region studied by Qin (2011) and Natowitz (2010), and with fission processes (Ropke 2020). The results enable future experiments at HADES, FAIR (CBM), and NICA (MPD, BM@N) to test the proposed correlation between the Mott line for alpha clusters and the chemical freeze-out line in the intermediate energy domain.
