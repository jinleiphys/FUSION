# Particle Production in Heavy Ion Collisions

> The status of thermal model descriptions of particle production in heavy ion collisions is presented. We discuss the formulation of statistical models with different implementation of the conservation laws and indicate their applicability in heavy ion and elementary particle collisions. We analyze experimental data on hadronic abundances obtained in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, in a very broad energy range starting from RHIC/BNL ($\sqrt s=200$ A GeV), SPS/CERN ($\sqrt s\simeq 20$ A GeV) up to AGS/BNL ($\sqrt s\simeq 5$ A GeV) and SIS/GSI ($\sqrt s\simeq 2$ A GeV) to test equilibration of the fireball created in the collision. We argue that the statistical approach provides a very satisfactory description of experimental data covering this wide energy range. Any deviations of the model predictions from the data are indicated. We discuss the unified description of particle chemical freeze--out and the excitation functions of different particle species. At SPS and RHIC energy the relation of freeze--out parameters with the QCD phase boundary is analyzed. Furthermore, the application of the extended statistical model to quantitative understanding of open and hidden charm hadron yields is considered.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a thermal (statistical) model based on a hadron resonance gas in the grand canonical ensemble provides a very satisfactory description of measured hadronic abundances across a broad energy range from SIS/GSI to RHIC/BNL, but the authors explicitly note that any deviations of the model predictions from the data are indicated, and that the interpretation of agreement implies statistical equilibrium only to the accuracy with which agreement is observed.

## Method

The authors formulate a grand canonical partition function for a hadron resonance gas, including all mesons with masses below approximately 1.5 GeV and baryons with masses below approximately 2 GeV, with resonance decays and excluded volume corrections for repulsive interactions. They fit the two independent parameters (temperature T and baryochemical potential μ_B) to experimental hadron multiplicity ratios from AGS, SPS, and RHIC energies, while fixing strangeness chemical potential via strangeness neutrality and charge chemical potential via isospin conservation conditions.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature from lattice gauge theory (two-flavour QCD): T_c = 173 ± 8 MeV, with critical energy density ε_c = 0.6 ± 0.3 GeV/fm³
- Initial energy density at SPS (√s = 17.3 GeV): ε_0^(SPS)(τ_0 ≈ 1 fm) ≈ 3.5 ± 0.5 GeV/fm³
- At RHIC (√s = 130 A GeV), with saturation model thermalization time τ_eq ≈ 0.2 fm, energy density ε_eq ≈ 98 GeV/fm³
- At LHC, expected initial energy density range: 400 < ε_eq^(LHC) < 1300 GeV/fm³
- Temperature range estimates: SPS 200 MeV < T^(SPS) < 330 MeV; RHIC 210 MeV < T^(RHIC) < 600 MeV; LHC 1000 MeV < T^(LHC) < 1200 MeV
- At T ≈ 200 MeV and μ_B ≈ 300 MeV, the strange chemical potential relation μ_S ≈ (1/3) μ_B is obtained from strangeness neutrality in the hadron resonance gas

## Context

This review extends the thermal model descriptions previously applied by the authors and others (e.g., Braun-Munzinger et al. 2001) to a unified analysis of particle production from SIS/GSI (√s ≈ 2 A GeV) through AGS/BNL (√s ≈ 5 A GeV), SPS/CERN (√s ≈ 20 A GeV), and RHIC/BNL (√s = 200 A GeV). The work connects freeze-out parameters extracted from data to the QCD phase boundary predicted by lattice gauge theory (Karsch et al. 2001) and discusses the extension of the statistical model to open and hidden charm hadron yields, enabling tests of whether chemical equilibration signals deconfinement in the early stage of heavy ion collisions.
