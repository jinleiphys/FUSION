# Expansion and evaporation of hot nuclei: Comparison between semi-classical and quantal mean-field approaches

> We present a general discussion of the mean field dynamics of finite nuclei prepared under extreme conditions of temperature and pressure. We compare the prediction of semi-classical approximation with complete quantum simulations. Many features of the dynamics are carefully studied such as the collective expansion, the evaporation process, the different time-scale... This study points out many quantitative differences between quantum and semi-classical approaches. Part of the differences are related to numerical features inherent in semi-classical simulations but most of them are a direct consequence of the non treatment of nuclei as quantal objects. In particular, we show that because of a too strong damping in semi-classical approaches the expansion of hot nuclei is quenched and the speed of the collective motion reduced.

# Expansion and evaporation of hot nuclei: Comparison between semi-classical and quantal mean-field approaches

## Key claim

Semi-classical mean-field approaches (Vlasov dynamics) systematically underestimate the collective expansion speed of hot nuclei compared to full quantum (TDHF) simulations, primarily because the numerical smoothing required in test-particle methods introduces a spurious finite-range surface term that quenches the expansion; however, part of the quantitative differences arise from this numerical artifact rather than from genuine quantum effects, as demonstrated by comparing TDHF with a modified interaction that mimics the semi-classical numerical range.

## Method

The authors compare Time-Dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) simulations of hot spherical nuclei with semi-classical Vlasov simulations using the lattice Hamiltonian method (TWINGO code), employing the same zero-range density-dependent Skyrme-type interaction (t0 and t3 terms only). Initial conditions are generated either by constrained mean-field calculations or by a scaling procedure that preserves entropy, producing families of isoentropic or approximately isothermal nuclei at various compressions and dilations. To disentangle numerical artifacts from genuine quantum-classical differences, a third set of TDHF simulations is performed with an added surface term designed to mimic the spurious finite-range effect introduced by the test-particle smoothing in the semi-classical method.

## Key numbers

- The Skyrme-type force parameters used: t0 = -1000 MeV fm^3, t3 = 15000 MeV fm^6, sigma = 1, yielding infinite nuclear matter saturation at E/A = -14 MeV, rho0 = 0.15 fm^-3, compression modulus K_infinity = 336.2 MeV, and zero-temperature spinodal density = 0.1 fm^-3.
- Quantum simulations used up to 40 orbitals, radial step Delta r = 0.2 fm, lattice size 480 fm, time step 0.75 fm/c.
- Semi-classical simulations used N_test = 200 test particles, smoothing range 2 fm, lattice grid step 1 fm, time step 0.75 fm/c, box size 40 x 40 x 40 fm^3.
- The constrained mean-field initialization used a small external constraint lambda = 0.25 MeV fm^-2.
- Semi-classical ground states were stable for more than 300 fm/c with no test-particle emission.

## Context

This work extends the benchmark scenario introduced by Vautherin (1994) for studying hot nucleus expansion in TDHF, providing the first systematic quantitative comparison between quantum and semi-classical mean-field dynamics for the same interaction. The study addresses long-standing questions about the validity of semi-classical transport theories (BUU, Vlasov) that are widely used in heavy-ion collision simulations (Bertsch 1988, Bonche 1976, Gregoire 1987, Suraud 1992). The findings enable improved interpretation of experimental data on hot nuclei by clarifying which differences between semi-classical models and quantum calculations are due to genuine quantum effects (wave dynamics, barrier transmission) versus numerical artifacts from test-particle smoothing.
