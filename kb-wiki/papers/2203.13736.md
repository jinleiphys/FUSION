# Interplay between surface and volume instabilities in heavy-ion collisions examined within mean-field extensions

> In the transition from nuclear matter to finite nuclei, complex finite-size effects which characterise open systems arise, in relation with either the nuclear surface or the bulk. In addition, the non-equilibrium character of the process, typical of violent heavy-ion collisions (from Fermi energy to the intermediate-energy domain) adds up as well. The resulting dynamics is the combination of surface and volume unstable modes which trigger large-amplitude fluctuations. A rich variety of fragmentation patterns may emerge, ranging from collimated streams of nuclear clusters to the split of a stretched nuclear complex into few large fragments. They imply different conditions of density and surface tension, and result in different chronologies. Such phenomenology has been observed in experiments, but it is often difficult to recognise and disentangle the underlying types of instabilities. To draw some example, two extremely deformed nuclear systems, produced below and above Fermi energy, are chosen and followed microscopically all along their evolution within the Boltzmann-Langevin One-Body approach.

## Key claim

The paper argues that two seemingly similar fragmentation patterns in heavy-ion collisions, multiple splits of very deformed heavy systems and collimated streams of clusters (nuclear jets), are actually driven by fundamentally different instability types: surface (Plateau-Rayleigh) instabilities dominate in deep-inelastic collisions near saturation density, while volume (spinodal) instabilities dominate in nuclear jets at Fermi energies where densities drop to about 1/3 saturation density, even though both produce fragments.

## Method

The authors employed the Boltzmann-Langevin One Body (BLOB) model to simulate two selected systems microscopically: 197Au+197Au at 15 A MeV (deep-inelastic regime) and 36Ar+58Ni at 74 A MeV (Fermi-energy regime). They extracted temperature and density conditions from the simulations at the moment of fragment formation, then constructed dispersion relations (growth rate versus wavenumber) for both volume and surface unstable modes. These numerical dispersion relations were compared to analytic calculations for spinodal (volume) and Plateau-Rayleigh (surface) instabilities in nuclear matter and in finite systems.

## Key numbers

- 197Au+197Au at 15 A MeV: the leading wavelength for surface instabilities is about 20 fm, associated to very massive fragments (up to Ge); the growth time reaches values of about 200 to 300 fm/c, up to ten times larger than the growth time of the spinodal leading mode in nuclear matter.
- 36Ar+58Ni at 74 A MeV: intermediate-mass fragments with A>4 form at a density of about 1/3 of saturation density (rho_sat); d, t, 3He and alpha clusters form at about 1/5 of saturation density.
- For nuclear matter at rho_sat/3, the leading spinodal wavelength is about 8 fm, corresponding to fragment sizes in the range from O to Ne in open systems.
- The spinodal dispersion relation for nuclear matter at rho_sat/2 was calculated for comparison with the 197Au+197Au system, confirming that spinodal conditions are not matched.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of nuclear fragmentation by distinguishing between surface and volume instabilities in finite systems, a distinction often ambiguous in experimental data. It builds on earlier BLOB model developments (Napolitani 2013, 2017) and prior simulations of 197Au+197Au using QMD (Tian 2010) and Stochastic Mean-Field (Rizzo 2014) approaches, which the authors found to be either too compact or too resistant to fragmentation. The analysis enables future experimental identification of instability types based on density and temperature signatures rather than fragment kinematics alone.
