# Probing r-Process Production of Nuclei Beyond Bi209 with Gamma Rays

> We estimate gamma-ray fluxes due to the decay of nuclei beyond Bi209 from a supernova or a supernova remnant assuming that the r-process occurs in supernovae. We find that a detector with a sensitivity of about 10**(-7) photons/cm**2/s at energies of 40 keV to 3 MeV may detect fluxes due to the decay of Ra226, Th229, Am241, Am243, Cf249, and Cf251 in the newly discovered supernova remnant near Vela. In addition, such a detector may detect fluxes due to the decay of Ac227 and Ra228 produced in a future supernova at a distance of about 1 kpc. As nuclei with mass numbers A > 209 are produced solely by the r-process, such detections are the best proof for a supernova r-process site. Further, they provide the most direct information on yields of progenitor nuclei with A > 209 at r-process freeze-out. Finally, detection of fluxes due to the decay of r-process nuclei over a range of masses from a supernova or a supernova remnant provides the opportunity to compare yields in a single supernova event with the solar r-process abundance pattern.

## Key claim

Gamma-ray fluxes from the decay of nuclei beyond Bi-209 in a supernova or supernova remnant can be detected with a sensitivity of about 10^-7 photons/cm^2/s at energies of 40 keV to 3 MeV, assuming that the r-process occurs in supernovae; the detection of such fluxes would provide the best proof for a supernova r-process site and direct information on yields of progenitor nuclei with A > 209 at r-process freeze-out, but the estimates depend on the assumption that the average production per supernova is approximately 2 x 10^-8 solar masses for each relevant nucleus.

## Method

The authors estimated gamma-ray fluxes using the standard formula for radioactive decay from a point source, incorporating the distance to the source, the age of the remnant, the lifetime of each nucleus, and the gamma-ray emission probability per decay. They calculated average production amounts of actinides per supernova using solar system abundances of Th-232, U-235, U-238, and Pu-244, assuming uniform Galactic production before solar system formation, and validated these estimates against observed thorium abundances in very metal-poor halo stars. For the newly discovered supernova remnant near Vela (RX J0852.0-4622/GRO J0852-4642), they assumed a distance of 200 pc and an age of 700 years, and for a future supernova they assumed a distance of 1 kpc.

## Key numbers

- Detector sensitivity required: approximately 10^-7 photons/cm^2/s at energies of approximately 40 keV to 3 MeV
- Distance to the newly discovered supernova remnant near Vela: approximately 200 pc
- Age of the newly discovered supernova remnant: approximately 700 years
- Estimated production per supernova for each selected nucleus (Ra-226, Th-229, Am-241, Am-243, Cf-249, Cf-251): 2 x 10^-8 solar masses
- Lifetime of Cf-249: 506 years; lifetime of Ra-226: 2.31 x 10^3 years; lifetime of Th-229 and Am-243: 1.06 x 10^4 years; lifetime of Am-241: 624 years; lifetime of Cf-251: 1.30 x 10^3 years
- Flux from Cf-249 decay at 388 keV in the new remnant: 2.1 x 10^-7 gamma/cm^2/s
- Flux from Am-241 decay at 59.5 keV in the new remnant: 1.2 x 10^-7 gamma/cm^2/s
- Flux from Cf-251 decay via Cm K X-rays in the new remnant: 1.2 x 10^-7 gamma/cm^2/s
- Flux from Ra-226 decay via Po-214 at 609 keV in the new remnant: 1.0 x 10^-7 gamma/cm^2/s
- Lifetime of Ac-227: 31.4 years; lifetime of Ra-228: 8.30 years
- Peak flux from a future supernova at 1 kpc at 931 keV at t approximately 1 year: approximately 1.6 x 10^-6 gamma/cm^2/s
- Peak flux from a future supernova at 1 kpc at 240 keV at t = 4.55 years: approximately 9.2 x 10^-7 gamma/cm^2/s
- Average production of Th-232 per supernova: 9.2 x 10^-8 solar masses; U-235: 9.9 x 10^-8 solar masses; U-238: 6.3 x 10^-8 solar masses; Pu-244: 1.9 x 10^-8 solar masses
- Thorium production inferred from CS 22892-052: 4.8 x 10^-7 solar masses; from HD 115444: 1.5 x 10^-7 solar masses

## Context

This work extends earlier discussions of gamma-ray signatures from supernova r-process events by Meyer and Howard (1991) and by Qian, Vogel, and Wasserburg (1998b), who focused on nuclei with A < 209 such as Sb-125, Cs-137, Ce-144, Eu-155, and Os-194. It also revisits the earlier proposal by Clayton and Craddock (1965) regarding gamma rays from nuclei beyond Bi-209 in supernova remnants, but uses the newly discovered young supernova remnant near Vela (RX J0852.0-4622/GRO J0852-4642) as a much more favorable target than the Crab nebula. The detection of these gamma rays would enable direct comparison of r-process yields from a single supernova event with the solar r-process abundance pattern, testing the diversity of supernova r-process events suggested by Wasserburg, Busso, and Gallino (1996), and would provide crucial constraints for theoretical r-process calculations that currently rely on solar system data.
