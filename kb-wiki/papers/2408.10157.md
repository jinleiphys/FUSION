# Nuclear Fragmentation at the Future Electron-Ion Collider

> We explore the potential of conducting low-energy nuclear physics studies, including nuclear structure and decay, at the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) at Brookhaven. By comparing the standard theory of electron-nucleus scattering with the equivalent photon method applied to Ultraperipheral Collisions (UPC) at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN. In the limit of extremely high beam energies and small energy transfers, very transparent equations emerge. We apply these equations to analyze nuclear fragmentation in UPCs at the LHC and $eA$ scattering at the EIC, demonstrating that the EIC could facilitate unique photonuclear physics studies. However, we have also shown that the fragmentation cross-sections at the EIC are about 1,000 times smaller than those at the LHC. At the LHC, the fragmentation of uranium nuclei displays characteristic double-hump mass distributions from fission events, while at the EIC, fragmentation is dominated by neutron emission and fewer few fission products, about 10,000 smaller number of events.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the equivalent photon method, derived from standard electron scattering theory, applies to low-energy nuclear fragmentation at the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), but the fragmentation cross sections at the EIC are about 1,000 times smaller than those in ultraperipheral collisions (UPCs) at the LHC, and the number of fission events at the EIC is about 10,000 times smaller.

## Method

The authors derived analytic expressions for the equivalent photon spectrum in electron-nucleus scattering directly from the Plane Wave Born Approximation (PWBA) formalism, showing that at high electron energies the cross section factorizes into a virtual photon flux times the real photonuclear cross section. They applied these equations to calculate fragmentation cross sections for 208Pb and 238U nuclei at the EIC (18 GeV electrons on 110 GeV/nucleon ions) and in UPCs at the LHC (5.5 TeV/nucleon Pb+Pb and U+U), using a Lorentzian parameterization for giant dipole and quadrupole resonances and the ABLA07 code with the Ewing-Weisskopf model to compute decay branching ratios and fission yields.

## Key numbers

- Fragmentation cross sections at the EIC are about 1,000 times smaller than at the LHC; for example, the total cross section for 238U is 85.48 barns at the LHC versus 41.32 millibarns at the EIC.
- The one-neutron evaporation cross section for 208Pb is 33.93 barns at the LHC and 20.24 millibarns at the EIC; for 238U it is 33.20 barns at the LHC and 15.58 millibarns at the EIC.
- The fission cross section for 238U is 18.24 barns at the LHC and 8.867 millibarns at the EIC, corresponding to fission branching ratios of 19.54% and 21.45%, respectively.
- At the EIC, assuming a luminosity of 10^34 cm^2 s^-1, approximately 10^8 neutrons per second can be produced from electron-uranium fragmentation.
- The Lorentz factor at the EIC is gamma = 1.6 x 10^5 (for E=80 GeV), compared to gamma = 1.73 x 10^7 at the LHC.

## Context

This work extends the equivalent photon method developed by Fermi (1924), Weizsacker and Williams (1934), and later applied to UPCs by Bertulani and collaborators (1988) to the energy regime of the future EIC at Brookhaven. The results contrast with studies of strong-interaction fragmentation in central heavy-ion collisions and with lepton-parton cascade models (Magdy 2024, Schmookler 2023), showing that electromagnetic excitation of giant resonances yields larger cross sections than initial lepton-parton hard scattering. The paper enables planning for low-energy nuclear physics experiments at the EIC, including studies of fission dynamics and neutron-rich isotope production, while also suggesting that UPCs at the LHC may be better suited for high-statistics nuclear fragmentation studies.
