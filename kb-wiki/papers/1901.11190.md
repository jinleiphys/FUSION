# Dynamically Integrated Transport Approach for High-Energy Nuclear Collisions at High Baryon Density

> To explore the structure of the QCD phase diagram in high baryon density domain, several high-energy nuclear collision experiments in a wide range of beam energies are currently performed or planned using many accelerator facilities. In these experiments search for a first-order phase transition and the QCD critical point is one of the most important topics. To find the signature of the phase transition, experimental data should be compared to appropriate dynamical models which quantitatively describe the process of the collisions. In this study we develop a new dynamical model on the basis of the non-equilibrium hadronic transport model JAM and 3+1D hydrodynamics. We show that the new model reproduce well the experimental beam-energy dependence of hadron yields and particle ratio by the partial thermalization of the system in our core-corona approach.

## Key claim
The new JAM+Hydro model, which dynamically integrates a hadronic transport model with ideal hydrodynamics, reproduces the experimental beam-energy dependence of hadron yields and particle ratios through partial thermalization of the system, but the improvement is conditional on the fluidization energy parameter and is most significant for strange hadrons and kaon-to-pion ratios at lower beam energies.

## Method
The authors combine the non-equilibrium hadronic transport model JAM with 3+1D ideal hydrodynamics using a dynamical initialization where fluids are created from transport-model particles via source terms, a dynamical core-corona separation controlled by fluidization and particlization energy density parameters (set to 0.5 GeV/fm³), and dynamical coupling where particles are absorbed into fluids in high-density regions and fluids are converted back to particles via the Cooper-Frye formula in low-density regions. The hydrodynamic part uses a phenomenological equation of state with a first-order phase transition (EOS-Q). Calculations are performed for central Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 2.7–4.9 GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at √s_NN = 6.4–17.3 GeV.

## Key numbers
- Fluidization and particlization energy densities set to e_f = e_p = 0.5 GeV/fm³ (with variations to 0.8 and 1.0 GeV/fm³ tested)
- Smearing profile width σ = 0.5 fm for particle-to-fluid conversion
- Beam energy range for calculations: √s_NN = 2.7–17.3 GeV
- Impact parameter b < 4.0 fm for 0–7% centrality at √s_NN = 6.4–12.4 GeV, or b < 3.4 fm for 0–5% centrality otherwise
- The K⁺/π⁺ ratio is sensitive to e_f at AGS energies (lower end of the beam energy range), with larger values of e_f improving the description

## Context
This work extends the core-corona approach previously applied to higher-energy collisions (e.g., at RHIC top energies) to the lower beam energy regime relevant for Beam Energy Scan programs at RHIC, NA61/SHINE at SPS, and future experiments like CBM at FAIR, MPD at NICA, and J-PARC-HI. The model improves upon the standalone JAM cascade model (Nara et al., 2000), which overestimates pions and underestimates strange particles, by incorporating partial thermalization via hydrodynamics. The dynamical initialization method builds on prior work by the authors (Murase et al., 2016) and enables description of the finite passing time of nuclei at lower collision energies. This model enables quantitative comparison with experimental data to search for signatures of a first-order phase transition and the QCD critical point in the high baryon density region of the phase diagram.
