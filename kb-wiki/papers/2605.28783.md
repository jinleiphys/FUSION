# Three-dimensional orbital-free density functional theory description of nuclear pasta in the inner crust of neutron stars

> Background: In the bottom layer of the inner crust of neutron stars, various crystalline structures are expected to emerge that are collectively called ``nuclear pasta.'' It is desired to know properties of nuclear pasta in a wide variety of conditions for astrophysical applications. However, three-dimensional fully-microscopic calculations require huge computational effort that makes it still challenging to carry out systematic calculations. Purpose: In this paper, we propose an efficient method to calculate various nuclear pasta configurations in a non-empirical manner, based on three-dimensional orbital-free density functional theory (OF-DFT). We demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed approach by applying it to densities throughout the inner crust of neutron stars. Methods: As a first application of OF-DFT for nuclear pasta, we employ the second-order extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) expansion of Skyrme energy density functional (EDF) to construct an EDF that depends only on neutron and proton number densities. Based on the variational principle, we derive Euler-Lagrange equations to determine optimal neutron and proton density distributions and solve them self-consistently. In this work, we call this approach the self-consistent ETF (SC-ETF) method. Results: We perform three-dimensional SC-ETF calculations with various box sizes. We successfully obtain various pasta structures, depending on given average nucleon number densities, consistent with earlier studies. Moreover, we find other exotic structures, such as bending and/or connected rods, slabs with a hole, etc., underlining the advantage of the self-consistent formalism. Conclusions: We demonstrate that the SC-ETF method proposed in this study, which can be regarded as a realization of OF-DFT, is a promising tool that can efficiently describe complex pasta structures without empirical assumptions on geometric shapes.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the self-consistent extended Thomas-Fermi (SC-ETF) method, a realization of orbital-free density functional theory (OF-DFT), can efficiently describe complex nuclear pasta structures in the inner crust of neutron stars without empirical assumptions on geometric shapes, though the method uses only the second-order (hbar^2) ETF expansion and may exhibit numerical instabilities for certain energy density functionals at fine mesh spacings.

## Method

The authors employed the second-order extended Thomas-Fermi expansion of a Skyrme-type energy density functional to construct an EDF depending only on neutron and proton number densities. They derived Euler-Lagrange equations from the variational principle and solved them self-consistently using the gradient descent method on a three-dimensional Cartesian mesh with periodic boundary conditions, representing densities as the square of functions phi_q(r). Calculations were performed for various box sizes (16^3, 24^3, 32^3, and 40^3 fm^3) starting from randomly-generated initial density distributions, with the chemical potential mu as an input parameter to control nucleon number.

## Key numbers

- Box sizes used: 16^3, 24^3, 32^3, and 40^3 fm^3 with periodic boundary conditions
- Energy difference between exotic structures (deformed nuclei, bending/connected rods, slabs with a hole) and standard pasta configurations: less than 1 keV per nucleon
- For the T6 EDF (alpha=1/3, m*/m=1), SC-ETF calculations converged for mesh spacings Delta x from 0.4 to 2.0 fm, yielding total energy of -367.15 MeV for 40Ca at Delta x=1.2 fm
- For the SLy4 EDF (alpha=1/6, m*/m=0.69), calculations diverged for Delta x <= 1.1 fm, oscillated for 1.2 <= Delta x <= 1.4 fm, and converged for 1.5 <= Delta x <= 2.0 fm, yielding total energy of -733.46 MeV for 40Ca at Delta x=1.2 fm
- For the Skz-1 EDF (alpha=0.1694, m*/m=0.70), calculations diverged for all Delta x <= 2.0 fm with no converged range

## Context

This work extends the conventional ETF approach used by Pearson (2020) and Shelley (2021), where density distributions were parametrized by physics-motivated functions (e.g., Wood-Saxon shapes), by instead determining densities self-consistently without assuming geometric shapes. It also contrasts with fully-microscopic three-dimensional Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations by Schuetrumpf (2019), which can describe arbitrary structures but require solving hundreds to thousands of complex partial differential equations. The SC-ETF method enables systematic surveys of pasta configurations across densities, temperatures, and proton fractions with greatly reduced computational cost, and can be extended to include finite-temperature effects, shell effects via higher-order expansions, or machine-learned orbital-free functionals as demonstrated by Hizawa (2023, 2024) and Wu (2025, 2026).
