# Quantum Entanglement in Nuclear Fission

> Nuclear fission presents a unique example of quantum entanglement in strongly interacting many-body systems. A heavy nucleus can split into hundreds of combinations of two complementary fragments in the fission process. The entanglement of fragment wave functions is persistent even after separation and impacts the partition of particles and energies between fragments. Based on microscopic dynamical calculations of the fission of $^{240}$Pu, this work finds that quantum entanglement is indispensable in the appearance of sawtooth distributions of average excitation energies of fragments and thus neutron multiplicities, but not in average neuron excess of fragments. Both sawtooth slopes from particle-number projections are found to be steep -- a feature which can be alleviated by random fluctuations. These findings may impact the understanding of quantum entanglement more broadly in mesoscopic systems.

## Key claim

Based on microscopic dynamical calculations of the fission of $^{240}$Pu, the authors find that quantum entanglement is indispensable for the appearance of sawtooth distributions of average excitation energies of fragments and thus neutron multiplicities, but not for the average neutron excess of fragments; however, the sawtooth slopes from particle-number projections are steep, a feature that can be alleviated by including random fluctuations.

## Method

The authors performed time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TD-BCS) dynamical calculations of the fission of $^{240}$Pu, combined with random fluctuations to mimic dynamical fluctuations and a double particle-number projection (PNP) scheme to incorporate couplings between two complementary fragments. Fragment excitation energies were obtained by subtracting projected binding energies during the splitting process from ground state energies, and the reduced entanglement entropy between fragments was calculated to estimate the entanglement magnitude.

## Key numbers

- The width of the mass yield distribution from projection on each individual fission event is about 8 mass units.
- The effective temperature parameter $T_f$ for random fluctuations was set to 1.5 MeV.
- The final splitting stage is about 100 fm/c in duration.
- The threshold for including a fragment configuration in excitation energy calculations was a denominator in the projection formula larger than $5 \times 10^{-4}$.
- Projected states with particle numbers deviating from the average number up to 10 particles were calculated.
- The neck size parameter $\delta$ was adopted as 0.8 fm.

## Context

This work extends microscopic time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) studies of nuclear fission by addressing the longstanding puzzle of sawtooth structures in fission observables, which have been observed in neutron multiplicities (e.g., by the authors of Neutron1 and Neutron2) and neutron excess ratios (e.g., by Ramos et al. (2018) and the authors of NZRatio2). Previous explanations based on statistical sharing mechanisms (e.g., by the authors of Neutron3) or static potential energy surface methods like TD-GCM (e.g., by the authors of SchunckProj) could not fully capture the energy sharing dynamics. The findings enable a microscopic, event-by-event fission simulator that inherently includes correlations between multiple fission observables, though further improvements are needed to match the feature set of existing simulators such as CGMF, FREYA, and FIFRELIN.
