# What can we learn from fluctuations of particle ratios?

> We explain how fluctuations of ratios can constrain and falsify the statistical model of particle production in heavy ion collisions, using $K/π$ fluctuations as an example. We define an observable capable of determining which statistical model, if any, governs freeze-out in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions. We calculate this observable for $K/π$ fluctuations, and show that it should be the same for RHIC and LHC energies, as well as independent of centrality, if the Grand-Canonical statistical model is an appropriate description and chemical equilibrium applies. We describe variations of this scaling for deviations from this scenario, such as light quark chemical non-equilibrium, strange quark over-saturation and local conservation (canonical ensemble) for strange quarks. We also introduce a similar observable capable, together with the published $K^*/K$ measurement, of ascertaining if an interacting hadron gas phase governs the system between thermal and chemical freeze-out, and of ascertaining its duration and impact on hadronic chemistry.

# What can we learn from fluctuations of particle ratios?

## Key claim
The paper argues that the observable Ψ (defined as the product of average pion multiplicity and the dynamical fluctuation ν_dyn for K/π ratios) should be constant across centrality and energy regimes (RHIC and LHC) if the Grand-Canonical statistical model with chemical equilibrium applies; deviations from this scaling would falsify the equilibrium statistical model or indicate which non-equilibrium scenario (such as light quark chemical non-equilibrium, strange quark over-saturation, or canonical ensemble effects) is at work.

## Method
The author calculates the dynamical fluctuation observable ν_dyn for K/π ratios using the statistical hadronization code SHAREv2.X, incorporating resonance decay corrections and chemical (non)equilibrium parameters. The observable Ψ = ⟨N_π⟩ ν_dyn is computed for K^-/π^- and K^+/K^- ratios at RHIC and LHC energies under various assumptions about light and strange quark phase-space occupancies (γ_q, γ_s) and ensemble choices (Grand-Canonical vs. Canonical). A second observable ΔΨ is introduced to compare with K^*/K^- measurements to probe the duration of the interacting hadron gas phase between chemical and thermal freeze-out.

## Key numbers
- For Cu-Cu 200 GeV collisions relative to Au-Au at the same energy: ⟨N_π⟩|_{Cu-Cu} / ⟨N_π⟩|_{Au-Au} ≈ 3.2, so ν_dyn|_{Cu-Cu} should equal 3.2 times ν_dyn|_{Au-Au} if the statistical model holds.
- For the Canonical ensemble scenario, Ψ for K^+/K^- should be lower by a factor of two compared to the Grand-Canonical expectation.
- The correction from higher-lying resonances and finite baryochemical potential to the ΔΨ observable is approximately a 10% effect, less than expected experimental error.
- The chemical freeze-out temperature from equilibrium statistical model fits is T_chem ∼ 170 MeV; thermal freeze-out temperature from particle spectra fits is T_therm ∼ 100 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the statistical model testing program initiated by Becattini (2000), Braun-Munzinger et al. (2001), and the SHARE collaboration, addressing the superficial consensus that statistical hadronization fits prove thermal and chemical equilibrium. The paper builds on fluctuation studies by Jeon (2000) and Gazdzicki and Hauer (2005) to propose a more stringent test than yield fits alone. It enables distinguishing between equilibrium and non-equilibrium freeze-out scenarios (such as those proposed by Rafelski and Letessier for γ_q,γ_s > 1 at LHC energies), and can resolve whether an interacting hadron gas phase alters resonance abundances between chemical and thermal freeze-out, a question raised by the failure of the HBT puzzle and the observed suppression of Λ(1520) and K^*(892) by Fachini, Markert, and collaborators.
