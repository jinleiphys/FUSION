# Light-particle emission from the fissioning nuclei 126Ba, 188Pt and (266,272,278)/110: theoretical predictions and experimental results

> We present a comparison of our model treating fission dynamics in conjunction with light-particle (n, p, alpha) evaporation with the available experimental data for the nuclei 126Ba, 188Pt and three isotopes of the element Z=110. The dynamics of the symmetric fission process is described through the solution of a classical Langevin equation for a single collective variable characterizing the nuclear deformation along the fission path. A microscopic approach is used to evaluate the emission rates for pre-fission light particles. Entrance-channel effects are taken into account by generating an initial spin distribution of the compound nucleus formed by the fusion of two deformed nuclei with different relative orientations.

# Light-particle emission from the fissioning nuclei 126Ba, 188Pt and (266,272,278)110: theoretical predictions and experimental results

## Key claim

The model combining Langevin fission dynamics with microscopic particle-emission rates reproduces measured pre-fission neutron multiplicities for 126Ba, 188Pt, and three isotopes of Z=110 only approximately, with systematic discrepancies (e.g., underprediction for 188Pt by about one neutron) that suggest the need for temperature-dependent friction or improved treatment of entrance-channel effects.

## Method

The symmetric fission dynamics of each compound nucleus is described by a classical Langevin equation for a single collective coordinate (the elongation q = R12/R0) using a TKS surface parametrization, with collective mass from incompressible fluid approximation, friction from the wall-and-window model, and a collective potential obtained from the liquid-drop model with Myers-Swiatecki parametrization and temperature-dependent SkM* force parameters. Pre-fission neutron, proton, and alpha-particle emission rates are computed using a microscopic phase-space approach (or the Weisskopf formula) that accounts for deformation and rotation of the emitting nucleus, and the initial spin distribution of the compound nucleus is generated by considering the fusion of two deformed nuclei with different relative orientations via a simplified trajectory method.

## Key numbers

- For 126Ba at E* = 101.4 MeV via entrance channel I (28Si + 98Mo): measured pre-fission neutron multiplicity Mn = 1.32, calculated Mn = 1.83.
- For 126Ba at E* = 101.4 MeV via entrance channel II (19F + 107Ag): measured Mn = 1.31, calculated Mn = 1.80.
- For 126Ba at E* = 118.5 MeV via entrance channel I: measured Mn = 2.01, calculated Mn = 1.71.
- For 126Ba at E* = 118.5 MeV via entrance channel II: measured Mn = 1.85, calculated Mn = 1.99.
- For 126Ba at T = 1.6 MeV and L = 0: fission barrier height is 43 MeV; barrier vanishes at L approximately 80 hbar.
- For 188Pt at T = 1.6 MeV and L = 70 hbar: fission barrier becomes negligible.
- For 188Pt at T = 4.8 MeV and L = 30 hbar: fission barrier is about four times lower than at T = 0 and L = 30 hbar.
- For 272110: fission barrier vanishes at L greater than 22 hbar.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier model applied to 160Yb (Pomorski et al., 1996) by incorporating explicit entrance-channel spin distributions and comparing with new experimental data from the SARA and VIVITRON facilities using the DEMON neutron detector (Badimon et al., 1998; Donadille et al., 1998; Kalinin et al., 1999). The results enable systematic testing of dynamical fission models across different mass regions and motivate future inclusion of temperature-dependent friction and a unified Langevin description from fusion through fission.
