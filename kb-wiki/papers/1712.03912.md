# Study of the sensitivity of observables to hot spot size in heavy ion collisions

> An open question in the field of heavy-ion collisions is to what extent the size of initial inhomogeneities in the system affects measured observables. Here we present a method to smooth out these inhomogeneities with minimal effect on global properties, in order to quantify the effect of short-range features of the initial state. We show a comparison of hydrodynamic predictions with original and smoothened initial conditions for four models of initial conditions and various observables. Integrated observables (integrated $v_n$, scaled $v_n$ distributions, normalized symmetric cumulants, event-plane correlations) as well as most differential observables ($v_n(p_T)$) show little dependence on the inhomogeneity sizes, and instead are sensitive only to the largest-scale geometric structure. However other differential observables such as the flow factorization ratio and sub-leading principal components are more sensitive to the granularity and could be a good tool to probe the short-scale dynamics of the initial stages of a heavy-ion collision, which are not currently well understood.

## Key claim

Integrated observables and most differential observables show little dependence on the size of initial inhomogeneities (hot spots) and are sensitive only to the largest-scale geometric structure, but the flow factorization ratio and sub-leading principal components are more sensitive to granularity and could probe short-scale dynamics of the initial stages.

## Method

The authors applied a cubic spline filter to smooth the energy density profiles of four initial condition models (NeXus, MC-KLN, IP-Glasma, and MC-Glauber) with smoothing parameters λ from 0.3 to 1 fm, then evolved the original and smoothed initial conditions through hydrodynamic simulations (NeXSPheRIO for NeXus and v-USPhydro for MC-KLN) to compute final-state observables. They compared the original and smoothed results for integrated flow coefficients, scaled flow distributions, symmetric cumulants, event-plane correlations, and differential observables to isolate sensitivity to small-scale structure from sensitivity to global eccentricities.

## Key numbers

- Smoothing parameter λ varied from 0.3 to 1 fm, the typical range of nucleonic inhomogeneities
- For NeXus events in the 20-25% centrality bin, the average radius increased by approximately 2.5% when λ changed from 0 to 1 fm, with eccentricities decreasing by roughly n × 2.5%
- The cubic spline filter uses a weighting function W(ξ) = (10/7πλ²) × f(ξ) where f(ξ) = 1 - 3/2 ξ² + 3/4 ξ³ for 0 ≤ ξ < 1 and f(ξ) = 1/4 (2-ξ)³ for 1 ≤ ξ ≤ 2

## Context

This work extends previous studies that used Gaussians to smooth small-scale fluctuations (Petersen 2010, Bhalerao 2011) and cubic splines (Noronha-Hostler 2015) by systematically comparing four initial condition models and a wide range of observables. The results show that most standard observables are insensitive to hot spot size, but the flow factorization ratio and principal component analysis of two-particle correlations offer a path to probe the poorly understood short-scale dynamics of the initial stages of heavy-ion collisions.
