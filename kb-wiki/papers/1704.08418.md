# Extension of the Bjorken energy density formula of the initial state for relativistic heavy ion collisions

> For relativistic heavy ion collisions, the Bjorken formula is very useful for estimating the initial energy density once an initial time $τ_0$ is specified. However, it cannot be trusted at low energies, e.g. well below $\sqrt{s_{_{\rm NN}}} \approx 50$ GeV for central Au+Au collisions, when $τ_0$ is smaller than the finite time it takes for the two nuclei to cross each other. Here I extend the Bjorken formula by including the finite time duration of the initial energy production. Analytical solutions for the formed energy density in the central spacetime-rapidity region are derived for several time profiles. Compared to the Bjorken formula at low energies, the maximum energy density reached is much lower, increases much faster with the collision energy, and is much less sensitive to the uncertainty of the formation time, while the energy density time evolution is much longer. Comparisons with results from a multi-phase transport confirm the key features of these solutions. The effect of the finite longitudinal width of the initial energy production, which is neglected in the analytical results, is investigated with the transport model and shown to be small. This work thus provides a general model for the initial energy production of relativistic heavy ion collisions that is also valid at low energies.

# Extension of the Bjorken energy density formula of the initial state for relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim

The Bjorken formula for initial energy density in relativistic heavy ion collisions cannot be trusted at low energies (well below sqrt(s_NN) ≈ 50 GeV for central Au+Au collisions) because it neglects the finite time it takes for the two nuclei to cross each other; this work extends the formula by including the finite time duration of initial energy production, making it valid at low energies where the original formula breaks down.

## Method

The author derives analytical solutions for the formed energy density in the central spacetime-rapidity region by assuming specific time profiles for the initial energy production over the finite crossing time dt = 2R_A/sinh(y_CM). Three time profiles are considered: uniform, beta distribution (with power n=4 to match transport model results), and triangular. The analytical results are compared with the string melting AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model, which includes finite nuclear thickness, partonic scatterings, quark coalescence, and hadronic scatterings.

## Key numbers

- The crossing time dt ≈ 0.5 fm/c at sqrt(s_NN) ≈ 50 GeV for central Au+Au collisions, which is the threshold below which the Bjorken formula cannot be trusted for tau_0 = 0.5 fm/c
- For central Au+Au at 4.84 GeV with tau_f = 0.1 to 0.9 fm/c, the maximum energy density from the extended formula changes by a factor of 2.1 (uniform profile) or 2.5 (beta profile), while the Bjorken formula changes by a factor of 9
- At 200 GeV, the crossing time dt ≈ 0.12 fm/c, making the extended results much closer to the Bjorken formula
- The maximum energy density ratio emax/epsilon_Bj(tau_f) = (tau_f/ttwoone) * ln(1 + ttwoone/tau_f) for the uniform profile, showing emax is always smaller than the Bjorken value
- The nuclear transverse area is taken as A_T = pi * R_A^2 with R_A = 1.12 * A^(1/3) fm for A=197
- The mid-rapidity dE_T/dy parameterization is 0.456 * N_part * ln(sqrt(s_NN)/2.35) for sqrt(s_NN) > 2.35 GeV

## Context

This work extends the Bjorken formula (Bjorken, 1983) which is widely used to estimate initial energy density in relativistic heavy ion collisions but assumes instantaneous energy production. The finite crossing time was previously noted by Kajantie (1983) and Adcox (2005) as a limitation. The analytical solutions are validated against the AMPT model (Lin, 2005) and provide a tool for modeling initial energy production in low-energy collisions such as those in the Beam Energy Scan at RHIC, where the relationship between energy density evolution and the QCD critical point becomes important (Stephanov, 2011; Bialas, 2016). The results can also inform hydrodynamic models that include energy source terms with finite time duration (Okai, 2017; Shen, 2017).
