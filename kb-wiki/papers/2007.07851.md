# Extracting the temperature dependence in high-$p_\perp$ particle energy loss

> The suppression of high-$p_\perp$ particles is one of the main signatures of parton energy loss during its passing through the QGP medium, and is reasonably reproduced by different theoretical models. However, a decisive test of the reliability of a certain energy loss mechanism, apart from its path-length, is its temperature dependence. Despite its importance and comprehensive dedicated studies, this issue is still awaiting for more stringent constraints. To this end, we here propose a novel observable to extract temperature dependence exponent of high-$p_{\perp}$ particle's energy loss, based on $R_{AA}$. More importantly, by combining analytical arguments, full-fledged numerical calculations and comparison with experimental data, we argue that this observable is highly suited for testing (and rejecting) the long-standing $ΔE/E \propto L^2 T^3$ paradigm. The anticipated significant reduction of experimental errors will allow direct extraction of temperature dependence, by considering different centrality pair in $A + A$ collisions (irrespective of the nucleus size) in high-$p_\perp$ region. Overall, our results imply that this observable, which reflects the underlying energy loss mechanism, is very important to distinguish between different theoretical models.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new observable, the temperature-dependent suppression ratio \(R^T_{AA}\), to extract the temperature dependence exponent of high-\(p_\perp\) particle energy loss in heavy-ion collisions, arguing that the long-standing \(\Delta E/E \propto L^2 T^3\) paradigm is inadequate and that the actual temperature dependence is close to linear (\(a \approx 1.2\)) rather than cubic, though the authors acknowledge that current experimental error bars prevent direct extraction from data and that the method relies on asymptotic scaling behavior at very high \(p_\perp \approx 100\) GeV.

## Method

The authors use their DREENA numerical framework, which implements a dynamical energy loss formalism including both radiative and collisional contributions computed within finite-temperature field theory with hard-thermal-loop resummation, running coupling, and finite magnetic mass. They define \(R^T_{AA}\) as the ratio of \((1-R_{AA})\) for two different centrality classes, derive scaling relations showing that this quantity depends only on temperature ratio at high \(p_\perp\) when path-length and temperature are linearly correlated, and extract the temperature exponent \(a\) from linear fits of \(\ln(R^T_{AA})\) versus \(\ln(T/T_{ref})\) using full-fledged numerical calculations for charged hadrons at \(p_\perp = 100\) GeV in 5.02 TeV Pb+Pb collisions.

## Key numbers

* Temperature dependence exponent extracted: \(a \approx 1.2\) for high-\(p_\perp\) particle energy loss at \(p_\perp = 100\) GeV in 5.02 TeV Pb+Pb collisions, obtained from linear fit yielding \(f = a + kb = 3.79 \sim 4\) with \(b \approx 1.4\) and \(k \approx 1.86\).
* Path-length dependence exponent: \(b \approx 1.4\), as previously estimated in NewObserv.
* Slope coefficient for \(\ln(L/L_{ref})\) vs. \(\ln(T/T_{ref})\) in constant temperature medium: \(k \approx 1.86\).
* Slope coefficient for \(\ln(L/L_{ref})\) vs. \(\ln(T_0/T_{0,ref})\) in evolving (Bjorken 1D) medium: \(\kappa \approx 1.3\).
* In evolving medium, the predicted slope of \(\ln(R^T_{AA})\) vs. \(\ln(L/L_{ref})\) is 1.93, which matches the full-fledged DREENA-B calculation result exactly.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies on path-length dependence (NewObserv) and the near-linear temperature dependence of dynamical energy loss (b, 2015) by proposing a practical observable for experimental extraction. It directly challenges the widely used \(\Delta E/E \propto L^2 T^3\) paradigm from BDMPS, ASW, GLV, AMY, and HT radiative energy loss models, as well as the \(T^2\) dependence commonly assumed for collisional energy loss from Bjorken (1982), Thoma and Gyulassy (1991), and Braaten and Thoma (1991). The results enable future direct extraction of the temperature dependence exponent from high-luminosity LHC Run 3 data with reduced error bars, and provide a discriminative tool for distinguishing between different energy loss models across various colliding systems (Pb+Pb, Xe+Xe, Kr+Kr, Ar+Ar, O+O).
