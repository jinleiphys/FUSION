# New magic numbers

> The nuclear shell model is a benchmark for the description of the structure of atomic nuclei. The magic numbers associated with closed shells have long been assumed to be valid across the whole nuclear chart. Investigations in recent years of nuclei far away from nuclear stability at facilities for radioactive ion beams have revealed that the magic numbers may change locally in those exotic nuclei leading to the disappearance of classic shell gaps and the appearance of new magic numbers. These changes in shell structure also have important implications for the synthesis of heavy elements in stars and stellar explosions. In this review a brief overview of the basics of the nuclear shell model will be given together with a summary of recent theoretical and experimental activities investigating these changes in the nuclear shell structure.

## Key claim
The classic magic numbers (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) are not universal across the nuclear chart; in exotic nuclei far from stability, these shell gaps can disappear and new magic numbers can appear locally, driven primarily by the monopole component of the tensor force between protons and neutrons.

## Method
This is a review paper that summarizes the theoretical foundations of the nuclear shell model, including the independent particle model and the interacting shell model, and explains how effective single-particle energies (ESPEs) are modified by the monopole component of the residual interaction. It then surveys experimental techniques for probing shell closures in exotic nuclei, mass measurements via Penning traps and storage rings, and spectroscopy of excited states (especially the first 2+ state in even-even nuclei) using gamma-ray detection following radioactive decay, Coulomb excitation, or inelastic scattering at radioactive beam facilities.

## Key numbers
- Classic magic numbers: 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126 (from the shell model with spin-orbit coupling, Mayer and Haxel, Suess and Jensen, 1949).
- Neutron separation energy jumps are clearly visible at N=8 in oxygen, N=20 and N=28 in calcium, and N=126 in lead (from AME2003 mass data).
- The first excited 2+ state in doubly magic nuclei lies at several MeV; in nuclei with one closed shell it lies at about 1–1.2 MeV; in mid-shell collective nuclei it lies at about 0.05–0.4 MeV.
- The r-process path follows a constant neutron separation energy given by S_n (MeV) = (T_9/5.04) * (34.075 - log n_n + 1.5 log T_9), where T_9 is temperature in 10^9 K and n_n is neutron density in cm^{-3}.
- The broad r-process abundance peaks occur at masses A=130 and A=195.

## Context
This review extends the classic shell-model picture established by Mayer, Haxel, Suess, and Jensen (1949) by incorporating the monopole effect of the tensor force, as emphasized by Otsuka and collaborators (2001, 2005, 2006, 2010), which explains the evolution of shell structure in exotic nuclei. The work connects to nucleosynthesis studies: the persistence or quenching of shell gaps at N=82 and N=126 in neutron-rich nuclei directly affects r-process abundance calculations (Che95, Pfe97, Pfe01). It enables future experiments at facilities such as FAIR (GSI) and FRIB (MSU) to map shell evolution toward the neutron drip line and to refine predictions for the astrophysical r-process site.
