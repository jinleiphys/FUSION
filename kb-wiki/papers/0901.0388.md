# Universality in QCD and Halo Nuclei

> Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a powerful framework to exploit a separation of scales in order to perform systematically improvable, model-independent calculations. We apply this method to strongly interacting quantum systems with short-range interactions and large scattering length. Such systems display remarkable universal properties which include a geometric spectrum of shallow three-body states called "Efimov states" and log-periodic dependence of scattering observables on the scattering length. We review the EFT for large scattering length and some of its applications in the physics of cold atoms and nuclear physics. In particular, we discuss the possibility of an infrared limit cycle in QCD and the extension of the EFT to halo nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that Effective Field Theory (EFT) for systems with large scattering length provides a universal description of few-body phenomena across cold atoms, nuclear physics, and potentially QCD, but the existence of an infrared limit cycle in QCD, where the triton would display the Efimov effect, can only be confirmed by directly solving QCD, such as through tuned lattice simulations.

## Method

The authors review the EFT for few-body systems with large S-wave scattering length, deriving the two-body scattering amplitude by summing bubble diagrams with a contact interaction and solving an integral equation for particle-dimer scattering that includes a three-body contact interaction regulated by an ultraviolet cutoff. They apply this universal EFT to predict correlations among three-body observables (e.g., the Phillips line for the triton and helium trimers), to study the triton spectrum near a critical pion mass in chiral EFT, and to extend the framework to halo nuclei by treating the core as structureless and including Coulomb interactions perturbatively.

## Key numbers

- The transcendental number \(s_0 = 1.00624\) governs the log-periodic behavior of the three-body contact interaction \(H(\Lambda)\), which returns to its original value when the cutoff \(\Lambda\) is increased by a factor of \(\exp(\pi/s_0) \approx 22.7\).
- The critical pion mass for the divergence of the spin-triplet nucleon-nucleon scattering length is in the range 170 MeV \(< M_\pi <\) 210 MeV, with one specific solution using \(\mpic = 197.8577\) MeV.
- For the Borromean halo nucleus \(^{14}\)Be, the leading-order halo EFT result for the neutron-neutron radius is \(\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 4.1 \pm 0.5\) fm, compared to the experimental value \(\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle_{exp}} = 5.4 \pm 1.0\) fm from Marques (2001).

## Context

This work extends the universal EFT framework developed by Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck (1999) for three-body systems with large scattering length, which itself confirmed and extended the Efimov effect predicted by Efimov (1971). The paper applies this EFT to nuclear physics, including chiral EFT studies of the quark mass dependence of nuclear forces by Epelbaum (2006) and Hammer (2007), and to halo nuclei following the EFT approach of Bertulani (2002) and Bedaque (2003). It enables future lattice QCD simulations to test whether tuning quark masses can realize an infrared limit cycle, and it provides a systematic method for calculating structure and radii of halo nuclei such as \(^{20}\)C and \(^{14}\)Be, with upcoming experiments expected to test predictions of universal behavior and excited Efimov states.
