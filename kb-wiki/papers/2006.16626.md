# Real time warm pions from the lattice using an effective theory

> Lattice measurements provide adequate information to fix the parameters of long distance effective field theories in Euclidean time. Using such a theory, we examine the analytic continuation of long distance correlation functions of composite operators at finite temperature from Euclidean to Minkowski space time. We show through an explicit computation that the analytic continuation of the pion correlation function is possible and gives rise to non-trivial effects. Among them is the possibility, supported by lattice computations of Euclidean correlators, that long distance excitations can be understood in terms of (very massive) pions even at temperatures higher than the QCD cross over temperature.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that an effective field theory (EFT) with parameters fixed by Euclidean lattice data can be analytically continued to real time, yielding non-trivial pion correlation functions; the key hedge is that this hadronic description may remain valid for long-distance excitations even at temperatures slightly above the QCD crossover temperature, but only as long as the pion remains the lightest mode around the symmetry-broken minimum.

## Method

The authors construct a Minkowski-space EFT of self-interacting quarks with a UV cutoff \(T_0\), matching its one-loop axial current correlators to lattice measurements. They then introduce pion fields via a Hubbard-Stratanovich transformation, derive a pion EFT Lagrangian with terms up to dimension 4, and compute the pion pole mass, screening mass, and kinetic mass at one-loop order and with Dyson-Schwinger resummation, using \(\overline{\text{MS}}\) regularization.

## Key numbers

- The pion pole mass at finite temperature is smaller than the zero-temperature pion mass; for example, in the warm region near \(T_{co}\) the pole mass differs significantly from \(m_\pi(T=0)\) (see Figure 3).
- The screening mass \(M_\pi = m_\pi / \sqrt{c^4}\) computed at one loop agrees with lattice measurements from Brandt et al. even at temperatures a little above \(T_{co}\) (see Figure 4).
- The kinetic mass is given by \(M_K = m_\pi / c^4\); its one-loop values are shown in Figure 4.
- The one-loop self-energy correction to the pion mass squared is \(\delta m_\pi^2 = \frac{c^{41}}{2(c^4)^{3/2}}(1+N_f^2) \left[ \frac{\mathfrak{m}^2}{16\pi^2}\left\{\log\left(\frac{\mathfrak{m}^2}{c^4 M^2}\right)-1\right\} + \int\frac{d^3k}{E_k(2\pi)^3} n_B(E_k) \right]\), where \(\mathfrak{m}^2 = c^2 T_0^2\) and \(E_k = \sqrt{k^2 + \mathfrak{m}^2}\).

## Context

This work extends the Euclidean EFT approach of Gupta and Sharma (2017) to real-time pion properties, addressing the long-standing problem of analytic continuation from Euclidean to Minkowski space at finite temperature. It contrasts with earlier computations in chiral perturbation theory (Gasser and Leutwyler, 1986; Toublan, 1997) and the NJL model (Hatsuda and Kunihiro, 1986; Lutz et al., 1992), which found the pion pole mass rising with temperature; here the pole mass is smaller than at zero temperature due to the breaking of Lorentz invariance captured by the parameter \(c^4\). The results enable future two-loop computations that will reveal non-trivial spectral functions and momentum-dependent corrections to couplings.
