# Limit distributions of many-particle spectra

> We find the limit distributions for a spectrum of a system of n particles governed by a k-body interaction. The hamiltonian of this system is modelled by a Gaussian random matrix. We show that the limit distribution is a q-deformed Gaussian distribution with the deformation parameter q depending on the value of the fraction \frac{k}{\sqrt{n}}. The family of q-deformed Gaussian distributions include the Gaussian distribution and the semicircular law; therefore our result is a generalization of the results of Wigner, Mon and French.

## Key claim
The limit distribution of eigenvalues for a system of n particles governed by a k-body interaction, modeled by a Gaussian random matrix, is a q-deformed Gaussian distribution whose deformation parameter q depends on the limit of the fraction k/√n, interpolating between the Gaussian distribution (constant k) and the semicircular law (k = n).

## Method
The Hamiltonian is constructed as a sum over all k-particle subsets of independent Gaussian random matrices acting on the corresponding k-particle Hilbert spaces, with each particle having s possible states. The author proves a theorem showing that when the limit of k(n)/√n equals a constant c (0 ≤ c ≤ ∞), the eigenvalue distribution converges to a q-deformed Gaussian distribution, with the deformation parameter q given by an explicit function of c and s.

## Key numbers
- The deformation parameter q is given by q = exp[-(1 - 1/s²) c²], where c = lim_{n→∞} k(n)/√n and s is the number of one-particle states.
- For fermions with s → ∞ faster than linearly, the formula simplifies to q = e^{-c²}.
- The q-deformed Gaussian distribution is supported on the interval [-2/√(1-q), 2/√(1-q)].
- For q = 0, the distribution coincides with the semicircular law (Wigner's result); for q = 1, it coincides with the standard Gaussian distribution (Mon and French result).

## Context
This work generalizes the classical results of Wigner (1950s), who found the semicircular law for n-body interactions (k = n), and Mon and French (1975), who found the Gaussian distribution for constant k as n → ∞. The paper shows that the square root of the number of particles sets the scale for the transition between these two regimes, and it opens the question of extending the analysis to fermionic or bosonic systems where the Pauli exclusion principle may modify the results.
