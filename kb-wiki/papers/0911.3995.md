# The efficacy of isotope thermometry: examining in the S-matrix approach

> Isotope thermometry, widely used to measure the temperature of a hot nuclear system formed in energetic nuclear collisions, is examined in the light of S-matrix approach to the nuclear equation of state of disassembled nuclear matter. Scattering between produced light fragment pairs, hitherto neglected, is seen to have an important bearing on the extraction of system temperature and volume at freeze-out from isotope thermometry. Taking due care of the scattering effects and decay of the primary fragments, a more reliable way to extract the nuclear thermodynamic parameters is suggested by exploiting least-squares fit to the observed fragment multiplicities.

# The efficacy of isotope thermometry: examining in the S-matrix approach

## Key claim

Isotope thermometry, as commonly applied to extract the temperature of hot nuclear systems formed in energetic collisions, systematically yields incorrect temperatures and volumes because it neglects strong interaction scattering between light fragment pairs; the authors show that including these scattering corrections, along with proper treatment of secondary decay, is necessary to recover the true freeze-out temperature and volume, and propose a least-squares fitting method as a more reliable alternative to the standard double-isotope ratio technique.

## Method

The authors perform a numerical experiment using the S-matrix approach to the grand partition function of disassembled nuclear matter, calculating primary fragment yields for the system ¹²⁴Sn at a given freeze-out temperature and volume, then applying secondary decay corrections via the Weisskopf-Ewing model to generate "observed" numerical data. They extract apparent temperatures and volumes using the standard double-isotope ratio method (with four fragments) under successive approximations: the ideal Albergo condition, gamma-decay correction, gamma-plus-particle-decay correction, and finally including scattering corrections from elastic channels (NN, Nt, N³He, Nα, and αα). They then compare these results to a proposed least-squares fit method applied to sets of 6 or 13 fragment species.

## Key numbers

- The gamma-decay corrected temperature T_γ underestimates the freeze-out temperature T_fz considerably; inclusion of particle-decay narrows the gap significantly, particularly for the broader set of fragment species.
- For the least-squares method with n_iso=13 (including ¹⁶O, ¹⁴N, ¹²C, ¹⁰B, ⁹Be, ⁷Li, ⁶Li, plus the light set), the uncertainty in the (gamma+p)-corrected volume is at most 25% and that for temperature is at most 5%.
- Calculations were performed at freeze-out volumes of 4V₀, 6V₀, and 8V₀ (where V₀ is the normal volume of ¹²⁴Sn); the conclusions do not change for this range.
- The ideal Albergo temperature T_alb is sensitive to the choice of thermometer (He-Li vs He-Be), while the other corrected temperatures are not very sensitive to thermometer choice.
- Scattering has a comparatively more significant role in volume determination than in temperature determination; its inclusion collapses the apparent volumes V_app/V_fz to unity.

## Context

This work extends the S-matrix approach to nuclear equation of state developed by Dashen, Ma, and Bernstein (1969) and applied to nuclear systems by Horowitz and Schwenk (2006) and Mallik et al. (2007). It directly addresses the standard double-isotope ratio thermometry method of Albergo et al. (1985), which assumes noninteracting fragments in their ground states, and the subsequent decay-correction work of Kolomiets et al. (1997). The authors' previous schematic calculation (Samaddar et al., 2008) in dilute infinite nuclear matter neglecting secondary decay found that scattering effects on extracted temperature and volumes are not negligible; the present work incorporates both secondary decay and scattering in a finite system. The proposed least-squares fit method enables extraction of temperature, volume, and neutron and proton chemical potentials, from which entropy can be determined as a signature for the liquid-gas type phase transition in hot finite nuclear matter.
