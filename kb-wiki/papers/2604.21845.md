# Multi-Nucleon Transfer Reactions and the Creation and the Evolution of the Compound Nucleus

> There is no microscopic quantum approach based on the many-body time-dependent Schrödinger equation which capable to describe the formation and the evolution of a compound nucleus. The most advanced microscopic approach developed so far to describe multi-nucleon transfer (MNT) reactions in complex nuclear systems (with total number of nucleons $\gg 100$) is the time-dependent Hartree Fock (TDHF) mean field theory. In any mean field approach, however, the mean field is an expectation value of a quantum operator, thus classical in nature and unable to describe its quantum fluctuations, which are often expected to be crucial. Quantum fluctuations can be in principle be included in a configuration interaction (CI) framework, which in the case of reactions has to be implemented in the continuum. Here we describe the first such implementation within a novel extension of the well known Generator Coordinate Method (GCM), dubbed the enhanced GCM (eGCM), applied to the MNT reaction $^{48}$Ca+$^{208}$Pb near the Coulomb barrier, which demonstrates major qualitative differences with either TDHF or GCM previous approaches.

## Key claim

The enhanced Generator Coordinate Method (eGCM) is the first microscopic quantum approach based on the many-body time-dependent Schrodinger equation capable of describing multi-nucleon transfer (MNT) reactions and compound nucleus formation, applied here to the collision of 48Ca+208Pb near the Coulomb barrier. The authors demonstrate that eGCM produces major qualitative differences from both TDHF and previous GCM approaches, specifically showing the formation of a long-lived compound nucleus with Z=102 and N=154 that was never observed in any prior TDHF simulation.

## Method

The authors performed TDDFT simulations (without pairing) of the 48Ca+208Pb collision at a center-of-mass energy of 235 MeV, using the SeaLL1 energy density functional in a 64^3 simulation box with 1 fm lattice spacing. They stored single-particle wave functions from 152 impact parameters within a ring of radius 5-6 fm in the impact parameter plane, at 385 time steps with spacing 6.4 fm/c, requiring about 80 TB of storage. The eGCM Hamiltonian matrix of dimension 39,630 was constructed using 48,000 GPUs on the Frontier supercomputer, and particle number projection in the final state used 56,000 GPUs.

## Key numbers

- Center-of-mass energy: 235 MeV for the 48Ca+208Pb collision, slightly above the Coulomb barrier
- Number of impact parameters in the ring: N_b = 152, with impact parameters between 5 and 6 fm
- Number of time steps stored: N_tau = 385 with step Delta_tau = 6.4 fm/c (also checked with 3.2 fm/c)
- Size of the eGCM matrix: 39,630, described as "arguably one of the largest basis sets ever used in a nuclear GCM calculation"
- Total energy of initial state: E_tot = -1817.5 MeV with standard deviation Delta_E = 27.9 MeV
- Initial relative velocity: v_rel = 0.113 c
- Contact time in TDHF: t_c = 162 fm/c; separation times: t_s = 2,262 fm/c for b=5 fm and t_s = 775 fm/c for b=6 fm
- Compound nucleus 256_102No_154 formed with probability P_H(154,102) = 0.34
- Excitation energy of compound nucleus: E_ex approximately 78 MeV
- Average level spacing in eGCM spectrum: approximately 4 keV
- Standard deviation of normalized level spacing distribution: 0.5273, compared to GOE value of 0.5227
- Average proton number in heavy fragment: 97.6 +/- 7.3; average neutron number: 147.4 +/- 10.2

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) and time-dependent Density Functional Theory (TDDFT) approaches developed by Simenel (2010, 2025), Sekizawa (2013, 2016), and Wu (2026), which are mean-field methods that cannot describe quantum fluctuations. The authors challenge the earlier GCM framework of Peierls and Griffin (1957) and Reinhard (1983), showing that the Gaussian overlap approximation (GOA) used in time-dependent GCM is inadequate. The eGCM approach enables the first microscopic description of compound nucleus formation as originally conjectured by Bohr (1936), with the spectrum showing Wigner-Dyson statistics consistent with random matrix theory predictions from Mehta (1991), Horoi (1995), and Zelevinsky (1996). Future work requires extending eGCM to describe fission and quasi-fission of the created compound nucleus, using theoretical tools recently developed in TDDFT.
