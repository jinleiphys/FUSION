# Phase transitions in neutron stars and their links to gravitational waves

> The recent direct observation of gravitational wave event $GW170817$ and its $GRB170817A$ signal has opened up a new window to study neutron stars and heralds a new era of Astronomy referred to as the Multimessenger Astronomy. Both gravitational and electromagnetic waves from a single astrophysical source have been detected for the first time. This combined detection offers an unprecedented opportunity to place constraints on the neutron star matter equation of state. The existence of a possible hadron-quark phase transition in the central regions of neutron stars is associated with the appearance of g-modes, which are extremely important as they could signal the presence of a pure quark matter core in the centers of neutron stars. Observations of g-modes with frequencies between 1 kHz and 1.5 kHz could be interpreted as evidence of a sharp hadron-quark phase transition in the cores of neutron stars. In this article, we shall review the description of the dense matter composing neutron stars, the determination of the equation of state of such matter, and the constraints imposed by astrophysical observations of these fascinating compact objects.

## Key claim

Observations of gravitational-wave g-modes with frequencies between 1 kHz and 1.5 kHz could be interpreted as evidence of a sharp hadron-quark phase transition in the cores of neutron stars, but this interpretation depends on the hadron-quark surface tension being sufficiently high (greater than about 70 MeV/fm^2) to produce a discontinuous equation of state and a sharp interface between phases.

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of hybrid equations of state for neutron star matter by combining a hadronic phase described by density-dependent relativistic mean-field (DDRMF) models (specifically GM1L and DD2 parametrizations) with a quark phase described by non-local Polyakov-Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) models and the Field Correlator Method. The authors discuss how the hadron-quark phase transition can be treated either as a Maxwell construction (sharp interface, for surface tension greater than about 70 MeV/fm^2) or a Gibbs construction (mixed phase, for lower surface tensions), and how these different treatments affect the predicted oscillation modes and gravitational wave signatures of neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density n0 = 0.16 fm^-3, with specific parametrizations giving n0 = 0.153 fm^-3 (GM1L) and n0 = 0.149 fm^-3 (DD2)
- Binding energy per nucleon at saturation: -16.30 MeV (GM1L) and -16.02 MeV (DD2)
- Nuclear incompressibility K0 = 300.0 MeV (GM1L) and 242.7 MeV (DD2)
- Isospin asymmetry energy J = 32.5 MeV (GM1L) and 32.8 MeV (DD2)
- Slope parameter L0 = 55.0 MeV (GM1L) and 55.3 MeV (DD2)
- Effective mass m*/m_N = 0.70 (GM1L) and 0.56 (DD2)
- Critical temperature for deconfinement at zero chemical potential T_c(mu=0) around 155 MeV from lattice QCD extrapolations
- Hadron-quark surface tension sigma_HQ less than about 30 MeV/fm^2 from recent works, with suggestions that values greater than 100 MeV/fm^2 could be possible
- Threshold for sharp phase transition: sigma_HQ greater than about 70 MeV/fm^2
- Pulsar PSR J1748-2446ad rotates at 716 Hz
- Surface magnetic fields of magnetars: 10^14-10^15 Gauss
- Quark chemical potentials inside neutron stars at most on the order of 500 MeV
- Temperature of cold catalyzed neutron star matter: T less than or equal to 1 MeV (about 10^10 K)

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework for connecting neutron star interior composition to gravitational wave observations following the landmark detection of GW170817 and its electromagnetic counterpart GRB170817A by LIGO and Virgo (2017). The work builds on prior studies of hybrid stars using NJL models (Orsaria 2012, 2013; Ranea-Sandoval 2015, 2017) and DDRMF parametrizations (Typel 2009; Spinella 2017), and discusses how future gravitational wave detections of non-radial oscillation modes could distinguish between different phase transition scenarios. The paper enables the interpretation of upcoming multimessenger observations to constrain the equation of state of dense matter and determine whether pure quark matter cores exist in neutron stars.
