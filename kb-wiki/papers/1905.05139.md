# Flow in AA and pA as an interplay of fluid-like and non-fluid like excitations

> To study the microscopic structure of quark-gluon plasma, data from hadronic collisions must be confronted with models that go beyond fluid dynamics. Here, we study a simple kinetic theory model that encompasses fluid dynamics but contains also particle-like excitations in a boost invariant setting with no symmetries in the transverse plane and with large initial momentum asymmetries. We determine the relative weight of fluid dynamical and particle like excitations as a function of system size and energy density by comparing kinetic transport to results from the 0th, 1st and 2nd order gradient expansion of viscous fluid dynamics. We then confront this kinetic theory with data on azimuthal flow coefficients over a wide centrality range in PbPb collisions at the LHC, in AuAu collisions at RHIC, and in pPb collisions at the LHC. Evidence is presented that non-hydrodynamic excitations make the dominant contribution to collective flow signals in pPb collisions at the LHC and contribute significantly to flow in peripheral nucleus-nucleus collisions, while fluid-like excitations dominate collectivity in central nucleus-nucleus collisions at collider energies.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that non-hydrodynamic (particle-like) excitations dominate collective flow signals in pPb collisions at the LHC and contribute significantly to peripheral nucleus-nucleus collisions, while fluid-like excitations dominate only in central nucleus-nucleus collisions at collider energies, with the relative weight determined by system size and energy density.

## Method
The authors solve a kinetic transport equation in the isotropization-time approximation (ITA) with boost-invariant geometry and broken transverse translational symmetry, using initial conditions with large momentum asymmetries and azimuthal symmetry broken by linear perturbations. They compare the resulting energy-momentum tensor to the 0th, 1st, and 2nd order gradient expansions of viscous fluid dynamics, quantifying deviations as a function of the single model parameter, the opacity \(\hat\gamma\). The kinetic theory results are then confronted with experimental data on azimuthal flow coefficients \(v_n\) across a wide centrality range in PbPb collisions at the LHC, AuAu collisions at RHIC, and pPb collisions at the LHC.

## Key numbers
- The opacity parameter \(\hat\gamma = R^{3/4}\gamma (\varepsilon_0 \tau_0)^{1/4}\) is the single physically meaningful model parameter, linearly related to \(R/\tau_{\rm iso}\) for \(\hat\gamma \ll 1\) and proportional to \(\hat\gamma^{8/9}\) in opaque systems.
- The shear viscosity \(\eta = \frac{1}{5} \frac{\varepsilon + p}{\gamma \varepsilon^{1/4}}\) and second-order coefficients \(\tau_{\Pi} = \frac{5\eta}{(\varepsilon+p)}\) and \(\lambda_1 = \frac{27}{5} \frac{\eta^2}{(\varepsilon+p)}\) are analytically known for the ITA model.
- The Woods-Saxon profile uses \(R_{\rm WS} = 6.49\) fm and \(\delta_{\rm WS} = 0.54\) fm for Pb nuclei, with \(c_{\rm rms} \approx 4.42\) for proper normalization.
- The quality measure \(Q_2(t,r)\) compares kinetic theory to second-order hydrodynamics, normalized to the local energy density via \(T_{\rm id}^{\mu\nu} {T_{\rm id}}_{\mu\nu} = \frac{4}{3} \varepsilon(t,r)\).

## Context
This work extends the study of hydrodynamization from simplified 1+1 dimensional analytic setups to a problem with sufficient complexity to inform phenomenological practice, building on prior kinetic theory solutions by Kurkela et al. (2018) that first broke transverse translational symmetry. The ITA model reproduces QCD weak coupling effective kinetic theory within approximately 15% as shown in earlier work by Kurkela et al. (2018), and the authors compare their results to the extensive experimental measurements of azimuthal asymmetries by ALICE (2011), CMS (2015, 2017), and ATLAS (2017) collaborations. The approach enables systematic discrimination between fluid-like and particle-like contributions to collectivity across system sizes, addressing the central question of whether small collision systems form transient fluid droplets or exhibit other mechanisms for collective behavior.
