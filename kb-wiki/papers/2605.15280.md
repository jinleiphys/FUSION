# Sivers Tomography from Charge and Angle Only

> We propose a one-point charge-correlator (OPCC) probe of the Sivers effect in back-to-back deep-inelastic scattering. This measurement uses only the signs and directions of charged tracks, with no calorimetric or particle-identification information required. The observable weights the final state by its electric charge and measures the azimuthal correlation between the charge flow and the transverse spin of the proton. This probe is shown to be IRC finite and admits a factorization involving the usual Sivers distribution and a perturbatively calculable charge-weighted jet function for small transverse seperation $b\ll Λ_{\rm QCD}^{-1}$, with no reliance on non-perturbative fragmentation functions or track functions due to charge conservation. We validate the factorization against the full fixed-order QCD and present resummed predictions at N\(^3\)LL accuracy for the unpolarized distribution and N\(^2\)LL for the Sivers asymmetry. The OPCC provides a theoretically clean and simple experimental measurement, and establishes a charge-and-angle measurement paradigm for spin physics at a future Electron-Ion Collider.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a one-point charge-correlator (OPCC) measurement in back-to-back deep-inelastic scattering provides a theoretically clean and experimentally simple probe of the Sivers effect, requiring only the signs and directions of charged tracks with no calorimetric or particle-identification information. The claim is conditioned on the back-to-back region (theta approaching pi in the Breit frame) and on small transverse separation b much less than 1/Lambda_QCD, where the observable becomes infrared-and-collinear (IRC) finite and admits a TMD factorization with a perturbatively calculable charge-weighted jet function.

## Method

The authors derive a TMD factorization theorem for the OPCC in transversely polarized DIS using the soft-collinear effective theory (SCET) framework, showing that the charge-weighted cross section factorizes into a hard function, a perturbatively calculable charge-weighted jet function, the standard unpolarized TMD or Sivers distribution, and a soft function. They validate the factorization by comparing the leading singular contributions from the factorization theorem against full fixed-order QCD calculations at both LO (O(alpha_s)) and NLO (O(alpha_s^2)) using the distress code. They then present resummed predictions at N^3LL accuracy for the unpolarized distribution and N^2LL accuracy for the Sivers asymmetry, implementing the matching of the Sivers function onto the Qiu-Sterman twist-three collinear quark-gluon correlation function at NLO.

## Key numbers

- Resummed predictions for the unpolarized OPCC distribution F_UU are presented at NLL, N^2LL, and N^3LL accuracy for EIC kinematics, with scale uncertainties estimated by varying mu and nu by a factor of 2.
- The non-perturbative TMD evolution kernel parameters used are: g_2 = 0.84, Q_0 = 1.55 GeV, b_max = 1.5 GeV^{-1}, with g_1 = 0.106 for the unpolarized TMD distribution and g_1 = 0.14 for the jet function.
- The Sivers asymmetry A^Sivers = F_UT/F_UU is predicted at N^2LL accuracy for EIC kinematics, with the Qiu-Sterman function parameterized at mu_0 = sqrt(1.9) GeV using parameters fitted in Echevarria (2020).
- The Sivers function matching coefficient at NLO includes a term (1 - alpha_s C_F/(2 pi) * pi^2/12) delta(1-x_1) delta(1-x_2) plus a term (alpha_s/(2 pi)) (C_F - C_A/2) (1-x_1) delta(1-x_2/x_1).

## Context

This work extends the recent application of energy correlators to spin physics, particularly the nucleon energy correlator (NEC) approach of Liu (2022), Cao (2023), and Liu (2024), by replacing the energy-flow operator with the electric charge-flow operator. It builds on the analysis of the charge-charge correlator in unpolarized e+e- annihilation by Monni (2025), and connects to the standard TMD factorization framework for SIDIS. The OPCC enables a new charge-and-angle measurement paradigm for spin physics at a future Electron-Ion Collider, avoiding the major experimental uncertainties associated with hadron identification, jet reconstruction, and calorimetry that affect traditional Sivers measurements.
