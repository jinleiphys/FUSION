# Fragmentation patterns of nuclear response: low-spin giant resonances and soft modes

> Nuclear resonances provide a rich and versatile testbed for exploring fundamental aspects of physics, particularly within the domain of strongly correlated many-body systems. The overarching goal of the theory is to develop a consistent and predictive framework that is (i) capable of a spectroscopically accurate description and (ii) sufficiently general to be applied across different energy scales and transferable to a wide range of complex systems. Thoroughly capturing emergent collective phenomena that arise in nuclear media is the central challenge for the theory, which is discussed in this contribution. It concentrates on the themes inspired and influenced by Angela Bracco's research, in particular, on the fragmentation patterns of the monopole and dipole responses of medium-heavy nuclei and associated open problems.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the leading-order quasiparticle-vibration coupling (qPVC) mechanism, while essential for describing the fragmentation of giant resonances and soft modes, introduces systematic uncertainties, particularly from the treatment of pairing gaps and the adequacy of QRPA phonons, that must be resolved to achieve spectroscopic accuracy across the nuclear chart.

## Method

The author employs the relativistic nuclear field theory (RNFT) framework, solving the Bethe-Salpeter-Dyson equation (BSDE) for the particle-hole propagator with a dynamical interaction kernel that includes qPVC via correlated two-quasiparticle (phonon) configurations. The static kernel is approximated by the effective meson-exchange interaction NL3* (with nuclear incompressibility K∞ = 258 MeV), and the dynamical kernel is treated at leading order (2q⊗phonon) using the relativistic time blocking approximation (RQTBA) or its equivalent relativistic equation-of-motion method (REOM²). Monopole pairing forces are adjusted to reproduce the energies of the lowest quadrupole states in each nucleus, and a subtraction procedure is applied to avoid double-counting of qPVC contributions when using effective interactions.

## Key numbers

- The isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) centroid in ²⁰⁸Pb is well described by RQRPA alone, while in ¹²⁰Sn the RQRPA centroid is positioned "noticeably too high"; RQTBA shifts the centroids downward in ⁵⁸Ni, ⁹⁰Zr, and ¹²⁰Sn to varying degrees while leaving ²⁰⁸Pb "almost intact."
- In the nickel isotopic chain ⁵⁶–⁷⁰Ni, the RQTBA centroid shifts Δ⟨E⟩_ISGMR correlate inversely with the experimental energies of the lowest quadrupole states E(2⁺₁): for example, E(2⁺₁;²⁰⁸Pb) = 4.09 MeV versus E(2⁺₁;¹²⁰Sn) = 1.17 MeV.
- The dynamical qPVC contribution to the pairing gap is "more than 50%" of the total value, rising to 60–70% for states close to the Fermi energy, as shown for neutron orbits in a representative calculation with variable pairing strength parameter G.
- The phonon model space included RQRPA modes of natural parity up to spin L = 6 and energy 20–30 MeV, excluding modes whose B(EL) values are less than 5% of the maximal one for each spin.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic nuclear field theory approach developed by Litvinova and collaborators (Litvinova 2023, Litvinova 2022, Litvinova and Schuck 2019) and addresses the long-standing puzzle that DFT parametrizations with commonly accepted K∞ values describe the ISGMR well in ²⁰⁸Pb but overestimate its centroid in tin isotopes (Garg 2018). The paper shows that parameter-free qPVC resolves this puzzle and enables a simultaneous realistic description of ISGMR across lead, tin, zirconium, and nickel mass regions. It also connects to earlier work on the role of pairing correlations in ISGMR centroid shifts (Li 2022) and to the established qPVC effect on pairing gaps (Barranco, Broglia, Gori et al. 1999; Litvinova 2021). The results enable systematic uncertainty quantification in future ab-initio calculations and motivate the development of approaches that treat phonons beyond the QRPA level.
