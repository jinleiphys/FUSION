# Signs of Non-Monotonic Finite-Volume Corrections to $g_A$

> We study finite-volume (FV) corrections to determinations of $g_A$ via lattice quantum chromodynamics (QCD) using analytic results and numerical analysis. We observe that $SU(2)$ Heavy Baryon Chiral Perturbation Theory does not provide an unambiguous prediction for the sign of the FV correction, which is not surprising when one also considers large-$N_c$ constraints on the axial couplings. We further show that non-monotonic FV corrections are naturally allowed when one considers either including explicit $Δ$-resonance degrees of freedom or one works to higher orders in the chiral expansion. We investigate the potential impact of these FV corrections with a precision study of $g_A$ using models of FV corrections that are monotonic and non-monotonic. Using lattice QCD data that is approximately at the 1% level of precision, we do not see significant evidence of non-monotonic corrections. Looking forward to the next phase of lattice QCD calculations, we estimate that calculations that are between the 0.1%-1%-level of precision may be sensitive to these FV artifacts. Finally, we present an update of the CalLat prediction of $g_A$ in the isospin limit with sub-percent precision, $g_A^{\rm QCD} = 1.2674(96)$.

## Key claim
The sign of finite-volume (FV) corrections to the nucleon axial coupling \(g_A\) is not unambiguously predicted by \(SU(2)\) Heavy Baryon Chiral Perturbation Theory, and non-monotonic FV corrections are naturally allowed when including explicit \(\Delta\)-resonance degrees of freedom or working to higher orders in the chiral expansion; at the current \(\sim 1\%\) precision level of lattice QCD data, no significant evidence of non-monotonic corrections is seen, but calculations at \(0.1\%\)-\(1\%\) precision may become sensitive to these artifacts.

## Method
The authors studied FV corrections to \(g_A\) using analytic results from \(SU(2)\) Heavy Baryon Chiral Perturbation Theory (HB\(\chi\)PT) both without and with explicit \(\Delta\)-resonance degrees of freedom, incorporating large-\(N_c\) constraints on the axial couplings. They performed a sensitivity study by fitting lattice QCD data from the CalLat collaboration (at \(a\simeq 0.12\) fm) and the RQCD collaboration (at \(m_\pi\simeq 280\)-\(290\) MeV) to two models: one using only next-to-leading order (NLO) FV corrections (monotonic) and one including next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) FV corrections (allowing non-monotonic behavior). The fits used separate parameters for infinite-volume and FV functions, with the FV coefficients determined from the RQCD data and priored as Gaussian distributions of zero mean and unit width.

## Key numbers
- CalLat prediction of \(g_A\) in the isospin limit: \(g_A^{\rm QCD} = 1.2674(96)\) (sub-percent precision).
- For the monotonic FV model (NLO only), the predicted \(g_A\) at the physical pion mass and infinite volume is \(1.284(12)\).
- For the non-monotonic FV model (including NNLO), the predicted \(g_A\) at the physical pion mass and infinite volume is \(1.266(12)\).
- RQCD data at \(m_\pi\simeq 281\)-\(290\) MeV show FV corrections that are negative at this pion mass, with values ranging from \(g_A = 1.261(15)\) at \(m_\pi L = 5.86\) to \(g_A = 1.131(20)\) at \(m_\pi L = 3.02\).
- The mass gap to the \(\Delta\)-resonance at the physical point is \(\Delta \equiv m_\Delta - m_N \simeq 293\) MeV.

## Context
This work extends the study of FV corrections to \(g_A\) beyond the leading-order prediction from HB\(\chi\)PT without \(\Delta\) degrees of freedom (Beane 2004), which had been used by lattice groups such as PNDME (Gupta 2018), RQCD (Bali 2023), and others to parameterize FV effects with a simple exponential form. The authors show that including \(\Delta\)-resonance contributions and higher-order chiral terms, as motivated by large-\(N_c\) constraints (Dashen 1993, Jenkins 1995), can lead to non-monotonic FV corrections that change sign with pion mass. This work enables future lattice QCD calculations targeting sub-percent precision to incorporate more flexible FV models and avoid unquantified systematic uncertainties, and it provides an updated CalLat prediction for \(g_A\) that can be used in precision tests of the Standard Model, including CKM unitarity and constraints on right-handed charged currents.
