# Two Nucleons on a Lattice

> The two-nucleon sector is near an infrared fixed point of QCD and as a result the S-wave scattering lengths are unnaturally large compared to the effective ranges and shape parameters. It is usually assumed that a lattice QCD simulation of the two-nucleon sector will require a lattice that is much larger than the scattering lengths in order to extract quantitative information. In this paper we point out that this does not have to be the case: lattice QCD simulations on much smaller lattices will produce rigorous results for nuclear physics.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD simulations of the two-nucleon sector do not require lattices much larger than the scattering lengths (which are unnaturally large, e.g., -23.714 fm in the singlet channel) to extract quantitative information; instead, rigorous results can be obtained from much smaller lattices, provided the lattice is large compared to the pion Compton wavelength and the appropriate finite-volume eigenvalue equations are used.

## Method

The authors develop a finite-volume effective field theory without pions (pionless EFT, or $\nopi$) to describe two-nucleon interactions in a periodic box. They derive the exact eigenvalue equation relating the energy levels in the box to the infinite-volume scattering phase shift via the three-dimensional zeta-function, and obtain new approximate formulas for the energy levels in the limits where the lattice size is much larger or much smaller than the scattering length. They then apply these formulas to the physical $^1S_0$ and $^3S_1$ channels using the known scattering lengths and effective ranges, solving numerically for the energy eigenvalues as a function of lattice size.

## Key numbers

- Singlet ($^1S_0$) scattering length: $a^{(^1S_0)} = -23.714$ fm; effective range: $r^{(^1S_0)} = 2.734$ fm
- Triplet ($^3S_1$) scattering length: $a^{(^3S_1)} = +5.425$ fm; effective range: $r^{(^3S_1)} = 1.75$ fm
- For the singlet channel, Lüscher's power-series expansion for the ground state requires $L \gsim 80$ fm ($\sim 3 a^{(^1S_0)}$) to converge, while the first excited state requires $L \gsim 150$ fm
- The new small-volume expansions for the singlet channel are applicable for $L \lsim 50$ fm, but the assumption $L \gg r_i$ breaks down for $L \lsim 5$ fm
- For the triplet channel, Lüscher's expansion for the lowest continuum state converges when $L \gsim 15$ fm ($\sim 3 a^{(^3S_1)}$)
- The deuteron bound state can be described by $\nopi$ for lattices with $L \gsim 10$ fm; the lowest continuum state in the triplet channel requires $L \gsim 15$ fm
- At $L = 10$ fm, the singlet first-state momentum is 39.0 MeV (imaginary, bound) and the second-state momentum is 104.3 MeV (outside $\nopi$ validity); at $L = 15$ fm, the singlet first-state momentum is 24.6 MeV (imaginary) and the second-state momentum is 67.0 MeV

## Context

This work extends Lüscher's finite-volume formalism (Lüscher 1986, 1990) for extracting scattering amplitudes from lattice simulations to the two-nucleon sector, where the scattering lengths are unnaturally large due to QCD being near an infrared fixed point (Kaplan 1998, Birse). The authors show that contrary to naive expectations, simulations on lattices with $L \gsim 15$ fm can extract both scattering lengths and effective ranges, enabling future lattice QCD calculations of electroweak matrix elements between two-nucleon states (such as $\nu d \rightarrow \nu n p$) that are vital for neutrino observatories like SNO. The work also identifies the need for future finite-volume eigenvalue equations that account for $^3S_1$-$^3D_1$ mixing at higher energies.
