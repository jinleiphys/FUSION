# Fission dynamics at low excitation energy

> The origin of mass asymmetry in the fission of uranium at a low excitation energy is clarified by a trajectory analysis of the Langevin equation. The positions of the peaks in the mass distribution of fission fragments are mainly determined by fission saddle points originating from the shell correction energy. The widths of the peaks, on the other hand, result from a shape fluctuation around the scission point caused by the random force in the Langevin equation. We found that a random vibration in the oblate direction of fissioning fragments is essential for the fission process. According to this picture, fission does not occur with continuous stretching in the prolate direction, similarly to that observed in starch syrup. This is expected to lead to a new viewpoint of fission dynamics and the splitting mechanism.

# Fission dynamics at low excitation energy

## Key claim

The positions of the peaks in the mass distribution of fission fragments for uranium at low excitation energy are determined by fission saddle points arising from shell correction energy, while the widths of those peaks result from shape fluctuation around the scission point caused by the random force in the Langevin equation. The authors claim that fission does not occur with continuous stretching in the prolate direction (like starch syrup), but rather through a random vibration in the oblate direction that is essential for the fission process.

## Method

The authors performed trajectory analysis using the multidimensional Langevin equation with three collective coordinates (distance between potential centers, fragment deformation, and mass asymmetry) for the fission of 236U at excitation energy 20 MeV. The potential energy surface included a liquid-drop part, rotational energy, and shell correction energy with temperature dependence, while the friction tensor employed wall-and-window one-body dissipation and the inertia tensor used the Werner-Wheeler approximation. Trajectories were started at the second minimum of the potential energy surface and tracked until they reached the scission point (zero neck radius).

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy studied: 20 MeV for 236U
- Average total kinetic energy of fission fragments: 171.8 MeV, in agreement with experimental data from Vand (1973)
- Shell damping energy used: 20 MeV
- Neck parameter used: 0.35
- Friction tensor multiplied by factors of 0.1, 1, and 5 showed no dependence on MDFF at 20 MeV without initial boost, but dependence appeared at 30 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous dynamical approach (Aritomo 2013) that successfully reproduced the mass distribution of fission fragments and total kinetic energy for U and Pu at low excitation energies. The study provides the first dynamical explanation for mass-asymmetric fission, which had remained a puzzle since its discovery by Hahn and Meitner (1939) when nuclei were described using the liquid-drop model (Bohr 1939). The results enable future work to include independent deformation parameters for each fragment and neutron emission from the fissioning system and fragments, and may help explain the abrupt changes between mass-asymmetric and symmetric fission modes observed in Fm and Th isotopes depending on neutron number.
