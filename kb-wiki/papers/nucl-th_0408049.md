# Interference effects in the Coulomb dissociation of 15,17,19C

> In this work the semiclassical model of pure Coulomb excitation was applied to the breakup of 15,17,19C. The ground state wave functions were calculated in the particle-rotor model including core excitation. The importance of interference terms in the dipole strength arising after including core degrees of freedom is analyzed for each isotope. It is shown that Coulomb interference effects are important for the case of 17C.

## Key claim

The paper shows that Coulomb interference effects in the dipole strength function are important for the breakup of 17C, but practically insignificant for 15C and 19C, contrary to the abstract's general statement that "Coulomb interference effects are important for the case of 17C", the hedge is that this importance is specific to the non-halo isotope 17C, where core-excited components dominate the ground state wave function.

## Method

The authors applied a semiclassical model of pure Coulomb excitation using virtual photon numbers to calculate breakup cross sections for 15,17,19C on a 208Pb target. Ground state wave functions were obtained from a particle-rotor model including core excitation, with the deformed Woods-Saxon potential expanded to first order in the quadrupole deformation parameter beta, and continuum states were treated as plane waves. The dipole strength function was computed from coherent sums over partial waves, allowing explicit evaluation of interference terms between different channel contributions.

## Key numbers

- For 19C: ground state composition is 71.5% s1/2 ⊗ 0+, 25.3% d5/2 ⊗ 2+, and 3.2% d3/2 ⊗ 2+; one-neutron separation energy Sn = 0.65 MeV; core 2+ excitation energy epsilon2+ = 1.2 MeV; deformation parameter beta = 0.5.
- For 17C: ground state composition is 14.5% d3/2 ⊗ 0+, 76.5% s1/2 ⊗ 2+, and 9.0% d3/2 ⊗ 2+; Sn = 0.73 MeV; epsilon2+ = 1.776 MeV; beta = 0.55.
- For 15C: ground state composition is 87.2% s1/2 ⊗ 0+, 10.9% d5/2 ⊗ 2+, and 1.9% d3/2 ⊗ 2+; Sn = 1.22 MeV; epsilon2+ = 7.1 MeV; beta = 0.42.
- In 19C, the dominant s1/2 → p3/2 transition contributes 94% of the total strength for the 1/2+ → 3/2- transition; all other six contributions add up to only 6%.
- In 17C, interference terms contribute destructively at about 30% of the total squared terms; the dominant ground state component is the core-excited s1/2 ⊗ 2+ at 76.5%.
- The weighted average one-neutron separation energy of 19C from time-of-flight experiments is 242 ± 95 keV; Nakamura (1999) gives 0.53 ± 0.13 MeV; Maddalena et al. (2001) gives 0.65 ± 0.15 MeV and 0.8 ± 0.3 MeV; adopted value is Sn = 0.58 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the particle-rotor model calculations of Ridikas et al. (1998) for carbon isotopes, providing a detailed analysis of interference terms in the dipole strength that were not fully assessed previously. The results confirm the findings of Shyam (2001) that core-excited state contributions are very small in 19C, but contradict the expectation that the simple cluster model works universally, for 17C, core excitation and quantum interference are essential. The calculations enable comparison with experimental data from Nakamura (1999) for 19C and Pramanik et al. (2003) for 15,17C, and suggest that future work should consider two-cluster models and nuclear-Coulomb interference corrections.
