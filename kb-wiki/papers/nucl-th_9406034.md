# Particle-Production Mechanism in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We discuss the production of particles in relativistic heavy-ion collisions through the mechanism of massive bremsstrahlung, in which massive mesons are emitted during rapid nucleon acceleration. This mechanism is described within the framework of classical hadrodynamics for extended nucleons, corresponding to nucleons of finite size interacting with massive meson fields. This new theory provides a natural covariant microscopic approach to relativistic heavy-ion collisions that includes automatically spacetime nonlocality and retardation, nonequilibrium phenomena, interactions among all nucleons, and particle production. Inclusion of the finite nucleon size cures the difficulties with preacceleration and runaway solutions that have plagued the classical theory of self-interacting point particles. For the soft reactions that dominate nucleon-nucleon collisions, a significant fraction of the incident center-of-mass energy is radiated through massive bremsstrahlung. In the present version of the theory, this radiated energy is in the form of neutral scalar ($Žā$) and neutral vector ($Žē$) mesons, which subsequently decay primarily into pions with some photons also. Additional meson fields that are known to be important from nucleon-nucleon scattering experiments should be incorporated in the future, in which case the radiated energy would also contain isovector pseudoscalar ($ŽĆ^+$, $ŽĆ^-$, $ŽĆ^0$), isovector scalar ($╬┤^+$, $╬┤^-$, $╬┤^0$), isovector vector ($Žü^+$, $Žü^-$, $Žü^0$), and neutral pseudoscalar ($╬Ę$) mesons.

## Key claim

The paper argues that particle production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can be described by massive bremsstrahlung, where massive mesons are emitted during rapid nucleon acceleration, but this mechanism is currently limited to neutral scalar (sigma) and neutral vector (omega) mesons and is expected to be valid only when the radiated center-of-mass energy exceeds the lightest meson mass of 550 MeV, a condition well satisfied at the three highest incident momenta studied but not at the two lowest.

## Method

The authors formulate classical hadrodynamics for extended nucleons of finite size interacting with massive scalar and vector meson fields, deriving second-order nonlinear integrodifferential equations of motion from an action principle. They solve these equations numerically using a fourth-order Adams-Moulton predictor-corrector algorithm with adaptive step sizes for soft nucleon-nucleon collisions at five laboratory momenta, without making mean-field approximations, perturbative expansions, or superpositions of two-body collisions.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon root-mean-square radius: 0.862 fm, determined from elastic electron scattering off protons
- Nucleon mass: 938.91897 MeV/c^2
- Scalar (sigma) meson mass: 550 MeV/c^2
- Vector (omega) meson mass: 781.95 MeV/c^2
- Scalar interaction strength g_s^2: 7.29
- Vector interaction strength g_v^2: 10.81
- Laboratory momenta studied for soft nucleon-nucleon collisions: 14.6, 30, 60, 100, and 200 GeV/c
- The classical approximation is expected to be valid when radiated center-of-mass energy exceeds 550 MeV, which is satisfied at the three highest incident momenta but not at 14.6 and 30 GeV/c

## Context

This work extends the classical hadrodynamics framework previously developed by the authors (Bush and Nix, 1994) and earlier work by Sharp, Harston, and Nix (1990), providing a covariant microscopic approach that avoids the preacceleration and runaway solutions that plagued classical theories of self-interacting point particles. The approach is intended to describe the soft reactions that dominate nucleon-nucleon collisions, as opposed to the rare hard collisions involving individual quarks or gluons described by perturbative QCD (Peshkin, 1987). Future incorporation of additional meson fields (pion, delta, rho, eta) known from nucleon-nucleon scattering experiments (Machleidt, 1989) and quantum corrections analogous to those used by Moniz and Sharp (1977) for nonrelativistic quantum electrodynamics are identified as necessary next steps for a quantitative description.
