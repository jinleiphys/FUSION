# Nucleon Short-Range Correlations and High-Momentum Dynamics: Implications on the Equation of State of Dense Matter

> Nucleon short-range correlations (SRCs) and their high-momentum tails (HMTs) encode key short-range dynamics in nuclei and dense matter. This review provides a concise overview of SRC features relevant to the Equation of State (EOS) of isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter. We summarize empirical and theoretical properties of the single-nucleon momentum distribution $n(k)$, emphasizing the role of the neutron--proton tensor force, the dominance of correlated np pairs, and the enhancement of minority-species HMTs. Links to nucleon effective E-masses, quasi-deuteron components, and orbital entanglement are briefly noted. We examine how SRC-induced HMTs modify kinetic and potential contributions to the EOS in both non-relativistic and relativistic frameworks, including the softening of the kinetic symmetry energy and departures from the isospin parabolic approximation of asymmetric nuclear EOS. Sensitivity to high-momentum components and generalizations to arbitrary dimensions are also highlighted. Implications for heavy-ion reactions are summarized, including effects on particle yields, collective flows, deeply sub-threshold particle production and hard photon emission, driven by modified initial nucleon momentum distributions and abundant high relative-momentum np pairs during the reaction. Finally, we outline SRC-HMT consequences for neutron-star matter, covering proton fractions, tidal deformabilities, $Z$-factors, cooling, and the core--crust transition, as well as possible connections to dark-matter interactions in dense environments.

## Key claim

This review argues that nucleon short-range correlations (SRCs) and their associated high-momentum tails (HMTs) in the single-nucleon momentum distribution significantly modify the Equation of State (EOS) of isospin-asymmetric nuclear matter, particularly at high densities and large isospin asymmetries, but the authors acknowledge that understanding of these correlations "remains incomplete and continues to evolve."

## Method

The authors provide a selective review synthesizing empirical and theoretical properties of the single-nucleon momentum distribution n(k) in asymmetric nuclear matter, examining how SRC-induced HMTs alter kinetic and potential contributions to the EOS within both non-relativistic and relativistic frameworks. They connect these microscopic features to observable consequences in heavy-ion reactions (particle yields, collective flows, sub-threshold particle production, hard photon emission) and neutron-star properties (proton fractions, tidal deformabilities, cooling, core-crust transition). The review also discusses generalizations to arbitrary spatial dimensions and estimates involving very high-momentum components.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic SRC length scale in finite nuclei is roughly about 1 fm (the size of nucleon)
- The high-momentum tail follows an approximate power-law behavior n(k) ~ k^(-4) at large k, with momentum up to about 2k_F
- The parameter a_2(A) for infinite nuclear matter extrapolates to a_2(infinity) ≈ 7 ± 1
- For ^4He nucleus, correlated terms contribute to about 23% of the total wave function, with tensor correlation contributing about 12% (roughly double the central contribution) and many-body contribution of about 4%
- The tensor correlation in ^4He dominates around k ≈ 2 fm^(-1) and short-range repulsion dominates around k ≈ 4 fm^(-1)
- The function I(|k|/k_F^J) near the Fermi surface behaves approximately as exp[-alpha|k|^2] where alpha ≈ 0.12 fm^2, giving alpha*k_F^2 ≈ 0.21 << 1 at saturation density rho_0

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes work on SRC physics from experimental programs at Jefferson Lab, GSI-FAIR, and other facilities, building on comprehensive reviews such as Hen et al. (2017) and Dickhoff & Barbieri (2004). The paper connects SRC-modified momentum distributions to the EOS of dense matter, enabling improved constraints on the symmetry energy near and above saturation density from heavy-ion reaction observables and neutron-star observations including gravitational-wave detections from LIGO/Virgo.
