# Simultaneous improvements of nuclear mass and charge radius predictions using multi-task Gaussian process approaches

> A multi-task Gaussian process (GP) machine learning model is introduced to simultaneously predict two important nuclear observables across the nuclear chart, namely nuclear masses and charge radii. Utilizing 12 physical input features, our multi-task GP consistently outperforms single-task learning, achieving overall root-mean-square deviations of 0.136 MeV for masses and 0.007 fm for charge radii. The good performance of the present model is confirmed by three complementary validations, namely various fractions for training and testing data, further extrapolations for newly reported nuclei far from stability, and popular Garvey-Kelson mass relations. The correlations between the two observables are explicitly analyzed within the multi-task learning framework. Furthermore, by employing the SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) method, we interpret the importance of different features for mass and radius predictions across distinct nuclear regions. These results demonstrate the effectiveness of the multi-task GP approach for high-accuracy nuclear property predictions.

## Key claim

The multi-task Gaussian process model achieves root-mean-square deviations of 0.136 MeV for masses and 0.007 fm for charge radii, but these results depend on using the 12-feature input set and a 70/30 train-test split; the model's performance degrades with fewer features (0.209 MeV and 0.008 fm for the 9-feature set) and varies with training fraction.

## Method

The authors constructed a multi-task Gaussian process using the intrinsic coregionalization model (ICM) framework, which combines a Matern-3/2 kernel over 12 physical input features (including N, Z, A, surface and Coulomb terms, pairing indicators, valence nucleon numbers, and the Casten factor) with a 2x2 task covariance matrix that captures correlations between nuclear mass and charge radius predictions. They trained on 2340 nuclear masses from AME2020 and 880 charge radii from CR2013 (with Z, N >= 8), using 70% of data for training and 30% for testing, with Z-score normalization applied to both observables before training. Model interpretation was performed using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values across three nuclear regions defined by distance from the beta-stability line.

## Key numbers

- Overall root-mean-square deviation for nuclear masses: 0.136 MeV (MTL-M12 model, full dataset)
- Overall root-mean-square deviation for charge radii: 0.007 fm (MTL-M12 model, full dataset)
- For the 9-feature set (MTL-M9), overall rms deviations were 0.209 MeV for masses and 0.008 fm for charge radii
- Testing set rms deviations for MTL-M12: 0.210 MeV for masses and 0.012 fm for charge radii
- Extrapolation to 21 newly measured masses gave rms deviation of 0.221 MeV (MTL-M12)
- Extrapolation to 153 newly measured charge radii gave rms deviation of 0.024 fm (MTL-M12)
- Pearson correlation coefficient between mass and radius tasks: 0.31 for MTL-M9, 0.57 for MTL-M12
- For 3106 independent Garvey-Kelson relations, the average absolute deviation was 0.083 MeV, with over 93% of values within 0.2 MeV

## Context

This work extends single-task Gaussian process models for nuclear masses (Ye 2025) and charge radii by introducing a multi-task framework that explicitly leverages correlations between the two observables, achieving improvements of approximately 17% over single-task learning for masses and comparable gains for radii. The approach contrasts with macroscopic-microscopic models like the finite-range droplet model (Moller 2012) and Weizsacker-Skyrme models (Wang 2014), which typically achieve rms deviations of 0.3-5 MeV for masses but do not simultaneously predict charge radii, and with microscopic density functional approaches (Agbemava 2014, Erler 2012) that yield rms deviations larger than 1 MeV for masses and 0.02-0.05 fm for radii. The multi-task framework enables consistent high-accuracy predictions across the nuclear chart and provides a foundation for extrapolating to nuclei far from stability where experimental data are scarce.
