# Reactors as a source of antineutrinos: the effect of fuel loading and burnup for mixed oxide fuels

> In a conventional light water reactor loaded with a range of uranium and plutonium-based fuel mixtures, the variation in antineutrino production over the cycle reflects both the initial core fissile inventory and its evolution. Under the assumption of constant thermal power, we calculate the rate at which antineutrinos are emitted from variously fueled cores, and the evolution of that rate as measured by a representative ton-scale antineutrino detector. We find that antineutrino flux decreases with burnup for Low Enriched Uranium cores, increases for full mixed-oxide (MOX) cores, and does not appreciably change for cores with a MOX fraction of approximately 75%. Accounting for uncertainties in the fission yields, in the emitted antineutrino spectra, and the detector response function, we show that the difference in core-wide MOX fractions at least as small as 8% can be distinguished using a hypothesis test. The test compares the evolution of the antineutrino rate relative to an initial value over part or all of the cycle. The use of relative rates reduces the sensitivity of the test to an independent thermal power measurement, making the result more robust against possible countermeasures. This rate-only approach also offers the potential advantage of reducing the cost and complexity of the antineutrino detectors used to verify the diversion, compared to methods that depend on the use of the antineutrino spectrum. A possible application is the verification of the disposition of surplus plutonium in nuclear reactors.

## Key claim

Under the assumption of constant thermal power, the authors show that the evolution of the detected antineutrino rate relative to its initial value can distinguish differences in core-wide MOX fractions as small as 8% using a hypothesis test, even when accounting for uncertainties in fission yields, antineutrino spectra, and detector response.

## Method

The authors performed full-core MCNPX2.7 simulations coupled with CINDER90 depletion calculations for a Westinghouse-type PWR core (3200 MW thermal power) loaded with various admixtures of low-enriched uranium (LEU) and weapons-grade mixed-oxide (WGMOX) fuel assemblies. They convolved the time-dependent fission rates from the simulation with parameterized antineutrino spectral densities from Huber (2011) to obtain the emitted antineutrino flux as a function of energy and time, then estimated detected rates for a 5 metric ton liquid scintillator detector at 25 meter standoff.

## Key numbers

- For a full LEU core, antineutrino emission decreases with burnup; for a full MOX core, the detection rate increases with burnup; for cores with approximately 75% MOX loading, antineutrino emission does not change appreciably with burnup.
- The number of antineutrinos per fission above the inverse beta decay threshold (1.806 MeV) is 1.92 ± 0.2 for UO2 (235U) and 1.45 ± 0.2 for MOX (239Pu).
- Energy released per fission is 201.7 ± 0.6 MeV for UO2 and 210.0 ± 0.9 MeV for MOX.
- A difference in core-wide MOX fractions as small as 8% can be distinguished using a hypothesis test that compares the evolution of the antineutrino rate relative to an initial value.
- The thermal fission cross section of 239Pu is 741 barns, compared to 577 barns for 235U.
- The delayed neutron fraction β is 0.0065 for 235U and 0.0020 for 239Pu.
- The weapons-grade plutonium vector used was 93.6% 239Pu, 5.9% 240Pu, 0.4% 241Pu, and 0.1% 242Pu by weight.
- The reactor-grade plutonium vector (33 MWd/kgHM burnup) was 56.0% 239Pu, 24.1% 240Pu, 12.8% 241Pu, and 5.4% 242Pu by weight.
- Systematic uncertainties on fission fractions range from ±2% to ±11%, with 241Pu having the highest uncertainty at 11%.
- The total detected antineutrino rate drops by a factor of about 1.5 when the detector energy threshold is increased from 1.8 MeV to 3.3 MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of antineutrino-based monitoring of LEU cores (Bernstein 2002, Bula) to the case of mixed-oxide (MOX) fuels, including both weapons-grade and reactor-grade plutonium compositions. The authors use the OECD/NEA benchmark for Westinghouse-type PWR cores with partial MOX loading (Benchmark) and the Huber parameterization (Huber 2011) for antineutrino spectra. The results enable non-intrusive verification of plutonium disposition in reactors, where the rate-only approach reduces sensitivity to independent thermal power measurements and could lower the cost and complexity of antineutrino detectors compared to methods that depend on spectral information.
