# From Quark Matter to Strange Machos

> This paper gives an overview of the properties of all possible equilibrium sequences of compact strange-matter stars with nuclear crusts, which range from strange stars to strange dwarfs. In contrast to their non-strange counterparts, --neutron stars and white dwarfs--, their properties are determined by two (rather than one) parameters, the central star density and the density at the base of the nuclear crust. This leads to stellar strange-matter configurations whose properties are much more complex than those of the conventional sequence. As an example, two generically different categories of stable strange dwarfs are found, which could be the observed white dwarfs. Furthermore we find very-low-mass strange stellar objects, with masses as small as those of Jupiter or even lighter planets. Such objects, if abundant enough in our Galaxy, should be seen by the presently performed gravitational microlensing searches.

## Key claim

If the strange matter hypothesis is correct, the complete equilibrium sequence of compact strange-matter stars with nuclear crusts is determined by two parameters (central star density and density at the base of the nuclear crust) rather than one, leading to stable configurations that range from strange stars to strange dwarfs and planetary-mass objects; the authors hedge that these objects "could be" the observed white dwarfs and that they "should be seen" by microlensing searches if abundant enough, rather than claiming definitive detection.

## Method

The authors compute equilibrium sequences of strange-matter stars by combining a bag-model equation of state for the strange quark core (with bag constant B^(1/4) = 145 or 160 MeV) with the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state for the nuclear crust at densities below neutron drip. They solve the general relativistic stellar structure equations for non-rotating and rotating configurations, including the Kepler (mass-shedding) period, and perform stability analyses against radial oscillations. Cooling simulations are carried out using neutrino emission rates from modified and direct Urca processes, pion/kaon condensates, and superfluid suppression factors.

## Key numbers

- Maximum inner crust density is set by neutron drip at about 4.3 x 10^11 g/cm^3.
- Minimum gap between strange core and crust base is about 200 fm, required to avoid strong interactions.
- A hot strange pulsar with T ~ 30 MeV can only carry crusts with electrostatic potential e V_crust less than about 0.1 MeV; crust potentials of 8-12 MeV (expected at neutron drip) require core temperatures T less than about 5 MeV.
- Minimum-mass strange star in the sequence has mass about 0.017 solar masses (about 17 Jupiter masses).
- Strange star with typical pulsar mass ~1.45 solar masses can rotate at Kepler periods as small as P ~ 0.5 msec, compared to about 1 msec for neutron stars of the same mass.
- Ratio of crustal moment of inertia to total moment of inertia (I_crust/I_total) varies between 10^-3 and about 10^-5 at the maximum mass.
- Observed glitch frequency changes Delta Omega/Omega = (10^-9 to 10^-6) can be explained with crustal moment of inertia changes of f less than about 0.1.
- Baryon numbers of strange cores in white-dwarf-like objects are smaller than about 2 x 10^55.

## Context

This work extends the strange matter hypothesis originally proposed by Bodmer (1971), Terazawa (1979), and Witten (1984) by computing the complete equilibrium sequences of strange stars with nuclear crusts, building on earlier calculations by Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto (1986) and Glendenning and Weber (1992). It contradicts the conventional view that neutron stars and white dwarfs form two separate, disconnected sequences, showing instead that strange stars and strange dwarfs are stably connected. The results enable future gravitational microlensing searches to identify planetary-mass strange objects (strange MACHOs) and provide a framework for interpreting pulsar glitches and cooling data as tests of the strange matter hypothesis.
