# Tensor-force effects on nuclear matter in relativistic ab initio theory

> Within the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory in the full Dirac space, the tensor-force effects on infinite nuclear matter are elucidated by subtracting the matrix elements of tensor forces from the realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction. The tensor-force effects for the binding energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) as well as the symmetry energy are attractive and are more pronounced around the empirical saturation density, while the tensor forces have little impact on the pure neutron matter. By tuning the tensor-force strength, an infinite (negative) scattering length in the spin-triplet channel is found. This locates the dilute SNM with only the $^3S_1$-$^3D_1$ channel interaction at the unitary limit. Its ground-state energy is found proportional to the energy of a free Fermi gas with a scaling factor 0.38, revealing good universal properties. This work paves the way to study the tensor-force effects in neutron stars as well as finite nuclei from realistic nucleon-nucleon interactions, highlights the role of the tensor force on the deviation of the nuclear physics to the unitary limit, and provides valuable reference for studies of the four-component unitary Fermi gas.

## Key claim

The tensor force has an attractive effect on the binding energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) and on the symmetry energy, with the effect most pronounced around the empirical saturation density; however, the tensor force has little impact on pure neutron matter (PNM). By tuning the tensor-force strength, the spin-triplet scattering length can be made infinite, placing dilute SNM at the unitary limit, where the ground-state energy scales with a factor of 0.38 relative to a free Fermi gas.

## Method

The authors used relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory in the full Dirac space, solving the Thompson equation in the medium with realistic Bonn potentials (A, B, and C). To isolate tensor-force effects, they subtracted the tensor-force matrix elements from the nucleon-nucleon interaction using non-relativistic reduced operators for each meson-nucleon coupling, and introduced a quenching factor lambda to control the tensor-force strength. For the unitary limit study, they tuned lambda to achieve a scattering length of approximately -100 fm in the spin-triplet channel and solved the Thompson equation only once with initial vanishing single-particle potentials at extremely low densities.

## Key numbers

- The tensor-force contribution to the symmetry energy is qualitatively consistent with a prior estimate of 36 MeV at density rho = 0.187 fm^-3 (Vidana, 2011), though with quantitative discrepancies.
- The scattering length in the spin-singlet channel for neutron-neutron (nn) interactions is a_s^nn = -18.9 fm.
- The Bertsch parameter for dilute PNM in the ^1S_0 channel, with scattering length tuned to -100 fm, is 0.576.
- The Bertsch parameter for dilute SNM in the ^1S_0 channel, with scattering length tuned to -100 fm, is 0.376.
- The Bertsch parameter for dilute SNM in the ^3S_1-^3D_1 channel, with scattering length tuned to approximately -100 fm, is 0.38.
- The quenching factors lambda needed to achieve a_t approximately -100 fm are 0.4029 for Bonn A, 0.5624 for Bonn B, and 0.6278 for Bonn C.
- The effective range r_t in the spin-triplet channel retains values in the range of 1.8-5.1 fm as lambda varies.
- The deuteron binding energy B_d decreases from 2.2246 MeV to zero as the tensor-force strength is reduced.

## Context

This work extends prior RBHF studies of nuclear matter (Brockmann, 1990; Wang, 2021) and directly quantifies tensor-force effects by subtracting tensor matrix elements, going beyond earlier work that used projection techniques or off-diagonal matrix element removal (Wang, 2022). It also connects to non-relativistic ab initio studies of tensor-force effects on symmetry energy (Vidana, 2011) and to studies of the unitary limit in dilute neutron matter (Vidana, 2021; Carlson, 2011). The results enable future studies of tensor-force effects in neutron stars and finite nuclei from realistic interactions, and provide a reference for four-component unitary Fermi gas studies.
