# Effects of an induced electric field on π^{-}/π^{+} ratio in heavy-ion collisions

> Using an isospin- and momentum-dependent transport model, we examine the effects of an electric field induced by a variable magnetic field on the π^{-}/π^{+} ratio in central to peripheral heavy-ion collisions at beam energies of 400 and 1500MeV/nucleon. It is shown that while the induced electric field does not affect the total multiplicities of both $π^{-}$ and $π^{+}$ mesons at both the lower beam energy of 400MeV/nucleon and the higher beam energy of 1500MeV/nucleon, it reduces (enhances) the emission of $π^{-}$ ($π^{+}$) mesons in midrapidity, but enhances (reduces) the emission of $π^{-}$ ($π^{+}$) mesons in forward and backward rapidities especially for the more peripheral collisions at the lower beam energy because of the rapidly transient variable magnetic field at more peripheral collisions and longer reaction duration time at the lower beam energy. These findings indicate that the total π^{-}/π^{+} ratio is still a precisely reliable probe of symmetry energy at both the lower and higher beam energies, but one should consider the induced electric field when using the differential π^{-}/π^{+} ratio to probe the symmetry energy especially for the lower beam energy and more peripheral collisions. Finally, the relative suppression factor based on the ratio of π^{-}/π^{+} in different rapidities is proposed to be an effective probe of the induced electric field generated in heavy-ion collisions due to its maximizing effects of induced electric fields on the differential π^{-}/π^{+} ratio but minimizing effects of some uncertainty factors in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The induced electric field from a variable magnetic field does not affect the total π⁻/π⁺ ratio at beam energies of 400 and 1500 MeV/nucleon, but it does alter the differential (rapidity-dependent) π⁻/π⁺ ratio, suppressing it at midrapidity and enhancing it at forward/backward rapidities, especially for more peripheral collisions at the lower beam energy, meaning the total ratio remains a reliable probe of symmetry energy but the differential ratio requires accounting for this field.

## Method

The authors used an isospin- and momentum-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model to simulate Au+Au collisions at beam energies of 400 and 1500 MeV/nucleon across central to peripheral impact parameters. They incorporated the Liénard-Wiechert electromagnetic potentials to compute the magnetic field and used Faraday's law to evaluate the induced electric field from the time-varying magnetic flux. They compared pion multiplicities and the π⁻/π⁺ ratio in different rapidity bins with and without the induced electric field, and defined a relative suppression factor F as the ratio of the π⁻/π⁺ ratio in midrapidity to that in forward/backward rapidities.

## Key numbers

- Beam energies studied: 400 MeV/nucleon and 1500 MeV/nucleon.
- Impact parameter range: central to peripheral (up to 10 fm, as shown in magnetic field calculations).
- Symmetry energy at saturation density: 30.5 MeV at ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- Nuclear matter incompressibility: 212 MeV for symmetric nuclear matter.
- Density slope of symmetry energy (parameter x=1): 16.4 MeV at normal density.
- The induced electric field reduces π⁻ emission in midrapidity (|y/y_beam| ≤ 0.5) and enhances π⁺ emission there, with effects increasing with impact parameter at 400 MeV/nucleon; at 1500 MeV/nucleon the effects are "almost negligible."
- The relative suppression factor F increases with impact parameter at 400 MeV/nucleon, indicating stronger induced electric fields in peripheral collisions.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of the π⁻/π⁺ ratio as a probe of symmetry energy, which have shown inconsistent conclusions between different transport model communities, for example, the BUU community (Xiao, 2009) favors a supersoft symmetry energy while the QMD community (Feng, 2010) favors a superstiff one. The paper also builds on earlier findings that a magnetic field perpendicular to the reaction plane can focus or diverge charged pions (Ou, 2011), and that a variable magnetic field may generate an induced electric field in relativistic collisions (Liu, 2012). The results enable future studies to use the total π⁻/π⁺ ratio as a reliable symmetry-energy probe while cautioning that differential rapidity measurements must account for induced electric fields, and they propose the relative suppression factor F as a new observable to isolate the induced electric field's effects.
