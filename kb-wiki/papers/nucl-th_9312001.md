# Comment on "Phase-Shift Analysis of NN Scattering Below 160 Mev: Indication of a Strong Tensor Force"

> In his recent publication of a NN phase shift analysis below 160 MeV, Henneck reports relatively large values for the mixing parameter $ε_1$. Based on these results, Henneck suggests that the strength of the $ρ$-meson tensor coupling to the nucleon may be weaker than used in present day NN interactions, like the Paris or Bonn potentials. We point out that at low energies ( < 100 MeV ) there is very little sensitivity to the strength of the $ρ$ coupling, due to the compensating effect of the second order tensor term. In order to establish sensitivity, one has to go to energies > 200 MeV, where the second order contribution has gone out. As it happens, the $ε_1$ mixing parameter is well determined in the region of energies 200--300 MeV, and there is agreement with the predictions by the Paris and Bonn potentials; whereas the weak-$ρ$ model is about 50\% above the data. This and additional considerations in triplet $P$-waves re-confirm that NN scattering requires the strong $ρ$, consistent with the $ππ-N\bar{N}$ partial-wave analysis by Höhler and Pietarinen.

# Comment on "Phase-Shift Analysis of NN Scattering Below 160 MeV: Indication of a Strong Tensor Force"

## Key claim

The authors argue that low-energy nucleon-nucleon (NN) scattering below 100 MeV is insensitive to the strength of the rho-meson tensor coupling because second-order tensor contributions compensate for changes in the first-order rho-exchange term; sensitivity to the rho coupling strength only emerges at energies above 200 MeV, where phase shift analyses favor the strong rho coupling (kappa_rho approximately 6) over the weak vector-dominance value (kappa_rho = 3.7).

## Method

The authors compare the mixing parameter epsilon_1 and triplet P-wave phase shifts from the Henneck phase shift analysis (below 160 MeV) with predictions from the Paris and Bonn potentials (strong rho) and the Reid potential (weak rho, vector-dominance coupling). They analyze the energy dependence of second-order tensor contributions, noting that these contributions drop out above approximately 200 MeV, and compare epsilon_1 values at 200-300 MeV from multiple phase shift analyses (Arndt, Bugg and Bryan, Nijmegen group) against potential model predictions.

## Key numbers

- Weak rho coupling is defined as kappa_rho = 3.7; strong rho coupling is defined as kappa_rho approximately 6, where kappa_rho is the ratio of tensor to vector coupling constant (f_rho/g_rho).
- At 50 MeV, the Henneck epsilon_1 value is about 2.8 degrees (consistent with zero rho coupling); the Reid potential (weak rho) gives approximately 2.4 degrees; Paris and Bonn potentials (strong rho) give less than or equal to 2.0 degrees; the quoted error in Henneck is plus or minus 0.25 degrees.
- In the 200-300 MeV region, the Reid potential predicts epsilon_1 approximately 7 degrees; Paris and Bonn potentials predict approximately 4 degrees, in agreement with phase shift analyses by Arndt, Bugg and Bryan, and the Nijmegen group.
- The weak-rho model is about 50% above the data for epsilon_1 in the 200-300 MeV region.
- The Hohler-Pietarinen analysis yields kappa_rho = 6.1 plus or minus 0.6 from a somewhat different method (Pietarinen, 1978).

## Context

This comment directly responds to Henneck (1993), who claimed that NN phase shift analysis below 160 MeV indicated a weaker rho tensor coupling than used in the Paris and Bonn potentials. The authors argue that this conclusion is invalid because low-energy data below 100 MeV cannot distinguish between weak and strong rho couplings due to compensating second-order tensor effects. The work extends earlier analyses by Klomp et al. (1992) and Machleidt and Slaus (1993), who showed that Bonn and Paris potentials reproduce epsilon_1 well, and by Wilburn et al. (1993), who found Henneck's epsilon_1 at 25 MeV lies on the Bonn curve. The strong rho coupling is consistent with the pi-pi to N-antiN partial-wave analysis by Hohler and Pietarinen (1975) and subsequent work by Grein (1977) using NN forward dispersion relations. The comment enables future studies to focus on energies above 200 MeV for determining the rho tensor coupling strength and points to triplet P-wave phase shifts as more reliable observables than epsilon_1 for this purpose.
