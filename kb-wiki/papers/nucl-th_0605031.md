# Conserved electromagnetic currents in a relativistic optical model

> A simple model of a relativistic optical model is constructed by reducing the three-body Bethe-Salpeter equation to an effective two-body optical model. A corresponding effective current is derived for use with the optical-model wave functions. It is shown that this current satisfies a Ward-Takahashi identity involving the optical potential which results in conserved current matrix elements.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a relativistic optical model can be derived from the three-body Bethe-Salpeter equation, and that the corresponding effective current operator satisfies a Ward-Takahashi identity involving the optical potential, resulting in conserved current matrix elements. The derivation is not unique, and the author explicitly notes that alternate approaches should be developed to improve phenomenology for Dirac optical model applications to electromagnetic processes.

## Method

The author reduces the three-body Bethe-Salpeter equation for distinguishable particles to an effective two-body optical model by separating the two-body t-matrix for particles 2 and 3 into a bound-state pole contribution and a residual scattering piece. An effective current operator is then constructed from the Bethe-Salpeter current matrix elements, with the bound state placed on-shell using a singular operator iQ^1, and the optical potential is defined in terms of the residual scattering matrix and bound-state wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The difference between calculations without medium-modified form factors and data for ^4He(e,e'p) reactions is on the order of 5 to 10 percent, requiring model calculations to be trusted to a similar level of accuracy.
- No other explicit numerical results are presented in the paper.

## Context

This work extends the Dirac optical model approach previously applied to (e,e') and (e,e'p) reactions (Van Orden et al., 1985-1989; Kim and Wright, 1997-2003) by providing a formal derivation from covariant many-body theory rather than proceeding by analogy to nonrelativistic multiple-scattering theory. The paper addresses the long-standing problem of current non-conservation in optical model calculations, which had previously been handled only approximately through on-shell equivalent forms of the single-nucleon current (Van Orden et al., 1985; Cooper and Jennings, 1992) or by including two-body meson-exchange currents (Martinez et al., 2002). This work enables future development of improved phenomenological optical models for analyzing electron scattering data, particularly for studies of medium modification of nucleons in nuclei.
