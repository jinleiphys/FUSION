# Quantum theory of complete and incomplete fusion in collisions of weakly bound nuclei

> We propose a new quantum mechanical method to evaluate complete and incomplete fusion in collisions of weakly bound nuclei. The method is applied to the $^{7}{\rm Li}+^{209}{\rm Bi}$ system and the results are compared to experimental data. The overall agreement between theory and experiment is very good, above and below the Coulomb barrier.

## Key claim

The authors propose a new quantum mechanical method to calculate complete fusion (CF) and incomplete fusion (ICF) cross sections separately for any weakly bound projectile that breaks into two fragments, overcoming the limitation of prior methods that only worked for projectiles breaking into a heavy charged and a light uncharged fragment. The method achieves "very good" overall agreement with experimental data for the \(^{7}\text{Li}+^{209}\text{Bi}\) system both above and below the Coulomb barrier, though above 35 MeV center-of-mass energy the calculated CF cross section underestimates the data by about 10% and the calculated TF cross section exceeds the data by up to 15%.

## Method

The method starts from the continuum discretized coupled channel (CDCC) expression for the total fusion (TF) cross section, using an imaginary potential that acts separately on each fragment (\(^{3}\text{H}\) and \(^{4}\text{He}\)) rather than on the projectile center of mass. The TF cross section is split into contributions from bound channels (assigned to direct complete fusion, DCF) and from continuum channels; the continuum contribution is further decomposed into probabilities for absorbing each fragment individually, from which the incomplete fusion (ICF) and sequential complete fusion (SCF) cross sections are derived using a product formula analogous to semiclassical treatments. The method was implemented in a new computer code (CF-ICF) that uses radial wave functions from the CDCC version of the FRESCO code, with the São Paulo potential for the real parts of fragment-target interactions and Woods-Saxon short-range imaginary potentials.

## Key numbers

- The Coulomb barrier for the \(^{7}\text{Li}+^{209}\text{Bi}\) system is \(V_{\text{B}} = 28.2\) MeV, which is 1.2 MeV lower than the São Paulo potential barrier for the same system when projectile breakup is ignored.
- The breakup threshold of \(^{7}\text{Li}\) into \(^{3}\text{H}\) and \(^{4}\text{He}\) is 2.47 MeV.
- The continuum discretization used bins with orbital angular momenta up to \(l_{\text{max}} = 4\hbar\) and energies up to \(\varepsilon_{\text{max}} = 8\) MeV, with bin densities up to 10 bins/MeV near resonances at 2.2 and 4.2 MeV for \(l = 3\hbar\).
- Above 35 MeV center-of-mass energy, the calculated CF cross section underestimates the experimental data by about 10%, and the calculated TF cross section exceeds the data by up to 15%.
- The imaginary potential parameters were: depth \(W_0 = 50\) MeV, radius \(R_{\text{w}} = 1.0\,[A_i^{1/3} + A_{\text{T}}^{1/3}]\) fm, diffuseness \(a_{\text{w}} = 0.2\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the quantum mechanical approach of Hagino et al. (2000) and Diaz-Torres and Thompson (2002), which could only treat projectiles like \(^{11}\text{Be}\) that break into a heavy charged fragment and a light neutral one, by introducing a fragment-dependent imaginary potential that allows individual fragment absorption. It also improves upon the semiclassical model of KCD18 and the recent spectator-participant inclusive breakup model of Lei and Moro (2019), which could not evaluate ICF separately. The method enables future application to projectiles breaking into three fragments (e.g., \(^{9}\text{Be}\)) and provides a fully quantum mechanical framework for analyzing the suppression of complete fusion observed in experiments such as those by Dasgupta et al. (2002, 2004) and Cook et al. (2019).
