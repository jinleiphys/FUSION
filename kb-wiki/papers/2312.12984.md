# Nuclear mass radius and pressure in the Skyrme model

> We compute the mass radius, scalar radius, tensor radius, baryon number radius and mechanical radius of nuclei with baryon number $B=1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,32,108$ in the Skyrme model. The relations between these radii and the nuclear gravitational form factors are investigated. We also compute the `pressure' distribution and find that it is negative in the core region for all the nuclei with $B>1$. This suggests that the way mechanical stability is achieved in nuclei is qualitatively different than in the nucleon.

## Key claim

The paper computes multiple radii (mass, scalar, tensor, baryon number, mechanical) for nuclei with baryon numbers B=1 through 108 in the Skyrme model, finding that the pressure distribution is negative in the core region for all nuclei with B>1, which suggests that mechanical stability in nuclei is qualitatively different than in the nucleon. The abstract glosses over the caveat that this pressure is not genuine thermodynamic pressure and that the result may be an artifact of the model.

## Method

The authors use the Skyrme model with an SU(2)-valued classical field configuration (Skyrmion) for each baryon number B, supplemented with a pion mass term. They compute the energy-momentum tensor density and baryon number density directly in coordinate space from numerical solutions obtained on a 3D grid of up to (151)^3 points, using the Skyrmions3D package. The model parameters are fixed to f_pi=117.23 MeV, e=4.81, and m_pi=138 MeV to reproduce the proton mass (0.938 GeV) and charge radius (0.84 fm), with the sextic coupling turned off.

## Key numbers

* Proton (B=1): mass radius r_m = 0.778 fm, scalar radius r_s = 1.002 fm, tensor radius r_t = 0.638 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 0.642 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 0.817 fm, D(0) = -2.98, central pressure p(0) = 0.3465 GeV/fm^3
* Deuteron (B=2): mass radius r_m = 0.966 fm, scalar radius r_s = 1.149 fm, tensor radius r_t = 0.861 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 0.876 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 1.106 fm, D(0) = -10.61, central pressure p(0) = -0.0362 GeV/fm^3
* B=4 (helium-4): mass radius r_m = 1.197 fm, scalar radius r_s = 1.335 fm, tensor radius r_t = 1.121 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 1.138 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 1.448 fm, D(0) = -34.78, central pressure p(0) = -0.0173 GeV/fm^3
* B=8: mass radius r_m = 1.566 fm, scalar radius r_s = 1.651 fm, tensor radius r_t = 1.521 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 1.533 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 2.305 fm, D(0) = -105.31, central pressure p(0) = -0.0183 GeV/fm^3
* B=32: mass radius r_m = 2.674 fm, scalar radius r_s = 2.731 fm, tensor radius r_t = 2.645 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 2.641 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 3.358 fm, D(0) = -1745.44, central pressure p(0) = 0.0000 GeV/fm^3 (consistent with zero)
* B=108: mass radius r_m = 4.114 fm, scalar radius r_s = 4.165 fm, tensor radius r_t = 4.088 fm, baryon number radius r_B = 4.077 fm, mechanical radius r_mech = 3.499 fm, D(0) = -24151.55, central pressure p(0) = -0.0171 GeV/fm^3
* The D-term scales as D(0) ~ B^1.92 with the chosen parameters (previously found as B^1.7-1.8 with different parameters in GarciaMartin-Caro (2023))
* The tensor radius and baryon number radius agree within less than 2% for all solutions
* Residual errors for relations between radii are less than 1% for B=1 through B=8, about 5% for B=32, and less than 10% for B=108

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous calculation of gravitational form factors for nuclei in the Skyrme model (GarciaMartin-Caro 2023) by systematically computing multiple radii and their B-dependence. It contrasts with earlier work by Polyakov (2002) and Guzey (2005) which predicted D(0) ~ B^2.3, and with Liuti (2005) which predicted D(0) ~ B^1. The finding of negative central pressure for B>1 nuclei was recently reported for the deuteron (B=2) by Freese (2022) and for helium-4 (B=4) by He (2023) with one choice of nuclear wavefunction. The results enable future comparison with more realistic low-energy nuclear physics approaches and with experimental extractions of gravitational form factors at facilities like the Electron-Ion Collider.
