# Majorana and the quasi-stationary states in Nuclear Physics

> A complete theoretical model describing artificial disintegration of nuclei by bombardment with alpha-particles, developed by Majorana as early as in 1930, is discussed in detail alongside the basic experimental evidences that motivated it. By following the quantum dynamics of a state resulting from the superposition of a discrete state with a continuum one, whose interaction is described by a given potential term, Majorana obtained (among the other predictions) the explicit expression for the integrated cross section of the nuclear process, which is the direct measurable quantity of interest in the experiments. Though this is the first application of the concept of quasi-stationary states to a Nuclear Physics problem, it seems also that the unpublished Majorana's work anticipates by several years the related seminal paper by Fano on Atomic Physics.

# Majorana and the quasi-stationary states in Nuclear Physics

## Key claim
Majorana developed a complete theoretical model for artificial disintegration of nuclei by alpha-particle bombardment in 1930, obtaining the explicit expression for the integrated cross section of the nuclear process. The work, which remained unpublished, appears to anticipate by several years the related seminal paper by Fano (1935) on autoionization in Atomic Physics, though the authors note that quasi-stationary states were earlier introduced by Rice in the context of molecular predissociation.

## Method
Majorana modeled the quantum dynamics of a state resulting from the superposition of a discrete state with a continuum one, with their interaction described by a perturbation potential term. He solved for the perturbed eigenfunctions of the total Hamiltonian in four successive problems: first for one discrete state coupled to one continuum, then to two continua, then for initial conditions in the continuum, and finally introducing linear combinations of continuum states with definite physical meaning. He applied this general formalism to the specific case of alpha-induced nuclear disintegration by considering an unstable state of the system formed by a nucleus plus an alpha-particle that decays with emission of either an alpha-particle or a proton, assuming s-wave emission and the daughter nucleus in its ground state.

## Key numbers
- The transition probability per unit time between the discrete state and continuum states is given by 1/T = (2π/ħ)|I|^2, where I is the perturbation matrix element.
- For the two-continuum case, the total transition probability per unit time is 1/T = (2π/ħ)(|I|^2 + |L|^2), with partial probabilities 1/T_1 = (2π/ħ)|I|^2 and 1/T_2 = (2π/ħ)|L|^2.
- The maximum value p_0 of the probability ratio p(ε) = |c_1|^2/|c_2|^2 varies between 0 and 1, reaching unity when |I|^2 = |L|^2 (k=1), and is expressed as p_0 = 4k/(k+1)^2 where k = |I|^2/|L|^2.
- The integrated cross section for the disintegration process is ∫ S(ε) dε = (ħλ^2/2T) * k/(k+1)^2, where λ is the wavelength of the alpha-particles and T is the lifetime of the unstable state.
- The incoming flux condition for alpha-particles with ℓ=0 gives |c_2|^2/(2πħ) = πħ^2/(M^2 v^2), where M and v are the mass and velocity of the alpha-particles.

## Context
This work reconstructs and analyzes previously unpublished notes by Majorana from 1930, showing he was the first in the Fermi group in Rome to study nuclear physics, beginning with his 1929 master thesis on "The quantum theory of radioactive nuclei." The theory extends the earlier work of Gamow (1928) on spontaneous alpha emission and addresses the puzzling experimental results of Rutherford and Chadwick (1929) showing continuous proton energy spectra in alpha-induced disintegrations, which Chadwick and Gamow later interpreted as disintegration without alpha-capture. The formalism anticipates Fano's (1935) treatment of configuration mixing in autoionization, and the authors note that Fano developed his theory shortly after moving to Rome to work with Fermi in 1934-35, suggesting possible indirect influence of Majorana's work.
