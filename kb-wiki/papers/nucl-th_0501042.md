# Isospin in Reaction Dynamics. The Case of Dissipative Collisions at Fermi Energies

> A key question in the physics of unstable nuclei is the knowledge of the $EOS$ for asymmetric nuclear matter ($ANM$) away from normal conditions. We recall that the symmetry energy at low densities has important effects on the neutron skin structure, while the knowledge in high densities region is crucial for supernovae dynamics and neutron star properties. The $only$ way to probe such region of the isovector $EOS$ in terrestrial laboratories is through very dissipative collisions of asymmetric (up to exotic) heavy ions from low to relativistic energies. A general introduction to the topic is firstly presented. We pass then to a detailed discussion on the $neck-fragmentation$ process as the main dissipative mechanism at the Fermi energies and to the related isospin dynamics. From Stochastic Mean Field simulations the isospin effects on all the phases of the reaction dynamics are thoroughly analysed, from the fast nucleon emission to the mid-rapidity fragment formation up to the dynamical fission of the $spectator$ residues. Simulations have been performed with an increasing stiffness of the symmetry term of the $EOS$. Some differences have been noticed, especially for the fragment charge asymmetry. New isospin effects have been revealed from the correlation of fragment asymmetry with dynamical quantities at the freeze-out time. A series of isospin sensitive observables to be further measured are finally listed.

## Key claim

The only way to probe the high-density region of the isovector equation of state (EOS) in terrestrial laboratories is through very dissipative collisions of asymmetric heavy ions; the paper argues that the stiffness of the symmetry term can be distinguished by observables such as fragment charge asymmetry and isospin diffusion, but the conclusions are drawn from Stochastic Mean Field simulations and require further experimental confirmation.

## Method

The author performed Stochastic Mean Field transport simulations of heavy-ion collisions at Fermi energies (30-50 AMeV) for systems including Sn-Sn, Sn-Ni, Fe-Fe, and Ni-Ni isotopes, using three different parametrizations of the symmetry energy (asysoft, asystiff, and superasystiff). The simulations analyzed the isospin dynamics across all reaction phases: fast nucleon emission, mid-rapidity fragment formation in the neck region, and dynamical fission of spectator residues. The results were compared to experimental data on isospin diffusion from Tsang et al. (2004) and to the Wilczynski-2 plot analysis of fragment velocity correlations.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction $^{124}Sn + ^{112}Sn$ at 50 AMeV in peripheral collisions ($b/b_{max} > 0.8$), the isoscaling parameter $\alpha = 0.42 \pm 0.02$ for $^{124}Sn + ^{112}Sn$ differs substantially from $\alpha = 0.16 \pm 0.02$ for $^{112}Sn + ^{124}Sn$.
- The isospin imbalance ratio $R_P(\alpha)$ was estimated to be around 0.5 for the $^{124}Sn + ^{112}Sn$ system at 50 AMeV, versus 0.0 for full equilibration.
- Simulations were performed for $^{124}Sn + ^{64}Ni$ at 35 AMeV at impact parameters from $b = 5$ fm to $b = 8$ fm.
- For $^{124}Sn + ^{124}Sn$ at 50 AMeV, neck fragmentation events were simulated at $b = 4$ fm and $b = 6$ fm.
- For Fe-Fe and Ni-Ni (mass 58) systems, simulations were performed at 30 and 47 AMeV at $b = 4$ fm.

## Context

This work extends the study of isospin dynamics in heavy-ion reactions from the lower-energy deep-inelastic regime to the Fermi energy domain, where neck fragmentation becomes the dominant dissipative mechanism. It builds on earlier transport model studies by Bertsch (1984), Colonna (1992), and Baran (2003) that predicted neck instabilities, and on experimental observations by Montoya (1994), Dempsey (1996), and Tsang (2004) of isospin effects in mid-rapidity fragments. The paper enables future experiments with radioactive beams to probe the density dependence of the symmetry energy by measuring isotopic content of neck fragments, isospin diffusion via imbalance ratios, and correlations between fragment asymmetry and dynamical quantities at freeze-out.
