# From bare two-nucleon interaction to nuclear matter and finite nuclei in a relativistic framework

> Understanding nuclear forces, infinite nuclear matter, and finite nuclei within a unified framework has remained a central challenge in nuclear physics for decades. While most \textit{ab initio} studies employ nonrelativistic Schrödinger-equation frameworks, this work offers a relativistic perspective. Using a leading-order (LO) relativistic chiral interaction, we describe two-nucleon scattering via the Thompson equation, symmetric nuclear matter, and medium-mass nuclei (Ca, Ni, Zr, Sn) via the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory. Systematic uncertainties from regulator cutoffs and interaction parameters are analyzed. The empirical saturation region of nuclear matter is reproduced, and the binding energies and charge radii of medium-mass nuclei agree reasonably well with experimental data, significantly improving the ``Coester line". These results highlight that the relativistic approach, employing a leading-order chiral force with only four low-energy constants and no three-nucleon forces, can capture the most important dynamics and offer a complementary pathway to address longstanding challenges in nuclear \textit{ab initio} studies.

## Key claim

Using a leading-order relativistic chiral force with only four low-energy constants and no three-nucleon forces, the authors demonstrate that a relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock framework can simultaneously describe two-nucleon scattering, symmetric nuclear matter saturation, and binding energies and charge radii of medium-mass nuclei (A=40 to 120), though the description is not high-precision and systematic uncertainties from regulator cutoffs remain.

## Method

The authors fit four low-energy constants of a leading-order relativistic chiral interaction (one-pion exchange plus four contact terms) to neutron-proton scattering phase shifts up to 100 MeV using a local momentum-space regulator with cutoffs of 500, 600, and 700 MeV. They then solve the in-medium Thompson equation within relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory to compute the equation of state of symmetric nuclear matter and, for spherical finite nuclei, solve the Bethe-Goldstone equation in a Dirac Woods-Saxon basis to obtain binding energies and charge radii for isotopes from Ca to Sn.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: \(\rho = 0.164 \pm 0.007\) fm\(^{-3}\) (empirical region reproduced by the LO relativistic chiral force)
- Saturation energy per nucleon: \(E/A = -15.86 \pm 0.57\) MeV (empirical region reproduced)
- Cutoffs considered: \(\Lambda = 500, 600, 700\) MeV, with two parameter sets per cutoff
- Best-fit \(\tilde{\chi}^2/N\) values for phase shifts: 7.6 and 7.4 (\(\Lambda\)=500 MeV), 4.2 and 4.1 (\(\Lambda\)=600 MeV), 3.6 and 3.3 (\(\Lambda\)=700 MeV)
- Low-energy constants for \(\Lambda\)700-II set: \(C_{\rm S} = -674.85\) GeV\(^{-2}\), \(C_{\rm V} = 637.83\) GeV\(^{-2}\), \(C_{\rm AV} = -145.61\) GeV\(^{-2}\), \(C_{\rm T} = -62.93\) GeV\(^{-2}\)
- Basis parameters for finite nuclei: spherical box radius \(R = 7.5\) to 8.8 fm, single-particle angular momentum cutoff \(l_{\rm cut} = 15\) to \(22 \hbar\), two-particle coupled angular momentum up to \(J_{\rm cut} = 24\) for \(^{120}\)Sn
- Largest two-body matrix dimension: 50000 for a given coupled angular momentum, parity, and isospin

## Context

This work extends the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach, previously applied with phenomenological potentials like Bonn A (Shen 2017, 2018), to a chiral effective field theory interaction at leading order. It builds on earlier studies showing that relativistic effects can mimic three-nucleon forces in nonrelativistic frameworks (Brown 1985, Sammarruca 2012, Muther 2016) and on recent developments of covariant chiral forces (Lu 2021) that reproduce phase shifts up to N\(^2\)LO. The results significantly improve the "Coester line" problem and enable a unified description of scattering, nuclear matter, and finite nuclei using only two-nucleon forces, providing a foundation for future inclusion of higher-order chiral forces and more advanced many-body methods.
