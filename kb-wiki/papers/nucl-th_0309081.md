# Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov Calculations in Coordinate Space: Neutron-Rich Sulfur, Zirconium, Cerium, and Samarium Isotopes

> Using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) mean field theory in coordinate space, we investigate ground state properties of the sulfur isotopes from the line of stability up to the two-neutron dripline ($^{34-52}S$). In particular, we calculate two-neutron separation energies, quadrupole moments, and rms-radii for protons and neutrons. Evidence for shape coexistence is found in the very neutron-rich sulfur isotopes. We compare our calculations with results from relativistic mean field theory and with available experimental data. We also study the properties of neutron-rich zirconium ($^{102,104}Zr$), cerium ($^{152}Ce$), and samarium ($^{158,160}Sm$) isotopes which exhibit very large prolate quadrupole deformations.

## Key claim

Using Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory in coordinate space with a Skyrme SLy4 interaction and a delta pairing interaction, the authors predict that the two-neutron dripline for sulfur isotopes lies at N=36 (\({}^{50}\)S), whereas relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with BCS pairing predicts stability at least up to \({}^{56}\)S; the abstract does not specify that this disagreement is a central finding.

## Method

The authors solved the HFB equations on a two-dimensional cylindrical coordinate-space lattice using Basis-Spline methods of order M=7, with a grid extending from 0 to 12 fm radially and -12 to +12 fm axially, and a lattice spacing of about 0.8 fm. They used the Skyrme SLy4 effective interaction for the particle-hole channel and a delta interaction with pairing strength V0 = -170.0 MeV fm^3 for the particle-particle channel, with an equivalent single-particle energy cutoff of 60 MeV and maximum angular momentum projection Omega_max = 21/2. The HFB Hamiltonian was diagonalized separately for fixed isospin and angular momentum projection, yielding quasiparticle eigenstates up to about 1000 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Two-neutron dripline for sulfur isotopes: HFB predicts \({}^{50}\)S as the last stable isotope; RMF predicts stability at least up to \({}^{56}\)S.
- For \({}^{48}\)S, experimental two-neutron separation energy is 3.64 MeV; HFB gives 3.49 MeV; RMF gives 5.24 MeV.
- For \({}^{50}\)S, HFB yields neutron rms radius r_n = 3.935 fm and proton rms radius r_p = 3.364 fm.
- For \({}^{48}\)S, HFB ground state binding energy is -362.56 MeV with quadrupole deformation beta_2 = 0.11; an oblate minimum at beta_2 = -0.15 lies 0.49 MeV higher.
- For \({}^{104}\)Zr, HFB gives beta_2(p) = 0.45, matching the experimental value 0.45(4).
- For \({}^{102}\)Zr, HFB gives beta_2(p) = 0.43 (experiment: 0.42(5)) and proton rms radius r_p = 4.47 fm (experiment: 4.54 fm).
- For \({}^{152}\)Ce, HFB gives beta_2(p) = 0.32 (experiment: 0.30(3)).
- For \({}^{158}\)Sm, HFB gives binding energy -1290.2 MeV (experiment: -1291.9 MeV) and beta_2(p) = 0.375 (experiment: 0.46(5)); RMF gives beta_2(p) = 0.292.

## Context

This work extends earlier self-consistent mean-field studies of sulfur isotopes by Werner et al. (1994) using Skyrme-HF and RMF with a constant pairing gap approximation, and by Lalazissis et al. (1999) using RMF with BCS pairing. The authors' HFB lattice code, described in Teran et al. (2003), is the first to solve the HFB continuum problem in coordinate space for deformed nuclei in two dimensions without approximations, enabling treatment of high-energy continuum states up to 60 MeV. The calculations for zirconium, cerium, and samarium isotopes are compared with recent Gammasphere experiments by Hwang et al. (2003) and laser spectroscopy by Campbell et al. (2002). The work enables future studies of shape coexistence and neutron skins near the dripline, and provides predictions for \({}^{156}\)Nd and \({}^{160}\)Sm awaiting experimental data.
