# Charm and Bottom Hadrons in Hot Hadronic Matter

> Heavy quarks, and the hadrons containing them, are excellent probes of the QCD medium formed in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, as they provide essential information on the transport properties of the medium and how quarks color-neutralize into hadrons. Large theoretical and phenomenological efforts have been dedicated thus far to assess the diffusion of charm and bottom quarks in the quark-gluon plasma and their subsequent hadronization into heavy-flavor (HF) hadrons. However, the fireball formed in heavy-ion collisions also features an extended hadronic phase, and therefore any quantitative analysis of experimental observables needs to account for the rescattering of charm and bottom hadrons. This is further reinforced by the presence of a QCD cross-over transition and the notion that the interaction strength is maximal in the vicinity of the pseudo-critical temperature. We review existing approaches for evaluating the interactions of open HF hadrons in a hadronic heat bath and the pertinent results for scattering amplitudes, spectral functions and transport coefficients. While most of the work to date has focused on $D$-mesons, we also discuss excited states as well as HF baryons and the bottom sector. Both the HF hadro-chemistry and bottom observables will play a key role in future experimental measurements. We also conduct a survey of transport calculations in heavy-ion collisions that have included effects of hadronic HF diffusion and assess its impact on various observables.

## Key claim

The paper reviews existing approaches for evaluating interactions of open heavy-flavor hadrons in a hot hadronic medium, arguing that any quantitative analysis of experimental observables from heavy-ion collisions must account for the rescattering of charm and bottom hadrons in the hadronic phase, especially given that the interaction strength is maximal near the pseudo-critical temperature of the QCD crossover transition.

## Method

The authors survey theoretical frameworks for computing heavy-flavor hadron interactions in a hadronic heat bath, including heavy-meson chiral perturbation theory combined with heavy-quark spin symmetry for low-energy regimes, and unitarized approaches (such as Bethe-Salpeter equations) or explicit resonance models to incorporate resonance formation at higher energies. They then review how these interactions are used to evaluate in-medium spectral functions and transport coefficients (drag, momentum diffusion, and spatial diffusion coefficients), and assess the impact of hadronic diffusion on observables like the nuclear modification factor and elliptic flow in heavy-ion collision simulations.

## Key numbers

- The spatial diffusion coefficient for charm quarks in the QGP near the pseudo-critical temperature is constrained to a range of 1.5 ≤ (2πT)Ds ≤ 4.5, where Tpc ≈ 160 MeV.
- Hadronic diffusion effects are predicted to contribute at the level of approximately 10-30% to the final observed elliptic flow (v2) of D-mesons.
- The ratio of charm baryon to charm meson yields, Λc/D0, reaches up to approximately 0.5 at low transverse momentum in hadronic and heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC, which is a factor of nearly 5 larger than observed in e+e- annihilation.
- The D0*(2300) resonance in the Dπ channel has a vacuum width of Γ = 229 MeV.
- At T = 100 MeV, the average thermal pion energy is ⟨Eπ⟩ = mπ + 3/2 T ≈ 300 MeV, while for D-mesons ⟨ED⟩ ≈ 2 GeV, giving a center-of-mass energy √s ≈ 2.3 GeV for a head-on collision.

## Context

This review extends prior work on heavy-quark diffusion in the quark-gluon plasma (e.g., Rapp (2018), Dong (2019), He (2022)) by focusing specifically on the hadronic phase that follows hadronization in heavy-ion collisions. It builds on effective field theory developments from the 1990s and early 2000s (e.g., Isgur (1989), Yan (1992), Wise (1992), Hofmann (2003), Guo (2006, 2008, 2009)) and on four independent works from 2011 (Laine, He, Ghosh, Abreu) that first systematically computed D-meson transport properties. The review enables future precision assessments of heavy-flavor transport from heavy-ion data, particularly for bottom hadrons and heavy-flavor baryons where information is currently sparse, and for lower-energy collisions where the hadronic phase becomes more important.
