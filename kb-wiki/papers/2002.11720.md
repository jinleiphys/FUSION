# Scalar spectrum in a graviton soft wall model

> In this study we present a unified phenomenological analysis of the scalar glueball and scalar meson spectra within an AdS/QCD framework in the bottom up approach. For this purpose we generalize the recently developed graviton soft-wall (GSW) model, which has shown an excellent agreement with the lattice QCD glueball spectrum, to a description of glueballs and mesons with a unique energy scale. In this scheme, dilatonic effects, are incorporated in the metric as a deformation of the AdS space. We apply the model also to the heavy meson spectra with success. We obtain quadratic mass equations for all scalar mesons while the glueballs satisfy an almost linear mass equation. Besides their spectra, we also discuss the mixing of scalar glueball and light scalar meson states within a unified framework: the GSW model. To this aim, the light-front holographic approach, which connects the mode functions of AdS/QCD to the light-front wave functions, is applied. This relation provides the probabilistic interpretation required to properly investigate the mixing conditions.

## Key claim

The graviton soft-wall (GSW) model can simultaneously describe scalar glueball and scalar meson spectra using a single energy scale, but the glueball masses follow an almost linear relation with mode number while meson masses follow a quadratic relation; the mixing probability between glueball and meson states is small when their mode numbers differ significantly, even when their masses are similar.

## Method

The authors generalize the GSW model by incorporating an exponential factor \(e^{\alpha \varphi(z)}\) into the AdS metric, where \(\varphi(z)=kz^2\) is the dilaton profile. They solve the Einstein equation for the graviton to obtain glueball modes and the equation of motion for the scalar field to obtain meson modes, both reduced to Schrodinger-type equations. The glueball spectrum is fitted to lattice QCD data from Morningstar (1999), Chen (2005), and Lucini (2004) to fix the product \(\alpha k\), then the meson spectrum from the Particle Data Group is fitted to determine \(\alpha\) and \(k\) separately. Light-front holography is applied to connect the mode functions to probability distributions for studying glueball-meson mixing.

## Key numbers

- Glueball fit yields \(\alpha k = (370\ \text{MeV})^2\) as the single scale parameter
- Exact glueball mode eigenvalues: \(\Lambda^2_0 = 53.88\), \(\Lambda^2_1 = 82.17\), \(\Lambda^2_2 = 117.02\), \(\Lambda^2_3 = 157.95\) (dimensionless)
- Approximate glueball mode eigenvalues: \(\Lambda^2_n = 4n + 36\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\)
- Meson fit without \(f_0(500)\) gives \(\alpha = 0.55 \pm 0.04\)
- Meson fit including \(f_0(500)\) gives \(\alpha = 0.61 \pm 0.04\)
- Heavy meson fit uses \(C_c = 2400\ \text{MeV}\) for \(c\bar{c}\) states and \(C_b = 8700\ \text{MeV}\) for \(b\bar{b}\) states
- Dilatonic constant \(\sqrt{k} = 0.5\ \text{GeV}\)
- Lattice glueball masses: \(0^{++}\) ground state at \(1730 \pm 94\ \text{MeV}\) (Morningstar 1999), \(1719 \pm 94\ \text{MeV}\) (Chen 2005), \(1475 \pm 72\ \text{MeV}\) (Lucini 2004)
- PDG scalar meson masses: \(f_0(500)\) at \(475 \pm 75\ \text{MeV}\), \(f_0(980)\) at \(990 \pm 20\ \text{MeV}\), \(f_0(1370)\) at \(1350 \pm 150\ \text{MeV}\), \(f_0(1500)\) at \(1504 \pm 6\ \text{MeV}\), \(f_0(1710)\) at \(1723 \pm 6\ \text{MeV}\)

## Context

This work extends the GSW model previously developed by Rinaldi (2017) for glueballs to include scalar mesons, providing a unified description that was not achieved by conventional soft-wall models. The model reproduces the Regge trajectories of both light and heavy scalar mesons from the PDG while matching lattice QCD glueball data from Morningstar (1999), Chen (2005), and Lucini (2004). The light-front holographic approach, connecting AdS/QCD mode functions to light-front wave functions as in Brodsky (2003) and deTeramond (2005), enables a probabilistic analysis of glueball-meson mixing, suggesting that at high masses mixing may be suppressed and nearly pure glueball states could exist.
