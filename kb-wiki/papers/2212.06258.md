# Overview of Seniority Isomers

> Nuclear isomers are the metastable excited states of nuclei. The isomers can be categorized into a few classes including spin, seniority, \emph{K}, shape and fission isomers depending upon the hindrance mechanisms. In this paper, we aim to present an overview of seniority isomers, which is a category related to the seniority quantum number. The discussion is mainly based on the concepts of seniority and generalized seniority. Various aspects of seniority isomers and their whereabouts have been covered along with the situations where seniority mixing prevents the isomerism.

## Key claim

This paper provides a pedagogical overview of seniority isomers, a class of nuclear isomers arising from Racah's seniority selection rules, but the authors explicitly state that it is "not an extensive review" of all recent efforts, instead focusing on a general overview based on simple concepts of seniority and generalized seniority in and around semi-magic nuclei.

## Method

The authors review the quasi-spin algebra formalism for both single-j and multi-j shells, deriving the seniority reduction formulas for electromagnetic transition probabilities and magnetic moments. They then apply these selection rules to identify where seniority isomerism occurs: at the middle of the valence shell where the factor (Ω−n) in the electric transition matrix elements causes vanishing B(EL) values, leading to extra hindrance and longer half-lives. The discussion covers both single-j and multi-j (generalized seniority) cases, including the recently identified possibility of odd-electric tensor seniority isomers in multi-j shells.

## Key numbers

- For a single-j shell with j=9/2, the off-diagonal interaction matrix element between two similar J states with different seniority is only "a few tens of keV," while the two J=2⁺ states with v=2 and v=4 have an energy splitting of about 1 MeV
- The pairing energy eigenvalue for a fully paired J=0, v=0 state is −nG, i.e., −(2j+1)G, where G is the pairing strength
- For the limiting value of Ω = n/2, the pairing energy value becomes zero for v=2 states
- The magnetic moment μ/J ratio (g-factor) for any seniority state in the jⁿ configuration is about equivalent to the g-factor of a single seniority (v=1) state
- The generalized seniority Schmidt Model (GSSM) uses g_s=5.59 nuclear magnetons and g_l=1 nuclear magneton for protons, and g_s=−3.83 nuclear magnetons and g_l=0 nuclear magnetons for neutrons

## Context

This overview extends the foundational work on seniority by Racah (1943), Flowers (1952), and Talmi (1971, 1993), and builds upon the specific discussion of seniority isomers for j=9/2 by Lanford (1969) and Van Isacker (2011). The authors highlight that the crucial consequences of generalized seniority selection rules on electromagnetic transitions were not pointed out until Maheshwari (2016), which opened the prospect of good seniority states decaying via M2, M4, E1, or E3 transitions rather than only M1, M3, E2, or E4 transitions. The paper enables future work by providing the theoretical framework for identifying seniority isomers in semi-magic nuclei across the nuclear chart, particularly in the 50–82 and 82–126 nucleonic valence spaces where higher-j orbitals (j>7/2) are found.
