# Wilson loops with neural networks

> Wilson loops are essential objects in QCD and have been pivotal in scale setting and demonstrating confinement. Various generalizations are crucial for computations needed in effective field theories. In lattice gauge theory, Wilson loop calculations face challenges, including excited-state contamination at short times and the signal-to-noise ratio issue at longer times. To address these problems, we develop a new method by using neural networks to parametrize interpolators for the static quark-antiquark pair. We construct gauge-equivariant layers for the network and train it to find the ground state of the system. The trained network itself is then treated as our new observable for the inference. Our results demonstrate a significant improvement in the signal compared to traditional Wilson loops, performing as well as Coulomb-gauge Wilson-line correlators while maintaining gauge invariance. Additionally, we present an example where the optimized ground state is used to measure the static force directly, as well as another example combining this method with the multilevel algorithm. Finally, we extend the formalism to find excited-state interpolators for static quark-antiquark systems. To our knowledge, this work is the first study of neural networks with a physically motivated loss function for Wilson loops.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that neural networks can parametrize gauge-equivariant interpolators for static quark-antiquark pairs, producing Wilson loops with signal quality matching Coulomb-gauge Wilson-line correlators while maintaining explicit gauge invariance; this is claimed as the first study to use a physically motivated loss function for neural-network-based Wilson loops.

## Method

The spatial Wilson line in a rectangular Wilson loop is replaced by a feedforward neural network whose layers (linear, bilinear, convolutional, and SU(3) exponential activation) are constructed to be gauge equivariant, preserving the gauge transformation property of the static quark-antiquark pair. The network is trained on lattice gauge configurations to maximize the ground-state overlap \(|c_0|^2\) by minimizing a loss function derived from the effective mass at short Euclidean times, with progressive layer addition to balance expressivity and numerical stability. The trained network output \(\tilde{S}\) is then used as the spatial interpolator in the Wilson loop observable, and the formalism is extended to excited states and to the static force via plaquette insertions on the temporal Wilson lines.

## Key numbers

* The initial number of plaquette-inserted Wilson lines (input channels) is \(24 + 16r\) for a given on-axis separation \(r\) in lattice units.
* The first linear layer reduces the number of channels to \(N^{(1)} = 16\) or \(N^{(1)} = 14\) as a memory-saving step.
* The norm of an SU(3) matrix state is always equal to 3, independent of the network weights.
* The static force is computed not by a discrete difference but by insertion of plaquettes along the temporal Wilson lines, following the approach of Vairo (2016), Brambilla (2021), and Brambilla (2023).

## Context

This work extends the lattice gauge theory methodology for Wilson loops, which have been foundational for scale setting and demonstrating confinement since Wilson (1974). It addresses the excited-state contamination at short times and signal-to-noise degradation at long times that plague traditional Wilson loop calculations. The neural-network approach is compared favorably to Coulomb-gauge Wilson-line correlators as used by Brambilla (2022), and it is complementary to machine-learning methods such as contour deformation (Detmold 2020, 2021) and renormalization-group-improved actions (Holland 2025). The technique enables direct measurement of the static force and can be combined with the multilevel algorithm; future applications to hybrid states, tetraquark potentials, and off-axis separations are anticipated.
