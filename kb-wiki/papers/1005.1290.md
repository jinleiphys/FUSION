# Replacing the Breit-Wigner amplitude by the complex delta function to describe resonances

> Whenever the Breit-Wigner amplitude appears in a calculation,there are many instances (e.g., Fermi's two-level system and the Weisskopf-Wigner approximation) where energy integrations are extended from the scattering spectrum of the Hamiltonian to the whole real line. Such extensions are performed in order to obtain a desirable, causal result. In this paper, we recall several of those instances and show that substituting the Breit-Wigner amplitude by the complex delta function allows us to recover such desirable results without having to extend energy integrations outside of the scattering spectrum.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the exact resonant amplitude is given by the complex delta function rather than the Breit-Wigner amplitude, and that substituting the Breit-Wigner amplitude by the complex delta function allows one to recover causal results for resonance decay without extending energy integrations from the scattering spectrum (which has a lower bound) to the whole real line.

## Method

The author recalls three standard examples where the Breit-Wigner amplitude is used: Fermi's two-level system, Taylor's treatment of resonant state decay, and the Weisskopf-Wigner approximation. In each case, the calculation requires extending an energy integral over the positive scattering spectrum to the whole real line to obtain a causal result. The author then shows that replacing the Breit-Wigner amplitude (proportional to 1/(E - z_R)) with the complex delta function delta(E - z_R), where z_R = E_R - i Gamma/2 is the complex resonant energy, yields the same exponential decay factor exp(-iE_R t) exp(-Gamma t/2) when integrated over the positive energy spectrum alone, without any extension to negative energies.

## Key numbers

* The complex resonant energy is written as z_R = E_R - i Gamma/2, where E_R is the resonant energy and Gamma is the decay width.
* The complex delta function is defined by the distributional relation: integral from 0 to infinity of dE g(E) delta(E - z_R) = g(z_R).
* The result obtained using the complex delta function is f(z_R) exp(-iE_R t) exp(-Gamma t/2) for t > 0, which up to a numerical factor matches the result from extending the Breit-Wigner integral to the whole real line.

## Context

This work extends the program of describing resonances via Gamow states, which are eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian with purely outgoing boundary conditions and eigenvalues equal to the poles of the S-matrix. The paper directly addresses a problem identified by Shirokov (1966) and generalized by Hegerfeldt (1994): that the semiboundedness of the Hamiltonian leads to conflicts with causality when using the Breit-Wigner amplitude. The author shows that using the complex delta function, previously introduced by Nakanishi for the Lee model and used by several authors, resolves this conflict without the approximation of extending energy integrations to negative energies, providing another argument for treating the complex delta function as the exact resonant amplitude.
