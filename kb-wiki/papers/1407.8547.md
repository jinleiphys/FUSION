# Mesoscopic superconductivity in ultrasmall metallic grains

> A nano-scale metallic grain (nanoparticle) with irregular boundaries in which the single-particle dynamics are chaotic is a zero-dimensional system described by the so-called universal Hamiltonian in the limit of a large number of electrons. The interaction part of this Hamiltonian includes a superconducting pairing term and a ferromagnetic exchange term. Spin-orbit scattering breaks spin symmetry and suppresses the exchange interaction term. Of particular interest is the fluctuation-dominated regime, typical of the smallest grains in the experiments, in which the bulk pairing gap is comparable to or smaller than the single-particle mean-level spacing, and the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) mean-field theory of superconductivity is no longer valid. Here we study the crossover between the BCS and fluctuation-dominated regimes in two limits. In the absence of spin-orbit scattering, the pairing and exchange interaction terms compete with each other. We describe the signatures of this competition in thermodynamic observables, the heat capacity and spin susceptibility. In the presence of strong spin-orbit scattering, the exchange interaction term can be ignored. We discuss how the magnetic-field response of discrete energy levels in such a nanoparticle is affected by pairing correlations. We identify signatures of pairing correlations in this response, which are detectable even in the fluctuation-dominated regime.

## Key claim

The paper studies the crossover between BCS and fluctuation-dominated regimes in ultrasmall metallic grains, finding that pairing correlations can be detected through thermodynamic observables and magnetic-field response even when the bulk pairing gap is comparable to or smaller than the single-particle mean-level spacing, a regime where BCS mean-field theory is no longer valid.

## Method

The authors employ a static-path approximation plus random-phase-approximation (SPA+RPA) approach to treat the pairing Hamiltonian, combined with spin projection techniques to handle the exchange interaction exactly. For grains with strong spin-orbit scattering, they use exact diagonalization in a model space of 13 single-particle levels and BCS-like calculations with 121 levels to study the magnetic-field response of discrete energy levels. Single-particle spectra are sampled from random-matrix theory ensembles (GOE for no spin-orbit, GSE for strong spin-orbit) to account for mesoscopic fluctuations.

## Key numbers

- Heat capacity and spin susceptibility calculated for grains with $\Delta/\delta = 0.5$, $1.0$, and $3.0$, with exchange interaction strengths $J_s/\delta = 0.0$, $0.4$, $0.8$ (heat capacity) and $J_s/\delta = 0.0$, $0.3$, $0.6$ (spin susceptibility)
- For $\Delta/\delta = 0.5$ and $J_s/\delta = 0.6$, the re-entrant behavior in the average spin susceptibility disappears entirely
- Level curvature distributions shown for $\Delta/\delta = 0$, $0.4$, and $3.0$, with curvatures expressed in units of the midspread $d_0$ of the single-particle curvature distribution
- The median curvature shifts negative with increasing $\Delta/\delta$, and the dispersion grows significantly

## Context

This work extends the study of mesoscopic superconductivity from the BCS regime (Anderson 1959, Bardeen 1957) into the fluctuation-dominated regime where $\Delta/\delta \lesssim 1$, relevant to experiments on Al nanoparticles (Black 1996, von Delft 2001). The universal Hamiltonian framework (Kurland 2000, Aleiner 2002) is used to include both pairing and exchange interactions. The results connect to nuclear physics where $\Delta/\delta \sim 3$ (Alhassid 2013), and the level curvature probe offers a new experimental signature detectable via single-electron tunneling spectroscopy, complementing earlier work on $g$-factor statistics in noble-metal nanoparticles (Petta 2001, Kuemmeth 2008).
