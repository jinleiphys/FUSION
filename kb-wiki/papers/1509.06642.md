# Precision Electron-Beam Polarimetry using Compton Scattering at 1 GeV

> We report on the highest precision yet achieved in the measurement of the polarization of a low energy, $\mathcal{O}$(1 GeV), electron beam, accomplished using a new polarimeter based on electron-photon scattering, in Hall~C at Jefferson Lab. A number of technical innovations were necessary, including a novel method for precise control of the laser polarization in a cavity and a novel diamond micro-strip detector which was able to capture most of the spectrum of scattered electrons. The data analysis technique exploited track finding, the high granularity of the detector and its large acceptance. The polarization of the $180~μ$A, $1.16$~GeV electron beam was measured with a statistical precision of $<$~1\% per hour and a systematic uncertainty of 0.59\%. This exceeds the level of precision required by the \qweak experiment, a measurement of the vector weak charge of the proton. Proposed future low-energy experiments require polarization uncertainty $<$~0.4\%, and this result represents an important demonstration of that possibility. This measurement is also the first use of diamond detectors for particle tracking in an experiment.

# Precision Electron-Beam Polarimetry using Compton Scattering at 1 GeV

## Key claim

The authors report the highest precision yet achieved for measuring the polarization of a low-energy (approximately 1 GeV) electron beam, with a systematic uncertainty of 0.59%, but this result is conditional on the specific beam energy of 1.16 GeV and a beam current of 180 microamps, and the quoted precision exceeds the 1% requirement of the Qweak experiment but falls short of the 0.4% requirement for future experiments like SOLID and MOLLER.

## Method

A Compton polarimeter was constructed in Hall C at Jefferson Lab, using a Fabry-Perot optical cavity to store circularly polarized 532 nm laser photons that interacted with the 1.16 GeV continuous-wave electron beam in a magnetic chicane. Scattered electrons were detected by four planes of diamond microstrip detectors with 200 micron strip pitch, operated in single-electron mode with a track-finding trigger requiring hits in 2 out of 3 planes. The electron beam polarization was extracted by fitting the measured asymmetry to the theoretical Compton asymmetry, with the laser polarization maximized using an optical reversibility technique that minimized back-reflected light through a polarizing beam splitter.

## Key numbers

- Electron beam polarization measured with systematic uncertainty of 0.59% for a 1.16 GeV, 180 microamp beam
- Statistical precision of less than 1% per hour routinely achieved
- Laser polarization uncertainty estimated at 0.18%
- Laser intensity of approximately 1.7 kW stored in the Fabry-Perot cavity with gain of approximately 200
- Detector signal approximately 9000 electrons per hit, with noise of approximately 1000 electrons
- Detector efficiency estimated at 70%
- Total untriggered rate of 130-180 kHz (approximately 2.5 kHz per strip) at 180 microamps beam current
- Signal-to-background ratio of 5-20 depending on detector strip
- Ratio of Compton to Moller polarization measurements was 1.007 plus or minus 0.003, compatible within total relative normalization uncertainty of 0.77%
- Detectors exposed to radiation dose of 100 kGy over 2 years with no performance degradation
- Maximum Compton asymmetry of approximately 0.04 at 1.16 GeV, with only 5% of electron energy transferred to the photon

## Context

This work extends Compton polarimetry techniques previously applied at higher energies (such as the SLD polarimeter at 46.5 GeV with 0.5% precision) to the challenging low-energy regime of approximately 1 GeV, where the maximum asymmetry is only about 0.04. The result directly compares with the Hall C Moller polarimeter (Magee, prior work) and demonstrates better than 1% agreement between two independent polarimeters for the first time. The measurement enables future parity-violation experiments at Jefferson Lab such as SOLID and MOLLER, which require polarization uncertainty below 0.4%, and establishes diamond microstrip detectors as viable tracking detectors in high-radiation environments.
