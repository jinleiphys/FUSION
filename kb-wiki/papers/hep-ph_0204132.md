# Thermal conductivity of dense quark matter and cooling of stars

> The thermal conductivity of the color-flavor locked phase of dense quark matter is calculated. The dominant contribution to the conductivity comes from photons and Nambu-Goldstone bosons associated with breaking of baryon number which are trapped in the quark core. Because of their very large mean free path the conductivity is also very large. The cooling of the quark core arises mostly from the heat flux across the surface of direct contact with the nuclear matter. As the thermal conductivity of the neighboring layer is also high, the whole interior of the star should be nearly isothermal. Our results imply that the cooling time of compact stars with color-flavor locked quark cores is similar to that of ordinary neutron stars.

## Key claim

The thermal conductivity of the color-flavor locked (CFL) phase of dense quark matter is very high, dominated by photons and the massless Nambu-Goldstone boson associated with breaking of baryon number, but the cooling time of compact stars with CFL quark cores remains similar to that of ordinary neutron stars because the thermal energy of the quark core is negligible compared to that of the surrounding nuclear matter.

## Method

The authors derived the thermal conductivity of the CFL phase from a Kubo-type formula using the spectral function of the (pseudo-) Nambu-Goldstone bosons, then estimated the mean free path of the massless NG boson from its decay into quark quasiparticles and from self-scattering. They also calculated the photon contribution to conductivity, accounting for reflection of photons at the quark-nuclear interface due to the plasma frequency of electrons in nuclear matter. The total thermal conductivity was then used to analyze the cooling dynamics of a compact star with a CFL quark core.

## Key numbers

- The mean free path of the NG boson from decay into quarks is approximately 30 km or more when \(T \lesssim \Delta/33\) with \(\Delta \simeq 50\) MeV.
- The mean free path from NG boson self-scattering is \(\ell_{\phi\phi} \sim 8 \times 10^5 \, \mu_{500}^8 / T_{MeV}^9\) km, where \(\mu_{500} \equiv \mu/(500\ \text{MeV})\) and \(T_{MeV} \equiv T/(1\ \text{MeV})\).
- The plasma frequency of nuclear matter is \(\Omega_p \simeq 4.7 \times 10^2 \sqrt{\rho Y_e / \rho_0}\) MeV, with \(Y_e \simeq 0.1\) and \(\rho_0 \approx 2.8 \times 10^{14}\ \text{g cm}^{-3}\).
- The total thermal conductivity of the CFL phase is \(\kappa_{CFL} \simeq 1.2 \times 10^{32} \, T_{MeV}^{3} R_{0,km}\ \text{erg cm}^{-1} \text{sec}^{-1} \text{K}^{-1}\), where \(R_{0,km}\) is the quark core radius in kilometers.
- The thermal energy of the CFL quark core is \(E_{CFL}(T) \simeq 2.1 \times 10^{42} R_{0,km}^{3} T_{MeV}^{4}\ \text{erg}\).
- The thermal energy of the outer nuclear layer is \(E_{NM}(T) \simeq 8.1 \times 10^{49} \frac{M-M_0}{M_\odot} (\rho_0/\rho)^{2/3} T_{MeV}^{2}\ \text{erg}\).
- A temperature gradient of 1 MeV across a core of 1 km is washed away in approximately \(6 \times 10^{-4}\) seconds.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of neutrino and photon emission rates in the CFL phase by Jaikumar, Prakash, and Schäfer (2002), which found those rates to be very small for cooling. The authors also build on the low-energy effective action for NG bosons derived by Casalbuoni and Gatto (1999) and by Son and Stephanov (2000), and on the modified action by Bedaque and Schäfer (2002) that predicted kaon condensation. The results imply that the cooling time of stars with CFL quark cores is essentially the same as for ordinary neutron stars, as studied by Page, Lattimer, Prakash, and Steiner (2000), because the quark core's thermal energy is negligible and heat is efficiently transferred to the nuclear layer.
