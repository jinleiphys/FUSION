# Nuclear matter fourth-order symmetry energy in non-relativistic mean-field models

> Based on systematic analyses of several popular non-relativistic energy density functionals with mean-field approximation, we estimate the value of the fourth-order symmetry energy $E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ)$ at nuclear normal density $ρ_0$ and its density dependence, and explore the correlation between $E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ and other macroscopic quantities of nuclear matter properties. We use the empirical values of some nuclear macroscopic quantities to construct model parameter sets by Monte Carlo method for the conventional Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) model, the extended Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (eSHF) model, the Gogny-Hartree-Fock (GHF) model, and the momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) model. The value of $E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ is estimated to be $1.02\pm0.49$ MeV for the SHF model, $1.02\pm0.50$ MeV for the eSHF model, $0.70\pm0.60$ MeV for the GHF model, and $0.74\pm0.63$ MeV for the MDI model. Moreover, our results indicate that the density dependence of $E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ)$ is model dependent, especially at higher densities. Furthermore, we find that the $E_{\text{sym},4}(ρ_0)$ has strong positive (negative) correlation with isoscalar (isovector) nucleon effective mass $m_{s,0}^*$ ($m_{v,0}^*$) at $ρ_0$. In particular, for the SHF and eSHF models, the $E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ)$ is completely determined by the isoscalar and isovector nucleon effective masses $m_{s}^*(ρ)$ and $m_{v}^*(ρ)$, and the analytical expression is given. In the mean-field models, the magnitude of $ E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ is generally less than $2$ MeV, and its density dependence depends on models, especially at higher densities. $ E_{\text{sym,4}}(ρ_0)$ is strongly correlated with $m_{s,0}^*$ and $m_{v,0}^*$.

## Key claim

Based on systematic analyses of four non-relativistic mean-field models, the fourth-order symmetry energy at saturation density is estimated to be generally less than 2 MeV, contradicting some recent predictions of much larger values; the density dependence of this quantity is model dependent, especially at higher densities.

## Method

The authors used Monte Carlo sampling to construct 0.1 million parameter sets for each of four energy density functionals: the conventional Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) model, the extended Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (eSHF) model, the Gogny-Hartree-Fock (GHF) model, and the momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) model. Each parameter set was determined by randomly sampling macroscopic nuclear matter quantities (such as saturation density, incompressibility, symmetry energy, and nucleon effective masses) from Gaussian distributions centered on empirical values with their uncertainties. The fourth-order symmetry energy was then evaluated analytically for each parameter set, and the resulting distributions were analyzed statistically.

## Key numbers

- SHF model: E_sym,4(rho_0) = 1.02 +/- 0.49 MeV
- eSHF model: E_sym,4(rho_0) = 1.02 +/- 0.50 MeV
- GHF model: E_sym,4(rho_0) = 0.70 +/- 0.60 MeV
- MDI model: E_sym,4(rho_0) = 0.74 +/- 0.63 MeV
- In all four models, the magnitude of E_sym,4(rho_0) is generally less than 2 MeV (within about 3-sigma)
- Linear correlation coefficient between E_sym,4(rho_0) and isoscalar effective mass m*_{s,0}: 0.41 (SHF), 0.39 (eSHF), 0.59 (GHF), 0.67 (MDI)
- Linear correlation coefficient between E_sym,4(rho_0) and isovector effective mass m*_{v,0}: -0.85 (SHF), -0.82 (eSHF), -0.74 (GHF), -0.70 (MDI)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the fourth-order symmetry energy that had produced widely conflicting predictions: while non-relativistic mean-field models (Chen 2009, Agrawal 2017), relativistic mean-field models (Cai 2012), and chiral pion-nucleon dynamics (Kaiser 2015) found values below 2 MeV, quantum molecular dynamics (Nandi 2016) predicted 3.27-12.7 MeV, and an extended semi-empirical mass formula (Wang 2017) estimated 20.0 +/- 4.6 MeV. The present systematic analysis across four models with consistent empirical constraints supports the lower range and identifies strong correlations with nucleon effective masses, which may guide future experimental constraints from heavy-ion collisions and neutron star observations.
