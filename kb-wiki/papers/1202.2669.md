# Stau-catalyzed d-t Nuclear Fusion

> The gravitino of mass 10-100 GeV is a well motivated scenario in supergravity. If the stau is the next lightest supersymmetry particle, its life-time becomes order of $10^{6-8}$ sec. If it is the case the stau makes a big impact on the nuclear fusion, since it is a charged particle. In this paper we perform a detailed calculation of a stau-catalyzed d-t fusion. We find that if certain technical conditions are satisfied, it is not hopeless to use the nuclear fusion as a source of energy.

# Stau-catalyzed d-t Nuclear Fusion

## Key claim

If the stau is the next-lightest supersymmetric particle with a lifetime of order 10^6-8 seconds, and if certain technical conditions for stau production and recycling are satisfied, then stau-catalyzed d-t fusion could in principle serve as a source of energy, yielding approximately 12 GeV per stau per chain, but the authors note that practical energy production would require recycling the stau at least 10^13 times, making the scheme currently impractical.

## Method

The authors performed a fully quantum-mechanical three-body calculation of the reactions (stau t)_1s + d → α + n + stau and (stau d)_1s + t → α + n + stau, using a complex nuclear optical-model potential between d and t fitted to reproduce the observed low-energy d-t fusion cross section. They solved the Schrödinger equation for the d + t + stau system using a combination of a scattering wave function for the incident channel and a short-range correlation term expanded in three-body Gaussian basis functions, then extracted the absorption cross section from the S-matrix. The sticking probability for stau-α bound state formation was computed in the sudden approximation from overlap integrals of the initial scattering wave function at the instant of fusion with final bound-state wave functions.

## Key numbers

- Reaction rate for (stau t)_1s + d: ⟨σv⟩ = 9.7 × 10^{-15} cm^3 s^{-1}, independent of temperature for kT ≲ 10^1 eV
- Reaction rate for (stau d)_1s + t: ⟨σv⟩ = 2.4 × 10^{-15} cm^3 s^{-1}, independent of temperature for kT ≲ 10^1 eV
- Averaged fusion cycling rate λ_f = 2.6 × 10^8 s^{-1} at density n ≃ 4.25 × 10^{22} atoms/cm^3
- Averaged sticking probability ω̄_s = 1.5 × 10^{-3}
- Energy product per stau per chain: E_stau dt = 17.6 MeV / (1.5 × 10^{-3}) ≃ 12 GeV
- Time scale of one cycle: 1/λ_c = 3.8 × 10^{-9} sec
- Time scale of a single chain: 2.5 × 10^{-6} sec
- Binding energy of (stau t)_1s: 74 keV; Bohr radius: 10 fm (for stau mass 200 GeV)
- Binding energy of (stau d)_1s: 50 keV; Bohr radius: 15 fm (for stau mass 200 GeV)
- Cross section for (stau t)_1s + d: σ(E) = 1.2 × 10^{-20} / √(E(eV)) cm^2
- Cross section for (stau d)_1s + t: σ(E) = 2.5 × 10^{-21} / √(E(eV)) cm^2

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study of stau-catalyzed d-d fusion (Hamaguchi, Hatsuda, Yanagida, 2006) to the d-t system, and is analogous to muon-catalyzed fusion studied by Kamimura (1989) and others (Zweig, Okun). Unlike the d-d case, the d-t system has no three-body bound state of (stau d t), so the fusion proceeds via "in-flight" collisions of neutral stau atoms with the complementary nucleus. The calculated sticking probability (1.5 × 10^{-3}) is several times smaller than the muon-catalyzed d-t fusion value (about 0.01), which is favorable for energy production, but the enormous difficulty of producing staus in the laboratory, requiring at least 10^14 GeV of input energy per stau in an optimistic estimate, means that practical energy generation would require recycling each stau approximately 10^13 times, necessitating a fast reactivation mechanism for sticked stau-α atoms. The authors also note that long-lived staus could serve as probes of the deep interior of heavy nuclei through exotic Coulomb bound states.
