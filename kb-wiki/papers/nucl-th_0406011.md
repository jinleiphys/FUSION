# Determination of S17(0) from published data

> The experimental landscape for the 7Be+p radiative capture reaction is rapidly changing as new high precision data become available. We present an evaluation of existing data, detailing the treatment of systematic errors and discrepancies, and show how they constrain the astrophysical S factor (S17), independent of any nuclear structure model. With theoretical models robustly determining the behavior of the sub-threshold pole, the extrapolation error can be reduced and a constraint placed on the slope of S17. Using only radiative capture data, we find S17(0) = 20.7 +/- 0.6 (stat) +/- 1.0 (syst) eV b if data sets are completely independent, while if data sets are completely correlated we find S17(0) = 21.4 +/- 0.5 (stat) +/- 1.4 (syst) eV b. The truth likely lies somewhere in between these two limits. Although we employ a formalism capable of treating discrepant data, we note that the central value of the S factor is dominated by the recent high precision data of Junghans et al., which imply a substantially higher value than other radiative capture and indirect measurements. Therefore we conclude that further progress will require new high precision data with a detailed error budget.

## Key claim

The authors find that the central value of the astrophysical S factor S17(0) is dominated by the recent high precision data of Junghans et al., which imply a substantially higher value than other radiative capture and indirect measurements, and that the truth likely lies somewhere between the two limiting cases of completely independent data sets (S17(0) = 20.7 ± 0.6 (stat) ± 1.0 (syst) eV b) and completely correlated data sets (S17(0) = 21.4 ± 0.5 (stat) ± 1.4 (syst) eV b).

## Method

The authors performed a statistical evaluation of existing 7Be(p,γ)8B radiative capture and Coulomb dissociation data below Ecm = 425 keV, using a formalism that propagates systematic normalization errors and defines a discrepancy error as a measure of fit quality. They applied two limiting treatments of multiple data sets (completely correlated and completely independent) and used three fitting approaches: a structure-model-dependent analysis using the Davids-Typel potential model and the Descouvemont-Dufour cluster model, a three-parameter pole model independent of nuclear structure, and a constrained two-parameter pole model with the subthreshold pole term fixed at a = 45 keV based on theoretical agreement.

## Key numbers

- S17(0) = 20.7 ± 0.6 (stat) ± 1.0 (syst) eV b for completely independent data sets using only radiative capture data
- S17(0) = 21.4 ± 0.5 (stat) ± 1.4 (syst) eV b for completely correlated data sets using only radiative capture data
- Using the DT 7Li + n potential model with independent normalization, all radiative capture data give S17(0) = 20.8 ± 0.4 (stat) eV b with a 5.1% discrepancy error
- Using the DD cluster model with independent normalization, all radiative capture data give S17(0) = 21.8 ± 0.4 (stat) eV b with a 4.9% discrepancy error
- The pole model analysis yields S17(0) between 19-24 eV b with a total uncertainty of ±3.5 eV b, independent of any structure model
- The constrained pole model with a = 45 keV gives S17(0) = 21.4 ± 0.6 (stat) eV b for Junghans-BE3 data with a 1.3% discrepancy error
- The pole term a is robustly determined by theory to lie in the range [44, 46] keV

## Context

This work extends the analysis techniques developed by Cyburt (2004) for big bang nucleosynthesis reaction cross sections to the 7Be(p,γ)8B reaction, which is critical for solar neutrino flux predictions. The authors find that nearly all previous analyses have underestimated the error in S17(0), and that the Junghans et al. (2003) data dominate the combined fit due to their small errors, showing a 1-2σ disagreement with other radiative capture measurements and a little over 2σ disagreement with Coulomb dissociation data from Davids et al. (2003) and Schumann et al. (2003). The analysis enables a robust determination of S17(0) independent of nuclear structure models, but the authors conclude that further progress requires new high precision data with a detailed error budget.
