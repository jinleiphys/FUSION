# Fissibility of compound nuclei

> Collisions between $^{248}$Cm and $^{48}$Ca are systematically investigated by time-dependent density functional calculations with evaporation prescription. Depending on the incident energy and impact parameter, fusion, deep-inelastic and fission events are expected to appear. In this paper, a microscopic method of calculating the fissibility of compound nuclei is presented.

## Key claim

The paper presents a microscopic method for calculating the fissibility (fission probability) of compound nuclei using time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) with an evaporation prescription, but the method is limited to collective dynamics and cannot explain very mass-asymmetric fission events such as alpha decay, which require additional thermal effects.

## Method

Time-dependent density functional calculations with a Skyrme interaction (SLy6) were performed for collisions between ²⁴⁸Cm and ⁴⁸Ca in a spatial box of 48 × 48 × 24 fm³ with periodic boundary conditions. The fissibility was obtained by performing additional TDDFT calculations starting from configurations of two nuclei at systematically varied initial distances R₀, where the initial many-body wave function was given as a single Slater determinant with orthogonalized single wave functions. The evaporation prescription was applied to the TDDFT final products to account for thermal effects, since TDDFT alone does not include thermal instability.

## Key numbers

- Compound nucleus ²⁹⁶Lv has fissility x = 0.909, calculated as 116²/(296 × 50)
- The contact distance for symmetric fission of ²⁹⁶Lv → ¹⁴⁸Ce + ¹⁴⁸Ce is 16.3 fm
- The contact distance for asymmetric fission of ²⁹⁶Lv → ⁴⁰Ca + ²⁵⁶Cm is 13.7 fm
- The Coulomb energy of symmetric fission is 1.75 times larger than that of asymmetric fission with mass asymmetry ratio 0.16
- The surface energy ratio between symmetric and asymmetric (mass asymmetry 0.16) fission is only 1.08
- The unit spatial spacing was fixed to 1.0 fm and the unit time spacing to 2/3 × 10⁻²⁴ s
- The spatial box dimensions were 48 × 48 × 24 fm³

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous research on evaporation prescriptions (Iwata 2012) and charge equilibration in heavy-ion reactions (Iwata 2010) to provide a microscopic derivation of fissibility. The method contrasts with the phenomenological liquid drop model fissility x, which only accounts for symmetric fission through the balance of surface and Coulomb effects. The results enable future investigation of the intermediate mass-asymmetry region (0.16 < min(A₁/A₂, A₂/A₁) < 1) and suggest that very mass-asymmetric fission requires thermal effects beyond collective dynamics, pointing to a transition in fission mechanism.
