# Towards constraining QCD phase transitions in neutron star interiors: Bayesian Inference with TOV linear response analysis

> The potential hadron-to-quark phase transition in neutron stars has not been fully understood as the property of cold, dense, and strongly interacting matter cannot be theoretically described by the first-principle perturbative calculations, nor have they been systematically measured through terrestrial low-to-intermediate energy heavy-ion experiments. Given the Tolman--Oppenheimer--Volkoff (TOV) equations, the equation of state (EoS) of the neutron star (NS) matter can be constrained by the observations of NS mass, radius, and tidal deformability. However, large observational uncertainties and the limited number of observations currently make it challenging to strictly reconstruct the EoS, especially to identify interesting features such as a strong first-order phase transition. In this work, we study the dependency of reconstruction quality of the phase transition on the number of NS observations of mass and radius as well as their uncertainty, based on a fiducial EoS. We conquer this challenging problem by constructing a neural network, which allows one to parametrize the EoS with minimum model-dependency, and by devising an algorithm of parameter optimization based on the analytical linear response analysis of the TOV equations. This work may pave the way for the understanding of the phase transition features in NSs using future $x$-ray and gravitational wave measurements.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a neural-network-based nonparametric Bayesian inference framework, combined with an analytical linear response analysis of the TOV equations, can systematically quantify how many neutron star mass-radius observations and what level of precision are required to identify a first-order hadron-to-quark phase transition in neutron star interiors, but only as a proof-of-principle study based on a fiducial equation of state.

## Method

The authors represent the equation of state (EoS) using a deep neural network with a large number of parameters (N_prm ~ 10^2-10^3), then solve the TOV equations directly in log-pressure coordinates to compute neutron star mass, radius, and tidal deformability. They derive analytical formulas for the linear response (functional derivatives) of these observables with respect to arbitrary perturbations in the EoS and in the central pressure, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization of the posterior distribution for Bayesian inference without requiring a separate neural network to approximate the TOV mapping.

## Key numbers

- The boundary pressure used to define the neutron star surface is taken as P_bnd = 1.4 x 10^-12 MeV/fm^3, and results are checked to be stable when varying P_bnd by a few orders of magnitude.
- For a neutron star with central pressure P_c = 10 MeV/fm^3 and a phase transition at P_pert = 1 MeV/fm^3, the scaled relative derivative of mass with respect to latent heat is approximately -0.77, meaning a latent heat of r x epsilon(P=1 MeV/fm^3) changes mass by M -> M - 0.77r x M.
- The sensitivity analysis shows that neutron star observables are only sensitive to the EoS for pressures in the range P in (P_c/10, P_c], with no sensitivity to local changes at pressures below approximately 1/10 of the central pressure.
- The maximum scaled derivatives for tidal deformability Lambda are systematically larger than those for mass M, indicating tidal deformability is more sensitive to EoS changes.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts by some of the authors (Soma 2022) where the EoS was represented by a DNN but the TOV equations were approximated by another DNN, introducing systematic uncertainties that are now eliminated by solving TOV directly and computing gradients analytically. The approach enables nonparametric Bayesian inference with large parameter sets, which is not feasible with standard Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo methods when N_prm exceeds approximately 100. The method is designed to address the open question of how many mass-radius observations from NICER and gravitational-wave measurements from LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA are needed to confirm a first-order phase transition, a question raised by recent studies (Essick 2023, Lin 2023, Annala 2023) that found such reconstruction remains elusive with current data.
