# Sensitivity of the neutron star r-mode instability window to the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy

> Using a simple model of a neutron star with a perfectly rigid crust constructed and a set of crust and core equations of state that span the range of nuclear experimental uncertainty in the density dependence of the symmetry energy from 25 MeV (soft EOS) to 115 MeV (stiff EOS), we calculate the instability window for the onset of the Chandrasekhar-Friedmann-Schutz (CFS) instability in r-mode oscillations for canonical neutron stars (1.4 M_{\odot}) and massive neutron stars (2.0 M_{\odot}). In these models the crust-core transition density, and thus crustal thickness, is calculated consistently with the core equation of state (EOS). For the canonical neutron star, the lower bound of the r-mode instability window is reduced in frequency by \approx150 Hz from the softest to the stiffest symmetry energy used, independent of mass and temperature. The instability window also drops by \approx 100 Hz independent of EOS when the mass is raised from 1.4 M_{\odot} to 2.0 M_{\odot}. Where temperature estimates are available, the observed neutron stars in low mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs) have frequencies below the instability window for the 1.4 M_{\odot} models, while some LMXBs fall within the instability window for 2.0 M_{\odot} stars if the symmetry energy is relatively stiff, indicating that a softer symmetry energy is more consistent with observations within this model. Thus we conclude that smaller values of L help stabilize neutron stars against runaway r-mode oscillations. The critical temperature, below which no star can reach the instability window without exceeding its Kepler frequency, varies by nearly an order of magnitude from soft to stiff symmetry energies. When the crust thickness and core EOS are treated consistently, a thicker crust corresponds to a lower critical temperature, the opposite result to previous studies in which the transition density was independent of the core EOS.

## Key claim
The r-mode instability window for neutron stars is sensitive to the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy, quantified by the slope parameter L. Within a simple rigid-crust model, smaller values of L (softer symmetry energy) help stabilize neutron stars against runaway r-mode oscillations, and values of L less than about 65 MeV are more consistent with observations of low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs), though this conclusion depends on the assumed neutron star mass and the use of a consistent crust-core transition density.

## Method
The authors used a simple neutron star model with a perfectly rigid crust and a set of crust and core equations of state (EOS) based on the Modified Skyrme-Like (MSL) model. The slope of the symmetry energy at saturation density L was varied from 25 MeV (soft) to 115 MeV (stiff) while keeping the symmetric nuclear matter EOS fixed, and the crust-core transition density was calculated consistently with the core EOS using a compressible liquid drop model. The r-mode instability window was computed by balancing the gravitational radiation growth timescale against the viscous damping timescale at the core-crust boundary layer, dominated by electron-electron scattering.

## Key numbers
- The lower bound of the r-mode instability window for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is reduced in frequency by approximately 150 Hz from the softest (L=25 MeV) to the stiffest (L=115 MeV) symmetry energy, independent of mass and temperature.
- The instability window drops by approximately 100 Hz, independent of EOS, when the neutron star mass is raised from 1.4 to 2.0 solar masses.
- The critical temperature below which no star can reach the instability window without exceeding its Kepler frequency varies from about 3 x 10^7 K (soft EOS) to 1.5 x 10^8 K (stiff EOS) for a 1.4 solar mass star, and from 4 x 10^6 K to 2 x 10^7 K for a 2.0 solar mass star.
- The crust-core transition densities for L values of 25, 45, 65, 85, and 105 MeV are 0.0940, 0.0831, 0.0762, 0.0726, and 0.0703 fm^{-3}, respectively.
- At a crust-core transition density of 1.5 x 10^14 g cm^{-3}, the critical temperature for a 1.4 solar mass star is about 4 x 10^7 K, almost a factor of 3 lower than the approximately 1.5 x 10^8 K found in prior work by Lindblom et al. (2000).

## Context
This work extends previous studies of the r-mode instability (e.g., Lindblom et al. 2000) by treating the crust-core transition density consistently with the core EOS, rather than using a fixed canonical value or allowing it to vary independently. The results contradict the earlier finding that a thicker crust corresponds to a higher critical temperature; here, a thicker crust (from a softer symmetry energy) gives a lower critical temperature. The study enables more realistic comparisons with observed LMXBs such as 4U 1608-522, 4U 1636-536, and EXO 0748-676, and suggests that ongoing experimental and theoretical constraints favoring L in the lower half of the 25-115 MeV range (e.g., Hebeler 2010, Gandolfi 2011) are more consistent with the observed spin frequencies of these systems.
