# Analytic structure of stress-energy response functions and new Kubo formulae

> Determining the transport properties of Quark-Gluon Plasma is one of the most important aspects of relativistic heavy ion collision studies. Field-theoretical calculations of the transport coefficients such as the shear and bulk viscosities require Kubo formulae which in turn require real-time correlation functions of stress-energy tensors. Consequently, knowing the analytic structure of these correlation functions is essential in any such studies. Using the energy-conservation laws and the results from the gravity-hydrodynamics analysis, we determine the low-frequency and low-wavenumber analytic structures of all stress-energy correlation functions in the rest frame of the medium. By comparing with the diffusion and sound spectra from the second-order and the third-order relativistic hydrodynamics, various new Kubo formulae are derived in the limit where the zero-frequency limit is taken first. We also show that the meaning of the Kubo formulae for relaxation times can change when higher-order terms are added to hydrodynamics. A subtle issue of taking the zero frequency and zero wavenumber limits when using skeleton diagrams is addressed as well.

# Analytic structure of stress-energy response functions and new Kubo formulae

## Key claim

The authors determine the low-frequency and low-wavenumber analytic structures of all stress-energy tensor correlation functions in the rest frame of a medium, using energy-conservation laws and gravity-hydrodynamics results, and derive new Kubo formulae in the limit where the zero-frequency limit is taken first rather than the zero-wavenumber limit. However, the meaning of Kubo formulae for relaxation times can change when higher-order terms are added to hydrodynamics, as the shear relaxation time extracted from the second derivative of the correlation function becomes a sum of multiple relaxation times in third-order hydrodynamics.

## Method

The authors use Ward identities derived from energy-momentum conservation to express all stress-energy correlation functions in terms of five orthogonal response functions (G1, G2, GL, GLT, GT) in a spatially isotropic system. They then compare these general analytic forms with explicit solutions obtained from linearized gravity-hydrodynamics equations up to second order, and subsequently with third-order conformal hydrodynamic results, to relate the zero-frequency and zero-wavenumber limits of the response functions to physical transport coefficients such as shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, relaxation times, and thermodynamic transport coefficients.

## Key numbers

- Shear diffusion constant: D_T = η/h0, where η is shear viscosity and h0 is enthalpy density
- Sound attenuation constant: Γ = (4η/3 + ζ)/h0, where ζ is bulk viscosity
- From second-order hydrodynamics: B_R D_R = τ_π (shear relaxation time), with D_R = D_T
- From third-order conformal hydrodynamics: B_R D_R = τ_π + τ_ξ + τ_ζ (sum of three relaxation times)
- The response function G2(ω, k^2) expansion yields: G2 = -i D_R h0 ω + B_R D_R^2 h0 ω^2 - K ω^2 - F_2R k^2 + O(ω k^2)
- Matching with derivative expansion: F_2R = κ/2 and K = κ/2 - κ*, where κ and κ* are thermodynamic transport coefficients
- From small-k constraints: F_LTR = 2η/v_s^2, F_T1R = η/v_s^2, F_T2R = 3η, where v_s is speed of sound
- From gravity-hydrodynamics: g_LT = -κ + 2κ*

## Context

This work extends the long history of Kubo formula derivations for transport coefficients in relativistic field theory, building on prior approaches by Jeon (1995), Hosoya (1983), and Kovtun (2004). It incorporates recent developments in gravity-hydrodynamics analysis by Baier (2007), Romatschke (2015), and Moore (2012), as well as third-order relativistic hydrodynamics results from de Brito (2023) and Panday (2024). The new Kubo formulae derived here, where the zero-frequency limit is taken first, enable extraction of transport coefficients from correlation functions that lack hydrodynamic poles, such as G2, and clarify the interpretation of relaxation time Kubo formulae when higher-order hydrodynamic terms are included.
