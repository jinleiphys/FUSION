# New Nuclear Reaction Flow during r-Process Nucleosynthesis in Supernovae: Critical Role of Light Neutron-Rich Nuclei

> We study the role of light neutron-rich nuclei during r-process nucleosynthesis in supernovae. Most previous studies of the r-process have concentrated on the reaction flow of heavy unstable nuclei. Although the nuclear reaction network includes a few thousand heavy nuclei, only limited reaction flow through light-mass nuclei near the stability line has been used in those studies. However, in a viable scenario of the r-process in neutrino-driven winds, the initial condition is a high-entropy hot plasma consisting of neutrons, protons, and electron-positron pairs experiencing an intense flux of neutrinos. In such environments light-mass nuclei as well as heavy nuclei are expected to play important roles in the production of seed nuclei and r-process elements. Thus, we have extended our fully implicit nuclear reaction network so that it includes all nuclei up to the neutron drip line for Z $ \leq 10$, in addition to a larger network for Z $ \geq 10$. In the present nucleosynthesis study, we utilize a wind model of massive SNeII explosions to study the effects of this extended network. We find that a new nuclear-reaction flow path opens in the very light neutron-rich region. This new nuclear reaction flow can change the final heavy-element abundances by as much as an order of magnitude.

## Key claim

The authors find that including all neutron-rich light nuclei up to the neutron drip line for Z ≤ 10 in the nuclear reaction network opens a new reaction flow path through light neutron-rich nuclei, which can change final heavy-element abundances by as much as an order of magnitude, but only in wind models with a short dynamic timescale (τ_dyn = 5.1 × 10⁻³ s).

## Method

The authors extended a fully implicit nuclear reaction network to include all nuclei up to the neutron drip line for Z ≤ 10 (63 nuclides and over 200 reactions), in addition to a larger network of 3036 nuclides up to Americium for Z ≥ 10. They applied this "full network" and a "small α-network" (similar to previous studies) to a single trajectory from a general-relativistic hydrodynamic simulation of neutrino-driven winds from a 2.0 M⊙ proto-neutron star, starting from nuclear statistical equilibrium at T₉ = 9.0 with electron fraction Yₑ = 0.42.

## Key numbers

- The adopted wind model has a dynamic expansion timescale τ_dyn = 5.1 × 10⁻³ s
- The total neutrino luminosity is 6 × 10⁵² erg s⁻¹
- The full network includes more than 63 nuclides for Z < 10 and more than 200 reactions among them, compared to only 27 nuclides in the small α-network used by Woosley et al. (1994)
- The total number of nuclides in the full network up to Americium isotopes is 3036
- At the start of α-process (t = 3.3 × 10⁻³ s), temperature T₉ = 3.4 and density ρ = 8.0 × 10⁴ g cm⁻³
- At near freezeout (t = 0.567 s), temperature T₉ = 0.62 and density ρ = 5.4 × 10² g cm⁻³
- The calculated single-neutron separation energy Sₙ along the r-process path is approximately 1 MeV, compared to typical literature values of 2-4 MeV
- The new reaction flow can change final heavy-element abundances by as much as an order of magnitude

## Context

This work extends previous r-process studies by Meyer et al. (1992) and Woosley et al. (1994), which used limited networks for light nuclei near the stability line and did not include neutrino interactions. The authors show that in neutrino-driven wind models with short dynamic timescales (as studied by Otsuki et al. (2000) and Sumiyoshi et al. (2000)), light neutron-rich nuclei play a critical role through two new reaction chains: the Be-isotope chain (α(α n,γ)⁹Be(n,γ)¹⁰Be(α,γ)¹⁴C) and the Li-B chain (α(t,γ)⁷Li(n,γ)⁸Li(α,n)¹¹B). This finding enables more accurate r-process simulations in supernova models and highlights the need for experimental measurements of reaction rates on neutron-rich light nuclei such as ¹⁸C(α,n)²¹O and ³⁶Mg(α,n)³⁹Al.
