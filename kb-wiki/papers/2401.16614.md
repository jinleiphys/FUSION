# Isovector Axial Charge and Form Factors of Nucleons from Lattice QCD

> I present an overview of the calculations of the isovector axial vector form factor of the nucleon, $G_A(Q^2)$, using lattice QCD. Based on a comparison of results from various collaborations, a case is made that lattice results are now consistent within 10\%. A similar level of uncertainty is found also in the axial charge $g_A^{u-d}$, the mean squared axial charge radius, $\langle r_A^2 \rangle$, the induced pseudoscalar charge $g_P^\ast$, and the pion-nucleon coupling $g_{πNN}$. These lattice results for $G_A(Q^2)$ are already compatible with those obtained from the recent MINER$ν$A experiment but lie 2-3$σ$ higher than the phenomenological extraction from the old $ν$-deuterium bubble chamber scattering data for $Q^2 > 0.3$~GeV${}^2$. Fits to our data show that the dipole ansatz does not have enough parameters to parameterize the form factor over the range $0 \le Q^2 \le 1$~GeV${}^2$, whereas even a $z^2$ truncation of the $z$-expansion or a low order Padé are sufficient. Looking ahead, lattice QCD calculations will provide increasingly precise results over the range $0 \le Q^2 \lesssim 1$~GeV${}^2$, and MINER$ν$A-like experiments will extend the range to $Q^2 \sim 2$~GeV${}^2$ or higher. To increase precision of lattice data to the percent level, new developments are needed to address two related issues: the exponentially falling signal-to-noise ratio in all nucleon correlation functions and removing excited state contributions. Nevertheless, even with the current methodology, significant reduction in errors is expected over the next few years with higher statistics data on more ensembles closer to the physical point.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD results for the isovector axial vector form factor of the nucleon are now consistent within 10% across different collaborations, and these results agree with the recent MINERνA experiment but lie 2-3σ higher than phenomenological extractions from old ν-deuterium bubble chamber data for Q² > 0.3 GeV².

## Method

The calculation uses lattice QCD to compute nucleon 2-point and 3-point correlation functions with axial and pseudoscalar current insertions, extracting ground-state matrix elements through spectral decomposition fits that include the lowest Nπ multihadron excited state. The axial Ward-Takahashi (PCAC) identity provides a data-driven validation of the excited state removal procedure. Form factors are obtained from the Lorentz covariant decomposition of the extracted ground-state matrix elements into G_A(Q²), the induced pseudoscalar form factor, and the pseudoscalar form factor G_P(Q²).

## Key numbers

- Axial charge g_A^{u-d} values from various lattice calculations with 2+1- and 2+1+1-flavors of sea quarks lie in the range 1.22 ≲ g_A^{u-d} ≲ 1.32
- Lattice results for G_A(Q²) over 0 < Q² ≤ 1 GeV² have ≲ 10% uncertainty
- Lattice results lie 2-3σ higher than phenomenological extraction from ν-deuterium data for Q² > 0.3 GeV²
- The lowest Nπ state contribution to the pseudoscalar form factor G_P is about 50% (enhanced excited state contribution)
- PCAC relation satisfied to within ≈ 10% at lattice spacing a ≈ 0.9 fm when including the lowest Nπ state
- Signal in nucleon correlation functions extends to approximately 2 fm for 2-point and 1.5 fm for 3-point functions with O(10⁵) measurements

## Context

This work reviews lattice QCD calculations of the isovector axial form factors, extending the comparisons made by Tomalak (2023) between lattice QCD, MINERνA experiment, and phenomenological extraction from neutrino-deuterium data by Meyer (2016). The analysis builds on the discovery by Bär (2018) using chiral perturbation theory that enhanced Nπ multihadron excited state contributions must be removed, and implements the data-driven method developed by Jang (2019) for exposing these states. The results enable improved inputs for neutrino-nucleus scattering cross-section calculations needed for oscillation experiments such as T2K, NOvA, MINERvA, MicroBooNE, and SBN, and support the broader program of first-principles calculations of neutrino-nucleus interactions for DUNE.
