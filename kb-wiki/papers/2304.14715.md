# Finite temperature description of Fermi gases with in-medium effective mass

> We investigate Fermi gases at finite temperature for which the in-medium effective mass may not be constant as a function of the density, the temperature, or the chemical potential. We suggest a formalism that separates the terms for which the mass is constant from the terms which explicitly treat the correction due to the in-medium effective mass. We employ the ensemble equivalence in infinite matter in order to treat these different terms. Our formalism is applied in nuclear matter and we show its goodness by comparing it to an exact treatment based on the numerical calculation of the Fermi integrals.

## Key claim

The paper presents a formalism that allows fast approximations for free Fermi gases (FFG) at finite temperature to be used for Fermi gases (FG) with in-medium effective masses that vary with density, temperature, or chemical potential, provided that correction terms are added to account for the mass dependence. The authors claim the formalism works because the residual difference between their approximation and exact numerical calculations is mostly due to the JEL approximation itself, not to their formalism.

## Method

The authors separate thermodynamic quantities into a term computed with a constant effective mass (using the JEL approximation from Johns, Ellis, and Lattimer (1996)) and a correction term that explicitly treats the in-medium effective mass dependence. They employ ensemble equivalence in infinite matter, using the canonical ensemble for density-dependent effective masses and the grand-canonical ensemble for chemical-potential-dependent effective masses. The formalism is applied to symmetric and asymmetric nuclear matter using the SLy5 parametrization for the density-dependent effective mass, and results are compared to exact calculations using Gauss-Legendre integration with 600 points.

## Context

This work extends the JEL approximation from Johns, Ellis, and Lattimer (1996), which assumed constant particle mass, to systems with in-medium effective masses that vary with thermodynamic conditions. The formalism enables fast calculation of finite-temperature equations of state for applications such as neutron star mergers and core-collapse supernovae, where density-dependent effective masses from Skyrme interactions (Tondeur (1984), Chabanat (1997), Bender (2003), Li (2018)) are commonly used. The approach also accommodates temperature-dependent effective masses as introduced by Fantina (2011, 2012) and chemical-potential-dependent forms.
