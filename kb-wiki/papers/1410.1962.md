# Spin physics and TMD studies at A Fixed-Target ExpeRiment at the LHC (AFTER@LHC)

> We report on the opportunities for spin physics and Transverse-Momentum Dependent distribution (TMD) studies at a future multi-purpose fixed-target experiment using the proton or lead ion LHC beams extracted by a bent crystal. The LHC multi-TeV beams allow for the most energetic fixed-target experiments ever performed, opening new domains of particle and nuclear physics and complementing that of collider physics, in particular that of RHIC and the EIC projects. The luminosity achievable with AFTER@LHC using typical targets would surpass that of RHIC by more that 3 orders of magnitude in a similar energy region. In unpolarised proton-proton collisions, AFTER@LHC allows for measurements of TMDs such as the Boer-Mulders quark distributions, the distribution of unpolarised and linearly polarised gluons in unpolarised protons. Using the polarisation of hydrogen and nuclear targets, one can measure transverse single-spin asymmetries of quark and gluon sensitive probes, such as, respectively, Drell-Yan pair and quarkonium production. The fixed-target mode has the advantage to allow for measurements in the target-rapidity region, namely at large x^uparrow in the polarised nucleon. Overall, this allows for an ambitious spin program which we outline here.

## Key claim

AFTER@LHC could enable spin and TMD measurements using unpolarised or polarised fixed targets with LHC beams extracted by bent crystals, achieving luminosities surpassing RHIC by more than three orders of magnitude in a similar energy region, but only if the technical challenges of beam extraction and target polarisation within the LHC underground complex are successfully addressed.

## Method

The paper outlines a proposal to extract a fraction of the LHC proton or lead-ion beam (approximately 5 × 10⁸ protons per second or 2 × 10⁵ lead ions per second) using bent-crystal channelling, directing the extracted beam onto fixed targets (hydrogen, deuterium, or nuclear targets, potentially polarised via Dynamic Nuclear Polarisation). The resulting centre-of-mass energies are approximately 115 GeV for 7 TeV protons on a fixed target and 72 GeV per nucleon-nucleon collision for 2.76 TeV lead ions. The authors then evaluate the feasibility of measuring specific TMDs and single-spin asymmetries using quarkonium, Drell-Yan, and quarkonium-plus-photon production channels, leveraging the large backward-rapidity acceptance and high luminosity of the fixed-target configuration.

## Key numbers

- Luminosity with extracted proton beam (5 × 10⁸ p⁺/s at 7 TeV) on a 1 m liquid hydrogen target yields approximately 20 fb⁻¹ per year (10⁷ s), comparable to LHC collider luminosities at 7 and 8 TeV.
- Centre-of-mass energy for 7 TeV protons on a fixed target is close to 115 GeV (√(2E_p m_N)).
- Centre-of-mass energy per nucleon-nucleon collision for 2.76 TeV lead ions on a fixed target is close to 72 GeV.
- Expected heating power from the extracted beam on a 1 cm thick target is on the order of 50 μW, allowing target temperatures as low as 50 mK.
- Expected yields per unit of rapidity per 20 fb⁻¹: approximately 10⁹ J/ψ and 10⁶ Υ.
- Differential cross section for back-to-back J/ψ + γ production is on the order of tens of fb/GeV, yielding a couple of thousand events per year (20 fb⁻¹) with a hydrogen target.
- Rapidity shift between centre-of-mass and laboratory frames is approximately 4.8 for 7 TeV protons and approximately 4.3 for 2.76 TeV lead ions.

## Context

This paper extends the spin-physics programme initiated at RHIC (which collided polarised protons up to 500 GeV) and complements ongoing and proposed fixed-target experiments such as COMPASS (pion-induced Drell-Yan), Fermilab proposals P1027 (polarised beam, large x↑) and P1039 (polarised target, lower x↑), as well as the future Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) projects. The proposal builds on earlier work by Brodsky et al. (2012) on the general physics opportunities of AFTER@LHC and by Boer (2012) on gluon TMDs in quarkonium production, and aims to enable measurements of the gluon Sivers effect, the sign change of the Sivers function between SIDIS and Drell-Yan (predicted by Collins, 2002), and TMDs such as the Boer-Mulders distributions and linearly polarised gluon distributions in unpolarised protons.
