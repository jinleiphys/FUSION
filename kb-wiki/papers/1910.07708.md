# Projected Cooling Algorithm for Quantum Computation

> In the current era of noisy quantum devices, there is a need for quantum algorithms that are efficient and robust against noise. Towards this end, we introduce the projected cooling algorithm for quantum computation. The projected cooling algorithm is able to construct the localized ground state of any Hamiltonian with a translationally-invariant kinetic energy and interactions that vanish at large distances. The term "localized" refers to localization in position space. The method can be viewed as the quantum analog of evaporative cooling. We start with an initial state with support over a compact region of a large volume. We then drive the excited quantum states to disperse and measure the remaining portion of the wave function left behind. For the nontrivial examples we consider here, the improvement over other methods is substantial. The only additional resource required is performing the operations in a volume significantly larger than the size of the localized state. These characteristics make the projected cooling algorithm a promising tool for calculations of self-bound systems such as atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The projected cooling algorithm can construct the localized ground state of any Hamiltonian with translationally-invariant kinetic energy and interactions that vanish at large distances, provided the spatial volume of the total system is significantly larger than the size of the localized state; the method works by driving excited quantum states to disperse away from a compact region and measuring only the portion of the wave function that remains.

## Method

The algorithm starts with an initial state confined to a compact spatial region rho and applies time evolution under a Hamiltonian that may be time-dependent. The projection operator P, which projects onto states entirely contained within rho, is applied only at the end of the time evolution. For Hamiltonians with multiple bound states, the kinetic energy operator is multiplied by a scale factor greater than 1 to push excited bound states into the continuum, then adiabatic evolution gradually returns to the desired Hamiltonian. The method was tested on one-dimensional lattice models with one particle (Models 1A and 1B) and two particles (Model 2), using both full time evolution and Trotterized time evolution with added stochastic noise.

## Key numbers

- For Model 1B with R=5, L=25, and time step Delta t = 0.3, standard adiabatic evolution achieved a normalized overlap of no more than 0.35, while projected cooling achieved an overlap of at least 0.94 in 40 time steps or less, even with Trotter approximation and stochastic noise of epsilon = 0.05.
- For Model 2 (two-particle sector) with R=5, L=25, and Delta t = 0.3, standard adiabatic evolution achieved a normalized overlap of no more than 0.24, while projected cooling achieved an overlap of at least 0.85 in 40 time steps or less, even with Trotter approximation and stochastic noise of epsilon = 0.05.
- The interior region for Model 1B had 11 dimensions in the one-particle sector; for Model 2 the interior region had 121 dimensions in the two-particle sector.

## Context

This work extends quantum computing approaches to the many-body problem by introducing a method analogous to evaporative cooling, in contrast to existing methods such as quantum phase estimation (Kitaev 1995, Abrams and Lloyd 1997), quantum variational methods (Peruzzo 2014, Dumitrescu 2018), quantum adiabatic evolution (Farhi 2000, Farhi 2001), spectral comb techniques (Kaplan 2017), resonance transition enhancement (Wang 2017), coupled heat bath approaches (Boykin 2002, Xu 2014), and dissipative open-system methods (Kraus 2008, Verstraete 2009). The algorithm is designed for self-bound systems such as atomic nuclei and uses a spatial lattice formalism analogous to lattice effective field theory calculations (Lee 2008, Lahde 2019). The method enables future applications to nuclear structure calculations on noisy quantum devices, with a recent application to the transverse Ising model (Gustafson 2020).
