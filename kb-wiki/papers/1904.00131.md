# Transport approaches for the Description of Intermediate-Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The transport approach is a useful tool to study dynamics of non-equilibrium systems. For heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies, where both the smooth nucleon potential and the hard-core nucleon-nucleon collision are important, the dynamics are properly described by two families of transport models, i.e., the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck approach and the quantum molecular dynamics approach. These transport models have been extensively used to extract valuable information of the nuclear equation of state, the nuclear symmetry energy, and microscopic nuclear interactions from intermediate-energy heavy-ion collision experiments. On the other hand, there do exist deviations on the predications and conclusions from different transport models. Efforts on the transport code evaluation project are devoted in order to understand the model dependence of transport simulations and well control the main ingredients, such as the initialization, the mean-field potential, the nucleon-nucleon collision, etc. A new era of accurately extracting nuclear interactions from transport model studies is foreseen.

## Key claim

Transport approaches (BUU and QMD) are essential for extracting the nuclear equation of state and symmetry energy from intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions, but systematic discrepancies between different transport codes must be understood and minimized through the transport code evaluation project before accurate extraction of nuclear interactions can be achieved.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical frameworks of the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) approach, which solves the time evolution of the one-body phase-space distribution using the test-particle method, and the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) approach, which models nucleon evolution under a many-body Hamiltonian with Gaussian wave packets. Both approaches incorporate three main ingredients: initialization of projectile and target nuclei, mean-field potential for each nucleon, and nucleon-nucleon collisions during dynamical evolution. The transport code evaluation project assigns homework calculations with identical reaction conditions and interaction parameters to code practitioners, then analyzes results to identify sources of discrepancy.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy range for intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions: 50 MeV to 2 GeV per nucleon
- 12 BUU-type codes and 11 QMD-type codes have participated in the transport code evaluation project
- The transport code evaluation project began in 2004 at ECT* in Trento, Italy, comparing particle productions, rapidity distributions, and transverse spectra in the 1 A GeV regime
- The International Workshop on Simulations of Low- and Intermediate-Energy Heavy-Ion Collisions was held in 2014 in Shanghai, China, focusing on stability of nuclei, anisotropic flows, and collision rates
- The Transport2017 workshop was held in 2017 in East Lensing, USA, testing nucleon-nucleon collision rate, nucleon evolution in mean-field potential, and pion-like particle production in a box system with periodic boundary condition

## Context

This review extends the line of work comparing BUU and QMD transport approaches for intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions, following earlier comparisons such as Kolomeitsev et al. (2005) at the 1 A GeV regime. The transport code evaluation project enables systematic identification of model dependence in simulations of the nuclear equation of state, nuclear symmetry energy, and microscopic nuclear interactions. The paper provides a comprehensive catalog of participating codes and their representative references, serving as a reference for future efforts to standardize transport simulations and accurately extract nuclear interactions from experimental data.
