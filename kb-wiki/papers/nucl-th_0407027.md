# Predicting total and total reaction cross sections using a simple functional form

> Total reaction cross sections have been predicted for neucleons scattering from nuclei ranging in mass 6 to 238 and for projectile energies from just above noticeable giant resonance excitation to 300 MeV. So also have been the mass variations of those cross sections at selected energies when they have been calculated using coordinate space optical potentials formed by full folding effective two-nucleon (NN) interactions with one body density matrix elements (OBDME) of the nuclear ground states. Good comparisons with data result when effective NN interactions defined by medium modification of free NN t matrices are used. However, there is a simple three parameter functional form that reproduces the partial wave total reaction cross section values determined from those optical potential calculations; a functional form also maps the total scattering cross section partial wave elements. Adjusting the theoretical defined parameter values has enabled us to fit the actual measured data values from scattering involving (15) nuclei spanning the mass range from 9Be to 238U and for proton energies from 10 to 300 MeV. Likewise total cross sections for neutron cross sections for neutron scattering from various nuclei can be equally well reproduced. Of import is that the three parameter values vary smoothly with mass and energy.

## Key claim

A three-parameter functional form can reproduce total reaction cross sections for proton scattering and total cross sections for neutron scattering across a wide range of target masses and energies, with the three parameters varying smoothly with mass and energy. The authors claim this works by first using g-folding optical potential calculations to define initial partial cross sections, then adjusting the parameter values to fit actual measured data.

## Method

The authors used coordinate-space optical potentials formed by full-folding effective two-nucleon (NN) interactions with one-body density matrix elements (OBDME) of nuclear ground states to calculate partial-wave total reaction cross sections. They then fit these partial cross sections to a three-parameter functional form: σ_l^(R)(E) = (2l+1)[1 + e^((l-l0)/a)]^(-1) + ε(2l0+1)e^((l-l0)/a)[1 + e^((l-l0)/a)]^(-2). For protons, ε was fixed at -1.5 and a(E,A) was set to vary linearly with wave vector as a ~ 1.02k - 0.25, while l0 was adjusted to match measured data. For neutrons, a and ε were expressed as parabolic functions of energy (a = 1.29 + 0.00250E - 1.76×10^(-6)E^2, ε = -1.47 - 0.00234E + 4.16×10^(-6)E^2) with l0 adjusted to fit measured total cross-section data.

## Key numbers

- Proton scattering: 15 nuclei from ^9Be to ^238U, energies from 10 to 300 MeV
- Neutron scattering: 9 nuclei from ^6Li to ^238U, energies from 10 to 600 MeV
- For protons: ε fixed at -1.5 (constant, independent of energy and mass)
- For protons: a(E,A) ~ 1.02k - 0.25, where k = (1/ħc)√(E^2 - m^2c^4)
- For neutrons: a = 1.29 + 0.00250E - 1.76×10^(-6)E^2 (E in MeV)
- For neutrons: ε = -1.47 - 0.00234E + 4.16×10^(-6)E^2 (E in MeV)
- For ^208Pb neutron scattering: the SKM* model gave a neutron skin thickness of 0.17 fm
- g-folding calculations for p-^118Sn underestimated data by 5 to 10%

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by the same group (Amos 2002, Deb 2003) that established the functional form for proton total reaction cross sections, now applying it also to neutron total cross sections. It builds on g-folding optical potential calculations (Amos 2000, Amos 2002) and compares with phenomenological global optical model potentials from Koning and Delaroche (2003). The method enables prediction of cross sections for nuclei and energies where experimental data are unavailable, with applications in medical radiotherapy, radiation protection, and radioactive waste management. The authors suggest that a parameter tabulation database could be formed to allow interpolation for any required cross section value to within a few percent.
