# Bubble $^{36}$Ar and Its New Breathing Modes

> The bubble nuclei are important components of exotic nuclear structures characterized by special depletions of central densities. Focusing on bubble structures of $^{36}$Ar, the characterizations of bubble nuclei were explored with the framework of the extended quantum molecular dynamics model. Three density distribution modes were uncovered for the first time, i.e. micro-bubble, bubble, and cluster resonances, which show unique spectral signature compared to the monopole resonance spectrum as the excitation intensity was increased in bubbles. Of pivotal importance is the revelation that the bubble mode's oscillation frequency closely resembles macroscopic bubble dynamics, building a connection between classical macroscopic phenomena and the quantum complexity of the nuclear structure. The discovery marks a crucial step forward in deciphering the relationship between classical and quantum domains within the enigmatic world of atomic nuclei.

# Bubble $^{36}$Ar and Its New Breathing Modes

## Key claim

Using the extended quantum molecular dynamics (EQMD) model, the authors claim that $^{36}$Ar exhibits three distinct density distribution modes, micro-bubble, bubble, and cluster resonance, as the excitation intensity parameter $\varepsilon$ is increased, and that the bubble mode's oscillation frequency closely resembles macroscopic bubble dynamics. The claim is conditional on the specific choice of $\varepsilon$ values (0.1, 0.7, and 1.3) and on the EQMD model's ability to reproduce the ground state binding energy and charge radius within 4.4% and 9.9% of experimental values, respectively.

## Method

The authors employed the extended quantum molecular dynamics (EQMD) model, which includes zero-point kinetic energy subtraction, an effective Pauli potential, and a complex variable wave packet width, to simulate the ground state and monopole resonance excitations of $^{36}$Ar. The initial ground state was obtained through EQMD initialization, revealing a 9$\alpha$ cluster configuration, and then excited by applying a perturbation operator $\hat{H}'(t) = \varepsilon \hat{\mathcal{M}} \delta(t)$ with varying intensity $\varepsilon$, where the monopole operator acts on coordinates and momenta as $\vec{r}_i' = \vec{r}_i + \varepsilon \vec{r}_i$ and $\vec{p}_i' = \vec{p}_i - \varepsilon \vec{p}_i$. The time evolution of the root-mean-square radius $R_{rms}(t)$ was tracked over 0–500 fm/$c$, and Fourier transforms of its second derivative yielded $\gamma$-ray spectra and vibrational frequencies.

## Key numbers

- Ground state binding energy of $^{36}$Ar calculated as 8.146 MeV/u, with a relative difference of 4.388% from the experimental value of 8.520 MeV/u.
- Ground state charge radius calculated as 3.0539 fm, with a relative difference of 9.93% from the experimental value of 3.3905 fm.
- Three distinct modes observed at excitation intensity parameters $\varepsilon = 0.1$ (micro-bubble), $\varepsilon = 0.7$ (bubble), and $\varepsilon = 1.3$ (cluster resonance).
- The micro-bubble and cluster resonance modes exhibit prominent $\gamma$-ray peaks at approximately 50 MeV and 70 MeV, respectively, while the bubble mode shows multiple peaks with decreasing intensity as energy increases.
- The surface tension coefficient $\sigma$ extracted for the bubble mode decreases with increasing $\varepsilon$, becoming negative at $\varepsilon = 1.0$, marking the boundary between bubble and cluster resonance modes.
- Critical values separating the three oscillation patterns in $R_{rms}(t)$ are $\varepsilon = 0.5$ and $\varepsilon = 1.0$.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering theoretical calculations on bubble $^{36}$Ar by Campi and Sprung (1972) and the classification of nuclear states into quantum liquids, clusters, and crystal-like structures by Ebran et al. (2012). It also connects to experimental work on bubble structures by Mutschler et al. (2017) on $^{34}$Si. The findings enable future studies of exotic excited states in nuclei near the $\beta$-stable line and may shed new light on reduced fusion potential barriers in superheavy nucleus synthesis using bubble nuclei with thin nuclear films.
