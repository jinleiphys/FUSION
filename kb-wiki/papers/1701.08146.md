# Surface tension of compressed, superheavy atoms

> Based on the relativistic mean field theory and the Thomas-Fermi approximation, we study the surface properties of compressed, superheavy atoms. By compressed, superheavy atom we mean an atom composed by a superheavy nuclear core (superheavy nucleus) with mass number of the order of $10^4$, and degenerate electrons that neutralize the system. Some electrons penetrate into the superheavy nuclear core and the rest surround it up to a distance that depends upon the compression level. Taking into account the strong, weak, and electromagnetic interactions, we numerically study the structure of compressed, superheavy atoms and calculate the nuclear surface tension and Coulomb energy. We analyze the influence of the electron component and the background matter on the nuclear surface tension and Coulomb energy of compressed, superheavy atoms. We also compare and contrast these results in the case of compressed, superheavy atoms with phenomenological results in nuclear physics and the results of the core-crust interface of neutron stars with global charge neutrality. Based on the numerical results we study the instability against Bohr-Wheeler surface deformations in the case of compressed, superheavy atoms. The results in this article show the possibility of the existence of such compressed, superheavy atoms, and provide the evidence of strong effects of the electromagnetic interaction and electrons on the structure of compressed, superheavy atoms.

## Key claim

Based on relativistic mean field theory and the Thomas-Fermi approximation, the authors show that compressed, superheavy atoms with mass numbers of order 10^4 could exist and remain stable against Bohr-Wheeler fission, provided the background density remains below a critical value; the abstract's claim of "possibility of existence" is conditional on the system not exceeding a specific background density threshold.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled system of Poisson and Klein-Gordon equations for the electromagnetic and meson fields (sigma, omega, rho) together with the Thomas-Fermi equilibrium conditions for neutrons, protons, and electrons in beta equilibrium, using the NL3 parametrization of the relativistic mean field model. They computed fermion density profiles and meson field profiles for spherical compressed, superheavy atoms with baryon number density near nuclear saturation density (0.16 fm^-3) and varying ratios of electron to proton Fermi momentum in the central region. The nuclear surface tension and Coulomb energy per unit area were then calculated using the Baym-Bethe-Pethick method of reference surfaces, and the Bohr-Wheeler condition (E_coul > 2E_sur) was applied to assess fission stability.

- Critical background density for Bohr-Wheeler instability: rho_bg^crit ~ 9.7 x 10^13 g cm^-3 for the case P^F_eb = 0.8 P^F_pb

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of the core-crust interface in neutron stars using the Einstein-Maxwell-Thomas-Fermi (EMTF) approach (Belvedere et al., 2012; Rueda et al., 2014), where the electron density was approximately equal to the proton density in the core bulk region. Here they consider the more general case of electron density smaller than proton density, corresponding to compressed, superheavy atoms first studied by Rotondo et al. (2011). The results show that the presence of electrons and the electromagnetic interaction strongly affect the surface properties, and that the critical background density for fission instability is lower than in the nearly charge-neutral case studied in Rueda et al. (2014). This work enables further investigation of exotic neutron-rich nuclei with mass numbers far beyond ordinary nuclei and their possible role in neutron star crusts and r-process environments.
