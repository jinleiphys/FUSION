# Investigating the crust of neutron stars with neural-network quantum states

> An accurate description of low-density nuclear matter is crucial for explaining the physics of neutron star crusts. In the density range between approximately 0.01 fm$^{-3}$ and 0.1 fm$^{-3}$, matter transitions from neutron-rich nuclei to various higher-density pasta shapes, before ultimately reaching a uniform liquid. In this work, we introduce a variational Monte Carlo method based on a neural Pfaffian-Jastrow quantum state, which allows us to model the transition from the liquid phase to neutron-rich nuclei microscopically. At low densities, nuclear clusters dynamically emerge from the microscopic interactions among protons and neutrons, which we model based on pionless effective field theory. Our variational Monte Carlo approach represents a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo method, which is severely hindered by the fermion-sign problem in this low-density regime and cannot capture the onset of clusters. In addition to computing the energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter, we analyze an intermediate isospin-asymmetry configuration to elucidate the formation of nuclear clusters. We also provide evidence that the presence of such nuclear clusters influences the amount of protons in the crust compared to protons in beta-equilibrated, neutrino-transparent matter.

# Investigating the crust of neutron stars with neural-network quantum states

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a variational Monte Carlo method based on a neural Pfaffian-Jastrow quantum state can model the transition from uniform nuclear matter to clustered, neutron-rich nuclei in the low-density regime of neutron star crusts, overcoming the fermion-sign problem that prevents auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo from capturing cluster formation. The claim is conditional on using a simplified leading-order pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian and a simulation cell with 28 nucleons, which introduces finite-size effects that may overestimate ground-state energies at low densities.

## Method

The authors simulate infinite nuclear matter using periodic boundary conditions with N=28 nucleons in a cubic cell, modeling interactions via a non-relativistic Hamiltonian based on leading-order pionless effective field theory with two- and three-nucleon forces. They employ a variational Monte Carlo approach using a neural Pfaffian-Jastrow quantum state with a message-passing neural network to capture pairing and backflow correlations, optimizing the wave function via stochastic reconfiguration with RMSProp-like regularization on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Simulations are performed at baryon densities of 0.001, 0.01, 0.04, and 0.08 fm^{-3} for pure neutron matter (x=0/28), symmetric nuclear matter (x=14/28), and an intermediate isospin-asymmetric case (x=4/28).

## Key numbers

- Energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter at n_B = 0.001 fm^{-3} reaches approximately -10 MeV per particle, plateauing near the energy of ^{28}Si with no electromagnetic contribution
- Kinetic energy corrections due to finite-size effects range from -0.01 MeV (SNM at 0.001 fm^{-3}) to -2.05 MeV (x=4/28 at 0.08 fm^{-3})
- The neural-network quantum state contains 10,772 trainable parameters, with each optimization step taking approximately 80 seconds and convergence requiring between 1,000 and 3,000 iterations
- The regularization constant a for amplitude clipping was fixed to 8, and the small constant epsilon for Fisher matrix regularization was set to 10^{-3}

## Context

This work extends the neural Pfaffian-Jastrow architecture previously applied to ultracold Fermi gases (Kim 2024) and the hidden-nucleon NQS approach for dilute neutron matter (Fore 2023) to the more complex problem of neutron star crust matter with nuclear potentials. It directly addresses the limitation of auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo methods (Schmidt 1999, Piarulli 2020, Lovato 2022), which suffer from the fermion-sign problem in the low-density regime and cannot capture cluster formation. The approach enables future ab initio studies of the crust-core transition, pasta phases, and beta-equilibrated matter with larger particle numbers and more realistic Hamiltonians from chiral effective field theory.
