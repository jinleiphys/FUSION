# Event patterns extracted from transverse momentum and rapidity spectra of Z bosons and quarkonium states produced in pp and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC

> Transverse momentum ($p_T$) and rapidity ($y$) spectra of $Z$ bosons and quarkonium states (some charmonium $c\bar c$ mesons such as $J/ψ$ and $ψ(2S)$, and some bottomonium $b\bar b$ mesons such as $Υ(1S)$, $Υ(2S)$, and $Υ(3S)$) produced in proton-proton ($pp$) and lead-lead (Pb-Pb) collisions at the large hadron collider (LHC) are uniformly described by a hybrid model of two-component Erlang distribution for $p_T$ spectrum and two-component Gaussian distribution for $y$ spectrum. The former distribution results from a multisource thermal model, and the latter one results from the revised Landau hydrodynamic model. The modelling results are in agreement with the experimental data measured in $pp$ collisions at center-of-mass energies $\sqrt{s}=2.76$ and 7 TeV, and in Pb-Pb collisions at center-of-mass energy per nucleon pair $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV. Based on the parameter values extracted from $p_T$ and $y$ spectra, the event patterns (particle scatter plots) in two-dimensional $p_T$-$y$ space and in three-dimensional velocity space are obtained.

## Key claim

A hybrid model combining a two-component Erlang distribution (from a multisource thermal model) for transverse momentum spectra and a two-component Gaussian distribution (from the revised Landau hydrodynamic model) for rapidity spectra can uniformly describe Z boson and quarkonium production in both pp and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies, with the caveat that the model's agreement with data is demonstrated for specific decay channels and centrality classes, and some fits yield large chi-squared values due to very small experimental errors.

## Method

The authors fitted experimental transverse momentum (p_T) spectra using a two-component Erlang distribution derived from a multisource thermal model, and rapidity (y) spectra using a two-component Gaussian distribution derived from the revised Landau hydrodynamic model. For symmetric collisions (pp and Pb-Pb), the two Gaussian components were constrained to have equal widths and opposite peak positions with equal relative contributions of 0.5. Using the best-fit parameters, Monte Carlo methods generated discrete p_T and y values, from which event patterns in two-dimensional p_T-y space and three-dimensional velocity space were constructed.

## Key numbers

- For Z bosons in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=2.76 TeV (dimuon channel): first component mean p_T per source = 4.85 +/- 0.20 GeV/c, second component mean p_T per source = 15.80 +/- 0.79 GeV/c, with k_1 = 0.78 +/- 0.02
- For Z bosons in Pb-Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV (dimuon channel): first component mean p_T per source = 3.35 +/- 0.17 GeV/c, second component mean p_T per source = 10.60 +/- 0.53 GeV/c, with k_1 = 0.45 +/- 0.02
- For J/psi in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV: root-mean-square p_T = 3.11 +/- 0.09 GeV/c, root-mean-square y = 2.68 +/- 0.05
- For Upsilon(1S) in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV: root-mean-square p_T = 5.53 +/- 0.11 GeV/c, root-mean-square y = 2.26 +/- 0.05
- For Z bosons in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=2.76 TeV (dimuon): root-mean-square beta_z = 0.76 +/- 0.01 c, maximum |beta_z| = 1.00 c
- For J/psi in pp collisions at sqrt(s)=7 TeV: root-mean-square beta_z = 0.90 +/- 0.01 c, maximum |beta_z| = 1.00 c

## Context

This work extends the multisource thermal model approach previously applied by Liu and collaborators (prior works from 2000s) and the revised Landau hydrodynamic model from Landau (1953) and subsequent revisions by various authors (1950s-2010s) to simultaneously describe both p_T and y spectra of electroweak bosons and heavy quarkonia. The hybrid model enables extraction of event patterns in kinematic spaces that provide a perceptual picture of the interacting system at kinetic freeze-out, which the authors note cannot be obtained from probability density functions alone. The approach can be applied to other collision systems and particle species measured by the CMS, ALICE, LHCb, and ATLAS collaborations at the LHC.
