# GW190814: Spin and equation of state of a neutron star companion

> The recent discovery by LIGO/Virgo of a merging binary having a $\sim 23 M_\odot$ black hole and a $\sim 2.6 M_\odot$ compact companion has triggered a debate regarding the nature of the secondary, which falls into the so-called mass gap. Here we explore some consequences of the assumption that the secondary was a neutron star (NS). We show with concrete examples of heretofore viable equations of state (EOSs) that rapid uniform rotation may neither be necessary for some EOSs nor sufficient for others to explain the presence of a NS. Absolute upper limits for the maximum mass of a spherical NS derived from GW170817 already suggest that this unknown compact companion might be a slowly or even a nonrotating NS. However several soft NS EOSs favored by GW170817 with maximum spherical masses $\lesssim 2.1 M_\odot$ cannot be invoked to explain this object, even allowing for maximum uniform rotation. By contrast, sufficiently stiff EOSs that yield $2.6 M_\odot$ NSs which are slowly rotating or, in some cases, nonrotating, and are compatible with GW170817 and the results of NICER, can account for the black hole companion.

# GW190814: Spin and Equation of State of a Neutron Star Companion

## Key claim
The secondary compact object in GW190814 (mass ~2.6 solar masses) could be a slowly rotating or even nonrotating neutron star if a sufficiently stiff equation of state is assumed, rather than requiring rapid uniform rotation. However, several soft equations of state favored by GW170817 with maximum spherical masses below ~2.1 solar masses cannot explain this object even when maximum uniform rotation is allowed.

## Method
The authors computed equilibrium models of uniformly rotating neutron stars using the relativistic rotating equilibrium code of Cook, Shapiro, and Teukolsky (1992, 1994) for two representative equations of state: the soft SLy and the moderately stiff DD2. They compared the maximum supramassive mass limits (mass-shedding limit) for each equation of state against the measured mass range of the GW190814 secondary (2.59 solar masses with error bars) and evaluated consistency with constraints from GW170817 and NICER observations.

## Key numbers
- The secondary mass in GW190814 is 2.59 (+0.08, -0.09) solar masses, with primary mass 23.2 (+1.1, -1.0) solar masses and mass ratio 0.112 (+0.008, -0.009)
- For the SLy equation of state, the maximum supramassive mass is below 2.59 solar masses, meaning no uniformly rotating star can reach the GW190814 secondary mass
- The supramassive limit for SLy has dimensionless spin chi = 0.7 and rotational period P = 0.55 milliseconds
- For the DD2 equation of state, the supramassive limit is 2.92 solar masses, allowing stable models with periods around 1 millisecond
- One DD2 model with period P = 1.2 milliseconds has chi = 0.34, T/W = 0.03, and polar-to-equatorial radius ratio Rp/Re = 0.91
- The DD2 model at the Keplerian limit has chi = 0.7, T/W = 0.13, Rp/Re = 0.56, and P = 0.7 milliseconds
- The fastest observed pulsar PSR J1748-2446ad has period ~1.4 milliseconds
- The DD2 equation of state yields radius R = 13.3 km for mass M = 1.44 solar masses and period P = 4.9 milliseconds, consistent with NICER results for PSR J0030+0451 (radius 13.02 (+1.24, -1.06) km, mass 1.44 (+0.15, -0.14) solar masses)
- Absolute upper limits for maximum spherical neutron star mass from GW170817: 2.51 solar masses for low-spin priors (beta = 1.20) and 3.01 solar masses for high-spin priors (beta = 1.20)
- The effective inspiral spin parameter chi_eff for GW190814 is -0.002 (+0.060, -0.061)

## Context
This work extends the debate following the GW190814 detection by Abbott et al. (2020) regarding whether the ~2.6 solar mass secondary is a neutron star or black hole. It directly addresses the proposal by Most et al. (2020) that the secondary must be rapidly spinning, showing instead that rapid uniform rotation is neither necessary (for stiff equations of state like DD2) nor sufficient (for soft equations of state like SLy). The analysis builds on maximum mass constraints from GW170817 derived by Margalit and Metzger (2017), Rezzolla et al. (2018), Shibata et al. (2017, 2019), and Ruiz et al. (2018), and on the NICER results for PSR J0030+0451 by Miller et al. (2019) and Riley et al. (2019). The work enables future studies to consider slowly rotating neutron star models for GW190814 without requiring exotic physics, while also ruling out many soft equations of state previously considered viable.
