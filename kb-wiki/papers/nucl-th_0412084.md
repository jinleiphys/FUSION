# In-Medium Properties of Hadrons - Observables II

> In this review we discuss the observable consequences of in-medium changes of hadronic properties in reactions with elementary probes, and in particular photons, on nuclei. After an outline of the theoretical method used we focus on a discussion of actual observables in photonuclear reactions; we discuss in detail $2π$- and vector-meson production. We show that the $2π^0$ photoproduction data can be well described by final state interactions of the pions produced whereas the semi-charged $π^0π^\pm$ channel exhibits a major discrepancy with theory. For $ω$ production on nuclei in the TAPS/CB@ELSA experiment we analyse the $π^0γ$ decay channel, and illustrate the strength of the method by simulating experimental acceptance problems. Completely free of final state interactions is dilepton production in the few GeV range. We show that the sensitivity of this decay channel to changes of hadronic properties in medium in photonuclear reactions on nuclei is as large as in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions and make predictions for the on-going G7 experiment at JLAB. Finally we discuss that hadron production in nuclei at 10 -- 20 GeV photon energies can give important information on the hadronization process, and in particular on the time-scales involved. We show here detailed calculations for the low-energy (12 GeV) run at HERMES and predictions for planned experiments at JLAB.

## Key claim

The authors argue that photonuclear reactions on nuclei can serve as a cleaner, baseline testing ground for in-medium modifications of hadronic properties compared to ultrarelativistic heavy-ion collisions, because the nuclear target remains close to equilibrium at densities at or below nuclear matter equilibrium density. However, they acknowledge that the average nuclear density felt by vector mesons in such experiments lies much below equilibrium density, so any density-dependent effects are naively expected to be much smaller than in heavy-ion reactions.

## Method

The authors separate photonuclear reactions into three steps: determining shadowing of the incoming photon via a Glauber multiple scattering model, producing the primary particle using either a nucleon resonance model (for invariant mass W below 2 GeV) or PYTHIA/FRITIOF (for W above 2 GeV), and propagating the produced particles through the nuclear medium using a semiclassical coupled-channel transport model based on the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equation. This BUU approach propagates spectral phase space distributions of all particles, allowing for off-shell particle production and transport with correct spectral functions, and includes both elastic and inelastic collisions as well as Pauli-principle effects.

## Key numbers

- The collision width of the S11(1535) resonance at nuclear matter equilibrium density is about 35 MeV, as estimated from collisional broadening calculations.
- For the omega meson, theoretical predictions for its mass in nuclear matter at rest range from 640 to 765 MeV, with width up to 50 MeV.
- The authors simulate omega production at photon energies of 1.2 GeV and 2.5 GeV, covering the accessible energies of the TAPS/Crystal Barrel experiment.
- For the omega meson, a mass shift of m_omega = m_omega^0 - 0.18 * rho/rho_0 (where rho_0 is nuclear matter equilibrium density) shows good agreement with preliminary TAPS/CB@ELSA data for a photon escape probability of 5 percent.
- In the 2pi^0 photoproduction channel on carbon and lead targets, the observed downward shift of the two-pion invariant mass spectrum can be reproduced by final state interactions without any in-medium modification of the pi-pi interaction.
- For the semi-charged pi^0 pi^pm channel, the theoretical result overestimates the data by up to a factor of 3.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous theoretical framework (Alvarez-Ruso et al., 2004, Part I) for studying in-medium hadron properties, applying it to observable consequences in photonuclear reactions. The paper directly addresses the interpretation of experiments by the TAPS collaboration (Krusche et al., 2000) on eta photoproduction, the CHAOS collaboration (2000) on pion-induced two-pion production, and the CERES experiment (2003) on dilepton production in heavy-ion collisions. The results enable future experiments at JLAB (G7 experiment) and HERMES (12 GeV run) to probe in-medium spectral functions and hadronization time scales, providing a baseline for interpreting more complex heavy-ion collision data from RHIC and the planned CBM experiment at FAIR.
