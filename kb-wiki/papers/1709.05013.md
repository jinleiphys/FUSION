# Constraining the Mass and Radius of Neutron Stars in Globular Clusters

> We analyze observations of eight quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries in globular clusters and combine them to determine the neutron star mass-radius curve and the equation of state of dense matter. We determine the effect that several uncertainties may have on our results, including uncertainties in the distance, the atmosphere composition, the neutron star maximum mass, the neutron star mass distribution, the possible presence of a hotspot on the neutron star surface, and the prior choice for the equation of state of dense matter. We find that the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is most likely from 10 to 14 km and that tighter constraints are only possible with stronger assumptions about the nature of the neutron stars, the systematics of the observations, or the nature of dense matter. Strong phase transitions are preferred over other models and interpretations of the data with a Bayes factor of 8 or more, and in this case, the radius is likely smaller than 12 km. However, radii larger than 12 km are preferred if the neutron stars have uneven temperature distributions.

## Key claim

The radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is most likely between 10 and 14 km, but tighter constraints require stronger assumptions about the neutron stars, observational systematics, or the nature of dense matter; strong phase transitions are preferred with a Bayes factor of 8 or more, in which case the radius is likely smaller than 12 km, though radii larger than 12 km are preferred if the neutron stars have uneven temperature distributions.

## Method

The authors analyzed X-ray spectra of eight quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries in globular clusters using Chandra and XMM-Newton data, fitting each spectrum with neutron star atmosphere models (hydrogen or helium) and including systematic uncertainties from pileup, distance, and interstellar absorption. They converted each spectrum into a probability distribution for mass and radius, then combined all eight distributions in a Bayesian framework that marginalizes over distance, atmosphere composition (with a prior of 2/3 hydrogen and 1/3 helium based on observed donor star ratios), and a parametrized equation of state, requiring the maximum neutron star mass to exceed 2 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: most likely from 10 to 14 km
- Bayes factor for strong phase transitions over other models: 8 or more
- Distance to 47 Tuc: 4.53 +/- 0.06 kpc
- Distance to omega Cen: 5.22 +/- 0.17 kpc (derived as 16 +/- 3% farther than 47 Tuc)
- Distance to NGC 6397: 2.47 +/- 0.07 kpc (derived as 54.5 +/- 2.5% of 47 Tuc's distance)
- Distance to M13: 7.8 +/- 0.36 kpc
- Distance to NGC 6304: 6.22 +/- 0.26 kpc
- Distance to M30: 8.78 +/- 0.33 kpc
- Distance to M28: 5.5 +/- 0.3 kpc
- Systematic error added to all spectra: 3%
- Maximum possible bias from hot spots on inferred radius: up to 28% smaller than true radius
- Prior probability for hydrogen atmosphere: 2/3 (baseline), with alternative models using 90% or 100%

## Context

This work extends previous combined analyses of quiescent LMXBs by Guillot (2013), who found a radius of 9.1 km forcing all neutron stars to the same radius, and by Steiner (2010) and Lattimer (2014), who used Bayesian frameworks with parametrized equations of state. It also builds on the individual source analyses of Bogdanov (2016) for 47 Tuc X7 and X5, and Ozel (2016) who combined thermonuclear burst and quiescent LMXB constraints. The key advance is allowing each neutron star to have either a hydrogen or helium atmosphere (except where independent evidence dictates otherwise) and systematically including pileup, distance uncertainties, and the possibility of hot spots, which enables quantitative assessment of how different assumptions affect the inferred radius and equation of state.
