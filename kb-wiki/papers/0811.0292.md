# Using CMBR analysis tools for flow anisotropies in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Recently we have shown that there are crucial similarities in the physics of cosmic microwave background radiation (CMBR) anisotropies and the flow anisotropies in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments (RHICE). We also argued that, following CMBR anisotropy analysis, a plot of root-mean square values of the flow coefficients, calculated in a lab fixed frame for RHICE, can yield important information about the nature of initial state anisotropies and their evolution. Here we demonstrate the strength of this technique by showing that elliptic flow for non-central collisions can be directly determined from such a plot without any need for the determination of event-plane.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a plot of root-mean-square flow coefficients ${\tilde v}_n^{rms}$ calculated in a lab-fixed frame (without event-plane determination) shows a prominent peak at $n=2$ for non-central collisions, and that the height of this peak increases with impact parameter, allowing direct determination of elliptic flow. However, this result depends on the assumption that all Fourier coefficients for momentum anisotropy are related to the corresponding coefficients for spatial anisotropy by roughly the same proportionality factor (taken as 0.2), and that the contribution from random initial-state fluctuations remains subdominant compared to the elliptic shape of the overlap region.

## Method

The authors used HIJING to generate Au-Au collision events at 200 GeV/A center-of-mass energy, calculating initial transverse energy density distributions at proper time $\tau_{eq}=1$ fm. They computed Fourier coefficients $F_n$ of spatial anisotropies in the lab-fixed frame from the parton energy distribution, then converted these to momentum anisotropy coefficients ${\tilde v}_n^{rms}$ using a proportionality factor of 0.2. The root-mean-square values were obtained by averaging over 10000 events for each impact parameter value ($b=0$, 5 fm, and 8 fm with 1 fm spread).

## Key numbers

- Impact parameters used: $b=0$, 5 fm, and 8 fm, with 1 fm spread for non-zero $b$ cases
- Averages taken over 10000 events for each impact parameter
- Proportionality factor between spatial and momentum Fourier coefficients taken as 0.2
- Initial thermalization time $\tau_{eq}=1$ fm
- Transverse area element $\Delta A \simeq 0.5$ fm$^2$ used for energy density calculation
- The minimum $n$ below which superhorizon suppression occurs is given by $n_{min} = \pi (1 + 0.05 {\bar R}(\tau_{eq}) / c_s^2)$

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous proposal (Mishra et al., 2007) that CMBR anisotropy analysis techniques can be applied to relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where root-mean-square flow coefficients in a lab-fixed frame replace the conventional event-plane analysis. The method contrasts with traditional elliptic flow analysis (flow0, 1990s) which requires determination of event plane for each event and has only extracted flow coefficients up to $v_6$. The technique enables direct determination of $v_2$ without event-plane determination, potentially allowing analysis of larger datasets. The authors also connect their approach to the concept of superhorizon fluctuations from inflationary cosmology, noting that Sorensen (2008) has proposed using suppression of superhorizon fluctuations to explain azimuthal correlations observed at RHIC.
