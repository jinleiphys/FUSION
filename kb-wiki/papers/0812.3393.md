# Free-streaming approximation in early dynamics of relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We investigate an approximation to early dynamics in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, where after formation the partons are free streaming and around the proper time of 1 fm/c undergo a sudden equilibration described in terms of the Landau matching condition. We discuss physical and formal aspects of this approach. In particular, we show that initial azimuthally asymmetric transverse flow develops for non-central collisions as a consequence of the sudden equilibration. Moreover, the energy-momentum tensor from the free-streaming stage matches very smoothly to the form used in the transverse hydrodynamics, whereas matching to isotropic hydrodynamics requires a more pronounced change in the energy-momentum tensor. After the hydrodynamic phase statistical hadronization is carried out with the help of THERMINATOR. The physical results for the transverse-momentum spectra, the elliptic-flow, and the Hanbury-Brown--Twiss correlation radii, including the ratio R_out/R_side as well as the dependence of the radii on the azimuthal angle (azHBT), are properly described within our approach. The agreement is equally good for a purely hydrodynamic evolution started at an early proper time of 0.25 fm/c, or for the free streaming started at that time, followed by the sudden equilibration at tau ~1 fm/c and then by perfect hydrodynamics. Thus, the inclusion of free streaming allows us to delay the start of hydrodynamics to more realistic times of the order of 1 fm/c.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a free-streaming phase followed by sudden equilibration (FS+SE) at around 1 fm/c can replace purely hydrodynamic evolution starting at 0.25 fm/c, producing equally good agreement with RHIC data, provided the initial flow generated by the sudden equilibration compensates for the spatial eccentricity reduction during free streaming.

## Method

The authors model the early stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions by letting massless partons free-stream from proper time tau_0 = 0.25 fm/c until tau = 1 fm/c, then apply Landau matching to instantaneously equilibrate the system into a perfect fluid. The subsequent evolution uses isotropic perfect hydrodynamics with a lattice-QCD-based equation of state, followed by statistical hadronization via the THERMINATOR code. The initial parton distributions are taken from either Gaussian profiles or Glauber-model profiles from GLISSANDO, with boost-invariant kinematics and a narrow rapidity distribution.

## Key numbers

- Free streaming starts at tau_0 = 0.25 fm/c and ends at tau = 1 fm/c (ratio tau/tau_0 = 4)
- Gaussian profile width parameters a = 1.9 fm and b = 2.5 fm for Au+Au collisions at RHIC at centrality 20-30%
- Glauber model uses 85.5% wounded nucleons and 14.5% binary collisions
- The transverse velocity from the small-expansion approximation is v = (Delta tau/3) * (x/a^2, y/b^2) for the Gaussian profile
- The energy-momentum tensor in the local rest frame after free streaming has T_xx = T_yy = epsilon/2 (transverse hydrodynamics form) to within a few percent
- The generated momentum asymmetry epsilon_p increases with matching time; at tau = 1 fm it is comparable to the purely hydrodynamic case starting at 0.25 fm
- Free-streaming times beyond tau - tau_0 ~ 1.5 fm spoil agreement with data

## Context

This work extends the FS+SE approach originally proposed by Kolb, Sollfrank, and Heinz (2000) and further studied by Jas and Mrowczynski (2007) and Sinyukov, Gyulassy, Karpenko, and Nazarenko (2006-2008). It directly builds on the authors' previous uniform description of RHIC soft hadron data (Broniowski et al., 2008) which used purely hydrodynamic evolution from 0.25 fm/c. The paper demonstrates that delaying hydrodynamics to 1 fm/c via FS+SE is viable, addressing the RHIC HBT puzzle by generating initial azimuthally asymmetric flow. This enables future work to use more realistic initial times for hydrodynamic modeling and connects to viscous hydrodynamics as an approximation to systems with time-decreasing viscosity.
