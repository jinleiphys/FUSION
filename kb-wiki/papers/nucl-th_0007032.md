# Hadron structure after 25 years of QCD

> We briefly review the status of our understanding of hadron structure based on QCD. This includes the role of symmetries, especially chiral symmetry, and the insights provided by lattice QCD. The main focus is on baryon structure and especially the nucleon, but this cannot be treated realistically without reference to spectroscopy. Our aim is to highlight recent insights and promising directions for future work.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the constituent quark model has been phenomenologically successful, a quantitatively reliable description of light-quark hadron structure must incorporate the chiral symmetry of QCD, particularly through the inclusion of Goldstone boson loops that produce non-analytic behavior in hadron properties as functions of quark mass.

## Method

The author reviews the status of hadron structure from QCD by examining the non-perturbative vacuum, lattice QCD results for quark and gluon propagators, and the successes and limitations of the non-relativistic quark model. The analysis focuses on chiral symmetry and its consequences, including the trace anomaly, QCD sum rules, and the leading non-analytic (LNA) behavior of hadron masses, magnetic moments, and parton distributions, using the cloudy bag model (CBM) as a framework that respects chiral constraints.

## Key numbers

- Vacuum energy density of pure gluonic QCD: \(\epsilon_{\rm vac} = -0.5\) GeV/fm\(^3\), an order of magnitude larger than the MIT bag constant \(B\)
- Light quark condensates: \(\langle \bar u u \rangle \approx \langle \bar d d \rangle \approx (-240\ \text{MeV})^3\)
- Quenched QCD gives \(f_B = 220\) MeV; full QCD gives \(f_B = 260\) MeV
- Lattice gluon propagator is non-perturbative for \(q^2 < 4\) GeV\(^2\)
- For a current quark mass of 110 MeV, lattice quark propagator gives \(M(0) \sim 400\) MeV, decreasing to \(\sim 300\) MeV in the chiral limit
- Lowest glueball mass from lattice: \(0^{++}\) at \(1670 \pm 20\) MeV
- LNA contribution to nucleon mass from pion loops: \(\sigma_{NN}^{LNA} = -5.6 m_\pi^3\); at physical pion mass this is \(-17\) MeV, but at \(m_\pi = 420\) MeV it is \(-460\) MeV
- Sigma commutator from chiral extrapolation: \(\sigma_N \in (45,55)\) MeV, compared to experimental value \(45 \pm 8\) MeV and lattice result \(18 \pm 5\) MeV
- LNA coefficient for nucleon magnetic moments: \(\alpha = 4.4\ \mu_N\) GeV\(^{-1}\); at physical pion mass the LNA contribution is \(0.6\ \mu_N\)
- Extrapolated proton magnetic moment: \(2.85 \pm 0.22\ \mu_N\); neutron: \(-1.90 \pm 0.15\ \mu_N\)

## Context

This invited talk at Few Body XVI (2000) reviews 25 years of QCD-based hadron structure research, extending the constituent quark model work of Capstick and Isgur (1990s) and the lattice QCD results from CP-PACS and UKQCD collaborations. It directly addresses the spin-orbit problem noted by Isgur and the chiral extrapolation method developed by Leinweber et al. (1999). The paper enables future work on chiral extrapolations of lattice data, coupled-channel treatments of baryon resonances (such as the Roper and Lambda(1405) studied by the Julich group), and the interpretation of hybrid meson candidates from BNL experiment E852.
