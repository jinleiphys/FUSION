# The imprint of conservation laws on correlated particle production

> The study of event-by-event fluctuations of net-baryon number in a subspace of full phase space is a promising direction for deciphering the structure of strongly interacting matter created in head-on collisions of relativistic heavy nuclei. Such fluctuations are generally suppressed by exact baryon number conservation. Moreover, the suppression is stronger if baryon number is conserved locally. In this report we present a conceptually new approach to quantify correlations in rapidity space between baryon-antibaryon, baryon-baryon, and antibaryon-antibaryon pairs and demonstrate their impact on net-baryon number fluctuations. For the special case of Gaussian rapidity distributions, we use the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, while the general case is introduced by exploiting the well-known Metropolis and Simulated Annealing methods. The approach is based on the use of the canonical ensemble of statistical mechanics for baryon number and can be applied to study correlations between baryons as well as strange and/or charm hadrons. It can also be applied to describe relativistic nuclear collisions leading to the production of multi-particle final states. One application of our method is the search for formation of proton clusters at low collision energies emerging as a harbinger of the anticipated first-order chiral phase transition. In a first step, the results obtained are compared to the recent measurements from the CERN ALICE collaboration. Such investigations are key to explore the phase diagram of strongly interacting matter and baryon production mechanisms at energy scales from several GeV to several TeV.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new method to quantify correlations in rapidity space between baryon-antibaryon, baryon-baryon, and antibaryon-antibaryon pairs, demonstrating that the suppression of net-baryon number fluctuations depends on the correlation length between baryons and antibaryons, not just on global baryon number conservation. The authors show that for LHC energies, the ALICE data imply long-range correlations (correlation length of about 12 units of rapidity), with the shortest range consistent within one standard deviation being 5.6 units of rapidity.

## Method

The authors use the Canonical Ensemble (CE) formulation of statistical mechanics to impose exact baryon number conservation, combined with a Metropolis algorithm to introduce correlations between baryons and antibaryons in rapidity space. For Gaussian rapidity distributions, they employ Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix to generate correlated random numbers, while for arbitrary rapidity distributions they use the Metropolis algorithm with simulated annealing to achieve a desired correlation coefficient. The probability of having a given number of baryons and antibaryons inside a finite acceptance is computed by summing over all possible full-phase-space configurations weighted by the CE probability distribution and the acceptance probability that depends on the correlation coefficient.

## Key numbers

- The central fireball volume at full LHC energy is estimated as V0 ≈ 6900 fm³ for a slice at rapidity 0 with δy = ±0.5, based on ALICE pion HBT measurements.
- For Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, the mean multiplicities in full phase space are NB = N_B̄ = 750, with single-particle partition function z = 750.25.
- The ALICE data for normalized second-order cumulants of net-proton number show a value of 0.97 at Δη = 1.6, deviating progressively from unity with increasing acceptance.
- The correlation length consistent with ALICE data is Δycorr = 12 (ρ = 0.1), with the shortest range consistent within one standard deviation being Δycorr = 5.6 units of rapidity (ρ = 0.8).
- For a fixed acceptance of 26% for single baryons, the pair acceptance ranges from αB² (for ρ = 0) to αB (for ρ = 1.0).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of net-baryon number fluctuations with emphasis on conservation laws by Braun-Munzinger (2018, 2020) and Bzdak (2012), and provides a non-critical baseline for fluctuation measurements that can be compared to experimental data from ALICE (2019, 2022), STAR (2020, 2021), and HADES (2020). The method enables quantitative extraction of correlation lengths from experimental cumulant measurements, which is essential for searching for critical phenomena such as the anticipated first-order chiral phase transition and for testing predictions from Lattice QCD (HotQCD 2018, Borsanyi 2020) and the Hadron Resonance Gas model (Andronic 2005, 2018). The approach can be extended to study correlations between strange and charm hadrons, and to search for proton clusters emerging from spinodal instabilities at low collision energies.
