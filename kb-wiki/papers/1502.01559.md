# Relativistic density functional theory for finite nuclei and neutron stars

> The main goal of the present contribution is a pedagogical introduction to the fascinating world of neutron stars by relying on relativistic density functional theory. Density functional theory provides a powerful--and perhaps unique--framework for the calculation of both the properties of finite nuclei and neutron stars. Given the enormous densities that may be reached in the core of neutron stars, it is essential that such theoretical framework incorporates from the outset the basic principles of Lorentz covariance and special relativity. After a brief historical perspective, we present the necessary details required to compute the equation of state of dense, neutron-rich matter. As the equation of state is all that is needed to compute the structure of neutron stars, we discuss how nuclear physics--particularly certain kind of laboratory experiments--can provide significant constrains on the behavior of neutron-rich matter.

## Key claim

Relativistic density functional theory provides a powerful framework for calculating both finite nuclei and neutron star properties, but the paper emphasizes that extrapolations into regions inaccessible by laboratory experiments must be accompanied by well-quantified theoretical uncertainties, and that certain empirical coefficients in the effective Lagrangian may remain poorly determined even after optimization.

## Method

The paper presents a relativistic mean-field approach based on a Lagrangian density with nucleons, three meson fields (isoscalar-scalar sigma, isoscalar-vector omega, isovector-vector rho), and the photon, supplemented by four nonlinear meson interaction terms (kappa, lambda, zeta, Lambda_v) that improve the model's quality. The equations of motion are solved self-consistently in the mean-field approximation, first for infinite nuclear matter to obtain the equation of state, and then the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are used to compute neutron star structure from that equation of state. The composition of the outer crust is determined by minimizing the chemical potential at fixed pressure, using nuclear mass tables combined with a relativistic Fermi gas description of electrons and a lattice contribution for the body-centered-cubic Coulomb crystal.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear matter saturation density: rho_0 approximately 2.4 x 10^14 g/cm^3, corresponding to baryon density n_0 approximately 0.15 fm^-3
- Outer crust spans baryon densities from about 10^4 g/cm^3 to 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3
- The present record for neutron star mass stands at 2 solar masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013)
- The original Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit for a neutron star supported only by degenerate neutron pressure was about 0.7 solar masses (Oppenheimer and Volkoff 1939)
- The Chandrasekhar mass limit for white dwarf collapse is about 1.4 solar masses (Chandrasekhar 1931)
- The Crab pulsar (PSR B0531+21) has a period of 33 ms, magnetic field of 10^12 G, and density of 10^15 g/cm^3
- The lattice constant for the bcc Coulomb crystal is C_l = 3.40665 x 10^-3 (dimensionless)

## Context

This pedagogical chapter extends the relativistic mean-field approach pioneered by Walecka (1974) and Serot (1984) and the nonlinear meson interactions introduced by Boguta and Bodmer (1977), connecting these nuclear structure models to neutron star astrophysics through the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. The work builds on the effective field theory perspective advocated by Furnstahl (1996, 1997) and the density functional theory framework of Kohn (1964, 1965), and it enables future constraints on the poorly-determined symmetry energy and its density dependence using both laboratory experiments with radioactive beams and astronomical observations of neutron star masses and radii.
