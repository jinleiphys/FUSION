# On the Application of the Analytical Discrete Ordinates Method to the Solution of Nonclassical Transport Problems in Slab Geometry

> In this work we investigate the use of the Analytical Discrete Ordinates (ADO) method when solving the spectral approximation of the nonclassical transport equation. The spectral approximation is a recently developed method based on the representation of the nonclassical angular flux as a series of Laguerre polynomials. This representation generates, as outcome, a system of equations that have the form of classical transport equations and can therefore be solved by current deterministic algorithms. Thus, the investigation of efficient approaches to solve the nonclassical transport equation is of interest and shall be pursued. This is the case of the ADO method which has been successfully used to solve a wide class of problems in the general area of particle transport. Numerical results are presented for two nonclassical test problems in slab geometry. These nonclassical transport problems are chosen in such way that their solution exactly reproduces the solution of the classical diffusion problem. Very accurate results are obtained for both test problems. However, the use of high precision arithmetic is sometimes required as illustrated in the second test problem. Limitations of the spectral approximation are also analyzed and discussed.

## Key claim

The Analytical Discrete Ordinates (ADO) method can successfully solve the spectral approximation of the nonclassical transport equation in slab geometry, producing very accurate results for problems whose solution reproduces classical diffusion, but the spectral approximation itself has a convergence limitation: the Laguerre-series coefficients diverge when the classical total cross section sigma_t is less than or equal to sqrt(3)/6.

## Method

The authors apply the Analytical Discrete Ordinates (ADO) method to solve the system of equations obtained from the spectral approximation of the nonclassical transport equation, which expands the nonclassical angular flux as a series of Laguerre polynomials in the path-length variable s. For slab geometry with isotropic scattering and vacuum boundary conditions, the general solution is written as a superposition of homogeneous and particular solutions, with the homogeneous solution obtained by solving an eigenvalue problem whose order is half the number of discrete angles. The test problems are constructed so that the nonclassical transport solution exactly reproduces the solution of the classical diffusion equation, using a free-path distribution p(s) = lambda^2 s exp(-lambda s) with lambda = sqrt(3) sigma_t.

## Key numbers

* The Laguerre-series coefficients L_k converge to zero only when sigma_t > sqrt(3)/6, which is approximately 0.288675; for sigma_t less than or equal to this value, the series diverges.
* For the first test problem at spatial position x = 0.0, the direct solution gives a scalar flux of 1.472760E+00, and the ADO solution with N = 30 gives 1.472740E+00 (three methods using N = 30 give values between 1.472740E+00 and 1.472780E+00).
* At x = 2.0, the direct solution is 1.243550E-01 and all four ADO-based methods with N = 30 give exactly 1.243550E-01.
* At x = 10.0, the direct solution is 1.045670E-06, while the ADO solutions with N = 30 range from 1.045680E-06 to 1.045760E-06.

## Context

This work extends the spectral method developed by Vasques (2020) for solving the nonclassical transport equation, which previously used the classical Diamond Difference method and pointed out numerical challenges. The paper is the first to apply the ADO method, previously used by Barichello (1999) and others for classical particle transport problems, to the nonclassical transport equations. The results enable further investigation of efficient deterministic algorithms for nonclassical transport, particularly for problems where the free-path distribution is not exponential, such as neutron transport in pebble-bed reactors and radiative transfer through atmospheric clouds as discussed by Davis (2006) and Larsen (2007, 2011).
