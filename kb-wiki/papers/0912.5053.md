# The Nuclear Born Oppenheimer Method and Nuclear Rotations

> We deal here with the application of the Nuclear Born Oppenheimer (NBO) method to the description of nuclear rotations. As an edifying illustration, we apply the NBO formalism to study the rotational motion of nuclei which are axially-symmetric and even, but whose shells are not closed. We focus, in particular, on the derivation of expressions for the rotational energy and for the moment of inertia. Additionally, we examine the connection between the NBO method and the self-consistent cranking (SCC) model. Finally, we compare the moment of inertia generated by the NBO method with the Thouless-Valantin formula and hence establish a connection between the NBO method and the large body of experimental data.

## Key claim

The Nuclear Born-Oppenheimer (NBO) method, when applied to axially-symmetric even nuclei with non-closed shells, yields a rotational energy expression that initially gives a constant moment of inertia (contradicting experimental data) but can generate a moment of inertia that increases with angular momentum if the restrictive assumption of time-reversal invariance of the intrinsic state is dropped.

## Method

The NBO method constructs a factorable trial wave function modeled after the molecular Born-Oppenheimer ansatz, decomposing the nuclear Hamiltonian into a series of operators that commute with a chosen collective tensor operator. For axially-symmetric nuclei, the collective degrees of freedom are separated into rotational and vibrational parts by transforming to a body-fixed frame, and the mean energy is expressed in terms of angular momentum operators acting on the collective amplitude and intrinsic state. The moment of inertia is derived from a variational condition that yields the Thouless-Valantin formula, and the formalism is extended to symmetry-violating intrinsic states to obtain a cranking-like description.

## Key numbers

- The molecular Born-Oppenheimer approximation accuracy is given by the ratio \(E_{\rm BO}/E_{\rm exact} = 1 - \frac{1}{4} \chi\) and wave function overlap \(|\langle\psi_{\rm BO}|\psi_{\rm exact}\rangle|^2 = 1 - \frac{3}{128} \chi^2\), where \(\chi = m_e/M \simeq 10^{-4}\).
- The rotational energy for the \(K=0\) band with time-reversal invariant intrinsic state is \(E_I = \frac{1}{2} B I(I+1) + \langle \hat{H}\rangle - \Delta E_{f\ell}\), where \(\Delta E_{f\ell} = \frac{B}{2} \langle \Delta \hat{J}^2_1 + \Delta \hat{J}^2_2\rangle\).
- The Inglis cranking formula for the moment of inertia is given by \(1/B \approx 2 \sum_{\sigma\mu} |\langle\phi| \hat{J}_a |\phi_{\sigma\mu}\rangle|^2 / ({\mathcal{E}}_\sigma - {\mathcal{E}}_\mu)\).
- The critical angular velocity \(\omega_c\) is determined by \(\langle \hat{J}_1\rangle^2_{\omega_c} = I(I+1)\).

## Context

This work extends the Nuclear Born-Oppenheimer method, previously applied to small-amplitude collective oscillations and zero-point fluctuations by Zettili (2009), to the description of nuclear rotations. The NBO method is shown to connect to the self-consistent cranking (SCC) model and yields the Thouless-Valantin formula for the moment of inertia, which reduces to the Inglis cranking formula when residual two-body interactions are neglected. The approach enables a variational description of rotational bands with angular-momentum-dependent moments of inertia, addressing the failure of constant-moment-of-inertia models to reproduce experimental data.
