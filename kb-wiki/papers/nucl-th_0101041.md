# Reaction mechanisms for two-neutron halo breakup

> We describe a reaction mechanism which is consistent with all available experimental information of high energy three-body breakup processes. The dominating channels are removal of one of the three halo particles leaving the other two either undisturbed or absorbed. We compare with the commonly used deceptive assumption of a decay through two-body resonance states. Our predictions can be tested by measuring neutron-neutron invariant mass spectra.

# Reaction mechanisms for two-neutron halo breakup

## Key claim

The dominating reaction channels for high-energy breakup of Borromean two-neutron halos on light targets are one-particle removal followed by decay of the resulting wave packet, not decay through two-body resonance states. The authors argue that the commonly used assumption of decay through two-body resonances or virtual s-states is conceptually inconsistent with the short reaction time implied by the sudden approximation, except when those two-body states are already populated within the initial three-body system.

## Method

The authors employ the participant-spectator model (PSM), where one halo particle (the participant) interacts with the target via a phenomenological optical model while the other two halo particles (the spectators) are treated in the black sphere approximation, absorbed within a given radius from the target or otherwise undisturbed. They compute the population of two-body continuum states after instantaneous removal of the third particle, and compare these PSM predictions with R-matrix fits that assume decay through two-body resonances, using experimental data for 300 MeV/nucleon $^6$He and $^{11}$Li projectiles on carbon targets.

## Key numbers

- $^6$He neutron-$^4$He $p_{3/2}$-resonance energy: 0.77 MeV with width 0.5 MeV used in PSM computation; any $\Gamma_0$ value from 0.4 MeV to 0.8 MeV reproduces the experiment fairly well.
- $^{11}$Li three-body wave function contains around 60% s-wave and 40% p$^2$-wave neutron-core configurations.
- Neutron-$^9$Li system has a low-lying virtual s-state at 240 keV and a p-resonance at 0.5 MeV.
- Root mean square distance between neutrons: more than 6 fm for $^{11}$Li and less than 4.5 fm for $^6$He.
- Two different R-matrix fits for $^{11}$Li neutron removal yield substantially different p-wave contents: about 35% for one fit and about 70% for the other fit (from Zinser 1997).

## Context

This work extends the participant-spectator model developed by Garrido (1998) and addresses the widespread but erroneous assumption that two-body invariant mass spectra from halo breakup can be interpreted as arising from decays of low-lying two-body resonances or virtual s-states, as used in analyses by Aleksandrov (1998), Aumann (1999), Zinser (1997), and Chulkov (1997). The paper clarifies that R-matrix analyses using a truncated basis of resonance states require renormalization that prohibits direct interpretation in terms of the "correct" two-body resonance states. The authors predict that measuring neutron-neutron invariant mass spectra after core breakup can distinguish between the PSM mechanism and resonance-decay models, since the PSM predicts different spectra for $^6$He and $^{11}$Li while a resonance-population mechanism would predict identical spectra for both projectiles.
