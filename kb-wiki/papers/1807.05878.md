# Beam energy dependence of the relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio in heavy-ion collisions

> In this article we investigate the beam energy dependence of relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the single and double $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratios in three heavy-ion reactions with an isospin- and momentum-dependent transport model IBUU11. With the beam energy increasing from 200 to 400 MeV/nucleon, effects of the relativistically retarded electrical fields on the $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio are found to increase gradually from negligibly to considerably significant as expectedly; it is however, the interesting observation is the relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio are becoming gradually insignificant as the beam energy further increasing from 400 to 800 MeV/nucleon. Moreover, we also investigate the isospin dependence of relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio in two isobar reaction systems of $^{96}$Ru+$^{96}$Ru and $^{96}$Zr+$^{96}$Zr at the beam energies from 200 to 800 MeV/nucleon. It is shown that the relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio are independent of the isospin of reaction. Furthermore, we also examine the double $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio in reactions of $^{96}$Zr+$^{96}$Zr over $^{96}$Ru+$^{96}$Ru at the beam energies from 200 to 800 MeV/nucleon with the static field and retarded field, respectively. It is shown the double $π^{-}/π^{+}$ ratio from two reactions is still an effective observable of symmetry energy without the interference of electrical field due to using the relativistic calculation compared to the nonrelativistic calculation.

## Key claim

The relativistic retardation effects of electrical fields on the π−/π+ ratio in heavy-ion collisions are not monotonic with beam energy: they increase from negligible to significant as beam energy rises from 200 to 400 MeV/nucleon, but then become gradually insignificant as beam energy further increases from 400 to 800 MeV/nucleon, due to the competition between enhanced anisotropic features of retarded fields and reduced reaction duration time.

## Method

The authors used the isospin- and momentum-dependent transport model IBUU11 to simulate central Au+Au collisions and isobar reactions of ⁹⁶Ru+⁹⁶Ru and ⁹⁶Zr+⁹⁶Zr at beam energies from 200 to 800 MeV/nucleon. The relativistically retarded electric fields were calculated using the Liénard-Wiechert expression, requiring storage of phase-space histories of all charged particles, while static Coulomb fields were used as the nonrelativistic comparison. The dynamical multiplicities of Δ(1232) resonances and charged pions were traced to understand how retarded fields affect pion production.

## Key numbers

- The relative effect of retarded electrical fields on the π−/π+ ratio reaches a maximum of about 8-9% at 400 MeV/nucleon in central Au+Au collisions.
- The relative effect is less than about 3% at both 200 MeV/nucleon and 800 MeV/nucleon in the same system.
- Beam energies studied range from 200 to 800 MeV/nucleon for Au+Au, ⁹⁶Ru+⁹⁶Ru, and ⁹⁶Zr+⁹⁶Zr reactions.
- The symmetry energy at saturation density is fixed at Esym(ρ0)=30.0 MeV, with normal density ρ0=0.16 fm⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Wei, 2018) which first showed that relativistically retarded electrical fields significantly affect the π−/π+ ratio in Au+Au collisions at 400 MeV/nucleon. The present paper systematically maps the beam energy dependence from 200 to 800 MeV/nucleon, finding a non-monotonic behavior with maximum effect around 400 MeV/nucleon. It also demonstrates that the double π−/π+ ratio from two isobar reactions (⁹⁶Zr+⁹⁶Zr over ⁹⁶Ru+⁹⁶Ru) can effectively eliminate electrical field interference while retaining sensitivity to the symmetry energy, building on the double-ratio concept proposed by Famiano (2006) and Yong (2006). The results caution that relativistic retardation effects must be carefully considered when using the π−/π+ ratio as a probe of nuclear symmetry energy at intermediate energies, especially around 400 MeV/nucleon.
