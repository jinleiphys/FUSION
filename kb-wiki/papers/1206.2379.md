# On the astrophysical robustness of neutron star merger r-process

> In this study we explore the nucleosynthesis in the dynamic ejecta of compact binary mergers. We are particularly interested in the question how sensitive the resulting abundance patterns are to the parameters of the merging system. Therefore, we systematically investigate combinations of neutron star masses in the range from 1.0 to 2.0 \Msun and, for completeness, we compare the results with those from two simulations of a neutron star black hole merger. The ejecta masses vary by a factor of five for the studied systems, but all amounts are (within the uncertainties of the merger rates) compatible with being a major source of cosmic r-process. The ejecta undergo a robust r-process nucleosynthesis which produces all the elements from the second to the third peak in close-to-solar ratios. Most strikingly, this r-process is extremely robust, all 23 investigated binary systems yield practically identical abundance patterns. This is mainly the result of the ejecta being extremely neutron rich (\ye $\approx0.04$) and the r-process path meandering along the neutron drip line so that the abundances are determined entirely by nuclear rather than by astrophysical properties. This robustness together with the ease with which both the second and third peak are reproduced make compact binary mergers the prime candidate for the source of the observed unique heavy r-process component.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the r-process nucleosynthesis in compact binary mergers is extremely robust: all 23 investigated binary systems (21 neutron star-neutron star and 2 neutron star-black hole mergers) yield practically identical abundance patterns, but this robustness depends on the ejecta being extremely neutron rich (electron fraction approximately 0.04) and the r-process path meandering along the neutron drip line, so that abundances are determined entirely by nuclear rather than astrophysical properties.

## Method

The authors performed 21 Smooth Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) simulations of neutron star mergers with masses from 1.0 to 2.0 solar masses in steps of 0.2 solar masses, plus two neutron star-black hole merger simulations with black hole masses of 5 and 10 solar masses. They used the Shen equation of state and an opacity-dependent multi-flavour neutrino leakage scheme to follow electron fraction changes via electron and positron captures. Nucleosynthesis calculations were performed with a large reaction network containing over 5800 isotopes from nucleons up to Z=111, using 30 representative hydrodynamic trajectories from each neutron star merger simulation and 20 from each neutron star-black hole merger simulation.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star masses explored: 1.0 to 2.0 solar masses in steps of 0.2 solar masses
- Ejecta masses range from 7.64 x 10^-3 to 3.9 x 10^-2 solar masses for neutron star mergers, and up to 4.93 x 10^-2 solar masses for the neutron star-black hole case
- Electron fraction of tidal ejecta: 0.04 plus or minus 0.02
- Initial density of tidal ejecta: (1.4 plus or minus 0.5) x 10^14 g/cm^3
- Ejecta masses vary by a factor of five across the studied systems
- Less than 0.008 of trajectories suffer shocks that increase electron fraction up to approximately 0.25
- Heating efficiency parameter used as default: epsilon_th = 0.5
- Nuclear heating power follows a power-law fit proportional to t^-1.3

## Context

This work systematically extends prior punctual studies of neutron star merger nucleosynthesis, which had explored only a narrow range of masses around 1.4 solar masses (Freiburghaus 1999; Goriely 2011; Roberts 2011). The results confirm the occurrence of a robust r-process found in those earlier studies but demonstrate that this robustness holds across a much wider parameter space. The findings contradict the historical view that supernovae are the natural source of r-process elements, which has been challenged by studies showing neutrino-driven winds in supernovae struggle to provide the necessary physical conditions (Arcones 2007; Roberts 2010; Fischer 2010; Huedepohl 2010). The work enables future studies of galactic chemical evolution and identifies the need for improved nuclear physics input, particularly nuclear masses near the neutron drip line and fission properties, to refine abundance predictions.
