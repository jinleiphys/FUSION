# Neutrino-pair emission from nuclear de-excitation in core-collapse supernova simulations

> We study the impact of neutrino-pair production from the de-excitation of highly excited heavy nuclei on core-collapse supernova simulations, following the evolution up to several 100 ms after core bounce. Our study is based on the AGILE-Boltztran supernova code, which features general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics and accurate three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport in spherical symmetry. In our simulations the nuclear de-excitation process is described in two different ways. At first we follow the approach proposed by Fuller and Meyer [Astrophys. J. 376,701 (1991)], which is based on strength functions derived in the framework of the nuclear Fermi-gas model of non-interacting nucleons. Secondly, we parametrize the allowed and forbidden strength distributions in accordance with measurements for selected nuclear ground states. We determine the de-excitation strength by applying the Brink hypothesis and detailed balance. For both approaches, we find that nuclear de-excitation has no effect on the supernova dynamics. However, we find that nuclear de-excitation is the leading source for the production of electron antineutrinos as well as heavy-lepton flavor (anti)neutrinos during the collapse phase. At sufficiently high densities, the associated neutrino spectra are influenced by interactions with the surrounding matter, making proper simulations of neutrino transport important for the determination of the neutrino-energy loss rate. We find that even including nuclear de-excitations, the energy loss during the collapse phase is overwhelmingly dominated by electron neutrinos produced by electron captures.

## Key claim

Nuclear de-excitation of highly excited heavy nuclei has no effect on supernova dynamics, but it is the leading source for electron antineutrinos and heavy-lepton flavor (anti)neutrinos during the collapse phase; however, even including this process, energy loss during collapse is overwhelmingly dominated by electron neutrinos from electron captures.

## Method

The authors implemented neutrino-pair production from nuclear de-excitation into the AGILE-Boltztran supernova code, which features general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics and three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport in spherical symmetry. They described the de-excitation process using two approaches: first, the Fermi-gas model of Fuller and Meyer (1991) based on non-interacting nucleons; second, a parametrization of allowed and forbidden strength distributions based on measurements for selected nuclear ground states, applying Brink's hypothesis and detailed balance. They evolved an 11.2 solar mass star from presupernova progenitor through core collapse, bounce, and post-bounce evolution for more than 300 ms.

## Key numbers

- The simulation follows evolution up to several 100 ms after core bounce.
- The progenitor star has mass 11.2 solar masses.
- The equation of state uses compressibility K = 220 MeV for matter at temperatures above T = 0.45 MeV.
- For the Gaussian strength parametrization: allowed strength centroid mu_A = 9 MeV with sigma_A = 5 MeV; forbidden strength centroid mu_F = 22 MeV with sigma_F = 7 MeV.
- Total allowed strength S_A = 5; total forbidden strength S_F = 7.
- The de-excitation process is relevant at temperatures around 1 MeV and higher, corresponding to nuclear excitation energies above about 10 MeV.
- The strength functions are compared at temperatures T = 0.7 MeV, T = 1.5 MeV, and T = 3.0 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Fuller and Meyer (1991) by implementing nuclear de-excitation in full supernova simulations for the first time, using the AGILE-Boltztran code. It also provides an alternative parametrization based on experimentally constrained Gamow-Teller and forbidden strength distributions, guided by measurements of giant resonances from experiments such as (p,p') and (e,e') studies. The results enable future simulations to properly account for neutrino-pair production from nuclear de-excitation, though the authors find it does not affect dynamics and is subdominant to electron capture for energy loss.
