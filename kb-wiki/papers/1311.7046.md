# The Nuclear Physics of Neutron Stars

> We explore the unique and fascinating structure of neutron stars. Although neutron stars are of interest in many areas of Physics, our aim is to provide an intellectual bridge between Nuclear Physics and Astrophysics. We argue against the naive perception of a neutron star as a uniform assembly of neutrons packed to enormous densities. Rather, by focusing on the many exotic phases that are speculated to exist in a neutron star, we show how the reality is different and far more interesting.

## Key claim
The paper argues that neutron stars are not uniform assemblies of neutrons but contain exotic phases (crust, pasta, core) whose structure is constrained by both laboratory nuclear physics experiments and astronomical observations. The author hedges that while recent observations of massive neutron stars (near 2 solar masses) and measurements of neutron-skin thickness in lead provide constraints, the equation of state remains poorly determined, with conflicting radius measurements suggesting either soft or stiff behavior.

## Method
The paper reviews the structure of neutron stars from outer crust to core, using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations of general relativity to relate pressure, energy density, and mass profiles. It connects nuclear density functional theory predictions for the symmetry energy slope L and neutron-skin thickness in 208Pb to neutron-star radii and maximum masses, comparing these predictions against astronomical observations of pulsars, X-ray bursters, and quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron-star mass likely does not exceed 3 solar masses (Lattimer 2006)
- Two accurately measured neutron stars have masses near 2 solar masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013)
- Millisecond pulsar PSR 1937+21 rotates with spin period 1.557806448872 milliseconds, giving an upper radius limit of about 15 km
- Crab pulsar (canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron star) has density 10^15 g/cm^3, pressure 10^29 atm, surface temperature 10^6 K, escape velocity 0.6c, magnetic field 10^12 G
- Nuclear matter saturation density is 2.48 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- Outer crust spans densities from about 10^4 g/cm^3 to 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3
- Neutron drip line reached at about 4 x 10^11 g/cm^3; mass models predict sequence ends with 118Kr (36 protons, 82 neutrons)
- Inner crust extends from neutron-drip density up to about one-third of nuclear matter saturation density
- PREX measurement of neutron-skin thickness in 208Pb: 0.33 +0.16 -0.18 fm (Abrahamyan 2012); future PREX-II aims for error of about 0.06 fm
- Correlation coefficient between neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb and slope of symmetry energy L is r = 0.979 (RocaMaza 2011)
- Analysis of five quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries gives common radius R_NS = 9.1 +1.3 -1.5 km at 90% confidence (Guillot 2013)
- X-ray burster analyses give radii of 8 to 10 km (Ozel 2010), 10 to 13 km (Steiner 2010), or lower limit of 14 km for masses below 2.3 solar masses (Suleimanov 2010)
- Isolated X-ray pulsars have spin periods constrained to at most 12 seconds (Pons 2013)

## Context
This review extends the line of work connecting nuclear physics observables (neutron-skin thickness, symmetry energy slope) to neutron-star structure, building on prior work by Lattimer (2006), Horowitz (2001), and RocaMaza (2011). It discusses how the PREX experiment (Abrahamyan 2012) and mass measurements of exotic nuclei at facilities like ISOLTRAP (Wolf 2013) constrain crust composition, while astronomical observations of massive neutron stars (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) and X-ray bursters (Ozel 2010, Steiner 2010, Suleimanov 2010) constrain the core equation of state. The paper enables future work by identifying tensions between small stellar radii and thick neutron skins that may indicate a phase transition in dense matter, and by highlighting the need for improved measurements from missions like LOFT and GAIA.
