# Fission dynamics at low excitation energy. 2

> The mass asymmetry in the fission of U-236 at low excitation energy is clarified by the analysis of the trajectories obtained by solving the Langevin equations for the shape degrees of freedom. It is demonstrated that the position of the peaks in the mass distribution of fission fragments is determined mainly by the saddle point configuration originating from the shell correction energy. The width of the peaks, on the other hand, results from the shape fluctuations close to the scission point caused by the random force in the Langevin equation. We have found out that the fluctuations between elongated and compact shapes are essential for the fission process. According to our results the fission does not occur with continuous stretching in the prolate direction, similarly to that observed in starch syrup, but is accompanied by the fluctuations between elongated and compact shapes. This picture presents a new viewpoint of fission dynamics and the splitting mechanism.

## Key claim

The mass-asymmetric fission of 236U at low excitation energy (E* = 20 MeV) is explained by a dynamical Langevin approach where the peak positions in the mass distribution are determined by the saddle point configuration (influenced by shell correction energy), while the peak widths result from shape fluctuations near the scission point caused by the random force. The authors claim that fission does not occur via continuous stretching like starch syrup, but rather through fluctuations between elongated and compact shapes, with sudden splitting driven by a strong vibration in the negative delta direction.

## Method

The authors solved three-dimensional Langevin equations for the collective coordinates (elongation z0, mass asymmetry alpha, and deformation delta) using the two-center shell model parameterization with a macroscopic-microscopic potential (finite-range liquid drop model plus Strutinsky shell and pairing corrections). The friction tensor was calculated using the wall-and-window one-body dissipation model, and the inertia tensor used the Werner-Wheeler approximation for hydrodynamical flow. Trajectories were started from the ground state deformation and integrated until the scission point (zero neck radius) for 236U at E* = 20 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of the compound nucleus: E* = 20 MeV for 236U
- Average pre-scission kinetic energy: 7.03 MeV
- Average total kinetic energy of fission fragments: <TKE> = 169.5 MeV
- Experimental TKE range cited: 168.2 to 171 MeV (Vand 1973)
- Neck parameter epsilon fixed at 0.35 (recommended by Yama 1988)
- Shell damping energy Ed = 20 MeV (following Igna 1975)
- Pairing strength constant: average gap parameter tilde-Delta = 12/sqrt(A) (following Nilsson 1969)
- Peak in mass distribution corresponds to mass number A ~ 140

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Langevin calculations (Aritomo 2013) for 234,236U and 240Pu, which reproduced experimental mass distributions and total kinetic energies. The present analysis clarifies the dynamical origin of mass asymmetry by examining individual trajectories, contrasting with static potential energy surface analyses. The results support the existence of three fission modes (standard, super-short, and mass-asymmetric) as suggested by Cahaivta, and the scission configurations resemble those from the statistical scission model of Fong (1956). The work enables future improvements by introducing independent deformation parameters for each fragment and incorporating microscopic transport coefficients from linear response theory (Ivan 1997, Hofm 1997, Yama 1997).
