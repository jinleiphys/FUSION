# Existing and expected manifestations of a new fundamental interaction

> A number of characteristics of the new fundamental interaction are described. The interaction is carried by a massless pseudoscalar boson and extends to at least the electron neutrino, proton, and neutron. A substantiation of the existence of such an interaction is supported by an good agreement between the theoretical and experimental rates of all the five observed processes with solar neutrinos. A bright manifestation of the new interaction is expected in the observation that its contribution to the rate of splitting of a number of light stable nuclei by reactor antineutrinos is approximately six orders of magnitude greater than the contribution of electroweak interaction.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a new "semi-weak" fundamental interaction, carried by a massless pseudoscalar boson coupling to electron neutrinos and nucleons, resolves the solar neutrino problem and predicts that its contribution to the splitting rate of certain light stable nuclei by reactor antineutrinos is approximately six orders of magnitude greater than the electroweak contribution, but this prediction depends on the assumption that the effective number of neutrino-nucleon collisions in the Sun is near 11.5 and on a specific approximation scheme for the collision distribution.

## Method

The author postulates a Yukawa interaction of a massless pseudoscalar boson with electron neutrinos, protons, and neutrons (but not electrons) described by Lagrangian (1). For solar neutrinos, the semi-weak interaction causes spin-flip scattering off nucleons in the Sun, converting left-handed neutrinos to right-handed ones and reducing neutrino energy; the author approximates the resulting neutrino spectrum using an effective collision number \(n_a\) and a linear interpolation between partial rates for even and odd numbers of collisions. For reactor antineutrinos, the cross sections for splitting light nuclei (³He, ⁷Li, ⁹Be, ¹⁹F) via semi-weak and electroweak interactions are estimated using approximate scaling relations from deuteron disintegration cross sections, and the rates are computed for a 2000 MW reactor at 20 m distance.

## Key numbers

- The product of coupling constants is \(\beta \equiv g_{\nu_e ps} g_{Nps} / 4\pi = (3.2 \pm 0.2) \times 10^{-5}\).
- The effective collision number for solar neutrinos is \(n_a = 11.5\) with the ⁸B neutrino flux \(\Phi = 6.01 \times 10^6\ \text{cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}\).
- For the ³⁷Cl → ³⁷Ar process, the theoretical rate at \(n_a = 11.5\) is 2.75 SNU, compared to the experimental value \(2.56 \pm 0.16 \pm 0.16\) SNU.
- For the ⁷¹Ga → ⁷¹Ge process, the theoretical rate at \(n_a = 11.5\) is 62.6 SNU, compared to experimental values \(62.9^{+6.0}_{-5.9}\) SNU and \(65.4^{+3.1}_{-3.0}{}^{+2.6}_{-2.8}\) SNU.
- For the neutral-current deuteron disintegration process \(\nu_e D \rightarrow \nu_e np\), the theoretical effective flux at \(n_a = 11.5\) is \(5.11 \pm 0.37 \times 10^6\ \text{cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}\).
- For ⁷Li splitting by reactor antineutrinos, the semi-weak contribution to the rate is \(2.5 \times 10^3\ \text{per mole per day}\), while the electroweak contribution is 0.023 per mole per day.
- For ⁹Be splitting, the semi-weak contribution is \(3.1 \times 10^3\ \text{per mole per day}\), versus the electroweak contribution of 0.046 per mole per day.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous proposal (Slad, 2015, 2019) of a semi-weak interaction as an alternative to the neutrino oscillation hypothesis (Gribov and Pontecorvo, 1969; Wolfenstein, 1978; Mikheev and Smirnov, 1985) for explaining the solar neutrino problem. The paper provides new numerical calculations for solar neutrino processes using an updated effective collision number and extends the predictions to reactor antineutrino interactions with light nuclei, where the semi-weak contribution is expected to dominate over the electroweak contribution by roughly six orders of magnitude. This enables experimental tests using reactor antineutrinos to distinguish between the semi-weak hypothesis and the standard electroweak model.
