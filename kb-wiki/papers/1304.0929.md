# Proton-rich nucleosynthesis and nuclear physics

> Although the detailed conditions for explosive nucleosynthesis are derived from astrophysical modeling, nuclear physics determines fundamental patterns in abundance yields, not only for equilibrium processes. Focussing on the nu-p- and the gamma-process, general nucleosynthesis features within the range of astrophysical models, but (mostly) independent of details in the modelling, are presented. Remaining uncertainties due to uncertain Q-values and reaction rates are discussed.

## Key claim

Nuclear physics, particularly reaction Q-values and Coulomb barriers, determines the fundamental patterns and limits of nucleosynthesis paths in the nu-p- and gamma-processes, with the specific location of the effective process path remaining remarkably unaffected by variations of astrophysical parameters such as entropy, expansion timescale, and details of the reverse shock.

## Method

The authors used rate field plots to examine the nu-p-process and gamma-process, where each nucleus is shaded by its relative abundance within an isotopic chain or its lifetime against the fastest destruction reaction, with arrows indicating the direction of the fastest net rate per target nucleus. They identified nuclei where remaining experimental uncertainties in Q-values or theoretical uncertainties in predicted reaction rates affect the nucleosynthesis results, marking these as circles and diamonds respectively on the plots.

## Key numbers

- For the nu-p-process, waiting points follow the Z=N line up to Mo, then extend to neutron-richer isotopes between Mo and Sn, providing a strong barrier for efficient production of any elements beyond Sn
- For the gamma-process, (gamma,alpha) deflections dominate in the higher mass range while (gamma,p) reactions dominate below N=82
- The gamma-process was examined at two characteristic temperatures: T=2.3 GK for higher mass range and T=2.8 GK for lighter elements

## Context

This work extends the classical B2FH approach (Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle 1957) by applying nuclear-physics constraints to the nu-p-process and gamma-process in explosive nucleosynthesis. The authors build on prior studies of the nu-p-process (Frohlich et al. 2006a, 2006b; Pruet et al. 2006; Wanajo et al. 2011) and gamma-process (Rauscher 2006; Rapp et al. 2006), showing that nuclear properties determine the window of favorable conditions for these processes while astrophysical hydrodynamics determines how far nucleosynthesis can proceed within that window. The findings enable trajectory-independent assessments of nuclear uncertainties and identify specific nuclei requiring further experimental investigation.
