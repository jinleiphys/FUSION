# X-ray bursts from the Terzan 5 transient IGR J17480-2446: nuclear rather than gravitational

> The 2010 outburst of the transient neutron star low-mass X-ray binary IGR J17480-2446 has exhibited a series of unique X-ray bursts, as well as millihertz (mHz) quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) related to these bursts. It has been recently proposed that these are type-II bursts, powered by the gravitational energy. This implies that the current nuclear-burning based model of mHz QPOs is not correct, and this timing feature cannot be used as a tool to measure the neutron star parameters. We report the analysis of the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer data of IGR J17480-2446 to show that the burst properties of this source are quite different from the properties of the type-II bursts observed from the rapid burster and GRO J1744-28. For example, the inferred ratio (~ 50-90) of the non-burst fluence to burst fluence is consistent with the thermonuclear origin of IGR J17480-2446 bursts, and is significantly different from this ratio (< 4) for type-II bursts. Our results suggest that the bursts and the mHz QPOs from IGR J17480-2446 are powered by the nuclear energy.

# X-ray bursts from the Terzan 5 transient IGR J17480-2446: nuclear rather than gravitational

## Key claim
The bursts from IGR J17480-2446 are powered by nuclear energy (thermonuclear type-I bursts) rather than gravitational energy (type-II bursts), contrary to a recent proposal by Galloway and Zand (2010). The ratio of non-burst fluence to burst fluence (~50-90) is consistent with thermonuclear origin and significantly different from the ratio (<4) observed for known type-II burst sources.

## Method
The authors analyzed Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer Proportional Counter Array data from the 2010 outburst of IGR J17480-2446, totaling ~297 ks of observations. They identified ~400 bursts and performed time-resolved spectroscopy using an absorbed blackbody model for burst spectra and a blackbody plus powerlaw plus Gaussian model for non-burst spectra, with a fixed hydrogen column density of 3.8×10^22 cm^-2. They compared seven burst properties (spectral shape, temperature evolution, fluence ratio, burst shape and duration, post-burst intensity dips, fluence versus interval correlation, and peak flux versus non-burst flux) against the known properties of type-II bursts from the rapid burster and GRO J1744-28.

## Key numbers
- Ratio of non-burst fluence to burst fluence for IGR J17480-2446: ~50-90, consistent with thermonuclear origin (expected ratio ~40)
- Ratio of non-burst fluence to burst fluence for rapid burster: <1 (type-II bursts)
- Ratio of non-burst fluence to burst fluence for GRO J1744-28: <4 (type-II bursts)
- Total observation time: ~297 ks (46 observations between Oct 13 and Nov 19, 2010)
- Fixed hydrogen column density: N_H = 3.8×10^22 cm^-2
- Spectral fitting energy range: 3-15 keV
- Burst blackbody temperature for bolometric correction estimate: 2 keV
- Non-burst flux overestimation from field sources: <0.1% based on Chandra count rate of ~0.06 cps

## Context
This work directly contradicts the proposal by Galloway and Zand (2010) that the bursts from IGR J17480-2446 (except the first one) are type-II bursts powered by gravitational energy, which would invalidate the use of mHz QPOs as a neutron star parameter measurement tool. The authors show that the burst properties differ substantially from the known type-II burst sources (rapid burster and GRO J1744-28) and instead resemble thermonuclear bursts from sources like GS 1826-238. This result supports the model by Heger et al. (2007) that mHz QPOs arise from marginally stable nuclear burning, enabling their use for measuring neutron star parameters. Subsequent papers by Cavecchi et al. (2011) and Linares et al. (2011) supported this conclusion.
