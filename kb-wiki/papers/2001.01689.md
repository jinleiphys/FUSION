# Ab initio calculations of reactor antineutrino fluxes with exact lepton wave functions

> New \textit{ab initio} calculations of the isotopic reactor antineutrino fluxes are provided with exact numerical calculations of the lepton wave functions, assuming all the decay branches are allowed GT transitions. We illustrate that the analytical Fermi function and finite size effect each could have the largest spectral deviation of $\mathcal{O}(10\%)$, whereas the effect of their combination could result in spectral deviations at the level of 5%-10%. Meanwhile, we also find that several forms of the extended charge distributions have negligible effects on the spectral variation. Using the state-of-the-art nuclear databases, compared to usual \textit{ab initio} calculations using the analytical single beta decay spectrum, our new calculation shows sizable but opposite spectral deviations at the level of 2%-4% for the cumulative antineutrino and electron energy spectra which may partially contribute to the observed spectral excess in the high energy antineutrino range. Finally we observe that the {bias} of analytical beta decay spectrum approximation is rather universal for all the four fissionable isotopes.

## Key claim
The paper provides new *ab initio* calculations of reactor antineutrino fluxes using exact numerical lepton wave functions, but only under the assumption that all decay branches are allowed Gamow-Teller (GT) transitions, temporarily neglecting forbidden decays and other spectral corrections. The authors find that the analytical Fermi function and finite-size effect each can cause spectral deviations of order 10%, but their combination partially cancels, yielding deviations of 5%–10% for individual branches and 2%–4% for cumulative spectra.

## Method
The authors solved the Dirac equation numerically for the electron radial wave functions in a nuclear Coulomb potential (using the *Radial* package) for each beta decay branch, assuming allowed GT transitions. They compared these exact numerical spectra to the standard analytical approximation that uses the Fermi function plus the Wilkinson finite-size correction, testing the effects of the neutrino long-wave approximation, series expansions, and different charge distributions (uniform, Gaussian, Fermi). The aggregate antineutrino spectra for the four fissionable isotopes (²³⁵U, ²³⁸U, ²³⁹Pu, ²⁴¹Pu) were then computed by summing over all beta decay branches using cumulative fission yields from ENDF/B-VIII.0 and beta decay data from the Evaluated Nuclear Structure Data File (ENSDF).

## Key numbers
- The analytical Fermi function alone can overestimate spectral strength by up to **20%** for a branch with endpoint energy Q ≈ 8 MeV and nuclear charge Z ≈ 55 (e.g., ¹⁴²Cs).
- The neutrino long-wave approximation (νLA) distorts the spectrum by around **1%** for large-Q branches and **0.1%** for small-Q branches.
- The analytical finite-size correction (Wilkinson) reaches about **10%** for a large-Q, large-Z branch, while the numerical finite-size correction is only about **3%** for the same branch.
- The combined effect of the analytical Fermi function and finite-size correction yields a total spectral variation of **9%** for the branch with Q ≈ 8 MeV and Z ≈ 55.
- For cumulative antineutrino and electron energy spectra of the four fissionable isotopes, the new exact calculation shows spectral deviations of **2%–4%** compared to the usual analytical *ab initio* calculation, with opposite sign for antineutrinos versus electrons.
- Different extended charge distributions (Gaussian, Fermi) cause spectral deviations of at most **2 per thousand** and **0.2 per thousand**, respectively, relative to the uniform distribution.

## Context
This work extends the *ab initio* summation method for reactor antineutrino fluxes (previously used by Mueller (2011), Fallot (2012), Hayes (2013), Dwyer (2014), and others) by replacing the analytical single-beta-decay spectrum with exact numerical lepton wave functions. It directly builds on the authors’ earlier study of finite-size effects (Fang, 2019) and systematically tests the approximations inherent in the standard analytical Fermi function and finite-size corrections. The results suggest that the bias from the analytical approximation is universal across the four fissionable isotopes and may partially explain the observed spectral excess near 5 MeV in reactor antineutrino experiments (the “5 MeV bump”), though the authors note that further refinement is needed, including Pandemonium-free data from Estienne (2019) and corrections for forbidden decays.
