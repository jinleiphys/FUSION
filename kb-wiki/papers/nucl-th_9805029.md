# Resonance trapping and saturation of decay widths

> Resonance trapping appears in open many-particle quantum systems at high level density when the coupling to the continuum of decay channels reaches a critical strength. Here a reorganization of the system takes place and a separation of different time scales appears. We investigate it under the influence of additional weakly coupled channels as well as by taking into account the real part of the coupling term between system and continuum. We observe a saturation of the mean width of the trapped states. Also the decay rates saturate as a function of the coupling strength. The mechanism of the saturation is studied in detail. In any case, the critical region of reorganization is enlarged. When the transmission coefficients for the different channels are different, the width distribution is broadened as compared to a chi_K^2 distribution where K is the number of channels. Resonance trapping takes place before the broad state overlaps regions beyond the extension of the spectrum of the closed system.

## Key claim

Resonance trapping in open many-particle quantum systems leads to a saturation of the mean width of trapped states (rather than a continued decrease) when additional weakly coupled channels are present or when the real part of the coupling to the continuum is included, contrary to the behavior predicted by standard random matrix theory where trapped widths decrease monotonically with increasing coupling strength.

## Method

The authors study the effective non-Hermitian Hamiltonian of an open quantum system, first using a random matrix model (RMM) with Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) background and random coupling vectors, then using the continuum shell model (CSM) for realistic many-body systems. They vary the overall coupling strength α and compute the complex eigenvalues (energies and widths) of the effective Hamiltonian, tracking the mean width of the long-lived (trapped) states as a function of the total coupling parameter κ^tot. The influence of multiple channels with different coupling strengths and the principal-value (real) part of the continuum coupling are investigated separately.

## Key numbers

- For N=300, K=1 in the RMM, the separation point where one broad state separates occurs at κ^tot ≈ 1, where the transmission coefficient τ_c = 1.
- In the RMM with K=4 channels and coupling ratios V_c^2/V_{c=1}^2 = 1, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, the separation points occur at κ^tot = 1.11, 11.1, 111, and 1110 respectively.
- In the RMM with K=4 channels and coupling ratios 1, 0.01, 0.0032, 0.001, the separation points occur at κ^tot = 1.01, 101, 321, and 1010.
- For the two-state model with equal coupling (φ=45°) and θ=0°, the critical coupling is κ_crit = 2, where the two eigenvalues meet in the complex plane and |Φ|^2 diverges.
- The CSM calculations involve 190 resonance states in ^16O with J^π = 1^-; two cases are studied: K=2 channels (^15O_{1/2^-}(n,n)^15O_{1/2^-}) and K=10 channels (^15N_{1/2^-}(p,x)Y).

## Context

This work extends the study of resonance trapping, previously examined in random matrix models by Sokolov and Zelevinsky (1989) and others, to more realistic many-body systems where the widths of trapped states saturate rather than decrease. The saturation mechanism is shown to arise from additional weakly coupled channels (which cause renewed width increases at higher coupling) and from the real part of the continuum coupling (which prevents the effective Hamiltonian from becoming low-rank). The results support the saturation observed in the "bottleneck" picture of transition state theory by Miller (1970) and in continuum shell model calculations by Iskra, Rotter, and collaborators (1993), and they contradict the decreasing-width prediction of standard random matrix models. The findings enable a better understanding of the separation of time scales in nuclear reactions and other open quantum systems at high level density.
