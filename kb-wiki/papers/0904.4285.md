# RPA calculations with Gaussian expansion method

> The Gaussian expansion method (GEM) is extensively applied to the calculations in the random-phase approximation (RPA). We adopt the mass-independent basis-set that has been tested in the mean-field calculations. By comparing the RPA results with those obtained by several other available methods for Ca isotopes, using a density-dependent contact interaction and the Woods-Saxon single-particle states, we confirm that energies, transition strengths and widths of their distribution are described by the GEM bases to good precision, for the $1^-$, $2^+$ and $3^-$ collective states. The GEM is then applied to the self-consistent RPA calculations with the finite-range Gogny D1S interaction. The spurious center-of-mass motion is well separated from the physical states in the $E1$ response, and the energy-weighted sum rules for the isoscalar transitions are fulfilled reasonably well. Properties of low-energy transitions in $^{60}$Ca are argued in some detail.

## Key claim

The Gaussian expansion method (GEM) reproduces RPA results for energies, transition strengths, and distribution widths with good precision compared to continuum RPA for Ca isotopes, with the caveat that the GEM basis set is mass-independent and can be applied to both stable and drip-line nuclei, though the precision for transition strengths is approximately 10% for discrete states.

## Method

The authors applied the Gaussian expansion method (GEM) to random-phase approximation (RPA) calculations of nuclear excitations in Ca-40, Ca-48, and Ca-60. They first tested the method using a Woods-Saxon mean-field potential with a density-dependent contact residual interaction, comparing results against continuum RPA, box-boundary, and quasi-harmonic oscillator basis methods. They then applied the GEM to fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus RPA calculations using the finite-range Gogny D1S interaction.

## Key numbers

- For discrete states in Ca-40, Ca-48, and Ca-60, the GEM provides precision of approximately 0.05 MeV for excitation energies and approximately 10% for transition strengths, with maximum deviation of 0.04 MeV in energy and 10% in strength compared to continuum RPA
- For the lowest 3- state in Ca-40, the GEM gives excitation energy 3.00 MeV and transition strength 8.32 x 10^4 fm^6, compared to continuum RPA values of 2.96 MeV and 8.49 x 10^4 fm^6
- For the lowest 3- state in Ca-60, the GEM gives excitation energy 1.97 MeV and transition strength 4.06 x 10^5 fm^6, compared to continuum RPA values of 1.96 MeV and 4.07 x 10^5 fm^6
- For the integrated strength distribution in Ca-60, the GEM reproduces the continuum RPA result of 4.09 x 10^3 fm^4 for the isoscalar 2+ mode in the 10.0-20.0 MeV range with identical value 4.09 x 10^3 fm^4
- The GEM basis set uses 6 real functions and 6 complex functions with parameters nu_0 = (2.40 fm)^(-2) and b = 1.25, applied irrespectively of angular momentum

## Context

This work extends the Gaussian expansion method previously developed for mean-field calculations by Nakada (2002, 2006, 2008) to the random-phase approximation for nuclear excitations. The method is tested against continuum RPA as developed by Shlomo and Bertsch (1975), which provides exact results for contact interactions. The GEM approach addresses limitations of harmonic oscillator basis sets, which are impractical for describing the broad nucleon distribution and continuum coupling in unstable nuclei, and enables self-consistent RPA calculations with finite-range interactions like the Gogny D1S force, which are difficult to implement with coordinate-space box-boundary methods.
