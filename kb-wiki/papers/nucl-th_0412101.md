# Effect of Three-body Interaction on Phase Transition of Hot Asymmetric Nuclear Matter

> The properties and the isospin dependence of the liquid-gas phase transition in hot asymmetric nuclear matter have been investigated within the framework of the finite temperature Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach extended to include the contribution of a microscopic three-body force. A typical Van der Waals structure has been observed in the calculated isotherms (of pressure) for symmetric nuclear matter implying the presence of the liquid-gas phase transition. The critical temperature of the phase transition is calculated and its dependence on the proton-to-neutron ratio is discussed. It is shown that the three-body force gives a repulsive contribution to the nuclear equation of state and reduces appreciably the critical temperature and the mechanical instable region. At fixed temperature and density the pressure of asymmetric nuclear matter increases monotonically as a function of isospin asymmetry. In addition, it turns out that the domain of mechanical instability for hot asymmetric nuclear matter gradually shrinks with increasing asymmetry and temperature. We have compared our results with the predictions of other theoretical models especially the Dirac Brueckner approach. A possible explanation for the discrepancy between the values of the critical temperature predicted by the present non-relativistic Brueckner calculations including the three-body force and the relativistic Dirac-Brueckner method is given.

## Key claim

The three-body force (TBF) gives a repulsive contribution to the nuclear equation of state and reduces the critical temperature of the liquid-gas phase transition in hot asymmetric nuclear matter, but the reduction depends on which components of the TBF are included; the full TBF yields a critical temperature of about 13 MeV, while including only the 2σ-NN̅ contribution lowers it to about 11 MeV, which is closer to the value of about 10 MeV from relativistic Dirac-Brueckner calculations.

## Method

The authors extended the finite-temperature Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (FTBHF) approach to include a microscopic three-body force (TBF) constructed from the meson-exchange current model, using the Argonne V18 potential as the two-body interaction. The TBF was reduced to a temperature-dependent effective two-body force via averaging over the third nucleon degrees of freedom, and the Bethe-Goldstone equation was solved self-consistently with this effective force. The free energy was obtained from the BHF energy and the mean-field entropy, and the pressure was extracted via numerical differentiation of the free energy with respect to density.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for symmetric nuclear matter without TBF: approximately 16 MeV.
- Critical temperature for symmetric nuclear matter with full TBF: approximately 13 MeV.
- Critical temperature for symmetric nuclear matter with only the 2σ-NN̅ component of TBF: approximately 11 MeV.
- Critical density for symmetric nuclear matter without TBF: approximately 0.08 fm⁻³.
- Critical density for symmetric nuclear matter with TBF: approximately 0.065 fm⁻³.
- Critical asymmetry for disappearance of mechanical instability without TBF: about 0.85.
- Critical asymmetry for disappearance of mechanical instability with TBF: about 0.75.
- Speed of sound in symmetric nuclear matter at ρ = 0.15 fm⁻³: 0.01 (without TBF) and 0.07 (with TBF); at ρ = 0.45 fm⁻³: 0.35 (without TBF) and 0.59 (with TBF).

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approach of Bombaci (1994) by incorporating a microscopic three-body force, following the zero-temperature TBF treatment of Zuo (2002). The results are compared with the relativistic Dirac-Brueckner calculations of Huber (1998), which gave a critical temperature of about 10 MeV, and with the variational method of Friedman (1981) using the Argonne V14 potential plus a phenomenological TBF, which gave about 17.5 MeV. The study enables a more microscopic understanding of the isospin dependence of the liquid-gas phase transition and provides a possible explanation for the discrepancy between non-relativistic BHF plus TBF and relativistic DB predictions for the critical temperature.
