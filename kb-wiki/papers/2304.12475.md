# Ultra-peripheral nuclear collisions

> This article presents a very brief review of the physics of Ultra-Peripheral Collisions (UPC) at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) and other nuclear facilities. I discuss several processes of interest such as electron-position pair production, the anti-hydrogen atom, giant resonances, exotic meson production and parton distribution functions.

## Key claim

Ultra-peripheral collisions (UPCs) are a broadly applicable tool for studying electromagnetic processes in nuclear and particle physics, not limited to relativistic heavy-ion collisions; the equivalent photon number method holds whenever first-order perturbation theory is accurate enough to describe a low-energy UPC process.

## Method

The paper reviews the equivalent photon number (EPN) formalism, where the cross section for an electromagnetic process in a UPC is factorized as an integral over photon energy of the product of the equivalent photon number and the real-photon cross section. For higher-order processes, coupled-channels equations are used to treat multiple photon exchange non-perturbatively, and nuclear absorption can be included via Glauber methods. The formalism is applied to a range of phenomena including giant resonances, lepton pair production, meson production, and parton distribution function extraction.

## Key numbers

- Lorentz boost factor γ ≈ 7000 for p-p collisions and γ ≈ 3000 for Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC.
- The electromagnetic fields in Pb-Pb UPCs exceed the Schwinger critical field (E_Sch = 1.3 × 10^16 V/cm) due to the Z^2 dependence.
- Cross sections for giant resonance excitation are several barns for heavy nuclei at bombarding energies of ~1 GeV/nucleon.
- Electron-positron pair production cross sections at LHC energies are of the order of kilobarns.
- The predicted cross section for Higgs production via UPCs at the LHC was about 1 nb.
- 11 anti-hydrogen atoms were first observed at LEAR/CERN in 1996; 57 events were later identified at FERMILAB.
- Beam loss from electron capture in UPCs was estimated to degrade a large-Z ion beam within 2 hours at the LHC.

## Context

This review extends the author's foundational work on relativistic Coulomb excitation (Bertulani and Baur, 1985) and his PhD thesis (Bertulani and Baur, 1988), which established the EPN method for UPCs. It builds on Fermi's original 1924 idea for atomic ionization and the Weizsäcker-Williams method (1934). The paper enables further searches for physics beyond the Standard Model, including axion-like particles, gravitons, and multiquark states, and consolidates experimental results from the LHC (ATLAS, ALICE, CMS) and GSI on double giant resonances, light-by-light scattering, and nuclear parton distribution functions.
