# Approaching Stable Quark Matter

> The determination of whether the ground state of baryon matter in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the ordinary nucleus or a quark matter state remains a long-standing question in physics. A critical parameter in this investigation is the bag parameter $B$, which quantifies the QCD vacuum energy and can be computed using nonperturbative methods such as Lattice QCD (LQCD). By combining the equation of state derived from perturbative QCD (pQCD) with the bag parameter to fit the LQCD-simulated data for isospin-dense matter, we address the stability of quark matter within the LQCD+pQCD framework. Our findings suggest that the current data imposes an upper bound on $B^{1/4} \lesssim 160$ MeV, approaching a conclusive statement on quark matter stability. Given the lower bound on $B$ from the quark condensate contribution to the vacuum energy, the stable 2-flavor quark matter remains possible, whereas the stable 2+1-flavor quark matter is excluded, assuming complete deconfinement and chiral-symmetry restoration and the reliability of pQCD at baryon chemical potentials around the proton mass. Additionally, we derive more general thermodynamic bounds on the quark matter energy-per-baryon and $B$, which, while weaker, provide complementary insights.

## Key claim

The paper finds that current Lattice QCD data for isospin-dense matter imposes an upper bound on the bag parameter of \(B^{1/4} \lesssim 160\) MeV, which excludes stable 2+1-flavor quark matter but leaves open the possibility of stable 2-flavor quark matter, under the assumptions of complete deconfinement, chiral-symmetry restoration, and the reliability of perturbative QCD at baryon chemical potentials around the proton mass.

## Method

The authors combine the equation of state from perturbative QCD (pQCD) up to \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^2)\) with the bag parameter \(B\) and color-superconducting contributions, then perform a two-parameter fit (renormalization scale parameter \(X\) and bag parameter \(B\)) to Lattice QCD simulation data for isospin-dense matter from the NPLQCD collaboration (Abbott 2024). They analyze the isospin-dense system because it avoids the fermion sign problem that plagues finite-baryon-density Lattice QCD, and they exploit the universality of the bag parameter between baryon-dense and isospin-dense matter. Additionally, they derive complementary thermodynamic bounds using a framework from Komoltsev (2021) that requires only two reference points rather than a full equation of state.

## Key numbers

* Upper bound on the bag parameter from LQCD+pQCD fit: \(B^{1/4} \lesssim 160\) MeV.
* The threshold for stable quark matter is an energy per baryon \(\epsilon/n_B \leq 930\) MeV, the averaged mass per nucleon of iron.
* The pQCD calculations break down for renormalization scale parameter \(X \leq 1.42\) because \(\mu_0 \leq 930\) MeV in that region.
* For the isospin-dense system, LQCD simulations were performed up to \(\mu_I \sim 3.2\) GeV (isospin chemical potential \(\mu \sim 1.6\) GeV) on a system of 6144 pions at \(T \sim \mathcal{O}(10)\) MeV.
* The QCD inequality bound from isospin-dense matter gives \(p_B(\mu_B = 930\ \text{MeV}) \leq (210\ \text{MeV})^4\), while pQCD gives \(p_B(\mu_B = 930\ \text{MeV}) \leq (150\ \text{MeV})^4\) for the \(X\) values considered.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Fujimoto (2024), who matched LQCD isospin-dense data to pQCD+CS calculations to estimate the color-superconducting gap parameter, by instead focusing on the bag parameter to address the long-standing question of quark matter stability originally posed by Bodmer (1971), Lee (1974), and Witten (1984). The analysis uses the most recent LQCD data from the NPLQCD collaboration (Abbott 2024) and the pQCD framework of Kurkela (2009) up to \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^2)\), with partial \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s^3)\) results from Gorda (2023) noted but not yet complete. The results enable future LQCD simulations with higher statistics (projected factor of 10 more ensembles) to potentially reach a conclusive statement on whether 2-flavor quark matter is stable, which would have implications for exotic nuclei and dark matter candidates in the form of quark nuggets.
