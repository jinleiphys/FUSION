# Improved predictions of phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulae with Bayesian optimization approach

> The model inputs play a key role in the performance of the Bayesian optimization approach. In this paper, we investigate the influence of the inputs on the improved predictions of phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulas using an approach combining those original formulas and the Bayesian neural network (BNN). We find that there is no improvement in predictions after the abnormal odd-even staggering effect of 181,183,185Hg is injected into the BNN, while the original phenomenological formulas themselves possess rich physical information or rigid constraints. It indicates the abundance and intensity of physical inputs affect the performance of the Bayesian optimization approach as well as the robustness of the BNN. We further demonstrate that, by ensuring that the number of neurons in the hidden layer is larger than the number of NN inputs, adding hidden layers into the BNN can significantly improve the predictions of nuclear charge radii formulas within the Bayesian optimization approach.

## Key claim

The paper claims that injecting abnormal odd-even staggering data (specifically for \(^{181,183,185}\)Hg) into a Bayesian neural network (BNN) does not improve predictions of nuclear charge radii when the original phenomenological formulas already contain rich physical information or strong constraints; the benefit of the BNN is instead contingent on the abundance and intensity of physical inputs, and on ensuring the number of hidden-layer neurons exceeds the number of inputs.

## Method

The authors combined five phenomenological nuclear charge radius formulas (NP94, Zhang, ELD13, Wang, Sheng) with a feed-forward Bayesian neural network (BNN) to predict residuals \(\delta R_{\rm ch} = R_{\rm exp} - R_{\rm th}\). They trained the BNN on 781 experimental charge radii and validated on 232 newer data points, varying the input features (e.g., isospin parameter \(I\), Casten factor \(P\), pairing parameter \(\delta\), and a binary \(LI\) flag for abnormal Hg isotopes) and the network architecture (single, double, or triple hidden layers with different neuron counts). The root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) and its relative improvement \(\Delta\sigma\) were used to quantify performance.

## Key numbers

* The original NP94 formula has an RMSD of 0.0538 fm on the entire set; after BNN optimization with a single hidden layer and no abnormal data, the RMSD drops to 0.0278 fm (48.2% improvement).
* The original Wang formula has an RMSD of 0.0213 fm; after BNN optimization with inputs \((I, \Delta E, LI)\), the RMSD is 0.0182 fm (14.4% improvement).
* For the NP94-BNN model on the validation set, adding a second hidden layer (30,30 neurons) improves \(\Delta\sigma\) from 32.7% to 59.6%, and a third hidden layer (30,20,10) yields 61.3% improvement.
* The Zhang formula with BNN inputs \((\beta, \eta, \eta^\ast)\) achieves \(\Delta\sigma = 57.2\%\) on the entire set (RMSD 0.0210 fm).
* The Sheng formula with BNN inputs \((I, P, \delta)\) gives \(\Delta\sigma = 37.4\%\) on the entire set (RMSD 0.0195 fm).

## Context

This work extends the BNN-based charge radius optimization of Dong (2022, 2023) and Utama (2016), who used similar feed-forward BNNs with different input features. It specifically tests the robustness of the BNN approach when abnormal odd-even staggering data (e.g., \(^{181,183,185}\)Hg) are included, finding that such data do not universally improve predictions, contrary to the suggestion in Dong (2023). The study also demonstrates that adding hidden layers (with neuron count larger than input count) significantly reduces overfitting in models with sparse physical information (e.g., NP94, ELD13), while models with strong physical constraints (e.g., Wang, Sheng) benefit less from architectural changes. This enables more informed design of BNN inputs and hyperparameters for nuclear charge radius predictions.
