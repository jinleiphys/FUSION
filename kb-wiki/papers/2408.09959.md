# Recent advancements in atomic many-body methods for high-precision studies of isotope shifts

> The development of atomic many-body methods, capable of incorporating electron correlation effects accurately, is required for isotope shift (IS) studies. In combination with precise measurements, such calculations help to extract nuclear charge radii differences, and to probe for signatures of physics beyond the Standard Model of particle physics. We review here a few recently-developed methods in the relativistic many-body perturbation theory (RMBPT) and relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) theory frameworks for calculations of IS factors in the highly charged ions (HCIs), and neutral or singly-charged ions, respectively. The results are presented for a wide range of atomic systems in order to demonstrate the interplay between quantum electrodynamics (QED) and electron correlation effects. In view of this, we start our discussions with the RMBPT calculations for a few HCIs by rigorously treating QED effects; then we outline methods to calculate IS factors in the one-valence atomic systems using two formulations of the RCC approach. Then we present calculations for two valence atomic systems, by employing the Fock-space RCC methods. For completeness, we briefly discuss theoretical input required for the upcoming experiments, their possibilities to probe nuclear properties and implications to fundamental physics studies.

## Key claim

This review argues that recent advances in relativistic many-body methods, particularly relativistic coupled-cluster (RCC) and relativistic many-body perturbation theory (RMBPT), now enable sufficiently accurate isotope shift (IS) calculations in many-electron systems (more than six electrons) to extract nuclear charge radii differences and probe new physics, but the accuracy of these methods still restricts determinations of radii differences in most atomic systems, and QED corrections must be treated rigorously for highly charged ions (HCIs) while approximate model approaches suffice for neutral or singly-charged systems.

## Method

The authors review two complementary theoretical frameworks: RMBPT for HCIs, where electron correlations are treated perturbatively and QED effects are included rigorously, and RCC theory for neutral or singly-charged ions, where electron correlations are treated non-perturbatively to all orders. For one-valence atomic systems, two formulations of the RCC approach are outlined, while two-valence systems are treated using Fock-space RCC methods. The review also presents a recently developed model QED approach for calculating QED contributions to both field shift (FS) and mass shift (MS) factors, which can be interfaced with relativistic program packages that treat electron correlation effects at the CC level.

## Key numbers

* For the nonrelativistic 2s-2p transition in Li I, the calculated NMS factor is approximately 245.10 GHz u from the virial theorem, compared to 245.13 GHz u from a direct calculation (Table III of Ref. [2008-Li] and Table I of Ref. [kozhedub-10-qed]), with a fractional deviation of order 1/M_A ≈ 10^{-4}.
* The model QED approach for FS factors in Li-like HCIs reproduced results of rigorous QED treatment (Zubova 2016) with a deviation of roughly 15%.
* The empirical formula for nuclear mean-squared charge radius is given as ⟨r²⟩ = 0.836 A^{1/3} + 0.57 fm (Ref. [1985-R]).
* For the neutral Al atom (Z=13), QED corrections to both FS and MS were shown to be significant compared to the achievable uncertainty of electronic calculation within the Dirac-Coulomb-Breit approximation.

## Context

This review extends the earlier review by [2012-Co], which emphasized the limitations of atomic theory for interpreting IS measurements and noted that accuracy of then-current methods restricted radii determinations in most atomic systems. The present work focuses on next-generation theoretical methods that have changed this situation, including RCC, RMBPT, and model QED approaches. The review enables future high-precision IS experiments (achieving sub-kHz precision as demonstrated by Man 2019, 2020-CaKP, 2020-YbKP, Hur 2022, Ono 2022, 2023-CaDoret, 2024-free, 2024-YbKP) to extract nuclear charge radii differences, higher-order radial moments ⟨r⁴⟩ (Reinhard 2020), and nuclear dipole polarizability (Pap 2016, Flam 2018), as well as to probe signatures of new physics beyond the Standard Model.
