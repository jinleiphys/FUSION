# Shell-model study of quadrupole collectivity in light tin isotopes

> A realistic shell-model study is performed for neutron-deficient tin isotopes up to mass A=108. All shell-model ingredients, namely two-body matrix elements, single-particle energies, and effective charges for electric quadrupole transition operators, have been calculated by way of the many-body perturbation theory, starting from a low-momentum interaction derived from the high-precision CD-Bonn free nucleon-nucleon potential. The focus has been put on the enhanced quadrupole collectivity of these nuclei, which is testified by the observed large B(E2;0+ -> 2+)s. Our results evidence the crucial role played by the Z=50 cross-shell excitations that need to be taken into account explicitly to obtain a satisfactory theoretical description of light tin isotopes. We find also that a relevant contribution comes from the calculated neutron effective charges, whose magnitudes exceed the standard empirical values. An original double-step procedure has been introduced to reduce effectively the model space in order to overcome the computational problem.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a realistic shell-model calculation, using a low-momentum interaction derived from the CD-Bonn potential and including explicit Z=50 proton cross-shell excitations, can reproduce the enhanced quadrupole collectivity (B(E2) values) of light tin isotopes up to A=106, but only when using theoretically derived effective charges rather than standard empirical values; the agreement deteriorates for A=108.

## Method

The authors start from the high-precision CD-Bonn nucleon-nucleon potential, smooth its high-momentum components using the V_low-k approach with a cutoff momentum of 2.6 fm^-1, and derive an effective Hamiltonian via time-dependent perturbation theory including diagrams up to third order. They first use a neutron-only model space above a 100Sn core, then enlarge the space to include proton excitations from the 1p_1/2 and 0g_9/2 orbitals into the sdgh orbitals, using 88Sr as an inert core. To overcome computational limits, they introduce a double-step procedure: they study the effective single-particle energies of the large Hamiltonian to identify the most relevant orbitals, then apply a unitary transformation to derive a truncated Hamiltonian (H_eff^42) defined in a reduced model space of four proton orbitals (1p_1/2, 0g_9/2, 1d_5/2, 0g_7/2) and two neutron orbitals (1d_5/2, 0g_7/2).

## Key numbers

- The measured B(E2;0+ -> 2+) for 104Sn from GSI (Guastalla 2013) is 1000 ± 400 e^2 fm^4; from NSCL (Bader 2013) it is 1800 ± 370 e^2 fm^4; from RIKEN (Doornenbal 2014) it is 1730 ± 280 e^2 fm^4.
- The calculated B(E2;0+ -> 2+) for 102Sn using H_eff^42 is 1065 e^2 fm^4, close to the value of 1135 e^2 fm^4 obtained with the larger H_eff^45.
- The calculated neutron effective charge for the 0g_7/2 orbital in the 100Sn-core model space is 1.20 e; in the 88Sr-core model space it is 0.94 e.
- The calculated proton effective charge for the 0g_9/2 orbital is 1.62 e; for the 0g_7/2 orbital it is 1.73 e.
- The calculated monopole component of the proton-neutron 0g_9/2,0g_7/2 interaction is -0.479 MeV.
- The energy gap between the neutron 1d_5/2,0g_7/2 subspace and the 2s_1/2,1d_3/2,0h_11/2 orbitals at Z=50 is around 2.4 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier realistic shell-model calculations for light tin isotopes (Engeland 1993, Hjorth-Jensen 1995, Andreozzi 1996) and directly addresses the discrepancy between experimental B(E2) values from recent Coulomb-excitation experiments (Guastalla 2013, Bader 2013, Doornenbal 2014) and previous theoretical predictions (Banu 2005, Vaman 2007, Qi 2012, Back 2013). The authors show that including Z=50 proton cross-shell excitations explicitly, combined with microscopically derived effective charges, can reproduce the observed quadrupole collectivity up to A=106, resolving a puzzle analogous to the case of nickel isotopes where Z=28 cross-shell excitations are required (Bader 2013). The double-step truncation procedure enables calculations in model spaces that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive, and the results provide predictions for 100,102Sn spectroscopic properties to guide future experiments.
