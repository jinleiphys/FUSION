# The Determination of Nuclear Level Densities from Experimental Information -

> A novel Information Theory based method for determining the density of states from prior information is presented. The energy dependence of the density of states is determined from the observed number of states per energy interval and model calculations suggest that the method is sufficiently reliable to calculate the thermal properties of nuclei over a reasonable temperature range.

## Key claim
The paper presents an Information Theory based method for extracting nuclear level densities from experimental data, but the method's reliability for extrapolation to higher energies depends on the assumption that no new physical processes occur beyond the energy range of the prior information.

## Method
The authors minimize the information entropy of the level density relative to a Bloch formula prior, subject to constraints from the observed number of states in discrete energy intervals. They test the method using model calculations where the input density of states is generated with an energy-dependent level density parameter, then fit the resulting Lagrange multipliers with low-order polynomials to extrapolate the density of states beyond the fitted energy region.

## Key numbers
- The energy intervals for the model calculations were fixed at 0.4 MeV, with ten intervals covering the energy range 1–5 MeV.
- The nuclear mass number A was set to 56 in the Bloch formula prior, with k₀ = 8 MeV.
- The extrapolated density of states was calculated up to 20 MeV, and the partition function upper limit E_max varied between 30 MeV and 150 MeV depending on temperature.
- For the constant k(E)=8 case, a constant fit λ′ = 4×10⁻⁵ provided satisfactory results.
- The partition function calculated with the constant λ′ fit was almost perfect up to T = 2 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the Fermi-gas model approach of Bethe (1936–1938) and Bloch (1954), which has known shortcomings including an energy-independent level density parameter and lack of coupling to collective nuclear spectrum components. The method addresses inconsistencies in the multiple inverse Laplace transform approach used by Grossmann and Hahn (1993) for folding nuclear level densities. The technique enables calculation of thermal properties of nuclei over a reasonable temperature range, though the authors caution that extrapolation may fail if higher-energy processes not reflected in lower-energy data occur.
