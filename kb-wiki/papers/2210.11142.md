# Covariant energy density functionals with and without tensor couplings at the Hartree-Bogoliubov level

> Background: The study of additional terms in functionals is relevant to better describe nuclear structure phenomenology. Among these terms, the tensor one is known to impact nuclear structure properties, especially in neutron-rich nuclei. However, its effect has not been studied on the whole nuclear chart yet. Purpose: The impact of terms corresponding to the tensor at the Hartree level, is studied for infinite nuclear matter as well as deformed nuclei, by developing new density-dependent functionals including these terms. In particular, we study in details the improvement such a term can bring to the description of specific nuclear observables. Methods: The framework of covariant energy density functional is used at the Hartree-Bogoliubov level. The free parameters of covariant functionals are optimized by combining Markov-Chain-Monte-Carlo and simplex algorithms. Results: An improvement of the RMS binding energies, spin-orbit splittings and gaps is obtained over the nuclear chart, including axially deformed ones, when including tensors terms. Small modifications of the potential energy surface and densities are also found. In infinite matter, the Dirac mass is shifted to a larger value, in better agreement with experiments. Conclusions: Taking into account additional terms corresponding to the tensor terms in the vector-isoscalar channel at the Hartree level, improves the description of nuclear properties, both in nuclei and in nuclear matter.

# Covariant energy density functionals with and without tensor couplings at the Hartree-Bogoliubov level

## Key claim

Including tensor couplings in the vector-isoscalar channel at the Hartree level improves the description of nuclear properties across the nuclear chart, but the improvement is conditional on the specific choice of parametrization: the selected functional with tensor terms achieves a binding energy RMS of 1.89 MeV over 828 nuclei, while the choice of best parametrization remains subjective because many parameter sets reach identical chi-squared values after thousands of optimization steps.

## Method

The authors developed two new covariant energy density functionals (cEDFs) with density-dependent coupling constants, one without tensor couplings (DD-MEV, 10 free parameters) and one with N-omega and N-rho tensor couplings (DD-MEVT, 12 free parameters), treated at the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) level. The free parameters were optimized by combining Markov-Chain-Monte-Carlo (MCMC) sampling with 200 walkers over 2 x 10^4 steps and simplex (Nelder-Mead) minimization, using constraints from infinite nuclear matter properties and data from 12 spherical nuclei (binding energies, charge radii, neutron-proton radius differences, spin-orbit splittings, and shell gaps), with the final selection based on the lowest binding energy RMS over 828 axially deformed nuclei.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy RMS of 1.89 MeV achieved for the selected DD-MEVT parametrization over 828 nuclei
- Saturation density target: 0.152 fm^-3 with uncertainty 0.03 fm^-3
- Energy per particle of symmetric nuclear matter at equilibrium target: -16.15 MeV with uncertainty 0.15 MeV
- Incompressibility target: 235 MeV with uncertainty 30 MeV
- Symmetry energy coefficient at saturation target: 31.5 MeV with uncertainty 3 MeV
- N-omega tensor coupling constant (Gamma_omega^T) for DD-MEVT: 1.5496
- N-rho tensor coupling constant (Gamma_rho^T) for DD-MEVT: -2.3243
- Sigma meson mass for DD-MEVT: 530.3956 MeV
- Saturation density for DD-MEVT: 0.1516 fm^-3
- MCMC simulations converged to chi-squared approximately 32
- Simplex minimization yielded parametrizations with quasi-constant BE RMS of approximately 2.1 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous covariant density functional studies by including tensor couplings at the Hartree level across the full nuclear chart, which had not been systematically studied before. The approach builds on the historical inclusion of tensor terms in RMF frameworks by Rufa (1988) and subsequent studies of tensor effects on spin-orbit splittings by Ren (1995), Furnstahl (1998), and Liang (2007), as well as on shell gaps by Wang (2013) and surface thickness by Jiang (2005). The new parametrizations enable large-scale calculations of deformed nuclei that were previously precluded by the complexity of full tensor covariant Hartree-Fock approaches, and address the known problem of low Dirac effective mass in relativistic functionals (typically M*/M approximately 0.6 instead of the empirically determined 0.75).
