# Thermodynamics - a valuable approach to multifragmentation?

> Since years it has been vividly debated whether multifragmentation is a thermal or a dynamical process. Recently it has been claimed \cite{toek1,po} that new data allow to decide this question. The conclusion, drawn in these papers, are, however, opposite. Whereas \cite{toek1} states that the behavior of different observables as a function of the fragment multiplicity excludes a thermal origin of the fragments in \cite{po} it has been argued that data show a first order phase transition between a liquid and a gaseous phase. It is the aim of this paper to show that both conclusions are premature. They are based on the salient assumption, that the system is sufficiently large to be susceptible to a canonical description. We will show that this is not the case. A micro canonical approach describes the data as good as dynamical calculations. Hence the quest for the physical origin of multifragmentation continues.

## Key claim

Both claims that multifragmentation data either exclude a thermal origin (Tõke et al.) or show a first-order phase transition (Pochodzalla et al.) are premature because they rely on the assumption that the nuclear system is large enough for a canonical description; a microcanonical approach reproduces the data as well as dynamical calculations, so the question of the physical origin of multifragmentation remains open.

## Method

The authors compare experimental data from the INDRA collaboration for central 50A MeV Xe + Sn collisions with two models: the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM), a microcanonical statistical model, and Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) simulations, which treat the reaction dynamically without assuming thermal equilibrium. Both model results are filtered through the experimental acceptance and compared to data in a restricted angular region (60° ≤ θ_CM ≤ 120°) to avoid contamination from pre-equilibrium emission. The authors also use a simple analytical ideal-gas model to study temperature fluctuations in small subsystems.

## Key numbers

- For central 50A MeV Xe + Sn collisions, the SMM calculation that best reproduces the data uses a freeze-out density of 1/3 ρ₀, source size Z_S = 78, A_S = 186, thermal excitation energy E_thermal = 7A MeV, and collective energy E_coll = 2.2A MeV.
- The relative fluctuation of the total kinetic energy in the observed angular bin is ⟨ΔE/Ē⟩ ≈ 0.2.
- In the analytical ideal-gas model for a typical system with N_N = 50 free nucleons and N_F = 8 fragments, the relative temperature fluctuation in the fragment subsystem is ΔT_F/⟨T_F⟩ = 0.24.
- The temperature difference between the fragment and light-charged-particle subsystems in SMM calculations fluctuates with a standard deviation of about 1.7 MeV around zero.

## Context

This paper extends the debate between Tõke et al. (who argued that observables as a function of fragment multiplicity exclude a thermal origin) and Pochodzalla et al. (who argued that isotope-ratio temperatures show a plateau indicative of a first-order phase transition). The authors show that both conclusions are invalid for finite nuclear systems, because the canonical assumption fails and microcanonical fluctuations are large. The work enables future studies to focus on observables that can genuinely distinguish statistical from dynamical fragmentation mechanisms, rather than relying on the canonical analyses used in prior work.
