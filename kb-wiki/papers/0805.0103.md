# Note on the ring approximation in nuclear matter

> The response function to an external prove is evaluated using the ring approximation in nuclear matter. Contrary to what it is usually assumed, it is shown that the summation of the ring series and the solution of the Dyson's equation are two different approaches. The numerical results exhibit a perceptible difference between both approximations.

## Key claim

The summation of the ring series and the solution of the Dyson equation are two different approximations for the response function in nuclear matter, contrary to the usual assumption that they are equivalent; the difference becomes perceptible only when the polarization insertion has a non-zero imaginary part (i.e., when one particle-hole configuration is on the mass shell).

## Method

The response function to an external probe is evaluated in nuclear matter using two approaches: (1) solving the Dyson equation for the polarization insertion, which yields a geometric series in \(V \Pi^0\); (2) summing the ring diagrams explicitly using Goldstone's rules, keeping only terms where a single particle-hole bubble is on the mass shell at a time. The lowest-order polarization insertion \(\Pi^0\) is computed for three external operators (isoscalar central, isovector spin-longitudinal, and isovector spin-transversal), and a residual interaction with central, pion-exchange, and rho-exchange terms is employed.

## Key numbers

- Momentum transfer chosen for numerical plots: \(q = 400\) MeV/c.
- Fermi momentum used: \(k_F = 1.36\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- Parameters in the residual interaction: \(f = 0.3\), \(g' = 0.5\), \(\Lambda_\pi = 1300\) MeV/c, \(\Lambda_\rho = 1700\) MeV/c.
- Response functions \(S_{C,L,T}\) are plotted in units of MeV\(^{-1}\) fm\(^{-3}\) \(\times 10^{-5}\).

## Context

This work extends the standard ring approximation (the direct part of the Random Phase Approximation, as in Fetter and Walecka (1971)) by showing that the usual identification of the Dyson equation solution with the sum of ring diagrams is incorrect when the imaginary part of the polarization is non-zero. The paper clarifies that the two approaches coincide only when the imaginary part vanishes, as in studies of zero sound (Jezek et al., 1980; Strack, 1983) or core polarization (Osnes, 1993). The result enables a correct treatment of the response function in nuclear matter and suggests further analysis using the Continued Fraction Approximation (Schuck, 1989; Ma, 2008).
