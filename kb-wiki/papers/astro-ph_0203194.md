# Neutrino-Induced Fission and r-Process Nucleosynthesis

> An r-process scenario with fission but no fission cycling is considered to account for the observed abundance patterns of neutron-capture elements in ultra-metal-poor stars. It is proposed that neutrino reactions play a crucial role in inducing the fission of the progenitor nuclei after the r-process freezes out in Type II Supernovae. To facilitate neutrino-induced fission, the proposed r-process scenario is restricted to occur in a low-density environment such as the neutrino-driven wind from the neutron star. Further studies to develop this scenario are emphasized.

# Neutrino-Induced Fission and r-Process Nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

An r-process scenario with fission but no fission cycling can account for the observed abundance patterns of neutron-capture elements in ultra-metal-poor stars, provided that neutrino reactions induce fission of progenitor nuclei after the r-process freezes out in Type II Supernovae, and that the r-process occurs in a low-density environment such as the neutrino-driven wind from the neutron star. The scenario is proposed as an alternative to fission cycling, which the author argues cannot explain the differing Th/Eu ratios observed in the two ultra-metal-poor stars CS 22892-052 and CS 31082-001.

## Method

The author proposes an r-process that produces a freeze-out distribution of progenitor nuclei covering mass numbers 190 to 320 with a peak at A ~ 195, assuming no fission cycling occurs during the r-process itself. After freeze-out, progenitor nuclei with A between 260 and 320 undergo spontaneous fission, while those with A between 190 and 260 require excitation by neutrino reactions (ν_e capture giving ~30 MeV excitation, reactions with ν_μ, ν_τ and their antiparticles giving ~15 MeV) to induce fission during their β-decay chains toward stability. The resulting fission fragments populate mass ranges from 86 to 190, with enhanced yields expected at A ~ 90 and A ~ 132.

## Key numbers

- The lifetime of ²³²Th is τ₂₃₂ = 20.3 Gyr.
- The age difference Δt between CS 31082-001 and CS 22892-052 calculated from their Th/Eu ratios is 19.2 ± 7.0 Gyr, which the author calls "totally unreasonable."
- Typical excitation energy from ν_e capture is ~30 MeV; from reactions with ν_μ, ν_τ and their antiparticles is ~15 MeV.
- The r-process duration in a low-density environment is < 4 s to reach progenitor nuclei with A ~ 320.
- Typical β-decay lifetimes are ~0.1 s for ~10 progenitor nuclei with closed neutron shells and ~0.01 s for those without.
- A fraction f ~ 20-40% of progenitor nuclei with 190 ≤ A < 260 must undergo fission to match observed patterns: f ~ 40% for CS 22892-052 and f ~ 20% for CS 31082-001.
- To achieve f ~ 20-40%, a total number n ~ 0.5-1.6 neutrino reactions per nucleus is required, assuming a fission probability P_f ~ 50%.
- The neutron-to-seed ratio R ~ 130 is required to produce an average mass number ⟨A⟩ ~ 220, with seed mass number A_sd ~ 90.
- Z could increase by ~8 units within ~10 s for progenitor nuclei with 190 ≤ A < 260 during β-decay chains.

## Context

This work extends the classic r-process framework of Burbidge et al. (1957) and Cameron (1957), who first discussed fission in r-process nucleosynthesis, and the detailed fission cycling studies of Seeger, Fowler, and Clayton (1965) and later works such as Freiburghaus, Rosswog, and Thielemann (1999). The author argues that the observed Th/Eu ratios in ultra-metal-poor stars CS 22892-052 (Sneden et al., 2000) and CS 31082-001 (Cayrel et al., 2001; Hill et al., 2001) contradict a robust r-pattern at A ≥ 130 expected from fission cycling, motivating the alternative scenario. The proposed mechanism enables a single r-process source to produce all neutron-capture elements observed in ultra-metal-poor stars, with variations in the number of neutrino reactions per nucleus explaining abundance differences between stars. The scenario could provide one of the diverse r-process sources required by the model of Wasserburg, Busso, and Gallino (1996) to explain meteoritic data on ¹²⁹I and ¹⁸²Hf, and may produce yield patterns similar to the high-frequency supernova component derived by Qian and Wasserburg (2001).
