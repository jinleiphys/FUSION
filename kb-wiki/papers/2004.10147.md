# The time-dependent generator coordinate method in nuclear physics

> The emergence of collective behaviors and the existence of large amplitude motions are both central features in the fields of nuclear structure and reactions. From a theoretical point of view, describing such phenomena requires increasing the complexity of the many-body wavefunction of the system to account for long-range correlations. One of the challenges, when going in this direction, is to keep the approach tractable within our current computational resources while gaining a maximum of predictive power for the phenomenon under study. In the Generator Coordinate Method (GCM), the many-body wave function is a linear superposition of (generally non-orthogonal) many-body states (the generator states) labeled by a few collective coordinates. Such a method has been widely used in structure studies to restore the symmetries broken by single-reference approaches. In the domain of reactions, its time-dependent version (TDGCM) has been developed and applied to predict the dynamics of heavy-ion collisions or fission where the collective fluctuations play an essential role. In this review, we present the recent developments and applications of the TDGCM in nuclear reactions. We recall the formal derivations of the TDGCM and its most common approximate treatment, the Gaussian Overlap Approximation. We also emphasize the Schrödinger Collective-Intrinsic Model (SCIM) variant focused on the inclusion of quasiparticle excitations into the description. Finally, we highlight several exploratory studies related to a TDGCM built on time-dependent generator states.

## Key claim

This review argues that the Time-Dependent Generator Coordinate Method (TDGCM) provides a tractable framework for describing large-amplitude collective nuclear motions such as fission and heavy-ion collisions, but its practical application is hindered by formal difficulties with energy kernels derived from effective Hamiltonians or energy density functionals, particularly regarding exchange terms, symmetry violations, and density-dependent terms.

## Method

The paper reviews the formal derivation of the TDGCM starting from the Griffin-Hill-Wheeler ansatz, where the many-body wave function is a continuous superposition of non-orthogonal generator states labeled by collective coordinates. It describes the mapping to collective wave functions via diagonalization of the norm kernel and the reduction to a time-dependent Schrodinger equation for the collective wave function. The review then examines the Gaussian Overlap Approximation as the most common approximate treatment, the Schrodinger Collective-Intrinsic Model (SCIM) variant for including quasiparticle excitations, and exploratory studies using time-dependent generator states.

## Key numbers

* The typical time for individual nucleon motion inside the nucleus is roughly 10^-22 s, while collective deformation time scales are roughly ten times larger (1 zs = 10^-21 s).
* In the exact TDGCM fission study of 239Pu(n,f), a total of 20212 constrained HFB generator states were obtained using a two-center axial harmonic oscillator basis with 12 shells.
* The norm kernel between generator states was found to be above a threshold of 1.0 x 10^-4 only in a neighborhood of the reference point, due to the large number of nucleons.
* The relative variation of the reduced Hamiltonian was almost constant at 2% around the ground state and 1% for an elongated configuration of 240Pu.

## Context

This review extends the body of work on the Generator Coordinate Method originating from Hill and Wheeler (1953) and Griffin (1957), which has been widely applied in nuclear structure for symmetry restoration and description of low excitation spectra. The review specifically addresses the less-developed time-dependent flavor, highlighting formal difficulties with energy kernels that have been discussed in the context of static GCM by Anguiano (2001), Lacroix (2009), Bender (2009), and others. The work enables future developments in building new energy functionals valid for multireference calculations, as pursued by Bennaceur (2017), and moving toward ab initio treatments as demonstrated by Yao (2019).
