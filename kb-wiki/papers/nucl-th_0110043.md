# Scaling in many-body systems and proton structure function

> The observation of scaling in processes in which a weakly interacting probe delivers large momentum ${\bf q}$ to a many-body system simply reflects the dominance of incoherent scattering off target constituents. While a suitably defined scaling function may provide rich information on the internal dynamics of the target, in general its extraction from the measured cross section requires careful consideration of the nature of the interaction driving the scattering process. The analysis of deep inelastic electron-proton scattering in the target rest frame within standard many-body theory naturally leads to the emergence of a scaling function that, unlike the commonly used structure functions $F_1$ and $F_2$, can be directly identified with the intrinsic proton response.

## Key claim

Scaling in deep inelastic electron-proton scattering can be derived from standard many-body theory in the target rest frame without assuming that proton constituents are massless or on mass shell; the resulting scaling function can be directly identified with the intrinsic proton response, unlike the conventional structure functions \(F_1\) and \(F_2\).

## Method

The author applies the impulse approximation (IA) formalism, originally developed for nonrelativistic many-body systems such as liquid helium and atomic nuclei, to deep inelastic electron-proton scattering. The proton is treated as a bound system of pointlike Dirac particles of mass \(m\), and the cross section is expressed as an incoherent sum over elementary electron-constituent scattering processes weighted by the constituent four-momentum distribution \(P_i(p)\). The scaling variable \(\widetilde y = \nu - |\mathbf{q}|\) is derived from energy conservation in the large-\(|\mathbf{q}|\) limit, and the scaling function \(F(\widetilde y)\) is obtained by dividing the measured structure functions \(W_1\) or \(W_2\) by the corresponding elementary cross section evaluated at a fixed representative constituent momentum.

## Key numbers

* Scaling sets in at momentum transfer \(|\mathbf{q}| > 10\) GeV, as shown by data from SLAC, NMC, and BCDMS rearranged in bins centered at 11, 19, and 27 GeV.
* The elementary cross sections are evaluated with constituent mass \(m = 300\) MeV and minimum binding energy \(B_0 = 200\) MeV.
* Varying \(m\) and \(B_0\) in the range 10–300 MeV does not affect the scaling behavior, but the scaling function is sensitive to the spectator mass \(M_{\mathcal{R}}\); increasing \(M_{\mathcal{R}}\) from 638 to 838 MeV shifts \(F(\widetilde y)\) toward larger \(\widetilde y\) for \(\widetilde y > -0.5\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the impulse-approximation scaling analysis previously applied to neutron scattering off superfluid \(^4\)He (scaling variable \(y = (m/|\mathbf{q}|)(\nu - |\mathbf{q}|^2/2m)\)) and to inclusive electron-nucleus scattering at \(|\mathbf{q}| > 500\) MeV. It contrasts with the standard parton model of deep inelastic scattering (Close, 1979), which requires massless on-shell constituents, and with the Nachtman variable \(\xi\) (Nachtmann, 1973), which is shown to be trivially related to \(\widetilde y\). The approach enables a reconsideration of constituent quark models of the proton, since scaling does not require massless constituents, and it highlights that part of the proton response may lie in the timelike region \(\nu > |\mathbf{q}|\) inaccessible to electron scattering, analogous to missing charge in electron-nucleus scattering (Book, 1990).
