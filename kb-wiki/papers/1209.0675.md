# Thermal Conductivity and Chiral Critical Point in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Background: Quantum Chromodynamics is expected to have a phase transition in the same static universality class as the 3D Ising model and the liquid-gas phase transition. The properties of the equation of state, the transport coefficients, and especially the location of the critical point are under intense theoretical investigation. Some experiments are underway, and many more are planned, at high energy heavy ion accelerators. Purpose: Develop a model of the thermal conductivity, which diverges at the critical point, and use it to study the impact of hydrodynamic fluctuations on observables in high energy heavy ion collisions. Methods: We apply mode coupling theory, together with a previously developed model of the free energy that incorporates the critical exponents and amplitudes, to construct a model of the thermal conductivity in the vicinity of the critical point. The effect of the thermal conductivity on correlation functions in heavy ion collisions is studied in a boost invariant hydrodynamic model via fluctuations, or noise. Results: We find that the closer a thermodynamic trajectory comes to the critical point the greater is the magnitude of the fluctuations in thermodynamic variables and in the 2-particle correlation functions in momentum space. Conclusions: It may be possible to discern the existence of a critical point, its location, and thermodynamic and transport properties near to it in heavy ion collisions using the methods developed here.

## Key claim

The paper constructs a semi-realistic model for the thermal conductivity that diverges at the QCD critical point, and uses it to show that the magnitude of fluctuations in thermodynamic variables and two-particle correlation functions in momentum space increases the closer a thermodynamic trajectory passes to the critical point, potentially allowing experimental detection of the critical point's existence and location.

## Method

The authors apply mode-coupling theory together with a previously developed model of the free energy that incorporates critical exponents and amplitudes to construct a model of the thermal conductivity in the vicinity of the critical point. They then study the effect of this thermal conductivity on correlation functions in heavy ion collisions using a 1+1 dimensional boost-invariant (Bjorken) hydrodynamic model with fluctuations, or noise, while ignoring shear and bulk viscosities to focus on thermal conductivity effects.

## Key numbers

* The critical exponents used are gamma = 1.24 for the baryon number susceptibility and nu = 0.63 for the correlation length, with delta = 4.815, consistent with the 3D Ising model universality class.
* The critical point is placed at T_c = 160 MeV and mu_c = 411.74 MeV, with entropy per baryon at the critical point equal to 19.96.
* Three trajectories are studied with initial chemical potentials mu_i = 420, 620, and 820 MeV, initial temperature T_i = 250 MeV, and initial time tau_i = 0.5 fm/c, giving entropy per baryon values of 37.98, 26.08, and 20.06 respectively.
* The final time when the zero pressure curve is reached is tau_f = 3.04, 3.30, and 3.68 fm/c for the three trajectories respectively.
* The background equation of state uses T_0 = 170 MeV and mu_0 = 1218.48 MeV for N_f = 2 massless quark flavors, with B = 0.8 T_0^2.

## Context

This work extends the Landau theory of fluctuations applied to the QCD critical point by Kapusta (2010), which estimated fluctuation probabilities but did not incorporate space-time evolution. It also builds on the relativistic theory of hydrodynamic fluctuations applied to heavy ion collisions by Kapusta (2012), which worked out an example for matter at zero average baryon density. The paper enables future studies using more sophisticated 3+1 dimensional viscous fluid dynamics to search for critical point signatures in heavy ion experiments at RHIC, FAIR, SHINE, and NICA.
