# A Perspective on Hadron Physics

> The phenomena of confinement and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking are basic to understanding hadron observables. They can be explored using Dyson-Schwinger equations. The existence of a systematic, nonperturbative and symmetry preserving truncation of these equations enables the proof of exact results in QCD, and their illustration using simple but accurate models. We provide a sketch of the material qualitative and quantitative success that has been achieved in the study of pseudoscalar and vector mesons. Efforts are now turning to the study of baryons, which we exemplify via a calculation of nucleon weak and pionic form factors.

## Key claim

The Dyson-Schwinger equation (DSE) framework, combined with a systematic, nonperturbative and symmetry-preserving truncation, can provide both exact results in QCD and quantitative predictions for hadron observables, but the baryon sector remains at a model-building stage approximately at the level of meson studies ten years prior.

## Method

The authors employ the Dyson-Schwinger equations for the quark propagator (gap equation) and the Bethe-Salpeter equation for meson bound states, using a renormalisation-group-improved rainbow-ladder truncation that preserves the one-loop ultraviolet behaviour of perturbative QCD. For baryons, they solve a Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation that treats the nucleon as a dressed-quark and nonpointlike diquark (scalar and axial-vector) correlation, with the dressed-quark propagator fixed from meson analyses and two diquark mass-scale parameters fitted to nucleon and Delta masses. The nucleon electromagnetic interaction is formulated to satisfy a Ward-Takahashi identity, and the axial-vector and pion-nucleon vertices are constructed using Ansätze consistent with the chiral-limit Ward-Takahashi identity and the Goldberger-Treiman relations.

## Key numbers

- The one-parameter renormalisation-group-improved rainbow-ladder model reproduces the one-loop ultraviolet behaviour of perturbative QCD and is applied on the infrared domain \(Q^2 \lesssim 1\) GeV\(^2\) (length-scales \(\gtrsim 0.2\) fm).
- The gluon dressing function \(F(p^2)\) is suppressed at small \(p^2\), with deviations from perturbation theory becoming apparent at \(p^2 \simeq 1\) GeV\(^2\).
- The nucleon Faddeev calculation predicts \(\mu_p G_E^p(Q^2)/G_M^p(Q^2) = 0\) at \(Q^2 \simeq 6.5\) GeV\(^2\) (the zero of \(G_E^p\)).
- The axial form factor \(g_A(Q^2)\) is fitted by a dipole with mass-scale \(m_D^A = 1.69\) GeV; the pseudoscalar form factor \(g_P(Q^2)\) is fitted by a dipole with mass-scale \(m_D^P = 1.77\) GeV.
- The diquark mass-scale parameters (in GeV) are: scalar diquark mass \(m_{0^+} = 0.79\) GeV, axial-vector diquark mass \(m_{1^+} = 0.89\) GeV, with width parameters \(\omega_{0^+} = 0.56\) GeV and \(\omega_{1^+} = 0.63\) GeV, fixed by fitting nucleon and Delta masses (1.18 GeV and 1.33 GeV respectively, offset to allow for pion cloud contributions).
- The chiral-limit vacuum quark condensate is given by \(f_H^0 \rho_H^0(\zeta) = -\langle \bar q q \rangle_\zeta^0\).
- For heavy-light pseudoscalar mesons, \(f_H \propto 1/\sqrt{M_H}\) and \(m_H \propto \hat m_f\) for \(1/\hat m_f \sim 0\).

## Context

This perspective extends the DSE-based approach to hadron physics reviewed in Bastirev, Roberts (2000), Reinhardrev, and Pieterrev, and builds on the systematic truncation scheme of Munczek and Truncscheme. The meson results (pion, rho, and their excitations) are placed in the context of lattice-QCD simulations (Bowman 2002, Latticegluon) and the Gell-Mann–Oakes–Renner relation. The baryon work addresses the discrepancy between Rosenbluth-separation and polarisation-transfer measurements of the proton electromagnetic form factor ratio (Jones 2000, Gayou 2002, Arrington 2002, Qattan 2005), and provides a framework for calculating weak and strong nucleon form factors that can constrain coupled-channel models for medium-energy production reactions. The next step is experimental verification of the predicted zero in \(\mu_p G_E^p/G_M^p\) at \(Q^2 \simeq 6.5\) GeV\(^2\) via an experiment planned at JLab (E04-108, expected to begin around 2008).
