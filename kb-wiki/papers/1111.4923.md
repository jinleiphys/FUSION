# Nuclear correlations and the r-process

> We show that long-range correlations for nuclear masses have a significant effect on the synthesis of heavy elements by the r-process. As calculated by Delaroche et al. [1], these correlations suppress magic number effects associated with minor shells. This impacts the calculated abundances before the third r-process peak (at mass number A~195), where the abundances are low and form a trough. This trough and the position of the third abundance peak are strongly affected by the masses of nuclei in the transition region between deformed and spherical. Based on different astrophysical environments, our results demonstrate that a microscopic theory of nuclear masses including correlations naturally smoothens the separation energies, thus reducing the trough and improving the agreement with observed solar system abundances.

## Key claim

Long-range nuclear correlations, as calculated by Delaroche et al. (2010) using the generator coordinate method, smooth the two-neutron separation energies in the transition region between deformed and spherical nuclei (mass numbers 180-190), which reduces the trough in r-process abundances before the third peak at A≈195; the effect depends on the astrophysical trajectory, with the strongest impact seen in hot wind scenarios where (n,γ)-(γ,n) equilibrium is achieved.

## Method

The authors used the mass table of Delaroche et al. (2010), which contains both Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations and calculations extended by the generator coordinate method to include quadrupole correlations, to compute neutron separation energies for even-even nuclei with odd-A values assigned by interpolation. They then calculated neutron-capture cross sections using a simplified Hauser-Feshbach model and ran r-process nucleosynthesis networks for three astrophysical trajectories: a cold neutrino-driven wind, a hot neutrino-driven wind, and a neutron star merger, using beta-decay rates from Moeller, Pfeiffer, and Kratz (2003).

## Key numbers

- The third r-process peak occurs at mass number A≈195, with the trough in abundances appearing before this peak in the region 180<A<190.
- The abrupt drop in two-neutron separation energy S₂n occurs around A=130 (magic number N=82) and A=195 (magic number N=126).
- Without correlations, S₂n/2 ≈ 3-4 MeV at A≈180 in the transition region between deformed and spherical nuclei.
- The r-process phase begins after temperature drops below T∼0.25 MeV, with initial evolution starting at high temperature ∼1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the mass calculations of Delaroche et al. (2010) and Benrachi et al. (2006, 2008) by applying their correlated mass tables to r-process nucleosynthesis for the first time. It addresses the long-standing problem of the trough before the third r-process peak, which previous work by Chen et al. (1995) had addressed by artificially quenching shell gaps, and which appears in other mass models such as Moeller, Nix et al. (1995), Pearson, Nayak, and Goriely (1996), and Goriely, Chamel, and Pearson (2009). The results motivate further theoretical work to treat beta decays and neutron captures consistently with correlated nuclear masses, and show that the astrophysical environment (hot wind, cold wind, or neutron star merger) strongly modulates the effect of nuclear correlations on final abundances.
