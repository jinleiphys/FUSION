# Extraction of Resonances from Meson-Nucleon Reactions

> We present a pedagogical study of the commonly employed Speed-Plot (SP) and Time-delay (TD) methods for extracting the resonance parameters from the data of two particle coupled-channels reactions. Within several exactly solvable models, it is found that these two methods find poles on different Riemann sheets and are not always valid. We then develop an analytic continuation method for extracting the nucleon resonances within a dynamical coupled-channel formulation of the $πN$ and $γN$ reactions. The main focus is on resolving the complications due to the coupling with the unstable $πΔ, ρN, σN$ channels which decay into $ππN$ states. By using the results from the considered exactly solvable models, explicit numerical procedures are presented and verified. As a first application of the developed analytic continuation method, we present the nucleon resonances in the $S_{11}$ and $S_{31}$ partial waves extracted within a recently developed coupled-channels model of $πN$ reactions. The results from this realistic $πN$ model, which includes $πN$, $ηN$, $πΔ$, $ρN$, and $σN$ channels, also show that the simple pole parametrization of the resonant propagator using the poles extracted from SP and TD methods works poorly.

## Key claim

The Speed-Plot (SP) and Time-Delay (TD) methods, commonly used to extract resonance parameters from meson-nucleon reaction data, are not always valid because they locate poles on different Riemann sheets of the complex energy plane; the authors develop an analytic continuation method within a dynamical coupled-channel framework to resolve complications from coupling with unstable channels such as πΔ, ρN, and σN that decay into ππN states.

## Method

The authors first analyze the SP and TD methods using exactly solvable two-channel Breit-Wigner models, deriving the analytic structure of the S-matrix on the four Riemann sheets (pp, uu, up, pu) and showing that SP and TD find poles on different sheets. They then develop an analytic continuation method within a Hamiltonian formulation of meson-nucleon reactions, establishing explicit numerical procedures to handle singularities from unstable particle channels, and verify the method using the solvable models. Finally, they apply the analytic continuation method to extract nucleon resonances in the S11 and S31 partial waves from a realistic coupled-channels model of πN reactions that includes πN, ηN, πΔ, ρN, and σN channels.

## Key numbers

- The two-channel Breit-Wigner model yields four poles on the complex energy plane, grouped into two pairs (Ea, Ea*) and (Eb, Eb*), with the resonance pole on the uu-sheet and the shadow pole on the up-sheet.
- For the simplified case with equal thresholds (μ1 = μ2, Δ = 0), the resonance pole energy is E_R = M - μγ_+^2 - iγ_+√[μ(2M - μγ_+^2)], where γ_+ = γ_1 + γ_2.
- The shadow pole energy E_S equals the zero of the S-matrix on the pp-sheet: E_S = M - μγ_-^2 - iγ_-√[μ(2M - μγ_-^2)], where γ_- = γ_1 - γ_2.
- The SP method yields real positive solutions for the resonance mass M_R only in the region α < 0.417, where α = μγ_+^2/M_R.

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical analysis of the Speed-Plot method developed by Hoehler (1992, 1993) and the Time-Delay method based on the work of Eisenbud and Wigner (1955), as applied by Kelkar et al. (2003, 2004) to multi-channel problems. The authors use the dynamical coupled-channels model developed by Matsuyama, Sato, and Lee (2007) and the πN model of Jülich et al. (2008) to demonstrate that simple pole parametrization using SP and TD extracted poles works poorly. The developed analytic continuation method enables more rigorous extraction of nucleon resonance parameters from πN and γN reaction data, addressing the complications from unstable particle channels that decay into ππN states.
