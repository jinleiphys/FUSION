# Consistency of particle-particle random-phase approximation and its renormalizations

> The consistency condition is tested within the particle-particle random-phase approximation (RPA), renormalized RPA (RRPA) and the self-consistent RPA (SCRPA) making use of the Richardson model of pairing. The two-particle separation energy is calculated in two ways, namely as the energy of the first addition mode, which adds two particles to a core with N particles, and as the energy of the first removal mode, which removes two particles from the (N+2) - particle system to get back to the same N-particle core. The corresponding transitions generated by the pairing operators are also calculated. It is found that the results obtained in these two ways of calculations are close to each other only at large values of particle number N and/or small interaction strength. At N below 10 for a given value of the interaction strength, the discrepancy between the results obtained in two ways of calculations within the SCRPA is much smaller than those given by the RPA and RRPA.

## Key claim

The particle-particle random-phase approximation (RPA), renormalized RPA (RRPA), and self-consistent RPA (SCRPA) are tested for consistency in the Richardson model of pairing, where consistency requires that the two-particle separation energy calculated as the first addition mode from an N-particle core equals that calculated as the first removal mode from the (N+2)-particle system. The consistency condition is well fulfilled only for sufficiently large particle number N (≥50) or small interaction strength, and the SCRPA is the most consistent approximation for light systems (N≤10).

## Method

The authors applied the RPA, RRPA, and SCRPA to the Richardson model, which consists of Ω doubly-fold equidistant levels interacting via a pairing force with parameter G. They calculated the two-particle separation energy in two ways: as the energy of the first addition mode (adding two particles to an N-particle core) and as the energy of the first removal mode (removing two particles from the (N+2)-particle system), and compared these results at various particle numbers N and interaction strengths G. The corresponding pair-transition probabilities between the ground states of N- and (N+2)-particle systems were also computed.

## Key numbers

- For N=2 and N=10, the SCRPA predicts the smallest difference between S2n(+) and S2n(-), while the RPA gives the largest difference; the consistency condition is perfectly restored at N=100.
- The RPA collapsing point is Gcrit=0.34 MeV for N=10 and Gcrit=0.18 MeV for N=100.
- At N=10 with G=0.33 MeV, the occupation number fh of the hole level just below the Fermi level is approximately 0.7 within the RPA, around 0.9 within the RRPA, and around 0.95 within the SCRPA.
- At N=100 with G=0.17 MeV, the occupation number fh=1 is around 0.85 within the RPA, while all other 49 hole levels have fh larger than 0.95.
- For the degenerate two-level model, the analytical RRPA solutions give S2p(+)(N+2)=√[(ε+G)(ε-G(⟨D⟩N-1))] and S2p(-)(N+2)=√[(ε+G)(ε-G(⟨D⟩N+1))], showing the consistency condition is asymptotically fulfilled only when N≫1.

## Context

This work extends previous tests of the RRPA and SCRPA in the Richardson model (Duk, Dang) and the Lipkin model (Hara, CaDaSa), where the SCRPA solutions were found closest to exact solutions. The study addresses the applicability of RPA to light neutron-rich nuclei, following calculations by Vinh Mau for 12,14C, 10,12Be, and 9,11Li. The results indicate that ground-state correlations beyond RPA are important in light systems and invalidate the quasiboson approximation, suggesting that RPA predictions of two-neutron separation energies in light neutron-rich nuclei are unreliable without renormalization, and that the SCRPA is the most consistent approach for such systems.
