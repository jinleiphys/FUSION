# Anisotropic neutron star crust, solar system mountains, and gravitational waves

> "Mountains" or non-axisymmetric deformations of rotating neutron stars (NS) efficiently radiate gravitational waves (GW). We consider analogies between NS mountains and surface features of solar system bodies. Both NS and moons such as Europa or Enceladus have thin crusts over deep oceans while Mercury has a thin crust over a large metallic core. Thin sheets may wrinkle in universal ways. Europa has linear features, Enceladus has "Tiger" stripes, and Mercury has lobate scarps. NS may have analogous features. The innermost inner core of the Earth is anisotropic with a shear modulus that depends on direction. If NS crust material is also anisotropic this will produce an ellipticity, when the crust is stressed, that grows with spin frequency. This yields a braking index (log derivative of spin down rate assuming only GW spin down) very different from $n=5$ and could explain the maximum spin observed for neutron stars and a possible minimum ellipticity of millisecond pulsars.

# Anisotropic neutron star crust, solar system mountains, and gravitational waves

## Key claim
If neutron star crust material is anisotropic with a shear modulus that depends on direction (analogous to Earth's innermost inner core), this will produce an ellipticity that grows with spin frequency, yielding a braking index very different from n=5 and potentially explaining the maximum spin observed for neutron stars and a possible minimum ellipticity of millisecond pulsars.

## Method
The authors derive an analytic expression for neutron star ellipticity by modeling the crust as a thin hollow cylinder in two dimensions, treating anisotropy as a first-order perturbation to the isotropic elastic stress-strain relations. They use the unperturbed stresses from a rotating star to calculate the change in strain from a small anisotropy parameter ⟨φ⟩, then integrate to obtain the difference in moments of inertia. Finite-element simulations of a three-dimensional neutron star with a polytropic equation of state using FEniCSx software confirm the order-of-magnitude estimate.

## Key numbers
- Maximum ellipticity the crust can support: ε_max ≈ few × 10⁻⁶ (from Ushomirsky et al. formalism)
- Smallest observed upper limit for a rapidly spinning nearby pulsar: ε_min ≈ few × 10⁻⁹ (Abbott 2022)
- Dynamic range: ε_max/ε_min ≈ 1000
- Crust mass fraction: m_cr/M ≈ 10⁻²
- Kepler frequency: Ω_K ≈ 1400 Hz (depending on equation of state)
- Earth's innermost inner core anisotropy: ⟨φ⟩ ≈ 10⁻⁵ when averaged over Earth's total mass
- Predicted ellipticity for ⟨φ⟩ ≈ 10⁻⁵ and (Ω²−Ω₀²)/Ω_K² ≈ 0.1: ε ≈ 10⁻⁸
- Equilibrium spin frequency for accretion torque balance: Ω_eq ≈ 300 Hz (for Ṁ = 10⁻⁸ M⊙ yr⁻¹ and ⟨φ⟩ = 10⁻⁴)
- Braking index in limit Ω ≫ Ω₀: n = 9
- Braking index at Ω = √(5/9) Ω₀: n = 0
- Newtonian Kepler frequency for n=1 polytrope: Ω_K,nr ≈ 2200 Hz

## Context
This work extends the formalism of Ushomirsky et al. (2000) for calculating neutron star crust mountains by introducing crustal anisotropy as a new mechanism for generating ellipticity. It draws analogies between neutron star crusts and solar system bodies with thin crusts over liquid layers (Europa, Enceladus) or metallic cores (Mercury), as well as Earth's anisotropic innermost inner core (Anisotropic 2023). The mechanism could explain the maximum spin observed for neutron stars and a possible minimum ellipticity of millisecond pulsars suggested by observations (2018ApJ...863L..40W), and provides a testable prediction for braking indices that differ from n=5.
