# The equation of state for neutron stars with speed of sound constraints via Bayesian inference

> The parametrized equation of state (EOS) of neutron stars is investigated by Bayesian inference method with various constraints from both nuclear physics and modern astronomical observations. The expansion coefficients correspond to the properties of symmetric nuclear matter and the density dependence of the symmetry energy. The empirical values of the symmetry energy at subsaturation density and the density of crust-core phase transition are considered to limit the low-density behavior of EOS, i.e. $L_{\mathrm{sym}} $, while the speed of sound of neutron star matter and mass-radius observations of millisecond pulsars PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620 are adopted to eliminate the high-order expansion coefficients, such as $Q_{\mathrm{sat}}$ and $Q_{\mathrm{sym}} $. Finally, our analysis reveals that the skewness coefficient $Q_{\mathrm{sat}}$ of the energy per nucleon in symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) exhibits the strongest correlation with the speed of sound, constrained to $Q_{\mathrm{sat}} = -69.50_{-31.93}^{+16.52} \, \mathrm{MeV}$, whose uncertainties are much smaller than those of the experiments of heavy-ion collisions. The symmetry energy parameters are determined as follows: slope $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = 34.32_{-11.85}^{+13.66} \, \mathrm{MeV}$, curvature $K_{\mathrm{sym}} = -58.45_{-89.46}^{+88.47} \, \mathrm{MeV}$, and skewness $Q_{\mathrm{sym}} = 302.28_{-231.89}^{+251.62} \, \mathrm{MeV}$. Additionally, the radii of canonical ($1.4 \, M_{\odot}$) and massive ($2.0 \, M_{\odot}$) neutron stars are predicted as $R_{1.4} = 11.85_{-0.15}^{+0.06} \, \text{km}$ and $R_{2.0} = 11.42_{-0.35}^{+0.23} \, \text{km}$, respectively, with a maximum mass of $M_{\mathrm{max}} = 2.12_{-0.05}^{+0.11} \, M_{\odot}$. The tidal deformability is $Λ_{1.4} = 303.57_{-45.22}^{+47.95}$ at $1.4 \, M_\odot$, which is consistent with the analysis of the GW170817 event.

## Key claim

The paper claims that incorporating speed-of-sound causality constraints alongside NICER mass-radius data and empirical bounds on the subsaturation symmetry energy and crust-core transition density can tightly constrain the high-order expansion coefficients of the neutron star equation of state, particularly the skewness coefficient \(Q_{\mathrm{sat}}\) of symmetric nuclear matter, with uncertainties much smaller than those from heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Method

The authors employed a meta-modeling approach in which the energy per nucleon is expanded as a polynomial function of density up to third order, with coefficients corresponding to saturation properties of symmetric nuclear matter and the density dependence of the symmetry energy. Bayesian inference was performed using uniform priors on four free parameters (\(Q_{\mathrm{sat}}\), \(L_{\mathrm{sym}}\), \(K_{\mathrm{sym}}\), \(Q_{\mathrm{sym}}\)), with the likelihood incorporating NICER mass-radius posteriors for PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620, empirical constraints on the symmetry energy at subsaturation density (26–30 MeV at 0.11 fm\(^{-3}\)), the crust-core transition density (0.05–0.11 fm\(^{-3}\)), and the causality condition that the speed of sound remains subluminal. The TOV equations were solved to obtain mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities, with the crust described by the SLy4 interaction.

## Context

This work extends previous Bayesian analyses of the neutron star equation of state by incorporating speed-of-sound causality constraints and empirical bounds on the subsaturation symmetry energy and crust-core transition density, which were not simultaneously considered in earlier meta-modeling studies by Zhang (2018, 2019, 2020, 2021) and Xie (2019, 2020). The results are consistent with the tidal deformability constraint from the GW170817 event and with NICER measurements of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620. The constrained parameters enable more precise predictions of neutron star radii and maximum mass, and the methodology can be extended to incorporate additional constraints from heavy-ion collision data and perturbative QCD.
