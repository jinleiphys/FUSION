# Causality and stability analysis for the minimal causal spin hydrodynamics

> We perform the linear analysis of causality and stability for a minimal extended spin hydrodynamics up to second order of the gradient expansion. The first order spin hydrodynamics, with a rank-3 spin tensor being antisymmetric for only the last two indices, are proved to be acausal and unstable. We then consider the minimal causal spin hydrodynamics up to second order of the gradient expansion. We derive the necessary causality and stability conditions for this minimal causal spin hydrodynamics. Interestingly, the satisfaction of the stability conditions relies on the equations of state for the spin density and chemical potentials. Moreover, different with the conventional relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics, the stability of the theory seems to be broken at the finite wave-vector when the stability conditions are fulfilled at small and large wave-vector limits. It implies that the behavior in small and large wave-vector limits may be insufficient to determine the stability conditions for spin hydrodynamics in linear mode analysis.

## Key claim

The first-order spin hydrodynamics with a rank-3 spin tensor antisymmetric only in the last two indices is acausal and unstable, and extending the theory to second order in the gradient expansion can remove these problematic modes, but the stability conditions depend on the equations of state for spin density and chemical potentials, and unlike conventional hydrodynamics, stability at small and large wave-vector limits may be insufficient to guarantee stability at finite wave-vector.

## Method

The authors perform a linear mode analysis by considering small perturbations on top of a static, irrotational global equilibrium background with vanishing spin density and spin chemical potential. They derive the linearized conservation equations for energy-momentum and total angular momentum, then solve the resulting dispersion relations in the small and large wave-vector limits to assess causality (requiring bounded and subluminal phase velocity) and stability (requiring positive imaginary part of frequency). The analysis is carried out for both first-order spin hydrodynamics and a minimal second-order extension constructed following the Israel-Stewart approach used in conventional relativistic hydrodynamics.

## Key numbers

- First-order spin hydrodynamics: dispersion relation in the \(k\rightarrow\infty\) limit gives \(\omega = \pm ik\sqrt{2\lambda^{\prime-1}(\gamma^{\prime}+\gamma_{\perp})} + O(k^{0})\), which contains an unstable mode
- First-order spin hydrodynamics: in the \(k\rightarrow\infty\) limit, modes with \(\omega \sim k^{4/3}\) violate the causality condition \(\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}|\omega/k|\) bounded
- First-order spin hydrodynamics: stability conditions from small-\(k\) analysis require \(D_s > 0\), \(\lambda^{\prime} < 0\), and \(D_b < -4c_s\lambda\gamma_{\|}^{-1}|\chi_e^{0x}| \leq 0\), which contradict the entropy principle requiring \(\lambda^{\prime} > 0\)
- First-order spin hydrodynamics: non-hydrodynamic mode from \(\det M_3 = 0\) gives \(\omega = 2iD_s\), requiring \(D_s > 0\) for stability
- First-order spin hydrodynamics: shear mode dispersion \(\omega = i\gamma_{\perp}k^2 + O(k^3)\) in the \(k\rightarrow 0\) limit

## Context

This work extends the causality and stability analysis previously performed for conventional relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics (Hiscock and Lindblom, 1985; Hiscock and Lindblom, 1987; Denicol et al., 2008; Pu et al., 2009) to the case of spin hydrodynamics. It directly addresses the first-order spin hydrodynamics framework developed by Hattori et al. (2019) and Fukushima and Pu (2020), confirming earlier suspicions of acausality and instability raised by Sarwar et al. (2022) and Daher et al. (2022). The minimal causal second-order theory constructed here enables future numerical simulations of spin hydrodynamics that respect causality and stability, and opens the question of whether the standard asymptotic analysis at small and large wave-vectors is sufficient to determine stability for theories with spin degrees of freedom.
