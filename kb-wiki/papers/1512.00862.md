# Model-independent determination of the shear viscosity of a trapped unitary Fermi gas: Application to high temperature data

> Determinations of the shear viscosity of trapped ultracold gases suffer from systematic, uncontrolled uncertainties related to the treatment of the dilute part of the gas cloud. In this work we present an analysis of expansion experiments based on a new method, anisotropic fluid dynamics, that interpolates between Navier-Stokes fluid dynamics at the center of the cloud and ballistic behavior in the dilute corona. We validate the method using a comparison between anisotropic fluid dynamics and numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation. We then apply anisotropic fluid dynamics to the expansion data reported by Cao et al. In the high temperature limit we find $η=0.282(mT)^{3/2}$, which agrees within about 5\% with the theoretical prediction $η=0.269(mT)^{3/2}$.

## Key claim

The paper presents anisotropic fluid dynamics as a method that interpolates between Navier-Stokes fluid dynamics in the dense center of the cloud and ballistic behavior in the dilute corona, enabling a model-independent determination of the shear viscosity of a trapped unitary Fermi gas. The authors find that in the high temperature limit the shear viscosity is η=0.282(mT)^(3/2), which agrees within about 5% with the theoretical prediction of η=0.269(mT)^(3/2), but note that the theoretical uncertainty inherent in the use of anisotropic fluid dynamics is much smaller than that difference.

## Method

The authors implement anisotropic fluid dynamics, treating the diagonal components of the dissipative stress tensor as additional independent fluid dynamical variables, with relaxation time equations that reduce to Navier-Stokes theory in the short mean free path limit and to free streaming in the long mean free path limit. They validate the method by comparing anisotropic fluid dynamics results to numerical solutions of the Boltzmann equation obtained using the test particle method for three different initial temperatures. They then apply the method to reanalyze expansion data from Cao et al. (2010) for a unitary Fermi gas at high temperatures, fitting the aspect ratio evolution using a shear viscosity of the form η=η_0(mT)^(3/2).

## Key numbers

- At T/T_F=0.79, the extracted shear viscosity coefficient is η_0=0.266
- At T/T_F=1.11, the extracted shear viscosity coefficient is η_0=0.302
- At T/T_F=1.54, the extracted shear viscosity coefficient is η_0=0.288
- Combining all data with η=η_0(mT)^(3/2) gives η=0.282(mT)^(3/2), which agrees within about 5% with the theoretical prediction η=0.269(mT)^(3/2)
- Fitting to the more general form η=η_0(mT)^(3/2)(mT/n^(2/3))^a yields a=0.05±0.1, consistent with a=0
- The variance of the extracted η_0 values across a factor of 1.95 in temperature is only 6%

## Context

This work extends prior analyses of shear viscosity in trapped unitary Fermi gases that suffered from uncontrolled systematic uncertainties in treating the dilute corona. Previous approaches include restricting the integration volume to within the surface of last scattering (Schafer 2007), assuming η(x)=n(x)[η(0)/n(0)] (Cao et al. 2010), and restricting the integration volume to the interior of an ellipsoid with a fitted temperature-independent coefficient (Joseph et al. 2014). The method enables model-independent extraction of the shear viscosity and provides a benchmark for reanalyzing data near the superfluid transition, which would require extending the method to superfluid hydrodynamics.
