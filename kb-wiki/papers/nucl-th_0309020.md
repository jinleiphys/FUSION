# Some new perspectives on pairing in nuclei

> Following a brief reminder of how the pairing model can be solved exactly, we describe how this can be used to address two interesting issues in nuclear structure physics. One concerns the mechanism for realizing superconductivity in finite nuclei and the other concerns the role of the nucleon Pauli principle in producing $sd$ dominance in interacting boson models of nuclei.

# Some new perspectives on pairing in nuclei

## Key claim

The electrostatic analogy for the exactly solvable Pairing Model can provide a pictorial representation of how superconductivity arises in finite nuclear systems and also suggests that repulsive pairing between bosons provides a new mechanism for $sd$ dominance in interacting boson models of nuclei, though the latter is presented as a suggestive result rather than a definitive proof.

## Method

The authors apply Richardson's exact solution of the Pairing Model to fermion and boson systems, establishing a classical two-dimensional electrostatic analogy where fixed charges (orbitons) at positions given by single-particle energies interact with free charges (pairons) representing collective pairs. They then use this electrostatic picture to study the evolution of pairon positions as a function of pairing strength $g$ for semi-magic tin isotopes and for boson models with linear and quadratic single-boson spectra.

## Key numbers

- For $^{114}$Sn, the physical pairing strength is roughly $-0.092$ MeV; a transition from isolated atoms to a cluster occurs at $g \approx -0.04$, interpreted as the superconducting transition.
- For $^{116}$Sn, the first merging of the two lowest atoms (containing 7 pairons) occurs at $g \approx -0.06$, and a second transition merging the eighth pairon occurs at $g \approx -0.095$.
- The orbiton charges for the tin calculations are: $d_{5/2}$ at position 0.0 with charge $-1.5$, $g_{7/2}$ at 0.44 with charge $-2.0$, $s_{1/2}$ at 3.80 with charge $-0.5$, $d_{3/2}$ at 4.40 with charge $-1.0$, and $h_{11/2}$ at 5.60 with charge $-3.0$.
- For boson models with 10 bosons in all even-$L$ states up to $L=12$, both linear ($\epsilon_l = l$) and quadratic ($\epsilon_l = l^2$) single-boson spectra show that as $N$ grows, the number of non-$sd$ bosons decreases, and in the thermodynamic limit there are only $s$ and $d$ bosons.

## Context

This work extends the exact solvability of the Pairing Model first demonstrated by Richardson (mid-1960s) and builds on recent applications of that method (Sieber, year not specified). The electrostatic analogy for pairing models (2DCoulomb, year not specified) is used to provide new insight into nuclear superconductivity in finite systems, while the boson pairing analysis offers a new perspective on the foundational assumption of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM, year not specified) that only $s$ and $d$ bosons are needed. The results suggest that repulsive pairing between bosons, reflecting Pauli exchange between nucleon constituents, naturally produces $sd$ dominance without requiring additional truncation assumptions.
