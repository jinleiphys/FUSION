# Neutron Energy Dependence of Delayed Neutron Yields and its Assessments

> Incident neutron energy dependence of delayed neutron yields of uranium and plutonium isotopes is investigated. A summation calculation of decay and fission yield data is employed, and the energy dependence of the latter part is considered in a phenomenological way. Our calculation systematically reproduces the energy dependence of delayed neutron yields by introducing an energy dependence of the most probable charge and the odd-even effect. The calculated fission yields are assessed by comparison with JENDL/FPY-2011, delayed neutron activities, and decay heats. Although the fission yields in this work are optimized to delayed neutron yields, the calculated decay heats are in good agreement with the experimental data. Comparison of the fission yields calculated in this work and JENDL/FPY-2011 gave an important insight for the evaluation of the next JENDL nuclear data.

# Neutron Energy Dependence of Delayed Neutron Yields and its Assessments

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the incident neutron energy dependence of delayed neutron yields for uranium and plutonium isotopes can be systematically reproduced by a summation calculation that introduces phenomenological energy dependence into the most probable charge and the odd-even effect, with parameters determined by least-square fitting to experimental data. However, the authors acknowledge that deviations remain for 233U and 235U around 4 MeV, which they attribute to unknown delayed neutron yields of 232U and 234U as fissioning nuclei from second-chance fission, as well as uncertainties in multi-chance fission cross sections.

## Method
The authors performed a summation calculation of beta-decay and fission yield data, where fission yields were computed using a five-component Gaussian mass yield model (Katakura systematics), the Zp model for fractional independent yields, and a new parametrization of the most probable charge energy dependence (linear plus quadratic terms in incident neutron energy). The odd-even effect was modeled with an energy-dependent Fermi-function form for the pairing factors, and multi-chance fission (first, second, and third chance) was included using JENDL-4.0 fission cross sections. Parameters alpha, beta, gamma, and xi were determined by least-square fitting to available experimental delayed neutron yield data for 233,235,236,238U and 239-242Pu.

## Key numbers
- For 235U thermal fission: delayed neutron yield calculated as 0.01603 (this work) versus experimental values of 0.0158 ± 0.0005 (Keepin 1957) and 0.0159 ± 0.0004 (Saleh 1997)
- For 239Pu thermal fission: delayed neutron yield calculated as 0.00625 (this work) versus experimental value of 0.0061 ± 0.0003 (Keepin 1957)
- For 238U fast fission: delayed neutron yield calculated as 0.04402 (this work) versus experimental value of 0.0448 ± 0.0042 (Keepin 1957)
- For 239Pu fast fission: delayed neutron yield calculated as 0.00658 (this work) versus experimental value of 0.0063 ± 0.0003 (Keepin 1957)
- Root mean square deviation of fission yields for ten most important precursors: 1.22 for 235U thermal, 1.57 for 239Pu thermal, 1.16 for 238U fast, 1.18 for 239Pu fast
- Parameter alpha for 235U: 0.99980; beta: 0.929; gamma: -3.561 × 10^-4; xi: 0.830
- Parameter alpha for 239Pu: 0.99997; beta: -0.116; gamma: 6.816 × 10^-4; xi: 0.481
- Pre-fission neutron energy variation of up to 6 MeV causes at most about 10% variation in delayed neutron yields for 238U and 241Pu

## Context
This work extends the approach of Alexander and Krick (1977), who studied the energy dependence of delayed neutron yields for 235U using Wahl's approach with 47 precursors, by applying a similar method to a broader set of actinides (233,235,236,238U and 239-242Pu) up to 20 MeV incident neutron energy. It also builds on the work of Ohsawa and Miura (2001) and Ohsawa and Fukuda (2007), who considered highly excited primary fission fragments to explain energy dependence between 4 and 7 MeV. The calculated fission yields are compared with the evaluated data in JENDL/FPY-2011 (Katakura 2011), and the results provide guidance for updating delayed neutron evaluations in the next JENDL nuclear data library. The approach enables simultaneous assessment of delayed neutron yields, delayed neutron activities, and decay heats, with the finding that only a few specific precursors (such as 90Br, 94Rb, and 139I for 235U) are responsible for improvements over JENDL/FPY-2011.
