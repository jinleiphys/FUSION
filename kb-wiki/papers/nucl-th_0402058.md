# An Introduction to Nuclear Supersymmetry: a Unification Scheme for Nuclei

> The main ideas behind nuclear supersymmetry are presented, starting from the basic concepts of symmetry and the methods of group theory in physics. We propose new, more stringent experimental tests that probe the supersymmetry classification in nuclei and point out that specific correlations should exist for particle transfer intensities among supersymmetric partners. We also discuss possible ways to generalize these ideas to cases where no dynamical symmetries are present. The combination of these theoretical and experimental studies may play a unifying role in nuclear phenomena.

## Key claim

Nuclear supersymmetry (SUSY) proposes a unified algebraic description of even-even, odd-A, and odd-odd nuclei within the same supermultiplet, but the paper emphasizes that the classification must be tested by new, more stringent experimental probes of particle transfer intensities among supersymmetric partners, and that generalizations are needed for cases where no dynamical symmetries are present.

## Method

The authors present the mathematical framework of group theory, Lie algebras, and dynamical symmetries, then extend it to superalgebras that mix boson and fermion operators. They apply this formalism to the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) and its extensions (IBFM) to classify nuclei into supersymmetric quartets, and propose that transfer reaction intensities between partner nuclei should exhibit specific correlations that can be measured experimentally.

## Key numbers

- The binding energies of the T=3/2 isobaric analog states in 13B, 13C, 13N, and 13O are fitted with parameters a=80.59, b=-2.96, and c=-0.26 MeV using the dynamical symmetry formula E(M_T)=a+bM_T+cM_T^2.
- The Gell-Mann-Okubo mass formula for the SU(3) octet baryons uses parameters a=1111.3, b=-189.6, d=-39.9, e=-3.8, and f=0.9 MeV to reproduce the mass spectrum.

## Context

This work extends the algebraic approach of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) developed by Iachello and collaborators, and the supersymmetric extension first proposed by Iachello (1980) for even-even and odd-A nuclei, later generalized to quartets by Van Isacker et al. The paper enables future experimental tests of supersymmetry classification via transfer reaction correlations, and opens the possibility of applying these ideas to nuclei without exact dynamical symmetries, potentially unifying a broader range of nuclear phenomena.
