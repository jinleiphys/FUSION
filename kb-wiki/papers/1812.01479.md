# Bounds on the Equation of State of Neutron Stars from High Energy Deeply Virtual Exclusive Experiments

> The recent detection of gravitational waves from merging neutron star events has opened a new window on the many unknown aspects of their internal dynamics. A key role in this context is played by the transition from baryon to quark matter described in the neutron star equation of state (EoS). In particular, the binary pulsar observation of heavy neutron stars requires appropriately stiff dense matter in order to counter gravitational collapse, at variance with the predictions of many phenomenological quark models. On the other side, the LIGO observations favor a softer EoS therefore providing a lower bound to the equation stiffness. We introduce a quantum chromodynamics (QCD) description of the neutron star's high baryon density regime where the pressure and energy density distributions are directly obtained from the matrix elements of the QCD energy momentum tensor. Recent ab initio calculations allow us to evaluate the energy-momentum tensor in a model independent way including both quark and gluon degrees of freedom. Our approach is a first effort to replace quark models and effective gluon interactions with a first principles, fully QCD-based description. Most importantly, the QCD energy momentum tensor matrix elements are connected to the Mellin moments of the generalized parton distributions which can be measured in deeply virtual exclusive scattering experiments. As a consequence, we establish a connection between observables from high energy experiments and from the analysis of gravitational wave events. Both can be used to mutually constrain the respective sets of data. In particular, the emerging QCD-based picture is consistent with the GW170817 neutron star merger event once we allow a first-order phase transition from a low-density nuclear matter EoS to the newly-constructed high-density quark-gluon one.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a first-principles QCD-based description of the neutron star equation of state in the high-density regime, derived from the matrix elements of the QCD energy-momentum tensor, and shows that this description is consistent with the GW170817 neutron star merger event only when a first-order phase transition from a low-density nuclear matter EoS to the high-density quark-gluon EoS is allowed.

## Method

The authors evaluate the energy density and pressure distributions for quark and gluon degrees of freedom by Fourier transforming the generalized parton distribution (GPD) moments \(A_2^{q,g}(t)\) and \(C_2^{q,g}(t)\), which are obtained from lattice QCD calculations. They fit the lattice data using a dipole form to enable Fourier transformation over the full \(t\) range, then eliminate the radial coordinate between the energy density and pressure to construct the EoS. The resulting quark-gluon EoS is stitched to a hadronic EoS (MPA1) at a chosen transition pressure to produce a hybrid star mass-radius relation.

## Key numbers

- The quark-gluon EoS is very similar to the strange quark matter EoS SQM3 in the energy density range \(0.5 < \varepsilon < 2\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- The transition pressure for stitching the quark-gluon EoS to the hadronic MPA1 EoS is chosen as 0.2 GeV/fm\(^3\).
- The maximum mass of the resulting hybrid star exceeds \(1.97 M_\odot\), which corresponds to the lower bound on the mass of the pulsar J0348+0432.
- The gluon contribution dominates the EoS, with the quark contribution being largely suppressed.

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron star EoSs beyond phenomenological quark models (such as the MIT-type bag model used in SQM3) by providing a first-principles QCD description using lattice QCD evaluations of the energy-momentum tensor from Shanahan (2018) and Hagler (2007). It establishes a connection between high-energy deeply virtual exclusive scattering experiments (which measure GPDs) and gravitational wave observations (GW170817 from LIGO), enabling mutual constraints between the two sets of data. The approach opens a new framework for understanding hybrid stars and the hadron-to-quark matter transition at zero temperature.
