# Geometric Foundation of Thermo-Statistics, Phase Transitions, Second Law of Thermodynamics, but without Thermodynamic Limit

> A geometric foundation thermo-statistics is presented with the only axiomatic assumption of Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V)=k\ln W. This relates the entropy to the geometric area e^{S(E,N,V)/k} of the manifold of constant energy in the finite-N-body phase space. From the principle, all thermodynamics and especially all phenomena of phase transitions and critical phenomena can unambiguously be identified for even small systems. The topology of the curvature matrix C(E,N) of S(E,N) determines regions of pure phases, regions of phase separation, and (multi-)critical points and lines. Within Boltzmann's principle, Statistical Mechanics becomes a geometric theory addressing the whole ensemble or the manifold of all points in phase space which are consistent with the few macroscopic conserved control parameters. This interpretation leads to a straight derivation of irreversibility and the Second Law of Thermodynamics out of the time-reversible, microscopic, mechanical dynamics. This is all possible without invoking the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or concavity of S(E,N,V). The main obstacle against the Second Law, the conservation of the phase-space volume due to Liouville is overcome by realizing that a macroscopic theory like Thermodynamics cannot distinguish a fractal distribution in phase space from its closure.

## Key claim

The paper claims that Boltzmann's principle \(S(E,N,V)=k\ln W\) alone provides a complete geometric foundation for thermodynamics, including phase transitions and the Second Law, without requiring the thermodynamic limit, extensivity, or concavity of the entropy; the key hedge is that this works because a macroscopic theory like thermodynamics cannot distinguish a fractal distribution in phase space from its closure, thereby overcoming the obstacle of Liouville's theorem.

## Method

The author defines entropy as the logarithm of the geometric area \(e^{S/k}\) of the constant-energy manifold in finite-\(N\)-body phase space, using the micro-canonical ensemble as fundamental. Phase transitions are identified by analyzing the topology of the curvature matrix (Hessian) \(C(E,N)\) of the entropy \(S(E,N)\), where convex intruders (positive largest eigenvalue \(\lambda_1 > 0\)) signal first-order transitions and regions of phase separation. Irreversibility and the Second Law are derived by considering the whole ensemble of points in phase space (not a single trajectory) and noting that the ensemble spreads irreversibly over accessible phase space, with the fractal nature of the distribution being unresolvable by macroscopic measurement.

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters of \(N_0 = 200\) atoms at 1 atm: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 940\) K, latent heat \(q_{lat} = 0.82\) eV.
- For sodium clusters of \(N_0 = 1000\) atoms at 1 atm: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 990\) K, latent heat \(q_{lat} = 0.91\) eV.
- For sodium clusters of \(N_0 = 3000\) atoms at 1 atm: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 1095\) K, latent heat \(q_{lat} = 0.94\) eV.
- For bulk sodium at 1 atm: transition temperature \(T_{tr} = 1156\) K, latent heat \(q_{lat} = 0.923\) eV.
- Surface tension over transition temperature \(\sigma/T_{tr}\) for sodium clusters: 2.75 (\(N_0=200\)), 5.68 (\(N_0=1000\)), 7.07 (\(N_0=3000\)), and bulk value 7.41.
- For the 2-dimensional Potts-3 lattice gas on a \(50 \times 50\) lattice, the known critical point of the ordinary \(q=3\) Potts model in the thermodynamic limit is at \(e_c = 1 + 1/\sqrt{q} \approx 1.58\).

## Context

This work extends the micro-canonical approach of Boltzmann (1877) and Gibbs (1902) to finite, non-extensive systems, contradicting the common claim that the thermodynamic limit (Lieb, 1997; Lebowitz, 1999) is essential for phase transitions and the Second Law. It provides a geometric classification of phase transitions that works for "Small" systems (e.g., nuclei, atomic clusters, globular star clusters) where the canonical ensemble fails due to non-self-averaging, and it enables the study of multi-critical points without sophisticated finite-size scaling, as demonstrated on the Potts lattice gas model.
