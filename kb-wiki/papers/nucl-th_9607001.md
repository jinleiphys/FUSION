# Nuclear Transparency to Intermediate-Energy Protons

> Nuclear transparency in the (e,e'p) reaction for 135 < Tp < 800 MeV is investigated using the distorted wave approximation. Calculations using density-dependent effective interactions are compared with phenomenological optical potentials. Nuclear transparency is well correlated with proton absorption and neutron total cross sections. For Tp < 300 MeV there is considerable sensitivity to the choice of optical model, with the empirical effective interaction providing the best agreement with transparency data. For Tp > 300 MeV there is much less difference between optical models, but the calculations substantially underpredict transparency data and the discrepancy increases with A. The differences between Glauber and optical model calculations are related to their respective definitions of the semi-inclusive cross section. By using a more inclusive summation over final states the Glauber model emphasizes nucleon-nucleon inelasticity, whereas with a more restrictive summation the optical model emphasizes nucleon-nucleus inelasticity; experimental definitions of the semi-inclusive cross section lie between these extremes.

# Nuclear Transparency to Intermediate-Energy Protons

## Key claim

Nuclear transparency in the (e,e'p) reaction for proton kinetic energies between 135 and 800 MeV is well correlated with proton absorption and neutron total cross sections, but distorted wave approximation calculations using either density-dependent effective interactions or phenomenological optical potentials substantially underpredict transparency data for energies above 300 MeV, with the discrepancy increasing with target mass A, and the difference between Glauber and optical model calculations arises from their respective definitions of the semi-inclusive cross section rather than from fundamental physics.

## Method

The authors computed nuclear transparency using a distorted wave approximation (DWA) for the (e,e'p) reaction, evaluating distorted spectral functions from single-particle wave functions (Woods-Saxon for A ≤ 16, Skyrme-Hartree-Fock for heavier nuclei) and optical potentials. They compared calculations using three classes of optical models: nonrelativistic Woods-Saxon potentials (Schwandt, Comfort-Karp), a global Dirac phenomenological potential (Cooper et al., EDAD1), and density-dependent effective interactions including an empirical effective interaction (EEI) and a relativistic IA2 interaction, all folded with nuclear densities using the local density approximation.

## Key numbers

- Energy range investigated: 135 ≤ T_p ≤ 800 MeV for the (e,e'p) reaction
- For T_p ≤ 200 MeV, the empirical effective interaction provides the best agreement with data but remains 5–10% low
- For T_p ≥ 300 MeV, calculations significantly underpredict transparency data and the discrepancy increases with A
- Refractive effects from the real part of the optical potential change transparency by |δT|/T ≤ 7% over the ranges of A and T_p considered
- The difference between parallel and perpendicular transparency definitions satisfies |T_∥ − T_⊥|/T_⊥ ≤ 6% for A ≥ 12 and 135 ≤ T_p ≤ 800 MeV
- Q² range of compared SLAC NE18 data: 1.0 ≤ Q² ≤ 6.8 (GeV/c)²
- The Schwandt potential is fitted for A ≥ 40 and 80 ≤ T_p ≤ 180 MeV
- The global Dirac potential (Cooper et al.) covers A ≥ 12 and 20 ≤ T_p ≤ 1040 MeV

## Context

This work extends the analysis of nuclear transparency from the correlated Glauber approximation used by Pandharipande and Pieper (1992) to analyze the MIT data of Garino et al. (1992), applying instead a distorted wave approximation that is more appropriate at intermediate energies where the Glauber model's linear-trajectory assumption is questionable. The calculations are compared with the MIT data (Garino 1992, Geesaman 1989) at T_p ≈ 180 MeV and with SLAC NE18 data (Makins 1994, O'Neill 1995) at higher energies, where no definitive signal for color transparency was observed. The results establish that for T_p > 300 MeV, existing optical models systematically underpredict transparency, indicating that improved treatments of final-state interactions are needed for reliable analysis of (e,e'p) reactions at CEBAF kinematics, and that the discrepancy between Glauber and optical model calculations stems from different inclusiveness in the definition of the semi-inclusive cross section.
