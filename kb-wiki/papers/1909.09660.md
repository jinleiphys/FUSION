# Constraining level densities through quantitative correlations with cross-section data

> The adopted level densities (LD) for the nuclei produced through different reaction mechanisms significantly impact the calculation of cross sections for the many reaction channels. Common LD models make simplified assumptions regarding the overall behavior of the total LD and the intrinsic spin and parity distributions of the excited states. However, very few experimental constraints are taken into account: LD at neutron separation energy coming from average resonance spacings, whenever they have been previously measured, and the sometimes subjective extrapolation of discrete levels. These, however, constrain the LD only for very specific spins, parities and excitation energies. This work aims to establish additional experimental constraints on LD through quantitative correlations between cross sections and LD. This allows for the fitting and determination of detailed structures in LD. For this we use the microscopic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) LD to associate variations predicted by the model with the structure observed in double-differential spectra at low outgoing neutron energy, which is dominated by the LD input. We also use \nuc{56}{Fe} ($n,p$) as an example cross sections are extremely sensitive to LD. For comparison purposes we also perform calculations with the GC model. With this approach we are able to perform fits of the LD based on actual experimental data, constraining the model and ensuring its consistency. This approach can be particularly useful in extrapolating the LD to nuclei for which high-excited discrete levels and/or resonance spacings are unknown. It also predicts inelastic gamma cross sections that can significantly differ from more standard phenomenological LD.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that microscopic Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) level densities can be quantitatively constrained by fitting them to reproduce experimental neutron-induced cross-section data, specifically for the 56Fe(n,p)56Mn reaction, thereby overcoming the typical deficiency of microscopic models in matching measured cross sections without sacrificing the more realistic spin and parity distributions that these models provide.

## Method

The authors used the reaction code EMPIRE to calculate neutron-induced cross sections on 56Fe, starting from the ENDF/B-VIII.0 evaluation parametrization but replacing the phenomenological Gilbert-Cameron (GC) level densities with microscopic HFB level densities from RIPL-3. They employed the KALMAN fitting code to vary the two HFB level-density parameters (a scaling factor and an excitation-energy shift) for 56Mn, minimizing chi-squared against experimental (n,p) cross-section data. Additionally, they rescaled the 56Fe HFB level density to improve agreement with calculated neutron double-differential spectra at low outgoing neutron energies, which are dominated by the level-density input.

## Key numbers

*   The experimental average s-wave resonance spacing D0 for 55Mn is 413 ± 25 MeV^-1; the fitted GC model gives 1228 MeV^-1, while HFB (RIPL) gives 488 MeV^-1 and GC (RIPL) gives 468 MeV^-1.
*   The experimental average p-wave resonance spacing D1 for 55Mn is 909 ± 83 MeV^-1; the fitted GC model gives 2163 MeV^-1, while HFB (RIPL) gives 1203 MeV^-1 and GC (RIPL) gives 824 MeV^-1.
*   The optimal fit of the 56Mn HFB level-density parameters required increasing one parameter by 45% and the other by 49% relative to the RIPL-3 default values.
*   The cutoff excitation energy for comparing spin distributions with experimental discrete levels was chosen as E_cut = 5.386 MeV, above which levels with undetermined spins or parities appear in RIPL.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Nobre et al. (2018) by showing how microscopic HFB level densities can be constrained using experimental cross-section data, addressing a known deficiency where phenomenological models like Gilbert-Cameron (1965) are often preferred by evaluators due to their fitting flexibility. The method enables improved predictions for nuclei where no experimental resonance spacings (D0 or D1) are available, such as 55Fe, and provides more reliable inelastic gamma cross sections from 56Fe(n,n'gamma) reactions, which differ significantly from those obtained with standard phenomenological level-density models.
