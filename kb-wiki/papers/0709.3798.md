# Neutron star crust beyond the Wigner-Seitz approximation

> For more than three decades, the inner crust of neutron stars, formed of a solid lattice of nuclear clusters coexisting with a gas of electrons and neutrons, has been traditionally studied in the Wigner-Seitz approximation. The validity of this approximation is discussed in the general framework of the band theory of solids, which has been recently applied to the nuclear context. Using this novel approach, it is shown that the unbound neutrons move in the crust as if their mass was increased.

## Key claim

Using band theory of solids applied to the neutron star crust, the unbound neutrons move as if their mass was increased, but this effective mass concept is a macroscopic average over all occupied single-particle states and depends on the shape of the Fermi surface, not simply a single-particle property.

## Method

The author applies the band theory of solids to the inner crust of neutron stars, solving the Hartree-Fock equations with the effective Skyrme nucleon-nucleon interaction (SLy4 force) for a body-centered cubic lattice of nuclear clusters. The single-particle states are obtained by applying Bloch boundary conditions to the Wigner-Seitz cell, and the neutron Fermi surface is computed to determine the macroscopic effective mass of the unbound neutrons.

## Key numbers

- The inner crust extends from approximately 4 × 10^11 g.cm^-3 up to approximately 10^14 g.cm^-3, at which point clusters dissolve into uniform matter.
- At average mass density ρ = 7 × 10^11 g.cm^-3, the crust is composed of a body-centered cubic lattice of zirconium-like clusters (Z=40) with N=160 neutrons per lattice site, of which 70 neutrons are unbound.
- The macroscopic effective mass of unbound neutrons at this density is m_n^* ≈ 4.3 m_n (where m_n is the bare neutron mass).
- The effective mass in the liquid core is slightly lower than the bare mass due to neutron-neutron and neutron-proton interactions.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering self-consistent quantum calculations of Negele and Vautherin (1973), which used the Wigner-Seitz approximation, and addresses the unreliability of that approximation at high densities found by Baldo (2006). The band theory approach enables the study of transport properties that the Wigner-Seitz approximation cannot address, and it opens the possibility that some crust layers might have properties similar to band gap materials, suggesting a new interdisciplinary field between nuclear and solid state physics.
