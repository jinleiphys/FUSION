# Wigner-Wilkins neutron/nucleus scattering kernel quantum-mechanically derived

> We undertake herein to derive the Wigner-Wilkins [W-W] neutron/nucleus scattering kernel, a foundation stone in neutron thermalization theory, on the basis of a self-contained calculation in quantum mechanics. Indeed, a quantum-mechanical derivation of the W-W kernel is available in the literature, cited below, but it is, in our opinion, robbed of conviction by being couched in terms of an excessive generality. Here, by contrast, we proceed along a self-contained route relying on the Fermi pseudopotential and a first-order term in a time-dependent Born approximation series. Our calculations are fully explicit at every step and, in particular, we tackle in its every detail a final integration whose result is merely stated in the available literature. Furthermore, and perhaps the most important point of all, we demonstrate that the quantum-mechanical W-W kernel outcome is identical down to the last iota with its classical antecedent, classical not only by virtue of historical precedence but also by being based on classical Newtonian mechanics.

## Key claim

The paper provides a fully explicit, step-by-step quantum-mechanical derivation of the Wigner-Wilkins neutron/nucleus scattering kernel using the Fermi pseudopotential and first-order time-dependent Born approximation, and demonstrates that the resulting kernel is identical to the classical result derived from Newtonian mechanics, contrary to any expectation that the quantum and classical approaches might yield different outcomes.

## Method

The author starts with a Fermi pseudopotential contact interaction between neutron and nucleus, uses a first-order term in the time-dependent Born approximation series to compute the scattered wavefunction, and extracts the differential cross section via projection onto final momentum eigenstates. After squaring the transition amplitude and handling the Dirac delta functions through Fourier integral representations, the result is averaged over the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of nuclear velocities at temperature T, and the final integration over the cosine of the scattering angle is performed explicitly to reduce the kernel to error function form.

## Key numbers

- The Fermi pseudopotential is given as \(U(\mathbf{r}_n,\mathbf{r}_\nu)=\frac{2\pi\hbar^2 a_{fr}(A+1)}{mA}\delta(\mathbf{r}_n-\mathbf{r}_\nu)\), where \(a_{fr}\) is the free scattering length and \(A\) is the nuclear mass number.
- The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution for nuclear velocities is \(\mathcal{N}\left(\frac{mA}{2\pi kT}\right)^{3/2}e^{-mAv_2^2/2kT}\) with nuclear density \(\mathcal{N}\) and temperature \(T\).
- The final kernel is expressed in terms of parameters \(\theta = (A+1)/2\sqrt{A}\) and \(\zeta = (A-1)/2\sqrt{A}\), with the unnormalized error function \(I(\xi)=\int_0^\xi e^{-t^2}dt\).
- The zero-energy scattering cross section is \(\sigma_0 = 4\pi a_{fr}^2\), independent of the sign of \(a_{fr}\).

## Context

This work extends the classical derivation of the Wigner-Wilkins kernel from Wigner and Wilkins (1944) by providing a fully explicit quantum-mechanical derivation that fills in the missing integration steps merely stated in the prior quantum treatment by other authors. The paper resolves a long-standing gap in the literature by demonstrating that the quantum-mechanical result matches the classical antecedent exactly, and it enables future workers to trust the kernel's validity without relying on the "excessive generality" of earlier quantum derivations.
