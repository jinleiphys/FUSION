# Hyperstatistics

> We propose a general approach, named by us hyperstatistics, to treat complex systems, in which Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics breaks down in domains of the system. Hyperstatistics preserves the concavity of nonadditive $q$-entropy. We obtain analytical closed-form expressions for the here proposed $q$-generalized Boltzmann factor $B_q$ considering uniform, $γ$, Log-normal, F, and the $q$-$γ$ probability distribution functions. Remarkably, for all investigated distribution functions, $B_q$ reduces to a $q$-exponential-type function. To demonstrate the applicability of hyperstatistics, we use a table top experiment of the discharge of a capacitor considering $γ$-distributed relaxation times, the pressure decay over time associated with the pumping of $^4$He lines of a closed cycle cryostat, midrapidity data for $p$-Pb collisions at the LHC, as well as data set for acceleration distribution in turbulent systems. Furthermore, we deduce the power-law-like dielectric response using the $q$-$γ$-distribution function. Our proposal is applicable to systems with inherent non-Boltzmann-Gibbsian statistics in domains of the system.

## Key claim

Hyperstatistics proposes that in complex systems where Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics breaks down within domains, a gamma-distribution of Boltzmann factors within each domain leads naturally to a q-generalized Boltzmann factor \(B_q\) that takes the form of a q-exponential function for all investigated probability distribution functions, not just for a specific choice.

## Method

The authors consider a gamma-distribution of Boltzmann factors within each domain of a complex system, then compute the Laplace transform of this distribution to obtain a q-generalized Boltzmann factor \(B_q(E) = \exp_q(-\langle\beta\rangle E)\). They derive closed-form expressions for \(B_q\) using uniform, gamma, Log-normal, F, and q-gamma probability distribution functions, and apply the resulting q-exponential functions to fit experimental data from capacitor discharge, cryostat pressure decay, LHC p-Pb collisions, and turbulent systems.

## Key numbers

* For the capacitor discharge experiment, the fit yields \(n = 1.020\) and a corresponding \(q\) value (explicit \(q\) value not given in the truncated text), with the voltage decay described by \(V(t) = V_0 \cdot \exp_q(-t/\langle\tau\rangle)\).
* For the midrapidity data from p-Pb collisions at the LHC, the fit yields \(n = 4.87\), indicating a more complex physical scenario than the capacitor discharge.
* The convergence criterion for the q-generalized Gamma function \(\Gamma(n,q)\) requires \(q < (1 + 1/n)\); for integer \(n = 1\) this gives \(q < 2\), while for fractionary \(n = 1/2\) it gives \(q < 3\).

## Context

This work extends the superstatistics framework of Beck and Cohen (2003), which considered Boltzmann-Gibbs-like fluctuations of intensive parameters within cells, by instead considering non-Boltzmann-Gibbsian statistics within each domain of the system. The approach is distinct from the hyperensembles of Crooks and from previous q-analogues of the Gamma function by Andrews and others. Hyperstatistics enables the description of systems with inherent non-Boltzmann-Gibbsian statistics, such as dielectric response in disordered materials, turbulent systems, and high-energy collisions, using a unified q-exponential form.
