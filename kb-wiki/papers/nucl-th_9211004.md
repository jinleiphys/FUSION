# Atomic Parity Nonconservation: Electroweak Parameters and Nuclear Structure

> There have been suggestions to measure atomic parity nonconservation (PNC) along an isotopic chain, by taking ratios of observables in order to cancel complicated atomic structure effects. Precise atomic PNC measurements could make a significant contribution to tests of the Standard Model at the level of one loop radiative corrections. However, the results also depend upon certain features of nuclear structure, such as the spatial distribution of neutrons in the nucleus. To examine the sensitivity to nuclear structure, we consider the case of Pb isotopes using various recent relativistic and non-relativistic nuclear model calculations. Contributions from nucleon internal weak structure are included, but found to be fairly negligible. The spread among present models in predicted sizes of nuclear structure effects may preclude using Pb isotope ratios to test the Standard Model at better than a one percent level, unless there are adequate independent tests of the nuclear models by various alternative strong and electroweak nuclear probes. On the other hand, sufficiently accurate atomic PNC experiments would provide a unique method to measure neutron distributions in heavy nuclei.

## Key claim

Atomic parity nonconservation (PNC) measurements along an isotopic chain of lead could test the Standard Model at the level of one-loop radiative corrections, but the spread among present nuclear models in predicted sizes of nuclear structure effects may preclude using Pb isotope ratios to test the Standard Model at better than a one percent level, unless there are adequate independent tests of the nuclear models by various alternative strong and electroweak nuclear probes.

## Method

The authors calculated the nuclear-structure-dependent corrections to atomic PNC observables in Pb isotopes by evaluating the spatial integrals of neutron and proton density distributions folded with the electron wave function over the nuclear volume, using several recent relativistic and non-relativistic nuclear model calculations (including Skyrme Hartree-Fock, Gogny finite-range interactions, and Relativistic Mean Field models). They also included contributions from nucleon internal weak structure via electroweak form factors, and propagated the nuclear model uncertainties to the extraction of the weak mixing angle from both single-isotope measurements and isotopic ratios.

## Key numbers

- For a single isotope measurement on 208Pb, the internal nucleon structure contributes about 0.002 to the neutron form factor integral q_n and about 0.005 to the proton form factor integral q_p; these contribute at about the 0.2% level in an extraction of the weak nuclear charge.
- A possible strangeness radius of 0.14 fm^2 would contribute about 5 times less than the internal nucleon structure to q_n, and about 0.004 to q_p, affecting a determination of the weak charge at below the 0.1% level in Pb.
- For 208Pb, the simple uniform model gives q_p = 1 - 0.260 (Zα)^2 + O(Zα)^4 and q_n ≈ 1 - (Zα)^2 (0.038 + 0.221 R_n^2/R_p^2) + O(Zα)^4, where Zα ≈ 0.6 for lead.
- The correction to the rms size of the single particle density distribution from two-body correlations is less than 2 × 10^{-4} for heavy nuclei with a_N/a_c ≈ 7.0/0.7 fm.

## Context

This paper extends the authors' earlier work (Pollock, Fortson, and Wilets, 1991) on nuclear structure effects in atomic PNC, and responds to proposals by Dzuba, Flambaum, and Khriplovich (1989) to measure PNC along isotopic chains of Cs, Dy, and Pb to cancel atomic theory uncertainties. The results show that for Pb, nuclear model uncertainties in neutron distributions are the limiting factor for extracting electroweak parameters from isotopic ratios, while for lighter nuclei like Cs (Z=55) these uncertainties can probably be safely ignored compared to atomic theory uncertainties. The work enables future combined analyses of atomic PNC with parity-violating electron-nucleus scattering asymmetries to separate nuclear structure effects from electroweak radiative corrections, and identifies atomic PNC as a unique probe of neutron distributions in heavy nuclei.
