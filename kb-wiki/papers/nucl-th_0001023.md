# Shell Corrections for Finite-Depth Deformed Potentials: Green's Function Oscillator Expansion Method

> Shell corrections of the finite deformed Woods-Saxon potential are calculated using the Green's function method and the generalized Strutinsky smoothing procedure. They are compared with the results of the standard prescription which are affected by the spurious contribution from the unphysical particle gas. In the new method, the shell correction approaches the exact limit provided that the dimension of the single-particle (harmonic oscillator) basis is sufficiently large. For spherical potentials, the present method is faster than the exact one in which the contribution from the particle continuum states is explicitly calculated. For deformed potentials, the Green's function method offers a practical and reliable way of calculating shell corrections for weakly bound nuclei.

## Key claim

The Green's function oscillator expansion method, combined with the generalized Strutinsky smoothing procedure, provides a practical and reliable way to calculate shell corrections for finite-depth deformed potentials, particularly for weakly bound nuclei, by subtracting the spurious contribution of the free particle gas that plagues the standard method.

## Method

The authors diagonalize the finite Woods-Saxon single-particle Hamiltonian and the free Hamiltonian (kinetic energy for neutrons; kinetic plus Coulomb potential for protons) in a large (up to 30 shells) stretched harmonic oscillator basis. The single-particle level density is approximated by the difference of the discrete level densities of the two projected Hamiltonians, which removes the unphysical gas contribution. The smoothed level density is then obtained via the generalized Strutinsky method, which replaces the standard plateau condition with a minimization of the deviation of the smoothed level density from linearity in a chosen energy interval.

## Key numbers

- For the spherical superheavy nucleus Z=114, N=184, the first minimum of the chi-squared deviation occurs at smoothing widths between 1.12 and 1.48 hbar-omega_0 (i.e., between 6.6 MeV and 8.6 MeV).
- For the same nucleus, using the smoothing width at the second minimum instead of the first changes the shell correction by 0.3 MeV.
- For the weakly bound nucleus 154Sn, at N_max=12 the total single-particle energy differs from the exact value by about 8 MeV, while the smoothed single-particle energy is shifted by about 8.5 MeV, yielding a shell correction that differs by only 0.8 MeV from the exact value.
- For the deformed nucleus 120Zr at beta_2=0.6, at least N_max=30 oscillator shells are needed to reach convergence of the total neutron single-particle energy with the standard oscillator frequency parameter eta=1.2.
- For the proton-rich nucleus 180Pb, N_max=19 already reproduces the exact smoothed level density in the whole range of negative energies, and N_max=30 gives excellent agreement.
- The systematic error in shell correction due to the particle continuum can be as large as several MeV at the neutron drip line.

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of Nazarewicz et al. (1994) and Vertse et al. (1998), which applied the macroscopic-microscopic method to nuclei far from beta stability and demonstrated the significant impact of the positive-energy single-particle spectrum. The new Green's function method improves upon the standard Strutinsky smoothing procedure (old method) by removing the spurious contribution of the particle gas, which becomes unreliable near the neutron drip line. The method enables more reliable global calculations of nuclear masses and level densities for spherical and deformed nuclei, and has recently been applied to self-consistent potentials from Hartree-Fock and relativistic mean-field calculations for spherical superheavy nuclei (Kruppa et al., 2000).
