# Coupled cluster approach to nuclear physics

> Using many-body perturbation theory and coupled-cluster theory, we calculate the ground-state energy of He-4 and O-16. We perform these calculations using a no-core G-matrix interaction derived from a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential. Our calculations employ up to two-particle-two-hole coupled-cluster amplitudes.

## Key claim

Using a no-core G-matrix interaction derived from a realistic nucleon-nucleon potential, the authors calculate ground-state energies of He-4 and O-16 with coupled-cluster theory truncated at the two-particle-two-hole level, but note that the results depend on the chosen harmonic oscillator parameter, the number of major oscillator shells, and the starting energy used in the G-matrix computation.

## Method

The authors first compute a G-matrix from the Idaho-A nucleon-nucleon interaction using an exclusion operator that defines a model space of up to eight major harmonic oscillator shells. They then define an effective two-body interaction by averaging the G-matrix at different starting energies determined from single-hole energies computed via Eq. (4). Finally, they solve the coupled-cluster equations including single and double excitation amplitudes (CCSD) for the ground states of He-4 and O-16, adding a center-of-mass correction term to the Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- For He-4 at N=8 major shells, third-order perturbation theory gives an optimal binding energy of E = -20.830 MeV at hbar-omega = 13.3 MeV (experimental value is E = -28 MeV)
- For O-16 at N=8 major shells, third-order perturbation theory gives an optimal binding energy per particle of E/A = -7.120 MeV at hbar-omega = 13.6 MeV (experimental value is E/A = -7.98 MeV)
- For O-16 at N=7 major shells, third-order perturbation theory gives E/A = -7.155 MeV at hbar-omega = 12.9 MeV
- For O-16 at N=6 major shells, third-order perturbation theory gives E/A = -7.416 MeV at hbar-omega = 12.6 MeV
- The Idaho-A nucleon-nucleon interaction yields a deuteron D-state probability of 4.17% and a triton binding energy of 8.14 MeV; Idaho-B gives 4.94% and 8.02 MeV respectively

## Context

This work extends the coupled-cluster approach to nuclear physics, which originated with Coester and Kummel (1958, 1960) and was applied to finite nuclei by Kummel (1978). It builds on recent applications by Mihaila and Heisenberg (1999, 2000) for O-16, but differs by using a G-matrix renormalization before the coupled-cluster calculation. The paper establishes a foundation for future systematic coupled-cluster calculations of nuclear properties, aiming to eventually include three-nucleon interactions and larger model spaces.
