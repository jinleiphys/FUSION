# The Sound Edge of the Quenching Jets

> When quenching jets deposit certain amount of energy and momentum into ambient matter, part of it propagates in the form of shocks/sounds. The "sound surface", separating disturbed and undisturbed parts of the fireball, makes what we call the sound edge of jets. In this work we semi-analytically study its shape, in various geometries. We further argue that since hadrons with in the kinematical range of $p_\perp\sim 2\, GeV$ originate mostly from the "rim" of the fireball, near the maximum of the radial flow at the freezeout surface, only the intersection of the "sound surface" with this "rim" would be observable. The resulting "jet edge" has a form of extra matter at the elliptic curve, in $Δϕ, Δη$ coordinates, with radius $|Δϕ| \sim |Δη|\sim 1$. In the case of large energy/momentum deposition $\sim 100 \, GeV$ we argue that the event should be considered as two sub-events, with interior of the "sound surface" having modified radial and directed flow. We further argue that in the kinematical range of $p_\perp\sim 3\, GeV$ the effect of that can be large enough to be seen on event-by-event basis. If so, this effect has a potential to become a valuable tool to address geometry of jet production and quenching.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observable signal from jet-induced sound perturbations in the quark-gluon plasma is not the full Mach cone, but only its intersection with the "rim" of the fireball, the narrow region near the maximum radial flow where high-transverse-momentum hadrons (p⊥ ∼ 2 GeV) are produced. This intersection produces an elliptic "jet edge" in Δφ, Δη coordinates, but the authors hedge that this signal is only clearly visible when the associate hadron momentum is chosen at the upper edge of the hydro-dominated region (p⊥ ∼ 2–3 GeV), and that for very large energy deposition (∼100 GeV) the effect might be large enough to be seen on an event-by-event basis.

## Method

The authors use linearized perturbations on top of the analytic Gubser flow solution for central heavy-ion collisions, convolving a pre-computed Green function (from Staig (2011)) with the jet energy deposition dE/dx along the jet path. They include both the BDMPS energy-loss prescription (dE/dx ∼ x) and a realistic shear viscosity (4πηshear/s = 2), and solve the separable equations in comoving coordinates (ρ, θ, φ, η) by summing over 20 values each of the angular momentum l and longitudinal momentum k. The temperature perturbation δT/T is evaluated at fixed proper time τ = 9.55 fm as an approximation to the freezeout surface, and the intersection of the perturbation with the fireball rim (r = 9.1 fm) is identified as the observable "jet edge."

## Key numbers

- The fireball rim radius is r = 9.1 fm, the point of maximal radial flow where the Cooper-Fry integrand peaks for p⊥ = 2.4 GeV and Tf = 0.12 GeV.
- The width of the contributing "spot" in azimuthal angle φ is √⟨(φ − φp)²⟩ ≈ 1/5 rad, derived from the large parameter A = p⊥/Tf sinh(κ) ≈ 26 with maximal flow rapidity κ ≈ 1.1.
- The Mach cone angle for the time-averaged sound velocity ⟨cs⟩ ≈ 0.4 is θM = arccos(⟨cs⟩/vjet) ≈ 1.1 rad, matching the observed double-peak positions at Δφ = ±2.0 rad in earlier data.
- For a large energy deposition of E⊥ ∼ 100 GeV, the estimated extra particle number is Nextra ∼ 200, giving a multiplicity increase of about 8% and a homogeneous temperature increase δT/T ∼ 2.7%.
- The mid-rapidity charged multiplicity used for the estimate is dNch/dη ≈ 1584 (ALICE value), scaled by 3/2 to include neutrals gives dN/dη ∼ 2400.
- The model parameter 1/q = 4.3 fm approximates heavy nuclei (Au, Pb) at RHIC and LHC.
- The freezeout is defined by the isotherm T = 120 MeV, with the dimensionless temperature parameter T̂0 = 10.1 for LHC conditions.

## Context

This work extends the earlier Mach cone studies by Casalderrey-Solana (2004) and the geometric acoustics approach of Khachatryan and Shuryak (2011), while incorporating the Gubser flow solution (Gubser, 2010) and its perturbation formalism (Gubser and Yarom, 2010) that the authors previously used for initial-state fluctuations (Staig, 2011). The paper clarifies that the double peaks at Δφ = ±2 rad observed by PHENIX and STAR at p⊥ ∼ 3 GeV are not Mach cones but rather triangular flow effects related to the "sound horizon" (Staig, 2010). The key new simplification is that for p⊥ ∼ 2 GeV, the Cooper-Fry integral localizes to the fireball rim, so only the intersection of the sound surface with this rim is observable, enabling a clean geometric prediction for the "jet edge" shape in Δφ, Δη coordinates. The work also opens the possibility of using very asymmetric dijets with ∼100 GeV energy deposition (observed at LHC) as a tool to study jet production geometry on an event-by-event basis.
