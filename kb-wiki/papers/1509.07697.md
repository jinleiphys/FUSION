# Matching effective few-nucleon theories to QCD

> The emergence of complex macroscopic phenomena from a small set of parameters and microscopic concepts demonstrates the power and beauty of physical theories. A theory which relates the wealth of data and peculiarities found in nuclei to the small number of parameters and symmetries of quantum chromodynamics is by that standard of exceptional beauty. Decade-long research on computational physics and on effective field theories facilitate the assessment of the presumption that quark masses and strong and electromagnetic coupling constants suffice to parameterize the nuclear chart. By presenting the current status of that enterprise, this article touches the methodology of predicting nuclei by simulating the constituting quarks and gluons and the development of effective field theories as appropriate representations of the fundamental theory. While the nuclear spectra and electromagnetic responses analyzed computationally so far with lattice QCD are in close resemblance to those which intrigued experimentalists a century ago, they also test the theoretical understanding which was unavailable to guide the nuclear pioneers but developed since then. This understanding is shown to be deficient in terms of correlations amongst nuclear observables and their sensitivity to fundamental parameters. By reviewing the transition from one effective field theory to another, from QCD to pionful chiral theories to pionless and eventually to cluster theories, we identify some of those deficiencies and conceptual problems awaiting a solution before QCD can be identified as the high-energy theory from which the nuclear landscape emerges.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while lattice QCD calculations of light nuclei at unphysically large pion masses (510 and 806 MeV) now exist, the program to match these to effective field theories and ultimately to QCD parameters is incomplete because chiral effective field theory lacks a consistent power-counting scheme and its theoretical uncertainty cannot be properly quantified, preventing a rigorous propagation of QCD parameter dependence to nuclear observables.

## Method

The paper reviews the methodology of matching effective few-nucleon theories across three levels: lattice QCD to chiral effective field theory (chiEFT), chiEFT to pionless effective field theory (EFT(slashed-pi)), and pionless theory to cluster EFTs. It examines how low-energy constants in the chiral Lagrangian are determined by matching two-nucleon amplitudes at a given pion mass, then used to extrapolate the pion-mass dependence of observables such as scattering lengths, binding energies, and effective ranges. The review compares results from different power-counting schemes (Weinberg, Kaplan-Savage-Wise, Beane-Bedaque-Savage-van-Kolck, and Beane-Kaplan-Vuorinen) applied to systems up to A=4.

## Key numbers

* The deuteron binding energy was found to vanish at a critical pion mass of approximately 100 MeV in one counting scheme (Beane et al., 2001), while another scheme (Epelbaum et al., 2002) found the deuteron more strongly bound in the chiral limit than in nature.
* The neutron-proton scattering lengths in both singlet and triplet S-wave channels diverge at a critical pion mass of approximately 198 MeV, as found by Epelbaum et al. (2006) using chiEFT matched to pionless EFT.
* Lattice QCD data exist at pion masses of 300 MeV (Yamazaki et al., 2015), 450 MeV (Orginos et al., 2015), 510 MeV (Yamazaki et al., 2012), and 806 MeV (Beane et al., 2012) for few-nucleon systems up to A=4.
* The asymptotic ratio of binding energies at the deuteron-neutron accumulation point was found to be approximately 515 in the three-nucleon Efimov spectrum (Epelbaum et al., 2006), with the triton ground state stable at 4 +/- 2 MeV in the pion-mass interval between 190 and 210 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting lattice QCD calculations of light nuclei (Beane et al., 2012; Yamazaki et al., 2012; Orginos et al., 2015) to effective field theories of nuclear interactions (Weinberg, 1991; Bedaque et al., 2002; Epelbaum et al., 2012). It identifies unresolved problems including the reconciliation of different power-counting schemes in chiral EFT, the missing formulation of a fully renormalization-group invariant version of chiEFT, and the need for LQCD data at pion masses below 500 MeV where chiral perturbation theory converges. The work enables future matching of LQCD amplitudes to pionless EFT in the region where their areas of applicability overlap, and eventually to cluster EFTs for larger nuclear systems.
