# Finite amplitude method in linear response TDDFT calculations

> The finite amplitude method is a feasible and efficient method for the linear response calculation based on the time-dependent density functional theory. It was originally proposed as a method to calculate the strength functions. Recently, new techniques have been developed for computation of normal modes (eigenmodes) of the quasiparticle-random-phase approximation. Recent advances associated with the finite amplitude method are reviewed.

## Key claim

The finite amplitude method (FAM) provides a feasible and efficient way to perform linear response calculations in time-dependent density functional theory by avoiding explicit evaluation of complex residual fields, but the paper reviews recent extensions that now also allow computation of discrete QRPA eigenmodes (not just strength functions) via either matrix construction or contour integration.

## Method

The FAM replaces the explicit linear expansion of the residual HFB Hamiltonian with a finite-difference evaluation using a small parameter η, requiring only one-body quantities with two-quasiparticle indices rather than the four-quasiparticle indices of standard QRPA matrices. Two complementary approaches are reviewed: the matrix-FAM (m-FAM) constructs the full QRPA matrix by applying FAM to unit vectors and then diagonalizes it, while the iterative FAM (i-FAM) solves the linear response equation at complex frequencies and extracts eigenmodes via contour integration in the complex frequency plane.

## Key numbers

- The computational task of the m-FAM scales as D³, where D is the size of the QRPA matrix.
- The contour integral for transition strength is given by |⟨n|F̂|0⟩|² = (1/2πi) ∮_{C_n} S(F;ω) dω, where C_n encloses ω=ω_n>0.
- The QRPA normal modes X^{(n)}_{ij} and Y^{(n)}_{ij} are obtained as contour integrals: X^{(n)}_{ij} = |⟨n|F̂|0⟩|⁻¹ (1/2πi) ∮_{C_n} X_{ij}(ω) dω, and similarly for Y.

## Context

This review extends the original FAM work by Nakatsukasa et al. (2007) that was limited to strength function calculations, and the first FAM applications to electric dipole modes using Skyrme functionals without pairing by Inakura et al. (2009, 2011). It was later extended to superfluid systems by Avogadro and Nakatsukasa (2011) and to relativistic frameworks by Liang et al. (2013) and others. The new m-FAM and i-FAM techniques enable calculation of discrete QRPA eigenmodes, complementing earlier iterative algorithms such as that of Toivanen et al. (2010) and the contour integral approach of Hinohara et al. (2013).
