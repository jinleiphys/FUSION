# Dynamical critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point with hydrodynamic cooling rate

> Within the model A in the Hohenberg's dynamical universality classification, we investigate the critical slowing down effects on the critical fluctuations driven by the expanding quark-gluon plasma, using a trajectory and cooling rate obtained from hydrodynamics. We numerically solved the Langevin dynamics of the non-conserved order parameter field and find that, compared with commonly used Hubble-like expansion, the cooling rate of a realistic hydrodynamic system is pretty large and the associated critical slowing down effects strongly suppress the higher-order cumulants of the order parameter field ({\it e.g.,} $C_4$). Furthermore, for an evolving system that approaches the critical point, such critical slowing down suppression overcomes the enhancement of the critical fluctuations, which indicates that the largest fluctuations of the order parameter field ({\it i.e.,} $C_2$) do not necessarily associate with the evolving trajectory closest to the critical point.

# Dynamical critical fluctuations near the QCD critical point with hydrodynamic cooling rate

## Key claim

The cooling rate from a realistic hydrodynamic system is large enough that critical slowing down effects strongly suppress higher-order cumulants (e.g., $C_4$) of the order parameter field; for a system approaching the critical point, this suppression overcomes the enhancement of critical fluctuations, meaning the largest fluctuations ($C_2$) do not necessarily occur on the trajectory closest to the critical point.

## Method

Within model A of Hohenberg's dynamical universality classification, the authors numerically solved the Langevin dynamics of the non-conserved order parameter field $\sigma$ using a QGP evolution trajectory and cooling rate obtained from 3+1-d hydrodynamic simulations (MUSIC) for 19.6 GeV Au+Au collisions at 0-5% centrality. They compared results using the hydrodynamic cooling rate against two Hubble-like expansion rates with initial times $\tau_I = 10$ fm and $\tau_I = 40$ fm, and varied the location of the critical point by changing the critical chemical potential $\mu_{Bc}$ while fixing $T_c = 165$ MeV.

## Key numbers

- The effective relaxation time is set as $\tau_{\mbox{\tiny eff}} = \tau_{\mbox{\tiny rel}}(\xi_{\mbox{\tiny eq}}/\xi_{\mbox{\tiny min}})^z$ with $\tau_{\mbox{\tiny rel}} = 0.05$ fm and dynamical critical exponent $z = 3$.
- The mapping from Ising variables to QCD variables uses $\Delta T = 20$ MeV, $\Delta \mu = 100$ MeV, $\Delta h = 2$, $\Delta r = (5/3)^{3/4}$, with $M_0 = 200$ MeV and $H_0 = M_0/5$.
- The critical temperature is fixed at $T_c = 165$ MeV; four critical chemical potentials are used: $\mu_{Bc} = 150, 160, 170, 180$ MeV.
- The hydrodynamic simulation uses starting proper time $\tau_{0I} = 0.4$ fm for AMPT initial conditions, $\tau_{0h} = 1.2$ fm for hydrodynamics, specific shear viscosity $\eta/s = 0.08$, bulk viscosity $\zeta/s = 0$, and switching temperature $T_{sw} = 147$ MeV.
- The Langevin equation is solved on a cubic box with lattice spacing $\Delta x = 1$ fm, volume $V = 10^3$ fm$^3$, temporal step $\Delta t = 0.01$ fm, and up to 4.5 million events.
- The Hubble-like expansion uses initial temperature $T_I = 196$ MeV, speed of sound $c_s^2 = 0.15$, and dimension $n_V = 3$.

## Context

This work extends earlier dynamical model calculations within model A and model B that used fixed chemical potential trajectories and Hubble-like expansion (Berdnikov 1999, Nonaka 2004, Mukherjee 2015, Jiang 2017, Nahrgang 2018, Pihan 2022). It introduces a realistic QGP trajectory and cooling rate from hydrodynamic simulations (MUSIC with AMPT initial conditions), finding that the critical slowing down suppression is much stronger than in Hubble-like expansion cases. This enables more quantitative comparisons with RHIC Beam Energy Scan measurements of net-proton cumulants (STAR 2013, Luo 2015, Thader 2016, STAR 2020, STAR 2021) and motivates future work coupling the order parameter field with hydrodynamics in inhomogeneous fireballs and extending to model B for conserved baryon density.
