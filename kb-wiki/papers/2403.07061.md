# Simulating Meson Scattering on Spin Quantum Simulators

> Studying high-energy collisions of composite particles, such as hadrons and nuclei, is an outstanding goal for quantum simulators. However, preparation of hadronic wave packets has posed a significant challenge, due to the complexity of hadrons and the precise structure of wave packets. This has limited demonstrations of hadron scattering on quantum simulators to date. Observations of confinement and composite excitations in quantum spin systems have opened up the possibility to explore scattering dynamics in spin models. In this article, we develop two methods to create entangled spin states corresponding to wave packets of composite particles in analog quantum simulators of Ising spin Hamiltonians. One wave-packet preparation method uses the blockade effect enabled by beyond-nearest-neighbor Ising spin interactions. The other method utilizes a quantum-bus-mediated exchange, such as the native spin-phonon coupling in trapped-ion arrays. With a focus on trapped-ion simulators, we numerically benchmark both methods and show that high-fidelity wave packets can be achieved in near-term experiments. We numerically study scattering of wave packets for experimentally realizable parameters in the Ising model and find inelastic-scattering regimes, corresponding to particle production in the scattering event, with prominent and distinct experimental signals. Our proposal, therefore, demonstrates the potential of observing inelastic scattering in near-term quantum simulators.

## Key claim
The paper proposes that inelastic scattering of composite particle wave packets (mesons) can be observed in near-term analog quantum simulators of Ising spin models, provided that the wave packets are prepared using either a blockade effect or a quantum-bus-mediated scheme, and that the model parameters are chosen to allow kinematically accessible inelastic channels.

## Method
The authors develop two methods to create entangled spin states corresponding to Gaussian wave packets of bound two-kink states (1-mesons) in one-dimensional Ising spin chains. One method uses the blockade effect from beyond-nearest-neighbor Ising interactions to resonantly drive a single-spin-flip wave packet from the all-spin-up state. The other method uses a quantum-bus-mediated exchange, such as spin-phonon coupling in trapped-ion arrays, to transfer a Gaussian profile from a bosonic mode to the spin chain. The wave packets are prepared in the free theory (zero transverse field \(h^z\)), then adiabatically ramped into the interacting theory by increasing \(h^z\), allowed to propagate and collide under the full Hamiltonian, and finally ramped back for measurement and analysis of outgoing scattering channels.

## Key numbers
- Combined final fidelity of \(95\%\) for preparing two wave packets (one on each side of the chain) using the blockade method with power-law coupling \(\alpha=1.5\) and total preparation time \(J_0 T = 31.5\).
- For the blockade method, the preparation time for a single wave packet is \(J_0 T = \pi/\Gamma\), where \(\Gamma\) is a tunable parameter relating the transverse-field amplitude to the Gaussian wave-packet amplitude.
- Bloch bands are computed at transverse fields \(h^z = 0.8 J_0\) and \(h^z = 1.4 J_0\) for the power-law model (\(\alpha=1.5\)), and at \(h^z = 0.5 J_0\) and \(h^z = 0.83 J_0\) for the exponentially decaying model (\(\beta=1\)).
- The critical separation \(\ell_c\) between two kinks is determined by the model parameters such that kinks separated by \(\ell < \ell_c\) form bound states (mesons) while those with \(\ell \geq \ell_c\) are unbound.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of confinement and bound-state dynamics in Ising spin models, including spectroscopy of meson states (Jurcevic 2015, Kranzl 2022), string breaking (Pichler 2016, Liu 2019, Surace 2020, Tan 2021, De 2024), and slow thermalization (Jurcevic 2014, Lerose 2020). It goes beyond global quench experiments by focusing on scattering of individual wave packets, building on tensor-network scattering studies (Van Damme 2021, Milsted 2022, Belyansky 2023) and earlier proposals for scattering in spin models (Surace 2021, Vovrosh 2022). The work enables experimental observation of inelastic scattering in near-term trapped-ion and neutral-atom quantum simulators, providing a pathway toward simulating hadron-like collision dynamics in controllable quantum systems.
