# Polarization Rotation Effects due to Parity Violation in Atoms

> We present a study of parity (P) violating polarization rotations of atoms in external electric fields. Five different types of rotations are identified and the consequences of time reversal invariance (T) are discussed. The role played by Zeldovich's electric dipole moment of unstable states is elucidated. To calculate the effects, we use the standard model of elementary particle physics where P violation in atoms is due to the exchange of the Z boson between the quarks in the nucleus and the atomic electrons. We consider in detail hydrogen-like systems in $n=2$ states, especially $^1_1$H, $^4_2$He$^+$, and $^{12}_6$C$^{5+}$. We discuss ways to obtain in some cases large enhancement factors for the P-violating polarization rotations and show that e.g. for $^1_1$H one could in principle observe rotations as large as a few percent, where for a statistically significant result one would need a total of $10^{15}$ polarized atoms. We point out that some of our P-violating polarization rotations are very sensitive to the nuclear spin-dependent part of the P-violating Hamiltonian which receives a contribution from the polarized strange quark density in polarized nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper identifies five distinct types of parity-violating polarization rotations for atoms in external electric fields and shows that, while the nominal rotation magnitude is \(10^{-11}-10^{-12}\), enhancement factors of order \(10^4\) can be achieved in hydrogen-like systems, potentially yielding observable rotations of a few percent for \(^1_1\)H, but only if a total of \(10^{15}\) polarized atoms are used to obtain a statistically significant result.

## Method

The authors use the Wigner-Weisskopf approach to describe unstable atomic states and construct the non-hermitian mass matrix \(\underline{\cal M}\) for hydrogen-like atoms in a constant external electric field, including the parity-violating Hamiltonian from Z boson exchange in the standard model. They diagonalize the mass matrix simultaneously with the third component of total angular momentum \(F_3\) and derive the consequences of time reversal invariance for the complex energy eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The analysis focuses on \(n=2\) states of hydrogen-like systems with spin-zero and spin-\(\frac{1}{2}\) nuclei, using the small Lamb shift between \(2S_{1/2}\) and \(2P_{1/2}\) states to enhance P-violating mixing.

## Key numbers

- Lamb shift for \(^1_1\)H: \(L(1,0) = 4.376\ \mu\text{eV} = h \cdot 1058\ \text{MHz} = \hbar \cdot 6.648 \cdot 10^9\ \text{s}^{-1}\)
- Fine structure splitting for \(^1_1\)H: \(\Delta(1,0) = 45.364\ \mu\text{eV} = h \cdot 10969\ \text{MHz} = \hbar \cdot 6.892 \cdot 10^{10}\ \text{s}^{-1}\)
- \(2P\) state lifetime: \(\tau_P(1,0) = 1.6\ \text{ns}\); \(2S\) state lifetime: \(\tau_S(1,0) = 0.12\ \text{s}\)
- Electric field required for \(\sqrt{3}\mathcal{F}(1,|\vec{\mathcal{E}}|)/L(1,0) = 1\): \(|\vec{\mathcal{E}}| = 477\ \text{V/cm}\)
- P-odd parameters for hydrogen isotopes: \(\delta_i(1,N) = -Q_W^{(i)}(1,N) \cdot 6.14 \cdot 10^{-12}\)
- Scaling laws: \(\Delta(Z,N) \cong Z^4 \Delta(1,0)\), \(L(Z,N) \cong Z^4 L(1,0)\), \(\Gamma_P(Z,N) \cong Z^4 \Gamma_P(1,0)\), \(\Gamma_S(Z,N) \cong Z^6 \Gamma_S(1,0)\)
- For a statistically significant result in \(^1_1\)H: need \(10^{15}\) polarized atoms
- Strange quark polarization from EMC: \(\frac{1}{2}(\Delta s)_p = -0.095 \pm 0.016 \pm 0.023\)

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Botz, Bruß, and Nachtmann (1993) for P-violating spin rotations in atoms and is related to earlier regeneration-type proposals for hydrogenic systems. The paper shows that measuring P-violating polarization rotations could provide precision determinations of standard model couplings at low energies, allow measurement of polarized strange quark densities in polarized nuclei (addressing the "spin crisis" for nucleons identified by the EMC collaboration in 1988), and demonstrate subtle quantum mechanical effects where P violation and T conservation interplay. The results are relevant for experimental advances in cold heavy ion beams and atomic beam interferometry.
