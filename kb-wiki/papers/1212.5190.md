# Discovering the New Standard Model: Fundamental Symmetries and Neutrinos

> This White Paper describes recent progress and future opportunities in the area of fundamental symmetries and neutrinos.

## Key claim

This White Paper argues that low-energy precision experiments in fundamental symmetries and neutrino physics are uniquely suited to discover new sources of symmetry violation (B, L, CP) that are essentially inaccessible at the LHC, and to discriminate among various New Standard Model models, but this potential depends on maintaining a balanced funding portfolio that includes both next-generation experiments and small-scale R&D efforts.

## Method

The paper summarizes the outcomes of the "Fundamental Symmetries and Neutrinos" workshop held August 10-11, 2012 in Chicago, which gathered input from the scientific community to prepare for the upcoming NSAC subcommittee deliberations. It reviews recent experimental progress across multiple subfields including neutrinoless double beta decay, electric dipole moment searches, neutron decay measurements, muon experiments, neutrino oscillation measurements, and parity-violating electron scattering, and presents four community recommendations for future funding priorities.

## Key numbers

- Neutrinoless double beta decay: current generation experiments aim to reach effective Majorana mass sensitivity below 100 meV, an order of magnitude below the current laboratory limit for the kinematic mass of 2.2 eV
- EXO-200: current limit on effective Majorana mass is less than 140-380 meV, with energy resolution sigma/Q = 1.67%
- Majorana Demonstrator: projected sensitivity less than 100-210 meV, with projected energy resolution sigma/Q = 0.1%
- CUORE: projected sensitivity less than 40-90 meV with 5 years of data; prototype (CUORICINO) achieved limit of 300-700 meV
- KamLAND-Zen: current limit on effective Majorana mass less than 128-348 meV, with energy resolution sigma/Q = 4.2%
- SNO+: projected sensitivity less than 90-175 meV with 3 years of data
- NEXT: projected sensitivity less than 80-130 meV, assuming three years of running at 10 atm
- Super-NEMO: projected sensitivity less than 200-400 meV
- Mercury-199 EDM: current limit of 0.3 x 10^-28 e·cm
- Neutron EDM: current limit of 300 x 10^-28 e·cm
- Electron EDM: current limit of 10 x 10^-28 e·cm
- Proposed proton EDM experiment: sensitivity goal of 0.1 x 10^-28 e·cm, using polarized protons at the "magic" momentum of 0.7 GeV/c with storage time of 1000 s
- Neutron EDM: at least one of eight worldwide experiments should produce a factor of five sensitivity improvement by 2020

## Context

This White Paper extends the 2007 NSAC Long Range Plan's "New Standard Model Initiative" and responds to the 2012 NAS Nuclear Physics Report, the 2011 NAS Assessment of Science Proposed for DUSEL, and the 2011 NSAC Neutron subcommittee (Kumar) report. It reviews progress since those reports, including the LHC discovery of a SM-like Higgs particle, new limits on neutrinoless double beta decay from EXO-200 and KamLAND-Zen, and new EDM limits for mercury-199, the neutron, and the electron. The paper enables prioritization of next-generation experiments including ton-scale double beta decay experiments (to reach the inverted hierarchy mass region) and a neutron EDM experiment at the SNS (to improve the current limit by two orders of magnitude), as well as continued support for theory efforts connecting experimental results to astrophysics and particle physics.
