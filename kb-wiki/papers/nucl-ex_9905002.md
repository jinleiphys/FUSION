# Phase Transitions in Finite Nuclei and the Integer Nucleon Number Problem

> The study of spherical-deformed ground--state phase transitions in finite nuclei as a function of N and Z is hindered by the discrete values of the nucleon number. A resolution of the integer nucleon number problem, and evidence relating to phase transitions in finite nuclei, are discussed from the experimental point of view and interpreted within the framework of the interacting boson model.

## Key claim

Finite nuclei can exhibit true phase transitional character between spherical and deformed ground-state shapes, despite the discrete integer nucleon number problem, by using the empirical energy E(2⁺₁) as a nearly continuous control parameter that reveals a sharp change in slope from 2.00 to 3.33 at a critical value E_c(2⁺₁) ∼ 120 keV.

## Method

The authors plotted E(4⁺₁) against E(2⁺₁) for collective even-even nuclei in the 50 < Z ≤ 66 region, adding data from successive elements to fill in the distribution and obtain a nearly continuous curve. They then analyzed the resulting trajectory using the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) with an extended consistent-Q formalism Hamiltonian, computing energy surfaces in the intrinsic state formalism to study the location of the potential minimum β_min as a function of the control parameter ξ.

## Key numbers

- The change in slope from 2.00 to 3.33 in the E(4⁺₁) versus E(2⁺₁) plot occurs at E_c(2⁺₁) ∼ 120 keV
- The nucleus ¹⁵²Sm has E(2⁺₁) = 121 keV, coinciding with the critical energy where the slope change occurs
- The IBM energy surface analysis used N_B = 10 and χ = -√7/2 for the transition from U(5) to SU(3)
- The analysis covered nuclei with R₄/₂ ≡ E(4⁺₁)/E(2⁺₁) > 2.05 (the collective criterion)
- Separation energies S₂ₙ were shifted to equal values at N = 88 for the N > 82 shell and at N = 76 for the N < 82 shell

## Context

This work extends earlier empirical correlations of E(4⁺₁) versus E(2⁺₁) discussed by Casten, Zamfir, and Brenner (1993) and theoretical analyses using the 1/N expansion method for the IBM by Jolos and collaborators. It directly connects to the recent evidence for phase coexistence in ¹⁵²Sm reported by Casten and collaborators (1998), showing that the same nucleus lies at the critical point identified through the regional trajectory. The approach enables the study of phase transitions across entire regions of nuclei rather than within single isotopic chains, and the IBM analysis demonstrates that the abrupt spherical-to-deformed transition is a nearly unavoidable feature of structural evolution in this mass region.
