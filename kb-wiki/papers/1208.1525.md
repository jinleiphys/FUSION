# Correlation Testing in Nuclear Density Functional Theory

> Correlation testing provides a quick method of discriminating amongst potential terms to include in a nuclear mass formula or functional and is a necessary tool for further nuclear mass models; however a firm mathematical foundation of the method has not been previously set forth. Here, the necessary justification for correlation testing is developed and more detail of the motivation behind its use is give. Examples are provided to clarify the method analytically and for computational benchmarking. We provide a quantitative demonstration of the method's performance and short-comings, highlighting also potential issues a user may encounter. In concluding we suggest some possible future developments to improve the limitations of the method.

## Key claim

Correlation testing provides a computationally inexpensive method for discriminating among potential terms to include in a nuclear mass formula or functional, but the method requires a firm mathematical foundation that had not been previously set forth; the paper develops this justification and demonstrates that the correlation coefficient indicates linear independence of functional terms in the linear algebraic sense, even though zero covariance does not imply statistical independence.

## Method

The author treats each term of a nuclear energy functional as a vector in the functional space and computes the Pearson correlation coefficient between a candidate new term and each term already present in the functional, using averages over the set of experimental binding energies. A candidate term is considered for inclusion only if its absolute correlation with every existing term falls below a threshold (here set at |R| < 0.5), with the threshold determined empirically from a training set of roughly 20% of the candidate terms. The method is benchmarked by comparing the reduction in least-squares error (Δχ) achieved by adding each candidate term individually via full minimization versus the computational cost of the correlation test alone.

## Key numbers

- The least-squares error of the base functional (Eq. 13) fitted to 2,049 nuclei from the 2003 atomic data evaluation (binding energy uncertainty below 200 keV) is χ = 1.687 MeV with seven fit coefficients.
- Adding a highly correlated term (f₁, ⟨|R|⟩ > 0.5) reduces χ to 1.686 MeV, a Δχ of only 0.001 MeV; adding another highly correlated term (f₂) gives the same Δχ = 0.001 MeV.
- Adding a low-correlation term (f₃, ⟨|R|⟩ < 0.5) reduces χ to 1.491 MeV, a Δχ of 0.196 MeV; adding another low-correlation term (f₄) gives χ = 1.444 MeV, a Δχ of 0.243 MeV.
- Of the full set of about 200 candidate terms, 85% had high correlation (⟨|R|⟩ > 0.5) and reduced the least-squares error by 50 keV or less; 39% reduced it by 10 keV or less; 20% gave Δχ = 0 MeV.
- Of the 15% of terms with low correlation (⟨|R|⟩ < 0.5), 86% produced Δχ > 150 keV and 14% produced Δχ < 150 keV.
- Full minimization for a single term required approximately 170 CPU seconds; the correlation test for all ~200 terms required 1.62 CPU seconds.

## Context

This work extends the eigen-decomposition approach of Bertsch et al. (2005), which identified redundant linear combinations of functional terms via principal axis methods, by providing a much cheaper correlation-based alternative suitable for screening hundreds to thousands of candidate terms. The method was demonstrated in Bertolli (2012) for developing an occupation-number-based energy functional, and the present paper provides the formal mathematical justification (via proof by contradiction in the appendix) that the correlation coefficient indicates linear independence of functions in the linear algebraic sense. The approach enables systematic term selection for nuclear density functionals and mass formulas, addressing the need identified by the UNEDF collaboration and others (Kortelainen et al., 2010; Goriely et al., 2009) to efficiently explore large candidate-term spaces while avoiding the computational expense of full least-squares minimization for each candidate.
