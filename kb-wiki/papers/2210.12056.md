# The exploration of hot and dense nuclear matter: Introduction to relativistic heavy-ion physics

> This article summarizes our present knowledge about nuclear matter at the highest energy densities and its formation in relativistic heavy ion collisions. We review what is known about the structure and properties of the quark-gluon plasma and survey the observables that are used to glean information about it from experimental data.

## Key claim

This review summarizes the present understanding of quark-gluon plasma properties and their formation in relativistic heavy ion collisions, but the authors explicitly note that definitive calculations remain limited to thermodynamic quantities accessible via static observables, while transport coefficients and properties of matter with substantial baryon excess still rely on tenuous extrapolations, effective models, or holographic methods that must be regarded as qualitative.

## Method

The paper reviews the connection between experimental observables and fundamental QCD matter properties, covering single-particle observables (particle yields, transverse momentum spectra, nuclear modification factor), two- and few-particle correlations (di-hadron correlations, photon-hadron correlations), and multi-particle collective observables (anisotropic flow, HBT femtoscopic radii, balance functions, reconstructed jets). The theoretical foundations are surveyed across three approaches: lattice QCD simulations for baryon-symmetric matter at zero chemical potential, thermal perturbation theory and effective models for finite density regimes, and holographic methods for strongly coupled gauge theories that serve as models for QCD.

## Key numbers

- The cross-over transition in QCD matter properties occurs in the temperature range 140 MeV < T < 170 MeV, established by lattice QCD simulations
- The early universe existed as quark-gluon plasma for the first roughly 20 microseconds when its temperature exceeded 2 x 10^12 K
- The LHC beam energy is more than a factor of 10 higher than that of RHIC
- Trigger particles in hard di-hadron correlations are usually chosen with p_T > 5 GeV/c, while both p_T ranges are 3 GeV/c and higher for pQCD applicability

## Context

This review extends the foundational results summarized in the RHIC experimental collaboration white papers (Arsene 2004, Adcox 2004, Back 2004, Adams 2005) and the subsequent LHC results from ALICE (Schukraft 2011), ATLAS (Steinberg 2011), and CMS (Wyslouch 2011). It builds upon earlier comprehensive assessments (Muller 2012) and more focused reviews of bulk QGP properties (Braun-Munzinger 2015) and open questions (Busza 2018). The review enables future work by identifying specific open questions, particularly the need for precision measurements over wide kinematic ranges and reliable calculations of transport coefficients combined with realistic dynamical simulations.
