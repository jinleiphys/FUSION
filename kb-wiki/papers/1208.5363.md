# What perspectives for the synthesis of heavier superheavy nuclei? Results and comparison with models

> The possibility to synthesize heavier superheavy elements in massive nuclei reactions is strongly limited by the hindrance to complete fusion of reacting nuclei: due to the onset of the quasifission process in the entrance channel, which competes with complete fusion, and by strong increase of the fission yield along the de-excitation cascade of the compound nucleus in comparison to the evaporation residue formation. We present a wide and detailed procedure allowing us to describe the experimental results (evaporation residue nuclei and fissionlike products) in the mass asymmetric and symmetric reactions. Very reliable estimations and perspectives for the synthesis of superheavy elements in many massive nuclei reactions up to Z = 120 and eventually also for Z > 120 have been obtained.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the synthesis of superheavy elements with Z = 120 may be possible under specific conditions, but that reaching Z = 122 is a "very doubtful venture" and Z = 124 and 126 are "impossible" with current experimental capabilities, contrary to some optimistic earlier estimates in the literature.

## Method

The authors employed the dinuclear system (DNS) model to calculate capture cross sections, fusion probabilities, quasifission, fast fission, and fusion-fission cross sections for a wide set of reactions leading to compound nuclei with Z = 100–126. They computed evaporation residue cross sections after neutron emission only, using two different mass tables (Nix-Möller and Sobiczewski's group) for binding energies and fission barriers, and analyzed the dependence of fusion probability on the charge asymmetry parameter z = (Z₁ × Z₂)/(A₁^{1/3} + A₂^{1/3}) at a fixed compound nucleus excitation energy of about 37 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the DNS model approach previously developed by the same group (Fazio 2005, Nasirov 2009) to systematically evaluate the prospects for synthesizing superheavy elements beyond Z = 118. It directly contradicts the optimistic estimates of Siwek-Wilczynska (2007), Smolanczuk (2001), and Zagrebaev (2007) regarding fusion probabilities in symmetric and near-symmetric reactions, and specifically criticizes the unrealistic neutron evaporation energies reported in Zagrebaev (2012). The paper also argues against the idea, put forward by Oganessian (2009) and others, that symmetric fission fragment distributions imply high fusion probabilities for symmetric entrance channels. The results provide quantitative guidance for experimental planning at laboratories attempting to synthesize elements with Z = 120 and above, showing that only reactions with the charge asymmetry parameter z below about 230 offer realistic prospects.
