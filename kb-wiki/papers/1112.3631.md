# Finite size effects in isobaric ratios

> The properties of isobaric ratios, between nuclei produced in the same reaction, are investigated using the canonical and grand-canonical statistical ensembles. Although the grand-canonical for- mulae furnish a means to correlate the ratios with the liquid drop parameters, finite size effects make it difficult to obtain their actual values from fitting nuclear collision data.

## Key claim

Although the grand-canonical formula for isobaric ratios provides a way to correlate the ratios with liquid drop parameters, finite size effects make it difficult to obtain their actual values from fitting nuclear collision data; the extracted parameters converge to asymptotic values only for system sizes much larger than those formed in actual experiments.

## Method

The authors used both canonical and grand-canonical statistical ensembles to calculate isobaric ratios R(I,I',A) for odd-A nuclei produced in nuclear collisions. They employed a simple liquid-drop binding energy formula with standard parameters (a_v=15.8 MeV, a_s=18.0 MeV, a_sym=23.5 MeV, a_c=0.72 MeV) and a temperature-dependent internal Helmholtz free energy, then compared the calculated ratios for different source sizes (A0,Z0) to experimental projectile fragmentation data from 48Ca+9Be, 40Ca+9Be, and 58Ni+9Be reactions.

## Key numbers

- For the grand-canonical ensemble at T=6.0 MeV, the Coulomb coefficient including Wigner-Seitz correction is a*_c=0.2208 MeV (a*_c/T=0.0368) and the chemical potential difference for 48Ca is Δμ=12.5458 MeV (Δμ/T=2.091)
- For the canonical ensemble with source (48,20), the fit to the linear behavior gives Δμ/T=0.55 and a*_c/T=0.420; for (72,30): Δμ/T=0.95 and a*_c/T=0.280; for (96,40): Δμ/T=1.52 and a*_c/T=0.178; for (384,160): Δμ/T=2.00 and a*_c/T=0.057
- The experimental 48Ca+9Be data give extracted slope parameter a*_c/T=0.75 and offset parameter Δμ/T=-1.7

## Context

This work extends the analysis of isobaric ratios proposed by Ma et al. (2011) using a modified Fisher model, and shows that the standard canonical and grand-canonical statistical ensembles (as reviewed by Das et al., 2005) also reproduce the observed linear behavior. The results contradict the interpretation that extracted parameters directly reflect liquid drop properties, and demonstrate that finite size effects, previously discussed by Botvina et al. (1987), prevent reliable extraction of nuclear properties from such ratios in experimentally accessible systems. This enables future work to focus on developing methods that properly account for finite size effects when comparing data to statistical models.
