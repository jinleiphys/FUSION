# Towards better nuclear charge radii

> Nuclear charge radii constitute a physical observable of growing significance across multiple subdisciplines of physics and related fields. Their determination relies on a combination of complementary experimental techniques and advanced theoretical frameworks. Current recommended values are informed by the outcomes of several independent working groups, each employing distinct methodological approaches and evaluation strategies. The present effort is directed toward a more precise and reliable extraction of charge radii, as well as the development of a modern, transparent, and methodologically robust compilation of recommended values.

# Towards better nuclear charge radii

## Key claim

The paper outlines a community-driven roadmap toward a more precise and reliable compilation of nuclear charge radii, but emphasizes that current recommended values are limited by systematic uncertainties in extraction methods (electron scattering, muonic atom spectroscopy, and laser spectroscopy) that are often larger than statistical errors, and that simply averaging results from different methods without understanding these systematics can lead to improper results.

## Method

The working group convened by the IAEA in 2025 reviewed the three primary experimental methods for determining nuclear charge radii: elastic electron scattering, muonic atom spectroscopy, and laser spectroscopy of atoms and ions (including highly charged ions). They assessed the current limitations of each method, evaluated the combined analysis approach using Barrett moments and V-factors, and formulated specific recommendations for future measurements, theoretical calculations, and compilation procedures to improve accuracy and transparency.

## Key numbers

- Accuracy of charge radii from electron scattering is poorer than approximately 1%; from single-transition muonic atoms, approximately 0.3%; from combined analysis, approximately 0.1%
- For precision tests of the Standard Model, nuclear charge radii must be known to 0.1-0.01%
- Current theoretical tools (lattice QCD for single nucleons, ab initio nuclear theory) are limited to approximately 1% precision
- Deviations of 1-2% between radii extracted from muonic atoms and from electron scattering are shown in Table VI of Fricke (1995) and in recent work on chlorine
- The Coulomb potential reaches a maximum of about 26 MeV for 208Pb, with a corresponding higher-order Born correction scaling factor of 0.52% for a 5 GeV beam
- When comparing the Barrett moment recipe with the approach using multiple muonic transitions, the resulting radii agree to better than 0.1%

## Context

This work extends the previous compilations by Angeli and Marinova (2013) and Angeli (2004), as well as the tables by Fricke and Heilig (1995, 2004). It responds to significant developments in experimental and theoretical approaches since those publications, including the growing use of highly charged ion spectroscopy and improved atomic theory calculations. The recommendations enable future compilations that are more transparent and methodologically robust, addressing the needs of precision tests of the Standard Model (such as superallowed beta decays and parity-violating electron scattering) and nuclear structure studies of mirror nuclei and neutron skins.
