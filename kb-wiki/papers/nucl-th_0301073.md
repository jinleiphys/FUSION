# Giant-dipole Resonance and the Deformation of Hot, Rotating Nuclei

> The development of nuclear shapes under the extreme conditions of high spin and/or temperature is examined. Scaling properties are used to demonstrate universal properties of both thermal expectation values of nuclear shapes as well as the minima of the free energy, which can be used to understand the Jacobi transition. A universal correlation between the width of the giant dipole resonance and quadrupole deformation is found, providing a novel probe to measure the nuclear deformation in hot nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper claims a universal linear relationship between the full-width-at-half-maximum (FWHM) of the giant dipole resonance and the mean quadrupole deformation \(\langle\beta\rangle\) for hot, rotating nuclei, but this relationship holds only for nuclei below the Jacobi transition and is most reliable for non-doubly magic nuclei at temperatures above 1.25 MeV.

## Method

The authors used the thermal fluctuation model (TFM) to compute GDR strength functions by averaging over nuclear shapes and orientations weighted by the free energy, evaluated via the Nilsson-Strutinsky procedure with a finite-temperature liquid-drop parameterization. They computed thermal expectation values of the quadrupole deformation parameters \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) and their variances for nuclei in the mass range A=44-208 as functions of temperature T and angular momentum J. Scaling properties were identified by plotting normalized deformation moments against the dimensionless variable \(\xi = J / A^{5/6}\) and applying a temperature-dependent power law to collapse the data onto universal curves.

## Key numbers

- The universal scaling functions for \(\langle\beta\rangle\) and \(\langle\beta^2\rangle\) are parameterized with coefficients \(a_i = (4.3, 1.64, 0.31)\) for shifted Fermi functions.
- The critical spin for the Jacobi transition is defined as \(J_c \sim 1.2 A^{5/6}\), occurring at \(\xi \sim 1.2\).
- The linear relation between \(\langle\beta\rangle\) and the GDR width is \(\langle\beta\rangle = 0.8 \frac{\Gamma - \Gamma_0}{E_0} + 0.12\), with an average uncertainty of \(\delta\langle\beta\rangle \approx \pm 0.05\).
- For \(^{194}\)Hg, the condition \(\sigma_\beta \sim \beta_{min}\) occurs when \(J \sim 37\sqrt{T}\); at \(T=1.3\) MeV this gives \(J < 42\).
- The \(J=0\) average deformation follows \(\langle\beta^n(J=0,T,A)\rangle \propto (T/C_0)^{n/2}\) with coefficients \(a_1=3.09\), \(a_2=-0.74\), \(a_3=0.12\), \(a_4=-0.066\) for the liquid-drop stiffness parameter \(C_0\).

## Context

This work extends the universal scaling law for the GDR width previously found by the same group (Kusnezov and Ormand, 2002) to include the deformation parameters \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\), and establishes a direct linear correlation between the GDR width and mean deformation. The results enable experimental extraction of nuclear deformation from GDR width measurements, as demonstrated for Sn isotopes using data from earlier experiments (Refs. r:1-r:4). The analysis also explains the absence of spin-dependent GDR width effects in \(^{194}\)Hg observed by Camera et al., by showing that deformation fluctuations wash out rotational effects when \(\sigma_\beta\) is comparable to \(\beta_{min}\).
