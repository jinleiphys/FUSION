# Perfect-fluid behavior of a dilute Fermi gas near unitary

> We present an ab initio calculation of the shear viscosity as a function of interaction strength in a two-component unpolarized Fermi gas near the unitary limit, within a finite temperature quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) framework and using the Kubo linear-response formalism. The shear viscosity decreases as we tune the interaction strength 1/ak_F from the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer side of the Feshbach resonance towards Bose-Einstein condensation limit and it acquires the smallest value for 1/ak_F approx 0.4, with a minimum value of (eta/s)_min approx 0.2 hbar/k_B, which is about twice as small as the value reported for experiments in quark-gluon plasma (eta/s)_QGP lesssim 0.4 hbar/k_B. The Fermi gas near unitarity thus emerges as the most "perfect fluid" observed so far in nature. The clouds of dilute Fermi gas near unitarity exhibit the unusual attribute that, for the sizes realized so far in the laboratory or larger (less than 10^9 atoms), can sustain quantum turbulence below the critical temperature, but at the same time the classical turbulence is suppressed in the normal phase.

## Key claim

The shear viscosity of a dilute Fermi gas reaches a minimum on the BEC side of the Feshbach resonance, not at unitarity itself, with the ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density reaching approximately 0.2 hbar/k_B at an interaction strength of 1/(a k_F) ≈ 0.4; however, the authors note that their QMC uncertainties do not permit a precise determination of the minimum position, and the experimental minimum reported by Elliott et al. occurs at a slightly weaker coupling of 1/(a k_F) ≈ 0.25.

## Method

The authors performed finite-temperature quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) simulations with auxiliary fields on a cubic lattice of size 10^3, using the Kubo linear-response formalism to compute the imaginary-time stress tensor-stress tensor correlator at zero momentum. The static shear viscosity was extracted by analytic continuation of this correlator to real frequencies using a combination of Singular Value Decomposition and self-consistent Maximum Entropy Method, with the asymptotic tail behavior and sum rule constraints imposed as prior information. The contact density C was obtained from the high-momentum tail of the momentum distribution, and the entropy density was derived from thermodynamic relations using QMC-computed energy, chemical potential, and contact.

## Key numbers

- Minimum shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: (eta/s)_min ≈ 0.2 hbar/k_B at interaction strength 1/(a k_F) ≈ 0.4
- This value is about twice as small as the reported value for quark-gluon plasma: (eta/s)_QGP ≲ 0.4 hbar/k_B
- The KSS holographic bound is 1/(4π) hbar/k_B ≈ 0.08 hbar/k_B
- The minimum ratio is about 2.5 times smaller than its value at the unitary limit and about 2.5 times larger than the KSS bound
- For temperature T/ε_F = 0.26, reliable inversions were limited to 1/(a k_F) ≤ 0.2; for T/ε_F = 0.4 and 0.5, to 1/(a k_F) ≤ 0.5
- The lattice spacing l = 1 with average number density n ≈ 0.04, and the effective range parameter p_F r_eff ≈ 0.43
- Reynolds number estimates: Re ≈ 60 for N = 1000 atoms, Re ≈ 620 for N = 10^6 atoms, with Re ≈ 10^4 requiring clouds of up to 10^9 atoms

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous ab initio QMC calculations of shear viscosity at unitarity (Wlazlowski et al., 2013; Wlazlowski et al., 2014) to interaction strengths beyond the unitary point, motivated by the experimental observation of Elliott et al. (2014) that the lowest viscosity occurs at 1/(a k_F) ≈ 0.25. The results qualitatively confirm the experimental finding of a minimum on the BEC side, though at a stronger coupling, and the authors note that the trapped experiment probes a range of temperatures and interaction strengths due to the trap-averaging procedure, making direct quantitative comparison challenging. The paper also discusses the implications for quantum and classical turbulence, arguing that the unitary Fermi gas below T_c can sustain quantum turbulence in clouds as small as 500-1000 fermions, while classical turbulence above T_c is suppressed for current experimental cloud sizes.
