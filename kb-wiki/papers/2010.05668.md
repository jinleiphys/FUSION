# Optimization and Supervised Machine Learning Methods for Fitting Numerical Physics Models without Derivatives

> We address the calibration of a computationally expensive nuclear physics model for which derivative information with respect to the fit parameters is not readily available. Of particular interest is the performance of optimization-based training algorithms when dozens, rather than millions or more, of training data are available and when the expense of the model places limitations on the number of concurrent model evaluations that can be performed. As a case study, we consider the Fayans energy density functional model, which has characteristics similar to many model fitting and calibration problems in nuclear physics. We analyze hyperparameter tuning considerations and variability associated with stochastic optimization algorithms and illustrate considerations for tuning in different computational settings.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether derivative-free optimization algorithms commonly used in machine learning can be effectively applied to calibrate computationally expensive nuclear physics models, specifically the Fayans energy density functional, where only dozens of training data points are available rather than millions. The authors find that the choice of algorithm and hyperparameter tuning significantly affects performance, with deterministic methods (POUNDERS and Nelder-Mead) and the adaptive sampling quasi-Newton method (AdaQN) generally outperforming stochastic gradient-based methods (Kiefer-Wolfowitz and Bandit) in terms of robustness and efficiency, though the relative performance depends on the computational budget and ability to evaluate component functions concurrently.

## Method

The authors formulated the calibration problem as minimizing a sum-of-squares objective function with 198 observables (binding energies, charge radii, diffraction radii, surface thicknesses, single-level energies, differential radii, and pairing gaps) across 72 spherical even-even nuclei, using 13 free parameters of the Fayans energy density functional. They tested five derivative-free optimization algorithm families: two deterministic methods (Nelder-Mead simplex and POUNDERS model-based trust-region) and three randomized methods (Kiefer-Wolfowitz finite-difference, two-point Bandit, and AdaQN adaptive sampling quasi-Newton), each with various hyperparameter settings, using a budget of 700×198 component function evaluations per run from five different starting points. To handle software failures (nonconvergent iterations and runtime errors), the authors modified the objective function with penalty terms and a projection onto an ℓ₁-ball around a reference parameter point.

## Key numbers

- Number of free model parameters: 13
- Total number of observables: 198, comprising 63 binding energies, 52 charge radii, 28 diffraction radii, 26 surface thicknesses, 5 neutron single-level energies, 5 proton single-level energies, 3 differential radii, 5 neutron pairing gaps, and 11 proton pairing gaps
- Number of spherical even-even nucleus configurations: 72
- Finite-difference parameter used: h = 5×10⁻⁷
- Penalty parameters for convergence failures: σₘ = 2 (marginally convergent), σₜ = 5 (nonconvergent), σᵣ = 100 (runtime failure)
- ℓ₁-ball radius for projection: 2 (in scaled parameter space)
- Batch sizes tested: 11, 33, 99, and 198
- Step-size hyperparameter set: {2⁻³, 2⁻⁴, 2⁻⁵, 2⁻⁶, 2⁻⁷}
- Computational budget per run: 700 × 198 = 138,600 component function evaluations

## Context

This work extends the line of energy density functional calibration studies exemplified by the UNEDF family of functionals (UNEDF0, UNEDF1, UNEDF2) and the Fayans EDF Fy(Δr) reported by Reinhard (2017), which previously relied primarily on point-based optimization. The paper addresses the practical challenge that Bayesian approaches, such as those demonstrated by Higdon et al. (2015) with approximately 200 model evaluations, can suffer from nontrivial failure rates (roughly 9% of designs) even in narrow posterior regions. The findings enable practitioners to select appropriate derivative-free optimization methods for nuclear model calibration based on their computational budget and tolerance for hyperparameter sensitivity, and they provide a framework for handling software failures that arise during automated optimization runs.
