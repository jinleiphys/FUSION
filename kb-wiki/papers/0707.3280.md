# Trapping of strangelets in the geomagnetic field

> Strangelets coming from the interstellar medium (ISM) are an interesting target to experiments searching for evidence of this hypothetic state of hadronic matter. We entertain the possibility of a {\it trapped} strangelet population, quite analogous to ordinary nuclei and electron belts. For a population of strangelets to be trapped by the geomagnetic field, these incoming particles would have to fulfill certain conditions, namely having magnetic rigidities above the geomagnetic cutoff and below a certain threshold for adiabatic motion to hold. We show in this work that, for fully ionized strangelets, there is a narrow window for stable trapping. An estimate of the stationary population is presented and the dominant loss mechanisms discussed. It is shown that the population would be substantially enhanced with respect to the ISM flux (up to two orders of magnitude) due to quasi-stable trapping.

## Key claim
The paper argues that fully ionized strangelets from the interstellar medium can become stably trapped in the Earth's geomagnetic field, but only within a narrow window of magnetic rigidities bounded by the geomagnetic cutoff on the low side and an adiabatic-motion threshold on the high side. The claimed enhancement of the trapped population relative to the interstellar flux (up to two orders of magnitude) is explicitly described as a "quasi-stable" trapping, not a permanent one, and depends on assumptions about the diffusion coefficient and the steady-state balance between injection and losses.

## Method
The authors applied Stormer theory to a dipole approximation of the geomagnetic field to determine the rigidity conditions for access and stable trapping of strangelets. They modeled the ionization state of incoming strangelets using a Bohr-atom approximation for collisions with electrons in the interstellar medium, concluding that full ionization is a good approximation for the relevant energy range. A radial diffusion equation driven by magnetic field fluctuations (third-invariant diffusion) was solved to obtain the steady-state distribution function, with boundary conditions set by the interstellar flux at L=6 and a sink at L≈1.05 due to atmospheric collisions.

## Key numbers
- The net positive charge of normal strangelets is approximately Z = 0.1 A (low baryon number regime) and for CFL strangelets Z = 0.3 A^(2/3), using a strange quark mass of 150 MeV and a CFL gap of 100 MeV.
- The mean free path for an electronic collision in the interstellar medium (density 1 particle/cm^3) is of order 10^15 cm.
- The geomagnetic cutoff rigidity is given by R > 59.6 cos^4(λ) / (L^2 [1 + (1 - cosγ cos^3λ)^(1/2)]^2) GV.
- For normal strangelets of A=100 at rigidity R=1 GV, the trapped flux at L=1.3 is 1.28 × 10^(-15) part cm^(-2) s^(-1) sr^(-1) (MeV/A)^(-1) (standard flux) and 3.83 × 10^(-14) part cm^(-2) s^(-1) sr^(-1) (MeV/A)^(-1) (improved flux).
- For CFL strangelets of A=100 at R=1 GV, the trapped flux at L=1.3 is 3.95 × 10^(-14) part cm^(-2) s^(-1) sr^(-1) (MeV/A)^(-1) (standard flux) and 1.64 × 10^(-13) part cm^(-2) s^(-1) sr^(-1) (MeV/A)^(-1) (improved flux).
- The enhancement factor for trapped flux relative to the interstellar flux is about 10 at L=2 and about 100 at L=1.3 for normal strangelets, and about twice those values for CFL strangelets.

## Context
This work extends the idea of a trapped strangelet population first suggested by Rosu (no year given in text) and is analogous to the trapping of anomalous cosmic ray (ACR) nuclei in the Earth's magnetosphere, as studied by Blake (no year given) and Biswas (no year given). The calculations rely on the interstellar strangelet flux estimated by Madsen (2005) for a binary strange star coalescence scenario. The results enable future searches for strangelets with experiments such as AMS-02, particularly during solar maximum when the ACR background is reduced, and suggest that the peak of the trapped distribution near L=1.3 is a robust target for observation.
