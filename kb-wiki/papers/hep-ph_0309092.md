# Can low-lying Roper states be explained as antidecuplet members?

## Key claim
The anomalously low-lying Roper states cannot be assigned as pentaquarks with the scalar diquark–scalar diquark–antiquark structure suggested by Jaffe and Wilczek (2003), because the Delta(1600) Roper state cannot be constructed from two strongly clustered scalar diquarks and an antiquark, making it unlikely that the other Roper states share such a structure.

## Method
The author examines the quantum numbers and properties of the known Roper states (N(1440), Delta(1600), Lambda(1600), Sigma(1660)) and tests whether they can be accommodated within the pentaquark antidecuplet and octet scheme proposed by Jaffe and Wilczek (2003). The analysis compares the broad widths and mass splittings of the Roper states with the narrow Theta+(1540) and checks whether all members of the Roper family can be constructed from the proposed diquark-diquark-antiquark configuration.

## Key numbers
- N(1440), Delta(1600), Lambda(1600), Sigma(1660) are the Roper states discussed, all lying approximately 0.5 GeV above their respective ground states (N, Delta, Lambda, Sigma).
- Theta+(1540) is the antidecuplet pentaquark candidate discovered experimentally by the LEPS Collaboration (2003).
- The nonstrange analog of Theta+ would be approximately 100 MeV below Theta+ if the mass difference is due to the strange quark mass.

## Context
This work directly challenges the pentaquark interpretation of Roper states proposed by Jaffe and Wilczek (2003) and independently by Nussinov (2003). The author argues that the Delta(1600) state, which is well established in pion-nucleon scattering and electroexcitation at Jefferson Laboratory (Weygand, 2003), cannot be constructed from the proposed diquark-diquark-antiquark structure, implying that the admixture of 5Q antidecuplet and 5Q octet components in the other Roper states should be at most small. The paper instead supports the explanation of Roper states as 3Q valence wave functions with flavor-spin dependent interactions of the Goldstone boson exchange type, as developed by Glozman and Riska (1996). The open question remains what the nonstrange analog of Theta+ actually is, with N(1710) suggested as a candidate by Diakonov, Petrov, and Polyakov (1997), though this state is also well described as a second nucleon excitation.
