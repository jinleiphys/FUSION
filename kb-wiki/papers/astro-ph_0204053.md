# Microlensing Neutron Stars

> We investigate the chances that neutron stars act as the lense in a gravitational microlensing event towards the galactic bulge or a spiral arm. The observation of neutron stars by means of gravitational microlensing would allow the estimation of neutron star masses independently of the property of being a pulsar in a binary system. We estimate the contribution of neutron stars to the optical depth and the lensing rate based on two different models of the pulsar distribution in the galaxy. Since only a small fraction of all neutron stars are pulsars, it is unlikely to find a pulsar that acts as a microlense by chance. A position comparison of known radio pulsars with observed microlensing candidates towards the galactic bulge and spiral arms shows no candidate pair, which is consistent with the theoretical expectation. To improve the probability of microlensing a pulsar, we suggest to search for gravitational microlensing events of known nearby high proper motion pulsars. The pulsar PSR J1932+1059 is a good candidate for an astrometric detection of gravitational lensing.

# Microlensing Neutron Stars

## Key claim

Neutron stars can in principle be detected as gravitational microlenses using present-day photometric techniques, but the expected rate is so low (about 10⁻⁷ per year per monitored source toward the galactic bulge) that only a handful of the roughly 800 observed microlensing candidates are likely due to neutron stars, and none of those have been identified as such. The authors propose that astrometric microlensing of known nearby high proper motion pulsars offers a more promising path to measuring isolated neutron star masses, with the pulsar PSR J1932+1059 identified as a good candidate.

## Method

The authors estimate the photometric microlensing rate and optical depth for neutron stars using two models of the galactic pulsar distribution from Hartman et al., assuming the neutron star number density is proportional to the pulsar number density scaled by the ratio of total neutron stars (10⁹) to total pulsars (10⁵). They compare the positions of 690 microlensing candidates from the OGLE, MACHO, and EROS surveys with 1308 radio pulsars from the ATNF catalogue to search for coincidences, and then identify high proper motion pulsars from the Princeton Pulsar Catalogue as targets for astrometric microlensing searches.

- Estimated photometric microlensing rate for neutron stars: ~10⁻⁷ yr⁻¹ per monitored source toward the galactic bulge
- Estimated photometric microlensing rate for pulsars: ~10⁻¹¹ yr⁻¹ per monitored source
- Optical depth for neutron star microlensing toward the galactic bulge: ~10⁻⁸
- Optical depth for microlensing toward the galactic bulge from observations: τ = (1.4 ± 0.3) × 10⁻⁶ (Popowski et al.)

- Number of microlensing candidates compared: 690 (OGLE 520 bulge, MACHO 144 bulge, EROS 19 bulge + 7 spiral arms)
- Number of radio pulsars compared: 1308

## Context

This work extends the study of gravitational microlensing as a tool for measuring stellar masses, specifically addressing the problem that neutron star masses have only been measured in binary systems (Thorsett and Chakrabarty, 1999), yielding a narrow range of 1.35 ± 0.04 M☉. The authors build on the pulsar population models of Hartman et al., Narayan, and Johnston, and on the microlensing surveys of OGLE, MACHO, and EROS. The proposed strategy of astrometric microlensing of known high proper motion pulsars complements the work of Gould (2000) on identifying neutron stars via astrometric microlensing with upcoming space missions like GAIA and SIM, and could enable mass measurements of isolated neutron stars that are not in binary systems.
