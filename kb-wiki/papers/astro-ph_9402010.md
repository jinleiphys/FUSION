# Refined Big Bang Nucleosynthesis Constraints on $Ω_{Baryon}$ and $N_ν$

> We include correlations between elemental abundances in a Monte Carlo statistical analysis of BBN predictions, which, along with updated reaction rates and an improved BBN code, lead to tightened constraints on $Ω_{B}$ and $N_ν$. Observational upper limits on the primordial \he and $\rm {D + {^3He}}$ fractions of $24 \%$ (by weight) and $ 10^{-4}$ respectively lead to the limits: $0.015 \le Ω_{B} \le 0.070$, and $N_ν \le 3.04$. The former limit appears to be incompatible with purely baryonic galactic halo dark matter, while the latter puts qualitatively new constraints on neutrinos, and physics beyond the standard model.

# Refined Big Bang Nucleosynthesis Constraints on $\Omega_{Baryon}$ and $N_\nu$

## Key claim

The authors claim that including correlations between elemental abundances in a Monte Carlo analysis, together with updated reaction rates and an improved BBN code, tightens constraints on $\Omega_B$ and $N_\nu$ beyond previous analyses; however, they explicitly qualify that the 95% confidence limits are only as reliable as the observational upper limits on primordial abundances, which are dominated by systematic rather than statistical uncertainties.

## Method

The authors performed a Monte Carlo statistical analysis of Big Bang Nucleosynthesis predictions using an improved version of the Kawano BBN code, incorporating updated reaction rates (including a new neutron half-life of $889 \pm 2.1$ sec and a 20% reduction in the $^7Be + p \rightarrow \gamma + ^8B$ rate), small corrections to the predicted $^4He$ mass fraction (totaling approximately +0.003 relative to previous analyses), and temperature-dependent uncertainties for the $^3He(\alpha,\gamma)^7Be$ and $^3H(\alpha,\gamma)^7Li$ reactions. They determined joint 95% confidence limits by requiring that at least 50 out of 1000 Monte Carlo models lie within the region bounded simultaneously by both the $^4He$ and D+$^3He$ observational upper limits, thereby accounting for the strong anti-correlation between these abundances.

## Key numbers

- Neutron half-life: $\tau_N = 889 \pm 2.1$ sec (world average, uncertainty almost twice as small as in previous BBN analyses)
- Observational upper limits used: primordial $^4He$ fraction $\le 24\%$ (by weight) and D+$^3He$ fraction $\le 10^{-4}$
- Resulting constraints: $0.015 \le \Omega_B \le 0.070$ (derived assuming $0.4 \le h \le 0.8$)
- Limit on effective neutrino species: $N_\nu \le 3.04$ (greater than 3.04 ruled out only when correlations are taken into account)
- Total change in predicted $Y_p$ (helium mass fraction) from previous analyses: approximately +0.003, composed of a +0.0017 change from finer integration of nuclear abundances, approximately +0.0012 from nucleon recoil and weak magnetism effects (Seckel correction), and +0.0002 from momentum-dependent neutrino decoupling
- Width of 95% C.L. band for $Y_p$: approximately 0.002
- Upper limit on $\eta_{10}$ from $^7Li$ alone: $\eta_{10} \le 5.27$ (using $^7Li/H \le 2.3 \times 10^{-10}$); this weakens to $\eta_{10} \le 4.15$ if the $^7Li$ upper limit is changed to $1.4 \times 10^{-10}$
- Allowed range for primordial $^7Li$ derived from the $\eta_{10}$ bounds: $9 \times 10^{-11}$ to $1.5 \times 10^{-10}$

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo approach first introduced by Krauss and Romanelli (1990) and refined by Smith et al. (1993), incorporating for the first time the full covariance between predicted elemental abundances rather than using symmetric single-element confidence limits. The authors find that accounting for the strong anti-correlation between $^4He$ and D+$^3He$ significantly tightens constraints, ruling out more than 3.04 effective neutrino species, a qualitatively stronger bound than the previous limit of 3.3 from Smith et al. (1993). The resulting upper limit on $\Omega_B$ (0.070) is claimed to be incompatible with purely baryonic galactic halo dark matter, and the $N_\nu$ bound rules out any Dirac neutrino mass without extensions to the standard model, as even a right-handed component freezing out above 300 GeV contributes 0.047 extra neutrinos. The authors caution that systematic errors in the observational abundance limits could broaden these constraints, and that an agnostic view is prudent pending further observational refinement.
