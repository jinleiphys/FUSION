# Dynamical study of spinodal decomposition in heavy ion collisions

> Nuclei undergo a phase transition in nuclear reactions according to a caloric curve determined by the amount of entropy. Here, the generation of entropy is studied in relation to the size of the nuclear system.

## Key claim
The transition temperature (plateau temperature \(T_p\)) in the caloric curve of nuclear fragmentation depends inversely on the size of the fragmenting system, and this dependence is linked to the entropy generated during the initial stage of the reaction. The authors confirm that lighter systems generate more entropy than heavier ones, but the underlying cause of this inverse relationship remains unexplained.

## Method
The authors used the molecular dynamics model LATINO combined with the fragment-recognition code ECRA to simulate central heavy-ion collisions (e.g., Ni+Ni and Ni+Ag) at beam energies between 600 and 3800 MeV. They tracked the density and temperature of the 50% most central particles over time, identified the residual fragment source size, and computed the entropy per particle using a classical gas formula. Caloric curves were then constructed for different source mass ranges.

## Key numbers
- Systems with masses between 30 and 60 nucleons have a plateau temperature \(T_p \approx 9\ \mathrm{MeV}\), while systems with masses between 180 and 240 nucleons yield \(T_p \approx 6\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (citing Natowitz (2002)).
- Simulated central collisions of Ni+Ni were performed at beam energies between 800 and 1600 MeV, producing residual systems with 49, 75, and 91 nucleons.
- Simulated central collisions of Ni+Ag were performed at beam energies between 1000 and 3800 MeV, producing residual sources grouped into mass ranges 40–59, 60–79, and 80–100 nucleons.
- Entropy was evaluated for central collisions of Ni+Ni at energies between 600 and 2000 MeV, plotted against the size of the residual fragmenting source.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies by Barrañón and López (2004) and others that linked the caloric curve plateau temperature to entropy generation in nuclear fragmentation. It confirms the inverse relationship between entropy and source size observed experimentally by Natowitz (2002) and in prior simulations. The results enable further investigation into the microscopic mechanisms that cause lighter systems to generate more entropy than heavier ones during heavy-ion collisions, a question the authors identify as unresolved.
