# Wigner's Sisters

> Paul A. M. Dirac was a great physicist. Wigner used to call him ``my famous brother-in-law''. How did they become brothers-in-law? Did these two great physicists have the same view toward physics?

# Wigner's Sisters

## Key claim
This paper does not present a scientific claim but rather offers a personal historical account of the relationship between Eugene Wigner and Paul A. M. Dirac, who became brothers-in-law when Wigner's younger sister Margit (Manci) married Dirac. The author suggests that despite their shared distaste for renormalization and a common goal of reconciling the uncertainty principle with special relativity, Dirac and Wigner had limited direct communication about physics, leaving a gap that younger physicists might profitably explore.

## Method
The author recounts personal conversations and interactions with Eugene Wigner during 1985–1990 and with Margit Dirac in 1978 and 1988, supplemented by historical anecdotes about how Wigner introduced his sister to Dirac at a Princeton restaurant in 1934. The paper also compares Dirac's 1949 paper on "Forms of Relativistic Dynamics" with Wigner's 1939 paper on the inhomogeneous Lorentz group, noting that Dirac's instant and front forms correspond to Wigner's O(3)-like and E(2)-like little groups for massive and massless particles respectively.

## Key numbers
- Dirac published eleven papers during the period 1939–1946.
- The author visited Wigner frequently during the period 1985–1990.
- The author met Mrs. Manci Dirac in 1978 in Miami (Florida) and again in the fall of 1988 at Princeton.
- The Hungarian Ambassador invited Wigner to his residence during the spring meeting of the American Physical Society in April 1988 held in Baltimore.
- The memorial service for Paul A. M. Dirac was held at Florida State University (Tallahassee) in November 1984.

## Context
This paper extends the personal and historical literature surrounding Eugene Wigner and Paul Dirac, complementing the biography "Recollections of Eugene P. Wigner as told by Andrew Szanton" (Szanton, 1992). It highlights the intellectual gap between Dirac's approach (seeking beautiful mathematics, using harmonic oscillators for relativistic models) and Wigner's approach (constructing representations of the Poincare group using quantum phase-space distribution functions), despite their shared skepticism toward renormalization. The author suggests that bridging these two perspectives could be a fruitful direction for young physicists, building on the author's own work with Marilyn Noz on the Poincare group (Kim and Noz, 1986).
