# Two-proton radioactivity within Coulomb and proximity potential model

> Considering the preformation probability of the two emitted protons in the parent nucleus, we extend the Coulomb and proximity potential model (CPPM) to systematically study two-proton (2p) radioactivity half-lives of the nuclei close to proton drip line. The proximity potential chosen is Prox. 81 proposed by Blocki et al. in 1981. Furthermore, we apply this model to predict the half-lives of possible 2p radioactive candidates whose 2p radioactivity is energetically allowed or observed but not yet quantified in the evaluated nuclear properties table NUBASE2016. The predicted results are in good agreement with those from other theoretical models and empirical formulas, namely the effective liquid drop model (ELDM), generalized liquid drop model (GLDM), Gamow-like model, Sreeja formula and Liu formula.

# Two-proton radioactivity within Coulomb and proximity potential model

## Key claim
The Coulomb and proximity potential model (CPPM), using the Prox.81 proximity potential and including a preformation probability for the two emitted protons, can reproduce experimental two-proton radioactivity half-lives for nuclei with 12 < Z < 36 to within about one order of magnitude for most cases, though deviations exist for certain nuclei such as ⁵⁴Zn (Q₂ₚ = 1.28 MeV) and ⁶⁷Kr that may indicate experimental inaccuracies or missing deformation effects.

## Method
The authors extended the CPPM, originally developed for cluster and alpha decay, to two-proton radioactivity by treating the emitted two protons as a ²He-like cluster penetrating a combined Coulomb, nuclear (Prox.81 proximity potential), and centrifugal barrier via the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) approximation. The preformation probability S₂ₚ was calculated using the cluster overlap approximation with a fitted parameter χ² = 0.0143, and the assault frequency was derived from the Nilsson harmonic oscillator formula. Half-lives were computed for five true two-proton emitters (¹⁹Mg, ⁴⁵Fe, ⁴⁸Ni, ⁵⁴Zn, ⁶⁷Kr) and predictions were made for eight additional candidates with Q₂ₚ > 0 and Qₚ < 0 from the AME2016 mass table.

## Key numbers
- For ¹⁹Mg (Q₂ₚ = 0.75 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -12.17 s, compared to experimental -11.40 s (Phys. Rev. Lett. 99, 182501)
- For ⁴⁵Fe (Q₂ₚ = 1.10 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -2.07 s, compared to experimental -2.40 s (Eur. Phys. J. A 14, 279)
- For ⁴⁵Fe (Q₂ₚ = 1.14 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -2.55 s, compared to experimental -2.07 s (Phys. Rev. Lett. 89, 102501)
- For ⁴⁵Fe (Q₂ₚ = 1.15 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -2.71 s, compared to experimental -2.55 s (Phys. Rev. C 72, 054315)
- For ⁴⁵Fe (Q₂ₚ = 1.21 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -3.33 s, compared to experimental -2.42 s (Eur. Phys. J. A 48, 179)
- For ⁴⁸Ni (Q₂ₚ = 1.29 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -2.41 s, compared to experimental -2.52 s (Phys. Rev. C 90, 014311)
- For ⁴⁸Ni (Q₂ₚ = 1.35 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -3.03 s, compared to experimental -2.08 s (Phys. Rev. C 72, 054315)
- For ⁵⁴Zn (Q₂ₚ = 1.28 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -0.71 s, compared to experimental -2.76 s (Phys. Rev. Lett. 107, 102502), largest deviation among all cases
- For ⁵⁴Zn (Q₂ₚ = 1.48 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -2.79 s, compared to experimental -2.43 s (Phys. Rev. Lett. 94, 232501)
- For ⁶⁷Kr (Q₂ₚ = 1.69 MeV): CPPM gives log₁₀T₁/₂ = -0.22 s, compared to experimental -1.70 s (Phys. Rev. Lett. 117, 162501)
- Predicted half-life for ²²Si (Q₂ₚ = 1.28 MeV): log₁₀T₁/₂ = -13.73 s (CPPM)
- Predicted half-life for ⁶⁴Se (Q₂ₚ = 0.46 MeV): log₁₀T₁/₂ = 15.04 s (CPPM), compared to 24.44 s from GLDM

## Context
This work extends the CPPM framework, previously applied to alpha decay (Santhosh et al., 2015; Phys. Rev. C 93, 024612) and proton radioactivity (Phys. Rev. C 96, 034619), to two-proton radioactivity for the first time. The results are compared with the effective liquid drop model (ELDM) from Phys. Lett. B 774, 14, the generalized liquid drop model (GLDM) from Phys. Rev. C 101, 014301, the Gamow-like model from Chin. Phys. C 45, 044110, and empirical formulas by Sreeja et al. (Eur. Phys. J. A 55, 33) and Liu et al. (Chin. Phys. C 45, 024108). The predicted half-lives for eight candidate nuclei (²²Si, ³⁴Ca, ³⁹Ti, ⁴²Cr, ⁴⁹Ni, ⁵⁵Zn, ⁶⁰Ge, ⁶⁴Se) provide theoretical guidance for future experiments, though the authors note that deformation effects (Goigoux et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 117, 162501) and three-body asymptotic behavior should be incorporated for more reliable predictions.
