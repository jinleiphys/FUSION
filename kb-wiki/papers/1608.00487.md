# Analysis of Neutron Stars Observations Using a Correlated Fermi Gas Model

> Background: The nuclear symmetry energy is a fundamental ingredient in determining the equation of state (EOS) of neutron stars (NS). Recent terrestrial experiments constrain both its value and slope at nuclear saturation density, however, its value at higher densities is unknown. Assuming a Free Fermi-gas (FFG) model for the kinetic symmetry energy, the high-density extrapolation depends on a single parameter, the density dependence of the potential symmetry energy. The Correlated Fermi-gas (CFG) model improves on the FFG model by including the effects of short-range, correlated, high-momentum, nucleons in nuclear matter. Using the CFG model for the kinetic symmetry energy along with constraints from terrestrial measurements leads to a much softer density dependence for the potential symmetry energy. Purpose: Examine the ability of the FFG and CFG models to describe NS observables that are directly sensitive to the symmetry energy at high-density. Specifically, examine the ability of the CFG model, with its softer density dependence of the potential symmetry energy, to describe a two solar-mass NS. Methods: Using a Bayesian analysis of NS observables we compare the CFG and FFG models and examine the resulting parameters in the NS EOS and the density dependence of the potential symmetry energy. Results: Despite the large difference in the density dependence of the potential part of the symmetry energy, both models can describe the NS data and support a two solar-mass NS. The different density dependences has only a small effect on the NS EOS. Conclusions: While sensitive to the high-density values of the symmetry energy, NS observables alone are not enough to distinguish between the CFG and FFG models. This indicates that the NS EOS, obtain from Bayesian analysis of NS observables, is robust and is not sensitive to the exact nuclear model used for the kinetic symmetry energy.

## Key claim

Despite the Correlated Fermi Gas (CFG) model producing a much softer density dependence for the potential symmetry energy than the Free Fermi Gas (FFG) model, both models can describe neutron star data and support a two solar-mass neutron star; neutron star observables alone are not enough to distinguish between the two models, indicating the neutron star equation of state is robust and insensitive to the exact nuclear model used for the kinetic symmetry energy.

## Method

The authors performed a Bayesian analysis of neutron star observables (including pulsar-timing mass extractions, photospheric radius expansion X-ray burst measurements, and thermal spectra from low-mass X-ray binaries) using both the CFG and FFG models for the kinetic symmetry energy. The neutron star equation of state was parameterized into three energy-density regions (low, medium, and high), with the medium region having a physically motivated functional form with fit parameters for incompressibility, skewness, and the density-dependent symmetry energy. The potential symmetry energy density dependence was parameterized as a single parameter gamma, and the analysis incorporated external constraints from terrestrial measurements and the observation of a two solar-mass neutron star.

## Key numbers

- The CFG kinetic symmetry energy at saturation density ranges from -2.5 to -17.5 MeV, while the FFG kinetic symmetry energy is either 12.5 or 17.0 MeV
- The total symmetry energy at saturation density is constrained to 29 MeV < E_sym(rho_0) < 36 MeV
- The slope parameter L = 3 rho * dE_sym/d rho at saturation density is constrained to 30 MeV < L < 70 MeV
- Nuclear saturation density rho_0 = 0.16 nucleons/fm^3 (approximately 160 MeV/fm^3)
- The CFG high-momentum tail parameters: C_infinity = c_0 * k_F where c_0 = 4.16 +/- 0.95, and lambda = 2.75 +/- 0.25
- 20 to 25% of nucleons in medium and heavy nuclei have momentum greater than the Fermi momentum
- Both models support a two solar-mass neutron star
- Both models are consistent with the empirical value of E/A = 16 MeV at saturation density

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian analysis approach of Steiner (2010) by replacing the Free Fermi Gas model for the kinetic symmetry energy with the Correlated Fermi Gas model developed by Hen (2015), which includes short-range correlated nucleon pairs. The results contradict the expectation that the softer CFG potential symmetry energy would fail to support massive neutron stars, and instead show the neutron star equation of state is robust to this choice. The authors suggest that future differentiation between models may come from relativistic mean-field calculations (Cai, 2015) or from heavy-ion collision transport models that incorporate short-range correlations.
