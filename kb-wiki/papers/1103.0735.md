# Bulk viscosity of low-temperature strongly interacting matter

> We study the bulk viscosity of a pion gas in unitarized Chiral Perturbation Theory at low and moderate temperatures, below any phase transition to a quark-gluon plasma phase. We argue that inelastic processes are irrelevant and exponentially suppressed at low temperatures. Since the system falls out of chemical equilibrium upon expansion,a pion chemical potential must be introduced, so we extend the existing theory to include it. We control the zero modes of the collision operator and Landau's conditions of fit when solving the Boltzmann equation with the elastic collision kernel. The dependence of the bulk viscosity with temperature is reminiscent of the findings of Fernandez-Fraile and Gomez Nicola, while the numerical value is closer to that of Davesne. In the zero-temperature limit we correctly recover the vanishing viscosity associated to a non-relativistic monoatomic gas.

## Key claim

The bulk viscosity of a low-temperature pion gas is dominated by elastic scattering processes, with inelastic processes exponentially suppressed, and a finite pion chemical potential must be introduced because elastic collisions conserve pion number; the numerical value of the bulk viscosity is closer to the earlier phase-shift-based result of Davesne (1995) than to the Chiral Perturbation Theory result of Fernandez-Fraile and Gomez Nicola (2008), though the temperature dependence is qualitatively similar to the latter.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Boltzmann equation for a pion gas using the elastic collision kernel only, employing the Inverse Amplitude Method (IAM) to construct the pion-pion scattering amplitude from unitarized Chiral Perturbation Theory. They introduce a polynomial basis for the perturbation function \(A_p\), correctly handle the zero modes of the collision operator (associated with particle-number and energy conservation), and fix the arbitrary coefficients via the two Landau-Lifschitz constraints (conditions of fit). The calculation is extended to include a finite pion chemical potential \(\mu\), which is required because elastic collisions conserve pion number.

## Key numbers

* Bulk viscosity \(\zeta\) of the pion gas shows a broad maximum at low temperatures, with \(\zeta = 0\) at zero temperature (consistent with a non-relativistic monoatomic gas).
* The ratio \(\zeta/s\) (bulk viscosity over entropy density) in the pion gas is monotonously decreasing as a function of temperature.
* The asymptotic high-temperature (quark-gluon plasma) estimate for \(\zeta/s\) is given by \(\zeta/s = 2 \cdot 10^{-4} \, g^4 / \log(6.344/g)\), with the running coupling \(g^{-2}(T) = (9/8\pi^2) \log(T / 30\ \mathrm{MeV}) + (4/9\pi^2) \log(2 \log(T / 30\ \mathrm{MeV}))\).
* The calculation is valid up to temperatures in the range 120–140 MeV; results are plotted up to slightly higher temperatures for archival comparison.
* Increasing the chemical potential \(\mu\) generally yields a larger viscosity; the natural end-value is \(\mu = m_\pi\), corresponding to chemical equilibrium.
* The peak in \(\zeta\) at moderate temperature is sensitive to the pion mass: increasing the pion mass decreases the bulk viscosity and shifts the peak to higher temperatures; increasing \(\mu\) reduces the peak, which disappears at \(\mu = m_\pi\).

## Context

This work reconciles contradictory prior results for the bulk viscosity of a low-temperature pion gas: the Chiral Perturbation Theory computation of Fernandez-Fraile and Gomez Nicola (2008) showed a clear maximum associated with the pion mass, while earlier studies by Davesne (1995) and Prakash (1993) based on empirical phase-shifts found different numerical values and no such peak. The present calculation adopts the best features of both approaches (unitarized ChPT with physical phase-shifts, correct zero-mode treatment, and Landau-Lifschitz constraints) and extends the theory to include a finite pion chemical potential for the first time. This enables more realistic modeling of the expanding pion gas in heavy-ion collisions, where the system falls out of chemical equilibrium; the authors propose an experimental observable for bulk viscosity in a separate publication (Dobado, 2011).
