# Response theory for quantum fields in isolation

> Response theory describes the reaction of observales to perturbations in external fields. We review this formalism for quantum fiels in isolation that have unitary time evolution. An emphasis is put on consequences of causality and the resulting spectral representations for linear and nonlinear response functions, on functional techniques and generating functionals, including the description of the initial state, the evolution, and measurements. We review consequences of time reversal symmetry and relations for the statistics of work, and discuss a large class of quantum correlation functions, and their relation to response functions through fluctuation-dissipation relations. Consequences of conservation laws and gauge symmetries are mentioned briefly.

## Key claim

This review formalizes response theory for quantum fields in isolation (unitary time evolution) around global thermal equilibrium, emphasizing that the formalism is valid for both weakly and strongly interacting theories and can be applied to relativistic or non-relativistic systems, but the expansion is explicitly performed around a grand-canonical equilibrium state with a timelike Killing vector field, so the results are conditional on the existence of such a stationary background.

## Method

The paper develops linear and nonlinear response theory using both Hamiltonian operator formalism and Lagrangian/action-based functional integral techniques, expanding the Hamiltonian or action in powers of time-dependent external source fields around a fixed initial equilibrium configuration. It derives spectral representations for response functions from causality, introduces generating functionals that incorporate the initial state, time evolution, and measurements, and connects response functions to quantum correlation functions through fluctuation-dissipation relations. The formalism is applied to a complex scalar field coupled to external gauge fields and metric as an explicit example.

## Key numbers

- The initial thermal density matrix is given by \(\rho_\text{i} = \frac{1}{Z(\beta, \mu, j_\text{i})} \exp\left( - \beta (H(j_\text{i}) - \mu N)\right)\) with inverse temperature \(\beta = 1/T\) and chemical potential \(\mu\).
- The isothermal static susceptibility is \(\chi^{(T, \mu)}_{mn} = T \frac{\partial^2}{\partial j^n \partial j^m} \ln Z(\beta, \alpha, j) {\big |}_{j=j_\text{i}} = - \frac{\partial^2}{\partial j^n \partial j^m} \Omega(T, \mu, j) {\big |}_{j=j_\text{i}}\).
- The isothermal susceptibility expressed via operators is \(\chi^{(T, \mu)}_{mn} = \beta \langle \phi_m \,;\, \phi_n \rangle_c + \langle \phi_{mn} \rangle\), where \(\langle \phi_m \,;\, \phi_n \rangle_c\) is the connected Bogoliubov-Kubo-Mori inner product.
- The Euclidean action for thermal states is \( - S_\text{E}[\chi, j] = i S[\chi, j] = i \int_{t_\text{i}}^{t_\text{i}-i\beta} dt L(\chi(t), \tfrac{\partial}{\partial t} \chi(t), j(t))\), with imaginary time integration from \(0\) to \(\beta\).

## Context

This review extends the long tradition of response theory from the works of Kubo (1950s), Callen and Welton (1951), and Onsager (1929-1931) to quantum field theories in isolation, covering both linear and nonlinear response. It sets the stage for non-perturbative quantum field theory developments using functional techniques, and refines the connection between response functions and quantum correlation functions, including a weak measurement scheme for intermediate and final times. The formalism is intended for applications in high-energy nuclear collisions, astrophysics, cosmology, and condensed matter experiments, building on prior work by Kapusta (2007), Calzetta and Hu (2009), Altland (2010), Bellac (2011), and Laine (2016).
