# Isobar Model for Photoproduction of K+Sigma0 and K0Sigma+ on the Proton

> Kaon photoproductions on the proton gamma p --> K+Sigma0 and gamma p --> K0Sigma+ have been simultaneously analyzed by using isobar models and new SAPHIR data. The result shows that isobar models such as KAON MAID require more resonances in order to explain the data.

## Key claim
The paper shows that the existing isobar model KAON MAID cannot reproduce new SAPHIR data for kaon photoproduction, and that including additional resonances (specifically a second S11 resonance) significantly improves the fit, though the authors caution that the data do not exhibit a clear resonance structure and arbitrary inclusion of new resonances is not advocated.

## Method
The authors simultaneously analyzed the reactions γp → K+Σ0 and γp → K0Σ+ using an isobar model based on KAON MAID, which includes Born terms, K* and K1 exchanges, and five nucleon resonances (S11(1650), P11(1710), P13(1720), S31(1900), P31(1910)). They performed three fitting steps: first refitting only coupling constants to new SAPHIR data, then allowing resonance masses and widths to vary, and finally introducing a second S11 resonance with free mass and width.

## Key numbers
- χ²/N for the original KAON MAID fit: 4.14
- χ²/N after refitting coupling constants only (Model 1): 2.44
- χ²/N after adding a second S11 resonance (Model 2): 1.76
- In Model 1, the S11(1650) mass shifted to 2167 MeV and P13(1720) mass to 2133 MeV
- In Model 2, two S11 resonances were obtained with masses 1795 MeV and 2112 MeV, with widths 158 MeV and 400 MeV respectively
- A significant discrepancy between KAON MAID and new data exists at center-of-mass energy W ≈ 2.1 GeV for the K+Σ0 channel

## Context
This work extends previous isobar model studies by Mart and collaborators (1999, 2000) and the KAON MAID model, which were fitted to older SAPHIR data. The new SAPHIR data from Lawall (2005) and Glan03b provide tighter constraints, and the results support earlier multipole studies by Mart (2004) suggesting the need for additional resonances. The findings are relevant to the "missing resonance" problem predicted by quark models (Capstick, 1994, 1998) and motivate future coupled-channels studies to confirm whether the required new resonances correspond to these predicted states.
