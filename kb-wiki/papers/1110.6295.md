# Distribution of neutron resonance widths

> Recent data on neutron resonance widths indicate disagreement with the Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD). I discuss the theoretical arguments for the PTD, possible theoretical modifications, and I summarize the experimantal evidence.

# Distribution of Neutron Resonance Widths

## Key claim
The Porter-Thomas distribution (PTD) for neutron resonance widths, long considered a standard prediction of random-matrix theory, may be rejected for the Pt isotopes at 99.997% significance and for the Nuclear Data Ensemble at 98.17% significance, but contradictory evidence from other tests (proton decay amplitudes, eigenvalue-eigenvector independence) supports the PTD, leaving the theoretical and experimental situation unresolved.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical basis for the PTD from the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) of random-matrix theory, examines modifications due to channel coupling strength, secular energy dependence of widths from single-particle states near threshold, and correlations between target and compound-nucleus eigenfunctions in shell-model calculations. It summarizes experimental tests including maximum-likelihood fits of the χ² distribution parameter ν to neutron width data from the Pt isotopes and the Nuclear Data Ensemble, as well as correlation-coefficient tests for proton decay amplitudes.

## Key numbers
- Mean spacing of isolated neutron resonances: about 10 eV; average total width: about 1 eV (for slow neutrons below 10 keV on medium-weight and heavy nuclei)
- Resonances occur at about 6 to 8 MeV excitation energy in the compound nucleus
- For 12 nucleons in the sd-shell (J=2, T=0, N=3276 states), shell-model information entropy approaches the GOE value only in the center of the spectrum
- Typical non-diagonal elements of the residual interaction in sd-shell nuclei: several 100 keV, comparable to spacing of single-particle states
- For 9 nuclei with A > 150 and more than 45 resonances each, error-weighted average ν = 0.98 ± 0.10 (consistent with PTD)
- For Pt isotopes (450 resonances): ν ≈ 0.6, PTD rejected at 99.997% significance
- For Nuclear Data Ensemble (1245 partial neutron widths): ν = 1.217 ± 0.092, PTD rejected at 98.17% significance
- Single-particle s-wave state within 5 keV from threshold would call entire R-matrix analysis into question

## Context
This work extends the foundational random-matrix theory of Wigner and the Porter-Thomas distribution tested by Bohigas (1983) and Haq (1982) on the Nuclear Data Ensemble. It directly addresses the recent experimental challenges from Koehler (2010) on Pt isotopes and Koehler (2011) on the re-analyzed Nuclear Data Ensemble. The paper enables a concerted re-analysis of all existing neutron resonance data using identical statistical criteria, and calls for new data to resolve the contradictory evidence between neutron width distributions, proton decay amplitude tests (Shriner 1987, 1989), and eigenvalue-eigenvector independence tests (Bohigas 1983, Lombardi 1994).
