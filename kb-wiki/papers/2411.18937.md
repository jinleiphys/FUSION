# Heavy quarkonium dissociation in the presence of magnetic field and anisotropy using dissociation energy criterion

> In this article, we have studied the dissociation temperature of 1S and 2S states of heavy quarkonium in the presence of anisotropy and a strong magnetic field background using the dissociation energy criterion. We utilized the medium-modified form of the Cornell potential, which depends on temperature as well as the anisotropic parameter ξ and the magnetic field. The binding energy (B.E.) and dissociation energy (D.E.) of heavy quarkonium have been examined for different values of the magnetic field and anisotropy. It is noted that B.E. starts decreasing from higher values as we increase the anisotropy, while D.E. exhibits the opposite behavior. The dissociation temperature appears to increase with anisotropy, while it decreases with the magnetic field, as shown in Table 1 and 2 respectively. These results align well with recent research findings.

## Key claim
The dissociation temperature of heavy quarkonium (1S and 2S states) increases with the anisotropy parameter ξ but decreases with the magnetic field strength eB, when determined using the dissociation energy criterion. The abstract's claim that results "align well with recent research findings" glosses over the fact that the dissociation temperatures obtained at eB = 0.3 GeV² and ξ = 0.3 fall between the lower and upper bounds of dissociation temperatures reported in a prior study by Lal (2023), rather than matching a single value.

## Method
The authors solved the radial Schrödinger equation using the real part of a medium-modified Cornell potential that depends on temperature T, the anisotropy parameter ξ, and the magnetic field eB. The Debye screening mass was computed within the Effective Quasi-Particle Model (EQPM) including a magnetic field background, using a two-loop running coupling constant with QCD scale λ_T = 197 MeV. Binding energy (B.E.) and dissociation energy (D.E.) were calculated for the 1S (J/ψ, Υ) and 2S (ψ', Υ') states, and the dissociation temperature was extracted from the intersection point of the B.E. and D.E. curves as a function of T/T_c.

## Key numbers
- For J/ψ at fixed eB = 0.3 GeV²: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 2.01 at ξ = 0.3, 2.08 at ξ = 0.5, and 2.13 at ξ = 0.7 (Table 1).
- For ψ' at fixed eB = 0.3 GeV²: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 1.44 at ξ = 0.3, 1.50 at ξ = 0.5, and 1.55 at ξ = 0.7 (Table 1).
- For Υ at fixed eB = 0.3 GeV²: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 2.15 at ξ = 0.3, 2.21 at ξ = 0.5, and 2.26 at ξ = 0.7 (Table 1).
- For Υ' at fixed eB = 0.3 GeV²: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 1.64 at ξ = 0.3, 1.69 at ξ = 0.5, and 1.74 at ξ = 0.7 (Table 1).
- For J/ψ at fixed ξ = 0.2: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 2.21 at eB = 0.1 GeV², 2.10 at eB = 0.2 GeV², and 1.97 at eB = 0.3 GeV² (Table 2).
- For ψ' at fixed ξ = 0.2: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 1.73 at eB = 0.1 GeV², 1.58 at eB = 0.2 GeV², and 1.40 at eB = 0.3 GeV² (Table 2).
- For Υ at fixed ξ = 0.2: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 2.23 at eB = 0.1 GeV², 2.12 at eB = 0.2 GeV², and 2.00 at eB = 0.3 GeV² (Table 2).
- For Υ' at fixed ξ = 0.2: dissociation temperature T_d/T_c = 1.76 at eB = 0.1 GeV², 1.61 at eB = 0.2 GeV², and 1.45 at eB = 0.3 GeV² (Table 2).
- Heavy quark masses used: m_q = 1.5 GeV for J/ψ and ψ', m_q = 4.5 GeV for Υ and Υ'.
- String tension σ = 0.224 GeV²; critical temperature T_c = 197 MeV.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of heavy quarkonium dissociation in anisotropic QGP by Solanki (PRC) and Lal (2023), who used thermal width and thermal energy criteria, by applying the dissociation energy criterion instead. The results are compared with holographic QCD models by Fadafan (2024), which also predict that increasing the anisotropic parameter raises the dissociation temperature, and with studies by Zhou (2020) and Chen (2022) showing that dissociation temperature decreases with magnetic field. The findings enable future investigations of quarkonium survival probability at different center-of-mass energies and applications to neutron star physics in the Compressed Baryonic Matter (CBM) experiment at FAIR.
