# Global Properties of Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions

> In this lecture note, we discuss the global properties of nucleus-nucleus collisions. After a brief introduction to heavy-ion collisions, we introduce useful kinematics and then discuss the bulk hadron production in A+A collisions. At the end we discuss the hadronization and hadronic freeze-out in A+A collisions. We have tried to cover the topic from very fundamental arguments especially for the beginners in the field. We also give very useful formulae frequently used by experimentalists, from a first principle derivation.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the bulk properties of nucleus-nucleus (A+A) collisions at high energies can be understood through a universal mechanism of parton redistribution that is already established during the primordial, non-equilibrium phase of the collision, with the observed scaling of particle production with the number of participant nucleons ($N_{part}$) resembling $e^+e^-$ annihilation rather than $pp$ collisions, but they hedge that this similarity holds only semi-quantitatively and that the Bjorken energy density estimates depend critically on the assumed formation time $\tau_0$, which may be shorter at RHIC than the conservative 1 fm/c used.

## Method

The authors review global observables of bulk hadron production in A+A collisions, including total transverse energy ($E_T$), charged particle multiplicity, and rapidity distributions, using data from SPS (Pb+Pb at $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV) and RHIC (Au+Au at $\sqrt{s}=130$ and 200 GeV). They apply the Bjorken model to estimate initial energy densities from measured $dE_T/dy$ values, compare these to lattice QCD results, and analyze the scaling of particle yields with $N_{part}$ determined via Glauber model Monte Carlo simulations and forward calorimeter measurements. The rapidity distributions of charged hadrons and net protons are compared across collision systems ($pp$, $p\overline{p}$, $e^+e^-$, and A+A) to infer stopping mechanisms and the approach to boost invariance.

## Key numbers

- At top SPS energy (Pb+Pb, $\sqrt{s}=17.3$ GeV), the total transverse energy of central collisions is about 1.2 TeV, with $dE_T/d\eta (mid)=400$ GeV per unit pseudo-rapidity.
- The Bjorken energy density estimate at SPS is $\epsilon (\tau_0=1fm)=3.0 \pm 0.6$ GeV/fm$^3$, exceeding the lattice QCD critical density of 0.6-1.0 GeV/fm$^3$.
- At top RHIC energy (Au+Au, $\sqrt{s}=200$ GeV), the Bjorken estimate grows to $\epsilon \approx 6.0 \pm 1$ GeV/fm$^3$ assuming $\tau_0=1$ fm/c.
- The average number of produced particles at SPS energies is $\approx 1600$, while at RHIC multiplicities of $\approx 4000$ are reached.
- The nuclear modification factor for bulk hadron rapidity distributions at top RHIC energy is $R^{AA}=1.5$ for $|\eta|<1$, and $R^{AA}=1.36$ for the pseudo-rapidity integrated total $N^{ch}$ in central Au+Au collisions.
- The average rapidity shift of net protons at SPS energy is $\left<\delta y\right>=1.7$, corresponding to $\left<\delta y\right>=0.58 y_p$.
- The initial temperature estimates range from 190 $\le T^{SPS} \le 220$ MeV, 220 $\le T^{RHIC} \le 400$ MeV (assuming $\tau_0 \approx 0.3$ fm/c at RHIC), and $T^{LHC} \ge 600$ MeV.
- The resolution for impact parameter determination via $N_{part}$ measurement at A=200 is about 1.5 fm.

## Context

This lecture note reviews and synthesizes the global properties of nucleus-nucleus collisions, extending the Bjorken model (Bjorken, 1983) for energy density estimates and comparing with lattice QCD results for the critical energy density. The work contrasts the observed scaling of particle production with $N_{part}$ in A+A collisions (which resembles $e^+e^-$ annihilation data) against the 40% suppression seen in $pp$ and $p\overline{p}$ collisions, and discusses the Landau hydrodynamical model (Landau, 1953) as an extreme stopping scenario that fails at higher energies. The findings enable further study of the quark-gluon plasma phase at RHIC and LHC energies, and point to saturation phenomena described by the color glass condensate model (McLerran and Venugopalan, 1994) as controlling the total inelasticity, with the scaling of total particle production going as $A^{1.08}$ rather than $A^{4/3}$.
