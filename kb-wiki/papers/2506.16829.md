# Parton Distributions on a Quantum Computer

> We perform the first quantum computation of parton distribution function (PDF) with a real quantum device by calculating the PDF of the lightest positronium in the Schwinger model with IBM quantum computers. The calculation uses 10 qubits for staggered fermions at five spatial sites and one ancillary qubit. The most critical and challenging step is to reduce the number of two-qubit gate depths to around 500 so that sensible results start to emerge. The resulting lightcone correlators have excellent agreement with the classical simulator result in central values, although the error is still large. Compared with classical approaches, quantum computation has the advantage of not being limited in the accessible range of parton momentum fraction $x$ due to renormalon ambiguity, and the difficulty of accessing non-valence partons. A PDF calculation with 3+1 dimensional QCD near $x=0$ or $x=1$ will be a clear demonstration of the quantum advantage on a problem with great scientific impact.

# Parton Distributions on a Quantum Computer

## Key claim

The authors perform the first quantum computation of a parton distribution function (PDF) on a real quantum device, but the results only become sensible after reducing the two-qubit gate depth to approximately 500, and the errors remain large despite excellent agreement with classical simulators in central values. The quantum approach avoids the renormalon ambiguity that limits classical lattice QCD near x=0 and x=1, but this advantage has not yet been demonstrated for QCD in 3+1 dimensions.

## Method

The authors compute the PDF of the lightest positronium state in 1+1 dimensional QED (Schwinger model) using IBM Eagle R3 quantum computers with 10 qubits for staggered fermions at five spatial sites plus one ancillary qubit. They prepare approximate zero-momentum eigenstates using wave packets with 65.1% overlap with the exact eigenstate, implement the lightcone correlator operator with zigzag Wilson lines and second-order Trotterization with Trotter step = 1, and apply error mitigation through dynamical decoupling, Pauli twirling with randomization=100, and Operator Decoherence Renormalization.

## Key numbers

- Two-qubit gate depth reduced from approximately 5,000 to approximately 500 through state preparation, operator choice, and Trotterization steps
- Wave packet overlap with exact eigenstate: 65.1%, reducing two-qubit gate depth from 1751 to 112 for state preparation
- Lattice parameters: e a = 0.3, m a = 0.5, with lattice size 2La = 10a (5 physical spatial sites)
- Positronium mass: M a = 1.617 ± 0.002 from classical simulator
- Momentum sum rule integral: ∫_{-1}^{1} x f(x) dx = 1.04^{+0.66}_{-0.76}, with central value 4% above the expected value of 1
- Number of fermion anti-fermion pairs: ∫_{0}^{1} f(x) dx = 1.37^{+0.89}_{-1.02}
- Two-qubit gate depths for z values: {529, 367, 112, 319, 615} for z = {-4, -2, 0, 2, 4} respectively

## Context

This work extends the program of computing parton distributions using quantum computers, building on the Hamiltonian formulation of QED₂ with staggered fermions from Sala (2018) and Savage (2025), and the lightcone operator construction from Li (2021). It contrasts with classical lattice QCD approaches such as Large-Momentum Effective Theory (LaMET) from Ji (2013, 2014), which suffers from IR renormalon ambiguity near the endpoints of x, and with the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model results from Li (2021) and Kang (2025) that gave fewer than one fermion pair in the positronium. The results enable future PDF calculations in 3+1 dimensional QCD on next-generation quantum computers, with the IBM roadmap suggesting Starling machines (2029) could support L~100 and Blue Jay machines (2033+) could support L~588.
