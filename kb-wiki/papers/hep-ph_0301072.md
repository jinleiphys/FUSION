# Global Analysis of Solar Neutrino and KamLAND Data

> A global analysis of the data from all the solar neutrino experiments combined with the recent KamLAND data is presented. A formula frequently used in the literature gives survival probability for three active solar neutrino flavors in terms of a suitably-modified two-flavor survival probability. Corrections to this formula, which depend on $θ_{13}$ and $δm_{31}^2$, are calculated. For the mass scale suggested by the atmospheric neutrino experiments the contributions of $δm_{31}^2$ to these corrections is found to be negligible. The role of $θ_{13}$ in solar neutrino physics is elaborated. For electron neutrino oscillations into another active flavor, we find best fit values of $\tan^2 θ_{12} \sim 0.46$, $\tan^2 θ_{13} \sim 0$, and $δm_{21}^2 \sim 7.1 \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$. It is found that the combined solar neutrino and KamLAND date provide the limit $\cos^4 θ_{13} < 0.8$ at the 90 % confidence level.

# Global Analysis of Solar Neutrino and KamLAND Data

## Key claim

The three-flavor electron neutrino survival probability can be accurately approximated by a two-flavor formula with a modified electron density $N_e \cos^2\theta_{13}$, plus small corrections proportional to $\xi = V_c(t=0)/\Delta_{31}$ that are negligible for solar neutrinos given the atmospheric mass scale; the combined solar and KamLAND data yield $\tan^2 \theta_{13} \sim 0$ and a limit $\cos^4 \theta_{13} > 0.8$ at 90% confidence, but this limit and the best-fit value depend on the assumption that $\delta m_{31}^2$ contributions to the correction terms are negligible.

## Method

The authors derived an analytic expression for the three-flavor electron neutrino survival probability in matter by performing sequential rotations of the MSW evolution equation, first removing the $\theta_{23}$ dependence via $T_{23}^\dagger$, then applying $T_{13}^\dagger$ and expanding in the small parameter $\xi = V_c(t=0)/\Delta_{31}$ under the assumption that $\delta m_{31}^2 \sim 3 \times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$ from atmospheric data. They performed a global $\chi^2$ analysis using 80 data points: the total rate from the chlorine experiment (Homestake), the average rate from gallium experiments (SAGE, GALLEX, GNO), 44 zenith-angle-spectrum bins from SuperKamiokande, and 34 day-night-spectrum bins from SNO, combined with KamLAND reactor neutrino data using a Poisson likelihood for 13 energy bins above threshold.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit mixing parameters for electron neutrino oscillations into another active flavor: $\tan^2 \theta_{12} \sim 0.46$, $\tan^2 \theta_{13} \sim 0$, and $\delta m_{21}^2 \sim 7.1 \times 10^{-5}$ eV$^2$
- Combined solar neutrino and KamLAND data provide the limit $\cos^4 \theta_{13} > 0.8$ at the 90% confidence level
- The atmospheric mass scale used is $\delta m_{32}^2 = 3 \times 10^{-3}$ eV$^2$ from SuperKamiokande observations
- The solar electron density profile is approximated as $N_e(r) = 245 \exp(-10.54 r/R_\odot) N_A$ cm$^{-3}$, giving $V_c(r) = 1.87 \times 10^{-5} \times \exp(-10.54 r/R_\odot)$ eV$^2$/MeV
- The KamLAND energy resolution is $7.5\%/\sqrt{E({\rm MeV})}$ and the systematic uncertainty is $\sigma_{\rm sys}^2 = 6.75\%$

## Context

This work extends the global analyses performed in summer 2002 by Bahcall (2002), Fogli (2002), Barger (2002), Maltoni (2002), Bandyopadhyay (2002), and others, which combined SNO and SuperKamiokande data with chlorine and gallium measurements to identify the large mixing angle (LMA) region. The paper incorporates the first KamLAND results announced in December 2002, confirming the LMA solution, and elaborates on the role of $\theta_{13}$ in solar neutrino physics, which was identified as the most pressing open question in neutrino physics at the time. The derived analytic formula with corrections proportional to $\xi$ enables future precise assessments of $\theta_{13}$ constraints as more neutrino data accumulate, and the treatment of CP violation in neutrino oscillations requires precise knowledge of $\theta_{13}$ as noted by Beavis (2002), Minakata (2002), and Akhmedov (2001).
