# Stellar Core Collapse with Hadron-Quark Phase Transition

> Hadronic matter undergoes a deconfinement transition to quark matter at high temperature and/or high density. It would be realized in collapsing cores of massive stars. In the framework of MIT bag model, the ambiguities of the interaction are encapsulated in the bag constant. Some progenitor stars that invoke the core collapses explode as supernovae, and other ones become black holes. The fates of core collapses are investigated for various cases. Equations of state including the hadron-quark phase transition are constructed for the cases of the bag constant B=90, 150 and 250 MeV fm^{-3}. To describe the mixed phase, the Gibbs condition is used. Adopting the equations of state with different bag constants, the core collapse simulations are performed for the progenitor models with 15 and 40Msolar. If the bag constant is small as B=90 MeV fm^{-3}, an interval between the bounce and black hole formation is shortened drastically for the model with 40Msolar and the second bounce revives the shock wave leading to explosion for the model with 15Msolar.

## Key claim

For a sufficiently small bag constant (B=90 MeV fm^{-3}), the hadron-quark phase transition can drastically shorten the time between bounce and black hole formation in a 40 solar mass progenitor, and can cause a second bounce that revives the shock wave leading to a successful supernova explosion in a 15 solar mass progenitor; these outcomes depend on the bag constant value and progenitor mass.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state (EOS) incorporating the hadron-quark phase transition using the MIT bag model for the quark phase and a relativistic mean field theory table from Shen (1998) for the hadronic phase, with the mixed phase described by the Gibbs condition. They performed general relativistic, spherically symmetric core-collapse simulations using an implicit Lagrangian hydrodynamics code that solves the neutrino Boltzmann equations, adopting progenitor models of 15 and 40 solar masses from Woosley (1995) and examining three values of the bag constant: B=90, 150, and 250 MeV fm^{-3}.

## Key numbers

- Maximum masses of cold compact stars for B=90, 150, and 250 MeV fm^{-3} are 1.54, 1.36, and 1.80 solar masses, respectively; the pure hadronic EOS maximum mass is 2.17 solar masses.
- For the 40 solar mass progenitor, intervals between bounce and black hole formation are 142.19 ms (B=90), 717.83 ms (B=150), 1085.52 ms (B=250), and 1345.40 ms (no quarks).
- For the 15 solar mass progenitor with B=90 MeV fm^{-3}, the second collapse starts at 200.54 ms after the first bounce, the second bounce occurs at 203.64 ms, and the estimated explosion energy is 1.54 x 10^{51} erg (including internal energy) or 1.7 x 10^{50} erg (kinetic plus gravitational only).
- Bounce density for the 40 solar mass model with B=90 MeV fm^{-3} is 6.9 x 10^{14} g cm^{-3}, while for other models it is 3.2 x 10^{14} g cm^{-3}.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Nakazato (2008, 2010) on the hadron-quark phase transition in core collapse, and independently confirms the scenario proposed by Sagert (2009) and Fischer (2011) that a second bounce from quark deconfinement can lead to supernova explosions, now for a different progenitor model (15 solar masses from Woosley 1995). The results show that the bag constant value critically determines whether the transition occurs early enough to affect the dynamics, and that the neutrino signal before black hole formation is not strongly modified by the phase transition. The models with quark matter cannot account for the 1.97 solar mass neutron star J1614-2230 (Demorest 2010), indicating that larger bag constants or more sophisticated quark models are needed.
