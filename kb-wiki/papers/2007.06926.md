# James Chadwick: ahead of his time

## Key claim

This biographical sketch argues that James Chadwick's earlier findings and ideas in the context of weak and strong nuclear forces, which are much less known than his discovery of the neutron, were often ahead of their time and paved the way for contemporary subatomic physics. The paper presents Chadwick's work as a series of prescient contributions that were only fully appreciated years later, with the hedge that many of these achievements remained contested or unrecognized for extended periods.

## Method

The paper reviews Chadwick's scientific career chronologically, drawing primarily on the biography by Brown (1997) and interviews recorded by Weiner. It examines his early work on gamma-ray absorption in gases, his 1914 demonstration that the beta spectrum is continuous using a Geiger counter rather than photographic plates, his scattering experiments with alpha particles on protons and light nuclei that revealed deviations from Coulomb's law, and his 1932 discovery of the neutron. The paper also covers his collaboration with Goldhaber on the precise measurement of the neutron mass and his later role in the Manhattan Project.

## Key numbers

- Chadwick measured gamma-ray absorption coefficients with uncertainties of only a few percent, establishing himself as an imaginative experimenter.
- In alpha-proton scattering, the measured numbers of scattered protons for fast alpha particles (high energies) exceeded expectations from Coulomb force alone by a factor of 100 in one case, with the angular distribution also differing.
- The deviation from Coulomb law for alpha-proton scattering occurred for distances d < 3.5 × 10^{-13} cm, as first reported by Rutherford (1919).
- The Joliot-Curies' interpretation of the penetrating radiation from beryllium as gamma rays would have required unrealistically high energy of 50 MeV.
- Chadwick and Goldhaber's 1934 experiment on deuteron photodisintegration produced a precise value for the neutron mass, showing that m_n > m_p + m_e and therefore the neutron cannot be a bound state of proton and electron.

## Context

This biographical sketch extends the historical accounts of Chadwick's life found in Brown (1997) and the interviews by Weiner. It highlights that Chadwick's 1914 discovery of the continuous beta spectrum was contested by Meitner for over a decade until Ellis and Wooster (1927) performed absolute calorimetric measurements, and that his 1921 work with Bieler on alpha-proton scattering was hailed by Pais (1988) as marking the birth of the strong interactions. The paper also notes that Chadwick's 1914 suggestion to Geiger that electrons might be scattered from crystal surfaces like X-rays anticipated de Broglie's 1923 idea of matter waves. The paper enables a reassessment of Chadwick's role in the development of both weak and strong nuclear force physics, showing how his work preceded the theoretical frameworks of Fermi (1934) for beta decay and Yukawa (1935) for the strong interaction.
