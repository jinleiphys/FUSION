# Interpolation of SUSY quantum mechanics

> Interpolation of two adjacent Hamiltonians in SUSY quantum mechanics $H_s=(1-s)A^{\dagger}A + sAA^{\dagger}$, $0\le s\le 1$ is discussed together with related operators. For a wide variety of shape-invariant degree one quantum mechanics and their `discrete' counterparts, the interpolation Hamiltonian is also shape-invariant, that is it takes the same form as the original Hamiltonian with shifted coupling constant(s).

## Key claim

For a wide class of shape-invariant degree one quantum mechanical systems and their discrete counterparts, the one-parameter interpolation Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H}_s = (1-s)\mathcal{A}^\dagger\mathcal{A} + s\mathcal{A}\mathcal{A}^\dagger\) (with \(0 \le s \le 1\)) retains shape-invariance, meaning it takes the same functional form as the original Hamiltonian but with shifted coupling constants and a shifted ground state energy.

## Method

The authors construct the interpolating Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H}_s\) as a convex combination of the original factorized Hamiltonian \(\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{A}^\dagger\mathcal{A}\) and its SUSY partner \(\mathcal{H}_r = \mathcal{A}\mathcal{A}^\dagger\). For ordinary quantum mechanics, they express the Hamiltonian in terms of a prepotential \(W(x;\boldsymbol{\lambda})\) and verify that \(\mathcal{H}_s\) can be rewritten as \(\mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{\lambda}') + \Delta\mathcal{E}(\boldsymbol{\lambda},s)\) for each known shape-invariant potential. For discrete quantum mechanics, they work with a similarity-transformed Hamiltonian \(\tilde{\mathcal{H}}_s\) acting on polynomial spaces and show that the interpolated potential function \(V_s(x;\boldsymbol{\lambda})\) equals the original potential with shifted parameters \(\boldsymbol{\lambda}'\).

## Key numbers

- Harmonic oscillator: \(\Delta\mathcal{E}(\omega,s) = 2s\omega\) (shift of ground state energy).
- Harmonic oscillator with centrifugal barrier: \(\mathcal{E}_n(\omega,g) = 4n\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); coupling constant \(g\) replaced by \(g' = (1 + \sqrt{1+4g(g+2s-1)})/2\).
- Symmetric Poschl-Teller potential: \(\mathcal{E}_n(g) = n(n+2g)\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); coupling constant \(g\) replaced by same \(g'\) formula; \(\Delta\mathcal{E}(g,s) = g'^2 - g^2\).
- Soliton (symmetric Rosen-Morse) potential: \(\mathcal{E}_n(g) = n(2g-n)\) for \(n=0,1,\ldots,[g]'\); coupling constant \(g\) replaced by \(g'' = (-1 + \sqrt{1+4g(g+1-2s)})/2\) (for \(g > 2s-1\)); \(\Delta\mathcal{E}(g,s) = g^2 - g''^2\).
- Morse potential: \(\mathcal{E}_n(g,\mu) = n(2g-n)\) for \(n=0,1,\ldots,[g]'\); coupling constant \(g\) replaced by \(g' = g-s\) (for \(g > s\)); \(\Delta\mathcal{E}(g,\mu,s) = g^2 - g'^2\).
- Meixner-Pollaczek polynomial (discrete): \(\mathcal{E}_n(\lambda) = 2n\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); \(\lambda' = \lambda + s/2\); \(\widetilde{\Delta\mathcal{E}}(\lambda;s) = 2s\).
- Continuous Hahn polynomial (discrete): \(\mathcal{E}_n(a,b) = n(n+2a+2b-1)\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); parameters \((a',b')\) satisfy \(a'+b' = a+b+s\) and \(a'b' = ab + (a+b)s/2 + s/4\); \(\widetilde{\Delta\mathcal{E}}(\boldsymbol{\lambda};s) = 2(a+b)s\).
- Continuous dual Hahn polynomial (discrete): \(\mathcal{E}_n(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) = n\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); parameters \((a',b',c')\) satisfy \(a'+b'+c' = a+b+c+3s/2\); \(\widetilde{\Delta\mathcal{E}}(\boldsymbol{\lambda};s) = s\).
- Wilson polynomial (discrete): \(\mathcal{E}_n(\boldsymbol{\lambda}) = n(n+a+b+c+d-1)\) for \(n=0,1,2,\ldots\); parameters \((a',b',c',d')\) satisfy \(a'+b'+c'+d' = a+b+c+d+2s\); \(\widetilde{\Delta\mathcal{E}}(\boldsymbol{\lambda};s) = (a+b+c+d)s\).

## Context

This work extends the known framework of shape-invariant SUSY quantum mechanics, originally developed by Gendenshtein (1983) and later systematized by Odake and Sasaki (2005-2006), by demonstrating that the interpolation between a Hamiltonian and its SUSY partner preserves exact solvability and shape invariance. The results cover both ordinary quantum mechanical systems (harmonic oscillator, Calogero model, Poschl-Teller, Rosen-Morse, Morse, and Coulomb potentials) and discrete quantum mechanical systems whose eigenfunctions are the Askey-Wilson, Wilson, continuous dual Hahn, continuous Hahn, and Meixner-Pollaczek polynomials. This enables the construction of continuous one-parameter families of exactly solvable Hamiltonians that interpolate between known solvable systems, which may be useful in studying quantum phase transitions or in applications such as those discussed by Balantekin and Pehlivan (2007).
