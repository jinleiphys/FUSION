# Description and prediction of even-A nuclear masses based on residual proton-neutron interactions

> The odd-even staggering of neighboring nuclear masses is very useful in calculating local mass relations and nucleon-pair correlations. During the past decades, there has been an increasing interest in the odd-even features of the mass relations and related quantities exhibited in masses of neighboring nuclei. In this work, after choosing a nucleus, we made an analysis of its neighboring nuclei on the upper left corner and the lower right corner respectively. We empirically obtained a new residual interaction formula of even-$A$ ($A$ is the mass number) nuclei, and it is an revision based on the existing empirical local formula of the proton-neutron interactions between the last proton and the last neutron ($δV_{1p-1n}$). We then calculated the even-$A$ nuclear masses. The differences between our calculated values and the AME2012 database show that the root-mean-squared deviations (RMSD) are small (for even-$A$ nuclei: $A$ $\geq$ 42, RMSD $\approx$ 161 keV; $A$ $\geq$ 100, RMSD $\approx$ 125 keV), while for heavy nuclei, some of our calculated values can reach an accuracy of a few tens of keV. With our residual interaction formula including one parameter, we have successfully predicted some unknown masses. Some of our predicted values compared well with the experimental values (AME2016). In addition, the accuracy and simplicity of our predicted masses for medium and heavy nuclei are comparable to those of the AME2012 (AME2016) extrapolations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new one-parameter empirical local formula for the residual proton-neutron interaction in even-A nuclei, claiming that it predicts masses with root-mean-squared deviations (RMSD) as low as 125 keV for A >= 100 when compared to the AME2012 database, though the formula is explicitly limited to even-A nuclei and its accuracy degrades for lighter nuclei.

## Method

The author analyzed neighboring nuclei on the upper left corner (Z+1, N-1) and lower right corner (Z-1, N+1) of a chosen nucleus M(Z,N) to derive an empirical formula for the residual proton-neutron interaction delta V_{1p-1n}. The resulting local mass relation uses the average interaction approximated as 13000/A keV (with A even and A >= 42) combined with three experimental masses from AME2012 to calculate or predict the mass of the fourth nucleus. The method involves iterative extrapolation using this one-parameter formula to compute even-A nuclear masses across the nuclear chart.

## Key numbers

- For even-A nuclei with A >= 42, RMSD between calculated values and AME2012 database is approximately 161 keV.
- For even-A nuclei with A >= 100, RMSD between calculated values and AME2012 database is approximately 125 keV.
- For even-A nuclei with A >= 192, calculated values reach an accuracy of approximately 90 keV RMSD.
- For even-A nuclei with A >= 42, RMSD between calculated values and AME2016 database is approximately 164 keV; for A >= 100, RMSD is approximately 126 keV.
- For even-A nuclei with A >= 42, RMSD between calculated values and AME2003 database is approximately 181 keV; for A >= 100, RMSD is approximately 134 keV.
- The theoretical uncertainty sigma_th estimated by maximum-likelihood method is 153 keV for A >= 42 and approximately 113 keV for A >= 100.
- For the predicted nucleus 198Fr, deviation from the experimental AME2016 value is approximately 37 keV; for 206Ac, deviation is approximately 2 keV.

## Context

This work extends the empirical local mass relations approach of the Garvey-Kelson relations (Garvey and Kelson, 1966) and the formulas of Ref. 24 (which gave delta V_{1p-1n} approximately -74 keV for even-A nuclei with A >= 100). The new formula improves upon the two-parameter formula of Ref. 24 by using only one parameter and achieving smaller RMSD (125 keV vs. 168 keV for even-A nuclei with A >= 100 compared to AME2003). The method enables prediction of unknown even-A nuclear masses with accuracy comparable to AME2012 and AME2016 extrapolations, and the author suggests it could be helpful for experimental planning.
