# Lepton Flavor and Number Conservation, and Physics Beyond the Standard Model

> The physics responsible for neutrino masses and lepton mixing remains unknown. More experimental data are needed to constrain and guide possible generalizations of the standard model of particle physics, and reveal the mechanism behind nonzero neutrino masses. Here, the physics associated with searches for the violation of lepton-flavor conservation in charged-lepton processes and the violation of lepton-number conservation in nuclear physics processes is summarized. In the first part, several aspects of charged-lepton flavor violation are discussed, especially its sensitivity to new particles and interactions beyond the standard model of particle physics. The discussion concentrates mostly on rare processes involving muons and electrons. In the second part, the status of the conservation of total lepton number is discussed. The discussion here concentrates on current and future probes of this apparent law of Nature via searches for neutrinoless double beta decay, which is also the most sensitive probe of the potential Majorana nature of neutrinos.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while neutrino oscillations have proven that lepton flavor is not conserved, the rates for charged-lepton flavor violation (CLFV) processes cannot be estimated model-independently and depend dramatically on the unknown mechanism behind neutrino masses; similarly, neutrino oscillations do not necessarily imply total lepton number is violated, as neutrinos could be either Dirac or Majorana fermions, and only neutrinoless double beta decay can distinguish between these possibilities.

## Method

The paper reviews the experimental status and theoretical context of two related areas: (1) searches for charged-lepton flavor violation (CLFV) in processes involving muons and electrons, including muon-to-electron conversion in nuclei, muon radiative decay, and muon three-body decay; and (2) searches for lepton-number violation via neutrinoless double beta decay. The authors present model-independent effective Lagrangian analyses to compare the sensitivity of different CLFV channels to new physics scales, and discuss predictions from specific beyond-Standard-Model scenarios including the Type-II seesaw mechanism and supersymmetric models with R-parity conservation.

## Key numbers

- Current bound on Br($\mu^+\to e^+\gamma$) < 2.4 × 10^{-12} at 90% confidence level (MEG experiment), with future sensitivity goal of ~10^{-13} and potential improvements approaching 10^{-14}
- Current bound on Br($\mu^+\to e^+e^-e^+$) < 1.0 × 10^{-12} at 90% confidence level, with proposed future sensitivity to branching ratios above 10^{-15} or 10^{-16}
- Current bound on Br($\mu\to e$ conversion in Au) < 7 × 10^{-13} at 90% confidence level, with proposed experiments aiming for sensitivity to Br($\mu\to e$ conversion in Al) > 10^{-16}
- The Standard Model prediction for Br($\mu \to e \gamma$) from active neutrino contributions is < 10^{-54}
- For dipole-dominated new physics ($\kappa\ll1$), an experiment sensitive to Br($\mu\to e\gamma$) > 10^{-13} probes new physics scales $\Lambda$ less than 2500 TeV
- For four-fermion-dominated new physics ($\kappa\gg1$), an experiment sensitive to Br($\mu\to e$ conversion in $^{27}$Al) > 10^{-16} probes $\Lambda$ less than 7000 TeV
- For four-fermion-dominated new physics ($\kappa\gg1$), an experiment sensitive to Br($\mu\to eee$) > 10^{-15} probes $\Lambda$ less than 1800 TeV
- Current muon $g-2$ data point to $\Lambda\sim 8$ TeV for the flavor-conserving case, and the $3.6\sigma$ discrepancy between measurement and Standard Model prediction constrains flavor violation in new physics to $\theta_{e\mu} < 10^{-4}$ if the discrepancy is due to new physics

## Context

This review summarizes the status of lepton-flavor and lepton-number violation searches as of 2013, extending earlier reviews by Kuno and Okada (1999) and Aysto et al. (2001). The paper discusses how the discovery of neutrino oscillations by experiments including KamLAND, Super-Kamiokande, and reactor experiments (Double Chooz, Daya Bay, RENO) has established that lepton flavor is violated, making CLFV searches a powerful probe of new physics at scales up to 10^4 TeV. The work enables future experimental programs at PSI, Fermilab, and J-PARC, and connects CLFV searches to the determination of neutrino mass mechanisms and potential tests of leptogenesis scenarios.
