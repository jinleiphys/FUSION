# Multiplicity Fluctuations in Nucleus-Nucleus Collisions: Dependence on Energy and Atomic Number

> Event-by-event multiplicity fluctuations in central C+C, S+S, In+In, and Pb+Pb as well as p+p collisions at bombarding energies from 10 to 160 AGeV are studied within the HSD and UrQMD microscopic transport approaches. Our investigation is directly related to the future experimental program of the NA61 Collaboration at the SPS for a search of the QCD critical point. The dependence on energy and atomic mass number of the scaled variances for negative, positive, and all charged hadrons is presented and compared to the results of the model of independent sources. Furthermore, the nucleus-nucleus results from the transport calculations are compared to inelastic proton-proton collisions for reference. We find a dominant role of the participant number fluctuations in nucleus-nucleus reactions at finite impact parameter $b$. In order to reduce the influence of the participant numbers fluctuations on the charged particle multiplicity fluctuations only the most central events have to be selected. Accordingly, the samples of the 1% most central nucleus-nucleus collisions with the largest numbers of the projectile participants are studied. The results are compared with those for collisions at zero impact parameter. A strong influence of the centrality selection criteria on the multiplicity fluctuations is pointed out. Our findings are essential for an optimal choice of colliding nuclei and bombarding energies for the experimental search of the QCD critical point.

## Key claim

The authors find that participant number fluctuations dominate multiplicity fluctuations in nucleus-nucleus collisions at finite impact parameter, and that only the most central events (the 1% with the largest number of projectile participants) can reduce this background sufficiently for a search of the QCD critical point; the centrality selection criterion itself strongly affects the scaled variances, and the results for the 1% most central events differ significantly from those at zero impact parameter, especially for light nuclei.

## Method

The authors used the Hadron-String-Dynamics (HSD v. 2.5) and Ultra-Relativistic-Quantum-Molecular-Dynamics (UrQMD v. 1.3) microscopic transport models to simulate central C+C, S+S, In+In, and Pb+Pb collisions, as well as inelastic p+p collisions, at bombarding energies from 10 to 158 AGeV. They calculated the scaled variances for negative, positive, and all charged hadrons in full 4π acceptance and in the projectile hemisphere (y>0), comparing results for zero impact parameter (b=0) with those for the 1% most central events selected by the largest number of projectile participants.

## Key numbers

* For Pb+Pb collisions at b=0, about 90% of nucleons are participants; for C+C collisions at b=0, this fraction is about 60-70%.
* The scaled variance of participant number fluctuations ω_P at b=0 is about 0.1-0.2 for Pb+Pb and In+In, but becomes 0.5-0.7 for C+C collisions.
* For C+C collisions at b=0, even at the maximal N_P^proj = A = 12, the scaled variance of target participant fluctuations ω_P^targ is about 0.25.
* For the 1% most central collisions selected by largest N_P^proj, the HSD scaled variance ω_- for C+C at 158 AGeV in full acceptance is 1.20; for Pb+Pb at the same energy it is 1.08.
* For the 1% most central collisions selected by largest N_P^proj, the HSD scaled variance ω_ch for C+C at 158 AGeV in full acceptance is 2.23; for Pb+Pb at the same energy it is 2.07.

## Context

This work directly supports the experimental program of the NA61 Collaboration at the SPS for a search of the QCD critical point, extending earlier transport model studies by Konchakovski et al. (2006) and Gorenstein et al. (2006) on multiplicity fluctuations in heavy-ion collisions. The authors compare their results to the model of independent sources and to inelastic p+p collisions, and they emphasize that the centrality selection criteria (b=0 versus 1% largest N_P^proj) produce markedly different A-dependencies, especially for light nuclei, which is essential for planning the optimal choice of colliding nuclei and bombarding energies in the NA61 experiment.
