# Statistical Model of the Early Stage of nucleus-nucleus collisions with exact strangeness conservation

> The Statistical Model of the Early Stage, SMES, describes a transition between confined and deconfined phases of strongly interacting matter created in nucleus-nucleus collisions. The model was formulated in the late 1990s for central Pb+Pb collisions at the CERN SPS energies. It predicted several signals of the transition (onset of deconfinement) which were later observed by the NA49 experiment. The grand canonical ensemble was used to calculate entropy and strangeness production. This approximation is valid for reactions with mean multiplicities of particles carrying conserved charges being significantly larger than one. Recent results of NA61/SHINE on hadron production in inelastic p+p interactions suggest that the deconfinement may also take place in these reactions. However, in this case mean multiplicity of particles with non-zero strange charge is smaller than one. Thus for the modeling of p+p interactions the exact strangeness conservation has to be implemented in the SMES. This extension of the SMES is presented in the paper.

## Key claim

The Statistical Model of the Early Stage (SMES), originally formulated for central Pb+Pb collisions where the grand canonical ensemble (GCE) is valid, is extended to incorporate exact strangeness conservation via the canonical ensemble (CE) for small systems such as p+p interactions, where the mean multiplicity of strange particles is smaller than one. The extension shows that the strangeness to entropy ratio in p+p interactions is approximately two times smaller than in central Pb+Pb collisions in the mixed phase region, but the model is only qualitatively similar to the measured data and requires further modifications for quantitative agreement.

## Method

The SMES assumes matter created at the early stage of collisions has zero conserved charges, with the volume defined as the Lorentz-contracted volume of participant nucleons from a single nucleus and the inelastic energy as a fixed fraction (η = 0.67) of the available energy. The model uses a bag model equation of state for the deconfined QGP phase (Q state) with u, d, and s quarks (strange quark mass 175 MeV, later adjusted to 216.5 MeV) and an effective parametrization for the confined hadron phase (W state) with massless bosons (16 degrees of freedom) and strange degrees of freedom (mass 500 MeV, 14 internal degrees). Exact strangeness conservation is implemented by replacing the GCE partition function with a CE partition function using Bessel functions I0 and I1, which modifies the strangeness density by a factor I1[V n^s]/I0[V n^s] that quantifies strangeness suppression in small systems.

## Key numbers

- The mixed phase in the CE for Ap = 1 starts at √sNN,1 = 7.20 GeV with T1 = 203.4 MeV and ends at √sNN,2 = 10.75 GeV with T2 = 202.9 MeV.
- In the GCE (large systems), the mixed phase starts at √sNN,1 = 7.42 GeV and ends at √sNN,2 = 10.83 GeV.
- The bag constant B = 570 MeV/fm³ is calculated from the Gibbs criterion at the transition temperature Tc = 200 MeV.
- The strange quark mass in the Boltzmann approximation is set to mQ = 216.5 MeV to match the Fermi distribution result with m*Q = 175 MeV at Tc.
- The CE strangeness to entropy ratio in p+p interactions (Ap = 1) is approximately two times smaller than in central Pb+Pb collisions in the mixed phase region (√sNN = 7-11 GeV).
- At √sNN > 10 GeV, the CE suppression parameter r (ratio of CE to GCE strangeness to entropy ratio) is close to unity for Ap > 10.

## Context

This work extends the SMES model originally formulated by Gazdzicki and Gorenstein (late 1990s) for central Pb+Pb collisions at CERN SPS energies, which predicted the onset of deconfinement signals later confirmed by the NA49 experiment. The extension is motivated by recent NA61/SHINE results on hadron production in inelastic p+p interactions, which suggest deconfinement may also occur in these small systems. The CE treatment enables modeling of p+p interactions and small nuclei collisions at SPS energies, but the authors note that the SMES must be significantly modified to achieve quantitative agreement with the NA61/SHINE data, leaving this for future work.
