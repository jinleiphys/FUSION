# Model study on $Υ(nS)$ modification in small collision systems

> Quarkonium production has been studied extensively in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments to understand the properties of the quark gluon plasma. The experimental results on the yield modification in heavy-ion collisions relative to that in $p$+$p$ collisions can be described by several models considering dissociation and regeneration effects. A yield modification beyond initial-state effects has also been observed in small collision systems such as $p$+Au and $p$+Pb collisions, but it is still premature to claim any hot medium effect. A model study in various small collision systems such as $p$+$p$, $p$+Pb, $p$+O, and O+O collisions will help quantitatively understanding nuclear effects on the $Υ(nS)$ production. A theoretical calculation considering the gluo-dissociation and inelastic parton scattering and their inverse reaction reasonably describes the suppression of $Υ(1S)$ in Pb+Pb collisions. Based on this calculation, a Monte-Carlo simulation is developed to more realistically incorporate the medium produced in heavy-ion collisions with event-by-event initial collision geometry and hydrodynamic evolution. We extend this framework to small systems to study the medium effects. In this work, we quantify the nuclear modification factor of $Υ(nS)$ as a function of charged particle multiplicity ($dN_{ch}/dη$) and transverse momentum. We also calculate the elliptic flow of $Υ(nS)$ in small collision systems.

## Key claim

The paper presents a model study of Upsilon(nS) modification in small collision systems (p+Pb, p+O, O+O) that considers only dissociation effects (gluo-dissociation and inelastic parton scattering) and explicitly excludes regeneration, which the authors note may explain discrepancies between their model and experimental data for excited states in central Pb+Pb collisions.

## Method

The authors developed a Monte-Carlo simulation combining MC-Glauber initial conditions with the SONIC hydrodynamic framework to model the medium evolution, then coupled this with a Boltzmann equation treatment of Upsilon dissociation using thermal widths calculated from potential non-relativistic QCD (pNRQCD) and hard thermal loop (HTL) perturbation theory. For each Upsilon state, a survival probability was calculated at each time step based on the local medium temperature and the state's thermal width, with dissociation temperatures set at 600 MeV, 240 MeV, and 190 MeV for Upsilon(1S), Upsilon(2S), and Upsilon(3S) respectively. Feed-down contributions from higher excited states were included using experimental branching ratios and cross section ratios from CMS and LHCb data.

## Key numbers

- Dissociation temperatures: 600 MeV for Upsilon(1S), 240 MeV for Upsilon(2S), 190 MeV for Upsilon(3S)
- Formation times: 0.5 fm/c for Upsilon(1S), 1.0 fm/c for Upsilon(2S), 1.5 fm/c for Upsilon(3S)
- Mean charged particle multiplicity from SONIC: 10.0 for p+O collisions, 31.2 for O+O collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 8 TeV
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of 0.08 used in SONIC calculations
- Nucleon-nucleon inelastic cross section of 72 mb used for small system collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 8 TeV
- Gaussian width of sigma = 0.4 fm for energy deposition of each wounded nucleon
- Critical temperature of 170 MeV below which dissociation calculations stop
- Elliptic flow (v2) values less than 0.01 for all Upsilon states in all three small collision systems

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Hong et al. (2019) for Upsilon dissociation in heavy-ion collisions to small collision systems, building on the SONIC hydrodynamic framework developed by Romatschke (2015). The study is motivated by experimental observations of QGP-like behavior in small systems from CMS (2010), ATLAS (2015), and ALICE (2021), and aims to provide predictions for upcoming LHC runs with oxygen ions. The results enable quantitative comparison with future experimental measurements of Upsilon suppression and elliptic flow in p+O and O+O collisions, which could help distinguish between cold nuclear matter effects and hot medium effects in small systems.
