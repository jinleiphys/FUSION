# UNEDF: Advanced Scientific Computing Transforms the Low-Energy Nuclear Many-Body Problem

> The UNEDF SciDAC collaboration of nuclear theorists, applied mathematicians, and computer scientists is developing a comprehensive description of nuclei and their reactions that delivers maximum predictive power with quantified uncertainties. This paper illustrates significant milestones accomplished by UNEDF through integration of the theoretical approaches, advanced numerical algorithms, and leadership class computational resources.

# UNEDF: Advanced Scientific Computing Transforms the Low-Energy Nuclear Many-Body Problem

## Key claim
The UNEDF collaboration demonstrates that integrating nuclear theory with advanced numerical algorithms and leadership-class computing resources can produce predictive descriptions of nuclei with quantified uncertainties, but the paper presents these as "significant milestones" rather than a complete, validated predictive framework, the results remain exploratory for some applications (e.g., fission simulations are described as "proof of principle").

## Method
The collaboration developed and interconnected multiple computational approaches: coupled-cluster (NUCCOR) and no-core shell model (MFDn) calculations for ab initio studies; density functional theory (DFT) optimization using the derivative-free POUNDerS algorithm; time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) on 3D spatial lattices; and reaction calculations coupling DFT results with scattering formalisms. These codes were scaled to run on leadership-class supercomputers (Jaguar at ORNL and Intrepid at ANL) using hybrid MPI/OpenMP parallelism, asynchronous dynamic load balancing (ADLB), and memory-aware algorithms.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy of ⁴⁸Ca calculated with chiral nucleon-nucleon interactions shows approximately 400 keV per nucleon missing binding energy attributed to chiral three-nucleon forces
- MFDn calculations for ¹⁴C involved diagonalization of a Hamiltonian matrix of dimension two billion using 214,668 cores on Jaguar, consuming roughly 30 million core-hours
- The TDSLDA implementation eliminates matrix operations, allowing a basis set 2–3 orders of magnitude larger than alternate approaches
- GFMC calculations using the ADLB library ran efficiently on over 100,000 cores on Intrepid
- POUNDerS optimization algorithm yields "dramatic computational savings" over alternative optimization methods (specific speedup factor not given in text)

## Context
This work extends the nuclear energy density functional program by introducing systematic optimization and uncertainty quantification tools previously unavailable in low-energy nuclear theory, building on prior coupled-cluster work by Hagen and no-core shell model developments by Vary. The UNEDF results enable future calculations of reaction observables for unstable nuclei outside experimental reach, provide a baseline functional (UNEDF0) for comparing future functionals, and establish a proof of principle for time-dependent simulations of fermion superfluids that could eventually treat nuclear fission. The cold-atom simulations (ASLDA and TDSLDA) serve as testbeds for methods applicable to neutron star crust physics.
