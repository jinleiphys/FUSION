# From Nuclear To Sub-Hadronic Physics : A Global View Of Indian Efforts

> A panoramic view seeks to trace the principal ideas governing the phenomenal growth of Nuclear Physics from a modest beginning in the Thirties, to an all-embracing field now protruding to the subhadronic on the one hand, and to high temperature QGP physics on the other. In this narrative, which makes no claims to completeness, Indian efforts have been highlighted vis-a-vis other similar efforts, within a common standard of global recognition.

# From Nuclear To Sub-Hadronic Physics: A Global View Of Indian Efforts

## Key claim

This paper provides a panoramic historical narrative of nuclear physics growth from the 1930s to the sub-hadronic and QGP regimes, with Indian contributions highlighted alongside global efforts, but makes no claims to completeness and focuses only on areas where Indian work has been globally visible during the last half century.

## Method

The author traces principal ideas governing nuclear physics development through three sectors: classical nuclear physics (shell model, collective excitations, direct reactions, microscopic models), pion-nuclear physics and quark models, and nuclear astrophysics with QGP transitions. Within each sector, specific Indian theoretical contributions are described in the context of contemporaneous international developments, citing named researchers and their published results.

## Key numbers

- The Pandya Theorem was verified by the Utrecht nuclear physics group to within approximately 10 KeV, compared to usual experimental agreement limits of order 100 KeV
- The first prominent nucleon resonance (Delta or "33" resonance) lies about 300 MeV above the nucleon mass
- The Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem for a saturating system at equilibrium yields the relation E = A (Fermi energy equals average energy per particle)
- Satpathy and Patra (Phys.Rev.Lett.51, 1983) pointed out inconsistency in the Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula using a generalized HVH theorem
- Yrast states are produced by medium energy projectiles of approximately 200 MeV (e.g., 48Ca on 120Sn targets)
- The V. Pandharipande group (1997) showed substantial deviations from mean field approximation due to pair correlation effects in the high momentum regime

## Context

This review extends the historical perspective of nuclear physics from its birth with Chadwick's neutron discovery (1932) and Yukawa's meson theory (1935) through to modern QCD developments, highlighting the shifting boundary between nuclear and particle physics. It specifically contextualizes Indian contributions such as the Pandya Theorem (named by J.B. French), Mitra's separable potential approach to few-body problems (1962), and Satpathy's work on extreme nuclear states and new magic numbers, within the broader framework of Bethe's Second Principle Theory and the Bethe-Brueckner many-body formalism. The paper enables future work by documenting how Indian theoretical efforts in shell model calculations, few-body systems, and quark model applications have contributed to global nuclear physics understanding.
