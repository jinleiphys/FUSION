# Laser-induced electronic bridge for characterization of the $~^{229\rm{m}}\rm{Th} \rightarrow ~^{229\rm{g}}\rm{Th}$ nuclear transition with a tunable optical laser

> An alternative method to determine the excitation energy of the $~^{229\rm{m}}\rm{Th}$ isomer via the laser-induced electronic bridge is investigated theoretically. In the presence of an optical or ultra-violet laser at energies that fulfill a two-photon resonance condition, the excited nuclear state can decay by transfering its energy to the electronic shell. A bound electron is then promoted to an excited state by absorption of a laser photon and simultaneous de-excitation of the nucleus. We present calculated rates for the laser-induced electronic bridge process and discuss the experimental requirements for the corresponding setup. Our results show that depending on the actual value of the nuclear transition energy, the rate can be very high, with an enhancement factor compared to the radiative nuclear decay of up to $10^8$.

# Laser-induced electronic bridge for characterization of the 229mTh → 229gTh nuclear transition with a tunable optical laser

## Key claim
The laser-induced electronic bridge (LIEB) process in 229Th3+ ions can achieve an enhancement factor of up to 10^8 relative to the radiative nuclear decay rate, but only if the nuclear isomeric energy Em lies near specific electronic resonance conditions, particularly around 12 eV or 17 eV, and the required laser spectral intensities are experimentally achievable.

## Method
The authors calculate the LIEB rate by relating it to the inverse spontaneous electronic bridge process, using the nuclear γ-decay rate Γγ = 10−4 1/s (corresponding to a reduced nuclear matrix element B↓ = 0.0076 W.u.) and ab initio Dirac-Hartree-Fock wavefunctions for the valence electron in Th3+. The electronic matrix elements are computed using a B-spline basis in a cavity of radius 60 a.u., with the initial electronic state chosen as the metastable 7s state and final states as 8pj and 9pj (j = 1/2, 3/2). The LIEB rate is evaluated as a function of the nuclear isomer energy for two laser spectral intensity regimes: Pω = 1 W/(m2·Hz) for optical lasers (1.2–2.2 eV photon energy) and Pω = 10−2 W/(m2·Hz) for ultraviolet lasers (above 2.2 eV).

## Key numbers
- Enhancement factor of LIEB rate over radiative nuclear decay: up to 10^8, achieved when Em is near electronic M1 resonances around 12 eV or 17 eV
- Assumed nuclear γ-decay rate: Γγ = 10−4 1/s, corresponding to reduced nuclear matrix element B↓ = 0.0076 W.u.
- Laser spectral intensity for optical regime: Pω = 1 W/(m2·Hz), achievable with tunable lasers in the range 560–1000 nm (1.2–2.2 eV photon energy)
- Laser spectral intensity for UV regime: Pω = 10−2 W/(m2·Hz), for photon energies above 2.2 eV
- Lifetime of the initial 7s electronic state in Th3+: 0.6 s
- Radiative lifetime of the isomeric state without LIEB: approximately 10^4 s
- Em range accessible with UV laser: as low as 8 eV under conditions of good detection efficiency
- Radiative decay rates of final electronic states: e.g., 8p3/2 → 6d5/2 at 15.6 eV with rate 24.9 × 10^8/s; 9p3/2 → 8d5/2 at 1.32 eV with rate 4.73 × 10^8/s

## Context
This work extends earlier theoretical proposals for electronic bridge processes in 229Th, particularly the work of Porsev and Flambaum (2010) on spontaneous electronic bridge in Th3+ and Th1+ ions, and the original LIEB proposal by Karpeshin et al. (1992) which assumed an isomer energy around 3.5 eV. The present paper updates the scheme for the currently known isomer energy range (7.8 ± 0.5 eV from Beck 2007, with uncertainty noted by Tkalya 2015) and addresses the fact that neutral Th decays too fast via internal conversion (Wense 2016) to use the original LIEB scheme. The method enables precise determination of the 229mTh isomer energy via laser spectroscopy and measurement of the nuclear γ-decay rate, which are critical for applications such as a nuclear frequency standard (Peik 2003, Campbell 2012) and nuclear laser (Tkalya 2011). The proposed experiment would use 10^4–10^5 trapped Th3+ ions from the Munich setup (Wense 2016, Seiferle 2017) with STIRAP preparation of the 7s state.
