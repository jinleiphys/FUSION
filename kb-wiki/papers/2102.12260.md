# Imaginary Time Propagation on a Quantum Chip

> Evolution in imaginary time is a prominent technique for finding the ground state of quantum many-body systems, and the heart of a number of numerical methods that have been used with great success in quantum chemistry, condensed matter and nuclear physics. We propose an algorithm to implement imaginary time propagation on a quantum computer. Our algorithm is devised in the context of an efficient encoding into an optimized gate, drawing on the underlying characteristics of the quantum device, of a unitary operation in an extended Hilbert space. However, we proved that for simple problems it can be successfully applied to standard digital quantum machines. This work paves the way for porting quantum many-body methods based on imaginary-time propagation to near-term quantum devices, enabling the future quantum simulation of the ground states of a broad class of microscopic systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a purely quantum algorithm for imaginary-time propagation (ITP) on a quantum computer, implemented via a unitary operation in an extended Hilbert space that includes a single auxiliary (ancilla) qubit. The authors claim the algorithm can be applied to standard digital quantum machines for simple problems, but the method is primarily devised for efficient encoding into an optimized gate that exploits the underlying characteristics of the quantum device.

## Method

The algorithm maps the non-unitary imaginary-time propagator \(e^{-(\hat{H}-E_T)\tau}\) onto a unitary operator \(\hat{U}(\tau)\) acting on the tensor product of the system Hilbert space and a single reservoir (ancilla) qubit. After applying \(\hat{U}(\tau)\) to the initial state \(\ket{0}\otimes\ket{\psi_s}\), a measurement of the ancilla in the \(\ket{0}\) state projects the system onto the normalized state \(\hat{Q}_{\rm ITP}(\tau)\ket{\psi_s}\), where \(\hat{Q}_{\rm ITP}(\tau)=(\mathbb{1}+e^{-2(\hat{H}-E_T)\tau})^{-1/2}e^{-(\hat{H}-E_T)\tau}\). The authors implement this unitary using either a decomposition into standard quantum gates (for the IBM device) or an optimal control approach for a transmon qudit, and demonstrate the method on two test systems: the Hydrogen atom (using a two-qubit circuit) and a two-neutron spin system (using a three-qubit encoding).

## Key numbers

* Hydrogen atom: single-shot propagation with \(\tau=15.0\) Hartree\(^{-1}\) achieved a fidelity of 0.942(4) on the IBM device and 0.9968(4) with amplitude amplification; the optimal control simulation with noise gave fidelity 0.9978.
* Two-neutron spin system: single-shot propagation with \(\tau=1\) MeV\(^{-1}\) achieved fidelity 0.9919 in the optimal control simulation with noise, and 0.999999996 without noise.
* The initial overlap with the ground state was \(|c_0|^2=0.361\) for the Hydrogen atom and \(|c_0|^2=0.407\) for the two-neutron system.
* The success probability for measuring the ancilla in \(\ket{0}\) satisfies \(P(0) \geq |c_0|^2/(1+\exp(2\tau(E_0-E_T)))\); for \(E_T \geq E_0\) this gives \(P(0) \geq |c_0|^2/2\) in the long-time limit.

## Context

This work extends prior proposals for implementing non-unitary operations on quantum computers via extended Hilbert spaces, first suggested by Walters (2014) and used in methods for state preparation and Gibbs sampling by Terhal and DiVincenzo (2000), Chowdhury and Somma (2017), and Metcalf et al. (2020). The algorithm differs from these earlier approaches by requiring only a single auxiliary qubit and by introducing a direct encoding into a single optimized gate using optimal control, as discussed in a prior study by the same group (Optimalcontrol, 2020). The results enable porting classical many-body methods based on imaginary-time propagation, such as those used in quantum Monte Carlo for nuclear structure (dmc_paper, 2015), to near-term quantum devices, with potential applications to ground-state simulation of a broad class of microscopic systems.
