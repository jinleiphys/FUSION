# Joint analysis of reactor and accelerator CE$ν$NS data on germanium: implications for the Standard Model and nuclear physics

> This work presents the first comprehensive joint analysis of all available Coherent Elastic Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering (CE$ν$NS) data on germanium: those observed at the Spallation Neutron Source (SNS) by the COHERENT collaboration and those of the nuclear reactors revealed by the CONUS+ experiment using germanium detectors. In addition to COHERENT and CONUS+, we incorporate reactor data from TEXONO and $ν$GeN, thereby enhancing both the statistical significance and the systematic reliability of our study. We provide state-of-the-art determinations of key nuclear physics and Standard Model parameters, including the neutron root-mean-square (rms) radius of germanium nuclei, the weak mixing angle, and the neutrino charge radius. The observed tension of about $2 σ$ between the COHERENT germanium measurement and the Standard Model prediction motivates a detailed reassessment of the theoretical cross-section. In particular, we examine the impact of nuclear form factors and uncertainties in the nuclear radius, as well as the potential influence of a systematic shift in the neutrino flux normalisation at the SNS. Our results highlight the reliability of CE$ν$NS as a precision tool, reinforced by the complementarity of different experimental inputs, and lay the groundwork for future advances in the field.

## Key claim

The first comprehensive joint analysis of all available CEνNS data on germanium (from COHERENT at the SNS, CONUS+, TEXONO, and νGeN) finds a tension of about 2σ between the COHERENT germanium measurement and the Standard Model prediction, but the combined analysis of all datasets yields a consistent global picture, with the tension potentially attributable to a systematic shift in the neutrino flux normalisation at the SNS rather than new physics.

## Method

The authors performed a simultaneous least-squares fit of beam-on and beam-off data from COHERENT's germanium detector at the SNS, incorporating timing information in 4 μs bins to separate neutrino flavour components, and combined this with reactor CEνNS data from CONUS+, TEXONO, and νGeN using the Helm parameterisation for nuclear form factors. They also considered alternative nuclear density distributions from Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer calculations using D1S and D1M Gogny forces, and included low-energy ν-e⁻ scattering data from TEXONO, LSND, LAMPF, LZ, PandaX, and XENONnT for clean weak mixing angle determinations.

## Key numbers

- COHERENT germanium data yields about 21.0^{+7.1}_{-6.4} CEνNS events, compared to a theoretical prediction of 33.3±3.4 events assuming a Helm neutron form factor with average neutron rms radius R_n^{NSM}=4.22 fm (1.7σ tension)
- Combined analysis of all CEνNS data gives sin²θ_W = 0.229^{+0.020}_{-0.019} (1σ), consistent with the Standard Model value of 0.23873±0.00005
- When fixing the weak mixing angle to its SM value, the extracted neutron rms radius of germanium is R_n(Ge)=6.5±1.4 fm (1σ), with an upper limit of <11.8 fm (3σ)
- The combined SNS neutrino flux normalisation from all COHERENT targets is Φ^{SNS}_{meas}/Φ^{SNS}_{pred}=0.92±0.10 (1σ)
- Neutrino charge radii extracted from the combined analysis: ⟨r_{ν_e}²⟩=(3.2±4.7)×10⁻³² cm² and ⟨r_{ν_μ}²⟩=-3.9^{+2.8}_{-2.7}×10⁻³² cm² (1σ)

## Context

This work extends the CEνNS analyses of COHERENT CsI (Akimov 2021) and Ar (COHERENT 2020) data by incorporating the first germanium-target measurements from both the SNS and reactor experiments, following the CONUS+ observation (Ackermann 2025) and constraints from TEXONO (2024) and νGeN (2025). The joint analysis demonstrates the complementarity between accelerator-based experiments (sensitive to multiple neutrino flavours and nuclear structure) and reactor-based experiments (sensitive only to electron flavour but with form factors near unity), enabling simultaneous extraction of the weak mixing angle, neutrino charge radii, and neutron nuclear radius. This work lays the groundwork for future precision measurements, particularly with the planned COHERENT heavy-water detector expected to reduce neutrino flux uncertainty to 2-3% and clarify whether the observed tension is statistical or systematic.
