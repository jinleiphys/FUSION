# Effects of Cluster Particle Correlations on Local Parity Violation Observables

> We investigate effects of cluster particle correlations on two- and three-particle azimuth correlator observables sensitive to local strong parity violation. We use two-particle angular correlation measurements as input and estimate the magnitudes of the effects with straightforward assumptions. We found that the measurements of the azimuth correlator observables by the STAR experiment can be entirely accounted for by cluster particle correlations together with a reasonable range of cluster anisotropy in non-peripheral collisions. Our result suggests that new physics, such as local strong parity violation, may not be required to explain the correlator data.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the two- and three-particle azimuth correlator measurements by the STAR experiment, previously interpreted as evidence for local strong parity violation, can be entirely explained by conventional cluster particle correlations combined with a reasonable range of cluster anisotropy in non-peripheral collisions, suggesting that new physics may not be required.

## Method

The author constructs a cluster model where events consist of hydrodynamic-like particles plus small-angle (SA) clusters and back-to-back (BB) clusters, using operational definitions based on the azimuthal separation of particle pairs. Inputs from measured two-particle angular correlation functions (parameterized as a near-side Gaussian plus a negative dipole and a quadrupole) are used to extract the fractions of unlike-sign (US) and like-sign (LS) pairs from SA and BB clusters, and to estimate the three-particle correlation effects under the assumption of independent particle emission within clusters. The remaining three-particle correlator after subtracting cluster three-particle effects is then factorized to extract the elliptic flow parameters of SA and BB clusters (vSA and vBB) needed to reproduce the STAR data.

## Key numbers

* The extracted near-side azimuthal spread wSA ranges from 0.58 in peripheral Au+Au collisions to 0.85 in central collisions.
* The back-to-back azimuthal spread wBB is a fixed value of pi/4 (approximately 0.785).
* The measured number of correlated pairs per charged particle (V) ranges from 0.3 in peripheral Au+Au collisions to 1.9 in central collisions.
* For binary-scaled clusters, the estimated three-particle correlator from clusters for US pairs is 8.0 x 10^-5 in the 70-60% centrality bin and 1.8 x 10^-6 in the 5-0% centrality bin.
* For binary-scaled clusters, the estimated three-particle correlator from clusters for LS pairs is -1.2 x 10^-5 in the 70-60% centrality bin and -8.8 x 10^-7 in the 5-0% centrality bin.
* With binary-scaled clusters and three-particle effects removed, the required SA-cluster elliptic flow vSA ranges from -0.20 +/- 0.09 (70-60%) to -0.21 +/- 0.03 (5-0%).
* With binary-scaled clusters and three-particle effects removed, the required BB-cluster elliptic flow vBB ranges from 0.36 +/- 0.12 (70-60%) to -0.17 +/- 0.03 (5-0%).
* With cluster three-particle correlation set to zero, the required vSA ranges from 0.23 +/- 0.02 (70-60%) to 0.03 +/- 0.01 (5-0%).
* With cluster three-particle correlation set to zero, the required vBB ranges from 0.79 +/- 0.07 (70-60%) to 0.07 +/- 0.01 (5-0%).

## Context

This work directly addresses the interpretation of azimuth correlator measurements by the STAR experiment (STAR, 2009) that had been proposed as evidence for local strong parity violation and the chiral magnetic effect (Kharzeev et al., 2008; Kharzeev and Zhitnitsky, 2007). The paper extends the background correlation analysis discussed in the STAR publication by quantitatively modeling cluster correlations using two-particle angular correlation measurements from Daugherity (2008) and STAR (2008). The results suggest that conventional physics from jet-like correlations and cluster anisotropy can account for the observed signals, enabling future work to focus on disentangling cluster contributions from any potential parity-violating effects through more differential measurements.
