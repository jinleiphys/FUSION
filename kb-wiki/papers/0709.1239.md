# Bridging over p-wave pi-production and weak processes in few-nucleon systems with chiral perturbation theory

> I study an aspect of chiral perturbation theory (χPT) which enables one to ``bridge'' different reactions. That is, an operator fixed in one of the reactions can then be used to predict the other. For this purpose, I calculate the partial wave amplitude for the p-wave pion production (pp\to pnπ^+) using the pion production operator from the lowest and the next nonvanishing orders. The operator includes a contact operator whose coupling has been fixed using a matrix element of a low-energy weak process (pp\to de^+ν_e). I find that this operator does not reproduce the partial wave amplitude extracted from experimental data, showing that the bridging over the reactions with significantly different kinematics is not necessarily successful. I study the dependence of the amplitude on the various inputs such as the NN potential, the πNΔcoupling, and the cutoff. I argue the importance of a higher order calculation. In order to gain an insight into a higher order calculation, I add a higher order counter term to the operator used above, and fit the couplings to both the low-energy weak process and the pion production. The energy dependence of the partial wave amplitude for the pion production is described by the operator consistently with the data. However, I find a result which tells us to be careful about the convergence of the chiral expansion for the pp\to pnπ^+ reaction.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether chiral perturbation theory can reliably "bridge" between a low-energy weak process (pp → de⁺ν_e) and a strong pion-production reaction (pp → pnπ⁺) by using the same low-energy constant d₁ fixed in the weak process to predict the p-wave pion production amplitude. The author finds that at next-to-leading order this bridging is not successful, showing that the chiral expansion for the pp → pnπ⁺ reaction requires careful treatment of convergence.

## Method

The author calculates the partial wave amplitude for p-wave pion production (pp → pnπ⁺) using chiral perturbation theory operators up to next-to-leading order, including one-body, Δ-excitation, pion-rescattering, and contact terms. The contact coupling d₁ (combined with d₂ as d̃ = d₁ + 2d₂) is fixed by fitting to a benchmark matrix element for the axial vector current in the pp → de⁺ν_e reaction, using the AV18 potential and kinematics with T_NNⁱ = 0.5 MeV and q = 2.5 MeV. The pion production amplitude is then calculated with several combinations of NN potentials (CD-Bonn, AV18, Reid93, Nijmegen I, chiral N³LO), πNΔ couplings (h_A = 2.10 and 2.68), and sharp cutoffs (Λ = 500, 600, 800 MeV), and compared to experimental data extracted by Flammang et al.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless contact coupling d̂ (≡ m_N f_π² d̃) ranges from −0.64 to 4.36 depending on the NN potential, h_A value, and cutoff Λ; for example, with Λ = 500 MeV and CD-Bonn potential, d̂ = 0.18 for h_A = 2.10 and d̂ = 0.06 for h_A = 2.68
- The pion decay constant used is f_π = 93 MeV, the nucleon mass m_N = 939 MeV, the pion mass m_π = 138 MeV, and the Δ mass m_Δ = 1232 MeV
- The Gaussian cutoff for the benchmark calculation uses Λ_χ = 500, 600, and 800 MeV; the sharp cutoff for Group I operators uses Λ = 500, 600, and 800 MeV
- The πNΔ coupling constants used are h_A = 2.10 (from Δ-decay width) and h_A = 2.68 (from large N_C); corresponding c_3 values are −1.87 GeV⁻¹ and −0.79 GeV⁻¹, and c_4 values are 1.87 GeV⁻¹ and 1.33 GeV⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the "bridging program" of chiral perturbation theory previously explored by Park et al. (2003) for weak processes, Hanhart et al. (2001) for pion production, and Gårdestig et al. (2006) for π⁻d → γnn. While Park et al. successfully bridged between tritium β-decay and weak proton capture (similar kinematics), and Hanhart et al. found consistency between three-nucleon observables and pion production (though with inconsistent nuclear forces), this paper shows that bridging between a weak process and pion production with significantly different kinematics fails at next-to-leading order. The work enables future higher-order calculations and highlights the need for consistent treatment of the Δ resonance and nuclear forces in chiral effective field theory.
