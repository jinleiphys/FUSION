# Emission of Scission Neutrons in the Sudden Approximation

> At a certain finite neck radius during the descent of a fissioning nucleus from the saddle to the scission point, the attractive nuclear forces can no more withstand the repulsive Coulomb forces producing the neck rupture and the sudden absorption of the neck stubs by the fragments. At that moment, the neutrons, although still characterized by their pre-scission wave functions, find themselves in the newly created potential of their interaction with the separated fragments. Their wave functions become wave packets with components in the continuum. The probability to populate such states gives evidently the emission probability of neutrons at scission. In this way, we have studied scission neutrons for the fissioning nucleus $^{236}$U, using two-dimensional realistic nuclear shapes. Both the emission probability and the distribution of the emission points relative to the fission fragments strongly depend on the quantum numbers of the pre-scission state from which the neutron is emitted. In particular it was found that states with $Ωπ$ = 1/2+ dominate the emission. Depending on the assumed pre- and post-scission configurations and on the emission-barrier height, 30 to 50% of the total scission neutrons are emitted from 1/2+ states. Their emission points are concentrated in the region between the newly separated fragments. The upper limit for the total number of neutrons per scission event is predicted to lie between 0.16 and 1.73 (depending on the computational assumptions).

## Key claim

The paper predicts that scission neutrons are emitted via a sudden transition at neck rupture, with the total number per fission event lying between 0.16 and 1.73, but these values represent upper limits due to the sudden approximation used, and the results are highly sensitive to the assumed minimum neck radius at rupture.

## Method

The authors applied the sudden approximation to the fissioning nucleus ²³⁶U, using two-dimensional realistic nuclear shapes described by Cassini ovals with two sets of deformation parameters (εᵢ → εf: 0.975 → 1.010 and 0.985 → 1.001). They diagonalized the single-neutron Hamiltonians in a deformed oscillator basis for the pre-scission and post-scission configurations, then calculated the expansion coefficients of initial eigenstates over final eigenstates to obtain emission probabilities from the overlap between bound states. The total scission neutron multiplicity was obtained by summing partial emission probabilities weighted by occupation probabilities, considering both independent and pairing-correlated neutrons via BCS theory.

## Key numbers

- Total scission neutron multiplicity per fission event for ²³⁶U: predicted upper limit between 0.16 and 1.73, depending on computational assumptions (pre- and post-scission configurations, emission-barrier height)
- For the shorter jump (εᵢ=0.985 → εf=1.001, neck radius 1.5 fm): 15% of total neutrons emitted during symmetric fission of ²³⁶U are scission neutrons
- For the longer jump (εᵢ=0.975 → εf=1.010, neck radius 1.9 fm): scission neutron multiplicity is larger by a factor of about 5 (0 MeV cut-off) or about 6 (2 MeV cut-off) compared to the shorter jump
- 30 to 50% of total scission neutrons are emitted from Ωπ = 1/2+ states
- More than 93% of scission neutrons are emitted from 1/2, 3/2, or 5/2 states; more than 55% from 1/2 states; more than 33% from 1/2+ states
- Primary fragments excitation energy: 12.2 MeV for the shorter jump, 51.88 MeV for the longer jump
- Neck radii at rupture: 1.9 fm and 1.5 fm for the two sets of deformation parameters

## Context

This work extends the sudden approximation formalism previously applied to scission alpha particles in a one-dimensional model by Serot (2000) to two dimensions with realistic nuclear shapes, and applies it to neutron emission in the ²³⁵U(n_th,f) reaction. It addresses the long-standing paradox that scission neutrons should be the most abundant light particles accompanying fission based on energetics, yet experimental results remain contradictory (Wagemans 1991, Bowman 1962). The calculations provide upper limits that can be compared with experimental re-analyses by Kornilov (2001) for ²⁵²Cf(sf), which found a neutron surplus of (30 ± 5)% at about 90° relative to fragment direction representing about 10% of total fission neutrons, and by Franklyn (1978) for ²³⁵U(n_th,f) which required a scission neutron contribution of 20% to fit angular distributions. The results enable determination of the minimum neck radius at scission from precise measurements of scission neutron multiplicity, and provide excitation energies of primary fragments (unavailable from other sources) that are decisive for prompt neutron emission calculations.
