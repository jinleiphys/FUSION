# Thermodynamic and Transport Properties of Quark-Gluon Plasma at Finite Chemical Potential with a DNN framework

> The characteristics of a thermal system depend strongly on its response to thermal gradients and the underlying microscopic interactions among constituents. In the present study, we investigate the thermodynamic and transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at finite baryon chemical potential within a deep-learning-assisted quasi-particle model (DLQPM). The temperature ($\mathrm{T}$) and baryon chemical potential ($μ_B$)-dependent thermal masses of quasi-particles are estimated using neural networks trained to reproduce lattice QCD (lQCD) results for the equation of state, obtained via a Taylor-like expansion around vanishing baryon chemical potential. The trained model acts as an effective emulator, enabling us to estimate the thermodynamic and transport properties at finite $μ_B$. We compute the speed of sound, specific heat, viscosity, and conductivity of the deconfined medium. Our findings are in good agreement with available lattice calculations and other phenomenological models. The present study demonstrates that a DNN-based approach provides an efficient framework for studying the properties of the QGP at finite baryon density.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that a deep-neural-network-assisted quasi-particle model (DLQPM) can reliably predict thermodynamic and transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma at finite baryon chemical potential, but only for temperatures T ≥ 1.1 Tc (Tc = 155 MeV) and under the condition that mass-regularization terms are added to the loss function to enforce the quasi-particle mass hierarchy and resolve the non-uniqueness of the mass solutions.

## Method
Three independent residual neural networks (ResNets) were trained simultaneously on lattice QCD results from the Wuppertal-Budapest group for entropy density, baryon number density, and trace anomaly at finite baryon chemical potential, using a Taylor-like expansion around μB = 0. The networks take (T, μB) as input and output positive scalar thermal masses for gluons, light quarks (u/d), and strange quarks, which are then fed into the grand canonical partition function to compute thermodynamic observables. A mass-regularization loss term was added to constrain the predicted mass ratios (mg/mu/d and mg/ms) to match those from the quasi-particle model of Plumari (2011), and the networks were trained for 5000 epochs using the AdamW optimizer with an 80%/20% train-validation split.

## Key numbers
- The deconfinement transition temperature is taken as Tc = 155 MeV, and the model is applied only for T ≥ 1.1 Tc (i.e., T ≥ 170.5 MeV).
- The strange quark rest mass is set to m0s = 93.5 MeV/c².
- The coupling constant parametrization uses λ = 2.6 and Ts/Tc = 0.57.
- The relaxation time parametrization uses k = 18.5 to yield a minimum η/s of 1/4π (the KSS bound).
- The mass regularization strength is set to β = 0.01.
- Training used 5000 epochs with initial learning rate 0.001, StepLR scheduler with step size 2000 epochs and decay factor γ = 0.92.

## Context
This work extends the deep-learning-assisted quasi-particle model previously introduced by Li (2022), Li (2025), and Jamal (2025) for vanishing baryon chemical potential to the finite-μB regime by incorporating Taylor-expansion coefficients from the Wuppertal-Budapest lattice QCD group (Borsanyi, 2021). The approach addresses the fermion sign problem that prevents direct lattice QCD simulations at finite baryon density, and provides an alternative to phenomenological models such as the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio model (Nambu, 1961) and the Polyakov-loop extended NJL model (Fukushima, 2003; Ratti, 2005). The results enable future hydrodynamic simulations of heavy-ion collisions at finite baryon density and studies of neutron star matter, by providing temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent transport coefficients including shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity.
