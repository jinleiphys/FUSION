# Bottom-up approach to describe groomed jet data in heavy-ion collisions

> The theoretical interpretation of jet observables in heavy-ion collisions is a complex task due to the intricate interplay of perturbative and non-perturbative effects. One way to reduce this complexity is to groom away soft, wide-angle radiation so that perturbative dynamics dominates. Even in this simplified scenario, there are competing explanations for the physical origin of the measured medium-induced modifications. In this paper, we present a minimal approach to compute groomed substructure observables. The core idea is to treat medium effects as an effective energy shift of the hard, vacuum-like substructure. This energy shift includes a gradual onset of colour decoherence effects and thus depends on the jet substructure itself. We first study a NLO-exact dijet configuration in vacuum and apply radiative energy-loss to the two subjets. We find that this minimal setup already captures the narrowing trend of groomed observables but it's not able to quantitatively describe the existing data. Next, we match the NLO matrix-element to a leading-logarithm accurate parton shower and perform a clustering algorithm to recover a two-prong system to which we again apply the energy-loss distribution. Despite its simplicity, the model results in a very good theory-to-data agreement (within $10\%$) for a broad range of observables including both ALICE and ATLAS kinematics. We also examine the discriminating power of groomed jet data in terms of colour decoherence effects and find that substructure-dependent energy loss yields an overall better agreement.

## Key claim
The paper presents a minimal "bottom-up" model where medium effects in heavy-ion collisions are treated solely as an effective energy shift of the hard, vacuum-like jet substructure, without explicit modifications of the splitting kernel. The model achieves good theory-to-data agreement (within 10%) for groomed jet observables across both ALICE and ATLAS kinematics, but only when including a substructure-dependent energy loss that incorporates colour decoherence effects.

## Method
The authors first compute a vacuum baseline by matching NLO hard matrix elements (using POWHEG-BOX) to a leading-logarithm accurate parton shower (PYTHIA8), then cluster jets with the anti-kt algorithm and apply SoftDrop grooming to identify a two-prong system. Medium effects are introduced as an afterburner: the energy loss probability distribution from Mehtar-Tani et al. (2017) is applied to the groomed two-prong system, where the energy loss depends on the opening angle of the splitting and the colour representation of the parent parton. The medium is treated in the brick approximation (homogeneous, static, length L=4 fm) and the calculation assumes the formation time of the hard splitting is much shorter than the decoherence time.

## Key numbers
- Theory-to-data agreement within 10% for a broad range of observables including both ALICE and ATLAS kinematics
- Medium length fixed to L=4 fm, corresponding roughly to a central PbPb collision
- In-medium strong coupling fixed to alpha_s^med = 0.24
- SoftDrop grooming condition uses z_cut = 0.2
- ALICE kinematic cuts: charged particles, p_T,jet in [60,80] GeV, |eta_jet| < 0.7, R=0.2
- ATLAS kinematic cuts: inclusive particles, p_T,jet in [158,315] GeV, |y_jet| < 2.1, R=0.4
- For fixed-order NLO calculation (p_T,jet > 90 GeV, R=0.4): 10%-30% enhancement of collinear configurations in the first bin of the theta_g-distribution when comparing PbPb to pp collisions
- Gluon fraction in vacuum approximately 70% for the fixed-order fiducial cuts; after quenching, this fraction goes down to 60%-45% depending on the q-hat value

## Context
This work extends the collimator formalism approach of Mehtar-Tani et al. (2017) and the energy loss calculations of Caucal et al. (2018, 2019) by applying them for the first time to groomed jet substructure observables. It contrasts with full jet quenching Monte Carlo models such as JetMed, the Hybrid Strong/Weak Coupling Model, and JetScape, which incorporate medium modifications directly into the shower evolution. The paper enables systematic quantification of how NLO corrections affect the vacuum baseline and extracted medium parameters (such as the transport coefficient q-hat), and provides a controlled framework to test whether current experimental data can disentangle colour coherence effects from selection bias effects in groomed jet measurements.
