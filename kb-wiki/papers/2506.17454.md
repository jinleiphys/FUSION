# Nuclear Cold QCD: Review and Future Strategy

> This review examines data from hadron-nucleus collisions, primarily focusing on hard processes like Drell-Yan, heavy flavor and quarkonium production. It highlights observed modifications of particle yields as functions of momentum and rapidity, aiming to clarify the underlying QCD effects in cold nuclear matter (CNM). The paper outlines strategies for future experiments, including the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC), to distinguish between these effects. Key questions address the universality of suppression mechanisms and the role of non-perturbative physics, providing a road map for upcoming nuclear data.

## Key claim

This review argues that despite decades of research, no unified theoretical framework exists to consistently describe cold nuclear matter effects across different hard processes, and that future experiments including the Electron-Ion Collider are needed to disentangle the relative contributions of nuclear parton distribution functions, gluon saturation, parton energy loss, and final-state interactions, which often produce similar suppression patterns.

## Method

The authors compile and review experimental data from fixed-target and collider experiments on Drell-Yan, heavy flavor, and quarkonium production in hadron-nucleus collisions, examining nuclear modification factors as functions of momentum fraction, Feynman x, transverse momentum, and rapidity. They compare these data against theoretical predictions from nuclear parton distribution function analyses, gluon saturation models, radiative energy loss calculations (including fully coherent energy loss), and final-state interaction models. The review identifies promising observables across multiple kinematic regimes that could help isolate individual cold nuclear matter effects in future measurements.

## Key numbers

- Drell-Yan fixed-target experiments span center-of-mass energies 15 to 38.7 GeV, with E772 using 800 GeV proton beam and E906 (SeaQuest) using 120 GeV proton beam
- LHC p+Pb Drell-Yan data from CMS at sqrt(s_NN) = 8.16 TeV cover dimuon mass range 15 to 600 GeV with pT from 15 to 120 GeV
- LHC p+Pb data reach x values as small as approximately 10^{-5}, where gluon saturation is expected to become significant
- E866 Drell-Yan data extended to x_F up to 0.95, providing first evidence for parton energy loss in the projectile at x_1 greater than approximately 0.7
- PHENIX d+Au data at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV show slight enhancement in pT spectra around pT = 2 GeV at forward rapidity
- ALICE probes rapidity regions -4.46 less than y less than -2.96 and 2.03 less than y less than 3.53, while LHCb accesses -4.0 less than y less than -2.5 and 1.5 less than y less than 4.0

## Context

This review extends the extensive experimental program on cold nuclear matter effects from fixed-target experiments (E772, E866, NA3, NA10, NA38, NA50, NA60, HERA-B) and collider experiments (PHENIX, STAR, ALICE, CMS, LHCb) at RHIC and LHC. It builds on prior nPDF analyses by de Florian (2011), Eskola (2016, 2021), and Kovarik (2015), as well as energy loss frameworks by Gyulassy (2000), Zakharov (2000), and Arleo (2010, 2012). The review identifies three fundamental questions regarding the relative roles of gluon saturation versus nPDFs, parton energy loss mechanisms, and final-state interactions, and outlines how upcoming facilities including the EIC, new fixed-target experiments, and ultraperipheral collisions at the LHC can address these questions.
