# Applying Noether's theorem to matter in the Milky Way: evidence for external perturbations and non-steady-state effects from Gaia Data Release 2

> We apply Noether's theorem to observations of main-sequence stars from the Gaia Data Release 2 archive to probe the matter distribution function of the Galaxy. That is, we examine the axial symmetry of stars at vertical heights $z$, $0.2 \le |z| \le 3$ kpc, to probe the quality of the angular momentum $L_z$ as an integral of motion. The failure of this symmetry test would speak to a Milky Way, in both its visible and dark matter, that is not isolated and/or not in steady state. The left-right symmetry-breaking pattern we have observed, north and south, reveals both effects, with a measured deviation from symmetry of typically 0.5%. We show that a prolate form of the gravitational distortion of the Milky Way by the Large Magellanic Cloud, determined from fits to the Orphan stream by Erkal et al., 2019, is compatible with the size and sign of the axial-symmetry-breaking effects we have discovered in our sample of up to 14.4 million main-sequence stars, speaking to a distortion of an emergent, rather than static, nature.

## Key claim

The paper finds that the Milky Way's stellar distribution shows a left-right axial asymmetry of typically 0.5% in star counts, which is interpreted as evidence that the Galaxy is not isolated and/or not in steady state; the authors specifically show that a prolate form of gravitational distortion from the Large Magellanic Cloud, including the Milky Way's reflex motion, is compatible with the observed asymmetry pattern, while oblate models are ruled out.

## Method

The authors selected up to 14.4 million main-sequence stars from Gaia Data Release 2 with vertical heights 0.2 ≤ |z| ≤ 3 kpc, Galactic latitude |b| > 30°, and Galactocentric radius R ∈ [7, 9] kpc, then counted stars left and right of the anti-center line (φ = 180°) separately for north (z>0) and south (z<0) samples. They computed the asymmetry parameter A(φ) = (n_L - n_R)/(n_L + n_R) as a function of Galactocentric azimuth and compared these measurements to predictions from distorted Milky Way halo models derived from fits to the Orphan stream by Erkal et al. (2019), including oblate and prolate halo shapes with and without the Milky Way's reflex motion.

## Key numbers

- Measured deviation from axial symmetry is typically 0.5% in the north-plus-south sample
- The north-plus-south asymmetry fit yields A(φ) = (-2.0963 ± 0.0003 + |180-φ|(-0.45050 ± 0.00003)) × 10^(-3), with both constant and linear terms non-zero beyond 5σ significance
- Sample size of up to 14.4 million main-sequence stars with |180°-φ| < 12°, and 11.7 million stars with |180°-φ| ≤ 6°
- The LMC system mass is 1.38^(+0.27)_(-0.24) × 10^11 M⊙, at distance 52 ± 2 kpc, producing a torque of 340,000 M⊙/pc
- χ²/(n=23) for the north-plus-south asymmetry: 3.64 (reflex prolate), 6.40 (oblate), 7.11 (prolate), 201 (reflex oblate)
- Raw number count asymmetry in the full sample is approximately 6 × 10^(-4) (0.06%)

## Context

This work extends studies of Galactic disk asymmetries by applying Noether's theorem to test whether angular momentum L_z is a conserved integral of motion, building on the theoretical framework of An (2017) regarding reflection symmetry in steady-state galaxies. The results directly confront the distorted halo models of Erkal et al. (2019), which were derived from Orphan stream fits, and discriminate between their oblate and prolate solutions. The findings connect to prior evidence for vertical asymmetries from Widrow et al. (2012) and Yanny et al. (2013), the Gaia phase-space spiral discovered by Antoja et al. (2018), and earlier suggestions of a prolate dark matter distribution from Banerjee & Jog (2011) and Posti et al. (2019).
