# Radiative Corrections in Super Rosenbluth Experiments

> Super Rosenbluth experiments, elastic electron-proton scattering experiments that eschew traditional electron detection and opt instead for the detection of the recoiling proton, have several experimental advantages. One claimed advantage is that radiative corrections are more favorable, i.e., smaller and with less kinematic dependence. In this paper, we explore this claim by conducting Monte Carlo simulations of both Super Rosenbluth and traditional Rosenbluth experiments with different models of radiative effects. When using a model that employs the peaking approximation, we indeed confirm the reduced kinematic dependence of the radiative corrections. However, we find that more sophisticated models that avoid the peaking approximation are unable to produce numerically stable results, due to a large enhancement to the cross section for bremsstrahlung radiation from the proton when the momentum transfer, $Q^2$, approaches zero. Since this enhancement is not modelled in the peaking approximation, a more robust approach to radiative corrections in Super Rosenbluth experiments is needed.

# Radiative Corrections in Super Rosenbluth Experiments

## Key claim

The authors confirm that within the peaking approximation, radiative corrections in Super Rosenbluth experiments (proton detection) are indeed smaller and less dependent on the virtual photon polarization parameter epsilon than in traditional electron-detection experiments. However, they find that more sophisticated non-peaking models produce numerically unstable results due to a large enhancement in the bremsstrahlung cross section from proton radiation when the momentum transfer Q^2 approaches zero, an effect not captured by the peaking approximation.

## Method

The authors performed Monte Carlo pseudo-experiments simulating both traditional electron-detection and Super Rosenbluth (proton-detection) elastic electron-proton scattering at 15 kinematic points spanning three Q^2 values (2.64, 3.2, and 4.1 GeV^2/c^2) and five epsilon values from 0.1 to 0.9. They compared radiative correction factors from three event generators: the NE-18 peaking approximation code, the ESEPP exact single-photon bremsstrahlung code, and the OLYMPUS radiative event generator which treats multi-photon bremsstrahlung with exponentiation and avoids the peaking approximation by evaluating the full bremsstrahlung matrix element including all interference terms.

## Key numbers

- Radiative corrections in the peaking approximation at Q^2 = 4.1 GeV^2/c^2 show reduced epsilon-dependence for proton detection compared to electron detection across epsilon values from 0.1 to 0.9
- At Q^2 = 3.2 GeV^2/c^2 and epsilon = 0.5, the differential cross section d sigma/d Omega in the proton detection experiment is approximately four times larger than in the electron detection experiment due to the Jacobian between solid angles
- The OLYMPUS generator with full bremsstrahlung calculation at Q^2 = 3.2 GeV^2/c^2 and epsilon = 0.5 shows large fluctuations in event weights when integrating over the full electron phase space, while the lepton-only version eliminates this variance
- The enhancement in cross section for proton radiation occurs when the outgoing electron has nearly zero energy, with the virtual photon propagator carrying a factor of 1/Q^2 as Q^2 approaches zero

## Context

This work extends the analysis of radiative corrections for Super Rosenbluth experiments first claimed by Qattan (2004), who stated that radiative corrections have smaller epsilon-dependence when the proton is detected. The authors confirm this claim within the peaking approximation developed by Ent (2001) for the NE-18 Experiment, but find that non-peaking models (the OLYMPUS generator described by Schmidt (2016) and Russell (2016), and the ESEPP code by Gramolin (2014)) reveal numerical instabilities from proton bremsstrahlung that the peaking approximation neglects. The results indicate that developing a numerically-stable non-peaking radiative correction procedure is needed for future Super Rosenbluth experiments, as the peaking approximation may be misleadingly inaccurate for this technique.
