# Event Topology Classifiers at the Large Hadron Collider

> Event classifiers are the most fundamental observables to probe the event topology of hadronic and nuclear collisions at relativistic energies. Over the last five decades, significant progress has been made to establish suitable event classifiers to probe different physics processes occurring in elementary $e^{+}e^{-}$ to heavy-ion collisions in a broad range of center of mass energies. One of the major motivations to revisit event classifiers at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) originates from the recent measurements of high multiplicity proton-proton collisions, which have revealed that these small collision systems exhibit features similar to the formation of quark-gluon plasma (QGP), traditionally believed to be only achievable in heavy nucleus-nucleus collisions at ultra-relativistic energies. To pinpoint the origin of these QGP-like phenomena with substantially reduced autocorrelation and selection biases, and to bring all collision systems on equal footing, along with charged-particle multiplicity, lately several event topology classifiers such as transverse sphericity, transverse spherocity, relative transverse activity classifier, and charged-particle flattenicity have been used extensively in experiments as well as in the phenomenological front. In addition, the infrared and collinear safety of event-shape observables makes them ideal for precision studies of jets and heavy-flavors at the LHC. In this review article, we summarise the motivation, scope, and practical use of these event-shape observables. The discussion integrates results and insights from all major LHC experiments, setting the stage for precision investigations for Run 3, Run 4, and future high luminosity upgrades of the LHC.

## Key claim

This review argues that event topology classifiers such as transverse sphericity, transverse spherocity, relative transverse activity classifiers, and charged-particle flattenicity can reduce autocorrelation and selection biases compared to charged-particle multiplicity alone, thereby placing all collision systems on equal footing for probing QGP-like phenomena in small systems; however, the authors explicitly state that "QGP-like" signals observed in selected events should be interpreted as the interplay of multihadron production dynamics, hadronization mechanisms, and global event geometry rather than as definitive evidence for QGP formation.

## Method

The authors conducted a comprehensive review of event topology classifiers used at the LHC, integrating results from all major LHC experiments (ALICE, ATLAS, CMS) and incorporating representative Monte Carlo simulations from PYTHIA 8 for proton-proton collisions and the AMPT model for heavy-ion collisions. The review covers the definitions, correlations with number of multi-parton interactions, selection biases, and applications to both soft probes (particle ratios, multiplicity) and hard probes (jets, heavy flavors), with discussion of extensions to heavy-ion collisions and machine learning techniques.

## Key numbers

* Charged-particle multiplicity at mid-pseudorapidity ($|\eta| < 0.8$) is measured by the ALICE silicon pixel detector and time projection chamber, while forward multiplicity uses V0 detectors covering $-3.7 < \eta < -1.7$ (V0C) and $2.8 < \eta < 5.1$ (V0A)
* The CMS experiment measures charged-particle multiplicity using tracklets from silicon pixel detector layers covering $|\eta| < 2.6$
* The ATLAS Inner Detector covers $|\eta| < 2.5$ with full azimuthal coverage for charged-particle multiplicity measurements
* The ALICE Fast Interaction Trigger (FIT) for Run 3 includes FT0, FV0, and FDD sub-detectors, while the Muon Forward Tracker covers $-3.6 < \eta < -2.5$
* The review uses PYTHIA8 simulations at $\sqrt{s}=13$ TeV for self-normalised charged-particle multiplicity distributions shown in mid and forward rapidity

## Context

This review extends the decades-long development of event shape observables first used in $e^{+}e^{-}$ collisions since the late 1970s (MovillaFernandez 1997, Donoghue 1979, Dasgupta 2003, Banfi 2004) and later applied at the Intersecting Storage Rings and S$p\bar{p}$S at CERN, as well as at Tevatron (CDF 2011). It directly addresses the need to understand QGP-like phenomena observed in high-multiplicity proton-proton collisions at the LHC (CMS 2016, ALICE 2019, ATLAS 2015), where measurements of radial and elliptic flow and enhanced strange hadron production have been reported. The review enables precision investigations for LHC Run 3, Run 4, and future high-luminosity upgrades by providing usage recommendations for event classifiers that can reduce selection biases compared to multiplicity-based event selection (ALICE 2019, ALICE 2022).
