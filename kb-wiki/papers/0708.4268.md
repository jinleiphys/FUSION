# Shell model study of the pairing correlations

> A systematic study of the pairing correlations as a function of temperature and angular momentum has been performed in the sd-shell region using the spherical shell model approach. The pairing correlations have been derived for even-even, even-odd and odd-odd systems near N=Z and also for the asymmetric case of N=Z+4. The results indicate that the pairing content and the behavior of pair correlations is similar in even-even and odd-mass nuclei. For odd-odd N=Z system, angular momentum I=0 state is an isospin, t=1 neutron-proton paired configuration. Further, these t=1 correlations are shown to be dramatically reduced for the asymmetric case of N=Z+4. The shell model results obtained are qualitatively explained within a simplified degenerate model.

# Shell model study of the pairing correlations

## Key claim

The pairing correlations in sd-shell nuclei show a smooth decrease with temperature and angular momentum rather than the sharp phase transition predicted by mean-field models, and the relative strengths of proton-proton, neutron-neutron, and proton-neutron isovector pairing are governed by isospin geometry, being equal only in T=0 states but generally different in T>0 states.

## Method

The authors performed spherical shell model diagonalizations using the USD interaction in the sd-shell for 28Si, 27Si, 26Al, and 24Ne, calculating the isovector monopole pair correlation energies via the canonical ensemble average over eigenstates. They computed both "correlated" pairing energy (with full two-body interaction) and "uncorrelated" pairing energy (with monopole matrix elements removed), and compared results to a simplified degenerate shell model with SO(5) dynamical symmetry.

## Key numbers

- For 28Si at Temp=0 and I=0, the correlated pairing energy is 4.6 MeV for both neutron-proton and identical particle channels
- The degenerate model scale factor for 28Si is 3.06 MeV, derived from the model prediction of 3/2 for the correlation energy
- For 27Si at zero temperature, proton-proton pair energy is 4.4 MeV, neutron-neutron is 3.0 MeV (about 30% lower due to blocking), and neutron-proton is 3.7 MeV
- The degenerate model scaled values for 27Si (T=1/2, N=4) give E_pp=4.6 MeV, E_np=3.8 MeV, E_nn=3.1 MeV, compared to shell model values of 4.4, 3.7, and 3.0 MeV
- For 26Al at I=0, neutron-proton pairing energy is 7.9 MeV versus like-particle pairing of 2.7 MeV; the degenerate model gives scaled values of 7.8 and 2.2 MeV
- For 24Ne at zero temperature, neutron pair energy is 5.5 MeV, proton pair energy is 3.4 MeV, and neutron-proton pair energy is only 1.2 MeV; degenerate model scaled values are 5.9, 2.8, and 1.0 MeV
- Calculated moments of inertia for low-lying states: 28Si = 2.62205 hbar^2/MeV, 27Si = 2.66220 hbar^2/MeV
- The phase transition temperature for 28Si is around Temp=2 MeV, compared to 0.5-0.7 MeV in HFB and shell model Monte-Carlo studies; for 24Mg using the HFB-type pairing expression, the transition was found at 0.9 MeV

## Context

This work extends the study of pairing correlations in finite nuclear systems beyond the mean-field BCS/HFB approximation, which predicts sharp phase transitions that are not observed experimentally. The results complement a similar sd-shell study by Horoi and Zelevinsky (2007) using the same model space but addressing different issues. The finding that pairing correlations persist at high temperatures and angular momenta contradicts mean-field predictions and supports earlier work in simplified few-level models by Frauendorf et al. (2003) showing re-entrance of pairing at finite temperature, though the effect is less dramatic in the realistic sd-shell space. The isospin analysis connects to the systematic experimental studies by Baktash et al. (1992) showing that moments of inertia differ by only 2% between even-even and odd nuclei, which the present calculations reproduce with values of 2.62 and 2.66 hbar^2/MeV respectively. The work enables further investigation of pairing fluctuations in realistic nuclear systems and provides benchmarks for number-projected mean-field approaches at finite temperature.
