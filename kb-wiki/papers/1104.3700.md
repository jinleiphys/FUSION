# Double Beta Decay, Nuclear Structure and Physics beyond the Standard Model

> Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay ($0νββ$) is presently the only known experiment to distinguisch between Dirac neutrinos, different from their antiparticles, and Majorana neutrinos, identical with their antiparticles. In addition $0νββ$ allows to determine the absolute scale of the neutrino masses. This is not possible with neutrino oscillations. To determine the neutrino masses one must assume, that the light Majorana neutrino exchange is the leading mechanism for $0νββ$ and that the matrix element of this transition can ba calculated reliably. The experimental $0νββ$ transition amplitude in this mechanism is a product of the light left handed effective Majorana neutrino mass and of this transition matrix element. The different methods, Quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), Shell Model (SM), Projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (PHFB) and Interacting Boson Model (IBM2) used in the literature and the reliability of the matrix elements in these approaches are reviewed. In the second part it is investigated how one can determine the leading mechanism or mechanisms from the data of the $0νββ$ decay in different nuclei. Explicite expressions are given for the transition matrix elements. is shown, that possible interference terms allow to test CP (Charge and Parity conjugation) violation.

## Key claim

Neutrinoless double beta decay is the only known experiment that can distinguish Dirac from Majorana neutrinos and determine the absolute neutrino mass scale, but this determination requires the assumption that light left-handed Majorana neutrino exchange is the leading mechanism and that the nuclear matrix element can be calculated reliably.

## Method

The paper reviews four many-body methods used to calculate the nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double beta decay: Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA), Shell Model (SM), Projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (PHFB), and Interacting Boson Model (IBM2). In the second part, the author derives expressions for the transition amplitude assuming multiple possible mechanisms (light Majorana neutrino exchange, heavy neutrino exchange, SUSY contributions) and shows how measurements in different nuclei can identify the leading mechanism and test CP violation through interference terms.

## Key numbers

- For the decay $^{76}$Ge $\to$ $^{76}$Se, the QRPA matrix element $M^{0\nu}_\nu$ is 4.69 (with deformation) or 5.30 (spherical), yielding half-lives of $7.15 \times 10^{26}$ years and $5.60 \times 10^{26}$ years respectively for an assumed effective Majorana mass of 50 meV
- For $^{150}$Nd $\to$ $^{150}$Sm, the matrix element is 3.34 (deformed) or 6.12 (spherical), with corresponding half-lives of $0.41 \times 10^{26}$ years and $0.12 \times 10^{26}$ years for 50 meV
- For $^{160}$Gd $\to$ $^{160}$Dy, the matrix element is 3.76 with half-life $2.26 \times 10^{26}$ years for 50 meV
- The Ikeda Sum Rule is violated by 34 to 50% in the Shell Model depending on the single-particle basis used, while QRPA satisfies it exactly
- Using the QRPA matrix element and the claimed experimental half-life from Klapdor et al. of $(2.23^{+0.44}_{-0.31}) \times 10^{25}$ years for $^{76}$Ge, the effective Majorana neutrino mass is $0.24$ eV with experimental uncertainty $\pm 0.02$ eV and theoretical uncertainty $\pm 0.01$ eV
- The ratio of half-lives for $^{100}$Mo to $^{76}$Ge is constrained to $0.15 \le T^{0\nu}_{1/2}(^{100}\text{Mo})/T^{0\nu}_{1/2}(^{76}\text{Ge}) \le 0.18$ for the light Majorana neutrino exchange mechanism

## Context

This review extends the long-standing effort to calculate nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double beta decay, comparing the QRPA approach used by the Tubingen-Bratislava-Jyvaskyla group with the Shell Model of the Strasbourg-Madrid group (Poves et al.), the projected HFB method of Rath and coworkers, and the IBM2 of Iachello and Barea. The work shows that the Shell Model's restricted single-particle basis leads to violation of the Ikeda sum rule and systematically smaller matrix elements (by roughly a factor of 2) compared to QRPA, PHFB, and IBM2. The second part enables future experimental programs to identify the dominant mechanism for neutrinoless double beta decay by measuring decays in multiple nuclei and comparing half-life ratios, and to test CP conservation through the relative phase of interfering mechanisms.
