# Exact Baryon, Strangeness and Charge Conservation in Hadronic Gas Models

> Relativistic heavy ion collisions are studied assuming that particles can be described by a hadron gas in thermal and chemical equilibrium. The exact conservation of baryon number, strangeness and charge are explicitly taken into account. For heavy ions the effect arising from the neutron surplus becomes important and leads to a substantial increase in e.g. the $π^-/π^+$ ratio. A method is developed which is very well suited for the study of small systems.

# Exact Baryon, Strangeness and Charge Conservation in Hadronic Gas Models

## Key claim

Exact conservation of baryon number, strangeness, and charge in a hadronic gas model produces corrections to particle ratios that are significant for small systems and for systems with a neutron surplus, but the method becomes impractical for large systems where the grand canonical ensemble is more appropriate. The authors emphasize that these corrections do not contain information about dynamics and do not introduce any new parameters.

## Method

The authors derive a canonical partition function for a hadronic gas with three exactly conserved quantum numbers (baryon number B, strangeness S, charge Q) by projecting the grand canonical partition function onto fixed quantum numbers using integral representations and introducing intermediate angles to factorize the angular dependence. This yields an expression involving products of modified Bessel functions with ten nested summations. Particle densities are then obtained by differentiating the partition function, producing R-factors that replace the fugacity factors used in the grand canonical ensemble, and all resonances are allowed to decay into stable particles using branching ratios.

## Context

This work extends the exact canonical treatment of baryon number and strangeness conservation from Cleymans and Muronga (1996) to also include exact charge conservation, allowing treatment of systems that are not isospin-symmetric. It differs from the grand canonical approach used by Braun-Munzinger, Stachel, and collaborators (1995-1996) and from the approach of Becattini (1996) and Becattini and Heinz (1997) by being more analytic. The method is designed for studying the transition from small systems (e+e-, p-p, p-pbar) to medium systems (S-S), and the numerical results can be compared with data from the E866 collaboration at Brookhaven. The authors argue that the disagreement between their canonical prediction and preliminary E866 data for the pbar/pi+ ratio proves that antiprotons are not in thermal equilibrium.
