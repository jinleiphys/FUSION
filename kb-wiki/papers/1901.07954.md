# The algebraic molecular model in $^{12}$C and its application to the $α$+$^{12}$C scattering: from densities and transition densities to optical potentials and nuclear formfactors

> The algebraic molecular model is used in $^{12}$C to construct densities and transition densities connecting low-lying states of the rotovibrational spectrum, first and foremost those belonging to the rotational bands based on the ground and the Hoyle states. These densities are then used as basic ingredients to calculate, besides electromagnetic transition probabilities, nuclear potentials and formfactors to describe elastic and inelastic $α$+$^{12}$C scattering processes. The calculated densities and transition densities are also compared with those obtained by directly solving the problem of three interacting alpha's within a three-body approach where continuum effects, relevant in particular for the Hoyle state, are properly taken into account.

# The algebraic molecular model in \(^{12}\)C and its application to the \(\alpha\)+\(^{12}\)C scattering

## Key claim
The algebraic molecular model can reproduce the ground-state and Hoyle-band densities and transition densities in \(^{12}\)C, and these can be used as inputs for double-folding optical potentials and nuclear formfactors for \(\alpha\)+\(^{12}\)C scattering. However, the model parameters (\(\beta\) and \(\beta_{\text{Hoyle}}\)) are tuned to match known observables (radius, \(B(E2)\) values) rather than derived from first principles, and the Hoyle-state density shows a central depletion and larger radial extension that are qualitatively consistent with microscopic three-body calculations.

## Method
The intrinsic density of \(^{12}\)C is constructed as a sum of three Gaussian \(\alpha\)-particle densities placed at the vertices of an equilateral triangle, with the radial parameter \(\beta\) optimized to fit the ground-state radius and the \(B(E2)\) to the first \(2^+\) state. The Hoyle-band intrinsic density is obtained by symmetric radial displacements (breathing mode) with amplitude \(\chi_1\) set by the measured monopole matrix element \(M(E0)\). These intrinsic densities and transition densities are expanded in spherical harmonics, and the lowest multipoles (\(\lambda\mu = 00, 20, 33\)) are used to construct laboratory-frame densities and transition densities for states in the ground and Hoyle rotational bands. The real part of the optical potential and the nuclear formfactors for \(\alpha\)+\(^{12}\)C scattering are then obtained via double folding with the M3Y Reid-type nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Key numbers
- \(\alpha = 0.56(2)\) fm\(^{-2}\): width parameter of the Gaussian \(\alpha\)-particle density.
- \(\beta = 1.82\) fm: radial parameter for the ground-state triangular configuration (compared to \(\beta = 1.74(4)\) fm used in Del17).
- \(\beta_{\text{Hoyle}} = 3.02\) fm: radial parameter for the Hoyle-band triangular configuration.
- \(\chi_1 = 0.247\) fm: intrinsic transition matrix element for the breathing mode, set using the measured \(M(E0)\) from Stre.
- The ground-state and Hoyle-state radial densities are compared in Fig. 4; the Hoyle state shows larger radial extension and central depletion.
- The intraband quadrupole transition density for the Hoyle band peaks at a larger radius than that for the ground band; the interband quadrupole and monopole transition densities have a node on the surface.

## Context
This work extends the algebraic cluster model of Iachello and collaborators (Bij02, Bij14, Bij95, Del17, Stel), itself a reformulation of Wheeler's alpha-cluster molecular model, to provide structure inputs for reaction calculations. The densities and transition densities are compared with those from a three-body hyperspherical approach using an Ali-Bodmer \(\alpha\)-\(\alpha\) potential (Nguyen13, Ishikawa14, JCasal16), which shows that the \(2^+_1\) state corresponds to an equilateral triangle while the Hoyle state exhibits a more extended, prolate-triangle configuration. The resulting double-folded potentials and formfactors enable future coupled-channel calculations of \(\alpha\)+\(^{12}\)C scattering, which may reveal interference effects between direct and two-step population of the \(2^+_2\) state and provide information on the radial size of the Hoyle band.
