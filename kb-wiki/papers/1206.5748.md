# The origin of order in random matrices with symmetries

> From Noether's theorem we know symmetries lead to conservation laws. What is left to nature is the ordering of conserved quantities; for example, the quantum numbers of the ground state. In physical systems the ground state is generally associated with `low' quantum numbers and symmetric, low-dimensional irreps, but there is no \textit{a priori} reason to expect this. By constructing random matrices with nontrivial point-group symmetries, I find the ground state is always dominated by extremal low-dimensional irreps. Going further, I suggest this explains the dominance of J=0 g.s. even for random two-body interactions.

## Key claim

By constructing random matrices with imposed point-group symmetries, the author finds that the ground state is always dominated by extremal low-dimensional irreps, and suggests this explains the dominance of J=0 ground states even for random two-body interactions, though the analysis relies on variance arguments rather than exact ground-state identification.

## Method

The author constructs random matrices with imposed discrete rotational symmetries (C_n, tetrahedral, octahedral, cubic) and continuous SU(2) symmetry, then block-diagonalizes them into irreducible representations. For each irrep block, the variance of eigenvalues is computed analytically assuming independent random matrix elements with equal variance, and the ground state is predicted to belong to the irrep with largest width. For the SU(2) case, the author computes effective widths sigma_J^eff = sqrt(N_J) sigma_J using numerically integrated Legendre polynomial variances and subspace dimensions N_J, then generates random Gaussian energies to predict ground-state J fractions, comparing against configuration-interaction diagonalizations of random two-body Hamiltonians in a shell-model basis.

## Key numbers

- For C_n symmetry: k=0 irrep has variance approximately sigma_0^2 (2n+1), while other irreps have approximately sigma_0^2 (n+1)
- For tetrahedron: 1-dimensional irrep variance = 10 sigma_0^2, 3-dimensional irrep variance = 2 sigma_0^2
- For octahedron: 1-dimensional irrep variance = 18 sigma_0^2, 2-dimensional irrep variance = 6 sigma_0^2, 3-dimensional irrep variance = 2 sigma_0^2
- For cube: 1-dimensional irreps have variance 20 sigma_0^2, 3-dimensional irreps have variance 4 sigma_0^2
- For SU(2): computed values of integral in Eq. (13) (leaving off factor 4 pi^2 sigma_bar^2) are 1.571, 0.393, 0.245, 0.178, 0.139 for J = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4 respectively
- Simulation uses eight fermions (neutrons) in the 1p_1/2-1p_3/2-0f_5/2-0f_7/2 shell-model space

## Context

This work extends the observation that rotationally invariant but otherwise random two-body Hamiltonians in nuclear structure tend to have J=0 ground states, first noted by Johnson, Bertsch, and Dean (1998) and confirmed by Zhao, Arima, and Yoshinaga (2004) and Zhao, Arima, and Yoshinaga (2004). Previous explanations focused on widths (Bertsch, Fujiwara, and Porrino, 1999; Papenbrock and Weidenmueller, 2004) or statistical averaging in single-j shells (Molinari, Vigezzi, and Zawischa, 2000), but the author notes a recent Letter stated "the simple question of symmetry and chaos asks for a simple answer which is still missing" (Vogt, 2008). The random-matrix approach with imposed symmetries provides a simple variance-based explanation that is decoupled from microphysics, potentially explaining the robustness and universality of the J=0 ground-state dominance.
