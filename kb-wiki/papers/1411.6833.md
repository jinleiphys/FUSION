# Superfluid phases of triplet pairing and rapid cooling of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A

> In a simple model it is demonstrated that the neutron star surface temperature evolution is sensitive to the phase state of the triplet superfluid condensate. A multicomponent triplet pairing of superfluid neutrons in the core of a neutron star with participation of several magnetic quantum numbers leads to neutrino energy losses exceeding the losses from the unicomponent pairing. A phase transition of the neutron condensate into the multicomponent state triggers more rapid cooling of superfluid core in neutron stars. This makes it possible to simulate an anomalously rapid cooling of neutron stars within the minimal cooling paradigm without employing any exotic scenarios suggested earlier for rapid cooling of isolated neutron star in Cassiopeia A.

# Superfluid phases of triplet pairing and rapid cooling of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A

## Key claim

The anomalously rapid cooling of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A can be explained within the minimal cooling paradigm if the neutron superfluid condensate in the core undergoes a phase transition from a one-component $^{3}$P$_{2}(m_j=0)$ state into a multicomponent state involving several magnetic quantum numbers, which enhances neutrino energy losses from pair breaking and formation processes. This explanation requires that the actual cooling rate of the Cas A neutron star is indeed rapid, which remains under debate due to systematic uncertainties in the Chandra observations.

## Method

The author derived an analytic expression for the neutrino emissivity from pair breaking and formation (PBF) processes in multicomponent $^{3}$P$_{2}$ superfluid phases of neutrons, extending earlier work that used an averaged gap approximation. This expression was then applied to a simplified cooling model of a superfluid neutron core enclosed in a thin envelope, assuming a constant superfluid transition temperature $T_c$ over the core, and solving the global thermal balance equation to obtain cooling curves for different phase states of the condensate.

## Key numbers

- The observed surface temperature of the Cas A neutron star is about $2.12 \times 10^{6}$ K.
- For standard cooling via the modified Urca process, the expected surface temperature decline over 10 years would be $0.2\%-0.3\%$.
- The relative neutrino emissivity factor $Z$ for the one-component phase $O_0$ ($m_j=0$) is 1; for the two-component phase $O_{\pm 3}$ it is 3.25; for $O_1$ it is 2.3528; for $O_2$ it is 3.8258.
- The phase transition between the nearly degenerate low-energy group ($O_0$ and $O_{\pm 3}$) and the higher group ($O_1$ and $O_2$) is predicted to occur at $T \simeq 0.7 T_c$ at Fermi momentum $p_F \simeq 2.1$ fm$^{-1}$.
- The superfluid transition temperature for the Cas A neutron star is constrained to $T_c(\rho) \approx (5-8) \times 10^{8}$ K.
- The adopted neutron star model has mass $M = 1.65 M_{\odot}$ and radius $R = 10.3$ km.
- The weighted mean temperature decline rate from all Chandra detectors is $2.9 \pm 0.5_{\mathrm{stat}} \pm 1_{\mathrm{sys}}\%$ over 10 years; excluding the ACIS-S graded mode data gives $1.4 \pm 0.6_{\mathrm{stat}} \pm 1_{\mathrm{sys}}\%$.

## Context

This work extends the minimal cooling paradigm of Page et al. (2004, 2009) and addresses the problem identified by Shternin et al. (2011) and Elshamouty et al. (2013) that PBF processes in the standard one-component $^{3}$P$_{2}(m_j=0)$ condensate are insufficient to explain the rapid cooling claimed for the Cas A neutron star. The author argues that multicomponent $^{3}$P$_{2}$ phases, cataloged by Clark et al. and Khodel et al., can naturally enhance neutrino losses without invoking exotic scenarios such as softened pion modes, quarks, or axions. The work enables more realistic simulations of neutron star cooling that incorporate the density-dependent phase state of the superfluid condensate, though the actual cooling rate of Cas A remains controversial due to calibration issues with Chandra detectors, as discussed by Elshamouty et al. (2013) and Posselt et al. (2013).
