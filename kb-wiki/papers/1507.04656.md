# Proton gaps and cooling of neutron stars with a stiff hadronic EoS

> The recent measurements of the masses of the pulsar J00737-3039B and of the companion J1756-2251 and pulsars PSR J1614-2230, PSR J0348-0432 demonstrate the existence of compact stars with masses in a broad range from 1.2 to 2 $M_\odot$. To fulfill the constraint $M_{\rm max}>2M_{\odot}$ and to demonstrate the possibility of cooling scenarios for purely hadronic and further for hybrid stars we exploit the stiff DD2 hadronic equation of state producing a maximum neutron star mass $M\simeq 2.43 M_{\odot}$. We show that the "nuclear medium cooling" scenario for neutron stars comfortably explains the whole set of cooling curves just by a variation of the star masses without the necessity for the occurrence of the direct Urca reaction. To describe the cooling data with the very stiff DD2 equation of state we select a proton gap profile from those exploited in the literature and allow for a variation of the effective pion gap controlling the efficiency of the medium modified Urca process. Fast cooling of young neutron stars like it is seen in the data for Cas A is explained with the DD2 equation of state when the following conditions are provided: the presence of an efficient medium modified Urca process, and a large proton gap at densities $n\le 2n_0$ vanishing for $n\ge (2.5 - 3) n_0$, where $n_0$ is the saturation nuclear density.

# Proton gaps and cooling of neutron stars with a stiff hadronic EoS

## Key claim

The "nuclear medium cooling" scenario can explain the full set of observed neutron star cooling curves, including the fast cooling of Cassiopeia A, using the very stiff DD2 hadronic equation of state without requiring the direct Urca reaction, provided that the proton pairing gap is large at densities below twice saturation density and vanishes above 2.5–3 times saturation density, and that the medium modified Urca process is efficient.

## Method

The authors computed neutron star cooling histories using the stiff DD2 hadronic equation of state (EoS) and compared them to results from the softer HDD EoS used in prior work. They varied the density-dependent proton 1S0 pairing gap (testing multiple gap profiles from the literature, including models labeled Yak, AV18, EEHOr, and CCYms) and the effective pion gap controlling the medium modified Urca neutrino emissivity, while keeping other cooling inputs (neutrino emissivities, specific heat, crust properties, heat conductivity) fixed from earlier calculations.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass for the DD2 EoS: M_max ≃ 2.43 M_sun
- With the HDD EoS and model I proton gap, Cas A cooling data are described by a neutron star of mass M_CasA ≃ 1.497 M_sun
- With the DD2 EoS, EEHOr proton gap, and dotted pion gap curve, Cas A cooling data are explained by a neutron star of mass M = 1.950 M_sun
- With the DD2 EoS, EEHOr proton gap, and solid (stronger softening) pion gap curve, Cas A cooling data are explained by a neutron star of mass M = 1.797 M_sun
- With the DD2 EoS, CCYms proton gap, and solid pion gap curve, Cas A HRC-S data are reproduced with M = 1.794 M_sun
- The hot source XMMU-J1732 is explained by a neutron star of mass ∼ 1.5 M_sun (dotted pion gap) or ∼ 1.4 M_sun (solid pion gap)
- The direct Urca reaction threshold is not reached within the DD2 EoS

## Context

This work extends the "nuclear medium cooling" scenario previously developed by the same group (Blaschke 2004, Grigorian 2005, Blaschke 2011, Blaschke 2013) from softer EoS (HHJ and HDD) to the stiffer DD2 EoS. It addresses the observational constraint from pulsar mass measurements (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013) requiring maximum neutron star masses above 2 M_sun, and recent radius determinations (Bogdanov 2012) favoring larger radii. The results contrast with the "minimal cooling paradigm" of Ho (2014) which explains Cas A with a 1.44 M_sun star but cannot simultaneously describe the hottest and coldest objects. The work enables future studies of hybrid stars with deconfinement phase transitions from this stiff hadronic EoS to color superconducting quark matter.
