# SU(2) Skyrme Model for Hadron

## Key claim
The SU(2) Skyrme model, which treats hadrons as topological solitons (Skyrmions), can be used to investigate nucleon and delta masses, but the paper presents only the formal framework and equations for calculating these masses, noting that numerical solution of the profile function and subsequent mass calculations are "work in progress", no actual numerical mass values are computed or reported.

## Method
The authors review the SU(2) Skyrme model Lagrangian density, which includes the chiral model term, the Skyrme term for stabilization, and a pion mass term. They derive the Euler-Lagrange equations in matrix form, then specialize to the static case where the left chiral currents have zero time component. Using a spherically symmetric Skyrme ansatz with a profile function g(r), they obtain a second-order nonlinear differential equation for g(r) and state that it will be solved numerically using the finite difference method with boundary conditions g(0)=π and g(∞)=0. The static energy, Skyrmion moment of inertia, and rotational energy are expressed in terms of integrals over the profile function, and the nucleon and delta masses are given as the sum of static mass and rotational energy contributions for spin 1/2 and spin 3/2, respectively.

## Key numbers
- Pion decay constant: F ≈ 123 MeV (used in the Lagrangian density)
- The static energy is expressed in units where (F/4a) = (1/12π²), where a is a dimensionless constant
- The pion mass parameter in scaled units is m_π = 2M_π / (aF), where M_π is the physical pion mass
- Boundary conditions for the profile function: g(0) = π, g(∞) = 0
- Nucleon mass formula: m_N = M + j(j+1)/(2I) for j = 1/2, where M = E_static / c²
- Delta mass formula: m_Δ = M + j(j+1)/(2I) for j = 3/2
- Wess-Zumino quantization condition requires j = 1/2, 3/2, 5/2, ..., implying the Skyrmion is a fermion

## Context
This paper reviews the SU(2) Skyrme model originally proposed by Skyrme (1962) as a framework for describing hadrons as topological solitons in a nonlinear sigma model. The work extends the classical Skyrme model formalism by presenting the full derivation of static energy, scale stability analysis, topological charge, and quantization via collective coordinates. The paper does not present new numerical results but provides the complete theoretical framework needed for subsequent numerical computation of nucleon and delta masses, which the authors state is "work in progress." This review is situated within the broader context of soliton models for hadrons, including the discovery of pentaquarks in 2003 which renewed interest in soliton approaches to elementary particles.
