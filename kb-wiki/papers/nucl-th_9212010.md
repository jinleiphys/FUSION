# Chiral colour-dielectric model with perturbative quantum pions and gluons

> Pionic contributions to static nucleon properties are calculated in a chiral extension of the colour-dielectric model. The pion field and residual gluon field are treated perturbatively. It is shown that with a simple choice for the energy of the scalar confining field and in the chiral limit, the system of equations describing the bare soliton and the perturbative pion and gluon fields may be cast in a dimensionless, parameter free form for large glueball mass. This enables a formula for the masses of the nucleon and delta including leading order pionic and gluonic contributions and corrections for spurious centre-of-mass motion, valid for a wide range of input parameters determining the bare soliton solutions, to be derived. A further consequence of the scaling behaviour is that pionic contributions to nucleon properties, calculated using the methods of the cloudy bag model, are insensitive to the soliton parameters, once the size of the soliton is fixed. The model results are very similar to those of the cloudy bag model but the predicted masses are about 20\% too large, and the pionic contributions to charge radii are underestimated.

# Chiral colour-dielectric model with perturbative quantum pions and gluons

## Key claim

The paper shows that in a chiral extension of the colour-dielectric model, with a simple quadratic potential for the scalar confining field and in the chiral limit, the system of equations for the bare soliton and perturbative pion and gluon fields can be cast in a dimensionless, parameter-free form for large glueball mass. However, the predicted nucleon and delta masses are about 20% too large, and pionic contributions to charge radii are underestimated compared to experiment.

## Method

The authors construct a chiral colour-dielectric model Hamiltonian including quark fields, a scalar confining field χ, perturbative pion fields, and residual gluon fields. They solve the coupled differential equations for the quark wavefunctions, the χ field, the pion field, and the gluon field as a non-linear boundary value problem. Pionic and gluonic contributions are treated in second-order perturbation theory, and centre-of-mass corrections are estimated using momentum expectation values for the quarks and the χ field. The pion coupling strength is fixed through the Goldberger-Treiman relation using the experimental pion decay constant and the calculated bare axial coupling constant, while the strong coupling constant α_s is adjusted to reproduce the nucleon-delta mass splitting.

## Key numbers

- Bare axial coupling constant: g_A^b = 1.318 (dimensionless, from scaled solution)
- Pion decay constant: f_π = 93 MeV (taken from experiment)
- Nucleon-delta mass splitting: 295 MeV (used to fix α_s)
- Isoscalar nucleon radius: 0.75 fm (used to fix soliton scale)
- Quark contribution to proton charge radius: 20% larger in soliton model than in Cloudy Bag Model (CBM) with bag radius R = 1.1 fm
- Pionic term ⟨r²⟩_π: 10% smaller in soliton model than in CBM
- Proton charge radius (excluding centre-of-mass corrections): 0.93 fm in soliton calculation vs. 0.87 fm in CBM
- Neutron charge radius (excluding centre-of-mass corrections): -0.31 fm in soliton calculation vs. -0.34 fm in CBM
- Pion generates about 25% of the nucleon-delta mass splitting
- Pionic energy shift decreased by about 17% when going from massless to massive pion
- Scaled constants from numerical solution: ε₀ = 2.426, c₁ = 1.456, c₂ = 6.68, c₃ = -0.04617, c₄ = -0.1469, c₅ = 16.12

## Context

This work extends the colour-dielectric model (CDM) calculations of Williams and Dodd (1991) by including both pionic and gluonic contributions to nucleon properties, along with centre-of-mass corrections. It follows the approach used to derive the Cloudy Bag Model (CBM) from the MIT bag model by Thomas (1984) and others. The scaling argument follows McGovern, Birse, and Spanos (1991) for large glueball mass. The results are compared directly with CBM calculations by Chin (1983) and others, showing qualitative similarity but with the soliton model predicting larger masses and smaller pionic contributions to charge radii. The work enables systematic testing of whether consistent values of the strong coupling constant can be obtained over a range of soliton parameters, and it identifies that the pion form factor falls off too quickly in momentum space compared to the CBM form factor.
