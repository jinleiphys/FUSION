# Gross shell structure at high spin in heavy nuclei

> Experimental nuclear moments of inertia at high spins along the yrast line have been determined systematically and found to differ from the rigid-body values. The difference is attributed to shell effects and these have been calculated microscopically. The data and quantal calculations are interpreted by means of the semiclassical Periodic Orbit Theory. From this new perspective, features in the moments of inertia as a function of neutron number and spin, as well as their relation to the shell energies can be understood. Gross shell effects persist up to the highest angular momenta observed.

## Key claim

Gross shell effects persist up to the highest angular momenta observed, and the substantial deviations of experimental moments of inertia from rigid-body values at high spin are caused by shell structure rather than by pair correlations, which are expected to be small in that regime.

## Method

The authors systematically extracted yrast moments of inertia from experimental data for even-Z nuclei in the mass range 80–220, fitting the highest 10ℏ of the yrast line with a linear function E(I) = E₀ + I(I+1)/(2𝒥). They compared these experimental values to rigid-body moments of inertia calculated from ground-state deformations and to microscopic cranked Woods-Saxon-Strutinsky calculations without pairing. The results were interpreted using semiclassical Periodic Orbit Theory, which relates shell structure to classical periodic orbits in the nuclear potential.

## Key numbers

- Experimental deviations of moments of inertia from rigid-body values: −25% for N ∼ 75, +20% for N ∼ 86, −15% for N ∼ 95, −40% for N ∼ 100, and −65% for N ∼ 120
- The rigid-body moment of inertia formula used: 𝒥_rig [ℏ²/MeV] = 0.01253 A^(5/3) (1 + 0.5 √(5/4π) β_g) + 0.048225 A
- Minimum spin for inclusion in the fit: >7ℏ for N<50, >10ℏ for 50<N<82, >12ℏ for N>82
- Maximum spin thresholds for inclusion: >15ℏ for N<50, >16ℏ for 50<N<82, >17ℏ for N>82
- Calculated shell moments of inertia were evaluated at rotational frequency ω = 0.3 MeV/ℏ
- Experimental shell moments of inertia are shifted about 10% to the negative side relative to calculated ones, attributed to residual dynamic pair correlations

## Context

This work extends the earlier observation by Pashkevich and Frauendorf (1974) that shell effects determine moments of inertia along the yrast line, and provides a systematic survey across a wide mass range. The authors connect their findings to the Periodic Orbit Theory developed by Brack and Bhaduri (1997) and to the Balian-Bloch (1972, 1974) treatment of shell structure in spherical cavities. The results enable a qualitative understanding of high-spin phenomena such as high-K isomers and smooth terminating bands, and draw analogies to persistent currents in metallic clusters and quantum dots as discussed by Frauendorf (2001).
