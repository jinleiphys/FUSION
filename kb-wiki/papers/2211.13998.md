# Deep-neural-network approach to solving the ab initio nuclear structure problem

> Predicting the structure of quantum many-body systems from the first principles of quantum mechanics is a common challenge in physics, chemistry, and material science. Deep machine learning has proven to be a powerful tool for solving condensed matter and chemistry problems, while for atomic nuclei it is still quite challenging because of the complicated nucleon-nucleon interactions, which strongly couple the spatial, spin, and isospin degrees of freedom. By combining essential physics of the nuclear wave functions and the strong expressive power of artificial neural networks, we develop FeynmanNet, a deep-learning variational quantum Monte Carlo approach for \emph{ab initio} nuclear structure. We show that FeynmanNet can provide very accurate solutions of ground-state energies and wave functions for $^4$He, $^6$Li, and even up to $^{16}$O as emerging from the leading-order and next-to-leading-order Hamiltonians of pionless effective field theory. Compared to the conventional diffusion Monte Carlo approaches, which suffer from the severe inherent fermion-sign problem, FeynmanNet reaches such a high accuracy in a variational way and scales polynomially with the number of nucleons. Therefore, it paves the way to a highly accurate and efficient \emph{ab initio} method for predicting nuclear properties based on the realistic interactions between nucleons.

## Key claim
FeynmanNet provides highly accurate ground-state energies and wave functions for nuclei up to ¹⁶O using pionless effective field theory Hamiltonians, but the accuracy is demonstrated only for leading-order (LO) and next-to-leading-order (NLO) Hamiltonians, not for fully realistic nuclear interactions.

## Method
The authors developed FeynmanNet, a deep-learning variational quantum Monte Carlo approach that represents the nuclear wave function as a complex-valued combination of Jastrow factors and multiple Slater determinants with backflow-transformed orbitals. The neural network architecture encodes translational invariance, antisymmetry, parity, total isospin projection, and time-reversal symmetry, and is trained by minimizing the energy expectation using stochastic reconfiguration with Metropolis Monte Carlo sampling.

## Key numbers
- For ⁴He at LO, FeynmanNet converges to the numerically exact hyperspherical-harmonics (HH) value of −28.17 MeV after about 200 iterations
- For ⁶Li at LO, FeynmanNet gives ground-state energies lower than the ANN-SJ result by about 500 keV and lower than the HH result by about 300 keV
- For ¹⁶O at LO, FeynmanNet yields an energy lower than the auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) result by more than 1 MeV
- For ⁴He at NLO, FeynmanNet reaches an accuracy of about 50 keV after 200 iterations and agrees with the HH value within 30 keV after 500 iterations
- The backflow transformation lowers the Slater-Jastrow energy for ¹⁶O by about 7 MeV with a single determinant

## Context
This work extends the application of deep neural network variational Monte Carlo methods from electronic systems (Pfau 2020, Hermann 2020) to nuclear many-body problems, overcoming limitations of prior ANN approaches (Adams 2021, Gnech 2021) that suffered from incorrect nodal surfaces due to single Slater determinants. The method improves upon conventional diffusion Monte Carlo methods (Carlson 2015) that face the fermion-sign problem, and enables polynomial-scaling ab initio calculations for medium-mass nuclei that were previously inaccessible to quantum Monte Carlo approaches.
