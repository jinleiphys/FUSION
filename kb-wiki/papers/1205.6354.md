# Fermion condensation around a Coulomb impurity in a Weyl semimetal as a manifestation of the Landau zero-charge problem

> A Coulomb impurity placed in an undoped Weyl semimetal spontaneously surrounds itself with a cloud of condensed Weyl fermions. We study this system within the Thomas-Fermi approximation. We find that the ground-state of the system is electrically neutral and exhibits an experimentally accessible Landau zero-charge effect: the impurity charge is screened out at any finite distance in the limit of vanishing impurity size. Specifically, we show how in this limit the Thomas-Fermi equation for the electrostatic potential transforms into the Gell-Mann-Low equation for the charge.

# Fermion condensation around a Coulomb impurity in a Weyl semimetal as a manifestation of the Landau zero-charge problem

## Key claim

The screening of a Coulomb impurity in an undoped Weyl semimetal exhibits a Landau zero-charge effect: the impurity charge is completely screened at any finite distance in the limit of vanishing impurity size, but this effect is experimentally accessible because the material parameters of the semimetal (unlike QED) allow both the weak-coupling and strong-coupling regimes to be realized.

## Method

The authors employ a Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation for a system of Weyl fermions with linear dispersion interacting with a point Coulomb impurity of charge Ze. They derive and solve a nonlinear differential equation for the electrostatic potential, which in the large-distance limit reduces to the Gell-Mann-Low equation for the running charge. The analysis covers both the weak-screening regime (λZ² ≪ 1) and the strong-screening regime (λZ² ≫ 1), where λ = 2gα³/3π is a dimensionless parameter characterizing the relative strength of electron-electron interactions.

## Key numbers

- The fine-structure constant for the Weyl semimetal is α = e²/(ħv_Fκ), with v_F ≈ 10⁸ cm/s and κ ≳ 1, giving α ≈ 1/κ.
- The critical charge for spontaneous electron condensation is Z_c₂ = 1/2, below which the bare impurity energy is lower than the condensed state energy.
- In the weak-screening regime (λZ² ≪ 1), the number of condensed electrons outside the impurity region is N(r > a) = λZ³ ln(r/a) to logarithmic accuracy.
- The Weyl cloud size in the weak-screening limit is R(λ) ≈ a exp(1/2λZ²), where a is the impurity size.
- In the strong-screening limit (λZ² ≫ 1), the residual charge within the impurity region is of order Ze/(λZ²)^(1/3) ≪ Ze.
- The number of Weyl nodes g ranges from g = 2 (topological insulator multilayer) to g = 24 (pyrochlore iridates).

## Context

This work extends the Abrikosov and Beneslavskii (1971) prediction of Weyl semimetals with linear dispersion to the problem of Coulomb impurity screening, connecting it to the Landau zero-charge problem in QED (Landau, 1955; Landau and Lifshitz). The authors draw parallels to the atomic collapse problem in superheavy nuclei (Migdal, 1975; Zeldovich and Popov, 1972) and to the Thomas-Fermi treatment of supercharged atoms in QED (Muller, Rafelski, and Greiner, 1972). The results enable experimental study of the zero-charge effect in condensed matter systems such as doped silver chalcogenides, pyrochlore iridates, and topological insulator multilayers, where the material parameters make both weak and strong screening regimes accessible.
