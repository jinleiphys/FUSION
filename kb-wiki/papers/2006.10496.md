# Elimination of the effect of internal activity in LaCl3:Ce scintillator

> The Lanthanum Halide scintillator detectors have been widely used for nuclear spectroscopy experiments because of their excellent energy and time resolutions. Despite having these advantages, the intrinsic alpha and beta contaminations in these scintillators pose a severe limitation in their usage in rare-event detections. In the present work, pulse shape discrimination (PSD) with a fast digitizer has been shown to be an efficient method to separate the effect of alpha contamination from the spectrum. The shape of the beta spectrum has been generated with the help of Monte Carlo based simulation code, and its contribution has been eliminated from the spectrum. The reduction in the background events generated by both intrinsic beta and alpha activities has been demonstrated. The present study will encourage the application of these detectors in low cross-section measurement experiments relevant to nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim
Pulse shape discrimination (PSD) with a fast digitizer, combined with Monte Carlo simulation of the beta continuum, can eliminate both intrinsic alpha and beta contamination from LaCl3:Ce scintillator spectra, reducing background events by 35% and enabling low-cross-section nuclear astrophysics measurements. The method relies on the two-component scintillation decay of LaCl3:Ce (fast component ~26 ns, slow component ~550 ns) which provides sufficient pulse shape difference between alpha and gamma events, unlike LaBr3:Ce where the difference is too small to be useful.

## Method
The authors used a 1" x 1" cylindrical LaCl3:Ce crystal coupled to a Hamamatsu PMT biased at -1800 V, with signals digitized by a CAEN DT5730 (500 MHz sampling rate). Pulse shape discrimination was performed using the charge integration method with an 80 ns short gate and a long gate covering the full pulse, calculating PSD = (Q_l - Q_s)/Q_l for each event. The beta continuum shape was simulated using a GEANT4-based Monte Carlo code (version 4.10.0) with a cylindrical source matching the detector dimensions, normalized to reproduce the observed 1436 keV gamma count from 138La electron capture decay.

## Key numbers
- Light output of LaCl3:Ce is ~50 photons/keV with energy resolution as low as 3.1% at 662 keV
- Principal decay constant is ~20 ns; non-linearity below 7% from 60 to 1300 keV
- 138La has half-life 1.05 x 10^11 years, with 0.09% natural abundance; decays by electron capture (66.4% probability) and beta-minus emission (33.6% probability)
- Beta continuum endpoint energy is 255 keV, followed by 789 keV gamma from 138Ce
- 138La electron capture emits 1436 keV gamma plus 32-38 keV characteristic X-rays of Ba
- Alpha contamination from 227Ac (half-life 21.772 years) produces peaks at 1.5-3 MeV in the energy spectrum
- Intrinsic background is about 1-2 counts/cm^3/s
- Lead shielding reduced room background by ~11%
- After elimination of both alpha and beta intrinsic activities, time-normalized background events reduced by ~35% compared to a bare detector
- Fast and slow decay times for LaCl3:Ce are tau_f ~26 ns and tau_s ~550 ns at 20 degrees C; ratio B/A is approximately 0.25 for gamma-rays at 20 degrees C

## Context
This work extends prior studies of internal activity in lanthanum halide scintillators, including identification of 227Ac contamination by Hartwell and Gehrke (2005) and Milbrath et al. (2005), pulse shape discrimination studies by Hoel et al. (2005) and Crespi et al. (2009), and beta spectrum measurements by Quarati et al. (2012). The method enables use of LaCl3:Ce detectors for low-energy capture cross-section measurements relevant to nucleosynthesis, where the 35% background reduction makes rare-event detection feasible. The results are directly applicable to LaBr3:Ce detectors as well, since both show very similar internal activity trends.
