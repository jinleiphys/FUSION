# A two-parameter criterion for classifying the explodability of massive stars by the neutrino-driven mechanism

> Thus far, judging the fate of a massive star (either a neutron star (NS) or a black hole) solely by its structure prior to core collapse has been ambiguous. Our work and previous attempts find a non-monotonic variation of successful and failed supernovae with zero-age main-sequence mass, for which no single structural parameter can serve as a good predictive measure. However, we identify two parameters computed from the pre-collapse structure of the progenitor, which in combination allow for a clear separation of exploding and non-exploding cases with only few exceptions (~1-2.5%) in our set of 621 investigated stellar models. One parameter is M4, defining the normalized enclosed mass for a dimensionless entropy per nucleon of s=4, and the other is mu4 = d(m/M_sun)/d(r/1000 km) at s=4, being the normalized mass-derivative at this location. The two parameters mu4 and M4*mu4 can be directly linked to the mass-infall rate, Mdot, of the collapsing star and the electron-type neutrino luminosity of the accreting proto-NS, L_nue ~ M_ns*Mdot, which play a crucial role in the "critical luminosity" concept for the theoretical description of neutrino-driven explosions as runaway phenomenon of the stalled accretion shock. All models were evolved employing the approach of Ugliano et al. for simulating neutrino-driven explosions in spherical symmetry. The neutrino emission of the accretion layer is approximated by a gray transport solver, while the uncertain neutrino emission of the 1.1 M_sun proto-NS core is parametrized by an analytic model. The free parameters connected to the core-boundary prescription are calibrated to reproduce the observables of Supernova 1987A for five different progenitor models.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a combination of two parameters derived from the pre-collapse structure of a massive star, the normalized enclosed mass at a specific entropy ($M_4$) and the normalized mass derivative at that same location ($\mu_4$), can predict whether the star will explode as a neutrino-driven supernova or collapse into a black hole with over 97% accuracy, a significant improvement over single-parameter criteria like compactness. This high success rate is conditional on the specific set of 621 stellar models and the particular calibration of the neutrino-driven explosion model used.

## Method
The authors performed spherically symmetric (1D) hydrodynamic simulations of core collapse and explosion for 621 progenitor models of various metallicities, using the parametric neutrino-driven explosion approach of Ugliano et al. (2012). The neutrino emission from the inner 1.1 solar mass proto-neutron star (PNS) core was modeled analytically, while the accretion layer's neutrino emission was treated with a gray transport solver. The free parameters of the PNS core model were calibrated to reproduce the observed explosion energy and nickel mass of Supernova 1987A for five different progenitor models.

## Key numbers
- The two-parameter criterion ($M_4$ and $\mu_4$) correctly separates exploding and non-exploding cases with only ~1-2.5% exceptions across the set of 621 stellar models.
- A single compactness parameter, $\xi_{2.5}$, correctly predicts explosions in less than 90% of cases, with the Ugliano et al. (2012) model finding only explosions for $\xi_{2.5} < 0.15$, a mix for $0.15 \leq \xi_{2.5} \leq 0.35$, and only black hole formation for $\xi_{2.5} > 0.35$.
- The calibration for Supernova 1987A aimed for an explosion energy of $E_\mathrm{exp} = (1.50 \pm 0.12) \times 10^{51}$ erg and an ejected $^{56}$Ni mass of $M_\mathrm{Ni} = 0.0723$–$0.0772 M_\odot$.

## Context
This work extends the findings of Ugliano et al. (2012), who first showed a non-monotonic variation of supernova outcomes with zero-age main-sequence mass using a similar parametric model. It directly addresses the ambiguity of single-parameter predictors like the compactness $\xi_{2.5}$ proposed by O'Connor & Ott (2011), which the authors show has many outliers. The new criterion provides a physically motivated link to the "critical luminosity" concept for neutrino-driven explosions and enables more reliable predictions of neutron star versus black hole formation from stellar evolution models, which is crucial for understanding compact object populations and gravitational wave sources.
