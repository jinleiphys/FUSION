# Assessment of uncertainties in QRPA $0νββ$-decay nuclear matrix elements

> The nuclear matrix elements $M^{0ν}$ of the neutrinoless double beta decay ($0νββ$) of most nuclei with known $2νββ$-decay rates are systematically evaluated using the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and Renormalized QRPA (RQRPA). The experimental $2νββ$-decay rate is used to adjust the most relevant parameter, the strength of the particle-particle interaction. New results confirm that with such procedure the $M^{0ν}$ values become essentially independent on the size of the single-particle basis. Furthermore, the matrix elements are shown to be also rather stable with respect to the possible quenching of the axial vector strength parametrized by reducing the coupling constant $g_A$, as well as to the uncertainties of parameters describing the short range nucleon correlations. Theoretical arguments in favor of the adopted way of determining the interaction parameters are presented. Furthermore, a discussion of other implicit and explicit parameters, inherent to the QRPA method, is presented. Comparison is made of the ways these factors are chosen by different authors. It is suggested that most of the spread among the published $0νββ$ decay nuclear matrix elements can be ascribed to these choices.

## Key claim

The paper claims that by adjusting the particle-particle interaction strength \(g_{pp}\) to reproduce the experimental \(2\nu\beta\beta\)-decay rate, the calculated \(0\nu\beta\beta\) nuclear matrix elements become essentially independent of the size of the single-particle basis, the choice of nucleon-nucleon potential, and the quenching of \(g_A\), thereby removing much of the spread previously seen among published QRPA results.

## Method

The authors used the Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) and its renormalized version (RQRPA) to calculate the \(0\nu\beta\beta\) nuclear matrix elements for all nuclei with known \(2\nu\beta\beta\)-decay rates. For each nucleus, the particle-particle strength parameter \(g_{pp}\) was adjusted separately for three different single-particle basis sizes (ranging from about \(1\hbar\omega\) to \(4\hbar\omega\) excitations) so that the calculated \(2\nu\beta\beta\) half-life matched the experimental value. The matrix elements were then averaged over the three basis choices, and the sensitivity to variations in the axial coupling constant \(g_A\) (using values 1.25 and 1.0) and to the short-range correlation parameter \(\gamma_1\) (varied from 0.9 to 1.2 fm\(^2\)) was tested.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{76}\)Ge, the average RQRPA matrix element \(\langle M'^{0\nu} \rangle\) with \(g_A=1.25\) is approximately 4.0 (dimensionless), with a variance \(\sigma\) of about 0.3; with \(g_A=1.0\) the average is about 3.2 with \(\sigma \approx 0.2\).
* For \(^{100}\)Mo, the average RQRPA matrix element with \(g_A=1.25\) is approximately 3.5 (\(\sigma \approx 0.3\)); with \(g_A=1.0\) it is about 2.8 (\(\sigma \approx 0.2\)).
* For \(^{130}\)Te, the average RQRPA matrix element with \(g_A=1.25\) is approximately 3.8 (\(\sigma \approx 0.3\)); with \(g_A=1.0\) it is about 3.0 (\(\sigma \approx 0.2\)).
* For \(^{136}\)Xe, the average RQRPA matrix element with \(g_A=1.25\) is approximately 2.8 (\(\sigma \approx 0.2\)); with \(g_A=1.0\) it is about 2.2 (\(\sigma \approx 0.2\)).
* Varying the short-range correlation parameter \(\gamma_1\) from 0.9 to 1.2 fm\(^2\) changes the calculated matrix element by only about 10% relative to the standard value \(\gamma_1 = 1.1\) fm\(^2\).
* The ratio of matrix elements calculated with \(g_A=1.0\) versus \(g_A=1.25\) is closer to unity (typically differing by about 20%) than the naive \(g_A^4\) scaling factor of 2.44.

## Context

This work extends the approach introduced by Rodin et al. (2003) for four nuclei to a systematic evaluation of all nuclei with known \(2\nu\beta\beta\)-decay rates. It contradicts earlier conclusions by Suhonen et al. (1991) and Stoica and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (2001) that the matrix elements depend strongly on the model space size; the present results show that adjusting \(g_{pp}\) to the \(2\nu\) rate removes that dependence. The paper enables more reliable predictions of \(0\nu\beta\beta\) half-lives for planning next-generation experiments, and suggests that the spread among published QRPA results is largely due to different choices of adjustable parameters rather than fundamental uncertainties.
