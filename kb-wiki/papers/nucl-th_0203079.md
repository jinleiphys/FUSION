# Coupling to the continuous spectrum and HFB approximation

> We propose a new method to solve the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations for weakly bound nuclei whose purpose is to improve the treatment of the continuum when a finite range two-body interaction is used. We replace the traditional expansion on a discrete harmonic oscillator basis by a mixed eigenfunction expansion associated with a potential that explicitely includes a continuous spectrum. We overcome the problem of continuous spectrum discretization by using a resonance expansion.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new method for solving the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) equations for weakly bound nuclei that replaces the traditional harmonic oscillator basis expansion with a mixed eigenfunction expansion derived from a square-well potential that explicitly includes a continuous spectrum, using a resonance expansion to avoid discretizing the continuum integral. The authors demonstrate the method on simplified one-body and HFB-type problems but note that the convergence for partial waves with ℓ=1 is slower than for other ℓ values, requiring more basis functions to achieve comparable accuracy.

## Method

The authors replace the harmonic oscillator reference potential with a finite-range square-well potential of depth V₀ and radius r₀, then restrict the problem to a bounded domain Ω=(0,r₀) and impose a non-selfadjoint "transparent" radiation boundary condition at r=r₀ that mimics the external scattering behavior. The resulting generalized eigenfunctions (bound, virtual, resonant, and anti-resonant states) are obtained by solving the transcendental equation p_n cot(p_n r₀) = i k_n, and these functions are used to expand the HFB quasiparticle wave functions, leading to a generalized complex eigenvalue problem. For the numerical tests, the basis is constructed from 30 bound states plus 3 resonances and 3 anti-resonances (36 functions total) for the Hartree-Fock case, and from 38 bound states plus 2 resonance/anti-resonance pairs (42 functions) for the HFB case.

## Key numbers

- For the Gaussian potential test (no pairing), bound state energies reproduced with five to six significant digits for ℓ=0 and ℓ=2 states (e.g., ℓ=0 bound state at -4.571182 MeV, ℓ=2 bound state at -0.759532 MeV)
- For the Gaussian potential test, narrow resonances well reproduced: ℓ=0 resonance at 2.252302 MeV (width 0.000118 MeV), ℓ=2 resonance at 2.384152 MeV (width 0.000083 MeV)
- For the HFB test with Saxon-Woods potential: box radius 40 fm, depth 180 MeV, yielding 38 bound states and 37 virtual states; particle-hole potential parameters U₀=32 MeV, r₀=3.7 fm, a=0.65 fm; particle-particle potential V₀=4 MeV, r₀=3.7 fm, a=0.65 fm
- For the HFB test, one loosely bound state at 124 keV below the continuum (ℓ=2, energy -0.124 MeV)
- Energy scale chosen such that ħ²/2m = 20 MeV in the HFB example

## Context

This work extends earlier approaches to solving HFB equations for weakly bound nuclei, which previously relied on harmonic oscillator basis expansions (Gogny and collaborators, 1980) that become prohibitively expensive for exotic nuclei near drip lines, or on lattice methods (Bonche and collaborators, 1985) and local-scale point transformations (Stoitsov and collaborators, 1998) adapted to zero-range Skyrme forces. The method enables self-consistent HFB computations for finite-range Gogny-type interactions in weakly bound nuclei where the pairing interaction couples discrete and continuum single-particle states, and is intended to allow description of halo nuclei and neutron skins.
