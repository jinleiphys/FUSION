# Nucleosynthesis: what direct reactions can do for it?

> The reactions of relevance for stellar evolution are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory at the small astrophysical energies. In recent years indirect reaction methods have been developed and applied to extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors. These methods require a combination of new experimental techniques and theoretical efforts, which are the subject of this short review.

# Nucleosynthesis: what direct reactions can do for it?

## Key claim
Indirect reaction methods (transfer reactions, Coulomb dissociation, knockout reactions) can extract low-energy astrophysical S-factors for stellar evolution reactions that are difficult to measure directly in the laboratory, but the reliability of these extractions depends on proper treatment of relativistic effects, continuum-continuum couplings, and medium modifications of nucleon-nucleon cross sections.

## Method
The paper reviews several indirect reaction techniques: the Trojan Horse method uses breakup reactions to induce low-energy reactions at vanishing relative energy; the Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) technique extracts radiative capture cross sections from peripheral transfer reactions by exploiting the dominance of the asymptotic overlap integral; Coulomb dissociation at intermediate energies (50-100 MeV/nucleon) relates breakup cross sections to photodisintegration and, via time reversal, to radiative capture; and knockout reactions probe single-particle properties of halo nuclei through momentum distributions. Each method requires coupled-channels calculations, often with Continuum Discretized Coupled-Channels (CDCC) for continuum states, and careful treatment of relativistic dynamics and nuclear absorption.

## Key numbers
- The astrophysical S-factor for $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B is given by the fit $S_{17}(E) = (22.109\ \text{eV.b}) \times (1 + 5.30E + 1.65E^2 + 0.857E^3) / (1 + E/0.1375)$, where E is the relative energy in MeV.
- The recommended value from a recent compilation is $S_{17} = 20.8 \pm 0.7\ \text{(expt)} \pm 1.4\ \text{(theor)}$ eV b.
- Relativistic corrections increase the scattering angle by up to 6% and the elastic scattering cross section by up to 13% for center-of-mass scattering angles around 0-4 degrees at 100 MeV/nucleon.
- At 10 MeV/nucleon, relativistic corrections are important only at the 1% level; at 500 MeV/nucleon, recoil corrections are relevant at the 1% level.
- At intermediate energies (50-100 MeV/nucleon), relativistic and recoil corrections can add up to 50%.
- The $^{15}$C halo neutron is bound by 1.218 MeV; knockout cross sections for $^{9}$Be($^{15}$C,$^{14}$C$_{gs}$) are calculated as a function of bombarding energy.
- Longitudinal momentum distributions for $^{9}$Be($^{11}$Be,$^{10}$Be) at 250 MeV/nucleon are reduced by 10% when using Brueckner theory compared to free nucleon-nucleon cross sections, but the shape remains unaltered.

## Context
This review extends the work on indirect methods for nuclear astrophysics, building on the Coulomb dissociation method proposed by Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel (1986) and the ANC technique developed by Mukhamedzhanov and collaborators (1990). It summarizes the status of the $^{7}$Be(p,$\gamma$)$^{8}$B reaction, which has been studied extensively via direct measurements and Coulomb dissociation experiments (e.g., by Tohru and collaborators, and the compilation by Adelberger et al. 2011). The paper enables future work by identifying the theoretical challenges (relativistic dynamics, continuum couplings, medium effects in knockout) that must be addressed to extract reliable astrophysical S-factors from next-generation radioactive beam facilities.
