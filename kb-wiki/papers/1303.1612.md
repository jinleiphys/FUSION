# The effect of a density dependent bag constant on the structure of hot neutron star with a quark core

> As we go from center toward the surface of a neutron star, the state of baryonic matter changes from the de-confined quark-gluon to a mixed phase of quark and hadronic matter, and a thin crust of hadronic matter. For the quark matter, within MIT bag model, the total energy density of the system is the kinetic energy for non-interacting quarks plus a bag constant. In this article first we have considered a density dependent bag constant obtained using the recent experimental results of CERN SPS on the formation of a quark-gluon plasma. For calculations of the hadron phase, we use the lowest order constrained variational method. The equation of state of mixed phase has been determined using Gibbs conditions. Finally, we have calculated the structure of a hot neutron star with quark core employing TOV equation. Our results show that a density dependent bag constant leads to a higher mass and lower radius for the hot neutron star with respect to the case in which we use a fixed bag constant.

# The effect of a density dependent bag constant on the structure of hot neutron star with a quark core

## Key claim

Using a density dependent bag constant (rather than a fixed constant) in the MIT bag model for quark matter leads to a higher maximum gravitational mass and a lower radius for a hot neutron star with a quark core, at temperatures of 10 and 20 MeV. This result depends on the specific Gaussian parametrization of the bag constant and the use of the LOCV method for the hadronic phase.

## Method

The authors compute the equation of state for three regions of a hot neutron star: a hadronic phase using the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) method at finite temperature, a mixed hadron-quark phase using Gibbs equilibrium conditions, and a pure quark phase using the MIT bag model with a density dependent bag constant. The bag constant is parametrized as a Gaussian function of baryon density, with parameters chosen to match CERN SPS experimental results on quark-gluon plasma formation (transition at about seven times normal nuclear matter energy density). The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations are then integrated numerically to obtain the mass and radius of the star at temperatures T = 10 and 20 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The density dependent bag constant is parametrized as ${\cal B}(n)={\cal B}_{\infty}+({\cal B}_{0}-{\cal B}_{\infty})\exp[-\beta(n/n_{0})^{2}]$ with ${\cal B}_{0}=400\ MeV/fm^{3}$ and $\beta=0.17$.
- The hadron-quark transition is assumed to occur at energy density ${\cal E} = 1100\ MeV\ fm^{-3}$, corresponding to a baryonic transition density $n_t$.
- The mixed phase exists up to baryon density $n \sim 0.5\ fm^{-3}$.
- Calculations are performed at two temperatures: $T=10$ and $20\ MeV$.
- The interior temperature of a newborn neutron star is of order $20-50\ MeV$.
- The fixed bag constant used for comparison is ${\cal B}=90\ MeV/fm^{3}$.

## Context

This work extends previous calculations by the same authors (Yazdizadeh and Bordbar, 2011) on hot neutron stars with a quark core using a fixed bag constant, and earlier work by Bordbar et al. (2006) on cold neutron stars with a quark core. It builds on the approach of Burgio et al. (2002, 2007) who used the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone formalism for the hadronic phase, and on the density dependent bag constant parametrization introduced by Burgio et al. (2002) and Baldo et al. (2006). The results enable comparison between density dependent and constant bag models for hot neutron star structure, and can be used to constrain the equation of state of dense matter from neutron star observations.
