# Comments on Baryon Transition Form Factors

> We discuss in rather general terms the properties of space-like baryon transition form factors. In particular, we argue why these are necessarily complex-valued, what can be deduced from the respective phase motion and why dealing with real valued transition form factors in general leads to misleading results. For illustration the transition form factors for the Roper resonance as derived in the Jülich-Bonn-Washington framework are discussed.

# Comments on Baryon Transition Form Factors

## Key claim

Baryon transition form factors (TFFs) are necessarily complex-valued even for space-like photons, and treating them as real-valued quantities, as done in Breit-Wigner based extractions, leads to misleading results, particularly for the Roper resonance where the phase motion reveals non-trivial meson-baryon dynamics.

## Method

The authors use the Jülich-Bonn-Washington (JBW) dynamical coupled-channel framework, which solves Lippmann-Schwinger equations for meson-baryon scattering and electroproduction amplitudes from a chiral-symmetric Lagrangian. The multipoles extracted from fits to 158k data points (including πN elastic and inelastic reactions, real photon reactions, and virtual photon electroproduction) are analytically continued to the resonance pole to extract reaction-independent transition form factors via the residue factorization formula of Workman (2013).

## Key numbers

- The JBW fit achieves χ²_γ*/dof < 1.5 for the virtual photon data.
- The fit uses 8k + 40k + 110k = 158k total data points from elastic πN, inelastic πN, real photon, and virtual photon reactions.
- The Roper resonance pole is located at W_pole corresponding to the N*(1440) mass region.
- The isospin factors used in the TFF definition are C_{1/2} = -√3 and C_{3/2} = √(2/3).

## Context

This work extends the dynamical coupled-channel approach of the Jülich-Bonn-Washington group (Mai 2021, 2023) and directly contradicts the real-valued TFF extractions by the CLAS collaboration (CLAS 2009, Mokeev 2015) and their interpretation as evidence for the Roper being a radial excitation (Burkert 2017). The paper demonstrates that the Roper resonance is dynamically generated in the JBW framework without an explicit pole term, consistent with earlier work by Krehl (1999). The results enable future extractions of TFFs that properly account for analytic continuation to resonance poles, which is necessary for interpreting data from CLAS@JLAB and upcoming facilities.
