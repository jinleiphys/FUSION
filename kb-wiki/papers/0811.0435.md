# A New Fundamental Duality in Nuclei and its Implications for Quantum Mechanics

> The Liquid Drop Models (LDM) and the Independent Particle Models (IPM) have been known to provide two conflicting pictures of the nucleus. The IPM being quantum mechanical, is believed to provide a fundamental picture of the nucleus and hence has been focus of the still elusive unified theory of the nucleus. It is believed that the LDM at best is an effective and limited model of the nucleus. Here, through a comprehensive study of one nucleon separation energy, we give convincing evidence that actually the LDM is as fundamental and as basic for the description of the nucleus as the IPM is. As such the LDM and the IPM provide simultaneously co-exiting complementary duality of the nuclear phenomena. This fundamental duality also provides solution to the decades old Coester Band problem in the nucleus. Similarity and differences with respect to the well known wave-particle duality, as envisaged in Bohr's Complementarity Principle, is pointed out. Thereafter implications of this new Duality in the nucleus for quantum mechanics is discussed.

## Key claim

Through a comprehensive study of one-nucleon separation energies, the paper argues that the Liquid Drop Model (LDM) is as fundamental for describing the nucleus as the Independent Particle Model (IPM), and that the two models provide a simultaneously co-existing complementary duality, but this effect is clearly manifest only in heavy nuclei (specifically for N=82, N=126, and Z=82) while remaining masked in lighter nuclei.

## Method

The author plotted single neutron separation energies (S_N) versus proton number Z for fixed neutron numbers N, and single proton separation energies (S_p) versus neutron number N for fixed proton numbers Z, using experimental data from Audi, Wapstra and Thibault (2003). These plots were compared with calculations from three comprehensive model compilations: the FRDM (Moeller, Nix and Kratz, 1997) representing the LDM category, and the HFBCS-1 (Goriely, Tondeur and Pearson, 2001) and INM (Nayak and Satpathy, 1997) representing the IPM category.

## Key numbers

- For S_N vs Z at fixed N=126, the constant difference between HFB (IPM) calculations and experimental data is approximately 0.5 MeV, attributed to a volume term b_v * A_magic where A_magic = 208 (N=126 + Z=82), yielding b_v = 0.0024 MeV.
- For S_N vs Z at fixed N=82, a similar constant difference of about 0.5 MeV is observed between IPM calculations and experiment.
- For S_p vs N at fixed Z=82, the same pattern appears with the doubly magic core A=82+126=206, and the IPM calculations show a constant offset from experiment below N=126.
- The standard volume term in liquid drop binding energy is a_v = 16 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the decades-long search for a unified theory of the nucleus, which has been dominated by the belief that the IPM provides the fundamental picture while the LDM is merely an effective model. The paper claims that the failure of IPM calculations (HFBCS-1 and INM) to reproduce separation energies in three specific cases (S_N vs Z for N=126 and N=82, and S_p vs N for Z=82) reveals a fundamental limitation of purely quantum mechanical approaches. The author argues that this duality also resolves the Coester Band problem (Coester, Cohen, Day and Vincent, 1970), where realistic interactions fail to reproduce nuclear matter saturation properties, by attributing the discrepancy to missing LDM effects. The paper draws parallels to the wave-particle duality in quantum mechanics and discusses implications for Bohr's Complementarity Principle, referencing experimental challenges to exclusive duality by Ghose, Home and Agarwal (1991) and Afshar, Flores and McDonald (2007).
