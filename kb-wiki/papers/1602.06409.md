# Effects of finite size and symmetry energy on the phase transition of stellar matter at subnuclear densities

> We study the liquid-gas phase transition of stellar matter with the inclusion of the finite-size effect from surface and Coulomb energies. The equilibrium conditions for two coexisting phases are determined by minimizing the total free energy including the surface and Coulomb contributions, which are different from the Gibbs conditions used in the bulk calculations. The finite-size effect can significantly reduce the region of the liquid-gas mixed phase. The influence of the symmetry energy on the liquid-gas phase transition is investigated with the inclusion of finite-size effects. It is found that the slope of the symmetry energy plays an important role in determining the boundary and properties of the mixed phase.

## Key claim

The inclusion of finite-size effects from surface and Coulomb energies, treated self-consistently via minimization of the total free energy rather than perturbatively, significantly reduces the liquid-gas coexistence region of stellar matter compared to bulk calculations that neglect these effects; the reduction is so large that the binodal curve with finite-size effects lies below the spinodal curve at higher temperatures, meaning the thermodynamically favorable state is the single phase in that region.

## Method

The authors employ a compressible liquid-drop (CLD) model within the Wigner-Seitz approximation, considering only spherical droplet and bubble configurations (neglecting other pasta phases). The nuclear interaction is described by two relativistic mean-field (RMF) parametrizations, TM1 and IUFSU, and the surface tension is calculated self-consistently using a Thomas-Fermi approach for a one-dimensional semi-infinite slab rather than using a parametrized form. The equilibrium conditions for two-phase coexistence are derived by minimizing the total free energy including surface and Coulomb contributions, yielding modified equilibrium equations that differ from the standard Gibbs conditions used in bulk calculations.

## Key numbers

- The TM1 model predicts a symmetry energy slope \(L = 110.8\) MeV at saturation density, while the IUFSU model predicts a smaller \(L\) value.
- At \(Y_p = 0.1\) using the TM1 parametrization, the critical temperature \(T_c\) is approximately 8 MeV for the original model with \(L = 110.8\) MeV, versus approximately 11.4 MeV for a generated model with \(L = 40\) MeV.
- At \(Y_p = 0.3\) and \(T = 10\) MeV using the TM1 model, the transition density to uniform matter is about 0.073 fm\(^{-3}\) in the CLD approach, close to the Thomas-Fermi value of 0.069 fm\(^{-3}\) from Zhang (2014).
- At \(T = 8\) MeV and \(Y_p = 0.3\) using the TM1 set, the transition density to uniform matter is about 0.093 fm\(^{-3}\) for \(L = 40\) MeV and 0.087 fm\(^{-3}\) for \(L = 110.8\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends previous bulk calculations of the liquid-gas phase transition in asymmetric nuclear matter by Muller and Serot (1995) and others, which neglected surface and Coulomb contributions, by incorporating these finite-size effects self-consistently rather than perturbatively as done in earlier coexisting phases methods by Menezes (2008) and Bao (2014). The results are compared with Thomas-Fermi calculations by Pais (2015) and Zhang (2014), showing that the CLD model with self-consistently calculated surface tension can reproduce Thomas-Fermi results for the crust-core transition density. The study enables more accurate modeling of the equation of state for supernova simulations and neutron star crust properties, particularly by clarifying how the symmetry energy slope \(L\) affects the phase boundary and critical temperature of stellar matter at subnuclear densities.
