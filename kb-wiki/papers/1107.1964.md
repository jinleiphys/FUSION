# Jet quenching via jet collimation

> The strong modifications of dijet properties in heavy ion collisions measured by ATLAS and CMS provide important constraints on the dynamical mechanisms underlying jet quenching. In this work, we show that the transport of soft gluons away from the jet cone - jet collimation - can account for the observed dijet asymmetry with values of $\hat{q}\, L$ that lie in the expected order of magnitude. Further, we show that the energy loss attained through this mechanism results in a very mild distortion of the azimuthal angle dijet distribution.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the transport of soft gluons away from the jet cone (jet collimation) can account for the observed dijet asymmetry in heavy ion collisions, with values of \(\hat{q}L\) that lie in the expected order of magnitude, while producing only a very mild distortion of the azimuthal angle dijet distribution. The authors emphasize that this mechanism is effective even in the absence of other medium-induced effects, and that the estimates of \(\hat{q}L\) they provide are upper bounds.

## Method

The authors model jet collimation by considering soft gluons radiated at small angles that undergo Brownian motion in the medium, accumulating an average squared transverse momentum \(\langle k_T^2 \rangle \sim \hat{q}L\). They use the vacuum intra-jet multiplicity distribution in the modified leading log approximation to compute the energy fraction carried by partons with energy fraction smaller than \(z\), and then determine the \(\hat{q}L\) values needed to account for the energy loss observed by ATLAS and CMS. To assess the effect on the azimuthal dijet distribution, they smear the transverse momentum of the recoiling jet with a Gaussian weight of average squared transverse momentum \(\hat{q}L\), considering two extreme models where either all recoiling jets interact with the medium or only a fraction (\(\sim 0.5\)) do.

## Key numbers

- For ATLAS data (Aad 2010), the average additional energy lost from the recoiling jet cone in the 10% most central events is \(10\ \text{GeV} < \Delta E < 21\ \text{GeV}\).
- For CMS data (Chatrchyan 2011), the average additional energy lost from the recoiling jet cone in the 10% most central events is \(8\ \text{GeV} < \Delta E < 18\ \text{GeV}\).
- For ATLAS data, the estimated \(\hat{q}L\) values from jet collimation alone are \(35 \le \hat{q}L \le 85\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- For CMS data, the estimated \(\hat{q}L\) values from jet collimation alone are \(24 \le \hat{q}L \le 62\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- Including a simple model of medium-induced radiation (Borghini 2005), the estimated \(\hat{q}L\) values for ATLAS are \(30 \le \hat{q}L \le 60\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- Including the same model for CMS, the estimated \(\hat{q}L\) values are \(18 \le \hat{q}L \le 40\ \text{GeV}^2\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously proposed jet collimation mechanism (Casalderrey-Solana, Milhano, and Wiedemann, 2011) to quantitatively explain the dijet asymmetry measurements from ATLAS (Aad 2010) and CMS (Chatrchyan 2011) in Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=2.76\) TeV. The paper shows that jet collimation can account for the observed energy loss without contradicting the very mild distortion of the azimuthal dijet distribution, and it provides upper bounds on \(\hat{q}L\) that are consistent with expectations. The results enable future implementation of jet collimation in existing in-medium Monte Carlo codes for a detailed quantitative assessment.
