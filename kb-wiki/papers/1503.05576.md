# Supporting the existence of the QCD critical point by compact star observations

> In order to prove the existence of a critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram it is sufficient to demonstrate that at zero temperature $T=0$ a first order phase transition exists as a function of the baryochemical potential $μ$, since it is established knowledge from ab-initio lattice QCD simulations that at $μ=0$ the transition on the temperature axis is a crossover. We present the argument that the observation of a gap in the mass-radius relationship for compact stars which proves the existence of a so-called third family (aka "mass twins") will imply that the $T=0$ equation of state of compact star matter exhibits a strong first order transition with a latent heat that satisfies $Δε/ε_c \gt 0.6$. Since such a strong first order transition under compact star conditions will remain first order when going to symmetric matter, the observation of a disconnected branch (third family) of compact stars in the mass-radius diagram proves the existence of a CEP in QCD. For the equation of state of the twins the quark matter description is based on a QCD-motivated chiral approach with higher-order quark interactions in the Dirac scalar and vector coupling channels. For hadronic matter we select a relativistic mean-field equation of state with density-dependent couplings. Since the nucleons are treated in the quasi-particle framework, an excluded volume has been included for the nuclear equation of state at super-saturation density that takes into account the finite size of the nucleons. Furthermore we show results of a Bayesian analysis (BA) using disjunct M-R constraints for extracting probability measures for cold, dense matter equations of state. This study reveals that measuring the radii of neutron star twins has the potential to support the existence of a first order phase transition for compact star matter.

## Key claim

The observation of a disconnected branch (third family) of compact stars in the mass-radius diagram, known as "mass twins," would prove the existence of a critical end point (CEP) in the QCD phase diagram, provided that the zero-temperature first-order phase transition in compact star matter has a latent heat satisfying Δε/ε_c > 0.6, because such a strong transition under compact star conditions will remain first order when going to symmetric matter.

## Method

The authors construct a hybrid equation of state (EoS) for compact star matter combining a relativistic mean-field hadronic EoS with density-dependent couplings and an excluded volume for nucleons at super-saturation density, together with a QCD-motivated chiral quark matter description incorporating higher-order interactions in the Dirac scalar and vector coupling channels. They perform a Bayesian analysis using disjunct mass-radius constraints to extract probability measures for cold, dense matter EoS, and examine the resulting mass-radius relations for the presence of a third family branch (mass twins) that would indicate a strong first-order phase transition.

## Key numbers

- The latent heat condition for a strong first-order transition that would remain first order when going to symmetric matter is Δε/ε_c > 0.6.
- The most massive neutron stars observed have masses of about 2 M⊙, as reported by Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013).
- The radius difference between mass twin stars on the third family branch and pure neutron stars is up to two kilometers for the models discussed.
- The high-mass millisecond pulsar PSR J1614-2230 has a rotation period of 3.15 ms.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical study of hybrid stars with a first-order phase transition to deconfined quark matter, building on prior approaches by Gerlach (1968), Kampfer (1981), Schertler (2000), and Glendenning (1998), and more recently by Alvarez-Castillo (2013), Blaschke (2013), and Benic (2014) who demonstrated the possibility of high-mass twins at 2 M⊙. The authors argue that the observation of mass twins would simultaneously resolve several open problems in compact star physics: the masquerade problem identified by Alford (2004), the hyperon puzzle noted by Baldo (2003), the reconfinement puzzle studied by Lastowiecki (2011) and Zdunik (2012), and the neutron star radius issue arising from conflicting measurements by Guillot (2013) and Bogdanov (2012). Future missions like NICER and SKA could provide the precise simultaneous mass-radius measurements needed to test this prediction.
