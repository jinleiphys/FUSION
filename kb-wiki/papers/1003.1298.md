# Relativistic models of the neutron-star matter equation of state

> Motivated by a recent astrophysical measurement of the pressure of cold matter above nuclear-matter saturation density, we compute the equation of state of neutron star matter using accurately calibrated relativistic models. The uniform stellar core is assumed to consist of nucleons and leptons in beta equilibrium; no exotic degrees of freedom are included. We found the predictions of these models to be in fairly good agreement with the measured equation of state. Yet the Mass-vs-Radius relations predicted by these same models display radii that are consistently larger than the observations.

## Key claim

The relativistic mean-field models predict equations of state that are in fairly good agreement with the astrophysically measured pressure of cold matter above saturation density, yet the same models produce neutron-star radii that are consistently larger than the observational 1-sigma confidence contours, suggesting a conflict that the authors note may be premature to interpret as evidence for exotic degrees of freedom.

## Method

The authors compute the equation of state of neutron-star matter using four accurately calibrated relativistic mean-field models (NL3, MS, FSUGold, and an "Extra Soft" XS model) that include nucleons and leptons in beta equilibrium but no exotic degrees of freedom. The models incorporate Yukawa couplings to isoscalar scalar and vector mesons and a vector-isovector meson, with nonlinear self-interactions including a vector self-interaction parameter ζ and an isoscalar-isovector mixing term Λ_v that control the high-density stiffness and symmetry energy, respectively. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved to obtain mass-vs-radius relations, and the transition density from the liquid core to the solid crust is determined using a relativistic random-phase-approximation analysis.

## Key numbers

- NL3 model predicts a maximum neutron star mass of 2.78 solar masses with a radius of 13.39 km; for a 1.4 solar-mass star the radius is 15.05 km.
- MS model (ζ=0.06) reduces the maximum mass to 1.81 solar masses with radius 11.64 km; radius for a 1.4 solar-mass star is 13.78 km.
- FSUGold model (ζ=0.06, Λ_v=0.03) gives a maximum mass of 1.72 solar masses with radius 10.97 km; radius for a 1.4 solar-mass star is 12.66 km.
- XS model (ζ=0.09, Λ_v=0.04) yields a maximum mass of 1.60 solar masses with radius 10.41 km; radius for a 1.4 solar-mass star is 11.73 km.
- NL3 predicts a neutron-skin thickness of 0.28 fm for 208Pb; FSUGold predicts 0.21 fm.
- NL3 central density at maximum mass is 0.667 fm^{-3} with central pressure 440.58 MeV fm^{-3}; XS central density is 1.252 fm^{-3} with central pressure 345.37 MeV fm^{-3}.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Ozel (2010), who reported combined mass-radius measurements for three neutron stars and extracted an equation of state that appeared softer than nucleonic-only predictions. The authors compare their relativistic mean-field models, including the NL3 model of Lalazissis (1996, 1999), the MS model of Mueller and Serot (1996), and the FSUGold model of Todd-Rutel (2005), against these observational data. The results highlight the tension between the pressure-density relation (which agrees with observation) and the mass-radius relation (which predicts radii too large), motivating future work on both observational and theoretical fronts to resolve whether exotic degrees of freedom are required.
