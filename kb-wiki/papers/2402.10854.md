# Global Fit of Electron and Neutrino Elastic Scattering Data to Determine the Strange Quark Contribution to the Vector and Axial Form Factors of the Nucleon

> We present a global fit of neutral-current elastic (NCE) neutrino-scattering data and parity-violating electron-scattering (PVES) data with the goal of determining the strange quark contribution to the vector and axial form factors of the proton. Previous fits of this form included data from a variety of PVES experiments (PVA4, HAPPEx, G0, SAMPLE) and the NCE neutrino and anti-neutrino data from BNL E734. These fits did not constrain the strangeness contribution to the axial form factor $G_A^s(Q^2)$ at low $Q^2$ very well because there was no NCE data for $Q^2<0.45$ GeV$^2$. Our new fit includes for the first time MiniBooNE NCE data from both neutrino and anti-neutrino scattering; this experiment used a hydrocarbon target and so a model of the neutrino interaction with the carbon nucleus was required. Three different nuclear models have been employed: a relativistic Fermi gas model, the SuperScaling Approximation model, and a spectral function model. We find a tremendous improvement in the constraint of $G_A^s(Q^2)$ at low $Q^2$ compared to previous work, although more data is needed from NCE measurements that focus on exclusive single-proton final states, for example from MicroBooNE.

# Global Fit of Electron and Neutrino Elastic Scattering Data to Determine the Strange Quark Contribution to the Vector and Axial Form Factors of the Nucleon

## Key claim

The inclusion of MiniBooNE neutral-current elastic (NCE) neutrino and antineutrino data for the first time in a global fit with parity-violating electron scattering (PVES) data provides a "tremendous improvement" in constraining the strange quark contribution to the axial form factor $G_A^s(Q^2)$ at low $Q^2$, although the MiniBooNE data are inclusive (including interactions on both protons and neutrons) and therefore have reduced sensitivity to $G_A^s$ compared to exclusive single-proton measurements.

## Method

The authors performed a global fit combining PVES data from the SAMPLE, G0, HAPPEx, and PVA4 experiments with NCE neutrino and antineutrino scattering data from BNL E734 and, for the first time, MiniBooNE. The MiniBooNE data required modeling of neutrino interactions on carbon nuclei, for which three different nuclear models were employed: a relativistic Fermi gas model, the SuperScaling Approximation model, and a spectral function model. Two parameterizations for the $Q^2$-dependence of $G_A^s$ were used: a modified-dipole model and a $z$-expansion model, each with three free parameters.

## Key numbers

- The BNL E734 data cover the momentum-transfer range $0.45 < Q^2 < 1.05$ GeV$^2$ for neutrino-proton and antineutrino-proton cross sections
- The axial coupling constant used is $g_A = 1.2670 \pm 0.0030$ from neutron beta decay
- The axial mass parameter used is $M_A = 1.014 \pm 0.014$ GeV for the charged-current axial form factor $G_A^{CC}$
- The Weinberg angle squared is taken as $\sin^2\theta_W = 0.23116$
- The MiniBooNE neutrino-induced data had a kinetic energy threshold as low as 50 MeV for the first analysis and 350 MeV for the second analysis using Cherenkov light
- The $z$-expansion model uses $t_{\rm cut} = (4 m_\pi)^2$ and $k_{\rm max} = 6$, reduced to three independent parameters ($a_0$, $a_1$, $a_2$) by imposing four boundary conditions at $z=1$

## Context

This work extends previous global fits by Pate et al. (2008) and Pate et al. (2013) that combined PVES data from HAPPEx, G0, SAMPLE, and PVA4 with BNL E734 NCE data, but lacked NCE data below $Q^2 = 0.45$ GeV$^2$. The inclusion of MiniBooNE data addresses this gap, though the authors note that exclusive single-proton final state measurements (e.g., from MicroBooNE) would provide even stronger constraints. The results are relevant to the long-standing tension between deep-inelastic scattering analyses (which typically find a negative $\Delta s$) and semi-inclusive DIS analyses (which find $\Delta s$ consistent with zero), as $\Delta s = G_A^s(Q^2=0)$.
