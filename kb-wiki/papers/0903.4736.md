# Phase transition dynamics for baryon-dense matter

> We construct a simple two-phase equation of state intended to resemble that of compressed baryon-rich matter and then introduce a gradient term in the compressional energy density to take account of fintie-range effects in non-uniform configurations. With this model we study the interface between the two coexisting phases and obtain estimates for the associated interface tension. Subsequently, we incorporate the finite-range equation of state into ideal or viscous fluid dynamics and derive the collective dispersion relation for the mechanically unstable modes of bulk matter in the spinodal region of the thermodynamic phase diagram. Combining these results with time scales extracted from existing dynamical transport simulations, we discuss the prospects for spinodal phase separation to occur in nuclear collisions. We argue that these can be optimized by a careful tuning of the collision energy to maximize the time spent by the bulk of the system inside the mechanically unstable spinodal region of the phase diagram. Our specific numerical estimates suggest cautious optimism that this phenomenon may in fact occur, though a full dynamical simulation is needed for a detailed assessment.

## Key claim

Spinodal phase separation in baryon-dense matter produced in nuclear collisions may be observable, but only if the collision energy is carefully tuned to maximize the time the bulk system spends inside the mechanically unstable spinodal region; a full dynamical simulation is still needed for a detailed assessment.

## Method

The author constructs a schematic two-phase equation of state for baryon-rich matter using a generalized classical gas in a density-dependent mean field, with parameters adjusted to produce a first-order phase transition. A gradient term proportional to \((\nabla \tilde{\rho})^2\) is added to the compressional energy density to incorporate finite-range effects, enabling the study of interfaces between coexisting phases and the derivation of collective dispersion relations for unstable modes in the spinodal region using ideal or viscous fluid dynamics.

## Key numbers

- The zero-temperature coexistence densities are set to \(\rho_1 = 3\rho_0\) and \(\rho_2 = 8\rho_0\), where \(\rho_0 \approx 0.153\,\text{fm}^{-3}\) is nuclear saturation density.
- The zero-temperature specific heat difference between the two phases is \(w_0(\rho_2) - w_0(\rho_1) = 590\,\text{MeV}/\text{fm}^3\).
- The critical temperature is \(T_c = 170\,\text{MeV}\) and the critical density is \(\rho_c = 4.70\,\rho_0\).
- The zero-temperature interface tension is \(\gamma_0^{12} \approx 16\,\text{MeV}/\text{fm}^2\), obtained with a gradient range parameter \(a = 0.2\,\text{fm}\).
- At \(T_0 = 0\), the interface width is \(b = 2.62\,a\) and the skewness parameter is \(\gamma_3 = 0.96\).

## Context

This work extends the study of spinodal decomposition from the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition (Borderie 1986, PhysRep) to the deconfinement phase transition in baryon-dense matter, addressing a shortcoming of standard local fluid dynamics which cannot produce the characteristic maximum in the growth rate versus wave number. The results enable future full dynamical simulations and are intended to guide experimental searches at RHIC and FAIR, complementing earlier explorations of possible signals by Bower (2001), Randrup (2002), Koch (2006), and Sasaki (2007).
