# Energy of vanishing flow: mass-isospin dependence

> We study the effect of isospin degree of freedom on balance energy throughout the mass range between 50 and 350 for two sets of isotopic systems with N/Z = 1.16 and 1.33 as well as isobaric systems with N/A = 1.0 and 1.4. Our findings indicate that different values of balance energy for two isobaric systems may be mainly due to the Coulomb repulsion. We also demonstrate clearly the dominance of Coulomb repulsion over symmetry energy.

## Key claim

The balance energy (E_bal) for heavy-ion collisions depends on the isospin content of the colliding nuclei, and the difference in E_bal between isobaric systems with different N/Z ratios is primarily due to Coulomb repulsion rather than symmetry energy, as demonstrated by artificially reducing the Coulomb force by a factor of 100.

## Method

The authors used the Isospin-dependent Quantum Molecular Dynamics (IQMD) model to simulate symmetric heavy-ion collisions for a series of isotopic systems with N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4 across a combined mass range from 50 to 350. They extracted the balance energy (E_bal) for each system at semicentral impact parameters (0.35-0.45) using a soft equation of state with an anisotropic, isospin- and energy-dependent nucleon-nucleon cross section of 0.8 times the free cross section. To isolate the role of Coulomb repulsion, they repeated the calculations with the Coulomb potential reduced by a factor of 100.

## Key numbers

- Power law exponent tau for the mass dependence of E_bal (E_bal proportional to A^tau) is -0.50 +/- 0.01 for N/Z = 1.0 and -0.45 +/- 0.01 for N/Z = 1.4.
- With Coulomb reduced by a factor of 100, tau becomes -0.25 +/- 0.02 for N/Z = 1.0 and -0.28 +/- 0.02 for N/Z = 1.4.
- The study covers combined masses from 50 to 350, using systems such as Mg-24, Cu-58, Kr-72, Cd-96, Nd-120, and Ho-135 for N/Z = 1.0, and Ne-24, Cr-58, Zn-72, Zr-96, Sn-120, and Ba-135 for N/Z = 1.4.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies on the mass dependence of balance energy, which reported a power law exponent tau close to -1/3 (Mota, year) for lighter systems, while later work found tau near -0.45 (Sood, year) for heavier masses. The present results confirm the deviation from the -1/3 scaling and demonstrate that the isospin dependence of E_bal is dominated by Coulomb repulsion rather than symmetry energy, clarifying the relative importance of these competing mechanisms. This enables future experimental studies with radioactive beams to isolate Coulomb effects from symmetry energy contributions in heavy-ion collisions.
