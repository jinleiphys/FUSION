# On the fourth P_11 resonance predicted by the constituent quark model

> We point out a distinguishing difference between constituent quark models based on one-gluon exchange and one-boson exchange dynamics. In the latter one, the P_11 nucleon resonance with predominantly symmetrical spatial wave function in the N=4 band gets strongly attracted such that it drops below some states in the N=2 band. Calculations of strong decay widths are presented in order to establish an identification with experimental states. Our results are relevant for the interpretation of the fourth P_11 resonance that was found in the partial wave analysis of the Zagreb group and recently discussed by Capstick et. al. in the framework of a model based on one-gluon exchange.

## Key claim

In constituent quark models based on Goldstone-boson exchange (GBE) dynamics, the fourth P11 nucleon resonance, which has a predominantly symmetrical spatial wave function and belongs to the N=4 band, is strongly attracted and drops below some states in the N=2 band, inverting the level ordering predicted by models based on one-gluon exchange (OGE). This inversion leads to qualitatively different decay widths that agree with the fourth P11 resonance found by the Zagreb group, but the large experimental uncertainties prevent a definitive discrimination between the two models.

## Method

The authors solved the Schrodinger equation for a constituent quark model based on Goldstone-boson exchange dynamics, including tensor components of the pseudoscalar meson-exchange interaction that were omitted in earlier GBE work. They then calculated strong decay widths for the πN and ηN channels using a microscopic 3P0 decay operator, using the theoretical masses from the GBE model as input for phase space factors. The results were compared to predictions from a relativized OGE model (Capstick and Roberts, 1993) and to the experimental four-P11-resonance solution from the Zagreb partial wave analysis (Batinic et al., 1995).

## Key numbers

- GBE model predicts the Roper resonance (P11(1440)) at mass 1443 MeV with Γπ = 646 MeV and Γη = 0 MeV; experimental values from the Zagreb analysis are mass 1439 ± 19 MeV, Γπ = (271 ± 18) +93/-81 MeV, Γη = (0 ± 0) +0/-0 MeV.
- For the P11(1710) state, GBE gives mass 1770 MeV, Γπ = 87 MeV, Γη = 15 MeV; OGE gives Γπ = 18 MeV, Γη = 67 MeV; experimental values are mass 1729 ± 16 MeV, Γπ = (40 ± 40) +11/-0 MeV, Γη = (11 ± 11) +6/-0 MeV.
- The GBE state at 1874 MeV (fourth P11 candidate) has Γπ = 70 MeV and Γη = 0 MeV; the OGE state at 1880 MeV has Γπ = 8 MeV and Γη = 28 MeV; the experimental candidate at 1740 ± 11 MeV has Γπ = (39 ± 39) +24/-0 MeV and Γη = (17 ± 13) +5/-1 MeV.
- The GBE state at 1970 MeV has Γπ = 5 MeV and Γη = 26 MeV; the OGE state at 1975 MeV has Γπ = 4 MeV and Γη = 0 MeV.
- For the P11(2100) state, GBE gives mass 2104 MeV, Γπ = 4 MeV, Γη = 14 MeV; OGE gives mass 2065 MeV, Γπ = 59 MeV, Γη = 3 MeV; experimental values are mass 2157 ± 42 MeV, Γπ = (57 ± 17) +19/-11 MeV, Γη = (295 ± 18) +77/-69 MeV.
- The GBE state at 1874 MeV is 98.5% L=0, S=1/2, identifying it as the second radial excitation (N=4 band) rather than an N=2 band state.

## Context

This work extends the constituent quark model program by comparing predictions from one-gluon exchange dynamics (Capstick and Roberts, 1993) and Goldstone-boson exchange dynamics (Glozman et al., 1998) for the P11 nucleon resonance spectrum. It directly addresses the "missing states" problem discussed by Capstick (1999) and the fourth P11 resonance found by the Zagreb group (Batinic et al., 1995) and later discussed by Capstick et al. (1999) in the OGE framework. The results enable future experimental discrimination between the two dynamical models once resonance parameters are determined with sufficient accuracy, and suggest that a model including three-body forces (Desplanques et al., 1992) might produce a similar level inversion in the upper spectrum.
