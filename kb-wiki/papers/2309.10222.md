# Thermodynamics of spin-1/2 fermions on coarse temporal lattices using automated algebra

> Recent advances in automated algebra for dilute Fermi gases in the virial expansion, where coarse temporal lattices were found advantageous, motivate the study of more general computational schemes that could be applied to arbitrary densities, beyond the dilute limit where the virial expansion is physically reasonable. We propose here such an approach by developing what we call the Quantum Thermodynamics Computational Engine (QTCE). In QTCE, the imaginary-time direction is discretized and the interaction is accounted for via a quantum cumulant expansion, where the coefficients are expressed in terms of noninteracting expectation values. The aim of QTCE is to enable the systematic resolution of interaction effects at fixed temporal discretization, as in lattice Monte Carlo calculations, but here in an algebraic rather than numerical fashion. Using this approach, in combination with numerical integration techniques (both known and alternative ones proposed here), we explore the thermodynamics of spin-1/2 fermions, focusing on the unitary limit in 3 spatial dimensions, but also exploring the effects of continuously varying the spatial dimension below 3. We find that, remarkably, extremely coarse temporal lattices, when suitably renormalized using known results from the virial expansion, yield stable partial sums for QTCE's cumulant expansion which are qualitatively and quantitatively correct in wide regions, compared with known experimental results.

## Key claim

The authors propose that extremely coarse temporal lattices (specifically \(N_\tau = 1\), the simplest possible Suzuki-Trotter factorization), when suitably renormalized using known virial expansion results, yield stable partial sums for the cumulant expansion that are qualitatively and quantitatively correct in wide regions compared with known experimental results, but only up to approximately \(\beta\mu = 1.0\) before the series begins to display oscillatory behavior requiring resummation.

## Method

The authors develop the Quantum Thermodynamics Computational Engine (QTCE), which discretizes the imaginary-time direction and accounts for interactions via a quantum cumulant expansion whose coefficients are expressed in terms of noninteracting expectation values. They use automated algebra to generate trace expressions for density correlations involving the propagator \(G(z) = z e^{-\beta T}/(1 + z e^{-\beta T})\) and first derivatives of the transfer matrix \(U\), then evaluate the resulting multidimensional momentum integrals using a large-fugacity expansion (LZE) in powers of \(z/(1+z)\) rather than the standard virial expansion in powers of \(z\). The method is applied to spin-\(1/2\) fermions with contact interactions, focusing on the unitary limit in 3 spatial dimensions while also exploring continuously varying spatial dimension below 3.

## Key numbers

* Pressure equation of state at unitarity: QTCE with \(N_\tau = 1\) and cumulant order \(N_\kappa = 6\) matches MIT experimental results up to at least \(\beta\mu = 1.0\), with a slight undershooting deviation around \(\beta\mu = 0\)
* The superfluid phase transition location is bounded by critical temperatures corresponding to \(T_c = 0.152(7)\) and \(T_c = 0.171(5)\), giving a vertical gray band around \(\beta\mu \approx 2.4\) in the unitary limit
* The weakest coupling considered is \(\Delta b_2 / \Delta b_2^\text{UFG} = 0.01\), which is indistinguishable from the noninteracting limit on the scale shown
* The unitary limit value of the second virial coefficient change is \(\Delta b_2^\text{UFG} = 1/\sqrt{2}\)

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior automated algebra investigations of the virial expansion for spin-\(1/2\) Fermi gases (Hou 2020, PhysRevA 2020, PhysRevResearch 2021) by proposing a method not a priori restricted to low-density regimes. The QTCE results are benchmarked against experimental data from the MIT group (MITExp) and the ENS group (ENS-Exp), as well as numerical approaches including bold-diagrammatic Monte Carlo (VanHoucke 2012), auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (PhysRevLett 2006), and self-consistent diagrammatic methods (PhysRevA 2007). The approach enables systematic exploration of thermodynamics across the BCS-BEC crossover and in non-integer spatial dimensions, though the cumulant expansion requires resummation methods such as Pade approximants near the superfluid phase transition.
