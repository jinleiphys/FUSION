# Study on axial fields in the dynamically assisted Schwinger effect

> The dynamically assisted Schwinger effect, the generation of fermion-antifermion pairs in vacuum under a strong, slow-varying field and a weak, high-frequency field, has become a promising avenue to probe the vacuum structure and the nonlinear dynamics of QED. However, the role of axial fields in this phenomenon has remained underexplored. This study aims at analyzing how spatial axial fields influence particle production in the dynamically assisted Schwinger effect. Employing the high-frequency effective theory based on the Floquet-Magnus expansion, we demonstrate that a spatial axial field can occur as the effective field of a circular polarized high-frequency plane wave and significantly increase the number of fermions produced across different timescales. This enhancement offers both theoretical insights and useful tools for the experimental implementation of the Schwinger effect.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a spatial axial field can arise as an effective field from a circularly polarized high-frequency plane wave in the dynamically assisted Schwinger effect, and that this axial field significantly enhances fermion pair production across different timescales, but this result is derived using the high-frequency effective theory based on the Floquet-Magnus expansion, which is valid only when the high-frequency field's frequency is much larger than other natural frequencies of the system.

## Method

The author applies the Floquet-Magnus expansion to the single-particle Dirac Hamiltonian under a static electric field plus a high-frequency electromagnetic field, deriving an effective static Hamiltonian up to first order in inverse frequency. The effective Hamiltonian is then used to compute the number of fermions produced in each state via the canonical formulation of the Schwinger effect, transforming the time-dependent problem into a static eigenvalue problem. The specific case of a circularly polarized high-frequency plane wave is analyzed to show how the effective spatial axial field emerges from the commutator structure of the first-order Floquet-Magnus correction.

## Key numbers

- The Schwinger threshold field strength is approximately $10^{16}\ \mathrm{V/cm}$ in SI units, corresponding to $eE_S \sim m^2$ in natural units.
- The particle production rate for a constant electric field satisfies $\Gamma \propto \exp\left(-\pi m^2/(e E)\right)$, where $m$ is the electron mass and $E$ is the field strength.
- The effective spatial axial field from a circularly polarized plane wave is $\bm{A}_5 = -\frac{4eA_\omega^2}{\omega}\bm{e}_3$ for a uniform envelope $f(\bm{x}) = A_\omega$, where $\omega$ is the high frequency.
- For a Gaussian beam envelope $f(\bm{x}) = A_\omega \exp(-r^2/(2\sigma^2))$, the effective axial magnetic field is $\bm{B}_5 = \frac{8eA_\omega^2}{\omega\sigma^2} e^{-r^2/\sigma^2} (\bm{x} \times \bm{e}_z)$.

## Context

This work extends the dynamically assisted Schwinger effect framework, originally proposed by Schutzhold (2008) and Dunne (2009), by incorporating spatial axial fields that were previously underexplored. It builds on Copinger et al. (2020), who showed that background axial fields enhance the imaginary part of the Euler-Heisenberg Lagrangian, and Copinger et al. (2022), who derived the QED one-loop effective action under constant axial fields. The high-frequency effective theory approach using Floquet-Magnus expansion connects to techniques used in condensed matter for Floquet engineering (Bukov 2015, Eckardt 2015) and to recent work on chiral magnetic effects under high-frequency fields by Fukushima (2023). This work enables experimental strategies to lower the Schwinger threshold using laser technology and provides a theoretical framework for studying axial magnetic effects and vortical currents in pair-produced fermion systems.
