# Postfission properties of uranium isotopes: A hybrid method with Langevin dynamics and the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model

> Background: Precise understanding of nuclear fission is crucial for experimental and theoretical nuclear physics, astrophysics, and industrial applications; however, the complete physical mechanics is unresolved due to the complexities. Purpose: In this study, we present a new method to describe the dynamical-fission process and following prompt-neutron emission, where we combine the dynamical fission calculation based on the Langevin method and the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model. Methods: Two methods are connected smoothly within the universal charge distribution and the energy conservation, allowing us to calculate a sequence of fission dynamics and post-fission phase, including prompt neutron emission. Results: Using a certain set of model parameters, we successfully reproduce the experimental primary-fission yields, total kinetic energy, independent-fission yields, and prompt neutron emissions for the neutron induced fission of ${}^{236}$U, a compound nucleus of ${\rm n} + {}^{235}{\rm U}$. We elucidate the physical mechanism of the characteristic features observed in previous experiments, such as shell properties. Additionally, we apply our calculation to two very neutron-rich uranium isotopes, i.e., ${}^{250}$U and ${}^{255}$U, which are not experimentally confirmed but are important for r-process nucleosynthesis. Theoretical results indicate that ${}^{250}$U exhibits an asymmetric multiple-peak fission yield distribution, while the neutron-rich ${}^{255}$U has a single peak due to symmetric fission. Our method predicts post-neutron emission fragments, where ${}^{250}$U shows a stronger neutron emissivity than ${}^{255}$U. Conclusions: Our framework is highly reproducible in the experiments and shows that the number of emitted neutrons after fission differs significantly in neutron-rich uranium fission depending on distributions of fission variables.

## Key claim

The hybrid method combining Langevin dynamics (KiLM) and the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model (CCONE) reproduces experimental fission observables for neutron-induced fission of 236U using a specific set of model parameters, and predicts that the very neutron-rich 250U exhibits an asymmetric multiple-peak fission yield distribution while 255U shows a single symmetric peak, with 250U having stronger neutron emissivity than 255U.

## Method

The authors couple the Kindai University Langevin model (KiLM) for dynamical fission calculations with the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model implemented in the CCONE code for postfission prompt-neutron emission. The two methods are connected using the unchanged-charge distribution (UCD) hypothesis and energy conservation, with the total excitation energy (TXE) partitioned between fragments using an anisothermal parameter RT = 1.2. The nuclear shape is described by three collective coordinates (z, δ, α) in a two-center parametrization, with a neck parameter ε determined by an empirical formula ε(Ac) = 0.01007Ac − 1.94.

- For 250U at E* = 9 MeV: average TKE = 162.50 MeV; average prompt neutron number ⟨n⟩ = 4.185

- Neck parameter ε values: 0.35 for 236U, 0.58 for 250U, and 0.63 for 255U

- Shell damping energy Ed = 20 MeV; zero-point energy estimated as 3.05 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous Langevin-based dynamical fission calculations (Aritomo 2014, 2013) by incorporating postfission neutron emission via the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model, which had not been included in earlier dynamical studies. The method reproduces experimental data for 236U including primary fission yields, TKE, independent yields, and the saw-tooth structure of prompt neutron emission, and enables predictions for neutron-rich uranium isotopes (250U and 255U) relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis in astrophysical environments such as compact-object binary mergers. The transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission in neutron-rich isotopes has been experimentally suggested in Fm isotopes (PhysRevC.16.1483, PhysRevC.5.1725, PhysRevC.40.770) and is now predicted for uranium isotopes.
