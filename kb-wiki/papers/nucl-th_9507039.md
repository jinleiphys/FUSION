# Final state interactions and $NN$ correlations: are the latter observable?

> Are effects of short range correlations in the ground state of the target nucleus (initial state correlations ISC) observable in experiments on quasielastic $A(e,e'p)$ scattering at large missing momentum $p_{m}$? Will the missing momentum spectrum observed at CEBAF be overwhelmed by final state interactions (FSI) of the struck proton? The recent advances in the theory of FSI and findings of complex interplay and strong quantum-mechanical interference of FSI and ISC contributions to scattering at $p_{m}\gsim 1$\,fm$^{-1}$ are reviewed. We conclude that for $p_m \gsim 1$ \, fm$^{-1}$ quasielastic scattering is dominated by FSI effects and the sensitivity to details of the nuclear ground state is lost.

## Key claim
The paper argues that for missing momenta \(p_m \gtrsim 1\) fm\(^{-1}\) in quasielastic \(A(e,e'p)\) scattering, final state interactions (FSI) dominate the cross section and destroy sensitivity to short-range correlations in the nuclear ground state (initial state correlations, ISC). The abstract’s claim that ISC are unobservable is hedged by the condition that this loss of sensitivity occurs only above this momentum threshold, and that the interplay between FSI and ISC involves strong quantum-mechanical interference.

## Method
The authors review recent theoretical advances in the treatment of FSI in quasielastic electron scattering, analyzing the complex interference between FSI and ISC contributions to the missing momentum spectrum. They evaluate the relative magnitudes of FSI and ISC effects for \(p_m \gtrsim 1\) fm\(^{-1}\) and assess whether the CEBAF experiment can isolate ISC signals.

## Key numbers
- The threshold for FSI dominance is \(p_m \gtrsim 1\) fm\(^{-1}\); above this, quasielastic scattering is dominated by FSI effects and sensitivity to the nuclear ground state is lost.
- The missing momentum spectrum at \(p_m \gtrsim 1\) fm\(^{-1}\) is overwhelmed by FSI, making ISC unobservable.

## Context
This work extends earlier theoretical studies of short-range correlations and FSI in \((e,e'p)\) reactions, particularly those by the authors and others in the early 1990s. It contradicts the hope that CEBAF experiments at large missing momentum could cleanly reveal ground-state correlations. The findings enable a more realistic interpretation of future \((e,e'p)\) data by showing that FSI must be fully accounted for before any claims about short-range correlations can be made.
