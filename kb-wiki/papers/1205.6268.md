# Neutron shell structure and deformation in neutron-drip-line nuclei

> Neutron shell-structure and the resulting possible deformation in the neighborhood of neutron-drip-line nuclei are systematically discussed, based on both bound and resonant neutron one-particle energies obtained from spherical and deformed Woods-Saxon potentials. Due to the unique behavior of weakly-bound and resonant neutron one-particle levels with smaller orbital angular-momenta $\ell$, a systematic change of the shell structure and thereby the change of neutron magic-numbers are pointed out, compared with those of stable nuclei expected from the conventional j-j shell-model. For spherical shape with the operator of the spin-orbit potential conventionally used, the $\ell_{j}$ levels belonging to a given oscillator major shell with parallel spin- and orbital-angular-momenta tend to gather together in the energetically lower half of the major shell, while those levels with anti-parallel spin- and orbital-angular-momenta gather in the upper half. The tendency leads to a unique shell structure and possible deformation when neutrons start to occupy the orbits in the lower half of the major shell. Among others, the neutron magic-number N=28 disappears and N=50 may disappear, while the magic number N=82 may presumably survive due to the large $\ell =5$ spin-orbit splitting for the $1h_{11/2}$ orbit. On the other hand, an appreciable amount of energy gap may appear at N=16 and 40 for spherical shape, while neutron-drip-line nuclei in the region of neutron number above N=20, 40 and 82, namely N $\approx$ 21-28, N $\approx$ 41-54, and N $\approx$ 83-90, may be quadrupole-deformed though the possible deformation depends also on the proton number of respective nuclei.

# Neutron shell structure and deformation in neutron-drip-line nuclei

## Key claim

Neutron magic numbers N=28 and N=50 may disappear near the neutron drip line due to the unique behavior of weakly-bound and resonant one-particle levels with small orbital angular momenta, while N=82 may survive because of the large spin-orbit splitting of the 1h11/2 orbit. The systematic grouping of levels with parallel spin and orbital angular momenta in the lower half of each oscillator major shell creates conditions for quadrupole deformation in specific neutron-number regions, though the actual deformation depends on the proton number of each nucleus.

## Method

The author solved the Schrödinger equation for neutron one-particle bound and resonant levels using spherical and axially-symmetric quadrupole-deformed Woods-Saxon potentials, integrating coupled differential equations in coordinate space with correct asymptotic behavior at large radius. For deformed nuclei, resonant energies were defined as the energy at which one of the eigenphases increases through π/2 as energy increases. Nilsson diagrams were constructed for nuclei representative of each neutron-number region of interest, using standard Woods-Saxon parameters (diffuseness a=0.67 fm, radius r0=1.27 fm, depth V = -51 + 33(N-Z)/A MeV) with a conventional spin-orbit potential.

## Key numbers

- Energy difference between 2s1/2 and 1d5/2 levels at β=0 in 18C: 509 keV
- 1d3/2 resonant energy at β=0 in 18C: 4.36 MeV, creating a large energy gap at N=16
- Energy difference between 1f7/2 (weakly bound) and 2p3/2 (low-lying resonant) levels at β=0 in 34Mg: 387 keV, indicating disappearance of N=28 magic gap
- Energy spread of the three levels 1g9/2, 3s1/2, and 2d5/2 at β=0 in 66Ti: within 1.43 MeV, indicating disappearance of N=50 magic gap
- Energy difference between 2f7/2 and 3p3/2 levels at β=0 in 126Ru: 419 keV
- The 3s1/2 bound one-particle level in 66Ti reaches zero binding energy at β=0.12

## Context

This work extends the earlier systematic study of shell-structure change in neutron drip-line nuclei by Hamamoto (2007) and is consistent with the self-consistent mean-field calculations of Dobaczewski et al. (1994), though it disagrees with that work's conclusion that magic gaps at N=28, 50, and 82 do not change appreciably near the drip line. The predicted N=16 magic number was previously suggested by Ozawa et al. (2000) based on experimental evidence, and the weakening of N=20 and N=28 shell closures was mentioned as early as 1975 by Beiner and Lombard using self-consistent calculations with pairing. The results enable predictions for experimental searches: odd-N drip-line nuclei with N=41 such as 63Ti should be studied for ground-state spin assignments, and the "island of inversion" around N=21-28 is explained by the near degeneracy of 1f7/2 and 2p3/2 levels. The work calls for future self-consistent Hartree-Fock calculations using coordinate-space integration with proper asymptotic behavior, rather than harmonic-oscillator basis expansions, to confirm these predictions.
