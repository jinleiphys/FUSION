# Scaling violation and relativistic effective mass from quasielastic electron scattering: implications for neutrino reactions

> The experimental data from quasielastic electron scattering from $^{12}$C are reanalyzed in terms of a new scaling variable suggested by the interacting relativistic Fermi gas with scalar and vector interactions, which is known to generate a relativistic effective mass for the interacting nucleons. By choosing a mean value of this relativistic effective mass $m_N^* =0.8 m_N$, we observe that most of the data fall inside a region around the inverse parabola-shaped universal scaling function of the relativistic Fermi gas. This suggests a method to select the subset of data that highlight the quasielastic region, about two thirds of the total 2,500 data. Regardless of the momentum and energy transfer, this method automatically excludes the data that are not dominated by the quasielastic process. The resulting band of data reflects deviations from the perfect universality, and can be used to characterize experimentally the quasielastic peak, despite the manifest scaling violation. Moreover we show that the spread of the data around the scaling function can be interpreted as genuine fluctuations of the effective mass $M^* \equiv m^*_N/m_N \sim 0.8 \pm 0.1$. Applying the same procedure we transport the scaling quasielastic band into a theoretical prediction band for neutrino scattering cross section that is compatible with the recent measurements and slightly more accurate.

## Key claim

By choosing a mean relativistic effective mass of \(m_N^* = 0.8 m_N\), most of the quasielastic electron scattering data from \(^{12}\text{C}\) collapse around the inverse-parabola scaling function of the relativistic Fermi gas, but the scaling is not perfect; the resulting band of data reflects manifest scaling violation, and the spread can be interpreted as fluctuations of the effective mass quotient \(M^* \equiv m^*_N/m_N \sim 0.8 \pm 0.1\).

## Method

The authors reanalyze over 2,500 experimental \((e,e')\) data points from \(^{12}\text{C}\) using a new scaling variable \(\psi^*\) derived from an interacting relativistic Fermi gas with scalar and vector interactions, which introduces a relativistic effective mass \(m_N^*\) for nucleons in the medium. They compute an experimental scaling function \(f_{\text{exp}}\) by dividing the measured cross section by the Mott cross section times the single-nucleon response factors \(G_L\) and \(G_T\) evaluated with \(m_N^* = 0.8 m_N\). A Gaussian low-pass filtering method (constant weighting function with radius \(r = 0.1\)) selects data clustered around the RFG parabola, retaining about 1,500 points; a Monte Carlo simulation then generates pseudo-data by sampling \(M^*\) from a Gaussian distribution with mean 0.8 and width 0.1 to reproduce the observed spread.

## Key numbers

- Effective mass quotient: \(M^* = m_N^* / m_N = 0.8\) (mean value) with fluctuations \(\pm 0.1\).
- Fermi momentum fixed to \(k_F = 225\) MeV/c.
- About two thirds of the total 2,500 data points (approximately 1,500) fall inside the clustered region around the RFG scaling function.
- Radius for the Gaussian low-pass filtering: \(r = 0.1\) (in \(\psi^*\) units).
- Monte Carlo simulation uses a Gaussian width \(\sigma = 0.1\) for the distribution of \(M^*\).
- Theoretical band for neutrino cross section has fluctuations of about 10\%.
- Axial dipole mass taken as \(M_A = 1\) GeV.

## Context

This work extends the superscaling approach (SuSA) of Amaro et al. (2004) and the SuSA-v2 update of Gonzalez-Jimenez et al. (2014) by connecting the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory to the universal scaling function of the relativistic Fermi gas via an effective mass, avoiding the gauge-invariance violations and translational-invariance issues of finite-nucleus RMF models (Caballero et al., 2007; Alberico et al., 2005, 2007). The method provides a way to isolate quasielastic data from the large \((e,e')\) database and transport the resulting scaling band into predictions for neutrino scattering cross sections, which are compatible with NOMAD (2009) and MiniBooNE (2010) measurements and slightly more accurate than previous models.
