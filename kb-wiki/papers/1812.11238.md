# Lattice QCD and nuclear physics for searches of physics beyond the Standard Model

> Low-energy tests of fundamental symmetries are extremely sensitive probes of physics beyond the Standard Model, reaching scales that are comparable, if not higher, than directly accessible at the energy frontier. The interpretation of low-energy precision experiments and their connection with models of physics beyond the Standard Model relies on controlling the theoretical uncertainties induced by the nonperturbative nature of QCD at low energy and of the nuclear interactions. In these proceedings, I will discuss how the interplay of Lattice QCD and nuclear Effective Field Theories can lead to improved predictions for low-energy experiments, with controlled uncertainties. I will describe the framework of chiral Effective Field Theory, and then discuss a few examples, including non-standard $β$ decays, neutrinoless double beta decay and searches for electric dipole moments, to highlight the progress achieved in recent years, and the role that Lattice QCD will play in addressing the remaining open problems.

## Key claim

The interplay of Lattice QCD and chiral Effective Field Theory can lead to improved predictions for low-energy precision experiments with controlled theoretical uncertainties, but the derivation of a consistent power counting for the strong interaction potential and external currents, allowing the theory to be renormalized, is necessary for the chiral realization of beyond-the-Standard-Model operators, particularly when consistency problems cannot be resolved by fitting low-energy constants to data.

## Method

The paper reviews the framework of chiral Effective Field Theory, which exploits the approximate chiral symmetry of QCD to construct a perturbative expansion of nuclear interactions and currents in powers of Q/Λχ, where Q is a momentum scale of order the pion mass and Λχ ~ 1 GeV is the breakdown scale. For systems with two or more nucleons, the strong interaction potential is constructed from irreducible diagrams and then used to solve the Lippman-Schwinger equation, with low-energy constants fit to nucleon-nucleon scattering data. The matching between quark-level operators and hadronic degrees of freedom is performed for three applications: dark matter direct detection and non-standard beta decays, neutrinoless double beta decay, and electric dipole moment searches.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon tensor charges for u- and d-quarks have been determined by Lattice QCD with 5% precision (Alexandrou 2017, Gupta 2018)
- The CPV pion-nucleon coupling from the QCD θ-bar term is (15.5 ± 2.0 ± 1.6) × 10^(-3) θ-bar, where the first error is from Lattice QCD on the strong contribution to the neutron-proton mass difference and the second is a conservative estimate of missing N^2LO chiral perturbation theory corrections
- Experimental limits include: electron EDM < 1.1 × 10^(-16) e fm (Andreev 2018), neutron EDM < 3.0 × 10^(-13) e fm (Afach 2015), 199Hg EDM < 6.2 × 10^(-17) e fm (Graner 2016), and 225Ra EDM < 1.2 × 10^(-10) e fm (Bishof 2016)
- The Fermi momentum k_F ~ 100 MeV sets the scale for the neutrino potential in neutrinoless double beta decay
- The pion decay constant F_π ~ 92 MeV and the nucleon axial coupling g_A ~ 1.27

## Context

This proceedings paper reviews how the combination of Lattice QCD and chiral Effective Field Theory (Weinberg 1990, 1991) enables controlled theoretical predictions for low-energy symmetry tests, extending earlier work on nuclear forces and currents (Pastore 2008, Kolling 2009, Baroni 2015, Krebs 2016). For neutrinoless double beta decay, the paper highlights that the leading-order transition operator has a short-range component not included in existing nuclear matrix element calculations, which requires Lattice QCD calculations of processes like π-π- → e-e- (Feng 2018) and nn → pp e-e- to determine the relevant low-energy constants. For electric dipole moments, the paper shows how CPV pion-nucleon couplings can be extracted from baryon spectrum calculations using chiral perturbation theory (Crewther 1979, deVries 2016, Cirigliano 2016), with Lattice QCD providing the necessary input for the strong contribution to the nucleon mass difference (Borsanyi 2013, 2014, Brantley 2016).
