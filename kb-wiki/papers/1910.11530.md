# Identifying the nature of the QCD transition in relativistic collision of heavy nuclei with deep learning

> Using deep convolutional neural network (CNN), the nature of the QCD transition can be identified from the final-state pion spectra from hybrid model simulations of heavy-ion collisions that combines a viscous hydrodynamic model with a hadronic cascade "after-burner". Two different types of equations of state (EoS) of the medium are used in the hydrodynamic evolution. The resulting spectra in transverse momentum and azimuthal angle are used as the input data to train the neural network to distinguish different EoS. Different scenarios for the input data are studied and compared in a systematic way. A clear hierarchy is observed in the prediction accuracy when using the event-by-event, cascade-coarse-grained and event-fine-averaged spectra as input for the network, which are about 80%, 90% and 99%, respectively. A comparison with the prediction performance by deep neural network (DNN) with only the normalized pion transverse momentum spectra is also made. High-level features of pion spectra captured by a carefully-trained neural network were found to be able to distinguish the nature of the QCD transition even in a simulation scenario which is close to the experiments.

## Key claim

A deep convolutional neural network can identify whether the QCD transition is a crossover or a first-order phase transition from final-state pion spectra in hybrid model simulations of heavy-ion collisions, achieving up to 99% accuracy when event-fine-averaged spectra are used, but only when the network is trained and tested on data from the same hybrid simulation framework.

## Method

The authors used the iEBE-VISHNU hybrid model, which combines a (2+1)D viscous hydrodynamic simulator (VISHNew) with a hadronic cascade afterburner (UrQMD), to generate event-by-event simulations of Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV and Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV. Two different equations of state were implemented in the hydrodynamic evolution: a crossover type based on lattice QCD parametrization (L-EOS) and a first-order type with a Maxwell construction at T_c=165 MeV (Q-EOS). The final-state pion spectra in transverse momentum (p_T up to 2 GeV) and azimuthal angle (24x24 bins) were used as input to train a convolutional neural network to distinguish between the two EoS types, with training data generated across multiple parameter sets including shear viscosity, equilibration time, and switching temperature.

## Key numbers

- Validation accuracy of approximately 80% when using event-by-event pion spectra as input with switching temperature T_sw=137 MeV
- Validation accuracy of approximately 92% when using cascade-coarse-grained spectra (averaged over 30 cascade repetitions) with T_sw=137 MeV
- Validation accuracy of approximately 99% when using 30-events-fine-averaged spectra with T_sw=137 MeV
- Testing accuracy of 95% on average for the 30-events-fine-averaged trained network on testing data simulated in centrality range 0-50% with MC-Glauber or MCKLN initial conditions
- Validation accuracy of approximately 78% for event-by-event input with early transition (T_sw=160 MeV)
- Validation accuracy of 87.5% at most for cascade-coarse-grained input with T_sw=155 or 160 MeV
- Testing accuracy of 83% on average for cascade-coarse-grained trained network with early transition

## Context

This work extends the previous pure hydrodynamic study by Pang et al. (2018), which achieved approximately 95% accuracy using a CNN with smooth primordial pion spectra from CLVisc hydrodynamic simulations, by incorporating more realistic hadronic cascade effects and resonance decays via the UrQMD afterburner. The study demonstrates that while finite-number fluctuations and hadronic rescattering degrade classification performance compared to the pure hydrodynamic case, event averaging within fine centrality bins can recover the discriminative power, suggesting that experimental measurements with sufficient statistics could potentially distinguish the nature of the QCD transition. The approach contrasts with traditional observables like anisotropic flow and particle multiplicity fluctuations, which have not provided a disentangled mapping to the EoS type, and with Bayesian analysis methods such as those by Bernhard et al. (2019) that use event-averaged data to infer transport properties rather than the transition type directly.
