# Factorization in large-scale many-body calculations

> One approach for solving interacting many-fermion systems is the configuration-interaction method, also sometimes called the interacting shell model, where one finds eigenvalues of the Hamiltonian in a many-body basis of Slater determinants (antisymmeterized products of single-particle wavefunctions). The resulting Hamiltonian matrix is typically very sparse, but for large systems the nonzero matrix elements can nonetheless require terabytes or more of storage. An alternate algorithm, applicable to a broad class of systems with symmetry, in our case rotational invariance, is to exactly factorize both the basis and the interaction using additive/multiplicative quantum numbers; such an algorithm can reduce the storage requirements by an order of magnitude or more. We discuss factorization in general as well as in the context of a specific configuration-interaction code, BIGSTICK, which runs both on serial and parallel machines.

## Key claim

The paper claims that by exactly factorizing both the many-body basis and the Hamiltonian using additive quantum numbers (specifically the z-component of angular momentum), one can reduce storage requirements for configuration-interaction calculations by an order of magnitude or more, but this approach is only applicable to systems with two or more species of fermions (e.g., protons and neutrons, or spin-up and spin-down electrons) and relies on rotational invariance of the Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors implement a factorization scheme in the M-scheme basis where the many-body basis states are constructed as products of proton and neutron Slater determinants, each organized into sectors labeled by the z-component of angular momentum (M_p and M_n). The Hamiltonian is similarly factorized into one-body and two-body operators acting separately on each species, with cross-species interactions decomposed into products of one-body operators. The code BIGSTICK uses a novel "double-factorization" method that speeds up basis generation and array setup by recreating many-body matrix elements on the fly rather than storing them.

## Key numbers

- For 5 protons and 6 neutrons in the sd valence space (²⁷Al), the total many-body basis dimension is 80,115, constructed from only 792 proton Slater determinants and 923 neutron Slater determinants
- For ⁵²Fe in the pf shell, the M-scheme basis dimension is 1.1 × 10⁸ with sparsity 1 × 10⁻⁵ and storage requirement of 720 GB for nonzero matrix elements
- For ⁵⁶Ni in the pf shell, the basis dimension is 1.1 × 10⁹ with sparsity 2 × 10⁻⁶ and storage requirement of 9600 GB
- For ¹²C in the N_shell = 4 space, the basis dimension is 5.9 × 10¹¹ with sparsity 8 × 10⁻⁹ and storage requirement of 1 × 10⁷ GB
- The average redundancy of Hamiltonian matrix elements ranges from 18 (for ⁴He, N_max = 16) to 5 × 10⁷ (for ⁵⁶Ni in pf shell)
- For ²⁸Si in the sd shell, the average redundancy is 9300, with 2.6 × 10⁷ nonzero matrix elements and only 2800 unique matrix elements

## Context

This work extends the factorization techniques previously implemented in major CI codes such as ANTOINE (by Caurier and collaborators), NATHAN, EICODE, and NuShellX, and provides a detailed exposition of the algorithmic foundations. The paper introduces a novel "double-factorization" method that forms the heart of the authors' unpublished code BIGSTICK, which enables calculations with basis dimensions up to 10¹¹ while dramatically reducing memory requirements compared to storage-based approaches like those used in MFDn or OXBASH.
