# Classical and Quantum Evolution in a Simple Coherent Neutrino Problem

> The extraordinary neutrino flux produced in extreme astrophysical environments like the early universe, core-collapse supernovae and neutron star mergers may produce coherent quantum neutrino oscillations on macroscopic length scales. The Hamiltonian describing this evolution can be mapped into quantum spin models with all-to-all couplings arising from neutrino-neutrino forward scattering. To date many studies of these oscillations have been performed in a mean-field limit where the neutrinos time evolve in a product state. In this paper we examine a simple two-beam model evolving from an initial product state and compare the mean-field and many-body evolution. The symmetries in this model allow us to solve the real-time evolution for the quantum many-body system for hundreds or thousands of spins, far beyond what would be possible in a more general case with an exponential number ($2^N$) of quantum states. We compare mean-field and many-body solutions for different initial product states and ratios of one- and two-body couplings, and find that in all cases in the limit of infinite spins the mean-field (product state) and many-body solutions coincide for simple observables. This agreement can be understood as a consequence of the fact that the typical initial condition represents a very local but dense distribution about a mean energy in the spectrum of the Hamiltonian. We explore quantum information measures like entanglement entropy and purity of the many-body solutions, finding intriguing relationships between the quantum information measures and the dynamical behavior of simple physical observables.

## Key claim

The mean-field (product state) and many-body solutions for simple one-body observables coincide in the limit of infinite spins for all initial product states and ratios of one- and two-body couplings studied, except when the initial product state is an eigenstate of the one-body Hamiltonian; this agreement holds despite the presence of substantial entanglement in the many-body state.

## Method

The authors study a two-beam model of neutrinos with all-to-all couplings from coherent forward scattering, mapped to a quantum spin Hamiltonian with two distinct momentum directions and energies. They exploit symmetries that allow exact diagonalization of the many-body Hamiltonian for hundreds to thousands of spins by working in the angular momentum basis where the Hamiltonian is tri-diagonal, avoiding the exponential scaling of the general case. They compare the full quantum evolution to mean-field solutions obtained from the Ehrenfest theorem with a product-state approximation for two-body expectation values, examining both bipolar oscillation and collective precession initial conditions.

## Key numbers

- The variance of the Hamiltonian with respect to an initial product state scales as ΔH² = c₁N + c₀, where c₀ = (N_A N_B)/(4N²)(1 - (n̂_A · n̂_B))² and c₁ contains terms proportional to Ω², Ω, and Ω⁰
- The number of energy states with nonzero overlap with the initial condition scales as N^(3/2), not exponentially
- For bipolar oscillations with mixing angle θ = 0.001 and N = 128, the ratio c₀/(c₁N) ≈ 4.0 × 10⁶, leading to significant disagreement between many-body and mean-field; for θ = 0.1 and N = 2048, c₀/(c₁N) ≈ 0.2, and mean-field behavior emerges
- The skewness of the Hamiltonian distribution approaches 0 and the kurtosis approaches 3 in the large N limit, indicating a Gaussian energy distribution
- The instability condition for bipolar oscillations is (√η - √(1-η))² < Ω < (√η + √(1-η))², where η = N_e/N

## Context

This work extends studies of dense neutrino gases that have been performed almost exclusively in the mean-field approximation (Raffelt 2007, Hannestad 2006, Duan 2007) and addresses recent findings by Patwardhan (2019), Patwardhan (2021), and Rrapaj (2020) suggesting that mean-field and many-body solutions can deviate significantly in some cases. The two-beam model with its symmetries enables exact many-body solutions for system sizes far beyond what is possible in general cases, and the results provide a foundation for testing quantum simulators including trapped-ion and Rydberg cold atom systems (Monroe 2021, Ebadi 2021). The work also connects to studies of dynamical phase transitions in spin models with all-to-all interactions (Roggero 2021).
