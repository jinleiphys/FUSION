# Layers of electron captures in the crust of accreting neutron stars

> The accumulation of accreted matter onto the neutron star surface triggers exothermic reactions in the crust. The heat released as a result influences the luminosity exhibited by the X-ray transient. The most common approach to the kinetics of exothermic reactions in the crust of accreting neutron stars is to consider an infinite reaction rate. Here, we investigate accretion-related heat release in the accreted outer crust of a neutron star by including a time-dependent accretion cycle and experimentally based reaction rates in the kinetics of electron captures above the reaction threshold. A simple model was used to compute the zero temperature equation of state of a crust in which two nuclei can coexist. We solved the abundance of parent nuclei as a function of the depth in the star and the time variable using astrophysically motivated features of the accreting system. We calculated the heat release and neutrino loss associated to reactions in the outer crust. We report the existence of layers in the outer crust, which contain both parent and grand-daughter nuclei of electron captures. The reactions can occur deeper in the shell than the reaction threshold, thus releasing more heat per accreted baryon for a given accretion rate. The electron capture layers continue to exist even when the accretion has stopped. The heat sources are time- and pressure-dependent in accreting crusts of neutron stars. The total heat released is a function of astrophysical (active and quiescent time) and microscopic (reaction rate) parameters Therefore, we conclude these parameters should be considered individually and carefully for a range of different sources.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that including finite, experimentally-based reaction rates for electron captures in the outer crust of accreting neutron stars produces layers where parent and grand-daughter nuclei coexist, and these reactions can occur deeper than the instantaneous threshold pressure, releasing more heat per accreted baryon than predicted by the standard infinite-rate approximation.

## Method

The authors constructed a zero-temperature equation of state for the outer crust using a linear mixing rule that allows coexistence of two nuclear species (parent and grand-daughter) in a lattice with a degenerate electron gas. They solved the continuity equation for parent nucleus abundance as a function of pressure and time, incorporating experimentally measured nuclear masses from AME2020 and beta-decay half-lives to compute finite electron capture rates. The model was applied to a fully accreted outer crust composed of 56Fe ashes, with astrophysically motivated accretion cycles alternating active phases (4 years) and quiescent phases (40 years) at a mean accretion rate of 10^-8 solar masses per year.

## Key numbers

- Four electron capture pairs occur in the outer crust: Z=26→24, Z=24→22, Z=22→20, and Z=20→18, with threshold pressures ranging from 4.13×10^-7 MeV/fm^3 to 2.40×10^-4 MeV/fm^3
- Electron capture timescales range from 7.34×10^3 s (Z=22→21) to 8.67×10^6 s (Z=26→25)
- Accretion timescales for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star with 11 km radius at 10^-8 solar masses per year range from 9.50×10^7 s (Z=26→24) to 5.67×10^10 s (Z=20→18)
- Energy thresholds W1 for the first electron capture range from 4.31 MeV (Z=26→25) to 21.09 MeV (Z=20→19)
- The first shell (Z=26→25) involves an excited state of 56Mn with excitation energy 110 keV

## Context

This work extends the instantaneous reaction rate approximation used in deep crustal heating models by Sato (1979), Haensel (1990, 2003, 2008), and Fantina (2018) to include finite, time-dependent electron capture kinetics. It builds on studies of finite reaction rates by Bildsten (1998) and Ushomirsky (2000) but adds nucleus-specific half-lives from experimental data and transient accretion cycles. The results enable more accurate modeling of thermal relaxation in X-ray transient sources such as EXO 0748-676, KS 1731-260, XTE J1701-462, and IGR J17480-2446, where the time-dependent heat release must be considered individually for each source.
