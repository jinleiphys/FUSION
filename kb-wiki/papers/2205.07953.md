# Application of multilayer perceptron with data augmentation in nuclear physics

> Neural networks have become popular in many fields of science since they serve as promising, reliable and powerful tools. In this work, we study the effect of data augmentation on the predictive power of neural network models for nuclear physics data. We present two different data augmentation techniques, and we conduct a detailed analysis in terms of different depths, optimizers, activation functions and random seed values to show the success and robustness of the model. Using the experimental uncertainties for data augmentation for the first time, the size of the training data set is artificially boosted and the changes in the root-mean-square error between the model predictions on the test set and the experimental data are investigated. Our results show that the data augmentation decreases the prediction errors, stabilizes the model and prevents overfitting. The extrapolation capabilities of the MLP models are also tested for newly measured nuclei in AME2020 mass table, and it is shown that the predictions are significantly improved by using data augmentation.

## Key claim

Using experimental uncertainties for data augmentation in multilayer perceptron (MLP) models for nuclear binding energy prediction decreases prediction errors, stabilizes the model, and prevents overfitting, but the improvement is architecture-dependent and some MLP configurations show degraded performance after simple error augmentation.

## Method

The authors trained MLP models with one to four hidden layers using proton number (Z) and mass number (A) as inputs to predict total nuclear binding energies from the AME2016 mass table (2408 nuclei with Z,N≥8). They applied two data augmentation techniques to the training set of 1685 nuclei: (1) resampling each nucleus twice by adding and subtracting its experimental uncertainty, and (2) Gaussian noise resampling up to 5 iterations using the experimental mean and standard deviation. The test set of 723 nuclei was fixed, and 10 calculations with different random seeds were performed for each architecture to obtain mean root-mean-square errors.

## Key numbers

* Without augmentation, MLP architectures predicted r.m.s. errors between 1.6 and 2.0 MeV for the test set nuclei.
* Simple error augmentation increased training data from 1685 to 4995 nuclei; the best improvement was 16.4% (from 1.903 to 1.591 MeV) for the (128) architecture, while the (32-16-8) architecture showed a 7.4% degradation (from 1.882 to 2.022 MeV).
* With 5-resampling Gaussian augmentation (10110 training nuclei), the predictive power increased by 18.86% for the (128) architecture, 25.44% for (32-32), 25.12% for (32-16-8), and 30.50% for (32-16-16-8) on the test set.
* For extrapolation to 71 new nuclei in AME2020, r.m.s. errors without augmentation were above 2.2 MeV; after 5-resampling Gaussian augmentation, performance improved by up to 23.47% for (128), 29.40% for (32-32), 28.68% for (32-16-8), and 36.33% for (32-16-16-8).
* Using the Sigmoid activation function with 9 resamplings (16850 training nuclei), improvement reached 72.22% on the test set and 83.90% for extrapolation nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the application of neural networks to nuclear binding energy prediction, following pioneering studies by Gazula (1992) and subsequent work by Gernoth (1993), Athanassopoulos (2004), and more recent MLP applications by Yuksel (2021) and others. The authors introduce data augmentation using experimental uncertainties for the first time in nuclear physics regression, addressing the scarcity of experimental nuclear data compared to the approximately 7000 predicted nuclei from energy density functional calculations by Erler (2012). This enables improved predictions for nuclei near the neutron drip line, as demonstrated using the newly measured nuclei in the AME2020 mass table by Wang (2021).
