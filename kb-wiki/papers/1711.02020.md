# Lattice QCD input for nuclear structure and reactions

> Explorations of the properties of light nuclear systems beyond their lowest-lying spectra have begun with Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics. While progress has been made in the past year in pursuing calculations with physical quark masses, studies of the simplest nuclear matrix elements and nuclear reactions at heavier quark masses have been conducted, and several interesting results have been obtained. A community effort has been devoted to investigate the impact of such Quantum Chromodynamics input on the nuclear many-body calculations. Systems involving hyperons and their interactions have been the focus of intense investigations in the field, with new results and deeper insights emerging. While the validity of some of the previous multi-nucleon studies has been questioned during the past year, controversy remains as whether such concerns are relevant to a given result. In an effort to summarize the newest developments in the field, this talk will touch on most of these topics.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD can provide first-principles input for nuclear structure and reactions, but the field remains in its infancy and controversy persists over whether ground-state saturation methods in multi-nucleon calculations yield reliable results, with the HAL QCD collaboration arguing that "fake plateaus" may appear in effective-mass plots at early times.

## Method

The paper reviews two main methodological approaches for extracting multi-baryon observables from Lattice QCD: (1) ground-state saturation of correlation functions, where momentum-projected hadronic correlation functions are fit to single or multi-exponential forms at intermediate to late times to extract finite-volume energy spectra, which are then used with Luscher's method to obtain elastic scattering amplitudes; and (2) the HAL QCD potential method, which uses equal-time Nambu-Bethe-Salpeter wavefunctions to construct a non-local two-baryon potential that is expanded in a low-order velocity expansion, from which scattering phase shifts are obtained without requiring ground-state saturation in the two-hadron sector.

## Key numbers

* Pion mass of approximately 806 MeV used in NPLQCD studies of two-nucleon systems at a SU(3) flavor-symmetric point, with a single lattice spacing a = 0.145(2) fm and three different volumes L ≈ 3.4, 4.5, and 6.7 fm
* HAL QCD collaboration reported a study at nearly physical quark masses corresponding to a pion mass of mπ = 146 MeV for systems involving two octet baryons and the maximally-strange Ω baryon
* NPLQCD obtained the cross section for the pp fusion process pp → d e⁺ ν_e at a pion mass of approximately 806 MeV
* NPLQCD obtained the matrix element relevant for the neutrinoless double-β decay process nn → ppee ν̄_e ν̄_e at a pion mass of approximately 806 MeV

## Context

This talk summarizes developments presented at the Lattice2017 conference, extending the first LQCD study of light nuclei and hypernuclei conducted in 2012 by the NPLQCD collaboration (Beane et al., 2012) at a SU(3) flavor-symmetric point with quark masses corresponding to approximately 806 MeV pions and kaons. The work addresses ongoing controversy between the ground-state saturation approach used by NPLQCD and PACS-CS collaborations and the potential method used by HAL QCD collaboration, with rebuttals by Beane et al. (2017) and Wagman et al. (2017) arguing that concerns about fake plateaus are not relevant to calculations using physically-motivated source and sink operators. The results enable future first-principles constraints on fundamental nuclear reaction cross sections such as those in the pp chain in the Sun and at facilities like the National Ignition Facility, as well as Standard Model contributions to searches for physics beyond the Standard Model.
