# Bayesian probability updates using Sampling/Importance Resampling: Applications in nuclear theory

> We review an established Bayesian sampling method called sampling/importance resampling and highlight situations in nuclear theory when it can be particularly useful. To this end we both analyse a toy problem and demonstrate realistic applications of importance resampling to infer the posterior distribution for parameters of $Δ$NNLO interaction model based on chiral effective field theory and to estimate the posterior probability distribution of target observables. The limitation of the method is also showcased in extreme situations where importance resampling breaks.

# Bayesian probability updates using Sampling/Importance Resampling: Applications in nuclear theory

## Key claim
Sampling/Importance Resampling (S/IR) can serve as a computationally efficient alternative to Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) for Bayesian posterior updates in nuclear theory, but only when the number of effective samples remains sufficiently large; the method fails when the likelihood becomes too complex or when prior samples fail to resolve a very peaked posterior, and failure can occur without clear warning if the prior has very small overlap with the posterior.

## Method
The authors apply the weighted bootstrap variant of Sampling/Importance Resampling to infer the posterior distribution of seven low-energy constants (LECs) of the ΔNNLO chiral interaction model, using deuteron observables (ground-state energy, point-proton radius, quadrupole moment) as calibration data. They draw 2×10⁴ samples from a prior distribution (a product of a multivariate Gaussian for four pion-nucleon LECs and a uniform distribution for three contact LECs, informed by a Roy-Steiner analysis and history matching), compute importance weights from the likelihood, and resample 1.5×10⁵ posterior samples. The results are compared with MCMC sampling using the emcee toolkit with 150 walkers and 5×10⁵ steps, and the method is also demonstrated for posterior updating when ³H and ⁴He observables are added to the calibration set.

## Key numbers
- S/IR used 2×10⁴ prior samples and 2×10⁴ likelihood evaluations, compared to 7.6×10⁷ likelihood evaluations for MCMC with 150 walkers and 5×10⁵ steps.
- For the deuteron-only calibration, the number of effective samples was n_eff = 1589.9; when ³H and ⁴He observables were added, n_eff dropped to 314.9.
- When all error assignments were reduced by an order of magnitude, n_eff fell to 4.4 and S/IR failed to reproduce the MCMC posterior.
- The emulators used for deuteron and few-body observables reduced computation time from seconds to milliseconds while keeping relative error within 0.001% compared to full no-core shell model calculations.
- Calibration data included deuteron ground-state energy E(²H) = -2.2298 MeV, point-proton radius R_p(²H) = 1.976 fm, and quadrupole moment Q(²H) = 0.27 e²fm², each with assigned model, method, and emulator errors.

## Context
This work extends the application of S/IR (Rubin 1988, Smith 1992) to nuclear theory, where MCMC sampling (Metropolis 1953, Hastings 1970) is standard but computationally expensive due to costly likelihood evaluations. The method is demonstrated on the ΔNNLO chiral interaction (Ekström 2017) with LEC constraints from a Roy-Steiner analysis (Siemens 2017) and history matching (Hu 2021). The paper enables efficient Bayesian updating when calibration data are added in stages or when prior sensitivity is explored, and it identifies the effective sample size as a diagnostic for method reliability. The authors note that history matching is a useful tool to ensure prior overlap with the posterior and avoid silent failure of S/IR.
