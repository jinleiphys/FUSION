# Dynamical magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions

> The magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions are important ingredients for many interesting phenomena, such as the Chiral Magnetic Effect, Chiral Magnetic Wave, the directed flow $v_1$ of $D^0$ mesons and the splitting of the spin polarization of the $Λ$/$\barΛ$. Quantitative studies of these phenomena however suffer from limited understanding on the dynamical evolution of these fields in the medium created by the collisions, which remains a critical and challenging problem. The initial magnetic fields from the colliding nuclei decay very fast in the vacuum but their lifetime could be extended through medium response due to electrically conducting quarks and antiquarks. Here we perform a detailed analysis of such medium effect on the dynamical magnetic fields by numerically solving the Maxwell's equations concurrently with the expanding medium described by viscous hydrodynamics, under the assumption of negligible back reaction of the fields on the fluid evolution. Our results suggest a considerable enhancement of late time magnetic fields, the magnitude of which depends sensitively on the fireball expansion as well as the medium electric conductivity both before and during hydrodynamic stage.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the lifetime of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions can be considerably extended by the conducting quark-gluon plasma medium, but the magnitude of this enhancement depends sensitively on the fireball expansion dynamics and the electric conductivity both before and during the hydrodynamic stage, under the assumption that the back reaction of the fields on the fluid evolution is negligible.

## Method

The authors numerically solve Maxwell's equations concurrently with a viscous hydrodynamic description of the expanding medium, using the weak-field method where the electromagnetic fields do not affect the fluid evolution. The simulation covers three stages: an initial vacuum stage (tau = 0.0 to 0.1 fm), a pre-equilibrium stage (tau = 0.1 to 0.4 fm) with Bjorken expansion and several models for the effective electric conductivity (zero, constant, linear), and a hydrodynamic stage (tau >= 0.4 fm) where the medium is described by Bjorken flow, Gubser flow, or realistic hydro profiles from the MUSIC package. The Maxwell equations are solved using the Yee algorithm on a staggered grid, separating the fields into external contributions from the moving nuclei and internal contributions from the medium response.

## Key numbers

- Initial magnetic fields in Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV reach about eB ~ m_pi^2 ~ 10^18 Gauss
- The vacuum field lifetime at mid-rapidity is estimated as tau_B ~ 0.06 fm for Au+Au at 200 GeV and about 0.005 fm for Pb+Pb at 2.76 TeV
- The electric conductivity from lattice QCD is taken as sigma_LQCD = 5.8 MeV, scaled by a factor lambda (tested at lambda = 1, 10, 100, 1000)
- For the hydrodynamic stage, the authors use sigma/T = 0.1 as a reasonable value, and also test sigma/T = 100 for the large conductivity limit
- The pre-equilibrium stage is taken from tau = 0.1 to 0.4 fm, with the hydrodynamic stage starting at tau_0 = 0.4 fm
- The impact parameter used is b = 6 fm for Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV
- The Gaussian spatial profile uses R_x = R_A - b/2, R_y = sqrt(R_A^2 - b^2/4), R_z = 3 fm, with R_A = 6.38 fm for gold nuclei

## Context

This work extends previous studies of magnetic field evolution in heavy-ion collisions that used simplified assumptions such as constant conductivity, static medium, or one-dimensional Bjorken expansion only (McLerran and Skokov 2013, Tuchin 2014, Gursoy 2014, Stewart 2017). The authors improve upon these by incorporating realistic three-dimensional hydrodynamic backgrounds from the MUSIC package (Gale 2013, Schenke 2010) and testing multiple models for the pre-equilibrium conductivity. The results enable more quantitative predictions for magnetic-field-induced phenomena such as the Chiral Magnetic Effect, Chiral Magnetic Wave, directed flow of D0 mesons, and Lambda/anti-Lambda spin polarization splitting.
