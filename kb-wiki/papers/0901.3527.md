# Efficient and robust calculation of femtoscopic correlation functions in spherical harmonics directly from the raw pairs measured in heavy-ion collisions

> We present the formalism for calculating the femtoscopic correlation function directly in spherical harmonics. The numerator and denominator are stored as a set of one-dimensional histograms representing the spherical harmonic decompositions of each. We present the formalism to calculate the correlation function from them directly, without going to any three-dimensional histogram. We discuss the practical implementation of the method and we provide an example of its use. We also discuss the stability of the method in the presence of $θ$-$ϕ$ holes in the underlying data (e.g. from experimental acceptance).

# Efficient and robust calculation of femtoscopic correlation functions in spherical harmonics directly from the raw pairs measured in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The authors present a formalism to compute the femtoscopic correlation function directly in spherical harmonics by constructing the raw pair distributions (numerator and denominator) as one-dimensional histograms of spherical harmonic coefficients, avoiding the use of three-dimensional histograms. The method remains stable in the presence of angular acceptance holes in the data, provided sufficient statistics remain outside the hole region, because the approach treats the ratio as an inverse problem with proper covariance weighting rather than a simple bin-by-bin division.

## Method

The numerator (true pair distribution) and denominator (mixed pair distribution) are expanded in spherical harmonics by summing over individual pairs, where each pair contributes the spherical harmonic weight \(Y^*_{\ell m}(\Omega_{\hat{\bf q}})\) to the appropriate radial bin in \(q = |{\bf q}|\). The correlation function \(C_{\ell m}(q)\) is then extracted by solving a linear inverse problem: minimizing a \(\chi^2\) that uses the full covariance matrix of the numerator, treating the denominator as known to high precision, via the standard imaging solution \( {\bf C}_q = {\bf \Delta}^{2}{\bf C}_q \cdot {\bf \tilde{M}}_q^{T} \cdot ({\bf \Delta}^{2}{\bf T}_q)^{-1} \cdot {\bf T}_q \). The method stores the real and imaginary parts of each \(\ell,m\) component as separate one-dimensional histograms in \(q\), and each \(q\) bin is solved independently.

## Key numbers

- The practical limit for the spherical harmonic expansion in femtoscopy is \(\ell \leq 4\), with most important information contained in \(\ell=0\), \(\ell=1\), and \(\ell=2\) components.
- In the acceptance hole test, a hole was introduced at midrapidity (small \(\cos\theta\)) at small \(q\) from 0.01 to 0.05 GeV/c with varying width in \(\phi\) from 0 to \(3\pi/2\) (i.e., up to 75% of acceptance removed).
- The source size in the Therminator model test was set to approximately 3-4 fm in the longitudinally co-moving system.
- The method was demonstrated on \(\pi^+K^+\) correlation functions simulated with the Therminator model using STAR detector acceptance.

## Context

This work extends the spherical harmonic decomposition approach previously applied to femtoscopic correlations by Brown (2005) and Chajecki (2008), but moves the decomposition from the correlation function level to the raw pair distribution level. Prior methods stored numerator and denominator as three-dimensional histograms in Bertsch-Pratt coordinates or spherical coordinates, requiring large statistics per bin and suffering from acceptance holes. The new method enables direct computation of the correlation function in spherical harmonics without three-dimensional binning, preserves the full cross-\(\ell,m\) data covariance that was previously ignored, and remains robust against angular acceptance holes common in experiments such as STAR. This enables more efficient analysis of femtoscopic data and allows application of experimental corrections (track merging/splitting weights, purity corrections) directly in the spherical harmonic representation.
