# Accuracy of the mean-field theory in describing ground-state properties of light nuclei

> The relativistic mean-field model, augmented with three types of center-of-mass corrections and two types of rotational corrections, is employed to investigate the ground-state properties of helium, beryllium, and carbon isotopes. The efficacy of the mean-field approach in describing the binding energies, quadrupole deformations, root-mean-square charge radii, root-mean-square matter radii, and neutron skins of these light nuclei is evaluated. By averaging the binding energies obtained from six selected effective interactions, a mass-dependent behavior of the mean-field approximation is elucidated. The findings from radii reveal that, unlike in heavy nuclei, the exchange terms of the center-of-mass correction play an indispensable role in accurately describing the radii of light nuclei. The mean-field approximation, when augmented with center-of-mass and rotational corrections, effectively reproduces the energies and radii of light nuclei. However, due to the absence of many-body correlations between valence neutrons, the mean-field approximation falls short in describing the deformations and shell evolutions of the helium and beryllium isotopic chains.

## Key claim

The relativistic mean-field approximation, when augmented with both microscopic center-of-mass corrections (including exchange terms) and rotational corrections, can effectively reproduce the binding energies and radii of light nuclei with Z<8, but it fails to describe the deformations and shell evolutions of helium and beryllium isotopic chains due to the absence of many-body correlations between valence neutrons.

## Method

The author employed the multidimensional constrained relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (MDCRHB) model with six selected effective interactions (PK1, PKDD, DD-ME2, DD-LZ1, DD-PC1, and NL5(A)) to calculate ground-state properties of even-even helium, beryllium, and carbon isotopes. Three types of center-of-mass corrections (harmonic oscillator approximation, microscopic method with direct terms only, and microscopic method with full exchange terms) and two types of rotational corrections were applied to the mean-field results. The root-mean-square radii were computed including full microscopic center-of-mass correction with exchange terms, which had previously been deemed negligible in the Z>8 mass region.

## Key numbers

- For $^4$He, the mean-field energy contributes about 53% and the center-of-mass energy contributes about 47% to the total binding energy; the rotational energy correction is zero.
- For $^8$Be, the mean-field energies contribute 59.9% to 71.1% of the $E_B/A$ values, center-of-mass correction accounts for more than 20%, and rotational correction accounts for about 10%.
- For $^{12}$C, the mean-field, center-of-mass, and rotational motions take up about 82%, 13%, and 5% of the $E_B/A$ values respectively, except for spherical results from DD-LZ1 and DD-PC1.
- The largest deviation of the $E_B/A$ values across all calculated nuclei is 0.416 MeV, calculated by DD-PC1 in $^8$Be.
- For $^4$He, the experimental charge radius is 1.681(4) fm; calculated values with full exchange terms range from 1.838 fm (PK1) to 1.982 fm (DD-LZ1).
- For $^6$He, the experimental matter radius is 2.30(7) fm; calculated values with full exchange terms range from 2.129 fm (NL5(A)) to 2.295 fm (DD-LZ1).
- For $^{12}$C, the experimental charge radius is 2.4702(22) fm; calculated values with full exchange terms range from 2.376 fm (DD-LZ1) to 2.491 fm (DD-ME2).

## Context

This work systematically evaluates the accuracy of covariant density functional theories (CDFTs) for light nuclei with Z<8, extending previous studies that had suggested CDFTs with corrections could describe bulk properties of light nuclei such as in Meng (1996) for lithium isotopes and Arumugam (2005) for beryllium and boron nuclei. The study enables a systematic comparison between mean-field and ab initio methods for light nuclei, and could help build new-generation density functionals with realistic nuclear forces such as relativistic chiral nucleon-nucleon interactions as proposed by Lu (2022).
