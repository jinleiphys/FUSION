# Possible identifications of newly observed magnetar quasi-periodic oscillations as crustal shear modes

> Quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) discovered in soft-gamma repeaters (SGRs) are expected to help us to study the properties of matter in neutron stars. In earlier investigations, we identified the QPOs of frequencies below $\sim100$ Hz observed in giant flares of SGR 1806$-$20 and SGR 1900+14 as the crustal torsional oscillations. For this purpose, we calculated the frequencies of the fundamental torsional oscillations with various angular indices $\ell$, by changing the stellar mass and radius. In this work, we try to explain the additional QPO frequencies recently reported by Huppenkothen et al. (2014a,b) within the same framework as before except that we newly take into account the effect of electron screening, which acts to decrease the frequencies by a small amount. Those QPOs were discovered in two different SGRs, i.e., SGR 1806$-$20 and SGR J1550$-$5418. Then, we find that the newly observed QPO frequency in SGR 1806$-$20 can be still identified as one of the frequencies of the fundamental torsional oscillations, while those in SGR J1550$-$5418 can also be explained in terms of the torsional oscillations although the relevant angular indices are difficult to identify.

## Key claim

The newly observed QPO frequencies in SGR 1806$-$20 (57 Hz) and SGR J1550$-$5418 (93 and 127 Hz) can be identified as crustal torsional shear oscillations, but the angular indices for SGR J1550$-$5418 remain ambiguous because multiple combinations of angular indices can reproduce the two observed frequencies depending on the unknown value of the symmetry energy parameter $L$.

## Method

The authors calculated the fundamental frequencies of crustal torsional oscillations (t-modes) for neutron star models with masses 1.4-1.8 solar masses and radii 10-14 km, using the relativistic Cowling approximation. They adopted the OI-EOS (Oyamatsu and Iida, 2007) for the crust and incorporated a modified shear modulus that includes the effect of electron screening (Kobyakov and Pethick, 2013), which reduces the shear modulus by a factor of $(1-0.010Z^{2/3})$. The superfluid density of dripped neutrons was taken from Chamel (2012), and the frequencies were computed as functions of the symmetry energy parameter $L$ for various angular indices $\ell$.

## Key numbers

- The newly observed QPO frequency of 57 Hz in SGR 1806$-$20 can be identified as the $\ell=9$ fundamental torsional oscillation, with a calculated frequency of 55.22 Hz (relative error 3.13%) for a stellar model with $M=1.4M_\odot$, $R=12$ km, and optimal $L=123$ MeV.
- The previously known QPO frequencies in SGR 1806$-$20 (18, 26, 30, and 92.5 Hz) are identified as $\ell=3, 4, 5$, and 15 respectively, with calculated frequencies of 18.62, 24.98, 31.15, and 90.76 Hz for the same stellar model.
- For SGR 1900$+$14, the QPO frequencies 28, 54, and 84 Hz are identified as $\ell=4, 8$, and 13, with calculated frequencies of 27.29, 53.80, and 86.26 Hz for $M=1.4M_\odot$, $R=12$ km, and optimal $L=110$ MeV.
- The combined constraint on the symmetry energy parameter from both SGR 1806$-$20 and SGR 1900$+$14 gives $97 \le L \le 127$ MeV, which is about 4 MeV lower than the constraint obtained without electron screening in Sotani et al. (2013b).
- For SGR J1550$-$5418, eight possible combinations of angular indices can reproduce the observed 93 and 127 Hz QPOs, with optimal $L$ values ranging from 81 to 161 MeV for $M=1.4M_\odot$, $R=12$ km.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous identification of low-frequency QPOs (below ~100 Hz) from giant flares of SGR 1806$-$20 and SGR 1900$+$14 as crustal torsional oscillations (Sotani et al., 2012, 2013a, 2013b) by incorporating newly reported QPOs from Huppenkothen et al. (2014a,b). The inclusion of electron screening (Kobyakov and Pethick, 2013) shifts the constraint on $L$ downward by about 4 MeV compared to Sotani et al. (2013b). The results enable future multi-messenger constraints on the nuclear symmetry energy by combining QPO identifications with mass-radius measurements from X-ray observations, and additional QPO detections in SGR J1550$-$5418 could further narrow the allowed range of $L$.
