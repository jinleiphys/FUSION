# Shape coexistence in even-even nuclei: A theoretical overview

> The last decade has seen a rapid growth of our understanding of the microscopic origins of shape coexistence, assisted by the new data provided by the modern radioactive ion beam facilities built worldwide. Islands of the nuclear chart in which shape coexistence can occur have been identified, and the different microscopic particle-hole excitation mechanisms leading to neutron-induced or proton-induced shape coexistence have been clarified. The relation of shape coexistence to the islands of inversion, appearing in light nuclei, to the new spin-aligned phase appearing in N=Z nuclei, as well as to shape/phase transitions occurring in medium mass and heavy nuclei, has been understood. In the present review, these developments are considered within the shell model and mean field approaches, as well as by symmetry methods. In addition, based on systematics of data, as well as on symmetry considerations, quantitative rules are developed, predicting regions in which shape coexistence can appear, as a possible guide for further experimental efforts, which can help in improving our understanding of the details of the nucleon-nucleon interaction, as well as of its modifications occurring far from stability.

## Key claim

This review argues that shape coexistence in even-even nuclei can be understood through microscopic particle-hole excitation mechanisms, and that quantitative rules based on systematics and symmetry considerations can predict regions where shape coexistence may appear, serving as a guide for experimental efforts, though the authors note these predictions depend on the specific theoretical framework (shell model, mean field, or symmetry methods) and are limited to even-even nuclei for the sake of size.

## Method

The authors review theoretical developments in shape coexistence using three main approaches: the nuclear shell model (including Monte Carlo shell model and no-core shell model), self-consistent mean field methods (Gogny, Skyrme, and relativistic mean field interactions with beyond-mean-field correlations), and symmetry methods (Elliott SU(3), pseudo-SU(3), quasi-SU(3), and proxy-SU(3)). They survey isotope series from Rn (Z=86) to Be (Z=4), focusing on spectra, B(E2) transition rates, and potential energy surfaces, and develop quantitative rules predicting regions of shape coexistence based on data systematics and symmetry considerations.

## Key numbers

* The P-factor P = N_p N_n / (N_p + N_n) is used as a measure of collectivity: deformed nuclei possess P > 4-5, while nuclei with P < 4 cannot be deformed; for a nucleus with N_p=4, N_n=6, P=2.4, but with 2p-2h proton excitations effectively giving N_p=8, N_n=6, P=3.43.
* The ratio R_{4/2} = E(4_1^+)/E(2_1^+) obtains values 2.0-2.4 for vibrational (near-spherical) nuclei, 2.4-3.0 for intermediate gamma-unstable nuclei, and 3.0-3.33 for rotational (deformed) nuclei.
* The 3D-HO closed shells occur at 2, 8, 20, 40, 70, 112, 168 protons or neutrons, while spherical shell model magic numbers are 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126.
* The tensor force between 1g_{9/2 >} and 1f_{7/2 >} is repulsive, pushing 1f_{7/2 >} up, while between 1g_{9/2 >} and 1f_{5/2 <} it is attractive, pushing 1f_{5/2 <} down, reducing the Z=28 gap and facilitating shape coexistence.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical understanding of shape coexistence since the authoritative review by Heyde and Wood (2011), incorporating advances from radioactive ion beam facilities and massive theoretical calculations over entire isotope series. It builds on the shell model framework of Caurier et al. (2005) and Poves (2016), the mean field approaches reviewed by Bender (2003), and the symmetry methods of Elliott (1958), pseudo-SU(3) by Arima (1969) and Hecht (1969), quasi-SU(3) by Zuker (1995), and proxy-SU(3) by Bonatsos (2017). The work enables future experimental guidance for exploring shape coexistence regions and calls for a similar review on odd-A nuclei.
