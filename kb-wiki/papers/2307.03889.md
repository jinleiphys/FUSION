# Quantum techniques for eigenvalue problems

> This article is a brief introduction to quantum algorithms for the eigenvalue problem in quantum many-body systems. Rather than a broad survey of topics, we focus on providing a conceptual understanding of several quantum algorithms that cover the essentials of adiabatic evolution, variational methods, phase detection algorithms, and several other approaches. For each method, we discuss the potential advantages and remaining challenges.

## Key claim

This paper is a pedagogical introduction to quantum algorithms for eigenvalue problems in many-body systems, not a comprehensive review; it focuses on conveying conceptual understanding of adiabatic evolution, variational methods, and phase detection algorithms while noting that current quantum hardware has significant limitations due to gate errors, qubit decoherence, and small qubit counts.

## Method

The author presents the adiabatic theorem and its application to eigenstate preparation, deriving rigorous error bounds from Jansen et al. (2007) and discussing Trotter-Suzuki decompositions for implementing time evolution. Variational quantum algorithms are described as hybrid quantum-classical approaches where a parameterized quantum circuit prepares trial wave functions and classical optimization minimizes the energy expectation value, with gradient computation via the parameter-shift rule. Phase detection algorithms including quantum phase estimation, iterative phase estimation, and the rodeo algorithm are explained, showing how ancilla qubits and the quantum Fourier transform extract eigenvalues from unitary time evolution operators.

## Key numbers

- The adiabatic error bound requires total time T ≥ (1/δ) times an integral involving the spectral gap Δ(s), where δ is the error tolerance for the overlap |⟨ψ(s)|ψ_U(s)⟩| ≥ 1-δ
- For the Trotter-Suzuki expansion, first-order approximation gives error O[(Δt)²] and second-order gives O[(Δt)³]
- In quantum phase estimation with n ancilla qubits, the error in determining eigenvalues scales inversely with 2^n, and the error in eigenstate preparation is O(2^{-n}) when eigenvalue spacings are much larger than 1/2^n
- The continuous MaxCut problem is shown to be NP-hard and equivalent to a variational quantum optimization for the Ising model Hamiltonian with d vertices

## Context

This tutorial extends the pedagogical approach of the comprehensive review by Ayral (2023) on quantum computing and quantum many-body systems, but deliberately narrows focus to eigenvalue problems. The paper enables researchers new to quantum computing to understand the conceptual foundations of adiabatic state preparation (building on work by Born (1928), Kato (1950), and Farhi (2000)), variational methods (extending Peruzzo (2014) and Cerezo (2021)), and phase estimation (following Kitaev (1995) and Abrams (1997)), while highlighting open challenges such as barren plateaus and local minima in variational optimization.
