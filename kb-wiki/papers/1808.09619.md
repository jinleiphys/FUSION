# Antinuclei in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> We review progress in the study of antinuclei, starting from Dirac's equation and the discovery of the positron in cosmic-ray events. The development of proton accelerators led to the discovery of antiprotons, followed by the first antideuterons, demonstrating that antinucleons bind into antinuclei. With the development of heavy-ion programs at the Brookhaven AGS and CERN SPS, it was demonstrated that central collisions of heavy nuclei offer a fertile ground for research and discoveries in the area of antinuclei. In this review, we emphasize recent observations at Brookhaven's Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and at CERN's Large Hadron Collider, namely, the antihypertriton and the antihelium-4, as well as measurements of the mass difference between light nuclei and antinuclei, and the interaction between antiprotons. Physics implications of the new observations and different production mechanisms are discussed. We also consider implications for related fields, such as hypernuclear physics and space-based cosmic-ray experiments.

## Key claim

This review paper synthesizes progress in antinuclei research from Dirac's equation through 2018, emphasizing that heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC have enabled observations of the antihypertriton and antihelium-4, as well as measurements of the mass difference between light nuclei and antinuclei and the interaction between antiprotons, while noting that future discoveries of heavier antinuclei beyond antihelium-4 are unlikely for the foreseeable future.

## Method

The authors review experimental results from the STAR detector at RHIC and the ALICE detector at LHC, focusing on observations of antinuclei produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. They discuss production mechanisms including statistical coalescence models (first proposed by Butler and Pearson) and thermal models, as well as CPT symmetry tests comparing properties of nuclei and antinuclei. The review also covers measurements of antiproton-antiproton interactions and their implications for the antinuclear force.

## Key numbers

- The mass difference between $K^0$ and $\overline{K}^0$ is known to be less than $4 \times 10^{-19}$ GeV/$c^2$ at 90% confidence level
- The threshold kinetic energy for antinucleon production in fixed-target configuration is 5.6 GeV; for two antinucleons, 15 GeV; for three antinucleons, 28 GeV
- The ALICE collaboration expects samples of about 5500 $^4\overline{\rm He}$ and $^4$He in LHC run 3 beginning in 2021
- At 2.76 TeV, the $^4\overline{\rm He}$ to $^4$He ratio is consistent with unity
- The deuteron to antideuteron ratio at the ISR was measured as $3.7 \pm 1.2$
- The probability of antinucleus formation drops about 4 orders of magnitude from antiprotons to antideuterons, and another 4 orders from antideuterons to antihelium-3

## Context

This review extends the historical line of antinuclei research from early discoveries at the Bevatron (Chamberlain et al., 1955), AGS (Dorfan et al., 1965), and CERN PS (Massam et al., 1965) through to modern heavy-ion experiments at RHIC and LHC. It builds on earlier reviews such as Ma (2013) and enables future searches for heavier antinuclei including $^5\overline{\rm Li}$ and $^4_{\bar{\Lambda}}\overline{H}$, as well as improved CPT symmetry tests and measurements of the antinuclear force. The paper also connects to cosmic-ray experiments searching for antihelium in space and to hypernuclear physics.
