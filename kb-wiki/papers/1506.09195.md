# Nuclear Energy Density Functionals: What do we really know?

> We present the simplest nuclear energy density functional (NEDF) to date, determined by only 4 significant phenomenological parameters, yet capable of fitting measured nuclear masses with better accuracy than the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula, while also describing density structures (charge radii, neutron skins etc.) and time-dependent phenomena (induced fission, giant resonances, low energy nuclear collisions, etc.). The 4 significant parameters are necessary to describe bulk nuclear properties (binding energies and charge radii); an additional 2 to 3 parameters have little influence on the bulk nuclear properties, but allow independent control of the density dependence of the symmetry energy and isovector excitations, in particular the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule. This Hohenberg-Kohn-style of density functional theory successfully realizes Weizsäcker's ideas and provides a computationally tractable model for a variety of static nuclear properties and dynamics, from finite nuclei to neutron stars, where it will also provide a new insight into the physics of the r-process, nucleosynthesis, and neutron star crust structure. This new NEDF clearly separates the bulk geometric properties - volume, surface, symmetry, and Coulomb energies which amount to 8MeV per nucleon or up to 2000MeV per nucleus for heavy nuclei - from finer details related to shell effects, pairing, isospin breaking, etc. which contribute at most a few MeV for the entire nucleus. Thus it provides a systematic framework for organizing various contributions to the NEDF. Measured and calculated physical observables - symmetry and saturation properties, the neutron matter equation of state, and the frequency of giant dipole resonances - lead directly to new terms not considered in current NEDF parameterizations.

## Key claim

The authors present what they describe as the simplest nuclear energy density functional (NEDF) to date, determined by only 4 significant phenomenological parameters, which can fit measured nuclear masses with better accuracy than the Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula while also describing density structures and time-dependent phenomena. However, the claim is qualified by noting that an additional 2 to 3 parameters have little influence on bulk nuclear properties but are needed for independent control of the density dependence of the symmetry energy and isovector excitations.

## Method

The authors constructed a Hohenberg-Kohn-style density functional theory for nuclei, separating bulk geometric properties (volume, surface, symmetry, and Coulomb energies) from finer details such as shell effects, pairing, and isospin breaking. The functional was parameterized with 4 significant parameters to describe bulk nuclear properties (binding energies and charge radii), with 2 to 3 additional parameters controlling the density dependence of the symmetry energy and isovector excitations, particularly the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule. The model was tested against measured nuclear masses, charge radii, neutron skins, and time-dependent phenomena including induced fission, giant resonances, and low energy nuclear collisions.

## Key numbers

- The bulk geometric properties (volume, surface, symmetry, and Coulomb energies) amount to 8 MeV per nucleon or up to 2000 MeV per nucleus for heavy nuclei
- Finer details related to shell effects, pairing, isospin breaking, etc. contribute at most a few MeV for the entire nucleus
- The functional is determined by only 4 significant phenomenological parameters for bulk nuclear properties, with an additional 2 to 3 parameters for isovector properties

## Context

This work extends the ideas of Weizsäcker by realizing them within a Hohenberg-Kohn-style density functional theory framework, providing a computationally tractable model that bridges finite nuclei and neutron star physics. The new NEDF leads to terms not considered in current NEDF parameterizations, based on measured and calculated physical observables including symmetry and saturation properties, the neutron matter equation of state, and giant dipole resonance frequencies. The framework enables new insights into the physics of the r-process, nucleosynthesis, and neutron star crust structure.
