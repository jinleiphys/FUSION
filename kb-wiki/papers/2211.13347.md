# Entropy Suppression through Quantum Interference in Electric Pulses

> The Schwinger process in strong electric fields creates particles and antiparticles that are entangled. The entropy of entanglement between particles and antiparticles has been found to be equal to the statistical Gibbs entropy of the produced system. Here we study the effect of quantum interference in sequences of electric pulses, and show that quantum interference suppresses the entanglement entropy of the created quantum state. This is potentially relevant to quantum-enhanced classical communications. Our results can be extended to a wide variety of two-level quantum systems.

## Key claim

Quantum interference in sequences of electric pulses suppresses the entanglement entropy of particles created via the Schwinger process, relative to the entropy produced by non-interfering pulses. The suppression is a leading-order effect that is independent of whether the created particles are bosons or fermions.

## Method

The authors solve the Klein-Gordon and Dirac equations for bosonic and fermionic fields in a time-dependent electric field using Bogoliubov transformations, treating the time evolution as a two-level system. They compare symmetric pulse sequences (all pulses with the same electric field sign) to antisymmetric sequences (alternating signs) for well-separated Sauter pulses in the semiclassical limit (E ≪ m², Eτ ≫ m), using both exact numerical solutions and semiclassical approximations based on complex turning points.

## Context

This work extends the finding of Florio (2021) that the entanglement entropy between particles and antiparticles in Schwinger pair creation equals the statistical Gibbs entropy of the produced system. It also builds on the semiclassical analysis of multiple-pulse interference by Akkermans (2011) and Dumlu (2011). The results are potentially relevant to quantum-enhanced classical communications, as the mutual information of the antisymmetric configuration exceeds that of the symmetric configuration, suggesting possible improvements in information transmission efficiency.
