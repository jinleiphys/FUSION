# Out of Equilibrium Temperature from Holography

> We define an effective temperature and study its properties for a class of out-of-equilibrium steady states in a heat bath. Our analysis is based on the anti-de Sitter spacetime/conformal field theory (AdS/CFT) correspondence, and examples include systems driven by applied electric fields and branes dragged in plasmas. We found that the effective temperature can be lower than that of the heat bath and that the out-of-equilibrium noise can be smaller than that in equilibrium. We show that a generalization of the fluctuation-dissipation relation holds for the effective temperature. In particular, we generalize the Johnson-Nyquist relation for large electric field.

## Key claim

The paper defines an effective temperature \(T_*\) for a class of out-of-equilibrium steady states in a heat bath using holography, and finds that this effective temperature can be lower than the heat bath temperature \(T\) and that the out-of-equilibrium noise can be smaller than in equilibrium, contrary to intuition. The key qualifier is that these results apply only to probe branes whose back-reaction on the bulk geometry is ignored, and the system is open and driven by an external energy source, so the second law of thermodynamics does not necessarily apply.

## Method

The authors use the AdS/CFT correspondence to model a heat bath as a bulk geometry with an event horizon at Hawking temperature \(T\). They introduce a probe \((q+1+n)\)-brane in this geometry, described by the Dirac-Born-Infeld (DBI) action, and drive it out of equilibrium either by applying a constant electric field \(E\) along the boundary or by dragging it with constant velocity \(v\). They compute the effective metric experienced by fluctuations of the brane's embedding coordinates and gauge fields, find that this effective metric has an event horizon at a location \(r_*\) different from the bulk horizon \(r_0\), and identify the Hawking temperature \(T_*\) of this effective horizon as the effective temperature of the nonequilibrium steady state.

## Key numbers

* For a D\(p\) brane heat bath with a probe D\((q+1+n)\) brane under an applied electric field \(E\), the effective temperature is \(T_* = c_0^{-1} \left[ (c_0 T)^{\frac{14-2p}{5-p}} + C E^2 \right]^{1/2} / \left[ (c_0 T)^{\frac{14-2p}{5-p}} + E^2 \right]^{1/(7-p)}\), where \(c_0 = 4\pi/(7-p)\) and \(C = \frac{1}{2}(q+3-p + \frac{p-3}{7-p}n)\).
* For a D4 brane heat bath (\(p=4\)) with a D2 probe brane (\(q=1, n=0\)) under an electric field, \(T_* = c_0^2 T^3 / [(c_0 T)^6 + E^2]^{1/3}\), which is a decreasing function of \(E^2\).
* For a dragged probe brane with velocity \(v\) in a D\(p\) brane heat bath, the effective temperature is \(T_* = (1-v^2)^{1/(7-p)} (1 + C v^2)^{1/2} T\).
* For a fundamental string (\(q=0, n=0\)) dragged in a D3 brane heat bath (\(p=3\)), \(T_* = (1-v^2)^{1/4} T\), reproducing the result of Gubser (2006) and Casalderrey-Solana (2007).
* In the zero-temperature limit (\(T \rightarrow 0\)) of the heat bath with an applied electric field, the effective temperature remains finite: \(T_* \rightarrow c_0^{-1} C^{1/2} E^{(5-p)/(7-p)}\), assuming \(C > 0\).

## Context

This work extends and unifies previous holographic studies of effective temperatures on out-of-equilibrium branes, such as the dragged fundamental string in a D3 brane heat bath (Gubser 2006, Casalderrey-Solana 2007) and branes with applied electric fields (Kim 2011, Sonner 2012). It generalizes these results to a larger class of brane configurations and shows that the effective temperature is the same for all degrees of freedom on the brane, addressing concerns raised by Giecold (2009) about anisotropic momentum fluctuations. The paper also proposes a generalization of the Johnson-Nyquist relation for large electric fields and demonstrates the possibility of negative excess noise, a phenomenon previously discussed in the nonequilibrium statistical physics literature. This work enables further study of fluctuation-dissipation relations and effective temperatures in far-from-equilibrium systems using holographic methods.
