# Progress and Challenges in Small Systems

> We present a comprehensive review of the theoretical and experimental progress in the investigation of novel high-temperature quantum chromodynamics phenomena in small systems at both the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the Large Hadron Collider. We highlight the challenges and opportunities associated with studying small systems, by which we generally mean collision systems that involve at least one light ion or even a photon projectile. We discuss perspectives on possible future research directions to better understand the underlying physics at work in the collisions of small systems.

## Key claim

This review argues that while small collision systems (proton-proton, proton-nucleus, and photon-induced collisions) exhibit collective behavior compatible with hydrodynamics, the applicability of standard relativistic viscous fluid dynamics is fundamentally challenged in these systems because Knudsen numbers and inverse Reynolds numbers become large, meaning the system is driven significantly far from equilibrium and the usual perturbative expansion around local thermal equilibrium is much less justifiable than in large heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors survey the multi-stage theoretical framework used to model small systems, which consists of initial state models (Monte Carlo Glauber, IP-Glasma, and Trento with subnucleon fluctuations), pre-equilibrium dynamics (free streaming or the KøMPøST effective kinetic theory), and relativistic viscous hydrodynamics in the Israel-Stewart formulation. They compare these model predictions to experimental flow measurements from RHIC and the LHC, and discuss the specific challenges that arise when applying this framework to systems with reduced size, shorter lifetimes, and larger spatial gradients.

## Key numbers

- The width of subnucleonic hot spots was constrained to approximately 0.4 fm, similar to the typical nucleon width used in the IP-Glasma model.
- The initial transverse volume in small systems is about 50 times smaller than in central Pb+Pb collisions.
- The temperature range expected in small systems is T ~ 150–450 MeV.
- The Knudsen number estimate uses a microscopic scale of ℓ ~ 1/T, where T is the temperature.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework developed for heavy-ion collisions (Gale 2013, Romatschke 2017, Shen 2020) to smaller systems, building on prior work that demonstrated collective flow in proton-nucleus collisions (Bozek 2011, Schenke 2014). It addresses the tension between the success of hydrodynamics in describing flow observables in small systems and the fundamental theoretical concerns about its applicability raised by large Knudsen numbers (Niemi 2014, Noronha-Hostler 2015). The review enables future work on pre-equilibrium dynamics, hydrodynamic attractors (Heller 2015, Strickland 2017), and the development of causal formulations of relativistic viscous hydrodynamics such as the BDNK framework (Bemfica, Disconzi, Noronha, and Kovtun 2019).
