# Gauge/String Duality, Hot QCD and Heavy Ion Collisions

> Over the last decade, both experimental and theoretical advances have brought the need for strong coupling techniques in the analysis of deconfined QCD matter and heavy ion collisions to the forefront. As a consequence, a fruitful interplay has developed between analyses of strongly-coupled non-abelian plasmas via the gauge/string duality (also referred to as the AdS/CFT correspondence) and the phenomenology of heavy ion collisions. We review some of the main insights gained from this interplay to date. To establish a common language, we start with an introduction to heavy ion phenomenology and finite-temperature QCD, and a corresponding introduction to important concepts and techniques in the gauge/string duality. These introductory sections are written for nonspecialists, with the goal of bringing readers ranging from beginning graduate students to experienced practitioners of either QCD or gauge/string duality to the point that they understand enough about both fields that they can then appreciate their interplay in all appropriate contexts. We then review the current state-of-the art in the application of the duality to the description of the dynamics of strongly coupled plasmas, with emphases that include: its thermodynamic, hydrodynamic and transport properties; the way it both modifies the dynamics of, and is perturbed by, high-energy or heavy quarks passing through it; and the physics of quarkonium mesons within it. We seek throughout to stress the lessons that can be extracted from these computations for heavy ion physics as well as to discuss future directions and open problems for the field.

## Key claim

The gauge/string duality (AdS/CFT correspondence) provides a framework for computing properties of strongly coupled non-abelian plasmas that are relevant to heavy ion collisions, but the results are derived in the limit of large 't Hooft coupling and large number of colors, and their applicability to QCD requires qualitative rather than quantitative comparison.

## Method

The paper reviews how transport coefficients such as shear viscosity can be extracted from retarded correlators of the stress tensor via the Green-Kubo formula, which relates the low-frequency limit of the imaginary part of the retarded correlator to the transport coefficient. For holographic calculations, the Hawking temperature of a black brane metric is derived by requiring regularity of the Euclidean continuation at the horizon. Euclidean two-point functions of scalar operators in N=4 SYM are computed by solving the classical equation of motion for the dual scalar field in AdS, imposing boundary conditions and regularity in the interior, and performing holographic renormalization to remove divergences.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity is given by the Green-Kubo formula: η = -lim_{ω→0} (1/ω) lim_{k→0} Im G_R^{xy,xy}(ω,k)
- The Hawking temperature for a general black brane metric of the form ds² = g(r)[-f(r)dt² + dx²] + (1/h(r))dr² is T = √(g(r₀)f'(r₀)h'(r₀))/(4π)
- For the lightest meson in the D3/D7 system at zero temperature, the mass squared is M_mes² = 4π² mbar² ≈ 39.5 mbar²
- The dissociation temperature for moving quarkonium mesons scales as T_d(v) ≈ T_d(v=0)(1-v²)^{1/4}
- For a D7-brane embedding with m=1.3 (temperature just below T_diss), the limiting velocity v_lim ≈ 0.35
- The limiting velocity at the dissociation transition (v=0) is v_lim = 0.27
- The function f(v) in T_d(v) = f(v)(1-v²)^{1/4}T_d(0) varies between 1.021 at its maximum and 0.924 at v=1

## Context

This review extends the application of gauge/string duality to strongly coupled plasmas, building on earlier work by Kruczenski (2003) on meson spectra at zero temperature and by Mateos (2007) on meson dispersion relations at finite temperature. The results enable phenomenological studies of quarkonium suppression in heavy ion collisions, particularly the velocity-dependent screening effects that modify the dissociation temperature for mesons moving through the plasma. The paper also discusses how finite-N_c and finite-coupling corrections generate nonzero meson decay widths, contrasting with the zero-width results in the large-N_c, strong-coupling limit.
