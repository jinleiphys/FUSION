# Direct real photons in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> Direct real photons are arguably the most versatile tools to study relativistic heavy ion collisions. They are produced, by various mechanisms, during the entire space-time history of the strongly interacting system. Also, being colorless, most the time they escape without further interaction, ie they are penetrating probes. This makes them rich in information, but hard to decypher and interpret. This review presents the experimental and theoretical developments related to direct real photons since the 1970s, with a special emphasis on the recently emerged "direct photon puzzle", the simultaneous presence of large yields and strong azimuthal asymmetries of photons in heavy ion collisions, an observation that so far eluded full and coherent explanation.

## Key claim

This review argues that direct real photons are the most versatile penetrating probes of relativistic heavy ion collisions, but their interpretation is fundamentally limited because all production mechanisms (from pre-equilibrium through QGP to hadron gas) are convolved in space-time, making it nearly impossible to disentangle contributions without relying on models. The paper emphasizes that the "direct photon puzzle", the simultaneous observation of large photon yields and strong azimuthal asymmetries, remains without a full and coherent explanation as of early 2019.

## Method

The paper provides a comprehensive review of experimental and theoretical developments in direct real photon production from the 1970s through early 2019, covering both high transverse momentum (pQCD hard scattering) and low transverse momentum ("thermal") regimes. It systematically presents the fundamental partonic processes (quark-gluon Compton scattering and quark-antiquark annihilation), the formalism for photon radiation from a thermalized QGP (including the Kapusta et al. rate formula and AMY rates), and radiation from hadron gas (including processes involving pions, rho mesons, and the a1 resonance). The review also discusses experimental techniques, the evolution of hydrodynamic and microscopic transport models, and the specific challenge of separating direct photons from the large background of decay photons from pi0 and eta mesons.

## Key numbers

- The ratio N_gamma/N_pi in the range 0.1–0.15 was estimated by Feinberg (1976) for realistic pion multiplicities, "remarkably close to today's measurements"
- The direct photon yield from QGP was estimated to be a factor of 20*alpha_s*ln(p_T/m_0)/(3*alpha) ~ 600 higher than dilepton yields (Shuryak, 1978)
- The Kapusta et al. (1991) rate formula for thermal QGP photon emission at E >> T: E*dR/d^3p = (5/9)*(alpha*alpha_s/(2*pi^2))*T^2*e^(-E/T)*ln(2.912*E/(g^2*T))
- The hadron gas and QGP were found to radiate photons at the same rate in the 1–3 GeV/c p_T range at the same temperature (Kapusta, 1991)
- Above E_gamma > 0.7 GeV, the dominant hadronic process is pi+rho -> pi+gamma (Kapusta, 1991)
- A radial expansion velocity v_0 = 0.3 described the low end of the WA98 data (Gallmeister, 2000)
- The a1 resonance was predicted to "outshine" the dominant hadronic process (Xiong, 1992)

## Context

This review extends and updates earlier reviews of direct photon physics by Ferbel (1984), Alam (1996), Cassing (1999), Peitzmann (2001), Stankus (2005), David (2006), and Linnyk (2015). It places the field in historical context starting from the early ISR and Fermilab era, through the SPS and RHIC programs, to the LHC. The paper specifically addresses the "direct photon puzzle" that emerged after the 2011 observation of elliptic flow of thermal photons, which contradicts the expectation that large yields and strong azimuthal asymmetries should be mutually exclusive. The review enables future work by identifying missing measurements and insufficiently precise data that prevent discrimination between competing theoretical models, and by emphasizing that a fully self-consistent description of direct photons in heavy ion collisions is not yet available.
