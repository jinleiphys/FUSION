# Asymmetric neutrino propagation in newly born magnetized strange stars: GRB and kicks

> We consider the early cooling evolution of strongly magnetized strange stars in a CFL phase with high gap Delta > 100 MeV. We demonstrate how this model may explain main features of the gamma-ray burst phenomena and also yield a strong star kick. The mechanism is based on beaming of neutrino emission along the magnetic vortex lines. We show that for sufficiently high initial temperatures T_0 ~ 30 - 60 MeV and surface magnetic fields B_s ~ 10^{15} - 10^{17} G, the energy release within the narrow beam is up to 10^{52} erg with a magnetic field dependent time scale between 10^{-2} s (for a smaller magnetic field) to 10 s. The above mechanism together with the parity violation of the neutrino-producing weak interaction processes in a magnetic field allow for the strange star kick. The higher the magnetic field the larger is the stars kick velocity. These velocities may cover the same range as observed pulsar kick velocities.

# Asymmetric neutrino propagation in newly born magnetized strange stars: GRB and kicks

## Key claim
The authors propose that strongly magnetized strange stars in a color-flavor-locked (CFL) superconducting phase with large pairing gaps (Δ > 100 MeV) can produce both gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and pulsar-like kicks via beamed neutrino emission along magnetic vortex lines, but only if the initial temperature is sufficiently high (T₀ ~ 30–60 MeV) and the surface magnetic field is extremely strong (Bₛ ~ 10¹⁵–10¹⁷ G).

## Method
The authors model the early cooling evolution of a strange star in the CFL phase, assuming a type-II superconductor with Abrikosov vortex structures. They compute the time-dependent neutrino luminosity, beaming angle, and temperature evolution using a transport equation, accounting for the different emissivities and mean free paths in the vortex regions (2SC+s + normal phase) versus the CFL phase. The resulting anisotropic neutrino emission is then used to estimate the energy release within the beam cone and the kick velocity from parity-violating weak interactions in the magnetic field.

## Context
This work extends the model of strange stars in the CFL phase (Rajagopal 2000) and builds on prior studies of vortex structures in color superconductors (Blaschke 1999, Sedrakian 2000, Giannakis 2003). It addresses the GRB phenomenon by proposing a beaming mechanism that requires lower energy (~10⁵¹ erg) than isotropic emission models (~10⁵³–10⁵⁴ erg), consistent with evidence for jet-like geometry (Frayl). For pulsar kicks, the mechanism complements earlier proposals such as asymmetric supernova explosions (Lai 2001), neutron star instabilities (Colpi 2003, Imshennik 2004), and magneto-rotational effects (Moiseenko 2003, Ardeljan 2004), and can potentially explain the highest observed pulsar velocities around 1600 km/s that many models struggle to reach. The authors note that all estimates are rough and depend strongly on model parameters, calling for more systematic investigation with consistent compact star structure modeling.
