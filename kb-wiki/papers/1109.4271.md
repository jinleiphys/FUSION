# Heavy quarkonia in a medium as a quantum dissipative system: Master-equation approach

> The problem of the evolution of a heavy quarkonium in a medium can be recast as that of a quantum dissipative system. Within the framework of the master-equation approach to open quantum systems, we consider the real-time dynamics of quarkonia. We find that in a plasma at fixed temperature, the populations of the various quarkonium states evolve together, while their momentum distribution satisfies a Fokker-Planck equation.

## Key claim

The paper shows that heavy quarkonia in a fixed-temperature medium evolve collectively rather than independently: after a transient regime, the populations of all bound states decay with the same time scale, contradicting the sequential-melting picture where states either survive or are destroyed independently based on temperature alone.

## Method

The authors model a heavy quarkonium as a small quantum system coupled to a reservoir of harmonic oscillators representing a gluon plasma, using the master-equation formalism for open quantum systems. They adopt a simplified model for bottomonia as bound states of a Coulomb potential with dipolar coupling to the chromoelectric field of the gluons, compute transition rates via Fermi's golden rule, and solve the resulting coupled differential equations for state populations and momentum distributions.

## Key numbers

- In a thermal bath at T = 5T_c (with T_c = 170 MeV), the characteristic decay time scale for all bottomonium bound states is 1.5 fm/c after the initial transient regime
- In a thermal bath at T = 2T_c, the characteristic time scale for bottomonium evolution is 8 fm/c
- The drift and diffusion coefficients in the Fokker-Planck equation satisfy the fluctuation-dissipation relation κ/M_S = η_D k_B T, confirming thermalization of external degrees of freedom

## Context

This work extends the open-quantum-system approach to quarkonia previously introduced by Borghini (2011) and Young (2010), providing a microscopic foundation for kinetic models that previously used Boltzmann equations (Levin and Plotnik, 1995; Polleri, 2003; Yan, 2006), Fokker-Planck or Langevin equations (Patra, 2001; Young, 2008), or rate equations (Grandchamp, 2003; Zhao, 2010). The results enable future studies of quarkonium dynamics in expanding and cooling media, and the computed transport coefficients are similar to those from hard-thermal-loop approaches (Beraudo, 2009) and the Upsilon(1S) lifetime from more elaborate models (Grandchamp, 2005).
