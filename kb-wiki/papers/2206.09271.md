# Nuclei near and at the proton dripline

> Nuclei in the vicinity of the proton dripline and beyond it are a fascinating realm within the chart of nuclei. In this chapter the main phenomena that characterize this domain and are not to be found elsewhere are explored. While moving away from the $β$-stability valley towards the proton dripline, phenomena like very exotic decay modes such as $β$-delayed (multi-) particle emission, and proton-, or two-proton radioactivity are encountered. Landmark nuclei are here two isotopes with magic proton and neutron numbers, $^{48}$Ni and $^{100}$Sn. Moreover, proton-rich nuclei ($N < Z$) display other interesting features, like breaking of isospin symmetry with consequent asymmetry in the energy spectra between mirror nuclei, the so-called Thomas-Ehrmann shift, or the phenomenon of proton-halo. Last but not least, nuclei close to the proton dripline play a very important role in nucleosynthesis, since they take part in the rapid-proton capture process and their properties are crucial in defining the flow followed and its termination close to $^{100}$Sn.

## Key claim

This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of the phenomena characterizing nuclei at and beyond the proton dripline, but it is presented as a review of established knowledge and open questions rather than advancing a single new claim; the abstract's description of "exploring" phenomena is accurate, with the caveat that many of the most exotic systems (e.g., $^{48}$Ni, $^{100}$Sn) remain extremely difficult to study experimentally and their properties are still under active investigation.

## Method

The authors survey the landscape of proton-rich nuclei from light elements up to uranium, defining the proton dripline via one-proton and two-proton separation energies. They describe the three main production methods (fusion-evaporation, projectile-fragmentation, and spallation reactions) and the two complementary separation techniques (in-flight separation and ISOL). The chapter then reviews the exotic decay modes characteristic of this region: beta-delayed charged-particle emission, one-proton radioactivity, and two-proton radioactivity, illustrating each with selected experimental examples from the literature.

## Key numbers

* The most neutron-deficient isotope of thallium known, $^{176}$Tl, has seven neutrons less than the last bound isotope $^{183}$Tl.
* The most neutron-deficient isotopes of gold ($^{170}$Au), iridium ($^{164}$Ir), rhenium ($^{159}$Re), and thulium ($^{144}$Tm) are all located six neutrons beyond the proton dripline.
* Production cross-sections for exotic proton-rich nuclei can be as low as a few femtobarns, with rates of the order of single ions per day (Kubiela 2021).
* The $\beta 3p$ branching ratio for $^{45}$Fe was measured as 11(4)% (Miernik 2007c).
* The $\beta 3p$ branching ratio for $^{43}$Cr was measured as $8(3) \times 10^{-4}$ (Pomorski 2011b).
* The $\beta 3p$ branching ratio for $^{31}$Ar was extracted as $7(2) \times 10^{-4}$ from 13 observed events (Lis 2015).
* The total branching ratio for $\beta\alpha p$/$\beta p \alpha$ decay of $^{17}$Ne is 1.6(4)$\cdot$10$^{-4}$ (Chow 2002).
* The total branching ratio for $\beta\alpha p$/$\beta p \alpha$ decay of $^{21}$Mg is 1.6(3)$\cdot$10$^{-4}$ (Lund 2015).
* The branching ratio for the fine-structure proton transition to the first excited $2^+$ state in $^{144}$Er from $^{145}$Tm decay is $(9.6 \pm 1.5)\%$ (Karny 2003).
* The decay energy $Q_{EC}$ for $^{31}$Ar is 18.3(2) MeV (Fynbo 2000).

## Context

This chapter extends the reviews of exotic decays by Blank (2008), Blank (2008c), and Pfutzner (2012), and the recent review of beta-delayed proton and alpha emission by Batch (2020). It incorporates the global proton-dripline predictions from Neufcourt (2020) using Bayesian Model Averaging within the nuclear density functional framework, and discusses the doubly-magic candidates $^{48}$Ni and $^{100}$Sn in light of the superallowed alpha decay chain observed by Auranen (2018) and the shell evolution discussed by Otsuka (2020). The work enables future studies of the most exotic proton-unbound nuclei, particularly those requiring next-generation facilities to reach systems like $^{164}$Pb.
