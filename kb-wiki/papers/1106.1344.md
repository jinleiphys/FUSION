# Transport-theoretical Description of Nuclear Reactions

> In this review we first outline the basics of transport theory and its recent generalization to off-shell transport. We then present in some detail the main ingredients of any transport method using in particular the Giessen Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (GiBUU) implementation of this theory as an example. We discuss the potentials used, the ground state initialization and the collision term, including the in-medium modifications of the latter. The central part of this review covers applications of GiBUU to a wide class of reactions, starting from pion-induced reactions over proton and antiproton reactions on nuclei to heavy-ion collisions (up to about 30 AGeV). A major part concerns also the description of photon-, electron- and neutrino-induced reactions (in the energy range from a few 100 MeV to a few 100 GeV). For this wide class of reactions GiBUU gives an excellent description with the same physics input and the same code being used. We argue that GiBUU is an indispensable tool for any investigation of nuclear reactions in which final-state interactions play a role. Studies of pion-nucleus interactions, nuclear fragmentation, heavy ion reactions, hyper nucleus formation, hadronization, color transparency, electron-nucleus collisions and neutrino-nucleus interactions are all possible applications of GiBUU and are discussed in this article.

## Key claim

This review argues that the Giessen Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (GiBUU) transport model provides a unified theoretical framework capable of describing a wide range of nuclear reactions (from pion-induced reactions to neutrino-nucleus interactions) with the same physics input and code, but the authors acknowledge that the method is most appropriate when the many-body final state evolves incoherently, and that quantum-mechanical reaction theory remains necessary for reactions preserving phase coherence.

## Method

The authors derive transport equations from nonequilibrium quantum field theory using the Schwinger-Keldysh real-time formalism, starting from the Dyson equation for one-particle Green's functions and truncating the hierarchy at the two-particle level within a Dirac-Brueckner approximation. They then describe the practical implementation in the GiBUU code, which solves a coupled set of semi-classical kinetic equations (Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equations) for hadronic systems, including mean-field potentials, a collision term with in-medium modifications, and off-shell transport for broad resonance-like quasi-particles. The code propagates mesons and baryons in phase space and time, with initial states obtained either directly from experimental conditions or via external models for photon-, electron-, and neutrino-induced reactions.

## Key numbers

* Heavy-ion collisions are described up to about 30 AGeV
* Photon-, electron-, and neutrino-induced reactions are covered in the energy range from a few 100 MeV to a few 100 GeV
* The energy range for hadronic degrees of freedom extends from a few 10 MeV to more than 100 GeV

## Context

This work extends earlier transport approaches that were primarily developed for heavy-ion collisions, such as those by Bertsch (1984), Stoecker (1986), and Bauer (1986), as well as precursors like the Monte Carlo generators of Serber (1947), Metropolis (1958), Bertini (1963), and Cugnon (1980). The first BUU calculation for pion and eta photoproduction off nuclei was performed by Hombach (1994) using the same code originally developed for heavy-ion collisions. The GiBUU framework enables systematic studies of final-state interactions in reactions with elementary projectiles, which is particularly important for neutrino long-baseline experiments where nuclear response must be understood to extract neutrino-oscillation parameters.
