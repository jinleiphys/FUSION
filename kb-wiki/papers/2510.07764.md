# GPU-Accelerated X-ray Pulse Profile Modeling

> Pulse-profile modeling (PPM) of thermal X-ray emission from rotation-powered millisecond pulsars enables simultaneous constraints on the mass $M$, radius $R$, and hence the equation of state of cold, dense matter. However, Bayesian PPM has faced a hard accuracy-speed bottleneck: current production resolutions used to keep inference tractable can under-resolve extreme hotspot geometries and bias the waveform computation, whereas the higher resolutions that remove this bias push forward models to minutes per evaluation, making inference impractical. We break this trade-off with, to our knowledge, the first public GPU-accelerated X-ray PPM framework that matches established benchmarks to within $\sim10^{-3}$ relative accuracy even for extreme geometries, while collapsing minutes-long high-fidelity computations to $2$--$5$ ms on an RTX 4080 ($10^{3}$--$10^{4}\times$ speedups), enabling posterior exploration at resolutions and complexities previously out of reach. We further uncover a bias near the interpolation boundaries of atmosphere lookup tables, demonstrate it with two diagnostic tests, and counter it with a mixed-order interpolator. Together, these advances enlarge the feasible hotspot model space and reduce key systematics in PPM, strengthening inferences for current and future X-ray missions.

# GPU-Accelerated X-ray Pulse Profile Modeling

## Key claim

The authors break the accuracy-speed trade-off in Bayesian pulse-profile modeling (PPM) by introducing the first public GPU-accelerated framework that matches established benchmarks to within ~10^{-3} relative accuracy even for extreme hotspot geometries, while collapsing high-fidelity computations from minutes to 2-5 ms on an RTX 4080. However, the framework adopts the oblate-Schwarzschild (OS) approximation which delivers less than 1% waveform accuracy for millisecond pulsars with spins up to ~400 Hz, and the authors note that at higher spins (above 700-800 Hz) frame dragging and external metric quadrupole terms become non-negligible, requiring Hartle-Thorne or fully relativistic rotating-star spacetimes.

## Method

The authors implemented a GPU-accelerated C++/CUDA framework that performs relativistic ray tracing in the Schwarzschild metric with rotation-induced oblateness (OS approximation), incorporating gravitational light bending, time-of-flight delays, special-relativistic Doppler boosting, and atmospheric beaming from precomputed atmosphere tables. They precomputed two-dimensional lookup tables mapping compactness and emission angle to deflection angles and time delays to avoid repeated geodesic integrations, and validated the GPU implementation against established CPU benchmarks (X-PSI) across standard and extreme hotspot geometries. They also identified a systematic error near atmosphere-table interpolation boundaries and mitigated it with a mixed-order interpolation scheme.

## Key numbers

- Relative accuracy of ~10^{-3} when reproducing established waveform benchmarks, including extreme hotspot configurations
- Per-evaluation runtime reduced from minutes to 2-5 ms on an RTX 4080
- Speedup factors of 10^{3}-10^{4}x over representative CPU baselines
- Typical equatorial surface speed for millisecond pulsars: beta_eq ~ 0.1-0.3 (dimensionless)
- Shape coefficient o_2 parameterization accurate for spin frequencies up to ~700 Hz and compactness R_S/R_e less than 1, with fractional errors less than 1%
- NICER bandpass: 0.2-12 keV, with ~100 ns absolute timing and ~10^2 eV energy resolution near 1 keV
- Typical NICER target spin frequencies: less than ~400 Hz
- Atmosphere model effective temperature range: (0.5-2) x 10^6 K

## Context

This work extends the open-source X-PSI framework (XPSI_2021, XPSI_joss) and the modeling practices summarized by Bogdanov (2019, 2021) and Salmi (2023), which established fully Bayesian PPM for NICER targets including PSR J0030+0451 (Miller 2019, Riley 2019) and PSR J0740+6620 (Miller 2021, Riley 2021). The GPU acceleration directly addresses the accuracy-speed bottleneck identified by Choudhury (2024) regarding resolution-induced likelihood biases in extreme hotspot geometries, and enables posterior exploration at resolutions and model complexities previously impractical. The framework is designed to support current NICER analyses and future missions such as eXTP (planned launch early 2030s), for which PPM is a key dense-matter science driver.
