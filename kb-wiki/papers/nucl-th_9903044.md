# Is binary sequential decay compatible with the fragmentation of nuclei at high energy?

> We use a binary sequential decay model in order to describe the fragmentation of a nucleus induced by the high energy collisions of protons with Au nuclei. Overall agreement between measured and calculated physical observables is obtained. We evaluate and analyse the decay times obtained with two different parametrisations of the decay rates and discuss the applicability of the model to high energy fragmentation.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether a binary sequential decay model can describe nuclear fragmentation in high-energy proton-gold collisions, finding that while the model reproduces charge and energy distributions, the calculated decay times are incompatible with experimental estimates of approximately 80 fm/c, raising fundamental questions about the validity of the sequential mechanism itself.

## Method

The authors simulate the decay of an excited nuclear system (A ≈ 160) produced by 8.1 GeV protons on a gold target, using a time-dependent binary sequential decay model governed by transition rates from either the Weisskopf detailed-balance principle (WTR) or the Swiatecki transition-state theory (STR). At each time step, any composite fragment can decay into two smaller species via a randomly chosen channel, with fragments flying apart along classical trajectories under Coulomb repulsion, and the process stops when all fragments fall below the lowest decay threshold. The simulation is repeated for statistics, with initial excitation energy set to E/A = 5 MeV, and results are compared to experimental data from Avdeyev and collaborators.

## Key numbers

- Initial excitation energy: E/A = 5 MeV, with calculations also performed at E/A = 4 MeV showing no significant difference
- Intermediate mass fragment (IMF) multiplicity defined for 3 ≤ Z ≤ 20
- For STR rates: generation time for light species Z=1 is of order 10^4 to 2 × 10^4 fm/c, for Z=3 about 10^4 fm/c, and IMF generation takes about 10^4 fm/c
- For WTR rates: light species generation is 5 × 10^3 to 10^4 fm/c for Z=1 and 5 × 10^2 fm/c for Z=3; most IMFs are generated within 300 fm/c
- Experimental characteristic correlation time from fragment correlations (Lips and collaborators) is approximately 80 fm/c or shorter
- Carbon energy distributions show agreement for energies below 60 MeV, with discrepancy in the high-energy tail

## Context

This work extends a previous study by Oeschler and Botvina that investigated simultaneous decay, and builds on sequential binary decay models developed by Friedman, Durand, and Pal, as well as an earlier time-dependent description by Richert. The results are compared to the Multifragmentation Multifragmentation Model (MMMC) by Gross and the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) by Bondorf, with the key finding that the sequential model's time scales (especially the STR scenario) strongly disagree with experimental correlation times, suggesting that for such rapid fragmentation, multiparticle correlations and Coulomb interactions cannot be treated independently, and a different mechanism must govern the process.
