# Nuclear symmetry energy and the r-mode instability of neutron stars

> We analyze the role of the symmetry energy slope parameter $L$ on the {\it r}-mode instability of neutron stars. Our study is performed using both microscopic and phenomenological approaches of the nuclear equation of state. The microscopic ones include the Brueckner--Hartree--Fock approximation, the well known variational equation of state of Akmal, Pandharipande and Ravenhall, and a parametrization of recent Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo calculations. For the phenomenological approaches, we use several Skyrme forces and relativisic mean field models. Our results show that the {\it r}-mode instability region is smaller for those models which give larger values of $L$. The reason is that both bulk ($ξ$) and shear ($η$) viscosities increase with $L$ and, therefore, the damping of the mode is more efficient for the models with larger $L$. We show also that the dependence of both viscosities on $L$ can be described at each density by simple power-laws of the type $ξ=A_ξL^{B_ξ}$ and $η=A_ηL^{B_η}$. Using the measured spin frequency and the estimated core temperature of the pulsar in the low-mass X-ray binary 4U 1608-52, we conclude that observational data seem to favor values of $L$ larger than $\sim 50$ MeV if this object is assumed to be outside the instability region, its radius is in the range $11.5-12$($11.5-13$) km, and its mass $1.4M_\odot$($2M_\odot$). Outside this range it is not possible to draw any conclusion on $L$ from this pulsar.

## Key claim

The r-mode instability region of neutron stars is smaller for nuclear equation-of-state models that give larger values of the symmetry energy slope parameter L, because both bulk and shear viscosities increase with L. Observational data from the pulsar in 4U 1608-52 favor values of L larger than about 50 MeV, but only if this object is assumed to be outside the instability region with a radius in the range 11.5-12 km (for 1.4 solar masses) or 11.5-13 km (for 2 solar masses).

## Method

The study employed both microscopic approaches (Brueckner-Hartree-Fock using the Argonne V18 potential with a three-body force, the variational Akmal-Pandharipande-Ravenhall equation of state, and a parametrization of Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo calculations) and phenomenological approaches (27 Skyrme forces and several relativistic mean field models including nonlinear Walecka models and density-dependent hadronic models). Bulk viscosity was computed from modified and direct Urca processes following Haensel et al., while shear viscosity included contributions from neutron scattering and electron scattering with Landau damping. The r-mode instability region was determined by solving for the critical angular velocity where the gravitational wave driving time equals the viscous damping times.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy slope parameter L takes values of 39.2 MeV (SLy10), 69.7 MeV (SkMP), and 93.4 MeV (SkT4) for the example Skyrme forces shown
- The estimated core temperature of the pulsar in 4U 1608-52 is T approximately 4.55 x 10^8 K
- The observational spin frequency of 4U 1608-52 is 620 Hz
- The Kepler frequency is approximately 7800 times the square root of (M/M_sun)(10 km/R)^3 inverse seconds
- The critical proton fraction for direct Urca processes is approximately 11-15 percent
- Muons appear at a density of about 0.14 inverse femtometers cubed in the BHF calculation
- Direct Urca processes open at densities of about 0.5 and 0.59 inverse femtometers cubed for electrons and muons respectively in the BHF model
- The maximum mass predicted by the BHF model is about 1.8 solar masses, and by the APR parametrization about 1.92 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the study of Wen, Newton and Li (2012), who used a simple equation-of-state model and found that neutron stars are stabilized against r-mode oscillations if L is smaller than about 65 MeV. The present analysis uses multiple microscopic and phenomenological approaches and considers both bulk and shear viscosity as dissipation mechanisms, rather than only electron-electron scattering at the crust-core boundary. The results provide constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy complementary to those from giant and pygmy resonances, isobaric analog states, isospin diffusion measurements, neutron skin thickness measurements, and neutron star mass-radius observations such as those by Steiner and Gandolfi (2012).
