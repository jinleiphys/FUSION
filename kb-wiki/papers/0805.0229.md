# Fermi at Los Alamos and the early Britain's way to nuclear energy

> A novel recovery of some important documents related to the Fermi course on neutron physics, held at Los Alamos in 1945, is announced. Its relevance for the effective launch of a British nuclear programme in the early postwar period, independently of the U.S. technical cooperation (precluded by the American authorities) and warmly supported by Chadwick, is discussed.

## Key claim

The paper announces the recovery of a previously unknown complete set of notes from Fermi's 1945 Los Alamos neutron physics course, taken by A.P. French, which contains more detail and explicit references to bomb applications than the previously known Halpern notes. The author argues that these notes, which came into the possession of James Chadwick by 1947 despite U.S. secrecy restrictions, were instrumental in enabling the successful launch of an independent British nuclear programme in the early postwar period without American technical cooperation.

## Method

The author examined archival documents from the Churchill Archive Centre in Cambridge (U.K.), specifically two folders from the Chadwick papers: CHAD I 17/3 (containing a letter from the Atomic Energy Commission, Fermi's "Elementary Theory of the Pile," and the first part of the Halpern notes) and CHAD I 4/1 (containing the complete set of Fermi lectures reorganized by A.P. French in June 1947). A comparative textual analysis was performed between the French version and the previously known Halpern version of the Fermi lecture notes to identify differences in content, detail, and organization.

## Key numbers

- The French notes were dated June 23, 1947, and were reorganized by French after he returned to England from Los Alamos.
- The first part of the Halpern notes was declassified on 5 September 1946; the remaining part was declassified only in 1962.
- The French notes contain at least 15 relevant additions (explanations, calculations, data, and 5 more exercises) not present in the Halpern version, while the Halpern version has only one relevant addition not in the French version.
- The French notes include six questions set as a final examination at the end of the lecture course.

## Context

This work extends the historical understanding of how British scientists acquired detailed knowledge of chain reactions and pile physics despite the 1946 McMahon Act, which made it illegal to pass "restricted data" to any foreign country including the United Kingdom. The paper builds on prior biographical work on Chadwick (BioMemo) and historical accounts by Gowing (1964) of the British nuclear programme, showing that the French notes provided key technical knowledge that supported Chadwick's optimistic view that Britain could proceed independently of the U.S. The recovery enables further studies for historians of physics and teachers of nuclear physics, as well as clarification of previously obscure points about the early British nuclear programme.
