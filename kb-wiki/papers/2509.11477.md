# Observation of quantum-field-theory dynamics on a spin-phonon quantum computer

> Simulating out-of-equilibrium dynamics of quantum field theories in nature is challenging with classical methods, but is a promising application for quantum computers. Unfortunately, simulating interacting bosonic fields involves a high boson-to-qubit encoding overhead. Furthermore, when mapping to qubits, the infinite-dimensional Hilbert space of bosons is necessarily truncated, with truncation errors that grow with energy and time. A qubit-based quantum computer, augmented with an active bosonic register, and with qubit, bosonic, and mixed qubit-boson quantum gates, offers a more powerful platform for simulating bosonic theories. We demonstrate this capability experimentally in a hybrid analog-digital trapped-ion quantum computer, where qubits are encoded in the internal states of the ions, and the bosons in the ions' motional states. Specifically, we simulate nonequilibrium dynamics of a (1+1)-dimensional Yukawa model, a simplified model of interacting nucleons and pions, and measure fermion- and boson-occupation-state probabilities. These dynamics populate high bosonic-field excitations starting from an empty state, and the experimental results capture well such high-occupation states. This simulation approaches the regime where classical methods become challenging, bypasses the need for a large qubit overhead, and removes truncation errors. Our results, therefore, open the way to achieving demonstrable quantum advantage in qubit-boson quantum computing.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a hybrid analog-digital trapped-ion quantum computer, using phonons as an active bosonic register rather than encoding bosons into qubits, can simulate out-of-equilibrium dynamics of a (1+1)-dimensional Yukawa model without truncation errors and with reduced qubit overhead, approaching the regime where classical methods become challenging.

## Method
The authors mapped fermionic fields of the Yukawa model onto qubit electronic states of trapped ions and scalar bosonic fields directly onto the ions' collective motional modes (phonons). They implemented Trotterized time evolution using a gate set that includes Molmer-Sorensen gates for spin-spin interactions, spin-dependent kicks for spin-phonon coupling, and single-qubit rotations, applied to chains of 2 and 4 ions. The dynamics were tracked by measuring fermion-occupation-state probabilities and boson-occupation-state probabilities for each motional mode after a quench from the vacuum state.

## Key numbers
* For the N=2 system (Q=0 sector): evolution up to T=6 with 12 Trotter steps of size δt=0.5, using parameters b=1, m_ψ=1, m_φ=1.5, and g=4; free-boson energies ε0≈3.48 and ε1=1.5.
* For the N=4 system (Q=-1 sector): evolution up to T=5 with 5 Trotter steps of size δt=1, using parameters b=1, m_ψ=1, m_φ=1, and g∈{0,2}; free-boson energies ε0≈3.30, ε1≈1.86, ε2=1, and ε3≈1.86.
* Phonon-occupation probabilities were accurately reproduced for occupation numbers as high as eight in the N=2 system, indicating a required cutoff Λ>8 would be needed for qubit-based encoding.
* For the N=4 system with Λ=8, the Hilbert-space dimension is 26,244, roughly equivalent to 15 qubits.
* The idle spin coherence time is ≈1.5 s, reducing to ≈400 ms under Raman laser control; motional coherence time is ≲10 ms, with recent demonstrations reaching τ_deph≈50 ms and τ_heat≈5 s.

## Context
This work extends proposals for hybrid spin-boson quantum simulation of field theories (Davoudi 2021) by providing the first experimental demonstration on a trapped-ion platform. It contrasts with fully digital approaches that require O(N log Λ) qubits and O(N²(log Λ)²) entangling gates for bosonic encoding (Jordan 2011, Kan 2021), reducing these to O(N) qubits and O(N²) spin-phonon gates. The results enable future simulations of more complex bosonic theories including self-interacting bosons, thermalization dynamics, and entanglement structure in quantum field theories, and complement a concurrent experiment on Z₂ gauge models (Saner 2025).
