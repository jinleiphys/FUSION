# Fission-Fragment Mass Distribution and Particle Evaporation at low Energies

> Fusion-fission dynamics is investigated with a special emphasis on fusion reactions at low energy for which shell effects and pairing correlations can play a crucial role leading in particular to multi-modal fission. To follow the dynamical evolution of an excited and rotating nucleus we solve a 2-dimensional Langevin equation taking explicitly light-particle evaporation into account. The confrontation theory-experiment is demonstrated to give interesting information on the model presented, its qualities as well as its shortcomings.

## Key claim

The paper extends a previously developed Langevin-based model of fission dynamics to lower excitation energies, where shell effects and pairing correlations become crucial and can lead to multi-modal fission. The authors hedge that the classical wall-and-window friction model and the Einstein relation are only valid at high energy, and that modifications to these transport coefficients are necessary at low temperature to correctly describe experimental data.

## Method

The authors solve a 2-dimensional Langevin equation in the (c, α) deformation space using the Funny-Hills parametrization (with the neck parameter h set to zero) to describe the dynamical evolution of an excited, rotating compound nucleus toward fission. The collective potential is computed as the Helmholtz free energy, including a temperature-dependent Liquid Drop Model term with Strutinsky shell corrections and BCS pairing correlations added at low temperatures. Light-particle evaporation (neutrons, protons, α particles) is coupled to the fission dynamics via emission widths calculated using either Weisskopf's evaporation theory or a phase-space distribution function approach, with the deformation dependence of the emission rates explicitly taken into account.

## Key numbers

- For the compound nucleus ²²⁷Pa at E*_tot = 26 MeV and L = 60 ħ, the average fission time in the 2-dimensional treatment is reduced by about 7% (5.96 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec) compared to the 1-dimensional value (6.36 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec).
- When quantal effects (shell corrections and pairing) are included for ²²⁷Pa at E*_tot = 26 MeV and L = 60 ħ, the average fission time increases from 8.0 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec (LDM only) to 16.2 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec.
- For ²²⁷Pa at E*_tot = 56 MeV and L = 60 ħ, the average fission time is 2.335 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec (LDM only) versus 3.275 × 10⁻¹⁷ sec (with quantal effects).
- For ²²⁷Pa at E*_tot = 56 MeV and L = 60 ħ, the neutron pre-scission multiplicity M_n is 1.806 (LDM only) versus 2.153 (with quantal effects); proton multiplicity M_p is 0.010 versus 0.006; α multiplicity M_α is 0.017 versus 0.011.
- The symmetric fission barrier in the LDM landscape for ²²⁷Pa at L = 60 ħ is about 0.7 MeV; with quantal effects the asymmetric barrier (α ≈ ±0.035) is about 0.1 MeV.
- Previous 1-dimensional calculations reproduced experimental pre-scission neutron multiplicities with an accuracy of 10–20% for nuclei ranging from ¹²⁶Ba to the region of superheavy elements.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier 1-dimensional Langevin model (Schmitt et al., 1996; Pomorski et al., 2000) which was limited to symmetric fission of highly excited nuclei, by introducing a second collective coordinate (asymmetry) and including temperature-dependent shell and pairing corrections to describe multi-modal fission at lower energies. The approach enables comparison with experimental fission-fragment mass distributions and neutron pre-scission multiplicities, and the authors note that other groups (Sierk et al., 1991; Adeev et al., 1996; Fröbrich, 1998) have also treated particle emission in fission dynamics but without explicitly accounting for nuclear deformation in the emission widths. The results highlight the strong sensitivity of fragment distributions to the fine structure of the energy landscape and the need for accurate determination of shell effects at large deformations near the scission point.
