# Inverse Problem Approach for Non-Perturbative QCD: Theoretical Foundation

> A novel theoretical framework, the inverse problem approach, is proposed to calculate non-perturbative quantities in quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Based on the dispersion relation of quantum field theory, this approach determines unknown low-energy non-perturbative quantities from known high-energy perturbative inputs via solving an inverse problem. The resulting inverse problem is rigorously proven to be ill-posed, with the solutions being unique but unstable. To address this instability, the well-established Tikhonov regularization is employed, yielding stable approximate solutions that converge to the true values as input errors vanish. The key features of this approach are illustrated through three toy models, demonstrating that solution precision can be systematically improved through reduced input errors and optimized regularization strategies.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that non-perturbative QCD quantities can be determined from perturbative high-energy inputs by solving an inverse problem based on dispersion relations, but the authors prove rigorously that this inverse problem is ill-posed (unique but unstable), requiring regularization methods such as Tikhonov regularization to obtain stable approximate solutions that converge to the true values only as input errors vanish.

## Method

The authors formulate a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind from the dispersion relation of quantum field theory, separating the low-energy non-perturbative spectral function from the high-energy perturbative region at a scale Lambda. They prove that the resulting linear integral operator K is compact and therefore cannot have a bounded inverse in infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces, establishing the ill-posedness. To obtain stable solutions, they apply Tikhonov regularization, where the regularization parameter is determined using well-established methods, and test the approach on three toy models.

## Key numbers

* The determinant of the discretized kernel matrix for the integral equation with x in [0,1] and y in [-2,-1] is approximately 10^{-88} for 10 discretization points and approximately 10^{-244} for 20 points, illustrating the extreme ill-conditioning of the naive matrix inversion approach.
* The boundedness of the operator K is established via the inequality that the operator norm is bounded by the finite constant ln|(Lambda - q_up^2)(t_min - q_low^2)/((Lambda - q_low^2)(t_min - q_up^2))|, which is derived from the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality.
* The condition for the geometric series expansion used in the uniqueness proof requires |q_up^2| > Lambda, ensuring that |x/y| <= Lambda/|q_up^2| < 1 for x in [t_min, Lambda] and y in [q_low^2, q_up^2].

## Context

This work provides the mathematical foundation for the inverse problem approach first proposed in 2020 by Li et al. for D0-D0bar mixing, where charm-quark scale quantities were obtained from perturbative inputs above the bottom-quark scale. The framework extends the dispersion relation methodology used in QCD sum rules (Shifman et al., 1979) and in data-driven determinations of hadronic vacuum polarization for muon g-2 (Aoyama et al., 2020), but replaces the quark-hadron duality assumption with a mathematically rigorous regularization procedure. The approach enables systematic improvement of non-perturbative calculations through reduced input errors and optimized regularization strategies, and can be applied to excited states and continuum spectra simultaneously with ground states.
