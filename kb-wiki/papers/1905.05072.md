# Direct comparison between Bayesian and frequentist uncertainty quantification for nuclear reactions

> Until recently, uncertainty quantification in low energy nuclear theory was typically performed using frequentist approaches. However in the last few years, the field has shifted toward Bayesian statistics for evaluating confidence intervals. Although there are statistical arguments to prefer the Bayesian approach, no direct comparison is available. In this work, we compare, directly and systematically, the frequentist and Bayesian approaches to quantifying uncertainties in direct nuclear reactions. Starting from identical initial assumptions, we determine confidence intervals associated with the elastic and the transfer process for both methods, which are evaluated against data via a comparison of the empirical coverage probabilities. Expectedly, the frequentist approach is not as flexible as the Bayesian approach in exploring parameter space and often ends up in a different minimum. We also show that the two methods produce significantly different correlations. In the end, the frequentist approach produces significantly narrower uncertainties on the considered observables than the Bayesian. Our study demonstrates that the uncertainties on the reaction observables considered here within the Bayesian approach represent reality more accurately than the much narrower uncertainties obtained using the standard frequentist approach.

## Key claim

The Bayesian approach produces 95% confidence intervals for nucleon elastic scattering and (d,p) transfer cross sections that are wider and more reliable than those from the frequentist approach, which yields unrealistically narrow uncertainties; the authors attribute this difference to strong deviations from the Gaussian distribution assumed in the frequentist chi-squared method.

## Method

The authors fitted the real volume, imaginary volume, and imaginary surface parameters of nucleon-nucleus optical potentials to elastic scattering angular distribution data for neutrons and protons on 48Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb, using both a frequentist chi-squared minimization and a Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling with wide Gaussian priors centered on the Becchetti-Greenlees global parameterization. Starting from identical initial assumptions, they collected 1600 parameter sets from each method and constructed 95% confidence intervals for the elastic scattering observables, then propagated the parameter posteriors through an adiabatic three-body reaction model to obtain confidence intervals for (d,p) transfer cross sections. Empirical coverage probabilities were evaluated by comparing the percentage of experimental data falling within the predicted confidence intervals against the nominal confidence level.

## Key numbers

- For 48Ca(n,n) at 12 MeV, the frequentist 95% confidence interval contained 61% of the data, while the Bayesian interval contained 100% of the data.
- For 48Ca(p,p) at 14 MeV, the frequentist 95% confidence interval contained 70% of the data, while the Bayesian interval contained 100% of the data.
- For 48Ca(p,p) at 25 MeV, the frequentist 95% confidence interval contained 51% of the data, while the Bayesian interval contained 94% of the data.
- For 90Zr(n,n) at 10 MeV, the frequentist 95% confidence interval contained 92% of the data, while the Bayesian interval contained 100% of the data.
- For 208Pb(p,p) at 35 MeV, the frequentist 95% confidence interval contained 44% of the data, while the Bayesian interval contained 88% of the data.
- For the transfer reaction 48Ca(d,p)49Ca(g.s.) at 19.3 MeV, the Bayesian confidence intervals were wider than the frequentist intervals across all angles, and the Bayesian approach was more reliable for higher confidence levels.

## Context

This work provides the first systematic and controlled comparison between frequentist and Bayesian uncertainty quantification methods in low-energy nuclear reaction theory, extending prior studies by Lovell (2017) and King (2018) that used frequentist chi-squared fitting of optical potentials. The results contradict the long-standing practice in the field, exemplified by the global optical potentials of Becchetti and Greenlees (1969), where frequentist methods produced strong parameter correlations that the Bayesian analysis shows are not present. The study enables future work to adopt Bayesian methods for more reliable uncertainty quantification in reaction calculations and suggests that other subfields, such as atomic and molecular physics, should perform similar comparisons.
