# Atomic spectra in a helium bubble

> Density functional theory (DFT) is applied to atomic spectra under perturbations of superfluid liquid helium. The atomic DFT of helium is used to obtain the distribution of helium atoms around the impurity atom, and the electronic DFT is applied to the excitations of the atom, averaging over the ensemble of helium configurations. The shift and broadening of the D1 and D2 absorption lines are quite well reproduced by theory, suggesting that the DFT may be useful for describing spectral perturbations in more complex environments.

## Key claim

Density functional theory (DFT) combined with a statistical ensemble of helium configurations reproduces the shift, broadening, and skewness of the Cs D1 and D2 absorption lines in superfluid helium without any adjustable parameters, suggesting the method may be useful for more complex environments.

## Method

The authors used the Orsay-Paris DFT functional for liquid helium to compute the equilibrium density profile of helium atoms around a Cs impurity, yielding a bubble radius near 6 Å. They then generated an ensemble of 100,000 random helium configurations sampled according to this density distribution, and applied first-order perturbation theory using electronic DFT wave functions (with gradient corrections for correct asymptotic behavior) to compute the energy shifts of the valence electron orbitals for each configuration. The line shape was obtained by histogramming the transition energy shifts across the ensemble, following the Franck-Condon principle (same helium configuration for ground and excited states).

## Key numbers

- Bubble radius: helium density rises sharply at approximately 6 Å from the impurity atom
- Free Cs D1 line wavelength: 894.9 nm; D2 line wavelength: 852.7 nm
- Histogram bin width for calculated spectra: 0.1 nm
- Ensemble size: 100,000 helium configurations sampled for line shape calculation
- Calculations performed at helium pressures of 0, 10, and 20 atm

## Context

This work extends earlier analyses of alkali-atom spectra in superfluid helium, specifically the collective vibration model of KFTY95 and KFY96 which reproduced average peak shifts but gave line widths less than half the observed values. It also contrasts with the Path-Integral Monte-Carlo method of Ogata (1999), which is computationally costly and difficult to apply to more complex systems. The DFT-plus-statistical approach enables application to more complex chromophores such as molecules and clusters, as noted in the authors' related paper NYB02.
