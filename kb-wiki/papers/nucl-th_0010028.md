# "Colliding beam" enhancement mechanism of deuteron-deuteron fusion reactions in matter

> We suggest a ``ping-pong'' mechanism of enhancement for fusion reactions between a low energy external deuteron beam and the deuterons in a condensed matter or molecular target. The mechanism is based on the possibility of acceleration of a target deuteron by the Coulomb field of a projectile deuteron with its subsequent rebound from a heavy atom in matter and the following fusion of the two deuterons moving towards each other. This effectively converts the fixed target process into a colliding beam reaction. In a simple limiting case this reduces the negative penetrability exponent by a factor of $\sqrt{2}$. We also discuss a contribution given by ``zero oscillations'' of a bound target deuteron. The proposed mechanism is expected to be efficient in compounds with target deuterons localized in the vicinity of heavy atoms.

# Colliding Beam Enhancement Mechanism of Deuteron-Deuteron Fusion Reactions in Matter

## Key claim
The paper proposes a "ping-pong" mechanism where a target deuteron is accelerated by the Coulomb field of an incoming projectile deuteron, rebounds from a nearby heavy atom, and then fuses with the projectile in a head-on collision, effectively converting a fixed-target experiment into a colliding-beam reaction. The authors hedge that this mechanism is expected to be efficient only for low-energy deuterons with projectile energy below 1 keV, and that the estimates for hard collisions (which could transfer all center-of-mass energy to relative motion) are not fully calculated and will be published separately.

## Method
The authors analyze the classical and quantum dynamics of two deuterons interacting via the Coulomb force in the presence of an external potential, modeled as a harmonic oscillator well representing the binding of a target deuteron in a molecule or solid. They derive the momentum transfer from the projectile to the target deuteron and from the target deuteron to the environment (heavy atoms), then compute the resulting increase in relative velocity and the corresponding exponential enhancement of the Coulomb barrier penetration factor. The analysis includes contributions from zero-point oscillations of the bound target deuteron and from classical excitation of the oscillator by the projectile.

## Key numbers
- The negative penetrability exponent in the barrier penetration factor is reduced by a factor of $\sqrt{2}$ in the limiting case of maximal momentum transfer ($q = m v_0$), corresponding to the relative velocity increasing from $v_0$ to $\sqrt{2} v_0$.
- For zero-point oscillations of a target deuteron confined in a harmonic potential with frequency $\hbar \omega = 0.3$ eV, the enhancement factor is $S = \exp\left[ (300 \text{ eV} / E_0)^2 \right]$.
- For the oscillator excitation effect with binding energy $E_b = 5$ eV, the enhancement factor is $S = \exp\left[ (200 \text{ eV} / E_0)^{3/2} \right]$.
- The combined enhancement factor estimate is $S \sim \exp\left[ (300 \text{ eV} / E_0)^2 + (200 \text{ eV} / E_0)^{3/2} \right]$, which the authors state is efficient only for $E_0 < 1$ keV.
- The classical minimal distance between deuterons is $r_{\text{min}} = 2 e^2 / E_0 = 2 a_B (27 \text{ eV} / E_0)$, where $a_B$ is the Bohr radius.
- The momentum transfer to the environment from the oscillator excitation mechanism is $q = \pi e^2 \omega / (2 v_0^2)$.

## Context
This work extends earlier claims of substantial enhancement of deuteron-deuteron fusion cross-sections in solids (referenced as publications by Ichimaru and others from the 1990s) by providing a specific physical mechanism, the "ping-pong" or colliding-beam effect, rather than attempting to interpret particular experimental results. The authors note that the mechanism could find applications in non-equilibrium processes such as chemical reactions with cracks in solids or cavities in liquids containing electric fields, and in laser-induced fusion where ions are accelerated by the laser field (citing work by Hora). They propose adding heavy atoms to deuterium-tritium mixtures to enable the rebound mechanism, and note that in laser-induced fusion where matter density can be $10^4$ times higher than in usual solids, the enhancement effects could be up to $10^8$ times larger. The paper also acknowledges the separate effect of partial screening of the Coulomb barrier by electrons, discussed by Ichimaru.
