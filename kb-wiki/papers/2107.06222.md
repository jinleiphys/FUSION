# Bottomonium production in heavy-ion collisions using quantum trajectories: Differential observables and momentum anisotropy

> We report predictions for the suppression and elliptic flow of the $Υ(1S)$, $Υ(2S)$, and $Υ(3S)$ as a function of centrality and transverse momentum in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We obtain our predictions by numerically solving a Lindblad equation for the evolution of the heavy-quarkonium reduced density matrix derived using potential nonrelativistic QCD and the formalism of open quantum systems. To numerically solve the Lindblad equation, we make use of a stochastic unraveling called the quantum trajectories algorithm. This unraveling allows us to solve the Lindblad evolution equation efficiently on large lattices with no angular momentum cutoff. The resulting evolution describes the full 3D quantum and non-abelian evolution of the reduced density matrix for bottomonium states. We expand upon our previous work by treating differential observables and elliptic flow; this is made possible by a newly implemented Monte-Carlo sampling of physical trajectories. Our final results are compared to experimental data collected in $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV Pb-Pb collisions by the ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS collaborations.

## Key claim

The paper presents predictions for bottomonium suppression and elliptic flow in 5.02 TeV Pb-Pb collisions by solving a Lindblad equation with a quantum trajectories algorithm, but the results depend on the hierarchy of scales $1/a_0 \gg \pi T \sim m_D \gg E$ and are only valid when the medium temperature exceeds $T_f = 250$ MeV, below which the evolution equations no longer hold.

## Method

The authors numerically solve a Lindblad equation derived from potential nonrelativistic QCD (pNRQCD) and the open quantum systems formalism, using a stochastic unraveling called the quantum trajectories algorithm to evolve the heavy-quarkonium reduced density matrix on a radial lattice with 4096 sites and volume 80 GeV$^{-1}$. They Monte-Carlo sample approximately 7-9 $\times 10^5$ independent physical trajectories through a 3+1D dissipative relativistic hydrodynamics medium (aHydroQP framework) for each combination of transport coefficients, binning survival probabilities by centrality, transverse momentum, and azimuthal angle to obtain differential observables and elliptic flow.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ is computed for $\Upsilon(1S)$, $\Upsilon(2S)$, and $\Upsilon(3S)$ as a function of $N_{\text{part}}$ and $p_T$ in $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.02$ TeV Pb-Pb collisions
- The heavy quark mass is taken as $M = m_b = m_{\Upsilon(1S)}/2 = 4.73$ GeV
- The strong coupling constant is $\alpha_s = 0.468$ evaluated at the inverse Bohr radius using 1-loop running with $N_f=3$ flavors and $\Lambda_{\overline{MS}}^{N_f=3}=332$ MeV
- The radial lattice uses $\texttt{NUM}=4096$ sites, volume $\texttt{L}=80$ GeV$^{-1}$, lattice spacing $a \approx 0.0195$ GeV$^{-1}$, and time step $\texttt{dt}=0.001$ GeV$^{-1}$
- The initial central temperature of the hydrodynamic medium is $T_0 = 630$ MeV at $\tau_0 = 0.25$ fm/c with constant specific shear viscosity $\eta/s = 0.159$
- The medium evolution is cut off at $T_f = 250$ MeV, below which the hierarchy of scales is no longer fulfilled
- Three temperature-dependent parameterizations of $\hat{\kappa}(T) = \kappa(T)/T^3$ (lower, central, upper) and three temperature-independent values of $\hat{\gamma} = \gamma/T^3 = \{-3.5, -1.75, 0\}$ are used
- The experimental cross sections used for feed-down corrections are $\vec{\sigma}_{\text{exp}} = \{57.6, 19, 3.72, 13.69, 16.1, 6.8, 3.27, 12.0, 14.15\}$ nb for the nine bottomonium states considered

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Brambilla et al., 2020) which computed only centrality-integrated suppression using path-averaged temperature profiles, by implementing Monte-Carlo sampling of individual physical trajectories to obtain differential observables as functions of transverse momentum and elliptic flow. The results are compared to experimental data from the ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS collaborations at 5.02 TeV, and the methodology builds on the pNRQCD and open quantum systems framework developed in earlier works (Brambilla et al., 2016, 2017, 2019). The approach enables future studies of next-to-leading-order corrections in the $E/T$ expansion to extend validity to lower temperatures.
