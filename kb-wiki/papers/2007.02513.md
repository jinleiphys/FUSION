# GW190814's secondary component with mass $(2.50-2.67)$ M$_{\odot}$ as a super-fast pulsar

> Using Stergioulas's RNS code for investigating fast pulsars with Equation of States (EOSs) on the causality surface (where the speed of sound equals that of light) of the high-density EOS parameter space satisfying all known constraints from both nuclear physics and astrophysics, we show that one possible explanation for the GW190814's secondary component of mass $(2.50-2.67)$ M$_{\odot}$ is that it is a super-fast pulsar spinning faster than 971 Hz about 42\% below its Kepler frequency. If confirmed, it would be the fastest pulsar with the highest mass observed presently. There is a large and physically allowed EOS parameter space below the causality surface where pulsars heavier than 2.50 M$_{\odot}$ are supported if they can rotate even faster with critical frequencies depending strongly on the high-density behavior of nuclear symmetry energy.

## Key claim

The authors show that one possible explanation for GW190814's secondary component of mass (2.50-2.67) M$_{\odot}$ is that it is a super-fast pulsar spinning faster than 971 Hz, about 42% below its Kepler frequency, using Equation of States on the causality surface (where the speed of sound equals that of light) of the high-density EOS parameter space satisfying all known constraints from both nuclear physics and astrophysics.

## Method

The authors used Stergioulas's RNS code to investigate rapidly rotating neutron stars with an explicitly isospin-dependent EOS-generator for neutron stars at beta equilibrium, parameterizing the energy per nucleon in symmetric nuclear matter and nuclear symmetry energy. They generated 12 EOS parameter sets on the bounded causality surface in the 3D parameter space of $K_{\rm sym}$, $J_{\rm sym}$, and $J_0$, constrained by the tidal deformability from GW170817, radii of canonical neutron stars from X-ray observations, and the minimum maximum mass of 2.14 M$_{\odot}$ from PSR J0740+6620. They then calculated the minimum rotational frequency necessary to support a neutron star of mass 2.50 M$_{\odot}$ for each EOS.

## Key numbers

- The minimum frequency $f_{2.5}$ that can rotationally support a neutron star of mass 2.50 M$_{\odot}$ on the causality surface is 971 Hz (for EOS1 with $J_{\rm sym} = -200$ MeV)
- The ratio $f_{2.5}/f_K$ ranges from 0.578 to 0.876 across the 12 EOS parameter sets on the causality surface
- The corresponding spin parameter $\chi_{2.5}$ ranges from 0.375 to 0.550
- The maximum mass of non-rotating neutron stars ($M_{\rm TOV}$) on the bounded causality surface is between 2.14 and 2.39 M$_{\odot}$
- The maximum mass of neutron stars rotating at Kepler frequency ($M_{\rm RNS}$) ranges from 2.53 to 2.87 M$_{\odot}$ for the EOSs that can support a 2.50 M$_{\odot}$ rotating star
- The equatorial radius $R_{2.5}$ of a 2.50 M$_{\odot}$ neutron star rotating at $f_{2.5}$ ranges from 11.92 km to 12.86 km

## Context

This work extends the debate following the LIGO/Virgo discovery of GW190814 (LV20), where the secondary component's nature as a neutron star or black hole was uncertain. It supports the findings of Most (2020) that the secondary can be a rapidly rotating neutron star, while contrasting with the LIGO/Virgo Collaboration's conclusion that taking a 2.3 M$_{\odot}$ maximum mass for non-rotating NSs at face value would make the secondary almost certainly not an NS. The results enable future studies to test whether GW190814's secondary is the most massive neutron star or the lightest black hole discovered, and may help constrain the high-density behavior of nuclear symmetry energy, particularly the $J_{\rm sym}$ parameter.
