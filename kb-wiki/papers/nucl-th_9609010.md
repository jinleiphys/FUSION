# The Spectral Line Shape of Exotic Nuclei

> The quadrupole strength function of $^{28}O$ is calculated making use of the SIII interaction, within the framework of continuum-RPA and taking into account collisions among the nucleons (doorway coupling). The centroid of the giant resonance is predicted at $\approx 14$ MeV, that is much below the energy expected for both isoscalar and isovector quadrupole resonances in nuclei along the stability valley. About half of this width arises from the coupling of the resonance to the continuum and about half is due to doorway coupling. This result is similar to that obtained in the study of giant resonances in light, $β$-stable nuclei, and shows the lack of basis for the expectation, entertained until now in the literature, that continuum decay was the main damping mechanism of giant resonances in halo nuclei.

# The Spectral Line Shape of Exotic Nuclei

## Key claim

The quadrupole giant resonance in the halo nucleus 28O is predicted to have a centroid near 14 MeV, much lower than the systematics of stable nuclei, and its width arises approximately equally from continuum coupling and doorway coupling (collisional damping), contradicting the prior expectation that continuum decay would be the dominant damping mechanism in halo nuclei.

## Method

The authors solved spherical Hartree-Fock equations with the Skyrme III (SIII) effective interaction to obtain the mean field and single-particle spectrum for 28O. They then calculated the quadrupole strength function using self-consistent continuum-RPA in coordinate space, and subsequently included coupling to doorway states consisting of a particle-hole excitation plus a collective surface vibration (isoscalar 2+, 3- and isovector 1- states).

## Key numbers

- The four d3/2 halo neutrons in 28O each have a binding energy of 1.1 MeV, compared to 30 MeV for the least bound proton states (1p1/2 orbital)
- The continuum-RPA giant quadrupole resonance centroid is at approximately 16 MeV, compared to the systematics expectation of approximately 21 MeV for the isoscalar GQR (63 A^{-1/3} MeV) and approximately 43 MeV for the isovector GQR (130 A^{-1/3} MeV) in stable nuclei
- Including doorway coupling lowers the GQR centroid by approximately 2 MeV to 14 MeV
- The GQR full width at half maximum increases from 4 MeV at the continuum-RPA level to 10 MeV including doorway coupling
- The continuum-RPA and unperturbed response functions each exhaust about 95% of the energy-weighted sum rule
- The low-energy region (1.1 to 13 MeV) carries 22% of the EWSR, the GQR region (13 to 19 MeV) carries approximately 55%, and the high-energy region (19 to 35 MeV) carries approximately 16%

## Context

This work extends the study of giant resonance damping mechanisms, previously developed for beta-stable nuclei (Bertsch, Bortignon, Broglia 1983; Bertsch, Broglia 1994), to the exotic halo nucleus 28O. The finding that continuum and doorway coupling contribute equally to the width contradicts the expectation from earlier work by Sagawa et al. (1995) and Hamamoto and Sagawa (1996) that continuum decay would be the main damping mechanism in halo nuclei. The results enable further theoretical investigation of how the extended neutron halo modifies both the energy and damping of collective vibrations in neutron-rich systems.
