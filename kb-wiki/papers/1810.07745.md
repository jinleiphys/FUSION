# Recent developments in radioactive charged-particle emissions and related phenomena

> The advent and intensive use of new detector technologies as well as radioactive ion beam facilities have opened up possibilities to investigate alpha, proton and cluster decays of highly unstable nuclei. This article provides a review of the current status of our understanding of clustering and the corresponding radioactive particle decay process in atomic nuclei. We put alpha decay in the context of charged-particle emissions which also include one- and two-proton emissions as well as heavy cluster decay. The experimental as well as the theoretical advances achieved recently in these fields are presented. Emphasis is given to the recent discoveries of charged-particle decays from proton-rich nuclei around the proton drip line. Those decay measurements have shown to provide an important probe for studying the structure of the nuclei involved. Developments on the theoretical side in nuclear many-body theories and supercomputing facilities have also made substantial progress, enabling one to study the nuclear clusterization and decays within a microscopic and consistent framework. We report on properties induced by the nuclear interaction acting in the nuclear medium, like the pairing interaction, which have been uncovered by studying the microscopic structure of clusters. The competition between cluster formations as compared to the corresponding alpha-particle formation are included. In the review we also describe the search for super-heavy nuclei connected by chains of alpha and other radioactive particle decays.

## Key claim

This review argues that a proper microscopic understanding of charged-particle decays (alpha, proton, two-proton, and heavy cluster) requires a parameter-free treatment of cluster formation, moving beyond effective theories that adjust parameters to fit data; the authors emphasize that the Thomas R-matrix formalism provides a framework where the decay width is independent of the channel radius when calculated correctly, contrary to historical confusion.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical formalism for charged-particle decays, centered on the Thomas expression for the decay width derived from R-matrix theory, which separates the process into a formation amplitude (the overlap between mother and daughter wave functions) and a penetrability factor through the Coulomb and centrifugal barriers. For proton decay, the formalism is applied to both spherical and deformed nuclei using single-particle wave functions from a Woods-Saxon potential, with the Gamow code for outgoing boundary conditions, and the universal decay law (UDL) is fitted to experimental half-lives to extract formation probabilities.

## Key numbers

* Nearly 50 proton decay events have been observed in odd-Z elements between Z=53 and Z=83.
* The shortest measurable mean lifetime is of order 10^-13 sec, corresponding to a resonance width of about 6.6 x 10^-9 MeV.
* For heavy isotopes of Ta, Re, Ir and Au, the root-mean-square deviation of extrapolated Q-values from known values is 34 keV.
* The UDL reproduces most experimental proton decay half-lives within a factor of 4 (0.25 ≤ R ≤ 4), with larger discrepancies (factor ~8) for emitters between Z=63 and Z=67 and the isomeric h11/2 hole state in 177Tl.

## Context

This review extends the long line of work on alpha decay dating to Gamow (1928) and the R-matrix formulation by Teichman and Wigner, as well as the Thomas expression for the decay width. It covers developments in proton radioactivity first observed by Jackson and Cerny (1970) and ground-state proton emission by Hoffman and Klepper, and it discusses the competition between alpha and proton decays observed in nuclei such as 185Ti, 177Tl, and 171Au. The work enables systematic studies of nuclear structure beyond the proton drip line, including the determination of single-particle orbitals and deformation effects, and provides a framework for searching for superheavy nuclei connected by chains of alpha and other radioactive particle decays.
