# From Neurons to Neutrons: A Case Study in Interpretability

> Mechanistic Interpretability (MI) promises a path toward fully understanding how neural networks make their predictions. Prior work demonstrates that even when trained to perform simple arithmetic, models can implement a variety of algorithms (sometimes concurrently) depending on initialization and hyperparameters. Does this mean neuron-level interpretability techniques have limited applicability? We argue that high-dimensional neural networks can learn low-dimensional representations of their training data that are useful beyond simply making good predictions. Such representations can be understood through the mechanistic interpretability lens and provide insights that are surprisingly faithful to human-derived domain knowledge. This indicates that such approaches to interpretability can be useful for deriving a new understanding of a problem from models trained to solve it. As a case study, we extract nuclear physics concepts by studying models trained to reproduce nuclear data.

## Key claim

The authors argue that mechanistic interpretability can extract human-meaningful scientific knowledge from neural networks trained on domain data, not just explain how the models make predictions. They hedge that this is demonstrated only for a specific case study (nuclear physics) and that the learned representations are "surprisingly faithful" to human-derived domain knowledge, implying the approach may not generalize universally.

## Method

The authors train small transformer-based models (with fixed attention, equivalent to feedforward MLPs) to predict up to 10 nuclear observables (binding energy, charge radius, separation energies) from proton number Z and neutron number N inputs for 3363 nuclei. They apply Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to the learned embeddings and penultimate-layer activations, then compare the resulting principal components to terms from the Semi-Empirical Mass Formula (SEMF) and nuclear shell model using cosine similarity. They also introduce quantitative metrics called "orderedness" (fraction of embeddings monotonically ordered along PC0) and "parity split" R_P (ratio of inter-parity to intra-parity L2 distances) to correlate embedding structure with generalization performance.

## Key numbers

* Models were trained on 3363 total nuclei with Z ranging from 1 to 118 and N from 0 to 178.
* Training errors were on the order of tens of keV for energy predictions.
* The "orderedness" metric (fraction of embeddings monotonically ordered along PC0) showed correlation with validation error across models trained with training fractions from 10% to 90% (3 random seeds each).
* The parity split ratio R_P (average distance between even/odd embeddings divided by average of within-parity distances) correlated with validation performance for proton embeddings.
* Using just a few principal components of activations recovered most of the model's performance on binding energy prediction (shown in a figure with error as function of number of PCs).

## Context

This work extends prior mechanistic interpretability studies on modular arithmetic by Liu et al. (2022) and Nanda et al. (2023), which showed that neural networks learn interpretable circular structures for modular addition. The authors apply similar PCA-based analysis to a real scientific domain (nuclear physics) rather than synthetic algorithmic tasks. The paper builds on the liquid-drop model and Semi-Empirical Mass Formula from Weizsäcker (1935) and nuclear shell theory as human-derived baselines for comparison. This enables a new direction where interpretability is used not just to understand models but to potentially discover scientific knowledge from trained neural networks.
