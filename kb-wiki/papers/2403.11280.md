# Revisiting the excitation of the low-lying $^{181\text{m}}$Ta isomer in optical laser-generated plasma

> The excitation of the $^{181\text{m}}$Ta isomer in the laser-plasma scenario was claimed to have been observed more than two decades ago. However, the reported experimental findings - and the respective high excitation rate - were later questioned as they could not be reproduced theoretically. The controversy has remained open ever since. In this work, we reinvestigate both theoretically and experimentally the $^{181\text{m}}$Ta nuclear excitation in an optical laser-generated plasma. Experimentally we have found no evidence for such an excitation process as consistently predicted by previous and our theoretical models.

## Key claim

The authors find no experimental evidence for the excitation of the low-lying 181mTa isomer in an optical laser-generated plasma, and their theoretical models predict excitation rates that are orders of magnitude too low to explain the positive claims made in prior work (Andreev 1999, Andreev 2000, Savel 2017). The key qualifier is that the earlier claims relied on excess counts between tantalum and tungsten targets, which the authors argue could arise from detector afterglow, surface variations, or ion-wall interactions rather than nuclear decay.

## Method

The authors generated plasma by focusing femtosecond laser pulses (40 fs duration, 0.78 micrometer wavelength, 4 kHz repetition rate) onto a 181Ta cylindrical target at peak intensities of 4e16 W/cm2 and 2e16 W/cm2. They used an AdvaPix TPX3 X-ray detector with 1.56 ns time resolution and 1 keV energy resolution, positioned above a deposit screen to collect the expanding plasma, and acquired energy-time histograms over 6-10 hour integration periods. The experimental results were compared to theoretical calculations of the expected number of excited nuclei per laser shot, modeling photoexcitation via bremsstrahlung and thermal radiation (TDE) as well as nuclear excitation by inelastic electron scattering (NEIES), using plasma parameters derived from scaling laws and the collisional-radiative code FLYCHK.

## Key numbers

* Laser peak intensity: 4e16 W/cm2 (and 2e16 W/cm2 for the lower-energy run), within the range of 1e16 to 5e16 W/cm2 reported in Andreev (1999).
* Total events detected over 10 hours: approximately 6.2e9 counts (rising to 9e9 after accounting for detector efficiency and Kapton foil absorption).
* Theoretical upper limit for gamma-deexcitations per laser shot: 1.27e-6 (TDE photoexcitation), corresponding to 0.58 detectable events over 10 hours; the bremsstrahlung model gives 1.4e-14 gamma-deexcitations per shot.
* Excitation rate from NEIES: 4.49e-4 per second, yielding 1.08e-7 gamma-deexcitations per shot.
* The claimed excitation rate from Andreev (1999) of 1e3 to 1e4 excited nuclei per shot would require an excitation rate of 1e4 to 1e6 per second, which is 4 to 8 orders of magnitude larger than the theoretical upper limit of 8.42 per second derived under the most favorable TDE conditions.

## Context

This work reinvestigates a controversy that began with claims of 181mTa isomer excitation in laser plasma by Andreev (1999, 2000), which could not be reproduced theoretically and were later questioned by Gobet (2008, 2011). A 2017 study by Savel estimated 30-50 excited nuclei per shot but still gave a theoretically inexplicable rate. The present experiment, with improved time-resolved spectroscopy and a detector capable of distinguishing nuclear decay from plasma radiation, finds no signal consistent with isomer decay, consistent with theoretical models from Wu (2018) and Gunst (2018). The authors conclude that observing this isomer excitation in laser plasma would require overcoming several orders of magnitude in excitation rate, making it impractical with current tabletop laser systems.
