# Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions: Viscous Hydrodynamic Simulations and Final State Interactions

> In this dissertation I introduce relativistic heavy ion collisions and describe theoretical approaches to understanding them--in particular, viscous hydrodynamic simulations and investigations of final state interactions. The successful ideal hydrodynamic models of the collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) were extended by performing viscous hydrodynamic simulations. This was done by making use of the recently derived full conformally invariant second order relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations. Results for multiplicity, radial flow and elliptic flow in sqrt{s_NN}=200 GeV Au+Au RHIC collisions are presented and the range of the ratio of shear viscosity over entropy density eta/s for which our hydrodynamic model is consistent with experimental data is quoted. In addition, simulations were performed of the planned sqrt{s_NN}=5.5 TeV Pb+Pb and sqrt{s}=14 TeV p+p collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). The elliptic flow coefficient v_2 is predicted to be 10% larger for the Pb+Pb collisions compared to top energy RHIC collisions, and is predicted to be consistent with zero for proton collisions unless eta/s < 0.08. Finally, final state interactions were investigated within the distorted wave emission function (DWEF) model. Work is presented on an improved understanding of the DWEF model, and the potential effect of final state interactions in the form of a pion optical potential on the elliptic flow coefficient v_2 was calculated to be at the ~20% level.

## Key claim

Viscous hydrodynamic simulations, using the full conformally invariant second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations, can describe RHIC data for multiplicity, radial flow, and elliptic flow in sqrt{s_NN}=200 GeV Au+Au collisions, but the extracted range of eta/s depends on the specific model assumptions; the DWEF model shows that including an imaginary part of the optical potential improves the description of HBT data quantitatively but not qualitatively, and the real part of the optical potential is qualitatively important.

## Method

Viscous hydrodynamic simulations were performed using the recently derived full conformally invariant second-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamic equations for sqrt{s_NN}=200 GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and for planned sqrt{s_NN}=5.5 TeV Pb+Pb and sqrt{s}=14 TeV p+p collisions at the LHC. Separately, the distorted wave emission function (DWEF) model was used to incorporate final state interactions via a relativistic optical model formalism, where pions interact with a time-independent external potential while being emitted from a parametrized source function inspired by hydrodynamic freeze-out.

## Key numbers

- For sqrt{s_NN}=200 GeV Au+Au RHIC collisions, the elliptic flow coefficient v_2 is predicted to be 10% larger for sqrt{s_NN}=5.5 TeV Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC compared to top energy RHIC collisions
- For sqrt{s}=14 TeV p+p collisions at the LHC, v_2 is predicted to be consistent with zero unless eta/s < 0.08
- The potential effect of final state interactions in the form of a pion optical potential on v_2 was calculated to be at the ~20% level
- In DWEF fits with vanishing imaginary optical potential, the temperature was reduced from about 160 MeV to about 120 MeV compared to fits including the imaginary part
- The emission duration in the DWEF fit with vanishing imaginary potential was reduced to 0 fm/c, while fits with fixed emission duration of 1.5 fm/c gave a chi-squared of 451 versus 300 for the vanishing imaginary potential fit
- The DWEF fit with vanishing optical potential gave a chi-squared of 1068, the largest deviation from data

## Context

This work extends ideal hydrodynamic models of RHIC collisions by incorporating viscosity using the conformally invariant second-order equations, and addresses the "RHIC HBT puzzle" (Heinz 2002) where ideal hydrodynamics failed to simultaneously describe HBT radii and single-particle data. The DWEF model builds on earlier work by Cramer (2004) and Miller (2005) to include final state interactions, and the results are compared to the blast wave model of Retiere (2003). The viscous simulations enable predictions for LHC energies, while the DWEF analysis clarifies the role of real versus imaginary optical potentials in describing pion emission data.
