# Condensation of Dibaryons in Nuclear Matter and Its Possible Signatures in Heavy Ion Collisions

> We consider the thermodynamics of the matter made up of equal number of neutrons and protons and of scalar dibaryons. They interact via the exchange of scalar and vector mesons. The interaction is taken into account in the mean field approximation. The condensation of dibaryons in this matter and the phase transition of matter to quark matter are considered. Possible signatures of dibaryons in Heavy Ion Collisions are speculated on.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a dibaryon condensate can form in symmetric nuclear matter at finite temperatures and densities, but only below about 36 MeV temperature and above about 0.54 fm⁻³ baryon density; at higher temperatures the system transitions to quark matter before dibaryon condensation can occur.

## Method

The authors extend the Walecka mean-field model by including a scalar dibaryon field (specifically the d′ dibaryon with mass 2060 MeV) coupled to σ- and ω-mesons, with coupling constants h_ω = 2g_ω and h_σ = 1.6g_σ. They solve self-consistency equations for the effective nucleon and dibaryon masses, compute the condensation curve on the temperature-density plane, and compare the nuclear matter equation of state to a quark matter equation of state (MIT bag model with α_c = 0.6 and B = 96 MeV/fm³) using the Gibbs criterion for first-order phase transitions.

## Key numbers

- The dibaryon condensation at zero temperature starts at baryon density ρ_TV = 0.54 fm⁻³, which is approximately 2.7 times normal nuclear density ρ₀.
- The maximum temperature for dibaryon condensation is 36 MeV; above this temperature the system undergoes a phase transition to quark matter instead.
- The dibaryon d′ has a mass of 2060 MeV and a decay width Γ_d′ ≈ 0.5 MeV.
- Pions from dibaryon decay have characteristic kinetic energy of 40–50 MeV.
- The quark matter bag constant is B = 96 MeV/fm³ and the gluonic coupling constant is α_c = 0.6.

## Context

This work extends earlier zero-temperature studies of dibaryon condensation in nuclear and neutron matter by Faessler et al. (Refs. [6,7,8] in the paper) to finite temperatures relevant for heavy ion collisions. It also incorporates the possibility of a phase transition to quark matter, which was not considered in those earlier works. The results suggest that while the compression curve from a one-dimensional shock wave model (Rankin-Hugoniot-Taub equation) lies above the condensation region, radiative cooling could allow the system to evolve into the condensate region, potentially producing observable pions with 40–50 MeV kinetic energy from dibaryon decays.
