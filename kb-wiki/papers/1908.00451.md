# PHSD -- a microscopic transport approach for strongly interacting systems

> We present the basic ideas of the Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) transport approach which is a microscopic covariant dynamical model for strongly interacting systems formulated on the basis of Kadanoff-Baym equations for Green's functions in phase-space representation (in 1st order gradient expansion beyond the quasi-particle approximation). The approach consistently describes the full evolution of a relativistic heavy-ion collision from the initial hard scatterings and string formation through the dynamical deconfinement phase transition to the strongly-interacting quark-gluon plasma (sQGP) as well as hadronization and the subsequent interactions in the expanding hadronic phase. The PHSD approach has been applied to p+p, p+A and A+A collisions from lower SIS to LHC energies and been successful in describing a large number of experimental data including single-particle spectra, collective flow and electromagnetic probes. Some highlights of recent PHSD results will be presented.

## Key claim

The Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) approach provides a consistent microscopic covariant description of the full evolution of relativistic heavy-ion collisions, from initial hard scatterings through the dynamical deconfinement phase transition to hadronization and subsequent hadronic interactions, but the quasiparticle properties (mass and width) used for nonequilibrium configurations are adopted from thermal equilibrium, which is a well-motivated approximation not fully equivalent to the exact solution of the Kadanoff-Baym equations.

## Method

The approach solves off-shell transport equations derived from Kadanoff-Baym equations for Green's functions in phase-space representation using a first-order gradient expansion beyond the quasi-particle approximation. The hadronic part is equivalent to the Hadron-String Dynamics (HSD) approach, while the partonic dynamics is based on the Dynamical QuasiParticle Model (DQPM), which describes QCD properties using single-particle Green's functions with complex selfenergies fitted to lattice QCD data. Hadronization is implemented via local covariant transition rates for quark-antiquark fusion to mesonic states and three-quark fusion to baryonic states, conserving four-momentum, flavor currents, and entropy.

## Key numbers

- The critical energy density for the deconfinement phase transition in the DQPM is approximately 0.5 GeV/fm³.
- The width parameter b for the Gaussian phase-space distribution of formed pre-hadrons is fixed by sqrt(⟨r²⟩) = b = 0.66 fm in the rest frame, corresponding to the average rms radius of mesons.
- The formation time of hadrons from string decay is approximately 0.8 fm/c.
- The ratio of shear viscosity to entropy density η/s as a function of scaled temperature T/Tc at μB = 0 shows values near the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound of 1/(4π), with results from the Kubo formalism and relaxation-time approximation agreeing within error bars.
- Energy conservation in heavy-ion collision calculations is violated by typically less than 1%.
- For LHC energies, the computational time was stopped at approximately 1000 fm/c due to time dilatation (γ_cm ≈ 1400) for partons near projectile or target rapidity.

## Context

This work extends the Hadron-String Dynamics (HSD) approach (Ehehalt, 1996) and the Dynamical QuasiParticle Model (Cassing, 2006; Cassing, 2007) by providing a unified framework that dynamically describes the transition between partonic and hadronic degrees of freedom. The approach is similar in spirit to the AMPT model (Lin, 2005) but differs by using off-shell partons with finite masses and widths from the DQPM, and by implementing hadronization through covariant transition rates that solve the entropy problem inherent in coalescence models. The results enable systematic studies of transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma, such as shear viscosity, and provide predictions for observables across a wide energy range from SIS to LHC that can be compared with experimental data from the E866, E877, E891, E896, and NA49 collaborations.
