# Chiral anomaly, dimensional reduction, and magnetoresistivity of Weyl and Dirac semimetals

> By making use of the Kubo formula, we calculate the conductivity of Dirac and Weyl semimetals in a magnetic field. We find that the longitudinal (along the direction of the magnetic field) magnetoresistivity is negative at sufficiently large magnetic fields for {\it both} Dirac and Weyl semimetals. The physical reason of this phenomenon is intimately connected with the dimensional spatial reduction $3 \to 1$ in the dynamics of the lowest Landau level. The off-diagonal component of the transverse (with respect to the direction of the magnetic field) conductivity in Weyl semimetals contains an anomalous contribution directly proportional to the momentum space separation between the Weyl nodes. This contribution comes exclusively from the lowest Landau level and, as expected, is independent of the temperature, chemical potential, and magnetic field. The other part of the off-diagonal conductivity is the same as in Dirac semimetals and is connected with a nonzero density of charge carriers. The signatures for experimental distinguishing Weyl semimetals from Dirac ones through the measurements of conductivity are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper claims that negative longitudinal magnetoresistivity, previously considered a fingerprint of Weyl semimetals due to the chiral anomaly, also occurs in Dirac semimetals at sufficiently large magnetic fields, meaning this phenomenon alone cannot unambiguously distinguish between the two types of semimetals.

## Method

The authors used the Kubo linear response formalism to calculate the direct current conductivity tensor for Dirac and Weyl semimetals in a magnetic field, starting from a low-energy Hamiltonian describing two Weyl nodes of opposite chirality separated by a momentum-space vector. They derived the quasiparticle propagator in the Landau-level representation, modeled finite quasiparticle lifetimes by replacing delta functions in the spectral function with Lorentzian functions, and computed the longitudinal and transverse components of the conductivity analytically and numerically.

## Key numbers

- The lowest Landau level (LLL) contribution to longitudinal conductivity is given by $\sigma_{33}^{\rm (LLL)} = e^2 v_F |eB| / (4 \pi^2 c \Gamma_0)$, where $\Gamma_0$ is the LLL quasiparticle width, showing linear dependence on magnetic field $B$.
- The Landau energy scale is defined as $\epsilon_L \equiv v_F/l \equiv v_F \sqrt{|eB|/c}$, where $l = \sqrt{c/|eB|}$ is the magnetic length.
- Numerical results for longitudinal magnetoconductivity and magnetoresistivity are presented as functions of $v^2_F |eB| / \mu^2 c$ for quasiparticle widths $\Gamma = 0.1 \mu$ and $\Gamma = 0.2 \mu$ in higher Landau levels.
- The LLL quasiparticle width $\Gamma_0$ is related to the collision time by $\Gamma_0 = \hbar / \tau$.

## Context

This work extends prior semiclassical Boltzmann kinetic equation studies of magnetoresistivity in Weyl semimetals by Nielsen (1980), Aji (2012), and Son (2013) by using a microscopic Kubo formalism that includes quantum effects. The paper contradicts the assumption that negative longitudinal magnetoresistivity uniquely identifies Weyl semimetals, showing it also appears in Dirac semimetals due to dimensional reduction $3 \to 1$ in the LLL dynamics. The results enable experimental distinction between Dirac and Weyl semimetals through measurements of the off-diagonal transverse conductivity, which contains an anomalous contribution proportional to the momentum-space separation between Weyl nodes in Weyl semimetals.
