# Evolution of Fluctuation in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We have studied the time evolution of the fluctuations in the net baryon number for different initial conditions and space time evolution scenarios. We observe that the fluctuations at the freeze-out depend crucially on the equation of state (EOS) of the system and for realistic EOS the initial fluctuation is substantially dissipated at the freeze-out stage. At SPS energies the fluctuations in net baryon number at the freeze-out stage for quark gluon plasma and hadronic initial state is close to the Poissonian noise for ideal as well as for EOS obtained by including heavier hadronic degrees of freedom. For EOS obtained from the parametrization of lattice QCD results the fluctuation is larger than Poissonian noise. It is also observed that at RHIC energies the fluctuations at the freeze-out point deviates from the Poissonian noise for ideal as well as realistic equation of state, indicating presence of dynamical fluctuations.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the net baryon number fluctuation at freeze-out depends crucially on the equation of state (EOS) of the system, and that for a realistic EOS obtained from lattice QCD parametrization the initial fluctuation is substantially dissipated, but the final fluctuation can still be larger than Poissonian noise at both SPS and RHIC energies, whereas for ideal gas EOS or an EOS with constant speed of sound squared of 0.18 the final fluctuation is close to Poissonian noise at SPS energies.

## Method

The authors model the time evolution of net baryon number fluctuations in relativistic heavy-ion collisions using a one-dimensional boost-invariant (Bjorken) expansion. They consider three initial-state scenarios: a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) with a first-order phase transition, a hadron gas, and a hadron gas with in-medium mass modification according to Brown-Rho scaling. The evolution of the fluctuation is separated into a dissipation term, arising from baryon diffusion between adjacent sub-volumes, and a generation term, arising from the exchange of baryons with neighboring sub-volumes; the total fluctuation at freeze-out is the sum of the variances from both processes. The calculations are performed for three different equations of state: an ideal gas (c_s^2 = 1/3), a hadronic EOS with particles up to 2.5 GeV (c_s^2 = 0.18), and a parametrization of 3-flavor lattice QCD results.

## Key numbers

*   For the QGP scenario at SPS energies with the lattice QCD EOS, the final fluctuation per unit baryon at freeze-out is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_f))^2/N_{b,y} \sim 2.0\).
*   For the hadron gas scenario at SPS energies with the lattice QCD EOS, the final fluctuation per unit baryon at freeze-out is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_f))^2/N_{b,y} \sim 2.8\).
*   For the hadron gas with mass variation scenario at SPS energies with the lattice QCD EOS, the final fluctuation per unit baryon at freeze-out is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_f))^2/N_{b,y} \sim 2.5\).
*   For the QGP scenario at RHIC energies (\(\sqrt{s}=200\) A GeV Au+Au) with the lattice QCD EOS, the final fluctuation per unit baryon at freeze-out is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_f))^2/N_{b,y} \sim 3.17\).
*   For the QGP scenario at RHIC energies with an ideal gas EOS (c_s^2 = 1/3), the final fluctuation per unit baryon at freeze-out is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_f))^2/N_{b,y} \sim 1.46\).
*   The initial fluctuation in net baryon number for the QGP scenario at SPS energies is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_i))^2 = 35\), and for the hadron gas scenario it is \((\Delta N_b(\tau_i))^2 \sim 72\).
*   The freeze-out temperature is taken as 120 MeV and the critical temperature for the QGP transition as 170 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by twin_prl1 and twin_prl2 (2000-2001) which used only ideal gas equations of state and did not account for the evolution of fluctuations during the mixed phase or modifications of hadronic spectral functions. The present study incorporates a realistic EOS from lattice QCD results (Karsch, 2002) and considers Brown-Rho scaling (Brown and Rho, 1991) for in-medium hadron masses. The findings enable experimental searches for dynamical fluctuations at RHIC, where the deviation from Poissonian noise is predicted to be larger than at SPS, and suggest that distinguishing between a pure hadron gas and a hadron gas with mass variation from fluctuation measurements alone may be difficult.
