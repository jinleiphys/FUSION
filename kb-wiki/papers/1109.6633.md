# Sound Propagation on Top of the Fireball

> We study the effect that initial state fluctuations have on final particle correlations in heavy ion collisions. More precisely, we focus on the propagation of initial perturbations on top of the expanding fireball using the conformal solution derived by Gubser and Yarom for central collisions. For small perturbations, the hydrodynamic equations are solved by separation of variables and the solutions for different modes are added up to construct initial point-like perturbations, that are then allowed to evolve until freeze-out. The Cooper-Frye prescription is used to determine the final particle distribution. We present the two-particle correlation functions and their Fourier spectra obtained for different viscosities and different initial widths of the perturbation. We find that viscosity kills the higher harmonics, but that the Fourier spectra presents maxima and minima, similar to what is seen in the study of cosmic microwave background radiation.

## Key claim

Initial state fluctuations in heavy ion collisions produce sound-like perturbations that propagate as expanding circles on top of the fireball, and the resulting two-particle correlation functions and Fourier spectra show maxima and minima similar to cosmic microwave background radiation, but only under the idealized assumptions of conformal matter with constant speed of sound and small linearized perturbations.

## Method

The authors placed a single Gaussian temperature perturbation on top of Gubser's conformal hydrodynamic flow solution for central heavy ion collisions, using separation of variables to solve the linearized hydrodynamic equations. They decomposed the initial perturbation into spherical harmonics up to l=30, evolved it until freeze-out using an isothermal freeze-out condition, and applied the Cooper-Frye prescription to obtain final particle distributions. Two-particle correlation functions were computed by multiplying single-particle distributions and averaging over the azimuthal position of the initial perturbation.

## Key numbers

- Speed of sound fixed at c_s = 1/√3 throughout evolution (conformal matter with equation of state ε = 3p)
- Shear viscosity values studied: η/s = 0, 0.08, 0.134, and 0.16
- Initial Gaussian perturbation widths studied: 0.4 fm, 0.7 fm, and 1.0 fm
- Evolution shown at proper times τ = 1, 4, and 6 fm/c
- Harmonics up to l=30 included in the decomposition
- Fourier coefficients v_n^2 normalized so that n=3 matches ATLAS data

## Context

This work extends the sound-propagation picture introduced by Shuryak (2009) and followed up by Staig and Shuryak (2010), using the analytic conformal flow solution developed by Gubser and Yarom (2010) as a generalization of Bjorken flow. The study builds on Alver and Roland's (2010) demonstration that initial fluctuations from random nucleon positions produce odd Fourier harmonics, particularly v_3, which are crucial for understanding the ridge and double-hump structure in two-particle correlations. The results enable comparison with experimental measurements from ATLAS (2011) and suggest that matching data requires either large initial perturbation widths or large viscosity to suppress higher harmonics.
