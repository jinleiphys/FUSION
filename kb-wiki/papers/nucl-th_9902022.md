# Nucleosynhthesis in a Simmering Universe

> Primordial nucleosynthesis is a success story of the standard big bang (SBB) cosmology. We explore nucleosynthesis in possible models very different from SBB in which the cosmological scale factor increases linearly with time right through the period during which nucleosynthesis occurs till the present.It turns out that weak interactions remain in thermal equilibrium upto temperatures which are two orders of magnitude lower than the corresponding (weak interaction decoupling) temperatures in SBB. Inverse beta decay of the proton ensures adequate production of helium while producing primordial metallicity much higher than that produced in SBB. Attractive features of such models are the absence of the horizon, flatness and age problems and consistency with classical cosmological tests.

## Key claim

In a universe where the cosmological scale factor increases linearly with time (a "coasting" Milne universe), weak interactions remain in thermal equilibrium down to temperatures two orders of magnitude lower than in standard big bang cosmology, and inverse beta decay of the proton can replenish neutrons fast enough to produce adequate helium-4 despite the very low neutron-to-proton ratio at the relevant temperatures.

## Method

The authors modified the Kawano (1988) nucleosynthesis code, rewriting essential subroutines in quadruple precision to handle the much stiffer rate equations arising from the slow expansion rate (the universe takes about 1000 years to cool from 10^10 K to 10^7 K, versus seconds in the standard model). They evolved a network of 88 reactions for 26 nuclides from an initial temperature of 10^11 K down to 10^7 K, varying the baryon-to-entropy ratio η as the single free parameter, while using a scale factor R(t) = t and the corresponding Hubble parameter H = 1/t throughout the nucleosynthesis epoch.

## Key numbers

- Weak interaction rates remain in equilibrium down to temperatures about two orders of magnitude lower than in standard big bang cosmology; the ratio σ_wk n_l / H for neutral-current processes scales as (T / 1.6×10^8 K)^4, compared to (T / 10^10 K)^3 in the standard model.
- The universe at T ≈ 10^10 K has an age of about 4.5 years (versus ~0.01 seconds in the standard model), and takes about 10^3 years to cool to 10^7 K.
- The expansion rate at around 10^9 K is about 10^7 times slower than the corresponding rate in standard cosmology.
- A helium-4 mass fraction Y_He ≈ 24% is obtained for a nucleosynthesis onset temperature T_9o ≈ 0.9 (i.e., 0.9×10^9 K), assuming all neutrons produced by inverse beta decay branch into helium formation.
- For baryon-to-entropy ratio η ≈ 10^(-8), the numerical code yields helium-4 mass fractions ranging from 0.203 (η = 9.0×10^(-9)) to 0.230 (η = 1.0×10^(-8)).
- The primordial metallicity (elements with mass number ≥ 8) produced is about 8 orders of magnitude greater than in the standard big bang model, with abundances around 10^(-8) relative to hydrogen for η ≈ 10^(-8).

## Context

This work challenges the conclusion of Kaplinghat, Steigman, Tkachev and Walker (1998) that a linearly expanding (R(t) = t) cosmology is ruled out by nucleosynthesis constraints, showing instead that inverse beta decay of the proton can salvage adequate helium production. The model extends the class of "coasting" cosmologies previously explored by Kolb (1989) and Sethi and Lohiya (1997), and offers an alternative to standard big bang nucleosynthesis that avoids the horizon, flatness, and age problems. The authors suggest that deuterium could be produced later by spallation of hydrogen-rich clouds on helium- and metal-rich regions in an inhomogeneous version of the model, following mechanisms discussed by Epstein, Lattimer and Schramm (1976).
