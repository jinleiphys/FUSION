# Tensor effective interaction in self-consistent Random Phase Approximation calculations

> We present a study of the effects of the tensor-isospin term of the effective interaction in Hartree-Fock and Random Phase Approximation calculations. We used finite-range forces of Gogny type, and we added to them a tensor-isospin term which behaves, at large internucleonic distances, as the analogous term of the microscopic interactions. The strength of this tensor force has been chosen to reproduce the experimental energy of the lowest 0$^-$ excited state in $^{16}$O, which shows large sensitivity to this term of the interaction. With these finite-range interactions, we have studied the effects of the tensor-isospin force in ground and excited states of carbon, oxygen, calcium, nickel, zirconium, tin and lead isotopes. Our results show that the tensor force affects mainly the nucleon single particle energies. However, we found some interesting cases where also bulk nuclear properties are sensitive to the tensor interaction.

## Key claim

The tensor-isospin term of the effective interaction primarily affects nucleon single-particle energies, but the authors identify the energy of the lowest 0⁻ excited state in ¹⁶O as a more reliable observable than single-particle energies for fixing the tensor force strength, because this excitation shows large and smooth sensitivity to the tensor term while avoiding ambiguities from spin-orbit contributions.

## Method

The authors added a finite-range tensor-isospin term, based on the radial form of the Argonne V18 interaction modified by a short-range correlation function, to the D1S and D1M Gogny-type effective interactions. They performed iterative self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus Random Phase Approximation calculations, adjusting the tensor strength parameter b and the spin-orbit term to simultaneously reproduce the experimental 0⁻ excitation energy at 10.6 MeV in ¹⁶O and the spin-orbit splitting of the 1p neutron states in ¹⁶O. The resulting interactions, D1ST and D1MT, were then applied to study ground and excited states of carbon, oxygen, calcium, nickel, zirconium, tin, and lead isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The tensor strength parameter b was fixed to 0.6 fm⁻² for the D1ST force and 0.25 fm⁻² for the D1MT force.
- The spin-orbit term was changed from 130 MeV (D1S) to 134 MeV (D1ST), and from 115 MeV (D1M) to 122.5 MeV (D1MT).
- The tensor force reduced the 0⁻ excitation energies by 4 to 8 MeV across the nuclei studied, corresponding to relative variations between 30% and 100%.
- The relative percentage differences in binding energies between forces with and without tensor term were within approximately ±2% for all nuclei except ¹⁴O.
- The relative changes in root-mean-square radii for both neutrons and protons were of the order of a few parts per thousand.

## Context

This work extends the study of tensor effects in effective interactions beyond the approach of Otsuka et al. (2005, 2006, 2008), who used single-particle energy systematics to constrain tensor forces, by instead using the 0⁻ excitation energy as a benchmark. The authors note that the tensor term in their D1ST and D1MT forces is much weaker than the bare Argonne V18 tensor term and also differs significantly from the tensor term in the GT2 force of Otsuka et al. (2006) and the M3YP2 force of Nakada (2003). The results enable future self-consistent RPA studies of unnatural-parity excitations and suggest that tensor effects on bulk nuclear properties are small, while single-particle level ordering near the Fermi surface can be modified, potentially affecting spin assignments in neighboring odd-even nuclei.
