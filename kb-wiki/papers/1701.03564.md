# Nuclear Symmetry Energy Extracted from Laboratory Experiments

> Some recent progress and open questions in extracting and understanding the new physics underlying the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy from laboratory experiments are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while significant progress has been made in constraining the nuclear symmetry energy around and below saturation density, its high-density behavior remains largely unconstrained, and the uncertainties in extracted values depend heavily on the specific models and observables used, with the density slope parameter \(L(\rho)\) being more uncertain than the symmetry energy itself due to its dependence on several poorly known quantities.

## Method

The paper reviews the decomposition of the nuclear symmetry energy \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho)\) and its density slope \(L(\rho)\) in terms of the density and momentum dependence of single-nucleon potentials at the mean-field level, using the Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem and Bruckner theory. It surveys 28 to 53 model analyses of terrestrial nuclear experiments and astrophysical observations, including atomic masses, neutron-skins of heavy nuclei, isospin diffusion in heavy-ion reactions, excitation energies of isobaric analog states, isoscaling, electric dipole polarizability, giant dipole resonances, nucleon-nucleus scattering, and neutron star observables. The paper also discusses transport model analyses of heavy-ion collision data, such as isospin diffusion and pion ratios, to probe the symmetry energy at sub-saturation and supra-saturation densities.

## Key numbers

*   Central values of \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_0)\) from 28 analyses up to August 2013: \(31.6 \pm 2.66\) MeV.
*   Central values of \(L(\rho_0)\) from 28 analyses up to August 2013: \(58.9 \pm 16\) MeV.
*   Central values of \(E_{\rm sym}(\rho_0)\) from 53 analyses up to October 2016: \(31.7 \pm 3.2\) MeV.
*   Central values of \(L(\rho_0)\) from 53 analyses up to October 2016: \(58.7 \pm 28.1\) MeV.
*   Kinetic and potential parts of the symmetry energy at saturation density extracted from nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering data are both approximately 18 MeV.
*   The kinetic symmetry energy of quasi-nucleons at \(\rho_0\) is about 43% larger than that of the free Fermi gas of about 12 MeV.
*   The current estimate of \(K_{\tau}\) (the isospin-dependent part of the incompressibility of asymmetric nuclear matter at \(\rho_0\)) is approximately \(-550 \pm 100\) MeV.
*   The linear relation between neutron skin thickness in \(^{208}\)Pb and \(L(\rho_0)\) is given by \(\Delta r_{np} ({\rm fm}) = 0.101 + 0.00147 \cdot L(\rho_0) ({\rm MeV})\).
*   The strongest correlation coefficient between \(L(\rho)\) and neutron skin thickness of \(^{208}\)Pb appears at a density of about \(\rho/\rho_0 = 0.59\).
*   The reference density for nuclear observables related to average properties of nuclei is approximately \(\rho \approx 0.11\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This review extends the work of earlier comprehensive reviews on constraining the symmetry energy (e.g., Steiner 2005, Tsang 2012, Lat 2013) and surveys by Li-han (2013) and Oer (2016). It discusses the decomposition of the symmetry energy and its slope in terms of nucleon potentials, building on the work of Xu, Li, and Chen (2010a). The paper highlights that while constraints around saturation density are converging, the high-density behavior remains controversial, with disagreements between transport model analyses of FOPI/GSI pion data (Xiao et al.) and ASY-EOS Collaboration flow data. It enables future work by emphasizing the need for better quantified uncertainties and coordinated theoretical efforts using systematically tested reaction models, as well as combining results from new laboratory experiments with radioactive beams and astrophysical observations from x-ray observatories and gravitational wave detectors.
