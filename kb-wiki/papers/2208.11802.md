# Midrapidity cluster formation in heavy-ion collisions

> We study the production of clusters and hypernuclei at midrapidity employing the Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular-Dynamics (PHQMD) approach, a microscopic n-body transport model based on the QMD propagation of the baryonic degrees of freedom with density dependent 2-body potential interactions. In PHQMD the cluster formation occurs dynamically, caused by the interactions. The clusters are recognized by the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) algorithm. We present the PHQMD results for cluster and hypernuclei formation in comparison with the available experimental data at relativistic energies. PHQMD allows to study the time evolution of formed clusters and the origin of their production, which helps to understand how such weakly bound objects are formed and survive in the rather dense and hot environment created in heavy-ion collisions. It offers therefore an explanation of the 'ice in the fire' puzzle. To investigate whether this explanation of the 'ice in the fire' puzzle applies only to the MST results we study also the deuterons production by coalescence. We embed MST and coalescence in the PHQMD and UrQMD transport approaches in order to obtain model independent results. We find that both clustering procedures give very similar results for the deuteron observables in the UrQMD as well as in the PHQMD environment. This confirms that our solution for the 'ice in the fire' puzzle is common to MST and coalescence and independent of the transport approach.

## Key claim

The Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular-Dynamics (PHQMD) approach, using either Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) or coalescence cluster identification, reproduces experimental cluster yields and transverse momentum spectra across a wide energy range, offering an explanation for the 'ice in the fire' puzzle: deuterons are produced at smaller transverse distances than free nucleons and remain spatially separated from the expanding fireball, which may prevent their destruction.

## Method

The authors employed the PHQMD microscopic n-body transport model, which propagates baryonic degrees of freedom with density-dependent two-body potential interactions, and identified clusters dynamically using the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) algorithm in coordinate space with a distance cutoff of r0 = 4 fm. For comparison, deuteron production was also studied using a coalescence procedure implemented in both PHQMD and the Ultra-relativistic-Quantum-Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) transport approach, where deuterons are formed at freeze-out when the relative distance and momentum of a proton-neutron pair fall below coalescence parameters. The PHQMD results were compared with experimental data from NA49, STAR, and PHENIX collaborations for central Au+Au collisions at various energies.

## Key numbers

* Proton dN/dy excitation function for central Au+Au collisions (5% most central) reproduced from lowest SPS energies up to highest RHIC energies, with PHQMD results shown at t = 50, 60, and 70 fm/c
* Deuteron dN/dy for central Au+Au collisions (7% most central) reproduced across the same energy range, compared with NA49 data at sqrt(s_NN) = 6.3, 7.6, 8.8, 12.3, and 17.3 GeV
* Antideuteron to antiproton ratio overestimated by about a factor of two compared to STAR data, while the shape of the sqrt(s_NN)-dependence is well reproduced
* PHQMD reproduces p_T distributions of p, d, t, ^3He, and ^4He for Au+Au central collisions at sqrt(s) = 3 GeV for different rapidity bins, with results shown at t = 60 fm/c
* For hypernuclei at sqrt(s_NN) = 3 GeV, PHQMD overestimates the differential yield of ^3_Lambda H but reproduces the yield of ^4_Lambda H, with results shown at t = 80, 85, and 90 fm/c
* Transverse spatial distributions show deuterons remain at smaller radial distances than free nucleons; the maxima of distributions shift by approximately 20 fm/c between 30 fm/c and 70 fm/c

## Context

This work extends the PHQMD approach (Aichelin 2019, Glassel 2021) for cluster and hypernuclei production at midrapidity, comparing with experimental data from NA49 (Anticic 2016), STAR (Adam 2019, STAR 2021), and PHENIX (2003) collaborations. The study builds on previous comparisons of MST and coalescence methods (Kireyeu 2022) and demonstrates that both clustering procedures give very similar results for deuteron observables in both PHQMD and UrQMD environments, confirming that the solution to the 'ice in the fire' puzzle is independent of the transport approach. The results enable further studies of hypernucleus production in heavy-ion collisions, particularly for the upcoming FAIR experiment, and provide a foundation for investigating the role of the Lambda N potential in hypernuclei formation.
