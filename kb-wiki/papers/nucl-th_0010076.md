# Comment on New Results for the Charged Pion Electromagnetic Form Factor

> In the paper nucl-ex/0010009 by The Jefferson Lab F_pi Collaboration the new results for the charged pion electromagnetic form factor are presented. The approach given in our paper hep-ph/9811318, "On a possible estimation of the constituent--quark parameters from Jefferson Lab experiments on the pion form factor" makes it possible to estimate the mass of constituent quark from such kind of experiment. Now this estimation gives M=0.21 GeV.

## Key claim

The charged pion electromagnetic form factor measured by the Jefferson Lab Fπ Collaboration in the momentum transfer region Q² = 0.6–1.6 (GeV/c)² is larger than previously assumed, and this new data allows the authors to refine their earlier estimate of the constituent quark mass from M = 0.22 GeV to M = 0.21 GeV.

## Method

The authors use a relativistic constituent quark model (CQM) in one of its relativistic forms to calculate the pion electromagnetic form factor. They fix the model parameters by requiring that calculations for any quark interaction model reproduce the experimentally measured mean square radius ⟨rπ²⟩^{1/2}_{exp} and the lepton decay constant fπ_exp, which are not influenced by the new data. The form factor curves are then computed for different quark interaction models and different constituent quark masses, and the group of curves that best describes the new Jefferson Lab data determines the constituent quark mass.

## Key numbers

- The momentum transfer region of the new Jefferson Lab measurements is Q² = 0.6–1.6 (GeV/c)².
- The constituent quark mass estimated from the new data is M = 0.21 GeV.
- The previous estimate from the authors' earlier work (hep-ph/9811318) was M = 0.22 GeV.

## Context

This comment extends the authors' earlier work (Krutov and Troitsky, hep-ph/9811318) on estimating constituent quark parameters from Jefferson Lab experiments on the pion form factor. The new Jefferson Lab Fπ Collaboration data (nucl-ex/0010009) provides greater accuracy than previous measurements, allowing a more precise determination of the constituent quark mass. The result enables further refinement of constituent quark model parameters and tests of the relativistic CQM approach against high-precision experimental data.
