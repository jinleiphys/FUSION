# Beyond the Wigner Distribution: Schr{ö}dinger Equations and Terrace Width Distributions

> The so-called generalized Wigner distribution has earlier been shown to be an excellent approximation for the terrace width distribution (TWD) of vicinal surfaces characterized by step-step interactions that are perpendicular to the average step direction and fall off as the inverse square of the step spacing. In this paper we show that the generalized Wigner distribution corresponds to a mean-field treatment of a single step, discuss generalizations to more general step-step interactions, and show that the mean-field predictions are in good agreement with TWDs derived from numerical transfer-matrix calculations and Monte Carlo simulations. We also show that this mean-field treatment allows the step-step interaction to be extracted from experimental TWDs.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the generalized Wigner distribution for terrace width distributions (TWDs) on vicinal surfaces can be derived from a mean-field treatment of a single step, modeled as a two-particle Calogero model with a position-dependent pressure from neighboring steps, and that this approach works for step-step interactions beyond the inverse-square form.

## Method

The authors map the vicinal surface steps to worldlines of spinless fermions in one spatial dimension (the Calogero-Sutherland model). They then derive a Schrödinger equation for the TWD by considering only two adjacent fermions explicitly and modeling the effect of other fermions through a position-dependent pressure, which yields a quadratic confining potential. This Schrödinger equation is solved for the ground state wavefunction, whose square gives the TWD, and the method is extended to step-step interactions of the form \(V(L) = A_3 L^{-3}\) and oscillatory potentials by solving the same Schrödinger equation numerically and comparing with Monte Carlo simulations and transfer-matrix calculations.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless interaction strength is defined as \(\tilde{A} \equiv \tilde{\beta}A/(k_B T)^2\), where \(\tilde{\beta}\) is step stiffness, \(k_B\) is Boltzmann's constant, and \(T\) is temperature.
- The TWD can be calculated exactly for \(\tilde{A} = -1/4\), 0, or 2.
- The relationship between \(\tilde{A}\) and the Wigner exponent \(\varrho\) is \(\tilde{A} = (\varrho/2)(\varrho/2 - 1)\).
- The ground state eigenvalue is \(\tilde{E}_0 = (\varrho+1)b_\varrho\), where \(b_\varrho = [\Gamma((\varrho+2)/2)/\Gamma((\varrho+1)/2)]^2\).
- For Cu surfaces vicinal to (001), experimental TWDs were fitted with mean step separations from 5.44\(a\) to 9.53\(a\) (lattice constant \(a = 0.255\) nm) and temperatures from 285 K to 360 K.

## Context

This work extends the generalized Wigner distribution approach previously applied to vicinal surfaces (Einstein 2001, Giesen 2000) by providing a formal derivation from a mean-field Schrödinger equation, rather than relying on the three exactly solvable cases of the Calogero-Sutherland model (Calogero 1969, Sutherland 1971). The method enables extraction of step-step interaction potentials from experimental TWDs, going beyond the inverse-square interaction form, and is tested against Monte Carlo simulations and transfer-matrix calculations. The authors note that their fitted potentials for Cu(001) surfaces differ from those derived from empirical many-body potential simulations (Raouafi, Barreteau, Desjonqueres), suggesting over-fitting issues that require further work.
