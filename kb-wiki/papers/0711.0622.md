# Excited hadrons on the lattice - State of the art and future challenges

> We review the techniques of lattice QCD calculations for excited hadrons with light quarks and outline the future challenges that are faced in calculations with fully dynamical fermions.

## Key claim
Lattice QCD calculations for excited hadrons are technically feasible using the variational method with a basis of interpolators, but definitive results for the Roper resonance (N(1440)) remain elusive, and the quenched approximation is a major unknown in current results.

## Method
The paper reviews the variational method for extracting excited-state masses from a matrix of Euclidean correlators built from multiple hadron interpolators with the same quantum numbers. Interpolators are constructed using Jacobi smeared quark sources (allowing radial wave functions with nodes) and covariant derivatives (for p-wave type wave functions). The generalized eigenvalue problem \(C(t) \vec{v}^{(n)} = \lambda(t)^{(n)} C(t_0) \vec{v}^{(n)}\) is solved, where the ordered eigenvalues behave as \(\lambda^{(n)}(t) = e^{-(t-t_0)M_n}[1+\mathcal{O}(e^{-(t-t_0)\Delta_n})]\), separating ground and excited states into individual channels for stable two-parameter fits.

## Key numbers
- For pseudoscalar and vector mesons in a quenched calculation, ground state masses are correct within 5 percent (10% for the \(\rho\)-meson).
- For positive parity baryons in a quenched calculation, ground state masses deviate by less than 5 percent from experimental numbers, except the \(\Delta^{++}\) ground state which comes out about 20% too high.
- Excited positive parity baryon states (Roper-like) come out typically 25% too high in the quenched calculation.
- Negative parity baryon states come out "on top of the experimental numbers" in the quenched calculation.

## Context
This review extends the early systematic lattice explorations of hadronic excitations from about 2002 (earlyexcite) and the variational method developed by Michael (1985) and Lüscher & Wolff (1990). The work contrasts with alternative extraction methods (otherextract) that the author judges as not reaching the quality of the variational method. The paper enables future fully dynamical simulations with light quarks, where the mixing of scattering and bound states must be disentangled using finite volume analysis (scattertechniques), and enables calculation of matrix elements for excited states (e.g., the decay constant for \(\pi(1300)\) from pidecay).
