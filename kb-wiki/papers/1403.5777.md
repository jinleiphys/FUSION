# Finite size effects in Neutron Star and Nuclear matter simulations

> In this work we study molecular dynamics simulations of symmetric nuclear matter using a semi-classical nucleon interaction model. We show that, at sub-saturation densities and low temperatures, the solutions are non-homogeneous structures reminiscent of the ``nuclear pasta'' phases expected in Neutron Star Matter simulations, but shaped by artificial aspects of the simulations. We explore different geometries for the periodic boundary conditions imposed on the simulation cell: cube, hexagonal prism and truncated octahedron. We find that different cells may yield different solutions for the same physical conditions (i.e. density and temperature). The particular shape of the solution at a given density can be predicted analytically by energy minimization. We also show that even if this behavior is due to finite size effects, it does not mean that it vanishes for very large systems and it actually is independent of the system size: The system size sets the only characteristic length scale for the inhomogeneities. We then include a screened Coulomb interaction, as a model of Neutron Star Matter, and perform simulations in the three cell geometries. In this case, the competition between competing interactions of different range produces the well known nuclear pasta, with (in most cases) several structures per cell. However, we find that the results are affected by finite size in different ways depending on the geometry of the cell. In particular, at the same physical conditions and system size, the hexagonal prism yields a single structure per cell while the cubic and truncated octahedron show consistent results with more than one structure per cell. In this case, the results in every cell are expected to converge for systems much larger than the characteristic length scale that arises from the competing interactions.

## Key claim

The paper shows that in molecular dynamics simulations of symmetric nuclear matter without Coulomb interaction at sub-saturation densities and low temperatures, the non-homogeneous structures that appear are shaped by artificial aspects of the simulation cell geometry and periodic boundary conditions, not by the physics of nuclear matter itself. The authors claim that even when a screened Coulomb interaction is added to model neutron star matter, the results remain affected by finite size effects in ways that depend on the geometry of the simulation cell.

## Method

The authors performed classical molecular dynamics simulations using the CMD semi-classical nucleon interaction model (Pandharipande potentials with Medium parametrization) for symmetric nuclear matter at nearly zero temperature (0.001 MeV). They simulated systems with 1728 and 4096 nucleons at three sub-saturation densities (0.05, 0.08, and 0.10 fm^-3) using three different periodic boundary condition cell geometries: cube, hexagonal prism, and truncated octahedron. For the neutron star matter simulations, they added a screened Coulomb potential with a screening length of 20 fm and used the first image convention for force evaluation.

## Key numbers

- Equilibrium density of symmetric nuclear matter in the CMD model: rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3
- Binding energy at equilibrium: E(rho_0) = 16 MeV/nucleon
- Compressibility: approximately 250 MeV
- Cutoff radius for nuclear potentials: r_c = 5.4 fm
- Screening length for Coulomb interaction: lambda = 20 fm
- Simulation temperatures: T = 0.1 MeV then cooled to T = 0.001 MeV
- System sizes: A = 1728 and A = 4096 nucleons
- Three simulation densities: rho = 0.05, 0.08, and 0.10 fm^-3
- Surface to volume ratio for unit volume: cube = 6, hexagonal prism approximately 5.72, truncated octahedron approximately 5.31
- Ratio of circumsphere to inscribed sphere radii: cube approximately 1.73, hexagonal prism approximately 1.53, truncated octahedron approximately 1.29

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous findings (Gimenez Molinelli and Dorso, 2014) that symmetric nuclear matter without Coulomb interaction produces "pseudo-pasta" structures that are minimal surface configurations under periodic boundary conditions, similar to those found in Lennard-Jones fluids by MacDowell et al. (2006) and Binder et al. (2009, 2012). The paper challenges the interpretation of nuclear pasta structures found in earlier simulations by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Williams and Koonin (1985), who used Wigner-Seitz approximations and cubic cells respectively, by demonstrating that the choice of simulation cell geometry can determine which structure appears. The results enable future work to account for finite size effects when interpreting neutron star matter simulations and suggest that the truncated octahedron cell may be the least biasing option for such studies.
