# Origin of $2_1^+$ Excitation Energy Dependence on Valence Nucleon Numbers

> It has been shown recently that a simple formula in terms of the valence nucleon numbers and the mass number can describe the essential trends of excitation energies of the first $2^+$ states in even-even nuclei. By evaluating the first order energy shift due to the zero-range residual interaction, we find that the factor which reflects the effective particle number participating in the interaction from the Fermi orbit governs the main dependence of the first $2^+$ excitation energy on the valence nucleon numbers.

# Origin of $2_1^+$ Excitation Energy Dependence on Valence Nucleon Numbers

## Key claim

The first-order energy shift from a zero-range residual interaction, evaluated via simple perturbation theory, reproduces the measured difference between twice the pairing gap and the $2_1^+$ excitation energy, and its variation follows the effective particle number $(2j_f+1)^2 u_{j_f}^2 v_{j_f}^2$ from the Fermi orbit. This effective particle number, rather than the full interaction matrix element, governs the dependence of $E_x(2_1^+)$ on valence nucleon numbers $N_p$ and $N_n$ as expressed in the empirical formula $E_x = \alpha A^{-\gamma} + \beta[\exp(-\lambda N_p) + \exp(-\lambda N_n)]$.

## Method

The authors calculated the first-order energy shift $E^{(1)}$ of the lowest two-quasiparticle state coupled to $J^\pi = 2^+$ using a zero-range residual interaction $\hat{V}(1,2) = -v_0 \delta^3(\vec{r}_1 - \vec{r}_2)$ with a single free strength parameter $v_0$. Single-particle wave functions and energies were generated from a Woods-Saxon potential ($V_0 = 50$ MeV, $R_0 = 1.27$ fm, $a = 0.67$ fm), and occupation probabilities $v_{j_k}^2$ were obtained by solving BCS equations with a monopole pairing interaction whose strength $G$ was adjusted to reproduce empirical gap energies from the three-point binding-energy formula. The calculated $-E^{(1)}$ was compared to the measured difference $2\Delta - E_x(2_1^+)$ for four isotopic/isotonic chains, and the expression was reduced analytically for the case where both quasiparticles occupy the same Fermi orbit with angular momentum $j_f$.

## Key numbers

* The empirical formula parameters fitted from data are $\alpha = 34.9$ MeV, $\beta = 1.00$ MeV, $\gamma = 1.19$, and $\lambda = 0.36$.
* The zero-range interaction strength was fixed at $v_0 = 1,700$ MeV fm$^3$, chosen so that the calculated $-E^{(1)}$ exactly matches the measured $2\Delta - E_x(2_1^+)$ for the heaviest lead isotope $^{184}_{82}$Pb.
* The Woods-Saxon potential parameters used are $V_0 = 50$ MeV, $R_0 = 1.27$ fm, and $a = 0.67$ fm.
* The radial integral $\langle R^4 \rangle$ and the Clebsch-Gordan coefficient $\langle j_f \frac{1}{2} j_f -\frac{1}{2} | 2,0 \rangle$ appear in the reduced expression for $E^{(1)}$ when both quasiparticles occupy the Fermi orbit.

## Context

This work extends the empirical $N_p N_n$ scheme pioneered by Hamamoto (1965) and Casten (1985, 1996), who showed that collective nuclear properties scale with the product of valence proton and neutron numbers. The authors previously proposed a simple formula in terms of $N_p$, $N_n$, and mass number $A$ (Ha and Cha, 2007) that reproduces $E_x(2_1^+)$ systematics across the periodic table. The present paper provides a microscopic justification for that formula by showing that the dominant dependence on valence nucleon numbers arises from the effective particle number $(2j_f+1)^2 u_{j_f}^2 v_{j_f}^2$ in a first-order perturbation calculation. This enables future work to connect phenomenological systematics to BCS-based descriptions of pairing and residual interactions in even-even nuclei.
