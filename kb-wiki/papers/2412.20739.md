# Machine learning orbital-free density functional theory: taming quantum shell effects in deformed nuclei

> Accurate description of deformed atomic nuclei by the orbital-free density functional theory has been a longstanding textbook challenge, due to the difficulty in accounting for the intricate quantum shell effects that are present in such systems. Orbital-free density functional theory is, in principle, capable of describing all effects of nuclear systems, as guaranteed by the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem. However, from a microscopic perspective, shell and deformation effects are believed to be intrinsically connected to single-orbital structures, posing a significant challenge for orbital-free approaches. Here, we develop a machine learning approach to the orbital-free density functional theory, which is capable of achieving a high level of accuracy in describing the ground-state properties and potential energy curves for both spherical $^{16}$O and deformed $^{20}$Ne nuclei. This is the inaugural instance where a fully orbital-free energy density functional has succeeded in taming the complex shell effects in deformed nuclei. It demonstrates that the orbital-free energy density functional, which is directly based on the Hohenberg-Kohn theorem, is not only a theoretical concept but also a practical one for nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a machine-learned orbital-free energy density functional can, for the first time, accurately reproduce the ground-state properties and potential energy curves of both spherical $^{16}$O and deformed $^{20}$Ne nuclei, including quantum shell and deformation effects that previous orbital-free approaches could not capture. The claim is hedged by the fact that the functional is trained on data generated from Kohn-Sham calculations for these two specific nuclei, and its generalization to other deformed nuclei remains to be tested.

## Method

The authors used kernel ridge regression (KRR) to learn the map from nucleon density to the sum of kinetic and spin-orbit energies, while the interaction energy was taken from the Skyrme functional SkP. Training data (24,000 samples) were generated by solving Schrodinger equations with randomly generated mean potentials (spherical plus quadrupole components) and spin-orbit potentials for $^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne. The resulting orbital-free energy density functional was then used in self-consistent calculations (unconstrained and constrained) to find ground-state densities and potential energy curves via gradient descent.

## Key numbers

- The KRR training achieved root-mean-square deviations of kinetic and spin-orbit energies between 5 keV and 25 keV, depending on quadrupole deformation $\beta_2$, for both validation and test sets.
- For $^{16}$O, the ML orbital-free DFT gave $\beta_2 = 0.00$ (0.03), $R_m = 2.82$ fm (0.02), and $E_{\rm tot} = -127.40$ MeV (0.13), compared to Kohn-Sham values of $\beta_2 = 0.00$, $R_m = 2.81$ fm, and $E_{\rm tot} = -127.45$ MeV.
- For $^{20}$Ne, the ML orbital-free DFT gave $\beta_2 = 0.49$ (0.04), $R_m = 3.05$ fm (0.02), and $E_{\rm tot} = -156.02$ MeV (0.35), compared to Kohn-Sham values of $\beta_2 = 0.48$, $R_m = 3.02$ fm, and $E_{\rm tot} = -156.58$ MeV.
- The hyperparameters were $\lambda = 2.19 \times 10^{-6}$ and $\sigma = 1.86$ fm$^{-2}$.
- The training set contained 20,000 data samples, with 2,000 each for validation and test sets.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous machine-learning orbital-free DFT for spherical nuclei (Wu 2022) to deformed systems, directly addressing the longstanding challenge that semiclassical Thomas-Fermi and extended Thomas-Fermi approaches (Brack 1985, Centelles 2007) always yield spherical ground states and lack quantum shell effects. The approach enables quantitative orbital-free DFT calculations for deformed nuclei, opening the door to studying nuclear systems without solving Kohn-Sham equations, and could be extended to heavier deformed nuclei and fission studies.
