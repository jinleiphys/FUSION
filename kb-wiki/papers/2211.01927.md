# Modeling Solids in Nuclear Astrophysics with Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics

> Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is a frequently applied tool in computational astrophysics to solve the fluid dynamics equations governing the systems under study. For some problems, for example when involving asteroids and asteroid impacts, the additional inclusion of material strength is necessary in order to accurately describe the dynamics. In compact stars, that is white dwarfs and neutron stars, solid components are also present. Neutron stars have a solid crust which is the strongest material known in nature. However, their dynamical evolution, when modeled via SPH or other computational fluid dynamics codes, is usually described as a purely fluid dynamics problem. Here, we present the first 3D simulations of neutron-star crustal toroidal oscillations including material strength with the Los Alamos National Laboratory SPH code FleCSPH. In the first half of the paper, we present the numerical implementation of solid material modeling together with standard tests. The second half is on the simulation of crustal oscillations in the fundamental toroidal mode. Here, we dedicate a large fraction of the paper to approaches which can suppress numerical noise in the solid. If not minimized, the latter can dominate the crustal motion in the simulations.

## Key claim

The authors present the first 3D simulations of neutron-star crustal toroidal oscillations that include material strength using the SPH code FleCSPH, but a large fraction of the paper is dedicated to approaches for suppressing numerical noise in the solid, because if not minimized such noise can dominate the crustal motion in the simulations.

## Method

The authors implemented an elastic-perfectly plastic (EPP) strength model with a maximum-strain damage model in the Los Alamos National Laboratory SPH code FleCSPH, using Hooke's Law for elasticity and the von-Mises yield criterion for plasticity. They verified the solid material implementation through three standard benchmark tests (colliding rubber rings, Verney implosion, and Taylor anvil impact) before applying the code to simulate fundamental toroidal mode oscillations of a neutron-star crust modeled as a solid shell.

## Key numbers

- Colliding rubber rings: density 1.01 g/cm^3, sound speed 8.54 x 10^4 cm/s, shear modulus 0.22 x rho_0 x c_0^2, collision velocity 0.118 c_0 (each ring 0.059 c_0), inner radius 3 cm, outer radius 4 cm, 4480 particles with 0.1 cm spacing
- 2D Verney implosion: beryllium shell with density 1.845 g/cm^3, inner radius 8 cm, thickness 2 cm, initial radial velocity with v_0 = 4.902 x 10^4 cm/s and alpha = 1, final inner radius 4 cm, stopping time 137 microseconds when 99.9% of kinetic energy is dissipated
- Beryllium strength parameters: shear modulus 1.5111 x 10^12 Ba, yield stress 3.30 x 10^9 Ba
- Mie-Gruneisen EoS parameters for beryllium: rho_0 = 1.85 g/cm^3, c_0 = 7.998 x 10^5 cm/s, Gamma = 1.16, s = 1.124

## Context

This work extends previous SPH solid modeling approaches such as those by Libersky (1991) and Benz (1995) to the astrophysical context of neutron-star crust dynamics, which had previously been treated as purely fluid in simulations by Rosswog (2021), Bernuzzi (2020), and others. The implementation enables future fully dynamical simulations of neutron-star mergers that include crustal effects such as interface-mode oscillations during inspiral (Pan 2020) and possible crust shattering (Tsang 2012, Neill 2020), which could modify gravitational wave signals.
