# Cumulative production of pions by heavy baryonic resonances in proton-nucleus collisions

> Pion production in proton-nucleus (p+A) collisions outside the kinematical boundary of proton-nucleon (p+N) reactions, the so-called cumulative effect, is studied. Restrictions from energy-momentum conservation on the energy of pions emitted in the backward direction in the target rest frame are analyzed. It is assumed that the cumulative pions are produced in p+A reactions by heavy baryonic resonances. The baryonic resonances are first created in p+N reactions. Due to successive collisions with nuclear nucleons the masses of these resonances may then increase and, simultaneously, their longitudinal velocities decrease. We also use the Ultra relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics model to reveal the key role of successive collisions of baryonic resonances with nuclear nucleons for cumulative pion production in p+A reactions. Further experimental studies of cumulative hadron production in p+A reactions at high collision energies are needed to search for heavy hadron-like objects and investigate their properties.

## Key claim

The paper argues that cumulative pions (pions emitted beyond the kinematic limit of proton-nucleon collisions) in proton-nucleus reactions can be produced by heavy baryonic resonances that gain mass and lose longitudinal velocity through successive collisions with nuclear nucleons, rather than requiring pre-existing multi-nucleon clusters or exotic nuclear effects. The authors qualify that this mechanism is a very rare process and that the probability to reach the optimal mass and velocity values in actual p+A reactions is very small.

## Method

The authors first derive analytic kinematic constraints from energy-momentum conservation for backward (180-degree) pion production in p+N reactions, establishing the maximal pion energy Eπ* as a function of projectile momentum p0. They then extend this analysis to successive collisions of a baryonic resonance with n nuclear nucleons, deriving the maximal pion energy Eπ,n* after n collisions and the corresponding resonance masses Mn* and velocities vn*. Finally, they perform simulations using the Ultra relativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) model (version 3.4) for p+p and p+A reactions at various beam momenta, comparing the simulated pion energy spectra to experimental data from Baldin (1974) and others.

## Key numbers

- The maximal pion energy in p+N reactions at p0=6 GeV/c is Eπ* ≈ 0.38 GeV; at p0=8.4 GeV/c it is Eπ* ≈ 0.41 GeV; these are about two times smaller than values observed in p+A data from Baldin (1974).
- The upper limit of Eπ* in p+N reactions as p0 → ∞ is (mN² + mπ²)/(2mN) ≈ 0.48 GeV.
- The threshold projectile momentum for backward pion production in p+N reactions is p0 ≈ 0.83 GeV/c.
- At p0 ≥ 20 GeV/c, the optimal resonance mass M1* for maximal pion energy in a single p+N collision satisfies M1* ≥ 4.3 GeV/c².
- After n=6 successive collisions at p0=6 GeV/c, the maximal pion energy Eπ,6* reaches approximately 1.6 GeV (from Fig. 4, reading the curve for n=6 at p0=6 GeV/c).
- In UrQMD simulations of p+p reactions at p0=158 GeV/c, the maximal observed pion energy Eπmax ≈ 0.45 GeV, smaller than the kinematic limit Eπ* = 0.48 GeV, because the required resonance mass M1* ≈ 12.2 GeV/c² exceeds the resonance mass limit in UrQMD.
- For a fixed upper resonance mass limit Mmax = 3.5 GeV/c², the maximal pion energy Eπmax becomes approximately constant at large p0.

## Context

This work extends the fireball model of Gorenstein (1977) for cumulative particle production, which proposed that massive hadronic fireballs formed in successive collisions could explain the effect. The authors contrast their approach with models invoking pre-existing multi-nucleon "grains" or "fluctons" (Baldin 1977, Burov 1977) and with parton-based calculations (Efremov 1988). The analysis enables future experimental searches for heavy hadron-like objects in p+A reactions at high collision energies, as the mechanism predicts that cumulative pions originate from baryonic resonances with masses far exceeding those accessible in elementary p+N collisions.
