# Splintering central nuclear collisions with a momentum-dependent lattice Hamiltonian theory

> We formulate a lattice Hamiltonian model for intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. Our approach incorporates a momentum-dependent nuclear mean field that yields an optical potential that agrees with proton-nucleus experiments and that also receives support from measurements involving heavy ions. We emphasize the precision of our numerical results in connection with energy and momentum conservation. We first outline the static properties of our solutions, after which we consider recent stopping power results for heavy ion collisions. The results obtained with different types of nuclear mean fields are compared with each other. Consequences of the stopping power data on the nuclear equation of state are outlined.

## Key claim

The paper formulates a lattice Hamiltonian model for intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions that incorporates a momentum-dependent nuclear mean field, but the key hedge is that the model's success in reproducing experimental stopping-power data depends on the specific choice of mean-field potential and the numerical method (Lattice Hamiltonian vs. test-particle) used; the MDYI potential combined with the Lattice Hamiltonian algorithm gives satisfactory agreement with data, while other combinations fail.

## Method

The authors solve the Vlasov and Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) equations using a lattice Hamiltonian approach (LHV/LHB) and compare it to the standard test-particle method (TPV/TPB). The nuclear mean field is implemented with a momentum-dependent MDYI potential, a simplified momentum-independent potential (H), and a second momentum-dependent potential (GBD); the equations of motion are derived from a discretized Hamiltonian on a six-dimensional phase-space lattice. Ground-state nuclei are initialized with neutron and proton radii from parameterizations by Warda (1999) and Sobotka (1999), and the model is applied to simulate stopping-power measurements for 40Ar beams on Cu, Ag, and Au targets at beam energies from 8 to 115 MeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear compressibility K = 215 MeV for the MDYI and GBD potentials; K = 380 MeV for the momentum-independent H potential.
- Ground-state binding energy per nucleon E/A(rho0) = -16 MeV, with single-particle potential U(rho0, p=0) = -75 MeV and U(rho0, p^2/2m = 300 MeV) = 0.
- Effective mass at the Fermi surface m*/m = 0.67; U(rho0, p -> infinity) = 30.5 MeV.
- For a single 208Pb nucleus at t = 100 fm/c with momentum-dependent potential, TPV (n=1) gains ~6.5 MeV/nucleon, TPV (n=2) gains ~71 MeV/nucleon, while LHV (n=1) gains ~730 keV/nucleon and LHV (n=2) gains ~860 keV/nucleon.
- For colliding 208Pb+208Pb at Ek/A = 50 MeV with momentum-dependent potential, LHV gains no more than ~1 MeV/nucleon; TPV (n=1) gains ~9 MeV/nucleon, TPV (n=2) gains ~80 MeV/nucleon.
- For Pb+Pb at Ek/A = 50 MeV with hard scattering included, LHB gains ~1 MeV/nucleon; TPB gains ~6 MeV/nucleon.
- Pauli blocking efficiency for ground-state nuclei is ~95%; collisions with center-of-mass kinetic energy below ~10 MeV are set to a constant cross section of 150 mb.

## Context

This work extends the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) model of Bertsch and Das Gupta (1988) and the lattice Hamiltonian algorithm of Lenk and Pandharipande (1989) by incorporating a momentum-dependent mean field (MDYI potential from Welke et al., 1988) that reproduces proton-nucleus optical potential data. The study compares its results to stopping-power measurements from the Michigan State University K1200 cyclotron (Danielewicz et al., 1999) and finds that the MDYI potential with the lattice Hamiltonian method gives the best overall description of both remnant mass and velocity distributions. The approach enables future investigations of balance energy and possible in-medium modifications of nucleon-nucleon cross sections, building on prior flow analyses by Zhang et al. (1994) and giant monopole resonance studies by Blaizot et al. (1995).
