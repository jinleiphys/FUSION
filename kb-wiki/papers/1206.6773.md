# Crossover from vibrational to rotational collectivity in heavy nuclei in the shell-model Monte Carlo approach

> Heavy nuclei exhibit a crossover from vibrational to rotational collectivity as the number of neutrons or protons increases from shell closure towards midshell, but the microscopic description of this crossover has been a major challenge. We apply the shell model Monte Carlo approach to families of even-even samarium and neodymium isotopes and identify a microscopic signature of the crossover from vibrational to rotational collectivity in the low-temperature behavior of $<\mathbf{J}^2>_T$, where $\bf J$ is the total spin and $T$ is the temperature. This signature agrees well with its values extracted from experimental data. We also calculate the state densities of these nuclei and find them to be in very good agreement with experimental data. Finally, we define a collective enhancement factor from the ratio of the total state density to the intrinsic state density as calculated in the finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation. The decay of this enhancement factor with excitation energy is found to correlate with the pairing and shape phase transitions in these nuclei.

## Key claim
The shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method can describe the crossover from vibrational to rotational collectivity in heavy even-even samarium and neodymium isotopes using a truncated spherical shell model, as identified by the low-temperature behavior of the thermal observable ⟨J²⟩_T. This signature agrees well with experimental values, but the description is limited to the specific model space and interaction parameters chosen, and the crossover is demonstrated only for these isotopic families.

## Method
The authors applied the shell-model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method to families of even-even samarium (¹⁴⁸–¹⁵⁴Sm) and neodymium (¹⁴⁴–¹⁵²Nd) isotopes using a configuration-interaction shell-model Hamiltonian with a single-particle model space larger than one major shell for both protons and neutrons. The effective interaction included monopole pairing and multipole-multipole terms (quadrupole, octupole, and hexadecupole), with pairing strengths parametrized by a suppression factor γ and multipole strengths by renormalization factors k_λ. The low-temperature behavior of ⟨J²⟩_T was computed and compared to analytical formulas for vibrational and rotational bands, and total state densities were calculated and compared to experimental level counting data, neutron resonance data, and backshifted Bethe formula parametrizations.

## Key numbers
- For ¹⁴⁸Sm, fitting the vibrational band formula to SMMC results gave E_{2+}^{vib} = 0.538 ± 0.031 MeV, consistent with the experimental value of 0.550 MeV.
- For ¹⁵⁴Sm, fitting the rotational band formula gave E_{2+}^{rot} = 0.087 ± 0.006 MeV, consistent with the experimental value of 0.082 MeV.
- For ¹⁴⁴Nd, fitting the vibrational band formula gave E_{2+}^{vib} = 0.702 ± 0.062 MeV, consistent with the experimental value of 0.697 MeV.
- For ¹⁵⁰Nd, fitting the rotational band formula gave E_{2+}^{rot} = 0.132 ± 0.012 MeV, consistent with the experimental value of 0.130 MeV.
- For ¹⁵²Nd, fitting the rotational band formula gave E_{2+}^{rot} = 0.107 ± 0.006 MeV, compared to the experimental value of 0.073 MeV.
- The pairing coupling strengths were parametrized as g_ν = γ · ḡ_ν with ḡ_p = 10.9/Z and ḡ_n = 10.9/N, and the suppression factor γ was parametrized as γ = 0.72 − 0.5/[(N−90)²+5.3].
- The quadrupole renormalization factor was parametrized as k_2 = 2.15 + 0.0025(N−87)².
- For ¹⁵⁰Sm, the shape transition to a spherical shape occurred at E_x ≈ 12.5 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the SMMC method previously applied to the well-deformed nucleus ¹⁶²Dy by Alhassid et al. (2008) to families of even-even samarium and neodymium isotopes, demonstrating that a truncated spherical shell model can describe the vibrational-to-rotational crossover. The approach enables microscopic calculation of collective enhancement factors, defined as the ratio of total SMMC state density to intrinsic state density from the finite-temperature Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) approximation, which had previously been treated only with phenomenological formulas in works such as Hansen and Jensen (1983), Ignatyuk (1985), and the RIPL database. The results enable future microscopic studies of the decay of collectivity with excitation energy, which is important for understanding fission reactions.
