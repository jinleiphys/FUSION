# Effects of the liquid-gas phase transition and cluster formation on the symmetry energy

> Various definitions of the symmetry energy are introduced for nuclei, dilute nuclear matter below saturation density and stellar matter, which is found in compact stars or core-collapse supernovae. The resulting differences are exemplified by calculations in a theoretical approach based on a generalized relativistic density functional for dense matter. It contains nucleonic clusters as explicit degrees of freedom with medium dependent properties that are derived for light clusters from a quantum statistical approach. With such a model the dissolution of clusters at high densities can be described. The effects of the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter and of cluster formation in stellar matter on the density dependence of the symmetry energy are studied for different temperatures. It is observed that correlations and the formation of inhomogeneous matter at low densities and temperatures causes an increase of the symmetry energy as compared to calculations assuming a uniform uncorrelated spatial distribution of constituent baryons and leptons.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the density dependence of the symmetry energy in dilute nuclear matter and stellar matter is significantly modified by both cluster formation (correlations) and the liquid-gas phase transition, with the effect being that correlations and inhomogeneous matter at low densities and temperatures cause an increase in the symmetry energy compared to calculations assuming a uniform, uncorrelated spatial distribution of baryons and leptons.

## Method

The authors employ a generalized relativistic density functional (gRDF) approach that includes nucleonic clusters (deuterons, alpha particles, etc.) as explicit degrees of freedom with medium-dependent properties derived from a quantum statistical model. They compute the symmetry energy under various thermodynamic conditions for nuclear matter (without Coulomb interaction) and stellar matter (with electrons and charge neutrality), and compare results obtained using different definitions (second derivative vs. finite difference, free energy vs. internal energy) while accounting for the liquid-gas phase transition via Gibbs conditions.

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature for nuclear matter in the gRDF model without clusters is \(T_{\rm crit} = 13.724\) MeV.
- The critical baryon density for the gRDF model without clusters is \(n_{B}^{(\rm crit)} = 0.04515\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Typical Bethe-Weizsäcker coefficients from fits to nuclear masses are: \(a_V = 15.73\) MeV, \(a_S = 17.77\) MeV, \(a_V^{(\rm sym)} = 26.46\) MeV, \(a_S^{(\rm sym)} = -17.70\) MeV, and \(a_C = 0.709\) MeV (Lun03).
- The drip asymmetry for infinite nuclear matter is \(\delta_{\rm drip} = 0.3632\) using the above parameters.
- For a free Fermi gas with \(C_{\rm kin} = 12\) MeV, \(J = 32\) MeV, and \(\gamma = 1\), the slope parameter is \(L = 84\) MeV.
- The saturation density of nuclear matter is \(n_{\rm sat} \approx 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Typel et al. (2010), Voskresenskaya et al. (2012), and Röpke et al. (2013) on cluster correlations in matter, with particular emphasis on the consequences for the symmetry energy in different systems. The paper contrasts with conventional model calculations where the symmetry energy vanishes linearly with decreasing density, and it addresses experimental results from Kowalski et al. (2007) and Natowitz et al. (2010) that suggest considerably higher symmetry energy values in the low-density limit. The findings enable more accurate modeling of the equation of state for compact stars and core-collapse supernovae, and provide a framework for comparing symmetry energy definitions used in finite nuclei, nuclear matter, and stellar matter.
