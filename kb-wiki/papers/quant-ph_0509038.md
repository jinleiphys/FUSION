# Stochastic mean-field dynamics for fermions in the weak coupling limit

> Assuming that the effect of the residual interaction beyond mean-field is weak and has a short memory time, two approximate treatments of correlation in fermionic systems by means of Markovian quantum jump are presented. A simplified scenario for the introduction of fluctuations beyond mean-field is first presented. In this theory, part of the quantum correlations between the residual interaction and the one-body density matrix are neglected and jumps occur between many-body densities formed of pairs of states $D=| Φ_a > < Φ_b |/< Φ_b . |Φ_a >$ where $| Φ_a >$ and $| Φ_b >$ are antisymmetrized products of single-particle states. The underlying Stochastic Mean-Field (SMF) theory is discussed and applied to the monopole vibration of a spherical $^{40}$Ca nucleus under the influence of a statistical ensemble of two-body contact interaction. This framework is however too simplistic to account for both fluctuation and dissipation. In the second part of this work, an alternative quantum jump method is obtained without making the approximation on quantum correlations. Restricting to two particles-two holes residual interaction, the evolution of the one-body density matrix of a correlated system is transformed into a Lindblad equation. The associated dissipative dynamics can be simulated by quantum jumps between densities written as $D = | Φ>< Φ|$ where $| Φ>$ is a normalized Slater determinant. The associated stochastic Schroedinger equation for single-particle wave-functions is given.

## Key claim

Under the assumption that the residual interaction beyond mean-field is weak and has a short memory time (Markovian limit), the correlated dynamics of fermionic systems can be replaced by quantum jumps between Slater determinants; however, the simplified Stochastic Mean-Field (SMF) theory that neglects part of the quantum correlations can account for fluctuations but not for dissipation, while a more complete treatment that avoids this approximation yields a Lindblad equation that does include dissipative effects.

## Method

The author starts from a perturbative expansion of the many-body wavefunction in the residual two-body interaction and applies the short-memory-time (Markovian) approximation to the autocorrelation function of the residual interaction. In the first approach (SMF), a gaussian approximation for quantal fluctuations is used to derive a stochastic equation for the one-body density matrix that preserves the form of the density as a pair of non-orthogonal Slater determinants. In the second approach, the full evolution of the many-body density is transformed into a Lindblad equation for the one-body density, which is then simulated by a quantum jump process between normalized Slater determinants using a stochastic Schroedinger equation for single-particle wavefunctions.

## Key numbers

- The SMF theory is applied to the monopole vibration of spherical \(^{40}\)Ca with a Skyrme interaction (from Str79) and a contact residual interaction of strength \(g_0 = 500\) MeV.fm, using a collision time \(\tau = 0.01\) fm.
- The mean-field quantal fluctuation width of \(r^2\) is \(\sigma_{MF} = 6.3\) fm\(^2\).
- The dispersion \(\Delta_r\) of the root-mean-square radius is parametrized as \(\Delta_r = \Delta_0 (1-e^{-\Gamma_0 t})\), where \(\Delta_0\) is proportional to \(g_0\) and \(\Gamma_0\) is independent of \(g_0\); values of \(g_0\) tested include 100, 250, and 500 MeV.fm.
- The initial constrained Hartree-Fock calculation uses a monopolar constraint \(\lambda r^2\) with \(\lambda = 0.25\) MeV.fm\(^{-2}\) at \(t<0\) fm/c.

## Context

This work extends earlier stochastic approaches to nuclear many-body dynamics, such as those by Ayik (2001) and Balian and Vénéroni (1981), by providing explicit quantum-jump formulations that preserve the Slater-determinant structure along each trajectory. The SMF theory is shown to reproduce the average TDHF evolution while adding fluctuations, but it lacks the collision term present in extended TDHF (Lacroix, 2004). The second, more complete formulation maps the problem onto a Lindblad equation, enabling the use of established quantum-jump simulation techniques from open quantum systems (Breuer and Petruccione, 2002) for self-interacting fermionic systems, which opens the way to including both fluctuation and dissipation in realistic nuclear dynamics.
