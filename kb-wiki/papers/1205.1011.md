# Possibility of Electroweak Phase Transition at Low Temperature

> We study models of strong first order `low' temperature electroweak phase transition. To achieve this we propose a class of Higgs effective potential models which preserve the known features of the present day massive phase. However, the properties of the symmetry restored massless phase are modified in a way that for a large parameter domain we find a strong first order transition occurring at a temperature hundreds of times lower than previously considered possible.

# Possibility of Electroweak Phase Transition at Low Temperature

## Key claim

The authors propose a class of Higgs effective potential models that preserve the known Higgs vacuum expectation value and mass in the symmetry-broken phase, but modify the potential near the symmetry-restored phase such that a strong first-order electroweak phase transition can occur at temperatures hundreds of times lower than previously considered possible, provided the parameter B is sufficiently large and the condition (k-1)B > 1 is satisfied.

## Method

The authors introduce a family of modified Higgs effective potentials W(h) = f(V(h)) where V(h) is the standard Higgs potential and f(x) = x/(1 + B x/V(0))^k, with parameters B and k controlling the shape away from the vacuum expectation value. They then compute the finite-temperature effective potential by adding free energy contributions from a gas of Standard Model particles (top quark, gauge bosons W± and Z0, bottom and charm quarks, tau lepton, and the Higgs itself) using Bose and Fermi gas free energy density formulas. The phase structure is analyzed by tracking three critical temperatures: T_c1 where the massless phase is restored, T_c2 where the two phases become degenerate (the phase transition temperature), and T_c3 where the massive phase is eliminated.

## Key numbers

- The standard Higgs vacuum expectation value is v0 = 246/√2 GeV, with Higgs mass m_h ≈ 125 GeV
- For B → 0 (standard Higgs potential limit), the three critical temperatures converge at T ≈ 0.63 v0, corresponding to a transformation temperature near 110 GeV
- For k=1 and B=10, a strong first-order phase transition occurs at T = 0.43 v0
- For k=1 and large B > 100, the jump in the Higgs field at the critical temperature Δh/v0 approaches unity
- The transition temperature can be pushed as low as T/v0 = 2×10^(-3) for heavy ion experiments at the LHC, requiring very large B
- The Planck mass to v0 ratio is M_P/v0 = 7×10^16

## Context

This work extends the approach of Grojean (2004) who modified the Higgs potential by adding a (h^2 - v0^2)^3 term to achieve a strong first-order phase transition, but differs by using a non-polynomial modification that primarily affects the potential near the massless phase rather than perturbative properties near v0. The paper builds on lattice simulations by Kajantie (1996), Rummukainen (1998), Csikor (1998), Fodor (1998), and Tsypin (1998) which showed that for the Standard Model Higgs mass in the physical domain there is no phase transition but a crossover. The results enable exploration of electroweak baryogenesis at much lower temperatures than previously considered possible, extending the time span for baryogenesis as discussed by Shaposhnikov (1994), and suggest potential laboratory signatures in relativistic heavy ion collisions through abundant production of top and other massive quarks.
