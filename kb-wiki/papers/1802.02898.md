# Spectroscopic Criteria for Identification of Nuclear Tetrahedral and Octahedral Symmetries: Illustration on a Rare Earth Nucleus

> We formulate criteria for identification of the nuclear tetrahedral and octahedral symmetries and illustrate for the first time their possible realization in a Rare Earth nucleus 152Sm. We use realistic nuclear mean-field theory calculations with the phenomenological macroscopic-microscopic method, the Gogny-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approach and the general point-group theory considerations to guide the experimental identification method as illustrated on published experimental data. Following group-theory the examined symmetries imply the existence of exotic rotational bands on whose properties the spectroscopic identification criteria are based. These bands may contain simultaneously states of even and odd spins, of both parities and parity doublets at well defined spins. In the exact-symmetry limit those bands involve no E2-transitions. We show that coexistence of tetrahedral and octahedral deformations is essential when calculating the corresponding energy minima and surrounding barriers and that it has a characteristic impact on the rotational bands. The symmetries in question imply the existence of long-lived shape-isomers and, possibly, new waiting point nuclei -- impacting the nucleosynthesis processes in astrophysics -- and an existence of 16-fold degenerate particle-hole excitations. Specifically designed experiments which aim at strengthening the identification arguments are briefly discussed.

# Spectroscopic Criteria for Identification of Nuclear Tetrahedral and Octahedral Symmetries: Illustration on a Rare Earth Nucleus

## Key claim

The paper formulates spectroscopic criteria for identifying nuclear tetrahedral and octahedral symmetries and presents evidence that these high-rank symmetries may be realized in the rare earth nucleus 152Sm, but the identification relies on the coexistence of both tetrahedral and octahedral deformations simultaneously, and the experimental data contain uncertainties that require specifically designed experiments to confirm.

## Method

The authors combine group-theoretic analysis of the tetrahedral double group T^D_d and octahedral double group O^D_h with realistic nuclear mean-field calculations using the phenomenological macroscopic-microscopic Woods-Saxon method and the Gogny-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (Gogny-HFB) self-consistent approach. They apply these theoretical predictions to published experimental data on 152Sm, selecting candidate rotational-band levels that satisfy group-theory constraints including the absence of E2 and E1 transitions, and fitting the energies to a parabolic rotational formula E_I ≈ aI^2 + bI + c.

## Key numbers

- The tetrahedral symmetry minimum in 152Sm occurs at (α20, α32) = (0, ±0.12) in the total energy surface.
- Combining tetrahedral and octahedral deformations brings the exotic minimum about 2.5 MeV lower compared to the spherical configuration, with nearly 40% of this effect due to the octahedral component.
- For the octahedral A1g symmetry hypothesis (positive parity states), the root-mean-square deviation between experimental energies and the fitted parabola is 1.6 keV for five levels.
- For the octahedral A2u symmetry hypothesis (negative parity states), the root-mean-square deviation is 7.5 keV for six levels.
- For the tetrahedral A1-type hypothesis (all spins and parities together), the root-mean-square deviation is 83.1 keV for 11 levels.
- The two octahedral parabolas extrapolated to I=0 meet at 1396 keV with a difference of only 0.7 keV.
- The estimated probability of finding 11 randomly distributed levels close to a single parabola with r.m.s. = 83.1 keV is approximately 1.1 × 10^{-14}.
- The estimated probability for five levels within r.m.s. = 1.6 keV is 1.4 × 10^{-15}, and for six levels within r.m.s. = 7.5 keV is 1.6 × 10^{-14}.
- The ground-state minimum energy from the macroscopic-microscopic calculation is E_min = -3.71 MeV for axial hexadecapole deformation, compared to E_min = -2.60 MeV for octahedral deformation.
- The difference between pure axial hexadecapole and octahedral deformation at ground-state amounts to about 1.1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier predictions of tetrahedral symmetry in nuclei by Dudek (2002), who proposed tetrahedral magic numbers, and by Dudek (2010) on high-rank symmetry effects in mean-field contexts. It builds on group-theoretic analyses of nuclear shapes by Dudek (2007) that established the multipole expansions for tetrahedral and octahedral surfaces. The paper addresses the long-standing difficulty that tetrahedral and octahedral symmetries, well known in molecular physics, had remained elusive in heavy nuclear systems like rare earths and actinides because the vanishing quadrupole and dipole moments eliminate the usual E2 and E1 transition signals. The results enable a new experimental identification method for high-rank symmetries and suggest the existence of long-lived shape-isomers that could impact nucleosynthesis processes in astrophysics, though the authors note that specifically designed experiments are needed to confirm the spin-parity assignments and reduce uncertainties in the existing data.
