# Relativistic few-body physics

> I discuss different formulations of the relativistic few-body problem with an emphasis on how they are related. I first discuss the implications of some of the differences with non-relativistic quantum mechanics. Then I point out that the principle of special relativity in quantum mechanics implies that the quantum theory has a Poincaré symmetry, which is realized by a unitary representation of the Poincaré group. This representation can always be decomposed into direct integrals of irreducible representations and the different formulations differ only in how these irreducible representations are realized. I discuss how these representations appear in different formulations of relativistic quantum mechanics and discuss some applications in each of these frameworks.

## Key claim

The paper argues that all formulations of relativistic few-body quantum mechanics are unified by the requirement of a unitary representation of the Poincaré group, and that different formulations differ only in how the irreducible representations of this group are realized in Hilbert space; the abstract glosses over the condition that cluster separability of the unitary representation requires special many-body interactions to avoid observable violations, though these violations appear small for nuclear reactions.

## Method

The paper reviews and compares five formulations of relativistic few-body quantum mechanics: covariant constraint dynamics, direct interaction quantum mechanics, manifestly covariant methods (Bethe-Salpeter equation), quasipotential methods, and Euclidean methods. For each formulation, the author examines how the Hilbert space is represented, how the Poincaré generators are constructed, and how irreducible representations of the Poincaré group appear. The relations between formulations are established through the common underlying decomposition into irreducible representations and through explicit mappings between wave functions and inner products.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Wigner (1939) on unitary representations of the Poincaré group and addresses the cluster separability problem raised by Weinberg, who suggested local field theory as a motivation to avoid the difficulties of constructing many-body interactions. The paper builds on Sokolov's solution for constructing many-body interactions that restore cluster properties, and relates the formulations to applications in constituent quark models, nuclear reactions, and electromagnetic probes of hadronic systems as discussed by Bakker, Karmanov, and Salmé in the same volume. The work enables practitioners to understand equivalences between different relativistic few-body models and to choose appropriate formulations for specific applications.
