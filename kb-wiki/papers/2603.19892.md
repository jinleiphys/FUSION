# Renormalization group evolution and power counting in nuclear matter

> In nuclear matter, for interparticle separations larger than the healing distance (a characteristic long-distance scale of finite-density fermionic systems), the in-medium two-body wave function is essentially a free wave function. In terms of the renormalization group (RG), this implies that the running of the effective field theory (EFT) couplings freezes for r-space cutoffs above this distance (or p-space cutoffs below the corresponding healing momentum scale). As a consequence the leading order EFT description of nuclear matter (understood here as the infrared limit of the RG) corresponds to the mean-field approximation and a set of tree-level (i.e. perturbative) leading contact-range couplings. Though the contacts do in principle inherit the power counting they had in the vacuum, their iteration is suppressed in the infrared, explaining why they become perturbative in nuclear matter. In addition to the contact-range potential, RG evolution requires the inclusion of density-dependent terms in the equation of state that can be represented by a pseudo-potential, which (unlike the genuine contacts) should not be iterated. The LO EFT description ends up being a subset of Skyrme forces previously identified by the Orsay group.

## Key claim

The leading-order effective field theory description of nuclear matter corresponds to the mean-field approximation with perturbative contact-range couplings, because the renormalization group flow of these couplings freezes for cutoffs softer than the healing distance (the characteristic long-distance scale of finite-density fermionic systems), making their iteration suppressed in the infrared regardless of whether they are non-perturbative in the vacuum.

## Method

The author analyzes the in-medium two-body wave function using the Bethe-Goldstone equation in r-space for a zero-range interaction, comparing it to the vacuum Lippmann-Schwinger solution. A delta-shell regulator at radius R_c is introduced to compute the running of the contact-range coupling constant C_0(R_c) in both vacuum and nuclear matter, and the perturbative expansion of the G-matrix is examined to determine the scaling of loop corrections. The relative strength of loop contributions to the potential energy per nucleon is evaluated numerically through the function h_F(k_F R_c) for both the delta-shell regulator and a sharp momentum-space cutoff.

## Key numbers

- The singlet neutron-proton scattering length used is a_0 = -23.7 fm, and the Fermi momentum at saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is k_F = 1.33 fm^{-1}
- For the delta-shell regulator, the first zero of the loop contribution function h_F(x) occurs at x ≈ 1.137, followed by zeros at x ≈ 1.701 and x ≈ 3.451, corresponding to cutoff radii R_c = 0.85, 1.28, and 2.59 fm respectively at equilibrium density
- For the sharp momentum-space cutoff, the zeros of h_F(x) occur at x ≈ 0.5592 and x ≈ 0.9210, where x = k_F / Lambda
- The in-medium G-matrix at zero external momenta takes the form G(E → 0) → (4π/m_N) * 1/(1/a_0 - (2/π)k_F)

## Context

This work extends the renormalization group analysis of contact-range interactions from few-nucleon systems (Valderrama, 2014) to finite-density nuclear matter, building on the concept of the healing distance originally developed by Brueckner (1955), Bethe (1957), and Gomes (1957). The resulting leading-order EFT description ends up being a subset of Skyrme forces previously identified by the Orsay group, providing a rigorous EFT foundation for these phenomenological effective interactions. This enables a systematic connection between the EFT description of few-nucleon systems in vacuum and that of extended nuclear systems, with the understanding that soft cutoffs are required to maintain the connection while hard cutoffs necessitate beyond-mean-field corrections.
