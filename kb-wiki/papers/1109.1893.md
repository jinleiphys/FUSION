# Search for variation of fundamental constants and violations of fundamental symmetries using isotope comparisons

> Atomic microwave clocks based on hyperfine transitions, such as the caesium standard, tick with a frequency that is proportional to the magnetic moment of the nucleus. This magnetic moment varies strongly between isotopes of the same atom, while all atomic electron parameters remain the same. Therefore the comparison of two microwave clocks based on different isotopes of the same atom can be used to constrain variation of fundamental constants. In this paper we calculate the neutron and proton contributions to the nuclear magnetic moments, as well as their sensitivity to any potential quark mass variation, in a number of isotopes of experimental interest including 201,199Hg and 87,85Rb, where experiments are underway. We also include a brief treatment of the dependence of the hyperfine transitions to variation in nuclear radius, which in turn is proportional to any change in quark mass. Our calculations of expectation-values of proton and neutron spin in nuclei are also needed to interpret measurements of violations of fundamental symmetries.

# Search for variation of fundamental constants and violations of fundamental symmetries using isotope comparisons

## Key claim
Comparisons of atomic microwave clocks based on different isotopes of the same atom can constrain variation of the quark mass to QCD scale ratio (m_q/Λ_QCD), with the caveat that the sensitivity coefficients κ depend strongly on nuclear model assumptions, particularly for isotopes where the valence nucleon is a neutron, where the entire effect arises from core polarization and its quark-mass-induced changes, leading to large theoretical uncertainties.

## Method
The authors calculate expectation values of proton and neutron spin (⟨s_p⟩ and ⟨s_n⟩) for nuclei with a single valence proton or neutron using three models: Model A uses the Schmidt single-particle values; Model B fits experimental magnetic moments assuming separate conservation of proton and neutron angular momenta; Model C additionally includes the variation of spin expectation values under quark mass change via the spin-spin interaction's dependence on π-meson exchange. These spin values are then used to compute sensitivity coefficients κ = κ_q + κ_s that relate fractional changes in nuclear magnetic moments to fractional changes in light and strange quark masses. The hyperfine transition frequency's sensitivity to quark mass variation also includes a nuclear radius contribution k_hq.

## Key numbers
- For the ⁸⁷Rb–⁸⁵Rb clock comparison: Model B gives κ = -0.160, Model C gives κ = -0.079 (δκ = -0.08 from the table)
- For the ²⁰¹Hg–¹⁹⁹Hg comparison: Model B gives δκ = 0.006, Model C gives δκ = 0.243
- For ¹⁶¹Yb–¹⁶⁹Yb comparison: δκ = 0.924 (largest within-element difference)
- For the cross-atom comparison ⁸⁵Rb–¹⁹⁹Hg: δκ = 0.171 (δC = 0.151, δk_hq = 0.020)
- For ¹³³Cs–¹⁶⁹Yb cross-atom comparison: δκ = 0.508 (δC = 0.501, δk_hq = 0.007)
- The g-factor sensitivity to light quark mass: δg_p/g_p = -0.087 δm_q/m_q; δg_n/g_n = -0.118 δm_q/m_q
- The spin-spin interaction parameter b varies as δb/b = -0.11 δm_q/m_q

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Flambaum and collaborators (2004, 2006) for calculating nuclear magnetic moment sensitivity to quark mass variation, applying it to specific isotopes of experimental interest including ²⁰¹,¹⁹⁹Hg (where experiments are underway by Burt et al., 2009) and ⁸⁷,⁸⁵Rb (Wei, 2011, private communication). The calculations of proton and neutron spin expectation values are also needed to interpret nuclear electric dipole moment and parity-nonconserving interactions. The results enable experimental searches for variation of fundamental constants using isotope comparisons, which are insensitive to α-variation and have reduced systematic effects compared to single-isotope clock comparisons. The paper identifies ytterbium isotopes as having the largest within-element sensitivity differences, though with significant model-dependent uncertainty, while the rubidium isotope pair shows more stable predictions across models.
