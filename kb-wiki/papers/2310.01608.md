# Neural Network Emulation of Spontaneous Fission

> Large-scale computations of fission properties are an important ingredient for nuclear reaction network calculations simulating rapid neutron-capture process (the r process) nucleosynthesis. Due to the large number of fissioning nuclei contributing to the r process, a microscopic description of fission based on nuclear density functional theory (DFT) is computationally challenging. We explore the use of neural networks (NNs) to construct DFT emulators capable of predicting potential energy surfaces and collective inertia tensors across the whole nuclear chart. We use constrained Hartree-Fock-Boguliubov (HFB) calculations to predict the potential energy and collective inertia tensor in the axial quadrupole and octupole collective coordinates, for a set of nuclei in the r-process region. We then employ NNs to emulate the HFB energy and collective inertia tensor across the considered region of the nuclear chart. Least-action pathways characterizing spontaneous fission half-lives and fragment yields are obtained using the nudged elastic band method. The potential energy predicted by NNs agrees with the DFT value to within a root-mean-square error of 500 keV, and the collective inertia components agree to within an order of magnitude. The exit points on the outer turning line are found to be well emulated. For the spontaneous fission half-lives the NN emulation provides values that are found to agree with the DFT predictions within a factor of $10^3$ across more than 70 orders of magnitude. Neural networks are able to emulate the potential energy and collective inertia well enough to reasonably predict physical observables. Future directions of study, such as the inclusion of additional collective degrees of freedom and active learning, will improve the predictive power of microscopic theory and further enable large-scale fission studies.

## Key claim

Neural network emulators can reproduce density functional theory (DFT) predictions for spontaneous fission half-lives within a factor of 10³ across more than 70 orders of magnitude, but this agreement depends on the quality of the collective inertia emulation, which introduces larger errors than the potential energy surface emulation alone.

## Method

The authors performed constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Gogny D1S interaction to compute potential energy surfaces and collective inertia tensors in the axial quadrupole (Q₂₀) and octupole (Q₃₀) collective coordinates for 194 nuclei in the r-process region. They trained feedforward neural networks with 2 to 7 hidden layers to emulate the HFB energy and the eigenvalue decomposition of the collective inertia tensor as functions of (A, Z, Q₂₀, Q₃₀). Least-action pathways for spontaneous fission were then obtained using the nudged elastic band method applied to both the DFT reference data and the neural network reconstructions.

## Key numbers

- Potential energy predicted by neural networks agrees with DFT values to within a root-mean-square error of 500 keV for most nuclei.
- Spontaneous fission half-lives from neural network emulation agree with DFT predictions within a factor of 10³ across more than 70 orders of magnitude.
- The neural network was trained on 194 nuclei, each on a regular grid of 4 b for 0 ≤ Q₂₀ ≤ 248 b and 6 b³/² for 0 ≤ Q₃₀ ≤ 60 b³/², with 693 grid points per potential energy surface.
- The collective inertia components agree with DFT values to within an order of magnitude; the off-diagonal component M₂₃ varies across almost 10 orders of magnitude.
- For the thorium (Z=90) chain, exit point differences can reach 5 b³/² due to accumulated random errors in the collective inertia reconstruction.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to compute fission barriers (Goriely 2009, Moller 2015), spontaneous fission half-lives (Staszczak 2013, Baran 2015, Giuliani 2018), and fragment distributions (Mumpower 2020, Sadhukhan 2022) for r-process nuclei. It builds on the approach of Lasseri (2020), who used committees of multilayer neural networks to emulate potential energy surfaces, but extends this to include collective inertia tensors and full spontaneous fission observables across a region of the nuclear chart. The results enable large-scale microscopic fission studies that would be computationally prohibitive with direct DFT calculations, and the authors identify future directions including additional collective degrees of freedom and active learning to improve predictive power.
