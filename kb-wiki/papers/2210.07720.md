# Bethe-Salpeter Bound-State Solutions: Examining Semirelativistic Approaches

> Within the formalism of relativistic quantum field theory an adequate framework for the description of two-particle bound states, such as, for instance, all conventional (i.e., non-exotic) mesons, is provided by the Poincaré-covariant homogeneous Bethe-Salpeter equation. In applications, however, this approach usually proves to be rather involved, whence it is not always quite easy to extract the predictions sought. In view of this, a coarse idea of the bound-state spectrum to be expected might be gained by adhering to some simplifying approximations - which constitutes an entirely legitimate first step. The reliability of the insights inferred from the arising simpler bound-state equation may be straightforwardly examined by taking into account a couple of rigorous constraints on the obtained discrete spectrum. Application of these tools is illustrated for popular potentials.

## Key claim
The spinless Salpeter equation, derived from the full Bethe-Salpeter formalism by a series of simplifying approximations (instantaneous approximation, free propagation, removal of negative-energy contributions, and neglect of spin), can provide a useful first estimate of bound-state spectra, but its reliability must be checked against rigorous constraints such as boundedness from below, upper limits on the number of bound states, and virial theorems.

## Method
The paper reviews the spinless Salpeter equation for two spinless constituents with relativistic kinetic energies and a static potential, then applies functional analysis to establish conditions for the Hamiltonian to be bounded from below, using the Coulomb potential as a worst-case reference. It derives rigorous upper bounds on the total number of bound states via Lebesgue-space criteria, and obtains upper bounds on discrete eigenvalues via the minimum-maximum theorem using a basis of generalized-Laguerre polynomials with variational parameters. These tools are illustrated for the Hulthén potential and for generalized Hellmann potentials (linear combinations of Coulomb and Yukawa terms).

## Key numbers
- For the spinless relativistic Coulomb problem with equal masses, the Hamiltonian is bounded from below if and only if the coupling satisfies \(\alpha < 4/\pi = 1.273239\dots\); the lower bound on the spectrum is then \(\sigma(\widetilde H) \ge 2m \sqrt{1 - (\pi\alpha/4)^2}\).
- For \(\alpha \le 1\), a tighter lower bound is \(\sigma(\widetilde H) \ge 2m \sqrt{(1 + \sqrt{1 - \alpha^2})/2}\).
- For equal-mass two-particle problems with a smooth negative potential of compact support, the total number of bound states \(N\) satisfies \(N \le \frac{C}{48\pi^2} \int d^3x [|V(\bm{x})|(|V(\bm{x})| + 4m)]^{3/2}\), with \(C = 14.107590867\) for \(m > 0\) and \(C = 6.074898097\) for \(m = 0\).
- For the Hulthén potential, the Hamiltonian is bounded from below if \(\eta/b < 4/\pi\).
- For generalized Hellmann potentials, well-definedness requires \(\kappa + \upsilon \le 4/\pi\).
- Upper bounds on binding energies \(B_{n_r\ell}\) (in units of the constituent mass \(m\)) for three example generalized Hellmann problems (with \(b = \mu = m\), \(\beta = 1\), subspace dimension \(d \gtrsim 29\)): for \((\kappa=1/2, \upsilon=1/2)\) the ground state (\(n_r=0, \ell=0\)) bound is \(-0.11673\,m\); for \((\kappa=1, \upsilon=-1)\) it is \(-0.17951\,m\); for \((\kappa=1, \upsilon=-2)\) it is \(-0.14410\,m\).

## Context
This work extends the rigorous analysis of semirelativistic bound-state equations previously developed for the Coulomb problem by Herbst (1977) and others, and applies it to the Hulthén potential and generalized Hellmann potentials (which generalize the Hellmann potential introduced by Hellmann (1935) for atomic physics). The constraints derived here enable systematic quality checks on approximate solutions of the spinless Salpeter equation, and the upper-bound techniques (using the minimum-maximum theorem and variational basis sets) provide a practical route to estimating spectra for meson-like systems without solving the full Bethe-Salpeter equation.
