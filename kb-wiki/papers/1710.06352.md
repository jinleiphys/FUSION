# Isospin-forbidden electric-dipole capture and the $α(d,γ)^6$Li reaction

> At the long-wavelength approximation, $E1$ transitions are forbidden between isospin-zero states. Hence $E1$ radiative capture is strongly hindered in reactions involving $N = Z$ nuclei but the $E1$ astrophysical $S$ factor may remain comparable to, or larger than, the $E2$ one. Theoretical expressions of the isoscalar and isovector contributions to $E1$ capture are analyzed in microscopic and three-body approaches in the context of the $α(d,γ)^6$Li reaction. The lowest non-vanishing terms of the operators are derived and the dominant contributions to matrix elements are discussed. The astrophysical $S$ factor computed with some of these contributions in a three-body $α+n+p$ model is in agreement with the recent low-energy experimental data of the LUNA collaboration. This confirms that a correct treatment of the isovector $E1$ transitions involving small isospin-one admixtures in the wave functions should be able to provide an explanation of the data without adjustable parameter. The exact-masses prescription which is often used to avoid the disappearance of the $E1$ matrix element in potential models is not founded at the microscopic level and should not be used for such reactions. The importance of capture components from an initial $S$ scattering wave is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the isovector component of the $E1$ transition, arising from small isospin-one admixtures in the wave functions of $N=Z$ nuclei, can explain the low-energy astrophysical $S$ factor of the $\alpha(d,\gamma)^6$Li reaction without adjustable parameters, but the authors caution that several potentially important $T=1$ contributions (including the $T_i=1$ component in the initial wave and the $T_f=1$ dipole excitation of the $\alpha$ core) are not included in their model, so the agreement with data depends on the assumption that these missing contributions do not interfere destructively.

## Method

The authors derived the lowest non-vanishing terms of the isoscalar and isovector $E1$ operators in microscopic and three-body approaches, then computed the astrophysical $S$ factor for the $\alpha(d,\gamma)^6$Li reaction using a three-body $\alpha+n+p$ model with hyperspherical coordinates. The initial scattering wave function coupled a deuteron ground state with $L=1$ partial waves, while the final $^6$Li$(1^+)$ ground state was described by a variational three-body wave function expanded in hyperspherical harmonics up to $K_{\rm max}=24$, using the Minnesota $NN$ potential and two different $\alpha+N$ potentials (Voronchev et al. and Kanada et al.) slightly renormalized to reproduce the experimental binding energy.

## Key numbers

- The squared norm of the isotriplet component in the $^6$Li ground state is $5.3 \times 10^{-3}$ with the Voronchev potential and $4.2 \times 10^{-3}$ with the Kanada potential.
- The matter r.m.s. radius of the $^6$Li ground state is approximately 2.25 fm (Voronchev) or 2.24 fm (Kanada), compared to the experimental value $2.32 \pm 0.03$ fm.
- The isovector $E1$ astrophysical $S$ factor at $E=0.01$ MeV is $6.38 \times 10^{-10}$ MeV b, and the combined isovector+isoscalar value is $6.23 \times 10^{-10}$ MeV b (a correction of about 2%).
- The $L=0$ asymptotic normalization coefficient for the $\alpha+d$ system is $C_0 \approx 2.05$ fm$^{-1/2}$, compared to the value $C_0 \approx 2.30$ fm$^{-1/2}$ extracted from experimental data.
- The total $S$ factor in Model B (Kanada potential) is lower by about 35% than in Model A (Voronchev potential) at low energies.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical treatment of isospin-forbidden $E1$ transitions in $N=Z$ nuclei, building on earlier microscopic cluster studies by Baye (1986) for $^{16}$O($\alpha,\gamma)^{20}$Ne and by Baye (1987) for $^{12}$C($\alpha,\gamma)^{16}$O, as well as on the three-body $\alpha+n+p$ model of Tursunov et al. (2016). The paper directly addresses the recent low-energy experimental data from the LUNA collaboration (2014) and argues against the "exact-masses" prescription commonly used in potential models by authors such as Bystritsky et al. (1990), Mukhamedzhanov et al. (2011), and Tursunov et al. (2015). The analysis enables future microscopic calculations that consistently include all isovector $E1$ contributions, particularly the virtual excitation of the $\alpha$ particle's giant dipole resonance, which is missing in the present three-body model.
