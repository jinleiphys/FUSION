# Accessing the Space-Time Development of Heavy-Ion Collisions With Theory And Experiment

> This thesis describes two lines of work. The first line is a study of the application of transport theory to massless particles, such as the partons in an ultrarelativistic collision at RHIC. The second line of work is an application of imaging techniques to extract the relative distribution of emission points from two-particle correlation functions measured in heavy-ion reactions. This thesis contains an introduction to transport theory and intensity interferometry as well as updates to several papers. These papers are D.A. Brown and P. Danielewicz, "Partons in Phase-Space," Phys. Rev. D 58 (1998) article no. 094003 (eprint nucl-th/9802015), D.A. Brown and P. Danielewicz, "Imaging of Sources in Heavy-Ion Reactions," Phys. Lett. B 398 (1997) pp. 252-258 (eprint nucl-th/9701010), and D.A. Brown and P. Danielewicz, "Optimized Discretization of Sources Imaged in Heavy-Ion Reactions," Phys. Rev. C 57 (1998) pp. 2474-2483 (eprint nucl-th/9712066).

## Key claim

This thesis develops phase-space propagators for massless scalar particles within transport theory, showing that both the retarded and Feynman phase-space propagators constrain particles to propagate within similar space-time regions despite their different boundary conditions, with the key condition that high-energy particles tend to follow their classical paths while low-energy particles can deviate.

## Method

The author derives analytic expressions for the Wigner transforms of the retarded and Feynman propagators for massless scalar particles, then extends the retarded propagator to nonzero mass using a Fourier-Bessel transform technique. The propagators are analyzed by studying the Lorentz invariant λ² = (x·p)² - x²p² in frames where the particle's three-momentum vanishes (for p²>0) or its energy vanishes (for p²<0), establishing constraints on the space-time region where propagation is significant. For the massive case, the retarded propagator is expressed as the massless propagator minus an integral involving a Bessel function, evaluated numerically and in the low-mass limit.

## Key numbers

* For massless particles with p²>0, the spatial propagation distance is constrained to |Δx'_T| ≲ 1/√|p²|, |Δx_L| ≲ 1/|p_L|, and |Δx_0| ≲ 1/|p_0|
* For massless particles with p²<0, the constraints become |Δx_T| ≲ 1/√|p²|, |Δx_L| ≲ 1/|p_0|, and |Δx_0| ≲ 1/|p_L|
* For on-shell non-relativistic particles with nonzero mass, the deviation from classical paths is bounded by the Compton wavelength λ_C = ħc/m, approximately 0.2 fm for nucleons and approximately 1.4 fm for pions
* In the low-mass limit m² ≪ |p²|, the retarded propagator correction is of order m²/p²

## Context

This work extends the phase-space transport theory approach of Danielewicz and collaborators, providing the detailed analytic structure of phase-space propagators that underlies the transport calculations in the main chapters of the thesis. The nonzero-mass results connect to Remler (1990) who previously derived the Feynman phase-space propagator for massive particles using an approximation that smooths transverse structure on scales larger than 1/m. The finding that nucleons are localized to their Compton wavelength (0.2 fm) justifies the Quasi-Particle Approximation used in heavy-ion transport models, while the larger pion Compton wavelength (1.4 fm) raises questions about treating pions as point-like particles in such approaches.
