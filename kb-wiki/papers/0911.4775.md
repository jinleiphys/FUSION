# Rapidity scaling of multiplicity and flow in weakly and strongly interacting systems

> We examine the "naturalness" of the scaling of multiplicity and elliptic flow $v_2$ with rapidity in weakly and strongly coupled systems. We show that multiplicity scaling is relatively straight-forward to incorporate in existing ansatze with no unnatural assumptions, and argue that this scaling is relatively insensitive to the transport properties of the system. On the other hand, we argue that the observed scaling of elliptic flow observed is problematic to describe within a hydrodynamic model (the Knudsen number $K \ll 1$), but arises more naturally within weakly coupled systems (where the Knudsen number $\sim 1$). We conclude by an overview of ways proposed to make weakly coupled systems compatible with the absolute value of elliptic flow, and by indicating experimental probes which could clarify these issues.

## Key claim

The observed scaling of elliptic flow \(v_2\) with rapidity is problematic to describe within a hydrodynamic model (where the Knudsen number \(K \ll 1\)), but arises more naturally within weakly interacting systems (where the Knudsen number \(\sim 1\)); the paper argues that this scaling is incompatible with the expected behavior of a strongly coupled system with a QCD equation of state, while multiplicity scaling is relatively insensitive to transport properties.

## Method

The author examines the "naturalness" of scaling of multiplicity and elliptic flow with rapidity by comparing the predictions of a hydrodynamic model (with a QCD equation of state and a small Knudsen number) against those of a weakly interacting system (with Knudsen number of order unity). The analysis uses the Brodsky-Gunion-Kuhn (BGK) model and a modified BGK picture to illustrate how limiting fragmentation and the logarithmic scaling of mid-rapidity multiplicity density can arise, and then contrasts this with the expected rapidity dependence of elliptic flow, which is argued to be sensitive to the phase of the system at thermalization.

## Key numbers

- The mid-rapidity multiplicity density scales as \(\left. dN/dy \right|_{y=0} \sim N_{part} \ln \sqrt{s}\), where \(N_{part}\) is the number of participants and \(\sqrt{s}\) is the center-of-mass energy.
- The critical temperature for the QCD phase transition is taken as \(T_c \sim 180\) MeV.
- For top RHIC energies, the critical rapidity where the system transitions from QGP to hadron gas is estimated to be \(y = 3.5-4\), corresponding to a critical \(\sqrt{s} \sim 3\) GeV (or 18 GeV fixed target).
- The viscosity-to-entropy ratio \(\eta/s\) is expected to jump from a relatively high value in the hadron gas (\(\eta/s \sim N_c^2\)) to a low value in the strongly interacting QGP (\(\eta/s \sim \order{0.1} N_c^0\)), then increase to the weakly coupled QGP (\(\eta/s \sim \order{\lambda^{-2}} N_c^0\)).

## Context

This work extends the discussion of scaling in heavy-ion collisions, particularly the limiting fragmentation of multiplicity and elliptic flow observed by the PHOBOS, STAR, and PHENIX collaborations (Busza 2004). It contrasts the hydrodynamic model, which has been successful at describing RHIC \(v_2\) data and led to the "perfect fluid" interpretation (Teaney 2003, Shuryak 2004, Romatschke 2007), with weakly coupled transport models (Bass 2002). The paper argues that the observed scaling of \(v_2\) with rapidity is not natural within hydrodynamics with a QCD equation of state, and suggests that experimental probes such as rapidity scans could clarify whether the system is strongly or weakly interacting.
