# Phase-space excluded-volume approach for light clusters in nuclear medium

> A phase-space excluded-volume approach is developed to investigate the in-medium properties of light clusters in nuclear matter. In this approach, light clusters can exist only if the total nucleon phase-space occupation of the surrounding nuclear medium -- including explicit contributions from light clusters -- is sufficiently low. The distribution functions of nucleons and light clusters are determined self-consistently by accounting for the interplay between in-medium effects and thermodynamic properties. By employing standard Skyrme energy-density functionals to model the nuclear mean-field potential, the approach enables the evaluation of the Mott momentum and the fraction of light clusters in nuclear matter. Furthermore, it can be readily integrated into dynamical models, to study in-medium effects on light clusters, based on measured yields in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper develops a phase-space excluded-volume approach for light clusters in nuclear matter that self-consistently includes the contribution of nucleons bound in light clusters to the total phase-space occupation, an aspect missing in existing models; the approach reproduces the main features of the more sophisticated in-medium Schrödinger equation with significantly reduced computational cost, though the cutoff parameter \(F_A^{\rm cut}\) must be kept constant and the density/temperature dependence of this parameter is mild but not fully eliminated.

## Method

The authors generalize the phase-space excluded-volume approach by introducing a criterion that a light cluster of species \(\nu\) with mass number \(A\) and center-of-mass momentum \(\vec{P}\) can exist only if the average nucleon phase-space occupation \(\langle f_\tau \rangle_\nu(\vec{P})\) is below a cutoff \(F_A^{\rm cut}\), where the average is taken over the free-space wave function of the cluster. The distribution functions of unbound nucleons and light clusters are determined self-consistently by solving coupled equations: unbound nucleons follow a modified Fermi-Dirac distribution that accounts for Pauli blocking from nucleons bound in clusters, while light clusters follow Fermi-Dirac or Bose-Einstein distributions truncated by a Heaviside step function at the Mott momentum. Standard Skyrme energy-density functionals are employed to model the nuclear mean-field potential, and the system is solved for given total nucleon density and temperature.

## Key numbers

* For symmetric nuclear matter at temperature \(T = 10\) MeV, the deuteron Mott momentum \(P^{\rm Mott}_d\) increases from approximately 0 MeV/\(c\) at baryon density \(\rho_B \approx 0.01\) fm\(^{-3}\) to about 200 MeV/\(c\) at \(\rho_B \approx 0.04\) fm\(^{-3}\) for a cutoff \(F_2^{\rm cut} = 0.5\).
* The triton Mott momentum \(P^{\rm Mott}_t\) at \(T = 10\) MeV reaches about 300 MeV/\(c\) at \(\rho_B \approx 0.04\) fm\(^{-3}\) for \(F_3^{\rm cut} = 0.5\).
* The \(\alpha\)-particle Mott momentum \(P^{\rm Mott}_\alpha\) at \(T = 10\) MeV reaches about 400 MeV/\(c\) at \(\rho_B \approx 0.04\) fm\(^{-3}\) for \(F_4^{\rm cut} = 0.5\).
* The deuteron fraction in symmetric nuclear matter at \(T = 10\) MeV and \(\rho_B = 0.01\) fm\(^{-3}\) is approximately 0.15 for \(F_2^{\rm cut} = 0.5\), decreasing to below 0.02 at \(\rho_B = 0.04\) fm\(^{-3}\).
* The \(\alpha\)-particle fraction at \(T = 10\) MeV and \(\rho_B = 0.01\) fm\(^{-3}\) is approximately 0.05 for \(F_4^{\rm cut} = 0.5\), dropping to near zero at \(\rho_B = 0.04\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the phase-space excluded-volume approach previously applied in transport models by Danielewicz (1993), Kuhn (2000), and Wang (2020) to equilibrated nuclear matter, now including the self-consistent contribution of nucleons bound in light clusters to the total phase-space occupation. The approach provides a computationally efficient alternative to solving the in-medium Schrödinger equation as formulated by Röpke (1982, 1992) and enables future integration into dynamical models of heavy-ion collisions to study in-medium effects on light clusters from measured yields, as well as applications to astrophysical scenarios such as supernovae and neutron star mergers.
