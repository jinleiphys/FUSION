# The regime of applicability of Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics

> Using analytical tools from linear response theory, we systematically assess the accuracy of several microscopic derivations of Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics near local equilibrium. This allows us to "rank" the different approaches in decreasing order of accuracy as follows: Inverse Reynolds Dominance (IReD), Denicol-Niemi-Molnár-Rischke (DNMR), second-order gradient expansion, and 14-moment approximation. We find that IReD theory is far superior to Navier-Stokes, being very accurate both in the asymptotic regime (i.e., for slow processes) and in the transient regime (i.e., on timescales comparable to the relaxation time). Also, the high accuracy of DNMR is confirmed, but neglecting second-order terms in the Knudsen number, which would render the equations parabolic, introduces serious systematic errors. Finally, in most cases, the second-order gradient expansion (a.k.a. non-resummed BRSSS) is found to be more inaccurate than Navier-Stokes in the transient regime. Overall, this analysis shows that Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics is falsifiable, and the relaxation time is observable, shedding new light on the debate on the viability of transient hydrodynamics as a well-defined physical theory distinguished from Navier-Stokes.

## Key claim

Using linear response theory, the authors systematically rank microscopic derivations of Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics and find that the Inverse Reynolds Dominance (IReD) approach is far superior to Navier-Stokes in both the asymptotic and transient regimes, but the second-order gradient expansion (non-resummed BRSSS) is often less accurate than Navier-Stokes in the transient regime.

## Method

The authors constructed a solvable linear-response model where a fluid element's non-equilibrium degrees of freedom A undergo coupled relaxation dynamics driven by an external gradient theta(t), producing a viscous flux Pi(t). They then compared six alternative dynamical equations for Pi (DNMR, truncated DNMR, IReD, 1-affinity approximation, second-order hydrodynamics, and Navier-Stokes) against the exact model prediction, using a smoothstep function for theta(t) with timescale t0 comparable to the relaxation times.

## Key numbers

- The IReD relaxation time is given by tau_Pi = (gamma tau kappa) / (gamma kappa) = (sum_n zeta_n tau_n) / (sum_m zeta_m), where zeta_n are the susceptibilities and tau_n the relaxation times of the eigenmodes.
- In a concrete example with three relaxation times tau_n = {5, 2, 1} and susceptibilities zeta_n = {1, 1, 8}, the DNMR Green function is G_D(t) = 2 e^{-t/5} while the IReD Green function is G_IR(t) = (20/3) e^{-2t/3}, compared to the exact G_ex(t) = e^{-t/5}/5 + e^{-t/2}/2 + 8 e^{-t}.
- The DNMR transport coefficient chi_D = gamma(tau_1 - tau) kappa = zeta (tau_1 - gamma tau kappa / gamma kappa), where tau_1 is the largest relaxation time.

## Context

This work addresses the long-standing debate over whether Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics is merely a mathematical trick to make Navier-Stokes causal (Geroch 2001), a refinement accurate to second order in gradients (Baier 2008), or an extension capturing transient non-equilibrium dynamics (Denicol 2012). The analysis shows that IReD can simultaneously fulfill both the second-order gradient interpretation and the transient dynamics interpretation, while DNMR in its original formulation fails to be hyperbolic and requires artificial removal of second-order terms. The results enable a clear criterion for choosing between interpretations (ii) and (iii) for a given system, and falsify Geroch's claim that Israel-Stewart effects are always undetectable.
