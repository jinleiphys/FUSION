# Nucleosynthesis in High-Entropy Hot-Bubbles of SNe and Abundance Patterns of Extremely Metal-Poor Stars

> There have been suggestions that the abundance of Extremely Metal-Poor (EMP) stars can be reproduced by Hypernovae (HNe), not by normal supernovae (SNe). However, recently it was also suggested that if the innermost neutron-rich or proton-rich matter is ejected, the abundance patterns of ejected matter are changed, and normal SNe may also reproduce the observations of EMP stars. In this letter, we calculate explosive nucleosynthesis with various Ye and entropy, and investigate whether normal SNe with this innermost matter, which we call "hot-bubble" component, can reproduce the abundance of EMP stars. We find that neutron-rich (Ye = 0.45-0.49) and proton-rich (Ye = 0.51-0.55) matter can increase Zn/Fe and Co/Fe ratios as observed, but tend to overproduce other Fe-peak elements. In addition to it, we find that if slightly proton-rich matter with 0.50 <= Ye < 0.501 with s/kb ~ 15-40 is ejected as much as ~ 0.06 Msolar, even normal SNe can reproduce the abundance of EMP stars, though it requires fine-tuning of Ye. On the other hand, HNe can more easily reproduce the observations of EMP stars without fine-tuning. Our results imply that HNe are the most possible origin of the abundance pattern of EMP stars.

# Nucleosynthesis in High-Entropy Hot-Bubbles of SNe and Abundance Patterns of Extremely Metal-Poor Stars

## Key claim

Normal supernovae can reproduce the observed abundance patterns of extremely metal-poor (EMP) stars only if they eject approximately 0.06 solar masses of slightly proton-rich matter with electron fraction 0.500 to 0.501 and entropy per Boltzmann constant of 15 to 40, but this requires fine-tuning of the electron fraction; hypernovae can reproduce the observations more easily without such fine-tuning, implying that hypernovae are the most likely origin of EMP star abundance patterns.

## Method

The authors performed explosive nucleosynthesis calculations as post-processing after hydrodynamical simulations using a simple alpha-network, adopting Pop III progenitors of 15 solar masses with explosion energy 1 x 10^51 erg (normal SN model) and 25 solar masses with 20 x 10^51 erg (HN model). They varied the electron fraction (Ye) from 0.45 to 0.55 and the entropy (s/kb) from approximately 5 to 150 by artificially reducing the density in the complete Si-burning region, mimicking the hot-bubble component from multi-dimensional simulations. Final yields were obtained by adding matter above and below a mass-cut (1.50 solar masses for normal SN, 1.88 solar masses for HN3 model) and compared to observed EMP star abundances.

## Key numbers

- Normal SN model ejects 0.07 solar masses of Ni-56, similar to SN1987A; HN3 model ejects 0.51 solar masses of Ni-56
- For normal SN models with s/kb ~ 15, [Co/Fe] peaks at Ye = 0.5004 with value ~ 0.7; for s/kb ~ 40, [Co/Fe] peaks at Ye = 0.5005 with value ~ 0.9
- [Zn/Fe] at Ye = 0.50 is ~ 0.1, 0.4, and 0.9 for s/kb ~ 5, 15, and 40 respectively
- Normal SN models require ejection of ~ 0.06 solar masses of matter with Ye = 0.500-0.501 and s/kb ~ 15-40 to reproduce EMP star abundances
- According to Janka et al. (2003), the amount of matter with Ye < 0.47 is less than 10^-4 solar masses, with Ye <= 0.50 is 6 x 10^-3 solar masses, and with Ye > 0.50 is 0.03 solar masses
- Varying the cooling timescale below T = 4 x 10^9 K by factors of 1/2 and 2 gives [Co/Fe] ~ 0.5 and 0.2 respectively, compared to ~ 0.4 with the original timescale; the required ejected mass becomes ~ 0.03 solar masses with upper Ye limit ~ 0.502

## Context

This work extends the parametric nucleosynthesis studies of Umeda and Nomoto (2002, 2005) and Tominaga, Umeda and Nomoto (2007), who showed that high-energy hypernovae can explain high [Zn,Co/Fe] and low [Cr,Mn/Fe] in EMP stars, while normal supernovae produce the opposite pattern. It also builds on the authors' previous work (Izutani, Umeda and Tominaga, 2009) on weak r-process elements, which assumed the same entropy for hot-bubble matter as the supernova shock. The present study incorporates higher entropy values (s/kb ~ 15-150) motivated by multi-dimensional simulations such as Janka et al. (2003), and finds that normal SNe with hot-bubble components still cannot match EMP star abundances without fine-tuning, reinforcing the conclusion that hypernovae are the primary origin. The results also suggest that neutron-rich hot-bubble matter with s/kb ~ 15 can produce Sr-Zr, while s/kb ~ 150 is needed for Mo-Rh, possibly linking to neutrino-driven wind models (Roberts et al., 2010).
