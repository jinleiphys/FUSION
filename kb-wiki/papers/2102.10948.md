# Crust-core interface and bulk neutron star properties

> The nuclear symmetry energy plays an important role in the description of the properties of finite nuclei as well as neutron stars. Especially, for low values of baryon density, the accurate description of the crust-core interface strongly depends on the symmetry energy. Usually, the well known parabolic approximation is employed for the definition of the symmetry energy without avoiding some drawbacks. In the present paper, a class of nuclear models, suitable for the description of the inner and outer core of neutron stars, is applied in studying the effect of higher orders of the expansion of the energy on the location of the crust-core transition. The thermodynamical and dynamical methods are used for the determination of the transition density $n_{\rm t}$ and pressure $P_{\rm t}$. The corresponding energy density functional is applied for the study of some relevant properties of both nonrotating and slowly rotating neutron stars. We found that the larger the value of the slope parameter $L$, the slower the convergence of the expansion. In addition, a universal relation is presented between $n_{\rm t}$ and $L$, by employing the full expression and dynamical approach. The crustal moment of inertia is very sensitive to the location of the transition while the effects are moderated concerning the critical angular velocity of the $r$-mode instability and minimum mass configuration. The effect on the tidal deformability is less but not negligible. In any case, the use of the parabolic approximation leads to the overestimation of $n_{\rm t}$ and $P_{\rm t}$ and consequently, on inaccurate predictions. Moreover, in some cases, even the matching process at the interface may affect considerably the predictions, introducing errors of the same order with the one due to the employed method.

# Crust-core interface and bulk neutron star properties

## Key claim

The parabolic approximation for the nuclear symmetry energy systematically overestimates the crust-core transition density and pressure, with the deviation growing larger for stiffer symmetry energies (higher slope parameter L); the full expression including higher-order terms up to I^10 is needed for accurate predictions, and the convergence of the expansion is slower when L is larger.

## Method

The authors employed a class of nuclear models (MDI, HLPS, and Skyrme forces including SkI4, Ska, and Sly4) to construct equations of state for neutron star matter, expanding the baryon energy per particle around the asymmetry parameter I up to tenth order. They applied both the thermodynamical method (based on the stability condition C_therm(n)=0) and the dynamical method (based on the condition U_dyn(n)=0) to determine the crust-core transition density n_t and pressure P_t, then used these to compute crustal moment of inertia, r-mode instability critical frequency, tidal deformability, and minimum mass configuration for nonrotating and slowly rotating neutron stars.

## Key numbers

- For the MDI(110) model (L=110 MeV) using the thermodynamical method: n_t^FE = 0.047 fm^-3, P_t^FE = 0.047 MeV fm^-3; under parabolic approximation: n_t^PA = 0.106 fm^-3, P_t^PA = 1.484 MeV fm^-3
- For the HLPS(29.5) model (L=29.5 MeV) using the thermodynamical method: n_t^FE = 0.098 fm^-3, P_t^FE = 0.455 MeV fm^-3; under parabolic approximation: n_t^PA = 0.104 fm^-3, P_t^PA = 0.495 MeV fm^-3
- For the Sly4(46) model (L=46 MeV) using the dynamical method: n_t^FE = 0.080 fm^-3, P_t^FE = 0.365 MeV fm^-3; under parabolic approximation: n_t^PA = 0.085 fm^-3, P_t^PA = 0.427 MeV fm^-3
- The minimum neutron star mass is well constrained to M_min approximately 0.1 solar masses, independent of the equation of state details
- A universal relation between n_t and L is given by n_t = S_30 (0.1327 - 0.0898 L_70 + 0.0228 L_70^2) fm^-3, where S_30 = E_sym,2(n_s)/(30 MeV) and L_70 = L/(70 MeV)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the crust-core transition by Carreau et al. (2019) and earlier work by Kubis (2007) and Moustakidis (2012), systematically examining the convergence of the symmetry energy expansion up to I^10 rather than the typical I^6 truncation used by Boquera (2017). The results enable more accurate predictions of neutron star properties that depend on the crust-core interface, particularly crustal moment of inertia (relevant for pulsar glitch observations in the Vela pulsar) and the r-mode instability critical frequency, while showing that tidal deformability is less affected. The universal n_t-L relation from Steiner et al. (2015) is confirmed across multiple nuclear models.
