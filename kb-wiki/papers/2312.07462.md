# Small-x structure of oxygen and neon isotopes as seen by the Large Hadron Collider

> Results on collisions of $^{16}$O nuclei performed at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) have been presented for the first time at Quark Matter 2023 by the STAR collaboration. $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O collisions are also expected to take place in the near future at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at much higher beam energies. We explore the potential of beam-energy-dependent studies for this system to probe small-$x$ dynamics and QCD evolution. We perform 3+1D IP-Glasma simulations to predict the rapidity dependence of the initial geometry of light-ion collisions, focusing on $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O and $^{20}$Ne+$^{20}$Ne collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 70$ GeV and 7 TeV. The choice of $^{20}$Ne is motivated by its strongly elongated geometry, which may respond differently to the effect of the high-energy evolution compared to the more spherical $^{16}$O. We find that smearing induced by soft gluon production at high energy causes mild variations in the initial-state eccentricities as a function of the collision energy. These effects could be resolved in future experiments and deserve further investigation.

## Key claim

The small-x QCD evolution (JIMWLK) causes only mild variations (less than 10%) in the initial-state spatial eccentricities of light-ion collisions as a function of collision energy, but these effects could potentially be resolved in future experiments at the LHC and RHIC.

## Method

The authors performed 3+1D IP-Glasma simulations to predict the rapidity dependence of initial geometry for 16O+16O and 20Ne+20Ne collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 70 GeV and 7 TeV. They generated 6k events for each system, constructing Wilson line configurations from nucleon positions sampled with spatial correlations from the Projected Generator Coordinate Method (PGCM), then evolved the Wilson lines via JIMWLK evolution to the relevant beam energies. For each event, separate 2+1D Classical Yang-Mills simulations at different rapidities produced the rapidity-dependent initial geometry, with centrality selection based on gluon multiplicity at mid-rapidity at proper time 0.2 fm/c.

## Key numbers

- Spatial eccentricities epsilon_2 and epsilon_3 in 0-5% central collisions show rapidity dependence not resolved by statistical precision, with impact less than 10%
- Beam energies studied: sqrt(s_NN) = 70 GeV (near top RHIC energy and LHCb SMOG2 fixed-target energy) and 7 TeV (top LHC energy)
- 6,000 events generated for each of the two collision systems (16O+16O and 20Ne+20Ne)
- Momentum anisotropy epsilon_p is higher at 70 GeV than at 7 TeV, reduced by randomization of color fields from soft gluon emission at high energy
- For 16O+16O collisions, epsilon_2{2} shows a stronger beam-energy dependence than for 20Ne+20Ne, with an observed increase from 70 GeV to 7 TeV

## Context

This work extends the 3+1D IP-Glasma model of Schenke (2016) to light-ion collisions, motivated by the first results on 16O collisions at RHIC presented by the STAR collaboration at Quark Matter 2023 and the upcoming 16O+16O runs at the LHC in 2024. The choice of 20Ne is motivated by its strongly elongated geometry from nuclear structure predictions of Bally (2022) and Ryssens (2023), which may respond differently to high-energy evolution compared to the more spherical 16O. The results enable future experimental studies at both RHIC and LHC to constrain the beam-energy and rapidity evolution of initial-state geometry, and call for coupling with hydrodynamics and fully 3D Wilson line configurations followed by 3+1D Classical Yang-Mills simulations as pursued by Schlichting (2020), Ipp (2017, 2021), and McDonald (2023).
