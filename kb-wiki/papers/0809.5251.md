# Exploring Effective Three-body Forces

> Topics related to the construction, phenomenological determination, and effects of the effective three-body forces within the traditional nuclear shell model approach are discussed. The manifestations of the three-body forces in realistic nuclei in the 0f7/2 and 1s0d shell model valence spaces are explored.

# Exploring Effective Three-body Forces

## Key claim

Effective three-body forces are shown to be necessary for reproducing nuclear spectra and symmetry-violating features in the $0f_{7/2}$ shell and $1s0d$ shell valence spaces, but the evidence is conditional on the specific fitting procedure and the statistical uncertainties in the extracted parameters, with some three-body parameters having uncertainties comparable to or larger than the parameter values themselves.

## Method

The author performs least-square fits of shell model Hamiltonians containing one-body, two-body, and three-body interaction parameters to experimental binding energies and excitation energies of nuclei in the $0f_{7/2}$ valence space (both $N=28$ isotones and $Z=20$ calcium isotopes) and in oxygen isotopes using the USD shell model as a starting two-body Hamiltonian. For the $f_{7/2}$ shell, an iterative fitting procedure is used because the matrix relating energies to parameters depends on the eigenstates, which in turn depend on the interaction parameters; for oxygen isotopes, three-body operators are defined from three-particle eigenstates of the USD Hamiltonian and fitted to ground states and low-lying states in $^{19-22}$O.

## Key numbers

- For $N=28$ isotones, the root-mean-square (RMS) deviation from experiment drops from 120 keV (two-body fit) to 80 keV (three-body fit); for $Z=20$ isotopes, RMS drops from 220 keV to 170 keV.
- The seniority $\nu=1$ three-body strength $V_{7/2}^{(3)}$ is $55 \pm 28$ keV for $N=28$ isotones and $53 \pm 70$ keV for $Z=20$ isotopes.
- In $^{52}$Cr, the $4_1^+$ and $4_2^+$ states have an experimental energy difference of 400 keV; the two-body $f_{7/2}$ model gives 84 keV, the two-body $f_{7/2}p$ model gives 200 keV, and the three-body $f_{7/2}$ model reproduces the splitting.
- The $B(E2)$ transition $4_2 \rightarrow 2_1$ in $^{52}$Cr is 0 $e^2$fm$^4$ in the two-body $f_{7/2}$ model, 3.3 $e^2$fm$^4$ in $f_{7/2}p$, 15.1 $e^2$fm$^4$ in $fp$, 56.8 $e^2$fm$^4$ in the three-body $f_{7/2}$ model, compared to the experimental value of $69 \pm 18$ $e^2$fm$^4$.
- For oxygen isotopes, the fitted three-body parameters are $V^{(3)}_{5/2} = 45$ keV, $V^{(3)}_{3/2} = -179$ keV, and $V^{(3)}_{1/2} = -231$ keV.
- The minimal fit to binding energies in $N=28$ isotones gives $V_{7/2}^{(3)} = 18 \pm 20$ keV; for $Z=20$ isotopes, $V_{7/2}^{(3)} = 31 \pm 31$ keV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of three-body forces in the nuclear shell model by Eisenstein (1973), Poves (1981), Hees (1989, 1990), and Zuker (2003), and builds on the author's own prior work (Volya 2008). The paper demonstrates that three-body forces can account for particle-hole symmetry violations and seniority mixing in the $f_{7/2}$ shell without requiring expansion of the valence space to include the $p_{3/2}$ and $p_{1/2}$ orbitals, as was done in earlier two-body calculations by Lips (1970) and Poves (2001). The proposed method of defining many-body operators from eigenstates of a lower-rank Hamiltonian offers a path toward managing the large parameter space in general shell model calculations with three-body forces.
