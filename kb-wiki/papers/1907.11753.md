# $^{15}$C: from Halo-EFT structure to the study of transfer, breakup and radiative-capture reactions

> Aside from being a one-neutron halo nucleus, $^{15}$C is interesting because it is involved in reactions of relevance for several nucleosynthesis scenarios. The aim of this work is to analyze various reactions involving $^{15}$C, using a single structure model based on Halo EFT. To develop a Halo-EFT model of $^{15}$C at NLO, we first extract the ANC of its ground state by analyzing $^{14}$C(d,p)$^{15}$C transfer data at low energy. Using this Halo-EFT description, we study the $^{15}$C Coulomb breakup at high (605AMeV) and intermediate (68AMeV) energies using eikonal models with a consistent treatment of nuclear and Coulomb interactions at all orders, and proper relativistic corrections. Finally, we study the $^{14}$C(n,$γ$)$^{15}$C radiative capture. Our theoretical cross sections are in good agreement with experimental data for all reactions, thereby assessing the robustness of the $^{15}$C Halo-EFT model. Since a simple NLO description is enough to reproduce all data, the only nuclear-structure observables that matter are the binding energy and its ANC, showing that all the reactions considered are purely peripheral. In particular, it confirms the ANC value obtained for the $^{15}$C ground state: 1.59$\pm$0.06fm$^{-1}$. Our model provides also a new estimate of the radiative-capture cross section at astrophysical energy (23.3keV): 4.66$\pm$0.14$μ$b. Including a Halo-EFT description of $^{15}$C within precise models of reactions is confirmed to be an excellent way to relate the nucleus reaction cross sections and structure. Its systematic expansion enables us to deduce which nuclear-structure observables are actually probed in the collision. From this, we can infer valuable information on both the structure of $^{15}$C and its synthesis through the $^{14}$C(n,$γ$)$^{15}$C radiative capture at astrophysical energies.

## Key claim

Using a single Halo-EFT description of 15C at next-to-leading order (NLO), the authors reproduce experimental data for transfer, Coulomb breakup, and radiative-capture reactions, showing that all these reactions are purely peripheral and depend only on the binding energy and asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) of the ground state, not on the short-range details of the nuclear wave function.

## Method

The authors first extract the ANC of the 15C ground state by analyzing 14C(d,p)15C transfer data at Ed=17.06 MeV using a finite-range adiabatic distorted wave approximation (FR-ADWA) model coupled with a leading-order Halo-EFT description of 15C, selecting data only at forward angles (θ<12°) to ensure peripheral conditions. They then construct an NLO Halo-EFT potential for the 14C-n system by fitting both the binding energy (1.218 MeV) and the extracted ANC, using three different Gaussian ranges (r0=1.2, 1.5, and 2.0 fm) to test sensitivity to short-range physics. Using this structure model, they compute Coulomb breakup cross sections at 605 MeV/nucleon and 68 MeV/nucleon using eikonal-based models with consistent treatment of nuclear and Coulomb interactions at all orders and relativistic corrections, and compute the 14C(n,γ)15C radiative-capture cross section.

## Key numbers

* ANC of the 15C ground state: C²_{1/2+} = 1.59 ± 0.06 fm⁻¹, extracted from 14C(d,p)15C transfer data at Ed=17.06 MeV with data selected at θ<12°
* ANC of the 15C excited state (5/2+ at -0.478 MeV): C_{5/2+} = 0.056 ± 0.001 fm⁻¹/²
* Radiative-capture cross section at astrophysical energy (23.3 keV): σ_{n,γ}(23.3 keV) = 4.66 ± 0.14 μb
* One-neutron separation energy of 15C ground state: Sn(15C) = 1.218 MeV
* One-neutron separation energy of 15C excited state: E_{e.s.} = -0.478 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Halo-EFT approach previously applied to 11Be by Capel, Phillips, and Hammer (2018) to the one-neutron halo nucleus 15C, which is relevant for nucleosynthesis in AGB stars, inhomogeneous big-bang nucleosynthesis, and the r-process in Type II supernovae. The analysis confirms the ANC value obtained by Summers and Nunes (2008) from Coulomb breakup data and provides a new estimate of the radiative-capture cross section that can be compared to the direct measurement by Reifarth et al. (2008). The results enable future work using ab initio overlap wave functions from no-core shell model with continuum (NCSMC) calculations by Navratil et al. to improve descriptions at larger angles where short-range physics matters.
