# QCD phase diagram in a magnetized medium from the chiral symmetry perspective: The linear sigma model with quarks and the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model effective descriptions

> We review the main features of the QCD phase diagram description, at finite temperature, baryon density and in the presence of a magnetic field, from the point of view of effective models, whose main ingredient is chiral symmetry. We concentrate our attention on two of these models: The linear sigma model with quarks and the Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model. We show that a main ingredient to understand the characteristics of the phase transitions is the inclusion of plasma screening effects that capture the physics of collective, long-wave modes, and thus describe a prime property of plasmas near transition lines, namely, long distance correlations. Inclusion of plasma screening makes possible to understand the inverse magnetic catalysis phenomenon even without the need to consider magnetic field-dependent coupling constants. Screening is also responsible for the emergence of a critical end point in the phase diagram even for small magnetic field strengths. Although versatile, the NJL model is also a more limited approach since, being a non-renormalizable model, a clear separation between pure vacuum and medium effects is not always possible. The model cannot describe inverse magnetic catalysis unless a magnetic field dependent coupling is included. The location of the critical end point strongly depends on the choice of the type of interaction and on the magnetic field dependence of the corresponding coupling. Overall, both models provide sensible tools to explore the properties of magnetized, strongly interacting matter. However, a cross talk among them as well as a consistent physical approach to determine the model parameters is much needed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that including plasma screening effects (via ring diagram resummation) in the Linear Sigma Model with quarks (LSMq) can explain the inverse magnetic catalysis phenomenon, where the critical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration decreases with increasing magnetic field, without requiring magnetic field-dependent coupling constants, contrary to what is needed in the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model.

## Method

The authors review and compare two effective models of QCD: the LSMq and the NJL model, both at finite temperature, baryon density, and in the presence of an external magnetic field. For the LSMq, they compute the effective potential at one-loop level for bosons and quarks using Schwinger's proper-time formalism in the weak magnetic field limit, then supplement this with the resummation of ring diagrams to account for plasma screening effects. For the NJL model, they discuss how the inclusion of a magnetic field-dependent coupling is necessary to describe inverse magnetic catalysis, and how the location of the critical end point depends on the choice of interaction type and coupling dependence.

## Key numbers

- The Hagedorn limiting temperature is given as T^H approximately 0.19 GeV, above which the hadronic description breaks down.
- The pseudocritical temperature for chiral/deconfining transition at zero baryon chemical potential from lattice QCD is T_c(mu_B=0) approximately 155 MeV.
- Lattice QCD results show that a critical end point is not found for mu_B/T less than or equal to 2 and 145 less than or equal to T less than or equal to 155 MeV.
- A more recent bound disfavors a critical end point for mu_B/T less than or equal to 2 and T/T_c(mu_B=0) greater than 0.9.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the magnetized QCD phase diagram that grew from the lattice QCD discovery of inverse magnetic catalysis by Bali et al. (2011, 2012, 2014). The paper contrasts the LSMq approach, where plasma screening effects naturally produce inverse magnetic catalysis, with the NJL model approach, which requires a magnetic field-dependent coupling as first proposed by Farias et al. (2014) and developed by Costa et al. (2015) and Ferreira et al. (2015). The work enables further cross-talk between effective models and a consistent physical approach to determine model parameters for describing magnetized strongly interacting matter.
