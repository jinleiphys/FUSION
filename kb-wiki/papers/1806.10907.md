# The Chiral Magnetic Effect and an experimental bound on the late time magnetic field strength

> We first compare different approaches to estimates of the magnitude of the chiral magnetic effect in relativistic heavy ion collisions and show that their main difference lies in the assumptions on the length of persistence of the magnetic field generated by the colliding nuclei. We then analyze recent measurements of the global polarization of $Λ$ and $\bar Λ$ hyperons in terms of the bounds they set on the magnitude of the late time magnetic field.

## Key claim

The widely different published estimates of the chiral magnetic effect (CME) contribution to experimental observables in heavy ion collisions arise primarily from differing assumptions about the persistence time of the magnetic field generated by the colliding nuclei, not from disagreements about topological charge density fluctuations; the authors then use recent hyperon polarization data to derive an upper bound on the late-time magnetic field that rules out models of plasma-induced magnetic field persistence.

## Method

The authors compare three approaches to estimating topological charge fluctuations in the quark-gluon plasma: a glasma-based calculation by Lappi and Schlichting (2018), a heuristic geometric estimate by Muller and Schafer (2010), and an anomalous hydrodynamics simulation by Hirono, Hirano, and Kharzeev (2014). They then analyze the difference in global polarization of Lambda and anti-Lambda hyperons measured by STAR in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV, using the statistical polarization model of Becattini et al. (2017) to extract an upper bound on the magnetic field strength at hadronization.

## Key numbers

- The topological charge density estimate from the glasma approach yields 0.0005 GeV^6 for the quantity (Q_5)^2 / V^2 * (S_perp / pi rho^2), while the heuristic approach gives 0.004 GeV^6 and the anomalous hydrodynamics approach gives 0.0003 GeV^6.
- The measured Lambda polarization is (0.277 +/- 0.040) x 10^{-2} and anti-Lambda polarization is (0.240 +/- 0.045) x 10^{-2}, giving Delta P = (0.37 +/- 0.60) x 10^{-3}.
- At the one-standard deviation limit, -Delta P < 2.3 x 10^{-4}, yielding an upper bound on the late-time magnetic field of |B| < 8.9 x 10^{11} T, corresponding to |eB| < 1.35 x 10^{-3} fm^{-2} (approximately 2.7 x 10^{-3} m_pi^2).
- The time-integrated magnetic field is bounded by tau_B e B_bar < 0.25 fm^{-1} (approximately 50 MeV).

## Context

This work reconciles the order-of-magnitude discrepancies between the CME estimates of Muller and Schafer (2010) and Hirono, Hirano, and Kharzeev (2014) by identifying the assumed magnetic field lifetime as the key difference. The derived bound on the late-time magnetic field rules out all models of plasma-induced magnetic field persistence considered by Inghirami et al. (2016) and is only modestly larger than the vacuum magnetic field estimate of Asakawa et al. (2010). The result enables future, more precise hyperon polarization measurements to further constrain the CME contribution in heavy ion collisions.
