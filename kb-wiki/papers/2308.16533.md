# Influence of the symmetry energy on the nuclear binding energies and the neutron drip line position

> A clear connection can be established between properties of nuclear matter and finite-nuclei observables, such as the correlation between the slope of the symmetry energy and dipole polarizability, or between compressibility and the isoscalar monopole giant resonance excitation energy. Establishing a connection between realistic atomic nuclei and an idealized infinite nuclear matter leads to a better understanding of underlying physical mechanisms that govern nuclear dynamics. In this work, we aim to study the dependence of the binding energies and related quantities (e.g. location of drip lines, the total number of bound even-even nuclei) on the symmetry energy $S_2(ρ)$. The properties of finite nuclei are calculated by employing the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model, assuming even-even axial and reflection symmetric nuclei. Calculations are performed by employing two families of relativistic energy density functionals (EDFs), based on different effective Lagrangians, constrained to a specific symmetry energy at saturation density $J$ within the interval of $30$--$36$ MeV. Nuclear binding energies and related quantities of bound nuclei are calculated between $8 \leq Z \leq 104$ from the two-proton to the two-neutron drip line. As the neutron drip line is approached, the interactions with stiffer $J$ tend to predict more bound nuclei, resulting in a systematic shift of the two-neutron drip line towards more neutron-rich nuclei. Consequentially, a correlation between the number of bound nuclei $N_{nucl}$ and $S_2(ρ)$ is established for a set of functionals constrained using the similar optimization procedures. The direction of the relationship between the number of bound nuclei and symmetry energy highly depends on the density under consideration.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the correlation between the number of bound even-even nuclei and the symmetry energy depends critically on the density at which the symmetry energy is evaluated; below an intersection density of approximately 0.10 fm⁻³ the number of bound nuclei decreases with increasing symmetry energy, while above that density the opposite trend occurs, resolving apparent contradictions in prior work.

## Method

The authors performed large-scale relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations for even-even axial and reflection symmetric nuclei with proton numbers 8 ≤ Z ≤ 104, using two families of relativistic energy density functionals (DD-PCJ and DD-MEJ) constrained to symmetry energy at saturation density J values of 30, 32, 34, and 36 MeV, plus three additional functionals (DD-PC1, DD-PCX, DD-ME2). The RHB equations were solved in an axially-deformed harmonic oscillator basis with 20 oscillator shells, and the ground state was determined by minimizing binding energy with respect to quadrupole deformation β₂ in the interval [-0.6, +0.6] using the augmented Lagrangian method.

## Key numbers

- Symmetry energy at saturation density J was varied systematically in the interval 30–36 MeV for the DD-PCJ and DD-MEJ families
- The intersection density ρ_c for the DD-PCJ family was approximately 0.10 fm⁻³
- At ρ = 0.08 fm⁻³ (below ρ_c), the number of bound nuclei decreased with increasing symmetry energy S₂
- At ρ = 0.12 fm⁻³ (above ρ_c), the number of bound nuclei increased with increasing symmetry energy S₂
- For neon isotopes, the neutron drip line was reached at ³⁴Ne, consistent with J = 32 and 34 MeV for both effective interactions
- For magnesium isotopes, ⁴⁰Mg was predicted as a drip line nucleus for J = 32 and 34 MeV, while it was not bound for J = 30 MeV
- The DD-PCJ functionals all had incompressibility K₀ = 230 MeV; the DD-MEJ functionals all had K₀ = 250 MeV

## Context

This work extends prior studies that found contradictory results regarding the correlation between symmetry energy and neutron drip line position: Ref. PhysRevC.89.054320 found no correlation with J at saturation density, while Ref. PhysRevC.92.031303 found a negative correlation at sub-saturation densities of 0.11/0.16 × ρ₀ using 42 relativistic and non-relativistic EDFs. The present study resolves this contradiction by showing that the direction of correlation depends on whether the symmetry energy is evaluated below or above the intersection density. The results enable future studies to constrain the symmetry energy by comparing theoretical predictions of drip line positions with experimental data as facilities uncover more neutron-rich nuclei, and could be extended to include odd nuclei.
