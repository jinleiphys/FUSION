# The Electrosphere of Macroscopic "Quark Nuclei": A Source for Diffuse MeV Emissions from Dark Matter

> Using a Thomas-Fermi model, we calculate the structure of the electrosphere of the quark antimatter nuggets postulated to comprise much of the dark matter. This provides a single self-consistent density profile from ultrarelativistic densities to the nonrelativistic Boltzmann regime that use to present microscopically justified calculations of several properties of the nuggets, including their net charge, and the ratio of MeV to 511 keV emissions from electron annihilation. We find that the calculated parameters agree with previous phenomenological estimates based on the observational supposition that the nuggets are a source of several unexplained diffuse emissions from the Galaxy. As no phenomenological parameters are required to describe these observations, the calculation provides another nontrivial verification of the dark-matter proposal. The structure of the electrosphere is quite general and will also be valid at the surface of strange-quark stars, should they exist.

## Key claim
Using a Thomas-Fermi model, the authors calculate a self-consistent density profile for the electrosphere of quark antimatter nuggets from ultrarelativistic densities to the nonrelativistic Boltzmann regime, finding that the calculated parameters agree with previous phenomenological estimates without requiring any free parameters. The hedge is that the calculation assumes the nuggets exist and that the core properties (such as pairing gaps and stability) are consistent with the model, but the electrosphere physics itself depends only on QED.

## Method
The authors solve the Poisson equation for the electrostatic potential outside the nugget core using a Thomas-Fermi model with a fully relativistic equation of state for positrons, including both particle and antiparticle contributions. They impose boundary conditions at the core surface (chemical potential μ_R ≈ 25 MeV) and at large distances where charge equilibrium with the interstellar medium sets the net charge. The one-dimensional approximation (neglecting curvature) is used for the density profile near the surface, which is valid when the electrosphere thickness is much smaller than the nugget radius.

## Key numbers
- Nugget baryon charge |B| ≈ 10^20 – 10^30, giving core radius R ~ 10^{-7} – 10^{-3} cm
- Core density ρ ~ 10 GeV/fm^3 (a few times nuclear density)
- Core chemical potential at boundary μ_R ≈ 25 MeV (range 10–100 MeV)
- Formation temperature T_form = 41 MeV required to explain observed photon-to-baryon ratio n_B/n_γ ~ 10^{-10}
- Pairing gap Δ ≈ 100 MeV with critical temperature T_c ~ Δ/√2 ≈ 60 MeV
- Dark-matter-to-visible-matter ratio Ω_DM ≈ 5Ω_B
- Antinugget:nugget:visible baryon ratios ≈ 3:2:1 from charge balance equations
- Cross-section-to-mass ratio σ/M ≈ 10^{-13} – 10^{-9} cm^2/g (well below astrophysical limits of σ/M < 1 cm^2/g)
- 511 keV emission from positronium decay: 25% as sharp line from singlet state, 75% as three-photon continuum from triplet state
- Observed positronium fraction in 511 keV spectrum: 94 ± 4%

## Context
This work extends the quark nugget dark matter model previously developed by Zhitnitsky (2002), Oaknin and Zhitnitsky (2003), Forbes and Zhitnitsky (2006, 2008), and Lawson and Zhitnitsky (2007). It provides a microscopic QED-based calculation of the electrosphere structure that replaces phenomenological parameters used in earlier work to explain five unexplained diffuse emissions from the Galactic core: the 511 keV line from INTEGRAL/SPI, the 1–20 MeV excess from COMPTEL/CGRO, diffuse X-rays from Chandra, GeV constraints from EGRET/CGRO, and the WMAP haze. The results also apply to the surface of strange-quark stars, should they exist, as discussed by Alcock (1986), Kettner (1994), Cheng (2003), and Usov (2004).
