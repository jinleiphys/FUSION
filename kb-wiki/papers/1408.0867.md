# Formulation of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics and its applications in heavy-ion collisions

> Relativistic fluid dynamics finds application in astrophysics, cosmology and the physics of high-energy heavy-ion collisions. In this thesis, we present our work on the formulation of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics within the framework of relativistic kinetic theory. We employ the second law of thermodynamics as well as the relativistic Boltzmann equation to obtain the dissipative evolution equations. We present a new derivation of the dissipative hydrodynamic equations using the second law of thermodynamics wherein all the second-order transport coefficients get determined uniquely within a single theoretical framework. An alternate derivation of the dissipative equations which does not make use of the two major approximations/assumptions namely, Grad's 14-moment approximation and second moment of Boltzmann equation, inherent in the Israel-Stewart theory, is also presented. Moreover, by solving the Boltzmann equation iteratively in a Chapman-Enskog like expansion, we have derived the form of second-order viscous corrections to the distribution function. Furthermore, a novel third-order evolution equation for shear stress tensor is derived. Finally, we generalize the collision term in the Boltzmann equation to include non-local effects. We find that the second-order dissipative equations derived using this modified Boltzmann equation contains all possible terms allowed by symmetry. In the case of one-dimensional scaling expansion, we demonstrate the numerical significance of these formulations on the evolution of the hot and dense matter created in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. We also study the effect of these new formulations on particle (hadron and thermal dilepton) spectra and femtoscopic radii.

## Key claim

This thesis presents a new derivation of relativistic dissipative fluid-dynamic equations from kinetic theory that determines all second-order transport coefficients uniquely within a single theoretical framework, and also provides an alternate derivation that avoids the two major approximations of the Israel-Stewart theory (Grad's 14-moment approximation and the second moment of the Boltzmann equation). The claim is qualified by the use of the relaxation-time approximation for the collision term and by the restriction to a Chapman-Enskog-like iterative expansion.

## Method

The author employs the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation together with the second law of thermodynamics to derive evolution equations for the shear stress tensor, bulk viscous pressure, and particle diffusion current. A Chapman-Enskog-like iterative expansion of the distribution function in powers of space-time gradients is used to obtain first-order, second-order, and novel third-order viscous corrections, with all transport coefficients expressed in terms of thermodynamic integrals \(J_{nq}^{(m)}\) and \(I_{nq}^{(m)}\). The collision term is later generalized to include non-local effects, and the resulting second-order dissipative equations are shown to contain all possible terms allowed by symmetry.

## Key numbers

* The first-order shear viscosity coefficient is \(\beta_\pi = \frac{4P}{5} + \frac{\epsilon-3P}{15} - \frac{m^4}{15}\langle (u\!\cdot\!p)^{-2}\rangle_0\), where \(\langle\cdots\rangle_0 = \int dp (\cdots) f_0\).
* The first-order bulk viscosity coefficient is \(\beta_\Pi = \frac{1}{3}(1-3c_s^2)(\epsilon+P) - \frac{2}{9}(\epsilon-3P) - \frac{m^4}{9}\langle (u\!\cdot\!p)^{-2}\rangle_0\).
* The first-order particle diffusion coefficient is \(\beta_n = -\frac{n^2}{\beta(\epsilon+P)} + \frac{2\langle 1\rangle_0}{3\beta} + \frac{m^2}{3\beta}\langle (u\!\cdot\!p)^{-2}\rangle_0\).
* The second-order shear relaxation coefficient is \(\tau_{\pi\pi} = 2\beta(2J^{(3)}_{63}+J^{(1)}_{42})/\beta_\pi\).
* The second-order shear-bulk coupling coefficient is \(\lambda_{\pi\Pi} = 2\big[(J^{(0)}_{31}+J^{(1)}_{42})\frac{J_{20}^{(0)}n - J_{10}^{(0)}(\epsilon+P)}{J_{20}^{(0)}J_{20}^{(0)}-J_{30}^{(0)}J_{10}^{(0)}} - (J^{(1)}_{31}+J^{(2)}_{42})\frac{J_{30}^{(0)}n - J_{20}^{(0)}(\epsilon+P)}{J_{20}^{(0)}J_{20}^{(0)}-J_{30}^{(0)}J_{10}^{(0)}} + \frac{1}{3}\beta(7J^{(3)}_{63}+5J^{(1)}_{42})\big]/\beta_\Pi\).
* For one-dimensional Bjorken flow, the expansion scalar is \(\theta = 1/\tau\), and the shear stress tensor component satisfies \(\pi^{\eta\eta} = -\Phi/\tau^2\) with \(\pi^{xx}=\pi^{yy}=\Phi/2\).
* The second-order viscous correction to the hadron spectrum in one-dimensional scaling expansion is given by an expression involving modified Bessel functions \(K_n(z_m)\) and integrals \(\mathcal{I}_n(z)\), with \(z_m = m_T/T\) and \(z_p = p_T/T\).

## Context

This work extends the Israel-Stewart theory of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics by providing a derivation that avoids Grad's 14-moment approximation and the second moment of the Boltzmann equation, instead using a Chapman-Enskog-like expansion. The formulation determines all second-order transport coefficients uniquely from kinetic theory, in contrast to earlier approaches where some coefficients remained undetermined. The results enable more precise modeling of the hot and dense matter created in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, including predictions for hadron spectra, thermal dilepton spectra, and femtoscopic radii, and provide a framework for testing the numerical significance of higher-order viscous corrections in one-dimensional scaling expansion.
