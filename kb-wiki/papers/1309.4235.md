# The angular momentum controversy: What's it all about and does it matter?

> The general question, crucial to an understanding of the internal structure of the nucleon, of how to split the total angular momentum of a photon or gluon into spin and orbital contributions is one of the most important and interesting challenges faced by gauge theories like Quantum Electrodynamics and Quantum Chromodynamics. This is particularly challenging since all QED textbooks state that such an splitting cannot be done for a photon (and a fortiori for a gluon) in a gauge-invariant way, yet experimentalists around the world are engaged in measuring what they believe is the gluon spin! This question has been a subject of intense debate and controversy, ever since, in 2008, it was claimed that such a gauge-invariant split was, in fact, possible. We explain in what sense this claim is true and how it turns out that one of the main problems is that such a decomposition is not unique and therefore raises the question of what is the most natural or physical choice. The essential requirement of measurability does not solve the ambiguities and leads us to the conclusion that the choice of a particular decomposition is essentially a matter of taste and convenience. In this review, we provide a pedagogical introduction to the question of angular momentum decomposition in a gauge theory, present the main relevant decompositions and discuss in detail several aspects of the controversies regarding the question of gauge invariance, frame dependence, uniqueness and measurability. We stress the physical implications of the recent developments and collect into a separate section all the sum rules and relations which we think experimentally relevant. We hope that such a review will make the matter amenable to a broader community and will help to clarify the present situation.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while a gauge-invariant separation of the total angular momentum of a photon or gluon into spin and orbital parts is mathematically possible, such a decomposition is not unique and the choice between infinitely many possible decompositions is essentially a matter of taste and convenience, with no compelling physical argument for any single choice.

## Method

The authors provide a pedagogical review of angular momentum decomposition in gauge theories, starting from Noether's theorem to derive canonical and Belinfante forms of the energy-momentum and angular momentum densities for QED and QCD. They analyze the Chen et al. (2008) approach that splits the vector potential into "pure" and "physical" parts, showing that this leads to a non-local field operator, and then demonstrate that there exist an infinite family of such decompositions related by a Stueckelberg symmetry, concluding that only the canonical and Belinfante versions are fundamentally distinct.

## Key numbers

- The paper states that the Chen et al. decomposition uses the condition \(\uvec\nabla\cdot \uvec A_\phys =0\) and \(\uvec\nabla\times \uvec A_\pure =\uvec 0\) to split the vector potential.
- The non-local operator \(\frac{1}{\uvec\nabla ^2}\) is defined as \(-\frac{1}{4\pi}\int \ud^3x' \, \frac{f(\uvec x')}{| \uvec x - \uvec x'|}\), showing the integral nature of the "physical" field.
- The commutation relation \([x^i, p^j]= i\hbar\,\delta^{ij}\) is given for the canonical momentum in quantum mechanics.
- The angular momentum commutation relation \([J^i , J^j]= i \epsilon^{ijk}J^k\) is stated.
- The boost-angular momentum commutation relation \([J^i , K^j]= i \epsilon^{ijk} K^k\) is given, showing that transverse angular momentum changes under boosts.

## Context

This review extends the controversy sparked by Chen et al. (2008), who claimed that gauge-invariant separation of photon angular momentum into spin and orbital parts was possible, contradicting standard QED textbook statements from authors such as Wentzel (1949), Gottfried (1966), and Merzbacher (1970). The paper synthesizes the subsequent theoretical work by Ji (2009, 2010, 2012), Wakamatsu (2010-2013), Hatta (2011-2012), and others, and clarifies that the ambiguity in decomposition has no unique resolution, enabling experimentalists to continue measuring quantities like \(\Delta G(x)\) in the light-front gauge \(A^+=0\) without needing to resolve the theoretical controversy.
