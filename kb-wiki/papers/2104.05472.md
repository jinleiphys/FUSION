# Nuclear Matrix Elements for Heavy Ion Sequential Double Charge Exchange Reactions

> The theoretical approach to a sequential heavy ion double charge exchange reaction is presented. A brief introduction into the formal theory of second-order nuclear reactions and their application to Double Single Charge Exchange (DSCE) reactions by distorted wave theory is given, thereby completing the theoretical background to our recent work [1]. Formally, the DSCE reaction amplitudes are shown to be separable into superpositions of distortion factors, accounting for initial and final state ion--ion interactions, and nuclear matrix elements. A broad space is given to the construction of nuclear DSCE response functions on the basis of polarization propagator theory. The nuclear response tensors resemble the nuclear matrix elements of $2νββ$ decay in structure but contain in general a considerable more complex multipole and spin structure. The QRPA theory is used to derive explicit expressions for nuclear matrix elements (NMEs). The differences between the NME of the first and the second interaction vertexes in a DSCE reaction is elucidated. Reduction schemes for the transition form factors are discussed by investigating the closure approximation and the momentum structure of form factors. DSCE unit strength cross sections are derived.

# Nuclear Matrix Elements for Heavy Ion Sequential Double Charge Exchange Reactions

## Key claim

The paper presents a theoretical reformulation of heavy ion double single charge exchange (DSCE) reaction amplitudes from the standard t-channel representation into an s-channel representation, thereby establishing a formal connection to the nuclear matrix elements of 2νββ decay, but with the caveat that the DSCE nuclear response tensors contain a considerably more complex multipole and spin structure than the corresponding double beta decay matrix elements.

## Method

The authors derive the second-order DSCE reaction amplitude using distorted wave theory, starting from the isovector NN T-matrix expressed as one-body operators acting in projectile and target nuclei. They perform extensive angular momentum recoupling to transform the nuclear polarization tensor from the t-channel (meson-exchange ordering) to the s-channel (nuclear state evolution ordering), thereby separating projectile and target response functions. The nuclear structure input is obtained from Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) theory for ground states and Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) for excited states, with the final states in the daughter nuclei described as four-quasiparticle configurations relative to the parent ground state.

## Key numbers

- The measured angular distribution for the reaction ¹⁸O + ⁴⁰Ca → ¹⁸Ne + ⁴⁰Ar at T_lab = 270 MeV covers linear momentum transfer up to about 500 MeV/c over the measured angular range.
- The full account of rank-0 central, rank-1 spin-orbit, and rank-2 tensor NN-vertices results in a total of at least 4⁴ = 256 form factors, distributed half by half in the projectile and target.
- For 0⁺ → 0⁺ transitions, the total angular momentum transfer is constrained to I_A = 0, allowing only the combinations L = 0, S = 0 (sequential Fermi or Gamow-Teller modes) and L = 2, S = 2 (sequential Gamow-Teller-type transitions of the same total multipolarity), while L = 1, S = 1 is forbidden by parity conservation.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous quantitative description of heavy ion DCE data (Bellone, 2020) by providing the formal theoretical framework for extracting double beta decay nuclear matrix elements from reaction cross sections. It contrasts with earlier pair transfer scenarios (Dasso, 1986; Dasso, 1985) which were found negligible for the reactions considered, and with the interacting boson model approach (Santopinto, 2018) which used eikonal and closure approximations. The formalism enables future extraction of DSCE multipole unit cross sections that account for reaction dynamical aspects, potentially allowing direct extraction of DSCE nuclear matrix elements from experimental data, which is a central goal of the NUMEN project (Cappuzzello, 2018).
