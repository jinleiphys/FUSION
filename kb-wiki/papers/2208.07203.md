# A Semi-analytical Method of Calculating Nuclear Collision Trajectory in the QCD Phase Diagram

> The finite nuclear thickness affects the energy density $ε(t)$ and conserved-charge densities such as the net-baryon density $n_B(t)$ produced in heavy ion collisions. While the effect is small at high collision energies where the Bjorken energy density formula for the initial state is valid, the effect is large at low collision energies, where the nuclear crossing time is not small compared to the parton formation time. The temperature $T(t)$ and chemical potentials $μ(t)$ of the dense matter can be extracted from the densities for a given equation of state (EOS). Therefore, including the nuclear thickness is essential for the determination of the $T$-$μ_B$ trajectory in the QCD phase diagram for relativistic nuclear collisions at low to moderate energies such as the RHIC-BES energies. In this proceeding, we will first discuss our semi-analytical method that includes the nuclear thickness effect and its results on the densities $ε(t), n_B(t), n_Q(t)$, and $n_S(t)$. Then, we will show the extracted $T(t), μ_B(t), μ_Q(t)$, and $μ_S(t)$ for a quark-gluon plasma using the ideal gas EOS with quantum or Boltzmann statistics. Finally, we will show the results on the $T$-$μ_B$ trajectories in relation to the possible location of the QCD critical end point. This semi-analytical model provides a convenient tool for exploring the trajectories of nuclear collisions in the QCD phase diagram.

## Key claim

The finite nuclear thickness significantly affects the energy density and conserved-charge densities produced in heavy ion collisions at low to moderate energies (such as RHIC-BES energies), and including this effect is essential for determining the T-μB trajectory in the QCD phase diagram; the authors present a semi-analytical method that extends the Bjorken formula by accounting for both the finite crossing time and the finite longitudinal width of the nuclear overlap region.

## Method

The method first calculates the time evolution of four densities (energy density ε, net-baryon density nB, net-electric-charge density nQ, and net-strangeness density nS) at mid-rapidity averaged over the transverse overlap area for central Au+Au collisions, using integrals over the production area in the z-t plane that account for the full nuclear crossing diamond. These densities are then converted into thermodynamic quantities (T, μB, μQ, μS) using an ideal gas equation of state for the quark-gluon plasma with either quantum or Boltzmann statistics, by solving the four equations relating densities to temperature and chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- For central Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 5 GeV with τF = 0.1 fm/c, the peak energy density εmax and peak net-baryon density nBmax are significantly lower than those from the Bjorken formula, while at √sNN = 200 GeV the results approach the Bjorken formula.
- The peak net-baryon density nBmax first increases and then decreases with √sNN, while the peak energy density εmax increases with √sNN.
- At the threshold energy √sNN = 2mN (with mN the nucleon mass), the model gives εmax = 2ρ0mN and nBmax = 2ρ0, where ρ0 ≈ 0.17 fm−3.
- The QCD critical end point from FRG calculations is located at (μB, T) = (0.635 GeV, 0.107 GeV).
- Two constant energy density curves from the partial-1 solution intersect the endpoints of the FRG crossover curve at ε = 1.23 GeV/fm3 and ε = 0.51 GeV/fm3.
- For the quantum ideal gas EOS at τF = 0.1 fm/c, the peak temperature Tmax increases with √sNN while the peak net-baryon chemical potential μBmax decreases with √sNN.

## Context

This work extends the Bjorken energy density formula (Bjorken, 1983) by including the finite nuclear thickness effect, building on earlier extensions that considered only the finite crossing time (Lin, 2018) and then both finite time and finite width (Mendenhall, 2021). The semi-analytical method provides a convenient tool for exploring collision trajectories in the QCD phase diagram that complements dynamical models such as transport and hydrodynamical models (Arsene, 2006; Wang, 2021) and approaches that assume constant s/nB along the trajectory (Noronha-Hostler, 2019). The results enable systematic studies of how the parton formation time τF and the equation of state affect the accessible region of the phase diagram, including the possible location of the QCD critical end point.
