# Generalized isobaric multiplet mass equation and its application to the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly

> The Wigner Isobaric Multiplet Mass Equation (IMME) is the most fundamental prediction in nuclear physics with the concept of isospin. However, it was deduced based on the Wigner-Eckart theorem with the assumption that all charge-violating interactions can be written as tensors of rank two. In the present work, the charge-symmetry breaking (CSB) and charge-independent breaking (CIB) components of the nucleon-nucleon force, which contribute to the effective interaction in nuclear medium, are established in the framework of Brueckner theory with AV18 and AV14 bare interactions. Because such charge-violating components can no longer be expressed as an irreducible tensor due to density dependence, its matrix element cannot be analytically reduced by the Wigner-Eckart theorem. With an alternative approach, we derive a generalized IMME (GIMME) that modifies the coefficients of the original IMME. As the first application of GIMME, we study the long-standing question for the origin of the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly found in the Coulomb displacement energy of mirror nuclei. We find that the naturally-emerged CSB term in GIMME is largely responsible for explaining the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly.

## Key claim

The paper derives a generalized isobaric multiplet mass equation (GIMME) that includes corrections from charge-symmetry breaking (CSB) and charge-independent breaking (CIB) components of the nucleon-nucleon force in nuclear medium, and finds that the naturally-emerged CSB term accounts for a large part of the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly without requiring any adjustable parameters.

## Method

The authors used Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory with AV18 and AV14 bare interactions to compute the density-dependent CSB and CIB contributions to the symmetry energy of nuclear matter, characterized by the 1st-order symmetry energy coefficient S1(CSB) and the 2nd-order coefficient S2(CIB). They then constructed Skyrme energy density functionals for these effective CSB and CIB interactions, and applied them to finite nuclei using Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-BCS calculations with SLy4, SLy5, and KDE interactions. The resulting symmetry energy terms were incorporated into a generalized IMME that modifies the coefficients of the original quadratic form, and the new expression for Coulomb displacement energy (CDE) was used to evaluate the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly.

## Key numbers

- The fitted coefficient a0 for S1(CSB)(rho) = a0 rho is -1.05132 MeV fm^-3.
- The fitted coefficients for S2(CIB)(rho) = a1 rho + a2 rho^2 + a3 rho^3 are: a1 = 1.49199 MeV fm^-3, a2 = -10.96773 MeV fm^-6, a3 = 39.58976 MeV fm^-9.
- For the T=3/2 quartets, the CSB effect reduces the coefficient of the T_z term by 2.0% to 3.1%, and the CIB effect enhances the coefficient of the T_z^2 term by 1.6% to 4.4%.
- The calculated Delta_NSA = -2*a_sym,1^(CSB) for the T=1/2 mirror pair 15O-15N is 0.16 MeV (SLy4, SLy5, KDE all give 0.16 MeV).
- For 17F-17O, Delta_NSA = 0.11 MeV (SLy4, SLy5, KDE all give 0.11 MeV).
- For 39Ca-39K, Delta_NSA = 0.15-0.16 MeV across the three interactions.
- For 41Sc-41Ca, Delta_NSA = 0.14-0.15 MeV across the three interactions.
- The CSB contribution to CDE for T=1 mirror pairs is 2% to 3% of the total CDE.
- For T_z = +/-1/2 mirror pairs, Delta_NSA adds 100-200 keV to CDE; for T_z = +/-3/2 mirror pairs, it adds 300-600 keV.

## Context

This work extends the original Wigner IMME (Wigner, 1937) by incorporating density-dependent CSB and CIB interactions derived from microscopic Brueckner theory, which cannot be treated as irreducible tensors and thus evade the Wigner-Eckart theorem. The GIMME is applied to the Nolen-Schiffer anomaly (Nolen and Schiffer, 1969), which had previously been studied using phenomenological corrections such as nucleon mass splitting or meson mixing (e.g., Hatsuda 1990, Cohen 1991, Epele 1992), and with shell-model calculations that add fitted symmetry-breaking terms (Ormand 1989, Lam 2013). The present approach provides a parameter-free explanation from nuclear medium effects, and enables future studies of charge-exchange reactions, Gamow-Teller transitions, and beta-decays using the derived effective CSB and CIB interactions.
