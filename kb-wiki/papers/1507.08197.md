# Dark Compact Planets

> We investigate compact objects formed by dark matter admixed with ordinary matter made of neutron star matter and white dwarf material. We consider non-self annihilating dark matter with an equation-of-state given by an interacting Fermi gas. We find new stable solutions, dark compact planets, with Earth-like masses or lower and radii about one meter for weakly interacting dark matter. For the strongly interacting dark matter case, we obtain dark compact planets with Jupiter-like masses or lower and radii of a kilometer. These objects could be formed primordially and accrete white dwarf material subsequently. They could be detected by observing exoplanets with unusually small radii. Moreover, we find that the recently observed 2 ${\rm M}_{\odot}$ pulsars set limits on the amount of dark matter inside neutron stars which is, at most, $10^{-5}{\rm M}_\odot$.

# Dark Compact Planets

## Key claim
The paper claims that stable "dark compact planets" (DCPs) can exist as gravitationally bound two-fluid systems of ordinary matter (neutron star matter or white dwarf material) and non-self-annihilating dark matter, but only under specific conditions: the dark matter must be non-self-annihilating with a particle mass of 100 GeV, and the stability depends on the ratio of central pressures between dark matter and ordinary matter, with the objects being stabilized by the mutual presence of both components.

## Method
The authors solve the coupled Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a two-fluid system of ordinary matter and dark matter interacting only through gravity, using the equation-of-state EoSI from Kurkela (2014) for neutron star matter and an interacting Fermi gas equation-of-state from Narain (2006) for 100 GeV non-self-annihilating dark matter particles. They perform a stability analysis via small radial perturbations and the Sturm-Liouville eigenvalue equation to identify stable configurations, studying both weakly interacting (strength parameter y=10^{-1}) and strongly interacting (y=10^{3}) dark matter cases across different ratios of central pressures.

## Key numbers
- For weakly interacting dark matter (y=10^{-1}) with pressure ratio p'_DM/p'_OM=10^{4}: Earth-like masses of M=10^{-4} to 10^{-7} M_sun and radii from few km to few hundred km
- For strongly interacting dark matter (y=10^{3}): Jupiter-like masses ranging from 10^{-2} to 10^{-5} M_sun with radii of few hundred km
- Dark matter content of Earth-like DCPs: M=10^{-3} to 10^{-8} M_sun
- Dark matter content of Jupiter-like DCPs: M=10^{-1} to 10^{-5} M_sun
- Maximum accreted dark matter mass onto neutron stars: 10^{-14} M_sun
- Maximum accreted dark matter mass onto white dwarfs: 10^{-12} M_sun
- Free streaming lower limit for primordial dark matter mass: 10^{-7} M_sun
- Kepler limits rule out primordial dark objects of 2x10^{-9} to 10^{-7} M_sun as constituting all Milky Way dark matter
- EROS limits rule out 0.6x10^{-7} to 15 M_sun as constituting all Milky Way dark matter
- For 2 M_sun neutron stars: dark matter content must be at most 10^{-6} M_sun (from the abstract) or 10^{-6} M_sun (from the text, for weakly interacting case)
- For strongly interacting dark matter, maximum neutron star mass falls below 2 M_sun for dark matter masses greater than 10^{-8} M_sun

## Context
This work extends previous studies of dark matter admixed with neutron star matter by Sandin (2008), Leung (2011), and Xiang (2013), and white dwarfs by Leung (2013), by performing the first stability analysis of the two-fluid system for 100 GeV non-self-annihilating dark matter. The paper introduces the concept of dark compact planets as a new class of astrophysical objects that could be detected through exoplanet searches (Kepler, gravitational microlensing by MACHO, MOA, OGLE, EROS) by observing unusually small radii implying extremely high mass densities. The results also set constraints on the maximum dark matter content in the recently observed 2 M_sun pulsars from Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013), limiting dark matter to at most 10^{-6} M_sun inside such neutron stars.
