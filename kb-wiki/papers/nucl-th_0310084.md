# Nuclear Periphery in Mean-Field Models

> The halo factor is one of the experimental data which describes a distribution of neutrons in nuclear periphery. In the presented paper we use Skyrme-Hartree (SH) and the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) models and we calculate the neutron excess factor $Δ_B$ defined in the paper which differs slightly from halo factor $f_{\rm exp}$. The results of the calculations are compared to the measured data.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a neutron excess factor (NEF) \(\Delta_B\) derived from Bethe's function \(\delta_B(\vec r)\), which is defined using only internal nuclear densities and does not require knowledge of the asymptotic tail behavior of the density distributions; this factor is shown to be proportional to the experimentally measured halo factor \(f_{\rm exp}\) from antiproton annihilation data.

## Method

The authors calculate the Bethe function \(\delta_B(\vec r) = (Z/N)\rho_N(\vec r) - \rho_Z(\vec r)\) from the neutron and proton densities obtained via the Skyrme-Hartree (SH) model with SkVII force and the Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) model with NL3 parameter set. They then compute the radial integral \(I_B(r) = -4\pi\int_0^r r'^2 \delta_B(r')\,dr'\) and define the neutron excess factor \(\Delta_B\) as the maximum value of \(I_B(r)\) with respect to \(r\). For deformed nuclei, the integrals are taken over the deformed surface region. The resulting \(\Delta_B\) values are compared to the experimental halo factor \(f_{\rm exp}\) measured by the LEAR group at CERN.

## Key numbers

* For \(^{208}\)Pb, the maximum of \(I_B(r)\) occurs at \(R = 6.5\) fm, which corresponds to the zero of \(\delta_B(r)\) and defines the neutron excess factor.
* The average radius of antiproton stopping is slightly larger than the average nuclear radius \(R = r_0 A^{1/3}\) by about 2 fm.
* The calculations of \(\Delta_B\) in both RMF and SH models are strongly correlated with the experimental halo factor \(f_{\rm exp}\) for the nuclei studied by the LEAR group at CERN (Lubinski 1998, Schmidt 1999).

## Context

This work extends the use of Bethe's function (Bethe 1970) to predict neutron halo properties without requiring asymptotic density tails, which are poorly reproduced by mean-field models that give \(\exp(-\gamma r^2)\) behavior instead of the correct \(\exp(-\beta r)\) form. The approach is tested against antiproton annihilation data from the LEAR facility at CERN (Lubinski 1998, Schmidt 1999) and shows that the NEF \(\Delta_B\) is a good indicator of the peripheral neutron excess. The results enable future comparisons of mean-field models with experimental halo data for a wider range of nuclei, including deformed systems (Baran 2004).
