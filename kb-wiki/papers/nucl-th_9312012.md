# S-wave $π$-Nucleus Repulsion and Dirac Phenomenology

> A relativistic $π$-nucleon potential is extended to $m^* \neq m$ to investigate the possibility of generating s-wave $π$-nucleus repulsion. We find that relativity does indeed generate significant repulsion, the exact amount depending on the details of the calculation. In contradistinction the $tρ$ approximation gives very little repulsion.

## Key claim

Relativity does generate significant s-wave π-nucleus repulsion, but the amount depends on the choice of in-medium coupling constants; with the more realistic choice of fixed dressed couplings, the repulsion is only about half of the 30 MeV needed to fit experimental data, so the effect is in the correct direction but insufficient to fully resolve the problem.

## Method

The authors extended the relativistic π-nucleon potential of Pearce and Jennings (1991) by replacing the free nucleon mass m with an effective mass m* in all energy denominators of nucleon and delta pole and crossed-pole terms and in the free spinors, while keeping the pseudo-vector coupling definition unchanged. They calculated s-wave scattering lengths for two choices of in-medium couplings (fixed bare couplings and fixed physical couplings) and compared with the tρ approximation, using a nuclear matter density of 0.17 fm⁻³ to extract isoscalar pseudopotentials.

## Key numbers

- The isoscalar πN s-wave scattering length at threshold is b₀ = -0.010 mπ⁻¹ (mπ being the pion mass).
- The central pion-nucleus repulsion required by experimental data fits is about 30 MeV.
- With fixed bare couplings, the repulsion at nuclear matter density is about 20 MeV.
- With fixed dressed (physical) couplings, the repulsion is cut almost in half relative to the fixed bare case.
- The nuclear matter density used for pseudopotential extraction is 0.17 fm⁻³.

## Context

This work extends the observation by Birbrair and Gridnev (1990) that Nuclear Dirac Phenomenology could offer additional s-wave repulsion, while addressing criticisms from Gal (1992) about off-shell ambiguities and from Koch and Oset (1992) about the validity of the impulse approximation. The results enable a first step toward developing a full relativistic optical potential for pion-nucleus scattering, which would require including other partial waves and Pauli blocking, and is relevant for reactions like (p,nπ⁺) where a Dirac approach is natural.
