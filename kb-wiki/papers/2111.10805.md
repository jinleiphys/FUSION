# Modulation of pulse profile as a signal for phase transitions in a pulsar core

> We calculate detailed modification of pulses from a pulsar arising from the effects of phase transition induced density fluctuations on the pulsar moment of inertia. We represent general statistical density fluctuations using a simple model where the initial moment of inertia tensor of the pulsar (taken to be diagonal here) is assumed to get random additional contributions for each of its component which are taken to be Gaussian distributed with certain width characterized by the strength of density fluctuations $ε$. Using sample values of $ε$, (and the pulsar deformation parameter $η$) we numerically calculate detailed pulse modifications by solving Euler's equations for the rotational dynamics of the pulsar. We also give analytical estimates which can be used for arbitrary values of $ε$ and $η$. We show that there are very specific patterns in the perturbed pulses which are observable in terms of modulations of pulses over large time periods. In view of the fact that density fluctuations fade away eventually leading to a uniform phase in the interior of pulsar, the off-diagonal components of MI tensor also vanish eventually. Thus, the modification of pulses due to induced wobbling (from the off-diagonal MI components) will also die away eventually. This allows one to distinguish these transient pulse modulations from the effects of any wobbling originally present. Further, the decay of these modulations in time directly relates to relaxation of density fluctuations in the pulsar giving valuable information about the nature of phase transition occurring inside the pulsar.

## Key claim

Phase transition induced density fluctuations in a pulsar core produce transient, non-zero off-diagonal components of the moment of inertia tensor, causing the pulsar to develop a wobble that modulates the pulse profile; these modulations decay as the density fluctuations relax, allowing them to be distinguished from any pre-existing wobble, provided the deformation parameter η is sufficiently large relative to the fluctuation strength ε.

## Method

The authors model the unperturbed pulsar as an oblate spheroid with a diagonal moment of inertia tensor and add Gaussian-distributed random perturbations δI_ij with width σ = ε I_0 to each component. They solve Euler's equations for the rotational dynamics, compute the resulting precession frequency Ω ≈ η ω, and numerically track the time-dependent orientation of a conical emission region on the pulsar surface to calculate the modified pulse profile as seen by a distant observer.

## Key numbers

- The fractional change in moment of inertia components δI_ij/I_0 is taken to be of order ε, with sample values ε = 10⁻⁸ and ε = 10⁻⁵.
- The deformation parameter η (oblateness) is sampled in the range 10⁻³ to 10⁻² for the numerical calculations.
- The precession frequency is Ω ≈ η ω; for a millisecond pulsar with η = 10⁻³, the precession period is of order 1 second.
- The angular shift of the principal axis immediately after the transition is θ₀ ≈ √2 (ε/η); for η = 10⁻³ and ε = 10⁻⁸, this gives θ₀ ≈ 1.4 × 10⁻⁵ rad.
- The amplitude of the precession angle θ_m ≈ √2 (ε/η²); for η = 10⁻³ and ε = 10⁻⁸, this yields θ_m ≈ 1°.
- The amplitude of frequency oscillations ω_m ≈ ω θ₀ ≈ √2 (ε/η) ω.

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Bagchi (2015) that phase transitions in pulsar cores produce statistical density fluctuations affecting the moment of inertia tensor, and provides the first detailed calculation of the resulting pulse profile modulations. It contrasts with standard glitch models (which invoke vortex unpinning and primarily affect spin rate without perturbing the rotation axis) and with studies of free precession in pulsars such as those applied to PSR 1828-11 by Stairs (2000) and modeled by Jones (2001) for crust-core coupling. The results enable future work to distinguish different phase transition types (first-order bubble nucleation, spinodal decomposition, second-order transitions) by their characteristic density fluctuation statistics and relaxation timescales.
