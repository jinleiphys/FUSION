# Improved pion mean fields and masses of singly heavy baryons

> A singly heavy baryon can be viewed as $N_c-1$ ($N_c$ as the number of colors) light valence quarks bound by the pion mean fields that are created by the presence of the $N_c-1$ valence quarks self-consistently, while the heavy quark inside a singly heavy baryon is regarded as a static color source. We investigate how the pion mean fields are created by the presence of $N_c$, $N_c-1$, and $N_c-2$ light valence quarks, which correspond to the systems of light baryons, singly heavy baryons, and doubly heavy baryons. As the number of color decreases from $N_c$ to $N_c-1$, the pion mean fields undergo changes. As a result, the valence-quark contributions to the moments of inertia of the soliton become larger than the case of the $N_c$ valence quarks, whereas the sea-quark contributions decrease systematically. On the other hand, the presence of the $N_c-2$ valence quarks is not enough to produce the strong pion mean fields, which leads to the fact that the classical soliton can not be formed. It indicates that the pion mean-field approach is not suitable to describe doubly heavy baryons. We show that the mass spectra of the singly heavy baryons are better described by the improved pion mean fields, compared with the previous work in which the pion mean fields are assumed to be intact with $N_c$ varied.

## Key claim

The pion mean fields that bind light valence quarks in singly heavy baryons are weaker and more compact than those in light baryons, because reducing the number of valence quarks from \(N_c\) to \(N_c-1\) weakens the vacuum polarization; the abstract's claim of "improved" description is conditional on solving the classical equations of motion self-consistently rather than assuming the mean fields are unchanged when the valence-quark number is varied.

## Method

The authors solve the classical equation of motion for the chiral quark-soliton model (χQSM) self-consistently for systems with \(N_c\), \(N_c-1\), and \(N_c-2\) light valence quarks, corresponding to light baryons, singly heavy baryons, and doubly heavy baryons respectively. The soliton profile function \(\Theta(r)\) is obtained by iterating the one-body Dirac equation and the mean-field equations until convergence (Hartree approximation). The collective zero-mode quantization is then performed, and the masses of singly heavy baryons in the antitriplet, sextet, and antidecapentaplet representations are computed including first-order strange-quark mass corrections and a phenomenological hyperfine interaction.

## Key numbers

- Soliton size: approximately 0.6 fm for light baryons (\(N_c=3\)) versus approximately 0.4 fm for singly heavy baryons (\(N_c-1=2\)), at dynamical quark mass \(M = 420\) MeV.
- \(1/I_1 = 178\) MeV (model value) for the mass splitting between baryon antitriplet and sextet; experimental data suggest approximately 172 MeV.
- \(1/I_2 = 379\) MeV for the moment of inertia relevant to the antidecapentaplet center masses.
- Hyperfine splitting parameters: \(\varkappa/m_c = 68.1\) MeV for charmed baryons; \(\varkappa/m_b = 20.3\) MeV for bottom baryons.
- No self-consistent pion mean-field solution exists for doubly heavy baryons (\(N_c-2\) valence quarks) at \(M = 420\) MeV; a solution appears only when the dynamical quark mass exceeds approximately 600 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the chiral quark-soliton model treatment of singly heavy baryons beyond the earlier "model-independent approach" of Yang (2016) and Kim (2018), which fixed dynamical parameters from experiment without decomposing valence- and sea-quark contributions, and beyond the self-consistent calculation of Kim (2018) that assumed the pion mean fields were unchanged when the valence-quark number was reduced from \(N_c\) to \(N_c-1\). The improved mean fields enable better description of the mass spectra of singly heavy baryons and satisfy the von Laue stability condition for gravitational form factors (Kim, forthcoming). The result that doubly heavy baryons cannot be described by this approach sets a clear limitation on the pion mean-field framework.
