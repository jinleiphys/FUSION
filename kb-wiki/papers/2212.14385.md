# Global angular momentum generation in heavy-ion reactions within a hadronic transport approach

> In 2017, the STAR collaboration at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) has measured finite global angular momentum in heavy-ion collisions through a spin polarization measurement of $Λ$ hyperons. This measurement revealed a high angular momentum of the heavy ions and provided experimental evidence for vorticity in the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) for the first time. In order to investigate the underlying mechanisms, a dynamic description of the transfer of angular momentum is required. In this work, the microscopic non-equilibrium transport approach SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) is applied to study the generation of global angular momentum by the interaction of two nuclei. As SMASH provides access to the whole phase-space evolution of every particle at any given time, it allows to assess the fraction of angular momentum generated in the fireball by all participants. We confirm the previous modeling by Becattini \textit{et al} within a geometric Glauber model approach, which found that the angular momentum transfer reaches a unique maximum in mid-central collisions during time evolution. The corresponding impact parameter is around $b=4-6$ fm for all beam energies from $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.41-200$ GeV. Even though angular momentum is not conserved locally in the transport approach a priori, we identify the contributions to the conservation violation and propose optimal setups for different energy regimes that recover conservation, based upon the test particle method and the treatment of Fermi motion. Furthermore, the system size and centrality dependence are investigated.

## Key claim

The hadronic transport approach SMASH confirms the previous geometric Glauber model prediction by Becattini et al. (2008) that the angular momentum transferred to the fireball reaches a unique maximum in mid-central collisions, with the corresponding impact parameter remaining approximately beam-energy independent across the range from \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.41\) to 200 GeV.

## Method

The microscopic non-equilibrium transport approach SMASH (Simulating Many Accelerated Strongly-interacting Hadrons) was used to simulate heavy-ion collisions, solving the relativistic Boltzmann equation for hadronic degrees of freedom up to masses of approximately 2.3 GeV. The angular momentum of participants, spectators, and the total system was tracked over time for AuAu collisions at various beam energies and impact parameters, with the test particle method applied to restore local angular momentum conservation and the frozen Fermi approximation used to treat Fermi motion.

## Key numbers

- Initial angular momentum for an AuAu collision at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 39\) GeV and impact parameter \(b=5\) fm is approximately \(|L_0| \sim 2 \times 10^5 \hbar\)
- The impact parameter maximizing angular momentum transfer lies in the range \(b_{max} \in [4.3\ \text{fm}, 6.2\ \text{fm}]\) without Fermi motion and \(b_{max} \in [4.6\ \text{fm}, 6.6\ \text{fm}]\) with frozen Fermi motion, across beam energies from \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=2.41\) to 200 GeV
- For AuAu collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}=8.7\) GeV, the maximum angular momentum in the fireball occurs at \(b_{max} \approx 6.7\) fm
- The maximum impact parameter for angular momentum transfer drops to zero beyond \(b=17.0\) fm, where nuclei no longer overlap on average
- Fermi momentum is typically of order \(p_F \approx 300\) MeV, corresponding to an excess kinetic energy of roughly \(p_F^2/2m_N \sim 45\) MeV

## Context

This work extends the geometric Glauber model predictions by Becattini et al. (2008) by providing a dynamical, time-resolved description of angular momentum generation using the SMASH transport approach, which resolves the full phase-space evolution of every particle. The results are consistent with the STAR collaboration's 2017 measurement of \(\Lambda\) hyperon polarization at RHIC, which provided experimental evidence for vorticity in the quark-gluon plasma. The findings guide experimental programs in selecting systems and centralities for studying angular-momentum-related observables and may help identify signals of a phase transition in hadronic matter.
