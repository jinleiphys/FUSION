# Principal components of nuclear mass models

> The principal component analysis approach is employed to extract the principal components contained in nuclear mass models for the first time. The effects coming from different nuclear mass models are reintegrated and reorganized in the extracted principal components. These extracted principal components are recombined to build new mass models, which achieve better accuracy than the original theoretical mass models. This indicates that the effects contained in different mass models can work together to improve the nuclear mass predictions with the help of the principal component analysis approach.

## Key claim

The paper claims that principal component analysis (PCA) can extract and reorganize the major physical effects contained in different nuclear mass models, and that recombining these principal components yields new mass models with better accuracy than any of the original theoretical models. The hedge is that this improvement is demonstrated only for the specific set of six mass models studied, and the method's reliability in truly unknown regions is inferred from an extrapolation test rather than proven.

## Method

The authors applied principal component analysis to the mass predictions of six nuclear mass models (FRDM2012, HFB17, KTUY05, D1M, RMF, and LDM) for 6254 overlapping nuclei. They constructed a covariance matrix from the vectorized mass predictions of these models, diagonalized it to obtain six principal components (eigenvectors), and then recombined these components with superposition coefficients determined by overlaps with experimental mass data from AME2020. The new mass models were evaluated by their root-mean-square deviations from experimental data, and an extrapolation validation was performed using only nuclei with Z ≤ 60 to determine coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The eigenvalue of PC1 is 6.2×10¹⁰, which is much larger than the eigenvalue of PC2 at 3.5×10⁷, indicating PC1 captures the dominant bulk properties.
- The new mass model including the first four principal components achieves a precision of 519 keV, which is smaller than the smallest rms deviation among the six original models (591 keV for HFB17).
- Including all six principal components yields an rms deviation of 509 keV, compared to 913 keV for arithmetic averaging and 525 keV for weighted averaging of the six original models.
- When the RCHB model (rms 7960 keV) is added to make seven models, the new seven-PC model achieves 456 keV; when the WS4 model (rms 298 keV) is added, the new seven-PC model achieves 292 keV.
- In the extrapolation validation using only Z ≤ 60 nuclei to determine coefficients, the new model achieves 549 keV for all experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the application of PCA from previous studies that used it to analyze effective parameters of the liquid drop model and Skyrme functional (Kejzlar 2020) to directly extracting and recombining principal components from multiple mass models. It contrasts with Bayesian model averaging (Neufcourt 2019, Zhang 2024), which constructs new models by weighted averaging of selected models based on Bayes theorem, whereas PCA extracts uncorrelated patterns ordered by importance. The results suggest that PCA can serve as a tool to combine effects from different theoretical models, potentially enabling theorists to develop models with new effects without worrying about balancing accuracy, as PCA can extract and utilize those effects.
