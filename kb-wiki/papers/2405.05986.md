# Chirality in rotating nuclei

## Key claim
Triaxial rotating nuclei can attain a chiral structure when an odd proton and an odd neutron hole occupy orbitals whose angular momenta align along different principal axes, creating left- and right-handed configurations that are mirror images under time reversal rather than spatial reflection. The chirality is not a static property but emerges only at sufficiently high angular momentum and depends on the coupling strength between the two configurations, which decreases as total angular momentum increases.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical framework for nuclear chirality by considering a triaxial odd-odd nucleus where an unpaired proton occupies an orbital with angular momentum \(\vec{j}_p\) aligned along the short axis, an unpaired neutron hole has angular momentum \(\vec{j}_h\) aligned along the long axis, and the even-even core generates collective angular momentum \(\vec{R}\) along the medium axis. The total angular momentum \(\vec{J} = \vec{j}_p + \vec{j}_h + \vec{R}\) defines the rotation axis, and the ordering of the three half-axes as seen from the tip of \(\vec{J}\) determines left- versus right-handedness. The coupling between the two chiral configurations is analyzed through the energy barrier that depends on the magnitude of \(\vec{R}\), which increases with total angular momentum \(J\).

## Key numbers
- In \(^{135}\)Nd, chirality sets in only at sufficiently large angular momentum when a proton pair with opposite \(\vec{j}_p\) is broken by flipping the direction of \(\vec{j}_p\) of one proton, such that both \(\vec{j}_p\) are aligned with the short axis.
- For \(J = \frac{27}{2}\hbar\) in \(^{135}\)Nd, the nucleus oscillates rapidly between left and right (chiral vibration), with wave functions of the ground and first excited states of a harmonic oscillator.
- For \(J = \frac{39}{2}\hbar\) in \(^{135}\)Nd, chirality is strongest, with left- and right-handed components of the wave function well separated and energies of the two bands approaching each other; the nucleus turns three times around before changing from left to right (static chirality).
- The molecule CH\(_3\)NHF oscillates between right and left with a frequency of 1000 GHz, provided as a molecular analogy for left-right coupling.

## Context
This work extends the concept of chirality from molecular physics to nuclear structure, first proposed by Frauendorf and Meng (1997) for triaxial rotating nuclei. It builds on earlier observations of tilted rotational axes in axial nuclei and identifies candidate chiral doublets in odd-odd nuclei with approximately 134 and approximately 104 nucleons, with the best demonstration in \(^{135}\)Nd (Zhu 2003, Muk 2007) and a different chiral configuration found in the even-even nucleus \(^{112}\)Ru (Luo 2009). The framework enables further experimental searches for chiral doublet bands and theoretical studies of the transition from chiral vibration to static chirality as a function of angular momentum.
