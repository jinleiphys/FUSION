# Transverse Momentum and Transverse Momentum Distributions in the MIT Bag Model

> The typical transverse momentum of a quark in the proton is a basic property of any QCD based model of nucleon structure. However, calculations in phenomenological models typically give rather small values of transverse momenta, which are difficult to reconcile with the larger values observed in high energy experiments such as Drell-Yan reactions and Semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering. In this letter we calculate the leading twist transverse momentum dependent distribution functions (TMDs) using a generalization of the Adelaide group's relativistic formalism that has previously given good fits to the parton distributions. This enables us to examine the $k_{T}$ dependence of the TMDs in detail, and determine typical widths of these distributions. These are found to be significantly larger than those of previous calculations. We then use TMD factorization in order to evolve these distributions up to experimental scales where we can compare with data on $\langle k_{T} \rangle$ and $\langle k^{2}_{T} \rangle$. Our distributions agree well with this data.

## Key claim

The MIT bag model, when generalized to enforce correct momentum conservation (proper support on \(0 \leq x < 1\)), yields transverse momentum dependent distributions (TMDs) with significantly larger transverse momentum widths than previous bag model calculations, and these widths can be evolved via TMD factorization to agree with experimental data on \(\langle k_T \rangle\) and \(\langle k_T^2 \rangle\) from Drell-Yan and semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering.

## Method

The authors used a Peierls-Yoccoz projection to construct momentum eigenstates from MIT bag model wavefunctions, then inserted a complete set of two-quark intermediate states into the light-cone correlator defining leading-twist T-even TMDs (\(f_1, g_1, h_1, h_{1T}^{\perp}\)). They enforced momentum conservation via delta functions in the plus and transverse components, which naturally restricts the distributions to \(0 \leq x < 1\) and imposes a kinematic relation between longitudinal and transverse recoil momentum. The TMDs were then Fourier-transformed to impact parameter space, evolved from the bag scale \(\mu_0 = 0.4\) GeV to experimental scales using leading-order TMD factorization with a non-perturbative Gaussian function \(g_K(b_T)\), and transformed back to momentum space for comparison with data.

## Key numbers

* For unpolarized valence quarks at the bag scale (\(R = 0.8\) fm, \(M_s - M_t = 100\) MeV) and \(x = 0.3\): \(\langle k_T^2 \rangle = 0.21\) GeV\(^2\) for \(u_v\) and \(0.19\) GeV\(^2\) for \(d_v\).
* After evolution to \(Q = \sqrt{2.4}\) GeV, the unpolarized valence distributions give \(\langle k_T^2 \rangle = (0.41, 0.39)\) GeV\(^2\) for \(u_v\) and \(d_v\) respectively, compatible with the Hermes SIDIS value \(0.38 \pm 0.06\) GeV\(^2\) from a Gaussian model.
* After evolution to \(Q = \sqrt{2.4}\) GeV, \(\langle k_T \rangle = (0.59, 0.57)\) GeV for unpolarized valence \(u\) and \(d\) quarks.
* Using the alternate Konychev-Nadolsky parameters (\(b_{\mathrm{max}} = 1.5\) GeV\(^{-1}\), \(g_2 = 0.184\) GeV\(^2\)), evolved \(\langle k_T^2 \rangle = (0.38, 0.35)\) GeV\(^2\) for \(u_v\) and \(d_v\).
* Sum rules at bag scale: \(\int dx \, f_1 = 0.78\), \(\int dx \, g_1 = 0.67\), \(\int dx \, h_1 = 0.73\), \(\int dx \, l_q^3 = 0.013\), \(\int dx \, (\frac{1}{2}g_1 + l_q^3) = 0.35\).
* Valence quark orbital angular momenta: \(L_u^3 = +0.0211\), \(L_d^3 = -0.0043\), total valence contribution \(0.016\).

## Context

This work extends the Adelaide group's relativistic formalism (Signal, Schreiber, Thomas in the 1990s) that previously gave good fits to longitudinal parton distributions, but now applies it to TMDs with correct support, correcting earlier bag model TMD calculations by Avakian et al. (2010) that lacked proper support and required renormalization. The results show larger transverse momentum widths than the light-cone constituent model of Boffi et al. (2009) and the earlier bag model calculation, and are similar in magnitude to the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model calculation of Meissner et al. (2012). The evolved distributions agree with Hermes and Compass data analyses by Anselmino et al. (2014) and with the Gaussian model extraction of Schweitzer et al. (2010). Future work will include pion cloud contributions to access sea quark distributions and the \(\bar{u} - \bar{d}\) asymmetry reported by the SeaQuest collaboration (2021).
