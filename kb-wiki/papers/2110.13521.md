# Machine learning spectral functions in lattice QCD

> We study the inverse problem of reconstructing spectral functions from Euclidean correlation functions via machine learning. We propose a novel neural network, SVAE, which is based on the variational autoencoder (VAE) and can be naturally applied to the inverse problem. The prominent feature of the SVAE is that a Shannon-Jaynes entropy term having the ground truth values of spectral functions as prior information is included in the loss function to be minimized. We train the network with general spectral functions produced from a Gaussian mixture model. As a test, we use correlators generated from four different types of physically motivated spectral functions made of one resonance peak, a continuum term and perturbative spectral function obtained using non-relativistic QCD. From the mock data test we find that the SVAE in most cases is comparable to the maximum entropy method (MEM) in the quality of reconstructing spectral functions and even outperforms the MEM in the case where the spectral function has sharp peaks with insufficient number of data points in the correlator. By applying to temporal correlation functions of charmonium in the pseudoscalar channel obtained in the quenched lattice QCD at 0.75 $T_c$ on $128^3\times96$ lattices and $1.5$ $T_c$ on $128^3\times48$ lattices, we find that the resonance peak of $η_c$ extracted from both the SVAE and MEM has a substantial dependence on the number of points in the temporal direction ($N_τ$) adopted in the lattice simulation and $N_τ$ larger than 48 is needed to resolve the fate of $η_c$ at 1.5 $T_c$.

## Key claim

The SVAE neural network, which incorporates a Shannon-Jaynes entropy term with ground truth spectral functions as prior information, reconstructs spectral functions from Euclidean correlators with quality comparable to the maximum entropy method (MEM) in most cases, and outperforms MEM specifically when spectral functions have sharp peaks and the correlator has an insufficient number of data points.

## Method

The authors constructed a variational autoencoder (VAE) with two encoders and one decoder, where the loss function includes a Shannon-Jaynes entropy term using ground truth spectral values as prior information and a Kullback-Leibler divergence term. They trained the network on general spectral functions generated from a Gaussian mixture model, then tested reconstruction quality on mock correlators produced from four physically motivated spectral functions (one resonance peak, continuum term, and perturbative NRQCD spectral functions). Finally, they applied the trained network to temporal correlation functions of charmonium in the pseudoscalar channel from quenched lattice QCD simulations.

## Key numbers

* At 0.75 T_c on 128^3 x 96 lattices and 1.5 T_c on 128^3 x 48 lattices, the resonance peak of eta_c extracted from both SVAE and MEM showed substantial dependence on N_tau (number of points in the temporal direction).
* N_tau larger than 48 is required to resolve the fate of eta_c at 1.5 T_c.
* The number of points in the temporal direction N_tau is normally of order O(10) due to computing costs, while the spectral function typically has O(1000) points in frequency space.

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to the ill-posed inverse problem of reconstructing spectral functions from Euclidean lattice QCD correlators, a problem previously addressed by the maximum entropy method (MEM) introduced by Asakawa (2003) and by stochastic methods such as stochastic optimization method (SOM) and stochastic analytical inference (SAI) from Ding (2017). The SVAE approach differs from prior supervised learning approaches (Yoon 2018, Kades 2019, Fournier 2020) by using an unsupervised variational autoencoder framework with ground truth prior information, and from kernel ridge regression (Offler 2019), radial basis functions (Zhou 2021), and Gaussian process regression (Horak 2021) by incorporating the Shannon-Jaynes entropy directly into the neural network loss function. The method enables more reliable extraction of spectral functions from lattice QCD data, particularly for resolving sharp resonance peaks with limited temporal data points.
