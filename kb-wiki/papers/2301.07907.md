# Revisiting angular momentum conservation in transport simulations of intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions

> Based on the well-calibrated IBUU transport model, we have studied the dynamical effect of incorporating rigorous angular momentum conservation in each collision of particles with homework setups. The constraint of the rigorous angular momentum conservation requires in-plane collisions and side jumps of particles after their collision. Since the option is not unique, we have compared two typical prescriptions with the original one. While the results depend quantitatively on the choice of the prescription, we found that the angular momentum conservation generally reduces local density fluctuations and thus the collision rate, and may have some influence on the density evolution, the collective flow, and even the pion production in transport simulations of intermediate-energy heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
Incorporating rigorous angular momentum conservation in each nucleon-nucleon collision within the IBUU transport model generally reduces local density fluctuations and the collision rate, but the quantitative effect on observables depends on which of two possible collision prescriptions (``vertical'' or ``rotation'') is chosen.

## Method
The authors implemented two prescriptions for angular momentum conservation in elastic and inelastic collisions within the IBUU transport model: the ``vertical'' prescription, which moves particle coordinates perpendicular to the final momentum direction, and the ``rotation'' prescription, which rotates both coordinates and momenta around the center of mass. They compared these to the original prescription without angular momentum conservation using homework setups (Skyrme-like mean-field, constant 40 mb elastic cross section, and standard inelastic channels for pion production) in non-central Au+Au collisions at beam energies of 100, 400, and 1000 AMeV.

## Key numbers
- In a box calculation at saturation density, the ``original'' prescription overestimates the theoretical collision rate limit of 111.4 c/fm, while the ``rotation'' prescription matches it and the ``vertical'' prescription underestimates it; both new prescriptions reproduce the theoretical limit within 5%.
- The side-jump distance in the computational frame is smaller than 1.2 fm for all collisions, and is smaller in the ``vertical'' prescription than in the ``rotation'' prescription at 100 AMeV.
- The violation of total angular momentum in the ``original'' prescription is about 3% at higher collision energies, while for the ``rotation'' and ``vertical'' prescriptions it is at the 0.1% level.
- The ``vertical'' prescription leads to a pion multiplicity about 20% larger than that from the ``original'' prescription at 400 AMeV.
- The ``vertical'' prescription yields a $(\pi^-/\pi^+)_{like}$ ratio about 1.5% larger than the other two prescriptions at 1000 AMeV.

## Context
This work extends the earlier finding by Gale (1990) that angular momentum conservation had a small effect on dynamics, by showing that the effect becomes significant at higher collision energies and for pion observables. The study was motivated by recent work on the ``sign problem'' of local spin polarization in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (PhysRevLett.125.062301), where angular momentum conservation was crucial. The results enable future incorporation of both orbital and spin contributions to total angular momentum conservation in transport simulations, building on the authors' previous work on spin dynamics (Xu 2015) and spin polarization (Xia 2019).
