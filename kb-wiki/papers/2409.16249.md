# Equilibrium expectations for non-Gaussian fluctuations near a QCD critical point

> With the highly anticipated results from the Beam Energy Scan II program at RHIC being recently revealed, an understanding of particle-number fluctuations and their significance as a potential signature of a possible QCD critical point is crucial. Early works that embarked on this endeavor sought to estimate the fluctuations due to the presence of a critical point assuming they stay in equilibrium. From these results came the proposal to focus efforts on higher, non-Gaussian, moments of the event-by-event distributions, in particular of the number of protons. These non-Gaussian moments are especially sensitive to critical fluctuations, as their magnitudes are proportional to high powers of the critical correlation length. As the equation of state provides key input for hydrodynamical simulations of heavy-ion collisions, we estimate equilibrium fluctuations from the BEST equation of state (EoS) that includes critical features from the 3D Ising Model. In particular, the proton factorial cumulants and their dependence on non-universal mapping parameters is investigated within the BEST EoS. Furthermore, the correlation length, as a central quantity for the assessment of fluctuations in the vicinity of a critical point, is also calculated in a consistent manner with the scaling equation of state. An understanding of the equilibrium estimates of proton factorial cumulants will be useful for further comparison to estimates of out-of-equilibrium fluctuations in order to determine the magnitude of the observable fluctuations to be expected in heavy-ion collision experiments, in which the time spent near a critical point is short.

## Key claim

The equilibrium proton factorial cumulants calculated from the BEST equation of state (EoS) that includes critical features from the 3D Ising Model show strong sensitivity to the non-universal mapping parameters, meaning that these parameters can be constrained by experimental measurements; however, these are equilibrium estimates only, and out-of-equilibrium effects must be studied before direct comparison with experiment is possible.

## Method

The authors compute the critical contribution to proton factorial cumulants by taking derivatives of the BEST equation of state, which maps the 3D Ising model scaling equation of state onto QCD using a quadratic mapping between Ising variables (r,h) and QCD variables (mu_B, T). The correlation length is calculated consistently with the scaling equation of state using the epsilon-expansion to O(epsilon^2). The critical fluctuations are then obtained from the thermodynamic derivatives of pressure with respect to baryon chemical potential, and the maximum entropy approach from Pradeep (2022) is used to convert baryon density fluctuations into proton factorial cumulants.

## Key numbers

- The 3D Ising critical exponent alpha = 0.11 is used in the parametric form of the Ising pressure
- The correlation length is calculated using the epsilon-expansion to O(epsilon^2) with the integral I = integral_0^1 ln[x(1-x)]/(1-x(1-x)) dx approximately equal to -2.3439
- For the example parameter choice, the critical point is located at mu_{B,c} = 600 MeV and T_c = 90 MeV, with alpha_1 = 16.6 degrees, w = 5, rho = 1, and alpha_2 = 0 degrees
- The freeze-out trajectories studied follow curves 4, 6, and 9 MeV below the critical point temperature
- The BESII program has probed the region with mu_B less than or approximately 420 MeV with no evidence of a critical point found to date

## Context

This work extends the BEST equation of state framework originally developed by Parotto (2018) and Karthein (2021) by implementing a quadratic mapping between Ising and QCD variables, as introduced by Kahangirwe (2024), which allows the critical features to follow the chiral phase transition line from lattice QCD rather than a tangent line. The equilibrium estimates of proton factorial cumulants provide a baseline for future comparison with out-of-equilibrium fluctuations, which are necessary because in heavy-ion collision experiments the time spent near a critical point is short. The results are intended to help interpret the recent BESII measurements of proton fluctuations from Pandav (2024), which show no critical point evidence but also do not match noncritical baseline models.
