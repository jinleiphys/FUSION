# A "Little Big Bang" Scenario of Multifragmentation

> We suggest a multifragmentation scenario in which fragments are produced at an early, high temperature and high density, stage of the reaction. In this scenario, self-bound clusters of particles in the hot and dense fluid are the precursors of the observed fragments. This solves a number of recurrent problems concerning the kinetic energies and the temperature of the fragments, encountered with the standard low density fragmentation picture. The possibility to recover the initial thermodynamic parameters from the inspection of the asymptotic fragment size and kinetic energy distributions is discussed.

# A "Little Big Bang" Scenario of Multifragmentation

## Key claim

The authors propose that nuclear multifragmentation fragments originate as pre-existing self-bound clusters in a hot, dense fluid at an early stage of the reaction, rather than forming via recondensation at a low-density freeze-out. This scenario is presented as a generic interpretation of classical molecular dynamics simulations, not as a finished model directly comparable to experimental data.

## Method

Classical molecular dynamics (CMD) simulations were performed for systems of 189 and 12,000 particles interacting via a Lennard-Jones potential, with Coulomb interactions added for the smaller system. Self-bound clusters were identified using Hill's criterion (relative kinetic energy less than potential energy between particles), and the confined systems were then allowed to expand freely after removal of the container walls. Fragment size distributions, kinetic energies, and effective temperatures were tracked as functions of time.

## Key numbers

- For a system of N=189 particles at density ρ=0.50 and temperature T=1.15 (Lennard-Jones units), the internal effective temperature of clusters T*(s) is lower than the fluid temperature, while the center-of-mass temperature T_cm(s) is higher; for large clusters (s>50), the internal velocity distribution approaches a Maxwell distribution.
- Along the percolation line in small systems, the slope of the cluster size distribution is τ ≃ 2.5, consistent with mean-field percolation theory.
- At densities as low as ρ=0.1–0.2, the strong interaction between clusters (without Coulomb) still represents about 40% of the total potential energy.
- In expanding systems with Etot=1.2, N=189, and ρ=0.8, the asymptotic fragment size distribution at t=400τ₀ is nearly identical to the cluster size distribution at t=0.
- The persistence of fragments of size s>3 reaches its asymptotic value at densities of order 0.8 during expansion (Etot=1.2, N=189, ρ=1.3).
- Kinetic energies of fragments depend on initial density: expansions without Coulomb from ρ=1.3 and ρ=0.3 (Etot=0.2) produce quantitatively different kinetic energy distributions, demonstrating non-equilibrium expansion.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of self-bound clusters in simple fluids (Campi 2001, Campi 2002, Sator 2002) and directly contradicts the standard "Statistical Equilibrium Models" (SEM) of multifragmentation (Bondorf 1995, Botvina 1997, Friedman 1990, GSI 1999). The standard scenario assumes a quasi-static expansion to low freeze-out density where fragments recondense as spherical, non-interacting objects. The proposed "Little Big Bang" scenario resolves two recurrent problems with SEM: the underprediction of fragment kinetic energies (which SEM corrects with ad hoc radial flow, as noted by Ekins 1995, Ekins 1997) and the observation that fragment internal temperatures are much lower than the system temperature (Tsang 1993, Serfling 1998). The work enables a method to extract initial thermodynamic parameters (T and ρ) from asymptotic fragment size and kinetic energy distributions, and calls for testing with Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) and Fermionic Molecular Dynamics (FMD) calculations.
