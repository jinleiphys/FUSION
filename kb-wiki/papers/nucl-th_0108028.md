# Relevance of high-momentum nucleons for nuclear phenomena

> A brief review is given concerning the status of the theoretical work on nucleon spectral functions. A recent concern about the validity of the concept of spectroscopic factors as deduced from (e,e'p) reactions at higher Q^2, is discussed in some detail. The consequences of the observed spectral strength are then considered in the context of nuclear saturation. It is argued that short-range correlations are mainly responsible for the actual value of the observed charge density in 208Pb and by extension for the empirical value of the saturation density of nuclear matter. This observation combined with the general understanding of the spectroscopic strength suggests that a renewed study of nuclear matter, emphasizing the self-consistent determination of the spectral strength due to short-range and tensor correlations, may shed light on the perennial nuclear saturation problem. First results using such a scheme are presented.

## Key claim

The paper argues that short-range and tensor correlations (SRC) are primarily responsible for the empirical saturation density of nuclear matter and the observed charge density in 208Pb, and that a self-consistent treatment of nucleon spectral functions including these correlations may resolve the nuclear saturation problem, though the authors present only first results from such a scheme rather than a complete solution.

## Method

The authors review theoretical work on nucleon spectral functions from (e,e'p) reactions and develop a consistent eikonal approximation for final-state interactions at higher Q^2, using self-energies from nuclear-matter calculations to describe the damping of outgoing protons. They then implement a self-consistent Green's function approach for nuclear matter that solves the ladder equation with fully dressed particle propagators, iterating until self-consistency is achieved for the complete Green's function, using the Reid potential and including relative S-waves with tensor coupling to the 3D1 channel.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factor of 0.65 for removal of the last 3s1/2 proton from 208Pb
- Deeply bound orbits in 208Pb depleted by about 15% due to short-range correlations
- Central density of heavy nuclei: 0.16 nucleons/fm^3 corresponding to k_F = 1.33 fm^-1
- For 16O, quasihole states (1s1/2, 1p3/2, 1p1/2) contribute only 37% of total binding energy, with 63% from continuum terms
- High-momentum components account for only 10% of particles in 16O but contribute significantly to the energy
- Self-consistent calculation performed at k_F = 1.36 fm^-1, achieving convergence in about ten iterations
- Quasiparticle pole strength z_kF reduced from 1 (Fermi gas) to 0.7 for dressed propagators at normal density
- Two-particle propagation reduction factor of z_kF^2 approximately 0.5, sufficient to suppress pairing instabilities

## Context

This work extends the line of research on nucleon spectral functions from (e,e'p) reactions pioneered by the NIKHEF group and others (Dieperink, Sick, Lapikás, Pasquini). It directly addresses a challenge by Kelly (2001) who questioned the constancy of spectroscopic factors with Q^2 using a Skyrme-Hartree-Fock plus Glauber approach. The authors' consistent eikonal treatment shows that spectroscopic factors from low Q^2 experiments at NIKHEF can successfully predict cross sections at higher Q^2 (JLab kinematics at Q^2 = 0.8 (GeV/c)^2). The self-consistent Green's function method builds on earlier work by Ramos, Dickhoff, and collaborators, and the results enable future calculations that may resolve the nuclear saturation problem by properly including short-range and tensor correlations.
