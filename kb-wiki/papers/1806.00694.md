# Fission Dynamics of 240Pu from Saddle-to-Scission and Beyond

> Calculations are presented for the time evolution of $^{240}$Pu from the proximity of the outer saddle point until the fission fragments are well separated, using the time-dependent density functional theory extended to superfluid systems. We have tested three families of nuclear energy density functionals and found that all functionals exhibit a similar dynamics: the collective motion is highly dissipative and with little trace of inertial dynamics, due to the one-body dissipation mechanism alone. This finding justifies the validity of using the overdamped collective motion approach and to some extent the main assumptions in statistical models of fission. This conclusion is robust with respect to the nuclear energy density functional used. The configurations and interactions left out of the present theory framework only increase the role of the dissipative couplings. An unexpected finding is varying the pairing strength within a quite large range has only minor effects on the dynamics. We find notable differences in the excitation energy sharing between the fission fragments in the cases of spontaneous and induced fission. With increasing initial excitation energy of the fissioning nucleus more excitation energy is deposited in the heavy fragment, in agreement with experimental data on average neutron multiplicities.

## Key claim
The collective motion of 240Pu from saddle-to-scission is highly dissipative with little trace of inertial dynamics, driven solely by one-body dissipation, and this conclusion is robust across three families of nuclear energy density functionals tested. The abstract's claim of "similar dynamics" across functionals hedges on the fact that while the dissipative character is universal, the specific fission barrier heights and isomer energies vary by several MeV between functionals.

## Method
The authors performed time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) calculations, evolving the quasiparticle wave functions of 240Pu on a spatial rectangular lattice from the outer saddle point until the fission fragments are well separated. They tested three nuclear energy density functionals (SkM*, SLy4, and SeaLL1) and varied the pairing strength over a large range to assess sensitivity. The simulations used a simulation box with 24x24x48 lattice points, giving 55,296 quasiparticle states per nucleon species.

## Key numbers
- The induced fission of 240Pu with normal pairing strength lasts up to 14,000 fm/c (approximately 47 x 10^-21 s) from saddle-to-scission.
- With enhanced pairing strength, the induced fission lasts about 1,400 fm/c.
- SeaLL1 underestimates the fission isomer excitation energy (E_II = 0.54 MeV compared with experimental 2.8 MeV).
- SeaLL1 inner barrier height E_A = 6.84 MeV (vs. experimental 6.05 MeV) and outer barrier height E_B = 4.20 MeV (vs. experimental 5.15 MeV), both within 1 MeV of experiment.
- The maximum quasiparticle energy in TDSLDA is approximately 400 MeV, compared to approximately 100 MeV in configuration space approaches.

## Context
This work extends the proof-of-concept study by Bulgac et al. (2016) using SLy4 to a systematic comparison of three functionals, including SkM* (designed for fission by Bartel et al., 1982) and the recently developed SeaLL1 (Shi et al., 2018). The results contradict the assumption of adiabatic collective dynamics used in time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) approaches and instead support overdamped Brownian motion models of Randrup et al. (2011, 2013, 2018). The finding that varying pairing strength has only minor effects on dynamics is unexpected and challenges the picture of Bertsch (1980, 1990) emphasizing pairing's crucial role. The work enables future studies of fluctuations around the mean-field trajectory and their role in fission fragment distributions.
