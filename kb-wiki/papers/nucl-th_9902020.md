# Two-Particle Correlations in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Two-particle momentum correlations between pairs of identical particles produced in relativistic heavy-ion reactions can be analyzed to extract the space-time structure of the collision fireball. We review recent progress in the application of this method, based on newly developed theoretical tools and new high-quality data from heavy-ion collision experiments. Implications for our understanding of the collision dynamics and for the search for the quark-gluon plasma are discussed.

# Two-Particle Correlations in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

Two-particle momentum correlations between identical particles can be analyzed to extract the space-time structure of the collision fireball, but the space-time structure cannot be fully recovered from the measured correlator because the mass-shell constraint eliminates one of the four relative momentum components, requiring additional model assumptions about the source to separate spatial and temporal information.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical formalism connecting the two-particle correlation function, defined as the ratio of the Lorentz-invariant two-particle coincidence cross section to the product of the two single-particle distributions, to the emission function S(x,K), an effective single-particle Wigner phase-space density of particles in the source. They derive Gaussian parametrizations of the correlation function in terms of HBT radii, using the Cartesian (Pratt-Bertsch) parametrization in the outward-sideward-longitudinal coordinate system and the Yano-Koonin-Podgoretskii parametrization, and discuss how final-state interactions (Coulomb and strong) modify the correlator via distorted wave treatments.

## Key numbers

- The two-particle correlation function is defined as C(p1,p2) = [E1 E2 dN/(d^3p1 d^3p2)] / [(E1 dN/d^3p1)(E2 dN/d^3p2)].
- For a pointlike source with Coulomb final-state interactions, the correlator reduces to the Gamov factor G(eta) = 2 pi eta / (e^{2 pi eta} - 1), where eta = alpha m / q.
- The mass-shell constraint gives q^0 = beta * q, with beta = K/K^0 approximately K/E_K.
- For azimuthally symmetric collisions, the correlation function is characterized by 4 HBT radii (R_s^2, R_o^2, R_l^2, R_ol^2) that are functions of only two variables: K_perp and Y.
- At K_perp = 0, the identities <x_o^2 - x_s^2> = <x_o x_l> = <t x_o> = 0 imply that R_o^2 - R_s^2 and R_ol^2 vanish.
- The difference R_o^2 - R_s^2 = beta_perp^2 <t^2> - 2 beta_perp <x_o t> + <x_o^2 - x_s^2> yields the duration of particle emission Delta t = sqrt(<t^2> - <t>^2) for particles with small K_perp.
- Intensity interferometry yields important source parameters at the level of 20% accuracy.

## Context

This review extends the Hanbury Brown and Twiss (HBT) method, originally developed in astronomy in the 1950s and first applied to particle physics by Goldhaber, Goldhaber, Lee and Pais (1960) for proton-antiproton annihilation, to relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The work synthesizes newly developed theoretical tools, including the Yano-Koonin-Podgoretskii parametrization, the imaging method of Brown and Danielewicz (1997), and Coulomb correction methods by Sinyukov and collaborators (1998), with high-quality data from CERN SPS and Brookhaven AGS experiments using S, Pb, Si, and Au beams. The review enables quantitative characterization of the freeze-out source for backward extrapolation to the dense early stages of the collision and provides stringent constraints on dynamical models of the quark-gluon plasma search.
