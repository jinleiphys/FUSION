# Fundamental Symmetry Tests with Nucleons, Nuclei, and Atoms: A Snowmass Report

> Present and prospective fundamental symmetry tests with nucleons, nuclei and atoms are probing for possible new physics at the TeV scale and beyond. These ongoing and proposed table-top as well as accelerator-based experiments are thus a vital component of the Intensity Frontier. At the same time, these tests provide increasingly sophisticated probes of long-distance strong interactions that are responsible for the structure of nucleons and nuclei. In this community report, some of the most compelling opportunities with nucleons, nuclei and atoms are summarized, drawing largely on input received from the nuclear and atomic physics communities. In particular, this report includes many contributions submitted to two recent Intensity Frontier Workshops.

## Key claim

This Snowmass community report argues that fundamental symmetry tests with nucleons, nuclei, and atoms can probe for new physics at the TeV scale and beyond, complementing energy-frontier searches at the LHC; however, the report explicitly states it makes "no editorial judgment" on relative priorities and that the discussion is "largely focused on experimental opportunities" while noting the "vital role of theoretical work" is not covered in depth.

## Method

The report synthesizes input received from the nuclear and atomic physics communities, drawing on contributions submitted to two Intensity Frontier Workshops (Rockville, MD in November-December 2011 and Argonne National Laboratory in April 2013). The authors adapted a series of two-page "mini white papers" written by researchers into various sections of the document, covering electric dipole moment searches, fundamental symmetry tests, and related experimental programs across multiple facilities worldwide.

## Key numbers

- Neutron EDM current upper limit: \(2.9 \times 10^{-26} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) from ultracold neutrons in a bottle (Bak06)
- Electron EDM current upper limit: \(8.7 \times 10^{-29} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) from ThO molecules in a beam (ACME:2013)
- Nucleus (\({}^{199}\)Hg) EDM current upper limit: \(3.1 \times 10^{-29} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) from Hg atoms in a vapor cell (Griffith:2009zz)
- Standard Model prediction for neutron EDM: approximately \(10^{-31} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\), five orders of magnitude below current limit
- Standard Model prediction for electron EDM: \(10^{-38} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- Standard Model prediction for \({}^{199}\)Hg EDM: \(10^{-33} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- PSI nEDM near-term goal: sensitivity of \(\sigma(d_n) = 2.6 \times 10^{-27} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\), corresponding to upper limit \(d_n < 5 \times 10^{-27} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) at 95% C.L.
- PSI n2EDM goal: sensitivity \(d_n < 5 \times 10^{-28} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) at 95% C.L.
- SNS nEDM goal: sensitivity \(< 3 \times 10^{-28} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- TRIUMF nEDM long-term goal: \(d_n < 1 \times 10^{-28} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- Munich nEDM goal: statistical limit \(d_n < 5 \times 10^{-28} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) at \(3\sigma\) with systematic control \(\sigma_{d,\mathrm{syst}} < 2 \times 10^{-28} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) at \(1\sigma\)
- Proton storage ring EDM goal: sensitivity to \(10^{-29} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- \({}^{199}\)Hg EDM apparatus potential: ten-fold increase in sensitivity achievable with existing apparatus
- \({}^{225}\)Ra atomic EDM enhancement factor: approximately 1000 over \({}^{199}\)Hg, arising from octupole deformation and relativistic atomic electrons
- \({}^{225}\)Ra projected sensitivity with next-generation facilities: \(10^{-28} - 10^{-29} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\)
- YbF electron EDM upper limit: \(d_e < 1.05 \times 10^{-27} \, e\cdot\mathrm{cm}\) at 90% C.L. (Hudson:2011)

## Context

This report extends the community planning efforts documented in the 2007 Nuclear Science Advisory Long Range Plan (NSAC LRP 2007), which identified fundamental symmetries and neutron properties as one of the top-four priorities for nuclear physics, and the 2006 P5 report "The Particle Physics Roadmap." It draws on a series of nine review articles published in a dedicated volume of Progress in Particle and Nuclear Physics (Cirigliano 2013, Engel 2013, Erler 2013, de Gouvea 2013, Balantekin 2013, Gardner 2013, Haxton 2013) that followed a meeting on Beyond the Standard Model in Nuclear Physics at the University of Wisconsin-Madison in October 2011. The report enables coordinated planning for next-generation EDM searches at facilities including PSI, ILL, SNS, TRIUMF, FRM-II, BNL, Argonne, and TRIUMF-ISAC, as well as future facilities such as FRIB and Project X.
