# Early Time Dynamics of Gluon Fields in High Energy Nuclear Collisions

> Nuclei colliding at very high energy create a strong, quasi-classical gluon field during the initial phase of their interaction. We present an analytic calculation of the initial space-time evolution of this field in the limit of very high energies using a formal recursive solution of the Yang-Mills equations. We provide analytic expressions for the initial chromo-electric and chromo-magnetic fields and for their energy-momentum tensor. In particular, we discuss event-averaged results for energy density and energy flow as well as for longitudinal and transverse pressure of this system. For example, we find that the ratio of longitudinal to transverse pressure very early in the system behaves as $p_L/p_T = -[1-\frac{3}{2a}(Qτ)^2]/[1-\frac{1}{a}(Qτ)^2]+\mathcal{O}(Qτ)^4$ where $τ$ is the longitudinal proper time, $Q$ is related to the saturation scales $Q_s$ of the two nuclei, and $a = \ln (Q^2/\hat{m}^2)$ with $\hat m$ a scale to be defined later. Our results are generally applicable if $τ\lesssim 1/Q$. As already discussed in a previous paper, the transverse energy flow $S^i$ of the gluon field exhibits hydrodynamic-like contributions that follow transverse gradients of the energy density $\nabla^i \varepsilon$. In addition, a rapidity-odd energy flow also emerges from the non-abelian analog of Gauss' Law and generates non-vanishing angular momentum of the field. We will discuss the space-time picture that emerges from our analysis and its implications for observables in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper presents an analytic calculation of the initial space-time evolution of the quasi-classical gluon field created in high-energy nuclear collisions, using a recursive solution of the Yang-Mills equations that is valid for proper times \(\tau \lesssim 1/Q\), where \(Q\) is related to the saturation scales \(Q_s\) of the two nuclei; the results are explicitly limited to the near-field regime before the classical approximation breaks down at \(\tau_0 \sim 1/Q_s\).

## Method

The authors solve the Yang-Mills equations for two colliding nuclei modeled as color glass condensate (CGC) sources on the light cone, using a power-series expansion of the gauge potentials in longitudinal proper time \(\tau\) around \(\tau=0\). They derive recursive relations for the expansion coefficients from the equations of motion and boundary conditions, obtaining analytic expressions for the chromo-electric and chromo-magnetic fields and the energy-momentum tensor up to fourth order in \(\tau\). Event-averaged results are computed by generalizing the McLerran-Venugopalan (MV) model to include transverse dynamics and evaluating expectation values of field correlators.

## Key numbers

* The ratio of longitudinal to transverse pressure at early times is given by \(p_L/p_T = -[1-\frac{3}{2a}(Q\tau)^2]/[1-\frac{1}{a}(Q\tau)^2]+\mathcal{O}(Q\tau)^4\), where \(a = \ln (Q^2/\hat{m}^2)\) and the result is valid for \(\tau \lesssim 1/Q\).
* The pressure anisotropy measure \((p_L-p_T)/((p_L+2p_T)/3) = -6 [1-\frac{5}{4a}(Q\tau)^2]/[1-\frac{1}{2a}(Q\tau)^2] + \mathcal{O}(Q\tau)^4\).
* The initial energy density at \(\tau=0\) is \(\varepsilon_0 = \frac{1}{2}(E_0^2 + B_0^2)\), with initial transverse pressure \(p_T = \varepsilon_0\) and initial longitudinal pressure \(p_L = -\varepsilon_0\).
* The convergence radius of the series expansion is parametrically set by the saturation scale, \(\tau_{\mathrm{conv}} \sim 1/Q_s\), with \(Q_s\) on the order of a few GeV in heavy ion collider experiments.

## Context

This work extends the analytic treatment of the glasma field beyond the leading-order results of Kovner, McLerran, and Weigert (1995) by providing a systematic small-\(\tau\) expansion that includes non-abelian effects and transverse dynamics. The results bridge the gap between known classical gluon fields in single nuclei before the collision and the glasma fields at time \(\tau_0 \sim 1/Q_s\), providing input for initial conditions in hydrodynamic simulations such as those by Schenke (2012) and Gale (2012). The calculation enables future studies of thermalization and can be used in ad-hoc thermalization approximations as in Fries (2005) and Gale (2012), or as a starting point for further work on thermalization by Gelis (2013) and Berges (2013).
