# Twin stars: probe of phase transition from hadronic to quark matter

> In agreement with the gravitational-wave events which are constantly increasing, new aspects of the internal structure of compact stars have come to light. A scenario in which a first order transition takes place inside these stars is of particular interest as it can lead, under conditions, to a third gravitationally stable branch (besides white dwarfs and neutron stars). This is known as the twin star scenario. The new branch yields stars with the same mass as normal compact stars but quite different radii. In the current work, we focus on hybrid stars undergone a hadron to quark phase transition near their core and how this new stable configuration arises. Emphasis is to be given especially in the aspects of the phase transition and its parameterization in two different ways, namely with Maxwell construction and with Gibbs construction. Qualitative findings of mass-radius relations of these stars will also be presented.

# Twin stars: probe of phase transition from hadronic to quark matter

## Key claim
A first-order hadron-to-quark phase transition (HQPT) in compact stars can produce a third gravitationally stable branch ("twin stars") with masses matching normal neutron stars but distinctly smaller radii, provided the energy density jump at the transition satisfies the Seidov condition for Maxwell construction or the free parameters are appropriately chosen for Gibbs construction.

## Method
The authors model hybrid stars using the APR equation of state with momentum-dependent interactions (MDI) for the hadronic phase and a constant speed-of-sound parametrization for the quark phase. They implement the phase transition in two ways: a sharp transition via Maxwell construction (MC), where hadronic and quark phases are in direct contact with an energy density discontinuity, and a non-sharp transition via Gibbs construction (GC), which includes a mixed phase region with a polytropic equation of state. The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved for non-rotating stellar models to obtain mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers
- Transition density of \(n_{\rm tr} = 2.1\,n_{\rm s}\) (where \(n_{\rm s}\) is nuclear saturation density) used for the primary mass-radius diagrams with Maxwell construction.
- Transition density of \(n_{\rm tr} = 2.2\,n_{\rm s}\) used for a second set of mass-radius diagrams to study the effect of transition location.
- Speed of sound in the quark phase assumed as \(c_{\rm s} = 1\) for the Maxwell construction results.
- The Seidov condition for instability in Maxwell construction: \(\Delta \mathcal{E} \geq \frac{3p_{\rm tr}+\mathcal{E}(p_{\rm tr})}{2}\), where \(\Delta \mathcal{E}\) is the energy density jump at the transition pressure \(p_{\rm tr}\).
- Maximum masses reproduced by the model are consistent with observed pulsar masses including those around \(2\,M_{\odot}\) (references to Arzoumanian, Antoniadis, and Cromartie observations).

## Context
This work extends the twin star concept introduced by Gerlach (1968) and later developed by Glendenning and Kettner (2000) and Schertler et al. (2000), which was revived by Blaschke et al. (2013) and Castillo et al. (2013) with the suggestion that high-mass twin stars could provide evidence for a strong first-order phase transition. The study builds on Bayesian analyses by Castillo et al. (2016) and comparisons of Maxwell versus mixed phase constructions by Ayriyan et al. (2018) and Maslov et al. (2019). The results enable future work on distinguishing between Maxwell and Gibbs constructions, determining the separation conditions between twin and normal neutron star branches, and extending the analysis to rotating stellar models.
