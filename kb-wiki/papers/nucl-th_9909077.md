# Theory of Nuclear Reactions Leading to Superheavy Elements

> Dynamical reaction theory is presented for synthesis of superheavy elements. Characteristic features of formation and surviving are discussed, which combinedly determine final residue cross sections of superheavy elements. Preliminary results on Z=114 are also given.

## Key claim

The residue cross section for superheavy element synthesis is determined by a compromise between formation probability and survival probability, which together depend on incident energy and the specific projectile-target combination; the paper presents a dynamical framework that treats both the dissipative fusion hindrance (extra-push energy) and the time-dependent restoration of the shell correction energy during cooling by neutron evaporation.

## Method

The authors assume the compound-nucleus picture and factorize the residue cross section into a formation probability and a survival probability. Formation is modeled by a three-dimensional Langevin equation on multi-dimensional potential-energy surfaces (including elongation and mass asymmetry), with friction from the one-body wall-and-window formula and a random force obeying the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. Survival is treated via a one-dimensional Smoluchowski equation (the overdamped limit of the Kramers equation) for fission decay, coupled to a statistical evaporation code that accounts for the temperature-dependent melting and restoration of the shell correction energy.

## Key numbers

- The fusion hindrance threshold is given by Z₁·Z₂ ≥ 1,800; the symmetric case shown has Z₁·Z₂ ≥ 3,000.
- For the channel ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴⁴Pu, Z₁·Z₂ ≈ 1,800, so the contact point lies inside the conditional saddle.
- Survival probabilities P_sur for J=10 are shown for several Z=114 compound-nucleus isotopes as a function of initial excitation energy; results are given at time 2,000 × 10⁻²¹ sec.
- The paper notes that a recent Dubna experiment observed an event that would be a signature for Z=114 at the energy predicted here to be most favourable in the ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴⁴Pu channel.

## Context

This work extends the dynamical treatment of fusion hindrance (Swiatecki, 1980s) and the Kramers description of fission (Kramers, 1940) to the synthesis of superheavy elements, where both formation and survival must be treated consistently. It contrasts with the dinucleus-system concept (Adamian et al., 1990s), which evaluates fusion only over the Businaro-Gallone peak, whereas the present Langevin approach includes all dynamical paths. The results enable systematic searches for optimal projectile-target combinations and incident energies for producing elements around Z=114, and the framework can be extended to secondary-beam reactions.
