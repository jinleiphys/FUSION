# Stochastic relativistic viscous hydrodynamics from the Metropolis algorithm

> We propose an algorithm for simulating stochastic relativistic fluid dynamics based on Metropolis updates. Each step of the algorithm begins with an update based on ideal hydrodynamics. This is followed by proposing random (spatial) momentum transfers between fluid cells, keeping the total energy fixed. These proposals are then accepted or rejected using the change in entropy as a statistical weight. The algorithm reproduces relativistic viscous hydrodynamics in the ``Density Frame", which is a formulation of viscous hydrodynamics we review and clarify here. This formulation is first order in time and requires no auxiliary dynamical fields such as $Π^{μν}$. The only parameters are the shear and bulk viscosities and the equation of state. By adopting the 3+1 split of general relativity, we extend the Metropolis algorithm to general space-time coordinates, such as Bjorken coordinates, which are commonly used to simulate heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a Metropolis-based algorithm for simulating stochastic relativistic viscous hydrodynamics that is first-order in time and requires no auxiliary dynamical fields such as the shear stress tensor, with the only parameters being shear and bulk viscosities and the equation of state; the algorithm reproduces viscous hydrodynamics in the "Density Frame" formulation, which the authors review and clarify.

## Method

The algorithm proceeds by first evolving the system with an ideal hydrodynamic step, then proposing random spatial momentum transfers between fluid cells while keeping total energy fixed, and accepting or rejecting these proposals using the change in entropy as a statistical weight. The authors adopt the 3+1 split of general relativity to extend the Metropolis algorithm to general space-time coordinates such as Bjorken coordinates commonly used in heavy-ion collision simulations. The Density Frame formulation is derived by using lowest-order equations of motion (ideal hydrodynamics) to eliminate time derivatives in the viscous strains, resulting in equations that are first order in time and second order in space.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity to entropy ratio is measured to be no more than four times a quantum limit of ℏ/4πk_B, suggested by gauge-gravity duality (Kovtun 2004)
- The critical point in QCD is estimated to exist at temperature T ∼ 85 MeV and baryon chemical potential μ_B/T ∼ 6.5, based on functional Renormalization Group and lattice QCD results
- The speed of sound squared c_s^2 = dP/de appears in the inverse susceptibility as the factor c_s^2/(1 - c_s^2 v^2), which controls the speed of sound waves propagating transverse to the fluid flow

## Context

This work extends the Metropolis update algorithm previously applied to the relativistic advection-diffusion equation (Basar 2024) to the full Navier-Stokes system, building on the Density Frame formulation of Armas and Jain (2020) which itself built upon earlier works by Novak (2019) and de Boer (2020). The approach contrasts with the Mueller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) framework (Muller 1967, Israel 1976, 1979) which adds auxiliary dynamical variables that relax on a collisional timescale, and with the BDNK formulation (Bemfica 2017, Kovtun 2019) which embraces second-order-in-time equations. The algorithm is intended to enable stochastic simulations of heavy-ion collisions, particularly for small colliding systems where particle numbers are limited and for searches for critical behavior in nucleus-nucleus collisions at the LHC and STAR fixed target program.
