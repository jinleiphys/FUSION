# Fission Dynamics of Compound Nuclei: Pairing versus Fluctuations

> Energy dependence of fission observables is a key issue for wide nuclear applications. We studied real-time fission dynamics from low-energy to high excitations in the compound nucleus $^{240}$Pu with the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS approach. It is shown that the evolution time of the later phase of fission towards scission is considerably lengthened at finite temperature. As the role of dynamical pairing is vanishing at high excitations, the random transition between single-particle levels around the Fermi surface to mimic thermal fluctuations is indispensable to drive fission. The obtained fission yields and total kinetic energies with fluctuations can be divided into two asymmetric scission channels, namely S1 and S2, which explain well experimental results, and give microscopic support to the Brosa model. With increasing fluctuations, S2 channel takes over S1 channel and the spreading fission observables are obtained.

## Key claim

The paper claims that thermal fluctuations, implemented as random single-particle level transitions, are indispensable for driving fission in compound nuclei at high excitations where dynamical pairing vanishes, and that these fluctuations naturally produce the two asymmetric scission channels (S1 and S2) of the Brosa model, with the S2 channel dominating as fluctuations increase.

## Method

The authors performed real-time fission dynamics of the compound nucleus ²⁴⁰Pu using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock+BCS (TD-BCS) approach with the Sky3D solver and SkM* interaction. To mimic thermal fluctuations at finite temperature, they implemented random transitions between single-particle levels around the Fermi surface using a symmetric Boltzmann distribution, with the transition amplitude proportional to exp(-|ε_k - ε_j|/T) and constrained by the Pauli principle. Initial configurations at finite temperatures were obtained using the SkyAx solver, and the time evolution used a fourth-order Taylor expansion with a time step of 0.1 fm/c in a box of 48×48×64 fm with 0.8 fm grid spacing.

## Key numbers

- At T=0.9 MeV (excitation energy 16.1 MeV), the evolution time to scission is 1900 fm/c, or 6.3×10⁻²¹ s.
- At T=0.9 MeV, the average masses of heavy fragments are A_H=135.0 for the S1 channel and A_H=138.7 for the S2 channel.
- At T=0.9 MeV, the associated average total kinetic energies (TKE) are 186.9 MeV for S1 and 173.3 MeV for S2.
- With thermal fluctuations at T=0.9 MeV (E*=16.1 MeV), the average heavy fragment mass is A_H=137.5(2.4) and TKE=177.4(6.9) MeV.
- With thermal fluctuations at T=1.5 MeV (E*=53.2 MeV), the average heavy fragment mass is A_H=138.5(4.9) and TKE=172.6(3.9) MeV.
- Without thermal fluctuations, fission does not occur above T=0.9 MeV within TD-BCS.

## Context

This work extends microscopic time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) studies of fission, which previously lacked fluctuations and could not reproduce distributions of fission yields (Lacroix 2014, Bulgac 2019). It provides microscopic support for the semi-empirical Brosa model (Brosa 1990) by identifying the origin of its two asymmetric standard channels (S1 and S2) in ²⁴⁰Pu, which had been ambiguous in shape dynamics models based on potential energy surfaces. The results enable a unifying framework connecting microscopic dynamical models and statistical Langevin models for energy-dependent fission observables, and offer a path toward predictive microscopic fission theory for extrapolations where experimental data are absent.
