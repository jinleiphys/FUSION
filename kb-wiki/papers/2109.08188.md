# SNEWPY: A Data Pipeline from Supernova Simulations to Neutrino Signals

> Current neutrino detectors will observe hundreds to thousands of neutrinos from a Galactic supernovae, and future detectors will increase this yield by an order of magnitude or more. With such a data set comes the potential for a huge increase in our understanding of the explosions of massive stars, nuclear physics under extreme conditions, and the properties of the neutrino. However, there is currently a large gap between supernova simulations and the corresponding signals in neutrino detectors, which will make any comparison between theory and observation very difficult. SNEWPY is an open-source software package which bridges this gap. The SNEWPY code can interface with supernova simulation data to generate from the model either a time series of neutrino spectral fluences at Earth, or the total time-integrated spectral fluence. Data from several hundred simulations of core-collapse, thermonuclear, and pair-instability supernovae is included in the package. This output may then be used by an event generator such as sntools or an event rate calculator such as SNOwGLoBES. Additional routines in the SNEWPY package automate the processing of the generated data through the SNOwGLoBES software and collate its output into the observable channels of each detector. In this paper we describe the contents of the package, the physics behind SNEWPY, the organization of the code, and provide examples of how to make use of its capabilities.

## Key claim

SNEWPY bridges the gap between supernova simulation data and predicted neutrino signals in detectors by providing an open-source software pipeline that can interface with simulation data from hundreds of core-collapse, thermonuclear, and pair-instability supernova models, then apply flavor transformation prescriptions to generate spectral fluences at Earth for use with event generators or rate calculators.

## Method

The SNEWPY package extracts neutrino fluxes from supernova simulation data as a function of time, energy, and flavor using an extensible module of SupernovaModel subclasses, each implementing custom input/output for specific simulation formats. It then applies one of 15 flavor transformation prescriptions (including adiabatic and nonadiabatic MSW effects, decoherence scenarios, neutrino decay, and active-sterile mixing) to compute the spectral fluence reaching Earth. The output can be processed through the SNOwGLoBES event rate calculator using automated routines that generate fluence files, simulate detector effects, and collate results into observable channels for each detector.

## Key numbers

- Current neutrino detectors will observe "hundreds to thousands" of neutrinos from a Galactic supernova; future detectors will increase this yield by "an order of magnitude or more"
- The SNEWPY package includes data from "several hundred" simulations of core-collapse, thermonuclear, and pair-instability supernovae
- Example code scales results to Super-Kamiokande inner volume of 32 kt using a factor of 0.32
- Example supernova distance used in demonstration: 10 kpc
- The Nakazato_2013 model covers progenitor masses of 13, 20, 30, and 50 solar masses with time ranges from -0.05 to 20.0 seconds (supernova) and -0.14 to 0.84 seconds (black hole formation)
- The Bollig_2016 model covers 11.2 and 27 solar masses with time ranges from -0.17 to 7.60 seconds (11.2) and -0.34 to 7.60 seconds (27)
- The Warren_2020 model includes 200 simulations covering progenitor masses from 9.0 to 100.0 solar masses
- The Fornax_2021 model includes 15 axisymmetric models with progenitor masses from 12 to 27 solar masses, simulated to 4.5 seconds post-bounce
- For neutrino decay prescriptions, example parameters used are neutrino mass of 1 eV and mean lifetime of 1 day
- For active-sterile mixing prescriptions, the mixing angle theta_14 is set to 10 degrees in example figures

## Context

This work extends prior efforts that bridged the gap between supernova simulations and detector signals on a case-by-case basis, such as Kneller (2008), Dasgupta (2011), and others cited in the paper, but those data sets were time-consuming to generate and did not cover the wide range of available supernova models. SNEWPY enables systematic comparison between theory and observation by providing a consistent interface to hundreds of simulation data sets from multiple modeling groups (Nakazato 2013, Tamborra 2014, O'Connor 2015, Sukhbold 2015, Bollig 2016, Walk 2018, Walk 2019, Fornax 2019, Warren 2020, Kuroda 2020, Fornax 2021, Zha 2021) and can be integrated with event generators like sntools (Migenda 2021) or rate calculators like SNOwGLoBES. The package enables future work on detector sensitivity studies, comparison of explosion models, and extraction of neutrino properties from a Galactic supernova signal.
