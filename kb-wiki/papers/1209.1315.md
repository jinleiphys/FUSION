# Path Integrals in Quantum Physics

> These lectures are intended for graduate students who want to acquire a working knowledge of path integral methods in a wide variety of fields in physics. In general the presentation is elementary and path integrals are developed in the usual heuristic, non-mathematical way for application in many diverse problems in quantum physics. Three main parts deal with path integrals in non-relativistic quantum mechanics, many-body physics and field theory and contain standard examples (quadratic Lagrangians, tunneling, description of bosons and fermions, quantization of gauge theories etc.) as well as specialized topics (scattering, dissipative systems, spin \& color in the path integral, lattice methods etc.). In each part simple Fortran programs which can be run on a PC, illustrate the numerical evaluation of (Euclidean) path integrals by Monte-Carlo or variational methods. Also included are the set of problems which accompanied the lectures and their solutions.

## Key claim

These lecture notes present path integral methods as a working tool for graduate students, developed in a heuristic, non-rigorous manner rather than with strict mathematical foundations, and the author explicitly states that the Feynman path integral is not defined mathematically in a rigorous way except for special cases such as the Euclidean formulation.

## Method

The notes derive Feynman's path integral from the usual quantum mechanical time-evolution operator by decomposing the time interval into N infinitesimal steps using Trotter's product formula, then inserting complete sets of position and momentum eigenstates between each factor. The resulting expression is taken to the continuum limit N to infinity, yielding a sum over all paths weighted by the exponential of i times the classical action divided by Planck's constant.

## Key numbers

* The Gaussian integral G(a) = integral from -infinity to +infinity of dx exp(-a x^2) equals sqrt(pi/a) for Re a > 0, which serves as the fundamental building block throughout the lectures.
* A mechanical watch with size d ~ 10^{-4} m, mass m ~ 10^{-4} kg, and typical time t ~ 1 s has a characteristic action S_1 ~ 10^{-12} J ~ 10^{22} hbar.
* A microprocessor with d ~ 2 x 10^{-7} m, electron mass m ~ 10^{-30} kg, and angular frequency omega ~ 6 GHz has a characteristic action S_2 ~ 2.4 x 10^{-34} J ~ 2 hbar.

## Context

This work is a lecture-based compilation that follows the pedagogical approach of Feynman and Hibbs (1965) and Schulman (1981) for non-relativistic quantum mechanics, Negele and Orland (1987) for many-body physics, and Peskin and Schroeder (1995) for field theory. It extends these textbook treatments by including specialized topics such as dissipative systems, spin and color in the path integral, and lattice methods, along with Fortran programs for numerical evaluation of Euclidean path integrals. The notes enable graduate students to apply path integral techniques across quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and field theory, and include problem sets with solutions for self-study.
