# The finite range simple effective interaction including tensor terms

> The prediction of single particle level crossing phenomenon between $2p_{3/2}$ and $1f_{5/2}$ orbitals in $Ni$- and $Cu$-isotopic chains by the finite range simple effective interaction without requiring the tensor part is discussed. In this case the experimentally observed crossing could be studied as a function of nuclear matter incompressibility, $K(ρ_0)$. The estimated crossing for the neutron number $N$=46 could be reproduced by the equation of state corresponding to $K(ρ_0)$=240 MeV. However, the observed proton gaps between the $1h_{11/2}$ and $1g_{7/2}$ shells in $Sn$ and $Sb$ isotopic chain, and the neutron gaps between the $1i_{13/2}$ and $1h_{9/2}$ shells in $N$=82 isotones, as well as the shell closure properties at $N$=28 require explicit consideration of a tensor part as the central contribution is not enough to initiate the required level splittings.

## Key claim
The finite range simple effective interaction (SEI) can predict the crossing of the $2p_{3/2}$ and $1f_{5/2}$ proton single-particle levels in neutron-rich Ni and Cu isotopes without requiring an explicit tensor term, but only for a specific nuclear matter incompressibility near $K(\rho_0)=240$ MeV; however, other observed level splittings (proton gaps in Sn/Sb chains, neutron gaps in N=82 isotones, and N=28 shell closure) cannot be reproduced by the central interaction alone and require explicit inclusion of a tensor term.

## Method
Spherical Hartree-Fock calculations were performed using the Quasi-local Density Functional Theory (QLDFT) approximation with the finite range simple effective interaction (SEI). The SEI parameters were fixed from nuclear matter constraints (momentum dependence, density dependence, and incompressibility) and finite-nucleus fits to binding energies of $^{40}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb, with the spin-orbit strength $W_0$ and parameter $t_0$ as the two open parameters. A short-range tensor force of Skyrme type (with strengths $T$ and $U$) was added to the SEI, and its two parameters $\alpha_T$ and $\beta_T$ were fixed subject to the constraint that the predicted crossing of $1f_{5/2}$ and $2p_{3/2}$ proton levels in Ni isotopes and the spin inversion in Cu ground states remain unchanged.

## Key numbers
- The crossing of $2p_{3/2}$ and $1f_{5/2}$ proton levels in Ni isotopes occurs at mass number $A=74$ for the SEI equation of state with $\gamma=1/2$, corresponding to nuclear matter incompressibility $K=245$ MeV.
- For the SEI EoS with $\gamma=1/6$ ($K=207$ MeV), the crossing occurs at $A=78$; for $\gamma$ between $1/2$ ($K=245$ MeV) and $2/3$ ($K=263$ MeV), the crossing occurs at $A=72$.
- The four SEI EoSs used correspond to incompressibility values of 207, 226, 245, and 263 MeV for $\gamma$ values of $1/6$, $1/3$, $1/2$, and $2/3$, respectively.
- The experimentally observed crossing for neutron number $N=46$ is reproduced by the equation of state with $K(\rho_0)=240$ MeV.
- Binding energies of Ni isotopes from $A=68$ to $78$ are reported for all four EoSs; for example, $^{68}$Ni: -591.60 MeV ($\gamma=1/6$), -591.08 MeV ($\gamma=1/3$), -590.37 MeV ($\gamma=1/2$), -590.46 MeV ($\gamma=2/3$), compared to experimental -590.407 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the finite range simple effective interaction (SEI) framework, previously developed by Behera et al. (1998) and applied to nuclear matter and finite nuclei, by adding a tensor term. It contrasts with Skyrme and Gogny interactions (e.g., Sk-III, SAMi-T, D1M) which require explicit tensor terms to reproduce the $2p_{3/2}$-$1f_{5/2}$ crossing in Ni isotopes, as shown by Brink (2018), Anguiano et al. (2012), and Otsuka et al. (2005). The finding that SEI can produce this crossing without a tensor term, depending on incompressibility, highlights the role of the central mean-field. The addition of a tensor term to SEI enables study of other phenomena such as proton gaps in Sn and Sb chains, neutron gaps in N=82 isotones, and spin-orbit splitting reduction along N=28 isotones, which the central interaction alone cannot describe.
