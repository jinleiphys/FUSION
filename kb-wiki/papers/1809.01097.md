# Individual low-energy E1 toroidal and compression states in light nuclei: deformation effect, spectroscopy and interpretation

> The existence of individual low-energy E1 toroidal and compression states (TS and CS) in $^{24}$Mg was predicted recently in the framework of quasiparticle random-phase-approximation (QRPA) model with Skyrme forces. It was shown that the strong axial deformation of $^{24}$Mg is crucial to downshift the toroidal strength to the low-energy region and thus make the TS the lowest E1(K=1) dipole state. In this study, we explain this result by simple mean-field arguments. Comparing TS in two strongly axial nuclei, $^{24}$Mg and $^{20}$Ne, we show that the lowest TS is not not a universal phenomenon but rather a peculiarity of $^{24}$Mg. The spectroscopy of TS and CS is analyzed and some additional interpretation of these states is suggested.

# Individual low-energy E1 toroidal and compression states in light nuclei: deformation effect, spectroscopy and interpretation

## Key claim

The strong axial deformation of $^{24}$Mg causes a significant downshift of the toroidal state (TS) to become the lowest E1(K=1) dipole state at 7.92 MeV, but this is not a universal phenomenon in strongly deformed light nuclei; in $^{20}$Ne the TS is not the lowest dipole state even at comparable deformation, making $^{24}$Mg a peculiar case rather than a general example.

## Method

Self-consistent quasiparticle random-phase-approximation (QRPA) calculations were performed using the Skyrme parametrization SLy6 on a 2D coordinate-space mesh in cylindrical coordinates for axial nuclei. The toroidal and compressional dipole responses were computed using current-dependent operators (curl for vortical toroidal mode, divergence for irrotational compression mode) with center-of-mass corrections for deformed nuclei. The single-particle spectrum was taken up to +55 MeV in the continuum, and the two-quasiparticle basis included approximately 1700-1900 (K=0) and 3200-3600 (K=1) states.

## Key numbers

- In $^{24}$Mg at equilibrium deformation $\beta$=0.536, the lowest toroidal K=1 state appears at 7.92 MeV, with major 2qp components (pp[211]$\uparrow$-[330]$\uparrow$ at 54% and nn[211]$\uparrow$-[330]$\uparrow$ at 39%) exhausting 93% of the state norm.
- In $^{24}$Mg, the compressional K=0 state appears at 9.56 MeV, with B(E3K)=17.0 W.u. and major 2qp components exhausting 70% of the norm.
- In $^{20}$Ne at $\beta$=0.56, the toroidal K=1 peak is at 10.11 MeV (B(E3K)=0.5 W.u.) and the compressional K=0 peak is at 7.91 MeV (B(E3K)=45.6 W.u.).
- In $^{20}$Ne at $\beta$=0.72, the toroidal K=1 state is preceded by other dipole states at approximately 6 and 7 MeV.
- The experimental equilibrium deformation for $^{24}$Mg is $\beta_{\rm{exp}}$=0.605; for $^{20}$Ne it is $\beta_{\rm{exp}}$=0.72.
- The Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule is exhausted by 100% and the isoscalar dipole energy-weighted sum rule by 97%.
- K=1 TS in $^{24}$Mg and $^{20}$Ne exhibit weak dipole transitions with B(E1) = 2.52 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$ and 0.93 $\times$ 10$^{-4}$ e$^2$fm$^2$, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous prediction of individual low-energy toroidal states in $^{24}$Mg (Nesterenko et al., 2018, Phys. Rev. Lett.) by explaining the deformation-induced downshift via mean-field arguments and testing universality in $^{20}$Ne. The study contrasts with earlier work on the giant isoscalar toroidal dipole resonance (TDR) in heavier nuclei (e.g., Repko et al., 2017; Kvasil et al., 2011), where experimental identification is complicated by overlap with other modes. The results suggest that low-energy TS in deformed nuclei may correspond to the low-energy isoscalar dipole states (LE-IDS) previously discussed for spherical doubly magic nuclei (e.g., Papakonstantinou et al., 2011), and that the compressional states show octupole character similar to the first 3$^-$ state in $^{208}$Pb (Rowe and Wong, 1987). The work enables future experimental searches for these states in light deformed nuclei using isoscalar reactions such as ($\alpha,\alpha'$).
