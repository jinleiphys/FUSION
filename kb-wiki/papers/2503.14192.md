# Strategic White Paper on AI Infrastructure for Particle, Nuclear, and Astroparticle Physics: Insights from JENA and EuCAIF

> Artificial intelligence (AI) is transforming scientific research, with deep learning methods playing a central role in data analysis, simulations, and signal detection across particle, nuclear, and astroparticle physics. Within the JENA communities-ECFA, NuPECC, and APPEC-and as part of the EuCAIF initiative, AI integration is advancing steadily. However, broader adoption remains constrained by challenges such as limited computational resources, a lack of expertise, and difficulties in transitioning from research and development (R&D) to production. This white paper provides a strategic roadmap, informed by a community survey, to address these barriers. It outlines critical infrastructure requirements, prioritizes training initiatives, and proposes funding strategies to scale AI capabilities across fundamental physics over the next five years.

## Key claim

This white paper argues that scaling AI capabilities in particle, nuclear, and astroparticle physics over the next five years requires coordinated investment in infrastructure, training, and organizational structures, but the authors qualify that broader adoption remains constrained by limited computational resources, lack of expertise, and difficulties transitioning from research and development to production.

## Method

The authors conducted a survey distributed to the ECFA, NuPECC, APPEC, and EuCAIF mailing lists, collecting 137 responses from July to November 2024 across 40 questions covering AI tool usage, computational infrastructure, and anticipated future requirements. Based on the survey results and community input, they formulated 12 strategic recommendations addressing scalability, data infrastructure, production integration, machine learning operations, large language models, foundation models, benchmarks, environmental impact, FAIR compliance, training, interdisciplinary research, and organizational coordination.

## Key numbers

- 137 responses were collected from July to November 2024 across the JENA communities
- 84% of respondents have a European affiliation; 56% came from high-energy physics (ECFA), 14% from astroparticle physics (APPEC), 10% from nuclear physics (NuPECC), and 10% from HPC centers
- 74% of respondents use commercial AI tools such as ChatGPT; 26% have also worked with open-source alternatives
- 20% of the community does not use large language model tools at all
- 71% use PyTorch, 44% use TensorFlow, and 49% use Scikit-learn for machine learning
- Over half of use cases have training data in the 10 GB to 1 TB range
- Only about 15% of users require multiple nodes with several GPUs; only 5% need more than 100 GB of GPU memory
- Around 12% of users require more than 128 GB of CPU memory
- 49% of respondents have successfully reproduced someone's paper results; 40% have not, with inadequate documentation cited by 55% as the primary reason
- Only 16% of respondents indicated limited computational resources were the main issue for reproducibility
- 55% of respondents support an organizational effort for common benchmarks and datasets
- 63% consider large-scale ML models important for advancing physics; 69% agree on the need for collaboration in developing these models
- 58% support creating a domain-specific large language model for fundamental physics
- 76% of respondents believe a large-scale distributed GPU facility would significantly benefit their research
- Nearly 75% of respondents support large distributed GPU facilities
- 77% anticipate scaling their ML work to large production levels over the next five years
- 73% of respondents highlighted the importance of shared ML frameworks
- 70% of survey respondents already use tools such as ChatGPT
- 34% of datasets used in ML model training range between 10-100 GB; 20% exceed 100 GB, with some surpassing 1 TB

## Context

This white paper extends the strategic planning efforts of the JENA communities (ECFA, NuPECC, APPEC) and the EuCAIF initiative, building on prior review articles on AI applications in particle physics (Albertsson 2018, Radovic 2018), nuclear physics (Boehnlein 2021), and gravitational wave science (Cuoco 2020). It complements the US Community Study on the Future of Particle Physics (Snowmass) overviews (Shanahan 2022, Boyda 2022) by providing a European perspective on AI infrastructure needs. The paper enables future coordinated funding proposals and organizational structures for AI in fundamental physics, with EuCAIF proposed as a model for such coordination.
