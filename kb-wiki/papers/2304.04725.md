# Machine learning one-dimensional spinless trapped fermionic systems with neural-network quantum states

> We compute the ground-state properties of fully polarized, trapped, one-dimensional fermionic systems interacting through a gaussian potential. We use an antisymmetric artificial neural network, or neural quantum state, as an ansatz for the wavefunction and use machine learning techniques to variationally minimize the energy of systems from 2 to 6 particles. We provide extensive benchmarks with other many-body methods, including exact diagonalisation and the Hartree-Fock approximation. The neural quantum state provides the best energies across a wide range of interaction strengths. We find very different ground states depending on the sign of the interaction. In the non-perturbative repulsive regime, the system asymptotically reaches crystalline order. In contrast, the strongly attractive regime shows signs of bosonization. The neural quantum state continuously learns these different phases with an almost constant number of parameters and a very modest increase in computational time with the number of particles.

## Key claim

The neural quantum state (NQS) provides the best variational energies across a wide range of interaction strengths for one-dimensional spinless fermions in a harmonic trap with Gaussian interactions, but the authors explicitly note that the even-parity Gaussian potential is an academic toy model that neglects the odd-parity interactions required for spinless fermions, so results should be considered methodological benchmarks rather than experimental predictions.

## Method

The authors use an antisymmetric neural network inspired by FermiNet as a variational ansatz for the ground-state wavefunction of fully polarized fermions in a one-dimensional harmonic trap interacting via a Gaussian potential. They pretrain the network to the noninteracting harmonic oscillator solution, then minimize the variational energy using Monte Carlo sampling with 4096 walkers over 10^5 epochs, computing local energies via automatic differentiation. The method is benchmarked against exact diagonalization and Hartree-Fock for systems of 2 to 6 particles.

## Key numbers

- Systems studied: A = 2 to 6 fully polarized fermions in a 1D harmonic trap
- NQS uses L=2 equivariant layers with H=64 hidden nodes each, with D=1 generalized Slater determinant
- The A=2 NQS has 8580 parameters; the A=6 NQS has 8844 parameters (only 3% increase)
- Pretraining uses 4096 walkers for 10^4 epochs; energy minimization uses 4096 walkers for 10^5 epochs
- Final energy estimates use 10^4 batches of 4096 walkers each (approximately 4.1 x 10^7 total samples)
- For A=6 with V0=-20 and sigma0=0.5, the energy converges to within 0.15% of the final value after about 10,000 epochs
- The time complexity of the determinant evaluation scales as D x A^3, while the GSM and envelope layers scale linearly in A for parameters and quadratically in A for time

## Context

This work extends the FermiNet approach of Pfau (2020) from electronic structure to a simple one-dimensional fermionic toy model, serving as a stepping stone toward neural quantum state descriptions of nuclear systems as previously explored by Keeble (2020) and Adams (2021). The Gaussian interaction model has been used in exact diagonalization studies by Rojo-Francàs (2020) and connects to the Gogny force used in nuclear physics. The paper enables future application of NQS methods to odd-parity interactions and more complex nuclear many-body problems.
