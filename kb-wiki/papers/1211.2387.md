# From light to hyper-heavy molecules and neutron-star crusts in a dynamical mean-field approach

> The richness of phenomena occurring in heavy-ion collisions calls for microscopic approaches where the motion of each nucleon is treated quantum mechanically. The most popular microscopic approach for low-energy collisions between atomic nuclei is the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory, providing a quantum mean-field dynamics of the system. The TDHF approach and some of its extensions are used to predict the evolution of out-of-equilibrium nuclear systems. The formation of di-nuclear systems with a structure close to molecular states is investigated. In particular, lifetimes and exit channels are described. The formation of light molecules and the dynamics of $\al$-clustering are discussed. Di-nuclear systems formed in transfer, deep-inelastic, and quasi-fission reactions, as well as hyper-heavy molecules produced in reactions between actinides are also investigated. The formation and stability of structures in neutron star crusts are finally discussed.

## Key claim

The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach, using the same energy density functional for both nuclear structure and reaction dynamics, can describe the formation and evolution of di-nuclear molecular systems from light to hyper-heavy nuclei and even neutron-star crusts, but the mean-field approximation is expected to fail at higher energies or for longer timescales, and beyond-mean-field correlations are needed for processes such as compound-nucleus formation.

## Method

The TDHF equation is solved as a set of non-linear Schrodinger-like equations for single-particle states, using a Skyrme energy density functional with zero-range effective interactions and gradient terms. Initial conditions assume the collision partners are in their Hartree-Fock ground states at a finite distance, with Galilean boosts applied to give initial momenta corresponding to a Rutherford trajectory. The time evolution uses a predictor-corrector algorithm with time steps of about 5e-25 to 1.5e-24 seconds on a regular cartesian grid with mesh spacing of 0.6-1.0 fm.

## Key numbers

* Fusion cross-section for 16O+16O computed with TDHF using the SLy4d Skyrme EDF, showing sharp increases at angular-momentum-dependent barriers B(L) (specific barrier energies not given numerically in the text).
* Typical regular mesh spacing used in TDHF calculations: Delta x approximately 0.6 fm, 0.8 fm, or 1.0 fm.
* Typical time step increments Delta t in nuclear TDHF calculations range from about 5e-25 s to 1.5e-24 s.
* Initial distances between centers-of-mass of approximately 40 fm are used as a compromise to limit computational time.
* The density of infinite symmetric nuclear matter at saturation is rho_0 approximately 0.16 fm^{-3} and energy per nucleon E/A approximately -16 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the TDHF approach, developed by Dirac (1930) and applied to nuclear dynamics by many authors, to study clustering phenomena ranging from light molecules (e.g., 12C+12C, 12C+16O, 16O+16O) to hyper-heavy molecules formed in reactions between actinides, as well as neutron star crust dynamics. It builds on earlier TDHF studies of alpha-clustering by Sandulescu (1983), light molecules by Umar (1985), and hyper-heavy molecules by Cusson (1980) and Strayer (1983), and on more recent three-dimensional TDHF calculations of fusion, transfer, deep-inelastic, and quasi-fission reactions by many groups. The approach enables future studies of nuclear molecule formation and stability across the entire nuclear chart using a single self-consistent framework.
