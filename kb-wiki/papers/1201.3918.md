# Hadron physics and dynamical chiral symmetry breaking

> Physics is an experimental science; and a constructive feedback between theory and extant and forthcoming experiments is necessary if an understanding of nonperturbative QCD is to be achieved. The Dyson-Schwinger equations connect confinement with dynamical chiral symmetry breaking, both with the observable properties of hadrons, and hence can plausibly provide a means of elucidating the empirical content of strong QCD. We illustrate these points via comments on: in-hadron condensates; dressed-quark anomalous chromo- and electro-magnetic moments; the self-limiting magnitudes of such moments and pion-loop contributions to the gap equation; deep inelastic scattering; the spectra of mesons and baryons; the critical role played by hadron-hadron interactions in producing these spectra; and nucleon elastic and transition form factors.

## Key claim
The Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) can plausibly provide a means of elucidating the empirical content of strong QCD by connecting confinement with dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) and with observable hadron properties, but this requires a constructive feedback between theory and experiments. The paper argues that so-called vacuum condensates are actually intrinsic properties of hadrons rather than spacetime-independent quantities filling the vacuum, which would resolve the factor of 10⁴⁶ mismatch between the predicted and observed cosmological constant.

## Method
The authors employ the Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs) and Bethe-Salpeter equations within a Poincaré-covariant framework to study dressed-quark propagators, meson Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes, and baryon Faddeev equations. They analyze in-hadron condensates through scalar form factors at zero momentum transfer, compute dressed-quark anomalous chromo- and electro-magnetic moments using a symmetry-preserving regularization of a vector-vector contact interaction, and calculate parton distribution functions for pseudoscalar mesons from the DSE approach.

## Key numbers
- The cosmological constant mismatch between QCD vacuum condensate predictions and observations is a factor of 10⁴⁶.
- The critical coupling below which DCSB is impossible via the gap equations is αᶜ/π ≈ 1/3 for QED and QCD, and αᶜ_IR/π ≈ 0.4 for a contact interaction, while describing hadron phenomena requires α_IR/π ≈ 1.
- The gluon mass-scale used in the contact interaction model is m_G = 0.8 GeV.
- The DSE prediction for the pion valence quark distribution function at large Bjorken-x follows q_v(x) ∝ (1-x)²⁺γ, where γ ≥ 0 is an anomalous dimension, in contrast to the (1-x)¹ behavior suggested by earlier data.
- The unified spectrum of u,d-quark hadrons computed using a contact interaction produces a root-mean-square-relative-error per degree of freedom of 13% when compared to Particle Data Group values.

## Context
This work extends the DSE-based approach to hadron physics developed by Maris, Roberts, and Tandy (2000) and the in-hadron condensate concept proposed by Brodsky, Roberts, Shrock, and Tandy (2010, 2011). It contradicts the traditional view of vacuum condensates as spacetime-independent quantities, following the argument of Casher, Kogut, and Susskind (1974) that DCSB can be realized as an intrinsic property of hadrons. The results enable unified calculations of meson and baryon spectra, elastic and transition form factors, and parton distribution functions within a single symmetry-preserving framework, with predictions testable at FNAL Drell-Yan experiments and COMPASS II at CERN.
