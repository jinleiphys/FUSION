# Neutron-Proton Interaction Modeled using Morse Function: Constructing Inverse Potentials Using Variational Monte-Carlo and Phase Function Method

> Understanding neutron-proton(np) interaction has been one of the most studied problems. One way to construct model interaction has been using inversion potentials obtained from experimental scattering phase shifts(SPS). Here, we show that, inverse potentials corresponding to SPS for various l-channels of np interaction can be obtained using variational Monte-Carlo(VMC) technique in tandem with phase function method(PFM) by modeling np-interaction as a Morse function. The S-channel SPS for 3S1 and 1S0 have been obtained, with a mean percentage error with respect to experimental multiple energy analysis data for lab energies up to 1050 MeV, to less than 5%. Similarly, inverse potential for 1S0 pp interaction, with Coulomb term modeled as proportional to erf(), has also been obtained to match experimental values to less than 4%. Non-local and spin-orbit terms are included to obtain inverse potentials for P and D channels and results match with available data to a good extent.

# Neutron-Proton Interaction Modeled using Morse Function: Constructing Inverse Potentials Using Variational Monte-Carlo and Phase Function Method

## Key claim
Inverse potentials for neutron-proton scattering phase shifts across multiple l-channels can be constructed using a Morse function as the interaction model, with parameters optimized by variational Monte-Carlo in tandem with the phase function method. The claim is hedged by the observation that the procedure depends strongly on initial conditions and may converge to local minima, and that for some channels (notably singlet P and D states) the mean percentage error remains above 15%.

## Method
The authors model the nucleon-nucleon interaction using a Morse potential with three parameters (strength V0, equilibrium distance rm, and shape parameter am). They solve the first-order phase function method differential equation using a 5th-order Runge-Kutta integrator to obtain simulated scattering phase shifts. A variational Monte-Carlo procedure iteratively adjusts the potential parameters to minimize the mean percentage error between simulated and experimental phase shifts from the Arndt multiple-energy analysis data. For higher partial waves (P and D channels), non-local potentials via the Aldrich-Green approximation and spin-orbit terms are included, with an additional parameter r0 for the spin-orbit strength.

## Key numbers
- For the 3S1 channel: mean percentage error of 3.6% (data up to 350 MeV) and 4.6% (data up to 1050 MeV); comparative parameter c2 = 0.24 (350 MeV) and 0.16 (1050 MeV)
- For the 1S0 np channel: mean percentage error of 2.8% (350 MeV) and 3.6% (1050 MeV); c2 = 0.03 (350 MeV) and 0.05 (1050 MeV)
- For the 1S0 pp channel (with Coulomb term): mean percentage error of 2.8% (350 MeV) and 3.8% (1050 MeV); Coulomb parameter beta = 0.750 fm^-1
- For the 3P1 channel: mean percentage error of 2.5% (350 MeV) and 12.7% (1050 MeV); c2 = 0.03 (350 MeV) and 0.18 (1050 MeV)
- For the 3D1 channel: mean percentage error of 3.7% (350 MeV) and 3.5% (1050 MeV); c2 = 0.004 (350 MeV) and 0.02 (1050 MeV)
- For the 1P1 channel: mean percentage error of 16.1% (350 MeV) and 13.7% (1050 MeV)
- For the 1D2 channel: mean percentage error of 26.4% (350 MeV) and 24.6% (1050 MeV)
- Deuteron binding energy constraint: ED = -2.224589 MeV used to reduce free parameters for the 3S1 channel

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Khachi et al., recent work) using the Morse potential for nucleon-nucleon interactions, and contrasts with established phenomenological potentials such as the Nijmegen potentials (Nijm group) and Argonne potentials, which have more complex mathematical forms from varied physical considerations. The approach offers a computational inversion technique alternative to the J-matrix method and Marchenko equation methods. The results enable reconstruction of inverse potentials for higher partial waves (l > 2) and could be applied to other optimization problems, as the authors have already applied similar VMC techniques to Woods-Saxon potentials for nuclear shell model single-particle energies and to Morse potentials for diatomic molecular ro-vibrational frequencies.
