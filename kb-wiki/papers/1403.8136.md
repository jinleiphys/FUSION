# Astronuclear Physics with Short-Lived Isotopes

> A revolution in nuclear physics is underway. If you know hadron physics you also know that it will last long, as most past developments in nuclear physics have shown. It will take many decades of dedicated efforts of theorists and experimentalists to give astronomers, cosmologists and stellar modelers what they need for the accuracy required in the description and modeling of many cosmic phenomena of interest. In this contribution I review a small number of examples of the utmost relevance for the nuclear in the astro and in particular the humongous importance of the new area of astronuclear physics studied in radioactive beam facilities.

# Astronuclear Physics with Short-Lived Isotopes

## Key claim
Radioactive beam facilities enable the study of short-lived nuclei far from stability, which is essential for understanding stellar evolution and cosmic phenomena, but the required accuracy for astrophysical modeling will take many decades of dedicated theoretical and experimental effort to achieve.

## Method
The paper reviews indirect nuclear reaction methods applied to short-lived isotopes at radioactive beam facilities, including Coulomb excitation and breakup analyzed with continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) calculations, the Trojan Horse Method (THM) for low-energy reactions via off-shell effects, asymptotic normalization coefficient (ANC) extraction from peripheral transfer reactions, surrogate reactions such as (d,p) for neutron-capture processes, single-nucleon knockout reactions for spectroscopic information, and charge-exchange reactions for weak interaction matrix elements. These techniques are combined with theoretical frameworks including effective field theories (EFT), the resonating group method (RGM), and no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations.

## Key numbers
- The triple-alpha reaction proceeds through a resonance in carbon at 7.65 MeV above the alpha emission threshold, as predicted by Hoyle (1954).
- The neutron separation energy in halo nuclei such as $^{11}$Li can be as low as a few hundred keV (approximately 300 keV for $^{11}$Li), compared to typical binding energies of 8 MeV for stable nuclei.
- Nuclear fusion of 4 protons into $^4$He releases 28 MeV (approximately $4.5 \times 10^{-12}$ J) per alpha particle produced.
- Nuclear reactions in the sun's core occur at relative energies of around 10 keV.
- For $^{33}$Ar, knockout reaction measurements improved the uncertainty in level energies by 2 orders of magnitude and the uncertainty in the $^{32}$Cl(p,$\gamma$)$^{33}$Ar reaction rate by 3 orders of magnitude.
- In core-collapse supernovae, nuclei with neutron number N > 40 dominate matter composition for densities larger than a few $10^{10}$ g cm$^{-3}$.

## Context
This review extends the tradition of applying nuclear physics to stellar modeling that began with Bethe's (1939) CNO cycle and the pp-chain (Adelberger et al., 2011), and Hoyle's (1954) prediction of the triple-alpha resonance later confirmed by Fowler. It connects the discovery of halo nuclei by Tanihata (1985) and Borromean/Efimov systems to modern radioactive beam facilities at GSI, GANIL, RIKEN, and MSU. The paper enables future work by describing how indirect methods (Coulomb dissociation, THM, ANC, surrogate reactions, knockout, charge-exchange) can provide nuclear data for the r-process, rp-process in X-ray bursts, supernova core collapse, and big bang nucleosynthesis, while noting that no small set of key reactions can replace systematic study across the nuclear chart.
