# Chemical freeze-out temperature in hydrodynamical description of Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV

> We study the effect of separate chemical and kinetic freeze-outs to the ideal hydrodynamical flow in Au+Au collisions at RHIC (sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV energy). Unlike in earlier studies we explore how these effects can be counteracted by changes in the initial state of the hydrodynamical evolution. We conclude that the reproduction of pion, proton and antiproton yields necessitates a chemical freeze-out temperature of T = 150 MeV instead of T = 160 - 170 MeV motivated by thermal models. Unlike previously reported, this lower temperature makes it possible to reproduce the p_T-spectra of hadrons if one assumes very small initial time, tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c. However, the p_T-differential elliptic flow, v_2(p_T) remains badly reproduced. This points to the need to include dissipative effects (viscosity) or some other refinement to the model.

## Key claim

Contrary to previous reports, it is possible to reproduce both nonstrange hadron yields and their transverse momentum spectra using ideal fluid hydrodynamics with separate chemical and kinetic freeze-outs, but only if one assumes a chemical freeze-out temperature of T = 150 MeV (instead of the 160-170 MeV used in thermal models) and a very small initial time of tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c. However, the p_T-differential elliptic flow, v_2(p_T), remains badly reproduced even with these adjustments, pointing to the need for dissipative effects or other refinements.

## Method

The author used a boost-invariant ideal hydrodynamical code with an equation of state (EoS) featuring a first-order phase transition at T_c = 170 MeV between an ideal massless parton gas (gluons + three quark flavours) and a hadron resonance gas. For the chemically frozen case, partial chemical equilibrium (PCE) was implemented below the chemical freeze-out temperature, conserving effective particle numbers while allowing resonance formation and decay. The initial state for the PCE calculation was adjusted to use a binary collision profile for entropy density and an initial time of tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c, with an impact-parameter-dependent normalization constant, whereas the chemical equilibrium (CE) calculation used a mixed participant+binary collision profile and tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c.

## Key numbers

- Chemical freeze-out temperature required to reproduce pion, proton, and antiproton yields: T = 150 MeV, instead of T = 160-170 MeV motivated by thermal models.
- Initial time used in the successful partial chemical equilibrium (PCE) calculation: tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c (compared to tau_0 = 0.6 fm/c for the chemical equilibrium case).
- Phase transition temperature: T_c = 170 MeV.
- Average kinetic freeze-out temperature: 120 MeV for PCE, 125 MeV for CE.
- Kinetic freeze-out energy density: 0.117 GeV/fm^3 for PCE, 0.065 GeV/fm^3 for CE.
- Measured midrapidity yields in 0-5% central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV: pi+ = 286.4 +/- 24.2, pi- = 281.8 +/- 22.8, p = 18.4 +/- 2.6, anti-p = 13.5 +/- 1.8 (PHENIX data).
- PCE calculation yields: pi+ = 278, pi- = 278, p = 18, anti-p = 14.
- Chemical equilibrium (CE) calculation yields: pi+ = 273, pi- = 273, p = 10, anti-p = 5.
- Chemical freeze-out at T_ch = 170 MeV (with CE initial state) yields: p = 25, anti-p = 20.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Arbex (2001), Teaney (2002), Hirano (2002), and Kolb (2002) that used separate chemical and kinetic freeze-outs but kept the same initial state as for chemically equilibrated calculations, leading to unsatisfactory results. The author shows that adjusting the initial state (binary collision profile and very early start time tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c) and lowering the chemical freeze-out temperature to 150 MeV can reproduce yields and p_T-spectra, contradicting the earlier conclusion by Hirano (2005) that ideal hydrodynamics with separate freeze-outs cannot reproduce both. The failure to reproduce v_2(p_T) points to the need for viscous hydrodynamics as explored by Chaudhuri (2007), Romatschke (2007), and Song (2007), or hybrid hydro+cascade models by Hirano (2005), Bass (2000), Teaney (2001), and Nonaka (2006).
