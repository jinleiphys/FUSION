# Strongly Correlated Quantum Fluids: Ultracold Quantum Gases, Quantum Chromodynamic Plasmas, and Holographic Duality

> Strongly correlated quantum fluids are phases of matter that are intrinsically quantum mechanical, and that do not have a simple description in terms of weakly interacting quasi-particles. Two systems that have recently attracted a great deal of interest are the quark-gluon plasma, a plasma of strongly interacting quarks and gluons produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions, and ultracold atomic Fermi gases, very dilute clouds of atomic gases confined in optical or magnetic traps. These systems differ by more than 20 orders of magnitude in temperature, but they were shown to exhibit very similar hydrodynamic flow. In particular, both fluids exhibit a robustly low shear viscosity to entropy density ratio which is characteristic of quantum fluids described by holographic duality, a mapping from strongly correlated quantum field theories to weakly curved higher dimensional classical gravity. This review explores the connection between these fields, and it also serves as an introduction to the Focus Issue of New Journal of Physics on Strongly Correlated Quantum Fluids: from Ultracold Quantum Gases to QCD Plasmas. The presentation is made accessible to the general physics reader and includes discussions of the latest research developments in all three areas.

## Key claim

This review argues that ultracold atomic Fermi gases and the quark-gluon plasma, despite differing by more than 20 orders of magnitude in temperature, exhibit very similar hydrodynamic flow characterized by a robustly low shear viscosity to entropy density ratio, which is a signature of strongly correlated quantum fluids described by holographic duality; the claim is hedged by noting that the holographic lower bound on this ratio is non-universal and can be violated in more general gravitational theories such as Gauss-Bonnet gravity.

## Method

The authors present a comparative review of three fields: ultracold quantum gases (focusing on unitary Fermi gases near a Feshbach resonance), quantum chromodynamic plasmas produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC, and holographic duality (the anti-de Sitter/conformal field theory correspondence). They compile experimental data on the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio from ultracold Fermi gas experiments, heavy ion collision analyses, lattice QCD simulations, and classical fluids, and compare these to theoretical predictions from kinetic theory and holographic models including the Kovtun-Starinets-Son (KSS) bound and Gauss-Bonnet gravity bounds.

## Key numbers

* The critical temperature for the unitary Fermi gas superfluid transition is \(T_c/T_F = 0.167(23)\), where \(T_F\) is the Fermi temperature of the non-interacting gas.
* The BCS critical temperature in the weak coupling limit is given by \(T_c/T_F \simeq 0.277 \exp(-\pi/(k_F |a|))\), where \(a\) is the s-wave scattering length and \(k_F\) is the Fermi momentum.
* The holographic lower bound on the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is \(\eta/s \ge \hbar/(4\pi k_B)\), known as the KSS bound.
* A more restrictive bound from Gauss-Bonnet gravity gives \(\eta/s \ge (16/25)\hbar/(4\pi k_B)\).
* The QGP critical temperature is \(T_c \simeq 150\ \mathrm{MeV}/k_B = 1.75 \times 10^{12}\ \mathrm{K}\), and experiments explore temperatures up to approximately \(3T_c\).
* Ultracold Fermi gases operate at temperatures in the nanokelvin to microkelvin range with typical densities from \(10^{11}\) to \(10^{14}\) atoms/cc.
* Neutron matter in proto-neutron stars is at \(T = 1\ \mathrm{MeV}/k_B = 1.2 \times 10^{10}\ \mathrm{K}\) and density \(n = 0.1 n_0\), where \(n_0 = 0.14/\mathrm{fm}^3\) is nuclear matter saturation density.

## Context

This review extends the connection between strongly correlated quantum fluids established by earlier work comparing the quark-gluon plasma and ultracold Fermi gases, particularly the observation of similar hydrodynamic flow and low viscosity. It builds on the holographic duality framework developed by Kovtun, Son, and Starinets (2004) and subsequent work on Gauss-Bonnet gravity bounds by Brigante et al. (2008), and serves as an introduction to a Focus Issue of New Journal of Physics covering all three fields. The review enables future cross-disciplinary research by providing a unified framework for understanding strongly correlated quantum fluids, with open questions in each field identified in the conclusions.
