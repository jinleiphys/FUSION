# Editorial: The Future of Nuclear Structure: Challenges and Opportunities in the Microscopic Description of Nuclei

> The past two decades have witnessed tremendous progress in the microscopic description of atomic nuclei. The Topical Review `The Future of Nuclear Structure' aims at summarizing the current state-of-the-art microscopic calculations in Nuclear Theory and to give a useful reference for young researches who wish to learn more about this exciting discipline.

## Key claim

This editorial introduces a Topical Review collection that summarizes the current state-of-the-art in microscopic *ab initio* nuclear structure calculations, but it explicitly frames the collection as favoring a selected but broad view over an attempt to cover every application, and aims to serve as a reference for young researchers entering the field.

## Method

The paper is an editorial that introduces a collection of topical review articles on microscopic nuclear theory. It surveys the major *ab initio* many-body methods currently in use, including Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) techniques, self-consistent Green's function (SCGF) theory, coupled cluster methods, in-medium similarity renormalization group (SRG), and lattice effective field theory. The editorial summarizes how each contributed article addresses specific challenges such as open-shell isotopes, infinite nucleon systems for astrophysics, neutrino-nucleus interactions, and the consistent description of nuclear structure and reactions.

## Key numbers

- The heaviest systems currently reached by *ab initio* computations have mass number A ≈ 140, with different degrees of accuracy.
- QMC techniques solve the many-body Schrödinger equation with high accuracy for light nuclei up to masses A ∼ 16–40.
- For heavier isotopes, *ab initio* theories can be pushed to masses A ∼ 140 when retaining only relevant nuclear excitations through all-orders resummations.

## Context

This editorial introduces a Topical Review that extends the discussion of nuclear interactions previously covered by "The Long-Lasting Quest for Nuclear Interactions: The Past, the Present and the Future" Topical Review in the same journal. The collection covers contributions from Hergert (2020) on general microscopic many-body approaches, Gandolfi et al. (2020) on QMC methods with local chiral interactions, Lee (2020) on lattice effective field theory for clustering phenomena, Somà (2020) on SCGF theory for spectral information, Coraggio and Itaco (2020) on the Q-box formalism for shell model Hamiltonians, Tichai et al. (2020) on many-body perturbation theory with broken particle number conservation, Tews (2020) on QMC calculations of neutron star equations of state, Rios (2020) on finite-temperature neutron matter using SCGF, Rocco (2020) on neutrino-nucleus interactions for neutrino oscillation experiments, and Rotureau (2020) on *ab initio* optical potentials using coupled cluster methods. The collection enables young researchers to gain a broad perspective on current challenges and opportunities in microscopic nuclear theory, particularly for electroweak observables, nuclear reactions, and astrophysical applications.
