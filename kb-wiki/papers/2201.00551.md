# Bootstrapping the deuteron

> Bootstrap is a novel and ambitious paradigm for quantum physics. It aims to solve the target problems by exploiting theoretical constraints from general physical principles and self-consistency conditions. The bootstrap philosophy dates back to the 1960s. Its real power has been recognized only recently in, e.g., conformal field theories and relativistic scattering amplitudes. Inspired by [X. Han, S. A. Hartnoll, and J. Kruthoff, Phys. Rev. Lett. 125, 041601 (2020)], we report the first bootstrap results in low-energy nuclear physics, where deuteron, with its Hamiltonian given by pionless effective field theory in harmonic oscillator space, is solved by directly exploiting the most fundamental quantum mechanical requirement that probability should never be negative. Our study shows that the bootstrap method can be helpful in studying realistic nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The authors report the first bootstrap results in low-energy nuclear physics, where the deuteron is solved by exploiting the fundamental quantum mechanical requirement that probability should never be negative, but they note that the method requires a tolerance factor to determine when tiny negative eigenvalues of the bootstrap matrix can be safely regarded as zero.

## Method

The deuteron Hamiltonian is taken from pionless effective field theory in harmonic oscillator space (oscillator piEFT), with the Hamiltonian matrix truncated at various Nmax values (2, 3, 9, and 19). Instead of using recursive relations as in previous bootstrap studies, the authors determine bootstrap matrix elements by imposing completeness and self-consistency constraints, excluding one constraint to avoid over-determination, and then test for positive semidefiniteness of the bootstrap matrix using a tolerance factor lambda.

## Key numbers

- Experimental deuteron energy: E_d^exp = -2.224566 MeV
- Theoretical deuteron energy from the Hamiltonian in the Nmax to infinity limit: E_d^th = -2.221 MeV (with V_0 = -5.68658111 MeV and hbar-omega = 7 MeV)
- For Nmax=2, exact lowest eigenvalue: E_2^exact = -2.045671 MeV; bootstrap at lambda=10^-8 gives -2.045671 MeV <= E_2 <= -2.0456701 MeV
- For Nmax=3, exact lowest eigenvalue: E_3^exact = -2.143981 MeV; bootstrap at lambda=10^-8 gives -2.143981 MeV <= E_3 <= -2.143980 MeV
- For Nmax=9, exact lowest eigenvalue: E_9^exact = -2.219002 MeV; bootstrap at lambda=10^-9 gives -2.21900225 MeV <= E_9 <= -2.21900217 MeV
- For Nmax=19, exact lowest eigenvalue: E_19^exact = -2.221187 MeV; bootstrap at lambda=10^-9 gives -2.22118726 MeV <= E_19 <= -2.22118714 MeV

## Context

This work extends the quantum mechanical bootstrap method of Han, Hartnoll, and Kruthoff (2020), who solved the quartic anharmonic oscillator, to a realistic nuclear system for the first time. The deuteron Hamiltonian from oscillator piEFT had previously been used in quantum computing studies by Dumitrescu (2018) and Siwach (2021). The method provides both upper and lower bounds on eigenvalues, unlike traditional variational methods which give only upper bounds, and could be applied to other nuclear systems.
