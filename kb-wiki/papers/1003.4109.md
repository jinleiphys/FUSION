# Microscopic Description of Spherical to Gamma-Soft Shape Transitions in Ba and Xe Nuclei

> The rapid transition between spherical and $γ$-soft shapes in Ba and Xe nuclei in the mass region $A \geq 130$ is analyzed using excitation spectra and collective wave functions obtained by diagonalization of a five-dimensional Hamiltonian for quadrupole vibrational and rotational degrees of freedom, with parameters determined by constrained self-consistent relativistic mean-field calculations for triaxial shapes. The results reproduce the characteristic evolution of excitation spectra and E2 transition probabilities and, in general, a good agreement with available data is obtained. The calculated spectra display fingerprints of a second-order shape phase transition that can approximately be described by analytic solutions corresponding to the E(5) dynamical symmetry.

# Microscopic Description of Spherical to Gamma-Soft Shape Transitions in Ba and Xe Nuclei

## Key claim

The rapid transition from spherical to γ-soft shapes in Ba and Xe nuclei (A ≥ 130) can be described by a five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian whose parameters are determined entirely from constrained self-consistent relativistic mean-field calculations, without any tuning to this specific mass region. The calculated spectra display fingerprints of a second-order shape phase transition that can approximately be described by analytic solutions corresponding to the E(5) dynamical symmetry, though the microscopic calculation breaks the E(5) symmetry for the excited 0+ states and their decay patterns.

## Method

Constrained self-consistent relativistic mean-field plus BCS calculations (using the PC-F1 point-coupling functional and a density-independent δ-force for pairing) were performed to generate triaxial quadrupole binding energy surfaces for even-even Ba and Xe isotopes. From these self-consistent solutions, the parameters of a five-dimensional collective Hamiltonian for quadrupole vibrational and rotational degrees of freedom, including three mass parameters, three moments of inertia (calculated via the Inglis-Belyaev formula), and zero-point energy corrections, were determined as functions of β and γ deformations. The Hamiltonian was then diagonalized to obtain excitation energies and collective wave functions, from which E2 transition probabilities were calculated without effective charges.

## Key numbers

- For 134Ba, the calculated ratio R4/2 ≈ 2.3, close to the E(5)-symmetry prediction of 2.2
- For 134Ba, the calculated B(E2) ratios R1 = B(E2; 4+1 → 2+1) / B(E2; 2+1 → 0+1) = 1.67 and R2 = B(E2; 6+1 → 4+1) / B(E2; 4+1 → 2+1) = 1.33 exactly coincide with E(5) symmetry predictions
- For 130Xe, the largest deviation from experimental R4/2 is approximately 0.15
- For Xe isotopes, the calculated B(E2; 2+1 → 0+1) values decrease from approximately 40 Weisskopf units in 128Xe to about 15 Weisskopf units in 134Xe
- For 134Ba, the average β values are 0.156 for the 2+1 state and 0.166 for the 4+1 state; average γ values are 23.4° for 2+1 and 23.7° for 4+1
- For 134Ba, the 2+2 state has average β = 0.174 and average γ = 29.6°
- The oblate configuration in 134Ba is approximately 0.5 MeV above the prolate minimum

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic studies of first-order shape phase transitions (X(5)-like) in the A ≈ 150 region (Li 2009, Niksic 2007) to the second-order spherical-to-γ-soft transition in Ba and Xe isotopes. The approach uses the same relativistic energy density functional (PC-F1) and collective Hamiltonian framework previously applied to X(5)-like transitions. The results are compared with the E(5) critical-point symmetry model of Iachello (2000) and experimental data for 134Ba (identified by Casten and Zamfir 2000 as the first E(5) candidate) and Xe isotopes. The study enables systematic microscopic investigation of shape phase transitions across different mass regions using the same universal energy density functional, though the authors note that the excited 0+ states are systematically calculated at higher energies than experiment, likely due to the neglect of coupling between shape oscillations and pairing vibrations, as previously studied by Pál and Papp (1993) and Rohozinski (1977).
