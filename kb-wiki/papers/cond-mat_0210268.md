# Conductivity in quasi two-dimensional systems

> The conductivity in quasi two-dimensional systems is calculated using the quantum kinetic equation. Linearizing the Lenard-Balescu collision integral with the extension to include external field dependences allows one to calculate the conductivity with diagrams beyond the GW approximation including maximally crossed lines. Consequently the weak localization correction as an interference effect appears here from the field dependence of the collision integral (the latter dependence sometimes called intra-collisional field effect). It is shown that this weak localization correction has the same origin as the Debye-Onsager relaxation effect in plasma physics. The approximation is applied to a system of quasi two-dimensional electrons in hetero-junctions which interact with charged and neutral impurities and the low temperature correction to the conductivity is calculated analytically. It turns out that the dynamical screening due to charged impurities leads to a linear temperature dependence, while the scattering from neutral impurities leads to the usual Fermi-liquid behavior. By considering an appropriate mass action law to determine the ratio of charged to neutral impurities we can describe the experimental metal-insulator transition at low temperatures as a Mott-Hubbard transition.

## Key claim

The weak localization correction to conductivity in quasi two-dimensional systems arises from the explicit field dependence of the collision integral (the "intra-collisional field effect"), and this interference effect has the same formal origin as the Debye-Onsager relaxation effect in plasma physics, rather than requiring diagrammatic summation of maximally crossed diagrams separately.

## Method

The author linearizes the quantum Lenard-Balescu collision integral, including all external field dependences, to derive the conductivity from a kinetic equation approach. The collision integral is evaluated analytically using a gauge-invariant Green's function ansatz (GKB ansatz) and the balance equation for momentum is solved to extract both the relaxation time (from on-shell scattering) and the relaxation function (from off-shell, field-dependent interference effects). The method is applied to a quasi two-dimensional electron gas in heterojunctions interacting with charged and neutral impurities, with dynamical screening treated via the dielectric function.

## Key numbers

- The inverse screening length in the low-temperature limit is given by \(\kappa = \frac{e_a^2 m_a s_a}{\hbar^2} \left( 1 + \left| \frac{e_b s_b}{e_a s_a} \right| \sqrt{\xi} \right)\), where \(\xi = m_b^2 e_b^2 / m_a^2 e_a^2\).
- The dimensionless screening parameter is \(\kappa_a = \frac{\hbar \kappa}{2 p_{fa}} = \sqrt{ \frac{s_a^3 e_a^4 m_a^2}{16 \pi \hbar^4 n_a} }\).
- For typical experimental densities of \(7 \times 10^{10} \text{cm}^{-2}\), \(\kappa_a = 284.9 / \sqrt{n_a / (7 \times 10^{10} \text{cm}^{-2})} \cdot (m^*/m)\), which is a large parameter.
- The Fermi energy is \(1.9 \text{ K} \times n_a / (7 \times 10^{10} \text{cm}^{-2})\) for the experimental system considered.
- The conductivity formula in the low-density limit is \(\sigma / \sigma_0 = 1 - \zeta/(2T) e^{E_b/T} - T/(2\epsilon_{fa}) + T/(2\epsilon_{fa} \kappa_a^2) \ln(2\kappa_a/e)\), with \(\sigma_0 = (e^2/h) \left( \zeta/(\sqrt{2}T) e^{E_b/T} + (T/\epsilon_{fa})(\kappa_a - \pi/4) \right)\).

## Context

This work extends the kinetic theory approach to weak localization, showing that the field-dependent collision integral produces the same correction as maximally crossed diagrams studied by Vollhardt and Wolfle (1980), Altshuler and Aronov (1985), and Bergmann (1984). The author connects this to the Debye-Onsager relaxation effect in electrolytes, originally derived by Debye and Huckel (1923) and Onsager (1927). The analytical result that dynamical screening from charged impurities yields a linear temperature dependence of conductivity (while neutral impurities give Fermi-liquid \(T^2\) behavior) agrees with numerical calculations by Das Sarma (1986) and Gold and Dolgopolov (1986). The comparison with experimental data from Kravchenko et al. (2000) supports a Mott-Hubbard transition picture rather than Anderson localization, consistent with effective mass measurements by Shashkin et al. (2001).
