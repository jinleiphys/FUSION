# Nucleon resonance production in $K^+N$ interactions

> In this work, we discuss the possibility of studying nucleon resonances in the $K^+N$ interactions. Based on our model calculations, it is found that the hyperon exchange plays an important role for the excitation of nucleon resonances in the $K^+N$ interactions. Therefore, the studies on nucleon resonance production in the $K^+N$ interactions can offer us valuable information about the couplings of nucleon resonances with strange particles. By selecting suitable decay channels of nucleon resonances, some reactions are also suitable to look for missing resonances.

## Key claim
The paper argues that studying nucleon resonance production in \(K^+N\) interactions can provide valuable information about the couplings of nucleon resonances with strange particles, but this claim is conditional on the assumption that hyperon exchange plays an important role in the excitation mechanism, which the author's model calculations support but do not definitively prove.

## Method
The author adopts an isobar resonance model using effective Lagrangians to describe particle interactions, calculating Feynman diagrams for \(t\)-channel and \(u\)-channel processes in \(K^+N\) reactions. Two specific reactions are analyzed: \(K^+ N \to K N \eta\) near threshold (assuming dominance of the \(N^*(1535)\) resonance) and \(K^+p \to K^+K^+\Lambda\) (considering \(N^*(1650)\), \(N^*(1710)\), and \(N^*(1720)\) excitations). The model parameters are adjusted to reproduce the few available experimental data.

## Key numbers
- For the \(K^+p \to K^+K^+\Lambda\) reaction, the \(\Lambda\) exchange gives dominant contributions at lower laboratory momenta (\(P_{lab} < 3\) GeV), while the \(\rho\) exchange plays the dominant role at higher momenta (\(P_{lab} > 5\) GeV).
- The \(N^*(1710)\) gives the dominant contributions in the whole energy region for the \(K^+p \to K^+K^+\Lambda\) reaction.

## Context
This work extends the study of nucleon resonances beyond the traditional \(\pi N\) and \(\gamma N\) reactions, which have been the main sources of knowledge on \(N^*\) properties (as summarized in the Particle Data Group book, 2012). The author notes that only one \(N^*\) state is ranked as three stars for its coupling with \(K\Lambda\) or \(K\Sigma\) channels, indicating poor knowledge. The paper builds on earlier experimental studies from the 1960s and 1970s (data1, data2, data3) and on theoretical work on nucleon resonance production in \(NN\) collisions and \(K^+N\) elastic scattering. The approach is complementary to studies of \(NN \to NN^*\) reactions, where pseudoscalar meson exchange is allowed but hyperon exchange is forbidden. The author suggests that selecting suitable decay channels could help search for "missing resonances" that have large couplings to strange particle channels, and references theoretical arguments by Weise and Oset that the \(N^*(1535)\) may be a \(K\Sigma-K\Lambda\) molecular state with large couplings to strange channels.
