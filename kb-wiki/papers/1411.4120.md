# The neutron-gamma Feynman variance to mean approach: gamma detection and total neutron-gamma detection (theory and practice)

> Two versions of the neutron-gamma variance to mean (Feynman-alpha method or Feynman-Y function) formula for either gamma detection only or total neutron-gamma detection, respectively, are derived and compared in this paper. The new formulas have a particular importance for detectors of either gamma photons or detectors sensitive to both neutron and gamma radiation. If applied to a plastic or liquid scintillation detector, the total neutron-gamma detection Feynman-Y expression corresponds to a situation where no discrimination is made between neutrons and gamma particles. The gamma variance to mean formulas are useful when a detector of only gamma radiation is used or when working with a combined neutron-gamma detector at high count rates. The theoretical derivation is based on the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation with inclusion of general reactions and passage intensities for neutrons and gammas, but with the inclusion of prompt reactions only. A one energy group approximation is considered. The comparison of the two different theories is made by using reaction intensities obtained in MCNPX simulations with a simplified geometry for two scintillation detectors and a 252Cf-source enclosed in a steel container. In addition, the variance to mean ratios, neutron, gamma and total neutron-gamma, are evaluated experimentally for a weak 252Cf neutron-gamma source in a steel container, a 137Cs random gamma source and a 22Na correlated gamma source. Due to the focus being on the possibility of using neutron-gamma variance to mean theories for both reactor and safeguards applications, we limited the present study to the general analytical expressions for Feynman-Y formulas.

## Key claim

The paper derives two new versions of the Feynman-alpha (variance-to-mean) formula for gamma-only detection and for total neutron-gamma detection without pulse shape discrimination, but the derivation is limited to prompt reactions only, uses a one-energy-group approximation, and assumes an infinite homogeneous medium with space-independent reaction intensities, which does not match typical safeguards measurement conditions where the detector is outside a small sample.

## Method

The authors used the Chapman-Kolmogorov forward master equation with a compound Poisson source emitting neutrons and gammas, considering neutron absorption, fission with gamma emission, and detection reactions, while excluding delayed neutrons, capture gammas, photofission, and photonuclear reactions. They derived analytical expressions for the variance-to-mean ratio (Feynman-Y function) for gamma detection and total neutron-gamma detection, then compared these formulas using reaction intensities obtained from MCNPX simulations of a simplified geometry with two EJ-309 liquid scintillation detectors and a 252Cf source. Experimental measurements were also performed with weak 252Cf, 137Cs, and 22Na sources.

- Ratio λ₂/λ₁ = 2.68476, leading to Y_t,∞ < Y_g,∞ and Y_g,∞ > Y_n,∞ for the specific case
- For a 252Cf source, the expression (2(r⁽⁰,¹⁾)²/r⁽⁰,²⁾ - 1) equals 0.924959
- The variance-to-mean ratio for neutrons reaches its asymptotic value between 500-1000 ns, while gamma and total detection ratios plateau starting in the time interval of 1-500 ns
- Detection efficiency for both neutrons and gammas was assumed to be 1% in the simulations

## Context

This work extends the traditional Feynman-alpha method for neutrons, which has been used for subcritical reactivity measurements in reactors and accelerator-driven systems (Freya) and more recently for nuclear safeguards (Twomey, Verbeke, Croft 2012), to include gamma detection. Previous work by Nakae showed that fast detection with liquid scintillators (100 seconds) outperformed thermal ³He detection (8.5 hours) for assaying weak 252Cf sources, but required pulse shape discrimination. The new formulas eliminate the need for discrimination by either using gamma-only detection or total detection without discrimination. The one-group approximation is justified by earlier work (Dinanew) showing that one-point one-group and two-point two-group versions yield the same results for fast neutron detection. The approach enables potential safeguards applications for characterizing unknown samples and orphan sources using gamma or total detection rather than neutron-only detection.
