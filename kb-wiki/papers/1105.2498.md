# Cooper Pair's Magnetic Moment in MCFL Color Superconductivity

> We investigate the effect of the alignment of the magnetic moments of Cooper pairs of charged quarks that form at high density in three-flavor quark matter. The high density phase of this matter in the presence of a magnetic field is known to be the Magnetic Color-Flavor-Locked (MCFL) phase of color superconductivity. We derive the Fierz identities of the theory and show how the explicit breaking of the rotational symmetry by the uniform magnetic field field opens new channels of interactions and allows the formation of a new diquark condensate. The new order parameter is a spin-1 diquark condensate proportional to the component in the field direction of the average magnetic moment of the pairs of charged quarks. In the region of large fields, the new condensate's magnitude becomes comparable to the larger of the two scalar gaps. Since there is no solution of the gap equations with nonzero scalar gaps and zero value of this magnetic moment condensate, its presence in the MCFL phase is unavoidable. This is consistent with the fact that the extra condensate does not break any symmetry that has not already been broken by the known MCFL gaps. The spin-1 condensate enhances the condensation energy of pairs formed by charged quarks and the magnetization of the system. We discuss the possible consequences of the new order parameter on the issue of the chromomagnetic instability that appears in color superconductivity at moderate density.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in the Magnetic Color-Flavor-Locked (MCFL) phase of color superconductivity, a spin-1 diquark condensate proportional to the magnetic moment of Cooper pairs of charged quarks is unavoidably generated alongside the scalar gaps, and at sufficiently strong magnetic fields its magnitude becomes comparable to the larger scalar gap.

## Method

The authors employ a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model with a pointlike four-fermion interaction to describe three-flavor quark matter at high density in a uniform external magnetic field. They derive the Fierz identities under the broken rotational symmetry (O(3) reduced to O(2) by the field), identify a new attractive pairing channel with Dirac structure \(C\gamma_5\sigma_{12}\), and introduce a new ansatz for the gap matrix that includes the magnetic-moment condensate \(\Delta_M\) alongside the known MCFL gaps \(\Delta\) and \(\Delta_B\). The free energy is obtained via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation in the mean-field approximation, and the coupled gap equations for the three order parameters are solved numerically.

## Key numbers

- The CFL gap at zero magnetic field is set to \(\Delta_0 = 25\ \mathrm{MeV}\) as a reference, with chemical potential \(\mu = 500\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and momentum cutoff \(\Lambda = 1\ \mathrm{GeV}\).
- At sufficiently strong magnetic fields (\({\tilde e}{\tilde B} \gtrsim \mu^2\)), the new condensate \(\Delta_M\) becomes comparable in magnitude to the larger scalar gap \(\Delta_B\).
- The gap \(\Delta_B\) is about twice as large at very strong fields when \(\Delta_M\) is included in the analysis compared to when it is ignored.
- Magnetar surface magnetic fields are in the range \(10^{14}-10^{16}\ \mathrm{G}\), with estimated interior fields up to \(10^{18}-10^{20}\ \mathrm{G}\).

## Context

This work extends the MCFL phase previously introduced by the same authors (Feng, Ferrer, de la Incera, 2008) by demonstrating that the explicit breaking of rotational symmetry by the magnetic field opens a new pairing channel, leading to a magnetic-moment condensate that is not symmetry-protected and is unavoidable in the ground state. The spin-1 condensate differs from those studied in 2SC models without magnetic fields (Buballa, 2005) because the rotational symmetry is already broken by the external field rather than spontaneously, and the pairs remain neutral under the rotated electromagnetism. The enhanced gap \(\Delta_B\) at strong fields may help stabilize the MCFL phase against chromomagnetic instabilities that appear at moderate densities (Fukushima, 2005), and could be relevant for magnetar cores and future low-temperature/high-density experiments at NICA, CBM@FAIR, and low-energy RHIC.
