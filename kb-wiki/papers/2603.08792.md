# Gamma-ray Signatures of r-Process Radioactivity from the Collapse of Magnetized White Dwarfs

> We predict the gamma-ray line emission from $r$-process nuclei synthesized in the ejecta of the accretion-induced collapse (AIC) of a magnetized, rapidly rotating white dwarf. Using ejecta from a two-dimensional general-relativistic neutrino-magnetohydrodynamic simulation, further evolved with a radiation-hydrodynamics code coupled to an in-situ nuclear reaction network, we construct angle-dependent gamma-ray spectra in the $0.01$-$10\,\mathrm{MeV}$ band via composition-dependent ray-tracing through the ejecta. The emission between $\sim$1 and $10\,$d is dominated by $^{132}$I ($t_{1/2} = 2.3\,$h), continuously replenished by the decay of its parent $^{132}$Te ($t_{1/2} = 3.2\,$d), with additional contributions from $^{131}$I, $^{133}$Xe, and $^{132}$Te. At $t\gtrsim 20$ d, $^{56}$Co (from $^{56}$Ni decay) becomes the primary emitter. The simultaneous presence of $r$-process and iron-peak gamma-ray lines is distinctive of AIC ejecta and absent in binary neutron star mergers, where iron-peak nuclei are generally not synthesized. Comparing with the $3σ$ continuum sensitivities of planned MeV gamma-ray telescopes (COSI, AMEGO-X, e-ASTROGAM, GRAMS, GammaTPC), we find the brightest $r$-process lines detectable to $\sim 10\,\mathrm{Mpc}$ by GammaTPC and GRAMS, with the signal approaching their sensitivity threshold at $30\,\mathrm{Mpc}$. The $r$-process spectral features survive time integration over $\sim 30$ d exposures, demonstrating robustness against the long observation times required by gamma-ray detectors.

## Key claim

The gamma-ray line emission from r-process nuclei synthesized in the ejecta of magnetized white dwarf accretion-induced collapse (AIC) is predicted to be detectable by planned MeV gamma-ray telescopes (GammaTPC and GRAMS) out to approximately 10 Mpc, with the signal approaching sensitivity thresholds at 30 Mpc, but only for the brightest lines and under the assumption that the ejecta properties from a single 2D simulation with a strong initial magnetic field (10^12 G) are representative of the physical outcome.

## Method

The authors used ejecta profiles from a two-dimensional general-relativistic neutrino-magnetohydrodynamic simulation of a magnetized, rapidly rotating white dwarf collapse, evolved with the radiation-hydrodynamics code kNECnn coupled to the SkyNet nuclear reaction network tracking 7836 isotopes. They constructed angle-dependent gamma-ray spectra in the 0.01-10 MeV band by post-processing nucleosynthesis outputs on a 2D spherical grid, performing composition-dependent ray-tracing through the ejecta with opacities from the NIST XCOM database, accounting for Doppler shifts from homologous expansion and treating Compton scattering, photoelectric absorption, and pair production as effective absorption.

## Key numbers

- Total ejected mass: M_ej ≈ 0.18 M_sun with kinetic energy E_ej ≈ 8 × 10^50 erg
- Mass-weighted average electron fraction: ⟨Y_e⟩ ~ 0.24, average velocity: ⟨v_∞⟩ ~ 0.1 c, average entropy: ⟨s⟩ ~ 9 k_B/baryon
- Lanthanide mass fraction: X_lan ≈ 6%
- 132I dominates emission between ~1 and 10 d (t_1/2 = 2.3 h), continuously replenished by decay of parent 132Te (t_1/2 = 3.2 d)
- 56Co (from 56Ni decay) becomes primary emitter at t ≳ 20 d
- Brightest r-process lines detectable to ~10 Mpc by GammaTPC and GRAMS, approaching sensitivity threshold at 30 Mpc
- r-process spectral features survive time integration over ~30 d exposures

## Context

This work extends the gamma-ray line emission approach previously applied to binary neutron star mergers (Hotokezaka 2015, Li 2019, Korobkin 2019, Chen 2021, Magistrelli 2025) to the AIC channel for the first time, using the ejecta from a companion paper (Pitik 2026) that established magnetized AIC as a viable r-process progenitor. The simultaneous presence of r-process and iron-peak gamma-ray lines is distinctive of AIC ejecta and absent in binary neutron star mergers, where iron-peak nuclei are generally not synthesized; this enables future missions (COSI, AMEGO-X, e-ASTROGAM, GRAMS, GammaTPC) to distinguish between progenitor channels for r-process nucleosynthesis events within ~10-30 Mpc.
