# Status of Lattice QCD Determination of Nucleon Form Factors and their Relevance for the Few-GeV Neutrino Program

> Calculations of neutrino-nucleus cross sections begin with the neutrino-nucleon interaction, making the latter critically important to flagship neutrino oscillation experiments, despite limited measurements with poor statistics. Alternatively, lattice QCD (LQCD) can be used to determine these interactions from the Standard Model with quantifiable theoretical uncertainties. Recent LQCD results of $g_{\mathrm{A}}$ are in excellent agreement with data, and results for the (quasi-)elastic nucleon form factors with full uncertainty budgets are expected within a few years. We review the status of the field and LQCD results for the nucleon axial form factor, $F_{\mathrm{A}}(Q^2)$, a major source of uncertainty in modeling sub-GeV neutrino-nucleon interactions. Results from different LQCD calculations are consistent, but collectively disagree with existing models, with potential implications for current and future neutrino oscillation experiments. We describe a road map to solidify confidence in the LQCD results and discuss future calculations of more complicated processes, important to few-GeV neutrino oscillation experiments.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD (LQCD) results for the nucleon axial form factor \(F_{\mathrm{A}}(Q^2)\) from different collaborations are consistent with each other but collectively disagree with existing phenomenological models, showing a slower falloff with increasing \(Q^2\) that could enhance neutrino-nucleon cross sections by 30–40% for neutrino energies above 1 GeV, though full uncertainty budgets for the form factors are expected only within a few years.

## Method

The authors review the status of LQCD calculations of nucleon form factors, focusing on the axial form factor \(F_{\mathrm{A}}(Q^2)\) relevant to few-GeV neutrino oscillation experiments. They survey existing LQCD results from multiple collaborations using independent computation methods, including different fermion discretization schemes (clover-Wilson, domain wall, twisted mass, and staggered fermions), and discuss systematic uncertainties such as continuum extrapolation, finite volume effects, excited state contamination, and the signal-to-noise problem. The review also compares LQCD results to experimental data from deuterium bubble-chamber experiments and to phenomenological parameterizations like the dipole ansatz and \(z\)-expansion fits.

## Key numbers

- The nucleon axial coupling \(g_{\mathrm{A}} = F_{\mathrm{A}}(0)\) has been determined by LQCD with a 1% total uncertainty, consistent with the experimental value from neutron decay.
- The axial radius (proportional to the slope of \(F_{\mathrm{A}}(Q^2)\) at \(Q^2=0\)) has a 50% uncertainty from deuterium scattering data alone, or approximately 35% when combining deuterium scattering and muonic hydrogen data.
- The slower falloff of the LQCD axial form factor with \(Q^2\) translates to an enhancement of neutrino-nucleon cross sections by as much as 30–40% for neutrino energies greater than 1 GeV.
- The tension between the BBBA05 and Borah et al. (2020) parameterizations of the proton magnetic form factor is significant over all \(Q^2 > 0\) at the level of several percent.
- DUNE has a neutrino energy spectrum peaked at approximately 2.5 GeV with significant contributions between 0.1–10 GeV; Hyper-K has a spectrum peaked at approximately 0.6 GeV with contributions between 0.1–2 GeV.

## Context

This review extends the work of Meyer et al. (2016) and Hill et al. (2017), which demonstrated that the dipole ansatz overconstrains the axial form factor and that a model-independent \(z\)-expansion yields uncertainties nearly an order of magnitude larger. The LQCD results contradict existing phenomenological models extracted from sparse deuterium bubble-chamber data from the 1960s–1980s (ANL Barish 1977, BNL Fanourakis 1980, BNL Baker 1981, Kitagaki 1983, Allasia 1990). The review enables future LQCD calculations of more complicated processes such as resonant pion production, inclusive scattering, and two-nucleon response functions, which are important for the DUNE and Hyper-K neutrino oscillation experiments.
