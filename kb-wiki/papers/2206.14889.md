# Applications of reduced basis methods to the nuclear single particle spectrum

> Reduced basis methods provide a powerful framework for building efficient and accurate emulators. Although widely applied in many fields to simplify complex models, reduced basis methods have only been recently introduced into nuclear physics. In this letter we build an emulator to study the single-particle structure of atomic nuclei. By scaling a suitable mean-field Hamiltonian, a "universal" reduced basis is constructed capable of accurately and efficiently reproduce the entire single-particle spectrum of a variety of nuclei. Indeed, the reduced basis model reproduces both ground- and excited-state energies as well as the associated wave-functions with remarkable accuracy. Our results bode well for more demanding applications that use Bayesian optimization to calibrate nuclear energy density functionals.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a reduced basis method (RBM) emulator, trained on random parameter samples spanning the range of Woods-Saxon parameters fitted to FSUGarnet predictions, can reproduce the entire single-particle spectrum (both ground and excited states) for multiple spherical nuclei using a single "universal" basis, with the caveat that the root-mean-square errors quoted (0.01-0.02 MeV) are only estimates because the codes cannot compute bound-state energies with better than approximately 10 keV precision.

## Method

The authors construct a dimensionless Schrodinger equation with a Woods-Saxon mean-field potential (including central, Coulomb, and spin-orbit terms) whose parameters are fitted to effective Schrodinger-like neutron potentials from the covariant energy density functional FSUGarnet for seven doubly-magic nuclei. They generate ten random triplets of the three dimensionless parameters (lambda_0, lambda_so, beta) drawn from uniform distributions spanning the fitted values, obtain exact solutions via the Runge-Kutta shooting method for each realization, then filter the trained set through singular value decomposition to produce an orthonormal reduced basis, keeping only states with singular values above 10^-3 of the largest singular value. The emulator solves the eigenvalue problem by direct diagonalization of the Hamiltonian matrix within each angular momentum subspace using the Galerkin projection approach.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square error for the neutron spectrum of 208Pb: approximately 0.01 MeV, defined over all N bound states
- Root-mean-square error for the neutron spectrum of 48Ca: approximately 0.02 MeV
- Reduced basis dimensions range from nine (for kappa=-1, l=0, j=1/2 sector) to four (for kappa=-7 sector)
- The training set uses ten random parameter triplets drawn from uniform distributions spanning lambda_0 from 28.02 to 144.98, lambda_so from 0.9608 to 1.61791, and beta from 4.5920 to 10.529
- The dimensionless Woods-Saxon parameter beta ranges from 4.5920 (16O) to 10.529 (208Pb), corresponding to half-density radii from 3.0551 fm to 7.19934 fm

## Context

This work extends eigenvector continuation (Frame, 2017) and reduced basis methods (Quarteroni, 2015; Hesthaven, 2016), which have only recently been introduced into nuclear physics (Bonilla, 2022; Melendez, 2022), to the nuclear single-particle problem using a mean-field Hamiltonian constrained by the FSUGarnet covariant energy density functional (Chen, 2014). The only prior extension of eigenvector continuation to excited states was by Franzke (2021) for the one-dimensional quartic anharmonic oscillator; this work demonstrates the approach for realistic nuclear systems. The results enable future applications that use Bayesian optimization to calibrate nuclear energy density functionals, where the same observables must be computed for many nuclei many times to sample parameter space.
