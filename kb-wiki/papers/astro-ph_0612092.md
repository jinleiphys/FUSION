# Unmasking neutron star interiors using cooling simulations

> We introduce a new tool for 'unmasking' the composition of neutron star (NS) interiors which is based on the fact that the state of matter at high densities determines the statistics of both NS observables, the temperature-age (TA) data as well as the mass distribution. We use modern cooling simulations to extract distributions of NS masses required to reproduce those of the yet sparse data in the TA plane. By comparing the results with a mass distribution for young, nearby NSs from population synthesis we can sharpen two NS cooling problems. The direct Urca (DU) problem consists in a narrowing of the NS population at the mass value for which the DU process as the most effective cooling mechanism in the hadronic layer of the star can occur. The Vela mass problem is a broadening of the population beyond the range of the typical mass window of 1.1 - 1.5 M_sun. Applying this tool to modern EoS we discuss examples for pure hadronic stars which are in conflict with these constraints while hybrid stars with a color superconducting quark matter core can predict a satisfactory mass distribution, provided the smallest diquark pairing gap has a properly defined density dependence.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new method for inferring the composition of neutron star interiors by comparing the mass distribution extracted from cooling simulations of temperature-age (TA) data with the mass distribution from population synthesis models, and argues that this tool can favor hybrid stars with a color superconducting quark matter core over pure hadronic stars, provided the smallest diquark pairing gap has a properly defined density dependence.

## Method

The authors used a given cooling code to simulate the thermal evolution of neutron stars for different equations of state (EoS) and cooling regulators, varying the star mass to produce cooling curves in the temperature-age (TA) plane. They then constructed a probability measure from the observed TA data points (with their error bars) to extract a mass distribution for each cooling model, normalizing the result to 100 objects. This extracted mass distribution was compared to the mass distribution of young, nearby neutron stars from a population synthesis model by Popov et al. (2005).

## Key numbers

- The typical mass window for neutron stars from population synthesis is 1.1 - 1.5 solar masses.
- The hadronic DU process in the DBHF model EoS occurs for all neutron stars with masses above 1.27 solar masses.
- The phase transition from hadronic to quark matter occurs at a critical density of 2.75 times the nuclear saturation density n0, corresponding to 0.44 fm^{-3}.
- The quark chemical potential at the transition is mu_c = 330 MeV.
- The small pairing gaps in possible residual single color/single flavor channels are typically in the interval 10 keV to 1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of the "direct Urca (DU) problem" and the "Vela mass problem" previously raised in the literature (Blaschke et al., 2004; Klahn et al., 2006; Kolomeitsev et al., 2004) by providing a quantitative measure for the mass distribution extracted from cooling data. The method is designed to distinguish between pure hadronic stars and hybrid stars with a color superconducting quark matter core, which otherwise "masquerade" as neutron stars due to similar mechanical properties (Alford et al., 2004). The approach enables future quantitative tests when better statistics for TA data and improved population synthesis models become available.
