# How quantum bound states bounce and the structure it reveals

> We investigate how quantum bound states bounce from a hard surface. Our analysis has applications to ab initio calculations of nuclear structure and elastic deformation, energy levels of excitons in semiconductor quantum dots and wells, and cold atomic few-body systems on optical lattices with sharp boundaries. We develop the general theory of elastic reflection for a composite body from a hard wall. On the numerical side we present ab initio calculations for the compression of alpha particles and universal results for two-body states. On the analytical side we derive a universal effective potential that gives the reflection scattering length for shallow two-body states.

## Key claim

The paper develops a general theory for how quantum bound states reflect elastically from a hard wall, showing that the reflection radius decreases monotonically with collision energy and that this decrease reveals the compressibility of the bound state under unilateral stress. The authors hedge that while a hard surface cannot be constructed for protons and neutrons in nuclear physics experiments, the analysis is still useful for *ab initio* numerical calculations, analogous to how Luscher's periodic boundary analysis provides the framework for lattice QCD calculations.

## Method

The authors compute the reflection phase shift for bound states scattering from a hard wall using an effective range expansion analogous to S-wave scattering in three dimensions, defining the reflection radius R(p) = -delta(p)/p. For alpha particles, they perform leading-order lattice calculations in chiral effective field theory with cubic boxes of varying lengths and vanishing Dirichlet boundaries. For two-body dimers in one and three dimensions, they use a Hamiltonian lattice formalism with an attractive single-site interaction, extrapolating to the continuum limit and infinite-size limits to obtain universal results in the shallow-binding limit. They also derive an analytic adiabatic expansion for the effective potential that gives the reflection scattering length for shallow two-body states.

## Context

This work extends the framework of Luscher (1985, 1986) for finite-volume analysis to the case of hard-wall boundaries, providing a theoretical tool for *ab initio* nuclear structure calculations analogous to how Luscher's periodic boundary analysis enables lattice QCD calculations. The results connect to recent lattice calculations of carbon-12 by Epelbaum (2011) that suggest alpha clusters are compressed when bound in nuclei, and the authors' alpha particle compression data appears consistent with that observation. The universal results for dimer-wall scattering can be applied to cold atomic dimers on optical lattices, Wannier excitons in semiconductor quantum dots and wells, and halo nuclei with a heavy core and one satellite nucleon.
