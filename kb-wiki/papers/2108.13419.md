# Lightweight self-conjugate nucleus $^{80}$Zr

> Protons and neutrons in the atomic nucleus move in shells analogous to the electronic shell structures of atoms. Nuclear shell structure varies across the nuclear landscape due to changes of the nuclear mean field with the number of neutrons $N$ and protons $Z$. These variations can be probed with mass differences. The $N=Z=40$ self-conjugate nucleus $^{80}$Zr is of particular interest as its proton and neutron shell structures are expected to be very similar, and its ground state is highly deformed. In this work, we provide evidence for the existence of a deformed double shell closure in $^{80}$Zr through high precision Penning trap mass measurements of $^{80-83}$Zr. Our new mass values show that $^{80}$Zr is significantly lighter, and thus more bound than previously determined. This can be attributed to the deformed shell closure at $N=Z=40$ and the large Wigner energy. Our statistical Bayesian model mixing analysis employing several global nuclear mass models demonstrates difficulties with reproducing the observed mass anomaly using current theory.

## Key claim

The paper provides evidence for a deformed double shell closure in the self-conjugate nucleus $^{80}$Zr through high-precision Penning trap mass measurements, but the authors note that the observed mass anomaly cannot be fully reproduced by current global nuclear mass models, and the enhancement in binding energy is attributed to both the deformed shell closure and an increase in the Wigner energy.

## Method

High-precision Penning trap mass spectrometry was performed on the neutron-deficient zirconium isotopes $^{80-83}$Zr at the LEBIT facility at Michigan State University. The isotopes were produced via projectile fragmentation of a 140 MeV/u $^{92}$Mo beam on a Be target, separated by the A1900 Fragment Separator, and stopped in the Advanced Cryogenic Gas Stopper. Cyclotron frequencies were measured using the time-of-flight ion cyclotron resonance technique in a 9.4 T Penning trap, with reference ions ($^{41}$K$^+$, $^{85,87}$Rb$^+$) used for magnetic field calibration.

## Key numbers

- $^{80}$Zr mass excess: -55,128 (80) keV/c$^2$, which is 370 keV/c$^2$ more bound than the extrapolated AME20 value and 950 keV/c$^2$ more bound than the Lanzhou extrapolated value
- $^{81}$Zr mass excess: -57,556 (10) keV/c$^2$, improving precision by one order of magnitude over AME20
- $^{82}$Zr mass excess: -63,618.6 (2.5) keV/c$^2$
- $^{83}$Zr mass excess: -65,916.33 (65) keV/c$^2$
- The $\delta V_{pn}$ value for $^{82}$Zr (reliant on $^{80}$Zr mass) is 185 keV lower than the macroscopic mass formula trend
- The empirical single-particle energy gap $\Delta e$ at $N=40$ for Zr isotopes, after Wigner energy corrections, is a factor of 2-3 larger than $\Delta e$ for $42 \le N \le 48$
- $^{80}$Zr has a very large prolate quadrupole deformation $\beta_2 \approx 0.4$

## Context

This work extends predictions from over 30 years ago by Hamilton (1984) and Nazarewicz (1985) that a deformed shell gap exists at $N=Z=40$, which had lacked precise experimental data for quantitative assessment. The results show that $^{80}$Zr exhibits binding-energy anomalies similar to those found in the doubly-magic self-conjugate nuclei $^{56}$Ni and $^{100}$Sn. The Bayesian model averaging analysis using eleven global mass models, following the procedure of Neufcourt (2020), demonstrates that current theory cannot fully account for the observed mass anomaly, particularly due to underestimated Wigner energy in models such as FRDM2012 (Moller, 2016) and HFB-24 (Goriely, 2013). This work enables further refinement of deformed shell closures through next-generation radioactive ion beam facilities and mass measurement techniques.
