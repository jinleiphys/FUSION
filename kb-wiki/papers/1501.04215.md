# Kenneth Wilson and lattice QCD

> We discuss the physics and computation of lattice QCD, a space-time lattice formulation of quantum chromodynamics, and Kenneth Wilson's seminal role in its development. We start with the fundamental issue of confinement of quarks in the theory of the strong interactions, and discuss how lattice QCD provides a framework for understanding this phenomenon. A conceptual issue with lattice QCD is a conflict of space-time lattice with chiral symmetry of quarks. We discuss how this problem is resolved. Since lattice QCD is a non-linear quantum dynamical system with infinite degrees of freedom, quantities which are analytically calculable are limited. On the other hand, it provides an ideal case of massively parallel numerical computations. We review the long and distinguished history of parallel-architecture supercomputers designed and built for lattice QCD. We discuss algorithmic developments, in particular the difficulties posed by the fermionic nature of quarks, and their resolution. The triad of efforts toward better understanding of physics, better algorithms, and more powerful supercomputers have produced major breakthroughs in our understanding of the strong interactions. We review the salient results of this effort in understanding the hadron spectrum, the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix elements and CP violation, and quark-gluon plasma at high temperatures. We conclude with a brief summary and a future perspective.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD, as originally formulated by Kenneth Wilson (1974), provides a non-perturbative framework for understanding quark confinement through an expansion in inverse powers of the bare gauge coupling at strong coupling, but the framework's practical utility depends on the existence of a second-order phase transition at some coupling value that allows the lattice spacing to be taken to zero while fixing a finite physical correlation length.

## Method

The paper reviews the conceptual development of lattice QCD from Wilson's original gauge-invariant lattice formulation through four decades of progress, covering the resolution of the conflict between lattice discretization and chiral symmetry via Wilson fermions, staggered fermions, and the Ginsparg-Wilson relation (1982) leading to domain-wall and overlap fermions. It surveys the history of dedicated parallel supercomputers built for lattice QCD simulations, algorithmic developments including the Hybrid Monte Carlo method (Duane, Kennedy, Pendleton, Roweth, 1987) and its refinements, and the triad of physics understanding, algorithm improvement, and supercomputer power that enabled large-scale numerical simulations.

## Key numbers

- The CRAY-1 supercomputer appeared in 1976 and revolutionized scientific computing for lattice QCD
- Wilson's original confinement paper was received by Physical Review D on 12 June 1974 and published in the 15 October issue of that journal (Phys. Rev. D10, 2445)
- The first hadron mass calculations on the lattice were performed in 1981 by Weingarten (Phys. Lett. B109, 57) and by Hamber and Parisi (Phys. Rev. Lett. 47, 1792)
- The Hybrid Monte Carlo algorithm was introduced in 1987 (Phys. Lett. B195, 216)
- The BMW Collaboration (2008) published ab-initio determination of light hadron masses in Science 322, 1224
- The Wuppertal-Budapest Collaboration (2010) reported the QCD transition temperature with physical masses in the continuum limit (JHEP 1009, 073)
- The HotQCD Collaboration (2014) published the equation of state in 2+1-flavor QCD (arXiv:1407.6387)
- The RBC and UKQCD Collaborations (2013) performed domain wall QCD with near-physical pions (Phys. Rev. D87, 094514)
- The PACS-CS Collaboration (2009) achieved 2+1 flavor lattice QCD toward the physical point (Phys. Rev. D79, 034503)

## Context

This review extends the historical narrative of lattice QCD from Wilson's original 1974 paper through the developments by Creutz, Jacobs, and Rebbi (1979) who demonstrated numerical calculation of observables, and the foundational work on chiral symmetry by Karsten and Smit (1981) and Nielsen and Ninomiya (1981). It enables future work by documenting the resolution of the fermion doubling problem, the development of exact chiral symmetry on the lattice via the Ginsparg-Wilson relation (1982) as realized by Luscher (1998), Kaplan (1992), and Neuberger (1998), and the ongoing efforts to compute CKM matrix elements, CP violation parameters, and quark-gluon plasma properties at finite temperature.
