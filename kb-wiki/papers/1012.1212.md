# Topological defect formation from 2PI effective action techniques

> We propose a quantum approach to nonequilibrium dynamics which combines the successful aspects of classical-statistical simulations on a lattice with the ability to take into account quantum corrections. It is based on the 2PI effective action for inhomogeneous fields and a volume average. This procedure does not involve any double counting which could appear in sampling prescriptions for inhomogeneous quantum evolutions. As an example, we study nonequilibrium dynamics of defect formation in 1+1 dimensional relativistic scalar field theory and compare to insufficient descriptions based on homogeneous quantum fields. The latter are analyzed in detail by coupling the field to an external source, such that the emerging influence of defects can be studied by lowering the source to zero.

## Key claim

The authors propose a quantum approach to nonequilibrium dynamics that combines classical-statistical lattice simulations with quantum corrections using the 2PI effective action for inhomogeneous fields and a volume average, claiming this procedure avoids double counting that can appear in sampling prescriptions for inhomogeneous quantum evolutions.

## Method

The method is based on the two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action for inhomogeneous fields in 1+1 dimensional relativistic scalar field theory, combined with a volume average over spatial coordinates. The authors study defect formation by comparing the inhomogeneous 2PI approach to homogeneous quantum field descriptions, where the latter are analyzed by coupling the field to an external source J and lowering the source to zero to examine the influence of defects.

## Context

This work extends the 2PI effective action approach to nonequilibrium quantum field theory by incorporating inhomogeneous field configurations, which previous homogeneous treatments could not properly describe defect formation. The method enables quantitative study of topological defect formation (kinks for N=1, textures for N=2) in relativistic scalar field theories, and provides a framework for comparing quantum and classical-statistical descriptions of nonequilibrium dynamics. The approach opens the possibility of studying defect formation in higher-dimensional theories where quantum corrections are important.
