# Variational approaches to constructing the many-body nuclear ground state for quantum computing

> We explore the preparation of specific nuclear states on gate-based quantum hardware using variational algorithms. Large scale classical diagonalization of the nuclear shell model have reached sizes of $10^9 - 10^{10}$ basis states, but are still severely limited by computational resources. Quantum computing can, in principle, solve such systems exactly with exponentially fewer resources than classical computing. Exact solutions for large systems require many qubits and large gate depth, but variational approaches can effectively limit the required gate depth. We use the unitary coupled cluster approach to construct approximations of the ground-state vectors, later to be used in dynamics calculations. The testing ground is the phenomenological shell model space, which allows us to mimic the complexity of the inter-nucleon interactions. We find that often one needs to minimize over a large number of parameters, using a large number of entanglements that makes challenging the application on existing hardware. Prospects for rapid improvements with more capable hardware are, however, very encouraging.

## Key claim

Variational quantum algorithms, specifically the unitary coupled cluster (UCC) ansatz, can produce accurate approximations of nuclear ground-state energies in small shell-model spaces, but the required number of variational parameters and entangling gates makes implementation on current noisy quantum hardware challenging; the authors note that prospects for rapid improvement depend on more capable hardware.

## Method

The authors used the unitary coupled cluster singles and doubles (UCCSD) ansatz to construct trial ground-state wavefunctions starting from a Hartree-Fock (HF) mean-field solution, employing the Jordan-Wigner mapping to encode fermionic states onto qubits. They tested this approach on phenomenological shell-model spaces (the Cohen-Kurath interaction in the p shell and the Wildenthal interaction in the sd shell) for several light nuclei, performing classical simulations and, for a small two-neutron system in the p shell, runs on the IBM Bogota quantum device with readout error mitigation and zero-noise extrapolation.

## Key numbers

* For two neutrons in the p shell (⁶He), the UCC ansatz with 2 parameters gave a ground-state energy of -3.85 MeV, compared to the exact value of -3.91 MeV and the Hartree-Fock value of -0.90 MeV.
* For ⁸Be in the p shell, including only two-particle, two-hole (2p-2h) configurations (12 parameters) gave -26.79 MeV; adding proton-neutron 2p-2h excitations (76 parameters) gave -29.37 MeV; and including four-particle, four-hole configurations (112 parameters) gave -30.67 MeV, versus the exact -31.12 MeV.
* For ²⁰O in the sd shell, the UCC ansatz with 10 parameters gave -23.18 MeV (exact -23.93 MeV, HF -21.29 MeV); for ²²O with 35 parameters, the result was -35.14 MeV (exact -35.27 MeV, HF -32.98 MeV).
* On the IBM Bogota device, a one-parameter variational circuit for two neutrons in the p shell yielded a mitigated energy with a chi-squared value of 331 (bare value 2,650) and a normalized sum of squared deviations of 4.3.
* On a noise-model simulator of the Bogota device, a two-parameter minimization gave a ground-state energy of -3.73 MeV (exact -3.85 MeV) after readout error mitigation and zero-noise extrapolation.

## Context

This work extends the application of variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) methods, previously developed in quantum chemistry (Cao 2018, Kivlichan 2017), to nuclear many-body problems in the shell-model framework. It builds on earlier quantum computing studies for nuclear systems (Dumitrescu 2018) and demonstrates that while UCCSD can achieve good accuracy in small model spaces, the rapid growth in the number of parameters and circuit depth with system size (scaling as O(N²(N_p+N_n)²) for the number of parameters) makes near-term hardware implementation difficult. The results enable future work on larger quantum devices and algorithmic improvements such as those proposed by Tang (2021) to reduce gate depth.
