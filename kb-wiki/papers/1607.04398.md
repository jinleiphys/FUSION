# Carbon-oxygen-neon mass nuclei in superstrong magnetic fields

> The properties of $\isotope[12]{C}$, $\isotope[16]{O}$, and $\isotope[20]{Ne}$ nuclei in strong magnetic fields $B\simeq 10^{17}\,$G are studied in the context of strongly magnetized neutron stars and white dwarfs. The SKY3D code is extended to incorporate the interaction of nucleons with the magnetic field and is utilized to solve the time-independent Hartree-Fock equations with a Skyrme interaction on a Cartesian three-dimensional grid. The numerical solutions demonstrate a number of phenomena, which include a splitting of the energy levels of spin-up and -down nucleons, spontaneous rearrangment of energy levels in $\isotope[16]{O}$ at a critical field, which leads to jump-like increases of magnetization and proton current in this nucleus, and evolution of the intrinsically deformed $\isotope[20]{Ne}$ nucleus towards a more spherical shape under increasing field strength. Many of the numerical features can be understood within a simple analytical model based on the occupation by the nucleons of the lowest states of the harmonic oscillator in a magnetic field.

# Carbon-oxygen-neon mass nuclei in superstrong magnetic fields

## Key claim

The properties of 12C, 16O, and 20Ne nuclei are studied at magnetic fields B ~ 10^17 G using a Skyrme Hartree-Fock code extended to include nucleon-magnetic-field interactions; the abstract's claim of "demonstrating a number of phenomena" is hedged by the finding that many numerical features can be understood within a simple analytical model based on harmonic oscillator states in a magnetic field, and that the analytical model is less reliable for the deformed 20Ne nucleus.

## Method

The SKY3D code was extended to incorporate the interaction of nucleons with a magnetic field via the Hamiltonian term h^(B)_q = -(l δ_{q,p} + g_q σ/2) · B̃_q, where neutrons couple only through spin and protons through both orbital angular momentum and spin. Time-independent Hartree-Fock equations with the SV-bas Skyrme force were solved on a 32^3 Cartesian grid with 1.0 fm spacing, iterating up to 4000 steps for convergence. The magnetic field was applied along the z-axis and incremented in steps of about 0.25 sqrt(MeV fm^{-3}) from zero up to approximately 5 × 10^17 G.

## Key numbers

- Critical field for level rearrangement in 16O: B_c[16O] = 4.0 × 10^17 G, above which the d_{5/2}, M=+5/2 state becomes occupied instead of the p_{1/2}, M=-1/2 state
- For 16O below B_c: rms radius = 2.69 fm (spherical); above B_c: rms radius = 2.72 fm with deformation β = 0.1 and γ = 60° (oblate)
- For 12C at zero field: rms radius = 2.47 fm; at B = 4.1 × 10^17 G: rms radius = 2.51 fm with β = 0.071 (oblate, γ = 60°)
- For 20Ne: rms radius decreases from 2.93 fm at B=0 to 2.87 fm at maximum field; β decreases from 0.32 to 0.15; γ increases from 0° to 11°
- Proton current in 16O is a factor of 4 larger than neutron current at B = 3.9 × 10^17 G
- For 20Ne, increasing the field from 4.0 × 10^13 G to 4.9 × 10^17 G (four orders of magnitude) increases the maximal current by about the same factor
- Energy splitting between spin-up and spin-down states becomes of order MeV for fields B ~ 10^17 G
- Discrepancy between numerical and analytical energy levels for mixed states is below 10% for 16O and at most 10% for 12C

## Context

This work extends prior studies of nuclei in strong magnetic fields that used Thomas-Fermi models (Fushiki 1989), liquid drop models (Lai 1991), the Nilsson model (Kondratyev 2000, 2001), relativistic density functional theory (Pena Arteaga 2011, Beloin 2015), and non-relativistic Skyrme functional theory (Chamel 2012), which concentrated on heavy nuclei beyond 56Fe in neutron star crusts. The focus on light carbon-oxygen-neon nuclei is motivated by their presence in magnetar atmospheres, accreting neutron star crusts, and supermassive strongly magnetized white dwarfs; the study also provides a benchmark for future magnetic-field extensions of the SKY3D code. The results enable predictions for how magnetic fields affect transport processes, neutrino and photon emission and absorption, and reaction rates in strongly magnetized compact objects, and can be extended to neutron-rich nuclei and non-spherical pasta phases.
