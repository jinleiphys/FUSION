# Neutron Star Crust and Molecular Dynamics Simulation

> In this book chapter we review plasma crystals in the laboratory, in the interior of white dwarf stars, and in the crust of neutron stars. We describe a molecular dynamics formalism and show results for many neutron star crust properties including phase separation upon freezing, diffusion, breaking strain, shear viscosity and dynamics response of nuclear pasta. We end with a summary and discuss open questions and challenges for the future.

## Key claim

This book chapter reviews molecular dynamics simulations of plasma crystals in laboratory plasmas, white dwarf interiors, and neutron star crusts, arguing that the neutron star crust is likely crystalline rather than amorphous based on MD results for diffusion, breaking strain, and shear viscosity. The abstract's claim of showing "results for many neutron star crust properties" is qualified by the chapter's status as a review that presents MD simulation results alongside open questions and challenges for future work.

## Method

The authors model neutron star crust as a classical Coulomb plasma with a screened Yukawa potential between ions, using a velocity Verlet integration scheme with a cutoff of about 8 times the screening length. They perform two-phase molecular dynamics simulations combining solid and liquid regions in a rectangular volume with periodic boundary conditions, evolving the system while adjusting temperature to maintain approximately half solid and half liquid. The simulations use a parallel Fortran 95 code with MPI and OpenMP, running on up to 768 cores of a Cray XT5 supercomputer, with simulation times of order 2×10^6/ω_p requiring one to two weeks of computer time.

## Key numbers

- The one-component plasma freezes near Γ = 175, while mixtures of ions are expected to freeze at a somewhat higher Γ
- The breaking stress of neutron star crust is 10 billion times larger than that for steel
- The rp process ash composition at a density of 2.16×10^11 g/cm^3 has 40% of ions with atomic number Z = 34, 10% with Z = 33, and the remaining half with Z ranging from 8 to 32
- The melting temperature of an approximately 50% carbon, 50% oxygen mixture is only slightly higher than the melting temperature of pure carbon
- The screening length cutoff of about 8λ gives good physical results, and a cutoff significantly smaller than 8λ leads to errors in the shear modulus
- The longest simulation times were of order 2×10^6/ω_p, involving a week or two of computer time with 768 cores

## Context

This review extends prior work on plasma crystallization in laboratory dusty plasmas (Fortov et al., 1994 observations) and white dwarf interiors (Winget et al., observations of crystallization latent heat in NGC 6397), and applies molecular dynamics techniques to neutron star crust properties. The results enable future gravitational wave searches with Advanced LIGO and Advanced VIRGO by quantifying the maximum mountain size on rotating neutron stars, and inform understanding of r-mode stability through shear viscosity calculations for nuclear pasta. The chapter specifically builds on prior MD simulations of phase separation (Horowitz et al., 2007), crust breaking strain (Horowitz and Kadau, 2009), and diffusion in Coulomb solids (Hughto et al., 2010).
