# Radius and equation of state constraints from massive neutron stars and GW190814

> Motivated by the unknown nature of the $2.50-2.67\,M_\odot$ compact object in the binary merger event GW190814, we study the maximum neutron star mass based on constraints from low-energy nuclear physics, neutron star tidal deformabilities from GW170817, and simultaneous mass-radius measurements of PSR J0030+045 from NICER. Our prior distribution is based on a combination of nuclear modeling valid in the vicinity of normal nuclear densities together with the assumption of a maximally stiff equation of state at high densities, a choice that enables us to probe the connection between observed heavy neutron stars and the transition density at which conventional nuclear physics models must break down. We demonstrate that a modification of the highly uncertain supra-saturation density equation of state beyond 2.64 times normal nuclear density is required in order for chiral effective field theory models to be consistent with current neutron star observations and the existence of $2.6\,M_\odot$ neutron stars. We also show that the existence of very massive neutron stars strongly impacts the radii of $\sim 2.0\,M_\odot$ neutron stars (but not necessarily the radii of $1.4\,M_\odot$ neutron stars), which further motivates future NICER radius measurements of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0740+6620.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a modification of the supra-saturation density equation of state above a transition density of 2.64 times normal nuclear density is required for chiral effective field theory models to be consistent with the existence of slowly rotating 2.6 solar mass neutron stars, but this condition applies only under the assumption that the GW190814 secondary object is a neutron star rather than a black hole.

## Method

The authors construct Bayesian posterior probability distributions for the neutron star equation of state using a prior that combines nuclear modeling valid near normal nuclear densities with a maximally stiff equation of state at high densities, where the transition density is varied uniformly between 2 and 4 times nuclear saturation density. Likelihood functions incorporate tidal deformability measurements from GW170817, simultaneous mass-radius measurements of PSR J0030+045 from NICER using bivariate kernel density estimators, and the mass distribution of the GW190814 secondary object. The posterior distributions are computed under two competing assumptions: that the secondary is a neutron star or a black hole.

## Key numbers

- The transition density required to support a 2.6 solar mass neutron star is 2.64 times nuclear saturation density (n_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3})
- To support 2.5, 2.6, and 2.7 solar mass neutron stars, the transition density must be less than 2.95, 2.64, and 2.39 times n_0 respectively
- At the largest transition density of 4 n_0, the maximum neutron star mass is 2.32 solar masses
- Under the black hole assumption for GW190814, the 95% credibility interval for the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 10.9 km to 12.9 km
- Under the neutron star assumption for GW190814, the 95% credibility interval for the radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 11.5 km to 13.0 km
- Under the black hole assumption, the 95% credibility interval for the radius of a 2.0 solar mass neutron star is 10.5 km to 12.5 km
- Under the neutron star assumption, the 95% credibility interval for the radius of a 2.0 solar mass neutron star is 11.8 km to 13.2 km
- The 95% credibility interval for the tidal deformability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star is 190 to 550 under the black hole assumption and 310 to 590 under the neutron star assumption

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Bayesian equation of state modeling (Lim 2019) by incorporating a transition to a maximally stiff high-density equation of state to probe the connection between heavy neutron stars and the breakdown density of conventional nuclear physics. The study is motivated by the GW190814 event reported by Abbott (2020), which discovered a 2.50-2.67 solar mass compact object in the mass gap between neutron stars and black holes. The results enable future NICER radius measurements of PSR J1614-2230 and PSR J0740+6620 to provide strong constraints on the nuclear equation of state at supra-saturation density, and they demonstrate that the existence of very massive neutron stars strongly impacts the radii of approximately 2.0 solar mass neutron stars but not necessarily the radii of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars.
