# Universal behavior of compact star based upon gravitational binding energy

> Similar to the compactness parameter ($β=M/R$), the gravitational binding energy (GBE) is also a characteristic parameter which can reflect the internal structure of a neutron star and thus can be used to expressing the universal relations. Scaling by the stellar mass, this investigation demonstrates a perfect universal relation between the GBE and the moment of inertia, where both of the normal neutron stars and the quark stars satisfy the same universal relation. Moreover, a fine empirical relation between the GBE and the tidal deformability is proposed, where the difference of the relations can be used to distinguish wether a pulsar is a normal neutron star or a quark star if the stellar mass and the tidal deformability can be observed or estimated rather accurately. These universal relations provide a potential way to estimate the GBE if the stellar mass and the moment of inertia/the tidal deformability are precisely measured.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the gravitational binding energy (GBE), rather than the total binding energy, yields a perfect universal relation with the moment of inertia that holds for both normal neutron stars and quark stars, and a separate empirical relation with tidal deformability that can distinguish between the two types of compact stars, provided the stellar mass and tidal deformability can be observed or estimated rather accurately.

## Method

The authors employed a parametric equation of state (EOS) model for neutron-rich nucleonic matter, generating about 10,000 screened EOSs that satisfy a maximum mass greater than 2.01 solar masses and the causal constraint. They also used 11 microscopic EOSs for normal or hybrid neutron stars and 3 EOSs for quark stars from the confined-density-dependent-mass model. For each EOS, they solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for non-rotating stars to compute the gravitational binding energy, moment of inertia, and tidal deformability, then fit universal relations between these quantities.

## Key numbers

* For canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron stars, the gravitational binding energy is about 1.3-1.6 times the total binding energy; for the WWF1 EOS, the gravitational binding energy per nucleon reaches -155.63 MeV.
* For maximum-mass stars, the average gravitational binding energy per nucleon can be up to -325 MeV (WWF2 EOS: -325.06 MeV).
* The universal relation between dimensionless moment of inertia and gravitational binding energy is: $\bar{I} = 0.1806 (|E_g|/M)^{-2} + 9.314$.
* For normal neutron stars, the universal relation between tidal deformability and gravitational binding energy is: $\Lambda = 3.646 \times 10^{-2} (|E_g|/M)^{-5} - 4.233$.
* For quark stars, the universal relation is: $\Lambda = 3.245 \times 10^{-3} (|E_g|/M)^{-5} + 108$.
* The gravitational-wave detection GW170817 constrains the tidal deformability to $\Lambda_{1.4} = 190^{+390}_{-120}$ at 90% confidence level.

## Context

This work extends the search for EOS-insensitive universal relations beyond the well-known I-Love-Q relations of Yagi (2013) and the binding energy-compactness relations of Lattimer (2001) and Breu and Rezzolla (2016). By focusing on gravitational binding energy rather than total binding energy, the authors achieve tighter universality than previous binding energy relations. The different universal relations for normal neutron stars and quark stars in the tidal deformability case provide a potential observational discriminant between these two classes of compact objects, which could be applied to future gravitational wave detections and supernova neutrino observations.
