# Vortex Creep Heating in Neutron Stars

> Recent observations of old warm neutron stars suggest the presence of a heating source in these stars, requiring a paradigm beyond the standard neutron-star cooling theory. In this work, we study the scenario where this heating is caused by the friction associated with the creep motion of neutron superfluid vortex lines in the crust. As it turns out, the heating luminosity in this scenario is proportional to the time derivative of the angular velocity of the pulsar rotation, and the proportional constant $J$ has an approximately universal value for all neutron stars. This $J$ parameter can be determined from the temperature observation of old neutron stars because the heating luminosity is balanced with the photon emission at late times. We study the latest data of neutron star temperature observation and find that these data indeed give similar values of $J$, in favor of the assumption that the frictional motion of vortex lines heats these neutron stars. These values turn out to be consistent with the theoretical calculations of the vortex-nuclear interaction.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the heating luminosity from vortex creep motion in neutron stars is proportional to the time derivative of the pulsar's angular velocity, with a proportionality constant \(J\) that is approximately universal across all neutron stars; this claim is conditional on the system reaching a steady state where the crust and superfluid components decelerate at the same rate, which the authors argue occurs for old neutron stars with ages greater than about \(10^5\) years.

## Method

The authors model the neutron star as a two-component system: a crust component (including the rigid crust, nuclei, and charged particles) and a superfluid component (the \(^1S_0\) neutron superfluid in the inner crust). They derive equations of motion for the angular velocities of both components, coupled through the radial creep motion of vortex lines driven by the Magnus force against the pinning force from nuclei. The heating luminosity is computed from the dissipation of rotational energy as vortices move outward, and in the steady-state phase it simplifies to \(L_{\rm H} = J |\dot{\Omega}_\infty|\), where \(J\) is evaluated by integrating the steady-state relative angular velocity over the pinning region using theoretical calculations of the pinning force from the literature.

## Key numbers

* The equilibrium surface temperature predicted by vortex creep heating is \(T_{\rm s}^{\rm eq} \simeq 1.0 \times 10^5~\mathrm{K} \left( \frac{J}{10^{43}~\mathrm{erg~s}} \right)^{1/4} \left( \frac{|\dot{\Omega}_\infty|}{10^{-14}~\mathrm{s}^{-2}} \right)^{1/4} \left( \frac{R_{\rm NS}}{11.43~\mathrm{km}} \right)^{-1/2}\).
* The zero-point frequency for vortex motion is \(\omega_0 \simeq 1.2 \times 10^{20}~\mathrm{s}^{-1} \left( \frac{R_{\rm WS}}{50~\mathrm{fm}} \right)^{-2} \left( \frac{\Lambda}{2} \right)\), where \(R_{\rm WS}\) is the Wigner-Seitz radius and \(\Lambda\) characterizes vortex tension.
* The quantum tunneling temperature threshold is \(T_{\rm q} \simeq 3.8 \times 10^8~\mathrm{K} \left( \frac{\omega_0}{10^{20}~\mathrm{s}^{-1}} \right)\).
* The typical creep distance traveled by a vortex line over the neutron star lifetime is approximately \(160~\mathrm{km} \times \left( \frac{\omega_0}{10^{20}~\mathrm{s}^{-1}} \right) \left( \frac{R_{\rm WS}}{50~\mathrm{fm}} \right) \left( \frac{t_{\rm age}}{10^5~\mathrm{yr}} \right)\), assuming \(|E_{\rm pin}| = 1\) MeV, which is much larger than the crust thickness of about 1 km.
* The relaxation timescale for thermal equilibrium is \(\tau_{\rm eq} \sim 3 \times 10^4~\mathrm{yrs} \left( \frac{C}{10^{35}~\mathrm{erg/K}} \right) \left( \frac{R_{\rm NS}}{11.43~\mathrm{km}} \right)^{-2} \left( \frac{T_{\rm s}}{10^5~\mathrm{K}} \right)^{-4} \left( \frac{T^\infty}{10^6~\mathrm{K}} \right)\).

## Context

This work extends the vortex creep heating mechanism originally developed by Alpar et al. (1984) and later studied by Schaab et al. (1999) and Gonzalez et al. (2010), applying it to recent observations of old warm neutron stars that are hotter than predicted by standard cooling theory (Yakovlev et al., 1999; Page et al., 2004). The paper enables a direct observational test of the vortex creep scenario by comparing the inferred \(J\) parameter from temperature and spin-down data of old neutron stars with theoretical calculations of the nuclear pinning force, using approaches such as the Argonne interaction (Wiringa, 1994) and Skyrme-type interactions (SLy4, SkM*).
