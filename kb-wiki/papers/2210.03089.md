# Quantum computation of dynamical quantum phase transitions and entanglement tomography in a lattice gauge theory

> Strongly-coupled gauge theories far from equilibrium may exhibit unique features that could illuminate the physics of the early universe and of hadron and ion colliders. Studying real-time phenomena has proven challenging with classical-simulation methods, but is a natural application of quantum simulation. To demonstrate this prospect, we quantum compute non-equal time correlation functions and perform entanglement tomography of non-equilibrium states of a simple lattice gauge theory, the Schwinger model, using a trapped-ion quantum computer by IonQ Inc. As an ideal target for near-term devices, a recently-predicted [Zache et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 122, 050403 (2019)] dynamical quantum phase transition in this model is studied by preparing, quenching, and tracking the subsequent non-equilibrium dynamics in three ways: i) overlap echos signaling dynamical transitions, ii) non-equal time correlation functions with an underlying topological nature, and iii) the entanglement structure of non-equilibrium states, including entanglement Hamiltonians. These results constitute the first observation of a dynamical quantum phase transition in a lattice gauge theory on a quantum computer, and are a first step toward investigating topological phenomena in nuclear and high-energy physics using quantum technologies.

## Key claim

The paper reports the first observation of a dynamical quantum phase transition in a lattice gauge theory on a quantum computer, specifically in the Schwinger model, using a trapped-ion device by IonQ Inc. The claim is hedged by the fact that the demonstration is performed in the non-interacting limit ($e=0$) and at finite coupling ($e/|m|=1$) with only a single Trotter step, and that the topological order parameter $\nu(t)$ is extracted from non-equal time correlation functions rather than directly measured.

## Method

The authors implement real-time evolution of the Schwinger model on a 1D spatial lattice with periodic boundary conditions, using a digital quantum computing scheme on IonQ's 11-qubit trapped-ion quantum computer. They prepare the ground state of the non-interacting theory with mass $+m$, then perform a quantum quench to mass $-m$ (corresponding to a $\Delta\theta=\pi$ quench of the topological angle) and track the subsequent non-equilibrium dynamics via three observables: Loschmidt echos, non-equal time correlation functions with topological content, and entanglement structure. The algorithm uses a composite Bogoliubov-staggered Fourier transformation to switch between momentum-space (free part) and position-space (interacting part) bases, with a Trotter scheme for finite coupling and a Ramsey interferometry scheme for measuring non-equal time observables.

## Key numbers

- For $N=4$ sites, $e=0$, $|m|a=0.9$: the Loschmidt echo rate function $\Gamma(t)$ shows a peak (non-analyticity) at $t|m| \approx 1.1$, marking the dynamical quantum phase transition, reproduced by the quantum computer with 1000 shots per data point.
- For $N=8$ sites, $e=0$, $|m|a=0.8$: up to 16,000 shots are required in the peak region at $t|m| \approx 1.1$ to resolve the DQPT feature on the quantum hardware.
- For $N=4$ sites, $e/|m|=1$, $|m|a=0.9$: the Loschmidt echo and rate function are computed with one Trotter step, with approximately 70% of events containing an identifiable symmetry-violating error.
- The symmetry-based error mitigation scheme identifies symmetry-violating device errors in about 25% of events for the $N=4$, $e=0$ case.

## Context

This work extends the prediction of dynamical quantum phase transitions in gauge theories by Zache et al. (2019) to a quantum computing implementation, and builds on prior quantum simulations of the Schwinger model on trapped-ion platforms by Martinez et al. (2016) and others. The results demonstrate that DQPTs are accessible on near-term quantum devices because they persist in small systems (4-8 sites) and on short time scales, and they enable future investigations of topological phenomena in nuclear and high-energy physics, including potential connections to the strong CP problem and the dynamics of the early universe.
