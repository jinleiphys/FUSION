# Mixing of one-particle-one-hole projected states with the variation after projection wave functions

> In this paper, we study the mixing of one-particle-one-hole projected states with the variation after projection (VAP) wave functions in attempt to improve the approximation of this method. It turns out that when minimizing only the lowest (yrast) energy with given spin and parity, the one-particle-one-hole projected states can not be mixed into the converged VAP wave function, which is very similar to the situation of the Hartree-Fock method. However, if one minimizes the sum of several lowest energies with the same spin and parity, the one-particle-one-hole mixing can make some improvements to the VAP wave functions. We expect such one-particle-one-hole mixing may be useful in the calculations of the low-lying excited states in heavy nuclei with a large model space.

# Mixing of one-particle-one-hole projected states with the variation after projection wave functions

## Key claim
When minimizing only the lowest (yrast) energy for a given spin and parity, one-particle-one-hole projected states cannot be mixed into the converged variation after projection (VAP) wave function; however, if one minimizes the sum of several lowest energies with the same spin and parity, the one-particle-one-hole mixing can improve the VAP wave functions, particularly for low-lying excited states in heavy nuclei with large model spaces.

## Method
The authors start from a set of Hartree-Fock Slater determinants (SDs) and perform angular momentum and parity projection on each. The VAP wave function is constructed as a linear combination of projected SDs, with coefficients determined by solving the Hill-Wheeler equation. The variational parameters are the complex coefficients in the Thouless representation of each SD, and the VAP iteration minimizes either the yrast energy or the sum of the lowest m energies using a trust-region Newton algorithm. After convergence, one-particle-one-hole (1p-1h) projected states built on the converged reference SDs are added to the basis, and the expanded Hill-Wheeler equation is solved to test whether the 1p-1h mixing lowers the energies.

## Key numbers
- For the yrast 8+ state in 26Mg with the USDB interaction, the VAP iteration converges after 43 iterations when using all 2J+1 projected states; the elapsed time per iteration with the simplified single-K wave function is about 3 seconds on an Intel Xeon CPU with 20 cores, compared to about 360 seconds for the full K-mixing calculation.
- Overlaps between the converged VAP wave functions using different K values for the 8+ state in 26Mg are 0.9867 (K=0), 0.9853 (K=2), and 0.9898 (K=5) relative to the full K-mixing result; overlaps between different K choices are 0.9930, 0.9911, and 0.9875.
- For the lowest five 9+ states in 48Cr and 15/2- states in 49Cr calculated with the GXPF1A interaction, all ratios of (E'_alpha - e_alpha) to (E_alpha - e_alpha) are below 0.8, with most less than 0.6, where E'_alpha are energies after 1p-1h mixing and e_alpha are exact shell-model energies.
- For 56Ni with spins from 0+ to 12+, VAP calculations with 10 projected SDs per spin and minimization of the sum of the lowest five energies reproduce the schematic pattern of low-lying levels; 1p-1h mixing yields further improvements, especially for states far from the yrast line.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous VAP method (Gao 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021) which uses projected Slater determinants with angular momentum and parity projection, as an alternative to the more computationally expensive VAP with Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov vacua (VAMPIR 2004, Shimizu 2021). The finding that 1p-1h mixing does not improve the yrast state is analogous to the Hartree-Fock theorem that no 1p-1h states can mix into a converged HF vacuum. The approach is similar in spirit to the Projected Shell Model (Sun 1995) and the MONSTER method (VAMPIR 2004), which include projected quasiparticle states on top of a selected vacuum. The results enable more efficient calculations of low-lying excited states in heavy deformed nuclei where full shell-model calculations are impractical.
