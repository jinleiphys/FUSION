# Projected Thermal Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation in a canonical ensemble

> The Thermal Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation with reprojection to good quantum numbers is analyzed in a canonical ensemble. Simple recipes are given for the evaluation of traces, gradients and expectation values in the canonical ensemble.

# Projected Thermal Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approximation in a canonical ensemble

## Key claim

The paper presents a variant of the thermal Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (THFB) approximation with quantum-number projection that works directly in a canonical ensemble, avoiding the difficulty of evaluating the logarithm of a projected operator; however, the free energy obtained this way may not be optimal compared to the direct minimization of the free-energy functional, and the functional Om_P is complex, requiring minimization of its real part with a penalty function to suppress the imaginary part.

## Method

The author reformulates the projected THFB by approximating the exact propagator exp(-beta H) with a trial propagator HU = exp(-beta hh), where hh is a quadratic Hamiltonian that breaks symmetries including particle number conservation, then evaluates the functional Om_P = (1/2) sum(sigma^2 + mu^2 + nu^2) - (1/beta) ln Tr(HP HU) + mu_F A. The central task is evaluating ln Tr(HP HU) and its gradients in a canonical ensemble, which is accomplished by expressing the statistical density operator in terms of a matrix S, diagonalizing it to obtain eigenvalues (lambda_k, -lambda_k), constructing the matrix M from the blocks of W = exp(S), and using the eigenvalues of M to compute canonical traces via recursion relations for the coefficients xi_N. Gradients are computed using first-order perturbation theory on the eigenvalues of S and M, and one-body and two-body expectation values are obtained by expanding generating functions to second order in a perturbation parameter.

## Key numbers

- The grand-canonical trace of the statistical density operator HW is given by Tr_gc HW = product_{k=1}^{N_s} (exp(lambda_k/2) + exp(-lambda_k/2)), where lambda_k are the eigenvalues of the matrix S and N_s is the number of single-particle states.
- The canonical trace for N particles of HW is Tr_c(HW) = xi_N * [product_{k=1}^{N_s} (exp(lambda_k/2) + exp(-lambda_k/2))] / [product_{k=1}^{N_s} (1 + mu_k)], where mu_k are the eigenvalues of the matrix M and xi_N is the coefficient of z^N in the polynomial product_{k=1}^{N_s} (1 + z mu_k).
- The fractional partition functions f_k satisfy the sum rule sum_{k=1}^{N_s} mu_k f_k = N, where N is the number of particles.
- The pair occupation numbers satisfy the sum rule sum_{i<j} mu_i^{(r)} mu_j^{(r)} f^{(2)}_{ij} = N(N-1)/2.

## Context

This work extends the approach of ref. [8] (approximately 30 years prior to this paper), which formulated the functional integral expression of the partition function to obtain the HFB approximation at the mean-field level, but only discussed the unprojected partition function. The paper also builds on ref. [12] for the properties of statistical density operators and ref. [13] for the temperature independence of the M matrix eigenvalues in the pure pairing model. The method enables projected THFB calculations with realistic effective interactions such as FPD6 or GXPF1A, which had not been attempted at finite temperature, and smooths out sharp phase transitions through the use of quantum-number projected statistical density operators as noted in ref. [7].
