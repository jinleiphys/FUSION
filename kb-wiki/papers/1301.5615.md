# Symmetry energy from nuclear multifragmentation

> The ratio of symmetry energy coefficient to temperature $C_{sym}/T$ is extracted from different prescriptions using the isotopic as well as the isobaric yield distributions obtained in different projectile fragmentation reactions. It is found that the values extracted from our theoretical calculation agree with those extracted from the experimental data but they differ very much from the input value of the symmetry energy used. The best possible way to deduce the value of the symmetry energy coefficient is to use the fragment yield at the breakup stage of the reaction and it is better to use the grand canonical model for the fragmentation analysis. This is because the formulas that are used for the deduction of the symmetry energy coefficient are all derived in the framework of the grand canonical ensemble which is valid only at the break-up (equilibrium) condition. The yield of "cold" fragments either from the theoretical models or from experiments when used for extraction of the symmetry energy coefficient using these prescriptions might lead to the wrong conclusion.

## Key claim

The ratio of symmetry energy coefficient to temperature \(C_{sym}/T\) extracted from experimental fragment yields agrees with values from theoretical calculations using secondary fragments, but these extracted values differ substantially from the input symmetry energy coefficient used in the model (23.5 MeV). The authors argue that the existing formulas for extracting the symmetry energy coefficient are derived from the grand canonical ensemble and are only valid at the breakup stage of the reaction, so using "cold" secondary fragments or experimental yields leads to incorrect conclusions.

## Method

The authors use a three-stage projectile fragmentation model: (i) abrasion to determine the projectile-like fragment (PLF) volume and composition using straightline geometry, (ii) multifragmentation of each PLF using the canonical thermodynamical model (CTM) based on equilibrium statistical mechanics, and (iii) evaporation of excited fragments using Weisskopf's theory including emissions of \(n,p,d,t,^3\)He, \(^4\)He and fission. They apply four different formulas (isoscaling source formula, isoscaling fragment formula, fluctuation formula, and isobaric yield ratio method) to extract \(C_{sym}/T\) from both primary (breakup-stage) and secondary (post-evaporation) fragment yields for reactions including \(^{58}\)Ni and \(^{64}\)Ni on \(^{9}\)Be at 140 MeV/nucleon and \(^{124}\)Xe and \(^{136}\)Xe on \(^{208}\)Pb at 1 GeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers

- Input symmetry energy coefficient used in the model: \(C_{sym} = 23.5\) MeV
- For a single source at \(T=5\) MeV, the canonical ensemble yields extracted \(C_{sym}\) values varying between 25 and 50 MeV, while the grand canonical ensemble yields values between 23 and 24 MeV (matching the input 23.5 MeV)
- Temperature profile used: \(T(b) = 7.5 - 4.5[(A_s(b)/A_0)]\) where \(A_s(b)\) and \(A_0\) are mass numbers of projectile spectator and original projectile respectively
- Freeze-out volume assumed to be three times the normal nuclear volume
- Liquid-drop parameters: volume energy \(W_0 = 15.8\) MeV, surface energy \(\sigma_0 = 18.0\) MeV with critical temperature \(T_c = 18.0\) MeV, Coulomb energy with \(a_c = 0.72\) MeV and Wigner-Seitz correction factor 0.31, and \(\epsilon_0 = 16.0\) MeV for excited states contribution

## Context

This work extends the analysis of symmetry energy extraction from multifragmentation reactions, addressing discrepancies noted in prior studies where extracted \(C_{sym}\) values ranged between 15 and 30 MeV (Souliotis, 2006; Shetty, 2007; Fevre, 2005; Henzlova, 2008; Shetty, 2005). The authors show that the canonical and grand canonical ensembles give different results for finite nuclei, consistent with earlier work by Mallik (2012). The paper enables a clearer understanding that experimental yields of "cold" fragments should not be used with formulas derived from the grand canonical ensemble, and that the breakup-stage yields are required for reliable extraction. The isobaric yield ratio method results are compared with AMD model plus GEMINI calculations reported by Huang (2010).
