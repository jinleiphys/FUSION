# Potential inversion with subbarrier fusion data revisited

> We invert experimental data for heavy-ion fusion reactions at energies well below the Coulomb barrier in order to directly determine the internucleus potential between the colliding nuclei. In contrast to the previous applications of the inversion formula, we explicitly take into account the effect of channel couplings on fusion reactions, by assuming that fusion cross sections at deep subbarrier energies are governed by the lowest barrier in the barrier distribution. We apply this procedure to the $^{16}$O +$^{144}$Sm and $^{16}$O +$^{208}$Pb reactions, and find that the inverted internucleus potential are much thicker than phenomenological potentials. A relation to the steep fall-off phenomenon of fusion cross sections recently found at deep subbarrier energies is also discussed.

# Potential inversion with subbarrier fusion data revisited

## Key claim
The authors invert experimental heavy-ion fusion cross sections at deep subbarrier energies to extract the internucleus potential, explicitly accounting for channel coupling effects by assuming that only the lowest adiabatic barrier governs fusion at those energies. The resulting inverted potentials are much thicker than conventional Woods-Saxon or double folding potentials, which may partly explain the recently observed steep fall-off of fusion cross sections at deep subbarrier energies.

## Method
The authors apply a WKB-based inversion formula that relates the thickness of the potential barrier (distance between inner and outer classical turning points) to the penetrability derived from experimental fusion cross sections. They extract the penetrability from the first derivative of $E\sigma_{\rm fus}$ after normalizing it to 0.5 at the lowest barrier peak in the experimental barrier distribution, thereby isolating the contribution of the lowest eigen-barrier. The outer turning point is estimated using a Woods-Saxon potential with standard diffuseness $a=0.63$ fm, and the effective moment of inertia in the penetrability formula is taken as $R(E)=\frac{1}{2}(R_b+Z_PZ_Te^2/E)$. The procedure is validated against coupled-channels calculations using the code CCFULL for the $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Sm system.

## Key numbers
- For $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Sm: the main peak of the barrier distribution is around $E_{\rm c.m.}\sim 60$ MeV, identified as the lowest barrier height $V_b$; experimental data exist from $E_{\rm c.m.}=56.6$ MeV (about 3.6 MeV below the lowest barrier height).
- For $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb: the deviation of the inverted potential from the phenomenological Woods-Saxon potential starts at around $E=70.4$ MeV.
- The point difference formula used for derivatives employs $\Delta E_{\rm c.m.}=1.8$ MeV for the $^{16}$O+$^{144}$Sm system.
- The Woods-Saxon range parameter is $R_0=\sum_{i=P,T}(1.233A_i^{1/3}-0.98A_i^{-1/3})+0.29$ fm, with diffuseness $a=0.63$ fm.

## Context
This work revisits the inversion method of Balantekin et al. (1983), who obtained unphysical multi-valued potentials because they neglected channel coupling effects. The present approach incorporates the modern understanding of barrier distributions from coupled-channels theory, as developed by Rowley et al. (1991) and Dasgupta et al. (1998). The results are consistent with the steep fall-off phenomenon observed by Jiang et al. (2002) and Dasgupta et al. (2006), and the thick potentials found are related to the one-body adiabatic potential energy surface at the touching configuration, as discussed by Krappe, Nix, and Sierk (1979) and Ichikawa et al. (2007). The method enables systematic determination of internucleus potentials for vibrational-target systems and opens questions about dynamical effects such as coordinate-dependent reduced mass and energy dissipation.
