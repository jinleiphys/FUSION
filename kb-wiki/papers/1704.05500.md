# Many-particle correlations and Coulomb effects on temperatures from fragment momentum fluctuations

> We investigate correlations in the fragment momentum distribution due to the propagation of fragments under the influence of their mutual Coulomb field, after the breakup of an excited nuclear source.The magnitude of the effects on the nuclear temperatures obtained from such distributions is estimated with the help of a simple approach in which a charged fragment interacts with a homogeneous charged sphere. The resuslts are used to correct the temperatures obtained from the asymptotic momentum distributions of fragments produced by a Monte-Carlo simulation in which the system's configuration at breakup is provided by the canonical version of the Statistical Multifragmentation Model. In a separate calculation, the dynamics of this many-particle charged system is followed in a molecular dynamics calculation until the fragments are far away from the breakup volume. The results suggest that, although the magnitude of the corrections is similar in both models, many-particle correlations present in the second approach are non-negligible and should be taken into account in order to minimize ambiguities in such studies.

## Key claim

Coulomb repulsion during fragment propagation after nuclear breakup significantly distorts the momentum distributions used to extract nuclear temperatures, and while a simple deterministic correction model gives similar magnitude corrections to a full Monte-Carlo treatment, many-particle correlations present in the latter are non-negligible and must be accounted for to avoid ambiguities, especially at low excitation energies.

## Method

The authors used the canonical Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM) to generate fragment configurations at a given breakup temperature for a source with A0=95 and Z0=45. In one approach, fragments were propagated under mutual Coulomb forces using classical molecular dynamics until well separated; in a second, simpler approach, each fragment was accelerated from a uniformly charged sphere representing the remainder of the source. Temperatures were reconstructed from the variance of the asymptotic momentum distribution using the relation σ² = 4A²m_n²T², and the results from both models were compared to the input SMM temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Source parameters: A0=95, Z0=45 (70% of the ⁵⁸Ni+⁷⁸Kr system from momentumTemperature2010)
- Breakup volume parameter χ=2 throughout the study
- Average fragment multiplicities at input temperatures: 2±1 at T=4 MeV, 5.5±1.8 at T=5 MeV, 10.5±2.1 at T=6 MeV, 13.5±2.1 at T=7 MeV, and 15.4±2.1 at T=8 MeV
- The Monte-Carlo model predicts that reconstructed temperature first decreases at low excitation energies (below E*≈6 MeV) then increases, while the deterministic model shows monotonous increase with excitation energy
- After applying the deterministic correction to Monte-Carlo results, agreement with input temperatures is substantially improved but discrepancies remain larger for fragments with larger atomic number

## Context

This work extends the momentum-fluctuation thermometry method proposed in momentumTemperature2010, which had yielded much larger temperatures than previous methods. It builds on earlier Coulomb correction studies by CoulombCorrectionsThermometers2013 and CoulombCorrectionsThermometers2014, who applied a similar deterministic correction to their model calculations. The results show that while the simple deterministic sphere model provides useful estimates, the many-particle correlations captured by the full Monte-Carlo treatment are important and should be incorporated into data analysis to reliably extract breakup temperatures, particularly at low excitation energies where fragment multiplicities are small.
