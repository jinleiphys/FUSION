# NUCLEAR ASTROPHYSICS WITH SECONDARY (RADIOACTIVE) BEAMS

> Some problems in nuclear astrophysics are discussed with emphasize on the ones central to the field which were not solved over the last two decades, including Helium Burning in Massive stars (the 12C(a,g)16O reaction) and the 8B Solar Neutrino Flux Problem (the 7Be(p,g)8B reaction). We demonstrate that a great deal of progress was achieved by measuring the time reverse process(es): the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of 16N and the Coulomb dissociation of 8B, using radioactive beams (of 16N and 8B). In this way an amplification of the sought for cross section was achieved, allowing a measurement of the small cross section(s) of relevance for stellar (solar) process(es).

# Nuclear Astrophysics with Secondary (Radioactive) Beams

## Key claim
The paper argues that two long-standing problems in nuclear astrophysics, the helium-burning $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction and the $^8B$ solar neutrino flux problem (the $^7Be(p,\gamma)^8B$ reaction), can be addressed by measuring their time-reverse processes using radioactive beams, thereby amplifying the small cross sections of interest. However, the author acknowledges that this approach replaces an impossible experiment with a very hard one, requiring sensitivity to branching ratios on the order of $10^{-9}$ or better.

## Method
The $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ reaction was studied via the beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of $^{16}N$, where low-energy alpha particles correspond to high-energy beta particles, providing enhanced phase space and sensitivity to the bound $1^-$ state in $^{16}O$. The $^7Be(p,\gamma)^8B$ reaction was studied via Coulomb dissociation of $^8B$ on a high-Z target ($^{208}Pb$), where the large virtual photon flux amplifies the small nuclear cross section. For the $^{16}N$ experiments, radioactive beams were produced at Yale University (via $^{15}N(d,p)^{16}N$) and at the TISOL facility at TRIUMF, while the $^8B$ Coulomb dissociation was performed at the RIKEN-RIPS radioactive beam facility.

## Key numbers
- The p-wave S-factor for $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ at 300 keV from the Yale experiment: $S_{E1}(300) = 95 \pm 6$ (stat) $\pm 28$ (syst) keV-b
- The p-wave S-factor from the TRIUMF II experiment: $S_{E1}(300) = 79 \pm 21$ keV-b
- The compiled pre-experiment estimate for the p-wave S-factor at 300 keV was $S_p(300) = 60^{+60}_{-30}$ keV-b, with $S_d(300) = 40^{+40}_{-20}$ keV-b
- The cross section for $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ at the Gamow window energy of 300 keV is estimated to be $10^{-8}$ nbarn
- The beta-delayed alpha-particle emission of $^{16}N$ required sensitivity to branching ratios of $10^{-9}$ to $10^{-10}$
- The anomalous interference structure in the delayed alpha-particle spectrum from $^{16}N$ was observed around 1.1 MeV
- The extracted $S_{17}(0)$ from the $^8B$ Coulomb dissociation is smaller than 20 eV-barn
- The M1 contribution to the $^7Be(p,\gamma)^8B$ cross section is predicted to be approximately 10% of the cross section measured just below 1 MeV

## Context
This work extends the approach of using time-reverse processes to measure astrophysically important reaction rates, building on earlier R-matrix analyses by Barker (1971) and Ji, Filippone, Humblet, and Koonin (1990), as well as K-matrix analyses by Humblet, Filippone, and Koonin (1991). The predictions of the anomalous interference structure in $^{16}N$ beta-delayed alpha spectra were first published by Baye and Descouvemont (1988). The $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ results are compared to the astrophysical abundance calculations of Weaver and Woosley (1993), who derived $S_{E1}(300) = 102 \pm 30$ keV-b from solar abundance matching. The $^8B$ Coulomb dissociation results are consistent with the lower values of the cross section measured by Filippone et al. (1983) and Vaughn et al. (1970), and are contrasted with the larger values assumed in the standard solar model of Bahcall et al. The work enables improved constraints on the carbon-to-oxygen ratio at the end of helium burning, which determines whether massive stars collapse into black holes or leave neutron stars.
