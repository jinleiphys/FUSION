# The Compression-Mode Giant Resonances and Nuclear Incompressibility

> The compression-mode giant resonances, namely the isoscalar giant monopole and isoscalar giant dipole modes, are examples of collective nuclear motion. Their main interest stems from the fact that one hopes to extrapolate from their properties the incompressibility of uniform nuclear matter, which is a key parameter of the nuclear Equation of State (EoS). Our understanding of these issues has undergone two major jumps, one in the late 1970s when the Isoscalar Giant Monopole Resonance (ISGMR) was experimentally identified, and another around the turn of the millennium since when theory has been able to start giving reliable error bars to the incompressibility. However, mainly magic nuclei have been involved in the deduction of the incompressibility from the vibrations of finite nuclei. The present review deals with the developments beyond all this. Experimental techniques have been improved, and new open-shell, and deformed, nuclei have been investigated. The associated changes in our understanding of the problem of the nuclear incompressibility are discussed. New theoretical models, decay measurements, and the search for the evolution of compressional modes in exotic nuclei are also discussed.

## Key claim

The review argues that while the nuclear incompressibility \(K_\infty\) extracted from doubly-magic nuclei like \(^{208}\)Pb is around 240 ± 20 MeV, the value associated with Sn and other open-shell nuclei appears to be lower, and this issue of the "softness" or "fluffiness" of open-shell nuclei has not been resolved; furthermore, the authors assert that recent RCNP results establish that determination of nuclear incompressibility is not influenced in any appreciable manner by the underlying nuclear structure, contradicting earlier TAMU claims of dramatic variations in Zr and Mo isotopes.

## Method

The paper is a review that synthesizes experimental measurements of the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) and isoscalar giant dipole resonance (ISGDR) using inelastic \(\alpha\)-particle scattering at energies of 240 MeV and 386 MeV, as well as deuteron and \(^6\)Li probes, with multipole decomposition analysis (MDA) to extract strength distributions. Exclusive measurements of charged-particle and neutron decay of the giant resonances are also reviewed, using coincidence techniques at facilities such as KVI Groningen and RCNP to isolate resonance strengths and suppress backgrounds. Theoretical calculations employing fully self-consistent linear response theory with nonrelativistic and covariant functionals, including pairing for superfluid nuclei, are discussed alongside experimental results.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear incompressibility \(K_\infty\) extracted from doubly-magic nuclei like \(^{208}\)Pb is reported as 240 ± 20 MeV.
- In \(^{208}\)Pb, the ISGMR peak energy is \(13.7 \pm 0.1\) MeV (RCNP, 386 MeV \(\alpha\)) and the ISGDR higher-lying component has an excitation energy of \(26.9 \pm 0.7\) MeV with a width of \(6.0 \pm 1.3\) MeV (KVI measurement).
- For \(^{90}\)Zr, the ISGMR peak energy is \(16.55 \pm 0.08\) MeV with a width of \(4.2 \pm 0.3\) MeV, exhausting \(95 \pm 6\%\) of the EWSR (RCNP, 386 MeV \(\alpha\)).
- For \(^{92}\)Zr, the ISGMR peak energy is \(16.12 \pm 0.04\) MeV with a width of \(4.5 \pm 0.2\) MeV, exhausting \(97 \pm 3\%\) of the EWSR (RCNP, 386 MeV \(\alpha\)).
- For \(^{92}\)Mo, the ISGMR peak energy is \(16.79 \pm 0.11\) MeV with a width of \(4.2 \pm 0.4\) MeV, exhausting \(84 \pm 6\%\) of the EWSR (RCNP, 386 MeV \(\alpha\)).
- In the deformed nucleus \(^{154}\)Sm, the ISGMR splits into two components: a lower component at \(10.83^{+0.32}_{-0.54}\) MeV (17% EWSR) and an upper component at \(15.45^{+0.13}_{-0.16}\) MeV (71% EWSR) (RCNP, 386 MeV \(\alpha\)).
- In \(^{24}\)Mg, a two-peak structure in the ISGMR strength distribution is observed, with one peak at \(E_x \sim 16\) MeV caused by deformation-induced coupling with the \(K=0\) part of the ISGQR.

## Context

This review extends the work summarized in earlier reviews and books covering the status up to the turn of the millennium, focusing on significant results from the past 15-20 years. It discusses the extraction of nuclear incompressibility from finite nuclei, which is a key parameter for the nuclear Equation of State relevant to core-collapse supernova and neutron star physics. The review addresses the controversy between TAMU group results (e.g., D. H. Youngblood et al., 2015) reporting dramatic variations in ISGMR strength in Zr and Mo isotopes, and RCNP results (e.g., Y. K. Gupta et al., 2016) that found no such anomalous effects, attributing the discrepancy to background subtraction procedures. The work enables future exploration of compression modes in exotic, neutron-rich nuclei using radioactive ion beams, where decoupling of core and skin nucleons may lead to two-fluid behavior with different incompressibilities.
