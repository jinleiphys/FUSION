# Pairing in the BCS and LN approximations using continuum single particle level density

> Understanding the properties of drip line nuclei requires to take into account the correlations with the continuum spectrum of energy of the system. This paper has the purpose to show that the continuum single particle level density is a convenient way to consider the pairing correlation in the continuum. Isospin mean-field and isospin pairing strength are used to find the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) and Lipkin-Nogami (LN) approximate solutions of the pairing Hamiltonian. Several physical properties of the whole chain of the Tin isotope, as gap parameter, Fermi level, binding energy, and one- and two-neutron separation energies, were calculated and compared with other methods and with experimental data when they exist. It is shown that the use of the continuum single particle level density is an economical way to include explicitly the correlations with the continuum spectrum of energy in large scale mass calculation. It is also shown that the computed properties are in good agreement with experimental data and with more sophisticated treatment of the pairing interaction.

## Key claim

The continuum single particle level density (CSPLD) provides an economical way to include pairing correlations with the continuum spectrum in large-scale mass calculations for drip line nuclei, and the computed properties for the Tin isotope chain show good agreement with experimental data and with more sophisticated treatments of pairing, though the method uses a constant pairing interaction and isospin-dependent mean field rather than a fully self-consistent approach.

## Method

The authors solve the constant pairing Hamiltonian in the BCS and Lipkin-Nogami (LN) approximations using a continuum single particle level density (CSPLD) derived from the phase shifts of a Woods-Saxon plus spin-orbit mean field. The single particle representation includes bound states as discrete levels and the continuum via the CSPLD, defined as \(g(\varepsilon) = \frac{1}{\pi}\sum_{lj}{(2j+1)\frac{d\delta_{lj}}{d\varepsilon}}\), with the pairing strength parametrized as \(G = \frac{\chi_{_1}}{A}(1-\chi_{_2} I)\) where \(\chi_{_2}=0.385\) MeV and \(\chi_{_1}\) is adjusted to reproduce experimental gaps. The equations are solved in a box representation then taken to the infinite-box limit, replacing sums over continuum states by integrals weighted by the CSPLD.

## Key numbers

- The parameter \(\chi_{_1}\) for the first major shell (50-82) is 18.637 MeV (BCS) and 18.269 MeV (LN), calibrated to reproduce \(\Delta(^{110}\text{Sn}) = 1.429\) MeV from experimental masses (Audi 2003).
- The parameter \(\chi_{_1}\) for the second major shell (82-126) is 15.631 MeV (BCS) and 14.972 MeV (LN), calibrated to reproduce \(\Delta(^{158}\text{Sn}) = 0.864\) MeV from theoretical masses (Koura 2005).
- The calculated BCS gap for isotopes \(^{134}\text{Sn}\) to \(^{164}\text{Sn}\) lies in the band 0.58-1.16 MeV, compared to 0.65-1.1 MeV from Bender (2000) using a delta force model.
- The calculated LN gap for the same isotopes lies in the band 0.7-1.1 MeV, compared to 0.55-1.3 MeV from Bender (2000).
- The two-neutron drip line from the CSPLD approach is not explicitly given as a single number, but the continuum particle number \(N_c\) starts to increase significantly from \(^{164}\text{Sn}\) onward, while bound configurations saturate at 62 valence neutrons.

## Context

This work extends the treatment of pairing in the BCS approximation with continuum inclusion via transformed harmonic oscillators (Lay 2016) and the CSPLD approach (Sandulescu 1997, Id Betan 2012) to the full Tin isotope chain from proton to neutron drip line. The results are compared with the HF+BCS calculations of Isakov (2013) and the state-dependent BCS and LN calculations of Bender (2000), showing similar accuracy for binding energies up to \(N=24\) and for gaps in the second major shell. The method enables large-scale mass calculations for heavy nuclei near the drip line, relevant for stellar nucleosynthesis (Arnould 2007), without the computational expense of full effective interactions.
