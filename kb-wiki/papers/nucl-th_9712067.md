# The structure of the QED-Vacuum and Electron-Positron Pair Production in Super-Intense, pulsed Laser Fields

> We discuss electron-positron pair-production by super-intense, short laser pulses off the physical vacuum state locally deformed by (stripped) nuclei with large nuclear charges. Consequences of non-perturbative vacuum polarisation resulting from such a deformation are shortly broached. Production probabilities per pulse are calculated.

## Key claim
Electron-positron pair production from the vacuum can occur via multi-photon transitions involving 10^5-10^6 photons in super-intense pulsed laser fields when the vacuum is locally deformed by a stripped nucleus with large nuclear charge, with measurable probability even at laser intensities near the reach of experimentation.

## Method
The authors model the system using a Dirac Hamiltonian with a time-dependent external potential combining a static Coulomb potential from a point-like nucleus (with finite-size corrections) and a linearly polarized, pulsed laser field in the dipole approximation. They solve the time-dependent Dirac equation as an initial-value problem for a positron scattering state, expanding in a basis of up to 1600 eigenfunctions of the Coulomb-Dirac problem (angular momenta j=1/2 through j=7/2) and integrating with a predictor-corrector routine. The probability per pulse is computed by projecting the time-evolved state onto single-electron bound and scattering states after the pulse has switched off.

## Key numbers
- Photon energy of the laser pulse: 5 eV
- Pulse duration: 20 cycles (ultra short pulse)
- Nuclear charges considered: Z=70 and Z=100
- Multi-photon transitions involve 10^5 to 10^6 photons
- Basis set size: up to 1600 eigenfunctions (j=1/2 through j=7/2)
- For realistic pulses (several thousand cycles), the calculated probabilities per pulse should be increased by at least an order of magnitude

## Context
This work extends the study of non-perturbative vacuum polarization effects previously investigated in heavy-ion collisions by Greiner et al., where repulsive forces prevent electron levels from penetrating into the Dirac sea even beyond critical nuclear Coulomb charges. The authors build on their own prior work (Dietz and Proebsting, 1997) on vacuum polarization charge and its jump at the positron threshold, and use numerical methods developed in earlier papers (Dietz, 1995). The calculation enables experimental investigation of vacuum structure using super-intense pulsed lasers as probes, provided sufficiently dense samples of stripped high-Z nuclei can be prepared.
