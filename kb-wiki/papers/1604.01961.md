# Opportunities for nuclear reaction studies at future facilities

> Opportunities for investigations of nuclear reactions at the future nuclear physics facilities such as radioactive ion beam facilities and high-power laser facilities are considered. Post-accelerated radioactive ion beams offer possibilities for study of the role of isospin asymmetry in the reaction mechanisms at various beam energies. Fission barrier heights of neutron-deficient nuclei can be directly determined at low energies. Post-accelerated radioactive ion beams, specifically at the future facilities such as HIE-ISOLDE, SPIRAL-2 or RAON-RISP can be also considered as a candidate for production of very neutron-rich nuclei via mechanism of multi-nucleon transfer. High-power laser facilities such as ELI-NP offer possibilities for nuclear reaction studies with beams of unprecedented properties. Specific cases such as ternary reactions or even production of super-heavy elements are considered.

# Opportunities for nuclear reaction studies at future facilities

## Key claim
Post-accelerated radioactive ion beams at facilities such as HIE-ISOLDE, SPIRAL-2, and RAON-RISP can directly determine fission barrier heights of neutron-deficient nuclei and produce very neutron-rich nuclei via multi-nucleon transfer, while high-power laser facilities such as ELI-NP may enable ternary reactions and potentially super-heavy element production, though the latter depends critically on the unproven reduction of stopping power in dense ion bunches.

## Method
The authors used the Talys code to estimate fission cross sections for transfer-induced fission of radioactive beams, and the Monte Carlo deep-inelastic transfer (DIT) model combined with the statistical multifragmentation model (SMM) to simulate production of neutron-rich nuclei in peripheral collisions below 10 AMeV. For laser-based studies, they performed rate estimates based on geometric fusion cross sections and assumed stopping powers for dense ion bunches, considering both normal and reduced stopping scenarios.

## Key numbers
- Fission barrier heights of neutron-deficient nuclei in the lead-to-uranium region need to be scaled down by 15–40% relative to theoretical values from Sierk and Moller; beta-delayed fission of 180Hg gave barriers 10–40% smaller than theoretical estimates.
- For 193Tl beam at HIE-ISOLDE, fission cross sections increase dramatically when the fission barrier is scaled down by 20% compared to standard values; using ACTAR TPC with 250 mbar deuterium gas and 5 AMeV initial beam energy, the beam slows to about 4.1 AMeV over 20 cm, yielding an average fission cross section around 2 mb and a fission rate of twenty per minute.
- For production of 78Ni via 74Zn+238U at 8 AMeV, the production cross section exceeds 1 mb; with a low-energy RIB rate of 10^8/s, 5% post-acceleration efficiency, and 10 mg/cm^2 target thickness, one 78Ni nucleus is produced in about five seconds.
- For super-heavy element production at ELI-NP using stable beam and normal stopping, at 1 pb cross section and 0.1 Hz repetition rate, the expected rate is one nucleus per 10000 hours; with reduced stopping (target thickness 50 mg/cm^2), the rate improves to one nucleus per 100 hours.

## Context
This paper extends prior work on deep-inelastic transfer at Fermi energies (Veselsky 2000, Souliotis 2003) to lower beam energies below 10 AMeV, and builds on beta-delayed fission measurements of 180Hg (Veselsky 2015) that showed discrepancies with theoretical fission barriers. The proposed transfer-induced fission experiment at HIE-ISOLDE using ACTAR TPC aims to resolve the long-standing question of fission barrier heights in proton-rich nuclei without the pairing-gap uncertainty inherent in beta-delayed fission. The laser-based production of super-heavy elements extends proposals by Habs (2011) for fission-fragment fusion at ELI-NP, and the ternary reaction concept could enable laboratory simulations of astrophysical processes.
