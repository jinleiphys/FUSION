# $S$-factor and scattering-parameter extractions from ${}^{3}\mathrm{He} +{}^{4}\mathrm{He} \rightarrow {}^{7}\mathrm{Be} + γ$

> Previous studies of the reaction ${}^{3}\mathrm{He} +{}^{4}\mathrm{He} \rightarrow {}^{7}\mathrm{Be} + γ$ have focused on providing the best central value and error bar for the $S$ factor at solar energies. Measurements of this capture reaction, the ${}^{3}\mathrm{He}$-${}^{4}\mathrm{He}$ scattering phase shifts, as well as properties of ${}^{7}\mathrm{Be}$, have been used to constrain employed theoretical models. Here we show that much more information than was previously appreciated can be extracted from angle-integrated capture data alone. We use the next-to-leading-order (NLO) amplitude in an effective field theory (EFT) for the reaction to perform the extrapolation. At this order the EFT describes the reaction using an s-wave scattering length and effective range, the asymptotic properties of the final bound states, and short-distance contributions to the $E1$ capture amplitude. We extract the multi-dimensional posterior of all these parameters via a Bayesian analysis. We find that properties of the ${}^{7}\mathrm{Be}$ ground and excited states are well constrained. The total $S$ factor $S(0)= 0.578^{+0.015}_{-0.016}$ keV~b, while the branching ratio for excited- to ground-state capture at zero energy, $Br(0)=0.406^{+0.013}_{-0.011}$, both at 68\% degree of belief. This $S(0)$ is broadly consistent with other recent evaluations, including the previously recommended value $S(0)=0.56 \pm 0.03$ eV b, but has a smaller error bar. We also find significant constraints on the scattering parameters, and we obtain constraints on the $S(E)$'s angular dependence. The path forward seems to lie with better measurements of the scattering phase shift and $S(E)$'s angular dependence, together with better understanding of the asymptotic normalization coefficients of the ${}^7$Be bound states' wave functions. Data on these could further reduce $S(0)$'s uncertainty.

## Key claim
The authors show that angle-integrated capture data alone, analyzed via a next-to-leading-order (NLO) halo effective field theory (EFT) with Bayesian methods, can tightly constrain not only the astrophysical S-factor but also s-wave scattering parameters and asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANCs) of the final states, provided the EFT expansion is validated against potential models and common-mode errors in data are treated carefully.

## Method
The authors constructed an NLO halo EFT amplitude for the reaction, describing the initial s-wave scattering via a scattering length and effective range, the final p-wave bound states via their ANCs, and short-distance E1 capture contributions via two parameters. They validated the EFT by fitting it to three prior theoretical models (Buck85, KimA, Nollett) and then performed a Bayesian Markov-Chain-Monte-Carlo analysis of six experimental total S-factor datasets below 2 MeV, including data-normalization corrections to account for common-mode errors in each experiment.

## Key numbers
- Total S-factor at zero energy: \(S(0) = 0.578^{+0.015}_{-0.016}\) keV·b (68% degree of belief)
- Branching ratio for excited- to ground-state capture at zero energy: \(Br(0) = 0.406^{+0.013}_{-0.011}\) (68% degree of belief)
- Previously recommended value: \(S(0) = 0.56 \pm 0.03\) keV·b (from earlier evaluations)
- Binding energies used: ground state \(B = 1.5874\) MeV, excited state \(B_\ast = 1.1583\) MeV (relative to the \(^3\)He-\(^4\)He threshold)
- Scattering length \(a_0\) from fits to potential models ranged from 18 to 37 fm; effective range \(r_0 \approx 1\) fm

## Context
This work extends the halo EFT approach previously applied to \(^7\)Be(p,γ)\(^8\)B by Zhang, Nollett, and Phillips (2015, 2017) to the \(^3\)He(α,γ)\(^7\)Be reaction, which is important for solar neutrino physics and Big Bang nucleosynthesis. It differs from recent halo EFT analyses by Higa et al. (2016) and Premarathna and Rupak (2019) by not including scattering phase-shift data (due to poorly quantified errors) and by more carefully treating correlations between data points. The results are broadly consistent with the recommended value from Adelberger et al. (2011) but with a smaller error bar, and the analysis identifies that better measurements of scattering phase shifts, angular distributions, and ANCs could further reduce the uncertainty on \(S(0)\).
