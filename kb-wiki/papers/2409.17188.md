# Neutron Star with Dark Matter Admixture: A Candidate for Bridging the Mass Gap

> Neutron stars, white dwarfs and black holes are the after death remnants of massive stars. However, according to the most recent observations, the neutron stars maximum mass is between $2.0-2.5 M_{\odot}$ while black holes of less than 5 $M_{\odot}$ has not yet been observed. The region between the most massive neutron star and the least massive black hole is called the mass-gap. If indeed its existence is confirmed by future observations, that indicates a gap in our understanding which seeks for explanation. In addition, the existence of compact objects within the mass-gap should also be supported with the help of possible new theoretical scenarios. In this study, we propose a possible explanation for the existence of compact objects within the mass-gap region. Specifically, we propose that the mass-gap region could be bridged by the existence of a hybrid compact object, composed of hadronic and self interacting and non-annihilating fermionic dark matter, considering that the interaction between these two fluids it is only gravitational. Fundamental questions about how these objects form and how they can be detected are also addressed.

## Key claim
The authors propose that stable hybrid compact objects composed of hadronic matter and self-interacting non-annihilating fermionic dark matter, interacting only gravitationally, can exist within the mass-gap region between the most massive neutron stars (2.0-2.5 solar masses) and the least massive black holes (below 5 solar masses). The claim is conditional on the dark matter particle mass and self-interaction strength falling within specific ranges that produce stable configurations.

## Method
The authors solve the coupled Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a two-fluid model where neutron star matter and dark matter interact only through gravity. The hadronic equation of state is taken from Relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) theory, while the dark matter is modeled as relativistic fermions with a repulsive Yukawa-type self-interaction. Stability is assessed using the Henriques-Liddle-Moorhouse method, examining the behavior of baryon and dark matter particle numbers at fixed total mass to identify stable and unstable branches.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass from RBHF theory aligns with observations of PSR J1614-2230, PSR J0348+0432, and PSR J0740+6620 (no explicit mass values given for these pulsars in the text)
- Dark matter self-interaction parameter y varies in the range approximately (0.001-0.1)(GeV/m_chi c^2)^(1/4) MeV^(-1), constrained by cross-section limits sigma/m_chi ~ 0.1-10 cm^2/g
- Three stable hybrid object masses are presented: 3.12, 4.18, and 5.24 solar masses, with dark matter particle mass m_chi = 1500 MeV and interaction strength y = 0.05 MeV^(-1)
- The dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda for these objects spans 2 to 3 orders of magnitude for a given mass
- The dark matter component radius is approximately ten times larger than the neutron star core radius, while the compactness beta remains comparable to that of neutron stars

## Context
This work extends theoretical studies of compact objects containing dark matter admixtures, building on prior work by Goldman (1989), Kouvaris (2008), and others who explored dark matter capture in neutron stars. It directly addresses the mass-gap problem highlighted by observations of GW190814 (Abbott 2020) and GW230529 (Abac 2024), which found compact objects with masses around 2.6 and 2.5-4.5 solar masses respectively. The study enables future gravitational wave observations to potentially identify such hybrid objects through their distinct tidal deformability signatures, which differ significantly from both pure neutron stars and black holes.
