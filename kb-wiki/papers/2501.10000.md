# Coupled-channel formalism

> This article provides a pedagogical exploration of coupled-channel scattering in hadronic physics, focusing on theoretical methodologies, and practical applications. It covers key concepts such as the $N/D$ method, Castillejo-Dalitz-Dyson poles, and the generalization of these techniques to coupled channels. The article also presents a parameterization for partial wave amplitudes, ensuring unitarity and analyticity, and addresses scattering near the threshold of dominant channels, with use also of the Lippmann-Schwinger equation. Additionally, it explores final-state interactions, Omnès and Muskhelishvily solutions, the Khuri-Treiman approach, and the two-potential model for resonant event distributions, highlighting the role of Riemann sheets in resonance behavior. These methods are also applicable to related fields like nuclear and atomic physics.

# Coupled-channel formalism

## Key claim
This pedagogical article presents coupled-channel scattering formalism as an indispensable framework for hadronic interactions, but the abstract's claim of "comprehensive exploration" is hedged by the author's explicit statement that the chapter is "rather self-contained, requiring only a basic understanding of the topic" and that "other more advanced topics not covered" include the first-iterated N/D method and the exact N/D method, which are referenced but not developed.

## Method
The paper develops the N/D method for partial-wave amplitudes, expressing the scattering amplitude as a quotient N(s)/D(s) where D(s) contains the unitarity cut and N(s) contains the left-hand cut, then generalizes to coupled channels using a matrix formalism with D(s)⁻¹N(s). The method incorporates Castillejo-Dalitz-Dyson (CDD) poles as additional terms in the dispersion relation for D(s), representing microscopic dynamics not generated by rescattering. The formalism is extended to non-relativistic scattering near threshold using the effective-range expansion and Flatté parameterization, and to final-state interactions via Omnès and Muskhelishvily-Omnès solutions.

## Context
This work extends the author's previous book (Oller 2019) and reviews (Oller 2020, Oller 2019) on hadronic scattering, building on classical references including Martin (2005), Barton (1965), and Weinberg (1995). The pedagogical treatment connects the N/D method from Chew (1960) and CDD poles from Castillejo, Dalitz, and Dyson (1955) to modern applications in ππ scattering, demonstrating that the σ resonance is dynamically generated by ππ self-interactions while the ρ(770) requires an elementary CDD pole, consistent with the analysis in Oller (1998). The formalism enables systematic parameterization of partial-wave amplitudes with unitarity and analyticity constraints, applicable to nuclear and atomic physics beyond hadronic systems.
