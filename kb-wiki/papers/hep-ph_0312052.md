# Information theory approach (extensive and nonextensive) to high energy multiple production processes

> We present an overview of information theory approach (both in its extensive and nonextensive versions) applied to high energy multiparticle production processes. It will be illustrated by analysis of single particle distributions measured in proton-proton, proton-antiproton and nuclear collisions. We shall demonstrate the particular role played by the nonextensivity parameter q in such analysis as summarizing our knowledge on the fluctuations existed in hadronizing system.

# Information theory approach (extensive and nonextensive) to high energy multiple production processes

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that information theory, particularly its nonextensive version using Tsallis entropy with parameter q, provides a model-independent framework for analyzing single-particle distributions in high-energy multiparticle production, where the nonextensivity parameter q summarizes the effect of intrinsic fluctuations in the hadronizing system. The authors show that the necessity of using q > 1 arises specifically when the multiplicity distribution is of Negative Binomial type rather than Poissonian, with q directly related to the normalized variance of the mean multiplicity.

## Method

The authors apply the maximum entropy principle using both Shannon entropy (q=1) and Tsallis entropy (q≠1) to derive the most probable rapidity and transverse momentum distributions for particles produced in pp, pbar-p, and nuclear collisions. The input constraints are the collision energy sqrt(s), the mean multiplicity of charged secondaries (parameterized as nbar_ch = -7.0 + 7.2 s^0.127), the mean transverse momentum (parameterized as <pT> = 0.3 + 0.044 ln(sqrt(s)/20) GeV/c), and the assumption that multiplicity distributions follow a Negative Binomial form with energy-dependent width parameter k. The resulting q-exponential distribution p(y) = (1/Z_q) exp_q(-beta_q * mu_T * cosh y) contains two parameters: the nonextensivity q and the q-inelasticity kappa_q, both determined from data.

## Key numbers

- Mean multiplicity parameterization: nbar_ch = -7.0 + 7.2 s^0.127, where s is in GeV^2
- Mean transverse momentum parameterization: <pT> = 0.3 + 0.044 ln(sqrt(s)/20) GeV/c, with sqrt(s) in GeV
- Negative Binomial width parameter: 1/k = -0.104 + 0.058 ln(sqrt(s)), with sqrt(s) in GeV
- For pp/pbar-p collisions: q varies from 1.05 at sqrt(s)=20 GeV to 1.33 at sqrt(s)=1800 GeV; partition temperature T_q = 1/beta_q varies from 1.76 GeV to 62.57 GeV over the same range
- For pT spectra at sqrt(s)=200, 500, and 900 GeV: (T_T; q_T) = (0.134 GeV; 1.095), (0.135 GeV; 1.105), and (0.14 GeV; 1.11) respectively
- For PbPb collisions at 17.3 GeV per nucleon: q=1.2, K_q=0.33; at 12.3 GeV: q=1.164, K_q=0.3; at 8.6 GeV: q=1.04, K_q=0.22
- Total inelasticity K_q for pp/pbar-p collisions varies slightly around K_q ~ 0.5 across energies

## Context

This work extends the early application of information theory to multiparticle production by Chao (1970s) and the subsequent MaxEnt approaches, incorporating the nonextensive Tsallis statistics developed by Tsallis (1988). The paper connects the nonextensivity parameter q to fluctuations in the hadronizing system, following the relation q = 1 + D(nbar) where D(nbar) is the normalized variance of the mean multiplicity, as established by Wilk and Wlodarczyk (2000) and Beck and Cohen (2003). The method enables model-independent extraction of the inelasticity parameter K and provides a framework for analyzing heavy-ion collision data, where the authors identify the need for two-source configurations or other new mechanisms at higher energies, opening the question of how to interpret the growing deviation from single-source q-exponential fits in nuclear collisions.
