# Neutron Star Radii, Universal Relations, and the Role of Prior Distributions

> We investigate constraints on neutron star structure arising from the assumptions that neutron stars have crusts, that recent calculations of pure neutron matter limit the equation of state of neutron star matter near the nuclear saturation density, that the high-density equation of state is limited by causality and the largest high-accuracy neutron star mass measurement, and that general relativity is the correct theory of gravity. We explore the role of prior assumptions by considering two classes of equation of state models. In a first, the intermediate- and high-density behavior of the equation of state is parameterized by piecewise polytropes. In the second class, the high-density behavior of the equation of state is parameterized by piecewise continuous line segments. The smallest density at which high-density matter appears is varied in order to allow for strong phase transitions above the nuclear saturation density. We critically examine correlations among the pressure of matter, radii, maximum masses, the binding energy, the moment of inertia, and the tidal deformability, paying special attention to the sensitivity of these correlations to prior assumptions about the equation of state. It is possible to constrain the radii of $1.4~\mathrm{M}_{\odot}$ neutron stars to a be larger than 10 km, even without consideration of additional astrophysical observations, for example, those from photospheric radius expansion bursts or quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries. We are able to improve the accuracy of known correlations between the moment of inertia and compactness as well as the binding energy and compactness. We also demonstrate the existence of a correlation between the neutron star binding energy and the moment of inertia.

## Key claim

Even without any astrophysical observations (such as photospheric radius expansion bursts or quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries), the radii of 1.4 solar mass neutron stars can be constrained to be larger than 10 km, but the exact allowed range depends sensitively on the choice of prior distribution for the equation of state parameters, with polytrope-based models giving a radius range of 10.8 to 12.4 km and models allowing stronger phase transitions giving 10.2 to 11.9 km.

## Method

The authors constructed two classes of equation of state models: one using piecewise polytropes with three polytropic exponents parameterized by pressures at boundary densities (1.85n_s and 3.7n_s), and another using piecewise continuous line segments that allow for stronger phase transitions. They performed Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov integrations to compute neutron star structure, imposing constraints from causality, hydrodynamic stability, the largest accurately measured neutron star mass (1.97 solar masses from PSR J0548+0432), and recent pure neutron matter calculations from Gandolfi, Carlson and Reddy (2012) and Drischler, Soma and Schwenk (2014) to bracket the pressure at 1.85 times nuclear saturation density.

## Key numbers

- Radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: 10.8 to 12.4 km for Model A (polytrope-based) and 10.2 to 11.9 km for Model C (allowing stronger phase transitions), giving a combined range of 10.2 to 12.4 km
- Pressure at twice nuclear saturation density inferred from neutron star radii: 9.1 to 23.0 MeV/fm^3 for Model A and 2.3 to 17.0 MeV/fm^3 for Model C
- The absolute minimum radius for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star from the "maximally compact" equation of state and a 2 solar mass maximum mass is 8.15 km
- Realistic neutron matter calculations give a pressure range at 1.85n_s of 8.125 to 20.933 MeV/fm^3
- The prior ambiguity increases the uncertainty in the radius by at least 30 percent

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian inference approach of Steiner (2010, 2013) and the piecewise polytrope parameterization of Read (2009) and Ozel (2009, 2010) by systematically examining how prior assumptions about the equation of state affect neutron star radius constraints and correlations among observables. The authors demonstrate that the choice between polytropic parameterizations (which disfavor phase transitions) and more flexible parameterizations significantly changes the inferred pressure at twice saturation density by up to a factor of four in the lower limit. This analysis enables more robust interpretation of future neutron star radius measurements from X-ray observations and gravitational wave detections by quantifying the systematic uncertainty arising from prior choices.
