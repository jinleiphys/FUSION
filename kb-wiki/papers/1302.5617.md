# Universal Relation for the Inelastic Two-Body Loss Rate

> Strongly-interacting systems consisting of particles that interact through a large scattering length satisfy universal relations that relate many of their central properties to the contact, which measures the number of pairs with small separations. We use the operator product expansion of quantum field theory to derive the universal relation for the inelastic 2-body loss rate. A simple universal relation between the loss rate and the contact is obtained by truncating the expansion after the lowest dimension operator. We verify the universal relation explicitly by direct calculations in the low-density limit at nonzero temperature. This universal relation can be tested experimentally using ultracold quantum gases of atoms in hyperfine states that have an inelastic spin-relaxation channel.

## Key claim

The authors derive a universal relation for the inelastic two-body loss rate using the operator product expansion (OPE) of quantum field theory, but the simple relation between loss rate and contact is obtained only by truncating the OPE after the lowest dimension operator; the validity of this truncation depends on the convergence properties of the OPE, which is plausible but not proven for nonrelativistic field theories, and the higher-dimension contributions may become equally important in the unitary limit where the scattering length is infinite.

## Method

The authors use the operator product expansion of quantum field theory to express the correlator of the contact density operator and the number density operator as a sum of local operators with increasing scaling dimensions. They start from a Hamiltonian with a complex bare coupling constant to account for inelastic scattering channels, derive the loss rate from the time evolution of the number density, and then apply the OPE to reduce the double spatial integral to single integrals over expectation values of local operators. The simple universal relation is obtained by keeping only the leading contact-density operator and omitting all higher-dimension operators, with the justification that these are parametrically suppressed in the BCS and BEC limits by powers of \(k_F |a|\) and numerically suppressed in the unitary limit by exponential convergence in scaling dimension.

## Key numbers

* The simple universal relation for the loss rate is \(\frac{d}{dt} N_\sigma = -\frac{\hbar}{4\pi \mu} \, \text{Im}(1/a) \, C\), where \(\mu\) is the reduced mass, \(a\) is the complex scattering length, and \(C\) is the contact.
* The contact density operator is defined as \(\mathcal{C}(\bm{R}) = \langle |g_0|^2 \psi_1^\dagger \psi_2^\dagger \psi_2 \psi_1 (\bm{R}) \rangle\), with scaling dimension 4.
* For equal-mass fermions, the next higher-dimension operator has scaling dimension 8.54, and the next operator after that has scaling dimension 9.33.
* For fermions with mass ratio \(m_2/m_1 > 13.6\), the lowest-dimension three-body operator has scaling dimension 5.
* For bosons, the lowest-dimension three-body operator has scaling dimension 5 for all mass ratios.
* In the BCS and BEC limits (\(a \to 0^\mp\)), the contribution from the first higher-dimension operator for equal-mass fermions is suppressed by a factor of \((k_F |a|)^{4.54}\).

## Context

This work extends the universal relations originally derived by Shina Tan (2005, 2008) for the strongly-interacting two-component Fermi gas, which relate properties such as energy and pressure to the contact. The authors previously rederived Tan's relations using quantum field theory and the operator product expansion (Braaten and Platter, 2008). The present paper provides the first explicit derivation of the universal relation for the inelastic two-body loss rate, which was previously stated without derivation in a review (Braaten, 2010). The result enables experimental tests using ultracold quantum gases of atoms in hyperfine states with inelastic spin-relaxation channels, and it provides a framework for calculating loss rates in systems with large scattering lengths, including near the unitary limit.
