# Exploring the Beam Energy Dependence of Flow-Like Signatures in Small System $d+$Au Collisions

> Recent analyses of small collision systems, namely $p+p$ and $p+$Pb at the LHC and $p+$Au, $d+$Au and $^{3}$He+Au at RHIC, have revealed azimuthal momentum anisotropies commonly associated with collective flow in larger systems. Viscous hydrodynamics and parton cascade calculations have proved successful at describing some flow-like observables in these systems. These two classes of calculations also confirm these observables to be directly related to the initial geometry of the created medium. However, the question of whether equilibrium dynamics is the dominant driver of the signal remains open, given the short lifetime of small systems. In this regime, pre-equilibrium dynamics and late stage hadronic interactions are expected to play a significant role. Hence, a beam energy scan of small systems---that amounts to varying the initial temperature and the lifetime of the medium---can provide valuable information to shed light on these issues. In this paper, we present predictions from viscous hydrodynamics (SONIC), partonic (AMPT) and hadronic (UrQMD) cascade calculations for elliptic $v_2$ and triangular $v_3$ anisotropy coefficients in $d$+Au at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 7.7, 20, 39, 62.4 and 200 GeV, corresponding to the expected running at RHIC in 2016. We also present predictions for $d$+Pb at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 5.02 TeV, an interesting system to compare to existing $p+$Pb data taken at the LHC.

## Key claim

Viscous hydrodynamics and parton cascade models predict substantial elliptic and triangular flow in d+Au collisions down to the lowest beam energies (7.7 GeV), with no sharp turn-off in the translation of initial geometry to final-state momentum anisotropy, despite the short lifetime of the system.

## Method

The authors performed viscous hydrodynamic calculations (SONIC and SUPERSONIC), parton cascade calculations (AMPT with string melting, partonic cross section 1.5 mb and 0 mb), and hadronic cascade calculations (UrQMD) for d+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7, 20, 39, 62.4, and 200 GeV, plus d+Pb at 5.02 TeV. All models used a common Monte Carlo Glauber initial geometry with deuteron nucleon positions sampled from the Hulthén wavefunction and energy-dependent nucleon-nucleon cross sections. Elliptic (v2) and triangular (v3) anisotropy coefficients were computed for unidentified charged hadrons at midrapidity relative to the participant plane.

## Key numbers

- v2 in d+Au at 200 GeV from SUPERSONIC hydrodynamics agrees with PHENIX experimental data within uncertainties; pre-equilibrium flow adds approximately +10% to v2 relative to SONIC
- v2 decreases by approximately 25-30% from 5.02 TeV to 200 GeV, and another 25-30% from 200 GeV to 7.7 GeV in both hydrodynamic and AMPT calculations
- v3 decreases by roughly 300% (five-fold) from 200 GeV to 7.7 GeV in SUPERSONIC hydrodynamics
- In AMPT, over 50% of quarks undergo zero partonic scattering events at all collision energies; the probability of no scattering varies by only about 10% across the energy range
- The summed space-time volume of QGP in hydrodynamics increases five-fold from 7.7 GeV to 200 GeV, with approximately 8 fm^2 Delta y fm/c at 7.7 GeV
- AMPT without partonic scattering (sigma_part = 0 mb) yields v2 approximately 50% lower than with sigma_part = 1.5 mb at all energies
- UrQMD v2 results from two-particle cumulants with |Delta eta| > 1.6 are dramatically larger than participant plane results and largest at the lowest collision energy

## Context

This paper extends the small-system flow program at RHIC, following measurements of azimuthal anisotropies in p+Au, d+Au, and 3He+Au collisions (Adare 2014, Adare 2015) and hydrodynamic modeling with SONIC (Habich 2014) and SUPERSONIC (Romatschke 2015). It provides predictions for the RHIC beam energy scan of d+Au planned for 2016, enabling tests of whether partonic effects remain important at lower energies or whether purely hadronic descriptions (as in UrQMD calculations by Zhou 2015) become sufficient. The work also motivates future d+Pb runs at the LHC to better constrain initial geometry compared to p+Pb, where proton shape fluctuations introduce larger uncertainties (Schenke 2014, Schlichting 2014).
