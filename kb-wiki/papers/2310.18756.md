# Uncertainty Quantification-Enabled Inversion of Nuclear Euclidean Responses

> Nuclear quantum many-body methods rely on integral transform techniques to infer properties of electroweak response functions from ground-state expectation values. Retrieving the energy dependence of these responses is highly non-trivial, especially for quantum Monte Carlo methods, as it requires inverting the Laplace transform -- a notoriously ill-posed problem. In this work, we propose an artificial neural network architecture suitable for accurate response function reconstruction with precise estimation of the uncertainty of the inversion. We demonstrate the capabilities of this new architecture benchmarking it against Maximum Entropy and previously developed neural network methods designed for a similar task, paying particular attention to its robustness against increasing noise in the input Euclidean responses.

## Key claim

The authors propose an artificial neural network architecture (UQ-NN) that provides accurate reconstruction of nuclear response functions from their Laplace transforms while also enabling rigorous propagation of statistical uncertainties from the input Euclidean responses into the reconstructed response, overcoming a limitation of their earlier Phys-NN approach (Raghavan 2020) which could not quantify uncertainty.

## Method

The authors train neural networks on synthetic datasets of 500,000 pairs of response functions and their corresponding Euclidean (Laplace-transformed) responses, using a parameterization inspired by the Maximum Entropy method that expresses the response in terms of singular vectors of the Laplace kernel. The UQ-NN architecture uses two parallel Ent-NN blocks sharing training parameters, one processing clean Euclidean responses and the other processing noisy versions, with a loss function combining entropy-based response accuracy and chi-squared Euclidean cost for both clean and noisy inputs.

## Key numbers

- The Laplace kernel is discretized on n_omega = 2000 grid points with a maximum energy of 2 GeV, and n_tau = 150 imaginary-time points with maximum imaginary time 0.07 MeV^{-1}
- The singular space dimension is n_s approximately 30, much smaller than n_omega = 2000
- Training uses 500,000 synthetic response-Euclidean pairs for each of the one-peak and two-peak datasets
- Noise levels tested: sigma = 10^{-5} (comparable to GFMC calculations of ^4He), sigma = 10^{-4}, sigma = 10^{-3} (comparable to GFMC calculations of ^{12}C), and sigma = 10^{-2} (comparable to AFDMC calculations of ^{16}O)
- For the combined test dataset, Ent-NN achieves average entropy S_R = 0.732 x 10^{-4} and average chi-squared = 3.535; UQ-NN achieves S_R = 0.859 x 10^{-4} and chi-squared = 12.351; Phys-NN achieves S_R = 1.031 x 10^{-4} and chi-squared = 5.153; MaxEnt achieves S_R = 83.7 x 10^{-4} and chi-squared = 1.015

## Context

This work extends the Phys-NN approach (Raghavan 2020) for inverting Laplace transforms of nuclear response functions, which itself improved upon the Maximum Entropy method (Bryan 1990, Jarrell 1996) that struggles in low-energy regions with multiple peaks. The new UQ-NN architecture enables uncertainty propagation from Green's Function Monte Carlo calculations into reconstructed response functions, which is essential for reliable comparisons with experimental data and for propagating cross-section uncertainties into neutrino-oscillation parameter error budgets (Ruso 2022).
