# Structure of ^{19}C from Coulomb dissociation studies

> We investigate the structure of the neutron rich nucleus ^{19}C through studies of its breakup in the Coulomb field of target nuclei. The breakup amplitude is calculated within an adiabatic treatment of the projectile excitation, which allows the use of the realistic wave functions for the relative motion between the fragments in the ground state of the projectile. The angular distribution of the center of mass of ^{19}C, longitudinal momentum distribution of ^{18}C and relative energy spectrum of the fragments (neutron - ^{18}C) following the breakup of ^{19}C on heavy targets at beam energies below 100 MeV/nucleon have been computed using different configurations for the ground state wave function of ^{19}C. In all the cases, the data seem to favor a ^{18}C(0$^+)\otimes1s_{1/2}$ configuration for the ground state of ^{19}C, with the one-neutron separation energy of 0.53 MeV.

## Key claim

The data favor a \(^{18}\text{C}(0^+)\otimes 1s_{1/2}\) configuration for the ground state of \(^{19}\text{C}\) with a one-neutron separation energy of 0.53 MeV, but this conclusion is conditional on the use of a specific adiabatic Coulomb breakup model; the semiclassical Coulomb excitation model requires a spectroscopic factor of 0.67 (rather than 1.0) to reproduce the same data, and both models underestimate the relative energy spectrum at higher energies, likely due to neglected nuclear breakup effects.

## Method

The authors calculated Coulomb breakup cross sections for \(^{19}\text{C}\) on heavy targets (Pb, Ta) using an adiabatic treatment of projectile excitation that allows realistic wave functions for the neutron-core relative motion in the ground state. They computed the angular distribution of the \(^{19}\text{C}\) center of mass, the longitudinal momentum distribution of \(^{18}\text{C}\), and the relative energy spectrum of the neutron-\(^{18}\text{C}\) fragments, comparing four different ground-state configurations: (a) \(1s_{1/2}\) bound to a \(0^+\) core by 0.24 MeV, (b) \(1s_{1/2}\) bound to a \(2^+\) core by 1.86 MeV, (c) \(0d_{5/2}\) bound to a \(0^+\) core by 0.24 MeV, and (d) \(1s_{1/2}\) bound to a \(0^+\) core by 0.53 MeV. The vertex function was evaluated at momentum \(\vec{q}_n = \vec{k}_n - \gamma \vec{k}_a\) rather than at \(\vec{k}_n\) as in the distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) approach of Shyam (1992).

## Key numbers

- One-neutron separation energy of \(^{19}\text{C}\): 0.53 MeV (favored by data), compared to the Nubase evaluation of \(160 \pm 110\) keV.
- RMS matter radius of \(^{19}\text{C}\) for configuration (d): 3.23 fm; RMS radius of the valence neutron for configuration (d): 7.07 fm.
- FWHM of the parallel momentum distribution of \(^{18}\text{C}\) from Coulomb breakup on Ta at 88 MeV/nucleon: 41 MeV/c for configuration (d), compared to the experimental value of \(41 \pm 3\) MeV/c.
- Total Coulomb breakup cross section predicted by the adiabatic model with configuration (d) and spectroscopic factor 1.0: 0.78 b; the semiclassical model predicts 1.53 b, while the experimental total breakup cross section is \(1.34 \pm 0.12\) b.
- FWHM of the experimental longitudinal momentum distribution of \(^{18}\text{C}\) on Be and Ta at 88 MeV/nucleon: \(42 \pm 4\) MeV/c and \(41 \pm 3\) MeV/c respectively; on carbon at \(\sim 1\) GeV/nucleon: \(69 \pm 3\) MeV/c.
- Experimental interaction cross section of \(^{19}\text{C}\) on \(^{12}\text{C}\) at \(\sim 960\) MeV/nucleon: \(1231 \pm 28\) mb, compared to \(1104 \pm 15\) mb for \(^{18}\text{C}\).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of \(^{19}\text{C}\) breakup data from Nakamura (1999) and Bazin (1995) by applying a fully quantum-mechanical, non-perturbative Coulomb breakup theory that includes finite-range effects and all-order Coulomb interactions, in contrast to the semiclassical Coulomb excitation approach used by Nakamura (1999). The results support the interpretation of \(^{19}\text{C}\) as a one-neutron halo nucleus, consistent with earlier work by Tostevin and Al-Khalili (1999) and Ridikas (1997), but the authors note that proper inclusion of nuclear breakup effects (as discussed by Dasso (1999) for \(^{11}\text{Be}\)) is needed to fully describe the relative energy spectrum at higher energies. The findings enable future studies that combine Coulomb and nuclear breakup amplitudes coherently to extract spectroscopic factors for halo nuclei.
