# Tomography of Massive Stars from Core Collapse to Supernova Shock Breakout

> Neutrinos and gravitational waves are the only direct probes of the inner dynamics of a stellar core collapse. They are also the first signals to arrive from a supernova and, if detected, establish the moment when the shock wave is formed that unbinds the stellar envelope and later initiates the optical display upon reaching the stellar surface with a burst of UV and X-ray photons, the shock breakout (SBO). We discuss how neutrino observations can be used to trigger searches to detect the elusive SBO event. Observation of the SBO would provide several important constraints on progenitor structure and the explosion, including the shock propagation time (the duration between the neutrino burst and SBO), an observable that is important in distinguishing progenitor types. Our estimates suggest that next generation neutrino detectors could exploit the overdensity of nearby SNe to provide several such triggers per decade, more than an order of magnitude improvement over the present.

# Tomography of Massive Stars from Core Collapse to Supernova Shock Breakout

## Key claim

Next-generation neutrino detectors of 0.5-5 megaton mass could provide several neutrino-triggered alerts per decade for extragalactic supernovae within ~10 Mpc, enabling targeted searches for the otherwise-elusive shock breakout (SBO) photon signal, though the actual detection rate depends on the unknown fraction of collapses that produce black holes rather than neutron stars.

## Method

The authors computed shock propagation times (the interval between core-collapse neutrino burst and SBO) by integrating analytic shock velocity profiles through progenitor density models from Woosley (2002, 2005) for red supergiants (RSG), blue supergiants (BSG), and Wolf-Rayet stars over 11-35 solar masses. They then estimated neutrino detection rates for 560 kton (Hyper-Kamiokande class) and 5 Mton water Cherenkov detectors using a Fermi-Dirac neutrino spectrum with average energy 15 MeV and total energy 5x10^52 erg, combined with galaxy catalogs from Karachentsev (2004) and Lee (2009) to determine the probability of associating a detected neutrino burst with specific nearby galaxies.

## Context

This work extends the analytic framework of Matzner (1999) for SBO observables by computing shock propagation times as a new observable that connects core-collapse neutrino signals to photon SBO detection. The authors build on the recognition by Ando (2005) that megaton-scale detectors could detect extragalactic supernova neutrinos, and on the rate estimates of Kistler (2008) for nearby supernovae. The paper enables a coordinated multi-messenger strategy where neutrino detectors trigger targeted UV/X-ray observations of likely host galaxies (such as IC 342, NGC 253, M82, and NGC 6946) to capture the SBO, which would otherwise require serendipitous observations like those of SN 2008D by Swift (Soderberg 2008).
