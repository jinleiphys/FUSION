# Interaction current in $p p \to p p γ$

> The nucleon-nucleon bremsstrahlung reaction is investigated based on a fully gauge-invariant relativistic meson-exchange model approach. In order to account consistently for the complicated part of the interaction current (which at present is too demanding to be calculated explicitly), a generalized contact current is introduced following the approach of Haberzettl, Nakayama, and Krewald [Phys. Rev. C74, 045202 (2006)]. The contact interaction current is constructed phenomenologically such that the resulting full bremsstrahlung amplitude satisfies the generalized Ward-Takahashi identity. The formalism is applied to describe the high-precision proton-proton bremsstrahlung data at 190 MeV obtained at KVI [H. Huisman et al., Phys. Rev. C65, 031001(R) (2002)]. The present results show good agreement with the data, thus removing the longstanding discrepancy between the theoretical predictions and experimental data. The present investigation, therefore, points to the importance of properly taking into account the interaction current for this reaction.

## Key claim

The long-standing discrepancy between theoretical predictions and high-precision proton-proton bremsstrahlung data at 190 MeV is resolved by including a generalized four-point contact current that accounts for the interaction current in the \(NM \to N\gamma\) subprocess, constructed such that the full bremsstrahlung amplitude satisfies the generalized Ward-Takahashi identity and is gauge invariant. The authors caution that the particular phenomenological contact current employed does not provide a definitive resolution of all problems in \(NN\) bremsstrahlung processes.

## Method

The authors use a fully relativistic meson-exchange model based on a three-dimensional reduction of the Bethe-Salpeter equation for the \(NN\) interaction, employing the OBEP-B version of the Bonn \(NN\) interaction. The photon production amplitude \(J^\mu\) is constructed from nucleonic, mesonic (including \(\pi\rho\gamma\) and \(\pi\omega\gamma\) exchange), and contact-type currents, where the complicated four-point contact current for the \(NM \to N\gamma\) subprocess is replaced by a phenomenological generalized contact term with two free parameters (\(h\) and \(a\)) adjusted to reproduce the KVI cross-section data. The five-point contact current is dropped entirely because it is fully transverse in the three-dimensional reduction and thus does not affect gauge invariance.

## Key numbers

- Incident proton energy: 190 MeV, well below the pion-production threshold of about 280 MeV.
- Free parameters of the generalized contact current: \(h = 2.5\) and \(a = 1000\) for the Bonn \(NN\) interaction; when using the Paris \(NN\) interaction (which includes Coulomb effects), the parameter \(h\) is readjusted to \(h = 2.0\).
- \(NN\omega\) coupling constant: \(g_{NN\omega} = 10\); cutoff parameter in the \(NN\pi\) form factor for the mesonic current: \(\Lambda_\pi = 900\) MeV; cutoff parameter for the off-shell nucleon form factor in the basic photon production current: \(\Lambda_N = 1000\) MeV.
- Switching off the generalized contact current produces results similar to earlier models, especially for asymmetric proton scattering angles, while including it reproduces the KVI cross-section data well for both symmetric (\(\theta_1 = \theta_2\)) and asymmetric (\(\theta_1 \ne \theta_2\)) proton scattering angles.

## Context

This work extends the gauge-invariant formalism developed by Haberzettl, Nakayama, and Krewald (2006) for pion photoproduction to the \(NN\) bremsstrahlung reaction, addressing the long-standing discrepancy between theoretical predictions and the high-precision KVI data from Huisman et al. (2002). Previous microscopic models by Martinus et al. (1997) and Herrmann et al. (1995) overestimated the measured cross sections, especially for asymmetric proton scattering angles, while soft-photon models reproduced cross sections but failed for analyzing powers. The present results demonstrate that the interaction current, previously neglected in all earlier models, is a necessary ingredient for bremsstrahlung calculations, and the approach can be extended to more sophisticated \(NN\) interactions that include explicit \(\Delta\) degrees of freedom or two-nucleon irreducible contributions.
