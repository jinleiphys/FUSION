# Validating neural-network refinements of nuclear mass models

> Nuclear astrophysics centers on the role of nuclear physics in the cosmos. In particular, nuclear masses at the limits of stability are critical in the development of stellar structure and the origin of the elements. In this contribution we test and validate the predictions of recently refined nuclear mass models against the newly published AME2016 compilation. The basic paradigm underlining the recently refined nuclear mass models is based on existing state-of-the-art models that are subsequently refined through the training of an artificial neural network. We observe a significant improvement in the Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) predictions relative to the corresponding "bare" models when compared to the nearly 50 new masses reported in the AME2016 compilation. Further, AME2016 estimates for the handful of impactful isotopes in the determination of r-process abundances are found to be in fairly good agreement with our theoretical predictions. Indeed, the BNN-improved Duflo-Zuker model predicts a root-mean-square deviation relative to experiment of about 400 keV. Given the excellent performance of the BNN refinement in confronting the recently published AME2016 compilation, we are confident of its critical role in our quest for mass models of the highest quality. Moreover, as uncertainty quantification is at the core of the BNN approach, the improved mass models are in a unique position to identify those nuclei that will have the strongest impact in resolving some of the outstanding questions in nuclear astrophysics.

## Key claim

The Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) refinement of existing nuclear mass models, trained exclusively on the AME2012 compilation, produces predictions for the 46 new masses in AME2012 that are significantly improved over the bare models, with all BNN-refined predictions consistent with experiment at the 2-sigma level when theoretical error bars are included.

## Method

The authors took two existing mass models (the Duflo-Zuker macroscopic-microscopic model and the HFB-19 microscopic density functional) and refined their predictions using a Bayesian Neural Network trained on the AME2012 mass compilation for nuclei from Ca-40 to U-240. They then compared the BNN-refined predictions against the 46 new masses in the AME2016 compilation that were not present in AME2012, as well as against AME2016 estimates for a set of neutron-rich isotopes identified as impactful for r-process nucleosynthesis.

## Key numbers

* The BNN-improved Duflo-Zuker model achieved a root-mean-square deviation of about 400 keV (0.479 MeV) relative to the 46 new AME2016 masses, compared to 1.018 MeV for the bare Duflo-Zuker model.
* The BNN-improved HFB-19 model achieved a root-mean-square deviation of 0.587 MeV for the 46 new masses, compared to 1.093 MeV for the bare HFB-19 model.
* For the 10 impactful r-process isotopes (in Pd, Cd, In, Sn) with AME2016 estimates from experimental trends, the BNN-Duflo-Zuker model gave a root-mean-square deviation of 369 keV, compared to 1.210 MeV for the bare Duflo-Zuker model.
* The WS3 model, without BNN refinement, achieved a root-mean-square deviation of 0.513 MeV for the 46 new masses and 1.117 MeV for the impactful r-process isotopes.

## Context

This work validates the BNN refinement approach previously introduced by Utama and Piekarewicz (2015, 2016, 2017) against the newly published AME2016 compilation, where the 46 new masses serve as true blind predictions since they were not in the AME2012 training set. The paper extends the comparison to include the WS3 model of Liu, Wang, Deng, and Wu (2011) and the HFB-27 model of Goriely, Chamel, and Pearson (2013), showing that the BNN approach reduces the theoretical spread between models and provides statistical uncertainties that are essential for identifying which nuclei most strongly impact r-process abundance calculations, where sensitivity studies require mass-model uncertainties below about 100 keV.
