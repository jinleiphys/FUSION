# Machine Learning for Correlations of Electromagnetic Properties in Ab Initio Calculations

> In ab initio nuclear structure theory, accurately predicting electromagnetic observables, such as moments and transition rates, is essential for a comprehensive understanding of nuclear properties. However, computational limitations and conceptual difficulties often hinder the precise calculation of these observables. In this work, we extend machine learning methods for model-space extrapolations to electric quadrupole moments. We further present a new machine learning approach that leverages the correlations between energies, radii, and electromagnetic observables. By learning these correlations from no-core shell model calculations in accessible model spaces, this new model enables the prediction of converged electromagnetic observables from predictions of converged energies and radii, which can be obtained with established machine learning extrapolation tools. An essential property of our approach is the capability for uncertainty quantification, allowing for reliable predictions with combined statistical error estimates for many-body and interaction uncertainties. Being solely built upon the physical correlations of different observables, it can be generalized across different ab initio methods. We demonstrate the power of this new extrapolation scheme through a precision study of electric quadrupole moments across a wide range of p-shell nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper extends machine-learning model-space extrapolation methods to electric quadrupole moments and introduces a new approach that predicts converged electromagnetic observables from converged energies and radii by learning correlations between these observables, with the caveat that the full-space prediction networks (FSPNs) slightly overestimate absolute E2 moment values due to limited training data from few-body systems.

## Method

The authors trained artificial neural networks (FSPNs) on fully converged no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations of few-body systems (A ≤ 4) to capture observable-specific convergence patterns, then applied these networks to predict converged E2 moments for heavier p-shell nuclei from small model-space calculations. They also developed observable transcoder networks (OTNs) that learn the correlation between E2 moments and point-proton radii from NCSM data across multiple harmonic oscillator frequencies and model spaces, then predict converged E2 moments using FSPN predictions of converged energies and radii as inputs. The training data for FSPNs used NCSM results for ²H at Nmax up to 50 with multiple chiral interactions, SRG evolutions, and oscillator frequencies, while OTNs were trained separately for each nucleus and state using NCSM data at Nmax = 2 to 12 with oscillator length parameters aHO = 1.2 to 2.4 fm.

## Key numbers

- Training data for FSPNs used NCSM results for ²H up to Nmax = 50 for ℏω = 12, 14, 16, 20, 24, 28, 32 MeV
- Chiral NN interactions at N²LO, N³LO, and N⁴LO' with cutoffs Λ = 450, 500, 550 MeV were used for FSPN training
- SRG evolutions up to α = 0.02, 0.04, 0.08 fm⁴ were employed for FSPN training data
- FSPN architecture: input layer (12 nodes), three hidden layers (48, 48, 24 nodes), output layer (1 node)
- OTN architecture: input layer (2 nodes for energy and radius), two hidden layers (8 nodes each), output layer (1 node)
- OTN training used 10000 epochs with batch size 4 and initial learning rate 0.001
- OTN training considered successful when MSE loss below 0.0001
- 100 OTNs with different random initializations were trained for uncertainty quantification
- 10,000 samples were drawn from FSPN prediction distributions for uncertainty propagation to E2 moments
- Evaluation data used EMN[500] interaction (N³LO, Λ = 500 MeV) with SRG evolution to α = 0.08 fm⁴
- Oscillator length window for evaluation: aHO = 1.2 to 1.8 fm (ℏω = 12.8 to 28.8 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the machine-learning extrapolation method previously applied to energies and radii by Wolfgruber et al. (2024) to electric quadrupole moments, and introduces a new approach that leverages correlations between observables, building on the known correlation between E2 moments and radii exploited by Caprio et al. (2022) and Caprio et al. (2024) in NCSM calculations. The method enables precision predictions of electromagnetic observables with quantified uncertainties from both many-body and interaction sources, and the authors note that the OTN approach can be generalized across different ab initio methods since it relies only on physical correlations between observables rather than method-specific parameters.
