# On the relative importance of second-order terms in relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics

> In Denicol et al., Phys. Rev. D 85, 114047 (2012), the equations of motion of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics were derived from the relativistic Boltzmann equation. These equations contain a multitude of terms of second order in Knudsen number, in inverse Reynolds number, or their product. Terms of second order in Knudsen number give rise to non-hyperbolic (and thus acausal) behavior and must be neglected in (numerical) solutions of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics. The coefficients of the terms which are of the order of the product of Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers have been explicitly computed in the above reference, in the limit of a massless Boltzmann gas. Terms of second order in inverse Reynolds number arise from the collision term in the Boltzmann equation, upon expansion to second order in deviations from the single-particle distribution function in local thermodynamical equilibrium. In this work, we compute these second-order terms for a massless Boltzmann gas with constant scattering cross section. Consequently, we assess their relative importance in comparison to the terms which are of the order of the product of Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers.

## Key claim

For a massless Boltzmann gas with constant scattering cross section in the 14-moment approximation, and under the assumption that Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers are of the same order of magnitude, the second-order terms in inverse Reynolds number (arising from nonlinear collision integral contributions) are an order of magnitude smaller than the first-order product terms of Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers, and can therefore be safely neglected in the equations of motion for the dissipative quantities.

## Method

The authors derived the equations of motion for relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics from the relativistic Boltzmann equation using a systematic power-counting scheme in Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers, following the method of moments developed in Denicol et al. (2012). They computed the coefficients of second-order terms in inverse Reynolds number (the tensors R, R^mu, and R^{mu nu}) for a massless Boltzmann gas with constant scattering cross section in the 14-moment approximation, and compared their magnitudes to the coefficients of terms of first order in the product of Knudsen and inverse Reynolds numbers (the J tensors) that were previously computed in Denicol et al. (2012).

## Key numbers

* The coefficient phi_4 P_0 in the particle diffusion equation (second order in inverse Reynolds number) equals 1/25, while the coefficients of first-order product terms in the same equation are: tau_n = 9/4 lambda_mfp, lambda_nn = 3/5 tau_n, delta_nn = 1 tau_n, ell_n_pi/beta_0 = 1/20 tau_n, tau_n_pi P_0/beta_0 = 1/80 tau_n, and lambda_n_pi/beta_0 = 1/20 tau_n.
* The coefficient phi_7 P_0 in the shear-stress tensor equation (second order in inverse Reynolds number) equals 9/70, while the coefficients of first-order product terms are: tau_pi = 5/3 lambda_mfp, tau_pi_pi = 10/7 tau_pi, delta_pi_pi = 4/3 tau_pi, and phi_8 beta_0^2 P_0 = 8/5.
* The Navier-Stokes coefficients are: kappa/n_0 = 3/16 lambda_mfp for particle diffusion and 2eta/P_0 = 8/3 lambda_mfp for shear stress.
* The coefficients ell_pi_n beta_0, lambda_pi_n beta_0, and tau_pi_n n_0 in the shear-stress tensor equation vanish identically in the 14-moment approximation.

## Context

This work extends the derivation of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics from kinetic theory presented in Denicol et al. (2012), which computed the coefficients of first-order product terms (Knudsen times inverse Reynolds number) but left the second-order inverse Reynolds number terms uncalculated. The results justify neglecting the nonlinear collision integral terms in practical applications of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics to heavy-ion collisions, particularly (far) above the QCD transition in the quark-gluon plasma phase where the velocity of sound is close to c_s^2 = 1/3. The simplified equations of motion derived here provide a computationally tractable form that should be applicable to modeling nuclear collisions, though the authors note that the transport coefficient values may change when accounting for proper quantum statistics, the correct number of degrees of freedom, and more realistic scattering cross sections.
