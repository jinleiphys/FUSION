# Charge Symmetry Breaking Effects on Neutron Beta Decay in Non-Relativistic Quark Models

> A formalism for the study of charge symmetry breaking (CSB) effects is discussed and used to analyze the effects of charge symmetry breaking on neutron beta decay. The effect of including CSB reduces the beta decay matrix element by an amount on the order of $10^{-4}$, a value much larger than the previous estimate. A much smaller contribution due to proton recoil is treated as well, which is found to be on the order of $10^{-6}$. The current uncertainty in the value of $V_{ud}$ is also of order $10^{-4}$. An improvement of that uncertainty by an order of magnitude would require that charge symmetry breaking effects should be included in future analyses.

## Key claim

Charge symmetry breaking (CSB) effects reduce the neutron beta decay matrix element by about \(10^{-4}\), which is approximately 100 times larger than the earlier estimate of \(10^{-6}\) by Behrends and Sirlin. The correction is of the same order as the current uncertainty in \(V_{ud}\), meaning that future improvements in precision will require including CSB effects.

## Method

The authors develop a formalism using first-order perturbation theory in the charge-symmetry-breaking Hamiltonian \(H_1\), applied to non-relativistic quark models with harmonic oscillator confinement. They compute the second-order correction to the matrix element \(\langle p|\tau_+|n\rangle\) by summing over radial excitations of the nucleon, including contributions from the quark mass difference, its effect on kinetic energy and one-gluon exchange, and electromagnetic interactions. Three model parameter sets are used, each constrained to reproduce the neutron-proton mass difference of 1.29 MeV, with varying confinement radius \(\beta\), average quark mass \(\overline{m}\), and strong coupling \(\alpha_s\).

## Key numbers

* The CSB correction to the beta decay matrix element \(\Delta(0)\) is \(4.0297 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 1), \(1.4668 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 2), and \(0.6146 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 3) for zero proton recoil.
* Including the average proton recoil momentum \(\langle Q \rangle = 4.1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ fm}^{-1}\) changes the correction by about \(10^{-6}\), giving \(\Delta(\langle Q \rangle) = 4.0101 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 1), \(1.4500 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 2), and \(0.6005 \times 10^{-4}\) (Model 3).
* The neutron-proton mass difference used to fix model parameters is \(M_n - M_p = 1.29 \text{ MeV}\) (more precisely 1293.333 keV).
* The ratio \(R \equiv ((1|\Delta H|0)/(0|\Delta H|0))^2\) varies between about 6 and 22 across the three models, explaining the enhancement over the earlier estimate.
* The current value of \(V_{ud}\) is \(0.97373 \pm 0.00031\), so the CSB effect is of order the current uncertainty.

## Context

This work extends the original order-of-magnitude estimate by Behrends and Sirlin (1960s), who predicted CSB effects of order \(10^{-6}\) using a schematic estimate based on the ratio \((1.3/940)^2\). The present calculation finds a much larger effect because the individual CSB terms (quark mass difference, kinetic energy, gluon exchange, and electromagnetic) add coherently in the excitation matrix elements, whereas they cancel in the ground-state neutron-proton mass difference. The results enable future analyses of \(V_{ud}\) from neutron beta decay to include CSB corrections, which will become necessary as experimental precision improves by an order of magnitude.
