# Microscopic Study of Spin Transfer in Near-Barrier Nuclear Reactions

> In quasi-fission, it is unclear what the interaction between the relative orbital angular momentum and the spin of the fragments is from a microscopic perspective. In macroscopic approaches, it is expected that the large value of the relative orbital angular momentum, of the order of 100~$\hbar$ is transferred through tangential dissipation to the fragments' intrinsic spin by sliding and rolling friction. The goal is to investigate the angular momentum transfer from the initial relative orbital angular momentum to the fragments' spin. How is the transferred spin shared between the fragments? What is the time scale associated with the different mechanisms? How does deformation play a role? A TDDFT simulation in the TDHF-Skyrme framework is used to describe several reactions at different impact parameters with increasing complexity. A method is proposed to study the evolution of the fragments' total spin as a function of time and angular velocity. The increasingly complex reaction allows for careful analysis of all the mechanisms responsible for the transfer of spin. In particular, it is shown that the transfer of nucleons, and neck formation can significantly affect the transfer of spin through tangential friction. Several mechanisms are in contradiction with previous macroscopic calculations. In particular, the spin of the fragments does not always increase during the collision which prevents it from being used to estimate the collision time of the reaction.

## Key claim

The paper shows that, contrary to macroscopic models which assume spin always increases during collision, the fragments' spin does not always increase during the collision, preventing its use as a simple estimator of collision time; the transfer of nucleons and neck formation significantly affect spin transfer through tangential friction.

## Method

Time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) simulations in the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) framework with the Skyrme functional Sly4d were performed for the reactions ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca, ²⁰⁸Pb+²⁰⁸Pb, ⁴⁰Ca+²⁰⁸Pb, and ⁵⁰Ca+¹⁷⁶Yb at energies above the Coulomb barrier. The evolution of each fragment's total spin as a function of time and angular velocity was computed using a method that separates the system into two fragments by a cutting plane perpendicular to the principal axis of deformation at the minimum density. The LISE code without superfluidity solved the TDHF equations with a Runge-Kutta method at order 4 with time step Δt=0.3 fm/c on a three-dimensional Cartesian grid.

## Key numbers

- For ²⁰⁸Pb+²⁰⁸Pb at E_c.m.=700 MeV and impact parameter b=4 fm: initial relative orbital angular momentum Λ₀=236.9 ℏ, of which 44 ℏ is transferred to fragment spins; the main transfer occurs in approximately 120 fm/c (0.4 zs)
- For ²⁰⁸Pb+²⁰⁸Pb: maximum spin of 22 ℏ per fragment; equilibration occurs when contact time is approximately 1.5 zs
- For ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca at E_c.m.=70 MeV: maximum fragment spin around 3 ℏ; maximum transfer rate of 6 ℏ/zs for b=5 fm and 2.3 ℏ/zs for b=5.2 fm
- For ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca: tangential friction coefficient fit C_Λ(D) ≃ exp(C₀D + C₁) with C₀ = -0.83 fm⁻¹ and C₁ = 10.25 for distances larger than 12 fm
- For ⁴⁰Ca+²⁰⁸Pb at E_c.m.=200 MeV: rolling relaxation time approximately 1 zs, sticking relaxation time approximately 2 zs; ratio τ_M/τ_J ≈ 50 for spin vs mass equilibration
- For ⁵⁰Ca+¹⁷⁶Yb at E_c.m.=172 MeV: ¹⁷⁶Yb has deformation parameter β₂=0.196; maximum heavy fragment spin of 30 ℏ for x-orientation at b≈5.4 fm

## Context

This work extends microscopic TDHF studies of angular momentum dissipation in nuclear reactions, building on the macroscopic friction models of Bass (1980) and Tsang (1974) which proposed sliding and rolling friction mechanisms. The results contradict the macroscopic expectation that fragment spin always increases during collision and that the rolling friction coefficient is an order of magnitude smaller than sliding friction (Bass 1980). The paper enables improved understanding of how to distinguish quasi-fission from fusion-fission reactions as proposed by Vardaci (2020), and provides microscopic benchmarks for macroscopic models of tangential dissipation.
