# Statistical shell model for neutrinoless double $β$-decay nuclear transition matrix elements: Results for $^{76}$Ge, $^{82}$Se, $^{100}$Mo, $^{124}$Sn, $^{130}$Te and $^{136}$Xe

> Statistical shell model (also called spectral distribution method or statistical spectroscopy method) based on random matrix theory and spherical shell model gives a theory for calculating neutrinoless double beta decay nuclear transition matrix elements (NDBD-NTME). This theory is briefly described and then applied to $^{76}$Ge, $^{82}$Se, $^{100}$Mo, $^{124}$Sn,$^{130}$Te and $^{136}$Xe NDBD-NTME. In these calculations, the Bethe's spin-cutoff factor and a bivariate correlation coefficient are varied in a range dictated by random matrix theory and trace propagation. The calculated NDBD-NTME are compared with the results from several other models as available in literature. The statistical shell model results are in general a factor 2 smaller compared to those from the spherical shell model.

## Key claim

The statistical shell model (SSM) yields neutrinoless double beta decay nuclear transition matrix elements (NDBD-NTME) that are in general a factor of 2 smaller than those from the spherical shell model, with the SSM results depending on the chosen range of the bivariate correlation coefficient (zeta between 0.6 and 0.8) and the spin-cutoff factor (sigma_J between 3 and 6).

## Method

The authors apply the statistical shell model (spectral distribution method) based on embedded Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (EGOE) random matrix theory to calculate NDBD-NTME for six candidate nuclei. They compute Gaussian partial densities over proton-neutron configurations using trace propagation for energy centroids and variances, determine ground states via the Ratcliff procedure using experimental reference levels, and then evaluate the squared transition matrix elements using a bivariate Gaussian approximation with the correlation coefficient zeta and the Bethe spin-cutoff factor sigma_J as free parameters varied within ranges dictated by random matrix theory.

## Key numbers

* For $^{76}$Ge: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 1.3-2.5$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=3-4), compared to shell model range 2.66-3.57
* For $^{82}$Se: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 1.4-2.4$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=3-4), compared to shell model range 2.72-3.39
* For $^{100}$Mo: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 0.63-1.1$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=4-5), compared to shell model value 2.4 from Coraggio et al.
* For $^{124}$Sn: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 0.6-0.9$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=4-5), a factor of 2 smaller than shell model values from Horoi et al. and Poves et al.
* For $^{130}$Te: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 0.8-1.3$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=4-5), compared to shell model range 1.79-3.16
* For $^{136}$Xe: SSM gives $M^{0\nu} \sim 1.8-2.5$ (with zeta=0.7-0.75, sigma_J=4-5), compared to shell model range 1.63-2.45

## Context

This work extends the statistical shell method previously used for nuclear level densities and beta-decay rates to the calculation of neutrinoless double beta decay matrix elements, complementing traditional approaches such as the nuclear shell model (by Horoi, Poves, Coraggio and others), QRPA (by Faessler, Simkovic, Suhonen and others), proton-neutron interacting boson model (by Iachello and collaborators), and energy density functional methods (by Rodriguez, Song and others). The SSM results, being systematically a factor of 2 smaller than shell model values, suggest that the method could provide a complementary statistical bound on NDBD-NTME, though the authors note that the results might improve with further refinements to the treatment of the correlation coefficient and spin-cutoff factor.
