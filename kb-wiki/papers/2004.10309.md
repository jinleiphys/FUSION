# Short-range correlation effects on the neutron star cooling

> Short range correlations (SRC) have been known to be an important aspect of nuclear theory for some time. Recent works have re-ignited interest on this topic, particularly due to the fact that it has recently been demonstrated that SRC may be responsible for breaking pairing gaps in nuclear matter. In this work we revisit the concept of SRC for beta equilibrated matter in neutron stars. We construct two equivalent models, with and without SRC and proceed to investigate the thermal evolution of stars described by such models. We show that SRC play a major role in the thermal evolution of neutron stars. It will be shown that while the SRC largely leaves the macroscopic properties of the star unaltered, it significantly alters the proton fraction, thus leading to an early onset of the direct Urca (DU) process, which in turns leads to stars exhibiting much faster cooling.

# Short-range correlation effects on the neutron star cooling

## Key claim
Short-range correlations (SRC) significantly alter the thermal evolution of neutron stars by increasing the proton fraction, which triggers an early onset of the direct Urca process and leads to much faster cooling, even though the macroscopic properties of the star (mass, radius) remain largely unchanged. The authors qualify that this is the first study of this nature as of the time of writing.

## Method
The authors constructed two equivalent relativistic mean-field (RMF) models of beta-equilibrated neutron star matter, one with and one without short-range correlations implemented via a modified momentum distribution function that includes a high-momentum tail proportional to k^{-4}. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain mass-radius profiles and then performed thermal evolution calculations using the general relativistic equations for energy balance and transport, accounting for direct Urca, modified Urca, and Bremsstrahlung neutrino emission processes.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass with SRC: 2.04 solar masses; without SRC: 1.96 solar masses (using coupling constant C=0.005)
- Bulk parameters used to calibrate the model: saturation density rho_0 = 0.15 fm^{-3}, binding energy B_0 = -16.0 MeV, effective mass ratio M*_0/M_nuc = 0.60, incompressibility K_0 = 230 MeV, symmetry energy J = 31.6 MeV, and L_0 = 58.9 MeV
- In the RMF model (without SRC), stars with masses up to approximately 1.6 solar masses exhibit slow cooling; above this mass the direct Urca process turns on
- In the SRC model, all stars allow the direct Urca process, meaning the threshold for DU onset is drastically reduced
- Direct Urca luminosity: approximately 10^{27} (T_9)^6 erg/cm^3/s, where T_9 is temperature in units of 10^9 K
- SRC parameters: C_0 = 0.161, C_1 = -0.25, phi_0 = 2.38, phi_1 = -0.56

## Context
This work extends the SRC phenomenology first implemented in a relativistic mean-field model by Cai (2016), but uses a different coupling constant C=0.005 instead of C=0.01 to ensure the maximum mass agrees with observations of massive pulsars such as PSR J1614-2230 (Demorest, 2010), PSR J0348+0432 (Antoniadis, 2013), and MSP J0740+6620 (Cromartie, 2019). The study demonstrates that SRC effects on cooling can be dramatic even when the equation of state and mass-radius diagrams are largely similar, enabling future observations of neutron star cooling to potentially constrain the intensity of short-range correlations in dense matter.
