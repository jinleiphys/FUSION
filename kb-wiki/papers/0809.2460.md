# Gamma flashes from relativistic electron-positron plasma droplets

## Key claim
Ultra-intense lasers are expected to produce relativistic electron-positron plasma droplets in the near future, and these droplets could serve as interesting sources of gamma ray flashes. The claim is conditional on the assumption that such droplets can be created and equilibrated at temperatures around 10 MeV with sufficient size exceeding the photon mean free path.

## Method
The authors calculate the local photon production rate using complete leading-order QED thermo-field theory, including two-loop processes (Compton scattering, pair annihilation, bremsstrahlung, and off-shell pair annihilation) with proper incorporation of the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal effect. They model the expansion dynamics of a spherical plasma droplet with a linear velocity profile and a prescribed surface acceleration, integrating the emission spectrum over space and time while accounting for photon escape only when the mean free path exceeds the distance to the droplet surface. The temperature evolution is determined by energy conservation among internal energy, kinetic energy of expansion, and emitted photon energy.

## Key numbers
- Initial temperature of the plasma droplet: T0 = 10 MeV
- Initial energy density at T0 = 10 MeV: epsilon(T0) = 11*pi^2*T0^4/60 ≈ 3.8 × 10^29 J/m^3
- Initial droplet radius assumed: R0 = 2 nm, giving initial energy E0 ≈ 13 kJ
- Expansion lasts until temperature cools to 1 MeV, at time tf ∼ (20–30) nm/c
- The photon emission rate formula applies for scaled photon energies y = E_gamma/T in the domain 0.1 ≤ y ≤ 30
- The thermal mass squared of the electron: m_th^2 = e^2*T^2/8, with e^2 = 4*pi*alpha
- The dynamically generated asymptotic mass squared: m_infinity^2 = 2*m_th^2

## Context
This work extends prior studies of relativistic electron-positron plasmas by Thoma (review of properties) and Rafelski (muon/pion production channels), and contrasts with particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations by Shen and Liang for the start-up phase of such plasmas. The authors use state-of-the-art QED photon emissivity from Moore (2001) and Aurenche (2002), rather than the simpler estimates used in earlier work. The results enable future calculations using relativistic hydrodynamics and detailed radiation transport, and suggest that such droplets could produce continuous gamma spectra up to many times the initial temperature, not just the 511 keV line argued by Shen.
