# Fusion Probability in Dinuclear System

> Fusion can be described by the time evolution of a dinuclear system with two degrees of freedom, the relative motion and transfer of nucleons. In the presence of the coupling between two collective modes, we solve the Fokker-Planck equation in a locally harmonic approximation. The potential of a dinuclear system has the quasifission barrier and the inner fusion barrier, and the escape rates can be calculated by the Kramers' model. To estimate the fusion probability, we calculate the quasifission rate and the fusion rate. We investigate the coupling effects on the fusion probability and the cross section of evaporation residue.

## Key claim

The paper claims that coupling between the relative motion and mass asymmetry modes in a dinuclear system, mediated by the inverse mass tensors, modifies the quasifission and fusion escape rates from the Kramers' rate, but only under the condition that the coupling is weak and the Fokker-Planck equation is solved perturbatively to first order.

## Method

The author solves the two-dimensional Fokker-Planck equation for a dinuclear system with collective coordinates of internuclear distance \(R\) and mass asymmetry \(\eta\), using a locally harmonic approximation around the barriers. The coupling between modes through the inverse mass tensors is treated perturbatively, expanding the distribution function in orders of the coupling strength. The first-order correction to the Kramers' escape rate is calculated separately for each coupling term in the Fokker-Planck equation, and the resulting diffusion currents and particle numbers yield modified quasifission and fusion rates.

* As the distance \(s\) increases from \(1.5\ \text{fm}\) for \(E^*=30\ \text{MeV}\) and \(\eta=0.6\), the fusion rates change by \(10\%\) to \(30\%\).

## Context

This work extends the Kramers' model (Kramers, 1940) for escape over a barrier to a two-dimensional dinuclear system, building on prior treatments of fusion as diffusion (FPkramers, othercite2, othercite) and the dinuclear system concept (dns). It enables more quantitative predictions of fusion probabilities and evaporation residue cross sections for asymmetric heavy-ion reactions, particularly by quantifying how coupling between the radial and mass-asymmetry modes alters the quasifission and fusion rates.
