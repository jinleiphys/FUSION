# Probing ab initio emergence of nuclear rotation

> Structural phenomena in nuclei, from shell structure and clustering to superfluidity and collective rotations and vibrations, reflect emergent degrees of freedom. Ab initio theory describes nuclei directly from a fully microscopic formulation. We can therefore look to ab initio theory as a means of exploring the emergence of effective degrees of freedom in nuclei. For the illustrative case of emergent rotational bands in the Be isotopes, we establish an understanding of the underlying oscillator space and angular momentum (orbital and spin) structure. We consider no-core configuration interaction (NCCI) calculations for 7,9,11Be with the Daejeon16 internucleon interaction. Although shell model or rotational degrees of freedom are not assumed in the ab initio theory, the NCCI results are suggestive of the emergence of effective shell model degrees of freedom (0 hbar-omega and 2 hbar-omega excitations) and LS-scheme rotational degrees of freedom, consistent with an Elliott-Wilsdon SU(3) description. These results provide some basic insight into the connection between emergent effective collective rotational and shell model degrees of freedom in these light nuclei and the underlying ab initio microscopic description.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that ab initio no-core configuration interaction (NCCI) calculations for 7,9,11Be with the Daejeon16 interaction produce rotational band structures that are consistent with an Elliott SU(3) description and LS-coupling scheme, suggesting that effective shell model and rotational degrees of freedom emerge naturally from the microscopic theory, though the authors note these are "suggestive of" rather than definitive proof of such emergence.

## Method

The authors performed no-core configuration interaction (NCCI) calculations for 7,9,11Be using the Daejeon16 internucleon interaction, varying the basis truncation parameter Nmax from 4 to 14 depending on the isotope. They analyzed the resulting wave functions through decompositions in oscillator space (0hw, 2hw, etc. contributions) and orbital angular momentum (LS) decompositions, and identified rotational bands from energy level patterns and enhanced E2 transition strengths. For 7Be, they additionally performed symplectic no-core configuration interaction (SpNCCI) calculations to examine the SU(3) and symplectic group structure of the wave functions.

## Key numbers

- The experimental 1/2+ ground state of 11Be lies 0.320 MeV below the 1/2- lowest negative parity level, and this parity inversion is reproduced in the NCCI calculations with Daejeon16
- The negative parity ground state band in 11Be has a rotational parameter A ≈ 0.6 MeV, while the "long" K^P=3/2- band has A ≈ 0.3 MeV, indicating an approximate doubling of the moment of inertia
- The positive parity ground state band in 11Be has A ≈ 0.4 MeV and the K^P=5/2+ excited band has A ≈ 0.25 MeV
- The K^P=1/2- excited band in 9Be shows Coriolis staggering consistent with zero (a ≲ 0.1), while the K^P=1/2+ band shows pronounced staggering (a ≈ 2)
- For 7Be, the excited K^P=1/2- band members lie within the symplectic irrep built on the ground state band at the level of ~50%-80% of their norm
- The 7Be excited band members show a crossing of the 5/2- states at Nmax=10, where E2 strengths indicate significant two-state mixing

## Context

This work extends prior NCCI calculations of rotational structure in Be isotopes by Maris (2015) and Caprio (2015) using the JISP16 and NNLOopt interactions, now employing the Daejeon16 interaction which shows more rapid convergence. The analysis connects to the Elliott SU(3) description of nuclear rotation and to symplectic Sp(3,R) dynamical symmetry as developed by Rosensteel (1977) and Rowe (1985). The results are consistent with antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (AMD) calculations by Kanada-En'yo (2012) describing cluster molecular orbitals in 11Be, and with the cluster molecular orbital description of 7Be discussed by Inglis (1953). The work enables future investigation of how collective rotational and vibrational degrees of freedom emerge from microscopic theory, particularly for heavier and more strongly bound systems.
