# The role of the in-medium four-quark condensates revised

> We calculate the nucleon self-energies in nuclear matter in the QCD sum rules approach, taking into account the contributions of the four-quark condensates. We analyze the dependence of the results on the model employed for the calculation of the condensates and demonstrate that the relativistic character of the models is important. The condensates are calculated with inclusion of the most important terms beyond the gas approximation. This corresponds to inclusion of the two-body nucleon forces and of the most important three-body forces. The results are consistent with the convergence of the operator product expansion. The density dependence of the nucleon self-energies is obtained. The results are consistent with those obtained by the standard nuclear physics methods, thus inspiring further development of the approach.

# The role of the in-medium four-quark condensates revised

## Key claim

The nucleon self-energies in nuclear matter computed via QCD sum rules with four-quark condensates depend strongly on whether the quark model used to calculate the condensates is relativistic or nonrelativistic; the matter appears bound at saturation density only if the nucleon matrix element κ_N = ⟨N|ūu+dd̄|N⟩ is large enough, and the results are consistent with standard nuclear physics methods only when relativistic effects are included.

## Method

The authors compute the nucleon vector self-energy Σ_V and effective mass m* in symmetric nuclear matter using QCD sum rules, including contributions from four-quark condensates of dimension d=6. The condensates are evaluated in the Perturbative Chiral Quark Model (PCQM), where the nucleon consists of three valence constituent quarks and a perturbative pion cloud, with the gas approximation as the baseline and inclusion of the most important nonlinear terms (two-body and three-body forces) beyond it. The four-quark condensates are separated into factorized terms (where two quark operators act on the vacuum and two on the nucleon), internal terms (all four operators act on valence quarks), and interference terms (operators act on both valence quarks and pions), and their contributions to the polarization operator are summed to solve the Borel-transformed sum rules equations for the nucleon self-energies.

## Key numbers

- The vacuum quark condensate ⟨0|q̄q|0⟩ ≈ (−241 MeV)^3, used as the scale a/(2π)^2 = ε_0^3 with ε_0 = 241 MeV.
- The characteristic nucleon size R = (0.618 ± 0.006) fm in the PCQM, giving the internal normalization N² = 1.37×10⁻³ GeV³ = 0.098 ε_0³.
- The PCQM relativistic parameter β = 0.39, fitted to reproduce the axial coupling constant.
- In the gas approximation, inclusion of four-quark condensates subtracts more than 100 MeV from both the vector self-energy Σ_V and the nucleon effective mass m* at saturation density.
- For the scalar channel, factorized terms determine about 80 percent of the total contribution.
- The PCQM valence quark contribution to the scalar condensate is J = 0.54, about half the nonrelativistic value J = 1.
- In the nonrelativistic quark model (NRQM) with R = 0.72 fm, the coefficients for symmetric matter are A^q = 1.15, A^P = −0.54, A^I = 1.45, while in the PCQM the total coefficients are A^q = −0.10, A^P = −0.88, A^I = 1.80 (for α = 0).
- The PCQM internal contributions for 4u condensates include A^q_S = −0.05, A^q_V = 0.01, A^P_V = −0.13, A^P_T = −0.09 (all for α = 0).
- For 2u2d condensates, internal contributions include A^q_V = −0.80, A^P_V = −0.41, A^I_AT = 0.77.

## Context

This work extends the QCD sum rules approach to nucleons in nuclear matter originally developed by Shifman, Vainshtein, and Zakharov (1979) for mesons and by Ioffe (1981) for nucleons, and later applied to nuclear matter by Drukarev and Levin (1990s). It revises earlier calculations by Drukarev, Ryskin, and Sadovnikova (2002) and by the PCQM group (Gutsche et al., 2002) by including previously overlooked axial-tensor condensate contributions and by systematically separating factorized, internal, and interference terms. The results contradict the nonrelativistic quark model predictions and show that relativistic treatment of the constituent quarks is essential for obtaining bound nuclear matter. The approach enables further calculations with more complete inclusion of three-body forces and can be extended to asymmetric nuclear matter.
