# Quality of Variational Trial States

> Besides perturbation theory (which clearly requires the knowledge of the exact unperturbed solution), variational techniques represent the main tool for any investigation of the eigenvalue problem of some semibounded operator H in quantum theory. For a reasonable choice of the employed trial subspace of the domain of H, the lowest eigenvalues of H usually can be located with acceptable precision whereas the trial-subspace vectors corresponding to these eigenvalues approximate, in general, the exact eigenstates of H with much less accuracy. Accordingly, various measures for the accuracy of the approximate eigenstates derived by variational techniques are scrutinized. In particular, the matrix elements of the commutator of the operator H and (suitably chosen) different operators with respect to degenerate approximate eigenstates of H obtained by variational methods are proposed as new criteria for the accuracy of variational eigenstates. These considerations are applied to precisely that Hamiltonian for which the eigenvalue problem defines the well-known spinless Salpeter equation. This bound-state wave equation may be regarded as (the most straightforward) relativistic generalization of the usual nonrelativistic Schroedinger formalism, and is frequently used to describe, e.g., spin-averaged mass spectra of bound states of quarks.

## Key claim

Variational techniques yield upper bounds on eigenvalues with acceptable precision, but the corresponding trial states approximate the exact eigenstates with much less accuracy; the paper proposes that the matrix elements of the commutator of the Hamiltonian with suitably chosen operators, evaluated with respect to degenerate approximate eigenstates, serve as new criteria for judging the quality of variational eigenstates, without requiring knowledge of the exact solution.

## Method

The authors study the Rayleigh–Ritz variational method applied to the spinless Salpeter equation (a semirelativistic bound-state equation) with a harmonic-oscillator potential. They use "Laguerre" trial states with fixed variational parameters (μ = m = 2 GeV, β = 1) and increase the trial-space dimension d from 1 to 25. For each dimension, they compute the variational upper bound on the energy, the overlap with the exact eigenstate, the expectation value of the virial operator (derived from the commutator with the dilation generator), and the maximum pointwise relative error in momentum space, then compare these to the exact solution obtained by numerical integration of the equivalent nonrelativistic Schrödinger equation.

## Key numbers

- For the 1S ground state with m = 2 GeV and a = 2 GeV³, the exact energy E = 3.82493 GeV; the variational upper bound with d=1 is 4.21624 GeV (relative error ε = 0.1023), with d=2 is 3.92759 GeV (ε = 0.0268), and with d=25 is 3.82494 GeV (ε ≈ 0).
- For the 1P state (ℓ=1), exact energy E = 4.90145 GeV; variational upper bound with d=1 is 6.50936 GeV (ε = 0.3280), with d=2 is 5.24154 GeV (ε = 0.0694), with d=25 is 4.90149 GeV (ε ≈ 0).
- For the 1D state (ℓ=2), exact energy E = 5.89675 GeV; variational upper bound with d=1 is 9.77866 GeV (ε = 0.6583), with d=2 is 7.18242 GeV (ε = 0.2180), with d=25 is 5.89681 GeV (ε ≈ 0).
- The normalized virial expectation value ν for the 1S state: with d=1, ν = -0.6120; with d=2, ν = +0.0308; with d=25, ν = 0.
- The deviation from unity of the squared overlap σ for the 1S state: with d=1, σ = 0.09618; with d=2, σ = 0.02375; with d=25, σ = 0.
- The nonrelativistic upper bound on the 1S energy is E_NR = 4.12132 GeV; the zero-mass lower bound is E(m=0) = 2.94583 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior systematic studies of relativistic virial theorems (Lucha 1990) and analytic upper bounds for the spinless Salpeter equation (Lucha 1994, 1996, 1998). It provides a practical, self-contained quality measure (the commutator expectation value) that does not require the exact solution, unlike overlap or pointwise error measures. The results enable more reliable variational calculations for semirelativistic bound-state problems, such as quarkonium mass spectra, by offering a convergence diagnostic that can be computed entirely within the variational framework.
