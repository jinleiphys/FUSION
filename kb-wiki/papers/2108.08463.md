# Comparison of simulated neutrino emission models with data on Supernova 1987A

> We compare models of supernova (SN) neutrino emission with the Kamiokande II data on SN 1987A using the Bayesian approach. These models are taken from simulations and are representative of current 1D SN models. We find that models with a brief accretion phase of neutrino emission are the most favored. This result is not affected by varying the overall flux normalization or considering neutrino oscillations. We also check the compatibility of the best-fit models with the data.

## Key claim

Models of supernova neutrino emission with a brief accretion phase (such as the 9.6 solar mass progenitor model) are the most favored when compared to the Kamiokande II data on SN 1987A using a Bayesian approach, but this preference is conditional on the specific set of models considered and is not affected by varying the overall flux normalization or including neutrino oscillations.

## Method

The authors compared three baseline 1D supernova neutrino emission models from the Garching group (models A, B, and C, with progenitor masses of 9.6, 20, and 27 solar masses respectively) against the eleven neutrino events observed by the Kamiokande II detector from SN 1987A. They used a Bayesian approach to calculate posterior probabilities and Bayes factors for each model, also considering variants with scaled flux normalizations (models A', B', C') and three cases of neutrino oscillations (no oscillations, normal hierarchy, and inverted hierarchy), for a total of eighteen models. Compatibility of the best-fit models with the data was checked using a frequentist p-value test with 10^6 simulated samples.

## Key numbers

- Model A (9.6 solar mass progenitor, SFHo equation of state) has total emitted neutrino energy 1.99 x 10^53 ergs; model B (20 solar mass, SFHo) has 4.28 x 10^53 ergs; model C (27 solar mass, LS220) has 3.30 x 10^53 ergs
- Bayes factor for model A over model B is approximately 34-65 (strong evidence), while for model A over model C it is approximately 2.2-5.4 (hardly noticeable to positive evidence)
- Best-fit distance for models A, B, and C is 51.39-51.45 kpc, consistent with the measured central value of 51.4 kpc with standard deviation 1.2 kpc
- Best-fit time offset for all models ranges from 0.024 to 0.054 seconds
- Best-fit scale factor K for model A' ranges from 1.15 to 1.32 depending on oscillation case, corresponding to total neutrino energy approximately (2.3-2.6) x 10^53 ergs
- Best-fit scale factor K for models B' and C' is approximately 0.46-0.61, corresponding to total neutrino energy approximately 2.0 x 10^53 ergs
- Expected total number of events for best-fit model A (NO) is 6.81; for model A' (NO) is 8.90
- p-values for best-fit models A', B', C' range from 0.12 to 0.39, all exceeding 0.05 threshold for rejection

## Context

This work extends the approach of Loredo and Lamb (2002) and Costantini et al. (2007), who used parametric models of supernova neutrino emission, by instead comparing detailed 1D simulation outputs directly with the SN 1987A data. The results favor models with brief accretion phases, consistent with a light progenitor for SN 1987A, and demonstrate that even with only eleven events, differentiation among neutrino emission models is possible. This analysis can be extended to other models not considered here, such as those from Nagakura et al., and the methods will be applicable to future galactic supernovae, which are expected to produce approximately 10^4 inverse beta decay events in Super-Kamiokande.
