# Missing levels in intermediate spectra

> We derive an expression for the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution $P(s)$ of the energy levels of quantum systems with intermediate dynamics between regularity and chaos and missing levels due to random experimental errors. The expression is based on the Brody distribution, the most widely used for fitting mixed spectra as a function of one parameter. By using Monte Carlo simulations of intermediate spectra based on the $β$-Hermite ensemble of Random Matrix Theory, we evaluate the quality of the formula and its suitability for fitting purposes. Estimations of the Brody parameter and the fraction of missing levels can be obtained by a least-square two-parameter fitting of the experimental $P(s)$. The results should be important to distinguish the origins of deviations from RMT in experimental spectra.

## Key claim

The authors derive a two-parameter formula for the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution that can simultaneously estimate the Brody parameter (degree of chaos) and the fraction of observed levels in a spectrum, but the abstract glosses over the condition that the formula is only reliable when the fraction of missing levels is not very large and when the dynamics are not too close to regularity.

## Method

The authors start from the Brody distribution for intermediate spectra and use the Bohigas-Pato formula relating the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution of an incomplete spectrum to the higher-order spacing distributions of the complete spectrum. They compute the first three higher-order spacing distributions from a generalization of the Brody formula by Abul-Magd and Simbel, and approximate all higher-order distributions (n greater than or equal to 3) with Gaussian distributions whose standard deviations are obtained from spline interpolation of values from spectra with different Brody parameters. The resulting two-parameter formula for P(s) is then tested against Monte Carlo simulations of the beta-Hermite random matrix ensemble, which provides a continuous transition from Poisson (beta=0) to GOE (beta=1) statistics.

## Key numbers

- The formula was tested using ensembles of 1000 beta-Hermite matrices of dimension N=10000, equivalent to spectra of dimension 10^7.
- For the joint distribution of fitted parameters, ensembles of 10000 spectra of dimension N=1000 were used.
- The chi-squared values between the formula and beta-ensemble results range from 0.0014 (for f=1, q=0.9) to 0.11 (for f=1, q=0.2), with most values below 0.1.
- For the parametrization of b_2, the authors give b_2(q) = 1/(1 + 6.7q + 1.3q^2 + 51q^3) for q values from 0.1 to 1.0.
- For the parametrization of b_1, the authors give b_1(q) = 1/(1 + 2.7q + 3.5q^2).

## Context

This work extends the line of research by Bohigas and Pato (1980s) on missing levels in chaotic spectra and by Abul-Magd and Simbel (2000) on higher-order spacing distributions for the Brody distribution. It addresses the gap that previous tools could either determine the degree of chaos assuming a complete spectrum (Brody, 1973; Izrailev, 1989) or estimate missing levels assuming a chaotic spectrum (Bohigas and Pato; Molina, 2007; Mulhall, 2011; Casal, 2021), but could not obtain both parameters simultaneously. The formula enables experimentalists to distinguish whether deviations from random matrix theory in their spectra arise from mixed dynamics, missing levels, or both.
