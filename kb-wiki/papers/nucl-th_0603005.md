# Giant halo in relativistic and non-relativistic approaches

> The phenomena of giant halo and halo of neutron-rich even-Ca isotopes are investigated and compared in the framework of the relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) and non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations. With two parameter sets for each of the RCHB and the Skyrme HFB calculations, it is found that although halo phenomena exist for Ca isotopes near neutron drip line in both calculations, the halo of the Skyrme HFB calculations starts at a more neutron-rich nucleus than that of the RCHB calculations, and the RCHB calculations have larger neutron root-mean-square (rms) radii systematically in N >= 40 than those of the Skyrme HFB calculations. The former difference comes from difference in shell structure. The reasons for the latter can be partly explained by the neutron 3s1/2 orbit, which causes more than 50% of the difference among the four calculations for neutron rms radii at 66Ca.

# Giant halo in relativistic and non-relativistic approaches

## Key claim
Halo phenomena exist for Ca isotopes near the neutron drip line in both relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) and non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations, but the halo in the Skyrme HFB calculations starts at a more neutron-rich nucleus than in the RCHB calculations, and the RCHB calculations systematically yield larger neutron root-mean-square (rms) radii for N >= 40. The difference in halo onset arises from differences in shell structure, while more than 50% of the difference in neutron rms radii at 66Ca can be attributed to the neutron 3s1/2 orbit.

## Method
The authors performed relativistic continuum Hartree-Bogoliubov (RCHB) calculations with the NL-SH and PK1 parameter sets and non-relativistic Skyrme Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the SkM* and SLy4 parameter sets for neutron-rich even-Ca isotopes up to the neutron drip line. Both calculations used a coordinate representation with a box size of 20 fm, a mesh size of 0.1 fm, and quasiparticle states up to 120 MeV, with a surface-type delta pairing interaction. The strength of the pairing interaction was determined by reproducing the pairing energy of the RCHB calculations (8.2 MeV at 66Ca) for consistency across approaches.

## Key numbers
- Two-neutron drip line: RCHB calculations predict N=50 (PK1) or N=52 (NL-SH); Skyrme HFB calculations predict N=46 (SLy4) or N=58 (SkM*)
- Experimental binding energy per nucleon at N=30: 8.550 MeV; at N=32: 8.396 +/- 0.013 MeV (RCHB calculations reproduce these well)
- Experimental two-neutron separation energy at N=32: 9.081 +/- 0.699 MeV; at N=30: 11.499 +/- 0.008 MeV (SLy4 gives a value very close to the measured one at N=30)
- Number of nucleons in the positive-energy region (N_h) for 62-72Ca: 0.6-2.2 (average 1.7) in NL-SH calculation; corresponding value for SkM* is always smaller than 0.5 (average 0.27)
- When there is no halo, two additional neutrons increase neutron rms radius by 0.05 fm; in halo nuclei, the increase rises to 0.11 fm
- At 66Ca: neutron rms radius = 4.363 fm (PK1), 4.314 fm (NL-SH), 4.205 fm (SLy4), 4.210 fm (SkM*)
- Neutron 3s1/2 orbit at 66Ca: single-particle energy = 0.133 MeV (PK1), 0.640 MeV (NL-SH), 1.939 MeV (SLy4), 1.445 MeV (SkM*); rms radius = 8.144 fm (PK1), 7.240 fm (NL-SH), 6.856 fm (SLy4), 5.765 fm (SkM*); occupation probability = 0.239 (PK1), 0.089 (NL-SH), 0.016 (SLy4), 0.010 (SkM*)
- The 3s1/2 orbit contributes 54% (0.082 fm) of the difference between PK1 and SkM* neutron rms radii (0.153 fm) at 66Ca

## Context
This work extends earlier predictions of giant halo phenomena in Ca and Zr isotopes using relativistic approaches (Meng 1998, Meng 2002) by providing the first systematic comparison with non-relativistic Skyrme HFB calculations. The study builds on prior surveys of halo signatures in proton-magic nuclei (Zhang 2003) and addresses the need for direct comparison between relativistic and non-relativistic frameworks that was previously missing in the literature. The results enable further investigation of how the choice of pairing interaction (surface-type versus volume-type) and the treatment of the continuum affect predictions of halo structure near the neutron drip line, and highlight the sensitivity of the neutron 3s1/2 orbit to the details of the central and pairing potentials.
