# Poisson bracket operator

> We introduce the Poisson bracket operator which is an alternative quantum counterpart of the Poisson bracket. This operator is defined using the operator derivative formulated in quantum analysis and is equivalent to the Poisson bracket in the classical limit. Using this, we derive the quantum canonical equation which describes the time evolution of operators. In the standard applications of quantum mechanics, the quantum canonical equation is equivalent to the Heisenberg equation. At the same time, this equation is applicable to c-number canonical variables and then coincides with the canonical equation in classical mechanics. Therefore the Poisson bracket operator enables us to describe classical and quantum behaviors in a unified way. Moreover, the quantum canonical equation is applicable to non-standard system where the Heisenberg equation is not defined. As an example, we consider the application to the system where a c-number and a q-number particles coexist. The derived dynamics satisfies the Ehrenfest theorem and the energy and momentum conservations.

## Key claim

The Poisson bracket operator provides an alternative quantum counterpart of the Poisson bracket that, unlike the standard commutator, has a clear classical correspondence because the operator derivative reduces to the standard derivative in the classical limit; the quantum canonical equation derived from it is equivalent to the Heisenberg equation for standard quantum systems but remains applicable to systems where the Heisenberg equation is not defined, such as those mixing c-number and q-number variables.

## Method

The author defines the Poisson bracket operator using the operator derivative from quantum analysis, where the derivative of a function of operators is expressed as an integral involving the commutator with the operator. For canonical variables satisfying a c-number commutation relation, the Poisson bracket operator is shown to be proportional to the commutator. The quantum canonical equation for time evolution is then derived and applied to a system where two pairs of canonical variables satisfy different commutation relations, specifically a c-number and a q-number particle coexisting.

## Key numbers

- The commutation relation for canonical operators is given by \([\widehat{A},\widehat{B}] = c\), where \(c\) is a c-number (no specific value given).
- For standard quantum mechanics, the canonical commutation relation is \([\widehat{A}_i(t),\widehat{B}_j(t)] = i\hbar \,\delta_{ij}\).
- The relation between commutator and Poisson bracket operator is \([f,g] = c \{f,g\}_{(\widehat{A},\widehat{B})}\).
- For the many-body generalization with \(N\) pairs, the commutator is \(\sum_{i=1}^N c_i \{f,g\}_{(\widehat{A}_i,\widehat{B}_i)}\).
- In the example of a single-particle system with Hamiltonian \(\widehat{H} = \widehat{p}_t^2/(2m) + V(\widehat{x}_t)\), the quantum canonical equation expansion includes terms with \(\hbar\) and higher-order derivatives of the potential \(V^{(m+1)}(\widehat{x}_t)\).

## Context

This work extends the program of quantum analysis developed by Suzuki (2000s) and provides an alternative to the standard canonical quantization where the commutator replaces the Poisson bracket. Unlike the phase-space approach using the Wigner-Weyl transformation and the Moyal bracket (deformation quantization), the Poisson bracket operator maintains a direct derivative structure. The formalism enables unified treatment of classical and quantum dynamics and suggests connections to the Lindblad equation for open quantum systems, as the diffusion equation quantized via operator derivatives reproduces the Lindblad form.
