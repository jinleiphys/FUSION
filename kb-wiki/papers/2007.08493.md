# GW190814 as a massive rapidly-rotating neutron star with exotic degrees of freedom

> In the context of the massive secondary object recently observed in the compact-star merger GW190814, we investigate the possibility of producing massive neutron stars from a few different equation of state models that contain exotic degrees of freedom, such as hyperons and quarks. Our work shows that state-of-the-art relativistic mean field models can generate massive stars reaching $\gtrsim 2.05\,\Msun$, while being in good agreement with gravitational-wave events and x-ray pulsar observations, when quark vector interactions and non-standard self-vector interactions are introduced. In particular, we present a new version of the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model in which a different quark-deconfinement potential allows for stable stars with a pure quark core. When rapid rotation is considered, our models generate stellar masses that approach, and in some cases surpass $2.5\,\Msun$. We find that in such cases fast rotation does not necessarily suppress exotic degrees of freedom due to changes in stellar central density, but require a larger amount of baryons than what is allowed in the non-rotating stars. This is not the case for pure quark stars, which can easily reach $2.5\,\Msun$ and still possess approximately the same amount of baryons as stable non-rotating stars. We also briefly discuss possible origins for fast rotating stars with a large amount of baryons and their stability, showing how the event GW190814 can be associated with a star containing quarks as one of its progenitors.

## Key claim

The paper shows that rapidly rotating neutron stars containing exotic degrees of freedom (hyperons and quarks) can reach masses approaching and surpassing 2.5 solar masses, potentially explaining the GW190814 secondary object, but only when the star contains a larger number of baryons than allowed in non-rotating configurations; pure quark stars can reach this mass with approximately the same baryon content as stable non-rotating stars.

## Method

The authors employed several relativistic mean-field equation of state models, including a new version of the Chiral Mean Field (CMF) model with modified quark-deconfinement potential and vector interactions, a Many-body Forces (MBF) model matched with a vector-enhanced Bag (vBag) model via Maxwell construction, and a pure vBag model for quark stars. Rapid rotation effects were incorporated using the RNS code, which numerically solves the full Einstein equations for rotating perfect fluid equilibria, and stability was assessed using the turning-point criterion for secular stability.

## Key numbers

- Maximum static mass of 2.08 solar masses for the CMF model with hadrons only and omega^4 interaction included
- Maximum static mass of 2.07 solar masses for the CMF model with quarks and omega^4 interaction (late deconfinement)
- Maximum static mass of 2.07 solar masses for the CMF model with quarks and omega^6 interaction (late deconfinement)
- Maximum rotating hybrid star mass of 2.474 solar masses at 1.38 kHz for CMF with omega^4 and late deconfinement
- Maximum rotating hybrid star mass of 2.455 solar masses at 1.39 kHz for CMF with omega^6 and late deconfinement
- Maximum rotating mass of 2.587 solar masses at 1.34 kHz for MBF + vBag model
- Maximum rotating mass of 3.189 solar masses at 1.58 kHz for pure vBag model
- Tidal deformability Lambda = 730 for 1.4 solar mass star in CMF with omega^4 (with quarks)
- Tidal deformability Lambda = 144 for 1.4 solar mass star in pure vBag model
- Radius of 11.1 km for 1.4 solar mass star in pure vBag model

## Context

This work extends previous studies of massive neutron stars containing exotic matter, addressing the tension noted by Sedrakian (2020) and Li (2020) that hyperons and heavier resonances act against interpreting GW190814 as a neutron star. The authors show that with appropriate vector interactions, hybrid stars with hyperons and quarks can reach the required masses, contrary to earlier findings by Shahrbaf (2019), Dhiman (2007), and others who found only nucleonic rotating stars could reach masses above 2.5 solar masses. The results enable future dynamical stellar simulations and cooling calculations to test whether such rapidly rotating exotic-matter stars could be progenitors of events like GW190814.
