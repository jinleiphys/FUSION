# Nucleon-Nucleon and Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Models for Energies to 3 GeV and the Question of NN Hadronization

> Within the key issues of hadronic physics one of the interesting issues in nuclear physics is whether there is a transition region between meson-nucleon and quark-gluon degrees of freedom in the NN interaction. This question is relevant for pairs of free nucleons as well as for nucleon pairs immersed in nuclear matter. From NN phase shifts we deduce a dibaryonic scale of 1 GeV for the soft core NN potential strengths at nucleon separation r equal 0.25 to 0.5 fm. A short range intermediate transition, with fusion and fission of the two scattered nucleons into a dibaryon with prevailing quark-gluon dynamics, is conjectured from NN optical models for T-lab greater 1.5 GeV. From efforts and progress of nucleon-nucleus scattering analysis in the GeV region some results are presented. This is our first step for an in-medium search for transitions from the meson-nucleon into the quark-gluon sector using NA optical models.

# Nucleon-Nucleon and Nucleon-Nucleus Optical Models for Energies to 3 GeV and the Question of NN Hadronization

## Key claim

The authors conjecture a short-range intermediate transition in which two scattered nucleons fuse into a dibaryon with prevailing quark-gluon dynamics for laboratory kinetic energies above 1.5 GeV, based on NN optical model analyses; however, this transition is presented as a conjecture from the model fits rather than a directly observed phenomenon, and the evidence is limited to low partial waves (¹S₀ and ³P₀,₁) in the energy interval 1.5 to 2.5 GeV.

## Method

The authors used Gel'fand–Levitan–Marchenko quantum inverse scattering theory to generate complex NN optical model potentials from GWU/SP00 phase shift analyses up to 3 GeV, adding channel-dependent complex separable potentials with energy-dependent strengths to reference potentials from Paris, Nijmegen, and Argonne. They then applied these NN optical models in a relativistically corrected full-folding optical model in momentum space to calculate nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering for targets ¹⁶O, ⁴⁰Ca, ⁹⁰Zr, and ²⁰⁸Pb at laboratory energies from 0.5 to 1.5 GeV.

## Key numbers

- A dibaryonic scale of 1 GeV is deduced for the soft core NN potential strengths at nucleon separation distances between 0.25 and 0.5 fm.
- Gaussian fits to the inversion potentials yield robust range parameters: r₀(¹S₀) = 0.46 fm, r₀(³P₀) = 0.66 fm, and r₀(³P₁) = 0.63 fm.
- The imaginary absorptive potential is 25% stronger in the p-channels than in the s-channel.
- Best-fit static Gaussian potential parameters for the ¹S₀ channel (power n=2) give r₀ = 0.48 fm, V = 948 MeV, and W = -117 MeV.
- Best-fit static Gaussian potential parameters for the ³P₀ channel (power n=1) give r₀ = 0.54 fm, V = 1037 MeV, and W = -126 MeV.
- Best-fit static Gaussian potential parameters for the ³P₁ channel (power n=1) give r₀ = 0.50 fm, V = 1029 MeV, and W = -171 MeV.
- The overlap of nucleon charge form factors is 80% for the ¹S₀ channel (r₀ = 0.45 fm) and 60% for the ³P₀,₁ channels (r₀ = 0.65 fm).
- The opening of NN inelasticities becomes visible at T_Lab approximately 400 MeV and contributes about 20% of the total and reaction cross sections at energies between 1 and 1.5 GeV for all isotopes studied.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous nonrelativistic full-folding optical model (Arellano 1995) by incorporating relativistic kinematic corrections and NN optical model potentials to reach projectile energies up to 1.5 GeV, whereas earlier studies using one-boson-exchange real NN interactions were restricted to energies below 350 MeV. The paper builds on prior inversion potential work (von Geramb 1998, Funk 2001) and phase shift analyses from the GWU group (2000), and it aims to search for evidence of a transition from meson-nucleon to quark-gluon degrees of freedom in both free NN scattering and nucleon pairs immersed in nuclear matter. The results enable microscopic NA optical model calculations for applied nuclear technologies including accelerator transmutation of waste, accelerator-based conversion, accelerator-driven energy production, and accelerator production of tritium, as referenced by Kalinowski (1996), Dubbers (1997), Maschek (1998), and Riplinger (1998).
