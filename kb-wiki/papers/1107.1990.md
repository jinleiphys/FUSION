# Electrical conductivity of quark matter in magnetic field

> Fermion currents in dense quark matter embedded into magnetic field are under intense discussions motivated by Chiral Magnetic Effect. We argue that conductivity of quark matter may be independent of the magnetic field direction and not proportional to the magnetic field strength.

# Electrical conductivity of quark matter in magnetic field

## Key claim
The authors argue that the quantum contribution to the electrical conductivity of quark matter in a magnetic field may be independent of the magnetic field direction and only weakly dependent on the field strength, contrary to expectations from the Chiral Magnetic Effect. This result is conditional on the specific regime where the magnetic length is much larger than both the mean free path and the phase-randomizing length.

## Method
The authors apply the Kubo formula to compute the electrical conductivity of quark matter, treating the system as a disordered medium with chaotically distributed scatterers characterized by a momentum relaxation time. They separate the conductivity into a classical Drude (Boltzmann) contribution and a quantum correction, then introduce a magnetic field via the Landau level formalism and integrate over momenta up to the inverse mean free path to obtain the quantum contribution in the presence of magnetic field and phase-breaking interactions.

## Key numbers
- Quark chemical potential taken as μ ≈ 0.4 GeV, with Fermi momentum p_F ≈ μ
- Mean free path l ≈ 0.5–1 fm
- Quantum correction σ_q ≥ 0.5 σ_cl for these parameters
- Magnetic field at RHIC |eB| ∼ m_π² ∼ 2 × 10⁴ MeV²
- Weak field regime defined as |eB| ≪ 10⁴ MeV², where quantum conductivity does not feel the magnetic field
- In the weak field limit, σ_q = −(e²/π²)(1/l − 1/l_φ)
- For stronger fields, the difference σ_q^> − σ_q^< ∼ √(eB)

## Context
This work extends methods from condensed matter physics (disordered systems and weak localization) to the problem of quark matter conductivity in magnetic fields, which is relevant for understanding the Chiral Magnetic Effect observed by the STAR collaboration. The approach patterns on the method developed in condensed matter physics (referred to as work5 in the text) and relates to hydrodynamic approaches to strongly coupled CFT (work6). The results are important for determining the relaxation time of the magnetic field created in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, and suggest that the quantum correction to conductivity may be significant enough that the term "correction" is no longer meaningful in the quark matter context.
