# Impacts of the $^{12}\rm{C}\left(α,γ\right)^{16}\!\rm{O}$ reaction rate on $^{56}{\rm Ni}$ nucleosynthesis in pair-instability supernovae

> Nuclear reactions are key to our understanding of stellar evolution, particularly the $^{12}\rm{C}\left(α,γ\right)^{16}\!\rm{O}$ rate, which is known to significantly influence the lower and upper ends of the black hole (BH) mass distribution due to pair-instability supernovae (PISNe). However, these reaction rates have not been sufficiently determined. We use the $\texttt{MESA}$ stellar evolution code to explore the impact of uncertainty in the $^{12}\rm{C}\left(α,γ\right)^{16}\!\rm{O}$ rate on PISN explosions, focusing on nucleosynthesis and explosion energy by considering the high resolution of the initial mass. Our findings show that the mass of synthesized radioactive nickel ($^{56}{\rm Ni}$) and the explosion energy increase with $^{12}\rm{C}\left(α,γ\right)^{16}\!\rm{O}$ rate for the same initial mass, except in the high-mass edge region. With a high (about twice the $\texttt{STARLIB}$ standard value) rate, the maximum amount of nickel produced falls below 70 $M_\odot$, while with a low rate (about half of the standard value) it increases up to 83.9 $M_\odot$. These results highlight that carbon "preheating" plays a crucial role in PISNe by determining core concentration when a star initiates expansion. Our results also suggest that the onset of the expansion, which means the end of compression, competes with collapse caused by helium photodisintegration, and the maximum mass that can lead to an explosion depends on the $^{12}\rm{C}\left(α,γ\right)^{16}\!\rm{O}$ reaction rate.

## Key claim

The mass of synthesized radioactive nickel (⁵⁶Ni) and the explosion energy in pair-instability supernovae (PISNe) increase with the ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O reaction rate for a given initial helium core mass, except near the high-mass edge of the explosion regime; however, the maximum nickel mass produced across all progenitor masses is larger for lower reaction rates, reaching up to 83.9 M⊙ for a rate about half the standard value, compared to below 70 M⊙ for a rate about twice the standard value.

## Method

The authors used the MESA stellar evolution code (version 15140) to simulate non-rotating, hydrogen-free helium stars with metallicity Z = 10⁻⁵, covering initial helium core masses from 40 to 180 M⊙ with fine resolution (down to 0.01 M⊙ increments near the upper mass boundary). They varied the ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O reaction rate from −2σ to +2σ relative to the STARLIB median rate, where σ is the log-normal uncertainty factor, and tracked the central density-temperature evolution, explosion energy, and synthesized ⁵⁶Ni mass for each model. A PISN was deemed successful when all parts of the star exceeded escape velocity; failure was defined by central density exceeding 10¹² g cm⁻³ and maximum infall velocity of the central iron core exceeding 8 × 10⁸ cm s⁻¹.

## Key numbers

- Maximum synthesized ⁵⁶Ni mass: 83.9 M⊙ for the low rate (−2σ, about half the STARLIB standard value); below 70 M⊙ for the high rate (+2σ, about twice the standard value).
- Maximum exploding progenitor mass: 160 M⊙ for the −2σ rate; 125 M⊙ for the +2σ rate.
- Explosion energy increases with ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O rate for fixed initial helium core mass; the maximum explosion energy across all masses is larger at lower rates.
- The transition from explosion to implosion occurs at a residual ⁴He number fraction Y_He ≈ 0.96 for all reaction rate series, determined by the ⁴He → 2n + 2p photodisintegration threshold.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Farmer et al. (2019, 2020) and others that showed the ¹²C(α,γ)¹⁶O rate affects the pair-instability mass gap boundaries, but it provides the first systematic calculation of synthesized ⁵⁶Ni mass as a function of the rate. The results are consistent with the carbon "preheating" mechanism described by Takahashi et al. (2018) and Farmer et al. (2020), where lower rates leave more carbon to burn before explosive oxygen burning, boosting energy and nickel production. The findings enable more realistic predictions of PISN light curves and detectability for surveys like Euclid, as the nickel mass directly sets the peak luminosity, and they inform interpretations of black hole mergers such as GW190521 by clarifying how the rate affects the upper edge of the pair-instability mass gap.
