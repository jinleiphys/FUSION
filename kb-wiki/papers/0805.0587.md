# Pressure isotropization of an equilibrating quark-gluon plasma

> Pressure isotropization of an equilibrating quark-gluon plasma produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions is studied within the framework of a multi-phase transport model (AMPT). The time evolution of the bulk properties of the quark-gluon plasma is found to depend on its expansion dynamics and hadronization scheme as well as the scattering cross sections among quarks and gluons. It is further found that the pressure isotropy of the produced quark-gluon plasma can only be achieved temporarily, indicating that there is only partial thermalization during the time evolution of the quark-gluon plasma.

# Pressure isotropization of an equilibrating quark-gluon plasma

## Key claim
Pressure isotropy of the quark-gluon plasma produced in relativistic heavy ion collisions can only be achieved temporarily, indicating only partial thermalization during the time evolution, rather than full equilibration as assumed in ideal hydrodynamics.

## Method
The study uses the multi-phase transport model (AMPT) with both its default and string melting options to simulate central relativistic heavy ion collisions. The energy-momentum tensor is computed from active (still-scattering) particles in the central cell, and the pressure anisotropy is characterized by the ratio of longitudinal pressure to transverse pressure as a function of time.

## Key numbers
- In the string melting model with a 10 mb parton cross section, the relaxation time is about 0.5 fm/c and the asymptotic pressure anisotropy value is about 0.8.
- The default model hadronizes at a higher energy density of about 5 GeV/fm^3, while the string melting model completes hadronization at about two orders of magnitude below that value.
- In the default model, the system becomes hadronic at 3 fm/c; in the string melting model, hadronic matter formation is delayed to 13 fm/c.

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of thermalization in transport models by examining pressure isotropization specifically, motivated by the fact that ideal hydrodynamics (Heinz, 2005) can describe RHIC data even when only local pressure isotropization is achieved rather than full equilibrium. The results show that the AMPT model produces only temporary isotropy, which may help explain discrepancies between ideal hydrodynamic descriptions and transport model results for HBT radii. The study also contrasts the default AMPT model (dominated by hadron description) with the string melting model (dominated by parton description), showing they represent two extreme limits of the collision dynamics.
