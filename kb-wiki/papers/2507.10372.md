# Stellar properties indicating the presence of hyperons in neutron stars

> We describe distinctive stellar features indicating the presence of hyperons in neutron stars as compared to purely nucleonic systems. A strongly negative curvature of the mass-radius relation $R(M)$ is characteristic of hyperons, which can be determined from measurements of neutron stars with three different masses. Similarly, a reduced second derivative of the tidal deformability as function of mass λ(M) points to hyperonic degrees of freedom in NS matter. The slopes of such curves R(M) and λ(M) can distinguish a hyperonic equation of state from purely nucleonic models if they appear increased (decreased for λ(M)) relative to the maximum mass of neutron stars.

## Key claim
A strongly negative curvature of the mass-radius relation R(M) and a reduced second derivative of the tidal deformability as a function of mass lambda(M) are characteristic signatures of hyperons in neutron star matter, but these features can only be identified through measurements of neutron stars with at least three different masses, and the slopes of these curves relative to the maximum mass can distinguish hyperonic from purely nucleonic equations of state.

## Method
The authors compiled a large set of 47 hyperonic and 248 purely nucleonic equation-of-state models from multiple sources, including the Compose repository and covariant density functional theory, all with maximum masses of at least 1.9 solar masses. They computed mass-radius relations and mass-tidal deformability curves for each model by solving the relativistic stellar structure equations, then analyzed the curvature and second derivatives of these curves to identify systematic differences between hyperonic and nucleonic models.

## Key numbers
- 47 hyperonic equation-of-state models and 248 purely nucleonic models were analyzed
- All models have maximum mass at least 1.9 solar masses (only 9 hyperonic and 8 purely nucleonic models have M_max < 2.0 solar masses)
- The DDLS(30)-N model has maximum mass 2.48 solar masses, radius at 1.6 solar masses of 13.06 km, and tidal deformability at 1.6 solar masses of 325.4 (no hyperons)
- The DDLS(30)-Y model has maximum mass 2.01 solar masses, radius at 1.6 solar masses of 13.04 km, and tidal deformability at 1.6 solar masses of 321.7 (with hyperons)
- The LS375 model has the highest maximum mass among nucleonic models at 2.71 solar masses, with radius 13.84 km and tidal deformability 446.0 at 1.6 solar masses
- The GS1 model has the largest radius at 1.6 solar masses among nucleonic models at 14.96 km, with maximum mass 2.75 solar masses and tidal deformability 649.2

## Context
This work extends previous statistical approaches by Ferreira (2025) that considered the slope of the mass-radius relation in the 1.2 to 1.4 solar mass range, and by Huang (2025) who employed statistical arguments for hyperon identification. The proposed curvature and second-derivative diagnostics provide a quantitative method to distinguish hyperonic from purely nucleonic matter, complementing the well-known kink signature from first-order phase transitions (such as quark deconfinement) discussed by Zdunik (1987) and others. This enables future X-ray timing measurements from NICER and gravitational-wave observations to potentially solve the "hyperon puzzle" regarding the composition of neutron star interiors.
