# The dependence of the nuclear charge form factor on short range correlations and surface fluctuation effects

> We investigate the effects of fluctuations of the nuclear surface on the harmonic oscillator elastic charge form factor of light nuclei, while simultaneously approximating the short-range correlations through a Jastrow correlation ~factor. Inclusion of surface-fluctuation effects within this description, by truncating the cluster expansion at the two-body part, is found to improve somewhat the fit to the elastic charge form-factor of $^{16}O$ and $^{40}Ca$. However, the convergence of the cluster expansion is expected to deteriorate. An additional finding is that the surface-fluctuation correlations produce a drastic change in the asymptotic behavior of the point-proton form factor, which now falls off quite slowly (i.e. as $const. \cdot q^{-4}$) at large values of the momentum transfer $q$.

## Key claim

Including surface-fluctuation correlations (SFC) alongside short-range correlations (SRC) in a cluster expansion truncated at the two-body part somewhat improves the fit to the elastic charge form factor of \(^{16}\text{O}\) and \(^{40}\text{Ca}\), but the convergence of the cluster expansion is expected to deteriorate. The surface-fluctuation correlations also produce a drastic change in the asymptotic behavior of the point-proton form factor, which falls off as \(\text{const.} \cdot q^{-4}\) at large momentum transfer \(q\), rather than the Gaussian falloff of the harmonic oscillator model.

## Method

The authors start from a harmonic oscillator (HO) independent-particle model and introduce short-range correlations via a Jastrow-type correlation function of the form \(\psi_{nlS}(r) = N_{nlS}[1-\exp(-\lambda r^{2}/b^{2})]\phi_{nl}(r)\), where \(\phi_{nl}(r)\) are HO wave functions. Surface-fluctuation correlations are incorporated by convoluting the HO proton density with a Gaussian of width \(\sigma\) (related to zero-point motion of collective surface vibrations), following the formalism of Esbensen (1983). The charge form factor is then computed using the factor cluster expansion of Ristig, Ter Low, and Clark, truncated at the two-body term, and fitted to experimental data for \(^{16}\text{O}\) and \(^{40}\text{Ca}\) with the three parameters \(b_1\) (HO parameter), \(\lambda\) (SRC parameter), and \(\sigma\) (SFC parameter) treated as free.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{16}\text{O}\) with both SRC and SFC included (case 3): best-fit parameters \(b_1 = 1.563\ \text{fm}\), \(\lambda = 8.840\), \(\sigma = 0.414\ \text{fm}\).
- For \(^{40}\text{Ca}\) with both SRC and SFC included (case 3): best-fit parameters \(b_1 = 1.804\ \text{fm}\), \(\lambda = 11.000\), \(\sigma = 0.293\ \text{fm}\).
- For \(^{16}\text{O}\) in case 2 (SRC only): healing integral \(\eta_{00}^2 = 0.00717\); in case 3 (SRC+SFC): \(\eta_{00}^2 = 0.01379\), indicating deterioration of cluster expansion convergence.
- For \(^{16}\text{O}\) in case 2: \(\eta_{01}^2 = 0.00027\); in case 3: \(\eta_{01}^2 = 0.00083\).
- The value \(\sigma = 0.293\ \text{fm}\) for \(^{40}\text{Ca}\) is considerably smaller than the value \(\sigma = 0.638\ \text{fm}\) reported by Barranco (1985).
- For \(^{4}\text{He}\), the best-fit value of \(\sigma\) goes to zero, so the approach is not applicable to this nucleus.

## Context

This work extends the earlier cluster-expansion approach of Massen, Garistov, and Grypeos (1988, 1989, 1990), which used only HO orbitals and Jastrow SRC to compute charge form factors, by adding surface-fluctuation correlations as introduced by Esbensen (1983) and studied by Barranco (1985) and Escudero (1987). The inclusion of SFC improves the fit at large \(q\) values for \(^{16}\text{O}\) and \(^{40}\text{Ca}\), but the convergence of the cluster expansion worsens, as indicated by increased healing integrals. The finding that the point-proton form factor falls off as \(q^{-4}\) at large \(q\) is consistent with assumptions made by Drecher (1974) for error envelopes on nuclear densities. The results suggest that the roles of mean-field, SRC, and SFC effects become mixed, and that future work should treat all three on an equal footing.
