# "Glueballs": results and perspectives from the lattice

> I review the present status of lattice calculations of properties of gluon-rich hadrons and comment on future prospects, in view of planned experiments.

## Key claim

Lattice calculations in the quenched approximation have established a glueball spectrum, but translating these results into physical masses requires an experimental input to set the scale, and quenched ratios of light hadronic masses can deviate from real-world experiment by as much as 10%, making the translation into physical units partly a matter of personal preference.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD simulations of glueball masses, primarily in the quenched approximation (QCD without sea quarks), and compares these with early un-quenched results using two flavors of dynamical sea quarks (n_f=2). The un-quenched results were obtained using three different lattice discretisations of the Dirac action: staggered, Wilson, and clover, with all sea quarks heavier than m_s/3.

## Key numbers

- Scalar glueball (J^PC=0^++) mass between 1.4 and 1.8 GeV in the quenched approximation.
- Tensor glueball mass between 1.9 and 2.3 GeV in the quenched approximation.
- Pseudoscalar glueball mass heavier than the tensor by another 150 MeV.
- All but five glueball states are heavier than 3 GeV in the quenched spectrum.
- The lightest spin-exotic state is well above 4 GeV.
- Quenched s\bar{s} isoscalar meson is about 200 MeV lighter than the scalar glueball.
- Statistical errors on raw lattice data for the scalar glueball are about 40 MeV.
- Quenched ratios of light hadronic masses can deviate from experiment by as much as 10%.

## Context

This review extends the quenched glueball spectrum established by Bali (1993) and Morningstar (1999) and compares it with early un-quenched results from collaborations including HEMCGC (Bitar, 1991), SESAM (Bali, 2000), and UKQCD (McNeile, 2001; Hart, 2001). The work enables future studies of mixing between glueballs and quark model mesons, particularly for interpreting the f_0(1370), f_0(1500), and f_0(1710) as possible mixtures with a scalar glueball, as discussed by Close (2001) and others.
