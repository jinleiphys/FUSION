# Nuclear Superconductivity in Compact Stars: BCS Theory and Beyond

> This chapter provides a review of microscopic theories of pairing in nuclear systems and neutron stars. Special attention is given to the mean-field BCS theory and its extensions to include effects of polarization of the medium and retardation of the interactions. Superfluidity in nuclear systems that exhibit isospin asymmetry is studied. We further address the crossover from the weak-coupling BCS description to the strong-coupling BEC limit in dilute nuclear systems. Finally, within the observational context of rotational anomalies of pulsars, we discuss models of the vortex state in superfluid neutron stars and of the mutual friction between superfluid and normal components, along with the possibility of type-I superconductivity of the proton subsystem.

## Key claim

This review argues that while mean-field BCS theory provides the foundation for understanding nucleonic superfluidity in neutron stars, quantitative predictions of pairing gaps require extensions that incorporate medium polarization effects, retardation from meson-exchange interactions, and consistent treatment of self-energies, with the magnitude and density dependence of the resulting gaps remaining significantly uncertain across different microscopic approaches.

## Method

The authors review the Green's function formalism for superfluid systems using Nambu-Gor'kov propagators, deriving the gap equation from the Schwinger-Dyson equations in the mean-field BCS approximation. They then describe extensions that include polarization effects via summation of particle-hole bubble diagrams using Landau Fermi-liquid parameters, and non-adiabatic (retarded) effects from dynamical pion exchange. The correlated basis functions (CBF) approach is also presented as an alternative many-body framework for computing pairing gaps with medium corrections.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar glitches involve abrupt increases in rotation rate by amounts ΔΩ/Ω ∼ 10⁻⁶–10⁻⁸ and ΔΩ̇/Ω̇ ∼ 10⁻³, with relaxation timescales of weeks to years
- Interior neutron star temperatures are typically a few hundred keV, far below characteristic Fermi energies of tens to hundreds of MeV
- The weak-coupling BCS gap formula gives Δ(p_F) ≈ 8μ* exp(−1/[ν(p_F)|V(p_F,p_F)|]), where the density of states ν(p_F) = m*p_F Z²(p_F)/2π² (for one isospin direction)
- Screening from spin-density fluctuations reduces the singlet S-wave pairing gap by approximately a factor of 3 relative to mean-field BCS predictions
- The critical isospin asymmetry for transition from ³S₁–³D₁ to ¹S₀ pairing is small compared to the proton-neutron asymmetries typical of neutron-star matter

## Context

This review extends the pioneering work of Migdal (1959) on neutron-star superfluidity and the early BCS pairing calculations for nuclear matter by Pines (1958) and Cooper (1959). It synthesizes results from multiple microscopic approaches, including the Green's function methods of Baldo (1990) and Lombardo (1991), the CBF studies of Chen (1986, 1993) and Fabrocini (1994), and Fermi-liquid-based calculations by Wambach (1993), Schulze (1996), and Schwenk (2003), to assess the current status of pairing gap predictions. The review enables future work to resolve discrepancies between different theoretical treatments and to connect microscopic pairing calculations with observational constraints from pulsar glitches, thermal X-ray emission, and gravitational wave signatures.
