# Isobaric-multiplet mass equation in a macroscopic-microscopic approach

> We study the a, b and c coefficients of the isobaric-multiplet mass equation using a macroscopic-microscopic approach developed by P. Moeller and his collaborators in ADNDT 59, 185 (1995) and ADNDT 109-110, 1 (2016). We show that already the macroscopic part of the finite-range liquid-drop model (FRLDM) describes the general trend of the a and b coefficients relatively well, while the staggering behavior of b coefficients for doublets and quartets can be understood in terms of the difference of average proton and neutron pairing energies. The sets of isobaric masses, predicted by the full macroscopic-microscopic approaches, are used to explore the general trends of IMME coefficients up to A=100. We conclude that while the agreement for a coefficients is quite satisfactory, the global approaches have less sensitivity to predict the staggering pattern observed for b coefficients of doublets and quartets. The best set of theoretical b coefficients for multiplets up to about A=100 is used to predict masses of proton-rich nuclei based on the known experimental masses of neutron-rich mirror partners, and subsequently to investigate their one- and two-proton separation energies. The estimated position of the proton-drip line is in fair agreement with known experimental data. These masses are important for simulations of the astrophysical rp-process.

## Key claim

The macroscopic part of the finite-range liquid-drop model (FRLDM) reproduces the general trends of the isobaric-multiplet mass equation (IMME) \(a\) and \(b\) coefficients relatively well, but the full macroscopic-microscopic approaches have less sensitivity to predict the detailed staggering pattern of \(b\) coefficients for doublets and quartets, and the \(c\) coefficients are not well described.

## Method

The authors used the macroscopic-microscopic approach of Möller et al. (1995, 2016), which combines a macroscopic finite-range liquid-drop model (FRLDM) with a microscopic shell-plus-pairing correction, to compute nuclear masses and extract IMME \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) coefficients for multiplets up to \(A=100\). They derived analytical expressions for the coefficients from the macroscopic part alone, then compared predictions from the macroscopic part, the modified macroscopic part (with adjusted parameters \(a_0\), \(c_a\), and \(r_{\text{mac}}\)), and the full FRLDM and FRDM against experimental data. The best set of theoretical \(b\) coefficients was used to predict masses of proton-rich nuclei from Mn to Te isotopes and to estimate one- and two-proton separation energies.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square (rms) error for \(a\) coefficients from the macroscopic part of FRLDM is reduced from 3 MeV to about 1.9 MeV by increasing \(a_0\) from 2.165 MeV to 4.7 MeV.
- The rms error for \(b\) coefficients from the macroscopic part is reduced from 189 keV to 91 keV by changing the charge-asymmetry parameter \(c_a\) from 0.10289 MeV to 0.186 MeV.
- Using separate proton and neutron pairing parameters (\(r_n = 6.83\) MeV, \(r_p = 6.65\) MeV) further reduces the rms error for \(b\) coefficients to 81 keV.
- The rms error for \(c\) coefficients of triplets from the macroscopic part is reduced from 841 keV to 632 keV by increasing the average pairing gap parameter \(r_{\text{mac}}\) from 4.80 MeV to 5.51 MeV.
- The macroscopic part of FRLDM predicts the \(b\) coefficient for doublets as \(b = -0.7448 A^{2/3} + 2.748 - 1.534 A^{-1/3} + 0.7823 A^{-1}\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of IMME coefficients using the macroscopic part of the FRLDM, which had been applied only to \(sd\) or \(pf\) shell nuclei by Ormand (1997) and Lam et al. (2013). It provides a global description of IMME coefficients up to \(A=100\) and shows that the staggering of \(b\) coefficients arises from the difference between average proton and neutron pairing energies. The predicted masses of proton-rich nuclei are relevant for simulations of the astrophysical \(rp\)-process powering type-I X-ray bursts, and the estimated position of the proton-drip line agrees fairly with known experimental data.
