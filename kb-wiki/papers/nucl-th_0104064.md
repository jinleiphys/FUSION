# Second Order Dissipative Fluid Dynamics for Ultra-Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> The Müller-Israel-Stewart second order theory of relativistic imperfect fluids based on Grad's moment method is used to study the expansion of hot matter produced in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. The temperature evolution is investigated in the framework of the Bjorken boost-invariant scaling limit. The results of these second-order theories are compared to those of first-order theories due to Eckart and to Landau and Lifshitz and those of zeroth order (perfect fluid) due to Euler.

## Key claim

The Muller-Israel-Stewart second-order theory of relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics, when applied to the Bjorken boost-invariant scaling expansion of hot matter produced in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions, predicts a temperature evolution that differs significantly from first-order (Eckart-Landau) theories at early times, particularly when the initial proper time is small (around 0.1 fm/c), but converges to similar behavior at late times or for larger initial times (around 1.0 fm/c).

## Method

The author applied the Muller-Israel-Stewart second-order theory, derived from Grad's 14-moment method, to the 1+1 dimensional Bjorken scaling solution for a weakly interacting plasma of massless u, d, s quarks and gluons with zero baryon chemical potential. The coupled differential equations for temperature T and the shear viscous tensor component Phi were solved numerically for initial conditions corresponding to RHIC (T0 = 500 MeV) and LHC (T0 = 1000 MeV) energies, using the uncertainty principle to estimate initial proper times (0.13 fm/c and 0.07 fm/c respectively). The results were compared to analytic solutions for perfect fluid (Euler) and first-order (Eckart-Landau) theories.

## Key numbers

- Initial temperature T0 = 500 MeV for RHIC scenario, with initial proper time tau0 = 0.13 fm/c estimated from the uncertainty principle
- Initial temperature T0 = 1000 MeV for LHC scenario, with initial proper time tau0 = 0.07 fm/c estimated from the uncertainty principle
- Shear viscosity given by eta = b T^3, where b = (1 + 1.70 Nf) * 0.342 / [(1 + Nf/6) * alpha_s^2 * ln(alpha_s^{-1})] with Nf = 3 quark flavors and alpha_s = 0.5
- Equation of state constant a = (16 + (21/2) Nf) * pi^2 / 90 for Nf = 3
- Relaxation coefficient for shear in massless case: beta_2 = 3/(4p)
- Perfect fluid temperature evolution: T(tau) = T0 * (tau0/tau)^(1/3)
- First-order theory temperature evolution: T(tau) = T0 * (tau0/tau)^(1/3) * {1 + (b/(6a)) * (1/(T0*tau0)) * (1 - (tau0/tau)^(2/3))}

## Context

This work extends the first-order relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics studies by Eckart (1940) and Landau and Lifshitz (1959), which were applied to heavy ion collisions by Kajantie (1984), Baym (1984), and others, and which are known to have undesirable features such as parabolic structure and acausal signal propagation. The second-order theory, developed by Grad (1949), Muller (1967), and Israel and Stewart (1976-1979), remedies these issues by including relaxation terms that allow dissipative fluxes to evolve from arbitrary initial states. This work enables future studies of dissipative effects on observables such as photon and dilepton yields, transverse energy, freeze-out time, and entropy production, and provides a foundation for coupling fluid dynamics with microscopic kinetic models as proposed by Bass and Dumitru (2000).
