# Nucleon-Nucleon Optical Model for Energies to 3 GeV

> Several nucleon-nucleon potentials, Paris, Nijmegen, Argonne, and those derived by quantum inversion, which describe the NN interaction for T-lab below 300$ MeV are extended in their range of application as NN optical models. Extensions are made in r-space using complex separable potentials definable with a wide range of form factor options including those of boundary condition models. We use the latest phase shift analyses SP00 (FA00, WI00) of Arndt et al. from 300 MeV to 3 GeV to determine these extensions. The imaginary parts of the optical model interactions account for loss of flux into direct or resonant production processes. The optical potential approach is of particular value as it permits one to visualize fusion, and subsequent fission, of nucleons when T-lab above 2 GeV. We do so by calculating the scattering wave functions to specify the energy and radial dependences of flux losses and of probability distributions. Furthermore, half-off the energy shell t-matrices are presented as they are readily deduced with this approach. Such t-matrices are required for studies of few- and many-body nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The authors extend several well-known real NN potentials (Paris, Nijmegen, Argonne AV18, and quantum-inversion potentials) into complex optical models valid from 300 MeV to 3 GeV by adding channel-dependent complex separable potentials, using the SP00 (FA00, WI00) phase-shift analyses of Arndt et al. as input; they claim this approach allows visualization of nucleon fusion and subsequent fission when the laboratory kinetic energy exceeds 2 GeV, but note that the resulting potentials are unique only for given input data and that the short-range core is reliably specified only to about 0.25 fm due to the 3 GeV phase-shift limit.

## Method

The authors use the Gel'fand–Levitan–Marchenko quantum inversion algorithm, generalized for non-unitary S-matrices, to generate complex separable optical potentials in r-space for each partial wave. They take real reference potentials (Paris, Nijmegen, Argonne AV18, or inversion-derived) and add channel-dependent complex separable terms with energy-dependent strengths, fitted to the SP00 phase-shift solutions from 300 MeV to 3 GeV. The imaginary parts account for flux loss into direct or resonant production channels, and the approach yields half-off-shell t-matrices needed for few- and many-body nuclear reaction calculations.

## Key numbers

* The phase-shift fits to 300 MeV from all three reference models (Nijmegen-I, Nijmegen-II, Argonne AV18) are considered high quality, with inversion potentials reproducing low-energy phase shifts to an accuracy of |delta(exp.) minus delta(rat.)| less than 0.25 degrees.
* The core strengths of the $^1S_0$ and $^3P_{0,1}$ inversion potentials reach a maximum of about 1 GeV at a radius of 0.3 to 0.4 fm.
* The rational function fits used in the inversion algorithm have order N between 2 and 6, with typical order 2 less than N less than 6.
* For the $^1S_0$, $^1P_1$, $^3P_{0,1}$, $^3D_2$, and $^1F_3$ channels, the fit weights were w_Low = 1 for T_lab less than 1.2 GeV and w_High = 0.05 for higher energies; for $^1D_2$ and $^3F_3$, the cutoff between w_Low and w_High was 300 MeV.
* The Delta(1232) resonance has a width of 120 MeV and is the only resonance expected to be obviously visible in the energy variation of elastic scattering phase shifts.
* The upper limit for the medium-energy interaction scheme (0.3 to 2 GeV) is significant because above 2 GeV the two nucleons can surpass the core potential barrier and fuse into a compound system.

## Context

This work extends the energy range of standard NN potentials (Paris by Lacombe et al. 1980, Nijmegen by Stoks et al. 1993, Argonne AV18 by Wiringa et al. 1995, and inversion potentials by Kircher et al. 1989 and Sander et al. 1997) from below 300 MeV up to 3 GeV by adding complex separable terms. It builds on earlier optical model studies by von Geramb et al. (1998, 2000) and uses the Feshbach projection operator formalism (Feshbach 1962) to connect elastic scattering to doorway states in the Q space. The resulting off-shell t-matrices are required for microscopic optical model potentials for nucleon-nucleus scattering and bremsstrahlung reactions as discussed by Amos et al. (2000), and the approach enables future studies of inelastic NN reaction channels using the distorted wave Born approximation.
