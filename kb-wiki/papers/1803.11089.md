# Systematic analysis of double-scale evolution

> Often the factorization of differential cross sections results in the definition of fundamental hadronic functions/distributions which have a double-scale evolution, as provided by a pair of coupled equations. Typically, the two scales are the renormalization and rapidity scales. The two-dimensional structure of their evolution is the object of the present study . In order to be more specific, we consider the case of the transverse momentum dependent distributions (TMD). Nonetheless, most of our findings can be used with other double-scale parton distributions. On the basis of the two-dimensional structure of TMD evolution, we formulate the general statement of the $ζ$-prescription introduced in \cite{Scimemi:2017etj}, and we define an optimal TMD distribution, which is a scaleless model-independent universal non-perturbative function. A significant part of this work is devoted to the study of the effects of truncation of perturbation theory on the double-scale evolution. We show that within truncated perturbation theory the solution of evolution equations is ambiguous and this fact generates extra uncertainties within the resummed cross-section. The alternatives to bypass this issue are discussed. We discuss the effects of the scale variation effects and show that these uncertainties reduce within the non-ambiguous solutions that we propose.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the double-scale (renormalization and rapidity) evolution of transverse momentum dependent distributions (TMDs) is fundamentally two-dimensional, and that within truncated perturbation theory the solution of the evolution equations is ambiguous, generating extra uncertainties in resummed cross-sections that are not cured by increasing the perturbative order; the authors propose a "zeta-prescription" and an "optimal TMD distribution" to resolve this ambiguity and disentangle perturbative from non-perturbative effects.

## Method

The authors formulate TMD evolution as a two-dimensional vector field in the (mu, zeta) plane, introducing a scalar potential and null-evolution curves to analyze path dependence. They compare three different integration paths (solutions 1, 2, and 3) for the evolution factor and study the effects of truncating perturbation theory on the integrability condition and transitivity property. They then define the zeta-prescription as a specific selection of scales that eliminates double-logarithm contributions and propose an "optimal TMD distribution" that is a scaleless, model-independent, universal non-perturbative function.

## Key numbers

* The saddle point position for quark flavor is approximately mu_saddle ≈ 2e^{-gamma_E}/b and zeta_saddle ≈ 4e^{-2gamma_E+3/2}/b^2 at one-loop order.
* The saddle point crosses the Landau pole line at an estimated value b_bar ≈ 2e^{-gamma_E}/Lambda ≈ 4 GeV^{-1}.
* The TMD evolution is known up to the third order in the strong coupling expansion, as cited from Li (2016) and Vladimirov (2016).
* The final scales for the evolution are typically set to (mu_f, zeta_f) = (Q, Q^2) where Q is the hard scale of the process.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of the zeta-prescription introduced by Scimemi (2017) and provides a systematic analysis of double-scale evolution that applies to TMD distributions and other double-scale parton distributions. It addresses ambiguities in the evolution procedure that were noted by Collins (2011) and Chiu (2012) but not previously studied in detail. The paper enables a cleaner separation of perturbative and non-perturbative effects in TMD phenomenology, which is relevant for Drell-Yan processes, vector boson production at the LHC, and the Electron-Ion Collider program.
