# Asymptotic Behaviors of Selected Unitary 9j Coefficients

> We consider the behaviors of selected unitary 9 coefficients in the large j limit.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for even total angular momenta \(I\) from 2 to 32, the unitary 9j coefficient \(U(I,j)\) follows an exponential decay multiplied by a power law \(j^{1.5}\) in the large-\(j\) limit, but only if one goes to sufficiently high \(j\) (up to 7000.5); at smaller \(j\) values, the extracted power-law exponent \(m\) appears to depend on \(I\), which would be a false conclusion.

## Method

The authors computed unitary 9j coefficients \(U(I,j)\) for all even \(I\) from 2 to 32, using Mathematica with fractional input (e.g., 1001/2 rather than 500.5) to maintain extreme precision. They extracted the power-law exponent \(m\) in the asymptotic form \(A j^m e^{-\alpha j}\) by analyzing the ratio \(RR = U(I,j+1)^2 / [U(I,j) U(I,j+2)]\) and comparing it to the expected ratio for a pure power law. For the largest \(I\) values (\(I = I_{\text{max}} = 4j-2\) and below), they examined power-law decays without the exponential factor.

## Key numbers

* For \(U(2,j)\) with \(j\) up to 7000.5, the extracted exponent \(m\) converges to 1.5 in the large-\(j\) limit.
* For \(j\) only up to 500.5, the extracted \(m\) values for \(I=2,4,10,20,30\) are respectively 1.495, 1.481, 1.391, 1.085, and 0.577, showing a false dependence on \(I\).
* For \(I = I_{\text{max}} = 4j-2\), the coefficient approaches a constant given analytically as \(\sqrt{(2j-1)(8j-1)} / [2(4j-1)]\).
* For \(I = I_{\text{max}} - 2 = 4j-4\), the coefficient drops asymptotically as \(1/j\); for \(I = I_{\text{max}} - 4 = 4j-6\), it drops as \(1/j^2\); in general the power is \(n = (I_{\text{max}} - I)/2\).
* Talmi's analytic result gives that for \(I = I_{\text{max}} - 2\), the unitary 9j coefficient goes asymptotically to \(-1/(8j)\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by the same group (Kleszyk, Zamick, and Bayman) that considered only the \(I=2\) case up to \(j=100.5\); here the authors push to \(j=7000.5\) to ensure true asymptotic behavior. The results correct a potential misinterpretation that different \(I\) values have different asymptotic power-law exponents. The motivation comes from understanding wave functions arising from a "maximum \(J\)-pairing" Hamiltonian, as discussed in prior work by the same authors. The exponential asymptotic behavior for 6j coefficients was previously presented by Varshalovich et al. (1988), and an analytic formula for the \(I=I_{\text{max}}\) case is given by Talmi (1993) and also by Varshalovich et al.
