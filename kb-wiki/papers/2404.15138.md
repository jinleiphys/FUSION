# Estimating Longitudinal Polarization of $Λ$ and $\barΛ$ Hyperons at Relativistic Energies using Hydrodynamic and Transport models

> The global and local spin polarization measurements of $Λ$ ($\barΛ$) hyperons by STAR and ALICE Collaborations open up an immense interest in investigating the spin polarization dynamics in heavy-ion collisions. Recent studies suggest the transverse component of the vorticity field is responsible for the global spin polarization. In contrast, the longitudinal component of the vorticity field accounts for the local spin polarization. The local (longitudinal) spin polarization of $Λ$-hyperons arises due to the anisotropic flows in the transverse plane, indicating a quadrupole pattern of the longitudinal vorticity along the beam direction. In this study, we derive a simple solution relating the longitudinal mean spin vector with the second-order anisotropic flow coefficient due to the thermal shear tensor for an ideal uncharged fluid in a longitudinal boost invariant scenario. The present study focuses on the local spin polarization of $Λ$ and $\barΛ$ in Au$+$Au and Pb$+$Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 200 GeV and 5.02 TeV, respectively. Further, we explore the azimuthal angle, centrality, and transverse momentum ($p_{\rm T}$) dependence study of longitudinal spin polarization using hydrodynamic and transport models. All these models predict a maximum longitudinal spin polarization in mid-central collisions around 30-50 \% centrality at $p_{\rm T} \approx$ 2.0 - 3.0 GeV/c. These findings on longitudinal spin polarization advocate the existence of a thermal medium in non-central heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors derive an analytical relation between the longitudinal mean spin vector of Λ and \(\bar{\Lambda}\) hyperons and the second-order anisotropic flow coefficient (elliptic flow) due to the thermal shear tensor, assuming an ideal uncharged fluid with temperature evolving only with Bjorken time. They find that the contributions from thermal shear and thermal vorticity to the longitudinal spin polarization are exactly equal in magnitude but opposite in sign under these assumptions.

## Method

The authors derived an analytical expression for the longitudinal mean spin vector by expanding the Cooper-Frye freeze-out integral in a Fourier series in azimuthal angle and retaining only the elliptic flow term, separately for thermal vorticity and thermal shear contributions. They then used three event generators, ECHO-QGP (a (3+1)D viscous hydrodynamic model with Israel-Stewart formalism), EPOS4 (a Monte-Carlo event generator), and the AMPT (a multiphase transport model), to obtain the elliptic flow coefficients and azimuthal angle distributions needed to compute the longitudinal spin polarization of Λ and \(\bar{\Lambda}\) hyperons in Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 200 GeV and Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 5.02 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Maximum longitudinal spin polarization predicted in mid-central collisions around 30-50% centrality at \(p_{\rm T} \approx\) 2.0 - 3.0 GeV/c for both collision systems.
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature set to 120 MeV for both Au+Au at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 200 GeV and Pb+Pb at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}\) = 5.02 TeV.
- Initial thermalization time \(\tau_{0}\) = 0.6 fm/c for Au+Au and 0.4 fm/c for Pb+Pb.
- Initial energy density \(e_{0}\) = 24.5 GeV/fm\(^3\) for Au+Au and 60.0 GeV/fm\(^3\) for Pb+Pb.
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(\eta/s\) = 0.1 used for both systems.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of Becattini et al. (2017) who calculated the contribution of thermal vorticity to longitudinal spin polarization, by additionally deriving the contribution from the thermal shear tensor. The study addresses the "spin sign puzzle" where experimental measurements from the STAR Collaboration (2019) showed opposite trends compared to earlier theoretical predictions. The results advocate for the existence of a thermal medium in non-central heavy-ion collisions and suggest that future work should incorporate hadronic scattering effects, which were not considered in the present models.
