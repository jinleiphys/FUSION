# Unveiling the role of vector potential in the Aharonov-Bohm effect

> The most popular interpretation of the Aharonov-Bohm (AB) effect is that the electromagnetic potential locally affects the complex phase of a charged particle's wave function in the magnetic field free region. However, since the vector potential is a gauge-variant quantity, not a few researchers suspect that it is just a convenient tool for calculating the force field. This motivates them to explain the AB effect without using the vector potential, which inevitably leads to some sort of non-locality. This frustrating situation is shortly summarized by the statement of Aharonov et al. that the AB effect may be due to a local gauge potential or due to non-local gauge-invariant fields. In the present paper, we shall give several convincing arguments, which support the viewpoint that the vector potential is not just a convenient mathematical tool with little physical entity. Despite its gauge arbitrariness, the vector potential certainly contains a gauge-invariant piece, which solely explains the observed AB phase shift. Importantly, this component has a property such that it is basically unique and cannot be eliminated by any regular gauge transformations. To make the discussion complete, we also discuss the role of remaining gauge arbitrariness still contained in the entire vector potential.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the vector potential in the Aharonov-Bohm effect contains a unique, gauge-invariant transverse component that cannot be eliminated by any regular gauge transformation, and that this component alone explains the observed AB phase shift, contrary to claims that the vector potential is merely a convenient mathematical tool.

## Method

The author analyzes the vector potential generated by an infinitely-long solenoid using the Biot-Savart law and demonstrates that the vector potential can be uniquely decomposed into transverse and longitudinal components, provided that multi-valued gauge transformations are excluded. The paper then examines the contribution of each component to the AB phase shift for both closed and non-closed electron paths, and critiques alternative explanations that attempt to dispense with the vector potential, including those by Vaidman (2012), Boyer (1971), and several recent interaction-energy approaches.

## Key numbers

* The vector potential outside the solenoid (ρ ≥ R) is given by A^(S)(x) = (Φ/2π)(1/ρ) e_φ, where Φ = π R² B is the total magnetic flux.
* The vector potential inside the solenoid (ρ < R) is given by A^(S)(x) = (Φ/2π)(ρ/R²) e_φ.
* The multi-valued gauge transformation χ^(sing)(x) = -(1/2π)Φ φ generates a string-like magnetic field B^(string)(x) = -(Φ/2π)(δ(ρ)/ρ) e_z, which exactly cancels the original flux, making the net magnetic field through the solenoid zero.
* The closed line integral of the transverse component A^(S) yields the total flux Φ, while the longitudinal component contributes zero due to the constraint ∇ × ∇χ(x) = 0.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous analysis (Wakamatsu, 2024) which showed that interaction-energy explanations of the AB effect by Boyer (1971) and others suffer from a cancellation problem between the classical interaction energy and the virtual-photon exchange contribution. The paper supports earlier arguments by Adachi (1992), Stewart (2003), and Li (2012) regarding the uniqueness of the transverse-longitudinal decomposition, but claims to provide a more complete justification by excluding multi-valued gauge transformations that alter the magnetic field distribution. The analysis enables a definitive resolution of the long-standing debate about whether the vector potential is physically real or merely a mathematical convenience in the AB effect.
