# Comparison of optical model results from a microscopic Schrödinger approach to nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering with those from a global Dirac phenomenology

> Comparisons are made between results of calculations for intermediate energy nucleon-nucleus scattering for 12C, 16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb, using optical potentials obtained from global Dirac phenomenology and from a microscopic Schrödinger model. Differential cross sections and spin observables for scattering from the set of five nuclei at 65 MeV and 200 MeV have been studied to assess the relative merits of each approach. Total reaction cross sections from proton-nucleus and total cross sections from neutron-nucleus scattering have been evaluated and compared with data for those five targets in the energy range 20 MeV to 800 MeV. The methods of analyses give results that compare well with experimental data in those energy regimes for which the procedures are suited.

# Comparison of optical model results from a microscopic Schrödinger approach to nucleon-nucleus elastic scattering with those from a global Dirac phenomenology

## Key claim
The global Dirac phenomenology (DP) and the microscopic g-folding Schrödinger model both produce results that compare well with experimental data, but only within the specific energy regimes for which each procedure is suited; the g-folding model systematically underpredicts total reaction cross sections above approximately 300 MeV for all five targets studied, while the global DP reproduces data well up to 800 MeV.

## Method
The authors compared predictions from two optical model approaches for proton and neutron elastic scattering from 12C, 16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, and 208Pb. The microscopic g-folding model used density-dependent effective NN interactions derived from Bonn-B g-matrices solved in infinite nuclear matter, folded with ground-state densities from large-basis shell model or Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations, with no adjustable parameters. The global Dirac phenomenology used scalar-vector (SV) potentials with energy- and mass-dependent parameters (EDAI and EDAD3 sets) previously fitted to a large body of elastic scattering data. Differential cross sections, analyzing powers, and spin rotations were calculated at 65 MeV and 200 MeV, and total reaction cross sections for protons and total cross sections for neutrons were evaluated from 20 MeV to 800 MeV.

## Key numbers
- Differential cross sections and spin observables calculated for five nuclei (12C, 16O, 40Ca, 90Zr, 208Pb) at 65 MeV and 200 MeV.
- Total reaction cross sections for protons and total cross sections for neutrons evaluated for the same five targets over the energy range 20 MeV to 800 MeV.
- The g-folding model under-predicts total reaction cross section data above approximately 300 MeV for all five targets; for 12C, data to 300 MeV are well reproduced but the approach is "inadequate" at higher energies.
- At 200 MeV, the g-folding predictions show "noticeable disparities at the larger scattering angles" where cross section magnitudes fall below 0.1 mb/sr and momentum transfer exceeds approximately 2.5 fm^-1.
- The global DP results (both EDAI and EDAD3) reproduce total reaction cross section data "very well falling within the uncertainties at most energies" up to 800 MeV.

## Context
This work extends a single prior comparison by Karataglidis et al. (2001) that examined only 208Pb at 100, 200, and 300 MeV, now covering five nuclei spanning the mass range from 12C to 208Pb. The g-folding approach builds on the work of Amos et al. (2000) using Bonn-B NN potentials and Skyrme-Hartree-Fock structure models, while the global DP follows the parameterization of Cooper et al. (1993). The results enable users of optical potentials to understand the limitations of each approach: the g-folding model is predictive but limited to energies below approximately 300 MeV, while the global DP provides reliable results across a wider energy range but is phenomenological and data-dependent. The authors note that a multi-channel algebraic scattering (MCAS) theory developed by Amos et al. (2003) is needed to cover the low-energy region below 20 MeV where mean-field optical potentials are inappropriate.
