# Physics of even-even superheavy nuclei with 96 < Z < 110 in the Quark-Meson-Coupling Model

> The Quark-Meson-Coupling (QMC) model has been applied to the study of the properties of even-even super-heavy nuclei with 96 < Z < 110, over a wide range of neutron numbers. The aim is to identify the deformed shell gaps at N = 152 and N = 162 predicted in macroscopic-microscopic (macro-micro) models, in a model based on the mean-field Hartree-Fock+BCS approximation. The predictive power of the model has been tested on proton and neutron spherical shell gaps in light doubly closed (sub)shell nuclei. In the super-heavy region, the ground state binding energies of 98 < Z < 110 and 146 < N < 160 differ, in the majority of cases, from the measured values by less than 2.5 MeV, with the deviation decreasing with increasing Z and N. The axial quadrupole deformation parameter, calculated over the range of neutron numbers 138 < N < 184, revealed a prolate-oblate coexistence and shape transition around N = 168, followed by an oblate-spherical transition towards the expected N = 184 shell closure in Cm, Cf, Fm and No. The closure is not predicted in Rf, Sg, Hs and Ds as another shape transition to a highly deformed shape in Sg, Hs and Ds for N > 178 appears, while 288Rf (N = 184) remains oblate. The bulk properties predicted by QMC are found to have a limited sensitivity to the deformed shell gaps at N = 152 and 162. However, the evolution of the neutron single-particle spectra with 0 < beta2 < 0.55 gives unambiguous evidence for the location and size of the N = 152 and 162 gaps as a function of Z and N. In addition, the neutron number dependence of neutron pairing energies provides supporting evidence for existence of the energy gaps.

## Key claim

The Quark-Meson-Coupling (QMC) model, a mean-field Hartree-Fock+BCS approach with only four global variable parameters (plus two pairing parameters) and no local adjustments specific to the superheavy region, provides evidence for the deformed shell gaps at N=152 and N=162 primarily through the evolution of neutron single-particle spectra and neutron pairing energies, even though bulk properties such as binding energies and separation energies show limited sensitivity to these gaps.

## Method

The authors employed the self-consistent Hartree-Fock (HF) approximation with a BCS pairing model, using the QMC-π-I energy density functional, which includes pion exchange Fock terms and treats nucleons as having internal quark structure with a scalar polarizability term. For each even-even isotope with 96 ≤ Z ≤ 110 and 138 ≤ N ≤ 184, the constrained Hartree-Fock method was used, varying the axial quadrupole deformation parameter β₂ on a mesh with step 0.02 from -0.5 to 0.6 to find the equilibrium ground state. The single-particle spectra were calculated for representative isotopes including ²⁴⁴Cm, ²⁴⁸Cf, ²⁵²Fm, ²⁵⁶No, ²⁶⁰Rf, ²⁶⁴Sg, ²⁶⁸Hs and ²⁷²Ds over a deformation range 0 ≤ β₂ ≤ 0.55.

## Key numbers

- Ground state binding energies for nuclei with 98 ≤ Z ≤ 110 and 146 ≤ N ≤ 160 differ from measured values (AME2016) by less than ±2.5 MeV in the majority of cases, with deviation decreasing with increasing Z and N
- The QMC model parameters: coupling strengths Gσ = 11.15 fm², Gω = 8.00 fm², Gρ = 7.38 fm²; σ meson mass Mσ = 712 MeV; nuclear matter saturation energy E₀ = -15.8 MeV, saturation density ρ₀ = 0.153 fm⁻³, incompressibility K₀ = 319 MeV, symmetry energy S₀ = 30 MeV and slope L = 27 MeV
- Total RMS deviation of QMC two-neutron separation energies from experiment is 0.470 MeV
- A prolate deformation with β₂ ∼ 0.28 is predicted for all isotopes with neutron numbers 138 ≤ N ≤ 152
- Highly deformed shapes with β₂ ∼ 0.4 appear in Sg, Hs and Ds for N > 178
- The empirical shell-gap parameter δ₂ₙ at N=152 for Z=102 fails by more than a factor of two to predict the enhancement seen in experiment; artificially increasing binding energies of N=152 and 162 isotopes by 150 keV (less than 0.01%) produces clear maxima at those neutron numbers

## Context

This work extends the QMC model, previously applied to nuclei below ²⁰⁸Pb (Stone 2016) and to a broader superheavy region (Stone 2017), to systematically examine deformed shell gaps at N=152 and N=162 in even-even nuclei with 96 < Z < 110. The results are compared with macroscopic-microscopic models (FRDM by Moller 2016 and MM by Muntian 2001, 2003) which predict these gaps and generally achieve closer agreement with experimental binding energies, as well as with mean-field models based on Skyrme and Gogny forces and relativistic mean field theory. The QMC model's predictions of shape coexistence and transitions, including the disappearance of the N=184 spherical shell gap in Rf, Sg, Hs and Ds, provide testable predictions for future experimental studies of superheavy nuclei, particularly regarding α-decay lifetimes and direct mass measurements.
