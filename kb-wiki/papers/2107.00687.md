# Post-explosion evolution of core-collapse supernovae

> We investigate the post-explosion phase in core-collapse supernovae with 2D hydrodynamical simulations and a simple neutrino treatment. The latter allows us to perform 46 simulations and follow the evolution of the 32 successful explosions during several seconds. We present a broad study based on three progenitors (11.2 $M_\odot$, 15 $M_\odot$, and 27 $M_\odot$), different neutrino-heating efficiencies, and various rotation rates. We show that the first seconds after shock revival determine the final explosion energy, remnant mass, and properties of ejected matter. Our results suggest that a continued mass accretion increases the explosion energy even at late times. We link the late-time mass accretion to initial conditions such as rotation strength and shock deformation at explosion time. Only some of our simulations develop a neutrino-driven wind that survives for several seconds. This indicates that neutrino-driven winds are not a standard feature expected after every successful explosion. Even if our neutrino treatment is simple, we estimate the nucleosynthesis of the exploding models for the 15 $M_\odot$ progenitor after correcting the neutrino energies and luminosities to get a more realistic electron fraction.

## Key claim

The first seconds after shock revival determine the final explosion energy, remnant mass, and properties of ejected matter, with continued mass accretion increasing the explosion energy even at late times; however, the authors note that their simplifications (2D Newtonian simulations with gray neutrino leakage) prevent solid quantitative conclusions, and that neutrino-driven winds are not a standard feature expected after every successful explosion.

## Method

The authors performed 2D hydrodynamical simulations using the FLASH code with a gray, three-flavor neutrino leakage scheme and a ray-by-ray approximation, artificially enhancing neutrino heating by a factor f_heat to trigger explosions. They simulated three non-rotating progenitor models (11.2 M_sun, 15 M_sun, and 27 M_sun) from Woosley (2002), superimposing parametric rotational profiles with six different rotation strengths, and followed 32 successful explosions for several seconds post-bounce. For nucleosynthesis estimates on the 15 M_sun progenitor, they used tracer particles and the WinNet nuclear reaction network, correcting the electron fraction from the leakage scheme to obtain more realistic values.

## Key numbers

* 46 simulations were performed, of which 32 produced successful explosions.
* Three progenitor masses were used: 11.2 M_sun, 15 M_sun, and 27 M_sun.
* The maximum resolution achieved was approximately 488 m.
* For the 15 M_sun progenitor, 21,281 tracer particles were initialized, each with a mass of 1.76 x 10^-4 M_sun.
* The explosion energy at 100 ms after explosion for model s15_F120_R001 was 0.19 B (B = 10^51 erg), and its final explosion energy was 1.20 B.
* For model s15_F150_R030, the explosion energy at 100 ms was 0.16 B, and the final explosion energy was 0.81 B.
* During neutrino-driven wind phases with no accretion, the explosion energy growth rate was (0.035 ± 0.007) B s^-1.
* The shock deformation parameter at explosion time ranged from -0.21 (oblate, for s15_F150_R020) to 1.26 (prolate, for s15_F108_R000).
* The neutrino-driven wind duration for model s15_F130_R010 was 5.10 s, and for model s15_F150_R006 was 5.34 s.

## Context

This work extends multidimensional core-collapse supernova studies that have followed post-explosion evolution for several seconds, such as those by Harris (2017), Eichler (2018), Wanajo (2018), Sieverding (2020), and Reichert (2021), but covers a larger sample of models (46 simulations) with varying rotation rates and neutrino-heating efficiencies. The results contradict the assumption from one-dimensional models (e.g., Arcones 2007, Perego 2015, Couch 2020) that explosion energy saturates promptly, showing instead that continued accretion via downflows can increase explosion energy for seconds. The finding that neutrino-driven winds are not a standard feature challenges expectations from earlier work (e.g., Arcones 2011) and indicates that the interplay between accretion and ejection can continue longer than previously thought, though the authors note that 3D simulations with better neutrino transport are needed to confirm the duration and impact of downflows and winds.
