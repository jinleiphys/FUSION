# Freeze-out and thermalization in relativistic heavy ion collisions

> High energy heavy-ion collisions in laboratory produce a form of matter that can test Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), the theory of strong interactions, at high temperatures. One of the exciting possibilities is the existence of thermodynamically distinct states of QCD, particularly a phase of de-confined quarks and gluons. An important step in establishing this new state of QCD is to demonstrate that the system has attained thermal equilibrium. We present a test of thermal equilibrium by checking that the mean hadron yields produced in the small impact parameter collisions as well as grand canonical fluctuations of conserved quantities give consistent temperature and baryon chemical potential for the last scattering surface. This consistency for moments up to third order of the net-baryon number, charge, and strangeness is a key step in the proof that the QCD matter produced in heavy-ion collision attains thermal equilibrium. It is a clear indication for the first time, using fluctuation observables, that a femto-scale system attains thermalization. The study also indicates that the relaxation time scales for the system are comparable to or smaller than the life time of the fireball.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the simultaneous description of mean hadron yields and event-by-event fluctuations (up to third-order cumulants) of net-baryon, net-charge, and net-strangeness in central Au+Au collisions at RHIC yields consistent freeze-out temperature and baryon chemical potential, providing a test of thermal equilibrium. The authors hedge that this consistency holds for collision energies of 19.6 GeV and above, but fails at the two lowest energies (7.7 GeV) and in peripheral collisions, where thermalization is not observed.

## Method

The authors fit a hadron resonance gas (HRG) model to 13 observables from STAR experiment data for 0-5% central Au+Au collisions at center-of-mass energies per nucleon pair (sqrt(s_NN)) from 7.7 to 200 GeV. The observables include mean yields of pions, kaons, and protons; second-order cumulant ratios (sigma^2/M for net-kaons, and off-diagonal ratios sigma^2_{QK}/sigma^2(NK), sigma^2_{pK}/sigma^2(NK), sigma^2_{Qp}/sigma^2(NP)); and third-order cumulant ratios S*sigma for net-protons, net-kaons, and net-charge. The fit minimizes chi^2 using statistical uncertainties only, extracting the freeze-out temperature T and chemical potentials mu_B, mu_S, and mu_Q. The same procedure is applied to synthetic data from the non-thermal UrQMD and AMPT models as a control.

## Key numbers

* For central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, the best-fit freeze-out temperature T and baryon chemical potential mu_B from the 13-observable fit agree with those from mean yields only, with chi^2/ndf of order 1 (improving to 1.0 when systematic uncertainties are included).
* At sqrt(s_NN) = 7.7 GeV, the chi^2/ndf is 11.9 (improving to 4.6 with systematics), indicating a poorer fit.
* For central collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 19.6 GeV and above, the differences Delta T and Delta mu_B between freeze-out parameters extracted from first-order, second-order, and third-order moments are consistent within uncertainties; at the two lowest energies (7.7 and 11.5 GeV), clear deviations appear.
* For peripheral collisions, fits to different subsets of data give significantly different freeze-out parameters, implying no thermalization.
* The UrQMD model yields chi^2/ndf of order 100-10000 when fit with the HRG model, and Delta T and Delta mu_B values normalized to uncertainties range from -12 to 18, indicating no agreement.

## Context

This work extends the standard approach of extracting freeze-out parameters from mean hadron yields alone (Cleymans 2005, Andronic 2005, Becattini 2003, Garg 2013) by adding fluctuation observables up to third order. It addresses the known problem that HRG models describe yields even in e+e- and pp collisions where thermalization is not expected (Becattini 1995, Das 2016). The study enables future searches for the QCD critical point by establishing a baseline for thermal behavior at high energies, and by identifying that the two lowest collision energies (7.7 and 11.5 GeV) may not produce thermalized systems, motivating further investigation of the phase boundary at low energies.
