# Optical potentials for the rare-isotope beam era

> We review recent progress and motivate the need for further developments in nuclear optical potentials that are widely used in the theoretical analysis of nucleon elastic scattering and reaction cross sections. In regions of the nuclear chart away from stability, which represent a frontier in nuclear science over the coming decade and which will be probed at new rare-isotope beam facilities worldwide, there is a targeted need to quantify and reduce theoretical reaction model uncertainties, especially with respect to nuclear optical potentials. We first describe the primary physics motivations for an improved description of nuclear reactions involving short-lived isotopes, focusing on its benefits for fundamental science discoveries and applications to medicine, energy, and security. We then outline the various methods in use today to build optical potentials starting from phenomenological, microscopic, and ab initio methods, highlighting in particular the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. We then discuss publicly-available tools and resources facilitating the propagation of recent progresses in the field to practitioners. Finally, we provide a set of open challenges and recommendations for the field to advance the fundamental science goals of nuclear reaction studies in the rare-isotope beam era.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the current state and future needs for nuclear optical potentials, arguing that while phenomenological optical models have achieved impressive descriptions of nucleon-nucleus scattering on stable targets up to 200 MeV, their extrapolations to exotic short-lived isotopes probed at rare-isotope beam facilities carry tremendous and largely unquantified uncertainties.

## Method

The authors survey three main approaches to constructing optical potentials: phenomenological (fitted to elastic scattering data using functional forms with approximately 40 parameters, as in the Koning and Delaroche potential), microscopic (derived from many-body theory via Watson multiple scattering theory, Feshbach projection formalism, or Green's function theory), and ab initio methods. They compare the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, discuss publicly available tools and resources, and provide a set of open challenges and recommendations for the field.

## Key numbers

- The Koning and Delaroche phenomenological global optical potential uses approximately 40 fitted parameters to describe energy and geometry dependence that also vary with mass number A and isospin asymmetry delta = (N-Z)/A.
- Phenomenological optical models have achieved description of nucleon-nucleus scattering on stable target isotopes up to projectile energies E less than or equal to 200 MeV.
- The impulse approximation in multiple scattering theory is expected to be valid at energies larger than roughly 60 to 100 MeV, where impacts of Pauli-blocking and three-body forces are diminished.
- One-nucleon transfer reactions are typically performed between approximately 5 and 50 MeV/nucleon, depending on the Q value.
- Quasifree scattering measurements are typically performed above 200 MeV/nucleon.
- The paper calls for developing optical potentials across the whole nuclear chart at energies ranging from a few MeV up to 400 MeV.

## Context

This review paper is the outcome of the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams Theory Alliance topical program "Optical Potentials in Nuclear Physics" held in March 2022 at FRIB. It extends decades of work on phenomenological optical potentials such as those by Becchetti and Greenlees (1969), Varner et al. (1991), and Koning and Delaroche (2003), and microscopic approaches dating to Watson (1953) and Kerman, McManus, and Thaler (1959). The paper enables future work by identifying specific open challenges and recommendations for developing optical potentials with quantified uncertainties needed to maximize the scientific impact of rare-isotope beam facilities worldwide, particularly for applications in nuclear astrophysics, medicine, energy, and security.
