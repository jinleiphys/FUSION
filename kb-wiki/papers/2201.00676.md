# Nuclear masses learned from a probabilistic neural network

> Machine learning methods and uncertainty quantification have been gaining interest throughout the last several years in low-energy nuclear physics. In particular, Gaussian processes and Bayesian Neural Networks have increasingly been applied to improve mass model predictions while providing well-quantified uncertainties. In this work, we use the probabilistic Mixture Density Network (MDN) to directly predict the mass excess of the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation within the range of measured data, and we extrapolate the inferred models beyond available experimental data. The MDN not only provides mean values but also full posterior distributions both within the training set and extrapolated testing set. We show that the addition of physical information to the feature space increases the accuracy of the match to the training data as well as provides for more physically meaningful extrapolations beyond the the limits of experimental data.

## Key claim

The Mixture Density Network (MDN) can directly predict nuclear mass excess from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation, and adding physics-informed features (such as semi-empirical mass formula terms, pairing indicators, and shell information) to the input space improves both the accuracy of predictions within the training set and the physical meaningfulness of extrapolations beyond measured data, though the improvement is conditional on the specific feature set used.

## Method

The authors trained a Mixture Density Network (MDN) with a single hidden layer of six nodes and one Gaussian mixture to predict mass excess directly from the 2016 Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2016), using 2074 nuclei where one-neutron separation energies could be defined. They constructed multiple training sets with increasing numbers of physics-informed features: starting from just neutron number N and proton number Z (model M2), then adding terms from the semi-empirical mass formula (M6), pairing information (M8), valence nucleon counts relative to magic numbers (M10), and shell model orbital indices (M12), with equivalent models that also predict one-neutron separation energy S_n (MS2 through MS12). The testing set consisted of 8668 nuclei from the 2012 Finite-Range Droplet Model (FRDM) predictions, spanning regions beyond experimental measurements.

## Key numbers

- Model M2 (only N and Z as inputs) achieved σ_RMS = 3.90 MeV for mass excess predictions relative to AME2016
- Model M6 (adding semi-empirical mass formula terms) reduced σ_RMS to 1.57 MeV for mass excess
- Model M10 (adding valence nucleon counts) achieved σ_RMS = 0.58 MeV for mass excess
- Model M12 (adding shell model orbitals) achieved σ_RMS = 0.56 MeV for mass excess
- Model MS12 (predicting both mass excess and one-neutron separation energy with full feature set) achieved σ_RMS = 0.64 MeV for mass excess and σ_RMS = 0.47 MeV for one-neutron separation energy
- Model M8 (adding pairing information) showed σ_RMS = 1.66 MeV for mass excess, a slight increase from M6

## Context

This work extends the application of machine learning to nuclear mass prediction, moving beyond the Gaussian Process (GP) and Bayesian Neural Network (BNN) approaches used by Neufcourt et al. (2018, 2019, 2020) that trained on residuals between mass models and experimental data. Unlike those approaches, the MDN directly predicts mass excess rather than correcting existing models, and the authors demonstrate that incorporating physics-informed features enables physically meaningful extrapolations beyond the training region. This enables future work on using the MDN for predictions of masses in astrophysical applications and for understanding how feature engineering can improve machine learning models in nuclear physics.
