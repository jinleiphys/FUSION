# The adjoint neutron transport equation and the statistical approach for its solution

> The adjoint equation was introduced in the early days of neutron transport and its solution, the neutron importance, has ben used for several applications in neutronics. The work presents at first a critical review of the adjoint neutron transport equation. Afterwards, the adjont model is constructed for a reference physical situation, for which an analytical approach is viable, i.e. an infinite homogeneous scattering medium. This problem leads to an equation that is the adjoint of the slowing-down equation that is well-known in nuclear reactor physics. A general closed-form analytical solution to such adjoint equation is obtained by a procedure that can be used also to derive the classical Placzek functions. This solution constitutes a benchmark for any statistical or numerical approach to the adjoint equation. A sampling technique to evaluate the adjoint flux for the transport equation is then proposed and physically interpreted as a transport model for pseudo-particles. This can be done by introducing appropriate kernels describing the transfer of the pseudo-particles in phase space. This technique allows estimating the importance function by a standard Monte Carlo approach. The sampling scheme is validated by comparison with the analytical results previously obtained.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Monte Carlo sampling technique for solving the adjoint neutron transport equation by introducing pseudo-particles (adjunctons) whose transport properties are defined through a formal mathematical transformation, but only under the assumption that the total collision rate for pseudo-particles equals that for physical neutrons; the adjoint flux obtained numerically coincides with the neutron importance function only if one accepts that the adjoint source in the pseudo-particle model corresponds to a particle density rather than a dimensionless quantity.

## Method

The authors first derive the adjoint neutron transport equation for an infinite homogeneous scattering medium with isotropic source, then construct a formal mapping between the adjoint kernel and a forward-like transport kernel by defining adjoint cross sections and pseudo-particle emission multiplicities. They develop a Monte Carlo sampling procedure where pseudo-particles are tracked through collisions with energy-dependent weights determined by the adjoint emission multiplicity, and validate the method by comparing against an analytical closed-form solution of the adjoint slowing-down equation derived using a source-iteration technique analogous to the classical Placzek function approach.

## Key numbers

- For s-wave scattering with constant cross section, the mean number of pseudo-particles outgoing from a scattering collision is given by $\nu_s^\dagger(E) = -\ln\alpha/(1-\alpha)$, where $\alpha = [(A-1)/(A+1)]^2$ and $A$ is the nucleus mass number.
- The analytical adjoint flux in the first energy interval $E_0 \le E \le E_0/\alpha$ is $\phi^\dagger_1(E) = \frac{1}{E_0\Sigma_s(E_0)(1-\alpha)}\left(\frac{E}{E_0}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}$.
- The analytical adjoint flux in the second energy interval $E_0/\alpha \le E \le E_0/\alpha^2$ is $\phi^\dagger_2(E) = \frac{1}{E_0\Sigma_s(E_0)(1-\alpha)^2}\left(\frac{E}{E_0}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}\left[(1-\alpha)\left(1-\alpha^{1/(1-\alpha)}\right)-\alpha^{1/(1-\alpha)}\ln\frac{\alpha E}{E_0}\right]$.
- The general adjoint flux in the $n$-th energy interval $E_0/\alpha^{n-1} \le E \le E_0/\alpha^n$ is given by a closed-form expression involving powers of $(1-\alpha)$, powers of $\alpha^{1/(1-\alpha)}$, and logarithmic terms up to $\ln^{n-1}(\alpha^n E/E_0)$.

## Context

This work extends the classical theory of neutron importance established by Weinberg and Wigner and formalized by Ussachoff (1955), and builds upon earlier Monte Carlo approaches for adjoint transport by De Matteis, Eriksson, and Irving. The analytical solution for the adjoint slowing-down equation provides a benchmark for validating Monte Carlo codes, analogous to how the classical Placzek functions serve as benchmarks for forward neutron transport. The pseudo-particle sampling technique enables direct Monte Carlo evaluation of the importance function without requiring backward neutron propagation methods as used by Hoogenboom, and can be extended to eigenvalue simulations and spatial problems.
