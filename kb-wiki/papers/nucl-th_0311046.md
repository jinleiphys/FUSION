# Short-range correlations in asymmetric nuclear matter

> The spectral function of protons in the asymmetric nuclear matter is calculated in the self-consistent T-matrix approach. The spectral function per proton increases with increasing asymmetry. This effect and the density dependence of the spectral function partially explain the observed increase of the spectral function with the mass number of the target nuclei in electron scattering experiments.

# Short-range correlations in asymmetric nuclear matter

## Key claim
The proton spectral function per proton increases with increasing neutron-proton asymmetry in nuclear matter, but this effect alone is insufficient to quantitatively explain the observed increase of the scaled spectral function with target mass number in electron scattering experiments; the density dependence of the spectral function also contributes, yet the experimentally observed increase for heavy targets (especially gold) is stronger than the calculation predicts.

## Method
The proton spectral function in asymmetric nuclear matter was calculated using the self-consistent T-matrix approach with fermion propagators dressed in the self-consistent self-energy, employing the CD-Bonn potential. The calculation was performed for a range of densities and asymmetries, and the hole spectral function was obtained from the retarded self-energy. The spectral function for finite nuclei was then computed using the local density approximation, integrating the nuclear matter spectral function over the density profile of the target nucleus.

## Key numbers
- Normal nuclear density used: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Asymmetry ratio studied: Z/N = 1 (symmetric) down to Z/N = 0.7
- The increase of the off-shell hole spectral function per proton when going from carbon to gold target is about a factor of 2
- The depletion of the occupation number inside the Fermi sphere and the high momentum component change by only a few percent when the proton-to-neutron ratio changes from 1 to 0.7

## Context
This work extends prior self-consistent T-matrix calculations (Dickhoff 1999, Bozek 1998, Dewulf 2003, Frick 2003) and Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approaches (Mahaux, Baldo 1992) that were restricted to symmetric or pure neutron nuclear matter. It directly addresses the experimental observation by Rohe (2003) that the scaled proton spectral function increases with target mass number in electron scattering on C, Al, Fe, and Au nuclei, which was interpreted as evidence for strong neutron-proton short-range correlations (Ryckebush). The calculation enables more quantitative comparisons between nuclear matter theory and finite-nuclei electron scattering data, though the residual discrepancy for heavy nuclei (especially gold) indicates that additional mechanisms beyond asymmetry and density dependence are needed.
