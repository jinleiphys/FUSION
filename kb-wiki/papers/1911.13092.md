# Machine learning the deuteron

> We use machine learning techniques to solve the nuclear two-body bound state problem, the deuteron. We use a minimal one-layer, feed-forward neural network to represent the deuteron S- and D-state wavefunction in momentum space, and solve the problem variationally using ready-made machine learning tools. We benchmark our results with exact diagonalisation solutions. We find that a network with 6 hidden nodes (or 24 parameters) can provide a faithful representation of the ground state wavefunction, with a binding energy that is within 0.1% of exact results. This exploratory proof-of-principle simulation may provide insight for future potential solutions of the nuclear many-body problem using variational artificial neural network techniques.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a minimal, single-layer feed-forward neural network with as few as 6 hidden nodes (24 parameters) can represent the deuteron ground-state wavefunction in momentum space variationally, yielding a binding energy within 0.1% of exact diagonalisation results; however, the variational energy never quite reaches the exact benchmark value even as the number of hidden nodes increases, with small discrepancies originating at low and high momenta.

## Method

The authors set up a variational problem in momentum space using a one-layer feed-forward neural network with a single input node (relative momentum q), one hidden layer with N_hid nodes, and two output nodes representing the S-state and D-state wavefunctions. The network parameters (weights and biases) serve as variational parameters to minimise the energy expectation value of the deuteron Hamiltonian with the N3LO Entem-Machleidt nucleon-nucleon force, using a Gauss-Legendre quadrature with 64 points and a tangential change of variables extending to k_max = 500 fm^-1. The variational minimisation proceeds in three steps: random initialisation, supervised training to match physically inspired target wavefunctions, and final energy minimisation using the RMSprop optimiser for up to 250,000 iterations.

## Key numbers

- The exact ground state energy benchmark is E_GS = -2.2267 MeV, obtained from exact diagonalisation using the same momentum quadrature.
- With N_hid = 6 hidden nodes, the variational ANN binding energy is -2.223(1) MeV for sigmoid activation and -2.220(6) MeV for softplus activation, within approximately 0.1% of the exact value.
- With N_hid = 6, the S-state fidelity is 0.99994(2) and the D-state fidelity is 0.99995(4) for sigmoid activation.
- The exact D-state probability is P^D_GS = 4.51%; with N_hid = 6, the ANN gives P^D = 4.51(7)% for sigmoid and 4.50(12)% for softplus.
- With N_hid = 4, the binding energy is -2.15(16) MeV for sigmoid, within about 5% of the benchmark but with significant standard deviation.
- The momentum quadrature uses N_k = 64 Gauss-Legendre points with a tangential change of variables to extend integration from 0 to k_max = 500 fm^-1, with only 7 mesh points beyond k = 5 fm^-1.
- The supervised pre-training step achieves overlaps within 1-5% of the target value K^L = 1, with an initial unphysically large S-D admixture of 50%.

## Context

This work extends the application of variational artificial neural network (VANN) techniques, pioneered by Carleo and Troyer (2017) for spin systems and later applied to bosonic systems by Saito (2017, 2018) and to quantum chemistry by Pfau et al. (2019) and Hermann et al. (2019), to a nuclear bound-state problem for the first time. The deuteron serves as a proof-of-principle test case, and the results suggest that minimal ANN architectures may provide a pathway toward solving the nuclear many-body problem variationally, though challenges remain in incorporating antisymmetrisation, multiple configurations, and spin-isospin degrees of freedom. The authors note that a preprint by Adams et al. (2020) reported similar few-body nuclear results while this manuscript was in preparation.
