# Thermal effects on nuclear symmetry energy with a momentum-dependent effective interaction

> The knowledge of the nuclear symmetry energy of hot neutron-rich matter is important for understanding the dynamical evolution of massive stars and the supernova explosion mechanisms. In particular, the electron capture rate on nuclei and/or free protons in presupernova explosions is especially sensitive to the symmetry energy at finite temperature. In view of the above, in the present work we calculate the symmetry energy as a function of the temperature for various values of the baryon density, by applying a momentum-dependent effective interaction. In addition to a previous work, the thermal effects are studied separately both in the kinetic part and the interaction part of the symmetry energy. We focus also on the calculations of the mean field potential, employed extensively in heavy ion reaction research, both for nuclear and pure neutron matter. The proton fraction and the electron chemical potential, which are crucial quantities for representing the thermal evolution of supernova and neutron stars, are calculated for various values of the temperature. Finally, we construct a temperature dependent equation of state of $β$-stable nuclear matter, the basic ingredient for the evaluation of the neutron star properties.

## Key claim

The nuclear symmetry energy decreases with increasing temperature, and this decrease occurs in both the kinetic and interaction parts of the symmetry energy, with the kinetic part showing a more pronounced thermal effect than the interaction part, especially at low baryon densities.

## Method

The author calculated the energy densities of pure neutron matter and symmetric nuclear matter as functions of baryon density for various fixed temperatures using a momentum-dependent effective interaction model originally introduced by Gale et al. (1987, 1990). The symmetry energy was then obtained from the difference between these two energy densities, and the proton fraction and electron chemical potential were determined by imposing beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions with electrons treated as a relativistic non-degenerate Fermi gas.

## Key numbers

- At zero temperature, the symmetry energy is parameterized as \(E_{sym}^{tot}(u;T=0)=13 u^{2/3}+17u\) where \(u=n/n_0\) and \(n_0=0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- The maximum decrease of \(E_{sym}^{tot}(n;T)\) between \(T=0\) MeV and \(T=50\) MeV is 40% at \(n=0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) and 4% at \(n=1\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- The decrease of \(E_{sym}^{kin}(n;T)\) between \(T=0\) MeV and \(T=50\) MeV is 57% at \(n=0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) and 5% at \(n=1\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- The decrease of \(E_{sym}^{int}(n;T)\) between \(T=0\) MeV and \(T=50\) MeV is 22% at \(n=0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) and 5% at \(n=1\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Thermal effects increase the proton fraction \(x\) between 57% at \(n=0.1\) fm\(^{-3}\) and 2% at \(n=1\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- The interaction part of the symmetry energy at \(T=50\) MeV is parameterized as \(E_{sym}^{int}(u;T=50)=13.404 u^{1.104}\)
- The kinetic part of the symmetry energy at \(T=50\) MeV is parameterized as \(E_{sym}^{kin}(u;T=50)=8.577 u^{0.891}\)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the nuclear symmetry energy at zero temperature by systematically investigating thermal effects separately in the kinetic and interaction contributions, an aspect that had received little theoretical attention. The results agree with those of Xu et al. (2007) despite using a different model, and the temperature-dependent symmetry energy parametrizations enable calculations of proton fraction and electron chemical potential needed for modeling supernova explosions and neutron star thermal evolution. The author notes that experimental data from Texas A and M University (Shetty, 2006) and the INDRA-ALADIN Collaboration at GSI (Le Fevre, 2005) are included for comparison, though the observed evolution of the symmetry energy in experiments is mainly due to density rather than temperature changes.
