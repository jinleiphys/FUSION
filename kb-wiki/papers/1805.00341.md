# Modeling magnetic neutron stars: a short overview

> Neutron stars are the endpoint of the life of intermediate mass stars and posses in their cores matter in the most extreme conditions in the universe. Besides their extremes of temperature (found in proto-neutron stars) and densities, typical neutron star' magnetic fields can easily reach trillions of times higher the one of the Sun. Among these stars, about $10\%$ are denominated \emph{magnetars} which possess even stronger surface magnetic fields of up to $10^{15}-10^{16}\,\mathrm{G}$. In this conference proceeding, we present a short review of the history and current literature regarding the modeling of magnetic neutron stars. Our goal is to present the results regarding the introduction of magnetic fields in the equation of state of matter using Relativistic Mean Field models (RMF models) and in the solution of Einstein's equations coupled to the Maxwell's equations in order to generate a consistent calculation of magnetic stars structure. We discuss how equation of state modeling affects mass, radius, deformation, composition and magnetic field distribution in stars and also what are the open questions in this field of research.

## Key claim
This conference proceeding reviews how magnetic fields are incorporated into both the equation of state (EoS) of neutron star matter and the solution of Einstein-Maxwell equations, but notes that self-consistent calculations combining both effects have only recently been implemented and that many open questions remain, particularly regarding the magnetic field profile inside stars and the inclusion of toroidal field components.

## Method
The authors survey the introduction of magnetic fields into Relativistic Mean Field (RMF) models via Landau quantization of charged particle energy levels and the resulting anisotropy in pressure components (parallel and perpendicular to the field). They then describe the numerical solution of the coupled Einstein-Maxwell equations using the LORENE library, which assumes an axisymmetric metric and a poloidal magnetic field generated by a current function, treating the energy-momentum tensor as a sum of perfect fluid and pure magnetic field contributions.

## Key numbers
- About 10% of neutron stars are magnetars, with surface magnetic fields of up to 10^15-10^16 G.
- Anomalous X-ray Pulsars (AXPs) present rotation periods P ~ 6-12 s and surface magnetic fields B ~ 10^13-10^15 G.
- Observations of source 4U 0142+61 suggest internal magnetic fields of 10^16 G.
- The Virial theorem estimates central magnetic fields can reach up to 10^18-10^20 G.
- For hadronic matter, significant impact from anomalous magnetic moment is only identified for fields up to B ~ 10^18 G.
- The LORENE code imposes a convergence limit of central magnetic fields B_c ~ 10^18 G, which does not depend strongly on the EoS model.
- Neglecting deformation by solving TOV equations leads to a 12% overestimation of maximum mass and a 20% underestimation of equatorial radius for a 1.4 solar mass star at the maximum magnetic field limit.
- The magnetic field profile inside neutron stars grows quadratically with baryon chemical potential in the polar direction, not differing by more than one order of magnitude from the surface field.

## Context
This review extends earlier work on magnetic neutron star modeling by Duncan and Thompson (1992) on the magnetohydrodynamic dynamo mechanism, and by Bonazzola et al. (1993) and Bocquet et al. (1995) on solving Einstein-Maxwell equations. It contrasts with earlier approaches that used ad hoc exponential magnetic field profiles (which do not satisfy Maxwell's equations) and that solved only the spherically symmetric TOV equations. The review enables future work on combined toroidal and poloidal magnetic field configurations, and on detecting particle re-population effects via gravitational wave emission as magnetic fields decay over time.
