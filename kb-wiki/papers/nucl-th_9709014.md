# Cascade-Exciton Model Analysis of Proton Spallation from 10 MeV to 5 GeV

> We have used an extended version of the Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) to analyze more than 600 excitation functions for proton induced reactions on 19 targets ranging from C-12 to Au-197, for incident energies ranging from 10 MeV to 5 GeV. We have compared the calculations to available data, to calculations using approximately two dozen other models, and to predictions of several phenomenological systematics. We present here our conclusions concerning the relative roles of different reaction mechanisms in the production of specific final nuclides. We comment on the strengths and weaknesses of the CEM and suggest possible further improvements to the CEM and to other models.

## Key claim

The extended Cascade-Exciton Model (CEM) as implemented in the CEM95 code describes the majority of measured excitation functions for proton-induced spallation on targets from Al-27 to Au-197 to within a factor of 2, but the agreement is worse for targets lighter than Al-27 and the model cannot reliably predict yields where fragmentation, fission, or alpha-particle emission are dominant.

## Method

The authors used the CEM95 code, an extended version of the Cascade-Exciton Model, to calculate more than 600 excitation functions for proton-induced reactions on 19 targets (C-12 through Au-197) at incident energies from 10 MeV to 5 GeV. They compared these calculations to available experimental data, to predictions from approximately two dozen other models and codes (including ALICE, HETC, LAHET, GNASH, and others), and to several phenomenological systematics. The study examined the dependence of results on input parameters, isotopic composition of targets, and proper modeling of independent versus cumulative yields.

## Key numbers

- More than 600 excitation functions were analyzed for proton-induced reactions on 19 targets from C-12 to Au-197
- Incident energies ranged from 10 MeV to 5 GeV
- For target nuclei from Al-27 to Au-197, CEM95 describes the majority of experimental excitation functions in the spallation region to within a factor of 2
- Cumulative yields of some nuclides are up to two orders of magnitude higher than the independent yields for medium and heavy targets
- Nuclear structure effects manifest themselves strongly even at an incident energy of 5 GeV

## Context

This work extends the Cascade-Exciton Model approach to a comprehensive set of targets and energies, comparing systematically with many other models including ALICE LIVERMORE 87, HETC/KFA-2, LAHET, GNASH, and others. The study identifies specific needed improvements for the CEM, including incorporation of high-energy fission models, fragmentation models for medium and heavy nuclei, Fermi breakup for light nuclei, and better nuclear mass tables. The authors note that excitation functions are a particularly "difficult" test of nuclear reaction models because they involve spallation, evaporation, fission, and fragmentation physics simultaneously, and that no existing code can yet predict arbitrary excitation functions reliably across the full energy range.
