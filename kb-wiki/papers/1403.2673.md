# Progress and open questions in the physics of neutrino cross sections

> New and more precise measurements of neutrino cross sections have renewed the interest in a better understanding of electroweak interactions on nucleons and nuclei. This effort is crucial to achieve the precision goals of the neutrino oscillation program, making new discoveries, like the CP violation in the leptonic sector, possible. We review the recent progress in the physics of neutrino cross sections, putting emphasis on the open questions that arise in the comparison with new experimental data. Following an overview of recent neutrino experiments and future plans, we present some details about the theoretical development in the description of (anti)neutrino-induced quasielastic scattering and the role of multi-nucleon quasielastic-like mechanisms. We cover not only pion production in nucleons and nuclei but also other inelastic channels including strangeness production and photon emission. Coherent reaction channels on nuclear targets are also discussed. Finally, we briefly describe some of the Monte Carlo event generators, which are at the core of all neutrino oscillation and cross section measurements.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while PCAC-based models successfully describe high-energy (Eν ≥ 2 GeV) coherent pion production data, they overestimate cross sections at lower energies (around 1 GeV) compared to more realistic microscopic models; the experimental situation remains puzzling because the SciBooNE measurement of the CC-COHπ⁺ to NC-COHπ⁰ cross section ratio on carbon (0.14⁺⁰·³⁰₋₀·₂₈) contradicts the theoretical expectation of approximately 1.5, representing a more than 4σ discrepancy.

## Method

The authors review theoretical models for neutrino-induced coherent pion, kaon, and photon production on nuclei, comparing PCAC-based approaches (which relate neutrino cross sections to elastic pion-nucleus scattering) with microscopic models that start from weak pion production on nucleons, include Δ(1232) resonance excitation, and incorporate nuclear medium effects such as in-medium Δ modification, pion distortion via optical potentials, and nonlocality in Δ propagation. They also survey recent and future neutrino oscillation experiments (K2K, MINOS, OPERA, MiniBooNE) and the measurements of charged-current quasielastic scattering and coherent pion production that motivate these theoretical developments.

## Key numbers

- SciBooNE measured σ(CC-COHπ⁺)/σ(NC-COHπ⁰) = 0.14⁺⁰·³⁰₋₀·₂₈ on carbon at average neutrino energy around 0.8 GeV, while theory predicts this ratio should be close to 1.5 (more than 4σ discrepancy)
- K2K obtained only an upper bound for CC coherent pion production at ⟨Eν⟩ = 1.2 GeV
- K2K extracted an effective axial vector mass MA = 1.20 ± 0.12 GeV for CCQE scattering from SciFi detector data
- Nonlocal Δ propagation effects can produce reductions as large as a factor of two at Eν ∼ 500 MeV in coherent pion production
- Using improved elastic pion-nucleus cross section data, predicted COHπ cross sections become reduced by a factor of 2-3 for neutrino energies around 1 GeV and light nuclei in the carbon region
- Neutrino COHγ cross sections are about a factor 15 smaller than incoherent ones; antineutrino COHγ cross sections are about a factor 10 smaller
- Coherent K⁺ production cross sections at 1-2 GeV are small, with cross sections per nucleon much smaller than corresponding ones on free nucleons

## Context

This review extends the theoretical work on coherent neutrino reactions following the unexpected K2K upper bound (Hasegawa 2005) and the subsequent measurements by MiniBooNE (Aguilar-Arevalo 2009) and SciBooNE (Kurimoto 2010), which revealed tensions with the PCAC-based Rein-Sehgal model (Rein 1982). The paper enables future experimental investigations at T2K, MINERνA, and other facilities by clarifying the open questions in coherent pion, kaon, and photon production, particularly the need for realistic microscopic calculations that include full nuclear effects and resolve the discrepancy between measured and predicted CC/NC coherent pion ratios.
