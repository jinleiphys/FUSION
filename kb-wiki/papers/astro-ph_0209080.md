# Solar Models: An Historical Overview

> I summarize in four slides the 40 years of development of the standard solar model that is used to predict solar neutrino fluxes and then describe the current uncertainties in the predictions. I next dispel the misconception that the p-p neutrino flux is determined by the solar luminosity and present a related formula that gives, in terms of the p-p and 7Be neutrino fluxes, the ratio of the rates of the two primary ways of terminating the p-p fusion chain. I will also attempt to explain why it took so long, about three and a half decades, to reach a consensus view that new physics is being learned from solar neutrino experiments. Finally, I close with a personal confession.

# Solar Models: An Historical Overview

## Key claim
The standard solar model predictions for solar neutrino fluxes are sufficiently accurate to conclude that new particle physics is required to explain the discrepancy between predicted and observed solar neutrino event rates, but the p-p neutrino flux is NOT determined solely by the solar luminosity as many in the literature have claimed; rather, it depends on the detailed temperature, density, and composition profiles of the solar interior.

## Method
The author traces the 40-year development of the standard solar model used to predict solar neutrino fluxes, from the first calculations in 1962-1963 through the inclusion of element diffusion in the 1990s. The paper presents the current uncertainties in the predictions using partial derivatives and Monte Carlo studies of 1000 standard solar models, where each input parameter was drawn from a probability distribution with the same mean and variance as assigned to that parameter. The author also derives a formula relating the ratio of the two primary terminating reactions of the p-p fusion chain to the measured p-p and 7Be neutrino fluxes.

## Key numbers
- The BP00 standard solar model predicts the 8B neutrino flux as 5.05 (+1.0/-0.8) × 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1
- The combined SNO CC + Super-Kamiokande measurement gives 5.44 ± 0.99 × 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1 for active 8B neutrinos, agreeing with the prediction at 0.3σ
- The SNO NC measurement gives 5.09 ± 0.64 × 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1, agreeing with the prediction at 0.03σ
- The p-p neutrino flux is predicted with ±1% uncertainty; the pep flux with ±1.5% uncertainty
- The 7Be neutrino flux is predicted with ±10% uncertainty; the 8B flux with about ±20% uncertainty
- The maximum possible p-p neutrino flux from solar luminosity alone is 6.51 × 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1; the standard solar model predicts 0.91 times this maximum
- The ratio R of 3He+4He to 3He+3He reaction rates is predicted by the standard solar model to be 0.174
- The 7Be(p,γ)8B cross section factor S17(0) = 19 (1 +0.14/-0.07) eV-b (1σ error is one-third the 3σ estimate from Adelberger et al.)
- The 3He-3He reaction rate changed by a factor of 3.9 between 1962-1968; the p-p reaction rate changed by 7%; the heavy element to hydrogen ratio Z/X decreased by a factor of 2.5
- Helioseismological sound speed measurements agreed with standard solar model predictions to better than 0.1% r.m.s. in the solar interior

## Context
This review extends the author's three decades of work on solar neutrino predictions, including collaborations with Ulrich (1988) on Monte Carlo uncertainty studies and with Pinsonneault, Basu, and Christensen-Dalsgaard (1995-1997) on helioseismological validation of solar models. The paper addresses the long-standing skepticism expressed by Pontecorvo (1968) and later by Georgi and Luke (1990) regarding the reliability of solar model predictions. The work enables precise determination of neutrino oscillation parameters (Δm², tan²θ) from global analyses of solar neutrino experiments and motivates future measurements of the low-energy p-p and 7Be neutrino fluxes to test the predicted ratio of fusion chain termination reactions.
