# Causal dissipative hydrodynamics obtained from the nonextensive/dissipative correspondence

> We derive the constitutive equations of causal relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics ($d$-hydrodynamics) from perfect nonextensive hydrodynamics ($q$-hydrodynamics) using the nonextensive/dissipative correspondence (NexDC) proposed by us recently. The $q$-hydrodynamics can be thus regarded as a possible model for the $d$-hydrodynamics facilitating its application to high energy multiparticle production processes. As an example we have shown that applying the NexDC to the perfect 1+1 $q$-hydrodynamics, one obtains a proper time evolution of the bulk pressure and the Reynolds number.

# Causal dissipative hydrodynamics obtained from the nonextensive/dissipative correspondence

## Key claim

The constitutive equations of causal relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics (d-hydrodynamics) can be derived from perfect nonextensive hydrodynamics (q-hydrodynamics) via the nonextensive/dissipative correspondence (NexDC), provided the nonextensivity parameter q is close to 1 (q-1 ≪ 1) so that the stationary state described by q-hydrodynamics can be regarded as a near-equilibrium state relative to the true equilibrium state at q=1.

## Method

The authors map a perfect q-hydrodynamic flow (based on Tsallis nonextensive statistics with parameter q) into a dissipative d-hydrodynamic flow by imposing relations between the energy densities and pressures of the two formulations: P(T) = P_q(T_q) and ε(T) = ε_q(T_q) + 3Π, where Π is identified as the bulk pressure. Using these NexDC relations together with the conservation of q-entropy and identities derived from the dissipative tensor relations, they obtain constitutive equations for d-hydrodynamics that include relaxation times and heat-viscous coupling lengths. The method is applied to a 1+1 dimensional Bjorken-type scaling flow of a relativistic pion gas to compute the proper time evolution of temperature, bulk pressure, and Reynolds number.

## Key numbers

- Initial proper time τ₀ = 1.0 fm for the q-hydrodynamical evolution
- Initial temperature T₀ ≡ T(τ₀) = 0.2 GeV for all values of q
- Pion mass m = 0.14 GeV used in the equation of state
- For comparison, maximal inverse Reynolds numbers from Baier (2008) are Rₑ⁻¹ ≈ 0.065 (for η/s = 0.08 at τ ≈ 2 fm) and Rₑ⁻¹ ≈ 0.21 (for η/s = 0.30 at τ ≈ 4 fm)
- The inverse Reynolds number from the q-hydrodynamics model shows a maximum at τ ∼ 10 fm, with values smaller than those quoted by Baier (2008)
- The q parameter ranges explored: q = 1.00 to 1.50

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously proposed nonextensive/dissipative correspondence (Osada, 2008) by demonstrating that the d-hydrodynamics obtained from perfect q-hydrodynamics preserves causality, addressing a key difficulty in dissipative hydrodynamic models. It builds on the causal dissipative hydrodynamics framework developed by Muronga (2004), Heinz (2005), Baier (2005), Koide (2007), and Chaudhuri (2006), and on the relativistic nonextensive kinetic theory of Lavagno (2002) and Lima (2001). The approach offers an alternative to solving complicated second-order equations of d-hydrodynamics by instead solving the simpler equations of q-hydrodynamics, with the nonextensivity parameter q serving as a phenomenological descriptor of the fluid's non-ideality. The small inverse Reynolds numbers obtained suggest stable hydrodynamic evolution for strongly interacting quark/hadronic matter.
