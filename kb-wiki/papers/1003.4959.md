# Neutrino Background Flux from Sources of Ultrahigh-Energy Cosmic-Ray Nuclei

> Motivated by Pierre Auger Observatory results favoring a heavy nuclear composition for ultrahigh-energy (UHE) cosmic rays, we investigate implications for the cumulative neutrino background. The requirement that nuclei not be photodisintegrated constrains their interactions in sources, therefore limiting neutrino production via photomeson interactions. Assuming a $dN_{\rm CR}/dE_{\rm CR} \propto E_{\rm CR}^{-2}$ injection spectrum and photodisintegration via the giant dipole resonance, the background flux of neutrinos is lower than $E_ν^2 Φ_ν\sim {10}^{-9} {\rm GeV} {\rm cm}^{-2} {\rm s}^{-1} {\rm sr}^{-1}$ if UHE nuclei ubiquitously survive in their sources. This is smaller than the analogous Waxman-Bahcall flux for UHE protons by about one order of magnitude, and is below the projected IceCube sensitivity. If IceCube detects a neutrino background, it could be due to other sources, e.g., hadronuclear interactions of lower-energy cosmic rays; if it does not, this supports our strong restrictions on the properties of sources of UHE nuclei.

# Neutrino Background Flux from Sources of Ultrahigh-Energy Cosmic-Ray Nuclei

## Key claim

If ultrahigh-energy cosmic-ray (UHECR) nuclei ubiquitously survive photodisintegration in their sources, the cumulative neutrino background flux is at least one order of magnitude lower than the analogous Waxman-Bahcall flux for UHE protons, and below the projected IceCube sensitivity; however, this landmark applies only under the condition that all UHECR sources satisfy the nucleus-survival requirement, and a minority of sources with high target photon densities could raise the flux.

## Method

The authors derived new neutrino background landmarks by imposing the condition that UHECR nuclei (assumed to be heavy, e.g., iron-like) must survive photodisintegration via the giant dipole resonance (GDR) in their sources, which limits the target photon density and therefore suppresses photomeson neutrino production. They calculated the photodisintegration optical depth for a power-law target photon spectrum and related it to the photomeson production optical depth using resonance approximations for both the GDR and the Delta resonance, then inserted the resulting upper bound on the photomeson optical depth into the standard relation between cosmic-ray injection rate and neutrino flux. Two variants were considered: a strict condition requiring no photodisintegration at all (tau_{A gamma} < 1) and a more conservative condition requiring only that the effective energy-loss optical depth be less than unity (f_{A gamma} < 1).

## Key numbers

- For the strict nucleus-survival condition (tau_{A gamma} < 1) with an E^{-2} injection spectrum and iron-like nuclei (A=56), the muon neutrino plus antineutrino background flux is bounded by E_nu^2 Phi_nu less than or similar to 1.5 x 10^{-11} f_z GeV cm^{-2} s^{-1} sr^{-1}, where f_z is a redshift evolution factor (0.6 for no evolution, 3 for fast evolution).
- For the more conservative condition (f_{A gamma} < 1) with the same assumptions, the bound is E_nu^2 Phi_nu less than or similar to 8.4 x 10^{-10} f_z (A/56)^{-0.21} GeV cm^{-2} s^{-1} sr^{-1}, which is still about one order of magnitude below the Waxman-Bahcall flux of approximately 1.0 x 10^{-8} f_z GeV cm^{-2} s^{-1} sr^{-1} for protons.
- The photomeson optical depth under the strict nucleus-survival condition is bounded by f_mes less than or similar to 1.5 x 10^{-3} (A/56)^{-1.21} for A > 4.
- For the conservative condition, the bound is f_mes less than or similar to 8.2 x 10^{-2} (A/56)^{-0.21}.

## Context

This work extends the Waxman-Bahcall (1998) and Mannheim-Protheroe-Rachen (2001) neutrino background landmarks for UHE protons to the case of UHE nuclei, motivated by Pierre Auger Observatory results favoring a heavy nuclear composition (PAO 2009). The key difference is that nuclei are constrained by photodisintegration rather than photomeson interactions, leading to much stricter limits on source photon densities. The results enable future IceCube and KM3Net observations to test whether UHECR sources universally satisfy the nucleus-survival condition; a non-detection would support strong restrictions on source properties, while a detection could indicate proton sources, hidden accelerators, or hadronuclear processes from lower-energy cosmic rays.
