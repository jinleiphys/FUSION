# Emergence of double scaling law in complex system

> We introduce a stochastic model to explain a double power-law distribution which exhibits two different Paretian behaviors in the upper and the lower tail and widely exists in social and economic systems. The model incorporates fitness consideration and noise fluctuation. We find that if the number of variables (e.g. the degree of nodes in complex networks or people's incomes) grows exponentially, normal distributed fitness coupled with exponentially increasing variable is responsible for the emergence of the double power-law distribution. Fluctuations do not change the result qualitatively but contribute to the second-part scaling exponent. The evolution of Chinese airline network is taken as an example to show a nice agreement with our stochastic model.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a double power-law distribution (two different Paretian behaviors in the upper and lower tails) emerges when normally distributed fitness is coupled with an exponentially growing number of variables; noise fluctuations do not change the qualitative shape but do contribute to the second scaling exponent, and the model is validated against the Chinese airline network.

## Method

The authors introduce a stochastic model where each variable \(k_i\) grows exponentially according to \(dk_i/dt = \eta_i k_i\), with fitness \(\eta_i\) drawn from a normal distribution, and the total number of variables \(N(t)\) grows exponentially as \(N(t) \propto e^{c_n t}\). They derive the resulting distribution analytically for limiting cases and perform numerical simulations for finite \(\sigma_\eta\). The model is then generalized by adding a noise term to produce \(dk_i = \rho\eta_i k_i dt + (1-\rho)(\mu_\eta dt + \sigma dw)k_i\), and the evolution of the Chinese airline network (CAN) is analyzed empirically to extract parameters and compare with the model predictions.

## Key numbers

- For the Chinese airline network, the total number of nodes grows as \(N(t) \propto e^{c_n t}\) with \(c_n \approx 0.088\).
- The number of edges grows as \(M(t) \propto e^{c_m t}\) with \(c_m \approx 0.154\).
- The first scaling exponent \(\gamma_1\) of the CAN degree distribution stabilizes at about 0.51 (observed from 1999 to 2003 and in 2008).
- The second scaling exponent \(\gamma_2\) of the CAN degree distribution fluctuates between 2.1 and 2.7 over the same period.
- The turning point \(k_c\) in the CAN degree distribution increases from 18 (1999) to 30 (2008).
- The mean fitness \(\mu_\eta\) for CAN is 0.18, with standard deviation \(\sigma_\eta = 0.02\).
- The noise increment \(d\varepsilon(t)\) follows a normal distribution with mean 0 and standard variance 0.09.
- The simulated first exponent from the model for CAN is \(\gamma_1 = 0.61\), compared to the observed 0.51; the simulated second exponent is \(\gamma_2 = 2.84\), compared to the observed 2.7.
- The transition point for \(\sigma_\eta\) is determined numerically as \(\sigma_{tr} \approx \mu_\eta / 2\).

## Context

This work extends earlier models by Reed (2001), who used geometric Brownian motion with exponentially distributed evolution time to produce a double Pareto-lognormal distribution for incomes, and by Dorogovtsev and Mendes (2001), who modeled the word network with fixed scaling exponents of -1.5 and -3. The present model generalizes these by incorporating normally distributed fitness and noise fluctuations, allowing variable scaling exponents. The empirical analysis of the Chinese airline network provides direct evidence for the model assumptions, showing that GDP (as a fitness proxy) correlates linearly with node degree, and that the degree evolution follows the proposed stochastic differential equation. This enables future studies to apply the same framework to other social and economic systems exhibiting double power-law distributions.
