# Relativistic two-body currents for one-nucleon knockout in electron-nucleus scattering

> We present a detailed study of the contribution from two-body currents to the one-nucleon knockout process induced by electromagnetic interaction. The framework is a relativistic mean-field model (RMF) in which bound and scattering nucleons are consistently described as solutions of Dirac equation with potentials. We show results obtained with the most general expression of the two-body operator, in which the intermediate nucleons are described by relativistic mean-field bound states; then, we propose two approximations consisting in describing the intermediate states as nucleons in a relativistic Fermi gas, preserving the complexity and consistency in the initial and final states. These approximations simplify the calculations considerably, allowing us to provide outcomes in a reasonable computational time. The results obtained under these approximations are validated by comparing with those from the full model. Additionally, the theoretical predictions are compared with experimental data of the longitudinal and transverse responses of carbon 12. The agreement with data is outstanding for the longitudinal response, where the contribution from the two-body operator is negligible. In the transverse sector, the two-body current increases the response from 30 to 15%, depending on the approximations and kinematics, in general, improving the agreement with data.

## Key claim

The paper presents a fully relativistic model for one-nucleon knockout in electron-nucleus scattering that includes two-body meson-exchange currents (MEC). The key claim is that the inclusion of two-body currents increases the transverse electromagnetic response of carbon-12 by 30% to 15% (depending on the approximation and kinematics), improving agreement with experimental data, while the longitudinal response is hardly affected; however, the magnitude of this increase is reduced when the intermediate bound-nucleon state is described more realistically (with RMF bound states) compared to simpler approximations (RFG or RFG*).

## Method

The authors use a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model for bound nucleon states and an energy-dependent RMF (ED-RMF) potential for the final scattering state, both derived from Dirac equation solutions. They compute the two-body current from one-pion exchange, including Delta-resonance mechanisms and background terms from chiral perturbation theory (ChPT), and evaluate the hadronic tensor for inclusive longitudinal and transverse responses of carbon-12. To manage computational cost, they introduce two approximations for the intermediate bound-nucleon state in the two-body current: a relativistic Fermi gas (RFG) and a modified RFG (RFG*) with effective mass and vector energy, comparing these to the full RMF treatment.

## Key numbers

* For carbon-12 at transferred momentum q = 380 MeV/c, the two-body current increases the transverse response by up to 31% (intermediate RFG-nucleon), 25% (intermediate RFG*-nucleon), and 19% (intermediate RMF-nucleon) relative to the one-body current result.
* The Fermi momentum used for carbon-12 in the RFG approximation is 228 MeV, for both protons and neutrons.
* The modified RFG* approximation uses an effective mass m* = 0.8 and a vector energy E_v = 141 MeV for carbon-12.
* The strong form factor cutoff for the pion-nucleon vertex is Lambda = 1.3 GeV, and for the pion-Delta vertex it is Lambda^2_{pi Delta N} = 1.5 M_N^2.
* The occupation numbers for carbon-12 shells are reduced from the independent-particle shell model: 3.3 for the 1s_{1/2} shell and 1.8 for the 1p_{3/2} shell.

## Context

This work extends previous relativistic models of quasielastic electron scattering that used a relativistic Fermi gas approach for two-body currents, such as those by Amaro et al. (2002) and Dekker et al. (1994), by implementing a fully consistent RMF description of the intermediate bound-nucleon state. The results agree with recent non-relativistic ab initio calculations by Lovato et al. (2016), showing an increase in the transverse response and little effect in the longitudinal sector. The model enables future application to neutrino-nucleus interactions for experiments like DUNE and MicroBooNE, and can be extended to heavier nuclei such as argon-40.
