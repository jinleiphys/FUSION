# Gadolinium in water Cherenkov detectors improves detection of supernova $ν_e$

> Detecting supernova $ν_e$ is essential for testing supernova and neutrino physics, but the yields are small and the backgrounds from other channels large, e.g., $\sim 10^2$ and $\sim 10^4$ events, respectively, in Super-Kamiokande. We develop a new way to isolate supernova $ν_e$, using gadolinium-loaded water Cherenkov detectors. The forward-peaked nature of $ν_e + e^- \rightarrow ν_e + e^-$ allows an angular cut that contains the majority of events. Even in a narrow cone, near-isotropic inverse beta events, $\barν_e + p \rightarrow e^+ + n$, are a large background. With neutron detection by radiative capture on gadolinium, the background events can be individually identified with high efficiency. The remaining backgrounds are smaller and can be measured separately, so they can be statistically subtracted. Super-Kamiokande with gadolinium could measure the total and average energy of supernova $ν_e$ with $\sim$ $20\%$ precision or better each ($90\%$ C.L.). Hyper-Kamiokande with gadolinium could improve this by a factor of $\sim$ 5. This precision will allow powerful tests of supernova neutrino emission, neutrino mixing, and exotic physics. Unless very large liquid argon or liquid scintillator detectors are built, this is the only way to guarantee precise measurements of supernova $ν_e$.

## Key claim

The paper develops a new method to isolate supernova νe events in water Cherenkov detectors by using gadolinium (Gd) to tag neutrons from inverse beta decay backgrounds, enabling measurements of the total and average energy of supernova νe with ∼20% precision or better (90% C.L.) in Super-Kamiokande, provided that the forward-peaked nature of νe + e− → νe + e− scattering is exploited and backgrounds are statistically subtracted.

## Method

The authors propose using νe + e− → νe + e− elastic scattering in a water Cherenkov detector, selecting events within a forward 40° cone (containing ∼68% of signal events) relative to the known supernova direction. The dominant background from near-isotropic inverse beta events (ν̄e + p → e+ + n) is reduced by detecting the neutron via radiative capture on gadolinium (∼90% efficiency), which produces detectable gamma rays of ∼8 MeV total energy. The remaining backgrounds from ν̄e + e−, νx + e−, and residual inverse beta events are statistically subtracted using precisely measured ν̄e and νx spectra from other channels.

## Key numbers

- Super-Kamiokande with gadolinium could measure the total and average energy of supernova νe with ∼20% precision or better each (90% C.L.)
- Hyper-Kamiokande with gadolinium could improve this precision by a factor of ∼5
- Expected νe + e− events in Super-K for a supernova at 10 kpc: 188 events (⟨Eνe⟩ = 12 MeV), 203 events (⟨Eνe⟩ = 15 MeV), 212 events (⟨Eνe⟩ = 18 MeV)
- Expected inverse beta events in Super-K: 5662 (⟨Eν̄e⟩ = 12 MeV), 7071 (⟨Eν̄e⟩ = 15 MeV), 8345 (⟨Eν̄e⟩ = 18 MeV)
- Neutron capture on gadolinium occurs in ∼20 μs (compared to ∼200 μs for capture on protons)
- Angular cut of 40° half-angle contains ∼68% of νe + e− events
- Neutron detection efficiency assumed to be 90% for Gd-loaded Super-K
- Supernova assumed at distance of 10 kpc with total binding energy 3 × 1053 erg, equipartitioned among 6 species (∼5 × 1052 erg per flavor)

## Context

This work extends the proposal by Beacom and Vagins (2003) to add gadolinium to Super-Kamiokande for improved ν̄e detection, showing that Gd also enables precise νe measurements that were previously considered difficult. The method addresses the long-standing problem noted by Haxton (1987) and others that νe detection in water Cherenkov detectors is challenging due to small yields (∼102 events) compared to large inverse beta backgrounds (∼104 events). The technique enables tests of supernova neutrino emission models, neutrino mixing scenarios (including distinguishing cases where mixing has or has not interchanged flavor spectra), and exotic physics, and is presented as the only guaranteed way to achieve precise νe measurements unless very large liquid argon or liquid scintillator detectors are built.
