# Intermittency route to chaos for the nuclear billiard - a quantitative study

> We extended a previous qualitative study of the intermittent behaviour of a chaotical nucleonic system, by adding a few quantitative analyses: of the configuration and kinetic energy spaces, power spectra, Shannon entropies, and Lyapunov exponents. The system is regarded as a classical "nuclear billiard" with an oscillating surface of a 2D Woods-Saxon potential well. For the monopole and dipole vibrational modes we bring new arguments in favour of the idea that the degree of chaoticity increases when shifting the oscillation frequency from the adiabatic to the resonance stage of the interaction. The order-chaos-order-chaos sequence is also thoroughly investigated and we find that, for the monopole deformation case, an intermittency pattern is again found. Moreover, coupling between one-nucleon and collective degrees of freedom is proved to be essential in obtaining chaotic states.

## Key claim

The paper provides quantitative evidence that, for a classical "nuclear billiard" with an oscillating Woods-Saxon potential well, the degree of chaoticity increases when the oscillation frequency is shifted from the adiabatic regime to the resonance stage of the interaction, and that an intermittency pattern (order-chaos-order-chaos) is again found for the monopole deformation case at a specific frequency, with the coupling between one-nucleon and collective degrees of freedom being essential for obtaining chaotic states.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the coupled Hamilton equations for a system of A nucleons (treated as classical, spinless point particles) confined in a two-dimensional Woods-Saxon potential well with an oscillating surface, using a Runge-Kutta algorithm (order 2-3) with optimized step size. They analyzed the monopole (L=0), dipole (L=1), and quadrupole (L=2) vibrational modes of the potential well, scanning oscillation frequencies from the adiabatic regime (0.02 c/fm) up to the resonance regime (0.145 c/fm), and performed quantitative analyses of configuration and kinetic energy spaces, power spectra, Shannon entropies, and Lyapunov exponents.

## Key numbers

- The one-nucleon collisional frequency (resonance frequency) is approximately 0.145 c/fm, calculated as ω_part = (π/R_0) * sqrt(2T/m) with R_0 = 6 fm and T = 36 MeV.
- For the uncoupled case (UCE), the fundamental frequency of the radial sawtooth temporal series is ω_0_UCE = 0.106 c/fm, with harmonics at 0.212, 0.318, and 0.424 c/fm.
- For the monopole intermittency "window" at Ω = 0.1 c/fm, the fundamental frequency is ω_0_int = 0.128 c/fm, with harmonics at 0.256, 0.384, and 0.512 c/fm.
- The Shannon entropy at t = 800 fm/c for the monopole case at resonance (Ω = 0.145 c/fm) is 4.1589, with 100% phase space filling; at the intermittency frequency (Ω = 0.1 c/fm) it is 3.6889 with only 62.50% filling.
- The time to reach 100% phase space filling for the monopole case is 729 fm/c at resonance, 11,442 fm/c at the intermittency frequency (Ω = 0.1 c/fm), and 6,023 fm/c at the adiabatic frequency (Ω = 0.02 c/fm).
- For the monopole case at resonance, a pack of five close trajectories reaches maximum Shannon entropy (1.60944) after 279 fm/c; at the intermittency frequency this takes 4,133 fm/c.
- The uncoupled case (UCE) never reaches 100% phase space filling even after 100,000 fm/c, and a trajectory pack never reaches maximum entropy within 10,000 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends a previous qualitative study by the same group (Felea 2009a) that identified an intermittent route to chaos for monopole vibrations near the resonance phase, by adding quantitative analyses of configuration and kinetic energy spaces, power spectra, Shannon entropies, and Lyapunov exponents. The study builds on the classical "nuclear billiard" model originally proposed by Burgio, Baldo et al. (1995, 1996, 1998) for studying dissipation of collective vibrational energy into thermal motion, and it complements earlier work by Felea et al. (2001, 2002) and Bordeianu et al. (2008a, 2008b, 2008c) on how the path to chaos changes with multipole order. The results enable future studies to classify the type of intermittency according to the Pomeau-Manneville scheme (Pomeau 1980) and to compare with experimental intermittency patterns from other systems.
