# Recombination in nuclear collisions

> Recombination is a hadronization process that converts partons to hadrons at late time, but the description has no quantitative significance without some meaningful input on the parton distributions at earlier time. Thus observations of particle spectra and correlations have definitive implications on the partonic processes at all transverse momenta. After presenting a general review of the subject at the Workshop, I selected two topics in nuclear collisions for more detailed discussion, which are summarized here. One is on the azimuthal anisotropy at low $p_T$ due to hard or semihard scattering of partons that create ridges with or without triggers. The ridge particles are the products of recombination of thermal partons enhanced by the energy loss of hard or semihard partons. Their $ϕ$ dependence at midrapidity can be determined essentially from geometry. The other topic is on the scaling behavior in $ϕ$ and centrality at high $p_T$, where the hadronization process is dominated by TS and SS recombination. The relevant RHIC data are found to have the same scaling behavior. But at LHC such scaling is badly broken at $p_T \sim 10$ GeV/c if two-jet recombination is important. At the end of this article some comments are made to relate our study of the effects of semihard partons to the observation of minijets in the analyses of STAR data.

## Key claim

Recombination can describe hadronization across all transverse momenta, but the approach has no quantitative significance without meaningful input on parton distributions at earlier times; the paper argues that semihard parton scatterings near the surface can produce azimuthal anisotropy at low pT without requiring rapid thermalization or hydrodynamics, and that at LHC energies two-jet recombination may break the scaling behavior observed at RHIC.

## Method

The author reviews the recombination formalism and then applies it to two specific problems in nuclear collisions. For azimuthal anisotropy at low pT, the ridge contribution to the single-particle distribution is modeled as proportional to a surface segment S(phi,b), which is analytically determined from the geometry of the elliptical nuclear overlap without hydrodynamics. For high-pT scaling, a dynamical path length xi is defined from the geometrical path length weighted by medium density, and the nuclear modification factor RAA is computed from thermal-shower (TS) and shower-shower (SS) recombination components, with one adjustable parameter gamma for momentum degradation.

## Key numbers

- For mid-central collisions (b approximately 1 in units of RA), there is a large difference between emission at phi=0 and phi=pi/2 as shown by the surface segment S(phi,b).
- The Gaussian width for the correlation between semihard parton direction phi1 and ridge particle direction phi2 is sigma approximately 0.33.
- The momentum degradation parameter gamma is found to be 0.11, fitted to RAA data for 4 < pT < 5 GeV/c.
- At pT=4.5 GeV/c, the pion RAA follows RAA^pi(xi) = exp(-2.6 xi) as an exponential suppression with average dynamical path length.
- At LHC, for pT=10 GeV/c, two-jet recombination can produce RAA values that exceed 1 and break the scaling behavior seen at RHIC.

## Context

This paper extends the recombination approach previously developed by Hwa and collaborators (the valon model, shower parton distributions) to address ridge production and scaling behavior in nuclear collisions. It contrasts with conventional hydrodynamical explanations of elliptic flow (Kolb and Heinz; Danielewicz and collaborators) and with the fragmentation-dominated two-component model of the UW-UTA alliance (Trainor and collaborators). The work enables predictions for LHC where two-jet recombination may become observable, and suggests that the surface segment S(phi,b) can unify triggered and untriggered ridge measurements.
