# Is the magnetic field making the inner crust of neutron stars more heterogeneous?

> We study the effect of strong magnetic fields, of the order of $10^{15}-10^{17}$ G, on the extension of the crust of magnetized neutron stars. The dynamical instability region of neutron-proton-electron ($npe$) matter at subsaturation densities and the mode with the largest growth rate are determined within a relativistic mean field model. It is shown that a strong magnetic field has a large effect on the instability region, defining the crust-core transition as a wide density range.

## Key claim

Strong magnetic fields (10^15–10^17 G) significantly alter the dynamical spinodal region of neutron-proton-electron matter at subsaturation densities, creating a banded structure with disconnected unstable regions that make the inner crust more heterogeneous than previously expected; however, the effect is very sensitive to the density dependence of the symmetry energy, and for sufficiently weak fields the finite-B spinodal converges to the B=0 result.

## Method

The authors performed linearized Vlasov calculations within a relativistic mean-field (RMF) framework, including the anomalous magnetic moments (AMM) of protons and neutrons, to determine the dynamical spinodal surface and the most unstable longitudinal modes for magnetized npe matter at zero temperature. They used the NL3 and NL3ωρ parametrizations (with symmetry energy slopes L=118 and 55 MeV, respectively) and considered magnetic field intensities from 2.2×10^14 G to 4.4×10^17 G, solving the dispersion relation for a wave number k=50 MeV to identify unstable regions and the mode with the largest growth rate.

## Key numbers

- For NL3 (L=118 MeV) at B=4.4×10^16 G and the crust-core proton fraction y_p=0.02, the dynamical spinodal extension is reduced to ρ~0.16 fm^-3; decreasing the field to 2.2×10^14 G reduces the extension to 0.105 fm^-3, converging to the B=0 result of 0.056 fm^-3.
- For NL3ωρ (L=55 MeV) at B=4.4×10^16 G and y_p=0.035, the dynamical spinodal extension is ρ~0.115 fm^-3; at 2.2×10^14 G it decreases to 0.102 fm^-3, converging to the B=0 result of 0.084 fm^-3.
- The magnitude of the growth rate at a density 10% larger than the B=0 crust-core transition density decreases with field: for NL3, from 0.3855 MeV at 4.4×10^16 G to 0.0921 MeV at 8.8×10^15 G and 0.0068 MeV at 4.4×10^14 G; for NL3ωρ, from 0.1481 MeV to 0.0378 MeV and 0.0020 MeV respectively.
- The extension of disconnected unstable regions depends on the symmetry energy slope L: for L=55 MeV the unstable region extends only to ρ=0.113 fm^-3, increasing to ρ~0.12, 0.13, and 0.16 fm^-3 for L=68, 88, and 118 MeV respectively under stellar matter conditions.
- For y_p=0.1, unstable regions still appear for ρ≤0.11 fm^-3 (NL3ωρ, L=55 MeV) and ρ≤0.135 fm^-3 (NL3, L=118 MeV).
- Above the B=0 crust-core transition density, the proton-neutron density fluctuation ratio δρ_p/δρ_n increases from ~0.35 to more than double for NL3ωρ and by a factor of 5 for NL3.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the thermodynamical spinodal in magnetized matter by Rabhi (2009), which considered fields ≥5×10^18 G without electrons or Coulomb effects, and complements a previous Thomas-Fermi calculation by Lima (2013) that neglected AMM and considered only fields above 10^17 G with large proton fractions. The results support the proposal by Pons (2013) that a highly resistive amorphous layer in the inner crust could explain the absence of isolated X-ray pulsars with periods above 12 s, and suggest that the heterogeneous structure may affect pulsar glitch mechanisms, as discussed by Link (1999) and in recent anti-glitch observations.
