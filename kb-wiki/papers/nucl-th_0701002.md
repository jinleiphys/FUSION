# What Do Electromagnetic Plasmas Tell Us about Quark-Gluon Plasma?

> Since the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) reveals some obvious similarities to the well-known electromagnetic plasma (EMP), an accumulated knowledge on EMP can be used in the QGP studies. After discussing similarities and differences of the two systems, we present theoretical tools which are used to describe the plasmas. The tools include: kinetic theory, hydrodynamic approach and diagrammatic perturbative methods. We consider collective phenomena in the plasma with a particular emphasis on instabilities which crucially influence temporal evolution of the system. Finally, properties of strongly coupled plasma are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the accumulated knowledge on electromagnetic plasmas (EMP) can be applied to quark-gluon plasma (QGP) studies due to similarities between QCD and QED, but it emphasizes that important differences exist, including the non-Abelian nature of QCD, the relativistic nature of QGP, and the absence of a mass hierarchy analogous to electrons and ions in EMP.

## Method

The authors present a comparative review of theoretical tools used to describe both EMP and QGP, including kinetic theory, hydrodynamic approaches, and diagrammatic perturbative methods. They derive the polarization tensor for an ultrarelativistic electron-positron equilibrium plasma using the linear response approximation of the transport equation in the collisionless limit, and they discuss the more complex transport theory of QGP where distribution functions are matrices in color space.

## Key numbers

* The Debye mass in QGP is of order \(gT\) where \(g\) is the QCD constant and \(T\) is the temperature, leading to roughly \(1/g^3\) partons in the Debye sphere in the weakly coupled regime.
* The thermal photon mass in an ultrarelativistic equilibrium electron-positron plasma is \(m_\gamma \equiv eT/3\).
* The fine structure constant in Gauss units is \(\alpha = e^2 \approx 1/137\).

## Context

This review extends the comparison between EMP and QGP that began with early QGP studies in the mid-1980s, referencing textbooks by Krall and Trivelpiece (1973) and Ichimaru (1973) for EMP principles, and the three-volume set edited by Hwa for QGP physics. The paper enables future work on collective phenomena in QGP, particularly instabilities that influence temporal evolution, and on properties of strongly coupled plasma, building on the kinetic theory formulation of Elze and Heinz (1989) and Mrowczynski (1998).
