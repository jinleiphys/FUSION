# Astrophysical implications of the proton-proton cross section updates

> The p(p,e^+ ν_e)^2H reaction rate is an essential ingredient for theoretical computations of stellar models. In the past several values of the corresponding S-factor have been made available by different authors. Prompted by a recent evaluation of S(E), we analysed the effect of the adoption of different proton-proton reaction rates on stellar models, focusing, in particular, on the age of mid and old stellar clusters (1-12 Gyr) and on standard solar model predictions. By comparing different widely adopted p(p,e^+ ν_e)^2H reaction rates, we found a maximum difference in the temperature regimes typical of main sequence hydrogen-burning stars (5x10^6 - 3x10^7 K) of about 3%. Such a variation translates into a change of cluster age determination lower than 1%. A slightly larger effect is observed in the predicted solar neutrino fluxes with a maximum difference, in the worst case, of about 8%. Finally we also notice that the uncertainty evaluation of the present proton-proton rate is at the level of few \permil, thus the p(p,e^+ ν_e)^2H reaction rate does not constitute anymore a significant uncertainty source in stellar models.

## Key claim

The adoption of different widely used proton-proton reaction rates leads to a maximum difference of about 3% in the temperature regimes typical of main sequence hydrogen-burning stars (5x10^6 to 3x10^7 K), but this translates into a change of cluster age determination lower than 1% and a maximum difference in solar neutrino fluxes of about 8% in the worst case; the authors conclude that the p(p,e+ νe)2H reaction rate no longer constitutes a significant uncertainty source in stellar models, given its uncertainty is at the level of few per mil.

## Method

The authors computed stellar evolutionary tracks for masses 0.4 to 1.5 solar masses and isochrones for ages 1 to 12 Gyr using the PROSECCO stellar evolutionary code, adopting the Marcucci et al. (2013) p-p reaction rate (with and without P-partial wave contributions) as reference and comparing results against rates from the NACRE99, AD11, and JINA compilations. They performed a differential analysis for two chemical compositions ([Fe/H]=+0.0 and [Fe/H]=-1.0) and calculated standard solar models by tuning initial helium, metallicity, and mixing length to reproduce observed solar luminosity, radius, and surface (Z/X) ratio.

## Key numbers

- Maximum difference among p-p reaction rates in the temperature range 5x10^6 to 3x10^7 K: about 3%
- Change in cluster age determination from adopting different p-p rates: lower than 1%
- Maximum difference in predicted solar neutrino fluxes (worst case, NACRE99 rate): about 8% for 15O and 17F neutrino fluxes
- S(0) value from Marcucci et al. (2013) including P-partial waves: (4.030 ± 0.006) x 10^(-25) MeV b
- S(0) value from NACRE99 compilation: 3.94 x 10^(-25) MeV b
- S(0) value from AD11 compilation: (4.01 ± 0.04) x 10^(-25) MeV b
- Maximum difference in H-exhaustion time from NACRE99 rate relative to reference: lower than 5-6 per mil
- Solar central temperature difference from NACRE99 rate: about 3 per mil
- Reference solar pp neutrino flux: 6.020 x 10^10 s^(-1) cm^(-2)
- Reference solar 8B neutrino flux: 3.694 x 10^6 s^(-1) cm^(-2)
- Effect of P-partial wave inclusion on 8B neutrino flux: 3%

## Context

This work extends the analysis of p-p reaction rate uncertainties on stellar models previously discussed by Chaboyer (1996), Brocato et al. (1998), and Valle et al. (2013a, 2013b), and is prompted by the recent chiral effective field theory calculation of the S-factor by Marcucci et al. (2013), which reduced the theoretical uncertainty by about a factor seven compared to the Adelberger et al. (2011) evaluation. The results enable stellar modelers to treat the p-p reaction rate as a negligible source of uncertainty in cluster age dating and standard solar model calculations, allowing focus on other input physics uncertainties such as chemical composition, opacities, and other nuclear reaction rates.
