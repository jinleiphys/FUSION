# Entropy Production via Particle Production

> I critically examine the notion of ``irreversibility,'' and discuss in what sense it applies to the spontaneous creation of particles in external fields. The investigation reveals that particle creation in very strong fields can only be described by a non-Markovian transport theory.

## Key claim

Spontaneous particle creation in external fields is strictly irreversible (i.e., the relevant entropy increases monotonically) only when the field is weak; for fields at or above the critical strength, memory effects cause temporary violations of the H-theorem, so the process must be described by a non-Markovian transport theory.

## Method

The author analyzes the Schwinger mechanism, spontaneous creation of electron-positron pairs in a constant, homogeneous external electric field, as a microscopic model. Using the projection method, the equation of motion for the occupation numbers of momentum states is derived, revealing a non-Markovian source term that involves an integral over the system’s entire history. The memory time of this equation is then compared to the production time scale to determine when the Markovian approximation (and thus the H-theorem) holds.

## Key numbers

- The memory time is given by \(\tau_{\rm mem} \sim \hbar/\epsilon_\perp + \epsilon_\perp/(qE)\), where \(\epsilon_\perp\) is the transverse energy or mass.
- The production time scale for \(p_{\|}=0\) is \(\tau_{\rm prod} \sim (\epsilon_\perp/(qE)) \exp(\pi\epsilon_\perp^2/(2\hbar qE))\).
- The Markovian approximation is valid only when \(E \ll m^2/(\hbar q)\); for \(E \sim m^2/(\hbar q)\), both memory time and production scale become \(\tau \sim \hbar/m\).
- Critical field strengths for particle masses of 0.5, 15, or 500 MeV correspond to \(10^{-3}\), 1, or \(10^3\) MeV/fm, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the conceptual framework of irreversibility developed for classical gases (e.g., the Boltzmann H-theorem and the role of time-scale separation) to the quantum field theory context of spontaneous pair creation. It builds on the Schwinger mechanism (Schwinger, 1951) and prior transport-theory treatments of pair creation in flux tubes (e.g., string model calculations). The key advance is the explicit derivation of a non-Markovian source term and the identification of a criterion for Markovianity, which shows that for physically relevant strong fields, a non-Markovian transport theory is required, contradicting the implicit Markovian assumption in standard quantum Boltzmann equations. This enables future development of transport equations that correctly handle memory effects in heavy-ion collisions and strong-field QED.
