# Self-organized criticality in atmospheric cascades

## Key claim

Atmospheric cascades observed in cosmic ray emulsion experiments can be understood as a manifestation of self-organized criticality, with their energy spectra described by Lévy distributions that emerge naturally from a multiplicative stochastic process. The authors hedge that the observed power-law distributions are only approximate, with small deviations directly attributable to the finite number of cascade generations \(N\), and that the equilibrium spectrum \(P(x) \propto x^{-2}\) is reached in practice only for \(N > 6\).

## Method

The authors performed Monte-Carlo simulations of a multiplicative process where each cascade generation multiplies the energy by a random factor \(\xi\) drawn from an exponential distribution, modeling the scaled energy dependence of a single elementary interaction. They fitted the resulting energy distributions to Lévy distributions (Eq. 4) and extracted the dependence of the Lévy exponent \(\alpha\) on the generation number \(N\), parameterized by a fitted constant \(c = 0.55\). They then compared these Lévy-type spectra to integral energy spectra of gamma quanta and hadrons from the Kambala experiment (at 520 g/cm²) and the Pamir experiment (at 600 g/cm²), as well as to a "halo" event from Chacaltaya.

## Key numbers

- For the multiplicative process simulation, the Lévy distribution parameter \(\alpha\) depends on generation number \(N\) as \(\alpha = 2 / (1 - c^{N-1})\) with fitted constant \(c = 0.55\); asymptotically \(\alpha \to 2\) as \(N \to \infty\).
- The nonextensivity parameter \(q\) from Tsallis statistics is given by \(q = 3/2 - c^{N-1}/2\); for \(N \to 1\), \(q \to 1\) (recovering exponential distribution), and for \(N \to \infty\), \(q \to 3/2\) (the limiting value from normalization).
- The equilibrium distribution \(P(x_N) \propto x_N^{-2}\) is reached for \(x_N \gg \langle x_N \rangle\) already for \(N > 6\).
- For gamma families from the Kambala experiment (520 g/cm²), the fitted Lévy parameter gives \(\alpha - 1 = 1.8\); for hadronic families at Kambala, \(\alpha - 1 = 2.0\).
- For the Pamir experiment (600 g/cm²), \(\alpha - 1 = 1.8\) for gamma quanta and \(\alpha - 1 = 1.7\) for hadronic components.
- For the "halo" event P06 at Chacaltaya, the integral energy distribution of shower cores gives \(\alpha - 1 = 16.0\), corresponding to \(q = 1.06\) (nearly exponential, with no multiplicative process influence).
- The average number of cascade generations leading to observed distributions is estimated at \(N \simeq 3\), with families typically containing particles from about 7 genetically connected nuclear interactions at \(\Sigma E > 100\) TeV.

## Context

This work extends the application of self-organized criticality (Bak, Tang, and Wiesenfeld, 1987) and Tsallis nonextensive statistics (Tsallis, 1988) to atmospheric cascade development, unifying these concepts through Lévy flights. The authors note that prior experimental data from Kambala (K) and Pamir (P) experiments had shown roughly power-law energy spectra but lacked quantitative analysis of their exact form; this paper provides the first such analysis showing they can all be described by Lévy-type spectra. The results connect to the energy transport equation for cascade processes (where the age parameter \(s \to 1\) gives the equilibrium spectrum \(N(E) = N_0 E^{-2}\)), to intermittent behaviour in families, to multifractal behaviour with fractal dimension \(D_N\) at each cascade stage, and to Zipf's law predictions of power-law behaviour with \(\alpha = 2\). The work enables future studies of how finite cascade generation numbers produce small deviations from exact power laws, and distinguishes pure nuclear "halo" events (exponential distributions) from cascades dominated by multiplicative processes.
