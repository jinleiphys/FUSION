# Astromaterial Science and Nuclear Pasta

> We define `astromaterial science' as the study of materials in astronomical objects that are qualitatively denser than materials on earth. Astromaterials can have unique properties related to their large density, though they may be organized in ways similar to more conventional materials. By analogy to terrestrial materials, we divide our study of astromaterials into hard and soft and discuss one example of each. The hard astromaterial discussed here is a crystalline lattice, such as the Coulomb crystals in the interior of cold white dwarfs and in the crust of neutron stars, while the soft astromaterial is nuclear pasta found in the inner crusts of neutron stars. In particular, we discuss how molecular dynamics simulations have been used to calculate the properties of astromaterials to interpret observations of white dwarfs and neutron stars. Coulomb crystals are studied to understand how compact stars freeze. Their incredible strength may make crust "mountains" on rotating neutron stars a source for gravitational waves that the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) may detect. Nuclear pasta is expected near the base of the neutron star crust at densities of $10^{14}$ g/cm$^3$. Competition between nuclear attraction and Coulomb repulsion rearranges neutrons and protons into complex non-spherical shapes such as sheets (lasagna) or tubes (spaghetti). Semi-classical molecular dynamics simulations of nuclear pasta have been used to study these phases and calculate their transport properties such as neutrino opacity, thermal conductivity, and electrical conductivity. Observations of neutron stars may be sensitive to these properties, and can be be used to interpret observations of supernova neutrinos, magnetic field decay, and crust cooling of accreting neutron stars. We end by comparing nuclear pasta shapes with some similar shapes seen in biological systems.

## Key claim

The paper introduces "astromaterial science" as the study of materials in astronomical objects that are qualitatively denser than terrestrial materials, and argues that molecular dynamics simulations of Coulomb crystals and nuclear pasta can be used to interpret observations of white dwarfs and neutron stars, with the caveat that these simulations ignore quantum effects on the ions and that the exact composition of neutron star crusts is not fully understood due to uncertainties in binding energies of neutron-rich nuclei.

## Method

The authors review molecular dynamics simulations of Coulomb crystals in white dwarf interiors and neutron star crusts, where ions interact via screened Coulomb potentials and the system is characterized by the Coulomb parameter Gamma. They also describe semi-classical molecular dynamics simulations of nuclear pasta phases expected near the base of the neutron star crust at densities around 10^14 g/cm^3, where competition between nuclear attraction and Coulomb repulsion produces non-spherical shapes such as sheets (lasagna) and tubes (spaghetti). The simulations calculate transport properties including neutrino opacity, thermal conductivity, electrical conductivity, and shear viscosity, as well as breaking stress of the crust.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear pasta is expected at densities of 10^14 g/cm^3, approximately 100 trillion times the density of water
- The nuclear saturation density n_0 is approximately 3 x 10^14 g/cm^3
- The one-component plasma freezes at Gamma approximately 175, as determined by Monte Carlo simulations starting with Brush, Sahlin and Teller (1966)
- The breaking stress of neutron star crust from molecular dynamics simulations is 10 billion times larger than that for steel
- The breaking strain (fractional deformation when the crust fails) is of order 0.1
- The ellipticity epsilon that neutron star crust can support is as large as 10^-6 to 10^-5
- LIGO searches have set upper limits on ellipticity of epsilon less than 10^-8 for the most sensitive cases
- Accreting neutron stars have not been observed to spin faster than about 700 Hz
- The gravitational wave strain from a rotating neutron star at distance d is given by h_0 = (4 pi^2 G I_3 f^2)/(d c^4) epsilon
- The outer crust forms when density exceeds about 10^10 g/cm^3, so that Gamma exceeds 175
- Neutron drip in the inner crust occurs at density about 10^11 g/cm^3
- The central density of a white dwarf can be as high as 10^9 g/cm^3
- rp-process nucleosynthesis builds nuclei up to mass numbers A approximately 107, limited to Z less than or equal to 52 by the SnSbTe cycle

## Context

This paper extends the concept of material science to astrophysical contexts, building on prior work on stellar metallurgy by Kobyakov (2014) and Ball (2014), and on metallurgical aspects of neutron star crusts by Chamel (2005). The molecular dynamics simulations of crust breaking stress by the authors and collaborators enable predictions for continuous gravitational wave sources detectable by LIGO, while the nuclear pasta simulations provide transport properties needed to interpret observations of supernova neutrinos, magnetic field decay, and crust cooling of accreting neutron stars. The paper also draws analogies between nuclear pasta shapes and self-assembled structures in biological systems made of phospholipids.
