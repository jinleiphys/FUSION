# Electron and Muon Dynamics in Neutron Stars Beyond Chemical Equilibrium

> A neutron star harbors of order $10^{56}$ electrons in its core, and almost the same number of muons, with muon decay prohibited by Pauli blocking. However, as macroscopic properties of the star such as its mass, rotational velocity, or magnetic field evolve over time, the equilibrium lepton abundances (dictated by the weak interactions) change as well. Scenarios where this can happen include spin-down, accretion, magnetic field decay, and tidal deformation. We discuss the mechanisms by which a star disrupted in one of these ways re-establishes lepton chemical equilibrium. In most cases, the dominant processes are out-of-equilibrium Urca reactions, the rates of which we compute for the first time. If, however, the equilibrium muon abundance decreases, while the equilibrium electron abundance increases (or decreases less than the equilibrium muon abundance), outward diffusion of muons plays a crucial role as well. This is true in particular for stars older than about 10,000 yrs whose core has cooled to $\lesssim 20$ keV. The muons decay in a region where Pauli blocking is lifted, and we argue that these decays lead to a flux of $\mathcal{O}$(10 MeV) neutrinos. Realistically, however, this flux will remain undetectable for the foreseeable future.

## Key claim

The paper argues that when a neutron star's macroscopic properties (rotation, magnetic field, accretion, tidal deformation) change, the equilibrium lepton abundances shift, and in older stars (≳10,000 yrs, core temperature ≲20 keV) the dominant re-equilibration mechanisms are not Urca processes but rather muon diffusion followed by decay, though the resulting neutrino flux is too small to be detectable.

## Method

The authors compute out-of-equilibrium Urca reaction rates for lepton production and absorption in neutron stars for the first time, and combine these with calculations of muon diffusion mean free paths derived from thermal conductivity (using the NScool code) and muon decay widths in dense electron gases. They also estimate the rate of "assisted muon decay" (muon decay with a spectator proton) and evaluate the resulting neutrino fluxes from muon decays in the outer stellar regions.

## Key numbers

- A neutron star harbors of order 10^56 electrons in its core, with muon abundance at most a factor 2–3 smaller.
- For rotational frequencies f ~ 700 Hz (PSR J1748-2446ad), the equilibrium muon abundance is several per mille lower than in a non-rotating star; the relative change scales as ΔN_μ/N_μ ∝ 0.01 (f/f_K)^2 ≃ 4×10^(-4) (f/700 Hz)^2.
- For tidal deformation during binary inspirals, ΔN_μ/N_μ ≃ 1.7×10^(-6) × (M_2/M_⊙) (d/1000 km)^(-2) for general tidal deformation, and ≃ 1.7×10^(-6) × (M/M_⊙)^(-1/2) (τ/10000 sec)^(-1/2) during binary inspirals.
- Muon mean free path λ_ℓ increases from around 10^(-8) cm hours after supernova to 10^(-6) cm after 10^5 yrs.
- The estimated assisted muon decay rate is Γ_amd ≃ 5.7×10^(-27) sec^(-1) × (k_Fp/250 MeV)^2 × (k_Fe/200 MeV)^2 × (T/10^8 K)^8.
- The estimated neutrino flux from muon loss is φ_ν ∼ 1.3×10^(-10) cm^(-2) sec^(-1) × (ΔN_μ/N_μ / 0.001) × (d/10 kpc)^(-2) × (1 Gyr / τ_NS).

## Context

This work extends the understanding of lepton dynamics in neutron stars beyond the standard picture of beta-equilibrium maintained by Urca processes, which was established by Gamow and Schoenberg (1970) and detailed by Yakovlev (1995, 2000) and Shapiro (1983). The paper builds on neutron star cooling codes like NScool (Page 2006) and considers how changes in rotation, magnetic field, accretion, and tidal deformation, studied by authors such as Friedman (1986), Komatsu (1989), and Goldreich (1992), drive the star out of equilibrium. The results enable future studies of non-equilibrium lepton transport in neutron stars and set quantitative limits on the detectability of muon-decay neutrinos, concluding they will remain undetectable for the foreseeable future.
