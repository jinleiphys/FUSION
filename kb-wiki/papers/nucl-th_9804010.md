# Nuclear Scales

> Nuclear scales are discussed from the nuclear physics viewpoint. The conventional nuclear potential is characterized as a black box that interpolates nucleon-nucleon (NN) data, while being constrained by the best possible theoretical input. The latter consists of the longer-range parts of the NN force (e.g., OPEP, TPEP, the $π$-$γ$ force), which can be calculated using chiral perturbation theory and gauged using modern phase-shift analyses. The shorter-range parts of the force are effectively parameterized by moments of the interaction that are independent of the details of the force model, in analogy to chiral perturbation theory. Results of GFMC calculations in light nuclei are interpreted in terms of fundamental scales, which are in good agreement with expectations from chiral effective field theories. Problems with spin-orbit-type observables are noted.

# Nuclear Scales

## Key claim

Nuclear observables in few-body systems exhibit power counting consistent with chiral effective field theory expectations, but this power counting applies to irreducible operators evaluated with wave functions rather than to the reducible graphs that build those wave functions; the approach is a "hybrid" where field-theoretic objects are embedded in a traditional nuclear potential, and problems remain with spin-orbit-type observables such as the nucleon asymmetry in N-d scattering and the p-shell splitting in 5He.

## Method

The paper reviews the conventional nuclear potential as a "black box" that interpolates nucleon-nucleon data using long-range parts calculated from chiral perturbation theory (OPEP, TPEP, the pi-gamma force) and short-range parts parameterized by moments of the interaction, analogous to counter terms in chiral perturbation theory. Green's Function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations for light nuclei (A=2 through A=8) using the AV18 potential plus the Urbana IX three-nucleon force are interpreted in terms of fundamental scales, and the coupling constants from a relativistic Hartree calculation with zero-range forces are checked for naturalness. The Nijmegen phase-shift analysis is used to extract the pion-nucleon coupling constant and to verify the chiral form of TPEP.

## Key numbers

- The composite pion-nucleon coupling constant from the Nijmegen PSA: f^2 = 0.0749(3), equivalent to G = 13.05(2) and G^2/4pi = 13.56(5)
- The Goldberger-Treiman discrepancy: delta_GT = 1.9(4)%, consistent with natural size ~ m_pi^2 / Lambda^2
- Extracted pion masses from the Nijmegen analysis: m_pi+ = 139.4(10) MeV, m_pi0 = 135.6(13) MeV
- The binding-energy difference of the isodoublet 3He and 3H: 764 keV experimentally, compared with calculated 750(25) keV
- Expectation values from GFMC calculations for A=2-4: <V_pi> ~ -15 MeV/pair, <V_sr> ~ -5 MeV/pair, <V_3NF> ~ -1 MeV/triplet, <T> ~ 15 MeV/nucleon, <V_C> ~ 2/3 MeV/pp pair
- OPEP dominates the potential energy at 70-80% of <V>
- The characteristic momentum scale in nuclei: Q ~ m_pi ~ 140 MeV/c, about half the Fermi momentum
- The regulator scale: Lambda ~ 1 GeV, consistent with 4 pi f_pi

## Context

This paper extends the line of work applying chiral perturbation theory to nuclear forces, building on the consistent TPEP calculation by Ordonez and van Kolck (1992) and the Nijmegen phase-shift analysis program. The "hybrid" approach contrasts with attempts to apply Weinberg power counting directly to reducible graphs, which several workshop talks showed to be ineffective. The results enable construction of "third-generation" potentials containing improved TPEP and the pi-gamma force, and point to the need for better three-nucleon forces with spin-orbit character to resolve the Ay puzzle and the underbinding of higher-isospin states in A=6-8 nuclei.
