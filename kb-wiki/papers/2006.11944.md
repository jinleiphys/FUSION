# Assessing the accuracy of the GENIE event generator with electron-scattering data

> Precision neutrino oscillation experiments of the future---of which DUNE is a prime example---require reliable event generator tools. The 1--4 GeV energy regime, in which DUNE will operate, is marked by the transition from the low-energy nuclear physics domain to that of perturbative QCD, resulting in rich and highly complex physics. Given this complexity, it is important to establish a validation procedure capable of disentangling the physical processes and testing each of them individually. Here, we demonstrate the utility of this approach by benchmarking the GENIE generator, currently used by all Fermilab-based experiments, against a broad set of inclusive electron-scattering data. This comparison takes advantage of the fact that, while electron-nucleus and neutrino-nucleus processes share a lot of common physics, electron scattering gives one access to precisely known beam energies and scattering kinematics. Exploring the kinematic parameter range relevant to DUNE in this manner, we observe patterns of large discrepancies between the generator and data. These discrepancies are most prominent in the pion-producing regimes and are present not only in medium-sized nuclei, including argon, but also in deuterium and hydrogen targets, indicating mismodeled hadronic physics. Several directions for possible improvement are discussed.

## Key claim

The GENIE event generator shows large discrepancies with inclusive electron-scattering data across the kinematic regime relevant to DUNE and NOvA, particularly in pion-producing regimes, and these discrepancies appear not only in medium-sized nuclei like argon but also in deuterium and hydrogen targets, indicating that the root cause is mismodeled hadronic physics rather than purely nuclear effects.

## Method

The authors compared predictions from GENIE version 2.12.10 (with default settings) against a broad set of inclusive electron-scattering data compiled from multiple experiments spanning several decades, covering beam energies from sub-GeV to approximately 6 GeV and a range of scattering angles. They analyzed double differential cross sections as a function of energy transfer ω and momentum transfer |q|, decomposing the generator predictions into contributions from quasielastic scattering, resonance excitation, meson-exchange currents, and deep-inelastic scattering to identify which physical processes are responsible for the observed discrepancies.

## Key numbers

- GENIE overestimates the cross section by up to ~40% in the region of high energy transfers where DIS and higher resonance excitation contribute, for carbon at 2.222 GeV beam energy and 15.54° scattering angle
- GENIE underestimates the Δ production peak by ~30% for the same kinematics (2.222 GeV, 15.54°)
- In the dip region between the QE and Δ peaks, the electron data are underestimated by up to ~40% for the same kinematics
- The predicted position of the QE peak agrees with data to within ~10 MeV for titanium and ~5 MeV for argon at 2.222 GeV and 15.54°
- The DUNE beam flux is peaked at energies around 2–3 GeV, with the strongest ν_e appearance signal at ~3 GeV given the baseline of 1,300 km and Δm²₂₃ ≃ 2.5 × 10⁻³ eV²
- The onset of the DIS regime in GENIE is defined at invariant hadronic mass W = 1.7 GeV

## Context

This work extends earlier tests of GENIE against electron-carbon data by Katori (2013) that were limited to sub-GeV beam energies (560 MeV) and large scattering angles, showing that the same discrepancies persist at the few-GeV energies relevant for DUNE and NOvA. The study systematically maps discrepancies across the (|q|, ω) plane using world carbon data, enabling identification of mismodeled hadronic physics as the primary cause rather than nuclear effects, and discusses several directions for possible improvement of the generator.
