# Detecting inhomogeneous chiral condensation from the bosonic two-point function in the $(1 + 1)$-dimensional Gross-Neveu model in the mean-field approximation

> The phase diagram of the $(1 + 1)$-dimensional Gross-Neveu model is reanalyzed for (non-)zero chemical potential and (non-)zero temperature within the mean-field approximation. By investigating the momentum dependence of the bosonic two-point function, the well-known second-order phase transition from the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetric phase to the so-called inhomogeneous phase is detected. In the latter phase the chiral condensate is periodically varying in space and translational invariance is broken. This work is a proof of concept study that confirms that it is possible to correctly localize second-order phase transition lines between phases without condensation and phases of spatially inhomogeneous condensation via a stability analysis of the homogeneous phase. To complement other works relying on this technique, the stability analysis is explained in detail and its limitations and successes are discussed in context of the Gross-Neveu model. Additionally, we present explicit results for the bosonic wave-function renormalization in the mean-field approximation, which is extracted analytically from the bosonic two-point function. We find regions -- a so-called moat regime -- where the wave function renormalization is negative accompanying the inhomogeneous phase as expected.

## Key claim

By analyzing the momentum dependence of the bosonic two-point function in the mean-field approximation, the second-order phase transition line from the Z2 symmetric phase to the inhomogeneous phase can be correctly localized via a stability analysis of the homogeneous phase, but this method provides only a sufficient condition for inhomogeneous condensation, not a necessary one.

## Method

The authors study the (1+1)-dimensional Gross-Neveu model in the large-Nf limit, where the mean-field approximation becomes exact. They compute the bosonic two-point function (the curvature of the effective action at the homogeneous ground state) as a function of momentum, temperature, and chemical potential, and determine where its sign becomes negative for nonzero momentum, indicating instability of the homogeneous phase toward spatially inhomogeneous perturbations. The wave-function renormalization is extracted analytically from the two-point function.

## Key numbers

- The critical endpoint (Lifshitz point) is located at (mu_L, T_L)/sigma_0 = (0.608, 0.318), where sigma_0 is the vacuum fermion mass.
- The second-order phase transition from the homogeneously broken phase to the symmetric phase at zero chemical potential occurs at T_c/sigma_0 = e^gamma/pi = 0.567.
- The homogeneous first-order phase boundary terminates at (mu_c,hom, T)/sigma_0 = (1/sqrt(2), 0.0) = (0.707, 0.0).
- The phase boundary between the inhomogeneous phase and the homogeneously broken phase at zero temperature is at mu_c/sigma_0 = 2/pi = 0.637.

## Context

This work extends earlier stability analyses of the Gross-Neveu model by Thies (2003) and Basar (2009) and complements the fermion doubler trick benchmarked by Braun (2014). It provides a pedagogical proof of concept for detecting inhomogeneous phases via stability analysis, which has been applied in mean-field studies of Nambu-Jona-Lasinio and Quark-Meson models by Nakano (2004), Nickel (2009), and Buballa (2018), as well as in Functional Renormalization Group calculations by Fu (2019). The method enables indirect detection of inhomogeneous condensation without requiring ansatz functions for the condensate shape.
