# A path integration approach to relativistic finite density problems and its particle content

> A path integration formulation for the finite density and temperature problems is shown to be consistent with the thermodynamics using an 8 component ``real'' representation for the fermion fields by applying it to a free fermion system. A relativistic quantum field theory is shown to be smoothly approached at zero temperature by a real-time thermal field theory so derived even at a finite density. The analysis leads to a new representation for the fermion fields which is shown to be inequivalent to the conventional 4 component theory at the quantum level by having a mirror universe with observable effects and to be better behaved at short distances.

## Key claim

The standard 4-component fermion representation fails to yield the correct thermodynamic grand potential from a Feynman-Matthews-Salam path integration at zero temperature and finite density, but an 8-component "real" representation resolves this inconsistency and introduces a mirror universe with potentially observable quantum interference effects, provided the system is in a coherent state mixing the two mirror sectors.

## Method

The author computed the grand potential for a free fermion system at zero temperature and finite density using both the conventional 4-component Dirac spinor and an 8-component "real" spinor representation within the Feynman-Matthews-Salam path integration formalism. The Euclidean action was evaluated by contour integration in the complex energy plane, comparing the results from different integration contours (FMS, Euclidean, and quasiparticle contours) to the known thermodynamic result. The particle content and observable consequences were then derived from the pole structure of the 8-component fermion propagator.

## Key numbers

- For a massless free fermion system using the 4-component representation, the grand potential computed on the Euclidean contour gives the correct result: $PV = -\Omega' = V \mu^4/12\pi^2$.
- The 8-component representation yields the same grand potential on the Euclidean contour but differs on the quasiparticle contours because the imaginary part of the integrand falls off as $O(\mu^2/|p_0|^2)$ on the physical sheet, compared to $O(\mu/|p_0|)$ for the 4-component case.
- The conserved fermion number density in the 8-component theory is $\bar n = \frac{1}{\pi^2}\int^\infty_0 d\mathbf{p} \mathbf{p}^2 \left[f^{(-)}_p - f^{(+)}_p\right]$ with $f^{(\pm)}_p = 1/[e^{\beta(E_p\pm \mu)}+1]$, matching elementary statistical mechanics.
- The predicted beat frequency for interference between the two mirror sectors in an external electric potential $A^0$ is $2qA^0$ for all directly produced particles with the same charge $q$.

## Context

This work extends the Feynman-Matthews-Salam path integration approach (Feynman and Hibbs, 1965; Matthews and Salam, 1955) to finite density systems, correcting inconsistencies found in the standard 4-component thermal field theory formulation. It builds on the Matsubara formalism (Matsubara, 1955) and real-time thermal field theory developments (Landsman and van Weert, 1987; Kapusta, 1989; Semenoff and Umezawa, 1983; Niemi and Semenoff, 1984). The 8-component representation enables a consistent real-time thermal field theory at finite density and suggests experimental tests in heavy ion collisions or astronomical observations through quantum interference effects between the mirror universes, with potential implications for CP violation, baryogenesis, and dark matter.
