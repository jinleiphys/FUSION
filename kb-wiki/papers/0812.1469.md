# Liquid-Drop and Independent-Particle Models Revisited

> Traditionally, the difference in binding energy from the experimental value with respect to the theoretical liquid-drop model value, has been seen as indication of independent-particle character along with magicity for particular number of protons and neutrons. We study this carefully to demonstrate that it actually indicates that the liquid-drop and the independent-particle phases of the nucleus have equal fundamental primacy and coexist simultaneously in a nucleus to provide a complete and a consistent description of the same.

# Liquid-Drop and Independent-Particle Models Revisited

## Key claim
The difference between experimental binding energies and liquid-drop model predictions, traditionally interpreted as evidence for independent-particle shell structure, actually demonstrates that the liquid-drop and independent-particle phases of the nucleus have equal fundamental primacy and coexist simultaneously in every nucleus. This coexistence is analogous to the liquid-gas phase transition in ordinary matter, with the shell-correction energy acting as an internal temperature scale specific to each nucleus.

## Method
The author compares two figures: one showing the difference between experimental nuclear binding energies and theoretical liquid-drop model values (the standard shell-correction plot from Myers and Swiatecki (1966)), and another showing boiling points of chemical elements from Cook (2006). By noting the striking similarity between the two curves, the author argues that the shell-correction energy behaves as an internal temperature scale, and that magic-number nuclei correspond to the lowest "boiling points" where the liquid (LDM) phase most easily converts to the gaseous (IPM) phase.

## Key numbers
- Magic numbers where relatively large deviations from the liquid-drop model appear: Z=28, 50, and 82; N=28, 50, 82, and 126 (from Cook (2006))
- The liquid-drop part of the mass formula was fitted with four parameters, and the shell-correction part with three parameters, fitted almost independently (from Myers and Swiatecki (1966))

## Context
This work extends the author's earlier proposal of a fundamental duality in nuclei (Abbas (2008)) by providing a specific thermodynamic analogy. It contradicts the conventional hierarchy in which the independent-particle model is considered more fundamental than the liquid-drop model, as described in standard textbooks and in the work of Myers and Swiatecki (1966). The paper enables a reinterpretation of shell corrections as a phase-coexistence phenomenon, potentially linking nuclear structure physics to classical thermodynamics and phase transition theory.
