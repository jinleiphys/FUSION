# Determination of S-factors with the LIT method

> The precise determination of astrophysical S-factors is essential for a detailed understanding of the nucleosynthesis in its various facets. It is discussed how the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method can be applied for such a determination. The astrophysical S-factor for the proton-deuteron radiative capture is considered as test case. The importance of a specific many-body basis used for the LIT equation solution is pointed out. The excellent results of the test are discussed.

## Key claim

The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method can reliably determine astrophysical S-factors, but only when a specific many-body basis (Jacobi basis) is used instead of the standard hyperspherical harmonics (HH) basis, because the HH basis incorrectly shifts strength below the three-body breakup threshold to higher energies.

## Method

The authors computed the unretarded dipole photodisintegration cross section of ³He using the LIT method with both a hyperspherical harmonics basis and a Jacobi basis (HH basis for the two-body subsystem plus an additional basis for the third nucleon). They then inverted the LIT to obtain the response function and cross section, and compared these results to a conventional calculation using explicit deuteron-proton continuum wave functions with correlated hyperspherical harmonics. The simple central MT-I/III nucleon-nucleon potential was used throughout.

## Key numbers

- The ³He ground state binding energy is 8.05 MeV and the ²H binding energy is 2.24 MeV with the MT-I/III potential.
- The three-body breakup threshold lies at about 8 MeV, while the two-body breakup threshold is at about 5.8 MeV.
- For the Jacobi basis with σ_I = 0.01 MeV, a dense grid of LIT states appears starting right at the 5.8 MeV breakup threshold, whereas the HH basis shows no LIT states below 8 MeV.
- The rule of thumb for inversion is that σ_I must be greater than the energy spacing ΔE_n between LIT states.

## Context

This work extends the LIT method previously applied to electromagnetic nuclear responses (Efros 2007) and to the ⁴He isoscalar monopole resonance width determination (Leidemann 2015). It directly tests the method against a conventional continuum calculation (Deflorian 2016) for the ²H(p,γ)³He reaction. The result enables reliable theoretical determination of S-factors for astrophysical reactions where Coulomb barriers make experimental measurement difficult or impossible.
