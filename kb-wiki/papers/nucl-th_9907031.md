# Kinetic-theory approach to low-energy collective modes in nuclei

> Two different solutions of the linearized Vlasov equation for finite systems, characterized by fixed and moving-surface boundary conditions, are discussed in a unified perspective. A condition determining the eigenfrequencies of collective nuclear oscillations, that can be obtained from the moving-surface solution, is studied for isoscalar vibrations of lowest multipolarity. Analytic expressions for the friction and mass parameters related to the low-enegy surface excitations are derived and their value is compared to values given by other models. Both similarities and differences are found with respect to the other approaches, however the close agreement obtained in many cases with one of the other models suggests that, in spite of some important differences, the two approaches are substantially equivalent. The formalism based on the Vlasov equation is more transparent since it leads to analytical expressions that can be a basis for further improvement of the model.

# Kinetic-theory approach to low-energy collective modes in nuclei

## Key claim
The moving-surface solution of the linearized Vlasov equation, when studied in a zero-order approximation that neglects mean-field fluctuations in the bulk, yields analytic expressions for friction and mass parameters that are substantially equivalent to those of the model by Koonin et al. (1977), despite important formal differences; however, for quadrupole (L=2) oscillations the model fails to produce a low-frequency collective mode with |s|<1 in the complex plane, suggesting that the zero-order approximation is inadequate for describing isoscalar quadrupole vibrations.

## Method
The authors solve the linearized Vlasov equation for a finite spherical system with a sharp moving surface, imposing boundary conditions that allow reflection of nucleons from the oscillating nuclear surface. They derive a self-consistency condition (Eq. main) linking the surface deformation amplitude to the momentum flux tensor at the nuclear radius, then perform a low-frequency expansion (s<1) of the response function chi_L(omega) to extract analytic expressions for the restoring force, friction, and mass parameters for multipoles L=0 through L=3. The zero-order approximation neglects the mean-field fluctuation delta U_LM in the bulk, retaining only the surface self-consistency condition.

## Key numbers
- Surface tension parameter: sigma ≈ 1 MeV fm^{-2}
- Monopole breathing mode energy: hbar omega_{L=0} ≈ 96 A^{-1/3} MeV (using R=1.2 A^{1/3} fm)
- For L=2, the mass parameter D_2 has a logarithmically divergent real part and a finite imaginary part; the low-frequency expansion yields chi_2(s) = A_0 {1/4 - s^2/20 - (9/16)s^2[(1/2)ln(16/s^2 - 1) + i(pi/2) sign(Re s) - 1]} + O(s^4)
- For L=3, the eigenfrequency condition becomes i omega gamma_3 + D_3 omega^2 = C_3, leading to overdamped octupole oscillations
- Friction coefficients gamma_L for odd multipoles agree perfectly with Koonin et al. (1977); for even multipoles the present values are systematically 3-4% smaller, decreasing to about 1% for L=12
- For L=0, the coefficient A_0 = -2 epsilon_F rho_0 R^3 is much larger than the surface tension term C_0, confirming the monopole as a compression mode

## Context
This work extends the Vlasov-equation approach of Brink et al. (1987) by replacing fixed-surface boundary conditions with moving-surface conditions inspired by the Goldhaber-Teller model, following the earlier work of Abrosimov et al. (1998). The results are compared to the liquid-drop model of Bohr and Mottelson (1975) and to the semiclassical model of Koonin et al. (1977). The formalism enables analytic calculation of dissipation (Landau damping) from nucleons on closed classical trajectories, and suggests that the zero-order approximation may need to include bulk mean-field fluctuations to describe quadrupole modes, pointing toward a more numerical treatment. The overdamped octupole result offers a possible explanation for the background observed in inelastic proton scattering at small angles, as studied by Esbensen and Bertsch (1984) and by Randrup (1978).
