# High-order Shakhov-like extension of the relaxation time approximation in relativistic kinetic theory

> In this paper we present a relativistic Shakhov-type generalization of the Anderson-Witting relaxation time model for the Boltzmann collision integral. The extension is performed by modifying the path on which the distribution function $f_{\mathbf{k}}$ is taken towards local equilibrium $f_{0\mathbf{k}}$, by replacing $f_{\mathbf{k}} - f_{0\mathbf{k}}$ via $f_{\mathbf{k}} - f_{{\rm S}\mathbf{k}}$. The Shakhov-like distribution $f_{{\rm S} \mathbf{k}}$ is constructed using $f_{0\mathbf{k}}$ and the irreducible moments $ρ_r^{μ_1 \cdots μ_\ell}$ of $f_\mathbf{k}$ and reduces to $f_{0\mathbf{k}}$ in local equilibrium. Employing the method of moments, we derive systematic high-order Shakhov extensions that allow both the first- and the second-order transport coefficients to be controlled independently of each other. We illustrate the capabilities of the formalism by tweaking the shear-bulk coupling coefficient $λ_{Ππ}$ in the frame of the Bjorken flow of massive particles, as well as the diffusion-shear transport coefficients $\ell_{Vπ}$, $\ell_{πV}$ in the frame of sound wave propagation in an ultrarelativistic gas. Finally, we illustrate the importance of second-order transport coefficients by comparison with the results of the stochastic BAMPS method in the context of the one-dimensional Riemann problem.

## Key claim

The paper presents a systematic high-order generalization of the Shakhov-type relaxation time approximation that allows both first-order and second-order transport coefficients to be controlled independently, going beyond the original Anderson-Witting model where all transport coefficients are determined by a single relaxation time.

## Method

The authors modify the Anderson-Witting collision integral by replacing the relaxation term \(f_{\mathbf{k}} - f_{0\mathbf{k}}\) with \(f_{\mathbf{k}} - f_{\mathrm{S}\mathbf{k}}\), where the Shakhov distribution \(f_{\mathrm{S}\mathbf{k}}\) is constructed using irreducible moments \(\rho_r^{\mu_1 \cdots \mu_\ell}\) of the distribution function. Using the method of moments and the Inverse-Reynolds Dominance (IReD) approach, they derive algebraic equations that allow an appropriate subset of transport coefficients to be set as essentially arbitrary thermodynamic functions. The model is implemented numerically using a discrete-velocity method with rapidity-based moments and Gauss-Legendre quadrature.

## Key numbers

- The shear viscosity of the Anderson-Witting model is \(\eta_{\mathrm{AW}} = \tau_R \alpha^{(2)}_0\) with \(\alpha^{(2)}_r = \beta J_{3+r, 2}\)
- The bulk viscosity of the Anderson-Witting model is \(\zeta_{\mathrm{AW}} = \tau_R \frac{m^2}{3} \alpha^{(0)}_0\) with \(\alpha^{(0)}_r = \mathcal{H}_\beta J_{r+1,0} - \mathcal{H}_\alpha J_{r0} - \beta J_{r+1,1}\)
- The diffusion coefficient of the Anderson-Witting model is \(\kappa_{\mathrm{AW}} = \tau_R \alpha^{(1)}_0\) with \(\alpha^{(1)}_r = J_{r+1,1} - \frac{1}{h} J_{r+2, 1}\)
- The first-order Shakhov transport coefficients are \(\zeta_{\mathrm{S}} = \frac{m^2}{3} \alpha^{(0)}_0 \tau_\Pi\), \(\kappa_{\mathrm{S}} = \alpha^{(1)}_0 \tau_V\), and \(\eta_{\mathrm{S}} = \alpha^{(2)}_0 \tau_\pi\), where \(\tau_\Pi\), \(\tau_V\), and \(\tau_\pi\) are new thermodynamic parameters

## Context

This work extends the first-order Shakhov model introduced by Ambruș (2023) and the Anderson-Witting relaxation time approximation (Anderson and Witting, 1974), which itself generalized the nonrelativistic BGK model (Bhatnagar, Gross, and Krook, 1954). The high-order extension enables control of second-order transport coefficients such as the shear-bulk coupling coefficient \(\lambda_{\Pi\pi}\) and the diffusion-shear transport coefficients \(\ell_{V\pi}\), \(\ell_{\pi V}\), which vanish in the original Anderson-Witting model but are nonzero for more realistic interactions like hard-sphere scattering (Denicol, 2012) or \(\lambda \varphi^4\) scalar field theory (Denicol, 2022). The model enables comparison with stochastic BAMPS simulations (Xu, 2004) for the Riemann problem and can be used to test second-order hydrodynamic formulations against kinetic theory results.
