# Nonaxial-octupole effect in superheavy nuclei

> The triaxial-octupole Y$_{32}$ correlation in atomic nuclei has long been expected to exist but experimental evidence has not been clear. We find, in order to explain the very low-lying 2$^-$ bands in the transfermium mass region, that this exotic effect may manifest itself in superheavy elements. Favorable conditions for producing triaxial-octupole correlations are shown to be present in the deformed single-particle spectrum, which is further supported by quantitative Reflection Asymmetric Shell Model calculations. It is predicted that the strong nonaxial-octupole effect may persist up to the element 108. Our result thus represents the first concrete example of spontaneous breaking of both axial and reflection symmetries in the heaviest nuclear systems.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the very low-lying 2⁻ bands observed in transfermium nuclei (Z≈100, N≈150) cannot be explained as simple two-quasiparticle excitations because their bandhead energies lie below the expected 1 MeV threshold set by twice the BCS pairing gap; instead, these bands are proposed to arise from nonaxial-octupole (Y₃₂) correlations, with the strongest effect predicted in elements 98 (Californium) and 106 (Seaborgium).

## Method
The authors generalized the Reflection Asymmetric Shell Model (RASM) to include the triaxial-octupole degree of freedom, using a deformed Nilsson basis with both quadrupole (ε₂) and octupole (ε₃₂) deformation parameters. The Hamiltonian included quadrupole-quadrupole, octupole-octupole, monopole pairing, and quadrupole pairing interactions, with strengths determined self-consistently from the deformation parameters. Parity and angular momentum projection were applied to multi-quasiparticle configurations, and the model was diagonalized to calculate energy levels and B(E3) transition probabilities for the N=150 isotonic chain.

## Key numbers
- The 2⁻ bandhead energies observed in the N=150 isotonic chain: 0.842 MeV in ²⁴⁶Cm, 0.592 MeV in ²⁴⁸Cf, 0.879 MeV in ²⁵⁰Fm, and 0.929 MeV in ²⁵²No.
- Typical BCS pairing gaps in this mass region are approximately 0.5 MeV for neutrons and 0.8 MeV for protons, implying any relatively pure 2-quasiparticle state should lie above 1 MeV.
- The octupole deformation parameters ε₃₂ used to reproduce the data: 0.105 for ²⁴⁶Cm, 0.118 for ²⁴⁸Cf, 0.110 for ²⁵⁰Fm, and 0.107 for ²⁵²No.
- The quadrupole deformation was fixed at ε₂=0.235 for all nuclei studied.
- The polynomial fits for 1⁻ and 2⁻ bandhead energies cross at mass number A=242, suggesting a transition from axial Y₃₀ dominance to nonaxial Y₃₂ dominance.
- The strongest triaxial-octupole collectivity appears in ²⁴⁸Cf, with the largest calculated B(E3; 0⁺→3⁻) value among the isotones.

## Context
This work extends theoretical discussions of tetrahedral symmetry and Y₃₂ deformation that began with Hamamoto (1991) and were developed by Takami (1998), Dudek (2002), Schunck (2004), and Dudek (2006). It contrasts with the well-established axial-octupole (Y₃₀) correlations in actinide nuclei (Ahmad, 1993) and the Ra-Pu region, where 1⁻ bands lie low while 2⁻ bands are high. The paper enables future experimental measurements of E3 transition rates via Coulomb excitation to confirm the nonaxial-octupole nature of these bands and their connection to tetrahedral symmetry in superheavy nuclei up to element 108.
