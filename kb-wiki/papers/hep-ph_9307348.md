# Dynamical Growth Rate of a Diffuse Interface in First Order Phase Transitions

> We compute the dynamical prefactor in the nucleation rate of bubbles or droplets in first order phase transitions for the case where both viscous damping and thermal dissipation are significant. This result, which generalizes previous work on nucleation, may be applied to study the growth of bubbles or droplets in condensed matter systems as well as in heavy ion collisions and in the expansion of the early universe.

## Key claim

The paper derives a general expression for the dynamical prefactor \(\kappa\) in the nucleation rate of critical bubbles in first order phase transitions, valid when both viscous damping and thermal dissipation are significant; this generalizes prior results that treated only one dissipation mechanism at a time.

## Method

The authors linearize the hydrodynamic equations around a stationary bubble configuration with a diffuse interface, using the Kotchine conditions (generalized Rankine-Hugoniot relations) to connect density, velocity, and temperature across the interface. They combine the energy flux balance at the interface with the Laplace formula for surface tension and the continuity of chemical potentials to obtain an expression for the bubble growth rate \(dR/dt\). The dynamical prefactor \(\kappa\) is then extracted from the exponential growth law \(R - R_* \propto \exp(\kappa t)\).

## Key numbers

* The dynamical prefactor is \(\kappa = \frac{2\sigma}{(\Delta w)^2 R_*^3}\left[\lambda T + 2\left(\frac{4}{3}\eta + \zeta\right)\right]\), where \(\sigma\) is surface tension, \(\Delta w\) is the difference in enthalpy densities, \(R_*\) is the critical bubble radius, \(\lambda\) is thermal conductivity, \(T\) is temperature, and \(\eta\) and \(\zeta\) are shear and bulk viscosities.
* In the limit of zero baryon number (\(\lambda \rightarrow 0\)), the result reduces to the earlier expression of Csernai and Kapusta, which depends linearly on shear viscosity.
* In the limit where viscous damping is negligible (\(\eta, \zeta \rightarrow 0\)), the result reduces to the earlier expression of Kawasaki and of Turski and Langer, which depends linearly on thermal conductivity.

## Context

This work extends the kinetic theory of homogeneous nucleation developed by Langer (1969) and the subsequent calculations of the dynamical prefactor by Turski and Langer (1980) for thermal dissipation, and by Csernai and Kapusta (1992) for viscous damping in systems with negligible baryon density. The general formula enables applications to condensed matter systems, baryon-rich matter in heavy ion collisions, and first order phase transitions in the early universe and neutron star formation, where both dissipation mechanisms may be relevant.
