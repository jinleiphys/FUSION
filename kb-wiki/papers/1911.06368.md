# Quantum Computing for Neutrino-nucleus Scattering

> Neutrino-nucleus cross section uncertainties are expected to be a dominant systematic in future accelerator neutrino experiments. The cross sections are determined by the linear response of the nucleus to the weak interactions of the neutrino, and are dominated by energy and distance scales of the order of the separation between nucleons in the nucleus. These response functions are potentially an important early physics application of quantum computers. Here we present an analysis of the resources required and their expected scaling for scattering cross section calculations. We also examine simple small-scale neutrino-nucleus models on modern quantum hardware. In this paper, we use variational methods to obtain the ground state of a three nucleon system (the triton) and then implement the relevant time evolution. In order to tame the errors in present-day NISQ devices, we explore the use of different error-mitigation techniques to increase the fidelity of the calculations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a full quantum computation of neutrino-nucleus scattering for a nucleus like 40Ar, using Trotter-based time evolution, would require approximately 4000 qubits and on the order of 10^10 CNOT gates, placing it at a complexity level comparable to factoring a 1024-bit integer and likely out of reach for near-term NISQ devices; however, the authors also show that simplified few-nucleon models can be implemented on current quantum hardware as proof-of-principle demonstrations.

## Method

The authors encode a pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian on a lattice using the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map fermionic modes to qubits, then implement time evolution via Trotter-Suzuki product formulas (first and second order) for both an "alpha" splitting (kinetic energy broken into individual hopping terms) and a "beta" splitting (kinetic energy treated as a single operator using fast fermionic Fourier transforms). They estimate resource requirements (qubit count, CNOT and rotation gate counts) for realistic systems up to 40Ar, and also analyze an alternative approach using qubitization. For a small-scale demonstration on actual quantum hardware, they implement a 3-nucleon system (triton) on a 2x2 lattice with one static nucleon, using a first-quantized mapping requiring only 4 qubits for the dynamical particles, and apply variational methods to obtain the ground state followed by time evolution with error-mitigation techniques.

## Key numbers

* For a 40Ar calculation with energy resolution Δω = 10 MeV, the estimated resource requirements are approximately 4012 qubits, ~10^10 CNOT gates, and ~10^9 rotation gates using the β-splitting method.
* For the same system at lower resolution Δω = 100 MeV, the estimates reduce to ~5×10^8 CNOT gates and ~10^8 rotation gates.
* The lattice spacing used in the resource estimates is a = 1.4 fm, with Hamiltonian parameters t = 10.5794 MeV, C0 = -98.2265511 MeV, and D0 = 127.839693 MeV.
* The triton toy model uses a 2x2 lattice with periodic boundary conditions, requiring 8 qubits in second quantization but only 4 qubits in the first-quantized mapping for the 2 dynamical particles.
* The ratio of qubiterate applications to base time-evolution applications is 8 for the α-splitting method and 128 for the β-splitting method in the 40Ar region.

## Context

This work extends the quantum algorithm for neutrino-nucleus scattering proposed by Roggero (2018) by providing detailed resource estimates for realistic implementations and demonstrating a small-scale proof-of-principle on current hardware. The paper contrasts its Trotter-based approach with qubitization (Low and Chuang, 2019) and finds that while qubitization has optimal asymptotic scaling, its implementation costs are higher than parallelized Trotter circuits for the systems considered. The simplified triton model is motivated by the finding of Pastore (2019) that two-nucleon physics captures much of the nuclear response, and the authors suggest that near-term quantum computers could test the event generator paradigm where a struck nucleon pair is treated quantum mechanically while propagating semiclassically through the rest of the nucleus.
