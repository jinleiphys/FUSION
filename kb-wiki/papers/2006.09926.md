# Properties of a separable representation of optical potentials

> Background: Separable interactions have a long history in nuclear physics. In the last few years, separable expansions have been used to represent the optical potential between a nucleon (proton or neutron) and a target. Purpose: We explore the non-local properties of these separable optical potentials as well as their convergence behavior. Method: For a couple of cases, we use the generalized Ersnt-Shakin-Thaler scheme to generate separable interactions starting from local optical potentials. We study the variation of the interaction with energy range and rank. Results: We find that, overall the off-diagonal behavior of the converged separable interaction deviates from the Gaussian form assumed by Perey and Buck. However, in the region surrounding the maximum depth the Gaussian form works quite well. Focusing on this region, we study potentials describing neutron elastic scattering on $^{16}$O and $^{48}$Ca for beam energies in the range of $ E=$10-50 MeV and explore several measures of non-locality of the separable interactions. Conclusions: When the energy range considered for generating the separable interaction is $0\le E_{range}\le 50$ MeV, the resulting non-locality is large and target dependent. Contrarily, the nonlocality obtained including larger energy ranges in the separable procedure is independent of the target and other details of the original local potential. We find that, even when including in the expansion many support points with energy ranges $0\le E_{range}\le 2400$ MeV, the resulting potential retains non-local behavior. Connections with microscopic optical potentials as well as other transformations used in the nucleon-nucleon domain are made.

## Key claim

The separable representation of local optical potentials induces significant non-locality that persists even when the energy range included in the expansion extends up to 2400 MeV, and this non-locality follows a universal, target-independent behavior for large energy ranges, contrary to the target-dependent non-locality seen for small energy ranges below 50 MeV.

## Method

The authors applied the generalized Ernst-Shakin-Thaler (EST) scheme to generate separable representations of the CH89 global optical potential for neutron elastic scattering on 16O and 48Ca. They studied the convergence of the separable interactions by varying both the rank (number of support points, up to N=53) and the energy range of support points (from 10 MeV up to 2400 MeV), then extracted non-locality parameters by fitting the cross-diagonal behavior of the resulting potentials to the Perey-Buck Gaussian form.

## Key numbers

- For small energy ranges (E_range less than 50 MeV), the non-locality parameter beta for s-wave neutrons on 48Ca at 5 MeV was approximately 0.89-0.97 fm, consistent with the Perey-Buck value of 0.85 fm
- For p-wave (l=1) neutrons on 48Ca at 5 MeV, the non-locality parameter beta was significantly larger at 1.46-1.59 fm
- The asymptotic value of beta as E_range approaches infinity (from exponential fits) for 48Ca at 5 MeV was 0.31 +/- 0.06 fm for J=1/2+ and 0.29 +/- 0.03 fm for J=3/2-
- For 16O at 5 MeV, the asymptotic beta value was 0.27 +/- 0.05 fm for J=1/2+ and -0.03 +/- 0.60 fm for J=3/2-
- The EST expansion required up to 53 support points (for E_range = 2400 MeV) to achieve convergence of the potential matrix elements
- The separable interactions were studied at beam energies of 5 MeV and 20 MeV, spanning the range of experimental interest for transfer reactions

## Context

This work extends the application of separable expansions to nucleon-nucleus optical potentials, building on the EST scheme previously used by Hlophe et al. (2013, 2014, 2017) for deuteron-induced reactions. The results connect to the Perey-Buck (1962) Gaussian non-locality assumption, showing that while the Gaussian form works well near the potential minimum, the overall off-diagonal behavior deviates from it. The findings also enable comparison with microscopic optical potentials from Rotureau et al. (2017, 2018), suggesting those ab-initio calculations contain physics corresponding to energy ranges below 10 MeV.
