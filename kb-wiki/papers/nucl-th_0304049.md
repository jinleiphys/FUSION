# Many-body effects in nuclear structure

> We calculate, for the first time, the state-dependent pairing gap of a finite nucleus (120Sn) diagonalizing the bare nucleon-nucleon potential (Argonne v14) in a Hartree-Fock basis (with effective k-mass m_k eqult to 0.7 m), within the framework of the BCS approximation including scattering states up to 800 MeV above the Fermi energy to achieve convergence. The resulting gap accounts for about half of the experimental gap. We find that a consistent description of the low-energy nuclear spectrum requires, aside from the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction, not only the dressing of single-particle motion through the coupling to the nuclear surface, to give the right density of levels close to the Fermi energy (and thus an effective mass m* approximately equal to m), but also the renormalization of collective vibrational modes through vertex and self-energy processes, processes which are also found to play an essential role in the pairing channel, leading to a long range, state dependent component of the pairing interaction. The combined effect of the bare nucleon-nucleon potential and of the induced pairing interaction arising from the exchange of low-lying surface vibrations between nucleons moving in time reversal states close to the Fermi energy accounts for the experimental gap.

## Key claim

The bare nucleon-nucleon potential (Argonne v14) diagonalized in a Hartree-Fock basis within BCS theory accounts for only about half of the experimental pairing gap in 120Sn; the full experimental gap is reproduced only when the induced pairing interaction from exchange of low-lying surface vibrations is added, along with self-energy and vertex corrections that satisfy Ward identities.

## Method

The authors diagonalized the Argonne v14 nucleon-nucleon potential in a Hartree-Fock basis (using the Skyrme Sly4 parametrization with effective k-mass m_k ≈ 0.7 m) within the BCS approximation, including scattering states up to 800 MeV above the Fermi energy in a spherical box of radius 15 fm. They then applied nuclear field theory (NFT) to renormalize single-particle states, collective vibrational modes, and the pairing interaction through particle-vibration coupling, solving the Dyson equation iteratively for all quasiparticle states simultaneously while including both self-energy and vertex correction processes.

## Key numbers

- The experimental pairing gap of 120Sn derived from odd-even mass difference is approximately 1.4 MeV.
- The BCS calculation using only the bare Argonne v14 potential yields a state-dependent pairing gap that accounts for about half of the experimental value (approximately 0.7 MeV average for levels around the Fermi energy).
- The B(E2) transition probability for the low-lying 2+ quadrupole vibration in 120Sn calculated with standard effective interactions (Gogny or Skyrme) is about a factor of 3 too small compared to experiment.
- After renormalization including particle-vibration coupling, the B(E2) value increases to bring theory into agreement with experiment.
- The calculated static quadrupole moment Q of the 2+ state is 8 e fm^2, consistent with the experimental range of 10 ± 10 e fm^2 or -5 ± 10 e fm^2.
- The renormalized state-dependent pairing gap including induced pairing interaction from surface vibrations is close to the experimental value of 1.4 MeV.

## Context

This work extends earlier nuclear field theory (NFT) approaches by Barranco, Broglia, and collaborators that applied particle-vibration coupling to schematic models and realistic situations, and it addresses the question of a long-range component of the pairing interaction in nuclei raised by recent studies (references 22-24 in the paper). The results show that mean-field theory with bare nucleon-nucleon potentials (such as Skyrme or Gogny forces) cannot simultaneously reproduce experimental transition strengths, pairing gaps, and quasiparticle densities, and that the explicit inclusion of surface vibration exchange in the pairing channel is essential. The authors suggest that future work should examine whether this framework can reduce the present r.m.s. error of 0.674 MeV in the best available Hartree-Fock mass formulas.
