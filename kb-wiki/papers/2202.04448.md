# Self-consistent energy density functional approaches to the crust of neutron stars

> In order to study structure of the crust in neutron stars, we develop a finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock method in the full three-dimensional coordinate space using the Fermion operator expansion method. It provides us with a possible order-N approach to non-uniform neutron-star matters.

## Key claim

The paper presents a finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock method in full three-dimensional coordinate space using the Fermion operator expansion (FOE) method, which provides a possible order-N approach to non-uniform neutron-star matter, but the test calculation is performed only for symmetric nuclear matter (proton ratio 0.5) with no pairing correlation, making the result not realistic for neutron star inner crust.

## Method

The authors apply the FOE method to the BKN energy density functional, a simplified Skyrme-like functional without spin-orbit coupling and with identical proton and neutron densities, using a three-dimensional coordinate-space representation discretized on a 25^3 cubic mesh with 1 fm spacing. The Fermi-Dirac distribution function is approximated by a series of Chebyshev polynomials up to degree M, where the density operator is constructed by M times operation of the Hamiltonian on each state. The calculation is performed at fixed baryon density and proton ratio, with the chemical potential adjusted at each iteration to maintain the correct particle number.

## Key numbers

- Baryon density fixed at ρ_B = 0.03 fm^{-3} with proton ratio Y_p = 0.5 (symmetric nuclear matter)
- Mesh size: 25^3 points with cubic mesh spacing of 1 fm, giving volume V = 25^3 fm^3
- Total nucleon number in the space: ρ_B × V = 468.75
- Temperature tested: T = 5 MeV and T = 10 MeV
- Density of the "sea" of symmetric nuclear matter: about 8.3 × 10^{-4} fm^{-3}
- M (polynomial degree) is order of thousands at T ~ 100 keV, while order ten at T ~ 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Thomas-Fermi (local density) approximation of Oya (1993) and the Wigner-Seitz approximation of Negele and Vautherin (1973) for non-uniform neutron-star matter, which cannot properly account for transport properties. The FOE method, originally developed in computational condensed matter physics by Goedecker and Teter (1995) and based on the "principle of nearsightedness" from Wigner (2002), offers an alternative to the three-dimensional Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov solver developed by Jin et al. (2017) and its finite-temperature extension by K. and N. (2020). The method enables future calculations of non-uniform neutron-star matter with thousands of nucleons without symmetry restrictions, though the current test uses symmetric matter and neglects pairing.
