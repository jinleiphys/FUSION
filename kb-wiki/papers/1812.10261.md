# Zemach moments and radii of 2,3H and 3,4He

> We present benchmark calculations of Zemach moments and radii of 2,3H and 3,4He using various few-body methods. Zemach moments are required to interpret muonic atom data measured by the CREMA collaboration at the Paul Scherrer Institute. Conversely, radii extracted from spectroscopic measurements can be compared with ab initio computations, posing stringent constraints on the nuclear model. For a given few-body method, different numerical procedures can be applied to compute these quantities. A detailed analysis of the numerical uncertainties entering the total theoretical error is presented. Uncertainties from the few-body method and the calculational procedure are found to be smaller than the dependencies on the dynamical modeling and the single nucleon inputs, which are found to be <= 2%. When relativistic corrections and two-body currents are accounted for, the calculated moments and radii are in very good agreement with the available experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that for light nuclei (A=2-4), the theoretical uncertainties from the few-body method and the numerical procedure used to compute Zemach moments and radii are smaller than the dependencies on the nuclear dynamical modeling and single nucleon inputs, which are found to be less than or equal to 2%, and that when relativistic corrections and two-body currents are included, the calculated moments and radii agree very well with available experimental data.

## Method

The authors benchmark electromagnetic observables, including charge radii, Zemach moments, magnetic radii, and magnetic moments, for deuterium, tritium, helium-3, and helium-4 using multiple ab initio few-body methods: the Numerov algorithm and harmonic oscillator expansion for A=2, and Variational Monte Carlo (VMC), Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC), hyperspherical harmonics in momentum-space (HH-p), and the effective interaction hyperspherical harmonics (EIHH) scheme for A=3 and A=4. They compare three numerical procedures (momentum-space, coordinate-space, and mixed momentum-and-coordinate-space) for extracting moments from form factors, and then study the effects of including relativistic corrections and two-body currents from chiral effective field theory, using wave functions from the AV18+UIX nuclear Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

* Deuteron charge radius with nucleon form factors (Kelly parameterization): 2.1218(1) fm (Numerov) and 2.1219(1) fm (harmonic oscillator), compared to experimental values 2.1413(25) fm (CODATA 2014) and 2.1256(8) fm (Pohl 2016)
* Helium-3 charge radius in impulse approximation: 1.953(1) fm (HH-p) and 1.953(2) fm (EIHH), compared to experimental 1.973(14) fm
* Helium-3 third Zemach moment in impulse approximation: 27.56(20) fm^3 (HH-p) and 27.65(10) fm^3 (EIHH), compared to experimental 28.15(70) fm^3
* Helium-3 charge radius with relativistic corrections and two-body currents included: 1.979(1)(10) fm, compared to experimental 1.973(14) fm
* Helium-3 magnetic moment with relativistic corrections and two-body currents: -2.093(1)(55) mu_N, compared to experimental -2.127 mu_N
* Helium-4 charge radius in impulse approximation: 1.649(1) fm (VMC), 1.648(2) fm (GFMC), 1.638(2) fm (EIHH), compared to experimental 1.681(4) fm
* Helium-4 third Zemach moment in impulse approximation: 16.0(1) fm^3 (VMC and GFMC), 15.6(2) fm^3 (EIHH), compared to experimental 16.73(10) fm^3
* Point-proton mean-square radius for helium-3 from EIHH: 1.953(2) fm (charge radius)
* Nucleon form factor moments from Kelly parameterization: r_p^2 = 0.744(7) fm^2, r_n^2 = -0.112(3) fm^2, r_p^4 = 1.6(1) fm^4, r_n^4 = -0.33(2) fm^4

## Context

This work extends the benchmark of few-body methods for light nuclei, which was last performed for binding energy and radius of helium-4 nearly two decades ago (Benchmark 2001) and did not use state-of-the-art nuclear forces. It directly supports the CREMA collaboration's muonic atom measurements at the Paul Scherrer Institute, which have exposed the "proton-radius" and "deuteron-radius" puzzles (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2013, Pohl 2016). The calculations enable more precise extraction of nuclear charge radii from muonic Lamb shift measurements and provide stringent constraints on nuclear models, with the authors noting that ab initio calculations from Ji (2018) currently provide the most precise determinations of the two-photon exchange contributions.
