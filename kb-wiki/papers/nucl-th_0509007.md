# Symmetries in physics

> The concept of symmetries in physics is briefly reviewed. In the first part of these lecture notes, some of the basic mathematical tools needed for the understanding of symmetries in nature are presented, namely group theory, Lie groups and Lie algebras, and Noether's theorem. In the second part, some applications of symmetries in physics are discussed, ranging from isospin and flavor symmetry to more recent developments involving the interacting boson model and its extension to supersymmetries in nuclear physics.

## Key claim

This lecture note reviews the concept of symmetries in physics, presenting group theory, Lie algebras, and Noether's theorem as mathematical tools, and illustrating applications from isospin and flavor symmetry to the interacting boson model and nuclear supersymmetry. The abstract's claim of a "brief review" is qualified by the paper's structure as pedagogical lecture notes that introduce foundational mathematical concepts before discussing applications, rather than presenting new research results.

## Method

The paper first defines abstract groups, Lie groups, and Lie algebras, using the rotation group SO(3) and its su(2) algebra as examples with explicit commutation relations and Casimir operators. It then introduces the concept of dynamical symmetry breaking, where a Hamiltonian is expressed as a combination of Casimir invariants of a chain of nested algebras, allowing eigenvalues to be obtained in closed form while eigenstates remain unchanged. Applications are presented for isospin symmetry in nuclei using SU_T(2) and for flavor symmetry in hadrons using SU(3), followed by a discussion of nuclear supersymmetry in the context of the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) and its extension to boson-fermion systems.

## Key numbers

- For the T=3/2 isobaric analog states in 13B, 13C, 13N, and 13O with J^pi=1/2^-, the dynamical symmetry formula E(M_T)=a+b M_T+c M_T^2 gives parameters a=80.59, b=-2.96, and c=-0.26 MeV.
- For the ground state baryon octet, the Gell-Mann-Okubo mass formula with parameters a=1111.3, b=-189.6, d=-39.9, e=-3.8, and f=0.9 MeV describes successive breaking from SU(3) symmetry.
- The su(2) algebra yields eigenvalues n_j=j(j+1) for the Casimir operator j^2, with j=0,1/2,1,... and m=-j,-j+1,...,j.

## Context

This paper extends the pedagogical tradition of group theory applications in physics, referencing foundational work by Pauli (1926) on the Coulomb problem, Heisenberg (1932) on isospin, and Gell-Mann and Okubo (1962) on SU(3) mass formulas. It builds on the Interacting Boson Model (IBM) developed by Iachello and collaborators, and discusses nuclear supersymmetry as a composite-particle phenomenon distinct from fundamental SUSY, citing evidence from the quartet of nuclei 194Pt, 195Au, 195Pt, and 196Au. The work enables further study of dynamical symmetries in nuclear structure and the classification of collective and single-particle excitations across neighboring nuclei.
