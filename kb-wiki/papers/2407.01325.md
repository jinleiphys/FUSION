# Ab initio description of monopole resonances in light- and medium-mass nuclei: IV. Angular momentum projection and rotation-vibration coupling

> Giant Resonances are, with nuclear rotations, the most evident expression of collectivity in finite nuclei. These two categories of excitations, however, are traditionally described within different formal schemes, such that vibrational and rotational degrees of freedom are separately treated and coupling effects between those are often neglected. The present work puts forward an approach aiming at a consitent treatment of vibrations and rotations. Specifically, this paper is the last in a series of four dedicated to the investigation of the giant monopole resonance in doubly open-shell nuclei via the ab initio Projected Generator Coordinate Method (PGCM). The present focus is on the treatment and impact of angular momentum restoration within such calculations. The PGCM being based on the use of deformed mean-field states, the angular-momentum restoration is performed when solving the secular equation to extract vibrational excitations. In this context, it is shown that performing the angular momentum restoration only after solving the secular equation contaminates the monopole response with an unphysical coupling to the rotational motion, as was also shown recently for (quasi-particle) random phase approximation calculations based on a deformed reference state. Eventually, the present work based on the PGCM confirms that an a priori angular momentum restoration is necessary to handle consistently both collective motions at the same time. This further pleads in favor of implementing the full-fledged projected (quasi-particle) random phase approximation in the future.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that performing angular momentum projection *after* solving the generator coordinate method secular equation (PAV-GCM) contaminates the monopole response with an unphysical coupling to rotational motion, confirming that *a priori* angular momentum restoration (VAP-GCM) is necessary for a consistent treatment of vibrational and rotational collective motions.

## Method

The authors compare three levels of calculation for the giant monopole resonance in doubly open-shell nuclei using the ab initio Projected Generator Coordinate Method (PGCM): GCM without angular momentum projection, PAV-GCM where projection is applied after solving the secular equation, and VAP-GCM where projection is included during the variational solution. They apply an empirical subtraction method to remove the spurious rotational coupling from the PAV-GCM results and compare the resulting strength functions. Calculations are performed for ²⁸Si using a chiral effective field theory Hamiltonian at N³LO with a spherical harmonic oscillator basis (ħω=12 MeV, e_max=10) and two-dimensional generator coordinates (r, β₂).

## Key numbers

- In ²⁸Si, the four dominant GCM monopole peaks in the interval [11, 23] MeV are shifted upward by 1.4, 1.0, 1.5, and 0.7 MeV respectively in the J=0 (monopole) VAP-GCM channel
- The same four peaks shift by 2.4, 2.1, 2.5, and 1.7 MeV respectively in the J=2 (quadrupole) VAP-GCM channel
- The overlap a_ν between excited GCM states and the PAV-GCM ground state reaches 0.05 in ²⁸Si and is up to three times larger in other studied nuclei such as ⁴⁶Ti or ²⁴Mg
- PAV-GCM and VAP-GCM J=0 energies typically differ by 0.5 MeV
- The quadrupole response strength of the first two peaks is divided by about a factor of three when comparing GCM to VAP-GCM

## Context

This work is the fourth in a series (Papers I-III, Porro 2024) investigating the giant monopole resonance via ab initio PGCM, extending prior work by Porro (2023) that demonstrated spurious rotational coupling in a posteriori angular-momentum projected RPA calculations. The results confirm that the full projected (quasi-particle) random phase approximation, whose formalism is available from Federman and Ring (1985) and Tsuchimochi (2015), should be implemented to properly handle rotation-vibration coupling, rather than relying on a posteriori projection methods.
