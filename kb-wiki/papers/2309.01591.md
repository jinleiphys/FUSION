# Unified equations of state for cold non-accreting neutron stars with Brussels-Montreal functionals. IV. Role of the symmetry energy in pasta phases

> Our previous investigation of neutron-star crusts, based on the functional BSk24, led to a substantial reduction of the pasta mantle when Strutinsky integral and pairing corrections were added on top of the fourth-order extended Thomas-Fermi method (ETF). Here, our earlier calculations are widened to a larger set of functionals within the same family, and we find that the microscopic corrections weaken significantly the influence of the symmetry energy. In particular, the correlation observed at the pure ETF level between the density for the onset of pasta formation and the symmetry energy vanishes, not only for the $L$ coefficient but also for the symmetry-energy values at the relevant densities. Moreover, the inclusion of microscopic corrections results in a much lower abundance of pasta for all functionals.

# Unified equations of state for cold non-accreting neutron stars with Brussels-Montreal functionals. IV. Role of the symmetry energy in pasta phases

## Key claim

The correlation observed at the pure extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) level between the density for the onset of pasta formation and the symmetry energy vanishes when Strutinsky integral and pairing corrections are added, not only for the L coefficient but also for the symmetry-energy values at the relevant densities; the inclusion of microscopic corrections results in a much lower abundance of pasta for all functionals.

## Method

The authors used the fourth-order extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) method followed by the addition of proton shell corrections via the Strutinsky integral method and proton pairing correlations in the Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) approximation (ETFSI approach) to calculate the equilibrium configurations of five nuclear pasta phases (gnocchi, spaghetti, bucatini, lasagna, and Swiss cheese) in Wigner-Seitz cells. They applied this formalism to four Brussels-Montreal nuclear energy-density functionals (BSk22, BSk24, BSk25, BSk26) which differ in their symmetry-energy coefficients and neutron-matter equation of state, and determined the most energetically favorable pasta phase at each average baryon density.

## Key numbers

- The transition density from spheres to pasta at the ETF level ranges from 0.042 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk25) to 0.056 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk22 and BSk26); at the ETFSI level these densities shift to 0.065 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk25) through 0.079 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk26)
- The crust-core transition density with pasta included ranges from 0.071 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk22) to 0.089 fm$^{-3}$ (BSk25)
- Within the ETF method, pasta phases account for about 35% to 55% of the mass of the crust; with microscopic corrections this drops to about 15% for BSk22, BSk24, and BSk26, and to about 30% for BSk25
- The symmetry-energy coefficients for the functionals are: J = 32.0 MeV (BSk22), 30.0 MeV (BSk24 and BSk26), 29.0 MeV (BSk25); L = 68.5 MeV (BSk22), 46.4 MeV (BSk24), 36.9 MeV (BSk25), 37.5 MeV (BSk26)

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Pearson et al. (2018, 2020, 2022) that used only the BSk24 functional, now widening the analysis to a larger set of Brussels-Montreal functionals to systematically investigate the role of the symmetry energy in pasta formation. The results contradict the correlation between pasta onset density and symmetry energy found in earlier Thomas-Fermi studies by Oyamatsu and Iida (2007), Bao and Shen (2015), and Grill et al. (2013), and show that liquid-drop model estimates by Newton et al. (2013), Balliet et al. (2021), Dinh Thi et al. (2021), and Parmar et al. (2022) overestimate pasta abundance because they neglect shell and pairing effects. The findings enable more realistic unified equations of state for neutron star structure calculations and provide input for interpreting observations of neutron star crust properties.
