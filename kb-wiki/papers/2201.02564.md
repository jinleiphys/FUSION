# Rethinking the ill-posedness of the spectral function reconstruction -- why is it fundamentally hard and how Artificial Neural Networks can help

> Reconstructing hadron spectral functions through Euclidean correlation functions are of the important missions in lattice QCD calculations. However, in a Källen--Lehmann(KL) spectral representation, the reconstruction is observed to be ill-posed in practice. It is usually ascribed to the fewer observation points compared to the number of points in the spectral function. In this paper, by solving the eigenvalue problem of continuous KL convolution, we show analytically that the ill-posedness of the inversion is fundamental and it exists even for continuous correlation functions. We discussed how to introduce regulators to alleviate the predicament, in which include the Artificial Neural Networks(ANNs) representations recently proposed by the Authors in~[Phys. Rev. D 106 (2022) L051502]. The uniqueness of solutions using ANNs representations is manifested analytically and validated numerically. Reconstructed spectral functions using different regularization schemes are also demonstrated, together with their eigen-mode decomposition. We observe that components with large eigenvalues can be reliably reconstructed by all methods, whereas those with low eigenvalues need to be constrained by regulators.

## Key claim

The ill-posedness of reconstructing hadron spectral functions from Euclidean correlation functions is fundamental and exists even for continuous correlation functions with infinite observation points, not merely due to having fewer observation points than spectral function points as commonly assumed; the instability arises because the Källen–Lehmann convolution operator has eigenvalues arbitrarily close to zero, making the inversion unstable against arbitrarily small noise in the correlation data.

## Method

The authors analytically solved the eigenvalue problem of the continuous Källen–Lehmann convolution operator, finding eigenfunctions and eigenvalues via a generalized Fourier transformation. They then demonstrated that components with large eigenvalues can be reliably reconstructed while those with small eigenvalues (null-modes) require regularization, and showed analytically that Artificial Neural Network (ANN) representations of the spectral function yield unique solutions. Numerical reconstructions using different regularization schemes (Tikhonov, Maximum Entropy Method, Bayesian Reconstruction, and ANN representations) were compared with eigen-mode decomposition.

## Key numbers

- Eigenvalues of the continuous Källen–Lehmann kernel: \(\lambda_{\pm,s} = 1/(2\cosh(\pi s/2))\), where \(s \in \mathbb{R}\) is a continuous label; these eigenvalues can be arbitrarily close to zero for large \(|s|\)
- A null-mode example with \(s_0 = 7\) and \(\sigma = 1\) produces an \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) perturbation in the spectral function but only \(\mathcal{O}(10^{-5})\) difference in the correlation functions \(D(k)\) and \(G_\beta(\tau)\)
- The Fourier transformation has constant eigenvalues \(|\lambda^{\text{[Fourier]}}_{\pm,s}| = \sqrt{\pi/2}\) (non-vanishing lower bound), while the Laplace transformation has eigenvalues \(|\lambda^{\text{[Laplace]}}_{\pm,s}| = \sqrt{\pi} \cosh^{-1/2}(\pi s)\) (can be arbitrarily small)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of McWhirter and Pike (1978) on eigenvalue problems for convolution kernels to the Källen–Lehmann representation used in lattice QCD, showing that the ill-posedness is more fundamental than previously thought. The authors' ANN-based regularization method (Wang et al., Phys. Rev. D 106, L051502, 2022) is shown to provide unique solutions, complementing traditional approaches like the Maximum Entropy Method (Jarrell, 1996; Asakawa, 2000) and Bayesian Reconstruction (Burnier, 2013). The analytical characterization of null-modes enables better design of regularization strategies for extracting transport properties (e.g., heavy quark diffusion coefficients, shear and bulk viscosities) and in-medium hadron properties from lattice QCD correlation functions.
