# Jet Quenching via Jet Collimation

> The ATLAS Collaboration recently reported strong modifications of dijet properties in heavy ion collisions. In this work, we discuss to what extent these first data constrain already the microscopic mechanism underlying jet quenching. Simple kinematic arguments lead us to identify a frequency collimation mechanism via which the medium efficiently trims away the soft components of the jet parton shower. Through this mechanism, the observed dijet asymmetry can be accomodated with values of $\hat{q}\, L$ that lie in the expected order of magnitude.

## Key claim

The observed dijet asymmetry in ATLAS data can be accommodated with values of \(\hat{q}\, L\) that lie in the expected order of magnitude, provided that the medium acts as a frequency collimator that trims away the soft components of the jet parton shower; this mechanism is presented as a qualitative picture rather than a definitive quantitative extraction.

## Method

The authors analyze ATLAS dijet data from Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76\) TeV, comparing the distribution of the fraction \(x = E_{T_2}/E_{T_1}\) and the azimuthal separation \(\Delta\phi\) between leading and recoiling jets to proton-proton collisions. They derive lower and upper bounds on the fractional energy lost outside the jet cone (radius \(R=0.4\)) using the mean of the \(x\) distribution and a corona-effect correction. They then estimate the required \(\hat{q}\, L\) by combining these bounds with the vacuum and medium-modified MLLA parton distributions, assuming that all partons with energy \(\omega \leq \sqrt{\hat{q}\, L}\) are decorrelated from the jet axis.

## Key numbers

* The mean fractional energy \(\left< x\right>_{pp} \leq 0.67\) and \(\left< x\right>_{PbPb} \leq 0.54\) for jets with \(E_{T_1}>100\) GeV, giving a difference \(\left< x\right>_{pp} - \left< x\right>_{PbPb} \geq 0.13\).
* At least 10 GeV of additional energy is deposited outside the cone of radius \(R=0.4\) for total jet energies \(E_T > 100\) GeV in Pb-Pb collisions.
* The fraction of unmodified jets \(\alpha\) is estimated as \(\alpha \lesssim 0.5\), leading to a mean energy loss in the medium \(\left<\Delta E\right>_m/E_T < 0.2\).
* Using vacuum fragmentation, the estimate for \(\hat{q}\, L\) is \(35\, (E_T/E_0)^2 \le \hat{q}\, L \le 85\, (E_T/E_0)^2\) GeV\(^2\) with \(E_0 = 100\) GeV.
* Using a medium-modified MLLA kernel consistent with a factor 5 suppression of leading hadron spectra, the estimate is \(30\, (E_T/E_0)^2 \le \hat{q}\, L \le 60\, (E_T/E_0)^2\) GeV\(^2\).
* For path lengths \(L\sim 6\) fm, this yields \(5 \le \hat{q} \le 10\) GeV\(^2\)/fm; for \(L\sim 10\) fm, \(3 \le \hat{q} \le 6\) GeV\(^2\)/fm.

## Context

This work extends the discussion of jet quenching from RHIC-era single inclusive hadron suppression to the first LHC dijet data from ATLAS (2010), which provided calorimetric jet measurements at TeV energies. The frequency collimation mechanism synthesizes earlier ideas on collisional energy loss (Wicks et al., 2005), medium response to jet propagation (Casalderrey-Solana et al., 2004; Li et al., 2010), and decoherence of partonic showers (Leonidov et al., 2010; Mehtar-Tani et al., 2010), and is intended to guide the development of Monte Carlo models for jet quenching (Zapp et al., 2008; Armesto et al., 2009; Schenke et al., 2009; Renk, 2010).
