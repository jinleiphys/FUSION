# Lattice QCD with Classical and Quantum Electrodynamics

> We are doubtlessly familiar with some edition of Jackson's tome on electrodynamics, and Schwinger's calculation of the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron in QED. From the perspective of strong interactions, however, electromagnetic effects usually amount to negligible contributions. Despite this fact, electromagnetic probes have always been a fundamental source for our knowledge of QCD experimentally. Elastic scattering of electrons off nucleons provides us a window to their distributions of charge and magnetism. To account for the spectrum of QCD at the percent level, moreover, we need isospin breaking introduced from both quark masses and electric charges. This overview concerns some of the prospects and progress of studying electromagnetic effects in QCD. Our focus is divided between classical and quantum effects. In classical electromagnetic fields, the dynamical response of QCD to external conditions can be investigated. The vacuum and hadrons alike should be viewed as media which respond to external fields: both magnetize and polarize in magnetic fields, for example. At the quantum level, electromagnetism and QCD renormalize each other. In the era of high precision lattice computations, both strong and electromagnetic contributions must be accounted for to make predictions at the percent level.

## Key claim

Electromagnetic effects in QCD, while usually negligible compared to strong interactions, must be accounted for to achieve percent-level precision in lattice QCD predictions; the paper surveys both classical electromagnetic field responses (hadron polarizabilities, vacuum magnetization) and quantum electrodynamic renormalization effects (isospin breaking, mass splittings) that are now accessible to lattice techniques.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD methods for incorporating classical electromagnetic fields via background field techniques, including quantization conditions for uniform fields on a torus and effective actions for charged and neutral hadrons. For quantum electrodynamics, the review covers simulations coupling QCD with a U(1) gauge field, addressing finite-size effects from long-range interactions on a torus and the need for gauge fixing with non-compact photon fields.

## Key numbers

- Electric polarizability of the hydrogen atom: α_E^(H-atom) = (27/8π) × (4/3 π a_B^3) ∝ e^(-6) m_e^(-3)
- Quantization condition for uniform electric fields on a lattice: q E = 2π n / (β L), with n an integer
- For m_π L ≈ 4, the expansion parameter e^2 B^2 / m_π^4 ∼ n^2 / 7, restricting magnetic field quanta to n < 3
- Neutron electric polarizability extraction includes a spin-interaction term ∼ 10% effect at physical pion mass, and ∼ 50% effect at m_π = 390 MeV
- Lattice spacing in early magnetic field studies: a ≈ 0.3 fm
- Critical magnetic field for charged rho condensation: B_c = M_ρ^2 / μ_ρ, with quenched SU(2) evidence at |e B_c| ∼ GeV^2
- Nucleon mass splitting: (M_n - M_p) / M_N ∼ +0.1%, with electromagnetic contribution estimated as (M_n - M_p)_QED ∼ -1 MeV and strong splitting (M_n - M_p)_QCD ∼ +2 MeV
- Kaon decay constant precision: f_K / f_π known to 0.4% from world average lattice results, with isospin breaking corrections ∼ 0.2% from valence quark mass dependence

## Context

This overview extends the line of work on electromagnetic effects in lattice QCD that began with background field calculations of nucleon magnetic moments (Bernard, 1982) and electric polarizabilities (Fiebig, 1988), and later quenched studies (Lee, 2005). It enables first-principles computation of hadron polarizabilities to resolve discrepancies with chiral perturbation theory predictions (Bernard, 1991; Holstein, 1990) and experimental efforts at COMPASS, MAX-Lab, and HIγS, as well as high-precision determination of isospin splittings and CKM matrix elements that require simultaneous QCD+QED simulations.
