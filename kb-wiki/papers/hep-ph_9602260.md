# Big Bang nucleosynthesis and physics beyond the Standard Model

> The Hubble expansion of galaxies, the $2.73\dK$ blackbody radiation background and the cosmic abundances of the light elements argue for a hot, dense origin of the universe --- the standard Big Bang cosmology --- and enable its evolution to be traced back fairly reliably to the nucleosynthesis era when the temperature was of $\Or(1)$ MeV corresponding to an expansion age of $\Or(1)$ sec. All particles, known and hypothetical, would have been created at higher temperatures in the early universe and analyses of their possible effects on the abundances of the synthesized elements enable many interesting constraints to be obtained on particle properties. These arguments have usefully complemented laboratory experiments in guiding attempts to extend physics beyond the Standard $SU(3)_{\c}{\otimes}SU(2)_Ł{\otimes}U(1)_{Y}$ Model, incorporating ideas such as supersymmetry, compositeness and unification. We first present a pedagogical account of relativistic cosmology and primordial nucleosynthesis, discussing both theoretical and observational aspects, and then proceed to examine such constraints in detail, in particular those pertaining to new massless particles and massive unstable particles. Finally, in a section aimed at particle physicists, we illustrate applications of such constraints to models of new physics.

## Key claim

Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) provides a powerful cosmological probe of particle properties beyond the Standard Model, but the derived constraints depend critically on the underlying assumptions of the standard cosmological model and the observational uncertainties in primordial elemental abundances; the paper argues that while cosmological data are often less precise than accelerator data, they can be much more sensitive to novel particle phenomena, though one should be cautious about rejecting theoretical possibilities based on restrictive cosmological constraints without critical appreciation of the assumptions.

## Method

The paper presents a pedagogical review of relativistic cosmology and primordial nucleosynthesis, covering the Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker models, the thermal history of the early universe, and the standard BBN model. It then derives general constraints on both relativistic and non-relativistic hypothetical particles that may be present during nucleosynthesis, and illustrates applications to specific beyond-Standard-Model scenarios including neutrinos, technicolour, supersymmetry, grand unification, and cosmic strings.

## Key numbers

* The cosmic microwave background temperature is measured as T_0 = 2.726 ± 0.01 K (Mather et al. 1994)
* The critical density today is ρ_c0 = 1.054 × 10^{-5} h^2 GeV cm^{-3}, where h ≡ H_0 / (100 km s^{-1} Mpc^{-1}) and h lies in the range 0.4 ≤ h ≤ 1
* The Hubble time is H_0^{-1} = 9.778 × 10^9 h^{-1} yr, giving a present Hubble radius of approximately 3000 h^{-1} Mpc
* The electroweak symmetry breaking scale is v ≡ (√2 G_F)^{-1/2} = 246 GeV
* The Planck mass is M_Pl ≡ G_N^{-1/2} = 1.221 × 10^{19} GeV
* The QCD confinement scale is T_c^QCD ∼ Λ_QCD ≈ 200 MeV
* The electroweak phase transition temperature is T_c^EW ∼ 250 GeV
* The GUT unification scale is M_GUT ≈ 2 × 10^{16} GeV (in supersymmetric theories)
* The present energy density in all gravitating matter gives Ω_0 ≈ 0.1-1 from dynamical measurements
* The matter-radiation equality temperature is T_eq = 5.63 × 10^{-9} GeV (Ω_0 h^2 Θ^{-3}) where Θ ≡ T_0/2.73 K
* The bound on non-relativistic matter density is Ω_NR0 h^2 ≤ 1, implying ρ_NR0 ≤ 1.05 × 10^{-5} GeV cm^{-3}

## Context

This review extends the earlier comprehensive discussions by Steigman (1979) and Dolgov and Zel'dovich (1981) on cosmological constraints from BBN, and complements the model review by Raffelt (1990) on astrophysical methods for constraining novel particle phenomena. The paper enables particle physicists to use BBN as a testing ground for new physics beyond the Standard Model, particularly for constraining properties of new massless particles and massive unstable particles, and provides the necessary cosmological background for assessing the reliability of these constraints.
