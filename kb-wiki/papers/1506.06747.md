# Cottingham formula and nucleon polarizabilities

> The difference between the electromagnetic self-energies of proton and neutron can be calculated with the Cottingham formula, which expresses the self-energies as an integral over the electroproduction cross sections---provided the nucleon matrix elements of the current commutator do not contain a fixed pole. We show that, under the same proviso, the subtraction function occurring in the dispersive representation of the virtual Compton forward scattering amplitude is determined by the cross sections. The representation in particular leads to a parameter-free sum rule for the nucleon polarizabilities. We evaluate the sum rule for the difference between the electric polarizabilities of proton and neutron by means of the available parameterizations of the data and compare the result with experiment.

## Key claim

Under the assumption of Reggeon dominance (i.e., that the nucleon matrix elements of the current commutator do not contain a fixed pole at angular momentum J=0), the subtraction function in the dispersive representation of the virtual Compton forward scattering amplitude is uniquely determined by the electroproduction cross sections, leading to a parameter-free sum rule for the nucleon polarizabilities.

## Method

The authors use the Cottingham formula, which relates the electromagnetic self-energies of the nucleon to an integral over electroproduction cross sections, combined with fixed-q² dispersion relations for the invariant amplitudes T₁(ν,q²) and T₂(ν,q²). They separate the amplitudes into elastic contributions (determined by the Sachs form factors G_E and G_M) and inelastic contributions, and apply the Reggeon dominance hypothesis to fix the subtraction function S₁(q²) that appears in the dispersion relation for T₁. The subtraction function is then expressed in terms of integrals over the transverse and longitudinal cross sections σ_T and σ_L, with the Regge contributions explicitly subtracted to ensure convergence. The chiral expansion of the relevant quantities is used to control the low-energy behavior.

## Key numbers

- The elastic parts of the polarizabilities are given by α_E^el = α_em κ²/(4m_N³) and β_M^el = -α_em κ²/(4m_N³), where κ is the anomalous magnetic moment and m_N the nucleon mass.
- For the proton: α_E = 10.65(0.50) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β_M = 3.15(0.50) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, α_E+β_M = 13.8(4) × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (from Baldin sum rule analysis by Olmos de Leon et al.).
- For the neutron: α_E = 11.55(1.50) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β_M = 3.65(1.50) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, α_E+β_M = 15.2(4) × 10⁻⁴ fm³ (from Baldin sum rule analysis by Levchuk et al.).
- The difference between proton and neutron: α_E(p-n) = -0.9(1.6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, β_M(p-n) = -0.5(1.6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³, (α_E+β_M)(p-n) = -1.4(6) × 10⁻⁴ fm³.
- The leading chiral expansion term for S₁^inel(q²) is of order 1/m_π and is determined by F_π = 92.21(14) MeV and g_A = 1.2723(23).

## Context

This work extends the original analysis of Gasser and Leutwyler (1975), who used the Cottingham formula with Bjorken scaling to estimate the proton-neutron mass difference. The present paper addresses the central issue of fixed poles in the current commutator, which was not treated in recent lattice-motivated reexaminations by Walker-Loud et al. (2010-2014) or by Thomas et al. (2014) and Erben et al. (2014). The derived sum rule for the magnetic polarizability is new, and the chiral expansion results for the subtraction function build on the work of Nevado and Pineda (2007). The results enable a parameter-free determination of the difference between the electric polarizabilities of proton and neutron from electroproduction data, and can be combined with the Baldin sum rule to predict the individual polarizabilities.
