# Analysis of proton and neutron pair breakings: High-spin structures of $^{124-127}$Te isotopes

> In the present work recently available experimental data for high-spin states of four nuclei, $^{124}_{\ 52}$Te, $^{125}_{\ 52}$Te, $^{126}_{\ 52}$Te, and $^{127}_{\ 52}$Te have been interpreted using state-of-the-art shell model calculations. The calculations have been performed in the $50-82$ valence shell composed of $1g_{7/2}$, $2d_{5/2}$, $1h_{11/2}$, $3s_{1/2}$, and $2d_{3/2}$ orbitals. We have compared our results with the available experimental data for excitation energies and transition probabilities, including high-spin states. The results are in reasonable agreement with the available experimental data. The wave functions, particularly, the specific proton and neutron configurations which are involved to generate the angular momentum along the yrast lines are discussed. We have also estimated overall contribution of three-body forces in the energy level shifting. Finally, results with modified effective interaction are also reported.

## Key claim

The paper claims that shell-model calculations using the SN100PN interaction in the 50-82 valence space can reasonably reproduce the recently measured high-spin states of 124-127Te, but the agreement is qualified by large r.m.s. deviations (up to ~1 MeV) that are attributed to necessary truncation of the neutron model space, and the results are improved by a modified effective interaction.

## Method

Shell-model calculations were performed in the 50-82 valence shell (1g7/2, 2d5/2, 1h11/2, 3s1/2, 2d3/2 orbitals) using the SN100PN interaction and the NuShellX code. For 124,126Te, the neutron g7/2 orbital was filled completely and a minimum of 4 particles were placed in the d5/2 orbital; for 125,127Te, the truncation was relaxed to a minimum of 2 particles in d5/2. The wave functions were decomposed into neutron (I_n) and proton (I_p) angular-momentum components to identify which nucleon pairs break to generate high spin. Transition probabilities were computed with two sets of effective charges, and the contribution of three-body forces was estimated from the occupancy of the neutron h11/2 orbital.

## Key numbers

* For 124Te, the r.m.s. deviation of calculated positive-parity states from experiment is 1056 keV; for negative-parity states it is 925 keV.
* For 125Te, the r.m.s. deviation of positive-parity states is 459 keV; for negative-parity states it is 929 keV.
* For 126Te, the r.m.s. deviation of positive-parity states is 844 keV; for negative-parity states it is 783 keV.
* For 127Te, the r.m.s. deviation of positive-parity states is 464 keV; for negative-parity states it is 576 keV.
* The calculated B(E2;2+1 -> 0+1) for 124Te is 12.27 W.u. with e_pi=1.5e, e_nu=0.5e and 25.83 W.u. with e_pi=1.5e, e_nu=1.0e; the experimental value is 31.1(5) W.u.
* The calculated B(M4;11/2-1 -> 3/2+1) for 127Te is 16.21 W.u. with free g_s and 7.95 W.u. with g_s^eff = 0.7 g_s^free; the experimental value is 3.6 W.u.
* The r.m.s. deviation for B(E2;0+1 -> 2+1) transitions across the Te chain is 0.049 e^2 b^2 with e_p=1.5e, e_n=1.0e and 0.225 e^2 b^2 with e_p=1.5e, e_n=0.5e.
* The estimated effect of three-body forces is 2.8 times larger for 126Te than for 124Te, based on the occupancy of the neutron h11/2 orbital (approximately 6 for 124Te and 8 for 126Te).

## Context

This work extends the shell-model analysis of high-spin states in Te isotopes beyond the earlier study by Astier et al. (2012), which reported experimental data for 124-131Te but only presented shell-model results for 128-131Te. The present calculations for 124-127Te show larger discrepancies (up to ~1 MeV) than previous SN100PN calculations for nuclei near the N=82 shell closure, which agreed within ~100 keV as reported by Srivastava and collaborators (2013). The results indicate that core-polarization effects beyond the 100Sn core may be needed, and the modified interaction (adding +0.200 MeV to 18 neutron-neutron two-body matrix elements involving h11/2 with d3/2 and s1/2) improves agreement for low-lying states to within ~100-200 keV.
