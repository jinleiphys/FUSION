# Three-body Unitarity in the Finite Volume

> The physical interpretation of lattice QCD simulations, performed in a small volume, requires an extrapolation to the infinite volume. A method is proposed to perform such an extrapolation for three interacting particles at energies above threshold. For this, a recently formulated relativistic $3\to 3$ amplitude based on the isobar formulation is adapted to the finite volume. The guiding principle is two- and three-body unitarity that imposes the imaginary parts of the amplitude in the infinite volume. In turn, these imaginary parts dictate the leading power-law finite-volume effects. It is demonstrated that finite-volume poles arising from the singular interaction, from the external two-body sub-amplitudes, and from the disconnected topology cancel exactly leaving only the genuine three-body eigenvalues. The corresponding quantization condition is derived for the case of three identical scalar-isoscalar particles and its numerical implementation is demonstrated.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method to extrapolate lattice QCD results for three interacting particles from a finite volume to infinite volume, using the constraints of two- and three-body unitarity to identify the leading power-law finite-volume effects. The key hedge is that the method is derived for the specific case of three identical scalar-isoscalar particles, and the authors note that the partial-wave expansion must be cut in a practical calculation, with both this cut and the energy dependence of the amplitude improvable as lattice data improve.

## Method

The authors adapt a previously formulated relativistic \(3\to 3\) scattering amplitude based on the isobar formalism to a finite cubic volume with periodic boundary conditions. The infinite-volume amplitude, which manifestly satisfies two- and three-body unitarity, is expressed in terms of on-shell two-body sub-amplitudes and real three-body forces; in the finite volume, the continuous momentum integrals are replaced by sums over discrete lattice momenta organized by shells, and the partial-wave projection is replaced by a projection onto the \(A_1^+\) irreducible representation of the cubic group. The resulting quantization condition is derived by requiring that the finite-volume \(\hat T\) matrix be singular, and the cancellation of spurious finite-volume poles from the singular interaction kernel, the two-body sub-amplitudes, and the disconnected topology is demonstrated analytically and numerically.

## Key numbers

* The finite-volume projection of the driving term \(B_{nm}^{A^+_1}(s)\) is evaluated for shells \(n \leq 8\), which introduces a momentum cutoff around \(\Lambda \sim 1\) GeV for a typical volume of \(L = 3\) fm.
* The numerical demonstration uses a lattice size of \(L = 3.0\) fm, a particle mass \(M = 138\) MeV, and parameters \(\lambda = 3476\), \(\beta = 5000\) MeV, and \(M_0 = 847\) MeV.
* The first pole in the finite-volume projection of the singular potential for shells \(m = n = 1\) occurs at the non-interacting energy where the incoming and outgoing spectator momenta are back-to-back (\(\theta = \pi\)), with subsequent poles at \(\theta = \pi/2\) and \(\theta = 0\).
* The isobar propagator in the finite volume is evaluated for a boost \(\boldsymbol{l} = (0, 0, 2\pi/L)\) at \(L = 3.5\) fm with the same parameters \(\lambda = 3476\), \(\beta = 5000\) MeV, and \(M_0 = 847\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic three-body formalism of Mai and Doring (2017) to the finite volume, building on the isobar formulation that reparametrizes the full two-body amplitude as shown by Bedaque (1999). It contrasts with the approach of Hammer (2017) by being relativistic and using three-body unitarity as the construction principle rather than effective field theory, and it addresses problems with earlier effective Hamiltonian models by Wu (2017) and Liu (2016) that approximated three-body channels via stable particles, which led to qualitatively incorrect finite-volume singularities. The method enables the analysis of lattice QCD simulations for systems such as the \(a_1(1260)\) meson and the Roper resonance \(N(1440)1/2^+\), where three-body dynamics are crucial.
