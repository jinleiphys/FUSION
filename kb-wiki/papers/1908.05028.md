# Evolution of nuclear structure in and around Z=50 closed shell: Generalized Seniority in Cd, Sn and Te isotopes

> We study the quadrupole moments and the B(E2; $2^+ \rightarrow 0^+$) values for the ${11/2}^-$ states and the first $2^+$ states, respectively, by using a multi-j generalized seniority approach in the Cd (Z = 48), Sn (Z = 50) and Te (Z = 52) isotopic chains. The g-factor trends have also been discussed. Although, Cd and Te isotopes represent two-proton hole and two-proton particle systems, thus involving both kind of particles (protons and neutrons) in contrast to Sn (Z = 50) where only neutrons play a role, we find that a similar model based on neutron valence space alone is able to explain nearly all the gross features and trends. This paper represents the first attempt to test the validity of the generalized seniority scheme away from the semi-magic region and appears to be surprisingly successful. The linearly varying quadrupole moments in Cd, Sn and Te isotopes, are described by using a consistent multi-j configuration. The asymmetric double-hump behavior of B(E2) values in Cd and Te isotopes are understood in a manner identical to that of Sn isotopes by using the generalized seniority scheme for the first time. No shell quenching is supported in the calculations; hence, the neutron magic numbers, N = 50 and N = 82, remain robust in these isotopic chains.

# Evolution of nuclear structure in and around Z=50 closed shell: Generalized Seniority in Cd, Sn and Te isotopes

## Key claim

The generalized seniority scheme, previously validated only for semi-magic nuclei, can successfully explain the quadrupole moments and B(E2) values in Cd (Z=48) and Te (Z=52) isotopes using only the neutron valence space, despite these nuclei containing both proton and neutron valence particles. The authors hedge that while the model is "surprisingly successful," the correlation coefficient between the realistic effective interaction and the simple pairing Hamiltonian is only 0.3, suggesting the realistic interactions are "far from" the simple pairing Hamiltonian.

## Method

The authors applied the multi-j generalized seniority scheme with quasi-spin operators defined as S⁺ = Σⱼ (-1)^(lⱼ) S⁺ⱼ, using effective-j configurations with pair degeneracies Ω=9, 10, and 12 corresponding to different sets of neutron orbitals in the N=50-82 valence space. They calculated quadrupole moments for 11/2⁻ states using the generalized seniority conserving formula (Δv=0) and B(E2; 2⁺→0⁺) values using the generalized seniority changing formula (Δv=2), fitting one experimental data point per isotopic chain to determine radial integral values.

## Key numbers

- The pair degeneracies used are Ω=9 for the {h₁₁/₂ ⊗ d₃/₂ ⊗ s₁/₂} configuration, Ω=10 for {g₇/₂ ⊗ d₅/₂ ⊗ d₃/₂ ⊗ s₁/₂}, and Ω=12 for {d₅/₂ ⊗ h₁₁/₂ ⊗ d₃/₂ ⊗ s₁/₂}
- The radial integral for the 11/2⁻ state in Sn isotopes was determined to be 42.04 fm², fitted from the experimental Q-moment at ¹¹⁷Sn (N=67, n=3)
- The radial integral for 11/2⁻ states in Cd isotopes was 45.78 fm² (fitted from ¹¹³Cd), and for Te isotopes was 60.05 fm² (fitted from ¹²⁹Te)
- The derived root-mean-square radii ⟨r²⟩¹/² were 6.77 fm for Cd, 6.48 fm for Sn, and 7.75 fm for Te isotopes
- The quadrupole moment changes from negative to positive with a nearly zero value at N=73 in all three isotopic chains (¹²³Sn, ¹²¹Cd, ¹²⁵Te)
- The first excited 2⁺ state energies are nearly constant from N=52 to N=80, with values in Cd and Te approximately half those in Sn isotopes
- The energy gap for 2⁺ states in Sn isotopes changes from approximately 1.2 MeV (before the middle of the shell) to approximately 1.1 MeV (after the middle), with a transitional kink at approximately 1.3 MeV

## Context

This work extends the generalized seniority scheme previously developed by Maheshwari, Jain, and collaborators (2016-2019) for semi-magic Sn isotopes to the non-semi-magic Cd and Te isotopic chains. It directly addresses the surprising linear behavior of quadrupole moments in neutron-rich Cd isotopes observed by Yordanov (2013), which had puzzled the nuclear structure community as noted by Wood (2013). The calculations support that the neutron magic numbers N=50 and N=82 remain robust with no shell quenching, and enable future application of generalized seniority to other isotopic chains away from closed shells, particularly where both protons and neutrons contribute to the valence space.
