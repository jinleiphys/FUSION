# Cooling of Neutron Stars admixed with Light Dark Matter: a case study

> Neutron Stars (NSs) are born as hot, lepton-rich objects that evolve according to the standard paradigm through subsequent stages where they radiate the excess of energy by emitting, first, neutrinos and, later on, photons. Current descriptions based on Standard Model calculations cannot fully explain all the existing cooling data series for the dozens of objects that have been reported. In this work, we consider the intriguing possibility that cooling NSs could be actually admixed with a fraction of light dark matter (LDM), $χ$. We focus on a particular case study assuming a generic light candidate with mass $m_χ=0.1$ $\rm GeV/c^2$ that undergoes self-annihilating reactions through pseudoscalar mediators producing neutrinos in the final state. We include one additional feature, allowing thermal conduction from LDM while inside the dark core. By performing simulations of the temperature evolution in the NS, we find that cooling patterns could be distorted by the presence of LDM and discuss these results in light of their observability.

## Key claim

The paper explores whether neutron star cooling data can be explained by a light dark matter (LDM) component with mass 0.1 GeV/c² that self-annihilates into neutrinos and conducts heat, finding that such a component can produce a temporary temperature plateau lasting up to about 10³ years, but the effect is model-dependent and constrained by observations of the coldest known neutron stars.

## Method

The authors solved the general relativistic equations of thermal evolution for spherically symmetric neutron stars, adding a heating term from LDM self-annihilation into neutrinos and an extra thermal conductivity term from LDM particles. They used the HDD nuclear equation of state with proton superconductivity and modeled the LDM as a thermalized Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution inside a dark core of radius ~0.8 km, with the dark emissivity parametrized as Q_h = 10^α (T/0.1 MeV)^{-3} erg cm^{-3} s^{-1} where 19 < α < 21.

## Key numbers

- LDM mass m_χ = 0.1 GeV/c² (fixed for the case study)
- Dark core radius R_χ ~ 0.8 km for m_χ = 0.1 GeV, ρ_N ~ 2ρ_0, T ~ 0.1 MeV
- Neutron star masses explored: M ∈ [1, 1.9] M_⊙
- DM capture rate C_χ ≃ 5.6 × 10^{26} s^{-1} for M = 1.5 M_⊙, R = 14 km, m_χ = 0.1 GeV, ρ_χ = 0.4 GeV/cm³
- Self-annihilation rate coefficient C_a ≃ 2 × 10^{-42} s^{-1} for m_χ = 0.1 GeV, ρ_N = 2ρ_0, T = 0.5 MeV
- Initial dark population N_χ,0 = 1.5 × 10^{39} for ρ_χ = 0.4 GeV/cm³, m_χ = 0.1 GeV, σ_s = 10^{-44} cm²
- Dark emissivity exponent α in range 19 < α < 21
- Temperature plateau duration τ_χ ~ 10³ yr for the most massive neutron stars
- For the coldest known neutron star PSR J2144-3933 (age 3 × 10⁸ yr, mass 1.4 M_⊙), the unredshifted temperature T < 42000 K constrains DM masses to the ~10 MeV range

## Context

This work extends previous studies of neutron star cooling with dark matter, including models with free and interacting Fermi gas (Schramm 1978), non-self-annihilating self-interacting matter (Tolos 2015), and milli-charged dark matter (Kouvaris 2015). It specifically builds on the authors' earlier work (Lineros 2016) on pseudoscalar-mediated dark matter annihilation. The results show that LDM self-annihilation can temporarily balance modified URCA cooling, producing a plateau that could explain some observed neutron star surface temperatures, but the coldest objects like PSR J2144-3933 constrain the scenario. The work enables future observational tests with next-generation infrared telescopes such as the James Webb Space Telescope and the Thirty Meter Telescope.
