# Nonequilibrium Dynamics of the Chiral Quark Condensate under a Strong Magnetic Field

> Strong magnetic fields impact quantum-chromodynamics (QCD) properties in several situations; examples include the early universe, magnetars, and heavy-ion collisions. These examples share a common trait: time evolution. A prominent QCD property impacted by a strong magnetic field is the quark condensate, an approximate order parameter of the QCD transition between a high-temperature quark-gluon phase and a low-temperature hadronic phase. We use the linear sigma model with quarks to address the quark condensate time evolution under a strong magnetic field. We use the closed time path formalism of nonequilibrium quantum field theory to integrate out the quarks and obtain a mean-field Langevin equation for the condensate. The Langevin equation features dissipation and noise kernels controlled by a damping coefficient. We compute the damping coefficient for magnetic field and temperature values achieved in peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions and solve the Langevin equation for a temperature quench scenario. The magnetic field changes the dissipation and noise pattern by increasing the damping coefficient compared to the zero-field case. An increased damping coefficient increases fluctuations and time scales controlling condensate's short-time evolution, a feature that can impact hadron formation at the QCD transition. The formalism developed here can be extended to include other order parameters, hydrodynamic modes, and system's expansion to address magnetic field effects in complex settings as heavy-ion collisions, the early universe, and magnetars.

## Key claim

The paper derives a mean-field Langevin equation for the time evolution of the chiral quark condensate under a strong magnetic field, finding that the magnetic field increases the damping coefficient compared to the zero-field case, which in turn increases fluctuations and the time scales controlling the condensate's short-time evolution, a feature that could impact hadron formation at the QCD transition, but only within the specific context of a temperature quench scenario and using the linear sigma model with quarks (LSMq), which does not describe quark confinement.

## Method

The authors use the closed time path (CTP) formalism of nonequilibrium quantum field theory to integrate out quarks from the linear sigma model with quarks (LSMq), obtaining a semiclassical two-particle irreducible (2PI) effective action. From this effective action, they derive a stochastic Langevin equation for the sigma mean field (the condensate), which contains dissipation and noise kernels controlled by a damping coefficient. They compute the damping coefficient using the lowest Landau level approximation for the thermomagnetic quark propagator at magnetic field and temperature values relevant to peripheral relativistic heavy-ion collisions, then solve the Langevin equation for a temperature quench scenario.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field strengths up to \( eB = 15 m^2_\pi \) are achievable in noncentral Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC (Skokov 2009).
- The pseudocritical temperature for the QCD crossover is \( T_{\rm pc} \simeq 150 \) MeV (Aoki 2006).
- In the LSMq mean field approximation at zero baryon chemical potential, a crossover occurs at \( T \simeq 150 \) MeV with Yukawa coupling \( g = 3.3 \); a second-order transition occurs at \( T \simeq 140 \) MeV with \( g = 3.63 \); and a first-order transition occurs at \( T \simeq 123 \) MeV with \( g = 5.5 \).

## Context

This work extends the semiclassical approach of Nahrgang (2011) to include magnetic field effects on the chiral condensate dynamics, which previously treated the zero-field case using the same LSMq model and 2PI effective action formalism. The study is motivated by lattice QCD calculations (D'Elia 2012, Endrodi 2014, Ding 2020) showing that strong magnetic fields impact the chiral condensate, and by the need to understand nonequilibrium dynamics in settings such as heavy-ion collisions, the early universe, and magnetars. The formalism developed here can be extended to include other order parameters, hydrodynamic modes, and system expansion to address magnetic field effects in more complex settings.
