# Investigating the pion emission source in pp collisions using the AMPT model with sub-nucleon structure

> The measurement of momentum correlations of identical pions serves as a fundamental tool for probing the space-time properties of the particle emitting source created in high-energy collisions. Recent experimental results have shown that, in pp collisions, the size of the one-dimensional primordial source depends on the transverse mass (\mt) of hadron pairs, following a common scaling behavior, similar to that observed in Pb--Pb collisions. In this work, a systematic study of the \pipi source function and correlation function is performed using the multiphase transport model (AMPT) to understand the properties of the emitting source created in high multiplicity pp collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13$ TeV. The \mt scaling behavior and pion emission source radii measured by ALICE experiment can be well described the model with sub-nucleon structure. These studies shed new light on the understanding of the effective size of the \pipi emission source and on studying the intensity interferometry in small systems with a transport model.

## Key claim

The AMPT model with sub-nucleon (constituent quark) structure can reproduce the transverse mass scaling behavior and pion emission source radii measured by ALICE in high-multiplicity pp collisions at 13 TeV, but only after the hadronic rescattering (ART) stage; the model's core source radius after ART is smaller than the experimental core radius, indicating that the resonance contribution in AMPT is insufficient to fully match the data.

## Method

The authors used the AMPT model with string melting and sub-nucleon geometry (three constituent quarks per proton) to simulate high-multiplicity pp collisions at 13 TeV. They extracted the pion-pion source function from the model by pairing charged pions in the pseudorapidity range |η| < 0.8 with transverse momentum 0.14–4.0 GeV/c, applying an equal emission time correction for particles with different freeze-out times. The correlation functions were then computed using the CATS framework, which numerically solves the Schrödinger equation for the two-particle wave function including Coulomb and quantum-statistical effects, and compared to ALICE data.

## Key numbers

- The mean relative distance ⟨r*⟩ before the ART stage for the "3 quarks" initial configuration is 1.58 fm, compared to 0.98 fm for "Normal" and 0.77 fm for "Point-like" initial distributions.
- After the ART stage, the source matches a Cauchy distribution with R_exp ≈ 2.22 fm for "3 quarks", 2.13 fm for "Normal", and 1.93 fm for "Point-like", with corresponding ⟨r*⟩ values of 4.15, 3.94, and 3.60 fm.
- In the k_T interval 0.15–0.30 GeV/c, the fitted Gaussian core radius R_π = 0.78 fm and Cauchy radius R_exp = 3.09 fm; in the interval 0.90–1.50 GeV/c, R_π = 0.63 fm and R_exp = 1.96 fm.
- The experimental core radii from ALICE (SourceMaxi, 2023) are R_core = 2.46 ± 0.028 fm for the low k_T interval and 1.13 ± 0.015 fm for the high k_T interval.
- In the AMPT "3 quarks" scenario, 46.3% of charged pions are primordial and 53.7% come from strong resonances, compared to 28.0% primordial and 72.0% from resonances in the Statistical Hadronization Model (THERMINATOR2) calculation.

## Context

This work extends the AMPT model with sub-nucleon geometry, previously shown to reproduce collective flow in pp collisions (Zhao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024), to femtoscopic observables for the first time. It builds on experimental measurements of pion source radii and m_T scaling by ALICE (SourceMaxi, 2023; ppSource, 2023) and earlier HBT analyses in pp and Pb–Pb collisions (CMS, 2020; ALICE, 2011). The results show that the correlation function is less sensitive to initial geometrical conditions than azimuthal observables like v_2, and that hadronic rescattering and resonance decays dominate the final source size. The work enables future studies of intensity interferometry in small systems using transport models, though the authors note that AMPT currently lacks several decay channels present in the Statistical Hadronization Model (THERMINATOR2) and EPOS.
