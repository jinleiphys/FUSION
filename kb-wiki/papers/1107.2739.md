# Microscopic Predictions for Cluster-Decays

> The decay dynamical path is determined within the macroscopic-microscopic model for the emission of 24Ne from 232U. The nuclear shape parametrization is characterized five degrees of freedom. The single particle energies and the nucleon wave functions are obtained within the superasymmetric Woods-Saxon two center shell model. It turns out that the cluster decay follows a potential magic valley, starting from the ground state of the parent and reaching a configuration of two touching nuclei at scission. A small pocket in the potential barrier is evidenced, as a result of large shell effects in the nascent fragments. The half-life is computed by using several approaches for the effective mass. It is shown that the inertia within by the gaussian overlap approach gives the closest values to the experimental ones. Half-lives for different cluster decays are predicted. The theoretical values are compared to various phenomenological estimates.

# Microscopic Predictions for Cluster-Decays

## Key claim

The cluster decay of \(^{24}\)Ne from \(^{232}\)U follows a "magic valley" in the potential energy surface that leads directly to the formation of \(^{208}\)Pb, and the half-life computed using the Gaussian Overlap Approximation (GOA) for the inertia agrees with experiment to within less than two orders of magnitude, whereas the cranking and diabatic cranking models give much larger discrepancies.

## Method

The decay path was determined dynamically within the macroscopic-microscopic model by minimizing the action integral in a five-dimensional configuration space (elongation, two fragment eccentricities, mass asymmetry, and necking parameter). Single-particle energies and wave functions were obtained from a superasymmetric Woods-Saxon two-center shell model, and the deformation energy was computed as the sum of a Yukawa-plus-exponential liquid-drop energy and shell-plus-pairing corrections from the Strutinsky procedure. The half-life was then calculated via the WKB approximation using three different approaches for the collective inertia: the cranking model, the Gaussian Overlap Approximation (GOA), and the diabatic cranking formula with time-dependent pairing equations (CTDPE).

## Key numbers

- For \(^{24}\)Ne emission from \(^{232}\)U, the calculated log\(_{10}(T_{1/2}[s])\) values are: 35.66 (cranking), 22.41 (GOA), and 29.48 (CTDPE); the experimental value is 21.06.
- The ground state of the parent nucleus is located at an elongation \(R = 4.6\) fm with mass asymmetry \(\eta \approx 1\); the scission configuration occurs at \(R_t \approx 10\) fm.
- The theoretical and phenomenological Coulomb barriers agree to within less than 2 MeV in the external part of the barrier.
- For \(^{28}\)Mg emission from \(^{234}\)U, the microscopic GOA half-life is log\(_{10}(T[s]) = 23.13\) (phenomenological: 24.50); for \(^{22}\)Ne emission, the microscopic GOA value is 25.84 (phenomenological: 26.70).
- The \(Q\)-value for \(^{24}\)Ne emission from \(^{232}\)U is 62.31 MeV; for \(^{28}\)Mg it is 74.32 MeV; for \(^{22}\)Ne it is 57.37 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic-microscopic fission approach previously applied to Th, U (Mirea et al., prior work) and Cf (Mirea et al., prior work) to the superasymmetric regime of cluster decay, treating it as a fission process rather than using a preformation probability as in alpha-decay models (Delion, 2010). The key finding, that the cluster decay follows a magic valley toward \(^{208}\)Pb, producing a small pocket in the barrier from shell effects of the nascent fragments, is contrasted with the double-humped fission barrier where the second minimum arises from an isomeric state of the parent nucleus. The results enable microscopic predictions for other cluster decays from \(^{234}\)U and contribute toward a unified treatment of fission and heavy-ion emission.
