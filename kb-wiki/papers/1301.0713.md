# 8Dim calculations of the third barrier in $^{232}$Th and a conflict between theory and experiment on uranium nuclei

> We find the height of the third fission barrier $B_{III}$ and energy of the third minimum $E_{III}$ in $^{232}$Th using the macroscopic - microscopic model, very well tested in this region of nuclei. For the first time it is done on an 8-dimensional deformation hypercube. The dipole distortion is included among the shape variables to assure that no important shapes are missed. The saddle point is found on a lattice containing more than 50 million points by the immersion water flow (IWF) method. The shallow third minimum, $B_{III}-E_{III}\approx 0.36$ MeV, agrees with experimetal data of Blons et al. This is in a sharp contrast with the status of the IIIrd minima in $^{232-236}$U: their experimental depth of $\geq3$ MeV contradicts all realistic theoretical predictions. We emphasize the importance of repeating the experiment on $^{232}$Th, by a technique similar to that used in the uranium nuclei, for settling the puzzle of the third minima in actinides.

## Key claim

The authors find that the third fission barrier in $^{232}$Th is shallow (depth approximately 0.36 MeV), agreeing with the old experimental data of Blons et al., but this stands in sharp contrast to the deep third minima (greater than or equal to 3 MeV) reported experimentally in uranium isotopes $^{232-236}$U, which contradict all realistic theoretical predictions.

## Method

The authors used a macroscopic-microscopic model with a deformed Woods-Saxon potential, calculating the potential energy on an 8-dimensional deformation hypercube grid containing over 50 million points. The grid included axially symmetric deformations $\beta_{10}$ through $\beta_{80}$, with the dipole distortion $\beta_{10}$ included to avoid missing important shapes. The saddle point was located using the immersion water flow (IWF) method on this giant grid.

## Key numbers

- The depth of the third minimum in $^{232}$Th, $B_{III} - E_{III} \approx 0.36$ MeV, agreeing with experimental data of Blons et al.
- The third barrier height $B_{III}^{th}$ in $^{232}$Th is 4.4 MeV, and the third minimum energy $E_{III}^{th}$ is 4.1 MeV (both relative to ground state).
- Including the dipole deformation $\beta_{10}$ lowers the third saddle by 4.1 MeV compared to calculations without it.
- The grid contained 50,803,200 total points, with deformation steps of 0.05 for each of the eight variables.
- In $^{232}$U, the experimental third minimum depth is 3.2 MeV with a third barrier of 6.0 MeV, while the theoretical calculation gives $E_{III}^{th} = 0.0$ MeV and $B_{III}^{th} = 0.0$ MeV (no third minimum or barrier).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous 6-dimensional hypercube calculations (Jachimowicz, Kowal, Skalski, 2012) and contrasts with earlier Woods-Saxon model calculations by Cwiok et al. (2005) that predicted abundant hyperdeformed minima in uranium. The results agree with self-consistent calculations using Skyrme SkM* (McDonnell et al., 2009) and Gogny D1S interactions (Bender et al., 2009) that find no deep third minima, as well as with macroscopic-microscopic calculations by Moller et al. (2012) showing minima no deeper than 300 keV in $^{236}$U. The authors emphasize that a new experiment on $^{232}$Th using highly monochromatic gamma-ray beams, similar to techniques used for uranium by the Munich-Debrecen group (Csige et al., 2012), is essential for resolving the puzzle of third minima in actinides.
