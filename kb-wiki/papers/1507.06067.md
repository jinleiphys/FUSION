# Neutrino emissivity in the quark-hadron mixed phase of neutron stars

> Numerous theoretical studies using various equation of state models have shown that quark matter may exist at the extreme densities in the cores of high-mass neutron stars. It has also been shown that a phase transition from hadronic matter to quark matter would result in an extended mixed phase region that would segregate phases by net charge to minimize the total energy of the phase, leading to the formation of a crystalline lattice. The existence of quark matter in the core of a neutron star may have significant consequences for its thermal evolution, which for thousands of years is facilitated primarily by neutrino emission. In this work we investigate the effect a crystalline quark-hadron mixed phase can have on the neutrino emissivity from the core. To this end we calculate the equation of state using the relativistic mean-field approximation to model hadronic matter and a nonlocal extension of the three-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model for quark matter. Next we determine the extent of the quark-hadron mixed phase and its crystalline structure using the Glendenning construction, allowing for the formation of spherical blob, rod, and slab rare phase geometries. Finally we calculate the neutrino emissivity due to electron-lattice interactions utilizing the formalism developed for the analogous process in neutron star crusts. We find that the contribution to the neutrino emissivity due to the presence of a crystalline quark-hadron mixed phase is substantial compared to other mechanisms at fairly low temperatures ($\lesssim 10^9$ K) and quark fractions ($\lesssim 30\%$), and that contributions due to lattice vibrations are insignificant compared to static-lattice contributions.

## Key claim

The neutrino emissivity from electron-lattice interactions in a crystalline quark-hadron mixed phase is substantial compared to other cooling mechanisms only at fairly low temperatures (≲ 10⁹ K) and quark fractions (≲ 30%), and contributions from lattice vibrations (phonons) are insignificant compared to static-lattice contributions.

## Method

The authors calculated the equation of state using the relativistic mean-field approximation for hadronic matter and a nonlocal three-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model for quark matter, employing the GM1 and NL3 parameterizations. They determined the extent and crystalline structure of the quark-hadron mixed phase using the Glendenning construction, allowing for spherical blob, rod, and slab rare phase geometries. The neutrino emissivity due to electron-lattice interactions was then calculated using the formalism developed for analogous processes in neutron star crusts, following the approach of Kaminker (1999) and extending prior work by Na (2012) to include rod and slab geometries and a nonlocal quark model.

## Key numbers

- The neutrino emissivity from electron-lattice interactions is substantial compared to other mechanisms at temperatures ≲ 10⁹ K and quark fractions ≲ 30%.
- The surface tension of the quark-hadron interface is constrained to fall below 50 MeV fm⁻², consistent with values suggested in recent literature.
- The dimensionless ion coupling parameter Γ determines lattice behavior: below Γ_melt = 175 the lattice behaves as a Coulomb liquid, above as a Coulomb crystal.
- The temperature range considered is 10⁷ K ≤ T ≤ 10¹⁰ K.
- The emissivity for spherical blobs is given by Q_blobs ≈ 3.35 × 10⁻⁶⁷ n_b T⁶ Z² L MeV s⁻¹ fm⁻³.
- The emissivity for rods and slabs is given by Q_rods,slabs ≈ 3.00 × 10⁻⁸⁸ k_e T⁸ J MeV s⁻¹ fm⁻³.
- The nuclear saturation density for the GM1 parameterization is ρ₀ = 0.153 fm⁻³, and for NL3 is ρ₀ = 0.148 fm⁻³.
- The compression modulus K is 300 MeV for GM1 and 272 MeV for NL3.
- The effective nucleon mass ratio m*/m_N is 0.78 for GM1 and 0.60 for NL3.
- The asymmetry energy a_sy is 32.5 MeV for GM1 and 37.4 MeV for NL3.

## Context

This work extends the study of neutrino emissivity in the quark-hadron mixed phase previously performed by Na (2012), who used the MIT Bag Model for quark matter and considered only spherical blob geometries. The authors replace the Bag Model with a nonlocal Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model and extend the geometric structures to include rods and slabs, following the formalism for electron-lattice interactions developed for neutron star crusts by Kaminker (1999), Haensel (1996), and Pethick (1997). The results enable more realistic modeling of neutron star thermal evolution under the minimal cooling paradigm, where the mechanism may enhance cooling of neutron stars that do not undergo direct Urca processes.
