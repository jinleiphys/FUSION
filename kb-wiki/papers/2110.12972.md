# Constraining exotic compact stars composed of bosonic and fermionic dark matter with gravitational wave events

> We investigate neutron star-black hole (NS-BH) merger candidates as a test for compact exotic objects. Using the events GW190814, GW200105 and GW200115 measured by the LIGO-Virgo collabration, which represent a broad profile of the masses in the NS mass spectrum, we demonstrate the constraining power for the parameter spaces of compact stars consisting of dark matter for future measurements. We consider three possible cases of dark matter stars: self-interacting, purely bosonic or fermionic dark matter stars, stars consisting of a mixture of interacting bosonic and fermionic matter, as well as the limiting case of selfbound stars. We find that the scale of those hypothetical objects are dominated by the one of the strong interaction. The presence of fermionic dark matter requires a dark matter particle of the GeV mass scale, while the bosonic dark matter particle mass can be arbitrarily large or small. In the limiting case of a selfbound linear equation of state, we find that the vacuum energy of those configurations has to be similar to the one of QCD.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that gravitational wave events GW190814, GW200105, and GW200115 can constrain the parameter spaces of exotic compact stars made of dark matter, but only under the working hypothesis that these objects have radii no larger than the Roche radius. The authors find that the characteristic scales necessary to explain GW190814 are close to the QCD scale, while the more compact objects GW200105 and GW200115 either allow only very restricted parameter spaces or no solutions at all.

## Method

The authors used the Roche radius as an upper bound on the radii of secondary compact objects in three neutron star-black hole merger candidates (GW190814, GW200105, GW200115) to constrain models of dark matter stars. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for three scenarios: purely self-interacting bosonic or fermionic dark matter stars, mixed boson-fermion dark matter stars with equal number densities, and self-bound stars with a linear equation of state. For each scenario, they derived constraints on dark matter particle masses, interaction strengths, and vacuum energy densities by requiring that the maximum mass and critical radius of the theoretical stars match the observed values.

## Key numbers

- For GW190814: secondary mass \(2.59^{+0.08}_{-0.09} M_\odot\), Roche radius 26 km, compactness C = 0.14
- For GW200105: secondary mass \(1.91^{+0.33}_{-0.24} M_\odot\), Roche radius 13 km, compactness C = 0.22
- For GW200115: secondary mass \(1.5^{+0.85}_{-0.29} M_\odot\), Roche radius 8.5 km, compactness C = 0.26
- For purely self-interacting fermionic dark matter stars explaining GW190814: minimum fermion mass \(m_f = 0.81\) GeV and interaction strength parameter \(f = m/y = 0.16\) GeV (corresponding to \(y = 3.6\))
- For mixed boson-fermion dark matter stars explaining GW190814: interaction strength \(y\) constrained to range \([2.5, 22.5]\)
- For self-bound stars with speed of sound \(s = 1/3\) explaining GW190814: vacuum energy density \(\varepsilon_0\) between 34 and 137 MeV fm\(^{-3}\)
- For self-bound stars with speed of sound \(s = 1\) explaining GW190814: \(\varepsilon_0\) between 39 and 370 MeV fm\(^{-3}\)
- For self-bound stars with \(s = 1\) explaining GW200115: \(\varepsilon_0\) between 576 and 1113 MeV fm\(^{-3}\)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of exotic compact objects following the detection of GW190814, which had a secondary object in the mass gap between neutron stars and black holes. Prior work by Most et al. (2020) showed rapidly rotating neutron stars could explain the signal, while Bombaci et al. (2020) showed quark stars could reach such masses. The authors use the additional events GW200105 and GW200115, reported by LIGO-Virgo in 2021, to test dark matter star models across the neutron star mass spectrum. The results enable future gravitational wave detections to further constrain or rule out dark matter star scenarios, particularly if next-generation detectors can observe post-merger signals to tighten radius constraints.
