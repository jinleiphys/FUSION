# Triaxiality and shape coexistence in Germanium isotopes

> The ground-state deformations of the Ge isotopes are investigated in the framework of Gogny-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) and Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus pairing in the BCS approximation. Five different Skyrme parametrizations are used to explore the influence of different effective masses and spin-orbit models. There is generally good agreement for binding energies and deformations (total quadrupole moment, triaxiality) with experimental data where available (i.e., in the valley of stability). All calculations agree in predicting a strong tendency for triaxial shapes in the Ge isotopes with only a few exceptions due to neutron (sub-)shell closures. The frequent occurrence of energetically very close shape isomers indicates that the underlying deformation energy landscape is very soft. The general triaxial softness of the Ge isotopes is demonstrated in the fully triaxial potential energy surface. The differences between the forces play an increasing role with increasing neutron number. This concerns particularly the influence of the spin-orbit model, which has a visible effect on the trend of binding energies towards the drip line. Different effective mass plays an important role in predicting the quadrupole and triaxial deformations. The pairing strength only weakly affects binding energies and total quadrupole deformations, but considerably influences triaxiality.

## Key claim

All six mean-field calculations (Gogny HFB and five Skyrme HF+BCS parametrizations) agree that Ge isotopes exhibit a strong tendency toward triaxial shapes and a very soft deformation energy landscape, but the detailed predictions for specific isotopes depend sensitively on the effective mass and spin-orbit model, with the higher effective mass forces (SkM*, SkP, SkT6) producing more spherical ground states near stability and the lower effective mass forces (SLy6, SkI3) and Gogny showing more consistent triaxiality.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent mean-field calculations for Ge isotopes from N=26 to N=76 using two approaches: Gogny-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) with the D1S parametrization solved in a three-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis with N0=8 shells, and Skyrme Hartree-Fock plus BCS pairing using five different Skyrme parametrizations (SkM*, SkT6, SkP, SLy6, SkI3) solved on a coordinate-space grid with no symmetry restrictions. The Skyrme calculations used a delta pairing force with strengths fitted separately for each parametrization, and the damped gradient iteration method was employed to find stationary states including isomeric minima.

## Key numbers

- Binding energy of 64Ge: Gogny gives -541.52 MeV, SLy6 gives -544.83 MeV, SkI3 gives -545.71 MeV, compared to experimental -545.88 MeV
- Binding energy of 70Ge: Gogny gives -605.94 MeV, SLy6 gives -609.36 MeV, SkI3 gives -610.06 MeV, compared to experimental -610.52 MeV
- For 64Ge, Gogny predicts ground-state deformation beta=0.25 and gamma=26.4 degrees; SLy6 predicts beta=0.24 and gamma=27.6 degrees; SkI3 predicts beta=0.26 and gamma=27.6 degrees
- For 70Ge, experimental deformation beta=0.23 and gamma=30.9 degrees; Gogny gives beta=0.24 and gamma=32.7 degrees; SLy6 gives beta=0.23 and gamma=33.9 degrees
- For 76Ge, the triaxial potential energy surface from SLy6 shows a shallow valley extending into the triaxial regime with a minimum at beta=0.16 and gamma=0.0 degrees for the ground state, with an isomer at beta=0.24 and gamma=24.2 degrees only 0.34 MeV higher
- The isoscalar effective masses used range from m*/m=1.00 (SkT6, SkP) down to m*/m=0.57 (SkI3)
- Pairing strengths vary dramatically across forces: for protons from -202.526 MeV fm^3 (SkT6) to -335.432 MeV fm^3 (SkI3)

## Context

This work extends previous theoretical studies of triaxiality in this mass region, such as the HFB calculation by Yamagami (2001) predicting 64Ge as extremely soft toward triaxial deformation with beta=0.27 and gamma=25 degrees, and the triaxial Routhian surface calculations by Ennis (1991) giving beta2=0.3 and gamma approximately 15 degrees for 64Ge. The systematic survey across the full Ge isotopic chain from proton to neutron drip line enables comparison of different mean-field models (Gogny versus Skyrme) and different Skyrme parametrizations, revealing which predictions are robust (general triaxial softness) and which depend on model choices (exact location of spherical sub-shell closures). The results motivate future calculations of collective ground-state correlations beyond the mean-field level, as done in the axial surveys by Fleckner (2004) and Bender (2005, 2006), to properly account for the shape fluctuations indicated by the very soft potential energy surfaces.
