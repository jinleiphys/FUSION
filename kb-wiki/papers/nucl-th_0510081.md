# Statistical description of nuclear break-up

> We present an overview of concepts and results obtained with statistical models in study of nuclear multifragmentation. Conceptual differences between statistical and dynamical approaches, and selection of experimental observables for identification of these processes, are outlined. New and perspective developments, like inclusion of in-medium modifications of the properties of hot primary fragments, are discussed. We list important applications of statistical multifragmentation in other fields of research.

## Key claim

Statistical models successfully describe nuclear multifragmentation when a thermalized source can be identified, but the transition from sequential compound-nucleus decay to simultaneous break-up occurs at excitation energies above a threshold of 2–4 MeV/nucleon, and the degree of equilibration must be judged by comparison with experiment.

## Method

The paper reviews the statistical multifragmentation approach, which assumes that after a dynamical stage forming an equilibrated source, the system disassembles into primary fragments at a freeze-out volume, followed by deexcitation of those hot fragments. The authors compare different statistical models (SMM, MMMC, ISMM, MMM) and contrast them with dynamical approaches (QMD, BUU, AMD, FMD), discussing how fragment partitions are determined by statistical weights including phase space and level density factors, with in-medium modifications of fragment properties incorporated via a liquid-drop description.

## Key numbers

- Multifragmentation threshold excitation energy: \(E_{th} = 2\)–4 MeV/nucleon; below this, evaporation/fission models apply, above it simultaneous break-up dominates.
- Time scale for equilibration of spectator matter: estimated around or less than 100 fm/c.
- Freeze-out density range: between 1/3 and 1/10 of normal nuclear density \(\rho_0 \approx 0.15\) fm\(^{-3}\); experimental analyses point to values of (0.1–0.4) \(\rho_0\).
- At thermal excitation energy \(E^* \approx 6\) MeV/nucleon, additional flow energy is around 0.2 MeV/nucleon in hadron-induced reactions and around 1.0 MeV/nucleon in central heavy-ion collisions around Fermi energy.
- Multifragmentation channels take 10–15% of the total cross section in high-energy hadron-nucleus reactions and about twice more in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions.
- Temperatures expected in the freeze-out volume: \(T \approx 3\)–8 MeV; densities: \(\rho \approx 0.1\)–\(0.3 \rho_0\).

## Context

This review extends the statistical multifragmentation concepts formulated in the 1980s by Randrup, Gross (MMMC), and Bondorf (SMM), and summarizes results from major experimental collaborations (ALADIN, EOS, ISIS, INDRA, NIMROD, FAZA). It contrasts the statistical approach with dynamical models (QMD, BUU, AMD, FMD) and discusses the liquid-gas phase transition in finite nuclear systems, linking to percolation models and Fisher's droplet model. The work enables applications in nuclear waste transmutation, electro-nuclear breeding, proton/ion therapy, space radiation protection, and astrophysical modeling of supernovae and neutron stars (Botvina 2004).
