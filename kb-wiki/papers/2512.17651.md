# Towards a fluid-dynamic description of an entire heavy-ion collision: from the colliding nuclei to the quark-gluon plasma phase

> The fluid-dynamical modeling of a nuclear collision at high energy usually starts shortly after the collision. A major source of uncertainty comes from the detailed modeling of the initial state. While the collision itself likely involves far-from-equilibrium dynamics, it is not excluded that a fluid theory of second order can reasonably well describe its soft features. Here we explore this possibility and discuss how the state before the collision can be described in that setup, examine the required fluid-dynamical equations of motion and study the resulting entropy production. While we do here only first steps, we outline a larger program, which could lead to a dynamical description of heavy-ion collisions where the only uncertainty lies in the thermodynamic and transport properties of quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The paper explores whether a second-order fluid-dynamic theory can describe the entire heavy-ion collision, including the pre-collision stage, without separate initial-state models, but the authors explicitly state they are only taking "first steps" and outline a larger program rather than claiming a complete solution.

## Method

The authors construct a fluid-dynamic description of two approaching nuclei using Woods-Saxon density profiles and Landau matching to obtain a single-fluid composite system. They then examine the interactionless limit by decomposing the composite system at different times, and solve the fluid-dynamic equations for a simplified isotropic, homogeneous toy model where nuclear matter undergoes contraction and expansion described by a time-dependent scale factor (Hubble-like universe). The equation of state combines lattice QCD, hadron resonance gas, and a nucleon-meson model.

## Key numbers

- Critical chemical potential at the liquid-gas phase transition: μ_crit ≈ 930 MeV, used to relate energy density to number density for nuclei at T=0
- In the interactionless limit, number density reaches its maximal value at full overlap of the two nuclei (no explicit numerical value given for the maximum)
- Final temperature after expansion increases monotonically with viscosity and is almost independent of relaxation time for small relaxation times (no explicit numerical temperatures provided in the truncated text)

## Context

This work extends the fluid-dynamic modeling approach reviewed by Paquet (2023) and Heinz (2024) by attempting to remove the need for separate initial-state models. It builds on the Israel-Stewart-Muller formalism (Israel, 1979; Muller, 1967) for second-order viscous fluid dynamics. The authors aim toward a description where the only inputs are the equation of state and transport properties of quantum chromodynamics, which would enable first-principles calculations of heavy-ion collisions without phenomenological initial-state parameterizations.
