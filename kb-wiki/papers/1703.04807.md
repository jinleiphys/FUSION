# Approximate transmission coefficients in heavy ion fusion

> In this paper we revisit the one-dimensional tunnelling problem. We consider different approximations for the transmission through the Coulomb barrier in heavy ion collisions at near-barrier energies. First, we discuss approximations of the barrier shape by functional forms where the transmission coefficient is known analytically. Then, we consider Kemble's approximation for the transmission coefficient. We show how this approximation can be extended to above-barrier energies by performing the analytical continuation of the radial coordinate to the complex plane. We investigate the validity of the different approximations considered in this paper by comparing their predictions for transmission coefficients and cross sections of three heavy ion systems with the corresponding quantum mechanical results.

## Key claim

The paper shows that Kemble’s approximation for the transmission coefficient, when extended to above-barrier energies via analytical continuation of the radial coordinate to the complex plane, becomes exact for both parabolic and Morse barriers, but the accuracy of the resulting fusion cross sections depends strongly on how well the chosen analytic barrier shape (parabola or Morse function) fits the actual Coulomb barrier, especially for light systems.

## Method

The authors approximate the Coulomb-plus-centrifugal barrier for three heavy-ion systems (⁴He + ¹⁶O, ¹²C + ¹⁶O, ¹⁶O + ²⁰⁸Pb) by two analytic forms: a symmetric parabola (Hill-Wheeler) and an asymmetric Morse function. They then compute transmission coefficients using Kemble’s formula (T = 1/[1+exp(2Φ)]) where Φ is the WKB integral between turning points; for above-barrier energies, the turning points are found on the complex plane. The approximate transmission coefficients and the resulting fusion cross sections (via partial-wave sum) are compared to exact quantum-mechanical results obtained with the Ingoing Wave Boundary Condition (IWBC) method.

## Key numbers

* For the ⁴He + ¹⁶O system, the Coulomb barrier height V_B = 2.90 MeV, barrier radius R_B = 7.42 fm, parabolic curvature ħω = 2.94 MeV, and Morse parameter a_M = 2.96 fm.
* For the ¹²C + ¹⁶O system, V_B = 7.99 MeV, R_B = 8.02 fm, ħω = 2.95 MeV, and a_M = 3.13 fm.
* For the ¹⁶O + ²⁰⁸Pb system, V_B = 76.55 MeV, R_B = 11.59 fm, ħω = 4.51 MeV, and a_M = 4.32 fm.
* For the ⁴He + ¹⁶O system at 0.5 MeV, the term exp(-4πα) equals 3×10⁻⁵, justifying its neglect in the Morse transmission formula.

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Toubiana, Canto, and Hussein (2017) on Kemble’s approximation for heavy-ion potentials, and it revisits the classic parabolic-barrier approximation of Hill and Wheeler (1953) and Wong (1973). The paper shows that while Kemble’s formula is exact for both parabolic and Morse barriers at all energies, the practical utility of these approximations is limited for light systems (⁴He + ¹⁶O, ¹²C + ¹⁶O) because the analytic fits to the Coulomb barrier are poor at large radii; the approximations are reasonable only for heavier systems like ¹⁶O + ²⁰⁸Pb. This enables future work to focus on improving barrier-shape approximations for light-ion fusion.
