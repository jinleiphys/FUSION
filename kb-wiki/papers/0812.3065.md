# Nuclear Lattice Simulations with Chiral Effective Field Theory

> We present recent results on lattice simulations using chiral effective field theory. In particular we discuss lattice simulations for dilute neutron matter at next-to-leading order and three-body forces in light nuclei at next-to-next-to-leading order.

## Key claim
Lattice simulations using chiral effective field theory can reproduce nuclear observables, but the leading-order action LO₂ introduced large attractive P-wave interactions in pure neutron matter that required cancellation at next-to-leading order, making NLO corrections large for Fermi momenta above 100 MeV. A new leading-order action LO₃ resolves this issue by using spin- and isospin-projected Gaussian-smeared contact interactions.

## Method
The authors performed lattice simulations of dilute neutron matter using auxiliary-field Monte Carlo with the LO₃ action and perturbative NLO corrections, on spatial lattices with spacing a = (100 MeV)⁻¹ and temporal spacing a_t = (70 MeV)⁻¹. For three-body forces at NNLO, they determined two unknown low-energy coefficients by fitting to the triton binding energy and spin-doublet nucleon-deuteron scattering phase shifts via Lüscher's finite volume formula, using the LO₂ action with a = (100 MeV)⁻¹ and a_t = (150 MeV)⁻¹.

## Key numbers
- Spatial lattice spacing a = (100 MeV)⁻¹ and temporal lattice spacing a_t = (70 MeV)⁻¹ used for neutron matter simulations with 8, 12, 16 neutrons in periodic boxes of lengths L = 4, 5, 6, 7.
- Physical constants used: nucleon mass m = 938.92 MeV, pion mass m_π = 138.08 MeV, pion decay constant f_π = 93 MeV, nucleon axial charge g_A = 1.26.
- NNLO results for the alpha particle ground state (without Coulomb) are within 5% of the Coulomb-subtracted alpha binding energy of about 29 MeV.
- The largest source of systematic error in earlier LO₂ neutron matter calculations was the large size of NLO corrections for Fermi momenta larger than 100 MeV.

## Context
This work extends lattice effective field theory calculations from leading-order studies of light nuclei (Borasoy 2006) and NLO neutron matter (Borasoy 2007) to a new LO₃ action that reduces systematic errors in pure neutron systems. The results for neutron matter ground state energies are compared with several prior calculations: Friedman and Pandharipande (1981), Akmal, Pandharipande, and Ravenhall (1998), Carlson et al. (2003), Schwenk and Pethick (2005), Gezerlis and Carlson (2007), and Gandolfi et al. (2008). The NNLO three-body force determination enables future lattice calculations of light nuclei with controlled chiral effective field theory uncertainties.
