# Observational appearance of rapidly rotating neutron stars: X-ray bursts, cooling tail method, and radius determination

> Neutron stars (NSs) in low-mass X-ray binaries rotate at frequencies high enough to significantly deviate from sphericity ($ν_* \sim$ 200--600 Hz). We investigate the effects of rapid rotation on the observational appearance of a NS. We propose analytical formulae relating gravitational mass and equatorial radius of the rapidly rotating NS to the mass $M$ and radius $R$ of a non-rotating NS of the same baryonic mass using accurate fully relativistic computations. We compute spectra from an oblate rotating NS observed at different inclination angles using the modified oblate Schwarzschild (MOS) approximation, where light bending is computed in Schwarzschild metric, but frame dragging and quadrupole moment of a NS are approximately accounted for in the photon redshift calculations. We generalize the cooling tail method to the case of a rapidly rotating NS to obtain the most probable values of $M$ and $R$ of the corresponding non-rotating NS with the same baryonic mass. We approximate the local spectra from the NS surface by a diluted blackbody using previously computed NS atmosphere models. We show that the NS radius could be overestimated by 3--3.5 km for face-on stars of $R\approx 11$ km rotating at $ν_* =$ 700 Hz if the version of the cooling tail method for a non-rotating NS is used. We apply the method to an X-ray burst observed from the NS rotating at $ν_* \approx$ 532 Hz in SAX J1810.8$-$2609. The resulting radius of the non-rotating NS (assuming $M=1.5 M_\odot$) becomes $11.8\pm0.5$ km if it is viewed at inclination i=60 deg and $R=11.2\pm0.5$ km for a face-on view, which are smaller by 0.6 and 1.2 km than the radius obtained using standard cooling tail method ignoring rotation. The corresponding equatorial radii of these rapidly rotating NSs are 12.3$\pm 0.6$ km (for i=60 deg) and 11.6$\pm 0.6$\,km (for i=0 deg).

# Observational appearance of rapidly rotating neutron stars: X-ray bursts, cooling tail method, and radius determination

## Key claim

Rapid rotation of neutron stars (NSs) in low-mass X-ray binaries systematically biases radius measurements from the cooling tail method; if rotation is ignored, the NS radius can be overestimated by 3–3.5 km for face-on stars of R ≈ 11 km rotating at ν* = 700 Hz, but the correction depends on both the rotation frequency and the unknown inclination angle, so the true radius of the corresponding non-rotating NS is smaller than previously inferred.

## Method

The authors constructed families of rotating NS models with the same baryonic mass as a non-rotating reference NS, using analytical fits to fully relativistic numerical models from Cook, Shapiro, and Teukolsky (1994) that relate equatorial radius and gravitational mass to rotation frequency. They computed observed spectra from oblate rotating NSs at various inclination angles using the modified oblate Schwarzschild (MOS) approximation, where light bending is computed in Schwarzschild metric while frame dragging and quadrupole moment are approximately included in photon redshift calculations. Local surface emission was modeled as a diluted blackbody using previously computed NS atmosphere models from Suleimanov, Poutanen, and Werner (2012) and Suleimanov et al. (2017), with luminosity-dependent dilution and colour-correction factors. The direct cooling tail method was generalized to rapidly rotating NSs and applied to an X-ray burst from SAX J1810.8−2609.

## Key numbers

- For a non-rotating NS with M = 1.5 M⊙ and R ≈ 11 km rotating at ν* = 700 Hz, the radius could be overestimated by 3–3.5 km if the standard cooling tail method for a non-rotating NS is used (face-on view).
- For SAX J1810.8−2609 (ν* ≈ 532 Hz), the radius of the non-rotating NS (assuming M = 1.5 M⊙) is 11.8 ± 0.5 km at inclination i = 60 deg and 11.2 ± 0.5 km for face-on view (i = 0 deg).
- These radii are smaller by 0.6 km (i = 60 deg) and 1.2 km (i = 0 deg) than the radius obtained using the standard cooling tail method ignoring rotation.
- The corresponding equatorial radii of the rapidly rotating NSs are 12.3 ± 0.6 km (i = 60 deg) and 11.6 ± 0.6 km (i = 0 deg).
- The analytical formula for equatorial radius ratio Re/R uses a critical frequency νcr = 1278 M1.4^(1/2) (10 km / R)^(3/2) Hz, where M1.4 = M / 1.4 M⊙.
- The fitting formula for Re/R has maximum errors not larger than 5% for the most massive and rapidly rotating NSs.
- The fitting formula for the gravitational mass ratio M'/M has accuracy typically better than 1%.

## Context

This work extends the direct cooling tail method developed by Suleimanov et al. (2017) and the earlier cooling tail method of Suleimanov, Poutanen, and Werner (2011) and Poutanen et al. (2014), which assumed spherical non-rotating NSs. It addresses the systematic effect of rapid rotation (ν* ∼ 200–600 Hz) discovered by RXTE through burst oscillations and accreting millisecond pulsars, building on approximate rotating NS models by Morsink et al. (2007) and AlGendy and Morsink (2014). The results enable more accurate NS radius constraints for rapidly rotating sources, which can be combined with equation-of-state constraints from Nättilä et al. (2016) and Lattimer and Prakash (2016), and the method can be applied to other X-ray bursters with known spin frequencies and inclination angles.
