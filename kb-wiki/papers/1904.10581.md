# Quantifying Correlated Truncation Errors in Effective Field Theory

> Effective field theories (EFTs) organize the description of complex systems into an infinite sequence of decreasing importance. Predictions are made with a finite number of terms, which induces a truncation error that is often left unquantified. We formalize the notion of EFT convergence and propose a Bayesian truncation error model for predictions that are correlated across the independent variables, e.g., energy or scattering angle. Central to our approach are Gaussian processes that encode both the naturalness and correlation structure of EFT coefficients. Our use of Gaussian processes permits efficient and accurate assessment of credible intervals, allows EFT fits to easily include correlated theory errors, and provides analytic posteriors for physical EFT-related quantities such as the expansion parameter. We demonstrate that model-checking diagnostics---applied to the case of multiple curves---are powerful tools for EFT validation. As an example, we assess a set of nucleon-nucleon scattering observables in chiral EFT. In an effort to be self contained, appendices include thorough derivations of our statistical results. Our methods are packaged in Python code, called gsum, that is available for download on GitHub.

## Key claim

The paper formalizes EFT truncation error as a Bayesian model using Gaussian processes to capture correlations across independent variables (e.g., energy or scattering angle), moving beyond pointwise uncertainty estimates that ignore curvewise structure; the model assumes that the dimensionless coefficients \(c_n\) are independent and identically distributed draws from a single underlying GP, which must be verified in each application rather than proven generally.

## Method

The authors model EFT predictions as \(\genobs_k(\kinparvec) = \genobsref(\kinparvec) \sum_{n=0}^k c_n(\kinparvec) Q^n(\kinparvec)\), where the dimensionless coefficients \(c_n\) are treated as independent draws from a Gaussian process with constant mean \(\mu\), marginal variance \(\sdth^2\), and a correlation kernel (e.g., squared exponential) with length scale \(\ell\). The truncation error \(\delta\genobs_k\) is then the sum of all uncomputed terms \(\genobsref(\kinparvec) \sum_{n=k+1}^\infty c_n(\kinparvec) Q^n(\kinparvec)\), with the GP parameters calibrated against known order-by-order predictions using conjugate priors or Markov chain Monte Carlo. The model is demonstrated on toy EFT predictions and nucleon-nucleon scattering observables in chiral EFT.

## Key numbers

* Expansion parameter \(Q\) is assumed to suppress the \(\NkLO{n}\) correction \(\Delta\genobs_n\) by \(Q^n\) (with other prescriptions like \(Q^{2n}\) easily incorporated).
* The GP marginal variance \(\sdth^2\) controls the width of variation about the mean; the length scale \(\ell\) controls correlation between points in the input domain.
* For regression with noise, the replacement \(K_{11} \to K_{11} + \sdnugget^2\identity_{N_1}\) is used, with \(\sdnugget = 0.15\) shown in an example (Fig. 4).
* The squared exponential kernel is defined as \(\kernel(\kinparvec, \kinparvec';\sdth, L) = \sdth^2 e^{-\frac{1}{2}(\kinparvec-\kinparvec')^\trans L^{-1}(\kinparvec-\kinparvec')}\), reducing to \(\sdth^2 e^{-(\kinparvec-\kinparvec')^\trans (\kinparvec-\kinparvec') / 2\ell}\) for one dimension.

## Context

This work extends the pointwise Bayesian truncation error model of Furnstahl (2015) and Melendez (2017), which computed posteriors for each \(\kinparvec\) independently but ignored correlations across the independent variable. The GP approach enables efficient assessment of credible intervals, inclusion of correlated theory errors in EFT fits, and analytic posteriors for quantities like the expansion parameter. The methods are packaged in Python code called gsum, available on GitHub, and enable model-checking diagnostics for EFT validation, demonstrated on nucleon-nucleon scattering observables in chiral EFT.
