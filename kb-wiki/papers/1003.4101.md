# Microscopic Description of Nuclear Fission Dynamics

> We discuss possible avenues to study fission dynamics starting from a time-dependent mean-field approach. Previous attempts to study fission dynamics using the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory are analyzed. We argue that different initial conditions may be needed to describe fission dynamics depending on the specifics of the fission phenomenon and propose various approaches towards this goal. In particular, we provide preliminary calculations for studying fission following a heavy-ion reaction using TDHF with a density contraint. Regarding prompt muon-induced fission, we also suggest a new approach for combining the time-evolution of the muonic wave function with a microscopic treatment of fission dynamics via TDHF.

# Microscopic Description of Nuclear Fission Dynamics

## Key claim
The authors argue that a time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) approach can describe fission dynamics, but only if the proper initial conditions are chosen for each specific fission type; the most straightforward application is fission following a heavy-ion reaction, while neutron-induced fission requires construction of excited initial states that are not yet fully developed.

## Method
The authors perform fully three-dimensional TDHF calculations with no symmetry assumptions and modern Skyrme forces for the systems ²⁴⁰Pu and ²³⁸U, using collective boost operators to attempt to induce fission. For fission following heavy-ion reactions, they use the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method on collisions of ¹⁰⁰Zr + ¹⁴⁰Xe to extract ion-ion potentials and identify transition states, then apply collective boosts to induce fission from those states. For muon-induced fission, they describe a framework combining time-evolution of the muonic wave function via the Dirac equation with TDHF nuclear dynamics.

## Key numbers
- For ²⁴⁰Pu, a new shape isomer was found with β₂ = 2.27, Q₂₀ = 230 b, and Q₃₀ = −28 b³/².
- Using a collective boost with p = 0.0025, the transition state nucleus fissions with an initial excitation energy of 7 MeV.
- The resulting fission fragment masses from the transition state are A₁ = 106, Z₁ = 42 and A₂ = 134, Z₂ = 52.
- For ¹⁰⁰Zr + ¹⁴⁰Xe collisions, at the highest bombarding energy the system shows complete fusion with no collective motion for 2500 fm/c.
- For prompt muon-induced fission in ²³⁷Np, the extracted dissipated energy is 0–10 MeV, corresponding to a fission time delay from saddle to scission of 2 × 10⁻²¹ s.
- The viscosity coefficient μ = 0.015 Tera Poise was extracted from earlier work by Davies et al. (1976) comparing theoretical and experimental fission fragment kinetic energies.

## Context
This work extends earlier TDHF fission studies by Negele and Koonin (1978) on ²³⁶U, which used axial symmetry and simplified Skyrme forces with time-dependent pairing, and by Orland and Schaeffer (1983), who used single-center or two-center trial functions with collective boosts. The authors also reference the path-integral method of Negele (1982) and the adiabatic TDHF approach of Goeke, Reinhard, and Reinhardt (1983). The DC-TDHF method was previously developed for fusion barriers by Umar and Oberacker (2006–2009). The muon-induced fission clock approach builds on experimental work by Rafelski, Bock, and collaborators (1991) and theoretical Dirac equation calculations by Oberacker and Umar (1993, 1999). The work enables future microscopic studies of energy dissipation in fission and tests of one-body versus two-body friction mechanisms.
