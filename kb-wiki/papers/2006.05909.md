# Thermal properties of $π$ and $ρ$ meson

> We computed the pole masses and decay constants of $π$ and $ρ$ meson at finite temperature in the framework of Dyson-Schwinger equations and Bethe-Salpeter equations approach. Below transition temperature, pion pole mass increases monotonously, while $ρ$ meson seems to be temperature independent. Above transition temperature, pion mass approaches the free field limit, whereas $ρ$ meson is about twice as large as that limit. Pion and the longitudinal projection of $ρ$ meson decay constants have similar behaviour as the order parameter of chiral symmetry, whereas the transverse projection of $ρ$ meson decay constant rises monotonously as temperature increases. The inflection point of decay constant and the chiral susceptibility get the same phase transition temperature. Though there is no access to the thermal width of mesons within this scheme, it is discussed by analyzing the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner (GMOR) relation in medium. These thermal properties of hadron observables will help us understand the QCD phases at finite temperature and can be employed to improve the experimental data analysis and heavy ion collision simulations.

# Thermal properties of π and ρ meson

## Key claim
The pole masses and decay constants of π and ρ meson are computed at finite temperature using Dyson-Schwinger equations and Bethe-Salpeter equations in the rainbow-ladder truncation, but the method cannot directly access the thermal width of mesons because it is related to the imaginary part of Bethe-Salpeter amplitudes, which cannot be obtained in the imaginary time formalism; instead, the thermal width is discussed indirectly by analyzing the deviation of the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner (GMOR) relation in medium.

## Method
The quark propagator at finite temperature is solved via the Dyson-Schwinger equation in the imaginary time formalism with Matsubara frequencies, using a quark-gluon interaction kernel that reproduces hadronic static properties at zero temperature and is extended to finite temperature by including a thermal gluon mass from perturbative QCD. The homogeneous Bethe-Salpeter equation is solved for π and ρ mesons in the rainbow-ladder truncation, and the pole masses are extracted by computing eigenvalues at Matsubara frequencies and extrapolating to the on-shell condition λ(P² = -M_pole²) = 1 using the Schlessinger Points Method. Decay constants are obtained by extrapolating from Matsubara frequencies to the pole mass location.

## Key numbers
- π meson vacuum mass: m_π = 138 MeV; vacuum decay constant: f_π = 97 MeV
- ρ meson vacuum mass: m_ρ = 760 MeV; vacuum decay constants: f_ρ = 153 MeV, f^T_ρ = 110 MeV
- Transition temperature from inflection point of pion decay constant: T^{f_π}_c = 146 MeV; from ρ meson decay constant: T^{f_ρ}_c = 149 MeV; from quark condensate: T^{⟨q̄q⟩}_c = 150 MeV; average estimate: T_c = (148 ± 2) MeV
- Above transition temperature, π pole mass approaches the free field limit ~2πT, while ρ pole mass is about twice as large as that limit
- Below T_s = 0.12 GeV, ρ meson pole mass decreases only slightly around 2% from its vacuum value
- At T ~ 0.3 GeV, both π pole mass and screening mass remain around 12% smaller than the free field limit
- GMOR relation deviation in vacuum is less than 4% with the two dominant Lorentz structures of the pion Bethe-Salpeter amplitude

## Context
This work extends the DSE-BSE approach to finite temperature for light meson pole masses, complementing lattice QCD screening mass results from Bazavov (2019) and earlier DSE studies by Maris (2000) and Wang (2013). The computed pole masses differ from screening masses at low temperature but agree at high temperature, and the decay constants serve as order parameters for chiral symmetry restoration. The results enable improved experimental data analysis and heavy ion collision simulations, and the deviation of the GMOR relation near the critical temperature signals the strongly-coupled nature of QCD matter in the phase transition region, where pion thermal width is generated via Landau damping.
