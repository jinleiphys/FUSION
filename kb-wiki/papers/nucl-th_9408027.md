# Nearby Doorways, Parity Doublets and Parity Mixing in Compound Nuclear States

> We discuss the implications of a doorway state model for parity mixing in compound nuclear states. We argue that in order to explain the tendency of parity violating asymmetries measured in $^{233}$Th to have a common sign, doorways that contribute to parity mixing must be found in the same energy neighbourhood of the measured resonance. The mechanism of parity mixing in this case of nearby doorways is closely related to the intermediate structure observed in nuclear reactions in which compound states are excited. We note that in the region of interest ($^{233}$Th) nuclei exhibit octupole deformations which leads to the existence of nearby parity doublets. These parity doublets are then used as doorways in a model for parity mixing. The contribution of such mechanism is estimated in a simple model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the common sign of parity-violating asymmetries measured in $^{233}$Th can be explained if the doorways responsible for parity mixing are located in the same energy neighborhood as the measured compound resonances, rather than being distant (about 7 MeV away) as assumed in earlier models. The authors propose that nearby parity doublets arising from octupole deformations in $^{233}$Th serve as these doorways, resolving a discrepancy of nearly three orders of magnitude between the parity-nonconserving matrix element required to explain the average asymmetry and the value consistent with existing models of the parity-nonconserving interaction.

## Method

The authors apply the doorway state approximation to the expression for the longitudinal asymmetry P(E_r) measured in epithermal neutron scattering on $^{233}$Th, comparing the cases of distant doorways (the giant spin-dipole resonance at about 7 MeV) and nearby doorways (parity doublet states in the third minimum of the fission potential). They estimate the parity-nonconserving matrix elements required to reproduce the experimental average asymmetry and spreading width using both scenarios, and then develop a model where the parity doublet states $\vert f^{\pm}\rangle$ serve as doorways, with the compound state $\vert r\rangle$ containing a significant component of the $\vert f^{-}\rangle$ state.

## Key numbers

- The PNC spreading width in $^{233}$Th: $\Gamma_{r}^{\downarrow \text{PNC}} \simeq 7.4 \times 10^{-7}$ eV
- The average asymmetry in $^{233}$Th: $\overline{P} \simeq (8 \pm 6)\%$
- For the distant doorway (spin-dipole resonance at about 7 MeV above the compound resonances, with $\Gamma_{\text{SD}}^{\downarrow}=3$ MeV), the PNC matrix element required to explain $\overline{P}=8\%$ is $\langle r\vert V^{\text{PNC}}\vert \text{SD}\rangle = 1700$ eV, while the spreading width gives $\langle r\vert V^{\text{PNC}}\vert \text{SD}\rangle = 3$ eV
- For a nearby doorway with $\Gamma_{d} \simeq 100$ eV and $\vert E_{r}-E_{d}\vert = \Gamma_{d}/2$, the PNC matrix element from the asymmetry is $\langle r\vert V^{\text{PNC}}\vert d\rangle = 4 \times 10^{-3}$ eV and from the spreading width is $6 \times 10^{-3}$ eV
- For the parity doublet model with $\Gamma_{f^{+}} \simeq 200$ eV, the PNC matrix element between the doublet states is $\vert\langle f^{+}\vert V^{\text{PNC}}\vert f^{-}\rangle\vert \simeq 10^{-2}$ eV
- The ratio of escape amplitudes from penetrability effects: $\gamma_{d}/\gamma_{r} \simeq 10^{3}$
- The number of particle-hole excitations in the collective spin-dipole in $^{232}$Th: $N \sim 100$
- Octupole deformations in the third minimum: $\beta_{2} \simeq 0.90$, $\beta_{3} \simeq 0.35$
- Intermediate structure parameters in neutron-induced fission: $\Gamma_{d}=200$ eV, spacing $D_{d}=700$ eV

## Context

This work extends the doorway state approach to parity violation in compound nuclei, previously applied by Auerbach (1992) using the spin-dipole resonance and by Bowman et al. (1992) using single-particle states, both of which involved distant doorways and could not simultaneously explain the average asymmetry and spreading width. The paper connects to the observed intermediate structure in neutron-induced fission reactions, as described by Bjornholm and Lynn (1980), and to the known octupole deformations and parity doublets in actinide nuclei reviewed by Ahmad and Butler (1993) and Aberg et al. (1990). The model enables a consistent explanation of the non-fluctuating sign of parity-violating asymmetries in $^{233}$Th and suggests a connection between parity violation in neutron scattering and parity violation in fission reactions studied by Petrov et al. (1989, 1994) and Sushkov and Flambaum (1980).
