# Charged pions tagged with polarized photons probing strong CP violation in a chiral-imbalance medium

> It is expected that in a hot QCD system, a local parity-odd domain can be produced due to nonzero chirality, which is induced from the difference of winding numbers carried by the gluon topological configuration (QCD sphaleron). This local domain is called the chiral-imbalance medium characterized by nonzero chiral chemical potential, which can be interpreted as the time variation of the strong CP phase. We find that the chiral chemical potential generates the parity breaking term in the electromagnetic form factor of charged pions. Heavy ion collision experiments could observe the phenomenological consequence of this parity-odd form factor through the elastic scattering of a pion and a photon in the medium. Then we quantify the asymmetry rate of the parity violation by measuring the polarization of the photon associated with the pion, and discuss how it could be measured in a definite lab frame. We roughly estimate the typical size of the asymmetry, just by picking up the pion resonant process, and find that the signal can be sufficiently larger than possible background events from parity-breaking electroweak process. Our findings might provide a novel possibility to make a manifest detection for the remnant of the strong CP violation.

## Key claim

The authors propose that a nonzero chiral chemical potential (mu_5), which mimics a time-varying strong CP phase in a hot QCD medium, generates a parity-breaking term in the electromagnetic form factor of charged pions. They claim that the resulting asymmetry in polarized-photon emission from pion-photon scattering could be large enough to be detectable above electroweak backgrounds, but note that the measurement would be "very challenging" experimentally due to complex background contamination in heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors employ a chiral Lagrangian based on the coset space G/H = [U(2)_L x U(2)_R]/U(2)_V including the Wess-Zumino-Witten term, introducing the chiral chemical potential mu_5 as the time component of an external axialvector gauge field. They extract the parity-odd electromagnetic form factor F_4 of the charged pion from this Lagrangian and compute the asymmetry in pi-gamma elastic scattering where the final-state photon is circularly polarized. The asymmetry is estimated by focusing only on the s-channel pion-exchange resonant process, expanding in powers of the loop suppression factor mu_5/(4 pi f_pi).

## Key numbers

* The pion decay constant f_pi is taken as approximately 92 MeV.
* The chiral chemical potential mu_5 is expected to be on the order of the QCD scale, O(100 MeV).
* The maximum asymmetry in the s-channel estimate is approximately 0.2 x (mu_5 / 200 MeV) x (E_pi / 1 GeV), where E_pi is the charged pion energy.
* The electroweak background from W boson exchange is suppressed relative to the signal by a factor of O(G_F^2 f_pi^4) = O(10^{-14}), where G_F is the Fermi constant approximately (290 GeV)^{-2}.
* The loop suppression factor (mu_5/(4 pi f_pi)) is estimated to be O(10^{-1}).

## Context

This work extends the study of parity-violating effects in a chiral-imbalance medium characterized by nonzero chiral chemical potential, previously addressed by Andrianov et al. (2012) and others, to a new observable: polarized photons tagged with charged pions. The proposed asymmetry measurement differs from earlier proposals that looked at resonance-mass distributions in the center-of-mass frame (such as dilepton mass distributions by Andrianov et al. 2012). The paper also notes connections to the chiral magnetic effect studied by Kharzeev et al. (2007-2008) and Fukushima (2008), where the F_4 form factor would generate a B dot j term in a magnetic field background that could contaminate charge separation measurements. The work enables a potential new experimental probe of strong CP violation in heavy-ion collisions, though the authors acknowledge that precise estimates including thermal effects and experimental backgrounds are needed.
