# Noncovariant parabolic theories of relativistic diffusion

> A new first-order theory of relativistic dissipation has been recently proposed, where viscous effects are incorporated using the traditional Navier-Stokes framework. Its main novelty is the avoidance of dynamical instabilities by allowing different observers to use equations that are not related by exact Lorentz transformations. In this work, we explore the implications of this non-covariance in depth. In particular, we discuss how predictions differ between observers moving at nearly luminal speeds relative to each other. We find that all disagreements stem from the relativity of simultaneity, which introduces frame-dependent anisotropic delays in the diffusive process. These anisotropies significantly limit the applicability of the equation used by observers who move very fast relative to the medium. However, the magnitude of the related error remains finite at infinite Lorentz factors, meaning that it is possible to find a regime where all observers agree on the outcome of experiments.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the recently proposed non-covariant parabolic theory of relativistic diffusion, which avoids dynamical instabilities by allowing different observers to use equations not related by exact Lorentz transformations, produces predictions that disagree with standard diffusion and higher-order theories by an amount that scales as the square root of the discrepancy between standard diffusion and microscopic theory, but this disagreement remains finite even at infinite Lorentz factors.

## Method

The author compares the predictions of four models for relativistic diffusion: the standard parabolic diffusion equation (Alice, at rest relative to the medium), the non-covariant boosted parabolic equation (Bob, moving at velocity -v relative to the medium), Cattaneo's hyperbolic theory (with α=1), and SuperBurnett third-order hydrodynamics (with β=1). The comparison is performed through three tests: (1) expansion of a point-like drop of charge from a delta-function source, (2) Fourier-space analysis of dispersion relations and truncation errors, and (3) expansion of a finite-size drop of charge injected slowly via a smooth bump function source. Discrepancy functions defined as the integrated absolute difference between density profiles quantify the agreement between models.

## Context

This work extends the recent proposal by Armas (2020), Basar (2024), and Bhambure (2024) for a non-covariant first-order theory of relativistic dissipation that avoids the instabilities identified by Hiscock and Lindblom in first-order relativistic hydrodynamics. The standard resolution to these instabilities has been to render equations hyperbolic, following Cattaneo (1958) and the Israel-Stewart framework (1979). The paper demonstrates that while the non-covariant approach avoids divergences at high Lorentz factors, its predictions converge to standard theories much more slowly (as √(D/t) versus D/t), limiting its practical applicability except at very late times or for observers moving slowly relative to the medium.
