# The potential for $α$ induced nuclear scattering, reaction and decay, and a resonance-pole-decay model with exact explicit analytical solutions

> The decay of $α$ particle from a nucleus is viewed as a quantum resonance state of a two-body scattering process of the $α$+daughter nucleus pair governed by a novel nucleus-nucleus potential in squared Woods-Saxon form. By the application of the rigorous optical model (OM) potential scattering (S-matrix) theory the genuineness of the potential for the system is established by giving good explanation of the elastic scattering and reaction cross sections data of the $α$+nucleus pair. From the pole position in the complex momentum (k) plane of the S-matrix defined above, the energy and width of the resonance state akin to the decaying state of emission of $α$ particle are extracted and from this width, the result of $α$-decay half-life is derived to account for the experimental result of half-life in the cases of large number of $α$-emitters including heavy and super-heavy nuclei. The S-matrix of the full OM calculation above is replaced by an analytical function expressed in terms of exact Schrödinger solutions of a global potential that closely represents the Coulomb-nuclear interaction in the interior and the pure Coulomb wave functions outside, and the resonant poles of this S-matrix in the complex momentum plane are used to give satisfactory results of decay half-lives of $α$ coming out from varieties of nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a single nuclear potential in squared Woods-Saxon form can simultaneously describe elastic scattering, reaction cross sections, and alpha-decay half-lives for alpha+daughter nucleus systems, but the potential parameters for the volume part (r_v and delta) must be marginally varied around 0.66 fm and 3 respectively to generate resonance energy exactly at the Q-value for each specific decay.

## Method

The authors apply optical model potential scattering theory using a complex nuclear potential in squared Woods-Saxon form (real part with five parameters, imaginary part with three parameters) plus a Coulomb potential based on homogeneous charge distribution. For each alpha+daughter system, they solve the radial Schrodinger equation numerically, match the wave function to Coulomb wave functions at the matching radius, and extract the S-matrix. They locate resonance poles of the S-matrix in the complex momentum plane using Newton-Raphson iteration, then derive the resonance energy (Q-value) and width, from which the alpha-decay half-life is obtained. Separately, they replace the full optical model S-matrix with an analytical S-matrix derived from exact Schrodinger solutions of a global Eckart-type potential that approximates the Coulomb-nuclear interaction in the interior region.

## Key numbers

- For the alpha+Pb-208 system, the s-wave barrier height is V_B = 20.27 MeV positioned at R_B = 10.75 fm
- For Po-212 decay (Q-value = 8.954 MeV), the optical model calculation gives half-life T_1/2(OMP) = 2.89 x 10^-7 s, compared to experimental T_1/2(expt) = 2.99 x 10^-7 s
- For Po-212 decay, the analytical model calculation gives T_1/2(anal) = 3.02 x 10^-7 s, compared to experimental T_1/2(expt) = 2.99 x 10^-7 s
- The optical model potential parameters for alpha+Pb-208 are: V_0 = 22 MeV, r_s = 1.27 fm, a_s = 0.62 fm, r_v = 0.66 fm, delta = 3.5, r_C = 1.2 fm, W_0 = 5 MeV, a_I = 0.28 fm, with r_I varying from 1.2 fm (at E_lab = 19 MeV) to 1.37 fm (at E_lab = 22 MeV)
- The analytical potential uses global parameters: r_0 = 1.07 fm, a_g = 0.63 fm, and diffuseness d = 9.63 fm for the alpha+Pb-208 pair

## Context

This work extends the approach of Michel et al. (1983) who used squared Woods-Saxon potentials for alpha+O-16 elastic scattering and cluster structure, and addresses the failure noted by Bhagwat and Gambhir (2008) to simultaneously describe fusion cross sections and decay half-lives using mean-field potentials. The paper enables a unified quantum mechanical treatment of elastic scattering, reaction cross sections, and alpha-decay within a single optical model framework, and provides an analytical S-matrix alternative that avoids tedious numerical wave function calculations for s-wave decays.
