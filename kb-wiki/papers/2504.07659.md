# Tidal heating in binary inspiral of strange quark stars

> We investigate tidal heating associated with the binary inspiral of strange quark stars and its impact on the resulting gravitational wave signal. Tidal heating during the merger of neutron stars composed of nuclear matter may be considered negligible, but it has been demonstrated recently that the presence of hyperons at high densities could significantly enhance the dissipation during inspiral. In this work, we evaluate the bulk viscosity arising from non-leptonic weak processes involving quarks and show that it can be several orders of magnitude higher than the viscosity of nuclear matter at temperatures relevant to the inspiral phase of the merger of strange stars. We model strange quark matter in the normal phase using a non-ideal bag model including electrons and ensure compatibility with astrophysical constraints. By analysing equal-mass binary systems with component masses ranging from 1.4 to 1.8 $\, M_{\odot}$, we find that temperatures close to 0.1 MeV are reached by the end of the inspiral phase. We also estimate the effect on the gravitational waveform and conclude that the additional phase shift could range from $0.1$ to $0.5$ radians for strange quark masses of 200 MeV, making it potentially detectable by next-generation gravitational wave detectors. Given that tidal heating from hyperons is dominant only for very massive neutron stars having masses 1.8 to 2.0 $\, M_{\odot}$, a successful detection of this phase shift during the inspiral of binary systems with relatively low masses of 1.4 to 1.6 $\, M_{\odot}$ could be a smoking gun signature for the existence of strange quark stars.

## Key claim

The authors claim that bulk viscosity from non-leptonic weak processes involving quarks in strange quark stars can be several orders of magnitude higher than the viscosity of nuclear matter at temperatures relevant to binary inspiral, and that the resulting gravitational wave phase shift of 0.1 to 0.5 radians for strange quark masses of 200 MeV could be a smoking gun signature for strange quark stars if detected in binary systems with relatively low component masses of 1.4 to 1.6 solar masses, where tidal heating from hyperons is not dominant.

## Method

The authors model strange quark matter in the normal phase using a non-ideal bag model with electrons, imposing charge neutrality and beta equilibrium conditions, and select EoS parameters (strange quark mass, nonperturbative parameter a4, and bag constant) consistent with the Bodmer-Witten conjecture and astrophysical constraints. They compute the bulk viscosity from the non-leptonic weak process u+d ↔ u+s at tree level in the low-temperature limit, then calculate the tidal dissipation energy from f-mode damping using a Newtonian tidal heating formalism, and estimate the temperature reached during inspiral and the additional phase shift in the gravitational waveform for equal-mass binary systems with component masses from 1.4 to 1.8 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Bulk viscosity in unpaired strange quark matter reaches a resonant maximum value of approximately 10^29 g cm^-1 s^-1 at T ~ 10^9 K for oscillation frequency of 1 kHz at baryon density n_B = 2.5 n_0 (where n_0 ≈ 0.15 fm^-3)
- Temperatures close to 0.1 MeV are reached by the end of the inspiral phase for equal-mass binary systems with component masses ranging from 1.4 to 1.8 solar masses
- The additional gravitational wave phase shift ranges from 0.1 to 0.5 radians for strange quark masses of 200 MeV
- f-mode frequencies for 1.4 solar mass stars range from 1.970 kHz (Set E, m_s=150 MeV, a4=0.6) to 2.333 kHz (Set B, m_s=200 MeV, a4=0.8)
- The maximum strange quark mass allowed by the Bodmer-Witten conjecture in the non-ideal bag model is m_s,max ≈ 310 MeV
- The bulk viscosity from strange quark matter is approximately 10^8 to 10^10 times higher than shear viscosity from electron-electron scattering in the temperature range of 10^6 to 10^8 K

## Context

This work extends the recent demonstration by Ghosh et al. (2024) that hyperonic bulk viscosity can produce detectable tidal heating in very massive neutron stars (1.8 to 2.0 solar masses), showing that strange quark stars could produce similar or larger effects at lower masses (1.4 to 1.6 solar masses) where hyperonic heating is negligible. The study builds on earlier calculations of bulk viscosity in strange quark matter by Sawyer (1989), Madsen (1992), Alford et al. (2006, 2010), and others, and on the Newtonian tidal heating formalism developed by Lai (1994). A successful detection of this phase shift by next-generation gravitational wave detectors such as Einstein Telescope or Cosmic Explorer could provide a smoking gun signature for the existence of strange quark stars, distinguishing them from ordinary neutron stars and hybrid stars.
