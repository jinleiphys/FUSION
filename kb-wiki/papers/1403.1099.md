# Mass radius relation of compact stars in the braneworld

> The braneworld scenario, based on the fact that the four dimension space-time is a hyper-surface of a five dimensional manifold, was shown to deal in a satisfactory way with the hierarchy problem. In this work we study macroscopic stellar properties of compact stars from the braneworld point of view. Using neutron star equations of state, we test the possibility of extra dimensions by solving the brane Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations obtained for three kinds of possible compact objects: hadronic, hybrid and quark stars. By comparing the macroscopic solutions with observational constraints, we establish a brane tension lower limit and the value for which the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations in the braneworld converge to the usual Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations.

## Key claim

The braneworld scenario can produce compact stars that are more compact (smaller masses and radii) than in standard general relativity, and the brane tension \(\lambda\) controls the maximum stellar mass while the parameter \(w\) (from the relation \(\mathcal{P}=w\mathcal{U}\)) influences the corresponding radii; however, the results converge to the standard TOV solutions when \(\lambda\) reaches approximately \(10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), and the lower limit for \(\lambda\) is set by requiring at least a \(1.44 M_\odot\) star to be achievable.

## Method

The authors solved the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for the braneworld (brane-TOV) using realistic equations of state for hadronic, hybrid, and quark stars, with the non-local Weyl terms assumed to obey the simple relation \(\mathcal{P}=w\mathcal{U}\). They integrated the system from the stellar core to the surface (null pressure point) for various values of the brane tension \(\lambda\) and the parameter \(w\), and compared the resulting mass-radius relations with observational constraints from massive pulsars and radius estimates.

## Key numbers

- For hadronic stars with \(\lambda=10^{37}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), the maximum mass ranges from \(1.69 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.1\)) to \(1.82 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.6\)), compared to \(2.04 M_\odot\) for the standard TOV solution.
- For hadronic stars with \(\lambda=10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), the maximum mass is approximately \(2.00 M_\odot\) for most \(w\) values, close to the standard TOV value of \(2.04 M_\odot\).
- The established range for the brane tension is \(3.89 \times 10^{36} < \lambda < 10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), with the lower limit ensuring at least a \(1.44 M_\odot\) star can be achieved.
- For hybrid stars with \(\lambda=10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), the maximum mass ranges from \(1.86 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.1\)) to \(1.89 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.6\)), compared to \(1.91 M_\odot\) for the standard TOV solution.
- For quark stars with \(\lambda=10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\), the maximum mass ranges from \(2.24 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.1\)) to \(2.30 M_\odot\) (for \(w=-0.6\)), compared to \(2.32 M_\odot\) for the standard TOV solution.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Germani and Maartens (2001) and Garcia-Aspeitia (2013) that first explored neutron stars as laboratories for testing extra dimensions, but uses more realistic equations of state (including hadronic, hybrid, and quark matter) instead of a perfect fluid. The results enable future observational tests of the braneworld scenario by showing that the brane tension can be constrained to a specific range (\(3.89 \times 10^{36} < \lambda < 10^{38}\) dyn/cm\(^2\)) and that the braneworld can produce smaller stellar radii, which may help reconcile theoretical models with recent radius estimates from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries (e.g., Guillot et al. 2013, Steiner et al. 2013).
