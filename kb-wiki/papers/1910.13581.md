# Fission in a microscopic framework: from basic science to support for applications

> Recent developments, both in theoretical modeling and computational power, have allowed us to make progress on a goal not fully achieved yet in nuclear theory: a microscopic theory of nuclear fission. Even if the complete microscopic description remains a computationally demanding task, the information that can be provided by current calculations can be extremely useful to guide and constrain more phenomenological approaches, which are simpler to implement. First, a microscopic model that describes the real-time dynamics of the fissioning system can justify or rule out some of the approximations. Second, the microscopic approach can be used to obtain trends, e.g., with increasing excitation energy of the fissioning system, or even to compute observables that cannot be otherwise calculated in phenomenological approaches or that can be hindered by the limitations of the method. We briefly present in this contribution the time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) approach to nuclear fission, approach that has become a very successful theoretical model in many areas of many-body research. The TDSLDA incorporates the effects of the continuum, the dynamics of the pairing field, and the numerical solution is implemented with controlled approximations and negligible numerical corrections. The main part of the current contribution will be dedicated to discussing the method, and recent results concerning the fission dynamics. In addition, we present results on the excitation energy sharing between the fragments, which are in agreement with a qualitative conclusions extracted from a limited number of experimental measurements of properties of prompt neutrons.

## Key claim

The time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) can provide microscopic guidance for phenomenological fission models, particularly for parametrizing the excitation energy sharing between fission fragments as a function of incident neutron energy, but the approach remains computationally demanding and the complete microscopic description of fission is not yet fully achieved.

## Method

The authors solve the time-dependent Schrödinger equation for 4-component quasiparticle wavefunctions on a rectangular lattice with dimensions 24×24×48 points and lattice constant 1.25 fm, using a fifth-order Adams-Bashford-Milne numerical method with errors of order O(Δt⁶). Initial states are taken beyond the outer barrier on the zero-temperature potential energy surface defined by constraints on quadrupole and octupole moments, and the system is evolved until fragment centers of mass are separated by 30 fm. Fragment properties are obtained by dividing the simulation box and using one-body densities in each half, with energies computed using the same Skyrme-type density functional (SLy4 or improved functionals) used for the evolution.

## Key numbers

- Lattice constant: 1.25 fm, corresponding to a momentum cutoff of about 500 MeV
- Simulation box dimensions: Nx=Ny=24, Nz=48 lattice points
- Fragment separation criterion: centers of mass separated by 30 fm
- Fragment properties established when separation between fragment tips is 4-5 fm
- Calculations performed for ²³⁹Pu neutron-induced fission at thermal and 5.5 MeV incident neutron energies
- Lattice constant reduction planned: 40% decrease (from 1.25 fm to 0.75 fm)

## Context

This work extends the TDSLDA approach previously applied to fission by Bulgac (2015) and others, using a larger number of initial conditions and improved density functionals. The results support the overdamped Brownian motion approach to fission yields pioneered by Möller and Randrup, while invalidating adiabatic approaches that transform collective potential energy into collective kinetic energy. The parametrization of excitation energy sharing extracted from ²⁴⁰Pu simulations has shown good reproduction of measured average prompt fission neutron multiplicity for ²³⁵U(n,f) and ²³⁷Np(n,f), suggesting robustness, and enables improved modeling in the CGMF code for prompt neutron and gamma emission.
