# Effective Kinetic Theory for High Temperature Gauge Theories

> Quasiparticle dynamics in relativistic plasmas associated with hot, weakly-coupled gauge theories (such as QCD at asymptotically high temperature $T$) can be described by an effective kinetic theory, valid on sufficiently large time and distance scales. The appropriate Boltzmann equations depend on effective scattering rates for various types of collisions that can occur in the plasma. The resulting effective kinetic theory may be used to evaluate observables which are dominantly sensitive to the dynamics of typical ultrarelativistic excitations. This includes transport coefficients (viscosities and diffusion constants) and energy loss rates. We show how to formulate effective Boltzmann equations which will be adequate to compute such observables to leading order in the running coupling $g(T)$ of high-temperature gauge theories [and all orders in $1/\log g(T)^{-1}$]. As previously proposed in the literature, a leading-order treatment requires including both $2<->2$ particle scattering processes as well as effective ``$1<->2$'' collinear splitting processes in the Boltzmann equations. The latter account for nearly collinear bremsstrahlung and pair production/annihilation processes which take place in the presence of fluctuations in the background gauge field. Our effective kinetic theory is applicable not only to near-equilibrium systems (relevant for the calculation of transport coefficients), but also to highly non-equilibrium situations, provided some simple conditions on distribution functions are satisfied.

## Key claim

The paper formulates an effective kinetic theory for hot, weakly-coupled gauge theories (such as QCD at asymptotically high temperature) that is adequate to compute transport coefficients and energy loss rates to leading order in the running coupling \(g(T)\) and to all orders in \(1/\log g(T)^{-1}\), provided the system satisfies specific conditions on separation of scales and smoothness of distribution functions; the theory applies not only to near-equilibrium systems but also to highly non-equilibrium situations under certain conditions on distribution functions.

## Method

The authors construct Boltzmann equations that include both \(2 \leftrightarrow 2\) particle scattering processes (all possible crossings of \(t\)-channel gauge boson exchange and quark exchange diagrams) and effective "\(1 \leftrightarrow 2\)" collinear splitting processes (nearly collinear bremsstrahlung and pair production/annihilation) that occur in the presence of soft gauge field fluctuations. The rates for the "\(1 \leftrightarrow 2\)" processes require a quantum mechanical treatment of multiple soft scatterings during the formation time (the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal effect), which is evaluated via a two-dimensional linear integral equation. The domain of validity is characterized by requiring that the momenta of primaries, scatterers, and screeners are all large compared to medium-dependent effective masses, and that distribution functions vary slowly on the scale of the formation time of near-collinear processes.

## Key numbers

- The typical momentum (or energy) of a quark or gluon in equilibrium is of order \(T\).
- Thermal masses for both quarks and gluons are \(O(gT)\) in size.
- The small angle mean free time \(\tau_g = 1/(g^2 T)\) characterizes scattering with momentum transfer \(q \sim gT\).
- The large angle mean free time \(\tau_* = 1/(g^4 T)\) characterizes scattering with momentum transfer \(q \sim T\) (up to logarithmic factors).
- The formation time of near-collinear "\(1 \leftrightarrow 2\)" processes is of order \(1/(g^2 T)\).
- The mean free path for hard "\(1 \leftrightarrow 2\)" collinear processes is of order \(1/(g^4 T)\).
- The Debye screening length is of order \((gT)^{-1}\).
- The non-perturbative magnetic length scale for colored fluctuations in non-Abelian gauge theories is of order \((g^2 T)^{-1}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier leading-log approximations for transport coefficients (Heiselberg, 1994; Arnold, Moore, Yaffe, 2000) by including "\(1 \leftrightarrow 2\)" collinear processes that contribute at next-to-leading log order, which were missed in prior calculations. The effective kinetic theory enables leading-order calculations of shear viscosity, electrical conductivity, flavor diffusion, and energy loss rates in hot gauge theories, and is applicable to non-equilibrium systems such as the intermediate stages of heavy ion collisions (Baier, Mueller, Schiff, Son, 2001). The paper also provides the foundation for evaluating the rates of near-collinear processes including the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal effect (Arnold, Moore, Yaffe, 2002).
