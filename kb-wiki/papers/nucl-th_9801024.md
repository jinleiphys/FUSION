# Highlight of the QCD Parallel Session

> I will give a review with some comments of the parallel session on QCD and the related talks in the Quark Matter'97 meeting.

## Key claim

This paper is a critical review of the QCD parallel session at the Quark Matter'97 meeting, highlighting that while perturbative QCD (pQCD) can calculate initial hard probe production rates, the effects of initial-state parton interactions (such as nuclear shadowing and the Cronin effect) must be fully understood before true QGP signals can be disentangled from conventional nuclear effects.

## Method

The author summarizes and comments on individual talks given in the QCD parallel session, covering three main areas: effects of initial-state parton interactions on hard probes (including nuclear shadowing models and double-scattering calculations for Drell-Yan production), equilibration processes in a parton gas (using Boltzmann equation approaches with elastic and inelastic scattering, and lattice QCD calculations of transport coefficients), and physics at high baryon density (including diquark condensates and color superconductivity). ## Key numbers

- Energy scale for pQCD dominance: Q0 = 2 GeV, corresponding to times less than or equal to 0.1 fm
- QCD critical temperature region: transport coefficients calculated around the QCD critical temperature are found very close to perturbative calculations at high temperatures
- Color superconductivity gap and critical temperature from perturbative calculation: in the MeV range (Bailin and Love, using effective strong coupling constant αs = 0.3)
- Color superconductivity gap and critical temperature from instanton-induced interactions: in the range of 100 MeV (Schäfer and Rajagopal)

## Context

This review extends the discussion of QCD in heavy-ion collisions from the Quark Matter'97 meeting, building on the overview by Matsui (1997) that QCD is central to the field. It contrasts the perturbative approach for hard probes (tested in e+e- annihilation, deep inelastic e-p scattering, and pp or p-pbar collisions) with nonperturbative hadronization processes where phenomenological fragmentation functions (Lund model) are used. The work enables future RHIC experiments to use hard processes as test grounds for QCD phenomena and probes of dense matter, while also highlighting open questions about parton equilibration, the role of quantum interference effects (emphasized by Dokshitzer), and the possibility of color superconductivity at high baryon density with implications for neutron stars.
