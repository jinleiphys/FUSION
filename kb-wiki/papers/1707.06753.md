# $K^*(892)$ and $ϕ(1020)$ production and their decay into the hadronic medium at the Large Hadron Collider

> The production of the $K^*(892)$ strange resonance in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV LHC energy is analyzed within the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) at different equations of state of superdense matter. The similar analysis is done also for the RHIC top energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV for comparison purposes. A modification of experimental $K^*(892)$-identification is studied for different centralities in view of possible re-scattering of the decay products at the afterburner stage of the fireball evolution. We see quite intensive rescattering of the decay products as well as recombination processes for $K^*(892)$. In addition, the production of the much longer-long-lived $ϕ(1020)$ resonance with hidden strange quark content is investigated.

## Key claim

The integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) shows that the observed yield of $K^*(892)$ resonances in Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies is reduced by only about 20% relative to a scenario without rescattering, because recombination of decay products partially compensates for the substantial loss (at least 70%) of direct resonances at early times; this conclusion depends on the particlization temperature and equation of state used.

## Method

The authors used the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM), which includes five stages: initial state formation, pre-thermal evolution, viscous hydrodynamic expansion, particlization (switching to particles at a chosen temperature), and a hadronic cascade stage modeled with UrQMD. They simulated Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV for eight centrality classes and Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV for comparison, using two different equations of state (Laine-Schroeder with $T_p=163$ MeV and HotQCD with $T_p=156$ MeV). The $K^*(892)$ and $\phi(1020)$ resonances were reconstructed from their decay products ($K\pi$ and $KK$ pairs) using invariant mass cuts and spatial coincidence criteria, and the yields were compared to ALICE and STAR data.

## Key numbers

- For central Pb+Pb collisions ($c=5-10\%$) at LHC, at times $\tau<15$ fm/$c$, only 30% of direct $K^*(892)$ resonances are observable in the UrQMD cascade scenario compared to free-streaming, meaning at least 70% are lost due to rescattering.
- At larger times ($15<\tau<30$ fm/$c$), the number of observed $K^*$ in the UrQMD case becomes about 1.5 times larger than in free-streaming, indicating recombination generates at least 50% of the direct yield.
- The net reduction of identified $K^{*0}$ in UrQMD is only about 20% compared to the calculation without rescatterings (for central collisions at $T_p=163$ MeV).
- The fraction of identified $K^*$ increases from 0.8 for 5% most central collisions to unity for peripheral collisions ($c=60-70\%$) at $T_p=163$ MeV; at $T_p=156$ MeV the central fraction is 0.88.
- For $\phi(1020)$, the fraction of observable resonances is about 20% larger than unity for non-central and 30% larger for central collisions.
- The $K^*/K^+$ ratio from iHKM for LHC 0-20% centrality is 0.18, compared to ALICE value $0.20 \pm 0.01 \pm 0.03$.
- The $\phi/K^+$ ratio from iHKM for LHC 0-5% centrality is 0.157, compared to ALICE value $0.127 \pm 0.004 \pm 0.014$.
- For RHIC Au+Au at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV, the iHKM $K^*/K^+$ ratio is 0.21 for 0-10% centrality, compared to STAR value $0.23 \pm 0.01 \pm 0.05$.

## Context

This work extends the integrated hydrokinetic model (iHKM) previously developed by the same group (Shapoval, Sinyukov, and collaborators) to study resonance production and rescattering effects in heavy-ion collisions. It directly compares with experimental data from the ALICE Collaboration (2015) for Pb+Pb at LHC and the STAR Collaboration for Au+Au at RHIC. The results support the concept of continuous freeze-out rather than a sharp chemical freeze-out, as the thermal model of Stachel, Andronic, Braun-Munzinger, and Redlich (2014) overestimates the $K^*/K$ ratio by a factor of about 1.5 for central LHC collisions. The findings enable better understanding of the hadronic afterburner stage and the role of resonance regeneration in interpreting experimental yields.
