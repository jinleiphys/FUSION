# Quark Spectral Function and Deconfinement at Nonzero Temperature

> The maximum entropy method is used to compute the quark spectral function at nonzero temperature. We solve the gap equation of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) self-consistently, employing a rainbow kernel which phenomenologically models results from Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSE) and lattice QCD. We use the criterion of positivity restoration of the spectral function as a signal for deconfinement. Our calculation indicates that the critical temperature of deconfinement $T_d$ is slightly smaller than the one of chiral symmetry restoration $T_c$: $T_d\sim 94% T_c$ in the chiral limit, and $T_d\sim 96% T_c$ with physical light quark masses. Since these deviations are within the systematic error of our approach, it is reasonable to conclude that chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement coincide at zero chemical potential.

# Quark Spectral Function and Deconfinement at Nonzero Temperature

## Key claim
The critical temperature of deconfinement is slightly smaller than that of chiral symmetry restoration, with T_d approximately 94% of T_c in the chiral limit and approximately 96% of T_c with physical light quark masses; however, because these deviations are within the systematic error of the approach, the authors conclude that chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement coincide at zero chemical potential.

## Method
The authors solved the QCD gap equation self-consistently using a rainbow truncation kernel that phenomenologically models results from Dyson-Schwinger equations and lattice QCD, with a Debye-like mass included in the longitudinal gluon propagator at nonzero temperature. They then applied the maximum entropy method (MEM) to extract the quark spectral function from the imaginary-time quark propagator, using positivity restoration of the spectral function as a signal for deconfinement. An order parameter L_rho was defined as the ratio of the difference between the integrated absolute value and the integrated value of the spectral function to the integrated absolute value, with L_rho = 0 indicating a positive definite spectral function and thus deconfinement.

## Key numbers
- T_d ~ 94% T_c in the chiral limit, averaged over the interaction width sigma in [0.4, 0.6] GeV
- T_d ~ 96% T_c with physical light quark masses (m_u=d = 3.4 MeV at renormalization point 19 GeV), averaged over the same sigma range
- For sigma = 0.5 GeV in the chiral limit: T_c = 0.142 GeV, T_d = 0.131 GeV, with Delta/T_c = 7.7%
- For sigma = 0.5 GeV with physical quark mass: T_c = 0.144 GeV, T_d = 0.136 GeV, with Delta/T_c = 5.6%
- For sigma = 0.4 GeV in the chiral limit: T_c = 0.165 GeV, T_d = 0.155 GeV, with Delta/T_c = 6.1%
- For sigma = 0.6 GeV in the chiral limit: T_c = 0.126 GeV, T_d = 0.122 GeV, with Delta/T_c = 3.2%
- The critical temperature range from the model overlaps with lattice QCD results: T_c in [0.146, 0.170] GeV (Aoki 2009)

## Context
This work extends the study of quark spectral functions above T_c from Qin (2011) to temperatures below T_c, using the maximum entropy method to extract spectral functions from Dyson-Schwinger equation solutions. The approach uses positivity violation of the Schwinger function as a confinement criterion, consistent with the findings of Mueller (2010) who also observed positivity violations below T_c. The results support the conclusion that chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement coincide at zero chemical potential, but the authors note that at nonzero chemical potential the difference between T_c and T_d could become more pronounced and give rise to a quarkyonic phase as proposed by McLerran (2007, 2009).
