# Quantum particles from coarse grained classical probabilities in phase space

> Quantum particles can be obtained from a classical probability distribution in phase space by a suitable coarse graining, whereby simultaneous classical information about position and momentum can be lost. For a suitable time evolution of the classical probabilities and choice of observables all features of a quantum particle in a potential follow from classical statistics. This includes interference, tunneling and the uncertainty relation.

## Key claim

Quantum particles can be derived from a classical probability distribution in phase space through coarse graining, but only if the time evolution of the classical probabilities is modified from the Liouville equation to a specific differential equation (Eq. AA2) that ensures unitary evolution of the coarse-grained subsystem; otherwise, the subsystem's time evolution does not decouple from the total system and is generally not unitary.

## Method

The author starts with a classical probability distribution \(w(z,p) \geq 0\) in phase space and defines a classical wave function \(\psi_C(z,p) = \pm \sqrt{w(z,p)}\). A Fourier transform maps this to a two-position-variable wave function \(\tilde\psi_C(x,y)\), from which a coarse-grained quantum density matrix \(\rho_Q(x,x') = \int_y \tilde\rho_C(x,x',y,y)\) is obtained by tracing out the \(y\) coordinate. The quantum position and momentum operators are identified as \(\hat X_Q = \hat X_{cl} + \frac12 \hat X_s\) and \(\hat P_Q = \hat P_{cl} + \frac12 \hat P_s\), where \(\hat X_s = i\partial_p\) and \(\hat P_s = -i\partial_z\) are statistical observables. The time evolution equation for \(\psi_C\) is postulated as \(\partial_t\psi_C = -\frac{p}{m}\partial_x\psi_C + K(x,\partial_p)\psi_C\) with \(K = -i[V(x+\frac{i}{2}\partial_p) - V(x-\frac{i}{2}\partial_p)]\), which yields the standard unitary quantum evolution for \(\rho_Q\).

## Key numbers

- The classical probability distribution is normalized as \(\int_{x,p} w(x,p) = 1\), with units \(\hbar = 1\).
- The quantum momentum dispersion contains an additional term \(\Delta^{(Q)2}_p = \frac{1}{16} \int_{z,p} \frac{(\partial_z w(z,p))^2}{w(z,p)}\) beyond the classical momentum dispersion.
- The quantum transform \(\bar\rho_w(z,p)\) is given by Eq. AA1: \(\bar\rho_w(x,p) = \int \psi_C(x+\frac{r}{2},p+s)\psi_C(x+\frac{r'}{2},p+s') \cos(s'r - sr')\) integrated over \(r,r',s,s'\).
- The commutation relation for quantum observables is \([\hat X_Q, \hat P_Q] = i\) (Eq. 113M).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous program (Wetterich, 2010, papers I and II) showing how quantum systems can arise as subsystems of classical statistical ensembles, building on earlier conceptual work (Wetterich, 2009-2010) on incomplete statistics and measurement correlations that can violate Bell's inequalities. The paper introduces a continuous interpolation between quantum and classical particles via a "zwitter parameter" \(\sin^2\gamma\), enabling future experimental tests to bound deviations from quantum behavior. The coarse-graining procedure contrasts with standard approaches like block spins or functional renormalization (Wilson, 1971; Wetterich, 1993) by defining a subsystem with fewer effective degrees of freedom rather than averaging over microscopic details.
