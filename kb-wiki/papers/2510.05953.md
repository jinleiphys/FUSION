# Low lying isomers in the region of superheavy nuclei

> In the present study we want to give an overview on low lying isomeric states in the heaviest nuclei. After a short report on the early history on the discovery of nuclear isomerism and attempts to understand their physical nature, decay probabilities and structure of all low lying isomeric states in heaviest nuclei with half-lives typically longer than one microsecond are presentet. Special emphasisis is laid on cases where the above mentioned properties are still unclear or under discussion. We do not claim to have solved the problems in that cases, we rather want to give som hints for further discussions.

# Low lying isomers in the region of superheavy nuclei

## Key claim
This paper provides an overview of low-lying isomeric states in the heaviest nuclei with half-lives typically longer than one microsecond, but explicitly does not claim to have solved cases where properties remain unclear or under discussion; instead it aims to give hints for further discussion.

## Method
The author reviews the historical development of nuclear isomerism from Soddy (1917) through Gamow, von Weizsäcker, and Bethe, then categorizes isomers into four types: spin isomers, seniority isomers, K isomers, and shape isomers. Experimental methods for studying isomers in superheavy nuclei (Z ≥ 100) produced by complete fusion reactions are described, including in-flight separation, implantation into silicon detectors, and spectroscopy of alpha particles, conversion electrons, and gamma rays. Specific isomeric cases in even-Z nuclei are then presented with their decay properties and structural assignments.

## Key numbers
- In $^{237}$Pu, the 1/2$^{+}$[631] isomer has a half-life of T$_{1/2}$ = 0.18 ± 0.02 s at excitation energy E$^{*}$ = 145 keV
- In $^{235}$U, the 1/2$^{+}$[631] isomer has a half-life of approximately 25 min at E$^{*}$ = 7.68 keV
- The 8$^{+}$ seniority isomer in $^{216}$Th has an alpha branch b$_{\alpha}$ = 0.028 ± 0.009
- The 8$^{+}$ isomer in $^{212}$Ra has a half-life of 8.3 μs
- For a nucleus with Z = 90 and ΔE = 500 keV, von Weizsäcker's estimate gave lifetimes from approximately 3×10$^{-19}$ s for Δl = 1 to approximately 2.4×10$^{4}$ s (approximately 7 h) for Δl = 7

## Context
This review extends the earlier compilation by Mattauch (1941), which listed 25 known isomeric states, and builds on the theoretical framework of von Weizsäcker (1936) and Bethe (1937) for understanding spin isomers. The paper covers isomers in transuranium nuclei up to the superheavy region, including N = 143 isotones ($^{233}$Th, $^{235}$U, $^{237}$Pu) and nuclei beyond ($^{239}$Cm, $^{241}$Cf), and discusses open questions regarding K isomers in strongly deformed nuclei and the possible occurrence of seniority isomers near predicted spherical superheavy shells at Z = 114, 120, or 126 and N = 184 or 172.
