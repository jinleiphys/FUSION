# Seniority in quantum many-body systems

> The use of the seniority quantum number in many-body systems is reviewed. A brief summary is given of its introduction by Racah in the context of atomic spectroscopy. Several extensions of Racah's original idea are discussed: seniority for identical nucleons in a single-$j$ shell, its extension to the case of many, non-degenerate $j$ shells and to systems with neutrons and protons. To illustrate its usefulness to this day, a recent application of seniority is presented in Bose--Einstein condensates of atoms with spin.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the concept of seniority as a quantum number in many-body systems, tracing its origins from Racah's work in atomic spectroscopy to modern applications in nuclear physics and Bose-Einstein condensates. The abstract glosses over the fact that seniority conservation is only guaranteed for any interaction in a single-\(j\) shell when \(j \leq 7/2\), and that for larger \(j\) values specific conditions on the interaction matrix elements must be satisfied.

## Method

The paper systematically develops the group-theoretical foundations of seniority, starting with Racah's original formulation using coefficients of fractional parentage and the orthogonal algebra SO(\(2\ell+1\)) for atomic electrons in \(LS\) coupling. It then extends the formalism to identical nucleons in a single-\(j\) shell using the symplectic algebra Sp(\(2j+1\)) and the quasi-spin SU(2) algebra, derives the necessary and sufficient conditions for seniority conservation in a general two-body interaction, and generalizes to multiple non-degenerate shells using Richardson's Bethe ansatz and Talmi's generalized seniority condition. The treatment is further extended to systems with both neutrons and protons using the SO(5) and SO(8) quasi-spin algebras, and finally applied to Bose-Einstein condensates of atoms with spin \(f \leq 2\) using the reduction chain U(\(2f+1\)) \(\supset\) SO(\(2f+1\)) \(\supset\) SO(3).

## Key numbers

- For identical nucleons in a single-\(j\) shell, the number of independent constraints on the interaction matrix elements \(\nu_J\) that ensure seniority conservation is \(\lfloor(2j-3)/6\rfloor\), with no constraints for \(j = 3/2, 5/2, 7/2\), one constraint for \(j = 9/2, 11/2, 13/2\), and so on.
- The pairing hamiltonian eigenvalue for identical nucleons in a single-\(j\) shell is \(-g(n-\upsilon)(2j-n-\upsilon+3)/4\), where \(n\) is the nucleon number, \(\upsilon\) is the seniority, and \(j\) is the total angular momentum.
- For the Richardson model with \(n=2\) fermions distributed over \(s=5\) single-particle orbits appropriate for tin isotopes (\(Z=50\)), the secular equation is \(\sum_j \Omega_j/(2\epsilon_j - E) = 1/g\), where \(\Omega_j = j+1/2\).
- For Bose-Einstein condensates of spin-2 atoms, the phase diagram parameters are \(b = (-7\nu_0 + 10\nu_2 - 3\nu_4)/70\) and \(c = (-\nu_2 + \nu_4)/14\), where \(\nu_F\) are the interaction strengths for total angular momentum \(F\).
- For spin-2 atoms, the three possible ground-state configurations are: \((\upsilon_0, F_0) = (n, 2n)\) for the aligned phase, \((\upsilon_0, F_0) = (0, 0)\) for the paired phase (even \(n\)), and \((\upsilon_0, F_0) = (n, 2\delta)\) with \(\delta = 0\) or 1 for the high-seniority low-spin phase.

## Context

This review extends Racah's original 1943 work on seniority in atomic spectroscopy to nuclear physics, building on the foundational contributions of Racah (1949), Flowers (1952), and Kerman (1961) for the single-\(j\) shell case, and Talmi (1971) for generalized seniority in multiple shells. The paper connects to Richardson's exact solution (1963) of the pairing hamiltonian via the Bethe ansatz, and to the SO(8) model of neutron-proton pairing developed by Hecht (1965) and Ginocchio (1965). The application to Bose-Einstein condensates of spin-1 and spin-2 atoms extends the work of Ho (1998) and Law et al. (1998), showing that the general hamiltonian is exactly solvable for \(f \leq 2\) and enabling the analytic determination of ground-state structures and relaxation properties of such condensates.
