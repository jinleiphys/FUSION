# Comparison of central collisions with thermalised systems in the framework of the classical N-body dynamics

> In this study we address the question of the thermal description of collisions of classical clusters in the framework of classical N-body dynamics. We compare the results of systems in central collisions and those of thermalised systems with the same sizes and with the same available energies. The comparisons are made on size distributions, on total multiplicities distributions and on IMF (N>3) multiplicities distributions. These distributions are found identical for available energies per particle below the energy of the least bound particle of the total system. They are notably different for higher available energies. These results may put a new light on the standard explanation for fragment formation in N-body collisions.

## Key claim

In the framework of classical N-body dynamics, the fragment size distributions, total multiplicity distributions, and intermediate-mass-fragment (IMF, N>3) multiplicity distributions from central collisions are identical to those from thermalised systems only when the available energy per particle is below the energy of the least bound particle of the total system; for higher available energies, the distributions differ notably, contradicting the standard assumption that fragment formation can be described as the decay of a thermalised system at all excitation energies.

## Method

The authors used the Classical N-Body Dynamics (CNBD) code, which evolves particles via Newtonian equations of motion with a third-degree polynomial two-body potential (repulsive at short range, attractive at medium range, zero at long range). They compared central collisions (impact parameter b/b_max < 0.1) of equal-mass projectile and target (N/2 particles each) with thermalised systems of N particles prepared by confining the system in a sphere with a quadratic recall potential, imparting a given kinetic energy, and evolving until the ratio of kinetic energy dispersion to mean kinetic energy approached the microcanonical ensemble value. After thermalisation, the confining sphere was removed and the system was allowed to decay freely for approximately 170 T.S.U. Comparisons were performed for systems of 26, 68, 100, and 200 particles at four excitation energies per particle relative to the least bound particle energy (E_LeastBound) and the binding energy per particle (E_Bind/N).

## Key numbers

- For the N=68 system at low excitation energy (E*/N < E_LeastBound, 53 E.S.U.), the mean total multiplicity for central collisions was 6.2 ± 1.5 and for thermalised systems was 5.9 ± 1.5; the mean IMF multiplicity was 1 ± 0.1 for both; the mean size of the biggest cluster was 62.5 ± 1.6 for collisions and 62.9 ± 1.6 for thermalised systems.
- For the N=68 system at E*/N = 73 E.S.U. (close to E_LeastBound = 60 E.S.U.), the mean total multiplicity was 15.4 ± 2.3 for collisions and 16.8 ± 1.9 for thermalised systems; the mean IMF multiplicity was 1.7 ± 0.8 for collisions and 1.1 ± 0.3 for thermalised systems; the mean biggest cluster size was 43.7 ± 9.3 for collisions and 50.2 ± 2.3 for thermalised systems.
- For the N=68 system at E*/N = 108 E.S.U. (close to E_Bind/N), the mean total multiplicity was 29.6 ± 2.4 for collisions and 32.6 ± 2.6 for thermalised systems; the mean IMF multiplicity was 4.3 ± 1.2 for collisions and 2.3 ± 1.1 for thermalised systems; the mean biggest cluster size was 13.8 ± 3.1 for collisions and 23.3 ± 5.5 for thermalised systems.
- For the N=68 system at E*/N = 133 E.S.U. (above E_Bind/N), the mean total multiplicity was 36.3 ± 2.9 for collisions and 40.4 ± 2.4 for thermalised systems; the mean IMF multiplicity was 3.9 ± 1.1 for collisions and 2.9 ± 1.0 for thermalised systems; the mean biggest cluster size was 8.7 ± 2.3 for collisions and 10.7 ± 3.9 for thermalised systems.
- The energy of the least bound particle (E_LeastBound) was 50 E.S.U. for N=26, 60 E.S.U. for N=68, and 65 E.S.U. for N=100 and N=200.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies using the CNBD code (Morisseau and Cussol, references CNBD1 and CNBD2), which established that the fragmentation threshold in colliding classical systems coincides with the energy of the least bound particle. The results contradict the standard two-step model of nucleus-nucleus collisions, where a dynamical stage forms hot sources that then decay thermally, as implemented in codes like Gemini (reference GEMINI). The findings suggest that the thermal description of fragment production at high excitation energies (above 4-5 A.MeV) should be reconsidered, and that signals interpreted as liquid-gas phase transitions in nuclear collisions (negative heat capacity, bimodality, Fisher scaling) may need re-interpretation in light of these classical simulations. The authors note that future work must include Coulomb-like repulsive interactions and quantum effects, and that only central collisions were studied here.
