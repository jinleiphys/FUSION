# Investigating Biological Matter with Theoretical Nuclear Physics Methods

> The internal dynamics of strongly interacting systems and that of biomolecules such as proteins display several important analogies, despite the huge difference in their characteristic energy and length scales. For example, in all such systems, collective excitations, cooperative transitions and phase transitions emerge as the result of the interplay of strong correlations with quantum or thermal fluctuations. In view of such an observation, some theoretical methods initially developed in the context of theoretical nuclear physics have been adapted to investigate the dynamics of biomolecules. In this talk, we review some of our recent studies performed along this direction. In particular, we discuss how the path integral formulation of the molecular dynamics allows to overcome some of the long-standing problems and limitations which emerge when simulating the protein folding dynamics at the atomistic level of detail.

# Investigating Biological Matter with Theoretical Nuclear Physics Methods

## Key claim

The paper argues that functional integral methods originally developed for non-perturbative QCD can be rigorously adapted to study protein folding dynamics, but only in the overdamped (diffusive) regime where the Langevin equation reduces to a Fokker-Planck equation that can be mapped onto an imaginary-time Schrödinger equation. The approach is presented as a practical alternative to classical molecular dynamics simulations, which cannot reach the millisecond-to-second timescales of protein folding.

## Method

The authors formulate protein dynamics using the overdamped Langevin equation and transform the Fokker-Planck equation into an imaginary-time Schrödinger equation with an effective Hamiltonian, where the effective potential depends on the gradient and Laplacian of the molecular force field. Dominant reaction pathways (biological instantons) are obtained by minimizing the effective Hamilton-Jacobi functional, replacing time with the curvilinear abscissa along the path to avoid the severe timescale gap. Thermal fluctuations around these dominant paths are included via one-loop functional determinant calculations, and an effective stochastic theory renormalizes the fast dynamics to improve convergence.

## Key numbers

- Classical MD simulations can follow dynamics over time intervals not exceeding a few hundred ns, while protein folding occurs at timescales from few ms to several seconds
- The damping timescale τ_D for biomolecular systems is of order a fraction of a ps, much smaller than the ns timescale of torsional angle dynamics
- For the Hamilton-Jacobi formulation, typically 100 discretization slices are sufficient for an accurate path representation, compared to ~10^6 time steps needed for direct time discretization
- The cut-off time scale Δt_c for the effective stochastic theory is typically ~10^{-3} ps with b ~ 10^{-2}
- Using the DRP method, a protein folding simulation that required special-purpose supercomputing or thousands of distributed CPUs was reproduced in less than a single day on only 32 CPUs

## Context

This work extends the analogy between strongly interacting matter and protein dynamics, noting that both systems exhibit collective excitations, cooperative transitions, and phase diagrams with low-entropy ordered phases and high-entropy disordered phases. The Dominant Reaction Pathways approach builds on earlier path integral formulations of stochastic dynamics and was validated against direct MD simulations on coarse-grained protein models (DRPtest2, 2010). The method was applied to reproduce and reconcile conflicting results from the Anton special-purpose machine (Shaw et al., 2010) and distributed computing approaches (Pande et al., 2000s), and enabled the first ab-initio quantum mechanical study of peptide folding, which had been inaccessible to Car-Parrinello or standard MD approaches. Future work includes simulations on much larger systems currently unaccessible to MD.
