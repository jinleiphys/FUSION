# Two-step nuclear reactions: The Surrogate Method, the Trojan Horse Method and their common foundations

> In this Letter I argue that the Surrogate Method, used to extract the fast neutron capture cross section on actinide target nuclei, which has important practical application for the next generation of breeder reactors, and the Trojan Horse Method employed to extract reactions of importance to nuclear astrophysics, have a common foundation, the Inclusive Non-Elastic Breakup (INEB)Theory. Whereas the Surrogate Method relies on the premise that the extracted neutron cross section in a (d,p) reaction is predominantly a compound nucleus one, the Trojan Horse Method, assumes a predominantly direct process for the secondary reaction induced by the surrogate fragment. In general, both methods contain both direct and compound contributions, and I show how theses seemingly distinct methods are in fact the same but at different energies and different kinematic regions. The unifying theory is the rather well developed INEB theory.

# Two-step nuclear reactions: The Surrogate Method, the Trojan Horse Method and their common foundations

## Key claim

The Surrogate Method (SM) and the Trojan Horse Method (THM) are not distinct approaches but share a common foundation in Inclusive Non-Elastic Breakup (INEB) theory, differing only in the energy and kinematic regime in which they are applied. The SM assumes predominantly compound nucleus formation for the extracted neutron cross section in a (d,p) reaction, while the THM assumes a predominantly direct process for the secondary reaction; however, both methods in general contain both direct and compound contributions.

## Method

The author derives the cross section for observing a spectator fragment b in a two-step reaction a + A → b + x + A → b + y + B using INEB theory, where projectile a = b + x. The INEB cross section σ^(b)_(INEB) = K_(INEB) · σ̃^(x+A)_R contains a kinematical factor K_(INEB) and a modified total reaction cross section σ̃^(x+A)_R of the surrogate fragment x with target A. This modified reaction cross section is decomposed into a direct component σ̃^(x+A)_D (sum of exclusive processes including the THM reaction of interest) and a compound nucleus component σ̃^(x+A)_CN (relevant for the SM). The SM extracts the neutron capture cross section from the observed proton spectrum in a (d,p) reaction, while the THM isolates the direct reaction x + A → y + B from the spectator b spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The THM is applied at projectile energies E_a = 100 – 400 keV to simulate nova environment conditions.
- The THM example reaction d(¹⁸F, α¹⁵O)n is measured in the quasi-elastic regime at higher energies and appropriate kinematics.
- The Serber cross section for the b spectrum is given by σ^(b)_(Serber) = K_(Serber) |Φ_a(k_b)|² σ^(x+A)_R, where the internal projectile wave function modified total reaction cross section σ̃^(x+A)_R tends at higher energies to the usual total reaction cross section σ^(x+A)_R.
- The SM branching ratio within Hauser-Feshbach theory is G_(f,l) = T_(f,l) / [Σ_g T_(g,l)], with T indicating the transmission coefficient.

## Context

This work unifies two previously separate lines of research: the Surrogate Method developed for fast neutron capture cross sections on actinide targets such as ²³⁸U and ²³²Th (relevant for fast breeder reactors), and the Trojan Horse Method used to extract astrophysical S-factors for reactions like ¹⁸F(p,α)¹⁵O at nova energies. The INEB theory, developed by Hussein and collaborators (1981, 1985), Ichimura, Austern and Vincent (1985), and Austern (1987), provides the common theoretical framework. Recent work by Ducasse (2015), Potel (2015), Moro (2015), and Carlson (2015) has applied INEB theory to justify the SM cross section. The unification enables practitioners of both methods to benefit from each other's experience and allows extraction of both direct and compound contributions from the same experimental data.
