# Consequences of neutron decay inside neutron stars

> The hypothesis that neutrons might decay into dark matter is explored using neutron stars as a testing ground. It is found that in order to obtain stars with masses at the upper end of those observed, the dark matter must experience a relatively strong self-interaction. Conservation of baryon number and energy then require that the star must undergo some heating, with a decrease in radius, leading to an increase in speed of rotation over a period of days.

## Key claim

If neutrons decay into dark matter inside neutron stars, the dark matter must experience a relatively strong repulsive self-interaction (G ≥ 26 fm²) for the star to reach observed masses up to 2 M⊙; conservation of baryon number and energy then forces the star to heat up and decrease in radius, causing a spin-up over roughly 100,000 seconds.

## Method

The authors model neutron star matter using the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, assuming nucleons only (no hyperons or strange quark matter). They solve the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations for a star containing both nuclear matter and dark matter, where the dark matter has a repulsive vector self-interaction parameterized by a strength G. The neutron decay channel considered is n → χ + φ, where χ is a baryonic dark fermion nearly degenerate with the neutron and φ is a light escaping boson; chemical equilibrium between neutrons and χ is enforced.

## Key numbers

- The maximum mass of a neutron star without dark matter self-interaction falls from 2.23 M⊙ to 0.7 M⊙ after neutron decay.
- To obtain a maximum mass of at least 2 M⊙, the dark matter self-interaction strength must be G = 26 fm².
- At G = 26 fm², if the total mass were held constant after decay, there would be approximately 2.5 × 10⁵⁵ (about 1%) more baryons inside the star.
- For a star with total baryon number fixed at 2.4 × 10⁵⁷, at G = 26 fm² the mass change is approximately 0.002 M⊙ (0.14%).
- For a 1.4 M⊙ star, heating the dark matter to 2 MeV (corresponding to an energy equivalent of 0.001 M⊙) causes a radius decrease of 620 m (5.06%) and a moment of inertia decrease of 2.28 × 10³⁷ kg·m² (14.1%).
- The relative population of dark matter particles is less than 5% even in heavier stars (∼1.8 M⊙) when G ≥ 26 fm².
- The time scale for the spin-up is around 100,000 seconds.

## Context

This work extends the Fornal-Grinstein (2018) hypothesis that neutrons might have a dark decay channel to explain the ∼8-second discrepancy between beam and bottle neutron lifetime measurements. Earlier work by Motta et al. (2018) and others showed that non-interacting dark matter from neutron decay would limit neutron star masses to ∼0.7 M⊙, contradicting observations of 2 M⊙ stars. The present study quantifies the required dark matter self-interaction strength (G ≥ 26 fm², consistent with Cline (2018)) and identifies a new observable signature: a spin-up of the neutron star over days following its birth, arising from conservation of baryon number and energy during the decay process.
