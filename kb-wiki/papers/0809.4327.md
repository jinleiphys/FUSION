# Azimuthal Asymmetry of pions in pp and pA collisions

> We investigate the azimuthal asymmetry v2 of produced pions in pp and pA collisions at both RHIC and SPS energies. In our approach, based on the pQCD parton model and the light-cone QCD-dipole formalism, the azimuthal asymmetry results from a correlation between the color-dipole orientation and the impact parameter of the collision. We introduce the color-dipole orientation within an improved Born approximation and the saturation model which satisfies available DIS data, showing that the azimuthal asymmetry of partons and pions is very sensitive to the choice of the model, and that it is reduced in the saturation model. We find that v2 of quarks and gluons in parton-nucleus collisions have very different patterns. The azimuthal asymmetry of gluons in gluon-nucleus collisions can be negative at small transverse momentum, changes the sign and becomes positive at high transverse momentum. The azimuthal asymmetry of quarks in quark-nucleus collisions is positive at all values of transverse momentum. We find that the azimuthal anisotropy v2 of produced pions in both pp and pA collisions is positive, albeit rather small.

## Key claim

The azimuthal asymmetry \(v_2\) of pions in both \(pp\) and \(pA\) collisions is positive but rather small, and arises from a correlation between the color-dipole orientation and the impact parameter of the collision, not from collective flow; the magnitude of \(v_2\) is very sensitive to the choice of model (improved Born approximation versus saturation model), and is reduced in the saturation model.

## Method

The authors combine the pQCD parton model with the light-cone QCD-dipole formalism to compute the azimuthal asymmetry of quarks, gluons, and pions. They introduce color-dipole orientation in two ways: an improved Born approximation (2g Model) and the Golec-Biernat–Wusthoff (GBW) saturation model fitted to DIS data, plus an \(s\)-dependent variant (KST model) for soft reactions. The transverse momentum distribution of a parton after multiple coherent interactions in a nucleus is expressed via eikonalized dipole amplitudes, and the second Fourier coefficient \(v_2\) is extracted from the azimuthal dependence of the single-particle spectra. For hadron production, they employ pQCD-improved factorization with parton distribution functions, fragmentation functions, and primordial transverse momentum distributions, distinguishing short- and long-coherence-length regimes.

## Key numbers

* The GBW dipole cross section parameters: \(\sigma_0 = 23.03\) mb, \(R_0(x) = 0.4\ \text{fm} \times (x/x_0)^{0.144}\) with \(x_0 = 3.04 \times 10^{-4}\).
* The \(s\)-dependent KST model: \(R_0(s) = 0.88\ \text{fm}\,(s_0/s)^{0.14}\) with \(s_0 = 1000\ \text{GeV}^2\); the pion-proton total cross section parametrization \(\sigma^{\pi p}_{tot}(s) = 23.6\,(s/s_0)^{0.08}\) mb.
* The elastic slope for \(\pi p\) scattering: \(B^{\pi p}_{el}(s) = B_0 + 2\alpha_\Pom^\prime \ln(s/\mu^2)\) with \(B_0 = 6\ \text{GeV}^{-2}\), \(\alpha_\Pom^\prime = 0.25\ \text{GeV}^{-2}\), \(\mu^2 = 1\ \text{GeV}^2\).
* The mean pion charge radius squared: \(\langle r^2_{ch}\rangle_\pi = 0.44\ \text{fm}^2\).
* The nonperturbative gluon interaction parameter: \(r_0 = 0.3\ \text{fm}\).
* Coherence length condition for quarks at midrapidity: \(p_T \lesssim \sqrt{s}/(m_N R_A)\); at RHIC \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV, only quarks with up to several GeV transverse momentum can be produced coherently.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposal (Kopeliovich, Rezaeian, Schmidt, 2007–2008) that azimuthal asymmetry can arise from the initial stage of relativistic nuclear collisions via color-dipole orientation, previously applied to prompt photons. It contrasts with the standard interpretation of elliptic flow in \(AA\) collisions as a signature of collective expansion of a strongly interacting quark-gluon plasma (observed at RHIC and described by ideal-fluid models). The study provides a baseline for jet-quenching models in \(AA\) collisions and shows that \(v_2\) in \(pp\) and \(pA\) collisions can be understood without invoking a hot dense medium, enabling future comparisons with data from RHIC and LHC.
