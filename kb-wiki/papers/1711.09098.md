# Uncertainties in the production of p nuclides in thermonuclear supernovae determined by Monte Carlo variations

> Thermonuclear supernovae originating from the explosion of a white dwarf accreting mass from a companion star have been suggested as a site for the production of $p$ nuclides. Such nuclei are produced during the explosion, in layers enriched with seed nuclei coming from prior strong $s$ processing. These seeds are transformed to proton-richer isotopes mainly by photodisintegration reactions. Several thousand trajectories from a 2D explosion model were used in a Monte Carlo approach. Temperature-dependent uncertainties were assigned individually to thousands of rates varied simultaneously in post-processing in an extended nuclear reaction network. The uncertainties in the final nuclear abundances originating from uncertainties in the astrophysical reaction rates were determined. In addition to the 35 classical $p$ nuclides, abundance uncertainties were also determined for the radioactive nuclides $^{92}$Nb, $^{97,98}$Tc, $^{146}$Sm, and for the abundance ratios $Y$(${}^{92}$Mo)/$Y$(${}^{94}$Mo), $Y$(${}^{92}$Nb)/$Y$(${}^{92}$Mo), $Y$(${}^{97}$Tc)/$Y$(${}^{98}$Ru), $Y$(${}^{98}$Tc)/$Y$(${}^{98}$Ru), and $Y$(${}^{146}$Sm)/$Y$(${}^{144}$Sm), important for Galactic Chemical Evolution studies. Uncertainties found were generally lower than a factor of two, although most nucleosynthesis flows mainly involve predicted rates with larger uncertainties. The main contribution to the total uncertainties comes from a group of trajectories with high peak density originating from the interior of the exploding white dwarf. The distinction between low-density and high-density trajectories allows more general conclusions to be drawn, also applicable to other simulations of white dwarf explosions.

## Key claim

The total production uncertainties for p nuclides in thermonuclear supernovae are generally well below a factor of two, despite most nucleosynthesis flows involving predicted rates with larger uncertainties; the main contribution to total uncertainties comes from a group of trajectories with high peak density originating from the interior of the exploding white dwarf.

## Method

Several thousand trajectories from a 2D delayed detonation model (DDT-a) of a Chandrasekhar-mass white dwarf explosion were post-processed using the pizbuin code suite, with 10,000 Monte Carlo iterations per trajectory. Temperature-dependent uncertainty ranges were assigned individually to thousands of reaction rates from Fe to Bi and varied simultaneously in an extended nuclear reaction network containing 1342 nuclides. Key rates were identified using the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient between rate variation and abundance variation, with a threshold of |r| >= 0.65.

## Key numbers

- 4624 trajectories were selected for Monte Carlo processing, showing peak temperatures between 1.5 and 3.7 GK
- 814 tracers fell into the high-density group (4x10^8 to 10^9 g cm^-3) and 3810 tracers into the low-density group (10^6 to 10^8 g cm^-3)
- Total production uncertainty for 180Ta: up factor 2.590, down factor 0.864 (the largest uncertainty among classical p nuclides)
- Total production uncertainty for 162Er: up factor 1.988, down factor 0.848 (approaching a factor of 2 at the upper limit)
- Total production uncertainty for 152Gd: up factor 1.601, down factor 0.764
- Total production uncertainty for 156Dy: up factor 1.733, down factor 0.737
- Only one key rate was found in the combined analysis: 145Eu + p <-> gamma + 146Gd (correlation factor r = -0.72) affecting 146Sm
- Five key rates were identified for the low-density group and seven for the high-density group

## Context

This work extends the Monte Carlo approach previously applied to p-nucleus production in core-collapse supernovae by Nishimura et al. (2017) to thermonuclear supernovae (Type Ia), using the same 2D model as Travaglio et al. (2011, 2015). The results differ from earlier sensitivity studies by Travaglio et al. (2015) that used individual rate variations rather than simultaneous Monte Carlo variations. The distinction between low-density and high-density trajectory groups allows the results to be applied to other white dwarf explosion simulations, such as the W7 model of Nomoto et al. (1984), by appropriately weighting the contributions from each density regime.
