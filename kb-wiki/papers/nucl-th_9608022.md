# Vlasov dynamics for fermions on phase space lattice

> Within the framework of stochastic one-body approach a simulation procedure to study the Vlasov dynamics for fermi system on phase space lattice is presented. To deal with fermions on lattice, the phase cell occupancy factors are taken to be either 1 or 0 in accordance with the Pauli exclusion principle. This has significant implications. First, the dynamical evolution does not alter initial temperature of the system and secondly, at finite temperature, the proper statistical behaviour related to the fluctuations over the samples is ensured. This method is applied for two distinct cases viz. the monopole vibration of cold dilute nucleus and the evolution of ring shaped matter distribution at finite temperature in the presence of radial flow. In the latter case, fragment multiplicities are found to be depend on the flow velocity. To check the reliability of the present calculations simulations have been performed with different choices of the grid size.

## Key claim
The paper presents a simulation method for Vlasov dynamics on a phase space lattice where occupancy factors are strictly binary (0 or 1), ensuring that the Pauli principle is obeyed exactly and that the initial temperature of the system is preserved during evolution; the method is claimed to maintain proper statistical fluctuations over samples regardless of the chosen phase-space cell size.

## Method
The phase space is discretized into cells of volume Δs, and each cell is either fully occupied (n=1) or empty (n=0) across an ensemble of samples, implementing a dichotomic stochastic process. The Vlasov equation is solved using a leap-frog algorithm where particles (filled cells) evolve on an integer grid, with the time step fixed by the condition that the Courant number C = (Δt/m)(Δp/Δx) equals unity to prevent two particles from occupying the same spatial cell. The mean-field potential is computed using a Skyrme interaction with Gaussian folding for smoothing, and the method is applied in two spatial dimensions to two test cases: monopole vibrations of a cold dilute nucleus and the fragmentation of a ring-shaped matter distribution with radial flow at finite temperature.

## Key numbers
- For the monopole vibration case, the Fermi momentum is taken as P_F = 260 MeV/c; the scale parameter η is chosen as ≈0.88 for Δp = 58 MeV/c and ≈0.75 for Δp = 74 MeV/c.
- Time steps used in the monopole vibration simulations: Δt ≈ 5.4 fm/c (Δp=58 MeV/c, Δx=1/3 fm), 7.1 fm/c (Δp=58 MeV/c, Δx=1/2 fm), 4.1 fm/c (Δp=74 MeV/c, Δx=1/3 fm), 6.5 fm/c (Δp=74 MeV/c, Δx=1/2 fm).
- The mass number of the nucleus in the monopole vibration study lies within the range 64±2, with initial radius R_m = 6 fm.
- The estimated error in total kinetic energy per particle due to momentum truncation is (Δp)^2/(2m) ≈ 1.5 MeV for Δp = 60 MeV/c; within 200 fm/c the error in E/A lies within 1 MeV.
- For the ring fragmentation case, the average density is ⟨ρ⟩ = 1/2 ρ₀, with the annular region between radii 5 fm and 15 fm; temperature is T = 7 MeV, and spatial density fluctuations of about 10% are introduced.
- Two flow velocities are used: v_fl = 0.12c and v_fl = 0.9c.
- For v_fl = 0.12c, the fragmentation time t_frag ≈ 50 fm/c and the onset time t_onset ≈ 125 fm/c; for lower flow velocities, t_onset ≈ 200 fm/c.
- The number of particles needed to describe normal nuclear matter in a 1 fm box is 603 for Δp=58 MeV/c and Δx=1/3 fm, and 148 for Δp=74 MeV/c and Δx=1/2 fm.

## Context
This work extends the stochastic one-body approach to nuclear dynamics based on the Boltzmann-Langevin equation, as developed by Ayik and Gregoire (1990) and Randrup and Remaud (1990), by implementing a binary occupancy scheme on a phase space lattice that strictly enforces the Pauli principle. The method is applied to two test cases previously studied in the literature: monopole vibrations (Reinhard and collaborators, 1990s) and fragmentation of toroidal structures in central collisions (Berlanger and collaborators, 1990s; Pal and collaborators, 1990s). The approach enables the study of fluctuation growth in hot Fermi systems within Vlasov dynamics without the dissipation errors that plague test-particle methods, and provides a framework for estimating fragment multiplicities in heavy-ion collisions that is independent of the chosen grid size.
