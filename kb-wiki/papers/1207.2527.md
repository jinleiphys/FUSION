# Neutrino-driven wind simulations and nucleosynthesis of heavy elements

> Neutrino-driven winds, which follow core-collapse supernova explosions, present a fascinating nuclear astrophysics problem that requires understanding advanced astrophysics simulations, the properties of matter and neutrino interactions under extreme conditions, the structure and reactions of exotic nuclei, and comparisons against forefront astronomical observations. The neutrino-driven wind has attracted vast attention over the last 20 years as it was suggested to be a candidate for the astrophysics site where half of the heavy elements are produced via the r-process. In this review, we summarize our present understanding of neutrino-driven winds from the dynamical and nucleosynthesis perspectives. Rapid progress has been made during recent years in understanding the wind with improved simulations and better micro physics. The current status of the fields is that hydrodynamical simulations do not reach the extreme conditions necessary for the r-process and the proton or neutron richness of the wind remains to be investigated in more detail. However, nucleosynthesis studies and observations point already to neutrino-driven winds to explain the origin of lighter heavy elements, such as Sr, Y, Zr.

## Key claim

Neutrino-driven winds are a promising site for producing lighter heavy elements (such as Sr, Y, Zr) via the weak r-process or the nu-p-process, but current hydrodynamic simulations do not reach the extreme conditions (high entropy, fast expansion, low electron fraction) necessary for a full r-process up to uranium.

## Method

The authors review steady-state and analytic models of neutrino-driven winds (e.g., Qian and Woosley 1996) that describe the outflow using mass, momentum, and energy conservation equations with neutrino heating, and they examine parametric nucleosynthesis trajectories that track the evolution from nuclear statistical equilibrium through charged-particle freeze-out to neutron capture processes. They also discuss recent long-time hydrodynamic simulations (e.g., Arcones, Janka, and Scheck 2007; Fischer et al. 2010; Huedepohl et al. 2010) that include wind termination shocks and improved neutrino opacities.

## Key numbers

- Initial proto-neutron star temperatures: kT approximately 30-50 MeV
- Neutrino-driven wind lasts for the first 10-20 seconds after the explosion
- The condition for a neutron-rich wind requires electron antineutrino and neutrino energies to satisfy epsilon_nu_bar - epsilon_nu_e greater than approximately 4 Delta, where Delta = 1.293 MeV (the neutron-proton mass difference), i.e., approximately 5 MeV
- For a strong r-process, conditions require entropy S greater than 150 k_B per nucleon and expansion time scale tau less than 2 ms
- The triple-alpha reaction and seed formation become effective at densities around 5 x 10^6 g cm^-3
- Charged-particle reactions freeze out at temperatures around 10^9 K
- The reverse shock radius scales as R_rs proportional to sqrt(M_dot v_w / P_rs)
- In medium modifications could change the electron fraction toward more neutron-rich conditions with Y_e approximately 0.45

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957) on nucleosynthesis in supernovae, and builds on analytic wind models by Qian and Woosley (1996) and steady-state models by Otsuki, Tagoshi, and colleagues (2000) and Thompson, Burrows, and Meyer (2001). The paper summarizes the current understanding that spherically symmetric neutrino-driven winds cannot produce a full r-process (as shown by Huedepohl et al. 2010 and Fischer et al. 2010), but can explain the lighter heavy element primary process (LEPP) contribution proposed by Travaglio, Gallino, and colleagues (2004), and enables future work on neutrino oscillations, magnetic fields, and in-medium modifications to neutrino opacities that might alter the wind electron fraction.
