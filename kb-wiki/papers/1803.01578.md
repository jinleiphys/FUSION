# Elliptic flow of hadrons via quark coalescence mechanism using Boltzmann transport equation for Pb+Pb collision at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$=2.76 TeV

> Elliptic flow of hadrons observed at relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments at Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) and Large Hadron Collider (LHC), provides us an important signature of possible de-confinement transition from hadronic phase to partonic phase. However, hadronization processes of de-confined partons back into final hadrons are found to play a vital role in the observed hadronic flow. In the present work, we use coalescence mechanism also known as Recombination (ReCo) to combine quarks into hadrons. To get there, we have used Boltzmann transport equation in relaxation time approximation to transport the quarks into equilibration and finally to freeze-out surface, before coalescence takes place. A Boltzmann-Gibbs Blast Wave (BGBW) function is taken as an equilibrium function to get the final distribution and a power-like function to describe the initial distributions of partons produced in heavy-ion collisions. In the present work, we try to estimate the elliptic flow of identified hadrons such as $π$, $K$, $p$ etc., produced in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV at the LHC for different centralities. The elliptic flow ($v_2$) of identified hadrons seems to be described quite well in the available $p_{\rm T}$ range. After the evolution of quarks until freeze-out time, has been calculated using BTE-RTA, the approach used in this paper consists of combining two or more quarks to explain the produced hadrons at intermediate momenta regions. The formalism is found to describe elliptic flow of hadrons produced in Pb+Pb collisions to a large extent.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the elliptic flow ($v_2$) of identified hadrons produced in Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV can be described to a large extent by combining a Boltzmann transport equation in relaxation time approximation (BTE-RTA) for quark evolution with a quark coalescence (ReCo) hadronization mechanism, but only in the intermediate transverse momentum region ($p_T \geq$ 2 GeV/c) and with the explicit caveat that the model fails to explain data for $p_T <$ 1.0 GeV/c due to the neglect of resonance decays and hadronic medium effects.

## Method

The authors used the Boltzmann transport equation in relaxation time approximation (BTE-RTA) to evolve initial parton distributions, obtained from leading-order pQCD calculations with a power-like parametrization, toward a Boltzmann-Gibbs Blast Wave (BGBW) equilibrium distribution at freeze-out. After transport, quarks were combined into mesons and baryons via a coalescence (ReCo) mechanism using Gaussian Wigner functions, with the final elliptic flow $v_2$ computed from the azimuthal dependence of the hadron spectra at mid-rapidity for various centralities of Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV.

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out temperature $T_f$ = 0.095 GeV for most central (0-5)% collisions and 0.11 GeV for most peripheral (50-60)% collisions.
- Nuclear overlap function $T_{AA}(b)$ = 260.50 fm$^{-2}$ for 0-5% centrality and 13.1 fm$^{-2}$ for 50-60% centrality at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}$ = 2.76 TeV.
- Quark masses used for initial distributions: $m_u$ = 2.3 MeV, $m_d$ = 4.5 MeV, $m_s$ = 95 MeV, $m_c$ = 1.25 GeV.
- Width of the Gaussian Wigner function fixed at $2\sigma^2$ = 0.0009 for mesons and 0.04 for baryons.
- A $p_T$ cut of 2 GeV/c was taken for jet production in the pQCD calculation.
- A factor $K$ = 2.5 was used to incorporate NLO processes in the pQCD cross-section.

## Context

This work extends prior transport and coalescence approaches, such as those by Fries et al. (2003) and Greco et al. (2003), which used a two-component model of thermalized partons at low $p_T$ and fragmentation at high $p_T$, by instead applying BTE-RTA to evolve initially non-equilibrated parton distributions from pQCD before coalescence. The study focuses on the intermediate $p_T$ region (2-5 GeV/c) and neglects gluon contributions, hadronic decay contributions, and hadronic medium effects, which the authors identify as limitations to be addressed in future work. The results enable further investigation of the correlation between radial flow and relaxation time parameters and their centrality dependence in heavy-ion collisions at LHC energies.
