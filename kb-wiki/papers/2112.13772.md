# Low-energy enhancement in the magnetic dipole $γ$-ray strength functions of heavy nuclei

> A low-energy enhancement (LEE), observed experimentally in the $γ$-ray strength function ($γ$SF) describing the decay of compound nuclei, would have profound effects on $r$-process nucleosynthesis if it persists in heavy neutron-rich nuclei. The LEE was shown to be a feature of the magnetic dipole ($M1)$ strength function in configuration-interaction shell-model calculations in medium-mass nuclei. However, its existence in heavy nuclei and its evolution with neutron number remain open questions. Here, using a combination of many-body methods, we find the LEE in the $M1$ $γ$SFs of heavy samarium nuclei. In particular, we use the static-path plus random-phase approximation (SPA+RPA), which includes static and small-amplitude quantal fluctuations beyond the mean field. Using the SPA+RPA strength as a prior, we apply the maximum-entropy method (MEM) to obtain finite-temperature $M1$ $γ$SFs from exact imaginary-time response functions calculated with the shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) method. We find that the slope of the LEE in samarium isotopes is roughly independent of the average initial energy over a wide range below the neutron separation energy. As the neutron number increases, strength transfers to a low-energy excitation, which we interpret as the scissors mode built on top of excited states.

# Low-energy enhancement in the magnetic dipole γ-ray strength functions of heavy nuclei

## Key claim
The low-energy enhancement (LEE) is present in the magnetic dipole γ-ray strength functions of heavy samarium nuclei (148-154Sm), and its slope is roughly independent of the average initial energy over a wide range below the neutron separation energy. This is the first theoretical description of a LEE in the M1 γSF of heavy nuclei, but the authors note that whether such an enhancement persists in heavy neutron-rich nuclei near the neutron drip line remains an open question.

## Method
The authors combined the static-path plus random-phase approximation (SPA+RPA), which includes both static and small-amplitude quantal fluctuations beyond the mean field, with exact shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) calculations of imaginary-time response functions. They applied the maximum-entropy method (MEM) using the SPA+RPA strength as a prior to obtain finite-temperature M1 γ-ray strength functions from the SMMC response functions. The calculations were performed for even-mass samarium isotopes 148-154Sm using a pairing plus quadrupole two-body interaction within a configuration-interaction shell-model framework.

## Key numbers
- The M1 γSF calculations were performed for four even-mass samarium isotopes: 148Sm, 150Sm, 152Sm, and 154Sm.
- The Lorentzian width used to replace delta functions in the σ-dependent strength functions was fixed at ε = 0.2 MeV.
- A small excitation around ω ≈ 2 MeV emerged in the γSF as neutron number increased, interpreted as the scissors mode built on excited states.
- An excitation at ω ≈ 6 MeV was observed with strength roughly independent of neutron number.
- The LEE structure was observed below Eγ ≈ 2 MeV in each of the isotopes.
- The TB parameter (from exponential fit B0 e^{-Eγ/TB}) remained roughly constant over a wide range of average initial energies Ei for each isotope, with somewhat larger values in the more deformed isotopes 152Sm and 154Sm.

## Context
This work extends configuration-interaction shell-model studies of the LEE in medium-mass nuclei (Schwengner 2013, Brown 2014, Karampagia 2017, Sieja 2017, Mütdbo 2018) to heavy nuclei, where conventional CI shell-model diagonalization methods are computationally prohibitive. The finite-temperature quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA) has been applied to γSF calculations (Litvinova 2013, 2018; Wibowo 2019; Yuksel 2020), but has limitations in only including small-amplitude quantal fluctuations around the mean field. This work enables future studies of the LEE in heavy neutron-rich nuclei, which would have profound effects on r-process nucleosynthesis calculations if the LEE persists near the neutron drip line.
