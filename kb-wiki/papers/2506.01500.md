# Thermal dilepton production within conformal viscous Gubser flow

> By employing the Gubser solutions of causal relativistic second-order Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics, we study the thermal dilepton production from heavy-ion collisions, considering the transverse expansion of the viscous hot QCD medium along with longitudinal boost-invariance. We analyze the evolution of the temperature and shear stress profiles of the QCD matter under Gubser flow for different values of the associated parameter $q$ (inverse length scale). We study the dilepton production using leading order Born rates from QGP and hadronic sectors under Gubser geometry. Viscous modified dilepton rate is calculated using the first-order Chapman-Enskog (CE) like non-equilibrium correction of the particle distribution function. Our study indicates that lower values of $q$ result in the enhancement of the emitted dilepton spectra. We also determine the effective temperature of the hot QCD medium from the inverse slope of transverse mass spectra, for different $q$. We find that the effective temperature determined from the dilepton spectra for a smaller system to be higher. Further, we compare the strength of CE like and Grad's viscous correction to the ideal dilepton spectra and find that CE type viscous corrections are well behaved compared to that of Grad's in the presence of transverse flow.

## Key claim

The study shows that lower values of the Gubser parameter \(q\) (inverse length scale, representing smaller transverse system size) enhance the emitted thermal dilepton spectra, and that the effective temperature determined from the dilepton spectra is higher for smaller systems; however, these conclusions are conditional on the use of conformal second-order Israel-Stewart hydrodynamics with a specific initial temperature of 0.5 GeV and shear viscosity to entropy density ratio \(4\pi(\eta/s)=1\).

## Method

The authors solved the causal relativistic second-order Israel-Stewart hydrodynamic equations under Gubser flow, which incorporates both longitudinal boost-invariance and transverse expansion, to obtain temperature and shear stress profiles of the hot QCD medium. They then computed thermal dilepton yields by integrating leading-order Born rates from the QGP (quark-antiquark annihilation) and hadronic (pion annihilation) sectors over the space-time history, using a first-order Chapman-Enskog like non-equilibrium correction to the particle distribution function to account for viscous effects. The calculations were performed for different values of the Gubser parameter \(q\) (0.25, 0.35, and 0.45 fm\(^{-1}\)) and compared with the one-dimensional Bjorken flow limit.

## Key numbers

*   Initial conditions: \(T(\tau_0, r=0) = 0.5\) GeV, \(\pi(\tau_0, r=0) = 0\), and \(\tau_0 = 0.4\) fm; in Gubser coordinates this corresponds to \(\hat{T}(\rho_0) = 1.02\).
*   Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio fixed at \(4\pi(\eta/s) = 1\) for the main analysis, with additional calculations at \(4\pi(\eta/s) = 2\).
*   Gubser parameter values used: \(q = 0.25, 0.35, \text{ and } 0.45\) fm\(^{-1}\).
*   For the hadronic dilepton rate, the pion form factor used \(m_\rho = 775\) MeV and \(\Gamma_\rho = 149\) MeV.
*   The massless pion approximation (\(m_\pi = 0\)) for the hadronic dilepton rate was found to have errors well within 10% for most kinematic regions, except at low invariant mass and low transverse momentum where errors reached approximately 50%.

## Context

This work extends the study of thermal electromagnetic probes from heavy-ion collisions by applying Gubser flow, which was previously used to study elliptic flow (Hatta, 2014) and thermal photon production (Paquet, 2023) without viscosity. It contrasts with the simpler one-dimensional Bjorken flow model (Bjorken, 1982) by including transverse expansion, and it builds on the second-order Israel-Stewart solutions within Gubser flow derived by Marrochio (2013). The results enable future comparisons of viscous dilepton spectra with experimental data from small collision systems and provide a framework for testing the convergence of different viscous correction schemes (Chapman-Enskog versus Grad's 14-moment method) in the presence of transverse flow.
