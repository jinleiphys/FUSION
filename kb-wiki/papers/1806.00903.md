# Neutron to Dark Matter Decay in Neutron Stars

> Recent proposals have suggested that a previously unknown decay mode of the neutron into a dark matter particle could solve the long lasting measurement problem of the neutron decay width. We show that, if the dark particle in neutron decay is the major component of the dark matter in the universe, this proposal is in disagreement with modern astro-physical data concerning neutron star masses.

## Key claim

If the dark fermion produced in the proposed neutron decay channel is the major component of dark matter in the universe, the hypothesis is in disagreement with modern astrophysical data on neutron star masses; even if the dark fermion is given a repulsive vector self-interaction, the required coupling is so large that it is ruled out by recent astrophysical measurements.

## Method

The authors model neutron star cores using the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model for infinite nuclear matter, solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to obtain mass-radius relations. They compare the standard equation of state (EOS) with a modified version where neutrons can decay to a dark fermion \(\chi\) (taken as degenerate with the neutron) under chemical equilibrium \(\mu_n = \mu_\chi\), and then introduce a repulsive vector self-interaction among the dark fermions with varying coupling strengths.

## Key numbers

- The maximum mass of a neutron star with dark matter present is approximately \(0.7\,M_\odot\).
- When requiring that the star with dark matter is a decay product of a normal neutron star (conserving total baryon number plus dark matter number), the maximum mass is \(0.58\,M_\odot\).
- The maximum mass reaches 2 solar masses only when the ratio \(g_V/m_V\) for the dark matter vector interaction is 10 times larger than the ratio \(g_\omega/m_\omega\) for the neutron–omega vertex.
- The neutron mass is taken as 939 MeV, the dark fermion mass as 939 MeV, and the allowed mass range for the dark fermion is \(937.900\text{ MeV} < m_\chi < 938.543\text{ MeV}\).
- The QMC model is fitted to saturation density \(0.16\text{ fm}^{-3}\), binding energy \(-15.8\text{ MeV}\), and symmetry energy \(30\text{ MeV}\).

## Context

This work directly tests the proposal by Fornal et al. (2018) that a neutron decay to a dark fermion could resolve the neutron lifetime discrepancy, and extends earlier arguments by Baym et al. and Reddy et al. that such a decay would drastically reduce neutron star maximum masses. The authors show that even with a repulsive vector self-interaction (as considered in a prior study), the required coupling is ruled out by astrophysical constraints from D'Amico et al. (2018) and the "Barbecue" data, unless the dark particle is unrelated to the dominant dark matter component. This enables future work to focus on other explanations for the neutron lifetime puzzle, such as neutron–mirror neutron oscillations.
