# Shock waves in colliding Fermi gases at finite temperature

> We study the formation and the dynamics of a shock wave originating from the collision between two ultracold clouds of strongly interacting fermions as observed at a lower temperature in an experiment by Joseph et al. [Phys. Rev. Lett. 106, 150401 (2011)]. We use the Boltzmann equation within the test-particle method to describe the evolution of the system in the normal phase. We also show a direct comparison with the hydrodynamic approach and insist on the necessity of including a shear viscosity and a thermal conductivity term in the equations to prevent unphysical behavior from taking place.

## Key claim
The Boltzmann equation with test particles reproduces the shock wave dynamics observed in ultracold Fermi gas collisions at finite temperature (T/T_F = 0.3), but the hydrodynamic extraction of viscosity from density profiles is unreliable without also including thermal conductivity to prevent unphysical behavior like a persistent density hole at the collision center.

## Method
The authors solved the semiclassical Boltzmann equation with elastic two-body collisions and Pauli blocking using the test-particle method, simulating 5×10^4 test particles per run averaged over 48 runs. They modeled a 3D gas of 2×10^5 ⁶Li atoms in two spin states with s-wave scattering, initially split by a repulsive barrier in an elongated harmonic trap (ω_⟂/2π = 437 Hz, ω_z/2π = 27.7 Hz), then let the two clouds collide after barrier removal. They compared these results to a 1D hydrodynamic model (equations for particle number, momentum, and energy conservation) that included shear viscosity and heat conductivity as source terms, using the equation of state of an ideal Fermi gas with harmonic radial confinement.

## Key numbers
- Initial temperature: T/T_F = 0.3 (T_F = 0.706 μK)
- Interaction strength: (k_F a)^{-1} = -0.1
- Total atom number: N = 2 × 10^5
- Trap frequencies: ω_⟂/2π = 437 Hz, ω_z/2π = 27.7 Hz
- Best-fit hydrodynamic parameters: dimensionless shear viscosity η̃ = 9, dimensionless heat conductivity κ̃ = 34
- Upstream Mach number at t = 2 ω̄^{-1}: Ma ≈ 2.6, decreasing to Ma ≈ 1.6 at t = 6 ω̄^{-1}
- In the experiment by Joseph et al. (2011), a viscosity parameter of η̃ = 7.5 was obtained at nearly zero temperature

## Context
This work extends the experimental study of shock waves in colliding ultracold Fermi gases by Joseph et al. (2011), who used zero-temperature viscous hydrodynamics to extract viscosity. The authors show that at finite temperature, the hydrodynamic description requires both shear viscosity and thermal conductivity to match the Boltzmann results, unlike the purely viscous or dispersive approaches used by Salasnich et al. (2011), Ancilotto et al. (2012), and Wen et al. (2015). The study enables future quantitative comparisons with finite-temperature experiments by Roof et al. (2018) and motivates development of anisotropic fluid dynamics as proposed by Bluhm et al. (2015) to better describe the shock front structure.
