# (Two) Open Questions in Stellar Nuclear Physics

> No doubt, among the most exciting discoveries of the third millennium thus far are {\bf Oscillations of Massive Neutrinos} and {\bf Dark Energy} that leads to an accelerated expansion of the Universe. Accordingly, Nuclear Physics is presented with two extraordinary challenges: the need for precise (5% or better) prediction of solar neutrino fluxes within the Standard Solar Model, and the need for an accurate (5% or better) understanding of stellar evolution and in particular of Type Ia super nova that are used as cosmological standard candle. In contrast, much confusion is found in the field with contradicting data and strong statements of accuracy that can not be supported by current data. We discuss an experimental program to address these challenges and disagreements.

## Key claim

Nuclear physics must provide solar neutrino flux predictions and stellar evolution models (especially for Type Ia supernovae) with 5% or better accuracy, but current data are contradictory and strong claims of precision (such as the Seattle group's stated 2.5% theoretical uncertainty on \(S_{17}(0)\)) cannot be supported by the measurements.

## Method

The paper reviews the experimental status of two critical nuclear reaction cross sections: the \(^{7}\text{Be}(p,\gamma)^{8}\text{B}\) reaction (parameterized by \(S_{17}(0)\)) and the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction (which determines the C/O ratio after helium burning). It compares existing data sets from GSI, Weizmann, and Seattle for \(S_{17}(0)\) and from TRIUMF, Hale, Notre Dame, and Stuttgart for the \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) reaction, highlighting disagreements in slopes, extrapolated values, and claimed uncertainties. The paper then proposes a new experimental approach using photodisintegration of \(^{16}\text{O}\) at the High Intensity Gamma Source (HI\(\gamma\)S) facility with an Optical Readout Time Projection Chamber to measure both E1 and E2 astrophysical S-factors down to 700 keV.

## Key numbers

- The \(^{8}\text{B}\) solar neutrino flux was measured with 7.3% accuracy by SNO and extracted from a global analysis with 4% accuracy.
- The \(S_{34}(0)\) astrophysical S-factor is still known with an error of 9%.
- The d-wave correction to \(S_{17}(0)\) reduces its value by as much as 15%, precluding accurate extrapolation despite the Seattle group's claim of 2.5% theoretical uncertainty.
- The TRIUMF group quotes the E1 astrophysical cross section factor for \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) with 25% uncertainty, while Hale extracts a value eight times smaller.
- The Notre Dame group extracted the E2 S-factor with claimed 20% accuracy, but the result is a factor of 2.5 smaller than the Stuttgart group's value.
- The \(^{12}\text{C}(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}\text{O}\) cross section needs to be known at approximately 300 keV, but has been measured only down to approximately 1.2 MeV.

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing effort to provide nuclear inputs for the Standard Solar Model (SSM) as defined by Adelberger et al. (1998) and for stellar evolution models of massive stars and Type Ia supernovae. It directly contradicts the strong accuracy claims of the Seattle group (2003) on \(S_{17}(0)\) and highlights the unresolved "holy grail" problem of the C/O ratio first declared by Willie Fowler twenty years prior. The proposed HI\(\gamma\)S experiment aims to resolve the factor-of-eight discrepancy between TRIUMF and Hale for the E1 S-factor and the factor-of-2.5 discrepancy between Notre Dame and Stuttgart for the E2 S-factor, enabling a physical understanding of the Phillips relationship (Phillips, 1993) that underpins the use of Type Ia supernovae as cosmological standard candles.
