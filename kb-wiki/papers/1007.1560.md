# Solving the Bethe-Salpeter Equation in Euclidean Space

> Different approaches to solve the spinor-spinor Bethe-Salpeter (BS) equation in Euclidean space are considered. It is argued that the complete set of Dirac matrices is the most appropriate basis to define the partial amplitudes and to solve numerically the resulting system of equations with realistic interaction kernels. Other representations can be obtained by performing proper unitary transformations. A generalization of the iteration method for finding the energy spectrum of the BS equation is discussed and examples of concrete calculations are presented. Comparison of relativistic calculations with available experimental data and with corresponding non relativistic results together with an analysis of the role of Lorentz boost effects and relativistic corrections are presented. A novel method related to the use of hyperspherical harmonics is considered for a representation of the vertex functions suitable for numerical calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the complete set of Dirac matrices is the most appropriate basis for solving the spinor-spinor Bethe-Salpeter equation numerically in Euclidean space with realistic interaction kernels, but notes that the physical interpretation of results in this representation is not transparent and requires unitary transformation to a spin-angular basis for meaningful comparison with nonrelativistic approaches.

## Method

The authors decompose the Bethe-Salpeter amplitude over the complete set of Dirac matrices (scalar, pseudoscalar, vector, axial-vector, tensor components), then expand the coefficient functions over spherical and vector angular harmonics to obtain a system of two-dimensional integral equations. After performing a Wick rotation to Euclidean space, they solve the resulting system by iteration, fixing the binding energy at the experimentally known value and searching for the coupling constants that enter linearly. They also develop a one-iteration approximation method using nonrelativistic wave functions as trial functions to estimate relativistic corrections, and generalize the iteration procedure via an "exhausting" method to find excited state energy spectra.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron mass \(M_d\) enters nonlinearly in the integral equations (via the scalar part of two propagators \(D(p_0,\mathbf{p})\)), requiring the iteration procedure to search for coupling constants rather than the mass eigenvalue
- For the deuteron, eight partial amplitudes are obtained in the Dirac representation, classified into three groups: two main components with positive \(\rho\)-spin (\({}^{3}S_1^{++}\) and \({}^{3}D_1^{++}\)), four negative \(P\)-waves with mixed \(\rho\)-spin, and two negligible components with both \(\rho\)-spins negative (\({}^{3}S_1^{--}\) and \({}^{3}D_1^{--}\))
- Relativistic effects become significant at laboratory momenta \(P_{lab} \ge 0.35-0.4\) GeV/c for backward elastic \(pD\) scattering
- The pion-nucleon coupling constant used in the one-iteration approximation is \(g_\pi^2 \approx 14.5\)
- The cross section for backward elastic \(pD\) scattering includes a Lorentz boost factor \(\mathcal{L}(P_{lab}) = \left(1 + \frac{P_{lab}^2}{4m^2} + \frac{7P_{lab}^4}{8m^4} + \cdots\right)\)

## Context

This work extends the numerical solution of the Bethe-Salpeter equation in Euclidean space previously developed by Rupp and Tjon (various references), and by the group of Maris and collaborators for quark-antiquark systems. The authors compare their results with nonrelativistic Schrodinger calculations using Bonn and Paris potentials, and with the three-dimensional Gross equation. The method enables calculation of deuteron properties in electromagnetic, weak, and hadronic processes, including backward elastic \(pD\) scattering and the reaction \(pD \to (pp)n\), where relativistic calculations show better agreement with experimental data than nonrelativistic approaches. The exhausting method generalizes the iteration procedure to find excited state spectra for heavier mesons such as \(J/\Psi\) and \(D\) mesons.
