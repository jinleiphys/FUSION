# Constraining the gravitational binding energy of PSR J0737-3039B using terrestrial nuclear data

> We show that the gravitational binding energy of a neutron star of a given mass is correlated with the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy at 1-2 times nuclear saturation density for equations of state without significant softening (i.e., those that predict maximum masses $M_{\rm max} > 1.44M_{\odot}$ in line with the largest accurately measured neutron star mass). Applying recent laboratory constraints on the slope of the symmetry energy to this correlation we extract a constraint on the baryon mass of the lower mass member of the double pulsar binary system, PSR J0737-3039B. We compare with independent constraints derived from modeling the progenitor star of J0737-3039B up to and through its collapse under the assumption that it formed in an electron capture supernova. The two sets of constraints are consistent only if $L \lesssim$ 70 MeV.

## Key claim
The gravitational binding energy (and thus baryon mass) of a neutron star of fixed gravitational mass is correlated with the slope of the nuclear symmetry energy at 1-2 times nuclear saturation density, but only for equations of state that predict maximum masses above 1.44 solar masses (i.e., without significant softening). Using this correlation, the authors find that the electron-capture supernova formation scenario for PSR J0737-3039B is consistent with terrestrial nuclear data only if the symmetry energy slope parameter L is less than about 70 MeV.

## Method
The authors computed neutron star models by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for a fixed gravitational mass of 1.2489 solar masses (the measured mass of pulsar B), using 63 Skyrme-Hartree-Fock parametrizations, 29 relativistic mean-field models, 6 microscopic potential-model equations of state, the MDI (modified Gogny) interaction with variable symmetry energy slope, and 2 quark-meson coupling models. They then plotted the baryon mass (equivalent to gravitational mass plus binding energy) against the symmetry energy slope parameter L for each equation of state, and compared the resulting correlation with two independent constraints on the baryon mass from electron-capture supernova progenitor modeling (Podsiadlowski 2005 and Kitaura 2006) and with experimental constraints on L from isospin diffusion, pygmy dipole resonance, neutron skin measurements, and surface symmetry energy analyses.

## Key numbers
- Gravitational mass of pulsar B: 1.2489 ± 0.0007 solar masses (measured)
- Gravitational mass of pulsar A: 1.3381 ± 0.0007 solar masses (measured)
- Orbital eccentricity: e = 0.088; estimated initial eccentricity e0 ≈ 0.11-0.12
- Mass expelled during supernova B (assuming symmetric explosion): ΔM ≈ 0.32 solar masses
- Transverse velocity of the system: ≈ 10 km/s
- Progenitor core mass range from Podsiadlowski (2005): 1.366 - 1.375 solar masses (baryon mass M2)
- Progenitor core mass from Kitaura (2006): 1.36 ± 0.002 solar masses (baryon mass M1)
- Conservative experimental constraint on L (L1): 25 < L < 115 MeV
- Isospin diffusion constraint on L (L2): 62 < L < 107 MeV (Chen 2005, Li 2005)
- Consistency between electron-capture constraints and nuclear data requires L ≲ 70 MeV
- Resulting baryon mass constraint from all current experimental data: 1.34 ≲ M_B ≲ 1.4 solar masses

## Context
This work extends the analysis of Podsiadlowski (2005), who first derived constraints on the neutron star equation of state from modeling the electron-capture supernova progenitor of pulsar B, and Kitaura (2006), whose simulation of an electron-capture supernova gave a slightly lower baryon mass. The authors connect these astrophysical constraints to terrestrial nuclear physics by demonstrating a correlation between the symmetry energy slope L and neutron star binding energy, building on earlier work by Lattimer (2001) and Steiner (2005) showing correlations between neutron star radius, pressure, and L. The results enable future experimental constraints on L from facilities such as FAIR, RIKEN, SPIRAL2/GANIL, CSR, FRIB, and the PREX experiment at JLab to directly test the electron-capture supernova formation scenario for double neutron star systems.
