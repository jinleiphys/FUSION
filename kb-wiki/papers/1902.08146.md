# Limiting magnetic field for minimal deformation of a magnetised neutron star

> In this work we study the structure of neutron stars under the effect of a poloidal magnetic field and determine the limiting highest magnetic field intensity which still allows a satisfactory description of magnetic neutron stars in the spherical symmetry regime. We describe different compositions of stars (nucleonic, hyperonic, and hybrid), using three state-of-the-art relativistic mean field models for the microscopic description of matter, which are in agreement with experimental and observational data. The structure of stars is described by the general relativistic solution of both Einstein's field equations assuming a spherical symmetry, and Einstein-Maxwell's field equations assuming an axi-symmetric deformation. We find a limiting magnetic moment of the order of $2\times 10^{31}$Am$^2$, which corresponds to magnetic fields of the order of 10$^{16}$ G at the surface, and $ \sim 10^{17}$ G at the centre of the star, above which the deformation due to the magnetic field is not negligible. We show that the intensity of the magnetic field developed in the star depends on the EoS, and, for a given baryonic mass and fixed magnetic moment, larger fields are attained with softer EoS. We also show that the appearance of exotic degrees of freedom, such as hyperons or a quark core, is disfavored in the presence of a very strong magnetic field. As a consequence, a highly magnetized nucleonic star may suffer an internal conversion due to the decay of the magnetic field, which could be accompanied by a sudden cooling of the star or a gamma ray burst.

## Key claim

The paper determines that a poloidal magnetic field induces a deformation (ratio of polar to equatorial radii) exceeding 2% only when the magnetic dipole moment is above approximately 2 × 10³¹ Am², corresponding to surface fields above ~10¹⁶ G and central fields above ~10¹⁷ G; below this threshold, spherical symmetry remains a satisfactory approximation for describing magnetised neutron stars.

## Method

The authors solved the coupled Einstein-Maxwell field equations using the LORENE numerical library, assuming a poloidal magnetic field configuration and an axi-symmetric metric (Maximal-Slicing-Quasi-Isotropic). They considered three different relativistic mean-field equations of state (NL3ωρ for nucleonic matter, MBF for hyperonic matter, and CMF for hybrid matter containing quarks), each unified with a BPS outer crust and a Thomas-Fermi inner crust. Families of stars were computed for fixed magnetic dipole moments, and single-star configurations with fixed baryonic mass M_b = 1.6 M⊙ were also analysed.

## Key numbers

- Limiting magnetic dipole moment: 2 × 10³¹ Am², above which deformation exceeds 2%.
- Corresponding surface polar magnetic field: ~10¹⁶ G.
- Corresponding central magnetic field: ~10¹⁷ G.
- For μ = 5 × 10³¹ Am², surface fields range from ~5 × 10¹⁶ G to 10¹⁷ G across the three models.
- For μ = 5 × 10³¹ Am², central fields range from ~2 × 10¹⁷ G to ~4 × 10¹⁷ G.
- For stars with M_g ≳ 1 M⊙, the ratio B_c/B_s is approximately 3–5.
- For a 2 M⊙ star at 1 kpc with B_s = 2 × 10¹⁶ G and spin frequency 1 Hz, the gravitational wave amplitude h₀ = 0.15 × 10⁻²⁴ and characteristic strain S = 1.5 × 10⁻²¹ Hz⁻¹/².
- Central density decreases by ≤ 0.04 fm⁻³ when μ increases from 0 to 10³² Am² for CMF stars.

## Context

This work extends earlier self-consistent studies of magnetised neutron stars by Bocquet et al. (1995) and Chatterjee et al. (2014), who first coupled magnetic fields to both the equation of state and stellar structure. It quantifies the deformation threshold more precisely than previous work, showing that for dipole moments below 10³² Am² the mass is not significantly affected, contrary to Bocquet et al. (1995) who considered higher moments. The results enable future studies of magnetic field decay triggering internal conversions (e.g., nucleonic to hyperonic or hybrid stars), which could produce sudden cooling or gamma-ray bursts, and provide estimates for gravitational wave detectability by next-generation detectors like BBO, DECIGO, and ALIA.
