# Adaptive Multi-resolution 3D Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov Solver for Nuclear Structure

> Complex many-body systems, such as triaxial and reflection-asymmetric nuclei, weakly-bound halo states, cluster configurations, nuclear fragments produced in heavy-ion fusion reactions, cold Fermi gases, and pasta phases in neutron star crust, they are all characterized by large sizes and complex topologies, in which many geometrical symmetries characteristic of ground-state configurations are broken. A tool of choice to study such complex forms of matter is an adaptive multi-resolution wavelet analysis. This method has generated much excitement since it provides a common framework linking many diversified methodologies across different fields, including signal processing, data compression, harmonic analysis and operator theory, fractals, and quantum field theory. To describe complex superfluid many-fermion systems, we introduce an adaptive pseudo-spectral method for solving self-consistent equations of nuclear density functional theory in three dimensions, without symmetry restrictions. The new adaptive multi-resolution Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) solver {\madnesshfb} is benchmarked against a two-dimensional coordinate-space solver {\hfbax} based on B-spline technique and three-dimensional solver {\hfodd} based on the harmonic oscillator basis expansion. Several examples are considered, including self-consistent HFB problem for spin-polarized trapped cold fermions and Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (+BCS) problem for triaxial deformed nuclei. The new {\madnesshfb} framework has many attractive features when applied to nuclear and atomic problems involving many-particle superfluid systems. Of particular interest are weakly-bound nuclear configurations close to particle drip lines, strongly elongated and dinuclear configurations such as those present in fission and heavy ion fusion, and exotic pasta phases that appear in the neutron star crust.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new adaptive multi-resolution Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) solver, MADNESS-HFB, that can solve self-consistent nuclear density functional theory equations in three dimensions without symmetry restrictions, but the benchmark results show that the method is currently demonstrated only for cold fermion systems and Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (+BCS) problems for triaxial nuclei, not yet for full HFB calculations of nuclei.

## Method

The solver uses Alpert's multiwavelet basis functions constructed from Legendre polynomials within the Multiresolution Adaptive Numerical Environment for Scientific Simulations (MADNESS) framework, combining multiresolution analysis with low-separation rank approximations of Green's functions based on Gaussian expansions. The HFB equations are rewritten in Lippmann-Schwinger integral form and solved iteratively using convolution operations, with the algorithm employing a parallel task-based computing model using MPI between nodes and POSIX threads within nodes. Benchmarking was performed against the 2D coordinate-space solver HFB-AX (based on B-splines) for cold fermion systems and against the 3D harmonic oscillator basis solver HFODD for triaxial nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For 10 particles in a spherical trap (SLDA, trap units): MADNESS-HFB total energy Et = 18.5641, chemical potential λ = 2.24917; HFB-AX gives Et = 18.5639, λ = 2.24916
- For 10 particles with polarization P=0.1 in a spherical trap (ASLDA): MADNESS-HFB total energy Et = 19.0436, average chemical potential (λ↑+λ↓)/2 = 2.1684; HFB-AX gives Et = 19.0443, (λ↑+λ↓)/2 = 2.1683
- For 100 particles with polarization P=0.2 in an elongated trap with aspect ratio η=16: simulation used box size L=120 (trap units), involved about 2,000 eigenstates and 5,000 cores on Titan supercomputer, taking about 4 hours to reach convergence
- For triaxial 110Mo ground state (Skyrme HF, SkM* parameterization): MADNESS-HF converged total binding energy Et = −922.119 MeV, mass rms radius Rrms = 4.6697 fm, mass quadrupole moments Q20 = 913.69 fm² and |Q22| = 368.88 fm²; HFODD with 1540 basis states gives Et = −921.932 MeV, Rrms = 4.6697 fm, Q20 = 913.58 fm², |Q22| = 368.48 fm²
- MADNESS-HF calculations used wavelet order p=9 with requested truncation precision ε=10⁻⁷ in a 3D box with L=50 fm

## Context

This work extends the MADNESS computational framework, previously developed for computational chemistry problems by Harrison et al. (2004) and Fann et al. (2009), to nuclear physics and cold atom systems. It contrasts with traditional nuclear HFB solvers based on harmonic oscillator basis expansion (such as HFODD by Schunck et al. (2012) and HFBTHO by Stoitsov et al. (2005)) and 2D coordinate-space B-spline solvers (such as HFB-AX by Pei et al. (2008)), which either require huge bases for weakly-bound systems or are computationally challenging for fully 3D calculations. The new framework enables future studies of weakly-bound nuclear configurations near drip lines, strongly elongated and dinuclear configurations in fission and heavy ion fusion, and exotic pasta phases in neutron star crusts.
