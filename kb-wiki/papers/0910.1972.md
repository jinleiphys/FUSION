# Boson stars: Chemical potential and quark condensates

> We study the properties of a star made of self-gravitating bosons gas in a mean-field approximation. A generalized set of Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov(TOV) equations is derived to incorporate the effect of chemical-potential in the general relativistic frame work. The metric-dependence of the chemical-potential gives a new class of solutions for the boson stars. It is demonstrated that the maximum mass and radius of the star change in a significant way when the effect of finite chemical-potential is considered. We also discuss the case of a boson star made of quark-condensates. It is found that when the self-interaction between the condensates is small as compared to their mass, the typical density is too high to form a diquark-boson star. Our results indicate that the star of quark-condensate may be formed in a low-density and high-pressure regime.

# Boson stars: Chemical potential and quark condensates

## Key claim

The authors derive a generalized set of Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations that incorporate the metric dependence of chemical potential, showing that finite chemical potential can significantly alter the maximum mass and radius of boson stars. However, for diquark-condensate boson stars, the stable equilibrium solutions require densities too high for the condensate to exist, unless the self-interaction is sufficiently large to allow a low-density, high-pressure regime.

## Method

The authors start from a scalar field Lagrangian with a chemical potential term and a quartic self-interaction, then derive the energy-momentum tensor and equation of state in the mean-field approximation (dropping spatial derivative terms of the scalar field). They combine the standard TOV equations with the Tolman condition for thermal equilibrium in a gravitational field and the Gibbs-Duhem relation to obtain a third differential equation for the radial evolution of the chemical potential. The resulting system of three coupled equations is solved numerically for various central pressures and central chemical potentials, imposing positivity of energy density and a no-node condition on the density profile.

## Key numbers

- For a non-interacting boson star with mass ~100 GeV, the critical mass is ~10^9 kg (~10^{-21} solar masses) and radius ~10^{-22} neutron star radii, giving a density ~10^45 times that of a neutron star.
- For a diquark condensate with mass 0.5-1 GeV, the corresponding star density would be ~10^41 times that of a neutron star.
- The maximum mass scaling for the interacting case (g ≠ 0, μ = 0) is given by M_0 ≈ (2/π) √Λ (M_planck^2 / m), where Λ = g M_planck^2 / (2π m^2).
- For the diquark case, the chemical potential range considered is μ ≈ 400-500 MeV with a running coupling constant of 3.56.
- The dimensionless radius unit is R_0 = G M_sun = 1.47 km, and mass is in units of solar mass M_sun.
- For central chemical potential μ_c = 1.5, the physical density profile is around 30 times larger than nuclear density.

## Context

This work extends the standard boson star formalism of Kaup (1955) and Ruffini and Bonazzola (1969), which considered only zero chemical potential, and the interacting boson star solutions of Colpi et al. (1986), which included self-interaction but no chemical potential. The authors incorporate the Tolman condition for chemical potential in curved spacetime, previously applied in other contexts by Bilic (1999), into the boson star framework. The analysis of diquark condensate stars builds on the BCS-BEC crossover scenario described by Pirner (2007) and extends earlier work on diquark stars that did not consider Bose-Einstein condensation effects. The results suggest that stable diquark-condensate boson stars are unlikely in the parameter regime studied, which constrains the possible existence of such objects and points toward the need for either stronger self-interactions or alternative formation mechanisms.
