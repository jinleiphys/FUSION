# Charm content of the proton: An analytic calculation

> According to general understanding, the proton as one of the main ingredients of the nucleus is composed of one down and two up quarks bound together by gluons, described by Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD). In this view, heavy quarks do not contribute to the primary wave function of the proton. Heavy quarks arise in the proton perturbatively by gluon splitting and the probability gradually increases as $Q^2$ increases (extrinsic heavy quarks). In addition, the existence of non-perturbative intrinsic charm quarks in the proton has also been predicted by QCD. In this picture, the heavy quarks also exist in the proton's wave function. In fact, the wave function has a five-quark structure $ \vert u u d c \bar{c}\rangle $ in addition to the three-quark bound state $ \vert u u d\rangle $. So far, many studies have been done to confirm or reject this additional component. One of the recent studies has been done by the NNPDF collaboration. They established the existence of an intrinsic charm component at the 3-standard-deviation level in the proton from the structure function measurements. Most of the studies performed to calculate the contribution of the intrinsic charm so far have been based on the global analyses of the experimental data. In this article, for the first time we directly calculate this contribution by an analytic method. We estimate a $x^{c\bar{c}} = (1.36 \pm 0.67)\% $ contribution for the $ \vert u u d c \bar{c}\rangle $ component of the proton.

## Key claim

The authors claim a direct analytic calculation of the intrinsic charm component of the proton, yielding a probability of \(x^{c\bar{c}} = (1.36 \pm 0.67)\%\) for the \(|uudc\bar{c}\rangle\) Fock state, which overlaps within uncertainties with the NNPDF collaboration's global-fit estimate of \((0.62 \pm 0.28)\%\) at the 3-standard-deviation level.

## Method

The authors used the two-point QCD Sum Rules (QCDSR) approach, constructing a correlation function from an interpolating current that is a superposition of the ordinary three-quark \(|uud\rangle\) current and a five-quark \(|uudc\bar{c}\rangle\) current. They calculated the spectral densities for both components via the Operator Product Expansion (OPE), applied Borel transformation and continuum subtraction, and equated the resulting sum rule for the proton mass to the experimental mass from the Particle Data Group (PDG) to extract the probability \(x^{c\bar{c}}\).

## Key numbers

*   Intrinsic charm probability: \(x^{c\bar{c}} = (1.36 \pm 0.67)\%\) for the \(|uudc\bar{c}\rangle\) component of the proton.
*   Charm quark mass: \(m_c = 1.27 \pm 0.02~\mathrm{GeV}\).
*   Light quark masses: \(m_u = 2.2_{-0.4}^{+0.5}~\mathrm{MeV}\), \(m_d = 4.7_{-0.3}^{+0.5}~\mathrm{MeV}\).
*   Quark condensate: \(\langle \overline{q}q\rangle = -(0.24 \pm 0.01)^3~\mathrm{GeV}^3\).
*   Gluon condensate: \(\langle \alpha_s G^2 / \pi \rangle = (0.012 \pm 0.004)~\mathrm{GeV}^4\).
*   Mixed condensate parameter: \(m_0^2 = (0.8 \pm 0.2)~\mathrm{GeV}^2\).
*   Working windows for auxiliary parameters: Borel parameter \(M^2 = 1.15\) to \(1.50~\mathrm{GeV}^2\); continuum threshold for the 3q-part \(s_0 = 2.15\) to \(2.30~\mathrm{GeV}^2\); continuum threshold for the 5q-part \(s'_0 = 24\) to \(26~\mathrm{GeV}^2\); auxiliary parameter \(\beta = -2.60\) to \(-1.80\).

## Context

This work provides the first direct analytic calculation of the intrinsic charm probability in the proton using QCD sum rules, contrasting with previous global analyses of parton distribution functions (PDFs) by the CTEQ collaboration (Dulat 2013) and the NNPDF collaboration (Ball 2022), which extracted this probability from experimental data. The result is consistent with the NNPDF estimate and supports the BHPS model (Brodsky, Hoyer, Peterson, and Sakai 1980, 1981) of a five-quark Fock state in the proton. This analytic method enables a complementary, non-perturbative check on PDF-based extractions and motivates further experimental searches for intrinsic charm at facilities like AFTER@LHC and LHCb fixed-target programs.
