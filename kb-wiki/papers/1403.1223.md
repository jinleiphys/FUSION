# Leading-order anisotropic hydrodynamics for systems with massive particles

> The framework of anisotropic hydrodynamics is generalized to include finite particle masses. Two schemes are introduced and their predictions compared with exact solutions of the kinetic equation in the relaxation time approximation. The first formulation uses the zeroth and first moments of the kinetic equation, whereas the second formulation uses the first and second moments. For the case of one-dimensional boost-invariant expansion, our numerical results indicate that the second formulation yields much better agreement with the exact solutions.

## Key claim

The paper generalizes anisotropic hydrodynamics to systems with massive particles, introducing two moment-based schemes; the second formulation (using first and second moments of the kinetic equation) yields much better agreement with exact kinetic theory solutions than the first formulation (using zeroth and first moments), particularly for massive particles and large initial anisotropies.

## Method

The authors compare two anisotropic hydrodynamics formulations against exact solutions of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation for a transversely homogeneous system undergoing one-dimensional boost-invariant expansion. The first formulation (saHydro) uses the zeroth and first moments of the kinetic equation, while the second formulation (eaHydro) uses the first and second moments; both employ a Romatschke-Strickland form for the anisotropic distribution function with a dynamical Landau matching condition to determine the effective temperature.

## Key numbers

- Initial effective temperature T0 = 600 MeV used for both massless and massive (M = 300 MeV) cases
- Finite particle mass of M = 300 MeV tested in the massive case
- Degeneracy factor g0 = 16 used for comparison with exact solutions from Florkowski (2014)
- For massless particles with initial anisotropy ξ0 = 100, saHydro shows relatively large deviations from exact solution in the pressure ratio, while eaHydro agrees very well
- For massive particles (M = 300 MeV) with initial anisotropy ξ0 = 0, saHydro shows a strong "kink" at early times and does not always approach isotropic equilibrium at late times

## Context

This work extends the anisotropic hydrodynamics framework previously developed for massless particles (Florkowski 2010, Martinez 2010, Ryblewski 2010) to include finite particle masses, addressing an open issue in the field. The results support earlier findings from Tinti (2013) that using the second moment of the kinetic equation more straightforwardly guarantees agreement with Israel-Stewart theory, and from Florkowski (2014) that two zeroth-moment equations are insufficient for systems with multiple momentum scales. The massive formulation enables more realistic modeling of heavy-ion collisions where particle masses become important at later stages of evolution.
