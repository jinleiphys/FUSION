# Comprehensive Analyses of the Neutrino-Process in the Core-collapsing Supernova

> We investigate the neutrino flavor change effects due to neutrino self-interaction, shock wave propagation as well as matter effect on the neutrino-process of the core-collapsing supernova (CCSN). For the hydrodynamics, we use two models: a simple thermal bomb model and a specified hydrodynamic model for SN1987A. As a pre-supernova model, we take an updated model adjusted to explain the SN1987A employing recent development of the $(n,γ)$ reaction rates for nuclei near the stability line $(A \sim 100)$. As for the neutrino luminosity, we adopt two different models: equivalent neutrino luminosity and non-equivalent luminosity models. The latter is taken from the synthetic analyses of the CCSN simulation data which involved quantitatively the results obtained by various neutrino transport models. Relevant neutrino-induced reaction rates are calculated by a shell model for light nuclei and a quasi-particle random phase approximation model for heavy nuclei. For each model, we present abundances of the light nuclei ($^7$Li, $^7$Be, $^{11}$B and $^{11}$C) and heavy nuclei ($^{92}$Nb, $^{98}$Tc, $^{138}$La and $^{180}$Ta) produced by the neutrino-process. The light nuclei abundances turn out to be sensitive to the Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) region around O-Ne-Mg region while the heavy nuclei are mainly produced prior to the MSW region. Through the detailed analyses, we find that neutrino self-interaction becomes a key ingredient in addition to the MSW effect for understanding the neutrino-process and the relevant nuclear abundances. The normal mass hierarchy is shown to be more compatible with the meteorite data. Main nuclear reactions for each nucleus are also investigated in detail.

## Key claim

The authors find that neutrino self-interaction is a key ingredient, in addition to the Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) effect, for understanding the neutrino-process in core-collapsing supernovae, and that the normal neutrino mass hierarchy is more compatible with meteorite data than the inverted hierarchy.

## Method

The authors used two hydrodynamic models (a thermal bomb model and a specified model for SN1987A) and two neutrino luminosity models (equivalent and non-equivalent) to simulate the neutrino-process. Neutrino-induced reaction rates were calculated using a shell model for light nuclei and a quasi-particle random phase approximation model for heavy nuclei. The time evolution of neutrino flavor change was computed by solving the Schrodinger-like equation with a Hamiltonian including vacuum terms, the MSW matter potential, and neutrino self-interaction.

## Key numbers

*   The MSW resonance occurs at a density of approximately \(10^3 \, \mathrm{g/cm^3}\), appearing at different mass coordinates depending on the hydrodynamic model: \(M_r \sim 4.6 M_\odot\) for the HKC18 model and \(M_r \sim 3.7 M_\odot\) for the KCK19 model, for a neutrino energy of \(E_\nu = 15 \, \mathrm{MeV}\).
*   The pre-supernova model for SN1987A is estimated to have had a mass of \(19 \pm 3\) solar masses (\(M_\odot\)) in the main sequence, with metallicity \(Z \sim Z_\odot/4\).
*   The explosion energy used in the KCK19 hydrodynamic model is \(10^{51} \, \mathrm{erg}\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the neutrino-process in core-collapsing supernovae, such as those by Woosley (1990) and Yoshida (2004, 2005, 2006, 2008), by incorporating both neutrino self-interaction and shock wave propagation effects alongside the MSW effect. It builds on the hydrodynamic models of SN1987A from Shigeyama (1988) and the pre-supernova model of Woosley (1995), and uses updated \((n,\gamma)\) reaction rates. The findings enable more precise comparisons between calculated neutrino-process abundances and meteorite data, potentially constraining neutrino mass hierarchy and providing a cosmic chronometer for supernova events.
