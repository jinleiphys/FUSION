# Quantum computing with and for many-body physics

> Quantum computing technologies are making steady progress. This has opened new opportunities for tackling problems whose complexity prevents their description on classical computers. A prototypical example of these complex problems are interacting quantum many-body systems: on the one hand, these systems are known to become rapidly prohibitive to describe using classical computers when their size increases. On the other hand, these systems are precisely those which are used in the laboratory to build quantum computing platforms. This arguably makes them one of the most promising early use cases of quantum computing. In this review, we explain how quantum many-body systems are used to build quantum processors, and how, in turn, current and future quantum processors can be used to describe large many-body systems of fermions such as electrons and nucleons. The review includes an introduction to analog and digital quantum devices, the mapping of Fermi systems and their Hamiltonians onto qubit registers, as well as an overview of methods to access their static and dynamical properties. We also highlight some aspects related to entanglement, and touch on the description, influence and processing of decoherence in quantum devices.

## Key claim

This review argues that quantum many-body systems are both the physical substrate for building quantum processors and the most promising early application domain for those processors, but the practical utility of near-term devices is constrained by noise and limited qubit quality, requiring specific denoising methods and NISQ-era algorithms.

## Method

The paper provides a comprehensive review of the intersection between quantum computing and many-body physics, covering: (1) the formal structure of many-body Hamiltonians in electronic structure, condensed matter, and nuclear physics; (2) the construction of analog and digital quantum devices from many-body systems; (3) methods for mapping fermionic Hamiltonians onto qubit registers; (4) quantum algorithms for static and dynamical properties including both fault-tolerant and NISQ approaches; and (5) the treatment of entanglement and decoherence in quantum devices.

## Key numbers

- The Hilbert space of a qubit register grows as $2^{n_q}$ where $n_q$ is the number of qubits
- Current shell-model approaches in nuclear physics can treat eigenvalue problems in spaces with $10^{11}-10^{12}$ states
- Clifford circuits are simulatable with time and space complexity $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ where $n$ is the number of qubits
- The ground state estimation problem is QMA-complete for $k$-local spin Hamiltonians when $k \geq 2$
- The Fermi-Hubbard model with local magnetic fields is QMA-complete
- The Bose-Hubbard model is QMA-complete

## Context

This review synthesizes work across multiple subfields including quantum chemistry (McArdle 2018, Cao 2019, Bauer 2020), condensed matter (Zeng 2019), and nuclear physics (Zhang 2021, Stetcu 2022, Roggero 2020). It extends prior reviews by emphasizing the dual role of many-body systems as both the hardware platform and the target application for quantum computing. The paper enables researchers in nuclear physics and condensed matter to understand how to map their specific Hamiltonians onto quantum processors, while also providing context for the NISQ-era limitations identified by Preskill (2018).
