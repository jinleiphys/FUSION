# Drag and Diffusion of Heavy Quarks in a hot and anisotropic QCD medium

> The propagation of heavy quarks (HQs) in a medium was quite often modeled by the Fokker-Plank (FP) equation. Since the transport coefficients, related to drag and diffusion processes are the main ingredients in the FP equation, the evolution of HQs is thus effectively controlled by them. At the initial stage of the relativistic heavy ion collisions, asymptotic weak-coupling causes the free-streaming motions of partons in the beam direction and the expansion in transverse directions are almost frozen, hence an anisotropy in the momentum space sets in. Since HQs are too produced in the same time therefore the study of the effect of momentum anisotropy on the drag and diffusion coefficients becomes advertently desirable. In this article we have thus studied the drag and diffusion of HQs in the anisotropic medium and found that the presence of the anisotropy reduces both drag and diffusion coefficients. In addition, the anisotropy introduces an angular dependence to both the drag and diffusion coefficients, as a result both coefficients get inflated when the partons are moving transverse to the direction of anisotropy than parallel to the direction of anisotropy.

## Key claim

The authors find that a momentum-space anisotropy in the quark-gluon plasma reduces both the drag and diffusion coefficients of heavy quarks compared to the isotropic case, and introduces an angular dependence such that both coefficients are larger when partons move transverse to the anisotropy direction than when they move parallel to it. This result is derived under the assumption of weak anisotropy (anisotropy parameter ξ ≪ 1) and considers only collisional energy loss.

## Method

The authors model heavy quark propagation using the Fokker-Planck equation derived from the Boltzmann transport equation under the soft-scattering (Landau) approximation, considering 2→2 elastic scattering of charm quarks with massless quarks, antiquarks, and gluons. They compute the drag and diffusion coefficients by evaluating the collision integral with matrix elements for quark-charm and gluon-charm scattering, using a one-loop running coupling and the Debye mass as a regulator, then generalize to an anisotropic medium by replacing the isotropic parton distribution with a Romatschke-Strickland form expanded to first order in the anisotropy parameter ξ.

## Key numbers

- Charm quark mass set to M = 1.5 GeV throughout the calculation.
- Drag coefficient decreases with increasing heavy quark momentum from p = 0.001 GeV (non-relativistic) to p = 5 GeV (relativistic) at fixed temperature T = 200 MeV.
- Changing the regulator from leading-order Debye mass m_D to 1.4 m_D (accounting for next-to-leading order corrections) changes the drag coefficient by about 35% for relativistic charm quarks (p = 5 GeV) and by 35-40% for non-relativistic charm quarks (p = 0.001 GeV) over T = 0.2 to 0.6 GeV.
- The same regulator change alters the diffusion coefficient by about 35% for relativistic charm quarks and by 30-35% for non-relativistic charm quarks over the same temperature range.
- The fluctuation-dissipation theorem ratio D_T/γ_T is approximately satisfied in the non-relativistic limit but violated as heavy quark momentum becomes relativistic, shown at T = 0.2 GeV.
- Drag and diffusion coefficients are computed for anisotropy parameter values ξ = 0, 0.3, and 0.6 at T = 0.2 GeV for both parallel (β = 0) and perpendicular (β = π/2) alignment relative to the anisotropy direction.

## Context

This work extends the Svetitsky (1988) formalism for heavy quark drag and diffusion in an isotropic QCD medium, using corrected matrix elements from Combridge (1979), to include the effect of momentum-space anisotropy that arises in the initial stage of relativistic heavy-ion collisions due to rapid longitudinal expansion. The authors previously applied a similar anisotropic treatment to electrical conductivity (Srivastava and Patra) and to quarkonium suppression (Lata, Patra, and collaborators, 2013, 2014). The results enable more realistic modeling of heavy quark energy loss and thermalization in the pre-equilibrium stages of heavy-ion collisions, which may affect predictions for observables such as the nuclear suppression factor R_AA and elliptic flow v_2.
