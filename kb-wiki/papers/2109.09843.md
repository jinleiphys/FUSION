# System Size and Flavour Dependence of Chemical Freeze-out Temperatures in ALICE Data from pp, pPb and PbPb Collisions at LHC Energies

> We present the system size and flavour dependence of the chemical freeze-out temperature ($T_\mathrm{ch}$) at vanishing baryo-chemical potential calculated via thermal fits to experimental yields for several multiplicity classes in pp, pPb and PbPb collisions measured by ALICE. Using the Thermal-FIST Hadron Resonance Gas model package, we compare the quality of fits across various treatments of strangeness conservation under different freeze-out conditions as a function of the charged particle multiplicity density $\big \langle dN_\mathrm{ch}/dη\big \rangle$. Additionally, we examine how the anti-hadron to pion yield ratios of light and strange baryons, as well as the $ϕ$ meson, evolve within a flavour-dependent model. Through a unique two-temperature chemical freeze-out approach, we show that flavour dependence of $T_\mathrm{ch}$ in a Strangeness Canonical Ensemble leads to a natural explanation of strangeness enhancement from small to large systems at LHC energies without requiring any non-equilibrium particle production at small $\big \langle dN_\mathrm{ch}/dη\big \rangle$.

## Key claim

The paper claims that strangeness enhancement from small (pp) to large (PbPb) collision systems at LHC energies can be explained naturally by a flavour-dependent two-temperature chemical freeze-out approach within a Strangeness Canonical Ensemble, without requiring any non-equilibrium particle production or strangeness saturation parameter, provided the charged particle multiplicity density is above approximately 10.

## Method

The authors performed thermal fits to experimental hadron yields from ALICE for pp collisions at √s = 7.00 TeV, pPb collisions at √s_NN = 5.02 TeV, and PbPb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV across multiple multiplicity classes, using the Thermal-FIST Hadron Resonance Gas model package with the PDG2016+ hadronic spectrum. They compared fits in both the Grand Canonical Ensemble (GCE) and Strangeness Canonical Ensemble (SCE) configurations, extracting chemical freeze-out temperatures separately for light hadrons (π, K, p) and strange hadrons (K, Λ, Ξ, Ω, K⁰_S, φ) as a function of charged particle multiplicity density ⟨dN_ch/dη⟩. They then calculated anti-hadron to π⁺ yield ratios using fixed flavour-dependent temperatures (T_L = 150 MeV for light hadrons, T_S = 165 MeV for strange hadrons) and compared these to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Light flavour freeze-out temperature T_L = 150.2 ± 2.6 MeV and strange flavour freeze-out temperature T_S = 165.1 ± 2.7 MeV, previously determined from central PbPb collisions at vanishing baryo-chemical potential in the GCE approach
- For the most central (0-10%) PbPb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76 TeV in GCE: π K p fit gives T_ch = 143.2 ± 2.79 MeV with χ²/dof = 5.65/4; full fit (π K p Λ Ξ Ω K⁰_S φ) gives T_ch = 149.6 ± 1.76 MeV with χ²/dof = 23.4/12; strange fit (K Λ Ξ Ω K⁰_S φ) gives T_ch = 153.9 ± 2.30 MeV with χ²/dof = 10.5/8
- In the SCE treatment, the quality of fit (χ²/dof) remains consistently ≤ 5 for all systems with ⟨dN_ch/dη⟩ ≥ 10, while GCE fits to pp data show χ²/dof > 5 for nearly all fits
- The flavour-dependent temperature separation of 15-20 MeV between light and strange quarks is consistent with lattice QCD predictions from susceptibility ratio calculations

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous two-temperature chemical freeze-out approach (Flor et al., 2020) from heavy-ion collisions to small collision systems (pp and pPb) measured by ALICE. It contrasts with earlier studies that attributed strangeness enhancement in small systems to non-equilibrium effects parameterized by a strangeness saturation factor γ_S (Tounsi, 2001; Sharma, 2019), or to canonical suppression with large correlation volumes (Vislavicius and Kalweit, 2018). The paper demonstrates that a flavour-dependent freeze-out scenario within the Strangeness Canonical Ensemble can describe particle production consistently from the smallest to the largest systems at LHC energies, without invoking non-equilibrium mechanisms. This enables future comparisons to lower-energy measurements where baryo-chemical potential is non-zero, and raises questions about the proper treatment of the φ meson's strangeness content in small systems.
