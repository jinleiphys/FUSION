# Floating block method for quantum Monte Carlo simulations

> Quantum Monte Carlo simulations are powerful and versatile tools for the quantum many-body problem. In addition to the usual calculations of energies and eigenstate observables, quantum Monte Carlo simulations can in principle be used to build fast and accurate many-body emulators using eigenvector continuation or design time-dependent Hamiltonians for adiabatic quantum computing. These new applications require something that is missing from the published literature, an efficient quantum Monte Carlo scheme for computing the inner product of ground state eigenvectors corresponding to different Hamiltonians. In this work, we introduce an algorithm called the floating block method, which solves the problem by performing Euclidean time evolution with two different Hamiltonians and interleaving the corresponding time blocks. We use the floating block method and nuclear lattice simulations to build eigenvector continuation emulators for energies of $^4$He, $^8$Be, $^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O nuclei over a range of local and non-local interaction couplings. From the emulator data, we identify the quantum phase transition line from a Bose gas of alpha particles to a nuclear liquid.

## Key claim
The floating block method enables efficient computation of inner products between ground-state eigenvectors corresponding to different Hamiltonians in quantum Monte Carlo simulations, a capability previously impractical for systems beyond very small size or where ground-state energies are not numerically close.

## Method
The method performs Euclidean time evolution with two different Hamiltonians and interleaves the corresponding time blocks, using the identity that the squared magnitude of the inner product equals the ratio of expectation values in the limit of large Euclidean time. For auxiliary-field Monte Carlo simulations, the authors reorder auxiliary fields in the numerator to match those in the denominator and gradually "float" time blocks one step at a time to reduce stochastic noise. The method is applied to nuclear lattice simulations of 4He, 8Be, 12C, and 16O nuclei to build eigenvector continuation emulators for energies over a range of local and non-local interaction couplings.

## Context
This work extends eigenvector continuation methods (Frame 2017, Sarkar 2021, Sarkar 2022) by providing a practical quantum Monte Carlo scheme for computing the norm matrix elements needed for emulator construction, which previously relied on reweighting techniques that were viable only for small systems with numerically close ground-state energies. The floating block method enables construction of fast and accurate emulators for nuclear systems using quantum Monte Carlo simulations, and the authors identify the quantum phase transition line from a Bose gas of alpha particles to a nuclear liquid, consistent with findings in Serdar (2016) that the local interaction coupling plays a dominant role. The method is compatible with both lattice auxiliary-field Monte Carlo and continuum path integral Monte Carlo simulations.
