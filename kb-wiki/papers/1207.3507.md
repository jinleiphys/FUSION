# A Fixed-Target ExpeRiment at the LHC (AFTER@LHC) : luminosities, target polarisation and a selection of physics studies

> We report on a future multi-purpose fixed-target experiment with the proton or lead ion LHC beams extracted by a bent crystal. The multi-TeV LHC beams allow for the most energetic fixed-target experiments ever performed. Such an experiment, tentatively named AFTER for "A Fixed-Target ExperRiment", gives access to new domains of particle and nuclear physics complementing that of collider experiments, in particular at RHIC and at the EIC projects. The instantaneous luminosity at AFTER using typical targets surpasses that of RHIC by more than 3 orders of magnitude. Beam extraction by a bent crystal offers an ideal way to obtain a clean and very collimated high-energy beam, without decreasing the performance of the LHC. The fixed-target mode also has the advantage of allowing for spin measurements with a polarised target and for an access over the full backward rapidity domain up to xF ~ - 1. Here, we elaborate on the reachable luminosities, the target polarisation and a selection of measurements with hydrogen and deuterium targets.

## Key claim

AFTER@LHC would achieve instantaneous luminosities surpassing those at RHIC by more than three orders of magnitude using a bent-crystal beam extraction scheme that does not degrade LHC performance, but the actual reachable luminosities depend on target material and thickness, and the beam extraction method remains under study by the CERN LUA9 collaboration.

## Method

The authors computed instantaneous and integrated luminosities for a 7 TeV proton beam extracted at 5 × 10⁸ protons/s incident on various 1 cm thick targets (solid H, liquid H, liquid D, Be, Cu, W, Pb). They evaluated target polarisation options using Dynamic Nuclear Polarisation (DNP) for materials such as butanol, ammonia, and Li₆D, and the brute-force method for HD targets, considering dilution factors, achievable polarisations, and beam heating constraints. They then surveyed potential physics measurements in pp and pd collisions, focusing on quarkonium yields, Drell-Yan pairs, prompt photons, and transverse single-spin asymmetries.

## Key numbers

- Instantaneous luminosity for a 1 cm solid hydrogen target: 26 μb⁻¹ s⁻¹; yearly integrated luminosity: 260 pb⁻¹ yr⁻¹
- Instantaneous luminosity for a 1 cm liquid hydrogen target: 20 μb⁻¹ s⁻¹; yearly integrated luminosity: 200 pb⁻¹ yr⁻¹
- Instantaneous luminosity for a 1 cm liquid deuterium target: 24 μb⁻¹ s⁻¹; yearly integrated luminosity: 240 pb⁻¹ yr⁻¹
- Instantaneous luminosity for a 1 cm copper target: 42 μb⁻¹ s⁻¹; yearly integrated luminosity: 420 pb⁻¹ yr⁻¹
- Instantaneous luminosity for a 1 cm lead target: 16 μb⁻¹ s⁻¹; yearly integrated luminosity: 160 pb⁻¹ yr⁻¹
- Center-of-mass energy for 7 TeV protons on a fixed target: close to 115 GeV
- Boost factor γ between center-of-mass and lab systems: approximately 60; rapidity shift: approximately 4.8
- Beam heating power for a 1 cm target: approximately 50 μW, allowing target temperatures as low as 50 mK
- Target irradiation damage threshold: 10¹⁵ p⁺ cm⁻², corresponding to one month of beam
- Dilution factors: butanol 0.13, ammonia 0.176, Li₆D approximately 0.5, HD approximately 0.9
- Achievable polarisations: 90% for protons and 50% for deuterons via DNP or brute-force methods
- Quarkonium yields at AFTER: approximately 1000 times those at RHIC

## Context

This paper extends the concept of a fixed-target experiment at the LHC, originally proposed by the LHB collaboration in the early 1990s and later developed by Uggerhoj (2005), by providing detailed luminosity calculations and target polarisation options. It complements collider programs at RHIC and the planned Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) by accessing the backward rapidity domain (xF approaching -1) and enabling spin measurements with polarised targets. The work enables future precision QCD studies of quarkonium production mechanisms, gluon PDF extraction from neutron targets, and transverse single-spin asymmetry measurements via the Sivers effect, building on prior results from E866 (Zhu 2007) and PHENIX (Adare 2010).
