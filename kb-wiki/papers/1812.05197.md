# Two-neutron transfer reactions and shape phase transitions in the microscopically-formulated interacting boson model

> Two-neutron transfer reactions are studied within the interacting boson model based on the nuclear energy density functional theory. Constrained self-consistent mean-field calculations with the Skyrme energy density functional are performed to provide microscopic input to completely determine the Hamiltonian of the IBM. Spectroscopic properties are calculated only from the nucleonic degrees of freedom. This method is applied to study the $(t,p)$ and $(p,t)$ transfer reactions in the assorted set of rare-earth nuclei $^{146-158}$Sm, $^{148-160}$Gd, and $^{150-162}$Dy, where spherical-to-axially-deformed shape phase transition is suggested to occur at the neutron number $N\approx 90$. The results are compared with those from the purely phenomenological IBM calculations, as well as with the available experimental data. The calculated $(t,p)$ and $(p,t)$ transfer reaction intensities, from both the microscopic and phenomenological IBM frameworks, signal the rapid nuclear structural change at particular nucleon numbers.

# Two-neutron transfer reactions and shape phase transitions in the microscopically-formulated interacting boson model

## Key claim

The microscopically-formulated interacting boson model (IBM), derived from self-consistent mean-field calculations with the Skyrme energy density functional, can describe two-neutron transfer reaction intensities in rare-earth nuclei without phenomenological parameter adjustment, but the shape phase transition appears more moderate and occurs at somewhat different neutron numbers than in purely phenomenological IBM calculations.

## Method

Constrained Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations with the Skyrme SkM* energy density functional were performed to obtain potential energy surfaces in the quadrupole (beta,gamma) deformation space for each nucleus. These surfaces were mapped onto the expectation value of the neutron-proton IBM-2 Hamiltonian in the boson coherent state, completely determining the Hamiltonian parameters epsilon, kappa, chi_nu, and chi_pi without any phenomenological adjustment. The resulting IBM-2 wave functions from m-scheme diagonalization were used to compute (t,p) and (p,t) transfer reaction intensities for monopole (L=0) and quadrupole (L=2) neutron pair transfers, with only the overall scale factors t_0 and t_2 fitted to experimental data for each isotopic chain.

## Key numbers

- Studied nuclei: 146-158Sm, 148-160Gd, and 150-162Dy, with neutron numbers N=84-96 and proton boson numbers N_pi=6 (Sm), 7 (Gd), and 8 (Dy)
- Neutron boson numbers range from N_nu=1 to N_nu=7, with the doubly-magic 132Sn as the inert core (Omega_nu=22 neutron pair degeneracy)
- In the m-IBM calculation for Sm isotopes, the PES minimum jumps from beta_min~0.5 in 148Sm to beta_min~1.0 in 150Sm, indicating the latter is already well deformed
- The m-IBM calculation for Sm isotopes shows the 0+_2 and 0+_3 energy levels become lowest at N=88, while phenomenological calculations place the minimum at N=90
- For Gd isotopes, the experimental 0+_2 energy level is lowest at N=88, which the m-IBM follows but systematically overestimates; phenomenological calculations place the minimum at N=90
- The (t,p) transfer intensity I^(tp)(N,0+_1 -> N+2,0+_1) and I^(tp)(N,0+_1 -> N+2,2+_1) from phenomenological IBM calculations show very good agreement with experimental data for Sm isotopes

## Context

This work extends the self-consistent mean-field to IBM mapping procedure of Nomura (2008) and Nomura (2011) to nucleon-pair transfer reactions, building on earlier IBM studies of shape phase transitions in rare-earth nuclei by Kotila (2012) and Zhang (2017). The results are compared with purely phenomenological IBM-2 calculations (with parameters fitted to experimental spectra) and IBM-1 calculations in the consistent-Q formalism from Zhang (2017). The study demonstrates that the microscopically-derived IBM predicts a more gradual shape transition than phenomenological approaches, resolving discrepancies in the predicted location of the critical point. This enables future applications of the method to predict transfer reaction intensities across the nuclear chart without reliance on experimental data for parameter fitting.
