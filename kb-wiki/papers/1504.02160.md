# Pre-equilibrium evolution effects on heavy-ion collision observables

> In order to investigate the importance of pre-equilibrium dynamics on relativistic heavy-ion collision observables, we match a highly non-equilibrium early evolution stage, modeled by free-streaming partons generated from the Monte Carlo Kharzeev-Levin-Nardi (MC-KLN) and Monte Carlo Glauber (MC-Glb) models, to a locally approximately thermalized later evolution stage described by viscous hydrodynamics, and study the dependence of final hadronic transverse momentum distributions, in particular their underlying radial and anisotropic flows, on the switching time between these stages. Performing a 3-parameter fit of the measured values for the average transverse momenta $\langle p_\perp \rangle$ for pions, kaons and protons as well as the elliptic and triangular flows of charged hadrons $v_{2,3}^\mathrm{ch}$, with the switching time $τ_s$, the specific shear viscosity $η/s$ during the hydrodynamic stage, and the kinetic decoupling temperature $T_\mathrm{dec}$ as free parameters, we find that the preferred "thermalization" times $τ_s$ depend strongly on the model of the initial conditions. MC-KLN initial conditions require an earlier transition to hydrodynamic behavior (at $τ_s \approx$ 0.13 fm/$c$) , followed by hydrodynamic evolution with a larger specific shear viscosity $η/s\approx$ 0.2, than MC-Glb initial conditions which prefer switching at a later time ($τ_s\approx$ 0.6 fm/$c$) followed by a less viscous hydrodynamic evolution with $η/s\approx$ 0.16. These new results including pre-equilibrium evolution are compared to fits without a pre-equilbrium stage where all dynamic evolution before the onset of hydrodynamic behavior is ignored. In each case, the quality of the dynamical descriptions for the optimized parameter sets, as well as the observables which show the strongest constraining power for the thermalization time, are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper shows that including a free-streaming pre-equilibrium stage before viscous hydrodynamics changes the preferred "thermalization" time and specific shear viscosity, but the optimal values depend strongly on the model used for the initial conditions (MC-KLN vs. MC-Glb), with the free-streaming stage generating "pre-flow" that actually increases final radial flow contrary to earlier expectations.

## Method

The authors match a free-streaming pre-equilibrium stage (modeled by collisionless Boltzmann evolution of massless partons from MC-KLN or MC-Glb initial conditions) to a viscous hydrodynamic stage using Landau matching at a switching time τ_s. They then perform a 3-parameter fit of the measured average transverse momenta ⟨p_⊥⟩ for pions, kaons, and protons, plus the elliptic and triangular flow coefficients v_2,3^ch of charged hadrons, with the switching time τ_s, specific shear viscosity η/s, and kinetic decoupling temperature T_dec as free parameters.

## Key numbers

- MC-KLN initial conditions prefer an early switching time τ_s ≈ 0.13 fm/c, followed by hydrodynamic evolution with η/s ≈ 0.2
- MC-Glb initial conditions prefer a later switching time τ_s ≈ 0.6 fm/c, followed by hydrodynamic evolution with η/s ≈ 0.16
- The event-averaged initial radial velocity reaches 25% of the speed of light after 1 fm/c of free-streaming, and grows at an approximate rate ⟨a_⊥⟩ ≈ 0.13 c^2/fm over the next 5 fm/c
- The inverse Reynolds number R^{-1} at the matter formation time τ_0 is position-independent and equal to √(3/2) ≈ 1.225

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Kolb (2000) which suggested that thermalization must happen within about 1 fm/c to explain observed anisotropies, and that free-streaming would delay pressure buildup and reduce collective flow. The authors show instead that free-streaming generates "pre-flow" that increases radial flow, contradicting those earlier expectations. The study enables systematic determination of upper limits on thermalization time for a given viscosity, and provides a framework for comparing MC-KLN and MC-Glb initial condition models against LHC data at √s = 2.76 ATeV.
