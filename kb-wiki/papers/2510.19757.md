# Beta-decay Half Lives beyond $^{54}$Ca: A Systematic Survey of Decay Properties approaching the Neutron Dripline

> In an experiment performed at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) using the FRIB Decay Station initiator (FDSi), 15 new half lives of isotopes near $^{54}$Ca were measured. A new method of extracting lifetimes from experimental data, taking into account the unknown $β$-delayed neutron emission branches of very neutron-rich nuclei, was developed to enable systematic uncertainty analysis. The experiment observed a dramatic change in the half-life systematics for the isotopes with neutron number N =34. Beyond N =34, the decline of nuclear lifetime is much slower, leading to longer than anticipated lifetimes for near-dripline nuclei. State-of-the-art shell-model calculations can explain the experimental results for Z$>$19 nuclei, revealing the imprint of shell effects and the need for modification of single-particle neutron states. The results from a newly developed QRPA model with potential for making global predictions were also tested against the experimental results and good agreement was found.

## Key claim

The paper reports 15 new beta-decay half-lives for neutron-rich isotopes near $^{54}$Ca and observes a dramatic change in half-life systematics beyond neutron number N=34, where the decline of nuclear lifetime becomes much slower than previously expected, leading to longer-than-anticipated lifetimes for near-dripline nuclei. The authors qualify that state-of-the-art shell-model calculations can explain the experimental results only for nuclei with proton number Z greater than 19, while for Z less than 20 the experimental data cannot yet confirm the predicted kink in systematics.

## Method

The experiment was performed at the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB) using the FRIB Decay Station initiator (FDSi), where a 215 MeV/nucleon $^{82}$Se primary beam bombarded a 5-mm carbon target to produce neutron-rich fragments. The Advanced Rare Isotope Separator (ARIS) selected two beam settings centered on $^{55}$Ca and $^{54}$K, and particle identification was done via energy loss and time-of-flight methods. Half-lives were extracted by fitting decay curves with Bateman equations including parent, daughter, and granddaughter generations, using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) analysis to account for unknown beta-delayed neutron emission branches and uncertainties in daughter and granddaughter half-lives.

## Context

This work extends systematic half-life measurements in the neutron-rich region around doubly magic $^{54}$Ca, building on previous studies of shell closures at N=32 and N=34 by Hagen (2012), Wienholtz (2013), and Steppenbeck (2013, 2015). The new data provide critical tests for large-scale shell-model calculations using the sdpf-mu and ufp-ca interactions, and benchmark a newly developed global QRPA model (PCpnRQRPA) that shows improved agreement over earlier global models such as FRDM+QRPA by Moller (2019) and RHB+pnRQRPA by Marketin (2016). The results have implications for understanding Urca cooling processes in quiescent X-ray binaries and for improving predictions of beta-decay properties near the neutron dripline.
