# The non-equilibrium attractor: Beyond hydrodynamics

> The quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions is not in local thermal equilibrium at early times. Despite this, dissipative hydrodynamics describes the evolution of the energy-momentum tensor quite well after only roughly 0.5 - 1 fm/c. This can be understood using the concept of a non-equilibrium dynamical attractor. The attractor is a uniquely identifiable solution to the dynamical equations to which all solutions are drawn as the system evolves. Once solutions collapse onto the non-equilibrium attractor they are ``pseudo-thermalized'' in the sense that they have lost information about the precise initial conditions used, but are not yet in exact local thermal equilibrium. Here I review recent work which demonstrates that there exists a non-equilibrium attractor in full kinetic theory models which goes beyond the usual low-order momentum moments considered in hydrodynamical treatments.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a non-equilibrium attractor exists not only for low-order hydrodynamic moments but also for general higher-order moments of the one-particle distribution function and for the distribution function itself in full kinetic theory, meaning that solutions converge to a universal attractor on a characteristic scaled time scale before reaching exact local thermal equilibrium.

## Method

The author solved the conformal 0+1d Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation (RTA) subject to boost-invariant Bjorken flow. The exact solution takes the form of a one-dimensional integral equation for the effective temperature, which was solved iteratively using a CUDA-based GPU code on large lattices. Once the effective temperature was obtained, all moments of the one-particle distribution function and the distribution function itself were computed using the exact solution.

## Key numbers

- Initial central temperature of QGP at LHC: \(T_0 \simeq 600\) MeV at \(\tau_0 = 0.25\) fm/c for central Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 5.5\) TeV
- Hydrodynamization time: \(\tau_{\rm hydro} \sim 2/T\), translating to \(\tau_{\rm hydro} \sim 0.5\) fm/c at highest LHC energies
- For the attractor solution, the initial anisotropy was tuned to \(\alpha_0 \simeq 0.0025\) with 4096 points spaced logarithmically between \(\tau = 0.001\) and 1000 fm/c
- For specific solutions with different initial conditions, 2048 points were used spaced logarithmically between \(\tau = 0.1\) and 100 fm/c with initial temperature \(T_0 = 1\) GeV at \(\tau_0 = 0.1\) fm/c and initial anisotropy parameter \(0.1 \leq \alpha_0 \leq 1.5\)
- Moments with \(m=0\) visibly merge with the attractor only after \(\overline{w} \gtrsim 6\); moments with \(m \neq 0\) visibly merge for \(\overline{w} \gtrsim 2\)

## Context

This work extends the concept of the hydrodynamical attractor, originally developed in second-order viscous hydrodynamics by Heller (2015), to full kinetic theory beyond low-order momentum moments. It builds on previous demonstrations of attractor behavior in strong coupling \({\cal N}=4\) supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory (Heller 2013, Keegan 2015, Romatschke 2017), QCD effective kinetic theory (Keegan 2015, Kurkela 2015, Romatschke 2017), third-order viscous hydrodynamics (Jaiswal), anisotropic hydrodynamics (Strickland 2017), and exact RTA Boltzmann solutions (Romatschke 2017, Strickland 2017, Behtash 2017, Denicol 2018, Strickland 2018). The results enable future comparisons with number-conserving RTA (Strickland 2019), leading-order scalar field theory (Almaalol 2018), and effective kinetic theory frameworks including quarks (Kurkela 2015, 2018).
