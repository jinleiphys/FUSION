# Heat can flow from cold to hot in Microcanonical Thermodynamics of finite systems and the microscopic origin of phase transitions

> Thermodynamics allows the application of Statistical Mechanics to finite and even small systems. As surface effects cannot be scaled away, one has to be careful with the standard arguments of splitting a system into two or bringing two systems into thermal contact with energy or particle exchange: Not only the volume part of the entropy must be considered. The addition of any other macroscopic constraint like a dividing surface, or the enforcement of gradients of the energy/particle reduce the entropy. As will be shown here, when removing such constraint in regions of a negative heat capacity, the system may even relax under a flow of heat against the temperature slope. Thus Clausius formulation of the Second Law: "Heat always flows from hot to cold" can be violated. However, the Second Law is still satisfied and the total Boltzmann-entropy is rising. In the final chapter the general microscopic mechanism leading to the convexity of the microcanonical entropy at phase separation is discussed. This is explained for the liquid--gas and the solid--liquid transition.

## Key claim

In microcanonical thermodynamics of finite or non-extensive systems, heat can flow from cold to hot when the system is in a region of negative heat capacity at phase separation, violating Clausius's formulation of the Second Law while still satisfying the overall Second Law as total Boltzmann entropy rises.

## Method

The paper analyzes the entropy balance when a finite system is split by a geometric dividing surface into two pieces with different specific energies, showing that constraining the energy difference reduces the entropy by a surface term. It then derives the microcanonical partition sum for a classical Hamiltonian system, separating it into ideal-gas and interaction parts, and examines how the accessible configuration volume changes with energy to explain the convex intruder in the microcanonical entropy at phase separation.

## Key numbers

- The surface entropy term \(\Delta\Sigma\) scales as \(N^{2/3}\) for short-range coupling, so \(\Delta\sigma = \Delta\Sigma/N \propto N^{-1/3}\) vanishes in the thermodynamic limit \(N\to\infty\) due to van Hove's theorem.
- For the liquid-gas transition, the interaction entropy \(S^{int}(E-E_0,N,\mathcal{V})\) approaches \(0\) at high energies (\(E \gg V^{int}\)) and approaches \(\ln\{[\mathcal{V}_0/\mathcal{V}]^{N-1}\} < 0\) as \(E \to E_0\), where \(\mathcal{V}_0\) is the eigen-volume of the condensate and \(\mathcal{V}\) is the container volume.
- For the solid-liquid transition, the interaction entropy approaches \(\ln\{[d^3/\mathcal{V}_0]^{N-1}\}\) at low energies, where \(d^3\) is the lattice cell volume.

## Context

This work extends the microcanonical thermodynamics developed by Gross (2001) to explicitly demonstrate that negative heat capacity and backbending caloric curves, previously shown for nuclear fragmentation and atomic-cluster fragmentation in Gross (1995, 2001), lead to violations of Clausius's heat-flow statement. The paper provides a microscopic explanation for the convex intruder in the microcanonical entropy at first-order phase transitions, contrasting with the canonical Yang-Lee singularity approach, and enables understanding of phase separation in finite systems including multifragmentation in nuclear physics.
