# An MLE analysis on the relationship between the initial-state granularity and final-state flow factorization

> In this study, we employ the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) to investigate the relationship between initial-state fluctuations and final-state anisotropies in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. The granularity of the initial state, reflecting fluctuations in the initial conditions (IC), is modeled using a peripheral tube model. Besides differential flow, our analysis focuses on a class of more sensitive observables known as flow factorization. Specifically, we evaluate these observables using MLE, an asymptotically normal and unbiased tool in standard statistical inference. Our findings show that the resulting differential flow remains essentially unchanged for different IC defined by the peripheral tube model. The resulting harmonic coefficients obtained using MLE and multi-particle cumulants are found to be consistent. However, the calculated flow factorizations show significant variations depending on both the IC and the estimators, which is attributed to their sensitivity to initial-state fluctuations. Thus, we argue that MLE offers a compelling alternative to standard methods such as multi-particle correlators, particularly for sensitive observables constructed from higher moments of the azimuthal distribution.

## Key claim

The authors argue that maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) offers a compelling alternative to standard multi-particle cumulant methods for analyzing flow observables in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, particularly for flow factorization which is more sensitive to initial-state fluctuations than differential flow harmonics. The claim is hedged by the finding that while differential flow remains essentially unchanged for different initial conditions, flow factorization shows significant variations depending on both the initial conditions and the estimator used.

## Method

The authors employed the peripheral tube model to generate initial conditions with varying granularity by changing the number of high-energy-density tubes (1, 3, 30, and 100 tubes) placed near the surface of a smooth background energy density profile. They performed hydrodynamic simulations using the NeXSPheRIO code for Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV in the 0-5% centrality class, then extracted flow harmonics and flow factorization ratios using both MLE and multi-particle cumulant methods. The flow factorization was evaluated as a Pearson correlation coefficient between flow vectors at different transverse momenta, and the results were compared across different numbers of tubes and between the two estimation methods.

## Key numbers

- The peripheral tube model parameters were calibrated for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV in the 0-5% centrality class, with tube radii R_tube = 2.3 and tube amplitude A_tube = 12.0
- The radial positions of tubes were drawn from a uniform distribution r_0 ~ U(0, 0.546) and azimuthal angles from theta ~ U(0, 2pi)
- The background energy density parameters were K = 9.33, L = 7.0, M = 2.0, a = 0.41, b = 0.186, c = 0.9
- For a single tube, the hydrodynamic evolution around an individual high-energy tube led to two peaks separated by roughly 120 degrees in the azimuthal distribution
- The flow factorization ratio r_n for elliptic flow with 100 tubes approached that of realistic Au+Au collisions generated by NeXuS, as indicated by black filled squares in the figures

## Context

This work extends previous studies using the peripheral tube model (sph-corr-02, sph-corr-04, sph-corr-18, sph-review-02) which provided an intuitive interpretation for triangular flow and ridge structures in di-hadron correlations. The study builds on the MLE approach proposed in earlier work (sph-vn-10) and applies it to flow factorization, which was previously identified as a sensitive probe of initial-state fluctuations by ALICE (2011) and Gardim (2012). The results enable future studies to use MLE as a robust tool for analyzing higher-moment observables that are sensitive to initial-state granularity, particularly for the extensive datasets obtained at RHIC and LHC.
