# Symmetry Energy from Systematic of Isobaric Analog States

> Excitation energies to isobaric states, that are analogs of ground states, are dominated by contributions from the symmetry energy. This opens up a possibility of investigating the symmetry energy on nucleus-by-nucleus basis. Upon correcting energies of measured nuclear levels for shell and pairing effects, we find that the lowest energies for a given isospin rise in proportion to the square of isospin, allowing for an interpretation of the coefficient of proportionality in terms of a symmetry coefficient for a given nucleus. In the (A,Z) regions where there are enough data, we demonstrate a Z-independence of that coefficient. We further concentrate on the A-dependence of the coefficient, in order to learn about the density dependence of symmetry energy in uniform matter, given the changes of the density in the surface region. In parallel to the analysis of data, we carry out an analysis of the coefficient for nuclei calculated within the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) approach, with known symmetry energy for uniform matter. While the data from isobaric analog states suggest a simple interpretation for the A-dependent symmetry coefficient, in terms of the surface and volume symmetry coefficients, the SHF results point to a more complicated situation within the isovector sector than in the isoscalar, with much stronger curvature effects in the first. We exploit the SHF results in estimating the curvature contributions to the symmetry coefficient. That assessment is hampered by instabilities of common Skyrme parameterizations of nuclear interactions.

## Key claim

The excitation energies to isobaric analog states (IAS), after correcting for shell and pairing effects, rise in proportion to the square of isospin, allowing extraction of a mass-dependent symmetry coefficient for individual nuclei; however, the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) results indicate that the simple volume-surface interpretation of the A-dependent coefficient is effective rather than directly representing infinite nuclear matter, and the assessment is hampered by instabilities of common Skyrme parameterizations.

## Method

The authors used measured excitation energies of ground-state isobaric analog states from the compilation of Antony (1997), corrected for microscopic and deformation effects using shell corrections from Koura et al. (2005). For each isobaric chain (fixed mass number A), they plotted the corrected excitation energies against the scaled change in T(T+1) and performed linear least-square fits forced through the origin to extract the generalized symmetry coefficient a_a(A). In parallel, they carried out an analysis of the same coefficient for nuclei calculated within the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) approach, where the symmetry energy for uniform matter is known, to test the interpretation of the mass dependence.

## Key numbers

* The generalized symmetry coefficient a_a(A) rises with mass number within the range of approximately 10 to 23 MeV.
* For A >= 22, the mass dependence is well described by a volume-surface formula yielding a_a^V approximately 35.0 MeV and a_a^S approximately 10.0 MeV.
* The r.m.s. magnitude of residual unsubtracted microscopic contributions to the input energies was estimated as delta_rms = 0.5 MeV.
* For mass numbers A > 100, the coefficient meets the typical expectation of a_a >= 20 MeV; for A < 10, values as low as a_a ~ 10 MeV are reached.
* From the correlation between a_a^S and the slope L of the symmetry energy in half-infinite matter calculations, one could deduce L ~ 95 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Danielewicz (2003) and Danielewicz (2009) for extracting symmetry energy from nuclear masses by using isobaric analog states to isolate the symmetry contribution from Coulomb and symmetric-matter effects. The results contradict claims of a Z-dependence of the symmetry coefficient made by Janecke (2003, 2007) and PhysRevC.81.067302, finding no evidence for such variation. The analysis enables future constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy and neutron star pressures, but the SHF analysis shows that the extracted volume and surface coefficients are effective rather than representing infinite matter, with further work needed to assess the properties of the latter.
