# The Sun as a probe of Fundamental Physics and Cosmology

> The high quality data provided by helioseismology, solar neutrino flux measurements, spectral determination of solar abundances, nuclear reactions rates coefficients among other experimental data, leads to the highly accurate prediction of the internal structure of the present Sun - the standard solar model. In this talk, I have discussed how the standard solar model, the best representation of the real Sun, can be used to study the properties of dark matter, for which two complementary approaches have been developed: - to limit the number of theoretical candidates proposed as the dark matter particles, this analysis complements the experimental search of dark matter, and - as a template for the study of the impact of dark matter in the evolution of stars, which possibly occurs for stellar populations formed in regions of high density of dark matter, such as stars formed in the centre of galaxies and the first generations of stars.

## Key claim

The standard solar model (SSM), which is the best representation of the real Sun, can be used to constrain the properties of dark matter (DM) particles, complementing experimental searches and providing a template for studying DM's impact on stellar evolution, particularly for stars formed in high-density DM regions such as galactic centers or the first generations of stars.

## Method

The author reviews how the SSM, validated by helioseismology and solar neutrino flux data, is used to model the capture, evaporation, and annihilation of DM particles (assumed to be Weakly Interacting Massive Particles, or WIMPs) inside the Sun and other stars. The capture rate depends on the DM particle mass, its spin-dependent (SD) and spin-independent (SI) scattering cross-sections with baryons, and the star's radius and chemical composition. The impact of DM on stellar evolution is computed by adding an extra energy source from DM annihilation or an alternative energy transport mechanism, and the resulting changes in observable quantities (e.g., solar neutrino fluxes, stellar isochrones) are compared to data to exclude DM parameter space.

## Key numbers

- The relic dark matter density measured by WMAP and Planck is $\Omega_{DM}h^2 = 0.1109 \pm 0.0056$, and the baryonic asymmetry parameter is $\eta_B = (0.88 \pm 0.021) \times 10^{-10}$.
- The difference between the radial sound speed profile from helioseismology and the SSM is smaller than 2%.
- For a DM particle mass of 10 GeV and a cross section of $10^{-41} \text{ cm}^2$, experiments such as DAMA/LIBRA, COGENT, CRESST, and CDMS II suggest possible detection, but XENON 10-100 excludes such particles.
- The local DM density is assumed to be $0.38 \text{ GeV cm}^{-3}$, with recent estimates ranging from $0.3 \text{ GeV cm}^{-3}$ to $0.85 \text{ GeV cm}^{-3}$.
- For a star like the Sun, DM particles with a mass smaller than 5 GeV can evaporate from the star.
- For the star HD 52266 (1.1 $M_\odot$) and alpha Centauri B (0.9 $M_\odot$), particles with a mass of 5 GeV and a spin-dependent scattering cross-section smaller than $3 \times 10^{-36} \text{ cm}^2$ can be excluded at the 5-sigma level.
- The $^8$B solar neutrino flux variation due to DM is compared to the SSM; DM particles causing a variation larger than 15% can be excluded, as this exceeds the current SSM uncertainty.

## Context

This talk extends the use of the Sun as a probe of fundamental physics, building on prior work that used helioseismology and solar neutrinos to test neutrino oscillations (Lopes 2013) and alternative theories of gravity (Casadio 2012). It complements direct and indirect DM detection experiments (e.g., DAMA/LIBRA, XENON, CDMS II) by using stellar physics to constrain DM particle properties, as previously explored for neutron stars (Kouvaris 2012). The approach enables future studies of DM in stellar populations formed in high-density DM environments, such as the first stars or stars at galactic centers, and can be improved by using oscillation spectra of other sun-like stars (Casanellas and Lopes 2013).
