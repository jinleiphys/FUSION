# Quantum computation algorithm for many-body studies

> We show in detail how the Jordan-Wigner transformation can be used to simulate any fermionic many-body Hamiltonian on a quantum computer. We develop an algorithm based on appropriate qubit gates that takes a general fermionic Hamiltonian, written as products of a given number of creation and annihilation operators, as input. To demonstrate the applicability of the algorithm, we calculate eigenvalues and eigenvectors of two model Hamiltonians, the well-known Hubbard model and a generalized pairing Hamiltonian. Extensions to other systems are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a detailed algorithm for simulating any fermionic many-body Hamiltonian on a quantum computer using the Jordan-Wigner transformation, but the efficiency of the simulation depends on the Hamiltonian being expressible in a form that leads to polynomial complexity in the number of operations; for a general m-body interaction, the number of terms scales as order m², making higher-order interactions increasingly harder to simulate.

## Method

The authors apply the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map fermionic creation and annihilation operators onto tensor products of Pauli matrices acting on qubits, then decompose the resulting many-qubit Hamiltonian terms into sequences of single-qubit gates and CNOT two-qubit gates. The time evolution operator is approximated using a Trotter decomposition, and the algorithm is demonstrated by calculating eigenvalues and eigenvectors for two model Hamiltonians: the Hubbard model and a generalized pairing Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- For a system of P fermions placed into N levels, the total number of basis states is given by the binomial coefficient (N choose P), illustrating the exponential growth in dimensionality on classical computers.
- For P spin-1/2 particles, a classical computer needs 2^P bits to represent all possible states, while a quantum computer needs only P qubits, making the complexity in number of qubits linear.
- The time evolution matrix for n quantum levels is a 2^n × 2^n matrix, which would require at most 2^n(2^n-1)/2 two-level unitary matrices to factorize naively.
- For a given term in an m-body fermionic Hamiltonian, the number of operations needed to simulate it is linear in the number of qubits or energy-levels of the system.
- The number of terms in the Hamiltonian is of the order of m² for a general m-body interaction.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Somma et al. (2002) and Ortiz et al. (2002), who showed that the Jordan-Wigner transformation enables efficient simulation of fermionic systems on quantum computers, by providing a concrete algorithm that generates the specific qubit gates needed for any two-body fermionic Hamiltonian. The algorithm is demonstrated on the Hubbard model and a pairing Hamiltonian, the latter having exact solutions from Richardson (1963) that serve as benchmarks; the results enable future quantum computer simulations of more complex many-body systems, including those with three-body forces relevant to nuclear physics.
