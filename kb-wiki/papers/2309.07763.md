# Magnetic flutter effect on validated edge turbulence simulations

> Small magnetic fluctuations ($B_1/B_0 \sim 10^{-4}$) are intrinsically present in a magnetic confinement plasma due to turbulent currents. While the perpendicular transport of particles and heat is typically dominated by fluctuations of the electric field, the parallel stream of plasma is affected by fluttering magnetic field lines. In particular through electrons, this indirectly impacts the turbulence dynamics. Even in low beta conditions, we find that $E\times B$ turbulent transport can be reduced by more than a factor 2 when magnetic flutter is included in our validated edge turbulence simulations of L-mode ASDEX Upgrade. The primary reason for this is the stabilization of drift-Alfvén-waves, which reduces the phase shifts of density and temperature fluctuations with respect to potential fluctuations. This stabilization can be qualitatively explained by linear analytical theory, and appreciably reinforced by the flutter nonlinearity. As a secondary effect, the steeper temperature gradients and thus higher $η_i$ increase the impact of the ion-temperature-gradient mode on overall turbulent transport. With increasing beta, the stabilizing effect on $E\times B$ turbulence increases, balancing the destabilization by induction, until direct electromagnetic perpendicular transport is triggered. We conclude that including flutter is crucial for predictive edge turbulence simulations.

## Key claim

Even in low-beta conditions where direct electromagnetic transport is negligible, including magnetic flutter in validated edge turbulence simulations of L-mode ASDEX Upgrade reduces \(E \times B\) turbulent transport by more than a factor of 2, primarily through stabilization of drift-Alfvén-waves rather than through direct flutter transport.

## Method

The authors performed global full-\(f\) fluid turbulence simulations using the GRILLIX code with flux-coordinate independent (FCI) approach for the diverted ASDEX Upgrade tokamak across the edge and SOL regions from \(\rho=0.90\) to \(\rho=1.05\). They compared two sets of simulations, one with magnetic flutter included and one without, using the drift-reduced Braginskii equations, extracting the pure magnetic perturbation \(A_1\) by removing the toroidal and time average of \(A_\parallel\) to avoid double-counting the Shafranov shift.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic flutter amplitude \(B_1/B_0 \sim 10^{-4}\) in the confined region, consistent with experimental measurements
- Total heating power \(P_\mathrm{heat}\) saturates at approximately 2.6 MW without flutter and 1.3 MW with flutter, compared to the experimentally observed 0.5 MW
- Reference simulation parameters: \(\delta = 2854.2\), \(\beta_0 = 3.227 \times 10^{-5}\), \(\mu = 2.723 \times 10^{-4}\), \(\nu_{e0} = 12.30\), \(\eta_{\parallel 0} = 0.0017\)
- Time step \(\mathrm{d}t = 5 \times 10^{-5} \tau_0\) with \(\tau_0 = 23.84\ \mu\mathrm{s}\); time average period for computing \(A_1\) is \(t_\mathrm{avg} = 0.2 \tau_0\)
- Simulations run until 3.5 ms, with saturation reached after 2 ms; statistical diagnostics taken from 2.098 ms to 2.575 ms (1600 samples)

## Context

This work extends the earlier validated GRILLIX simulations of Zholobenko (2021) for ASDEX Upgrade discharge #36190 by adding magnetic flutter and neoclassical ion viscosity. It contradicts the common practice in many comprehensive edge turbulence codes (e.g., Stegmeir 2019, Giacomin 2020, Zholobenko 2021) that neglect flutter due to small direct transport. The results confirm the indirect stabilization mechanism first identified by Jenko (1999) in sheared slab geometry and extend it to full-size diverted tokamak geometry, demonstrating that flutter is crucial for predictive edge turbulence simulations even at low beta.
