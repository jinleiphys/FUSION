# Effective Field Theory analysis of ${}^3$He-$α$ scattering data

> We treat low-energy $^3$He-$α$ elastic scattering in an Effective Field Theory (EFT) that exploits the separation of scales in this reaction. We compute the amplitude up to Next-to-Next-to-Leading Order (NNLO), developing a hierarchy of the effective-range parameters that contribute at various orders. We use the resulting formalism to analyze data for recent measurements at center-of-mass energies of 0.38-3.12 MeV using the SONIK gas target at TRIUMF as well as older data in this energy regime. We employ a likelihood function that incorporates the theoretical uncertainty due to truncation of the EFT and use Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling to obtain the resulting posterior probability distribution. We find that the inclusion of a small amount of data on the analysing power $A_y$ is crucial to determine the sign of the p-wave splitting in such an analysis. The combination of $A_y$ and SONIK data constrains all effective-range parameters up to $O(p^4)$ in both s- and p-waves quite well. The ANCs and s-wave scattering length are consistent with a recent EFT analysis of the capture reaction ${}^3$He($α$,$γ$)${}^7$Be.

# Effective Field Theory analysis of ${}^3$He-$\alpha$ scattering data

## Key claim

The authors construct an Effective Field Theory (Halo EFT) for ${}^3$He-$\alpha$ elastic scattering up to Next-to-Next-to-Leading Order (NNLO) and demonstrate that, when combined with a Bayesian error model for EFT truncation and Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling, the inclusion of a small amount of analysing-power $A_y$ data is crucial to resolve the sign of the p-wave phase-shift splitting at low energies, even though p-wave contributions to cross sections become smaller as scattering energy decreases.

## Method

The authors develop a Halo EFT for ${}^3$He-$\alpha$ scattering using a dimer formalism with contact interactions for s-wave and p-wave channels, incorporating Coulomb interactions through the Coulomb Green's function. They match the EFT low-energy constants to the effective-range expansion parameters (ERPs) up to $O(p^4)$ in both s- and p-waves, and organize the amplitude into LO, NLO, and NNLO contributions based on a proposed power counting scheme. They analyze differential cross section data from the SONIK gas target experiment at TRIUMF (center-of-mass energies 0.38-3.12 MeV) together with older Barnard data (laboratory energy 2.5-5.7 MeV) and a few analysing-power data points, using a likelihood function that incorporates both experimental uncertainties and theoretical EFT truncation errors, with posterior sampling via Markov Chain Monte Carlo.

## Key numbers

* s-wave scattering length from SONIK data: $a_0 = 60 \pm 6$ fm
* EFT breakdown scale: $\Lambda \approx 150$-$200$ MeV
* Center-of-mass energy range of SONIK data: 0.38-3.12 MeV
* Laboratory energy range of Barnard data: 2.5-5.7 MeV
* ${}^7$Be ground-state binding energy: 1.6 MeV
* ${}^7$Be excited state energy: 1.2 MeV
* Proton separation energy of ${}^3$He: 5.5 MeV
* First excitation energy of the $\alpha$ particle: approximately 20 MeV
* Momentum (lab) validity region of the EFT: 60-90 MeV
* ${}^7$Be $\frac{7}{2}^-$ resonance laboratory energy: 5.22 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous Halo EFT analyses of the ${}^3$He($\alpha$,$\gamma$)${}^7$Be capture reaction by Higa et al. (2018), Zhang et al. (2020), and Premarathna et al. (2020) to the elastic scattering channel, and is the first EFT description of ${}^3$He-$\alpha$ elastic scattering data (as opposed to phase shifts). The results are consistent with optical model phase shifts from Mohr et al. (1993) and with R-matrix analyses of the SONIK data by Paneru (2021). The s-wave scattering length agrees with that obtained from capture data in the same EFT by Zhang et al. (2020), though the effective range $r_0$ values differ, indicating the need for experimental data clarifying the energy dependence of the s-wave phase shift. The analysis enables future work including combined EFT analyses of both scattering and capture data, extension to N$^3$LO, and consistent extrapolation to lower energies once higher-order electromagnetic effects are incorporated.
