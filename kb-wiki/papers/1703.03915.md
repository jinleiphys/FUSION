# Successive variational method of the tensor-optimized antisymmetrized molecular dynamics for central interaction in finite nuclei

> Tensor-optimized antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (TOAMD) is the basis of the successive variational method for nuclear many-body problem. We apply TOAMD to finite nuclei to be described by the central interaction with strong short-range repulsion, and compare the results with the unitary correlation operator method (UCOM). In TOAMD, the pair-type correlation functions and their multiple products are operated to the AMD wave function. We show the results of TOAMD using the Malfliet-Tjon central potential containing the strong short-range repulsion. Adding the double products of the correlation functions in TOAMD, the binding energies are converged quickly to the exact values of the few-body calculations for s-shell nuclei. This indicates the high efficiency of TOAMD for treating the short-range repulsion in nuclei. We also employ the s-wave configurations of nuclei with the central part of UCOM, which reduces the short-range relative amplitudes of nucleon pair in nuclei to avoid the short-range repulsion. In UCOM, we further perform the superposition of the s-wave configurations with various size parameters, which provides a satisfactory solution of energies close to the exact and TOAMD values.

# Successive variational method of the tensor-optimized antisymmetrized molecular dynamics for central interaction in finite nuclei

## Key claim

TOAMD with double products of the central-type correlation functions reproduces the exact few-body energies for s-shell nuclei using the Malfliet-Tjon central potential, but the convergence is demonstrated only for the central interaction without tensor force; the method's efficiency for the full nuclear force including tensor correlations is asserted from prior work rather than demonstrated in this paper.

## Method

The authors apply the tensor-optimized antisymmetrized molecular dynamics (TOAMD) framework using only the central-type short-range correlation function \(F_S\), multiplying it to the AMD wave function as a power series expansion \((1+F_S+F_S F_S)\Phi_{\rm AMD}\). The correlation functions are expressed as sums of Gaussians with variational parameters, and all resulting many-body operators from the cluster expansion are treated exactly without truncation. For comparison, the unitary correlation operator method (UCOM) is applied with a two-body approximation of the transformed Hamiltonian, using s-wave harmonic oscillator configurations and a generator coordinate method (GCM) superposition of basis states with different size parameters.

## Key numbers

- For \(^3\)H with MT-V potential: double TOAMD gives \(-8.24\) MeV, compared to the exact few-body value of \(-8.25\) MeV; single TOAMD gives \(-7.68\) MeV.
- For \(^4\)He with MT-V potential: double TOAMD gives \(-31.28\) MeV, compared to the exact value of \(-31.36\) MeV; single TOAMD gives \(-29.35\) MeV.
- UCOM with single HO configuration gives \(-6.51\) MeV for \(^3\)H and \(-30.77\) MeV for \(^4\)He; UCOM+GCM improves to \(-7.91\) MeV for \(^3\)H and \(-31.73\) MeV for \(^4\)He.
- The radius of \(^3\)H in double TOAMD is 1.67 fm at energy minimum, matching the few-body value of 1.68 fm; for \(^4\)He the TOAMD radius is 1.40 fm.
- The optimized range parameter \(\nu\) in double TOAMD is 0.11 fm\(^{-2}\) for \(^3\)H and 0.25 fm\(^{-2}\) for \(^4\)He.

## Context

This work extends the TOAMD method previously applied with the AV8' bare NN interaction (Myo 2015, Myo 2016) to isolate and test the treatment of short-range central correlations using the Malfliet-Tjon V potential. The results are compared with the unitary correlation operator method of Feldmeier and Neff (1998, 2004), which uses a two-body approximation for the transformed Hamiltonian. The success for s-shell nuclei enables future application of TOAMD to clustering phenomena such as the Hoyle state in \(^{12}\)C, where the power series expansion of correlation functions would need to be extended to higher orders for multi-cluster systems.
