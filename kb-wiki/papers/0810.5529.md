# The strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma created at RHIC

> The Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) was built to re-create and study in the laboratory the extremely hot and dense matter that filled our entire universe during its first few microseconds. Its operation since June 2000 has been extremely successful, and the four large RHIC experiments have produced an impressive body of data which indeed provide compelling evidence for the formation of thermally equilibrated matter at unprecedented temperatures and energy densities -- a "quark-gluon plasma (QGP)". A surprise has been the discovery that this plasma behaves like an almost perfect fluid, with extremely low viscosity. Theorists had expected a weakly interacting gas of quarks and gluons, but instead we seem to have created a strongly coupled plasma liquid. The experimental evidence strongly relies on a feature called "elliptic flow" in off-central collisions, with additional support from other observations. This article explains how we probe the strongly coupled QGP, describes the ideas and measurements which led to the conclusion that the QGP is an almost perfect liquid, and shows how they tie relativistic heavy-ion physics into other burgeoning fields of modern physics, such as strongly coupled Coulomb plasmas, ultracold systems of trapped atoms, and superstring theory.

## Key claim

The quark-gluon plasma (QGP) created at RHIC behaves like an almost perfect fluid with extremely low viscosity, contrary to the theoretical expectation of a weakly interacting gas of quarks and gluons. The claim is hedged by the fact that the experimental data for elliptic flow exhaust the ideal fluid dynamic prediction only when using Glauber model initial conditions; with Color Glass Condensate (CGC) initial conditions, which give up to 30% larger initial eccentricities, the measured elliptic flow reaches only about 2/3 of the ideal fluid limit, opening some room for viscous effects.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental evidence from RHIC heavy-ion collisions, focusing on three key observations: large elliptic flow measured as the second Fourier coefficient of the azimuthal hadron distribution in non-central collisions; collective flow of heavy charm and bottom quarks; and jet quenching, where high-transverse-momentum hadrons are suppressed by a factor 5-6 compared to scaled proton-proton collisions. The analysis compares experimental data with ideal and viscous relativistic hydrodynamic simulations, using initial conditions from either a geometric Glauber model or the Color Glass Condensate model, and extracts constraints on the shear viscosity to entropy ratio from elliptic flow measurements.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for QGP hadronization: \(T_c = 173 \pm 15\) MeV, corresponding to critical energy density \(e_c \simeq 0.7\) GeV/fm\(^3\), from lattice QCD.
- Chemical decoupling temperature from hadron yield ratios in Au+Au collisions: \(T_\mathrm{chem} = 163 \pm 4\) MeV, with baryon chemical potential \(\mu_B = 24 \pm 4\) MeV.
- Strangeness saturation factor in central and semi-central collisions: \(\gamma_s = 0.99 \pm 0.07\).
- Suppression of high-\(p_T\) hadrons: factor 5-6, almost independent of \(p_T\) in the region \(4 < p_T < 15\) GeV/\(c\).
- Upper limit on shear viscosity to entropy ratio: \(\eta/s < 0.5\), with \(\eta/s > 3 \times (1/4\pi) \approx 0.24\) difficult to accommodate, where \((1/4\pi) \approx 0.08\) is the conjectured lower limit from AdS/CFT.
- Lorentz contraction factor for heavy ions at RHIC: \(\gamma \approx 110\).
- Thermalization time required by hydrodynamic simulations: about 1 fm/\(c\).

## Context

This review extends the work of Muller and Nagle (2006) and Shuryak (2008) by synthesizing the experimental evidence that established the QGP as a strongly coupled liquid rather than a weakly interacting gas. The findings connect relativistic heavy-ion physics to strongly coupled Coulomb plasmas, ultracold trapped atom systems (where the unitary limit may produce a similarly perfect fluid), and superstring theory via the AdS/CFT correspondence. The results enable a program to extract precise values for QGP transport coefficients from phenomenological modeling of experimental data, with first results from Luzum and Romatschke (2008) providing an upper limit on \(\eta/s\).
