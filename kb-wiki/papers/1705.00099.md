# Critical assessment of nuclear sensitivity metrics for the r-process

> Any simulation of the r-process is affected by uncertainties in our present knowledge of nuclear physics quantities and astrophysical conditions. It is common to quantify the impact of these uncertainties through a global sensitivity metric, which is then used to identify specific nuclides that would be most worthwhile to measure experimentally. Using descriptive statistics, we assess a set of metrics used in previous sensitivity studies, as well as a new logarithmic measure. For certain neutron-rich nuclides lying near the r-process path for the typical hot-wind scenario, we find opposing conclusions on their relative sensitivity implied by different metrics, although they all generally agree which ones are the most sensitive nuclei. The underlying reason is that sensitivity metrics which simply sum over variations in the r-process distribution depend on the scaling used in the baseline, which often varies between simulations. We show that normalization of the abundances causes changes in the reported sensitivity factors and recommend reporting a minimized F statistic in addition to a scale estimation for rough calibration to be used when comparing tables of sensitivity factors from different studies.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that different global sensitivity metrics used to rank which neutron-rich nuclides most influence r-process abundance patterns can yield opposing conclusions on the relative importance of specific isotopes, even though they generally agree on the most sensitive nuclei overall. The root cause is that these metrics depend on the arbitrary scaling or normalization of the simulated abundance baseline, which varies between simulations.

## Method

The authors used a waiting-point approximation r-process simulation (SiRop code, an extension of r-Java 2.0) with a parametric density trajectory and initial temperature of 3e9 K, varying the masses of individual isotopes from 128Cd to 139Te by plus or minus 0.0005% (approximately 0.6 MeV). They computed four global sensitivity metrics (two absolute differences, one relative difference, and one new logarithmic ratio) for each mass variation and compared the resulting rankings of isotope sensitivity, then analyzed how normalization of the abundance distributions affects the computed sensitivity values.

## Key numbers

* Baseline simulation used density trajectory rho = rho0/(1+t/2tau)^2 with rho0 = 1e11 g/cm^3 and tau = 0.001 sec, initial temperature T = 3e9 K, and initial composition 50% 70Fe by mass (neutron-to-seed ratio of 62)
* Mass variations were plus or minus 0.0005% of the isotope mass, corresponding to approximately 0.6 MeV
* For metric A1 (absolute mass fraction difference), the default sensitivity factor for 132Sn (mass decreased) was 53.6, which changed to 50.7 after minimization (a 5.4% reduction)
* For metric R2 (log-ratio), the default sensitivity factor for 137Sb (mass decreased) was 10.3, which changed to 6.32 after minimization (a 38.8% reduction)
* The maximum abundance in the baseline was Y_max = 0.165 at A=132
* For metric A1, the reported values were of order A_max * Y_max = 21.8; for metric A2, values were of order Y_max = 0.165

## Context

This work extends sensitivity studies by Mumpower (2016) and others (PhysRevC.92.035807, 2012EPJA...48..184B, 2012PhRvC..86c5803M) that used global sensitivity metrics to identify key isotopes for r-process measurements. The paper is the first to compare multiple metric definitions on a single simulation, showing that metrics A1 and A2 (absolute differences) overweight changes near abundance peaks while metrics R1 and R2 (relative and log-ratio) respond more evenly across the distribution. The authors recommend reporting a minimized F statistic and a scale calibration (peak abundance) to enable comparison of sensitivity factors from different studies, and caution against relying on any single global sensitivity metric as the sole analysis tool.
