# Theory overview of Heavy Ion collisions

> This presentation discusses some recently active topics in the theoretical interpretation of high energy heavy ion collisions at the LHC and at RHIC. We argue that the standard paradigm for understanding the spacetime evolution of the bulk of the matter produced in the collision is provided by viscous relativistic hydrodynamics, which can be used to systematically extract properties of the QCD medium from experimental results. The initial conditions of this hydrodynamical evolution are increasingly well understood in terms of gluon saturation, and can be quantified using Classical Yang-Mills fields and QCD effective kinetic theory. Hard and electromagnetic probes of the plasma provide additional constraints. A particularly fascinating subject are high multiplicity proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions, where some of the characteristics previously attributed to only nucleus-nucleus collisions have been observed.

## Key claim

The standard paradigm for understanding the spacetime evolution of the bulk of matter produced in high energy heavy ion collisions is provided by viscous relativistic hydrodynamics, but the applicability of this framework to small systems (high multiplicity proton-proton and proton-nucleus collisions) remains an open question where both hydrodynamical flow and initial-state color field correlations can generate the observed azimuthal multiparticle correlations.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to heavy ion collisions, focusing on the initial stages described by gluon saturation and Classical Yang-Mills fields, the subsequent evolution modeled by viscous relativistic hydrodynamics, and the use of hard and electromagnetic probes to constrain medium properties. The author discusses QCD effective kinetic theory (EKT) calculations that interpolate between the anisotropic glasma initial state and viscous hydrodynamics, as well as Bayesian global fitting strategies to extract transport coefficients like the shear viscosity to entropy density ratio η/s from experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The transition to a deconfined phase occurs at a temperature around 150 MeV, as confirmed by lattice QCD studies.
- The IPglasma model calculates energy density at a time of 0.2 fm after a heavy ion collision, with color charge density from a fit to HERA data.
- The saturation scale Qs grows with energy such that Qs >> Λ_QCD, enabling a weak coupling expansion around a nonperturbatively large classical field.
- Bayesian fits to experimental heavy ion data favor values of the generalized mean parameter p ≈ 0 for the initial entropy density parametrization, consistent with semihard initial particle production.
- For small systems, different commonly used models of initial particle production generate differences of more than a factor of 2 in the coefficients ε_n.

## Context

This conference summary extends the line of work on the "Condensed Matter Physics of QCD" paradigm, where heavy ion collisions at LHC and RHIC create macroscopic amounts of QCD matter. The paper discusses recent advances in understanding thermalization via QCD effective kinetic theory (Arnold 2002), the IPglasma model (Schenke 2012) based on HERA data (Kowalski 2006), and Bayesian global fits (Bernhard 2016) for extracting medium properties. It enables further work on distinguishing between hydrodynamical flow and initial-state correlations in small systems, and on implementing new theoretical developments for jet quenching into Monte Carlo event generators.
