# Discrete Wave-Packet Representation in Nuclear Matter Calculations

> The Lippmann-Schwinger equation for the nucleon-nucleon $t$-matrix as well as the corresponding Bethe-Goldstone equation to determine the Brueckner reaction matrix in nuclear matter are reformulated in terms of the resolvents for the total two-nucleon Hamiltonians defined in free space and in medium correspondingly. This allows to find solutions at many energies simultaneously by using the respective Hamiltonian matrix diagonalization in the stationary wave packet basis. Among other important advantages, this approach simplifies greatly the whole computation procedures both for coupled-channel $t$-matrix and the Brueckner reaction matrix. Therefore this principally novel scheme is expected to be especially useful for self-consistent nuclear matter calculations because it allows to accelerate in a high degree single-particle potential iterations. Furthermore the method provides direct access to the properties of possible two-nucleon bound states in the nuclear medium. The comparison between reaction matrices found via the numerical solution of the Bethe-Goldstone integral equation and the straightforward Hamiltonian diagonalization shows a high accuracy of the method suggested. The proposed fully discrete approach opens a new way to an accurate treatment of two- and three-particle correlations in nuclear matter on the basis of three-particle Bethe-Faddeev equation by an effective Hamiltonian diagonalization procedure.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the Bethe–Goldstone equation for the Brueckner reaction matrix in nuclear matter can be reformulated so that its solution is obtained via a single diagonalization of the total two-nucleon Hamiltonian matrix in a stationary wave-packet basis, rather than requiring separate solutions at many energies; the method is tested only under the angle-average approximation for the Pauli operator and for a parametrized single-particle spectrum.

## Method

The authors discretize the continuous spectrum of the free two-nucleon Hamiltonian into non-overlapping momentum bins and construct an orthonormal stationary wave-packet basis. For free-space scattering, the total Hamiltonian matrix is diagonalized once in this basis to obtain pseudostates, and the off-shell \(t\)-matrix at any energy is reconstructed from the finite spectral expansion of the total resolvent. For nuclear matter, the Bethe–Goldstone equation is rewritten using an effective Hamiltonian \(\hat{H}_Q(K) = \hat{H}_{0Q}(K) + \hat{Q}^{1/2} \hat{V} \hat{Q}^{1/2}\) in the Pauli-allowed subspace, and the reaction matrix is obtained from the same diagonalization procedure applied to this effective Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- Phase shifts for uncoupled spin-singlet and spin-triplet channels using the CD-Bonn NN potential are shown for laboratory energies from zero up to 800 MeV; the diagonalization results are "almost indistinguishable" from direct numerical solution of the Lippmann–Schwinger equation.
- Coupled-channel phase shifts and mixing parameters for the \(^3S_1-{}^3D_1\) and \(^3F_2-{}^3P_2\) channels are presented over the same energy range (0–800 MeV) with "very good accuracy" compared to the direct matrix solution.
- The binding energy per nucleon (equation of state) is given by \(\frac{E}{A} = \frac{3}{k_F^3} \int_0^{k_F} k^2\,dk\, \frac12\left[\frac{k^2}{2m} + \varepsilon(k)\right]\), where \(\varepsilon(k)\) is the self-consistent single-particle energy; no numerical values for the binding energy are reported in the truncated text.

## Context

This work extends the wave-packet continuum discretization technique previously developed by the same authors (Annals, 2014) for few-body scattering to the Bethe–Goldstone equation in nuclear matter, which is central to Brueckner–Bethe–Goldstone calculations (Muether, 1999; Tabakin, 1970). The method is intended to accelerate the self-consistent iteration of the single-particle potential in BHF calculations and to enable efficient treatment of three-body correlations via the Bethe–Faddeev equation (Sartor, 2000). The approach also provides direct access to possible two-nucleon bound states in the medium, such as di-neutron states discussed by Ding (2016).
