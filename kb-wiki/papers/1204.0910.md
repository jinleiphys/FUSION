# Phase Transition and Anisotropic Deformations of Neutron Star Matter

> The Skyrme model is a low energy, effective field theory for QCD which when coupled to a gravitational field provides an ideal semi-classical model to describe neutron stars. We use the Skyrme crystal solution composed of a lattice of $α$-like particles as a building block to construct minimum energy neutron star configurations, allowing the crystal to be strained anisotropically. We find that below 1.49 solar masses the stars' crystal deforms isotropically and that above this critical mass, it undergoes anisotropic strain. We then find that the maximum mass allowed for a neutron star is 1.90 solar masses, in close agreement with a recent observation of the most massive neutron star yet found. The radii of the computed solutions also match the experimentally estimated values of approximately 10km.

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron star matter modeled as a Skyrme crystal undergoes a phase transition at a critical mass of 1.49 solar masses: below this mass the crystal deforms isotropically, while above it the crystal deforms anisotropically with less strain in the radial direction than in the tangential direction. The maximum mass of 1.90 solar masses is achieved only when anisotropic deformations are allowed, contradicting the common assumption of isotropic matter in neutron star models.

## Method

The authors used the Skyrme model as a low-energy effective field theory for QCD, taking the face-centred cubic lattice of B=4 Skyrmions (alpha-like particles) as the building block for neutron star matter. They combined the equation of state for this crystal under anisotropic deformations, computed by Castillejo et al. (1989), with a generalized Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation that allows for anisotropic stresses, following the formalism of Bowers and Liang (1974). The minimum energy configurations for given baryon numbers were found numerically using a simulated annealing algorithm to optimize the radial and tangential Skyrmion lengths as functions of radius.

## Key numbers

- Critical mass for isotropic-to-anisotropic phase transition: 1.49 solar masses (baryon number 2.61 x 10^57)
- Maximum mass of neutron star: 1.90 solar masses (baryon number 3.41 x 10^57)
- Radii of computed solutions: approximately 10 km, matching experimental estimates
- For the heaviest isotropic star (1.49 solar masses): radius 10,367.5 m
- For the heaviest anisotropic star (1.90 solar masses): radius approximately 9,500 m
- Energy per baryon at maximum mass: 9.97510 x 10^-11 J (for baryon number 3.25 x 10^57)
- Global minimum energy of the Skyrme crystal: 727.4 MeV per baryon at number density corresponding to L_0 = 1.666 x 10^-15 m
- Skyrme parameters used: F_pi = 129 MeV, e = 5.45 (from Adkins, Nappi and Witten, 1983)

## Context

This work extends previous Skyrme model approaches to neutron stars by Walhout (1987, 1990), who assumed isotropic compression of Skyrmion lattices and obtained a maximum mass of 2.57 solar masses, and by Jaikumar and Ouyed (2005), who used a Skyrme fluid and obtained 3.6 solar masses. The key difference is that the present work treats the neutron star as a solid crystal that can deform anisotropically, rather than as an isotropic fluid or gas. The maximum mass of 1.90 solar masses is in close agreement with the then-most-recent observation of the most massive neutron star found. The authors' earlier work (Piette 2007, Nelmes 2011) using layered 2D Skyrme lattices achieved only a maximum mass of 0.574 solar masses due to energy overestimation in their ansatz.
