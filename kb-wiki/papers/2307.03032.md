# Quarkyonic matter and quarkyonic stars in an extended RMF model

> By combining RMF models and equivparticle models with density-dependent quark masses, we construct explicitly ``a quark Fermi Sea'' and ``a baryonic Fermi surface'' to model the quarkyonic phase, where baryons with momentums ranging from zero to Fermi momentums are included. The properties of nuclear matter, quark matter, and quarkyonic matter are then investigated in a unified manner, where quarkyonic matter is more stable and energy minimization is still applicable to obtain the microscopic properties of dense matter. Three different covariant density functionals TW99, PKDD, and DD-ME2 are adopted in our work, where TW99 gives satisfactory predictions for the properties of nuclear matter both in neutron stars and heavy-ion collisions and quarkyonic transition is unfavorable. Nevertheless, if PKDD with larger slope of symmetry energy $L$ or DD-ME2 with larger skewness coefficient $J$ are adopted, the corresponding EOSs are too stiff according to both experimental and astrophysical constraints. The situation is improved if quarkyonic transition takes place, where the EOSs become softer and can accommodate various experimental and astrophysical constraints.

## Key claim

By combining RMF models and equivparticle models with density-dependent quark masses, the authors construct a quarkyonic phase where baryons with momentums ranging from zero to Fermi momentums are included (rather than simply removing lower momentum components as in prior work). The quarkyonic transition is unfavorable for the TW99 functional, but improves the too-stiff equations of state from PKDD and DD-ME2, making them consistent with experimental and astrophysical constraints.

## Method

The authors combine relativistic mean field (RMF) models for baryonic matter with equivparticle models for quark matter, using density-dependent baryon and quark masses. Baryon-baryon interactions are treated via exchange of σ, ω, and ρ mesons with density-dependent couplings from three functionals (TW99, PKDD, DD-ME2), while quarks are quasi-free particles with density-dependent masses including confinement and perturbative interactions. The quark-hadron interface in momentum space is set by matching single-particle energies, and energy minimization determines the microscopic properties of quarkyonic matter under charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium.

## Key numbers

- TW99 predicts saturation density n0 = 0.153 fm⁻³, binding energy B = -16.24 MeV, incompressibility K = 240.2 MeV, symmetry energy S = 32.8 MeV, slope L = 55.3 MeV
- PKDD predicts n0 = 0.150 fm⁻³, B = -16.27 MeV, K = 262.2 MeV, S = 36.8 MeV, L = 90.2 MeV
- DD-ME2 predicts n0 = 0.152 fm⁻³, B = -16.13 MeV, K = 250.8 MeV, S = 32.3 MeV, L = 51.2 MeV
- For TW99 with (B=300 MeV/fm³, C=0.7, √D=180 MeV): maximum mass M_TOV = 2.04 M⊙, radius R₁.₄ = 12.27 km, tidal deformability Λ₁.₄ = 405
- For PKDD with (B=150, C=0.7, √D=150): M_TOV = 2.06 M⊙, R₁.₄ = 12.80 km, Λ₁.₄ = 530
- For DD-ME2 with (B=100, C=0.5, √D=160): M_TOV = 2.06 M⊙, R₁.₄ = 12.74 km, Λ₁.₄ = 557
- Quarkyonic transition onset occurs at around 1.4n0 for symmetric nuclear matter and at n_on ≈ 0.1 fm⁻³ for pure neutron matter
- Maximum sound speed v_max for quarkyonic matter ranges from 0.63c to 0.83c depending on parameters

## Context

This work extends the RMF approach by including quark degrees of freedom, building on the authors' previous study (Xia 2018) and the quarkyonic matter concept proposed by McLerran and Pisarski (2007) in the large Nc limit. Unlike prior treatments that simply removed lower momentum components (McLerran 2019, Margueron 2021, Cao 2020, Cao 2022), this work includes baryons with momentums from zero to Fermi momentums, analogous to Cooper pair formation. The results enable testing quarkyonic matter against pulsar observations (PSR J1614-2230 at 1.928 M⊙, PSR J0348+0432 at 2.01 M⊙) and gravitational wave constraints from GW170817 (70 ≤ Λ₁.₄ ≤ 580), and suggest that quarkyonic transitions are favored if nuclear matter has large symmetry energy slope L (as from PREX-2) or large skewness coefficient J.
