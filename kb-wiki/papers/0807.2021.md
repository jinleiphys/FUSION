# Canonical quantum potential scattering theory

> A new formulation of potential scattering in quantum mechanics is developed using a close structural analogy between partial waves and the classical dynamics of many non-interacting fields. Using a canonical formalism we find non-linear first-order differential equations for the low energy scattering parameters like scattering length and effective range. They significantly simplify typical calculations, as we illustrate for atom-atom and neutron-nucleus scattering systems. A generalization to charged particle scattering is also possible.

## Key claim

The paper reformulates quantum potential scattering using a canonical (Hamiltonian) formalism that yields first-order nonlinear differential equations for low-energy scattering parameters (scattering length, effective range, shape parameter), which can simplify calculations compared to solving the second-order Schrödinger equation, but the method has limitations for long-range potentials in higher partial waves due to a constant slope of the scattering length function.

## Method

The authors construct a classical "Hamiltonian" function from the coefficients of the Riccati-Bessel and Riccati-Neumann function expansion of the radial Schrödinger equation solution, treating the radial coordinate as a "time" variable. They perform a canonical transformation to render the new coordinate cyclic, which yields first-order nonlinear differential equations (Calogero-type equations) for the scattering length function \(a_l(k,r)\) and, through expansion in \(k\), for the effective range and shape parameter functions. These equations are integrated numerically using a variable transformation that maps the spatial domain and function range onto compact intervals to handle singularities from bound states.

## Context

This work extends the Calogero equation approach (Calogero, 1967) for the phase shift function by deriving a canonical (Hamiltonian) formulation that yields first-order differential equations directly for the scattering length, effective range, and shape parameter. The method is applied to atom-atom scattering with long-range van der Waals potentials (Flambaum, 1999) and neutron-nucleus scattering with short-range Woods-Saxon potentials (Baye, 2005), reproducing results from conventional Schrödinger equation solutions. The formalism enables direct calculation of low-energy scattering parameters without solving the full radial Schrödinger equation at multiple momenta, and it provides a framework for developing new approximation schemes, though it faces numerical challenges for higher partial waves in long-range potentials and for the shape parameter.
