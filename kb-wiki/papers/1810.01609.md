# Simulating the weak death of the neutron in a femtoscale universe with near-Exascale computing

> The fundamental particle theory called Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) dictates everything about protons and neutrons, from their intrinsic properties to interactions that bind them into atomic nuclei. Quantities that cannot be fully resolved through experiment, such as the neutron lifetime (whose precise value is important for the existence of light-atomic elements that make the sun shine and life possible), may be understood through numerical solutions to QCD. We directly solve QCD using Lattice Gauge Theory and calculate nuclear observables such as neutron lifetime. We have developed an improved algorithm that exponentially decreases the time-to solution and applied it on the new CORAL supercomputers, Sierra and Summit. We use run-time autotuning to distribute GPU resources, achieving 20% performance at low node count. We also developed optimal application mapping through a job manager, which allows CPU and GPU jobs to be interleaved, yielding 15% of peak performance when deployed across large fractions of CORAL.

## Key claim

The authors claim that their improved algorithm exponentially decreases the time-to-solution for calculating the neutron axial coupling \(g_A\) from Lattice QCD, but the actual hedge is that this exponential improvement comes from accessing parameter space where the signal-to-noise is exponentially larger, and the full calculation still requires near-Exascale computing resources on the CORAL supercomputers Sierra and Summit.

## Method

The authors directly solve Quantum Chromodynamics using Lattice Gauge Theory on a finite space-time grid, employing a Monte Carlo approach to compute the axial coupling of the neutron \(g_A\). They developed an improved algorithm that accesses parameter space with exponentially larger signal-to-noise, and applied it using run-time autotuning to distribute GPU resources and a job manager called mpi_jm that allows CPU and GPU jobs to be interleaved on dense-node architectures.

## Key numbers

- Achieved 20% performance at low node count using run-time autotuning to distribute GPU resources on the CORAL supercomputers Sierra and Summit.
- Achieved 15% of peak performance when deployed across large fractions of CORAL using the mpi_jm job manager to interleave CPU and GPU jobs.
- The transition from Titan to Sierra/Summit shows a \(\sim 35\times\) increase in flops per node, compared to only a \(\sim 3\times\) increase in network bandwidth per node.
- On Sierra, a 4224 node job was brought up and running in 3-5 minutes using the lump-based startup process with MVAPICH2.
- The experimental neutron lifetime values are \(\tau_n^{trap}=879.4(6)\) seconds and \(\tau_n^{beam}=888(2)\) seconds from two different measurement techniques.
- The Standard Model prediction for neutron lifetime is \(\tau_n = \frac{5172.0 \pm 1.0 }{1 + 3 g_A^2}\) seconds, requiring \(g_A\) with 0.2% precision to impact the experimental discrepancy.

## Context

This work extends the Lattice QCD community's previous estimate that a determination of \(g_A\) with 2% precision would be possible by approximately 2020 with the CORAL generation of supercomputers (USQCD, 2016). The authors had previously achieved a 1% precision determination of \(g_A\) (Berkowitz 2017, Chang 2017), and this work enables pushing toward the 0.2% precision needed to resolve the experimental discrepancy between trapped neutron and neutron beam lifetime measurements. The mpi_jm job manager is made available on github for widespread adoption beyond Lattice QCD applications.
