# An overview of the CUJET model: Jet Flavor Tomography applied at RHIC and LHC

> Jet Flavor Tomography is a powerful tool used to probe the properties of Quark Gluon Plasma formed in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. A new Monte Carlo model of jet quenching developed at Columbia University, CUJET, was applied to predict the jet flavor and centrality dependence of the nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$. The predictions for fragments $f=π,D,B,e$, derived from quenched jet flavors $a=g,u,c,b$ in central and peripheral collisions at RHIC and LHC, exhibit novel features such as a level crossing pattern in $R_{AA\rightarrow a\rightarrow f}$ over a broad transverse momentum range which can test jet-medium dynamics in quark gluon plasmas and help discriminate between current energy loss models. Furthermore, the inclusion of running coupling effects seems to change the jet energy dependence of the jet energy loss to a non trivial constant behavior, with a visible impact on the predictions for $R_{AA}$.

## Key claim

The CUJET model predicts a novel level crossing pattern in the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) for different hadron flavors (\(\pi, D, B, e\)) over a broad transverse momentum range, which can discriminate between energy loss models; however, the inclusion of running coupling effects changes the jet energy dependence of energy loss to a non-trivial constant behavior, and the fixed-coupling LHC extrapolation systematically overpredicts quenching compared to preliminary ALICE and CMS data.

## Method

CUJET uses Monte Carlo techniques to compute finite order in opacity contributions to the jet-medium induced gluon radiative spectrum, limited here to first order in opacity with a pure HTL dynamical interaction potential. The energy loss is computed from the induced radiated gluon number per collinear light cone momentum fraction, with fluctuations included via Poisson expansion and elastic losses estimated using the Thoma-Gyulassy model. The plasma is modeled as inhomogeneous (Glauber profile) and nonstatic (1+1D Bjorken expansion), with density constrained by observed rapidity density \(dN/dy\), and the final state nuclear modification factors are obtained by averaging over initial jet production points and directions and convoluting over pQCD initial jet cross sections and in-vacuum fragmentation.

## Key numbers

- At RHIC (\(\sqrt{s}=0.2\) GeV), the initial rapidity density is set to \(dN/dy = 1000\); at LHC (\(\sqrt{s}=2.76\) GeV), it is set to \(dN/dy = 2200\).
- The initial thermalization time is \(\tau_0 = 1\) fm/c, and the decoupling temperature is \(T \approx 100\) MeV.
- The strong coupling constant \(\alpha_s = 0.3\) is fixed by fitting the reference point \(R_{AuAu}^\pi(p_T=10\ \text{GeV}) = 0.2\) at RHIC.
- Elastic energy losses contribute approximately \(25-30\%\) of the total energy loss.
- For running coupling calculations, the parameter \(\alpha_0 = 0.4\) is fixed by fitting \(R_{AA}^{LHC}(p_T=40\ \text{GeV}) = 0.35\) for pions.

## Context

This work extends the GLV, DGLV, and WHDG opacity series approaches (Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev; Djordjevic, Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev; Wicks, Horowitz, Djordjevic, Gyulassy) by including dynamical jet interaction potentials, running coupling effects, and full path proper time integration over expanding QGP geometries. The predictions aim to resolve the "heavy quark puzzle" at RHIC and improve agreement with preliminary LHC data from ALICE and CMS, enabling more stringent constraints on jet-medium dynamics and energy loss models.
