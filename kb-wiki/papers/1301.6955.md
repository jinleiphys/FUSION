# Neutron-rich nuclei and the equation of state of stellar matter

> In this contribution we will review our present understanding of the matter equation of state in the density and temperature conditions where it can be described by nucleonic degrees of freedom. At zero temperature, all the information is contained in the nuclear energy functional in its isoscalar and isovector channels. At finite temperature, particular emphasis will be given to the specificity of the thermodynamics in the nucleonic regime, with the simultaneous presence of long range electromagnetic and short range nuclear interactions. The astrophysical implications of the resulting phase diagram, as well as of different observables of exotic nuclei on the neutron-rich side, will be touched upon.

## Key claim

The paper reviews the current understanding of the nuclear matter equation of state under conditions relevant to supernovae and neutron stars, but emphasizes that the density dependence of the symmetry energy remains poorly constrained even at subsaturation densities, and that all experimental constraints come from comparisons to non-ab-initio nuclear models, making systematic error bars difficult to evaluate.

## Method

The author surveys theoretical approaches to the nuclear matter equation of state including Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF), relativistic Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DBHF), quantum Monte Carlo, chiral effective field theory, and phenomenological density functional methods (Skyrme and relativistic mean-field). Experimental constraints are drawn from isoscalar giant monopole resonance measurements in spherical nuclei, heavy-ion collision data (transverse flow, isospin diffusion, subthreshold kaon production), neutron skin measurements, pygmy dipole resonance studies, and astrophysical observations of neutron star masses and radii from X-ray data. At finite temperature, the paper discusses the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter and its modification by Coulomb frustration in stellar matter, using nuclear statistical equilibrium (NSE) models with excluded volume and self-consistent Hartree-Fock interactions.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: \(\rho_0 = 0.166 \pm 0.018\) fm\(^{-3}\) from electron scattering
- Energy per particle at saturation: \(E/A = -16 \pm 1\) MeV from the bulk term of the mass formula
- Incompressibility coefficient constraint: \(K_\infty = 240 \pm 30\) MeV from monopole resonance and heavy-ion collision data
- Incompressibility of the NL3 relativistic mean-field parameter set: \(K_\infty = 272\) MeV
- Baryonic density range in supernova cores: from \(\rho > 10^{10}\) g\(\cdot\)cm\(^{-3}\) \(\approx 10^{-4}\rho_0\) to several times \(\rho_0\)
- Temperature range in supernova cores: between some hundreds of keV and around 20 MeV
- Proton fraction range in supernova cores: between approximately 0.5 and 0.3
- Proton fractions in neutron stars: as low as approximately 0.1-0.2
- Density below which hyperonic degrees of freedom can be neglected: below about 0.3 fm\(^{-3}\)
- Temperature for the NSE model calculation shown in Figure 8: \(T = 1.6\) MeV
- Asymmetry chemical potential for the NSE model calculation: \(\mu_I = 1.68\) MeV

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the nuclear matter equation of state that began with Brueckner (early theoretical framework), Day and Pandharipande (late 1970s microscopic studies), and Vautherin, Brack, and Walecka (early phenomenological approaches). It discusses constraints from giant monopole resonance calculations by Colo (2004) and heavy-ion flow analyses by Fuchs and Lynch, as well as symmetry energy constraints from mass fits (Moller), isospin diffusion (Tsang), and neutron thickness measurements (Zenihiro). The paper enables future work by identifying that the marginal compatibility between laboratory constraints (probing subsaturation densities) and astrophysical constraints (probing supersaturation densities) may suggest new phenomena at high density, and that the observed bimodality in nuclear fragmentation by the INDRA collaboration (2009) provides a compelling signature of the liquid-gas phase transition that needs to be explored with exotic beams.
