# NuGNN: a Graph Neural Network for Nuclear Reaction Network Equations

> Nuclear reaction networks are a major computational bottleneck in astrophysical simulations when large isotope sets are required, because of the stiffness of the network equations and the repeated calls to Jacobian-based solvers required by implicit methods. In this work, we develop a deep learning surrogate solver for a large 690-isotope nuclear reaction network under general Type I X-ray burst conditions using a graph neural network, NuGNN. Unlike conventional fully connected or convolutional neural networks, NuGNN directly reflects the structure of the reaction network through heterogeneous isotope and reaction nodes and message-passing along reaction connections. The model is trained on data spanning many orders of magnitude in stellar temperature and density and in simulation time step size. We compare NuGNN with a Res-U-Net and fully connected neural network and find that NuGNN consistently achieves significantly better accuracy with errors of only a few percent. More importantly, when implemented in the network evolution code in place of the original solver, NuGNN successfully reproduces the final abundance patterns, whereas the other architectures fail to do so. We also show that the trained model can substantially improve computational speed, demonstrating its practical potential for large-scale simulations. These results show that graph neural networks provide a robust and promising framework for accurate surrogate modeling of large nuclear reaction networks.

## Key claim

NuGNN, a graph neural network surrogate solver, can replace the Jacobian construction and implicit solver step for a 690-isotope nuclear reaction network under Type I X-ray burst conditions, achieving errors of only a few percent and successfully reproducing final abundance patterns where Res-U-Net and fully connected architectures fail.

## Method

The authors trained NuGNN on 90,000 input-output pairs from the PRISM reaction network code, where each pair corresponds to one time step of the implicit Euler integration scheme with Newton-Raphson iterations. The graph neural network represents the reaction network as a heterogeneous bipartite graph with isotope nodes and reaction nodes, using message-passing along three connection types (isotope-to-isotope, isotope-to-reaction, and reaction-to-isotope) with attention mechanisms and separate learned layers for each reaction type. Training data spanned three categories: physically realistic samples from X-ray burst thermodynamic trajectories (Type A), augmented samples with randomly rescaled compositions (Type B), and fully random synthetic samples (Type C), with labels preprocessed as signed logarithms of relative abundance change.

## Key numbers

* Training data ranges: temperature from 10^6 to 2.5 x 10^9 K, density from 10^-2 to 10^8 g cm^-3, time step size from 10^-15 to 10^-4 s
* Network size: 690 isotopes in the reaction network
* Total training samples: 90,000 (30,000 per data type), split 0.8/0.1/0.1 into training/validation/test
* NuGNN total mean absolute error on test dataset: 0.037 (versus 0.11 for Res-U-Net and 0.56 for FNN)
* NuGNN error on Type A data (physically realistic): 0.0079 (versus 0.020 for Res-U-Net and 0.034 for FNN)
* NuGNN sign accuracy: approximately 97%
* NuGNN architecture: five message-passing blocks, each with two fully connected layers of 64 hidden channels per reaction type
* Res-U-Net architecture: three encoder blocks, bottleneck, three decoder blocks with channel sizes 64, 128, 256, 512, 256, 128, 64

## Context

This work extends prior efforts by Fan (2022) on 3-isotope networks for Type Ia supernovae, Grichener (2025) on 80- and 151-isotope networks for core-collapse supernova progenitors, and Zhang (2025) on 3- and 13-isotope networks, all of which used fully connected architectures with limited temperature and density ranges (about 1-2 orders of magnitude) and fixed time step sizes. NuGNN demonstrates that graph neural networks can handle a much larger 690-isotope network across 3 orders of magnitude in temperature and 10 orders of magnitude in density with variable time step sizes, enabling future multi-dimensional astrophysical simulations that currently must use reduced reaction networks.
