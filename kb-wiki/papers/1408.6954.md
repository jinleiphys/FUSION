# New charge radius relations for atomic nuclei

> We show that the charge radii of neighboring atomic nuclei, independent of atomic number and charge, follow remarkably very simple relations, despite the fact that atomic nuclei are complex finite many-body systems governed by the laws of quantum mechanics. These relations can be understood within the picture of independent-particle motion and by assuming neighboring nuclei having similar pattern in the charge density distribution. A root-mean-square (rms) deviation of 0.0078 fm is obtained between the predictions in these relations and the experimental values, i.e., a comparable precision as modern experimental techniques. Such high accuracy relations are very useful to check the consistence of nuclear charge radius surface and moreover to predict unknown nuclear charge radii, while large deviations from experimental data is seen to reveal the appearance of nuclear shape transition or coexsitence.

## Key claim

The authors propose that charge radii of neighboring nuclei satisfy simple difference equations (e.g., δR₁ₚ₋₁ₙ ≈ 0) that hold with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.0078 fm from experimental data, a precision comparable to modern experimental techniques. The hedge is that the relations assume neighboring nuclei have similar patterns in charge density distribution, and they work better for heavier systems (A > 60) while showing larger deviations for light nuclei and in regions of shape transition or shape coexistence.

## Method

The authors constructed difference equations for charge radii of neighboring nuclei, analogous to Garvey-Kelson mass relations, by assuming that the charge density distribution pattern is similar for neighboring nuclei. They tested three relations (δR₁ₚ₋₁ₙ, δR₁ₚ₋₂ₙ, δR₂ₚ₋₁ₙ) against experimental charge radius data from the compilation of Angeli (2013), using weighted averaging of multiple predictions for each nucleus when available. The predictive power was evaluated by comparing predicted values to experimental data across the nuclear chart, with special attention to regions of known shape transitions.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation between predictions and experimental values: 0.0078 fm for all nuclei with Z ≥ 2 and N ≥ 2
- Weighted mean value of δR₁ₚ₋₁ₙ: -0.7 × 10⁻³ fm with weighted rms deviation of 5 × 10⁻³ fm
- For nuclei with Z ≥ 20, global rms deviation decreases to 0.0069 fm; for light nuclei with Z < 20, it increases to 0.0194 fm
- For nuclei with n ≥ 5 predictions, rms deviations are around 0.004 fm
- 788 nuclei could be predicted using the relations; 325 nuclides could be predicted within accuracy of about 0.01 fm
- Only 17 nuclei have known charge radii that can be predicted in all 12 possible ways

## Context

This work extends the concept of Garvey-Kelson mass relations (Garvey and Kelson, 1969) to nuclear charge radii, providing a simple empirical approach that outperforms global nuclear models. The relations achieve significantly better precision than microscopic models such as the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov model (Goriely, 2009, 2010), relativistic mean-field theory (Geng, 2003; Zhao, 2010), and the four-parameter formula with shell corrections (Wang, 2013), which yield rms deviations of more than 0.02 fm. The relations enable prediction of unknown charge radii and provide a new probe for identifying nuclear shape transitions and shape coexistence, complementing other observables such as mass and spectroscopic data.
