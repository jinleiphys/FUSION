# Large-Momentum Effective Theory's Asymptotic Extrapolation vs the Inverse Problem

> Large-Momentum Effective Theory (LaMET) is a physics-guided systematic expansion to calculate light-cone parton distributions, including collinear (PDFs) and transverse-momentum-dependent ones, at any fixed momentum fraction $x$ within a range of $[x_{\rm min}, x_{\rm max}]$. It theoretically solves the ill-posed inverse problem that afflicts other theoretical approaches to collinear PDFs, such as short-distance factorizations. Recently, arXiv:2504.17706 [1] raised practical concerns about whether current or even future lattice data will have sufficient precision in the sub-asymptotic correlation region to support an error-controlled extrapolation -- and if not, whether it becomes an inverse problem where the relevant uncertainties cannot be properly quantified. While we agree that not all current lattice data have the desired precision to qualify for an asymptotic extrapolation, some calculations do, and more are expected in the future. We comment on the analysis and results in Ref. [1] and argue that a physics-based systematic extrapolation still provides the most reliable error estimates, even when the data quality is not ideal. In contrast, re-framing the long-distance asymptotic extrapolation as a data-driven-only inverse problem with ad hoc mathematical conditioning could lead to unnecessarily conservative errors.

## Key claim

LaMET is a forward-problem formalism that theoretically solves the ill-posed inverse problem afflicting short-distance factorization approaches to collinear PDFs, but the authors concede that not all current lattice data have sufficient precision in the sub-asymptotic correlation region to qualify for an error-controlled asymptotic extrapolation, though some calculations do and more are expected in the future.

## Method

The authors compare LaMET and short-distance factorization (SDF) as two frameworks for extracting parton distributions from lattice QCD matrix elements of the form h(z,P) = (1/2P^t)⟨P| ψ̄(z) W(z,0) γ^t ψ(0) |P⟩. LaMET uses data at all distances z to compute quasi-PDFs via Fourier transform, then applies a perturbative matching kernel to obtain light-cone PDFs at fixed momentum fraction x within [x_min, x_max]. The asymptotic extrapolation of the coordinate-space correlation at large z is guided by the physics constraint that the correlator decays exponentially with a decay length controlled by the residual mass of a heavy-light meson, independent of hadron momentum. The authors test this by examining effective-mass plots from lattice data to identify the plateau region where exponential decay dominates.

## Key numbers

* The LaMET expansion is reliable within a moderate range of x ∈ [x_min, x_max] with x_min ≈ 1 − x_max ∼ Λ_QCD/(2P^z), where Λ_QCD is the QCD scale.
* The SDF framework is reliable only up to z = 0.2–0.3 fm, corresponding to the breakdown of perturbation theory when α_s(1/z) ∼ 1.
* The maximum λ = zP^z reachable in SDF is λ_max ∼ 3.0–4.0, far from the asymptotic region where the light-cone correlation decays algebraically.
* For the nucleon, the sub-asymptotic region begins at z_SA ∼ 0.5 fm and the asymptotic region at z_A ∼ 1.2 fm, with the exponential decay plateau reached after z ∼ 0.75 fm for the nucleon transversity quasi-PDF data from Ref. [LatticeParton:2022xsd].
* The residual mass in the renormalized correlator can be chosen to be on the order of Λ_QCD, say 0.2–0.3 GeV, depending on the renormalon regularization scheme.

## Context

This paper responds to concerns raised in Dutrieux et al. (2025) (arXiv:2504.17706) about whether lattice data in the sub-asymptotic region (0.7 fm ≲ z ≲ 1.0 fm) are precise enough for controlled extrapolation in LaMET analyses. The authors argue that LaMET is fundamentally different from SDF approaches such as pseudo-PDF (Radyushkin, 2017) and good lattice cross sections (Ma, 2017), which require reconstructing PDFs from a limited segment of leading-twist correlation and constitute an inverse problem with uncontrolled uncertainties. The work extends the extensive discussion in Ji et al. (2020, 2022, 2024) on the distinction between LaMET and SDF, and enables continued use of physics-guided asymptotic extrapolation for future high-precision lattice calculations of parton distributions.
