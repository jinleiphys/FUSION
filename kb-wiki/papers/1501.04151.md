# The neutron drip line: single-particle degrees of freedom and pairing properties as sources of theoretical uncertainties

> The sources of theoretical uncertainties in the prediction of the two-neutron drip line are analyzed in the framework of covariant density functional theory. We concentrate on single-particle and pairing properties as potential sources of these uncertainties. The major source of these uncertainties can be traced back to the differences in the underlying single-particle structure of the various covariant energy density functionals (CEDF). It is found that the uncertainties in the description of single-particle energies at the two-neutron drip line are dominated by those existing already in known nuclei. Only approximately one third of these uncertainties are due to the uncertainties in the isovector channel of CEDF's. Thus, improving the CEDF description of single-particle energies in known nuclei will also reduce the uncertainties in the prediction of the position of two-neutron drip line. The predictions of pairing properties in neutron rich nuclei depend on the CEDF. Although pairing properties affect moderately the position of the two-neutron drip line they represent only a secondary source for the uncertainties in the definition of the position of the two-neutron drip line.

## Key claim

The major source of theoretical uncertainties in predicting the two-neutron drip line using covariant density functional theory is traced to differences in the underlying single-particle structure of the various covariant energy density functionals (CEDFs), with uncertainties in single-particle energies at the drip line dominated by those already present in known nuclei rather than by uncertainties in the isovector channel.

## Method

The authors performed systematic relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations for all even-even nuclei from the proton drip line to the neutron drip line using four different CEDFs (NL3*, DD-ME2, DD-MEδ, and DD-PC1) with a separable finite-range pairing interaction. They analyzed the global behavior of neutron pairing energies and pairing gaps across the nuclear chart, and studied the impact of varying pairing strength on the position of the two-neutron drip line, particularly in the Rn isotope chain. Single-particle spectra at spherical shape were compared for selected nuclei to assess the role of shell structure.

## Key numbers

- For the CEDF NL3*, absolute values of neutron pairing energies near the two-neutron drip line are higher by a factor of 3-4 than those in known nuclei; this factor reduces to about 2 for DD-ME2 and becomes even smaller for DD-MEδ and DD-PC1.
- In the Yb isotope chain (Z=70), neutron pairing gaps Δ_uv in neutron-rich N≥126 nuclei are on average 25% larger than in known nuclei for NL3*, while for the three density-dependent sets the gaps have on average the same magnitude as in known nuclei.
- For the Rn isotope chain (Z=86), the two-neutron drip line is located at N=206 for NL3* and at N=184 for DD-ME2, DD-MEδ, and DD-PC1.
- Decreasing the pairing strength by 8% in NL3* calculations for Rn isotopes causes nuclei with N=186, 188, 190, 202, 204, and 206 to become unbound, shifting the drip line from N=206 to a primary position at N=184 with a secondary peninsula at N=192-200.
- Increasing the pairing strength by 8% in DD-ME2 and DD-PC1 calculations for Rn isotopes does not affect the position of the two-neutron drip line.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of theoretical uncertainties in drip-line predictions from earlier studies by Erler et al. (2012) and Afanasjev et al. (2013, 2014), which compared results across relativistic and non-relativistic density functional theories. The paper systematically investigates two proposed sources of uncertainty, single-particle structure and pairing properties, that had not been examined in detail previously. The findings enable future efforts to reduce drip-line uncertainties by focusing on improving the description of single-particle energies in known nuclei, and they highlight that even careful fitting of pairing forces to known nuclei does not guarantee reliable predictive power toward the neutron drip line.
