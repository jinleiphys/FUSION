# Time-Dependent Generator Coordinate Method for Many-Particle Tunneling

> It has been known that the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method, or the time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT), fails to describe many-body quantum tunneling. We overcome this problem by superposing a few time-dependent Slater determinants with the time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM). We apply this method to scattering of two $α$ particles in one dimension, and demonstrate that the TDGCM method yields a finite tunneling probability even at energies below the Coulomb barrier, at which the tunneling probability is exactly zero in the TDHF. This is the first case in which a many-particle tunneling is simulated with a microscopic real-time approach.

## Key claim
The time-dependent generator coordinate method (TDGCM) can simulate many-body quantum tunneling by superposing a few time-dependent Slater determinants, yielding a finite tunneling probability at energies below the Coulomb barrier where the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method gives exactly zero. This is demonstrated for a simplified one-dimensional alpha-alpha scattering system as a proof of principle, with the caveat that the exact tunneling probability is difficult to evaluate with only two Slater determinants.

## Method
The authors apply the TDGCM to one-dimensional scattering of two alpha particles, parameterizing single-particle wave functions as Gaussians with fixed width and assuming symmetric motion about x=0. They construct a superposition of 2 or 10 Slater determinants with different initial relative distances and relative momenta as generator coordinates, then evolve both the weight functions and the Slater determinants simultaneously using the time-dependent variational principle, without replacing the Slater determinant evolution by TDHF trajectories.

## Key numbers
- The Coulomb barrier between the two alpha particles is located at 3.84 fm with a height of 0.13 MeV, while the potential at x=0 is 7.1 MeV (frozen density approximation).
- For the two-Slater-determinant calculation: initial positions of x0 = ±30.0/2 fm with E = 0.113 MeV for the first determinant, and x0 = ±30.1/2 fm with E = 0.100 MeV for the second; the average initial relative energy is 0.099 MeV, below the Coulomb barrier.
- The fraction for the transmitted trajectory changes from 0.5 at t=0 to 0.489 at t=∞, which the authors suggest might be identified with the tunneling probability.
- For the 10-Slater-determinant calculation: initial distances centered at 30.0/2 fm with Gaussian width 0.1 fm; initial energies centered at 0.1 MeV with width 0.02 MeV; average energy of the ensemble is 0.11 MeV; 3 out of 10 Slater determinants undergo fusion while 7 are reflected.

## Context
This work addresses the known failure of the TDHF method (and TDDFT) to describe quantum many-body tunneling, as noted by Negele (1982), Simenel (2012), and Nakatsukasa (2016). The approach is inspired by the entangled trajectory molecular dynamics (ETMD) method from quantum chemistry (Donoso and Martens, 2001; Wang et al., 2009, 2012; Xu et al., 2016), which simulates tunneling using classical test particles. The TDGCM presented here differs from the time-dependent Hill-Wheeler method used in fission studies by Zdeb (2017), Regnier (2016), Bernard (2011), Goutte (2005), and Tao (2017), because those works use static Slater determinants while this method uses time-dependent ones that automatically include non-adiabatic effects. This proof-of-principle study enables future applications to heavier nuclear reactions in three dimensions, nuclear fission, and chemical reactions.
