# Nuclear shape dynamics in low-energy heavy-ion reactions

> We discuss recent theoretical developments in low-energy heavy-ion reactions. To this end, we put emphasis on a viewpoint of probing nuclear shapes with heavy-ion reactions. We first discuss a single-channel problem with an optical potential model. We particularly discuss a microscopic modeling of the imaginary part of an optical potential as well as a visualization of quantum interference phenomena observed in heavy-ion elastic scattering. We then discuss multi-channel scattering problems, and demonstrate that heavy-ion fusion reactions at energies around the Coulomb barrier are sensitive to the shape of colliding nuclei, providing a powerful tool to probe nuclear shapes. We finally point out that relativistic heavy-ion collisions have large similarities to low-energy heavy-ion reactions in the context of nuclear shape dynamics.

## Key claim

The paper argues that low-energy heavy-ion reactions, particularly fusion at energies around the Coulomb barrier, are sensitive to the shape of colliding nuclei and thus provide a powerful tool to probe nuclear shapes; however, this sensitivity is conditional on the adiabatic approximation where the rotational energy of deformed nuclei is much smaller than the typical energy scale of the reaction, allowing the orientation angle to be frozen during the collision.

## Method

The author reviews single-channel scattering using an optical potential model with a complex potential, then extends to multi-channel coupled-channels calculations that incorporate nuclear deformation. For deformed nuclei, the orientation average formula integrates fusion cross sections over all orientation angles of the target nucleus, assuming the rotational Hamiltonian can be neglected. The fusion barrier distribution is defined as the second derivative of the product of energy and fusion cross section with respect to energy, and its shape is analyzed to extract deformation parameters including hexadecapole deformation. For relativistic collisions, the eccentricity parameter of the initial density distribution is computed using deformed Woods-Saxon densities, comparing static deformation (fixed deformation parameters with random Euler angles) against dynamical surface vibrations (random deformation parameters from harmonic oscillator ground state fluctuations).

## Key numbers

- For the $^{16}$O+$^{154}$Sm system, the Coulomb barrier height is $V_b$ = 60.35 MeV, estimated with a typical Woods-Saxon potential
- Deformation parameters for $^{154}$Sm are $(\beta_2,\beta_4) = (0.33, 0.05)$ and for $^{186}$W are $(\beta_2,\beta_4) = (0.29, -0.03)$
- Extracted deformation parameters for $^{24}$Mg are $(\beta_2,\beta_4) = (0.43 \pm 0.02, -0.11 \pm 0.02)$ from Bayesian analysis of quasi-elastic barrier distribution
- Extracted deformation parameters for $^{28}$Si are $(\beta_2,\beta_4) = (-0.38 \pm 0.01, 0.03 \pm 0.01)$
- The STAR collaboration extracted the deformation parameter of $^{238}$U as $\beta_2 = -0.286 \pm 0.025$ and $\gamma = 8.7 \pm 4.8^\circ$
- For $^{58}$Ni, the dynamical deformation parameter $\beta_2$ is estimated as 0.218 from the measured $B(E2)$ for the transition from ground state to first $2^+$ state at 1.45 MeV
- Mean eccentricity values for $^{58}$Ni+$^{58}$Ni collision are $\langle|\epsilon_2|\rangle = 0.112$ for surface vibration and 0.119 for static deformation

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels framework for heavy-ion fusion reactions developed by Balantekin (1998), Hagino (2012), and Dasgupta (1998), who established that sub-barrier fusion cross sections are enhanced by nuclear deformation and that barrier distributions provide a fingerprint of reaction dynamics. The paper connects this low-energy approach to recent work in relativistic heavy-ion collisions by Jia (2024) and the STAR collaboration (2025), who have used similar adiabatic approximations to extract nuclear deformation parameters from ultra-central collisions. The imaging technique for quantum interference in elastic scattering builds on the double-slit analogy for nearside-farside interference proposed by Hussein (1984) and was subsequently applied to string theory scattering by Hashimoto (2023). The eigenvector continuation emulator method extends the work of Frame (2018) and Duguet (2024) from bound-state problems to nuclear reaction calculations, enabling efficient Bayesian parameter estimation.
