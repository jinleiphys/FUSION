# Towards experimental search for the atomic effect in alpha decay: comparison of the adiabatic approach with the frozen shell model

> We study the important for the experiment features of the effect of the electron shell on alpha decay in the adiabatic approach. The effect is of tenths of a percent or less in magnitude, depending on the transition energy and the atomic number. On the other hand, we show the predominant role of the inner shells: more than 80% of the effect are due to the 1s electrons. This fact is crucial for the experiment, allowing one to perform measurements in the same storage rings, comparing for example, the probability of decay in the bare nuclei and Helium-like ions. The analysis is presented concerning the reasons for the relative success and the limits of applicability of the model of "frozen" electron shell used to calculate the effect for more than half a century.

## Key claim
The electron shell effect on alpha decay is of order 10⁻³ or less, but more than 80% of the effect comes from 1s electrons, enabling measurements in storage rings by comparing decay probabilities in bare nuclei versus helium-like ions rather than neutral atoms. The adiabatic approach gives an upper bound for the effect, which is several times smaller than predictions from the frozen shell model used for over half a century.

## Method
The authors compute the electron term energy for two-center quasimolecular states using relativistic Dirac-Fock wavefunctions from the RAINE package, then calculate the tunneling probability through the Coulomb barrier for bare nuclei and for neutral atoms using the action integral with the adiabatic potential. The relative change in decay probability Y = (P/P_at) - 1 is evaluated for six alpha-emitters spanning from Nd-144 to element 294, comparing the adiabatic first-order perturbation approach with the frozen shell model.

## Key numbers
- For Ra-226, the 1s electron contribution to the effect is 81% at a typical distance R = 40 fm; L1 and L2 shells contribute 13% and 1.4% respectively.
- The relative change Y in half-periods between bare nuclei and neutral atoms ranges from 0.02% for Rn-214 to 0.28% for Cf-252.
- For Nd-144 (Q = 1.905 MeV, half-life 2.29 × 10¹⁵ yr), Y = 0.24%.
- For Ra-226 (Q = 4.871 MeV, half-life 1600 yr), Y = 0.23%.
- For element 294 (Q = 11.65 MeV, half-life 0.89 ms), Y = 0.27%.
- The difference between the frozen shell potential U_e(0) and the true energy change ΔQ is approximately 50 keV for typical alpha-emitters.
- The non-relativistic calculation of Δε(R) differs from the relativistic result by a factor of about 4 even in the barrier region at R ≈ 200 fm, and by an order of magnitude at R ≈ 10 fm.

## Context
This work extends the adiabatic approach of Karpeshin (earlier work) for alpha decay in neutral atoms and contradicts the frozen shell model used by Perlman (earlier work), Rasmussen (earlier work), and others for decades. The results are relevant for planned experiments at GSI and Lanzhou storage rings, and for (n,α) experiments with MeV neutrons as in Gledenov (earlier work). The paper also connects to muon-induced fission studies by Oberacker (earlier work) and Leander (earlier work), where adiabatic quasimolecular states similarly affect decay probabilities.
