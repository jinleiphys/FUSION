# Nuclear Structure in the Framework of the Unitary Correlation Operator Method

> Correlations play a crucial role in the nuclear many-body problem. We give an overview of recent developments in nuclear structure theory aiming at the description of these interaction-induced correlations by unitary transformations. We focus on the Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM), which offers a very intuitive, universal and robust approach for the treatment of short-range correlations. We discuss the UCOM formalism in detail and highlight the connections to other methods for the description of short-range correlations and the construction of effective interactions. In particular, we juxtapose UCOM with the Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) approach, which implements the unitary transformation of the Hamiltonian through a very flexible flow-equation formulation. The UCOM- and SRG-transformed interactions are compared on the level of matrix elements and in many-body calculations within the no-core shell model and with Hartree-Fock plus perturbation theory for a variety of nuclei and observables. These calculations provide a detailed picture of the similarities and differences as well as the advantages and limitations of unitary transformation methods.

# Nuclear Structure in the Framework of the Unitary Correlation Operator Method

## Key claim

The Unitary Correlation Operator Method (UCOM) provides a universal and intuitive tool for treating short-range central and tensor correlations in nuclear many-body systems through explicit unitary transformations, but the two-body approximation of the transformed interaction cannot fully substitute three-body forces, especially for heavier nuclei and saturation properties.

## Method

The authors formulate UCOM in momentum representation, harmonic oscillator basis, and operator representation, using explicit correlation functions to describe short-range central and tensor correlations. They compare UCOM-transformed interactions with Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG)-evolved interactions by performing no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations and Hartree-Fock plus second-order many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) calculations for sequences of closed-shell nuclei from helium-4 to lead-208. Optimal correlation functions are determined either by energy minimization in two-body space or through mapping to the SRG approach.

## Key numbers

- UCOM(var.) and UCOM(SRG) interactions yield Hartree-Fock ground-state energies per nucleon of about 4 to 5 MeV throughout the mass range from helium-4 to lead-208, with a practically constant offset of about 4 MeV per nucleon between HF energies and experiment for UCOM(var.)
- For lead-208, the UCOM interaction underestimates the charge radius by about 1 fm, while the SRG interaction underestimates it by about 2 fm
- The second-order MBPT estimate of correlation energy shows changes of typically 0.5 MeV per nucleon when going from 13 to 15 major oscillator shells for heavier isotopes
- The harmonic oscillator basis used for HF calculations includes 15 major shells (2n+l less than or equal to 14), with oscillator lengths ranging from 1.3 fm to 2.4 fm

## Context

This work extends the Unitary Correlation Operator Method developed by the same authors and compares it systematically with the Similarity Renormalization Group approach for nuclear structure calculations. The paper demonstrates that UCOM-transformed interactions allow bound Hartree-Fock calculations throughout the nuclear chart, whereas SRG-evolved interactions show overbinding at the HF level due to neglected induced many-body forces. The results enable ab initio calculations for heavier nuclei using effective two-body interactions, though the authors note that three-body forces remain necessary for correct saturation properties, as also indicated by earlier work on nuclear matter. The paper connects to prior developments in NCSM calculations (Roth 2009), coupled cluster methods (Wloch 2005, Kowalski 2004, Hagen 2007, 2008, 2009), and various perturbation theory approaches (Barbieri 2006, Dickhoff 2004).
