# Mass splitting of vector meson and spontaneous spin polarization under rotation

> In the present paper, we study the effect of the rotation on the masses of scalar meson as well as vector meson in the framework of 2-flavor Nambu--Jona-Lasinio model. The existence of rotation causes a tedious quark propagator and corresponding polarization function. Applying the random phase approximation, the meson mass is calculated numerically. It is found that the behavior of scalar and pseudoscalar meson masses under the angular velocity $ω$ is similar to that at finite chemical potential, both rely on the behavior of constituent quark mass and reflect the property related to the chiral symmetry. However, masses of vector meson $ρ$ have more profound relation with rotation. After tedious calculation, it turns out that at low temperature and small chemical potenial, the mass for spin component $s_z=0,\pm 1$ of vector meson under rotation shows very simple mass splitting relation $m_ρ^{s_z}(ω)=m_ρ(ω=0)-ωs_z$, similar to the Zeeman splitting of charged meson under magnetic fields. Especially it is noticed that the mass of spin component $s_z=1$ vector meson $ρ$ decreases linearly with $ω$ and reaches zero at $ω_c=m_ρ(ω=0)$, this indicates the system will develop $s_z=1$ vector meson condensation and the system will be spontaneously spin polarized under rotation.

## Key claim

The authors claim that at low temperature and small chemical potential, the mass of each spin component \(s_z = 0, \pm 1\) of the vector \(\rho\) meson under rotation follows the simple splitting relation \(m_{\rho}^{s_z}(\omega) = m_{\rho}(\omega=0) - \omega s_z\), analogous to Zeeman splitting in a magnetic field; however, this result is derived within the 2-flavor Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model using the random phase approximation (RPA) and the pole approximation, and is valid only in the regime where the chiral condensate is not yet restored.

## Method

The authors employed the 2-flavor NJL model with scalar and vector four-fermion interactions in a co-rotating frame, incorporating a spinor connection term that couples rotation to the total angular momentum. Using the mean-field approximation for the chiral condensate and the RPA for meson polarization functions, they computed the masses of scalar (\(\sigma\)), pseudoscalar (\(\pi\)), and vector (\(\rho\)) mesons as functions of angular velocity \(\omega\), temperature \(T\), and quark chemical potential \(\mu\). The numerical calculations used a soft cutoff regulator \(\Lambda = 582\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and coupling constants \(G_S \Lambda^2 = 2.388\) and \(G_V \Lambda^2 = 1.73\), with a current quark mass \(m_0 = 5\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Key numbers

- The critical angular velocity for the \(s_z = 1\) component of the \(\rho\) meson to reach zero mass is \(\omega_c = m_{\rho}(\omega=0)\), where \(m_{\rho}(\omega=0)\) is the \(\rho\) meson mass in the absence of rotation.
- The soft cutoff parameter is \(\Lambda = 582\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- The scalar coupling constant is \(G_S \Lambda^2 = 2.388\) and the vector coupling constant is \(G_V \Lambda^2 = 1.73\).
- The current quark mass is \(m_0 = 5\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- At low temperature (\(T = 10\ \mathrm{MeV}\)) and zero chemical potential, a first-order chiral phase transition occurs at \(\omega \simeq 0.8\ \mathrm{GeV}\).
- The study is restricted to quark chemical potentials \(\mu_q < 200\ \mathrm{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of meson masses under rotation using the NJL model, particularly following the analysis of chiral phase transitions in rotating matter by Jiang et al. (2016) and Wang et al. (2018). It also parallels earlier investigations of \(\rho\) meson mass splitting under magnetic fields by Liu et al. (2014) and Liu et al. (2018), where a similar Zeeman-like splitting was observed for charged \(\rho\) mesons. The finding that the \(s_z = 1\) component mass vanishes at \(\omega_c\) suggests the possibility of spontaneous spin polarization and vector meson condensation in rapidly rotating systems, which could be relevant for interpreting polarization measurements in heavy-ion collisions, such as those from the STAR collaboration (2017) and ALICE collaboration (2019).
