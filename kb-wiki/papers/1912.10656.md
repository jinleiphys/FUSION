# Toward precision jet event shape for future Electron-Ion Collider

> We present angularity differential cross-section for the deep inelastic scattering process (DIS) in the framework of soft-collinear effective theory (SCET). Using SCET, the cross-section is factorized in terms of hard, jet, beam and soft functions. Our result includes resummation of the large logarithms up to next-to-next leading logarithmic (NNLL) accuracy. The numerical results presented here for DIS angularity cross-section can be explored by the future EIC.

## Key claim
The paper presents the first SCET-based factorization and NNLL resummation for the DIS angularity event shape, but the numerical results are preliminary and limited to the choice-I axis definition (jet axis aligned with the physical jet), not the choice-II axis (Breit-frame hemispheres).

## Method
The authors use soft-collinear effective theory (SCET) to factorize the DIS angularity differential cross-section into hard, jet, beam, and soft functions. They compute the one-loop beam function for DIS angularity at \(\mathcal{O}(\alpha_s)\) for the first time, combine it with known one-loop jet and soft functions from Hornig (2009) and two-loop numerical results from Bell (2018), and perform resummation of large logarithms up to NNLL accuracy using renormalization group evolution with profile functions.

## Key numbers
- Center-of-mass energies considered: \(\sqrt{s}=45\) and \(140\ \text{GeV}\) (EIC design parameters).
- Fixed kinematics for numerical results: \(x=0.2\), \(Q=60\ \text{GeV}\), \(\sqrt{s}=140\ \text{GeV}\).
- Angularity parameter values shown: \(a=-0.5\) and \(a=0\) (the latter corresponds to DIS thrust).
- Rapidity range covered by EIC: \(0.01 \leq y \leq 0.95\).

## Context
This work extends the SCET-based event-shape program from \(e^+e^-\) collisions (where thrust was resummed to N\(^3\)LL by Becher (2008) and angularity to NNLL by Bell (2018)) and from proton-proton collisions (Stewart 2010, Berger 2010) to deep inelastic scattering. It builds on earlier DIS thrust studies by Kang (2013, 2014) and provides the first NNLL prediction for DIS angularity, enabling future EIC measurements to test QCD resummation and extract the strong coupling \(\alpha_s\) with improved precision.
