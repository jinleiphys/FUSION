# Resonances in Small Fermi Systems

> The systematics of the size dependence of the resonant response of small metal particles and nuclei to incident electromagnetic radiation is studied. The known radius$^{-1}$ variation of the full width at half maximum (FWHM) in matrix-embedded metal particles is qualitatively accounted for by a quantum calculation of the response within a simple model. In free clusters, the behaviour is more complicated, possibly because of thermal excitation of surface modes. For nuclei, the FWHM shows strong shell-structure-linked oscillations across the periodic table. Focussing on the lower envelope of the oscillations (magic nuclei), the downward trend of the FWHM is consistent with the radius$^{-1}$ variation. A schematic theoretical description of the systematics in nuclei is presented. If the FWHMs are scaled by the respective Fermi energies and the inverse radii by the Fermi wave vectors, the data sets for matrix-embedded metal particles and nuclei become comparable in magnitude.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the full width at half maximum (FWHM) of the resonant response in both matrix-embedded metal particles and nuclei follows a radius$^{-1}$ variation, but only when focusing on the lower envelope of the oscillations in nuclei (magic nuclei) and after scaling by Fermi energies and wave vectors; the claim is qualified by the fact that free clusters do not show this simple behavior, possibly due to thermal excitation of surface modes.

## Method

The authors performed a quantum calculation of the Mie resonance width in small metal particles using linear response theory within the random phase approximation (RPA), modeling the confining potential as an infinitely deep spherical well and evaluating the dielectric constant via one-electron energies and eigenfunctions. For nuclei, they examined the FWHM of the giant dipole resonance (GDR) in about 120 nuclei ranging from $^3He$ to $^{239}Pu$, using compiled photoneutron cross-section data and extracting FWHM values by drawing smooth curves through data points, including cases with split peaks. They then compared the systematics across both systems by scaling the FWHMs by Fermi energies and inverse radii by Fermi wave vectors.

## Key numbers

- For matrix-embedded metal particles, the FWHM follows $\Gamma_{av} = K ~{\hbar v_F \over R} + \Gamma_\infty$, where $K$ is a constant of order unity and depends on the matrix (e.g., $K$ changes by a factor of about 3 when the matrix is changed from glass to an inert element solid like Ar or Ne).
- The quantum calculation yields $\Gamma = {3 \over 4} {G(\nu_0) \over \nu_0}  {v_F \over R}$, where in the limit $\nu \rightarrow 0$ the ratio $G(\nu)/\nu$ approaches unity.
- For nuclei, the peak frequency follows $\omega_0 = 31.2 A^{-1/3} + 20.6 A^{-1/6}~{\rm MeV}$.
- The amplitude of FWHM oscillations in light nuclei ($A<50$) is at least 5-6 MeV (e.g., from $^3He$ to $^4He$, or $^{40}Ca$ to $^{45}Sc$), and sometimes as large as 14 MeV (e.g., from $^9Be$ to $^{14}N$).
- The error in FWHM for light nuclei is less than 1 MeV in almost all cases.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between electronic properties of metal particles and nuclear properties previously discussed by Sugano (1993), and builds on experimental studies of Mie resonances in metal particles by Kreibig (1974) and Kawabata and Kubo (1966), as well as nuclear giant dipole resonance systematics by Bergere (1984) and Snover (1986). The paper enables a unified scaling comparison between the two systems, showing that when FWHMs are scaled by Fermi energies and inverse radii by Fermi wave vectors, the data sets for matrix-embedded metal particles and nuclei become comparable in magnitude, suggesting common underlying physics despite vastly different energy and length scales.
