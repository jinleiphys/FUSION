# Elucidating the role of the surface energy in density functional theory

> The saturation of symmetric nuclear matter -- reflected in the nearly constant interior density of heavy nuclei -- is a defining property of nuclear matter. Modern relativistic energy density functionals (EDFs) calibrated exclusively to the properties of finite nuclei, make robust predictions with quantified uncertainties about the bulk properties of symmetric nuclear matter in the vicinity of the saturation density. Following the same fitting protocol, nonrelativistic Skyrme EDFs systematically predict higher saturation densities than their relativistic counterparts. To investigate this tension in the bulk limit, we study the ground-state properties of hypothetical symmetric macroscopic nuclei containing thousands of nucleons. Using both relativistic and non-relativistic EDF frameworks, we extract the corresponding liquid-drop parameters. We find a clear correlation between the volume and surface energy coefficients: Skyrme models, which saturate at higher densities, develop softer and more diffuse surfaces with lower surface energies, whereas relativistic EDFs, which saturate at lower densities, produce more defined and less diffuse surfaces with higher surface energies. This compensating behavior allows both classes of models to reproduce empirical nuclear radii despite their distinct saturation properties. Our analysis suggests that the apparent disparity in saturation densities arises from the intrinsic balance among saturation density, bulk binding energy, and surface tension, rather than from the fitting protocol.

## Key claim

The apparent disparity in saturation densities predicted by nonrelativistic Skyrme versus relativistic energy density functionals arises from an intrinsic balance among saturation density, bulk binding energy, and surface tension, rather than from differences in the fitting protocol used to calibrate the models.

## Method

The authors computed ground-state energies of hypothetical symmetric macroscopic nuclei containing thousands of nucleons (A between about 250 and 3500) with Coulomb repulsion turned off, using both nonrelativistic Skyrme-Hartree-Fock and covariant relativistic mean-field frameworks. They extracted liquid-drop parameters (volume and surface energy coefficients) from a leptodermous expansion of the energy per nucleon as a function of A^{-1/3}, and analyzed correlations using Markov Chain Monte Carlo sampling. A constant-gap BCS pairing treatment was included to stabilize iterative solutions for the large nuclear systems.

## Key numbers

- Skyrme models predict saturation densities ranging from 0.160 fm^{-3} (SKM*, RATP, SLy4) to 0.175 fm^{-3} (LNS), while relativistic models predict lower values from 0.148 fm^{-3} (NL3) to 0.153 fm^{-3} (FSUGarnet)
- Volume energy coefficients from the leptodermous expansion: Skyrme models give a_v from -15.26 MeV (LNS) to -16.20 MeV (KDE0v1); relativistic models give a_v from -16.28 MeV (FSUGarnet) to -16.32 MeV (FSUGold2)
- Surface energy coefficients: Skyrme models give a_s from 15.36 MeV (LNS) to 19.45 MeV (RATP); relativistic models give a_s from 19.82 MeV (FSUGarnet) to 20.12 MeV (FSUGold2)
- For FSUGarnet: a_v = -16.28(2) MeV, a_s = 19.82(21) MeV, with Pearson correlation coefficient r = 0.998
- For SLy4: a_v = -15.95(2) MeV, a_s = 18.54(22) MeV, with Pearson correlation coefficient r = 0.998
- The ratio a_s/a_v is nearly constant at about 1.22 for relativistic models, while for nonrelativistic models it ranges from 1.0 to 1.21

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Drischler (2024) showing that nonrelativistic Skyrme functionals systematically predict higher saturation densities than relativistic counterparts, despite both classes reproducing experimental charge radii. The paper demonstrates that relativistic models compensate for lower saturation densities by having stiffer surface energies (higher a_s values) than Skyrme models, which have softer, more diffuse surfaces. This finding is timely for addressing the "CREX-PREX dilemma" noted by Reinhard (2022), where surface contributions, particularly from spin-orbit interactions, may play a critical role in resolving tensions between measurements of neutron skin thicknesses in lead-208 and calcium-48.
