# Equilibration and hydrodynamics at strong and weak coupling

> We give an updated overview of both weak and strong coupling methods to describe the approach to a plasma described by viscous hydrodynamics, a process now called hydrodynamisation. At weak coupling the very first moments after a heavy ion collision is described by the colour-glass condensate framework, but quickly thereafter the mean free path is long enough for kinetic theory to become applicable. Recent simulations indicate thermalization in a time $t\sim40(η/s)^{4/3}/T$ [1], with $T$ the temperature at that time and $η/s$ the shear viscosity divided by the entropy density. At (infinitely) strong coupling it is possible to mimic heavy ion collisions by using holography, which leads to a dual description of colliding gravitational shock waves. The plasma formed hydrodynamises within a time of $0.41/T$. A recent extension found corrections to this result for finite values of the coupling, when $η/s$ is bigger than the canonical value of $1/4π$, which leads to $t\sim(0.41+1.6(η/s-1/4π))/T$ [2]. Future improvements include the inclusion of the effects of the running coupling constant in QCD.

## Key claim

The paper reviews that both weak-coupling kinetic theory and strong-coupling holography predict rapid hydrodynamisation of the quark-gluon plasma, but the specific timescales and initial conditions differ qualitatively; the abstract's claim of thermalization at weak coupling in time \(t\sim40(\eta/s)^{4/3}/T\) is conditioned on the shear viscosity over entropy ratio \(\eta/s\) and the temperature \(T\) at that time, while the strong coupling result \(t\sim(0.41+1.6(\eta/s-1/4\pi))/T\) is a perturbative correction valid only for finite coupling when \(\eta/s\) exceeds the canonical value \(1/4\pi\).

## Method

The paper reviews two complementary approaches: at weak coupling, the initial stage is described by the colour-glass condensate (CGC) framework, followed by kinetic theory simulations of pure Yang-Mills theory (with 't Hooft coupling \(\lambda=10\)) that evolve the gluon distribution until the system matches viscous hydrodynamics. At strong coupling, holographic duals of colliding gravitational shock waves are used, with the stress-tensor extracted from the dual geometry; finite-coupling corrections are included via Einstein-Gauss-Bonnet gravity treated perturbatively to second order in the Gauss-Bonnet coupling \(\lambda_{GB}\).

## Key numbers

- Weak coupling hydrodynamisation time: \(t\sim40(\eta/s)^{4/3}/T\), where \(T\) is the temperature at hydrodynamisation and \(\eta/s\) is the shear viscosity over entropy density (from kinetic theory simulations with \(\lambda=10\)).
- Strong coupling (infinitely strong) hydrodynamisation time: \(t\sim0.41/T\) for narrow shocks, with \(T\) the temperature at that time.
- Finite coupling correction to strong coupling hydrodynamisation time: \(t\sim(0.41+1.6(\eta/s-1/4\pi))/T\), valid when \(\eta/s>1/4\pi\).
- For \(\mathcal{N}=4\) supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory with finite coupling corrections, the relaxation time is \(\tau \sim \frac{0.72}{2 \pi T}(1+(\eta/s-1/4\pi)7.7)\).
- In holographic collisions with a conserved U(1) charge, about 41% of the charge from a left-moving shock ends up moving to positive \(z\) (i.e., "bounces") after the collision.
- At strong coupling, the initial rapidity profile at hydrodynamisation is Gaussian with width 0.955 (for infinitely strong coupling).

## Context

This review extends earlier work on hydrodynamisation at weak coupling (Kurkela et al., 2015) and at strong coupling (Chesler and Yaffe, 2009; Heller et al., 2011), and incorporates finite-coupling corrections from higher-derivative gravity (Waeber et al., 2015; Grozdanov et al., 2016). The results enable future simulations that combine weak-coupling initial conditions (e.g., from classical Yang-Mills or glasma) with strong-coupling evolution, and motivate including a running coupling constant in QCD to bridge the two regimes.
