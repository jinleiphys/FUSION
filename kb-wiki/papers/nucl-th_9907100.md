# Calculations for populations of selected isotopes in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions

> We compute the populations of isotopes of Boron, Carbon and Nitrogen measured experimentally in intermediate energy heavy ion collisions. A two component soluble statistical model is used to find the initial populations of different nuclei at a finite temperature. These initial populations are both in particle stable and particle unstable states. The particle unstable states then decay. The final populations after these decays are computed and compared with experimental data.

## Key claim

The authors compute final isotope populations of Boron, Carbon and Nitrogen from intermediate energy heavy ion collisions using a two-component statistical model for primary yields followed by a Weisskopf secondary decay formalism, but note that the predictions are the result of two models applied in tandem and that the effect of secondary decays is not small, so the comparison with experiment tests the combined models rather than the primary model alone.

## Method

The authors first calculate primary populations of all isotopes using a two-component soluble statistical model with a recursion relation, where the system is characterized by a temperature T and a freeze-out volume V_f. They then model secondary decay of particle-unstable states using a Weisskopf formalism that considers emission of six species (neutron, proton, deuteron, 3He, triton, and alpha particles), with decay rates computed from the density of states and the reverse reaction cross sections. The decay chain is followed through multiple generations (up to quadruple decay) by assuming the residue population at each stage is canonically distributed with a new temperature, and the final stable populations are compared to experimental data from S + Ag collisions at 22.3 AMeV.

## Key numbers

- The S + Ag heavy-ion collision energy is 22.3 AMeV (from the experiment cited as Xu (1989))
- The parameter f in V_f = f V_0 is varied to obtain best fit with experimental data (V_0 is the normal volume for Z+N nucleons)
- The binding energy parametrization uses W_0 = 15.8 MeV, sigma = 18.0 MeV, kappa = 0.72 MeV, s = 23.5 MeV, and e = 16.0 MeV
- The radius parameter r_0 = 1.2 fm and the Coulomb radius parameter r_c = 1.44 fm
- In about 3% of cases, the new temperature 1/beta' after decay turned out higher than the initial temperature 1/beta

## Context

This work extends the two-component statistical model previously developed by Das Gupta and collaborators (1998, 1999) and applied to caloric curve calculations by Bhattacharyya (1999), by adding a detailed secondary decay treatment using Weisskopf formalism following Friedman and Lynch (1983). The calculation enables comparison with experimental data from the NSCL-MSU facility reported by Xu (1989), and the authors note that the predictions are sensitive to both the initial temperature and the freeze-out volume, with the secondary decay effects being non-negligible.
