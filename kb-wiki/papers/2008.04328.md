# Quantum Entangled-Probe Scattering Theory

> We develop an entangled-probe scattering theory, including quantum detection, that extends the scope of standard scattering approaches. We argue that these probes may be revolutionary in studying entangled matter such as unconventional phases of strongly correlated systems. Our presentation focuses on a neutron beam probe that is mode-entangled in spin and path as is experimentally realized in [1], although similar ideas also apply to photon probes. We generalize the traditional van Hove theory [2] whereby the response is written as a properly-crafted combination of two-point correlation functions. Tuning the probe's entanglement length allows us to interrogate spatial scales of interest by analyzing interference patterns in the differential cross-section. Remarkably, for a spin dimer target we find that the typical Young-like interference pattern observed if the target state is un-entangled gets quantum erased when that state becomes maximally entangled.

## Key claim

The paper develops a scattering theory for a single-particle neutron probe that is mode-entangled in spin and path, and shows that by tuning the probe's entanglement length one can interrogate spatial scales of interest in the target. The key hedge is that the claimed ability to detect target entanglement via quantum erasure of Young-like interference patterns is demonstrated only for the specific case of a spin dimer target, and the authors note that the effect depends on the relation between various length scales involved in the scattering process.

## Method

The authors generalize the standard T-matrix formalism to include a mode-entangled probe state, where the probe Hilbert space is a tensor product of path and spin subsystems. They compute the differential cross section (DCS) in the Born approximation for a general interaction potential, then specialize to magnetic neutron scattering to extend van Hove's theory. The formalism is applied to a spin dimer target governed by the Heisenberg Hamiltonian, with the probe described by a Gaussian wave packet of mean momentum k₀, spatial width Δ, and tunable entanglement vector ξ.

## Key numbers

- The probe's mean momentum is k₀ ≈ 1.5 × 10⁴ μm⁻¹, with tunable entanglement length ξ in the range 25 nm < ξ < 25 μm, as experimentally realized in Shen (2019) and Kuhn (2020)
- The dimer target consists of two electrons separated by distance |d|, with exchange coupling J; the singlet energy is E_λs = 3J and triplet energy is E_λt = -J
- The purity of a pure triplet state is given by P = |c* × c|², where a maximally entangled triplet state has |c* × c| = 0 and an un-entangled triplet state has |c* × c| = 1
- A specific partially entangled triplet state with coefficient vector c = ((√3-1)/(2√2), -i(√3+1)/(2√2), 0) has purity 1/4

## Context

This work extends the traditional van Hove theory (van Hove, 1954) of neutron scattering to incorporate quantum entanglement in the probe beam, building on recent experimental demonstrations of mode-entangled neutron beams by Shen (2019), Lu (2019), and Kuhn (2020). The formalism enables future experimental studies of entangled matter such as unconventional phases of strongly correlated systems, and the authors propose that altering the detection scheme (e.g., using spin-echo devices) could provide additional information about target entanglement. The paper contrasts mode-entangled single-particle probes with multiparticle entangled probes (such as entangled photon pairs studied by Schotland, 2016), noting that these provide fundamentally different types of correlation functions.
