# Properties of quark matter produced in heavy ion collision

> We describe the hadronization of quark matter assuming that quarks creating hadrons coalesce from a continuous mass distribution. The pion and antiproton spectrum as well as the momentum dependence of the antiproton to pion ratio are calculated. This model reproduces fairly well the experimental data at RHIC energies.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a quark coalescence model for hadronization in which the constituent quarks have a continuous mass distribution (rather than a fixed effective mass), and this model reproduces experimental data at RHIC energies fairly well across a transverse momentum range from about 1 GeV to 8 GeV.

## Method

The authors assume that effective quarks in deconfined matter have a mass distribution ρ(m) peaking around 0.25–0.4 GeV, and that their transverse momentum distribution follows a Tsallis-like form with parameters q and T. Hadronization proceeds via coalescence of two (for mesons) or three (for baryons) quarks with strict momentum conservation and additive mass conservation, using Gaussian coalescence functions. The model is applied to central Au+Au collisions at √s = 200 AGeV, and the calculated pion and antiproton spectra are compared to PHENIX experimental data.

## Key numbers

- Quark mass distribution peak parameter μ = 0.25 GeV; critical temperature Tc = 0.26 GeV
- Tsallis distribution parameters: T = 0.07 GeV, q = 1.19
- Transverse flow velocity vflow = 0.56
- The model reproduces data in the transverse momentum range from about 1 GeV to 8 GeV
- Combinatorial factor for pions: gπ = 1/36; for antiprotons: g‾p = 1/108

## Context

This work extends earlier coalescence models (Dover et al., ALCOR, Fries et al., Molnar) by introducing a continuous mass distribution for quarks instead of a fixed effective mass, and by using a single analytic momentum distribution (Tsallis form) across all transverse momenta rather than separate thermal, coalescence, and fragmentation components. The approach contrasts with earlier first-order phase transition hadronization models (Muller 1980, Biro and Zimanyi) and supports the picture of a smooth cross-over transition. The results enable future studies of azimuthal correlations arising from spherical inhomogeneities in the quark momentum distribution.
