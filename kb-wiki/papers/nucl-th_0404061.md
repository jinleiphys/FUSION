# Effect of exotic S=+1 resonances on $K^0_L p$ scattering data

> We consider the effect of an exotic S=+1 $Θ^+$ resonance on the scattering of neutral kaons off protons. Explicit results are presented for the $K^0_L p$ total cross sections.

## Key claim
The paper predicts that a narrow exotic S=+1 Θ⁺(1540) resonance would produce a measurable signal in K⁰_L p total cross sections, but the existing data are insufficiently precise to confirm its existence, with the main limiting factor being knowledge of weak Σ resonances rather than Fermi motion effects.

## Method
The authors combined results from existing analyses of KN and anti-KN scattering data to construct the K⁰_L p scattering amplitude, then added a narrow Breit-Wigner resonance in the S=+1 channel. They accounted for the incident K⁰_L beam momentum spread (assumed Gaussian with widths of 10 and 20 MeV/c at 440 MeV/c) and averaged over 20 MeV/c bins to match the experimental conditions of the Cleland (1972) measurement.

## Key numbers
- The Θ⁺ resonance mass is near 1540 MeV, with an upper bound on its width of order 10 MeV from experimental resolution limitations
- A resonance with 5 MeV width was added to the P₀₁ partial wave for demonstration
- The most pronounced deviations from smooth behavior occur near beam momenta of 280 and 460 MeV/c, corresponding to center-of-mass energies of 1480 and 1550 MeV
- The overall momentum scale has a quoted uncertainty of 2%
- A 3.5 standard deviation signal for a Σ(1480) was seen in anti-K⁰ p from K⁻ p → anti-K⁰ p π⁻ (Sayer, 1970 does not show this structure)

## Context
This work extends the search for the Θ⁺(1540) resonance, which was predicted by Diakonov, Petrov, and Polyakov (1997) and first observed by Nakano (2003), by examining K⁰_L p scattering as an alternative to K⁺ d experiments that are limited by Fermi motion effects (Nussinov, 2003; Cahn and Trilling, 2003). The method enables a cleaner search for narrow resonances than deuteron-target experiments, but requires improved data with better momentum resolution to confirm the Θ⁺ and distinguish it from weak Σ resonances such as the Σ(1480) and Σ(1560) listed in the Particle Data Group review.
