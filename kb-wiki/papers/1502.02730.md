# The Hot QCD White Paper: Exploring the Phases of QCD at RHIC and the LHC

> The past decade has seen huge advances in experimental measurements made in heavy ion collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and more recently at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). These new data, in combination with theoretical advances from calculations made in a variety of frameworks, have led to a broad and deep knowledge of the properties of thermal QCD matter. Increasingly quantitative descriptions of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) created in these collisions have established that the QGP is a strongly coupled liquid with the lowest value of specific viscosity ever measured. However, much remains to be learned about the precise nature of the initial state from which this liquid forms, how its properties vary across its phase diagram and how, at a microscopic level, the collective properties of this liquid emerge from the interactions among the individual quarks and gluons that must be visible if the liquid is probed with sufficiently high resolution. This white paper, prepared by the Hot QCD Writing Group as part of the U.S. Long Range Plan for Nuclear Physics, reviews the recent progress in the field of hot QCD and outlines the scientific opportunities in the next decade for resolving the outstanding issues in the field.

## Key claim

The quark-gluon plasma (QGP) created in heavy ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC is a strongly coupled liquid with the lowest value of specific viscosity ever measured, but the precise nature of the initial state, the variation of QGP properties across the phase diagram, and the microscopic emergence of collective properties from quark and gluon interactions remain open questions that require further experimental and theoretical work.

## Method

The Hot QCD Writing Group reviewed experimental measurements from heavy ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC, combined with theoretical calculations from lattice QCD, hydrodynamic models, and perturbative QCD frameworks. The paper synthesizes progress in understanding thermal QCD matter and outlines scientific opportunities for the next decade, including the Beam Energy Scan (BES) program at RHIC and future measurements at the LHC.

## Key numbers

- The QGP has the lowest value of specific viscosity ever measured (no explicit numerical value given in the text).
- The BES-I program covers collision energies from $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 7.7$ GeV up to 200 GeV, with a machine upgrade planned to increase beam luminosity by about a factor of 10 for BES-II.
- Three-particle correlations related to charge separation across the reaction plane turn off at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 7.7$ GeV, while elliptic flow $v_2$ remains robust at that energy.
- The normalized kurtosis of the net-proton distribution drops below the Poisson baseline at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 27$ and 19.6 GeV, with hints of a rise above one at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 11.5$ and 7.7 GeV in central collisions (preliminary data with substantial error bars).
- The PHENIX experiment extracted upper limits on U-gamma mixing at 90% CL for dark photon masses in the range $30 < m_U < 90$ MeV/$c^2$, excluding the mixing parameter space that could explain the $(g-2)_\mu$ anomaly except for $30 < m_U < 32$ MeV/$c^2$.
- The STAR collaboration reported detection of anti-helium 4 nuclei (${}^4\overline{\mathrm{He}}$), the heaviest anti-nucleus observed to date.

## Context

This white paper reviews progress since the early RHIC discoveries and extends the work of the JET and TECHQM collaborations, as well as lattice QCD calculations by groups such as Borsanyi et al. (2011, 2012, 2013, 2014) and Bazavov et al. (2012). It enables future searches for the QCD critical point through the BES-II program at RHIC (2018-2019), and motivates hydrodynamic calculations with nonzero baryon density that couple to fluctuating chiral order parameters. The paper also notes cross-fertilization with condensed matter physics, where the chiral magnetic effect has been observed in Dirac semi-metals such as ZrTe5 (Li et al., 2014).
