# Ultra-High Energy Cosmic Rays: Some General Features, and Recent Developments Concerning Air Shower Computations

> We present an introductory lecture on general features of cosmic rays, for non-experts, and some recent developments concerning cascade equations for air shower developments.

## Key claim

This paper provides an introductory overview of ultra-high energy cosmic rays for non-experts, and presents recent developments in solving cascade equations for air shower development, noting that the 1D version (CONEX) already gives practically the same results as Monte Carlo calculations (CORSIKA) with enormously reduced computation time, while the 3D version is still work in progress.

## Method

The authors derive and present the full 1D hadronic cascade equations for the energy spectra of hadrons at a given atmospheric depth, including collision and decay loss terms, gain terms from higher energies, and a source term, with energy discretization on a logarithmic grid. They also formulate the 3D electromagnetic cascade equations for electrons, positrons, and photons using differential spectra in coordinates (α, β, x, y, z) and energy, including Coulomb scattering terms, and show that in the small-angle approximation the 3D equations reduce to a 1D form after integration over transverse variables.

## Key numbers

- The cosmic ray spectrum follows a power law E^{-γ} with exponent 2.7 below 3×10^15 eV and 3 above that energy (the "knee").
- Fermi acceleration mechanisms give energy gain per iteration of ΔE/E = 4/3 β^2 (second-order Fermi) or ΔE/E = 4/3 β (first-order Fermi at shock fronts).
- Possible accelerators include supernova shock waves up to ≤10^15 eV and rotating neutron stars with B=10^13 G producing up to 10^20 V via dynamo effect.
- In the Heitler toy model, the number of particles at shower maximum N_max ∝ E_0 and the depth of shower maximum X_max ∝ ln E_0.
- Results are shown for proton-induced showers at 10^14 eV, 10^16 eV, and 10^18 eV, with comparisons between CONEX (cascade equation) and CORSIKA (Monte Carlo) calculations.

## Context

This paper extends the long history of cosmic ray research from the discoveries of Coulomb, Wilson, Wulf, Hess (1912), and Auger (1932) to modern computational methods for air shower simulation. The work presents an alternative to the standard Monte Carlo technique (CORSIKA, as implemented by the Karlsruhe group) which becomes very slow at high energies since particle number scales with primary energy, and is even impossible at 10^20 eV. The cascade equation approach (CONEX) enables much faster computations at ultra-high energies, and the paper sets the stage for developing a full 3D solution of the cascade equations, which is stated as work in progress.
