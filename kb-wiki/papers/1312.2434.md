# The relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller treatment at finite temperatures

> The Feynman-Metropolis-Teller treatment of compressed atoms has been recently generalized to relativistic regimes and applied to the description of static and rotating white dwarfs in general relativity. We present here the extension of this treatment to the case of finite temperatures and construct the corresponding equation of state (EOS) of the system; applicable in a wide regime of densities that includes both white dwarfs and neutron star outer crusts. We construct the mass-radius relation of white dwarfs at finite temperatures obeying this new EOS and apply it to the analysis of ultra low-mass white dwarfs with $M\lesssim 0.2 M_\odot$. In particular, we analyze the case of the white dwarf companion of PSR J1738+0333. The formulation is then extrapolated to compressed nuclear matter cores of stellar dimensions, systems with mass numbers $A\approx (m_{\rm Planck}/m_n)^3$ or mass $M_{\rm core}\approx M_{\odot}$, where $m_{\rm Planck}$ and $m_n$ are the Planck and the nucleon mass. For $T \ll m_e c^2/k_B \approx 5.9\times 10^9$ K, a family of equilibrium configurations can be obtained with analytic solutions of the ultra-relativistic Thomas-Fermi equation at finite temperatures. Such configurations fulfill global but not local charge neutrality and have strong electric fields on the core surface. We find that the maximum electric field at the core surface is enhanced at finite temperatures with respect to the degenerate case.

## Key claim

The paper extends the relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller treatment of compressed atoms to finite temperatures, constructing an equation of state applicable to both white dwarfs and neutron star outer crusts, but only for temperatures satisfying \(T \ll m_e c^2/k_B \approx 5.9 \times 10^9\) K, where the presence of anti-particles can be neglected.

## Method

The authors generalize the relativistic Thomas-Fermi equation to finite temperatures by incorporating Fermi-Dirac statistics for electrons and a constant proton distribution within a finite nuclear radius, then solve the Poisson equation numerically for each Wigner-Seitz cell at fixed temperature and chemical composition. They compute the total energy of each cell including nucleus, kinetic, and Coulomb contributions, then derive the density and pressure to construct the equation of state. The resulting EOS is used to integrate the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for spherically symmetric white dwarfs in general relativity, producing mass-radius relations for selected compositions and temperatures.

## Key numbers

- Temperature threshold for neglecting anti-particles: \(T \ll m_e c^2/k_B \approx 5.9 \times 10^9\) K
- For \(^{12}\)C at \(T=10^7\) K, deviations from the degenerate EOS start at density \(\rho \approx 2 \times 10^4\) g cm\(^{-3}\); at \(T=10^8\) K, deviations start at \(\rho \approx 10^6\) g cm\(^{-3}\)
- Critical densities for inverse \(\beta\)-decay in the degenerate case (unchanged at finite temperatures): \(^{4}\)He at \(1.37 \times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), \(^{12}\)C at \(3.88 \times 10^{10}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), \(^{16}\)O at \(1.89 \times 10^{10}\) g cm\(^{-3}\), \(^{56}\)Fe at \(1.14 \times 10^9\) g cm\(^{-3}\)
- Estimate for density where temperature effects become important: \(\rho \lesssim 1.5 \times 10^3 (T/10^7\ \text{K})^{3/2}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) for non-relativistic electrons with \(A/Z \approx 2\)
- The maximum electric field at the core surface of compressed nuclear matter cores is enhanced at finite temperatures compared to the degenerate case

## Context

This work extends the relativistic Feynman-Metropolis-Teller treatment developed by de Carvalho, Rotondo, Rueda, and Ruffini (2011) from zero temperature to finite temperatures, improving upon earlier approaches by Chandrasekhar (1931) and Salpeter (1961) that used uniform electron distributions or approximate Coulomb corrections. The new EOS is applied to ultra-low mass white dwarfs (\(M \lesssim 0.2 M_\odot\)) such as the companion of PSR J1738+0333, where degenerate approximations break down at central densities \(\lesssim 10^6\) g cm\(^{-3}\). The formulation also enables future studies of pycnonuclear reaction rates with proper electron screening and analysis of neutron star outer crusts and atmospheres, extending the non-relativistic Thomas-Fermi work of Testa et al. (1998).
