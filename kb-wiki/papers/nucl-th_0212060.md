# Is the tetraneutron a bound dineutron-dineutron molecule?

> In light of a new experiment which claims a positive identification, we discuss the possible existence of the tetraneutron. We explore a novel model based on a dineutron-dineutron molecule. We show that this model is not able to explain the tetraneutron as a bound state, in agreement with other theoretical models already discussed in the literature.

## Key claim

The tetraneutron is very unlikely to be bound as a dineutron-dineutron molecule, although the authors note that more complex variational approaches, different spatial wavefunctions, or more realistic interactions including tensor parts could still be explored.

## Method

The authors constructed a four-neutron wavefunction as two dineutron clusters in spin-singlet states, using Gaussian spatial wavefunctions with an oscillator parameter. They performed a variational calculation with the Volkov two-body nucleon-nucleon potential, integrating over internal coordinates to obtain an effective Schrödinger equation for the relative motion of the two dineutrons. The resulting effective potential was examined for attractive pockets that could support a bound state.

## Key numbers

- The dineutron has a virtual singlet state at E = 66 keV.
- The oscillator parameter b = 1.5 fm was used as the central value, with variation from 1.2 fm to 2.0 fm.
- Eight different parameterizations of the Volkov potential from Vo65 were tested; none produced an attractive pocket in the effective potential.
- The internal kinetic energy of each cluster is T^{int} = 3ħ²/(4mb²).

## Context

This work responds to a new experiment (Ma02) claiming positive identification of a bound tetraneutron via fragmentation of 30-50 MeV/nucleon ¹⁴Be. It extends earlier theoretical work from the 1960s (e.g., Ar62, Gol63, Vla64) and more recent calculations (Bev80, Bad85, Gorr89, Var95, Sof97) that generally concluded the tetraneutron cannot be bound, contradicting a hyperspherical calculation (Gut89) that suggested a resonance at 1-3 MeV. The authors note that a detailed variational Monte-Carlo calculation by Pieper (Pie03) reached similar conclusions, and that lattice QCD calculations cannot yet achieve the O(1) MeV accuracy needed for this problem.
