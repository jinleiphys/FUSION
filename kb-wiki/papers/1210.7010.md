# Resonance Decay Contributions to Higher-Order Anisotropic Flow Coefficients

> We show that in hydrodynamic simulations for relativistic heavy-ion collisions, strong resonance decay calculations can be performed with fewer species of particle resonances while preserving good accuracy in single particle spectra and flow anisotropies. Such partial resonance calculations boost computation efficiency by a factor of 10 which is essential for large scale event-by-event simulations.

## Key claim
The authors show that for accurate determination of anisotropic flow coefficients $v_n$ and single-particle spectra, only the top 60% of resonance decay contributions need to be included, provided the missing yield is renormalized. This claim is conditional on using renormalized truncated decay spectra rather than simply omitting the weaker decay channels.

## Method
The authors used the (2+1)-dimensional boost-invariant viscous hydrodynamic code VISH2+1 to generate final states for 200 $A$ GeV Au+Au collisions at RHIC and 2.76 $A$ TeV Pb+Pb collisions at LHC at various centralities. They reordered the AZHYDRO resonance table (319 hadron species with masses up to 2.25 GeV) by decreasing contribution to each stable hadron species, computed via Cooper-Frye integrals with viscous corrections and effective branching ratios accounting for multi-step decay cascades. They then truncated the decay sums at various cumulative contribution thresholds and compared the resulting spectra and flow coefficients to the full calculation.

## Key numbers
- The full resonance decay calculation takes about 2-3 hours on a typical single-core personal computer, compared to 10-15 minutes for the preceding hydrodynamic evolution.
- Including only the 9 strongest decay channels for pions (accounting for 60% of total resonance decay yield) reproduces the full pion $p_T$-spectrum shape with less than 5% error between $p_T = 0$ and 2 GeV.
- Including only the 4 strongest decay channels for protons (three charge states of $\Delta(1232)$ and one charge state of $\Delta(1600)$, accounting for 60% of total resonance decay yield) reproduces the full proton spectrum with less than 5% error between $p_T = 0$ and 2 GeV.
- The truncation reduces the number of resonance decay channels by a factor greater than 10, with corresponding reduction in computing time.

## Context
This work extends event-by-event hydrodynamic simulations by Holopainen (2010), Schenke (2011), and Qiu (2011) which identified the hydro-to-particle conversion as the computational bottleneck. The authors build on the resonance decay formalism of Sollfrank (1990) and the VISH2+1 viscous hydrodynamic code of Song (2007, 2008). The results enable faster iterative extraction of QGP shear viscosity from experimental data, and the authors note that future work will extend the method to hybrid models with Boltzmann cascades as used by Song (2010).
