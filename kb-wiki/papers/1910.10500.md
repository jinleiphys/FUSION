# Spinodal Instability at the Onset of Collective Expansion in Nuclear Collisions

> Using transport theory to model central Au + Au collisions in the energy region of 20 - 110 MeV/u, at impact parameters b <= 5 fm, we predict a measurable impact of spinoidal instability as the collective expansion sets in with energy. Two transport models are employed, the pBUU model, solving a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equation, and the Brownian Motion (BM) model, solving a set of Langevin equations to describe the motion of individual nucleons in a noisy nuclear medium. We find without ambiguity, for the first time, that a combination of delayed equilibration, onset of collective expansion and the spinodal instability produces a pair of transient ring structures, made of the projectile and target remnants, with spectator nucleons predicted to end in the entities reminiscent of stones in jewelry, on the rings. The ring structures, calculated in the configuration space and mapped onto the velocity space, could be detected in experimental collective flow data.

## Key claim

For the first time, a combination of delayed equilibration, onset of collective expansion, and the spinodal instability in low-density nuclear matter is predicted to produce a pair of transient ring structures in central Au + Au collisions, but only in a specific beam energy window around 60 MeV/u where expansion is strong enough to prevent collapse yet weak enough for rings to persist.

## Method

The authors employed two semiclassical transport models to simulate central Au + Au collisions at impact parameters b ≤ 5 fm and beam energies from 20 to 110 MeV/u: the pBUU model, which solves the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck equation, and the Brownian Motion (BM) model, which solves Langevin equations to describe nucleon motion in a noisy nuclear medium. The baryon density evolution in configuration space was tracked over time, and the resulting spatial patterns were mapped onto velocity-space observables such as transverse momentum distributions as functions of rapidity.

## Key numbers

- Ring structures form in head-on (b = 0) Au + Au collisions at beam energies around 60 MeV/u, with the energy window including 60 MeV/u in both models but somewhat narrower in BM.
- At times t ∼ 120 fm/c in head-on collisions, maximal densities reach ρ_max ≃ 0.45 ρ_0 at 40 MeV/u, 0.33 ρ_0 at 60 MeV/u, and 0.20 ρ_0 at 100 MeV/u, where ρ_0 = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- The spinodal instability region corresponds to negative adiabatic compressibility (∂P/∂ρ)_{S/A} < 0, operating exclusively at densities ρ ≲ 2ρ_0/3.
- Fragments from the forward ring populate laboratory angles of 5°–7° at the onset of collective expansion.
- The ring structures persist for impact parameters b ≲ 4 fm at 60 MeV/u and above.

## Context

This work extends early transport model predictions by Moretto (1992), Bauer (1992), and Norbeck (1996) that suggested disk and ring formation in head-on collisions, but those models assumed complete stopping which is now known not to occur experimentally (Andronic, 2006; Zhang, 2018). The key advance is the prediction of two disjoint rings rather than one, formed from matter that equilibrates late and is fully immersed in the spinodal instability region, with the mechanism unambiguously attributed to volume instability rather than surface Rayleigh instabilities. The results provide specific experimental guidance for detecting signatures of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition through measurements of intermediate mass fragment distributions in velocity space, particularly using forward detectors.
