# Global description of beta-minus decay in even-even nuclei with the axially-deformed Skyrme finite amplitude method

> We use the finite amplitude method for computing charge-changing Skyrme-QRPA transition strengths in axially-deformed nuclei together with a modern Skyrme energy-density functional to fit several previously unconstrained parameters in the charge-changing time-odd part of the functional. With the modified functional we then calculate rates of beta-minus decay for all medium-mass and heavy even-even nuclei between the valley of stability and the neutron drip line. We fit the Skyrme parameters to a limited set of beta-decay rates, a set of Gamow-Teller resonance energies, and a set of spin-dipole resonance energies, in both spherical and deformed nuclei. Comparison to available experimental beta-decay rates shows agreement at roughly the same level as in other global QRPA calculations. We estimate the uncertainty in our rates all the way to the neutron drip line through a construction that extrapolates the errors of known beta-decay rates in nuclei with intermediate Q values to less stable isotopes with higher Q values.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a Skyrme energy-density functional, with only two free parameters (the isoscalar pairing strength \(V_0\) and the spin-density coupling constant \(C^s_1\)) fitted to a limited set of beta-decay rates and resonance energies, can reproduce experimental beta-minus half-lives for even-even nuclei at roughly the same level of accuracy as other global QRPA calculations, but that fitting additional time-odd coupling constants leads to overfitting and does not improve predictive power.

## Method

The authors use the finite amplitude method (FAM) within the charge-changing Skyrme quasiparticle random phase approximation (pnQRPA) to compute beta-minus decay rates in axially-deformed even-even nuclei. They first construct ground states with the HFB solver hfbtho using the SkO' functional, then fit the isoscalar pairing strength \(V_0\) and the spin-density coupling constant \(C^s_1\) to a set of Gamow-Teller resonance energies, spin-dipole resonance energies, and beta-decay half-lives from both spherical and deformed nuclei. The resulting functional is used to compute rates for all medium-mass and heavy even-even nuclei from the valley of stability to the neutron drip line, with uncertainties estimated via a phenomenological model that extrapolates the \(Q\)-value dependence of the residuals.

## Key numbers

* The average systematic error in computed ground-state-to-ground-state \(Q\) values with the SkO' functional is \(0.154\) MeV, with a statistical error of \(0.576\) MeV.
* The two-parameter fit (1E) yields a largest singular value of \(122.53\) for the Jacobian (corresponding to \(C^s_1\)) and a second largest singular value of \(10.85\) (corresponding to \(V_0\)); the third largest value is \(1.648\), nearly two orders of magnitude smaller.
* The beta-decay half-lives in fitting set A range from \(0.069\) s (\(^{102}\)Sr) to \(1.84\) s (\(^{92}\)Kr); in set B from \(95.6\) s (\(^{74}\)Zn) to \(45360\) s (\(^{200}\)Pt); in set C from \(0.54\) s (\(^{114}\)Ru) to \(9399.6\) s (\(^{92}\)Sr); and in set E from \(0.046\) s (\(^{98}\)Kr) to \(444\) s (\(^{226}\)Rn).
* The Primakoff-Rosen approximation is used for the phase-space integral in the uncertainty model, giving \(f'(x, Z_f)/f(x, Z_f) \approx (5x^4 - 20x + 15)/(x^5 - 10x^2 + 15x - 6)\).

## Context

This work extends the finite amplitude method (previously applied to charge-exchange transitions by Mustonen and Engel in 2014) to a global survey of beta-minus decay in deformed nuclei, building on earlier global QRPA calculations by Möller et al. (1997, 2003) and Marketin et al. (2015). The authors find that their Skyrme-QRPA approach reproduces known half-lives at a level comparable to these prior works, but that the non-unique first-forbidden \(1^-\) contribution is important in many neutron-rich nuclei, suggesting that future comparisons with data may differentiate between models. The uncertainty model developed here enables extrapolation of theoretical errors to nuclei with high \(Q\) values where no experimental data exist, which is essential for \(r\)-process simulations.
