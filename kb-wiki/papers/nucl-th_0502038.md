# Nuclear masses set bounds on quantum chaos

> It has been suggested that chaotic motion inside the nucleus may significantly limit the accuracy with which nuclear masses can be calculated. Using a power spectrum analysis we show that the inclusion of additional physical contributions in mass calculations, through many-body interactions or local information, removes the chaotic signal in the discrepancies between calculated and measured masses. Furthermore, a systematic application of global mass formulas and of a set of relationships among neighboring nuclei to more than 2000 nuclear masses allows to set an unambiguous upper bound for the average errors in calculated masses which turn out to be almost an order of magnitude smaller than estimated chaotic components.

# Nuclear masses set bounds on quantum chaos

## Key claim
The paper shows that the apparent chaotic signal in discrepancies between calculated and measured nuclear masses is not an inherent limit on predictability, but rather an artifact of neglecting many-body interactions or local mass information. When sufficient physical contributions are included, the average error in calculated masses is bounded to approximately 100 keV, almost an order of magnitude smaller than previously estimated chaotic components.

## Method
The authors systematically applied three global mass formulas (liquid-drop model, finite-range droplet model, and Duflo-Zuker) and a set of Garvey-Kelson relations to more than 2000 nuclear masses. They organized the mass differences between calculated and experimental values into a one-dimensional boustrophedon ordering and performed a power spectrum analysis of the Fourier transforms of these deviations, fitting the spectral distributions to power laws of the form |F(ω)|² ∼ ω^m.

## Key numbers
- Root-mean-square deviation for the liquid-drop model: 3211 keV for A ≥ 16, 3177 keV for A ≥ 60
- Root-mean-square deviation for the finite-range droplet model: 653 keV for A ≥ 16, 529 keV for A ≥ 60
- Root-mean-square deviation for the Duflo-Zuker model: 362 keV for A ≥ 16, 320 keV for A ≥ 60
- Root-mean-square deviation for Garvey-Kelson estimates using at least 12 relations (GK-12): 86 keV for A ≥ 16, 80 keV for A ≥ 60
- Root-mean-square deviation for Garvey-Kelson estimates using at least 1 relation (GK-1): 171 keV for A ≥ 16, 115 keV for A ≥ 60
- Average experimental error for nuclei in GK-12: approximately 20 keV
- Average error of 215 keV for 67 light nuclei in the fp shell from large-scale shell model calculations (Cau99)
- Errors in binding energies for N=126 Po-Pu isotones smaller than 50 keV (Cau03)

## Context
This work directly addresses the claim by Abe (2002) and Bohigas and Leboeuf (2002) that chaotic motion inside the nucleus might impose a fundamental limit on the accuracy of nuclear mass calculations. The authors extend prior mass formula work by Moller (1995) (FRDM) and Duflo and Zuker (1994, 1995) (DZ), and systematically apply Garvey-Kelson relations (Garvey and Kelson, 1966, 1969) in a novel extended fashion. The results enable confidence that nuclear mass predictions far from stability can achieve accuracies better than 100 keV, supporting large-scale shell model extrapolations such as those by Caurier (1999, 2003).
