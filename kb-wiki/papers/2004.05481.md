# Classical excluded volumes of loosely bound light (anti)nuclei and their chemical freeze-out in heavy ion collisions

> From the analysis of light (anti)nuclei multiplicities that were measured recently by the ALICE collaboration in Pb+Pb collisions at the center-of-mass collision energy $\sqrt{s_{NN}} =2.76$ TeV there arose a highly non-trivial question about the excluded volume of composite particles. Surprisingly, the hadron resonance gas model (HRGM) is able to perfectly describe the light (anti)nuclei multiplicities under various assumptions. Thus, one can consider the (anti)nuclei with a vanishing hard-core radius (as the point-like particles) or with the hard-core radius of proton, but the fit quality is the same for these assumptions. It is clear, however, that such assumptions are unphysical. Hence we obtain a formula for the classical excluded volume of loosely bound light nuclei consisting of A baryons. To implement a new formula into the HRGM we have to modify the induced surface tension concept to treat the hadrons and (anti)nuclei on the same footing. We perform a simultaneous analysis of hadronic and (anti)nuclei multiplicities measured by the ALICE collaboration. The HRGM with the induced surface tension allows us to verify different assumptions on the values of hard-core radii and different scenarios of chemical freeze-out of light (anti)nuclei. It is shown that the unprecedentedly high quality of fit $χ^2_{tot}/dof \simeq 0.769$ is achieved, if the chemical freeze-out temperature of hadrons is about $T_h=150$ MeV, while the one for all (anti)nuclei is $T_A=174-175.2$ MeV.

## Key claim

The hadron resonance gas model (HRGM) can describe light (anti)nuclei multiplicities equally well whether they are treated as point-like particles or with a proton-sized hard-core radius, but both assumptions are unphysical; the authors derive a correct classical excluded volume formula for loosely bound light nuclei and show that a high-quality fit requires separate chemical freeze-out temperatures for hadrons and (anti)nuclei, with the latter being about 174-175.2 MeV.

## Method

The authors derived a formula for the classical excluded volume of a loosely bound light nucleus consisting of A baryons interacting with a hadron, based on the fact that the nuclear radius is much larger than the hadron hard-core radius. They implemented this formula into the HRGM with induced surface tension, producing two formulations: one using the exact excluded volume (V-approach) and one using an effective hard-core radius for nuclei (R-approach). They performed a simultaneous fit of hadronic and (anti)nuclei multiplicities measured by the ALICE collaboration in Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV, testing both a single freeze-out scenario and a separate freeze-out scenario for hadrons and nuclei.

## Key numbers

* The best fit quality for the separate freeze-out model (M2) with the R-approach gave chi^2_tot/dof = 11.3/16 = 0.706, with a nuclear cluster freeze-out temperature T_A = 168.5 (+6.7, -2.6) MeV.
* The best fit quality for the separate freeze-out model (M2) with the V-approach gave chi^2_tot/dof = 11.18/16 = 0.699, with a nuclear cluster freeze-out temperature T_A = 189 (+38, -15) MeV.
* The overlapping region where both EoS provide similar fit quality gives T_A = 174-175.2 MeV, with chi^2_tot/dof = 12.3/16 = 0.769.
* The hadron freeze-out temperature is T_h = 150 MeV.
* The hard-core radius of baryons used is R_b = 0.365 fm, and the pion hard-core radius is R_pi = 0.15 fm.

## Context

This work extends previous HRGM analyses of light (anti)nuclei by Bugaev et al. (2019) that used an approximate expression for the hard-core radius of nuclei, and by the ALICE collaboration measurements of light nuclei yields. The derived formula for classical excluded volumes of loosely bound nuclei (Eq. 7) provides a physically motivated alternative to the unphysical assumptions of point-like nuclei or nuclei with proton-sized hard-core radii. The finding that hadrons and (anti)nuclei freeze out at different temperatures (T_h = 150 MeV vs. T_A = 174-175.2 MeV) supports the hypothesis of separate chemical freeze-out surfaces for different particle species, and the nuclear cluster freeze-out temperature is consistent with the highest RHIC freeze-out temperatures found in earlier HRGM studies.
