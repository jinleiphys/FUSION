# New physics searches with heavy-ion collisions at the LHC

> This document summarises proposed searches for new physics accessible in the heavy-ion mode at the CERN Large Hadron Collider (LHC), both through hadronic and ultraperipheral $γγ$ interactions, and that have a competitive or, even, unique discovery potential compared to standard proton-proton collision studies. Illustrative examples include searches for new particles -- such as axion-like pseudoscalars, radions, magnetic monopoles, new long-lived particles, dark photons, and sexaquarks as dark matter candidates -- as well as new interactions, such as non-linear or non-commutative QED extensions. We argue that such interesting possibilities constitute a well-justified scientific motivation, complementing standard quark-gluon-plasma physics studies, to continue running with ions at the LHC after the Run-4, i.e. beyond 2030, including light and intermediate-mass ion species, accumulating nucleon-nucleon integrated luminosities in the accessible fb$^{-1}$ range per month.

## Key claim

Heavy-ion collisions at the LHC offer competitive or unique discovery potential for beyond-Standard-Model physics compared to proton-proton studies, particularly for low-mass or weakly-coupled new particles, due to advantages such as negligible pileup, reduced trigger thresholds, and enhanced photon-photon luminosities in ultraperipheral interactions, provided that the LHC heavy-ion programme continues beyond Run-4 (after 2029) with sufficient integrated luminosities.

## Method

The paper surveys four BSM production mechanisms accessible in heavy-ion collisions: ultraperipheral photon-photon collisions (UPCs) for particles such as axion-like particles and radions; Schwinger pair production via strong classical electromagnetic fields for magnetic monopoles; hard scattering processes for long-lived particles and dark photons; and thermal production in the quark-gluon plasma for sexaquark dark matter candidates. For each mechanism, the document evaluates the expected LHC heavy-ion performance, including projected luminosities for various ion species (from oxygen to lead), and compares the discovery potential to standard proton-proton searches.

## Key numbers

- PbPb collisions run at nucleon-nucleon center-of-mass energy sqrt(s_NN) = 5.5 TeV, compared to 14 TeV for pp collisions
- Nucleon-nucleon luminosity for PbPb is about 2.5 x 10^32 cm^-2 s^-1, roughly 100 times smaller than the pp luminosity of 2 x 10^34 cm^-2 s^-1
- The effective gamma-gamma luminosity in PbPb UPCs is enhanced by a factor Z^4 = 4.5 x 10^7 relative to pp, yielding an overall factor of about 10 in usable gamma-gamma luminosity after accounting for the lower beam luminosity
- Integrated luminosity per one-month PbPb run is estimated at 3.1 nb^-1 (AA) or 0.15 fb^-1 (NN); for lighter ions, NN integrated luminosities range from 0.210 fb^-1 (Pb) to 2.98 fb^-1 (O)
- CMS has set the best current limits on axion-like particles in the mass range m_a = 5 to 50 GeV for photon-only couplings, and m_a = 5 to 10 GeV for hypercharge coupling scenarios
- The first LHC measurement of light-by-light scattering in PbPb UPCs has extended the upper limit on non-linear QED modifications to scales Lambda_BI > about 100 GeV, imposing a lower limit of 11 TeV on the magnetic monopole mass in Born-Infeld extensions
- Magnetic monopole searches in heavy-ion collisions could test masses in the hundreds of GeV range, 2-3 orders of magnitude heavier than the previous SPS search

## Context

This document extends the scientific case for the LHC heavy-ion programme beyond traditional quark-gluon-plasma physics, complementing BSM searches in proton-proton collisions that are limited by pileup and higher trigger thresholds at high luminosity. It builds on prior work such as the CMS light-by-light measurement (Sirunyan 2018) and the ALP search framework of Knapen (2016), and proposes that future runs with light and intermediate-mass ion species (oxygen, argon, krypton) could achieve 2-15 times higher nucleon-nucleon luminosities than PbPb, enabling new searches for long-lived particles, dark photons, and other weakly-coupled states.
