# Meltdown in quantum computers needs not occur: Nuclear experiments show a way out

> We show that phase memory can be much longer than energy relaxation in systems with exponentially large dimensions of Hilbert space; this finding is documented by fifty years of nuclear experiments, though the information is somewhat hidden. For quantum computers Hilbert spaces of dimension $2^{100}$ or larger will be typical and therefore this effect may contribute significantly to reduce the problems of scaling of quantum computers to a useful number of qubits.

## Key claim

Phase memory can be much longer than energy relaxation in systems with exponentially large Hilbert spaces, such as heavy nuclei, which suggests that the "meltdown" time scale for quantum computers may be orders of magnitude longer than previously claimed by Georgeot and Shepelyansky (2002) based on the spreading width.

## Method

The authors reanalyzed inelastic proton-nucleus scattering data from Gugelot (1954) for 18 MeV protons on targets including platinum and gold, comparing the energy spectra of outgoing protons at different angles. They also examined more recent low-energy proton angular distributions from scattering on bismuth targets. The energy relaxation time was estimated from the spreading width of the compound nucleus, while the process time was estimated from the total decay width, and the angular distributions were analyzed for forward peaking as evidence of retained phase memory.

## Key numbers

- For platinum, the spreading width is approximately 1 MeV, giving an energy relaxation time of about 0.66 × 10^(-21) seconds.
- For platinum, the total decay width of the compound state is approximately 0.02 keV, giving a process time five orders of magnitude longer than the energy relaxation time.
- The effective Hilbert space dimension for p + Pt is 10^20, corresponding to approximately 67 qubits.
- The effective Hilbert space dimension for p + Fe is 10^9, corresponding to approximately 30 qubits.
- The exponential slope of the scaled spectra for heavy nuclei indicates energy relaxation occurred at approximately 0.7 MeV per proton.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Georgeot and Shepelyansky (2002), who used a two-body random Hamiltonian model for quantum computer hardware and claimed that information loss occurs on a time scale given by qubit mixing of eigenstates. The authors use fifty years of nuclear experimental data, starting with Gugelot (1954), to show that the energy relaxation time is not the relevant time scale for memory conservation in exponentially large Hilbert spaces. The finding enables a potential path for scaling quantum computers to large numbers of qubits, though the authors note that more theoretical insight is needed for engineering such systems.
