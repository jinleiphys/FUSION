# "Nonbaryonic" Dark Matter as Baryonic Color Superconductor

> We discuss a novel cold dark matter candidate which is formed from the ordinary quarks during the QCD phase transition when the axion domain wall undergoes an unchecked collapse due to the tension in the wall. If a large number of quarks is trapped inside the bulk of a closed axion domain wall, the collapse stops due to the internal Fermi pressure. In this case the system in the bulk, may reach the critical density when it undergoes a phase transition to a color superconducting phase with the ground state being the quark condensate, similar to the Cooper pairs in BCS theory. If this happens, the new state of matter representing the diquark condensate with a large baryon number $B \sim 10^{32}$ becomes a stable soliton-like configuration. Consequently, it may serve as a novel cold dark matter candidate.

# Nonbaryonic Dark Matter as Baryonic Color Superconductor

## Key claim
The paper proposes that cold dark matter could consist of "QCD balls", stable, soliton-like configurations of ordinary quarks in a color superconducting phase, formed during the QCD phase transition when axion domain walls collapse and trap quarks. The claim is conditional on the existence of both axion domain walls with QCD-scale substructure (which prevent trapped quarks from escaping) and a color superconducting phase at high baryon density, with the resulting objects being absolutely stable only within a specific range of baryon numbers B_min < B < B_max.

## Method
The author models the equilibrium of a QCD ball by balancing Fermi pressure against axion domain wall surface tension and bag constant pressure, expressing the total energy per baryon as a function of radius and baryon number B. Corrections are incorporated for quark-quark interactions on the Fermi surface (which produce a negative energy contribution from diquark condensation) and for the variation of the bag constant with baryon density, using two independent parameterizations motivated by QCD low-energy theorems and studies of QCD-like theories. The equilibrium radius is found by minimizing the energy per baryon, and absolute stability is assessed by comparing the energy per quark to the nucleon mass.

## Key numbers
- Baryon number of a typical QCD ball: B ~ 10^32
- Superconducting gap: Δ ~ 100 MeV, with critical temperature T_c ≃ 0.6 Δ
- Axion domain wall tension: σ ≃ f_a m_π f_π, with f_a ~ (10^10 – 10^12) GeV
- Critical chemical potential for vanishing baryon density: μ_c ≃ 330 MeV
- Gluon condensate at zero density: ⟨(α_s/π) G²⟩_{μ=0} ≃ 1.2 × 10^{-2} GeV^4
- Nuclear saturation density: n_0 ≃ (108 MeV)^3
- Upper limit on dimensionless radius for quark description validity: x̄ ≃ 0.6
- Bag constant variation scale from gluon condensate: (264 MeV)^3
- Ratio of vacuum energy scales: r ≃ (340 MeV)/(150 MeV) ≃ 2.25
- Energy per quark at equilibrium without wall tension: ε(x_0) ≃ 1.9 E_B^{1/4}
- Dimensionless equilibrium radius without wall tension: x_0 ≃ 0.48
- Dark matter density parameter: Ω_DM ≃ 0.3
- Baryon density parameter from nucleosynthesis: Ω_B h^2 ≃ 0.02

## Context
This work extends the long-standing idea that strange quark matter nuggets (Witten 1984, Jaffe, Madsen) could constitute dark matter, and the concept of non-topological solitons as dark matter candidates (Lee, Q-balls, Widrow). The novel elements are the use of axion domain walls (Sikivie) as a "squeezer" to compress quarks, rather than relying on a first-order QCD phase transition, and the incorporation of the color superconducting phase (CS phase) with diquark condensation (Krishna, recent reviews). The paper builds on recent advances showing that axion domain walls have a QCD-scale sandwich substructure (FZ, SG) that prevents trapped quarks from escaping, and on the demonstration that the color superconducting gap is large (Δ ~ 100 MeV). The scenario offers a natural explanation for why Ω_B and Ω_DM are of the same order of magnitude, as both originate from the same QCD phase transition epoch. The work enables future searches for QCD balls via their interactions with ordinary matter, including possible seismic signals (Teplitz).
