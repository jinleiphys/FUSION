# Perspectives on Core-Collapse Supernova Theory

> Core-collapse theory brings together many facets of high-energy and nuclear astrophysics and the numerical arts to present theorists with one of the most important, yet frustrating, astronomical questions: "What is the mechanism of core-collapse supernova explosions?" A review of all the physics and the fifty-year history involved would soon bury the reader in minutiae that could easily obscure the essential elements of the phenomenon, as we understand it today. Moreover, much remains to be discovered and explained, and a complicated review of an unresolved subject in flux could grow stale fast. Therefore, in this paper I describe what I think are various important facts and perspectives that may have escaped the attention of those interested in this puzzle. Furthermore, I attempt to describe the modern theory's physical underpinnings and briefly summarize the current state of play. In the process, I identify a few myths (as I see them) that have crept into modern discourse. However, there is much more to do and humility in the face of this age-old challenge is clearly the most prudent stance as we seek its eventual resolution.

# Perspectives on Core-Collapse Supernova Theory

## Key claim

The mechanism of core-collapse supernova explosions remains unresolved after fifty years of theory, and while multi-dimensional neutrino-driven convection is considered the frontrunner mechanism for generic progenitors, no simulation using the most advanced codes and detailed microphysics has yet explained or reproduced any of the five key observational facts of core-collapse supernovae (explosion energy, neutron star mass distribution, nucleosynthetic yields, pulsar proper motions, and explosion morphologies), except perhaps pulsar kicks.

## Method

The paper reviews the physical underpinnings of core-collapse supernova theory, including progenitor evolution, the collapse and bounce phases, neutrino trapping, and the stalled accretion shock problem. It identifies what the author considers myths in modern discourse and highlights aspects of the theory that have received insufficient attention, while summarizing the current state of multi-dimensional simulations incorporating neutrino transport, hydrodynamics, and nuclear physics.

## Key numbers

- Canonical explosion energy is ~10^51 ergs (one "Bethe"), with a putative distribution from ~0.1 Bethes to ~10 Bethes
- Average pulsar proper motion speeds are ~350 km s^-1, ranging beyond ~1000 km s^-1
- Core collapse to nuclear densities takes no more than ~350 milliseconds, with characteristic dynamical time roughly ~40 ms / sqrt(rho_10) where rho_10 is in units of 10^10 gm cm^-3
- The effective Chandrasekhar mass varies from ~1.25 to ~2.0 M_sun depending on progenitor mass and evolutionary details
- Initial core densities at collapse are ~6-15 x 10^9 gm cm^-3 and temperatures ~6-10 x 10^9 Kelvin
- Initial core entropies are ~0.7-1.2 k_B/baryon, with outer shell entropies jumping to ~2-5 k_B/baryon
- Initial core electron fraction (Y_e) values are ~0.4-0.43
- The neutrino breakout burst luminosity is ~3-4 x 10^53 ergs s^-1, within an order of magnitude of the total optical output of the observable Universe
- The stalled accretion shock sits at a radius near ~100-200 km
- Accretion rate during the delay to explosion decreases from ~1.0 to 0.3 M_sun per second for the most massive progenitors, and from ~0.4 to 0.05 M_sun per second for the least massive iron-core progenitors
- The proto-neutron star baryon mass is ~1.2-1.5 M_sun, with central density at or above ~4.0 x 10^14 gm cm^-3 and central temperature of ~10 MeV
- The only 1D (spherical) explosions obtained credibly are for the lowest mass progenitor (~8.8 M_sun), yielding weak explosions of ~10^50 ergs

## Context

This paper extends the long line of core-collapse supernova reviews dating back to Bethe (1988, 1990), Janka (2001, 2012), and Mezzacappa (2005), while offering a deliberately selective and idiosyncratic perspective rather than a comprehensive review. It identifies specific open questions including the progenitor-to-supernova mapping, the explosion energy distribution as a function of progenitor mass, and the branching between neutron star and black hole formation. The work enables future progress by clarifying which aspects of the theory are robust (such as neutrino trapping and the breakout burst) versus which remain unresolved (the revival mechanism for the stalled shock in generic progenitors).
