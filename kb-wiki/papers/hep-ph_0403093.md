# The Narrow $Θ_5$ Pentaquark As The First Non-planar Hadron With the Diamond Structure And Negative Parity

> Using the picture of the flux tube model, we propose that the $Θ_5$ pentaquark as the first candidate of the three-dimensional non-planar hadron with the extremely stable diamond structure. The up and down quarks lie at the corners of the diamond while the anti-strange quark sits in the center. Various un-excited color flux tubes between the five quarks bind them into a stable and narrow color-singlet. Such a configuration allows the lowest state having the negative parity naturally. The decay of the $Θ_5$ pentaquark into the nucleon and kaon requires the breakup of the non-planar diamond configuration into two conventional planar hadrons, which involves some kind of structural phase transition as in the condensed matter physics. Hence the width of the $Θ^+$ pentaquark should be narrow despite that it lies above the kaon nucleon threshold. We suggest that future lattice QCD calculation adopt non-planar interpolating currents to explore the underlying structure of the $Θ_5$ pentaquark.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the narrow width of the $\Theta^+$ pentaquark arises because it is a three-dimensional non-planar hadron with a diamond structure, whose decay into planar hadrons requires a structural phase transition, rather than from accidental cancellations or selection rules as proposed in other models.

## Method

Using the flux tube model, the authors propose a spatial configuration where the up and down quarks lie at the four corners of a diamond while the anti-strange quark sits at the center, with unexcited color flux tubes binding the five quarks into a color-singlet. They argue that the ground state has no orbital excitation, yielding negative parity naturally, and that decay into the nucleon and kaon requires breaking the non-planar diamond configuration into two conventional planar hadrons, which they liken to a structural phase transition in condensed matter physics.

## Key numbers

- The $\Theta^+$ pentaquark has minimal quark content $uudd\bar s$ and mass around $1540$ MeV, with an experimental upper bound on its width around $20$ MeV; recent kaon-nucleon scattering analysis indicates the width is less than several MeV.
- The $\Xi_5^{--}$ pentaquark candidate has strangeness $S=-2$, baryon number $B=1$, isospin $I=\frac32$, and mass around $1862$ MeV as reported by the NA49 Collaboration.
- For comparison, the $\Lambda(1520)$ $D_{03}$ state has $J^P={3\over 2}^-$, decays via D-wave with width $15.6\pm 1.0$ MeV, and lies in the same mass region as the $\Theta^+$ pentaquark.
- In the Carlson et al. model, the overlap probability between the pentaquark and the nucleon-kaon system is ${5\over 96}$, reducing to ${5\over 596}$ when including orbital wave functions from the Jaffe-Wilczek diquark model.

## Context

This work extends the flux tube model to propose a non-planar hadron configuration, contrasting with the planar structure of conventional mesons and baryons. It offers an alternative to the Jaffe-Wilczek diquark model (Jaffe and Wilczek, 2003), which predicts positive parity and faces difficulty explaining both the Roper resonance's large width and the $\Theta^+$'s narrow width simultaneously, and to the chiral soliton model (Diakonov et al., 1997), whose predictions are sensitive to inputs like the $\sigma$-term. The authors suggest future lattice QCD calculations should use non-planar interpolating currents to test the diamond structure, and propose that replacing the anti-strange quark with heavier anti-quarks ($\bar c$, $\bar b$) would yield similarly narrow heavy pentaquarks.
