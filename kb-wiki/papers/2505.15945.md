# Bloch oscillation with a diatomic tight-binding model on quantum computers

> We aim to explore a more efficient way to simulate few-body dynamics on quantum computers. Instead of mapping the second quantization of the system Hamiltonian to qubit Pauli gates representation via the Jordan-Wigner transform, we propose to use the few-body Hamiltonian matrix under the statevector basis representation which is more economical on the required number of quantum registers. For a single-particle excitation state on a one-dimensional chain, $Γ$ qubits can simulate $N=2^Γ$ number of sites, in comparison to $N$ qubits for $N$ sites via the Jordan-Wigner approach. A two-band diatomic tight-binding model is used to demonstrate the effectiveness of the statevector basis representation. Both one-particle and two-particle quantum circuits are constructed and some numerical tests on IBM hardware are presented.

## Key claim
The authors propose that using a statevector basis representation (directly encoding the few-body Hamiltonian matrix) is more qubit-efficient for simulating few-body dynamics on quantum computers than the standard Jordan-Wigner mapping, but this advantage only holds when the system size \(N^q\) (for \(q\) particles) is smaller than \(2^N\); the paper demonstrates this for a diatomic tight-binding model but notes that current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware fails for circuits deeper than about 2 qubits.

## Method
The authors map the single-particle and two-particle Hamiltonians of a one-dimensional diatomic tight-binding model (with intra-cell hopping \(\Delta_a/4\) and inter-cell hopping \(\Delta_b/4\), a linear electric potential \(F(t)\), and contact interaction \(V\)) onto statevector basis representations, requiring \(\Gamma = \log_2 N\) qubits for \(N\) sites per particle. They construct explicit quantum circuits for the unitary time-evolution operators of each Hamiltonian term (hopping, electric field, interaction) and implement lowest-order Trotterization to simulate Bloch oscillation dynamics on IBM quantum hardware (ibm_brisbane and ibm_sherbrooke) for 2-qubit (4-site) and 3-qubit (8-site) single-particle systems, and a 4-qubit (two sets of 2-qubit) two-particle system.

## Key numbers
- For a single-particle excitation on a 1D chain, \(\Gamma\) qubits simulate \(N = 2^\Gamma\) sites, compared to \(N\) qubits for \(N\) sites via Jordan-Wigner.
- Two-qubit (4-site) single-particle results on IBM hardware without error mitigation show "excellent agreement" with exact solution under parameters \(\Delta_a = 5\), \(\Delta_b = 1\), \(F = 1.5\), time step \(\delta t = 0.02\).
- Three-qubit (8-site) single-particle results on IBM hardware show expectation values "drop off dramatically" compared to exact solution; Zero Noise Extrapolation (ZNE) shows "significant improvement" but is still "way off the exact result."
- For the 4-qubit (two sets of 2-qubit) two-particle interacting system with \(V = 10\), \(\Delta_a = 5\), \(\Delta_b = 1\), \(F = 1.5\), \(\delta t = 0.02\), the IBM hardware result is "way off the exact result even with error mitigation effort."
- The 3-qubit non-interacting circuit has depth 25 layers with 4 \(U1\) gates, 13 \(U3\) gates, and 14 \(CX\) gates; \(U(10\delta t)\) would involve 250 layers.

## Context
This work extends the statevector basis approach previously applied to scattering problems by Guo (2025) and contrasts with the standard Jordan-Wigner transform (Jordan and Wigner, 1928; Nielsen and Chuang, 2002) for mapping fermionic Hamiltonians to qubits. The diatomic tight-binding model exhibits Bloch oscillation (Bloch, 1929; Zener, 1934) and Wannier-Stark localization (Wannier, 1962), phenomena previously observed in semiconductor superlattices (Mendez et al., 1993), cold atoms (Ben Dahan et al., 1996), and photonic crystals (Peschel et al., 1999; Morandotti et al., 1999), as well as on a superconducting quantum processor (Song, 2024; Guo, 2021). The paper identifies that current NISQ hardware decoherence times (hundreds of microseconds) are insufficient for circuits deeper than about 2 qubits, and suggests future work on non-Hermitian \(\mathcal{PT}\)-symmetric systems via Naimark dilation (Dogra et al., 2021).
