# Testing the Field Correlator Method with astrophysical constraints

> We study the structure of hybrid stars with the Field Correlator Method, extended to the zero temperature limit, for the quark phase. For the hadronic phase, we use the microscopic Brueckner-Hartree- Fock many-body theory. The comparison with the neutron star mass phenomenology puts serious constraints on the currently adopted values of the gluon condensate $G_2 \simeq 0.006-0.007 \rm {GeV^4}$, and the large distance static $Q \bar Q$ potential.

## Key claim
The Field Correlator Method (FCM) for quark matter, when combined with a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock hadronic equation of state, can reproduce neutron star masses up to about 1.8 solar masses, but the commonly adopted gluon condensate value of G2 ≈ 0.006-0.007 GeV^4 and the large-distance static Q\bar{Q} potential V1 ≈ 0.5 GeV extracted from lattice QCD at zero chemical potential are difficult to reconcile with the requirement that the maximum neutron star mass be at least 1.44 solar masses and the existence of stars around 1.6 solar masses.

## Method
The hadronic phase was described using the Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) many-body theory with the Argonne v18 two-nucleon potential supplemented by Urbana three-body forces, producing matter in beta equilibrium. The quark phase was modeled using the Field Correlator Method (FCM) extended to zero temperature and finite chemical potential, with the equation of state including gluon and quark pressures plus a vacuum energy shift proportional to the gluon condensate G2. The phase transition between hadronic and quark matter was treated via the Maxwell construction, and the resulting equations of state were used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for neutron star structure.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear matter saturation density: ρ0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}, with binding energy E/A = -16 MeV
- Gluon condensate G2 = 0.012 ± 0.006 GeV^4 (central value with large uncertainty); values tested in the range giving critical temperatures between 160 and 190 MeV
- Maximum neutron star masses span a range between 1.4 and 1.8 solar masses depending on the value of G2
- For V1 = 100 MeV, the phase transition cannot occur in neutron stars, and the maximum mass is around 2 solar masses
- The well-established neutron star mass value of at least 1.44 solar masses (Taylor, 1992) and the existence of stars around 1.6 solar masses (Vela pulsar observations) constrain V1 to values well below 100 MeV

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Burgio et al., 2008) by systematically testing the FCM against neutron star mass phenomenology, comparing with alternative quark matter models such as the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model, the MIT Bag model, and the Color Dielectric model which cannot be tested across both high-temperature and high-density regimes. The results show a tension between the FCM parameters that reproduce lattice QCD data at zero chemical potential (V1 ≈ 0.5 GeV at the critical temperature) and those required to produce hybrid stars consistent with observed neutron star masses, suggesting that refinements to the FCM are needed when applying it to astrophysical conditions.
