# The Hoyle Family: The search for alpha-condensate states in light nuclei

> Our present understanding of the structure of the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C and other near-threshold states in $α$-conjugate nuclei is reviewed in the framework of the $α$-condensate model. The $^{12}$C Hoyle state, in particular, is a candidate for $α$-condensation, due to its large radius and $α$-cluster structure. The predicted features of nuclear $α$-particle condensates are reviewed along with a discussion of their experimental indicators, with a focus on precision break-up measurements. Two experiments are discussed in detail, firstly concerning the break-up of $^{12}$C and then the decays of heavier nuclei. With more theoretical input, and increasingly complex detector setups, precision break-up measurements can, in principle, provide insight into the structures of states in $α$-conjugate nuclei. However, the commonly-held belief that the decay of a condensate state will result in $N$ $α$-particles is challenged. We further conclude that unambiguously characterising excited states built on $α$-condensates is difficult, despite improvements in detector technology.

## Key claim

The paper reviews evidence that the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C and analogous near-threshold states in alpha-conjugate nuclei may be described as alpha-condensates, but concludes that unambiguously characterising such states is difficult despite improvements in detector technology, and challenges the commonly-held belief that the decay of a condensate state will result in N alpha-particles.

## Method

The authors review the Tohsaki-Horiuchi-Schuck-Roepke (THSR) alpha-condensate model and its predictions for light alpha-conjugate nuclei, comparing theoretical form factors from inelastic electron scattering to experimental data from four laboratories. They then analyse precision break-up measurements of the Hoyle state in $^{12}$C using Dalitz plots of three-alpha-particle fractional energies, applying both frequentist and Bayesian statistical approaches to extract upper limits on the direct 3-alpha decay branching ratio, testing multiple direct decay models including equal phase space (DDPhi), equal energies (DDE), collinear (DDL), and phase-space-plus-penetrability (DDP$^2$) models.

## Key numbers

- The Hoyle state lies 300 keV above the 3-alpha threshold (as originally proposed by Hoyle) and 380 keV above the 3-alpha threshold (current value)
- The Hoyle state volume may be up to four times larger than the ground state of $^{12}$C, based on model-dependent analyses of inelastic electron scattering form factors
- The THSR wave function for the Hoyle state has a 70% overlap with three alpha-particles in the lowest 0s-orbital, with 30% contribution from other orbitals
- The THSR calculation using Volkov No. 2 forces gives an excitation energy of 7.73 MeV for the Hoyle state, 250 keV above the calculated 3-alpha threshold
- The direct 3-alpha decay of the Hoyle state is suppressed by a factor of 10$^3$ relative to sequential decay through $^8$Be + alpha, based on phase space calculations using the Fermi breakup model
- An upper limit of 0.0019% was placed on the 3-alpha direct decay branching ratio using approximately 2 x 10$^4$ Hoyle state decay events (Rana et al.)
- The frequentist analysis (Smith et al.) placed a 95% C.L. upper limit of 0.0470% (4.70 x 10$^{-4}$) for the DDPhi direct decay model
- The Bayesian analysis gave a slightly lower branching ratio of 0.0465% (4.65 x 10$^{-4}$) at 95% C.L.
- For the DDE/DDP$^2$ direct decay model, the 95% C.L. upper limit is 2.57 x 10$^{-4}$
- The inelastic electron scattering data cover a range in momentum transfer q from 0.27 to 3.04 fm$^{-1}$

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of Tohsaki, Horiuchi, Schuck and Roepke (2001), who proposed that alpha-condensate states could exist in light alpha-conjugate nuclei at energies around the alpha-decay threshold, and builds on the earlier alpha cluster model of Brink (1966). The paper discusses experimental searches for alpha-condensates in $^{12}$C, $^{16}$O, and heavier nuclei, with particular focus on precision break-up measurements following the work of Kokalova et al. (2006) on branching ratios as signatures of condensation. The results challenge the simple picture that condensate decay yields N free alpha-particles and highlight the need for more theoretical input on tunnelling and Coulomb effects, while enabling future work with Time Projection Chambers and higher-dimensional Dalitz plots for $^{16}$O and heavier systems.
