# CVE for photons: black-hole vs. flat-space derivation

> The chiral vortical effect (CVE) was derived first for non-interacting massless fermions. Recently, an alternative derivation of the CVE was suggested which relates it to the radiation from the horizon of a rotating black hole. We attempt to generalize the latter derivation to the case of photons and encounter a crucial factor of two difference between the two ways of visualizing the CVE. Reservations and possible explanations are briefly discussed.

# CVE for photons: black-hole vs. flat-space derivation

## Key claim

The chiral vortical effect (CVE) for photons, when derived via the gravitational anomaly approach (black-hole horizon radiation), gives a value four times larger than the Weyl fermion CVE, whereas the flat-space Kubo-type relation gives a factor of two; for higher spins the gravitational approach predicts an S^3 scaling while thermal field theory predicts linear-in-S scaling, indicating a qualitative mismatch that may be resolved by infrared regularization subtleties.

## Method

The authors compare two derivations of the CVE for massless particles: (1) a gravitational-anomaly approach following Stone (2012), where the CVE current is obtained by integrating the gravitational chiral anomaly (proportional to R-tilde-R) near a rotating black-hole horizon and matching to the Hawking temperature; (2) flat-space thermal field theory using Kubo-type relations (retarded correlator of the chiral current with the momentum density) and statistical averaging of matrix elements over single-particle states in a rotating medium. For photons, the chiral current K^mu is defined via the Chern-Simons-like expression epsilon^{munurhosigma} A_nu partial_rho A_sigma, and its gravitational anomaly coefficient is taken from Vainshtein (1972) and Erdmenger (1999). The spin dependence is examined by generalizing to arbitrary spin S using the gravitational anomaly coefficient proportional to (2S^3 - S) from Duff (1977) and Vainshtein (2006), and comparing with the flat-space statistical operator approach of Becattini (2015) that couples spin to acceleration and vorticity.

## Key numbers

* For a single right-handed Weyl fermion, the CVE number current is j^N_CVE = (mu^2/(4 pi^2) + T^2/12) Omega (Eq. 1).
* The photonic CVE from the black-hole gravitational anomaly gives (CVE)_photons / (CVE)_Weyl fermions = 4 (Eq. 8).
* The photonic CVE from the Kubo-type flat-space relation gives (CVE)_photons / (CVE)_Weyl spinor = 2 (Eq. 11).
* The statistical averaging approach of Landsteiner (2019) yields J^gamma_CVE = (2/9) T^2 Omega + O(Omega^2) for photons (Eq. 14), which differs from the Kubo result by a factor of 4/3.
* For arbitrary spin S, the gravitational anomaly prediction for the T^2 term is K_S = [(-1)^{2S} (2S^3 - S)/3] T^2 Omega (Eq. 17).
* The flat-space thermal field theory prediction for spin S gives J_chi ~ S * Omega (Eq. 18), linear in spin.

## Context

This work extends the black-hole/gravitational-anomaly derivation of the CVE, originally formulated for massless fermions by Vilenkin (1988) and Stone (2012), to the case of photons and higher-spin particles. It directly confronts the gravitational approach with flat-space thermal field theory calculations using Kubo relations (Megias (2014), Songolkar (2019), Ren (2019)) and statistical averaging (Landsteiner (2019)). The discrepancy for photons (factor of 2 vs. factor of 4) and the qualitative S^3 vs. S scaling for higher spins highlight unresolved infrared regularization issues, potentially related to zero modes in rotating systems and the conflict between evaluating the CVE on the rotation axis (rho=0) and in the zero-momentum limit required for gauge invariance. The authors also discuss an alternative statistical approach using the density operator of Becattini (2015) that reproduces the S^3 dependence through spin-acceleration coupling, and note that theories with massless particles of spin S >= 3/2 suffer from vacuum instability.
