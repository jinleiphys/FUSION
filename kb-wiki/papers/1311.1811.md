# The many facets of the (non relativistic) Nuclear Equation of State

> A nucleus is a quantum many body system made of strongly interacting Fermions, protons and neutrons (nucleons). This produces a rich Nuclear Equation of State whose knowledge is crucial to our understanding of the composition and evolution of celestial objects. The nuclear equation of state displays many different features; first neutrons and protons might be treated as identical particles or nucleons, but when the differences between protons and neutrons are spelled out, we can have completely different scenarios, just by changing slightly their interactions. At zero temperature and for neutron rich matter, a quantum liquid gas phase transition at low densities or a quark-gluon plasma at high densities might occur. Furthermore, the large binding energy of the $α$ particle, a Boson, might also open the possibility of studying a system made of a mixture of Bosons and Fermions, which adds to the open problems of the nuclear equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the nuclear equation of state (NEOS) is extremely sensitive to the symmetry energy, and that by slightly changing the symmetry energy one can obtain at zero temperature either a quantum liquid-gas phase transition or a quark-gluon plasma by increasing the neutron concentration, but this is presented as an illustrative assumption rather than a confirmed result.

## Method

The authors use the semi-empirical Weizsacker mass formula and a Fermi gas model with finite-size corrections (volume, surface, and curvature terms) to extract the symmetry energy from experimental binding energies and Isobaric Analog State (IAS) excitation energies. They fit the symmetry energy coefficients using expansions up to sixth order in the neutron-proton asymmetry parameter \(m_\chi = (N-Z)/A\), including Wigner term corrections, and compare different parametrizations against data from known nuclei.

## Key numbers

* Ground state nuclear density: \(\rho_0 = 0.16\ \mathrm{fm}^{-3}\)
* Fermi momentum at ground state density: \(p_F = 263\ \mathrm{MeV}/c\)
* Fermi energy at ground state density: \(\epsilon_F = 36.8\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* Nuclear compressibility: \(K = 250 \pm 25\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* Volume term in mass formula: \(a_v = 15.835\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* Symmetry energy coefficient from mass formula: \(a_a = 23.2\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* Average symmetry energy target: \(\bar E_s = 32\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* From IAS analysis, volume symmetry coefficient: \(a_a^V = 35.51\ \mathrm{MeV}\) and surface symmetry coefficient: \(a_a^S = 9.89\ \mathrm{MeV}\)
* From Fermi gas fit to IAS data, volume symmetry coefficient: \(a_a^V = 28\ \mathrm{MeV}\) to \(29\ \mathrm{MeV}\)

## Context

This work reviews and extends the liquid drop model and Weizsacker mass formula (Weizsacker 1935) by incorporating finite-size Fermi gas corrections and IAS methods developed by Danielewicz and collaborators (Danielewicz 2004, 2007). It enables improved constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which is crucial for understanding neutron star structure and supernovae explosions, and motivates the need for experimental data from exotic nuclei facilities such as FRIB, FAIR, and RIKEN.
