# Tagging boosted hadronic objects with dynamical grooming

> We evaluate the phenomenological applicability of the dynamical grooming technique, introduced in [1], to boosted W and top tagging at LHC conditions. An extension of our method intended for multi-prong decays with an internal mass scale, such as the top quark decay, is presented. First, we tackle the reconstruction of the mass distribution of W and top jets quantifying the smearing due to pileup. When compared to state-of-the-art grooming algorithms like SoftDrop and its recursive version, dynamical grooming shows an enhanced resilience to background fluctuations. In addition, we asses the discriminating power of dynamical grooming to distinguish W (top) jets from QCD ones by performing a two-step analysis: introduce a cut on the groomed mass around the W (top) mass peak followed by a restriction on the N-subjettinnes ratio $τ_{21}$ ($τ_{32}$). For W jets, the out-of-the-box version of dynamical grooming, free of ad-hoc parameters, results into a comparable performance to SoftDrop. Regarding the top tagger efficiency, 3-prong dynamical grooming, in spite of its simplicity, presents better performance than SoftDrop and similar results to Recursive SoftDrop.

## Key claim

Dynamical grooming, particularly the 3-prong extension for multi-scale decays, can match or exceed the performance of SoftDrop and Recursive SoftDrop for boosted W and top tagging at LHC conditions, while being free of ad-hoc parameters that require Monte-Carlo tuning; the method shows enhanced resilience to pileup contamination, especially at high-luminosity conditions with 200 pileup events.

## Method

The authors simulate 25k p+p collisions at 13 TeV using Pythia 8.235, clustering particles into anti-kT jets with R=0.8 and reclustering with Cambridge/Aachen. For two-prong W tagging, they apply dynamical grooming to select the hardest splitting in the primary Lund plane using three hardness definitions: zDrop (a=0.1), kTDrop (a=1), and TimeDrop (a=2), then drop all branches at larger angles. For three-prong top tagging, they extend the method to identify both the hardest and next-to-hardest splittings, dropping branches outside the angular region defined by these two splittings. The tagging performance is evaluated by applying a mass window cut around the resonance peak followed by a cut on the N-subjettiness ratio (τ21 for W, τ32 for top), generating ROC curves by varying the τ cut value.

## Key numbers

- W mass resolution ΓW is less than 5 GeV (∼6% of MW) for kTDrop and TimeDrop in the absence of pileup
- For HL-LHC conditions (nPU=200), kTDrop achieves the smallest mass width ΓW, 20-30% smaller than RSD2 and SD respectively, while keeping the mass peak within 2 GeV of the resonance value
- For top jets with no pileup, 3-Prong kTDrop and TimeDrop give a mass peak 1 GeV closer to the resonance value than SoftDrop, with similar Γtop values
- For top jets at nPU=200, the mass distribution width is roughly doubled compared to the no-pileup scenario for all methods
- The W tagging ROC curves show kTDrop and TimeDrop perform comparably to SoftDrop for W tagging efficiencies above 40%
- For top tagging, kTDrop matches the performance of Recursive SoftDrop (RSD∞) without requiring parameter tuning

## Context

This work extends the dynamical grooming technique introduced by Mehtar-Tani (2019) to multi-prong decays with an internal mass scale, specifically W bosons and top quarks. It compares against state-of-the-art grooming algorithms including SoftDrop (Larkoski, 2014) and Recursive SoftDrop (Dreyer, 2018), finding that dynamical grooming offers comparable or better performance while eliminating the need for ad-hoc parameter optimization. The method enables a parameter-free approach to boosted object tagging at the HL-LHC, with potential extensions to 4-prong decays for Higgs searches as suggested by Butterworth (2008).
