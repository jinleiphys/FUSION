# Novae breves from magnetar giant flares: Potential probes of neutron star crusts

> Matter ejected from the magnetar crust during giant flares (GFs) may undergo $r$-process nucleosynthesis, producing short-lived optical transients termed "novae breves". Although intrinsically much fainter than kilonovae from compact binary mergers, novae breves may occur within or near the Galaxy, making them promising observational targets. We aim to investigate how the neutron star (NS) equation of state (EOS) and the mass of the central magnetar affect the ejecta properties following GFs and the resulting nova brevis emission. We employ a semi-analytical ejecta model combined with nuclear reaction network calculations to compute nucleosynthesis yields and multi-band light curves for different EOSs and magnetar masses, and assess their detectability with current and future facilities. We find that variations in the EOS and magnetar mass modify the ejecta mass and its density and velocity distributions, etc., leading to observable differences in nova brevis light curves. In particular, both the peak luminosity and the characteristic peak timescale are EOS-dependent. Assuming a fixed Galactic magnetar mass of 1.4 solar mass and taking the $u$ band as an example, we find that the minimum apparent AB magnitudes range from 7 mag (H4 EOS) to 8.5 mag (WFF EOS) with peak timescales of 100-1000 s. A more massive magnetar produces fainter emission with a shorter peak timescale. For a magnetar mass of 1.4 solar mass, novae breves associated with known magnetars may reach peak luminosities of 1e37-1e39 erg/s, enabling targeted searches, particularly following high-energy GF alerts. Moreover, a detection horizon of 10 Mpc or beyond is achievable with current and future facilities, allowing searches for novae breves from previously unknown magnetars in the Local Volume. Although challenging, detection of such rapidly evolving transients is feasible.

# Novae breves from magnetar giant flares: Potential probes of neutron star crusts

## Key claim
Variations in the neutron star equation of state (EOS) and magnetar mass modify the ejecta mass, density, and velocity distributions from magnetar giant flares, leading to observable differences in nova brevis light curves; however, there is a degeneracy between EOS and magnetar mass that requires additional observational constraints to break.

## Method
The authors employed a semi-analytical ejecta model combined with the SkyNet nuclear reaction network to compute nucleosynthesis yields and multi-band light curves for five representative EOSs (APR, ENG, H4, SLy, WFF) at two magnetar masses (1.4 and 2.0 solar masses). The ejecta was divided into 30 concentric layers, each evolved independently for dynamical, thermal, and nucleosynthetic evolution, with radioactive heating rates from the Evaluated Nuclear Data File library (ENDF/B-VIII.1). Detectability was assessed against current and future facilities including LSST, UVOT, WFST, and CSST.

## Key numbers
- Minimum apparent AB magnitudes in the u band range from ~7 mag (H4 EOS) to ~8.5 mag (WFF EOS) for a 1.4 solar mass magnetar at a fiducial Galactic distance of 10 kpc
- Peak timescales for novae breves are 100-1000 seconds
- Peak bolometric luminosities for a 1.4 solar mass magnetar reach ~10^37-10^39 erg/s
- Ejecta masses range from 6.58 x 10^-8 solar masses (WFF EOS, 1.4 solar mass) to 30.1 x 10^-8 solar masses (H4 EOS, 1.4 solar mass); for 2.0 solar mass magnetars, ejecta masses range from 2.44 x 10^-8 (WFF) to 7.70 x 10^-8 (H4)
- Detection horizon of ~10 Mpc or beyond is achievable with current and future facilities
- For SGR 1806-20, peak bolometric luminosities across EOSs range from 12.9 x 10^38 erg/s (WFF) to 28.5 x 10^38 erg/s (H4) at a distance of 8.7 kpc

## Context
This work extends the novae breves framework proposed by Cehula (2023) and Patel (2025, 2026) by systematically investigating how different neutron star EOSs and magnetar masses affect the resulting optical transients. The study addresses the discrepancy that compact binary mergers alone may not fully account for observed heavy-element abundances in the Galaxy, as noted by Cote (2018) and Zevin (2019). The results enable future targeted searches for novae breves following high-energy giant flare alerts, potentially constraining neutron star crustal properties and the EOS, and complement kilonova studies from compact binary mergers such as AT2017gfo associated with GW170817.
