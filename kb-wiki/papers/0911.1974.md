# Understanding the proton's spin structure

> We discuss the tremendous progress that has been towards an understanding of how the spin of the proton is distributed on its quark and gluon constituents. This is a problem that began in earnest twenty years ago with the discovery of the proton ``spin crisis'' by the European Muon Collaboration. The discoveries prompted by that original work have given us unprecedented insight into the amount of spin carried by polarized gluons and the orbital angular momentum of the quarks.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the modern experimental value of the quark spin contribution to the proton spin, approximately one third of the total, can be explained by well-known non-perturbative QCD effects, specifically the relativistic motion of confined valence quarks, the quark-quark hyperfine interaction from effective one-gluon exchange, and the pion cloud, without requiring a large contribution from polarized gluons, contrary to early interpretations of the "spin crisis."

## Method

The authors review experimental data from deep inelastic scattering (DIS) and polarized proton-proton reactions at CERN, DESY, JLab, RHIC, and SLAC, focusing on the first moment of the spin structure function g₁. They then compute the flavor-singlet axial current matrix element Σ using the cloudy bag model (CBM), incorporating relativistic valence quark corrections (reduction factor ~0.65), two-quark axial currents from the one-gluon-exchange hyperfine interaction (reducing Σ by ~0.15), and pion-cloud Fock state contributions (Nπ and Δπ probabilities ~20-25% and ~5-10%, respectively). The model results are compared to lattice QCD evaluations of quark orbital angular momentum from the LHPC collaboration and to generalized parton correlation function (GPCF) measurements.

## Key numbers

- The experimental quark spin contribution to the proton spin is Σ = 0.33 ± 0.03 (stat.) ± 0.05 (syst.) from combined COMPASS, HERMES, and other data.
- The relativistic reduction factor for the axial charge of light quarks in the bag model is B = Ω/3(Ω - 1) ≈ 0.65, where Ω ≈ 2.04 for massless quarks in a 1 fm cavity.
- The one-gluon-exchange correction reduces the quark spin fraction by 0.15, with a correction term G ≈ 0.05 proportional to α_s times a bag model matrix element.
- The pion cloud probabilities are P_{Nπ} ~ 0.20-0.25 and P_{Δπ} ~ 0.05-0.10, with a bare nucleon probability Z ~ 0.7.
- The model yields Σ_{quark} in the range (0.35, 0.40), consistent with the experimental Σ.
- At Q² ≈ 3 (GeV/c)², α_s ≈ 0.3; if ΔG ≈ 0.4, the anomaly term contributes about 0.06 to Σ.
- The extracted orbital angular momenta from the model are L^{u+ū} ≈ 0.50 and L^{d+d̄} ≈ 0.12, giving J^{u+ū} ≈ 0.72 and J^{d+d̄} ≈ -0.10.

## Context

This review extends the work of Myhrer and Thomas (2008) and earlier studies by Høgaasen and Myhrer (1988) and Schreiber and Thomas (1988), which used the cloudy bag model to explain the proton spin structure. It contrasts with the interpretation that polarized gluons (ΔG) are primarily responsible for the small Σ, as suggested by Efremov, Altarelli, and others following the EMC discovery (Ashman, 1987). The paper enables future lattice QCD calculations and experimental measurements of generalized parton distributions (GPDs) and transverse momentum distributions (TMDs) to discriminate between models of quark orbital angular momentum, such as the chiral quark soliton model of Wakamatsu and Tsujimoto (2005).
