# High-spin torus isomers and their precession motions

> We systematically investigate the existence of exotic torus isomers and their precession motions for a series of $N=Z$ even-even nuclei from $^{28}$Si to $^{56}$Ni. We analyze the microscopic shell structure of the torus isomer and discuss why the torus shape is generated beyond the limit of large oblate deformation. We use the cranked three-dimensional Hartree-Fock (HF) method with various Skyrme interactions in a systematic search for high-spin torus isomers. We use the three-dimensional time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method for describing the precession motion of the torus isomer. We obtain high-spin torus isomers in $^{36}$Ar, $^{40}$Ca, $^{44}$Ti, $^{48}$Cr, and $^{52}$Fe. The emergence of the torus isomers is associated with the alignments of single-particle angular momenta, which is the same mechanism as found in $^{40}$Ca. It is found that all the obtained torus isomers execute the precession motion at least two rotational periods. The moment of inertia about a perpendicular axis, which characterizes the precession motion, is found to be close to the classical rigid-body value. The high-spin torus isomer of $^{40}$Ca is not an exceptional case. Similar torus isomers exist widely in nuclei from $^{36}$Ar to $^{52}$Fe and they execute the precession motion. The torus shape is generated beyond the limit of large oblate deformation by eliminating the $0s$ components from all the deformed single-particle wave functions to maximize their mutual overlaps.

## Key claim

High-spin torus isomers exist systematically in a series of N=Z even-even nuclei from 36Ar to 52Fe, not only in 40Ca, and all such isomers execute precession motion for at least two rotational periods, with the moment of inertia about a perpendicular axis close to the classical rigid-body value.

## Method

The authors used the cranked three-dimensional Hartree-Fock (HF) method with SLy6, SkI3, and SkM* Skyrme interactions to systematically search for stable torus isomers in N=Z even-even nuclei from 28Si to 56Ni, starting from alpha-cluster ring configurations. They then applied the three-dimensional time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method to describe the precession motion of each obtained torus isomer, providing an impulsive force to excite the motion and tracking the time evolution for up to 3000 fm/c.

## Key numbers

* Torus isomers were found in 36Ar (Jz=36 hbar), 40Ca (Jz=60 hbar), 44Ti (Jz=44 hbar), 48Cr (Jz=72 hbar), and 52Fe (Jz=52 hbar) with all three Skyrme interactions.
* Excitation energies for the torus isomers with the SLy6 interaction range from 123.89 MeV (36Ar) to 191.25 MeV (48Cr).
* The torus ring radius R0 ranges from 5.12 fm (36Ar) to 7.47 fm (52Fe) with SLy6; the cross-section width d is approximately 1.60-1.62 fm for all isomers with SLy6.
* The moment of inertia about a perpendicular axis from TDHF (SLy6) ranges from 13.5 hbar^2/MeV (36Ar) to 37.3 hbar^2/MeV (52Fe), close to the rigid-body values (14.3 to 39.1 hbar^2/MeV respectively).
* Precession periods (SLy6) range from 402.5 fm/c (40Ca) to 872.8 fm/c (52Fe).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous finding of a torus isomer in 40Ca (Ichikawa 2012, 2014) to a systematic survey of neighboring nuclei, confirming that 40Ca is not an exceptional case. About forty years ago, Wong (1973) suggested the possible existence of torus isomers using a macroscopic-microscopic method, and recently Staszczak and Wong (2014) explored torus isomers using constrained cranked HF with harmonic-oscillator basis expansion, which the authors note is insufficient to treat unbound states. The present results enable further study of these exotic high-spin states as a new form of nuclear matter with macroscopic circulating currents, as suggested by Bohr and Mottelson (1981).
