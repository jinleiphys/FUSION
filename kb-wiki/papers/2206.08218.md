# Influence of nuclear structure in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Many probes are proposed to determine the quark-gluon plasma and explore its properties in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Some of them are related to initial states of the collisions, such as collective flow, Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) correlation, chiral magnetic effects and so on. The initial states can come from geometry overlap of the colliding nuclei, fluctuations or nuclear structure with the intrinsic geometry asymmetry. The initial geometry asymmetry can transfer to the final momentum distribution in the aspect of hydrodynamics during the evolution of the fireball. Different from traditional methods for nuclear structure study, the ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions could provide a potential platform to investigate nuclear structures with the help of the final-state observables after the fireball expansion. This chapter first presents a brief introduction of the initial states in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, and then delivers a mini-review for the nuclear structure effects on experimental observables in the relativistic energy domain.

## Key claim

Ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions can serve as a potential platform to investigate nuclear structures (such as α-clustering and neutron skin) through final-state observables after fireball expansion, but the transformation from initial geometry asymmetry to final momentum distribution depends on hydrodynamic evolution and is not yet fully understood for different-size collision systems.

## Method

The authors review how initial-state geometry in relativistic heavy-ion collisions arises from nuclear overlap, fluctuations, and intrinsic nuclear structure (e.g., α-clustered configurations in light nuclei like ¹²C and ¹⁶O, or neutron skin in neutron-rich nuclei). They describe simulations using the AMPT model and event-by-event (3+1)–dimensional viscous hydrodynamics, where initial nucleon positions are configured with Woods-Saxon distributions or with α-cluster arrangements (triangle for ¹²C, tetrahedron for ¹⁶O), and the resulting anisotropic flow coefficients vₙ and eccentricity coefficients εₙ are compared.

## Key numbers

- For ¹²C + ¹⁹⁷Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV, the triangle α-cluster configuration enhanced ε₃{PP} with increasing N_track, while the Woods-Saxon case showed ε₂{PP} and ε₃{PP} slightly decreasing with N_track (PhysRevC.95.064904-SZhang2017).
- The rms radius of α-clustered ¹²C was 2.47 fm and of α-clustered ¹⁶O was 2.699 fm, consistent with experimental data (2.47 fm for ¹²C, 2.6991 fm for ¹⁶O) (ADNDT.99.69-nucleiRMS-ExpData).
- For ¹²C + ¹⁹⁷Au at √s_NN = 200 GeV, the ratio v₃{PP}/v₂{PP} from triangle α-clustered ¹²C showed an increasing trend with N_track, while the Woods-Saxon configuration gave a flat pattern at low p_T (PhysRevC.95.064904-SZhang2017).
- In ¹⁶O + Pb collisions at SPS energy √s_NN = 17 GeV, the ratio v₃{4}/v₃{2} showed a similar increasing trend with wounded nucleons N_w for α-clustered ¹⁶O (AlphaClusterHIC-Wojciech-PRC-2018).
- For ¹²C + Au collisions, the ratio v₂{4}/v₂{2} increased with N_w for α-clustered ⁷,⁹Be beams and decreased for uniform configuration (AlphaClusterHIC-Wojciech-PRC-2018).
- The side length parameters used were l_3α = 1.8 fm for ¹²C and l_4α = 3.42 fm for ¹⁶O, inherited from EQMD calculations (EQMD_PRL_113_032506).

## Context

This chapter reviews and extends work by Broniowski et al. (2014) and Broniowski et al. (2018) who first proposed using α-clustered ¹²C + Au collisions to study collective flow signatures, and by Zhang et al. (2017) who used the AMPT model to simulate ¹²C + ¹⁹⁷Au collisions with different α-cluster configurations. The review also discusses system-scan proposals by Li et al. (2020) for ¹⁶O beams at RHIC or LHC. The work enables future experimental programs at RHIC and LHC to use light-ion collisions (e.g., ¹⁶O + Pb) as a platform to distinguish α-clustered from uniform nuclear structure via flow observables, and opens the question of how to understand the transformation coefficient from initial geometry to final momentum distribution in different-size systems.
