# Second-order nonlocal shifts of scattered wave-packets: What can be measured by Goos-Hänchen and Imbert-Fedorov effects ?

> The scattering of wavepackets with arbitrary energy dispersion on surfaces has been analyzed. Expanding up to second order in scattering shifts, it is found that besides the known Goos-Hänchen or Imbert-Fedorov spatial offset, as well as the Wigner delay time, new momentum and frequency shifts appear. Furthermore, the width of the scattered wave packet becomes modified as well, which can lead to a shrinking of pulses by multiple scattering. For a model of dielectric material characterized by a longitudinal and transverse dielectric function the shifts are calculated analytically. From the Goos-Hänchen and Imbert-Fedorov shifts one can access the longitudinal and transversal dielectric function. Perfectly aligned crystal symmetry axes with respect to scattering beam shows no Imbert-Fedorov effect. It is found that the Goos-Hänchen and Imbert-Fedorov effect are absent for homogeneous materials. Oppositely it is found that the Wigner delay time and the shrinking of the temporal pulse width allows to access the dielectric function independent on the beam geometry.

## Key claim

The paper derives six distinct nonlocal shifts for scattered wave-packets, including new momentum and frequency shifts and a modification of the pulse width, by expanding the scattering amplitude to second order in energy and momentum dispersion. The claim that the Goos-Hanchen and Imbert-Fedorov shifts allow access to the longitudinal and transverse dielectric functions is qualified by the condition that these shifts vanish for homogeneous materials, whereas the Wigner delay time and temporal pulse width shrinking provide access to the dielectric function independent of beam geometry.

## Method

The author expands a three-dimensional incident wave-packet with arbitrary energy dispersion to second order in the scattering shifts, using derivatives of the scattering amplitude with respect to energy and momentum. The formalism is applied to a model of light scattering on a dielectric material characterized by longitudinal and transverse dielectric functions, where the scattering amplitude is proportional to the dot product of the electric field vector with the dielectric tensor. Gaussian averaging over the beam profile is performed to compute the shifts analytically.

## Key numbers

- The Goos-Hanchen shift vanishes quadratically with the parameter \(x_0 = k_0 / (\sigma \sqrt{2})\), where \(k_0\) is the mean wavenumber and \(\sigma\) is the momentum width of the wave-packet.
- For a Drude model of gold with plasma frequency \(\hbar \omega_p = 5.8\) eV and transverse relaxation time \(\tau = 20\) fs, the sign of the Goos-Hanchen shift changes when longitudinal and transverse relaxation times differ (\(\tau_l > \tau_t\)).
- The Wigner delay time changes sign at \(\omega_\Delta^2 = (\omega_p^2 / 3)(\omega_p \tau - 1/(\omega_p \tau))\) for \(\tau > 1\).
- The real part of the energy derivative of the dielectric function changes sign at \(\omega_\varphi^2 = (\omega_p^2 / 2)[1 + \sqrt{1 + 2/(\omega_p^2 \tau^2)}]\) for any relaxation time \(\tau\).
- The temporal width shrinking (effect 4) has a maximum at the plasma frequency for large relaxation times and a zero at \(\omega = \omega_p / \sqrt{3}\) for large \(\tau\).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of nonlocal shifts in wave-packet scattering, building on the foundational Goos-Hanchen effect (Goos and Hanchen, 1947) and Imbert-Fedorov effect (Fedorov, 2013). It generalizes prior treatments that average over Fresnel coefficients (Zhang and Deng, 2020; Li et al., 2020) or use transmission coefficients (Shen and Ai, 2021; Ma and Bliokh, 2022) by providing a complete second-order expansion valid for arbitrary surface materials and beam dispersions. The results enable experimental access to dielectric functions through time-domain measurements (Wigner delay and pulse width) that are independent of beam geometry, complementing spatial shift measurements that require specific crystal alignment.
