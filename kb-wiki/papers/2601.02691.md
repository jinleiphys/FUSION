# Collision energy and system size dependence of longitudinal flow decorrelation in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC energies

> In heavy-ion collisions, the initial collision geometry and its fluctuations drive the collective expansion of final-state hadrons in the transverse plane. However, longitudinal fluctuations induce event-plane twist and flow magnitude asymmetries, collectively known as longitudinal flow decorrelation. Using a multi-phase transport (AMPT) model, we systematically investigate the dependence of collision energy and system size of this phenomenon with Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}$ = 19.6, 27, 54.4, 200 GeV and isobar collisions (Zr+Zr and Ru+Ru) at $\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}$ = 200 GeV. The results reveal two distinct decorrelation components: $r_n(η)$, which includes flow magnitude asymmetry and event-plane twist, and $R_n(η)$ which arises purely from event-plane twist. Both $r_n(η)$ and $R_n(η)$ decrease linearly with $η$ and exhibit a significant dependence on collision energy and the size of the system. Through the slope parameters $F_n$ in the linear parametrization $r_n(η) = 1-2F_nη$, we can quantify the strength of decorrelation. We further observe that both $F_2$ and $F_3$ demonstrate a pronounced power-law scaling behavior with collision energy, following the relation $F_n \propto log \sqrt{s_{NN}}$. These results provide valuable insights into the three-dimensional modeling of the initial stage and the evolution of relativistic heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

Using the AMPT model, the authors find that longitudinal flow decorrelation in heavy-ion collisions depends on both collision energy and system size, with lower energies and smaller systems producing stronger decorrelation; however, the energy dependence persists even after normalizing pseudorapidity by beam rapidity, indicating that the effect cannot be fully explained by simple kinematic scaling.

## Method

The study uses the string-melting version of the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model with a 3 mb elastic parton-parton cross section to simulate Au+Au collisions at four center-of-mass energies (19.6, 27, 54.4, and 200 GeV) and isobar (Zr+Zr and Ru+Ru) collisions at 200 GeV. The flow decorrelation is quantified using two observables: \(r_n(\eta)\), which combines flow magnitude asymmetry and event-plane twist, and \(R_n(\eta)\), which isolates the event-plane twist contribution. The slope parameters \(F_2\) and \(F_3\) are extracted from a linear parametrization \(r_n(\eta) = 1 - 2F_n\eta\) to quantify decorrelation strength across centrality bins determined by charged-particle multiplicity within \(|\eta| < 0.5\).

## Key numbers

- Au+Au collisions simulated at \(\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}\) = 19.6, 27, 54.4, and 200 GeV; isobar collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}\) = 200 GeV
- Transverse momentum range for final-state hadrons: \(0.4 < p_T < 4\) GeV; pseudorapidity range: \(|\eta| < 1.5\)
- Reference pseudorapidity: \(3.1 < |\eta_{\mathrm{ref}}| < 5.1\) for \(r_2\) and \(2.1 < |\eta_{\mathrm{ref}}| < 5.1\) for \(r_3\)
- Beam rapidities: \(y_{\mathrm{beam}} = 5.36\) for 200 GeV, 4.06 for 54.4 GeV, 3.36 for 27 GeV, and 3.04 for 19.6 GeV
- \(r_2\) values in Au+Au are approximately 4% larger than in isobar collisions in 0-10% centrality and 5% larger in 10-40% centrality; \(r_3\) difference is around 1% in 0-10% and 2% in 10-40% centrality
- Both \(F_2\) and \(F_3\) follow \(F_n \propto \log \sqrt{s_{\mathrm{NN}}}\) scaling with collision energy

## Context

This work extends previous AMPT studies of longitudinal flow decorrelation at RHIC energies, such as Dixit (2023) which investigated energy dependence of \(r_2\) and \(r_3\) but may have been affected by nonflow contributions due to a different pseudorapidity range. The study also builds on the torqued fireball model of Bozek (2015), the 3DMCG model of Shen (2017), and the 3DGlasma approach of Schenke (2016). The results provide constraints for three-dimensional modeling of the initial stage and dynamical evolution in heavy-ion collisions, and can be tested against experimental data from the RHIC STAR experiment.
