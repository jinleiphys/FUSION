# Radii in the $sd$ shell and the $s_{1/2}$ "halo" orbit: A game changer

> Proton radii of nuclei in the $sd$ shell depart appreciably from the asymptotic law, $ρ_π=ρ_0A^{1/3}$. The departure exhibits systematic trends fairly well described by a single phenomenological term in the Duflo-Zuker formulation, which also happens to explain the sudden increase in slope in the isotope shifts of several chains at neutron number $N=28$. It was recently shown that this term is associated with the abnormally large size of the $s_{1/2}$ and $p$ orbits in the $sd$ and $pf$ shells respectively. Further to explore the problem, we propose to calculate microscopically radii in the former. Since the (square) radius is basically a one body operator, its evolution is dictated by single particle occupancies determined by shell model calculations. Assuming that the departure from the asymptotic form is entirely due to the $s_{1/2}$ orbit, the expectation value $\langle s_{1/2}|r^2|s_{1/2}\rangle$ is determined by demanding that its evolution be such as to describe well nuclear radii. It does, for an orbit that remains very large (about 1.6 fm bigger than its $d$ counterparts) up to $N,\,Z=14$ then drops abruptly but remains some 0.6 fm larger than the $d$ orbits. An unexpected behavior bound to challenge our understanding of shell formation.

# Radii in the $sd$ shell and the $s_{1/2}$ "halo" orbit: A game changer

## Key claim

The departure of proton radii from the asymptotic $A^{1/3}$ law in $sd$-shell nuclei can be explained microscopically by assuming that the $s_{1/2}$ orbit is solely responsible for the fluctuations, with its size evolving as a step function: very large (about 1.6 fm bigger than the $d$ orbits) up to $N, Z = 14$, then dropping abruptly but remaining some 0.6 fm larger than the $d$ orbits. This behavior challenges the conventional understanding of shell formation and suggests that magic closures may be associated with unbinding of halo orbits rather than self-binding of high-$j$ orbits.

## Method

The authors constructed a microscopic parametrization of nuclear radii as a functional of single-particle occupation numbers, inspired by the Duflo-Zuker phenomenological fit. They assumed that only the $s_{1/2}$ orbit contributes to the correlation term, setting $\delta_i = 0$ for the $0d_{5/2,3/2}$ orbits and $\delta_i = \delta$ for the $s_{1/2}$ orbit, with $\delta$ taking two values separated by a step function at the extruder-intruder magic number 14. The occupancies were determined by diagonalizing the USDa and MCI (monopole corrected interaction derived from the N3LO potential) Hamiltonians, and the optimal $\delta$ values were found by minimizing the root-mean-square deviation of calculated radii from experimental data sets.

## Key numbers

- The $s_{1/2}$ orbit is about 1.6 fm bigger than its $d$ counterparts (which have radii of about 3.5 fm) up to $N, Z = 14$, then drops abruptly but remains some 0.6 fm larger than the $d$ orbits.
- For the Duflo data set with the MCI interaction: $\delta_< = 4.25$, $\delta_> = 1.35$, rmsd = 0.020 fm.
- For the Duflo data set with the USDa interaction: $\delta_< = 4.90$, $\delta_> = 1.40$, rmsd = 0.023 fm.
- For the Angeli-Marinova data set with the MCI interaction: $\delta_< = 4.80$, $\delta_> = 1.35$, rmsd = 0.018 fm.
- For the Angeli-Marinova data set with the USDa interaction: $\delta_< = 5.50$, $\delta_> = 1.45$, rmsd = 0.023 fm.
- The Duflo-Zuker correlated fit (Eq. 2 with $\lambda$ term) reduces the rmsd from 0.0299 fm to 0.0132 fm for the D data set (636 data points, $Z \leq 96$), and from 0.0365 fm to 0.0203 fm for the AM data set (876 data points, $Z \leq 96$).
- The difference $\rho_s - \rho_d$ between root-mean-square radii of the $s_{1/2}$ and $0d$ orbits: mean values of (1.56 fm, 0.6 fm) for MCI with D data, (1.75 fm, 0.62 fm) for USDa with D data.

## Context

This work extends the Duflo-Zuker phenomenological approach (Duflo and Zuker, 1995) which introduced a correlation term that explained the sudden increase in slope in isotope shifts of Ca and K chains at neutron number $N=28$, as shown in Bonnard, Lenzi, and Zuker (2016). The microscopic calculation demonstrates that the correlation term originates from the abnormally large $s_{1/2}$ orbit, and the results reveal a peculiar behavior that challenges the conventional understanding of shell formation. The authors suggest that existing shell-model calculations using harmonic oscillator wave functions may fail to capture halo orbits, and that relativistic mean-field calculations (such as those using the DD-ME2 interaction) appear capable of reproducing the slope increase in isotope shifts. This work enables future investigations into whether magic closures are associated with unbinding of halo orbits rather than self-binding of high-$j$ orbits, and highlights the need for non-local potentials to describe these halo orbits.
