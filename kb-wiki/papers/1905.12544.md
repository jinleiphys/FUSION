# Dynamics of Phase Separation from Holography

> We use holography to develop a physical picture of the real-time evolution of the spinodal instability of a four-dimensional, strongly-coupled gauge theory with a first-order, thermal phase transition. We numerically solve Einstein's equations to follow the evolution, in which we identify four generic stages: A first, linear stage in which the instability grows exponentially; a second, non-linear stage in which peaks and/or phase domains are formed; a third stage in which these structures merge; and a fourth stage in which the system finally relaxes to a static, phase-separated configuration. On the gravity side the latter is described by a static, stable, inhomogeneous horizon. We conjecture and provide evidence that all static, non-phase separated configurations in large enough boxes are dynamically unstable. We show that all four stages are well described by the constitutive relations of second-order hydrodynamics that include all second-order gradients that are purely spatial in the local rest frame. In contrast, a Müller-Israel-Stewart-type formulation of hydrodynamics fails to provide a good description for two reasons. First, it misses some large, purely-spatial gradient corrections. Second, several second-order transport coefficients in this formulation, including the relaxation times $τ_π$ and $τ_Π$, diverge at the points where the speed of sound vanishes.

## Key claim

The authors show that the real-time evolution of the spinodal instability in a strongly-coupled gauge theory with a first-order phase transition proceeds through four generic stages, and that all four stages are well described by second-order hydrodynamics that includes all second-order gradients that are purely spatial in the local rest frame, whereas a Muller-Israel-Stewart-type formulation fails because it misses some large purely-spatial gradient corrections and because several second-order transport coefficients, including the relaxation times tau_pi and tau_Pi, diverge at points where the speed of sound vanishes.

## Method

The authors numerically solve the Einstein-scalar equations in five dimensions, using a potential derived from a superpotential that yields a first-order thermal phase transition in the dual four-dimensional gauge theory. They place the system in an initial homogeneous state with an energy density in the spinodal region, compactify the z-direction on a circle of length L in the range L*Lambda between 107 and 213, and follow the evolution by extracting the boundary stress tensor from the dynamical metric. They perform 15 runs and publish the boundary data as open data.

## Key numbers

- The critical temperature of the first-order phase transition is T_c = 0.247 Lambda.
- The high-energy phase at T_c has energy density E_high = 5.9 x 10^(-2) Lambda^4; the low-energy phase has E_low = 9.4 x 10^(-5) Lambda^4; the ratio E_high/E_low is approximately 0.6 x 10^3.
- The ratio of bulk viscosity over entropy density zeta/s has a maximum value of approximately 0.0678 at T/Lambda = 0.245.
- The second-order transport coefficient f_L extracted from fits for two energy densities is f_L(E_2) = f_L(E_3) = -0.136 / Lambda^2.
- The box sizes used are L*Lambda in the range (107, 213), compared to L*Lambda = 57 in prior work (Attems 2017).
- The initial energy densities studied are {E_1, E_2, E_3, E_4, E_5} = {2.6, 1.9, 1.6, 1.2, 1.0} x 10^(-2) Lambda^4.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Attems (2017) on the same holographic model, which first studied the spinodal instability, and complements the work of Janik (2017) on a three-dimensional gauge theory. The results are relevant for understanding the real-time dynamics of phase separation in strongly-coupled non-Abelian plasmas, which may be important for future heavy ion collision experiments such as the beam energy scan at RHIC, the compressed baryonic matter experiment at FAIR, and experiments at NICA, where a first-order phase transition line ending at a critical point is expected in the QCD phase diagram. The paper enables further study of hydrodynamic descriptions near phase transitions and provides evidence that static non-phase-separated configurations in large enough boxes are dynamically unstable.
