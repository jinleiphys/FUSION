# The anatomy of the simplest Duflo-Zuker mass formula

> The simplest version of the Duflo-Zuker mass model (due entirely to the late Jean Duflo) is described by following step by step the published computer code. The model contains six macroscopic monopole terms leading asymptotically to a Liquid Drop form, three microscopic terms supposed to mock configuration mixing (multipole) corrections to the monopole shell effects, and one term in charge of detecting deformed nuclei and calculating their masses. A careful analysis of the model suggests a program of future developments that includes a complementary approach to masses based on an independently determined monopole Hamiltonian, a better description of deformations and specific suggestions for the treatment of three body forces.

# The anatomy of the simplest Duflo-Zuker mass formula

## Key claim
The DZ10 mass formula achieves a root-mean-square deviation of 0.55 MeV against the AME03 mass compilation, but its three "anomalous" spherical terms contain three-body effects of unknown origin and scale with the total particle number A rather than with A^{1/3} as shell effects should, revealing that the model incorrectly treats A^{1/3} contributions as genuine shell effects rather than as part of the asymptotic liquid-drop form.

## Method
The authors dissect the ten-parameter Duflo-Zuker (DZ10) mass model by following its published computer code step by step. They fit the six macroscopic monopole terms (volume, surface, Coulomb, asymmetry, surface asymmetry, and pairing) and the three microscopic spherical terms plus one deformation term to the 2149 nuclear masses in AME03 with N,Z ≥ 8, using the Minuit minimization routine. For each nucleus, both spherical and deformed calculations are performed, and the one with the larger binding energy is selected; for Z < 50 only the spherical calculation is used.

## Key numbers
- RMSD of the full DZ10 fit (all 10 terms) against AME03: 0.554 MeV for 2149 nuclei with N,Z ≥ 8; 0.482 MeV for 1825 nuclei with N,Z ≥ 28
- RMSD of the macroscopic sector alone (6 terms): 2.852 MeV
- RMSD of the macroscopic plus three spherical terms (9 terms, no deformation): 0.717 MeV
- RMSD of the macroscopic plus deformation term only (7 terms, no spherical terms): 2.280 MeV
- Predictive test: fitting on AME95 subset and predicting new AME03 masses gives RMSD 0.782 MeV (N,Z ≥ 8) and 0.628 MeV (N,Z ≥ 28)
- Predictive test: fitting nuclei with A ≤ 160 and predicting heavier nuclei gives RMSD 0.905 MeV (N,Z ≥ 8) and 1.233 MeV (N,Z ≥ 28)
- Predictive test: fitting nuclei with A ≤ 200 and predicting the remainder gives RMSD 0.501 MeV (N,Z ≥ 8) and 0.473 MeV (N,Z ≥ 28)
- Number of nuclei classified as deformed in the fit: 322 out of 2149

## Context
This work provides a detailed pedagogical exposition of the DZ10 model originally developed by Jean Duflo, which was shown by Lunney, Pearson, and Thibault (2003) to have outstanding predictive power compared to other mass models when tested against the 300+ new masses in AME03 that were not in AME95. The paper extends earlier work by Duflo (1994) and Zuker (1994) on algebraic mass formulas and their microscopic derivation, and builds on the demonstration by Dufour and Zuker (1996) that realistic interactions yield a dominant collective monopole term. The analysis identifies the need for future developments including an independently determined monopole Hamiltonian, better treatment of deformations, and explicit inclusion of three-body forces, which the authors argue are essential to explain the cubic terms in the valence-space operators.
