# Combinatorial Level Densities from a Microscopic Relativistic Structure Model

> A new model for calculating nuclear level densities is investigated. The single-nucleon spectra are calculated in a relativistic mean-field model with energy-dependent effective mass, which yields a realistic density of single-particle states at the Fermi energy. These microscopic single-nucleon states are used in a fast combinatorial algorithm for calculating the non-collective excitations of nuclei. The method, when applied to magic and semi-magic nuclei, such as $^{60}$Ni, $^{114}$Sn and $^{208}$Pb, reproduces the cumulative number of experimental states at low excitation energy, as well as the s-wave neutron resonance spacing at the neutron binding energy. Experimental level densities above 10 MeV are reproduced by multiplying the non-collective level densities by a simple vibrational enhancement factor. Problems to be solved in the extension to open-shell nuclei are discussed

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a combinatorial level density calculation based on single-nucleon spectra from a relativistic mean-field model with an energy-dependent effective mass can reproduce experimental cumulative state counts and s-wave neutron resonance spacings for magic and semi-magic nuclei, but only if a simple vibrational enhancement factor is multiplied into the non-collective level densities to match data above 10 MeV excitation energy.

## Method

Single-nucleon spectra were computed using a relativistic mean-field (RMF) model with an energy-dependent effective nucleon mass, which yields a realistic density of single-particle states near the Fermi energy. These microscopic single-nucleon states were then used as input to a fast combinatorial algorithm based on Gaussian polynomial expansions of generating functions, implemented in the SPINDIS code, which generates the full many-body state space for non-collective excitations with angular momentum, seniority, energy, and parity as good quantum numbers. For comparison with experimental data above 10 MeV, the non-collective level densities were multiplied by a vibrational enhancement factor derived from a grand-canonical treatment of quadrupole and octupole phonons with temperature-dependent damping.

## Key numbers

- For $^{208}$Pb, the total state density at approximately 50 MeV excitation energy is $\omega \simeq e^{46} \text{ MeV}^{-1}$.
- The density-averaged effective mass from the model ranges from $1.12m$ at $A=40$ to $1.13m$ at $A=210$, consistent with an empirical average $\langle m^*(E_F) \rangle / m = 1.09 \pm 0.13$ from neutron resonance spacing analysis.
- The lowest quadrupole vibrational mode energy is $E(2^+) \simeq 30A^{-2/3}$ MeV, and the lowest octupole mode energy is $E(3^-) \simeq 50A^{-2/3}$ MeV.
- The damping factor for phonons is parameterized as $\gamma_i(T) \simeq 0.0075 A^{1/3} [(\omega_i)^2 + 4\pi^2 T^2]$ MeV.
- The level density parameter $a = \frac{\pi^2}{6} g(E_F)$ decreases from an average of $A/8$ MeV$^{-1}$ at $T \le 1$ MeV to $A/11$ MeV$^{-1}$ at $T \simeq 2.5$ MeV in the mass region $A \simeq 160$.

## Context

This work extends the combinatorial level density approach of Sunko (1997) and the relativistic mean-field model with energy-dependent effective mass of Vretenar (2002) to the calculation of nuclear level densities, building on earlier combinatorial methods by Hilaire, Delaroche and Girod (2001) who used Gogny interaction single-particle states. The method is limited to magic and semi-magic nuclei (applied to $^{60}$Ni, $^{114}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb) and requires a vibrational enhancement factor to match experimental data above 10 MeV; the authors note that extension to open-shell nuclei will require solving problems related to pairing correlations and rotational enhancement, and that a proper treatment of boson-fermion coupling is needed for reliable spin-parity distributions.
