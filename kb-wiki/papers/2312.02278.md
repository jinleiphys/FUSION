# Learning PDFs through Interpretable Latent Representations in Mellin Space

> Representing the parton distribution functions (PDFs) of the proton and other hadrons through flexible, high-fidelity parametrizations has been a long-standing goal of particle physics phenomenology. This is particularly true since the chosen parametrization methodology can play an influential role in the ultimate PDF uncertainties as extracted in QCD global analyses; these, in turn, are often determinative of the reach of experiments at the LHC and other facilities to non-standard physics, including at large $x$, where parametrization effects can be significant. In this study, we explore a series of encoder-decoder machine-learning (ML) models with various neural-network topologies as efficient means of reconstructing PDFs from meaningful information stored in an interpretable latent space. Given recent effort to pioneer synergies between QCD analyses and lattice-gauge calculations, we formulate a latent representation based on the behavior of PDFs in Mellin space, i.e., their integrated moments, and test the ability of various models to decode PDFs from this information faithfully. We introduce a numerical package, $\texttt{PDFdecoder}$, which implements several encoder-decoder models to reconstruct PDFs with high fidelity and use this tool to explore strengths and pitfalls of neural-network approaches to PDF parametrization. We additionally dissect patterns of learned correlations between encoded Mellin moments and reconstructed PDFs which suggest opportunities for further improvements to ML-based approaches to PDF parametrizations and uncertainty quantification.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that encoder-decoder neural networks can reconstruct parton distribution functions (PDFs) from their Mellin-space moments with high fidelity, but only when the latent space is explicitly constrained to represent these moments; the authors hedge that this is a toy-model demonstration using flavor-SU(2) PDF combinations, and that full PDF reconstruction would require an infinite tower of moments, whereas only the lowest few moments are practically accessible from lattice QCD.

## Method

The authors constructed five encoder-decoder neural network architectures (AE, AE-CL, AE-WC, VAE, and VAIM) and trained them to reconstruct four PDF combinations [u±ū](x) and [d±d̄](x) from logarithmically sampled x values between 10⁻² and 0.999. The latent spaces of the AE-CL, AE-WC, and VAIM models were explicitly constrained to match known Mellin moments of the PDFs, with the encoder compressing the 196-dimensional input through five fully connected hidden layers using ELU activation functions down to either a 32-dimensional or 8-dimensional latent vector. The authors introduced the PDFdecoder numerical package implementing these architectures and assessed reconstruction fidelity using mean-squared error loss.

## Key numbers

- Input PDFs were sampled at 196 logarithmically spaced points from x_min = 10⁻² to x_max = 0.999
- The baseline latent dimensionality was 32 (8 dimensions per PDF combination), with a more compressed 8-dimensional latent (2 dimensions per PDF combination) also tested
- The encoder network used five fully connected hidden layers, each stepping down the number of nodes by a factor of 2
- Lattice QCD can reliably calculate the lowest few Mellin moments, specifically n ≤ 3, beyond which computational artifacts degrade signal-to-noise

## Context

This work extends recent efforts to synergize QCD global analyses with lattice-gauge calculations (Hobbs 2019, Bringewatt 2020, Hou 2022), specifically building on the Mellin-moment approach to PDF determination (Detmold 2001). The study also builds upon prior work using autoencoders for inverse problems in QCD global analyses (Almaeen 2022) and for learning associations between PDFs and their log-likelihood functions (Liu 2022). The results enable future development of ML-based PDF parametrizations that can directly incorporate lattice-calculable Mellin moments as physics-motivated constraints, potentially improving uncertainty quantification in global fits.
