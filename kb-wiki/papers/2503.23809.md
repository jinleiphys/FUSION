# Nuclear clustering process in heavy-ion collisions : experimental constraints on the low-temperature region of the QCD phase diagram

> In this article, we study the production of Hydrogen and Helium isotopes in heavy-ion collisions in the incident energy range between 80 and 150 MeV/nucleon. We compare their inclusive multiplicities emitted in the transverse plane of the reaction with the predictions given by the thermal model. As a first step, we validate the choice of this approach to describe the experimental measurements. We also show that the transient states have to be explicitly taken into account for a good statistical description of the experimental multiplicities. From the thermodynamical parameter values obtained we complete the existing database built with the use of thermal-statistical models to reproduce particle production in the (ultra-)relativistic-energy measurements. We then proposed a new constraint on the so-called freeze-out region in the temperature (T) versus baryonic chemical potential (muB) phase diagram of the quantum chromodynamics. These new results indicate that there is a common framework to describe the hadron production and nuclear clustering processes in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors show that a thermal-statistical model, when explicitly including transient states (resonances and excited states), can reproduce hydrogen and helium isotope multiplicities from heavy-ion collisions at 80-150 MeV/nucleon, and that the resulting thermodynamic parameters provide a new constraint on the freeze-out line in the QCD phase diagram at high baryonic chemical potential (above 800 MeV), where no data previously existed.

## Method

The authors measured inclusive multiplicities of hydrogen (1,2,3H) and helium (3,4,6He) isotopes emitted in the transverse plane of 12 reactions (Ni+Ni, Xe+Sn, Au+Au) at beam energies between 80 and 150 MeV/nucleon using the INDRA 4π detector. They performed a three-parameter fit (temperature T, baryonic chemical potential μB, and electric chemical potential μQ) of a grand-canonical thermal model to the experimental multiplicities, comparing three cases: no transient states, only resonances included, and both resonances and excited states included. The fit used the nucleon-sharing reproduction (NSR) estimator and the MINUIT minimization package.

## Key numbers

- Incident energy range: 80 to 150 MeV/nucleon for all three systems (Ni+Ni, Xe+Sn, Au+Au)
- NSR estimator above 97% for cases 2 and 3 (with transient states) for all reactions; NSR around 95% for Ni+Ni and dropping to 90% for Xe+Sn and Au+Au for case 1 (no transient states)
- Percentage of nucleons redistributed by transient states: 21-28% for case 2 (resonances only), 35-45% for case 3 (resonances and excited states)
- Temperature values from fits: range 5.3 ± 0.2 MeV to 7.9 ± 0.3 MeV across all reactions and cases
- Baryonic chemical potential values: range 925.7 ± 0.6 MeV to 929.4 ± 0.3 MeV across all reactions and cases
- Baryonic density values: range 0.006 ± 0.002 nuc.fm⁻³ to 0.018 ± 0.005 nuc.fm⁻³ across all reactions and cases
- Systematic temperature shift when including excited states: ΔT ≈ -0.87 MeV
- New freeze-out line parameters: a = 0.167 ± 0.001 GeV, b = 0.135 ± 0.007 GeV⁻¹, c = 0.060 ± 0.007 GeV⁻³ (compared to previous a = 0.166 ± 0.002 GeV, b = 0.139 ± 0.016 GeV⁻¹, c = 0.053 ± 0.021 GeV⁻³)
- Parameter uncertainties reduced by a factor of 2 to 3 compared to previous systematics

## Context

This work extends the freeze-out line systematics established by Cleymans et al. (2006) for relativistic heavy-ion collisions (μB ≤ 800 MeV) into the low-temperature, high-μB region using data from the Fermi energy regime. The results confirm the need to include transient states in thermal model comparisons, as previously noted by Vovchenko et al. (2020). The new constraints on the freeze-out region at μB > 800 MeV are valuable for microscopic approaches aiming to follow the T(μB) parameterization, such as those discussed by Blaschke et al. (2025). The extracted baryonic densities (one order of magnitude below normal nuclear density) are qualitatively compatible with cluster analyses at Fermi energies by Kowalski et al. (2007), Qin et al. (2012), Mabiala et al. (2014), and Bougault et al. (2020), though the present work uses a more complete set of accessible states.
