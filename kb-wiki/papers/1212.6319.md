# An artificial neural network application on nuclear charge radii

> The artificial neural networks (ANNs) have emerged with successful applications in nuclear physics as well as in many fields of science in recent years. In this paper, by using (ANNs), we have constructed a formula for the nuclear charge radii. Statistical modeling of nuclear charge radii by using ANNs has been seen as to be successful. Also, the charge radii, binding energies and two-neutron separation energies of Sn isotopes have been calculated by implementing of the new formula in Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations. The results of the study shows that the new formula is useful for describing nuclear charge radii.

## Key claim

The authors claim that an artificial neural network (ANN) trained on approximately 900 nuclei can produce a simple mass-dependent formula for nuclear charge radii that is useful for describing those radii, but the formula's success is qualified by the fact that the ANN was tested only on a held-out subset of the same experimental data and the resulting formula was then applied specifically to Sn isotopes within Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations.

## Method

The authors used a feed-forward artificial neural network with one input neuron (atomic mass), one hidden layer of 44 neurons, and one output neuron (nuclear charge radius), trained via back-propagation with the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm on experimental nuclear masses and charge radii of approximately 900 nuclei. After training, they performed a least-squares fit to the ANN outputs to obtain a mass-dependent formula of the form \(R_c = r_0 A^{\beta}\). They then implemented this formula in HFB calculations with the Skyrme force SLy4 to compute ground-state properties (binding energies per nucleon, two-neutron separation energies, and charge radii) for even-even \(^{100-144}\)Sn isotopes.

## Key numbers

- The ANN achieved mean square error (MSE) values of 0.0027 for the training stage and 0.0029 for the test stage.
- The fitted charge radius formula is \(R_c = 1.234 A^{0.28}\), where \(R_c\) is the root-mean-square charge radius in femtometers and \(A\) is the mass number.
- The root mean square deviation between charge radii from the formula and experimental values is \(\sigma \approx 1.4 \times 10^{-2}\) fm.
- For Sn isotopes, the maximum difference between calculated and experimental binding energies per nucleon is approximately 0.04 MeV at neutron number \(N=50\).
- For Sn isotopes, the maximum difference between calculated and experimental charge radii is 0.032 fm at neutron number \(N=82\).

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to derive nuclear charge radius formulas based on \(A^{1/3}\) and \(Z^{1/3}\) dependence, as reviewed by Zhang (2002), by applying artificial neural networks as a nonlinear function approximator to the updated experimental dataset of Angeli (2009). The resulting formula was then used within HFB calculations with the SLy4 Skyrme force, following the method of Stoitsov et al. (2005), to predict ground-state properties of Sn isotopes. This enables future application of ANN-derived formulas to other isotopic chains and to nuclei far from stability, particularly those exhibiting skin and halo phenomena.
