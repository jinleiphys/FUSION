# Relativistic hydrodynamics with momentum dependent relaxation time

> A second order relativistic hydrodynamic theory has been derived using momentum dependent relaxation time in the relativistic transport equation. In order to do that, an iterative technique of gradient expansion approach, namely Chapman-Enskog (CE) expansion of the particle distribution function has been employed. The key findings of this work are, (i) momentum dependent relaxation time in collision term results in an extended Landau matching condition for the thermodynamic variables, (ii) the result from numerical solution of Boltzmann equation lies somewhere in between the two popular extreme limits : linear and quadratic ansatz, indicating a fractional power of momentum dependence in relaxation time to be appropriate, (ii) an equivalence has been established between the iterative gradient expansion method like CE and the well known moment approach like Grad's 14-moment method.

# Relativistic hydrodynamics with momentum dependent relaxation time

## Key claim
The paper derives a second-order relativistic hydrodynamic theory using a momentum-dependent relaxation time in the Boltzmann transport equation via the Chapman-Enskog expansion, finding that the numerical solution of the Boltzmann equation lies between the linear (n=0) and quadratic (n=1) ansatz limits, indicating that a fractional power of momentum dependence (n=1/2) is appropriate for describing quark-gluon plasma dynamics. The work also establishes an equivalence between the Chapman-Enskog iterative gradient expansion method and Grad's 14-moment method for specific choices of the momentum power parameter.

## Method
The author employs the Chapman-Enskog (CE) iterative gradient expansion of the particle distribution function in the relativistic Boltzmann transport equation with a collision term expressed in the relaxation time approximation, where the relaxation time is taken as a power law of the scaled particle energy: τ_R(x,p) = τ_R^0(x) τ_p^n, with τ_p = p·u/T. The first-order and second-order corrections to the distribution function are derived, and the resulting shear stress evolution equation is obtained. The hydrodynamic equations are solved for a boost-invariant Bjorken flow of a massless Boltzmann gas with ultrarelativistic equation of state (ε=3P), and the pressure anisotropy is compared with numerical solutions from the BAMPS parton cascade model.

## Key numbers
- For linear ansatz (n=0): τ_π = 5η/(4P) and λ = 10/7
- For quadratic ansatz (n=1): τ_π = 3η/(2P) and λ = 2
- For fractional power n=1/2: τ_π = 2.3 τ_R^0 = 1.31η/P and λ = 12/7
- The relaxation time of the shear viscous field τ_π = τ_R^0 × (2n+4)!/(n+4)!; for n=0, τ_π = τ_R^0; for n=1, τ_π = 6τ_R^0
- The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio relates to τ_R^0 as: τ_R^0 = (η/s)/T × 5!/(n+4)!
- Initial conditions for Bjorken flow: τ_i = 0.4 fm, T_i = 0.5 GeV, initial viscous pressure π_i = 0
- The n=1/2 case agrees with BAMPS data even for large viscosity η/s = 3.0 throughout the evolution range

## Context
This work extends the relativistic hydrodynamic formalism of Jaiswal (2013) and Denicol (2014) by incorporating momentum-dependent relaxation time, which addresses the limitation that previous relaxation time approximations assumed momentum-independent relaxation, ignoring microscopic momentum anisotropies. The fractional power n=1/2 is motivated by Dusling (2009), who showed that in the context of radiative energy loss, the momentum dependence of τ_R for a two-flavor quark-gluon gas lies between the linear and quadratic ansatz limits. The extended Landau matching condition resolves thermodynamic stability issues raised by Monnai (2009) and Osada (2011, 2012), and the equivalence between CE and Grad's 14-moment method connects the work of Denicol (2014) (DNMR theory, corresponding to n=0, r=-1) with the ultrarelativistic Grad's 14-moment results (n=1, r=0). This enables more accurate second-order hydrodynamic descriptions of quark-gluon plasma at RHIC and LHC without requiring third-order theories as attempted by Jaiswal (2013) and El (2009).
