# David Maurice Brink, 20 July 1930 - 8 March 2021

> David Brink was one of the leading theoretical nuclear physicists of his generation. He made major contributions to the study of all aspects of nuclear physics embracing nuclear structure, nuclear scattering, and nuclear instability. His wide ranging interests and interactions with theorists and experimentalists alike helped him in providing both theoretical analysis and interpretations and suggesting experiments. He had the gift of visualising complex problems in simple terms and provided clear analysis of the underlying processes. He was an expert on the use of semi-classical methods which provided an intuitively clear picture of complex phenomena. His research work and books are characterised by scientific clarity, transparency, and depth. David possessed outstanding skills in mathematical computation, and he was an expert on special functions, group theory, and the Feynman path integral method. David had many research students and collaborated with a large number of scientists from across the world, for whom he was a source of scientific and human inspiration and admiration. His most fundamental belief was that research was a means of trying to discover and understand the beauties of Nature and explain them in simple terms to others. His absolute belief in the value of truth and his unselfish and generous attitude in sharing knowledge makes him an outstanding figure in contemporary Nuclear Physics.

# David Maurice Brink, 20 July 1930 - 8 March 2021

## Key claim
This biographical memoir presents David Brink as one of the leading theoretical nuclear physicists of his generation, whose most fundamental belief was that research was a means of discovering and explaining the beauties of Nature in simple terms. The paper emphasizes that his contributions spanned nuclear structure, scattering, and instability, and that his use of semi-classical methods provided intuitively clear pictures of complex phenomena.

## Method
The paper is a biographical review of David Brink's life, career, and scientific contributions, drawing on personal recollections from colleagues and students, descriptions of his doctoral thesis and subsequent publications, and accounts of his teaching and administrative roles at Oxford University, the European Centre for Theoretical Studies in Nuclear Physics and Related Areas (ECT*) in Trento, and other institutions. It traces the development of his key ideas from his 1955 D.Phil. thesis through his work on effective interactions, the Skyrme force, semi-classical reaction theory, path integral methods, and cluster models.

## Key numbers
- Brink's D.Phil. thesis "Some Aspects of the Interactions of Fields with Matter" was submitted in May 1955.
- The Vautherin-Brink paper on Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations for spherical nuclei has received over 2000 citations, as noted by T. Otsuka.
- The Brink-Axel hypothesis, first stated on page 101 of Brink's thesis and expressed in equation (11) of Axel's 1962 paper, relates gamma decay rates to absorption cross sections on excited states.
- Experimental confirmation of the Brink-Axel hypothesis first came from Berkeley experiments in 1981.
- The Brink-Boeker effective nucleon-nucleon interaction proposed a gaussian form that "all nuclear physicists have used at least once in their lives."
- David Brink was born on 20 July 1930 in Hobart, Tasmania, and died on 8 March 2021.

## Context
This biographical memoir extends the historical record of major figures in 20th-century nuclear theory, following similar accounts of contemporaries such as Rudolf Peierls, Tony Skyrme, and J.P. Elliott. It documents Brink's foundational contributions to the Skyrme energy-density functional approach (developed with Vautherin in the early 1970s), the Brink-Axel hypothesis for giant dipole resonances, and semi-classical methods for heavy-ion reactions. The paper enables future historians to trace the development of nuclear theory from the shell model through density-functional theory to modern treatments of exotic nuclei and halo systems, and it highlights open questions about the recognition of Brink's early work on compound-nucleus width fluctuations, which was not published by him but later attributed to other authors.
