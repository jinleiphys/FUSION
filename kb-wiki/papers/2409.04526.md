# Constraining the nuclear equation of state from orbits of primordial black holes inside neutron stars

> Lacking terrestrial experimental data, our best constraints on the behavior of matter at high densities up to and above nuclear density arise from observations of neutron stars. Current constraints include those based on measurements of stellar masses, radii, and tidal deformabilities. Here we explore how orbits of primordial black holes - should they exist - inside neutron stars could provide complementary constraints on the nuclear equation of state (EOS). Specifically, we consider a sample of candidate EOSs, construct neutron star models for these EOSs, and compute orbits of primordial black holes inside these stars. We discuss how the pericenter advance of eccentric orbits, i.e. orbital precession, results in beat phenomena in the emitted gravitational wave signal. Observing this beat frequency could constrain the nuclear EOS and break possible degeneracies arising from other constraints, as well as provide information about the host star.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that if primordial black holes (PBHs) exist and orbit inside neutron stars, the gravitational wave beat frequency resulting from orbital precession of eccentric orbits could provide complementary constraints on the nuclear equation of state (EOS), potentially breaking degeneracies that arise from existing mass, radius, and tidal deformability measurements, but only if future gravitational wave detectors become sensitive enough to detect such signals.

## Method

The authors selected a sample of 24 candidate nuclear EOSs from existing catalogues, approximated them using a generalized piecewise polytrope approach, and constructed nonrotating neutron star models by solving the Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. They then computed relativistic geodesic orbits of PBHs inside these stellar models, starting with zero initial radial velocity and reducing the circular orbital frequency by a factor of 0.95 to produce eccentric orbits, and estimated the gravitational wave signal using a Newtonian quadrupole formalism.

## Key numbers

- The pulsar J0740+6620 has a mass of M = 2.08 ± 0.07 solar masses (68.3% credibility limits), used as the maximum mass constraint.
- The radius of J0030+0451 (mass 1.44 solar masses) is measured as 12.33 +0.76/-0.81 km (95% credibility limits) from NICER observations.
- The dimensionless tidal deformability from GW170817 is Λ = 300 +420/-230 (95% confidence limits).
- For a hypothetical GW observation with angular frequency ω = 1.3 × 10^4 rad/s, the beat-to-orbital period ratio N_GW ranges from 1.884 (MPA1, 2.0 solar masses) to 127.3 (SKMP, 2.0 solar masses) for nearly circular orbits.
- The maximum circular orbital frequency ω_max for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star ranges from 9.335 rad/s (MS1) to 14.68 rad/s (WFF1).
- The maximum allowed gravitational mass for the EOS sample ranges from 1.778 solar masses (FPS) to 2.746 solar masses (MS1b).

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Baumgarte and Shapiro (2024) that used a simple polytropic EOS, by applying the same PBH orbital precession analysis to a comprehensive sample of realistic nuclear EOSs from the catalogues assembled by Abbott et al. (2020) and the CompOSE and Oezel EOS projects. The approach complements existing constraints from mass measurements (pulsar J0740+6620), NICER radius measurements (J0030+0451 and J0740+6620), and tidal deformability from GW170817, and could help break degeneracies between EOSs that produce similar predictions for these observables. The paper notes that event rates for PBH-NS collisions are estimated to be small, so detection would likely require more sensitive gravitational wave detectors than currently available.
