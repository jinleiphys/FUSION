# Get on the BAND Wagon: A Bayesian Framework for Quantifying Model Uncertainties in Nuclear Dynamics

> We describe the Bayesian Analysis of Nuclear Dynamics (BAND) framework, a cyberinfrastructure that we are developing which will unify the treatment of nuclear models, experimental data, and associated uncertainties. We overview the statistical principles and nuclear-physics contexts underlying the BAND toolset, with an emphasis on Bayesian methodology's ability to leverage insight from multiple models. In order to facilitate understanding of these tools we provide a simple and accessible example of the BAND framework's application. Four case studies are presented to highlight how elements of the framework will enable progress on complex, far-ranging problems in nuclear physics. By collecting notation and terminology, providing illustrative examples, and giving an overview of the associated techniques, this paper aims to open paths through which the nuclear physics and statistics communities can contribute to and build upon the BAND framework.

## Key claim

The paper describes the BAND (Bayesian Analysis of Nuclear Dynamics) framework as a cyberinfrastructure under development that will unify nuclear models, experimental data, and uncertainties through Bayesian methodology, with the explicit hedge that the framework is not yet complete but rather a proposed set of tools that will be built, and that its primary novel contribution is advocating for Bayesian Model Mixing (BMM) over Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) for nuclear physics applications.

## Method

The authors outline a Bayesian framework with four computational tools: model emulation (Tool A) to create computationally cheap surrogates for expensive nuclear models, model calibration (Tool B) to obtain posterior probability distributions for model parameters, model mixing (Tool C) to combine multiple calibrated models into unified predictions, and experimental design (Tool D) to optimize selection of future measurements. They also specify two input tools for prior specification and likelihood formulation, and present four case studies from nuclear physics (equation of state, reactions, masses, and transfer reactions) where elements of the framework have been partially implemented.

## Key numbers

* Approximately 3,000 nuclei are involved in the astrophysical rapid neutron capture (r-)process that FRIB aims to study, with FRIB unable to measure all of them
* The paper references "tonne-scale experiments" searching for neutrinoless double-beta decay as a potential application of the framework
* Four case studies are presented to highlight elements of the framework: equation of state, reactions, masses, and Bayesian Model Averaging for transfer reactions

## Context

This work extends the growing practice of Bayesian uncertainty quantification in nuclear physics, building on prior efforts in model calibration and emulation described in references such as Sacks (1989), Santner (2019), and Gramacy (2020). The framework aims to enable reliable predictions for experimentally inaccessible environments like neutron star cores and early universe conditions, and to optimize experimental programs at facilities like FRIB. The paper specifically critiques Bayesian Model Averaging (BMA) as having "important shortcomings which could be damaging in the nuclear-physics context" and advocates instead for the more general Bayesian Model Mixing (BMM) approach, referencing Tebaldi (2007) and Smith (2009) for BMM methodology.
