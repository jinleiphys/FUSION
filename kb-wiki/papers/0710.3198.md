# Equation of State and Transport Coefficients of Relativistic Nuclear Matter

> In order to evaluate qualitatively the space-time evolution of hot and dense nuclear matter the underlying equation of state and transport coefficients must be known. In this study a specific equation of state is studied: the pion gas. The classical or standard transport coefficients, namely the bulk viscosity, shear viscsoity and thermal conductivity are devided by the relaxation times for the corresponding dissipative fluxes and then studied as functions of mass to temperature ratio.

## Key claim

The ratios of the standard transport coefficients (bulk viscosity, shear viscosity, and thermal conductivity) to their corresponding relaxation times are governed by the equation of state, meaning that for consistency the same model that provides the equation of state should also yield the transport coefficients. This claim is demonstrated specifically for a pure pion gas system, not for general nuclear matter.

## Method

The author calculates the energy density, number density, and pressure for a resonance gas of pions using relativistic Bose-Einstein statistics with zero chemical potential. The ratios of the three standard transport coefficients (bulk viscosity ζ, shear viscosity η, and thermal conductivity κ) to their associated relaxation times (τ_Π, τ_π, and τ_q respectively) are then expressed in terms of second-order coefficients β_0, β_1, and β_2, which are derived from the equation of state. These ratios are studied as functions of the temperature parameter z = m/T, where m is the pion mass and T is the temperature.

## Key numbers

- The bulk viscosity of pionic matter is very small; in the figure it is magnified one thousand times to compare its temperature dependence with the other two coefficients.
- The thermal conductivity times temperature (λ = κT) is comparable to the shear viscosity for pure pionic matter.
- The ratios λ/τ_q, ζ/τ_Π, and η/τ_π are inversely proportional to the β_A coefficients and directly proportional to the pressure, thus increasing with temperature.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic dissipative fluid dynamics framework developed by Muronga (2004, 2007) and others (Heinz, Romatschke, Koide) by explicitly connecting transport coefficients to the equation of state for a pion gas. The study is relevant for modeling the space-time evolution of matter created in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, as well as for neutron star physics where transport processes affect burning and diffusion as discussed by Heiselberg and Prakash. The approach enables future work on multi-component mixtures (e.g., pion-kaon or quark-gluon systems) where diffusion coefficients become important, as noted by Muronga (2004) and Sasaki.
