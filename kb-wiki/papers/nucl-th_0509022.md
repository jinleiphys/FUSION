# Superscaling and Charge-changing Neutrino Cross Sections

> 3 pages, 2 figures, to appear in the Proceedings of the 7th International Workshop on Neutrino Factories and Superbeams, Laboratori Nazionali di Frascati, Frascati (Rome), June 21 - 26, 2005

## Key claim

The superscaling function extracted from inclusive electron scattering data can be used to predict charge-changing neutrino cross sections in the quasi-elastic and Delta regions, but the predictions are unreliable for excitations above the Delta peak where meson production and deep inelastic scattering processes are not fully accounted for.

## Method

The authors extracted a universal quasi-elastic superscaling function from experimental longitudinal electron scattering responses for several nuclei, and a Delta-region superscaling function by subtracting the quasi-elastic contribution (recalculated using the universal function) from total cross sections for carbon-12 and oxygen-16. These empirical superscaling functions were then multiplied by the corresponding charge-changing weak interaction single-nucleon cross sections (N to N and N to Delta) to predict neutrino-nucleus cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The empirical quasi-elastic superscaling function reaches a maximum value of about 0.6, compared to the relativistic Fermi gas model value of 3/4.
- The electron scattering data used span energies from 300 MeV to 4 GeV and scattering angles from 12 to 145 degrees.
- Deviations between the scaling-based predictions and inclusive electron scattering data are at most 10-20 percent, attributed to meson exchange currents and correlations.
- Scaling of the second kind in the resonance region is violated at roughly the 20 percent level.

## Context

This work extends the superscaling analysis of electron-nucleus scattering developed in earlier studies (Scaling1, Scaling2) to predict neutrino-nucleus cross sections needed for neutrino oscillation experiments at GeV energies. The approach contrasts with relativistic Fermi gas models and mean-field theories, which the authors note differ significantly from the scaling predictions. The method enables more robust predictions for neutrino reactions than existing nuclear models, though it does not fully account for meson production, higher resonances, or deep inelastic scattering processes.
