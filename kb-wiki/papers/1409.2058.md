# Comment on chiral symmetry restoration at finite density in large-$N_c$ QCD

> In the article "On chiral symmetry restoration at finite density in large-$N_c$ QCD" by Adhikari, Cohen, Ayyagari and Strother [Phys. Rev. C 83, 065201 (2011)] the description of dense nuclear matter by means of Skyrmions in hyperspherical unit cells is severely criticized. We point out that this criticism is based on invalid assumptions and therefore unwarranted.

## Key claim

The criticism by Adhikari et al. (2011) of the hypersphere (S^3) approach to dense matter in Skyrme models is based on invalid assumptions and is therefore unwarranted; the S^3 geometry is not an arbitrary choice but is uniquely dictated by its chiral symmetry group SO(4) ≃ SU(2) × SU(2), which made the approach promising in the first place.

## Method

The paper identifies and refutes three specific assumptions underlying the criticism of Adhikari et al. (2011): that the hypersphere approach was intended to approximate a Skyrmion in a crystal, that the cell shape is insignificant and motivated only by computational convenience, and that using a hypersphere to restrict the Skyrmion volume acts generically like other volume restrictions. The author argues that these assumptions contradict the unique symmetry properties of the S^3 geometry documented in the pioneering papers by Manton (1986) and Forkel (1989), and that the qualitative differences between spatial averaging in flat space and on S^3 prevent any conflict with general large-N_c QCD arguments.

## Key numbers

- No original numerical results are presented; this is a comment paper that refutes methodological criticisms without introducing new calculations or numerical data.

## Context

This comment defends the hypersphere (S^3) approach to dense nuclear matter, which was pioneered by Manton (1986) and Forkel (1989) and criticized by Adhikari, Cohen, Ayyagari, and Strother (2011). The S^3 approach models aspects of dense matter, particularly the transition to chiral restoration, by placing Skyrmions in cells with the geometry of a three-dimensional sphere. The author notes that even in the quark-based Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model (Nambu, 1961), curvature-induced interactions in S^3 cells (rather than just reduced volume) generate a transition to a chirally restored phase (Forkel, 1992). The paper enables continued use of the hypersphere approach for studying chiral symmetry restoration at finite density in large-N_c QCD, and suggests that S^3 cells may capture chiral features more completely than the crystal approach, at least for finite N_c.
