# Analytical coalescence formula for particle production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> Based on a covariant coalescence model with a blast-wave-like parametrization for the phase-space configuration of constituent particles at freeze-out, we derive an approximate analytical formula for the yields of clusters produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. Compared to previous existing formulae, the present work additionally considers the contributions from the longitudinal dimension in momentum space, the relativistic corrections and the finite size effects of the produced clusters relative to the spatial distribution of constituent particles at freeze-out. The new analytical coalescence formula provides a useful tool to evaluate the yield of produced clusters, such as light nuclei from nucleon coalescence and hadrons from quark coalescence, in heavy-ion collisions. As a first application of the new analytical formula, we explore the strangeness population factor $S_3 = ^3_Λ$H/($^3$He$\times Λ/$p) based on nucleon/$Λ$ coalescence as well as the production of exotic hadrons based on quark coalescence, in central Pb+Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=2.76$ TeV. The results are compared with the predictions from other models.

# Analytical coalescence formula for particle production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The authors derive a new analytical coalescence formula (COAL-SH) that includes contributions from the longitudinal dimension in momentum space, relativistic corrections, and finite size effects of produced clusters, which previous formulae neglected. The new formula saturates to a constant yield for large cluster root-mean-square radii, unlike the earlier COAL-Ex formula which predicts yields that grow as a power of the radius.

## Method

Starting from a covariant coalescence model with a blast-wave-like parametrization for the phase-space configuration of constituent particles at freeze-out, the authors perform a Jacobi transformation to separate center-of-mass and relative coordinates, adopt a harmonic oscillator assumption for the cluster internal wave function, and integrate the Wigner function over full three-dimensional phase space. They incorporate relativistic corrections to leading order from the one-particle momentum distribution and propose an empirical expression for finite size corrections when the cluster radius is comparable to the fireball radius. The resulting analytical formula is applied to compute the strangeness population factor S3 and yields of exotic hadrons in central Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV.

## Key numbers

- The strangeness population factor S3 measured by ALICE for central (0-10% centrality) Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV is 0.60 ± 0.13(stat.) ± 0.21(syst.)
- The COAL-SH formula predicts S3 = 0.54 to 0.57, consistent with the measured value, while the COAL-Ex formula predicts S3 larger than about 7
- The harmonic oscillator frequencies used are: w = 0.184 GeV for light quarks, w_s = 0.078 GeV for strange quarks, and w_c = 0.087 GeV for charm quarks
- The RMS radius of hypertriton (^3_Lambda H) is 4.9 fm, and of ^3He is 1.76 fm
- The fireball radius R at freeze-out in central Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV is about 19.7 fm
- The ratio g_size(^3_Lambda H)/g_size(^3He) = 0.60 from the empirical finite size correction formula
- The freeze-out temperature T is set to 0.154 GeV for the quark coalescence calculations
- The fireball volume V for COAL-SH is 13.8 × 10^3 fm^3; for COAL-Ex it is 6.06 × 10^3 fm^3
- The constituent quark masses used are: m_u,d = 0.3 GeV, m_s = 0.5 GeV, m_c = 1.5 GeV

## Context

This work extends the analytical coalescence formulae developed by the ExHIC collaboration (Cho et al., 2011) by including longitudinal momentum integration, relativistic corrections, and finite size effects that were neglected in the earlier COAL-Ex formula. The new COAL-SH formula resolves a problematic feature of COAL-Ex where cluster yields increase as a power of the RMS radius, instead saturating to a constant for large clusters. The results are compared with predictions from the thermal (statistical) model (Andronic et al., 2011) and with experimental data from ALICE. The formula enables more reliable predictions for loosely bound clusters like hypertriton and for exotic hadron production from quark coalescence in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC energies.
