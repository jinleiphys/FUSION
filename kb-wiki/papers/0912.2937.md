# Applications of the Similarity Renormalization Group to the Nuclear Interaction

> The Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) is investigated as a powerful yet practical method to modify nuclear potentials so as to reduce computational requirements for calculations of observables. The key feature of SRG transformations that leads to computational benefits is the decoupling of low-energy nuclear physics from high-energy details of the inter-nucleon interaction. We examine decoupling quantitatively for two-body observables and few-body binding energies. The universal nature of this decoupling is illustrated and errors from suppressing high-momentum modes above the decoupling scale are shown to be perturbatively small. To explore the SRG evolution of many-body forces, we use as a laboratory a one-dimensional system of bosons with short-range repulsion and mid-range attraction, which emulates realistic nuclear forces. The free-space SRG is implemented for few-body systems in a symmetrized harmonic oscillator basis using a recursive construction analogous to no-core shell model implementations. Building on one-dimensional results we performed the first practical evolution of three-dimensional many-body forces within the No-Core Shell Model basis. Results for the 3H binding energy are consistent with previous calculations involving momentum-space evolution of only two-body forces, and validate expectations from calculations in the one-dimensional oscillator basis. When applied to 4He calculations, the two- and three-body oscillator matrix elements yield rapid convergence of the ground-state energy with a small net contribution of the induced four-body force. The radius of light nuclei is also explored in the three-dimensional basis.

## Key claim

The Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) provides a practical method to modify nuclear potentials that decouples low-energy nuclear physics from high-energy details of the inter-nucleon interaction, but the decoupling and convergence benefits depend on the choice of SRG generator and basis, with the harmonic oscillator basis not being optimal for long-ranged observables like nuclear radii.

## Method

The SRG evolution was implemented in a symmetrized Jacobi coordinate harmonic oscillator basis using a recursive construction analogous to No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) implementations. The Hamiltonian was evolved in the A=2 space to fix two-body matrix elements, then in the A=3 space to determine combined two-plus-three-body matrix elements, with the three-body matrix elements isolated by subtracting evolved two-body elements embedded in the A=3 basis. The initial NN potential was the 500 MeV N3LO interaction, and the initial NNN potential was the N2LO interaction with LEC constants c_D = -0.205 and c_E = 0.029.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of 3H with initial interactions: -8.473(4) MeV (compared with experimental -8.482 MeV)
- Ground-state energy of 4He with initial interactions: -28.50(2) MeV (compared with experimental -28.296 MeV), showing a 200 keV discrepancy with experiment
- Induced four-body forces contribute about 50 keV net at lambda = 2 fm^-1 to the 4He binding energy
- SRG-evolved asymptotic values for 4He at different harmonic oscillator parameters differ by less than 10 keV
- Induced four-body forces for 4He are about 30-60 keV, less than the 200 keV discrepancy with experiment attributed to omission of initial four-nucleon forces
- For 3H calculations with Nmax = 36 and hw = 28 MeV, all energies converged to better than 10 keV
- The N3LO NN-only point with initial NNN zero shows a short trajectory when including induced NNN interaction for lambda >= 1.8 fm^-1

## Context

This work extends the SRG approach from momentum-space implementations (Bogner 2006) to practical evolution of three-dimensional many-body forces within the NCSM basis developed by Navratil and collaborators. The results validate expectations from one-dimensional model studies and demonstrate that induced three-body forces are of natural size and comparable to initial NNN contributions. The work enables future evolution of four-body matrix elements in A=4 when nuclear structure codes can accommodate them, and establishes that the decreasing hierarchy of many-body forces is maintained with induced four-body contributions being small compared to other uncertainties.
