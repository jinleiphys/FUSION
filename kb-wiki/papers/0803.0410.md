# Expanding color flux tubes and instabilities

> We present an analytic study of the physics of the glasma which is a strong classical gluon field created at early stage of high-energy heavy-ion collisions. Our analysis is based on the picture that the glasma just after the collision is made of color electric and magnetic flux tubes extending in the longitudinal direction with their diameters of the order of 1/Q_s (Q_s is the saturation scale of the colliding nuclei). We find that both the electric and magnetic flux tubes expand outwards and the field strength inside the flux tube decays rapidly in time. Next we investigate whether there exist instabilities against small rapidity-dependent perturbations for a fixed color configuration. We find that the magnetic background field exhibits an instability induced by the fluctuations in the lowest Landau level, and it grows in the time scale of 1/Q_s. For the electric background field we find no apparent instability while the possible relation to the Schwinger mechanism for particle pair creations is suggested.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the boost-invariant glasma, consisting of color electric and magnetic flux tubes with transverse size \(1/Q_s\), is stable against rapidity-independent perturbations but exhibits an instability for rapidity-dependent fluctuations specifically in the magnetic background field, growing exponentially on a time scale of \(1/Q_s\); no such instability is found for the electric background field, though a connection to the Schwinger mechanism is suggested.

## Method

The authors analytically solve the source-free Yang-Mills equations in \(\tau\)-\(\eta\) coordinates for a boost-invariant glasma background, modeling the initial condition as a collection of isolated color flux tubes with Gaussian transverse profiles of size \(1/Q_s\). They then introduce small rapidity-dependent perturbations to the gauge fields and linearize the equations of motion around the background, analyzing stability separately for constant magnetic and electric fields in \(SU(2)\) color gauge group, using Fourier decomposition in rapidity and cylindrical coordinates.

## Key numbers

* The transverse size of the flux tubes is of order \(1/Q_s\), where \(Q_s\) is the saturation scale of the colliding nuclei.
* The initial field strengths are \(gE^z \sim gB^z \sim Q_s^2\).
* The magnetic background field instability grows exponentially on a time scale of \(1/Q_s\).
* For a single Gaussian flux tube, the longitudinal magnetic field squared averaged over the transverse plane decays as \(\overline{(B^z)^2} \sim 1/\tau\) at large \(\tau\).
* For a single Gaussian flux tube, the transverse electric field squared averaged over the transverse plane behaves as \(\overline{(E^T)^2} \sim 1/\tau\) at large \(\tau\).
* The longitudinal electric field for a single Gaussian flux tube is \(E^z(\tau, r) = E_0 \, e^{-Q_s^2 r^2} e^{-Q_s^2 \tau^2} I_0(2 Q_s^2 r \tau)\).
* The transverse magnetic field for a single Gaussian flux tube is \(B^T(\tau, r) = E_0 \, e^{-Q_s^2 r^2} e^{-Q_s^2 \tau^2} I_1(2 Q_s^2 r \tau)\).

## Context

This work extends the analytic understanding of the glasma, a strong classical gluon field created in the early stage of high-energy heavy-ion collisions, which was previously studied numerically by Lappi and McLerran (2006) and in lattice simulations by Raju (2006). The paper provides an analytic counterpart to those numerical results, showing that the boost-invariant glasma alone cannot lead to thermalization because it lacks rapidity dependence, and that the instability in the magnetic background (similar to the Weibel instability in expanding geometry studied by Romatschke and Rebhan (2006)) may help drive the system toward isotropization. This enables further analytic study of how rapidity-dependent fluctuations can break boost invariance and contribute to the thermalization process in the glasma.
