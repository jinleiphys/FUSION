# Electroexcitation of Nucleon Resonances and the Emergence of Hadron Mass

> Developing an understanding of phenomena driven by the emergence of hadron mass (EHM) is one of the most challenging problems in the Standard Model. This discussion focuses on the impact of results on nucleon resonance ($N^\ast$) electroexcitation amplitudes (or $γ_vpN^\ast$ electrocouplings) obtained from experiments during the 6-GeV era in Hall~B at Jefferson Lab on understanding EHM. Analyzed using continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs), these results have revealed new pathways for the elucidation of EHM. A good description of the $Δ(1232)3/2^+$, $N(1440)1/2^+$, and $Δ(1600)3/2^+$ electrocouplings, achieved by CSM analyses that express a realistic dressed quark mass function, sheds light on the strong interaction dynamics that underlies EHM. Extensions to nucleon resonance studies for higher-mass states are outlined, as well as experimental results anticipated in the 12-GeV era at Jefferson Lab and those that would be enabled by a further increase of the beam energy to 22~GeV.

## Key claim

Continuum Schwinger function method (CSM) analyses of nucleon resonance electroexcitation amplitudes from Jefferson Lab's 6-GeV era have achieved a good description of the Delta(1232)3/2+, N(1440)1/2+, and Delta(1600)3/2+ electrocouplings, but only when those analyses incorporate a realistic dressed quark mass function that expresses the emergent hadron mass (EHM) phenomenon.

## Method

The authors review experimental results on gamma_v p N* electrocouplings extracted from exclusive meson electroproduction data collected in Hall B at Jefferson Lab during the 6-GeV era. These electrocoupling data are then analyzed using continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs), which solve the QCD Dyson-Schwinger and Bethe-Salpeter equations to compute nucleon resonance transition form factors from quark-gluon dynamics.

## Key numbers

- Delta(1232)3/2+, N(1440)1/2+, and Delta(1600)3/2+ electrocouplings were described by CSM analyses that incorporate a realistic dressed quark mass function.
- The experimental data were obtained during the 6-GeV era in Hall B at Jefferson Lab.
- Future experimental results are anticipated in the 12-GeV era at Jefferson Lab, and would be further enabled by a beam energy increase to 22 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the program of mapping nucleon resonance electroexcitation amplitudes that began with the 6-GeV Jefferson Lab experiments, building on prior CSM analyses of hadron properties. The results demonstrate that electrocoupling data provide a direct probe of the dressed quark mass function, which is the key signature of emergent hadron mass (EHM). The paper outlines extensions to higher-mass nucleon resonances and anticipates new data from the 12-GeV Jefferson Lab upgrade and from a potential future 22-GeV beam energy increase.
