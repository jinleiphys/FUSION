# Neutrinos in Stellar Astrophysics

> The physics of the mysterious and stealthy neutrino is at the heart of many phenomena in the cosmos. These particles interact with matter and with each other through the aptly named weak interaction. At typical astrophysical energies the weak interaction is some twenty orders of magnitude weaker than the electromagnetic interaction. However, in the early universe and in collapsing stars neutrinos can more than make up for their feeble interaction strength with huge numbers. Neutrinos can dominate the dynamics in these sites and set the conditions that govern the synthesis of the elements. Here we journey through the history of the discovery of these particles and describe their role in stellar evolution and collapse, the big bang, and multi-messenger astrophysics. Neutrino physics is at the frontier of elementary particle physics, nuclear physics, astrophysics and cosmology. All of these fields overlap in the neutrino story.

## Key claim

Neutrinos, despite interacting via the weak force which is some twenty orders of magnitude weaker than the electromagnetic force at typical astrophysical energies, can dominate the dynamics of the early universe and collapsing stars due to their enormous numbers, thereby setting the conditions that govern the synthesis of the elements.

## Method

The paper presents a comprehensive review of neutrino physics in stellar astrophysics, tracing the historical discovery of the neutrino from Pauli's 1930 hypothesis through to modern experiments. It describes the standard solar model (SSM) based on hydrostatic equilibrium, energy transport via radiation and convection, hydrogen fusion through the pp chain and CN I cycle, and constraints from solar radius, mass, and luminosity. The authors then detail the experimental programs that resolved the solar neutrino problem, including the Homestake chlorine experiment, Kamiokande, SAGE/GALLEX/GNO, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and Borexino.

## Key numbers

- The weak interaction at typical astrophysical energies is some twenty orders of magnitude weaker than the electromagnetic interaction.
- The Sun produces approximately 2 × 10^38 neutrinos each second from the conversion of four protons into helium.
- The KATRIN experiment limits neutrino mass to < 0.9 eV/c^2 at 90% CL.
- The atmospheric neutrino oscillation frequency suggests a right-handed Majorana mass m_R ~ 10^16 GeV, near the Grand Unified scale.
- The solar core temperature is T_c ~ 1.57 × 10^7 K, with typical center-of-mass energies for reacting particles of ~ 10 keV.
- The ppII/ppI and ppIII/ppI branching ratios vary approximately as T_c^7 and T_c^18, respectively.
- The 8B neutrino flux from the ppIII cycle has an exceedingly sharp growth ~ e^(t/τ) where τ ~ 0.9 billion years.
- The SSM predicts a luminosity increase of ~ 40% over 4.6 billion years.
- The 4He/H mass fraction ratio from SSM is typically 0.27 ± 0.01, compared to the Big-Bang value of 0.23 ± 0.01.
- The pp neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 5.98(1 ± 0.006) × 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The 8B neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 5.58(1 ± 0.14) × 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The 7Be neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 5.00(1 ± 0.07) × 10^9 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The pep neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 1.44(1 ± 0.012) × 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The 13N neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 2.96(1 ± 0.14) × 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The 15O neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 2.23(1 ± 0.15) × 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The hep neutrino flux from the GS98-SFII SSM is 8.04(1 ± 0.30) × 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1.
- The Ellis and Wooster experiment found energy deposited per event in the target of 0.34 ± 0.04 MeV, differing from the Q value of 1.05 MeV for 210Bi decay.
- A galactic core-collapse supernova within 10 kpc could produce up to 10^4-10^5 events in current and future massive detectors such as Super- and Hyper-Kamiokande.

## Context

This review extends the historical and theoretical framework established by Bahcall (1982) and the standard solar model tradition dating to Bethe and Critchfield (1938) and Bethe (1939). The paper synthesizes results from the Homestake experiment (Davis), Kamiokande, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, SAGE, GALLEX/GNO, and Borexino, which collectively resolved the solar neutrino problem through the discovery of neutrino oscillations. The work enables future multi-messenger astrophysics connecting neutrino observations from core-collapse supernovae, neutron star mergers, and the early universe to nuclear physics and cosmology, while highlighting open questions such as the solar abundance problem and the nature of neutrino mass.
