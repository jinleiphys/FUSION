# Central depression in nuclear density and its consequences for the shell structure of superheavy nuclei

> The influence of the central depression in the density distribution of spherical superheavy nuclei on the shell structure is studied within the relativistic mean field theory. Large depression leads to the shell gaps at the proton Z=120 and neutron N=172 numbers, while flatter density distribution favors N=184 for neutrons and leads to the appearance of a Z=126 shell gap and to the decrease of the size of the Z=120 shell gap. The correlations between the magic shell gaps and the magnitude of central depression are discussed for relativistic and non-relativistic mean field theories.

# Central depression in nuclear density and its consequences for the shell structure of superheavy nuclei

## Key claim
The magnitude of central depression in the density distribution of spherical superheavy nuclei determines which magic shell gaps appear: a large central depression favors shell gaps at proton Z=120 and neutron N=172, while a flatter density distribution favors Z=126 and N=184. The authors argue that the predicted shell gaps at Z=120,126 and N=172,184 are most likely, but note that the RMF theory gives a pronounced double shell closure at Z=120,N=172 while non-relativistic theories give a large shell gap at N=184 with less pronounced gaps at Z=120 and 126.

## Method
The authors used relativistic mean field (RMF) theory for spherical nuclei without pairing and the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) theory to study the single-particle spectra and density distributions of superheavy nuclei. They compared ground-state configurations (with wine-bottle potentials) to excited configurations where neutrons were artificially moved from high-j to low-j subshells to produce flatter density distributions, and examined how the radial density profile correlates with shell gaps across different RMF parametrizations (NL3, NL-Z, NLSH) and compared with non-relativistic Skyrme and Gogny forces.

## Key numbers
- The RMF NL3 force (compression modulus K_infinity = 272 MeV) was used as the primary parametrization for the detailed density and potential calculations
- Compression moduli of RMF forces compared: NL-Z (K_infinity = 173 MeV), NL3 (K_infinity = 272 MeV), NLSH (K_infinity = 355 MeV)
- Effective masses compared: Gogny D1S (m*/m = 0.67), Skyrme SkI3 (m*/m = 0.57), SLy6 (m*/m = 0.69), RMF Lorentz effective mass at Fermi surface m*(k_F)/m approximately 0.66, Skyrme SkP (m*/m = 1.0), Skyrme SkM* (m*/m = 0.789)
- The energy of the nu 4s_{1/2} state would need to be overestimated by approximately 1 MeV for a large gap at N=184 to appear in RMF theory

## Context
This work extends earlier studies of central depression in nuclear density by Bender et al. (1999) and Dobaczewski et al. (1999), who first noted the phenomenon in self-consistent calculations. The paper reconciles conflicting predictions of magic numbers for superheavy nuclei across different theoretical approaches: the shell correction method (predicting Z=114, N=184), RMF theory (predicting Z=120, N=172), and non-relativistic Skyrme and Gogny forces (predicting various combinations). The results enable understanding of why different models disagree and suggest that experimental search strategies for superheavy nuclei should consider both Z=120,N=172 and Z=126,N=184 as candidate doubly magic systems depending on the actual density profile. The authors note that all experimentally known nuclei with Z >= 100 are deformed, which produces flatter density profiles and explains the success of the shell correction method for known nuclei while cautioning against its extrapolation to spherical superheavy nuclei.
