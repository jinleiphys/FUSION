# Energy dependence of potential barriers and its effect on fusion cross-sections

> Couplings between relative motion and internal structures are known to affect fusion barriers by dynamically modifying the densities of the colliding nuclei. The effect is expected to be stronger at energies near the barrier top, where changes in density have longer time to develop than at higher energies. Quantitatively, modern TDHF calculations are able to predict realistic fusion thresholds. However, the evolution of the potential barrier with bombarding energy remains to be confronted with the experimental data. The aim is to find signatures of the energy dependence of the barrier by comparing fusion cross-sections calculated from potentials obtained at different bombarding energies with the experimental data. This comparison is made for the $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca and $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb systems. Fusion cross-sections are computed from potentials calculated with the density-constrained TDHF method. The couplings decrease the barrier at low-energy in both cases. A deviation from the Woods-Saxon nuclear potential is also observed at the lowest energies. In general, fusion cross-sections around a given energy are better reproduced by the potential calculated at this energy. The coordinate-dependent mass plays a crucial role for the reproduction of sub-barrier fusion cross-sections. Effects of the energy dependence of the potential can be found in experimental barrier distributions only if the variation of the barrier is significant in the energy-range spanned by the distribution. It appears to be the case for $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb but not for $^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca. These results show that the energy dependence of the barrier predicted in TDHF calculations is realistic. This confirms that the TDHF approach can be used to study the couplings between relative motion and internal degrees of freedom in heavy-ion collisions.

# Energy dependence of potential barriers and its effect on fusion cross-sections

## Key claim
The energy dependence of fusion barriers predicted by time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) calculations is realistic, but signatures of this dependence in experimental barrier distributions appear only when the barrier variation is significant over the energy range spanned by the distribution, which is the case for 16O+208Pb but not for 40Ca+40Ca.

## Method
The authors used the density-constrained TDHF (DC-TDHF) method to compute ion-ion interaction potentials for the 40Ca+40Ca and 16O+208Pb systems at multiple bombarding energies. They then calculated fusion cross-sections by solving the Schrodinger equation with the Incoming Wave Boundary Condition method using these potentials, including coordinate-dependent mass effects. The resulting cross-sections were compared with experimental data to identify signatures of the energy-dependent barrier.

## Key numbers
- For 40Ca+40Ca, barrier heights ranged from 52.6 to 53.6 MeV, all located near a nuclear separation of R=10.2 fm, obtained from TDHF calculations at 1 MeV intervals in the 53-65 MeV range.
- For 40Ca+40Ca, the potential corresponding to the lowest TDHF energy reproduced sub-barrier fusion data but overestimated cross-sections at higher energies, while the highest-energy potential reproduced high-energy data but underestimated lower-energy data.
- For 16O+208Pb, the energy dependence of the barrier was found to be significant enough to appear in experimental barrier distributions, unlike for 40Ca+40Ca.
- The numerical accuracy of static binding energies and the deviation of the computed DC-TDHF potential from the point Coulomb energy was of order 50-150 keV.

## Context
This work extends earlier TDHF studies by Washiyama and Lacroix (2008) that predicted energy-dependent barriers, and by Umar et al. (2006) who developed the DC-TDHF method. It confronts the predicted energy dependence of barriers with experimental fusion data for the first time. The results validate the TDHF approach for studying couplings between relative motion and internal degrees of freedom in heavy-ion collisions, and enable future microscopic studies of fusion dynamics without adjustable parameters. The work also relates to coupled-channels calculations by Hagino (2012) and the Sao Paulo potential by Chamon (2002), which introduced energy dependence phenomenologically.
