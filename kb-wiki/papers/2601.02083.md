# Entanglement Viscosity: from Unitarity to Irreversibility in Accelerated Frames

> We demonstrate that the unitarity of quantum field theory, through the positivity of spectral functions, underlies thermodynamic irreversibility for a subsystem separated by a horizon, in direct analogy with the irreversibility of renormalization-group flows. To this end, we explicitly find the shear and bulk viscosities -- the entanglement viscosities -- for thermal radiation in Rindler space using the universal spectral representation. A direct consequence of the obtained general formulas is the relationship between the acceleration-induced shear viscosity in flat space and the conformal quantum anomaly in curved space, pointing to a possible novel probe of the conformal anomaly in systems with extreme acceleration. Moreover, for conformal field theories, we explicitly show that globally entanglement viscosity saturates the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound.

# Entanglement Viscosity: from Unitarity to Irreversibility in Accelerated Frames

## Key claim
The unitarity of quantum field theory in Minkowski space, through the positivity of spectral functions c⁽⁰⁾(μ) and c⁽²⁾(μ), guarantees the non-negativity of shear and bulk viscosities for Unruh radiation in Rindler space, thereby establishing thermodynamic irreversibility for a subsystem separated by a horizon. This holds for a wide class of theories including those with massive and interacting fields in any spacetime dimension, provided the spectral densities are non-negative.

## Method
The author derives the shear viscosity η(ρ) and bulk viscosity ζ(ρ) for thermal radiation in d-dimensional Rindler space using the universal spectral representation of the energy-momentum tensor correlator, which expresses all theory-specific information through spin-0 and spin-2 spectral densities c⁽⁰⁾(μ) and c⁽²⁾(μ). The Kubo formulas are evaluated in Rindler coordinates by relating Green functions to their Minkowski counterparts via coordinate transformation, integrating over transverse coordinates, and using the momentum representation of the Bessel function K₀ to factorize the dependence on the Rindler coordinate ρ and the time variable τ.

## Key numbers
- Shear viscosity: η(ρ) = k_d ρ ∫₀^∞ dμ c⁽²⁾(μ) μ² K₀(μρ), where k_d = π^(d/2) / [(d+1) 2^(d-1) Γ(d) Γ(d/2)] and ρ is the distance from the Rindler horizon.
- Bulk viscosity: ζ(ρ) = [2 k_d ρ / (d-1)²] ∫₀^∞ dμ c⁽⁰⁾(μ) μ² K₀(μρ).
- For conformal field theory in four dimensions: η = 8 a α³, where a is the conformal anomaly coefficient and α is the acceleration magnitude (ρ = 1/α).
- For conformal field theory in four dimensions: entropy density s(T=T_U, α) = (32π a / 3) α³.
- Global viscosity integrated above a stretched horizon at distance l_c: η_glob = C_T π² / (480 l_c²), where C_T is the conformal central charge.
- Global entropy integrated above the stretched horizon: s_glob = C_T π³ / (120 l_c²).
- Ratio of global viscosity to global entropy: η_glob / s_glob = 1/(4π), saturating the Kovtun-Son-Starinets bound.
- Ratio of local viscosity to local entropy for conformal field theory: η/s = 3/(4π).

## Context
This work extends the concept of entanglement viscosity introduced by Chirco (2010) and Lapygin (2025) for free massless fields to arbitrary conformal field theories and to theories with massive and interacting fields via the universal spectral representation of Cappelli (1990). The derivation parallels the irreversibility of renormalization-group flows, where unitarity similarly ensures positivity of the c-function as shown by Zamolodchikov (1986) and Komargodski (2011). The relationship between shear viscosity and the conformal anomaly places this effect alongside other anomalous transport phenomena such as the chiral magnetic effect and chiral vortical effect, and the saturation of the KSS bound generalizes the membrane paradigm of Damour (1979) and Parikh (1997) to quantum field theory. The results enable potential experimental probes of the conformal anomaly in systems with extreme acceleration, such as the early stages of heavy-ion collisions.
