# An Introduction To Solar Neutrino Research

> The first lecture describes the conflicts between the combined standard model (minimal electroweak and solar) predictions and the results of solar neutrino experiments. The second lecture discusses the possibilities for detecting with solar neutrinos ``smoking gun'' indications of departures from minimal electroweak theory. Viewgraphs and related data are available at http://www.sns.ias.edu/~jnb .

## Key claim

The combined standard model (minimal electroweak theory plus standard solar model) is inconsistent with the results from all four pioneering solar neutrino experiments, but this conclusion depends on the assumption that at least three of the four experiments are not systematically misleading; the alternative is that new physics beyond the minimal electroweak model is required.

## Method

Bahcall compares the measured event rates from the four pioneering solar neutrino experiments (Homestake chlorine, Kamiokande, GALLEX, and SAGE) against predictions from the combined standard model, which includes both a standard solar model and the minimal standard electroweak theory. He then examines helioseismological measurements of sound speeds to test the solar model component independently, and reviews model-independent analyses by Hata, Bludman, and Langacker (1994) and Heeger and Robertson (1996) that treat neutrino fluxes as free parameters without solar model constraints.

## Key numbers

- Chlorine experiment measured rate: 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU, compared to predicted 9.5 (+1.2, -1.4) SNU, a factor of approximately 3.6 less than predicted
- The partial rate from 8B neutrinos in the chlorine experiment, calculated from Kamiokande observations, is 3.2 ± 0.45 SNU, which exceeds the total observed chlorine rate of 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU
- Combined GALLEX and SAGE average observed rate: 70.5 ± 7 SNU, compared to a predicted 73 SNU from pp and pep neutrinos alone, leaving no room for the expected 34 ± 4 SNU from 7Be neutrinos
- Sound speed agreement between standard solar models and helioseismology: rms discrepancy of 0.1% for the best model with diffusion
- Maximum temperature differences allowed by helioseismology: approximately 0.2%, which would change neutrino fluxes by several percent or less
- Model-independent analysis by Heeger and Robertson (1996) found inconsistency with standard model at 99.5% C.L. including the luminosity constraint, and at 94% C.L. without it

## Context

This lecture extends the long-standing solar neutrino problem first identified by the Homestake chlorine experiment (Davis, 1994) and sharpens the conflict using helioseismological measurements from Basu (1996) that validate standard solar models to high precision. The work enables the interpretation of upcoming experiments, Super-Kamiokande, SNO, and BOREXINO, as tests for neutrino oscillations, specifically MSW and vacuum oscillation solutions, by searching for "smoking gun" signatures such as spectral distortions, day-night effects, and neutral-current to charged-current ratios.
