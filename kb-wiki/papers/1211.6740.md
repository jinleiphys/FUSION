# Using the Standard Solar Model to Constrain Composition and S-Factors

> While standard solar model (SSM) predictions depend on approximately 20 input parameters, SSM neutrino flux predictions are strongly correlated with a single model output parameter, the core temperature $T_c$. Consequently, one can extract physics from solar neutrino flux measurements while minimizing the consequences of SSM uncertainties, by studying flux ratios with appropriate power-law weightings tuned to cancel this $T_c$ dependence. We re-examine an idea for constraining the primordial C+N content of the solar core from a ratio of CN-cycle $^{15}$O to pp-chain $^8$B neutrino fluxes, showing that nonnuclear SSM uncertainties in the ratio are small and effectively governed by a single parameter, the diffusion coefficient. We point out that measurements of both CN-I cycle neutrino branches -- $^{15}$O and $^{13}$N $β$-decay -- could in principle lead to separate determinations of the core C and N abundances, due to out-of-equilibrium CN-cycle burning in the cooler outer layers of the solar core. Finally, we show that the strategy of constructing "minimum uncertainty" neutrino flux ratios can also test other properties of the SSM. In particular, we demonstrate that a weighted ratio of $^7$Be and $^8$B fluxes constrains a product of S-factors to the same precision currently possible with laboratory data.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that weighted ratios of solar neutrino fluxes can be used to constrain the primordial carbon-plus-nitrogen content of the solar core and to test nuclear S-factors, with the crucial hedge that the CN-cycle neutrino flux ratio to $^8$B neutrinos reduces environmental SSM uncertainties to a residual dominated by a single parameter, the diffusion coefficient, rather than eliminating all such uncertainties entirely.

## Method
The authors computed logarithmic partial derivatives of solar neutrino fluxes with respect to 19 SSM input parameters (environmental, nuclear, and abundance parameters) for the SFII-GS98 standard solar model. They then constructed weighted flux ratios, specifically $\phi(^{15}\text{O})/\phi(^8\text{B})^{0.785}$ and $\phi(^{13}\text{N})/\phi(^8\text{B})^{0.576}$, by solving for the eigenvector with minimum eigenvalue of the nuisance parameter error matrix, thereby minimizing dependence on core temperature and most environmental parameters while preserving sensitivity to C+N abundances and the diffusion coefficient.

## Key numbers
- Super-Kamiokande IV $^8$B neutrino flux measurement: $(2.34 \pm 0.03 \pm 0.04) \times 10^6/\text{cm}^2\text{s}$, with combined uncertainty about 2%
- Borexino $^7$Be neutrino measurement precision: 4.5%
- SFII-GS98 SSM predicted CN-cycle neutrino fluxes: $\phi(^{13}\text{N}) = 2.96 (1 \pm 0.14) \times 10^8/\text{cm}^2\text{s}$ and $\phi(^{15}\text{O}) = 2.23 (1 \pm 0.15) \times 10^8/\text{cm}^2\text{s}$
- In the weighted ratio $\phi(^{15}\text{O})/\phi(^8\text{B})^{0.785}$, the residual environmental uncertainty (excluding diffusion) is reduced to 0.3%, compared to 2.6% when diffusion is included as an 11th environmental parameter
- The $^{15}$O neutrino flux varies linearly with the sum of C+N number densities, with exponents 0.794 (C) and 0.212 (N) summing to approximately 1
- Systematic change in C+N abundance equivalent to the GS98-AGSS09 difference alters the $^{15}$O neutrino flux by 30.7%
- Diffusion coefficient uncertainty of 15% contributes a 2.6% uncertainty in relating contemporary flux measurements to primordial C+N abundance
- S-factor uncertainties contribute 10.6% to the error budget for the $^{15}$O/$^8$B ratio, dominated by S$_{114}$ (7.5%) and S$_{17}$ (7.7%)
- Borexino CNO neutrino interaction rate upper limit: $<7.9$ counts/(day $\times$ 100 ton) at 95% C.L.
- SNO+ potential $^{15}$O flux measurement accuracy: about 10% in three years

## Context
This work extends the approach of Haxton and Serenelli (2008) by explicitly separating the diffusion coefficient from other environmental parameters, showing that the residual uncertainty from 10 environmental parameters is only 0.3% rather than the previously reported 2.6%. The paper addresses the solar abundance problem arising from the tension between the GS98 composition (which agrees with helioseismology) and the AGSS09 composition (which is about 30% lower and derived from 3D atmospheric modeling). The results enable a direct experimental test of the SSM assumption that primordial core and modern solar surface metallicities are the same, using future measurements from SNO+ and improved nuclear physics determinations of S$_{17}$ and S$_{114}$.
