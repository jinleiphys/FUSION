# Finite-temperature Equations of State for Neutron Star Mergers

> The detection of gravitational waves from a neutron star merger has opened up the possibility of detecting the presence or creation of deconfined quark matter using the gravitational wave signal. To investigate this possibility, we construct a family of neutron star matter equations of state at nonzero density and temperature by combining state-of-the-art nuclear matter equations of state with holographic equations of state for strongly interacting quark matter. The emerging picture consistently points toward a strong first order deconfinement transition, with a temperature-dependent critical density and latent heat that we quantitatively examine. Recent neutron star mass measurements are further used to discriminate between the different equations of state obtained, leaving a tightly constrained family of preferred equations of state.

## Key claim

By combining state-of-the-art nuclear matter equations of state with holographic V-QCD models for quark matter, the authors construct finite-temperature equations of state for neutron star mergers that consistently point toward a strong first-order deconfinement transition; however, the quantitative properties of this transition (critical density and latent heat) depend significantly on the choice of nuclear model and V-QCD potential, and only after applying neutron star mass observations do the results become tightly constrained.

## Method

The authors model the confined (nuclear) phase using three phenomenological finite-temperature equations of state (DD2, IUF, SFHx) that satisfy zero-temperature observational constraints. They model the deconfined (quark) phase using the holographic V-QCD framework in the Veneziano limit, with three representative potentials (5b, 7a, 8b) chosen from Jokela (2018). For each combination, they compute the grand canonical pressure as a function of quark chemical potential and temperature, determine the phase transition via Maxwell construction (equating pressures at fixed chemical potential), and then apply neutron star mass measurements to discriminate among the resulting equations of state.

## Key numbers

- The zero-temperature transition chemical potential varies between 460 and 700 MeV across all model combinations.
- The zero-temperature transition baryon number density lies between 3 and 14 times nuclear saturation density n_s.
- The latent heat at zero temperature lies between 700 and 1500 MeV/fm^3.
- After applying the constraint that the maximum neutron star mass lies in the range 2.07–2.33 solar masses, the transition density is constrained to between 3.3 and 7.0 n_s and the latent heat to between 770 and 1550 MeV/fm^3.
- The maximum neutron star masses for the pure nuclear models are 2.42 solar masses (DD2), 1.95 solar masses (IUF), and 2.13 solar masses (SFHx); the V-QCD potentials reduce these by up to 15% in some cases.
- The phase transition line ends at a temperature around 155 MeV, above which no phase transition exists.

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature interpolation studies of Hebeler (2013), Kurkela (2014), and Annala (2017) to finite temperatures, and complements the merger simulations of Most (2018) and Bauswein (2018) that used model equations of state. The results provide a family of observationally-constrained finite-temperature equations of state immediately usable in neutron star merger simulations, and demonstrate that neutron star mass observations significantly constrain the behavior of the equation of state even at nonzero temperatures.
