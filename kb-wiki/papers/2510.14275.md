# Model Independence of Effective Field Theory

> The use of effective field theory offers a systematic way to improve calculations of nuclear reactions and the properties of atomic nuclei. Its successes have led to the widespread belief that the predictions of this approach are model independent. We explain why this is definitely not the case.

## Key claim

Despite the widespread belief that chiral effective field theory predictions for nuclear forces are model independent, the short-distance nucleon-nucleon force is not uniquely determined by fitting phase shifts, because unitary transformations can generate an infinite number of two-body interactions that fit exactly the same phase shifts while yielding different results for nuclear binding energies and three-body forces.

## Method

The author applies a specific unitary transformation to the Hamiltonian for nucleon-nucleon scattering in the \(^1S_0\) channel, using a two-term Mongan (case II) separable potential in the \(^1S_0\) and \(^3S_1 - ^3D_1\) partial waves. The transformation parameter \(\alpha\) is restricted to values larger than 2.6 fm\(^{-1}\) to ensure the one-pion-exchange tail of the nucleon-nucleon interaction is not altered and charge symmetry is respected. The resulting non-local potentials are then used to compute the triton binding energy and the spin-1/2 neutron-deuteron scattering length.

## Key numbers

- The variation in the binding energy of the triton is as much as 2 MeV, or 20% of its experimental value, for a selection of two-body potentials that produce exactly the same phase shifts at all energies.
- The parameter \(\alpha\) in the unitary transformation is restricted to values larger than 2.6 fm\(^{-1}\) to avoid altering the one-pion-exchange tail.
- The parameter \(\beta\) in the unitary transformation was chosen to be 1 fm\(^{-1}\).
- The Coulomb-modified proton-proton scattering length \(a_{pp}\) remains within 5% of that for the untransformed potential for the chosen range of \(\alpha\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier calculations of Afnan and Thomas (1975) demonstrating that fitting nucleon-nucleon phase shifts over a wide range of energies is insufficient to guarantee model-independent predictions for nuclear properties. The results contradict the widespread assumption that chiral effective field theory yields a unique three-body force, as commonly claimed in the literature following Weinberg (1990). The findings enable a critical evaluation of dense matter equations of state, particularly for neutron stars, and suggest that models such as the quark meson coupling (QMC) model should be judged against experimental data rather than their agreement with chiral nuclear forces.
