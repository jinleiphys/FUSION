# Calculation of Particle Pair Correlation Functions with Classical Trajectory Approximation

> Femtoscopic interferometry is a powerful tool for probing the spatio-temporal evolution of emission sources in heavy-ion collisions. A major challenge in the field is formulating a self-consistent description of the source function, final-state interactions between the particle pair, and interactions inherent to the source itself. To address this, we have developed a novel Monte Carlo model for calculating two-particle correlation functions in a classical trajectory approximation (CTA-I). The model incorporates self-consistently the emission source of thermal equilibrium and three-body final state interactions. Application of the model shows satisfactory fit to experimental data, revealing that the correlation function is highly sensitive to the source's spatio-temporal extent. In contrast, the temperature parameter governing the emitted particles' energy spectra has a negligible influence. Our approach offers the potential to extract the spatio-temporal information from the emission source, thereby advancing the applicability of femtoscopic interferometry in the Fermi energy domain.

# Calculation of Particle Pair Correlation Functions with Classical Trajectory Approximation

## Key claim

The authors develop a classical trajectory approximation model (CTA-I) that self-consistently treats the emission source in thermal equilibrium, the three-body final-state interactions, and the source potential field, showing that the two-particle correlation function is highly sensitive to the source's spatio-temporal extent while the temperature parameter governing emitted particles' energy spectra has negligible influence. The model offers potential to extract spatio-temporal information from emission sources in the Fermi energy domain, though the authors acknowledge that the Gaussian source parameterization and neglect of quantum statistics are approximations.

## Method

The model constructs a thermal equilibrium emission source described by a Gaussian spatial distribution with size parameter sigma_R and Boltzmann momentum distribution with temperature T, then derives a self-consistent mean-field potential comprising Coulomb, volume (Woods-Saxon), and surface terms constrained by the thermal equilibrium condition. Particle trajectories are simulated classically using a predictor-corrector time-stepping scheme that incorporates both the mean-field potential from the residual nucleus and the Coulomb interaction between the emitted particle pair, with emission times following an exponential decay law. The correlation function is computed as the normalized ratio of coincidence yields to single-particle yields as a function of relative momentum q, with detector filtering applied for comparison to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The source spatial distribution is parameterized as a Gaussian with standard deviation sigma_R, where the mean-field potential near r=0 satisfies kappa = k_B T / sigma_R^2 for the harmonic oscillator term
- The Coulomb potential between emitted particles is given by V_12(r_12) = (Z_1 Z_2 e^2)/(4 pi epsilon_0) * (1/r_12)
- The constraint inequality for the potential parameters is: (gamma_d^2 U_c)/(U_c + U_0) * (k_B T/(2 U_c) + 1/(6 gamma_c^2)) < 1/4
- The time distribution of particle emission follows p(t) proportional to exp(-t/tau), where tau is the reciprocal of the Poisson rate
- The residual nucleus velocity in the laboratory frame is v_res = sqrt(2 A_p m_u E_b) / ((A_p + A_t) m_u)

## Context

This work extends the classical trajectory approach of the MENEKA model (Elmaani, 1992) by introducing a self-consistent treatment of the thermal equilibrium source and its mean-field potential, addressing a limitation where previous models like CRAB (Lisa, 2005; Zhang, 2001) neglect the influence of the source potential field on emitted particle pairs, and where the Lednicky-Lyuboshits model (Lednicky, 1981) neglects the residual nucleus entirely. The model enables extraction of spatio-temporal source parameters in Fermi-energy heavy-ion collisions, particularly for intermediate mass fragment pairs where three-body Coulomb effects from the source are significant, and can be applied to interpret experimental correlation data from facilities operating in this energy domain.
