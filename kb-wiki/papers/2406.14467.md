# A More Precise Measurement of the Radius of PSR J0740+6620 Using Updated NICER Data

> PSR J0740+6620 is the neutron star with the highest precisely determined mass, inferred from radio observations to be $2.08\pm0.07\,\rm M_\odot$. Measurements of its radius therefore hold promise to constrain the properties of the cold, catalyzed, high-density matter in neutron star cores. Previously, Miller et al. (2021) and Riley et al. (2021) reported measurements of the radius of PSR J0740+6620 based on Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) observations accumulated through 17 April 2020, and an exploratory analysis utilizing NICER background estimates and a data set accumulated through 28 December 2021 was presented in Salmi et al. (2022). Here we report an updated radius measurement, derived by fitting models of X-ray emission from the neutron star surface to NICER data accumulated through 21 April 2022, totaling $\sim1.1$ Ms additional exposure compared to the data set analyzed in Miller et al. (2021) and Riley et al. (2021), and to data from X-ray Multi-Mirror (XMM-Newton) observations. We find that the equatorial circumferential radius of PSR J0740+6620 is $12.92_{-1.13}^{+2.09}$ km (68% credibility), a fractional uncertainty $\sim83\%$ the width of that reported in Miller et al. (2021), in line with statistical expectations given the additional data. If we were to require the radius to be less than 16 km, as was done in Salmi et al. (2024), then our 68% credible region would become $R=12.76^{+1.49}_{-1.02}$ km, which is close to the headline result of Salmi et al. (2024). Our updated measurements, along with other laboratory and astrophysical constraints, imply a slightly softer equation of state than that inferred from our previous measurements.

## Key claim

The equatorial circumferential radius of PSR J0740+6620 is measured to be $12.92_{-1.13}^{+2.09}$ km (68% credibility) using updated NICER data through April 2022, with a fractional uncertainty approximately 83% the width of that reported in Miller et al. (2021); the result implies a slightly softer equation of state than previously inferred, but the measurement depends on modeling assumptions including atmospheric composition and ionization state.

## Method

The authors fit models of X-ray emission from two circular hot spots on the neutron star surface to phase- and energy-resolved NICER pulse profiles (0.3-1.24 keV) combined with XMM-Newton imaging data, using the oblate Schwarzschild approximation for light propagation and accounting for interstellar absorption. Parameter estimation employed a hybrid approach: initial nested sampling with MultiNest followed by Markov chain Monte Carlo refinement, incorporating Gaussian priors on mass ($2.08\pm0.09 M_\odot$) and distance from radio timing measurements. Analyses were performed separately with fully ionized and partially ionized hydrogen atmosphere models, as well as helium atmosphere models.

## Key numbers

- Equatorial circumferential radius: $12.92_{-1.13}^{+2.09}$ km (68% credibility) from the primary analysis
- With an imposed prior requiring radius less than 16 km (as in Salmi et al. 2024), the 68% credible region becomes $R=12.76^{+1.49}_{-1.02}$ km
- Net NICER exposure time: 2733.81 ks, compared to 1602.68 ks in Miller et al. (2021) and Riley et al. (2021)
- Additional NICER exposure relative to previous analyses: approximately 1.1 Ms
- Pulsar mass from radio observations: $2.08\pm0.07 M_\odot$ (68% credibility)
- Spin period: 0.00289 s; period derivative: $1.219\times10^{-20}$
- Estimated surface magnetic field strength: approximately $5\times10^8$ G (assuming non-antipodal spots)

## Context

This work extends the NICER-based radius measurements of PSR J0740+6620 reported by Miller et al. (2021) and Riley et al. (2021) by incorporating approximately two additional years of NICER data, and builds on exploratory analyses by Salmi et al. (2022) and Salmi et al. (2024). The improved precision constrains the equation of state of cold dense matter at densities above nuclear saturation, complementing constraints from the gravitational wave event GW170817 and from laboratory nuclear experiments. The posterior samples are publicly available on Zenodo.
