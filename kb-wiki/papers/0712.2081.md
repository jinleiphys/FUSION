# Missing monopole strength of the Hoyle state in the inelastic $α$+$^{12}$C scattering

> Analyses of the inelastic $α$+$^{12}$C scattering at medium energies have indicated that the strength of the Hoyle state (the isoscalar 0$^+_2$ excitation at 7.65 MeV in $^{12}$C) seems to exhaust only 7 to 9% of the monopole energy weighted sum rule (EWSR), compared to about 15% of the EWSR extracted from inelastic electron scattering data. The full monopole transition strength predicted by realistic microscopic $α$-cluster models of the Hoyle state can be shown to exhaust up to 22% of the EWSR. To explore the missing monopole strength in the inelastic $α$+$^{12}$C scattering, we have performed a fully microscopic folding model analysis of the inelastic $α$+$^{12}$C scattering at $E_{\rm lab}=104$ to 240 MeV using the 3-$α$ resonating group wave function of the Hoyle state obtained by Kamimura, and a complex density-dependent M3Y interaction newly parametrized based on the Brueckner Hartree Fock results for nuclear matter. Our folding model analysis has shown consistently that the missing monopole strength of the Hoyle state is not associated with the uncertainties in the analysis of the $α$+$^{12}$C scattering, but is most likely due to the short lifetime and weakly bound structure of this state which significantly enhances absorption in the exit $α$+$^{12}$C$^*(0^+_2)$ channel.

# Missing monopole strength of the Hoyle state in the inelastic α+¹²C scattering

## Key claim
The missing monopole strength of the Hoyle state in inelastic α+¹²C scattering is not due to uncertainties in the scattering analysis, but is most likely caused by the short lifetime and weakly bound structure of the Hoyle state, which significantly enhances absorption in the exit α+¹²C*(0⁺₂) channel. This conclusion is drawn from a fully microscopic folding model analysis that uses a new complex density-dependent M3Y interaction calibrated against Brueckner Hartree-Fock results.

## Method
The authors performed a fully microscopic folding model analysis of inelastic α+¹²C scattering at laboratory energies from 104 to 240 MeV, using the 3-α resonating group method (RGM) wave function of the Hoyle state obtained by Kamimura (1981) and a new complex density-dependent M3Y-Paris interaction (CDJLM) calibrated against Brueckner Hartree-Fock results for nuclear matter. Both distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) and coupled-channel (CC) calculations were performed using the code ECIS97, with the CC scheme including coupling among the 0⁺₁, 2⁺₁, and 0⁺₂ states of ¹²C.

## Key numbers
- The Hoyle state (isoscalar 0⁺₂ excitation at 7.65 MeV in ¹²C) exhausts only 7 to 9% of the monopole energy weighted sum rule (EWSR) in inelastic α+¹²C scattering analyses, compared to about 15% from inelastic electron scattering data.
- The RGM, BEC, and FMD cluster models predict monopole transition moments of 6.62, 6.45, and 6.53 e·fm², respectively, which correspond to S₀ ≈ 22.8%, 21.7%, and 22.2% of the EWSR.
- The experimental monopole moment from (e,e') data is M(E0) ≈ 5.37 ± 0.22 e·fm², giving S₀ ≈ 15.0 ± 1.3% of the EWSR.
- To match DWBA calculations to data, both RGM and breathing mode transition densities required scaling by a factor of 0.55, reducing the sum rule strength to S₀ ≈ 6.9% of the EWSR.
- In the CC calculation, a good description of the inelastic 0⁺₂ data required N_I values of 2.5 to 3.4 for the imaginary optical potential in the exit α+¹²C*(0⁺₂) channel, compared to N_I ≈ 1.0 to 1.3 for the entrance channel.
- The 2⁺₁ state at 4.44 MeV in ¹²C has a mean lifetime τ ≈ 6×10⁻¹⁴ s, about 600 times longer than the Hoyle state (τ ≈ 10⁻¹⁶ s), and required no enhanced absorption in the exit channel.

## Context
This work extends earlier folding model studies of inelastic α+¹²C scattering by Bauhoff (1984), Ohkubo and Hirabayashi (2002, 2004), and Takashina (2006, 2007), which used real density-dependent interactions and phenomenological imaginary potentials. The present study uses a fully complex density-dependent interaction (CDJLM) calibrated against Brueckner Hartree-Fock results by Jeukenne, Lejeune, and Mahaux (1977). The enhanced absorption effect in the exit channel was previously observed in inelastic ¹⁶O+¹⁶O scattering to the 2⁺₁ and 3⁻₁ states of ¹⁶O by Khoa (2005) and in one-neutron transfer reactions by Bohlen (2002). The results indicate that standard DWBA or CC analyses using the same optical potential in entrance and exit channels may lead to large uncertainties in deduced transition strengths for weakly bound, short-lived excited states.
