# Rotation-driven phase transitions in the cores of pulsars

> In this paper, we discuss the impact of rotation on the particle composition of rotating neutron stars (pulsars). Particular emphasis is put on the formation of quark matter during stellar spin-down, driven by continuous gravitational compression. Our study is based on modern models for the nuclear equation of state whose parameters are tightly constrained by nuclear data, neutron star masses, and the latest estimates of neutron star radii.

## Key claim

Rotation-driven changes in central density (up to 60% as spin varies from zero to the Kepler frequency) can drive phase transitions in neutron star cores, but the quark-hadron mixed phase is spun out of the star as rotation increases, meaning non-rotating stars have the most complex compositions; pure quark matter is not obtained for any of the nuclear equation of state models studied.

## Method

The authors constructed models of rotating neutron stars in general relativity using the metric of Eq. (1) and solving Einstein's field equations, with the nuclear equation of state GM1 and a non-local extension of the SU(3) Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model for the quark-hadron phase transition under the Gibbs condition. They computed stellar configurations at fixed baryon number for rotation rates from zero up to the Kepler frequency (mass-shedding limit), mapping the particle composition and quark-hadron mixed phase fraction as functions of gravitational mass and spin frequency.

## Key numbers

- Central density change of up to 60% as neutron star spin varies from 0 to the Kepler frequency.
- Kepler period for 1.4 solar mass neutron stars is typically around 1 ms; for strange quark matter stars it can be as low as 0.5 ms.
- The most rapidly rotating known pulsar, PSR J1748-2446ad, rotates at 1.39 ms (719 Hz).
- For the GM1 equation of state, the Kepler frequency for the computed star is 1361 Hz; the gravitational mass of the non-rotating star is 2.10 solar masses, increasing to 2.20 solar masses at the Kepler frequency.
- Up to about 8% of the total gravitational mass of massive neutron stars may exist in the mixed quark-hadron phase.
- For pulsar PSR J0348+0432 (mass 2.01 ± 0.04 solar masses, frequency 26 Hz), up to about 7% of its mass could be in the mixed quark-hadron phase.
- For pulsar PSR J1614-2230 (mass 1.928 ± 0.017 solar masses, frequency 318 Hz), the core may be hovering right at the quark deconfinement density.
- The threshold mass for quark deconfinement as a function of spin frequency follows a quadratic fit: M(Omega) = a Omega^2 + c.

## Context

This work extends previous calculations for non-rotating neutron stars (based on the Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation) to include rapid rotation, following earlier studies by Weber (1999) and Glen (1997) on rotating configurations. It introduces the concept of supra-massive rotating neutron stars (SURONS), which have higher baryon numbers than stable non-rotating solutions and may collapse into black holes as potential sources of fast radio bursts (Falcke, 2014). The results enable future comparisons with pulsar observations from radio telescopes, X-ray satellites, and gravitational-wave detectors to constrain the phase diagram of cold dense matter.
