# Calculations of Branching Ratios for Radiative-Capture, One-Proton, and Two-Neutron Channels in the Fusion Reaction $^{209}$Bi+$^{70}$Zn

> We discuss the possibility of the non-one-neutron emission channels in the cold fusion reaction $^{70}$Zn + $^{209}$Bi to produce the element Z=113. For this purpose, we calculate the evaporation-residue cross sections of one-proton, radiative-capture, and two-neutron emissions relative to the one-neutron emission in the reaction $^{70}$Zn + $^{209}$Bi. To estimate the upper bounds of those quantities, we vary model parameters in the calculations, such as the level-density parameter and the height of the fission barrier. We conclude that the highest possibility is for the 2n reaction channel, and its upper bounds are 2.4$%$ and at most less than 7.9% with unrealistic parameter values, under the actual experimental conditions of [J. Phys. Soc. Jpn. {\bf 73} (2004) 2593].

# Calculations of Branching Ratios for Radiative-Capture, One-Proton, and Two-Neutron Channels in the Fusion Reaction $^{209}$Bi+$^{70}$Zn

## Key claim
Under the actual experimental conditions of Morita et al. (2004), the two-neutron (2n) evaporation channel is the most likely non-one-neutron channel, with an upper bound of 2.4% relative to the 1n channel; even with unrealistic parameter variations, this upper bound reaches at most 7.9%. The one-proton and radiative-capture channels are negligible, with upper bounds below 0.5% and 0.2% respectively.

## Method
The authors extended the fusion-by-diffusion (FBD) model of Swiatecki et al. (2003, 2005) to calculate evaporation-residue cross sections for the 1p, 0n (radiative-capture), and 2n channels relative to the 1n channel in the $^{209}$Bi($^{70}$Zn,n)$^{278}$113 reaction. They applied the transition-state method for neutron and proton decay widths, varied the asymptotic level-density parameter by up to 10%, adjusted fission barrier heights by ±1 MeV, and used three different mass models (FRDM95, MS96, HFB02) to estimate excitation energies. The expected value of each non-1n channel relative to the 1n channel was computed by integrating over the incident-energy distribution due to target thickness.

## Key numbers
- For the 1p channel: $P^{(1p/1n)} = 8.51 \times 10^{-4}$ with standard parameters; at most $4.97 \times 10^{-3}$ in the worst-case scenario.
- For the 0n (radiative-capture) channel: $P^{(0n/1n)} = 5.14 \times 10^{-4}$ with standard parameters; at most $1.70 \times 10^{-3}$ in the worst-case scenario.
- For the 2n channel: $P^{(2n/1n)} = 2.44 \times 10^{-2}$ (2.44%) with standard parameters; at most $7.91 \times 10^{-2}$ (7.91%) with unrealistic parameter values.
- Total non-1n branching ratio: $P^{(non-1n/1n)} = 2.55 \times 10^{-2}$ (2.55%) with standard parameters; at most $8.51 \times 10^{-2}$ (8.51%) in the worst-case scenario.
- Excitation energies of the compound nucleus $^{279}$113 at beam energy 261.4 MeV: 15.76 MeV (FRDM95), 14.11 MeV (MS96), 13.61 MeV (HFB02), with ±2 MeV uncertainty from target thickness.

## Context
This work extends the FBD model, previously optimized for 1n channels in cold fusion reactions by Swiatecki et al. (2003, 2005) and validated against experimental data by various groups (Nelson 2008, Dracoulis 2008, Gates 2008, Folger 2009), to non-1n evaporation channels. It directly addresses the reaction assignment in the Morita et al. (2004) experiment that produced element Z=113 via $^{209}$Bi($^{70}$Zn,n). The calculations provide quantitative confidence limits for the 1n channel assignment, complementing the later experimental confirmation by Morita et al. (2009) through alpha-decay chain comparison with $^{266}$Bh. The results enable future experiments to estimate the required statistics to exclude competing channels in superheavy element synthesis via cold fusion reactions.
