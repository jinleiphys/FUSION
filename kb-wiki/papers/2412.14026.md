# Dynamics of Hot QCD Matter 2024 -- Hard Probes

> The hot and dense QCD matter, known as the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP), is explored through heavy-ion collision experiments at the LHC and RHIC. Jets and heavy flavors, produced from the initial hard scattering, are used as hard probes to study the properties of the QGP. Recent experimental observations on jet quenching and heavy-flavor suppression have strengthened our understanding, allowing for fine-tuning of theoretical models in hard probes. The second conference, HOT QCD Matter 2024, was organized to bring the community together for discussions on key topics in the field. This article comprises 15 sections, each addressing various aspects of hard probes in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, offering a snapshot of current experimental observations and theoretical advancements. The article begins with a discussion on memory effects in the quantum evolution of quarkonia in the quark-gluon plasma, followed by an experimental review, new insights on jet quenching at RHIC and LHC, and concludes with a machine learning approach to heavy flavor production at the Large Hadron Collider.

## Key claim
The momentum diffusion of heavy quarks in a hot magnetized medium is calculated for arbitrary magnetic field strengths, moving beyond the common Lowest-Landau-Level (LLL) or weak-field approximations. The study finds that the anisotropy in the diffusion coefficients depends on the interplay of the heavy quark mass, momentum, temperature, and magnetic field, and that a simplified Debye mass approximation fails to capture the full dynamics at high magnetic fields.

## Method
The authors compute the heavy quark (HQ) momentum diffusion coefficients by evaluating the scattering rate for $2 \leftrightarrow 2$ processes between a heavy quark and light partons (quarks and gluons) in a hot, magnetized medium. They use effective gluon and quark propagators derived via the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) technique for a hot, arbitrarily magnetized medium, which yields gauge-independent form factors valid for all Landau levels. The scattering rate is then related to the imaginary part of the HQ self-energy via Weldon's formula, from which the longitudinal ($\kappa_L$) and transverse ($\kappa_T$) momentum diffusion coefficients are analytically calculated for both the static limit and the case of a moving heavy quark (with velocity $\vec{v}$ parallel or perpendicular to the magnetic field $\vec{B}$).

## Key numbers
*   For charm quarks ($M_c = 1.27$ GeV) and bottom quarks ($M_b = 4.18$ GeV) at temperatures $T = 0.4$ and $T = 0.6$ GeV, the ratio of the longitudinal and transverse diffusion coefficients to the zero-field coefficient ($\kappa_{L/T}^{(s)}/\kappa^{(s)}$) increases sharply at low $eB$ and then saturates.
*   In the static limit, the longitudinal diffusion coefficient $\kappa_L$ is larger than the transverse coefficient $\kappa_T$ for both charm and bottom quarks across the studied $eB$ range.
*   For a heavy quark moving parallel to the magnetic field ($\vec{v} \parallel \vec{B}$) with momentum $p = 1$ GeV, the transverse coefficient $\kappa_T$ dominates over $\kappa_L$ for charm quarks across the entire $eB$ range, while for bottom quarks, $\kappa_L$ exceeds $\kappa_T$ until a crossover at a higher $eB$ threshold.
*   For the perpendicular case ($\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$), the transverse components $\kappa_1$ and $\kappa_2$ dominate over the longitudinal component $\kappa_3$, and the bottom quark coefficients are roughly an order of magnitude lower than those for charm quarks.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of heavy quark diffusion in a magnetized QGP, which were often restricted to the Lowest-Landau-Level (LLL) approximation (Fukushima, 2015; Bandyopadhyay, 2021) or the weak magnetic field approximation (Dey, 2023). By calculating the diffusion coefficients for arbitrary magnetic field strengths using the full HTL-resummed gluon propagator, the authors provide a more general framework. The results enable more realistic simulations of heavy quark in-medium evolution in non-central heavy-ion collisions, which could impact predictions for experimental observables such as the directed and elliptic flow of open heavy flavor mesons.
