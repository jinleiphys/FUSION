# Heavy particle radioactivities of superheavy nuclei

> The concept of heavy particle radioactivity (HPR) is changed to allow emitted particles with Z_e>28 from parents with Z>110 and daughter around 208Pb. Calculations for superheavy (SH) nuclei with Z=104-124 are showing a trend toward shorter half-lives and larger branching ratio relative to alpha decay for heavier SHs. It is possible to find regions in which HPR is stronger than alpha decay. The new mass table AME11 and the theoretical KTUY05 and FRDM95 masses are used to determine the released energy. For 124 we found isotopes with half-lives in the range of ns to ps.

## Key claim

The concept of heavy particle radioactivity (HPR) should be extended to allow emitted particles with atomic numbers larger than 28 from superheavy parents with Z > 110, with the daughter nucleus around doubly magic 208Pb; if the KTUY05 and FRDM95 calculated masses are reliable, isotopes of element 124 would have half-lives in the range of nanoseconds to picoseconds, making identification experiments practically impossible.

## Method

The authors used the analytical superasymmetric fission (ASAF) model with the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin (WKB) quasiclassical approximation to calculate half-lives for all possible parent-emitted cluster combinations (more than 10^5) for parent nuclei with Z = 94-124. The released energy (Q value) was determined using three mass tables: the experimental AME11 table (3290 nuclides), and the theoretical KTUY05 and FRDM95 mass calculations. For each parent nucleus, the half-lives were computed for all emitted clusters with atomic numbers 2 < Z_e ≤ Z_e^max, where Z_e^max = Z - 82.

## Key numbers

- For 222Ra, the shortest half-lives correspond to alpha decay and 14C radioactivity, with the calculated values in good agreement with measured data
- For 288114, the most probable emitted heavy particle is 80Ge with a calculated branching ratio relative to alpha decay b_α = 10^{-5.01}
- For 84Se emission from 288114 (magic neutron number N_e = 50), the branching ratio is b_α = 10^{-5.42}
- For 128Sn emission from 256Fm (Q = 252.129 MeV), log10 T(s) = 4.88
- For 130Te emission from 262Rf (Q = 274.926 MeV), log10 T(s) = 0.53
- For element 124, many isotopes have predicted half-lives in the range of nanoseconds to picoseconds
- The shortest measured HPR half-life is T_c = 10^{11.01} s for 14C radioactivity of 222Ra
- The largest measured branching ratio relative to alpha decay is b_α = 10^{-8.9} for 14C radioactivity of 223Ra

## Context

This work extends the concept of heavy particle radioactivity beyond the previously accepted maximum emitted particle atomic number of 28 (Poenaru 1991), allowing for emissions as heavy as 114Mo from superheavy parents. The calculations use the ASAF model that previously successfully predicted HPR half-lives for parent nuclei with Z = 87-96, where emissions such as 14C, 20O, 23F, 22-26Ne, 28,30Mg, and 32,34Si have been experimentally confirmed (Bonetti 2007). The results suggest that for the heaviest superheavy nuclei not yet synthesized, HPR may compete with or even dominate over alpha decay, though for element 124 the predicted half-lives are so short that experimental identification would be extremely difficult; more promising candidates for future experiments may be proton-rich isotopes of element 122 with branching ratios b_α > 1.
