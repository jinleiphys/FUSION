# Isoscalar Giant Resonances: Experimental Studies

> Giant resonances--highly collective, high-frequency oscillations of the atomic nucleus--are the focus of this chapter. We discuss the isoscalar excitations, where the protons and neutrons oscillate in phase, up to angular-momentum transfers $L$ = 3. The procedures of experiments and data analysis employed in extracting the strength distributions associated with these resonances are discussed. The experimentally extracted strength distributions are presented, along with information on the properties of these resonances available to date. The effect of deformation of the nuclear ground state on the resonance strength distributions is discussed. Furthermore, the exciting opportunities being opened with the current and future availability of rare isotope beams the world over are expounded.

## Key claim

This chapter reviews experimental studies of isoscalar giant resonances up to angular-momentum transfer L = 3, emphasizing that while the field is "mature" after four decades of activity, the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (ISGMR) remains of particular interest because it provides an experimental value for nuclear incompressibility K∞, a key parameter for the equation of state of infinite nuclear matter and astrophysical phenomena such as neutron stars.

## Method

The experimental strength distributions were extracted primarily via small-angle inelastic scattering of 386-MeV α particles at the Research Center for Nuclear Physics (RCNP) and 240-MeV α beams at Texas A&M University, using magnetic spectrometers (Grand Raiden and MDM, respectively) for particle detection. The background-subtracted spectra were subjected to multipole-decomposition analysis (MDA), where experimental angular differential cross sections in each excitation-energy bin were fitted by linear combinations of distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) calculations for multipoles L = 0–7, using a hybrid optical-model potential with a density-dependent Gaussian α-nucleon interaction. The IVGDR contribution, excited via Coulomb excitation, was accounted for using previously-known photonuclear cross-section data and DWBA calculations based on the Goldhaber-Teller model.

## Key numbers

- The ISGMR strength distributions were obtained for nuclei over the mass range A = 16–208, exhausting nearly 100% of the energy-weighted sum rule (EWSR) for L = 0 in practically all cases.
- The excitation energy of each resonance has a roughly A⁻¹/³ dependence; the centroid energies of ISGMR and ISGQR are generally taken as 80 A⁻¹/³ MeV and 64 A⁻¹/³ MeV, respectively.
- The 1ħω shell gap is approximately 40 A⁻¹/³ MeV.
- Inelastic scattering measurements used α-particle beams at energies of 35–100 MeV/nucleon; the RCNP measurements employed 386-MeV α particles, while TAMU used 240-MeV α beams.
- The MDA fits included multipoles up to L = 7, with uncertainties estimated via Monte Carlo sampling constituting a 68% confidence interval in more recent work.
- The "difference-spectrum" technique was used in the first clear identification of the ISGDR in ²⁰⁸Pb.

## Context

This chapter extends the comprehensive monograph by Harakeh and van der Woude and the recent review of ISGMR and ISGDR by Garg and Colò (2018), drawing heavily on both. The history of giant resonances began with the isovector giant dipole resonance (IVGDR) observed in the late 1940s via photofission of U and Th, followed by identification of the ISGQR in early 1970s using 65-MeV electrons (Pithan, 1971) and 62-MeV protons (Bertrand, 1972), and the ISGMR in the mid-to-late 1970s using 82-MeV deuterons and 96–120 MeV α particles (Harakeh, 1977). The work enables future studies with rare isotope beams, which are expected to open exciting opportunities for investigating giant resonances in exotic nuclei.
