# Nuclear statistics, microcanonical or canonical? The physicists vs. the chemists approach

> Nuclei are small and inhomogeneous. Multi-fragmented nuclei are even more inhomogeneous and the fragments even smaller. System studied in chemical thermodynamics (CTh) consist of several homogeneous macroscopic phases. Evidently, macroscopic statistics as in Chemistry cannot and should not be applied. Taking this serious, fascinating perspectives open for statistical nuclear fragmentation.

# Nuclear statistics, microcanonical or canonical? The physicists vs. the chemists approach

## Key claim

The canonical ensemble is not physically justified for fragmenting nuclei because the system is inhomogeneous and the fragments are too small to be considered macroscopic homogeneous phases; only the microcanonical Boltzmann-Planck statistics is valid for such systems, and this opens the possibility to study statistical mechanics away from the thermodynamic limit.

## Method

The paper compares the foundations of the microcanonical and canonical ensembles, showing that the canonical ensemble is only equivalent to the microcanonical one in the thermodynamic limit (N → ∞ at constant density) for homogeneous phases. It then argues that multi-fragmented nuclei and their individual fragments do not satisfy this limit, and that the canonical ensemble cannot describe systems with negative heat capacity, which occur in nuclear fragmentation and self-gravitating systems.

## Key numbers

- The relative width of the canonical energy for a homogeneous phase α scales as ΔE_α ∝ 1/√N_α, vanishing only in the thermodynamic limit N_α → ∞.
- The canonical heat capacity at constant volume is given by C_α|_(V_α) = (⟨E_α²⟩ − ⟨E_α⟩²)/T_α² ≥ 0, which is always non-negative, preventing description of systems with negative heat capacity by a single common canonical distribution.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous arguments (Gross, cond-mat/0411408 v3; Gross, papers on microcanonical statistics) that the microcanonical ensemble is the fundamental basis of statistical mechanics, following Boltzmann and Planck. It contradicts the application of chemical thermodynamics (CTh) as defined by Guggenheim (1967) to nuclear fragmentation, and argues against the need for non-extensive statistics such as Tsallis statistics (Tsallis, 1988; Tsallis, 1999) for equilibrated non-extensive systems. The paper enables the study of phase transitions in non-homogeneous phases composed of several nuclei, analogous to self-gravitating systems (Thirring, 1970), and allows direct measurement of full statistical distributions event-by-event in nuclear fragmentation experiments.
