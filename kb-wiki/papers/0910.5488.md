# Thermalisation time and specific heat of neutron stars crust

> We discuss the thermalisation process of the neutron stars crust described by solving the heat transport equation with a microscopic input for the specific heat of baryonic matter. The heat equation is solved with initial conditions specific to a rapid cooling of the core. To calculate the specific heat of inner crust baryonic matter, i.e., nuclear clusters and unbound neutrons, we use the quasiparticle spectrum provided by the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approach at finite temperature. In this framework we analyse the dependence of the crust thermalisation on pairing properties and on cluster structure of inner crust matter. It is shown that the pairing correlations reduce the crust thermalisation time by a very large fraction. The calculations show also that the nuclear clusters have a non-negligible influence on the time evolution of the surface temperature of the neutron star.

## Key claim

Pairing correlations reduce the crust thermalisation time by a very large fraction, with the reduction depending on whether the pairing gap corresponds to a BCS scenario (maximum gap about 3 MeV) or to calculations including in-medium effects (maximum gap about 1 MeV); the cluster structure of inner crust matter also has a non-negligible and non-trivial effect on the time evolution of the surface temperature.

## Method

The authors solved the relativistic heat transport equation for the inner crust of a 1.44 solar mass neutron star, using the SLy4 Skyrme interaction for the equation of state and the thermal conductivity parametrization of Lattimer et al. (1994). The specific heat of unbound neutrons was calculated from the quasiparticle spectrum obtained by solving Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov equations at finite temperature in Wigner-Seitz cells taken from Negele and Vautherin (1973), using two pairing forces with strengths V0 = -570 and -430 MeV fm^-3 to simulate strong and weak pairing scenarios respectively.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star mass: 1.44 solar masses; total radius: 11.59 km; central density: 3.51 times the saturation density rho0 = 2.9 x 10^14 g.cm^-3
- Inner crust extends from Rc = 10.74 km (core-crust interface) to 11.29 km, covering densities from rho_drip = 3.285 x 10^11 g.cm^-3 to rho0/2
- For initial crust temperature Ti = 500 keV, the thermalisation time drops from about 75 years (normal-phase neutrons) to about 55 years for weak pairing and to about 30 years for strong pairing
- The constant for fast neutrino emission in the core was taken as Qf = 10^26 erg.cm^-3.s^-1
- Thermal conductivity parametrization: K = C (rho/rho0)^(2/3) with C = 10^21 ergs cm^-1 s^-1 for temperatures above 10^8 K

## Context

This work extends earlier studies by Lattimer et al. (1994), Pizzochero (2001), and Monrozeau et al. (2007) that showed the thermalisation time depends significantly on superfluid properties of inner crust baryonic matter, but uses a more realistic dynamical solution of the heat equation rather than a simple random walk model. The results confirm the qualitative finding of Monrozeau et al. (2007) that pairing scenarios differ by a large fraction, but the quantitative thermalisation times differ substantially from that schematic model. The calculations enable observational discrimination between pairing scenarios through measurements of crust cooling in isolated neutron stars or thermal afterburst relaxation in X-ray transients.
