# Study of the Log-third-difference method for the computation of even-odd staggering in fission yields

> The even-odd staggering in fission fragments yield distributions is an important observable to address the role of intrinsic excitations in the fission process. However, its computation as a function of the fragment split is an ill-posed problem that is usually solved through certain assumptions. Arguably, the most widely used method for its computation is the Log-third-difference method, which assumes an underlying local Gaussian distribution. Experimental data of the even-odd effect show sharp features and peaks that are a challenge for the Log-third difference method (or any method for that matter). This paper presents a detailed study of the performance of the Log-third difference method and describes three strategies for improving the precision and accuracy around these sharp features. The best results are obtained with an iterative application of the method and with the use of a multi-Gaussian fit. In regions with a smooth behaviour, the direct application of the Log-third-difference method remains the best option.

## Key claim

The Log-third-difference method for computing even-odd staggering in fission yields performs well for smooth distributions but systematically underestimates the effect around sharp features such as the peak at Z≈50, with an average error exceeding −0.05 at the peak center; the paper proposes three correction strategies, of which iterative application and multi-Gaussian fitting yield the best improvements, while the direct method remains optimal in smooth regions.

## Method

The authors generated simulated fission fragment yield distributions by summing two asymmetric and one symmetric Gaussian mode, with fissioning system atomic numbers randomly selected between 84 and 101. They independently simulated the even-odd staggering δ_z(Z) as the sum of a parabolic shape, a narrow Gaussian centered at Z=50 (standard deviation 0.5–1.5 Z units), and a wider Gaussian centered between Z=54 and 58 (standard deviation 2–6 Z units). They then applied the Log-third-difference formula and three correction strategies, second-derivative correction, iterative residual removal, and multi-Gaussian fitting, to evaluate accuracy and precision across a large ensemble of simulated distributions.

## Key numbers

- The Log-third-difference formula yields an average error ε^(T*) of the order of 10^(−3) for most of the fragment Z distribution, but underestimates δ_z by more than −0.05 on average at the center of the peak near Z≈50, with precision worse than 0.1 at that peak.
- The iterative correction reduces the average error at Z≈50 from more than −0.07 to −0.01 by the third iteration (i=3), and 95% of cases fall within ε = ±0.1 after the first iteration.
- For the case δ_z=0, the amplitude of ΔG (deviation from Gaussian behavior) is below ~0.01 at symmetry in 95% of cases, and below ~0.003 in the tails.
- The statistical uncertainty of the corrected Log-third-difference formula follows σ_(δ_z^(T*)) ≈ √(5/16) σ_R (1 − δ_z^2), requiring σ_R below 0.018 (corresponding to ~3×10^3 events per Z) to be sensitive to ΔG ~0.01.
- The correlation coefficient ρ between the multi-Gaussian fit result δ_z^G and the iterative result δ_z^(T*(5)) reaches 0.9 around Z=50 and drops to ~0.1 near symmetry.

## Context

This work extends the Log-third-difference method introduced by Tracy et al. for computing local even-odd staggering in fission yields, addressing limitations identified in recent studies by Ramos et al. (2023) that revealed sharp features and narrow peaks in δ_z(Z) distributions. The paper enables more accurate characterization of the even-odd effect near closed shells (particularly Z=50) and in regions of rapid change, which is relevant for understanding the role of intrinsic excitations and shell effects in the fission process as studied by Steinhäuser et al. and Caamaño et al.
