# Comment to the paper D. V. Anchishkin and S. N. Yezhov "Thermalization in Heavy-ion Collisions"

> Here I discuss the major pitfalls and the most severe mistakes of the above mentioned paper. The thorough analysis shows that despite all the claims the present work has nothing to do with the thermalization process in relativistic heavy ion collisions. In contrast to the authors' beliefs I show that their main result is not derived, but is a combination of mathematical mistakes and hand waving arguments.

## Key claim

The paper by Anchishkin and Yezhov does not actually derive a thermalization mechanism for relativistic heavy-ion collisions; their claimed main result is a combination of mathematical mistakes and hand-waving arguments, and their model (the maximal isotropization model) has nothing to do with heavy-ion collisions because it neglects inelastic reactions and particle production.

## Method

Bugaev analyzes the mathematical structure of the maximal isotropization model, focusing on the authors' use of the microcanonical ensemble and their subsequent Laplace transform to the canonical ensemble. He identifies that the authors' approximation in their Eq. (15) produces an exponentially decreasing microcanonical density of states, which leads to anomalous system properties including the possibility of negative temperatures. He further shows that the authors never perform the required inverse Laplace transform back to the microcanonical ensemble, instead inserting the canonical temperature by hand through the partition function.

## Key numbers

- The model's energy range is restricted from above by the pion mass \( m_\pi \approx 140 \) MeV per particle if inelastic reactions are neglected, but the authors apply their model to pions with mean energy up to 800 MeV.
- The authors' approximation (15) produces a microcanonical density of states that is an exponentially decreasing function of particle energy, which allows the existence of negative temperatures (citing Rumer, paragraph 67).

## Context

This comment extends the long-standing critique of oversimplified thermalization models in heavy-ion physics, contrasting with established approaches such as the gluon transport model, hadronic cascade models, and hydro-kinetic approaches (Bugaev, 2002). The author notes that realistic interactions between relativistic particles (relativistic hard-core repulsion) lead to far more complicated momentum distributions than the Boltzmann form (RelVDW), and that non-Boltzmann momentum spectra in high-energy nuclear collisions can arise from collective motion or kinetic freeze-out processes (Collect; FO:1, FO:2, FO:3, FO:4; Bugaev, 2002). The comment enables future work by clarifying that any valid thermalization model must account for particle production and inelastic reactions, and must properly handle the inverse Laplace transform between microcanonical and canonical ensembles (Laplace:1, Laplace:2).
