# How to quantify coherence: Distinguishing speakable and unspeakable notions

> Quantum coherence is a critical resource for many operational tasks. Understanding how to quantify and manipulate it also promises to have applications for a diverse set of problems in theoretical physics. For certain applications, however, one requires coherence between the eigenspaces of specific physical observables, such as energy, angular momentum, or photon number, and it makes a difference which eigenspaces appear in the superposition. For others, there is a preferred set of subspaces relative to which coherence is deemed a resource, but it is irrelevant which of the subspaces appear in the superposition. We term these two types of coherence unspeakable and speakable respectively. We argue that a useful approach to quantifying and characterizing unspeakable coherence is provided by the resource theory of asymmetry when the symmetry group is a group of translations, and we translate a number of prior results on asymmetry into the language of coherence. We also highlight some of the applications of this approach, for instance, in the context of quantum metrology, quantum speed limits, quantum thermodynamics, and NMR. The question of how best to treat speakable coherence as a resource is also considered. We review a popular approach in terms of operations that preserve the set of incoherent states, propose an alternative approach in terms of operations that are covariant under dephasing, and we outline the challenge of providing a physical justification for either approach. Finally, we note some mathematical connections that hold among the different approaches to quantifying coherence.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum coherence must be divided into two distinct types, "unspeakable" coherence (where the specific eigenvalues of the observable matter, as in energy or angular momentum) and "speakable" coherence (where only the set of subspaces matters, not which ones), and that the resource theory of asymmetry under translation groups already provides a complete framework for unspeakable coherence, while speakable coherence requires a separate treatment that remains physically unjustified.

## Method

The authors translate prior results from the resource theory of asymmetry into the language of coherence, specializing to translational symmetry groups (phase shifts, time translations, spatial translations). For unspeakable coherence, they identify the free operations as translationally-covariant operations and the free states as symmetric (incoherent) states. For speakable coherence, they review the Baumgratz-Cramer-Plenio (BCP) approach based on incoherent operations (operations preserving the set of incoherent states) and propose an alternative approach based on operations covariant under dephasing, noting the lack of physical justification for either.

## Key numbers

- The Mandelstam-Tamm bound and Margolus-Levitin bound are identified as upper bounds on the minimum time for a system to evolve to a distinguishable state, related to the amount of coherence between energy eigenstates (no explicit numerical bound given in the text).
- Coherence of order q in NMR is defined as the norm of off-diagonal terms ρ_{m1 m2}|m1⟩⟨m2| with m2 − m1 = q, where |m⟩ are eigenstates of J_z (the magnetic moment operator); no explicit numerical values are provided in the text.
- The paper discusses states |ψ⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 and |ϕ⟩ = (|0⟩ + |2⟩)/√2 as examples distinguishing speakable from unspeakable coherence, but provides no numerical results for these states.

## Context

This work extends the resource theory of asymmetry developed by Gour and Spekkens (2008), Marvian and Spekkens (2013, 2014), and others, translating it into the language of quantum coherence. It contrasts with the approach of Baumgratz, Cramer, and Plenio (2014) for speakable coherence, which the authors criticize for lacking physical justification. The paper enables future work to apply asymmetry measures (such as the Wigner-Yanase-Dyson skew information) as coherence monotones in quantum metrology, quantum thermodynamics, quantum speed limits, and NMR, while highlighting that the BCP approach's measures are not valid for unspeakable coherence tasks.
