# The Hypertriton Puzzle in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> The yields of hadrons and light nuclei in relativistic collisions of heavy-nuclei at a center of mass energy of 2.6 TeV can be described remarkably well by a thermal distribution of an ideal gas of hadrons and light nuclei interacting only via the decay of resonances. Given the particularly small binding energy of hypertritons relative to the temperature describing the yields (about 156 MeV), one might naturally expect hypertrions to dissociate in medium, making the agreement of hypertriton yields with thermal predictions highly puzzling. The puzzle is compounded by the fact that small binding energy is associated with the large size of the hypertriton. This size is on a similar scale to the overall size of the fireball and much larger than the length scale over which temperatures in the fireball vary over phenomenologically relevant amounts. This paper quantifies the tension this effect causes and shows that it is sufficiently large to render the thermal model inconsistent: its natural assumptions are in conflict with its outputs. The possibility that hypertritons are formed at freeze out as compact objects, quark droplets, that subsequently evolve into hypertritons is considered as a way to resolve the puzzle. It is noted that beyond making the assumption that compact quark droplets form, additional detailed dynamical assumptions which have not been justified are needed to make the thermal model work. The issue of why, despite these issues, the hypertriton is well described by a simple statistical description at freeze out is unresolved. Resolving the hypertriton puzzle is important as it may clarify whether the phenomenological success of the simple thermal model for yields accurately reflects the simple picture of the underlying physics on which it is based.

# The Hypertriton Puzzle in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

## Key claim

The thermal model's success in describing hypertriton yields at a chemical freeze-out temperature of 156.5 MeV is internally inconsistent because the hypertriton's large size (approximately 10 fm) and extremely low binding energy (148 keV) cannot be reconciled with the model's own assumptions about the fireball's spatial and temporal structure; the probability that a hypertriton can fit inside the fireball under the model's assumptions is typically less than 25 percent.

## Method

The authors used a 1+1 dimensional boost-invariant hydrodynamic simulation (MIS second-order hydrodynamics implemented in the VH1+1 code) with a Glauber initial profile, shear viscosity to entropy ratio of 0.12, and initial central temperatures of 330, 400, and 500 MeV to model the fireball evolution. They modeled the hypertriton as a two-body bound state of a Lambda and a deuteron using finite square-well potentials, with the Lambda-deuteron potential depth tuned to reproduce the 148 keV binding energy and a radial extent of 2.5 fm. The probability of the hypertriton fitting inside the fireball was computed by comparing the quantum-mechanical wavefunction distribution to the spatial temperature profile of the fireball at the freeze-out hypersurface.

## Key numbers

- Hypertriton binding energy: 148 ± 40 keV (from Bertulani 2022 and other references)
- Chemical freeze-out temperature: 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV (from Andronic 2017)
- Hypertriton rms radius: approximately 10 fm (derived from binding energy)
- Deuteron binding energy: 2.2 MeV (from Van Der Leun 1982)
- Deuteron charge radius: approximately 2.13 fm (from Hernandez 2017)
- Fireball initial central temperatures used: 330 MeV, 400 MeV, and 500 MeV at tau = 1 fm
- Fireball initial radius parameter: R = 6.4 fm with Wood-Saxon parameter a = 0.54 fm
- Volume from statistical hadronization model: 5280 ± 410 fm^3
- Baryon chemical potential: 0.7 ± 3.8 MeV
- Shear viscosity to entropy density ratio: eta/s = 0.12
- Spatial lattice: 1024 points spaced by 0.0123 fm with time step 0.005 fm
- Probability that hypertriton fits inside fireball: typically much less than 25 percent

## Context

This work extends the discussion of the "hypertriton puzzle" first raised by Cai (2019), who noted a causality-based conflict between the hypertriton formation time and its lifetime in the hadron resonance gas. The authors quantify a separate tension: the spatial size of the hypertriton (approximately 10 fm) is comparable to the fireball size and much larger than the length scale over which temperature varies in the fireball, making the statistical hadronization model's assumptions inconsistent with its outputs. Andronic (2017) had speculated that hypertritons might form as compact colorless quark droplets that later evolve into the weakly bound state, but the authors argue this scenario requires additional unjustified dynamical assumptions. The resolution of this puzzle is important for determining whether the phenomenological success of the thermal model reflects the simple physical picture on which it is based.
