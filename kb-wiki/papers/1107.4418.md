# Ultra High Energy Cosmic Rays in Our Universe

> Our Universe is described jointly by Einstein's relativity principle and the quantum principle; there the existence of the smallest units of matter, such as electrons, neutrinos, quarks, and photons, is well established and the smallest units of matter are described by the Standard Model. Based on these, we have a clear picture for the propagation of ultra high energy cosmic rays (UHECR's), for energies greater than $E > 10^{13}\, eV$ but less than $E < 10^{26}\, eV$, in the cosmic medium of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) and of the cosmic background neutrinos (CB$ν$'s). We find that the CMB plays a pivot role in this energy range, so that the observed "knee(s)" and the "ankle" could be understood in reasonable terms. What we might observe at the energy near $10^{25}\,eV$', the so-called "$W^\pm$ bursts" or "$Z^0$ bursts", is briefly discussed. Meanwhile, in the early Universe, the CB$ν$'s, in view of the tiny neutrino mass, gave rise to the clustering phenomenon, resulting in a neutrino halo attached to each visual ordinary-matter object, such as the Earth, the Sun, and stars - with the $PeV$ neutrinos near the surface of the Fermi-Dirac sphere of the neutrino halo of the Earth playing the most fundamental role.

## Key claim

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) and cosmic background neutrinos (CBν's) together determine which particle species can survive as ultra-high-energy cosmic rays (UHECRs) in the energy range \(10^{13}\) eV to \(10^{26}\) eV, with the CMB playing a "pivot role" in explaining the observed "knee(s)" and "ankle" features in the cosmic-ray spectrum, provided the Standard Model is correct and no new physics is needed.

## Method

The authors analyze the propagation of UHECRs through the cosmic media of CMB photons and CBν's by computing thresholds and cross sections for elastic and inelastic reactions (e.g., Compton scattering, pair production, photodisintegration) using Standard Model particle physics and kinematics. They also model neutrino halos as Fermi-Dirac gases attached to ordinary-matter objects (Earth, Sun, stars), with a Fermi momentum \(k_F^\nu \sim 200\) TeV, and examine the reaction \(\nu(\text{Solar}) + \bar{\nu}(\text{CB}; k_F^\nu) \to e^- + e^+\) as a signature of the CBν's.

## Key numbers

- The heaviest neutrino mass is taken as \(0.058\) eV, giving a threshold energy of \(10^{13}\) eV for the free reaction \(\nu(\text{UHE}) + \bar{\nu}(\text{free}) \to e^- + e^+\).
- The Fermi momentum of the neutrino halo is estimated as \(k_F^\nu \sim 200\) TeV, corresponding to surface neutrinos of about \(10^{15}\) eV (PeV).
- For a proton of energy \(10^{22}\) eV interacting with a CMB photon, the center-of-mass energy squared is \(1.3 \times 10^{19}\) eV\(^2\), yielding a cross section \(\sigma \approx 4\) pb and a mean free path \(\lambda_p \approx 2 \times 10^5\) Mpc (exceeding the size of the observable Universe, \(\sim 4500\) Mpc).
- For a proton of energy \(10^{18}\) eV, the mean free path for Compton scattering on CMB photons is \(\lambda_p \approx 20\) Mpc.
- For an electron of energy \(10^{15}\) eV, the cross section for Compton scattering on CMB photons is \(\sigma \approx 0.17\) barns, corresponding to \(\lambda_e \approx 50\) kpc.
- The cross section for \(\nu(\text{Solar}) + \bar{\nu}(\text{CB}; k_F^\nu) \to e^- + e^+\) is estimated as \(\sigma \sim 10^{-27}\) cm\(^2\) if PeV neutrinos from the Fermi-Dirac sphere are present.
- The Earth's mass is given as \(M = 5.794 \times 10^{24}\) kg and radius \(R = 6378\) km; Venus has \(M = 4.87 \times 10^{24}\) kg and \(R = 6051\) km.
- The density of the neutrino halo (assuming five times the ordinary mass distributed uniformly over 2.5 times the radius) is \(\rho = 1.68\) g/cm\(^3 = 5.61 \times 10^{32}\) (eV/c\(^2\))/cm\(^3\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier proposals (Hwang, 2007; Hwang, 2008) that CBν's form neutrino halos around ordinary-matter objects and constitute the 25% dark matter in the Universe, and that black holes do not exist because incompressible neutrino halos prevent gravitational collapse. It also builds on the Standard Model framework (Hwang, 2004) and the PDG compilation (2010) for cosmic-ray data. The paper aims to show that the "knee" and "ankle" features in the cosmic-ray spectrum can be explained by interactions with the CMB and CBν's without invoking new physics, and that IceCube's detection of PeV neutrinos (IceCube Collaboration, 2013) may be evidence for the Fermi-Dirac sphere of the Earth's neutrino halo.
