# Strangeness $S=-1$ hyperon-nucleon interactions: chiral effective field theory vs. lattice QCD

> Hyperon-nucleon interactions serve as basic inputs to studies of hypernuclear physics and dense (neutron) stars. Unfortunately, a precise understanding of these important quantities have lagged far behind that of the nucleon-nucleon interaction due to lack of high precision experimental data. Historically, hyperon-nucleon interactions are either formulated in quark models or meson exchange models. In recent years, lattice QCD simulations and chiral effective field theory approaches start to offer new insights from first principles. In the present work, we contrast the state of art lattice QCD simulations with the latest chiral hyperon-nucleon forces and show that the leading order relativistic chiral results can already describe the lattice QCD data reasonably well. Given the fact that the lattice QCD simulations are performed with pion masses ranging from the (almost) physical point to 700 MeV, such studies provide a highly non-trivial check on both the chiral effective field theory approaches as well as lattice QCD simulations. Nevertheless more precise lattice QCD simulations are eagerly needed to refine our understanding of hyperon-nucleon interactions.

# Strangeness $S=-1$ hyperon-nucleon interactions: chiral effective field theory vs. lattice QCD

## Key claim

The leading order relativistic chiral effective field theory results can describe lattice QCD data reasonably well, but only for small laboratory momenta (below about 300 MeV/c) and for the HAL QCD data at center-of-mass energies below 40 MeV; discrepancies remain at larger momenta and for the NPLQCD data, indicating that higher-order chiral studies are needed.

## Method

The authors used a covariant (relativistic) chiral effective field theory approach with leading order (LO) potentials consisting of non-derivative four-baryon contact terms and one-pseudoscalar-meson exchange terms, solving the coupled-channel Kadyshevsky equation. They extrapolated the hyperon-nucleon interactions to unphysical pion masses by fixing the pion mass dependence of the kaon/eta and octet baryon masses using LO and next-to-leading order (NLO) chiral perturbation theory fits to lattice QCD data from the PACS-CS and LHPC collaborations. The resulting phase shifts were compared with three lattice QCD simulations: the NPLQCD collaboration's $\Lambda n$ and $\Sigma^- n$ data at pion masses of 354, 493, and 592 MeV, the HAL QCD collaboration's $\Lambda N$ data at pion masses of 570 and 700 MeV, and the HAL QCD collaboration's $\Sigma N$ (I=3/2) data at an almost physical pion mass of about 146 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The LO relativistic chiral EFT with a cutoff of 600 MeV achieved a $\chi^2$ of about 16 for 20 degrees of freedom in fitting experimental $\Lambda N$ and $\Sigma N$ cross sections; cutoffs ranging from 550 to 850 MeV all yield $\chi^2$/d.o.f. smaller than 1.
- The NPLQCD $\Lambda n$ $^1S_0$ scattering length at $m_\pi = 354$ MeV is $1.04 \pm 0.24 \pm 0.115$ fm; at $m_\pi = 493$ MeV it is $0.63 \pm 0.12 \pm 0.014$ fm; at $m_\pi = 592$ MeV it is $0.8 \pm 1.4 \pm 0.4$ fm.
- The HAL QCD $\Lambda N$ $^1S_0$ scattering length at $m_\pi = 700(1)$ MeV is $0.83(27)$ fm; at $m_\pi = 570(1)$ MeV it is $0.39(17)$ fm.
- The HAL QCD $\Sigma N$ (I=3/2) simulations were performed at an almost physical pion mass of about 146 MeV, with rather large statistical uncertainties.
- The ChEFT phase shifts agree with HAL QCD data for $E_{\text{cm}} < 40$ MeV, corresponding to $P_{\text{lab}} \le 506$ MeV/c (at $m_\pi = 570$ MeV) or $527$ MeV/c (at $m_\pi = 700$ MeV).

## Context

This work extends the relativistic chiral EFT approach previously applied to nucleon-nucleon interactions by Ren et al. (2016) and to strangeness $S=-1$ hyperon-nucleon systems by Li et al. (2016). It contrasts these results with lattice QCD simulations from the NPLQCD collaboration (Beane et al., 2006) and the HAL QCD collaboration (Miyamoto et al., 2016; Nemura et al., 2017). The study provides a nontrivial check on both chiral EFT and lattice QCD approaches, and the authors note that more precise lattice QCD simulations are needed to refine understanding of hyperon-nucleon interactions, particularly to distinguish between different theoretical models such as the Juelich04 and NSC97f potentials.
