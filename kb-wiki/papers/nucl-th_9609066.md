# Temperature determination from the lattice gas model

> Determination of temperature from experimental data has become important in searches for critical phenomena in heavy ion collisions. Widely used methods are ratios of isotopes (which rely on chemical and thermal equilibrium), population ratios of excited states etc. Using the lattice gas model we propose a new observable: $n_{ch}/Z$ where $n_{ch}$ is the charge multiplicity and $Z$ is the charge of the fragmenting system. We show that the reduced multiplicity is a good measure of the average temperature of the fragmenting system.

# Temperature determination from the lattice gas model

## Key claim
The reduced charged particle multiplicity \(m = n_{ch}/Z\) (where \(n_{ch}\) is the charge multiplicity and \(Z\) is the charge of the fragmenting system) is proposed as a thermometer for fragmenting systems in heavy ion collisions, provided the lattice gas model is valid and the freeze-out density is known; the method is largely independent of system size but the temperature–multiplicity relation depends on freeze-out density.

## Method
The authors performed Monte Carlo simulations of the lattice gas model with an attractive neutron–proton bond (\(\epsilon_{np} = \epsilon\)) and slightly repulsive neutron–neutron and proton–proton bonds (\(\epsilon_{nn} = \epsilon_{pp} = -\epsilon/5\)), using \(\epsilon \approx -5.33\) MeV from infinite nuclear matter binding energy considerations. They simulated fragmenting systems of two sizes (A=137, Z=57 and A=87, Z=37) at freeze-out densities of \(0.26\rho_0\) and \(0.4\rho_0\), where \(\rho_0\) is normal nuclear matter density, and extracted the reduced multiplicity \(m = n_{ch}/Z\) as a function of temperature. A parametrization \(T/T_c = a \ln\left( b\sqrt{m} / (1-\sqrt{m}) \right)\) was fitted to the Monte Carlo results, and the model predictions were compared to experimental data from the Au+C system at 1 GeV/A incident energy from Hauger et al. (1996).

## Key numbers
- For the Au+C system at 1 GeV/A, the empirical parametrization relating temperature to reduced multiplicity uses \(a = 1/5.3\), \(b = 40\), and \(T_c = 8.3\) MeV.
- The thermal critical temperature in the lattice gas model is \(T_c = -1.1275\epsilon\), with \(\epsilon \approx -5.33\) MeV for infinite nuclear matter.
- Two freeze-out densities were used: \(0.26\rho_0\) (light fragmenting systems) and \(0.4\rho_0\) (medium fragmenting systems).
- The parametrization agrees with Monte Carlo simulations up to \(m = 0.8\).
- For the Au+C data, the relations \(n_{ch} = 0.3 n_{tot}\) and \(Z = -0.68 n_{tot} + 80.5\) were used to convert total multiplicity \(n_{tot}\) to reduced multiplicity.
- Good agreement between data and calculations was found over the region \(n_{ch}/Z = 0.1\) to 0.4.

## Context
This work extends the lattice gas model approach of Das Gupta and collaborators (1995, 1996) to propose a new thermometer based on reduced multiplicity, complementing existing methods such as double isotope ratios (Albergo et al., 1985) and excited-state population ratios. The method addresses ambiguities in isotope-ratio thermometers arising from sequential decay and chemical potential effects, as discussed by Hauger et al. (1996) and others. The extracted parametrization enables future estimation of critical exponents in searches for the nuclear liquid–gas phase transition, and the comparison with Au+C data at 1 GeV/A shows that the model reproduces experimental temperatures when \(T_c\) is treated as an adjustable parameter.
