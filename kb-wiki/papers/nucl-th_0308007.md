# Structure of cold nuclear matter at subnuclear densities by Quantum Molecular Dynamics

> Structure of cold nuclear matter at subnuclear densities for the proton fraction $x=0.5$, 0.3 and 0.1 is investigated by quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations. We demonstrate that the phases with slablike and rodlike nuclei, etc. can be formed dynamically from hot uniform nuclear matter without any assumptions on nuclear shape, and also systematically analyze the structure of cold matter using two-point correlation functions and Minkowski functionals. In our simulations, we also observe intermediate phases, which has complicated nuclear shapes. It is found out that these phases can be characterized as those with negative Euler characteristic. Our result implies the existence of these kinds of phases in addition to the simple ``pasta'' phases in neutron star crusts and supernova inner cores. In addition, we investigate the properties of the effective QMD interaction used in the present work to examine the validity of our results. The resultant energy per nucleon $ε_{n}$ of the pure neutron matter, the proton chemical $μ_{p}^{(0)}$ in pure neutron matter and the nuclear surface tension $E_{\rm surf}$ are generally reasonable in comparison with other nuclear interactions.

## Key claim

Using quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations without any assumptions on nuclear shape, the authors demonstrate that the "pasta" phases (slablike, rodlike nuclei, etc.) can form dynamically from hot uniform nuclear matter, and they also observe intermediate phases with complicated nuclear shapes characterized by negative Euler characteristic, implying these phases exist in addition to simple pasta phases in neutron star crusts and supernova inner cores.

## Method

The authors performed QMD simulations of infinite (n,p,e) systems with fixed proton fractions x=0.5, 0.3, and 0.1 at various nucleon densities (0.05-1.0 ρ₀), using 2048 nucleons (1372 in some cases) for x=0.5 and 0.3, and 10976 nucleons for x=0.1, in cubic boxes with periodic boundary conditions. They first prepared a uniform hot nucleon gas at kBT~20 MeV, then cooled it slowly over O(10³-10⁴) fm/c using frictional relaxation until the temperature reached ~0.1 MeV or less, with relativistic degenerate electrons treated as a uniform background and Coulomb interactions calculated by the Ewald method.

## Key numbers

- For x=0.5: sphere phase at 0.1 ρ₀ (box size 43.65 fm, N=1372); cylinder phase at 0.225 ρ₀ (box size 38.07 fm, N=2048); slab phase at 0.4 ρ₀ (box size 31.42 fm, N=2048); cylindrical hole phase at 0.5 ρ₀ (box size 29.17 fm, N=2048); spherical hole phase at 0.6 ρ₀ (box size 27.45 fm, N=2048)
- For x=0.3: sphere phase at 0.1 ρ₀ (box size 49.88 fm, N=2048); cylinder phase at 0.18 ρ₀ (box size 41.01 fm, N=2048); slab phase at 0.35 ρ₀ (box size 32.85 fm, N=2048); cylindrical hole phase at 0.5 ρ₀ (box size 29.17 fm, N=2048); spherical hole phase at 0.55 ρ₀ (box size 28.26 fm, N=2048)
- For x=0.1: sphere phase at 0.075 ρ₀ (box size 96.08 fm); cylinder phase at 0.2 ρ₀ (box size 69.29 fm); wide intermediate phase from ~0.25 ρ₀ to ~0.525 ρ₀
- The exchange energy between nucleons in different nuclei at internuclear distance R~10 fm and binding energy E_i~8 MeV is estimated as K~5×10⁻⁶ MeV, much smaller than typical energy differences between pasta phases (0.1 keV for neutron star matter to 10 keV for supernova matter)
- The normal nuclear density ρ₀ = 0.165 fm⁻³

## Context

This work extends the original "pasta" phase predictions by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Hashimoto et al. (1984), which were based on free energy calculations with liquid drop models assuming specific nuclear shapes, and the Thomas-Fermi calculations by Williams and Koonin (1985) and Lassaut et al. (1987) that could not incorporate fluctuations of nucleon distributions at a satisfying level. The QMD approach enables the study of formation and melting processes of nonspherical nuclei without assumptions on nuclear shape, and the results imply the existence of intermediate phases with complicated structures (characterized by negative Euler characteristic) in addition to the simple pasta phases in neutron star crusts and supernova inner cores, which could affect neutrino transport, hydrodynamics, and elastic properties of inner crust matter.
