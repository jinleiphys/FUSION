# Spectral Functions from the Functional Renormalization Group

> We present results for in-medium spectral functions obtained within the Functional Renormalization Group framework. The analytic continuation from imaginary to real time is performed in a well-defined way on the level of the flow equations. Based on this recently developed method, results for the sigma and the pion spectral function for the quark-meson model are shown at finite temperature, finite quark-chemical potential and finite spatial momentum. It is shown how these spectral function become degenreate at high temperatures due to the restoration of chiral symmetry. In addition, results for vector- and axial-vector meson spectral functions are shown using a gauged linear sigma model with quarks. The degeneration of the $ρ$ and the $a_1$ spectral function as well as the behavior of their pole masses is discussed.

# Spectral Functions from the Functional Renormalization Group

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that in-medium spectral functions for sigma, pion, rho, and a1 mesons can be obtained from the Functional Renormalization Group by performing analytic continuation on the level of the flow equations, but the results are limited by the use of low-energy effective models (quark-meson model and gauged linear sigma model) that do not incorporate confinement, leading to quark-antiquark threshold artifacts in the spectral functions.

## Method
The authors used the Functional Renormalization Group (FRG) framework with the Wetterich equation to compute scale-dependent effective actions. They performed a two-step analytic continuation procedure: first analytically evaluating Matsubara sums using three-dimensional regulator functions, then continuing the resulting flow equations for two-point functions from imaginary to real energies by replacing the discrete Euclidean energy p0 with a continuous real energy omega. The spectral functions were then obtained from the imaginary part of the retarded propagator at the infrared scale k=0. For the quark-meson model, the local potential approximation (LPA) was used, while for the vector meson sector, a gauged linear sigma model with quarks was employed, separating transverse and longitudinal components relative to the heat bath.

## Key numbers
- In the quark-meson model vacuum, the pion decay constant f_pi = 93.5 MeV, pion mass m_pi = 138 MeV, sigma mass m_sigma = 509 MeV, and constituent quark mass m_psi = 299 MeV (UV cutoff Lambda = 1000 MeV)
- The chiral crossover transition occurs at T approximately 170 MeV for zero chemical potential
- The chiral critical endpoint (CEP) is located at T_c approximately 9 MeV and mu approximately 292 MeV
- In the gauged linear sigma model vacuum, f_pi = 93.0 MeV, m_pi = 140 MeV, m_sigma = 557 MeV, m_psi = 300 MeV, rho pole mass = 789 MeV, and a1 pole mass = 1275 MeV (UV cutoff Lambda = 1500 MeV)
- The rho and a1 pole masses become degenerate at T approximately 200 MeV

## Context
This work extends the FRG-based analytic continuation method developed by Tripolt et al. (2013) and Kamikado et al. (2013) to compute spectral functions at finite temperature, chemical potential, and spatial momentum. The results for vector meson spectral functions are consistent with the "broadening-rho" scenario from phenomenological approaches and supported by experimental data from Rapp (1999) and Damjanovic (2007). The framework enables future calculations of dilepton rates and extensions to baryonic degrees of freedom, though it currently lacks confinement and wave-function renormalization effects.
