# N* Masses from an Anisotropic Lattice QCD Action

> We report N* masses in the spin 3/2 sector from a highly-improved anisotropic action. States with both positive and negative parity are isolated via a parity projection method. The extent to which spin projection is needed is examined. The gross features of the splittings from the nucleon ground state show a trend consistent with experimental results at the quark masses explored.

# N* Masses from an Anisotropic Lattice QCD Action

## Key claim
The gross features of the splittings from the nucleon ground state for spin-3/2 N* states show a trend consistent with experimental results at the quark masses explored, but the authors state that more study is needed to address systematic errors before definitive conclusions can be drawn.

## Method
The authors used a highly-improved anisotropic D234 quark action with tadpole-improved tree-level coefficients on a 10^3 x 30 lattice with spatial spacing a_s ~ 0.24 fm and anisotropy xi = a_s/a_t = 3. They computed quark propagators on 100 configurations using a multi-mass solver with quark masses ranging from approximately 780 to 90 MeV, corresponding to pion-to-rho mass ratios pi/rho from 0.95 to 0.65. A parity projection method was applied to isolate positive- and negative-parity states, and spin projection was used to separate spin-3/2 from spin-1/2 contributions in the correlation functions.

## Key numbers
- Lattice size: 10^3 x 30 with a_s ~ 0.24 fm and anisotropy xi = a_s/a_t = 3
- 100 configurations analyzed
- 9 quark propagators per configuration, with quark masses from approximately 780 to 90 MeV
- Corresponding mass ratio pi/rho from 0.95 to 0.65
- Source located at (x,y,z,t) = (2,2,2,2)

## Context
This work extends previous lattice QCD studies of the N* spectrum, which focused mostly on the spin-1/2 sector (Derek 1995, Lee 1999, Lee 2000, Sasaki 2000, Dong 2000, Dong 2001), into the spin-3/2 sector with isospin 1/2. The results enable future systematic studies of excited baryon spectroscopy on the lattice, particularly for understanding parity splitting patterns that are a direct manifestation of spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in QCD. The authors note that the mass ratio plots appear headed in the right direction compared to experimental values, but systematic errors remain to be addressed.
