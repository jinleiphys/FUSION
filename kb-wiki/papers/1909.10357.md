# Consistency checks for two-body finite-volume matrix elements: I. Conserved currents and bound states

> Recently, a framework has been developed to study form factors of two-hadron states probed by an external current. The method is based on relating finite-volume matrix elements, computed using numerical lattice QCD, to the corresponding infinite-volume observables. As the formalism is complicated, it is important to provide non-trivial checks on the final results and also to explore limiting cases in which more straightforward predications may be extracted. In this work we provide examples on both fronts. First, we show that, in the case of a conserved vector current, the formalism ensures that the finite-volume matrix element of the conserved charge is volume-independent and equal to the total charge of the two-particle state. Second, we study the implications for a two-particle bound state. We demonstrate that the infinite-volume limit reproduces the expected matrix element and derive the leading finite-volume corrections to this result for a scalar current. Finally, we provide numerical estimates for the expected size of volume effects in future lattice QCD calculations of the deuteron's scalar charge. We find that these effects completely dominate the infinite-volume result for realistic lattice volumes and that applying the present formalism, to analytically remove an infinite-series of leading volume corrections, is crucial to reliably extract the infinite-volume charge of the state.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the finite-volume formalism for two-hadron matrix elements satisfies two nontrivial consistency checks: for a conserved vector current, the finite-volume matrix element of the charge is exactly volume-independent and equals the sum of constituent charges; for a two-particle bound state, the infinite-volume limit reproduces the expected matrix element, but the leading finite-volume corrections scale as \(e^{-\kappa_{\text{B}} L}\) and can dominate the infinite-volume result for realistic lattice volumes, making the full formalism essential for reliable extraction of the bound-state charge.

## Method

The authors analytically test the general finite-volume formalism of Briceño (2015) and Baroni (2018) by applying it to two special cases. First, they use the Ward-Takahashi identity to show that the finite-volume matrix element of a conserved vector current reduces to the total charge of the two-particle state, independent of the volume size \(L\). Second, they analytically continue the formalism below two-particle threshold to study an \(S\)-wave bound state, deriving the leading finite-volume corrections to the matrix element for a scalar current and providing numerical estimates for the deuteron's scalar charge using realistic lattice parameters.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy: \(m_n + m_p - M_{d} \approx 2.2\) MeV.
- Binding momentum \(\kappa_{\text{B}}\) defined by \(\kappa_{\text{B}}^2 = m^2 - M_{\text{B}}^2/4\), where \(m\) is the constituent mass and \(M_{\text{B}}\) the bound-state mass.
- Leading finite-volume corrections scale as \(e^{-\kappa_{\text{B}} L}\), while the full formalism holds up to corrections of order \(e^{-m L}\).
- For realistic lattice volumes in the range \(mL \approx 4 - 7\), the \(e^{-\kappa_{\text{B}} L}\) corrections can become comparable in size with the infinite-volume result, dominating systematic uncertainties.

## Context

This work extends the finite-volume formalism for two-hadron matrix elements developed by Briceño (2015) and Baroni (2018), providing essential consistency checks. It directly addresses the challenge of extracting bound-state properties from lattice QCD, where finite-volume effects become a dominant systematic uncertainty for loosely bound states like the deuteron as calculations approach physical quark masses. The results motivate applying the full formalism to remove binding-momentum-enhanced finite-volume artifacts, analogous to how the Lüscher formalism has been used for spectroscopy in works such as Francis (2018) and Berkowitz (2015).
