# Composition and stability of hybrid stars with hyperons and quark color-superconductivity

> The recent measurement of a $1.97\pm 0.04$ solar-mass pulsar places a stringent lower bound on the maximum mass of compact stars and therefore challenges the existence of any agents that soften the equation of state of ultra-dense matter. We investigate whether hyperons and/or quark matter can be accommodated in massive compact stars by constructing an equation of state based on a combination of phenomenological relativistic hyper-nuclear density functional and an effective model of quantum chromodynamics (the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model). Stable configurations are obtained with $M \ge 1.97 M_{\sun}$ featuring hyper-nuclear and quark matter in color superconducting state if the equation of state of nuclear matter is stiff above the saturation density, the transition to quark matter takes place at a few times the nuclear saturation density, and the repulsive vector interactions in quark matter are substantial.

## Key claim

Stable hybrid stars with masses at or above 1.97 solar masses can contain both hyperons and color-superconducting quark matter, but only if three conditions are simultaneously met: the nuclear equation of state is stiff above saturation density, the transition to quark matter occurs at a few times nuclear saturation density, and the repulsive vector interactions in quark matter are substantial.

## Method

The authors constructed an equation of state combining a relativistic mean-field nuclear density functional (Walecka-type Lagrangian with the NL3 parameterization) for hypernuclear matter with the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model for quark matter, including the t' Hooft interaction and vector interactions. They used the Maxwell construction to match the hadronic and quark phases at a transition density controlled by an effective bag constant, and solved the Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain sequences of non-rotating spherically symmetric stellar configurations. The quark matter was considered in the two-flavor 2SC phase and the three-flavor color-flavor-locked (CFL) phase.

## Key numbers

- The pulsar mass measurement providing the observational constraint is 1.97 ± 0.04 solar masses (Demorest et al. 2010).
- Stable configurations with M ≥ 1.97 solar masses were obtained featuring hypernuclear and quark matter in color superconducting state.
- The nucleon-meson couplings used are g_σN/m_σ = 3.967 fm, g_ωN/m_ω = 3.244 fm, g_ρN/m_ρ = 1.157 fm; hyperon-meson couplings are obtained by multiplying by factors 0.6, 0.658, and 0.6 respectively.
- The self-interaction couplings of the σ-field are b = 0.002055 and c = -0.002651.
- NJL model parameters: m_u,d = 5.5 MeV, m_s = 140.7 MeV, Λ = 602.3 MeV, G_SΛ^2 = 1.835, KΛ^5 = 12.36, G_D/G_S = 1.
- Varying the effective bag constant B* in the range -40 to 50 MeV fm^{-3} gives transition densities in the range 2.4ρ_0 to 4ρ_0, where ρ_0 is nuclear saturation density.
- For a transition density of ρ_tr = 2.5ρ_0, the 2SC phase extends up to about 1/3 of the star radius for soft quark matter (G_V = 0); for the strongest coupling studied (G_V = 0.6 G_S), the combined 2SC and CFL paired quark matter is confined within about half the star radius.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of hybrid stars containing quark matter in color-superconducting phases, particularly addressing the challenge posed by the 1.97 solar mass pulsar measurement (Demorest et al. 2010). Prior work using the NJL model had found that stable stars in the CFL phase could not be obtained with standard parameterizations (Buballa 2003), while later studies obtained stable sequences for 2SC and CFL matter (Klahn et al. 2006, Alford et al. 2007) and crystalline color-superconducting stars with masses around 2 solar masses (Ippolito et al. 2008). The present work systematically explores the parameter space of vector coupling strength and transition density, demonstrating that hyperons and color-superconducting quark matter can coexist in stars meeting the observational mass constraint, and enabling future searches for even more massive pulsars that would challenge current dense matter models.
