# A Test of the Eikonal Approximation in High-Energy (e,e'p) Scattering

> The Glauber method is extensively used to describe the motion of a hadronic projectile in interaction with the surrounding nuclear medium. One of the main approximations consists in the linearization of the wave equation for the interacting particle. We have studied the consequences of such an assumption in the case of the $^{12}\mathrm{C(e,e}'\mathrm{p)}^{11}\hbox{\rm B}^*$ reaction at high proton momenta by comparing the results with the predictions obtained when all the ingredients of the calculation are unchanged but the second-order differential equation for the scattered wave, which is solved exactly for each partial wave up to a maximum of 120 spherical harmonics. We find that the Glauber cross section is always larger by a factor $10 ÷20 \%$, even at vanishing missing momenta. We give a quantum-mechanical explanation of this discrepancy. Nevertheless, a good correlation is found between the two predictions as functions of the missing momentum, especially in parallel kinematics.

## Key claim

The Glauber (eikonal) approximation systematically overestimates the (e,e'p) cross section by 10–20% compared to the exact partial-wave solution, even at vanishing missing momenta, due to the linearization of the wave equation which neglects reflected flux and its destructive interference with the incoming wave.

## Method

The authors compared two methods for computing the distorted spectral density \(S^{\mathrm{D}}_{\alpha}\) for the \(^{12}\mathrm{C(e,e'p)}^{11}\mathrm{B}^*\) reaction at proton momenta up to 2 GeV/c and momentum transfer \(q = 1.4\) GeV/c. Method I solved the Schrödinger equation exactly by partial-wave expansion up to \(L_{\mathrm{max}} = 120\) spherical harmonics; method II used the Glauber (eikonal) approximation which linearizes the wave equation along the propagation axis. Both methods employed the same Woods-Saxon optical potential (with various choices of real and imaginary parts \(U, W\)) and the same bound-state wave function, and the Coulomb potential was omitted.

## Key numbers

- The Glauber cross section is always larger by a factor of 10–20% compared to the exact partial-wave result, even at vanishing missing momenta.
- The partial-wave expansion was performed up to a maximum of \(L_{\mathrm{max}} = 120\) spherical harmonics; results were stable already with \(L_{\mathrm{max}} = 100\).
- The study covered proton momenta \(p\) in the range 0.6–2 GeV/c and missing momenta \(p_{\mathrm{m}} = p - q\) from 0 to 600 MeV/c.
- The momentum transfer was fixed at \(q = 1.4\) GeV/c.
- The optical potential used a Woods-Saxon form with radius \(R = 1.2 \times A^{1/3}\) fm and diffuseness \(a = 0.5\) fm.
- The Fermi momentum for carbon is 221 MeV/c, corresponding to \(\gamma \sim 10^\circ\) in the angular distributions shown.

## Context

This work extends the study of the Glauber approximation's validity from low proton momenta (below 0.3 GeV/c, as in Boffi et al. (1993)) to the high-momentum regime (1–2 GeV/c) relevant for experiments at CEBAF. It directly addresses the systematic overestimation observed in the NE18 experiment (1994) and provides a quantum-mechanical explanation for the discrepancy. The results enable improved analysis of exclusive (e,e'p) reactions at high missing momenta, where final-state interactions produce a diffractive pattern that requires a completely exclusive measurement to be observed, in contrast to semi-inclusive measurements like those of the NE18 collaboration.
