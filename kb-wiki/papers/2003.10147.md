# The observation of vibrating pear shapes in radon nuclei: update

> There is a large body of evidence that atomic nuclei can undergo octupole distortion and assume the shape of a pear. This phenomenon is important for measurements of electric-dipole moments of atoms, which would indicate CP violation and hence probe physics beyond the standard model of particle physics. Isotopes of both radon and radium have been identified as candidates for such measurements. Here, we have observed the low-lying quantum states in $^{224}$Rn and $^{226}$Rn by accelerating beams of these radioactive nuclei. We report here additional states not assigned in our 2019 publication. We show that radon isotopes undergo octupole vibrations but do not possess static pear-shapes in their ground states. We conclude that radon atoms provide less favourable conditions for the enhancement of a measurable atomic electric-dipole moment.

## Key claim
Radon isotopes (²²⁴Rn and ²²⁶Rn) undergo octupole vibrations but do not possess static pear-shapes in their ground states, making them less favourable for enhancing a measurable atomic electric-dipole moment than previously thought. The authors hedge that while radon atoms have been proposed as candidates for EDM searches, the octupole vibrational character means parity doublets in odd-mass neighbours will not be closely spaced in energy.

## Method
The authors produced radioactive beams of ²²⁴Rn and ²²⁶Rn via spallation at CERN ISOLDE, accelerated them to 5.08 MeV per nucleon (about 3% below the Coulomb barrier), and bombarded ¹²⁰Sn targets. Gamma-rays from Coulomb excitation were detected using the Miniball array of 24 high-purity germanium detectors, while scattered ions were detected in a segmented silicon detector. Gamma-gamma coincidence analysis was used to assign energy levels and construct level schemes for both isotopes.

## Context
This work extends the authors' 2019 publication (Butler et al., 2019) by reporting additional energy levels in ²²⁴Rn and ²²⁶Rn, including tentative assignments for negative-parity states. The results contrast with the radium isotopes ²²⁴Ra (Gaffney et al., 2013) and ²²⁶Ra (Wollersheim et al., 1993), which show clear signatures of static octupole deformation. The finding that radon isotopes are octupole vibrators rather than static pear-shapes means that odd-mass radon nuclei (such as ²²³Rn) will have parity doublets separated by approximately 500 keV rather than the ~55 keV seen in ²²⁵Ra, making radon less promising for EDM enhancement than previously assumed by theoretical calculations (Dzuba et al., 2002; Dobaczewski et al., 2018) and experimental planning (Tardiff et al., 2008; Rand et al., 2011).
