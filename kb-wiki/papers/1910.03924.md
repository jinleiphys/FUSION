# Density functional theory for nuclear fission -- a proposal

> The fission process is a fascinating phenomenon in which the atomic nucleus, a compact self-bound mesoscopic system, undergoes a spontaneous or induced quantum transition into two or more fragments. A predictive, accurate and precise description of nuclear fission, rooted in a fundamental quantum many-body theory, is one of the biggest challenges in science. Current approaches assume adiabatic motion of the system with internal degrees of freedom at thermal equilibrium. With parameters adjusted to data, such modelling works well in describing fission lifetimes, fragment mass distributions, or their total kinetic energies. However, are the assumptions valid? For the fission occurring at higher energies and/or shorter times, the process is bound to be non-adiabatic and/or non-thermal. The vision of this project is to go beyond these approximations, and to obtain a unified description of nuclear fission at varying excitation energies. The key elements of this project are the use of nuclear density functional theory with novel, nonlocal density functionals and innovative high-performance computing techniques. Altogether, the project aims at better understanding of nuclear fission, where slow, collective, and semi-classical effects are intertwined with fast, microscopic, quantum evolution.

# Density functional theory for nuclear fission – a proposal

## Key claim
The project proposes to go beyond the standard adiabatic and thermal assumptions in nuclear fission modeling by using nuclear density functional theory with novel nonlocal density functionals and time-dependent methods, aiming to determine whether and under what conditions the adiabatic and thermal approximations hold for fission at varying excitation energies.

## Method
The work plan is divided into three work packages: (WP1) solving static superfluid DFT equations with novel nonlocal functionals in a two-centre 3D harmonic-oscillator basis, determining multi-dimensional fission paths, and implementing Langevin stochastic evolution; (WP2) solving linear-response equations for arbitrary multipolarity, developing time-dependent DFT (TDDFT) with adaptive time-dependent bases, and treating adiabatic/diabatic evolution in odd fermion systems; (WP3) coupling TDDFT evolution to two-quasiparticle correlations at varying excitation energies and linking to instanton solutions in classically forbidden regions.

## Key numbers
- For one fission path in 240Pu, existing 3D space-lattice algorithms require about 16,000 GPU hours for local-density-approximation calculations (Bul16).
- A full 3D space-lattice implementation for a nonlocal functional would require about three orders of magnitude more resources than for the LDA (Dob09), making it practically impossible at present.
- Spontaneous-fission lifetimes of odd nuclei are about five orders of magnitude longer than those of their even-even neighbours (Hof89).

## Context
This proposal extends the standard Bohr-Wheeler (1939) compound-nucleus framework and the adiabatic approaches used in most current fission modeling (Sch13, And18). It aims to address the validity of adiabaticity and thermalisation assumptions that underlie works such as Dub08, Pei09, Ich12, Mol12, War12, McD14, Rod14, Sch14, Sch15, Giu14, Zha15, Bar15, Lem15, Sad16, Pas16, And16, and Tan17. The project builds on existing DFT solvers like HFODD (Sch17, Dob19) and the two-centre Gogny solver (Ber80, Gou05, Dub08), and aims to enable a unified description of fission from low to high excitation energies, including non-adiabatic and non-thermal regimes.
