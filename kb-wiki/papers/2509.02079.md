# Doppler shifted Hawking radiation from acoustic black holes in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> In a hydrodynamic flow, with flow becoming supersonic at some point, the subsonic-supersonic boundary behaves as the horizon of a black hole. Possibility of detecting Hawking radiation from such acoustic black holes has been investigated in a variety of laboratory systems, ranging from cold atom systems, to condensed matter systems with hydrodynamic flow of electrons, to relativistic heavy-ion collisions (at relatively lower collision energies). Ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions, with boost-invariant longitudinal flow of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) in a wide rapidity window has eluded this remarkable possibility because in this case the black hole horizon is dynamical, moving away from center with sound velocity, leading to infinite red shift of Hawking radiation. We show here that such a conclusion is premature. The QGP flow at very large rapidities, necessarily deviates from Bjroken boost invariant flow. Due to this, an observer close to that region sees black hole horizon with a finite redshift. It leads to non-trivial prediction of Hawking radiation affecting particle momentum distributions for a window of rapidities, leaving near central rapidity regions unaffected.

## Key claim

Even in ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions with boost-invariant longitudinal flow in a wide rapidity window, Hawking radiation from acoustic black holes can be observable because the QGP flow at very large rapidities necessarily deviates from Bjorken boost-invariant flow, allowing an observer at non-zero rapidity to see the acoustic horizon with a finite redshift rather than an infinite one.

## Method

The authors use digitized results from hydrodynamic simulations of Au-Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV (Bozek, 2009) to obtain fluid rapidity as a function of space-time rapidity and proper time. For a given observer at space-time rapidity η₀ and proper time τ₀, they compute the fluid velocity, locate the acoustic horizon where fluid velocity equals the speed of sound (c_s = 1/√3), and calculate the recession velocity of the horizon relative to the observer. The Hawking temperature is then computed from the velocity gradient at the horizon, modified by a Doppler redshift factor derived from the horizon recession velocity.

## Key numbers

- For Bjorken boost-invariant flow, the acoustic horizon recession velocity is 1.03 c_s, leading to infinite redshift (f_R = ∞) for all observer locations η₀ = 0.0, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0 at τ₀ = 2.0 fm/c.
- For non-boost-invariant flow from hydrodynamic simulations at √s = 200 GeV, the redshift factor f_R ranges from 1.47 to 3.19 for observer locations η₀ = 0.0 to 3.0 at τ₀ = 2.0 fm/c.
- The Hawking temperature including Doppler redshift (T^R_H) varies from 5.09 MeV to 8.73 MeV for observer locations η₀ = 0.0 to 3.0 at τ₀ = 2.0 fm/c, compared to static Hawking temperatures (T^0_H) of 12.83 to 16.26 MeV.
- The ratio v_H/c_s decreases from 0.91 to 0.73 as η₀ increases from 0.0 to 3.0.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of acoustic black holes in heavy-ion collisions from Das (2020), which was restricted to lower collision energies where boost invariance is absent. It contradicts the previous expectation that ultra-relativistic collisions with boost-invariant flow would produce infinitely redshifted, unobservable Hawking radiation (Ganguly, 2019; Nielsen, 2008; Nielsen, 2005). The results enable predictions of Hawking radiation affecting particle transverse momentum distributions in specific rapidity windows at LHC energies, with central rapidity regions remaining unaffected, and suggest that correlations between perturbed distributions on both sides of the acoustic horizon warrant further investigation.
