# A mathematical model to describe the alpha dose rate from a UO2 surface

> A model to determine the dose rate of a planar alpha-emitting surface, has been developed. The approach presented is a computationally efficient mathematical model using stopping range data from the Stopping Ranges of Ions in Matter (SRIM) software. The alpha dose rates as a function of distance from irradiated UO2 spent fuel surfaces were produced for bench-marking with previous modelling attempts. This method is able to replicate a Monte Carlo (MCNPX) study of an irradiated UO2 fuel surface within 0.6 % of the resulting total dose rate and displays a similar dose profile.

## Key claim
The paper presents a computationally efficient mathematical model for calculating alpha dose rates from planar UO₂ surfaces that replicates a full Monte Carlo (MCNPX) study within 0.6% of the total dose rate, but the authors identify that a prior analytical model by Hansson et al. (2020) overestimates the average probability of alpha escape by using a 2D geometrical assumption (Pα = 1/π) instead of the correct 3D value (Pα = 1/4).

## Method
The model uses stopping range data from the SRIM software to construct linear energy transfer (LET) curves for alpha particles traversing UO₂ and water. A "base-function" Bragg curve for alpha particles in water is combined with a linear peak-shift parameter (σ) that depends on the distance traveled in UO₂, enabling efficient matrix-based computation in a 2D Cartesian geometry. The dose rate is calculated by summing contributions from 100,000 simulated decay trajectories, accounting for the probability of alpha escape from the fuel surface and the energy deposited in each water layer as a function of perpendicular distance from the interface.

## Key numbers
- The model replicates a Monte Carlo (MCNPX) study of an irradiated UO₂ fuel surface within 0.6% of the resulting total dose rate.
- For 5.3 MeV alpha particles with activity 4.73×10⁸ Bq g⁻¹, the model gives an average dose rate over 30 μm of 731 Gy h⁻¹, compared to 791 Gy h⁻¹ from Tribet et al. (2017).
- For 5.8 MeV alpha particles with activity 5.6×10⁸ Bq g⁻¹, the model gives an average dose rate over 30 μm of 1136 Gy h⁻¹, compared to 1628 Gy h⁻¹ from Hansson et al. (2020).
- The Bragg peak shift σ shows a linear correlation with distance traveled in UO₂, with gradient -3.269 ± 0.025 and R² value of 0.999 over 12 values of x′_UO₂.
- The model converges to within 0.5% fluctuation after 50,000 simulated decays.
- The total dose for 5.3 MeV decays with activity 4.73×10⁸ Bq g⁻¹ is 23880 Gy h⁻¹, compared to 23740 Gy h⁻¹ from Tribet et al. (2017).

## Context
This work extends prior analytical dosimetry models by Nielsen et al. (2006) and Monte Carlo approaches by Tribet et al. (2017), while directly contradicting the escape probability assumption in Hansson et al. (2020). The authors demonstrate that Hansson et al. incorrectly used a 2D geometrical derivation giving Pα = 1/π instead of the correct 3D value of Pα = 1/4, leading to a 27% overestimation in total dose. The model enables coupling with chemical reaction and diffusion models to better predict spent fuel dissolution rates and hydrogen release as a function of fuel age and repository conditions, though the authors note that dose rates closer than 3 μm from the surface are not calculated, which could lead to underestimation of corrosion rates.
