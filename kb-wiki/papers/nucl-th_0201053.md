# Proton Drip-Line Calculations and the Rp-process

> One-proton and two-proton separation energies are calculated for proton-rich nuclei in the region $ A=41-75 $. The method is based on Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations of Coulomb displacement energies of mirror nuclei in combination with the experimental masses of the neutron-rich nuclei. The implications for the proton drip line and the astrophysical rp-process are discussed. This is done within the framework of a detailed analysis of the sensitivity of rp process calculations in type I X-ray burst models on nuclear masses. We find that the remaining mass uncertainties, in particular for some nuclei with $N=Z$, still lead to large uncertainties in calculations of X-ray burst light curves. Further experimental or theoretical improvements of nuclear mass data are necessary before observed X-ray burst light curves can be used to obtain quantitative constraints on ignition conditions and neutron star properties. We identify a list of nuclei for which improved mass data would be most important.

# Proton Drip-Line Calculations and the Rp-process

## Key claim

The authors use Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations of Coulomb displacement energies combined with experimental masses of neutron-rich nuclei to predict proton-rich masses in the A=41-75 region, but they find that remaining mass uncertainties, particularly for some N=Z nuclei, still lead to large uncertainties in X-ray burst light curve calculations, preventing quantitative constraints on ignition conditions and neutron star properties until further experimental or theoretical improvements are made.

## Method

The authors calculate displacement energies (the difference in binding energy between mirror nuclei) using spherical Hartree-Fock calculations with the SkX_csb Skyrme interaction, which includes a charge-symmetry breaking component. They combine these calculated displacement energies with experimentally known binding energies of neutron-rich mirror nuclei to extrapolate the binding energies of proton-rich nuclei in the A=41-75 mass region. Exact pairing calculations are incorporated at each Hartree-Fock iteration, and the resulting masses are used to compute one- and two-proton separation energies and to perform rp-process simulations in a one-zone X-ray burst model.

## Key numbers

- The calculated displacement energies reproduce experimental values with an rms deviation of about 100 keV for the A=41-75 mass region
- The non-observation of ⁶⁹Br implies it is proton unbound by more than 500 keV (lifetime less than 24 nsec)
- The non-observation of ⁷³Rb gives an upper limit of 30 nsec for the half-life, implying it is proton unbound by more than 570 keV
- ⁶⁴Ge has a measured mass uncertainty of 270 keV; ⁶⁸Se has an Audi-Wapstra extrapolated uncertainty of 310 keV; ⁷²Kr has a measured uncertainty of 290 keV
- The burst length varies from 150 to 250 seconds depending on mass assumptions within the new model's error bars
- The lower limit of the average ⁶⁴Ge half-life in the rp process is 12.6 seconds (compared to 0.9 seconds from previous extrapolations)
- Predicted proton capture branchings for ⁶⁴Ge cover 0.5% to 86% and for ⁶⁸Se cover 0.0% to 26% within the model's mass uncertainties

## Context

This work extends the displacement-energy method previously used by Ormand (1997) for A=46-70, applying it to the broader A=41-75 region using a global Skyrme Hartree-Fock approach rather than shell-model configuration mixing. The calculations are relevant for the rp process in type I X-ray bursts, where the waiting points ⁶⁴Ge, ⁶⁸Se, and ⁷²Kr were identified as critical by Schatz et al. (1998). The authors find that their new predictions provide tighter constraints than the Audi-Wapstra 1995 extrapolations, but that experimental mass measurements of specific proton-rich nuclei, particularly ⁶⁴Ge, ⁶⁸Se, ⁷²Kr, ⁷³Rb, and ⁷⁰Kr, are needed before X-ray burst light curves can be used to obtain quantitative constraints on neutron star properties. The work also identifies candidates for one-proton emission (⁵⁴Cu, ⁵⁸Ge, ⁶⁴As, ⁶⁸Br, ⁶⁹Br, ⁷²Rb, ⁷³Rb) and diproton emission (⁶⁴Zn, ⁵⁹Ge, ⁶³Se, ⁶⁷Kr, ⁷¹Sr).
