# Possible relevance of the softening of the sigma meson to $η$ decay into 3$π$ in the nuclear medium

> We investigate the role of the softening of the scalar-isoscalar (sigma) meson in the $η\rightarrowπ^+π^-π^0$ and $3π^0$ decay widths in the symmetric nuclear medium using a linear sigma model. Our calculation shows that these decay widths in the nuclear medium increase by up to a factor of four to ten compared with those in the free space mainly depending on the mass of the sigma meson in the free space, which is an input parameter of the model. The enhancements are considerable even at a half of the normal nuclear density. Thus, the $η$ decay into $3π$ could be a possible new probe for the chiral restoration in the nuclear medium. We find that the density dependence of the $η\rightarrow3π^0$ decay is moderate in comparison with that of $η\rightarrowπ^+π^-π^0$, although the former width is greater than the latter one at a given density. This is because the softening of the sigma meson causes cancellation of the terms appearing from the Bose symmetry in the $η\rightarrow3π^0$ decay. The difference between the density dependences should be helpful for experimental confirmation of the findings of the present study.

## Key claim

The $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi$ decay widths in symmetric nuclear medium increase by up to a factor of four to ten compared with free-space values, depending on the input value of the sigma meson mass in free space; the enhancement is considerable even at half of normal nuclear density, and the density dependence of the $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi^0$ decay is more moderate than that of $\eta \rightarrow \pi^+ \pi^- \pi^0$ due to cancellation from Bose symmetry when the sigma meson mass approaches the $2\pi$ threshold.

## Method

The authors used a linear sigma model (L$\sigma$M) with chiral SU(3) symmetry to compute the $\eta \rightarrow \pi^+ \pi^- \pi^0$ and $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi^0$ decay widths in symmetric nuclear matter. The nuclear medium effect was incorporated via a Fermi gas approximation up to $O(k_f^3)$, including nucleon tadpole and particle-hole contributions to meson self-energies and vertex corrections, with the sigma meson propagator given a finite width from its $2\pi$ decay. The sigma meson mass in free space was treated as an input parameter, varied among 441, 550, and 668 MeV, and the meson-nucleon coupling constant was fixed to reproduce a 30% reduction of the quark condensate at normal nuclear density $\rho_0 = 0.17$ fm$^{-3}$.

## Key numbers

- The $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi$ decay widths in the nuclear medium increase by up to a factor of four to ten compared with those in free space, depending on the free-space sigma meson mass (441, 550, or 668 MeV).
- The enhancement is considerable even at half of the normal nuclear density ($\rho_0/2 = 0.085$ fm$^{-3}$).
- The input parameters include $f_\pi = 92.2$ MeV, $f_K = 110.4$ MeV, $m_\pi = 138.04$ MeV, $m_K = 495.64$ MeV, and $m_\eta = 547.85$ MeV.
- The sigma meson mass in free space is varied as 441, 550, and 668 MeV; the corresponding $f_0$ masses are 1238, 1247, and 1261 MeV, respectively.
- The normal nuclear density is taken as $\rho_0 = 0.17$ fm$^{-3}$.
- The quark condensate reduction at $\rho_0$ is 30%, based on analysis of deeply bound pionic atoms (Suzuki, 2004).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Sakai and Kunihiro, 2014) of $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi$ decay in asymmetric nuclear matter using a nonlinear sigma model, where the enhancement was linked to the 4-meson-N-N vertex and the sigma meson dynamics. The present paper focuses specifically on the role of sigma meson softening in symmetric nuclear matter, connecting to earlier work on in-medium $\pi\pi$ cross sections in the $I=J=0$ channel (Jido et al., 2000; Hatsuda et al., 1999) and to the broader topic of chiral restoration probes (Hayano and Hatsuda, 2010). The results suggest that the different density dependences of the two $\eta \rightarrow 3\pi$ decay channels could serve as an experimental signature for chiral restoration in nuclear matter.
