# Nuclear Physics of Binary Neutron Star Mergers

> Binary neutron star mergers provide a unique laboratory for studying matter under conditions that cannot be reproduced in terrestrial experiments. They probe dense matter at supranuclear density, finite temperature, rapid rotation, strong gravity, and extreme neutron excess, while producing observable signals in gravitational waves, electromagnetic radiation, and, in principle, neutrinos. This review focuses on the nuclear physics of binary neutron star mergers. We discuss the dense-matter equation of state (EoS), the inspiral and merger dynamics, the structure and lifetime of the post-merger remnant, transport and dissipative processes, weak interactions and neutrino transport, and the production of heavy elements through $r$-process nucleosynthesis. Particular emphasis is placed on the connection between microscopic physics and multimessenger observables, including tidal deformability, post-merger gravitational-wave spectra, kilonova light curves, short gamma-ray bursts, and afterglows. We also review how observations of events such as GW170817, together with neutron star mass and radius measurements, laboratory nuclear experiments, and theoretical many-body calculations, constrain the EoS and the composition of dense matter. The goal is to summarize the current understanding of how nuclear physics controls the dynamics and observable signatures of binary neutron star mergers, and to identify the open questions that future multimessenger observations and improved nuclear theory will address.

## Key claim

This review argues that binary neutron star mergers are a unique laboratory for studying dense matter under conditions (supranuclear density, finite temperature, rapid rotation, strong gravity, extreme neutron excess) that cannot be reproduced in terrestrial experiments, but the central hedge is that the connection between microscopic nuclear physics and multimessenger observables remains incomplete: the equation of state at suprasaturation densities is weakly constrained, transport coefficients are in early stages of calculation, and the contribution of mergers to heavy-element production relative to other sources is not yet settled.

## Method

The paper is a comprehensive review that synthesizes results from numerical simulations of merger dynamics, relativistic magneto-hydrodynamics, nuclear many-body theory (chiral effective field theory, Brueckner theory, self-consistent Green's function theory, quantum Monte Carlo, covariant density functionals), and Bayesian inference frameworks that combine astrophysical observations (gravitational-wave tidal deformability, NICER radius measurements, pulsar masses) with laboratory nuclear experiments (heavy-ion collisions, neutron skin measurements, nuclear mass and radius data). It covers the equation of state from sub-saturation to supranuclear densities, transport and dissipative processes, weak interactions and neutrino transport, and r-process nucleosynthesis networks.

## Key numbers

* The tidal deformability of GW170817 was measured as \(\tilde{\Lambda} = 300^{+420}_{-230}\) at 90% confidence, with total binary mass \(2.73^{+0.04}_{-0.01}\,M_{\odot}\) and component masses \(1.16\)–\(1.60\,M_{\odot}\) (Abbott 2019).
* For GW190425, the total mass was \(3.4^{+0.3}_{-0.1}\,M_{\odot}\) with component masses \(1.46\)–\(1.87\,M_{\odot}\), yielding an upper limit \(\tilde{\Lambda} \leq 600\) (Abbott 2020).
* Nuclear saturation density is \(n_0 \simeq 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\), corresponding to a mass density of roughly \(2.7\times 10^{14}\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
* The symmetry energy at saturation is \(J_{\rm sym} \simeq 32\) MeV; the binding energy per nucleon is \(E_{\rm sat} \simeq -16\) MeV.
* The incompressibility \(K_{\rm sat}\) lies in the range 200–300 MeV from giant-resonance measurements.
* Neutrino luminosities during the hot post-merger phase can reach \(10^{53}\)–\(10^{54}\) erg s\(^{-1}\).
* The neutron drip density in the crust is \(\rho \simeq 4\times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\).
* NICER measurements of PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620 favor radii of order \(R \simeq 11\)–\(13\) km for canonical-mass neutron stars (Miller 2019, Miller 2021, Riley 2019, Riley 2021).
* Massive pulsars such as PSR J1614-2230, PSR J0740+6620, and PSR J0952-0607 require any viable EoS to support neutron stars with masses at least around \(2\,M_{\odot}\) (Freire 2024).

## Context

This review extends and updates earlier comprehensive reviews of binary neutron star merger physics by Faber (2012), Rosswog (2015), Baiotti (2017), and Baiotti (2019), with a specific focus on nuclear physics aspects. It synthesizes constraints from the GW170817 event (Abbott 2017) and subsequent multimessenger observations (Cowperthwaite 2017, Pian 2017) with laboratory nuclear experiments including PREX and CREX (Lattimer 2023) and theoretical many-body calculations from chiral EFT (Epelbaum 2009, Machleidt 2011, Drischler 2021) and covariant density functionals (Typel 2018). The review identifies open questions that future multimessenger observations and improved nuclear theory will address, particularly the equation of state at suprasaturation densities, the composition of dense matter (hyperons, quarks), the maximum neutron star mass, the contribution of mergers to heavy-element production relative to collapsars or core-collapse supernovae, and the role of transport properties in determining observable signals.
