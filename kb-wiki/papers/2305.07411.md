# Phase Transition Phenomenology with Nonparametric Representations of the Neutron Star Equation of State

> Astrophysical observations of neutron stars probe the structure of dense nuclear matter and have the potential to reveal phase transitions at high densities. Most recent analyses are based on parametrized models of the equation of state with a finite number of parameters and occasionally include extra parameters intended to capture phase transition phenomenology. However, such models restrict the types of behavior allowed and may not match the true equation of state. We introduce a complementary approach that extracts phase transitions directly from the equation of state without relying on, and thus being restricted by, an underlying parametrization. We then constrain the presence of phase transitions in neutron stars with astrophysical data. Current pulsar mass, tidal deformability, and mass-radius measurements disfavor only the strongest of possible phase transitions (latent energy per particle $\gtrsim 100\,\mathrm{MeV}$). Weaker phase transitions are consistent with observations. We further investigate the prospects for measuring phase transitions with future gravitational-wave observations and find that catalogs of \result{$O(100)$} events will (at best) yield Bayes factors of $\sim 10:1$ in favor of phase transitions even when the true equation of state contains very strong phase transitions. Our results reinforce the idea that neutron star observations will primarily constrain trends in macroscopic properties rather than detailed microscopic behavior. Fine-tuned equation of state models will likely remain unconstrained in the near future.

## Key claim

Current astrophysical observations of neutron stars disfavor only the strongest possible phase transitions (latent energy per particle greater than about 100 MeV), while weaker phase transitions remain consistent with all data; even future gravitational-wave catalogs of O(100) events will at best yield Bayes factors of roughly 10:1 in favor of phase transitions, reinforcing that neutron star observations will primarily constrain trends in macroscopic properties rather than detailed microscopic behavior.

## Method

The authors developed a nonparametric representation of the neutron star equation of state (EoS) using Gaussian processes, which allows them to extract phase transition features directly from the EoS without relying on a fixed parametric form. They then performed Bayesian inference using current pulsar mass measurements, tidal deformability from gravitational-wave events, and mass-radius constraints from X-ray observations, computing both maximized likelihood ratios and marginal likelihood (Bayes factor) comparisons between models with and without phase transitions. They also projected the constraining power of future gravitational-wave catalogs by simulating O(100) events.

## Key numbers

- Current pulsar mass, tidal deformability, and mass-radius measurements disfavor only phase transitions with latent energy per particle greater than about 100 MeV.
- Future gravitational-wave catalogs of O(100) events will yield Bayes factors of at most roughly 10:1 in favor of phase transitions, even when the true EoS contains very strong phase transitions.
- Bayes factors for branch counting (presence of any phase transition) using combined PSR+GW+Xray data: low-mass bin 0.362 +/- 0.036, mid-mass bin 0.030 +/- 0.006, high-mass bin 0.147 +/- 0.028, wide-mass bin 0.062 +/- 0.009.
- Bayes factors for moment-of-inertia features (latent energy threshold) using combined PSR+GW+Xray data: low-mass bin with huge latent energy (100 MeV) 0.117 +/- 0.008, mid-mass bin with huge latent energy 0.152 +/- 0.009, high-mass bin with huge latent energy 0.383 +/- 0.023.
- Maximized likelihood ratio for branch counting with PSR+GW+Xray data: low-mass bin 0.47, mid-mass bin 0.14, high-mass bin 0.20, wide-mass bin 0.20.

## Context

This work extends the line of Bayesian inference on the neutron star equation of state pioneered by parametric approaches (e.g., piecewise polytropes or spectral parameterizations) that include extra parameters for phase transitions. By introducing a nonparametric Gaussian process representation, the authors avoid the restrictions inherent in fixed parametric forms, allowing a more agnostic search for phase transition signatures. The results enable future observational campaigns to set realistic expectations: even with O(100) gravitational-wave detections, distinguishing phase transitions from smooth EoS behavior will remain challenging, and fine-tuned EoS models will likely stay unconstrained in the near future.
