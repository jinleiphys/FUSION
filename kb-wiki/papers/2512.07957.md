# On radiative corrections to inverse beta decay at low energies

> We compute electromagnetic radiative corrections in the inverse beta decay, $\barν_e + p \rightarrow e^+ + n$, at reactor antineutrino energies within the heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory, provide the most accurate cross-section predictions for this process, and present a complete error budget. For the first time, we consistently include quantum electrodynamics, chromodynamics, and electroweak contributions and present the positron energy spectrum accounting for radiative corrections. Our calculation also improves on previous evaluations by incorporating permille-level contributions. The results can be readily applied to normalize the reactor antineutrino flux, make precise measurements of neutrino oscillation parameters, and search for new physics at nuclear power plants.

# On radiative corrections to inverse beta decay at low energies

## Key claim
The paper provides the most accurate cross-section predictions for inverse beta decay at reactor antineutrino energies by consistently including quantum electrodynamics, electroweak, and quantum chromodynamics corrections within heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory for the first time, but the claimed improvement is conditional on the use of a recent effective field theory determination of the low-energy vector coupling constant and a fully analytical phase-space integration that violates rigorous effective field theory power counting near kinematic endpoints.

## Method
The authors compute electromagnetic radiative corrections to the process $\bar{\nu}_e + p \rightarrow e^+ + n$ within heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory, treating nucleons as heavy fields and working without pions at reactor antineutrino energies below 10 MeV. They evaluate virtual photon exchange diagrams, external field renormalization factors, and one-photon bremsstrahlung contributions, performing phase-space integration for radiative inverse beta decay without the static-limit approximation. Ultraviolet divergences are absorbed into renormalized low-energy coupling constants $g_V$ and $g_A$ in the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}_\chi$ renormalization scheme, while infrared divergences cancel between virtual diagrams and soft-photon phase-space contributions.

## Key numbers
- Total cross-section theoretical uncertainty: 1.85 permille across the entire reactor antineutrino energy range
- Vector coupling constant at chiral renormalization scale $\mu_\chi = m_e$: $g_V = 1.02499(13)$
- Ratio of axial-vector-to-vector coupling constants: $\lambda = g_A/g_V = 1.2754(13)$
- CKM matrix element: $V_{ud} = 0.97348(31)$
- Fermi coupling constant: $G_F = 1.1663787(6) \times 10^{-5}~\mathrm{GeV}^{-2}$
- Quantum electrodynamics radiative corrections shift cross-section predictions downwards by approximately 1-2%
- Short-distance electroweak and quantum chromodynamics contributions are approximately the same size but enter with opposite sign
- Deviations from the Strumia (2003) parameterization are approximately 1%
- Deviations from previous radiative correction treatments are at the level of 1-2 permille
- The improvements can account for approximately 10-15% of the remaining 5-MeV bump in reactor antineutrino spectra

## Context
This work extends the heavy baryon chiral perturbation theory treatment of inverse beta decay proposed by Ando (2004) and Raha (2011), benefiting from a recent systematic effective field theory determination of the low-energy vector coupling constant by Cirigliano (2023). The calculation improves on prior evaluations by Sirlin (1977), Kurylov (2002), Vogel (1999), Fukugita (2004), and Ricciardi (2022) by incorporating permille-level contributions and providing the positron energy spectrum for the first time. The results can be applied to normalize reactor antineutrino flux, make precise measurements of neutrino oscillation parameters at experiments such as JUNO, and search for new physics at nuclear power plants, while also serving as a basis for charged-current elastic scattering cross sections from supernova neutrinos.
