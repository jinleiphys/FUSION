# Effective field theories for collective excitations of atomic nuclei

> Collective modes emerge as the relevant degrees of freedom that govern low-energy excitations of atomic nuclei. These modes - rotations, pairing rotations, and vibrations - are separated in energy from non-collective excitations, making it possible to describe them in the framework of effective field theory. Rotations and pairing rotations are the remnants of Nambu-Goldstone modes from the emergent breaking of rotational symmetry and phase symmetries in finite deformed and finite superfluid nuclei, respectively. The symmetry breaking severely constrains the structure of low-energy Lagrangians and thereby clarifies what is essential and simplifies the description. The approach via effective field theories exposes the essence of nuclear collective excitations and is defined with a breakdown scale in mind. This permits one to make systematic improvements and to estimate and quantify uncertainties. Effective field theories of collective excitations have been used to compute spectra, transition rates, and other matrix elements of interest. In particular, predictions of the nuclear matrix element for neutrinoless double beta decay then come with quantified uncertainties. This review summarizes these results and also compares the approach via effective field theories to well-known models and ab initio computations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that effective field theories (EFTs) for nuclear collective excitations, rotations, pairing rotations, and vibrations, are systematically improvable and provide quantified uncertainties, but this approach is explicitly limited to low-energy phenomena where collective modes are well separated from non-collective excitations; the EFTs are "the lowest rung in a ladder of increasing resolution that ends with *ab initio* computations."

## Method

The authors construct effective Lagrangians for deformed and superfluid nuclei using emergent symmetry breaking of rotational and phase symmetries, employing a power counting that separates rotational (slow) and vibrational (fast) energy scales. They derive Hamiltonians at leading, next-to-leading, and higher orders, fitting low-energy constants to experimental data (e.g., energies of band heads and first excited states) and estimating uncertainties from the breakdown scale. The approach is compared to the Bohr Hamiltonian, the interacting boson model, the Nilsson model, and *ab initio* computations, with symmetry projection providing the link between mean-field and EFT descriptions.

## Key numbers

- In \(^8\)Be, the binding energy is about 50 MeV while the excitation energy of the \(2^+\) state is just 3 MeV, illustrating the separation of scales.
- For \(^{166}\)Er, the EFT at next-to-next-to-leading order predicts energies for states in the ground, \(\beta\), and \(\gamma\) bands below 2 MeV, with theory errors estimated as \((\varepsilon I^2)^2\) times predicted energies using breakdown scale \(\Lambda = 3\omega_0\).
- The rotational energy at next-to-leading order is \(E_{\text{NLO}} = \frac{1}{2C_0}[I(I+1) - K^2]\), with \(C_0 \sim \xi^{-1}\).
- Vibrational energies scale as \(A^{-2/3}\) while rotational energies scale as \(A^{-5/3}\) for a liquid droplet with mass number \(A\).
- The energy gain from angular-momentum projection is estimated as \(E_0 - \langle E \rangle = -(E_2 - E_0) \frac{\langle J^2 \rangle}{6}\).

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Bohr (1952, 1953) on the unified collective model and Elliott (1958) on SU(3) symmetry, and contrasts the EFT approach with the interacting boson model (Iachello, 1987), the Nilsson model (1955), and mean-field computations (Bender, 2003; Niksic, 2011). It enables systematic uncertainty quantification for nuclear matrix elements relevant to neutrinoless double beta decay and provides a framework to connect *ab initio* computations (e.g., Hagen, 2022; Hu, 2024a,b) with collective models, allowing checks of *ab initio* quality via power counting.
