# Chiral crossover vs chiral density wave in dense nuclear matter

> We employ a model based on nucleonic and mesonic degrees of freedom to discuss the competition between isotropic and anisotropic phases in cold and dense matter. Assuming isotropy, the model exhibits a chiral phase transition which is of second order in the chiral limit and becomes a crossover in the case of a realistic pion mass. This observation crucially depends on the presence of the nucleonic vacuum contribution. Allowing for an anisotropic phase in the form of a chiral density wave can disrupt the smooth crossover. We identify the regions in the parameter space of the model where a chiral density wave is energetically preferred. A high-density re-appearance of the chiral density wave with unphysical behavior, as seen in previous studies, is avoided by a suitable renormalization scheme. A nonzero pion mass tends to disfavor the anisotropic phase compared to the chiral limit and we find that, within our model, the chiral density wave is only realized for baryon densities of at least about 6 times nuclear saturation density.

## Key claim

The paper shows that including the nucleonic vacuum contribution (Dirac sea) in a nucleon-meson model changes the chiral transition from first-order to a smooth crossover in the isotropic case, and that a chiral density wave (CDW) can disrupt this crossover, but only at baryon densities of at least about 6 times nuclear saturation density.

## Method

The authors employ a nucleon-meson model with nucleonic and mesonic degrees of freedom, using a mean-field approximation with a chiral density wave ansatz where the scalar and pseudoscalar condensates oscillate as sigma = phi cos(2q·x) and pi_3 = phi sin(2q·x). They compute the free energy including the Dirac sea contribution, which requires proper time regularization and renormalization with a q-dependent renormalization scale to avoid unphysical high-density behavior. The model parameters are fitted to reproduce vacuum properties and nuclear matter saturation properties including binding energy, saturation density, and incompressibility.

## Key numbers

- The chiral density wave is only realized for baryon densities of at least about 6 times nuclear saturation density (n_0 = 0.153 fm^-3)
- The pion decay constant is f_pi = 93 MeV
- The vector meson mass is m_omega = 782 MeV
- The nucleon mass in vacuum is m_N = 939 MeV
- The binding energy at saturation is E_B = -16.3 MeV, giving mu_0 = 922.7 MeV
- The effective Dirac mass at saturation is M_0 approximately (0.7 - 0.8) m_N
- The incompressibility K is in the range (200-300) MeV
- The pion mass for the physical case is m_pi = 139 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of chiral density waves in nuclear matter by Dautry (1979), Takahashi (2001, 2002), Heinz (2013), and Takeda (2018), which ignored the nucleonic vacuum contribution. It also contrasts with NJL and quark-meson model studies by Carignano (2011, 2014) and Andersen (2018) that included the Dirac sea but used quark degrees of freedom. The model was previously used by Drews (2014), Fraga (2018, 2022), Schmitt (2020), and Brandes (2021) for studying chiral transitions and mixed phases. The work enables future studies of whether the CDW is stable against more complicated inhomogeneous structures that break translational invariance, and provides a framework for combining nucleon-based and quark-based approaches to understand the QCD phase diagram at high density relevant for neutron stars.
