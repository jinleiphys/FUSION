# A New Constraint on the Nuclear Equation of State from Statistical Distributions of Compact Remnants of Supernovae

> Understanding how matter behaves at the highest densities and temperatures is a major open problem in both nuclear physics and relativistic astrophysics. This physics is often encapsulated in the so-called high-temperature nuclear equation of state, which influences compact binary mergers, core-collapse supernovae, and many more phenomena. One such case is the type (either black hole or neutron star) and mass of the remnant of the core collapse of a massive star. For each of six candidate equations of state, we use a very large suite of spherically symmetric supernova models to generate a suite of synthetic populations of such remnants. We then compare these synthetic populations to the observed remnant population. We thus provide a novel constraint on the high-temperature nuclear equation of state and describe which EOS candidates are more or less favored by this metric.

## Key claim
The paper provides a novel constraint on the high-temperature nuclear equation of state by comparing synthetic populations of supernova remnants (neutron stars and black holes) generated from six candidate EOS models to observed remnant populations, using an information-theoretic metric to determine which EOS candidates are more or less favored. The key hedge is that the authors assume their spherically symmetric simulations with a parameterized explosion treatment would closely mimic physical reality if the EOS were tuned to an unknown ideal, and they acknowledge this assumption may suffer from an identifiability issue.

## Method
The authors used 681 spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova simulations with the PUSH framework, which mimics multi-dimensional energy deposition in spherical symmetry, across initial masses of 10.8 to 40 solar masses at three metallicities (z=0, 10^-4, 1 Z_sun) and six nuclear EOS models (SFHo, SFHx, HS(DD2), HS(TM1), HS(NL3), BHB-lambda-phi). They mapped a Kroupa initial mass function through these simulations to generate synthetic remnant mass distributions, then compared these to observed black hole and neutron star mass distributions using statistical distance measures. For black holes, they used a truncated power-law model from Abbott (2019), and for neutron stars they used four observed populations (slow pulsars, recycled pulsars, circular double-degenerate, eccentric double-degenerate) each modeled as Gaussians from Ozel.

## Key numbers
- 681 total simulations performed: 508 resulted in neutron stars, 173 in black holes
- Initial mass range of progenitors: 10.8 to 40 solar masses
- Three metallicities used: z=0, 10^-4, and 1 Z_sun
- Six EOS models tested: SFHo, SFHx, HS(DD2), HS(TM1), HS(NL3), BHB-lambda-phi
- Sample size per EOS: approximately 100 simulations (approximately 180 for DD2)
- Observed black hole mass distribution parameters from Abbott (2019): alpha=7.1, lower mass cutoff at 6.8 solar masses, upper mass cutoff at 75 solar masses, smoothing width delta_m=3
- Observed neutron star mass distribution means: 1.49 solar masses (slow pulsars), 1.54 solar masses (recycled pulsars), 1.33 solar masses (circular double-degenerate), 1.29 solar masses (eccentric double-degenerate)
- Observed neutron star mass distribution standard deviations: 0.19 (slow pulsars), 0.23 (recycled pulsars), 0.09 (circular double-degenerate), 0.24 (eccentric double-degenerate)
- Black hole measurement error standard deviation: sigma_BH(M_BH) = 0.120213 * M_BH + 0.355936
- Neutron star measurement error standard deviations: 0.12 (slow pulsars), 0.088 (recycled pulsars), 0.003 (circular double-degenerate), 0.28 (eccentric double-degenerate)

## Context
This work extends previous sensitivity studies of EOS dependence in core-collapse supernovae, such as those by Richers (2017), Yasin (2020), and Schneider (2019), which typically simulated only 1-3 progenitors with 1-3 EOS models. It also builds on traditional EOS constraints from neutron star mass-radius relationships (Miller 2019) and multi-messenger constraints from GW170817 (Margalit and Metzger 2017, Rezzolla 2018). The novel statistical approach enables systematic comparison of EOS models against observed remnant populations, which was previously difficult due to the computational expense of 3D simulations and the inability to connect specific progenitors to specific remnants. This enables future work to incorporate additional EOS candidates and more sophisticated simulation frameworks as they become available.
