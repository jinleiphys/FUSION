# The impact of $^{17}$O$+α$ reaction rate uncertainties on the s-process in rotating massive stars

> Massive stars are crucial to galactic chemical evolution for elements heavier than iron. Their contribution at early times in the evolution of the Universe, however, is unclear due to poorly constrained nuclear reaction rates. The competing $^{17}$O($α,γ$)$^{21}$Ne and $^{17}$O($α,n$)$^{20}$Ne reactions strongly impact weak s-process yields from rotating massive stars at low metallicities. Abundant $^{16}$O absorbs neutrons, removing flux from the s-process, and producing $^{17}$O. The $^{17}$O($α,n$)$^{20}$Ne reaction releases neutrons, allowing continued s-process nucleosynthesis, if the $^{17}$O($α,γ$)$^{21}$Ne reaction is sufficiently weak. While published rates are available, they are based on limited indirect experimental data for the relevant temperatures and, more importantly, no uncertainties are provided. The available nuclear physics has been evaluated, and combined with data from a new study of astrophysically relevant $^{21}$Ne states using the $^{20}$Ne($d,p$)$^{21}$Ne reaction. Constraints are placed on the ratio of the ($α,n$)/($α,γ$) reaction rates with uncertainties on the rates provided for the first time. The new rates favour the ($α,n$) reaction and suggest that the weak s-process in rotating low-metallicity stars is likely to continue up to barium and, within the computed uncertainties, even to lead.

## Key claim

The new reaction rates favor the $^{17}$O($\alpha,n$)$^{20}$Ne reaction over the $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne reaction, suggesting that the weak s-process in rotating low-metallicity massive stars is likely to continue up to barium and, within the computed uncertainties, even to lead, but this conclusion depends on the unknown $\alpha$-particle widths of key resonances and the spin-parity assignments adopted for several states in $^{21}$Ne.

## Method

The authors performed a new measurement of the $^{20}$Ne($d,p$)$^{21}$Ne single-neutron transfer reaction at the Triangle Universities Nuclear Laboratory (TUNL) using a 14 MeV deuteron beam and the Enge split-pole spectrograph, collecting data at five laboratory angles. They extracted excitation energies and angular distributions for states in $^{21}$Ne above the $\alpha$-particle threshold, used distorted-wave Born approximation (DWBA) calculations with the code FRESCO to determine transferred angular momenta and neutron partial widths, and combined these results with an evaluation of existing literature data. The $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne and $^{17}$O($\alpha,n$)$^{20}$Ne reaction rates were then calculated using the RatesMC Monte Carlo code, providing uncertainties for the first time.

## Key numbers

- The $E_x = 7981(2)$ keV state (resonance at $E_r = 633$ keV) has a neutron width $\Gamma_n = 14(5)$ keV from the present experiment, compared to the previously known $\Gamma_n = 6(2)$ keV from neutron resonance scattering (PhysRev.114.194).
- The $E_x = 8069(1)$ keV state (resonance at $E_r = 721$ keV) has a measured $\alpha$-particle width $\Gamma_\alpha = 46.2(46) \times 10^{-3}$ eV and neutron width $\Gamma_n = 1600(200)$ eV.
- The $E_x = 7982.1(7)$ keV state (resonance at $E_r = 634.2$ keV) has a resonance strength $\omega\gamma_{(\alpha,\gamma)} = 4.98(97)$ $\mu$eV from DRAGON measurements (WilliamsPRCSubmitted).
- The $E_x = 8159(2)$ keV state (resonance at $E_r = 811$ keV) has a weighted average resonance strength $\omega\gamma_{(\alpha,\gamma)} = 7.72(55)$ meV from WilliamsPRCSubmitted and PhysRevC.83.052802.
- The $E_x = 7655.7(22)$ keV state (resonance at $E_r = 307.8$ keV) has a neutron width $\Gamma_n = 0.10(7)$ eV and is assigned $J^\pi = 7/2^+$ (from ENSDF), reducing its contribution to the $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne rate compared to the previous $J^\pi = 5/2^+$ assignment in PhysRevC.87.045805.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ reactions by PhysRevC.87.045805 and CAUGHLAN1988283, which provided rates without uncertainties, and by Mohr (2017), who raised consistency concerns about the high-energy cross section. The new rates, which favor the ($\alpha,n$) channel, enable more reliable predictions of weak s-process yields from rotating massive stars at low metallicities, particularly for elements beyond strontium up to barium and lead, as modeled by Frischknecht (2016) and Choplin (2018). The dominant remaining uncertainty is the unknown $\alpha$-particle width of the $E_r = 308$ keV resonance, which contributes most to the $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne rate within the astrophysically relevant Gamow window.
