# Dissipation in finite Fermi systems

> We present a systematic theory of dissipation in finite Fermi systems like nuclei and metallic clusters. This theory is based on the application of semiclassical methods and random matrix theory to linear response of many-body systems. The theory is developed in the approximation wherein the many-body system can be treated as a single particle in an effective, time-dependent mean-field. We find semiclassical expressions for energy dissipation relevant in one-body dissipation in heavy nuclei. We also show that this energy dissipation, related to damping of collective excitations, is irreversible. The irreversibility is proved by our development of a quantum diffusion equation. It may be noted that the quantum diffusion equation is derived from the von Neumann equation and makes no assumption about the initial form of the density operator. It is shown that, in the semiclassical limit, the quantum diffusion equation reduces to the classical Smoluchowski equation. Further, we show that the dissipation is a purely quantal phenomenon as it is related to the geometric phase acquired by a single-particle wavefunction as the system evolves in a slow-varying mean-field. It is explicitly shown that the dissipation rate is related to the nature of dynamics and the spectrum of the classical Liouvillian operator. Finally, we present an expression for the viscosity tensor encountered in nuclear fission in terms of periodic orbits of the single particle in an adiabatically deforming nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper claims that dissipation in finite Fermi systems like nuclei is a purely quantal phenomenon arising from the geometric phase acquired by single-particle wavefunctions in a slowly varying mean-field, and that this dissipation is irreversible on observational timescales only when the spectrum is coarse-grained (e.g., by random matrix ensemble averaging), despite the system being isolated with a pure point spectrum.

## Method

The author develops a semiclassical theory of one-body dissipation by applying the Gutzwiller trace formula to linear response theory, reducing the many-body problem to a single particle in an effective time-dependent mean-field. The response function is expressed in terms of periodic orbits of the classical single-particle dynamics, and a generalized Smoluchowski equation for the energy distribution is derived from the von Neumann equation using a double expansion in the adiabaticity parameter and Planck's constant. Random matrix theory results are also presented for comparison, and the formalism is extended to systems with mixed (partially chaotic) dynamics using Creagh's trace formula for partially broken symmetry.

## Key numbers

- The time-scale for a nucleus participating in a reaction is approximately \(10^{-20}\) s or shorter.
- The rate of energy dissipation for fully chaotic single-particle dynamics is given by the wall formula: \(\dot{E}_{\mathrm{wall}} = \rho \overline{v} \int u^2(a) d^2a\), where \(\rho\) is nuclear mass density and \(\overline{v}\) is average nucleon speed.
- For mixed dynamics, the dissipation rate is scaled by \(\overline{\mu}\), the average fraction of chaotic trajectories, as \(\dot{E} = \overline{\mu} \dot{E}_{\mathrm{wall}}\).
- The generalized Smoluchowski equation is derived valid up to times of order \(\epsilon^{-1}\) (where \(\epsilon\) is the small adiabaticity parameter) and up to order \(\hbar^2\).
- The two-level cluster function \(Y_2(\hbar \omega)\) for the unitary ensemble is given by \([\sin(\pi \hbar \omega)/(\pi \hbar \omega)]^2\), with analogous expressions for orthogonal and symplectic ensembles.

## Context

This work extends the classic one-body dissipation theory of Gross (1975) and Swiatecki (1974) by incorporating semiclassical periodic-orbit corrections and random matrix theory into the linear response framework of Koonin and Randrup (1977) and Hofmann (1976). It builds on Srednicki's (1994) derivation of equilibrium momentum distributions from chaotic eigenfunction ansatz and Jain and Alonso's (1997) extension to anyon gases, now including dissipation. The theory enables system-specific predictions for nuclear fission viscosity in terms of periodic orbits, going beyond the empirical chaos-weighted wall formula of Pal (1991), and provides a foundation for understanding the interplay between single-particle and collective motion in finite Fermi systems.
