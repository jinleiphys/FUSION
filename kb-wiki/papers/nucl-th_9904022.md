# Correlation effects in single-particle overlap functions and one-nucleon removal reactions

> Single-particle overlap functions and spectroscopic factors are calculated on the basis of the one-body density matrices (ODM) obtained for the nucleus $^{16}O$ employing different approaches to account for the effects of correlations. The calculations use the relationship between the overlap functions related to bound states of the (A-1)-particle system and the ODM for the ground state of the A-particle system. The resulting bound-state overlap functions are compared and tested in the description of the experimental data from (p,d) reactions for which the shape of the overlap function is important.

## Key claim

Single-particle overlap functions and spectroscopic factors for bound states in $^{16}O$ can be extracted from one-body density matrices (ODMs) obtained via correlated basis function (CBF) and Green function methods, and these overlap functions, when used directly as form factors in DWBA calculations for $(p,d)$ reactions, yield absolute differential cross-sections in qualitative agreement with experiment without the need for an additional fitted spectroscopic factor.

## Method

The authors used the procedure of Van Neck et al. (1993) to extract bound-state overlap functions, spectroscopic factors, and separation energies from the ground-state one-body density matrix of $^{16}O$. ODMs were obtained from three approaches: (i) the correlated basis function (CBF) method using variational Monte Carlo calculations with Argonne NN forces, including central, spin-isospin, and tensor-isospin correlations; (ii) the Fermi hypernetted chain (FHNC) formalism within CBF theory using central nucleon-nucleon interactions and an average correlation approximation; and (iii) the Green function method (GFM) solving the Dyson equation with a realistic self-energy. The resulting overlap functions were then used as form factors in zero-range DWBA calculations for the $^{16}O(p,d)^{15}O$ reaction at three incident proton energies, replacing the standard DWBA form factor and omitting the separate spectroscopic factor.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factor for the $1s$ state in $^{16}O$: 0.883 (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 0.904 (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 0.940 (Jastrow correlation method, Stoitsov et al. 1996), 0.977 (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1996), 0.980 (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1997), compared to 1.000 in Hartree-Fock.
- Spectroscopic factor for the $1p_{3/2}$ state: 0.909 (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 0.915 (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 0.983 (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1997).
- Spectroscopic factor for the $1p_{1/2}$ state: 0.912 (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 0.905 (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 0.981 (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1996).
- Separation energy for the $1s$ state: 37.70 MeV (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 31.12 MeV (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 34.85 MeV (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1996), compared to 34.89 MeV in Hartree-Fock.
- Separation energy for the $1p_{3/2}$ state: 23.30 MeV (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 20.74 MeV (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 20.05 MeV (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1997).
- Separation energy for the $1p_{1/2}$ state: 16.79 MeV (CBF, Van Neck et al. 1997), 16.68 MeV (GFM, Polls et al. 1996), 20.09 MeV (CBF, Arias de Saavedra et al. 1996).
- DWBA spectroscopic factor for the $1/2^{-}$ state at $E_p = 31.82$ MeV: 1.05 (exceeds unity, indicating the need for overlap functions).
- DWBA spectroscopic factor for the $3/2^{-}$ state at $E_p = 65$ MeV: 0.54.

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations by Stoitsov et al. (1996) and Dimitrova et al. (1997) that used a simpler Jastrow correlation method with a harmonic-oscillator basis and Gaussian correlation functions, by applying the same extraction procedure to more realistic ODMs from CBF theory (Van Neck et al. 1997) and the Green function method (Polls et al. 1996; Muther et al. 1995). The results confirm that tensor correlations, not just central short-range correlations, are responsible for the majority of the depletion of spectroscopic factors (7-11% depletion from tensor versus 1-2% from central correlations). The approach enables absolute cross-section calculations for one-nucleon removal reactions without the need for fitted spectroscopic factors, and the resulting overlap functions can be tested against experimental $(p,d)$ data at multiple incident energies.
