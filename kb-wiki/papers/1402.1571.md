# On the complex branch points in $πN$ scattering amplitude and the multiple poles feature of resonances

> A simple heuristic argument to understand the existence of complex branch points in the $πN$ scattering amplitude is presented. It is based on a hypothesis that the singularity structure of the $πN$ scattering amplitude is a smooth varying function of the pion mass. We then show that the two-pole structure found to correspond to the Roper resonance could just a simple direct mathematical consequence of including additional Riemann surface in the analysis. Our study indicates that it is always possible to have multiple poles, either two or four etc., in different Riemann sheet, to be associated with a resonance. The poles in all Riemann sheets should be looked for to determine whether the two-pole feature of the Roper resonance $N^*(1440)$ is a manifestation of the "exact degeneracy" discussed here but masked by numerical indeterminacy, or an "accidental" one. The determination of the multiplicity of a pole could provide some information of the analytical structure of the numerator of the pole term, as the numerator of the pole term is related to resonance form factor.

## Key claim

The two-pole structure found for the Roper resonance \(N^*(1440)\) could be a direct mathematical consequence of including additional Riemann surfaces in the analysis, rather than a unique physical feature; multiple poles (two, four, or more) in different Riemann sheets can always be associated with a resonance, and it remains to be determined whether the observed near degeneracies are exact but masked by numerical indeterminacy or accidental.

## Method

The author presents a heuristic argument based on the hypothesis that the singularity structure of the \(\pi N\) scattering amplitude varies smoothly with the pion mass, using lattice QCD results on the evolution of nucleon and \(\Delta(1232)\) masses. The analysis then examines model functions with square-root branch cuts and a pole term to mathematically demonstrate how the number of Riemann sheets (determined by the number of branch cuts) governs the multiplicity and residue pattern of poles across sheets.

## Key numbers

* The Roper resonance \(N^*(1440)\) exhibits two nearly degenerate poles in the \(P_{11}\) partial wave: one at \(1384 - 119i\) MeV (sheet I) and another at \(1382 - 138i\) MeV (sheet II), with residues \(19 - 68i\) and \(-6 - 105i\) respectively, from the analysis of Cutkosky (1990) using VPI data of Arndt (1985).
* Four poles near 1700 MeV were found by Cutkosky (1990): sheet I at \(1689 - 54i\) MeV (residue \(-10 + 3i\)), sheet II at \(1676 - 83i\) MeV (residue \(-54 + 21i\)), sheet III at \(1690 - 61i\) MeV (residue \(-8 + 0i\)), and sheet IV at \(1683 - 83i\) MeV (residue \(-39 + 12i\)).
* Two additional nearly degenerate pole pairs near 1513 MeV and 1514 MeV were also reported by Cutkosky (1990): sheet III at \(1513 - 54i\) MeV (residue \(-15 - i\)) and sheet IV at \(1514 - 66i\) MeV (residue \(-38 - 5i\)).

## Context

This work extends the earlier findings of Arndt (1985) and Cutkosky (1990) on the two-pole structure of the Roper resonance in \(\pi N\) \(P_{11}\) partial-wave analysis, and the demonstration by Ceci (2011) that complex branch points exist from general S-matrix properties. The heuristic argument using smooth variation with pion mass provides a physical rationale for complex branch cuts, and the mathematical analysis shows that the multiplicity of poles across Riemann sheets can reveal information about the analytical structure of the resonance form factor, motivating searches for poles in all Riemann sheets in future analyses.
