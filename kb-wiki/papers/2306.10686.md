# Quantum theory of isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th in strong laser fields

> A general quantum mechanical theory is developed for the isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th in strong femtosecond laser pulses. The theory describes the tripartite interaction between the nucleus, the atomic electrons, and the laser field. The nucleus can be excited both by the laser field and by laser-driven electronic transitions. Numerical results show that strong femtosecond laser pulses are very efficient in exciting the $^{229}$Th nucleus, yielding nuclear excitation probabilities on the order of $10^{-11}$ per nucleus per pulse. Laser-driven electronic excitations are found to be more efficient than direct optical excitations.

## Key claim

The paper develops a quantum mechanical theory for isomeric excitation of $^{229}$Th in strong femtosecond laser pulses, finding that laser-driven electronic excitations (LDEE) are more efficient than direct optical excitations (OE) when the laser frequency is far off resonance, with nuclear excitation probabilities on the order of $10^{-11}$ per nucleus per pulse. The abstract's claim of "very efficient" excitation is qualified by the fact that these probabilities remain extremely small in absolute terms, and the dominance of LDEE over OE only holds when the laser frequency is far from the 8.28 eV isomeric resonance.

## Method

The authors develop a tripartite quantum mechanical framework coupling the nucleus, atomic electrons, and laser field, treating the laser-electron interaction non-perturbatively while using time-dependent perturbation theory for the weaker nucleus-laser and electron-nucleus couplings. The electronic dynamics are solved using the time-dependent zero-order-regular-approximation (ZORA) Hamiltonian to account for relativistic effects near the nucleus, with the wave function expanded in spherical spinors and propagated via a split-operator method. Nuclear excitation probabilities are calculated separately for the outermost electrons (up to four) under the single-active-electron approximation, with contributions from both magnetic dipole (M1) and electric quadrupole (E2) channels.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear excitation probability per nucleus per pulse: approximately $9.1 \times 10^{-12}$ for 800 nm wavelength, $10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$ peak intensity, and 5 optical cycles duration
- Nuclear excitation probability per nucleus per pulse: approximately $2.7 \times 10^{-11}$ for 400 nm wavelength, $10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$ peak intensity, and 5 optical cycles duration (about three times higher than the 800 nm case)
- For 400 nm wavelength, $10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$ intensity, and 10 optical cycles: total excitation probability about $1.2 \times 10^{-11}$; for 15 optical cycles: also about $1.2 \times 10^{-11}$ (about two times lower than the 5-cycle case)
- For 400 nm wavelength, 10 optical cycles, and $2 \times 10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$ intensity: total excitation probability about $1.5 \times 10^{-11}$; for $4 \times 10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$: about $1.8 \times 10^{-11}$
- The OE channel at exact resonance (8.28 eV) with $10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$ and 20 optical cycles yields excitation probability about $10^{-12}$, which is 3 to 4 orders of magnitude lower than LDEE for off-resonant frequencies
- Reduced transition probabilities used: $B(M1, e\rightarrow g) = 0.005$ W.u. and $B(E2, e\rightarrow g) = 30$ W.u., from Minkov and Palffy (2021)

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous semiclassical recollision-induced nuclear excitation (RINE) calculations (Wang 2022), which gave a probability of $4 \times 10^{-12}$ for 800 nm and $10^{14}$ W/cm$^2$, by providing a full quantum treatment that also includes free-bound and bound-bound electronic transitions beyond the RINE process. The quantum result ($9.1 \times 10^{-12}$ for the same parameters) confirms the semiclassical order-of-magnitude estimate. The paper enables direct comparison between OE and LDEE channels within a single framework, showing that both channels can be comparable when the laser frequency is near the 8.28 eV isomeric resonance, and that neglecting either channel would be dangerous. The excitation probability per femtosecond pulse ($\sim 10^{-11}$) is comparable to the 29-keV indirect OE method of Masuda (2019), which gives about $10^{-11}$ per nucleus per second.
