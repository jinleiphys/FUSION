# Phi-measure of azimuthal fluctuations

> The event-by-event azimuthal fluctuations in high-energy heavy-ion collisions are analyzed by means of the so-called Phi-measure. The fluctuations due to the collective transverse flow and those caused by the quantum statistics and resonance decays in the hadron gas are discussed in detail.

# Phi-measure of azimuthal fluctuations

## Key claim

The Phi-measure, which does not require reaction plane reconstruction, can detect azimuthal fluctuations from collective transverse flow and quantum statistics in heavy-ion collisions, but it cannot easily disentangle these different sources; the integrated information must be combined with other methods such as Fourier expansion.

## Method

The author defines the fluctuation measure Phi using the single-particle variable z = x - x̄ (where x is the azimuthal angle) and the event variable Z = Σ(x_i - x̄), then computes Phi = sqrt(⟨Z²⟩/⟨N⟩) - sqrt(z̄²). The measure is calculated analytically for two cases of transverse flow (maximally correlated and independent event-plane angles) and for an ideal quantum gas of pions, including modifications for resonance decays from ρ(770) and ω(782) mesons.

## Key numbers

- For Pb-Pb collisions at 158 GeV per nucleon with v₁ = v₂ = 0.03 and ⟨N⟩ = 170, Phi equals 0.058 in the first case (correlated angles) and 0.105 in the second case (independent angles).
- For massless bosons with zero chemical potential, Phi = 0.309; for massless fermions with zero chemical potential, Phi = -0.082.
- At freeze-out temperature T = 120 MeV and μ = 0 for pions, Phi = 0.078 without resonances and Phi = 0.066 with resonances included.

## Context

This work extends the Phi-measure approach previously introduced by Gazdzicki (1992) for studying event-by-event fluctuations, applying it specifically to azimuthal fluctuations as an alternative to the Fourier expansion method developed by Voloshin (1996), Poskanzer (1998), and Ollitrault (1997). The author notes that the Fourier method is insensitive to collective motion independent of the reaction plane, such as that from jets or color instabilities, and that simultaneous measurement of Phi for azimuthal and transverse momentum fluctuations (as studied by Liu (1999)) may help disentangle different correlation sources. The calculations of quantum statistics effects build on the author's prior work (Mrowczynski, 1998, 1999a, 1999b).
