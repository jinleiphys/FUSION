# Colliding shock waves and hydrodynamics in small systems

> Using numerical holography, we study the collision of a planar sheet of energy with a bounded localized distribution of energy. The collision, which mimics proton-nucleus collisions, produces a localized lump of debris with transverse size $R \sim 1/T_{\rm eff}$ with $T_{\rm eff}$ the effective temperature, and has large gradients and large transverse flow. Nevertheless, the postcollision evolution is well described by viscous hydrodynamics. Our results bolster the notion that debris produced in proton-nucleus collisions may be modeled using hydrodynamics.

# Colliding shock waves and hydrodynamics in small systems

## Key claim
Using holographic duality to model proton-nucleus collisions, the post-collision debris forms a droplet of liquid with transverse size as small as R ~ 1/Teff, and despite large gradients and large transverse flow, the evolution is well described by viscous hydrodynamics. The hedge is that the colliding objects are not QCD bound states but rather caricatures in N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, and the claim applies specifically in the low-energy limit where Teff is small.

## Method
The author numerically solves the full 5D Einstein equations for the collision of a planar gravitational shock wave (representing the "nucleus") with a transversely localized shock wave (representing the "proton") in asymptotically AdS spacetime. The precollision geometry is constructed by superimposing the metrics of left- and right-moving shock waves, and time evolution is performed using a characteristic formulation on a spectral grid of size 39×39×155×48 in the spatial and radial directions. The SYM stress tensor is extracted from the subleading metric coefficients near the boundary, and the hydrodynamic approximation is constructed using second-order conformal hydrodynamic constitutive relations with transport coefficients from fluid/gravity duality.

## Key numbers
- The "proton" transverse width is σ = 3 (in units where the peak energy density μ±(x⊥=0) = 1).
- The smeared longitudinal delta function has width w = 0.375.
- At the collision point x = 0, the system begins to evolve hydrodynamically at t ≈ 1.2, after which the hydrodynamic residual Δ < 0.13.
- The transverse radius of the region where Δ ≤ 0.2 at t = 1.5 is R ~ 4, giving R Teff ~ 1 with average Teff ~ 0.25.
- The hydrodynamization time satisfies thydro Teff ~ 0.3.
- Inside the Δ = 0.15 contour, the first-order gradient measure R(1) ~ 0.85 and the second-order measure R(2) ~ 0.1.
- The anisotropy A ~ 4 in the hydrodynamic region.
- Upon fixing σ to the proton radius (~1 fm), the "proton" energy is ~20 GeV and Teff ~ 200 MeV.

## Context
This work extends holographic shock wave collision studies (Chesler 2009, Casalderrey-Solana 2013, Heller 2013) from 1+1 dimensional flows to a more realistic geometry with transverse localization, mimicking proton-nucleus collisions. The results contradict the expectation that hydrodynamics should fail when system size is comparable to the microscopic scale 1/Teff, and bolster the notion that debris in proton-nucleus collisions at RHIC and the LHC can be modeled hydrodynamically. The analysis opens the question of how small droplets can become before hydrodynamics breaks down, and suggests that near a critical energy Ec (where black hole formation ceases in the dual gravity), hydrodynamic behavior would turn off entirely.
