# Electrostrong Nuclear Disintegration in Condensed Matter

> Photo- and electro-disintegration techniques have been traditionally used for studying giant dipole resonances and through them nuclear structure. Over a long period, detailed theoretical models for the giant dipole resonances were proposed and low energy electron accelerators were constructed to perform experiments to test their veracity. More recently, through laser and "smart" material devices, electrons have been accelerated in condensed matter systems up to several tens of MeV. We discuss here the possibility of inducing electro-disintegration of nuclei through such devices. It involves a synthesis of electromagnetic and strong forces in condensed matter via giant dipole resonances to give an effective "electro-strong interaction" - a large coupling of electromagnetic and strong interactions in the tens of MeV range.

# Electrostrong Nuclear Disintegration in Condensed Matter

## Key claim
If electrons are accelerated to several tens of MeV in condensed matter systems (such as electrochemical cells or fractured piezoelectric materials), then nuclear reactions mediated by giant dipole resonances, termed "electrostrong processes", should occur at rates much higher than electroweak neutron production, yielding neutrons, fission products, and new nuclei.

## Method
The authors review the established physics of giant dipole resonances (GDRs), including their photoexcitation cross sections, energy systematics as a function of nucleon number A, and decay modes (primarily neutron emission). They then extend this framework to the case where electrons in condensed matter systems reach energies of 10–25 MeV, arguing that such electrons can excite GDRs via virtual photon exchange or bremsstrahlung, leading to nuclear disintegration. The paper presents specific example reactions, such as symmetric fission of iron-56 into aluminum and silicon, and discusses observable signatures including positron annihilation lines and new elemental abundances.

## Key numbers
- GDR energies lie in the (10–20) MeV range for heavy nuclei and (15–25) MeV for light nuclei.
- GDR peak cross section is approximately σ_GDR ≈ (3 × 10⁻³ A) barns, where A is the nucleon number.
- GDR width Γ_GDR is typically between 4 and 8 MeV, with narrower widths for magic nuclei.
- Neutron yield per electron per MeV from GDR excitation in thick targets is given by 8 × 10⁻⁶ (Z¹/² + 0.12 Z³/² – 0.001 Z⁵/²).
- For thick targets of C, Al, Cu, Ta, Pb, and U, neutron yields from GDR decays are around 10⁻³–10⁻² per electron for electrons from 10 to 36 MeV (Barber and George).
- The minimum photon energy to split ⁵⁶Fe into two ²⁷Al nuclei plus two neutrons is 42.350354 MeV.
- The thermal neutron capture cross section of ²⁷Al is approximately 12 barns.
- The integrated dipole strength up to 30 MeV is given by the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule: m₁ ≈ 60 (NZ/A) MeV·mb.

## Context
This paper extends the well-established physics of giant dipole resonances, first observed by Baldwin and Klaiber (1937) and interpreted by Goldhaber and Teller (1948), to the novel setting of condensed matter systems where electrons may be accelerated to tens of MeV without traditional accelerators. It builds on prior proposals by Widom and collaborators for electron acceleration in electrochemical cells and by Carpinteri and colleagues for neutron production in fractured piezoelectric materials. The work enables experimental searches for nuclear transmutations (e.g., aluminum and silicon appearing in iron samples) and for 511 keV positron annihilation gamma rays as signatures of high-energy electron production in condensed matter, complementing existing studies of laser-driven GDR excitation and medical accelerator activation.
