# Tin-accompanied and true ternary fission of $^{242}$Pu

> True ternary fission and Tin-accompanied ternary fission of $^{242}$Pu are studied by using Three Cluster Model. True ternary fission is considered as formation of heavy fragments in the region $28\leq Z_1,Z_2,Z_3\leq 38$, with comparable masses. The possible fission channels are predicted from potential-energy calculations. Interaction potentials, Q-values and relative yields for all possible fragmentations in equatorial and collinear configurations are calculated and compared to each other. It is found out that ternary fission with formation of a double magic nucleus like $^{132}Sn$ is more probable than the other fragmentations. Also the kinetic energies of the fragments for the group $Z_1=32$, $Z_2=32$ and $Z_3=30$ are calculated for all combinations in the collinear geometry, as a sequential decay.

## Key claim

The paper claims that for $^{242}$Pu, ternary fission accompanied by a double magic $^{132}$Sn nucleus is more probable than true ternary fission (three fragments of comparable mass), based on potential-energy calculations that show the $^{132}$Sn channel has a barrier roughly 30 MeV lower than any true ternary channel. The hedge is that this conclusion rests on the Three Cluster Model using the proximity potential (Prox2010) and assumes spherical fragments in their ground states, with no experimental verification.

## Method

The authors used the Three Cluster Model (TCM) to calculate interaction potentials (Coulomb plus Prox2010 nuclear potential), Q-values, and penetrabilities via the one-dimensional WKB approximation for all possible fragmentations of $^{242}$Pu. For true ternary fission, they considered 14 groups of fragments with atomic numbers $28 \leq Z_1, Z_2, Z_3 \leq 38$ under the condition $Z_3 \leq Z_2 \leq Z_1$, evaluating both equatorial and collinear geometries. For Tin-accompanied fission, they fixed $^{132}$Sn as one fragment and varied the other two. Kinetic energies for the most favorable group ($Z_1=32$, $Z_2=32$, $Z_3=30$) were calculated assuming a sequential decay in collinear geometry.

## Key numbers

- The group $Z_1=32$, $Z_2=32$, $Z_3=30$ had the lowest minimum interaction potential among all 14 true ternary groups.
- The combination $^{82}\text{Ge}+^{78}\text{Zn}+^{82}\text{Ge}$ (with $N=50$ for both Ge isotopes) showed the minimum interaction potential and maximum yield within the $Z_1=32$, $Z_2=32$, $Z_3=30$ group.
- For Tin-accompanied fission, the lowest barrier in collinear geometry belonged to the combination $^{132}\text{Sn}+^{22}\text{O}+^{88}\text{Kr}$.
- The ternary fragmentation potential barrier with $^{132}\text{Sn}$ as the fixed fragment was almost 30 MeV lower than the true ternary groups shown in the comparison figure.
- In the sequential decay calculation for the $Z_1=32$, $Z_2=32$, $Z_3=30$ group, the middle fragment ($^{A_3}\text{Zn}$) took a very small part of the total kinetic energy, with the two Ge fragments carrying most of it.

## Context

This work extends previous studies by the same group on ternary fission of $^{242}$Pu, which had examined light third fragments (Zadehrafi et al.) and $^{34}\text{Mg}$-accompanied fission (Zadehrafi et al.), and compares with results for $^{252}\text{Cf}$ from Denisov et al. The finding that collinear geometry is preferred over equatorial agrees with earlier work by the group and others (refs. 39, 42, 46 in the paper). The result that closed neutron shells are more effective than closed proton shells in lowering potential barriers is consistent with prior studies by Sandulescu et al. and Ismail et al. The paper enables future experimental searches for ternary fission of $^{242}$Pu to focus on $^{132}\text{Sn}$-accompanied channels rather than true ternary channels, and suggests that the middle fragment in collinear geometry may escape detection due to its very low kinetic energy.
