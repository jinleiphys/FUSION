# Explaining Snowball-in-hell Phenomena in Heavy-ion Collisions Using a Novel Thermodynamic Variable

> A loosely bound hadronic molecule produced by a relativistic heavy-ion collision has been described as a ``snowball in hell'' since it emerges from a hadron resonance gas whose temperature is orders of magnitude larger than the binding energy of the molecule. This remarkable phenomenon can be explained in terms of a novel thermodynamic variable called the ``contact'' that is conjugate to the binding momentum of the molecule. The production rate of the molecule can be expressed in terms of the contact density at the kinetic freezeout of the hadron resonance gas. It approaches a nonzero limit as the binding energy goes to 0.

# Explaining Snowball-in-hell Phenomena in Heavy-ion Collisions Using a Novel Thermodynamic Variable

## Key claim
The production rate of a loosely bound hadronic molecule in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can be expressed in terms of the contact density at kinetic freezeout of the hadron resonance gas, and this multiplicity approaches a nonzero limit as the binding energy goes to zero, contradicting the intuition that production should vanish for vanishing binding energy. The derivation relies on the contact density scaling with anomalous dimension 4/3 near the unitary fixed point before crossing over to conventional 1/V scaling.

## Method
The authors introduce the contact, a thermodynamic variable conjugate to the binding momentum, originally developed for ultracold Fermi gases by Tan (2005). They derive the molecule multiplicity from the contact density at kinetic freezeout using a toy model of an expanding hadron resonance gas with decreasing temperature and increasing volume. The contact density is approximated by the leading term in the virial expansion for the molecule's constituents, and the crossover time from anomalous to conventional scaling is estimated using the mean pion distance relative to the molecule's constituent separation. The resulting expression for the multiplicity ratio of two loosely bound molecules is then applied to the hypertriton and the χc1(3872) charm-meson molecule.

## Key numbers
- Deuteron binding energy: 2.225 MeV; mean constituent separation r_d = 2.16 fm.
- ALICE deuteron mean multiplicity in 0-10% centrality Pb-Pb at sqrt(s_NN)=2.76 TeV: (9.8 ± 1.6) × 10^{-2}.
- Kinetic freezeout temperature T_kf = 78.3 ± 1.6 MeV with pion chemical potential μ_πkf ≈ 90 MeV from blast-wave fits.
- Mean pion distance at kinetic freezeout: r_πkf = 1.61 fm.
- Extracted phenomenological parameter κ_d = 0.18 ± 0.04 for the deuteron.
- Hypertriton Λ separation energy: 148 ± 40 keV (average of existing measurements).
- ALICE hypertriton mean multiplicity (times branching fraction to ³He π⁻) in 0-10% centrality: (3.67 ± 0.74) × 10^{-5}.
- Predicted hypertriton multiplicity: (10.4 ± 3.9) × 10^{-5}; multiplied by 25% branching fraction, consistent with ALICE result.
- χc1(3872) binding energy relative to D*0 D̅0 threshold: -50 ± 93 keV.
- Predicted χc1(3872) mean multiplicity in 0-10% centrality Pb-Pb at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV: (23.4 ± 7.8) × 10^{-5}.

## Context
This work extends the concept of the contact, introduced by Tan (2005) for strongly interacting Fermi gases and verified experimentally with ultracold atoms (Jin, 2010), to relativistic heavy-ion collisions. It addresses the "snowball in hell" puzzle, the observation that loosely bound hadronic molecules like the deuteron, hypertriton, and χc1(3872) survive in a hadron resonance gas with temperature orders of magnitude above their binding energies. Previous theoretical approaches include thermal models (Braun-Munzinger, 2018) and coalescence models (BP-1963; Nagle, 1996; Scheibl, 1998), but these cannot easily describe transverse momentum distributions or depend on detailed molecular properties. The contact-based approach enables predictions of molecule multiplicities from the contact density at kinetic freezeout, with the hypertriton prediction consistent with ALICE data. This method can be extended to other loosely bound molecules such as T_cc⁺(3875) and to transverse momentum distributions, and may apply to cold-atom systems where the scattering length can be controlled experimentally.
