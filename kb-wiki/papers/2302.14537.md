# Signatures of quark deconfinement through the r-modes of twin stars

> The observation and distinction of two compact stars with an identical mass but a different radius would be a clear sign of hadron-quark phase transition in nuclear matter. Motivated by studies searching for significant deviations in the observables of twin stars, we investigate the differences that manifest in their r-mode instability windows and spin-down evolution. Firstly, we obtain a set of hybrid equations of state (which predict the existence of a third stable branch of compact objects) by employing the well-known Maxwell construction within the phenomenological framework of constant speed of sound parametrization. Then, we systematically study the influence of certain parameters, such as the energy density jump (in the resulting hybrid equation of state) and the crust elasticity, on the deviations appearing in the r-mode instability windows and spin-down evolution of twin stars. We conclude that two stars with an identical mass and fairly similar spin frequency and temperature, may behave differently with respect to r-modes. Thus, the future possible detection of gravitational waves (due to unstable r-modes) from a star laying in the stable region of the frequency-temperature plane would be a strong indication for the existence of twin stars. Furthermore, we consider current data for the spin frequencies and temperatures of observed pulsars and compare them to the predictions made from equations of state employed in this study. We find that, depending on the transition density and the rigidness of the crust, hybrid equations of state may be a viable solution for the explanation of existing data.

## Key claim

The paper claims that twin stars (two compact stars with identical mass but different radii, indicating a hadron-quark phase transition) will exhibit measurably different r-mode instability windows and spin-down evolution, meaning that a future detection of gravitational waves from unstable r-modes in a star that lies in the stable region of the frequency-temperature plane would be a strong indication for the existence of twin stars. The hedge is that this conclusion depends on the specific parameters of the energy density jump, transition density, and crust rigidity, and that hybrid equations of state may be a viable solution for explaining existing pulsar data only under certain parameter choices.

## Method

The authors constructed hybrid equations of state using the Maxwell construction with a constant speed of sound parametrization for quark matter, combining hadronic models (DD2 and NL3) with a quark phase where the speed of sound was set equal to the speed of light. They then computed the r-mode instability windows by balancing gravitational radiation driving timescales against damping from bulk viscosity, shear viscosity, and crust-core boundary layer dissipation, using standard expressions for both hadronic and quark matter viscosities. Finally, they modeled the spin-down evolution due to unstable r-modes coupled with standard cooling via the modified Urca process, and compared the resulting critical frequency curves to observed spin frequencies and temperatures of pulsars in low-mass X-ray binaries.

## Key numbers

- The critical energy density jump for twin star existence is given by \(\Delta \mathcal{E}_{\rm cr} = \frac{1}{2}\mathcal{E}_{\rm tr} + \frac{3}{2}P_{\rm tr}\), where \(\mathcal{E}_{\rm tr}\) and \(P_{\rm tr}\) are the energy density and pressure at the transition.
- The Kepler frequency is approximately \(\Omega_{\rm K} = \frac{2}{3}\Omega_0\) where \(\Omega_0 = \sqrt{\pi G \overline{\rho}}\) and \(\overline{\rho} = 3M/4\pi R^3\) is the mean density.
- For twin stars, the Kepler frequency of the hybrid branch may be up to 20% higher than that of the hadronic branch due to different radius values.
- The critical frequency scales with radius as \(\Omega_c \sim R^{-3/2}\) at low temperatures and \(\Omega_c \sim R^{-3/4}\) at high temperatures.
- The bulk viscosity for quark matter is given by \(\xi_{BV}^Q = \alpha T^2 / [(\kappa\Omega)^2 + \beta T^4]\) with \(\alpha T^2 = 6.66 \times 10^{20} (\mu_d/{\rm MeV})^3 (m_s/{\rm MeV})^4 T_9^2\) g cm\(^{-1}\) s\(^{-3}\) and \(\beta T^4 = 3.57 \times 10^{-8} (\mu_d/{\rm MeV})^6 (1 + m_s^2/4\mu_d^2)^2 T_9^4\) s\(^{-2}\), where \(m_s = 100\) MeV and \(\kappa = 2/3\).
- The shear viscosity for quark matter is \(\eta_q = 5 \times 10^{15} (0.1/\alpha_s)^{3/2} (\rho/\rho_0)^{14/9} T_9^{-5/3}\) g cm\(^{-1}\) s\(^{-1}\) with \(\alpha_s = 0.1\).
- The cooling model uses \(T(t) = [t/t_c + (10^9 {\rm K}/T_i)^6]^{-1/6} \times 10^9\) K with \(t_c \simeq 1\) year and initial temperature \(T_i \simeq 10^{11}\) K.

## Context

This work extends studies of twin stars by Lyra et al. (2023) on thermal evolution, Tan et al. (2022) on binary Love universal relations, and Landry and Chakravarti (2022) on tidal deformabilities, by providing the first systematic investigation of r-mode instability window deviations in twin stars. It builds on the finding by Papazoglou et al. (2016) that the r-mode instability window is sensitive to stellar radius, and on prior work showing that bulk viscosity in quark matter (Madsen, 1992; Jaikumar, 2008) can stabilize r-modes. The results enable future gravitational wave detections from r-modes to serve as a smoking-gun signature of the hadron-quark phase transition, and suggest that hybrid equations of state may explain current low-mass X-ray binary data depending on transition density and crust rigidity.
