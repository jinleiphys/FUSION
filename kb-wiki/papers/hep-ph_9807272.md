# Free energy of bubbles and droplets in the quark-hadron phase transition

> Using the MIT bag model, we calculate the free energy of droplets of quark-gluon plasma in a bulk hadronic medium, and of hadronic bubbles in a bulk quark-gluon plasma, under the assumption of vanishing chemical potentials. We investigate the validity of the multiple reflection expansion approximation, and we advise a novel procedure for calculating finite-size corrections to the free energy of hadronic bubbles in a bulk quark-gluon plasma. While our results agree largely with earlier calculations, we show that the usual multiple reflection expansion should be used with caution, and we propose a modification of the multiple reflection expansion, which makes this approximation agree nicely with direct numerical calculations. The results should be of relevance in connection with the cosmological quark-hadron transition as well as for ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions.

# Free energy of bubbles and droplets in the quark-hadron phase transition

## Key claim

The multiple reflection expansion (MRE) approximation for the density of states, commonly used to calculate finite-size corrections to the free energy of quark-gluon plasma droplets and hadronic bubbles, is shown to be inaccurate and sometimes unphysical (producing negative densities of states) at small radii; the authors propose a modified MRE with a physically motivated truncation of the density of states below a certain momentum threshold, which brings the approximation into agreement with direct numerical calculations, but they caution that the usual MRE should be used with caution even at larger radii where it is a good approximation to the density of states, because errors at low momenta propagate through the free energy integral.

## Method

The authors calculate the free energy of quark-gluon plasma droplets in a hadronic medium and of hadronic bubbles in a quark-gluon plasma using the MIT bag model with three quark flavors (u, d, s with m_s = 150 MeV) and eight non-interacting gluons, assuming zero chemical potentials and a transition temperature T_0 = 150 MeV. For plasma droplets, they perform a direct sum over discrete energy levels from the bag model boundary conditions and compare with the MRE approximation; for hadronic bubbles, they introduce a novel "concentric spheres method" that extracts the surface free energy from a calculation of particles between two concentric spheres, and compare this with the phase shift approach of Mardor and Svetitsky (1992) and with the MRE with inverted radius (MRE(-R)).

## Key numbers

- Bag constant B = 312.6 MeV/fm^3 = (221.4 MeV)^4, fixed by demanding bulk pressure balance at T_0 = 150 MeV
- Transition temperature T_0 = 150 MeV
- Quark masses: m_u = m_d = 0, m_s = 150 MeV; pion mass m_pi = 138 MeV
- For a plasma droplet at T = 152 MeV, the energy barrier separating the R=0 and R=infinity minima occurs for R approximately 2 fm
- For gluons in the vacuum bubble case at T = 152 MeV, the MRE(-R) curve has an offset of about 1000 MeV relative to the concentric spheres method
- For massless quarks and gluons in the limit RT << 1, the free energy behaves as R^3 (no terms proportional to R or R^2 appear), in contrast to the MRE(-R) which has a curvature term proportional to R

## Context

This work extends and critically examines the calculations of Mardor and Svetitsky (1992), who first studied the free energy of quark-gluon plasma droplets and hadronic bubbles within the MIT bag model and observed a peculiar negative minimum in the free energy of hadron bubbles above the transition temperature. The authors confirm the earlier numerical results but show that the standard multiple reflection expansion, developed by Balian and Bloch (1970), is unreliable for these systems; they demonstrate that the MRE(-R) conjecture (inverting the sign of R to go from droplets to bubbles) works reasonably for quarks but poorly for gluons. The results are relevant for the cosmological quark-hadron transition and for ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions, and the improved MRE with truncation enables more reliable calculations of nucleation rates and mixed-phase configurations such as the "Swiss cheese" strangelet structure suggested by earlier work.
