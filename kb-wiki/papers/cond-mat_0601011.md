# Spin noise spectroscopy to probe quantum states of ultracold fermionic atomic gases

> Ultracold alkali atoms provide experimentally accessible model systems for probing quantum states that manifest themselves at the macroscopic scale. Recent experimental realizations of superfluidity in dilute gases of ultracold fermionic (half-integer spin) atoms offer exciting opportunities to directly test theoretical models of related many-body fermion systems that are inaccessible to experimental manipulation, such as neutron stars and quark-gluon plasmas. However, the microscopic interactions between fermions are potentially quite complex, and experiments in ultracold gases to date cannot clearly distinguish between the qualitatively different microscopic models that have been proposed. Here, we theoretically demonstrate that optical measurements of electron spin noise -- the intrinsic, random fluctuations of spin -- can probe the entangled quantum states of ultracold fermionic atomic gases and unambiguously reveal the detailed nature of the interatomic interactions. We show that different models predict different sets of resonances in the noise spectrum, and once the correct effective interatomic interaction model is identified, the line-shapes of the spin noise can be used to constrain this model. Further, experimental measurements of spin noise in classical (Boltzmann) alkali vapors are used to estimate the expected signal magnitudes for spin noise measurements in ultracold atom systems and to show that these measurements are feasible.

## Key claim

Electron spin noise spectroscopy can distinguish between qualitatively different microscopic models of interatomic interactions in ultracold fermionic gases, specifically the 2-level model versus multi-level models, by revealing different sets of resonances in the noise spectrum; however, this ability depends on the magnetic field regime and the orientation of the magnetic field relative to the laser propagation direction.

## Method

The authors theoretically calculate the electron spin noise spectrum via Faraday rotation spectroscopy for ultracold fermionic alkali gases, using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) formalism to compute zero-temperature spin-spin response functions. They compare predictions from a 2-level model, which only includes interactions between the two lowest hyperfine states, and a 3-level model that additionally includes a closed-channel hyperfine state, focusing on the specific case of potassium-40. Experimental spin noise measurements in a classical (Boltzmann) vapor of potassium-41 at 184 degrees Celsius are used to estimate expected signal magnitudes for ultracold systems.

## Key numbers

- Integrated spin noise values for four peaks in a potassium-41 vapor at 184 degrees Celsius: 1.3, 2.3, 2.6, and 2.1 microradians (measured at density N = 7.3 x 10^13 cm^-3, with probe laser detuned 100 GHz from the D1 transition at 770 nm, B_perp = 25.6 G, 10 mm cell length, 65 micron beam diameter)
- The overall magnitude of detected spin noise in the classical vapor is a factor 2.7 lower than theoretical expectation
- For an ultracold potassium gas with density 10^13 cm^-3 and trap length 0.2 mm, similar noise peak magnitudes are expected with a 20 micron diameter laser detuned 15 GHz from the optical resonance
- The dimensionless parameter characterizing the system is eta = (k_F a)^(-1), where k_F is the Fermi momentum and a is the s-wave scattering length

## Context

This work extends theoretical proposals for using noise spectroscopies to probe quantum systems, specifically addressing the unresolved issue of whether the effective interatomic interactions in ultracold fermionic gases follow the Fermi-Bose model (Holland 2001, Timmermans 1999) or multi-level Hamiltonian models (Bruun 2004, Parish 2005). The paper enables experimental distinction between these competing models through spin noise measurements, with potassium-40 identified as an ideal test case because its s-wave Feshbach resonance involves only three interacting atomic levels. The approach also connects to broader questions about universality in dilute degenerate fermionic systems and their relation to neutron stars (Pethick 2004) and quark-gluon plasmas.
