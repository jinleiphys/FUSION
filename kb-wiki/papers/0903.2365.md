# Beam-like Excitations of Kerr-Schild Geometry and Semiclassical Mechanism of Black-Hole Evaporation

> It has been observed that exact solutions for electromagnetic (EM) excitations of the Kerr-Schild (KS) geometry form outgoing beams which have very strong back reaction to metric and break the black hole horizon. As a result, interaction of a black hole with nearby electromagnetic field and electromagnetic vacuum has to cover the horizon by a set of fluctuating microholes. We integrate and analyze the Debney-Kerr-Schild equations for electromagnetic excitations of a black-hole and obtain that the exact solutions for outgoing radiation contain two related but radically different components which shed light on a possible semi-classical mechanism of black-hole evaporation: a) first component consists of the singular beam pulses which perforate horizon, breaking its impenetrability, and b) another component is regular and responsible for the loss of mass similar to the known Vaidya `shining star' radiation. We show also that the mysterious twosheeted twistor structure of the Kerr-Schild geometry corresponds to a holographic structure of quantum black hole spacetimes predicted by Stephens, t' Hooft and Whiting. The resulting Kerr-Schild geometry of fluctuating twistor-beams takes an intermediate position between the classical and quantum gravity.

## Key claim

The paper claims that exact electromagnetic excitations of the Kerr-Schild geometry produce outgoing beam-like solutions that break the black hole horizon, but this effect is conditional on the electromagnetic field being described by a non-constant holomorphic function of the complex angular coordinate Y, which necessarily introduces singular beams; the resulting semiclassical mechanism of black-hole evaporation contains two distinct components, one singular and one regular, and the geometry takes an intermediate position between classical and quantum gravity.

## Method

The author integrates and analyzes the Debney-Kerr-Schild (DKS) equations for electromagnetic excitations of a black hole in the Kerr-Schild formalism, using the null tetrad and complex angular coordinate Y to describe the Kerr congruence. Exact stationary and time-dependent solutions are constructed from holomorphic functions ψ(Y, τ) that generate singular beam pulses along the null rays of the Kerr congruence, and the back reaction on the metric is computed via the function H = (mr - |ψ|²/2)/(r² + a² cos²θ). The time-dependent case introduces a second function γ from the DKS equations, which is regularized by tuning the free function φ to cancel poles, yielding a subclass of self-regularized solutions γ_(reg).

## Key numbers

- The function H determining the metric is H = (mr - |ψ|²/2)/(r² + a² cos²θ), where ψ(Y) is any holomorphic function of the complex angular coordinate Y.
- The elementary beam solution has ψ_i(Y) = q_i/(Y - Y_i), where q_i is the charge and Y_i = e^{iφ_i} tan(θ_i/2) specifies the beam direction.
- The retarded-time parameter for nonstationary solutions is τ = t - r - ia cos θ.
- The averaged mass loss from the regularized field is given by ⟨m⟩_t = m₀ + 2π Σ_k ω_k ⟨q_k q̄_k / |P_{kk}|²⟩, where ω_k is the carrier frequency and P_{kk} = (1 + Y_k Ȳ_k)/√2.
- The averaged mass evaporation rate is ⟨ṁ⟩_t = -½ Σ_k ω_k² ⟨q_k q̄_k / |P_{kk}|⁴⟩.

## Context

This work extends the exact Kerr-Schild solutions of Debney, Kerr and Schild (1968) and the author's prior analysis of nonstationary electromagnetic solutions (Burinskii, 2004) to show that the twosheeted twistor structure of Kerr-Schild geometry corresponds to the holographic structure of quantum black hole spacetimes predicted by Stephens, t' Hooft and Whiting (1994). The results enable a semiclassical description of black-hole evaporation that bridges classical gravity and quantum gravity, with the singular beam pulses providing a classical analog of quantum tunnelling and the regular γ component corresponding to Vaidya-type radiation; the approach also connects to the Kerr-Schild multicenter solutions (Burinskii, 1995) and the twistor-based scattering amplitudes program of Nair and Witten.
