# Taming the Pion Cloud of the Nucleon

> We present a light-front determination of the pionic contribution to the nucleon self-energy, $Σ_π$, to second-order in pion-baryon coupling constants that allows the pion-nucleon vertex function to be treated in a model-independent manner constrained by experiment. The pion mass $μ$ dependence of $Σ_π$ is consistent with chiral perturbation theory results for small values of $μ$ and is also linearly dependent on $μ$ for larger values, in accord with the results of lattice QCD calculations. The derivative of $Σ_π$ with respect to $μ^2$ yields the dominant contribution to the pion content, which is consistent with the $\bar{d}-\bar{u}$ difference observed experimentally in the violation of the Gottfried sum rule.

# Taming the Pion Cloud of the Nucleon

## Key claim
The authors present a light-front treatment of the pionic contribution to the nucleon self-energy that resolves ambiguities in the pion-nucleon vertex function by projecting the intermediate baryon onto its mass shell, allowing the use of on-mass-shell form factors constrained by experimental data. The method is model-independent for the vertex function and reproduces both the linear dependence of the nucleon mass on pion mass seen in lattice QCD and the flavor asymmetry of the nucleon sea, but the results are limited to second-order in pion-baryon coupling constants and neglect higher-order contributions estimated at about 10% uncertainty.

## Method
The authors evaluate the nucleon self-energy Feynman diagrams for intermediate nucleon and Delta baryon states using light-front coordinates, integrating over the light-front energy k^- in the upper half-plane so that only the pole where the intermediate baryon is on its mass shell contributes. The pion-nucleon vertex function is expressed in terms of the invariant momentum transfer t using the Goldberger-Treiman relation, which relates the pion-nucleon form factor to the experimentally constrained axial form factor, and a dipole parametrization with axial mass M_A = 1.03 GeV is employed. The total pionic self-energy is computed as the sum of nucleon and Delta intermediate state contributions, and the derivative with respect to mu^2 yields the pion content relevant for deep inelastic scattering.

## Key numbers
- Nucleon mass M = 0.939 GeV, pion decay constant f_pi = 92.6 MeV, pion-nucleon coupling constant g_piN = 13.2, axial coupling G_A(0) = 1.267 ± 0.04
- Axial mass M_A = 1.03 ± 0.04 GeV, with a lower value of 0.85 GeV obtained from very low Q^2 extractions
- The term independent of pion mass provides a -0.222 M correction to the bare nucleon mass
- The term of order mu^3 reproduces the standard expression -3g_A^2/(32 pi f_pi^2) mu^3
- Bare masses M_0 = 2.42 GeV and tilde-M_0 = 2.06 GeV are added to reproduce the lattice data point at mu/(4 pi f_pi) = 0.252 (mu = 293 MeV)
- The nucleonic contribution to the sea asymmetry D_pi is 0.173, the Delta contribution is -0.064, giving a total of 0.109, within the experimental range 0.118 ± 0.012
- Varying M_A within its stated range changes Sigma_pi by 5% or less for mu > 0.5 GeV

## Context
This work extends the understanding of pion cloud effects in nucleon structure by resolving ambiguities in the treatment of the pion-nucleon vertex function that have plagued phenomenological calculations, particularly those using the Rarita-Schwinger formalism for spin-3/2 baryons as discussed in nucl-th/9905065. The method reproduces the "surprisingly linear" dependence of nucleon mass on pion mass found in lattice QCD simulations reviewed in hep-lat/0608010 and by the LHP collaboration (arXiv:0806.4549), and provides a natural explanation of the Gottfried sum rule violation measured by the NuSea collaboration (Towell 2001). The approach differs from the heavy baryon limit used by Leinweber (1999) and the relativistic expansion procedure of Becher (1999) by including all recoil terms and using a cut-off procedure constrained by experimental data. The results enable improved calculations of pion cloud effects on nucleon-nucleon scattering and elastic electromagnetic form factors.
