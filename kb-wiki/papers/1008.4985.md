# Neutrino cooling and spin-down of rapidly rotating compact stars

> The gravitational-wave instability of r-modes in rapidly rotating compact stars is believed to spin them down to angular frequencies of about a tenth of the Kepler frequency soon after their birth in a Supernova. We point out that the r-mode perturbation also impacts the neutrino cooling and viscosity in hot compact stars via processes that restore weak equilibrium. We illustrate this fact with a simple model of spin-down due to gravitational wave emission in compact stars composed entirely of three-flavor degenerate quark matter (a strange quark star). Non-equilibrium neutrino cooling of this oscillating fluid matter is quantified. Our results imply that a consistent treatment of thermal and spin-frequency evolution of a young and hot compact star is a requisite in estimating the persistence of gravitational waves from such a source.

## Key claim

The r-mode perturbation in rapidly rotating compact stars affects neutrino cooling and viscosity via processes that restore weak equilibrium, meaning that thermal evolution, r-mode evolution, and spin-down are all coupled and must be treated consistently; the authors illustrate this with a simple model of spin-down due to gravitational wave emission in compact stars composed entirely of three-flavor degenerate quark matter (a strange quark star), finding that non-equilibrium neutrino rates change the cooling history considerably.

## Method

The authors adopt the phenomenological model of Owen (1998) for r-mode evolution and spin-down, solving coupled differential equations for the mode amplitude and angular velocity. They compute bulk and shear viscosity damping timescales for strange quark matter including both leptonic and non-leptonic weak processes, and evaluate non-equilibrium neutrino emissivities from the r-mode-induced density fluctuations that destroy weak equilibrium. The thermal evolution is determined by balancing neutrino cooling against viscous heating, using an equation of state for degenerate three-flavor quark matter with electrons.

## Key numbers

- The stellar model has gravitational mass 1.49 solar masses and radius 9.47 km (Newtonian gravity), with r-mode frequency 0.88 kHz
- Initial spin frequency assumed to be the Kepler frequency, approximately 1200 Hz
- With equilibrium neutrino rates, the star spins down from its initial frequency in about 10,000 seconds (a few hours) to about one-third of the starting value
- With non-equilibrium neutrino rates, the star enters the stable region in about 1,000 seconds (less than one hour)
- The stable region hiatus lasts approximately 100 years (about 10^9 seconds) for equilibrium cooling, but only about 15 years (5 x 10^8 seconds) for non-equilibrium cooling
- The second spin-down epoch lasts about 10 years (10^8 seconds) for equilibrium rates, but about 4 months (10^7 seconds) for non-equilibrium rates
- The leptonic viscosity can be important if the initial spin frequency is less than or approximately 600 Hz

## Context

This work extends the r-mode instability model of Owen (1998) and the bulk viscosity calculations for strange quark matter by Madsen (1992, 1993) and Sa'd (2007), incorporating the previously neglected effect that r-mode perturbations alter neutrino cooling rates. The results imply that gravitational wave emission epochs from isolated quark stars are shorter than previously estimated, narrowing the detection window for experiments such as LIGO and VIRGO. The authors note that similar effects should occur for neutron stars with hadronic equations of state, and suggest future work should extend the analysis to accreting neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries and include more realistic saturation amplitudes from Bondarescu (2007).
