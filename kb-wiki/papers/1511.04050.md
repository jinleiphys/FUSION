# Chiral Magnetic and Vortical Effects in High-Energy Nuclear Collisions --- A Status Report

> The interplay of quantum anomalies with magnetic field and vorticity results in a variety of novel non-dissipative transport phenomena in systems with chiral fermions, including the quark-gluon plasma. Among them is the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) -- the generation of electric current along an external magnetic field induced by chirality imbalance. Because the chirality imbalance is related to the global topology of gauge fields, the CME current is topologically protected and hence non-dissipative even in the presence of strong interactions. As a result, the CME and related quantum phenomena affect the hydrodynamical and transport behavior of strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma, and can be studied in relativistic heavy ion collisions where strong magnetic fields are created by the colliding ions. Evidence for the CME and related phenomena has been reported by the STAR Collaboration at Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider at BNL, and by the ALICE Collaboration at the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. The goal of the present review is to provide an elementary introduction into the physics of anomalous chiral effects, to describe the current status of experimental studies in heavy ion physics, and to outline the future work, both in experiment and theory, needed to eliminate the existing uncertainties in the interpretation of the data.

## Key claim

The review argues that experimental evidence for the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) and related anomalous chiral transport phenomena in heavy-ion collisions exists but remains ambiguous, because conventional physics backgrounds (such as local charge conservation and momentum conservation) can mimic the observed charge-dependent correlations, and no definitive conclusion can be drawn until these uncertainties are resolved through further dedicated measurements and improved theoretical calculations.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental measurements of the three-particle azimuthal correlator (gamma correlator) and the charge-dependent elliptic flow (v2) as functions of centrality, beam energy, and charge asymmetry in Au+Au collisions at RHIC and Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC. These observables are compared with predictions from the Chiral Magnetic Effect, Chiral Magnetic Wave, and Chiral Vortical Effect, as well as with background calculations from event generators (MEVSIM, UrQMD, HIJING) and Blast Wave models incorporating local charge conservation.

## Key numbers

* The gamma correlator difference between opposite-charge and same-charge pairs in Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV shows the "right" ordering consistent with CME expectations, with the signal having a pseudorapidity width of about one unit of rapidity.
* In the most central 0-1% U+U collisions at 193 GeV, the signal (gamma_OS minus gamma_SS) multiplied by N_part disappears while v2 remains about 2.5%, demonstrating smallness of background.
* The slope parameter r from the v2 versus charge asymmetry measurement for pions in 30-40% Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV is positive, consistent with CMW expectations, with r values showing a rise-and-fall centrality dependence for beam energies from 19.6 to 200 GeV.
* At beam energies of 11.5 and 7.7 GeV, the slope parameters are consistent with zero within large statistical uncertainties.
* The third harmonic three-particle correlator in Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV shows correlation strength about 10 times smaller than the second harmonic, suggesting smallness of local charge conservation effects.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of anomalous chiral transport developed by Kharzeev and collaborators (2007-2010) and summarizes experimental results from the STAR Collaboration at RHIC and the ALICE Collaboration at the LHC. The work addresses the ongoing debate between CME/CMW interpretations and background explanations such as local charge conservation (Schlichting 2010) and Blast Wave models. The review identifies key open questions requiring future work: the need for higher statistics at lower beam energies in the RHIC Beam Energy Scan phase II, more accurate estimation of the parameter kappa, and quantitative understanding of normal viscous hydrodynamic charge transport (Hatta 2015) to firmly establish the observation of anomalous chiral effects.
