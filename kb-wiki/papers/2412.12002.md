# Non-Radial Oscillation Modes in Hybrid Stars with Hyperons and Delta Baryons

> We study the effects of hyperons, delta baryons, and quark matter phase transitions on $f$-mode oscillations in neutron stars. Using the density-dependent relativistic mean-field model (DDME2) for the hadronic phase and the density-dependent quark mass (DDQM) model for the quark phase, we construct hadronic and hybrid equations of state (EoSs) consistent with astrophysical constraints. Including hyperons and delta baryons soften the EoS, reducing maximum mass, while phase transition to the quark matter further softens the EoS, decreasing the speed of sound and hence the maximum mass. We confirm the well-known overestimation of $f$-mode frequencies by the Cowling approximation (by about 10-30\%) compared to full General Relativity calculation, and show that this discrepancy persists across models including hyperons, $Δ$ baryons, and a phase transition to quark matter. While the discrepancy generally decreases with stellar mass, it increases near the maximum mass in the presence of a phase transition compared to EoSs without this phenomenology. We derive universal relations connecting the frequencies of the $f$-mode to the average density, compactness, and tidal deformability, finding significant deviations due to hyperons and delta baryons. These deviations could provide distinct observational signatures in gravitational wave data, offering new insights into dense matter physics and advancing gravitational wave asteroseismology of neutron star interiors. Empirical relations for mass-scaled and radius-scaled frequencies are also provided, highlighting the importance of GR calculations for accurate modeling.

## Key claim

The Cowling approximation overestimates f-mode frequencies by about 10-30% compared to full General Relativity calculations, and this discrepancy persists across all studied compositions including hyperons, delta baryons, and quark matter phase transitions; while the discrepancy generally decreases with stellar mass, it increases near the maximum mass specifically in the presence of a phase transition compared to equations of state without this phenomenology.

## Method

The authors constructed hadronic equations of state using the density-dependent relativistic mean-field model (DDME2) for nucleonic matter including hyperons and delta baryons, and the density-dependent quark mass (DDQM) model for quark matter, with a Maxwell construction for the first-order hadron-quark phase transition. They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for stellar structure and then computed non-radial f-mode oscillation frequencies by solving the full set of four coupled linear differential equations for metric and fluid perturbations in general relativity, comparing these results with the Cowling approximation where gravitational potential perturbations are neglected.

## Key numbers

- Cowling approximation overestimates f-mode frequencies by about 10-30% compared to full GR calculations
- Delta baryons have a mass of around 1232 MeV, about 30% heavier than nucleons
- Hyperons and delta baryons are expected to appear at densities in the range of 2-3 times nuclear saturation density (n0)
- The DDME2 model predicts nuclear saturation density n0 = 0.152 fm^-3, binding energy B/A = 16.14 MeV, incompressibility K0 = 252 MeV, symmetry energy S0 = 32.3 MeV, and symmetry energy slope L0 = 51 MeV
- For GW170817, the 90% credible interval for the f-mode frequency was estimated between 1.43 kHz and 2.90 kHz for the more massive neutron star and 1.48 kHz and 3.18 kHz for the less massive one

## Context

This work extends previous studies of non-radial oscillations in neutron stars that considered nucleonic and hyperonic compositions under the Cowling approximation (Pradhan 2020, Roy 2023, Maiti 2024) and studies with delta baryons but without metric perturbations (Kalita 2023). For the first time, the analysis includes delta baryons and hyperons in scenarios with a hadron-quark phase transition using full general relativity, building on prior work on radial oscillations with exotic phases (Rather 2023, Rather 2024). The universal relations derived between f-mode frequencies and average density, compactness, and tidal deformability could provide observational signatures in gravitational wave data from facilities like the Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer.
