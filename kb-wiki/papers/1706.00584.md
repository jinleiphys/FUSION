# Localisation and dimensionless studies in nuclei and many-body systems

> Dimensionless ratios characterizing many-body systems are a powerful tool to reveal the main universal quantities involved. The recently-introduced localisation parameter allow to study the occurrence of crystal, clusterisation, and quantum liquid states in many-body systems such as nuclei. Its concomitant use with other dimensionless quantities such as the quantality, allows to pinpoint fundamental lengthscales and dimensionless ratios at work in nuclei. Within the present approach, the impact of delocalisation on nuclear saturation is discussed. The transition from a homogeneous to a clusterised state in low density systems is studied. The spin-orbit effect in nuclei and fermionic systems is also reviewed in the light of this analysis. A generalised localisation parameter is derived, showing that the delocalisation properties are not always driven by the quantality. The concepts of quantum fluidity and mobility are finally introduced.

# Localisation and dimensionless studies in nuclei and many-body systems

## Key claim

The paper argues that three fundamental dimensionless ratios (the coupling constant, the inertia parameter, and the quantality) are sufficient to characterize many-body systems, and that the localisation parameter provides a finite-size generalization that can describe the transition from homogeneous to clusterised states in nuclei. The authors claim that delocalisation effects, rather than the hard-core repulsion alone, explain why nuclear saturation density is about one order of magnitude smaller than the packed density, but this conclusion depends on the specific values chosen for the interaction range and the hard-core size.

## Method

The authors derive analytic expressions for the localisation parameter, quantality, coupling constant, and inertia parameter from three basic quantities: the magnitude of the two-body interaction V'0, its equilibrium lengthscale a, and the constituent mass mN. They apply these dimensionless ratios to nuclear systems using a harmonic oscillator approximation for the confining potential and compare with relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) mean-field calculations using the covariant functional DD-ME2, with broken rotational, reflection, and gauge symmetries. The relation between the localisation parameter and the quantality is derived, and the Mott-like density for clusterisation is obtained by inverting the density-dependent localisation parameter expression.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ0 ≈ 0.16 fm⁻³, which is about one order of magnitude smaller than the packed density corresponding to nucleons in hard-core contact
- Hard-core size of nucleon-nucleon interaction: c ≈ 0.5 fm; equilibrium range of interaction: a ≈ 0.9 fm; interconstituent distance: r0 ≈ 1.2 fm
- Localisation parameter for an A ≈ 100 nucleus: αloc ≈ 1.5, corresponding to homogeneous density (quantum liquid regime)
- Quantality for nuclei: Λ ≈ 0.5, using V'0 ≈ 100 MeV and r0 ≈ 1 fm
- Coupling constant for strong interaction in nuclei: αS ≈ 0.5–1
- Inertia parameter for nuclei: η ≈ 9.4 (using nucleon mass and V'0 ≈ 100 MeV)
- Mott-like density for cluster transition: ρM ≈ ρ0/3, obtained from αloc = 1 for A = 4
- Number of constituents for cluster phase in quantum liquid systems: A0 ≈ few units to a couple of dozens (from A0 ≈ (2γ/Λ)^(3/2) with Λ ≈ 0.1)
- Packing density using rl ≈ 0.5 fm: ρl ≈ 1.8 fm⁻³
- For electrons in atoms: coupling constant αEM = 1/137, inertia parameter η ≈ 5×10⁴, quantality Λ ≈ 3.1

## Context

This work extends the use of dimensionless ratios in many-body physics, building on the quantality concept introduced by de Boer (1948) and applied to nuclei by Mottelson (1996), as well as on the localisation parameter previously introduced by the same authors (Ebran et al., 2012, 2013, 2014). The paper connects these approaches to the long-standing problem of nuclear saturation discussed by Bohr and Mottelson (1969), who noted that the transition from independent-particle motion to the crystalline state presents significant unsolved problems. The results enable a unified description of clusterisation in light nuclei, the Mott-like phase transition in expanding nuclear systems studied by Schuck et al. (2013) and Ebran et al. (2014), and the spin-orbit effect in fermionic systems. The authors also introduce new concepts of quantum fluidity and mobility, and suggest applications to other systems such as graphene.
