# Antineutron and antiproton nuclear interactions at very low energies

> Experimental annihilation cross sections of antineutrons and antiprotons at very low energies are compared. Features of Coulomb focusing are observed for $\bar p$ annihilation on protons. Direct comparisons for heavier targets are not straightforward due to lack of overlap between targets and energies of experimental results for $\bar p$ and $\bar n$. Nevertheless, the annihilation cross sections for $\bar n$ on nuclei cannot be described by an optical potential that fits well all the available data on $\bar p$ interactions with nuclei. Comparisons made with the help of this potential reveal in the $\bar n$ data features similar to Coulomb focusing. Direct comparisons between $\bar n$ and $\bar p$ annihilations at very low energies would be possible when $\bar p$ cross sections are measured on the same targets and at the same energies as the available cross sections for $\bar n$. Such measurements may be possible in the foreseeable future.

# Antineutron and antiproton nuclear interactions at very low energies

## Key claim
The annihilation cross sections for antineutrons on nuclei cannot be described by the same isoscalar optical potential that fits all available antiproton-nucleus data across threshold, even though such a potential successfully reproduces antiproton elastic scattering and annihilation on nuclei from sub-threshold up to 600 MeV/c. The discrepancy is largest below 100 MeV/c, where calculations underestimate experimental antineutron cross sections by up to a factor of 3-4, and the single available direct comparison on Sn suggests antineutron cross sections may be equal to or larger than antiproton cross sections, contrary to expectations from Coulomb focusing.

## Method
The author performed global fits of an isoscalar optical potential (with a Gaussian finite-range folding) to 90 data points from antiprotonic atom strong-interaction level shifts and widths across 29 nuclei from 16O to 208Pb, then tested the same potential against elastic scattering data for antiprotons on 12C, 40Ca, and 208Pb at 48 MeV (300 MeV/c) and against measured antiproton annihilation cross sections on Ne, Ni, Sn, and Pt. The same potential was then used to calculate total reaction cross sections (approximating annihilation cross sections) for antineutrons on the six targets studied by the OBELIX collaboration, and these predictions were compared directly to the experimental antineutron data.

## Key numbers
- The best fit to antiprotonic atom data gave chi-squared per degree of freedom = 2.2 for 90 data points across 29 nuclei (16O to 208Pb), with parameters Im b0 = (1.25 ± 0.05) fm and aG = (1.34 ± 0.05) fm from fits to elastic scattering on 12C and 40Ca (68 data points, chi-squared = 127).
- For antiproton annihilation on Ne at 57 MeV/c, the experimental cross section was 2210 ± 1105 mb versus a calculated value of 2760 mb; at 192.8 MeV/c, 956 ± 47 mb experimental versus 1040 mb calculated.
- For antiproton annihilation on Ni at 100 MeV/c, the experimental cross section was 3300 ± 1500 mb versus 3170 mb calculated; on Sn, 4200 ± 900 mb experimental versus 5560 mb calculated; on Pt, 8600 ± 4100 mb experimental versus 8620 mb calculated.
- For antineutrons on nuclei below 100 MeV/c, the optical potential that fits all antiproton data underestimates experimental annihilation cross sections by up to a factor of 3-4.
- To improve the antineutron fit, the range parameter aG had to be increased from 1.3 fm to 3.3 fm, implying an rms radius of 4.0 fm for the folding interaction.

## Context
This work extends the global optical model analysis of antiproton-nucleus interactions developed by Friedman, Gal, and collaborators (2005, 2007) from antiprotonic atoms and elastic scattering to comparisons with antineutron annihilation data from the OBELIX collaboration (Astrua et al., 2002). The key puzzle is that the same isoscalar potential that successfully describes antiproton interactions across threshold fails for antineutrons at very low energies, and the single available direct comparison on Sn (from the ASACUSA collaboration, 2011) suggests antineutron cross sections may exceed antiproton cross sections, contrary to the Coulomb focusing effect seen for antiproton-proton annihilation. The author proposes that measuring antiproton annihilation cross sections on the same six nuclear targets and at the same energies as the existing antineutron data would resolve this puzzle.
