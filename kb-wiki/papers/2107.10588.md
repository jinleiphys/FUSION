# A formalism to assess the accuracy of nuclear-structure weak interaction effects in precision $β$-decay studies

> Multiple high precision $β$-decay measurements are being carried out these days on various nuclei, in search of beyond the Standard Model signatures. These measurements necessitate accurate standard model theoretical predictions to be compared with. Motivated by the experimental surge, we present a formalism for such a calculation of $β$-decay observables, with controlled accuracy, based on a perturbative analysis of the theoretical observables related to the phenomena, including high order nuclear recoil and shape corrections. The accuracy of the corrections is analyzed by identifying a hierarchy of small parameters, related to the low momentum transfer characterizing $β$-decays. Furthermore, we show that the sub-percent uncertainties, targeted by on-going and planned experiments, entail an accuracy of the order of 10\% for the solution of the nuclear many body problem, which is well within the reach of modern nuclear theory for light to medium mass nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper presents a formalism for calculating nuclear-structure corrections to beta-decay observables with controlled theoretical accuracy, showing that the sub-percent experimental uncertainties targeted by current and planned experiments require only about 10% accuracy in the solution of the nuclear many-body problem, which is achievable for light to medium mass nuclei using modern nuclear theory.

## Method

The authors develop a perturbative expansion of beta-decay observables based on a multipole decomposition of the weak nuclear current, identifying a hierarchy of small parameters including the momentum-transfer parameter epsilon_qr = qR, the non-relativistic parameter epsilon_NR = P_Fermi/m_N, the recoil parameter epsilon_recoil = q/m_N, and the Coulomb parameter epsilon_c = alpha*Z_f. They derive general expressions for the differential decay rate including next-to-leading order shape and recoil corrections for allowed transitions, unique forbidden transitions, and non-unique forbidden transitions, expressing the corrections in terms of ratios of reduced nuclear matrix elements of Coulomb, longitudinal, electric, and magnetic multipole operators.

## Key numbers

- The momentum transfer in beta-decays is typically up to 10 MeV/c, giving epsilon_qr = qR approximately 0.01*A^(1/3) for an endpoint of about 2 MeV
- The non-relativistic expansion parameter epsilon_NR = P_Fermi/m_N is approximately 0.2
- The recoil correction epsilon_recoil = q/m_N is approximately 0.002 for an endpoint of about 2 MeV
- The Coulomb correction parameter epsilon_c = alpha*Z_f, where alpha is approximately 1/137
- The Coulomb displacement energy approximation for a homogeneously charged sphere is Delta_E_c approximately (6/5)*(alpha*Z_f/R)
- The Fierz interference term for Fermi transitions is b_F^(0+) = (m_e/q)*2*Re(<L_0^V>/<C_0^V>) plus higher order terms
- The beta-nu correlation coefficient for unique transitions at leading order is a_beta_nu^(J^((-1)^(J-1))) = -1/(2J+1)
- The nuclear model uncertainty epsilon_NM reaches a few percent in modern calculations (Gysbers 2019)

## Context

This work extends the formalism of multipole expansion for weak interactions originally developed by Walecka, and builds upon the treatment of Coulomb corrections by Holstein (1974, 1979) and Behrens (1969, 1982). The paper provides a systematic framework for assessing theoretical uncertainties in beta-decay observables needed for current experiments at SARAF (Israel), the National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory (USA), LPC CAEN (France), and the University of Washington CENPA, which target per-mill level accuracy in searches for beyond Standard Model physics. The formalism enables controlled comparisons between experimental measurements and Standard Model predictions, particularly for light nuclei where ab initio nuclear structure calculations are feasible.
