# Production cross sections of superheavy elements: insights from the dinuclear system model with high-quality microscopic nuclear masses

> To accurately predict the synthesis cross-sections of superheavy elements, identifying the optimal projectile-target combinations and the evaporation channels at specific collision energies, we have attempted to utilize high-quality microscopic nuclear masses (HQMNM) within the dinuclear system (DNS) model, which are obtained by fitting experimental data with the Skyrme energy density functional theory (DFT), as published in Phys. Lett. B 851 (2024) 138578. The atomic nuclear mass serves as a crucial input for the DNS model, as the Q-values and separation energies it generates directly influence the calculated fusion and survival probabilities. Our calculations have reproduced the experimental data for hot fusion and have been compared with results based on the finite-range droplet model (FRDM12) mass calculations. Compared to the FRDM12 mass results, we have found that the HQMNM provides a better fit to the experimental outcomes. For the specific reaction of \(^{48}\rm{Ca} + ^{243}\rm{Am} \rightarrow ^{291}\rm{Mc}^*\), we have conducted a detailed calculation of capture, fusion, and survival based on the HQMNM model and compared these with calculations based on other mass models. Based on these findings, we have systematically calculated available projectile target combinations for the synthesis of elements 119 and 120, and identified the optimal combinations. We provided the synthesis cross-sections, collision energies, and evaporation channels, offering a reference for conducting experiments on the synthesis of superheavy elements.

# Production cross sections of superheavy elements: insights from the dinuclear system model with high-quality microscopic nuclear masses

## Key claim

Using high-quality microscopic nuclear masses (HQMNM) from Skyrme DFT within the dinuclear system (DNS) model yields a better fit to experimental hot-fusion evaporation residue cross sections for superheavy elements than the finite-range droplet model (FRDM12), but the relative magnitude of excitation functions between the two mass models is not fixed and depends on the specific reaction system.

## Method

The authors employed the dinuclear system (DNS) model, which decomposes the evaporation residue cross section into capture probability (calculated via Hill-Wheeler formula with barrier distribution), fusion probability (solved from master equations for nucleon transfer across the potential energy surface), and survival probability (evaluated with a statistical evaporation model using Weisskopf theory and Bohr-Wheeler fission widths). Nuclear masses from HQMNM, FRDM12, WS4, KUTY, and HFB02 mass tables were used as inputs for Q-values, binding energies, and neutron separation energies that enter the fusion and survival calculations. The study systematically computed excitation functions for nine known hot-fusion reactions producing elements Z=112-118 and ten candidate reactions for elements 119 and 120.

## Key numbers

- For the reaction ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴³Am → ²⁹¹Mc*, the Q-values are -165.97 MeV (HQMNM), -164.98 MeV (FRDM12), and -168.24 MeV (WS4)
- The internal fusion barriers for ⁴⁸Ca + ²⁴³Am → ²⁹¹Mc* are 9.53 MeV (HQMNM and FRDM12) and 10.41 MeV (WS4)
- For ⁴⁸Ca(²⁴³Am,3n)²⁸³Mc, the HQMNM calculation gives 17 pb at 30 MeV excitation energy, compared to the experimental value of 17.5 pb at 34 MeV
- For ⁴⁸Ca(²³⁸U,3n)²⁸³Cn, HQMNM gives 2.85 pb at 34 MeV, versus experimental 0.74 pb at 34 MeV
- For element 119 synthesis, the ⁵⁸Fe + ²³⁷Np system with HQMNM gives the largest cross section at 23.7 fb (36 MeV excitation energy)
- For element 119, ⁵¹V + ²⁴⁸Cm gives 3.63 fb (36 MeV) and ⁵⁴Cr + ²⁴³Am gives 4.64 fb (34 MeV) with HQMNM
- For element 120 synthesis, the ⁵⁰Ti + ²⁴⁹Cf system with HQMNM gives 4.35 fb (34 MeV)
- For element 120, ⁵⁵Mn + ²⁴³Am gives 2.19 fb (34 MeV) with HQMNM

## Context

This work extends the DNS model framework previously applied by Feng (2006) and Chen (2023) to superheavy element synthesis, specifically addressing the sensitivity of predictions to nuclear mass input. The HQMNM mass table, published by Guan (2024) in Phys. Lett. B, was fitted to experimental alpha decay energies using Skyrme DFT. The study compares against the FRDM12 model of Moller (2016), the WS4 model of Wang (2014), the KUTY model, and the HFB02 model of El Bassem (2017). The results provide systematic cross-section predictions and optimal projectile-target combinations for experiments aiming to synthesize elements 119 and 120 at facilities such as CAFE2/SHANS2 in Lanzhou, DGFRS in Dubna, GAERIS at RIKEN, and superSHIP at GSI.
