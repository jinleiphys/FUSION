# Large-Momentum Effective Theory

> Since the parton model was introduced by Feynman more than fifty years ago, we have learned much about the partonic structure of the proton through a large body of high-energy experimental data and dedicated global fits. However, calculating the partonic observables such as parton distribution function (PDFs) from the fundamental theory of strong interactions, QCD, has made limited progress. Recently, the authors have advocated a formalism, large-momentum effective theory (LaMET), through which one can extract parton physics from the properties of the proton travelling at a moderate boost-factor, e.g., $γ\sim (2-5)$. The key observation behind this approach is that Lorentz symmetry allows the standard formalism of partons in terms of light-front operators to be replaced by an equivalent one with large-momentum states and time-independent operators of a universality class. With LaMET, the PDFs, generalized PDFs or GPDs, transverse-momentum-dependent PDFs, and light-front wave functions can all be extracted in principle from lattice simulations of QCD (or other non-perturbative methods) through standard effective field theory matching and running. Future lattice QCD calculations with exa-scale computational facilities can help to understand the experimental data related to the hadronic structure, including those from the upcoming Electron-Ion Colliders dedicated to exploring the partonic landscape of the proton. Here we review the progress made in the past few years in development of the LaMET formalism and its applications, particularly on the demonstration of its effectiveness from initial lattice QCD simulations.

## Key claim

Large-Momentum Effective Theory (LaMET) provides a systematic framework to extract parton physics, such as parton distribution functions (PDFs), generalized parton distributions (GPDs), transverse-momentum-dependent PDFs, and light-front wave functions, from lattice QCD simulations of the proton at a moderate boost factor (gamma ~ 2–5), by using effective field theory matching and power corrections to connect finite-momentum quantities to the infinite-momentum-frame parton observables.

## Method

The authors formulate LaMET by starting from the observation that the proton's structure at large but finite momentum P admits an expansion in powers of Lambda_QCD/P, with the leading term corresponding to the infinite-momentum-frame parton distributions. They define "quasi-PDFs" as matrix elements of time-independent, spatially separated quark bilinear operators in a proton state with large momentum P_z, then derive matching kernels in perturbative QCD that relate these quasi-PDFs to the standard light-front PDFs, systematically accounting for ultraviolet divergences and power-suppressed corrections. The approach is extended to GPDs, transverse-momentum-dependent PDFs, and light-front wave functions through analogous matching procedures, and the renormalization of the nonlocal operators, including the handling of power divergences on the lattice, is discussed in detail.

## Key numbers

- The proton boost factor required for LaMET is gamma ~ 2–5, corresponding to momenta P of a few GeV.
- The Bjorken x range accessible with LaMET is approximately x_min ~ 0.1 to x_max ~ 0.9.
- The Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) will achieve center-of-mass collision energies E_cm from 40 to 170 GeV, with electron energies of 10–30 GeV and ion energies up to 100 GeV per nucleon.
- The EIC luminosity is up to 10^33–10^34 collisions/(cm^2·s), and the kinematic range covers Bjorken x_B down to sub-10^-4 and Q^2 up to 10^4 GeV^2.

## Context

This review systematizes the LaMET formalism, which was first proposed by Ji (2013) for the gluon helicity and then extended to collinear quark PDFs, and which has since generated extensive theoretical and lattice QCD activity. The approach directly addresses the long-standing difficulty of computing x-dependent parton distributions from first principles in QCD, complementing earlier methods such as the calculation of PDF moments via local twist-two operators on the lattice (Kronfeld, 1984; Martinelli, 1987) and light-front quantization approaches (Brodsky, 1997). LaMET enables future lattice QCD calculations with exascale computing to interpret data from the upcoming Electron-Ion Collider, and it has been tested in various models including the 't Hooft model and in initial lattice simulations.
