# Heavy-Quark Diffusion in the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> The diffusion of heavy quarks through the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) as produced in high-energy heavy-ion collisions has long been recognized as an excellent probe of its transport properties. In addition, the experimentally observed heavy-flavor hadrons carry valuable information about the hadronization process of the transported quarks. Here we review recent progress in the theoretical developments of heavy-quark interactions in the QGP and how they relate to the nonperturbative hadronization process, and discuss the recent status of the pertinent phenomenology in heavy-ion collisions at the RHIC and the LHC. The interactions of heavy quarks in the QGP also constitute a central building block in the description of the heavy quarkonia which controls their transport parameters as well. We will thus focus on theoretical approaches that aim for a unified description of open and hidden heavy-flavor particles in medium, and discuss how they can be constrained by lattice QCD "data" and utilized to deduce fundamental properties of the microscopic interactions and emerging spectral properties of the strongly coupled QGP.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a unified theoretical description of both open heavy-flavor hadrons and quarkonia in the quark-gluon plasma can be achieved through nonperturbative approaches such as the thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix, and that these approaches can be constrained by lattice QCD data to extract fundamental transport properties, but the authors acknowledge that multiple selfconsistent solutions (a strongly-coupled scenario and a weakly-coupled scenario) exist that both fit the lattice QCD free energy data, meaning the microscopic interaction strength is not uniquely determined.

## Method

The authors review the thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix approach, which solves a three-dimensional scattering equation for heavy quarks interacting with thermal partons using an in-medium screened Cornell potential as the driving kernel. The single-particle spectral functions of heavy quarks are computed selfconsistently from the \(T\)-matrix via the selfenergy, and the resulting spectral functions are used to compute the static quark-antiquark free energy, which is then fitted to lattice QCD data to constrain the in-medium potential. The approach is extended to compute transport coefficients such as the spatial diffusion coefficient and to relate open heavy-flavor diffusion to quarkonium dissociation through the same microscopic scattering amplitudes.

## Key numbers

* The pseudo-critical temperature of the QCD transition is \(T_{\rm pc} \simeq 155\) MeV.
* The thermal relaxation time of charm quarks from leading-order pQCD calculations is \(\tau_c \ge 20\) fm/\(c\) for temperatures \(T \le 300\) MeV and \(\alpha_s = 0.3-0.4\).
* Leading-order pQCD yields a normalized spatial diffusion coefficient of \({\cal D}_s (2\pi T) \simeq 35\).
* Next-to-leading order pQCD corrections are more than a factor of 5 for \(\alpha_s = 0.2\).
* Including running coupling and selfconsistent Debye mass reduces the spatial diffusion coefficient to \({\cal D}_s (2\pi T) \simeq 3-4\).
* The \(D\)-meson resonance model reduces the diffusion coefficient to \({\cal D}_s (2\pi T) \simeq 5-6\) for charm quarks.
* AdS/CFT correspondence yields \({\cal D}_s (2\pi T) \simeq 0.9-3\).
* Phenomenological constraints from \(D\)-meson observables in Pb-Pb (5 TeV) and Au-Au (0.2 TeV) collisions give \({\cal D}_s (2\pi T) \simeq 2-4\) near the transition temperature.
* The charm-quark collisional widths from the selfconsistent \(T\)-matrix are about 500-700 MeV.
* The QGP fireball lifetime is \(\tau_{\rm QGP} \simeq 5-10\) fm/\(c\).

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes earlier work on heavy-quark diffusion, including the pioneering pQCD calculations of Svetitsky (1987), the resonance model of van Hees and Rapp (2004), and the thermodynamic \(T\)-matrix approach developed by Mannarelli, Riek, and Rapp (2005) and later by Liu and Rapp (2017). It connects open heavy-flavor phenomenology, reviewed by Prino and Rapp (2016) and Dong et al. (2019), with quarkonium physics, reviewed by Mocsy, Sorensen, and Bass (2013) and Rothkopf (2019), by emphasizing that the same microscopic interactions govern both sectors. The work enables future extraction of the heavy-quark diffusion coefficient from combined fits to open and hidden heavy-flavor observables at RHIC and the LHC, and provides a framework for testing against lattice QCD data on euclidean correlators and susceptibilities.
