# Time-dependent coupled-cluster method for atomic nuclei

> We study time-dependent coupled-cluster theory in the framework of nuclear physics. Based on Kvaal's bi-variational formulation of this method [S. Kvaal, arXiv:1201.5548], we explicitly demonstrate that observables that commute with the Hamiltonian are conserved under time evolution. We explore the role of the energy and of the similarity-transformed Hamiltonian under real and imaginary time evolution and relate the latter to similarity renormalization group transformations. Proof-of-principle computations of He-4 and O-16 in small model spaces, and computations of the Lipkin model illustrate the capabilities of the method.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that within Kvaal's bi-variational formulation of time-dependent coupled-cluster theory, observables that commute with the Hamiltonian are conserved under time evolution, resolving a long-standing conceptual problem regarding energy conservation in the method. The conservation holds exactly in the bi-variational framework, whereas earlier formulations had issues with energy being complex or not conserved.

## Method

The authors implement time-dependent coupled-cluster theory with singles and doubles (TD-CCSD) using Kvaal's bi-variational formulation, where the right state is given by an exponential excitation operator acting on a reference state and the left state involves a linear de-excitation operator. They solve the coupled equations of motion for both the excitation amplitudes S and the de-excitation amplitudes L using a fourth-order Runge-Kutta method, employing a low-momentum two-body interaction from a similarity renormalization group transformation of the N3LO chiral interaction at cutoff Lambda = 1.9 fm^-1 in a small model space of four oscillator shells. For the Lipkin model calculations, they use both the bi-variational density matrices and compare with the earlier Hermitian approach of Hoodbhoy and Negele (1978).

## Key numbers

- Energy conservation precision for He-4: changes on the order of 10^-10 MeV using a time step width of 0.05 fm/c, and changes of average order 10^-2 MeV for a step width of 1 fm/c
- Excited-state energies for He-4 from TD-CCSD: E1 = 3.04 MeV, E2 = 29.45 MeV, E3 = 35.52 MeV, E4 = 41.10 MeV, compared with EOM-CCSD values of 3.02, 29.47, 35.53, and 41.11 MeV respectively
- Excited-state energies for O-16 from TD-CCSD: E1 = 32.20 MeV, E2 = 33.85 MeV, E3 = 37.40 MeV, E4 = 48.15 MeV, compared with EOM-CCSD values of 32.29, 33.91, 37.60, and 48.12 MeV respectively
- Energy uncertainty in Fourier spectra: about 0.06 MeV for He-4 (time evolution of 20000 fm/c) and about 0.2 MeV for O-16 (time evolution of 6000 fm/c)
- Imaginary time evolution for He-4: 400 steps of width 0.5 fm/c, with exponential convergence to the CCSD ground-state energy
- Lipkin model calculation: 90 time steps of size 0.4 fm/c for a total of 12 x 10^-23 s, with parameters epsilon = 1 and V = 0.357 for strong coupling

## Context

This work extends the time-dependent coupled-cluster method originally proposed by Hoodbhoy and Negele (1978) for nuclear collisions and by Schonhammer and Gunnarson (1978) for spectral functions, resolving conceptual problems about energy conservation that had limited its application. The authors build on Kvaal's bi-variational formulation (2012) and relate imaginary-time evolution to similarity renormalization group transformations used by Bogner, Furnstahl, and collaborators (2007, 2010). The method enables the description of time-dependent phenomena such as nuclear collisions beyond time-dependent mean-field methods like those of Bonche (1976) and Umar and Oberacker (2007), and provides an alternative to equation-of-motion coupled-cluster methods for computing excited states, as developed by Koch and Jorgensen (1990) and Stanton and Bartlett (1993).
