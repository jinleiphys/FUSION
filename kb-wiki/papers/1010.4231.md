# Quark spectral density and a strongly-coupled QGP

> The maximum entropy method is used to compute the dressed-quark spectral density from the self-consistent numerical solution of a rainbow truncation of QCD's gap equation at temperatures above that for which chiral symmetry is restored. In addition to the normal and plasmino modes, the spectral function also exhibits an essentially nonperturbative zero mode for temperatures extending to 1.4-1.8-times the critical temperature, T_c. In the neighbourhood of T_c, this long-wavelength mode contains the bulk of the spectral strength and so long as this mode persists, the system may fairly be described as a strongly-coupled state of matter.

## Key claim

The dressed-quark spectral density at temperatures above the chiral symmetry restoration temperature exhibits an essentially nonperturbative zero mode (a long-wavelength collective excitation at zero energy and momentum) that persists for temperatures up to approximately 1.4–1.8 times the critical temperature \(T_c\), and this mode contains the bulk of the spectral strength near \(T_c\), justifying the description of the system as a strongly-coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP) on that temperature domain.

## Method

The authors solved the quark Dyson-Schwinger equation (gap equation) in the rainbow truncation for massless two-flavor QCD at finite temperature, using a phenomenologically-efficacious one-loop renormalization-group-improved interaction kernel whose temperature dependence was constrained by zero-temperature hadron phenomenology and lattice-QCD results. From the self-consistent numerical solution for the dressed-quark propagator in the Wigner phase (where chiral symmetry is restored), they computed the quark spectral density via the maximum entropy method (MEM), extracting the positions and residues of quasiparticle poles as functions of temperature and momentum.

## Key numbers

- Critical temperature for coincident chiral symmetry restoration and deconfinement: \(T_c = 0.14\ \text{GeV}\) (140 MeV).
- The zero mode persists up to \(T_s \approx 1.35\,T_c\).
- The sQGP domain is characterized as \(T/T_c \in [1, 1+\Delta]\) with \(\Delta \simeq 0.4\)–\(0.8\).
- At \(T = 1.1\,T_c\), the dispersion relations near zero momentum are: \(\omega_{\pm}(p) \approx m_T \mp 0.2\,p\) and \(\omega_0(p) \approx 0.80\,p\).
- At \(T = 3\,T_c\) (where the zero mode has vanished), the dispersion relations near zero momentum are: \(\omega_{\pm}(p) \approx m_T \pm 0.33\,p\).
- The thermal quark mass for \(T \gtrsim 2\,T_c\) is \(m_T = 0.8\,T\), consistent with lattice-QCD results from Karsch (2009).
- The infrared mass-scale associated with the dressing boson is \(m_G = 0.12\ \text{GeV}\).

## Context

This work extends nonperturbative Dyson-Schwinger equation studies of the quark propagator at finite temperature (Bender 1996, Holl 1998, Fischer 2009) by computing the full spectral density rather than just the propagator itself. It contradicts the expectation from hard thermal loop (HTL) perturbation theory (Pisarski 1988, Braaten 1990) that the plasmino branch should disappear before \(T_c\) due to increasing damping; instead, the authors find a zero mode that dominates near \(T_c\). The results are consistent with lattice-QCD simulations of quenched QCD (Karsch 2009) regarding the persistence of the plasmino near \(T_c\), and the zero mode's existence has also been observed in one-loop model calculations (Kitazawa 2005). The findings enable future calculations of color-singlet vacuum polarizations on the temperature domain \(T \in [T_c, T_s]\) using the methods of Chang (2008), which could reveal how this long-range mode affects observable quantities in heavy-ion collisions.
