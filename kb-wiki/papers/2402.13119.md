# Causal and Stable Relativistic Hydrodynamic Fluctuations

> When two nuclei collide close to the speed of light, a fluid state known as the quark-gluon plasma is formed. Attempts to understand the dynamics of this fluid have generated significant research into dissipative relativistic fluid dynamics. The fluctuation-dissipation theorem implies that any dissipative dynamical system will also experience thermal fluctuations; however, such fluctuations are not typically included in the modeling of the quark-gluon plasma. This work discusses a new method of determining whether a hydrodynamic framework is consistent with thermal fluctuations. We develop a new method for calculating the noise correlator of relativistic hydrodynamic systems and apply it to Israel-Stewart theory in a general hydrodynamic frame.

## Key claim
The paper develops a new method for calculating the noise correlator of relativistic hydrodynamic systems, enabling a consistency check of whether a given hydrodynamic framework (specifically Israel-Stewart theory in a general hydrodynamic frame) satisfies the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and thus correctly incorporates thermal fluctuations.

## Method
The authors construct a general formalism for computing the noise correlator in relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics by analyzing the linear response of the system around equilibrium. They apply this method to Israel-Stewart theory, working in a general hydrodynamic frame to derive the stochastic noise terms that must be present for consistency with thermal fluctuations.

## Key numbers
- No specific numerical results are presented; the paper develops a formal theoretical method for calculating noise correlators in relativistic hydrodynamics.

## Context
This work extends the study of thermal fluctuations in relativistic fluid dynamics, which has been largely absent from quark-gluon plasma modeling despite the fluctuation-dissipation theorem requiring their presence. The method provides a tool to test whether frameworks like Israel-Stewart theory (developed by Israel and Stewart in the 1970s) are consistent with thermal fluctuations, enabling future inclusion of stochastic noise in simulations of heavy-ion collisions.
