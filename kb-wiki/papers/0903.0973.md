# A further look at prolate dominance in nuclear deformation

> The observed almost complete dominance of prolate over oblate deformations in the ground states of deformed even-even nuclei is related to the splitting of high $\ell$ "surface" orbits in the Nilsson diagram: on the oblate side the occurrence of numerous strongly avoided crossings which reduce the fanning out of the low $Λ$ orbits, while on the prolate side the same interactions increase the fanning out. It is further demonstrated that the prolate dominance is rather special for the restricted particle number of available nuclei and is not generic for finite systems with mean-field potentials resembling those in atomic nuclei.

## Key claim

The observed overwhelming dominance of prolate over oblate deformations in the ground states of deformed even-even nuclei is not a generic property of finite Fermi systems with mean-field potentials, but rather a feature specific to systems with a relatively small number of particles, arising from the splitting of high-ℓ "surface" orbits in the Nilsson diagram.

## Method

The authors performed numerical calculations of independent-particle ground states in two axially-symmetric quadrupole-deformed potentials: the pure harmonic oscillator and the infinite-well potential (spheroidal cavity), with exact volume conservation. For each particle number, they summed the lowest-lying one-particle energies at a given deformation to obtain the total energy, then searched for the energy minimum on both the prolate and oblate sides. They extended these calculations to particle numbers up to 850 to examine the prolate-oblate competition in larger systems.

## Key numbers

- Of the 98 known deformed even-even nuclei identified in Fig. 4.3 of Bohr and Mottelson (1975), only one (¹²C) is oblate.
- For the major shell with particle number 138-186 in the spheroidal cavity, only six systems prefer oblate while the prolate minimum is lower than the oblate minimum in seventeen systems.
- The decrease of deformation with particle number follows a power law A⁻¹/².
- Calculations were extended to particle numbers up to 850.

## Context

This work extends the calculations of Hamamoto, Mottelson, and Xie (1991) on the spheroidal cavity to larger systems, aiming to understand the origin of prolate dominance beyond the particle-hole symmetry present in the harmonic oscillator model. The authors contrast their mechanism, based on avoided crossings on the oblate side of the Nilsson diagram for high-ℓ surface states, with the interpretation of Tajima et al. (1996), who attributed prolate dominance to the spin-orbit splitting changing the nature of major shells from harmonic-oscillator to Mayer-Jensen shells. The results enable future investigation of prolate-oblate competition in drip-line nuclei, where the shell structure and surface role may differ.
