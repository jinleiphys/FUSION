# Decoding Beta-Decay Systematics: A Global Statistical Model for Beta^- Halflives

> Statistical modeling of nuclear data provides a novel approach to nuclear systematics complementary to established theoretical and phenomenological approaches based on quantum theory. Continuing previous studies in which global statistical modeling is pursued within the general framework of machine learning theory, we implement advances in training algorithms designed to improved generalization, in application to the problem of reproducing and predicting the halflives of nuclear ground states that decay 100% by the beta^- mode. More specifically, fully-connected, multilayer feedforward artificial neural network models are developed using the Levenberg-Marquardt optimization algorithm together with Bayesian regularization and cross-validation. The predictive performance of models emerging from extensive computer experiments is compared with that of traditional microscopic and phenomenological models as well as with the performance of other learning systems, including earlier neural network models as well as the support vector machines recently applied to the same problem. In discussing the results, emphasis is placed on predictions for nuclei that are far from the stability line, and especially those involved in the r-process nucleosynthesis. It is found that the new statistical models can match or even surpass the predictive performance of conventional models for beta-decay systematics and accordingly should provide a valuable additional tool for exploring the expanding nuclear landscape.

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that fully-connected, multilayer feedforward artificial neural networks trained with Levenberg-Marquardt optimization combined with Bayesian regularization and cross-validation can match or surpass the predictive performance of traditional microscopic and phenomenological models for beta-minus halflives, but only for nuclei within the training distribution; the models are "theory-thin" and do not provide physical insights.

## Method
The authors constructed feedforward artificial neural networks with one or two hidden layers using hyperbolic tangent activation functions for hidden units and symmetric saturating linear functions for the output unit. They trained the networks on experimental beta-minus halflives from the Nubase2003 evaluation using the Levenberg-Marquardt backpropagation algorithm with Bayesian regularization and cross-validation, using only proton number Z and neutron number N as inputs scaled to the interval [-1,1], with the base-10 logarithm of the halflife as the target output.

## Key numbers
- Training data set NuSet-A contained 905 nuclides with halflives ranging from 0.15 × 10^(-2) s (for 35Na) to 2.43 × 10^(23) s (for 113Cd)
- The restricted data set NuSet-B contained 838 nuclides with halflives up to 0.20 × 10^(6) s (for 247Pu)
- Training set consisted of 543 nuclides (60%) from NuSet-A, with 181 (20%) each for validation and test sets
- For NuSet-B, the training set contained 503 nuclides (~60%), validation set 167 (~20%), and test set 168 (~20%)

## Context
This work extends previous statistical modeling studies using neural networks and support vector machines for nuclear properties such as atomic masses, neutron separation energies, and beta-decay halflives. It contrasts with traditional "theory-thick" approaches including the Gross Theory developed by Takahashi, Yamada and Kondoh, the FRDM + pnQRPA calculations by Moller and co-workers, and various pnQRPA studies by Klapdor and co-workers, Staudt et al., and Hirsch et al. The models enable predictions for neutron-rich nuclei involved in r-process nucleosynthesis where experimental data are unavailable, but the authors note that the statistical approach cannot compete with traditional models in generating new physical insights.
