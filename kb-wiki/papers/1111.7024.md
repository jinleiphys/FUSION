# Linking the exotic structure of ${}^{17}$C to its unbound mirror ${}^{17}$Na

> The structure of ${}^{17}$C is used to define a nuclear interaction that, when used in a multichannel algebraic scattering theory for the $n+{}^{16}$C system, gives a credible definition of the (compound) excitation spectra. When couplings to the low-lying collective excitations of the ${}^{16}$C-core are taken into account, both sub-threshold and resonant states about the $n+{}^{16}$C threshold are found. Adding Coulomb potentials to that nuclear interaction, the method is used for the mirror system of $p+{}^{16}$Ne to specify the low-excitation spectrum of the particle unstable $^{17}$Na. We compare the results with those of a microscopic cluster model. A spectrum of low excitation resonant states in ${}^{17}$Na is found with some differences to that given by the microscopic-cluster model. The calculated resonance half-widths (for proton emission) range from $\sim 2$ to $\sim 672$ keV.

## Key claim

The structure of the exotic neutron-rich nucleus ${}^{17}$C can be used to define a nuclear interaction that, when combined with Coulomb potentials, predicts the low-excitation spectrum of its unbound mirror ${}^{17}$Na, but the results show "some differences" from a microscopic cluster model (MCM) calculation by Timofeyuk and Descouvemont (2010), particularly in the ordering and energy gaps of the lowest states.

## Method

The authors used the multichannel algebraic scattering (MCAS) method to model ${}^{17}$C as an $n+{}^{16}$C system, coupling three states of the ${}^{16}$C core: the $0^+$ ground state, the $2^+$ state at 1.766 MeV, and the $4^+$ state at 4.142 MeV. The nuclear interaction parameters were adjusted to reproduce the known low-excitation spectrum of ${}^{17}$C. Then, adding Coulomb potentials derived from a Woods-Saxon charge distribution with the same geometry and deformation as the nuclear interaction, the same method was applied to the mirror system $p+{}^{16}$Ne to predict the spectrum of ${}^{17}$Na, both with zero-width target states and with resonant target states having finite widths.

## Key numbers

- The $n+{}^{16}$C threshold in ${}^{17}$C lies at 0.728 MeV.
- The three sub-threshold states of ${}^{17}$C are at 0.00 MeV ($\frac{3}{2}^+$), 0.201 MeV ($\frac{1}{2}^+$), and 0.390 MeV ($\frac{5}{2}^+$) from the MCAS calculation.
- The MCAS calculation for ${}^{17}$Na with zero-width target states gives the lowest three resonant states at 1.03 MeV ($\frac{1}{2}^+$, half-width 0.005 MeV), 2.26 MeV ($\frac{3}{2}^+$, half-width 0.004 MeV), and 1.13 MeV ($\frac{5}{2}^+$, half-width 0.002 MeV) above the $p+{}^{16}$Ne threshold.
- When resonant target states are used (${}^{16}$Ne ground state width 122 keV, $2^+$ width 200 keV, assumed $4^+$ width 1 MeV), the calculated half-widths for ${}^{17}$Na resonances range from approximately 2 keV to 672 keV.
- The MCM study by Timofeyuk and Descouvemont (2010) predicted the ${}^{17}$Na ground state at 2.40 MeV above threshold with a half-width of 1.360 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the MCAS approach previously applied to mirror systems such as ${}^{15}$F (Canton et al., 2006) and mass-7 isobars (Canton et al., 2006), and is a direct response to the MCM study of ${}^{17}$Na by Timofeyuk and Descouvemont (2010). The results confirm that channel coupling effects are crucial for predicting spectra of nuclei beyond the proton drip line, as was also found for ${}^{15}$F. The predictions await experimental verification, which the authors note is challenging because direct $n+{}^{16}$C experiments are unlikely and ${}^{16}$Ne is itself a proton emitter.
