# Scaling and universality in strange quark stars

> We derive scaling laws that connect certain macroscopic observables of strange quark stars with key microscopic properties of self-bound quark matter, such as the energy per baryon at zero pressure and the strength of repulsive interactions. We also identify universal relations linking global properties of strange quark stars - specifically, their moment of inertia, tidal deformability, and both gravitational and baryonic compactness. Remarkably, these relations hold for two substantially different microscopic models - the quark-mass density-dependent model with excluded-volume corrections and the vector MIT bag model - underscoring their robust, model-independent nature. We demonstrate that the universal relations for strange quark stars differ significantly from those previously established for neutron stars composed of hadronic matter, thus enabling discrimination between the two types of objects without requiring detailed knowledge of their equations of state. Moreover, observational constraints on the maximum mass of compact stars could place bounds on both the depth of quark-matter self-binding and the strength of quark repulsive interactions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that macroscopic observables of strange quark stars, such as maximum mass, radius, moment of inertia, and tidal deformability, obey scaling laws tied to the energy per baryon at zero pressure and the strength of repulsive interactions, and that these relations hold across two substantially different microscopic models (the quark-mass density-dependent model with excluded-volume corrections and the vector MIT bag model), provided the parameters lie within the stability window where strange quark matter is self-bound.

## Method

The authors solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for hydrostatic equilibrium using two distinct equations of state for strange quark matter: the quark-mass density-dependent (QMDD) model with excluded-volume corrections and the vector MIT bag model (vMIT). For each model, they identified the parameter space where three-flavor quark matter is energetically favored over atomic nuclei (the stability window) and computed stellar sequences to obtain mass-radius relations, tidal deformabilities via the Love number formalism, and moments of inertia under the slow-rotation approximation. They then fitted these numerical results to derive scaling relations linking macroscopic properties to the microscopic parameters \(e^0\) (energy per baryon at zero pressure) and the repulsive interaction strength (excluded volume fraction \(q\) in QMDD, coupling \(G_V\) in vMIT).

- The maximum mass scales linearly with \(1/\sqrt{q}\) for fixed \(e_{uds}^0\) and linearly with \(e_{uds}^0\) for fixed \(q\), consistent with the analytic scaling \(m = m_\mathrm{pl}/\sqrt{q}\) derived from the TOV equations.
- The moment of inertia scales as \(\mathcal{I} = \mathcal{I}_\mathrm{pl}/q^{3/2}\), while the tidal deformability \(\Lambda\) remains invariant under changes in \(q\) (\(\Lambda = \Lambda_\mathrm{pl}\)).
- The energy per baryon at zero pressure \(e_{uds}^0\) ranges from 880 MeV to 930 MeV within the stability window, with the boundary at 930 MeV corresponding to the binding energy per nucleon of \(^{62}\mathrm{Ni}\).

## Context

This work extends prior scaling studies for the MIT bag model (Haensel, 2007) by generalizing relations to the QMDD framework and linking them to the energy per baryon at zero pressure as a universal descriptor. It also builds on universal relation research for compact stars (Yagi, 2013; Andersson, 1997) but focuses exclusively on strange quark stars, finding that their universal relations for moment of inertia, tidal deformability, and compactness differ significantly from those established for neutron stars composed of hadronic matter. The results enable discrimination between strange quark stars and neutron stars without detailed knowledge of their equations of state, and observational constraints on the maximum mass of compact stars could place bounds on both the depth of quark-matter self-binding and the strength of quark repulsive interactions.
