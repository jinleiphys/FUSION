# Neutron Stars with Baryon Number Violation, Probing Dark Sectors

> The neutron lifetime anomaly has been used to motivate the introduction of new physics with hidden-sector particles coupled to baryon number, and on which neutron stars provide powerful constraints. Although the neutron lifetime anomaly may eventually prove to be of mundane origin, we use it as motivation for a broader review of the ways that baryon number violation, be it real or apparent, and dark sectors can intertwine and how neutron star observables, both present and future, can constrain them.

## Key claim

The paper uses the neutron lifetime anomaly as motivation for a broad review of how baryon number violation (BNV) and dark sectors can intertwine, and how neutron star observables can constrain them, while explicitly acknowledging that the anomaly "may eventually prove to be of mundane origin" and that "different lines of evidence suggest that the entirety of the anomaly would not reasonably arise from new-physics effects."

## Method

The authors review the neutron lifetime anomaly and its connection to precision measurements of neutron beta decay within the Standard Model, including updated assessments of electroweak radiative corrections and the CKM unitarity test. They then analyze the thermodynamics of neutron stars in the presence of BNV, considering processes that may be either an appreciable fraction of or much slower than the timescale of ordinary neutron beta decay, and examine the consequences for neutron star structure across three categories of BNV models: apparent, explicit, and spontaneous.

## Key numbers

- The neutron lifetime anomaly: beam experiments yield 887.7 ± 1.2 (stat) ± 1.9 (sys) s, while the most precise magnetic trap experiment yields 877.75 ± 0.28 (stat) +0.22/-0.16 (sys) s
- The Standard Model relation eta = 4908.6 (1.9) s (Marciano 2005), 4903.6 (1.0) s (Seng 2018), or 4905.7 (1.5) s (Czarnecki 2019), depending on the radiative correction calculation used
- The most precise determination of lambda = |g_A/g_V| from neutron decay correlation measurements is 1.27641 (56) (Markisch 2018)
- The FLAG average for g_A from lattice QCD with N_f = 2+1+1 flavors is 1.246 (28); the most precise lattice result is 1.271 (13) (Chang 2018)
- The estimated upper limit on exotic neutron decay branching ratio is Br(exotic neutron decays) < 0.16% (95% one-sided C.L.) (Czarnecki 2019)
- A typical neutron star can contain in excess of 10^57 baryons, with central density in excess of a few times nuclear matter saturation density n_sat ≈ 0.16 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends the discussion of neutron star constraints on dark neutron decay models originally proposed by Fornal and Grinstein (2018) and analyzed in neutron star contexts by McKeen et al. (2018), Motta et al. (2018), and Baym et al. (2018). It also builds on the long-standing recognition that neutron stars serve as "graveyards" for dark matter models (Gould 1989, McDermott 2011) and that proto-neutron star cooling provides sensitive probes of light new physics such as axions (Iwamoto 1984, Turner 1987) and dark photons (Dent 2012, Dreiner 2014). The review enables future work connecting neutron star observables, including gravitational wave observations of mergers, to constraints on BNV and dark sector models.
