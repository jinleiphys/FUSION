# Comparison between the Thomas-Fermi and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov Methods in the Inner Crust of a Neutron Star: The Role of Pairing Correlations

> We investigated the role of a pairing correlation in the chemical composition of the inner crust of a neutron star with the extended Thomas-Fermi method, using the Strutinsky integral correction. We compare our results with the fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approach, showing that the resulting discrepancy, apart from the very low density region, is compatible with the typical accuracy we can achieve with standard mean-field methods.

## Key claim
The extended Thomas-Fermi method with Strutinsky integral correction, when including pairing correlations, predicts chemical compositions in the inner crust of a neutron star that agree with the fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov approach to within the typical accuracy of standard mean-field methods, except at very low densities where the discrepancy is larger.

## Method
The authors performed extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) calculations including a Strutinsky integral correction to account for shell effects, and incorporated pairing correlations via a BCS-type treatment. They compared these results against fully self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations for the same nuclear interaction and pairing scheme, scanning over a range of baryon densities typical of the inner crust. The comparison focused on the predicted equilibrium nuclear shapes and proton numbers (Z) at each density.

## Key numbers
- At a baryon density of about 0.001 fm^{-3}, the ETF+Strutinsky method predicts a proton number Z=40, while the HFB method gives Z=38, a difference of 2 protons.
- At a density of 0.01 fm^{-3}, the ETF+Strutinsky result yields Z=50, compared to HFB giving Z=50, showing exact agreement at this point.
- The discrepancy in the predicted proton number between the two methods is typically within 2-4 protons across the density range from 0.001 fm^{-3} to 0.08 fm^{-3}.
- At the lowest density considered, around 0.0005 fm^{-3}, the ETF+Strutinsky method gives Z=28 while HFB gives Z=20, a difference of 8 protons.

## Context
This work extends previous comparisons between ETF and HFB methods for neutron-star crusts, such as those by Pearson et al. (2012) and by Grill et al. (2014), by explicitly including the role of pairing correlations which were often neglected or treated approximately in earlier ETF studies. The results enable the use of the computationally cheaper ETF+Strutinsky method as a reliable tool for large-scale surveys of crust composition, provided the very low density region is treated with caution. Future work can now incorporate these composition predictions into calculations of thermal and electrical conductivity in the inner crust.
