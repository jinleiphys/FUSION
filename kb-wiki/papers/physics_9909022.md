# Dissociation, fragmentation and fission of simple metal clusters

> This pedagogical review presents the Shell Correction Method (SCM) and variants thereof, appropriate for describing shape deformations and electronic shell effects, energetics and decay pathways of metal-cluster fragmentation processes (e.g., monomer/dimer dissociation and fission). The experimental trends are compared to the theoretical SCM interpretations, and in addition theoretical results for fission from first-principles molecular-dynamics simulations are discussed. Some latest insights concerning the importance of electronic-entropy and finite-temperature effects are given special attention.

## Key claim

This pedagogical review argues that the Shell Correction Method (SCM), originally developed for nuclear fission, can be successfully adapted to describe shape deformations, electronic shell effects, energetics, and decay pathways of metal-cluster fragmentation processes, but emphasizes that shell effects in metal clusters can be the largest contribution to the fission barrier even when the liquid-drop-model component exhibits no barrier, a situation analogous to superheavy nuclei stabilized by shell structure.

## Method

The authors present the Shell Correction Method (SCM) in two variants: a microscopic LDA-SCM approach based on the Harris functional approximation to Kohn-Sham LDA total energy, and a semiempirical SE-SCM that combines a liquid-drop model (LDM) smooth part with a Strutinsky shell correction calculated from a modified Nilsson Hamiltonian for ellipsoidal (triaxial) shapes. The smooth LDM energy includes volume, surface, and curvature terms for neutral clusters plus a Coulomb term for charged clusters, while the shell correction is obtained by averaging single-particle spectra from the modified Nilsson potential using an analytical semiclassical expansion. First-principles molecular-dynamics simulations based on electronic-structure calculations are also discussed for comparison.

## Key numbers

- Critical size for stability of doubly charged Na clusters: \(N_c^{2+}=27\) for Na and \(N_c^{2+}=20\) for K
- Minimum size for metastability above a fission barrier: \(N_b^{2+}=7\) for both Na\(_N^{2+}\) and K\(_N^{2+}\)
- LDM fissility parameter: \((Z^2/N)_{cr}=0.39\) for Na\(_N^{Z+}\) and 0.44 for K\(_N^{Z+}\) using bulk Wigner-Seitz radius and surface energy values
- For K\(_N^{2+}\) with \(N \leq 9\) and Na\(_N^{2+}\) with \(N \leq 10\), barrierless fission should occur according to LDM (\(\xi > 1\))
- Spherical ETF-LDA total energy parametrization: \(E_{\text{ETF}}^{\text{sph}} = \alpha_v N + \alpha_s N^{2/3} + \alpha_c N^{1/3}\)
- Spherical oscillator frequency: \(\hbar \omega_0 (N)= \frac{49 \; \text{eV bohr}^2}{r_s^2 N^{1/3}} \left[ 1 + \frac{t}{r_s N^{1/3}} \right]^{-2}\)
- Analytical shell correction for isotropic oscillator: \(\Delta E^{\text{Str}}_{\text{sh,0}}(x) = \frac{1}{24} \hbar \omega_0 (3N_e)^{2/3} (-1 + 12 x (1-x))\), with \(\Delta E^{\text{Str}}_{\text{sh,0}}(0) = - \frac{1}{24} \hbar \omega_0 (3N_e)^{2/3}\) for a filled shell

## Context

This review extends the nuclear-physics Shell Correction Method (Strutinsky, 1967) and Liquid Drop Model (Bohr and Wheeler, 1939) to metal clusters, building on earlier jellium-model work by Ekardt (1984) and Beck (1984) and on deformed-cluster studies by Clemenger (1985) and Saunders (1986). The authors' own prior work (Yannouleas, Landman, and Barnett, 1990s) had already applied SCM to metal clusters, fullerenes, helium clusters, and nanowires; this chapter systematizes those results and compares them with experimental trends from Brechignac (1990s) and molecular-dynamics simulations by Barnett and Landman (1991). The work enables systematic studies of fission barriers, shell effects, and finite-temperature effects in finite fermionic systems bridging nuclear and condensed-matter physics.
