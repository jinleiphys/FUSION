# Comparison of Pauli projection and supersymetric transformation methods for three-body nuclear structure and reactions

Two approaches for eliminating the Pauli-forbidden deeply-bound states are compared: projecting out those states by a nonlocal term in the potential, and by using a supersymmetric transformation of the potential. # Comparison of Pauli projection and supersymmetric transformation methods for three-body nuclear structure and reactions

## Key claim
The Pauli projection (PP) method is preferred by experimental data for deuteron-⁴He scattering, but for bound and resonant states neither the PP nor the supersymmetric (SS) transformation method shows clear superiority; instead, systematic differences between them are found, with SS predicting larger binding energies for ground and 2⁺ states while PP predicts larger binding for 0₂⁺ excited states.

## Method
Three-body Faddeev-type equations for bound, resonant, and scattering states in systems with a nuclear core and two nucleons were solved in momentum-space partial-wave representation using transition operators. Two approaches for eliminating Pauli-forbidden deeply-bound states were compared: projecting out those states by adding a nonlocal projection term to the two-body potential (PP), and applying a supersymmetric transformation of the potential that creates a repulsive r⁻² term (SS). The nucleon-nucleon interaction was the CD Bonn potential, and nucleon-nucleus potentials were of Woods-Saxon form with spin-orbit term.

## Context
This work extends comparisons of PP and SS methods previously performed by Hesse et al. (1999) for weakly bound two-neutron halo nuclei (⁶He, ¹¹Li, ¹⁴Be) and by Thompson et al. (2000) for ⁶He and ⁶Be systems, which found no significant differences for bound and resonant states but visible effects for nonresonant continuum. The present study goes beyond earlier work by including deuteron-⁴He scattering (where Deltuva (2006) had shown large differences between PP and RC models but had not implemented SS), by examining more tightly bound systems with excited states (¹⁶C, ¹⁸O), and by calculating ¹⁶Be resonances. The results enable future work to assess which method is more appropriate for specific applications, particularly for breakup reactions where momentum distributions differ significantly between PP and SS models.
