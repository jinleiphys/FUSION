# Fourier-over-Spheroid shape parametrization applied to nuclear fission dynamics

> We propose a new, rapidly convergent, the so-called Fourier over Spheroid (FoS), shape parametrization to model fission of heavy nuclei. Four collective coordinates are used to characterize the shape of the fissioning system, being its elongation, left-right asymmetry, neck size, and non-axiality. The potential energy landscape is computed within the macroscopic-microscopic approach, on the top of which the multi-dimensional Langevin equation is solved to describe the dynamics. Charge equilibration at scission and de-excitation of the primary fragments after scission are further considered. The model gives access to a wide variety of observables, including fission fragments mass, charge, and kinetic energy yields, fragment mean N/Z and post-scission neutron multiplicities, and importantly, their correlations. The latter are crucial to unravel the complexity of the fission process. The parameters of the model were tuned to reproduce experimental observation from thermal neutron-induced fission of 235U, and next used to discuss the transition from the asymmetric to symmetric fission along the Fm isotopic chain.

## Key claim

The Fourier-over-Spheroid (FoS) shape parametrization, using only four collective coordinates (elongation, left-right asymmetry, neck size, and non-axiality), provides a rapidly convergent description of fission shapes that, when combined with a macroscopic-microscopic potential and Langevin dynamics, can reproduce a wide variety of experimental observables and their correlations for thermal neutron-induced fission of 235U, though discrepancies remain in describing specific scission configurations (Standard I and II modes) and the resulting post-scission neutron multiplicities.

## Method

The nuclear surface is described by a Fourier expansion of deviations from a sphere, scaled to elongation parameter \(c\), with deformation parameters \(a_3\) (reflectional asymmetry), \(a_4\) (neck size), and \(\eta\) (non-axiality). The potential energy landscape is computed in this 4D deformation space using the macroscopic-microscopic approach with the Lublin-Strasbourg-Drop (LSD) formula for the macroscopic part and Yukawa-folded single-particle potential with Strutinsky shell correction and BCS pairing for the microscopic part. Multi-dimensional Langevin equations with irrotational-flow inertia and wall-friction tensors are solved to simulate the dynamical evolution from the second saddle to scission, after which charge equilibration at scission is treated by minimizing the total LSD energy with respect to fragment charge, and post-scission neutron evaporation is computed using the Weisskopf formula with level densities from Ref. NPB02.

## Key numbers

- The scission criterion is set at neck radius \(r_{\rm neck} = r_0\) (nucleon radius), corresponding to neck parameter \(a_4 = 0.72\) for the fissioning system.
- The nuclear interaction energy at scission is approximately \(V_{\rm nuc}(q_{\rm sc}) \approx -9\) MeV for neck radius equal to nucleon radius.
- The zero-point collective energy used in the Langevin equation is \(E_0 = 3 \times 0.5\) MeV.
- The most probable primary fragments for thermal neutron-induced fission of 235U are 140Xe and 96Sr.
- The largest calculated TKE values are \(\gtrsim 190\) MeV for neutron-rich fragments with mass A between approximately 130 and 140, while symmetric fission yields TKE values around 140 MeV.
- The average neutron separation energy used as the cutoff for neutron evaporation is 6 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Fourier parametrization (Pomorski et al. 2015, Schmitt et al. 2017) and its 3D Langevin implementation (Liu et al. 2021) by introducing the FoS shape parametrization and adding charge equilibration beyond the unchanged charge density (UCD) assumption, as well as post-scission neutron evaporation. It contrasts with models by Aritomo et al. (2014) and Usang et al. (2019) which assume UCD and do not compute post-scission de-excitation, and with the Brownian shape motion model by Randrup and Moller (2013) and its extension by Albertsson et al. (2021) which also assume UCD. The model goes beyond the "6D" approach of Moller and Ichikawa (2015) by including post-scission evaporation, though it currently lacks multi-chance fission and fragment angular momentum treatment. The results enable systematic studies of fission fragment mass, charge, and TKE yields along with their correlations across the nuclear chart, with the transition from asymmetric to symmetric fission along the Fm isotopic chain identified as a key application.
