# Mixed-Symmetry Shell-Model Calculations in Nuclear Physics

> We consider a novel approach to the nuclear shell model. The one-dimensional harmonic oscillator in a box is used to introduce the concept of an oblique-basis shell-model theory. By implementing the Lanczos method for diagonalization of large matrices, and the Cholesky algorithm for solving generalized eigenvalue problems, the method is applied to nuclei. The mixed-symmetry basis combines traditional spherical shell-model states with SU(3) collective configurations. We test the validity of this mixed-symmetry scheme on 24Mg and 44Ti. Results for 24Mg, obtained using the Wilthental USD intersection in a space that spans less than 10% of the full-space, reproduce the binding energy within 2% as well as an accurate reproduction of the low-energy spectrum and the structure of the states - 90% overlap with the exact eigenstates. In contrast, for an m-scheme calculation, one needs about 60% of the full space to obtain compatible results. Calculations for 44Ti support the mixed-mode scheme although the pure SU(3) calculations with few irreps are not as good as the standard m-scheme calculations. The strong breaking of the SU(3) symmetry results in relatively small enhancements within the combined basis. However, an oblique-basis calculation in 50% of the full pf-shell space is as good as a usual m-scheme calculation in 80% of the space. Results for the lower pf-shell nuclei 44-48Ti and 48Cr, using the Kuo-Brown-3 interaction, show that SU(3) symmetry breaking in this region is driven by the single-particle spin-orbit splitting. In our study we observe some interesting coherent structures, such as coherent mixing of basis states, quasi-perturbative behavior in the toy model, and enhanced B(E2) strengths close to the SU(3) limit even though SU(3) appears to be rather badly broken.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a mixed-symmetry (oblique) basis combining spherical shell-model states with SU(3) collective configurations can achieve comparable or better accuracy than standard m-scheme calculations while using a significantly smaller fraction of the full model space, though the effectiveness depends on the degree of SU(3) symmetry breaking in the nucleus under study.

## Method

The author implements the Lanczos method for diagonalization of large matrices and the Cholesky algorithm for solving generalized eigenvalue problems within an oblique-basis shell-model framework. The mixed-symmetry basis combines traditional spherical shell-model states with SU(3) collective configurations, and the method is tested on 24Mg and 44Ti using the Wildenthal USD interaction and on lower pf-shell nuclei (44-48Ti and 48Cr) using the Kuo-Brown-3 interaction. A one-dimensional harmonic oscillator in a box is used as a toy model to introduce the concept of oblique-basis theory and to study convergence properties with respect to basis state scaling and displacement parameters.

## Key numbers

- For 24Mg, using the Wildenthal USD interaction in a space spanning less than 10% of the full space, the binding energy is reproduced within 2% and the eigenstates have 90% overlap with exact eigenstates
- For 24Mg, an m-scheme calculation requires about 60% of the full space to obtain results compatible with the mixed-symmetry approach
- An oblique-basis calculation in 50% of the full pf-shell space is as good as a usual m-scheme calculation in 80% of the space
- Convergence criteria for the toy model used an accuracy limit of 10^{-4} for both eigenvalues and eigenvectors

## Context

This work extends the nuclear shell-model approach by introducing an oblique-basis method that addresses the difficulty of treating mid-shell nuclei where collective degrees of freedom are essential and the shell-model configuration space becomes very large. The method builds on the spherical shell model described by Heyde and the SU(3) symmetry framework of Elliott, and the toy model analysis of basis convergence relates to earlier work by Martin and Cruz (1991) on confined systems. The results suggest that a mixed-mode shell-model theory may be useful in situations where competing degrees of freedom dominate the dynamics and full-space calculations are not feasible, with the code package designed to interface with the Glasgow code for spherical basis states and the SU(3) RME code for collective configurations.
