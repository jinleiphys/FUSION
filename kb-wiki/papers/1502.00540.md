# Multichannel one-to-two transition amplitudes in a finite volume

> We derive a model-independent expression for finite-volume matrix elements. Specifically, we present a relativistic, non-perturbative analysis of the matrix element of an external current between a one-scalar in-state and a two-scalar out-state. Our result, which is valid for energies below higher-particle inelastic thresholds, generalizes the Lellouch-Luscher formula in two ways: we allow the external current to inject arbitrary momentum into the system and we allow for the final state to be composed an arbitrary number of strongly coupled two-particle states with arbitrary partial waves (including partial-wave mixing induced by the volume). We also illustrate how our general result can be applied to some key examples, such as heavy meson decays and meson photo production. Finally, we point out complications that arise involving unstable resonance states, such as $B\rightarrow K^*\ell^+\ell^-$ when staggered or mixed-action/partially-quenched calculations are performed.

## Key claim

The paper derives a model-independent, relativistic, non-perturbative master formula relating finite-volume matrix elements to infinite-volume transition amplitudes for a one-scalar to two-scalar process with an external current, valid only for energies below higher-particle inelastic thresholds. The result generalizes the Lellouch-Luscher formula by allowing arbitrary momentum injection from the external current and an arbitrary number of strongly coupled two-particle final states with arbitrary partial waves, including partial-wave mixing induced by the cubic volume.

## Method

The authors start from the known two-particle quantization condition in a finite cubic volume, expressed as a determinant over angular momentum and open-channel space involving the K-matrix and a finite-volume matrix F^V. They then derive the residue of the fully dressed two-particle propagator in finite volume, and use this to construct a master equation that relates the finite-volume matrix element of an external current to the infinite-volume transition amplitude vector A. The derivation is performed for scalar particles with no intrinsic spin, and the result is illustrated by truncating to specific partial waves (S-wave for K→ππ, P-wave for πγ*→ππ and B→ππ+ℓℓ) and by examining the narrow-resonance limit.

## Key numbers

* For the S-wave K→ππ case, the ratio of infinite to finite-volume matrix elements is given by: |A_{S,n_f}|^2 / |⟨ππ|J|K⟩|^2 = (16π E_K E*_{n_f})/(N_i N_f q*_{n_f} ξ) × ∂(δ_S + φ^d_00)/∂P_{0,M} evaluated at P_{0,M}=E_{n_f}, where ξ=1/2 for identical final particles.
* In the non-interacting limit for two identical final-state pions with E_{n_f}=E_K, the ratio reduces to (2E_K)/(N_i N_f) × (E_{n_f}^2/ξ) L^3, recovering the original Lellouch-Luscher result.
* For a P-wave ππ final state with a narrow ρ resonance, near the resonance mass the finite-volume matrix element squared equals |F^{X→ρ}|^2 × (N_i N_f)/(2E_i 2E_ρ), where F^{X→ρ} is the infinite-volume X→ρ transition amplitude evaluated at E*=m_ρ.
* The derivative of the phase shift near a narrow Breit-Wigner resonance (E*=m_ρ) is approximately 2E_ρ/(Γ_ρ m_ρ), leading to a divergence as Γ_ρ→0.

## Context

This work extends the original Lellouch-Luscher formula (Lellouch and Luscher, 2000) for K→ππ decays, which was restricted to S-wave final states at rest with no partial-wave mixing, and the subsequent generalization by Lin (2001) for elastic channels. It also generalizes the boosted two-particle quantization condition developed by Rummukainen and Gottlieb (1995) and Kim, Sachrajda, and Sharpe (2005). The result enables lattice QCD calculations of processes such as meson photo production and heavy meson decays (e.g., B→ππ+ℓℓ) by providing a systematic method to extract infinite-volume transition amplitudes from finite-volume matrix elements. The authors also point out that calculations using staggered fermions or mixed-action/partially-quenched schemes (e.g., Horgan et al., 2013 for B→K*ℓ+ℓ-) are invalidated by unphysical hair-pin interactions that violate unitarity, requiring a numerical formulation that respects unitarity.
