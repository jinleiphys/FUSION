# Solar neutrino physics

> As a free, intensive, weakly interacting, and well directional messenger, solar neutrinos have been driving both solar physics and neutrino physics developments for more than half a century. Since more extensive and advanced neutrino experiments are under construction, being planned or proposed, we are striving toward an era of precise and comprehensive measurement of solar neutrinos in the next decades. In this article, we review recent theoretical and experimental progress achieved in solar neutrino physics. We present not only an introduction to neutrinos from the standard solar model and the standard flavor evolution, but also a compilation of a variety of new physics that could affect and hence be probed by solar neutrinos. After reviewing the latest techniques and issues involved in the measurement of solar neutrino spectra and background reduction, we provide our anticipation on the physics gains from the new generation of neutrino experiments.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that solar neutrino physics is entering an era of precise and comprehensive measurement, but the standard solar model still faces the unresolved "solar metallicity problem" where high-metallicity models (GS98) and low-metallicity models (AGSS09) predict different neutrino fluxes, and neither class of models is fully consistent with helioseismological measurements.

## Method

The paper reviews the standard solar model (SSM) construction from hydrostatic equilibrium, energy transport, and nuclear reaction rates, then presents the pp-chain and CNO-cycle neutrino production mechanisms with their energy spectra and production rate distributions as functions of solar radius. It derives the MSW-LMA survival probability for electron neutrinos using the adiabatic approximation and the PMNS mixing matrix, and compares predictions from two SSM calculations (BSB05 and B16) using different chemical composition datasets (GS98, AGS05, AGSS09).

## Key numbers

- pp neutrino flux: (5.98-6.03) x 10^10 cm^-2 s^-1 across B16-GS98 and B16-AGSS09 models, with ~0.5-0.6% uncertainty
- hep neutrino endpoint: 18.77 MeV, the highest energy from solar nuclear fusion
- 8B neutrino flux: (4.50-5.46) x 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1 across B16 models, with 12% uncertainty
- pep neutrino energy: 1.442 MeV (monochromatic)
- 7Be neutrino lines: 0.861 MeV (90% branching) and 0.383 MeV (10% branching)
- CNO neutrino fluxes: 13N at (2.04-2.78) x 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1, 15O at (1.44-2.05) x 10^8 cm^-2 s^-1, 17F at (3.26-5.29) x 10^6 cm^-2 s^-1 across B16 models
- Electron capture neutrino fluxes suppressed by ~10^-4 relative to beta+ decay fluxes: Phi_e13N = 7.9 x 10^-4 Phi_13N, Phi_e15O = 3.9 x 10^-4 Phi_15O, Phi_e17F = 5.8 x 10^-4 Phi_17F
- Solar atmospheric neutrino flux at 300 GeV: ~3 x 10^-13 cm^-2 s^-1 GeV^-1, following E_nu^-3 power law from 10 GeV to 10^5 GeV
- NuFit 5.2 (2022) global fit: sin^2 theta_12 = 0.303 +/- 0.012, Delta m^2_21 = (7.41 +/- 0.21) x 10^-5 eV^2, sin^2 theta_13 = 2.225 x 10^-2, Delta m^2_31 = +2.507 x 10^-3 eV^2 (normal ordering)

## Context

This review extends the line of SSM calculations by Bahcall (1989) and the Barcelona group (Vinyoles 2016), and updates the status of the solar metallicity problem that has persisted since the GS98 (Grevesse and Sauval 1998) versus AGSS09 (Asplund 2009) composition measurements. The paper enables future precision measurements by upcoming experiments (JUNO, Hyper-Kamiokande, DUNE) to resolve the metallicity problem through precise CNO and 8B flux measurements, and to search for new physics effects in the solar neutrino survival probability up-turn region (2-6 MeV) that remains poorly measured.
