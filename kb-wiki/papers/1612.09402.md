# Nuclear Weak Rates and Detailed Balance in Stellar Conditions

> Detailed balance is often invoked in discussions of nuclear weak transitions in astrophysical environments. Satisfaction of detailed balance is rightly touted as a virtue of some methods of computing nuclear transition strengths, but we argue that it need not necessarily be strictly obeyed, especially when the system is far from weak equilibrium. We present the results of shell model calculations of nuclear weak strengths in both charged current and neutral current channels at astrophysical temperatures. Using these strengths to compute some reaction rates, we find that, despite some violation of detailed balance, our method is robust up to high temperature, and we comment on the relationship between detailed balance and weak equilibrium in astrophysical conditions.

## Key claim
Detailed balance of nuclear weak transition strengths need not be strictly obeyed in astrophysical environments, especially when the system is far from weak equilibrium; the paper argues that directly computing forward reaction rates to appropriate precision is sufficient, and that imposing detailed balance can actually cause missing strength at high temperatures.

## Method
The author used the shell model code OXBASH with the USDB Hamiltonian to compute nuclear energy levels and transition strengths for both charged current and neutral current channels in several sd-shell nuclei (27Al, 28Si, 32P). Individual initial states were considered up to 15 MeV excitation, with states above 15 MeV binned into 1 MeV intervals and averaged; transition strengths were computed to all final states in the daughter nucleus with total energy less than 35 MeV above the parent ground state. Thermal strengths were then computed from these distributions and compared between forward and reverse reactions using the imbalance metric I(A,B) = (A-B)/(A+B).

## Key numbers
- For 32P at T9 = 30 (2.585 MeV), the detailed balance method predicts positron capture rates lower than the direct calculation method by a factor of ~1.74 at all densities.
- At T9 = 10 (0.862 MeV) and below, both methods (direct calculation and detailed balance) agree to high precision for 32P reaction rates.
- The thermal strength distributions fall off rapidly above ~15 MeV transition energy, justifying the truncation in transition energy.
- For 27Al isospin-lowering charged current at T = 0.17 MeV and T = 1.0 MeV, detailed imbalance is near zero within ~10 MeV of the region where thermal strength imbalance is not extreme.
- At T = 3.0 MeV for 27Al, the gap between large detailed imbalance and small thermal strength imbalance becomes relatively narrow.

## Context
This work extends the modification of the Brink-Axel hypothesis proposed by Misch, Fuller, and Brown (2014) for computing electron capture and energy loss rates, further modifying it to include initial states above 15 MeV to see neutrino spectra at very high neutrino energy. It contrasts with the approach of Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1980-1985), who assumed log(ft) = 5 for all unknown transitions and adapted the Brink-Axel hypothesis to weak interactions, and with Oda et al (1994), who performed large-scale shell model calculations on sd-shell nuclei including all 100 lowest-lying states. The paper enables future work using the projected shell model for heavier deformed nuclei, as suggested by Sun (2016) and Hara and Sun (1995), by showing that strict adherence to detailed balance is not necessary for computing rates in environments far from weak equilibrium.
