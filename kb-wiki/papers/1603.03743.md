# Time-dependent density functional theory with twist-averaged boundary conditions

> Time-dependent density functional theory is widely used to describe excitations of many-fermion systems. In its many applications, 3D coordinate-space representation is used, and infinite-domain calculations are limited to a finite volume represented by a box. For finite quantum systems (atoms, molecules, nuclei), the commonly used periodic or reflecting boundary conditions introduce spurious quantization of the continuum states and artificial reflections from boundary; hence, an incorrect treatment of evaporated particles. These artifacts can be practically cured by introducing absorbing boundary conditions (ABC) through an absorbing potential in a certain boundary region sufficiently far from the described system. But also the calculations of infinite matter (crystal electrons, quantum fluids, neutron star crust) suffer artifacts from a finite computational box. In this regime, twist- averaged boundary conditions (TABC) have been used successfully to diminish the finite-volume effects. In this work, we extend TABC to time-dependent framework and apply it to resolve the box artifacts for finite quantum systems using as test case small- and large-amplitude nuclear vibrations. We demonstrate that by using such a method, one can reduce finite volume effects drastically without adding any additional parameters. While they are almost equivalent in the linear regime, TABC and ABC differ in the nonlinear regime in their treatment of evaporated particles.

# Time-dependent density functional theory with twist-averaged boundary conditions

## Key claim

Twist-averaged boundary conditions (TABC) can be extended to the time-dependent framework and applied to finite quantum systems to reduce finite-volume artifacts without adding any additional parameters; while TABC and absorbing boundary conditions (ABC) are almost equivalent in the linear regime, they differ in the nonlinear regime in their treatment of evaporated particles.

## Method

The authors implemented TABC into the 3D Skyrme-TDHF solver Sky3D, which uses an equidistant Cartesian grid with grid spacing of 1 fm and time steps of 0.1 fm/c, employing the SV-bas Skyrme energy density functional. They performed independent TDHF calculations for different sets of twist angles (phases applied via Bloch boundary conditions) and averaged the results, using Gauss-Lagrange quadrature with n points per direction between 0 and pi. Benchmarking calculations were performed for electric dipole (E1) and quadrupole (E2) oscillations of 16O triggered by an initial boost, comparing TABC results with those from ABC (implemented via a mask function) and periodic boundary conditions (PBC).

## Key numbers

- Grid spacing of 1 fm and time steps of 0.1 fm/c were used for all calculations
- Two box sizes were tested: L=32 fm with absorption zone thickness labs=6 fm, and L=40 fm with labs=10 fm, both giving an active zone radius of 20 fm
- For the isoscalar E2 mode at E*=3 MeV with L=40 fm, PBC induced unphysical beating after about 1000 fm/c with magnitude as large as half the maximum amplitude at t=0
- For the isovector E1 mode at E*=1 MeV, ABC showed a small loss of about 0.1 nucleons
- For the isoscalar E2 mode at E*=20 MeV with L=32 fm, integrating the gas density gave 0.38 neutrons and 0.47 protons, with gas densities of rho_n,gas=1.15e-5 fm^-3 and rho_p,gas=1.42e-5 fm^-3
- TABC with n=4 twists per direction was chosen as a good compromise between feasibility and accuracy

## Context

This work extends the use of TABC, previously applied successfully to time-independent simulations of infinite nucleonic matter (Carter, Chamel 2007, Gandolfi 2009, Gulminelli, Hagen 2014, Schuetrumpf 2015) and lattice QCD (Bedaque 2005, Tiburzi 2005, Bernard 2011, Doring 2011, Briceno 2014, 2015), into the time-dependent DFT framework for finite quantum systems. It directly compares TABC with the established ABC method used in nuclear TDHF (Nakatsukasa 2005, Reinhard 2006, Pardi 2013), demonstrating that TABC removes spurious finite-volume effects as efficiently as ABC without adding parameters. The method enables future applications to excitations of heavy superfluid nuclei and to infinite systems such as nuclear pasta oscillations in neutron star crusts, where the nucleonic gas represents physical reality.
