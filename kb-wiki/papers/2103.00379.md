# The description of giant dipole resonance key parameters with multitask neural networks

> Giant dipole resonance (GDR) is one of the fundamental collective excitation modes in nucleus. Continuous efforts have been made to the evaluation of GDR key parameters in different nuclear data libraries. We introduced multitask learning (MTL) approach to learn and reproduce the evaluated experimental data of GDR key parameters, including both GDR energies and widths. Compared to the theoretical GDR parameters in RIPL-3 library, the accuracies of MTL approach are almost doubled for 129 nuclei with experimental data. The significant improvement is largely due to the right classification of unimodal nuclei and bimodal nuclei by the classification neural network. Based on the good performance of the neural network approach, an extrapolation to 79 nuclei around the $β$-stability line without experimental data is made, which provides an important reference to future experiments and data evaluations. The successful application of MTL approach in this work further proofs the feasibility of studying multi-output physical problems with multitask neural network in nuclear physics domain.

## Key claim

The multitask learning (MTL) neural network approach reproduces evaluated experimental GDR energies and widths with roughly double the accuracy of the theoretical Goldhaber-Teller (GT) model parameters in the RIPL-3 library for 129 nuclei, but this improvement depends critically on the classification neural network correctly distinguishing unimodal (single-peak) from bimodal (double-peak) nuclei, which the GT model often misclassifies for nuclei near magic numbers.

## Method

The authors built three neural networks: a classification network (Net$_0$) that takes proton number Z, neutron number N, mass number A, and deformation parameter β₂ as inputs to predict whether a nucleus has one or two Lorentzian components (unimodal or bimodal), and two separate multitask learning networks (Net$_1$ for unimodal, Net$_2$ for bimodal) that share a hidden layer between tasks to predict the GDR resonance energies and widths simultaneously. The networks were trained on 116 nuclei from the IAEA Photonuclear Data Library 2019 (with experimental errors for widths below 1.5 MeV) and validated on the remaining 13 nuclei, using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.008.

## Key numbers

- The classification neural network (Net$_0$) achieved 98.5% accuracy in distinguishing unimodal from bimodal nuclei across all mass regions, compared to 77.2% for the GT model.
- For all 129 nuclei, the MTL approach reduced the rms deviation for peak energies by 51.2% relative to the GT model, and for resonance widths by 41.4%.
- For unimodal nuclei, Net$_1$ improved the rms deviation by 63.1% for energies and 42.1% for widths; for bimodal nuclei, Net$_2$ improved by 31.6% for energies and 38.7% for widths.
- The rms deviation of energies from the MTL approach was below 0.41 MeV for all nuclei except the 24 unimodal nuclei with A < 80, where it was 0.70 MeV.
- The GT model misclassified unimodal nuclei as bimodal for nuclei with proton or neutron numbers near magic numbers (e.g., 18, 22, 28, 29, 32, 40, 50, 58, 64, 79).

## Context

This work extends machine learning applications in nuclear physics, following successful uses of Bayesian neural networks for nuclear masses (Niu 2018, Utama 2016, Neufcourt 2018) and beta-decay half-lives (Niu 2019), and radial basis function approaches for masses (Niu 2016). It directly improves upon the phenomenological Goldhaber-Teller model predictions compiled in the RIPL-3 library (Capote 2009), which often misclassifies deformed versus spherical nuclei based solely on the β₂ deformation parameter. The MTL approach enables extrapolation to 79 nuclei around the beta-stability line without experimental data, providing predictions for future experiments and data evaluations.
