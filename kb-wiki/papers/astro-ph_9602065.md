# How Does the Sun Shine?

> Assuming that MSW neutrino oscillations occur and ignoring all solar physics except for the constraint that nuclear fusion produces the solar luminosity, we show that new solar neutrino experiments are required to rule out empirically the hypothesis that the sun shines via the CNO cycle.

# How Does the Sun Shine?

## Key claim
Assuming MSW neutrino oscillations occur and ignoring all solar physics except the luminosity constraint, the four operating solar neutrino experiments (chlorine, Kamiokande, GALLEX, and SAGE) are consistent with a hypothetical solar neutrino spectrum in which CNO reactions produce essentially all of the solar luminosity, though the authors do not believe this is actually the case.

## Method
The authors performed a computer search over a numerical grid of neutrino fluxes and mixing parameters consistent with the luminosity constraint (that fusion energy release equals observed solar luminosity) and a nuclear physics inequality relating ⁷Be, ⁸B, pp, and pep neutrino fluxes. For each set of fluxes, they computed survival probabilities for electron-type neutrinos using standard MSW techniques and compared calculated event rates in the four operating experiments with observed rates, treating the ⁸B flux as a free parameter rather than fixing it to the standard solar model value.

## Key numbers
- The best-fit CNO solution has χ²_min = 0.28, compared to χ²_min = 0.31 for the small mixing angle pp-based solution and χ²_min = 2.5 for the large mixing angle pp-based solution.
- In the specific CNO solution presented, pp neutrinos represent 0.05% of total solar luminosity; CNO neutrinos correspond to 99.95% of total energy output.
- The total ⁸B neutrino flux (all flavors) is about 1.5 times the standard solar model flux.
- For the CNO solution, the survival probability for the 0.86 MeV ⁷Be line is 0.11; for neutrinos above 4 MeV it is approximately exp(−17 MeV/energy).
- The CNO contribution to the chlorine detector is suppressed by about a factor of 34, and to the gallium experiments by about a factor of 9.
- The best-fit CNO solution has neutrino parameters Δm² = 7×10⁻⁶ eV² and sin²2θ = 0.14.
- For vacuum oscillations (not MSW), the largest CNO contribution found was 12% of solar luminosity, with parameters Δm² = 6.4×10⁻¹¹ eV² and sin²2θ = 1.0.
- The CNO solution predicts ⁷Be neutrinos will be unobservable in BOREXINO, with an interaction rate of 10⁻⁴ the standard model prediction; pp neutrinos would be ∼3×10⁻⁴ the standard model prediction in HELLAZ and HERON.
- The CNO contribution to event rate in BOREXINO, HELLAZ, and HERON would be about three times the standard model prediction in the relevant energy ranges.

## Context
This paper extends the long-standing debate initiated by Bethe (1939) on whether the sun shines via the pp chain or the CNO cycle, challenging the consensus that gallium experiments (GALLEX and SAGE) had already established pp chain dominance. The authors explicitly note that standard solar models (e.g., Bahcall and Pinsonneault 1995) predict only about 2% CNO contribution to solar luminosity, and that helioseismological measurements show sound velocity profiles agreeing with standard models to within a percent. The work demonstrates the limits of radiochemical experiments that lack energy resolution and motivates future experiments with energy-resolving capability (BOREXINO, HELLAZ, HERON) as necessary to empirically determine the solar neutrino spectrum, while also showing that SuperKamiokande, SNO, and ICARUS cannot rule out the CNO solution.
