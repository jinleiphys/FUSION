# Machine learning the deuteron: new architectures and uncertainty quantification

> We solve the ground state of the deuteron using a variational neural network ansatz for the wave function in momentum space. This ansatz provides a flexible representation of both the $S$ and the $D$ states, with relative errors in the energy which are within fractions of a percent of a full diagonalisation benchmark. We extend the previous work on this area in two directions. First, we study new architectures by adding more layers to the network and by exploring different connections between the states. Second, we provide a better estimate of the numerical uncertainty by taking into account the final oscillations at the end of the minimisation process. Overall, we find that the best performing architecture is the simple one-layer, state-independent network. Two-layer networks show indications of overfitting, in regions that are not probed by the fixed momentum basis where calculations are performed. In all cases, the error associated to the model oscillations around the real minimum is larger than the stochastic initialisation uncertainties. The conclusions that we draw can be generalised to other quantum mechanics settings.

## Key claim

The variational neural network ansatz for the deuteron ground state in momentum space achieves relative energy errors within fractions of a percent of a full diagonalisation benchmark, but the best performing architecture is the simple one-layer, state-connected network rather than deeper or state-disconnected alternatives; two-layer networks show indications of overfitting in regions not probed by the fixed momentum basis.

## Method

The authors solve the deuteron ground state using a variational neural network wavefunction ansatz in momentum space with a partial wave expansion separating S-state (L=0) and D-state (L=2) components. They employ the N3LO Entem-Machleidt nucleon-nucleon force on a one-dimensional momentum grid with N_q=64 points using tangential Gauss-Legendre quadrature, with a three-step training process: random parameter initialization, pretraining to match a physically-motivated target function using overlap-based cost functions, and energy minimization using RMSprop for 2.5e5 epochs. Four architectures are compared: one-layer and two-layer networks with either state-connected (sc) or state-disconnected (sd) configurations, using 20 random initializations per architecture and hidden node counts N_hid in {20,30,40,60,80,100}.

## Key numbers

- Benchmark ground state energy from exact diagonalization: E_GS = -2.2267 MeV
- Converged models selected within energy range E in (-2.220, -2.227) MeV, corresponding to within 0.3% of the benchmark
- Stochastic uncertainties (standard deviation across 20 initializations) are well within 1 keV (0.04%) of each other
- Post-evolution oscillation uncertainties are typically smaller than 4 keV (0.2%), but always larger than stochastic uncertainties
- One-layer state-connected (1sc) architecture achieves perfect convergence rate (r=100%) across all N_hid values
- Two-layer state-disconnected (2sd) architecture with N_hid=20 has convergence rate r approximately 1%, requiring N_tot=1100 runs to obtain 20 converged models
- For the 1sc architecture with N_hid=100, post-evolution mean energy is approximately -2.2258 MeV with oscillation errors delta E_+ = 0.5 keV and delta E_- = 0.3 keV
- Distance between post-evolution lower bound and real minimum is always less than 2 keV

## Context

This work extends the elementary approach of Keeble (2020), which used only a one-layer state-connected architecture for the deuteron, by exploring deeper networks and state-disconnected architectures. The study provides systematic uncertainty quantification for variational neural network solvers by introducing post-evolution oscillation analysis as a new error source, finding that oscillation uncertainties dominate over stochastic initialization uncertainties. The conclusions can be generalised to other quantum mechanics settings, and the methodology follows the variational philosophy used in condensed matter by Carleo (2017) and in quantum chemistry by FermiNet (Pfau 2020).
