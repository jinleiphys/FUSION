# On the proton charge extensions

> We examine how corrections to $S$-state energy levels, $ E_{nS}$, in hydrogenic atoms due to the finite proton size are affected by moments of the proton charge distribution. The corrections to $E_{nS}$ are computed moment by moment. The results demonstrate that the next-to-leading order term in the expansion is of order $r_p / a_B $ times the size of the leading order $ \langle r_p^2 \rangle $ term. Our analysis thus dispels any concern that the larger relative size of this term for muonic hydrogen versus electronic hydrogen might account for the current discrepancy of proton radius measurements extracted from the two systems. Furthermore, the next-to-leading order term in powers of $r_p / a_B $ that we derive from a dipole proton form factor is proportional to $\langle r_p^3 \rangle $, rather than $\langle r_p^4 \rangle$ as would be expected from the scalar nature of the form factor. The dependence of the finite-size correction on $\langle r_p^3 \rangle $ and higher odd-power moments is shown to be a general result for any spherically symmetric proton charge distribution. A method for computing the moment expansion of the finite-size correction to arbitrary order is introduced and the results are tabulated for principal quantum numbers up to $n=7$.

# On the proton charge extensions

## Key claim

The next-to-leading order term in the finite-size correction to hydrogenic S-state energy levels is of order \(r_p / a_B\) times the leading \(\langle r_p^2 \rangle\) term, but this correction is far too small (about 0.02 meV for muonic hydrogen) to account for the 4% discrepancy in proton radius measurements between electronic and muonic hydrogen. The next-to-leading order term is proportional to \(\langle r_p^3 \rangle\) rather than \(\langle r_p^4 \rangle\), which is a general result for any spherically symmetric proton charge distribution.

## Method

The authors computed finite-size corrections to S-state energy levels in hydrogenic atoms by expanding the proton charge distribution in a moment expansion, using both a dipole form factor approximation and a general coordinate-space analysis with a series representation of the spherically symmetric charge density. They evaluated the energy correction term by term in powers of \(\langle r_p^j \rangle / a_\ell^j\) using a specially constructed sequence of test functions to handle the Dirac delta distributions on the positive real axis, and tabulated the dimensionless coefficients \(c_n^{(j)}\) for principal quantum numbers up to \(n=7\) and moments up to \(j=10\).

## Key numbers

- For muonic hydrogen with a dipole form factor, the ratio of the next-to-leading order term to the leading term is \(\xi_{2S}(\rho ,\mu) \approx -4.1 \times 10^{-3}\); for electronic hydrogen, \(\xi_{2S}(\rho ,e) \approx -2.2 \times 10^{-5}\).
- The missing terms needed to reconcile the muonic hydrogen proton radius with the CODATA value would need to amount to 0.31 meV in the Lamb shift; the next-to-leading order term contributes only 0.02 meV.
- The dipole form factor parameter is \(\Lambda^2 \simeq 0.71\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- The leading-order finite-size correction is \(\Delta E_{nS} = \frac{2 \alpha}{3 n^3 a_B^3} \langle r_p^2 \rangle\).
- The CODATA proton radius is \(r_p = 0.8775(51)\ \text{fm}\); the CREMA muonic hydrogen value is \(r_p = 0.84087(39)\ \text{fm}\).

## Context

This work addresses the proton radius puzzle, specifically the discrepancy between the CODATA value from hydrogen spectroscopy and electron-proton scattering (2010) and the CREMA collaboration's measurement from muonic hydrogen Lamb shifts (Pohl 2010, Antognini 2013). The authors examine a proposal by Gluck (2014) that lepton-dependent parametrization of the finite-size correction might resolve the discrepancy, showing that proposal leads to a divergence. The analysis parallels and generalizes the numerical work of Carroll (2011) using analytic methods, and provides a general moment expansion framework (tabulated for \(n\) up to 7) that can be applied to nuclear targets with larger moments or transitions involving higher S-states in light atoms.
