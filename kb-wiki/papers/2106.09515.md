# A Neutron Star is born

> A neutron star was first detected as a pulsar in 1967. It is one of the most mysterious compact objects in the universe, with a radius of the order of 10 km and masses that can reach two solar masses. In fact, neutron stars are star remnants, a kind of stellar zombies (they die, but do not disappear). In the last decades, astronomical observations yielded various contraints for the neutron star masses and finally, in 2017, a gravitational wave was detected (GW170817). Its source was identified as the merger of two neutron stars coming from NGC 4993, a galaxy 140 million light years away from us. The very same event was detected in $γ$-ray, x-ray, UV, IR, radio frequency and even in the optical region of the electromagnetic spectrum, starting the new era of multi-messenger astronomy. To understand and describe neutron stars, an appropriate equation of state that satisfies bulk nuclear matter properties is necessary. GW170817 detection contributed with extra constraints to determine it. On the other hand, magnetars are the same sort of compact objects, but bearing much stronger magnetic fields that can reach up to 10$^{15}$ G on the surface as compared with the usual 10$^{12}$ G present in ordinary pulsars. While the description of ordinary pulsars is not completely established, describing magnetars poses extra challenges. In this paper, I give an overview on the history of neutron stars and on the development of nuclear models and show how the description of the tiny world of the nuclear physics can help the understanding of the cosmos, especially of the neutron stars.

## Key claim

This paper provides a comprehensive review of how nuclear physics models, particularly relativistic mean-field models, are used to describe neutron stars, emphasizing that the equation of state must satisfy both bulk nuclear matter properties and observational constraints from multi-messenger astronomy, with the caveat that the exact composition of neutron star interiors remains unknown and depends on model extrapolations to densities up to 10 times nuclear saturation density.

## Method

The author reviews the historical development of nuclear models from the liquid drop model and semi-empirical mass formula through the free Fermi gas model to relativistic mean-field (RMF) models such as the Walecka σ-ω model, showing how equations of state are derived from Lagrangian densities using the Euler-Lagrange equations and the energy-momentum tensor. The paper then connects these nuclear matter properties, saturation density, symmetry energy, incompressibility, to neutron star macroscopic properties through the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations, and discusses how observational constraints from gravitational wave event GW170817 and NICER measurements of PSR J0740+6620 restrict the allowed equations of state.

## Key numbers

* Neutron star radius is of order 10 km, with masses that can reach two solar masses (M⊙)
* The heaviest known pulsar, PSR J0740+6620, has mass 2.08 ± 0.07 M⊙ and diameter 25–27 km as measured by NICER
* GW170817 originated from NGC 4993, a galaxy 140 million light years away
* Magnetar surface magnetic fields reach up to 10^15 G, compared to 10^12 G for ordinary pulsars
* Nuclear saturation density ρ₀ ranges between 0.148 and 0.17 fm⁻³ from experimental data
* PREX2 experiment gives neutron skin thickness θ = 0.283 ± 0.071 fm for ²⁰⁸Pb and interior baryon density ρ₀ = 0.1480 ± 0.0036 (exp) ± 0.0013 (theo) fm⁻³
* Binding energy per nucleon is approximately 8.5 MeV for most nuclei, with saturation value a_v ≈ 16 MeV
* Semi-empirical mass formula parameters: a_v = 15.68 MeV, a_s = 18.56 MeV, a_c × e² = 0.72 MeV, a_i = 18.1 MeV
* Symmetry energy J accepted range: 25 < J < 35 MeV; slope L₀ accepted range: 25 < L₀ < 115 MeV; PREX2 gives L₀ = 106 ± 37 MeV
* Incompressibility K₀ ranges between 190 and 270 MeV
* Symmetry energy curvature K_sym ranges between -400 and 100 MeV
* The σ-ω model with C_s² = 267.1 and C_v² = 195.9 gives binding energy E/N − M = −15.75 MeV at saturation density ρ = 0.19 fm⁻³

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting nuclear matter models to compact object astrophysics, building on the foundational models of Walecka (quantum hadrodynamics), the liquid drop model (1929), and the semi-empirical mass formula of Bethe and Weizsäcker (1935). The paper contextualizes recent observational breakthroughs including the gravitational wave detection GW170817 from the merger of two neutron stars and NICER measurements of PSR J0740+6620, which together provide new constraints on the equation of state. The work enables future studies to refine nuclear models using multi-messenger astronomy data, particularly for understanding the inner core composition of neutron stars, whether it consists of deconfined quarks or a mixed hadron-quark phase, and for addressing the additional challenges posed by magnetar descriptions.
