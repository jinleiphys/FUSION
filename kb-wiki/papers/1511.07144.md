# Symmetry energy and neutron star properties in the saturated Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model

> In this work, we adopt the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model that ensures the nuclear matter saturation properties to study the density dependence of the symmetry energy. With the interactions constrained by the chiral symmetry, the symmetry energy shows novel characters different from those in conventional mean-field models. First, the negative symmetry energy at high densities that is absent in relativistic mean-field (RMF) models can be obtained in the RMF approximation by introducing a chiral isovector-vector interaction, although it would be ruled out by the neutron star (NS) stability. Second, with the inclusion of the isovector-scalar interaction the symmetry energy exhibits a general softening at high densities even for the large slope parameter of the symmetry energy. The NS properties obtained in the present NJL model can be in accord with the observations. The NS maximum mass obtained with various isovector-scalar couplings and momentum cutoffs is well above the $2M_\odot$, and the NS radius obtained well meets the limits extracted from recent measurements. In particular, the significant reduction of the canonical NS radius occurs with the moderate decrease of the slope of the symmetry energy.

## Key claim
The saturated Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model can produce a super-soft symmetry energy that becomes negative at high densities when a chiral isovector-vector interaction is included, but such a configuration is ruled out by neutron star stability; with an isovector-scalar interaction instead, the symmetry energy softens generally at high densities even for large slope parameters at saturation.

## Method
The authors adopt a hadron-level NJL model that respects chiral symmetry and includes scalar, vector, scalar-vector, isovector, isovector-vector, and isovector-scalar interactions in the mean-field approximation. They fix the model parameters (momentum cutoff Λ, bare nucleon mass, coupling constants) by requiring nuclear matter saturation at ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³ with binding energy −16 MeV and symmetry energy 31.6 MeV at saturation. They then compute the density-dependent symmetry energy, solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equation for neutron star mass-radius relations, and compare with observational constraints.

## Key numbers
- Saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³; binding energy per nucleon at saturation: −16 MeV.
- Symmetry energy at saturation density fixed to 31.6 MeV.
- Bare nucleon mass m₀ = 41.3 MeV for Λ = 400 MeV; scalar coupling G_S = 1.669 GeV·fm³; vector coupling G_V = 1.581 GeV·fm³; scalar-vector coupling G_SV = 2.054 GeV·fm⁹.
- Slope parameter L at saturation density: 93.6 MeV for Λ = 400 MeV with no isovector-scalar coupling; reduced to 48.9 MeV with G_ρS = −0.7 G_SV.
- Incompressibility κ = 296 MeV for Λ = 400 MeV (constant across isovector-scalar variations).
- Neutron star maximum mass for Λ = 400 MeV: 2.43–2.44 M⊙ (various G_ρS); for Λ = 320 MeV: 3.15 M⊙; for Λ = 500 MeV: 2.17 M⊙.
- Radius of canonical 1.4 M⊙ neutron star for Λ = 400 MeV: 13.7 km (G_ρS = 0); 13.0 km (G_ρS = −0.7 G_SV); range across all cutoffs and non-positive G_ρS: 12.6–14.9 km.
- Critical density for chiral symmetry restoration (p_F = Λ): ρ_c/ρ₀ = 3.53 for Λ = 400 MeV; 1.81 for Λ = 320 MeV; 6.90 for Λ = 500 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the saturated NJL model of Inhi et al. (2012) to study the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which is poorly constrained at supra-normal densities. It contrasts with relativistic mean-field (RMF) models such as NL3 (Lalazissis et al., 1997) that cannot produce super-soft or negative symmetry energies at high densities. The results enable comparisons with neutron star mass constraints from pulsars J1614-2230 (1.97±0.04 M⊙) and J0348+0432 (2.01±0.04 M⊙), and with radius constraints from various observational analyses (Steiner et al., 2016; Bogdanov et al., 2013; others). The work also relates to nonrelativistic models with MDI interactions (Wen et al., 2009) that encountered similar stability issues with super-soft symmetry energies.
