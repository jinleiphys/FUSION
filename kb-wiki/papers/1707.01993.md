# The Way Forward - Closing Remarks at Quark Matter 2017

> This contribution is a written version of my closing talk presented at the Quark Matter 2017 conference. Neither the talk nor this contribution to the conference proceedings is intended as a comprehensive summary. Rather, a brief discussion is presented of emerging themes and challenges in the field of relativistic heavy ion physics.

## Key claim

This closing talk does not present a new result but instead identifies emerging themes and challenges in relativistic heavy ion physics, arguing that the field is transitioning from discovery to precision while facing unresolved puzzles such as the compatibility of hydrodynamic and transport approaches in small systems and the interpretation of chiral magnetic effect measurements.

## Method

The author synthesizes selected results presented at the Quark Matter 2017 conference, including Bayesian parameter extraction from hydrodynamic models, comparisons of flow harmonics across p+p, p+Pb, and Pb+Pb collisions, AMPT transport model calculations, chiral magnetic effect correlator measurements from STAR and CMS, and ultraperipheral collision data for nuclear parton distribution functions. The talk also reviews upcoming experimental programs at RHIC, LHC, NICA, FAIR, and JPARC.

## Key numbers

- The RHIC Beam Energy Scan II program will cover collision energies from sqrt(s_NN) ~ 40 GeV down to ~2 GeV to access the region where mu_B ~ T.
- AMPT model calculations reproduce PHENIX v_2(p_T) data in d+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 20 to 200 GeV.
- The isotope run at RHIC in 2018 will compare Ru-96 + Ru-96 collisions to Zr-96 + Zr-96 collisions, varying the magnetic field by 10% while controlling for other factors.
- LHC Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 and 5.02 TeV provide the basis for ultraperipheral photoproduction measurements.
- The proposed Future Circular Collider would have center of mass energy three times that of the LHC.

## Context

This closing talk extends the tradition of Quark Matter conference summaries, following Schukraft's opening overview at the same meeting. It highlights the tension between hydrodynamic models (exemplified by Weller and Romatschke's "One Fluid to Rule Them All" approach) and transport models like AMPT in describing small-system flow, a puzzle identified at the Mont Sainte Odile II meeting after Quark Matter 2015. The talk also connects the Bayesian methodology for parameter extraction (Bernhard 2016, Pratt 2015) to future precision measurements, and positions ultraperipheral collision results as a bridge to the physics program of a future Electron Ion Collider.
