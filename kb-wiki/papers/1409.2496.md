# Deconfinement as an entropic self-destruction: a solution for the quarkonium suppression puzzle?

> The entropic approach to dissociation of bound states immersed in strongly coupled systems is developed. In such systems, the excitations of the bound state are often delocalized and characterized by a large entropy, so that the bound state is strongly entangled with the rest of the statistical system. If this entropy $S$ increases with the separation $r$ between the constituents of the bound state, $S = S(r)$, then the resulting entropic force $F = T\ {\partial S}/{\partial r}$ ($T$ is temperature) can drive the dissociation process. As a specific example, we consider the case of heavy quarkonium in strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma, where lattice QCD indicates a large amount of entropy associated with the heavy quark pair at temperatures $0.9\ T_c \leq T \leq 1.5\ T_c$ ($T_c$ is the deconfinement temperature); this entropy $S(r)$ grows with the inter-quark distance $r$. We argue that the entropic mechanism results in an anomalously strong quarkonium suppression in the temperature range near $T_c$. This "entropic self-destruction" may thus explain why the experimentally measured quarkonium nuclear modification factor at RHIC (lower energy density) is smaller than at LHC (higher energy density), possibly resolving the "quarkonium suppression puzzle" - all of the previously known mechanisms of quarkonium dissociation operate more effectively at higher energy densities, and this contradicts the data. Moreover, we find that near $T_c$ the entropic force leads to delocalization of the bound hadron states; we argue that this delocalization may be the mechanism underlying deconfinement.

## Key claim

The entropic force arising from the entanglement entropy of a heavy quark-antiquark pair with the quark-gluon plasma can drive quarkonium dissociation anomalously strongly near the deconfinement temperature \(T_c\), potentially explaining why the quarkonium nuclear modification factor is smaller at RHIC (lower energy density) than at LHC (higher energy density), a puzzle for conventional dissociation mechanisms that operate more effectively at higher energy densities.

## Method

The author derives the entropic force \(F = T \, \partial S / \partial r\) from the entropy \(S(r,T)\) of a bound state as a function of inter-constituent distance \(r\) and temperature \(T\). For heavy quarkonium in strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma, the entropy from lattice QCD (Kaczmarek et al., 2005) is approximately linear in \(r\) at intermediate distances. Balancing this entropic force against the linear confining force \(\sigma\) yields an expression for the mean squared separation \(\langle x^2 \rangle\) of the quark-antiquark pair; when the denominator vanishes, the bound state becomes completely delocalized.

## Key numbers

* The entropy associated with the heavy quark pair is observed in lattice QCD at temperatures \(0.9 \, T_c \leq T \leq 1.5 \, T_c\), where \(T_c\) is the deconfinement temperature.
* For two-flavor QCD, \(k T_c \simeq 200\) MeV (Kaczmarek et al., 2005).
* The divergence of the mean inter-quark distance occurs at \(k T = \sigma(T)/s'(T) \simeq 280\) MeV \(\simeq 1.4 \, k T_c\), using \(\sigma \simeq 0.2\) GeV\(^2\) and \(s' \simeq 0.7\) GeV.
* The dissociation temperature estimated from the linear confining potential alone (neglecting entanglement entropy) is \(T_d \simeq 300\) MeV.
* The linear dependence of entropy on \(r\) holds within the range \(0.2\ \mathrm{fm} < r < 0.6\ \mathrm{fm}\) from lattice data.

## Context

This work extends the entropic-force concept from polymer physics and Einstein diffusion (Neumann) to the dissociation of bound states in QCD matter, specifically addressing the quarkonium suppression puzzle noted by Satz (2013). It contrasts with conventional mechanisms such as Debye screening (Matsui and Satz, 1986) and thermal gluon activation (Kharzeev, 1994; Shuryak, 1978), which predict stronger suppression at higher energy densities. The proposed entropic self-destruction near \(T_c\) may also connect to the percolation picture of deconfinement (Celik et al., 1980; Satz, 1998) and to the condensation of long strings (Kogut et al., 1974; Aharonov et al., 1987; Deo et al., 1989). A detailed quantitative study using lattice QCD results and real-time plasma evolution is needed for a definitive conclusion.
