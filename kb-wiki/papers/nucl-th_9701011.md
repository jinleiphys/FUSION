# Extended Hauser-Feshbach Method for Statistical Binary-Decay of Light-Mass Systems

> An Extended Hauser-Feshbach Method (EHFM) is developed for light heavy-ion fusion reactions in order to provide a detailed analysis of all the possible decay channels by including explicitly the fusion-fission phase-space in the description of the cascade chain. The mass-asymmetric fission component is considered as a complex-fragment binary-decay which can be treated in the same way as the light-particle evaporation from the compound nucleus in statistical-model calculations. The method of the phase-space integrations for the binary-decay is an extension of the usual Hauser-Feshbach formalism to be applied to the mass-symmetric fission part. The EHFM calculations include ground-state binding energies and discrete levels in the low excitation-energy regions which are essential for an accurate evaluation of the phase-space integrations of the complex-fragment emission (fission). In the present calculations, EHFM is applied to the first-chance binary-decay by assuming that the second-chance fission decay is negligible. In a similar manner to the description of the fusion-evaporation process, the usual cascade calculation of light-particle emission from the highly excited complex fragments is applied. This complete calculation is then defined as EHFM+CASCADE. Calculated quantities such as charge-, mass- and kinetic-energy distributions are compared with inclusive and/or exclusive data for the $^{32}$S+$^{24}$Mg and $^{35}$Cl+$^{12}$C reactions which have been selected as typical examples. Finally, the missing charge distributions extracted from exclusive measurements are also successfully compared with the EHFM+CASCADE predictions.

# Extended Hauser-Feshbach Method for Statistical Binary-Decay of Light-Mass Systems

## Key claim

The Extended Hauser-Feshbach Method (EHFM) treats mass-asymmetric fission as a complex-fragment binary-decay equivalent to light-particle evaporation in statistical-model calculations, but only for first-chance binary-decay under the assumption that second-chance fission decay is negligible. The method is applied specifically to light-mass systems where the fissility parameters are below the Businaro-Gallone point, making asymmetric fission assimilable to complex-fragment emission.

## Method

The authors extend the standard Hauser-Feshbach formalism to include phase-space integrations for binary-decay of complex fragments (fission) from the compound nucleus, using four distinct phase-space integration formulas depending on whether the lighter and/or heavier fragment is in discrete low-lying states or in the continuum. The transmission coefficients for complex-fragment emission are evaluated using a simplified Fermi-function formula with a diffuseness parameter of 0.5 MeV, and the scission-point barrier height is calculated from Coulomb and rotational energies with a neck-length parameter d = 3.0 ± 0.5 fm. The first-chance binary-decay calculation is followed by a standard CASCADE calculation for sequential light-particle evaporation from the excited fragments, with the complete procedure defined as EHFM+CASCADE.

## Key numbers

- Neck-length parameter d = 3.0 ± 0.5 fm, used as the only adjustable parameter of the model
- Diffuseness parameter Δ_s = 0.5 MeV for the transmission coefficient formula
- Constant radius parameter r_s = 1.2 fm for fragment radii
- Level density parameter a = A/8, with pairing energy Δ_pair = 12/√A
- Angular-momentum diffuseness Δ_J = 1 ħ for the fusion cross-section transmission coefficients
- Energy integrations performed with 1 MeV steps and angular momentum steps of 1 ħ
- The method is applied to the ³²S+²⁴Mg and ³⁵Cl+¹²C reactions (compound nucleus mass A_CN ≤ 60)

## Context

This work extends the Hauser-Feshbach formalism (Hauser, 1952) to treat complex-fragment emission and fusion-fission in light-mass systems, providing an alternative to the transition-state model used by Sanders (1991) which employs phase-space at the saddle point. The approach builds on earlier EHFM communications by Matsuse (1984, 1991) and is applied to reactions previously studied by Sanders (1987) for ³²S+²⁴Mg and by Beck (1992, 1996) for ³⁵Cl+¹²C. The method enables systematic investigation of fusion-fission processes in light-mass systems and can be extended to a wider mass range from light to intermediate-mass nuclear systems.
