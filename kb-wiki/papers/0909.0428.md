# Long range rapidity correlations as seen in the STAR experiment

> We analyze long range rapidity correlations observed in the STAR experiment at RHIC. Our goal is to extract properties of the two particle correlation matrix, accounting for the analysis method of the STAR experiment. We find a surprisingly large correlation strength for central collisions of gold nuclei at highest RHIC energies. We argue that such correlations cannot be the result of impact parameter fluctuations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the forward-backward correlation strength observed by STAR for central Au-Au collisions at the highest RHIC energy violates a general bound of b < 1/2 derived from a Gaussian approximation, and that impact parameter fluctuations alone cannot explain this large correlation.

## Method

The authors model the three correlated multiplicities (forward, backward, and reference windows) using a Gaussian approximation for the probability distribution, then derive an expression for the conditional correlation coefficient b measured at fixed reference multiplicity. They compare this bound to the STAR data and test a toy model with Poissonian particle production from fluctuating participant nucleons (Monte Carlo Glauber), evaluating both the Gaussian approximation and a direct Monte Carlo implementation.

## Key numbers

- The bound b < 1/2 is derived for the conditional correlation coefficient when the reference window is wider than the forward/backward windows (δ_R = 1.0, δ = 0.2).
- For the toy model with ω_p ≈ 2.5 (0–10% centrality bin), the Gaussian approximation gives b ≈ 0.14 for central collisions and b ≈ 0.12 for midcentral (40–50%) collisions.
- The direct Monte Carlo Glauber evaluation of the toy model yields b ≈ δ/(δ + δ_R) = 1/6, corresponding to the limit ω_p → ∞.
- Typical forward/backward multiplicities are N_F,B ∼ 100 for central events and ∼ 20 for 50% centrality events.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of forward-backward correlations reported by the STAR collaboration (Abelev 2009, Tarnowsky 2008) and addresses the claim by Konchakovski (2008) and Bzdak (2009) that impact parameter fluctuations within centrality bins could explain the observed correlations. The paper shows that such fluctuations are insufficient and that intrinsic long-range correlations (e.g., from the Color Glass Condensate/Glasma or parton percolation) are needed. The results enable future extraction of the rapidity correlation function K(η−η') from experimental data.
