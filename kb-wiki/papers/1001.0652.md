# Quantum Monte-Carlo method applied to Non-Markovian barrier transmission

> In nuclear fusion and fission, fluctuation and dissipation arise due to the coupling of collective degrees of freedom with internal excitations. Close to the barrier, both quantum, statistical and non-Markovian effects are expected to be important. In this work, a new approach based on quantum Monte-Carlo addressing this problem is presented. The exact dynamics of a system coupled to an environment is replaced by a set of stochastic evolutions of the system density. The quantum Monte-Carlo method is applied to systems with quadratic potentials. In all range of temperature and coupling, the stochastic method matches the exact evolution showing that non-Markovian effects can be simulated accurately. A comparison with other theories like Nakajima-Zwanzig or Time-ConvolutionLess ones shows that only the latter can be competitive if the expansion in terms of coupling constant is made at least to fourth order. A systematic study of the inverted parabola case is made at different temperatures and coupling constants. The asymptotic passing probability is estimated in different approaches including the Markovian limit. Large differences with the exact result are seen in the latter case or when only second order in the coupling strength is considered as it is generally assumed in nuclear transport models. On opposite, if fourth order in the coupling or quantum Monte-Carlo method is used, a perfect agreement is obtained.

## Key claim

The quantum Monte-Carlo method exactly reproduces the dissipative dynamics of a system coupled to a heat bath for quadratic potentials across all temperatures and coupling strengths, but the method requires a specific noise-centering technique (Eq. 8) to reduce statistical errors enough for practical simulation; without this modification, statistical fluctuations grow too fast in time.

## Method

The authors replace the exact Liouville-von Neumann equation for the total system-plus-environment density with a set of stochastic differential equations for separable system and environment densities, using complex Gaussian noises whose correlations are chosen to recover the exact average evolution. They then project out the environment degrees of freedom by integrating the environment dynamics analytically, obtaining a closed stochastic master equation for the system density alone that depends on memory functions (the dissipation kernel D and the noise kernel D₁) derived from the spectral density. The method is applied to the Caldeira-Leggett model with a Drude spectral density, first for a harmonic oscillator and then for an inverted parabola, and compared to Nakajima-Zwanzig (NZ) and Time-ConvolutionLess (TCL) expansions truncated at second and fourth order in the coupling constant.

## Context

This work extends the stochastic formulation of open quantum systems proposed by Lacroix (2008) to the Caldeira-Leggett model with an inverted barrier, directly relevant to nuclear fusion and fission where collective degrees of freedom couple to internal excitations. The authors compare their quantum Monte-Carlo method against the Nakajima-Zwanzig (Nakajima, 1958; Zwanzig, 1960) and Time-ConvolutionLess (Shi and Hasse, 1977; Hashitsume et al., 1977) projection formalisms, showing that TCL truncated at fourth order in the coupling constant matches the exact result while TCL at second order (commonly used in nuclear transport models by Takigawa et al., 2004; Ayik et al., 2005; Washiyama et al., 2007) shows large deviations, especially near the barrier. The method enables accurate simulation of non-Markovian quantum dissipative dynamics for barrier transmission without the complexity of path-integral approaches (Caldeira and Leggett, 1981; Frobrich and Lipperheide, 1996).
