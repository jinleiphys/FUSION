# Fluctuations in non-ideal pion gas with dynamically fixed particle number

> We consider a non-ideal hot pion gas with the dynamically fixed number of particles in the model with the $λϕ^4$ interaction. The effective Lagrangian for the description of such a system is obtained after dropping the terms responsible for the change of the total particle number. Reactions $π^+π^-\leftrightarrowπ^0π^0$, which determine the isospin balance of the medium, are permitted. Within the self-consistent Hartree approximation we compute the effective pion mass, thermodynamic characteristics of the system and the variance of the particle number at temperatures above the critical point of the induced Bose-Einstein condensation when the pion chemical potential reaches the value of the effective pion mass. We analyze conditions for the condensate formation in the process of thermalization of an initially non-equilibrium pion gas. The normalized variance of the particle number increases with a temperature decrease but remains finite in the critical point of the Bose-Einstein condensation. This is due to the non-perturbative account of the interaction and is in contrast to the ideal-gas case. In the kinetic regime of the condensate formation the variance is shown to stay finite also.

## Key claim

The normalized variance of the particle number in a non-ideal pion gas with dynamically fixed particle number remains finite at the critical point of induced Bose-Einstein condensation, in contrast to the ideal-gas case where it diverges; this finiteness holds both in thermal equilibrium and in the kinetic regime of condensate formation, provided the pion-pion interaction is treated non-perturbatively within the self-consistent Hartree approximation.

## Method

The authors start from a \(\lambda\phi^4\) Lagrangian for the pion triplet and derive an effective Lagrangian that drops terms changing the total pion number while retaining reactions \(\pi^+\pi^-\leftrightarrow\pi^0\pi^0\) that fix isospin balance. They then apply the self-consistent Hartree approximation to compute the effective pion mass, thermodynamic quantities, and the normalized variance of the particle number for electrically neutral pion gases of various isospin compositions (symmetric \(\pi^\pm,\pi^0\) gas, \(\pi^\pm\) gas, and \(\pi^0\) gas). The analysis covers both thermally equilibrated systems and non-equilibrium systems evolving toward Bose-Einstein condensation, using initial momentum distributions such as a delta-function and a step-function.

## Key numbers

- The coupling constant is taken as \(\lambda = m_\pi^2 / 2 f_\pi^2 \simeq 1.13\), with \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV and \(m_\pi = 139\) MeV.
- For the symmetric interacting pion gas at \(\lambda = 1\), the critical density for Bose-Einstein condensation from a delta-function initial distribution is \(n_{\rm in,c} \sim 0.3\,n_0\) for \(k_0 = 100\) MeV and \(n_{\rm in,c} \sim 1.4\,n_0\) for \(k_0 = 200\) MeV, where \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\) is nuclear saturation density.
- For a step-function initial distribution with \(k_0 \lesssim 0.7 m_\pi\), Bose-Einstein condensation starts to develop if \(n_{\rm in} \ge n_c \sim 2.5\, n_0\).
- The normalized variance at the critical point for the interacting gas is given by \(w(T_c^{\rm ind}) = \frac{g_n m^{*2} T_c^{\rm ind}}{g_m \lambda n} [4 + g_m \lambda C(T_c^{\rm ind})]\), where \(g_m\) and \(g_n\) depend on composition (e.g., \(g_m=10\), \(g_n=3\) for the symmetric gas).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of pion Bose-Einstein condensation in heavy-ion collisions, particularly the ideal-gas treatment of Begun (2006, 2008, 2015) which predicted a divergent normalized variance at the critical point, and the self-consistent Hartree calculations of Kolomeitsev (1996) for thermodynamic properties. The authors show that repulsive pion-pion interactions, treated non-perturbatively, remove the divergence of the normalized variance even in the thermodynamic limit, and they analyze conditions for condensate formation during thermalization of non-equilibrium pion distributions. This enables more realistic comparisons with fluctuation measurements in high-multiplicity events at SPS, RHIC, and LHC, though the authors caution that experimental background fluctuations must be carefully accounted for.
