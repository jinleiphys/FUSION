# $g_{9/2}$ neutron strength in the $N=29$ isotones and the $^{52}$Cr($d,p$)$^{53}$Cr reaction

> We performed a measurement of the $^{52}$Cr$(d,p)^{53}$Cr reaction at 16 MeV using the Florida State University Super-Enge Split-Pole Spectrograph (SE-SPS) and observed 26 states. While all of the states observed here had been seen in previous $(d,p)$ experiments, we changed five $L$ assignments from those reported previously and determined $L$ values for nine states that had not had such assignments made previously. The $g_{9/2}$ neutron strength observed in $^{53}$Cr in the present work and in the $N=29$ isotones $^{49}$Ca, $^{51}$Ti, and $^{55}$Fe via $(d,p)$ reactions is much smaller than the sum rule for this strength. Most of the observed $L=4$ strength in these nuclei is located in states near 4 MeV excitation energy. The remaining $g_{9/2}$ strength may be located in the continuum or may be fragmented among many bound states. A covariant density functional theory calculation provides support for the hypothesis that the $g_{9/2}$ neutron orbit is unbound in $^{53}$Cr. The ($α,^3$He) reaction may provide a more sensitive probe for the missing $g_{9/2}$ neutron strength. In addition, particle-$γ$ coincidence experiments may help resolve some remaining questions in this nucleus.

## Key claim

The observed $g_{9/2}$ neutron strength in $^{53}$Cr and the other $N=29$ isotones $^{49}$Ca, $^{51}$Ti, and $^{55}$Fe is much smaller than the sum rule predicts, with the possibility that the remaining strength may be located in the continuum (supported by covariant density functional theory calculations showing the $g_{9/2}$ orbit is unbound in $^{53}$Cr) or may be fragmented among many bound states beyond experimental sensitivity.

## Method

The authors measured the $^{52}$Cr$(d,p)^{53}$Cr reaction at 16 MeV using the Florida State University Super-Enge Split-Pole Spectrograph (SE-SPS), collecting proton angular distributions from 15 to 50 degrees in 5-degree increments. Spectroscopic factors were extracted by comparing measured angular distributions to calculations using the adiabatic deuteron breakup approach of Johnson and Soper, implemented with the FRESCO code, using optical potentials from Koning and Delaroche and binding potentials of Woods-Saxon form.

## Key numbers

- Sum of $g_{9/2}$ spectroscopic factors in $^{53}$Cr: 0.26(1) from two $L=4$ states (at 3706 keV with S=0.22(1) and 6230 keV with S=0.036(2))
- Sum of $f_{5/2}$ spectroscopic factors in $^{53}$Cr: 0.57(3)
- In $^{49}$Ca: sum of $g_{9/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.53; sum of $f_{5/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.97 (from Uozumi et al. (1994))
- In $^{51}$Ti: sum of $g_{9/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.20(3); sum of $f_{5/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.47(4) (from Riley et al. (2021))
- In $^{55}$Fe: sum of $g_{9/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.32(4); sum of $f_{5/2}$ spectroscopic factors = 0.74(6) (from Riley et al. (2023))
- Covariant density functional theory predicts the $g_{9/2}$ neutron orbit is unbound in $^{48}$Ca, $^{50}$Ti, and $^{52}$Cr, and bound by only 1.42 MeV in $^{54}$Fe
- The $g_{9/2}$ state at 3706 keV in $^{53}$Cr has spectroscopic factor 0.22(3); the lowest $9/2^+$ state in $^{49}$Ca at 4017.5 keV has spectroscopic factor 0.14 (from Uozumi et al. (1994))

## Context

This work extends recent $(d,p)$ studies of $N=29$ isotones by Riley et al. (2021) on $^{51}$Ti and Riley et al. (2023) on $^{55}$Fe, and compares with the earlier $^{48}$Ca$(d,p)^{49}$Ca study by Uozumi et al. (1994). The authors propose that the missing $g_{9/2}$ strength may be found using the $(\alpha,^3$He) reaction, which is more sensitive to high-angular-momentum orbits, or through particle-gamma coincidence experiments with CeBr$_3$ detectors at the SE-SPS. The work also connects to the island of inversion phenomenon near $^{60}$Cr, where pairs of neutrons are promoted from $fp$ orbits into the $g_{9/2}$ orbit.
