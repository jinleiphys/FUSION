# Deformed Coordinate-Space Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov Approach to Weakly Bound Nuclei and Large Deformations

> The coordinate space formulation of the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) method enables self-consistent treatment of mean-field and pairing in weakly bound systems whose properties are affected by the particle continuum space. Of particular interest are neutron-rich, deformed drip-line nuclei which can exhibit novel properties associated with neutron skin. To describe such systems theoretically, we developed an accurate 2D lattice Skyrme-HFB solver {\hfbax} based on B-splines. Compared to previous implementations, we made a number of improvements aimed at boosting the solver's performance. These include: explicit imposition of axiality and space inversion, use of the modified Broyden's method to solve self-consistent equations, and a partial parallelization of the code. {\hfbax} has been benchmarked against other HFB solvers, both spherical and deformed, and the accuracy of the B-spline expansion was tested by employing the multiresolution wavelet method. Illustrative calculations are carried out for stable and weakly bound nuclei at spherical and very deformed shapes, including constrained fission pathways. In addition to providing new physics insights, {\hfbax} can serve as a useful tool to assess the reliability and applicability of coordinate-space and configuration-space HFB solvers, both existing and in development.

## Key claim

The paper presents a new 2D lattice Skyrme-HFB solver called hfb-ax that enables self-consistent treatment of mean-field and pairing in weakly bound, neutron-rich deformed nuclei, but the solver is limited to axially symmetric and reflection-symmetric shapes, meaning it cannot describe the handful of nuclei with reflection-asymmetric ground-state deformations.

## Method

The authors developed a coordinate-space Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov solver using B-spline expansion on a 2D cylindrical grid with explicit imposition of axial symmetry and space inversion symmetry. They implemented the modified Broyden's method for accelerated convergence of self-consistent iterations and partial parallelization of the diagonalization step across different angular momentum blocks. The solver was benchmarked against the spherical finite-difference code hfbrad, the axial harmonic oscillator code hfbtho, and the multiresolution wavelet method for a two-center potential test case.

## Key numbers

- For $^{120}$Sn with a box of 19.2 fm, maximum grid spacing h=0.6 fm, and B-spline order M=13, the Hamiltonian matrix requires about 0.45 GB of storage and one diagonalization takes approximately 30 CPU minutes
- For $^{22}$O in a 2D square box of R=12 fm, h=0.6 fm and M=11, one diagonalization takes about 15 minutes without reflection symmetry and about 100 seconds with reflection symmetry imposed
- With Broyden's mixing using M_B=7, the binding energy variation is within 0.1 keV after 30 iterations, compared to over 80 iterations needed with linear mixing at alpha=0.6
- For the two-center potential test, the B-spline calculation with h=0.6 fm and M=13 reproduced wavelet reference energies to within 0.00001 MeV for the lowest states, while harmonic oscillator with N_sh=20 gave errors up to 0.034 MeV for the highest (halo) states
- The pairing strength V_0 = -187.05 MeV fm^3 was adjusted to reproduce the experimental neutron pairing gap of Delta_n = 1.245 MeV for $^{120}$Sn

## Context

This work extends the coordinate-space HFB approach previously implemented in the hfbvan code by the Vanderbilt group (Teran, Oberacker, Blazkiewicz) by adding reflection symmetry, Broyden's method, and partial parallelization to improve performance. The solver enables large-scale calculations of neutron-rich deformed nuclei near the drip line and constrained fission pathways, and can serve as a benchmark for next-generation nuclear DFT tools such as the wavelet-based solver under development (Fann, 2007). The paper demonstrates that harmonic oscillator basis methods (Dobaczewski, 2004) perform poorly for weakly bound halo states and large deformations compared to the coordinate-space approach.
