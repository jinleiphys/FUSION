# Symmetry Energy

> Examination of symmetry energy is carried out on the basis of an elementary binding-energy formula. Constraints are obtained on the energy value at the normal nuclear density and on the density dependence of the energy at subnormal densities.

# Symmetry Energy

## Key claim

The paper argues that the standard Bethe-Weizsäcker mass formula must include a surface symmetry energy term, and that the symmetry energy coefficient becomes mass-dependent (weakening for light nuclei) rather than constant, with the ratio of volume to surface symmetry coefficients constrained by both nuclear skin data and isobaric analogue state (IAS) excitation energies.

## Method

The author derives a modified binding-energy formula by treating volume and surface symmetry energies as coupled capacitors, then minimizes the total energy to obtain expressions for asymmetry partitioning and neutron-skin thickness. Constraints on the volume symmetry coefficient \(a_A^V\) and the ratio \(a_A^V/a_A^S\) are extracted by fitting the macroscopic formula to experimental neutron-skin data from Na isotopes and other nuclei, and separately by analyzing IAS excitation energies from the literature using charge-invariance arguments.

## Key numbers

- Volume symmetry coefficient: \(30.0 \, \text{MeV} \lesssim a_A^V \lesssim 32.5 \, \text{MeV}\)
- Ratio of volume to surface symmetry coefficients: \(2.6 \lesssim a_A^V/a_A^S \lesssim 3.0\)
- Symmetry energy at half normal density relative to volume coefficient: \(0.58 \lesssim S(\rho_0/2)/a_A^V \lesssim 0.69\)
- Power-law exponent for density dependence of symmetry energy: \(0.54 \lesssim \gamma \lesssim 0.77\) in \(S(\rho) \simeq a_A^V (\rho/\rho_0)^\gamma\)
- Neutron-star radius for \(1.4 \, M_\odot\) mass: \(11.5 \, \text{km} \lesssim R \lesssim 13.5 \, \text{km}\)
- Normal nuclear density binding energy: \(E_0 \approx -16.0 \, \text{MeV}\)
- Accuracy of macroscopic theory in reproducing microscopic skin correlations: at the level of \(0.01 \, \text{fm}\)

## Context

This work extends the classic Bethe-Weizsacker mass formula by incorporating a surface symmetry term, resolving an inconsistency noted in earlier treatments. The approach builds on Gibbs' thermodynamic construction for surface contributions and connects to microscopic mean-field calculations by Typel and Brown (2001) and Fuhrnstahl (2002). The results enable improved predictions for neutron-star structure by linking the symmetry energy density dependence to neutron-star radii, following the correlation found by Lattimer (2001) between radius and pressure in neutron matter.
