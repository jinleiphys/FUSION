# Auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo methods in nuclei

> Auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo methods enable the calculation of thermal and ground state properties of correlated quantum many-body systems in model spaces that are many orders of magnitude larger than those that can be treated by conventional diagonalization methods. We review recent developments and applications of these methods in nuclei using the framework of the configuration-interaction shell model.

## Key claim

Auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) enables thermal and ground-state calculations in model spaces many orders of magnitude larger than conventional diagonalization, but the method relies on interactions whose dominant components have a "good sign" (negative eigenvalues in a time-reversal representation) to avoid the sign problem; smaller bad-sign components can be treated by extrapolation.

## Method

The Gibbs operator \(e^{-\beta H}\) is expressed via a Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation as a functional integral over one-body propagators describing non-interacting nucleons in time-dependent external auxiliary fields. Thermal expectation values are computed by Monte Carlo sampling of these auxiliary fields, using particle-number projection (discrete Fourier transform) to fix proton and neutron numbers in the canonical ensemble. For odd-particle systems, the ground-state energy is extracted from the imaginary-time single-particle Green's functions of the neighboring even-even nucleus, circumventing the odd-particle sign problem.

## Key numbers

- The pairing strength \(g = 0.212\) MeV was determined to reproduce pairing gaps from experimental odd-even mass differences for spherical nuclei in the mass region \(A = 40\)–\(80\) with \(Z = 20\), \(N = 28\), \(Z = 28\) or \(N = 40\).
- The multipole interaction strengths use renormalization factors \(k_2 = 2\), \(k_3 = 1.5\), and \(k_4 = 1\).
- Level densities for nickel isotopes \(^{59-64}\)Ni were calculated and compared with experimental data including neutron resonance data and proton evaporation spectra (Voinov 2012).
- The Green's function method for \(^{57}\)Fe used linear fits for \(0.5\) MeV\(^{-1} \leq |\tau| \leq 2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) at \(\beta = 4\) MeV\(^{-1}\) to extract the ground-state energy.

## Context

This review extends the AFMC (also called shell model Monte Carlo) approach developed by Lang (1993), Alhassid (1994), Koonin (1997), and Alhassid (2001) for the configuration-interaction shell model, enabling calculations in model spaces inaccessible to conventional diagonalization methods such as the no-core shell model (Barrett 2013) or coupled-cluster method (Hagen 2014). The method enables microscopic calculation of nuclear level densities, spin distributions, and pairing gaps in mid-mass and heavy nuclei, and can be extended to project on additional quantum numbers such as spin and axial quadrupole moment.
