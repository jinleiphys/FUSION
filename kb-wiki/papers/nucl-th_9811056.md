# Pole versus Breit-Wigner resonance description of the orbitally excited baryons

> We consider the masses used in recent studies involving the nonstrange sector of the l=1 baryons. The use of T-matrix and K-matrix poles versus the conventional Breit-Wigner masses is discussed within the context of a large-Nc fitting scheme.

# Pole versus Breit-Wigner resonance description of the orbitally excited baryons

## Key claim
The choice between Breit-Wigner masses and pole masses (T-matrix or K-matrix) significantly affects the fitted parameters and mixing angles in large-Nc QCD analyses of the nonstrange l=1 baryons, and the K-matrix pole mass is argued to be the most appropriate quantity for comparison with large-Nc formalisms, provided one accepts the model-dependence introduced by non-resonant background contributions.

## Method
The author repeated the six-parameter mass fit of Ref. [ln1] to the seven orbitally excited SU(6) 70-plet baryon masses, using both the conventional Breit-Wigner masses and a set of pole masses (taking the real part of the pole position). The resulting operator coefficients and mixing angles for the S11 (N(1535), N(1650)) and D13 (N(1520), N(1700)) resonances were compared between the two fits, and the theoretical connection between T-matrix, K-matrix, and Breit-Wigner definitions was examined using an effective Hamiltonian formalism for overlapping resonances.

## Key numbers
- The heaviest and lightest orbitally excited SU(6) 70-plet baryons are separated by only about 200 MeV, while the difference between Breit-Wigner and pole masses can be 50 MeV or more.
- The widths of the resonances are typically about 150 MeV, which is not small compared to the mass differences.
- The masses can be reproduced to the few MeV level without including O(Nc^{-2}) terms in the fit.
- The coefficient c2 remained small and consistent with zero in both the Breit-Wigner and pole-mass fits.

## Context
This work extends the large-Nc fitting scheme of Refs. [ln1, ln2, ln3, ln4] for orbitally excited baryons, which had previously used only Breit-Wigner masses. It directly addresses the controversy over mass definitions raised by Höhler and the Particle Data Group's recent addition of pole positions to the Baryon Summary Tables. The finding that K-matrix poles are most appropriate for large-Nc comparisons enables future studies to use K-matrix masses from analyses such as Green (1998), though the author notes that recent photo-decay amplitude measurements for N(1535) and N(1520) contradict the PDG values used in Ref. [ln3] and could test the consistency of the large-Nc picture.
