# Effects from inhomogeneities in the chiral transition

> We consider an approximation procedure to evaluate the finite-temperature one-loop fermionic density in the presence of a chiral background field which systematically incorporates effects from inhomogeneities in the chiral field through a derivative expansion. We apply the method to the case of a simple low-energy effective chiral model which is commonly used in the study of the chiral phase transition, the linear sigma-model coupled to quarks. The modifications in the effective potential and their consequences for the bubble nucleation process are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a systematic derivative-expansion method to incorporate effects from inhomogeneities in the chiral background field into the finite-temperature one-loop fermionic density, showing that the first nontrivial correction significantly modifies the effective potential by lowering the nucleation barrier and facilitating bubble nucleation, though the quantitative results are presented as estimates relying on several approximations (thin-wall limit, polynomial fits, and omission of pion-quark interactions).

## Method

The authors evaluate the finite-temperature one-loop fermionic density in the presence of a spatially varying chiral background field by expanding the fermion mass operator \(M(\hat{x}+\vec{x}_0)\) around a point \(\vec{x}_0\) in powers of derivatives of the field, then expanding the resulting expression for the density as a power series in \(\Delta M/M\). They apply this gradient expansion to the linear sigma-model coupled to quarks (with parameters \(f_\pi=93\) MeV, \(m_\pi=138\) MeV, \(\lambda^2=20\), and quark-sigma coupling \(g=5.5\)), compute the leading homogeneous term and the first inhomogeneity correction \(\rho_1+\rho_2\), and use the resulting modified Euler-Lagrange equation to study bubble nucleation properties in the thin-wall approximation.

## Key numbers

* The chiral phase transition is first-order even at \(\mu_q=0\) for \(g=5.5\), with critical temperature \(T_c\approx 124\) MeV and spinodal temperature \(T_{sp}\approx 108\) MeV.
* At \(T=108\) MeV (spinodal), the corrected critical bubble radius is \(R_c\approx 0.98\) fm and \(F_b/T\approx 0.20\), compared to \(R_c\approx 1.1\) fm and \(F_b/T\approx 0.9\) in the homogeneous case.
* At \(T=116\) MeV, the corrected values are \(R_c\approx 2.15\) fm and \(F_b/T\approx 1.14\), versus \(R_c\approx 2.2\) fm and \(F_b/T\approx 2.1\) for the homogeneous calculation.
* At \(T=124\) MeV (the homogeneous critical temperature), the homogeneous critical radius and \(F_b/T\) diverge, while the inhomogeneity-corrected values are \(R_c\approx 35\) fm and \(F_b/T\approx 394\).

## Context

This work extends the standard homogeneous mean-field treatment of the linear sigma-model coupled to quarks (used by Scavenius et al. (2001) and others for studying bubble nucleation and spinodal decomposition) by incorporating spatial variations of the chiral field via a derivative expansion, a technique previously applied to low-dimensional condensed matter systems at zero temperature by Sakita. The results suggest that inhomogeneities significantly reduce the nucleation barrier and critical bubble free energy, potentially accelerating phase conversion in heavy-ion collisions; the authors note that a full quantitative analysis should include numerical integration beyond the thin-wall approximation, pion-quark interactions, and dissipation/noise effects as studied by Fraga (2004).
