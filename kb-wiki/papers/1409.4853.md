# Addressing spectroscopic quality of covariant density functional theory

> The spectroscopic quality of covariant density functional theory has been accessed by analyzing the accuracy and theoretical uncertainties in the description of spectroscopic observables. Such analysis is first presented for the energies of the single-particle states in spherical and deformed nuclei. It is also shown that the inclusion of particle-vibration coupling improves the description of the energies of predominantly single-particle states in medium and heavy-mass spherical nuclei. However, the remaining differences between theory and experiment clearly indicate missing physics and missing terms in covariant energy density functionals. The uncertainties in the predictions of the position of two-neutron drip line sensitively depend on the uncertainties in the prediction of the energies of the single-particle states. On the other hand, many spectroscopic observables in well deformed nuclei at ground state and finite spin only weakly depend on the choice of covariant energy density functional.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while covariant density functional theory (CDFT) provides a reasonable description of many spectroscopic observables, the remaining differences between theory and experiment clearly indicate missing physics and missing terms in covariant energy density functionals, especially for single-particle states; the analysis is limited by the fact that only a small set of CEDFs is typically compared, and statistical independence of the functionals is not satisfied.

## Method

The author reviews the accuracy and theoretical uncertainties of CDFT by comparing calculated energies of single-particle states in spherical and deformed nuclei, rotational properties, and two-neutron drip line predictions against experimental data. For spherical nuclei, the analysis includes mean-field calculations and calculations that incorporate particle-vibration coupling (PVC) using the relativistic PVC model with the NL3* CEDF for three nuclei: $^{56}$Ni, $^{132}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb. For deformed nuclei, a statistical analysis of one-quasiparticle state energies is performed using the NL1 and NL3* CEDFs in the rare-earth and actinide regions, and rotational band properties are compared using the CRHB+LN calculations with the same two CEDFs.

## Key numbers

- Average deviation per state $\Delta\varepsilon$ for $^{208}$Pb proton states: 1.53 MeV in the "def+TO" scheme (mean field with time-odd fields) versus 0.84 MeV in the "hybrid" scheme (including PVC); for $^{208}$Pb neutron states: 1.00 MeV versus 0.47 MeV.
- Average deviation per state $\Delta\varepsilon$ for $^{132}$Sn proton states: 1.02 MeV (def+TO) versus 0.68 MeV (hybrid); for $^{132}$Sn neutron states: 0.89 MeV versus 0.39 MeV.
- Average deviation per state $\Delta\varepsilon$ for $^{56}$Ni proton states: 0.76 MeV (def+TO) versus 0.77 MeV (hybrid); for $^{56}$Ni neutron states: 0.89 MeV versus 0.71 MeV.
- In the actinide region, only approximately 33% of one-quasiparticle states are described with an accuracy better than 200 keV, and approximately 22% with an accuracy between 200 and 400 keV in the NL3* and NL1 CEDFs; some states deviate by more than 1 MeV, up to approximately 1.4 MeV.
- After rescaling by the Lorentz effective mass, more than 75% of deformed one-quasiparticle states are described with an accuracy better than 400 keV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of theoretical uncertainties in CDFT from ground-state bulk properties (as studied in Ref. AARR.14 for even-even nuclei) to spectroscopic observables, and it builds on earlier comparisons of single-particle spectra in spherical nuclei for CEDFs such as NL1, NL3, NLSH, DD-PC1, PC-PK1, and NL3* (e.g., Refs. ALR.98, BRRMG.99, DD-PC1, PC-PK1). The paper identifies that the inclusion of particle-vibration coupling improves the description of single-particle states in medium and heavy spherical nuclei, consistent with findings in Skyrme PVC calculations (Ref. TDTC.14), but that no such PVC model has yet been developed for deformed nuclei. The results enable future work to focus on developing PVC models for deformed systems and on incorporating single-particle information into the fitting protocols of CEDFs to reduce systematic errors.
