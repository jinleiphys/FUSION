# Quasireal photons in nucleus-nucleus collisions at intermediate and high energies, applications to nuclear structure, nuclear astrophysics and particle physics

## Key claim

The equivalent photon approximation provides a theoretically well-understood framework for electromagnetic excitation and dissociation in peripheral nucleus-nucleus collisions, but the clean interpretation of experimental data requires careful assessment of higher-order electromagnetic effects and nuclear background contributions, which depend on beam energy, impact parameter, and the strength parameter χ.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical methods for electromagnetic excitation in nucleus-nucleus collisions, including the semiclassical straight-line approximation, Glauber theory for quantal projectile motion, and the equivalent photon approximation where the cross section is written as σ = ∫ (dω/ω) n(ω) σ_γ(ω). Higher-order electromagnetic effects are treated using a coupled-channels approach, higher-order perturbation theory, or direct integration of the time-dependent Schrödinger equation; for sudden collisions a small-ξ approximation allows summation to infinite order, with analytical results obtained for a zero-range model of the neutron-core interaction.

## Key numbers

- The equivalent photon number for high beam energies is approximated as n(ω) = (2/π) Z² α ln(γv/ωR), where R is a cut-off radius.
- The strength parameter for higher-order effects is χ = (2 Z_X Z_eff^(1) e²)/(v b ħ k), where Z_X is the target charge number, b is impact parameter, and v is projectile velocity.
- The primordial big bang nucleosynthesis baryon-to-photon ratio η is constrained to 2.5×10⁻¹⁰ < η < 6×10⁻¹⁰.
- The astrophysical S-factor for the α(d,γ)⁶Li capture reaction is relevant at energies E ≈ 60–400 keV.
- For heavy-ion colliders, the electron capture cross section scales approximately as Z⁷ and is of order 100 b for Au-Au (Z=79) or Pb-Pb (Z=82) systems.
- At RHIC, photon-photon collisions can explore invariant masses up to several GeV; at LHC, up to about 100 GeV.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical and experimental work on Coulomb excitation and dissociation summarized in earlier treatments by Bertulani and collaborators (1990s), and by the authors' own prior work on higher-order effects (Typel et al., 1990s). It provides a unified framework connecting nuclear structure studies of halo nuclei (e.g., ¹¹Be and ¹¹Li at GANIL, RIKEN, and MSU), nuclear astrophysics measurements of radiative capture reactions such as ⁷Be(p,γ)⁸B and α(d,γ)⁶Li via Coulomb dissociation, and particle physics applications including antihydrogen production at LEAR and FERMILAB and electromagnetic processes at RHIC and LHC. The formalism enables future experiments at radioactive beam facilities to assess how low in beam energy one can go while still extracting first-order Coulomb dissociation results without excessive higher-order corrections.
