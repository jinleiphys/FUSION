# The Delta I=4 Bifurcation in Superdeformed Bands

> The origin of the Delta I=4 staggering effect which has recently been observed in several superdeformed bands is discussed. The Hamamoto and Mottelson model, which is based on a phenomenological parametrization of the hamiltonian as a quartic function of angular momentum, is analyzed. A stability of the description with respect to the C_4 - symmetry breaking terms is studied.

## Key claim
The Delta I=4 staggering in superdeformed bands can arise from a small C4-symmetric perturbation even when a large C2-invariant term dominates the Hamiltonian, provided the C4-breaking term is below a threshold value.

## Method
The authors analyzed exact solutions of a single-j shell model with quadrupole-quadrupole and hexadecapole-hexadecapole interactions for j=15/2 and j=21/2 with various particle numbers. They then studied a phenomenological Hamiltonian (based on the Hamamoto and Mottelson model) that includes a C4-symmetric term (B1) and an additional C2-symmetric term (D1), varying D1 while keeping A=90 and B1=1 fixed to test the stability of the staggering against C4 symmetry breaking.

## Key numbers
- The staggering effect is of the order of 50 eV, while excitation energies of superdeformed bands are about 20 MeV, corresponding to a 10^-6 perturbation of energy levels.
- For the chosen parameters (A=90, B1=1), the staggering vanishes at D1 approximately equal to 1.4.
- The staggering appears in yrast bands when the hexadecapole coupling constant chi_4 is larger than a certain critical value chi_4^crit (no explicit numerical value given for chi_4^crit).

## Context
This work extends the Hamamoto and Mottelson (1994) model, which proposed a quartic Hamiltonian with exact C4 symmetry to explain the Delta I=4 staggering first observed in the superdeformed yrast band of 149Gd by Flibotte et al. (1993) and later in 194Hg by Cederwall et al. (1994) and 153Dy by Asztalos et al. (1994). The study shows that the staggering persists even when the C4 symmetry is broken by a C2 term, suggesting that the four-fold symmetry need not be exact, and it enables further microscopic searches for hexadecapole correlations as the origin of the effect, noting that cranked-Nilsson calculations by Ingebretsen et al. (1994) found no evidence for strong four-fold symmetry breaking relative to the axial symmetry axis.
