# Detecting gravity modes in the solar $^8B$ neutrino flux

> The detection of gravity modes produced in the solar radiative zone has been a challenge in modern astrophysics for more than 30 yr and their amplitude in the core is not yet determined. In this Letter, we develop a new strategy to look for standing gravity modes through solar neutrino fluxes. We note that due to a resonance effect, the gravity modes of low degree and low order have the largest impact on the $^{8}B$ neutrino flux. The strongest effect is expected to occur for the dipole mode with radial order $2$, corresponding to periods of about 1.5 hr. These standing gravity waves produce temperature fluctuations that are amplified by a factor of 170 in the boron neutrino flux for the corresponding period, in consonance with the gravity modes. From current neutrino observations, we determine that the maximum temperature variation due to the gravity modes in the Sun's core is smaller than $5.8\times 10^{-4}$. This study clearly shows that due to their high sensitivity to the temperature, the $^8B$ neutrino flux time series is an excellent tool to determine the properties of gravity modes in the solar core. Moreover, if gravity mode footprints are discovered in the $^{8}B$ neutrino flux, this opens a new line of research to probe the physics of the solar core as non-standing gravity waves of higher periods cannot be directly detected by helioseismology but could leave their signature on boron neutrino or on other neutrino fluxes.

## Key claim

The authors propose that standing gravity modes (g-modes) of low degree and low order produce temperature fluctuations in the solar core that are amplified by a factor of up to 170 in the \({}^{8}\)B neutrino flux due to a resonance effect, making neutrino time series a viable probe for g-modes that have remained undetected by helioseismology for over 30 years. The claim is conditional on the existence of such modes and on the assumption that the temperature perturbation amplitude is at least \(10^{-5}\) to be detectable by next-generation neutrino detectors.

## Method

The authors computed g-mode eigenfrequencies and temperature eigenfunctions for a solar standard model (SSM) using the CESAM stellar evolution code and the adipls pulsation code, with a refined grid in the central 5% of the solar radius to properly resolve core boundary conditions. They then constructed a "gravito-neutrino eigenfunction" \(\Psi_{\ell n}\) by integrating the product of the temperature eigenfunction, the \({}^{8}\)B neutrino production profile, and a temperature-sensitivity factor \(\zeta = 25.5\) (derived from the \(\mu=24\) power-law dependence of the \({}^{8}\)B flux on central temperature). The fractional neutrino flux variation for each mode was computed as \(|\Delta\phi/\phi|_{\ell n,\text{max}} = 25.5\, |\mathcal{A}|_{\ell n}\, |\mathcal{B}|_{\ell n}\), where \(\mathcal{A}_{\ell n}\) is the maximum fractional temperature perturbation and \(\mathcal{B}_{\ell n}\) is the spatial overlap integral between the eigenfunction and the neutrino production region.

## Key numbers

* The dipole mode with radial order \(n=-2\) has a period of about 1.5 hours (frequencies 185.5 \(\mu\)Hz for SSM, 191.1 \(\mu\)Hz for SeSM).
* For a temperature fluctuation of \(|\delta T/T| = 10^{-3}\), the dipole \(g_{-2}\) mode produces a fractional \({}^{8}\)B neutrino flux variation of 17.0% (amplification factor of 170 relative to the temperature fluctuation).
* The overlap integral \(\mathcal{B}_{1,-2}\) for the dipole \(g_{-2}\) mode is 6.7 (dimensionless).
* From the SNO upper limit of 10% variability in the \({}^{8}\)B neutrino flux, the maximum temperature fluctuation in the solar core due to g-modes is constrained to \(|\delta T/T| \le 5.8 \times 10^{-4}\).
* The dipole \(g_{-1}\) mode (period 1.1 hours) gives a flux variation of 5.8%; the dipole \(g_{-3}\) mode (period 1.9 hours) gives 12.2%.
* Quadrupole (\(\ell=2\)) modes produce flux variations between 2.6% and 4.3% for the same \(10^{-3}\) temperature fluctuation.
* The authors estimate core temperature perturbations lie between \(10^{-5}\) and \(10^{-3}\), with next-generation detectors (SNO+, Hyper-Kamiokande) expected to improve statistics by a factor of 25.

## Context

This work extends the search for solar g-modes beyond traditional helioseismology, which has been limited by the extremely low surface velocity amplitudes of these modes (estimated at a few mm s\({}^{-1}\) by Belkacem et al. 2009 and detected only tentatively by the GOLF instrument on SOHO, as reported by Garcia et al. 2007). The authors build on earlier calculations by Lopes (2013) of acoustic and f-mode impacts on the \({}^{8}\)B neutrino flux, and on the temperature sensitivity scaling established by Bahcall and Ulrich (1996) and Turck-Chieze et al. (1993). The proposed method enables the detection of non-standing gravity waves with periods longer than those accessible to helioseismology, and could be extended to other neutrino fluxes (e.g., pp neutrinos) as statistics improve with detectors like SNO+ and Hyper-Kamiokande.
