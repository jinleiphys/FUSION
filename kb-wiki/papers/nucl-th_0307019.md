# Specific heat at constant volume in the thermodynamic model

> A thermodynamic model for multifragmentation which is frequently used appears to give very different values for specific heat at constant volume depending upon whether canonical or grand canonical ensemble is used. The cause for this discrepancy is analysed.

## Key claim

The thermodynamic model for multifragmentation yields very different values for specific heat at constant volume depending on whether the canonical or grand canonical ensemble is used; the discrepancy arises from large fluctuations in particle number in the grand canonical ensemble, which smear out the sharp peak seen in the canonical calculation.

## Method

The authors compare canonical and grand canonical calculations of specific heat per particle for a one-component system of nucleons at fixed freeze-out density rho = rho_0/2.7, using the same binding energy and level density parameters as in the original formulation of Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998). They compute the canonical partition function numerically for A = 200 and A = 2000 particles, and solve the grand canonical equations with the largest cluster size k_m set equal to the system size (200 or 2000). Fluctuations in particle number are evaluated both from the grand canonical partition function and from the known canonical partition functions via the relation Q_gr.can = sum_K exp(beta mu K) Q_K,k_m.

## Key numbers

* For A = 2000 at rho = rho_0/2.7, the canonical specific heat shows a much sharper peak than the grand canonical result; the peak height rises and width decreases dramatically when going from A = 200 to A = 2000 in the canonical model.
* At temperature T = 7.7 MeV, the grand canonical particle number distribution is narrow and centered near K = 2000; at T = 7.3 MeV the distribution is wide and does not maximize at K = 2000, and at T = 2.0 MeV the probability of having zero particles exceeds that of having K = 2000.
* The periodicity in the probability distribution at low temperature has period 2000, linked to the largest cluster size; at T = 2 MeV with K = 8000 nucleons the system forms essentially 4 clusters of size 2000.

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic model of multifragmentation developed by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) and later studied in the grand canonical ensemble by Bugaev, Gorenstein, Mishustin, and Greiner (2000). It resolves a puzzle about the discrepancy between canonical and grand canonical specific heat predictions, showing that the grand canonical ensemble introduces large particle-number fluctuations that wash out the sharp phase-transition signal. The authors note that a recent work by Das, Das Gupta, Liu, and Tsang (2001) demonstrates that interpreting data using the grand canonical ensemble can lead to serious errors in temperature estimation, and the present analysis reinforces the need for caution when applying the grand canonical ensemble to finite nuclear systems.
