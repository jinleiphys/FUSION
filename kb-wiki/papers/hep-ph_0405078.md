# Double Beta Decay

> We review recent developments in double-beta decay, focusing on what can be learned about the three light neutrinos in future experiments. We examine the effects of uncertainties in already measured neutrino parameters and in calculated nuclear matrix elements on the interpretation of upcoming double-beta decay measurements. We then review a number of proposed experiments.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how upcoming double-beta decay experiments can probe the absolute neutrino mass scale and determine whether neutrinos are Majorana particles, but emphasizes that uncertainties in nuclear matrix elements (contributing a factor of 2-3 uncertainty in the extracted effective Majorana mass) currently dwarf uncertainties from oscillation parameters and will be the primary limitation on quantitative conclusions about neutrino masses and hierarchies.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical formalism for neutrinoless double-beta (0νββ) decay mediated by light Majorana neutrinos, deriving the relationship between the decay half-life and the effective Majorana mass ⟨mββ⟩. They then propagate uncertainties from measured neutrino oscillation parameters (solar and atmospheric mass-squared differences, mixing angles θ12, θ23, and θ13) through to the allowed ranges of ⟨mββ⟩ for normal and inverted mass hierarchies, and compare the sensitivity of 0νββ decay to other mass measurements from tritium beta decay and cosmology.

## Key numbers

- Atmospheric mass-squared difference: |δm²_atm| = 2.0^{+1.0}_{-0.7} × 10⁻³ eV² (90% CL)
- Solar mass-squared difference: δm²_sol = 7.1^{+1.2}_{-0.6} × 10⁻⁵ eV² (68% CL)
- Solar mixing angle: θ_sol = 32.5^{+2.4}_{-2.3} degrees (68% CL)
- Reactor limit on θ13: < 9 degrees (68% CL)
- Upper limit on effective electron neutrino mass from tritium beta decay: ⟨m_β⟩ ≤ 2200 meV (95% CL)
- For normal hierarchy with m₁ near zero: ⟨m_ββ⟩^Nor_max ≈ 4 meV (using extremum oscillation parameters)
- For inverted hierarchy with m₃ lightest: ⟨m_ββ⟩^Inv_min ≈ 12 meV (using extremum oscillation parameters)
- Cosmological limit on sum of neutrino masses Σ: < 0.69 eV to < 1.7 eV (95% CL, depending on dataset combination)

## Context

This review extends earlier work by Elliott and Vogel (2002) on the impact of neutrino discoveries on double-beta decay, incorporating new results from KamLAND and WMAP. It builds on comprehensive theoretical treatments by Primakoff and Rosen (1959), Haxton and Stephenson (1984), Doi et al. (1985), Tomoda (1991), Suhonen and Civitarese (1998), Vergados (2000), Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (2000), and Faessler and Simkovic (1998). The paper enables future experimental programs to interpret 0νββ decay measurements in terms of neutrino mass hierarchy and Majorana phases, while identifying that improved nuclear matrix element calculations are the critical prerequisite for quantitative conclusions.
