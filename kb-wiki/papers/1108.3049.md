# Weakly and strongly coupled degrees of freedom in the quark-gluon plasma

> Talk presented at the workshop "Excited QCD 2011", Les Houches, France, Feb. 20-25, 2011. 6 pages v2: reference added

## Key claim

The strongly coupled character of the quark-gluon plasma is not due to a large coupling constant, but rather arises from the interplay of degrees of freedom with different wavelengths, where long-wavelength modes remain strongly coupled even for arbitrarily small coupling while short-wavelength modes that dominate thermodynamics may be weakly coupled.

## Method

The paper analyzes the breakdown of thermal perturbation theory in QCD by examining the expansion parameter γ_κ = g²⟨A²⟩_κ/κ² at different momentum scales (hard scale κ∼T, soft scale κ∼gT, and ultra-soft scale κ∼g²T). It compares this behavior with zero-temperature perturbation theory and reviews evidence from lattice QCD calculations, weak-coupling resummations, and the color glass condensate formalism for initial stages of heavy ion collisions.

## Key numbers

- The running coupling at the GeV scale is α_s ≈ 0.3 to 0.4
- Weak-coupling resummations reproduce lattice results for temperatures greater than 2.5 to 3 T_c
- The expansion parameter at the hard scale (κ∼T) is γ_T ∼ g², comparable to zero-temperature perturbation theory
- The expansion parameter at the soft scale (κ∼gT) is γ_{gT} ∼ g, giving an expansion in powers of g rather than g²
- The expansion parameter at the ultra-soft scale (κ∼g²T) is γ_{g²T} ∼ 1, indicating complete breakdown of perturbation theory
- The saturation momentum scales as Q_s² ∼ x^{-0.3} with decreasing x and Q_s² ∝ α_s A^{1/3} for nuclei with A nucleons

## Context

This talk extends the analysis of thermal QCD perturbation theory previously developed by Blaizot (2000) and Andersen (2010), and contrasts with the strong coupling expansion methods based on the AdS/CFT correspondence (Gubser, 2009). The work connects the non-perturbative behavior of long-wavelength magnetic fluctuations in the high-temperature plasma to the saturation regime in the color glass condensate formalism (Iancu, 2003), suggesting that both phenomena arise from the cooperation of many degrees of freedom rather than large coupling.
