# Realistic evaluation of Coulomb potential in spherical nuclei and test of the traditional approach

> A realistic evaluation of Coulomb potential has been made for some selected nuclei using the available model-independent data for the charge density and the recent development of Coulomb energy-density functional. Within the Woods-Saxon potential as a nuclear component, we are able to quantify the differences in proton single-particle energies due to the differences from the model-independent data of the uniform distribution, the two-parameter Fermi function, as well as the charge density obtained from a microscopic Hartree-Fock calculation using the effective Skyrme interaction. The obtained energy differences are generally small in magnitude, namely about 100~keV or less if the parameters of the charge density models are appropriately determined. Considerable larger differences appear when the last occupied state is highly filled and, at the same time, has a small orbital angular momentum. Sulfur isotopes ($Z=16$) are a perfect example of these nuclei. Unfortunately, despite its simplicity, the uniform distribution cannot be used for evaluating the Coulomb exchange term within a well-established method because it is not differentiable at the surface of a nucleus. Traditionally, the missing of exchange term is corrected for by simply excluding the last-proton contribution to the direct term. %% We also investigate this approach and find that its effect is simply an introduction of the factor $(Z-1)/Z$ into the Coulomb direct term. From medium to heavy nuclei (typically beyond the $sd$ shell) the resulting proton levels are 300-800~keV higher than those obtained with the exact Fock term. The result for lighter nuclei tends to be opposite because the factor $(Z-1)/Z$ decreases rapidly towards the limit of $Z\to 1$. Therefore, this traditional approach should be avoided for a precision nuclear structure calculation.

## Key claim

The traditional approach of correcting for the missing Coulomb exchange term by simply excluding the last-proton contribution to the direct term (introducing a factor (Z-1)/Z) produces proton single-particle energies that are 300-800 keV higher than those obtained with the exact Fock term for medium to heavy nuclei, and should be avoided for precision nuclear structure calculations.

## Method

The authors evaluated the Coulomb potential for spherical nuclei using model-independent charge density data from electron scattering (Fourier-Bessel and sum-of-Gaussians analyses) and the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) for the Coulomb exchange term. They compared proton single-particle energies obtained with the Woods-Saxon potential using the uniform charge distribution, the two-parameter Fermi function, and microscopic Skyrme Hartree-Fock charge densities against the model-independent reference, and separately tested the traditional (Z-1)/Z correction against the exact Fock exchange term.

## Key numbers

- Energy differences between charge density models are generally about 100 keV or less when parameters are appropriately determined
- For medium to heavy nuclei (beyond the sd shell), the traditional (Z-1)/Z approach yields proton levels 300-800 keV higher than those from the exact Fock term
- Radial mismatch factors Lambda between Fourier-Bessel and sum-of-Gaussians data range from 3.441e-3 percent (O-16) to 0.209 percent (S-32)
- The uniform distribution cannot be used for evaluating the Coulomb exchange term because it is not differentiable at the nuclear surface

## Context

This work extends the development of Coulomb energy-density functionals by Naito et al. (2019) and the model-independent charge density compilation by De Vries et al. (1987). It quantifies the systematic errors in the traditional uniform-sphere Coulomb potential with (Z-1) correction used in Woods-Saxon calculations (Schwierz et al., 2007) and demonstrates that this approximation is inadequate for precision nuclear structure calculations, particularly for superallowed beta decay studies where realistic radial wave functions are required (Townsend and Hardy, 2008; Xayavong and Nakatsukasa, 2018).
