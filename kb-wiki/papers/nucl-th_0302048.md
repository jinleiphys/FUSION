# Can Modern Nuclear Hamiltonians Tolerate a Bound Tetraneutron?

> I show that it does not seem possible to change modern nuclear Hamiltonians to bind a tetraneutron without destroying many other successful predictions of those Hamiltonians. This means that, should a recent experimental claim of a bound tetraneutron be confirmed, our understanding of nuclear forces will have to be significantly changed. I also point out some errors in previous theoretical studies of this problem.

# Can Modern Nuclear Hamiltonians Tolerate a Bound Tetraneutron?

## Key claim
It does not seem possible to modify modern realistic nuclear Hamiltonians to bind a tetraneutron (⁴n) without destroying many other successful predictions for nuclei up to A=10. If the experimental claim of a bound tetraneutron is confirmed, our understanding of nuclear forces would have to be significantly changed, but the paper shows that simple modifications to existing Hamiltonians are not viable.

## Method
The author used Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) calculations with the Argonne v₁₈ two-nucleon potential plus the Illinois-2 three-nucleon potential (AV18/IL2), which reproduces energies for A=3–10 nuclei with rms errors of 0.6–1.0 MeV. Various modifications to the Hamiltonian were tested: changing the ¹S₀ partial wave of the NN potential, adding a T=3/2 three-nucleon potential, and adding a T=2 four-nucleon potential, each adjusted to produce a bound tetraneutron at approximately -0.5 MeV. The consequences of each modification were then computed for other light nuclei and neutron systems.

## Key numbers
- The unmodified AV18/IL2 Hamiltonian produces no bound tetraneutron; GFMC calculations with no external well give positive energies that steadily decrease as the rms radius increases.
- Extrapolation of Woods-Saxon well calculations to zero well depth gives a tetraneutron energy of +2 MeV, suggesting a possible broad resonance near 2 MeV.
- Increasing the two-pion-range part of the ¹S₀ partial wave by 4.9% gives a tetraneutron energy of -0.87(3) MeV, but also produces a bound dineutron at -0.88 MeV and changes ¹S₀ phase shifts by 12° over a large energy range.
- Using the AV1′ potential in the ¹S₀ partial wave gives a tetraneutron energy of -0.52 MeV and a dineutron energy of -0.42 MeV, with rms radii of 10.3 fm for ⁴n and 2.8 fm for ²n.
- The T=3/2 three-nucleon potential requires V₃ = -440 MeV to produce a tetraneutron at -0.60(5) MeV, with an rms radius of 1.88 fm and kinetic energy expectation value of ~87 MeV.
- The T=2 four-nucleon potential requires V₄ = -4750 MeV to produce a tetraneutron at -0.55(6) MeV, with an rms radius of 1.61 fm.
- With the T=3/2 three-nucleon potential, pure neutron systems with A=5, 6, 8 have energies of -62, -220, and -650 MeV respectively; with the T=2 four-nucleon potential, these energies are -358, -1370, and -6690 MeV.
- The Volkov potentials (non-realistic) do bind tetraneutrons with energies from -0.47 to -1.04 MeV and rms radii of about 11.5 fm, but also bind dineutrons with energies comparable to their deuteron binding energies.

## Context
This work extends previous theoretical studies by Timofeyuk (2002), Bertulani, and Timofeyuk (2003) that used simplified Hamiltonians and approximate solutions to conclude that nuclear potentials do not bind four neutrons. The paper corrects errors in those studies by using modern realistic Hamiltonians (AV18/IL2) that successfully describe nuclei up to A=10, and accurate GFMC calculations. The results contradict the experimental claim of a bound tetraneutron cluster (2002) and show that the Volkov potentials, which were previously thought not to bind ⁴n, actually do bind it due to their bound dineutrons and the diffuse nature of the tetraneutron state. The paper demonstrates that any modification to bind a tetraneutron produces unacceptable changes to other nuclear systems, including bound dineutrons, overbinding of ³H by ~50%, and catastrophic binding of pure neutron systems with A>4.
