# Strangeness in Astrophysics and Cosmology

> Some recent developments concerning the role of strange quark matter for astrophysical systems and the QCD phase transition in the early universe are addressed. Causality constraints of the soft nuclear equation of state as extracted from subthreshold kaon production in heavy-ion collisions are used to derive an upper mass limit for compact stars. The interplay between the viscosity of strange quark matter and the gravitational wave emission from rotation-powered pulsars are outlined. The flux of strange quark matter nuggets in cosmic rays is put in perspective with a detailed numerical investigation of the merger of two strange stars. Finally, we discuss a novel scenario for the QCD phase transition in the early universe, which allows for a small inflationary period due to a pronounced first order phase transition at large baryochemical potential.

# Strangeness in Astrophysics and Cosmology

## Key claim
The paper argues that a first-order QCD phase transition in the early universe, combined with a small inflationary period of about seven e-folds, can reconcile a large initial baryochemical potential (μ/T ∼ 1) with the observed baryon-to-photon ratio of 10⁻⁹, provided that the transition occurs at high baryochemical potential rather than the standard crossover at μ ∼ 0.

## Method
The authors derive an upper mass limit for neutron stars by combining causality constraints with a soft nuclear equation of state extracted from subthreshold kaon production data in heavy-ion collisions (KaoS collaboration). They analyze the r-mode instability in rotating neutron stars by comparing the viscosity of strange quark matter in different color-superconducting phases (2SC and CFL) against observational data from low-mass X-ray binaries. They perform 3D relativistic smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulations of strange star mergers for two bag constants (B = 60 and 80 MeV/fm³) to estimate ejected strangelet masses. Finally, they develop a cosmological scenario where a first-order QCD phase transition at large baryochemical potential produces a small inflationary period, and calculate the resulting gravitational wave spectrum.

## Key numbers
- Maximum neutron star mass limit: about 2.7 solar masses, derived from a fiducial density of ε_f = 2ε₀ (where ε₀ is normal nuclear matter energy density) using the scaling relation M_max = 4.2 M_⊙ (ε₀/ε_f)^{1/2}
- Pulsar PSR J1903+0327 mass: M = (1.67 ± 0.11) M_⊙ from Shapiro delay parameters alone, and M = (1.67 ± 0.01) M_⊙ when combined with periastron advance
- Ejected strangelet mass from strange star mergers: (1.4–2.8) × 10⁻⁴ M_⊙ for bag constant B = 60 MeV/fm³, and zero for B = 80 MeV/fm³
- QCD phase transition temperature: T ≈ 170 MeV, occurring at time t ≈ 10⁻⁵ seconds after the big bang
- Peak gravitational wave frequency from first-order QCD phase transition: ν_peak ∼ 10⁻⁷ Hz
- Maximum gravitational wave amplitude: h ∼ 10⁻¹²
- Number of e-folds needed for little inflation: N ∼ 7 (compared to N ∼ 50 for standard inflation)
- Mass scale for large-scale structure modification: M ∼ 10⁹ M_⊙ (dwarf galaxy scale), compared to horizon mass ∼ 10⁻⁹ M_⊙ without QCD inflation

## Context
This conference proceedings paper extends prior work on strange quark matter in astrophysics, including the Bodmer-Witten hypothesis for absolutely stable strange quark matter, and builds on earlier maximum mass calculations by Rhoades and Ruffini (1974) and Kalogera and Baym (1996). The kaon production analysis relies on the KaoS collaboration data (Sturm et al., 2000; Forster et al., 2007) and Skyrme parametrization studies by Fuchs et al. (2000) and Hartnack et al. (2005). The r-mode instability analysis extends work by Andersson (1997) and Madsen (1999), and the strange star merger simulations follow Bauswein et al. (2008). The cosmological little inflation scenario is a novel proposal that enables observable gravitational wave signals detectable by the Parkes Pulsar Timing Array and future Square Kilometre Array, and could explain primordial magnetic fields within the standard model, as calculated by Caprini et al. (2009).
