# Physics-informed neural networks for angular-momentum conservation in computational relativistic spin hydrodynamics

> Theoretical developments in relativistic spin hydrodynamics, which describes the macroscopic transport of spin angular momentum alongside other fundamental conserved quantities, have progressed rapidly since the experimental observation of the global spin polarization of $Λ$ hyperons in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments. However, numerical simulations of relativistic spin hydrodynamics remain largely unaddressed due to computational challenges, particularly the accurate numerical conservation of total angular momentum. In this work, we propose the use of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) for computational relativistic spin hydrodynamics. As a concrete application, we consider a rotating fluid confined within a cylindrical container. We show that angular-momentum conservation can be accurately achieved in the PINNs-based numerical framework. Furthermore, we investigate the spin-orbit conversion induced by the rotational viscous effect, which is the intrinsic dissipative process of relativistic spin hydrodynamics. Our analysis numerically identifies the mismatch between the transverse thermal vorticity and the spin potential as the driving mechanism of the spin-orbit conversion.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) can accurately enforce total angular momentum conservation in relativistic spin hydrodynamics simulations, a challenge for conventional finite-volume methods. The authors numerically identify the mismatch between transverse thermal vorticity and spin potential as the driving mechanism for spin-orbit conversion, but only for a simplified (1+1)-dimensional rotating fluid in a cylindrical container with translational and rotational symmetry assumptions.

## Method
The authors solve second-order relativistic spin hydrodynamics equations using a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with three hidden layers of 250 units each, trained via the Adam optimizer. The loss function incorporates terms for the hydrodynamic equations, boundary conditions, and both local and global angular momentum conservation. The system is reduced to (1+1)-dimensional evolution in time and radial coordinate by assuming translational invariance along the cylinder axis and rotational symmetry, applied to a rotating fluid confined within a cylindrical container of radius R.

## Key numbers
- Rotational viscosity values tested: γ = 0 and γ = 2 (normalized units)
- Initial velocity amplitude for Initial Condition F: u^θ = 0.2 sin^4(πr) (normalized by cylinder radius R)
- Initial spin polarization amplitude for Initial Condition S: S^z = 0.2 sin^4(πr) (normalized by cylinder radius R)
- Neural network architecture: 3 hidden layers with 250 units each, batch size of 150,000 training points
- Learning rate range: 10^{-3} to 10^{-5} (Adam optimizer)
- Cylinder radius: R = 1 (normalized units)
- Global equilibrium state: e = 1 with all other fields vanishing

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of relativistic spin hydrodynamics developed following the experimental observation of Λ hyperon spin polarization by STAR (2017) and ALICE (2019). It addresses the computational gap noted by Singh (2024) and Sapna (2025), who performed scarce numerical simulations but did not incorporate spin-orbit conversion. The PINNs approach, introduced by Raissi (2019), offers an alternative to conventional finite-volume methods that struggle with angular momentum conservation. This work enables future simulations of spin-orbit conversion in heavy-ion collisions and validates the Barnett and Einstein–de Haas effects in relativistic fluid dynamics.
