# Cluster emission and phase transition behaviours in nuclear disassembly

> The features of the emissions of light particles (LP), charged particles (CP), intermediate mass fragments (IMF) and the largest fragment (MAX) are investigated for $^{129}Xe$ as functions of temperature and 'freeze-out' density in the frameworks of the isospin-dependent lattice gas model and the classical molecular dynamics model. Definite turning points for the slopes of average multiplicity of LP, CP and IMF, and of the mean mass of the largest fragment ($A_{max}$) are shown around a liquid-gas phase transition temperature and while the largest variances of the distributions of LP, CP, IMF and MAX appear there. It indicates that the cluster emission rate can be taken as a probe of nuclear liquid--gas phase transition. Furthermore, the largest fluctuation is simultaneously accompanied at the point of the phase transition as can be noted by investigating both the variances of their cluster multiplicity or mass distributions and the Campi scatter plots within the lattice gas model and the molecular dynamics model, which is consistent with the result of the traditional thermodynamical theory when a phase transition occurs.

## Key claim

The slopes of average multiplicities of light particles, charged particles, and intermediate mass fragments, as well as the mean mass of the largest fragment, show definite turning points around the liquid-gas phase transition temperature for a finite nuclear system; the cluster emission rate can therefore serve as a probe of the phase transition, but this behavior is conditional on the chosen 'freeze-out' density and the inclusion or neglect of the Coulomb interaction.

## Method

The study used the isospin-dependent lattice gas model (I-LGM) and the isospin-dependent classical molecular dynamics (I-CMD) model to simulate the disassembly of 129Xe at various temperatures (1.5–7 MeV) and 'freeze-out' densities (0.097ρ₀, 0.18ρ₀, 0.38ρ₀, and 0.60ρ₀). In I-LGM, nucleons were placed on a cubic lattice with Metropolis sampling to achieve thermal equilibrium, then cluster distributions were determined using a kinetic-energy criterion. In I-CMD, the same initial configurations from I-LGM were evolved in time using isospin-dependent potentials, with and without Coulomb interaction, and clusters were identified at asymptotic times.

## Key numbers

- Phase transition temperatures extracted from slope extrema in I-LGM: 3.5 MeV at ρ_f = 0.097ρ₀, 4 MeV at 0.18ρ₀, 5 MeV at 0.38ρ₀, and 6 MeV at 0.60ρ₀.
- Phase transition temperatures extracted from slope extrema in I-CMD with Coulomb: around 3 MeV at ρ_f = 0.097ρ₀, 3.5 MeV at 0.18ρ₀, 4.5 MeV at 0.38ρ₀, and 5 MeV at 0.60ρ₀.
- For I-CMD at fixed ρ_f = 0.38ρ₀, the turning temperature was about 4 MeV with Coulomb and about 5 MeV without Coulomb; for I-LGM at the same density it was about 5.5 MeV.
- The interaction constant for unlike nucleons in I-LGM was ε_pn = -5.33 MeV; like-nucleon interactions were set to 0 MeV.
- The normal nucleon density was ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³, with each lattice site volume 6.25 fm³.

## Context

This work extends previous lattice gas model studies by Pan and Das Gupta (2000) and others that identified phase transitions via the effective power-law parameter τ, second moment S₂, and specific heat. It also builds on the author's prior work using multiplicity information entropy and nuclear Zipf's law (Ma, 2001) as phase transition diagnostics. The results enable experimental searches for nuclear liquid-gas phase transition signals by measuring excitation functions of cluster multiplicities, their slopes, and variances in heavy-ion collisions, and they clarify the role of Coulomb repulsion in shifting the apparent transition temperature.
