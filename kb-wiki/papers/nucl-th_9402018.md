# Hydrodynamical assessment of 200 AGeV collisions

> We are analyzing the hydrodynamics of 200 A GeV S+S collisions using a new approach which tries to quantify the uncertainties arising from the specific implementation of the hydrodynamical model. Based on a previous phenomenological analysis we use the global hydrodynamics model to show that the amount of initial flow, or initial energy density, cannot be determined from the hadronic momentum spectra. We additionally find that almost always a sizeable transverse flow deve- lops, which causes the system to freeze out, thereby limiting the flow velocity in itself. This freeze-out dominance in turn makes a distinction between a plasma and a hadron resonance gas equation of state very difficult, whereas a pure pion gas can easily be ruled out from present data. To complete the picture we also analyze particle multiplicity data, which suggest that chemical equilibrium is not reached with respect to the strange particles. However, the over- population of pions seems to be at most moderate, with a pion chemical potential far away from the Bose divergence.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, for 200 A GeV S+S collisions, the initial energy density and initial longitudinal flow cannot be determined from hadronic momentum spectra alone, and that the transverse flow at freeze-out is almost always sizeable (around 0.5 c) and self-limiting, making it very difficult to distinguish between a quark-gluon plasma and a hadron resonance gas equation of state using only hadronic observables.

## Method

The authors used a global hydrodynamics model, which couples longitudinal and transverse expansion with a dynamical freeze-out criterion based on the competition between the expansion time scale and the scattering time scale. They varied the initial longitudinal flow rapidity (from complete stopping to partial transparency) and the initial energy density, requiring that the final longitudinal flow rapidity match the experimentally extracted value of 1.7. The resulting freeze-out temperatures and transverse flow velocities were then compared to the correlation extracted from the transverse mass spectra of pions.

## Key numbers

*   The longitudinal flow rapidity at freeze-out is extracted as \(\eta_{\rm max} = 1.7 \pm 0.2\) from the rapidity spectra of all produced particles.
*   For the L-scenario (complete stopping, \(\eta_0=0\)), the initial energy density required is \(\varepsilon_0 > 10 \,\rm GeV/fm^3\).
*   For the L-scenario, the freeze-out temperature is \(T_f = 167 \,\rm MeV\) and the transverse flow is \(\beta_{s,f} = 0.58\,c\); for the B-scenario (\(\eta_0=1.3\)), the freeze-out temperature is \(T_f = 157 \,\rm MeV\) and the transverse flow is \(\beta_{s,f} = 0.50\,c\).
*   The transverse flow at freeze-out is consistently near \(\beta_s \approx 0.5\,c\) for a wide range of initial conditions (\(0\le \eta_0 \le 1.6\)).
*   The freeze-out temperature is always close to \(150 \,\rm MeV\).
*   The initial energy loss of projectile protons is given as \(E_{\rm loss}=313\,\rm GeV\).

## Context

This work extends a previous phenomenological analysis (Schnedermann and Heinz, 1993) by using global hydrodynamics to quantify uncertainties in the initial conditions. It contradicts the hope that transverse flow alone could serve as a clear signature for a quark-gluon plasma phase transition, as suggested by van Hove (1982). The paper enables future studies to focus on other observables, such as lepton pairs, to determine the initial energy density, and it confirms that a pure pion gas equation of state can be ruled out, as previously shown by Gersdorff et al. (1986).
