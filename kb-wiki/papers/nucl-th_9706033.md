# The importance of initial-final state correlations for the formation of fragments in heavy ion collisions

> Using quantum molecular dynamics simulations, we investigate the formation of fragments in symmetric reactions between beam energies of E=30AMeV and 600AMeV. After a comparison with existing data we investigate some observables relevant to tackle equilibration: dsigma/dErat, the double differential cross section dsigma/pt.dpz.dpt,... Apart maybe from very energetic E>400AMeV and very central reactions, none of our simulations gives evidence that the system passes through a state of equilibrium. Later, we address the production mechanisms and find that, whatever the energy, nucleons finally entrained in a fragment exhibit strong initial-final state correlations, in coordinate as well as in momentum space. At high energy those correlations resemble the ones obtained in the participant-spectator model. At low energy the correlations are equally strong, but more complicated; they are a consequence of the Pauli blocking of the nucleon-nucleon collisions, the geometry, and the excitation energy. Studying a second set of time-dependent variables (radii, densities,...), we investigate in details how those correlations survive the reaction especially in central reactions where the nucleons have to pass through the whole system. It appears that some fragments are made of nucleons which were initially correlated, whereas others are formed by nucleons scattered during the reaction into the vicinity of a group of previously correlated nucleons.

## Key claim

Using quantum molecular dynamics simulations, the authors find that for symmetric heavy-ion reactions between 30A MeV and 600A MeV, the system does not pass through a state of global equilibrium except possibly for very energetic (E ≥ 400A MeV) and very central reactions; instead, nucleons that end up in fragments exhibit strong initial-final state correlations in both coordinate and momentum space at all energies studied.

## Method

The authors employed the quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) model, which follows the time evolution of the full multi-nucleon phase space distribution from the initial collision through fragment formation, using a variational principle with coherent states as test wave functions. They analyzed symmetric reactions (Au+Au and Xe+Sn) at beam energies from 30A MeV to 600A MeV, tracking the centroids of nucleon wave functions to study time correlations and prefragment evolution. For each fragment, they computed the center-of-mass trajectories of projectile-origin and target-origin nucleons separately, averaging over many events to identify correlations between initial positions/momenta and final fragment classification (projectile-like, target-like, or mid-rapidity fragments).

## Key numbers

- Beam energy range studied: 30A MeV to 600A MeV for symmetric reactions
- Threshold for possible equilibration: E ≥ 400A MeV for very central reactions only
- Impact parameter definitions: b< = 3 fm for all systems; b> = 6 fm for Xe+Sn and 8 fm for Au+Au
- Heavy fragment (HF) definition: any fragment with mass A ≥ 5
- Light fragment (LF) definition: any fragment with mass 2 ≤ A ≤ 4
- Intermediate mass fragment (IMF) definition: objects with 3 ≤ Z ≤ 25
- At 400A MeV and 3 fm impact parameter, projectile nucleons emerging in projectile-like fragments had initial mean transverse momentum Px ≈ 20 MeV/c
- For Xe+Sn at 50A MeV and 3 fm impact parameter, projectile-like fragment nucleons traversed the entire target nucleus rather than rotating around it
- At 150A MeV, equilibration (Erat = 2) was achieved only in rare events
- At 32 and 50A MeV for Xe+Sn, Erat values larger than 1.5 were almost never obtained

## Context

This work extends the quantum molecular dynamics approach of Aichelin (1991) to systematically investigate whether heavy-ion collisions reach thermal equilibrium before fragmenting, contradicting the assumptions of statistical models such as those by Gross (1990), Bondorf (1995), and Stokstad (1993) which presume equilibrium is reached. The finding of strong initial-final state correlations at all energies provides a mechanism explaining why single-arm experiments show thermal-like spectra while multi-detector measurements reveal angle-dependent source properties, as observed by Warwick (1983) and the FOPI collaboration. The results enable future work to distinguish between fragments formed from initially correlated nucleons versus those assembled from nucleons scattered into the vicinity of correlated groups during the reaction.
