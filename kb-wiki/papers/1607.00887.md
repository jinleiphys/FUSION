# Quasidynamical symmetries in the backbending of chromium isotopes

> Background: Symmetries are a powerful way to characterize nuclear wave functions. A true dynamical symmetry, where the Hamiltonian is block-diagonal in subspaces defined by the group, is rare. More likely is a quasidynamical symmetry: states with different quantum numbers (i.e. angular momentum) nonetheless sharing similar group-theoretical decompositions. Purpose: We use group-theoretical decomposition to investigate backbending, an abrupt change in the moment of inertia along the yrast line, in $^{48,49,50}$Cr: prior mean-field calculations of these nuclides suggest a change from strongly prolate to more spherical configurations as one crosses the backbending and increases in angular momentum. Methods: We decompose configuration-interaction shell-model wavefunctions using the SU(2) groups $L$ (total orbital angular momentum) and $S$ (total spin), and the groups SU(3) and SU(4). We do not need a special basis but only matrix elements of Casimir operators, applied with a modified Lanczos algorithm. Results: We find quasidynamical symmetries, albeit often of a different character above and below the backbending, for each group. While the strongest evolution was in SU(3), the decompositions did not suggest a decrease in deformation. We point out with a simple example that mean-field and SU(3) configurations may give very different pictures of deformation. Conclusions: Persistent quasidynamical symmetries for several groups allow us to identify the members of a band and to characterize how they evolve with increasing angular momentum, especially before and after backbending.

## Key claim

The authors find persistent quasidynamical symmetries in the yrast bands of $^{48,49,50}$Cr, but the character of these symmetries changes across the backbending region; contrary to prior mean-field interpretations, the SU(3) decompositions do not suggest a decrease in deformation after backbending, and the authors point out that mean-field and SU(3) configurations can give very different pictures of deformation.

## Method

The authors performed configuration-interaction shell-model calculations in the $1p$-$0f$ ($pf$) valence space using the GXPF1 effective interaction, diagonalizing the Hamiltonian via the Lanczos algorithm with the BIGSTICK code. They decomposed the resulting wavefunctions into subspaces labeled by eigenvalues of Casimir operators for the groups SU(2) (total orbital angular momentum $L$ and total spin $S$), SU(3), and SU(4), using a modified Lanczos algorithm that requires only matrix elements of the Casimir operators rather than a special basis.

## Key numbers

- The $M$-scheme basis dimensions for $^{48}$Cr, $^{49}$Cr, and $^{50}$Cr in the $1p$-$0f$ valence space are 2 million, 6 million, and 14.6 million, respectively.
- For $^{48}$Cr, the ground state is dominated by $L=1$, while states $I=2,4,6,\ldots$ have strength centering roughly around $L = I$ below the backbend; above the backbend at $I \approx 10$, the strength centers roughly around $L \approx I-2$.
- For $^{48}$Cr below the backbend, the $S$ decomposition is dominated by $S=1$, with some $S=0$ and $S=2$; after the backbend, $S=2$ dominates with $S=1,3$ subdominant.
- The maximum angular momentum for the $(0f_{7/2})^8$ configuration in $^{48}$Cr is $I=16$, and the $I=18$ state shows significantly different decompositions.
- For $^{49}$Cr, below the backbend the $L$-decompositions center around $L \approx I-1/2$, while in the upper sub-band they center around $L \approx I-3/2$.
- For $^{50}$Cr, backbending is seen around $I \approx 10$ with a second backbending around $I \approx 16$, the latter interpreted as the terminus of levels generated within the $(0f_{7/2})^{10}$ configuration.

## Context

This work extends prior configuration-interaction and mean-field studies of backbending in chromium isotopes, particularly the work of Caurier et al. (1995) who compared cranked Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations with full $pf$-shell diagonalization for $^{48}$Cr, and the projected configuration interaction calculations of PhysRevC.83.057303 which suggested that the lower sub-band of $^{48}$Cr corresponds to a well-defined intrinsic state but not the upper sub-band. The authors also build on the concept of quasidynamical symmetry developed by Rowe (2000) and others, demonstrating that group-theoretical decomposition can identify band members and characterize their evolution across backbending, even when true dynamical symmetries are broken by the spin-orbit and pairing components of the nuclear force.
