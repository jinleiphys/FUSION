# Searches for topological defect dark matter via non-gravitational signatures

> We propose schemes for the detection of topological defect dark matter using pulsars and other luminous extraterrestrial systems via non-gravitational signatures. The dark matter field, which makes up a defect, may interact with standard model particles, including quarks and the photon, resulting in the alteration of their masses. When a topological defect passes through a pulsar, its mass, radius and internal structure may be altered, resulting in a pulsar `quake'. A topological defect may also function as a cosmic dielectric material with a distinctive frequency-dependent index of refraction, which would give rise to the time delay of a periodic extraterrestrial light or radio signal, and the dispersion of a light or radio source in a manner distinct to a gravitational lens. A topological defect passing through Earth may give rise to temporary non-zero electric dipole moments for an electron, proton, neutron, nuclei and atoms. The biggest advantage of such astrophysical observations over recently proposed terrestrial detection methods is the much higher probability of a defect been found in the vast volumes of outer space compared with one passing through Earth itself.

## Key claim

Topological defect dark matter may be detectable through non-gravitational signatures in pulsars and other luminous extraterrestrial systems, with the key advantage that the probability of encountering a defect in the vast volumes of outer space is much higher than for a defect passing through Earth itself. The authors infer from existing pulsar glitch data that neutron mass variations inside defects are in the range δm_n/m_n ∼ 10⁻¹¹ – 10⁻⁵, but note this is an upper limit range since the cause of a pulsar glitch may be different.

## Method

The authors model the effect of a scalar dark matter field φ that couples quadratically to fermions (quarks, electrons, neutrons) and photons, altering their masses inside a topological defect. For pulsar interactions, they use a non-relativistic degenerate neutron gas model to estimate changes in pulsar mass, radius, and rotational frequency when a defect passes through the pulsar. For electromagnetic signatures, they derive the frequency-dependent index of refraction inside a defect from the photon mass term and calculate the resulting phase shift and time delay for signals passing through the defect along the line-of-sight between a pulsar and Earth.

## Key numbers

- Pulsar rotational periods range from T = 1.5 ms to 8.5 s
- Pulsar timing stabilities are of order ∼10⁻¹⁵, second only to terrestrial atomic clocks with stabilities ∼10⁻¹⁸
- Observed pulsar glitch frequency increases are in the range δω/ω = 10⁻¹¹ – 10⁻⁵, with recovery periods T_d = 1 day – 3 years
- From glitch data, inferred neutron mass variations inside defects are δm_n/m_n ∼ 10⁻¹¹ – 10⁻⁵
- Assuming ρ_TDM = ρ_CDM ≈ 7.6 × 10⁻⁴ eV⁴, v_TD ∼ 10⁻³ c, and τ ∼ 1 year, the parameter combination d/Λ_n² is in the range (10⁻²⁷ – 10⁻²¹) eV⁻³
- Combined with constraints Λ_n ≳ 10 TeV, defect transverse sizes satisfy d ≳ (10⁻⁷ – 10⁻¹) m
- For a spherical defect the size of Earth travelling at v ∼ 10⁻³ c, transit time through a pulsar is t ∼ 40 s
- Existing Cs hyperfine standards achieve ∼10⁻¹³ sensitivity and Rb standards ∼10⁻¹² on timescales of seconds

## Context

This work extends the terrestrial detection proposals of Pospelov (2013) (magnetometer networks for axion-like domain walls) and Derevianko (2013) (global atomic clock networks for defect detection) by proposing astrophysical observations with much higher encounter probabilities. The pulsar glitch mechanism is connected to the extensive literature on internally-driven glitch theories (Baym 1969, Anderson 1975, Alpar 1984, and many others), with the authors suggesting defect passage as an alternative or complementary explanation. The proposed non-gravitational lensing via a frequency-dependent refractive index is distinct from conventional gravitational lensing searches for defects, which are frequency-independent. The work enables searches using existing pulsar timing data, atomic clock networks, and global positioning system satellites, as well as concurrent electric dipole moment experiments for transient signals from axion-like pseudoscalar defects passing through Earth.
