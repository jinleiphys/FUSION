# Gamow-Teller strength distributions in Fe and Ni stable isotopes

> We study Gamow-Teller strength distributions in some selected nuclei of particular Astrophysical interest within the iron mass region. The theoretical framework is based on a proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation built on a deformed selfconsistent mean field basis obtained from two-body density-dependent Skyrme forces. We compare our results to available experimental information obtained from (n,p) and (p,n) charge exchange reactions.

## Key claim

The proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (pnQRPA) built on a deformed selfconsistent mean field from Skyrme forces can reproduce the main features of Gamow-Teller strength distributions in Fe and Ni stable isotopes with a quality comparable to large-scale Shell Model calculations, provided the particle-hole coupling strength is chosen smaller than the typical 1/A systematic law would suggest for this mass region.

## Method

The authors performed deformed Hartree-Fock plus BCS calculations with the Skyrme force SG2 to generate the single-particle basis, then applied proton-neutron Quasiparticle Random Phase Approximation (pnQRPA) with separable Gamow-Teller residual interactions. The particle-hole coupling strength was set to 0.10 MeV and the particle-particle coupling strength to 0.05 MeV globally for all nuclei, fitted to reproduce the experimental GT+ resonance positions from (n,p) charge-exchange reactions. The theoretical GT strength distributions were quenched by a factor of (0.7)^2 and folded with 2 MeV width Gaussians for comparison with experimental data.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous pnQRPA calculations (Sarriguren et al., 1998-2001) from exotic nuclei around A~70 and rare-earth M1 transitions to the Fe-Ni mass region of astrophysical interest. It directly compares with experimental GT+ data from (n,p) charge-exchange reactions (Vetterli et al., 1989; Ronnqvist et al., 1993; El-Kateb et al., 1994; Williams et al., 1995; Alford et al., 1993) and with Shell Model calculations by Caurier et al. (1999) and Radha et al. (1997). The method enables calculations for heavier nuclei beyond the reach of full Shell Model diagonalization, though both approaches fail to reproduce the observed high-energy strength beyond approximately 6-7 MeV excitation energy, indicating the need for higher-order correlations or larger single-particle bases.
