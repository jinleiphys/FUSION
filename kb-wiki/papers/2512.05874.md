# Topical issue on the intersection of low-energy nuclear structure and high-energy nuclear collisions

> High-energy heavy-ion physics and low-energy nuclear structure physics have historically been disconnected fields. The hydrodynamic description of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) requires input from nuclear structure to model the initial states of the colliding nuclei. Advances in both theory and experiment now show that the hydrodynamic evolution of the QGP is sensitive to the detailed features of the colliding nuclei, with remarkable consequences for experimental observables. The topical collection represents a joint effort between the low- and high-energy nuclear communities, reflecting the growing recognition that precision modeling of nuclear structure is essential for interpreting high-energy collision data. This new experimental approach opens outstanding opportunities to deepen our understanding of strong-interaction matter. Indeed, by probing many-body correlations of nucleons directly in the nuclear ground state, high-energy collisions provide a unique way to "image" nuclei, fully complementary to the techniques of low-energy experiments, where nuclear collectivity is usually inferred from spectroscopic information on excited states. Do emergent many-body QCD phenomena in nuclei manifest consistently across experiments and energy scales? Addressing this question requires synergy between collider data and state-of-the-art nuclear structure calculations. In view of the rapid progress of ab initio methods based on low-energy effective field theories of QCD, the implications are far-reaching: heavy-ion collisions can probe nuclear forces, while nuclear structure insights refine our understanding of QGP dynamics.

## Key claim

High-energy heavy-ion collisions are now sensitive enough to probe detailed nuclear structure features (deformation, neutron skin, triaxiality) of the colliding nuclei, and this sensitivity provides a new method to "image" nuclear ground-state many-body correlations that is complementary to traditional low-energy spectroscopic techniques; however, whether the Lorentz-boosted structure probed at high energies is identical to that measured in low-energy experiments remains an open question.

## Method

The paper is a topical issue introduction that reviews the emerging intersection of low-energy nuclear structure and high-energy heavy-ion physics. It describes how hydrodynamic simulations of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) evolution now require detailed nuclear structure input, including deformation parameters and neutron skin thickness, to model the initial-state geometry of colliding nuclei, and how experimental data from multiple collision systems (U+U, Xe+Xe, isobar collisions) are used to constrain these nuclear properties on a collision-by-collision basis.

## Key numbers

- Collisions of $^{238}$U nuclei at RHIC (data released 2015) require inclusion of strong prolate deformation to reproduce hydrodynamic simulation results.
- Collisions of $^{129}$Xe nuclei at the LHC (data collected 2017) require knowledge of triaxial structure of this isotope.
- Isobar collisions of $^{96}$Ru+$^{96}$Ru and $^{96}$Zr+$^{96}$Zr at RHIC (data released 2021) show signatures of the larger neutron skin of $^{96}$Zr driven by its larger neutron number.
- The ultra-short time scale of the scattering process is approximately $t \approx 10^{-24}$ s.
- Future collisions involving oxygen-16 isotopes and other systems with $A \lesssim 100$ will be performed at the CERN LHC.

## Context

This work extends the recognition, formalized by Jia (2022), that heavy-ion collision observables are sensitive to nuclear structure details beyond simple charge-density parametrizations. It builds on experimental results from the STAR collaboration (2015, 2024, 2025) for uranium collisions, the ALICE, CMS, and ATLAS collaborations (2018-2024) for xenon collisions, and the STAR collaboration (2021) for isobar collisions. The topical issue enables a new synergy between collider data and ab initio many-body calculations (Hergert, 2020), allowing heavy-ion collisions to serve as a probe of nuclear forces while nuclear structure insights refine understanding of QGP dynamics.
