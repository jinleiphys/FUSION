# Non-observability of Spectroscopic Factors

> The spectroscopic factor has long played a central role in nuclear reaction theory. However, it is not an observable. Consequently it is of minimal use as a meeting point between theory and experiment. In this paper the nature of the problem is explored. At the many-body level, unitary transformations are constructed that vary the spectroscopic factors over the full range of allowed values. At the phenomenological level, field redefinitions play a similar role and the spectroscopic factor extracted from experiment depend more on the assumed energy dependence of the potentials than on the measured cross-sections. The consistency conditions, gauge invariance and Wegmann's theorem play a large role in these considerations.

# Non-observability of Spectroscopic Factors

## Key claim

Spectroscopic factors are not observables; they can be varied arbitrarily by unitary transformations at the many-body level or by field redefinitions at the phenomenological level, without changing any observables. The values extracted from experiment depend more on the assumed energy dependence of the potentials than on the measured cross-sections.

## Method

The paper constructs explicit unitary transformations in the many-body Hilbert space that change spectroscopic factors while leaving all observables invariant, demonstrating that spectroscopic factors can be driven to zero or one. At the phenomenological level, field redefinitions of the single-particle wave function (multiplication by a spatially-varying function f(r) that goes to unity at infinity) are shown to produce phase-equivalent potentials with different energy dependence, which in turn change the extracted spectroscopic factor through Wegmann's theorem and gauge-invariance constraints on the transition operator.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factors calculated from soft potentials tend to be approximately 0.9, while those from traditional potentials with strong short-range repulsion give approximately 0.65.
- In a one-dimensional four-body Gaussian example, the spectroscopic factor goes as exp[-6c²/r₀²], explicitly dependent on the unitary transformation parameter c.
- For the 1/2⁺ state in ²¹O, the spectroscopic factor (norm of φ) is very small (consistent with a state absent in a simple single-particle model), while the normalization of the barred auxiliary function φ̄ is almost one.
- For the 5/2⁺ state in ²³O, a similar but less extreme difference is observed between the spectroscopic factor and the normalization of φ̄.
- In comparisons of spectroscopic factors for the same state extracted from different reactions, differences up to a factor of two are reported when published values are used, but agreement is achieved when consistent potentials are employed.

## Context

This work extends the argument from Amado (1979) and Friar (1979) that the d-state probability of the deuteron is not an observable, and from Fearing (1998) showing off-shell NN behavior is unobservable in bremsstrahlung. It directly builds on Furnstahl and Hammer (2002), who showed spectroscopic factors are not observables in general, and on Mukhamedzhanov (2010), who showed they are not invariant under finite-range unitary transformations. The paper argues that the ambiguity arises from the Duhem-Quine thesis: contributions can be shifted between nuclear structure (spectroscopic factor) and reaction mechanism (transition operator), making it impossible to test either in isolation. The work enables a shift toward asymptotic observables such as asymptotic normalization coefficients rather than spectroscopic factors.
