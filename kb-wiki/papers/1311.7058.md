# Isovector pairing in a formalism of quartets for N=Z nuclei

> We describe the ground state of the isovector pairing Hamiltonian in self-conjugate nuclei by a product of collective quartets of different structure built from two neutrons and two protons coupled to total isospin T=0. The structure of the collective quartets is determined by an iterative variational procedure based on a sequence of diagonalizations of the pairing Hamiltonian in spaces of reduced size. The accuracy of the quartet model is tested for N=Z nuclei carrying valence nucleons outside the $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{100}$Sn cores. The comparison with the exact solutions of the pairing Hamiltonian, obtained by shell model diagonalization, shows that the quartet model is able to describe the isovector pairing energy with very high precision. The predictions of the quartet model are also compared to those of the simpler quartet condensation model in which all the collective quartets are assumed to be identical.

## Key claim

The quartet model, which describes the ground state of the isovector pairing Hamiltonian in N=Z nuclei as a product of collective quartets of different structure, reproduces the exact pairing correlation energies with very high precision; for systems with more than one quartet outside the core the state is not exact, but the errors relative to the exact solution are very small and smaller than those of the simpler quartet condensation model.

## Method

The authors construct the ground state as a product of collective T=0 quartets, each built from two isovector pairs (neutron-neutron, proton-proton, and proton-neutron) coupled to total isospin T=0. The mixing amplitudes defining each collective quartet are determined by an iterative variational procedure: at each step, the Hamiltonian is diagonalized in a space of reduced size (at most Ω(Ω+1)/2 states, where Ω is the number of single-particle states) formed by replacing one quartet at a time with all possible non-collective quartets, and the process is repeated until convergence. The model is tested for N=Z nuclei with valence nucleons outside the ¹⁶O, ⁴⁰Ca, and ¹⁰⁰Sn cores, using both spherical single-particle states with pairing forces from standard shell model interactions (USDB, KB3G, G-matrix) and axially deformed single-particle states from Skyrme-HF calculations with a state-independent pairing force of strength g=24/A.

## Key numbers

- For spherical single-particle states and realistic pairing forces, the quartet model (QM) gives correlation energies (in MeV) with errors relative to exact diagonalization of: 0.02% for ²⁴Mg (14.458 MeV vs 14.461 MeV), 0.04% for ²⁸Si (15.780 vs 15.787), 0.06% for ⁴⁸Cr (9.573 vs 9.579), 0.23% for ⁵²Fe (10.725 vs 10.750), and 0.02% for ¹¹²Ba (8.678 vs 8.680); for ²⁰Ne, ³²S, ⁴⁴Ti, ¹⁰⁴Te, and ¹⁰⁸Xe the QM results are exact (error marked as "-").
- For axially deformed single-particle states with state-independent pairing (g=24/A), the QM correlation energies are essentially exact for all nuclei tested, with the largest error being 0.0001 MeV for ⁵²Fe (5.4531 vs exact 5.4532) and ¹¹²Ba (2.7034 vs exact 2.7035).
- The quartet condensation model (QCM) shows larger errors: for spherical cases, up to 0.37% for ⁵²Fe (10.710 vs 10.750) and 1.00% for ¹¹²Ba (8.593 vs 8.680); for deformed cases, up to 0.57% for ¹¹²Ba (2.688 vs 2.7035).

## Context

This work extends the quartet condensation model (QCM) of Sambataro and Sandulescu (2013) by allowing the collective quartets to have different structures rather than being identical, which improves the accuracy of the correlation energy. The approach goes beyond generalized BCS models (Goswami, 1964; Camiz, 1966; Ginocchio, 1965) which, as shown by Engel (1997) and Dobes (1998), cannot properly describe isovector pairing due to isospin symmetry breaking, and also beyond the Generator Coordinate Method applied to projected-BCS states (Chen, 2012). The quartet model enables treatment of N=Z nuclei with more valence nucleons and/or larger shells than standard shell model diagonalizations, and can be extended to more complex Hamiltonians such as the isovector plus isoscalar pairing Hamiltonian.
