# Predicting reaction observables for the two-neutron halo candidates $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na

> Microscopic description of two-neutron ($2n$) halo candidates $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na has been realized from nuclear structure to reaction observables for the first time. The reliability of the Glauber reaction model has been confirmed by exactly reproducing the momentum distributions of the benchmark $2n$ halo nucleus $^{11}$Li, with the identical structural inputs from the former work. Combined with the structure from the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc), the Glauber model is applied to predict the reaction observables, including the reaction cross sections (RCSs) for the fluorine and sodium isotopes bombarding a carbon target at 240~MeV/A and the longitudinal momentum distributions of the fragments after $2n$ knockout reactions. It turns out that the calculated RCSs agree well with the available experimental data and a pronounced increase occurs to $^{29, 31}$F + $^{12}$C and $^{37, 39}$Na + $^{12}$C, which deviate from the original trend of their neighbours. Furthermore, the narrower longitudinal momentum distributions of the fragments after $2n$ knockout reactions demonstrate that $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na have the dilute $2n$ halo structure. Such a new combination is promising to suggest new $2n$ halo candidates for future measurements.

# Predicting reaction observables for the two-neutron halo candidates $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na

## Key claim
The combination of the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum (DRHBc) with the Glauber reaction model predicts that $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na exhibit dilute two-neutron halo structure, but this conclusion depends on the reliability of the Glauber model being first validated against the benchmark halo nucleus $^{11}$Li using identical structural inputs from prior work.

## Method
The authors first validated the Glauber reaction model for two-neutron halo systems by reproducing the reaction cross sections and longitudinal momentum distributions of $^{11}$Li + $^{12}$C using structural inputs from a harmonic oscillator basis. They then replaced the structural inputs with density distributions and wave functions from the DRHBc theory using the PC-PK1 density functional, and applied the Glauber model in the microscopic optical-limit approximation to calculate reaction cross sections at 240 MeV/A and longitudinal momentum distributions after two-neutron knockout for fluorine and sodium isotopes on a carbon target.

## Key numbers
- $^{31}$F: two-neutron separation energy $S_{2n} \approx 0.41$ MeV from DRHBc theory
- $^{39}$Na: two-neutron separation energy $S_{2n} \approx 1$ MeV from DRHBc theory
- Reaction cross sections for $^{21-31}$F + $^{12}$C at 240 MeV/A: calculated values agree with experimental data for $^{21-29}$F; pronounced increase occurs for $^{29,31}$F
- Reaction cross sections for $^{22-39}$Na + $^{12}$C at 240 MeV/A: calculated values agree with experimental data up to $^{35}$Na; notable increase from $^{37}$Na to $^{39}$Na
- Full width at half maximum of longitudinal momentum distributions after $2n$ knockout: 182 MeV/c for $^{37}$Na + $^{12}$C, 158 MeV/c for $^{39}$Na + $^{12}$C at 240 MeV/A
- Validation of Glauber model for $^{11}$Li + $^{12}$C: reaction cross sections 1051 mb for $(s_{1/2})^{2}$ configuration and 1059 mb for $(p_{1/2})^{2}$ configuration, within 1% relative error of results from Ogawa (2001)

## Context
This work extends the DRHBc + Glauber approach previously applied to one-neutron halo nuclei such as $^{15,19}$C (Wang 2024, An 2025), $^{31}$Ne (Zhang 2022, Zhong 2022), and $^{37}$Mg (Zhang 2023, An 2024) to the more complex two-neutron halo case for the first time. It builds on structural studies of $^{31}$F by Singh (2022) using three-body hyperspherical formalism and of $^{39}$Na by Zhang (2023) using DRHBc theory, which predicted quenched $N=28$ shell closure and shape decoupling. The predictions of reaction observables for $^{31}$F and $^{39}$Na provide quantitative targets for future measurements at facilities such as RIKEN, and the approach enables systematic searches for new halo nuclei in the medium-mass region around $A \approx 40$.
