# Probing the finite density equation of state of QCD via resummed perturbation theory

> In this Ph.D. thesis, the primary goal is to present a recent investigation of the finite density thermodynamics of hot and dense quark-gluon plasma. As we are interested in a temperature regime, in which naive perturbation theory is known to lose its predictive power, we clearly need to use a refined approach. To this end, we adopt a resummed perturbation theory point of view and employ two different frameworks. We first use hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory (HLTpt) at leading order to obtain the pressure for nonvanishing quark chemical potentials, and next, inspired by dimensional reduction, resum the known four-loop weak coupling expansion for the quantity. We present and analyze our findings for various cumulants of conserved charges. This provides us with information, through correlations and fluctuations, on the degrees of freedom effectively present in the quark-gluon plasma right above the deconfinement transition. Moreover, we compare our results with state-of-the-art lattice Monte Carlo simulations as well as with a recent three-loop mass truncated HTLpt calculation. We obtain very good agreement between the two different perturbative schemes, as well as between them and lattice data, down to surprisingly low temperatures right above the phase transition. We also quantitatively test the convergence of an approximation, which is used in higher order loop calculations in HTLpt. This method based on expansions in mass parameters, is unavoidable beyond leading order, thus motivating our investigation. We find the ensuing convergence to be very fast, validating its use in higher order computations.

## Key claim

Using two different resummed perturbation theory frameworks (hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory at leading order and a dimensional-reduction-inspired resummation of the four-loop weak coupling expansion), the author obtains the pressure of hot and dense quark-gluon plasma for nonvanishing quark chemical potentials and finds very good agreement with lattice Monte Carlo simulations down to surprisingly low temperatures right above the deconfinement transition, despite the fact that naive perturbation theory loses predictive power in this regime.

## Method

The author employs hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory (HTLpt) at leading order to compute the pressure for nonzero quark chemical potentials, and separately resums the known four-loop weak coupling expansion using a dimensional reduction framework. Both calculations are performed in the limit of vanishing bare quark masses and focus on the strong interaction only, disregarding the electroweak force. The results are compared with state-of-the-art lattice Monte Carlo simulations and with a recent three-loop mass truncated HTLpt calculation, and the convergence of the mass-parameter expansion method used in higher-order HTLpt loops is quantitatively tested.

## Key numbers

- The pseudo-critical temperature of the deconfinement transition is given as \(T_c = 154 \pm 9\) MeV.
- The analysis covers temperatures right above this phase transition, down to surprisingly low temperatures where naive perturbation theory fails.
- The convergence of the mass-parameter expansion used in higher-order HTLpt calculations is found to be very fast, validating its use in higher-order computations.

## Context

This work extends previous analytic calculations of QCD thermodynamics at finite density, including unresummed perturbation theory (e.g., Aleksi, 2009), various hard-thermal-loop motivated approaches (e.g., Blaizot, 2001; Mustafa, 2005), and earlier hard-thermal-loop perturbation theory studies (e.g., Baier and Redlich, 2000; Andersen and Strickland, 2011). It provides a first-principles perturbative crosscheck of lattice QCD results at nonzero density, where lattice Monte Carlo simulations suffer from the sign problem and are limited to small chemical potentials. The results enable bridging the gap between low and asymptotically high temperature regions using analytic methods, and offer insight into the degrees of freedom present in the quark-gluon plasma just above the deconfinement transition through cumulants of conserved charges.
