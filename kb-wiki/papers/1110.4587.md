# Heavy flavor nuclear modification factor: more baryons than mesons less energy loss

> The suppression of the nuclear modification factor for heavy flavor hadrons is usually attributed to the energy loss of heavy quarks propagating in a QCD plasma. Nevertheless it is puzzling that the suppression is as strong as for light flavors. We show that when accounting for the quark momentum shift associated to the opening of the recombination/coalescence channel for hadron production in the plasma, it is not necessary to invoke such strong energy loss. This shift is expressed in terms of an increase of the heavy baryon to meson ratio in nuclear with respect to proton collisions. When this mechanism is included along with a moderate energy loss, data from RHIC and LHC for the nuclear modification factor of electrons coming from heavy flavor decays as well as of charm mesons, can be reasonably described.

## Key claim
The suppression of the heavy flavor nuclear modification factor can be explained without invoking a large energy loss, provided one accounts for the quark momentum redistribution from recombination/coalescence hadron formation in the medium. This redistribution is encoded in an enhanced heavy baryon-to-meson ratio in nuclear collisions relative to proton collisions, and when included, only a moderate energy loss is needed to describe the data.

## Method
The authors used the Dynamical Quark Recombination Model (DQRM) to compute the charm baryon-to-meson ratio in heavy-ion collisions, incorporating a transverse expansion velocity and a time-dependent temperature. They then derived analytic expressions for the nuclear modification factor of D mesons and of electrons from heavy flavor decays, expressed in terms of the baryon-to-meson ratios in AA and pp collisions and an energy loss parameter epsilon. The model was compared to ALICE and STAR data using PYTHIA simulations for the pp baseline.

## Key numbers
- Charm baryon mass set to 2.29 GeV and D meson mass to 1.87 GeV for the DQRM calculation.
- Initial hadronization proper time tau_0 = 1 fm, initial temperature T_0 = 175 MeV, final temperature T_f = 100 MeV corresponding to tau_f = 8 fm.
- Transverse expansion velocity v_t = 0.65 used for LHC energies and v_t = 0.55 for RHIC energies.
- Energy loss parameter epsilon = 0.55 (upper curves) and epsilon = 0.4 (lower curves) used to describe both R_AA^D and R_AA^e data.
- For electrons from heavy flavor decays, effective masses used: m^D = 3.57 GeV (average of D^0 and B^0) and m^Lambda = 3.95 GeV (average of Lambda_c and Lambda_b).
- Branching ratios for semileptonic decays: D^pm (16%), D^0 (6.53%), D_s^pm (8%), B^pm (10.8%), B^0 (10.1%), Lambda_c (4.5%), Lambda_b (5.35%).

## Context
This work addresses the puzzle that heavy flavor hadrons are as suppressed as light hadrons in nuclear collisions, despite the dead cone effect that should reduce radiative energy loss for heavy quarks. It extends the recombination/coalescence scenario previously applied to light and strange hadrons (as measured by experiments at RHIC and LHC) to heavy flavors, using the DQRM model. The results enable a reinterpretation of heavy flavor suppression data without requiring the extremely small energy loss parameters needed for light flavors, and the authors note that upcoming detector upgrades at RHIC and ALICE should allow direct experimental tests of the predicted enhanced heavy baryon-to-meson ratio.
