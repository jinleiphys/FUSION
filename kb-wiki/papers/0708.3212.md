# A New Study of the Transition to Uniform Nuclear Matter in Neutron Stars and Supernovae

## Key claim
The paper outlines a comprehensive microscopic study of bulk matter at densities just below nuclear saturation, but presents only preliminary results; the full study is still in progress, with calculations being performed on the Cray XT3 supercomputer Jaguar at Oak Ridge National Lab.

## Method
The study uses a three-dimensional Hartree-Fock method with a Skyrme effective nuclear interaction, imposing Bloch boundary conditions to account for the band structure of unbound neutrons. The simulation scans across different cell sizes and neutron quadrupole moments at constant density, temperature, and proton fraction, using simple periodic boundary conditions first, then extending to nonzero Bloch momentum. The calculations are performed self-consistently in three dimensions without the Wigner-Seitz approximation, making no distinction between nuclear clusters and the external neutron gas.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: $\rho_s = 2.5 \sim 10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$
- Density range of interest: $0.05\rho_s \lesssim \rho \lesssim 0.16\rho_s$
- Temperature range: $0 < T \lesssim 10$ MeV
- Proton fraction range: $0.01 \lesssim y_p \lesssim 0.3$
- Typical nuclear radii: approximately 10 fm
- Simulation volumes for Hartree-Fock: 20-40 fm (smaller volumes) compared to Quantum Molecular Dynamics volumes of order 100 fm
- Stellar mass threshold for core collapse: $M \gtrsim 8M_{\odot}$

## Context
This work extends earlier three-dimensional studies of nuclear pasta phases using Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) and Hartree-Fock (HF) methods, which neglected band structure effects of unbound nucleons. The study aims to cover a more comprehensive range of density and proton fraction space than previous work, and extends to finite temperature to explore matter in core collapse supernovae. The results will help constrain models of neutron star cooling, interpret quasi-periodic oscillations from soft gamma-ray repeaters, understand pulsar glitches, and evaluate gravitational wave emission from accreting neutron stars.
