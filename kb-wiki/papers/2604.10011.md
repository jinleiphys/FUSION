# Possible Supermassive Dark Object Composed of Light Fermionic Gas with an Embedded Neutron Star Core

> The structure of dark matter admixed neutron stars (DANSs) are investigated, adopting a non-annihilating self-interacting fermionic dark matter (DM) model, with a particular focus on the case of the light DM particle mass $m_D \in [10^{-10}, 1]$ GeV. The DANSs become DM-dominated configurations when $m_D <10^{-1}$ GeV, where a compact neutron star core becomes embedded within an extremely large DM halo. It is found that the maximum mass of DANSs is inversely proportional to $m_{ D}$, approximately as $ 0.627 (\mathrm{GeV/} m_{\rm D})^2 ~\mathrm{M_{\odot}}$, which implies that extremely large masses can be achieved for small $m_{\rm D}$. For $m_D \sim5\times10^{-4}$ GeV, the calculated mass and size of the DM halo can be comparable to those of supermassive black holes such as Sgr A*. Our findings hint at a scenario where neutron stars might serve as strong gravitational seeds for such supermassive dark objects.

## Key claim
For dark matter particle masses below about \(10^{-1}\) GeV, dark matter admixed neutron stars (DANSs) become DM-dominated configurations where a compact neutron star core is embedded within an extremely large DM halo; the maximum mass of such DANSs follows an inverse-square scaling with DM particle mass, potentially reaching supermassive scales comparable to Sgr A* for \(m_D \sim 5\times10^{-4}\) GeV, but the authors note that whether such equilibrium states can be reached via dynamical accretion remains an open question.

## Method
The authors solve the two-fluid Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for a system where nuclear matter (NM) and fermionic dark matter (DM) couple only through gravity. DM is modeled as a non-annihilating self-interacting Fermi gas with particle mass \(m_D\) treated as a free parameter in the range \([10^{-10}, 1]\) GeV, using both weak interaction (WI, with interaction mass scale \(m_I \sim 300\) GeV) and strong interaction (SI, with \(m_I \sim 0.1\) GeV) scenarios. Nuclear matter is described using the relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory with the DDME2 parameter set, with the central energy density of NM fixed at 1050 MeV/fm\(^3\) (the value at which a pure neutron star reaches its maximum mass).

## Key numbers
- Maximum mass of DANSs scales as approximately \(0.627 (\mathrm{GeV}/m_{\mathrm{D}})^2 ~\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) for small \(m_D\) (where \(m_D/m_I \ll 1\)).
- For \(m_D = 10^{-5}\) GeV, the DM mass reaches \(6.58\times10^{9}\) \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) with a DM radius of \(7.86\times10^{10}\) km, while the embedded neutron star core has \(M_N = 2.48\) \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) and \(R_N = 11.7\) km.
- For \(m_D = 10^{-10}\) GeV, the DM mass reaches \(6.58\times10^{19}\) \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) with a DM radius of \(7.86\times10^{20}\) km.
- For \(m_D = 1\) GeV, the maximum DM mass reaches 1.89 \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) for SI and 0.41 \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) for WI.
- For \(m_D \sim 5\times10^{-4}\) GeV (approximately 500 keV), the calculated mass and size of the DM halo are comparable to those of Sgr A*.
- The DM radius at maximum mass for \(m_D = 10^{-10}\) GeV is about \(8.58\times10^{20}\) km, while the maximum DM radius is approximately \(1.5\times10^{21}\) km.

## Context
This work extends studies of dark matter admixed neutron stars (DANSs) into the light DM regime below 1 GeV, where previous work by Wang (2019) found maximum DM masses of only about 0.27 \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) for \(m_D = 10\) GeV and 0.027 \(\mathrm{M_{\odot}}\) for \(m_D = 100\) GeV, far below supermassive scales. The results connect to the RAR model (Ruffini 2015, Arguelles 2018) which proposes supermassive compact objects composed of self-gravitating fermionic DM as alternatives to black holes, including Sgr A*. The predicted DM particle mass of approximately 500 keV is close to the 48-345 keV range suggested by Arguelles (2018) for the black hole alternative scenario, and the resulting core-halo morphology qualitatively matches the fermionic DM structures of the RAR model. The work enables future tests via very-long-baseline interferometry to distinguish between black hole and dark matter object hypotheses.
