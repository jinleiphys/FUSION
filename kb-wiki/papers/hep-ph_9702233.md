# Density and Boundary Effects on Pion Distributions in Relativistic Heavy--Ion Collisions

> We compute the pion inclusive momentum distribution in a heavy-ion collision, assuming thermal equilibrium and accounting for boundary effects at the time of decoupling. We calculate the chemical potential corresponding to an average pion multiplicity in central collisions and explore the consequences of having the pion system produced close to the critical temperature for Bose-Einstein condensation.

## Key claim

The enhancement of the pion momentum distribution at both low and high transverse momentum in heavy-ion collisions relative to nucleon-nucleon collisions can be explained by a combination of Bose-Einstein statistics near the critical temperature for condensation (affecting low p_t) and boundary effects from a finite spherical fireball at decoupling (affecting high p_t), provided the pion system is treated with discrete quantum states rather than a continuum approximation.

## Method

The authors model the pion system at decoupling as a thermalized gas of weakly interacting spin-zero bosons confined within a rigid spherical volume of radius R, imposing Dirichlet boundary conditions on the Klein-Gordon wave functions. They compute the discrete energy eigenvalues and momentum-space wave functions for free particles in a sphere, then construct the thermal momentum distribution by summing over these discrete states weighted by the Bose-Einstein occupation factor. The chemical potential is determined self-consistently from the fixed average pion multiplicity using the discrete level sum, and the resulting transverse momentum distributions are compared to the continuum Bose-Einstein distribution.

## Key numbers

- At AGS energies (sqrt(s) ~ 5 A GeV), average pion multiplicity per event in central collisions is on the order of 400-500, so pions of a particular species number roughly 100-200.
- For R = 8 fm, T = 150 MeV, N = 150: discrete-state calculation yields chemical potential mu = 94.3 MeV, while continuum Bose-Einstein with same parameters gives mu = 67.4 MeV.
- For R = 6 fm, T = 120 MeV, N = 200: discrete-state calculation yields mu = 169.1 MeV, while continuum Bose-Einstein gives mu = 83.5 MeV.
- The critical temperature for Bose-Einstein condensation is computed from an implicit equation involving volume, pion mass, and multiplicity; for a given R, T_c decreases as volume increases.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Mostafa and Wong (1995) by explicitly computing the discrete-state momentum distribution and its deviation from the continuum Bose-Einstein form, and connects to the observation of low-p_t enhancement discussed by Gerber and Leutwyler (1990) and Pratt (1993). The calculation enables future work incorporating expansion effects and suggests that the presence or absence of a condensate could serve as a probe of collision transparency and affect correlation experiments such as Hanbury-Brown Twiss interferometry.
