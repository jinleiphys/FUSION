# Soft heavy-ion physics from hydrodynamics with statistical hadronization - predictions for the Large Hadron Collider

> Hydrodynamics merged with single-freeze-out statistical hadronization is used to describe the midrapidity hadron production in relativistic heavy-ion collisions at the highest RHIC energies and to make predictions for the LHC. Thermodynamic properties of the quark-gluon plasma are taken from lattice simulations, at low temperatures the hadron-gas equation of state is used, while in the cross-over region an interpolation between the two equations of state is constructed. The initial condition for hydrodynamics is obtained from a Glauber profile for the entropy, with the initial central temperature Ti. The conditions obtained from the hydrodynamic expansion at the freeze-out temperature Tf are used as input for the thermal event generator THERMINATOR. Basic physical observables are obtained: the transverse-momentum spectra, the elliptic flow coefficient v2, and the HBT radii. The femtoscopic observables are evaluated with the help of the two-particle method which accounts for the resonance decays and Coulomb final-state interactions. The problem of a simultaneous description of all discussed observables is addressed, with the conclusion that at the highest RHIC energies our approach gives a quite satisfactory global description of soft hadronic observables. Some discrepancies may be attributed to the absence of the final-state elastic interactions among hadrons. Extrapolating Ti to higher values allows for global predictions for soft hadronic physics at the LHC. We test Ti=400, 450, and 500 MeV, and observe the expected growth of particle multiplicities and the increase of the flow, resulting in smaller slopes of the pT-spectra. The elliptic flow of pions exhibits saturation, with v2 remaining practically constant, while the HBT radii increase moderately with Ti.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a combined hydrodynamic plus statistical hadronization model, which omits final-state elastic rescattering, provides a "quite satisfactory global description" of soft hadronic observables at the highest RHIC energies, with agreement at the 10-15% level, and that extrapolating the initial central temperature to higher values yields predictions for the LHC.

## Method

The authors used inviscid (perfect-fluid) hydrodynamics with a temperature-dependent sound velocity derived from lattice QCD at high temperatures and a hadron-gas equation of state at low temperatures, with a smooth interpolation in the cross-over region. The initial condition for the entropy density was obtained from a Glauber profile with a mixed model (14% binary collisions, 86% wounded nucleons). The freeze-out hypersurface and flow profile from the hydrodynamic expansion at a constant freeze-out temperature were fed into the THERMINATOR event generator, which implements statistical hadronization with complete treatment of hadronic resonances and their decays, but without final-state elastic rescattering.

## Key numbers

*   The initial central temperature for RHIC calculations was \(T_i = 320\) MeV, and the freeze-out temperature was \(T_f = 150\) MeV.
*   For LHC predictions, initial central temperatures of \(T_i = 400\), 450, and 500 MeV were tested, with \(T_f\) fixed at 150 MeV.
*   At RHIC, the model agrees with data at the level of 10-15%.
*   The HBT radii at RHIC show \(R_{\rm side}\) 10-15% below experimental data, \(R_{\rm out}\) within experimental errors, and \(R_{\rm long}\) about 15% too large.
*   The ratio \(R_{\rm out}/R_{\rm side}\) is about 1.2-1.25 and almost constant as a function of the transverse momentum of the pair.
*   For LHC predictions, the HBT radii \(R_{\rm side}\) and \(R_{\rm long}\) increase roughly 1 fm for each 100 MeV increase of \(T_i\).
*   The nucleon-nucleon total inelastic cross section used was \(\sigma = 42\) mb for RHIC and \(\sigma = 66\) mb for LHC.
*   The initial time for the hydrodynamic evolution was \(\tau_{\rm init} = 1\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic approach of Chojnacki (2004, 2006, 2007) by merging it with the statistical hadronization model THERMINATOR of Kisiel (2005) to provide a global description of midrapidity observables at RHIC and predictions for the LHC. It builds upon prior hydrodynamic analyses of RHIC data by Huovinen (2001), Teaney (2000, 2001), Hirano (2002), Kolb (2002), Hama (2005), Eskola (2005), and Nonaka (2006), and uses the lattice QCD equation of state from Aoki (2005). The predictions for the LHC enable future experimental comparisons and detector simulations, and the authors note that the absence of final-state elastic interactions among hadrons may account for some discrepancies with data.
