# Appearance of the Single Gyroid Network Phase in Nuclear Pasta Matter

> Nuclear matter under the conditions of a supernova explosion unfolds into a rich variety of spatially structured phases, called nuclear pasta. We investigate the role of periodic network-like structures with negatively curved interfaces in nuclear pasta structures, by static and dynamic Hartree-Fock simulations in periodic lattices. As the most prominent result, we identify for the first time the {\it single gyroid} network structure of cubic chiral $I4_123$ symmetry, a well known configuration in nanostructured soft-matter systems, both as a dynamical state and as a cooled static solution. Single gyroid structures form spontaneously in the course of the dynamical simulations. Most of them are isomeric states. The very small energy differences to the ground state indicate its relevance for structures in nuclear pasta.

# Appearance of the Single Gyroid Network Phase in Nuclear Pasta Matter

## Key claim
The single gyroid network structure of cubic chiral I4_123 symmetry, previously known only in soft-matter systems, appears in nuclear pasta matter both as a dynamical state and as a cooled static solution, but most of these gyroid structures are isomeric states with very small energy differences to the ground state (typically the slab structure), indicating their relevance rather than dominance.

## Method
The authors performed static Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations with biased initialization using external guiding potentials shaped like the gyroid, primitive, and slab nodal surfaces, followed by 9000 pure HF iterations without the guiding potential. They also conducted time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) simulations initialized with randomly placed alpha-particles and plane-wave neutrons at high excitation energy (T approximately 7 MeV), running for 1000 fm/c. All calculations used the SLy6 Skyrme parametrization at proton fraction X_p = 1/3, with periodic boundary conditions in cubic boxes of side length 15-26 fm and grid spacings 0.875-1.125 fm. Structures were identified using Minkowski functionals (particularly the Euler characteristic) and graphical verification of the gyroid nodal network.

## Key numbers
- Mean density for dynamical simulations: rho = 0.06 fm^{-3}
- Box length range for static calculations: a = 15-26 fm; for dynamical simulations: a = 20-24 fm
- Grid spacing range: 0.875 fm <= Delta r <= 1.125 fm
- Proton fraction: X_p = 1/3 throughout
- Guiding potential strength for biased initialization: phi_0 = 10 MeV
- Excitation temperature in dynamical simulations: T approximately 7 MeV
- Binding energy for cooled dynamical gyroid at a = 22 fm: E/N = 10.357 MeV versus static HF gyroid: E/N = 10.377 MeV
- Euler characteristic chi = -4 is the necessary condition for gyroid identification
- Gyroid structures were stable for box lengths a >= 22 fm in static calculations
- Predicted box length for maximum gyroid binding energy: a approximately 27 fm (from Bonnet transformation scaling relative to primitive surface maximum at a approximately 22 fm)
- Surface area A and mean curvature H of pasta gyroid and primitive surfaces agree with exact constant-mean-curvature values within voxelization uncertainty (see Table I for full numerical comparison across densities 0.0326-0.0977 fm^{-3} and volume fractions 0.22-0.70)
- Eigenvalue ratio beta_1^{0,2} of the interface tensor: not below 0.75 for gyroid, between 0.4 and 1 for primitive structures

## Context
This work extends prior studies of nuclear pasta structures by Schuetrumpf (2013) that found only primitive (P) surface topologies in smaller boxes (a = 16 fm), and by Nakazato (2009, 2011) who examined double gyroids within a liquid-drop model. The authors demonstrate for the first time that the single gyroid appears in fully quantum-mechanical Skyrme-Hartree-Fock simulations, closely competing with the primitive surface and slab (lasagna) structures. The results enable future investigations of neutrino transport in supernova cores, since the percolating network-like nature of the gyroid could significantly affect transport properties; the authors note that larger-scale calculations are needed to unambiguously assess box-size effects and confirm the trends observed here.
