# Relativistic three-particle dynamical equations: II. Application to the trinucleon system

> We calculate the contribution of relativistic dynamics on the neutron-deuteron scattering length and triton binding energy employing five sets trinucleon potential models and four types of three-dimensional relativistic three-body equations suggested in the preceding paper. The relativistic correction to binding energy may vary a lot and even change sign depending on the relativistic formulation employed. The deviations of these observables from those obtained in nonrelativistic models follow the general universal trend of deviations introduced by off- and on-shell variations of two- and three-nucleon potentials in a nonrelativistic model calculation. Consequently, it will be difficult to separate unambiguously the effect of off- and on-shell variations of two- and three- nucleon potentials on low-energy three-nucleon observables from the effect of relativistic dynamics.

## Key claim

The relativistic correction to the triton binding energy and neutron-deuteron scattering length varies widely in magnitude and even sign depending on which relativistic three-body formulation is used, and these corrections follow the same universal correlated trend as off- and on-shell variations of two- and three-nucleon potentials in nonrelativistic models, making it impossible to unambiguously separate relativistic effects from potential-model variations in low-energy three-nucleon observables.

## Method

The authors solved nonrelativistic and four types of three-dimensional relativistic Faddeev equations (labeled A, B, C, D) for the triton binding energy and neutron-deuteron scattering length, using five combinations of separable Yamaguchi and Tabakin nucleon-nucleon potentials in the triplet and singlet channels. The relativistic two-nucleon dynamics was governed by the Blankenbecler-Sugar equation, and phase-equivalent nonrelativistic potentials were generated via a transformation of the form factors. Spin variables were treated nonrelativistically.

## Key numbers

- Triton binding energy for the YY (Yamaguchi-Yamaguchi) potential: nonrelativistic 10.65 MeV; relativistic model A 10.73 MeV; model B 10.92 MeV; model C 10.39 MeV; model D 10.91 MeV.
- Neutron-deuteron scattering length for the YY potential: nonrelativistic -0.77 fm; relativistic model A -0.83 fm; model B -0.94 fm; model C -0.61 fm; model D -0.94 fm.
- Triton binding energy for the YT-2 (Yamaguchi triplet, Tabakin-2 singlet) potential: nonrelativistic 7.69 MeV; relativistic model A 7.87 MeV; model B 8.30 MeV; model C 7.52 MeV; model D 8.19 MeV.
- Neutron-deuteron scattering length for the YT-2 potential: nonrelativistic 1.30 fm; relativistic model A 1.15 fm; model B 0.82 fm; model C 1.42 fm; model D 0.90 fm.
- The magnitude of the relativistic correction to triton binding energy varied from 0.1 MeV to 0.7 MeV across different potential models and relativistic formulations.
- The deuteron binding parameter alpha_0 was set to 0.23161 fm^{-1}; the singlet virtual-state parameter alpha_1 was 0.03992 fm^{-1}.

## Context

This work extends prior relativistic three-nucleon bound-state calculations by Jackson and Tjon (1970), Rupp and Tjon (1988, 1992), Garcilazo (1981), and Sammarruca, Xu, and Machleidt (1992), and the one previous study of relativistic effects on the neutron-deuteron scattering length by Garcilazo, Mathelitsch, and Zankel (1985). The authors introduce three relativistic three-body propagators (models B, C, D) that had not been used numerically before in this context, alongside the established Aaron-Amado-Young propagator (model A). The key finding that relativistic corrections cannot be distinguished from off-shell potential variations in low-energy observables confirms the existence of a shape-independent approximation for these observables, as previously argued by Tomio, Delfino, and Adhikari (1987), and suggests that future work should focus on observables more directly sensitive to relativistic dynamics, such as charge form factors, though these are complicated by meson-exchange currents and non-nucleonic components.
