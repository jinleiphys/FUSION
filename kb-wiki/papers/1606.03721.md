# Mobile impurity in a Fermi sea from the functional renormalization group analytically continued to real time

> Motivated by experiments with cold atoms, we investigate a mobile impurity immersed in a Fermi sea in three dimensions at zero temperature by means of the functional renormalization group. We first perform the derivative expansion of the effective action to calculate the ground state energy and Tan's contact across the polaron-molecule transition for several mass imbalances. Next we study quasiparticle properties of the impurity by using a real-time method recently developed in nuclear physics, which allows one to go beyond the derivative expansion. We obtain the spectral function of the polaron, the effective mass and quasiparticle weight of attractive and repulsive polarons, and clarify how they are affected by mass imbalances.

## Key claim
The authors use a functional renormalization group (FRG) approach with a derivative expansion, followed by analytic continuation to real time, to compute both thermodynamic and spectral properties of a mobile impurity in a Fermi sea. The hedge is that the derivative expansion alone is inadequate for spectral properties, but the authors circumvent this by first solving the flow equations in the derivative expansion and then substituting those solutions into the flow equations for the full momentum- and frequency-dependent two-point functions, performing the analytic continuation directly at the level of the flow equation.

## Method
The authors start from a two-channel model of two-component fermions with an s-wave interaction and apply the Wetterich equation for the average action. They first perform a leading-order derivative expansion of the impurity and molecule inverse propagators to obtain flow equations for the wave-function renormalizations and gaps, using a 3d sharp cutoff regulator. In a second step, they substitute these derivative-expansion solutions back into the flow equations for the full momentum- and frequency-dependent two-point functions, analytically continue to real frequency via \(p_0 = -i(\omega+i\epsilon)\), and integrate from the ultraviolet scale \(k=\Lambda\) to \(k=0\) to obtain retarded Green's functions and spectral functions.

## Key numbers
* Polaron-molecule transition point for equal masses (\(\alpha=1\)): \((k_Fa)^{-1}=0.973\), compared with 0.90(2) from diagrammatic Monte Carlo and 0.904(5) from BMW-type FRG.
* Ground state energy at unitarity for \(\alpha=1\): \(\hat{\mu}_{\downarrow}=-0.535\), compared with \(-0.57\) from BMW-type FRG and \(-0.618\) from diagrammatic Monte Carlo.
* Ground state energy at unitarity for heavy impurity (\(\alpha=6.6\)): \(\hat{\mu}_{\downarrow}=-0.385\), compared with \(-0.44\) from a T-matrix approach.
* Ground state energy at unitarity for light impurity (\(\alpha=0.5\)): \(\hat{\mu}_{\downarrow}=-0.772\), compared with \(\sim -0.9\) from diagrammatic Monte Carlo.
* Quasiparticle weight of the attractive polaron at unitarity for \(\alpha=1\): \(Z_{\rm att}=0.813\), close to values 0.78–0.80 from variational, T-matrix, and BMW-type FRG methods.
* Effective mass of the attractive polaron at unitarity for \(\alpha=1\): \(m_{\down {\rm att}}/M_\down=1.125\), compared with 1.09(2) from fixed-node diffusion Monte Carlo and 1.17 from variational methods.

## Context
This work extends the FRG approach to the Fermi polaron problem, previously studied by Schmidt and Enss (2011) using a BMW-type FRG with Pade approximation for analytic continuation, which is computationally expensive. The authors apply a real-time method developed in nuclear physics by Kamikado et al. (2012, 2013) and Tripolt et al. (2013, 2014) that avoids numerically expensive approximate analytic continuation. The results for thermodynamic quantities and quasiparticle properties are compared with diagrammatic Monte Carlo (Prokofiev and Svistunov, 2008; Vlietinck et al., 2013; Kroiss and Pollet, 2014), variational methods (Combescot et al., 2007, 2008; Punk et al., 2009), T-matrix approaches (Massignan and Bruun, 2011; Baarsma et al., 2010), and fixed-node diffusion Monte Carlo (Lobo et al., 2006; Pilati and Giorgini, 2008). The method enables future studies of nonequilibrium physics in experimentally accessible cold-atom systems.
