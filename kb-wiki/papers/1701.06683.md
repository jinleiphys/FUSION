# Dynamics of Fragmented Condensates and Macroscopic Entanglement

> The relative phase of the order parameters in the collision of two condensates can influence the outcome of their collision in the case of weak coupling. With increasing interaction strength however, the initially independent phases of the two order parameters in the colliding partners quickly become phase locked, as the strong coupling favors an overall phase rigidity of the entire condensate, and upon their separation the emerging superfluid fragments become entangled.

## Key claim

The initial relative phase of two colliding condensates influences the collision outcome only in the weak-coupling regime; with increasing interaction strength, the phases become locked across the entire system and the final fragments emerge macroscopically entangled, making the outcome largely independent of the initial relative phase.

## Method

The authors analyze the collision dynamics of fragmented Bose-Einstein condensates using the time-dependent Gross-Pitaevskii (GP) equation, and compare with time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) simulations of colliding superfluid nuclei (e.g., \(^{120}\)Sn+\(^{120}\)Sn and \(^{240}\)Pu fission). They vary the interaction strength (coupling constant \(g\) for bosons; pairing field strength for fermions) and the initial relative phase \(\alpha\) between the two condensates, tracking the evolution of the phase of the order parameter and the final fragment properties.

## Key numbers

- In the collision of \(^{240}\)Pu on \(^{240}\)Pu near the Coulomb barrier, pairing effects can change the total kinetic energy of emerging fragments by up to 20 MeV and the apparent fusion barrier height by 10 MeV or more, as reported by Magierski, Sekizawa, and Wlazłowski (2016).
- The pairing field amplitude \(\Delta\) in nuclei is of order 1 MeV, significantly smaller than the normal single-particle field of order 50 MeV.
- The condensation energy \(E_{cond} = -N(0)|\Delta|^2/2\) is at most a few MeV.
- In simulations of \(^{240}\)Pu fission with artificially increased pairing field to \(\approx 3-4\) MeV, phase locking occurs and the evolution becomes nearly identical to an ideal fluid.

## Context

This work extends the surprising observation by Magierski, Sekizawa, and Wlazłowski (2016) that the relative phase of pairing condensates strongly affects heavy-ion collisions near the Coulomb barrier, and reconciles it with Anderson's (1986) gedanken experiment on two independently cooled buckets of liquid helium. The authors show that the strength of the pairing interaction determines whether the initial relative phase matters (weak coupling) or is erased by phase locking (strong coupling), clarifying that the weak nuclear pairing (BCS-type, with \(\Delta/\varepsilon_F \ll 1\)) is responsible for the phase sensitivity seen in TDDFT simulations. The results enable a correct interpretation of future TDDFT simulations of colliding superfluid nuclei and suggest that fission fragments emerge entangled when pairing is strong.
