# Effective theories for nuclei at high energies

> We discuss the application of the Color Glass Condensate (CGC), an effective field theory of Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD), to describe high-energy nuclear interactions. We first provide an introduction to the methods and language of the CGC, its role in understanding gluon saturation in heavy-ion collisions at the LHC and RHIC, and its relevance in various scattering processes such as Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS). The application of the CGC effective field theory to describe hadron-hadron collisions is discussed in the scope of asymmetric \textit{dilute-dense} collisions, and Heavy-Ion Collisions in the \textit{dense-dense} limit. The review covers theoretical foundations, recent advancements, and phenomenological applications, focusing on using the CGC to determine the initial conditions of heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The Color Glass Condensate (CGC) effective field theory provides a weak-coupling description of high-energy nuclear scattering in the multi-parton regime at low Bjorken-x, where gluon densities become non-perturbatively large (∼1/α_S), but the review acknowledges that the weight functional W_x[V] encoding correlated scattering is not known a priori and must be modeled at an initial scale x_0 ∼ 0.1, then evolved via JIMWLK/BK evolution and constrained by fits to experimental data.

## Method

The authors review the CGC framework by first establishing the eikonal approximation for high-energy QCD scattering, where interactions are described by light-like Wilson lines V(x_T) that resum multiple gluon exchanges. They then discuss the JIMWLK evolution equation for the statistical weight functional W_x[V] and present the IP-Glasma model as a phenomenologically successful implementation, where nucleon positions are sampled from nuclear structure input, each nucleon contains fluctuating color charge densities concentrated around hot spots, and the resulting color fields are evolved to smaller x via JIMWLK evolution. The review covers applications to deep inelastic scattering (DIS) in the dipole picture and to hadronic collisions in both dilute-dense (forward p+A) and dense-dense (A+A) limits.

## Key numbers

- Saturation scale phenomenology: Q_s^2(x) ∼ x^{-λ} with λ ≈ 0.2–0.3 (Golec-Biernat, 1998)
- Nuclear enhancement of saturation scale: Q_{s,A}^2 ∼ A^{1/3} Q_{s,p}^2 (McLerran, 1993)
- Initial scale for IP-Glasma model: x_0 ∼ 0.1
- JIMWLK evolution illustrated for Pb^{208} over ΔY = 2.4 and ΔY = 4.8 units of rapidity evolution (Schenke, 2016)
- Dipole cross-section in DIS: D(x,b,r) = ⟨(1/N_c) tr[1 - V(b+r/2)V^†(b-r/2)]⟩_x

## Context

This review extends the pedagogical and phenomenological literature on the Color Glass Condensate, building on foundational work by McLerran (1993), the JIMWLK/BK evolution equations (Balitsky, 1995; Kovchegov, 1999; Iancu, 2000), and the IP-Glasma model (Schenke, 2012). It contrasts the CGC approach with collinear factorization, which is limited to large/moderate x where hadronic structure is dominated by few partons, and with linear BFKL evolution (Kuraev, 1976; Balitsky, 1978) that does not include gluon recombination. The review enables future work on using CGC to determine initial conditions for heavy-ion collisions and to probe nuclear structure at very small x, including possible tests of Gribov diffusion and the washing-out of individual nucleon structure at high energies.
