# A Comment on Conical Flow Induced by Heavy-Quark Jets

> The suppression of high transverse momentum particles, recently discovered at RHIC, is commonly interpreted as due to parton energy loss. In high energy nuclear collisions, QCD jets would deposit a large fraction of their energy and into the produced matter. The question of how this energy is degraded and whether we can use this phenomenon to probe the properties of the produced matter is now under active discussion. It has been proposed that if this matter, which is now being referred to as a {\em strongly coupled Quark-Gluon Plasma} (sQGP), may behave as a liquid with a very small viscosity. In this case, a very specific collective excitation should be produced, called the ``conical flow'', similar e.g. to the sonic booms generated by the shock waves produced by supersonic planes. The RHIC experiments seem indeed to be obtaining some indication that the production of particles emitted opposite to a high-$p_t$ jet may actually be peaked away from the quenched jet direction, at an angle roughly consistent with the direction expected in case a shock wave is produced (i.e. orthogonal to the Mach cone). In this note we speculate that for tagged heavy-quark jets one may observe a shrinkage of the Mach cone at moderate $p_t$. The experimental observation of such an effect would be a very good test for the validity of the whole picture currently emerging from the study of partonic matter in nuclear collisions.

# A Comment on Conical Flow Induced by Heavy-Quark Jets

## Key claim

The authors speculate that for tagged heavy-quark jets (specifically b quarks) at moderate transverse momentum, the Mach cone angle should shrink compared to the fixed angle expected for light partons, and that experimental observation of this effect at LHC would test the validity of the conical flow picture emerging from RHIC data. The claim is explicitly hedged as a speculation, with the authors noting the treatment is "very crude" and serves only to estimate the magnitude of the effects.

## Method

The authors apply the linearized hydrodynamics Mach cone formalism from Casalderrey-Solana, Shuryak, and Teaney (2005), extending the standard Mach angle relation cosθ_M = c_s/v_jet to massive partons by substituting v_jet = p/√(p² + m²). They compute the resulting Mach angle as a function of b-quark transverse momentum for two values of the time-averaged speed of sound (c_s = 1/√3 ≈ 0.577 and c_s = 1/2), using a b-quark mass of 4.5 GeV, and discuss experimental feasibility for LHC experiments based on expected b-quark production rates and vertex detector capabilities.

## Key numbers

- For RHIC, the time-weighted speed of sound is estimated as c_s^{RHIC} ≈ 0.33, yielding a conical flow angle of about 1.2 radians (≈ 70 degrees) for light partons.
- For LHC, the time-weighted speed of sound is estimated as c_s^{LHC} = 1/2, giving an asymptotic Mach angle of about 60 degrees for massless partons.
- A b quark with mass 4.5 GeV and momentum 4 GeV has velocity only about 2/3 c.
- For b quarks at LHC, the Mach cone angle shrinks to about 40 degrees for transverse momenta of 3-4 GeV, compared to the asymptotic value of about 60 degrees.
- A b quark becomes subsonic (Mach cone disappears) for transverse momenta of about 3 GeV.
- In Pb-Pb collisions at LHC, about 4.5 b-bbar pairs are expected per central collision from NLO pQCD calculations with ~15% shadowing.
- ALICE expects approximately one b-decay electron with pT > 3 GeV every 50 central (5% cross section) events, including inefficiencies.
- With an impact parameter cut of 200 μm on electrons with pT > 3 GeV, ALICE expects b-decay sample purity of about 90%, yielding more than one such electron every 500 events.
- For a sample of 10^7 central Pb-Pb events (collectable in one month at luminosity 10^27 cm^{-2}s^{-1}), this would yield statistics of over 10^4 tagged electrons.

## Context

This comment extends the conical flow framework developed by Casalderrey-Solana, Shuryak, and Teaney (2005) for light partons to the case of heavy quarks, building on the interpretation of RHIC jet quenching data from STAR and PHENIX that show particle emission peaked away from the quenched jet direction at angles consistent with Mach cone predictions. The work is motivated by the emerging picture of the strongly coupled Quark-Gluon Plasma (sQGP) as a near-perfect liquid with very small viscosity, as discussed by Shuryak and collaborators and by Teaney (2004). The authors propose that LHC experiments (ALICE, ATLAS, CMS) could test this picture by measuring the predicted shrinkage of the Mach cone for b-quark jets, which would also provide a rough measurement of the propagating quark's inertial mass.
