# Direct Inference of Nuclear Equation-of-State Parameters from Gravitational-Wave Observations

> The observation of neutron star mergers with gravitational waves (GWs) has provided a new method to constrain the dense-matter equation of state (EOS) and to better understand its nuclear physics. However, inferring nuclear microphysics from GW observations necessitates the sampling of EOS model parameters that serve as input for each EOS used during the GW data analysis. The sampling of the EOS parameters requires solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations a large number of times -- a process that slows down each likelihood evaluation in the analysis on the order of a few seconds. Here, we employ emulators for the TOV equations built using multilayer perceptron neural networks to enable direct inference of nuclear EOS parameters from GW strain data. Our emulators allow us to rapidly solve the TOV equations, taking in EOS parameters and outputting the associated tidal deformability of a neutron star in only a few tens of milliseconds. We implement these emulators in \texttt{PyCBC} to directly infer the EOS parameters using the event GW170817, providing posteriors on these parameters informed solely by GWs. We benchmark these runs against analyses performed using the full TOV solver and find that the emulators achieve speed ups of nearly \emph{two orders of magnitude}, with negligible differences in the recovered posteriors. Additionally, we constrain the slope and curvature of the symmetry energy at the 90\% upper credible interval to be $L_{\rm sym}\lesssim106$ MeV and $K_{\rm sym}\lesssim26$ MeV.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that neural-network emulators of the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations can replace the full numerical solver during gravitational-wave data analysis, enabling direct inference of nuclear equation-of-state (EOS) parameters from strain data with negligible loss of accuracy, provided the number of live sampling points is sufficiently large (8000 live points) to achieve convergence.

## Method

The authors constructed multilayer perceptron neural-network emulators that take EOS parameters as input and output the tidal deformability of a neutron star on a mass grid between 1 and 2 solar masses, using a bagging ensemble of 100 independently trained networks. They implemented these emulators in the PyCBC gravitational-wave analysis framework to perform Bayesian inference on the GW170817 event, sampling both gravitational-wave parameters (chirp mass, mass ratio, spins, inclination, time offset) and nuclear EOS parameters (K_sat, L_sym, K_sym, and squared sound speeds at discrete density points) simultaneously. The EOS was generated using a metamodel for densities below twice nuclear saturation density and a speed-of-sound model at higher densities, with either 5 or 10 total parameters varied.

## Key numbers

* Speedup of nearly two orders of magnitude: emulator runs required 40 CPU hours for the 5-parameter model versus 3100 CPU hours for the full TOV solver.
* Emulator evaluation time of 0.028 seconds per call compared to 2.4 seconds for the full TOV solver.
* Average emulator uncertainty of 0.1% for the tidal deformability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star.
* Constraint on the slope of the symmetry energy at 90% upper credible interval: L_sym less than or equal to 106 MeV.
* Constraint on the curvature of the symmetry energy at 90% upper credible interval: K_sym less than or equal to 26 MeV.
* Inferred radius of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: 11.8 (+1.1, -0.7) km at 90% credible level.
* Inferred tidal deformability of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: approximately 335 (+362, -113) at 90% credible level.
* Inferred maximum neutron star mass: 2.32 +/- 0.21 solar masses.
* Classifier accuracy for identifying EOS parameter sets producing maximum mass above 2 solar masses: over 99.99%.

## Context

This work extends previous efforts to constrain the neutron-star equation of state from gravitational-wave data, such as the original GW170817 analysis by the LIGO Scientific Collaboration (2017) that placed an upper limit of Lambda less than 800, and subsequent analyses by De (2018) and Capano (2019) that used pregenerated EOS sets. The direct sampling approach overcomes limitations of post-processing EOS inferences, which can introduce systematic uncertainties from limited resolution of pregenerated EOS sets and difficulty implementing different Bayesian priors a posteriori. The emulator framework enables rapid joint inference of nuclear parameters and gravitational-wave parameters, and can be extended to multiple events and more flexible EOS models in future work.
