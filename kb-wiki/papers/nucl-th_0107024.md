# Toroidal dipole resonances in the relativistic random phase approximation

> The isoscalar toroidal dipole strength distributions in spherical nuclei are calculated in the framework of a fully consistent relativistic random phase approximation. It is suggested that the recently observed "low-lying component of the isoscalar dipole mode" might in fact correspond to the toroidal giant dipole resonance. Although predicted by several theoretical models, the existence of toroidal resonances has not yet been confirmed in experiment. The strong mixing between the toroidal resonance and the dipole compression mode might help to explain the large discrepancy between theory and experiment on the position of isoscalar giant dipole resonances.

## Key claim

The paper suggests that the "low-lying component of the isoscalar dipole mode" recently observed in experiments by Clark et al. (2001) might correspond to the toroidal giant dipole resonance (TGDR), rather than being a separate compression mode. The authors propose that strong mixing between the toroidal resonance and the dipole compression mode could explain the large discrepancy (4-5 MeV) between theoretical predictions and experimental measurements of isoscalar giant dipole resonance centroid energies.

## Method

The authors performed fully consistent relativistic random phase approximation (RRPA) calculations for spherical nuclei, using the NL3 effective interaction with nonlinear meson self-interaction terms. The configuration space included both particle-hole pairs and pairs formed from hole states and negative-energy states from the Dirac sea. The toroidal dipole strength distributions were computed using the isoscalar baryon current operator, with spurious center-of-mass motion components removed either by including a subtraction term in the operator or by subtracting the spurious transition current at each energy.

## Key numbers

- For $^{208}$Pb, the toroidal dipole strength distribution shows two broad structures: one at low energies between 8 and 15 MeV, and one in the high-energy region 25-30 MeV
- Experimental centroid energies of the "low-lying component" from Clark et al. (2001): $16.2\pm 0.8$ MeV for $^{90}$Zr, $14.7\pm 0.5$ MeV for $^{116}$Sn, and $12.2\pm 0.6$ MeV for $^{208}$Pb
- In $^{116}$Sn, the four most pronounced toroidal dipole peaks occur at 8.82 MeV, 10.47 MeV, 17.11 MeV, and 30.97 MeV
- The low-energy component observed experimentally follows $E_x \approx 72/A^{1/3}$ MeV, containing 15-28% of the total isoscalar E1 strength
- The higher compression mode follows $E_x \approx 114/A^{1/3}$ MeV, containing approximately 100% of the E1 energy-weighted sum rule
- The NL3 effective interaction used has nuclear matter compressibility modulus $K_{\rm nm} = 271.8$ MeV

## Context

This work extends the relativistic mean-field plus RRPA framework previously applied to isoscalar giant dipole resonances by Vretenar et al. (2000), and directly addresses the experimental findings of Clark et al. (2001) on isoscalar dipole strength in $^{90}$Zr, $^{116}$Sn, and $^{208}$Pb. The toroidal dipole mode was first predicted in a hydrodynamic model by Semenko (1981) with excitation energy $E_x \approx (50-70)/A^{1/3}$ MeV, and later studied in time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory by Balbutsev and Mikhailov (1988). The present RRPA calculations predict that the toroidal strength is concentrated around 10 MeV, significantly lower than the hydrodynamic prediction, and that strong coupling between the toroidal and compression modes causes repulsion that pushes the compression mode to higher energies and the toroidal mode to lower energies, potentially resolving the long-standing discrepancy between theory and experiment for isoscalar giant dipole resonance positions.
