# Solar Neutrinos: Where We Are

> This talk compares standard model predictions for solar neutrino experiments with the results of actual observations. Here `standard model' means the combined standard model of minimal electroweak theory plus a standard solar model. I emphasize the importance of recent analyses in which the neutrino fluxes are treated as free parameters, independent of any constraints from solar models, and the stunning agreement between the predictions of standard solar models and helioseismological measurements.

# Solar Neutrinos: Where We Are

## Key claim
The combined standard model (minimal electroweak theory plus standard solar model) predictions disagree with all four pioneering solar neutrino experiments, and this disagreement persists even when neutrino fluxes are treated as free parameters independent of any solar model constraints. The paper argues that the only remaining viable explanation is new physics beyond the standard electroweak model, such as neutrino oscillations, because helioseismological measurements confirm the standard solar model's sound-speed predictions to better than 0.2% rms.

## Method
The author compares the measured event rates from the chlorine (Homestake), Kamiokande, GALLEX, and SAGE experiments against predictions from the combined standard model, then performs a model-independent analysis in which the neutrino fluxes from different solar nuclear reactions are treated as free parameters (using the known laboratory shapes of neutrino energy spectra) and fit to the experimental data with and without a solar luminosity constraint. The sound-speed profiles from standard solar models are compared with helioseismological measurements from the Global Oscillation Network Group (GONG) and the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO), using models with and without diffusion and with different equations of state.

## Key numbers
- Chlorine experiment measured rate: 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU, compared to predicted 9.5 (+1.2, -1.4) SNU, a factor ~3.6 lower.
- Kamiokande experiment measures neutrinos above 7 MeV; the 8B neutrino contribution to chlorine inferred from Kamiokande data is 3.2 ± 0.45 SNU, exceeding the total observed chlorine rate of 2.55 ± 0.25 SNU.
- Gallium experiments (GALLEX and SAGE) average observed rate: 70.5 ± 7 SNU; standard model predicts 73 SNU from p-p and pep neutrinos alone, leaving no room for the expected 34 ± 4 SNU from 7Be neutrinos.
- Model-independent analysis by Heeger and Robertson (1996) shows inconsistency with standard electroweak theory at 99.5% C.L. with the luminosity constraint, and at 94% C.L. without it.
- Sound-speed agreement between standard solar model with OPAL equation of state and helioseismology: rms discrepancy 0.1% throughout most of the sun.
- Maximum temperature differences allowed by helioseismology: |δT/T| ≤ 0.004 (0.4%).
- 8B neutrino flux scales as T^24, so a 0.2% temperature change produces only a few percent change in flux.
- Mixed solar model of Cumming and Haxton (1996) shows sound-speed discrepancies of +8% to -5%, compared to the 0.1-0.2% rms agreement of standard models.

## Context
This 1997 review extends the long-standing solar neutrino problem first identified by Davis (1964) and Bahcall (1964) at the 2nd Texas Conference, and sharpens the discrepancy using the model-independent analysis method pioneered by Hata, Bludman, and Langacker (1994). The helioseismological comparisons rule out non-standard solar models such as the 3He mixing hypothesis of Cumming and Haxton (1996) and models without diffusion like Turck-Chieze and Lopez (1993), while confirming the standard solar model of Bahcall and Pinsonneault (1995) and Christensen-Dalsgaard et al. (1996). The paper sets the stage for the next generation of electronic detectors (Super-Kamiokande, SNO, Borexino) that would test the MSW neutrino oscillation hypothesis as the preferred solution over solar-model modifications.
