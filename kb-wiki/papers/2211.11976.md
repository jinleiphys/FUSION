# Pion Exchange Interaction in Bonn Potential and Relativistic and Non-relativistic Framework in Nuclear Matter

> As the residual interaction of quantum chromodynamics in low-energy region, the nucleon-nucleon (NN) potential can only be exactly described by the model picture now. In the Bonn potential, one of the most well-known NN interaction models, the nucleons interact with each other through exchanging the pion and several heavier mesons, where the pion plays an essential role. It provides a partial contribution of tensor force in the intermediate-range region and the main component in the long-range region in NN potential. However, it is very difficult to be treated in the nuclear many-body system due to its pseudovector or pseudoscalar property. Recently, three high-precision charge-dependent Bonn potentials were proposed with pseudovector coupling types and different pion-nucleon coupling strengths and applied them to study the properties of nuclear matter and neutron stars in the non-relativistic and relativistic frameworks. Furthermore, to properly deal with the strong short-range repulsion and tensor force of the NN potential, some new relativistic {\it ab initio} methods have also been developed in the past decade to discuss the role of pion and relativistic effects in nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper presents three high-precision charge-dependent Bonn potentials with pseudovector (PV) pion-nucleon coupling (pvCDBonn A, B, and C) that can reproduce Nijmegen partial wave analysis data, but the abstract's claim of applicability to nuclear matter is qualified by the finding that only the relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (RBHF) framework, not the non-relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) framework, yields saturation densities close to the empirical value of 0.16 fm⁻³.

## Method

The authors constructed three charge-dependent Bonn potentials by replacing the original pseudoscalar (PS) pion-nucleon coupling with pseudovector (PV) coupling, varying the pion-nucleon coupling strength (g_π²/4π = 13.9, 13.7, 13.6) and cut-off (Λ_π = 1.12, 1.50, 1.72 GeV) to produce potentials named A, B, and C. They fitted the scalar meson parameters to reproduce the Nijmegen partial wave analysis phase shifts for proton-proton scattering up to E_lab = 300 MeV via the Lippmann-Schwinger equation, then applied these potentials to symmetric nuclear matter and pure neutron matter using both non-relativistic BHF and relativistic RBHF methods with the Bethe-Brueckner-Goldstone equation.

## Key numbers

- Three potentials defined by g_π²/4π values: 13.9 (A), 13.7 (B), 13.6 (C), with corresponding Λ_π = 1.12, 1.50, 1.72 GeV
- Deuteron D-state probability P_D: 4.279% (A), 5.493% (B), 6.152% (C), compared to 4.850% for original CD-Bonn
- Saturation density n_sat from RBHF: 0.189 fm⁻³ (A), 0.155 fm⁻³ (B), 0.137 fm⁻³ (C); empirical value 0.16 ± 0.01 fm⁻³
- Saturation energy E_sat/A from RBHF: -16.69 MeV (A), -13.81 MeV (B), -12.42 MeV (C); empirical value -16 ± 1 MeV
- Incompressibility K from RBHF: 280 MeV (A), 182 MeV (B), 144 MeV (C); empirical value 240 ± 20 MeV
- Symmetry energy E_sym at saturation: 34.90 MeV (A), 28.99 MeV (B), 26.07 MeV (C); empirical value 31.7 ± 3.2 MeV
- Slope of symmetry energy L at saturation: 77.34 MeV (A), 49.86 MeV (B), 39.01 MeV (C); empirical value 58.7 ± 28.1 MeV
- Scattering length a(np) for spin singlet: -23.711 fm (A), -23.757 fm (B), -23.752 fm (C); empirical -23.749(20) fm

## Context

This work extends the charge-dependent Bonn (CD-Bonn) potential of Machleidt (2001) by replacing its pseudoscalar pion-nucleon coupling with pseudovector coupling, motivated by the finding of Fuchs (1998) that PS coupling produces excessive attraction in relativistic calculations. The three pvCDBonn potentials enable systematic study of pion tensor force effects in nuclear matter, showing that the relativistic RBHF method (Anastasio 1983, Horowitz 1987, Brockmann 1990) can reproduce empirical saturation properties without three-body forces, unlike non-relativistic BHF which gives saturation densities twice the empirical value. The results enable future application of these potentials to finite nuclei and neutron star calculations within relativistic ab initio methods.
