# Electron capture strength on odd-A nucleus $\mathbf{^{59}}$Co in explosive astrophysical environment

> The Gamow-Teller (GT) transitions within massive stars play sumptuous role in the dynamics of core collapse supernovae. GT strength distributions and electron capture rates have been calculated for odd-A nucleus $^{59}$Co within the proton-neutron quasiparticles random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) formalism. The pn-QRPA results are compared with other model calculations and (n, p) reaction experiment carried out at TRIUMF charge-exchange facility. The pn-QRPA calculated a total $B(GT_{+})$ strength of 3.3 for $^{59}$Co to be compared with the shell model value of 2.5 and the 1.9 $\pm$ 0.1 in the (n, p) charge-exchange reaction. Aufderheide et. al. \cite{Aufderheide93} extracted total strength equaling 2.4 $\pm$ 0.3. The placement of GT centroid at 5.6 MeV by the pn-QRPA model is in reasonable agreement with the shell model centroid at 5.1 MeV whereas the measured GT centroid was placed at 4.4 $\pm$ 0.3 MeV in the (n, p) experiment. Fuller, Fowler and Newman (FFN) \cite{FFN80, FFN82, FFN82b}, placed the GT centroid at too low excitation energy of 2.0 MeV in the daughter nucleus $^{59}$Fe, and this misplacement led to the enhancement of FFN rates. The suppressed pn-QRPA and shell model electron capture rates are in good agreement with each other. The rates are suggestive of higher value of $Y_{e}$ (electron-to-baryon ratio) and may contribute to a more massive homologously collapsing core resulting in a more energetic shock. It might be interesting for the simulators to check the effect of these suppressed rates on the fine-tuning of the time rate of $Y_{e}$, the concomitant heavy element nucleosynthesis, and, on the energetics of the subsequent shock wave.

## Key claim

The pn-QRPA model calculates a total B(GT+) strength of 3.3 for 59Co, which is larger than both the shell model value of 2.5 and the experimentally measured value of 1.9 ± 0.1 from the (n,p) charge-exchange reaction, yet the resulting electron capture rates are suppressed relative to the earlier FFN rates and in good agreement with shell model rates.

## Method

The authors used the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) formalism with a model space of 7ħω to calculate Gamow-Teller (GT) strength distributions and electron capture rates for the odd-A nucleus 59Co. The calculation included both particle-hole and particle-particle GT forces, with a quenching factor of 0.66 applied to the axial-vector coupling constant ratio (gA/gV). The resulting GT strength distribution was compared with experimental data from the (n,p) charge-exchange reaction at the TRIUMF facility and with large-scale shell model calculations.

## Key numbers

- Total B(GT+) strength for 59Co: pn-QRPA calculated 3.3, compared to shell model value of 2.5, experimental (n,p) value of 1.9 ± 0.1, and Aufderheide et al. extracted value of 2.4 ± 0.3
- GT centroid placement: pn-QRPA at 5.6 MeV in daughter 59Fe, shell model at 5.1 MeV, experimental (n,p) centroid at 4.4 ± 0.3 MeV, and FFN centroid at 2.0 MeV
- Shell model electron capture rates are smaller than pn-QRPA rates by a factor of 8 to 20 at low temperature and density conditions
- At high density (ρYe = 10^11 g/cm^3), pn-QRPA rates are suppressed by roughly an order of magnitude compared to FFN rates, and by a factor of two compared to shell model rates

## Context

This work extends the microscopic calculation of GT strength distributions and electron capture rates for fp-shell nuclei relevant to core-collapse supernovae, following the earlier FFN calculations (Fuller, Fowler, and Newman, 1980-1982) which placed the GT centroid at too low excitation energy and used Brink's hypothesis. The pn-QRPA results are compared with large-scale shell model calculations by Langanke (2000) and Caurier (1999), and with experimental (n,p) data from Alford (1993) at TRIUMF. The suppressed electron capture rates suggest a higher electron-to-baryon ratio (Ye) in stellar cores, which may lead to a more massive homologously collapsing core and a more energetic shock wave, providing input for supernova simulators to test the effect on nucleosynthesis and shock dynamics.
