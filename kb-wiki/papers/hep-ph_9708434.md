# One-loop self energies at finite temperature

> The complete one-loop self energies (real and imaginary parts) for photons, gluons, electrons and quarks at finite temperature are calculated numerically and compared to the results of the hard thermal loop (HTL) approximation used for the resummation technique of Braaten and Pisarski. In this way some light is shed on the validity of the weak coupling limit assumption ($g \ll 1$) or equivalently the high temperature assumption, on which the HTL approximation is based. Furthermore, the gauge dependence of the fermion self energy beyond the HTL approximation is considered. Finally the dispersion relations following from the real part of the self energies are compared to the HTL results.

## Key claim

The hard thermal loop (HTL) approximation, which assumes weak coupling (g << 1) or equivalently high temperature (T >> p0, p), is qualitatively correct for a large momentum regime even for large coupling values (g > 1) or temperatures of order p, provided that the four-momentum squared P^2 = p0^2 - p^2 is soft rather than requiring both p0 and p individually to be soft.

## Method

The authors numerically evaluated the complete one-loop self energies (real and imaginary parts) for photons, gluons, electrons, and quarks at finite temperature and zero chemical potential using the imaginary time formalism with Saclay representation of propagators. They performed the Matsubara frequency sums analytically, integrated over the angle analytically, and carried out the remaining momentum integral numerically. The results were compared to the analytic HTL expressions of Braaten and Pisarski (1990) and Weldon (1982), and for the fermion case the gauge dependence was studied by introducing a general covariant gauge with gauge fixing parameter xi.

## Key numbers

- Thermal photon mass: m_gamma = eT/3
- Thermal gluon mass: m_g = gT/sqrt(3)
- Thermal fermion mass squared: m_f^2 = C_R * g^2 T^2 / 8, where C_R = 4/3 for quarks
- For the photon dispersion relation with coupling extrapolated to e = 3, the difference between one-loop and HTL results is less than 5%
- For the quark dispersion relation with g = 3, the maximum deviation between one-loop and HTL results is of order 10%
- The HTL approximation is considered useful for realistic coupling values g = 1.5 to 2.5 at temperatures T = 200 to 300 MeV accessible in relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Context

This work extends the HTL resummation technique of Braaten and Pisarski (1990) by computing the full one-loop self energies without the high-temperature approximation, going beyond earlier work by Toimela (1986) who evaluated only the next term in the high-temperature expansion for the gluon self energy. The results justify the use of HTL effective masses in equation-of-state models for a gluon gas by Peshier (1996) and for strange quark matter by Schertler (1997), and validate the use of the HTL resummed quark propagator for soft four-momenta in calculations of hard photon production rates from the quark-gluon plasma by Kapusta, Lichard, and Seibert (1991). The paper also reveals that the one-loop gluon self energy in Feynman gauge has positive imaginary parts above the light cone, which historically motivated the development of the Braaten-Pisarski method.
