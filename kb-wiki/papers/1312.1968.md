# Koppe's Work of 1948: A fundamental for non-equilibrium rate of particle production

> In 1948, Koppe formulated an almost complete recipe for statistical-thermal models including particle production, formation and decay of resonances, temporal and thermal evolution of the interacting system, statistical approaches and equilibrium condition in final state of the nuclear interaction. As the rate of particle production was one of the basic assumptions, recalling Koppe's work would be an essential input to be involved in the statistical prediction of non-equilibrium particle production in recent and future ultra-relativistic collisions.

## Key claim

Koppe (1948) provided an almost complete statistical-thermal recipe for particle production, resonance formation and decay, and thermal evolution of the interacting system, but the original work was limited to estimating a very small number of produced mesons in alpha-nucleus collisions at Betatron energy, with the rate formula depending on an exponential suppression factor.

## Method

Koppe treated the produced particle gas as cavity radiation, relating energy density and radiation loss to the cross-section of excited nuclei. He derived a rate equation for particle production as a function of time-dependent temperature, then integrated it to obtain the total number of produced mesons per unit time, using the excitation energy relation \(U = m_2/(m_1+m_2)^2 E\) and the temperature relation \(T_0 = 3.8\sqrt{N}\), where \(N\) is the number of excited resonances.

## Key numbers

- Temperature of the excited nucleus was measured as \(T_0 \sim 10\) MeV, related to excitation energy by \(T_0 = 3.8\sqrt{N}\).
- The number of mesons produced in \(\alpha\)-A collisions at 380 MeV per unit time was estimated as \(\sim 1.7 \times 10^{-4}\).
- The integration constant in the yield formula \(n = a (m_1+m_2) T_0 \exp(-m_b c^2 / T_0)\) was given as \(a = 0.031\).

## Context

This paper recalls and translates Koppe's 1948 work, which predates modern statistical-thermal models of heavy-ion collisions. The rate formula and non-equilibrium approach are positioned as a fundamental input for studying particle production in ultra-relativistic collisions, particularly for solving the Fokker-Planck or Boltzmann-Vlasov master equation with gain and loss terms. The work enables qualitative estimation of non-equilibrium particle production rates in quark-gluon plasma studies, extending earlier statistical approaches by Koppe (1948) and connecting to more recent work by Tawfik (2010) on transport properties.
