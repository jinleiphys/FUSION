# Branching Ratios in Proton Antiproton Annihilation at Rest from Large Nc QCD

> We use classical or large Nc QCD to describe the mesons (pion, rho, omega) coming from proton-antiproton annihilation at rest as classical fields, which we then quantize as coherent states. This treatment gives a nearly parameter free account of the pion branching ratios in annihilation.

# Branching Ratios in Proton Antiproton Annihilation at Rest from Large Nc QCD

## Key claim
Large Nc (classical) QCD, with meson fields treated as classical fields and then quantized as coherent states, gives a nearly parameter-free account of pion branching ratios in proton-antiproton annihilation at rest, provided the size of the annihilation region is first fixed by fitting the single-pion momentum spectrum.

## Method
The authors start with a spherically symmetric blob of pionic matter with total energy equal to two nucleon masses and zero baryon number, parameterized by a single range parameter a. They evolve the pion, rho, and omega fields using classical dynamical equations from large Nc QCD, with vector mesons treated as massive Yang-Mills fields gauging the U_V(2) symmetry of the nonlinear sigma model. The asymptotic fields are then quantized as coherent states projected onto good isospin and four-momentum, from which branching probabilities into all possible combinations of pions, rho mesons, and omega mesons are computed.

## Key numbers
- The average pion number in proton-antiproton annihilation at rest is about 5 with a variance of 1 (experimental value).
- The range parameter a = 1.1 fm gives a good fit to the experimental pion momentum distribution and yields a mean pion number of 5 with variance 1.
- The vector meson masses are taken as m_rho = m_omega = 770 MeV.
- The pion decay constant is fixed at f_pi = 75 MeV.
- The percentage of secondary pions from resonance decays is 33% experimentally; the calculation finds about 30%.
- For the isospin mix, the authors assume equal amounts of I=0 and I=1, noting results would change very little with a 63% I=0 and 37% I=1 mix as suggested by other work.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous large Nc QCD treatment of annihilation using only pions in the Skyrme model (Lu and Amado, earlier work) and their subsequent inclusion of the omega meson. It now adds the rho meson and makes direct comparison with experimental branching ratio data from Amsler and Dover. The approach contrasts with the traditional thermal fireball decay picture, treating annihilation as a coherent burst of classical radiation. The method enables future studies of annihilation in flight, Bose-Einstein correlations, strange particle channels via SU(3) flavor symmetry, and spin-charge correlations from polarized protons.
