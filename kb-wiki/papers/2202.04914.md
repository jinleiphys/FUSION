# Random-Matrix Approach to Transition-State Theory

> To model a complex system intrinsically separated by a barrier, we use two random Hamiltonians, coupled to each other either by a tunneling matrix element or by an intermediate transition state. We study that model in the universal limit of large matrix dimension. We calculate the average probability for transition from scattering channel coupled to the first Hamiltonian to a scattering channel coupled to the second Hamiltonian. Using only the assumption that the sum of transmission coefficients of channels coupled to the second Hamiltonian is large we retrieve transition-state theory in its general form. For tunneling through a very thick barrier independence of formation and decay of the tunneling process hold more generally.

# Random-Matrix Approach to Transition-State Theory

## Key claim

Transition-state theory (independence of formation and decay) holds for a random-matrix model of barrier penetration if the sum of transmission coefficients for channels coupled to the second Hamiltonian is large (∑_{b'} T_{b'} ≫ 1), or for a very thick barrier under the weaker condition that the square roots of those sums are substantially larger than the dimensionless tunneling matrix element.

## Method

The author constructs two random-matrix Hamiltonians, each drawn from the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) of dimension N, coupled either by a rank-one tunneling matrix element (modeling barrier penetration) or via an intermediate single state (modeling a transition state above the barrier). Open scattering channels are coupled to each Hamiltonian by real coupling matrix elements. The average transition probability ⟨P_{ab}⟩ from an entrance channel a feeding the first Hamiltonian to an exit channel b coupled to the second Hamiltonian is calculated analytically in the limit N → ∞ using the supersymmetry approach, with the fluctuations of the key random variables ξ₁ and ξ₂ characterized by their variances expressed as threefold integrals over transmission coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The average level density of each GOE Hamiltonian is ρ(E) = (N/πλ)(1 − [E/(2λ)]²)^{1/2}, with spectra ranging from −2λ to +2λ.
- The average ⟨ξ₁⟩ = −i, with magnitude unity and independent of Γ₁.
- For ∑_{a'} T_{a'} ≫ 1, the variance of ξ₁ is ⟨|ξ₁|²⟩ − 1 = 2/(∑_{a'} T_{a'}).
- For ∑_{a'} T_{a'} ≫ 1, the variance of the formation amplitude equals T_a/(∑_{a'} T_{a'}).
- The dimensionless tunneling matrix element is defined as Ṽ = V/λ, and the spreading widths are Γ^↓₁ = (2π/d) V̅²₁ and Γ^↓₂ = (2π/d) V̅²₂, where d = πλ/N is the mean GOE level spacing at E = 0.

## Context

This work extends the statistical model of Bertsch and Hagino (2021) and Hagino and Bertsch (2021) for transition-state processes, correcting an issue where those models lacked a realistic GOE limit with non-vanishing fluctuations as N → ∞. The random-matrix approach provides a universal tool for modeling complex quantum systems without specific dynamical assumptions, following the framework of Weidenmüller (1984) for scattering matrices and Verbaarschot (1985, 1986) for analytic calculations of mean values and variances. The results enable a rigorous foundation for transition-state theory in nuclear fission (Bohr and Wheeler, 1939; Krappe and Pomorski, 2012) and physical chemistry (Truhlar, 1996), showing precisely when the factorization of formation and decay probabilities holds.
