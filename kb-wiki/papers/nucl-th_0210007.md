# On Hydrodynamical Description of Thermal Photons

> The WA98 collaboration in the CERN SPS has reported an excess of photons over those originating from the decays of final hadrons in the lead-lead collisions. These photons can originate either from primary interactions of partons from colliding nuclei or from secondary interactions among produced particles. Photons produced in the secondary interactions, often called thermal photons, can be calculated by using thermal production rates and equilibrium hydrodynamics for the evolution of the expanding matter. I will review the main features of hydrodynamical studies for the WA98 data. The data can be reproduced both with or without a phase transition to the QGP, but high initial temperature, over the values predicted for the phase transition temperature, is required by the data. I will also show a prediction for the photon excess for central gold-gold collisions at the Brookhaven RHIC collider. In this prediction, the initial state for the hydrodynamical expansion is obtained from a perturbative QCD calculation.

## Key claim

The WA98 excess photon data can be reproduced both with or without a phase transition to the quark-gluon plasma, but high initial temperatures well above the predicted critical temperature of 150-180 MeV are required by the data in all cases.

## Method

The author reviews three hydrodynamical approaches to the WA98 lead-lead collision data: boost-invariant (1+1)-dimensional hydrodynamics with zero initial radial velocity (Srivastava and Sinha 2002), boost-invariant hydrodynamics with non-zero initial radial velocity (Peressounko and Petrov 2002), and (2+1)-dimensional hydrodynamics with finite longitudinal extent (Omana 2002). Thermal photon emission rates are calculated using parametrizations from Xiong et al. (1999) and Nadeau et al. (1999) for hadron gas, and 2-loop or resummed rates for QGP, with pQCD prompt photon contributions added from Wong and Wang (1998) or Dumitru et al. (2002). For the RHIC prediction, the initial state is obtained from a pQCD + saturation model that determines the initial time and energy density without free parameters.

## Key numbers

- Average initial temperature T_ini = 335 MeV required to reproduce WA98 data with boost-invariant hydrodynamics and zero initial radial velocity at thermalization time tau_0 = 0.2 fm/c (Srivastava and Sinha 2002)
- With non-zero initial radial velocity, initial temperature can be lowered to 180-250 MeV with thermalization times tau_0 = 0.7-2.0 fm/c and maximum radial velocities V_max = 0.26-0.40 c (Peressounko and Petrov 2002)
- In (2+1)-dimensional hydrodynamics, maximum initial temperature T_max = 325 MeV (with QGP phase transition) or 245 MeV (purely hadronic scenario) reproduces WA98 data (Omana 2002)
- For central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 AGeV at RHIC, the pQCD + saturation model gives tau_0 = 0.17 fm/c, leading to maximum initial temperature T_max = 580 MeV and average initial temperature T_ini = 340 MeV
- Increasing initial time from tau_0 = 0.17 to 1.0 fm/c at RHIC reduces thermal photon emission by a factor of 20 at k_t = 4 GeV
- Thermal photon emission at RHIC is roughly a factor of 100 higher than at SPS at k_t = 4 GeV

## Context

This review extends the hydrodynamical studies of the WA98 excess photon data by Srivastava and Sinha (2002), Peressounko and Petrov (2002), and Omana (2002), and provides a prediction for RHIC energies using the pQCD + saturation model of Eskola, Kajantie, Ruuskanen, and Tuominen (2000). The work highlights the ambiguity in distinguishing QGP formation from purely hadronic scenarios due to uncertainties in initial conditions, particularly the thermalization time and initial radial velocity. The RHIC prediction enables experimental tests by the PHENIX collaboration, where a change in the slope of the direct photon spectrum in the region k_t ~ 2.0-3.5 GeV could help constrain the initial time, though pQCD photons from Dumitru et al. (2002) may dominate at higher transverse momenta.
