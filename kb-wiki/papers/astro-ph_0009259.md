# How the sun shines

> This historical essay describes the struggle to understand how the sun shines. The story begins with the disagreement between Kelvin and Darwin over the age of the sun in the middle of the 19th century and continues through the revelations of solar neutrino experiments at the conclusion of the 20th century.

# How the Sun Shines

## Key claim

This historical essay argues that the resolution of the 19th-century debate over the age and energy source of the Sun required discoveries from seemingly unrelated fields of physics, radioactivity, relativity, quantum mechanics, and nuclear physics, and that solar neutrino experiments, while confirming nuclear fusion as the Sun's energy source, revealed a new puzzle about neutrino properties that remains unresolved at the time of writing.

## Method

The paper traces the historical development of solar physics from the 1854 gravitational contraction hypothesis of von Helmholtz through Kelvin's 1862 calculation of a 30-million-year solar lifetime, the 1896 discovery of radioactivity by Becquerel, Aston's 1920 mass measurements showing four hydrogen nuclei are heavier than one helium nucleus, Eddington's 1920 proposal that hydrogen-to-helium conversion powers stars, Gamow's 1928 quantum-mechanical tunneling formula, and Bethe's 1938 calculation of the p-p chain and CNO cycle as the specific nuclear processes. The author then describes the 1964 proposal by Bahcall and Davis to test nuclear burning via chlorine-based solar neutrino detection, the 1968 Davis experiment results showing fewer neutrinos than predicted, and subsequent experiments (Kamiokande, SAGE, GALLEX, Super-Kamiokande) that confirmed the deficit.

## Key numbers

- Kelvin's 1862 gravitational calculation yielded a solar lifetime of only **30 million years**, based on dividing the Sun's gravitational energy by its radiative output
- Chemical energy could sustain the Sun for only about **3,000 years**, as Kelvin noted in 1862
- Darwin's 1859 estimate of the age of the Earth from erosion of the Weald was **300 million years**
- The conversion of hydrogen to helium releases about **0.7%** of the mass equivalent as energy, according to Einstein's relation
- Bethe's calculated central temperature of the Sun was within **20%** of the modern value of **16 million degrees Kelvin**
- Radioactive dating of meteorites shows the Sun is **4.6 billion years old**
- The predicted solar neutrino arrival rate depends approximately upon the **25th power** of the central temperature of the Sun
- The agreement between observed and predicted solar neutrino rates is within a factor of **three**
- Studies of solar vibrations show standard solar model predictions for central temperatures are consistent with observations to an accuracy of at least **0.1%**

## Context

This essay extends the historical narrative of solar energy research from Kelvin and Helmholtz (1854-1862) through Darwin (1859) and the geologists who argued for much longer timescales, to the resolution provided by nuclear physics. The author situates his own work with Davis (1964) and the subsequent solar neutrino experiments as the empirical confirmation of Bethe's (1938) nuclear fusion theory, while also noting that the neutrino deficit, first proposed as an oscillation phenomenon by Gribov and Pontecorvo (1969) and extended by Wolfenstein (1978) and Mikheyev and Smirnov (1985), points to physics beyond the standard model of particle physics. The paper enables readers to understand how the 1998 Super-Kamiokande atmospheric neutrino oscillation results provided indirect support for the solar neutrino oscillation hypothesis.
