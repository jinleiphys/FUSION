# Excited-state quantum phase transitions in a two-fluid Lipkin model

> Background: Composed systems have became of great interest in the framework of the ground state quantum phase transitions (QPTs) and many of their properties have been studied in detail. However, in these systems the study of the so called excited-state quantum phase transitions (ESQPTs) have not received so much attention. Purpose: A quantum analysis of the ESQPTs in the two-fluid Lipkin model is presented in this work. The study is performed through the Hamiltonian diagonalization for selected values of the control parameters in order to cover the most interesting regions of the system phase diagram. [Method:] A Hamiltonian that resembles the consistent-Q Hamiltonian of the interacting boson model (IBM) is diagonalized for selected values of the parameters and properties such as the density of states, the Peres lattices, the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution, and the participation ratio are analyzed. Results: An overview of the spectrum of the two-fluid Lipkin model for selected positions in the phase diagram has been obtained. The location of the excited-state quantum phase transition can be easily singled out with the Peres lattice, with the nearest-neighbor spacing distribution, with Poincaré sections or with the participation ratio. Conclusions: This study completes the analysis of QPTs for the two-fluid Lipkin model, extending the previous study to excited states. The ESQPT signatures in composed systems behave in the same way as in single ones, although the evidences of their presence can be sometimes blurred. The Peres lattice turns out to be a convenient tool to look into the position of the ESQPT and to define the concept of phase in the excited states realm.

## Key claim

The authors extend the previous ground-state quantum phase transition (QPT) analysis of the two-fluid Lipkin model (Garcia-Ramos, 2016) to excited states, showing that excited-state quantum phase transitions (ESQPTs) occur in this composed system and that their signatures behave similarly to those in single-fluid systems, although the evidence can sometimes be blurred by the presence of an extra quantum number (j) that splits the spectrum into families.

## Method

The authors diagonalized a Hamiltonian inspired by the consistent-Q formalism of the interacting boson model for the two-fluid Lipkin model, using N1=N2=70 bosons (Hilbert space dimension 5041). They analyzed the density of states, Peres lattices (plotting ⟨n_t1⟩/N1 versus excitation energy), nearest-neighbor spacing distributions, Poincaré sections from the classical limit, and the participation ratio in the u1(1)⊗u2(1) basis to locate ESQPTs for selected values of the control parameters x, y, and y′.

## Key numbers

- System size: N1 = N2 = 70 bosons, giving a Hilbert space dimension of 71 × 71 = 5041.
- For the case y=0, y′=0, x=1/2 (point A in the phase diagram, deformed phase after a second-order QPT at x_c=4/5): the density of states shows peaks at energies around E≈0 for individual j-families, shifting to E≈30 for the smallest j-values when all states are summed.
- For the same case, the Peres lattice shows sharp dips at energies around E≈0 for each j-family, shifting to E≈30 for decreasing j.
- The participation ratio exhibits a sharp minimum at the ESQPT energy for each j-family, with the minimum position moving toward higher energies for decreasing j-values.

## Context

This work completes the analysis of QPTs for the two-fluid Lipkin model begun by Garcia-Ramos (2016), extending it to excited states. The ESQPT concept was first coined by Cejnar (2006) and studied in detail by Caprio (2008), who showed that QPTs propagate to excited states as singularities in the density of states. The present study shows that the two-fluid Lipkin model exhibits ESQPTs analogous to those in single-fluid systems, with the Peres lattice emerging as a convenient tool to locate the ESQPT and define the concept of phase in excited states. The results enable future studies of ESQPTs in other composed bosonic systems, such as the proton-neutron interacting boson model.
