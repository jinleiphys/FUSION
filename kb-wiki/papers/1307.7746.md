# Symmetry Energy Constraints from Giant Resonances: A Theoretical Overview

> Giant resonances encapsulate the dynamic response of the nuclear ground state to external perturbations. As such, they offer a unique view of the nucleus that is often not accessible otherwise. Although interesting in their own right, giant resonances are also enormously valuable in providing stringent constraints on the equation of state of asymmetric matter. We this view in mind, we focus on two modes of excitation that are essential in reaching this goal: the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (GMR) and the isovector giant dipole resonance (GDR). GMR energies in heavy nuclei are sensitive to the symmetry energy because they probe the incompressibility of neutron-rich matter. Unfortunately, access to the symmetry energy is hindered by the relatively low neutron-proton asymmetry of stable nuclei. Thus, the measurement of GMR energies in exotic nuclei is strongly encouraged. In the case of the GDR, we find the electric dipole polarizability of paramount importance. Indeed, the electric dipole polarizability appears as one of two laboratory observables -- with the neutron-skin thickness being the other -- that are highly sensitive to the density dependence of the symmetry energy. Finally, we identify the softness of skin and the nature of the pygmy resonance as important unsolved problems in nuclear structure.

## Key claim

Giant resonances, specifically the isoscalar giant monopole resonance (GMR) and the isovector giant dipole resonance (GDR), can provide stringent constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, but access to the symmetry energy from GMR energies is hindered by the relatively low neutron-proton asymmetry of stable nuclei, so measurements in exotic nuclei are strongly encouraged.

## Method

The author reviews the relativistic random phase approximation (RPA) formalism required to compute the distribution of isoscalar monopole and isovector dipole strength in finite nuclei. This involves solving self-consistent mean-field equations for the nuclear ground state, then computing the linear response to external perturbations by iterating the lowest-order polarization tensor to all orders using Dyson's equation. The residual interaction used in the RPA is identical to the one used to generate the mean-field ground state, ensuring consistency and the conservation of fundamental symmetries.

## Key numbers

- The saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is given as ρ₀ = 0.148 fm⁻³, with a corresponding radius parameter r₀ = 1.17 fm.
- The binding energy per nucleon of symmetric nuclear matter at saturation is ε₀ ≈ −16 MeV, and the symmetry energy coefficient is J ≈ 32 MeV.
- The neutron-skin thickness of ²⁰⁸Pb measured by the PREX experiment is r_skin²⁰⁸ = 0.33⁺⁰·¹⁶₋₀·₁₈ fm.
- For ¹³²Sn, the neutron-proton asymmetry squared is α² = (0.242)² = 0.059.

## Context

This theoretical overview extends the liquid-drop model of Bethe and Weizsäcker by incorporating the response of the nucleus to density fluctuations, moving beyond the static mass formula that constrains only the EOS at saturation density. The work builds on the relativistic mean-field approach of Walecka (1974) and the subsequent introduction of scalar meson self-interactions by Boguta and Bodmer (1977) to correct the incompressibility coefficient. The paper identifies the electric dipole polarizability and the neutron-skin thickness as the two laboratory observables most sensitive to the density dependence of the symmetry energy, and points to the softness of skin and the nature of the pygmy resonance as important unsolved problems in nuclear structure.
