# Shock propagation and stability in causal dissipative hydrodynamics

> We studied the shock propagation and its stability with the causal dissipative hydrodynamics in 1+1 dimensional systems. We show that the presence of the usual viscosity is not enough to stabilize the solution. This problem is solved by introducing an additional viscosity which is related to the coarse-graining scale of the theory.

## Key claim

The authors show that standard bulk viscosity alone is insufficient to stabilize shock wave solutions in causal dissipative hydrodynamics; stability requires an additional viscosity term whose origin is linked to the coarse-graining scale introduced by the numerical method (smoothed particle hydrodynamics, SPH).

## Method

The authors numerically solve 1+1 dimensional causal dissipative hydrodynamics using the smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH) method, which introduces a coarse-graining scale \(h\) via a kernel function. They study the time evolution of a Landau-type initial condition (uniform temperature 200 MeV within \(x \in [-1,1]\) fm, with surface thickness \(10h\)) for a massless ideal gas equation of state (\(p = \epsilon/3\)), using the memory function formalism (equivalent to Israel-Stewart theory in 1+1 dimensions) with bulk viscosity parametrized as \(\zeta = a s\) and relaxation time \(\tau_R = b \zeta/(\epsilon + p)\).

## Key numbers

* The coarse-graining scale is set to \(h = 0.01\) fm, with the surface thickness of the initial distribution taken as \(10h\).
* Two values of the viscosity parameter \(a\) are used: \(a = 0.1\) and \(a = 1.0\), with fixed \(b = 6\).
* The initial temperature is 200 MeV, and the initial fluid occupies the region \(x \in [-1, 1]\) fm.
* The maximum signal propagation speed in the causal theory is \(v_M = \sqrt{1/b + \alpha}\), where \(\alpha = dp/d\varepsilon\) is the square of the ideal sound speed.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of relativistic dissipative hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart theory, memory function method of Denicol, Kodama, Koide, Mota) by addressing the behavior of solutions with discontinuities and large viscosity, relevant for heavy-ion collisions at LHC energies. The authors show that standard viscosity fails to stabilize shock fronts, and they introduce an additional viscosity tied to the SPH coarse-graining scale \(h\), which they interpret as the physical mean-free path of partons in QGP (approximately 0.1 fm). This enables future studies of shock phenomena in heavy-ion collisions, such as those from high-energy jet propagation or first-order QCD phase transitions.
