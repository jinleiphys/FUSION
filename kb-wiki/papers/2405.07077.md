# A Potential Model Study of the Nucleon's Charge and Mass Radius

> We study the charge and mass distributions within a nucleon and compute the associated squared radii based on a potential model approach. Different constituent quark configurations such as $Δ$, $Y$, and quark-diquark are considered and compared, with model parameters calibrated by experimental measurements of the proton and neutron charge radius. The results suggest that while the charge radius is dictated by quark dynamics, the mass radius is strongly influenced by nonperturbative QCD contributions to a nucleon's mass that are not sensitive to the constituent quarks. As a result, the mass radius could become substantially different from the charge radius. The obtained nucleon mass distributions of different configurations are further used for simulations of the initial conditions in heavy ion collisions. The computed eccentricities $\varepsilon_2$ and $\varepsilon_3$ are found to demonstrate a considerable sensitivity to the input nucleon profiles, especially to the mass radius in the peripheral region as well as for systems with fewer participants.

# A Potential Model Study of the Nucleon's Charge and Mass Radius

## Key claim

The charge radius is dictated by quark dynamics, but the mass radius is strongly influenced by nonperturbative QCD contributions to a nucleon's mass that are not sensitive to the constituent quarks; as a result, the mass radius could become substantially different from the charge radius, with the offset energy term (representing vacuum contributions) being the primary driver of this difference rather than the quark wavefunction size.

## Method

The authors solve the nonrelativistic Schrodinger equation for three constituent quarks using hyper-spherical harmonics, considering three quark configurations: the Delta model (pairwise Cornell potential), the Y model (pairwise Coulomb plus three-body confining term via a central junction), and a quark-diquark model. Model parameters (constituent quark mass m and offset energy V0) are calibrated by reproducing the experimental proton charge radius of 0.84 fm, with a Foldy-Wouthuysen smearing correction applied to account for relativistic zitterbewegung of light quarks. The energy density distribution is constructed from kinetic, potential (Coulomb and confining), and offset energy contributions, with the offset energy distributed as a Gaussian around the center-of-mass with variable width w.

## Key numbers

- Proton charge radius fixed to 0.84 fm for model calibration
- Constituent quark mass m = 0.2744 GeV for the Delta model and m = 0.2668 GeV for the Y model
- Offset energy V0 = 1.103 GeV for the Delta model and V0 = 1.225 GeV for the Y model
- String tension sigma = 0.18 GeV^2 and Coulomb parameter kappa = 0.3, consistent with lattice simulations of heavy quarkonia (Bali 2001, Mateu 2019)
- Flux tube perpendicular radius a = 0.1 fm used for potential energy spatial distribution
- Neutron squared charge radius (-0.110 +/- 0.008) fm^2 from Atac (2021) used as additional constraint
- Proton mass M = 0.938 GeV used to fix the ground state energy

## Context

This work extends potential model studies of nucleon structure (Richard 1992, Carlson 1983) by explicitly computing both charge and mass distributions within a unified framework. It addresses the current tension between mass radius values extracted from near-threshold J/Psi photoproduction at JLab (suggesting approximately 0.5 fm, as in Kharzeev 2021, Mamo 2021, Duran 2023) and those preferred by Bayesian analyses of heavy ion collision data (some well above the charge radius, as in Bernhard 2019, JETSCAPE 2021, Parkkila 2021, Parkkila 2022). The computed nucleon mass distributions are used for simulations of initial conditions in heavy ion collisions, finding that eccentricities epsilon_2 and epsilon_3 show considerable sensitivity to the input nucleon profiles, especially to the mass radius in the peripheral region and for systems with fewer participants. The work also incorporates the neutron charge radius constraint from Atac (2021) to motivate a quark-diquark configuration.
