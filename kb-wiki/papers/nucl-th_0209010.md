# Model of multifragmentation, Equation of State and phase transition

> We consider a soluble model of multifragmentation which is similar in spirit to many models which have been used to fit intermediate energy heavy ion collision data. We draw a p-V diagram for the model and compare with a p-V diagram obtained from a mean-field theory. We investigate the question of chemical instability in the multifragmentation model. Phase transitions in the model are discussed.

## Key claim

The thermodynamic model of multifragmentation, which includes surface energy and symmetry energy terms and allows for many cluster species, produces an equation of state that is nearly flat (with almost no region of negative compressibility) and naturally yields isospin fractionation, in contrast to mean-field models which require Maxwell construction to eliminate mechanical instability and a bifurcation in isotopic space to produce isospin fractionation.

## Method

The authors compare two models: (1) a canonical thermodynamic model (with up to 1000 nucleons) that uses a recursion relation to compute the partition function for a system of protons and neutrons fragmenting into clusters, with internal partition functions including volume, surface, symmetry, and Fermi-gas excitation energies; and (2) a mean-field model with a Skyrme-type potential energy density fitted to nuclear matter saturation properties. For both models, they compute pressure-density isotherms, chemical potentials, and specific heats, and examine mechanical instability (negative compressibility) and chemical instability (negative derivative of proton chemical potential with respect to proton fraction).

## Key numbers

- The thermodynamic model uses binding energy parameters: W = 15.8 MeV (volume), σ = 18 MeV (surface), s = 23.5 MeV (symmetry), and ε₀ = 16.0 MeV (level density parameter).
- The mean-field model uses compressibility K = 201 MeV, symmetry energy 23.5 MeV, and saturation density ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- The thermodynamic model is restricted to freeze-out densities ρ/ρ₀ < 0.5 (V ≥ 2V₀) and temperatures T ≥ 3 MeV, with proton fraction y between 0.3 and 0.7.
- The specific heat C_V/A shows a progressively sharper peak as the particle number increases from 200 to 500 to 1000 particles, for all y values between 0.3 and 0.5.
- For y = 0.4 and T = 7-10 MeV, the average neutron multiplicity ⟨n_n⟩ is approximately 10 times the average proton multiplicity ⟨n_p⟩; at T = 4 MeV, ⟨n_n⟩ ≈ 100 ⟨n_p⟩.

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic model of multifragmentation previously developed by Das Gupta and Mekjian (1998) and Bhattacharyya, Das Gupta, and Mekjian (1999), and contrasts it with mean-field approaches used by Muller and Serot (1995, 2001). The paper shows that the thermodynamic model naturally reproduces isospin fractionation observed experimentally by Xu et al. (2000), without requiring the bifurcation in isotopic space needed in mean-field treatments. The results enable further study of phase transitions in finite nuclear systems and suggest that the negative compressibility regions seen in the thermodynamic model are finite-size effects rather than fundamental model deficiencies.
