# Neutron-star Measurements in the Multi-messenger Era

> Neutron stars are compact and dense celestial objects that offer the unique opportunity to explore matter and its interactions under conditions that cannot be reproduced elsewhere in the Universe. Their extreme gravitational, rotational and magnetic energy reservoirs fuel the large variety of their emission, which encompasses all available multi-messenger tracers: electromagnetic and gravitational waves, neutrinos, and cosmic rays. However, accurately measuring global neutron-star properties such as mass, radius, and moment of inertia poses significant challenges. Probing internal characteristics such as the crustal composition or superfluid physics is even more complex. This article provides a comprehensive review of the different methods employed to measure neutron-star characteristics and the level of reliance on theoretical models. Understanding these measurement techniques is crucial for advancing our knowledge of neutron-star physics. We also highlight the importance of employing independent methods and adopting a multi-messenger approach to gather complementary data from various observable phenomena as exemplified by the recent breakthroughs in gravitational-wave astronomy and the landmark detection of a binary neutron-star merger. Consolidating the current state of knowledge on neutron-star measurements will enable an accurate interpretation of the current data and errors, and better planning for future observations and experiments.

## Key claim
This review systematically categorizes neutron-star measurement techniques by their degree of model dependence (green, yellow, orange) and messenger type, arguing that robust progress requires combining independent methods across electromagnetic and gravitational-wave channels. The authors explicitly state they do not aim to summarize current constraints on neutron-star observables, instead focusing on the methodology itself.

## Method
The authors conducted a comprehensive literature review of methods used to measure neutron-star properties, including mass, radius, moment of inertia, tidal deformability, compactness, magnetic field, crustal physics, and superfluidity. For each property, they classified techniques by their reliance on theoretical models using a three-color scheme (green for relatively model-independent, yellow for somewhat model-dependent, orange for strongly model-dependent) and by the messenger or wavelength regime employed (radio, optical, X-ray, gamma-ray, gravitational waves).

## Key numbers
- Neutron-star masses are typically around 1-2 solar masses within a radius of approximately 10-12 km.
- Nuclear saturation density is approximately 2.8 x 10^14 g cm^-3.
- Neutron-drip density marking the transition to the inner crust is approximately 4.3 x 10^11 g cm^-3.
- Surface magnetic field strengths range between 10^8 G and 10^15 G.
- Rotation periods range approximately between 1 ms and 10 s.
- A fiducial neutron-star moment of inertia is 10^45 g cm^2, used to calculate rotational luminosity.
- Magnetar flares release energies of 10^40 to 10^47 erg.
- X-ray Dim Isolated Neutron Stars have black-body temperatures of approximately 40-100 eV.
- Central Compact Objects have black-body temperatures of approximately 0.2-0.5 keV.

## Context
This review consolidates the state of knowledge on neutron-star measurements following the landmark multi-messenger detection of a binary neutron-star merger by LIGO and Virgo (GW170817). It extends prior reviews on neutron-star structure by Lattimer & Prakash (2001, 2016) and on the crust by Chamel (2008), and it provides a framework for interpreting data from current facilities like NICER and future observations. The work enables better planning for future experiments by clarifying the model dependencies and systematics of different measurement techniques.
