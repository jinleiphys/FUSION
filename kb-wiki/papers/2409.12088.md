# Quark saturation in the QCD phase diagram

> We determine the onset of Quarkyonic Matter corresponding to values of temperature and baryon chemical potential at which the quark phase space density becomes one. At zero temperature for baryon chemical potentials below the mass of the Lambda baryon, only nucleons contribute to the quark density. This is different at finite temperature, where all baryons, mesons and their resonances can be excited and thus add quarks to the phase space. The probability density to find a quark inside a hadron is determined using the Yukawa ansatz of the IdylliQ model of Quarkyonic Matter. We estimate separately the magnitude of the various contributions of nucleons, Delta baryons, pions as well as further hadrons and resonances. The uncertainty in the parametrization of the probability density to find a quark inside a nucleon is spanned by assuming that at zero temperature the transition density to Quarkyonic Matter is between one and three times that of nuclear matter. Various predictions for a possible critical point associated with the chiral phase transition are found close to a triple point at which the line of the deconfinement transition and the curve associated with the transition to Quarkyonic Matter intersect. These considerations provide an estimate for the region in the QCD phase diagram where Quarkyonic Matter may be found.

## Key claim

The onset of Quarkyonic Matter occurs when the quark phase-space density reaches one, and this boundary can be determined without knowledge of the dynamic properties of Quarkyonic Matter, using only the relation between quark and hadron phase-space densities. The authors hedge that the uncertainty in the parametrization of the probability density to find a quark inside a nucleon is spanned by assuming the zero-temperature transition density lies between one and three times nuclear matter density.

## Method

The authors compute the quark phase-space density by convolving the momentum distribution of quarks inside hadrons (using the Yukawa ansatz of the IdylliQ model with QCD scale Λ) with the thermal distribution functions of hadrons. They generalize from a pure nucleon gas to a hadron resonance gas including nucleons, pions, Delta baryons, and all hadrons and resonances up to a mass cutoff of 2.25 GeV, evaluating the saturation condition f_Q(q=0)=1 for each system. The deconfinement boundary is estimated using the condition that the Debye screening mass equals the QCD scale Λ, and the uncertainty in Λ is constrained by requiring the zero-temperature saturation density to lie between one and three times nuclear matter density.

## Key numbers

- The QCD scale Λ₀ ≈ 0.29 GeV corresponds to a zero-temperature nucleon density at saturation equal to nuclear matter density n₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³.
- The uncertainty band in Λ is taken as 1 ≤ Λ/Λ₀ ≤ 1.44, corresponding to Λ between 0.29 GeV and 0.42 GeV.
- At zero temperature, the onset baryon chemical potential varies by about 38 MeV across this Λ range; at the upper temperature limit, the variation is about 100 MeV in μB_sat.
- The deconfinement temperature at zero baryon chemical potential is taken as T_c = 0.158 GeV from lattice QCD (Borsanyi 2020).
- The triple point where hadronic matter, deconfined matter, and Quarkyonic Matter meet occurs at baryon chemical potential approximately 0.63 ≤ μB/GeV ≤ 0.72.
- At zero temperature, the Quarkyonic Matter region corresponds to baryon chemical potential in the range M_N ≤ μB ≤ √3 M_N.

## Context

This work extends the IdylliQ model of Quarkyonic Matter (McLerran 2007, McLerran 2018) from zero temperature to finite temperature by generalizing the ideal gas of nucleons to a hadron resonance gas. The results are quantitatively similar to the boundary region for new physics discussed by Andronic (2009), but the authors claim a stronger theoretical and quantitative basis. The triple point found is close to current estimates for the chiral critical point from lattice QCD (Clarke 2024), functional methods (Gao 2020, Gunkel 2021, Fu 2019), and finite-size-scaling analysis (Sorensen 2024), and the Quarkyonic Matter region lies within reach of low-energy heavy-ion collisions and neutron star observations.
