# Propagation of Hauser-Feshbach uncertainty estimates to r-process nucleosynthesis: Benchmark of statistical property models for neutron rich nuclei far from stability

> Multimessenger observations of the neutron star merger event GW170817 have re-energized the debate over the astrophysical origins of the most massive elements via the r-process nucleosynthesis. A key aspect of such studies is comparing astronomical observations to theoretical nucleosynthesis yields in a meaningful way. To perform realistic nucleosynthesis calculations, understanding the uncertainty in microphysics details such as nuclear reaction rates is as essential as understanding uncertainties in modeling the astrophysical environment. We present an investigation of neutron capture rate calculations' uncertainty away from stability using the Hauser-Feshbach model. We provide a quantitative measure of the calculations' dependability when we extrapolate models of statistical properties to nuclei in an r-process network. We select several level density and gamma-ray strength models appropriate for neutron-capture and use them to calculate the reaction rate for each nucleus in the network. We observe how statistical properties affect the theoretical reaction rates. The rates are then sampled with the Monte Carlo technique and used in network calculations to map the range of possible r-process abundances. The results show that neutron capture rates can vary by a couple of orders of magnitude between calculations. Phenomenological models provide smoother results than semi-microscopic. They cannot, however, reproduce nuclear structure changes such as shell closures. While semi-microscopic models predict nuclear structure effects away from stability, it is not clear that these results are quantitatively accurate. The effect of the uncertainty on r-process yields is large enough to impede comparisons between observation and calculations. Progress in developing better microscopic models of gamma strengths and level densities is urgently needed to improve the fidelity of r-process models.

## Key claim

The paper provides a quantitative benchmark of how extrapolating Hauser-Feshbach statistical property models (level density and gamma-ray strength) to neutron-rich nuclei far from stability introduces uncertainties in neutron capture rates that can reach a few orders of magnitude, and these uncertainties are large enough to impede meaningful comparisons between r-process nucleosynthesis calculations and observational data.

## Method

The authors used the TALYS statistical model code to calculate neutron capture rates for neutron-rich nuclei with atomic numbers between 10 and 100, employing five nuclear level density models and four E1 gamma-ray strength function models selected from the literature. For each nucleus in the r-process network, they calculated reaction rates across stellar temperatures from 10^-4 GK to 10 GK, then used Monte Carlo sampling with a flat distribution over the different model combinations to propagate the rate uncertainties into r-process nucleosynthesis network calculations and map the resulting range of possible abundance yields.

## Key numbers

- Neutron capture rates calculated with different extrapolated models vary by up to a factor of 100 (alpha values up to 2, where alpha = log(R_max/R_min)) for the worst-case nuclei at 1 GK
- The intrinsic uncertainty of Hauser-Feshbach predictions for stable or long-lived isotopes, evaluated by Beard et al. (2014), is approximately a factor of 2
- For europium isotopes, the divergence between level density calculations increases significantly above A=181, driving corresponding variations in neutron capture rates
- For gallium isotopes, the disagreement between reaction rate calculations increases drastically above A=81, coinciding with crossing the N=50 shell closure
- The Koning-Delaroche optical potential and semi-microscopic Bauge et al. (1998) approach yielded less than 50% difference in reaction rates when compared

## Context

This work extends previous sensitivity studies by Mumpower et al. (2016) and Surman et al. (2014), who used constant-factor variations of neutron capture rates (factors of 2, 10, and 100) to study effects on r-process yields, and earlier limited-region studies by Denissenkov et al. (2018) and McKay et al. (2019) that used model-to-model variations as uncertainty estimates. The paper maps uncertainty estimates across the entire region of neutron-rich nuclei participating in r-process scenarios, enabling future work to focus on developing better microscopic models of gamma strengths and level densities to improve the fidelity of r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
