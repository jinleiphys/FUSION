# Comparison of Relativistic Nucleon-Nucleon Interactions

> We investigate the difference between those relativistic models based on interpreting a realistic nucleon-nucleon interaction as a perturbation of the square of a relativistic mass operator and those models that use the method of Kamada and Glöckle to construct an equivalent interaction to add to the relativistic mass operator. Although both models reproduce the phase shifts and binding energy of the corresponding non-relativistic model, they are not scattering equivalent. The example of elastic electron-deuteron scattering in the one-photon-exchange approximation is used to study the sensitivity of three-body observables to these choices. Our conclusion is that the differences in the predictions of the two models can be understood in terms of the different ways in which the relativistic and non-relativistic $S$-matrices are related. We argue that the mass squared method is consistent with conventional procedures used to fit the Lorentz-invariant cross section as a function of the laboratory energy.

# Comparison of Relativistic Nucleon-Nucleon Interactions

## Key claim

The mass-squared method and the Kamada-Glockle method for constructing relativistic nucleon-nucleon interactions from non-relativistic potentials are not scattering equivalent, even though both reproduce the same non-relativistic phase shifts and binding energy; the mass-squared method is consistent with conventional procedures for fitting the Lorentz-invariant cross section as a function of laboratory energy, while the Kamada-Glockle method would require refitting to be consistent with experiment at high momenta.

## Method

The authors compare two relativistic formulations of the two-nucleon system based on the same realistic Argonne V18 potential: (1) the mass-squared method, where the non-relativistic interaction kernel is added directly to the square of the free mass operator, and (2) the Kamada-Glockle method, which uses a rescaling and renormalization of the relative momentum variable to construct an equivalent interaction added to the mass operator. Both models are formulated in Dirac's point form of dynamics. The differences are quantified by comparing deuteron wave functions and by computing elastic electron-deuteron scattering observables (A, B, and T20) in the one-photon-exchange approximation using the point-form spectator approximation with Gari-Krumpelmann single-nucleon form factors.

## Key numbers

- The relativistic and non-relativistic deuteron binding energies differ by a factor of approximately 1/2000, as given by (epsilon_r - epsilon_nr)/epsilon_r ~ epsilon_r/(4m).
- The momentum-space wave functions of the two models are similar for momenta out to about 5 fm^-1, where q^2/(4m^2) ~ 0.25.
- Differences in the elastic electron-deuteron observable A(Q^2) become measurable near momentum transfers of approximately 1 (GeV)^2.
- Differences in B(Q^2) and T20(Q^2) appear at momentum transfers as low as a few tenths of a (GeV)^2.
- The relation between the relative momenta in the two models is given by k_KG^2 = k_m^2 (1 + k_m^2/(4m^2)).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of relativistic two-body interactions by comparing two methods that utilize existing non-relativistic potentials without refitting: the mass-squared method introduced by Coester, Pieper, and Serduke (1975) and the method of Kamada and Glockle (1998). The paper shows that these methods, while both scattering equivalent to the same non-relativistic model individually, are not scattering equivalent to each other. The analysis uses elastic electron-deuteron scattering as a test case for three-body sensitivity, following earlier work by Schiavilla (1993) who refit the Argonne V14 interaction relativistically. The results demonstrate that the choice of relativistic interpretation has measurable consequences for three-body observables at momentum transfers far below the scale where the models' momentum-space wave functions begin to differ significantly, which has implications for the consistent treatment of relativistic dynamics in few-body systems.
