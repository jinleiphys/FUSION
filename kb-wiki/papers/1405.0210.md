# Local and global effects of beta decays on r-process

> Nuclear beta decay rates are an essential ingredient in simulations of the astrophysical r-process. Most of these rates still rely on theoretical modeling. However, modern radioactive ion-beam facilities have allowed to measure beta half lives of some nuclei on or close to the r-process path. These data indicate that r-process half lives are in general shorter than anticipated in the standard theoretical predictions based on the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM). The data have also served as important constraints for improved predictions of half lives based on continuum QRPA calculations on top of the energy-density functional theory. Although these calculations are yet limited to spherical nuclei, they include the important r-process waiting point nuclei close to and at the neutron magic numbers $N=50, 82$ and 126. We have studied the impact of these new experimental and theoretical half lives on r-process nucleosynthesis within the two astrophysical sites currently favored for the r process: the neutrino-driven wind from the freshly born neutron star in a supernova explosion and the ejecta of the merger of two neutron stars. We find that the, in general, shorter beta decay rates have several important effects on the dynamics of r-process nucleosynthesis. At first, the matter flow overcomes the waiting point nuclei faster enhancing matter transport to heavier nuclei. Secondly, the shorter half lives result also in a faster consumption of neutrons resulting in important changes of the conditions at freeze-out with consequences for the final r-process abundances. Besides these global effects on the r-process dynamics, the new half lives also lead to some local changes in the abundance distributions.

## Key claim
The paper finds that the generally shorter beta decay half-lives from recent experiments and improved theoretical calculations (DF3/continuum QRPA) have both local effects on abundance peaks and global effects on r-process dynamics, including faster matter flow through waiting points and faster neutron consumption that alters freeze-out conditions, but these effects are partially compensating and depend on the astrophysical scenario (neutrino-driven wind vs. neutron star merger).

## Method
The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis simulations using a reaction network of over 5000 nuclei, adopting FRDM masses and neutron capture cross sections from NON-SMOKER. They compared two sets of beta decay half-lives: the standard FRDM-based QRPA rates and newer rates from Borzov's energy-density functional (DF3) calculations with continuum QRPA, which include Gamow-Teller and first-forbidden transitions and agree well with experimental data. Simulations were run for three astrophysical scenarios: cold r-process in neutrino-driven wind, hot r-process in neutrino-driven wind, and neutron star merger ejecta.

- For the cold r-process, the neutron density evolution shows a noticeable difference between FRDM and DF3(82) sets starting at approximately t = 150 ms (Figure 4).
- The cold r-process simulation used a trajectory ejected 8 seconds after the explosion of a 15 solar mass progenitor, with entropy increased by a factor of two to approximately S = 200 k_B/nucleon to produce elements up to the third r-process peak (A approximately 195).
- The neutron star merger simulation used a binary system of two neutron stars with 1.4 solar masses each.

## Context
This work extends previous studies by Nishimura (2012), Hosmer (2010), and Madurga (2012) that showed shorter experimental half-lives affect r-process dynamics, and by Surman (2012) who studied half-life variations by factors of 10. It uses the same network and setup as Arcones and Martinez-Pinedo (2011) and Arcones and Bertsch (2012). The new DF3 rates from Borzov (2003, 2008, 2011) provide improved predictions for spherical nuclei including waiting points at N=50, 82, and 126, which the authors apply to both neutrino-driven wind and neutron star merger scenarios to separate local and global effects on abundance patterns.
