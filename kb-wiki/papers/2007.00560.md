# Electromagnetic fields and directed flow in large and small colliding systems at ultrarelativistic energies

> The hot and dense QCD matter produced in nuclear collisions at ultrarelativistic energy is characterized by very intense electromagnetic fields which attain their maximal strength in the early pre-equilibrium stage and interplay with the strong vorticity induced in the plasma by the large angular momentum of the colliding system. A promising observable keeping trace of these phenomena is the directed flow of light hadrons and heavy mesons produced in symmetric and asymmetric heavy-ion collisions as well as in proton-induced reactions. In particular, the splitting of the directed flow between particles with the same mass but opposite electric charge as a function of rapidity and transverse momentum gives access to the electromagnetic response of medium in all collision stages and in the different colliding systems. The highest influence of electromagnetic fields is envisaged in the pre-equilibrium stage of the collision and therefore a significant imprint is left on the early-produced heavy quarks. The aim of this review is to discuss the current developments towards the understanding of the generation and relaxation time of the electromagnetic fields embedded in both large and small systems and their impact on the charge-odd directed flow of light and heavy particles, highlighting the experimental results and the different theoretical approaches. Since it is possible to perform realistic simulations of high-energy collisions that incorporate also the generated electromagnetic fields and vorticity, the study of the directed flow can provide unique insight into the early nonequilibrium phase and the ensuing QGP formation and transport properties.

## Key claim

This review argues that the charge-dependent splitting of directed flow $v_1$ for light and heavy hadrons is a promising observable for probing the electromagnetic fields generated in ultrarelativistic nuclear collisions, but the interpretation depends critically on the still-debated relaxation time of these fields and the electric conductivity of the medium, especially in the pre-equilibrium stage.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches for computing electromagnetic fields in nuclear collisions, including the Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) framework that solves retarded Liénard-Wiechert potentials for all charged particles dynamically, and semi-analytic solutions of Maxwell equations with constant electric conductivity. It compares these calculations for symmetric (Au+Au, Pb+Pb), asymmetric (Cu+Au), and small (p+Au) colliding systems at RHIC and LHC energies, and surveys experimental measurements of directed flow $v_1$ for light hadrons and heavy mesons.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic field in peripheral Au+Au collisions at top RHIC energy: $eB_y \approx 5 m_\pi^2$; about one order of magnitude higher at LHC energies
- Magnetic field in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV with $b=10$ fm drops by three orders of magnitude in the first fm/$c$
- Electric conductivity ratio $\sigma_{el}/T$ from DQPM rises quadratically with temperature above $T_c$, in agreement with lattice QCD data for $N_f=2$ and $N_f=2+1$
- In Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV with $b=7$ fm, $|eB_y| \simeq 4 m_\pi^2$ at maximum overlap time
- In p+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}}=200$ GeV with $b=4$ fm, both $B_y$ and $E_x$ reach values of about $2 m_\pi^2$
- Vorticity in heavy-ion collisions: $\omega \sim 0.1$ c/fm $\sim 10^{22}$ s$^{-1}$
- Pseudocritical temperature of QCD transition: $T_c \sim 155$ MeV $\sim 10^{12}$ K
- Magnetic field magnitude: $eB \sim 50 m_\pi^2 \sim 10^{19}$ G

## Context

This review extends the line of work on electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions initiated by Rafelski (1975) and Voskresensky (1980), and later developed through realistic estimates by Kharzeev (2007), Skokov (2009), and Voronyuk (2011). It builds on the discovery that small colliding systems (proton-proton and proton-nucleus) also form QGP droplets, and addresses the tension between theoretical predictions and experimental measurements of directed flow, particularly at LHC energies. The work enables future studies that combine QGP dynamics, heavy-flavor transport, and electromagnetic fields in realistic simulations to resolve the decay timescale of the magnetic field and its impact on charge-dependent observables.
