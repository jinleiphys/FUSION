# Producing and Studying Rare Isotopes in $e+A$ Collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider

> The Electron--Ion Collider (EIC) offers a unique environment to study kinematically controlled lepton--nucleus (e+A) reactions, where a primary hard scattering is followed by an intranuclear cascade and the subsequent statistical de-excitation of the nuclear remnant. Utilizing the BeAGLE model, we demonstrate that event-by-event fluctuations in nucleon removal and energy deposition populate a diverse ensemble of excited remnants. Furthermore, we show that varying the target mass systematically shifts the distribution of these remnants across the (N, Z) plane. Although this excited prefragment remnant is not directly observable, its properties are shown to be strongly correlated with final-state fragments; specifically, the largest nuclear residue and the intensity of evaporation activity serve as effective experimental proxies for event-level remnant characterization. We also evaluate photon observables essential for nuclear spectroscopy. While various photon sources overlap significantly in pseudorapidity, we find that in the nucleus-rest frame, the low-energy spectrum is dominated by de-excitation $γ$ rays and exhibits distinct discrete structures. These findings motivate an EIC research program that correlates rare-isotope production and de-excitation radiation with well-defined initial conditions, providing a collider-based approach to nuclear spectroscopy that is complementary to existing fixed-target facilities.

# Producing and Studying Rare Isotopes in $e+A$ Collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider

## Key claim

Using the BeAGLE model, electron-nucleus collisions at the Electron-Ion Collider can populate a broad, target-dependent ensemble of excited nuclear remnants across the (N, Z) plane, and the largest final-state nuclear residue combined with forward evaporation energy serves as a calibrated experimental proxy for the unobservable remnant mass. However, this is a proof-of-principle simulation study using untuned model parameters, and the extraction of excited-nucleus populations will carry systematic uncertainties from both experimental limitations and the chosen theoretical framework.

## Method

The study used version 1.03 of the BeAGLE Monte Carlo event generator, which integrates PYTHIA6 for hard lepton-parton scattering and Lund string fragmentation, PyQM for medium-induced partonic energy loss using Salgado-Wiedemann quenching weights, DPMJet for intranuclear hadronic transport with a formation time of 5 fm/c, and FLUKA (version 2011.2b.5) for statistical de-excitation via particle evaporation, fission, or multifragmentation. For each nuclear system, 10^7 inelastic events were generated at a center-of-mass energy of 18 x 110 GeV, corresponding to approximately 200 seconds of data collection at an assumed instantaneous luminosity of 10^33 cm^-2 s^-1.

## Key numbers

- Virtual photon energy range: 0.05 to 17 GeV, corresponding to electron beam energies of 5-18 GeV
- Formation time set to 5 fm/c, consistent with previous BeAGLE studies
- De-excitation gamma rays dominate the photon spectrum below approximately 8 MeV in the nucleus rest frame, exhibiting discrete spectroscopic peaks
- Evaporation neutrons detected within Zero Degree Calorimeter acceptance of pseudorapidity > 6.0
- Scaling factor of 110 GeV used to convert calorimetric evaporation energy into neutron multiplicity, corresponding to nominal beam energy per nucleon
- Inelastic e+A collision rate of approximately 50 kHz over full Q^2 > 0 range at assumed luminosity of 10^33 cm^-2 s^-1
- 10^7 inelastic events generated per nuclear system, corresponding to roughly 200 seconds of data collection

## Context

This work extends the concept that the Electron-Ion Collider can serve not only as a probe of partonic structure but also as a laboratory for rare-isotope science through controlled fragmentation, as suggested by Abdul Khalek (2021) and others. It complements existing fixed-target rare-isotope facilities such as the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams at Michigan State University, the RI Beam Factory at RIKEN, ISOLDE at CERN, GANIL/SPIRAL2, and the FAIR Super-FRS/NUSTAR program at GSI, which emphasize inclusive yields integrated over excitation history. The EIC approach provides differential, lepton-tagged correlations connecting fragment production and de-excitation observables to well-defined initial-state kinematics, enabling controlled systematics across multiple ion species at fixed beam energy and forward acceptance. Future work must systematically map the dependence of isotopic reach on collider kinematics, specifically scanning (x, Q^2) and beam energy.
