# Similarity renormalization group for nuclear forces

> Renormalization group methods generate low-resolution Hamiltonians that are more diagonal and easier to solve. This chapter reviews the similarity renormalization group for nuclear Hamiltonians, which is a popular method for generating low-resolution nuclear forces. It presents the similarity renormalization group flow equations, analyzes how the similarity renormalization group drives the Hamiltonian towards the diagonal, and studies the effect of induced many-body interactions. It concludes by highlighting the progress in first-principles calculations of nuclei driven by low-resolution nuclear Hamiltonians.

## Key claim

The similarity renormalization group (SRG) generates low-resolution nuclear Hamiltonians that are unitarily equivalent to the original high-resolution Hamiltonians, but the transformation induces many-body interactions (three-body, four-body, and beyond) that must be included to achieve exact renormalization group invariance of observables; in practice, induced four-body and higher forces are neglected because solving the SRG equations in the four-body Hilbert space is currently impractical, and their smallness must be verified by checking residual resolution-scale dependence of predictions.

## Method

The SRG is implemented as a continuous unitary transformation governed by the flow equation dH(s)/ds = [η(s), H(s)], where the anti-Hermitian generator η(s) is typically chosen as η(s) = [T_rel, H(s)] for nuclear Hamiltonians, with T_rel the relative kinetic energy. The flow equations are solved in a Jacobi momentum-space partial-wave basis for nucleon-nucleon potentials, evolving from the initial Hamiltonian at s=0 (or λ=∞) toward lower resolution scales λ = s^{-1/4} (in fm^{-1}), which exponentially suppresses off-diagonal matrix elements with momentum differences larger than λ^2. For three-nucleon forces, the SRG transformation must be performed simultaneously in the two- and three-body Hilbert spaces, with the induced three-nucleon potential extracted as V_3N(s) = H_3(s) - T_rel - V_NN(s).

## Key numbers

- The SRG resolution scale λ is defined as λ = s^{-1/4} with units of fm^{-1}, and the flow is integrated from λ = ∞ toward λ = 0.
- Off-diagonal matrix elements are suppressed as V_λ(p',p) = V_∞(p',p) exp[-(p'^2 - p^2)^2 / λ^4], meaning matrix elements with momentum differences larger than λ^2 are exponentially suppressed.
- The EM500 chiral potential has a cutoff Λ = 500 MeV (approximately 2.5 fm^{-1}), and its matrix elements with p, p' ≳ 3.2 fm^{-1} are approximately zero at the initial resolution.
- At λ = 1.8 fm^{-1}, all matrix elements V(0,p) with p > 1.8 fm^{-1} are strongly suppressed, decoupling low- and high-momentum degrees of freedom.
- For the ³H ground-state energy, including SRG-induced three-nucleon interactions is required for invariance, and for λ ≥ 1.8 fm^{-1}, the predicted ⁴He ground-state energy is approximately independent of λ, indicating induced four-body forces are small.

## Context

This chapter reviews the similarity renormalization group method introduced by Glazek and Wilson (1994) and Wegner (1994) and applied to nuclear physics by Bogner et al. (2007). It extends the understanding that traditional high-resolution nuclear potentials with hard repulsive cores (e.g., the AV18 potential by Wiringa et al., 1995) strongly couple low- and high-energy states, making them nonperturbative, while SRG-evolved potentials collapse to universal low-momentum forms as noted by Bogner et al. (2003). The work enables first-principles calculations of nuclei using low-resolution Hamiltonians that are more perturbative and exhibit faster convergence, with the key open challenge being the consistent treatment of induced many-body forces beyond three-body interactions.
