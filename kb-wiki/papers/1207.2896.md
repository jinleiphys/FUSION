# Migdal and his theory in Jülich

> We review the application of Migdal's "Theory of Finite Fermi System" to the structure of deformed nuclei, approaches beyond the conventional linear response, and microscopic calculations of the Migdal-parameters.

## Key claim

This review demonstrates that Migdal's Theory of Finite Fermi Systems (TFFS) has been successfully applied to strongly deformed nuclei, with most predictions experimentally verified, but the approach relies on phenomenological single-particle models and parametrized residual interactions rather than fully first-principles calculations.

## Method

The authors review the application of TFFS to deformed nuclei using the many-body Green function formalism, extending the conventional linear response (RPA) to include pairing correlations via generalized Green functions and a second-order response theory. The input consists of single-particle wave functions from a deformed Woods-Saxon potential, single-particle energies adjusted to experimental data, and parametrized Landau-Migdal interactions (ph and pp) with density-dependent parameters. The QRPA equations are solved to obtain excitation energies, transition probabilities, and transition densities for electric and magnetic states in rare earth and actinide nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The low-lying collective states in deformed nuclei (beta-vibrations and gamma-vibrations) appear at energies of about 1 MeV, with considerable variations in energies and transition probabilities across the deformed rare earth region.
- The isoscalar giant quadrupole resonance strength in deformed nuclei is concentrated around 11 MeV, with splitting into K^pi = 0^+, 1^+, and 2^+ components.
- The electric dipole resonance in 170Yb was calculated with a Gaussian width of 1.5 MeV FWHM folded into the discrete levels to simulate single-particle widths.
- The change of nuclear charge radii due to rotation, delta<r^2>, was measured via the Mossbauer effect and muonic atoms, yielding both positive and negative values in different nuclei, contradicting the liquid-drop model prediction of always positive stretching.

## Context

This review extends the earlier work by Speth et al. (1977) on spherical nuclei in the lead region and the self-consistent TFFS approach by Khodel and Saperstein (1982) for closed-shell nuclei. It builds on the extension by Drozdz et al. (1990) that included 1p1h and 2p2h configurations for giant resonances, and the more recent work by Kamerdzhiev et al. (2004) incorporating 1p1h x phonon configurations. The review focuses on deformed nuclei in the rare earth and actinide regions, which were largely neglected in prior reviews, and demonstrates that the second-order response theory successfully explained the sign change of isomer shifts in rotational states, a puzzle that had been controversially discussed in the 1970s.
