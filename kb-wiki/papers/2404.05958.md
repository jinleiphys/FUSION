# Entanglement suppression and low-energy scattering of heavy mesons

> Recently entanglement suppression was proposed to be one possible origin of emergent symmetries. Here we test this conjecture in the context of heavy meson scatterings. The low-energy interactions of $D^{(*)}\bar D^{(*)}$ and $D^{(*)} D^{(*)}$ are closely related to the hadronic molecular candidates $X(3872)$ and $T_{cc}(3875)^+$, respectively, and can be described by a nonrelativistic effective Lagrangian manifesting heavy-quark spin symmetry, which includes only constant contact potentials at leading order. We explore entanglement suppression in a tensor-product framework to treat both the isospin and spin degrees of freedom. Using the $X(3872)$ and $T_{cc}(3875)^+$ as inputs, we find that entanglement suppression indeed leads to an emergent symmetry, namely, a light-quark spin symmetry, and as such the $D^{(*)}\bar D^{(*)}$ or $D^{(*)} D^{(*)}$ interaction strengths for a given total isospin do not depend on the total angular momentum of light (anti)quarks. The $X(3872)$ and $T_{cc}(3875)^+$ are predicted to have five and one isoscalar partner, respectively, while the corresponding partner numbers derived solely from heavy-quark spin symmetry are three and one, respectively. The predictions need to be confronted with experimental data and lattice quantum chromodynamics results to further test the entanglement suppression conjecture.

## Key claim

Entanglement suppression in low-energy heavy meson scattering leads to an emergent light-quark spin symmetry, such that the interaction strengths for a given total isospin do not depend on the total angular momentum of the light (anti)quarks; this prediction goes beyond what heavy-quark spin symmetry alone provides, but must be tested against experimental data and lattice QCD results.

## Method

The authors construct a nonrelativistic effective field theory with constant contact potentials at leading order, respecting heavy-quark spin symmetry and isospin symmetry, to describe S-wave scattering of \(D^{(*)}D^{(*)}\) and \(D^{(*)}\bar{D}^{(*)}\) pairs. They compute the entanglement power of the S-matrix in a tensor-product framework treating both isospin and spin degrees of freedom, using linear entropy averaged over all initial tensor-product states. The vanishing of entanglement power imposes constraints on the phase shifts, which are then combined with the empirical existence of the \(X(3872)\) and \(T_{cc}(3875)^+\) as unitary-limit inputs to determine the low-energy constants and predict partner states.

## Key numbers

* \(M_{X} - M_{D^0} - M_{\bar D^{*0}} = 0.00_{-0.15}^{+0.09}\,\text{MeV}\) (mass of \(X(3872)\) relative to \(D^0\bar D^{*0}\) threshold)
* \(M_{T_{cc}^+} - M_{D^{*+}} - M_{D^0} = (-0.36\pm0.04)\,\text{MeV}\) (mass of \(T_{cc}(3875)^+\) relative to \(D^{*+}D^0\) threshold)
* Entanglement power for isospin subspace: \(E(S_J)=\frac16\sin^2[2(\delta_{0J}-\delta_{1J})]\), vanishing when \(|\delta_{0J}-\delta_{1J}|=0\) or \(\pi/2\)
* Entanglement power for spin subspace of vector mesons: a function of three phase shifts, vanishing only when \(|\delta_{I0*}-\delta_{I1*}|=|\delta_{I2*}-\delta_{I1*}|=0\) or \(\pi/2\)
* The \(X(3872)\) is predicted to have five isoscalar partners under entanglement suppression, compared to three from heavy-quark spin symmetry alone
* The \(T_{cc}(3875)^+\) is predicted to have one isoscalar partner under both entanglement suppression and heavy-quark spin symmetry

## Context

This work extends the entanglement suppression conjecture, previously tested for nucleon-nucleon scattering (Beane 2018), pionic scattering (Beane 2021), and light octet baryon scattering (Liu 2022, Liu 2023), to the heavy meson systems that host the hadronic molecular candidates \(X(3872)\) and \(T_{cc}(3875)^+\). The results predict additional molecular partners beyond those expected from heavy-quark spin symmetry alone (Guo 2009, Voloshin 2011, Mehen 2011, Cleven 2015), and the authors note that the large-\(N_c\) limit may give different predictions for these systems, motivating future comparison. The predictions require confrontation with experimental data and lattice QCD results to further test the entanglement suppression conjecture.
