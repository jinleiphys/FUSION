# The equation of state for dense nucleonic matter from a meta-modeling. II. Predictions for neutron stars properties

> Employing a recently proposed metamodeling for the nucleonic matter equation of state we analyze neutron star global properties such as masses, radii, momentum of inertia, and others. The impact of the uncertainty on empirical parameters on these global properties is analyzed in a Bayesian statistical approach. Physical constraints, such as causality and stability, are imposed on the equation of state and different hypotheses for the direct Urca (dUrca) process are investigated. In addition, only metamodels with maximum masses above 2$M_\odot$ are selected. Our main results are the following: the equation of state exhibits a universal behavior against the dUrca hypothesis under the condition of charge neutrality and $β$-equilibrium; neutron stars, if composed exclusively of nucleons and leptons, have a radius of 12.7$\pm$0.4~km for masses ranging from 1 up to 2$M_\odot$; a small radius lower than 11~km is very marginally compatible with our present knowledge of the nuclear empirical parameters; and finally, the most important empirical parameters which are still affected by large uncertainties and play an important role in determining the radius of neutrons stars are the slope and curvature of the symmetry energy ($L_{sym}$ and $K_{sym}$) and, to a lower extent, the skewness parameters ($Q_{sat/sym}$).

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron stars composed exclusively of nucleons and leptons have a radius of 12.7 ± 0.4 km for masses ranging from 1 up to 2 solar masses, but this result is conditional on selecting only metamodels that produce maximum masses above 2 solar masses and imposing physical constraints such as causality and stability.

## Method

The authors employed a metamodeling approach (ELFc) for the nucleonic matter equation of state, where the energy per particle is expressed as a function of isoscalar and isovector empirical parameters (such as E_sat, K_sat, E_sym, L_sym, K_sym, Q_sat, Q_sym, Z_sat, Z_sym). They solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for spherical non-rotating neutron stars, matching the core meta-EOS to a crust EOS (SLY or FPS) via a cubic spline interpolation in log density - log pressure space. A Bayesian statistical approach was used to analyze the impact of empirical parameter uncertainties on neutron star global properties, applying filters for stability, positive symmetry energy, causality, and the requirement that the maximum mass exceeds 2 solar masses.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star radius: 12.7 ± 0.4 km for masses ranging from 1 up to 2 solar masses, assuming exclusively nucleonic and leptonic composition
- A radius lower than 11 km is described as "very marginally compatible" with current knowledge of nuclear empirical parameters
- The uncertainty in L_sym leads to an uncertainty of about 2 km at low neutron star mass and 1 km at high neutron star mass, about 1.5 km at canonical mass (1.4 solar masses)
- The uncertainty in K_sym leads to an uncertainty of about 1 km at low neutron star mass and 500 m at high neutron star mass
- The impact of varying K_sat on the radius is about 200 m at the low mass boundary up to 400 m at high mass, and about 300 m at canonical mass
- The crust EOS matching procedure induces an uncertainty of about 100 m for low mass neutron stars and 50 m for high mass neutron stars
- The Schwarzschild radius R_s = 2.955 km for M = 1 solar mass
- The fastest observed pulsar PSR J1748-2446ad at 716 Hz gives R^3Ω^2/(GM) ≈ 0.11 assuming M = 1.4 solar masses and R = 10 km

## Context

This work extends the metamodeling approach introduced in Margueron (2017a) (paper I) to predict neutron star properties, building on prior work by Lattimer (2001a) on the empirical R-P^{-1/4} correlation. The analysis enables future inversion of neutron star mass and radius measurements to constrain nuclear empirical parameters, particularly the slope and curvature of the symmetry energy (L_sym and K_sym) and the skewness parameters (Q_sat and Q_sym). The results are relevant for interpreting observations from the upcoming NICER mission (Gendreau 2012) and for guiding nuclear physics experiments such as PREX and CREX (Abrahamyan 2012).
