# The phase diagram of nuclear and quark matter at high baryon density

> We review theoretical approaches to explore the phase diagram of nuclear and quark matter at high baryon density. We first look over the basic properties of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) and address how to describe various states of QCD matter. In our discussions on nuclear matter we cover the relativistic mean-field model, the chiral perturbation theory, and the approximation based on the large-Nc limit where Nc is the number of colors. We then explain the liquid-gas phase transition and the inhomogeneous meson condensation in nuclear matter with emphasis put on the relevance to quark matter. We commence the next part focused on quark matter with the bootstrap model and the Hagedorn temperature. Then we turn to properties associated with chiral symmetry and exposit theoretical descriptions of the chiral phase transition. There emerge some quark-matter counterparts of phenomena seen in nuclear matter such as the liquid-gas phase transition and the inhomogeneous structure of the chiral condensate. The third regime that is being recognized recently is what is called quarkyonic matter, which has both aspects of nuclear and quark matter. We closely elucidate the basic idea of quarkyonic matter in the large-Nc limit and its physics implications. Finally, we discuss some experimental indications for the QCD phase diagram and close the review with outlooks.

# The phase diagram of nuclear and quark matter at high baryon density

## Key claim

This review assembles theoretical descriptions of the QCD phase diagram at high baryon density by collecting results from nuclear matter, quark matter, and the intermediate concept of quarkyonic matter, but explicitly restricts the density region considered to before color-superconducting (CSC) phases are turned on, because the phase structure involving CSC is sensitive to model uncertainties at intermediate density and difficult to identify robust parts from model-dependent results.

## Method

The authors review theoretical approaches to the QCD phase diagram in the baryon chemical potential-temperature plane, covering the relativistic mean-field model, chiral perturbation theory, the large-Nc (number of colors) approximation, the bootstrap model with the Hagedorn temperature, chiral models (Nambu-Jona-Lasinio and quark-meson models), and the Sakai-Sugimoto holographic QCD model. They discuss the liquid-gas phase transition, inhomogeneous meson condensation, chiral phase transitions, and quarkyonic matter as formulated in the McLerran-Pisarski conjecture, then compare theoretical predictions with experimental indications from heavy-ion collisions and neutron star observations.

## Key numbers

- Current quark masses: up quark = 2.3 (+0.7/-0.5) MeV, down quark = 4.8 (+0.7/-0.3) MeV, strange quark = 95 +/- 5 MeV, in a mass-independent subtraction scheme at a scale of approximately 2 GeV
- Strong coupling constant world average: alpha_s(m_Z) = 0.1184(7)
- Upper limit on the CP-violating theta parameter from neutron electric dipole moment: |theta| < 10^(-10)
- QCD energy scale: Lambda_QCD ~ 200 MeV
- The heaviest neutron star discovered has almost two solar masses, providing constraints on the equation of state of dense matter

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the QCD phase diagram that began with Cabibbo and Parisi (1975) based on the Hagedorn limiting temperature, and incorporates the more recent concept of quarkyonic matter introduced by McLerran (2007). The paper connects nuclear matter approaches (relativistic mean-field models, chiral perturbation theory) with quark matter descriptions (NJL model, quark-meson model) and the Sakai-Sugimoto holographic QCD model, which is unique in describing both baryonic and quark matter within a single framework. The review enables future work by identifying two possible scenarios for the phase diagram (with and without a first-order phase boundary) and emphasizing that the QCD phase diagram can be non-trivial even without a first-order phase transition and critical point.
