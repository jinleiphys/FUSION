# Constraining the neutron star equation of state using Pulse Profile Modeling

> One very promising technique for measuring the dense matter Equation of State exploits hotspots that form on the neutron star surface due to the pulsar mechanism, accretion streams, or during thermonuclear explosions in the neutron star ocean. This article explains how Pulse Profile Modeling of hotspots is being used by the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER), an X-ray telescope installed on the International Space Station in 2017 - and why the technique is a mission driver for the next, larger-area generation of telescopes including the enhanced X-ray Timing and Polarimetry (eXTP) mission and the Spectroscopic Time-Resolving Observatory for Broadband Energy X-rays (STROBE-X).

## Key claim

Pulse Profile Modeling can deliver simultaneous measurement of neutron star mass and radius at the level of a few percent, but the technique requires sources with rapid spin (above 100 Hz) and high quality phase- and energy-resolved waveforms with roughly 10^6 pulsed photons; NICER is expected to achieve 5-10% level mass-radius constraints for individual sources, while precision equation of state inference will likely require the next generation of larger-area telescopes.

## Method

The paper reviews the Pulse Profile Modeling technique, which exploits general and special relativistic effects (gravitational light-bending, Doppler boosting, aberration, time delays, and rotational stellar oblateness) on rotationally-modulated X-ray emission from neutron star surface hotspots. The method couples lightcurve models to Bayesian inference codes to derive posterior probability distributions for mass and radius, or equation of state parameters, from pulse profile data (X-ray counts binned by energy and rotational phase). The technique is applied to three source classes: rotation-powered pulsars, accretion-powered pulsars, and thermonuclear burst oscillation sources, each with different hotspot formation mechanisms and observational challenges.

## Key numbers

- Neutron star core densities can reach up to ten times that of normal nuclear matter
- Pulse Profile Modeling requires sources with spin above 100 Hz to ensure strong special relativistic effects
- The technique needs time resolution of 10 microseconds or better and roughly 10^6 pulsed photons to deliver constraints at the few percent level
- NICER is expected to deliver mass and radius at the 5-10% level for four relatively bright sources (PSR J0437-4715, PSR J0030+0451, PSR J1231+1411 and PSR J2124-3358)
- For accretion-powered pulsars, observations of total duration roughly 100 kiloseconds are needed to collect sufficient photons
- For thermonuclear burst oscillation sources, estimated observation times are of order a few hundred kiloseconds
- eXTP's Spectroscopic Focusing Array and Large Area Detector would reach a total effective area of about 4 square meters
- In a simulation for NICER using synthetic rotation-powered pulsar data, radius was inferred at the 7% level and mass at the 4% level (assuming a tight prior on mass from radio data)
- For NICER-level mass-radius posteriors, the range of pressures spanned by the 95% credible band for the equation of state at twice nuclear saturation density was up to a factor of about 5 narrower than the full range considered plausible

## Context

This work reviews the Pulse Profile Modeling technique that extends earlier attempts using data from the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE, 1995-2012) and XMM-Newton, which had large uncertainties due to low photon numbers and did not use full Bayesian inference frameworks (Bogdanov 2007, 2009, 2013; Poutanen 2003; Leahy 2004, 2009; Morsink 2011; Bhattacharyya 2005). The paper discusses how NICER (installed on the International Space Station in 2017) will provide the first firm proof of principle for per-source mass-radius inference at the few percent level, while the next-generation missions eXTP (proposed launch 2025) and STROBE-X (proposed launch 2031) would enable equation of state mapping across all three source classes and cross-checking of results from different techniques. The paper also highlights that equation of state inference from small numbers of mass-radius posteriors can be biased by model parametrization and prior choices, an issue that may also affect gravitational wave event analyses.
