# Skeletal Kinetics Reduction for Astrophysical Reaction Networks

> A novel methodology is developed to extract accurate skeletal reaction models for nuclear combustion. Local sensitivities of isotope mass fractions with respect to reaction rates are modeled based on the forced optimally time-dependent (f-OTD) scheme. These sensitivities are then analyzed temporally to generate skeletal models. The methodology is demonstrated by conducting skeletal reduction of constant density and temperature burning of carbon and oxygen relevant to SNe Ia. The 495-isotopes Torch model is chosen as the detailed reaction network. A map of maximum production of $^{56}\text{Ni}$ in SNe Ia is produced for different temperatures, densities, and proton to neutron ratios. The f-OTD simulations and the sensitivity analyses are then performed with initial conditions from this map. A series of skeletal models are derived and their performances are assessed by comparison against currently existing skeletal models. Previous models have been constructed intuitively by assuming the dominance of $α$-chain reactions. The comparison of the newly generated skeletal models against previous models is based on the predicted energy release and $^{44}\text{Ti}$ and $^{56}\text{Ni}$ abundances by each model. The consequences of $\mathtt{y}_e \neq 0.5$ in the initial composition are also explored where $\mathtt{y}_e$ is the electron fraction. The simulated results show that $^{56}\text{Ni}$ production decreases by decreasing $\mathtt{y}_e$ as expected, and that the $^{43}\text{Sc}$ is a key isotope in proton and neutron channels toward $^{56}\text{Ni}$ production. It is shown that an f-OTD skeletal model with 150 isotopes can accurately predict the $^{56}\text{Ni}$ abundance in SNe Ia for $\mathtt{y}_e \lesssim 0.5$ initial conditions.

# Skeletal Kinetics Reduction for Astrophysical Reaction Networks

## Key claim
The f-OTD (forced optimally time-dependent) methodology can automatically generate skeletal reaction models for Type Ia supernova burning conditions without requiring prior expertise or assumptions about alpha-chain dominance, and a skeletal model with 150 isotopes can accurately predict ⁵⁶Ni abundance for electron fractions ye ≲ 0.5, though the method's performance depends on the specific temperature, density, and electron fraction conditions chosen for training.

## Method
The authors applied the f-OTD reduced-order modeling technique to compute local sensitivities of isotope mass fractions with respect to reaction rates during constant-density and constant-temperature burning of carbon and oxygen. They ranked isotopes and reactions based on time-integrated sensitivity measures derived from the f-OTD decomposition, then eliminated isotopes and their associated reactions below a threshold to produce skeletal models. The method was demonstrated on the 495-isotope Torch reaction network, with 24 simulation cases covering temperatures T₉ ∈ {2, 4, 6}, densities ρ₉ ∈ {0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1.0}, and electron fractions ye ∈ {0.4955, 0.5}.

## Key numbers
- The detailed Torch reaction network contains 495 isotopes and 6012 reactions.
- A skeletal model with 150 isotopes (f-OTD-150) can accurately predict ⁵⁶Ni abundance in SNe Ia for ye ≲ 0.5 initial conditions.
- The f-OTD simulation used r = 1 mode for sensitivity matrix approximation, with the first eigenvalue being an order of magnitude larger than the second eigenvalue most of the time.
- The Approx21 network contains 21 isotopes and 112 reactions; the f-OTD-10 model extracted from it contains 10 isotopes (⁴He, ¹²C, ²⁰Ne, ²⁴Mg, ²⁸Si, ³²S, ³⁶Ar, ⁴⁰Ca, ¹⁶O, and ⁴⁴Ti).
- The map of maximum ⁵⁶Ni production was generated on a 100×100 grid of T₉ and ρ₉ for ye values of 0.4955 and 0.5, with peak production around T₉ = 4.0 at lower densities.
- The f-OTD simulations were run for 24 cases with T₉ ∈ {2, 4, 6}, ρ₉ ∈ {0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1.0}, and ye ∈ {0.4955, 0.5}.

## Context
This work extends sensitivity-based skeletal reduction techniques previously developed for chemical combustion by Nouri et al. (2022) and Liu et al. (2024) to nuclear astrophysical reaction networks. It contrasts with traditional skeletal models such as the Approx13 network of Timmes et al. (2000) and the Approx21 network used in MESA (Paxton et al., 2015), which were constructed intuitively by assuming dominance of alpha-chain reactions. The methodology enables automated generation of skeletal models for conditions where prior expertise may be lacking, particularly for neutron-rich scenarios with ye < 0.5, and identifies ⁴³Sc as a key isotope in proton and neutron channels toward ⁵⁶Ni production.
