# Simulations of Subatomic Many-Body Physics on a Quantum Frequency Processor

> Simulating complex many-body quantum phenomena is a major scientific impetus behind the development of quantum computing, and a range of technologies are being explored to address such systems. We present the results of the largest photonics-based simulation to date, applied in the context of subatomic physics. Using an all-optical quantum frequency processor, the ground-state energies of light nuclei including the triton ($^3$H), $^{3}$He, and the alpha particle ($^{4}$He) are computed. Complementing these calculations and utilizing a 68-dimensional Hilbert space, our photonic simulator is used to perform sub-nucleon calculations of the two-body and three-body forces between heavy mesons in the Schwinger model. This work is a first step in simulating subatomic many-body physics on quantum frequency processors---augmenting classical computations that bridge scales from quarks to nuclei.

# Simulations of Subatomic Many-Body Physics on a Quantum Frequency Processor

## Key claim
Using an all-optical quantum frequency processor (QFP) with up to 68-dimensional Hilbert spaces, the authors compute ground-state energies of light nuclei and extract two-body and three-body interaction potentials between composite particles in the Schwinger model, but note that these results are not of comparable complexity or precision to classical computations and represent an early step toward scalable quantum simulations.

## Method
The authors implement a variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) algorithm on a photonic quantum frequency processor that encodes quantum information in frequency bins of a single photon. For nuclear structure calculations, they project a next-to-leading order (NLO) pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian onto Hilbert spaces with fixed spin and parity, then map the resulting single-particle Hamiltonians onto frequency-bin operations. For the Schwinger model, they use a staggered fermion discretization on eight fermion sites (four spatial sites), enforce Gauss's law, and project onto ground-state quantum numbers to extract interaction potentials between static charges. The VQE uses a unitary coupled-cluster ansatz with an anti-Hermitian contracted Schrodinger equation method for gradient estimation, requiring approximately 20 iterations to converge.

## Key numbers
- Ground-state energies computed for triton (³H), ³He, and alpha particle (⁴He) using NLO pionless EFT with harmonic oscillator basis spacing ℏω = 22 MeV and interaction momentum cutoff 337 MeV
- Hilbert space dimension up to 68 for the Schwinger model calculations
- Hadamard gate fidelity improved to ℱ = 0.999999 with reflectivity ℛ = 48.7% and transmissivity 𝒯 = 48.77% (photon leakage 2.53%)
- VQE convergence achieved in ~20 iterations compared to ~500 iterations using BFGS algorithm
- Four continuous-wave laser modules at 192.0, 192.4, 192.8, and 193.2 THz combined with EOM driven at 25 GHz, producing 40 available comb lines
- Five parallel beamsplitters implemented simultaneously, reducing required computations by a factor of five per iteration

## Context
This work extends prior photonic quantum simulations of molecular systems (Lanyon 2010, Peruzzo 2014, Santagati 2018) and previous nuclear physics quantum simulations of the deuteron (Dumitrescu 2018) and Schwinger model on trapped-ion platforms (Martinez 2016, Klco 2018). The authors build on their own earlier demonstrations of single-qubit gates (Lu 2018a, 2018b) and two-qubit controlled-NOT gates (Lu 2018c) in the QFP architecture. The nuclear structure calculations use pionless EFT parameters constrained by experimental data (Bedaque 2002, Epelbaum 2009, Machleidt 2011), while the Schwinger model calculations provide a proof-of-principle for extracting effective field theory parameters directly from quantum simulations of lattice gauge theories, analogous to the program of connecting lattice QCD to nuclear EFTs (Beane 2012, Barnea 2013, Kirscher 2015, Contessi 2017, Bansal 2017). This work enables future multi-photon QFP implementations that could achieve quantum advantage for subatomic physics problems.
