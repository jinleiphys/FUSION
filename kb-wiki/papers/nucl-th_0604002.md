# Nuclear Masses, Chaos, and the Residual Interaction

> We interpret the discrepancy between semiempirical nuclear mass formulas and actual nuclear masses in terms of the residual interaction. We show that correlations exist among all binding energies and all separation energies throughout the valley of stability. We relate our approach to chaotic motion in nuclei.

## Key claim

The discrepancy between semiempirical nuclear mass formulas and actual nuclear masses (about 0.5 MeV) can be interpreted as a many-body effect of the residual interaction rather than solely as a consequence of chaotic single-particle motion, and the two viewpoints are not fundamentally different because chaos in nuclei is generically caused by the residual interaction.

## Method

The authors apply the two-body random ensemble (TBRE) of the shell model, treating the two-body matrix elements of the residual interaction as Gaussian-distributed random variables, to estimate variances of binding energies and separation energies. They then use the identity relating binding energy to a sum of sequential separation energies to derive constraints on the variances and covariances of these quantities, showing that the observed decrease of the discrepancy with mass number forces the existence of correlations among all separation energies and binding energies throughout the valley of stability.

## Key numbers

- The overall difference between the best-fit semiempirical mass formula and actual nuclear masses is of order 0.5 MeV, with a tendency to become smaller for heavier nuclei.
- The square root of the variance of binding energy due to chaotic single-particle motion, from Bohigas and Leboeuf, is given by sigma = 2.78 / A^(1/3) MeV, which agrees with the observed discrepancy except for the lightest nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the discussion initiated by Bohigas and Leboeuf (2002), who attributed the mass formula discrepancy to chaotic single-particle motion using Berry's result for the form factor. The authors contrast their many-body interpretation with that mean-field approach and relate their findings to earlier work on the TBRE by French and collaborators and to studies of chaos in the sd-shell by Papenbrock and collaborators. The results suggest that correlations among binding energies extend over ranges given by major shells, which cannot be accommodated within the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble of random matrices, pointing to a limitation of canonical random-matrix theory.
