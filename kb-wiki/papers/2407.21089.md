# Determination of electron screening potential of 6 Li(p,α)3 He reaction using MultiLayer Perceptron based neural network

> Background: Understanding the nuclear reactions between light charged nuclei at sub-coulomb energy region holds significant importance in several astrophysical processes. Determination of the precise reaction cross-section within the astrophysically important Gamow range is difficult because of electron screening. Various polynomial fits, R-Matrix and Indirect Trojan horse method estimate much higher electron screening energies as compared to the adiabatic limit. Purpose: Obtain the bare astrophysical S-factor of 6 Li(p,α)3 He using Multi-Layer Perceptron based Artificial Neural Network based analysis and extract the electron screening energies. Methods: Experimental S-factor of 6 Li(p,α)3 He, available in literature, are reanalyzed using the Multi-LayerPerceptron based Artificial Neural Network based algorithm to obtain the energy dependent astrophysical S-factor. Bare astrophysical S-factor is also calculated using the same Feed-forward Artificial Neural Network from the data range above 60 keV where the electron screening effect is expected to be negligible. Electron screening potential is then obtained by taking the ratio of total shielded S-factor with the bare S-factor. Results and Conclusions: The electron screening potential obtained from the Present work through the Artificial Neural network based algorithm is found to be 220 eV. The extracted electron screening potential through the present analysis indicates that the Artificial Neural Network might be an alternative tools for estimation the electron screening potential involving light nuclei.

## Key claim
The electron screening potential for the 6Li(p,α)3He reaction is determined to be 220 eV using a Multi-Layer Perceptron based Artificial Neural Network, which the author suggests may serve as an alternative tool for estimating electron screening potentials in light nuclei, though the result is presented without an explicit uncertainty.

## Method
Experimental S-factor data for 6Li(p,α)3He from eight literature sources (Engstler et al., Gemeinhardt et al., Elwyn et al., Kwon et al., Shinozuka et al., Feidler et al., and Cruz et al.) were taken from the EXFOR database and reanalyzed using a feed-forward Multi-Layer Perceptron neural network with four hidden layers (128, 64, 32, 16 neurons) and one output layer. The network was trained on 70% of the data and tested on 30%, using hyperparameter optimization via RandomizedSearchCV with Adam optimizer (initial learning rate 0.001, decay constants 0.9 and 0.999) over 5000 maximum iterations. Bare astrophysical S-factor was calculated from data above 60 keV where electron screening is negligible, and the electron screening potential was obtained from the ratio of total shielded S-factor to bare S-factor.

## Key numbers
- Electron screening potential Ue = 220 eV for the 6Li(p,α)3He reaction (no uncertainty quoted)
- Bare astrophysical S-factor at zero energy Sb(0) = 3.36 ± 0.12 MeV-barn from the ANN analysis
- Data range used spans center-of-mass energies from 10.7 keV to 927.4 keV
- Training used 70% of data, testing used 30% of data
- Network architecture: input layer, four hidden layers (128, 64, 32, 16 neurons), output layer

## Context
This work extends previous determinations of the electron screening potential for 6Li(p,α)3He, which have consistently yielded values above the adiabatic limit of 175 eV: Engstler et al. (1992) found 470 ± 150 eV and 440 ± 150 eV using polynomial fits, Cruz et al. (2008) found 273 ± 111 eV, Lamia et al. (2013) found 355 ± 100 eV using the Trojan Horse Method, and Spitaleri et al. (2023) found 290 ± 75 eV using R-Matrix analysis. The ANN result of 220 eV is lower than most previous determinations and falls within the range of values obtained by Wang et al. (2011) (218 ± 38 eV and 310 ± 109 eV). The approach enables a data-driven alternative to traditional polynomial fitting, R-Matrix, and Trojan Horse methods for extracting electron screening potentials in light-ion reactions relevant to Big Bang nucleosynthesis and stellar lithium depletion.
