# Comparison of the electromagnetic responses of $^{12}$C obtained from the Green's function Monte Carlo and spectral function approaches

> The electromagnetic responses of carbon obtained from the Green's function Monte Carlo and spectral function approaches using the same dynamical input are compared in the kinematical region corresponding to momentum transfer in the range 300-570 MeV. The results of our analysis, aimed at pinning down the limits of applicability of the approximations involved in the two schemes, indicate that the factorization ansatz underlying the spectral function formalism provides remarkably accurate results down to momentum transfer as low as 300 MeV. On the other hand, it appears that at 570 MeV relativistic corrections to the electromagnetic current not included in the Monte Carlo calculations may play a significant role in the transverse channel.

## Key claim

The factorization ansatz underlying the spectral function formalism provides remarkably accurate results down to momentum transfer as low as 300 MeV, but at 570 MeV relativistic corrections to the electromagnetic current not included in the Green's function Monte Carlo calculations may play a significant role in the transverse channel.

## Method

The authors compared the longitudinal and transverse electromagnetic response functions of $^{12}$C computed using the Green's function Monte Carlo (GFMC) method and the spectral function (SF) approach, employing the same nuclear Hamiltonian (Argonne $v_{18}$ two-body potential plus a three-body term) and one-body current operators. The GFMC responses were obtained by inverting Euclidean (Laplace-transformed) responses using maximum-entropy techniques, while the SF responses were computed within the impulse approximation using a spectral function derived from the same Hamiltonian, with final-state interactions and Pauli blocking corrections included.

## Key numbers

- Momentum transfer values studied: 300, 380, and 570 MeV
- Fermi momentum used for Pauli blocking: $k_F = 211$ MeV
- Computational cost of GFMC: approximately 5 million core-hours to compute response functions for a single value of momentum transfer
- At $|{\bf q}| = 300$ and 380 MeV, relativistic effects in the longitudinal channel are small, with all four SF variants agreeing closely with GFMC
- At $|{\bf q}| = 570$ MeV in the longitudinal channel, relativistic effects become sizable, leading to a shift and enhancement of the peak with reduced width

## Context

This work extends the comparison of ab initio nuclear response calculations to $^{12}$C, following earlier GFMC calculations for $^4$He and $^{12}$C by Lovato et al. (2015, 2016) and spectral function calculations by Benhar et al. (2015) and Rocco et al. (2015). The study is motivated by the need to reduce systematic uncertainties in neutrino-oscillation experiments, particularly following the MiniBooNE collaboration's observation of a charged-current quasielastic cross section exceeding Relativistic Fermi Gas Model predictions, which prompted models by Martini et al. (2009, 2010) and Nieves et al. (2011) incorporating meson-exchange currents. The comparison enables benchmarking of the more approximate spectral function approach against the computationally expensive GFMC method, and identifies where relativistic corrections to currents and kinematics become necessary.
