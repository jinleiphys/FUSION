# Brueckner Theory of Nuclear Matter with Nonnucleonic Degrees of Freedom and Relativity

> For the past 40 years, Brueckner theory has proven to be a most powerful tool to investigate systematically models for nuclear matter. I will give an overview of the work done on nuclear matter theory, starting with the simplest model and proceeding step by step to more sophisticated models by extending the degrees of freedom and including relativity. The final results of a comprehensive hadronic theory of nuclear matter are compared to the predictions by currently fashionable two-nucleon force models. It turns out that a two-nucleon force can, indeed, reproduce those results if the potential is nonlocal, since nonlocality is an inherent quality of the more fundamental fieldtheoretic approach. This nonlocality is crucial for creating sufficient nuclear binding.

# Brueckner Theory of Nuclear Matter with Nonnucleonic Degrees of Freedom and Relativity

## Key claim
A comprehensive hadronic theory of nuclear matter that includes meson and Delta-isobar degrees of freedom, medium effects, and many-body forces can be reproduced by a two-nucleon potential, but only if that potential is nonlocal; local potentials underbind nuclear matter even when supplemented with repulsive three-nucleon forces.

## Method
The author systematically extends conventional Brueckner theory stepwise: first including meson degrees of freedom via time-ordered perturbation theory with medium-modified one-boson-exchange quasipotentials, then adding Delta-isobar degrees of freedom with their associated Pauli and dispersion effects on the two-nucleon interaction, and finally incorporating many-nucleon force contributions from ring diagrams involving isobars. The final extension uses Dirac-Brueckner-Hartree-Fock theory, where single-particle motion obeys the Dirac equation with density-dependent scalar and vector fields. All calculations use the two hole-line approximation with the continuous choice for the single-particle potential.

## Key numbers
- Empirical nuclear matter saturation: density ρ₀ = 0.17 ± 0.02 fm⁻³ (Fermi momentum k_F = 1.35 ± 0.05 fm⁻¹), energy per nucleon E/A = −16 ± 1 MeV.
- Medium effect on meson propagation reduces binding by about 2 MeV at nuclear matter density.
- In triton calculations, Delta-isobar three-nucleon force diagrams contribute −0.66 MeV attraction, while the dispersive effect on the two-nucleon force involving Delta isobars contributes +1.08 MeV repulsion, giving a net repulsion of 0.42 MeV.
- The CD-Bonn nonlocal potential reproduces the comprehensive hadronic theory result for k_F ≤ 1.6 fm⁻¹, where the two curves are indistinguishable on the figure scale.
- Local potentials (Argonne V_18, Nijm-II, Reid93) produce nearly identical saturation curves that underbind nuclear matter relative to the empirical region.

## Context
This work extends the Brueckner theory program initiated by Brueckner and Gammel (1958) and elaborated by Bethe and students in the 1960s, following the nuclear matter crisis of the mid-1970s that was resolved by consistent calculations from Day (1978, 1981) and the variational approach of the Urbana group. The author compares results to high-precision potentials from the 1990s (Argonne V_18, Nijm-II, Reid93, CD-Bonn) and to variational calculations by Akmal et al. (1998) that use a repulsive phenomenological three-nucleon force. The findings enable a resolution of the Coester band problem: nonlocality in the two-nucleon force, combined with relativistic Dirac effects, can reproduce empirical saturation, while local potentials cannot.
