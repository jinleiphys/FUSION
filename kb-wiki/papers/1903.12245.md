# The Schwinger Scattering of Twisted Neutrons by Nuclei

> Thanks to J.~Schwinger, the process of elastic scattering of neutrons by nuclei is known to depend on the interference between a nuclear amplitude and an electromagnetic one for small scattering angles, resulting in spin asymmetries of a cross section or in polarization of the scattered neutrons. While this interference depends on the neutron's {\it transverse} polarization and on {\it an imaginary part} of the nuclear amplitude, this conclusion holds only for the incident plane-wave neutrons with a definite momentum. Here we show that this scattering is altered when the twisted neutrons, recently obtained experimentally, are used instead -- that is, neutrons with an orbital angular momentum. For bulk targets, the angular distributions of the scattered neutrons get modified, while scattering of a superposition of states with the different angular momenta also reveals dependence on the longitudinal polarization. For well-localized targets, the observables develop a dependence on the neutron's {\it helicity} and on {\it a real part} of the nuclear amplitude, providing full access to its phase already in the Born approximation. We argue that the corresponding spin asymmetries are measurable at existing neutron facilities. Thus, scattering of the twisted neutrons by nuclei can provide means for quantum tomography of the neutron states and become a useful tool for hadronic studies, low-energy nuclear physics, tests of fundamental symmetries, and neutron optics.

## Key claim

The paper shows that using twisted neutrons (neutrons carrying orbital angular momentum) alters the standard Schwinger scattering process; for well-localized targets, the observables develop a dependence on the neutron's helicity and on the real part of the nuclear amplitude, providing full access to its phase already in the Born approximation, whereas for plane-wave neutrons this dependence arises only beyond tree level.

## Method

The authors model the incident twisted neutrons as Bessel states with a well-defined longitudinal momentum, transverse momentum magnitude, total angular momentum projection, and helicity. They compute the scattering cross section for two scenarios: (1) a macroscopic (bulk) target, where the cross section is obtained by averaging over the atoms' positions relative to the beam axis, and (2) a single nucleus or a mesoscopic (sub-wavelength-sized) target, where the full scattering amplitude is derived from the coherent superposition of plane-wave components. The analysis includes both single-Bessel-state beams and coherent superpositions of two Bessel states with different total angular momentum projections.

## Key numbers

* For thermal neutrons (energy near 25 meV) on a gold target (197Au), the ratio of the electromagnetic to nuclear amplitude is epsilon = |beta/a| ≈ 0.03, and |Im(a)/a| ≈ 2 × 10^{-4}.
* For a macroscopic target with a superposition of two Bessel states, the longitudinal polarization asymmetry A_zeta_z is of order 0.01 × Im(a)/|a|, amounting to a few ppm for thermal neutrons on gold.
* For a macroscopic target with a superposition of two Bessel states, the final neutron polarization after scattering from an unpolarized initial beam is |⟨zeta'⟩| ∼ 0.1 × |Im(a)|/|a|, amounting to tens of ppm for thermal neutrons on gold.
* For a well-localized (mesoscopic) target with Gaussian width sigma_t ∼ 1/kappa to 10/kappa and scattering angles theta' < theta ∼ 1° to 10°, the helicity asymmetry |A_lambda| reaches values of 10^{-3} to 10^{-1}.
* The predicted spin asymmetries range from 10^{-6} to 10^{-1} for realistic parameters, detectable at existing neutron facilities that measure asymmetries down to 10^{-8}.

## Context

This work extends the classic Schwinger scattering theory (Schwinger, 1948) for plane-wave neutrons to the case of twisted neutrons, which were recently generated experimentally at NIST (2015). The results are analogous to effects seen in scattering of relativistic twisted electrons (Ivanov and Karlovets, prior work). The predicted helicity asymmetries can be measured using experimental methodology developed for parity-violating neutron scattering (Musolf, review), and the ability to access the real part of the nuclear amplitude in the Born approximation provides a new tool for low-energy nuclear physics where perturbative quantum chromodynamics is not applicable.
