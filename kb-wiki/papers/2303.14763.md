# Can the PREX-2 and CREX results be understood by relativistic mean-field models with the astrophysical constraints?

> We construct new effective interactions using the relativistic mean-field models with the isoscalar- and isovector-meson mixing, $σ^{2}\bmδ^{2}$ and $ω_μω^μ\bmρ_ν\bmρ^ν$. Taking into account the particle flow data in heavy-ion collisions, the observed mass of PSR J0740$+$6620, and the tidal deformability of a neutron star from binary merger events, GW170817 and GW190814, we study the ground-state properties of finite, closed-shell nuclei, and try to explain the recent results from the PREX-2 and CREX experiments. It is found that the $σ$--$δ$ mixing is very powerful to understand the terrestrial experiments and astrophysical observations of neutron stars self-consistently. We can predict the large neutron skin thickness of $^{208}$Pb, $R_{\rm skin}^{208}=0.243$~fm, using the slope parameter of nuclear symmetry energy, $L=70$~MeV, which is consistent with the PREX-2 result. However, to explain the CREX data, it is preferable to adopt the small value of $L=20$~MeV. It is very difficult to understand the PREX-2 and CREX results simultaneously within relativistic mean-field models.

# Can the PREX-2 and CREX results be understood by relativistic mean-field models with the astrophysical constraints?

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that relativistic mean-field models incorporating isoscalar-isovector meson mixing (sigma-delta and omega-rho) cannot simultaneously reproduce both the PREX-2 neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and the CREX neutron skin thickness of 48Ca, even when constrained by heavy-ion collision flow data, neutron star mass measurements, and tidal deformability observations. The authors find that explaining PREX-2 requires a slope parameter L=70 MeV, while explaining CREX requires L=20 MeV, and these values are incompatible within a single model framework.

## Method

The authors constructed three new effective interactions (OMEG1, OMEG2, OMEG3) using relativistic mean-field theory with nonlinear couplings including sigma-delta mixing (Λσδ) and omega-rho mixing (Λωρ). They solved the Dirac equation for nucleons and Klein-Gordon equations for meson fields in spherical nuclei, calibrating model parameters to reproduce binding energies per nucleon and charge radii of closed-shell nuclei while simultaneously satisfying constraints from heavy-ion collision flow data, the observed mass of PSR J0740+6620 (2.072 solar masses), and tidal deformability measurements from GW170817 and GW190814. The models were then used to compute neutron skin thicknesses of 48Ca and 208Pb for comparison with PREX-2 and CREX experimental results.

## Key numbers

- Neutron skin thickness of 208Pb predicted by OMEG1: R_skin^208 = 0.243 fm, using slope parameter L = 70 MeV, consistent with PREX-2 result (0.283 ± 0.071 fm)
- CREX result for 48Ca: R_skin^48 = 0.121 ± 0.026 (exp.) ± 0.024 (model) fm
- Slope parameter range covered by OMEG family: 20 ≤ L (MeV) ≤ 70
- Nuclear incompressibility for OMEG family: K0 = 256 MeV
- Tidal deformability constraint from GW170817: Λ_1.4 = 190 (+390, -120)
- Tidal deformability constraint from GW190814: Λ_1.4 = 616 (+273, -158)
- Observed mass of PSR J0740+6620: M = 2.072 (+0.067, -0.066) solar masses
- RCNP measurement of 208Pb skin: R_skin^208 = 0.156 (+0.025, -0.021) fm
- MAMI measurement of 208Pb skin: R_skin^208 = 0.15 ± 0.03 (stat.) (+0.01, -0.03) (sys.) fm
- RCNP measurement of 48Ca skin: R_skin^48 = 0.14-0.20 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous RMF study (Miyatsu 2022) that introduced sigma-delta and omega-rho mixing, and addresses the tension between PREX-2 results (Reed 2021, PREX Collaboration 2021) and CREX results (CREX Collaboration 2022). The study builds on earlier RMF models such as IOPB-I (Kumar 2017) and FSUGarnet (Chen 2014), which satisfy flow data and maximum mass conditions but fail to meet the GW170817 tidal deformability constraint. The OMEG family improves upon these by incorporating sigma-delta mixing, which suppresses the symmetry energy above 2ρ0 and enables agreement with the stringent Λ_1.4 constraint. The paper confirms the conclusion of Reinhard (2022) that PREX-2 and CREX results are very difficult to reconcile simultaneously, and suggests future work should explore isovector self-interactions and isospin symmetry breaking effects from the quark level (Guichon 1987, Saito 1994).
