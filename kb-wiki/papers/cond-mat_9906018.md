# On mesoscopic forces and quantized conductance in model metallic nanowires

> Energetics and conductance in jellium modelled nanowires are investigated using the local-density-functional-based shell correction method. In analogy with studies of other finite-size fermion systems, e.g., simple-metal clusters or He-3 clusters, we find that the energetics of the wire as a function of its radius (transverse reduced dimension) leads to formation of self-selecting magic wire configurations (MWC's, i.e., discrete sequence of wire radii with enhanced stability), originating from quantization of the electronic spectrum, namely formation of subbands which are the analogs of electronic shells in clusters. These variations in the energy result in oscillations in the force required to affect a transition from one MWC of the nanowire to another, and are correlated directly with step-wise variations of the quantized conductance of the nanowire in units of 2*e^2/h.

## Key claim

Using a jellium model that deliberately omits atomic crystallographic structure, the authors show that oscillations in the elongation force and stepwise quantized conductance in metallic nanowires originate from electronic shell effects (subband quantization) rather than from atomistic structural rearrangements, establishing a mesoscopic analogy to finite-size fermion systems like metal clusters and atomic nuclei.

## Method

The authors applied the local-density-approximation-based shell correction method (SCM), previously developed for metal clusters, to a cylindrical jellium nanowire with a uniform positive background and circular cross section, assuming volume conservation during elongation. The total energy was separated into a smooth extended Thomas-Fermi (ETF) contribution and an oscillatory shell correction term arising from the discrete quantized electronic subbands, with the chemical potential determined by charge neutrality. The elongation force was then derived from the derivative of the total energy with respect to length, and the conductance was calculated using the Landauer expression with unit transmission coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The smooth contribution to the energy per unit volume decreases slowly toward the bulk value of -2.25 eV per atom for sodium
- The shell corrections are much smaller in magnitude than the smooth contribution and exhibit oscillatory behavior (shown in units of 10^-4 eV/a.u.^3)
- The magnitude of the total elongation forces is comparable to measured values in the nN range
- The conductance steps follow the sequence 1g0, 2g0, 2g0, 1g0, ... (where g0 = 2e^2/h), reflecting the circular symmetry of the cylindrical wire cross section
- The Fermi wavelength for sodium is lambda_F = 12.91 a.u.
- The jellium background density corresponds to sodium with rs = 4 a.u.

## Context

This work extends the shell correction methodology developed for simple-metal clusters by Yannouleas and Landman (1993, 1995) and for atomic nuclei by Strutinsky (1967) to the case of metallic nanowires, providing a mesoscopic framework that complements atomistic first-principles molecular dynamics simulations by Landman, Luedtke, and Barnett (1990, 1996, 1997). The results enable a unified understanding of force oscillations and conductance quantization in nanowires as manifestations of electronic shell effects, and the authors note that the Sharvin-Weyl formula corrected for finite confining potential height yields conductance values that approximate well the step-rises shown in their calculations.
