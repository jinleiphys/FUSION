# Neutron production from thermonuclear reactions in laser-generated plasmas

> The production of intense neutron beams via thermonuclear reactions in laser-generated plasmas is investigated theoretically. So far, state-of-the-art neutron beams are produced via laser-induced particle acceleration leading to high-energy particle beams that subsequently interact with a secondary target. Here we show that neutron beams of two orders of magnitude narrower bandwidth can be obtained from thermonuclear reactions in plasmas generated by Petawatt-class lasers. The intensity of such neutron beams is about one or two orders of magnitude lower than the one of the state-of-the-art laser-driven neutron beams. We study to this end the reaction $^2$H($d$, $n$)$^3$He in plasmas generated by Petawatt-class lasers interacting with D$_2$ gas jet targets and CD$_2$ solid-state targets. The results also shows the possibility of direct measurements of reaction rates at low temperatures of astrophysical interests. In addition, the use of CD$_2$ solid-state targets can also lead to great enhancements on the plasma screening compared to the case of D$_2$ gas jet targets, opening new possibilities to study this so far unsolved issue in the field of astrophysics.

## Key claim

The paper claims that neutron beams with approximately two orders of magnitude narrower energy bandwidth than state-of-the-art laser-driven neutron beams can be obtained from thermonuclear reactions in plasmas generated by Petawatt-class lasers, but with the qualification that the intensity of such neutron beams is about one or two orders of magnitude lower than the state-of-the-art laser-driven neutron beams.

## Method

The author theoretically investigates the reaction 2H(d,n)3He in plasmas generated by Petawatt-class lasers interacting with D2 gas jet targets and CD2 solid-state targets. For the low-density D2 gas jet case, a spherical plasma model with a scaling law connecting laser parameters to electron temperature is used, while for the high-density CD2 solid-state case, 2-dimensional particle-in-cell (PIC) simulations using the EPOCH code are performed to model plasma generation. Neutron spectra are calculated using relativistic formulas for fusion product spectra in thermonuclear reactions, and plasma screening effects are analyzed using standard weak and strong screening models.

## Key numbers

- Neutron events reach a maximal value of approximately 10^9 per pulse at a temperature of approximately 10 keV for D2 gas jet targets with deuterium number density n_d = 10^21 cm^-3, laser pulse energy E_laser = 500 J, and laser absorption fraction f_abs = 0.1
- Neutron events reach a maximal value of approximately 10^10 per pulse at a temperature of approximately 10 keV for CD2 solid-state targets with carbon number density 4 x 10^22 cm^-3, laser pulse energy E_laser = 500 J, and laser absorption fraction f_abs = 0.2
- The FWHM of the neutron spectrum for a plasma with a temperature of a few keV is in the order of 100 keV, about two orders of magnitude narrower than state-of-the-art laser-driven neutron beams which have FWHM in the order of 10 MeV or higher
- The 2-D PIC simulation predicts a conversion efficiency from laser energy to neutron yield of 10^6 n/J, leading to approximately 10^9 neutrons per pulse with a laser pulse energy of 500 J
- The laser intensity at approximately 10^16 W/cm^2 is favourable for neutron productions in the D2 gas jet case
- The electron-ion equilibration time is approximately 100 ps for temperatures of a few keV in the low-density case and approximately 10 ps for the solid-state density case
- The plasma lifetime is more than 500 ps for the low-density case and approximately 20 ps for the solid-state case

## Context

This work extends the study of neutron production in laser-generated plasmas beyond the common beam-target interaction approach used in experiments such as those by Roth (2013) and Pomerantz (2014), which produce intense neutron beams with broad bandwidth via laser-induced particle acceleration. The paper enables direct measurements of reaction rates at low temperatures of astrophysical interest, which has been identified as a need by Adelberger (2011) and Negoita (2016). It also opens possibilities to study the plasma screening effect for nuclear reactions, a long-standing unsolved issue in astrophysics discussed theoretically by Salpeter (1954) and others, by using CD2 solid-state targets where the screening enhancement factor becomes significantly different between weak and strong screening regimes at temperatures of approximately 100 eV.
