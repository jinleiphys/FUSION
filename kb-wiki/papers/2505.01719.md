# Realization of all-to-all fermion propagator for the first principle high accuracy strong interaction prediction

> We propose a ``blending" algorithm that projects the all-to-all fermion propagator onto spatial low-frequency modes (LFM) combined with a stochastic estimate of spatial high-frequency modes (SHFM) at each time slice. This approach enables the calculation of arbitrary-point correlation functions for arbitrary hadron states in strongly interacting quantum field theories (QFT) with fermions, such as quantum chromodynamics (QCD). Specifically, LFM allows the construction of spatially extended hadron states below a certain energy threshold by diagonalizing multi-fermion interpolation fields. Meanwhile, the local interactions required for N-point correlation functions in QFT can be approximated in an unbiased manner through a reweighted summation of both LFM and SHFM contributions. To demonstrate the efficiency of this algorithm, we obtained {\color{black} $g_A^u=0.895(15)$, $g_A^d=-0.338(15)$, $g_A^s=-0.0245(72)$, $g_A^{u+d+s}=0.533(28)$ and $g_A^{u-d}=1.2339(43)$ } for nucleon at $m_π=300$ MeV and $a=0.077$ fm using 40 configurations. The consistency check of the pion electric form factor and charge radius derived from 3-point and 4-point correlation functions is also provided.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a "blending" algorithm that projects the all-to-all fermion propagator onto spatial low-frequency modes combined with a stochastic estimate of spatial high-frequency modes at each time slice, enabling unbiased calculation of arbitrary-point correlation functions for arbitrary hadron states. The method achieves sub-percent precision on the nucleon axial coupling using only 40 configurations, but the results are demonstrated at a single pion mass of 300 MeV and a single lattice spacing of 0.077 fm, with the claim that the method is unbiased only in the limit of infinite stochastic samples.

## Method

The method projects the fermion propagator onto a "blending space" consisting of low-lying eigenvectors of the discrete Laplace operator (the distillation subspace) combined with random vectors sampled from the orthogonal complement subspace. The identity operator on the full space is approximated by assigning reweighting factors to the random vectors, ensuring unbiased estimation of the complement subspace contributions. The projected propagator is then compressed from (4N_c N_T N_L^3)^2 to (4N_T (N_e + N_st))^2, where N_e is the number of low-lying eigenvectors and N_st is the number of stochastic vectors, enabling efficient computation of N-point correlation functions including disconnected diagrams.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon axial charges at m_pi = 300 MeV and a = 0.077 fm using 40 configurations: g_A^u = 0.895(15), g_A^d = -0.338(15), g_A^s = -0.0245(72), g_A^{u+d+s} = 0.533(28), and g_A^{u-d} = 1.2339(43)
- Pion charge radius from three-point function: <r^2_pi> = 0.332(29) fm^2 on C24P29 ensemble (m_pi ~ 292 MeV, a = 0.105 fm)
- Pion charge radius from four-point function: <r^2_pi> = 0.329(33) fm^2 on C24P29 ensemble
- Conserved vector current test on C24P29 ensemble with N_e = 100 and N_st = 400: R_pi^V = 1.0002(29) using 90 configurations, averaged over t/a in [1,9]
- Excited state mass gap from joint 3-state fit: Delta_1 = 0.493(27) GeV
- Singlet renormalization factor Z_A^s / Z_A^ns = 1.054(14) at MSbar 2 GeV

## Context

This work extends the distillation method of Peardon et al. (2009) and Morningstar et al. (2011) by adding stochastic sampling of high-frequency modes to recover unbiased estimates of local operators, overcoming the limitation that distillation alone cannot eliminate excited-state contamination in nucleon matrix elements. The results for g_A^{u-d} = 1.2339(43) are consistent with the RQCD collaboration result of 1.217(10) (two-state) and 1.238(24) (three-state) at similar pion mass using 1132 configurations (Bali et al., 2023), and with the CalLAT result of 1.235(11) at a ~ 0.09 fm using 784 configurations (Chang et al., 2018). The method enables future calculations of QED and isospin symmetry breaking corrections via N-point functions, as well as applications to parton physics through large momentum effective theory and hadronic tensor calculations.
