# The Rare Earth Peak : An Overlooked r-Process Diagnostic

> The astrophysical site or sites responsible for the r-process of nucleosynthesis still remains an enigma. Since the rare earth region is formed in the latter stages of the r-process it provides a unique probe of the astrophysical conditions during which the r-process takes place. We use features of a successful rare earth region in the context of a high entropy r-process (S>100k_B) and discuss the types of astrophysical conditions that produce abundance patterns that best match meteoritic and observational data. Despite uncertainties in nuclear physics input, this method effectively constrains astrophysical conditions.

## Key claim

The rare earth peak (A~160) can serve as a diagnostic to constrain the freeze-out conditions of high-entropy r-process scenarios (S > 100 k_B), even when nuclear physics uncertainties are large, by requiring that simulations simultaneously match the shape of the rare earth peak, the ratio of the rare earth peak to the A=195 platinum peak, and avoid excessive late-time neutron capture.

## Method

The authors performed r-process nucleosynthesis calculations using a fully dynamic nuclear reaction network of approximately 4000 nuclides, with a parameterized density evolution that distinguishes "hot" freeze-out (n~2) from "cold" freeze-out (n > 5). They varied entropy per baryon (S from 100 to 400 k_B), electron fraction (Y_e = 0.30 or 0.40), and used three different nuclear data sets (FRDM, ETFSI-Q, HFB-17) to account for nuclear physics uncertainties. Final abundances were compared to both an averaged data set of five r-process rich halo stars and to solar r-process residuals, using three quantitative constraints: the goodness-of-fit of the rare earth peak shape (G_Z^REP or G_A^REP), the ratio of the A=195 peak to the rare earth peak (eta_Z or eta_A), and the late-time neutron capture effect (Delta G_A^REE).

## Key numbers

* Favorable freeze-out conditions without fission for Y_e = 0.30: entropy window S ~ 150 k_B to S ~ 200 k_B, with dynamical timescale tau ~ 27 ms.
* Favorable freeze-out conditions without fission for Y_e = 0.40: entropy window S ~ 300 k_B to S ~ 350 k_B, with same timescale.
* With fission included, a second favorable region appears around S ~ 325 k_B to S ~ 375 k_B for Y_e = 0.30.
* The cut-off value for good agreement with halo star rare earth peak shape was G_Z^REP = 0.03; for solar isotopic data the cut-off was G_A^REP = 0.15.
* The upper bound for the A=195 to rare earth peak ratio constraint was eta_Z = 2 for halo star data and eta_A = 4 for solar data.
* The late-time neutron capture constraint used an upper bound of Delta G_A^REE = 1.
* Fission becomes influential for abundances at or above the rare earth region at S ~ 275 k_B for Y_e = 0.30 and S ~ 375 k_B for Y_e = 0.40.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of r-process freeze-out conditions, which had focused primarily on the neutron-to-seed ratio at the onset of the r-process (e.g., Hoffman, Woosley, and Qian 1997; Meyer and Brown 1997). The authors build on the discovery by Surman (1997) that the rare earth peak can form during freeze-out, and on later work by Mumpower (2011) showing a cold freeze-out mechanism. The paper introduces three new constraints based on the rare earth peak itself, enabling a more detailed probe of the late-time freeze-out conditions than the classical neutron-to-seed ratio alone. The method can be applied to any astrophysical r-process model to test whether its freeze-out conditions are consistent with observed abundance patterns.
