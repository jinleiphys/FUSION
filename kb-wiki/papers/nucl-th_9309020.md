# Perturbation schemes for systems of nucleons and pions: The relationship of covariant perturbation theory, the convolution integral and time-ordered perturbation theory

> This paper is the first in a series of three which attempt to resolve the difficulties that have plagued the $NN-πNN$ problem for the past ten years. Various theoretical inconsistencies in the current formulation have been pointed out and this work aims to eliminate these inconsistencies and so, we hope, produce agreement with experiment. This is to be done by using covariant perturbation theory, in which these inconsistencies are not present. The covariant perturbation theory is developed starting from a model Lagrangian, in order to fix notation and phases. It is shown that both old-fashioned "time-ordered" perturbation theory and the convolution integral of Kvinikhidze and Blankleider may be recovered from the covariant perturbation theory when certain approximations are made. The connection of these results with the work of Klein, Lévy, Macke and Kadyshevsky is discussed. Two forthcoming papers will pursue this covariant calculation in the $NN-πNN$ system using the model and perturbation scheme developed in this paper and derive fully covariant $NN-πNN$ equations without the double counting problems present in previous covariant equations.

## Key claim

This paper argues that the failure of existing $NN-\pi NN$ equations to fit experimental data stems from the use of time-ordered perturbation theory, which omits certain time-orders of diagrams that are automatically included in covariant perturbation theory; the authors claim that only by using a fully covariant four-dimensional approach, rather than three-dimensional reductions, can all relevant time-orders be summed consistently.

## Method

The authors construct a covariant perturbation theory starting from a model Lagrangian with pseudoscalar $\pi N$ coupling and a form factor, then derive Feynman rules for single- and two-nucleon Green's functions. They impose a "no-anti-nucleon" approximation (valid at low energies) and show that both time-ordered perturbation theory and the convolution integral of Kvinikhidze and Blankleider can be recovered from the covariant scheme when specific approximations are made. The equal-time two-nucleon Green's function is expressed as a convolution integral over the covariant Green's function, and the connection to the work of Klein, Lévy, Macke, and Kadyshevsky is discussed.

## Key numbers

- The free single-nucleon propagator in the no-anti-nucleon approximation is given by $G_N^{(0)}(q) = \frac{im}{E_N(\vec{q})} \frac{\sum_\alpha u_\alpha(\vec{q}) \overline{u}_\alpha(\vec{q})}{q_0^+ - E_N(\vec{q})}$, where $E_N(\vec{q}) = \sqrt{m^2 + \vec{q}^2}$ for relativistic kinematics.
- The free single-pion propagator in the no-anti-nucleon approximation is ${G_\pi}^{(0)}_{ji}(k) = \frac{1}{2 \omega_\pi(\vec{k})} \frac{i \delta_{ij}}{k_0^+ - \omega_\pi(\vec{k})}$, with $\omega_\pi(\vec{k}) = \sqrt{m_\pi^2 + \vec{k}^2}$.
- The equal-time two-nucleon Green's function satisfies $\overline{G}_{NN}(E', \vec{p}_1', \vec{p}_2'; E, \vec{p}_1, \vec{p}_2) = \int \frac{dz dz'}{(2\pi)^2} G_{NN}(E'-z', \vec{p}_1', z', \vec{p}_2'; E-z, \vec{p}_1, z, \vec{p}_2)$.

## Context

This work extends the line of $NN-\pi NN$ equations developed by Varma (1967), Afnan and Thomas (1974), and later groups using Feshbach projection operators or Taylor's diagram classification, which suffered from incomplete dressing of the two-nucleon propagator and omission of the "Jennings mechanism" (Jennings, 1988). The paper directly addresses the inconsistency pointed out by Sauer et al. (1985) that incomplete dressing causes the $\pi NN$ coupling constant to vanish in the nuclear matter limit. It enables the derivation of fully covariant $NN-\pi NN$ equations without double-counting problems, to be presented in two forthcoming papers, and contrasts with the three-dimensional reduction approaches of Kvinikhidze and Blankleider (1992) and Klein, Lévy, Macke, and Kadyshevsky (1950s-1970s), which the authors argue lose essential physics by reducing dimensionality.
