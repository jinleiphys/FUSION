# General relativistic effects in the neutrino-driven wind and r-process nucleosynthesis

> We discuss general relativistic effects in the steady-state neutrino-driven ``wind'' which may arise from nascent neutron stars. In particular, we generalize previous analytic estimates of the entropy per baryon $S$, the mass outflow rate $\dot M$, and the dynamical expansion time scale $τ_{dyn}$. We show that $S$ increases and $τ_{dyn}$ decreases with increasing values of the mass-to-radius ratio describing the supernova core. Both of these trends indicate that a more compact core will lead to a higher number of neutrons per iron peak seed nucleus. Such an enhancement in the neutron-to-seed ratio may be required for successful r-process nucleosynthesis in neutrino-heated supernova ejecta.

## Key claim

General relativistic effects in the neutrino-driven wind from a nascent neutron star increase the entropy per baryon and decrease the dynamical expansion timescale, both of which enhance the neutron-to-seed ratio needed for r-process nucleosynthesis; however, the net effect on the neutron-to-seed ratio depends on competing effects from differential neutrino redshifts that increase the electron fraction.

## Method

The authors generalized the steady-state, analytic wind model of Qian & Woosley (1996) to include general relativistic effects in a static Schwarzschild spacetime, incorporating redshift factors in neutrino energy moments and luminosities, curved neutrino trajectories, and relativistic outflow velocities. They derived semi-analytic expressions for the final entropy per baryon, mass outflow rate, and dynamical expansion timescale as functions of the compactness parameter \(2M/R\), using a fixed \(\bar{\nu}_e\) luminosity of \(10^{51}\) erg s\(^{-1}\), core mass \(M = 1.4 M_\odot\), and \(\epsilon_{\bar{\nu}_e} = 20\) MeV, with the initial temperature obtained by numerically solving the heating-cooling balance at the neutron star surface.

## Key numbers

- For \(2M/R = 0.68\), the semi-analytic estimates give \(\tau_{\rm dyn} \simeq 0.004\) s and \(S \simeq 255\).
- For an expansion time of 0.005 s (corresponding to \(\tau_{\rm dyn} \simeq 0.004\) s), an entropy of only 147 is needed to obtain a neutron-to-seed ratio \(\gtrsim 100\), even for the "worst case" value of \(Y_e\) (from Hoffman et al. 1996).
- For the extreme case \(2M/R = 0.68\), the estimated mass outflow rate is \(\dot M \simeq 1.3 \times 10^{-6} M_\odot\) s\(^{-1}\).
- The initial entropy is taken as \(S_i = 4\) and the boundary temperature as \(T_b = 0.1\) MeV.
- The \(\bar{\nu}_e\) luminosity is set to \(10^{51}\) erg s\(^{-1}\), core mass \(M = 1.4 M_\odot\), and \(\epsilon_{\bar{\nu}_e} = 20\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analytic wind model of Qian & Woosley (1996) by including full general relativistic effects, motivated by "soft" nuclear matter equations of state (e.g., kaon condensation from Thorsson, Prakash, & Lattimer 1994) that could produce very compact supernova cores. The results confirm and generalize the numerical findings of Qian & Woosley (1996) that general relativistic effects improve conditions for r-process nucleosynthesis, but also highlight the competing effect of increased electron fraction from differential neutrino redshifts (Fuller & Qian 1996). The paper enables future work to assess whether the increased entropy and shorter expansion timescale can overcome the unfavorable increase in electron fraction, and whether the reduced mass outflow rate remains consistent with Galactic r-process enrichment estimates.
