# Dissipative spin hydrodynamics in Bjorken flow and thermal dilepton production

> We investigate the boost-invariant expansion of a recently developed first-order spin hydrodynamic framework in which the spin chemical potential is treated as a leading-order hydrodynamic variable. Considering a symmetric energy-momentum tensor and a separately conserved spin tensor, we derive the coupled evolution equations for the medium temperature and the independent components of the spin chemical potential in the presence of both viscous and spin-diffusive transport coefficients. For a boost-invariant system, only the magnetic-like components of the spin chemical potential survive, and their evolution is shown to depend sensitively on the spin transport coefficients. The transverse spin components decay more rapidly due to spin dissipation, while the longitudinal component survives for a longer duration. We further demonstrate that the evolution of the spin degrees of freedom modifies the temperature profile of the expanding medium. Using the resulting temperature profiles, we calculate thermal dilepton production rates from quark-antiquark annihilation. We find that the presence of spin dynamics enhances the dilepton yield relative to standard dissipative hydrodynamics, with the magnitude of the enhancement depending on the spin transport coefficients. Our results indicate that thermal dileptons can provide an indirect probe of spin dynamics and spin transport in the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that treating the spin chemical potential as a leading-order hydrodynamic variable (order one in the gradient expansion) rather than a subleading term leads to a consistent first-order spin hydrodynamic framework where spin dissipation and spin diffusion affect the temperature evolution of a boost-invariant expanding medium, and that this modified temperature profile enhances thermal dilepton production relative to standard dissipative hydrodynamics without spin.

## Method

The authors apply the first-order spin hydrodynamic framework of Dey (2024) to a boost-invariant (Bjorken) flow of a baryon-free quark-gluon plasma, solving the coupled evolution equations for temperature and the independent components of the spin chemical potential. They incorporate viscous transport coefficients (shear viscosity eta, bulk viscosity zeta) and spin-diffusive transport coefficients (chi_1, chi_2, chi_3, chi_4) from entropy current analysis, using a spin equation of state where spin density is linear in the spin chemical potential. The resulting temperature profiles are then used to compute thermal dilepton production rates from quark-antiquark annihilation.

## Key numbers

- The spin chemical potential components are decomposed into electric-like (kappa^mu) and magnetic-like (omega^mu) parts; for Bjorken flow, only the magnetic-like components survive, with coefficients C_omegaX, C_omegaY, C_omegaZ depending only on proper time tau.
- The transverse spin components (C_omegaX, C_omegaY) decay more rapidly due to spin dissipation, while the longitudinal component (C_omegaZ) survives for a longer duration.
- The presence of spin dynamics enhances the dilepton yield relative to standard dissipative hydrodynamics, with the magnitude of enhancement depending on the spin transport coefficients (eta, zeta, chi_1, chi_2, chi_3, chi_4).
- The spin equation of state used is S^{mu nu}(T, omega^{mu nu}) = S_0(T) omega^{mu nu}, where S_0(T) = 2 P_1(T) and P(T, omega^{mu nu}) = P_0(T) + P_1(T) omega^{mu nu} omega_{mu nu}.

## Context

This work extends the first-order spin hydrodynamic framework developed by Dey (2024) to a boost-invariant flow, incorporating both viscous and spin-diffusive dissipation, which goes beyond earlier Bjorken flow studies of ideal spin hydrodynamics by Florkowski (2019) and works by Wang (2021) and Biswas (2022) where spin diffusion did not play a role. The results indicate that thermal dileptons can serve as an indirect probe of spin dynamics and spin transport in the quark-gluon plasma, enabling future comparisons with experimental dilepton measurements from heavy-ion collisions.
