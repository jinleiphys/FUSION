# Microscopic studies of production cross sections in multinucleon transfer reaction $^{58}$Ni+$^{124}$Sn

> \item[Background] Multinucleon transfer reaction at low-energy collisions is considered to be a promising method for the production of new exotic nuclei, which are difficult to be produced by other methods. The theoretical studies are required to provide reliable predictions for the experiments and promote the understanding of the microscopic mechanism in multinucleon transfer reactions. \item[Purpose] We provide a predictive approach for production cross sections, and testify how and to what extent the microscopic approach works well in multinucleon transfer reaction. \item[Methods] We employ the approach TDHF+GEMINI, which combines the microscopic time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) with the state-of-art statistical model GEMINI++, to take into account both the multinucleon transfer dynamics and the secondary deexcitation process. The properties of primary products in multinucleon transfer process, such as transfer probabilities and primary cross sections, are extracted from TDHF dynamics by using the particle-number projection method. Production cross sections for secondary products are evaluated by using the statistical model GEMINI++. \item[Results] We investigate the influence of colliding energies and deformation orientations of target and projectile nuclei on multinucleon transfer dynamics in the reaction $^{58}$Ni+$^{124}$Sn...... \item[Conclusions] The microscopic approach TDHF+GEMINI reasonably reproduces the experimental data at energies close to the Coulomb barrier and well accounts for the multinucleon transfer mechanism. The present studies clearly reveal the applicability of TDHF+GEMINI method in multinucleon transfer reactions, which thus deserves as a promising tool to predict the properties of new reactions.

## Key claim

The TDHF+GEMINI microscopic approach reasonably reproduces experimental production cross sections for multinucleon transfer in the reaction ⁵⁸Ni+¹²⁴Sn at energies close to the Coulomb barrier, but at sub-barrier energy the theoretical results gradually deviate from experimental data as the number of transferred neutrons increases, implying limitations of the single mean-field approximation in TDHF.

## Method

The authors combined the time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) method with the statistical deexcitation code GEMINI++ to describe both the multinucleon transfer dynamics and the subsequent secondary decay of primary fragments. Transfer probabilities and primary cross sections were extracted from TDHF wave functions using the particle-number projection method, and the average excitation energy and angular momentum of each primary fragment were used as input to GEMINI++ to obtain production cross sections for secondary products. Calculations were performed for tip and side deformation orientations of the prolately deformed projectile ⁵⁸Ni and target ¹²⁴Sn at four center-of-mass energies (150, 153, 157, and 160.6 MeV) using the Skyrme SLy5 interaction.

## Key numbers

- The TDHF capture barrier for tip collision was found to be 153.8 MeV, while the side collision barrier was significantly higher at 160.6 MeV.
- At energies 157 and 160.6 MeV, fusion occurred at impact parameters smaller than 1.71 fm and 2.43 fm, respectively, for tip collisions.
- The transferred nucleon number became negligibly small at a cutoff impact parameter b_cut greater than 9 fm.
- The ground-state quadrupole deformations from HF calculations were β ≈ 0.11 for ⁵⁸Ni and β ≈ 0.054 for ¹²⁴Sn (prolate).
- The ratio N/Z is 1.07 for ⁵⁸Ni, 1.48 for ¹²⁴Sn, and 1.33 for the equilibrium value of the total system.

## Context

This work extends previous applications of the TDHF+GEMINI method to multinucleon transfer reactions, which had been applied to other systems by Sekizawa (2017) and Jiang (2018). The results are compared with experimental data from Jiang (1998) at Argonne National Laboratory and with calculations from the semiclassical GRAZING model by Winther (1994). The study demonstrates that the microscopic approach, despite having no adjustable parameters for the reaction dynamics, gives a quantitatively good description comparable to semiclassical models, and it enables future predictions for new projectile-target combinations and energies to guide experiments at radioactive-ion beam facilities.
