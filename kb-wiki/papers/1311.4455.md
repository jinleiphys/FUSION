# Coupled-channel continuum eigenchannel basis

> The goal of this paper is to calculate bound, resonant and scattering states in the coupled-channel formalism without relying on the boundary conditions at large distances. The coupled-channel solution is expanded in eigenchannel bases i.e. in eigenfunctions of diagonal Hamiltonians. Each eigenchannel basis may include discrete and discretized continuum (real or complex energy) single particle states. The coupled-channel solutions are computed through diagonalization in these bases. The method is applied to a few two-channels problems. The exact bound spectrum of the Poeschl-Teller potential is well described by using a basis of real energy continuum states. For deuteron described by Reid potential, the experimental energy and the S and D contents of the wave function are reproduced in the asymptotic limit of the cutoff energy. For the Noro-Taylor potential resonant state energy is well reproduced by using the complex energy Berggren basis. It is found that the expansion of the coupled-channel wave function in these eigenchannel bases require less computational efforts than the use of any other basis. The solutions are stable and converge as the cutoff energy increases.

## Key claim
The paper claims that bound, resonant, and scattering states in coupled-channel problems can be calculated by expanding channel wave functions in eigenchannel bases (eigenfunctions of diagonal Hamiltonians) and solving via matrix diagonalization, thereby avoiding explicit asymptotic boundary conditions; the method is demonstrated for two-channel test cases, with the caveat that convergence for states near threshold is slow and that the complex-energy Berggren basis is required to simultaneously reproduce bound and resonant states.

## Method
The coupled-channel Schrödinger equation is solved by expanding each channel's radial wave function in the eigenbasis of its own single-particle channel Hamiltonian \(h_\alpha\), which includes the diagonal part of the residual interaction. The basis may contain bound states and discretized continuum states of either real or complex energy (Berggren basis). The coupled equations are transformed into a sparse symmetric eigenvalue problem of dimension \(M = M_1 + \dots + M_N\), which is diagonalized to yield all bound, resonant, and scattering states simultaneously. The method is applied to three two-channel systems: the exactly solvable Poeschl-Teller potential, the deuteron with the Reid soft-core potential, and the Noro-Taylor potential.

## Key numbers
- Poeschl-Teller potential: exact bound spectrum reproduced with relative errors as low as 0.000% for state \(E_3 = -30.250\) and up to 39.972% for the shallowest state \(E_{10} = -0.15007\) (exact \(-0.25000\)) using cutoff energy \(\varepsilon_{\rm max}=70\) and \(N_c=70\) continuum states.
- Deuteron ground state energy: \(E_d = -2.210\) MeV (experimental \(-2.224\) MeV) with 93.7% \(^3S_1\) and 6.3% \(^3D_1\) partial wave content, obtained in the asymptotic limit of the cutoff energy.
- Noro-Taylor potential: resonant state energy \(E_{CR} = (4.769, -0.00075)\) compared to exact \((4.768, -0.00071)\) using complex-energy Berggren basis; bound states reproduced with energies \(-2.316\), \(-1.312\), \(-0.5396\), \(-0.06496\) (exact \(-2.314\), \(-1.310\), \(-0.5374\), \(-0.06526\)).

## Context
This work extends the use of continuum bases (real-energy and complex-energy Berggren bases) from single-channel problems to coupled-channel systems for bound, resonant, and scattering states, building on prior developments in Gamow states (Gamow 1928, Siegert 1939), the Berggren basis (Berggren 1968), and the Gamow Shell Model (Id Betan 2002, Michel 2002). It contrasts with direct numerical integration of coupled equations (Thompson 1988) by avoiding boundary-condition instabilities and with complex-scaling methods (Rakityansky 1998, Masui 1999, Kato 1999, Dote 2013) by treating all states on equal footing via diagonalization. The method enables future applications to Coulomb breakup problems, such as electron-impact ionization (McCurdy 2004) and astrophysical breakup reactions (Alt 2005, Capel 2012), as well as three-body Coulomb breakup of \(^{11}\)Li (Kikuchi 2013).
