# Active-sterile neutrino mixing constraint using reactor antineutrinos with the ISMRAN set-up

> In this work, we present an analysis of the sensitivity to the active-sterile neutrino mixing with the Indian Scintillator Matrix for Reactor Anti-Neutrino (ISMRAN) experimental set-up at very short baseline. In this article, we have considered the measurement of electron antineutrino induced events employing a single detector which can be placed either at a single position or moved between near and far positions from the given reactor core. Results extracted in the later case are independent of the theoretical prediction of the reactor anti-neutrino spectrum and detector related systematic uncertainties. Our analysis shows that the results obtained from the measurement carried out at a combination of the near and far detector positions are improved significantly at higher $Δm^{2}_{41}$ compared to the ones obtained with the measurement at a single detector position only. It is found that the best possible combination of near and far detector positions from a 100 MW$_{th}$ power DHRUVA research reactor core are 7 m and 9 m, respectively, for which ISMRAN set-up can exclude in the range 1.4 $eV^{2} \leq Δm^{2}_{41} \leq$ 4.0 $eV^{2}$ of reactor antineutrino anomaly region along with the present best-fit point of active-sterile neutrino oscillation parameters. At those combinations of detector positions, the ISMRAN set-up can observe the active sterile neutrino oscillation with a 95$\%$ confidence level provided that $\sin^{2}2θ_{14}\geq 0.09$ at $Δm^{2}_{41}$ = 1 eV$^{2}$ for an exposure of 1 ton-yr. The active-sterile neutrino mixing sensitivity can be improved by about 22\% at the same exposure by placing the detector at near and far distances of 15 m and 17 m, respectively, from the compact proto-type fast breeder reactor (PFBR) facility which has a higher thermal power of 1250 MW$_{th}$.

## Key claim

The ISMRAN setup can exclude the reactor antineutrino anomaly region in the range 1.4 eV² ≤ Δm²₄₁ ≤ 4.0 eV² and observe active-sterile neutrino oscillations at 95% confidence level if sin²2θ₁₄ ≥ 0.09 at Δm²₄₁ = 1 eV², but only for the specific combination of near (7 m) and far (9 m) detector positions from a 100 MWₜₕ DHRUVA reactor core with an exposure of 1 ton-yr.

## Method

The authors simulated electron antineutrino events from the inverse beta decay process using a Monte-Carlo method, incorporating the 3+1 neutrino mixing model for active-sterile oscillations. They considered a single 1-ton segmented plastic scintillator detector placed at either a single position or moved between near and far positions from three reactor types (DHRUVA, PFBR, U-Apsra), folding in detector energy resolution (σ/Eₑ⁺ ~ 20%/√Eₑ⁺), 25% detection efficiency, 80% fiducial volume, and 70% reactor duty cycle. Sensitivity was extracted using a χ² analysis with rate-only, shape-only, and combined rate+shape methods, including systematic uncertainties of 3% normalization, 1% nonlinear energy response, 0.5% energy calibration, and 2% bin-to-bin error.

## Key numbers

- Best near and far detector positions from DHRUVA reactor core: 7 m and 9 m, respectively
- Excluded range of Δm²₄₁: 1.4 eV² ≤ Δm²₄₁ ≤ 4.0 eV² at those positions
- 95% confidence level sensitivity requires sin²2θ₁₄ ≥ 0.09 at Δm²₄₁ = 1 eV² for 1 ton-yr exposure
- Active-sterile neutrino mixing sensitivity improves by about 22% at the same exposure by placing the detector at near (15 m) and far (17 m) distances from PFBR facility (1250 MWₜₕ)
- DHRUVA reactor thermal power: 100 MWₜₕ
- PFBR reactor thermal power: 1250 MWₜₕ
- Detector energy resolution: σ/Eₑ⁺ ~ 20%/√Eₑ⁺
- Assumed detection efficiency: 25%, fiducial volume: 80%, reactor duty cycle: 70%

## Context

This work extends previous single-detector studies by Behera (2019) by considering a movable detector placed at near and far positions to cancel theoretical reactor antineutrino spectrum uncertainties and detector-related systematics. The approach is similar to that used by DANSS (Alekseev, 2018), STEREO (Almazan, 2018), PROSPECT (Ashenfelter, 2018), and Neutrino-4 (Serebrov, 2018), but with the ISMRAN setup at Indian reactor facilities (DHRUVA, PFBR, U-Apsra) which have different fuel compositions and thermal powers, enabling comparison with existing worldwide results regarding the 5 MeV reactor bump.
