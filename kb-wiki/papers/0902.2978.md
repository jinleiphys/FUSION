# Effective nucleon mass and the nuclear caloric curve

> Assuming a schematic form of the nucleon effective mass as a function of nuclear excitation energy and mass, we provide a simple explanation for understanding the experimentally observed mass dependence of the nuclear caloric curve. It is observed that the excitation energy at which the caloric curve enters into a plateau region, could be sensitive to the nuclear mass evolution of the effective nucleon mass.

## Key claim

The experimentally observed mass dependence of the nuclear caloric curve can be reproduced using a schematic, empirical form of the effective nucleon mass that depends on both excitation energy and nuclear mass, rather than requiring a liquid-gas phase transition interpretation.

## Method

The authors assume a Fermi-gas relation between temperature and excitation energy for an expanding nucleus, incorporating an inverse level density parameter that depends on density and effective nucleon mass. They adopt an empirical density evolution from Bondorf et al. and tune a schematic effective mass function (shown in Fig. 2a) to match experimental caloric curve data from Natowitz et al. (2002) across four mass ranges (A = 30–60, 60–100, 100–140, 180–240), using two alternative expressions (Eq. 5 and Eq. 7) that differ in how expansion energy is treated.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state effective mass ratios used: m*(ρ₀, A) = 0.87 for A = 30–60; 0.73 for A = 60–100; 0.67 for A = 100–140; 0.53 for A = 180–240 (Table I).
- The inverse level density parameter of a uniform non-dissipative Fermi gas, K₀, is taken as approximately 15.
- The plateau in the caloric curve appears at excitation energies above 3 MeV/nucleon for the A = 100–140 mass range.
- For light nuclei (A = 30–60), the inverse level density parameter K_eff remains essentially constant up to excitation energy of 5 MeV/nucleon; for heavier nuclei it varies between 8 and 16 for excitation energies below 2–3 MeV/nucleon.
- The expansion energy uses a ground-state binding energy ε_b = 8 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the model of Sobotka et al. (2004, 2006), who showed that the caloric curve plateau arises from thermal expansion and effective mass evolution rather than liquid-gas phase coexistence, but whose calculation gave a plateau at too low excitation energy (about 2 MeV/nucleon) and no mass dependence of the plateau temperature. The present phenomenological approach reproduces the experimentally observed mass dependence from Natowitz et al. (2002), where plateau temperature decreases with increasing mass. The results enable using the caloric curve as a tool to empirically constrain the effective nucleon mass and its isospin (N/Z) dependence in asymmetric nuclei, which could be studied with radioactive beam measurements.
