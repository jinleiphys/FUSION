# Whither Nuclear Physics ?

> Nuclear Physics has had its ups and downs. However in recent years, bucked up by some new and often puzzling data, it has become a potentially very rich field. We review some of these exciting developments in a few important sectors of nuclear physics. Emphasis shall be on the study of exotic nuclei and the new physics that these nuclei are teaching us.

# Whither Nuclear Physics?

## Key claim

The paper argues that the study of exotic nuclei, particularly neutron-rich nuclei and halo nuclei, reveals phenomena that cannot be explained by conventional nuclear physics and instead point toward "new physics" requiring a fundamental rethinking of nuclear structure. The author proposes that this new physics can be understood through a framework where neutron-rich nuclei are composed of triton (³H) clusters, governed by a new "nusospin" symmetry SU(2)ₐ that supplements the familiar isospin symmetry.

## Method

The paper reviews experimental data on halo nuclei, fusion reactions with halo nuclei, and systematic trends in neutron separation energies across the nuclear chart. The author analyzes one-neutron and two-neutron separation energies plotted as functions of proton number Z for fixed neutron numbers N, identifying peaks that indicate enhanced stability. Based on these systematics, the author develops a cluster model in which neutron-rich nuclei of the form ᶻ³Z A₂Z are treated as assemblies of Z triton (³H) clusters, with an inter-triton bond energy extracted from binding energy data. The model is extended to introduce a "nusospin" symmetry SU(2)ₐ treating triton (t) and helion (h, ³He) as elementary isospin-1/2 entities for low-energy nuclear excitations.

- The radius of ⁶He is 2.57 fm, while that of ⁴He is only 1.46 fm

- The first excited state of ⁴He is a 0⁺, T=0 state at 20.2 MeV

## Context

This review extends the work of Tanihata (1996, 2001) on halo nuclei and the discovery that neutron-rich nuclei such as ¹²Be and ³²Mg show that conventional magic numbers N=8 and 20 are no longer magic (Dlouhy et al., 2003; Thoennessen et al., 2003), while new magic numbers like N=14 in ⁴²Si have been discovered (Fridmann et al., 2005). The author's cluster model and nusospin symmetry, previously introduced in Abbas (2005), aim to provide a unified explanation for halo formation, the absence of fusion enhancement in halo nuclei (contradicting conventional expectations as shown by Raabe et al., 2004), and the emergence of new magic numbers. The framework enables predictions of which nuclei should exhibit neutron halos and suggests that the product group SU(2)ᵢ ⊗ SU(2)ₐ, combining isospin and nusospin, may resolve puzzles such as the structure of the ⁴He ground state and its 20.2 MeV excited state.
