# SU(2) Chiral Sigma Model Study of Phase Transition in Hybrid Stars

> We use a modified SU(2) chiral sigma model to study nuclear matter component and simple bag model for quark matter constituting a neutron star. We also study the phase transition of nuclear matter to quark matter with the mixed phase characterized by two conserved charges in the interior of highly dense neutron stars. Stable solutions of Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations representing hybrid stars are obtained with a maximum mass of 1.67$M_{\odot}$ and radius around 8.9 km.

## Key claim

Using a two-component (baryon number and electric charge) Gibbs formalism for the mixed phase, rather than the simpler one-component Maxwell construction, yields a stable hybrid star with a maximum mass of 1.67 solar masses and radius around 8.9 km, but only for central energy densities up to 1800 MeV/fm³; beyond that the star becomes unstable.

## Method

The authors used a modified SU(2) chiral sigma model for the hadronic phase, including higher-order scalar field interactions to fix nuclear incompressibility, and a simple bag model for the quark phase (u, d, s quarks plus electrons). They applied Gibbs' phase equilibrium conditions with two conserved chemical potentials (baryon and electric charge) to construct the mixed phase, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain hybrid star mass-radius relations.

## Key numbers

- Maximum hybrid star mass: 1.67\(^{+0.007}_{-0.001}\) \(M_{\odot}\)
- Maximum hybrid star radius: 8.9\(^{+0.29}_{-0.03}\) km
- Mixed phase density range: 0.20 – 0.58 fm\(^{-3}\) (number density) and 191 – 638 MeV/fm\(^{-3}\) (energy density)
- For central energy density 700 MeV/fm³: pure quark core radius 3.29 km, mixed phase 5.08 km, nuclear crust 0.21 km
- For central energy density 1800 MeV/fm³: pure quark core radius 6.69 km, mixed phase 1.63 km, nuclear crust 0.1 km
- Maximum Keplerian angular velocity: 1.18 × 10⁴ sec\(^{-1}\), corresponding to a minimum period of 0.53 ms
- Surface gravitational redshift range: 0.2 to 0.5
- Saturation density used: 0.153 fm\(^{-3}\), binding energy per nucleon: -16.3 MeV, symmetry energy: 32 MeV, nuclear incompressibility: ~300 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier one-component analysis (Jena and Singh, 2003) by applying the two-component Gibbs formalism introduced by Glendenning (1992), which treats baryon number and electric charge as independent conserved quantities. The results show that the two-component treatment produces a more compact hybrid star (1.67 \(M_{\odot}\), 8.9 km) compared to the one-component result (1.69 \(M_{\odot}\), 9.3 km) and compared to a pure neutron star from the same chiral model (2.1 \(M_{\odot}\), 12.1 km). The broader mixed phase and thinner nuclear crust are key distinguishing features, and the sub-millisecond periods agree with results from Burgio et al. (2003).
