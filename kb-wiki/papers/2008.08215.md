# Strange Hadron Spectroscopy with Secondary KL Beam in Hall D

> We propose to create a secondary beam of neutral kaons in Hall D at Jefferson Lab to be used with the GlueX experimental setup for strange hadron spectroscopy. The superior CEBAF electron beam will enable a flux on the order of $1\times 10^4~K_L/sec$, which exceeds the flux of that previously attained at SLAC by three orders of magnitude. The use of a deuteron target will provide first measurements ever with neutral kaons on neutrons. The experiment will measure both differential cross sections and self-analyzed polarizations of the produced $Λ$, $Σ$, $Ξ$, and $Ω$ hyperons using the GlueX detector at the Jefferson Lab Hall D. The measurements will span CM $\cosθ$ from $-0.95$ to 0.95 in the range W = 1490 MeV to 2500 MeV. The new data will significantly constrain the partial wave analyses and reduce model-dependent uncertainties in the extraction of the properties and pole positions of the strange hyperon resonances, and establish the orbitally excited multiplets in the spectra of the $Ξ$ and $Ω$ hyperons. Comparison with the corresponding multiplets in the spectra of the charm and bottom hyperons will provide insight into he accuracy of QCD-based calculations over a large range of masses. The proposed facility will have a defining impact in the strange meson sector through measurements of the final state $Kπ$ system up to 2 GeV invariant mass. This will allow the determination of pole positions and widths of all relevant $K^\ast(Kπ)$ $S$-,$P$-,$D$-,$F$-, and $G$-wave resonances, settle the question of the existence or nonexistence of scalar meson $κ/K_0^\ast(700)$ and improve the constrains on their pole parameters. Subsequently improving our knowledge of the low-lying scalar nonet in general.

## Key claim

The proposed K-Long Facility (KLF) at Jefferson Lab will create a secondary beam of neutral kaons with a flux of approximately 1×10⁴ K_L/sec, exceeding previous SLAC fluxes by three orders of magnitude, but the actual impact on strange hadron spectroscopy depends on the successful integration of this beam with the GlueX detector and the completion of 100 days of data collection with both hydrogen and deuterium targets.

## Method

The experiment will use the CEBAF 12 GeV electron beam to produce a secondary K_L beam via photoproduction on a radiator, then direct this beam onto liquid hydrogen and deuterium targets inside the GlueX spectrometer. Differential cross sections and self-analyzed polarizations of Λ, Σ, Ξ, and Ω hyperons will be measured across the center-of-mass cosθ range from -0.95 to 0.95 and invariant mass W from 1490 MeV to 2500 MeV. For the strange meson sector, the final state Kπ system will be analyzed up to 2 GeV invariant mass to extract pole positions and widths of K* resonances.

## Key numbers

- K_L beam flux of 1×10⁴ K_L/sec, exceeding the flux previously attained at SLAC by three orders of magnitude
- Center-of-mass cosθ coverage from -0.95 to 0.95 for hyperon production measurements
- Invariant mass range W = 1490 MeV to 2500 MeV for hyperon spectroscopy
- Kπ final state measurements up to 2 GeV invariant mass for strange meson spectroscopy
- Projected hyperon-nucleon scattering measurements will provide approximately 40 times higher statistics than previous CLAS data
- Approximately 700 in-flight K_L beta-decay events (K⁰_L → K⁺ e⁻ ν̄_e) expected during 200 days of beam operation, with branching ratio BR ∼ 4×10⁻⁹

## Context

This proposal extends the work of previous strange hadron spectroscopy experiments at SLAC and complements ongoing efforts at RHIC and LHC to understand strangeness hadronization in heavy-ion collisions. The measurements will directly address discrepancies between Lattice QCD calculations and Hadron Resonance Gas model predictions noted by Bellwied (2013), Bazavov (2014), and Alba (2017), particularly regarding the flavor hierarchy in the QCD crossover transition region. The experiment will also enable first-ever measurements of neutral kaon interactions on neutrons using a deuteron target, providing crucial constraints for the hyperon-nucleon interaction that is key to understanding neutron star equations of state and the so-called "hyperon puzzle."
