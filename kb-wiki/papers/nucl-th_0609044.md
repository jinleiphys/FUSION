# Thermodynamic properties of nuclear matter at finite temperature

> A self-consistent approach based on finite temperature Green's functions is used to investigate thermodynamic properties of nuclear matter. The internal energy is derived from the diagrams associated to the interaction energy. Pressure and entropy up to T=20 MeV are obtained from the generating functional form of the thermodynamic potential.

# Thermodynamic properties of nuclear matter at finite temperature

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a self-consistent T-matrix approximation using finite-temperature Green's functions can reliably calculate pressure and entropy in symmetric nuclear matter up to T=20 MeV, but only when the thermodynamic potential is derived from the generating functional rather than by integrating thermodynamic relations, which introduces uncontrollable numerical errors at small temperatures. The results are restricted to two-body CD-Bonn interactions at empirical saturation density, and the authors note that three-body forces are needed for a realistic comparison to experimental data.

## Method
The authors employ a self-consistent T-matrix approximation within finite-temperature Green's functions, where the in-medium two-particle scattering matrix is solved iteratively together with the single-particle self-energy and dressed propagator via the Dyson equation. The internal energy is calculated both from the Galitskii-Koltun sum rule and from direct summation of diagrams for the kinetic and interaction energies. The pressure is obtained from the generating functional form of the thermodynamic potential, where the functional Phi is computed by integrating the interaction energy over an artificial coupling parameter lambda while keeping the propagators dressed as in the fully correlated system.

## Key numbers
- Internal energy per particle at T=0 and saturation density (rho=0.16 fm^-3): -16.63 MeV from diagram summation, -15.80 MeV from Galitskii-Koltun sum rule
- Internal energy per particle at T=20 MeV: -1.21 MeV from diagram summation, -1.29 MeV from Galitskii-Koltun sum rule
- Pressure per density (P_tot/rho) at T=0: -7.69 MeV
- Pressure per density (P_tot/rho) at T=20 MeV: 6.49 MeV
- Numerical discretization inaccuracy for direct energy estimation: about 0.5 MeV
- Difference between the two internal energy methods: about 1 MeV at all temperatures
- Entropy calculation uncertainty: |TS| approximately 1 MeV at zero temperature; entropy reliable only for T greater than or equal to 5 MeV

## Context
This work extends self-consistent T-matrix calculations by Bozek (2002) and Dewulf (2003) to finite temperatures, and provides a method to avoid the numerical errors that plague direct integration of thermodynamic relations at low temperatures. The results are compared to the Bloch-De Dominicis approach of Baldo and Ferreira using the Argonne v14 interaction, which gave qualitatively similar negative pressure at saturation density. The finding that the entropy of a free Fermi gas with in-medium effective mass closely matches the full calculation simplifies modeling of protoneutron star evolution, as noted in work by Prakash (1996). The authors plan to extend this scheme to include three-body forces and asymmetric nuclear matter in future work.
