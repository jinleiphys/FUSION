# Evolution of quasiperiodic structures in non-ideal hydrodynamic description of phase transitions

> Various phase transitions could have taken place in the early Universe, and may occur in the course of heavy-ion collisions and supernova explosions, in proto-neutron stars, cold compact stars, and in the condensed matter at terrestrial conditions. Most generally, the dynamics of the density and temperature at first- and second-order phase transitions can be described with the help of the equations of non-ideal hydrodynamics. In the given work some novel solutions are found describing the evolution of quasiperiodic structures that are formed in the course of the phase transitions. Although this consideration is very general, particular examples of quark-hadron and nuclear liquid-gas first-order phase transitions to the uniform k_0=0 state and pion condensate second-order phase transition to non-uniform k_0\neq 0 state in dense baryon matter are considered.

# Evolution of quasiperiodic structures in non-ideal hydrodynamic description of phase transitions

## Key claim

Novel solutions describing the evolution of quasiperiodic structures formed during phase transitions are found using non-ideal hydrodynamics, but these solutions are valid only under the condition of small overcriticality near the critical point, where processes are slowed down and the velocity of a seed of the new phase is much less than the mean thermal velocity.

## Method

The author derives a modified Ginzburg-Landau-type equation from the Navier-Stokes equation, continuity equation, and heat transport equation of non-ideal hydrodynamics, expanded near a reference point close to the critical point but outside the fluctuation region. The equation is second-order in time derivatives (unlike the standard first-order Ginzburg-Landau equation) and includes viscous, surface tension, and thermal conductivity effects. Stationary solutions are found for both first-order phase transitions to the uniform state (k₀=0) and second-order phase transitions to the non-uniform state (k₀≠0), with specific examples of quark-hadron, nuclear liquid-gas, and pion condensate transitions in dense baryon matter.

## Key numbers

- For the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition, the dimensionless inertia parameter β ∼ 0.01
- For the quark-hadron transition, β ∼ 0.02–0.2, even for a very low value of the ratio η/s ≃ 1/(4π)
- The fog size R_fog for the quark-hadron phase transition in energetic heavy-ion collisions is estimated as R_fog ∼ (0.1–1) fm
- For the nuclear liquid-gas transition at low energies, R_fog ∼ (1–10) fm, which is less than or comparable to the fireball size at freeze-out R(t_f.o.)
- For the nuclear liquid-gas transition, the surface tension scales as σ = σ₀ |δT|^(3/2) / T_cr^(3/2) with σ₀² = 32 m* n_cr² T_cr c
- The time scale for density relaxation is t₀ ∝ 1/(T_cr − T), showing slowing down near the critical point
- In neutron stars, an overcritical pion-condensate drop reaches size R ∼ 0.1 km for t ∼ 10⁻³ sec by density mode growth, then may reach R ∼ (1–10) km for t_T varying from ∼10 sec up to several hours

## Context

This work extends the non-ideal hydrodynamic approach to phase transitions developed by Skokov, Voskresensky, and collaborators (2008–2010) for first-order transitions, now also treating second-order transitions to non-uniform states such as pion condensation. The analysis differs from standard condensed-matter Ginzburg-Landau treatments by retaining the second-order time derivative, which is essential for describing the initial stage of seed dynamics. The results enable quantitative estimates of time scales and spatial structures for phase transitions in heavy-ion collisions (relevant for NICA and FAIR experiments), supernova explosions, and neutron star interiors, including the possibility of delayed neutrino bursts from pion condensate formation as potentially observed in SN 1987A.
