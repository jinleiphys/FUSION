# Astrophysical constraints on the confining models : the Field Correlator Method

> We explore the relevance of confinement in quark matter models for the possible quark core of neutron stars. For the quark phase, we adopt the equation of state (EoS) derived with the Field Correlator Method, extended to the zero temperature limit. For the hadronic phase, we use the microscopic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock many-body theory. We find that the currently adopted value of the gluon condensate $G_2 \simeq 0.006-0.007 \rm {GeV^4}$, which gives a critical temperature $T_c \simeq 170 \rm MeV$, produces maximum masses which are only marginally consistent with the observational limit, while larger masses are possible if the gluon condensate is increased.

# Astrophysical constraints on the confining models: the Field Correlator Method

## Key claim

The Field Correlator Method (FCM) for quark matter, when combined with a Brueckner-Hartree-Fock hadronic equation of state, can produce neutron stars with maximum masses above 1.44 solar masses, but only if the gluon condensate G2 is increased above the currently adopted value of 0.006-0.007 GeV^4 that gives a critical temperature of about 170 MeV; with the standard G2 value, the maximum masses are only marginally consistent with the observational limit.

## Method

The authors adopt the FCM equation of state for the quark phase, extended to zero temperature, and the microscopic Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone (BBG) many-body theory with the Argonne v18 nucleon-nucleon potential plus Urbana three-body forces for the hadronic phase. They impose a first-order hadron-quark phase transition using the Maxwell construction under beta-equilibrium and charge neutrality conditions, then solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equations to obtain neutron star mass-radius relations for various values of the gluon condensate G2 and the static Q-Qbar potential V1.

## Key numbers

- Maximum gravitational masses range from 1.43 to 1.78 solar masses depending on G2 and V1 values
- For V1 = 0 and G2 = 0.006 GeV^4: maximum mass = 1.43 M_sun, radius = 9.27 km, central density = 9.85 rho_0
- For V1 = 0 and G2 = 0.007 GeV^4: maximum mass = 1.46 M_sun, radius = 10.2 km, central density = 7.92 rho_0
- For V1 = 0 and G2 = 0.012 GeV^4: maximum mass = 1.76 M_sun, radius = 10.58 km, central density = 5.76 rho_0
- For V1 = 0.01 GeV and G2 = 0.006 GeV^4: maximum mass = 1.47 M_sun, radius = 9.79 km, central density = 8.81 rho_0
- For V1 = 0.01 GeV and G2 = 0.012 GeV^4: maximum mass = 1.78 M_sun, radius = 10.46 km, central density = 6.06 rho_0
- The gluon condensate G2 = 0.012 +/- 0.006 GeV^4 from QCD sum rules
- The critical temperature at zero chemical potential is T_c about 170 MeV for G2 about 0.006 GeV^4
- The correlation length lambda = 0.34 fm for full QCD
- The nuclear matter saturation density rho_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3} with E/A = -16 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier suggestion by the same group (2007) that confinement dynamics is linked to neutron star stability, now using the FCM which provides a natural treatment of confinement through color electric and magnetic field correlators. The results contrast with the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, which produces unstable neutron stars at the quark onset, and with the MIT bag model, which can produce stable configurations for certain parameter ranges. The findings constrain the gluon condensate to values that are only marginally compatible with those extracted from lattice QCD at zero chemical potential, and show that for V1 values around 0.5 GeV (as suggested by lattice calculations) the phase transition cannot occur in neutron stars at all, leaving only purely hadronic configurations with maximum masses near 2 solar masses.
