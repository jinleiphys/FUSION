# Nucleon axial, scalar, and tensor charges using lattice QCD at the physical pion mass

> We report on lattice QCD calculations of the nucleon isovector axial, scalar, and tensor charges. Our calculations are performed on two 2+1-flavor ensembles generated using a 2-HEX-smeared Wilson-clover action at the physical pion mass and lattice spacings $a\approx$ 0.116 and 0.093 fm. We use a wide range of source-sink separations - eight values ranging from roughly 0.4 to 1.4 fm on the coarse ensemble and three values from 0.9 to 1.5 fm on the fine ensemble - which allows us to perform an extensive study of excited-state effects using different analysis and fit strategies. To determine the renormalization factors, we use the nonperturbative Rome-Southampton approach and compare RI'-MOM and RI-SMOM intermediate schemes to estimate the systematic uncertainties. Our final results are computed in the MS-bar scheme at scale 2 GeV. The tensor and axial charges have uncertainties of roughly 4%, $g_T=0.972(41)$ and $g_A=1.265(49)$. The resulting scalar charge, $g_S=0.927(303)$, has a much larger uncertainty due to a stronger dependence on the choice of intermediate renormalization scheme and on the lattice spacing.

## Key claim

The paper reports lattice QCD calculations of the nucleon isovector axial, scalar, and tensor charges at the physical pion mass, finding that the tensor and axial charges have uncertainties of roughly 4% while the scalar charge has a much larger uncertainty due to a stronger dependence on the choice of intermediate renormalization scheme and on the lattice spacing.

## Method

The authors performed lattice QCD calculations using a tree-level Symanzik-improved gauge action and 2+1 flavors of tree-level improved Wilson-clover quarks with two levels of HEX smearing, on two ensembles at the physical pion mass with lattice spacings a ≈ 0.116 fm (coarse, 48^4 volume) and a ≈ 0.093 fm (fine, 64^4 volume). They computed nucleon two-point and three-point correlation functions using sequential propagators through the sink, with a wide range of source-sink separations (eight values from 0.4 to 1.4 fm on the coarse ensemble and three values from 0.9 to 1.5 fm on the fine ensemble) to study excited-state effects. Renormalization factors were determined using the nonperturbative Rome-Southampton approach, comparing RI'-MOM and RI-SMOM intermediate schemes to estimate systematic uncertainties, with final results computed in the MS-bar scheme at scale 2 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Axial charge: g_A = 1.265(49) in the MS-bar scheme at 2 GeV, with roughly 4% uncertainty
- Tensor charge: g_T = 0.972(41) in the MS-bar scheme at 2 GeV, with roughly 4% uncertainty
- Scalar charge: g_S = 0.927(303) in the MS-bar scheme at 2 GeV, with much larger uncertainty
- Lattice spacings: a ≈ 0.1163(4) fm for the coarse ensemble and a ≈ 0.0926(6) fm for the fine ensemble
- Pion masses: m_pi = 137(2) MeV on the coarse ensemble and m_pi = 133(1) MeV on the fine ensemble
- Source-sink separations on the coarse ensemble: T/a ∈ {3,4,5,6,7,8,10,12} ranging from roughly 0.4 to 1.4 fm
- Source-sink separations on the fine ensemble: T/a ∈ {10,13,16} ranging from roughly 0.9 to 1.5 fm
- Ground-state energy on coarse ensemble: aE_0 = 0.5550(56)
- Ground-state energy on fine ensemble: aE_0 = 0.4279(36)
- Energy gap on coarse ensemble: aΔE_1 = 0.53(11) (from E_1 - E_0 with aE_1 = 1.08(11))
- Energy gap on fine ensemble: aΔE_1 = 0.309(70) (from E_1 - E_0 with aE_1 = 0.737(70))

## Context

This work extends previous lattice QCD calculations of nucleon charges reviewed by FLAG (Aoki 2019) and builds on calculations by Green (2012), Bali (2014), Bhattacharya (2016), Alexandrou (2017), and others. The axial charge result (g_A = 1.265(49)) is compared to the experimentally well-determined PDG value of 1.2724(23), serving as a benchmark for lattice QCD methods. The tensor and scalar charge results provide input for ongoing experimental searches for beyond-the-Standard-Model physics in neutron beta decay, as studied by Bhattacharya (2011), and the tensor charge is equal to the isovector first moment of the proton's transversity parton distribution function, which will be further constrained by the SoLID experiment at Jefferson Lab (Ye 2016).
