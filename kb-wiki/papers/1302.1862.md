# Radiative Beta Decay for Studies of CP Violation

> A triple-product correlation in the radiative β-decay rate of neutrons or of nuclei, characterized by the kinematic variable ξ\equiv (\vec{l}_ν\times\vec{l}_e)\cdot\vec{k}, where, e.g., n(p) \to p(p') + e^-(l_e) + \barν_e(l_ν) + γ(k), can be generated by the pseudo-Chern-Simons term found by Harvey, Hill, and Hill as a consequence of the baryon vector current anomaly and SU(2)_{L} x U(1)_Y gauge invariance at low energies. The correlation probes the imaginary part of its coupling constant, so that its observation at anticipated levels of sensitivity would reflect the presence of sources of CP violation beyond the standard model. We compute the size of the asymmetry in n\to p e^- \barν_e γdecay in chiral effective theory, compare it with the computed background from standard-model final-state interactions, and consider the new physics scenarios which would be limited by its experimental study.

# Radiative Beta Decay for Studies of CP Violation

## Key claim

A triple-product momentum correlation in radiative beta decay, characterized by the kinematic variable ξ = (l_ν × l_e) · k, can be generated by a pseudo-Chern-Simons term arising from the baryon vector current anomaly; observation of this correlation at anticipated sensitivity levels would indicate CP violation beyond the Standard Model, but the correlation is also mimicked by Standard Model final-state interactions at a calculable level.

## Method

The authors compute the T-odd asymmetry in neutron and nuclear radiative beta decay using chiral effective theory, incorporating the pseudo-Chern-Simons operator of Harvey, Hill, and Hill (2010, 2012) at N^2LO in the chiral expansion. They evaluate the asymmetry for neutron decay and for the mirror nuclear transitions of 19Ne and 35Ar in the impulse approximation at leading recoil order, and compare these results with the asymmetry induced by Standard Model electromagnetic final-state interactions computed for the same systems.

## Key numbers

- For neutron radiative beta decay with ω_min = 0.3 MeV, the HHH-induced asymmetry is A^HHH(n) = -5.34 × 10^(-2) in units of Im C_HHH [MeV^(-2)], with branching ratio BR(n) = 8.60 × 10^(-5)
- For 19Ne radiative beta decay with ω_min = 0.3 MeV, A^HHH(19Ne) = -0.165 in units of Im C_HHH [MeV^(-2)], with BR(19Ne) = 8.86 × 10^(-3)
- For 35Ar radiative beta decay with ω_min = 0.3 MeV, A^HHH(35Ar) = -0.943 in units of Im C_HHH [MeV^(-2)], with BR(35Ar) = 0.0185
- The Standard Model FSI asymmetry for neutron decay at ω_min = 0.3 MeV is A^FSI(n) = 1.31 × 10^(-4)
- The Standard Model FSI asymmetry for 35Ar decay at ω_min = 0.3 MeV is A^FSI(35Ar) = -2.55 × 10^(-3)
- The best constraint on Im(g_V) from the D term measurement (NIST, 2012) is Im g_V < 7 × 10^(-4) at 68% CL
- The constraint on Im(c_5/M^2) from the neutron radiative beta decay branching ratio measurement (2010) is |Im(c_5/M^2)| < 12 MeV^(-2) at 68% CL for ω in [15, 340] keV
- The maximum positron energy (Q_EC) is 3.23883 ± 0.00030 MeV for 19Ne and 5.96614 ± 0.00070 MeV for 35Ar
- The half-lives are t_1/2(19Ne) = 17.248 ± 0.029 s and t_1/2(35Ar) = 1.7752 ± 0.0010 s

## Context

This work extends the study of pseudo-T-odd correlations in beta decay, previously limited to spin-dependent observables, to a new spin-independent triple-product momentum correlation accessible only in radiative beta decay. The correlation arises from the pseudo-Chern-Simons term discovered by Harvey, Hill, and Hill (2010, 2012) as a consequence of gauging the QCD axial anomaly under the full electroweak symmetry. The authors compute the Standard Model background from electromagnetic final-state interactions for the first time in these systems, and show that lighter nuclear candidates with large energy release and Gamow-Teller to Fermi mixing parameter ρ ~ 1.7 (such as 35Ar) are optimal for future experimental searches. A measurement of this asymmetry would constrain CP-violating kinetic mixing between QCD gauge bosons and a strongly coupled hidden sector, potentially connecting to models of baryogenesis and dark matter origin (Nussinov, 1985; Kaplan, 2009; Zurek, 2014).
