# Phase transition from quark-meson coupling hyperonic matter to deconfined quark matter

> We investigate the possibility and consequences of phase transitions from an equation of state (EOS) describing nucleons and hyperons interacting via mean fields of sigma, omega, and rho mesons in the recently improved quark-meson coupling (QMC) model to an EOS describing a Fermi gas of quarks in an MIT bag. The transition to a mixed phase of baryons and deconfined quarks, and subsequently to a pure deconfined quark phase, is described using the method of Glendenning. The overall EOS for the three phases is calculated for various scenarios and used to calculate stellar solutions using the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. The results are compared with recent experimental data, and the validity of each case is discussed with consequences for determining the species content of the interior of neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether a phase transition from hyperonic matter (described by the quark-meson coupling model) to deconfined quark matter (described by the MIT bag model) can occur in neutron star interiors, finding that such transitions are possible but depend sensitively on the bag constant and the choice of quark matter model.

## Method

The authors construct equations of state for three phases: hadronic matter using the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model with the full baryon octet, a mixed phase using the Glendenning construction, and pure deconfined quark matter modeled as a Fermi gas in an MIT bag. They solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations to obtain stellar mass-radius relations and compare results across different bag constant values and quark matter models (MIT bag and Nambu-Jona-Lasinio).

## Context

This work extends the quark-meson coupling model of Guichon (2004, 2006) and Rikovska-Stone (2006) by incorporating hyperonic matter and phase transitions to deconfined quark matter using the Glendenning (2001) mixed-phase construction. It builds on earlier QHD models by Chin (1974) and Serot (1984) that treated baryons as structureless particles, and addresses the limitation that those models produce negative effective baryon masses at high density. The results enable comparison with neutron star observations such as those by Ozel (2006) and provide a framework for determining whether hyperons or deconfined quarks dominate in neutron star interiors.
