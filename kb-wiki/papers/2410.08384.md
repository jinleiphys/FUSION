# Hole in the Deuteron

> We introduce a new observable, $A_{node}$, that allows to isolate the node in the S-partial wave distribution of the deuteron in high $Q^2$ electro-disintegration processes with tensor polarized target. The node is a signature of nuclear repulsive core and represents a crucial test of its strength, size as well as the role of the relativistic and non-nucleonic effects in a deeply bound state. Within plane wave impulse approximation the node immitates a ``hole" through which incoming probe passes through without interaction. It is demonstrated that high $Q^2$ electro-disintegration processes due to their strong anisotropy of final state interaction effects, allow to gain an unprecedented access to the node, opening up a new venue in probing elusive dynamics of the nuclear repulsive core.

# Hole in the Deuteron

## Key claim

The paper introduces a new observable, \(A_{node}\), that can isolate the node in the S-partial wave momentum distribution of the deuteron in high \(Q^2\) electro-disintegration processes with a tensor polarized target, provided that final state interaction (FSI) effects are suppressed by choosing kinematics where the recoil nucleon angle \(\theta_{rq}\) is small (e.g., \(25^\circ\) rather than \(60^\circ\)). The node is a signature of the nuclear repulsive core, but the paper demonstrates feasibility only within plane wave impulse approximation (PWIA) and generalized eikonal approximation (GEA) calculations; the actual experimental measurement remains to be performed.

## Method

The authors propose combining cross section measurements from unpolarized and tensor-polarized deuteron targets in the \(d(e,e^\prime N_f)N_r\) reaction to construct the asymmetry \(A_{node}(p) = [u^2(p) + 2\sqrt{2} u(p)w(p)] / [u^2(p) + w^2(p)]\), which eliminates the \(|w(p)|^2\) D-wave contribution and retains the S-wave node. Calculations are performed within PWIA and GEA, the latter including both PWIA and single-rescattering FSI amplitudes, using deuteron wave functions from Paris, V18, and CD-Bonn potentials. The GEA approach is validated against existing JLab data for unpolarized deuteron targets at recoil momenta up to 550 MeV/c.

## Key numbers

- The node in the S-wave momentum distribution for realistic deuteron wave functions appears at internal momenta \(p \ge 400\) MeV/c, with position sensitive to the choice of NN potential (Paris, V18, CD-Bonn).
- A calibration node exists at \(p \approx 180\) MeV/c, corresponding to the condition \(u(p) = -2\sqrt{2} w(p)\), where the deuteron wave function is well known.
- Calculations are performed at \(Q^2 = 4\) GeV\(^2\) for recoil angles \(\theta_{rq} = 25^\circ\) (suppressed FSI) and \(\theta_{rq} = 60^\circ\) (large FSI).
- The repulsive core radius is estimated from historical analyses to be \(r_c \approx 0.4-0.6\) fm in the \(^3S_1\) channel (Arndt 1966, Walecka 1995).
- Existing JLab data at missing momenta \(p_m \ge 750\) MeV/c show qualitative disagreement with conventional deuteron wave function predictions (HallC 2020).

## Context

This work extends decades of effort to probe the nuclear repulsive core, first inferred from Berkeley 345 MeV pp scattering data and modeled by Jastrow (1950) as a hard core with radius \(\sim 0.4-0.6\) fm. It addresses the long-standing problem that the S-wave node in the deuteron momentum distribution is hidden by the D-wave contribution in unpolarized measurements. The proposed \(A_{node}\) observable builds on the use of tensor-polarized deuteron targets, an idea more than half a century old, but now made feasible by the 12 GeV energy upgrade at Jefferson Lab, which enables high \(Q^2\) kinematics where FSI, meson-exchange currents, and isobar contributions are suppressed. The GEA approach used here has successfully described JLab data from Hall A (2011) and Hall C (2020) for unpolarized deuteron breakup. The next step is an actual experimental measurement of \(A_{node}\), which the authors note is currently feasible albeit expensive, with no alternative experiments planned for addressing the nature of the nuclear repulsive core.
