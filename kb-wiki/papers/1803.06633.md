# Effects of the equation of state on the core-crust interface of slowly rotating neutron stars

> We systematically study the symmetry energy effects of the transition density $n_{\rm t}$ and the transition pressure $P_{\rm t}$ around the crust-core interface of a neutron star in the framework of the dynamical and the thermodynamical method respectively. We employ both the parabolic approximation and the full expansion, for the definition of the symmetry energy. We use various theoretical nuclear models, which are suitable for reproducing the bulk properties of nuclear matter at low densities, close to saturation density as well as the maximum observational neutron star mass. Firstly we derive and present an approximation for the transition pressure $P_{\rm t}$ and crustal mass $M_{\rm crust}$. Secondly, we explore the effects of the Equation of State (EoS) on a few astrophysical applications which are sensitive to the values of $n_{\rm t}$ and $P_{\rm t}$ including neutron star oscillation frequencies, thermal relaxation of the crust, crustal fraction of the moment of inertia and the r-mode instability window of a rotating neutron star. We found that the above quantities are sensitive mainly on the applied approximation for the symmetry energy (confirming previous results). Furthermore, an additional sensitivity also exists, depending on the used method (dynamical or thermodynamical). The above findings lead us to claim that the determination of the $n_{\rm t}$ and $P_{\rm t}$ must be reliable and accurate before they are used to constrain relevant neutron star properties.

## Key claim

The sensitivity of neutron star crust-core interface properties (transition density \(n_{\rm t}\) and transition pressure \(P_{\rm t}\)) to the nuclear symmetry energy is confirmed, but the authors show that the choice of approximation for the symmetry energy (parabolic vs. full expansion) and the method (dynamical vs. thermodynamical) introduces additional systematic uncertainty that must be resolved before these quantities can reliably constrain neutron star observables.

## Method

The authors employed both the dynamical method (including Coulomb and density gradient terms) and the thermodynamical method to compute the transition density \(n_{\rm t}\) and transition pressure \(P_{\rm t}\) at the crust-core interface of slowly rotating neutron stars. They used three families of nuclear models (MDI, Skyrme, and HLPS) that reproduce bulk nuclear matter properties and the maximum observed neutron star mass, and they compared results obtained with the parabolic approximation for the symmetry energy against those from the full expansion. The derived \(n_{\rm t}\) and \(P_{\rm t}\) values were then applied to compute oscillation frequencies, thermal relaxation times, crustal moment of inertia fractions, and r-mode instability windows, using the Tolman VII analytical solution of the TOV equations for the latter.

## Key numbers

- Transition density \(n_{\rm t}\) values vary systematically with the symmetry energy approximation and method; for example, the dynamical method with full expansion yields different \(n_{\rm t}\) than the thermodynamical method with parabolic approximation, though specific numerical values are model-dependent and not given as a single number in the truncated text.
- The gradient term coefficient was fixed to \(D = 72\ \mathrm{MeV\ fm^5}\) (giving \(D_{nn}=D_{pp}=48\ \mathrm{MeV\ fm^5}\)) to produce reasonable surface thickness and surface tension values for symmetric nuclear matter.
- The saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter is taken as \(n_s = 0.16\ \mathrm{fm^{-3}}\) and the binding energy at saturation as \(-16\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- The incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter is set to \(K = 240\ \mathrm{MeV}\) for the MDI model.
- The chemical potential at the neutron star surface is taken as \(\mu_0 = 930.4\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
- An approximate expression for crustal mass is derived: \(M_{\rm crust} = \frac{4\pi P_{\rm t} R_{\rm core}^4}{G M_{\rm core}} \left(1 - \frac{2G M_{\rm core}}{R_{\rm core} c^2}\right)\), matching the form given by Zdunik et al. (2017).

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the crust-core interface by systematically comparing the dynamical method (Baym, Bethe, and Pethick 1971; Pethick and Ravenhall 1995) and the thermodynamical method (Kubis 2007; Moustakidis 2010, 2012) across multiple nuclear models, while also contrasting the parabolic approximation with the full expansion of the symmetry energy. It confirms earlier findings that the symmetry energy approximation strongly affects \(n_{\rm t}\) and \(P_{\rm t}\) (e.g., Xu 2009; Lattimer 2013) and shows that the choice of method adds further sensitivity. The derived analytical relations for crustal mass and r-mode instability conditions enable more direct connections between microscopic EoS parameters and observable neutron star phenomena such as pulsar glitches, quasi-periodic oscillations, and thermal relaxation.
