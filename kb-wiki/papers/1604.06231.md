# Evidence for creation of strong electromagnetic fields in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim
The paper proposes that a strong electric field created in relativistic collisions of asymmetric nuclei can be identified by observing pseudorapidity and transverse momentum distributions of hadrons with the same mass but opposite charges. The separation effect is observable at the NICA energy of 9 GeV as clearly as at 200 GeV, but at the lower energy of 5 GeV the separation effect is weaker and falls within statistical error bars.

## Method
The authors performed calculations within the Parton-Hadron String Dynamics (PHSD) model for Cu+Au collisions at center-of-mass energies of 9 and 5 GeV per nucleon pair. They computed the directed flow (first flow harmonic v1) as a function of pseudorapidity and transverse momentum for pions, kaons, and protons/antiprotons, both with and without including the electromagnetic field generated by the colliding nuclei. The electromagnetic field was calculated from the spectator nucleons in the early stage of the collision, and detector acceptance was taken into account.

## Key numbers
- At sqrt(s_NN) = 9 GeV and impact parameter b = 4.5 fm, the average electric field component reaches <eE_x> ≈ 0.050 m_pi^2 and the magnetic field component reaches <eB_y> ≈ 0.085 m_pi^2 in the time interval 1-2 fm/c.
- The maximal average energy density in a cylinder of radius R = 2 fm and length |z| < 2.5/gamma fm is about 1.6 GeV/fm^3 at 9 GeV and 0.9 GeV/fm^3 at 5 GeV.
- The impact parameter interval used for the directed flow calculations is 4.4-9.5 fm.
- The planned NICA luminosity L = 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1 yields a collision rate of (sigma L epsilon) = 500 events/s.

## Context
This work extends earlier findings by the same group (Toneev et al., 2014) and others (Huang et al., 2014) that the compensation between electric and magnetic forces observed in symmetric Au+Au collisions is absent in asymmetric collisions such as Cu+Au. The paper builds on the observation by Skokov et al. (2009) that charged particles in relativistic heavy-ion collisions can produce extremely strong electromagnetic fields, and on the finding by Voronyuk et al. (2011) that the compensation effect prevents observable signatures in symmetric systems. The results enable experimental searches for strong electromagnetic fields at the NICA collider using asymmetric ion collisions, with the top NICA energy being most favorable for such measurements.
