# Thallium-208: a beacon of in situ neutron capture nucleosynthesis

> We demonstrate that the well-known 2.6 MeV gamma-ray emission line from thallium-208 could serve as a real-time indicator of astrophysical heavy element production, with both rapid (r) and intermediate (i) neutron capture processes capable of its synthesis. We consider the r process in a Galactic neutron star merger and show Tl-208 to be detectable from ~12 hours to ~10 days, and again ~1-20 years post-event. Detection of Tl-208 represents the only identified prospect for a direct signal of lead production (implying gold synthesis), arguing for the importance of future MeV telescope missions which aim to detect Galactic events but may also be able to reach some nearby galaxies in the Local Group.

# Thallium-208: a beacon of in situ neutron capture nucleosynthesis

## Key claim

The well-known 2.6 MeV gamma-ray emission line from thallium-208 could serve as a real-time indicator of astrophysical heavy element production, with both rapid (r) and intermediate (i) neutron capture processes capable of its synthesis. However, the treatment of decays in the neutron-rich, A > 208 region (including theoretical alpha- and beta-decay, as well as theoretical neutron-induced, beta-delayed, and spontaneous fission rates) is presently uncertain and could affect the production of Tl-208 on observable timescales.

## Method

The authors used the PRISM nucleosynthesis network with nuclear data from FRDM2012 mass model, FRLDM fission barrier heights, GEF2016 fission yields, and AME2020/NUBASE2020 experimental data to model Tl-208 production in r-process dynamical ejecta from a 1.2-1.4 solar mass neutron star merger simulation and in i-process one-zone models. Gamma-ray spectra were computed using ENDF/B-VIII.0 database, and radiation transfer through merger ejecta was performed including Rayleigh scattering, Compton scattering, photoelectric absorption, and pair production for a uniform, spherical, homologously expanding ejecta with outermost velocity of 0.3c.

## Key numbers

- The 2.6 MeV gamma-ray line from Tl-208 is detectable from a Galactic neutron star merger at 10 kpc from approximately 12 hours to 10 days, and again approximately 1-20 years post-event
- Bi-212 alpha-decays to populate Tl-208 in approximately 61 minutes, with the A=212 chain corresponding to an emission timescale of approximately 12 hours
- Ra-224 decays with a half-life of 3.6 days, providing a gamma-ray signal around approximately 4 days post-event
- Ra-228 has a half-life of 5.75 years, ultimately producing Tl-208 via the Ra-224 decay series following a several year delay, with Tl-208 gamma-ray emission observable approximately 8 years post-event
- The earlier Tl-208 signal (on the order of days) could be observable out to approximately 200 kpc, reaching nearby Local Group galaxies such as the SMC and LMC
- The Milky Way neutron star merger rate is reported as 25 (+19/-9) Myr^-1 from one study and approximately 440 Myr^-1 from another
- The birth rate of rapidly accreting white dwarfs (RAWDs) is estimated to be between 500-700 Myr^-1 in the Milky Way

## Context

This work extends previous investigations of MeV gamma-ray emission from neutron star mergers and remnants (OlegGammas, WuRemnants, ChenGammas, TeradaGammas, Hotokezaka 2016) which focused on longer timescales and did not identify the impact of Tl-208 in a real-time event. It also builds on prior work on fission gamma rays (fissiongammas) which explored energies above 3.5 MeV and showed those were exclusively produced when fissioning isotopes are present. The paper enables future MeV telescope missions such as MeVGRO, COSI, and AMEGO to search for real-time heavy element nucleosynthesis signals, and identifies Tl-208 as the only known direct signal of lead production (implying gold synthesis) in a real-time astrophysical event.
