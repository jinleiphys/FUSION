# Projection algorithm for state preparation on quantum computers

> We present an efficient method to prepare states of a many-body system on quantum hardware, first isolating individual quantum numbers and then using time evolution to isolate the energy. Our method in its simplest form requires only one additional auxiliary qubit. The total time evolved for an accurate solution is proportional to the ratio of the spectrum range of the trial state to the gap to the lowest excited state, and the accuracy increases exponentially with the time evolved. Isolating the quantum numbers is efficient because of the known eigenvalues, and increases the gap thus shortening the propagation time required. The success rate of the algorithm, or the probability of producing the desired state, is a simple function of measurement times and phases and is dominated by the square overlap of the original state to the desired state. We present examples from the nuclear shell model and the Heisenberg model. We compare this algorithm to previous algorithms for short evolution times and discuss potential further improvements.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Quantum Projection Filter (QPF) algorithm that prepares many-body states on quantum hardware by first projecting onto good quantum numbers and then using unitary time evolution to filter by energy; the total evolution time required is proportional to the ratio of the spectrum range to the gap to the lowest excited state, and the accuracy increases exponentially with the time evolved, but the method assumes the desired energy, approximate width of the spectrum, and approximate gap are known to within 10-20%.

## Method

The algorithm uses a single auxiliary qubit and a series of unitary transformations of the form exp(-i(t_i O + delta_i) ⊗ Y_a), where O is either a symmetry operator (e.g., J^2, particle number) for quantum number projection or the Hamiltonian H for energy projection. After each transformation the auxiliary qubit is measured, and requiring it to be in state |0⟩ projects the system onto the desired subspace; the product of phase-shifted cosines defines the filter function. For energy projection, the times t_i and phases delta_i are optimized on a classical computer using a pseudo-Hamiltonian with a known gap and uniform excited-state spectrum, assuming minimal knowledge of the ground state energy, spectral width, gap, and overlap.

## Key numbers

- For the Heisenberg model on a 4x4 square lattice, the J=0 projection success probability is approximately 11%, corresponding to the fraction of the initial Neel state probability in the J=0 subspace.
- The gap from the ground state to the lowest relevant excited state is enhanced by J^2 projection from approximately 0.03 to 0.15 of the total spectrum width.
- A Qiskit implementation of the 4x4 Heisenberg problem uses 21,800 CNOT gates and 23,000 Rz and Ry gates, producing an approximation to the exact ground state with a relative energy error of 0.6%.
- The number of T gates needed to synthesize the Rz gates with precision 10^{-7} is approximately 1.5 × 10^6.
- For the nuclear shell model calculation of 10B, the total evolution time for energy projection is up to t = Δ/π ≈ 1.2, where Δ is the exact excitation energy of the first 3+ excited state.

## Context

This work extends projection methods based on unitary evolution, such as those by Ge (2018) and the Rodeo algorithm by Choi (2021), by incorporating symmetry projections to increase spectral gaps and reduce required evolution times. The algorithm is compared to constant-time and Gaussian-sampled time distributions, and is demonstrated on the Heisenberg model and the nuclear shell model for 10B using the phenomenological Cohen-Kurath interaction (1965). The method enables efficient state preparation for early fault-tolerant quantum computers and is expected to be useful for linear response studies and final state measurements in time-evolved quantum systems.
