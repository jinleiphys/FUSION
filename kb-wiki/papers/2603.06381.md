# Survival of ultraheavy nuclei in astrophysical sources: applications to protomagnetar outflows

> Outflows of rapidly rotating protomagnetars have been considered as attractive sites for the synthesis of nuclei heavier than iron, but the question remains whether these nuclei are able to survive against photodisintegration as they make their way out of their formation environments. In this work, we present new analytic fitting formulae for the photodisintegration cross sections applicable to heavy nuclei beyond iron. We confirm that the results from the TALYS simulations are consistent with the theory of the giant dipole resonance, and apply the obtained new formulae to investigate whether ultraheavy nuclei entrained in protomagnetar outflows can be disintegrated by thermal and nonthermal photons before leaving the stellar envelope. We explore two outflow models: a spherical wind model and a jetted outflow model. For nuclei accelerated to the bulk speed of these outflows, their survival depends on the model and engine properties. For spherical winds, nuclei may survive for the first $\sim100\,{\rm s}$ post-core collapse, but as the wind Lorentz factor increases, the photodisintegration optical depth sharply rises and nuclei may no longer survive. For the jetted outflows arising from progenitors surrounded with stellar envelopes, nuclei can only survive before the jet breakout time in cases where the central engine has low spin-down energy, that is, with a low magnetic field strength and longer spin period. In progenitors with more extended envelopes, the jet break out time is much longer, allowing for nonthermal photons to readily photodisintegrate nuclei in high spin-down energy cases. These results also have implications for the capabilities of protomagnetars to source ultra-high energy cosmic ray nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper shows that ultraheavy nuclei synthesized in protomagnetar outflows can survive photodisintegration only under specific conditions: for spherical winds, survival is possible during the first ~100 seconds post-core collapse, but becomes impossible as the wind Lorentz factor increases; for jetted outflows, nuclei survive only before jet breakout in cases with low spin-down energy (low magnetic field and longer spin period), while in progenitors with extended envelopes the longer breakout time allows nonthermal photons to disintegrate nuclei in high spin-down energy cases.

## Method

The authors used the TALYS simulation tool (version 1.95) to calculate photodisintegration cross sections for nuclei up to gold (A=197) in the giant dipole resonance (GDR) energy range, then derived new analytic fitting formulae for the peak cross section, resonance energy, and width. They applied these formulae to two protomagnetar outflow models, a spherical wind model and a jetted outflow model, and computed the Thomson optical depth and photodisintegration optical depth as functions of time post-core collapse for various combinations of dipole magnetic field strength (10^13 G to 10^15 G) and spin period (1 ms to 30 ms), considering Wolf-Rayet, blue supergiant, and red supergiant progenitor stars.

## Key numbers

- GDR peak cross section: σ_GDR ≈ 0.43 × 10^(-27) A^1.35 cm^2, valid for 4 ≤ A ≤ 197
- GDR resonance energy: ε_GDR ≈ 42.65 A^(-0.21) MeV
- GDR resonance width: Δε_GDR ≈ 21.05 A^(-0.35) MeV (updated from the previous constant value of 8 MeV)
- Product σ_GDR × Δε_GDR ≈ 9 A × 10^(-27) cm^2 MeV, giving approximately linear A-dependence
- For spherical winds, Thomson optical depth τ_T = 1 occurs around 100–300 seconds post-core collapse
- For spherical winds, the transition to pair-dominated outflow (t_GJ) occurs at ~300–500 seconds
- For Wolf-Rayet progenitors in jetted outflows, t_Th > t_bo,WR for all configurations except the B_dip = 10^13 G, P_i = 30 ms case
- For red supergiant progenitors, t_Th < t_bo,RSG for all cases considered

## Context

This work extends prior photodisintegration cross section parametrizations by Karakula (1993), which were based on nuclear data only up to iron (A=56), to heavier nuclei up to gold (A=197) using TALYS simulations. The results confirm the Goldhaber-Teller picture of the giant dipole resonance and are consistent with the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn sum rule scaling. The findings have implications for the ability of protomagnetars to serve as sources of ultra-high energy cosmic ray nuclei, building on earlier work by Murase (2008), Metzger (2011), Horiuchi (2012), and Bhattacharya (2022) on nucleus survival in relativistic outflows.
