# On the sensitivity of extracting the astrophysical cross section factor of the 12C(a,g) reaction from existing data [Comment on Schuermann et al. Phys. Lett. B711(2012)35]

> We address a conflicting report on the value and uncertainty of the astrophysical cross section factor of the 12C(a,g) reaction extracted from existing data. In sharp contrast to previously reported ambiguities (by up to a factor 8), Schuermann et al. suggest an accuracy of 12%. We demonstrate that the so claimed "rigorous data selection criteria" used by Schuermann et al. relies on the s-factors extracted by Assuncao et al. But these results were shown in a later analysis (by this author) to have large error bars (considerably larger than claimed by Assuncao em et al.) which render these data not appropriate for a rigorous analysis. When their "rigorous data selection" is adjusted to remove the results of Assuncao et al. the astrophysical cross section factor cannot be extracted with 12% accuracy, or even close to it. Such data on the S_E2 values at low energies deviate by up to a factor two from their fit and exhibit a sharper slope rising toward low energies, leading to strong doubt on their extrapolated S_E2(300) value and the quoted small error bar. Contrary to their claim the small value of S_E1(300) ~10 keVb cannot be ruled out by current data including the most modern gamma-ray data. As previously observed by several authors current data reveal ambiguities in the value of S_E1(300) ~10 keVb or ~80 keVb, and the new ambiguity that was recently revealed (by this author) of S_E2(300) ~60 keVb or ~154 keVb, appear to be a more reasonable evaluation the status of current data.

## Key claim

The 12% accuracy claimed by Schuermann et al. (2012) for the astrophysical cross section factor of the 12C(α,γ) reaction is not supported by existing data; when the data of Assuncao et al. are removed from the analysis due to their large, previously unrecognized error bars, the remaining low-energy data are insufficient to extract the cross section factor with such precision, and the ambiguities in both SE1(300) and SE2(300) persist.

## Method

The author re-examines the global R-matrix analysis performed by Schuermann et al. (2012), focusing on their "rigorous data selection criteria." He demonstrates that the analysis relied heavily on the s-factors extracted by Assuncao et al., which a prior chi-square analysis by this author (Gai, 2013) showed to have error bars close to 100%, rendering them unsuitable for rigorous chi-square fitting. The author then removes the Assuncao et al. data from the Schuermann et al. sample and compares the remaining low-energy SE2 data (primarily from Kunz et al.) to the R-matrix fit, finding a discrepancy of up to a factor of two and a mismatched slope at low energies.

## Key numbers

- The SE2 data at low energies deviate from the Schuermann et al. R-matrix fit by up to a factor of two.
- The SE1(300) value remains ambiguous between approximately 10 keVb and approximately 80 keVb, as previously observed.
- The SE2(300) value is ambiguous between approximately 60 keVb and approximately 154 keVb, as reported by Gai (2013).
- The chi-square difference of 32 between the constructive and destructive interference solutions for SE1(300) (with 243 capture data points) is not statistically significant enough to rule out the small SE1(300) = 7.9 keVb solution, especially given that a one-sigma variation corresponds to Δχ² ≈ 21.
- The total astrophysical cross section factor quoted by Schuermann et al. of 161 ± 19 +8 -2 keVb sits at the boundary (170 keVb) where a 25 solar masses star is predicted to become oxygen-rich and skip carbon burning.

## Context

This comment directly contradicts the claim of Schuermann et al. (2012) that the 12C(α,γ) astrophysical cross section factor can be determined with 12% accuracy, and instead supports the earlier conclusion of Gai (2013) that large ambiguities persist. It extends the critique of the Assuncao et al. (2006) data previously raised by Brune et al. (2013) and by Gai (2013), who identified systematic problems and a violation of unitarity in the E1-E2 phase angle. The paper argues that current data cannot resolve the long-standing ambiguity in SE1(300) (approximately 10 or 80 keVb) or the newly revealed ambiguity in SE2(300) (approximately 60 or 154 keVb), and it motivates new measurements using gamma-beams at facilities such as HIγS and ELI-NP, as proposed by Gai (2014).
