# Constructing approximate shell-model wavefunctions by eigenvector continuation

> Shell-model calculations play a key role in elucidating various properties of nuclei. In general, those studies require a huge number of calculations to be repeated for parameter calibration and quantifying uncertainties. To reduce the computational burden, we propose a new workflow of shell-model calculations using a method called eigenvector continuation (EC). It enables us to efficiently approximate the eigenpairs under a given Hamiltonian by previously sampled eigenvectors. We demonstrate the validity of EC as an emulator of the valence shell-model, including first application of EC to electromagnetic transition matrix elements. Furthermore, we propose a new usage of EC: preprocessing, in which we start the Lanczos iterations from the approximate eigenvectors, and demonstrate that this can accelerate subsequent research cycles. With the aid of the EC, the eigenvectors obtained during the parameter optimization are not necessarily to be discarded, even if their eigenvalues are far from the experimental data. Those eigenvectors can become accumulated knowledge.

## Key claim

Eigenvector continuation (EC) can serve as an efficient emulator for valence shell-model calculations, including first application to electromagnetic transition matrix elements, and as a preprocessing method to accelerate Lanczos iterations; however, the accuracy of approximate wavefunctions for observables like quadrupole moments degrades for nearly degenerate states, and the method's effectiveness depends on the sampling strategy and number of sample eigenvectors used.

## Method

The authors implemented a new shell-model code (ShellModel.jl) in Julia and generated 50 random sd-shell interactions by adding Gaussian noise with standard deviation σ_int = 1.0 MeV to the USDB interaction, sampling the five lowest eigenvectors for each total angular momentum J (0-4 for even nuclei, 1/2-9/2 for odd nuclei). They then solved the generalized eigenvalue problem in the subspace spanned by these sample eigenvectors to approximate eigenpairs for a validation set of 100 additional random interactions, using the factorization of one- and two-body transition densities to reduce computational cost when evaluating Hamiltonian matrix elements for new parameters.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation of USDB interaction: 126 keV for 380 energy data in mass region A=16-40
- Execution time for 10 lowest states of ²⁸Si (largest M-scheme dimension 93,710): about 3 seconds on a MacBook Air (2020, Apple M1)
- With N_s=250 samples (50 interactions × 5 states), relative errors for absolute energies: 0.7% (²⁸Si), 0.9% (²⁶Al), 0.8% (²⁵Mg), 0.5% (²⁴Mg)
- Excitation energy errors with N_s=250: 0.39 MeV (²⁸Si), 0.70 MeV (²⁶Al), 0.37 MeV (²⁵Mg), 0.51 MeV (²⁴Mg)
- With σ_int=3.0 MeV and N_s=250, relative errors still ~3% for ²⁸Si
- Execution time for approximate eigenvalues of a random sd-shell interaction: about a few tens of milliseconds
- MCMC sampling of 100,000 posterior samples (after 10,000 burn-in) achieved in about 30 minutes on a laptop
- For ²⁴Mg J=4⁺₁ state: exact energy -75.951 MeV with Q=+28.340 e fm², EC energy -74.751 MeV with Q=-25.635 e fm² (opposite sign)

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation method, previously applied to coupled-cluster calculations (EC_CC, 2018), scattering problems (EC_Scattering, 2019), and no-core shell model (EC_NCSM, 2020), to valence shell-model calculations for the first time. The approach addresses the computational bottleneck of repeated shell-model diagonalizations needed for parameter calibration and uncertainty quantification, which has been limited to the sd shell in prior work by Yoshida (2020) and Fox (2020) using principal component analysis. The method enables efficient Monte Carlo sampling for uncertainty quantification and can accelerate subsequent research cycles through preprocessing of Lanczos iterations, with potential extension to heavier nuclei and neutron-rich regions.
