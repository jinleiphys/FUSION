# Quark-Antiquark Condensates in the Hadronic Phase

> We use a hadron resonance gas model to calculate the quark-antiquark condensates for light (up and down) and strange quark flavors at finite temperatures and chemical potentials. At zero chemical potentials, we find that at the temperature where the light quark-antiquark condensates entirely vanish the strange quark-antiquark condensate still keeps a relatively large fraction of its value in the vacuum. This is in agreement with results obtained in lattice simulations and in chiral perturbation theory at finite temperature and zero chemical potentials. Furthermore, we find that this effect slowly disappears at larger baryon chemical potential. These results might have significant consequences for our understanding of QCD at finite temperatures and chemical potentials. Concretely, our results imply that there might be a domain of temperatures where chiral symmetry is restored for light quarks, but still broken for strange quark that persists at small chemical potentials. This might have practical consequences for heavy ion collision experiments.

## Key claim

Using a hadron resonance gas model, the authors find that at zero chemical potential the strange quark-antiquark condensate retains a relatively large fraction of its vacuum value at the temperature where the light quark-antiquark condensate entirely vanishes, and that this effect slowly disappears at larger baryon chemical potential.

## Method

The authors model the hadronic phase as a free gas of all hadron resonances up to 2 GeV, computing the pressure from the grand canonical partition function at finite temperature and chemical potentials. The quark-antiquark condensates for light and strange flavors are obtained by taking derivatives of the pressure with respect to constituent quark masses, using lattice-derived relations for the quark mass dependence of hadron masses and chiral perturbation theory results for vacuum condensate ratios.

## Key numbers

- At zero chemical potential, where the light quark-antiquark condensate vanishes, the strange quark-antiquark condensate retains a value of $\frac{<\bar{s}s>}{<\bar{s}s>_0}=0.4\pm0.2$
- The hadron resonance gas model includes all resonances with masses up to approximately 2 GeV
- The ratio of kaon to pion decay constants used is $F_K/F_{\pi}=1.16\pm0.04$ from lattice QCD
- The ratio of strange to light quark-antiquark condensates in vacuum is $0.8\pm0.3$ from QCD sum rules
- The coefficient $\kappa=0.021\pm0.008$ is obtained from chiral perturbation theory low-energy coupling constants
- The lattice-derived parameter $A$ in the quark mass dependence of hadron masses ranges from $0.9$ to $1.2$
- The effect persists in a range of a few tens of MeV around $T=180$ MeV

## Context

This work extends results from MILC lattice simulations (MILC Collaboration, 2004) and chiral perturbation theory (Pelaez, 2004) that indicated different critical temperatures for light and strange quark condensates at zero chemical potential. The hadron resonance gas model had previously been successfully used to study the hadronic phase and determine the critical temperature separating hadronic and quark-gluon plasma phases. The results suggest a possible domain where chiral symmetry is restored for light quarks but remains broken for strange quarks, which could have consequences for heavy-ion collision experiments and the understanding of the QCD phase diagram at finite temperature and chemical potential.
