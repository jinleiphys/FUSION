# The Nuclear Physics of Neutron Stars

> A remarkable fact about spherically-symmetric neutron stars in hydrostatic equilibrium - the so-called Schwarzschild stars - is that the only physics that they are sensitive to is the equation of state of neutron-rich matter. As such, neutron stars provide a myriad of observables that may be used to constrain poorly known aspects of the nuclear interaction under extreme conditions of density. After discussing many of the fascinating phases encountered in neutron stars, I will address how powerful theoretical, experimental, and observational constraints may be used to place stringent limits on the equation of state of neutron-rich matter.

## Key claim

Neutron stars in hydrostatic equilibrium are sensitive only to the equation of state of neutron-rich matter, but the highest densities attained in the stellar core depend critically on this equation of state, which remains poorly constrained at high density.

## Method

The paper uses relativistic mean-field models (specifically the NL3 and FSUGold parameter sets) to compute the equation of state of neutron-rich matter. These models are calibrated to ground-state properties of medium-to-heavy nuclei and then applied to predict neutron star mass-radius relationships. The predictions are compared against three types of constraints: theoretical constraints from the universal behavior of dilute Fermi gases with large scattering lengths, experimental constraints from energetic nuclear collisions, and observational constraints from X-ray binaries and pulsar timing.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear-matter saturation density: ρ₀ = 2.48 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Densities in neutron stars span from 10⁴ g/cm³ to 10¹⁵ g/cm³ (over 11 orders of magnitude)
- Outer crust extends from about 10⁴ g/cm³ to 4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³ (Baym 1971)
- Neutron-neutron scattering length: a_nn = -18.5 fm; effective range: r_e = +2.7 fm
- Universal constant for dilute Fermi gases: ξ ≈ 0.4 (Baker 1999, Heiselberg 2000, Carlson 2003, Nishida 2006)
- FSUGold incompressibility coefficient: K = 230 MeV; NL3: K = 271 MeV
- Gravitational redshift of EXO 0748-676: z = 0.35, giving M/R ≈ 0.15 (M in solar masses, R in kilometers)
- Mass and radius limits for EXO 0748-676: M ≥ 2.10 ± 0.28 M_⊙ and R ≥ 13.8 ± 1.80 km (Ozel 2006)
- Radius estimate from rotational broadening: R ≈ 11.5^{+3.5}_{-2.5} km (Villarreal and Strohmayer 2004)
- Revised mass of PSR J0751+1807: M ≈ 1.3 ± 0.2 M_⊙ (Nice 2005, revised)

## Context

This work extends the relativistic mean-field approach pioneered by Walecka (1974) and refined by Horowitz and Serot (1981), Boguta and Bodmer (1977), and Lalazissis, Ring, and collaborators (1996, 1999) with the NL3 parameter set. The paper contrasts the FSUGold model (Todd-Rutel 2005) against NL3, showing that both reproduce ground-state nuclear properties but yield different high-density predictions. The theoretical constraint from dilute Fermi gases (Schwenk and Pethick 2005) rules out many models displayed in Brown (2000). The experimental constraint from heavy-ion collisions (Danielewicz 2002) rules out overly stiff equations of state like NL3. The observational constraints from EXO 0748-676 (Ozel 2006) and the revised pulsar mass measurement (Nice 2005) provide simultaneous limits on neutron star masses and radii. The work enables future determinations of the equation of state using new facilities such as Facilities for Rare Ion Beams and Constellation X.
