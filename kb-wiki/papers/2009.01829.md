# Moving away from singly-magic nuclei with Gorkov Green's function theory

> Ab initio calculations of bulk nuclear properties (ground-state energies, root mean square charge radii and charge density distributions) are presented for seven complete isotopic chains around calcium, from argon to chromium. Calculations are performed within the Gorkov self-consistent Green's function approach at second order and make use of two state-of-the-art two- plus three-nucleon Hamiltonians, $NN$+$3N\text{(lnl)}$ and NNLO$_{\text{sat}}$. An overall good agreement with available experimental data is found, in particular for differential energies (charge radii) when the former (latter) interaction is employed. Remarkably, neutron magic numbers $N=28,32,34$ emerge and evolve following experimental trends. In contrast, pairing gaps are systematically underestimated. General features of the isotopic dependence of charge radii are also reproduced, as well as charge density distributions. A deterioration of the theoretical description is observed for certain nuclei and ascribed to the inefficient account of (static) quadrupole correlation in the present many-body truncation scheme. In order to resolve these limitations, we advocate the extension of the formalism towards incorporating breaking of rotational symmetry or, alternatively, performing a stochastic sampling of the self-energy.

## Key claim

The Gorkov self-consistent Green's function approach at second order, using two state-of-the-art Hamiltonians, successfully reproduces bulk nuclear properties for seven isotopic chains around calcium, but the description deteriorates for certain doubly open-shell nuclei, which the authors attribute to missing quadrupole correlations (static deformation) rather than to a fundamental failure of the U(1)-breaking, SU(2)-conserving framework itself.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio calculations using the Gorkov self-consistent Green's function (GSCGF) approach truncated at second order in the algebraic diagrammatic construction expansion [ADC(2)], employing a harmonic oscillator basis with e_max = 13 and e_3max = 16. Two Hamiltonians were used: the NN+3N(lnl) interaction (SRG-evolved to lambda = 2 fm^-1) and the NNLO_sat interaction (SRG-unevolved), with three-nucleon forces treated via convolution with the correlated one-body density matrix to produce effective one- and two-body interactions.

## Key numbers

- Total binding energy per nucleon deviation from experiment for calcium isotopes: 0.21 MeV at ADC(2) level, improving to 0.06 MeV at ADC(3) level
- Absolute r.m.s. deviation of total energies for even-Z isotopes: 10 MeV for calcium, 14 MeV for argon and titanium, 19 MeV for chromium
- R.m.s. deviation of two-neutron separation energies: 2.9 MeV (argon), 1.5 MeV (calcium), 2.0 MeV (titanium), 2.2 MeV (chromium)
- R.m.s. deviation of two-neutron shell gaps: 3.8 MeV (argon), 1.9 MeV (calcium), 2.9 MeV (titanium), 2.4 MeV (chromium)
- Optimal harmonic oscillator frequency: hbar omega = 18 MeV for both energies and radii with NN+3N(lnl), hbar omega = 14 MeV for radii with NNLO_sat
- Total method uncertainty for NN+3N(lnl) energies: 2.9%; for NN+3N(lnl) radii: 1.1%; for NNLO_sat radii: 0.6%
- The Z=20 proton gap is overestimated by at least 5 MeV in all considered isotones
- Smallest computed two-neutron separation energies: approximately 100 keV for 56-57Ar

## Context

This work extends the GSCGF calculations of Somà (2020) from semi-magic oxygen, calcium, and nickel isotopes to doubly open-shell systems around calcium (argon through chromium), systematically probing the limits of U(1)-breaking, SU(2)-conserving correlation expansion methods. The study follows the strategy of using symmetry-broken reference states pioneered in Gorkov Green's function theory by Somà (2011) and later extended to Bogolyubov many-body perturbation theory by Tichai (2018, 2020). The results enable future work incorporating rotational symmetry breaking (as being developed by Yao, Novario, Hergert, and Frosini, all 2020-2021) or stochastic self-energy sampling to resolve the observed deficiencies in quadrupole correlations.
