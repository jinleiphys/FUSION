# The gluon condensation effect in the cosmic hadron spectra

> Hardening of cosmic proton- and nuclei-spectra is explained by using the gluon condensation (GC) model, which states that a large amount of gluons in proton may condense near the high energy threshold. The results present the GC-effect as common origin of a series of anomalous astrophysical phenomena including the broken power-law in gamma-ray spectra, the excess in positron- and electron-spectra and hardening of proton- and nuclei-spectra.

# The gluon condensation effect in the cosmic hadron spectra

## Key claim

The gluon condensation (GC) model explains the hardening of cosmic proton and nuclei spectra as a common origin shared with the broken power-law in gamma-ray spectra and the excess in positron and electron spectra, but only if the GC threshold energy falls within the observable high-energy region and the secondary hadrons are produced through a specific cold-ball reaction chain involving helium-4 decomposition.

## Method

The authors model proton-proton and nucleus-nucleus collisions inside supernova remnants using the gluon condensation framework from QCD, where gluons accumulate at a critical momentum and produce a maximum number of constituent quarks that form a cold hadronic cluster dominated by neutral pions and helium-4. The helium-4 is then decomposed by photons into deuterons and nucleons, and part of these nucleons recombine into light nuclei according to a recombination probability parameter. The GC contribution to each cosmic-ray flux is calculated by integrating over the production chain with power-law indices for propagation and incident spectra, then added to a background power-law to produce the total flux.

## Key numbers

- The GC threshold for helium-4 production is fixed at E_He^GC = 10.8 TeV, derived from the relation E_He^GC = (m_He4 / m_pi) * E_pi^GC using the observed gamma-ray broken point at 400 GeV from Tycho's SNR.
- The predicted extra peak in the proton spectrum appears at E ~ 10.8 TeV, consistent with data showing an extra peak at E ~ 11 TeV.
- Hardening in the proton spectrum begins at kinetic energy ~ 300 GeV/n, corresponding to the slope part of the excess peak.
- The recombination probability R_A values extracted from data are: 0.46 for He, 0.14 for Li, 0.19 for Be, 0.34 for B, 0.51 for C, 0.51 for N, and 0.60 for O.
- The GC contribution to the proton flux is given piecewise: for E ≤ 10800 GeV, Phi_p^GC = 3.7e-7 (E/10800 GeV)^(-1.23) - 2.9e-7 (E/10800 GeV)^(-0.73); for E > 10800 GeV, Phi_p^GC = 8e-8 (E/10800 GeV)^(-3.03).
- The ratio of baryon to meson numbers is taken as eta = 0.01, with the inelasticity K ~ 0.5.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous GC-model applications to positron and gamma-ray spectra (Zhu et al., 2019) by predicting analogous hardening in proton and light-nuclei spectra, proposing that all these anomalies share a common QCD origin. The GC-effect is contrasted with alternative explanations for spectral hardening such as energy-dependent diffusion coefficients (e.g., Blasi et al., 2012), nonlinear acceleration in sources, and extra local sources inside SNRs. The predicted hardening in a series of light nuclei spectra (He through O) enables experimental tests of the GC model using upcoming cosmic-ray measurements, and the cold-ball state is presented as a new physical state distinct from the fireball in heavy-ion collisions and from big bang nucleosynthesis.
