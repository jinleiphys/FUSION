# Nuclear Multifragmentation: Basic Concepts

> We present a brief overview of nuclear multifragmentation reaction. Basic formalism of canonical thermodynamical model based on equilibrium statistical mechanics is described. This model is used to calculate basic observables of nuclear multifragmentation like mass distribution, fragment multiplicity, isotopic distribution and isoscaling. Extension of canonical thermodynamical model to a projectile fragmentation model is outlined. Application of the projectile fragmentation model for calculating average number of intermediate mass fragments and the average size of largest cluster at different $Z_{bound}$, differential charge distribution and cross-section of neutron rich nuclei of different projectile fragmentation reactions at different energies are described. Application of nuclear multifragmentation reaction in basic research as well as in other domains is outlined.

# Nuclear Multifragmentation: Basic Concepts

## Key claim

The canonical thermodynamical model (CTM), based on equilibrium statistical mechanics with a recursive technique that avoids computationally intensive Monte Carlo procedures, can successfully describe nuclear multifragmentation observables and be extended to projectile fragmentation reactions, provided that the freeze-out temperature of the projectile spectator is taken as independent of incident beam energy and parametrized as a function of the wound in the projectile.

## Method

The authors describe the canonical thermodynamical model (CTM), which partitions a system of A₀ nucleons and Z₀ protons at temperature T into composites according to equilibrium statistical mechanics, using a recursive relation for the canonical partition function that eliminates Monte Carlo sampling. The model is extended to projectile fragmentation by adding an abrasion stage (using straightline geometry to calculate projectile spectator composition at each impact parameter), a multifragmentation stage (applying CTM separately to each abraded nucleus with an impact-parameter-dependent freeze-out temperature), and an evaporation stage (using Weisskopf theory for light particle emission and including fission).

## Key numbers

- Freeze-out volume used in CTM calculations: V_f = 6V₀, where V₀ is normal nuclear volume
- Time scale of multifragmentation: at most several hundred fm/c (1 fm/c = 3.33 × 10⁻²⁴ sec)
- Temperature parametrization for projectile fragmentation: T(b) = 7.5 − 4.5(A_s(b)/A₀) MeV, where A_s(b) is the mass of the projectile spectator at impact parameter b and A₀ is the original projectile mass
- Freeze-out volume in projectile fragmentation: V_f(b) = 3V(b), where V(b) is the volume of the projectile spectator at impact parameter b
- System studied for CTM mass distributions: A₀ = 168, Z₀ = 75 (representing ¹¹²Sn + ¹¹²Sn central collisions after preequilibrium emission) at T = 3 MeV, 5 MeV, and 7 MeV
- At T = 3 MeV, total fragment multiplicity is about 2 (fission-dominated); at T = 5 MeV, multifragmentation dominates with intermediate mass fragments; at T = 7 MeV, system breaks into many smaller fragments
- Projectile fragmentation reactions studied: ¹⁰⁷Sn on ¹¹⁹Sn and ¹²⁴Sn on ¹¹⁹Sn at 600A MeV (ALADIN collaboration, GSI)
- Intermediate mass fragments defined as 3 ≤ Z ≤ 20
- Cross-section parametrization: σ = C exp[(B/A)(1/τ)], where B/A is binding energy per nucleon and τ is a fitting parameter
- Neutron-rich isotope production studied from ⁴⁸Ca and ⁶⁴Ni beams at 140 MeV per nucleon on ⁹Be and ¹⁸¹Ta targets

## Context

This review extends the canonical thermodynamical model originally introduced by Das Gupta and collaborators, which differs from the Copenhagen statistical multifragmentation model (Bondorf et al.) and the microcanonical models of Gross and of Randrup and Koonin by using analytic evaluation of the partition function via the recursive technique of Chase and Mekzian. The projectile fragmentation extension builds on prior work by Mallik et al. and enables calculation of observables such as intermediate mass fragment multiplicity, largest cluster size, differential charge distributions, and cross-sections of neutron-rich nuclei, with applications to radioactive ion beam production, nuclear structure studies, astrophysics, spallation reactions, nuclear waste management, proton and ion therapy, and space radiation protection.
