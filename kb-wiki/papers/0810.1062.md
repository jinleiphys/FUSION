# Theory of the spontaneous buckling of doped graphene

> Graphene is a realization of an esoteric class of materials -- electronic crystalline membranes. We study the interplay between the free electrons and the two-dimensional crystal, and find that it induces a substantial effect on the elastic structure of the membrane. For the hole-doped membrane, in particular, we predict a spontaneous buckling. In addition, attenuation of elastic waves is expected, due to the effect of corrugations on the bulk modulus. These discoveries have a considerable magnitude in graphene, affecting both its mesoscopic structure, and its electrical resistivity, which has an inherent asymmetry between hole- and electron-doped graphene.

## Key claim

For hole-doped graphene, the interplay between free electrons and the two-dimensional crystal lattice induces a spontaneous buckling instability, but this effect is conditional on the sign of the effective stress term tau being negative, which occurs only for a negative external electrochemical potential (hole doping). The abstract's claim of "spontaneous buckling" glosses over this sign condition and the requirement that the graphene be suspended and not covered by a water layer.

## Method

The author integrates out the fast electronic degrees of freedom (Dirac fermions) to obtain their contribution to the elastic free energy of the graphene membrane, working to second order in the deformation potential strength. The resulting polarization operator for the static electrochemical potential is evaluated in the one-loop approximation, yielding a renormalization of the bulk modulus that depends on the wavevector q, and a cross-term between the external chemical potential and the strain that produces an effective stress term in the elastic free energy.

## Key numbers

- Bulk modulus renormalization at q=0: 0.6 eV Angstrom^{-2} * (D/30 eV)^2 * (T/300 K), compared to the bare lambda = 2 eV Angstrom^{-2}
- Bulk modulus renormalization for ripples of 50-150 Angstrom: 3.85 eV Angstrom^{-2} * (D/30 eV)^2 * (q/0.1 Angstrom^{-1})
- Characteristic ripple wavelength for hole doping: xi = 144 Angstrom * (T/300 K)^{-1/2} * (D/30 eV)^{-1/2} * (|n|/10^{12} cm^{-2})^{-1/4}
- For adsorbed molecules with n = 10^{13} cm^{-2}: ripple wavelength as small as 100 Angstrom
- For electrical doping: xi = 270 Angstrom * (|V_g|/10 V)^{-1/4}
- Added resistivity from buckling: 200-600 Ohm * (T/300 K) * (|n|/10^{12} cm^{-2})^{-1/2}
- Speed of sound v_ph = 2 * 10^6 cm/sec; Fermi velocity v_f = 10^8 cm/sec
- Bending energy kappa = 1.1 eV; bulk modulus lambda+mu = 11 eV Angstrom^{-2}; shear modulus mu = 9 eV Angstrom^{-2}
- Deformation potential D = 10-30 eV; gauge field coupling g_2 = 1-4 eV

## Context

This work extends the theory of crystalline membranes (Bowick 2001, Nelson 1987) by incorporating the effect of Dirac fermions on the elastic properties, building on the deformation potential approach of Ando (2002). It predicts a mechanism for the experimentally observed asymmetry between hole and electron resistivity in graphene (Bolotin 2008, Morozov 2008) and offers an explanation for ripples observed in suspended graphene (Meyer 2007). The theory enables experimental measurement of the poorly-known deformation potential D through sound attenuation, and predicts that gate voltage or chemical doping can control ripple formation in suspended graphene, which should be testable by direct imaging.
