# Bulk Viscosity and Cavitation in Boost-Invariant Hydrodynamic Expansion

> We solve second order relativistic hydrodynamics equations for a boost-invariant 1+1-dimensional expanding fluid with an equation of state taken from lattice calculations of the thermodynamics of strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma. We investigate the dependence of the energy density as a function of proper time on the values of the shear viscosity, the bulk viscosity, and second order coefficients, confirming that large changes in the values of the latter have negligible effects. Varying the shear viscosity between zero and a few times s/(4 pi), with s the entropy density, has significant effects, as expected based on other studies. Introducing a nonzero bulk viscosity also has significant effects. In fact, if the bulk viscosity peaks near the crossover temperature Tc to the degree indicated by recent lattice calculations in QCD without quarks, it can make the fluid cavitate -- falling apart into droplets. It is interesting to see a hydrodynamic calculation predicting its own breakdown, via cavitation, at the temperatures where hadronization is thought to occur in ultrarelativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a sufficiently large peak in the bulk viscosity near the crossover temperature Tc can cause the expanding quark-gluon plasma fluid to cavitate (break apart into droplets), with the hydrodynamic calculation predicting its own breakdown at temperatures where hadronization is thought to occur; the authors find that cavitation occurs if the peak in ζ/s is between 1/4 and 4 times as wide as suggested by lattice calculations and higher than a threshold height between 1/2 and 1/4 that suggested by those calculations.

## Method

The authors solved second-order relativistic hydrodynamic equations for a boost-invariant 1+1-dimensional expanding fluid, using an equation of state from lattice QCD calculations (Bazavov 2009). They evolved the energy density, shear stress Φ, and bulk stress Π as functions of proper time τ, with the shear viscosity η/s set to a baseline of 1/4π and second-order coefficients τΠη and λ1 taken from N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, then systematically varied η/s, ζ/s, and the second-order coefficients to determine conditions for cavitation (when the longitudinal pressure Pξ becomes negative).

## Key numbers

- The baseline initial condition is ε = 15.8 GeV/fm³ (T = 305 MeV) at τ = 0.5 fm/c; at τ = 1 fm/c the energy density is ε = 7.12 GeV/fm³ (T = 252 MeV)
- The crossover temperature Tc is taken as 190 MeV, consistent with the lattice equation of state from Bazavov (2009) which gives a crossover between 180 MeV and 200 MeV
- The baseline shear viscosity is η/s = 1/4π; varying η/s between 0 and 2/4π produces significant effects on the evolution
- The second-order coefficients τΠη and λ1 can be increased by factors of 8 and 16 respectively with only small effects on ε(τ); λ1 must be increased by factors of ~100 to produce ~10% change in ε(τ)
- At T = 1.58 Tc, lattice calculations in QCD without quarks (Meyer 2009) give (η + 3ζ/4)/s = 0.20 ± 0.03 and 2πT τΠη = 3.1 ± 0.3

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of viscous hydrodynamic evolution in heavy ion collisions (Baier 2006, Romatschke 2007, Song 2007, Luzum 2008) by focusing on the breakdown of hydrodynamics via cavitation rather than the early-time thermalization question. The possibility that bulk viscosity could cause the expanding fluid to break into fragments was previously discussed by Torrieri, Tomasik and Mishustin (2007) using a different formalism; the present work quantifies the threshold for cavitation using lattice QCD input for both the equation of state and the bulk viscosity peak near Tc. The results motivate including the peak in bulk viscosity near Tc in hydrodynamic calculations that include transverse expansion, and searching for cavitation signatures in more realistic simulations.
