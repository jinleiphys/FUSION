# Heavy Quarkonia and Quark Drip Lines in Quark-Gluon Plasma

> Using the potential model and thermodynamical quantities obtained in lattice gauge calculations, we determine the spontaneous dissociation temperatures of color-singlet quarkonia and the `quark drip lines' which separate the region of bound $Q\bar Q$ states from the unbound region. The dissociation temperatures of $J/ψ$ and $χ_b$ in quenched QCD are found to be 1.62$T_c$ and $1.18T_c$ respectively, in good agreement with spectral function analyses. The dissociation temperature of $J/ψ$ in full QCD with 2 flavors is found to be 1.42$T_c$. For possible bound quarkonium states with light quarks, the characteristics of the quark drip lines severely limit the stable region close to the phase transition temperature. Bound color-singlet quarkonia with light quarks may exist very near the phase transition temperature if their effective quark mass is of the order of 300-400 MeV and higher.

# Heavy Quarkonia and Quark Drip Lines in Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim

Using a potential model with a color-singlet Q-Qbar potential defined as a specific linear combination of the lattice free energy F1 and internal energy U1, the spontaneous dissociation temperatures of heavy quarkonia in both quenched and full QCD can be reproduced in agreement with spectral function analyses; however, bound color-singlet quarkonia with light quarks may exist only very near the phase transition temperature, and only if their effective quark mass is of order 300-400 MeV or higher.

## Method

The author constructs the color-singlet Q-Qbar potential U_{QQ}^{(1)} as a linear combination of the lattice free energy F1 and internal energy U1, weighted by the equation-of-state parameter a(T)=3p/epsilon from independent lattice calculations. Using this potential, the Schrodinger equation is solved for quarkonium states with quark masses m_c=1.4 GeV and m_b=4.3 GeV, and the spontaneous dissociation temperature is identified as the temperature at which the bound state disappears. The free energy F1 and internal energy U1 are taken from Kaczmarek et al. (2003, 2005) for both quenched QCD and full QCD with 2 flavors, with equations of state from Boyd et al. (1996) and Karsch et al. (2000).

## Key numbers

- J/psi dissociation temperature in quenched QCD: 1.62 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential), in agreement with spectral analysis range 1.62-1.70 T_c from Asakawa (2003)
- chi_b dissociation temperature in quenched QCD: 1.18 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential), in agreement with spectral analysis range 1.15-1.54 T_c from Petreczky (2005)
- J/psi dissociation temperature in full QCD with 2 flavors: 1.42 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential)
- Upsilon dissociation temperature in full QCD with 2 flavors: 3.40 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential)
- chi_b dissociation temperature in full QCD with 2 flavors: 1.22 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential)
- Upsilon' dissociation temperature in full QCD with 2 flavors: 1.18 T_c (U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential)
- For light quark quasiparticle mass 0.3-0.4 GeV, a light quarkonium may be bound below (1.05-1.07) T_c and an open heavy quarkonium (one light, one heavy quark) below (1.11-1.19) T_c
- For a light quark mass ~1.2 GeV at 1.5 T_c (from Petreczky 2002), a "light" quarkonium may be bound below ~1.31 T_c

## Context

This work extends the potential-model approach to quarkonium dissociation in quark-gluon plasma, addressing the ambiguity between using free energy F1 (Digal 2001, Wong 2001, Blaschke 2005) versus internal energy U1 (Kaczmarek 2002, Shuryak and Zahed 2003, Alberico 2005) as the Q-Qbar potential. The proposed U_{QQ}^{(1)} potential resolves this by subtracting the plasma internal energy contribution, yielding dissociation temperatures that match spectral function analyses from Asakawa (2003), Datta (2003), and Petreczky (2005). The "quark drip line" concept, borrowed from nuclear physics (Werner and Wheeler 1958), maps the boundary between bound and unbound Q-Qbar states as a function of reduced mass and temperature. The results contradict the suggestion by Shuryak and Zahed (2003) that light quarkonium states could be bound up to a few T_c, instead confining such states to temperatures very near T_c. This enables quantitative predictions for heavy quarkonium survival in heavy-ion collisions and supports the baryon-strangeness correlation study by Koch (2005) indicating no bound Q-Qbar component at 1.5 T_c.
