# Nuclear Transport at Small Excitations: Thermal and Quantal Aspects

> The application of the locally harmonic approximation to large scale collective motion is briefly reviewed. Particular emphasis is paid to issues which might be useful in the more general context, or which are specific to our treatment, like there are: Self-consistency between collective and intrinsic motion, a $T$ dependent coupling constant, the inclusion of quantum effects for the dynamics of fluctuations. Finally, open problems are addressed, like the use of thermodynamic concepts at smaller excitations, the ergodicity question, the variation of transport coefficients with the nuclear shape due to shell structure, the limits of the LHA at smaller $T$.

## Key claim

The locally harmonic approximation (LHA) can describe large-scale collective nuclear motion (such as fission) by constructing Gaussian propagators over small time steps, but the approach faces unresolved problems at low excitation energies, including the validity of thermodynamic concepts, the ergodicity question, and the limits of the LHA itself when temperature is small.

## Method

The paper derives transport coefficients (inertia, friction, stiffness) for collective motion by applying linear response theory within a locally harmonic approximation, expanding the nuclear Hamiltonian around a given deformation Q₀ and treating the coupling between collective and intrinsic degrees of freedom via a causal response function. The collective response function is constructed analogously to the Clausius-Mosotti polarizability, and the transport coefficients are obtained by fitting the dissipative part of the oscillator response to selected peaks of the strength distribution. For fluctuations, a modified Nakajima-Zwanzig projection approach is used, with the quantal fluctuation-dissipation theorem providing the diffusion coefficients.

## Key numbers

- The collisional damping width Γ is parameterized as Γ = [(ℏω − μ)² + π²T²] / [Γ₀ (1 + ((ℏω − μ)² + π²T²)/c²)], with Γ₀ = 33 MeV and c = 20 MeV used in numerical computations.
- For the low-frequency mode along the fission path, ℏϖ (the local oscillator frequency) is typically of order 1 MeV, independent of temperature.
- The damping parameter η = γ/(2√(M|C|)) increases from about 1 at T = 1 MeV to about 3 at T = 5 MeV.
- The stiffness C(0) of the static energy can decrease by more than an order of magnitude when temperature increases from 0 to about 2–3 MeV.
- In the high-temperature limit, the equilibrium fluctuations satisfy equipartition: ⟨P²⟩/(2M) = T/2 = (C/2)⟨q²⟩.
- In the zero-damping limit, the effective temperature replacing T in the Einstein relation is T*(ϖ) = (ℏϖ/2) coth(ℏϖ/(2T)).

## Context

This work extends the author's earlier review (Hofmann, 1997) and builds on the Kramers (1940) transport description of fission, using linear response theory rather than Markovian approximations. The approach contrasts with common finite-temperature RPA methods (such as thermo-field theory) by producing strongly overdamped motion at higher temperatures, with all strength concentrating in one low-frequency mode. The paper identifies open problems including the use of thermodynamic concepts at small excitations (where excitation energies may be as low as 10 MeV), the ergodicity condition χ_ad − χ(0) = 0, and the variation of transport coefficients with nuclear shape due to shell structure.
