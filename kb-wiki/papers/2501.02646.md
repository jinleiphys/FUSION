# Diagrammatic Monte Carlo for Finite Systems at Zero Temperature

> High-order virtual excitations play an important role in microscopic models of nuclear reactions at intermediate energies. However, the factorial growth of their complexity has prevented their consistent inclusion in ab initio many-body calculations. For infinite systems at finite temperature, such drawbacks can be overcome using diagrammatic Monte Carlo techniques to resum entire series of Feynman diagrams. We present a diagrammatic Monte Carlo algorithm that can be applied to self-bound systems with discrete energy levels at zero temperature, and demonstrate its potential for the Richardson model of nuclear pairing. We show that sampling the topological space of diagrams allows the inclusion of high-order excitations that are neglected in state-of-the-art approximations used in nuclear physics and quantum chemistry. We propose that sampling the diagrammatic space can overcome the long-standing gap between our microscopic understanding of structure and reactions in nuclear physics.

# Diagrammatic Monte Carlo for Finite Systems at Zero Temperature

## Key claim
The authors present a diagrammatic Monte Carlo algorithm that can be applied to self-bound systems with discrete energy levels at zero temperature, demonstrating that sampling the topological space of diagrams allows inclusion of high-order excitations neglected in state-of-the-art approximations used in nuclear physics and quantum chemistry. The method is demonstrated only for the Richardson model of nuclear pairing in the weak-coupling regime, and the authors note that convergence acceleration methods such as Borel resummation were not needed, leaving room for approaching more realistic problems.

## Method
The algorithm expresses the exact self-energy as a stochastic sum over diagram topologies and internal quantum numbers, sampled via a Markov chain using Metropolis-Hastings updates. The static tadpole diagrams are computed separately to avoid Monte Carlo instability, while the dynamic part is sampled up to eighth order in perturbation theory using only ladder diagrams, which dominate the self-energy expansion for the Richardson model. A nonlinear least-square minimization fits the imaginary part of the self-energy as a sum of Lorentzians, enforcing the Källén-Lehmann representation and causality principle, with dispersion relations used to reconstruct the full self-energy.

## Key numbers
- Correlation energy deviation from exact full configuration interaction results is at most 1% for both coupling constants g = 0.1 and g = 0.3, using regulator η = 0.1 and model space D = 10
- The regulator η = 0.1 is used for all correlation energy calculations, with the 1% error taken as the uncertainty for all results
- Calculations performed with D = 10 equidistant doubly degenerate energy levels at half-filling with A = 10 particles (2P = 10 fermions)
- Ladder diagrams sampled up to eighth order in perturbation theory for the self-energy components p = 1 and p = 6 at coupling g = 0.3

## Context
This work extends diagrammatic Monte Carlo techniques, originally developed for infinite systems at finite temperature in condensed matter physics (Prokofev 1998, Prokofev 2008, Van Houcke 2012, Van Houcke 2019), to bound finite systems at zero temperature such as atomic nuclei. The method addresses the factorial growth of Feynman diagrams that prevents inclusion of high-order configurations in self-consistent Green's function approaches and the algebraic diagrammatic construction (ADC) scheme, which is state-of-the-art for nuclear physics and quantum chemistry (Schirmer 1983, Barbieri 2016). The success with the Richardson model represents an initial step toward a unified description of nuclear structure and reactions by stochastic sampling over Feynman diagram space.
