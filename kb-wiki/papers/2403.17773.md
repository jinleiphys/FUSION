# Synthesis of superheavy elements in the outer crust of a magnetar

> A theoretical understanding of a possible mechanism for synthesizing superheavy elements in the outer crust of magnetars is presented. We demonstrate that such a mechanism can be present whenever the baryon density in the outer crust of a neutron star reaches values around $10^{-2}$ fm$^{-3}$. This scenario could be realized in magnetars with hypothetical large magnetic fields, $B \geq 10^{18}$ G. Under such conditions, the Coulomb lattice, formed by ionized nuclei, enables a mechanism that synthesizes superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that superheavy elements (SHE) can appear in the innermost layers of a magnetar's outer crust when the magnetic field exceeds approximately \(10^{18}\) G, but only if the Coulomb lattice contribution to the Gibbs free energy is included; without the lattice term, SHE do not appear. The mechanism relies on a cancellation between the nuclear Coulomb term and the lattice term at a critical baryon density around \(10^{-2}\) fm\(^{-3}\), which is only reached under extreme magnetic fields.

## Method

The authors minimize the Gibbs free energy per baryon for the outer crust system (nuclei in a body-centered cubic Coulomb lattice plus degenerate relativistic electrons in a uniform magnetic field) as a function of proton number \(Z\) and mass number \(A\) at fixed pressure and magnetic field. They use two state-of-the-art nuclear energy density functionals (the non-relativistic UNEDF1 and the relativistic DDPC1) for the nuclear binding energies, and also employ a simplified Liquid Drop Model (LDM) to derive an analytic understanding of the mechanism. The electronic contribution is treated exactly using Landau-Rabi quantized energy levels, and the lattice energy is taken from the standard \(B=0\) result since it is independent of the magnetic field.

## Key numbers

- Critical average Fermi momentum for Coulomb-lattice cancellation: \(p_F^c \approx 157\) MeV, corresponding to a critical baryon density \(n^c \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-2}\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Magnetic field threshold for SHE appearance: \(B \gtrsim 10^{18}\) G; the highest field studied is \(B = 5.0 \times 10^{18}\) G.
- For \(B \sim 10^{16}\) G (below threshold), maximum values are \(Z \approx 60\) and \(N \approx 130\); for \(B \gtrsim 10^{18}\) G, SHE with much larger \(Z\) and \(N\) appear at densities \(n \sim 10^{-2}\) fm\(^{-3}\).
- Neutron drip density shifts from \(\sim 10^{-4}\) fm\(^{-3}\) at \(B = 10^{16}\) G to \(\sim 10^{-2}\) fm\(^{-3}\) at \(B \sim 10^{18}\) G.
- LDM prediction for the proton fraction at the critical density: \(y \approx 0.33\).

## Context

This work extends the recent finding by Sekizawa (2023) that superheavy elements appear in magnetar crusts at \(B \gtrsim 10^{18}\) G, by providing a clear physical explanation and confirming the result with multiple nuclear mass models (UNEDF1, DDPC1, and also HFB-32 and UNEDF0). It builds on earlier studies of magnetar crust composition by Chamel (2012), Basilico (2015), and others, which did not consider magnetic fields above \(10^{18}\) G or did not include nuclei beyond \(Z \sim 120\). The mechanism identified here, the screening of the nuclear Coulomb term by the lattice contribution at high density, enables future work on nuclear stability and drip lines in extreme astrophysical environments, though the conditions cannot be reproduced in terrestrial laboratories.
