# Review of the theoretical heavy-ion physics

> In this contribution we briefly give an overview of the theoretical models used to describe experimental data from heavy-ion collisions from $\sqrt{s_{NN}} \approx $ 4 GeV to ultra-relativistic energies of $\sqrt{s_{NN}} \approx $ 5 TeV. We highlight the successes and problems of statistical or hadron-resonance gas models, address the results of macroscopic approaches like hydrodynamics (in different hybrid combinations) as well as the results from microscopic transport approaches in comparison to experimental data. Finally, the transport coefficients like shear $η$ and bulk viscosity $ζ$ - entering the macroscopic models - are confronted with results from lattice QCD in thermal equilibrium for vanishing chemical potential.

# Review of the theoretical heavy-ion physics

## Key claim
This review argues that while statistical, hydrodynamic, and microscopic transport models each have distinct strengths and limitations, the different theoretical methods for extracting transport coefficients (shear viscosity η and bulk viscosity ζ) have now "almost converged to the same results" for η/s, demonstrating that complementary strategies lead to a closer physical understanding of strongly interacting matter produced in heavy-ion collisions. The authors hedge that open problems remain in microscopic transport approaches including many-body reactions, dynamical modeling of first-order transitions, cluster formation, and inclusion of chiral anomalies.

## Method
The authors provide an overview comparing three classes of theoretical models for heavy-ion collisions from √s_NN ≈ 4 GeV to 5 TeV: hadron-resonance gas (HRG) or statistical models, macroscopic hydrodynamic and hybrid models (including one-fluid and three-fluid approaches with viscous hydrodynamics and hadronic afterburners), and microscopic transport models (including Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics or PHSD, based on Kadanoff-Baym equations and the Dynamical Quasi-Particle Model). They then confront transport coefficients from these models with results from lattice QCD in thermal equilibrium for vanishing chemical potential, comparing shear viscosity η/s and bulk viscosity ζ/s as functions of scaled temperature T/T_c.

## Key numbers
- The energy range covered spans from √s_NN ≈ 4 GeV (AGS energies) to √s_NN ≈ 5 TeV (LHC energies).
- The excluded volume parameter for hadrons in HRG models is r_0 ≈ 0.3 fm.
- The critical temperature T_c ≈ 158 MeV, near which bulk viscosity ζ(T) shows a maximum and shear viscosity η/s shows a minimum.
- The Kovtun-Son-Starinets lower bound for η/s is 1/(4π), referenced as the KSS bound.
- The electromagnetic coupling constant α_e ≈ 1/137, explaining why photons and dileptons are not in equilibrium with their environment.
- Hybrid models involve approximately 15 independent parameters that must be optimized via Bayesian analysis against experimental data.

## Context
This review extends and synthesizes work on heavy-ion collision modeling from AGS through LHC energies, comparing statistical models (Andronic, Stachel), hydrodynamic approaches (Heinz and Kolb 2002, Shuryak 2009, Ivanov), and microscopic transport models including UrQMD, HSD, AMPT, BAMPS, and PHSD. It builds on lattice QCD calculations (Cheng 2008, Aoki 2010) and Bayesian analyses of transport coefficients (Bass and collaborators, Pratt and collaborators). The convergence of results for η/s from different methods (PHSD, VISHNU hydrodynamics, and lattice QCD) enables more reliable extraction of transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma, while open questions about the QCD phase diagram at finite baryon chemical potential, chiral symmetry restoration, and first-order phase transitions motivate future experiments at FAIR and NICA energies.
