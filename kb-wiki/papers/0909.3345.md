# Test of finite temperature RPA on a Lipkin model

> We investigate the applicability of finite temperature random phase approximation (RPA) using a solvable Lipkin model. We show that the finite temperature RPA reproduces reasonably well the temperature dependence of total strength, both for the positive energy (i.e., the excitation) and the negative energy (i.e., the de-excitation) parts. This is the case even at very low temperatures, which may be relevant to astrophysical purposes.

## Key claim

The finite temperature RPA reproduces the temperature dependence of total strength reasonably well for both the positive energy (excitation) and negative energy (de-excitation) parts of the strength function in the Lipkin model, even at very low temperatures that may be relevant to astrophysical purposes; however, for stronger coupling strengths the RPA significantly underestimates the thermal broadening of the strength function.

## Method

The authors test finite temperature RPA against exact solutions of the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model with half-filling (N=20 particles, 2Omega=20 degeneracy). They compute strength functions for the operator F = (K_+ + K_-)/2 using both canonical and grand canonical ensemble exact solutions, and compare these to finite temperature RPA and thermal Hartree-Fock results at several temperatures and coupling strengths.

## Key numbers

- For VN/epsilon = 0.5 at T/epsilon = 0.05, the first excited state energy is 0.897 epsilon in the exact solution and 0.88 epsilon in RPA.
- For VN/epsilon = 2.0 at T/epsilon = 0.675, the total strength values are: canonical exact 7.92 (negative part 2.02), grand canonical exact 7.40 (negative part 1.84), thermal RPA 8.04 (negative part 2.01), and thermal Hartree-Fock 2.81 (negative part 0.52).
- The strength function is smeared with a width of eta/epsilon = 0.1.
- The coupling strength VN/epsilon = 0.5 is used for the main calculations, with additional tests at VN/epsilon = 2.0 and 4.0.

## Context

This work extends the earlier study by Rossignoli and Ring (1998), who investigated finite temperature RPA but only examined the positive energy part of the strength function and considered only the grand canonical ensemble. The present paper separately investigates both positive and negative energy parts and compares canonical and grand canonical ensemble results, which is important because finite temperature RPA is based on the grand canonical ensemble while actual nuclei have well-defined particle numbers. The results support the use of finite temperature RPA for estimating beta decay rates in stellar environments and for r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
