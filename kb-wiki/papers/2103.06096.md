# Fluctuations and phases in baryonic matter

> The phase structure of baryonic matter is investigated with focus on the role of fluctuations beyond the mean-field approximation. The prototype test case studied is the chiral nucleon-meson model, with added comments on the chiral quark-meson model. Applications to the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter and extensions to dense matter are performed. The role of vacuum fluctuations and thermal excitations is systematically explored. It is pointed out that such fluctuations tend to stabilise the hadronic phase characterised by spontaneously broken chiral symmetry, shifting the chiral restoration transition to very high densities. This stabilisation effect is shown to be further enhanced by additional dynamical fluctuations treated with functional renormalisation group methods.

## Key claim

The paper argues that fluctuations beyond the mean-field approximation, specifically vacuum fluctuations and dynamical fluctuations treated with functional renormalisation group methods, tend to stabilise the hadronic phase with spontaneously broken chiral symmetry, shifting the chiral restoration transition to very high densities; this stabilisation is shown to be further enhanced when additional dynamical fluctuations are included, but the exact location and existence of a first-order chiral phase transition remain sensitive to the treatment of these fluctuations.

## Method

The authors use the chiral nucleon-meson (ChNM) model as a prototype, with fermionic vacuum fluctuations incorporated via an extended renormalised mean-field (EMF) framework following Skokov (2010), and additional dynamical fluctuations of chiral boson and nucleon fields treated using the functional renormalisation group (FRG) with Wetterich's flow equation in the local potential approximation. The model parameters are fixed by reproducing empirical nuclear matter properties at saturation density, the liquid-gas phase transition characteristics, and the nuclear surface tension, with the vector fields treated as mean fields throughout. The chiral order parameter (sigma field expectation value) is then computed as a function of baryon chemical potential at zero temperature for symmetric nuclear matter and neutron-rich matter, comparing mean-field, EMF, and FRG results.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of nuclear matter: \(n_0 = 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\) with ground state energy \(E_0/A = -16\ \text{MeV}\) and symmetry energy \(S_0 = 32\ \text{MeV}\).
- Critical point of the liquid-gas phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter: \(T_{\text{crit}} = 17.5\ \text{MeV}\), \(P_{\text{crit}} = 0.33\ \text{MeV/fm}^3\), \(n_{\text{crit}} = 0.06\ \text{fm}^{-3}\), \(\mu_{\text{crit}} = 908\ \text{MeV}\).
- Nuclear surface tension from the EMF potential: \(\Sigma = 1.1\ \text{MeV/fm}^2\).
- Landau effective mass at the Fermi surface of nuclear matter: \(M^*_L = 0.79\,M_N\).
- Compression modulus at saturation: \(K = 282\ \text{MeV}\).
- Symmetry energy at twice saturation density: \(S(2n_0) = 62\ \text{MeV}\).
- In the EMF treatment, the scalar field at the nuclear matter ground state is \(\langle\sigma\rangle_0 = 68.2\ \text{MeV} = 0.74\,f_\pi\) with \(f_\pi \simeq 93\ \text{MeV}\).

## Context

This work extends previous mean-field studies of the chiral phase transition in dense baryonic matter, particularly those using Nambu and Jona-Lasinio type models (Asakawa 1989, Klimt 1990, Scavenius 2001) and chiral nucleon-meson models (Drews 2015, Drews 2017), by systematically comparing the effects of vacuum fluctuations and FRG-treated dynamical fluctuations. The results challenge earlier mean-field predictions of a first-order chiral transition at moderate densities, showing instead that fluctuations push the transition to much higher densities, which has implications for the equation of state of neutron star matter and for interpreting constraints from heavy neutron star masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019) and gravitational wave observations (Abbott 2017). The work enables future studies to incorporate these fluctuation effects into more realistic models of dense matter and to explore the possible existence of a critical end point in the QCD phase diagram.
