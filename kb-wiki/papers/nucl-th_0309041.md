# Neutron Stars for Undergraduates

> Calculating the structure of white dwarf and neutron stars would be a suitable topic for an undergraduate thesis or an advanced special topics or independent study course. The subject is rich in many different areas of physics accessible to a junior or senior physics major, ranging from thermodynamics to quantum statistics to nuclear physics to special and general relativity. The computations for solving the coupled structure differential equations (both Newtonian and general relativistic) can be done using a symbolic computational package, such as Mathematica. In doing so, the student will develop computational skills and learn how to deal with dimensions. Along the way he or she will also have learned some of the physics of equations of state and of degenerate stars.

## Key claim

This paper presents a pedagogical framework for undergraduate students to numerically compute the structure of white dwarf and neutron stars using symbolic computation packages like Mathematica, arguing that the project is accessible to junior or senior physics majors despite involving thermodynamics, quantum statistics, nuclear physics, and general relativity.

## Method

The authors guide students through deriving the Newtonian and general relativistic Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations, then constructing equations of state (EoS) starting from the non-interacting Fermi gas model for electrons (white dwarfs) and for neutrons, protons, and electrons (neutron stars). Students integrate the coupled differential equations numerically from the center outward to find the radius where pressure reaches zero, using polytropic approximations and later a more realistic nuclear matter EoS that includes the nuclear symmetry energy and compressibility.

## Key numbers

- Newton's gravitational constant: \( G = 6.673 \times 10^{-8} \) dyne-cm\(^2\)/g\(^2\)
- Solar mass: \( {\rm M}_\odot = 1.989 \times 10^{33} \) g
- Solar radius: \( R_\odot = 6.96 \times 10^5 \) km
- Constant \( R_0 = G {\rm M}_\odot / c^2 = 1.47 \) km (half the Schwarzschild radius of the Sun)
- For relativistic electron Fermi gas (\( k_F \gg m_e \)): white dwarf mass is approximately 1.243 \( {\rm M}_\odot \) independent of central pressure, with radii ranging from 4840 km (for \( \bar{p}(0) = 10^{-14} \)) to 15,080 km (for \( \bar{p}(0) = 10^{-16} \))
- For non-relativistic electron Fermi gas (\( k_F \ll m_e \)): white dwarf masses vary with central pressure, e.g., \( M = 0.3941 \, {\rm M}_\odot \) with \( R = 10,620 \) km for \( \bar{p}(0) = 10^{-15} \), and \( M = 0.1974 \, {\rm M}_\odot \) with \( R = 13,360 \) km for \( \bar{p}(0) = 10^{-16} \)
- Constant \( \epsilon_0 = 1.42 \times 10^{24} \) erg/cm\(^3\) for the electron Fermi gas model

## Context

This work extends the pedagogical approach of Balian and Blaizot (who taught a full-year course at Ecole Polytechnique on similar material) and complements the textbook by Shapiro and Teukolsky by emphasizing hands-on numerical computation rather than analytic derivations. The paper enables undergraduate students to reproduce classic results such as the Oppenheimer-Volkov mass limit for pure neutron stars and to explore how nuclear interactions modify neutron star structure, using the nuclear matter EoS model learned from Prakash.
