# 2D Cooling of Magnetized Neutron Stars

> Context: Many thermally emitting isolated neutron stars have magnetic fields larger than 10^13 G. A realistic cooling model that includes the presence of high magnetic fields should be reconsidered. Aims: We investigate the effects of anisotropic temperature distribution and Joule heating on the cooling of magnetized neutron stars. Methods: The 2D heat transfer equation with anisotropic thermal conductivity tensor and including all relevant neutrino emission processes is solved for realistic models of the neutron star interior and crust. Results: The presence of the magnetic field affects significantly the thermal surface distribution and the cooling history during both, the early neutrino cooling era and the late photon cooling era. Conclusions: There is a large effect of the Joule heating on the thermal evolution of strongly magnetized neutron stars. Both magnetic fields and Joule heating play a key role in keeping magnetars warm for a long time. Moreover, this effect is important for intermediate field neutron stars and should be considered in radio-quiet isolated neutron stars or high magnetic field radio-pulsars.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that anisotropic heat transport and Joule heating from magnetic field decay significantly affect the thermal evolution of magnetized neutron stars, particularly during both the early neutrino cooling era and the late photon cooling era, with the caveat that the magnetic field geometry is assumed fixed and its decay follows a phenomenological law rather than a fully coupled magneto-thermal evolution.

## Method

The authors solved the 2D heat transfer equation with an anisotropic thermal conductivity tensor in axial symmetry, including all relevant neutrino emission processes, for realistic models of the neutron star interior and crust. They considered several magnetic field configurations (force-free fields, crustal poloidal fields, core dipolar solutions, and configurations with different toroidal components) and implemented a phenomenological magnetic field decay law that combines an initial rapid Hall-dominated phase with a later exponential Ohmic decay phase. The outer envelope boundary condition was treated using magnetized envelope models relating the temperature at the base of the envelope to the surface temperature as a function of magnetic field strength and inclination angle.

## Key numbers

- Magnetic fields considered are larger than \(10^{13}\) G, with magnetar fields up to \(5\times10^{14}\) G
- For magnetar field strength, the Hall drift influences Ohmic dissipation on a characteristic timescale of about \(10^{4}\) yr
- The Ohmic dissipation timescale is \(\tau_{\rm Ohm}\simeq 10^{6}\) yr
- The heat source in the outer crust of a young magnetar should be located at a density \(\lesssim 5\times 10^{11}\) g cm\(^{-3}\) with a heating rate \(\gtrsim 10^{20}\) erg cm\(^{-3}\) s\(^{-1}\) for at least \(5\times10^{4}\) yr
- The hot emitting area inferred from multiwavelength fits to isolated neutron stars is \(\simeq 10\)–20 km\(^2\)
- X-ray pulsations in some objects have amplitudes \(\simeq 5\)–30%
- The surface temperature boundary condition is valid for \(B < 10^{16}\) G and \(10^{7}\) K \(\leq T_b \leq 10^{9.5}\) K, with the constraint \(T_s > 2\times10^{5}\) K

## Context

This work extends earlier 1D cooling models by Yakovlev (2004) and Page (2006) that assumed spherically symmetric temperature distributions, and builds on stationary 2D crustal temperature calculations by Geppert (2004) and Azorin (2006) that considered crustal confined magnetic fields. The paper also incorporates results from 2D magnetic field evolution simulations by Pons and Geppert (2007) that studied Ohmic dissipation and Hall drift. The findings enable more realistic comparisons with observations of magnetars, radio-quiet isolated neutron stars, and high magnetic field radio-pulsars, and provide a foundation for future fully coupled magneto-thermal evolution simulations.
