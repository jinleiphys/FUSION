# Shape transitions and ground-state properties of tungsten isotopes in covariant density functional theory

> This study investigates the structural evolution of even-even tungsten isotopes ($^{154\text{--}264}$W) using covariant density functional theory (CDFT) with four relativistic functionals: DD-ME1, DD-ME2, DD-PC1, and DD-PCX. Key nuclear properties, including binding energies, quadrupole deformation parameters, two-neutron separation energies, neutron pairing energies, nuclear radii, and potential energy curves, are analyzed to explore shape transitions and stability from neutron-deficient to neutron-rich isotopes up to the drip line. The results reveal a dynamic shape evolution, with spherical configurations at $N = 82$ and $N = 126$, prolate dominance in intermediate regions, and shape coexistence in isotopes such as $^{158}$W, $^{160}$W, $^{194}$W, $^{196}$W, $^{206}$W, and near $^{244\text{--}248}$W. A potential subshell closure at $N = 118$ is identified, supported by anomalies in separation energies and vanishing pairing energies. The neutron drip line is predicted at $N = 184$, marked by a return to spherical symmetry. Comparisons with experimental data and other theoretical models, including the deformed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method with the Skyrme SLy4 interaction, the Finite Range Droplet Model, and the Relativistic Mean Field model with NL3, show strong agreement, validating the robustness of CDFT. These findings enhance our understanding of nuclear structure in the medium-to-heavy mass region and provide insights relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis, thereby guiding future experimental studies at radioactive ion beam facilities.

# Shape Transitions and Ground-State Properties of Tungsten Isotopes in Covariant Density Functional Theory

## Key claim
The study claims that covariant density functional theory (CDFT) predicts a dynamic shape evolution across even-even tungsten isotopes from $^{154}$W to $^{264}$W, with a potential neutron subshell closure at $N = 118$ supported by anomalies in separation energies and vanishing pairing energies, though the authors acknowledge that the structure at $N = 112$ is weaker and more model-dependent, and that subshell features can originate from competing prolate-oblate configurations rather than definitive new magic numbers.

## Method
The authors performed constrained relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov calculations using four density-dependent covariant density functionals (DD-ME1, DD-ME2, DD-PC1, and DD-PCX) with a separable finite-range pairing force (strength $G = 728$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$, range $a = 0.644$ fm) and a harmonic oscillator basis expansion with $N_{\text{sh}} = 20$ major shells. They computed binding energies, quadrupole deformation parameters, two-neutron separation energies, shell gaps, neutron pairing energies, nuclear radii, and potential energy curves for even-even tungsten isotopes from neutron number $N = 80$ to the predicted neutron drip line, comparing results with the deformed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method with Skyrme SLy4 interaction, the Finite Range Droplet Model, the deformed relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theory in continuum with PC-PK1, and the Relativistic Mean Field model with NL3.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy per nucleon peaks around $A = 174$ ($N = 100$), identifying $^{174}$W as the most bound isotope in the chain
- Neutron drip line predicted at $N = 184$ ($^{258}$W), where the two-neutron separation energy becomes very small and negative for the next isotope
- Shape coexistence identified in $^{158}$W, $^{160}$W, $^{194}$W, $^{196}$W, $^{206}$W, and near $^{244\text{--}248}$W, with prolate and oblate minima differing by less than 1 MeV in these cases
- For isotopes $^{208}$W through $^{242}$W, prolate minima are typically $2.0$--$4.0$ MeV deeper than oblate minima
- Proton radius increases by approximately $0.1$ fm across the isotopic chain
- Pairing strength set to $G = 728$ MeV$\cdot$fm$^3$ with range parameter $a = 0.644$ fm

## Context
This work extends systematic CDFT studies of shape evolution in the $Z = 72\text{--}80$ region, where previous investigations by Nomura (2010s), Ramos (2014), and others have identified shape coexistence and quantum phase transitions. The study provides predictions for neutron-rich tungsten isotopes relevant to r-process nucleosynthesis and guides future experiments at radioactive ion beam facilities. The identification of a potential subshell closure at $N = 118$ adds to ongoing discussions about shell evolution in neutron-rich nuclei, complementing work by Siddiqui (2020) and Sharma (2025) on magic numbers in heavy isotopes.
