# Quantum dynamics and thermalization for out-of-equilibrium phi^4-theory

> The quantum time evolution of ϕ^4-field theory for a spatially homogeneous system in 2+1 space-time dimensions is investigated numerically for out-of-equilibrium initial conditions on the basis of the Kadanoff-Baym equations including the tadpole and sunset self-energies. Whereas the tadpole self-energy yields a dynamical mass, the sunset self-energy is responsible for dissipation and an equilibration of the system. In particular we address the dynamics of the spectral (`off-shell') distributions of the excited quantum modes and the different phases in the approach to equilibrium described by Kubo-Martin-Schwinger relations for thermal equilibrium states. The investigation explicitly demonstrates that the only translation invariant solutions representing the stationary fixed points of the coupled equation of motions are those of full thermal equilibrium. They agree with those extracted from the time integration of the Kadanoff-Baym equations in the long time limit. Furthermore, a detailed comparison of the full quantum dynamics to more approximate and simple schemes like that of a standard kinetic (on-shell) Boltzmann equation is performed. Our analysis shows that the consistent inclusion of the dynamical spectral function has a significant impact on relaxation phenomena. The different time scales, that are involved in the dynamical quantum evolution towards a complete thermalized state, are discussed in detail. We find that far off-shell 1 <-> 3 processes are responsible for chemical equilibration, which is missed in the Boltzmann limit. Finally, we address briefly the case of (bare) massless fields. For sufficiently large couplings $λ$ we observe the onset of Bose condensation, where our scheme within symmetric ϕ^4-theory breaks down.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Kadanoff-Baym equations for phi^4-theory in 2+1 dimensions, including both tadpole and sunset self-energies, drive the system to full thermal equilibrium in the long-time limit, but only when the dynamical spectral function (off-shell information) is consistently included; the standard on-shell Boltzmann equation misses chemical equilibration because far off-shell 1-to-3 processes are responsible for it.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the Kadanoff-Baym equations for a spatially homogeneous scalar phi^4-field theory in 2+1 space-time dimensions, using the tadpole self-energy (local, generating a dynamical mass) and the sunset self-energy (non-local, providing dissipation and collisions). They initialized the system with four different polar-symmetric momentum distributions (D1, D2, D3, and a thermal DT distribution) all having the same energy density, and integrated the two-time Green functions forward in time. The numerical algorithm employed a finite volume with momentum mode discretization, and ultraviolet divergences were handled by renormalization with counterterms.

## Key numbers

* The renormalized mass m = 1 sets the unit of time (t * m dimensionless) and coupling (lambda/m dimensionless).
* The initial thermal distribution DT corresponds to a free Bose gas at initial temperature T0 approximately 1.736 m, fixed by the initial energy density.
* The system is studied in 2+1 space-time dimensions (d=2 spatial dimensions) on a finite volume V = a^2.
* The coupling constant lambda appears in the tadpole self-energy as lambda/2 and in the sunset self-energy as -lambda^2/6.

## Context

This work extends earlier numerical studies of Kadanoff-Baym equations in 1+1 dimensions by Berges et al. (2002) and Berges and Aarts (2002), which showed equilibration but where on-shell 2-to-2 collisions are strictly forward and cannot contribute to momentum-space thermalization. It also builds on the seminal nonrelativistic Kadanoff-Baym calculations by Danielewicz (1984) and later work by Kohler (1995) on correlation buildup. The results enable a quantitative understanding of thermalization time scales in quantum field theory and provide a benchmark for approximate transport schemes such as the Boltzmann equation, with direct relevance to modeling heavy-ion collisions where off-shell propagation of broad resonances (e.g., rho meson) is important.
