# Quark mass dependence of baryon properties

> I discuss the quark mass dependence of various baryon properties derived from chiral perturbation theory. Such representations can eventually be used as chiral extrapolation functions when lattice data at sufficiently small quark masses become available. The quark mass dependence is encoded in loop and contact term contributions, the latter given in terms of low-energy constants. I stress the importance of utilizing phenomenological input to constrain a certain class of low-energy constants and discuss the ensuing theoretical uncertainty for various baryon observables, like the nucleon and the baryon octet masses, the nucleon isovector anomalous magnetic moment and the axial-vector coupling of the nucleon. I stress the role of resonance decoupling and present first results for the delta mass based on an effective field theory in which the nucleon-delta mass splitting is counted as a small parameter. I also discuss briefly the pion mass dependence of the nuclear force as derived from chiral nuclear effective field theory.

## Key claim

Chiral perturbation theory provides quark-mass expansions of baryon observables that can serve as chiral extrapolation functions for lattice QCD, but only if the pion mass is sufficiently small (below about 400-550 MeV) and if phenomenological input is used to constrain the dynamical low-energy constants, thereby controlling the theoretical uncertainty.

## Method

The author performs complete one-loop (fourth-order) SU(2) and SU(3) chiral perturbation theory calculations for baryon observables, expanding in small parameters (external momenta and meson masses relative to the chiral symmetry breaking scale of about 1 GeV). Low-energy constants (LECs) are separated into dynamical LECs (non-vanishing in the chiral limit, constrained from pion-nucleon scattering and peripheral nucleon-nucleon scattering) and symmetry-breaking LECs (vanishing in the chiral limit, best obtained from lattice simulations). For the delta baryon, the nucleon-delta mass splitting is treated as an additional small parameter in the small scale expansion.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon mass in the SU(2) chiral limit: m0 ≈ 0.88 GeV from a best fit to MILC and CP-PACS lattice data
- Dimension-two LECs from pion-nucleon scattering (all in GeV⁻¹): c1 = -0.9⁺⁰·⁵₋₀·₂, c2 = 3.3 ± 0.2, c3 = -4.7⁺¹·²₋₁·₀, c4 = 3.5⁺⁰·⁵₋₀·₂
- Contributions to the physical nucleon mass at the physical pion mass: quadratic term 70 MeV, cubic term -17 MeV, quartic term -4 MeV
- SU(3) chiral limit value of the nucleon mass: 710 MeV ≲ m̃0 ≲ 1070 MeV
- Pion-nucleon sigma term: 39.5 MeV ≲ σπN(0) ≲ 46.7 MeV
- Strangeness fraction in the proton: 0.07 ≲ y ≲ 0.22
- Strong neutron-proton mass difference LEC: c5 = -0.09 ± 0.01 GeV⁻¹
- For the isovector anomalous magnetic moment κv, the chiral limit value is c6 = 5 (with e106 = 0.45) to reproduce the physical value κv = 3.71 nuclear magnetons at the physical pion mass
- For the axial-vector coupling gA, the cubic-in-pion-mass correction ΔgA⁽³⁾ ≈ 0.32 at the physical pion mass, using F0 = 86.5 MeV, m0 = 880 MeV, and g0 = 1.2

## Context

This work extends the chiral perturbation theory framework for baryons developed by Weinberg (1979), Gasser and Leutwyler (1983, 1984), and the heavy baryon approach of Bernard, Kaiser, and Meißner (1995). The analysis of the nucleon mass in SU(2) builds on the fourth-order calculation by Steininger, Meißner, and Weise (1998) and the LEC determinations from pion-nucleon scattering by Buettiker, Fettes, Meißner, and others (1999). The SU(3) octet mass analysis extends the work of Frink and Meißner (2004, 2005) and Borasoy and Meißner (1996). The delta mass calculation using the small scale expansion follows Hemmert, Holstein, and Kambor (1997) and the infrared regularization extension by Bernard, Meißner, and others (2003, 2005). The results enable model-independent chiral extrapolations of lattice QCD data at sufficiently small quark masses, but the author emphasizes that global fits to multiple observables with shared LECs are necessary, and that regularization-scheme independence must hold for the approach to be reliable.
