# Temperature and energy partition in fragmentation

> We study fragmentation of small atomistic clusters via molecular dynamics. We calculate the time scales related to fragment formation and emission. We also show that some degree of thermalization is achieved during the expansion process, which allows the determination of a local temperature. In this way we can calculate the break-up temperature as a function of excitation energy, i.e. the fragmentation caloric curve. Fragmentation appears as a rather constant temperature region of the caloric curve. Furthermore, we show that different definitions of temperature, related to different degrees of freedom, yield very similar values.

# Temperature and energy partition in fragmentation

## Key claim
The authors demonstrate that during fragmentation of small Lennard-Jones clusters, a local temperature can be defined because the expanding system achieves some degree of local thermal equilibrium; the break-up temperature is nearly independent of the total excitation energy, appearing as a constant-temperature plateau in the caloric curve, with no evidence of a "gas branch" (rising temperature at high energies) that some nuclear experiments have reported.

## Method
The authors performed molecular dynamics simulations of 147-particle three-dimensional Lennard-Jones drops, with initial configurations cut from a thermalized periodic system at controlled density (0.85 σ⁻³ for most energies) and temperature (1.4 to 4.3 ε). They used the Early Cluster Recognition Algorithm (ECRA) to identify fragment formation times in phase space and the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) method to identify fragment emission times in coordinate space, then computed local temperatures from velocity fluctuations around the radial collective expansion in concentric spherical regions.

## Key numbers
- System size: N = 147 particles in three-dimensional Lennard-Jones drops
- Energy range studied: from E = -2.4 ε to E = 2.2 ε
- Fragment formation times: τ_ff(1.8ε) ~ 20 t₀, τ_ff(0.9ε) ~ 35 t₀, τ_ff(0.5ε) ~ 52 t₀, τ_ff(-0.5ε) ~ 75 t₀
- Fragment emission times: τ_fe(1.8ε) ~ 40 t₀, τ_fe(0.9ε) ~ 60 t₀, τ_fe(0.5ε) ~ 67 t₀, τ_fe(-0.5ε) ~ 100 t₀
- Initial drop density: ρ = 0.85 σ⁻³ for energies from E = -1.5 ε to E = 2.2 ε; for E = -2 ε, densities of 1 σ⁻³ and 1.09 σ⁻³ were used
- Integration time step: t_int = 0.001 t₀, with energy conserved to approximately one part per million
- Clusterization radius: R_cl = 3 σ
- Cut-off radius: r_c = 3 σ
- Number of events: at least 120 explosions per energy; 300 evolutions for E = 1.8 ε, 0.9 ε, 0.5 ε, and -0.5 ε
- Power-law mass spectra observed near E = 0.5 ε

## Context
This work extends previous molecular dynamics studies of two-dimensional Lennard-Jones fragmentation (which also found a flat caloric curve region) to three dimensions, and addresses the ongoing experimental controversy in nuclear fragmentation: Pochodzalla et al. (1995) found a plateau at ~5 MeV followed by a rising "gas branch" at high excitation energies using isotope temperatures from ALADIN data, while the EOS collaboration (Hauger et al.) confirmed the plateau but did not find the gas branch, and Serfling et al. found emission temperature constant throughout the whole energy range. The authors' classical Lennard-Jones model shows no gas branch, supporting the interpretation that different temperature definitions (isotope temperature vs. emission temperature vs. local temperature) may explain the experimental discrepancies, as suggested in the review by Pochodzalla (1997). The results enable a clearer characterization of the break-up state in fragmentation and provide a framework for understanding why fragment internal temperatures are independent of initial excitation energy.
