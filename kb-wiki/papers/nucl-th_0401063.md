# Radii in weakly-bound light halo nuclei

> A systematic study of the root-mean-square distance between the constituents of weakly-bound nuclei consisting of two halo neutrons and a core is performed using a renormalized zero-range model. The radii are obtained from a universal scaling function that depends on the mass ratio of the neutron and the core, as well as on the nature of the subsystems, bound or virtual. Our calculations are qualitatively consistent with recent data for the neutron-neutron root-mean-square distance in the halo of $^{11}$Li and $^{14}$Be nuclei.

# Radii in weakly-bound light halo nuclei

## Key claim
The root-mean-square distances between constituents of two-neutron halo nuclei can be obtained from a universal scaling function that depends only on the mass ratio of neutron to core and on whether each two-body subsystem is bound or virtual, but the model is restricted to s-wave interactions and therefore may show deviations for nuclei where p-wave interactions are important for binding (such as $^6$He and $^{11}$Li).

## Method
The authors solved subtracted Faddeev equations with a zero-range s-wave pairwise potential for a three-body system of two identical neutrons and a core, using a renormalization scheme that introduces a three-body subtraction energy. The scaling functions for the root-mean-square radii were computed as functions of the mass ratio $A = m_\beta/m_\alpha$ and the ratios of two-body subsystem energies to the three-body binding energy, covering four configuration types: Borromean, Tango, Samba, and All-bound.

## Key numbers
- For $^6$He ($A=^4$He): with $E_3 = 0.973$ MeV and $E_{nA}=0$ (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 5.1$ fm; with $E_{nA}=0.3$ MeV (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 4.6$ fm; experimental value is $5.9 \pm 1.2$ fm.
- For $^{11}$Li ($A=^9$Li): with $E_3 = 0.32$ MeV and $E_{nA}=0$ (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 9.2$ fm; with $E_{nA}=0.8$ MeV (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 5.9$ fm; experimental value is $6.6 \pm 1.5$ fm.
- For $^{14}$Be ($A=^{12}$Be): with $E_3 = 1.337$ MeV and $E_{nA}=0$ (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 4.6$ fm; with $E_{nA}=0.2$ MeV (virtual), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 4.2$ fm; experimental value is $5.4 \pm 1.0$ fm.
- For $^{20}$C ($A=^{18}$C): with $E_3 = 3.50$ MeV and $E_{nA}=0.16$ MeV (bound), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 3.0$ fm; with $E_{nA}=0.53$ MeV (bound), $\sqrt{\langle r_{nn}^2\rangle} = 4.4$ fm; no experimental value available.
- The nn virtual state energy was taken as $-143$ keV for all calculations.

## Context
This work extends the universal scaling approach previously applied to molecules (Yamashita et al., 2003) to light halo nuclei with a neutron-neutron-core structure, building on the classification scheme of Jensen et al. (2003) for three-body systems. The results are compared to experimental nn root-mean-square distances from Marques et al. (2000) for $^6$He, $^{11}$Li, and $^{14}$Be. The approach enables prediction of unknown virtual-state energies of neutron-core subsystems (such as $^{10}$Li) from measured halo radii, and provides a zero-order approximation for realistic calculations with short-range interactions, though limitations arise from the s-wave restriction for nuclei like $^6$He where p-wave interactions dominate (Zheng et al., 1993) and $^{11}$Li where both s- and p-waves may be relevant (Caggiano et al., 1999).
