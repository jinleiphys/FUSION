# Structure in the Event-by-Event Energy-Dependent Neutron-Gamma Multiplicity Correlations in $^{252}\text{Cf}$(sf)

> The emission of neutrons and gamma rays by fission fragments reveal important information about the properties of fragments immediately following scission. The initial fragment properties, correlations between fragments, and emission competition give rise to correlations in neutron-gamma emission. Neutron-gamma correlations are important in nonproliferation applications because the characterization of fissionable samples relies on the identification of signatures in the measured radiation. Furthermore, recent theoretical and experimental advances have proposed to explain the mechanism of angular momentum generation in fission. In this paper, we present a novel analysis method of neutrons and gamma rays emitted by fission fragments that allows us to discern structure in the observed correlations. We have analyzed data collected on \ce{^{252}Cf}(sf) at the Chi-Nu array at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center. Through our analysis of the energy-differential neutron-gamma multiplicity covariance, we have observed enhanced neutron-gamma correlations, corresponding to rotational band gamma-ray transitions, at gamma-ray energies of $0.7$ and $1.2$ MeV. To shed light on the origin of this structure, we compare the experimental data with the predictions of three model calculations. The origin of the observed correlation structure is understood in terms of a positive spin-energy correlation in the generation of angular momentum in fission.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the observed enhancements in neutron-gamma correlations at gamma-ray energies of 0.7 and 1.2 MeV can be explained by positive spin-energy correlations in the generation of angular momentum in fission fragments, but the authors qualify that they have not attempted to quantify the strength of these correlations from the experimental data and that much more work is needed.

## Method

The authors analyzed 1.7 x 10^10 spontaneous fission events of Cf-252 collected at the Chi-Nu array at the Los Alamos Neutron Science Center, using 42 EJ-309 organic scintillation detectors. They developed and applied a normalized differential multiplicity covariance analysis to extract energy-dependent neutron-gamma correlations from the detected events, unfolding the detector response using MCNPX-PoliMi simulations with Tikhonov regularization. The experimental results were compared to predictions from three model calculations: CGMF, FIFRELIN (with both constant and energy-dependent angular momentum generation), and FREYA.

## Key numbers

- 1.7 x 10^10 Cf-252 spontaneous fission events were analyzed, each tagged by an ionization chamber
- Enhanced neutron-gamma correlations were observed at gamma-ray energies of 0.7 and 1.2 MeV
- The effective acceptance regions in the unfolded distributions were 1.2 to 8 MeV for neutrons and 0.22 to 3.2 MeV for gamma rays, covering 66% and 78% of the respective spectra
- Energy bins of width 0.2 MeV for neutron energy and 0.08 MeV for gamma-ray energy were used in the analysis
- The enhancement at 0.7 MeV is attributed to electric quadrupole Yrast transitions with initial spins of 4 hbar, 6 hbar, and 8 hbar
- The enhancement at 1.2 MeV corresponds to transitions from quasi-spherical nuclei near Sn-132

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Marin 2020) on event-by-event neutron-gamma multiplicity correlations in Cf-252(sf), which found significant negative correlations but was affected by energy-dependent systematic biases. The new energy-differential analysis reveals structure that the authors interpret as evidence for spin-energy correlations in fission fragment formation, a mechanism that has been supported by some (Nifenecker 1972, Chebboubi 2017) and disputed by others (Wilhelmy 1972, Schmid-Fabian 1988). The results enable future experiments combining neutron-gamma correlations with fragment mass and kinetic energy measurements to further quantify these correlations, and suggest that the observed correlation structure could serve as a signature for identifying fissionable material in nonproliferation applications.
