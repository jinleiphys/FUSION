# Energy Rates Due to Weak Decay Rates of Vanadium Isotopes in Stellar Environment

> The neutrino cooling and gamma heating rates are considered as an important input needed to study the final phases of the evolution of high-mass stars. The weak-interaction mediated processes, namely the $β$-decay and electron capture, significantly change the lepton to baryon ratio and accelerate the contraction of the core. The emission of resulting neutrinos/antineutrinos tends to cool the stellar core. On the other hand, gamma rays are produced because of electron capture and $β$-decay to excited states in daughter nuclei. These gamma rays heat the core and contribute to an increase of entropy which may cause convection to occur. In the present work, the weak-interaction heating and cooling rates on a chain of twenty-two isotopes of vanadium having mass in the range $43-64$ have been estimated using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation theory. The rates have been computed for the temperature ranging from ($10^{7} - 3 \times 10^{10}$)\;K and for the density range ($10-10^{11}$)\;g/cm$^{3}$. Our calculated neutrino energy loss rates have also been compared with the previously reported rates calculated using other theoretical models. At high stellar temperatures, our rates are larger by 1-2 orders of magnitude as compared to previous results.

## Key claim

The authors claim that their pn-QRPA calculated neutrino and anti-neutrino cooling rates for vanadium isotopes are larger by 1-2 orders of magnitude at high stellar temperatures (T9 = 30) compared to previous IPM and LSSM calculations, but this enhancement is conditional on the specific temperature and density regime and does not hold uniformly across all isotopes or lower temperatures.

## Method

The authors employed the deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory with a Nilsson model single-particle basis, BCS pairing, and both particle-particle and particle-hole Gamow-Teller residual interactions to compute GT strength distributions, beta-decay half-lives, neutrino cooling rates, and gamma heating rates for 22 vanadium isotopes (A = 43-64). The rates were calculated over a temperature range of 10^7 K to 3 x 10^10 K and density range of 10 to 10^11 g/cm^3, using a quenching factor of 0.6 for GT strengths and adjusting interaction constants to reproduce experimental half-lives and satisfy the Ikeda sum rule.

## Key numbers

- Calculated beta-decay half-lives for 22 vanadium isotopes (43-64V) were compared with experimental data from Audi (2017); for example, 43V calculated half-life is 8.14 x 10^-2 s versus experimental 7.93 x 10^-2 s, and 64V calculated is 1.54 x 10^-2 s versus experimental 1.50 x 10^-2 s.
- At high stellar temperature (T9 = 30, corresponding to 30 x 10^9 K), the pn-QRPA neutrino cooling rates exceed previous IPM and LSSM rates by 1-2 orders of magnitude for many vanadium isotopes.
- For 45V at T9 = 30, pn-QRPA rates are a factor of 6 larger than LSSM rates; for 47V at the same temperature, the factor is 4.
- For 50V and 51V at T9 = 30, pn-QRPA cooling rates surpass IPM and LSSM rates by factors of 6-45.
- Anti-neutrino cooling rates for 45-47V, 50V, and 54V at lower temperatures (T9 less than or equal to 5) show IPM and LSSM rates larger than pn-QRPA rates by up to 1-9 orders of magnitude.
- The renormalized Ikeda sum rule was satisfied for all isotopes; for example, 43V calculated Re-ISR is -3.29 versus theoretical -3.24, and 64V calculated is 19.50 versus theoretical 19.44.

## Context

This work extends the pn-QRPA calculations of weak-interaction rates previously performed by Nabi and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (1999, 2004) for 709 nuclei, now specifically applied to vanadium isotopes identified by Aufderheide (1994) as critical for electron-to-baryon fraction in late stellar evolution. The results contradict the earlier IPM calculations of Fuller (1982) and LSSM calculations that employed the Brink hypothesis for excited-state GT transitions, which the authors argue is an unsafe approximation. The computed neutrino cooling and gamma heating rates are intended for use in core-collapse supernova simulations to model presupernova evolution of massive stars.
