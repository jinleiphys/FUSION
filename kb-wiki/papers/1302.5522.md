# Strangeness in proton and properties of nucleons in nuclear matter revisited

> The properties of the nucleons in nuclear medium have been investigated in the context of the flux tube model incorporating strangeness $(s\bar{s})$ contribution to proton structure in conformity with the experimental indication. Proton is described as a pentaquark system with strange quark contribution whereas neutron is described in three quark configuration. The Quasi particle model of diquark is used to describe the structures of the nucleons. Modifications of the properties like swelling, mass, incompressibility, ratio of the structure functions ($\frac{F_{2}^{n}(x)}{F_{2}^{p}(x)}$), Gottfried Sum rule for nucleons in nuclear medium have been studied and significant effects have been observed. It has been suggested that the change of the size degree of freedom of the nucleon in the nuclear medium plays an important role in describing the properties in medium. The results are discussed in detail and compared with existing experimental and theoretical predictions. Some interesting observations are made.

# Strangeness in proton and properties of nucleons in nuclear matter revisited

## Key claim

The authors propose that incorporating a strange quark-antiquark (s\bar{s}) contribution into the proton structure, treating the proton as a pentaquark [uuds\bar{s}] system in a diquark-diquark-antiquark configuration, yields modifications to nucleon properties in nuclear matter that differ between protons and neutrons. The key hedge is that the results depend on treating the intrinsic strange sea as valence-like and using a specific quasi-particle model for diquarks, with the radius parameter of the nucleon being the only free parameter, which introduces uncertainty particularly in the Roper resonance excitation energy.

## Method

The authors model the proton as a five-quark pentaquark system [uuds\bar{s}] with scalar diquark pairs [ud]₀ and [us]₀ plus an anti-strange quark, while the neutron is described in a standard diquark-quark configuration [(ud)₀d]. They use a quasi-particle effective mass approximation for diquarks, analogous to electrons in a crystal lattice, to compute diquark masses. The flux tube model of Mathieu and Watson (1986) is employed with a statistical model wave function to calculate the Hamiltonian for nucleons in nuclear matter, minimizing it to obtain radius parameters as functions of nuclear matter density.

## Key numbers

- Diquark masses computed: m_ud = 0.590 GeV, m_us = 0.674 GeV (using constituent quark masses m_u = m_d = 0.360 GeV, m_s = 0.540 GeV)
- Critical density for proton: 4.17 times normal nuclear density (ρ₀); for neutron: 4.69 ρ₀
- Swelling at critical density: 18 percent for proton radius, 13 percent for neutron radius
- Swelling at normal nuclear density: 2.15 percent for proton, 1.96 percent for neutron
- Decrease in mass from free to normal nuclear density: 0.8 percent for proton, 0.6 percent for neutron
- Decrease in incompressibility ΔK from free to normal nuclear medium: 0.156 GeV for proton, 0.132 GeV for neutron
- Roper resonance excitation energy for free proton: 0.757 GeV; at normal nuclear density: 0.699 GeV
- Roper resonance excitation energy for free neutron: 0.728 GeV; at normal nuclear density: 0.678 GeV
- Gottfried Sum Rule S_G = 0.173 at 0 < x < 1 for free nucleons (compared to experimental value 0.197 ± 0.011 stat. ± 0.083 sys. from EMC and 0.221 ± 0.008 stat. ± 0.019 sys. from NMC)

## Context

This work extends the flux tube model of Mathieu and Watson (1986) for nucleons in nuclear matter by incorporating strange quark contributions to the proton structure, following experimental indications of strangeness in the proton from COMPASS, HERMES, SAMPLE, and G0 collaborations. It builds on the pentaquark description of the proton by Zou and Riska (2005) and Riska and Zou (2006), and uses the quasi-particle diquark model previously developed by the same authors (Bhattacharya et al., 2011). The study enables comparison of medium modifications between protons and neutrons with different internal configurations, and suggests that the Gottfried sum rule violation increases with nuclear matter density, which could be probed in future experiments on nuclear targets.
