# Quantum Computer Systems for Scientific Discovery

> The great promise of quantum computers comes with the dual challenges of building them and finding their useful applications. We argue that these two challenges should be considered together, by co-designing full-stack quantum computer systems along with their applications in order to hasten their development and potential for scientific discovery. In this context, we identify scientific and community needs, opportunities, a sampling of a few use case studies, and significant challenges for the development of quantum computers for science over the next 2--10 years. This document is written by a community of university, national laboratory, and industrial researchers in the field of Quantum Information Science and Technology, and is based on a summary from a U.S. National Science Foundation workshop on Quantum Computing held on October 21--22, 2019 in Alexandria, VA.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum computers for scientific discovery should be developed through a co-design approach that integrates full-stack system engineering with applications, rather than treating hardware building and algorithm development as separate challenges. The authors hedge that this approach is specifically advocated for the next 2–10 years using existing quantum technologies, and they explicitly do not focus on new qubit technologies that may emerge.

## Method

The paper is a community position document based on a U.S. National Science Foundation workshop held on October 21–22, 2019 in Alexandria, VA, authored by university, national laboratory, and industrial researchers in Quantum Information Science and Technology. It surveys the quantum computer stack from algorithms and software through control engineering to qubit hardware, identifies scientific opportunities at each level and at the interfaces between levels, and presents a sampling of use case studies including quantum simulation for molecular properties, chemistry, materials science, nuclear and particle physics, and combinatorial optimization.

## Key numbers

- Running Shor’s algorithm to factor a 2048-bit number using gates with a 10^{-3} error rate could be achieved with 20 million physical qubits using currently known methods (Gidney, 2019).
- Maintaining fidelities above 99% in fixed-frequency superconducting systems requires periodic calibration of RF pulse amplitudes.
- Electronic control systems need analog outputs with faster than 1 Gsamples/s, synchronized coherent output with over 100 channels extensible to above 1000 channels, and PID feedback control on each channel with at least kHz bandwidth.
- Optical control systems need phase and/or amplitude modulation with a bandwidth of approximately 100 MHz, over 100 channels extensible to above 1000 channels, and precision better than 12 bits.

## Context

This paper extends the community discussion from earlier quantum computing roadmaps (Ladd, 2010) and the DiVincenzo criteria (DiVincenzo, 2000) by advocating for a vertically-integrated co-design approach rather than separate development of qubit technologies and algorithms. It builds on prior work in quantum simulation (Feynman, 1982; Lloyd, 1996; Carr, 2019), variational quantum algorithms (Peruzzo, 2014; McClean, 2016; Farhi, 2014), and quantum error correction (Calderbank, 1996; Steane, 1999; Preskill, 1997). The document enables future efforts to design quantum computer systems specifically optimized for scientific applications, with parallel efforts in quantum simulation and quantum communication (Loncar, 2019).
