# Neutron skins as laboratory constraints on properties of neutron stars and on what we can learn from heavy ion fragmentation reactions

> The equation of state (EOS) of infinite nuclear matter with a small proton/neutron fraction is a crucial input to determine the properties of neutron stars and compare model predictions to astronomical observations. The so-called `symmetry energy' is the part of the EOS accounting for the difference in the number of neutrons and protons. Numerous experiments have been devised to assess the symmetry energy and constrain its functional dependence with the nucleon density. Further constraints follow from a stellar modeling using the EOS to reproduce astronomical observations such as neutron star masses and radii. The recent detection of gravitational waves emitted from neutron star mergers and the nucleosynthesis ensuing from these events caused a surge of interest for such studies. Several types of nuclear reactions have been proposed to study the symmetry energy part of the EOS. Some of them consist in determining the neutron skin in nuclei and exploit its correlation with the slope parameter of the symmetry energy. In this article we explore a particular set of reactions using high energy ($E_{lab} \sim 1$ GeV/nucleon) neutron-rich projectiles. We explore measurements of all reaction fragments (a) in the same isotopic chain, i.e., only by removal of neutrons, (b) in all charge-changing channels, and (c) total interaction cross sections. Using Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HBF) predictions for neutron and proton densities with Skyrme interactions, we explore the sensitivity of these cross sections with the neutron skin in nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that measurements of neutron-changing cross sections (σΔN) and total interaction cross sections (σI) in high-energy (∼1 GeV/nucleon) heavy-ion fragmentation reactions with neutron-rich projectiles can constrain the neutron skin thickness in nuclei, and thereby the slope parameter L of the symmetry energy, but the sensitivity is modest (∼4–7% variation across Skyrme models) and is complicated by poorly controlled contributions from giant resonance excitation (∼50–100 mb uncertainty).

## Method

The authors used the Glauber model (optical limit avoided, full binomial expression retained) to calculate neutron-changing, charge-changing, and total interaction cross sections for Ni, Sn, and Pb isotopes incident on carbon targets at 1 GeV/nucleon. They employed 23 different Skyrme interactions within the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) framework to generate proton and neutron density distributions, and separately estimated Coulomb and nuclear excitation of giant resonances (IVGDR and ISGQR) using first-order perturbation theory with a deformed potential model.

## Key numbers

* Neutron skin Δrnp for stable tin isotopes (A=116, 118, 120) varies in the range 0.1–0.3 fm depending on the Skyrme model.
* For ⁶⁴Ni, neutron-changing cross sections vary within 337–350 mb across Skyrme interactions (∼4% sensitivity).
* For ²⁰⁸Pb, neutron-changing cross sections vary within 537–576 mb across Skyrme interactions (∼7% dispersion).
* For ²⁰⁸Pb projectiles on carbon at 1 GeV/nucleon, the nuclear excitation cross section for the ISGQR is 43.27 mb and for the IVGDR is 1.11 mb.
* The slope parameter L from the Skyrme interactions ranges from 9.91 MeV (SIII) to 129 MeV (SKI5), with J ranging from 28.2 MeV (SIII) to 36.6 MeV (SKI5).
* Coulomb excitation cross sections for IVGDR in lead projectiles on carbon are not negligible but are smaller than the multi-nucleon stripping cross sections; for proton targets they are smaller by a factor of 30–40.

## Context

This work extends the approach proposed by Aumann (2017) for extracting neutron skin from fragmentation reactions, using a more complete Glauber treatment without the optical-limit approximation. It connects to the broader effort to constrain the symmetry energy slope parameter L, which is poorly determined (0–150 MeV theoretically, per references Roc11 and Roc15), and which is critical for neutron star structure and core-collapse supernova modeling (Gle97, Web05, Hae07, LP04, LP12, BP12, Heb13). The results suggest that neutron-changing cross sections can discriminate among Skyrme models, but the ∼50–100 mb uncertainty from giant resonance excitation (especially ISGQR) limits precision, and the authors note that angular distribution measurements may help separate these contributions experimentally.
