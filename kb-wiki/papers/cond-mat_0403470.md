# Landau levels and the Thomas-Fermi structure of rapidly rotating Bose-Einstein condensates

> We show that, within mean-field theory, the density profile of a rapidly rotating harmonically trapped Bose-Einstein condensate is of the Thomas-Fermi form as long as the number of vortices is much larger than unity. Two forms of the condensate wave function are explored: i) the lowest Landau level (LLL) wave function with a regular lattice of vortices multiplied by a slowly varying envelope function, which gives rise to components in higher Landau levels; ii) the LLL wave function with a nonuniform vortex lattice. From variational calculations we find it most favorable energetically to retain the LLL form of the wave function but to allow the vortices to deviate slightly from a regular lattice. The predicted distortions of the lattice are small, but in accord with recent measurements at lower rates of rotation.

## Key claim

Within mean-field theory, the density profile of a rapidly rotating harmonically trapped Bose-Einstein condensate takes the Thomas-Fermi (inverted parabola) form rather than a Gaussian, provided the number of vortices is much larger than unity and the interaction parameter satisfies \(Na_{\rm s}/Z \gg 1-\Omega/\omega_\perp\); this condition is much weaker than the previously assumed \(Na_{\rm s}/Z \gg 1\).

## Method

The authors introduce a trial condensate wave function that multiplies a lowest Landau level (LLL) wave function (with either a uniform or a nonuniform vortex lattice) by a slowly varying real envelope function \(h(r)\), which admixes higher Landau levels. They compute the energy per particle in the rotating frame using the averaged vortex approximation, then minimize it variationally with respect to the envelope shape and the cloud width. For the uniform-lattice case they use a Gaussian-to-Thomas-Fermi (G-TF) trial function that interpolates between the two limits via a shape index \(\alpha\); for the distorted-lattice case they set \(h=1\) and allow the vortex density to vary radially.

## Key numbers

- For the experiments of Schweikhard et al. (2004) with \(^{87}\)Rb: \(N = 1.5 \times 10^5\), \(a_{\rm s} = 5.6\) nm, \(\omega_z = 2\pi \times 5.3\) Hz, and \(\Omega = 0.989\,\omega_\perp\), the interaction parameter is \(Na_{\rm s}/Z \gtrsim 26\) for uniform axial density and \(\kappa \gtrsim 52\); for a Gaussian axial profile \(\kappa \simeq 1.4 \times 10^2\).
- In the strong-interaction limit (\(\kappa \to \infty\)), the Thomas-Fermi energy is \(E'_{\rm TF} \simeq \Omega + \frac{4}{3}\omega_\perp \left(1-\Omega/\omega_\perp\right)^{1/2} \kappa^{1/2}\).
- The relative energy difference between the optimized G-TF profile and the Gaussian profile converges to \((2^{3/2}/3)-1 \simeq -0.0572\) as \(\kappa \to \infty\), independent of \(\Omega\).
- For a Thomas-Fermi density profile \(n(r) \sim 1-r^2/R^2\), the vortex density is \(n_{\rm v}(r) = 1/(\pi d_\perp^2) - (1/\pi R^2)(1-r^2/R^2)^{-2}\), and the mean radial vortex displacement is \(\delta r/r \sim 1/N_{\rm v}\), where \(N_{\rm v} \simeq R^2/d_\perp^2\) is the total number of vortices.

## Context

This work extends the earlier LLL analysis of Ho (2001), who predicted a Gaussian density profile for a uniform vortex lattice, and the Thomas-Fermi approach of Baym and Pethick (2004) for a uniform array. It reconciles the two by showing that allowing the vortex lattice to distort slightly from a regular triangular lattice (as first suggested by A.H. MacDonald and N. Read) yields a Thomas-Fermi profile even when the wave function is restricted to the LLL. The predicted small lattice distortions are consistent with measurements at lower rotation rates by Coddington et al. (2004) and with the theory of Sheehy and Radzihovsky (2004). The results enable quantitative modeling of rapidly rotating condensates in the mean-field quantum Hall regime, where \(\hbar\Omega\) becomes comparable to the interaction energy.
