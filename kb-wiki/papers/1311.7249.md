# From supernovae to neutron stars

> The gravitational collapse, bounce, the explosion of an iron core of an 11.2 $M_{\odot}$ star is simulated by two-dimensional neutrino-radiation hydrodynamic code. The explosion is driven by the neutrino heating aided by multi-dimensional hydrodynamic effects such as the convection. Following the explosion phase, we continue the simulation focusing on the thermal evolution of the protoneutron star up to $\sim$70 s when the crust of the neutron star is formed using one-dimensional simulation. We find that the crust forms at high-density region ($ρ\sim10^{14}$ g cm$^{-3}$) and it would proceed from inside to outside. This is the first self-consistent simulation that successfully follows from the collapse phase to the protoneutron star cooling phase based on the multi-dimensional hydrodynamic simulation.

## Key claim

This paper presents the first self-consistent simulation that follows the evolution of an iron-core collapse supernova from the collapse phase through the protoneutron star cooling phase, up to the formation of the neutron star crust at approximately 70 seconds post-bounce. The simulation relies on a specific 11.2 solar mass progenitor with a steep density gradient, which enables a successful explosion in two dimensions but would not explode in spherical symmetry.

## Method

The simulation uses a two-dimensional (2D) neutrino-radiation hydrodynamic code based on ZEUS-2D, with the Shen equation of state and the isotropic diffusion source approximation (IDSA) for spectral neutrino transport of electron neutrinos and antineutrinos. The 2D simulation runs from core collapse through bounce and explosion up to 690 ms post-bounce, after which all hydrodynamic quantities are angle-averaged and the evolution continues in one-dimensional (1D) spherical symmetry up to approximately 70 seconds, when the crust formation condition is met.

## Key numbers

* Progenitor mass: 11.2 solar masses (M_sun), from Woosley (2002).
* Core bounce occurs at a central density of approximately 3.1 x 10^14 g cm^-3, 150 ms after simulation start.
* Explosion diagnostic energy is approximately 10^50 erg.
* The protoneutron star mass is approximately 1.3 M_sun.
* Crust formation occurs at a density of approximately 10^14 g cm^-3 and a temperature of approximately 3 x 10^10 K, at a postbounce time of approximately 70 s.
* The critical temperature for lattice formation is given by T_c approximately 6.4 x 10^9 K times (rho/10^14 g cm^-3)^(1/3) times (Y_e/0.1)^(1/3) times (x_a/0.3)^(1/3) times (Z/26)^(5/3), with Gamma = 175.

## Context

This work extends prior multi-dimensional supernova simulations (e.g., Buras 2006, Marek 2009, Suwa 2010, Takiwaki 2012, Mueller 2012, Bruenn 2013) that achieved shock revival but did not follow the long-term protoneutron star cooling. It contrasts with earlier long-term studies by Burrows (1986) and Pons (1999), which used handmade initial conditions, and by Fischer (2010, 2012), which used one-dimensional codes with artificially amplified reaction rates and could not self-consistently explode an iron core. The simulation enables future studies of neutron star crust formation timing and properties, though the author notes that three-dimensional simulations, improved neutrino interactions, and general relativistic effects are needed for quantitative precision.
