# Excitation Energy as a Basic Variable to Control Nuclear Disassembly

> Thermodynamical features of Xe system is investigated as functions of temperature and freeze-out density in the frame of lattice gas model. The calculation shows different temperature dependence of physical observables at different freeze-out density. In this case, the critical temperature when the phase transition takes place depends on the freeze-out density. However, a unique critical excitation energy reveals regardless of freeze-out density when the excitation energy is used as a variable insteading of temperature. Moreover, the different behavior of other physical observables with temperature due to different $ρ_f$ vanishes when excitation energy replaces temperature. It indicates that the excitation energy can be seen as a more basic quantity to control nuclear disassembly.

# Excitation Energy as a Basic Variable to Control Nuclear Disassembly

## Key claim

The critical temperature for the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear disassembly depends on the freeze-out density, but when excitation energy per nucleon is used as the variable instead of temperature, a unique critical excitation energy appears that is independent of freeze-out density. This suggests excitation energy is a more fundamental control parameter than temperature for describing nuclear disassembly, particularly because excitation energy can be extracted from experiments while freeze-out density cannot be directly measured.

## Method

The authors employed the lattice gas model (LGM) to simulate the disassembly of a ¹²⁹Xe system at three different freeze-out densities (0.18ρ₀, 0.38ρ₀, and 0.60ρ₀). Nucleons were placed on cubic lattices of sizes 9³, 7³, and 6³ respectively using Monte Carlo sampling with the Metropolis algorithm, with momenta generated from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. Fragment mass distributions were constructed by identifying neighboring nucleons as belonging to the same fragment if their relative kinetic energy was insufficient to overcome the attractive bond. The critical point was identified through the power-law exponent τ of the mass distribution, the second moment S2 of fragment distribution, the average multiplicity of intermediate mass fragments, and the information entropy H.

## Key numbers

- Three freeze-out densities were studied: 0.18ρ₀, 0.38ρ₀, and 0.60ρ₀, corresponding to lattice sizes of 9³, 7³, and 6³ respectively
- Critical temperatures were 4.25 MeV for freeze-out density 0.18ρ₀, 5.5 MeV for 0.38ρ₀, and 6.5 MeV for 0.60ρ₀
- Calculations were performed from 3 MeV to 7 MeV with 1000 events accumulated at each temperature and freeze-out density
- The interaction constant for unlike nucleons (proton-neutron) was set to εₚₙ = -5.33 MeV; like-nucleon interactions (εₙₙ, εₚₚ) were set to 0 MeV
- The system studied was ¹²⁹Xe

## Context

This work extends the lattice gas model calculations of Pan and Das Gupta (1995) for isospin-symmetric nuclear systems, who found that critical behavior depends on both temperature and freeze-out density. The paper addresses the experimental difficulty that different thermometers give different apparent temperatures (Morrissey, 1994; Ma, 1997) and that freeze-out density is not directly measurable. By demonstrating that excitation energy collapses the freeze-out-density-dependent behavior onto a single curve, the authors enable a more straightforward comparison between theoretical lattice gas model predictions and experimental data from 4π multidetector measurements, where excitation energy can be reconstructed.
