# Comment on "Pulsar kicks via spin-1 color superconductivity"

> In a recent paper, Phys. Rev. Lett. 94, 211101 (2005) [hep-ph/0502166], Schmitt et al. have proposed a new mechanism to explain the observed velocities of pulsars. The proposed explanation is based on anisotropic emission of neutrinos from a cooling neutron star at temperatures below 0.1 MeV, thousands of years after the supernova. However, while neutrinos are copiously produced during the first minute of the supernova explosion, at later times the neutrino emission is negligible. Therefore, the proposed mechanism is not viable.

# Comment on "Pulsar kicks via spin-1 color superconductivity"

## Key claim
The pulsar kick mechanism proposed by Schmitt, Shovkovy, and Wang (2005), based on anisotropic neutrino emission from a cooling neutron star at temperatures below 0.1 MeV thousands of years after the supernova, is not viable because neutrino emission is negligible at such late times and because neutrinos produced in thermal equilibrium diffuse out isotropically regardless of anisotropic production cross sections.

## Method
The author examines the timing and physical conditions required for the proposed mechanism, comparing the neutrino emission timescale (first 10–15 seconds after supernova, negligible after 50 seconds) against the claimed operating temperature (T ≲ 0.1 MeV, reached 10³–10⁶ years post-supernova). The author also invokes a known no-go theorem (Kusenko and Segrè, 1996) that neutrinos in thermal equilibrium isotropize through scattering and cannot produce a net kick, and checks whether the mechanism could work if the phase transition occurred earlier.

## Key numbers
- Neutrino emission is copious only during the first 10–15 seconds after supernova onset, and almost none are produced after 50 seconds.
- The proposed mechanism requires temperatures T ≲ 0.1 MeV, reached 10³–10⁶ years after the supernova explosion.
- The thermal energy of a neutron star at 0.1 MeV is E_T ∼ 10⁴⁸ erg, which is smaller than the kinetic energy E_K ∼ 10⁴⁹ erg of a pulsar moving at 10³ km/s.

## Context
This comment directly refutes the pulsar kick mechanism proposed by Schmitt, Shovkovy, and Wang (2005) in Physical Review Letters. It extends the no-go theorem of Kusenko and Segrè (1996) regarding isotropization of thermal neutrinos, and contrasts the proposed mechanism with viable alternatives such as sterile neutrinos (keV mass range, also a dark matter candidate) and Majorons (Farzan, 2005). The comment clarifies that viable pulsar kick mechanisms must involve out-of-equilibrium or free-streaming neutrinos, such as those outside neutrinospheres or sterile neutrinos.
