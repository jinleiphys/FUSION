# Nuclear Skins and Halos in the Mean-Field Theory

> Nuclei with large neutron-to-proton ratios have neutron skins, which manifest themselves in an excess of neutrons at distances greater than the radius of the proton distribution. In addition, some drip-line nuclei develop very extended halo structures. The neutron halo is a threshold effect; it appears when the valence neutrons occupy weakly bound orbits. In this study, nuclear skins and halos are analyzed within the self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov and relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov theories for spherical shapes. It is demonstrated that skins, halos, and surface thickness can be analyzed in a model-independent way in terms of nucleonic density form factors. Such an analysis allows for defining a quantitative measure of the halo size. The systematic behavior of skins, halos, and surface thickness in even-even nuclei is discussed.

# Nuclear Skins and Halos in the Mean-Field Theory

## Key claim

Neutron skins and halos in spherical even-even nuclei can be analyzed in a model-independent way using nucleonic density form factors within the Helm model, allowing a quantitative decomposition of the neutron-proton radius difference into separate contributions from the skin (bulk size effect) and the halo (weak binding effect). However, in heavy nuclei the pairing anti-halo effect prevents the development of extremely extended halos like those seen in light nuclei, because pairing correlations keep the asymptotic decay constant finite even when the Fermi energy approaches zero.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent spherical Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Skyrme parametrizations SkP and SLy4, and relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) calculations using the NL3 and NL-SH parametrizations, for even-even nuclei across the nuclear chart. They extracted neutron and proton density form factors from the microscopic densities and fitted them to the Helm model, which parametrizes the density as a convolution of a sharp-surface sphere (diffraction radius R0) with a Gaussian folding function (surface thickness sigma). The halo parameter was defined as the difference between the geometric radius R_geom (derived from the rms radius) and the Helm radius R_Helm (derived from R0 and sigma), while the skin was defined as the difference between neutron and proton Helm radii.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-proton difference of surface thickness (sigma_n - sigma_p) gradually increases from about 0.2 fm around the beta stability line to about 0.5 fm near the neutron drip line.
- The neutron halo parameter delta_R_halo(n) does not exceed 1 fm in heavy even-even nuclei, as a result of the pairing anti-halo effect.
- The pairing gap Delta_n near the two-neutron drip line stays between approximately 1.8 MeV in light nuclei and approximately 1.2 MeV in the heaviest elements.
- The proton halo parameter is very small, of the order of 0.02 fm, except in very light sd-shell nuclei where delta_R_halo(p) can exceed 0.1 fm, with the largest values around 0.15 fm for 20Mg and 24Si.
- In the square-well model analysis, the total geometric radius diverges as (-epsilon)^{-1} when the binding energy of the 3s1/2 orbital approaches zero, while the Helm radii of both the total system and the core remain nearly constant.

## Context

This work extends the Helm model, originally introduced for electron scattering experiments by Helm (1956) and developed by Friedrich (1982, 1986), to the analysis of self-consistent mean-field densities of neutron-rich nuclei. It provides a quantitative framework that separates skin and halo contributions to neutron-proton radius differences, building on earlier studies of radii by Dobaczewski (1996) and others. The pairing anti-halo effect, introduced by Bennaceur (2000), is shown to suppress halo formation in heavy even-even nuclei, in contrast to the extreme single-particle picture of halos in light systems described by Riisager (1992). The systematic calculations enable future comparisons with experimental data on neutron radii from antiprotonic atoms (Lubinski, 1998; Schmidt, 1999) and giant resonance studies (Krasznahorkay, 1994, 1999).
