# An Alternative Approach to Quantum Imaginary Time Evolution

> There is increasing interest in quantum algorithms that are based on the imaginary-time evolution (ITE), a successful classical numerical approach to obtain ground states. However, most of the proposals so far require heavy post-processing computational steps on a classical computer, such as solving linear equations. Here we provide an alternative approach to implement ITE. A key feature in our approach is the use of an orthogonal basis set: the propagated state is efficiently expressed in terms of orthogonal basis states at every step of the evolution. We argue that the number of basis states needed at those steps to achieve an accurate solution can be kept of the order of $n$, the number of qubits, by controlling the precision (number of significant digits) and the imaginary-time increment. The number of quantum gates per imaginary-time step is estimated to be polynomial in $n$. Additionally, while in many QAs the locality of the Hamiltonian is a key assumption, in our algorithm this restriction is not required. This characteristic of our algorithm renders it useful for studying highly nonlocal systems, such as the occupation-representation nuclear shell model. We illustrate our algorithm through numerical implementation on an IBM quantum simulator.

## Key claim

The authors present an alternative quantum imaginary time evolution (QITE) algorithm that avoids the heavy classical post-processing (solving linear equations) required by prior QITE approaches, by using an orthogonal basis set that eliminates correlations between basis states, provided the number of basis states needed per step can be kept of order n (the number of qubits) by controlling precision and imaginary-time increment.

## Method

The algorithm implements imaginary-time evolution by expressing the propagated state in terms of orthogonal basis states at each step, obtained by executing the circuit U† Q U on the initial state and measuring in the computational basis to extract dominant components. For each Pauli string operator Q_k in the Hamiltonian, the real and imaginary parts of the coefficients c_j are measured using ancillary qubit circuits, and the corresponding unitary e^{i y_j P_j} is constructed using a decomposition that reduces multi-qubit gates to O(2n) one- and two-qubit gates. The algorithm builds the parametric quantum circuit layer by layer without classical optimization, with parameters in each layer uncorrelated by construction due to the orthogonal basis.

## Key numbers

- The number of quantum gates per imaginary-time step is estimated to be polynomial in n, the number of qubits.
- The circuit depth for implementing a multi-qubit gate of the form cos(α)I + i sin(α) O_1 ... O_n is of order O(2n).
- To achieve a precision of 10^{-ε} in determining amplitudes |c_j|, the number of circuit executions χ must satisfy χ ≥ η · 10^{2ε}, where η is the number of dominant basis states.
- The nuclear physics example Hamiltonian contains 84 Pauli string terms, with weights ranging from -0.56401 to 0.56401.

## Context

This work extends the QITE approach of Motta et al. (2019) by removing the need to solve linear equations for the parameters y_j, which in prior QITE required generalized inversion or iterative algorithms due to non-orthogonal basis sets. The algorithm is specifically designed for studying highly nonlocal systems such as the occupation-representation nuclear shell model, as it does not require the Hamiltonian to be local. The approach enables future applications to nuclear physics low-lying spectra, machine learning data preparation, and fusion energy simulations of Navier-Stokes fluids, though the authors note the algorithm is not suitable for current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) systems due to expected large circuit depths.
