# Collective motion in finite Fermi systems within Vlasov dynamics

> A semiclassical theory of linear response in finite Fermi systems, based on the Vlasov equation, and its applications to the study of isoscalar vibrations in heavy nuclei are reviewed. It is argued that the Vlasov equation can be used to study the response of small quantum systems like (heavy) nuclei in regimes for which the finite size of the system is more important than the collisions between constituents. This requires solving the linearized Vlasov equation for finite systems, however, in this case the problem of choosing appropriate boundary conditions for the fluctuations of the phase-space-density is non-trivial. Calculations of the isoscalar response functions performed by using different boundary conditions, corresponding to fixed and moving nuclear surface, are compared for different multipoles and it is found that, in a sharp-surface model, the moving-surface boundary conditions give better agreement with experiment. The semiclassical strength functions given by this theory are strikingly similar to the results of analogous quantum calculations, in spite of the fact that shell effects are not included in the theory, this happens because of a well known close relation between classical trajectories and shell structure.

# Collective motion in finite Fermi systems within Vlasov dynamics

## Key claim

The paper argues that the Vlasov equation can provide a semiclassical theory of linear response in finite Fermi systems like heavy nuclei, but only in regimes where finite-size effects dominate over collisions between constituents. The authors show that the choice of boundary conditions for the phase-space-density fluctuations is critical: in a sharp-surface model, moving-surface boundary conditions give better agreement with experiment than fixed-surface conditions, and the resulting semiclassical strength functions are strikingly similar to quantum calculations despite the absence of shell effects.

## Method

The authors solve the linearized Vlasov equation for a spherical nucleus by transforming to generalized coordinates (energy, angular momentum, radial coordinate, and Euler angles) that exploit constants of motion, reducing the seven-dimensional integro-differential equation to one-dimensional equations. They then compare two sets of boundary conditions at the nuclear surface: fixed-surface (mirror reflection at a static boundary) and moving-surface (reflection in a frame moving with the vibrating surface), with the surface vibration amplitude determined by equating the pressure fluctuation from the liquid-drop model to the microscopic pressure tensor. The collective response is obtained by solving an integral equation analogous to the RPA equation, using a separable multipole-multipole residual interaction.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear radius parameter: R = 1.2 A^(1/3) fm
- Surface tension parameter: σ ≈ 1 MeV fm^(-2)
- Restoring force parameter for surface vibrations (neglecting Coulomb): C_L = σ R^2 (L-1)(L+2)
- Dimensionless frequency parameter: s = ω / (v_F / R), where v_F = p_F / m
- Dimensionless eigenfrequency for a particle trajectory: s_{nN}(x) = (nπ + N α(x)) / x, with x = sin α and α related to angular momentum

## Context

This review extends earlier work by Bertsch (1977) who proposed the Vlasov equation as a semiclassical theory of giant resonances, and by Briere and collaborators who derived a similar solution of the linearized Vlasov equation for nuclear response. The approach builds on the solution method of Polyacenko and Shukhman from galactic dynamics and on the sharp-surface model with moving-surface boundary conditions introduced by Abrosimov, Di Toro, and Strutinsky. The paper enables a semiclassical description of both compression and surface modes in heavy nuclei, linking microscopic Vlasov dynamics to the macroscopic liquid-drop model, and shows that classical trajectory concepts can reproduce features usually attributed to quantum shell effects.
