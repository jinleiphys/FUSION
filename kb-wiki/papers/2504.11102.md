# Origin of Reactor Antineutrino Anomaly

> The reactor antineutrino anomaly, discovered in 2011, means a noticeable difference between the observed rate of inverse beta decays and the expected (theoretical) rate of such processes based on the measurement of the spectra of electrons in beta decay of $^{235}$U, $^{239}$Pu, $^{241}$Pu, and $^{238}$U nuclei and their subsequent conversion into right-handed antineutrino spectra. This paper provides a rationale for the fact that both right-handed and left-handed antineutrinos are produced in beta decays of nuclei. But the conversion procedure in any case assigns a right-handed antineutrino to each electron, which leads to the superiority of the theoretical rate of inverse beta decays over the experimental one. The right-handed antineutrino is produced in the mode of beta decay of the nucleus due to the standard electroweak interaction. The left-handed antineutrino appears in the mode caused by the existence of a interaction, the carrier of which is a massless pseudoscalar boson having a Yukawa coupling with the electron neutrino and nucleons. The emission of such a boson from a virtual right-handed antineutrino converts it into a free left-handed antineutrino.

## Key claim

The reactor antineutrino anomaly arises because both right-handed and left-handed antineutrinos are produced in nuclear beta decay, but the standard conversion procedure assigns a right-handed antineutrino to every emitted electron, artificially inflating the theoretical rate of inverse beta decays relative to the observed rate. The left-handed antineutrino appears via a new interaction mediated by a massless pseudoscalar boson that couples to the electron neutrino and nucleons.

## Method

The author calculates the partial and total beta decay rates for two specific fission fragments of 235U (143Xe → 143Cs and 90Br → 90Kr) in two modes: the standard electroweak mode producing a right-handed antineutrino, and a mode with emission of a massless pseudoscalar boson that converts the antineutrino to left-handed. The ratio of the two decay probabilities is equated to the ratio of observed to expected inverse beta decay rates (0.943 ± 0.023) from the reactor antineutrino anomaly, using a virtual antineutrino mass λ as an infrared regulator. The Yukawa coupling constant of the boson with the neutrino is then extracted for several values of λ.

## Key numbers

- Ratio of observed to expected inverse beta decay event rates: 0.943 ± 0.023 (from the reactor antineutrino anomaly, citing the original 2011 work).
- For the 143Xe → 143Cs transition: maximum electron energy E0 = 7.247 MeV; maximum difference between maximum and minimum antineutrino energy (H2 − H1)max = 197 eV.
- For the 90Br → 90Kr transition: maximum electron energy E0 = 10.338 MeV; (H2 − H1)max = 638 eV.
- Extracted Yukawa coupling constant of the massless pseudoscalar boson with the neutrino: gνps2/4π = 0.11 ± 0.05 (adopted estimate, averaging results for λ = 0.001 eV and λ = 0.000001 eV).
- Extracted Yukawa coupling constant of the boson with nucleons: gNps2/4π = (0.93 ± 0.54) × 10−9.
- Product of coupling constants from solar neutrino analysis (from prior work): (gνeps gNps)/4π = (3.2 ± 0.2) × 10−5.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous field-theoretical solution to the solar neutrino problem (Slad, 2020, 2021), which introduced a massless pseudoscalar boson interaction to explain solar neutrino observations without invoking neutrino oscillations. The paper directly contradicts the sterile neutrino hypothesis proposed by the original reactor anomaly discoverers (Mention et al., 2011) and argues that the particle oscillation concept violates conservation of energy-momentum for mass differences above 5.9 × 10−13 eV2. The result enables a unified explanation of both the reactor antineutrino anomaly and the gallium anomaly (from 51Cr source experiments) within the same framework, and suggests that left-handed antineutrinos in reactor spectra have been systematically misidentified.
