# Microscopic dynamics simulations of multi-nucleon transfer in $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni at 25 MeV/nucleon

> Multi-nucleon transfer in $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni at an incident energy of 25 MeV/nucleon is for the first time investigated with a microscopic dynamics model: improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model. The measured isotope distributions are reasonably well reproduced by using the ImQMD model together with a statistical code (GEMINI) for describing the secondary decay of fragments. The reaction mechanism is explored with the microscopic dynamics simulations from central to peripheral collisions. In central collisions there exists a strong competition among fusion, deep-inelastic scattering and multi-fragmentation at such an incident energy. In semi-peripheral collisions, binary scattering together with nucleon transfer is dominant, and the probability of elastic+inelastic scattering events increases rapidly with impact parameter in peripheral collisions and approaches to one when $b>14$ fm. The mass-TKE distribution in central collisions due to the competition is quite different from those in peripheral collisions and the distribution of total kinetic energy loss (TKEL) for binary events with nucleon transfer is much more broader than those without transfer.

# Microscopic dynamics simulations of multi-nucleon transfer in $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni at 25 MeV/nucleon

## Key claim
The improved quantum molecular dynamics (ImQMD) model, combined with the GEMINI statistical code for secondary decay, can reasonably reproduce measured isotope distributions for elements Z=30-35 in the $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni reaction at 25 MeV/nucleon, but only when the simulated events are filtered either by an angular range of $10^\circ$ to $27^\circ$ in the laboratory frame or by an impact parameter range of $8 < b < 14$ fm to select peripheral collisions. The claim is qualified by noting that discrepancies from experimental data are within one order of magnitude in general, comparable to the predictions of the deep inelastic transfer (DIT) code.

## Method
The authors performed ImQMD-v2.2 simulations of $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni at 25 MeV/nucleon for impact parameters from $b=1$ to $14$ fm in steps of 1 fm, generating 100,000 events per impact parameter and evolving the system to 1000 fm/c. The simulations used the Skyrme energy density functional (parameter set IQ3a) with the spin-orbit term omitted, and the Fermi constraint was applied to maintain fermionic features. Primary fragments from the ImQMD simulations were then fed into the GEMINI statistical code to model secondary decay via light particle evaporation and fission, with unmeasured nuclear masses taken from the Weizsacker-Skyrme (WS4) mass model.

## Key numbers
- Incident energy: 25 MeV/nucleon for the $^{86}$Kr+$^{64}$Ni system.
- Impact parameter range simulated: $b = 1$ to $14$ fm with $\Delta b = 1$ fm.
- Time step in ImQMD calculations: $\Delta t = 1$ fm/c; total simulation time: 1000 fm/c.
- Touching distance between the two nuclei: $R_{12} \simeq 11$ fm, estimated from charge radii plus a neutron-skin correction $\Delta R_{np} \simeq 0.6$ fm.
- In central collisions, the probability of fusion events is about 0.46, comparable to that of binary scattering events; the probability of ternary breakup and multi-fragmentation is about 0.1.
- The probability of ternary breakup and multi-fragmentation reaches 0.26 at $b = 6$ to $7$ fm.
- The probability of elastic+inelastic scattering events approaches one when $b > 14$ fm.
- Most events with nucleon transfer are localized at TKEL values above 40 MeV.
- In central collisions, the TKEL gradually approaches a value of about 600 MeV.
- In very peripheral collisions, the contact time is smaller than 100 fm/c and the corresponding TKEL is smaller than 20 MeV.

## Context
This work extends previous studies of multi-nucleon transfer (MNT) reactions at near-barrier energies (5-8 MeV/nucleon) by Watanabe et al., Barrett et al., and Corradi et al. to the higher incident energy of 25 MeV/nucleon, where competition among fusion, deep-inelastic scattering, and multi-fragmentation becomes significant. The ImQMD model results are compared with the semiclassical GRAZING model and the deep inelastic transfer (DIT) code of Tassan-Got and Stephan (1991), as well as with experimental data from Souliotis et al. (2002, 2003). The work enables future studies of transfer processes at very large impact parameters to learn about correlations, and provides a theoretical approach for estimating production cross sections and optimal projectile-target-energy combinations for synthesizing new neutron-rich isotopes through MNT reactions.
