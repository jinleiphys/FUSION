# Monitoring of the eclipsing Wolf-Rayet ULX in the Circinus galaxy

> We studied the eclipsing ultraluminous X-ray source CG X-1 in the Circinus galaxy, re-examining two decades of {\it Chandra} and {\it XMM-Newton} observations. The short binary period (7.21 hr) and high luminosity ($L_{\rm X} \approx 10^{40}$ erg s$^{-1}$) suggest a Wolf-Rayet donor, close to filling its Roche lobe; this is the most luminous Wolf-Rayet X-ray binary known to-date, and a potential progenitor of a gravitational-wave merger. We phase-connect all observations, and show an intriguing dipping pattern in the X-ray lightcurve, variable from orbit to orbit. We interpret the dips as partial occultation of the X-ray emitting region by fast-moving clumps of Compton-thick gas. We suggest that the occulting clouds are fragments of the dense shell swept-up by a bow shock ahead of the compact object, as it orbits in the wind of the more massive donor.

# Monitoring of the eclipsing Wolf-Rayet ULX in the Circinus galaxy

## Key claim

CG X-1 is the most luminous Wolf-Rayet X-ray binary known to-date and a potential progenitor of a gravitational-wave merger, but the X-ray eclipses are not caused by the donor star alone; instead, the dipping pattern in the X-ray lightcurve, variable from orbit to orbit, is interpreted as partial occultation of the X-ray emitting region by fast-moving clumps of Compton-thick gas, likely fragments of the dense shell swept-up by a bow shock ahead of the compact object as it orbits in the wind of the more massive donor.

## Method

The authors re-examined two decades of Chandra and XMM-Newton observations of CG X-1 in the Circinus galaxy, phase-connecting all observations to derive an average binary period. They analyzed individual orbital lightcurves to characterize the eclipse and dipping patterns, and constructed an Observed minus Calculated (O-C) diagram from the ingress mid-times to search for a period derivative. Spectral modelling was used to determine the de-absorbed X-ray luminosity and to show that during egress the flux recovery is caused by a variable covering fraction of Compton-thick clouds rather than a gradual decrease of absorbing column density.

## Key numbers

- Binary period: P = 25,970.1 ± 0.4 s ≈ 7.214 hr
- Average density inside the Roche lobe of the donor star: ρ ≈ 1.2 ρ⊙ ≈ 1.7 g cm⁻³ for mass ratio M₂/M₁ = 2, or ρ ≈ 0.74 ρ⊙ ≈ 1.1 g cm⁻³ for M₂/M₁ = 10
- De-absorbed X-ray luminosity out of eclipse: L_X ≈ 10⁴⁰ erg s⁻¹, with variability range from L_X ≈ 2.9 × 10⁴⁰ erg s⁻¹ (XMM-Newton observation of 2001 August 6) to L_X ≈ 3.5 × 10³⁹ erg s⁻¹ (Chandra observation of 2008 October 26)
- Period derivative: Ṗ/P = (8.4 ± 5.0) × 10⁻⁷ yr⁻¹, significant at the 95% confidence level
- Estimated orbital velocity of the compact object: ≈700 km s⁻¹ for a 10 M⊙ black hole orbiting a 20-30 M⊙ Wolf-Rayet star
- Mach number of the compact object through the donor wind: M ≈ 20 for a wind temperature ≈10⁵ K
- Estimated mass accretion rate onto the compact object: at least ≈10⁻⁶ M⊙ yr⁻¹ for radiative efficiency η ≈ 0.15
- If the Wolf-Rayet collapses into another black hole without disrupting the binary, the timescale for gravitational merger is ≈50 Myr (Esposito 2015)

## Context

This work extends the study of CG X-1 first reported by Bauer (2001) and Esposito (2015), who identified the 7.2 hr period and eclipsing behavior. The source is compared to other candidate Wolf-Rayet X-ray binaries listed in Table 1, including Cygnus X-3 (Hjalmarsdotter 2009, Koljonen 2010, Zdziarski 2012, McCollough 2016) and sources in NGC 4214, NGC 4490, NGC 253, NGC 300, and IC 10. CG X-1 is the only Wolf-Rayet X-ray binary with luminosity ∼10⁴⁰ erg s⁻¹; all others have luminosities ≲10³⁹ erg s⁻¹, consistent with wind accretion. The dipping behavior is interpreted via analogy with bow shocks in Wolf-Rayet/O-star binaries (Usov 1991, Stevens 1992) and with hydrodynamic instabilities of cooling shells (Park and Ricotti 2013). The period derivative measurement enables constraints on binary evolution models and the predicted rate of gravitational wave merger events from LIGO/Virgo (Abbott 2016a, 2016b, 2017a, 2017b, 2017c).
