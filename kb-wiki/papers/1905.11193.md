# Parallel tale of seniority isomers in 130Cd and 206Hg: Testing the robustness of magic numbers

> The neutron-rich nuclei 130Cd and 206Hg, so important in the astrophysical processes, may also be useful in tracking the evolution of nuclear shell gaps as one traverses the neutron-rich region. The high spin 8+ isomer in 130Cd and the 10+ isomer in 206Hg turn out to be the lampposts, which may shed light on the shell gaps and validity of the seniority scheme in the neutron-rich systems. We explore the robustness of the N=82 and N=126 magic numbers in the neutron-rich 130Cd and 206Hg nuclides, respectively. A parallel between the two nuclides in terms of the high-spin isomers allows us to investigate these waiting-point nuclei, which have limited experimental data, by using the concept of seniority as the stepping stone. In this paper, we report large scale shell model calculations by using the available realistic effective interactions derived from the Charge Dependent Bonn potential through the renormalized G matrix. We also explore if any change in the interaction is also required to consistently explain both the level structures as well the B(E2) values. A structural similarity between the 8+ isomer in 130Cd and the 10+ isomer in 206Hg is noticed due to goodness of seniority. They are found to possess a maximally aligned, seniority v = 2 configuration from their respective intruder orbits. No shell quenching seems to be needed for the seniority isomers in these nuclei. Therefore, N=82 and 126 appear to be very robust magic numbers even in the neutron-rich region.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the N=82 and N=126 magic numbers remain robust in the neutron-rich nuclei 130Cd and 206Hg, respectively, because the seniority v=2 isomers in these nuclei can be explained without invoking shell quenching; however, this conclusion is conditional on the use of modified two-body matrix elements (a 100 keV adjustment to the diagonal TBME of the relevant proton-hole configurations) to achieve consistency between calculated level energies and B(E2) values.

## Method

Large-scale shell model calculations were performed using the NUSHELL code with realistic effective interactions derived from the Charge Dependent Bonn potential via the renormalized G matrix method. For 130Cd, the "jj45pna" interaction was used with a 78Ni core (Z=28, N=50) and four active proton orbits (1f5/2, 2p3/2, 2p1/2, 1g9/2); for 206Hg, the "CWG" interaction was used with a 132Sn core (Z=50, N=82) and five active proton orbits (1g7/2, 2d5/2, 2d3/2, 3s1/2, 1h11/2). The interactions were then modified by adjusting the diagonal two-body matrix elements of the Πg9/2−2 configuration (for 130Cd) and the Πh11/2−2 configuration (for 206Hg) by 100 keV to improve agreement with experimental level energies and B(E2) values.

## Key numbers

- The experimental B(E2; 8+ → 6+) value for the 8+ isomer in 130Cd is 50 e2fm4; the calculated value with both original and modified interactions is 46 e2fm4.
- The experimental B(E2; 10+ → 8+) value for the 10+ isomer in 206Hg is 72 e2fm4; the calculated value with both original and modified interactions is 62.7 e2fm4.
- The calculated E(4+)/E(2+) ratio in 130Cd is 1.377, matching the experimental value of 1.406.
- The calculated E(4+)/E(2+) ratio in 206Hg is 1.423.
- The experimental separation between the 8+ and 6+ states in 130Cd is 138 keV; the calculated separation is 145 keV.
- The modification to the interaction in 130Cd involved increasing the diagonal Πg9/2−2 TBME by 100 keV; in 206Hg, the diagonal Πh11/2−2 TBME were reduced by 100 keV.

## Context

This work extends the study of seniority isomers in neutron-rich nuclei, building on prior investigations of Sn isotopes beyond 132Sn by Jain et al. (2017) and the experimental observation of the 8+ isomer in 130Cd by Jungclaus et al. (2007). The paper draws a parallel between the 8+ isomer in 130Cd (N=82) and the 10+ isomer in 206Hg (N=126) to test the robustness of magic numbers, contrasting with earlier suggestions of shell quenching in Cd isotopes by Dillmann et al. (2003). The results enable future experimental verification of predicted seniority isomers in lighter N=82 isotones down to Z=42 and highlight the need for improved effective interactions near the Z=82 shell closure, potentially using data from 207Tl.
