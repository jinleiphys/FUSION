# Self-learning Monte-Carlo for non-abelian gauge theory with dynamical fermions

> In this paper, we develop the self-learning Monte-Carlo (SLMC) algorithm for non-abelian gauge theory with dynamical fermions in four dimensions to resolve the autocorrelation problem in lattice QCD. We perform simulations with the dynamical staggered fermions and plaquette gauge action by both in HMC and SLMC for zero and finite temperature to examine the validity of SLMC. We confirm that SLMC can reduce autocorrelation time in non-abelian gauge theory and reproduces results from HMC. For finite temperature runs, we confirm that SLMC reproduces correct results with HMC, including higher-order moments of the Polyakov loop and the chiral condensate. Besides, our finite temperature calculations indicate that four flavor QC${}_2$D with $\hat{m} = 0.5$ is likely in the crossover regime in the Colombia plot.

## Key claim

The self-learning Monte-Carlo (SLMC) algorithm, when applied to non-abelian gauge theory with dynamical fermions in four dimensions, can reduce autocorrelation time compared to the hybrid Monte-Carlo (HMC) algorithm while exactly reproducing HMC results, provided the effective action used for proposing updates is sufficiently close to the target action; the method is exact regardless of the effective action quality, but acceptance rate and efficiency depend on how well the effective action approximates the target.

## Method

The authors implemented SLMC for SU(2) gauge theory (QC₂D) with four flavors of dynamical staggered fermions and the plaquette gauge action, using a heavy-mass expanded effective action containing plaquette, rectangular, and Polyakov loop terms whose coefficients were determined by linear regression from prior HMC runs. The proposal step used the heatbath algorithm with the effective action, followed by a Metropolis-Hastings test using the exact fermion determinant (computed via exact diagonalization) to correct for the difference between effective and target actions. Simulations were performed on lattices of sizes 4⁴, 6⁴, and 8⁴ at various β values (0.8–4.0) and quark masses (m̂ = 0.05–0.5), comparing results from HMC and SLMC for plaquette, rectangular Wilson loop, Polyakov loop, chiral condensate, and their higher-order cumulants.

## Key numbers

- Integrated autocorrelation time for the Polyakov loop at β = 2.5, 6⁴ lattice, m̂ = 0.5: τ_int^HMC = 62(24) versus τ_int^SLMC = 4.0(4), a reduction by a factor of approximately 15.
- Acceptance rate for SLMC with plaquette-only effective action at β = 2.5, 6⁴, m̂ = 0.5: approximately 57%; with plaquette+rectangular+Polyakov loop effective action: 73%; with additional 3×1-rectangular, chair, and crown operators: 77%.
- For lighter mass m̂ = 0.05 at β = 2.5, 6⁴: SLMC acceptance rate was 34%, compared to 82% for HMC; results remained consistent between algorithms.
- For volume 8⁴ at β = 2.5, m̂ = 0.5: SLMC acceptance rate was lower than for 6⁴ but not quantified precisely; for 4⁴ volume, SLMC acceptance reached approximately 80%.

## Context

This work extends the self-learning Monte-Carlo method, originally developed for classical spin models (Liu 2017) and quantum condensed matter systems (Nagai 2017), to non-abelian gauge theory with dynamical fermions, addressing the critical slowing-down problem that plagues HMC in certain parameter regimes (Schaefer 2010, Berg 1992, Ding 2020). The approach differs from earlier truncated-determinant methods (Irving 1997, Irving 1998, Duncan 1998) by performing extensive volume and action dependence studies, reformulating the algorithm in a machine-learning context, and demonstrating autocorrelation reduction at finite temperature. The results enable future applications of SLMC to larger lattices and more realistic QCD simulations, with the possibility of using stochastic estimators (Hasenfratz 2008, DeGrand 2008) to replace the exact diagonalization used here for the fermion determinant.
