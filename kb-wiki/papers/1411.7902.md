# The Brief Life of a Hadron: QCD unquenched

> Once upon a time, the picture of hadrons was of mesons made of a quark and an antiquark, and baryons of three quarks. Though hadrons heavier than the ground states inevitably decay by the strong interaction, the successes of the quark model might suggest their decays are a mere perturbation. However, Eef van Beveren, whose career we celebrate here, recognised that decays are an integral part of the life of a hadron. The channels into which they decay are often essential for their very existence. These hold the secrets of strong coupling QCD and teach us the way quarks really build hadrons.

## Key claim

The paper argues that hadronic decay channels are not a mere perturbation on a quark-model core but are integral to the existence of many mesons; states such as the light scalar mesons and the X, Y, Z charmonium-like states can be dynamically generated by strong S-wave coupling to hadronic channels, with the proportion of quark-antiquark versus molecular components varying case by case.

## Method

The paper reviews the analytic structure of meson amplitudes using Jost functions and complex-energy-plane pole counting, applied to the f0(980) as a test case. It compares fits to ππ and K̅K scattering data and to Ds decay data from BaBar, examining whether one pole (molecule-like) or two nearby poles (quark-core-like) are required. The same pole-counting logic is extended qualitatively to the X(3872) and other XYZ states.

## Key numbers

- The X(3872) has a mass 130–140 MeV above D̅D thresholds but a width of at most 2.3 MeV, making it live about 50 times longer than expected for a typical charmonium state.
- The Zc±(4340) has a Breit-Wigner mass of 4475 MeV and width 172 MeV from LHCb data.
- The charged and neutral kaon mass difference of 8 MeV separates the two K̅K thresholds and is used as the sole source of isospin breaking in the f0(980) analysis.
- The f0(980) pole on Sheet II is well-defined, but a second pole on Sheet III could not be distinguished in fits; when a two-pole fit was forced, the second pole’s residues were much smaller than those of the Sheet II pole.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing insight of van Beveren and collaborators (van Beveren, 1980s) that hadronic degrees of freedom can generate states orthogonal to simple quark-model configurations, and it applies the pole-counting theorem of Weinberg (1965) and Morgan (1990s) to the f0(980) and X(3872). It enables future analyses of XYZ states using high-precision data from heavy-flavor decays and lattice QCD, and it underscores that understanding the long-range (confinement) aspects of QCD is critical even for interpreting LHC collisions.
