# Modern Dynamical Coupled-Channels Calculations for Extracting and Understanding the Nucleon Spectrum

> We give an overview of recent progress in the spectroscopic study of nucleon resonances within the dynamical coupled-channels analysis of meson-production reactions. The important role of multichannel reaction dynamics in understanding various properties of nucleon resonances is emphasized.

# Modern Dynamical Coupled-Channels Calculations for Extracting and Understanding the Nucleon Spectrum

## Key claim

The dynamical coupled-channels (DCC) approach is necessary, not merely economical, for understanding the physics of reaction dynamics behind nucleon resonance properties, because the (on-shell) K-matrix approach, while cheaper and easier to fit data, cannot reveal how multichannel reaction dynamics generates resonance poles and meson-cloud effects. The paper argues that a naive one-to-one correspondence between physical resonances and baryons from static hadron models does not exist in general, as a single bare state can generate multiple physical resonance poles through coupling to reaction channels.

## Method

The ANL-Osaka DCC approach solves a multichannel Lippmann-Schwinger equation for partial-wave amplitudes, using a transition potential composed of non-resonant terms (hadron-exchange and Z-diagram potentials) plus a sum over bare N* states. The model includes seven channels (pi N, eta N, pi Delta, rho N, sigma N, K Lambda, K Sigma) where the quasi-two-body channels pi Delta, rho N, and sigma N subsequently decay into the three-body pi pi N channel. Model parameters were determined by a simultaneous fit to over 27,000 data points of differential cross sections and spin polarization observables for pi N -> pi N up to W=2.3 GeV, pi N -> eta N, K Lambda, K Sigma and gamma p -> pi N, eta N, K Lambda, K Sigma up to W=2.1 GeV, and gamma n -> pi N up to W=2 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Nearly 50 N* and Delta* baryons have been reported by the Particle Data Group, but more than half have no definitive conclusions even for their existence
- The decay widths of N* and Delta* resonances are very broad, approximately 300 MeV on average, which can be broader than the energy range of the two peaks at sqrt(s) ~ 1.5 GeV and sqrt(s) ~ 1.7 GeV that contain approximately 20 resonances
- The bare N* state for the P11 partial wave has a real mass of 1763 MeV, from which three physical resonance poles (A, B, C) are generated through coupling to multireaction channels
- Approximately 30% of the full dressed M1 transition form factor for the nucleon to Delta(1232)3/2+ resonance comes from the meson cloud in the low Q^2 region
- The ANL-Osaka DCC model was constructed by a simultaneous fit of more than 27,000 data points
- Electroproduction analysis covers the kinematical region up to Q^2 = 6 GeV^2 and W = 1.7 GeV
- The meson-cloud contribution to the A_{3/2} helicity amplitude for the Delta(1232)3/2+ transition is almost 30% at low Q^2 and becomes smaller as Q^2 increases

## Context

This work extends the dynamical coupled-channels approach developed by the ANL-Osaka collaboration (2007, 2013, 2016) and is part of a broader effort alongside the Bonn-Gatchina, Juelich, and SAID groups that have performed comprehensive partial-wave analyses of meson-production reaction data from photon- and electron-beam facilities (ELPH, ELSA, JLab, MAMI, SPring-8). The paper shows that the existence and mass values for low-lying N* and Delta* resonances below Re(M_R) ~ 1.7 GeV have been firmly established across these analyses, with one exception being the second P33 resonance (the Roper-like state of the Delta baryon) where pole masses fluctuate significantly between analyses. The ANL-Osaka DCC framework has also been applied to hyperon resonance spectroscopy via K- p and K- d reactions, meson spectroscopy via three-meson decay processes, and neutrino-induced reactions for neutrino-oscillation experiments.
