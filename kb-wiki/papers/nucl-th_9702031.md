# Treatment of the $Δ$ current in electromagnetic two-nucleon knockout reactions

> The contribution of the $Δ$(1232) isobar to the electromagnetic current of the two-nucleon system and its role in ($γ$,NN) processes is investigated. The difference between the genuine $Δ$-excitation current and that part of the current connected to the deexcitation of a preformed $Δ$ in the target nucleus is stressed. The latter cannot lead to a resonant behaviour of matrix elements for energies in the $Δ$ region. The reaction $^{16}O(γ,pp)^{14}C$, where the $Δ$ contribution is dominant at intermediate energies, is considered. The large variations found in the cross sections for different treatments stress the need for a proper treatment of the $Δ$ current for a clear understanding of the reaction mechanism of two-nucleon emission processes.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the $\Delta$(1232) isobar current in electromagnetic two-nucleon knockout reactions consists of two physically distinct parts (excitation and deexcitation) that must be treated with different energy-dependent propagators; the common practice of replacing the static low-energy propagator with a resonant propagator for both parts simultaneously is unjustified and leads to large errors, particularly overestimating cross sections by more than a factor of five at intermediate energies.

## Method

The authors derived the effective two-body $\Delta$ current operator for ($\gamma$,NN) reactions, separating it into a $\Delta$-excitation part (diagram I, where the photon excites the $\Delta$ which then decays via pion exchange) and a $\Delta$-deexcitation part (diagram II, where a virtual $\Delta$ is created by pion exchange and then deexcited by the photon). They compared three treatments of the $\Delta$ propagator: using the resonant propagator only in the excitation part (correct, labeled RN), using the resonant propagator in both parts (incorrect, labeled RR), and using the static low-energy approximation in both parts (labeled NN). These were applied to calculate exclusive cross sections for the $^{16}\text{O}(\gamma,pp)^{14}\text{C}$ reaction using a model with Jastrow-type correlations and optical potential final-state interactions.

## Key numbers

- At $E_\gamma=150$ MeV, the peak cross sections for $^{16}\text{O}(\gamma,pp)^{14}\text{C}$(g.s.) using $\vec\jmath_\Delta$(RN) and $\vec\jmath_\Delta$(RR) differ by nearly a factor of two.
- At $E_\gamma=300$ MeV, the peak cross sections for the same reaction differ by more than a factor of five between the correct (RN) and incorrect (RR) treatments.
- The $\Delta$ resonance mass is $M_\Delta=1232$ MeV, with an energy-dependent decay width $\Gamma_\Delta(\sqrt s)$.
- The low-energy approximation is considered justified only for energy transfers below 100 MeV.
- Using the alternative energy assignment $\sqrt{s_I}=E_\gamma+M_N$ (as in Ryckebusch et al.) shifts the resonance position to lower energies and further overestimates the cross section for energies below 260 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of nuclear isobar configurations (Green, 1976; Weber and Arenhoevel, 1978) and the effective operator approach for two-nucleon currents (Riska and Radomski, 1977; Van Orden and Donnelly, 1981). It corrects a common error in prior calculations (Dekker et al., 1991; Ryckebusch et al., 1994) where the resonant propagator was incorrectly applied to both excitation and deexcitation parts. The results enable proper analysis of experimental ($\gamma$,NN) data in the $\Delta$ region (Kanazawa et al., 1987; Arends et al., 1991; Audit et al., 1993; Cross et al., 1995) and future $(e,e'pp)$ measurements, particularly for extracting information about short-range correlations in nuclei.
