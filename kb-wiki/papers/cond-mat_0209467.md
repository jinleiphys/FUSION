# Second Law in Classical Non-Extensive Systems

> Equilibrium statistics of Hamiltonian systems is correctly described by the microcanonical ensemble, whereas canonical ones fail in the most interesting, mostly inhomogeneous, situations like phase separations or away from the thermodynamic ``limit'' (e.g. self-gravitating systems and small quantum systems). A new derivation of the Second Law is presented that respects these fundamental complications. Our ``geometric foundation of Thermo-Statistics'' opens the fundamental (axiomatic) application of Thermo-Statistics to non-diluted systems or to ``non-simple'' systems which are not similar to (homogeneous) fluids. Supprisingly, but also understandably, a so far open problem c.f. Uffink: cond-mat/0005327, page 50 and page 72.

## Key claim

The Second Law of thermodynamics (ΔS ≥ 0) can be derived for closed, small systems without invoking the thermodynamic limit, by reformulating Boltzmann's principle using a box-counting method that accounts for finite resolution in phase space.

## Method

The author starts from Boltzmann's principle S = ln W, where W is the area of the energy shell in phase space, and considers an initial equilibrium manifold M(t₀) = ℰ(E, Vₐ) that is suddenly expanded to a larger volume Vₐ + V_b. The time evolution of this manifold under Hamiltonian dynamics is followed; although the manifold remains compact and constant in volume due to Liouville's theorem, it becomes increasingly filamented. The entropy is redefined using a box-counting volume W_δ(t) with finite resolution δ, where the closure of the manifold at infinite times equals the new equilibrium manifold ℰ(E, Vₐ + V_b), yielding S(t→∞) > S(t₀).

## Key numbers

- The box-counting volume uses boxes of volume δ^(6N), where δ = √(2πℏ) is a natural quantum-mechanical resolution.
- The entropy at infinite times is given by S(t→∞) = N ln[(Vₐ + V_b)/Vₐ] + S(t₀).
- The Poincaré recurrence time t_rec for macroscopic systems is stated to be "several orders of magnitude larger than the age of the universe," citing Boltzmann (1896) and Gallavotti (1999).

## Context

This work extends the geometric foundation of thermo-statistics developed by Gross (2001, 2002) to provide a derivation of the Second Law that avoids the thermodynamic limit, addressing a problem identified as open by Uffink (2001). It contradicts the common view that the Second Law requires large recurrence times or the thermodynamic limit, as argued by Lebowitz (1999) and Gaspard (1997). The approach enables application of thermo-statistics to non-extensive systems such as self-gravitating systems and small quantum systems, where canonical ensembles fail.
