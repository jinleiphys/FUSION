# Fluctuation and Dissipation in Classical Many-Particle Systems

> Coarse-grained Langevin-type effective field equations are derived for classical systems of particles. These equations include the effects of thermal fluctuation and dissipation which may arise from coupling to an external bath, as in the Brownian motion of a single particle, or which may arise from statistical fluctuations in small parts of an isolated many-particle system, as in sound waves. These equations may provide some guidance for the analysis of mesoscopic or microscopic molecular systems, or for systems of hundreds to thousands of subatomic particles produced in high energy nuclear collisions.

## Key claim

Coarse-grained Langevin-type effective field equations that include thermal fluctuation and dissipation can be derived for classical many-particle systems in two distinct limits: one where particles are coupled to an external heat bath (yielding a damping term linear in velocity) and one where the system itself acts as its own heat bath via interactions among subsystems (yielding a damping term proportional to the Laplacian of the velocity), with the caveat that the derivation assumes the coarse-graining time is much larger than the correlation time of the fluctuating forces and that velocities vary slowly on those scales.

## Method

The authors start from the generalized Langevin equation for a single Brownian particle and extend it to a chain of coupled Brownian particles interacting with an external heat bath via fluctuating forces, using linear response theory to relate the ensemble-averaged fluctuating force to the velocity-dependent friction kernel via the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. They then remove the external heat bath and instead partition the many-particle system into small subsystems, treating the random momentum transfers between neighboring subsystems as the source of fluctuation and dissipation, and take the continuum limit to obtain field equations for the displacement field. The resulting Langevin field equations are expressed in terms of a scalar density field \(\phi = -\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\eta}\) describing local compression, and the dissipative terms are compared with the Navier-Stokes equation to identify shear and bulk viscosities.

## Key numbers

- The damping constant for an external heat bath in the continuum limit is \(\gamma = \frac{1}{3}\gamma_{\rm L} + \frac{2}{3}\gamma_{\rm T}\), where \(\gamma_{\rm L} = 4\pi \Gamma(3) \langle f^2_{\rm L} \rangle \tau_{\rm L} \lambda_{\rm L}^3 / (\rho T)\) and similarly for \(\gamma_{\rm T}\).
- The shear viscosity derived from the internal heat bath case is \(\eta = \frac{1}{120} \int_0^{\infty} ds \int d^3y \ y^2 \ (4k_{\rm T} + k_{\rm L})\).
- The bulk viscosity derived from the internal heat bath case is \(\zeta = \frac{1}{72} \int_0^{\infty} ds \int d^3y \ y^2 \ (k_{\rm L} - 2k_{\rm T})\).
- The effective damping coefficient for the scalar field equation in the internal heat bath case is \(\gamma^* = \frac{1}{120 \rho} \int_0^{\infty} ds \int d^3y \ y^2 \ (3 k_{\rm L} + 2 k_{\rm T})\).

## Context

This work extends the textbook treatment of Brownian motion (Reif, 1965) and the generalized Langevin equation formalism (Cortes and others) to continuous classical many-particle systems, addressing a gap in the literature where thermal fluctuations in dense, interacting classical continua were not well described. The authors were motivated by the problem of disoriented chiral condensates (DCC) in high-energy nuclear collisions (Wilczek, and other DCC references), where coarse-grained field equations including fluctuation and dissipation are needed to describe the evolution of pion fields. The derived field equations, particularly the distinction between external and internal heat bath cases (the latter yielding a Laplacian damping term that reduces to the Navier-Stokes equation in the low-frequency limit), provide a framework for analyzing mesoscopic molecular systems and the hundreds to thousands of subatomic particles produced in nuclear collisions.
