# Exact Dynamical and Partial Symmetries

> We discuss a hierarchy of broken symmetries with special emphasis on partial dynamical symmetries (PDS). The latter correspond to a situation in which a non-invariant Hamiltonian accommodates a subset of solvable eigenstates with good symmetry, while other eigenstates are mixed. We present an algorithm for constructing Hamiltonians with this property and demonstrate the relevance of the PDS notion to nuclear spectroscopy, to quantum phase transitions and to mixed systems with coexisting regularity and chaos.

# Exact Dynamical and Partial Symmetries

## Key claim

Partial dynamical symmetries (PDS) represent an intermediate symmetry structure where a non-invariant Hamiltonian accommodates a subset of solvable eigenstates with good symmetry, while other eigenstates are mixed; this is not an exact symmetry of the Hamiltonian, but rather a property that the commutator of the Hamiltonian with the symmetry generators vanishes only when acting on those "special" states.

## Method

The authors present an algorithm for constructing Hamiltonians with partial dynamical symmetry based on identifying n-particle annihilation operators that annihilate all states in a chosen irreducible representation of the symmetry group G. Any normal-ordered interaction written as a sum of products of these annihilation operators and their Hermitian conjugates, added to a dynamical symmetry Hamiltonian, yields a PDS Hamiltonian where the chosen irrep states remain solvable with good symmetry while other states mix. The method is illustrated using the interacting boson model (IBM) with U(6) dynamical algebra, focusing on the SU(3) chain relevant to deformed nuclei.

## Key numbers

- For the SU(3)-PDS Hamiltonian applied to ¹⁶⁸Er (N=16 bosons): parameters h₀=8, h₂=4, C=13 keV produce a spectrum where the ground g(K=0) and γ(K=2) bands remain solvable with good SU(3) symmetry, while the excited K=0⁺₂ band involves about 13% SU(3) admixtures into the dominant (2N-4,2) irrep.
- The solvable γ-band states in the SU(3)-PDS have energies E = h₂·6k(2N-2k+1) for k=1, with angular momenta L=K,K+1,...,(2N-2k).
- For the first-order critical Hamiltonian Ĥ_cri = h₂ P†₂·P̃₂ with h₂=0.05 and N=10, the spherical L=3 state has energy E = 6h₂(2N-1) and is degenerate with the SU(3) γ-band.
- In the chaos study with N=25 bosons and h₂/h₀=7.5, the fraction of solvable states decreases like 1/N² with boson number, yet all classical and quantum measures of chaos show a pronounced minimum near β₀=√2 where the partial SU(3) symmetry occurs.

## Context

This work extends the concepts of exact and dynamical symmetries by introducing partial dynamical symmetries as a hierarchy of broken symmetries, generalizing earlier work on spectrum generating algebras in nuclear physics (Iachello and Arima, 1987; Iachello, 2006). The algorithm for constructing PDS Hamiltonians was developed in Alhassid and Leviatan (1992) and further elaborated in Ramos, Leviatan, and Van Isacker (2009). The paper demonstrates PDS relevance to three areas: nuclear spectroscopy of ¹⁶⁸Er, where SU(3)-PDS lifts the β-γ degeneracy that plagues the exact SU(3) dynamical symmetry; quantum phase transitions, where critical-point Hamiltonians exhibit coexisting U(5)-PDS and SU(3)-PDS; and mixed regular-chaotic dynamics, where PDS suppresses chaos even though the fraction of solvable states vanishes in the classical limit.
