# Heavy-ion physics studies for the Future Circular Collider

> The Future Circular Collider (FCC) design study is aimed at assessing the physics potential and the technical feasibility of a new collider with centre-of-mass energies, in the hadron-hadron collision mode including proton and nucleus beams, more than seven-times larger than the nominal LHC energies. An electron-positron collider in the same tunnel is also considered as an intermediate step, which would provide the electron-hadron option in the long term. First ideas on the physics opportunities with heavy ions at the FCC are presented, covering the physics of Quark-Gluon Plasma, gluon saturation, photon-induced collisions, as well as connections with ultra-high-energy cosmic rays.

## Key claim

The paper presents first ideas on the physics opportunities with heavy ions at the Future Circular Collider (FCC), noting that the increased centre-of-mass energy and luminosity relative to the LHC open new opportunities, but the study is explicitly described as "first general considerations" and "first ideas" rather than a definitive physics case.

## Method

The authors extrapolate global event characteristics (charged-particle multiplicity, transverse energy, Bose-Einstein correlation parameters) from LHC measurements to FCC energies using power-law parametrizations, and complement these with full hydrodynamic simulations. They compute hard scattering cross sections at next-to-leading order (NLO) using the MCFM code for various processes as a function of centre-of-mass energy, and estimate kinematic coverage in the (x, Q²) plane for proton-nucleus and ultra-peripheral collisions.

## Key numbers

- Pb-Pb collisions at FCC would have centre-of-mass energy per nucleon-nucleon collision of 39 TeV (from 100 TeV pp collisions); p-Pb collisions would reach 63 TeV
- Charged-particle multiplicity at mid-rapidity in central Pb-Pb collisions is extrapolated to 3600 at 39 TeV, compared to 1600 at 2.76 TeV (LHC)
- Bose-Einstein homogeneity volume is extrapolated to 11000 fm³ at 39 TeV, compared to 5000 fm³ at 2.76 TeV
- Bose-Einstein decoupling time is extrapolated to 13 fm/c at 39 TeV, compared to 10 fm/c at 2.76 TeV
- Energy density at 1 fm/c is estimated at 35 GeV/fm³ for FCC, compared to 12 GeV/fm³ at 2.76 TeV
- Initial temperature could be as large as approximately 800 MeV if thermalization time is significantly lower than 0.1 fm/c
- Cross sections at 39 TeV relative to 5.5 TeV increase by factors: top production 80, Z⁰+1 Jet (p_T > 50 GeV/c) 20, bottom production 8, Z⁰ production 7
- An experiment at FCC could record about 2.5 × 10⁴ fully reconstructed top-antitop events with dilepton topology using a Pb-Pb sample of 5 nb⁻¹ integrated luminosity
- FCC extends kinematic coverage in x down to approximately 10⁻⁷ for p-Pb collisions, and down to 10⁻⁶ even in the region Q² > 10 GeV²
- Quarkonium production in ultra-peripheral collisions probes gluon densities at x values down to 10⁻⁷, more than two orders of magnitude lower than at LHC energy
- Pb-Pb collisions at FCC correspond to a primary cosmic-ray interaction energy of a few 10¹⁸ eV, reducing the extrapolation to the highest observed cosmic-ray energies to about one order of magnitude

## Context

This paper extends the heavy-ion physics program from the LHC to the proposed FCC, building on extrapolations from LHC measurements at 2.76 TeV and 5.5 TeV. The work enables future studies of Quark-Gluon Plasma at higher temperatures and energy densities, gluon saturation at lower x values than accessible at LHC, and connections to ultra-high-energy cosmic-ray physics where current LHC data correspond to primary cosmic-ray energies of a few 10¹⁶ eV, about three orders of magnitude below the highest observed energies. The paper references specific prior work including the FCC kickoff meeting (2014), extrapolation studies, hydrodynamic simulations by Florchinger, lattice QCD calculations by Ratti, and the CMS Collaboration projections for top quark measurements.
