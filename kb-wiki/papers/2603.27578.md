# Heavy-ion collision simulation with high performance computer

> Heavy-ion collision is an important tool to understand the dense nuclear matter properties. In order to understand the results of the heavy-ion collision experiments, both theoretical approaches to dense nuclear matter using effective models and the computer simulations with given theoretical models have been performed. Due to the complexity of the system and the theoretical framework, the heavy-ion collision simulations require heavy computer resources. In this talk, we report our recent preliminary work on the heavy-ion collision simulation using DaeJeon Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (DJBUU) and Sindong Quantum Molecular Dynamics (SQMD) model with high performance computers (HPC).

## Key claim

The authors report preliminary work showing that heavy-ion collision simulations using the DaeJeon Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (DJBUU) and Sindong Quantum Molecular Dynamics (SQMD) models on high performance computers can produce consistent results at lower beam energies (50 AMeV), but show relatively large model-dependent differences at higher beam energies (100 AMeV) and for unstable projectiles, with the caveat that these are preliminary results and further model refinement is needed.

## Method

The authors performed transport model simulations of heavy-ion collisions using two complementary approaches: the DJBUU model (a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck type) which solves the relativistic BUU equation using test particles with polynomial profile functions, and the SQMD model (a Quantum Molecular Dynamics type) which treats nucleons as Gaussian wave packets with density-dependent Skyrme potential. They simulated collisions of stable nuclei (208Pb + 40,48Ca) at beam energies of 50 and 100 AMeV with various impact parameters, and also performed simulations with the unstable projectile 20Na + 208Pb. Additionally, they implemented the Quark-Meson Coupling (QMC) model in DJBUU and compared it with the original Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) implementation for 40Ca + 40Ca collisions at 100 AMeV.

## Key numbers

- For 208Pb + 40,48Ca simulations at 50 AMeV beam energy, both models produced similarly sized biggest fragments (BFs); for example at b=0 fm with 40Ca target, DJBUU produced 163Ta, 162Ta, 164Ta while SQMD produced 163Tm, 173W, 169Hf
- At 100 AMeV beam energy with 40Ca target and b=0 fm, DJBUU produced BFs around mass number 121-124 (e.g., 123Ba, 121Cs) while SQMD produced BFs around mass number 78-124 (e.g., 78As, 114Sn, 124Xe)
- For 20Na + 208Pb simulations, DJBUU showed about 30% bigger BFs than SQMD
- In DJBUU simulations of 196Au+196Au, the simulation used 100 test-particles per nucleon in a 100 fm x 100 fm x 100 fm cubic box with 1,000,000 grid cells, 39,200 particles, and 700 time steps over 140 fm/c with 0.2 fm/c intervals
- The Skyrme potential parameters used in SQMD are alpha = -218 MeV, beta = 164 MeV, and gamma = 4/3, with saturation density rho_0 = 0.16 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends the development of transport models for heavy-ion collision simulations at the Rare isotope Accelerator complex for ON-line experiment (RAON) being built in Korea, building on prior BUU-type models by Bertsch (1984) and QMD-type models by Aichelin (1991). The comparative study between DJBUU and SQMD models, and the implementation of the QMC model originally developed by Guichon (1988) into DJBUU, enables future investigations of dense nuclear matter properties that can be compared with neutron star observations from gravitational wave events like GW170817 by LIGO/Virgo (2017) and NICER X-ray observations by Miller (2019). The results provide direction for further model refinement to better simulate rare isotope beam collisions at RAON and to study observables sensitive to the nuclear equation of state, such as direct flow and pion yield ratio.
