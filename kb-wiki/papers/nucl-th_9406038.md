# Nuclear Transparency in Quasifree Electron Scattering - Theory and Experiment

> We make detailed comparisons between the measured $Q^2$ dependency of nuclear transparency in quasifree electron scattering and theoretical predictions based on Glauber theory. We also describe a theory-free method to extract $T$ from data.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the measured nuclear transparency in quasifree electron scattering shows no evidence for color transparency, and that the data can be explained by standard Glauber theory calculations using on-shell proton-nucleon cross sections, provided nuclear correlations are included.

## Method

The authors performed Glauber theory calculations of nuclear transparency for quasifree (e,e'p) reactions, incorporating Jastrow-type two-body spatial correlations in the nuclear wavefunction. They generated N-particle distributions that reproduce both single-nucleon densities and accepted measures of two-body correlations, then computed the transmission coefficient as the product of standard Glauber factors for each nucleon weighted by the N-1 particle density. The calculations used measured on-shell total p-p and p-n cross sections, assuming identical proton and neutron densities.

## Key numbers

- At the lowest Q² of 1.04 GeV² (corresponding to proton momentum p = 1.20 GeV/c), the cross sections are low and transparency T should be higher than average
- The ratio T(Q² = 1.04)/T(Q² = 3.06) increases when going from carbon to gold, as expected from theory since higher A nuclei allow more rescattering
- To fit the data without correlations would require decreasing the effective cross section by about 12 mb from the normal on-shell cross section
- For ¹²C calculations, a harmonic oscillator shell-model density was used with length parameter b = 1.64 fm
- The deuteron calculation used a Hulthén wavefunction with a hard core of 0.43 fm

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Glauber calculations of transparency in both (e,e'p) and (p,2p) reactions (Walet 1994; Frati earlier version), and compares directly with data from the SLAC NE18 collaboration (Makins 1994; O'Neill 1994). The results contradict the prediction by Brodsky (1979) that color transparency effects should appear at high momentum transfers. The paper also describes a method to extract transparency independent of the spectral function, which could enable cleaner experimental determinations in future experiments.
