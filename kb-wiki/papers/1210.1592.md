# Do Skyrme forces that fit nuclear matter work well in finite nuclei?

> A shortlist of Skyrme force parameterizations, recently found to have passed a series of constraints relating to nuclear matter properties is analyzed for their ability to reproduce data in finite nuclei. We analyse binding energies, isotope shifts and fission barriers. We find that the subset of forces have no common ability to reproduce (or otherwise) properties of finite nuclei, despite passing the extensive range of nuclear matter constraints.

## Key claim
Skyrme force parameterizations that pass a comprehensive set of nuclear matter constraints do not necessarily reproduce properties of finite nuclei; the subset of forces shows no common ability to fit binding energies, fission barriers, or isotope shifts, despite all passing the same nuclear matter tests.

## Method
The authors selected five Skyrme parameter sets (SKRA, KDE0v1, NRAPR, LNS, and SQMC700) that had previously passed nuclear matter constraints from Dutra (2012). They performed Hartree-Fock calculations for binding energies of 15 doubly-magic and semi-magic nuclei, computed fission barriers for 240Pu using constrained Hartree-Fock with volume delta pairing, and calculated the charge radius isotope shift between 40Ca and 48Ca. Results were compared against reference forces SkI4 (for binding energies and isotope shifts) and SkM* (for fission barriers).

## Key numbers
- Binding energies were evaluated for 15 nuclei: 16O, 34Si, 40Ca, 48Ca, 48Ni, 56Ni, 68Ni, 78Ni, 80Zr, 90Zr, 100Sn, 114Sn, 132Sn, 146Gd, and 208Pb; results were plotted against sqrt(NZ) to separate points.
- The experimental isotope shift between 40Ca and 48Ca is r2(48Ca) - r2(40Ca) = 0.007 fm2.
- For the fission barrier in 240Pu, KDE0v1 reproduced the SkM* reference barrier very well, while SKRA and NRAPRii showed weak barriers, and LNS showed no barrier structure (hyper-deformed minimum as ground state).
- The derivative L of the symmetry energy at saturation density showed a strong correlation with the calcium isotope shift, with the subset of forces falling within the allowed range from the nuclear matter constraints (labeled MIX2).

## Context
This work extends the nuclear matter constraint study of Dutra (2012), which produced a shortlist of Skyrme parameterizations based on macroscopic and microscopic nuclear matter properties plus neutron star observations, but did not test finite-nucleus performance. The authors compare against SkM*, which was specifically fitted to reproduce the fission path of 240Pu (Bartel et al., 1982), and SkI4, which was fitted to ground-state data including isotope shifts. The calcium isotope shift correlation with L had been previously shown in lead isotopes by Chen (2009). The results suggest that fitting to nuclear matter alone is insufficient for reliable finite-nucleus predictions, and that KDE0v1, which included finite-nucleus data in its fit, performs best overall.
