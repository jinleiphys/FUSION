# Effective viscosities in a hydrodynamically expanding boost-invariant QCD plasma

> Background: The near-equilibrium properties of a QCD plasma can be encoded into transport coefficients such as bulk and shear viscosity. In QCD, the ratio of these transport coefficients to entropy density, $ζ/s$ and $η/s$, depends non-trivially on the plasma's temperature. Purpose: We show that in a 0+1D boost-invariant fluid, a temperature-dependent $ζ/s(T)$ or $η/s(T)$ can be described by an equivalent effective viscosity $\left\langle ζ/s \right\rangle_{\textrm{eff}}$ or $\left\langle η/s \right\rangle_{\textrm{eff}}$. We extend the concept of effective viscosity in systems with transverse expansion, and discuss how effective viscosities can be used to identify families of $ζ/s(T)$ and $η/s(T)$ that lead to similar hydrodynamic evolution. Results: In 0+1D, the effective viscosity is expressed as a simple integral of $ζ/s(T)$ or $η/s(T)$ over temperature, with a weight determined by the speed of sound of the fluid. The result is general for any equation of state with a moderate temperature dependence of the speed of sound, including the QCD equation of state. In 1+1D, a similar definition of effective viscosity is obtained in terms of characteristic trajectories in time and transverse direction. This leads to an infinite number of constraints on an infinite functional space for $ζ/s(T)$ and $η/s(T)$. Conclusions: The definition of effective viscosity in a 0+1D system clarifies how infinite families of $ζ/s(T)$ and $η/s(T)$ can result in nearly identical hydrodynamic temperature profiles. By extending the study to a boost-invariant cylindrical (1+1D) fluid, we identify an approximate but more general definition of effective viscosity that highlight the potential and limits of the concept of effective viscosity in fluids with limited symmetries.

## Key claim

In a 0+1D boost-invariant fluid, a temperature-dependent viscosity ratio $\zeta/s(T)$ or $\eta/s(T)$ can be represented by an equivalent effective viscosity $\left\langle \zeta/s \right\rangle_{\textrm{eff}}$ or $\left\langle \eta/s \right\rangle_{\textrm{eff}}$, but this definition requires a series of approximations even in this highly symmetric system, and the concept becomes approximate and more limited when extended to systems with transverse expansion (1+1D).

## Method

The authors used the relativistic Navier-Stokes hydrodynamic equations for a boost-invariant (Bjorken) fluid, first in 0+1D (no transverse expansion) and then in 1+1D (cylindrically-symmetric transverse expansion). They derived expressions for effective viscosity by applying the mean value theorem to the viscous term in the temperature evolution equation, using an iterative solution that approximates the viscous temperature profile with the ideal one. For the 0+1D case, the analysis was extended to Israel-Stewart-type second-order hydrodynamics to examine the effect of relaxation times and initial conditions on the effective viscosity concept.

## Context

This work extends the common practice of assuming a constant effective shear viscosity in heavy-ion phenomenology (as noted in the introduction referencing Busza (2018) and Bernhard (2019)) by providing a formal definition of effective viscosity for temperature-dependent $\eta/s(T)$ and $\zeta/s(T)$. It builds on Bjorken's (1982) boost-invariant hydrodynamics and uses the QCD equation of state from Borsanyi (2013). The results clarify why infinite families of temperature-dependent viscosities can produce nearly identical hydrodynamic temperature profiles in 0+1D, and they identify the limits of this degeneracy when transverse expansion or second-order hydrodynamic effects (such as relaxation times) are included. This enables more systematic identification of which features of $\eta/s(T)$ and $\zeta/s(T)$ can be constrained by experimental data from heavy-ion collisions.
