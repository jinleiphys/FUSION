# Pulsar Glitches: The Crust may be Enough

> Pulsar glitches-the sudden spin-up in the rotational frequency of a neutron star-suggest the existence of an angular-momentum reservoir confined to the inner crust of the neutron star. Large and regular glitches observed in the Vela pulsar have originally constrained the fraction of the stellar moment of inertia that must reside in the solid crust to about 1.4%. However, crustal entrainment-which until very recently has been ignored-suggests that in order to account for the Vela glitches, the fraction of the moment of inertia residing in the crust must increase to about 7%. This indicates that the required angular momentum reservoir may exceed that which is available in the crust. We explore the possibility that uncertainties in the equation of state provide enough flexibility for the construction of models that predict a large crustal thickness and consequently a large crustal moment of inertia. Given that analytic results suggest that the crustal moment of inertia is sensitive to the transition pressure at the crust-core interface, we tune the parameters of the model to maximize the transition pressure, while still providing an excellent description of nuclear observables. In this manner we are able to obtain fractional moments of inertia as large as 7% for neutron stars with masses below 1.6 solar masses. In particular, we find that if the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb falls within the (0.20-0.26) fm range, large enough transition pressures can be generated to explain the large Vela glitches without invoking an additional angular-momentum reservoir beyond that confined to the solid crust. Our results suggest that the crust may be enough.

## Key claim

The authors argue that uncertainties in the equation of state of neutron-rich matter may be large enough to allow crustal moments of inertia as high as 7% of the total stellar moment of inertia for neutron stars below 1.6 solar masses, potentially explaining Vela pulsar glitches without invoking an additional angular-momentum reservoir beyond the solid crust, provided the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb falls within the 0.20–0.26 fm range.

## Method

The authors computed total and crustal moments of inertia in the slow-rotation approximation using relativistic mean-field models to generate the equation of state of neutron-star matter. They tuned the isoscalar-isovector coupling parameter Λ_v to maximize the transition pressure at the crust-core interface while maintaining an excellent description of nuclear observables, and used a relativistic random phase approximation to determine the crust-core transition density. The equation of state combined a Baym-Pethick-Sutherland outer crust, a polytropic inner crust, and a relativistic mean-field uniform core.

## Key numbers

- The glitch activity parameter for Vela is A_g ≈ 2.277 × 10⁻¹⁴ s⁻¹, derived from 17 glitches over 41.421 years with cumulative spin-up ∑_n ΔΩ_n/Ω = 2.9760 × 10⁻⁵.
- The original constraint on the fractional crustal moment of inertia from Vela glitches is I_crust/I ≳ 0.016 (1.6%).
- Including crustal entrainment with ⟨m_n^⋆⟩/m_n ≈ 4.3 raises the constraint to I_crust/I ≳ 0.07 (7%).
- The authors obtained fractional moments of inertia as large as I_crust/I ≈ 0.09 for a canonical 1.4 M_⊙ neutron star.
- The neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb measured by PREX is R_skin²⁰⁸ = 0.33⁺⁰·¹⁶₋₀·₁₈ fm.
- The models that maximize transition pressure predict R_skin²⁰⁸ values of 0.20 fm (NL3max), 0.22 fm (FSUmax), and 0.26 fm (TFcmax).
- The Vela pulsar has spin frequency Ω = 70.338 060 018 s⁻¹ and spin-down rate Ω̇ = −9.8432 × 10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹.

## Context

This work extends the standard glitch model of Anderson and Pines (1975) and the constraint formalism of Link, Epstein, and Lattimer (1999) by addressing the recent challenge from Andersson et al. (2012) and Chamel (2012) that crustal entrainment raises the required crustal moment of inertia to about 7%, suggesting the crust alone may be insufficient. The authors show that uncertainties in the equation of state, particularly the density dependence of the symmetry energy and its correlation with the neutron-skin thickness of 208Pb, can produce models with large enough transition pressures to satisfy the entrainment-corrected constraint. This enables a direct laboratory test of the glitch mechanism via measurements of the neutron-skin thickness at Jefferson Laboratory (PREX-II).
