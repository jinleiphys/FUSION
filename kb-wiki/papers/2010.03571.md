# Quantum simulation of open quantum systems in heavy-ion collisions

> We present a framework to simulate the dynamics of hard probes such as heavy quarks or jets in a hot, strongly-coupled quark-gluon plasma (QGP) on a quantum computer. Hard probes in the QGP can be treated as open quantum systems governed in the Markovian limit by the Lindblad equation. However, due to large computational costs, most current phenomenological calculations of hard probes evolving in the QGP use semiclassical approximations of the quantum evolution. Quantum computation can mitigate these costs, and offers the potential for a fully quantum treatment with exponential speedup over classical techniques. We report a simplified demonstration of our framework on IBM Q quantum devices, and apply the Random Identity Insertion Method (RIIM) to account for CNOT depolarization noise, in addition to measurement error mitigation. Our work demonstrates the feasibility of simulating open quantum systems on current and near-term quantum devices, which is of broad relevance to applications in nuclear physics, quantum information, and other fields.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that simulating the Lindblad evolution of a hard probe (modeled as a two-level open quantum system) in a quark-gluon plasma is feasible on current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, provided error mitigation techniques (readout correction and the Random Identity Insertion Method for CNOT depolarization noise) are applied; the demonstration is limited to a simplified toy model with a single-qubit subsystem and a scalar-field environment, not a full QCD simulation.

## Method

The authors formulate the evolution of a hard probe in a hot QGP as a Markovian Lindblad equation. They implement a quantum algorithm based on the Stinespring dilation theorem, which couples the subsystem qubit to an auxiliary register of qubits and applies a non-unitary evolution operator \(J\) and the subsystem Hamiltonian \(H_S\) in repeated cycles of time step \(\Delta t\). The quantum circuit is compiled into single-qubit and CNOT gates using the qsearch compiler, and the algorithm is run on the IBM Q Vigo device (and validated on Valencia and Santiago devices) for a toy model consisting of a two-level subsystem interacting with a massless scalar field environment. Error mitigation is applied via constrained matrix inversion for readout errors and the Random Identity Insertion Method (RIIM) for CNOT depolarization noise.

## Key numbers

* The toy model uses a subsystem energy splitting \(\Delta E = 1\,T\) (in units of the medium temperature \(T\)).
* The quantum circuit for one cycle has approximately 70 gates, including about 10 CNOT gates.
* The simulation on the IBM Q Vigo device used 49152 shots (runs) per time point, with results averaged over 5 evenly spaced time points.
* The simulator results used 80192 shots per time point for the coupling scan (Fig. 2) and 800192 shots per time point for the device comparison (Fig. 3).
* The number of cycles used was \(N_{\rm cycle}=1\) for the device runs and \(N_{\rm cycle}=100\) for the simulator coupling scan; \(N_{\rm cycle}=3\) was shown in the simulator to yield considerably improved convergence toward the Runge-Kutta solution.

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum simulation to open quantum systems in nuclear physics, specifically for hard probes in heavy-ion collisions, where most current phenomenological calculations use semiclassical Boltzmann or Fokker-Planck (Langevin) equations (e.g., Gossiaux (2008), Schenke (2009), Cao (2016)). Prior quantum simulations of open systems on real devices without error mitigation were reported by Hu (2019). The framework enables future fully quantum treatments of larger subsystems (e.g., multi-parton jet states or multiple heavy quarks) that would challenge classical computation, and may help circumvent the sign problem in real-time lattice QCD calculations.
