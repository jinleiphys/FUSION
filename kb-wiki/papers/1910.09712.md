# LRP2020: The cosmic origin and evolution of the elements

> The origin of many elements of the periodic table remains an unsolved problem. While many nucleosynthetic channels are broadly understood, significant uncertainties remain regarding certain groups of elements such as the intermediate and rapid neutron-capture processes, the p-process, or the origin of odd-Z elements in the most metal-poor stars. Canada has a long tradition of leadership in nuclear astrophysics, dating back to the work of Alastair Cameron in the 1950s. Recent faculty hires have further boosted activity in the field, including transient observation and theory, survey science on galactic nucleosynthesis, and nuclear experiments. This white paper contains a brief overview of recent activity in the community, highlighting strengths in each sub-field, and provides recommendations to improve interdisciplinary collaboration. Sustaining Canadian leadership in the next decade will require, on the observational side, access to transient and non-transient surveys like LSST, SKA, or MSE, support for target-of-opportunity observing in current and future Canadian telescopes, and participation in next-generation X-ray telescopes such as ATHENA. State-of-the-art theoretical predictions will require an ambitious succession plan for the Niagara supercomputer to support large parallel jobs. We propose a funding instrument for postdoctoral training that reflects the interdisciplinary nature of nuclear astrophysics research, and the creation of a national collaborative funding program that allows for joint projects and workshop organization.

## Key claim

The origin of many elements of the periodic table remains an unsolved problem, with significant uncertainties remaining regarding the intermediate and rapid neutron-capture processes, the p-process, and the origin of odd-Z elements in the most metal-poor stars, despite broad understanding of many nucleosynthetic channels.

## Method

This white paper surveys recent Canadian activity in nuclear astrophysics across observational diagnostics (stellar abundances, ISM studies, supernova remnants), multi-dimensional stellar modeling (3D hydrodynamic simulations of stellar convection), explosive transients (neutron star mergers, Type Ia and core-collapse supernovae), and experimental nuclear physics at TRIUMF-ISAC and SNOLAB. The authors then provide recommendations for sustaining Canadian leadership through access to surveys like LSST, SKA, and MSE, support for target-of-opportunity observing, participation in next-generation X-ray telescopes such as ATHENA, a succession plan for the Niagara supercomputer, and new interdisciplinary funding instruments.

## Key numbers

- The 2017 detection of a neutron star merger in gravitational waves (GW170817) confirmed neutron star mergers as sites for the rapid neutron capture process, with optical/IR spectral energy distribution showing shift from blue optical to near infrared within a few days
- The UVic group performed the highest-resolution 3D simulation of core convection in a massive star using the Compute Canada Niagara supercomputer in 2018
- As an absolute minimum, a next generation machine needs to have three times the computing capability by 2023 and nine-fold capability by 2028; the 2019-launched NSF machine Frontera (TACC) has 5.3 times as many nodes as Niagara, with each node about 1.7 times more powerful
- Upcoming experiments with the EMMA recoil mass spectrometer in 2020/21 will constrain neutron-rich $^{137,139,142}$Cs$(n,\gamma)$ and $^{139}$Ba$(n,\gamma)$ reactions

## Context

This white paper extends the 2010 Canadian white paper "Nuclear Astrophysics in Canada" and summarizes work by the Canadian community up to 2019, building on the legacy of Alastair Cameron at Chalk River Laboratory in the 1950s. It complements white papers led by Ruan (E035, transients), Spekkens (E042, SKA), Hlozek (E023, LSST), Venn (E015, Machine Learning), Cote (E045, Gemini), and Hoffman (E048, Colibri). The paper enables future interdisciplinary collaboration between observational astronomy, theoretical astrophysics, and experimental nuclear physics, proposing a Canadian virtual centre modeled on the US NSF Physics Frontiers Center JINA (Joint Institute for Nuclear Astrophysics).
