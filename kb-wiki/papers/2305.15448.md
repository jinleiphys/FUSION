# A post-Gaussian approach to dipole symmetries and interacting fractons

> We use a post-Gaussian variational approach to non-perturbatively study a general class of interacting bosonic quantum field theories with generalized dipole symmetries and fractonic behaviour. We find that while a Gaussian approach allows to carry out a consistent renormalization group (RG) flow analysis of these theories, this only grasps the interaction terms associated to the longitudinal motion of dipoles, which is consistent with previous analysis using large $N$ techniques. Remarkably, our post-Gaussian proposal, by providing a variational improved effective potential, is able to capture the transverse part of the interaction between dipoles in such a way that a non trivial RG flow for this term is obtained and analyzed. Our results suggest that dipole symmetries that manifest due to the strong coupling of dipoles, may robustly emerge at low energies from short distance models without that symmetry.

## Key claim

Using a post-Gaussian variational approach based on nonlinear canonical transformations, the author finds that dipole symmetries may robustly emerge at low energies from short-distance models without that symmetry, and that the transverse part of the dipole-dipole interaction, which a Gaussian or large-\(N\) approach cannot capture, acquires a nontrivial renormalization group flow.

## Method

The author applies the functional Schrödinger picture to a \((D+1)\)-dimensional bosonic quantum field theory with dipole symmetry, using a Gaussian variational wavefunctional to obtain the ground state energy density and the Gaussian effective potential (GEP). A post-Gaussian ansatz is then constructed via nonlinear canonical transformations (NLCT) to compute the connected part of the four-point function (dipole two-point function) and an improved effective potential, from which the \(\beta\)-functions for the longitudinal (\(\lambda_S\)) and transverse (\(\lambda_T\)) dipole-dipole couplings are derived.

## Key numbers

* The Gaussian effective potential yields a renormalized mass defined as \(m_R^2 = m^2 + 2 I_1(0)\), where \(I_1(0)\) is a regularized momentum integral involving \(\bar{V}^{(4)}(\mathbf{k},0)\).
* The renormalized coupling at scale \(M\) is \(\lambda_M = \lambda_B - I_2(M) = \lambda_B [1 - \lambda_B \mathcal{I}_2(M)]\), with \(\lambda_B = 2\gamma^2 \lambda_S\).
* The 1-loop \(\beta\)-function for the coupling is \(\beta(\lambda_M) = 3 \lambda_M^3 \Lambda(M) + \mathcal{O}(\lambda_M^5)\), where \(\Lambda(M) \equiv M^2 \mathcal{I}_3(M)\).
* The Hamiltonian is bounded from below for \(\lambda_T \geq 0\) and \(\lambda_T + D \lambda_S \geq 0\).

## Context

This work extends the large-\(N\) analysis of dipole-symmetric models by Jensen (2022) and Islam (2023), which only captured the longitudinal part of the dipole-dipole interaction. It also builds on the renormalization group studies of Li (2021), who found that dipole symmetry can emerge at low energies from less symmetric UV models, and on the work of Grosvenor (2022), who found that transverse fractonic couplings did not get renormalized in a lattice model. The post-Gaussian approach enables a non-perturbative calculation of the transverse interaction’s RG flow, which was inaccessible to previous Gaussian or large-\(N\) methods, and suggests that dipole symmetries may arise robustly from short-distance models without that symmetry.
