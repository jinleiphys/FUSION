# Evidence for Z=6 `magic number' in neutron-rich carbon isotopes

> The nuclear shell structure, which originates in the nearly independent motion of nucleons in an average potential, provides an important guide for our understanding of nuclear structure and the underlying nuclear forces. Its most remarkable fingerprint is the existence of the so-called `magic numbers' of protons and neutrons associated with extra stability. Although the introduction of a phenomenological spin-orbit (SO) coupling force in 1949 helped explain the nuclear magic numbers, its origins are still open questions. Here, we present experimental evidence for the smallest SO-originated magic number (subshell closure) at the proton number 6 in 13-20C obtained from systematic analysis of point-proton distribution radii, electromagnetic transition rates and atomic masses of light nuclei. Performing ab initio calculations on 14,15C, we show that the observed proton distribution radii and subshell closure can be explained by the state-of-the-art nuclear theory with chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces, which are rooted in the quantum chromodynamics.

## Key claim

The paper presents experimental evidence for a proton subshell closure (magic number) at Z=6 in neutron-rich carbon isotopes 13-20C, based on systematic analysis of point-proton distribution radii, electromagnetic transition rates, and atomic masses. The authors qualify that 12C is excluded from this conclusion due to its non-minimum proton radius, relatively large B(E2) value, and significant many-body correlations such as cluster correlations that compete with the single-particle 1p3/2 subshell closure.

## Method

The authors extracted point-proton distribution radii for 12-19C from charge-changing cross section measurements on 12C targets at both low energy (around 45 MeV per nucleon at RCNP, Osaka University) and high energy (around 900 MeV per nucleon at GSI, Darmstadt), using a global parameter set in Glauber model calculations. They performed ab initio coupled-cluster calculations in the singles-and-doubles approximation with perturbative triples corrections [Lambda-CCSD(T)] on 14,15C using various chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon interactions, and computed ground-state binding energies and proton radii. The systematic analysis combined these new proton radii data with existing nuclear charge radii, electric quadrupole transition rates B(E2) between 2+1 and ground states, and atomic mass data to examine three independent signatures of shell closure.

## Key numbers

- Proton radii for 12-19C are almost constant throughout the isotopic chain, fluctuating by less than 5%
- Proton radii for 17C: 2.43(4) fm; 18C: 2.42(5) fm; 19C: 2.43(4) fm (extracted from charge-changing cross sections)
- B(E2) values smaller than 3 Weisskopf units observed at Z=6 for N=8, 10, 12 and 14 isotones
- Empirical proton subshell gap Delta(3)p(N,Z)-Delta p exceeds 5 MeV at Z=6 for N=7-15
- Without three-nucleon forces, calculated proton radii are about 9-15% smaller (18% for 15C), while ground states are overbound by as much as about 24% (26% for 15C)
- The empirical Delta(3)p for 12C is about 14 MeV, with estimated total proton-proton and proton-neutron pairing energy of about 10.7 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous hints of possible subshell closures in 8He (Otsuka 1987, Skaza 2006), 14C and 14O (Angeli 1999), and theoretical predictions of near-constant proton distributions in neutron-rich carbon isotopes from anti-symmetrized molecular dynamics (Kanada-En'yo 2015), shell model (Fujimoto 2003, 2007), and ab initio no-core shell model (Forssen 2013) calculations. The results enable future studies of the microscopic origins of the spin-orbit coupling force, which has remained an open question since its introduction by Goeppert-Mayer and Jensen in 1949, and provide a testing ground for chiral nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces rooted in quantum chromodynamics.
