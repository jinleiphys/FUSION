# Supernovae, Hypernovae and Color Superconductivity

> We argue that Color Superconductivity (CSC, Cooper pairing in quark matter leading to the breaking of SU(3) color symmetry) may play a role in triggering the explosive endpoint of stellar evolution in massive stars (M > 8 M_{\odot}). We show that the binding energy release in the transition of a sub-core region to the CSC phase can be of the same order of magnitude as the gravitational binding energy release from core collapse. The core temperature during collapse is likely below the critical temperature for CSC, and the transition is first order, proceeding on Fermi timescales when the pressure reaches a critical value of several times nuclear density. We also discuss the implications for hypernova events with total ejecta energy of 10-100 times that of type II supernova.

# Supernovae, Hypernovae and Color Superconductivity

## Key claim
Color Superconductivity (CSC) may trigger the explosive endpoint of stellar evolution in massive stars (M > 8 M⊙) by releasing binding energy comparable to gravitational binding energy from core collapse, but only if the core temperature during collapse is below the CSC critical temperature and the pressure reaches a critical value of several times nuclear density, with the transition proceeding on Fermi timescales.

## Method
The authors combine known results from QCD at high density (gap size Δ ~ 40-120 MeV, critical temperature Tc ~ 0.57Δ, binding energy density E_CSC = (5.8/4π²)Δ²μ²) with the standard astrophysical scenario of iron core collapse in massive stars. They estimate the CSC binding energy release as a fraction of core mass using the ratio (Δ/μ)², and compare this to the gravitational binding energy Eb ~ 3×10⁵³ erg (Mcore/M⊙)² (R/10 km)⁻¹. They argue that the core at bounce reaches densities of 5-10×10¹⁴ g/cm³ and temperatures of 10-20 MeV, which is likely below Tc, and that the first-order transition proceeds by bubble nucleation at strong coupling with no large exponential suppression.

## Key numbers
- Gap size Δ ~ 40-120 MeV at quark chemical potential μ ≈ 400 MeV
- Critical temperature Tc ≈ 0.57Δ (so Tc ~ 23-68 MeV for the given Δ range)
- Binding energy density E_CSC = (5.8/4π²) Δ² μ² at asymptotic density
- Gravitational binding energy release from core collapse: Eb ~ 3×10⁵³ erg (Mcore/M⊙)² (R/10 km)⁻¹
- Ratio of CSC binding energy to quark energy density: (Δ/μ)² between 0.01 and 0.08 (1-8%)
- Total CSC energy release could be a few percent of a solar mass, or ~10⁵² ergs
- Core bounce densities: 5-10×10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Core bounce temperatures: 10-20 MeV
- Hypernova ejecta kinetic energies: 10-100 times larger than ordinary type II supernovae (of order 10⁵²-10⁵³ ergs)
- Maximum stellar mass for hypernova scenario: M < 35 M⊙ (above this the hydrogen envelope is lost and core size decreases)

## Context
This work extends the suggestion by Bombaci and collaborators (2000) that strange matter formation might overcome energetic difficulties in producing type II supernova explosions, but argues that the CSC transition is more likely to be explosive because it is first order with a well-defined latent heat. The paper also addresses the "energy crisis" for gamma ray burst models that invoke neutron star mergers, as proposed by other authors. The results enable future simulations to incorporate CSC phase transitions into core collapse models, potentially resolving the long-standing problem that standard shockwave energy from core bounce is insufficient to produce observed supernova explosions, even with neutrino reheating unless non-spherical phenomena like rotation or convection are included.
