# Light clusters, pasta phases and phase transitions in core-collapse supernova matter

> The pasta phase in core-collapse supernova matter (finite temperatures and fixed proton fractions) is studied within relativistic mean field models. Three different calculations are used for comparison, the Thomas-Fermi (TF), the Coexisting Phases (CP) and the Compressible Liquid Drop (CLD) approximations. The effects of including light clusters in nuclear matter and the densities at which the transitions between pasta configurations and to uniform matter occur are also investigated. The free energy, pressure, entropy and chemical potentials in the range of particle number densities and temperatures expected to cover the pasta region are calculated. Finally, a comparison with a finite temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculation is drawn.

# Light clusters, pasta phases and phase transitions in core-collapse supernova matter

## Key claim

The pasta phase and light clusters lower the free energy and increase the pressure of core-collapse supernova matter, making it more stable than homogeneous matter, but the magnitude of this stabilization depends on the approximation used: the Coexisting Phases method overestimates the effect compared to the more self-consistent Thomas-Fermi and Compressible Liquid Drop approaches, particularly at low densities.

## Method

The authors use three different approximations within relativistic mean field (RMF) models with the FSU parametrization: the Thomas-Fermi (TF) approximation, where surface effects are treated self-consistently; the Coexisting Phases (CP) method, where Gibbs equilibrium conditions are imposed and surface and Coulomb terms are added separately; and the Compressible Liquid Drop (CLD) model, which includes both surface and Coulomb terms in the minimization of total energy. Light clusters (deuterons, tritons, helions, and alpha particles) are included in the CP calculation, and the results are compared for fixed proton fraction y_p = 0.3 at temperatures T = 4 MeV and 8 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density for FSU parametrization: ρ₀ = 0.148 fm⁻³, with binding energy B/A = -16.3 MeV, incompressibility K = 230 MeV, symmetry energy E_sym = 32.6 MeV, and symmetry energy slope L = 60.5 MeV
- Light clusters appear only for densities below approximately ρ ≈ 0.001 fm⁻³ and melt for ρ ≳ 0.001 fm⁻³
- Droplet-to-rod transition occurs at ρ_{d-r} = 0.024 fm⁻³ for T = 4 MeV and at ρ_{d-r} = 0.023 fm⁻³ for T = 8 MeV
- Binding energies used for clusters: B_d = 2.224 MeV (deuteron), B_t = 8.482 MeV (triton), B_h = 7.718 MeV (helion), B_α = 28.296 MeV (alpha particle)

## Context

This work extends previous studies of the pasta phase in core-collapse supernova matter using RMF models (Grill 2014) and compares three different approximation methods to assess their reliability. The results are compared with the statistical model of Raduta and Gulminelli (2010), which found no discontinuities over the whole thermodynamic range, and with the self-consistent Thomas-Fermi calculation of Zhang and Shen (2014), which considered only droplets and bubbles. The study also draws comparison with 3D finite temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock calculations (Pais 2012, Pais 2014), where subtle variations in the low and high density transitions into and out of the pasta phase were found. The work enables improved modeling of neutrino transport in core-collapse supernovae by clarifying which approximation methods are reliable for describing the pasta phase and its effect on the equation of state.
