# Neutrino-less Double Beta Decay and Particle Physics

> We review the particle physics aspects of neutrino-less double beta decay. This process can be mediated by light massive Majorana neutrinos (standard interpretation) or by something else (non-standard interpretations). The physics potential of both interpretations is summarized and the consequences of future measurements or improved limits on the half-life of neutrino-less double beta decay are discussed. We try to cover all proposed alternative realizations of the decay, including light sterile neutrinos, supersymmetric or left-right symmetric theories, Majorons, and other exotic possibilities. Ways to distinguish the mechanisms from one another are discussed. Experimental and nuclear physics aspects are also briefly touched, alternative processes to double beta decay are discussed, and an extensive list of references is provided.

# Neutrino-less Double Beta Decay and Particle Physics

## Key claim
Neutrino-less double beta decay can be mediated by light massive Majorana neutrinos (the standard interpretation) or by other lepton-number-violating physics (non-standard interpretations), and the two cases may be distinguishable through experimental observables; however, even if observed, the process does not uniquely determine the underlying mechanism without additional information from multiple isotopes or other observables.

## Method
The paper reviews the particle physics aspects of neutrino-less double beta decay by surveying all proposed mechanisms: light Majorana neutrino exchange, heavy neutrino exchange, left-right symmetric theories, R-parity violating supersymmetry, Majoron emission, and other exotic possibilities. It compiles experimental half-life limits from current experiments (Heidelberg-Moscow, IGEX, NEMO-3, CUORICINO, DAMA, CANDLES, SOLOTVINO) and tabulates nuclear matrix element calculations from five theoretical approaches (QRPA, Nuclear Shell Model, Interacting Boson Model, Generating Coordinate Method, projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov). The review also discusses how to distinguish mechanisms using angular distributions of emitted electrons, spectral shapes, and comparisons across different isotopes.

## Key numbers
- Current best half-life limit for $^{76}$Ge: $1.9 \times 10^{25}$ yrs (Heidelberg-Moscow experiment), corresponding to effective mass limits of 0.21–0.53 eV
- Current best half-life limit for $^{130}$Te: $2.8 \times 10^{24}$ yrs (CUORICINO), corresponding to effective mass limits of 0.27–0.57 eV
- Phase space factor for $^{76}$Ge: $G = 0.623 \times 10^{-14}$ yrs$^{-1}$ (using $r_0 = 1.2$ fm, $g_A = 1.25$)
- Phase space factor for $^{130}$Te: $G = 4.09 \times 10^{-14}$ yrs$^{-1}$
- Q-value for $^{76}$Ge: 2039.1 keV; for $^{130}$Te: 2530.3 keV
- Nuclear matrix element range for $^{76}$Ge: 2.81 (NSM) to 4.6–7.24 (Tübingen QRPA)
- Nuclear matrix element range for $^{136}$Xe: 1.71–3.53 (Tübingen QRPA) to 4.2 (GCM)
- Estimated 4-loop contribution to neutrino mass from the Schechter-Valle theorem: $\lesssim 10^{-23}$ eV
- Planned sensitivity of GERDA Phase II (40 kg $^{76}$Ge): $2 \times 10^{26}$ yrs half-life
- Planned sensitivity of CUORE (200 kg $^{130}$Te): $6.5 \times 10^{26}$ yrs half-life (for background $10^{-3}$/keV/kg/yr)

## Context
This review extends and systematizes the earlier reviews by Haxton (1984), Doi (1985), and Vergados (2002) on double beta decay, and provides an updated comprehensive survey of particle physics mechanisms beyond the standard light-neutrino interpretation. It enables future experimental programs (GERDA, CUORE, EXO, SuperNEMO, KamLAND-Zen, SNO+) to interpret their results in terms of multiple possible lepton-number-violating scenarios, and highlights the need for multi-isotope measurements to distinguish mechanisms. The paper also connects to the Schechter-Valle theorem (1982) which links any observation of neutrino-less double beta decay to a Majorana neutrino mass, and discusses the claimed observation by part of the Heidelberg-Moscow collaboration (2001) at $2 \times 10^{25}$ yrs, which remains controversial and requires experimental verification with the same isotope.
