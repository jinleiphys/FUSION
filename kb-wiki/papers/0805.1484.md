# HBT interferometry with quantum transport of the interfering pair

> In the late stage of the evolution of a pion system in high-energy heavy-ion collisions when pions undergo multiple scatterings, the quantum transport of the interfering pair of identical pions plays an important role in determining the characteristics of the Hanbury-Brown-Twiss (HBT) interference. We study the quantum transport of the interfering pair using the path-integral method, in which the evolution of the bulk matter is described by relativistic hydrodynamics while the paths of the two interfering pions by test particles following the fluid positions and velocity fields. We investigate in addition the effects of secondary pion sources from particle decays, for nuclear collisions at AGS and RHIC energies. We find that quantum transport of the interfering pair leads to HBT radii close to those for the chemical freeze-out configuration. Particle decays however lead to HBT radii greater than those for the chemical freeze-out configuration. As a consequence, the combined effects give rise to HBT radii between those extracted from the chemical freeze-out configuration and the thermal freeze-out configuration. Proper quantum treatments of the interfering pairs in HBT calculations at the pion multiple scattering stage are important for our understanding of the characteristics of HBT interferometry in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The quantum transport of an interfering pair of identical pions during the multiple-scattering stage between chemical freeze-out and thermal freeze-out leads to HBT radii close to those of the chemical freeze-out configuration, while secondary pions from particle decays increase the HBT radii; the combined effects produce HBT radii between those extracted from the chemical freeze-out and thermal freeze-out configurations.

## Method

The authors model the bulk matter evolution using relativistic hydrodynamics with spherical symmetry, solving conservation equations for energy-momentum, baryon number, and entropy. They treat the quantum transport of the interfering pion pair via a path-integral formalism, where the dominant contribution comes from classical trajectories following the fluid velocity fields. They consider three production scenarios (chemical freeze-out, partial chemical equilibrium with decays, and chemical equilibrium up to thermal freeze-out) for nuclear collisions at AGS and RHIC energies, using appropriate equations of state (mixed hadron gas for AGS, QGP-to-hadron transition for RHIC).

## Key numbers

- AGS initial conditions: temperature 155 MeV, baryon chemical potential 540 MeV, initial energy density 0.56 GeV/fm³
- AGS chemical freeze-out temperature: 124 MeV (0.8 × initial temperature), corresponding chemical potential 565 MeV
- RHIC initial temperature: 250 MeV, initial energy density 4.64 GeV/fm³
- RHIC phase transition temperature: 170 MeV, transition width 0.05 × Tc
- RHIC chemical freeze-out temperature: 153 MeV (0.9 × Tc)
- Parameter Θ in RHIC equation of state: 5
- Grid spacing in hydrodynamic calculations: Δx = 0.04 × initial radius r₀, time step Δt = 0.99 Δx

## Context

This work extends earlier path-integral treatments of HBT interferometry by Wong (2003, 2004, 2005) and Zhang (2004c) that recognized the importance of quantum phase accumulation during multiple scattering. It addresses the long-standing "HBT puzzle" where experimental HBT radii from AGS, SPS, and RHIC show only small changes with energy (Lis 2005, Aggarwal 2000, Bearden 2000, Adler 2002, Star 2001, 2004, 2005), contradicting expectations from thermal freeze-out models. The approach enables future studies of HBT interferometry that properly account for quantum transport of the interfering pair rather than treating the late-stage evolution as classical random collisions.
