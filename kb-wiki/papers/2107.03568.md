# Nuclear fission chain reaction in cooling white dwarf stars

> The first solids that form as a white dwarf (WD) starts to crystallize are expected to be greatly enriched in actinides. Previously [PRL 126, 1311010] we found that these solids might support a nuclear fission chain reaction that could ignite carbon burning and provide a new Type Ia supernova (SN Ia) mechanism involving an {\it isolated} WD. Here we explore this fission mechanism in more detail and calculate the final temperature and density after the chain reaction and discuss a number of open physics questions.

# Nuclear fission chain reaction in cooling white dwarf stars

## Key claim
The first solids that crystallize in a cooling white dwarf may be sufficiently enriched in actinides to support a nuclear fission chain reaction that could ignite carbon burning and produce a Type Ia supernova from an isolated white dwarf, but only if the white dwarf mass is between approximately 0.85 and 1.05 solar masses and the delay time from uranium production to crystallization is short enough that the 235U enrichment remains above 12%.

## Method
The authors calculate the composition of the first solids to crystallize in a cooling white dwarf using a Coulomb plasma model where melting temperature scales with atomic number Z^(5/3). They compute the neutron multiplication factor k_infinity for an infinite system with the predicted actinide-rich composition using neutron cross sections at 1 MeV from the ENDF data set, and determine the critical enrichment of 235U needed for a chain reaction. They then model the fission chain reaction using energy conservation equations, accounting for electron gas heat capacity, thermal conductivity losses, and the temperature-dependent density change, to estimate the final temperature reached and whether it can ignite carbon burning.

## Key numbers
- The melting temperature of pure uranium is 95 times higher than the melting temperature of carbon
- The 235U enrichment must be at least 12% for the system to be critical (k_infinity >= 1)
- The initial 235U enrichment after r-process nucleosynthesis is estimated at f_5^0 ≈ 50%
- The delay time from star formation to uranium crystallization must be less than 2.4 Gyr for f_5 > 0.12 if initial enrichment is 50%, or less than 1.1 Gyr if average enrichment is 25%
- For a 0.837 solar mass white dwarf (main sequence mass 3.50 solar masses), the total delay time is 860 Myr
- For a 0.934 solar mass white dwarf (main sequence mass 4.33 solar masses), the total delay time is 610 Myr
- The critical mass of the actinide crystal is approximately 3 × 10^(-8) g, much smaller than the estimated 5 mg crystal size
- The final temperature if all uranium and thorium fission is T_f = 6.9 × 10^9 K; if only uranium fissions, T_f = 5.2 × 10^9 K
- The density decreases by only about 25% during the fission reaction (from approximately 2 × 10^8 g/cm^3), insufficient to stop the chain reaction
- The fission heating rate (3.5 × 10^11 MeV/s) is approximately 1000 times larger than the heat conduction loss rate (6.0 × 10^8 MeV/s)
- A trigger mass of 5 mg needs to be heated above approximately 5 × 10^9 K for carbon ignition

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous proposal (Horowitz and Caplan, PRL 126, 131101, 2021) that fission in isolated white dwarfs could provide a new Type Ia supernova mechanism, calculating the final temperature and density after the chain reaction in more detail. It builds on prior work on white dwarf crystallization (Van Horn 1968, Tremblay 2019), 22Ne sedimentation (Bildsten 2001, Caplan 2020), and carbon ignition conditions (Timmes and Woosley 1992). The mechanism offers an alternative to the standard binary companion scenarios for Type Ia supernovae (Wang 2012, Hillebrandt 2013) and could potentially contribute to the Hubble constant tension (Riess 2021, Di Valentino 2021) if the relative contribution of fission-ignited supernovae changes with metallicity. Future work needed includes molecular dynamics simulations of the phase diagram for high-Z plasmas, calculations of temperature-dependent fission cross sections for 238U, and hydrodynamic simulations to determine whether fission can ignite carbon burning and produce a supernova.
