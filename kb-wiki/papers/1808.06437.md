# Beta-decay properties of neutron-rich Ca, Ti, and Cr isotopes

> Beta-decay properties of neutron-rich Ca, Ti, and Cr isotopes are studied within a deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation. The underlying mean field is described self-consistently from deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations with pairing correlations. Residual spin-isospin interactions in the particle-hole and particle-particle channels are also included in the formalism. The energy distributions of the Gamow-Teller strength, the beta-decay feedings, the beta-decay half-lives, and the beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities are discussed and compared with other theoretical results, as well as with the available experimental information. The evolution of these nuclear beta-decay properties is investigated in isotopic chains in a search for structural changes. A reliable estimate of the beta-decay properties in this mass region is a valuable information for evaluating decay rates in astrophysical scenarios.

## Key claim

The paper claims that deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) calculations based on Skyrme Hartree-Fock with pairing correlations can reliably reproduce the available experimental half-lives of neutron-rich Ca, Ti, and Cr isotopes, but the agreement is qualified by discrepancies in specific isotopes (e.g., half-life underestimated in ⁵²Ca and overestimated in lighter Ti isotopes) and by the fact that first-forbidden transitions, which are neglected, could reduce half-lives by up to 10% in the heaviest isotopes considered.

## Method

The authors performed deformed Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations with SLy4 interaction and BCS pairing correlations to generate the mean field for even-even ⁵⁰⁻⁶⁴Ca, ⁵²⁻⁶⁶Ti, and ⁵⁶⁻⁷⁰Cr isotopes. They then applied a deformed proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pnQRPA) with separable spin-isospin residual interactions in both particle-hole and particle-particle channels, using coupling strengths χ^{ph}_{GT}=0.15 MeV and κ^{pp}_{GT}=0.03 MeV. Gamow-Teller strength distributions were calculated for the equilibrium shapes (spherical, oblate, or prolate) obtained from potential-energy curves, and half-lives and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities were computed using phase-space factors with a standard quenching factor of 0.77 for the axial-vector coupling constant.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of ⁵²Ca: calculated value underestimates the experimental data from Audi (2012)
- Half-lives of ⁵²Ti and ⁵⁴Ti: calculated values overestimate the experimental data from Audi (2012)
- Half-lives of ⁵⁸Cr, ⁶⁰Cr, and ⁶²Cr: calculated values underestimate the experimental data from Audi (2012)
- For the heaviest Ca, Ti, and Cr isotopes, first-forbidden transitions contribute at most 8% (Mustonen 2016) to 13% (Marketin 2016) of the total decay rate
- In ⁵⁸Ca, the one-neutron separation energy S_n=2.34 MeV lies above the excitation energy 1.68 MeV that receives most of the feeding, resulting in near-zero P_n
- In ⁶⁰Ca, S_n=1.82 MeV lies below the excitation energy 1.92 MeV that receives the feeding, resulting in nearly 100% P_n
- In ⁶²Ti, S_n=3.18 MeV lies above the excitation energy 0.34 MeV that takes all the feeding, giving near-zero P_n
- In ⁶⁴Ti, S_n=1.84 MeV lies below the excitation energy 2.53 MeV that receives most of the feeding

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous deformed pnQRPA studies of neutron-rich isotopes in the mass region 32<Z<46 and 50<N<82 (Sarriguren 2014, 2015) and of rare-earth isotopes (Sarriguren 2017) to the Ca, Ti, and Cr isotopic chains near N=32 and N=40 sub-shell closures. The calculations are compared with other theoretical approaches including the spherical relativistic QRPA of Marketin (2016), the deformed Skyrme pnQRPA of Mustonen (2016), the finite-range droplet model calculations of Moller (2003), and shell-model results of Kumar (2016). The results are intended to provide predictions for beta-decay half-lives and beta-delayed neutron emission probabilities that are needed for the URCA cooling process in white dwarfs and neutron stars (Schatz 2014) and for nuclear reactor safety applications (Valencia 2017), with experimental verification expected from the BRIKEN Collaboration at RIKEN (Dillmann 2018).
