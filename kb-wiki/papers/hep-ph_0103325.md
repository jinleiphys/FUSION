# Quantum algebras in phenomenological description of particle properties

> Quantum and q-deformed algebras find their application not only in mathematical physics and field theoretical context, but also in phenomenology of particle properties. We describe (i) the use of quantum algebras U_q(su_n) corresponding to Lie algebras of the groups SU(n), taken for flavor symmetries of hadrons, in deriving new high-accuracy hadron mass sum rules, and (ii) the use of (multimode) q-oscillator algebras along with q-Bose gas picture in modelling the properties of the intercept λof two-pion (two-kaon) correlations in heavy-ion collisions, as λshows sizable observed deviation from the expected Bose-Einstein type behavior. The deformation parameter q is in case (i) argued and in case (ii) conjectured to be connected with the Cabibbo angle θ_C.

## Key claim

Quantum and q-deformed algebras can be used to derive high-accuracy hadron mass sum rules and to model the intercept of two-pion and two-kaon correlations in heavy-ion collisions, with the deformation parameter q argued or conjectured to be connected to the Cabibbo angle θ_C.

## Method

For hadron mass sum rules, the q-analogs U_q(su_n) of the Lie algebras of SU(n) groups are adopted for flavor symmetries, and mass operators are constructed from generators of the dynamical algebra U_q(u_{n+1}) using Gelfand-Tsetlin basis vectors. For two-particle correlations, a model of ideal q-Bose gas based on algebras of q-deformed oscillators (Biedenharn-Macfarlane or Arik-Coon type) is used to evaluate thermal averages and derive expressions for the intercept λ as a function of temperature, particle mass, pair mean momentum, and the deformation parameter q.

## Context

This work extends the application of quantum algebras from mathematical physics to hadron phenomenology, building on prior work by Gavrilik (1999) and collaborators on q-deformed mass sum rules, and by Anchishkin and Gavrilik (2000) on q-Bose gas models for particle correlations. The approach supersedes the traditional SU(3)-based scheme with singlet mixing by using q-deformation to account for symmetry breaking, and it enables a topological labeling of flavors via knot invariants (Alexander polynomials of torus knots). The connection of the deformation parameter to the Cabibbo angle suggests a possible noncommutative-geometric origin of fermion mixing, pointing toward future research involving noncommutative geometry in extra dimensions.
