# Laboratory electron screening in nuclear resonant reactions

> Both nonresonant and resonance reaction data are subject to laboratory electron screening effects. For nonresonant reactions, such effects are well documented and the measured cross sections can be corrected to find the unscreened ones. Frequently, the procedure and expression to calculate laboratory electron screening factors for nonresonant reactions are also applied to isolated narrow resonances, without much theoretical support or experimental evidence. A simple model is applied to estimate electron screening factors, lengths, and potentials for narrow resonances. The corrections to the measured data result in an enhancement of the unscreened resonance strengths by less than 0.2%, contrary to published narrow-resonance screening correction factors, which predict a reduction of the unscreened strengths by up to 25%. Unless it can be proven otherwise, it is recommended that measured strengths of isolated narrow resonances not be corrected for laboratory electron screening. The prospects of investigating laboratory electron screening effects by measuring almost negligible differences in resonance strengths are not promising. Instead, the difference of the resonance energy for the unscreened and screened situation may be measurable. As an example, the case of the E_cm = 956-keV resonance in the 27Al(p,gamma)28Si reaction is discussed. It is also demonstrated that the claim of a previously reported detection of a resonance near 800 keV in the 176Lu(p,n)176}Hf reaction is incorrect.

## Key claim

The standard practice of applying nonresonant electron screening correction factors to isolated narrow resonances is incorrect because it disregards the shift in resonance energy caused by the screening potential; when this shift is properly accounted for, the unscreened resonance strengths are enhanced by less than 0.2%, contrary to published corrections that predict reductions of up to 25%.

## Method

The author derives the resonance electron screening correction factor by numerically integrating the WKB transmission coefficients for the screened and unscreened Coulomb barriers, using a Yukawa-type screened potential with screening lengths estimated from K-shell radii (from Hartree-Fock calculations) and Thomas-Fermi conduction-electron screening lengths. The constant screening potential inside the nucleus is approximated from the Yukawa potential, and the resonance energy is shifted downward by this potential before computing the transmission coefficient.

## Key numbers

- For six narrow resonances in reactions including 17O(p,α)14N, 18O(p,γ)19F, 22Ne(p,γ)23Na, 25Mg(p,γ)26Al, and 27Al(p,γ)28Si, the screening correction factors fr are unity within 0.2%
- The constant screening potentials Ue inside the compound nucleus range from -0.5 keV to -2.0 keV
- Literature screening "enhancement" factors for these same resonances predict screened strengths exceeding unscreened values by 7% to 25%
- K-shell radii rK range from 0.58 a0 (a0 = 5.29 × 10^4 fm) for carbon to 0.094 a0 for iron
- Thomas-Fermi screening length LTF ≈ 6.10 × 10^4 fm for typical metals (EF ≈ 10 eV, ρ ≈ 10 × 10^22 cm^-3)
- Nuclear radius assumed as Rn = 1.2(A0^1/3 + A1^1/3) fm

## Context

This work extends the discussion of laboratory electron screening from nonresonant reactions (well documented in experiments reviewed by Aliotta and Langanke) to isolated narrow resonances, correcting the approach of Assenbaum (1987) who first suggested applying nonresonant screening factors to resonances. The author shows that the resonance energy shift, previously noted by Zinner (2007) and Cussons for alpha radioactivity and astrophysical plasmas, cancels the screening enhancement when the screening potential is constant, and produces a slight reduction when the potential declines with distance. The work recommends that measured strengths of isolated narrow resonances not be corrected for laboratory electron screening unless proven otherwise, and suggests that measuring the resonance energy shift (rather than strength changes) may be a more promising experimental avenue.
