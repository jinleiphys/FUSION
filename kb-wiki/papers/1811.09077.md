# The nucleon effective mass and its isovector splitting

> Using an energy density functional (EDF) based on the thermodynamic Gibbs-Duhem relation, found equivalent to the standard Skyrme EDF for infinite nuclear matter, it is demonstrated that the parameters of this EDF are not uniquely determined from the fit of the empirical and theoretical data related to nuclear matter. This prevents an unambiguous determination of the nucleon effective mass ($\frac{m_0^*}{m}$) and its isovector splitting ($Δm_0^*$). Complementary information from dipole polarizability of atomic nuclei helps in removing this ambiguity and plausible values of $\frac{m_0^*}{m}$ and $Δm_0^*$ can be arrived at. Presently considered fit data on infinite nuclear matter and dipole polarizability of finite nuclei yield $\frac{m_0^*}{m} =0.68 \pm 0.04 $ and $Δm_0^* =(-0.20 \pm 0.09)δ$. This EDF is consistent with the constraint on the maximum mass of the neutron star.

## Key claim

Fitting only infinite nuclear matter data to the energy density functional yields a very shallow chi-squared minimum, preventing unique determination of the nucleon effective mass and its isovector splitting; adding experimental dipole polarizability data for finite nuclei removes this ambiguity and yields plausible values of m_0^*/m = 0.68 ± 0.04 and Δm_0^* = (−0.20 ± 0.09)δ.

## Method

The authors constructed an energy density functional based on the thermodynamic Gibbs-Duhem relation, equivalent to the standard Skyrme functional for infinite nuclear matter, with seven parameters including k_+ and k_- that govern the effective mass and its isovector splitting. They first fitted the parameters to empirical and theoretical nuclear matter data (pressure of symmetric and pure neutron matter, symmetry energy, and chiral effective field theory results for low-density neutron matter) but found that only a linear combination of k_+ and k_- could be determined. They then used experimental dipole polarizability data for ⁴⁸Ca, ⁶⁸Ni, ¹²⁰Sn, and ²⁰⁸Pb to extract the isovector parameter Θ_V, which provides the complementary linear combination needed to fix k_+ and k_- uniquely.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon effective mass at saturation: m_0^*/m = 0.68 ± 0.04
- Isovector effective mass splitting at saturation: Δm_0^* = (−0.20 ± 0.09)δ
- Isovector effective mass at saturation: m_{v,0}^*/m = 0.78 (+0.05/−0.04)
- Symmetry energy coefficient at saturation: C₂(ρ₀) = 33.94 ± 0.50 MeV
- Density slope of symmetry energy: L₀ = 68.50 ± 3.72 MeV
- Maximum neutron star mass: M_max^NS = 2.06 ± 0.03 M_⊙
- Radius of 1.4 M_⊙ neutron star: R₁.₄ = 12.62 ± 0.57 km
- Saturation density: ρ₀ = 0.1620 ± 0.003 fm⁻³
- Incompressibility: K₀ = 225.23 ± 6.38 MeV
- Saturation energy per nucleon: ε₀ = −15.93 ± 0.20 MeV
- Isovector parameter Θ_V = 145.0 ± 40.0 MeV fm⁵ (average from two choices of E_x)

## Context

This work extends the approach of Mondal (2017) by demonstrating that nuclear matter data alone cannot fix the effective mass parameters, and it resolves the ambiguity by incorporating dipole polarizability data from Birkhan (2017), Rossi (2013), Hashimoto (2015), and Tamii (2011). The resulting negative isovector splitting (−0.20δ) contrasts with positive values from optical model analyses by Li (2015) and from ISGQR studies by Zhang (2016), but agrees with the negative splitting found for the "best-fit" Skyrme EDFs of Brown (2013). The EDF satisfies the neutron star maximum mass constraint from Demorest (2010) and Antoniadis (2013), unlike the recent Skyrme EDF of Zhang (2018) which yields only 1.8 M_⊙.
