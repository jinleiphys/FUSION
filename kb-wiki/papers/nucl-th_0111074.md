# Critical Tsallis exponent in heavy ion reaction

## Key claim

The numerical solution of a nonlocal kinetic equation for heavy ion reactions around Fermi energy yields expansion velocity and density profiles that can be described by a Tsallis exponent of q = 5/3, which the author suggests "might be considered as an indication of a phase transition", the hedge being that this is a tentative interpretation rather than a confirmed observation.

## Method

The author solves a nonlocal quantum kinetic equation derived from the Kadanoff-Baym formalism, which includes gradient terms in the scattering integral and nonlocal corrections given by derivatives of the scattering phase shift. The numerical simulation tracks the radial dependence of expansion velocity and particle density for central heavy ion collisions at various bombarding energies around the Fermi energy, fitting the resulting profiles to power laws.

## Key numbers

- For bombarding energies below or around Fermi energy, the surface velocity exponent is α ∼ 2 (velocity v ∝ r^α) and the density exponent is β ∼ 3 (density n ∝ r^−β).
- For higher bombarding energies of 60 MeV and 90 MeV, the velocity exponent approaches α ∼ 1, consistent with Hubble expansion, with a vanishing density exponent.
- The Tsallis exponent extracted from the fractional derivative Fokker-Planck equation is q = 5/3, corresponding to a fractional derivative order of γ = 2.
- The critical value γ = 2 yields a cubic density profile P₂(x,t) ∝ x^−3 and a quadratic velocity profile.

## Context

This work extends prior findings by Morawetz et al. (2000) that central heavy ion reactions around Fermi energy exhibit a non-Hubblean expansion profile, which was attributed to long-range correlations typical of fluctuations near a phase transition. The author connects these numerical results to nonextensive statistical mechanics as developed by Tsallis (1988) and to fractional derivative Fokker-Planck equations from Barkai, Metzler, and Klafter (2000). The value q = 5/3 is noted as the border between Gaussian and Levy-like fluctuations, as discussed by Prato and Tsallis (1999), which the author interprets as a possible signal of phase transition.
