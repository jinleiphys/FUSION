# $0νββ$ to the first $2^+$ state with two-nucleon mechanism for L-R symmetric model

> We develop the formalism for calculating the decay rate of neutrinoless double beta decay to the $2^+$ excited states within L-R symmetric model. We consider the effects from induced hadronic currents up to NLO. The QRPA method in a spherical basis is adopted for the nuclear many-body calculation and the corresponding nuclear matrix elements are given. Also, the phase space factors are obtained with numerical electron wave functions. Our results suggest that the nuclear matrix elements are nucleus dependent and they are generally smaller than that of the decay to the ground states. And finally, we give a naive analysis of how current experiment data constrains the L-R symmetric model.

## Key claim
The decay rate of neutrinoless double beta decay to the first $2^+$ excited state within the L-R symmetric model may be larger than previously expected, contrary to earlier calculations by Tomoda (1988) which suggested heavy suppression. The nuclear matrix elements are nucleus dependent and generally smaller than those for decay to the ground state, but the authors find their results are orders of magnitude larger than the earlier PHFB calculation, suggesting the decay mode is not as heavily suppressed as previously thought.

## Method
The authors develop the formalism for $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay to the $2^+$ excited state within the L-R symmetric model, including induced hadronic currents up to next-to-leading order (NLO) with vector, axial-vector, pseudo-scalar (from the pion pole), and weak-magnetism contributions. They adopt the quasiparticle random phase approximation (QRPA) method in a spherical basis with realistic nuclear forces (AV-18 and CD-Bonn for short-range correlations) and obtain nuclear matrix elements for eight candidate nuclei. Phase space factors are computed using numerical electron wave functions from the Radial package with a uniform charge distribution, neglecting screening effects.

## Key numbers
- For $^{76}$Ge: phase space factor $G_1 = 6.86 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$ and $G_2 = 4.77 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$; total NME $M_\lambda = 0.398$ and $M_\eta = 0.597$ (with bare $g_A = 1.27$)
- For $^{136}$Xe: phase space factor $G_1 = 35.61 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$ and $G_2 = 26.33 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$; total NME $M_\lambda = 1.527$ and $M_\eta = 0.854$ (with bare $g_A = 1.27$)
- For $^{100}$Mo: phase space factor $G_1 = 134.2 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$ and $G_2 = 111.3 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$; total NME $M_\lambda = 0.284$ and $M_\eta = 0.112$ (with bare $g_A = 1.27$)
- For $^{96}$Zr: phase space factor $G_1 = 140.4 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$ and $G_2 = 117.2 \times 10^{-15} \, \text{y}^{-1}$; total NME $M_\lambda = 0.262$ and $M_\eta = 0.005$ (with bare $g_A = 1.27$)
- Experimental half-life limit for $^{136}$Xe: $t_{1/2}^{\text{limit}} > 2.6 \times 10^{25} \, \text{yr}$, yielding constraint $|\langle \lambda \rangle| < 2.43 \times 10^{-6}$ for the $2^+$ decay mode
- Experimental half-life limit for $^{76}$Ge: $t_{1/2}^{\text{limit}} > 2.1 \times 10^{24} \, \text{yr}$, yielding constraint $|\langle \lambda \rangle| < 2.13 \times 10^{-5}$ for the $2^+$ decay mode

## Context
This work extends the formalism for $0\nu\beta\beta$ decay to excited $2^+$ states within the L-R symmetric model, which was previously calculated only by Tomoda (1988) using the projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (PHFB) approach. The authors' earlier work (Fang and Faessler, 2021) suggested that the PHFB results underestimated the nuclear matrix elements. The present calculation includes pseudo-scalar and weak-magnetism contributions up to NLO, following the treatment of induced hadronic currents advocated by Cirigliano et al. (2017). The results enable experimental constraints on the L-R symmetric model parameters $\langle \lambda \rangle$ and $\langle \eta \rangle$ from current half-life limits, and suggest that comparing decay rates to ground and $2^+$ excited states could help identify the underlying decay mechanism in future experiments.
