# Consistency Examinations of Calculations of Nuclear Matrix Elements of Double-$β$ Decay by QRPA

> The neutrinoless double-$β$ decay is a hypothetical rare nuclear decay, which can be used for determining the neutrino-mass scale. The scheme to use this decay for determining the neutrino-mass scale is one of few limited methods possible to determine that. Nuclear matrix element of this decay is an important input to this method, and this matrix element cannot be determined by experiment. I examine the validity of the transition density used for calculating the nuclear matrix element by comparing the experimental data and my calculated result of the charge-change strength functions of $^{48}$Ca and $^{48}$Ti. The nuclear wave functions are obtained by the quasiparticle random-phase approximation. A new idea is proposed on the transition operator for this strength function, and the data of those nuclei are reproduced well consistently. Reduced half-life of a few nuclei to the neutrinoless double-$β$ decay are shown.

## Key claim
The experimental charge-change strength functions of ⁴⁸Ca and ⁴⁸Ti, which do not satisfy the Gamow-Teller sum rule, can be reproduced consistently by adding an isovector spin monopole term (α r² στ) to the usual Gamow-Teller operator (στ), thereby indirectly confirming the transition density used for neutrinoless double-β decay nuclear matrix element calculations within the quasiparticle random-phase approximation (QRPA). The author notes that the experimental data alone cannot satisfy the sum rule because the β⁺-decay-like contribution was not measured and the tail of the experimental strength function is decreasing.

## Method
The author performed Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the Skyrme SkM* interaction and contact volume-type pairing interactions, followed by proton-neutron QRPA calculations to obtain nuclear wave functions. The charge-change (Gamow-Teller) strength functions for ⁴⁸Ca(p,n)⁴⁸Sc and ⁴⁸Ti(n,p)⁴⁸Sc were calculated and compared to experimental data from Yako et al. (2009). A modified transition operator consisting of the standard Gamow-Teller operator plus an isovector spin monopole term (α r² στ) was introduced, with the constant α determined to match the average height of the experimental strength function in the high-energy region. The reduced half-life R^(0ν)_(1/2) for neutrinoless double-β decay was then calculated for several nuclei.

## Key numbers
- QRPA sum rule for ⁴⁸Ca: S^(QRPA)_(β-) - S^(QRPA)_(β+) = 24.638 - 0.633 = 24.005 (exact sum-rule value is 24)
- QRPA sum rule for ⁴⁸Ti: S^(QRPA)_(β-) - S^(QRPA)_(β+) = 15.257 - 3.268 = 11.989 (exact sum-rule value is 12)
- Measured transition strengths of ⁴⁸Ca sum to 64 ± 9% of the sum-rule value
- Modified transition operator constant α = -0.03 fm⁻² for ⁴⁸Ca → ⁴⁸Sc and α = -0.0253 fm⁻² for ⁴⁸Ti → ⁴⁸Sc
- Effective g_A values used in this work are approximately 0.4 to 0.5
- With ⟨m_ν⟩ = 10 meV, the predicted half-life T^(0ν)_(1/2) for ⁴⁸Ca is 2 × 10²⁹ years

## Context
This work extends the ongoing effort to reduce the factor 2-3 uncertainty in nuclear matrix elements for neutrinoless double-β decay noted by Engel and Menéndez (2017). The author addresses the difficulty that nuclear matrix elements cannot be confirmed experimentally because the effective neutrino mass is unknown. The charge-change strength function comparison method follows the experimental measurements of Yako et al. (2009) for ⁴⁸Ca and ⁴⁸Ti. The calculated reduced half-lives are compared with results from multiple other groups using different methods: shell model (Menéndez et al. 2009, Horoi and Brown 2016, Iwata et al. 2016), QRPA (Šimkovic et al. 2013, Mustonen and Engel 2013, Hyvärinen and Suhonen 2015, Fang et al. 2015), generator-coordinate method (Vaquero et al. 2013, Yao et al. 2015, Jiao et al. 2017), and interacting-boson model (Barea et al. 2015). The work enables further checks of transition densities and improved reliability of nuclear matrix element calculations for determining the neutrino-mass scale from future neutrinoless double-β decay experiments.
