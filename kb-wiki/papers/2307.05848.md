# Empirical radius formulas for canonical neutron stars from bidirectionally selecting EOS features in extended Bayesian analyses of observational data

> Given the significant advancement in Bayesian inference of nuclear Equation of State (EOS) from gravitational wave and X-ray observations of neutron stars (NSs), especially since GW170817, is there a data-driven and robust empirical formula for the radius $R_{1.4}$ of canonical NSs in terms of the characteristic EOS parameters (features)? What is the single most important but currently poorly known EOS parameter for determining the $R_{1.4}$? We study these questions by extending the traditional Bayesian analysis which normally ends at presenting the marginalized posterior probability distribution functions (PDFs) of individual EOS parameters and their correlations (or sometimes only the Pearson correlation coefficients which are only reliably useful when the variables are linearly correlated while they are actually often not). Using three regression model-building methodologies: bidirectional step-wise feature selection, Least Absolute Shrinkage Selection Operator (LASSO) regression, and neural network regression on a large set of posterior EOSs and the corresponding $R_{1.4}$ values inferred from earlier comprehensive Bayesian analyses of NS observational data, we systematically and rigorously develop the most probable $R_{1.4}$ formulas with varying statistical accuracy and technical complexity. The most important EOS parameters for determining $R_{1.4}$ are found consistently in each of the feature/model selection processes to be (in order of decreasing importance): curvature $K_{sym}$, slope $L$, skewness $J_{sym}$ of nuclear symmetry energy, skewness $J_{0}$, incompressibility $K_{0}$ of symmetric nuclear matter, and the magnitude $E_{sym} (ρ_0)$ of symmetry energy at the saturation density $ρ_0$ of nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The paper derives data-driven empirical formulas for the radius \(R_{1.4}\) of canonical neutron stars (mass \(\approx 1.4 M_\odot\)) in terms of nuclear Equation of State (EOS) parameters, finding that the curvature \(K_{sym}\) of the symmetry energy is the single most important parameter for determining \(R_{1.4}\), followed by the slope \(L\), skewness \(J_{sym}\), skewness \(J_0\), incompressibility \(K_0\), and magnitude \(E_{sym}(\rho_0)\), a ranking that holds consistently across three regression methodologies but is conditional on the minimum six-parameter meta-model EOS used and the specific set of astrophysical observations employed as constraints.

## Method

The authors used 68,000 posterior EOS parameter sets and their corresponding \(R_{1.4}\) values from earlier Bayesian analyses of neutron star observations (GW170817, quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries, and NICER data for PSR J0030+0451). They applied three regression model-building methodologies, bidirectional step-wise feature selection, Least Absolute Shrinkage Selection Operator (LASSO) regression, and neural network regression, on a set of features derived from six EOS parameters (\(E_{sym}, L, K_0, K_{sym}, J_0, J_{sym}\)) including polynomial terms up to degree six, cubed roots, reciprocals, and logarithms, after filtering features with Pearson correlation coefficients above 0.85.

## Key numbers

* The posterior mean radius of canonical neutron stars is \(R_{1.4} = 12.10 \pm 0.38\) km (mean and standard deviation from the Bayesian analysis).
* The posterior means and standard deviations of the six EOS parameters are: \(K_{sym} = -158.97 \pm 80.45\), \(L = 54.13 \pm 13.63\), \(J_{sym} = 473.01 \pm 247.72\), \(K_0 = 239.95 \pm 11.52\), \(J_0 = -163.01 \pm 69.07\), and \(E_{sym} = 31.87 \pm 1.84\) (all in MeV except \(K_{sym}, L, K_0, J_0, J_{sym}\) in MeV and \(E_{sym}\) in MeV).
* The Pearson correlation coefficient between \(K_{sym}\) and \(R_{1.4}\) is the highest among all EOS parameters, but exceeds that of \(L\) by less than 0.01.
* The four observational radius constraints used were: \(R_{1.4}=11.9\pm 1.4\) km from LIGO/VIRGO (GW170817), \(R_{1.4}=10.8^{+2.1}_{-1.6}\) km from De et al. (2018), \(R_{1.4}=11.7^{+1.1}_{-1.1}\) km from quiescent low-mass X-ray binaries, and \(R=13.02^{+1.24}_{-1.06}\) km (mass \(1.44^{+0.15}_{-0.14} M_\odot\)) or \(R=12.71^{+1.83}_{-1.85}\) km (mass \(1.34\pm0.24 M_\odot\)) from NICER for PSR J0030+0451.

## Context

This work extends the traditional Bayesian inference approach that typically presents only marginalized posterior PDFs and Pearson correlation coefficients, which the authors note can be misleading when variables are nonlinearly correlated. It builds on earlier Bayesian analyses by Xie et al. (2019, 2020) and Li et al. (2021) that provided the posterior EOS ensembles, and contrasts with forward-modeling approaches that vary one parameter at a time. The resulting empirical formulas enable quick estimation of \(R_{1.4}\) from new EOS parameter values without solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, and the ranking of parameter importance informs future experimental and observational priorities for constraining the nuclear EOS.
