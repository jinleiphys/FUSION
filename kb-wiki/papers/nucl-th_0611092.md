# A new class of quantum bound states: diprotons in extreme magnetic fields

> This paper considers the possibility that two charged particles with an attractive short-ranged potential between them which is not strong enough to form bound states in free space, may bind in uniform magnetic fields. It is shown that in the formal limit where Coulomb repulsion is negligible (q -> 0 and B_0 -> \infty with q B_0 fixed where q is the charge and B_0 the field strength) there always exists a bound state for a system of two identical charged particles in a constant magnetic field, provided that there is a short-range uniformly attractive potential between them. Moreover, it is shown that in this limit {\it any} potential with an attractive s-wave scattering length will posses bound states provided that the range of the potential is much smaller than the characteristic magnetic length, r_0 = (\frac{q B_0}{4})^{-1/2}. For this case, the binding is computed numerically. We estimate the size of the magnetic field needed to approximately reach a regime where the formal limit considered becomes a good approximation to the dynamics. These numerical estimates indicate that two protons in an extremely strong magnetic field such as might be found in a magnetar will bind to form a diproton.

## Key claim

Two identical charged particles with a short-range attractive potential that is too weak to bind them in free space will always form a bound state in a uniform magnetic field, provided one works in the formal limit where Coulomb repulsion is negligible (charge q goes to zero and field strength B_0 goes to infinity with q B_0 fixed) and the range of the potential is much smaller than the characteristic magnetic length. The authors estimate that two protons in the extremely strong magnetic fields found in magnetars (approximately 10^12 T) may bind to form a diproton, though this requires the magnetic field to be strong enough to suppress Coulomb repulsion while still satisfying the weak-field condition that the magnetic length exceeds the potential range.

## Method

The authors prove a general theorem using a variational argument: for a uniformly attractive potential in the limit where Coulomb repulsion is negligible, the relative-motion Hamiltonian separates into a two-dimensional harmonic oscillator in the transverse plane plus a one-dimensional effective potential along the magnetic field direction, and the one-dimensional system must have a bound state. They then derive an analytic expression for the binding energy in the weak-field limit where the magnetic length r_0 is much larger than both the potential range R and the scattering length a, obtaining E_b = 2 (a/r_0)^2 omega. For the more general case of unnatural scattering lengths (a much larger than R, as in nucleon-nucleon scattering), they numerically diagonalize the Hamiltonian in a truncated three-dimensional harmonic oscillator basis with a zero-range boundary condition at r=0, computing the binding energy as a function of the dimensionless ratio a/r_0.

## Key numbers

- For the infinite scattering length limit (a approaching infinity), the binding energy is E_b = F_infinity * omega with F_infinity approximately 0.60486, where omega = q B_0 / (4 mu) is the ground-state Landau level spacing for the relative motion.
- In the weak-field limit where a is much smaller than r_0, the binding energy is E_b = 2 (a/r_0)^2 omega.
- The characteristic magnetic length is r_0 = (q B_0 / 4)^{-1/2} in natural units (hbar = c = 1).
- The magnetic fields needed for diproton formation are estimated to be approximately 10^12 T, as found in magnetars.
- The relative error from finite-range corrections scales as R^2 / r_0^2, and the relative error from neglecting Coulomb repulsion scales as q^2 mu r_0 log(R/r_0), with the optimal error at the level of (alpha mu R)^{2/3} where alpha is the fine-structure constant.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of bound states in magnetic fields beyond the well-known result that attractive potentials in one or two dimensions always bind, showing that three-dimensional systems can also bind in the presence of a magnetic field even when the potential is too weak to bind in free space. The analysis builds on effective field theory methods developed for nuclear systems with unnatural scattering lengths, as in the nucleon-nucleon case where the scattering length is approximately 20 fm while the range is about 2 fm (de Swart 1995). The results enable future investigation of whether diprotons formed in magnetar-strength magnetic fields have observable astrophysical consequences, and open the question of whether such bound states exist for other nuclear systems in extreme magnetic environments.
