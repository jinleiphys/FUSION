# Toward nuclear physics from lattice QCD on quantum computers

> One of the ultimate missions of lattice QCD is to simulate atomic nuclei from the first principle of the strong interaction. This is an extremely hard task for the current computational technology, but might be reachable in coming quantum computing era. In this paper, we discuss the computational complexities of classical and quantum simulations of lattice QCD. It is shown that the quantum simulation scales better as a function of a nucleon number and thus will outperform for large nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum simulation of lattice QCD for nuclear many-body systems scales exponentially better with nucleon number \(A\) than classical simulation, but this advantage depends on the assumption that the central motion excitation can be excluded or that the spatial volume scales proportionally to \(A\); otherwise the quantum scaling can be worse in volume dependence.

## Method

The authors compare the computational cost of classical lattice QCD (path integral formalism with Hybrid Monte Carlo, conjugate gradient solvers, and contraction algorithms) against quantum simulation (Hamiltonian formalism with the quantum adiabatic algorithm) for computing the ground-state mass of a nucleus. They estimate scaling with spatial volume \(V\) and nucleon number \(A\) for both approaches, then demonstrate the quantum adiabatic algorithm on a noiseless simulator using a toy model of nonrelativistic fermions with contact interactions on a one-dimensional lattice.

## Key numbers

- Classical simulation total cost scales as \(O(V^{5/4} e^{c_1 A}) + O(V e^{c_2 A} A^3)\), where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants depending on computational details.
- Quantum simulation total cost scales as \(O(V^{10/3} A^2)\) for the quantum adiabatic algorithm, which is at least exponentially better in \(A\) than the classical scaling.
- If central motion excitation can be excluded, quantum scaling improves to \(O(A) + O(V^2)\).
- Example central motion energy gap: \(\Delta_{\text{cen}} \simeq 0.4\) MeV for \(V=128^3\), \(a^{-1}=2\) GeV, and \(A=12\).
- Toy model demonstration: convergence requires \(S/a \simeq 15\) for \(A=2\) and \(S/a \simeq 40\) for \(A=4\), with the ratio matching the squared energy gap ratio of approximately 0.4.
- Required qubits for a realistic lattice: \(D_{\text{qubit}} = 119,537,664\) for \(V=128^3\) with \(\log_2 d = 11\).

## Context

This work extends the discussion of quantum advantage in lattice QCD from sign-problem systems (real-time dynamics, dense quark matter) to the nuclear many-body problem, which is computationally prohibitive on classical computers due to factorial scaling of contractions and exponential signal-to-noise problems (Lepage 1989). The paper builds on prior lattice QCD results for few-body systems: two-baryon interactions near the physical point (HAL QCD Collaboration 2018, 2019; Lyu 2021) and three-nucleon interactions at heavy quark masses (Doi 2011; Aoki 2020), as well as two-baryon interactions from the finite volume method (Francis 2018; Horz 2020; Amarasinghe 2021). The quantum adiabatic algorithm approach enables future calculations of ground-state masses, state vectors for nuclear reactions, and real-time simulations of nucleus-nucleus scattering and fusion/fission, though practical implementation requires solving the huge qubit requirement and quantum error correction.
