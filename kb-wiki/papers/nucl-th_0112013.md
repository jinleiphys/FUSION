# A soft mode in a finite Fermi-system: anharmonic effects near the instability point

> We consider a finite Fermi-system where the residual interactions create a soft mode of the excitation spectrum. Because of the large vibrational amplitude, the standard random phase approximation does not work in this situation. We develop a regular method for constructing the anharmonic potential and illustrate the application of the formalism by a simple model.

# A soft mode in a finite Fermi-system: anharmonic effects near the instability point

## Key claim

The standard random phase approximation (RPA) fails when a vibrational frequency approaches zero in a finite Fermi-system because the vibrational amplitude grows as 1/sqrt(omega) and anharmonic effects dominate; the authors develop a regular method for constructing the anharmonic potential that restores stability at the RPA instability point, illustrated by the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model.

## Method

The authors map the exact operator equations of motion for the generalized density matrix onto a collective subspace spanned by collective coordinate and momentum operators, truncating the intermediate states to those within a collective band. They expand the collective Hamiltonian in powers of these operators and derive a hierarchy of equations linking the expansion coefficients to microscopic matrix elements, keeping terms up to fourth order in the anharmonic potential. The formalism is applied to the two-level Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick model, where the collective dynamics are exactly decoupled and the Holstein-Primakoff transformation maps the SU(2) algebra onto bosonic operators.

## Key numbers

- The RPA frequency is given by omega_RPA^2 = epsilon^2 - V^2 (4J^2 - 1/4), where epsilon is the single-particle level spacing, V is the interaction strength, and J is the total quasispin (J = N/2 for N particles in the largest representation).
- The instability point occurs at V^2 approximately equal to epsilon^2/(4J^2), emerging first for the largest J with the greatest degree of collectivity.
- For the case N=20 particles (J=10) with epsilon=1, the quartic potential parameter Lambda^(40) grows strongly at the RPA instability point (shown in Fig. 1, upper panel).
- At the instability point with J >> 1 and epsilon >> V, the approximate collective Hamiltonian becomes H = pi^2/2 + 4 epsilon^4 V alpha^4, a pure quartic oscillator.
- The effective potential U(alpha) = (1/2) omega^2 alpha^2 + (1/4) Lambda^(40) alpha^4 evolves from harmonic (V=0) through pure quartic (V=0.05) to a "Mexican hat" shape with two minima (V=0.08).

## Context

This work extends the RPA-based approach to collective vibrations in nuclei, as developed by Belyaev (1959), Belyaev and Zelevinsky (1962), and Soloviev (1992), by addressing the regime where the vibrational frequency approaches zero and harmonic approximations break down. The method differs from boson expansion techniques (Klein and Marshalek, 1991) and the generator coordinate method by providing a fully quantum variational approach that avoids requantization of a classical Hamiltonian. The results enable treatment of soft vibrational bands in spherical nuclei near the point of deformation onset, complementing phenomenological models such as the gamma-unstable quartic potential (Jean and Wilets, 1956) and the interacting boson model (Iachello and Arima, 1987).
