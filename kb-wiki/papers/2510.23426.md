# Anti-Flatness and Non-Local Magic in Two-Particle Scattering Processes

> Non-local magic and anti-flatness provide a measure of the quantum complexity in the wavefunction of a physical system. Supported by entanglement, they cannot be removed by local unitary operations, thus providing basis-independent measures, and sufficiently large values underpin the need for quantum computers in order to perform precise simulations of the system at scale. Towards a better understanding of the quantum-complexity generation by fundamental interactions, the building blocks of many-body systems, we consider non-local magic and anti-flatness in two-particle scattering processes, specifically focusing on low-energy nucleon-nucleon scattering and high-energy Moller scattering. We find that the non-local magic induced in both interactions is four times the anti-flatness (which is found to be true for any two-qubit wavefunction), and verify the relation between the Clifford-averaged anti-flatness and total magic. For these processes, the anti-flatness is a more experimentally accessible quantity as it can be determined from one of the final-state particles, and does not require spin correlations. While the MOLLER experiment at the Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility does not include final-state spin measurements, the results presented here may add motivation to consider their future inclusion.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that for any two-qubit wavefunction, the non-local magic is exactly four times the anti-flatness, and this relation holds for both low-energy nucleon-nucleon scattering and high-energy Moller scattering; however, the fraction of total magic that is non-local depends on the specific initial state and interaction, with some initial states producing final states where all magic is non-local while others produce significant local magic that can be removed by local unitary operations.

## Method

The authors computed the non-local magic and anti-flatness for two-particle scattering processes by mapping the spin degrees of freedom onto two qubits, then applying the S-matrix (for low-energy S-wave nucleon-nucleon scattering using Nijmegen phase shifts) or the T-matrix amplitude (for high-energy Moller scattering at leading order in perturbation theory) to initial tensor-product stabilizer states. They performed minimization over local unitary transformations on each qubit to extract the non-local magic, and compared these results to the anti-flatness computed from the reduced density matrix of one particle, verifying the relation for each group of initial states.

## Key numbers

- For low-energy nucleon-nucleon scattering, the non-local magic power averaged over tensor-product stabilizer states is given by (1/48)(11 + 5 cos(4 Delta-delta)) sin^2(2 Delta-delta), where Delta-delta is the difference between spin-singlet and spin-triplet phase shifts
- For nucleon-nucleon scattering at lab momenta below approximately 150 MeV, only about one third of the generated magic is non-local
- For Group-2 initial states in nucleon-nucleon scattering, the non-local magic equals the total magic: M_lin^(NL) = M_lin = (1/4) sin^2(4 Delta-delta)
- For Group-3 initial states in nucleon-nucleon scattering, the non-local magic is exactly one third of the total magic: M_lin^(NL) = (1/3) M_lin = (1/32)(7 + cos(4 Delta-delta)) sin^2(2 Delta-delta)
- For high-energy Moller scattering, the proportionality constant relating Clifford-averaged anti-flatness to total linear magic is c(4,2) = 1/10
- The MOLLER experiment aims to measure the parity-violating asymmetry to a precision of 0.7 parts per billion

## Context

This work extends previous studies of entanglement in Standard Model scattering processes by Cervera-Lierta (2019) and Beane (2018) to the domain of non-local magic and anti-flatness, building on the framework for non-local magic introduced by Cao (2024) and the relation between anti-flatness and magic established by Tirrito (2023). The results connect to prior work on magic in nucleon-nucleon scattering by Robin (2024) and in Moller scattering by Liu (2025), and suggest that anti-flatness measurements on a single final-state particle could serve as a practical experimental probe of quantum complexity generation, potentially motivating the addition of spin measurements to experiments like MOLLER at Jefferson Lab.
