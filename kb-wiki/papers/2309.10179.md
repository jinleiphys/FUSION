# Quantum benefit of the quantum equation of motion for the strongly coupled many-body problem

> We investigate the quantum equation of motion (qEOM), a hybrid quantum-classical algorithm for computing excitation properties of a fermionic many-body system, with a particular emphasis on the strong-coupling regime. The method is designed as a stepping stone towards building more accurate solutions for strongly coupled fermionic systems, such as medium-heavy nuclei, using quantum algorithms to surpass the current barrier in classical computation. Approximations of increasing accuracy to the exact solution of the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick Hamiltonian with $N=8$ particles are studied on digital simulators and IBM quantum devices. Improved accuracy is achieved by applying operators of growing complexity to generate excitations above the correlated ground state, which is determined by the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE). We demonstrate explicitly that the qEOM exhibits a quantum benefit due to the independence of the number of required quantum measurements from the configuration complexity. Post-processing examination shows that quantum device errors are amplified by increasing configuration complexity and coupling strength. A detailed error analysis is presented, and error mitigation based on zero noise extrapolation is implemented.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the quantum equation of motion (qEOM) algorithm exhibits a "quantum benefit" because the number of quantum measurements required to evaluate the generalized eigenvalue equation (GEE) matrix elements is independent of the configuration complexity \(\alpha_m\) of the excitation operator; for a fixed particle number, one can increase \(\alpha_m\) and achieve better accuracy without performing additional measurements on the quantum computer. This benefit is demonstrated for the Lipkin-Meshkov-Glick (LMG) Hamiltonian with \(N=8\) particles, but the authors note that quantum device errors are amplified by increasing configuration complexity and coupling strength, and that error mitigation based on zero noise extrapolation is required.

## Method

The authors apply the qEOM algorithm to the LMG Hamiltonian for \(N=8\) particles, using a hierarchy of excitation operators with configuration complexity \(\alpha_m = 1, 2, 3\) (i.e., including up to three particle-hole pairs). The correlated ground state is first obtained via the variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) on digital simulators and IBM quantum devices. The matrix elements \(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B}, \mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}\) of the generalized eigenvalue equation are then computed from expectation values of Pauli-string operators measured on the quantum device, and the eigenvalue equation is solved classically to obtain excitation energies and transition amplitudes.

## Key numbers

* The LMG Hamiltonian is studied with \(N=8\) particles, and the configuration complexity is varied as \(\alpha_m = \{1, 2, 3\}\).
* The number of required quantum measurements scales at most quadratically with the number of particles for the Lipkin model, i.e., \(\mathcal{O}(N^2)\), and is independent of \(\alpha_m\).
* The qEOM results for \(\alpha_m=3\) after error mitigation show "reasonable agreement" with the exact solution in the strong-coupling regime; the paper does not report a specific numerical accuracy (e.g., energy error in MeV) for this agreement.

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior study (Hlatshwayo 2022) of the qEOM for the LMG Hamiltonian, now focusing on the strong-coupling regime and the quantum benefit of measurement-count independence. The qEOM algorithm was originally developed for quantum chemistry (Ollitrault 2020) and is here adapted to nuclear-physics-like fermionic systems; the paper connects this approach to the response-theory framework of Litvinova and Schuck (2019, 2023) and to the broader goal of building a hierarchy of approximations for medium-heavy nuclei. The results suggest that qEOM could enable calculations with configuration complexity beyond the current classical limit of \(\alpha_m = 3\) for such systems, provided quantum device noise can be sufficiently mitigated.
