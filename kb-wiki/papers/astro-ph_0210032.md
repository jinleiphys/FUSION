# Fusion cycles in stars and stellar neutrinos

> Starting from the early works by Weizsaecker and Bethe about fusion cycles and energy conversion in stars, a brief survey of thermonuclear processes in stars leading to contemporary research problems in this field is given. Special emphasis is put on the physics of stellar and, in particular, solar neutrinos which is at the frontline of current investigations.

# Fusion Cycles in Stars and Stellar Neutrinos

## Key claim
This survey argues that the historical development of fusion cycle theory by Weizsäcker and Bethe, combined with modern solar neutrino measurements, has confirmed that pp-fusion is the main source of solar energy, but the actual value of the neutrino mass remains an open issue with only an upper limit of 2.2 eV/c² from tritium beta decay and a model-dependent lower limit of 0.05–0.2 eV/c² from neutrinoless double beta decay.

## Method
The paper reviews thermonuclear reaction mechanisms in stars, focusing on the proton-proton chain and the CNO-cycle as the two main hydrogen-burning pathways. It then examines the production, detection, and flux measurements of solar neutrinos from these reactions, comparing experimental results from radiochemical detectors (Davis chlorine experiment, Gallex, Sage) and water-Cherenkov detectors (Kamiokande, SuperKamiokande, SNO) with predictions from standard solar models.

## Key numbers
- Solar central temperature: Tc = 1.57 × 10⁷ K from numerical solutions by Bahcall et al., with central pressure Pc = 2.34 × 10¹⁶ Pa and central density ρc = 1.53 × 10⁵ kg/m³
- Mass loss in hydrogen-to-helium fusion: 0.71 per cent of initial masses, or 0.029 u, converted to about 26.2 MeV per four hydrogen nuclei
- Total solar mass loss rate: 4.25 × 10⁹ kg/s from the known solar luminosity
- S-factor for the ³He(³He,2p)⁴He reaction at the Gamow peak: 5.3 MeVb, with LUNA data extending down to 21 keV
- Davis chlorine experiment result: (2.3 ± 0.3) SNU, versus predicted total of about 8 SNU from standard solar models
- Gallex result: (69.7 +7.8/−8.1) SNU, confirmed by Sage at (69 ± 12) SNU, versus predicted range of 120–140 SNU
- SuperKamiokande boron-8 flux ratio observed/predicted: 0.47 ± 0.02
- SNO neutral current total flux: (5.09 +0.64/−0.61) × 10⁶ neutrinos per (cm²·s), in agreement with solar model value of (5.05 +1.01/−0.81)
- Atmospheric neutrino oscillation parameters: Δm²_atm ≈ 2.5 × 10⁻³ eV² with maximal mixing sin²(2θ_atm) ≈ 1
- Upper limit on antielectron-neutrino mass from tritium beta decay: 2.2 eV/c²
- Lower limit from neutrinoless double beta decay: 0.05–0.2 eV/c² (valid only if neutrino is its own antiparticle)

## Context
This review extends the foundational works of Weizsäcker (1937–1938) and Bethe (1938–1939) on the CNO-cycle and pp-chain, and connects them to the modern resolution of the solar neutrino deficit through oscillation measurements by SuperKamiokande (1998) and SNO (2001–2002). The paper enables future precision measurements of low-energy reaction rates (e.g., LUNA collaboration) and upcoming detectors like Borexino and LENS that will determine neutrino mixing parameters and test the Large Mixing Angle solution.
