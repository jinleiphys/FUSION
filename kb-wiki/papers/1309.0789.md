# Instabilities near the QCD phase transition in the holographic models

> The paper discusses phenomena close to the critical QCD temperature, using the holographic model. One issue studied is the overcooled high-T phase, in which we calculate quasi normal sound modes. We do not find instabilities associated with other first order phase transitions, but nevertheless observe drastic changes in sound propagation/dissipation. The rest of the paper considers a cluster of the high-T phase in the UV in coexistence with the low-T phase, in a simplified ansatz in which the wall separating them is positioned only in the holographic coordinate. This allows to find the force on the wall and classical motion of the cluster. When classical motion is forbidden, we evaluate tunneling probability through the remaining barrier.

## Key claim

The paper investigates instabilities near the QCD critical temperature using holographic models, finding no classical density instability in the overcooled high-T phase (the imaginary part of quasi-normal modes remains negative), but observing drastic changes in sound propagation and dissipation. For QGP clusters at the end of the inhomogeneous phase, the authors evaluate both classical motion and quantum tunneling of the domain wall separating the two phases, depending on whether classical collapse is allowed or forbidden.

## Method

The authors study two distinct regimes: (i) the "beginning" stage of overcooled homogeneous high-T phase, where they calculate quasi-normal sound modes from linearized Einstein and dilaton equations in a holographic dilaton-gravity model; (ii) the "near-end" stage where a dilute gas of high-T phase clusters coexists with low-T phase, modeled by a simplified ansatz with a membrane positioned only in the holographic coordinate. For the cluster dynamics, they derive an effective action for the membrane motion by patching a blackhole solution in the UV and a thermal gas solution in the IR, with continuity conditions on the dilaton and metric components, then evaluate the tunneling probability when classical motion is forbidden.

## Key numbers

- The critical QCD temperature is approximately \(T_c \approx 170\, \text{MeV}\)
- The freeze-out temperature in heavy ion collisions is in the range \(T_f = 120-150\, \text{MeV}\)
- The multiplicity per cluster from two-particle rapidity correlations is in the range 5-10
- The 5D gravitational constant is given by \(\frac{1}{16\pi G_5} = \frac{N_c^2-1}{45\pi^2 l^3}\), which evaluates to \(2 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{GeV}^3\)
- Quasi-normal modes are computed for super-cooled blackhole phases corresponding to \(\af_h = 1.2\) and \(\af_h = 1.6\), with the latter corresponding to the lowest temperature \(T_{min}\)

## Context

This work extends the holographic study of QCD phase transitions by examining instabilities and cluster dynamics near \(T_c\), building on the equilibrium coexistence treatment of Aharony, Minwalla and Wiseman (2005) who formulated the flat domain wall solution and evaluated surface tension. The paper is motivated by the search for observable consequences of QCD critical behavior in heavy ion collisions, following earlier suggestions by Shuryak (1997) to detect enhanced event-by-event critical fluctuations and by Stephanov (1998) regarding the hypothetical second-order QCD critical point. The results enable future study of sound emission from collapsing QGP clusters as proposed by Shuryak (2013), and the observed stoppage of sound propagation in the supercooled blackhole phase may be related to mode crossing phenomena similar to those found by Landsteiner in the diffusion mode of normal QGP at finite momentum.
