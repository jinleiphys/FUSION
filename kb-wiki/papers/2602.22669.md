# Symmetry-imposed correlation in nuclear level statistics: The spin distribution

> Despite long-term research, the origin of spin cutoff in the angular-momentum (spin) distribution of nuclear level densities remains incompletely elucidated. We demonstrate that this problem can be traced back to Bethe's assumption that nucleons in finite Fermi systems are independent random variables. By constructing a statistical ensemble that enforces rotational invariance through angular-momentum coupling, we obtain an analytical expression for the spin cutoff parameter, which includes a previously unidentified finite-population correction. Our results show that, even in the absence of interactions, nuclear many-body states exhibit non-negligible correlations arising from fermionic antisymmetry and angular-momentum coupling. From this perspective, spin cutoff may be interpreted as a quantitative measure of correlation imposed by symmetry in nuclear level statistics.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the spin cutoff parameter in nuclear level densities arises from correlations imposed by rotational symmetry and fermionic antisymmetry, not from random coupling of independent nucleons as assumed by Bethe. The authors show that even without interactions, nuclear many-body states exhibit non-negligible correlations, and they derive an analytical expression for the spin cutoff parameter that includes a previously unidentified finite-population correction (FPC) term.

## Method

The authors construct a statistical ensemble that enforces rotational invariance through angular-momentum coupling, using a model space of multiple spherical \(j\)-shells with single-particle energies \(\varepsilon_i\) and angular momenta \(j_i\). They treat nucleons in different \(j\)-shells as independent random variables, but apply a finite population correction (sampling without replacement) for nucleons within the same \(j\)-shell to account for Pauli exclusion. The variance of the total magnetic quantum number \(M\) is computed by summing variances from each \(j\)-shell, using the Fermi-Dirac distribution to determine average occupation numbers \(n_i\) as functions of temperature \(T\) and chemical potential \(\mu\).

## Key numbers

- The variance of \(m\) for a single nucleon in a \(j_i\)-shell is \(\mathrm{Var}(m) = \frac{1}{3}j_i(j_i+1)\).
- The variance of \(M_i\) for \(n_i\) nucleons in a single \(j_i\)-shell is \(\mathrm{Var}(M_i) = \frac{1}{6}n_i(j_i+1)(2j_i+1-n_i)\), which includes the finite population correction factor \(\frac{2j_i+1-n_i}{2j_i}\).
- The total variance of \(M\) is \(\mathrm{Var}(M) = \frac{1}{6}\sum_i \frac{e^{\beta(\varepsilon_i - \mu)}}{[e^{\beta(\varepsilon_i-\mu)}+1]^2} (j_i+1)(2j_i+1)^2\).
- The squared spin-cutoff parameter \(\sigma_{tot}^2\) is the sum of proton and neutron contributions: \(\sigma_{tot}^2 = \sigma_n^2 + \sigma_p^2\).

## Context

This work extends Bethe's original 1936 derivation and Ericson's 1960 formula for the spin distribution \(P(J)\) in nuclear level densities, which assumed nucleons are independent and identically distributed random variables. The authors show that the standard Bethe-Ericson formula lacks a finite population correction that becomes significant when occupation numbers are comparable to shell degeneracies, particularly at low temperatures. The method enables calculation of the spin cutoff parameter \(\sigma\) using only single-particle energies as input, without requiring external fitting parameters, and can be applied to nuclei such as \({}^{48,51,52}\)Cr as demonstrated in the Supplemental Materials. This approach contradicts the common rigid-rotor hypothesis of Landau and provides a framework for understanding spin distribution in terms of correlations imposed by symmetry rather than collective rotation.
