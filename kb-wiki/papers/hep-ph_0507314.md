# Bottomonium Production at RHIC and LHC

> Properties of bottomonia (Upsilon, chi_b and Upsilon') in the Quark-Gluon Plasma (QGP) are investigated by assessing inelastic reaction rates and their interplay with open-bottom states (b-quarks or B-mesons) and color-screening. The latter leads to vanishing quarkonium binding energies at sufficiently high temperatures (close to the dissolution point), which, in particular, renders standard gluo-dissociation, g+Upsilon -> b + b-bar, inefficient due to a substantial reduction in final-state phase space. This problem is overcome by invoking a "quasifree" destruction mechanism, g,q,q-bar + Upsilon -> g,q,q-bar + b + b-bar, as previously introduced for charmonia. The pertinent reaction rates are implemented into a kinetic theory framework to evaluate the time evolution of bottomonia in heavy-ion reactions at RHIC and LHC within an expanding fireball model. While bottom quarks are assumed to be exclusively produced in primordial nucleon-nucleon collisions, their thermal relaxation times in the QGP, which importantly figure into Upsilon-formation rates, are estimated according to a recent Fokker-Planck treatment. Predictions for the centrality dependence of Upsilon production are given for upcoming experiments at RHIC and LHC. At both energies, Upsilon suppression turns out to be the prevalent effect.

## Key claim

The paper argues that standard gluo-dissociation of bottomonium becomes inefficient at high temperatures due to reduced binding energies from color screening, and that a "quasifree" destruction mechanism involving scattering off thermal partons must be invoked instead. The authors predict that Upsilon suppression will be the prevalent effect at both RHIC and LHC, with regeneration playing a negligible role when in-medium binding energies are properly accounted for.

## Method

The authors computed inelastic reaction rates for bottomonium states (Upsilon, chi_b, Upsilon') using both gluo-dissociation and quasifree scattering cross sections, implementing temperature-dependent binding energies from a Debye-screened Cornell potential solved via the Schrodinger equation. These rates were incorporated into a kinetic rate-equation framework coupled with an expanding thermal fireball model to simulate the time evolution of bottomonia in central Au-Au collisions at RHIC and Pb-Pb collisions at LHC, with bottom-quark thermal relaxation times estimated from a Fokker-Planck treatment.

## Key numbers

- Upsilon production cross section in pp collisions: 3.5 nb at RHIC (sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV) and 152 nb at LHC (sqrt(s_NN)=5.5 TeV), the latter including a factor 0.8 for shadowing corrections
- bbbar production cross section: 2.0 microbarn at RHIC and 160 microbarn at LHC (with 0.8 shadowing factor)
- Upsilon vacuum binding energy: approximately 1.1 GeV relative to the BBbar threshold (2m_B)
- Upsilon binding energy at T_c: reduced to about 550 MeV, reaching zero at approximately 4.3 T_c
- chi_b and Upsilon' binding energies at T_c: 150-200 MeV, reaching zero at about 1.8 T_c
- QGP phase duration: about 3 fm/c at RHIC with initial temperature T_0 close to 400 MeV; about 8 fm/c at LHC with T_0 approximately 1 GeV
- Upsilon suppression at RHIC: nearly 50% of the initial number (after nuclear absorption) in the quasifree scenario with in-medium binding energies
- Nuclear absorption cross section: 3.1 mb at RHIC and 4.6 mb at LHC, assumed identical for all bottomonium states

## Context

This work extends the quasifree dissociation approach previously introduced for charmonia by Grandchamp and Rapp (2001-2004) to the bottomonium sector, addressing limitations of earlier analyses that focused solely on suppression mechanisms using either Debye-screening pictures (Gavai and Vogt, 1997; Pal et al., 2000; Goncalves et al., 2001) or gluo-dissociation processes (Peskin, 1979; Bhanot and Peskin, 1979; Yellin, 2003). The study enables quantitative predictions for Upsilon production at RHIC and LHC experiments, with the key finding that bottomonium suppression rather than regeneration dominates at both collider energies, in contrast to the charmonium case where recombination may be significant.
