# Hybrid Oscillator-Qubit Quantum Processors: Simulating Fermions, Bosons, and Gauge Fields

> We develop a hybrid oscillator-qubit processor framework for quantum simulation of strongly correlated fermions and bosons that avoids the boson-to-qubit mapping overhead encountered in qubit hardware. This framework gives exact decompositions of particle interactions such as density-density terms and gauge-invariant hopping, as well as approximate methods based on the Baker-Campbell Hausdorff formulas including the magnetic field term for the $U(1)$ quantum link model in $(2+1)$D. We use this framework to show how to simulate dynamics using Trotterisation, perform ancilla-free partial error detection using Gauss's law, measure non-local observables, estimate ground state energies using a oscillator-qubit variational quantum eigensolver as well as quantum signal processing, and we numerically study the influence of hardware errors in circuit QED experiments. To show the advantages over all-qubit hardware, we perform an end-to-end comparison of the gate complexity for the gauge-invariant hopping term and find an improvement of the asymptotic scaling with the boson number cutoff $S$ from $\mathcal{O}(\log(S)^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ in our framework as well as, for bosonic matter, a constant factor improvement of better than $10^4$. We also find an improvement from $\mathcal{O}(\log(S))$ to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ for the $U(1)$ magnetic field term. While our work focusses on an implementation in superconducting hardware, our framework can also be used in trapped ion, and neutral atom hardware. This work establishes digital quantum simulation with hybrid oscillator-qubit hardware as a viable and advantageous method for the study of qubit-boson models in materials science, chemistry, and high-energy physics.

# Hybrid Oscillator-Qubit Quantum Processors: Simulating Fermions, Bosons, and Gauge Fields

## Key claim

The authors claim that hybrid oscillator-qubit quantum processors can simulate strongly correlated fermions and bosons with asymptotically better gate complexity than all-qubit hardware, specifically achieving constant-depth (O(1)) implementations of gauge-invariant hopping and magnetic field terms compared to O(log(S)^2) and O(log(S)) respectively on qubit-only devices, where S is the boson number cutoff. However, this advantage relies on the availability of native oscillator-qubit interactions and the ability to implement three-wave mixing Hamiltonians via driven sideband transitions, which require careful experimental calibration and may introduce hardware-specific errors that the authors only partially address through ancilla-free error detection using Gauss's law.

## Method

The authors develop a framework for digital quantum simulation on hybrid processors where bosonic modes are encoded in oscillator Fock states and fermionic degrees of freedom are encoded in qubits, avoiding the boson-to-qubit mapping overhead of all-qubit approaches. They provide exact decompositions for density-density interactions and gauge-invariant hopping terms, and approximate methods based on Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formulas for the U(1) magnetic field term in (2+1)D quantum link models. The framework is analyzed through Trotterized time evolution, ancilla-free partial error detection using Gauss's law constraints, variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) ground state estimation, and quantum signal processing (QSP) for ground state preparation, with numerical studies of hardware errors in circuit QED architectures.

## Key numbers

- Gauge-invariant hopping term: asymptotic scaling improvement from O(log(S)^2) on all-qubit hardware to O(1) in the hybrid framework, where S is the boson number cutoff.
- For bosonic matter: constant factor improvement of better than 10^4 compared to all-qubit implementations.
- U(1) magnetic field term: asymptotic scaling improvement from O(log(S)) on all-qubit hardware to O(1) in the hybrid framework.
- Beamsplitter operation on qubit-only hardware: total two-qubit gate count of N_BS = 4(2673 n^2 + 1160 n - 34) CNOT gates, where n is the register size representing the Fock state cutoff.
- Controlled beamsplitter on qubit-only hardware: N_CBS = 12(2673 n^2 + 1160 n - 34) CNOT gates.
- QSP ground state preparation: polynomial degree O((1/Delta) log(1/epsilon)) required for fidelity 1-epsilon, where Delta is the spectral gap of the Hamiltonian.

## Context

This work extends the hybrid oscillator-qubit approach previously developed for bosonic simulations (Crane et al., prior work) and applies it to lattice gauge theories including the Z2-Higgs model and the Schwinger model, building on fermionic simulation techniques such as Jordan-Wigner mapping (Jordan and Wigner, 1993) and fermionic SWAP networks (Kivlichan et al., 2018). The framework enables digital quantum simulation of qubit-boson models relevant to materials science, chemistry, and high-energy physics, with proposed implementations in superconducting hardware as well as trapped ion and neutral atom platforms. The authors note that while their ancilla-free implementation of bosonic on-site interactions using Gauss's law can propagate photon-loss and qubit dephasing errors as effective gate errors, these can be partially mitigated by post-selection on states satisfying Gauss's law, though at the cost of an exponential increase in the number of shots with system size.
