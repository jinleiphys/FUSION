# Non-equilibrium dynamics in quantum field theory at high density: the tsunami

> The dynamics of a dense relativistic quantum fluid out of thermodynamic equilibrium is studied in the framework of the Phi^4 scalar field theory in the large N limit. The time evolution of a particle distribution in momentum space (the tsunami) is computed. The effective mass felt by the particles in such a high density medium equals the tree level mass plus the expectation value of the squared field. The case of negative tree level squared mass is particularly interesting. In such case dynamical symmetry restoration as well as dynamical symmetry breaking can happen. Furthermore, the symmetry may stay broken with vanishing asymptotic squared mass showing the presence of out of equilibrium Goldstone bosons. We study these phenomena and identify the set of initial conditions that lead to each case. We compute the equation of state which turns to depend on the initial state. Although the system does not thermalize, the equation of state for asymptotically broken symmetry is of radiation type. We compute the correlation functions at equal times. The two point correlator for late times is the sum of different terms. One stems from the initial particle distribution. Another term accounts for the out of equilibrium Goldstone bosons created by spinodal unstabilities when the symmetry is asymptotically broken.Both terms are of the order of the inverse of the coupling for distances where causal signals can connect the two points. The contribution of the out of equilibrium Goldstones exhibits scaling behaviour in a generalized sense.

## Key claim

For a dense relativistic quantum fluid out of thermodynamic equilibrium in the large N limit of Phi^4 scalar field theory, the system does not thermalize even at late times, and the equation of state depends explicitly on the initial state; however, when the symmetry is asymptotically broken, the equation of state takes the radiation form P = E/3 despite the lack of thermalization, and out-of-equilibrium Goldstone bosons appear with vanishing asymptotic squared mass.

## Method

The authors studied the time evolution of a scalar O(N) field with quartic self-interaction in the large N limit, using Gaussian wave functionals as initial conditions and a "tsunami" particle distribution peaked at wavenumber k0 with high density ~ m_R^3/lambda. They solved the self-consistent mode equations and the order parameter equation analytically for narrow initial momentum distributions using elliptic functions, and compared these with full numerical solutions of the renormalized evolution equations.

## Key numbers

- Energy density threshold for dynamical symmetry breaking in case II: E > (|m_R|^2 / lambda_R) * k0^2 for initially unbroken symmetry
- Energy density threshold for asymptotically broken symmetry in both cases I and II: E > (|m_R|^4 / lambda_R) + 2 * (|m_R|^2 / lambda_R) * k0^2
- Energy interval for dynamical symmetry breaking in case II: (|m_R|^2 / lambda_R) * k0^2 < E < (|m_R|^4 / lambda_R) + 2 * (|m_R|^2 / lambda_R) * k0^2
- Asymptotic equation of state for broken symmetry: P = E/3 (radiation type)
- Group velocity v_g = 1 for dynamically broken symmetry (where asymptotic effective mass M_infinity = 0)
- The two-point correlator pulse term: C_p(|x|,t) = (1/(lambda |x|)) * F(|x| - 2*v_g*t - c)
- The two-point correlator Goldstone term: C_s(|x|,t) = (K/(lambda |x|)) * Q(|x|/(2t))

## Context

This work extends the non-perturbative treatment of out-of-equilibrium scalar field dynamics previously developed by the same authors and collaborators (Cao and de Vega, earlier works; references nos1, nos2, nos3, FRW) from vacuum initial conditions to high-density "tsunami" initial conditions. It contrasts with the parametric resonance shut-off mechanism found in vacuum initial conditions (where backreaction stops the resonance) by showing that for high-density initial states the resonance is shut off by decoherence instead. The results enable the study of non-equilibrium Goldstone bosons and scaling behavior in dense relativistic systems relevant to heavy ion collisions, dense stars, and early universe cosmology.
