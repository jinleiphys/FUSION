# Energy flow along the medium-induced parton cascade

> We discuss the dynamics of parton cascades that develop in dense QCD matter, and contrast their properties with those of similar cascades of gluon radiation in vacuum. We argue that such cascades belong to two distinct classes that are characterized respectively by an increasing or a constant (or decreasing) branching rate along the cascade. In the former class, of which the BDMPS, medium-induced, cascade constitutes a typical example, it takes a finite time to transport a finite amount of energy to very soft quanta, while this time is essentially infinite in the latter case, to which the DGLAP cascade belongs. The medium induced cascade is accompanied by a constant flow of energy towards arbitrary soft modes, leading eventually to the accumulation of the initial energy of the leading particle at zero energy. It also exhibits scaling properties akin to wave turbulence. These properties do not show up in the cascade that develops in vacuum. There, the energy accumulates in the spectrum at smaller and smaller energy as the cascade develops, but the energy never flows all the way down to zero energy. Our analysis suggests that the way the energy is shared among the offsprings of a splitting gluon has little impact on the qualitative properties of the cascades, provided the kernel that governs the splittings is not too singular.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the fundamental difference between medium-induced (BDMPS) and vacuum (DGLAP) parton cascades lies in the energy dependence of the branching time: in the BDMPS cascade the branching time decreases along the cascade as \(t_\ast(\omega)\sim\sqrt{\omega}\), causing branchings to accelerate and allowing energy to reach arbitrarily soft modes in a finite time, whereas in the DGLAP cascade the branching time is constant, making the time to transport energy to zero energy essentially infinite.

## Method

The authors analyze the inclusive gluon distribution \(D(x,t)\) (where \(x=\omega/E\) is the energy fraction) using a rate equation derived from the BDMPS-Z theory for medium-induced cascades and a simplified version of the DGLAP evolution equation for vacuum cascades. They isolate the branching rate \(1/t_\ast(x)\) from the splitting kernel \(\mathcal{K}(z)\) to compare the two cascade classes, and construct analytic solutions for the BDMPS cascade using a simplified kernel \(\mathcal{K}(z)\approx 1/[z^{3/2}(1-z)^{3/2}]\) and for the DGLAP cascade via Mellin transform techniques.

## Key numbers

* The stopping time for the BDMPS cascade is \(t_\ast = (1/\bar\alpha)\sqrt{E/\hat q}\), where \(\bar\alpha\equiv \alpha_s N_c/\pi\) and \(\hat q\) is the jet-quenching parameter; this is the time at which most of the initial energy has been radiated into soft gluons.
* The branching time for a gluon of energy \(\omega\) in the BDMPS cascade scales as \(\tau_{\rm br}(\omega)=\sqrt{\omega/\hat q}\), decreasing as \(\omega\) decreases.
* The analytic solution for the ideal BDMPS cascade is \(D(x,\tau)=[\tau/\sqrt{x}(1-x)^{3/2}]\exp(-\pi\tau^2/(1-x))\) with \(\tau\equiv t/t_\ast\); at small \(x\) this reduces to \(D(x,\tau)\approx (\tau/\sqrt{x})e^{-\pi\tau^2}\).
* The total energy stored in the spectrum for the BDMPS cascade is \(\lim_{x_0\to 0}\mathcal{E}(x_0,\tau)=e^{-\pi\tau^2}\), and the flux of energy reaching \(x=0\) is \(\mathcal{F}(0,\tau)=2\pi t e^{-\pi\tau^2}\).
* For the DGLAP cascade, the asymptotic behavior at small \(x\) is \(D(x,t)\approx (1/x)^{2\sqrt{t/\ln(1/x)}}\) for \(\ln(1/x)\gg t\), showing no factorization of time and energy dependence.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of medium-induced parton cascades from the BDMPS-Z theory (Baier 1996, Zakharov 1996) and contrasts them with vacuum cascades described by DGLAP evolution (Gribov 1972, Altarelli 1977, Dokshitzer 1977). The paper builds on earlier studies of the BDMPS cascade by Blaizot (2013) and the concept of stopping time from Arnold (2009). The results enable a deeper understanding of jet modification in heavy ion collisions, particularly the mechanism for energy transport to large angles that contrasts with the strong angular ordering of vacuum QCD cascades, as previously discussed by Mehtar-Tani (2014) and Kurkela (2014).
