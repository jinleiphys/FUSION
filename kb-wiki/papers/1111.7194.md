# Future directions in kaonic atom physics

> Recent progress and open problems in kaonic atom physics are presented. A connection between phenomenological deep potentials and the underlying $K^-N$ interaction is established as well as the need for a theory for multinucleon absorption of kaons. $K^-$ absorption at rest to specific $Λ$ hypernuclei states is briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The long-standing "deep vs. shallow" puzzle in kaonic atom potentials has been resolved by using in-medium sub-threshold K-N scattering amplitudes, but the resulting optical potentials still require a phenomenological term dominated by a ρ² dependence, indicating that multi-nucleon absorption processes not yet included in the theory are essential for agreement with experiment.

## Method

Global optical-model fits to K⁻-atom strong-interaction shift and width data across the periodic table were performed using chiral-motivated K⁻N scattering amplitudes evaluated at sub-threshold energies determined self-consistently from the kaon binding energy and nuclear density. The in-medium amplitudes were computed using a Fermi gas model for the nucleus, with the Mandelstam parameter √s evaluated including nucleon binding energies, kaon kinetic energy, and the optical potential itself, requiring iterative self-consistency at each radial point for each nucleus.

## Key numbers

- Deep real optical potentials from global fits: Re V_K⁻(ρ₀) ∼ -(150–200) MeV, compared to shallow potentials of Re V_K⁻(ρ₀) ∼ -(40–60) MeV from threshold chiral amplitudes with self-energy corrections.
- The real potential based on TW1 amplitudes for K⁻Ni is 80 MeV deep, compared to 40–50 MeV deep in the threshold approach of Ramos and Oset (2000).
- Adding a four-parameter phenomenological term to the sub-threshold model (for 65 data points) reduced χ² per point from 10 to 2.
- Predicted shifts and widths for the 2p state in ³He: shift 0.3 eV, width 2.1 eV; for ⁴He: shift −0.2 eV, width 1.6 eV.
- Current predictions for absolute formation rates in stopped kaon absorption experiments underestimate experimental results by as much as a factor of six.

## Context

This work extends the sub-threshold approach of Cieplý et al. (2011) that resolved the deep vs. shallow puzzle by reproducing the empirical density-dependent characterization of Friedman, Gal, and Batty (1994). It contrasts with the threshold chiral amplitudes of Ramos and Oset (2000) that gave shallower potentials. The next steps enabled are the implementation of microscopic multi-nucleon absorption terms to replace the phenomenological ρ² term, and studies of the absolute scale of hypernuclear formation rates from FINUDA experiments (Agnello et al., 2011).
