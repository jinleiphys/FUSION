# Ultra-Dense Neutron Star Matter, Strange Quark Stars, and the Nuclear Equation of State

> With central densities way above the density of atomic nuclei, neutron stars contain matter in one of the densest forms found in the universe. Depending of the density reached in the cores of neutron stars, they may contain stable phases of exotic matter found nowhere else in space. This article gives a brief overview of the phases of ultra-dense matter predicted to exist deep inside neutron stars and discusses the equation of state associated with such matter.

## Key claim

This paper reviews the possible phases of ultra-dense matter predicted to exist deep inside neutron stars, but it does not present a single new experimental or observational result; instead it surveys theoretical models and existing observational constraints, noting that the actual composition depends on whether densities in neutron star cores reach the thresholds for exotic phases such as hyperons, meson condensates, or quark deconfinement.

## Method

The paper surveys theoretical frameworks for the nuclear equation of state (EoS) including relativistic mean-field (RMF), density-dependent relativistic Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (DD-RBHF), and Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) models, comparing their predicted pressure versus energy density curves. It reviews observational mass measurements of neutron stars from radio pulsars and X-ray binaries, and applies the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations generalized to electrically charged stars to compute mass-radius relations for polytropic models with charge distributions proportional to energy density.

## Key numbers

- Maximum neutron star mass from the Hulse-Taylor pulsar: \(M = 1.44\, M_\odot\)
- Average neutron star mass from radio pulsar observations: \(1.350 \pm 0.004\, M_\odot\) (Thorsett and Chakrabarty, 1999)
- Observed high neutron star masses: \(M_{\rm J0751+1807} = 2.1 \pm 0.2\, M_\odot\), \(M_{\rm 4U\,1636+536} = 2.0 \pm 0.1\, M_\odot\), \(M_{\rm Vela\, X-1} = 1.86 \pm 0.16\, M_\odot\), \(M_{\rm Cyg\, X-2} = 1.78 \pm 0.23\, M_\odot\)
- Theoretical upper limit on neutron star mass from causality: \(3.2\, M_\odot\) (Rhoades and Ruffini, 1974); increases to about \(5\, M_\odot\) if causality is abandoned
- Nuclear matter saturation properties: binding energy \(E/A = -16.0\) MeV, effective nucleon mass \(m^*_{\rm N} = 0.79\, m_{\rm N}\), incompressibility \(K \simeq 240\) MeV, symmetry energy \(a_{\rm s} = 32.5\) MeV, saturation density \(n_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Threshold for \(\Lambda\) hyperon appearance: neutron Fermi momentum \(\kFn \sim 3\) fm\(^{-1}\), corresponding to density \(\sim 2 n_0\)
- \(K^-\) meson effective mass at \(\sim 3 n_0\): \(m^*_{K^-} \simeq 200\) MeV, with relation \(m^*_{K^-} / m_{K^-} \simeq 1 - 0.2 n / n_0\) for neutron-rich matter
- Maximum mass of nucleon stars with \(K^-\) condensation: \(\sim 1.5\, M_\odot\) (Thorsson et al., 1994)
- Onset density for H-dibaryon matter: above \(\sim 4 n_0\)
- Bag constants for strange quark matter: \(B = 57\) MeV/fm\(^3\) (\(\bag = 145\) MeV) gives \(E/A = 829\) MeV; \(B = 85\) MeV/fm\(^3\) (\(\bag = 160\) MeV) gives \(E/A = 915\) MeV
- Neutron drip density at base of strange star crust: \(\sim 4 \times 10^{11}\) g/cm\(^3\)
- Electric field at strange star surface: up to \(10^{19}\) V/cm

## Context

This review extends the line of work on neutron star interiors and the nuclear equation of state summarized in textbooks by Glendenning (1997) and Weber (1999), and in reviews by Heiselberg and Pandharipande (2000), Lattimer and Prakash (2001), and Sedrakian (2006). It discusses the strange matter hypothesis originally proposed by Alcock, Farhi, and Olinto (1986, 1988) and Madsen (1988, 1998), and the possibility of color superconducting quark matter phases (Rajagopal and Wilczek, 2001; Alford, 2001). The paper enables future work on observational signatures distinguishing neutron stars from strange stars, particularly through mass-radius measurements, cooling curves, and the possibility of identifying strange dwarf candidates among compact white dwarfs (Mathews et al., 2004).
