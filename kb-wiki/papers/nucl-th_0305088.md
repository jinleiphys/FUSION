# Eigenstates of the time-dependent density-matrix theory

> An extended time-dependent Hartree-Fock theory, known as the time-dependent density-matrix theory (TDDM), is solved as a time-independent eigenvalue problem for low-lying $2^+$ states in $^{24}$O to understand the foundation of the rather successful time-dependent approach. It is found that the calculated strength distribution of the $2^+$ states has physically reasonable behavior and that the strength function is practically positive definite though the non-hermitian hamiltonian matrix obtained from TDDM does not guarantee it. A relation to an extended RPA theory with hermiticity is also investigated. It is found that the density-matrix formalism is a good approximation to the hermitian extended RPA theory.

# Eigenstates of the time-dependent density-matrix theory

## Key claim

The small-amplitude limit of time-dependent density-matrix theory (STDDM), when solved as a time-independent eigenvalue problem for low-lying 2+ states in 24O, yields physically reasonable strength distributions that are practically positive definite, despite the fact that the non-Hermitian Hamiltonian matrix does not guarantee positivity, and STDDM is shown to be a good approximation to a more elaborate extended RPA (ERPA) theory that has Hermiticity.

## Method

The authors linearize the time-dependent density-matrix theory (TDDM) equations of motion for the one-body density matrix ρ and the correlated part C of the two-body density matrix, then Fourier-transform to obtain a time-independent eigenvalue problem of the form (a c; b d)(x; X) = ω (x; X). They solve this eigenvalue problem numerically for low-lying 2+ states in 24O using a truncated single-particle space consisting of the neutron 1d5/2, 2s1/2, and 1d3/2 orbitals, with a Skyrme III mean field and a density-dependent delta-function residual interaction. The strength function is computed from the eigenstates using a formula analogous to that used in the time-dependent approach, with an artificial damping factor Γ = 0.5 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The dimension of the Hamiltonian matrix is about 650 × 650 when using the neutron 1d5/2, 2s1/2, and 1d3/2 single-particle states.
- The time constant τ for the adiabatic switching-on of the residual interaction is 300 fm/c.
- The time step used to solve the TDDM equations is 0.75 fm/c.
- The density-dependent delta interaction parameters are ρ0 = 0.16 fm−3 and v0 = −1000 MeV fm3.
- The imaginary parts of eigenvalues for some 2+ states are less than 0.08 MeV.
- The artificial damping factor Γ used in the strength function is 0.5 MeV.
- The mesh size for single-particle wavefunctions is 0.1 fm, confined to a sphere of radius 12 fm.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous time-dependent density-matrix theory (TDDM) calculations for low-lying states in unstable oxygen isotopes (Tohyama 2001, 2002) by formulating and solving the small-amplitude limit as a time-independent eigenvalue problem. It clarifies the foundation of the time-dependent approach and compares STDDM with extended RPA (ERPA) theories that have Hermiticity (Tohyama and Schuck). The results are consistent with quasiparticle RPA calculations by Matsuo and by Khan and collaborators, which used larger single-particle spaces including continuum states. The work enables future applications of STDDM to small-amplitude collective motions with a clearer theoretical justification, and establishes that the non-Hermiticity of STDDM is mild enough to produce physically meaningful strength functions.
