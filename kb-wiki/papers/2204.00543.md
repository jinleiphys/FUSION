# Complementarity of experimental and lattice QCD data on pion parton distributions

> We extract pion parton distribution functions (PDFs) in a Monte Carlo global QCD analysis of experimental data together with reduced Ioffe time pseudo-distributions and matrix elements of current-current correlators generated from lattice QCD. By including both experimental and lattice QCD data, our analysis rigorously quantifies both the uncertainties of the pion PDFs and systematic effects intrinsic to the lattice QCD observables. The reduced Ioffe time pseudo-distributions significantly decrease the uncertainties on the PDFs, while the current-current correlators are limited by the systematic effects associated with the lattice. Consistent with recent phenomenological determinations, the behavior of the valence quark distribution of the pion at large momentum fraction is found to be $\sim (1-x)^{ β_{\rm eff}}$ with $β_{\rm eff} \approx 1.0-1.2$.

## Key claim

The simultaneous global QCD analysis of experimental Drell-Yan and leading-neutron data together with lattice QCD reduced Ioffe time pseudo-distributions and current-current correlator matrix elements yields a pion valence quark distribution at large momentum fraction behaving as (1-x)^β_eff with β_eff ≈ 1.0–1.2, consistent with recent phenomenological determinations, but the current-current correlator data are limited by systematic effects associated with the lattice and do not significantly reduce PDF uncertainties.

## Method

The authors performed a Monte Carlo global QCD analysis within the JAM framework, fitting experimental Drell-Yan cross sections from E615 and NA10 (at beam energies 194, 252, and 286 GeV) and leading-neutron electroproduction structure functions from H1 and ZEUS at HERA, together with lattice QCD data on reduced Ioffe time pseudo-distributions (Rp-ITDs) from the HadStruc collaboration on ensembles with a ≈ 0.127 fm and m_π ≈ 413 MeV, and current-current correlator matrix elements from the same collaboration on four ensembles with lattice spacings 0.094–0.127 fm and pion masses 278–415 MeV. The lattice observables were factorized to light-cone PDFs using the short-distance factorization formalism with matching coefficients at next-to-leading order, while the Drell-Yan hard coefficients were computed at both NLO and NLO+NLL (using the double Mellin method for threshold resummation). Systematic corrections for higher-twist, lattice spacing, and finite-volume effects were parametrized and fitted simultaneously with the PDF parameters.

## Key numbers

- Valence quark large-x exponent β_eff ≈ 1.0–1.2 from the combined analysis, consistent with recent phenomenological determinations
- Drell-Yan data included: 61 points from E615 at 252 GeV, 36 points from NA10 at 194 GeV, and 20 points from NA10 at 286 GeV, after kinematic cuts 4.16 < Q < 7.68 GeV and 0 < x_F < 0.9
- Leading-neutron data: 58 points from H1 and 50 points from ZEUS, with cut x_L > 0.8 (x_L_bar < 0.2)
- Lattice Rp-ITD data from two ensembles: a127m413 (L=24a, a=0.127(2) fm, m_π=413(4) MeV) and a127m413L (L=32a, a=0.127(2) fm, m_π=413(5) MeV), with momenta p_i = i × (2π/La) for i=1,2,3 and separations z up to 6a–8a
- Lattice current-current correlator data from four ensembles: a127m413, a127m413L, a94m358 (a=0.094(1) fm, m_π=358(3) MeV), and a94m278 (a=0.094(1) fm, m_π=278(4) MeV)

## Context

This work extends the JAM collaboration's previous global analyses of pion PDFs (Barry 2018, Barry 2021) by incorporating lattice QCD data on the same footing as experimental data, following the methodology previously applied to nucleon PDFs (Bringewatt 2020). The analysis tests the universality of PDFs across different lattice observables (Rp-ITDs and current-current correlators) and quantifies systematic uncertainties intrinsic to lattice calculations. The results enable future combined analyses of experimental and lattice data for other hadrons, and provide a framework for assessing lattice systematic effects in hadron structure calculations, particularly relevant for planned measurements at Jefferson Lab and the Electron-Ion Collider.
