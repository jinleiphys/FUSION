# New collective mode due to collisional coupling

> Starting from a nonmarkovian conserving relaxation time approximation for collisions we derive coupled dispersion relations for asymmetric nuclear matter. The isovector and isoscalar modes are coupled due to asymmetric nuclear meanfield acting on neutrons and protons differently. A further coupling is observed by collisional correlations. The latter one leads to the appearance of a new soft mode besides isoscalar and isovector modes in the system. We suggest that this mode might be observable in asymmetric systems. This soft mode approaches the isovector mode for high temperatures. At the same time the isovector mode remains finite and approaches a constant value at higher temperatures showing a transition from zero sound like damping to first sound. The damping of the new soft mode is first sound like at all temperatures.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a new soft collective mode, distinct from the standard isovector and isoscalar modes, appears in asymmetric nuclear matter due to collisional correlations between neutrons and protons; this mode is predicted to be observable only when both asymmetry and collisions are present, and it vanishes in symmetric matter or when the collision integral is turned off.

## Method

The authors start from a non-Markovian (memory-dependent) relaxation time approximation for the collision integral in coupled quantum kinetic equations for neutrons and protons. They linearize the kinetic equations around equilibrium, derive a generalized multicomponent Mermin polarization function, and obtain a dispersion relation for collective modes that includes both mean-field coupling (due to asymmetric mean fields acting differently on neutrons and protons) and an additional collisional coupling term \(D_{np}\). The dispersion relation is then expanded for small wave vectors appropriate for giant dipole resonances in the Steinwedel-Jensen model, yielding a polynomial equation solved for centroid energies and damping widths as functions of excitation energy (temperature) for specific nuclei like \(^{11}\text{B}\) and \(^{11}\text{Be}\).

## Key numbers

* The isovector mode centroid energy vanishes at about 5 MeV excitation energy for \(^{11}\text{B}\), while the isoscalar mode vanishes at about 4 MeV.
* The new soft mode has a centroid energy around 1 MeV for \(^{11}\text{B}\) and a higher centroid energy for the more asymmetric \(^{11}\text{Be}\).
* For \(^{11}\text{Be}\), the isovector mode decreases and remains constant at about 5 MeV at higher excitation energies.
* The damping width of the new soft mode is independent of asymmetry across isobaric states of \(^{11}\text{Be}\).
* The markovian relaxation time is given by \(\tau^{-1}_{ab} = \frac{4 m}{3 \hbar^3} \sigma_{ab} T^2\).
* The wave vector for giant dipole resonances is \(q = 2.1 / R\) with nuclear radius \(R = 1.13 A^{1/3}\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the dispersion relation derived by Colonna, Toro, and Lanza (1997) by including an explicit collisional coupling term \(D_{np}\) that is absent in their treatment. The new mode is analogous to coupling effects seen in plasma two-stream instabilities (Hasegawa and Watanabe, 1991). The authors suggest that a low-lying structure observed experimentally at around 6 MeV excitation energy with a damping of about 1 MeV in \(^{11}\text{Be}\) (Charvet, 1996), which was not reproduced by coupled channel calculations (Eberlein et al., 1995), might be explained by this new soft mode. The results are limited to a liquid drop model and neglect shell and surface effects, which are important for realistic calculations in small systems.
