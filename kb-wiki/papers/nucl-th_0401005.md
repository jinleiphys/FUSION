# Nuclear alpha-clustering, superdeformation, and molecular resonances

> Nuclear alpha-clustering has been the subject of intense study since the advent of heavy-ion accelerators. Looking back for more than 40 years we are able today to see the connection between quasimolecular resonances in heavy-ion collisions and extremely deformed states in light nuclei. For example superdeformed bands have been recently discovered in light N=Z nuclei such as $^{36}$Ar, $^{40}$Ca, $^{48}$Cr, and $^{56}$Ni by $γ$-ray spectroscopy. The search for strongly deformed shapes in N=Z nuclei is also the domain of charged-particle spectroscopy, and our experimental group at IReS Strasbourg has studied a number of these nuclei with the charged particle multidetector array {\sc Icare} at the {\sc Vivitron} Tandem facility in a systematical manner. Recently the search for $γ$-decays in $^{24}$Mg has been undertaken in a range of excitation energies where previously nuclear molecular resonances were found in $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C collisions. The breakup reaction $^{24}$Mg$+^{12}$C has been investigated at E$_{lab}$($^{24}$Mg) = 130 MeV, an energy which corresponds to the appropriate excitation energy in $^{24}$Mg for which the $^{12}$C+$^{12}$C resonance could be related to the breakup resonance. Very exclusive data were collected with the Binary Reaction Spectrometer in coincidence with {\sc Euroball IV} installed at the {\sc Vivitron}.

# Nuclear alpha-clustering, superdeformation, and molecular resonances

## Key claim
The paper argues that highly deformed shapes observed in light N=Z nuclei via charged-particle spectroscopy are not necessarily linked to quasimolecular resonances, but may instead correspond to shape isomerism of rotating compound nuclei. The search for γ-decays in ²⁴Mg from ¹²C+¹²C molecular resonances remains inconclusive because the excitation energies and spin regions previously chosen were too high for measurable γ-ray branches.

## Method
The author's group at IReS Strasbourg used the Icare charged-particle multidetector array at the Vivitron Tandem facility to measure light charged particles (protons, deuterons, tritons, ³He, and α particles) in coincidence with heavy fragments from reactions including ²⁸Si+²⁸Si, ²⁸Si+¹²C, ²⁷Al+¹²C, ³²S+¹²C, and ¹⁶O+²⁸Si. The experimental data were analyzed using the Cacarizo statistical-model code (a Monte Carlo version of Cascade) to extract deformation parameters. Separately, the ²⁴Mg+¹²C breakup reaction at Elab(²⁴Mg) = 130 MeV was studied with the Binary Reaction Spectrometer in coincidence with Euroball IV to search for γ-decays from ¹²C+¹²C molecular states in ²⁴Mg.

## Key numbers
- ²⁸Si+²⁸Si reaction studied at Elab = 112 MeV (corresponding to ⁵⁶Ni excitation energy of the conjectured Jπ = 38⁺ quasimolecular resonance) and at Elab = 180 MeV (outside the molecular window)
- Deformation parameter β ≈ 0.5 deduced for ⁵⁶Ni from α-particle spectra at both energies
- ²⁴Mg+¹²C reaction investigated at Elab(²⁴Mg) = 130 MeV, covering excitation energies up to E* = 30 MeV in ²⁴Mg
- γ-ray branching ratios for ¹²C+¹²C molecular states predicted in the range of 10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁵ fractions of the total width
- Angular range for BRS detection: θlab = 12°–40° in the lab system (with θlab = 12°–25° for recoils)
- Solid angle transformation gives a factor of 10 for detection of heavy fragments in inverse kinematics
- γ-ray transitions observed in ²⁴Mg up to the 8⁺₂ level, with a candidate 10⁺₁ → 8⁺₂ transition at approximately 5.95 MeV
- Level density parameter set to a = A/8 MeV⁻¹

## Context
This work extends the long-standing investigation of quasimolecular resonances in light α-cluster nuclei, first identified by Bromley (1960) in ¹²C+¹²C collisions, and connects them to the more recent discovery of superdeformed rotational bands in N=Z nuclei such as ³⁶Ar (Svensson, 2000), ⁴⁰Ca (Ideguchi, 2001), ⁴⁸Cr (Lenzi, 1996), and ⁵⁶Ni (Rudolph, 1999). The charged-particle spectroscopy results are compared with statistical-model calculations and with the generalized liquid drop model of Royer (2003). The search for γ-decays from ¹²C+¹²C molecular states in ²⁴Mg builds on previous breakup studies by Fulton (1986), Curtis (1995), and Singer (2000), and on earlier unsuccessful γ-ray searches by McGrath (1981), Metag (1982), and Haas (1997). The paper enables future experiments with highly efficient spectrometers (the Dragon separator at TRIUMF and the FMA at Argonne) to study the overlap between ²⁴Mg states observed in breakup reactions and radiative capture states.
