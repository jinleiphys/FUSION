# Equation of state, QCD phase diagram: predictions from lattice QCD

> I review recent results on phase structure and equation of state of strong interaction matter from lattice QCD. Particular emphasis is given to the axes where direct simulations are possible and results are obtained with sufficient control over systematic effects. I also discuss the status of approaching the region of non-zero baryochemical potentials using indirect methods.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD can make direct predictions for the QCD phase structure and equation of state only along axes where Monte-Carlo simulations are sign-problem free (temperature axis, isospin chemical potential axis, and magnetic field axis); the region of non-zero baryon chemical potential remains accessible only through indirect methods such as Taylor expansion and analytic continuation, and the existence and location of a critical endpoint is not yet conclusively determined.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD results for the phase diagram and equation of state of strong-interaction matter, covering direct simulations at zero baryon chemical potential, at non-zero isospin chemical potential, and in external magnetic fields. For the region of non-zero baryon chemical potential, the review summarizes results from Taylor expansion of the pressure in powers of chemical potentials and from analytic continuation of simulations performed at imaginary chemical potentials, including recent developments such as the T'-expansion and methods to locate a possible critical endpoint via entropy spinodals and Lee-Yang edge singularities.

## Key numbers

- Pseudocritical temperature at zero baryon chemical potential: \(T_{pc} \sim 158\) MeV (Aoki 2006, HotQCD 2018, Borsanyi 2020)
- Equation of state computed with high accuracy up to \(T \lesssim 2\) GeV (Borsanyi 2013, HotQCD 2014, Bazavov 2017); extended via step scaling up to \(T \lesssim 165\) GeV (Bresciani 2025)
- Continuum phase diagram of isospin-asymmetric QCD mapped up to \(\mu_I \lesssim 325\) MeV (Brandt 2017, Brandt 2018)
- Pseudo-tricritical point in the isospin phase diagram at \((T, \mu_I) = (151(7), 70(5))\) MeV (Brandt 2017)
- Critical endpoint in the magnetic field phase diagram located between \(\vert e\mathbf{B} \vert = 4\) and 9 GeV\(^2\) (D'Elia 2021)
- Taylor expansion coefficients available at 10th order on \(N_t=8\) lattices with \(LT=2\) and 8th order with \(LT=4\) (Adam 2025, Bollweg 2022); continuum results only for 4th order coefficients (Bollweg 2022, Abuali 2025)
- Equation of state from 8th order Taylor expansion applicable up to \(\mu_B/T \lesssim 2.5\) (Bollweg 2022); from T'-expansion up to \(\mu_B/T \approx 3.5\) (Borsanyi 2022)
- Curvature coefficient \(\kappa_2 \approx 0.015\) for the chiral crossover in \(\mu_B\)-direction (Bonati 2018, HotQCD 2018, Borsanyi 2020); \(\kappa_4\) consistent with zero
- Entropy spinodal analysis found a candidate critical endpoint at \([T^{\rm CEP}, \mu_B^{\rm CEP}] = [114.3(6.9), 602(62)]\) MeV (Shah 2024); improved analysis could only exclude the CEP up to \(\mu_B = 450\) MeV (Borsanyi 2025)
- Lee-Yang edge singularity analysis found a candidate CEP at \([T^{\rm CEP}, \mu_B^{\rm CEP}] = [105^{+8}_{-18}, 422^{+80}_{-35}]\) MeV (Clarke 2024); high-accuracy study gave only an upper bound \(T^{\rm CEP} \lesssim 103\) MeV (Adam 2025)

## Context

This review extends the line of lattice QCD results on the phase structure and equation of state that began with the discovery of the crossover nature of the QCD transition (Aoki 2006) and the computation of the equation of state at zero chemical potential (Borsanyi 2013, HotQCD 2014, Bazavov 2017). It covers the mapping of the isospin phase diagram predicted by chiral perturbation theory (Son 2000) and the magnetic field phase diagram (Bali 2011, Endrodi 2015, D'Elia 2021). The indirect methods discussed (Taylor expansion from Allton 2002, analytic continuation from deForcrand 2003) are the current state-of-the-art for accessing non-zero baryon chemical potential, and the review highlights the tension between different analyses attempting to locate the critical endpoint, indicating that systematic control remains the primary challenge for future work.
