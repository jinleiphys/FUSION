# $ρ- ω$ Mixing at High Temperature and Density

> The temperature and density dependence of the $ρ-ω$ mixing amplitude has been studied from a purely hadronic model. The in-medium baryon masses and chemical potentials have been obtained at the Mean Field (MF) Level and these results have been used to calculate the mixing amplitude. It has been observed that the mixing amplitude changes substantially at high temperature and density.

## Key claim

The rho-omega mixing amplitude, calculated from a purely hadronic model using nucleon-antinucleon loops, changes substantially at high temperature and density, with the specific behavior depending on whether the density is zero, nuclear matter density, or twice nuclear matter density.

## Method

The author uses the non-linear Walecka model in the Mean Field Approximation to compute in-medium baryon masses and chemical potentials for protons and neutrons, accounting for the asymmetry (44/208) of a lead-lead system. These medium-dependent quantities are then fed into the polarization function, which is split into vector-vector and vector-tensor contributions, to calculate the rho-omega mixing amplitude at q² = m_omega².

## Key numbers

- At zero density (rho = 0 fm⁻³) and T = 0 MeV, the modulus of the mixing amplitude is about 4300 MeV; at T = 200 MeV it rises to about 5000 MeV.
- At nuclear matter density (rho = 0.15 fm⁻³) and T = 0 MeV, the amplitude is about 30000 MeV; it decreases to about 15000 MeV at T = 180 MeV, then increases slightly.
- At rho = 0.3 fm⁻³ and T = 0 MeV, the amplitude is about 100000 MeV; it decreases to about 36000 MeV at T = 200 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the momentum-dependent rho-omega mixing calculation of the author's previous reference (labeled e in the text) to finite temperature and density, using the same hadronic interaction prescription. The results are relevant for understanding dilepton emission rates in hot and dense hadronic matter produced in high-energy heavy-ion collisions, and the author notes that using baryon masses and chemical potentials from other models could change results quantitatively but not qualitatively.
