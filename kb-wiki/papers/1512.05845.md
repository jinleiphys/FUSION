# Correlations between neutrons and protons near Fermi surface and $Q_α$ of super-heavy nuclei

> The shell corrections and shell gaps in nuclei are systematically studied with the latest Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS4) mass model. We find that most of asymmetric nuclei with (sub)-shell closures locate along the shell stability line (SSL), $N=1.37Z+13.5$, which might be due to a strong correlation between neutrons and protons near Fermi surface. The double magicity of nuclei $^{46}$Si and $^{78}$Ni is predicted according to the corresponding shell gaps, shell corrections and nuclear deformations. The unmeasured super-heavy nuclei $^{296}$118 and $^{298}$120, with relatively large shell gaps and shell corrections, also locate along the SSL, whereas the traditional magic nucleus $^{298}$Fl evidently deviates from the line. The $α$-decay energies of super-heavy nuclei with $Z=113-126$ are simultaneously investigated by using the WS4 model together with the radial basis function corrections. For super-heavy nuclei with large shell corrections, the smallest $α$-decay energy for elements $Z=116$, 117 and 118 in their isotope chains locates at $N=178$ rather than $184$.

## Key claim
The paper argues that many asymmetric nuclei with (sub)-shell closures lie along a "shell stability line" (SSL) defined by $N=1.37Z+13.5$, which the authors attribute to a strong correlation between neutrons and protons near the Fermi surface; however, the claim is hedged as "might be due to" this correlation, and the SSL is presented with an uncertainty of $\Delta N < 2$ in neutron number.

## Method
The authors used the macroscopic-microscopic Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS4) mass model, with shell corrections obtained via the Strutinsky procedure from an axially deformed Woods-Saxon potential. They calculated shell gaps (scaled by $A^{1/2}$) and shell corrections for nuclei across the nuclear landscape, and predicted $\alpha$-decay energies ($Q_\alpha$) for super-heavy nuclei with $Z=113-126$ using the WS4 model augmented with radial basis function (RBF) corrections. The ratio of active-neutrons to active-protons ($N_a/Z_a$) and an effective ratio $T_{\rm eff}=N_a/Z_a - I_{\rm core}$ were introduced to quantify correlations near the Fermi surface.

## Key numbers
- The shell stability line (SSL) is given by $N=1.37Z+13.5$, with an uncertainty of $\Delta N < 2$.
- The WS4$^{\rm RBF}$ model reproduces 2353 measured masses ($N\ge 8$, $Z\ge 8$) from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation with an rms deviation of 170 keV.
- The mean scaled shell gap for all known nuclei is $\langle \Delta_{\rm scale}\rangle = 41.9$ MeV; for 39 known nuclei around the SSL it is $\langle \Delta^{\rm SSL}_{\rm scale}\rangle = 50.4$ MeV.
- For $^{46}$Si: scaled shell gap = 105.1 MeV, shell correction = $-7.96$ MeV, quadrupole deformation $\beta_2 = -0.01$.
- For $^{78}$Ni: scaled shell gap = 115.1 MeV, shell correction = $-7.98$ MeV, $\beta_2 = 0.01$.
- For $^{296}$118: scaled shell gap = 48.0 MeV, shell correction = $-5.93$ MeV, $\beta_2 = -0.08$, predicted $Q_\alpha = 11.73$ MeV.
- For $^{298}$120: scaled shell gap = 48.6 MeV, shell correction = $-5.89$ MeV, $\beta_2 = -0.08$, predicted $Q_\alpha = 12.98$ MeV.
- For super-heavy nuclei with $Z=116$, 117, and 118, the smallest $Q_\alpha$ in their isotope chains occurs at $N=178$, not $N=184$.
- For $^{298}$Fl ($Z=114$, $N=184$): scaled shell gap = 96.2 MeV, shell correction = $-5.16$ MeV (smaller than for $^{270}$Hs and $^{298}$120).

## Context
This work extends the systematic study of shell effects in super-heavy nuclei using the WS4 mass model, building on prior macroscopic-microscopic approaches such as the finite-range droplet model (FRDM) of Moller et al. (1995) and the two-center shell model (TCSM) of Kuzmina et al. (2012). The authors compare their $Q_\alpha$ predictions with those from these models and with experimental data from Oganessian et al. (2006, 2015). The finding that the smallest $Q_\alpha$ for elements 116-118 occurs at $N=178$ rather than the traditionally expected $N=184$ challenges the conventional picture of the island of stability centered on $^{298}$Fl, and suggests that the unmeasured nuclei $^{296}$118 and $^{297}$118 are crucial targets for future experimental synthesis to test the model predictions.
