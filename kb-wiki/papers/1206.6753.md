# Nuclear first order phase transition associated with Helmholtz free energy of canonical ensemble

> It was shown that in the canonical ensemble the simple exactly soluble statistical model of nuclei decay into nucleons, which is a limiting case of the statistical multifragmentation model, predicts the nuclear first order phase transition associated with the Helmholtz free energy different from the first order phase transition of the liquid-gas type associated with the Gibbs free energy. The main thermodynamic properties of this phase transition were explored on the basis of the method of the thermodynamic potential and its first and second derivatives in the thermodynamic limit. It was established that the thermodynamic potential $F$ is a piecewise smooth function and its first order partial derivatives with respect to variables of state are piecewise continuous functions. At the points of phase transition, the energy in the caloric curve is discontinuous at the constant temperature and fixed values of the specific volume, while the pressure and the chemical potential in the equations of state are discontinuous at the constant specific volume and fixed values of the temperature.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that a simple statistical model of nuclei decaying into nucleons, a limiting case of the statistical multifragmentation model, exhibits a first-order phase transition associated with the Helmholtz free energy in the canonical ensemble, which is distinct from the liquid-gas type first-order phase transition associated with the Gibbs free energy. The key qualifier is that this phase transition is defined by the equality of temperature and specific volume across phase boundaries, rather than the equality of temperature, pressure, and chemical potential.

## Method
The author constructed an exactly soluble statistical model in the canonical ensemble where a system of N nuclei of A nucleons decays into B free nucleons, with the partition function containing only terms for free nucleons and intact nuclei. The thermodynamic limit (N → ∞, V → ∞, v = V/B constant) was taken, and the Helmholtz free energy per nucleon and its first and second partial derivatives with respect to temperature, volume, and baryon number were derived analytically. The phase transition was identified by analyzing the piecewise smooth behavior of the free energy and the discontinuities of its first derivatives (entropy, pressure, chemical potential) as functions of temperature at fixed specific volume and as functions of specific volume at fixed temperature.

## Key numbers
- Binding energy per nucleon for ¹²C nuclei: W_A/A = 7.680144 MeV
- Spin-isospin degeneracy factors: g₁ = 4 (free nucleons), g_A = 1 (nuclei)
- Specific volume used in temperature-dependent calculations: v = 3v₀, where v₀ = 1/ρ₀ (normal nuclear density)
- Temperature used in volume-dependent calculations: T = 18.4 MeV
- Specific volume used in caloric curve calculations: v = 2.5v₀
- Temperature used in isotherm calculations: T = 21.4 MeV
- The coexistence curve in the T-v phase diagram begins at critical point (T_c = ∞, v_c = v₀) and finishes at (T = 0, v = ∞)
- The coexistence curve in the T-ρ phase diagram begins at (T = 0, ρ = 0) and finishes at critical point (T_c = ∞, ρ_c = ρ₀)

## Context
This work extends the statistical multifragmentation models (SMM) of Bondorf (1985), Gross (1990), and others, which predict plateaus in caloric curves in canonical and microcanonical ensembles, by providing an exactly soluble limiting case. It contrasts with the liquid-gas type first-order phase transition associated with the Gibbs free energy, which is represented by coexistence lines in the T-μ and T-p planes (as discussed in Parvan (2012) and standard nuclear matter treatments by Serot (1986) and Kapusta). The results enable a clearer classification of first-order phase transitions by the thermodynamic potential of the statistical ensemble and provide a new Gibbs phase rule (equality of T and v across phase boundaries) that differs from the usual rule for liquid-gas transitions.
