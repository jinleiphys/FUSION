# Thermal noise in non-boost-invariant dissipative hydrodynamics

> We study the effects of hydrodynamic fluctuations in non-boost-invariant longitudinal expansion of matter formed in relativistic heavy ion collisions. We formulate the theory of thermal noise within second-order viscous hydrodynamics treating noise as a perturbation on top of the non-boost-invariant flow. We develop a numerical simulation model to treat the (1+1)-dimension hydrodynamic evolution. The code is tested to reproduce the analytic results for the Riemann solver for expansion of matter in vacuum. For viscous hydrodynamic expansion, the initial energy density distribution are obtained by reproducing the measured charged hadron rapidity distribution at the RHIC energies. We show that the longitudinal rapidity correlations arising from space-time dependent thermal noise and from an induced thermal perturbation have distinct structures. In general, the rapidity correlations are found to be dominated by temperature fluctuations at small rapidity separation and velocity fluctuations at large rapidities. We demonstrate that thermal noise produce ridge-like two-particle rapidity correlations which persist at moderately large rapidities. The magnitude and pattern of the correlations are quite sensitive to various second-order dissipative formalisms and to the underlying equations of state, especially at large rapidities. The short-range part of the rapidity correlation is found to be somewhat enhanced as compared to that in boost-invariant flow of matter.

## Key claim

Thermal noise in non-boost-invariant (1+1)D longitudinal expansion produces ridge-like two-particle rapidity correlations that persist at moderately large rapidities, with the short-range part of the correlation somewhat enhanced compared to boost-invariant flow; however, the magnitude and pattern depend sensitively on the choice of second-order dissipative formalism (Müller-Israel-Stewart vs. Chapman-Enskog) and on the equation of state, especially at large rapidities.

## Method

The authors formulate thermal noise within second-order viscous hydrodynamics by treating fluctuations as linear perturbations on top of a non-boost-invariant (1+1)D longitudinal expansion background in Milne coordinates. They develop a numerical simulation using the SHASTA-FCT algorithm for the background evolution and the MacCormack predictor-corrector method for the fluctuation equations, with initial conditions constrained by reproducing measured charged hadron rapidity distributions at RHIC energies. The noise autocorrelation functions are derived from the fluctuation-dissipation theorem for both the Müller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) and Chapman-Enskog (CE) formalisms, and the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription is used to compute two-particle rapidity correlations.

## Key numbers

- The code is tested against analytic Riemann solver results for expansion of matter in vacuum.
- Initial energy density distributions are obtained by reproducing the measured charged hadron rapidity distribution at RHIC energies.
- The noise autocorrelation in MIS theory is given by ⟨ξ^{μν}(x)ξ^{αβ}(x')⟩ = 4η_v T Δ^{μναβ} δ^4(x−x').
- The noise autocorrelation in CE theory includes additional terms proportional to (5/7)β_2 and vorticity-dependent terms.
- The effective noise autocorrelation in (1+1)D is ⟨ξ(τ₁,η₁)ξ(τ₂,η₂)⟩ = (8η_v T)/(3τ A_⟂) [1 − A β_2 π_0] δ(τ₁−τ₂)δ(η₁−η₂), where A=0 for MIS and A=5/7 for CE.
- The short-range part of the rapidity correlation is found to be somewhat enhanced compared to that in boost-invariant flow.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of thermal noise in relativistic hydrodynamics that were limited to boost-invariant (Bjorken) flow, such as Kapusta (2011) and Chattopadhyay (2017). It enables more realistic modeling of longitudinal rapidity correlations in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, particularly at large rapidities where boost-invariance breaks down. The results show sensitivity to different second-order dissipative formalisms (MIS vs. CE) and to the equation of state (conformal vs. lattice QCD), which may help constrain transport coefficients and initial conditions in future phenomenological studies.
