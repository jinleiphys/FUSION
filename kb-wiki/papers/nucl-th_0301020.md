# Do multineutrons exist?

> Following recent work in which events which may correspond to a bound tetraneutron ($^4$n) were observed, it is pointed out that from the theoretical perspective the two-body nucleon-nucleon (NN) force cannot by itself bind four neutrons, even if it could bind a dineutron. Unrealistic modifications of the NN force or introduction of unreaslistic four-nucleon force would be needed in order to bind the tetraneutron. The existence of other multineutron systems is discussed.

# Do multineutrons exist?

## Key claim

The two-body nucleon-nucleon (NN) force cannot by itself bind four neutrons into a tetraneutron, even if it could bind a dineutron; unrealistically large modifications of the NN force or introduction of an unrealistic four-nucleon force would be required to produce a bound tetraneutron, making it unlikely that the observed events in the breakup of 14Be were caused by a bound tetraneutron.

## Method

The author performed hyperspherical functions method (HSFM) calculations of the tetraneutron (4n), hexaneutron (6n), octaneutron (8n), and decaneutron (10n) using the Reichstein-Tang (RT) and Volkov V1 effective NN potentials, within the extreme uncoupled adiabatic approximation. The model space was increased up to Kmax = 16 for 4n, and diagonalized hyperradial potentials were computed to search for bound states or resonances. The calculations were also repeated with a simulated four-nucleon force to estimate the strength required to bind the tetraneutron.

## Key numbers

- The tetraneutron was found to be unbound by 18.5 MeV in a 4ħω oscillator shell model calculation (Bev, cited in text).
- With the Volkov V1 potential, the dineutron is bound by 0.547 MeV, yet the tetraneutron remains unbound.
- To bind the tetraneutron with the RT potential, the odd-wave parameter u must be increased to u = 2.3, which would overbind 4H and 5He by about 6.5 MeV and 32 MeV respectively (in reality these nuclei are unbound by 3 MeV and 0.9 MeV).
- To bind the tetraneutron with the Volkov V1 potential, the Majorana parameter m must be changed from 0.6 to -0.2, giving E(4n) = -1.2 MeV but overbinding 4H and 5He by 7 MeV and 25 MeV respectively.
- A simulated four-nucleon force of strength W0 = -410, -1460, and -2530 MeV (for ranges α = 0.7, 1.2, and 1.5 fm⁻¹) would be needed to bind the tetraneutron with the RT potential; these strengths are two orders of magnitude larger than the 3N spin-orbit force values of -1, -10, and -20 MeV.
- If the same 4N force existed in 4He, its binding energy with V1 would become -88, -82, and -134 MeV instead of the actual -28.3 MeV.
- For 6n, with the same unrealistic modifications that bind 4n (u = 2.3 for RT, m = -0.2 for V1), 6n would be bound by about 22 MeV or 50 MeV respectively.

## Context

This work responds to the experimental observation by Marques (2002) of events possibly corresponding to a bound tetraneutron in the breakup of 14Be. The author extends earlier theoretical studies that found no bound tetraneutron using various methods: the oscillator shell model (Bev, cited in text), the angular potential functions method (Gorb, cited in text), the stochastic variational method (Varga, cited in text), and the hyperspherical functions method (BBKE, SRV, cited in text). The calculations contradict the suggestion by GNO (cited in text) that the tetraneutron might exist as a resonance between 1 and 3 MeV, showing that the RT potential produces no resonance when the model space is increased. The work also discusses the possible role of three-nucleon and four-nucleon forces, referencing Green's function Monte Carlo calculations by Pieper (2001) for neutron drops, and notes that the required 4N force strength would be two orders of magnitude larger than existing 3N forces and would dramatically overbind 4He.
