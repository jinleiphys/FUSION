# Flow in p-Pb collisions at the LHC

> We present the predictions of a hydrodynamic model for the flow observables recently measured in the highest-multiplicity p+Pb collisions at the LHC. We focus o the ridge phenomenon, which provides an important probe of the long-range dynamics and may be used to support the collective interpretation of the p+A data.

# Flow in p-Pb collisions at the LHC

## Key claim
The hydrodynamic model can describe the ridge phenomenon and flow observables in the highest-multiplicity p+Pb collisions at LHC energies, supporting a collective interpretation of these small-system data. The authors hedge that this explanation follows the path of A+A analyses and that discrimination from Color Glass Condensate scenarios may require future particle-identified data.

## Method
The authors use a three-stage event-by-event approach: (1) a fluctuating initial state from Glauber simulations with smeared sources at participating nucleon centers; (2) 3+1D viscous hydrodynamics with shear viscosity η/s=0.08, bulk viscosity, and hydro ignition time τ=0.6 fm; (3) statistical hadronization at constant freeze-out temperature T_f=150 MeV. They compute the per-trigger correlation function C_trig(Δη,Δφ) and the projected ZYAM-subtracted correlation Y(Δφ) for comparison to CMS and ATLAS data.

## Key numbers
- Collision energy: √s_NN = 5.02 TeV for p+Pb collisions at the LHC
- Shear viscosity: η/s = 0.08 used in the viscous hydrodynamic simulations
- Hydro ignition time: τ = 0.6 fm
- Freeze-out temperature: T_f = 150 MeV
- Transverse momentum range for particles: 1.0 < p_T < 3.0 GeV (soft range)
- Pseudorapidity range for projection: 2 < |Δη| < 5 (ATLAS analysis)
- Total transverse momentum conservation condition: P_T < 5 GeV
- Centrality classes: N_track ≥ 110 and 90 ≤ N_track ≤ 110 (CMS analysis)

## Context
This work extends the collective flow and ridge explanation from A+A collisions at RHIC and the LHC (Ollitrault 1992, Takahashi 2009, Alver 2010) to the much smaller p+Pb system. It builds on the authors' prior hydrodynamic predictions (Bozek 2012, 2013) and contrasts with Color Glass Condensate approaches (Dusling 2012, Kovchegov 2012, Kovner 2012) that do not invoke hydrodynamical collectivity. The results enable future discrimination between these mechanisms using particle-identified data.
