# Symmetry energy from fragment observables in the canonical thermodynamic model

> Different formulas relying measurable fragment isotopic observables to the symmetry energy of excited nuclei have been proposed and applied to the analysis of heavy ion collision data in the recent literature. In this paper we examine the quality of the different expressions in the framework of the McGill Canonical Thermodynamic Model. We show that even in the idealized situation of canonical equilibrium and in the absence of secondary decay, these formulas do not give a precise reconstruction of the symmetry energy of the fragmenting source. However, both isotopic widths and isoscaling appear very well correlated to the physical symmetry energy.

## Key claim

Even in the idealized situation of canonical equilibrium and without secondary decay, formulas that relate measurable fragment isotopic observables to the symmetry energy do not give a precise reconstruction of the symmetry energy of the fragmenting source; however, both isotopic widths and isoscaling appear very well correlated to the physical symmetry energy.

## Method

The authors used the McGill Canonical Thermodynamic Model (CTM) to compute the canonical partition function for fragmenting sources with specified proton number Z=75 and mass numbers A=168 and A=186 at various temperatures and break-up volumes. They then extracted the isoscaling parameter α from the slopes of differential cluster yields averaged over Z=1–5 and applied three different formulas from the literature (eq. 1 from Botvina, eq. 2 from Ono et al., and eq. 3 from Raduta et al.) to reconstruct the input symmetry energy coefficient, testing constant, mass-dependent, and temperature/surface-dependent prescriptions for csym.

## Key numbers

- Input symmetry energy fixed to csym = 23.5 MeV; extracted apparent csym from eq. (1) ranged from 20.63 MeV (at T=4.5 MeV, V/V0=6) to 26.10 MeV (at T=7.5 MeV, V/V0=4), with deviations never exceeding 12%.
- For V/V0=6 at T=3.5 MeV, the apparent csym was 22.02 MeV, recovering the input value at the lowest temperature.
- Isoscaling parameter α decreased with increasing temperature: e.g., at V/V0=4, α dropped from 0.870 (T=3.5 MeV) to 0.511 (T=7.5 MeV).
- For the fluctuation formula (eq. 3), the apparent csym for a cluster of charge j=10 showed a linear correlation with the input csym, but with a systematic bias.

## Context

This work extends the debate between Das Gupta et al. (2008) and Raduta et al. (2008) over whether the decreasing apparent symmetry energy extracted from data reflects a true physical decrease or is an artifact of the statistical model used. The authors show that the CTM and MMM models produce very similar results when compared under consistent conditions. The findings imply that while no single formula yields a model-independent measurement, the strong correlation between isotopic observables and the symmetry energy means that ratios of these observables measured in different systems should allow extraction of the physical trend, provided secondary decay effects are accounted for.
