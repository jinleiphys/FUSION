# Particle Ratios at CERN, BNL and GSI : Unified Description of Freeze-Out Parameters

> It is shown that the chemical freeze-out parameters obtained at CERN/SPS, BNL/AGS and GSI/SIS energies all correspond to a unique value of 1 GeV for the average energy per hadron.

## Key claim

The chemical freeze-out parameters extracted from heavy-ion collisions at GSI/SIS, BNL/AGS, and CERN/SPS all correspond to a unique value of 1 GeV for the average energy per hadron, independent of beam energy; the abstract omits that this condition defines the point at which inelastic collisions cease to be important and hadronic abundances become fixed.

## Method

The authors compiled published chemical freeze-out temperatures and baryon chemical potentials from fits to measured particle ratios at GSI/SIS (Ni-Ni at 1.0 AGeV), BNL/AGS (Si-Au at 11.0 AGeV), and CERN/SPS (Pb-Pb). They compared these extracted parameters to the curve of constant average energy per hadron (1 GeV) calculated in a hadronic gas model, showing that all data points lie on or near this curve.

## Key numbers

- At GSI/SIS (Ni-Ni at 1.0 AGeV), the freeze-out temperature is low enough that exact canonical conservation of strangeness is required; the fit uses full chemical equilibrium with \(\gamma_s = 1\).
- At BNL/AGS (Si-Au at 11.0 AGeV), \(T_{ch} = 118.4 \pm 11.6\) MeV and \(\mu_B^{ch} \approx 522\) MeV.
- At CERN/SPS (Pb-Pb), two analyses yield \(T_{ch} = 165\) MeV (Gorenstein) and \(T_{ch} = 170\) MeV (Heppe), a difference of less than 3 percent; the strangeness suppression factor \(\gamma_s\) is disputed (0.55 vs. 1.0).
- The common condition across all three energy regimes is an average energy per hadron of 1 GeV.

## Context

This work extends the systematic analysis of chemical freeze-out parameters from earlier studies at SIS (Keranen, Oeschler), AGS (Becattini, Heppe), and SPS (Heppe, Gorenstein, Rafelski) by showing that all data obey a single scaling condition. The result enables predictions for the SPS 40 and 80 GeV beams and sets a baseline for interpreting future RHIC data. The authors note that recent results from the E895 collaboration presented at the same conference are compatible with the 1 GeV per hadron condition.
