# Measured proton electromagnetic structure deviates from theoretical predictions

> The visible world is founded on the proton, the only composite building block of matter that is stable in nature. Consequently, understanding the formation of matter relies on explaining the dynamics and the properties of the proton's bound state.A fundamental property of the proton involves the response of the system to an external electromagnetic field. It is characterized by the electromagnetic polarizabilities that describe how easily the charge and magnetization distributions inside the system are distorted by the electromagnetic field. Moreover, the generalized polarizabilities map out the resulting deformation of the densities in a proton subject to an electromagnetic field. They disclose essential information about the underlying system dynamics and provide a key for decoding the proton structure in terms of the theory of the strong interaction that binds its elementary quark and gluon constituents. Of particular interest is a puzzle in the electric generalized polarizability of the proton that remains unresolved for two decades. Here we report measurements of the proton's electromagnetic generalized polarizabilities at low four-momentum transfer squared. We show evidence of an anomaly to the behaviour of the proton's electric generalized polarizability that contradicts the predictions of nuclear theory and derive its signature in the spatial distribution of the induced polarization in the proton. The reported measurements suggest the presence of a new, not-yet-understood dynamical mechanism in the proton and present notable challenges to the nuclear theory.

## Key claim

The paper reports measurements of the proton's electric generalized polarizability that show a local enhancement at low four-momentum transfer squared, contradicting theoretical predictions of a monotonic decrease; the anomaly is confirmed with improved precision compared to earlier work, but the world data at the relevant Q² reconcile only at the approximately 2σ level.

## Method

The experiment used the virtual Compton scattering (VCS) reaction γ*p → pγ, accessed via ep → epγ at Jefferson Lab Hall C with a 4.56 GeV electron beam on a liquid hydrogen target. The scattered electron and recoil proton were detected in coincidence using the Super High Momentum Spectrometer and the High Momentum Spectrometer, and the real photon was identified through missing-mass reconstruction. The generalized polarizabilities were extracted by fitting the measured cross sections with a dispersion relation (DR) model, with the two scalar generalized polarizabilities as free parameters adjusted via χ² minimization.

## Key numbers

- Measurements were taken at three values of four-momentum transfer squared: Q² = 0.28 GeV², 0.33 GeV², and 0.40 GeV².
- The mean square electric polarizability radius of the proton was found to be ⟨r²_αE⟩ = 1.36 ± 0.29 fm².
- The mean square magnetic polarizability radius was found to be ⟨r²_βM⟩ = 0.63 ± 0.31 fm².
- The beam energy was 4.56 GeV with a beam current up to 20 μA.
- The coincidence timing resolution was approximately 1 ns (FWHM).

## Context

This work addresses a two-decade-old puzzle in the proton's electric generalized polarizability, first indicated by measurements at Q² = 0.33 GeV² (exp:1, exp:2) but with large uncertainty and later questioned by other experiments (exp:7, exp:8). The results contradict predictions from chiral effective field theory (ceft:1, ceft:2, ceft:3), constituent quark models (nrqm:1, nrqm:2), and other theoretical approaches, and they provide high-precision benchmark data for upcoming lattice QCD calculations. The measurements complement spin-dependent generalized polarizabilities of the nucleon (natphys1, natphys2, natphys3) and call for further experimental mapping and improved theoretical description of the underlying dynamics.
