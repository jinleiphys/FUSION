# Experimental searches for the chiral magnetic effect in heavy-ion collisions

> The chiral magnetic effect (CME) in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) refers to a charge separation (an electric current) of chirality imbalanced quarks generated along an external strong magnetic field. The chirality imbalance results from interactions of quarks, under the approximate chiral symmetry restoration, with metastable local domains of gluon fields of non-zero topological charges out of QCD vacuum fluctuations. Those local domains violate the $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{CP}$ invariance, potentially offering a solution to the strong $\mathcal{CP}$ problem in explaining the magnitude of the matter-antimatter asymmetry in today's universe. Relativistic heavy-ion collisions, with the likely creation of the high energy density quark-gluon plasma and restoration of the approximate chiral symmetry, and the possibly long-lived strong magnetic field, provide a unique opportunity to detect the CME. Early measurements of the CME-induced charge separation in heavy-ion collisions are dominated by physics backgrounds. Major efforts have been devoted to eliminate or reduce those backgrounds. We review those efforts, with a somewhat historical perspective, and focus on the recent innovative experimental undertakings in the search for the CME in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The chiral magnetic effect (CME) in heavy-ion collisions remains experimentally unconfirmed because early measurements of CME-induced charge separation are dominated by physics backgrounds, and the magnitude of the CME is not theoretically guaranteed due to uncertainties in the time evolution of the magnetic field and final-state interactions that may reduce the initial charge separation by up to a factor of ten.

## Method

The authors review experimental searches for the CME using the three-point correlator γ = ⟨cos(φα+φβ−2ψ)⟩, which measures charge separation relative to the reaction plane (or harmonic plane) in non-central heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and the LHC. They describe how the correlator is evaluated either by constructing an event plane from final-state particle azimuthal distributions or by the three-particle correlation method using a third particle to replace the reaction plane. The review covers early measurements, identification of physics backgrounds, and recent innovative experimental efforts to eliminate or reduce those backgrounds.

## Key numbers

- The initial magnetic field in Au+Au collisions at RHIC is estimated to be on the order of B ∼ 10¹⁴ Tesla (eB ∼ mπ², where mπ is the pion mass)
- The CME magnitude is estimated to be on the order of 10⁻² from semi-quantitative estimates based on typical pion multiplicities of order 100
- An alternative estimate assuming winding number transition density of 8 fm⁻³ and temperature of 350 MeV gives CME magnitude approximately 6×10⁻⁴
- AMPT simulation indicates that the quark charge separation magnitude at the initial time is reduced by a factor of ten in the final-state freeze-out quarks before hadronization
- The dilepton pair pT broadening observed by STAR is on the order of 30 MeV/c, consistent with an integral of ∫ eB dℓ ∼ 0.3 mπ²·fm, or effectively a magnetic field of 0.3 mπ² acting over 1 fm
- The Coulomb effect from the net positive charge in the collision fireball gives a net pT to dilepton pairs on the order of 10 MeV/c for a typical fireball size

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework of the CME originally proposed by Kharzeev (2007) and Fukushima (2008), and builds on early experimental measurements at RHIC and the LHC. It addresses the strong CP problem in QCD and the matter-antimatter asymmetry in the universe, contrasting with the Peccei-Quinn mechanism proposed by Peccei and Quinn (1977) which predicts axions that have not been detected after four decades of search. The work enables future experimental searches using improved techniques such as the invariant mass method and harmonic plane measurements to distinguish CME signals from background correlations.
