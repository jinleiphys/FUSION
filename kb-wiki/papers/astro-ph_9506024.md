# Influence of the symmetry energy on the birth of neutron stars and supernova neutrinos

> We study the influence of the symmetry energy of the equation of state on the thermal evolution of protoneutron stars and the properties of supernova neutrinos by the numerical simulations after the protoneutron stars are formed. As for the equation of state (EOS) of nuclear matter, we take two EOS's with different symmetry energies obtained by the relativistic mean field theory. We find the symmetry energy plays the essential role on the evolution of lepton profiles and the neutrino fluxes.

## Key claim

The symmetry energy of the nuclear equation of state (EOS) plays an essential role in the thermal evolution of protoneutron stars and the properties of supernova neutrinos, specifically by altering the chemical composition and the chemical potential for neutrinos rather than the stiffness of the EOS. The study uses two EOSs with different symmetry energies (36.9 MeV vs. 28.2 MeV) from the relativistic mean field theory, but the initial entropy and electron fraction profiles are kept identical to isolate the symmetry energy effect, neglecting its influence on the earlier dynamical phase.

## Method

The authors constructed two tables of the EOS using the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory: one with the standard TM1 parameter set (symmetry energy 36.9 MeV) and one with a modified TMS set (symmetry energy 28.2 MeV), achieved by reducing the coupling constant of the rho meson. They then performed numerical simulations of the quasistatic cooling of protoneutron stars over 15 seconds, solving the general relativistic Oppenheimer-Volkoff equation for hydrostatic structure and the multigroup flux-limited diffusion scheme for neutrino transport, including electron neutrinos, electron antineutrinos, and muon/tau neutrinos.

## Key numbers

- Symmetry energy: 36.9 MeV for TM1, 28.2 MeV for TMS; corresponding rho-meson coupling constants: 4.63 for TM1, 3.50 for TMS.
- Incompressibility K at normal nuclear matter density: 281 MeV for both TM1 and TMS.
- Maximum neutron star mass: approximately 2.2 solar masses for both EOSs.
- Initial central chemical potential of electron neutrinos in beta-equilibrium: 142 MeV for TM1, 170 MeV for TMS.
- Initial central lepton fractions: 0.315 for TM1, 0.330 for TMS.
- Central lepton fractions at t = 15 seconds: 0.179 for TM1, 0.155 for TMS.
- Central temperatures at t = 15 seconds: 23.2 MeV for TM1, 26.3 MeV for TMS.
- Total baryon mass of protoneutron stars: fixed at 1.62 solar masses.
- Gravitational mass of initial protoneutron star: 1.5482 solar masses for TM1, 1.5479 solar masses for TMS.
- Gravitational mass of cold neutron star: 1.485 solar masses for TM1, 1.475 solar masses for TMS.
- Total energy released from protoneutron star: 1.13 x 10^53 erg (0.063 solar masses) for TM1, 1.31 x 10^53 erg (0.073 solar masses) for TMS.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the symmetry energy's role in neutron star cooling and supernova explosions by Boguta (1981), Lattimer et al. (1991), Bruenn (1989), and Swesty et al. (1994), but is the first systematic study focused on the birth of neutron stars and supernova neutrinos using EOS tables from the RMF theory constrained by unstable nuclei (Sumiyoshi and Toki 1994; Sumiyoshi et al. 1995). The results enable future work to simulate the dynamical phase of core collapse with the same EOSs and to extract information on the symmetry energy from observational signals of the next Galactic supernova.
