# Role of nucleon-nucleon correlation in transport coefficients and gravitational-wave-driven $r$-mode instability of neutron stars

> The thermal conductivity and shear viscosity of dense nuclear matter, along with the corresponding shear viscosity timescale of canonical neutron stars (NSs), are investigated, where the effect of Fermi surface depletion (i.e., the $Z$-factor effect) induced by the nucleon-nucleon correlation are taken into account. The factors which are responsible for the transport coefficients, including the equation of state for building the stellar structure, nucleon effective masses, in-medium cross sections, and the $Z$-factor at Fermi surfaces, are all calculated in the framework of the Brueckner theory. The Fermi surface depletion is found to enhance the transport coefficients by several times at high densities, which is more favorable to damping the gravitational-wave-driven $r$-mode instability of NSs. Yet, the onset of the $Z$-factor-quenched neutron triplet superfluidity provides the opposite effects, which can be much more significant than the above mentioned $Z$-factor effect itself. Therefore, different from the previous understanding, the nucleon shear viscosity is still smaller than the lepton one in the superfluid NS matter at low temperatures. Accordingly, the shear viscosity cannot stablize canonical NSs against $r$-mode oscillations even at quite low core temperatures $10^6$ K.

## Key claim

The Fermi surface depletion (Z-factor) enhances nucleonic thermal conductivity and shear viscosity by several times at high densities, which would favor damping of r-mode instability; however, the Z-factor-quenched neutron triplet superfluidity reduces shear viscosity much more significantly, so that even at core temperatures as low as 10^6 K the shear viscosity cannot stabilize canonical neutron stars against r-mode oscillations.

## Method

The authors calculated thermal conductivity and shear viscosity of dense nuclear matter using the Abrikosov-Khalatnikov (AK) framework from Landau kinetic equations, with all inputs (equation of state, nucleon effective masses, in-medium cross sections, and Z-factor at Fermi surfaces) obtained consistently from Brueckner-Hartree-Fock (BHF) theory using the AV18 two-body interaction plus a microscopic three-body force. The Z-factor effect was incorporated by modifying both the collision integral and the driving term of the kinetic equation, and the neutron 3PF2 superfluidity was included via a suppression factor from the literature. The shear viscosity timescale and gravitational-wave-driven r-mode growth timescale were then computed for canonical 1.4 solar mass neutron stars by solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov equation with the BHF equation of state.

## Key numbers

- At density rho = 0.6 fm^-3, the nucleonic thermal conductivity and shear viscosity are enhanced by about three to four times by the Z-factor effect.
- At the crust-core transition density rho ≈ 0.08 fm^-3, the nucleon shear viscosity with Z-factor is about four times larger than the lepton shear viscosity.
- The neutron 3PF2 superfluid gap has a peak value of about 0.04 MeV at density rho = 0.17 fm^-3.
- At temperature T = 5×10^7 K, the nucleon shear viscosity is reduced by about six orders of magnitude at rho = 0.17 fm^-3 due to superfluidity.
- For a canonical neutron star rotating at 716 Hz, the gravitational-wave-driven r-mode growth timescale tau_GW = 196 s.
- At T = 10^6 K, the shear viscosity timescale tau_eta = 402 s without superfluidity (66% nucleonic contribution) but tau_eta = 1200 s with superfluidity (0% nucleonic contribution).
- At T = 10^7 K, tau_eta = 2.99×10^4 s without superfluidity (50% nucleonic) and tau_eta = 4.38×10^4 s with superfluidity (9% nucleonic).

## Context

This work extends previous calculations of transport coefficients in neutron star matter by Benhar (2007, 2010), Zhang (2012), and Baldo (2013) by consistently including the Z-factor effect from nucleon-nucleon correlations within the Brueckner framework. It also builds on the finding by Dong et al. that the Z-factor strongly quenches neutron 3PF2 superfluidity. The results contradict earlier conclusions (e.g., from Andersson 2005 and Haskell 2015) that nucleon shear viscosity dominates over lepton viscosity at low temperatures, showing instead that lepton viscosity remains dominant in superfluid matter. The work enables more realistic modeling of r-mode instability windows for neutron stars and suggests that additional damping mechanisms beyond shear viscosity are needed to stabilize rapidly rotating neutron stars.
