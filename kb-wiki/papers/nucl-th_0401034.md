# Structure of Exotic Nuclei

> The progress in the modeling of exotic nuclei with an extreme neutron-to-proton ratio is discussed. Two topics are emphasized: (i) the quest for the universal microscopic nuclear energy density functional and (ii) the progress in the continuum shell model.

## Key claim

The paper argues that a universal microscopic nuclear energy density functional and the continuum shell model are the two key theoretical tools needed to describe exotic nuclei far from stability, but notes that extrapolating existing models to these systems is "very unreliable" due to weak binding and altered interactions.

## Method

The authors review two main theoretical approaches: (i) self-consistent Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using Skyrme-type energy density functionals, solved with local-scaling point transformations and parallel computing to produce mass tables; and (ii) the Gamow Shell Model (GSM), which uses the Berggren completeness relation containing Gamow (resonant) states and non-resonant continuum states to treat many-body correlations and continuum effects simultaneously in a complex-symmetric eigenvalue problem.

## Key numbers

- Self-consistent HFB models reproduce measured nuclear binding energies with an rms error of approximately 700 keV.
- The deformed even-even mass table was calculated in a single 24 wall-clock hour run (approximately 4,800 processor hours) using 200 processors.
- The GSM calculation for 19O used a discretized (10 points) d3/2 non-resonant continuum in the full sd space of Gamow states.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on self-consistent mean-field methods by Goricki (2002), Bender (2003), and Stoitsov (2003), and on global shell-model approaches by Caurier (2002), Honma (2002), and Langanke (2002, 2003). It also builds on the Gamow Shell Model formulation by Michel (2002, 2003). The paper enables future development of a universal nuclear energy density functional fitted to masses, radii, and giant vibrations, and applications of GSM to halo nuclei, particle-unstable states, and reactions of astrophysical interest.
