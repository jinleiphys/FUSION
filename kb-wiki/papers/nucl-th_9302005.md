# Can Plasma Scattering Mimic a Cosmological Red-Shift?

> We investigate the extent to which the scattering of light from plasma fluctuations can mimic a cosmological red-shift, as has been suggested by Wolf et al. A plasma model for the plasma structure function results in a spectrum of scattered light very different from that associated with a constant red-shift, implying that the "Wolf effect" cannot be involved to perturb the cosmological distance scale.

## Key claim

The scattering of light from realistic, causally consistent plasma fluctuations produces a spectrum very different from a constant cosmological redshift, meaning the "Wolf effect" cannot perturb the cosmological distance scale, although it may distort line shapes.

## Method

The authors model the scattering of photons from a gas of non-interacting quasiparticles (plasmons) using standard linear response theory, with the particle distribution described by Bose statistics and a dispersion relation \(E_k^2 = k^2 + m^2\). They compute the scattering kernel \(\tilde{\cal K}(q,q_0)\) for two limiting cases of the parameter \(\mu = m/T\) (relativistic \(\mu=1\) and non-relativistic \(\mu=10\)), and also consider an anisotropic dispersion relation with an asymmetry parameter \(a\) to model the effect of a magnetic field.

## Key numbers

- For an incoming photon with wavelength \(\lambda = 500\) nm and scattering angle \(\theta = 20^\circ\), the scattered line is redshifted and broadened significantly, with the broadening comparable to the shift.
- Line widths smaller than about 100 nm can be achieved only for \(\mu > 50\), but such large values suppress the scattered intensity by about a factor of \(e^{-\mu}\), corresponding to an increase of some 54 magnitudes for \(\mu = 50\).
- Standard models of quasars give \(n_e \lesssim 10^{10}~\mathrm{cm}^{-3}\) and \(T \sim 10^4\) K, implying \(\mu \lesssim 4 \times 10^{-6}\), the opposite extreme from the regime needed to mimic a cosmological redshift.

## Context

This work directly tests the proposal by Wolf (1989) and collaborators that scattering of light by a fluctuating medium (the "Wolf effect") could mimic a cosmological redshift, which had been invoked to explain unusual quasar emission features (Arp 1987). By replacing the earlier Gaussian, causality-violating scattering kernel with a realistic plasma model based on Bose statistics, the authors show the effect cannot produce a constant redshift. The results imply that any attempt to invoke plasma scattering to perturb cosmological distance scales would require either extreme anisotropy (of order \(10^3\) or more) or strongly non-relativistic conditions (\(\mu \gg 1\)), both of which are incompatible with standard quasar models (Rees, Netzer, and Ferland 1989).
