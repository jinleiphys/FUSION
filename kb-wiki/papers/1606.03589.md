# Chiral magnetic effect by synthetic gauge fields

> We study the dynamical generation of the chiral chemical potential in a Weyl metal constructed from a three-dimensional optical lattice and subject to synthetic gauge fields. By numerically solving the Boltzmann equation with the Berry curvature in the presence of parallel synthetic electric and magnetic fields, we find that the spectral flow and the ensuing chiral magnetic current emerge. We show that the spectral flow and the chiral chemical potential can be probed by time-of-flight imaging.

## Key claim
By numerically solving the collisionless Boltzmann equation with Berry curvature for a Weyl metal in an optical lattice under parallel synthetic electric and magnetic fields, the authors demonstrate that the chiral chemical potential is dynamically generated via spectral flow only when the dot product of the electric and magnetic fields is nonzero, and that this effect can be observed via time-of-flight imaging.

## Method
The authors constructed a three-dimensional optical lattice system with Weyl nodes using a Harper Hamiltonian stacked along the third direction, with synthetic gauge fields implemented via laser-assisted tunneling. They numerically solved the collisionless Boltzmann equation for the momentum distribution function using the constrained interpolation profile (CIP) scheme, incorporating Berry curvature corrections to the quasiparticle energy and equations of motion, with slip-free boundary conditions at the outer boundaries and fixed equilibrium distribution at inner boundaries near the Weyl nodes.

## Context
This work extends the theoretical understanding of the chiral magnetic effect, originally proposed by Vilenkin (1980) and Nielsen (1983) and applied to heavy-ion collisions by Kharzeev (2007) and Fukushima (2008), into the domain of ultracold atom systems with synthetic gauge fields. The study builds on the chiral kinetic theory developed by Stephanov (2012), Son (2012), and Chen (2012), and uses the optical lattice design from a prior proposal (PhysRevLett.114.225301). The results enable experimental investigation of anomaly-induced transport in controlled laboratory settings, with potential applications to heavy-ion collision experiments, neutron star physics, and early-universe neutrino physics.
