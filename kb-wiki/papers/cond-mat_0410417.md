# Universality in Few-body Systems with Large Scattering Length

> Particles with short-range interactions and a large scattering length have universal low-energy properties that do not depend on the details of their structure or their interactions at short distances. In the 2-body sector, the universal properties are familiar and depend only on the scattering length a. In the 3-body sector for identical bosons, the universal properties include the existence of a sequence of shallow 3-body bound states called "Efimov states" and log-periodic dependence of scattering observables on the energy and the scattering length. The spectrum of Efimov states in the limit a -> +/- infinity is characterized by an asymptotic discrete scaling symmetry that is the signature of renormalization group flow to a limit cycle. In this review, we present a thorough treatment of universality for the system of three identical bosons and we summarize the universal information that is currently available for other 3-body systems. Our basic tools are the hyperspherical formalism to provide qualitative insights, Efimov's radial laws for deriving the constraints from unitarity, and effective field theory for quantitative calculations. We also discuss topics on the frontiers of universality, including its extension to systems with four or more particles and the systematic calculation of deviations from universality.

## Key claim

The paper argues that for systems with short-range interactions and a large scattering length, universal low-energy properties in the three-body sector depend not only on the scattering length \(a\) but also on an additional three-body parameter \(\kappa_*\), which the abstract glosses over by describing universality as depending only on the scattering length. The authors explicitly relax the traditional definition of "universal" to include dependence on this three-body parameter, which persists even in the resonant limit \(a \to \pm\infty\).

## Method

The authors use three complementary theoretical tools: the hyperspherical formalism for qualitative insights into the three-body problem, Efimov's radial laws to derive constraints from unitarity, and effective field theory for quantitative calculations of low-energy observables. They focus primarily on the system of three identical bosons, deriving a generalized Skorniakov-Ter-Martirosian integral equation within the effective field theory framework, and apply renormalization group concepts to show that the three-body system exhibits a limit cycle with discrete scaling symmetry.

## Key numbers

- The ratio of binding energies of successive Efimov states in the resonant limit \(a = \pm\infty\) approaches \(E_T^{(n+1)}/E_T^{(n)} \to 1/515.03\) as \(n \to +\infty\), independent of particle mass or interaction details.
- The discrete scaling factor \(s_0\) for three identical bosons is approximately \(s_0 \approx 1.00624\), which appears in the log-periodic function \(\sin\left(\frac{1}{2} s_0 \ln[m E_T/(\hbar^2 \kappa_*^2)]\right) = 0\) describing the asymptotic Efimov spectrum.
- For a two-body bound state near threshold with positive scattering length \(a\), the binding energy is \(E_D = \hbar^2/(m a^2)\), with corrections suppressed by powers of \(r_0/a\).
- The low-energy limit of the elastic cross section for identical bosons is \(\sigma(E) \to 8\pi a^2\) as \(E \to 0\).
- The van der Waals length scale is defined as \(\ell_{\rm vdW} = (m C_6/\hbar^2)^{1/4}\), which sets the natural low-energy length scale for atomic systems.

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Efimov (1970), who first discovered the Efimov effect for three identical bosons with large scattering length, and builds on earlier universal results for two-body systems and dilute Bose-Einstein condensates (Andersen, 2003; Hu and Pi, 1959). The paper provides a comprehensive treatment of universality in three-body systems, summarizing known results for identical bosons, distinguishable particles, fermions, and systems with unequal masses or scattering lengths. It enables future work on extending universality to systems with four or more particles, calculating systematic deviations from universality (e.g., effective range corrections), and applying these ideas to ultracold atomic gases and nuclear physics systems with fortuitously large scattering lengths.
