# Three-body effects in the Hoyle-state decay

> We use a sequential $R$-matrix model to describe the breakup of the Hoyle state into three $α$ particles via the ground state of $^8\mathrm{Be}$. It is shown that even in a sequential picture, features resembling a direct breakup branch appear in the phase-space distribution of the $α$ particles. We construct a toy model to describe the Coulomb interaction in the three-body final state and its effects on the decay spectrum are investigated. The framework is also used to predict the phase-space distribution of the $α$ particles emitted in a direct breakup of the Hoyle state and the possibility of interference between a direct and sequential branch is discussed. Our numerical results are compared to the current upper limit on the direct decay branch determined in recent experiments.

## Key claim

Even in a purely sequential decay picture of the Hoyle state through the ground state of ⁸Be, features resembling a direct breakup branch appear in the phase-space distribution of the α particles; the sequential model predicts a low-intensity tail extending from the ⁸Be ground-state peak toward the apex of the Dalitz plot, which could be misinterpreted as evidence for a direct decay branch.

## Method

The authors employ a sequential R-matrix model (Model I) to describe the three-body breakup of the Hoyle state (the first excited 0⁺ state in ¹²C) into three α particles via the ground state of ⁸Be. They develop two extensions: Model II, which applies a constant-distance correction for final-state Coulomb interactions (FSCI) by replacing the primary-channel penetrability with a product of penetrabilities for each α pair; and Model III, which uses a variable distance for the FSCI correction derived from the energy-dependent lifetime of the intermediate ⁸Be system, calculated from the resonant phase shift. The decay weights are computed from symmetrized amplitudes and compared to experimental upper limits on the direct decay branch.

## Key numbers

- The ⁸Be ground state has energy E = 91.84 ± 0.04 keV and observed width Γ_obs = 5.57 ± 0.25 × 10⁻³ keV (channel radius 4.5 fm).
- For the Hoyle state decay, only 57 ± 2% of the ⁸Be ground-state strength appears in the observed ground-state peak (for channel radius a₂ = 4.5 fm); with a larger radius a₂ = 7.0 fm the peak area increases to 86 ± 1%.
- Fractional intensity of decays with E₂₃ > E_gs + 50 keV: Model I gives 1.7 × 10⁻⁵, Model II gives 3.4 × 10⁻⁴, Model III gives 4.0 × 10⁻³; the Faddeev calculation (Ishikawa 2017) gives 1.4 × 10⁻⁴.
- Experimental upper limits on the direct decay branch are 4.7 × 10⁻⁴ (Smith 2017) and 4.2 × 10⁻⁴ (Dell'Aquila 2017) at 95% confidence level.
- The Hoyle state decay Q-value is 379 keV; the Coulomb barrier is approximately 35 fm wide.

## Context

This work extends the sequential R-matrix approach previously applied to other ¹²C states (the 1⁺ state at 12.71 MeV studied by Fynbo (2003) and Kirsebom (2010), the 2⁺ state at 16.11 MeV studied by Laursen (2016), and the 2⁻ state at 16.57 MeV studied by Cockburn (1970)) to the Hoyle state decay. It addresses shortcomings of simpler models that treat the sequential branch with a δ-function for the ⁸Be resonance and assume uniform phase-space for the direct branch, ignoring Coulomb interactions. The results are compared to the Faddeev-equation calculations of Ishikawa (2014, 2017) and to experimental upper limits from Smith (2017) and Dell'Aquila (2017). The work enables more systematic treatment of three-body Coulomb effects in low-energy α-particle decays and highlights the need for improved models of tunneling velocities through wide Coulomb barriers.
