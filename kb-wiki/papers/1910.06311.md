# Quantum Tomography Measures Entanglement in Collider Reactions

> Entanglement in high energy and and nuclear reactions is receiving great attention. A proper description of these reactions uses density matrices, and the express of entanglement in terms of {\it separability}. Quantum tomography bypasses field-theoretic formalism to determine density matrices in terms of experimental observables. We review recent work applying quantum tomography to practical experimental data analysis. We discuss the relation between separability, as defined in quantum information science, and factorization, as defined in high energy physics. When factorization applies, it comes from using separable probes, which tomographically determine separable projections of entangled density matrices.

## Key claim

Quantum tomography can reconstruct density matrices from collider data without field-theoretic formalism, and the paper argues that the concept of "factorization" in perturbative QCD is equivalent to "separability conditional upon a particular probe," meaning that factorizable processes are those where entanglement is present but made unobservable by the choice of probe.

## Method

The authors review the mathematical framework of quantum tomography, where a density matrix ρ is expanded in an orthonormal basis of Hermitian operators Gℓ, with each expansion coefficient being the expectation value ⟨Gℓ⟩ = tr(ρGℓ) that is directly measurable. They apply this to inclusive hadronic reactions, showing that the cross section can be written as dσ = tr(ρlepρX), where the lepton probe ρlep and hadronic density matrix ρX are both positive matrices. The reconstruction proceeds by measuring projections of the cross section and using the "mirror trick" to obtain the density matrix of the system from the same subspace probed.

## Key numbers

- The entropy S of a density matrix ρ is given by S = -tr(ρ log(ρ)), with minimum S=0 for a pure state and maximum S = log(D) for a D×D matrix with no information.
- For a rank-1 density matrix (pure state), the expectation value reduces to ⟨A⟩ → ⟨ψ|A|ψ⟩, which is the exceptional case of wave-function-based quantum mechanics.
- The paper references a prototype example (Martens, Ralston, and Takaki, 2019) where computer code goes from lab-frame 4-vectors to density matrix elements "in seconds" using a convex optimization algorithm with one global minimum.

## Context

This work extends the quantum tomography formalism originally developed by Fano (1957) and later revived in quantum information science, applying it to collider physics for the first time in a practical data-analysis context. The paper connects the quantum information concept of separability, defined by Werner (1989), to the perturbative QCD concept of factorization, arguing that factorization theorems describe systems that are separable conditional upon a particular probe. This enables future experimental measurements of entanglement entropy and other quantum observables from collider data, bypassing traditional structure functions and parton model assumptions.
