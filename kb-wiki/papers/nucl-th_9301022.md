# Geometry, Scaling and Universality in the Mass Distributions in Heavy Ion Collisions

> Various features of the mass yields in heavy ion collisions are studied. The mass yields are discussed in terms of iterative one dimensional discrete maps. These maps are shown to produce orbits for a monomer or for a nucleus which generate the mass yields and the distribution of cluster sizes. Simple Malthusian dynamics and non-linear Verhulst dynamics are used to illustrate the approach. Nuclear cobwebbing, attractors of the dynamics, and Lyapanov exponents are discussed for the mass distribution. The self-similar property of the Malthusian orbit offers a new variable for the study of scale invariance using power moments of the mass distribution. Correlation lengths, exponents and dimensions associated with scaling relations are developed. Fourier transforms of the mass distribution are used to obtain power spectra which are investigated for a $1/f^β$ behavior.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that mass yields in heavy ion collisions can be characterized by iterative one-dimensional discrete maps, with the Malthusian geometric model serving as an approximate representation of more complex soluble fragmentation models, though the authors acknowledge that neither the x-model nor the linear Malthus map fully reproduces features such as the flat "shoulder" region near large cluster sizes.

## Method

The authors relate the ensemble-averaged mass distribution from their previously developed soluble fragmentation model to iterative one-dimensional maps, where cluster size k serves as the discrete iteration index and mass m_k corresponds to population size. They analyze experimental data using linear Malthusian maps (m_{k+1} = r m_k) and nonlinear Verhulst maps (m_{k+1} = R_V m_k / (m_k + K_V)), fitting these to mass yield data from proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions. Power moments of the mass distribution are computed for varying bin sizes L, and Fourier transforms of the mass distribution are used to obtain power spectra.

## Key numbers

- For the x-model, the mean cluster distribution is given by <n_k> = (x/k) * (A!/(A-k)!) * (Gamma(x+A-k)/Gamma(x+A)), where A is total nucleon number and x is a tuning parameter ranging from 0 to infinity.
- For p + Au data, a linear Malthus map with k-dependent coefficient r(k) = a + b k, where a = 0.9833 and b = 0.000333, fits the whole region with geometric mean r-bar of 1.016 and minimum n_k at k approximately 85.
- For the Verhulst map, the bifurcation condition is m_s/m_1 = e^(2-zeta) + 1, where zeta = -ln(K_V/R_V).
- For the x-model with x = m = 1, the second-order power moment P_2(L,1) = (1/2)[(A+1)/A + L/A].
- For the x-model with x = m = 2, P_2(L,2) = (1/3)[(A+2)/A + 3L/(A+1)].
- For the x-model with x = m = 3, P_2(L,3) = (1/4)[(A+3)/A + 3(2A^2+3A-1)L/(A(A+1)(A+2)) - 3L^3/(A(A+1)(A+2))].
- For large A with x << A, the leading order L/A dependence in P_2(L,x) is approximately P_2(L,x) = 1/(x+1) + x/(A(x+1)) + C_2 L/A, where C_2 is approximately x/2 + x^2 Gamma(x)/A^x.
- The correlation length zeta_2 = 2(A+x)/((x+1)C_2).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously developed soluble fragmentation model (Mekjian 1991, Lee and Mekjian 1992) by reinterpreting the mass distribution through the lens of iterative maps from population dynamics and nonlinear dynamics. The approach connects nuclear fragmentation to concepts from population genetics via the Ewens sampling formula (Ewens 1972) and to fractal geometry and scaling theory as discussed in Schroeder (1991). The paper enables future studies of mass distributions using Fourier power spectra to search for 1/f^beta noise patterns, and suggests that the self-similar property of the Malthusian orbit provides a new variable for studying scale invariance in fragmentation data.
