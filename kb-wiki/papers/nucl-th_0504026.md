# Level density of a Fermion gas: average growth, fluctuations, universality

> It has been shown by H. Bethe more than 70 years ago that the number of excited states of a Fermi gas grows, at high excitation energies $Q$, like the exponential of the square root of $Q$. This result takes into account only the average density of single particle (SP) levels near the Fermi energy. It ignores two important effects, namely the discreteness of the SP spectrum, and its fluctuations. We show that the discreteness of the SP spectrum gives rise to smooth finite--$Q$ corrections. Mathematically, these corrections are associated to the problem of partitions of an integer. On top of the smooth growth of the many--body density of states there are, generically, oscillations. An explicit expression of these oscillations is given. Their properties strongly depend on the regular or chaotic nature of the SP motion. In particular, we analyze their typical size, temperature dependence and probability distribution, with emphasis on their universal aspects.

# Level density of a Fermion gas: average growth, fluctuations, universality

## Key claim

The many-body density of states of a noninteracting Fermi gas receives smooth finite-excitation-energy corrections from the discreteness of the single-particle spectrum (mathematically equivalent to integer partitions), plus superimposed oscillatory corrections whose statistical properties are universal at low temperatures and depend on whether the single-particle motion is regular or chaotic. The author shows that the oscillatory corrections enter as an additional term in the exponent of the density of states, not as an effective level-density parameter.

## Method

The author derives an explicit expression for the many-body density of states by performing a saddle-point approximation of an inverse Laplace transform of the many-body density, incorporating the fluctuating part of the energy of the gas at chemical potential and temperature. For a perfectly regular (1D harmonic oscillator) single-particle spectrum, the exact many-body density is obtained by mapping the problem to integer partitions, yielding an asymptotic expansion (Hardy-Ramanujan-Rademacher formula). For arbitrary spectra, the oscillatory corrections are expressed semiclassically as a sum over classical periodic orbits of the mean-field potential, and their statistical properties are analyzed through the variance of the shell correction term.

## Key numbers

- Bethe's formula: $\rmb (A,Q) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{48}\ Q} \exp \left( 2 \sqrt{a \ Q} \right)$ with $a = \pi^2 \rhob/6$, where $\rhob$ is the average single-particle density of states near the Fermi energy.
- For a Woods-Saxon-like mean field: $a \approx A/15$ MeV$^{-1}$ using $\ef \approx 37$ MeV, but experimental neutron resonance data give a coefficient closer to $1/8$ MeV$^{-1}$.
- For a 1D harmonic oscillator spectrum, the exact asymptotic expansion of $\log(\rmb^{\scriptscriptstyle HO}/\rhob)$ includes terms: $\sqrt{ \frac{2}{3} \pi^2 \rhob \ Q } - \log \left( \sqrt{48} \ \rhob Q \right) - \frac{\pi^2 + 72}{24 \sqrt{6} \pi} (\rhob Q)^{-\frac{1}{2}} - \left(\frac{3}{4 \pi^2}- \frac{1}{24} \right) (\rhob Q)^{-1} + \mathcal{O} ((\rhob Q)^{-\frac{3}{2}})$.
- For chaotic systems at low temperatures $T \ll T_\delta$: $\langle \scat^2 \rangle = c_4 T/(2 T_\delta)$ with $c_4 = 0.0609...$.
- For chaotic systems at intermediate temperatures $T_\delta \ll T \ll T_c$: $\langle \scat^2 \rangle = c_3/\beta$ with $c_3 = 0.1023...$ and $\beta = 1$ (2) for systems with (without) time-reversal invariance.
- For chaotic systems at high temperatures $T \gg T_c$: $\langle \scat^2 \rangle = \langle \ect^2 (\mu,0) \rangle [ 1 - 8 \ {\rm e}^{-T/T_c} ]/ T^2$.
- The ratio $g = E_c /\deltab$ is much larger than one; for a three-dimensional cavity, $g \sim A^{2/3}$.
- The partition function $p(200) = 397999029388$ (computed by P. MacMahon in 1918).

## Context

This work extends Bethe's (1936) original formula for the level density of a Fermi gas, which considered only the average single-particle density of states. It builds on earlier shell-correction calculations by Rosenzweig (1957) and by Bohr and Mottelson (1969) that introduced the back-shifted Bethe formula, and on the phenomenological models of Gilbert and Cameron (1965), Ignatyuk et al., and others. The author's approach connects the problem to integer partitions (Hardy and Ramanujan, 1918; Rademacher) and to semiclassical periodic-orbit theory (Gutzwiller; Berry and Tabor). The results enable a theoretical framework for understanding the oscillatory structure of the level-density parameter $a$ observed in neutron resonance data, and distinguish between chaotic and integrable single-particle dynamics in their effect on many-body level density fluctuations.
