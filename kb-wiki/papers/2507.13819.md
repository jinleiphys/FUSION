# Comparison of variational quantum eigensolvers in light nuclei

> Quantum computing is one of the most promising technologies of the near future, and the simulation of quantum many-body systems is a natural application. In this work, we present classical simulations of the ground states of light atomic nuclei within the $p$ shell, from $^{6}$He to $^{10}$B, calculated within the nuclear shell model. We compare the performance of two leading variational quantum eigensolver algorithms: the Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCC) and the Adaptive Derivative-Assembled Pseudo-Trotter (ADAPT) methods, introducing a new metric to quantify the use of quantum resources in each simulation. We find that Slater determinants are the most useful reference states for both approaches. Our analysis suggests that ADAPT is more efficient for nuclei close to magic numbers, while UCC tends to require fewer resources toward the mid shell. This work lays the groundwork for robust benchmarking of quantum algorithms in nuclear structure studies.

## Key claim

The paper claims that ADAPT is more efficient for nuclei close to magic numbers, while UCC tends to require fewer resources toward the mid shell, but this conclusion is qualified by the finding that the performance of UCC is roughly independent of the reference state whereas ADAPT's performance depends strongly on the choice of reference state, with some reference states requiring twice as many operations as others.

## Method

The authors performed classical simulations of ground states of eleven p-shell nuclei (from 6He to 10B) using the nuclear shell model with the Cohen-Kurath interaction, comparing the Unitary Coupled Cluster (UCC) and Adaptive Derivative-Assembled Pseudo-Trotter (ADAPT) variational quantum eigensolvers. They introduced a new metric called "total operations" that counts the number of unitary operators acting on the reference state throughout the entire algorithm, accounting for both the number of function calls and the number of layers in the ansatz. For UCC, they averaged over multiple minimizations with random operator orderings and random initial parameters, while for ADAPT they performed single runs for each reference state.

## Key numbers

- The many-body Hilbert space dimensions range from dim(H)=5 for 6He and 6Be to dim(H)=84 for the mid-shell nucleus 10B
- The operator pool size Delta ranges from 8 for 6He and 6Be to 145 for 8Be, 10Be, 10C, and 10B
- For 8Be, UCC required average total operations of approximately (5-6)x10^5, while ADAPT required approximately (6-12)x10^5 depending on the reference state
- For 8B, UCC required approximately (4-5)x10^5 total operations, while ADAPT required approximately (1-2)x10^5
- For 10Li, UCC required approximately (4-5)x10^4 total operations, while ADAPT required approximately 2400-2700 total operations
- The convergence criterion was a relative error epsilon = |E_VQE - E_NSM|/|E_NSM| <= 10^-4
- For 8Be, the ground state energy from classical diagonalization was E = -30.29 MeV

## Context

This work extends previous studies of VQE methods in nuclear structure, particularly building on the UCC approach applied to nuclei by Dumitrescu (2018), Stetcu (2022), and Sarma (2023), and the ADAPT approach applied to nuclei by Romero (2022) and Perez-Obiol (2024). The paper addresses the gap identified in the literature where an equal-footing comparison of different VQEs applied to the same nuclei was missing, as noted by Oriel Kiss (2021) who compared various strategies within UCC but not across different VQE methods. This work enables future benchmarking of quantum algorithms for nuclear structure studies by providing a consistent metric (total operations) and systematic comparison across multiple p-shell nuclei.
