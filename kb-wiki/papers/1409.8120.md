# Extracting femtoscopic radii in the presence of significant additional correlation sources

> The Large Hadron Collider has provided large amounts of data on collisions of small systems, such as proton-proton and proton-lead at unprecedented collision energies. Their space-time size and structure can be inferred from the measurement of the femtoscopic correlations for pairs of identical particles. The analysis is complicated by the presence of significant additional sources of two-particle correlations, which influence the correlation function in the region of the femtoscopic effect. In this work we use p-Pb events generated in a model that includes such additional correlation sources to characterize them and propose a robust method of taking them into account in the extraction of the femtoscopic information.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that femtoscopic radii can be reliably extracted from small collision systems (p–Pb) even when significant non-femtoscopic correlations are present, provided that both the magnitude and the three-dimensional shape of the background are constrained using a Monte Carlo simulation; the remaining systematic uncertainty from the method itself is 3–8%.

## Method

The authors used the EPOS 3.076 event generator to simulate p–Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV, producing three types of correlation functions: a pure quantum-statistics plus final-state interaction (QS+FSI) function \(C_{\rm QS}\), a pure background function \(C_{\rm B}\) containing only non-femtoscopic correlations, and a full function \(C_{\rm F}\) containing both. They extracted femtoscopic radii by fitting Gaussian (GGG) and exponential-Gaussian-exponential (EGE) functional forms to \(C_{\rm QS}\) as a reference, then applied the same forms with an additional multiplicative background term \(\Omega(\mathbf{q})\) to \(C_{\rm F}\), testing three scenarios for constraining \(\Omega\) from \(C_{\rm B}\): fixing only the width, fixing both width and magnitude, or fixing the full three-dimensional shape. All correlation functions were analyzed in three dimensions using a spherical harmonic decomposition in the Longitudinally Co-Moving System (LCMS).

## Key numbers

- The EPOS model was run for p–Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV, matching the LHC run 1 energy.
- Correlation functions were calculated for seven \(k_{\rm T}\) ranges: 0.2–0.3, 0.3–0.4, 0.4–0.5, 0.5–0.6, 0.6–0.7, 0.7–0.8, and 0.8–1.0 GeV/\(c\).
- Without any background treatment, extracted radii were underpredicted by up to 30% relative to the reference.
- With the full three-dimensional background shape fixed, Gaussian fit radii deviated from the reference by 4–8% in the out direction, 6–8% in the side direction, and no more than 3% in the long direction.
- The overall systematic uncertainty of the proposed method was estimated at 3–8%.
- A fitting range maximum of 1.0 GeV/\(c\) was sufficient to obtain stable radii for all directions, all \(k_{\rm T}\), and both functional forms; using a too-narrow range could introduce systematic deviations of up to 30%.

## Context

This work addresses the challenge of extracting femtoscopic source sizes in small collision systems (pp and p–Pb) at the LHC, where significant non-femtoscopic correlations from energy-momentum conservation and mini-jet phenomena contaminate the Bose-Einstein signal. It extends prior experimental approaches used by ALICE (Aamodt 2011), CMS (Khachatryan 2011), and PHENIX (Adare 2014), which relied on double-ratio or background parametrization methods, by systematically testing the robustness of these procedures using a controlled Monte Carlo framework (EPOS 3.076). The results enable more reliable femtoscopic measurements in small systems, which are critical for distinguishing between hydrodynamic collective expansion models (Bozek 2012, Bozek 2013) and Color-Glass Condensate predictions (Dusling 2012, Dusling 2013, Bzdak 2013) that differ by 30–50% in predicted system size.
