# Generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck approach to the thermodynamics of quark-hadron matter

> We present a unified approach to the transition from hadronic matter to quark matter where hadrons are treated as bound states of quarks which dissociate at high densities due to quark Pauli blocking. The newly developed approach makes use of a cluster virial expansion formulated in terms of a generalized $Φ$-derivable approach to multi-quark correlations with bound and continuum states in their spectrum encoded in hadron phase shifts. Our model can be used to obtain thermodynamic functions not only at zero and small chemical potentials, where they are consistent with lattice QCD simulations, but also at large chemical potentials where lattice QCD simulations have the sign problem. By applying a reaction-kinetic criterion for the chemical freeze-out of multi-quark clusters in heavy-ion collisions, we demonstrate that the chemical freeze-out coincides with their Mott transition. The approach can be applied to study the effects of the QCD transition on primordial black hole formation in the early Universe and on hybrid neutron star formation in supernova explosions and binary neutron star mergers.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the chemical freeze-out of hadrons in heavy-ion collisions coincides with their Mott dissociation (the vanishing of hadron binding energy due to quark Pauli blocking), using a reaction-kinetic criterion where the expansion rate of the fireball exceeds the reaction rate of each hadron species. This claim is conditional on the assumption that the geometric Povh-Hufner scaling law for hadron-hadron cross sections, with medium-dependent hadron radii, remains robust in the hot hadron gas environment.

## Method

The authors develop a generalized Beth-Uhlenbeck approach to the cluster virial expansion of QCD matter, where the thermodynamic potential is separated into a quark-gluon plasma sector described by a Polyakov-loop Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (PNJL) model with perturbative QCD corrections, and a Mott hadron resonance gas sector describing hadrons as multi-quark clusters (bound and continuum states) whose phase shifts encode the spectrum. The density and entropy density are expressed in terms of Polyakov-loop modified distribution functions and phase shifts, with the "no sea" approximation removing divergent vacuum contributions. The chemical freeze-out criterion is applied by comparing the Hubble expansion rate of the fireball, estimated from entropy conservation, with the reaction rate obtained from the relaxation time approximation using the Povh-Hufner geometric scaling law for cross sections.

## Key numbers

- Pseudocritical temperature for chiral symmetry restoration: \( T_c = 156.5 \) MeV, taken from lattice QCD simulations (HotQCD Collaboration, 2018)
- Temperature range for equation of state computation: \( T \in [1; 1300] \) MeV
- Parameter \(\lambda = 0.197 \) GeV\(^2\) for the Povh-Hufner scaling law \(\sigma_{ij} = \lambda \langle r_i^2 \rangle \langle r_j^2 \rangle\), adjusted to describe reactions at zero temperature and baryon density
- The collision time \(\tau_i^{-1}\) for hadrons jumps by 3-4 orders of magnitude at the chiral restoration temperature, while the Hubble expansion rate \(H_{\rm exp}\) behaves smoothly
- The pion radius diverges as \(\langle r^2\rangle_\pi^{1/2} \sim |T - T_{\rm Mott, \pi}|^{-1/2}\) near its Mott temperature, and the pion-quark coupling scales as \(g_{\pi qq} \sim E_{B,\pi}(T)^{1/4}\) and \(g_{\pi qq} \sim |T - T_{\rm Mott, \pi}|^{1/2}\)

## Context

This work extends the cluster virial expansion approach of Blaschke (2023) to provide a unified description of hadronic and quark-gluon plasma phases of QCD, building on earlier formulations of the Mott transition idea by Blaschke (2011). The approach is consistent with lattice QCD simulations at finite temperature and small chemical potentials from Borsanyi et al. (2014, 2021) and the HotQCD Collaboration (2018). The method enables applications to primordial black hole formation at the QCD transition in the early Universe (Gonin, 2025) and to hybrid neutron star formation in supernova explosions and binary neutron star mergers, where the open question of whether the transition is first-order or a crossover can be addressed with this microphysical approach.
