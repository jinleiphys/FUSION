# Nuclear effects are relevant to the calorimetric reconstruction of neutrino energy

> As the calorimetric method of neutrino-energy reconstruction is generally considered to be largely insensitive to nuclear effects, its application seems to be an effective way for reducing systematic uncertainties in oscillation experiments. To verify the validity of this opinion, we quantitatively study the sensitivity of the calorimetric energy reconstruction to the effect of final-state interactions in an ideal detector and in a realistic scenario. We find that when particles escaping detection carry away a non-negligible fraction of neutrino energy, the calorimetric reconstruction method becomes sensitive to nuclear effects which, in turn, affects the outcome of the oscillation analysis. These findings suggest that the best strategy for reduction of systematic uncertainties in future neutrino-oscillation studies---such as the Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment---is to increase their sensitivity to particles of low energy. The ambitious precision goals appear also to require an extensive development of theoretical models capable of providing an accurate predictions for exclusive cross sections of well-controlled uncertainties.

## Key claim

The calorimetric method of neutrino-energy reconstruction is generally considered insensitive to nuclear effects, but this holds only when detector effects are negligible; when particles escaping detection carry away a non-negligible fraction of neutrino energy, the method becomes sensitive to final-state interactions, which can bias the extracted oscillation parameters.

## Method

The study used the GENIE 2.8.0 Monte Carlo generator with the nuT package to simulate neutrino interactions on a carbon target, including quasielastic scattering, resonant pion production, and deep-inelastic scattering. The analysis compared reconstructed energy distributions and oscillation parameter confidence regions obtained with and without accounting for final-state interactions, both in an ideal detector scenario and in a realistic scenario with finite energy resolutions and detection thresholds comparable to those of the Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE). The chi-squared calculations and confidence regions were implemented following the methods of Coloma (2012) and Coloma (2013).

## Key numbers

- For quasielastic scattering at true neutrino energy 0.6 GeV with no detector effects, the mean reconstructed energies with and without final-state interactions differ by approximately 1 MeV.
- For quasielastic scattering at true neutrino energy 0.6 GeV with detector effects included, the mean reconstructed energies with and without final-state interactions differ by approximately 25 MeV.
- In the realistic detector scenario, if proton and neutron detection thresholds were lowered to 25 MeV (30 MeV), the difference between extracted and true oscillation parameters would fall within the 1-sigma (2-sigma) confidence region.
- A single undetected pion with a detection threshold of 100 MeV would cause an underestimation of reconstructed neutrino energy by up to approximately 240 MeV (threshold plus pion mass).
- The idealized experiment considered a narrow-band off-axis beam peaked at approximately 600 MeV, collecting data for 5 years at 750 kW beam intensity, with near detector fiducial mass 1.0 kt at 1.0 km and far detector fiducial mass 22.5 kt at 295.0 km.

## Context

This work extends the author's previous studies (Ankowski 2015) that showed finite detection capabilities affect calorimetric energy reconstruction, but which did not consider nuclear model uncertainties. It contradicts the common assumption that the calorimetric method is largely insensitive to nuclear effects, which had been argued by Mosel (2015). The findings point to the need for lowering detection thresholds and developing more accurate theoretical models for exclusive cross sections, particularly for future experiments such as DUNE.
