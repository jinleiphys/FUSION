# Response Functions from Integral Transforms with a Lorentz Kernel

> We propose to calculate inelastic response functions from the inversion of their integral transform with a Lorentz kernel. The transform can be obtained using bound-state type methods. Thus one does not need to solve the much more complicated continuum problem with many open channels. Contrary to other integral transforms considered in the literature, the inversion leads to results of excellent accuracy and stability. This is explicitly shown for the longitudinal deuteron response.

## Key claim

The authors propose that inelastic response functions can be accurately and stably obtained by inverting an integral transform with a Lorentz kernel, avoiding the need to solve the many-body continuum problem; the method works provided the transform is computed using bound-state-type methods and the inversion is performed with a sufficiently large basis (N >= 10) and appropriate sigma_I values (around 10 MeV for few-body systems).

## Method

The response function R(omega) at constant momentum transfer is obtained by inverting its integral transform with a Lorentz kernel, where the transform Phi(sigma_R, sigma_I) is computed by solving a Schrodinger-like equation with a source term that vanishes at large distances like a bound state. The transform is evaluated for the longitudinal deuteron response at q_cm^2 = 5 fm^-2 using radial equations for the complex functions phi_{L,jl}, and the inversion is performed by expanding R(omega) in a set of basis functions chi_n(omega) and determining the coefficients from a best fit to Phi(sigma_R, sigma_I) at many sigma_R points for fixed sigma_I.

## Key numbers

- The longitudinal deuteron response was calculated at momentum transfer q_cm^2 = 5 fm^-2.
- For sigma_I = 10 MeV, the relative error of the transform Phi(sigma_R, sigma_I) was up to 20% for sigma_I = 5 MeV.
- The inversion used N = 14 basis functions for the total response and N = 10 for separate multipole contributions.
- Typical sigma_I values of about 10 MeV are appropriate for few-body responses, as these are less than the widths of typical structures in the response.
- For sigma_R < 200 MeV and sigma_I about 10 MeV, the required hyperspherical harmonic K_max values are quite acceptable.

## Context

This work extends earlier efforts by Efros (1985), Carlson and Schiavilla (1992), and others (Stieltjes 1993) who investigated obtaining response functions from inversion of Stieltjes and Laplace transforms, which suffered from poor uniqueness and stability especially in the quasielastic peak region. The Lorentz kernel method enables realistic response calculations for 4-body systems and considerably simplifies 3-body calculations, as it avoids the need to sum many breakup contributions that would be required in conventional continuum-state approaches like those of Gloeckle for 3N systems or the simplified S-wave calculation by MeT.
