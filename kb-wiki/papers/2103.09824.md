# Implications of Lithium to Oxygen AMS-02 spectra on our understanding of cosmic-ray diffusion

> We analyze recent AMS-02 comic-ray measurements of Lithium, Beryllium, Boron, Carbon, Nitrogen and Oxygen. The emphasis of the analysis is on systematic uncertainties related to propagation and nuclear cross sections. To investigate the uncertainties in the propagation scenario, we consider five different frameworks, differing with respect to the inclusion of a diffusion break at a few GV, the presence of reacceleration, and the presence of a break in the injection spectra of primaries. For each framework we fit the diffusion equations of cosmic rays and explore the parameter space with Monte Carlo methods. At the same time, the impact of the uncertainties in the nuclear production cross sections of secondaries is explicitly considered and included in the fit through the use of nuisance parameters. We find that all the considered frameworks are able to provide a good fit. In particular, two competing scenarios, one including a break in diffusion but no reacceleration and the other with reacceleration but no break in diffusion are both allowed. The inclusion of cross-section uncertainties is, however, crucial, to this result. Thus, at the moment, these uncertainties represent a fundamental systematic preventing a deeper understanding of the properties of CR propagation. We find, nonetheless, that the slope of diffusion at intermediate rigidities is robustly constrained in the range $δ\simeq0.45-0.5$ in models without convection, or $δ\simeq0.4-0.5$ if convection is included in the fit. Furthermore, we find that the use of the AMS-02 Beryllium data provides a lower limit on the vertical size of the Galactic propagation halo of $z_\mathrm{h}\gtrsim3$ kpc.

## Key claim

The analysis finds that two competing propagation scenarios, one with a break in diffusion but no reacceleration, and the other with reacceleration but no break in diffusion, are both able to provide a good fit to AMS-02 data, but only when the systematic uncertainties in nuclear production cross sections are explicitly included in the fit; without these uncertainties, the degeneracy between scenarios would be artificially broken.

## Method

The authors fit the cosmic-ray diffusion equation using the Galprop code to five different propagation frameworks that differ in whether they include a diffusion break at a few GV, reacceleration via Alfven waves, and a break in the injection spectra of primary nuclei. They simultaneously fit propagation parameters and nuclear cross-section uncertainties using Monte Carlo scanning with MultiNest, treating cross-section normalizations and low-energy slopes as nuisance parameters for the dominant fragmentation reactions of carbon and oxygen into lithium, beryllium, boron, carbon, and nitrogen. The fits use AMS-02 data on the absolute fluxes of carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen, and the ratios B/C, Li/C, and Be/B.

## Key numbers

* The diffusion slope at intermediate rigidities is robustly constrained to delta = 0.45–0.5 in models without convection, or delta = 0.4–0.5 if convection is included.
* AMS-02 Beryllium data provide a lower limit on the vertical size of the Galactic propagation halo of z_h greater than or approximately 3 kpc.
* The diffusion coefficient is normalized to D_0 at a reference rigidity of 4 GV.
* The first diffusion break, if included, is typically in the range of 1–10 GV; a second break is included around R_D,1 approximately 300 GV.
* Cross-section nuisance parameters for normalization are scanned in the range [0.1, 10] for Li, Be, and B, and [0.5, 1.5] for C and N; slope parameters are scanned in [-0.3, 0.3].

## Context

This work extends recent analyses of AMS-02 nuclei data by Genolini (2019), Weinrich (2020), Evoli (2019), and others, who have stressed the importance of nuclear cross-section uncertainties for secondary cosmic rays. The key advance is the simultaneous exploration of propagation and cross-section parameter spaces in a global fit, which shows that cross-section uncertainties are a fundamental systematic preventing a deeper understanding of cosmic-ray propagation properties. The results enable future work to break the degeneracy between diffusion-break and reacceleration scenarios, particularly through more precise measurements of the beryllium isotope Be-10, whose lifetime of approximately 1 Myr is similar to the propagation time scale of cosmic-ray nuclei.
