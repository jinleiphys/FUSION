# Numerical solution of NLO Q^2 evolution equations for spin-dependent structure functions

> Numerical solution of DGLAP $Q^2$ evolution equations is studied for polarized parton distributions by using a ``brute-force" method. NLO contributions to splitting functions are recently calculated,and they are included in our analysis. Numerical results in polarized parton distributions and in the structure function $g_1$ are shown. In particular, we discuss how numerical accuracy depends on number of steps in the variable $x$ and in $Q^2$.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a "brute-force" numerical method for solving the NLO DGLAP evolution equations for polarized parton distributions yields accurate results, provided that the number of steps in Bjorken x exceeds one thousand and the number of steps in t exceeds two hundred; however, they caution that the presented numerical results should be considered preliminary as the analysis is still in progress.

## Method

The authors solve the coupled integrodifferential DGLAP evolution equations for polarized quark and gluon distributions using a brute-force finite-difference method, dividing the variables into small steps in ln x and t and approximating derivatives and integrals by simple finite differences and Riemann sums. They include the recently calculated next-to-leading-order (NLO) splitting functions for spin-dependent distributions, and evolve the Gehrmann-Stirling set A parton distributions from Q² = 4 GeV² to Q² = 200 GeV² with N_f = 4 and Λ_MSbar = 231 MeV. The accuracy of the method is tested by varying the number of steps N_t (from 20 to 1000) and N_x (from 100 to 4000) and examining the resulting evolved distributions.

## Key numbers

- N_t = 50 steps in the variable t are sufficient for accurate evolution, as the evolved distributions are almost the same when N_t is varied from 20 to 1000 with fixed N_x = 1000.
- Several hundred x steps are necessary for good accuracy; with N_t = 200 and N_x = 1000, the evolution results are good enough.
- Accurate solution is obtained in the region 10⁻⁴ < x < 0.8 by taking more than two-hundred t steps and more than one-thousand x steps.
- The initial distributions are given at Q² = 4 GeV², and evolution is studied to Q² = 200 GeV².

## Context

This work extends the brute-force method previously applied to spin-independent evolution equations by Miyama and Kumano (1996) to the spin-dependent case, now incorporating the recently calculated NLO splitting functions for polarized parton distributions from Mertig and van Neerven (1996) and Vogelsang (1996). The authors note that an alternative Laguerre polynomial method by Kobayashi, Konuma, and Kumano (1995) is faster but has poor convergence for valence-like distributions at small x, making the brute-force method preferable for accuracy. This numerical tool enables future extraction of gluon polarization from scaling violation data of the structure function g₁, particularly at small x where NLO contributions are conspicuous.
