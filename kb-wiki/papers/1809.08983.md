# The microscopic mechanism behind the fission-barrier asymmetry (II): The rare-earth region $50 < Z < 82$ and $82 < N < 126$

> It is well known that most actinides fission into fragments of unequal size. The first attempt to understand this difference suggested that division leading to one of the fragments being near doubly magic $^{132}$Sn is favored by gain in binding energy. After the Strutinsky shell-correction method was developed an alternative idea that gained popularity was that the fission saddle might be lower for mass-asymmetric shapes and that this asymmetry was preserved until scission. Recently it was observed [Phys. Rev. Lett. {\bf 105} (2010) 252502] that $^{180}$Hg preferentially fissions asymmetrically in contradiction to the fragment-magic-shell expectation which suggested symmetric division peaked around $^{90}$Zr, with its magic neutron number $N=50$, so it was presented as a "new type of asymmetric fission". However, in a paper [Phys. Lett. 34B (1971) 349] a "simple" microscopic mechanism behind the asymmetry of the actinide fission saddle points was proposed to be related the coupling between levels of type [40$ΛΩ$] and [51$ΛΩ$]. The paper then generalizes this idea and made the remarkable prediction that analogous features could exist in other regions. In particular it was proposed that in the rare-earth region couplings between levels of type [30$ΛΩ$] and [41$ΛΩ$] would favor mass-asymmetric outer saddle shapes. In this picture the asymmetry of $^{180}$Hg is not a "new type of asymmetric fission" but of analogous origin as the asymmetry of actinide fission. This prediction has never been cited in the discussion of the recently observed fission asymmetries in the "new region of asymmetry", in nuclear physics also referred to as the rare-earth region. We show by detailed analysis that the mechanism of the saddle asymmetry in the sub-Pb region is indeed the one predicted half a century ago.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the asymmetric fission observed in the rare-earth region (specifically for \(^{180}\)Hg) is not a "new type of asymmetric fission" but instead originates from the same microscopic mechanism predicted half a century ago: coupling between specific single-particle level types ([30\(\Lambda\Omega\)] and [41\(\Lambda\Omega\)]) at the fission saddle point, analogous to the mechanism already established for actinides.

## Method

The authors performed detailed single-particle level calculations using the Strutinsky shell-correction method within a five-dimensional (5D) three-quadratic-surface parameterization of nuclear shapes, analyzing the behavior of neutron levels as a function of the asymmetry coordinate \(\beta_3\) at the fission saddle point. They compared the level diagrams for the actinide nucleus \(^{240}\)Pu and the rare-earth nucleus \(^{186}\)Pt, demonstrating that the same coupling mechanism between specific asymptotic quantum number levels drives the saddle asymmetry in both regions.

## Key numbers

- The calculated saddle asymmetry for \(^{186}\)Pt occurs at \(\beta_3 = 0.08\), with a saddle elongation of \(\beta_2 = 1.31\).
- For comparison, the actinide nucleus \(^{240}\)Pu has a saddle asymmetry of \(\beta_3 = 0.175\) and a saddle elongation of \(\beta_2 = 0.85\).
- The most probable mass split observed experimentally for \(^{180}\)Hg is 100/80; the Brownian shape-motion model predicts 104.4/75.6 for low-energy \(^{180}\)Hg.
- The neutron number of \(^{186}\)Pt studied is \(N = 108\).

## Context

This work extends the mechanism proposed by Gustafsson (1971) for actinide fission saddle asymmetry to the rare-earth region, directly contradicting the claim by Andreyev (2010) that \(^{180}\)Hg fission represents a "new type of asymmetric fission." The paper enables future experimental tests of the predicted extended region of asymmetric fission in the rare-earth region, supported by both the microscopic mechanism and the quantitative Brownian shape-motion model developed by Randrup and Moller (2011, 2013).
