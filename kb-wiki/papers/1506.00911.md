# Spin-orbit coupling rule in bound fermions systems

> Spin-orbit coupling characterizes quantum systems such as atoms, nuclei, hypernuclei, quarkonia, etc., and is essential for understanding their spectroscopic properties. Depending on the system, the effect of spin-orbit coupling on shell structure is large in nuclei, small in quarkonia, perturbative in atoms. In the standard non-relativistic reduction of the single-particle Dirac equation, we derive a universal rule for the relative magnitude of the spin-orbit effect that applies to very different quantum systems, regardless of whether the spin-orbit coupling originates from the strong or electromagnetic interaction. It is shown that in nuclei the near equality of the mass of the nucleon and the difference between the large repulsive and attractive potentials explains the fact that spin-orbit splittings are comparable to the energy spacing between major shells. For a specific ratio between the particle mass and the effective potential whose gradient determines the spin-orbit force, we predict the occurrence of giant spin-orbit energy splittings that dominate the single-particle excitation spectrum.

## Key claim

The paper derives a universal rule for the relative magnitude of spin-orbit splitting compared to major-shell energy spacings in bound fermion systems, expressed as a function of the ratio η between the particle mass and the effective potential whose gradient determines the spin-orbit force. The rule predicts that when η ≈ 1/2, giant spin-orbit splittings dominate the single-particle excitation spectrum, though this regime has not been observed experimentally.

## Method

Starting from the relativistic mean-field framework for nuclei, the authors perform the standard non-relativistic reduction of the single-particle Dirac equation to obtain a Schrödinger-like form with an explicit spin-orbit potential. They derive an analytic expression for the ratio x between the major-shell energy spacing (e.g., harmonic oscillator ℏω₀) and the spin-orbit splitting, expressed as a function of η = m/(V−S), where m is the particle mass and V−S is the difference between the vector and scalar potentials. The same functional form is then applied to atoms, Λ hypernuclei, and quarkonia by identifying the appropriate effective potentials in each system.

## Key numbers

- In nuclei: nucleon mass m ≈ 940 MeV, vector potential V ≈ 350 MeV, scalar potential S ≈ −400 MeV, giving V−S ≈ 750 MeV and η = 1.25; the ratio x = ℏω₀/|Δ⟨V^LS⟩| lies in the interval 1–5, meaning spin-orbit splittings are comparable to major-shell spacings.
- In hydrogen atoms: electron mass m = 0.5 MeV, Coulomb potential V(r₀) = −2.72 × 10⁻⁵ MeV at the Bohr radius, giving η ∼ −1/α² ≈ −2 × 10⁴; the ratio x ∼ 1/α² ≈ 10⁴.
- In charmonium: mass m_c ≈ 1.27 GeV, 1S–2S spacing ≈ 600 MeV, fine splittings between 1P_J states ΔM₂₁ = 45.6 ± 0.2 MeV and ΔM₁₀ = 95.3 ± 0.4 MeV, giving x ≈ 5–10.
- In bottomonium: mass m_b ≈ 4.2 GeV, 1S–2S spacing ≈ 560 MeV, fine splittings ΔM₂₁ = 19.4 ± 0.4 MeV and ΔM₁₀ = 33.3 ± 0.5 MeV, giving x ≈ 5–10.
- In Λ hypernuclei: Λ mass m_Λ = 1.12 GeV, scalar and vector fields reduced by factor ≈ 0.4–0.5 compared to nucleons, giving η ≈ 3–4 and x ranging from a few units to a few dozens; empirical Λ spin-orbit splittings ≈ 100 keV, major-shell spacings ≈ 10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of spin-orbit coupling from nuclear physics (where the relativistic mean-field framework of Duerr (1956), Serot and Walecka (1986, 1997), and Furnstahl and Serot (2000) naturally produces the empirical spin-orbit strength) to atoms, hypernuclei, and quarkonia, showing that the same functional form governs the ratio of major-shell to spin-orbit splittings regardless of whether the interaction is strong or electromagnetic. The rule explains the long-known fact from Sommerfeld's early work that atomic fine structure is ∼ α⁴ while principal spacings are ∼ α², and provides a unified framework for understanding why nuclear spin-orbit splittings are anomalously large compared to atomic ones. The prediction of giant spin-orbit splittings for η ≈ 1/2 suggests that bound antibaryon-nuclear systems, if they could be produced, would exhibit qualitatively new single-particle spectra, though such systems have not been observed and the potentials are subject to large uncertainties as noted by Greiner (2009).
