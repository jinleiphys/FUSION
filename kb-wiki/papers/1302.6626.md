# Stellar Superfluids

> Neutron stars provide a fertile environment for exploring superfluidity under extreme conditions. It is not surprising that Cooper pairing occurs in dense matter since nucleon pairing is observed in nuclei as energy differences between even-even and odd-even nuclei. Since superfluids and superconductors in neutron stars profoundly affect neutrino emissivities and specific heats, their presence can be observed in the thermal evolution of neutron stars. An ever-growing number of cooling neutron stars, now amounting to 13 thermal sources, and several additional objects from which upper limits to temperatures can be ascertained, can now be used to discriminate among theoretical scenarios and even to dramatically restrict properties of nucleon pairing at high densities. In addition, observations of pulsars, including their spin-downs and glitch histories, additionally support the conjecture that superfluidity and superconductivity are ubiquitous within, and important to our understanding of, neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed thermal and dynamical evolution of neutron stars can be explained by the presence of neutron superfluidity and proton superconductivity in their interiors, but this conclusion depends on the specific pairing channels (spin-singlet at low densities, spin-triplet at high densities) and on the assumption that no drastically enhanced neutrino emission processes occur (the "Minimal Cooling Paradigm").

## Method

The authors review theoretical models of neutron star cooling and spin-down, incorporating the effects of Cooper pairing on neutrino emissivities and specific heats. They compare these models with observational data from 13 thermal neutron star sources and several additional upper-limit temperature objects, as well as pulsar spin-down and glitch histories. The analysis uses the APR equation of state and state-of-the-art microphysics including general relativity, thermal conductivities, and neutrino emissivities.

## Key numbers

- 13 thermal neutron star sources are currently available for discriminating among theoretical cooling scenarios
- The fastest known radio pulsar, Terzan 5 ad (PSR J1748-2446ad), has a period of 1.39 ms, implying a strict upper limit on radius of 65 km from causality
- The average density of a neutron star must exceed 8 × 10^13 g cm^-3 based on gravitational binding arguments
- Nuclear saturation density is ρ_nuc ≈ 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- Critical temperatures for pairing are expected to be in the range ~10^8–10^10 K
- The neutron star in Cassiopeia A shows a cooling rate more than 10 times faster than expected without both neutron superfluidity and proton superconductivity
- Pairing energies in nuclei range from about 3 to 0.5 MeV, decreasing with increasing neutron number
- The bulk nuclear symmetry energy parameter S_v ≈ 31 MeV
- At nuclear saturation density, the proton fraction from beta-equilibrium is x_p,nuc ≈ 0.034
- Neutron drip occurs at ρ ≈ 4 × 10^11 g cm^-3
- The inner core may reach densities up to 10 ρ_nuc

## Context

This work extends the earlier studies of pairing in neutron stars initiated by Migdal (1959) and the BCS theory of Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer. It builds on the observation of pairing in laboratory nuclei by Bohr, Mottelson, and Pines (1958) and the phase-shift analysis of Tamagaki (1970). The paper enables future observational tests of superfluidity through continued monitoring of the Cassiopeia A neutron star cooling and through analysis of pulsar glitch phenomena, which are thought to originate from superfluid vortices in the neutron star crust.
