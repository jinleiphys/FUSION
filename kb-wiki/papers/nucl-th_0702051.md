# Thermal Signatures of Pairing Correlations in Nuclei and Nanoparticles

> The Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) mean-field theory of the pairing interaction breaks down for nuclei and ultra-small metallic grains (nanoparticles). Finite-temperature pairing correlations in such finite-size systems can be calculated beyond the BCS theory in an auxiliary-field Monte Carlo approach. We identify thermal signatures of pairing correlations in both nuclei and nanoparticles that depend on the particle-number parity.

## Key claim

Pairing correlations in finite-size systems such as nuclei and ultra-small metallic grains survive in the crossover regime between the bulk BCS limit and the fluctuation-dominated regime, but manifest through thermal signatures that depend on the particle-number parity rather than through a sharp phase transition.

## Method

The author used auxiliary-field Monte Carlo (AFMC) methods, also known as shell model Monte Carlo (SMMC) in the nuclear context, to compute fully correlated finite-temperature properties beyond the BCS mean-field approximation. For nuclei in the iron region, calculations were performed in the complete \(fpg_{9/2}\) shell with a Woods-Saxon single-particle potential plus monopole pairing and multipole-multipole interactions. For nanoparticles, the Hamiltonian included spin-degenerate orbital levels, a charging energy term, and a reduced BCS pairing interaction, with the model space truncated and the coupling constant renormalized to keep the BCS gap fixed.

## Key numbers

* For nuclei in the iron region, the Monte Carlo approach in a single major shell is valid for temperatures up to \(T \sim 1.5-2\) MeV.
* For nanoparticles with \(\Delta/\delta = 1\), the number of single-particle levels is approximately 180; a model space of \(N_r = 25\) levels is good for temperatures up to \(T \sim 3\delta\), while \(N_r = 15\) is used for \(T \leq 1.5\delta\) and \(N_r = 10\) for \(T \leq 0.8\delta\).
* For an aluminum grain, the Debye frequency is \(\omega_D \approx 34\) meV and the experimental thin-film gap is \(\Delta \approx 0.38\) meV, giving a coupling constant \(g/\delta \approx 0.193\).
* The heat capacity for nanoparticles with an even number of electrons shows an S-shaped enhancement; at higher temperatures it approaches the Fermi gas result \(C = 2\pi^2 T / 3\delta\).
* The spin susceptibility for nanoparticles with an odd number of electrons initially decreases with decreasing temperature, displays a minimum at a finite temperature, and then diverges as \(T \to 0\); for large temperatures it approaches \(\chi_p = g_B^2 \mu_B^2 / 2\delta\).

## Context

This work extends the study of pairing correlations beyond the BCS mean-field theory, which breaks down when the pairing gap \(\Delta\) is comparable to or smaller than the single-particle mean-level spacing \(\delta\) (the fluctuation-dominated regime). For nuclei, the results for heat capacity and moment of inertia in iron isotopes show even-odd effects similar to those measured in rare-earth nuclei by Schiller (2001). For nanoparticles, the AFMC results go beyond the static path approximation (SPA) and SPA plus RPA approaches of Lauritzen (1993), as well as diagonalization methods of Mastellone (1998) and the Richardson-based method of Lorenzo (2000), by enabling calculations for large numbers of single-particle levels at both low and high temperatures. The findings enable future studies of pairing correlations in finite-size fermionic systems where fluctuations wash out the BCS phase transition.
