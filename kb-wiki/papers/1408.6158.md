# Extrapolations of nuclear binding energies from new linear mass relations

> We present a method to extrapolate nuclear binding energies from known values for neighbouring nuclei. We select four specific mass relations constructed to eliminate smooth variation of the binding energy as function nucleon numbers. The fast odd-even variations are avoided by comparing nuclei with same parity. The mass relations are first tested and shown to either be rather accurately obeyed or revealing signatures of quickly varying structures. Extrapolations are initially made for a nucleus by applying each of these relations. Very reliable estimates are then produced either by an average or by choosing the extrapolation where the smoothest structures enter. Corresponding mass relations for $Q_α$ values are used to study the general structure of super-heavy elements. A minor neutron shell at $N = 152$ is seen, but no sign of other shell structures are apparent in the super-heavy region. Accuracies are typically substantially better than $0.5$~MeV.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to extrapolate nuclear binding energies using four specific linear mass relations that eliminate smooth variations and odd-even effects, achieving accuracies typically better than 0.5 MeV, but the reliability depends on avoiding regions where shell effects produce systematic deviations from zero in the mass relations.

## Method

Four mass relations were constructed from combinations of binding energies that cancel smooth liquid-drop contributions up to second order in a Taylor expansion: Δ₂ₙ (horizontal, fixed Z), Δ₂ₚ (vertical, fixed N), Δ₂ₐ (diagonal, fixed A), and Δ₂₍ₙ₋₂₎ (diagonal, fixed N-Z). For each unknown nucleus, the mass relation was tested locally using three preceding values to compute a non-zero average, which was then used for prediction, with uncertainty combining measurement errors and the spread of the average. The method was also applied to Qₐ values for super-heavy elements, where only three measured values enter the relation instead of four.

## Key numbers

- Accuracies are typically substantially better than 0.5 MeV for the extrapolations.
- Only results with σ < 500 keV were included as meaningful extrapolations.
- Shell deviations in the mass relations are always greater than 1 MeV and often 2-4 MeV at magic numbers.
- Away from shells, Δ₂ₙ results are typically less than 300 keV numerically, and Δ₂ₚ results are typically less than 500 keV numerically.
- The root mean square deviation of the 12-point Garvey-Kelson mass interpolation is less than 100 keV (cited from Barea et al. 2008).
- A suggested chaotic component of nuclear masses amounts to 2.78/A¹/³ MeV, between 0.5 and 1.4 MeV (cited from Molinari and Weidenmüller 2006).

## Context

This work extends the approach of Jensen et al. (Aksel) who formulated mass relations obeying a general principle of cancellation of smooth terms up to any desired order, and is related to the Garvey-Kelson mass relations (Garvey) which also eliminate smooth terms up to second order. The method enables extrapolation of binding energies beyond experimentally known regions without relying on specific mass formula parametrizations, and the Qₐ analysis reveals a minor neutron shell at N=152 in the super-heavy region but no sign of other shell structures there, which can guide future experimental searches at facilities like SHIPTRAP (Minaya).
