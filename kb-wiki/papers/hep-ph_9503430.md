# THE SOLAR NEUTRINO PROBLEM

> The solar neutrino problem has persisted for almost three decades. Recent results from Kamiokande, SAGE, and GALLEX indicate a pattern of neutrino fluxes that is very difficult to reconcile with plausible variations in standard solar models. This situation is reviewed and suggested particle physics solutions are discussed. A summary is given of the important physics expected from SNO, SuperKamiokande, and other future experiments.

## Key claim

The observed pattern of solar neutrino fluxes, with a strongly suppressed \(^7\)Be flux and a moderately suppressed \(^8\)B flux, cannot be produced by plausible variations in standard solar model parameters or by most nonstandard solar models, because such variations inevitably drive the \(\phi(^7\text{Be})/\phi(^8\text{B})\) ratio to higher, not lower, values.

## Method

The paper reviews the standard solar model (SSM) assumptions and the nuclear reaction network of the pp chain and CNO cycle, then compares SSM flux predictions from Bahcall and Pinsonneault (1992) and Turck-Chièze and Lopez (1993) to the measured capture rates from four experiments: the Homestake \(^{37}\)Cl radiochemical detector, the Kamiokande water Cherenkov detector, and the SAGE and GALLEX \(^{71}\)Ga radiochemical detectors. The author examines how varying SSM input parameters (e.g., the astrophysical S-factors \(S_{17}(0)\) and \(S_{33}(0)\), metallicity, opacity) affects the predicted fluxes, and shows that the fluxes track the central temperature \(T_c\) via power laws (\(\phi(^7\text{Be}) \propto T_c^8\), \(\phi(^8\text{B}) \propto T_c^{18}\)), making it impossible to simultaneously suppress \(^7\)Be and \(^8\)B as observed.

## Key numbers

- Homestake \(^{37}\)Cl capture rate: \(2.55 \pm 0.17 \pm 0.18\) SNU (1\(\sigma\)), compared to BP SSM prediction of \(8.0 \pm 1.0\) SNU and TCL SSM prediction of \(6.4 \pm 1.4\) SNU.
- Kamiokande II/III \(^8\)B neutrino flux: \(\phi_{\nu_e}(^8\text{B}) = (2.91 \pm 0.08 \pm 0.12) \times 10^6 / \text{cm}^2\text{s}\) (1\(\sigma\)), corresponding to 51% of the BP SSM prediction and 63% of the TCL SSM prediction.
- Inferred flux ratios from experiments: \(\phi(pp) \sim 0.9\,\phi^{\text{SSM}}(pp)\), \(\phi(^7\text{Be}) \sim 0\), \(\phi(^8\text{B}) \sim 0.43\,\phi^{\text{SSM}}(^8\text{B})\).
- The ratio \(\phi(^7\text{Be})/\phi(^8\text{B})\) scales as \(T_c^{-10}\) in SSM variations, so reducing \(T_c\) to lower both fluxes forces the ratio upward, opposite to the observed pattern.
- The astrophysical S-factor for \(^7\text{Be}(p,\gamma)^8\text{B}\) is \(S_{17}(0) = 22.4 \pm 2.1\) eV barns from a weighted average of six data sets (Johnson et al. 1992); a preliminary Coulomb breakup measurement gives \(S_{17}(0) = 16.7 \pm 3.2\) eV barns (Motobayashi et al. 1994).
- The \(^3\text{He}+^3\text{He}\) S-factor is \(S_{33}(0) = 5.0 \pm 0.3\) MeV barns (Parker and Rolfs 1991) adopted by BP, versus 5.24 MeV barns adopted by TCL.

## Context

This review summarizes the state of the solar neutrino problem as of 1995, extending the earlier comprehensive treatment by Bahcall (1989) and the experimental discussions by Bowles and Gavrin (1993). The paper argues that the flux pattern from Homestake, Kamiokande, SAGE, and GALLEX cannot be explained by standard solar model variations (e.g., those explored by Bahcall and Ulrich (1988) or Castellani et al. (1994, 1995)) or by nonstandard models such as low-metallicity suns or intermittent core mixing (Dilke and Gough 1972). This forces consideration of particle physics solutions, neutrino mass and mixing via vacuum oscillations or the Mikheyev-Smirnov-Wolfenstein (MSW) mechanism, which would have implications for the standard model of electroweak interactions and for astrophysical problems like dark matter. The paper enables future tests by the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO) and SuperKamiokande, which were then under construction.
