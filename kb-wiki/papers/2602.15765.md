# Development of an accurate formalism to predict properties of two-neutron halo nuclei: case study of $^{22}$C

> When moving away from stability or in loosely-bound systems, few-body clusterized structures like two-neutron halo nuclei appear. These emerge from the interplay between the many- and few-body degrees of freedom, and/or strong coupling between bound and continuum states. This motivates the development of models that can accurately describe few-body dynamics while enforcing shell effects. This work has two goals: understanding how to accurately enforce the Pauli principle in few-body models, as well as presenting new technical developments that allow for more robust and cheaper three-body calculations. We focus on properties of the two-neutron halo 22C, but expect the conclusions to apply to other few-body systems. We use a three-body, hyperspherical harmonics formalism combined with the R-matrix method. We compare predictions for properties of 22C starting from phenomenological interactions and using two methods to remove Pauli-forbidden states, the projection and supersymmetric methods. We also present the algorithms and derivations used. Additionally, we explore model space truncations that allow for reduced computational time. We show convergence of the calculation of both bound and scattering states for $K_{max}\sim 40$. The two methods to enforce the Pauli-exclusion principle lead to different predictions of 22C properties; the projection method is more accurate. We find one efficient channel truncation that reduces the computational cost of our calculations by 20%. Our study clarifies that the projection method is more accurate than the supersymmetric one to enforce the Pauli-exclusion principle. Technical and algorithmic developments enable accurate and efficient computation of two-neutron halo properties. This development paves the way to robust uncertainty quantification in three-body predictions, and is a useful starting point to tackle more complex systems and observables.

## Key claim

The projection method for enforcing the Pauli principle in three-body calculations of two-neutron halo nuclei is more accurate than the supersymmetric transformation method, though both methods produce similar convergence patterns for binding energy and root-mean-square hyperradius when applied to \(^{22}\)C.

## Method

The authors used a three-body hyperspherical harmonics formalism combined with the R-matrix method to solve the hyperradial Schrödinger equation for both bound and scattering states of \(^{22}\)C. They compared two approaches to remove Pauli-forbidden states from the core-neutron system: the supersymmetric transformation, which modifies the two-body potential to eliminate bound states without changing phase shifts, and the projection method, which explicitly projects forbidden states out of the three-body Hilbert space using a large energy shift. The calculations used a Woods-Saxon potential for the \(^{20}\)C-neutron interaction and the Minnesota potential for the neutron-neutron interaction, with a three-body force adjusted to fix the ground-state binding energy at \(-0.5\) MeV.

## Key numbers

- Convergence of bound and scattering state calculations was achieved for \(K_{max} \sim 40\), with \(\rho_{max} = 60\) fm and \(N = 60\) mesh points.
- The s-wave scattering length for the \(^{20}\)C-neutron system was set to \(a_s = -2.50\) fm, consistent with experimental data from Mosby (2013).
- The \(d_{5/2}\) bound state in the core-neutron system was placed at \(-1.62\) MeV, corresponding to the \(l=2\) peak observed in neutron knockout spectra from \(^{21}\)C by Leblond (2015).
- The three-body force depth was \(-14.36\) MeV for the projection method and \(-14.93\) MeV for the supersymmetric method to produce a bound state at \(-0.5\) MeV.
- The root-mean-square hyperradii of the \(0^+\) ground state at \(K_{max}=30\) were 8.82 fm (projection) and 8.86 fm (supersymmetry), converging within 0.02 fm.
- One efficient channel truncation reduced computational cost by 20%.

## Context

This work extends previous comparisons of Pauli-forbidden state removal methods, which had only been performed for light systems such as \(^{6}\)He (Thompson 2000, Descouvemont 2003) and \(^{6}\)Li (Tursunov 2020), to the heavier Borromean halo nucleus \(^{22}\)C, which contains multiple forbidden states with valence neutrons in the \(sd\) region. The study builds on prior theoretical work on \(^{22}\)C by Horiuchi (2006) and Pinilla (2016), who used supersymmetry and suggested a dominant \((\nu 1s_{1/2})^2\) ground-state configuration. The technical developments, including new algorithms for the projection method and automated three-body force adjustment, enable more efficient calculations and pave the way for robust uncertainty quantification in three-body predictions, as well as application to more complex systems and observables.
