# Nuclear energy density functionals grounded in ab initio calculations

> We discuss the construction of a nuclear Energy Density Functional (EDF) from ab initio calculations, and we advocate the need of a methodical approach that is free from ad hoc assumptions. The equations of state (EoS) of symmetric nuclear and pure neutron matter are computed using the chiral NNLO$_{\rm sat}$ and the phenomenological AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$ Hamiltonians as inputs in the Self-consistent Green's Function (SCGF) and Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) methods, respectively. We propose a convenient parametrization of the EoS as a function of the Fermi momentum and fit it on the SCGF and AFDMC calculations. We apply the ab initio-based EDF to carry out an analysis of the binding energies and charge radii of different nuclei in the local density approximation. The NNLO$_{\rm sat}$-based EDF produces encouraging results, whereas the AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$-based one is farther from experiment. Possible explanations of these different behaviors are suggested, and the importance of gradient and spin-orbit terms is analyzed. Our work paves the way for a practical and systematic way to merge ab initio nuclear theory and DFT, while at the same time it sheds light on some of the critical aspects of this procedure.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a nuclear Energy Density Functional (EDF) can be constructed directly from ab initio calculations of infinite nuclear matter using the Local Density Approximation (LDA), but the success of this approach depends strongly on the choice of nuclear interaction: the chiral NNLO$_{\rm sat}$ interaction yields encouraging results for finite nuclei, while the phenomenological AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$ interaction produces EDFs that are farther from experiment, suggesting that the LDA mapping may struggle with the strong correlations induced by the hard core of phenomenological potentials.

## Method

The equations of state (EoS) of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) and pure neutron matter (PNM) were computed using two different Hamiltonians and many-body methods: the chiral NNLO$_{\rm sat}$ interaction with the Self-consistent Green's Function (SCGF) method, and the phenomenological AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$ interaction with the Auxiliary Field Diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) method. The EoS were parametrized as a polynomial function of the Fermi momentum, and a model selection procedure identified the optimal parametrizations. These parametrizations were then used to construct LDA-based EDFs, which were applied to calculate binding energies and charge radii of closed-subshell nuclei, with some calculations extended to the Gradient Approximation (GA) level by adding surface terms.

## Key numbers

- The AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$ Hamiltonian yields binding energies of light and medium-mass nuclei that deviate by about 10% from experiment.
- AFDMC calculations for nuclear matter used 66 neutrons for PNM and 76 nucleons for SNM in a periodic box.
- Charge radii were estimated from proton radii using the formula $r_{ch}^2 = r_p^2 + (0.8\,{\rm fm})^2$.
- SCGF calculations are presently possible for mass numbers up to $A \approx 60-90$ for ground-state energies, and up to $A=138$ for charge radii and charge density distributions in Sn and Xe isotopes.
- The AFDMC method can treat nuclei as large as $^{90}$Zr with the AV4$^\prime$+UIX$_{c}$ potential.

## Context

This work extends the "reductionist" or non-empirical approach to nuclear DFT, which is well-established in electronic DFT (Perdew and others), by constructing EDFs from ab initio calculations rather than fitting to experimental data. It contrasts with traditional empirical EDFs (Chabanat and others) that reproduce experimental binding energies within 1-2 MeV and charge radii within 0.01-0.02 fm, and with earlier attempts to constrain EDF terms using ab initio calculations (Baldo and others, Roca-Maza and others). The paper enables a systematic pathway to merge ab initio nuclear theory with DFT, and identifies critical aspects such as the role of gradient and spin-orbit terms and the impact of interaction hardness on the LDA approximation.
