# Viscous fluid dynamics

> We briefly discuss the phenomenological theory of dissipative fluid. We also present some numerical results for hydrodynamic evolution of QGP fluid with dissipation due to shear viscosity only. Its effect on particle production is also studied.

## Key claim

The paper argues that even small shear viscosity significantly affects the evolution of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) and particle production in heavy-ion collisions, but the numerical results shown are from a first-order dissipative hydrodynamics code, which the author acknowledges violates causality and can produce unphysical effects like early reheating, though no such reheating was observed in this study.

## Method

The author solved first-order relativistic viscous hydrodynamics in 2+1 dimensions with boost invariance, using the AZHYDRO-KOLKATA code, for Au+Au collisions at impact parameter b = 6.8 fm (16-24% centrality). Only shear viscosity was included, with two values: the AdS/CFT motivated eta/s = 0.08 and the perturbative QCD estimate eta/s = 0.135, using the EOS-Q equation of state with a first-order phase transition at critical temperature Tc = 164 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity values used: eta/s = 0.08 (AdS/CFT motivated) and eta/s = 0.135 (perturbative estimate)
- Critical temperature for phase transition: Tc = 164 MeV
- Freeze-out temperature for particle spectra: T_F = 0.158 GeV
- At pT = 3 GeV, pion production increased by a factor of 3 (for eta/s = 0.08) and a factor of 5 (for eta/s = 0.135)
- At pT = 3 GeV, elliptic flow reduced by a factor of 2 (for eta/s = 0.08) and a factor of 3 (for eta/s = 0.135)
- Viscous fluid requires 20-30% less initial temperature than ideal fluid to produce the same pT spectra

## Context

This work extends the ideal fluid dynamics analyses of Au+Au collisions at RHIC (QGP3, Heinz 2004) by implementing dissipative effects, following recent numerical advances by Teaney (2003), Muronga and Rischke (2004), and Chaudhuri's own earlier work (2006). The results show that viscosity flattens the pT spectra and causes elliptic flow to saturate at large pT, matching experimental observations, and suggest that initial temperatures extracted from ideal fluid analyses may be overestimated by 20% or more.
