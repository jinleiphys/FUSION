# Relating Measurable Correlations in Heavy Ion Collisions to Bulk Properties of Equilibrated QCD Matter

> In order to compare theoretical calculations of thermal fluctuations of conserved quantities, such as charge susceptibilities or the specific heat, to experimentally measured correlations and fluctuations in heavy ion collisions, one must confront the reality of changing conditions within the collision environment, and transport of conserved quantities within the finite duration of the expansion and dissolution of the reaction. In previous work, fluctuations of conserved charges from lattice calculations, where charge is allowed to fluctuate within the designated volume consistent with the grand canonical ensemble, was linked to correlations in heavy-ion collisions, which accounted for the finite time with which to transport absolutely conserved quanatities. In this case details of the correlations were related to the evolution of the susceptibility. In this work, this paradigm is extended to compare fluctuations of momentum or energy to transverse energy correlations that can be measured in heavy-ion collisions. The sensitivity of these correlations to the equation of state, viscosity and diffusion is illustrated by considering simple models without transverse expansion. Only correlations in relative spatial rapidity are discussed here, but the prospects for extending these ideas to realistic calculations and for making realistic connections with experiment are discussed.

# Relating Measurable Correlations in Heavy Ion Collisions to Bulk Properties of Equilibrated QCD Matter

## Key claim
The authors extend a previously developed paradigm linking lattice QCD charge susceptibilities to measurable correlations in heavy-ion collisions, now applying it to fluctuations of momentum and energy. However, this extension is demonstrated only for simplified models without transverse expansion, and the authors explicitly state that the approach used for evolving correlation functions is "untenable for a realistic three-dimensional simulation."

## Method
The authors develop algorithms for evolving two-particle correlation functions of conserved quantities (charge, energy, momentum) through a hydrodynamic medium, then projecting these correlations onto final-state particles. They compare two approaches: one based on explicitly evolving correlation functions (used in simplified boost-invariant scenarios without transverse expansion) and one based on generating pairs of charges at each space-time point and propagating them independently through three ensembles of hydrodynamic runs. The equivalence between these methods and thermal noise-based approaches is demonstrated in the appendix.

## Key numbers
- For a simplified noise model with Gaussian sources of width σ=1 and rapidity range Lη=12, the uncertainty in the correlation function from a single calculation is approximately 0.46
- Averaging n=100 calculations reduces the uncertainty to approximately 0.46/√100 = 0.046
- To obtain accuracy of better than 2 percent, roughly 1000 independent hydrodynamic calculations are required (or 2000 runs given two ensembles)
- The characteristic time scale for creating the short-range correlation peak is a²/D, where a is the mesh size and D is the diffusion constant
- Lattice calculations justify treating the equilibrated gas as uncorrelated at temperatures around 155 MeV

## Context
This work extends the framework of Pratt (2012) and Pratt, McCormack, and Ratti (2015), which linked charge fluctuations from lattice QCD to experimentally measurable correlations in heavy-ion collisions by accounting for finite-time transport of conserved charges. The new contribution is applying this paradigm to energy and momentum fluctuations, which are sensitive to the equation of state, viscosity, and diffusion. The paper enables future connections between transverse energy correlations measured in experiments and bulk properties of QCD matter, though the authors note that realistic three-dimensional calculations will require the noisy ensemble method rather than direct correlation function evolution. The approach is contrasted with thermal noise methods of Kapusta, Mueller, and Stephanov (2013) and Ling, Springer, and Stephanov (2014), which should give identical results for sufficiently small mesh sizes.
