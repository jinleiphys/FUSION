# Can one ever prove that neutrinos are Dirac particles?

> According to the "Black Box" theorem the experimental confirmation of neutrinoless double beta decay ($0 ν2 β$) would imply that at least one of the neutrinos is a Majorana particle. However, a null $0 ν2 β$ signal cannot decide the nature of neutrinos, as it can be suppressed even for Majorana neutrinos. In this letter we argue that if the null $0 ν2 β$ decay signal is accompanied by a $0 ν4 β$ quadruple beta decay signal, then at least one neutrino should be a Dirac particle. This argument holds irrespective of the underlying processes leading to such decays.

## Key claim

The authors argue that if a neutrinoless quadruple beta decay ($0\nu4\beta$) signal is observed without a corresponding neutrinoless double beta decay ($0\nu2\beta$) signal, then at least one neutrino must be a Dirac particle. This conclusion holds for a broad class of models, barring extremely fine-tuned cancellations at the $\sim 10^{-30}$ level.

## Method

The authors analyze the symmetry breaking of the global lepton number $U(1)_L$ symmetry to discrete $Z_m$ subgroups and classify the allowed neutrinoless beta decay processes ($0\nu2n\beta$) based on how neutrinos transform under the residual symmetry. They compare the effective operator dimensions and estimate the relative decay rates for $0\nu2\beta$ and $0\nu4\beta$ processes under different symmetry-breaking scenarios, using dimensional power counting with typical nuclear momentum transfer and new physics scale parameters.

## Key numbers

- For case (a), where $0\nu2\beta$ and $0\nu4\beta$ are induced by effective $d=9$ and $d=18$ operators respectively, the ratio $R = \Gamma_{0\nu2\beta}/\Gamma_{0\nu4\beta} \sim 10^{82}$ for $\Lambda \sim 1$ TeV.
- For case (b), where $0\nu2\beta$ is induced by a Majorana neutrino mass and $0\nu4\beta$ by a $d=6$ "lepton quarticity" operator, the ratio $R \sim 10^{30}$ for $\Lambda \sim 1$ TeV.
- The typical momentum transfer in the nucleus is taken as $q \simeq 0.1$ GeV.
- The Q-values $Q_{\beta\beta}$ and $Q_{4\beta}$ for the decays are both of order MeV.

## Context

This work extends the "Black Box" theorem of Schechter and Valle (1981), which states that observation of $0\nu2\beta$ decay implies at least one neutrino is Majorana. The authors address the converse problem: how to prove neutrinos are Dirac if $0\nu2\beta$ is not observed. The paper builds on earlier proposals for $0\nu4\beta$ decay by Heeck and Rodejohann (2013) and on concrete gauge models with family symmetries leading to Dirac neutrinos (Chulia et al., 2016, 2017). The NEMO-3 collaboration's recent experimental search for $0\nu4\beta$ in $^{150}$Nd (Arnold, 2017) provides the experimental context. The result enables a potential experimental distinction between Dirac and Majorana neutrinos through the combined observation of $0\nu2\beta$ and $0\nu4\beta$ signals.
