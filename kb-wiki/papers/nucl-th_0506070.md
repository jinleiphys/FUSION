# An exactly solvable limit of low energy QCD

> Starting from the QCD Hamiltonian, we derive a schematic Hamiltonian for low energy quark dynamics with quarks restricted to the lowest s-level. The resulting eigenvalue problem can be solved analytically. Even though the Hamiltonian exhibits explicit chiral symmetry the severe restriction of the number of degrees of freedom breaks the pattern of chiral symmetry breaking for finite quark masses.

# An exactly solvable limit of low energy QCD

## Key claim

The authors derive a schematic Hamiltonian from QCD in Coulomb gauge by restricting quarks to the lowest s-level, which yields an exactly solvable eigenvalue problem; however, the severe truncation of degrees of freedom breaks the expected pattern of chiral symmetry breaking for finite quark masses, producing a linear (rather than quadratic) dependence of the pion mass on the bare quark mass.

## Method

Starting from the QCD Hamiltonian in Coulomb gauge, the authors approximate the Coulomb kernel by a contact potential between quark charge densities and remove gluon degrees of freedom. They then restrict quarks and antiquarks to the lowest momentum mode (n=0) in a finite volume, producing a Hamiltonian that depends only on the total color angular momentum squared. The eigenvalue problem is solved analytically using group theory, with the Fock space classified by the unitary group reduction U(8) ⊃ U_c(2) ⊗ U_{fS}(4) for SU(2) color and flavor.

## Context

This work extends schematic model approaches to QCD such as the Lipkin-type model of Hess and collaborators (2000) that described meson and baryon states with phenomenological parameters, and the gluon-sector truncation of Szczepaniak and collaborators (1999) that reproduced lattice gauge results. The authors aim to build models that are derived from QCD rather than purely phenomenological, depending only on well-determined coupling constants. The model enables systematic relaxation of approximations by adding higher momentum modes and gluon degrees of freedom to address low-energy phenomena with a finite number of degrees of freedom.
