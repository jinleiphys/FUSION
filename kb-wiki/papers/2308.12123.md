# Solving the matrix exponential function for special orthogonal groups SO(n) and the exceptional G$_2$

> In this work the matrix exponential function is solved analytically for the special orthogonal groups $SO(n)$ up to $n=9$. The number of occurring $k$-th matrix powers gets limited to $0\leq k \leq n-1$ by exploiting the Cayley-Hamilton relation. The corresponding expansion coefficients can be expressed as cosine and sine functions of a vector-norm $V$ and the roots of a polynomial equation that depends on a few specific invariants. Besides the well known case of $SO(3)$, a quadratic equation needs to be solved for $n=4,5$, a cubic equation for $n=6,7$, and a quartic equation for $n=8,9$. As an interesting subgroup of $SO(7)$, the exceptional Lie group $G_2$ of dimension $14$ is constructed via the matrix exponential function through a remarkably simple constraint on an invariant, $ξ=1$. The calculation of the trace of the $SO(n)$-matrices arising from the exponential function, results in a sum of cosines of several angles, which specify the associated conjugation class as a point on a maximal torus.

## Key claim

The matrix exponential function for special orthogonal groups \(SO(n)\) up to \(n=9\) is solved analytically by reducing the required matrix powers to \(0 \leq k \leq n-1\) via the Cayley-Hamilton relation, with expansion coefficients expressed as cosine and sine functions of a vector-norm \(V\) and the roots of a polynomial equation that depends on a few specific invariants; the exceptional Lie group \(G_2 \subset SO(7)\) is constructed through the remarkably simple constraint \(\xi = 1\) on the invariant \(\xi = \operatorname{tr}(\vec J \cdot \hat v)^4\).

## Method

The author exploits the Cayley-Hamilton relation for antisymmetric \(n \times n\) matrices (elements of the Lie algebra \(so(n)\)) to limit the number of independent matrix powers in the exponential series to \(0\) through \(n-1\). The expansion coefficients are obtained by solving a linear recursion derived from the characteristic polynomial, which for \(n=4,5\) requires solving a quadratic equation, for \(n=6,7\) a cubic equation, and for \(n=8,9\) a quartic equation (the latter via a cubic resolvent). For \(G_2 \subset SO(7)\), seven homogeneous linear relations among the 21 parameters of a general \(so(7)\) Lie algebra element are derived from the automorphism condition of the octonions, which translate into the invariant constraint \(\xi = 1\).

## Key numbers

- For \(SO(4)\): the invariant \(\eta = \det(\vec J \cdot \hat v)\) satisfies \(0 \leq \eta \leq 1/4\); the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_4 = 2\cos(V\sqrt{z}) + 2\cos(V\sqrt{1-z})\) with \(z = (1 - \sqrt{1-4\eta})/2\).
- For \(SO(5)\): the invariant \(\xi = \operatorname{tr}(\Sigma^4)\) lies in the interval \(1 \leq \xi \leq 2\); the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_5 = 1 + 2\cos(V\cos\theta) + 2\cos(V\sin\theta)\) with \(\theta = \frac{1}{2}\arccos\sqrt{\xi-1}\).
- For \(SO(6)\): the invariants \(\xi\) and \(\eta\) satisfy \((3\xi-2)^3 \geq (9\xi + 108\eta - 10)^2\) and the maximal value of \(\eta\) is \(\eta_{\text{max}} = 1/27\); the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_6 = 2\sum_{j=1}^3 \cos(V\sqrt{y_j})\).
- For \(SO(7)\): the invariants \(\xi\) and \(\zeta\) satisfy \((3\xi-2)^3 \geq 4[4 - 9(\xi+\zeta)]^2\), with extremal values \(\zeta_{\text{max}} = -2/9\) and \(\zeta_{\text{min}} = -2\); the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_7 = 1 + 2\sum_{j=1}^3 \cos(V\sqrt{y_j})\).
- For \(G_2 \subset SO(7)\): the constraint is \(\xi = 1\) and the invariant \(\zeta\) lies in the interval \(-11/18 \leq \zeta \leq -1/2\); the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_{G_2} = 8\cos(\varphi_2/2)\cos(\varphi_3/2)\cos((\varphi_2+\varphi_3)/2) - 1\) with \(\varphi_1 = \varphi_2 + \varphi_3\).
- For \(SO(8)\): the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_8 = 2\sum_{j=1}^4 \cos(V\sqrt{y_j})\).
- For \(SO(9)\): the trace is \(\operatorname{tr} R_9 = 1 + 2\sum_{j=1}^4 \cos(V\sqrt{y_j})\).

## Context

This work extends the author's previous approach for solving the matrix exponential function for \(SU(3)\), \(SU(4)\), and \(Sp(2)\) (Kaiser, 2023) to the special orthogonal groups \(SO(n)\) up to \(n=9\) and the exceptional Lie group \(G_2\). The analytical formulas enable explicit construction of \(SO(n)\) rotation matrices and their conjugation classes on maximal tori, and the \(G_2\) construction via the \(\xi=1\) constraint provides a new algebraic characterization of this exceptional group. The results for \(n=10,11\) are noted to require solving quintic equations, for which no direct algebraic solution formulas exist.
