# Spin-tensor decomposition of nuclear transition matrix elements for neutrinoless double-$β$ decay of $^{76}$Ge and $^{82}$Se nuclei within PHFB approach

> Employing the PHFB model, nuclear transition matrix elements $M^{\left( K\right) }$ for the neutrinoless double-$β^{-} $ decay of $\ ^{76}$Ge and $^{82}$Se isotopes are calculated within mechanisms involving light as well as heavy Majorana neutrinos, and classical Majorons by considering the spin-tensor decomposition of realistic KUO and empirical JUN45 effective two-body interaction. It is noticed that the effects due to the SRC on NTMEs $M^{\left( 0ν\right) }$ and $M^{\left( 0N\right) }$ due to the exchange of light and heavy Majorana neutrinos, respectively, is maximally incorporated by the central part of the effective two-body interaction, which varies by a small amount with the inclusion of spin-orbit and tensor components. The maximum uncertainty in the average NTMEs $\overline{M}^{(0ν)}$ and $\overline{M}^{(0N)}$ turns out to be about 10\% and 37\%, respectively.

## Key claim

Using the PHFB model with spin-tensor decomposition of the KUO and JUN45 effective interactions, the authors find that short-range correlation (SRC) effects on nuclear transition matrix elements for neutrinoless double-beta decay of 76Ge and 82Se are maximally incorporated by the central part of the effective two-body interaction, with only small changes when spin-orbit and tensor components are added. The maximum uncertainty in the average NTMEs is about 10% for light neutrino exchange and about 37% for heavy neutrino exchange.

## Method

The authors employed the projected Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (PHFB) approach in a valence space spanned by the 1p1/2, 1p3/2, 0f5/2, and 0g9/2 orbits with 56Ni as an inert core, using two effective interactions: the realistic KUO interaction and the empirical JUN45 interaction. They performed spin-tensor decomposition of these interactions into central (C), spin-orbit (S), and tensor (T) components, generating three sets of wave functions (C, CS, and CST) to calculate NTMEs for light Majorana neutrino exchange, heavy Majorana neutrino exchange, and classical Majoron emission mechanisms. Three different short-range correlation parametrizations (SRC1 for Miller-Spencer, SRC2 for Argonne V18, SRC3 for CD-Bonn potentials) were applied using a Jastrow-type function.

## Key numbers

- For 76Ge with HFB1 wave functions and SRC1, the NTME M(0ν) for light neutrino exchange ranges from 1.277 (C only) to 4.858 (CST), and M(0N) for heavy neutrino exchange ranges from 45.40 (C only) to 104.34 (CST)
- For 82Se with HFB1 wave functions and SRC1, the NTME M(0ν) ranges from 3.003 (C only) to 5.494 (CST), and M(0N) ranges from 110.74 (C only) to 123.04 (CST)
- The maximum uncertainty in average NTMEs is about 10% for M(0ν) and about 37% for M(0N)
- Extracted limit on effective light neutrino mass for 76Ge: <mν> < 0.20 eV (HFB1, SRC3) to < 0.26 eV (HFB2, SRC1)
- Extracted limit on effective heavy neutrino mass for 76Ge: <MN> > 4.20×10^7 GeV (HFB1, SRC1) to > 1.05×10^8 GeV (HFB2, SRC3)
- Most stringent extracted limit on effective Majoron-neutrino coupling constant: <gM> = (6.37-8.18)×10^-5
- With SRC1, NTMEs M(0ν) are reduced by approximately 14%-16% and M(0N) by approximately 64%-66% compared to the finite nucleon size (FNS) case

## Context

This work extends previous PHFB calculations by Rath et al. (2013, 2012) for light and heavy Majorana neutrino exchange mechanisms, now incorporating spin-tensor decomposition of the effective interactions to study the role of central, spin-orbit, and tensor components separately. The calculated NTMEs can be compared with results from other approaches such as the interacting shell model (Caurier et al. 2008, Menendez et al. 2009, Horoi et al. 2014, Senkov et al. 2016), QRPA (Vogel et al. 1986, Civitarese et al. 1987), and the interacting boson model (Barea et al. 2013, 2015), which typically differ by factors of 2-3. The extracted limits on effective neutrino masses and Majoron coupling constants from experimental half-life limits (Heidelberg-Moscow, IGEX, GERDA-I for 76Ge; NEMO-3 for 82Se) provide input for planning next-generation experiments and constraining beyond-Standard-Model physics.
