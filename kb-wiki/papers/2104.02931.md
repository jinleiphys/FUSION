# Measurement of Neutron Lifetime and Purcell Effect

> Purcell effect predicts that spontaneous radiation is not an intrinsic property of matter, but is affected by the environment in which it is located, and is the result of the interaction of matter and field. Purcell effect can be inferred from Fermi Gold rule through strict quantum electrodynamics (QED), and through it can achieve the enhancement or suppression of radiation. We suggest that, in principle, the Purcell effect can be detected at the percentage level of neutron decay in experiments with trapped ultra-cold neutrons. As a test of our claim, we propose a currently achievable experimental protocol that can detect whether Purcell effect has occurred in an trapped ultra-cold neutron lifetime measurement experiment. Finally, we discuss the discrepancy in current methods of measuring neutron lifetime, which may be caused by different experimental setups.

## Key claim

The authors suggest that the Purcell effect, the modification of spontaneous decay rates by the electromagnetic environment, may occur in bottle-type ultra-cold neutron lifetime experiments, potentially explaining the approximately 9-second discrepancy between beam and bottle measurements of the neutron lifetime. This is presented as a qualitative hypothesis rather than a demonstrated effect, with the caveat that the Purcell enhancement factor is estimated at only about 1.0097.

## Method

The authors treat neutron decay as a transition between two quantum states (neutron and proton) in a pseudo-two-level system, analogous to atomic spontaneous emission. They apply Fermi's golden rule and cavity quantum electrodynamics (CQED) formalism to derive how the presence of a trap bottle modifies the density of states and thus the decay rate, using the Jaynes-Cummings model and Markov approximation. They also propose a specific experimental protocol using graphene nanoribbons (GNR) as an equivalent cavity coupled to neutrons, driven by pump and probe laser fields, to detect the Purcell effect via changes in the probe absorption spectrum.

## Key numbers

- The beam method average neutron lifetime is given as τ_n^beam = 888.0 ± 2.0 s.
- The bottle method average neutron lifetime is given as τ_n^bottle = 879.5 ± 0.5 s.
- The difference between beam and bottle measurements is approximately 9 seconds.
- The beam method decay rate is Γ^beam = 1.126 × 10^{-3} s^{-1}.
- The estimated Purcell factor is Fp ≈ 1.0097.
- The mass difference between neutron and proton is M_n − M_p ≈ +1.29 MeV/c^2.
- The proposed GNR eigenmode frequency used in calculations is ω_k = 7.477 GHz, with pump Rabi frequency Ω = 10 GHz and pump detuning Δ_pu = 100 GHz.

## Context

This work extends the concept of the Purcell effect, originally proposed by Purcell (1946) for atomic spontaneous emission in cavities, to nuclear β-decay of neutrons. It addresses the "neutron decay anomaly", the discrepancy between beam and bottle lifetime measurements, which has been discussed by Byrne (1996), Serebrov (2005), and others, and which has prompted alternative explanations including dark matter decay (Fornal and Grinstein, 2018), mirror matter theory (Berezhiani, 2019), and the inverse quantum Zeno effect (2019). The paper builds on experimental demonstrations that nuclear decay rates can be modified by chemical environment, such as the 0.83% enhancement of ⁷Be decay in C₆₀ reported by Ohtsuki et al. (2004). The proposed GNR-based detection scheme aims to enable future experimental tests of whether the Purcell effect contributes to the neutron lifetime puzzle.
