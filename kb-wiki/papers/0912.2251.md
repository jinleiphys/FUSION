# Physics of Electroweak Interactions with Nuclei

> In this series of lectures it is illustrated how one can study the strong dynamics of nuclei by means of the electroweak probe. In particular, the most important steps to derive the cross sections in first order perturbation theory are reviewed. In the derivation the focus is put on the main ingredients entering the hadronic part (response functions), i.e. the initial and final states of the system and the operators relevant for the reaction. Emphasis is put on the electromagnetic interaction with few-nucleon systems. The Lorentz integral transform method to calculate the response functions ab initio is described. A few examples of the comparison between theoretical and experimental results are shown. The dependence of the response functions on the nuclear interaction and in particular on three-body forces is emphasized.

## Key claim

The paper reviews how electroweak probes can reveal the strong dynamics of nuclei, emphasizing that the comparison between ab initio theoretical calculations and experimental data can discriminate among different realistic nucleon-nucleon potentials and assess the importance of three-body forces, but only if the many-body problem is solved accurately enough to control theoretical uncertainties.

## Method

The author derives the inclusive unpolarized electron scattering cross section within the one-photon-exchange approximation and non-relativistic quantum mechanics, separating the cross section into longitudinal and transverse response functions that contain the nuclear matrix elements. The Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method is described as an ab initio technique to calculate these response functions for continuum states without explicitly solving for the final-state wavefunction. The derivation emphasizes the role of the nuclear four-current operator and its connection to the nuclear potential via the continuity equation, highlighting that exchange currents arise from implicit degrees of freedom (e.g., mesons) not present in the Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

* The electromagnetic coupling constant is α = 1/137, and the weak coupling is suppressed by a factor of 10⁻⁵ due to the large mass of the weak gauge bosons.
* The four-momentum transfer squared is Q² = 4|k||k'| sin²(θ/2) ≥ 0, derived from the electron kinematics in the one-photon-exchange approximation.
* In the ultra-relativistic limit (electron mass much smaller than its momentum, valid for electrons of tens or hundreds of MeV), the Mott cross section is σ_M = (1/(4E²)) (cos²(θ/2) / sin⁴(θ/2)).
* For A=3 and 4, bound-state binding energies can be calculated with an accuracy on the percentage level or less using methods such as Faddeev-Yakubovsky, hyperspherical harmonics, or Green's Function Monte Carlo.

## Context

This lecture series extends the standard derivation of electroweak cross sections found in textbooks such as Bjorken and Drell (1964) and more modern treatments by Donnelly and Sick (1985), Arenhoevel et al. (2001), and Amaro et al. (2005). The work emphasizes ab initio approaches for few-nucleon systems, contrasting with model-dependent calculations used for astrophysical reaction rates (e.g., in the pp-chain and CNO cycle for ⁴He synthesis). The LIT method, reviewed here, enables calculation of inclusive response functions for continuum states, which is otherwise particularly challenging in nuclear physics due to the complicated structure of the nuclear potential.
