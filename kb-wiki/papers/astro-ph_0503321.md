# Detection of Neutrinos from Supernovae in Nearby Galaxies

> While existing detectors would see a burst of many neutrinos from a Milky Way supernova, the supernova rate is only a few per century. As an alternative, we propose the detection of ~ 1 neutrino per supernova from galaxies within 10 Mpc, in which there were at least 9 core-collapse supernovae since 2002. With a future 1-Mton scale detector, this could be a faster method for measuring the supernova neutrino spectrum, which is essential for calibrating numerical models and predicting the redshifted diffuse spectrum from distant supernovae. It would also allow a > 10^4 times more precise trigger time than optical data alone for high-energy neutrinos and gravitational waves.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that a future 1-Mton water-Cherenkov detector could detect approximately one neutrino per core-collapse supernova from galaxies within 10 Mpc, rather than requiring a rare Milky Way supernova for a large burst; the authors note that the calculated nearby supernova rate of about one per year is likely a conservative underestimate, with observed rates suggesting a factor of about three higher.

## Method

The authors calculate the expected core-collapse supernova rate within 10 Mpc using the GALEX z=0 star formation rate (continuum limit) and a galaxy catalog with individual galaxy distances, morphological types, and optical luminosities converted to supernova rates. They then compute detection probabilities for a 1-Mton water-Cherenkov detector via the inverse beta decay reaction (anti-electron neutrino on proton), considering both neutrino-neutrino coincidence (two events within 10 seconds) and neutrino-optical coincidence (one event within a 1-day optical window) detection modes, with background estimates from spallation and invisible muon decays.

## Key numbers

- At least 9 core-collapse supernovae occurred within 10 Mpc since 2002, compared to 2.8 expected from the conservative calculation
- The calculated core-collapse supernova rate within 10 Mpc is about one per year
- For a 1-Mton detector, the accidental background coincidence rate for two events within 10 seconds is about 0.7 per year, based on singles rates of less than 650 per year above 15 MeV and less than 400 per year below 35 MeV
- In the neutrino-optical coincidence mode, expected signal and background numbers in 18-30 MeV are N_nu = 10 D_Mpc^-2 V_Mton and N_bg = 0.9 delta_t_day V_Mton; in 12-38 MeV with GdCl3 they are N_nu = 19 D_Mpc^-2 V_Mton and N_bg = 1.2 delta_t_day V_Mton
- The total neutrino detection rates could be as large as one per year when accounting for the factor of about 3 underestimate in supernova rates
- A single neutrino detection would reduce the start time uncertainty from about 1 day to about 10 seconds

## Context

This work extends the intermediate regime between Milky Way supernova neutrino detection (many events per burst, but rate of a few per century) and the diffuse supernova neutrino background (DSNB, less than one event per supernova) as studied by Ando (2002) and others. The proposal enables a faster method for measuring the supernova neutrino spectrum at z=0, which is essential for calibrating numerical models and predicting the redshifted diffuse spectrum from distant supernovae, and also provides a greater than 10^4 times more precise trigger time for high-energy neutrinos and gravitational waves compared to optical data alone.
