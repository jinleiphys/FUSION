# Quiescent times in Gamma-Ray-Bursts: hints of a dormant inner engine

> We perform a statistical analysis of the temporal structure of long Gamma-Ray-Bursts (GRBs). First we consider a sample of bursts in which a long quiescent time is present. Comparing the pre-quiescence with the post-quiescence emission we show that they display similar temporal structures, hardness ratios and emitted powers, but, on the average, the post-quiescence emission is roughly twice as long as the pre-quiescence emission. We then consider a sample of long and bright GRBs. We show that the duration of each emission period is compatible with the duration of an active period computed in various inner engine models. At the contrary, if the inner engine is assumed to be always active, i.e. also during the quiescent times, in several cases the total duration of the burst largely exceeds the theoretical durations. Our analysis therefore does not support the interpretation of long quiescent times in terms of stochastic modulation of a continuous wind. Instead the quiescent times can be interpreted as dormancy periods of the inner engine. Before and after a dormancy period the inner engine produces similar emissions.

## Key claim

Long quiescent times (QTs) in gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) are most probably periods of inactivity of the inner engine (a "dormant engine") rather than stochastic modulations of a continuous relativistic wind, because the total burst duration including the QT would otherwise exceed the theoretical active periods of inner engine models (e.g., the collapsar model or the quark deconfinement model) by roughly a factor of three.

## Method

The authors performed a statistical analysis of light curves from the entire BATSE catalogue, using a peak-finding algorithm to identify time intervals between adjacent peaks. They defined active periods separated by quiescent times (QTs) and selected a subsample of 36 bursts containing a QT longer than 40 seconds. For each burst in this subsample, they divided the emission into a pre-quiescence emission (PreQE) and a post-quiescence emission (PostQE) and compared their temporal structures (distributions of time intervals between peaks), durations, hardness ratios, and emitted powers.

## Key numbers

- The subsample of bursts with a QT longer than 40 s contains 36 GRBs from the BATSE catalogue.
- The average duration of PreQE is 21 s; the average duration of PostQE is 41 s (PostQE is roughly twice as long as PreQE).
- The standard deviations of the duration distributions are 36 s for PreQE and 33 s for PostQE.
- The average hardness of PostQE is about 20% smaller than the average hardness of PreQE.
- The average power emitted during PreQE and PostQE is essentially the same.
- The average QT duration in the subsample is about 80 s.
- The statistical correlation between the durations of QT and PostQE is very small, with a correlation coefficient r = 0.13.
- For the example burst GRB #7301, the observed PreQE is about 40 s, PostQE about 70 s, and QT about 100 s; after dividing by a redshift factor of 3, the active periods are about 13 s and 23 s (compatible with model predictions of about 20 s), but the total burst duration including QT is about 73 s (roughly three times the model duration).

## Context

This work extends the statistical analysis of GRB time structures by Nakar and Piran (2002), who identified three time scales in GRB light curves including quiescent times. It directly tests two competing interpretations: the wind modulation model (WMM) proposed by Ramirez-Ruiz et al. (2001) and the dormant inner engine scenario. The results favor the dormant engine model and provide constraints for specific inner engine models, including the collapsar model (Woosley 1993) and the quark deconfinement model (Cheng et al. 1993; Datta et al. 1995; Ouyed et al. 2002). The analysis enables future work to distinguish between different physical mechanisms for dormancy, such as jet interruption by Kelvin-Helmholtz instabilities (Woosley et al. 1999) or stellar core fragmentation (Osborne et al. 2004).
