# Azimuthal Anisotropy at high transverse momentum in $p$-$p$ and $p$-$A$ collisions

> We explore the possibility that the initial transverse momentum distribution of unpolarized and polarized partons within unpolarized nucleons, both with and without the anisotropy of unpolarized hadrons produced in the fragmentation of outgoing partons, could lead to the observed azimuthal anisotropy of high transverse momentum (high-$p_T$) hadrons produced in high energy proton-proton ($p$-$p$) or proton-ion ($p$-$A$) collisions. Including simple Gaussian forms for transverse momentum dependent parton distribution functions (PDF) and fragmentation functions, and assuming an $A^{1/3}$ enhancement of a PDF in $p$-$Pb$ collisions, we show that the observed anisotropy, with \emph{no modification} to the angle integrated spectra ($R_{pA}=1$) for 5~GeV~$\lesssim p_T\lesssim 50$~GeV, can be straightforwardly understood as arising from a few processes dominated by gluon-gluon to gluon-gluon scattering.

## Key claim

The observed azimuthal anisotropy ($v_2$) of high-transverse-momentum hadrons in proton-proton and proton-lead collisions can arise entirely from initial-state effects, specifically from intrinsic transverse momentum and spin-polarization dependent parton distributions and fragmentation functions, without any modification to the angle-integrated spectrum ($R_{pA}=1$), contrary to the final-state energy-loss mechanism invoked in heavy-ion collisions.

## Method

The authors compute the differential cross section for unpolarized hadron production in proton-proton and proton-lead collisions using leading-order 2-to-2 partonic scattering, incorporating transverse-momentum-dependent parton distribution functions (TMD-PDFs) and fragmentation functions (TMD-FFs) with Gaussian forms. They include both spin-independent distributions and spin-polarization dependent contributions: the Boer-Mulders function for linearly polarized gluons in the initial state and the Collins function for linearly polarized gluons fragmenting to unpolarized hadrons. For proton-lead collisions, the mean intrinsic transverse momentum of the proton parton is enhanced by a factor $A^{1/3}$ relative to proton-proton collisions, while the angle-integrated spectrum remains unchanged. The elliptic anisotropy $v_2$ is extracted from the azimuthal correlation between the hadron direction and the net transverse momentum vector of the two incoming hard partons, which defines an approximate event plane.

## Key numbers

- The calculation covers the transverse momentum range $5\ \text{GeV} \lesssim p_T \lesssim 50\ \text{GeV}$ for hadrons in both $p$-$p$ and $p$-$Pb$ collisions.
- For $p$-$p$ collisions at $\sqrt{s}=13\ \text{TeV}$, the product of the Boer-Mulders and Collins functions is constrained to $10\%-40\%$ of the Soffer bound ($0.1 \leq b\cdot B \leq 0.4$).
- The mean intrinsic transverse momentum squared for $p$-$p$ collisions is taken as $\langle k_\perp^2 \rangle_{pp} = 1\ \text{GeV}^2$; for $p$-$Pb$ collisions it is enhanced to $\langle k_\perp^2 \rangle_{pA} = A^{1/3}\ \text{GeV}^2$.
- The mean transverse momentum for pion TMD fragmentation is fixed at $\langle k_\perp^2 \rangle^{1/2} = 0.8\ \text{GeV}$.
- The polarization-dependent contribution dominates the anisotropy for $p_T \gtrsim 10\ \text{GeV}$.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of transverse-momentum-dependent parton distributions and spin-polarization effects, previously applied to Drell-Yan and semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering processes by Boer (1997), Mulders (2000), and Pisano (2013), to explain the puzzling high-$p_T$ azimuthal anisotropy observed in small collision systems by ATLAS (2016, 2019). It directly contradicts the expectation from heavy-ion phenomenology that such anisotropy requires final-state energy loss in a quark-gluon plasma, as no jet quenching is observed in these systems. The results enable future calculations of joint $x$-$k_\perp$ distributions for proton-nucleus collisions and motivate studies of spin-polarization dependent multiple scattering in nuclear matter.
