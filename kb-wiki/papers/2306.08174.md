# Classical and Bayesian error analysis of the relativistic mean-field model for doubly magic nuclei

> The information-geometric statistical analysis on the stability of model reductions, reported previously [Imbrišak and Nomura, Phys. Rev. C 107, 034304 (2023)] with a focus on the manifold boundary approximation method in the application to the nuclear density-dependent point-coupling model of infinite nuclear matter, is extended to the numerically more challenging case of finite nuclei. A simple procedure is presented for determining the binding energies of doubly magic nuclei within the relativistic mean-field framework using the Woods-Saxon potential. The proposed procedure, employing the Fisher information matrix combined with algorithmic differentiation, is shown to provide reliable estimates of parameter uncertainties of the nuclear energy density functional for finite nuclei, while reducing the time-consuming sampling of the parameter space, which would be required in the numerically more involved Bayesian statistical techniques.

# Classical and Bayesian error analysis of the relativistic mean-field model for doubly magic nuclei

## Key claim

The Fisher information matrix (FIM) combined with algorithmic differentiation provides reliable estimates of parameter uncertainties for the relativistic mean-field description of finite nuclei, while avoiding the time-consuming parameter-space sampling required by Bayesian Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods. This claim is conditional on the use of a Woods-Saxon potential as a simplified proxy for the full density-dependent point-coupling model, and on the assumption that the model errors are approximately Gaussian.

## Method

The authors implemented a relativistic mean-field (RMF) procedure in PYTHON using a Woods-Saxon potential to compute binding energies, charge radii, and single-particle energies for the doubly magic nuclei ⁴He, ¹⁶O, and ⁴⁰Ca. They computed the Fisher information matrix (FIM) using algorithmic differentiation via the AUTOGRAD package to obtain parameter uncertainties, and compared these to uncertainties obtained from Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling using the EMCEE package with 24 chains of length 1000. The model had 11 free parameters (after excluding the isospin-asymmetry parameter κ due to N=Z nuclei) and was fitted to 23 data points consisting of charge radii and single-neutron and single-proton energies.

## Key numbers

- The data set comprised 23 data points: charge radii for ⁴He (1.65 fm), ¹⁶O (2.41 fm), and ⁴⁰Ca (3.29 fm), plus single-particle energies for occupied neutron and proton states in these nuclei (e.g., neutron 1s₁/₂ energies: -25.30 MeV in ⁴He, -43.20 MeV in ¹⁶O, -53.34 MeV in ⁴⁰Ca)
- A homoscedastic error of 0.1 fm for charge radii and 0.1 MeV for single-particle energies was assumed
- The optimal maximal total quantum number was N_F ≈ 5, where the reduced χ² value was approximately 1
- The optimal numerical differentiation step for comparing FIM components was h ≈ 10⁻⁴ across the entire range of N_F
- FIM-derived uncertainties (σ_FIM) and MCMC-derived uncertainties (σ_MCMC) agreed within statistical significance, with all Z-scores less than 1 (e.g., for V₀: σ_FIM = 0.2228 MeV, σ_MCMC = 0.1941 MeV, |Z| = 0.39 and 0.45 respectively)
- The MCMC chains stabilized after approximately 50 initial burn-in steps

## Context

This work extends the information-geometric analysis previously applied to infinite nuclear matter by Imbrišak and Nomura (2023) to the numerically more challenging case of finite nuclei. It builds on the manifold boundary approximation method (MBAM) developed by Transtrum and collaborators (2011, 2014, 2015, 2016) and previously applied to nuclear density functionals by Nikšić and collaborators (2016, 2017). The results enable future application of this FIM-based error analysis to full density-dependent point-coupling models such as DD-PC1 for realistic studies of finite nuclei, including open-shell systems where pairing correlations must be included.
