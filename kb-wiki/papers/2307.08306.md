# System size dependence of pre-equilibrium and applicability of hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions

> We simulate the space-time dynamics of high-energy collisions based on a microscopic kinetic description, in order to determine the range of applicability of an effective description in relativistic viscous hydrodynamics. We find that hydrodynamics provides a quantitatively accurate description of collective flow when the average inverse Reynolds number $\mathrm{Re}^{-1}$ is sufficiently small and the early pre-equilibrium stage is properly accounted for. By determining the breakdown of hydrodynamics as a function of system size and energy, we find that it is quantitatively accurate in central lead-lead collisions at LHC energies, but should not be used in typical proton-lead or proton-proton collisions, where the development of collective flow can not accurately be described within hydrodynamics.

# System size dependence of pre-equilibrium and applicability of hydrodynamics in heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

Hydrodynamics provides a quantitatively accurate description of collective flow only when the average inverse Reynolds number Re⁻¹ is sufficiently small (≤ 0.75) and the early pre-equilibrium stage is properly accounted for via a scaled initial condition; for typical proton-proton and proton-lead collisions, the development of collective flow cannot be accurately described within hydrodynamics, while central lead-lead collisions at LHC energies are within the regime of applicability.

## Method

The authors simulate the space-time dynamics of high-energy collisions using the Boltzmann equation in conformal relaxation time approximation (RTA) for massless on-shell bosons, assuming boost invariance and vanishing longitudinal momentum and transverse anisotropy at initial time. They compare these kinetic theory results to relativistic viscous hydrodynamics simulations using the vHLLE code, both with naive initial conditions and with a locally scaled initial condition derived from Bjorken flow to correct for pre-equilibrium differences. They also perform hybrid simulations where the system evolves first in kinetic theory and switches to hydrodynamics when the inverse Reynolds number drops below a threshold.

## Key numbers

- Hydrodynamics becomes accurate at the 5% level when the average inverse Reynolds number satisfies ⟨Re⁻¹⟩_ε ≤ 0.75
- The onset of transverse expansion occurs at τ_Exp ∼ 0.2 R, mostly independent of opacity
- The timescale for applicability of hydrodynamics follows a power law τ_Hydro ∝ γ̂⁻⁴/³ before transverse expansion
- The curves for onset of transverse expansion and hydrodynamic applicability cross at opacity γ̂ ∼ 3
- Agreement between scaled hydrodynamics and kinetic theory remains reasonable down to γ̂ ≳ 4
- The large opacity limit of elliptic flow ε_p is approximately 0.25
- Naive hydrodynamics with unmodified initialization at τ₀ = 0.4–1 fm underestimates elliptic flow and vastly overestimates transverse energy

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies (Ambrus 2022) on pre-equilibrium dynamics in heavy-ion collisions by systematically mapping the regime of hydrodynamic applicability as a function of system size and energy. The study uses the opacity parameter γ̂ introduced by Kurkela (2019) to characterize the total interaction rate. The results contradict the assumption that hydrodynamics can be applied to small systems such as proton-proton and proton-lead collisions, which have been attempted following the discovery of collective flow in small systems (Nagle 2018). The findings enable a quantitative criterion (Re⁻¹ ≤ 0.75) for when hydrodynamics can be reliably used, and suggest that oxygen-oxygen collisions at LHC energies may cover the transition regime between hydrodynamic and non-hydrodynamic behavior.
