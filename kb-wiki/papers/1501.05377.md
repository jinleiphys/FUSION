# Modern Studies of the Deuteron: from the Lab Frame to the Light Front

> We review the recent progress made in studies of deuteron structure at small internucleon distances. This progress is largely facilitated by the new generation of experiments in deuteron electrodisintegration carried out at unprecedentedly high momentum transfer. The theoretical analysis of these data confirms the onset of the high energy eikonal regime in the scattering process which allows one to separate long range nuclear effects from the effects genuinely related to the short distance structure of the deuteron. Our conclusion is that for the first time the deuteron is probed at relative momenta beyond 300 MeV/c without dominating long range effects. As a result, at these large nucleon momenta the cross section is sensitive to the nuclear dynamics at sub-fermi distances. Due to large internal momenta involved we are dealing with the relativistic bound state that is best described by the light-cone momentum distribution of nucleons in the deuteron. We present the first attempt of extracting the deuteron light-cone momentum distribution function from data and discuss the importance of this quantity for studies of QCD structure of the bound nucleon in deep inelastic scattering off the deuteron. We conclude the review giving an outlook of the next generation of high energy experiments which will extend our reach to much smaller distances in the deuteron.

## Key claim

The paper asserts that for the first time, the deuteron can be probed at relative momenta beyond 300 MeV/c without dominating long range effects, but only under the specific condition of high energy kinematics where the transferred momentum is several GeV/c and the virtuality Q² exceeds 1 GeV², allowing the eikonal regime to separate long range nuclear effects from short distance structure.

## Method

The authors review exclusive deuteron electrodisintegration experiments (d(e,e'p)n) performed at Jefferson Lab using the CLAS detector in Hall B and the two high resolution spectrometers in Hall A, measuring cross sections at four-momentum transfers Q² between 0.8 and 5.5 GeV² and missing momenta up to 600 MeV/c. The theoretical analysis employs the generalized eikonal approximation (GEA) to describe final state interactions, comparing experimental cross sections to plane-wave impulse approximation (PWIA) calculations through the ratio R = σ_exp / σ_PWIA and reduced cross sections σ_red = σ_exp / (K σ_ep). The analysis isolates kinematics where final state interactions are minimized by selecting forward or backward recoil nucleon angles relative to the momentum transfer direction.

## Key numbers

- Missing momenta probed up to 600 MeV/c in the CLAS experiment and up to 500 ± 20 MeV/c in the Hall A experiment
- Four-momentum transfer ranges: 1.75 < Q² < 5.5 GeV² for CLAS; Q² = 0.8 ± 0.25, 2.1 ± 0.25, and 3.5 ± 0.25 GeV² for Hall A
- The NN inelastic threshold corresponds to deuteron internal momenta above approximately 370 MeV/c
- The ΔΔ component of the deuteron wave function is constrained experimentally to ≤ 1% overall contribution
- The pn component is dominant for deuteron internal momenta up to 650 MeV/c, with reliable understanding only up to 400 MeV/c

## Context

This review extends earlier low and intermediate energy experiments at Kharkov (Antufev 1974, 1975) and Saclay (Bussiere 1981, Turck-Chieze 1984) that reached missing momenta up to 500 MeV/c but were dominated by long range processes such as final state interactions, meson exchange currents, and intermediate isobar contributions. The work also builds on the Mainz experiment (Blomqvist 1998, Boeglin 2008) that extended measurements to 928 MeV/c but found cross sections dominated by long range effects. The new Jefferson Lab data enable the first extraction of the deuteron light-cone momentum distribution function and open the possibility for studying deuteron structure at internal momenta in and above the GeV region after the 12 GeV energy upgrade at Jefferson Lab.
