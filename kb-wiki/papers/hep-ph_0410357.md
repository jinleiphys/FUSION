# Bose-Einstein correlations in the Quantum Clan Approach

> We propose novel numerical method of modelling Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) observed among identical (bosonic) particles produced in multiparticle production reactions. We argue that the most natural approach is to work directly in the momentum space in which Bose statistics of secondaries reveals itself in their tendency to bunch in a specific way in the available phase space. Because such procedure is essentially identical to the clan model of multiparticle distributions proposed some time ago, therefore we call it the Quantum Clan Model.

## Key claim

The authors propose that Bose-Einstein correlations (BEC) among identical bosons produced in multiparticle reactions can be modelled directly in momentum space by treating particles as bunching into "elementary emitting cells" (EEC), a procedure they argue is essentially identical to the clan model of multiparticle distributions. The hedge is that this approach is presented as a novel numerical method rather than a definitive physical theory, and the authors note that the actual form of the seed energy function \(f(E)\) must "reflect somehow our a priori knowledge of the particular collision process under consideration."

## Method

The authors construct events by first selecting a seed particle of energy \(E^{(1)}_1\) and charge \(Q^{(1)}\) from an assumed function \(f(E) = \exp(-E/T)\), where \(T\) is a temperature-like parameter. Additional particles of the same charge are added to form an elementary emitting cell (EEC) according to a distribution \(P(E) = P_0 \cdot f(E)\), where \(P_0\) is a chemical-potential-like parameter, yielding a geometrical (Bose-Einstein) distribution for the cell multiplicity. To produce a finite width in the two-particle correlation function \(C_2(Q)\), each additional particle's energy is smeared around the seed energy using a distribution \(G(E^{(1)}_1 - E^{(1)}_i)\) with width \(\sigma\). The procedure is repeated until the available energy is exhausted, with corrections applied for energy-momentum conservation and charge neutrality.

## Key numbers

- The multiplicity distribution of particles in each elementary emitting cell follows a geometrical (Bose-Einstein) distribution, leading to an overall Pólya-Aeppli distribution \(P_{PA}(n)\) rather than a negative binomial distribution \(P_{NB}(n)\).
- The two-particle correlation function \(C_2(Q=|p_1-p_2|)\) is shown for one-dimensional phase space; two different parameter sets produce essentially similar \(P(n)\) but "drastically different" \(C_2(Q)\) (no explicit numerical values for the correlation function are given in the truncated text).
- The model parameters are: temperature \(T\), chemical-potential-like parameter \(P_0\) (related to chemical potential \(\mu = T \cdot \ln P_0\)), and the width \(\sigma\) of the energy smearing distribution \(G\).
- The cell size in rapidity space, \(\delta y\), is described as a fitted parameter in prior work (O.M.T. 1999) and is related to the source size via \(R \sim 1/\delta y\).

## Context

This work extends the clan model of multiparticle distributions (Giovannini and Van Hove, 1986) by incorporating quantum statistical effects directly into the production mechanism, rather than applying afterburner modifications to existing Monte Carlo event generators. It builds on earlier proposals by O.M.T. (1999) and Zajc (1987) that introduced bunching of identical bosons in phase space, and contrasts with conventional approaches (e.g., those reviewed in BEC reviews) that deduce source sizes from correlation functions. The quantum clan model suggests that BEC measurements primarily characterize the elementary emitting cells rather than the overall hadronization source, potentially resolving "strange" results obtained from BEC data.
