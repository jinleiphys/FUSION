# How big are the smallest drops of quark-gluon plasma?

> Using holographic duality, we present results for both head-on and off-center collisions of Gaussian shock waves in strongly coupled $\mathcal N = 4$ supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory. The shock waves superficially resemble Lorentz contracted colliding protons. The collisions results in the formation of a plasma whose evolution is well described by viscous hydrodynamics. The size of the produced droplet is $R \sim 1/T_{\rm eff}$ where $T_{\rm eff}$ is the effective temperature, which is the characteristic microscopic scale in strongly coupled plasma. These results demonstrate the applicability of hydrodynamics to microscopically small systems and bolster the notion that hydrodynamics can be applied to heavy-light ion collisions as well as some proton-proton collisions.

# How big are the smallest drops of quark-gluon plasma?

## Key claim
Using holographic duality, the author demonstrates that droplets of quark-gluon plasma produced in collisions of Gaussian shock waves in strongly coupled N=4 supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory can be as small as R ~ 1/T_eff (the characteristic microscopic scale) and still have their evolution well described by viscous hydrodynamics, but only after a hydrodynamization time t_hydro ≈ 1.2 (in units where μ=1) and only inside a region where the hydrodynamic residual Δ < 0.2.

## Method
The author numerically solves Einstein's equations in asymptotically AdS_5 spacetime for collisions of gravitational shock waves dual to Gaussian energy density profiles in strongly coupled N=4 SYM, with transverse width σ=3 and impact parameters b=0 (head-on) and b=3 (off-center). The exact expectation value of the SYM stress tensor ⟨T^{μν}⟩ is extracted from the subleading metric coefficients, and a hydrodynamic residual Δ is constructed by comparing the full stress tensor to the hydrodynamic constitutive relations (including first and second order gradient corrections) using the fluid velocity and energy density obtained from the eigenvectors and eigenvalues of ⟨T^{μν}⟩.

## Key numbers
- Shock wave transverse width: σ = 3 (in units where μ=1)
- Impact parameters studied: b = 0 (head-on) and b = 3 (off-center, along x-direction)
- Hydrodynamization time at spatial origin: t_hydro ≈ 1.2 (in units where μ=1)
- At t = 1.5 (1.25 t_hydro), the droplet transverse radius is R ~ 3, equal to the shock width σ
- Average effective temperature inside the Δ=0.2 surface at t=1.5: T_eff ~ 0.25 (in units where μ=1)
- At t=0, energy and momentum densities are at the order 10% level just the linear superposition of incoming "protons"
- Shear viscosity: η = (1/(3π T_eff)) ε
- Second order transport coefficient τ_Π = (2 - log 2)/(2π T_eff)

## Context
This work extends the author's previous study of localized shock wave collisions with planar shock waves (Chesler, 2015), which resembled proton-nucleus collisions, to collisions of two localized Gaussian shocks that resemble proton-proton collisions. The results support the surprising applicability of hydrodynamics to very small systems, consistent with experimental observations of collective flow in heavy-light ion and proton-proton collisions at RHIC and the LHC (Chatrchyan, 2013; Abelev, 2012; Aad, 2012; Adare, 2013, 2015; Aad, 2015). The work enables further investigation of whether hydrodynamics can be applied to systems as small as a proton, and suggests that generalizations to confining theories could be explored.
