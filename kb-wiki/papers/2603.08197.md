# E0 transition strengths as a tool to constraint model parameters. Application to even-even Xe isotopes

> Background: In any nuclear structure model, the accurate description of E0 transition rates constitutes a significant challenge; at the same time, these observables provide a stringent constraint on the admissible parameter space of the model. Purpose: This work pretend to study the value of certain $ρ^2(E0)$ values or ratios over the whole parameter space of the interacting boson model. As an application, the case of the even-even Xe isotopes will be explored. Method: The interacting boson model will be considered for the calculation of $ρ^2(E0)$ values for some key transitions and to explore how these observables change over the parameter space of the model. Results: Several key $ρ^2(E0)$ values or ratios are considered and contour plots in the parameter space of the interacting boson model (Casten triangle) are computed. The results for even-even Xe isotopes are superimposed on the contour plots. Conclusions: The presented analysis confirms that $ρ^2(E0)$ values and, in particular, certain ratios allow to constraint the parameter space of the interacting boson model. There are certain regions where the $ρ^2(E0)$ values cannot be altered by the fine tuning of the Hamiltonian parameters.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that $\rho^2(E0)$ values and their ratios can effectively constrain the parameter space of the interacting boson model (IBM), but there exist certain regions in the Casten triangle where these values cannot be altered by fine-tuning the Hamiltonian parameters, limiting the model's ability to adjust to experimental data.

## Method

The authors employed the extended consistent-Q formalism (ECQF) of the interacting boson model to calculate $\rho^2(E0)$ values for key transitions involving $0^+$ and $2^+$ states across the entire Casten triangle parameter space. They performed a least-squares fit to experimental excitation energies and $B(E2)$ reduced transition probabilities for the even-even $^{114-134}$Xe isotope chain, using five Hamiltonian parameters per nucleus, and then computed $\rho^2(E0)$ values and ratios using the $\hat{n}_d$ operator as the E0 transition operator.

## Key numbers

- For the Xe isotope chain, the fitted Hamiltonian parameters include $\varepsilon$ ranging from 523.4 keV ($^{124}$Xe) to 881.9 keV ($^{134}$Xe), $\kappa$ from -19.22 keV ($^{118}$Xe) to -40.00 keV ($^{128,130}$Xe), and $\chi$ from -0.97 ($^{114}$Xe) to 0.00 ($^{128}$Xe)
- The charge radius operator parameters were determined as $\expval{r^2}_0 = (-3.3 \pm 0.4)$ fm$^2$, $\gamma = (0.027 \pm 0.003)$ fm$^2$, and $\beta = (0.05 \pm 0.03)$ fm$^2$
- The ratio $\rho^2(E0: 0_2^+ \rightarrow 0_1^+) / \rho^2(E0: 0_3^+ \rightarrow 0_1^+)$ varies over approximately 9 orders of magnitude across the Casten triangle
- The ratio $\rho^2(E0: 2_2^+ \rightarrow 2_1^+) / \rho^2(E0: 2_3^+ \rightarrow 2_1^+)$ varies over approximately 6 orders of magnitude, with a rapid variation of 4 orders of magnitude in a small region between the U(5)-SU(3) leg and the arc of regularity
- The ratio $\rho^2(E0: 0_2^+ \rightarrow 0_1^+) / \rho^2(E0: 2_2^+ \rightarrow 2_1^+)$ varies by approximately 3 orders of magnitude
- For Xe isotopes, $\rho^2(E0)$ values for $2^+$ states are 4-5 times larger than for $0^+$ states

## Context

This work extends previous studies of E0 transitions in the IBM, which generally considered only $0^+$ states (VonBrentano 2004), by also analyzing $2^+$ states and demonstrating that non-vanishing $\rho^2(E0)$ values can be obtained without configuration mixing as noted by Wood (1999). The analysis of Xe isotopes builds on earlier IBM-2 calculations by Puddu (1980) and more recent Bohr Hamiltonian calculations by Baid (2024), confirming that Xe isotopes do not possess O(6) dynamical symmetry despite being close to $\gamma$-unstable shapes. The results enable future experimental measurements of $\rho^2(E0)$ in Xe isotopes to directly constrain the IBM Hamiltonian parameters, particularly around mid-shell where small parameter changes produce significant variations in the ratios.
