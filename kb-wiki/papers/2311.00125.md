# Correlation between the symmetry energy slope and the deconfinement phase transition

> We study how the nuclear symmetry energy slope ($L$) can affect the hadron-quark phase transition and neutron star properties. We show that the main physical quantities as the critical chemical potential and pressure are strongly influenced by the symmetry energy slope. In extreme cases, the total amount of deconfined quarks can reach up to 99$¥%$ of the hybrid star mass.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the nuclear symmetry energy slope $L$ strongly influences the hadron-quark deconfinement phase transition, with higher values of $L$ (above about 100 MeV) producing critical chemical potentials below 1050 MeV and phase transition densities below 1.3 times nuclear saturation density, which the authors argue disfavors such high slopes and the scalar-isovector $¥delta$ meson used to produce them.

## Method

The authors construct hybrid star equations of state using an extended quantum hadrodynamics model for the hadronic phase (including either $¥omega¥rho$ nonlinear coupling to reduce the slope or the scalar-isovector $¥delta$ meson to increase it) and a thermodynamically consistent vector MIT bag model for the quark phase. They apply the Maxwell construction for the deconfinement phase transition and solve the TOV equations to obtain mass-radius relations, varying the symmetry energy slope $L$ from 44 to 116 MeV while fixing the symmetry energy at saturation to $S_0 = 31.7$ MeV, and using three values of the quark vector coupling $G_V$ (0.38, 0.40, and 0.42 fm$^2$).

## Key numbers

- Critical chemical potential $¥mu_0$ ranges from 1391 MeV (for $G_V = 0.42$ fm$^2$, $L = 44$ MeV) down to 996 MeV (for $G_V = 0.38$ fm$^2$, $L = 116$ MeV)
- Critical pressure $p_0$ ranges from 180.2 MeV/fm$^3$ (for $G_V = 0.42$ fm$^2$, $L = 44$ MeV) down to 0.92 MeV/fm$^3$ (for $G_V = 0.38$ fm$^2$, $L = 116$ MeV)
- Energy density gap $¥Delta¥epsilon$ ranges from 24.1 MeV/fm$^3$ (for $G_V = 0.40$ fm$^2$, $L = 76$ MeV) up to 214.6 MeV/fm$^3$ (for $G_V = 0.38$ fm$^2$, $L = 116$ MeV)
- Hadronic number density at phase transition $n_c/n_0$ ranges from 3.79 (for $G_V = 0.42$ fm$^2$, $L = 44$ MeV) down to 0.60 (for $G_V = 0.38$ fm$^2$, $L = 116$ MeV)
- Maximum hybrid star masses range from 2.16 $M_¥odot$ (for $G_V = 0.42$ fm$^2$, $L = 44$ MeV) to 2.01 $M_¥odot$ (for $G_V = 0.38$ fm$^2$, $L = 116$ MeV)
- For $L = 116$ MeV, the quark core can constitute up to 99% of the total mass and 96% of the radius of the most massive hybrid star
- For models satisfying all observational constraints, the quark core mass reaches 81% of the total mass (for $L = 92$ MeV)

## Context

This work extends the study of Lopes et al. (2022) on pure hadronic neutron stars and gravitational wave signals by incorporating the hadron-quark phase transition, motivated by the conflict between CREX and PREX II results summarized by Tagami (2022). The authors assume, following Nature (2020), that all supermassive neutron stars contain quark cores. The results disfavor the scalar-isovector $¥delta$ meson in the standard $¥sigma¥omega¥rho¥delta$ model of quantum hadrodynamics, and support Bayesian analyses such as Mondal (2022) that rule out models with very large slopes. The work enables future studies to constrain the symmetry energy slope through combined observations of neutron star masses, radii, and the properties of the deconfinement phase transition.
