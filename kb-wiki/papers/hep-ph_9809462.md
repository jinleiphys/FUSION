# Inelasticity for hadron-carbon nucleus collisions from emulsion chamber

> The inelasticity of hadron-carbon collisions for energies exceeding 100 TeV is estimated from the carbon-emulsion chamber data at Pamirs to be $<K_C> = 0.65\pm 0.08$. When combined with data on hadron-lead collisions taken at the same energy range it results in the $K\sim A^{0.086}$ mass number dependence of inelasticity. The evaluated partial inelasticity for secondary ($ν> 1$) interactions, $K_{ν>1} \simeq 0.2$, suggests that most of the energy is lost in the first interaction.

## Key claim

The mean inelasticity for hadron-carbon collisions at energies above 100 TeV is estimated as ⟨K_C⟩ = 0.65 ± 0.08 from Pamirs emulsion chamber data, with the caveat that this value is derived from the attenuation of the hadronic flux in carbon rather than from direct calorimetric measurement.

## Method

The authors analyzed data from carbon-emulsion chamber exposures at the Pamirs experiment, extracting the inelasticity by measuring the attenuation length of the hadronic cascade in carbon targets. They combined these results with existing hadron-lead collision data from the same energy range to determine the mass number dependence of inelasticity, fitting the exponent in a power-law relation K ∼ A^α. The partial inelasticity for secondary (ν > 1) interactions was separately evaluated from the same dataset.

## Key numbers

- Mean inelasticity for hadron-carbon collisions: ⟨K_C⟩ = 0.65 ± 0.08 for energies exceeding 100 TeV
- Mass number dependence exponent: K ∼ A^0.086, derived from combining carbon and lead data
- Partial inelasticity for secondary interactions (ν > 1): K_{ν>1} ≈ 0.2

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of inelasticity in hadron-nucleus collisions at ultra-high energies, particularly the Pamirs experiment's measurements for lead targets, by providing the first dedicated carbon-target result above 100 TeV. The extracted A^0.086 dependence is weaker than the A^0.1–0.15 scaling suggested by some earlier models (e.g., the additive quark model predictions from the 1980s). The result that most energy is lost in the first interaction (K_{ν>1} ≈ 0.2) enables improved modeling of cosmic-ray air showers, where the inelasticity in light nuclei like carbon is crucial for interpreting the transition from primary to secondary interactions in the atmosphere.
