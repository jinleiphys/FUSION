# Nucleon form factors in dispersively improved Chiral Effective Field Theory II: Electromagnetic form factors

> We study the nucleon electromagnetic form factors (EM FFs) using a recently developed method combining Chiral Effective Field Theory ($χ$EFT) and dispersion analysis. The spectral functions on the two-pion cut at $t > 4 M_π^2$ are constructed using the elastic unitarity relation and an $N/D$ representation. $χ$EFT is used to calculate the real functions $J_\pm^1 (t) = f_\pm^1(t)/F_π(t)$ (ratios of the complex $ππ\rightarrow N \bar N$ partial-wave amplitudes and the timelike pion FF), which are free of $ππ$ rescattering. Rescattering effects are included through the empirical timelike pion FF $|F_π(t)|^2$. The method allows us to compute the isovector EM spectral functions up to $t \sim 1$ GeV$^2$ with controlled accuracy (LO, NLO, and partial N2LO). With the spectral functions we calculate the isovector nucleon EM FFs and their derivatives at $t = 0$ (EM radii, moments) using subtracted dispersion relations. We predict the values of higher FF derivatives with minimal uncertainties and explain their collective behavior. We estimate the individual proton and neutron FFs by adding an empirical parametrization of the isoscalar sector. Excellent agreement with the present low-$Q^2$ FF data is achieved up to $\sim$0.5 GeV$^2$ for $G_E$, and up to $\sim$0.2 GeV$^2$ for $G_M$. Our results can be used to guide the analysis of low-$Q^2$ elastic scattering data and the extraction of the proton charge radius.

## Key claim

The method combines Chiral Effective Field Theory (χEFT) with dispersion analysis to compute isovector nucleon electromagnetic form factors up to t ∼ 1 GeV² with controlled accuracy, but the agreement with experimental data is limited to Q² ≤ 0.5 GeV² for the electric form factor and Q² ≤ 0.2 GeV² for the magnetic form factor, and the magnetic spectral function calculation is restricted to NLO accuracy because N2LO corrections could not be estimated.

## Method

The authors construct isovector electromagnetic spectral functions on the two-pion cut using the elastic unitarity relation and an N/D representation, where χEFT calculates the real functions J±¹(t) = f±¹(t)/Fπ(t) (ratios of ππ → N̄N partial-wave amplitudes to the timelike pion form factor) that are free of ππ rescattering. Rescattering effects are included through the empirical timelike pion form factor |Fπ(t)|² from e⁺e⁻ → π⁺π⁻ data using a Gounaris-Sakurai parametrization with ρ-ω mixing. The isovector form factors and their derivatives at t = 0 are then evaluated using subtracted dispersion relations, and individual proton and neutron form factors are obtained by adding an empirical parametrization of the isoscalar sector with ω and φ poles.

## Key numbers

- The isovector electric radius from the NLO+N2LO calculation is ⟨r²⟩_E^V = (0.33, 0.43) fm², compared to 0.416(8) fm² from Lorenz (2012) and 0.405(36) fm² from Hoferichter (2016)
- The isovector magnetic radius from the NLO calculation is ⟨r²⟩_M^V = (2.87, 3.50) fm², compared to 1.78⁺⁰·¹⁰₋₀·₁₁ fm² from Lorenz (2012) and 1.81(7) fm² from Epstein (2014)
- The proton electric radius from NLO+N2LO is ⟨r²⟩_E^p = (0.46, 0.94) fm², compared to the PDG range of (0.706, 0.707) fm² from electronic hydrogen and (0.755, 0.777) fm² from muonic hydrogen
- The neutron electric radius from NLO+N2LO is ⟨r²⟩_E^n = (−0.29, 0.18) fm², compared to the PDG value of −0.1161(22) fm²
- The timelike pion form factor |Fπ(t)|² reaches a value of ∼2 at t ∼ 0.2 GeV² and ∼10 at t ∼ 0.4 GeV²
- The ρ resonance appears at t = M_ρ² ∼ 0.6 GeV²
- The two-pion threshold is at t = 4 M_π²

## Context

This work extends the dispersively improved χEFT method previously applied to the nucleon isoscalar-scalar form factor by Alarcón and Weiss (2017) to the electromagnetic form factors. It improves upon conventional χEFT calculations of nucleon form factors by Gasser (1987), Bernard (1996), Becher (1999), Kubis (2000), and Kaiser (2003) by incorporating ππ rescattering effects through the empirical pion form factor rather than perturbatively. The results can guide analysis of low-Q² elastic scattering data and extraction of the proton charge radius, addressing the proton radius puzzle raised by Pohl (2010) and subsequent measurements.
