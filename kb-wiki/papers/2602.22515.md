# Confined and Deconfined Phases of Qubit Regularized Lattice Gauge Theories

> We construct simple qubit-regularized Hamiltonian lattice gauge theories formulated in the monomer--dimer--tensor-network (MDTN) basis that are free of sign problems in the pure gauge sector. These models naturally realize both confined and deconfined phases. Using classical Monte Carlo methods, we investigate the associated finite-temperature phase transitions and show that they exhibit the expected universality classes of conventional SU(N) lattice gauge theories in various spacetime dimensions. Furthermore, we argue that second-order quantum phase transitions separating the confined and deconfined phases are likely to exist. Such critical points would provide a nonperturbative route to defining continuum limits of qubit-regularized gauge theories, potentially allowing Yang--Mills theory and related continuum gauge theories to emerge from finite-dimensional lattice constructions.

# Confined and Deconfined Phases of Qubit Regularized Lattice Gauge Theories

## Key claim

Simple qubit-regularized Hamiltonian lattice gauge theories formulated in the monomer-dimer-tensor-network (MDTN) basis, which are free of sign problems in the pure gauge sector, can exhibit both confined and deconfined phases; the author argues that second-order quantum phase transitions separating these phases are likely to exist, which would provide a nonperturbative route to defining continuum limits of qubit-regularized gauge theories, potentially allowing Yang-Mills theory to emerge from finite-dimensional lattice constructions.

## Method

The author constructs qubit-regularized lattice gauge theories by restricting the allowed irreducible representations on links to anti-symmetric irreps of SU(N) (the ASQR scheme), using the MDTN basis where physical states are described by networks of monomer and dimer tensors with gauge invariance manifest. A Hamiltonian H = sum over links of E_ell - delta times sum over plaquettes of (U_P + U_P^dagger) is introduced, where E_ell is diagonal in the MDTN basis and U_P changes irreps on plaquette links while preserving gauge invariance. Classical Monte Carlo methods (loop Monte Carlo algorithms) are used to study finite-temperature confinement-deconfinement transitions, and for quasi-one-dimensional systems the model is mapped to the transverse-field Ising model (for SU(2)) or the three-state Potts model (for SU(3)), with density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) methods employed for one-dimensional systems.

## Key numbers

- For SU(2) qubit regularization, link irreps are restricted to lambda_ell = singlet (1) and doublet (2); for SU(3), link irreps are restricted to lambda_ell = singlet (1), triplet (3), and anti-triplet (bar-3)
- The Hamiltonian parameter delta controls the transition: at delta = 0 the ground state has all link irreps equal to the singlet, producing confinement; as delta approaches infinity, non-singlet irreps are not energetically suppressed, producing deconfinement
- For the SU(2) plaquette chain, when kappa_r = 2, delta = 1, and kappa_c = 0, the transverse-field Ising model mapping is critical, described by a conformal field theory of free massless Majorana fermions
- The susceptibility chi is defined as chi = (1/L^d) sum over x,y of Z^(x,y)/Z, where L^d is the spatial lattice volume, and is expected to approach a constant in the confined phase and scale as L^d in the deconfined phase in the thermodynamic limit

## Context

This work extends the qubit regularization framework introduced by Chandrasekharan (2024) and builds on the MDTN basis construction from MDTN (2025) and Liu (2022). The finite-temperature confinement-deconfinement transitions are shown to follow the order-disorder physics of Z_N spin models as established by Susskind and Yaffe (1982). The quasi-one-dimensional SU(2) plaquette chain maps to the transverse-field Ising model, reproducing the flow from a critical Majorana CFT to a massive E_8-invariant quantum field theory as shown by Zamolodchikov. This work enables future studies using continuous-time path-integral Monte Carlo methods developed by Karna (2025) to search for second-order quantum critical points in higher spatial dimensions, potentially allowing Yang-Mills theory or other interacting gauge theories to emerge from finite-dimensional lattice constructions.
