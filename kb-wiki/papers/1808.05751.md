# Large Baryon Densities Achievable in High Energy Heavy Ion Collisions Outside the Central Rapidity Region

> Nuclei are nearly transparent to each other when they collide at high energy, but the collisions do produce high energy density matter in the central rapidity region where most experimental measurements are made. What happens to the receding nuclear fireballs? We calculate the energy loss of the nuclei using the color glass condensate model. We then use a simple space-time picture of the collision to calculate the baryon and energy densities of the receding fireballs. For central collisions of large nuclei at the BNL Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider and the CERN Large Hadron Collider we find baryon densities more than ten times that of normal nuclear matter. These results provide initial conditions for subsequent hydrodynamic evolution and could test the equation of state at very high baryon densities.

## Key claim

The authors claim that in central collisions of large nuclei at RHIC and LHC energies, the receding nuclear fireballs (outside the central rapidity region) achieve baryon densities more than ten times that of normal nuclear matter, but this result depends on the specific choice of the proper time (0.6 fm/c) at which the transition from glasma to quark-gluon plasma is assumed to occur, and the energy density values are less robust than the baryon density values because the excitation energy continues to grow slowly with time.

## Method

The authors used the color glass condensate model (specifically the McLerran-Venugopalan model) to calculate the energy and momentum deposited in the central rapidity region, then imposed energy-momentum conservation on the whole system to compute the rapidity loss and excitation energy of the receding nuclear tubes. They treated the collision as a sum of independent tube-tube collisions at different transverse positions, solving coupled differential equations for the rapidity and effective mass of each tube as functions of proper time, and used a realistic crossover equation of state to estimate temperature and baryon chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- For central Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV, the central core of the gold nucleus loses about 2.9 units of rapidity within the first 0.1-0.2 fm/c, with final rapidity in the center-of-momentum frame of 2.47.
- The excitation energy per baryon in the central core reaches M_P / M_P(tau=0) = 6.97 at tau = 0.6 fm/c, approximately seven times larger than the nucleon rest mass.
- The maximum baryon density is about 3 baryons/fm^3, which is 20 times greater than the normal nuclear matter density of 0.155 nucleons/fm^3.
- The maximum energy density is about 20 GeV/fm^3, much larger than the critical energy density of about 1.0 GeV/fm^3 for quark-gluon plasma formation.
- The average rapidity loss for Au+Au at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV is computed to be about 2.4, consistent with the BRAHMS measurement of 2.05 +0.4/-0.6 for 0-5% centrality.

## Context

This work extends the earlier line of research by Anishetty, Koehler and McLerran (1980), who found that nuclei were significantly compressed and excited when colliding at extreme relativistic energies, and follows the authors' own prior work (Li and Kapusta, 2016) on rapidity loss and nuclear compression. The results provide initial conditions for subsequent hydrodynamic evolution of the baryon-rich fireballs and could test the equation of state at very high baryon densities, complementing the extensive focus on the baryon-free central rapidity region that followed Bjorken (1983).
