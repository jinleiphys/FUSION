# Theory of shell structure and of the "magic" effect in spherical nuclei

> A consistent theory is developed of the volume energy oscillations of spherical nuclei due to sharpness of the Fermi distribution boundary for quasiparticles. The lowest value of the oscillating part of the energy corresponds to a magic nucleus. A formula is obtained for the corresponding limiting momentum of a quasiparticle and it is shown that we have here an isolated point of a temperatureless second-order phase transition. An expression for the discontinuity of the derivative of the energy of the body with respect to the number of particles is obtained in the case of a sharp (step-like) Fermi distribution limit. Comparison with experimental nuclear-mass data permits some conclusions to be drawn regarding the true structure of the boundary layer of the Fermi distribution and regarding its variation with increasing nuclear size. In the region of magic nuclei actually accessible up to the present time, apparently no signs are observed of any appreciably expressed residual phenomenon, such as the Cooper phenomenon, which would result in instability of the energy spectrum of infinite nuclear matter with an absolutely sharp Fermi limit for quasiparticles.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the "magic" cusps observed in nuclear binding energies are macroscopic quantum oscillations arising from the sharpness of the Fermi distribution boundary for quasiparticles in spherical nuclei, rather than being exclusively due to the filling of single-particle shells in a spherically symmetric potential. The authors hedge that this macroscopic theory is an expansion in inverse powers of the magic number and cannot give a quantitative description for extremely light nuclei, where the separation of smooth and oscillating effects becomes impossible.

## Method

The authors apply Poisson's summation formula to the double sum over orbital angular momentum \(l\) and principal quantum number \(n\) of the occupation numbers of quasiparticles in a spherical nucleus, using the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rule for a particle in a spherical well. They identify the saddle-point contribution that yields macroscopic oscillations, deriving a universal periodic function \(\mathfrak{M}(\rho_f)\) that describes the oscillating part of the energy as a function of the dimensionless limiting momentum \(\rho_f = k_f R\). The smooth part of the number of particles \(N(\rho_f)\) is expanded as a power series in \(\rho_f\) with coefficients \(s\) and \(q\) determined by fitting to experimental magic numbers.

## Key numbers

- The magic values of the dimensionless limiting momentum satisfy \(k_f R = \frac{\pi}{2} p\) with \(p = 2, 3, 4, \ldots\) (Eq. 19).
- The surface coefficient in the expansion of \(N(\rho_f)\) is \(s = 1.1\) and the curvature coefficient is \(q = 6.8\) (Eq. 26), determined from magic numbers 82 and 126.
- The theoretical formula for the number of particles in a magic nucleus is \(N = 0.548 p^3 - 2.79 p^2 + 10.7 p\) (Eq. 27), which gives \(N_{theor} = 82\) for \(p=6\) and \(N_{theor} = 126\) for \(p=7\), matching the known magic numbers.
- The discontinuity in the derivative of the oscillating energy at a magic cusp is given by \(\Delta(dE_1/d\rho_f) = 2\rho_f \bar{\varepsilon}\) (Eq. 22), where \(\bar{\varepsilon}\) is the average nucleon binding energy on either side of the cusp.

## Context

This work extends the macroscopic treatment of nuclei initiated by von Weizsacker (1935) and the theory of the phase transition to deformed shapes developed by Nosov (1966), providing a unified explanation of magic numbers as a temperatureless second-order phase transition. The authors contrast their approach with the conventional shell-model explanation of magic numbers (Mayer and Jensen, 1955), arguing that the latter requires excessive parameter fitting and cannot explain why only the "true" magic numbers (28, 50, 82, 126) appear as cusps rather than every filled level. The theory enables prediction of new magic numbers (e.g., \(N = 184\) for \(p=8\), \(N = 270\) for \(p=9\), \(N = 376\) for \(p=10\)) and provides a framework for studying the structure of the Fermi distribution boundary layer in nuclei.
