# Deformation dependence of 2p-radioactivity half-lives: Probe with a new formula across the mass region with Z<82

> Effect of deformation on half-life of two-proton (2p) radioactivity is investigated across the periodic chart for nuclei with Z$<$82. 2p-decay half-lives are estimated by employing our newly proposed semi-empirical formula wherein the nuclear deformation has been incorporated in a phenomenological way. Robustness of the formula is demonstrated as it estimates the measured values quite accurately and, hence, reliably applied to predict the other possible 2p-emitters. For many proton rich nuclei for which experimental data on the decay energies are not available, we have used the theoretical values obtained from our calculations using the relativistic mean-field (RMF) approach. The uncertainties in the theoretical decay energy values are minimised by machine learning (ML) technique. Correlation of 2p-radioactivity with 2p-halo and deformation is probed. Our calculations show the phenomenon of shape coexistence in several 2p-emitters, wherein the prolate shape is found to be more predominant for the ground state.

## Key claim
The paper proposes a new semi-empirical formula for two-proton (2p) radioactivity half-lives that explicitly includes quadrupole deformation, and claims that this inclusion reduces the root-mean-square error (RMSE) significantly compared to formulas without deformation, but the improvement is demonstrated only for a limited set of 24 measured or extracted decay cases, and the deformation values used are theoretical (from relativistic mean-field calculations) rather than experimental.

## Method
The authors constructed a semi-empirical formula for log₁₀(T₁/₂) that depends on the Q-value, reduced mass, daughter atomic number, mass number, angular momentum l, and quadrupole deformation β (with a power p=3). They performed a least-squares fit to a database of 24 2p-decay cases (17 with experimental data and 7 with l≠0 extracted from prior work) to determine the formula parameters. For nuclei without experimental Q-values, they used theoretical Q-values from relativistic mean-field (RMF) calculations, then applied a machine learning (XGBoost) technique to minimize uncertainties in those theoretical Q-values. Quadrupole constrained RMF calculations were also performed to study shape coexistence in the parent and daughter nuclei.

## Key numbers
- The final formula (including deformation and angular momentum terms) yields an RMSE of 0.81 for log₁₀(T₁/₂) across the 24 fitted cases, compared to RMSE values of 1.48 (without deformation and angular momentum terms) and 1.14 (with angular momentum but without deformation).
- The fitted coefficients are: a = -21.3232 ± 0.0719, b = -0.6491 ± 0.0069, c = 0.8703 ± 0.0013, d = 0.6909 ± 0.0114, and e = -36.1448 ± 1.1700 (units: a, b, d, e in seconds; c in sec·MeV¹/²).
- For the known 2p-emitter ⁶⁷Kr (Q₂ₚ = 1.69 ± 0.017 MeV, β = -0.27), the present formula gives log₁₀(T₁/₂) = -0.99 ± 0.26 seconds, compared to the experimental value of -1.70 ± 0.18 seconds.
- For the predicted candidate ³⁸Ti, using the machine-learning-modified Q₂ₚ of 2.57 MeV and β = 0.22, the formula gives log₁₀(T₁/₂) = -14.11 ± 0.19 seconds.
- Out of 33 deformed cases studied, 17 nuclei exhibit shape coexistence with energy differences ΔE between minima in the range 40 keV ≤ ΔE ≤ 1 MeV.

## Context
This work extends prior studies of deformation effects on 2p-radioactivity, which were first speculated by Mukha (2006) and later theoretically investigated by Saxena and Aggarwal (multiple papers, 2018-2021) and by Santhosh (2021) using a Coulomb and proximity potential model for deformed nuclei. The new formula improves upon existing semi-empirical formulas by Sreeja (2019) and Liu (2020) that did not include deformation, and it enables predictions of potential 2p-emitters across the mass region Z<82, including candidates such as ³⁸Ti, ⁷²Sr, and ¹⁵⁴W. The identification of shape coexistence in several 2p-emitters suggests that future experiments should probe these nuclei for meta-stable states or shape isomers that could affect half-life measurements.
