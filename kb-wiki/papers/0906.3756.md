# Improved basis selection for the Projected Configuration Interaction method applied to heavy nuclei

> In a previous paper we proposed a Projected Configuration Interaction method that uses sets of axially deformed single particle states to build up the many body basis. We show that the choice of the basis set is essential for the efficiency of the method, and we propose a newly improved algorithm of selecting the projected basis states. We also extend our method to model spaces that can accomodate both parities, and can include odd-multipole terms in the effective interaction, such as the octupole contributions. %A universal algorithm of the choice of the PCI basis was presented in details. Examples of $^{52}$Fe, $^{56}$Ni, $^{68}$Se, $^{70}$Se and $^{76}$Se are calcualted showing good agreement with the full Configuration Interaction results.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that the Projected Configuration Interaction (PCI) method can reproduce full Configuration Interaction (CI) results for heavy nuclei with dramatically reduced basis dimensions, provided the projected basis states are selected using a new iterative algorithm that chooses different basis sets for different spins and automatically avoids large overlaps between basis states.

## Method
The authors generate deformed single-particle states using the Nilsson Hamiltonian and build Slater determinants at various deformations (quadrupole, hexadecupole, and octupole). For each spin, they iteratively select basis states by solving generalized eigenvalue problems: the first basis state is the Slater determinant with the lowest projected energy at that spin, and subsequent states are chosen by minimizing the sum of eigenvalues when each candidate is added to the existing set. Particle-hole excitations up to 4p-4h are included on top of each selected basis state, with an energy cutoff parameter controlling the number of excitations retained.

## Key numbers
- For $^{56}$Ni with GXPF1A interaction: the projected energy surface minimum for I=0 is at -204.473 MeV (673 keV below the HF energy of -203.800 MeV, and 1.236 MeV above the exact CI ground state of -205.709 MeV)
- For $^{56}$Ni with n=1 basis state and E_cut=1 keV, the PCI dimension is only 180 and the first 0+ energy is -205.409 MeV (300 keV above the exact full CI value)
- For $^{56}$Ni with n=15 basis states and E_cut=1 keV, the ground state PCI energy becomes -205.603 MeV (100 keV above the exact value)
- For $^{68}$Se: the lowest oblate HF minimum is at -40.718 MeV, the second prolate minimum at -39.956 MeV; after projection, the lowest $|\kappa_1,0\rangle$ for 0+ is at -42.405 MeV and $|\kappa_2,0\rangle$ at -41.549 MeV
- For $^{70}$Se: PCI dimensions range from 3665 to 4799 for positive parity and 4056 to 4778 for negative parity (spins 0-8), compared to full CI dimensions of 6.7×10^6 to 5.9×10^7 (roughly 10^4 times smaller)
- For $^{70}$Se 0+: from 801 possible 2p-2h excitations for $|\kappa_1,0\rangle$, only 138 were retained with E_cut=1 keV
- PCI calculation for $^{70}$Se takes about 5 hours per spin, compared to approximately 20 days for full CI using NuShellX

## Context
This work extends the PCI method previously proposed by the same authors (Gao, Horoi, and Chen, 2009) by introducing an improved algorithm for selecting the projected basis states. The method addresses limitations of the Projected Shell Model (Hara and Sun, 1995; Chen, 2001) and the Deformed Shell Model (DSM), which either require tuned Hamiltonians or struggle with quasi-spherical nuclei. The new algorithm incorporates ideas from the Generator Coordinate Method (Hill and Wheeler, 1953) and enables calculations in mixed-parity valence spaces (f5pg9) with octupole interactions, making it applicable to heavy nuclei relevant for double beta decay studies such as $^{76}$Ge and $^{76}$Se, where full CI calculations become unmanageable for heavier systems like $^{150}$Nd and $^{238}$U.
