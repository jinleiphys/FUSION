# Excited Eigenstates and Strength Functions for Isolated Systems of Interacting Particles

> Eigenstates in finite systems such as nuclei, atoms, atomic clusters and quantum dots with few excited particles are chaotic superpositions of shell model basis states. We study criterion for the equilibrium distribution of basis components (ergodicity, or Quantum Chaos), effects of level density variation and transition from the Breit-Wigner to the Gaussian shape of eigenstates and strength functions. In the model of $n$ interacting particles distributed over $m$ orbitals, the shape is given by the Breit-Wigner function with the width in the form of gaussian dependence on energy.

## Key claim

The paper develops a method to describe the shape of chaotic eigenstates and strength functions for isolated systems of interacting particles (such as nuclei, atoms, atomic clusters, and quantum dots) without diagonalizing the full many-body Hamiltonian matrix, showing that the shape transitions from Breit-Wigner to Gaussian as the interaction strength increases, with the condition that a smooth self-consistent solution exists only when the interaction matrix elements are larger than the spacing between final basis states.

## Method

The authors start from a Hamiltonian consisting of a mean-field term \(H_0\) and a residual two-body interaction \(V\), expressing exact eigenstates as superpositions of shell-model basis states. They derive self-consistent integral equations for the spreading width \(\Gamma_k(E)\) and energy shift \(\delta_k\) in terms of the unperturbed spectrum and Hamiltonian matrix elements, which allow calculation of the strength function (local density of states) without diagonalization. The equations are solved analytically by approximating the density of final states \(\rho_f(E)\) as a Gaussian distribution, leading to a Gaussian expression for the spreading width \(\Gamma_k(E)\).

## Key numbers

- The ratio of the spreading width to the spacing between final basis states must satisfy \(\Gamma \gg d_f\) for statistical equilibrium; when \(H_{kp} < d_f\) the smooth self-consistent solution disappears.
- For the numerical test: \(n=6\) Fermi-particles distributed over \(m=13\) orbitals, giving a Hamiltonian matrix size of \(N = C_m^n = 1716\) basis states, with unperturbed single-particle spacing \(d_0 = 1\) and \(\epsilon_s \approx d_0 s\).
- The variance of the strength function is given by \(\overline{(\Delta E)_k^2} = V^2 n(n-1)(m-n)(3+m-n)/4\).
- The transition from Breit-Wigner to Gaussian shape occurs when \(\Gamma \approx \sigma\), where \(\sigma\) is the effective energy band width of the Hamiltonian matrix.
- The density of two-particle transitions has variance \(\sigma_2^2 = 2\sigma_\epsilon^2 + (4n-6)V^2\), where \(\sigma_\epsilon^2\) is the variance of the single-particle spectrum and \(V^2\) is the variance of non-diagonal two-body matrix elements.

## Context

This work extends the statistical approach to chaotic eigenstates developed by Flambaum and collaborators in the 1990s (Flambaum 1994, Flambaum et al. 1996, Flambaum and Izrailev 1997) for finite many-body systems, providing an analytical method that avoids diagonalization of large Hamiltonian matrices. The results enable calculation of occupation numbers, expectation values, transition amplitudes, and weak interaction enhancements in complex systems such as nuclei, atoms, and quantum dots, and they connect to earlier work on statistical spectroscopy by French and Wong (1970) and the Breit-Wigner formalism of Bohigas and Mahaux (1969).
