# Virtues and Flaws of the Pauli Potential

> Quantum simulations of complex fermionic systems suffer from a variety of challenging problems. In an effort to circumvent these challenges, simpler ``semi-classical'' approaches have been used to mimic fermionic correlations through a fictitious ``Pauli potential''. In this contribution we examine two issues. First, we address some of the inherent difficulties in a widely used version of the Pauli potential. Second, we refine such a potential in a manner consistent with the most basic properties of a cold Fermi gas, such as its momentum distribution and its two-body correlation function.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the widely used "standard" Pauli potential, which penalizes particles only when both their positions and momenta are close, fails to reproduce the momentum distribution and two-body correlation function of a cold Fermi gas, even though it can reproduce the kinetic energy. The authors propose a refined Pauli potential that enforces the Pauli principle more stringently by penalizing particles when either their positions or their momenta are close, but they note that this approach still produces an unrealistically soft kinematical velocity distribution, which is a likely general flaw of the Hamiltonian approach.

## Method

The authors performed classical Monte Carlo simulations of N=1000 identical fermions at a low temperature (τ = T/T_F = 0.05) using a new Pauli potential consisting of a sum of a configuration-space repulsion, a momentum-space repulsion, and a one-body term that enforces the Fermi-Dirac occupancy. The parameters of the potential were adjusted to reproduce the kinetic energy, momentum distribution, and two-body correlation function of a free Fermi gas, with the strength parameters following a simple density-dependent power law and the range parameters scaling with the Fermi momentum. The results were compared against exact analytic results from Sommerfeld expansions and against simulations using the standard Pauli potential of Wilets et al. (1977) and Maruyama et al. (1997).

## Key numbers

- The new Pauli potential reproduces the kinetic energy of a free Fermi gas to better than 5% for a system of N=1000 identical fermions at τ=0.05.
- The strength parameters of the new potential at a reference density ρ₀ = 0.037 fm⁻³ are: V_A⁰ = 13.517 MeV, V_B⁰ = 1.260 MeV, V_C⁰ = 3.560 MeV, with density exponents α_A = 0.629, α_B = 0.665, α_C = 0.831, and dimensionless range parameters β_A = 0.845, β_B = 0.193, and η = 30.
- The standard Pauli potential (from Maruyama et al. 1997) produces a two-body correlation function that does not vanish at r=0 and develops artificial oscillations, while the new potential yields g(r) that vanishes at r=0 and scales correctly with density.
- The kinematical velocity distribution from the new potential peaks at about 1/10 of the Fermi velocity, significantly softer than the exact Fermi gas distribution.

## Context

This work extends the semi-classical approach to fermionic systems pioneered by Wilets et al. (1977) and refined by Dorso et al. (1987), Boal et al. (1988), Peilert et al. (1992), Maruyama et al. (1997), and Watanabe et al. (2003), which used a Pauli potential to mimic fermionic correlations in simulations of heavy-ion collisions and neutron-star crust matter. The authors show that previous approaches, which only constrained the kinetic energy, fail to reproduce the momentum distribution and two-body correlation function, and they provide a new parametrization that does so. The work also highlights a fundamental limitation identified by Neumann and Fai (1994): momentum-dependent Pauli potentials produce a distinction between canonical and kinematical momenta, leading to an unrealistically soft velocity distribution that risks artificial crystallization, an issue that remains open for future work.
