# Challenges in nucleosynthesis of trans-iron elements

> Nucleosynthesis beyond Fe poses additional challenges not encountered when studying astrophysical processes involving light nuclei. Astrophysical sites and conditions are not well known for some of the processes involved. On the nuclear physics side, different approaches are required, both in theory and experiment. The main differences and most important considerations are presented for a selection of nucleosynthesis processes and reactions, specifically the $s$-, $r$-, $γ$-, and $νp$-processes. Among the discussed issues are uncertainties in sites and production conditions, the difference between laboratory and stellar rates, reaction mechanisms, important transitions, thermal population of excited states, and uncertainty estimates for stellar rates. The utility and limitations of indirect experimental approaches are also addressed. The presentation should not be viewed as confining the discussed problems to the specific processes. The intention is to generally introduce the concepts and possible pitfalls along with some examples. Similar problems may apply to further astrophysical processes involving nuclei from the Fe region upward and/or at high plasma temperatures. The framework and strategies presented here are intended to aid the conception of future experimental and theoretical approaches.

# Challenges in nucleosynthesis of trans-iron elements

## Key claim

The paper argues that nucleosynthesis of elements heavier than iron introduces fundamentally different nuclear physics challenges than those for lighter nuclei, requiring different experimental and theoretical approaches. The author emphasizes that many of the issues discussed are not confined to the specific processes mentioned (s-, r-, γ-, and νp-processes) but apply generally to any astrophysical process involving nuclei from the iron region upward at high plasma temperatures.

## Method

The paper reviews the key differences in nuclear physics approaches required for trans-iron nucleosynthesis compared to light-element nucleosynthesis, focusing on stellar reaction rate formalism, the impact of high nuclear level density in heavy nuclei, and sensitivity analysis of reaction rates to input quantities. It then applies these considerations to four specific nucleosynthesis processes (s-process, r-process, γ-process, and νp-process), discussing both astrophysical site uncertainties and nuclear physics challenges including thermal population of excited states, reaction mechanisms, and uncertainty estimation for stellar rates.

## Key numbers

- Neutron number densities for the s-process are typically 10⁶–10¹² cm⁻³
- Relevant temperatures for explosive burning do not exceed 3.5 GK because forward and reverse rates are in equilibrium above this temperature
- Charged particle reactions freeze out at temperatures below 1 GK
- Relevant energies for charged particle reactions on trans-iron nuclei are below 10 MeV; for neutron reactions, up to a few 100 keV
- For the s-process, typical neutron energies are kT = 0.008 and 0.03 MeV for interpulse and flash burning, and around 0.09 MeV in massive stars
- For ¹⁸⁵W(n,γ), the ground-state contribution X₀ = 0.98 at kT = 8 keV and X₀ = 0.75 at kT = 30 keV
- For ¹⁸⁶W(γ,n), the ground-state contribution X₀ = 7×10⁻³ at T₉ = 0.1 and X₀ = 5×10⁻³ at T₉ = 0.4
- The solar ¹⁸⁶Os/¹⁵⁰Sm abundance ratio is best reproduced by a value 1.81 times larger than the averaged value from two photodisintegration experiments
- For the r-process, neutron number densities larger than 10²⁰ cm⁻³ and temperatures around 1 GK are required
- r-process waiting point half-lives are a few 100 ms; total processing time from Z=26 to Z=83 is on the order of seconds

## Context

This review extends the framework of nucleosynthesis studies beyond the well-understood light-element regime, building on the foundational work of Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler, and Hoyle (1957) and Cameron (1957) who first identified the s- and r-processes. The paper addresses the limitations of indirect experimental approaches such as (d,p) and (d,n) reactions and Coulomb dissociation when applied to heavy nuclei, and highlights the work of Kappeler and collaborators on s-process neutron capture compilations (KADoNiS). The discussion of stellar enhancement factors and excited-state contributions extends the earlier formalism of Fowler (1974) and the Hauser-Feshbach statistical model approach. The paper enables future experimental and theoretical work by providing a framework for evaluating whether measurements actually constrain the astrophysically relevant stellar rates, and by identifying the fundamental impossibility of assigning statistical error bars to theoretical model uncertainties.
