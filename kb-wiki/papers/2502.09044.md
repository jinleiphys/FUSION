# Quarkonium Spectroscopy in the Quark-Gluon Plasma

> The properties of bound states are fundamental to hadronic spectroscopy and play a central role in the transition from hadronic matter to a quark-gluon plasma (QGP). In a strongly coupled QGP (sQGP), the interplay of temperature, binding energy and large collisional widths of the partons poses formidable challenges in evaluating the in-medium properties of hadronic states and their eventual melting. In particular, the existence of heavy quarkonia in the QGP is a long-standing problem that is hard to solve by considering their spectral properties on the real-energy axis. We address this problem by analyzing in-medium thermodynamic quarkonium $T$-matrices in the complex energy plane. We first validate this method in vacuum, where the $T$-matrix poles of observed states are readily identified. When deploying this approach to recent self-consistently calculated $T$-matrices in the QGP, we find that poles in the complex energy plane can persist to surprisingly large temperatures, depending on the strength of the in-medium interactions. While the masses and widths of the pole positions are precisely defined, the notion of a binding energy is not due to the absence of thresholds caused by the (large) widths of the underlying anti-/quark spectral functions. Our method thus provides a new and definitive quantum-mechanical criterion to determine the melting temperature of hadronic states in the sQGP while increasing the accuracy in the theoretical determination of transport parameters.

# Quarkonium Spectroscopy in the Quark-Gluon Plasma

## Key claim

The paper claims that analyzing quarkonium T-matrices in the complex energy plane provides a definitive quantum-mechanical criterion for determining the melting temperature of hadronic states in a strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (sQGP), but this criterion depends on the strength of the in-medium interactions and the notion of a binding energy is not well-defined due to the absence of thresholds caused by large widths of the underlying quark spectral functions.

## Method

The authors employ a thermodynamic T-matrix approach, a quantum many-body framework that self-consistently solves coupled one- and two-body correlation functions for heavy quarks and antiquarks in the QGP. They extend the two-body propagator into the complex energy plane while retaining the full off-shell properties of the in-medium single-particle propagators, then identify poles of the T-matrix which signal quarkonium states with precisely defined masses and widths. The method is validated in vacuum and then applied to bottomonium states using two different input potentials: one constrained by lattice QCD Wilson line correlators (strong coupling scenario) and another constrained by heavy-quark free energies (weakly coupled scenario).

## Key numbers

- Vacuum bottomonium T-matrix analysis recovers the Upsilon(1S), Upsilon(2S), Upsilon(3S), and additionally identifies the Upsilon(4S) located very close to the vacuum b-bbar threshold of 10.513 GeV
- At T=195 MeV in the strong coupling scenario, the Upsilon(3S) has a width of approximately 0.35 GeV with a nominal binding energy below 0.1 GeV; the Upsilon(4S) persists above the nominal threshold with a width of approximately 0.6 GeV
- The Upsilon(4S) vanishes at T approximately 250 MeV, the Upsilon(3S) melts at T approximately 330 MeV, the Upsilon(2S) survives up to T approximately 700 MeV, and the Upsilon(1S) persists to even higher temperatures
- In the weakly coupled scenario, collisional parton widths are smaller by up to a factor of approximately 5 compared to those emerging from the Wilson line correlator constraints

## Context

This work extends the thermodynamic T-matrix approach previously developed by Liu et al. (2017) and Tang et al. (2023) for studying heavy quarks and quarkonia in the QGP. It addresses the long-standing problem of defining quarkonium melting temperatures, which previous studies restricted to the real-energy axis could not resolve due to broad spectral peaks at high temperatures. The complex-pole analysis provides a more rigorous criterion than earlier prescriptions based on vanishing binding energy or the binding energy becoming comparable to the dissociation width, as reviewed by Rapp et al. (2008), Braun-Munzinger et al. (2009), Kluberg et al. (2009), Mocsy et al. (2013), and Liu et al. (2015). The results enable more accurate theoretical determination of transport parameters for heavy-ion collision phenomenology and may relate to recent lattice QCD studies by Larsen et al. (2019) using extended-meson operators.
