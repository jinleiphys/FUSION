# Primordial Nucleosynthesis with Non-Extensive Statistics

> The conventional Big Bang model successfully anticipates the initial abundances of 2H(D), 3He, and 4He, aligning remarkably well with observational data. However, a persistent challenge arises in the case of 7Li, where the predicted abundance exceeds observations by a factor of approximately three. Despite numerous efforts employing traditional nuclear physics to address this incongruity over the years, the enigma surrounding the lithium anomaly endures. In this context, we embark on an exploration of Big Bang nucleosynthesis (BBN) of light element abundances with the application of Tsallis non-extensive statistics. A comparison is made between the outcomes obtained by varying the non-extensive parameter q away from its unity value and both observational data and abundance predictions derived from the conventional big bang model. A good agreement is found for the abundances of 4He, 3He and 7Li, implying that the lithium abundance puzzle might be due to a subtle fine-tuning of the physics ingredients used to determine the BBN. However, the deuterium abundance deviates from observations.

## Key claim

Applying Tsallis non-extensive statistics to Big Bang nucleosynthesis can simultaneously bring the predicted abundances of 4He, 3He, and 7Li into agreement with observations, potentially resolving the lithium abundance puzzle, but the deuterium abundance then deviates from observational data, implying the lithium problem may stem from fine-tuning of the physics inputs rather than from new physics alone.

## Method

The authors replaced the standard Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of particle kinetic energies in the BBN reaction rate integrals with the Tsallis non-extensive distribution characterized by a parameter q, where q=1 recovers the standard statistics. They computed the modified Gamow energy distributions and nuclear reaction rates for the key BBN reactions, including charged-particle and neutron-induced reactions, as functions of the non-extensive parameter q and temperature. The resulting light-element abundances were compared to both observational data and predictions from the conventional BBN model for values of q significantly different from unity (q=0.5 and q=2).

## Key numbers

- The conventional BBN prediction for 7Li exceeds observations by a factor of approximately three.
- The baryon-to-photon ratio from WMAP observations is eta = 6.104 +/- 0.055 x 10^(-10).
- The neutron lifetime used is tau_n = 877.75 +/- 0.28_stat +0.22/-0.16_syst seconds.
- For q=2, the kinetic energy cutoff in the non-extensive distribution occurs at 0.086 MeV (86 keV) at T_9=1.
- The Gamow energy for charged-particle reactions is given by E_0 = 0.122 (Z_i^2 Z_j^2 A)^(1/3) T_9^(2/3) MeV, and the Gamow width by Delta E = 0.2368 (Z_i^2 Z_j^2 A)^(1/6) T_9^(5/6) MeV.

## Context

This work extends prior efforts to address the lithium problem using "new physics" approaches, as reviewed by Fields (2011), and specifically builds on studies by Hou et al. (2017) that showed small departures of q from unity could resolve the lithium puzzle. The use of Tsallis statistics in BBN follows earlier applications of non-extensive statistics to nuclear fusion in stars by Degl'Innocenti et al. (1998), Coraddu et al. (1999), and others, and is motivated by the finding of Bernui et al. (2007) that CMB temperature fluctuations are better described by Tsallis statistics with q = 1.045 +/- 0.005 than by Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics. The results enable further investigation into whether the lithium problem reflects a genuine departure from Maxwellian distributions in the early universe or instead points to systematic uncertainties in the nuclear physics inputs to BBN.
