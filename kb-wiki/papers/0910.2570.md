# Decoherence and thermalization of a pure quantum state in quantum field theory

> We study the real-time evolution of a self-interacting O(N) scalar field initially prepared in a pure quantum state. We present a complete solution of the nonequilibrium quantum dynamics from a 1/N-expansion of the two-particle-irreducible effective action at next-to-leading order, which includes scattering and memory effects. Restricting one's attention (or ability to measure) to a subset of the infinite hierarchy of correlation functions, the system is described by an effective (reduced) density matrix which, unlike the full density matrix, has a nontrivial time evolution. In particular, starting from a pure quantum state, we observe the loss of putity/coherence and, on longer time scales, thermalization of the reduced density matrix. We point out that the physics of decoherence is well described by classical statistical field theory.

## Key claim

Starting from a pure, coherent quantum state of a self-interacting O(N) scalar field, an observer who only measures the subset of equal-time two-point functions will observe an effective loss of purity and coherence, and eventually apparent thermalization, even though the full system remains in a pure state under unitary evolution; no environment of incoherent degrees of freedom is needed.

## Method

The authors solved the nonequilibrium quantum dynamics of a relativistic O(N) scalar field using a 1/N-expansion of the two-particle-irreducible (2PI) effective action at next-to-leading order (NLO), which includes scattering and memory effects. They numerically solved the integro-differential equations of motion for the statistical (F) and spectral (rho) components of the two-point function, starting from a pure Gaussian initial state with high quantum coherence for modes below a cutoff momentum p_c. The effective (reduced) density matrix for an observer with access only to equal-time two-point functions was constructed, and its purity and coherence were tracked via the canonical invariant n_p(t) and the coherence parameter gamma_p(t).

## Key numbers

- The initial state was prepared with modes p <= p_c = 3.6 M_0 in a pure, highly coherent state with n_p(0) << 1 and gamma_p(0) = gamma_0 ~ 1, where M_0 = M(0) is the initial effective mass; the ultraviolet cutoff was Lambda/M_0 = 10.
- The trace of the squared reduced density matrix for a given momentum mode, tr[D_p^2(t)] = (2n_p(t)+1)^{-1}, decays rapidly from 1 (pure state) over time for modes p/a = 1, 5, 10, 15, 20, 24 where a/M_0 = 0.15.
- The coherence parameter gamma_p(t) decays exponentially; for the mode p/M_0 = 0.15, runs with (gamma_0=0.99, lambda=3.05) and (gamma_0=0.95, lambda=6.62) both gave lambda_eff = 21.7 and were essentially indistinguishable, demonstrating scaling with lambda_eff = lambda / sqrt(1-gamma_0^2).
- Decoherence rates approximately follow a perturbative-like lambda_eff^2 scaling, with values shown for lambda_eff = 21.7, 7.215, and 3.0.

## Context

This work provides the first complete microscopic description of decoherence in a realistic quantum field theory, extending prior 2PI functional techniques used by Berges (2001) and Aarts (2001) for far-from-equilibrium dynamics and thermalization. It contrasts with Gaussian approximations (leading-order Large-N or Hartree) which fail to capture decoherence because they conserve the canonical invariant n_p(t). The finding that decoherence is well-described by classical statistical field theory enables future studies using standard Monte-Carlo techniques, and opens the question of how including higher-order equal-time correlation functions in the measured set modifies the results.
