# Statistical errors in Weizsaecker-Skyrme mass model

> The statistical uncertainties of 13 model parameters in the Weizsäcker-Skyrme (WS*) mass model are investigated for the first time with an efficient approach, and the propagated errors in the predicted masses are estimated. The discrepancies between the predicted masses and the experimental data, including the new data in AME2016, are almost all smaller than the model errors. For neutron-rich heavy nuclei, the model errors increase considerably, and go up to a few MeV when the nucleus approaches the neutron drip line. The most sensitive model parameter which causes the largest statistical error is analyzed for all bound nuclei. We find that the two coefficients of symmetry energy term significantly influence the mass predictions of extremely neutron-rich nuclei, and the deformation energy coefficients play a key role for well-deformed nuclei around the $β$-stability line.

## Key claim

The statistical uncertainties of the 13 parameters in the WS* mass model are extracted for the first time using a maximum-likelihood-based approach that assumes weak correlations between macroscopic and microscopic parameters, and the propagated errors in predicted masses are estimated; the discrepancies between predicted and experimental masses are almost all smaller than the estimated model errors, but the authors note that if correlations between macroscopic parameters were considered, the statistical errors could be slightly reduced.

## Method

The authors varied each of the 13 WS* model parameters individually around its optimal value while keeping the other 12 fixed, and for each measured nucleus they determined the "best" value of that parameter (where the calculated mass matched the experimental data exactly). The distribution of these "best" values across all 2149 measured nuclei (from AME2003) was fitted with two Gaussian functions, and the standard deviation at 68.3% confidence level was taken as the statistical uncertainty for each parameter. The statistical error in the predicted mass of a given nucleus was then taken as the maximum energy uncertainty among the 13 parameters, computed by shifting each parameter by its standard deviation.

## Key numbers

- RMS deviation of WS* model with respect to 2149 measured masses in AME2003: 441 keV
- RMS deviation of WS* model with respect to 270 new nuclei in AME2016: 589 keV
- Statistical uncertainty in volume coefficient a_v: 0.0030 MeV (optimal value -15.6223 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in surface coefficient a_s: 0.0156 MeV (optimal value 18.0571 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in Coulomb coefficient a_c: 0.0007 MeV (optimal value 0.7194 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in volume symmetry energy coefficient c_sym: 0.1298 MeV (optimal value 29.1563 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in surface-symmetry coefficient kappa: 0.0186 (optimal value 1.3484)
- Statistical uncertainty in pairing coefficient a_pair: 1.9095 MeV (optimal value -5.4423 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in deformation energy coefficient g_1: 0.0024 (optimal value 0.00895)
- Statistical uncertainty in deformation energy coefficient g_2: 0.0668 (optimal value -0.4632)
- Statistical uncertainty in shell correction factor c_1: 0.1566 (optimal value 0.6297)
- Statistical uncertainty in Woods-Saxon potential depth V_0: 3.3602 MeV (optimal value -46.8784 MeV)
- Statistical uncertainty in radius coefficient r_0: 0.0835 fm (optimal value 1.3840 fm)
- Statistical uncertainty in diffuseness a: 0.1038 fm (optimal value 0.7842 fm)
- Statistical uncertainty in spin-orbit strength lambda_0: 2.7898 (optimal value 26.3163)
- For intermediate and heavy nuclei around the beta-stability line, statistical errors in predicted masses are generally smaller than 1 MeV
- For super-heavy nuclei with neutron number larger than 180 and heavy nuclei approaching the neutron drip line, statistical errors exceed 4 MeV

## Context

This work extends the WS* mass model (Wang 2010) by providing the first systematic statistical error analysis for a macroscopic-microscopic mass model, using a more efficient approach than the traditional covariance matrix method used for energy-density functionals (Gao 2013, Maza 2015). The results enable quantitative uncertainty estimates for mass predictions of unmeasured nuclei, particularly for extremely neutron-rich nuclei and super-heavy nuclei, and identify the most sensitive parameters for different regions of the nuclear landscape. The WS* model's RMS deviation of 589 keV for the 270 new nuclei in AME2016 is better than the finite range droplet model (901 keV) and the Duflo-Zuker DZ28 model (763 keV), despite having only 13 parameters.
