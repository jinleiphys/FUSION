# Nuclear correlation functions using first-principle calculations of lattice quantum chromodynamics

> Exploring nuclear physics through the fundamental constituents of the strong force -- quarks and gluons -- is a formidable challenge. While numerical calculations using lattice quantum chromodynamics offer the most promising approach for this pursuit, practical implementation is arduous, especially due to the uncontrollable growth of quark-combinatorics, the so-called Wick-contraction problem of nuclei. We present here two novel methods providing a state-of-the-art solution to this problem. In the first, we exploit randomized algorithms inspired from computational number theory to detect and eliminate redundancies that arise in Wick contraction computations. Our second method explores facilities for automation of tensor computations -- in terms of efficient utilization of specialized hardware, algorithmic optimizations, as well as ease of programming and the potential for automatic code generation -- that are offered by new programming models inspired by applications in machine learning (e.g., TensorFlow). We demonstrate the efficacy of our methods by computing two-point correlation functions for Deuteron, Helium-3, Helium-4 and Lithium-7, achieving at least an order of magnitude improvement over existing algorithms with efficient implementation on GPU-accelerators. Additionally, we discover an intriguing characteristic shared by all the nuclei we study: specific spin-color combinations dominate the correlation functions, hinting at a potential connection to an as-yet-unidentified symmetry in nuclei. Moreover finding them beforehand can reduce the computing time further and substantially. Our results, with the efficiency that we achieved, suggest the possibility of extending the applicability of our methods for calculating properties of light nuclei, potentially up to A ~12 and beyond.

## Key claim

The authors present two novel methods for solving the Wick-contraction problem in lattice QCD calculations of nuclear correlation functions, claiming at least an order of magnitude improvement over existing algorithms for nuclei up to A=7, with the caveat that the signal-to-noise ratio problem remains a separate bottleneck that must be addressed for realistic precision physics.

## Method

The first method uses randomized algorithms from computational number theory to detect and eliminate redundancies in Wick contraction computations, combined with a determinant-based formulation whose time complexity is reduced from O(n_u^3 n_d^3) to O(n_u^3 + n_d^3) by exploiting block matrix structure. The second method employs tensor-based programming models such as TensorFlow to automatically generate and optimize contraction code for GPU accelerators, enabling automatic code generation and efficient execution on specialized hardware. The authors compute two-point correlation functions for Deuteron, Helium-3, Helium-4, and Lithium-7 using both methods on lattice QCD configurations.

## Key numbers

- For Deuteron (A=2), the naive Wick contraction count is approximately 4 x 10^15 floating-point operations on a 32^3 x 96 lattice with 100 configurations
- For Helium-3 (A=3), the naive count is approximately 2 x 10^20 operations
- For Helium-4 (A=4), the naive count is approximately 2 x 10^25 operations
- For Lithium-7 (A=7), the naive count is approximately 9 x 10^41 operations
- The optimized determinant method achieves at least an order of magnitude improvement over existing algorithms for nuclei up to A=7
- The authors suggest extending applicability to nuclei potentially up to A ~ 12 and beyond

## Context

This work extends the determinant-based formulation introduced by Detmold et al. (2013) and the unified contraction algorithm of Doi et al. (2013), improving upon the block algorithm of Ishii et al. (2007) and Yamazaki et al. (2010). The authors also build on rank-1 update optimizations from Vachaspati (2014) and recent factor-tree approaches from 2022. The methods enable future first-principles lattice QCD calculations of nuclear energy spectra and structures for light nuclei, provided the separate signal-to-noise ratio problem can be controlled through larger volumes and improved interpolating operators.
