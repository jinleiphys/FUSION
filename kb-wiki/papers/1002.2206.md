# The theory and phenomenology of perturbative QCD based jet quenching

> The study of the structure of strongly interacting dense matter via hard jets is reviewed. High momentum partons produced in hard collisions produce a shower of gluons prior to undergoing the non-perturbative process of hadronization. In the presence of a dense medium this shower is modified due to scattering of the various partons off the constituents in the medium. The modified pattern of the final detected hadrons is then a probe of the structure of the medium as perceived by the jet. Starting from the factorization paradigm developed for the case of particle collisions, we review the basic underlying theory of medium induced gluon radiation based on perturbative Quantum Chromo Dynamics (pQCD) and current experimental results from Deep Inelastic Scattering on large nuclei and high energy heavy-ion collisions, emphasizing how these results constrain our understanding of energy loss. This review contains introductions to the theory of radiative energy loss, elastic energy loss, and the corresponding experimental observables and issues. We close with a discussion of important calculations and measurements that need to be carried out to complete the description of jet modification at high energies at future high energy colliders.

## Key claim

This review argues that while the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) can be described by multiple energy-loss formalisms tuned to the data, the simultaneous comparison of \(R_{AA}\) and the recoil suppression \(I_{AA}\) provides a first indication that the Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) interference effect plays an important role, leading to an energy loss scaling with the square of the path length (\(L^2\)), but a precise determination of the medium density or transport coefficient from experiment is currently precluded by the complex modeling of the evolving medium.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical formalisms for parton energy loss in cold and hot QCD matter, focusing on elastic and radiative mechanisms, with radiative energy loss dominated by the LPM effect. It compares experimental data from Deep Inelastic Scattering (HERMES) and heavy-ion collisions at RHIC (PHENIX, STAR) to calculations using four main energy-loss formalisms (ASW, HT, AMY, GLV), often coupled with 3D hydrodynamical medium evolution, to extract the transport coefficient \(\hat{q}\) and test path-length and energy dependence.

## Key numbers

- The transport coefficient extracted from HERMES DIS data using BDMPS-ASW quenching weights is \(\hat{q} = 0.6\) GeV\(^2\)/fm in the center of the nucleus.
- Using the higher twist formalism on HERMES data, a quark \(\hat{q} \simeq 0.08\) GeV\(^2\)/fm is obtained; an earlier calculation with only one in-medium emission gave \(\hat{q} = 0.12\) GeV\(^2\)/fm.
- In central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, the nuclear modification factor \(R_{AA}\) for \(\pi^0\) at high \(p_T\) is around 0.2.
- The initial transport coefficient \(\hat{q}_0\) (at \(\tau = 0.6\) fm/\(c\)) at the center of most central collisions, extracted from \(R_{AA}\) fits with a 3D hydrodynamical evolution, is 10 GeV\(^2\)/fm (ASW), 2.3 GeV\(^2\)/fm (HT), and 4.2 GeV\(^2\)/fm (AMY) when \(\hat{q} \propto T^3\).
- The recoil yield in di-hadron correlations for trigger particles with \(8 < p_T < 15\) GeV/\(c\) is suppressed by a factor \(\sim 4\) in central Au+Au compared to d+Au.
- The simultaneous fit to \(R_{AA}\) and \(I_{AA}\) using the ASW formalism yields a scale factor \(K\) between energy density and \(\hat{q}\) in the range 3–5.

## Context

This review extends the factorization paradigm developed for particle collisions to the case of jet quenching in dense QCD matter, building on prior work on the LPM effect and energy loss formalisms (e.g., Baier (2006), Wang (2002), Vitev (2002)). It enables future critical tests of pQCD-based energy loss using more differential observables like \(\gamma\)-jet and reconstructed jet measurements at RHIC and LHC, where the regime of energy loss significantly smaller than the jet energy will be accessible.
