# The Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn Sum Rule and the Spin Structure of the Nucleon

> The Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn sum rule is one of several dispersive sum rules that connect the Compton scattering amplitudes to the inclusive photoproduction cross sections of the target under investigation. Being based on such universal principles as causality, unitarity, and gauge invariance, these sum rules provide a unique testing ground to study the internal degrees of freedom that hold the system together. The present article reviews these sum rules for the spin-dependent cross sections of the nucleon by presenting an overview of recent experiments and theoretical approaches. The generalization from real to virtual photons provides a microscope of variable resolution: At small virtuality of the photon, the data sample information about the long range phenomena, which are described by effective degrees of freedom (Goldstone bosons and collective resonances), whereas the primary degrees of freedom (quarks and gluons) become visible at the larger virtualities. Through a rich body of new data and several theoretical developments, a unified picture of virtual Compton scattering emerges, which ranges from coherent to incoherent processes, and from the generalized spin polarizabilities on the low-energy side to higher twist effects in deep inelastic lepton scattering.

## Key claim

The Gerasimov-Drell-Hearn (GDH) sum rule, which relates the anomalous magnetic moment of the nucleon to an energy-weighted integral over photoabsorption cross sections, is tested by comparing the left-hand side (known to eight decimal places) with experimental data for the helicity-difference integral up to finite energies; the agreement within approximately 10% indicates that the physics responsible for the anomalous magnetic moment is largely the same as the physics of photoproduction up to several GeV, but the convergence of the unsubtracted dispersion relation on which the sum rule rests can never be proven or disproven by experiment.

## Method

The authors review forward dispersion relations for Compton scattering on the nucleon, deriving the GDH sum rule, Baldin's sum rule, and the forward spin polarizability from low-energy theorems and the optical theorem. They compare the GDH integral, defined as the energy-weighted integral of the helicity difference \(\sigma_{3/2} - \sigma_{1/2}\) over photon energy \(\nu\), with the value predicted by the anomalous magnetic moment, using experimental photoabsorption data from MAMI (200–800 MeV), ELSA (up to 2.9 GeV), and preliminary CLAS data (2.5–5.5 GeV), supplemented by multipole analyses of pion photoproduction in the threshold region.

## Key numbers

*   The GDH sum rule predicts \(I_p = 204.8\ \mu\)b for the proton, based on the anomalous magnetic moment \(\kappa_p = 1.79\).
*   The GDH sum rule predicts \(233\ \mu\)b for the neutron, based on \(\kappa_n = -1.91\).
*   The isovector anomalous magnetic moment \(\kappa_V = \kappa_p - \kappa_n = 3.71\) (experimental value).
*   The forward spin polarizability \(\gamma_0\) is defined by a \(\nu^{-3}\)-weighted integral of the helicity difference (Eq. \ref{DDeq2.2.16}).
*   The total photoabsorption cross section \(\sigma_T\) reaches a minimum of approximately \(115\ \mu\)b at \(W \approx 10\) GeV.
*   Lattice calculations for the isovector magnetic moment \(\kappa_V\) reach only about 50% of its experimental value for pion masses above 400 MeV, and the experimental value is obtained only when the pion mass approaches its physical value of 140 MeV.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on dispersive sum rules for Compton scattering initiated by Gerasimov (1965), Drell and Hearn (1965), and Burkhardt and Cottingham (1970), and connects to the Bjorken sum rule (1966) and Wandzura-Wilczek relation (1977) in the deep inelastic regime. The paper synthesizes recent experimental results from MAMI, ELSA, and JLab with theoretical approaches including chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) and lattice QCD, as discussed by Goecker et al. (2003) and others. It enables a unified picture of virtual Compton scattering from the low-energy generalized spin polarizabilities to higher-twist effects in deep inelastic scattering, and motivates further measurements at SLAC (E-159) to test the convergence of the GDH integral at high energies.
