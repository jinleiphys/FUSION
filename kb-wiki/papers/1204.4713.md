# Dissipative Hydrodynamic Effects on Baryon Stopping

> The quark-gluon plasma is considered to behave as a relativistic viscous fluid in the high-energy heavy ion collisions. In this study, I develop and estimate a second order dissipative hydrodynamic model at finite baryon density with effects of baryon dissipation together with those of shear and bulk viscosities. It is found that the hydrodynamic evolution effectively reduces baryon stopping, suggesting that the collisions are less transparent at the initial stage. Also the net baryon distribution is found sensitive to baryon dissipation as well as to viscosities. The results indicate that the dissipative hydrodynamic modeling would be important for understanding unique properties of the hot medium even in the high-energy collisions.

## Key claim

The hydrodynamic evolution of the quark-gluon plasma effectively reduces baryon stopping, meaning the collisions appear less transparent at the initial stage than previously assumed; the net baryon distribution is sensitive to baryon dissipation as well as to shear and bulk viscosities, but the actual magnitude of the effect depends on the still-unknown transport coefficients and initial conditions.

## Method

A (1+1)-dimensional second-order relativistic dissipative hydrodynamic model was developed for longitudinal expansion at finite baryon density, incorporating baryon dissipation current together with shear and bulk viscosities for the first time. The initial energy and net baryon density profiles were taken from the color glass condensate (CGC) framework using the MC-KLN model and valence quark distributions, and the equation of state was constructed from lattice QCD via Taylor expansion to second order in μB/T. The hydrodynamic equations were solved with a multiple iteration algorithm coupled to the piecewise parabolic method, and freezeout was performed on an isothermal hypersurface using the Cooper-Frye formula.

## Key numbers

- Initial mid-rapidity temperature T = 419 MeV and chemical potential μB = 20.6 MeV for √sNN = 200 GeV Au-Au at RHIC; T = 490 MeV and μB = 6.5 MeV for √sNN = 2.76 TeV Pb-Pb at LHC.
- Average rapidity loss ⟨δy⟩ for the initial net baryon distribution is 2.67; after ideal, viscous, and dissipative hydrodynamic evolution at Tf = 0.16 GeV, ⟨δy⟩ becomes 2.09, 2.16, and 2.26 respectively; at Tf = 0.14 GeV, ⟨δy⟩ becomes 1.99, 2.06, and 2.19 respectively.
- Initial time τ0 = 1 fm/c; freezeout temperatures Tf = 0.16 GeV and 0.14 GeV.
- Shear viscosity set to η/s = 1/4π (AdS/CFT bound); effective bulk viscosity ζ = 5(1/3 − cs²)η; baryon charge conductivity κV = cV χB(2) T² / (2π) with cV = 1.

## Context

This work extends earlier ideal and viscous hydrodynamic calculations at finite baryon density (e.g., Israel-Stewart theory, Monnai (2010)) by including baryon dissipation as a vector off-equilibrium process alongside shear and bulk viscosities. It builds on the color glass condensate initial conditions of Kharzeev-Levin-Nardi (2002) and the lattice QCD equation of state from Borsanyi (2010, 2011). The results enable future extraction of transport coefficients such as the baryon diffusion coefficient from net baryon distributions measured at RHIC and LHC, and contribute to the search for the QCD critical point by providing a consistent dissipative framework at finite density.
