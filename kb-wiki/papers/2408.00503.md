# Fully strange tetraquark resonant states as the cousins of $X(6900)$

> We conduct systematic calculations of the S-wave fully strange systems with ``normal" $\left(J^{P C}=0^{++}, 1^{+-}, 2^{++}\right)$ and ``exotic" $\left(J^{P C}=0^{+-}, 1^{++}, 2^{+-}\right)$ C-parities, which are the strange analogue of the fully charmed tetraquark state $X(6900)$. Within a constituent quark potential model, we employ the Gaussian expansion method to solve the four-body Schrödinger equation and the complex scaling method to identify resonant states. We obtain a series of resonant states and zero-width states in the mass range of 2.7 to 3.3 GeV, with their widths ranging from less than 1 MeV to about 50 MeV. Their rms radii strongly indicate that they are compact tetraquark states. Among these states, the $T_{4s,2^{++}}(2714)$ may be the most likely one to be observed experimentally. We urge the experimental exploration of the $2^{++}$ $s s \bar{s} \bar{s}$ state around 2.7 GeV in the $ϕϕ$ channel. Since the lowest S-wave $s s \bar{s} \bar{s}$ state is around 2.7 GeV, the compact P-wave $s s \bar{s} \bar{s}$ states are expected to be heavier. Hence, $ϕ(2170)$ and $X(2370)$ are unlikely to be compact tetraquark states.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a series of compact fully strange tetraquark resonant states exist in the mass range of 2.7 to 3.3 GeV, with the state \(T_{4s,2^{++}}(2714)\) being the most promising for experimental observation; however, the results are model-dependent and rely on the specific AL1 constituent quark potential, and the authors explicitly state that \(\phi(2170)\) and \(X(2370)\) are unlikely to be compact tetraquark states based on their calculated mass predictions.

## Method

The authors solved the four-body Schrödinger equation for the \(ss\bar{s}\bar{s}\) system using the Gaussian expansion method (GEM) within a nonrelativistic constituent quark potential model (the AL1 potential), incorporating both diquark-antidiquark and dimeson configurations. They employed the complex scaling method (CSM) to distinguish genuine resonant states from meson-meson scattering states, and calculated root-mean-square (rms) radii using both a conventional definition and a newly defined decomposition-based approach to classify the spatial structure as compact or molecular.

## Key numbers

* The \(T_{4s,2^{++}}(2714)\) state has a mass of 2714 MeV and a width of 12 MeV (half-width 6 MeV), with a dominant decay channel into \(\phi\phi\).
* The resonant states span a mass range of 2.7 to 3.3 GeV, with widths ranging from less than 1 MeV to about 50 MeV.
* For the \(0^{++}\) channel, five resonant states were found: \(2852-40i\) MeV, \(2917-9i\) MeV, \(3133-7i\) MeV, \(3175-4i\) MeV, and \(3248-10i\) MeV.
* For the \(1^{+-}\) channel, five resonant states were found: \(2819-3i\) MeV, \(2940-46i\) MeV, \(3142-12i\) MeV, \(3228-2i\) MeV, and \(3237-4i\) MeV.
* For the \(2^{++}\) channel, four resonant states were found: \(2714-6i\) MeV, \(2993-48i\) MeV, \(3164-3i\) MeV, and \(3266-2i\) MeV.
* For the exotic \(1^{++}\) channel, three resonant states were found: \(2723-0.5i\) MeV, \(2863-4i\) MeV, and \(3151-0.1i\) MeV.
* The lowest S-wave \(ss\bar{s}\bar{s}\) state is around 2.7 GeV, and the authors conclude that \(\phi(2170)\) and \(X(2370)\) are unlikely to be compact tetraquark states because compact P-wave states are expected to be heavier.

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of fully heavy tetraquark systems (such as the fully charmed \(X(6900)\) studied by LHCb (2020)) to the fully strange sector, providing the first comprehensive calculation of S-wave \(ss\bar{s}\bar{s}\) resonant states with both normal and exotic C-parities using the complex scaling method. Previous theoretical studies, such as those by Ebert (2008) and Lu (2019), focused only on the diquark-antidiquark picture and did not properly identify resonant states, while studies by Liu (2019) and Tan (2023) used the real scaling method which is less precise. This work enables future experimental searches for fully strange tetraquarks, particularly the \(2^{++}\) state around 2.7 GeV in the \(\phi\phi\) channel, and provides a benchmark for distinguishing compact tetraquarks from molecular states or conventional mesons.
