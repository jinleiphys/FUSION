# Predictions for the heavy-ion programme at the Large Hadron Collider

> I review the main predictions for the heavy-ion programme at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN, as available in early April 2009. I begin by remembering the standard claims made in view of the experimental data measured at the Super Proton Synchrotron (SPS) at CERN and at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) at the BNL. These claims will be used for later discussion of the new opportunities at the LHC. Next I review the generic, qualitative expectations for the LHC. Then I turn to quantitative predictions: First I analyze observables which characterize directly the medium produced in the collisions - bulk observables or soft probes -: multiplicities, collective flow, hadrochemistry at low transverse momentum, correlations and fluctuations. Second, I move to calibrated probes of the medium i.e. typically those whose expectation in the absence of any medium can be described in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) using perturbative techniques (pQCD), usually called hard probes. I discuss particle production at large transverse momentum and jets, heavy-quark and quarkonium production, and photons and dileptons. Finally, after a brief review of pA collisions, I end with a summary and a discussion about the potentiality of the measurements at the LHC - particularly those made during the first run - to further substantiate or, on the contrary, disproof the picture of the medium that has arisen from the confrontation between the SPS and RHIC data, and theoretical models.

# Predictions for the heavy-ion programme at the Large Hadron Collider

## Key claim
The review compiles predictions for PbPb collisions at the LHC as of early 2009, but explicitly states that none of the standard claims from SPS and RHIC data are devoid of alternative explanations, and that the presentation contains personal bias. The LHC measurements will either further substantiate or disprove the picture of a strongly coupled, nearly ideal fluid of partonic matter that emerged from RHIC data.

## Method
The author classifies observables into bulk observables (soft probes: multiplicities, collective flow, hadrochemistry, correlations, fluctuations) and hard probes (particle production at large transverse momentum, jets, heavy quarks, quarkonia, photons, dileptons) whose expectations in the absence of medium can be calculated using perturbative QCD. Predictions are reviewed from the CERN Theory Institute on Heavy Ion Collisions at the LHC (May-June 2007) and other sources, with qualitative expectations derived from scaling arguments based on charged multiplicity at mid-rapidity, which is used as a key parameter linking energy density, thermalization time, and other observables.

## Key numbers
- PbPb collisions at the LHC will have center-of-mass energy sqrt(s_NN) = 5.5 TeV, a factor of about 30 increase over RHIC (sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV)
- Nominal peak luminosity for PbPb is L0 = 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1, with average luminosity half that value and estimated running time 10^6 s/year
- For central PbPb collisions with N_part = 350, the charged multiplicity at mid-pseudorapidity ranges from 900 (wounded nucleon model lower bound) to 8400 (binary collision scaling upper bound)
- The Bjorken energy density estimate at formation time for the LHC is at least 22 GeV/fm^3 for the lowest multiplicity case (900), compared to 12 GeV/fm^3 at RHIC
- For the reference multiplicity case of 2600, the estimated energy density at thermalization (tau ~ 0.30 fm) is 64.4 GeV/fm^3 with initial temperature 0.422 GeV
- Elliptic flow v2/epsilon_x is predicted to increase by 35% to 205% depending on multiplicity scenario, while ideal hydrodynamics calculations show increases in integrated v2 of 15% to 60% from RHIC to LHC

## Context
This review extends the predictions made for RHIC by Bass et al. (1999) and Armesto et al. (2000) to the LHC energy regime, building on the standard claims from SPS and RHIC data about jet quenching, elliptic flow, and partonic matter formation. The work enables comparison of upcoming LHC measurements with the strongly coupled Quark-Gluon Plasma picture that emerged from RHIC, and addresses open questions about whether pQCD or strong coupling explains the observed phenomena, the role of viscosity in hydrodynamical calculations, and how early isotropization can be achieved.
