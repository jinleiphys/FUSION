# Memory effects on descent from nuclear fission barrier

> Non-Markovian transport equations for nuclear large amplitude motion are derived from the collisional kinetic equation. The memory effects are caused by the Fermi surface distortions and depend on the relaxation time. It is shown that the nuclear collective motion and the nuclear fission are influenced strongly by the memory effects at the relaxation time $τ\geq 5\cdot 10^{-23}{\rm s}$. In particular, the descent of the nucleus from the fission barrier is accompanied by characteristic shape oscillations. The eigenfrequency and the damping of the shape oscillations depend on the contribution of the memory integral in the equations of motion. The shape oscillations disappear at the short relaxation time regime at $τ\to 0$, which corresponds to the usual Markovian motion in the presence of friction forces. We show that the elastic forces produced by the memory integral lead to a significant delay for the descent of the nucleus from the barrier. Numerical calculations for the nucleus $^{236}$U shows that due to the memory effect the saddle-to-scission time grows by a factor of about 3 with respect to the corresponding saddle-to-scission time obtained in liquid drop model calculations with friction forces.

# Memory effects on descent from nuclear fission barrier

## Key claim

The paper claims that non-Markovian (memory) effects, arising from dynamic Fermi surface distortions in the nuclear Fermi liquid, significantly influence the descent from the fission barrier, causing a delay in saddle-to-scission time by a factor of about 3 relative to standard liquid drop model calculations with friction forces, but only when the relaxation time τ is ≥ 5×10⁻²³ s; at shorter relaxation times the memory effects vanish and the motion becomes Markovian.

## Method

The authors derived non-Markovian transport equations for nuclear shape variables from the collisional Landau-Vlasov kinetic equation, using p-moment techniques and restricting the Fermi surface distortion to multipolarity l=2. Assuming an incompressible and irrotational nuclear fluid with a sharp surface, they reduced the local Euler-like equation to equations of motion for shape parameters, solving the Neumann problem for the velocity field potential. Numerical calculations were performed for symmetric fission of ²³⁶U using a Lorentz parameterization of the nuclear shape, with the deformation energy taken from the liquid drop model and the scission line determined by the instability condition of the neck radius.

## Key numbers

- The memory effects become significant for relaxation time τ ≥ 5×10⁻²³ s
- For ²³⁶U, the saddle-to-scission time grows by a factor of about 3 due to memory effects compared to liquid drop model calculations with friction forces
- The quadrupole eigenvibration energy is given by ℏω₂⁺ ≈ 64.5 A⁻¹/³ MeV, which agrees with the experimental value of ℏω₂⁺ᵉˣᵖ ≈ 63·A⁻¹/³ MeV
- The characteristic frequency at the saddle point is ℏω_f = ℏ√(|C̃_LDM|/B_f) = 1.16 MeV for ²³⁶U
- The experimental fission-fragment kinetic energy for ²³⁶U is E_kinᵉˣᵖ = 168 MeV
- Good agreement with experimental data is obtained at relaxation time τ = 8×10⁻²³ s
- At the "experimental" relaxation time τ = 8×10⁻²³ s, the saddle-to-scission time increases by a factor of about 2 due to memory effects
- The descent time without memory effects is about 1 to 3×10⁻²¹ s
- The prescission Coulomb energy for ²³⁶U is about 5 MeV lower than the Coulomb interaction energy of the scission point shape

## Context

This work extends the traditional liquid drop model (LDM) theory of nuclear fission, as developed by Bohr and Wheeler (1939) and later by Swiatecki (1956) and others, to include the Fermi liquid nature of the nucleus. It builds on earlier non-Markovian approaches by Ayik and collaborators (1990) and by Boilley and Suraud (1993), but focuses specifically on the descent from the fission barrier rather than on the fission rate problem. The paper shows that the dynamic Fermi surface distortion, previously studied by Nix and Sierk (1980) for giant resonances, produces elastic forces that hinder the descent and delay scission, enabling a consistent description of fission-fragment kinetic energies that agrees with experimental data when the relaxation time is about 8×10⁻²³ s.
