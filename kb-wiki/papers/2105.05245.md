# Pauli energy contribution to nucleus-nucleus interaction

> Background: The Pauli exclusion principle plays a crucial role as a building block of many-body quantal systems comprised of fermions. It also induces a "Pauli repulsion" in the interaction between di-nuclear systems. It has been shown in [Phys. Rev. C 95, 031601 (2017)] that the Pauli repulsion widens the nucleus-nucleus potential barrier, thus hindering sub-barrier fusion. Purpose: To investigate the proton and neutron contributions to the Pauli repulsion, both in the bare potential neglecting shape polarization and transfer between the reactants, as well as in the dynamical potential obtained by accounting for such dynamical rearrangements. Methods: As the basis of our study we utilize the Pauli kinetic energy (PKE) obtained by studying the nuclear localization function (NLF). Recently this approach has been generalized to incorporate all of the dynamical and time-odd terms present in the nuclear energy density functional. This approach is employed in the DCFHF and DC-TDHF methods. Results: The PKE spatial distribution shows that a repulsion occurs in the neck between the nuclei when they first touch. Inside the barrier, neutrons can contribute significantly more to the Pauli repulsion in neutron-rich systems. Dynamical effects tend to lower the Pauli repulsion near the barrier. Proton and neutron dynamical contributions to the PKE significantly differ inside the barrier for asymmetric collisions, which is interpreted as an effect of multinucleon transfer. Conclusions: The PKE is shown to make a significant contribution to nuclear interaction potentials. Protons and neutrons can play very different roles in both the bare potential and in the dynamical rearrangement. Further microscopic studies are required to better understand the role of transfer and to investigate the effect of pairing and deformation.

# Pauli energy contribution to nucleus-nucleus interaction

## Key claim

The Pauli kinetic energy (PKE) makes a significant contribution to nucleus-nucleus interaction potentials, with neutrons contributing significantly more than protons to the Pauli repulsion inside the barrier in neutron-rich systems; however, dynamical effects from density rearrangement tend to lower the Pauli repulsion near the barrier, partially compensating the increase in barrier height.

## Method

The authors computed nucleus-nucleus potentials using three microscopic methods: frozen Hartree-Fock (FHF, which neglects Pauli repulsion between nuclei), density-constrained frozen Hartree-Fock (DCFHF, which includes Pauli repulsion with frozen densities), and density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DC-TDHF, which includes both Pauli repulsion and dynamical rearrangement). They decomposed the Pauli repulsion into proton and neutron contributions using the nucleon localization function (NLF) formalism, which expresses the Pauli kinetic energy as a spatial integral of a localization measure derived from the one-body density matrix, including all time-odd and spin-dependent terms of the Skyrme energy density functional.

## Key numbers

- The Pauli repulsion increases the barrier height by up to approximately 1.6 MeV in the 16O+208Pb system.
- In 48Ca+48Ca at R approximately 9 fm (inner turning point at about 0.9 times the barrier height), the proton Pauli repulsion is about 3 MeV while the neutron Pauli repulsion is about 5 MeV, giving a neutron-to-proton ratio exceeding the neutron-to-proton ratio of the system.
- The barrier radius for 48Ca+48Ca is approximately 10.8 fm, and for 16O+208Pb is approximately 12 fm.
- The neck density at the barrier radius does not exceed 20% of saturation density in the frozen calculations.
- The dependence of the Pauli energy on the choice of spin quantization axis reaches about 10% at very short distances but remains small in the physically relevant region for sub-barrier fusion.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous demonstration (Simenel 2017) that Pauli repulsion widens the nucleus-nucleus potential barrier and hinders sub-barrier fusion, by providing a microscopic decomposition into proton and neutron contributions using the NLF formalism. The NLF approach was originally developed for electronic systems (Becke 1990) and introduced to nuclear physics (Reinhard 2011), with recent generalization to include all time-odd terms (Li 2020). The results enable future microscopic studies of multinucleon transfer effects on Pauli repulsion, as well as investigations of pairing and deformation effects that were not addressed in this work.
