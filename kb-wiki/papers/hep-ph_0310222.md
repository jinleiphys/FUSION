# Light front thermal field theory at finite temperature and density

> We investigate quark matter at finite temperature and finite chemical potential as an example for a relativistic many-particle quantum system. Special relativity is realized through the front form that allows for a Hamiltonian formulation of a statistical operator. Utilizing our previous results we generalize the present formulation of a relativistic thermal field theory to include a finite chemical potential. As an application we use the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model to investigate the gap equation and chiral restoration.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that light-front thermal field theory, when properly formulated with a grand canonical ensemble, yields a Fermi distribution function that depends on both transverse momentum and the longitudinal light-front momentum \(k^+\), and that this formulation reproduces the same gap equation and chiral phase transition results as the conventional instant-form approach for the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model, provided a temperature- and chemical-potential-dependent cutoff is used.

## Method

The authors generalize their previous light-front thermal field theory to include a finite chemical potential by constructing the grand canonical partition operator from the light-front Hamiltonian \(P^-\) and the number operator \(N\). They then apply the imaginary-time (Matsubara) formalism to derive the in-medium fermion propagator and the Fermi distribution functions for particles and antiparticles. As an application, they use the NJL model in the mean-field (Hartree) approximation to derive the gap equation for the constituent quark mass at finite temperature and density, regularizing the divergent integrals with a light-front \(\Omega\) scheme that is shown to be equivalent to the instant-form three-momentum cutoff scheme via the Sawicki transformation.

## Key numbers

* Constituent quark mass in the isolated case: \(m = 336\) MeV, with bare mass \(\tilde m_0 = 5.67\) MeV and coupling \(\tilde G = 5.51 \times 10^{-6}\) MeV.
* Pion mass reproduced: \(m_\pi = 140\) MeV; pion decay constant: \(f_\pi = 93\) MeV.
* Quark condensate in the isolated case: \(\langle \bar u u\rangle^{1/3} = -247\) MeV.
* Critical temperature at zero chemical potential: \(T_c \simeq 190\) MeV.
* Cutoff parameter for the isolated case: \(\Omega = 714\) MeV; for in-medium calculations, \(\Lambda_\mathrm{3M} = 630\) MeV is fixed for all \(T\) and \(\mu\).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous light-front thermal field theory (Beyer 2001) to include a finite chemical potential, and it addresses a debate in the literature regarding the correct form of the light-front Fermi function, contrasting with the naive generalization proposed by Brodsky (2001, 2002) which leads to divergent integrals (Alves 2002). The paper demonstrates the equivalence of the light-front and instant-form formulations of the NJL model at finite temperature and density, reproducing the phenomenology reviewed by Klevansky (1992) and Schwarz (1999). This enables future applications of light-front methods to QCD at finite chemical potential, where lattice QCD is still limited, and to studies of multi-quark correlations near the phase transition.
