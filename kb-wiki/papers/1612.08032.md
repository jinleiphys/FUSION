# Heavy-flavor production and medium properties in high-energy nuclear collisions - What next?

> Open and hidden heavy-flavor physics in high-energy nuclear collisions are entering a new and exciting stage towards reaching a clearer understanding of the new experimental results with the possibility to link them directly to the advancement in lattice Quantum Chromo-dynamics (QCD). Recent results from experiments and theoretical developments regarding open and hidden heavy-flavor dynamics have been debated at the Lorentz Workshop "Tomography of the quark-gluon plasma with heavy quarks}, which was held in October 2016 in Leiden, the Netherlands. In this contribution, we summarize identified common understandings and developed strategies for the upcoming five years, which aim at achieving a profound knowledge of the dynamical properties of the quark-gluon plasma.

## Key claim

The paper summarizes the common understandings and strategic priorities identified at the 2016 Lorentz Workshop "Tomography of the quark-gluon plasma with heavy quarks," aiming to link experimental heavy-flavor results directly to advances in lattice QCD over the following five years, but it explicitly states that the problem of quarkonium properties at high temperatures is far from solved and that further improvements in lattice precision and Bayesian methods alone will not be sufficient.

## Method

The workshop organized three Discussion Groups to debate implications and open issues concerning theoretical and experimental results on open and hidden heavy-flavor dynamics in high-energy nucleus-nucleus collisions. The groups focused on: which proposed energy-loss mechanisms are compatible with lattice results; next steps for comparing different heavy-quark energy-loss models; and identification of current experimental issues, limitations, and key observables. The paper synthesizes the conclusions and recommendations from these group discussions.

## Key numbers

- Lattice QCD computations have converged on an equation of state in the range of 150-300 MeV for a realistic QCD medium with light u, d, and s quarks.
- Effects of dynamical charm quarks set in at temperatures as low as 400 MeV, provided the plasma lives long enough to reach chemical equilibrium.
- For the Upsilon(1S), a dissociation temperature of about 450 MeV is obtained within pQCD.
- Lattice studies of heavy-quark diffusion have reached the continuum limit, with results that can be directly used in phenomenology; the analysis of Francis et al. (2015) contains a realistic estimate of systematic uncertainties.
- Temperatures up to 2 T_c (approximately 400 MeV) are studied in NRQCD lattice calculations for bottom quarks.
- Charm fluctuations and correlations are well described by hadron resonance gas below T_c, and for temperatures above 300 MeV the description in terms of weak coupling calculations appears sufficiently accurate.

## Context

This paper extends the long-standing program of using heavy quarks as probes of the quark-gluon plasma, building on the foundational work of Matsui and Satz (1986) on quarkonium melting and the extensive reviews by Brambilla et al. (2014) and others. It synthesizes discussions from the Lorentz Workshop to identify strategies for the next five years, aiming to connect experimental results from RHIC and LHC with lattice QCD calculations of transport coefficients, spectral functions, and the in-medium heavy-quark potential. The work enables future progress by identifying specific synergies needed between lattice NRQCD, pNRQCD, T-matrix approaches, and open quantum systems frameworks to reliably establish quarkonium properties at high temperature.
