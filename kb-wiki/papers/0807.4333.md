# Stress Tensor and Bulk Viscosity in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

> We discuss the influence of different initial conditions for the stress tensor and the effect of bulk viscosity on the expansion and cooling of the fireball created in relativistic heavy-ion collisions. In particular, we explore the evolution of longitudinal and transverse components of the pressure and the extent of dissipative entropy production in the one-dimensional, boost-invariant hydrodynamic model. We find that a bulk viscosity consistent with recent estimates from lattice QCD further slows the equilibration of the system, however it does not significantly increase the entropy produced.

# Stress Tensor and Bulk Viscosity in Relativistic Nuclear Collisions

## Key claim

Using a one-dimensional boost-invariant hydrodynamic model with a lattice-QCD-inspired equation of state and minimal shear viscosity, the authors find that a bulk viscosity consistent with recent lattice estimates further slows the equilibration of the fireball but does not significantly increase entropy production, contrary to some earlier expectations. The result is conditional on the bulk viscosity not exceeding roughly twice the lattice-inspired value, beyond which the longitudinal pressure becomes unacceptably small or negative and the hydrodynamic approximation breaks down.

## Method

The authors solve the second-order (Israel-Stewart) viscous hydrodynamic equations for a one-dimensional boost-invariant Bjorken expansion with transverse translational and rotational symmetry, using a lattice QCD equation of state for two light and one heavy quark flavor (Cheng et al., 2007). The shear viscosity is fixed at the KSS bound η = s/(4π), while the bulk viscosity ζ is parameterized from quenched lattice QCD results (Meyer, 2007) using the conformal measure ω = (ε-3P)/(ε+P) as a scaling variable, with the peak height and width varied by factors c_ζ and a_ζ. Three initial conditions for the stress tensor are compared: equilibrium (Π=Φ=0), first-order Navier-Stokes values, and a maximally anisotropic case with zero longitudinal pressure.

## Key numbers

- Initial energy density ε(τ₀) = 50 GeV/fm³ at starting time τ₀ = 0.3 fm/c, corresponding to an initial equilibrium temperature of roughly 400 MeV.
- Critical temperature T_c = 196 MeV from the lattice equation of state used.
- The bulk viscosity ζ/s exceeds the shear viscosity η/s = 1/(4π) for temperatures below 220 MeV.
- For the lattice-inspired bulk viscosity (c_ζ=1), the longitudinal pressure reaches at most half of the equilibrium pressure throughout the entire quark-gluon plasma phase lifetime.
- The entropy produced by bulk viscosity is at most 10% of the final entropy for the standard parametrization (c_ζ=1).
- The time to reach T_c is approximately τ_c ≈ 8.4 fm/c for the anisotropic initial condition with c_ζ=1.
- The pressure anisotropy |P_T - P_z|/P remains as large as 20% around T_c.
- Different initial conditions for the shear stress are wiped out after Δτ ≈ 0.5 fm/c, after which the system evolves universally.

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of dissipative effects in boost-invariant expansion by Baym (1984) and Heiselberg and Wang (1995, 1996), who used relaxation-time approximations to the Boltzmann equation, and addresses questions raised by Karsch et al. (2007) about the role of bulk viscosity near the QCD phase transition. The results contrast with those of Torrieri and Mishustin (2008), who used a much more strongly peaked bulk viscosity parametrization and found hydrodynamic instabilities; the present authors show that such instabilities arise only for bulk viscosity values far exceeding current lattice estimates. The findings enable future work on the influence of bulk viscosity on identical-particle correlations (HBT interferometry) and dilepton yields, as suggested by Martinez and Strickland (2008), and motivate full three-dimensional viscous hydrodynamic simulations including both bulk and shear viscosity.
