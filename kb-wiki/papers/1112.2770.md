# The Impact of Recent Advances in Laboratory Astrophysics on our Understanding of the Cosmos

> An emerging theme in modern astrophysics is the connection between astronomical observations and the underlying physical phenomena that drive our cosmos. Both the mechanisms responsible for the observed astrophysical phenomena and the tools used to probe such phenomena - the radiation and particle spectra we observe - have their roots in atomic, molecular, condensed matter, plasma, nuclear and particle physics. Chemistry is implicitly included in both molecular and condensed matter physics. This connection is the theme of the present report, which provides a broad, though non-exhaustive, overview of progress in our understanding of the cosmos resulting from recent theoretical and experimental advances in what is commonly called laboratory astrophysics. This work, carried out by a diverse community of laboratory astrophysicists, is increasingly important as astrophysics transitions into an era of precise measurement and high fidelity modeling.

## Key claim

This review paper argues that laboratory astrophysics, encompassing theoretical and experimental studies in atomic, molecular, condensed matter, plasma, nuclear, and particle physics, is increasingly critical as astrophysics transitions into an era of precise measurement and high-fidelity modeling, but the authors explicitly note that their overview is "broad, though non-exhaustive" and limited primarily to the past decade.

## Method

The authors, comprising past and current members of the American Astronomical Society Working Group on Laboratory Astrophysics, surveyed recent advances across six subfields of physics (atomic, molecular, condensed matter, plasma, nuclear, and particle) and organized their report into five astronomical categories: planetary systems and star formation, stars and stellar evolution, the galactic neighborhood, galaxies across time, and cosmology and fundamental physics. Within each category, they reviewed specific laboratory astrophysics results that have advanced understanding of astrophysical phenomena, citing both experimental measurements and theoretical calculations.

## Key numbers

* Electron density of ~10^13 cm^-3 expected at accretion shocks in young late-type stars, based on ram pressure balance
* Free-fall velocity of ~500 km s^-1 for accreting material onto low-mass young stars, producing shock temperatures around a few MK
* Observed density of cooler O VII in TW Hya is lower than that of hotter Ne IX by a factor of 4, and lower than the standard post-shock model prediction by a factor of 7
* Photodesorption efficiency per photon for CO under UV radiation is approximately 10^-3, sufficient to explain gas-phase CO detection in protoplanetary disks at temperatures below its sublimation point
* Cometary volatile ices are dominated by H2O ice at about 70-90%, with other major components including CO, CH3OH, CO2, and H2CO
* Nearly 500 exoplanets discovered to date using various techniques, with approximately 100 transiting exoplanets where both mass and radius can be determined
* Close to 500 diffuse interstellar absorption bands (DIBs) reported to date in local and extragalactic environments

## Context

This review complements previous reviews on laboratory astrophysics in atomic physics (Beiersdorfer 2003; Kallman and Palmeri 2007), molecular physics (Salama 1999; Tielens 2005; Herbst and van Dishoeck 2009), condensed matter physics (Draine 2003; Whittet 2003), plasma physics (Drake 1999; Remington et al. 2006; Zweibel and Yamada 2009; Yamada et al. 2010), nuclear physics (Kappeler et al. 2011; Wiescher et al. 2010; Adelberger et al. 2011), and particle physics (Grupen 2005; Aprile and Profumo 2009). The paper enables astronomers to understand which laboratory measurements and calculations are most urgently needed to interpret current and future observations, particularly as missions like Kepler and Chandra continue to produce high-precision data requiring accurate atomic, molecular, and nuclear data.
