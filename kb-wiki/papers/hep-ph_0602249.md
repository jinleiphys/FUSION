# Dissipative Hydrodynamics and Heavy Ion Collisions

> Recent discussions of RHIC data emphasized the exciting possibility that the matter produced in nucleus-nucleus collisions shows properties of a near-perfect fluid. Here, we aim at delineating the applicability of fluid dynamics, which is needed to quantify the size of corresponding dissipative effects. We start from the equations for dissipative fluid dynamics, which we derive from kinetic theory up to second order (Israel-Stewart theory) in a systematic gradient expansion. In model studies, we then establish that for too early initialization of the hydrodynamic evolution ($τ_0 \lsim 1$ fm/c) or for too high transverse momentum ($p_T \gsim 1$ GeV) in the final state, the expected dissipative corrections are too large for a fluid description to be reliable. Moreover, viscosity-induced modifications of hadronic transverse momentum spectra can be accommodated to a significant degree in an ideal fluid description by modifications of the decoupling stage. We argue that these conclusions, drawn from model studies, can also be expected to arise in significantly more complex, realistic fluid dynamics simulations of heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The paper argues that dissipative hydrodynamics (Israel-Stewart theory) is only reliable for heavy ion collisions when the hydrodynamic evolution is initialized late enough (tau_0 greater than about 1 fm/c) and when final-state transverse momenta are low enough (p_T less than about 1 GeV); outside these bounds, dissipative corrections become too large for the gradient expansion to converge, and the fluid description loses predictive power.

## Method

The authors derive second-order dissipative hydrodynamic equations (Israel-Stewart theory) from kinetic theory via a systematic gradient expansion of the Boltzmann equation. They then apply these equations to a simplified one-dimensional, boost-invariant, transversely homogeneous model of heavy ion collisions, solving numerically for the temperature evolution and the shear tensor component Pi^{eta eta} using an ideal equation of state (epsilon = 3p) and perturbative QCD estimates for the shear viscosity over entropy density ratio eta/s. They compute hadronic transverse momentum spectra at freeze-out via the Cooper-Frye formalism, including dissipative corrections to the phase space distribution.

## Key numbers

* For initialization time tau_0 less than about 1 fm/c, dissipative corrections become too large for the fluid description to be reliable (Section IV A).
* For final-state transverse momentum p_T greater than about 1 GeV, dissipative corrections become too large for the fluid description to be reliable (abstract, Section IV B).
* For eta/s = 0.3 and initial conditions T_0 = 300 MeV, tau_0 = 1 fm/c, the critical transverse momentum p_T^{crit} is below 0.5 GeV (Section IV B, Fig. 3).
* For eta/s = 0.08 and initial conditions T_0 = 300 MeV, tau_0 = 1 fm/c, the dissipative strength Phi/(epsilon+p) at freeze-out (T_fo = 150 MeV) is about 0.02 (Section IV C, Fig. 4).
* The freeze-out time tau_fo (when T = 150 MeV) increases with decreasing initialization time tau_0 for fixed T_0^3 tau_0 = constant, with eta/s = 0.3 and eta/s = 0.08 (Section IV A, Fig. 2).
* For a massless Boltzmann gas, the ratio beta_2 = tau_Pi/(2 eta) equals 3/(4p) (Section III C 3, Eq. 3.22).

## Context

This work extends the application of Israel-Stewart (1979) second-order dissipative hydrodynamics to heavy ion collisions, building on earlier viscous hydrodynamic studies by Muronga (2004) and Heinz (2005). It directly addresses the interpretation of RHIC data that suggested a near-perfect fluid (Shuryak 2004, Heinz 2005), by quantifying the regime where dissipative corrections are small enough for hydrodynamics to be valid. The results enable future work to extract transport coefficients like eta/s from data, but only if the hydrodynamic initialization time and transverse momentum range are carefully chosen to ensure the gradient expansion is under control.
