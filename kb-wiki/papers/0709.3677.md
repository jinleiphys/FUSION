# Detailed description of accelerating, simple solutions of relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics

> In this paper we describe in full details a new family of recently found exact solutions of relativistic, perfect fluid dynamics. With an ansatz, which generalizes the well-known Hwa-Bjorken solution, we obtain a wide class of new exact, explicit and simple solutions, which have a remarkable advantage as compared to presently known exact and explicit solutions: they do not lack acceleration. They can be utilized for the description of the evolution of the matter created in high energy heavy ion collisions. Because these solutions are accelerating, they provide a more realistic picture than the well-known Hwa-Bjorken solution, and give more insight into the dynamics of the matter. We exploit this by giving an advanced simple estimation of the initial energy density of the produced matter in high energy collisions, which takes acceleration effects (i.e. the work done by the pressure and the modified change of the volume elements) into account. We also give an advanced estimation of the life-time of the reaction. Our new solutions can also be used to test numerical hydrodynamical codes reliably. In the end, we also give an exact, 1+1 dimensional, relativistic hydrodynamical solution, where the initial pressure and velocity profile is arbitrary, and we show that this general solution is stable for perturbations.

## Key claim

This paper presents a new family of exact, explicit, and simple solutions of relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics that, unlike the well-known Hwa-Bjorken solution, include acceleration effects; the solutions are obtained under the specific condition that the equation of state is \(\varepsilon = \kappa p\) and the flow rapidity is linear in the space-time rapidity, \(\Omega = \lambda \eta\), with the parameter \(\lambda\) controlling the acceleration.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic Euler and energy conservation equations for a perfect fluid with an equation of state \(\varepsilon = \kappa p\). They introduce Rindler coordinates \((\tau, \eta)\) inside the forward lightcone and generalize the Hwa-Bjorken ansatz by setting the fluid rapidity to \(\Omega = \lambda \eta\), where \(\lambda\) is a constant. Substituting this ansatz into the hydrodynamical equations yields a constraint equation that determines the allowed combinations of \(\lambda\), the number of spatial dimensions \(d\), and the equation-of-state parameter \(\kappa\); solving this constraint produces five distinct solution families, which are then extended to the region outside the lightcone and used to compute the rapidity distribution and improved estimates of initial energy density and reaction lifetime.

## Key numbers

- For the \(\lambda = 2\) solution (case a.), the equation of state requires \(\kappa = d\); in three spatial dimensions this gives \(\kappa = 3\), corresponding to an ultrarelativistic ideal gas.
- For the \(\lambda = 1/2\) solution (case b.), the equation of state is fixed to \(\kappa = 1\) for any number of spatial dimensions \(d\).
- For the \(\lambda = 3/2\) solution (case c.), the equation of state is \(\kappa = (4d-1)/3\); for \(d = 3\) this yields \(\kappa = 11/3\).
- The approximate rapidity distribution for \(\lambda > 1/2\) and \(m/T_f \gg 1\) is given by \(\frac{dn}{dy} \approx \frac{dn}{dy}\big|_{y=0} \cosh^{\pm \frac{\alpha}{2} - 1}(y/\alpha) \, e^{-\frac{m}{T_f}[\cosh^\alpha(y/\alpha) - 1]}\), where \(\alpha = (2\lambda - 1)/(\lambda - 1)\).
- The Gaussian width of the rapidity distribution at \(y = 0\) is \(\Delta y^2 = \alpha / (m/T_f \mp 1/2 + 1/\alpha)\).
- The correction factor for the initial energy density relative to the Bjorken estimate is \(\varepsilon_c / \varepsilon_{Bj} = (2\lambda - 1) (\tau_f / \tau_0)^{\lambda - 1}\).

## Context

This work extends the classic Hwa-Bjorken solution (Hwa, 1974; Bjorken, 1982), which is boost-invariant and accelerationless, by introducing a family of accelerating exact solutions that overcome a major limitation of previous exact solutions. The new solutions provide a more realistic description of the matter created in high-energy heavy ion collisions than the accelerationless Buda-Lund type solutions (Csorgo, 2003) and the implicit Landau-Khalatnikov solution (Landau, 1953; Khalatnikov, 1954). They enable improved estimates of initial energy density and reaction lifetime that account for pressure work and volume-element changes, and they can serve as test cases for numerical relativistic hydrodynamics codes.
