# Nucleon Charge Symmetry Breaking and Parity Violating Electron-Proton Scattering

> The consequences of the charge symmetry breaking effects of the mass difference between the up and down quarks and electromagnetic effects for searches for strangeness form factors in parity violating electron scattering from the proton are investigated. The formalism necessary to identify and compute the relevant observables is developed by separating the Hamiltonian into charge symmetry conserving and breaking terms. Using a set of SU(6) non-relativistic quark models, the effects of the charge symmetry breaking Hamiltonian are considered for experimentally relevant alues of the momentum transfer and found to be less than about 1 percent. The charge symmetry breaking corrections to the Bjorken sum rule are also studied and shown to vanish in first-order perturbation theory.

# Nucleon Charge Symmetry Breaking and Parity Violating Electron-Proton Scattering

## Key claim

Charge symmetry breaking effects from the up-down quark mass difference and electromagnetic interactions contribute less than about 1% to the parity-violating asymmetry in electron-proton scattering, but this estimate depends on the specific non-relativistic quark model used and could reach up to 2% for models with large strong coupling constant. The charge symmetry breaking corrections to the Bjorken sum rule vanish in first-order perturbation theory.

## Method

The author separates the Hamiltonian into charge symmetry conserving and breaking terms, then uses first-order perturbation theory to compute the correction term δX^μ_fi(Q²) that modifies the relationship between proton and neutron electromagnetic form factors. Three different SU(6) non-relativistic quark models with harmonic oscillator confinement are employed, with parameters determined from the neutron-proton mass difference and the Δ-nucleon mass splitting. A closure approximation is used to sum over intermediate excited states, with the average excitation energy determined by requiring that the first correction to the closure approximation vanishes.

## Key numbers

- Charge symmetry breaking effects on the electric and magnetic form factors are found to be less than about 1% for experimentally relevant momentum transfers, with model 1 (largest α_s = 2.3) showing effects up to 2%
- The average quark mass is set to \bar{m} = 337 MeV, chosen to reproduce the proton magnetic moment of 2.79 nuclear magnetons
- The neutron-proton mass difference is expressed as M_n - M_p = (m_d - m_u)[1 - 1/(2β\bar{m}²) - (α_s/\bar{m}³β^{3/2})√(2/π)(5/9)] - (α/3)√(2/πβ)(1 - 5/(12\bar{m}²β))
- The planned parity violation experiments cover Q² values from about 0.1 to 3 GeV²/c²
- The maximum Q² values for the three models are 1.6, 2.2, and 3.1 GeV²/c² respectively
- The kinetic energy contribution to the average excitation energy is ΔE_{E,M}(Q², ΔK) = -2/(\bar{m}β) = -2ℏω
- The low Q² limit for the long-range electromagnetic contribution is ΔE_{E,M}(0, ΔV_L) = -4/(\bar{m}β)
- The low Q² limit for the short-range contribution is ΔE_{E,M}(0, ΔV_S) = -40/(9\bar{m}β)

## Context

This work extends the formalism of charge symmetry breaking developed in reviews by Miller, Nefkens, and Slaus (1990) and others, applying it specifically to the interpretation of parity-violating electron-proton scattering experiments searching for strange quark form factors. The analysis is motivated by the EMC spin effect and the subsequent search for strangeness in the nucleon, following the theoretical framework of Kaplan and Manohar (1988) and McKeown, Kowalski, et al. (1989). The results enable experimentalists to quantify the systematic uncertainty from charge symmetry breaking when extracting strange form factors from parity-violating asymmetry measurements, with the first such measurement by the SAMPLE collaboration finding a strange magnetic form factor consistent with zero. The author compares his approach with those of Dmitrasinovic and Pollock (1995) and Shakin and collaborators, noting that differences in treatment of the nucleon mass in form factor definitions introduce effects of order (M_n-M_p)/\bar{M} ≈ 1.3×10⁻³ which are negligible.
