# Hadron spectroscopy from strangeness to charm and beauty

> Quarks of different flavors have different masses, which will cause breaking of flavor symmetries of QCD. Flavor symmetries and their breaking in hadron spectroscopy play important role for understanding the internal structures of hadrons. Hadron spectroscopy with strangeness reveals the importance of unquenched quark dynamics. Systematic study of hadron spectroscopy with strange, charm and beauty quarks would be very revealing and essential for understanding the internal structure of hadrons and its underlying quark dynamics.

## Key claim
Flavor symmetry breaking in QCD, revealed by hadron spectroscopy with strangeness, indicates that the dominant excitation mechanism for the lowest spatial excited states is not the excitation of a constituent quark to a higher orbital angular momentum state, but rather the dragging out of a light quark-antiquark pair from the gluon field, leading to substantial multi-quark components (e.g., pentaquark or tetraquark configurations) in hadrons.

## Method
The paper reviews experimental data and theoretical models for meson and baryon spectroscopy across strangeness, charm, and beauty sectors. It compares the predictions of classical quenched constituent quark models (fixed number of quarks) with unquenched models incorporating multi-quark components (e.g., tetraquark, pentaquark, and meson-baryon molecular states) and meson-baryon dynamical models using local hidden gauge formalism. The analysis focuses on the scalar meson nonet and the lowest \(1/2^-\) and \(3/2^-\) baryon nonets, including a combined fit of Crystal Ball and older data for \(K^-p \to \pi^0\Lambda\) and \(K^-n \to \pi^-\Lambda\) reactions.

## Key numbers
- The classical constituent quark model predicted the \(\Omega(sss)\) baryon mass around 1670 MeV, which was later confirmed by experiment.
- The unquenched quark models predict the \(1/2^-\) \(\Sigma^*\) and \(\Xi^*\) masses around 1400 MeV and 1550 MeV, respectively, while classical quenched models predict them around 1650 MeV and 1760 MeV, respectively.
- Meson-baryon dynamical models predict the \(1/2^-\) \(\Sigma^*\) and \(\Xi^*\) masses around 1450 MeV and 1620 MeV, respectively.
- A narrow \(\Lambda(1670)3/2^-\) resonance with a width of about 1.5 MeV was claimed from an analysis of \(K^-p \to \eta\Lambda\) data.
- Several narrow \(N^*\) and \(\Lambda^*\) resonances with hidden charm are predicted with masses around 4.3 GeV and widths smaller than 100 MeV.
- Two \(N^*_{b\bar b}\) states and four \(\Lambda^*_{b\bar b}\) states with hidden beauty are predicted with masses above 11 GeV and widths of only a few MeV.
- For the \(pp \to pp \eta_b\) reaction, the best center-of-mass energy for observing the predicted \(N^*_{b\bar{b}}\) is 13 to 25 GeV, with a production cross section of about 0.01 nb.
- For the \(e^-p \to e^-p \Upsilon\) reaction, when the center-of-mass energy is larger than 14 GeV, the production cross section should be larger than 0.1 nb.

## Context
This paper reviews and extends the long-standing debate on the nature of hadron resonances, particularly the scalar meson nonet (Jaffe (1977) proposing tetraquark states) and the lowest \(1/2^-\) baryon nonet, where the classical quark model fails to explain the mass ordering and decay patterns. It builds on the unquenched quark dynamics approach (Zou (2008)) and meson-baryon dynamical models (Oller, Weise, and others) to propose that multi-quark components are essential. The work enables future experimental searches for predicted super-heavy \(N^*\) and \(\Lambda^*\) resonances with hidden charm and beauty at facilities like PANDA/FAIR, JLab 12-GeV upgrade, and JPARC, and notes the recent observation of the iso-vector meson partners \(Z_b(10610)\) and \(Z_b(10650)\) by the Belle Collaboration as supporting evidence.
