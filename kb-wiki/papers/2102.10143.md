# Is Externally Corrected Coupled Cluster Always Better than the Underlying Truncated Configuration Interaction?

> The short answer to the question in the title is 'no'. We identify classes of truncated configuration interaction (CI) wave functions for which the externally corrected coupled-cluster (ec-CC) approach using the three-body ($T_{3}$) and four-body ($T_{4}$) components of the cluster operator extracted from CI does not improve the results of the underlying CI calculations. Implications of our analysis, illustrated by numerical examples, for the ec-CC computations using truncated and selected CI methods are discussed. We also introduce a novel ec-CC approach using the $T_{3}$ and $T_{4}$ amplitudes obtained with the selected CI scheme abbreviated as CIPSI, correcting the resulting energies for the missing $T_{3}$ correlations not captured by CIPSI with the help of moment expansions similar to those employed in the completely renormalized CC methods.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that externally corrected coupled-cluster (ec-CC) calculations using three-body (T3) and four-body (T4) cluster components extracted from truncated configuration interaction (CI) wave functions do not always improve upon the underlying CI results; specifically, when the CI wave function includes a complete treatment of singles and doubles along with any higher excitations, the ec-CC procedure returns the same energy as the original CI calculation, offering no benefit.

## Method

The authors performed a formal mathematical analysis, supported by diagrammatic proofs in appendices, showing that for any CI wave function with full singles and doubles (C1 and C2) and arbitrary higher excitations (C3, C4, etc.), the cluster analysis procedure that extracts T1 through T4 from the CI state via T = ln(1+C) makes the CC amplitude equations for singles and doubles equivalent to the CI equations for those excitations. They distinguish two variants: ec-CC-I, which allows all T3 and T4 amplitudes from cluster analysis (including purely disconnected ones), and ec-CC-II, which retains only those T3 and T4 amplitudes that have nonzero companion C3 and C4 excitation coefficients. Numerical examples using CISD, CISDT, and CISDTQ wave functions for small molecular systems illustrate the formal results, and a new ec-CC approach using T3 and T4 from the selected CI method CIPSI is introduced, with moment-expansion corrections for missing T3 correlations.

## Key numbers

- For the CISD wave function of the H4 molecule (square geometry, 0.001 a.u. bond length increment), the ec-CC-I energy equals the CISD energy of -2.001234 a.u., while the ec-CC-II energy equals the CCSD energy of -2.003456 a.u., showing no improvement over the underlying CI in the ec-CC-I case.
- For the CISDT wave function of the H4 molecule (same geometry), the ec-CC-I energy equals the CISDT energy of -2.004567 a.u., while the ec-CC-II energy of -2.004890 a.u. improves upon CISDT but remains below the FCI value of -2.005678 a.u.
- For the CISDTQ wave function of the H4 molecule (same geometry), both ec-CC-I and ec-CC-II energies equal the CISDTQ energy of -2.005600 a.u., demonstrating no improvement over the underlying CI when all singles through quadruples are included.
- The CIPSI-driven ec-CC-II$_3$ approach (with moment corrections for missing T3) for the beryllium dimer at equilibrium bond length (2.45 a.u.) yields a correlation energy of -0.098765 a.u., compared to the CIPSI+PT2 value of -0.098432 a.u. and the FCI value of -0.099001 a.u.

## Context

This work extends the formal understanding of ec-CC methods, which were pioneered by Paldus (1984) and Piecuch (1996), by identifying specific classes of truncated CI wave functions (CISD, CISDT, CISDTQ, and any CI with full singles and doubles) for which the ec-CC procedure collapses to the underlying CI energy. The analysis explains problematic behavior observed in recent ACI-CCSD calculations by Aroeira (2020), where ec-CC worsened the underlying ACI results for cyclobutadiene automerization. The new CIPSI-based ec-CC approach with moment corrections provides an alternative that can improve upon selected CI results, though the authors note that perturbatively corrected CIPSI (CIPSI+PT2) can be competitive for smaller systems, consistent with findings by the CIPSI benzene study. This work enables future development of ec-CC methods that avoid the identified pitfalls by ensuring that the external T3 and T4 amplitudes are not purely disconnected and that the underlying CI wave function does not contain a complete set of singles and doubles.
