# Large long-distance contributions to the electric dipole moments of charged leptons in the standard model

> We reevaluate the electric dipole moment (EDM) of charged leptons in the standard model using hadron effective models. We find unexpectedly large EDM generated by the hadron level long-distance effect, $d_e = { 5.8 \times 10^{-40} }$, $d_Ој= { 1.4 \times 10^{-38}}$, and $d_П„= { -7.3 \times 10^{-38} }$ ecm, with an error bar of 70\%, exceeding the conventionally known four-loop level elementary contribution by several orders of magnitude.

## Key claim

The standard model's CKM-driven electric dipole moment of charged leptons is several orders of magnitude larger than previously thought, because the hadron-level long-distance effect avoids the severe GIM cancellation that suppresses the elementary four-loop contribution. The quoted values carry a 70% theoretical uncertainty.

## Method

The authors evaluate one-loop diagrams involving a K* meson, a charged lepton, and an unflavored vector meson (ПЃ, П‰, П†) using hadron effective models. The K*-lepton coupling is derived from short-distance W-boson and Z-boson loops, while the K*-to-vector-meson transition is obtained by factorizing the |О”S|=1 four-quark effective Hamiltonian in the vacuum saturation approximation. The three-vector-meson strong interaction is described by hidden local symmetry.

## Context

This work extends the long-distance mechanism previously applied to nucleon EDMs (Khriplovich 1981, McKellar 1987, Seng 2014) and nuclear Schiff moments (Yamanaka 2015, 2016; Lee 2018) to the case of charged leptons. The result shows that the standard model background for lepton EDM searches is much larger than the elementary four-loop estimates of Pospelov (1991, 2013) and Booth (1993), potentially narrowing the window for new physics. The authors note that future experimental sensitivities (Vutha 2017) reaching 10вЃ»ВівЃµвЂ“10вЃ»ВівЃ· e cm could probe this contribution, and that lattice QCD methods used for light-by-light scattering (Blum 2019) and Kв†’ПЂПЂ decay (Bai 2015) may help reduce the hadronic uncertainty.
