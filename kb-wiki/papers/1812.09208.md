# Energy dependence of fission-fragment neutron multiplicity in $^{235}\textrm{U}(n,f)$

> A consistent framework for treating the energy dependence of fission-fragment neutron multiplicities is presented. The shape evolution of the compound nucleus towards scission is treated in the strong damping limit using the Metropolis walk method. The available excitation energy at scission is then divided statistically between the two fragments using microscopic level densities. Deformation energies, which contribute to the excitation energy when the fragments relax to their ground-state shapes, are also computed. From the total fragment excitation energies, the number of emitted neutrons is obtained and illustrated for neutron-induced fission of $^{235}\textrm{U}$.

# Energy dependence of fission-fragment neutron multiplicity in $^{235}\textrm{U}(n,f)$

## Key claim
The Metropolis walk method combined with microscopic level densities can reproduce the energy dependence of fission-fragment neutron multiplicities for neutron-induced fission of $^{235}\mathrm{U}$, with all calculated total neutron multiplicity values falling inside experimental error bars, though the calculated multiplicity distribution is slightly narrower than the experimental data.

## Method
The shape evolution of the compound nucleus from the second minimum to scission is simulated in the strong damping limit using a Metropolis walk on a five-dimensional potential-energy surface (quadrupole moment, neck radius, two fragment deformations, and mass asymmetry) calculated with the macroscopic-microscopic model. At scission, the available excitation energy is partitioned between the two fragments using a microcanonical distribution based on microscopic level densities calculated up to 12 MeV for the fragments, and deformation energies from fragment shape relaxation are added. Neutron evaporation from each fragment is then modeled sequentially using microscopic level densities at ground-state deformations, with one million walks performed for convergence.

## Key numbers
- Critical neck radius set to $c_0 = 1.5$ fm based on comparisons with experimental total kinetic energy data.
- Microscopic level densities employed up to excitation energy $E^\ast \approx 6$ MeV for the compound nucleus and up to $E^\ast \approx 12$ MeV for fragments, then extrapolated using an analytical formula with parameter $e_0$ determined by matching in the 9-12 MeV range.
- For the mass split $(A_{\mathrm{H}}:A_{\mathrm{L}}) = (130:106)$ at scission energy $E^\ast_{\mathrm{sc}} = 10$ MeV, the energy distribution $P(E^\ast_{\mathrm{H}})$ is shown for both microscopic and Fermi-gas level densities.
- All calculated average total neutron multiplicity values for $^{235}\mathrm{U}(n,f)$ as a function of incident neutron energy lie inside experimental error bars from Ethvignot (2005).
- The calculated multiplicity distribution for thermal-neutron-induced fission is compared to experimental data from Boldeman (1985).

## Context
This work extends the Metropolis walk method developed by Ward (2017) and Randrup (2011) by incorporating microscopically calculated level densities for both the compound nucleus and fragments, rather than using Fermi-gas level densities. It builds on the macroscopic-microscopic model of Moller (2009) for potential-energy surfaces and the microscopic level density method of Uhrenholt (2013). The approach contrasts with phenomenological models like the Los Alamos Model (Madland, 1982), FREYA (Randrup, 2009), CGMF (Becker, 2013), and FIFRELIN (Litaize, 2010) that rely on experimental mass and kinetic energy distributions, and with the semi-empirical GEF model (Schmidt, 2016) that requires parameter adjustment. The event-by-event nature of the model enables predictions for fission cases where no experimental data exist, using only potential-energy surfaces and level densities as input.
