# Meson-exchange currents and nuclear correlations in neutrino and electron scattering with nuclei

> This thesis is dedicated to the study of electron and neutrino scattering on nuclei, with special emphasis on meson-exchange currents (MEC) and short-range correlations (SRC) between nucleon pairs in one-particle emission processes. In chapter 2, the SuSAM* model is improved by redefining the single nucleon hadronic tensor, averaging it over a Fermi distribution instead of using the previous extrapolation from the relativistic Fermi gas. This formulation removes the inconsistency associated with negative contributions in kinematics far from the QE peak. The new definition allows extending the SuSAM* formalism to include MEC, which is the focus of chapter 3. In chapter 3, a scaling analysis of 12C data is performed incorporating MEC explicitly at the single nucleon level, leading to a new phenomenological scaling function. Using this model, the effect of MEC on EM responses is studied and compared with the relativistic Fermi gas (RFG) and the relativistic mean field (RMF) models. Chapter 4 presents a detailed analysis of the interference between 1b2b in the RT. It is shown that this interference is always negative within the independent-particle approximation. Chapter 5 extends the study of MEC to the CCQE neutrino scattering within the RFG, RMF, and SuSAM* frameworks. It is found that OB-MEC interference reduces both the RT and the neutrino cross section. Chapter 6 addresses SRC in the wave function of a nucleon pair in nuclear matter. The BG equation is solved using the NN potential developed by the Granada group, allowing for the calculation of high-momentum components. Finally, chapter 7 combined the effect of MEC and SRC, which extends the FG by including the high-momentum components of nucleon pairs affected by MEC. It is found that SRC enhance the RT, in contrast with what is observed in uncorrelated models.

## Key claim
The thesis demonstrates that meson-exchange currents (MEC) in the one-particle-one-hole (1p1h) channel produce a negative interference with one-body currents that reduces the transverse response and neutrino cross section, contrary to the transverse enhancement observed in experimental data; this negative interference is shown to be universal across independent-particle models, while short-range correlations (SRC) can reverse this effect and produce an enhancement.

## Method
The SuSAM* superscaling model was improved by replacing the sharp Fermi gas momentum distribution with a smeared Fermi-type distribution to average the single-nucleon hadronic tensor, eliminating unphysical negative contributions in kinematics far from the quasielastic peak. The Bethe-Goldstone equation was solved in nuclear matter using the coarse-grained Granada 2013 nucleon-nucleon potential with the angular-average approximation for the Pauli-blocking operator, allowing calculation of correlated wave functions for nucleon pairs with non-zero center-of-mass momentum. These correlated wave functions were then incorporated into the Fermi gas response formalism to evaluate the combined effect of MEC and SRC on the transverse electromagnetic response.

## Key numbers
- The effective nucleon mass in the RMF model is defined as \(m_N^* = m_N - g_s\phi_0 = M^* m_N\), where \(M^*\) is the ratio of effective to free nucleon mass
- The lower limit \(\epsilon_0\) of the integral in the response function corresponds to the minimum energy for an initial nucleon absorbing energy \(\omega\) and momentum \(q\)
- The 2p-2h MEC contribution typically accounts for about 15-20% of the total cross section depending on kinematics and model (Meg16b, Ben15, Gra13, Sob20, Meg17, Gon23)
- The RFG model uses a sharp Fermi surface with momentum distribution \(n(\epsilon) = \theta(\epsilon_F - \epsilon)\)

## Context
This thesis extends the superscaling approach (SuSA, SuSA-v2, SuSAM*) developed by Amaro et al. (2005, 2016, 2018) by improving the single-nucleon averaging procedure and incorporating MEC into the 1p1h channel for the first time. It contradicts the assumption that MEC alone explain the transverse enhancement observed in electron scattering data (Jourdan 1996, Bodek and Christy 2022), instead showing that SRC are necessary to produce this enhancement, consistent with the correlated basis function calculations of Fabrocini (1997) and ab initio Green's Function Monte Carlo results for light nuclei (Carlson et al. 2002, Lovato et al. 2016). The work enables future systematic inclusion of both MEC and SRC in neutrino-nucleus cross-section models for oscillation experiments.
