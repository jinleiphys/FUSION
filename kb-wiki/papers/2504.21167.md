# Quarkonium suppression in strongly coupled plasmas

> Suppression of open heavy quarks and quarkonia in heavy-ion collisions are among the most informative probes of quark-gluon plasma (QGP). Interpreting the full wealth of data obtained from the collision events requires a precise theoretical understanding of the evolution of heavy quarks and quarkonia as they propagate through a strongly coupled plasma. Such calculations require the evaluation of a gauge-invariant correlator of chromoelectric fields. This chromoelectric correlator encodes all the characteristics of QGP that the dissociation and recombination dynamics of quarkonium are sensitive to, which is to say can in principle measure. We review its distinctive qualitative features at weak coupling in QCD up to next-to-leading order and at strong coupling in $\mathcal{N}=4$ SYM using the AdS/CFT correspondence, as well as its formulation in Euclidean QCD. Furthermore, we report on recent progress in applying our results to the calculation of the final quarkonium abundances after propagating through a cooling droplet of QGP, which illustrates how we may learn about QGP from quarkonium measurements. We devote special attention to how the presence of a strongly coupled plasma modifies the transport description of quarkonium, in comparison to approaches that rely on weak coupling approximations to describe quarkonium dissociation and recombination.

# Quarkonium suppression in strongly coupled plasmas

## Key claim

The paper argues that non-Markovian (memory) effects may dominate quarkonium transport in strongly coupled QGP, because the leading-order Markovian terms that both the quantum optical and quantum Brownian motion limits rely on may vanish in a strongly coupled plasma, as suggested by calculations in strongly coupled N=4 SYM. This means that existing phenomenological approaches that assume Markovian dynamics may not be quantitatively controlled even when the small parameter rT is satisfied.

## Method

The authors review calculations of the gauge-invariant chromoelectric correlator (Generalized Gluon Distribution, GGD) at weak coupling in QCD up to next-to-leading order and at strong coupling in N=4 SYM using the AdS/CFT correspondence. They compare the spectral functions from these calculations and use time-dependent perturbation theory within potential nonrelativistic QCD (pNRQCD) to compute the Upsilon(1S) formation probability from initially unbound color octet states, evolving the heavy quark-antiquark density matrix with different GGD inputs.

## Key numbers

- The spectral function comparison is shown for |omega|/T up to 100, where perturbation theory is reliable
- The temperature profile used for regeneration probability calculations follows Bjorken flow scaling: T(tau) = (tau_f/tau)^(1/3) T_f, with T_f = 155 MeV, tau_i = 0.6 fm/c, and tau_f = 10 fm/c
- The coupling constant for the regeneration calculation is set to lambda = 13, corresponding to g approximately 2.1
- The regeneration probability for the strongly coupled correlator shows a dominant peak at initial separation sigma_0 approximately 0.13 fm, close to the size of bottomonium bound states
- The plateau in the weak-coupling regeneration probability begins at approximately sigma_0 = 0.15 fm, identified as the size of the Upsilon(1S) state

## Context

This work extends the program of applying open quantum systems and effective field theory techniques to quarkonium suppression, building on the pNRQCD framework of Brambilla (1999) and earlier work on the Generalized Gluon Distributions by Nijs (2023), Binder (2021), and Scheihing-Hitschfeld (2022). The paper contrasts the quantum Brownian motion limit of Brambilla (2016, 2017) and the quantum optical limit of Yao (2018) with the new finding that strongly coupled plasmas may suppress the Markovian terms these approaches rely on. The results point toward the need for non-Markovian transport formalisms and motivate lattice QCD calculations of the Euclidean version of the GGD, as formulated in Scheihing-Hitschfeld (2023), to determine whether non-Markovian effects are quantitatively important in QCD.
