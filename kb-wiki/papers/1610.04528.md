# Heavy-Quark QCD Exotica

> This review presents an overview of the remarkable progress in the field of heavy-quark exotic hadrons over the past 15 years. It seeks to be pedagogical rather than exhaustive, summarizing both the progress and specific results of experimental discoveries, and the variety of theoretical approaches designed to explain these new states.

## Key claim

This review presents a pedagogical overview of heavy-quark exotic hadrons discovered over the past 15 years, but explicitly states that no single theoretical picture has yet accommodated all experimental data on masses, production mechanisms, decay modes, and decay rates of these states, and that it is likely no single picture will suffice to explain all the new data.

## Method

The authors survey experimental discoveries of charmoniumlike and bottomoniumlike exotic hadrons (states labeled X, Y, Z, and P_c) and summarize the leading theoretical approaches used to explain them: hadronic molecules, hadrocharmonium, diquark models, hybrids, and kinematical effects. They organize the experimental findings semi-historically by physical process and provide detailed discussion of each theoretical picture's successes and shortcomings, including comparisons to lattice QCD predictions and quark-potential model calculations.

## Key numbers

- The X(3872) mass satisfies m_X(3872) - m_D*0 - m_D0 = +0.01 ± 0.18 MeV, indicating a state at least 10 times more weakly bound than a deuteron
- The X(3872) width is less than 1.2 MeV, while the lighter conventional χ_c1(1P) has width 840 keV
- The Z_c(3900)+ lies about 20 MeV above the nearest (D D̅*) threshold and dominantly decays through this mode
- The lightest charmonium hybrid multiplet is expected near 4180 MeV, with quantum numbers J^PC = (0,1,2)^(-+) and 1^(--)
- 14 c̅c and 17 b̅b states predicted by the quark-potential model have been observed
- The QCD scale Λ_QCD = O(200 MeV) is larger than m_u,d,s but much smaller than m_c,b

## Context

This review extends earlier reviews of heavy-quark exotics dating back to 2006 (Swanson 2006, Zhu 2007, Godfrey 2008, Drenska 2010, Pakhlova 2010, Yuan 2014, Esposito 2014, Olsen 2014, Chen 2016, Ali 2016) and reviews of heavy quarkonium that discuss exotics (Brambilla 2004, Eichten 2007, Klempt 2007, Voloshin 2007, Brambilla 2010, Bodwin 2013). It provides a comprehensive snapshot of the field as of 2016, enabling non-expert researchers to understand the discovery history, current status, and future prospects of heavy-quark exotic states, while emphasizing that many open questions remain about the nature of these states and that ongoing experiments (BESIII, LHCb, CMS) and future facilities (Belle II, JLab12, PANDA) will continue to test theoretical models.
