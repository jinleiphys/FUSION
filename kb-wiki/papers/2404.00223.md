# Decoding rotating neutron stars: Role of the symmetry energy slope

> In December 2023, the Fermi LAT Catalog announced the discovery of 33 new millisecond pulsars. Motivated by that, in this work, I study how different values of the symmetry energy slope $(L)$ affect the properties of static and slowly rotating neutron stars. For fixed values of angular velocity, I study how the slope influences the increase of the maximum mass, the radii of the canonical 1.4 solar mass, its eccentricity, as well the same quantities for the 2.01 $M_\odot$ stars. I show that different slope values cause different variations not only the absolute quantities but also in relative ones. Indeed, different slope values predict different values for the eccentricity, which does not depend on the absolute value of the neutron stars' radii. Therefore, this quantity can be a powerful tool to constrain the symmetry energy slope.

# Decoding rotating neutron stars: Role of the symmetry energy slope

## Key claim
The eccentricity of rotating neutron stars, which does not depend on the absolute value of the stellar radius, can serve as a powerful tool to constrain the symmetry energy slope (L), provided the angular velocity is known. Different slope values cause different variations not only in absolute quantities but also in relative ones, meaning that for the same angular velocity, a star will appear flatter if the slope is higher.

## Method
The author uses quantum hadrodynamics (QHD) with the traditional σ-ω-ρ mesons, extended with non-linear ω-ρ coupling (IUFSU model) to reduce the slope and with the scalar-isovector δ meson to increase the slope. Rotation is introduced via the Hartle-Thorne slowly-rotating formalism for four fixed angular velocities (1500, 3000, 4500, and 5000 s⁻¹) and four values of the symmetry energy slope (44, 76, 100, and 116 MeV). The Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations are solved for static stars, and the perturbed Einstein field equations are solved for rotating configurations, with the BPS+BBP equation of state used for the crust.

## Key numbers
- For L = 44 MeV, the equatorial radius of the canonical 1.4 M⊙ star increases from 12.58 km (static) to 13.72 km at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹, an increase of 1.14 km or 9.1%
- For L = 116 MeV, the equatorial radius of the canonical 1.4 M⊙ star increases from 14.30 km (static) to 16.51 km at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹, an increase of 2.21 km or 15%
- For L = 44 MeV at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹, the eccentricity of the canonical star is 0.51; for L = 116 MeV at the same angular velocity, the eccentricity is 0.61
- For L = 44 MeV, the maximum mass increases from 2.31 M⊙ (static) to 2.37 M⊙ at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹, a change of 2.6%; for L = 116 MeV, the maximum mass increases from 2.39 M⊙ to 2.47 M⊙, a change of 3.3%
- For the 2.01 M⊙ star at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹, the equatorial radius increases from 12.40 km to 13.07 km for L = 44 MeV (5.4%) and from 13.82 km to 15.03 km for L = 116 MeV (11%)
- The central density of the maximum mass star decreases from 0.939 fm⁻³ to 0.899 fm⁻³ for L = 44 MeV (4.3%) and from 0.872 fm⁻³ to 0.823 fm⁻³ for L = 116 MeV (5.6%) at Ω = 5000 s⁻¹

## Context
This work extends studies of the symmetry energy slope's effect on neutron star properties (Steiner 2014, Lattimer 2013, Lopes 2023) by incorporating rotation at realistic angular velocities close to those observed in millisecond pulsars, rather than only at the Keplerian mass-shedding limit. The study is motivated by the December 2023 Fermi LAT Catalog discovery of 33 new millisecond pulsars (Smith 2023), and it addresses the current tension between CREX results (0 < L < 51 MeV) and PREX II results (76 MeV < L < 165 MeV) as summarized by Tagami (2022). The finding that eccentricity depends on the slope independently of absolute radius values enables a new observational avenue to constrain L using the newly discovered millisecond pulsars.
