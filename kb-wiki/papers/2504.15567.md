# Study of neutron-deficient nucleus 224Np and its α decay by particle-number-conserving method in the framework of deformed shell model

> The particle-number-conserving (PNC) method in the framework of the deformed shell model (DSM) is employed to study the properties of the newly discovered short-lived neutron-deficient nucleus 224Np and its α-decay. The calculated energy of α-particle lies within 300 keV of the experimental data. This is the first application of the PNC method to the region of neutron-deficient nuclei. This work provides the first attempt to combine the microscopic PNC theory with empirical formulas to study the nuclear α decay. The configurations of ground states are assigned as π5/2-[523]{\otimes}ν5/2+[633] for 224Np, π1/2-[530]{\otimes}ν3/2+[642] for 220Pa, π3/2+[651]{\otimes}ν1/2-[501] for 216Ac, and π7/2-[514]{\otimes}ν3/2-[501] for 212Fr. The absence of the Z = 92 subshell closure in 224Np is explained by analyzing the proton single-particle levels. Low-lying excited statesare predicted for nuclei along the α-decay chain by the PNC method. Based on the PNC predicted α-decay energy and the assigned configurations, the α-decay half-lives are calculated by the empirical formulas, in which the angular momentum taken away by the α particle is taken into account. The angular momentum have an important effect on the α-decay half-life. The errors of the empirical formulas calculation are in two orders of magnitude with the experimental data.

## Key claim

The particle-number-conserving (PNC) method in the deformed shell model reproduces the alpha-decay energy of 224Np within 300 keV of experiment, but the empirical formulas for half-lives show errors of up to two orders of magnitude compared to experimental data, with angular momentum effects being critical for unfavored decays.

## Method

The authors diagonalized the deformed shell model Hamiltonian with monopole- and quadrupole-pairing in a large many-particle configuration space, using the particle-number-conserving (PNC) method to treat pairing exactly without number projection. They calculated alpha-decay energies from the difference between parent and daughter ground-state energies, assigned configurations via occupation probabilities, and then used these PNC results as input to three empirical formulas (Royer-type and Geiger-Nuttall-type) that include centrifugal and isospin terms to compute alpha-decay half-lives.

## Key numbers

- The calculated alpha-particle energy for 224Np lies within 300 keV of the experimental data.
- For the decay 224Np (ground state pi5/2-[523]⊗nu5/2+[633]) to 220Pa (pi1/2-[530]⊗nu3/2+[642]), the experimental Q_alpha is 9303.1 keV (Huang 2018) and the calculated Q_alpha is 9190.8 keV, with l_min=2 and log10(T_1/2) values of -3.25 (Eq. 4), -3.67 (Eq. 5), and -3.41 (Eq. 6) compared to the experimental -4.42 (Huang 2018).
- For the decay 220Pa (excited state pi3/2+[651]⊗nu1/2-[501]) to 216Ac (ground state pi3/2+[651]⊗nu1/2-[501]), the experimental Q_alpha is 9843.0 keV (Ma 2021) and the calculated Q_alpha is 9942.0 keV, with l_min=2 and log10(T_1/2) values of -5.79, -6.33, and -6.07 compared to the experimental -6.63 (Ma 2021).
- For the decay 216Ac (excited state 1-) to 212Fr (ground state 5+), the angular momentum taken away is l=5, and the calculated log10(T_1/2) values are -3.55, -5.25, and -3.35 compared to the experimental -3.46 (Ma 2021).
- The deformation parameters used for 224Np are epsilon_2=0.118, epsilon_4=-0.021, epsilon_6=0.004.

## Context

This work extends the PNC method, previously applied to well-deformed nuclei, to the neutron-deficient region for the first time, specifically studying the newly discovered odd-odd nucleus 224Np and its alpha-decay chain. It confirms the absence of the Z=92 subshell closure in 224Np, consistent with experimental findings from Sun (2017) on 223Np, and provides the first microscopic configuration assignments for these nuclei. The results enable future microscopic calculations of alpha-decay in heavy neutron-deficient nuclei and demonstrate that including angular momentum in empirical formulas is essential for unfavored decays, as shown by the large l=5 case in the 216Ac to 212Fr transition.
