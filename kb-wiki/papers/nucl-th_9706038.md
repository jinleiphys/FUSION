# The multifragmentation of spectator matter

> We present the first microscopic calculation of the spectator fragmentation observed in heavy ion reactions at relativistic energies which reproduces the slope of the kinetic energy spectra of the fragments as well as their multiplicity, both measured by the ALADIN collaboration. In the past both have been explained in thermal models, however with vastly different assumptions about the excitation energy and the density of the system. We show that both observables are dominated by dynamical processes and that the system does not pass a state of thermal equilibrium. These findings question the recent conjecture that in these collisions a phase transition of first order, similar to that between water and vapor, can be observed.

## Key claim

The authors present the first microscopic calculation (Quantum Molecular Dynamics with the Simulated Annealing Cluster Algorithm) that simultaneously reproduces both the fragment multiplicity and the slope of kinetic energy spectra measured by the ALADIN collaboration, showing that these observables are dominated by dynamical processes and that the system does not pass through a state of thermal equilibrium, thereby questioning the conjecture of a first-order phase transition in these collisions.

## Method

The authors used the Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) approach to simulate Au+Au collisions at 600 MeV/nucleon, with nucleons propagating on classical trajectories obtained from a variational solution of the n-body Schrodinger equation, including stochastic elastic and inelastic NN collisions. Fragment recognition was performed using the Simulated Annealing Cluster Algorithm (SACA), which searches for the configuration of nucleons and clusters with the largest total binding energy, applied at t = 60 fm/c when the size of the largest fragment reaches its minimum value. This contrasts with the earlier Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) method that required fragments to be well separated in coordinate space and could only be applied at much later times.

## Key numbers

- For Au+Au at 600 MeV/nucleon and impact parameter b = 8 fm, the density averaged over all nucleon centroids reaches a minimum at t = 50 fm/c, after which the heaviest fragment size re-increases due to reabsorption of intermediate mass fragments.
- The internal binding energy per nucleon of fragments at formation is about -3 MeV, compared to -6.85 MeV average binding energy of projectile nucleons initially.
- Using the Fermi gas formula with a = 0.15 MeV^-1, the apparent temperature extracted from internal excitation energy is T = 5.5 MeV, matching the value extracted from experimental isotope ratios.
- The variance of the rapidity distribution decreases with fragment mass roughly as sigma(y) proportional to 1/sqrt(A), yielding an apparent temperature of about 30 MeV from the kinetic energy spectra.
- The Goldhaber formula fit to ALADIN data yields a Fermi momentum of k_Fermi = 370 MeV/c, about 50% larger than that extracted from electron scattering.
- The initial Fermi momentum squared is <p^2>/3 = p_Fermi^2/5 = 12.5 x 10^-3 (GeV/c)^2, giving initial sigma(y) = 0.05 and sigma(p_x) = 0.0469 GeV/c.

## Context

This work extends earlier QMD calculations that had failed to reproduce ALADIN data (Begemann-Blaich 1993) by introducing the SACA fragment recognition algorithm (Puri 1996), which allows fragment identification at early times. The results contradict thermal models by Botvina (1995) that assumed thermal equilibrium and vastly different excitation energies, and challenge the phase transition conjecture of Pochodzalla (1995). The findings show that the Goldhaber model (Goldhaber 1974) provides a better physical picture than thermal equilibrium assumptions, though the interaction between projectile and target increases the momentum distribution width beyond the Goldhaber prediction. The work enables future investigation of whether the observed energy independence of fragment multiplicity is reproduced.
