# Rotating nuclei: from ground state to the extremes of spin and deformation

> The rotating nuclei represent one of most interesting subjects for theoretical and experimental studies. They open a new dimension of nuclear landscape, namely, spin direction. Contrary to the majority of nuclear systems, their properties sensitively depend on time-odd mean fields and currents in density functional theories. Moreover, they show a considerable interplay of collective and single-particle degrees of freedom. In this chapter, I discuss the basic features of the description of rotating nuclei in one-dimensional cranking approximation of covariant density functional theory. The successes of this approach to the description of rotating nuclei at low spin in pairing regime and at high spin in unpaired regime in wide range of deformations (from normal to hyperdeformation) are illustrated. I also discuss the recent progress and open questions in our understanding of the role of proton-neutron pairing in rotating nuclei at $N\approx Z$, the physics of band termination and other phenomena in rotating nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that covariant density functional theory (CDFT) in the one-dimensional cranking approximation successfully describes rotating nuclei from low to high spin across a wide range of deformations, but the abstract's claim of success is qualified by the fact that time-odd mean fields (nuclear magnetism) are essential for reproducing experimental moments of inertia, and their impact depends sensitively on the underlying single-particle structure, rotational frequency, and configuration.

## Method

The author reviews the cranked relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (CRHB) and cranked relativistic mean field (CRMF) theories, which transform the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov equations to a frame rotating with constant frequency \(\Omega_x\) around the x-axis. The formalism includes time-odd mean fields arising from the Coriolis operator and from the space-like components of vector mesons (nuclear magnetism), with pairing correlations treated via the Gogny D1S force and approximate particle number projection using the Lipkin-Nogami method. Calculations are performed for rotational bands in various mass regions, comparing results obtained with and without nuclear magnetism (NM vs. WNM) to isolate its effects on moments of inertia, band crossing frequencies, and single-particle alignments.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear magnetism typically increases calculated kinematic moments of inertia of normal-deformed rotational bands in the rare-earth region by 10–30%.
- The contribution of nuclear magnetism to kinematic moments of inertia falls below 15% at rotational frequencies \(\Omega_x \sim 2.5\) MeV.
- For superdeformed bands in the \(A \sim 150\) mass region, the increase in kinematic moment of inertia due to nuclear magnetism is around 20%.
- For hyperdeformed bands in the \(Z = 40–58\) region, the increase is around 20–25% at typical observation frequencies.
- In the lowest superdeformed band of \(^{194}\)Pb, nuclear magnetism shifts the unpaired proton band crossing frequency by 120 keV, from 0.465 MeV (WNM) to 0.345 MeV (NM).
- In CRHB+LN calculations for the same band, nuclear magnetism shifts the paired neutron band crossing by 70 keV, from 0.485 MeV (WNM) to 0.415 MeV (NM), and places the paired proton band crossing at \(\Omega_x = 0.535\) MeV (NM) while only its beginning is seen without NM.
- The kinematic moment of inertia calculated with nuclear magnetism is typically within 5% of the rigid body value.

## Context

This review extends the pre-2005 studies of rotating nuclei in CDFT, which were overviewed by Vretenar (2005), by focusing on developments from 2005 onward. It builds on earlier work by Koepf (1989, 1990) and Konig (1993) that first demonstrated the importance of nuclear magnetism in cranking calculations, and it systematically quantifies the impact of time-odd mean fields on moments of inertia and band crossing properties across normal, superdeformed, and hyperdeformed regimes. The work enables future studies of proton-neutron pairing in rotating nuclei at \(N \approx Z\), band termination phenomena, and the exploration of rotating nuclei at the extremes of spin and deformation.
