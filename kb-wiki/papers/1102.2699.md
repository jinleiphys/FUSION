# Electromagnetic production of hypernuclei

> A formalism for the electromagnetic production of hypernuclei is developed where the cross section is written as a contraction between a leptonic tensor and a hadronic tensor. The hadronic tensor is written in a model-independent way by expanding it in terms of a set of five nuclear structure functions. These structure functions are calculated by assuming that the virtual photon interacts with only one bound nucleon. We use the most recent model for the elementary current operator which gives a good description of the experimental data for the corresponding elementary process. The bound state wave functions for the bound nucleon and hyperon are calculated within a relativistic mean-field model. We calculate the unpolarized triple differential cross section for the hypernuclear production process e + 12C --> e + K+ + 12_Lambda{B} as a function of the kaon scattering angle. The nuclear structure functions are calculated within a particle-hole model. The cross section displays a characteristic form of being large for small values of the kaon scattering angle with a smooth fall-off to zero with increasing angle. The shape of the cross section is essentially determined by the nuclear structure functions. In addition, it is found that for the unpolarized triple differential cross section one structure function is negligible over the entire range of the kaon scattering angle.

## Key claim

The unpolarized triple differential cross section for hypernuclear electroproduction can be written in a model-independent way as a contraction between a leptonic tensor and a hadronic tensor expanded in terms of four nuclear structure functions, but the actual calculation of those structure functions relies on the impulse approximation (virtual photon interacts with only one bound nucleon) and a specific isobar model for the elementary current operator.

## Method

The authors developed a formalism where the triple differential cross section for the reaction e + 12C → e + K+ + 12ΛB is expressed as the contraction of a leptonic tensor and a hadronic tensor, with the hadronic tensor expanded in terms of four nuclear structure functions (W1 through W4). The structure functions were calculated using a particle-hole model under the impulse approximation, employing bound state wave functions for the nucleon and hyperon from three different relativistic mean-field models (linear Walecka model, NL3 parameter set, and FSUGold parameter set), and using the elementary current operator from an isobar model that includes resonances S11(1650), P11(1710), P13(1720), D13(1895), K*(892), and K1(1270).

## Key numbers

- The elementary process e + p → e + K+ + Λ was calculated at W = √s = 1.84 GeV and kaon scattering angle θ1' = 0°, with results shown as a function of Q2 = -q2 (four-momentum transfer).
- The fine-structure constant used in the formalism is e2/4π = 1/137.
- The triple differential cross section for hypernuclear production e + 12C → e + K+ + 12ΛB was calculated as a function of the kaon scattering angle, displaying a characteristic form of being large for small kaon scattering angles with a smooth fall-off to zero with increasing angle.
- One of the four nuclear structure functions was found to be negligible over the entire range of the kaon scattering angle for the unpolarized triple differential cross section.

## Context

This work extends the formalism of Picklesimer (1986) and van der Ventel (2004, 2006) for writing the hadronic tensor in terms of structure functions, now applied specifically to hypernuclear electroproduction. It builds upon prior theoretical work on electromagnetic hypernuclei production by Hsiao (1983), Rosenthal (1987), Bennhold (1989, 1991), Lee (1999), Motoba (1994), Cohen (1985), and Sotona (1994), while incorporating the most recent elementary current operator model from Mart (1999) and three different relativistic mean-field models for nuclear structure. The formalism enables future Rosenbluth-type separations of the cross section and provides a framework for studying medium modifications of produced mesons and resonances contributing to the elementary amplitude.
