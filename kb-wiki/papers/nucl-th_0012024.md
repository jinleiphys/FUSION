# Solution of the Dyson equation for nucleons in the superfluid phase

> We investigate the role the interweaving of surface vibrations and nucleon motion has on Cooper pair formation in spherical superfluid nuclei. A quantitative calculation of the state-dependent pairing gap requires to go beyond the quasiparticle approximation, treating in detail the breaking of the single-particle strength and of the associated poles. This is done solving self-consistently the Dyson equation, including both a bare nucleon-nucleon interaction (which for simplicity we choose as a monopole-pairing force of constant matrix elements g) and an induced interaction arizing from the exchange of vibrations (calculated microscopically in QRPA) between pairs of nucleons moving in time reversal states. Both the normal and anomalous density Green functions are included, thus treating self-energy and pairing processes on equal footing. We apply the formalism to the superfluid nucleus 120Sn. Adjusting the value of g so as to reproduce, for levels close to the Fermi level, the empirical odd-even mass difference (Delta approx 1.4 MeV), it is found that the pairing gap receives about equal contributions from the monopole-pairing force and from the induced interaction. ## Key claim

The pairing gap in the superfluid nucleus 120Sn receives approximately equal contributions from the bare monopole-pairing force and from the phonon-induced interaction, but only when the Dyson equation is solved self-consistently including both normal and anomalous Green functions; the induced interaction alone accounts for only about half of the experimental pairing gap.

## Method

The authors solved the Nambu-Gorkov (Dyson) equation self-consistently for neutrons in 120Sn, including both a bare monopole-pairing force of constant matrix elements g and an induced interaction from the exchange of surface vibrations calculated microscopically in QRPA. The self-energy and pairing processes were treated on equal footing by including both the normal and anomalous density Green functions, and the equations were iterated until convergence of the poles and residues of the Green function. The calculation used a Woods-Saxon single-particle basis with effective mass m_k = 0.7 m and included phonon modes of multipolarities 2+, 3-, 4+, and 5-.

## Key numbers

- The experimental odd-even mass difference for 120Sn is Delta ≈ 1.4 MeV.
- With only the phonon-induced interaction, the average pairing gap in the valence shell is 0.54 MeV.
- With both the induced interaction and a monopole-pairing force of g = 0.166 MeV (≈ 20/A MeV), the average pairing gap in the valence shell is 1.46 MeV, close to the experimental value.
- To reproduce the experimental Delta using only the monopole-pairing force (no phonon coupling), a value of g ≈ 0.233 MeV (≈ 28/A MeV) is needed, 50% larger than in the full calculation.
- Self-energy processes reduce the contribution of the phonon-induced interaction to the pairing gap by approximately 20% compared to the value obtained without including the breaking of single-particle strength.
- The pairing correlation energy from the Dyson calculation is E_pair(pho) = -3.9 MeV, compared to E_pair(BCS+BH) = -4.6 MeV from the BCS+BH approximation.
- Using only the low-lying 2+ and 3- phonon modes gives an average pairing gap of 0.29 MeV, about 50% of the value obtained with all effective phonons.
- Differences between the Dyson calculation and the BCS+BH approximation for state-dependent gaps near the Fermi level reach up to 400 keV, with an average difference of about 200 keV in the valence shell.

## Context

This work extends the BCS+Bloch-Horowitz (BCS+BH) approximation of Barranco et al. (1999), which calculated phonon-induced pairing gaps but neglected self-energy effects and the energy dependence of the effective mass. The present self-consistent Dyson equation approach treats self-energy and pairing processes on equal footing, going beyond the quasiparticle approximation. The formalism, previously used in condensed matter physics for strong-coupling superconductors and in nuclear matter studies, is here applied to a finite nucleus. The results enable more realistic calculations of pairing correlations in nuclei and show that the induced interaction enhances the pairing gap in finite nuclei, in contrast to nuclear matter where polarization effects typically reduce the gap (Schulze et al., 1996).
