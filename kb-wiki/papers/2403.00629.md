# Pseudoscalar Mesons and Emergent Mass

> Despite its role in the continuing evolution of the Universe, only a small fraction of the mass of visible material can be attributed to the Higgs boson alone. The overwhelmingly dominant share may/should arise from the strong interactions that act in the heart of nuclear matter; namely, those described by quantum chromodynamics. This contribution describes how studying and explaining the attributes of pseudoscalar mesons can open an insightful window onto understanding the origin of mass in the Standard Model and how these insights inform our knowledge of hadron structure. The survey ranges over distribution amplitudes and functions, electromagnetic and gravitational form factors, light-front wave functions, and generalized parton distributions. Advances made using continuum Schwinger function methods and their relevance for experimental efforts are highlighted.

## Key claim

The overwhelming majority of visible mass in the Universe (≥98%) arises not from the Higgs boson but from strong interactions described by quantum chromodynamics (QCD) through a dynamical mechanism called emergent hadron mass (EHM); studying pseudoscalar mesons, particularly pions and kaons as Nature's most fundamental Nambu-Goldstone bosons, provides a uniquely insightful window into understanding both EHM and its interplay with Higgs-boson couplings.

## Method

The authors employ continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs), implemented via QCD's Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSEs), to compute the properties of pseudoscalar mesons. They solve the coupled system of quark propagator Dyson-Schwinger equations and meson Bethe-Salpeter equations using symmetry-preserving truncation schemes (rainbow-ladder and beyond), then project the resulting Poincaré-covariant Bethe-Salpeter wave functions onto the light-front to obtain distribution amplitudes, distribution functions, form factors, light-front wave functions, and generalized parton distributions.

## Key numbers

- The Higgs boson contributes only about 1–2% of visible mass; the remaining ≥98% owes to strong interactions (EHM)
- The proton mass is approximately 1 GeV, with about 94% from EHM, ~5% from constructive EHM+HB interference, and ~1% from the Higgs boson alone
- The proton size is approximately 0.8 fm
- Pion mass is approximately 0.15 times the proton mass; kaon mass is approximately 0.53 times the proton mass
- The ρ-meson mass is approximately 0.82 times the proton mass, approximately 5.6 times the pion mass
- The process-independent QCD coupling at zero momentum is α̂(k²=0) = 0.97(4)π, with just 4% uncertainty
- The hadron scale ζ_H is approximately 0.331 GeV, defining the boundary between soft and hard physics
- Gluon and chiral-limit quark mass functions both saturate in the infrared at approximately 400 MeV
- L=1 components provide approximately 20% of the canonical normalization in pion and kaon Bethe-Salpeter wave functions
- DA-weighted momentum fractions: π = 0.5, K = 0.48, D = 0.32, B = 0.19, and 0.12(1) for one infinitely heavy quark
- The η_s pseudoscalar meson mass is approximately 0.69 GeV, where its DA approximately equals the asymptotic form φ_asy(x) = 6x(1-x)

## Context

This work extends the continuum Schwinger function method program developed over the past decade by Roberts, Bashir, Binosi, and collaborators, which has successfully reproduced much of the hadron spectrum and numerous structural properties. The paper synthesizes results from multiple prior CSM studies (Chang 2013, Shi 2014, Binosi 2018, Ding 2015, Ding 2019, Cui 2020, Raya 2021) and compares with lattice QCD simulations (Zhang 2020). It enables a unified framework for interpreting experimental measurements at facilities such as Belle II, Jefferson Lab, and the Electron-Ion Collider, and establishes pseudoscalar mesons as the optimal laboratory for disentangling the two mass-generating mechanisms in the Standard Model.
