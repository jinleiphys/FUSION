# Quantum entanglement in the initial and final state of relativistic heavy ion collisions

> The possibility of quantum entanglement leading to a seemingly thermal distribution of the initial partonic state that maps to the final hadronic state in the evolution of the deconfined phase generated in relativistic heavy ion collisions is discussed in the context of early thermalization and final state particle distributions obtained in experiments at RHIC and the LHC.

# Quantum entanglement in the initial and final state of relativistic heavy ion collisions

## Key claim
Quantum entanglement in the initial partonic state of relativistic heavy ion collisions could explain both early thermalization and the thermal-like yields of final state hadrons, including composite objects such as light nuclei and hypernuclei, without requiring actual thermal equilibrium through partonic interactions. The entanglement entropy of the initial state may map directly onto the thermodynamic entropy of the final hadronic state, provided the system does not decohere during its dynamical evolution through the QCD crossover.

## Method
The author applies conformal field theory results from Calabrese and Cardy (2004) to relate entanglement entropy to a time-dependent temperature in a strongly coupled system, using the relation T = 1/(2πτ) where τ is proper time. The initial gluon-saturated state is treated as a maximally entangled system, and the entanglement entropy is propagated through the partonic phase using quenched lattice QCD calculations, with the assumption that parton-hadron duality preserves the entropy through hadronization. The final state particle multiplicities, particularly for light nuclei, are compared to thermal model fits and coalescence model predictions at RHIC and LHC energies.

## Key numbers
- The time-dependent temperature from entanglement is given by T = 1/(2πτ), where τ is proper time in fm/c
- The entanglement entropy is S(τ, Δη) = (c/3) ln((2τ/ε) sinh(Δη/2)) + const., where c is the central charge and ε is a cutoff
- Light nuclei binding energies are in the hundreds of keV range, while the thermalization temperature at freeze-out is around 150-160 MeV
- The local entanglement extent is given by L = τ Δη, where Δη is the rapidity interval

## Context
This work extends the application of quantum entanglement ideas from ultracold atom experiments (Kaufman et al.) and from elementary e⁺e⁻ and pp collisions (Kharzeev 2017, Berges 2017, Baker 2017) to heavy ion collisions at RHIC and LHC. It provides a microscopic explanation for the early finding by Siemens and Kapusta (1979) that entropy per baryon is fixed in the compressed fireball, and for the 'snowball in hell' interpretation by Braun-Munzinger and Stachel (1994). The approach offers an alternative to hydrodynamical models that lack a quantitative understanding of thermalization, and it predicts that chemical freeze-out temperature should become energy-independent at RHIC and LHC energies, following the Hagedorn temperature. The paper notes that recent ALICE measurements (Adam 2015) confirm thermal-like yields of light nuclei, but that coalescence models give similar predictions, so the interpretation is not unique.
