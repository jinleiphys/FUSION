# Extended Gibbs ensembles with flow

> A statistical treatment of finite unbound systems in the presence of collective motions is presented and applied to a classical Lennard-Jones Hamiltonian, numerically simulated through molecular dynamics. In the ideal gas limit, the flow dynamics can be exactly re-casted into effective time-dependent Lagrange parameters acting on a standard Gibbs ensemble with an extra total energy conservation constraint. Using this same ansatz for the low density freeze-out configurations of an interacting expanding system, we show that the presence of flow can have a sizeable effect on the microstate distribution.

## Key claim

The paper shows that for a finite unbound system with collective flow, the microstate distribution can be significantly altered even in the simplified approximation of non-interacting particles, because the collective energy component acts as a heat bath that allows extra configurational energy fluctuations within the total-energy-conserving dynamics. The authors caution that their ansatz is exact only for non-interacting particles or local interactions, and its quality may be doubtful when the collective velocity approaches the thermal velocity (e.g., \(v_F/v_{th} \approx 0.79\) in their example).

## Method

The authors treat a classical Lennard-Jones system of 147 particles using molecular dynamics simulations. They construct an "extended Gibbs ensemble" with a radial flow term by adding a radial momentum \(\vec{p}_r = m \tilde{h} r \vec{u}_r\) to each particle and imposing microcanonical sorting on the total energy including flow. The Hubble factor \(\tilde{h}\) is taken from the measured collective velocity of the same system freely expanding in vacuum. Microcanonical thermodynamics is accessed by sorting canonical ensembles (obtained via Andersen thermostat coupling) into total energy bins, and the resulting distributions with and without flow are compared at matched average thermal energy.

## Context

This work extends the information-theory formalism for including collective motion in statistical ensembles previously developed by the same authors (annals, 2006). It contrasts with standard approaches that assume full decoupling between intrinsic and collective motion (Bondorf, 1995; Shuryak, 2004) and with empirical treatments that found no modification of correlation properties (Dasgupta, 1992). The results suggest that bimodal signals of phase transitions, typically associated with canonical ensembles, can appear in microcanonical frameworks when flow is consistently included, potentially explaining experimental observations in nuclear multifragmentation (Pichon, 2006).
