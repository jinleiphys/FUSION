# Insights into Nucleon Resonances via Continuum Schwinger Function Methods

> The first baryon resonance was discovered in the early 1950s. The Roper resonance joined the collection ten years later. Today, many baryon resonances are known and more are being discovered. As baryons, these states are the most fundamental three-body systems in Nature. They must all be understood, not just the isolated ground state nucleon. This contribution sketches applications of continuum Schwinger function methods to the baryon resonance problem. Whilst spectroscopy is of value, particular emphasis is placed on resonance electroproduction because transition form factors extracted from electroproduction data provide a keen tool for revealing resonance structure.

# Insights into Nucleon Resonances via Continuum Schwinger Function Methods

## Key claim

Continuum Schwinger function methods (CSMs) using a quark + dynamical diquark picture reveal that nucleon resonances such as the Roper $N(1440)1/2^+$ and the $\Delta(1600)3/2^+$ are far more complex than simple radial or orbital excitations, possessing rich orbital angular momentum structure in their Poincaré-covariant wave functions; however, the paper hedges that while the $q(qq)$ framework delivers agreement with electroproduction data for $Q^2 \gtrsim 2 m_N^2$, the meson cloud materially affects long-wavelength characteristics and reduces the observed Roper mass by roughly 20%.

## Method

The authors employ a continuum Schwinger function method (CSM) approach using a quark + dynamical diquark ($q(qq)$) Faddeev equation to compute Poincaré-covariant wave functions for nucleon and $\Delta$ resonances. The bound-state equation describes dressed quarks and fully-interacting diquark correlations (isoscalar-scalar and isovector-axialvector) that bind into baryons through continual exchange of roles between spectator and diquark-participant quarks. Rest-frame angular momentum decompositions of the canonical normalisation are performed to reveal the $S$, $P$, $D$, and $F$-wave content of each resonance wave function.

## Key numbers

- Roper resonance $N(1440)1/2^+$: pole mass $\approx 1.37$ GeV, width $\approx 0.18$ GeV; lies about $0.4$ GeV above the ground-state nucleon $N(940)1/2^+$ and $0.15$ GeV below the first spin-half negative-parity state $N(1535)1/2^-$
- Quark core of the Roper has a charge radius 80% larger than the proton analogue; meson cloud reduces the observed Roper mass by roughly 20%
- For the proton, diquark component breakdown of canonical normalisation: SC$\otimes$SC (isoscalar-scalar), 60%; SC$\otimes$AV, 15%; AV$\otimes$AV, 25%
- For the Roper $N(1440)1/2^+$: SC$\otimes$SC, 67%; SC$\otimes$AV, 15%; AV$\otimes$AV, 18%
- For the $\Delta(1700)3/2^-$, vector diquarks contribute just 5% of the amplitude and their omission leaves the mass practically unchanged; adding partial waves beyond $P$-waves yields only a 4% increase in mass
- Zeros in zeroth Chebyshev moment projections of the $\Delta(1700)3/2^-$ wave function lie within the domain $\tfrac{1}{3}$ fm to $\tfrac{1}{2}$ fm

## Context

This work extends the continuum Schwinger function method treatment of baryons pioneered by Segovia et al. (2015) for the Roper resonance and Liu et al. (2022) for $\Delta$ resonances, using the refined $q(qq)$ framework of Cheng et al. (2025). The paper directly contradicts quark potential model interpretations (Hey, 1982) that label resonances as simple $n\,^{2s+1}\!\ell_J$ states, arguing that Poincaré covariance requires every hadron to contain orbital angular momentum and that no system is simply a radial excitation of another. The results enable future validation of alternative molecular interpretations (Leinweber, 2024) against electroproduction data, and the framework has already been used to predict $\gamma^\ast + p \to \Delta(1600)$ transition form factors that were confirmed by CLAS Collaboration analyses at Jefferson Lab (Mokeev, 2023).
