# Measuring the neutron star equation of state using X-ray timing

> One of the primary science goals of the next generation of hard X-ray timing instruments is to determine the equation of state of the matter at supranuclear densities inside neutron stars, by measuring the radius of neutron stars with different masses to accuracies of a few percent. Three main techniques can be used to achieve this goal. The first involves waveform modelling. The flux we observe from a hotspot on the neutron star surface offset from the rotational pole will be modulated by the star's rotation, giving rise to a pulsation. Information about mass and radius is encoded into the pulse profile via relativistic effects, and tight constraints on mass and radius can be obtained. The second technique involves characterising the spin distribution of accreting neutron stars. The most rapidly rotating stars provide a very clean constraint, since the mass-shedding limit is a function of mass and radius. However the overall spin distribution also provides a guide to the torque mechanisms in operation and the moment of inertia, both of which can depend sensitively on dense matter physics. The third technique is to search for quasi-periodic oscillations in X-ray flux associated with global seismic vibrations of magnetars (the most highly magnetized neutron stars), triggered by magnetic explosions. The vibrational frequencies depend on stellar parameters including the dense matter equation of state. We illustrate how these complementary X-ray timing techniques can be used to constrain the dense matter equation of state, and discuss the results that might be expected from a 10m$^2$ instrument. We also discuss how the results from such a facility would compare to other astronomical investigations of neutron star properties. [Modified for arXiv]

## Key claim

The paper argues that a suite of three complementary X-ray timing techniques (waveform modelling, spin distribution characterisation, and magnetar seismology) can determine the neutron star equation of state by measuring radii to a few percent accuracy, but the achievable precision depends on the specific instrument (a 10 m² hard X-ray telescope is discussed) and on resolving systematic uncertainties that have limited previous spectroscopic methods.

## Method

The authors review three timing-based techniques for constraining neutron star mass and radius. Waveform modelling uses relativistic pulse profile modulations from surface hotspots on accreting or rotation-powered pulsars. Spin distribution analysis uses the mass-shedding limit of the most rapidly rotating accreting neutron stars to provide a clean constraint on mass and radius. Magnetar asteroseismology searches for quasi-periodic oscillations in X-ray flux from global seismic vibrations triggered by magnetic explosions, whose frequencies depend on stellar parameters including the dense matter equation of state.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: ρ_sat = 2.8 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Neutron drip density separating inner from outer crust: ~4 × 10¹¹ g/cm³
- Density at which nuclei dissolve completely (crust-core boundary): ~2 × 10¹⁴ g/cm³
- Maximum observed neutron star masses: ≈2 M⊙ (from Demorest 2010 and Antoniadis 2013)
- Current model radii for masses above 1 M⊙: 8-15 km
- NICER effective area: 0.2 m² at 2 keV, dropping to 0.06 m² at 6 keV
- NICER expected radius accuracy: ≈2% if mass and surface pattern are known a priori
- LAXPC effective area: 0.8 m² in the 5-20 keV range
- Athena effective area requirement: 2 m² at 1 keV, dropping to 0.25 m² at 6 keV
- Perturbative QCD calculations performed at densities above 10 GeV/fm³ (~64 ρ_sat)
- Moment of inertia of PSR J0737-3039 expected to be determined to within 10% within 20 years, yielding radius constraint ~5%
- Advanced LIGO/VIRGO expected to achieve ~10% (1σ) uncertainty in radius for closest detected binaries

## Context

This paper extends the observational programme of constraining the neutron star equation of state beyond spectroscopic methods (quiescent neutron star atmospheres from Rutledge 1999, Heinke 2003, Guillot 2013; thermonuclear X-ray bursts from Ozel 2006, Guver 2010, Suleimanov 2011) and radio pulsar mass measurements (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013). It synthesises the science case developed for the Large Observatory for X-ray Timing (LOFT; Feroci 2012, 2014) and compares the expected results with those from NICER (Gendreau 2012), Athena (Nandra 2013), the Square Kilometer Array (Bourke 2015), and Advanced LIGO/VIRGO (LIGO 2015, Acernese 2015). The work enables cross-calibration of multiple independent techniques on the same neutron star sources to address systematic uncertainties.
