# Comment on the Neutrino-Mixing Interpretation of the GSI Time Anomaly

> It is shown that neutrino mixing cannot explain the GSI time anomaly, refuting recent claims in this direction. Addendum 1: Remarks on arXiv:0801.1465. Addendum 2: Quantum effects in GSI nuclear decay.

## Key claim

Neutrino mixing cannot explain the GSI time anomaly because decay rates and cross sections are given by the incoherent sum over channels corresponding to different massive neutrinos, contrary to the claims in Refs. [0801.2121] and [0801.3262] that attempted to interpret the oscillatory modulation as a coherent superposition of neutrino mass eigenstates.

## Method

The author re-examines the time-dependent perturbation theory calculation of electron capture decay used in the earlier works, showing that the amplitude they wrote as a sum over massive neutrino channels actually describes a final neutrino state that is a simple sum of mass eigenstates, not an electron neutrino state. By inserting the correct normalized electron neutrino state (which includes the mixing matrix elements and time-dependent amplitudes), the decay probability reduces to an incoherent sum of the squared amplitudes for each massive neutrino channel, eliminating any interference terms.

## Key numbers

- The GSI experiment observed oscillatory time modulation of electron-capture decay of 140Pr58+ and 142Pm60+ ions with periods T(140Pr58+) = 7.06(8) s and T(142Pm60+) = 7.10(22) s.
- For the quantum beat interpretation in Addendum 2, the energy splitting between two coherent states of the decaying ion needed to produce the observed period of about 7 s must be of order 10^(-16) eV.
- The ions in the ESR storage ring are monitored with a frequency of about 2 MHz.

## Context

This comment refutes the neutrino-mixing interpretation of the GSI time anomaly proposed in Refs. [0801.2121] and [0801.3262], and also addresses arguments from Ref. [0801.1465]. The author shows that the standard theory of neutrino oscillations (Bilenky 1978, Bilenky 1987, Giunti and Kim 2007) requires decay rates to be given by incoherent sums over massive neutrino channels, not coherent sums. In Addendum 2, the author acknowledges that quantum beats from interference between two coherent states of the decaying ion could explain the anomaly if the energy splitting is about 10^(-16) eV, but notes the difficulty in finding a mechanism producing such a small splitting.
