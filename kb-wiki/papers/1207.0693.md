# Neutrino-nucleus coherent scattering as a probe of neutron density distributions

> Neutrino-nucleus coherent elastic scattering provides a theoretically appealing way to measure the neutron part of nuclear form factors. Using an expansion of form factors into moments, we show that neutrinos from stopped pions can probe not only the second moment of the form factor (the neutron radius) but also the fourth moment. Using simple Monte Carlo techniques for argon, germanium, and xenon detectors of 3.5 tonnes, 1.5 tonnes, and 300 kg, respectively, we show that the neutron radii can be found with an uncertainty of a few percent when near a neutrino flux of $3\times10^{7}$ neutrinos/cm$^{2}$/s. If the normalization of the neutrino flux is known independently, one can determine the moments accurately enough to discriminate among the predictions of various nuclear energy functionals.

## Key claim

Neutrino-nucleus coherent elastic scattering from stopped-pion sources can probe not only the second moment (neutron radius) but also the fourth moment of the neutron density distribution, provided the neutrino flux normalization is known independently; with a flux uncertainty of ±10%, the neutron radii can still be determined to within a few percent for argon, germanium, and xenon detectors of specified masses.

## Method

The authors expanded the neutron form factor into a Taylor series in momentum transfer Q, expressing it as a sum of even moments of the neutron density distribution. They performed Monte Carlo simulations for 3.5 tonnes of argon, 1.5 tonnes of germanium, and 300 kg of xenon detectors placed near a stopped-pion neutrino source, generating recoil-energy spectra using the Skyrme functional SkM* as the "true" density, adding Gaussian statistical noise, and extracting the moments via chi-squared minimization. For germanium and xenon, which have multiple naturally occurring isotopes, the authors defined effective isotope-weighted moments to reduce the number of free parameters.

## Key numbers

- Neutron radius in argon can be determined with a 91% confidence region spanning approximately ±5% around the best value using a 3.5 tonne detector at a flux of 3×10⁷ neutrinos/cm²/s per flavor.
- For germanium, 1.5 tonnes are required to achieve similar precision on the effective neutron radius at the same flux.
- For xenon, 300 kg are sufficient for comparable precision at the same flux.
- A 10% change in the RMS neutron radius of argon produces a difference of approximately 780 events/tonne/year, or about 1.2% of the total event rate, concentrated near 30 keV recoil energy.
- In germanium, a 10% change in effective RMS radius yields approximately 8100 events/tonne/year (about 6% of total), concentrated near 15 keV.
- In xenon, a 10% change in effective RMS radius yields approximately 20200 events/tonne/year (about 8% of total), concentrated near 8 keV.
- The effective RMS neutron radius for germanium from the SkM* functional is 4.0495 fm (first definition) and 4.0505 fm (second definition), a difference of 0.02%.
- The effective RMS neutron radius for xenon from SkM* is 4.8664 fm (first definition) and 4.8668 fm (second definition), a difference of 0.009%.

## Context

This work extends the proposal of Amanik and McLaughlin (2009) for using neutrino-nucleus coherent scattering to probe neutron distributions, which considered a one-tonne argon detector with a simplified form factor and suggested 10% uncertainty. The present paper improves on that approach by using a moment expansion to access both the second and fourth moments of the neutron density, and by demonstrating that ton-scale detectors at existing or proposed spallation sources (Spallation Neutron Source, DAEδALUS, European Spallation Source) can discriminate among predictions of different Skyrme energy density functionals such as SkM*, SLy4, UNEDF0, and UNEDF1. This capability would complement parity-violating electron scattering measurements like PREX (Abrahamyan et al., 2012) and could constrain the symmetry energy and neutron star properties.
