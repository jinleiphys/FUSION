# Solution of multi-channel problems using MCAS for spectra and scattering cross sections

> A multi-channel algebraic scattering theory, to find solutions of coupled-channel scattering problems with interactions determined by collective models, has been structured to ensure that the Pauli principle is not violated. Positive (scattering) and negative (sub-threshold) solutions can be found to predict both the compound nucleus sub-threshold spectrum and all resonances due to coupled-channel effects that occur on a smooth energy varying background. The role of the Pauli principle is crucial in defining what interaction potentials are required to fit data. The theory also gives an algebraic form for the dynamic polarization potential which adds to the ground state interaction to define the optical potential that gives the same elastic scattering cross sections.

## Key claim

The multi-channel algebraic scattering (MCAS) theory provides solutions to coupled-channel scattering problems using collective-model interactions while ensuring the Pauli principle is not violated, but the method's success depends critically on using an orthogonalizing pseudo-potential (OPP) to eliminate spurious states that otherwise arise from Pauli-principle violations in collective-model prescriptions.

## Method

The MCAS method solves coupled Lippmann-Schwinger equations in momentum space by expanding the potential matrix elements in finite sums of energy-independent separable terms using Sturmian-state expansions. The Sturmians are obtained as solutions of coupled-channel Schrodinger equations for the chosen potential matrix, with an OPP method applied to enforce orthogonality to Pauli-blocked single-particle states. The S-matrix is then constructed from the T-matrix using the Sturmian basis, and resonances are identified by diagonalizing a complex-symmetric matrix and tracking its eigenvalues as functions of energy.

## Key numbers

- For the n+12C system, a simplified rotational model with quadrupole deformation beta2 = -0.52 and a Woods-Saxon potential of depth -49.92 MeV (radius 2.885 fm, diffuseness 0.63 fm) was used
- The OPP method uses lambda = 1000 MeV as a sufficient approximation to the infinite-limit requirement
- Three resonances were found in n+12C near 0.7, 2.1, and 3.3 MeV in calculations without the Pauli principle; applying the OPP reduced this to two resonances in the same energy range
- The 2+1 state threshold in 12C is at 4.4389 MeV, and the 0+2 state is at 7.6542 MeV
- For the p+6He system, the target states used were the 0+ ground state and two 2+ states at 1.78 MeV and 5.68 MeV
- A 3/2+ resonance centroid in n+12C was found at 3.0 MeV, with a second broader 3/2+ resonance at 3.4 MeV
- The dynamic polarization potential for 2.73 MeV neutrons on 12C was calculated for partial waves l = 0, 1, 2, and is purely real below the first inelastic threshold at 4.4389 MeV

## Context

This work extends the MCAS approach previously developed by Amos (2003) and applies it to nucleon scattering from zero-ground-state-spin targets, specifically n+12C and p+6He systems. The method improves upon coordinate-space coupled-channel codes such as ECIS97 (Raynal, 1994) by incorporating the Pauli principle through the OPP method, which was shown to be crucial by Canton (2005). The results enable prediction of both sub-threshold compound-nucleus spectra and all resonances in low-energy nucleon-nucleus scattering, and provide an algebraic construction of the dynamic polarization potential for use in optical-model calculations.
