# The Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method and its applications to perturbation induced reactions

> The LIT method has allowed ab initio calculations of electroweak cross sections in light nuclear systems. This review presents a description of the method from both a general and a more technical point of view, as well as a summary of the results obtained by its application. The remarkable features of the LIT approach, which make it particularly efficient in dealing with a general reaction involving continuum states, are underlined. Emphasis is given on the results obtained for electroweak cross sections of few--nucleon systems. Their implications for the present understanding of microscopic nuclear dynamics are discussed.

## Key claim

The Lorentz Integral Transform (LIT) method reduces the calculation of continuum-state reaction cross sections to a bound-state-like problem, avoiding the need to solve the many-body scattering problem with complicated asymptotic boundary conditions. The method's feasibility depends critically on choosing a Lorentzian kernel rather than Stieltjes or Laplace kernels, because the Lorentzian kernel produces a transform that can be inverted with sufficient accuracy even when the input transform has only moderate numerical precision.

## Method

The method computes an integral transform of the response function \( r(E) \) using a Lorentzian kernel \( K(\sigma_R,\sigma_I,E) = 1/[(E-E_0-\sigma_R)^2 + \sigma_I^2] \), where \( E_0 \) is the ground-state energy and \( \sigma_I \) is a nonzero halfwidth. This transform \( L(\sigma_R,\sigma_I) \) is evaluated by solving the inhomogeneous Schrödinger-like equation \( (\hat{H} - E_0 - \sigma_R - i\sigma_I)|\tilde{\Psi}\rangle = \hat{O}|\Psi_0\rangle \), whose solution \( |\tilde{\Psi}\rangle \) is localized and can be obtained with bound-state techniques such as expansions in hyperspherical harmonics. The response function \( r(E) \) is then recovered by numerically inverting the Lorentz integral transform.

## Key numbers

- The Lorentzian kernel is defined with halfwidth \( \sigma_I \neq 0 \) and centroid parameter \( \sigma_R \), where \( \sigma = E_0 + \sigma_R + i\sigma_I \).
- In a test on the deuteron, the Stieltjes kernel led to "quite inaccurate results" (Efros 1993), while the Lorentzian kernel in the analogous test (Efros 1994) gave "very accurate results".
- For the \( d(e,e'p)n \) cross section, the general and two-fragment breakup methods gave results differing by only about 1% (Lapiana 2000).

## Context

This review extends the original LIT idea introduced by Efros (1985, 1994), which itself was developed after the Stieltjes kernel approach (Efros 1985) proved unsatisfactory for realistic electromagnetic cross sections. The method avoids the difficulties of solving Faddeev-Yakubowski equations for systems with more than three or four nucleons, and it enables ab initio calculations of electroweak cross sections in light nuclei that can test whether two-body forces alone or three-body forces are required, and discriminate among phenomenological, boson-exchange, and effective field theory potentials.
