# Equation of State of Nuclear Matter at high baryon density

> A central issue in the theory of astrophysical compact objects and heavy ion reactions at intermediate and relativistic energies is the Nuclear Equation of State (EoS). On one hand, the large and expanding set of experimental and observational data is expected to constrain the behaviour of the nuclear EoS, especially at density above saturation, where it is directly linked to fundamental processes which can occur in dense matter. On the other hand, theoretical predictions for the EoS at high density can be challenged by the phenomenological findings. In this topical review paper we present the many-body theory of nuclear matter as developed along different years and with different methods. Only nucleonic degrees of freedom are considered. We compare the different methods at formal level, as well as the final EoS calculated within each one of the considered many-body schemes. The outcome of this analysis should help in restricting the uncertainty of the theoretical predictions for the nuclear EoS.

## Key claim

This topical review paper compares different many-body theoretical methods for calculating the nuclear Equation of State (EoS) at high baryon density, considering only nucleonic degrees of freedom, with the aim of restricting the uncertainty of theoretical predictions rather than providing a single definitive EoS.

## Method

The authors review and compare the Bethe-Brueckner-Goldstone (BBG) approach and the variational method (VM) for nuclear matter, presenting the formal structure of the perturbation expansion including the G-matrix formalism and the introduction of an auxiliary single-particle potential to improve convergence. They examine the hole-line expansion (cluster expansion) where diagrams are ordered according to the number of hole lines they contain, corresponding to n-body correlations, and compare the final EoS calculated within each many-body scheme.

## Key numbers

- Saturation Fermi momentum: k_F = 1.36 fm^{-1}
- Core radius parameter: c = 0.4 fm^{-1} (commonly adopted value for the repulsive core)
- The probability parameter p for two particles to be at distance less than the core radius is estimated as p ≈ (8/9π)(k_F c)^3, which is small at saturation density

## Context

This review extends the long history of microscopic nuclear matter theory, building on foundational work such as the Gell-Mann and Low theorem (Gell-Mann and Low 1951), Goldstone diagrams (Goldstone 1957), and the pedagogical review by Baldo (1999). The paper connects theoretical many-body predictions to constraints from astrophysical observations of compact objects and heavy ion reaction data, aiming to establish links between these two realms of nuclear phenomena.
