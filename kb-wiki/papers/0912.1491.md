# Gravitational Waves From Low Mass Neutron Stars

> Low mass neutron stars may be uniquely strong sources of gravitational waves (GW). The neutron star crust can support large deformations for low mass stars. This is because of the star's weaker gravity. We find maximum ellipticities $ε$ (fractional difference in moments of inertia) that are 1000 times larger, and maximum quadrupole moments $Q_{22}$ over 100 times larger, for low mass stars than for 1.4 $M_\odot$ neutron stars. Indeed, we calculate that the crust can support an $ε$ as large as 0.01 for a minimum mass neutron star. A 0.12 $M_\odot$ star, that is maximally strained and rotating at 100 Hz, will produce a characteristic gravitational wave strain of $h_0=2.1\times 10^{-24}$ at a distance of 1 kpc. The GW detector Advanced LIGO should be sensitive to such objects through out the Milky Way Galaxy.

# Gravitational Waves From Low Mass Neutron Stars

## Key claim
Low mass neutron stars (below about 0.6 solar masses) can support crustal deformations that produce gravitational wave strains up to 1000 times larger than those from conventional 1.4 solar mass neutron stars, but only if such stars can actually form, which is uncertain because hot protoneutron stars of low mass are not gravitationally bound.

## Method
The author calculates the maximum quadrupole moment Q22 and ellipticity epsilon that the solid neutron star crust can support as a function of stellar mass, using the Ushomirsky formalism with a breaking strain of 0.1 from molecular dynamics simulations. The stellar structure is computed using the Douchin and Haensel equation of state for the inner crust and core, and the Baym-Pethick-Sutherland equation of state for the outer crust, with the shear modulus taken from recent molecular dynamics results.

## Key numbers
- Maximum ellipticity epsilon as large as 0.005 for a minimum mass neutron star (0.094 solar masses)
- Maximum ellipticity epsilon for low mass stars is 1000 times larger than for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star
- Maximum quadrupole moment Q22 for low mass stars is over 100 times larger than for a 1.4 solar mass star
- A 0.12 solar mass star, maximally strained and rotating at 100 Hz, produces a characteristic gravitational wave strain h0 = 2.1 x 10^-24 at a distance of 1 kpc
- The 0.12 solar mass star has a radius of 29 km
- Maximum rotation frequency for a 0.12 solar mass star is approximately 70 Hz from extrapolation, or 100 Hz from explicit calculations by Haensel et al. using the SLy equation of state
- Minimum neutron star mass for the equation of state used is 0.094 solar masses

## Context
This work extends the formalism of Ushomirsky et al. (known for calculating maximum crust-supported quadrupole moments) by applying it to low mass neutron stars, where weaker gravity allows larger deformations. The author's previous molecular dynamics simulations (Horowitz, 2009) established that neutron star crust has a breaking strain of 0.1, which is used here. The paper enables searches by Advanced LIGO for continuous gravitational waves from low mass neutron stars throughout the Milky Way, and suggests that such searches could provide a "smoking gun" identification via a small observed moment of inertia, with implications for supernova mechanisms and nucleosynthesis.
