# Neutron superfluidity in strongly magnetic interiors of neutron stars and its effect on thermal evolution

> The possibility of a neutron m=2-superfluid in the interior of neutron stars is investigated. This pairing state is energetically favoured in strong magnetic fields ($H\sim 10^{16}-10^{17}$ G). Because of the node in the angular-dependent energy gap along the field direction the neutrino emissivity is only suppressed polynomially as function in $T/T_{c}$ instead of exponentially, as it is obtained for a nodeless pairing state. The effect of this pairing state on the thermal evolution of neutron stars is studied, and its outcome is compared with the evolution of ``normal'', i.e. nodeless, superfluid and non-superfluid neutron stars, and also with observations. We find that particularly the predicted surface temperatures of the enhanced cooling scenario considerably change and come into agreement with temperatures deduced from observational data within the hydrogen atmosphere model. Furthermore the surface temperature depends on the magnetic field strength as an additional parameter aside from the neutron star mass. The latter is however only operative in the case of the intermediate cooling scenario.

## Key claim

The paper investigates whether neutron stars with strong interior magnetic fields (H ~ 10^16-10^17 G) can host a neutron m=2 superfluid state with nodes in the energy gap, which suppresses neutrino emissivity only polynomially rather than exponentially; the authors find that this pairing state can bring predicted surface temperatures into agreement with observational data under the hydrogen atmosphere model, but only for specific cooling scenarios and magnetic field strengths.

## Method

The authors numerically solved the general relativistic equations of stellar structure and thermal evolution using an implicit finite difference scheme with a Newton-Raphson algorithm, incorporating neutrino emissivities from multiple processes (direct Urca, modified Urca, bremsstrahlung, pair breaking and formation) with suppression factors for both nodeless (m=0) and nodal (m=2) neutron superfluidity. They classified cooling scenarios into four classes (standard, intermediate, enhanced nucleon, enhanced hyperon) based on the dominant neutrino process, and combined these with four classes of neutron superfluidity (m=0, m=2 above critical field, m=2 transitioning to m=0, and weak or absent superfluidity) to produce 16 distinct cooling scenarios, plus a separate case where the critical field is exceeded only inside proton flux tubes (class 2γ).

## Key numbers

- Critical magnetic field strength for m=2 superfluidity: H_c(T=0) = 3 × 10^17 × (k_B T_c / 1 MeV) G, yielding H_c(T=0) ≈ 2 × 10^17 G for a maximum energy gap of Δ_max ≈ 4.5 MeV
- The m=2 gap function has nodes at θ = 0, π, with |Δ_{m=2}(θ)|^2 ∝ sin^2 θ, while the m=0 gap function has |Δ_{m=0}(θ)|^2 ∝ 1 + 3 cos^2 θ (nodeless)
- For the direct Urca process at T → 0, the suppression factor for m=0 is R_n^d ∝ v^5 e^{-v} (exponential), while for m=2 it is R_n^d ∝ v^{-2} (polynomial), where v = Δ(T)/(k_B T)
- Observed surface magnetic fields of neutron stars range from B = 1.7 × 10^8 G (PSR B1957+20) to 2.1 × 10^13 G (PSR B0154+61)
- The London penetration depth for proton flux tubes is λ ∼ 40-100 fm, with internal field H ∼ 10^14-10^16 G; the flux quantum Φ_0 ≈ 2.1 × 10^-7 G cm^2
- For class 2γ, the volume fraction γ = H_ex / H_tube, with H_tube ≈ 2 × 10^17 G; the authors studied γ = 10^-4 (corresponding to bulk field H = 2 × 10^13 G) and γ = 10^-2 (corresponding to H = 2 × 10^15 G)
- The second critical temperature for transition from m=2 to m=0 is T_{c,2} = T_c (1 - H / H_c(T=0))
- Surface temperatures of observed pulsars: PSR 0833-45 (Vela) has log T_s^∞ = 6.24 ± 0.03 K (blackbody) or 5.85 ± 0.09 K (magnetic H-atmosphere); PSR 0630+18 (Geminga) has log T_s^∞ = 5.75^{+0.05}_{-0.08} K (blackbody) or 5.42^{+0.12}_{-0.04} K (magnetic H-atmosphere)

## Context

This work extends prior studies of neutron star thermal evolution by Schaab (1995) and Voskresenskii (1986) that established the standard, intermediate, and enhanced cooling scenarios, and by Muzikar (1980) who showed that m=2 pairing is energetically favoured above a critical magnetic field. The authors compare their results with observational data from ROSAT and ASCA for five pulsars (PSRs 0002+62, 0833-45, 0656+14, 0630+18, and 1055-52) as compiled by Oegelman (1995), and find that the m=2 superfluid state can reconcile enhanced cooling scenarios with observed surface temperatures under the hydrogen atmosphere model, whereas previous work by Page (1995) and Schaab (1995) required reducing the superfluid critical temperature to achieve agreement. The paper enables future studies of how individual neutron star magnetic field strengths (which vary by five orders of magnitude across observed pulsars) could produce different surface temperatures even for stars of the same age and mass.
