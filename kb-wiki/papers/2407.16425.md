# COLOSS: Complex-scaled Optical and couLOmb Scattering Solver

> We introduce COLOSS, a program designed to address the scattering problem using a bound-state technique known as complex scaling. In this method, the oscillatory boundary conditions of the wave function are transformed into exponentially decaying ones, accommodating the long-range Coulomb interaction. The program implements the Woods-Saxon form of a realistic optical potential, with all potential parameters included in a well-designed input format for ease of use. This design offers users straightforward access to compute \(S\)-matrices and cross-sections of the scattering process. We provide thorough discussions on the precision of Lagrange functions and their benefits in evaluating matrix elements. Additionally, COLOSS incorporates two distinct rotation methods, making it adaptable to potentials without analytical expressions. Comparative results demonstrate that COLOSS achieves high accuracy when compared with the direct integration method, Numerov, underscoring its utility and effectiveness in scattering calculations.

## Key claim

COLOSS solves the two-body scattering problem with long-range Coulomb potentials and complex optical potentials by using complex scaling to transform oscillatory boundary conditions into exponentially decaying ones, but the method imposes a restriction on the rotation angle \(\theta\) such that \(\tan\theta < \pi a / R\) to avoid poles of the Woods-Saxon function in the complex plane.

## Method

The program implements the complex scaling method by rotating the radial coordinate \(r \rightarrow re^{i\theta}\), converting the scattered part of the wave function into a square-integrable form. It solves the resulting inhomogeneous complex-scaled Schrödinger equation using Lagrange-Laguerre basis functions expanded via either direct Hamiltonian rotation (for potentials with analytical forms) or backward rotation of the basis functions (for potentials given only numerically, such as from folding models). The scattering amplitude is then computed from the expansion coefficients, and differential cross sections are obtained by summing nuclear and Coulomb scattering amplitudes.

## Key numbers

- The Woods-Saxon potential exhibits poles at \(z_n = R + i(2n+1)\pi a\) for \(n = \pm 1, \pm 2, \ldots\), imposing the rotation angle restriction \(\tan\theta < \pi a / R\).
- The Lagrange-Laguerre basis functions of order \(N\) with parameter \(\alpha\) are defined with a scaling factor \(h_S\) (units of fm) mapping dimensionless variable \(x\) to radial coordinate \(r = h_S x\).
- The inner product of \(x\)-regularized Lagrange-Laguerre functions is given by \(N_{ij} = \delta_{ij} + (-1)^{i-j} / \sqrt{x_i x_j}\).
- Figure 2 demonstrates that the generalized Laguerre polynomial \(L_{40}^0(x)\) oscillates rapidly and its modulus increases exponentially as \(x\) grows, making root determination numerically challenging.

## Context

This work extends the complex scaling method, previously applied to resonance problems in atomic and molecular physics (Ho 1983, Moiseyev 1998) and nuclear physics for determining resonance half-lives, to handle scattering with long-range Coulomb forces. It builds on earlier adaptations of complex scaling for Coulomb scattering (PhysRevC.63.054313) and provides a practical computational tool that can handle both analytical optical potentials and numerically-defined potentials from folding models. The program enables straightforward calculation of S-matrices and cross sections for elastic scattering between nuclei, with validation against the direct integration Numerov method.
