# Barrier and internal wave contributions to the quantum probability density and flux in light heavy-ion elastic scattering

> We investigate the properties of the optical model wave function for light heavy-ion systems where absorption is incomplete, such as $α+ ^{40}$Ca and $α+ ^{16}$O around 30 MeV incident energy. Strong focusing effects are predicted to occur well inside the nucleus, where the probability density can reach values much higher than that of the incident wave. This focusing is shown to be correlated with the presence at back angles of a strong enhancement in the elastic cross section, the so-called ALAS (anomalous large angle scattering) phenomenon; this is substantiated by calculations of the quantum probability flux and of classical trajectories. To clarify this mechanism, we decompose the scattering wave function and the associated probability flux into their barrier and internal wave contributions within a fully quantal calculation. Finally, a calculation of the divergence of the quantum flux shows that when absorption is incomplete, the focal region gives a sizeable contribution to nonelastic processes.

# Barrier and internal wave contributions to the quantum probability density and flux in light heavy-ion elastic scattering

## Key claim

Strong focusing effects inside the nucleus, where the probability density can reach values much higher than that of the incident wave, are correlated with the presence of anomalous large angle scattering (ALAS) in light heavy-ion systems where absorption is incomplete, such as α+⁴⁰Ca and α+¹⁶O around 30 MeV incident energy; this focusing is shown to originate specifically from the internal wave component of the scattering wave function, not from the barrier wave component.

## Method

The authors performed optical model calculations of the full scattering wave function ψ(r) and the associated quantum probability flux j(r) for α-particle scattering from ⁴⁰Ca, ⁴⁴Ca, and ¹⁶O targets. They decomposed the wave function into barrier and internal wave contributions by artificially enhancing the absorptive potential in the nuclear interior to suppress the internal component, then subtracting the resulting barrier wave function from the full wave function to obtain the internal wave component. The divergence of the quantum flux was calculated to locate where non-elastic processes occur, and classical trajectories were computed using the real part of the optical potential for comparison.

## Key numbers

- For α+⁴⁰Ca at 29 MeV, the probability density at the focus reaches about 20 times the value of the incident wave; for α+⁴⁴Ca at the same energy, the peak at the focus is lower than that of the incident wave.
- The refractive index near the origin for the α+⁴⁰Ca system at 29 MeV is about 2.5, comparable to that of diamond for ordinary light.
- For α+¹⁶O scattering, the real potential depth at small distances ranges from about 160 MeV to 120 MeV as incident energy increases from 30 to 150 MeV; the refractive index varies from about 2.7 to 1.4 over the same energy range.
- The barrier wave contribution to the reaction cross section for α+⁴⁴Ca is 1382 mb, only 3.5% larger than the 1334 mb obtained for α+⁴⁰Ca.
- The internal wave contribution to the reaction cross section is 6.8 mb for α+⁴⁰Ca (about 0.5% of the total reaction cross section) and completely negligible at 0.04 mb for α+⁴⁴Ca.

## Context

This work extends the pioneering calculations of McCarthy (1959, 1962) and Amos (1966) on focusing effects in nucleon-nucleus scattering to light heavy-ion systems, and builds upon the semiclassical barrier-internal wave decomposition of the scattering amplitude introduced by Brink and Takigawa (1977). The authors use the quantal implementation of this decomposition developed by Allen and collaborators (1982), which allows extraction of barrier and internal wave components of the wave function itself rather than just the scattering amplitude. The results demonstrate that the focus region inside the nucleus gives a sizeable contribution to non-elastic processes when absorption is incomplete, and that the internal wave contribution dominates the backward-angle scattering responsible for ALAS, which had previously been established by Brink and Takigawa (1977) using semiclassical methods.
