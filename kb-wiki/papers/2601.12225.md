# Radiative strength functions from the energy-localized Brink-Axel hypothesis

> Radiative strength functions (RSFs) model the bulk electromagnetic response of highly-excited nuclei and are critical inputs for statistical reaction codes. In this paper, we present a definition of the RSF that is consistent with Hauser-Feshbach reaction codes and that can be efficiently computed with the shell model using the Lanczos strength-function (LSF) method. We introduce a variant of the shell model LSF method that exploits the energy-localized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which makes it possible to compute both electric and magnetic RSFs across all energies relevant to capture reactions. We verify agreement with the conventional definition of RSFs with benchmark calculations of $^{24}$Mg, then present novel results for $^{56}$Fe. For $^{56}$Fe we find that: (i) the M1 RSF shape evolves smoothly with excitation energy, consistent with the energy-localized Brinkl-Axel hypothesis, (ii) both M1 and E1 transitions contribute significantly to the radiative strength below the photo-absorption threshold, and (iii) within the sdpf model space, the strength below 3 MeV observed in Oslo-type experiments cannot be fully reproduced. These results pave the way for a coherent microscopic description of the RSFs and further motivate the use of energy-dependent RSFs in modern reaction codes.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that radiative strength functions (RSFs) can be efficiently computed using the Lanczos strength-function method by exploiting the energy-localized Brink-Axel hypothesis, which holds that the RSF shape evolves smoothly with the excitation energy of the de-excited level rather than being independent of it as the strong Brink-Axel hypothesis assumes.

## Method

The authors introduce a level-density-free (LDF) definition of the RSF that depends only on partial decay widths, making it compatible with Hauser-Feshbach reaction codes and amenable to shell-model calculations. They apply the Lanczos strength-function (LSF) method using interior eigenstates as pivot vectors to compute RSFs for individual de-excited levels, then average over a small number of nearby excited states to approximate the average RSF. Benchmark calculations are performed for 24Mg in the sd model space using the USDB interaction, followed by novel calculations for 56Fe in the pf-shell using the GX1A interaction.

## Key numbers

- For 24Mg: calculations used the lowest 1000 states generating 180,000 nonzero M1 transitions and the lowest 5000 states generating 4 million transitions, with a bin size of Delta E = 200 keV
- For 24Mg: the ground state RSF shows no strength below approximately 4 MeV (the photo-absorption threshold), while a highly-excited level at Ed = 17.05 MeV (d=239) shows maximum strength below 1 MeV
- For 24Mg: the ILSF method required approximately 1400 Lanczos iterations total (80 block iterations for 5 interior wave functions plus 200 iterations per level), compared to more than 18,000 iterations for converging 5000 levels in the full calculation
- For 56Fe: the full configuration interaction matrix dimension in the pf-shell is more than 500 million M-scheme states
- For 56Fe: within the sdpf model space, the strength below 3 MeV observed in Oslo-type experiments cannot be fully reproduced

## Context

This work extends the Lanczos strength-function method developed by Whitehead (1980), Bloom (1984), and Caurer (2005) for sum-rule strength functions to the computation of RSFs used in Hauser-Feshbach reaction codes. It builds on the energy-localized Brink-Axel hypothesis established by Johnson (2015) and Herrera (2022), which showed that total strength varies smoothly with excitation energy. The results address the long-standing challenge of computing low-energy electric dipole RSFs with the large-scale shell model, which has been impractical due to the need for extended model spaces and thousands of individual wave functions. The approach enables comparison with Oslo-method experimental data for 56Fe from Voinov (2004), Algin (2008), Larsen (2013), and Jingo (2018), and with the D1M+QRPA calculations of Goriely (2018), which require empirical correction terms to account for strength to excited states.
