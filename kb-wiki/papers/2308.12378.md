# Nuclear physics constraints from binary neutron star mergers in the Einstein Telescope era

> The next generation of ground-based gravitational-wave detectors, Einstein Telescope (ET) and Cosmic Explorer (CE), present a unique opportunity to put constraints on dense matter, among many other groundbreaking scientific goals. In a recent study the science case of ET was further strengthened, studying in particular the performances of different detector designs. In this paper we present a more detailed study of the nuclear physics section of that work. In particular, focusing on two different detector configurations (the single-site triangular-shaped design and a design consisting of two widely separated "L-shaped" interferometers), we study the detection prospects of binary neutron star (BNS) mergers, and how they can reshape our understanding of the underlying equation of state (EoS) of dense matter. We employ several state-of-the-art EoS models and state-of-the-art synthetic BNS merger catalogs, and we make use of the Fisher information formalism (FIM) to quantify statistical errors on the astrophysical parameters describing individual BNS events. To check the reliability of the FIM method, we further perform a full parameter estimation for a few simulated events. Based on the uncertainties on the tidal deformabilities associated to these events, we outline a mechanism to extract the underlying injected EoS using a recently developed meta-modelling approach within a Bayesian framework. Our results suggest that with $\gtrsim 500$ events with signal-to-noise ratio greater than $12$, we will be able to pin down very precisely the underlying EoS governing the neutron star matter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that with approximately 500 or more binary neutron star merger events detected at signal-to-noise ratios greater than 12 by Einstein Telescope, the underlying equation of state governing neutron star matter can be pinned down very precisely, though the analysis relies on the Fisher information matrix approximation which may underestimate uncertainties for some waveform models compared to full Bayesian parameter estimation.

## Method

The authors performed a population study using synthetic binary neutron star merger catalogs generated with the MOBSE population synthesis code, employing four different equation of state models (APR, RG(SLy2), GPPVA(NL3ωρ), and PCP(BSk24)) from the CompOSE database. They used the Fisher information matrix formalism implemented in the GWFAST code to forecast detection prospects and statistical uncertainties on tidal deformabilities for two Einstein Telescope configurations (triangular 10 km design and two L-shaped 15 km interferometers), then applied a Bayesian framework with a nuclear meta-modeling approach to extract the underlying equation of state from the simulated tidal deformability measurements.

## Key numbers

- Approximately 720,000 simulated binary neutron star sources per year in the synthetic catalogs
- Signal-to-noise ratio threshold of 12 for event detection, with the requirement of at least 500 events above this threshold for precise equation of state reconstruction
- Source masses sampled uniformly between 1.1 solar masses and the maximum mass for each equation of state, with maximum masses ranging from 2.05 solar masses to 2.75 solar masses across the four models
- Four selected test sources for Fisher versus full parameter estimation comparison, with signal-to-noise ratios ranging from 79.08 to 570.68 in the 2L configuration
- Luminosity distances for test sources of 100 Mpc, 360 Mpc, and 460 Mpc

## Context

This work extends the nuclear physics section of the Einstein Telescope science case study by Branchesi et al. (2023), providing a more detailed analysis of how different detector designs (triangular versus two L-shaped configurations) affect constraints on the neutron star equation of state. The meta-modeling approach builds on the density expansion formalism developed by Margueron et al. (2017) and enables incorporating nuclear physics priors while remaining computationally efficient compared to piecewise polytropes or Gaussian process-based methods. The results enable future comparisons between the scientific return of the two Einstein Telescope configurations and inform detector design decisions for the third-generation gravitational wave observatory.
