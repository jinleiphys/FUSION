# Lattice field theory for superconducting circuits

> Large superconducting quantum circuits have a number of important applications in quantum computing. Accurately predicting the performance of these devices from first principles is challenging, as it requires solving the many-body Schrödinger equation. This work introduces a new, general ab-initio method for analyzing large quantum circuits based on lattice field theory, a tool commonly applied in nuclear and particle physics. This method is competitive with state-of-the-art techniques such as tensor networks, but avoids introducing systematic errors due to truncation of the infinite-dimensional Hilbert space associated with superconducting phases. The approach is applied to fluxonium, a specific many-component superconducting qubit with favorable qualities for quantum computation. A systematic study of the influence of impedance on fluxonium is conducted that parallels previous experimental studies, and ground capacitance effects are explored. The qubit frequency and charge noise dephasing rate are extracted from statistical analyses of charge noise, where thousands of instantiations of charge disorder in the Josephson junction array of a fixed fluxonium qubit are explicitly averaged over at the microscopic level. This is difficult to achieve with any other existing method.

## Key claim

This work introduces a general ab-initio method for analyzing large superconducting quantum circuits by adapting lattice field theory techniques from nuclear and particle physics, which avoids systematic errors from Hilbert space truncation that affect tensor network approaches, and demonstrates the method on fluxonium qubits where it can reproduce previous results and explore many-body effects beyond the superinductance model.

## Method

The method formulates the circuit-QED Hamiltonian as a Euclidean path integral, Trotterizes the time evolution into discrete steps, and computes correlation functions via Monte Carlo sampling of the compact U(1) field variables associated with each superconducting phase. The many-body spectrum is extracted from fits to Euclidean-time separated correlation functions, with discretization errors controlled by performing calculations at multiple temporal lattice spacings and extrapolating to the continuum limit. Gate charges are incorporated via reweighting, allowing thousands of charge disorder instantiations to be averaged from a single Monte Carlo calculation.

## Key numbers

* Qubit frequency extracted for a fluxonium device with N=40 array junctions, C^b = 7.5 fF, E^b_J = 8.9 h GHz, array plasma frequency ℏω_pl = 12.5 h GHz, and reduced impedance z = 0.14, at external flux φ_ext = π
* Eleven equally-spaced gate charges between n_g = 0 and n_g = 1/2 were explored via reweighting from a single n_g = 0 calculation
* Statistical uncertainties estimated with 100 bootstrap resamplings of the Monte Carlo data
* Lattice spacing errors show quadratic dependence on Δt as expected from theory, with continuum extrapolation performed at three or more lattice spacings

## Context

This work extends the long history of path integral and Monte Carlo methods in quantum electrical circuits dating back to the 1960s (PismaZhETF.3.141, PhysRevB.3.762), which previously focused on bulk thermodynamic behavior of Josephson junction arrays, by enabling computation of the entire spectrum including qubit properties. The method is validated against tensor network calculations of fluxonium by Di Paolo et al. (2021), providing the first independent verification of those results, and extends beyond them by incorporating ground capacitance effects and performing explicit charge disorder averaging over thousands of instantiations. The approach enables systematic study of many-body effects such as coherent quantum phase slips that go beyond the superinductance model of Manucharyan et al. (2009), and can be applied to next-generation qubit designs including the 0-π, blochnium, bifluxon, gridium, and harmonium qubits.
