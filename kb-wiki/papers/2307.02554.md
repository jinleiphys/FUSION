# Viscous hydrodynamic evolution of neutron star merger accretion disks: a code comparison

> The accretion disk formed after a neutron star merger is an important contributor to the total ejecta from the merger, and hence to the kilonova and the $r$-process yields of each event. Axisymmetric viscous hydrodynamic simulations of these disks can capture thermal mass ejection due to neutrino absorption and in the advective phase -- after neutrino cooling has subsided -- and are thus likely to provide a lower-limit to the total disk ejecta relative to MHD evolution. Here we present a comparison between two viscous hydrodynamic codes that have been used extensively on this problem over the past decade: ALCAR and FLASH. We choose a representative setup with a black hole at the center, and vary the treatment of viscosity and neutrino transport. We find good overall agreement ($\sim 10\%$ level) in most quantities. The average outflow velocity is sensitive to the treatment of the nuclear binding energy of heavy nuclei, showing a larger variation than other quantities. We post-process trajectories from both codes with the same nuclear network, and explore the effects of code differences on nucleosynthesis yields, heating rates, and kilonova light curves. For the latter, we also assess the effect of varying the number of tracer particles in reconstructing the spatial abundance distribution for kilonova light curve production.

# Viscous Hydrodynamic Evolution of Neutron Star Merger Accretion Disks: A Code Comparison

## Key claim

Axisymmetric viscous hydrodynamic simulations of neutron star merger accretion disks using the ALCAR and FLASH codes show good overall agreement (approximately 10% level) in most quantities, but the average outflow velocity is sensitive to the treatment of nuclear binding energy of heavy nuclei beyond alpha particles, showing larger variation than other quantities. The authors find that viscous hydrodynamic simulations likely provide a lower limit to total disk ejecta relative to MHD evolution.

## Method

The authors compared two viscous hydrodynamic codes (ALCAR and FLASH) using a representative axisymmetric setup with a central black hole of 3 solar masses and spin parameter 0.8, starting from an equilibrium torus with mass 0.1 solar masses, constant initial electron fraction 0.1, and constant entropy of 8 k_B per baryon. They varied the Shakura-Sunyaev viscosity parameter (alpha = 0.03 and 0.06), the treatment of neutrino transport (multi-group M1 for ALCAR versus gray leakage plus lightbulb absorption for FLASH), and the equation of state (with and without a representative heavy nucleus, 54Mn, in nuclear statistical equilibrium). Tracer particles from both codes were post-processed with the same nuclear reaction network of 7362 nuclei to compare nucleosynthesis yields, heating rates, and kilonova light curves.

## Key numbers

- Total ejected mass at 10^9 cm for model A-full-a6: 1.87 x 10^-2 solar masses (grid measurement); for F-full-a6: 2.06 x 10^-2 solar masses; for Fh-full-a6 (with heavy nucleus): 2.26 x 10^-2 solar masses
- Average outflow velocity at 10^9 cm for model A-full-a6: 4.19 x 10^-2 c; for F-full-a6: 3.73 x 10^-2 c; for Fh-full-a6: 4.27 x 10^-2 c
- Average electron fraction at 5 GK for model A-full-a6: 0.256; for F-full-a6: 0.283; for Fh-full-a6: 0.279
- Average expansion time at 5 GK for model A-full-a6: 75 ms; for F-full-a6: 79 ms; for Fh-full-a6: 77 ms
- Additional nuclear binding energy released per nucleon when including 54Mn versus only alpha particles: approximately 1.4 MeV, corresponding to specific kinetic energy of 2.9 x 10^-3 c^2
- Neutrino luminosities between codes consistent to within 10-20% for electron-type neutrinos after approximately 3 ms; heavy-lepton luminosities show discrepancy up to a factor of approximately 2
- Boost in ejected mass of approximately 10% and boost in average outflow velocity of 10-20% when comparing FLASH models with and without heavy nucleus

## Context

This work provides the first quantitative code comparison between the two viscous hydrodynamic codes (ALCAR and FLASH) that have been used extensively over the past decade for neutron star merger accretion disk simulations. It extends previous work by Just (2015) and Fernandez (2013, 2014) and others who developed these simulation frameworks individually, and follows the tradition of code comparisons in the core-collapse supernova community (Messer 1998, Liebendoerfer 2005, O'Connor 2018) to estimate theoretical uncertainties. The results enable better quantification of systematic uncertainties in predictions of disk ejecta masses, r-process nucleosynthesis yields, and kilonova light curves, and motivate future work toward improving how nuclear physics and r-process heating are included in post-merger simulations.
