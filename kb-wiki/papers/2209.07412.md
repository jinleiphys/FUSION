# Snowmass 2021 Cosmic Frontier White Paper: The Dense Matter Equation of State and QCD Phase Transitions

> Our limited understanding of the physical properties of matter at ultra-high density, high proton/neutron number asymmetry, and low temperature is presently one of the major outstanding problems in physics. As matter in this extreme state is known to only exist stably in the cores of neutron stars (NSs), complementary measurements from electromagnetic and gravitational wave astrophysical observations of NSs, combined with terrestrial laboratory constraints and further theoretical investigations, hold the promise to provide important insight into the properties of matter in a region of the quantum chromodynamics phase space that is otherwise inaccessible. This multidisciplinary endeavor imposes the following requirements for facilities and resources in the upcoming decade and beyond: * A next generation of gravitational wave detectors to uncover more double NS and neutron star-black hole mergers; * Sensitive radio telescopes to find the most massive and fastest spinning NSs; * Large-area, high-time-resolution and/or high angular resolution X-ray telescopes to constrain the NS mass-radius relation; * Suitable laboratory facilities for nuclear physics experiments to constrain the dense matter equation of state; * Funding resources for theoretical studies of matter in this regime; * The availability of modern large-scale high performance computing infrastructure. The same facilities and resources would also enable significant advances in other high-profile fields of inquiry in modern physics such as the nature of dark matter, alternative theories of gravity, nucleon superfluidity and superconductivity, as well as an array of astrophysics, including but not limited to stellar evolution, nucleosynthesis, and primordial black holes.

## Key claim
This white paper argues that a multidisciplinary approach combining electromagnetic observations, gravitational wave detections, terrestrial nuclear experiments, and theoretical investigations can definitively answer how matter behaves in neutron star cores, but emphasizes that this will likely require the gradual elimination of possibilities through multiple advances rather than a single dramatic observation.

## Method
The paper reviews and synthesizes multiple complementary techniques for constraining the dense matter equation of state: radio pulsar timing for mass measurements via Shapiro delay; X-ray observations for mass-radius constraints; gravitational wave detections of neutron star mergers for tidal deformability measurements; laboratory nuclear experiments measuring symmetry energy parameters; and theoretical calculations including perturbative QCD and chiral effective field theory. The authors then identify required facilities and resources for the coming decade.

## Key numbers
- Nuclear saturation density: n_s = 0.16/fm^3 or ρ_s > 2.7 × 10^14 g cm^-3
- Neutron star central densities reach 5-10 n_s, several times nuclear saturation density
- The linear relation R/km = (8.76 ± 0.37) + (L/19.6 MeV) characterizes about 200 Skyrme-type nuclear interactions to 68% confidence
- Symmetry parameters constrained as S_V = 32 ± 2 MeV and L = 50 ± 10 MeV from binding energy fits and neutron matter theory
- This suggests R = 11.3 ± 0.7 km from the linear relation above
- The maximum neutron superfluidity critical temperature T_cn = (5-10) × 10^8 K and proton superconductivity T_cp > 2 × 10^9 K from Cassiopeia A observations
- The moment of inertia constraint for the 1.338 M_sun primary of PSR J0737-3039: I_1.338 = 64^(+65)_(-44) M_sun km^2
- This implies R_1.338 = 11.5^(+6.9)_(-6.3) km
- Perturbative QCD calculations become reliable at densities around and above n = 40 n_s
- The EOS-insensitive relation I = (0.87 ± 0.05)(GM/Rc^2)^(1/2) MR^2 is subject to causality and a 2 M_sun minimum maximum mass

## Context
This Snowmass 2021 white paper extends the community effort reviewed in Watts (2016) and builds on recent observational advances including gravitational wave detections of binary neutron star mergers, X-ray observations from NICER, and radio timing of massive pulsars. It outlines requirements for next-generation facilities including gravitational wave detectors, radio telescopes like the Square Kilometre Array, and X-ray telescopes, while noting that the same infrastructure would enable advances in dark matter studies, alternative gravity theories, and nucleon superfluidity research.
