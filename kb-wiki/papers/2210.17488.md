# Wavefunction matching for solving quantum many-body problems

> Ab initio calculations play an essential role in our fundamental understanding of quantum many-body systems across many subfields, from strongly correlated fermions to quantum chemistry and from atomic and molecular systems to nuclear physics. One of the primary challenges is to perform accurate calculations for systems where the interactions may be complicated and difficult for the chosen computational method to handle. Here we address the problem by introducing a new approach called wavefunction matching. Wavefunction matching transforms the interaction between particles so that the wavefunctions up to some finite range match that of an easily computable interaction. This allows for calculations of systems that would otherwise be impossible due to problems such as Monte Carlo sign cancellations. We apply the method to lattice Monte Carlo simulations of light nuclei, medium-mass nuclei, neutron matter, and nuclear matter. We use high-fidelity chiral effective field theory interactions and find good agreement with empirical data. These results are accompanied by new insights on the nuclear interactions that may help to resolve long-standing challenges in accurately reproducing nuclear binding energies, charge radii, and nuclear matter saturation in ab initio calculations.

## Key claim

Wavefunction matching transforms a realistic high-fidelity Hamiltonian into a new Hamiltonian whose short-range wavefunctions match those of an easily computable simple Hamiltonian, enabling perturbative calculations that evade Monte Carlo sign problems while keeping observable physics unchanged; the method is approximate because the unitary transformation is restricted to two-body channels up to a finite separation distance \(R\) and only maps the lowest eigenvector of the simple Hamiltonian to the lowest eigenvector of the realistic Hamiltonian in each channel.

## Method

The authors define a unitary transformation \(U\) acting on two-body states with separation distance less than a chosen range \(R\) (taken as 3.7 fm in the lattice calculations), such that the ground-state wavefunction of the transformed Hamiltonian \(H' = U^\dagger H U\) is proportional to the ground-state wavefunction of a simple Hamiltonian \(H^S\) for \(r < R\) and identical to the original wavefunction for \(r > R\). They then apply first-order perturbation theory in the difference \(H' - H^S\) to lattice Monte Carlo simulations of nuclei up to \(A=40\), neutron matter, and symmetric nuclear matter, using chiral effective field theory at N3LO for the realistic Hamiltonian and leading-order chiral interactions for the simple Hamiltonian.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy from wavefunction matching: 2.02 MeV, compared to 2.21 MeV for the true Hamiltonian and 2.22 MeV experimentally; without the method, first-order perturbation gives 0.68 MeV.
- Residual error per nucleon from first-order perturbation: approximately 0.1 MeV.
- Root-mean-square deviation for energy per nucleon drops from about 2 MeV to 0.4 MeV with one additional parameter, and further to 0.1 MeV with five additional parameters.
- Charge radii predictions for nuclei up to \(A=40\) yield an RMSD of about 0.03 fm.
- Lattice spacing used: \(a = 1.32\) fm.
- Range \(R\) for the unitary transformation: 3.7 fm; the difference between wavefunctions is appreciable at distances less than 1.9 fm.
- Saturation density for nuclear matter: \(\rho_0 = 0.16\) fm\(^{-3}\).

## Context

This work extends the line of ab initio nuclear structure calculations using chiral effective field theory (Epelbaum 2008, Machleidt 2011) and lattice Monte Carlo methods (Lee 2008, Lahde 2019), addressing the long-standing challenge of simultaneously reproducing nuclear binding energies, charge radii, and nuclear matter saturation. The method is qualitatively different from renormalization group softening approaches (Bogner 2001, Bogner 2006) and shares conceptual similarities with the Unitary Correlation Operator Method (Feldmeier 1997, Neff 2002, Roth 2010), though the operator-based transformations differ significantly. The results enable future calculations of heavier nuclei and nuclear matter with controlled systematic uncertainties, and the method is applicable to any quantum many-body system where a simple computable Hamiltonian exists.
