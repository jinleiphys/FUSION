# Decoupling of valence nucleons in configuration-interaction shell model approach

> The unusual ratio of the neutron and proton transition matrix elements, $M_n/M_p$, may indicate the exotic feature of decoupled neutron and proton density distributions. In this contribution we show that these transition matrix elements are not the same as the ones calculated within the configuration-interaction shell model approach.

## Key claim
The ratio of neutron to proton transition matrix elements, \(M_n/M_p\), often used to indicate decoupled neutron and proton distributions, is not the same as the ratio of the effective shell-model transition matrix elements \(\mathcal{M}_n/\mathcal{M}_p\); the two ratios differ by a factor that depends on the choice of effective charges.

## Method
The author derives the relationship between the experimentally extracted transition matrix elements \(M_p\) and \(M_n\) (obtained from \(B(E2)\) values and deformation lengths) and the effective shell-model transition matrix elements \(\mathcal{M}_p\) and \(\mathcal{M}_n\) (calculated within a configuration-interaction shell model). Using isospin symmetry for mirror nuclei, an expression for the neutron transition matrix element \(M_n\) in terms of the shell-model quantities is obtained, and the ratio \(M_n/M_p\) is then expressed as a function of the effective charges and the ratio \(\mathcal{M}_n/\mathcal{M}_p\).

## Key numbers
- For shell-model calculations in the \(psd\) shell, the ratio of effective transition matrix elements is \(\mathcal{M}_n/\mathcal{M}_p \sim 4\).
- With standard effective charges \(e^p_{eff} = 1.5e\) and \(e^n_{eff} = 0.5e\), the resulting ratio of the actual transition matrix elements is \(M_n/M_p \sim 2\), which differs from \(\mathcal{M}_n/\mathcal{M}_p\) by a factor of two.

## Context
This work clarifies a misinterpretation in earlier studies (e.g., Ele09, Sag04) that equated the shell-model effective matrix elements \(\mathcal{M}_p\) and \(\mathcal{M}_n\) directly with the experimental transition matrix elements \(M_p\) and \(M_n\). The derivation shows that the two sets of quantities are related through the effective charges, and that the ratio \(M_n/M_p\) extracted from shell-model calculations must be corrected accordingly. This enables more accurate comparisons between shell-model predictions and experimental data on neutron-rich nuclei, particularly for boron and carbon isotopes where decoupled neutron and proton distributions have been suggested (Ogawa03, Dom05, Im04, Ele04, Ele09).
