# Neutron elastic scattering on calcium isotopes from chiral nuclear optical potentials

> We formulate microscopic neutron-nucleus optical potentials from many-body perturbation theory based on chiral two- and three-body forces. The neutron self energy is first calculated in homogeneous matter to second order in perturbation theory, which gives the central real and imaginary terms of the optical potential. The real spin-orbit term is calculated separately from the density matrix expansion using the same chiral interaction as in the self energy. Finally, the full neutron-nucleus optical potential is derived within the improved local density approximation utilizing mean field models consistent with the chiral nuclear force employed. We compare the results of the microscopic calculations to phenomenological models and experimental data up to projectile energies of $E = 200$ MeV. Experimental elastic differential scattering cross sections and vector analyzing powers are generally well reproduced by the chiral optical potential, but we find that total cross sections are overestimated at high energies.

## Key claim

The microscopic chiral optical potential reproduces experimental elastic differential scattering cross sections and vector analyzing powers generally well, but total cross sections are overestimated at high energies; the authors note that the chiral expansion is expected to break down near 200 MeV and that the microscopic imaginary term becomes overly absorptive at high energy.

## Method

The neutron self-energy was first calculated in homogeneous nuclear matter to second order in many-body perturbation theory using chiral two- and three-body forces with a momentum-space cutoff of 450 MeV, yielding the central real and imaginary terms of the optical potential. The real spin-orbit term was calculated separately from the improved density matrix expansion using the same chiral interaction. The full neutron-nucleus optical potential was then derived within the improved local density approximation (ILDA) using nuclear density distributions from mean field theory with the Skχ450 Skyrme interaction, and the resulting potentials were parameterized to the Koning-Delaroche phenomenological form for implementation in the TALYS reaction code.

## Key numbers

- Projectile energies considered: 3.2 to 185 MeV for differential elastic scattering cross sections, up to 200 MeV for total cross sections
- ILDA range parameter for central term: varied over 1.25 fm < t_C < 1.35 fm to estimate theoretical uncertainty
- ILDA range parameter for spin-orbit term: varied over 1.0 fm < t_SO < 1.1 fm to estimate uncertainty
- Three-body forces reduce the central depth of the real part of the optical potential by approximately 12 MeV across all energies shown
- For energies below 45 MeV, three-body forces reduce the depth of the imaginary term; above 45 MeV, three-body contributions increase the depth by a growing amount as energy increases
- Differential elastic scattering cross sections computed for n-40Ca at 14 energies: 3.2, 5.3, 6.52, 11.9, 16.9, 21.7, 25.5, 30, 40, 65, 85, 107.5, 155, 185 MeV
- Differential elastic scattering cross sections computed for n-48Ca at 3 energies: 7.97, 11.9, 16.9 MeV
- Vector analyzing powers calculated for n-40Ca at 11.9 and 16.9 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (Whitehead, Lim, and Holt, 2019) that focused exclusively on proton elastic and total reaction cross sections, now addressing neutron scattering as a novel challenge. The microscopic approach based on chiral effective field theory provides an alternative to global phenomenological optical potentials such as Koning and Delaroche (2003), which are optimized to experimental data and may not reliably extrapolate to unexplored regions of the nuclear chart. The results enable future development of a microscopic global optical potential for nucleon-nucleus scattering across a large range of isotopes, including those off stability, in support of experiments at radioactive ion beam facilities and for applications such as neutron-capture cross sections in r-process nucleosynthesis.
