# On the time-dependent Aharonov-Bohm effect and the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem

> The time-dependent Aharonov-Bohm (AB) effect considers the situation in which the magnetic flux inside the solenoid changes time-dependently. Different from the standard AB-effect, the problem is unexpectedly subtle and not easy to solve without any doubt, which is the reason why it is still in a state of unsettlement even theoretically. The difficulty originates from the fact that its theoretical analysis requires line-integrals of the time-dependent vector potential along paths in the 4-dimensional Minkowski space. Owing to the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem, this closed line-integral of the vector potential can be related to the integral of the electric and magnetic fields over the 2-dimensional area, the boundary of which is given by the above-mentioned closed path. The central controversy concerns the success or failure of the claim by Singleton and collaborators based on the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem, which states that the time-dependent part of the AB-phase shift due to the magnetic vector potential is precisely cancelled by the effect of induced electric field generated by the time-variation of the magnetic flux. In the present paper, we carefully reanalyze their cancellation argument by going back to the basic quantum mechanical analysis of the charged particle motion under the presence of the time-dependent external electromagnetic potential combined with more careful treatment of the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem. Careful analysis of the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem shows that the cancellation argument by Singleton et al. is partially correct but their central claim that only the time-independent part of the magnetic field contributes to the AB-phase shift so that there is no time-dependent AB-effect is not justified, thereby supporting likely existence of the time-dependent AB-effect.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the cancellation argument by Singleton and collaborators, which claims that the time-dependent part of the Aharonov-Bohm phase shift due to the magnetic vector potential is precisely canceled by the induced electric field, is only partially correct; their central claim that no time-dependent AB-effect exists is not justified, thereby supporting the likely existence of the time-dependent AB-effect.

## Method

The author reanalyzes the time-dependent AB-effect by returning to the basic Schrodinger equation for a charged particle moving under a time-dependent external electromagnetic potential from an infinitely-long solenoid, combined with a careful treatment of the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem. The analysis parametrizes the space-time paths C1 and C2 as circular arcs around the solenoid and evaluates the surface integral of the field-strength tensor over the 2-dimensional surface bounded by the closed space-time path. The author also examines the gauge transformation properties and the retarded potential solution of the Maxwell equations in the Lorenz gauge to establish the correct expression for the AB-phase shift.

## Key numbers

* The magnetic field inside the solenoid is given as B(t) = B0 + B1 t, a linear function in time, derived from the condition that the time derivative of the induced electric field must vanish (E-dot = 0) based on the sourceless Maxwell equation in the region excluding the solenoid surface.
* The solenoid radius is denoted R, with the magnetic field distribution given as B(x,t) = B(t) e_z for rho < R and zero for rho >= R.
* The vector potential outside the solenoid (rho >= R) is A(x,t) = (R^2 B(t))/(2 rho) e_phi, and inside (rho < R) is A(x,t) = (rho B(t))/2 e_phi.
* The induced electric field is E(x,t) = -partial A/partial t, giving E = -(rho B-dot(t))/2 e_phi inside and E = -(R^2 B-dot(t))/(2 rho) e_phi outside the solenoid.

## Context

This work extends the ongoing theoretical controversy over the time-dependent Aharonov-Bohm effect, directly addressing the cancellation argument by Singleton and collaborators (2013, 2014, 2016) who claimed that only the time-independent part of the magnetic field contributes to the AB-phase shift. The paper also engages with the work of Jing and collaborators (2017), who argued for the existence of the time-dependent AB-effect but lacked a convincing explanation of why the Singleton argument fails. The analysis enables a more rigorous foundation for predicting the time-dependent AB-phase shift and clarifies the conditions under which the 4-dimensional Stokes theorem can be correctly applied to this problem.
