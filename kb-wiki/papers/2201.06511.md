# Gravitational search for near Earth black holes or other compact dark objects

> Primordial black holes, with masses comparable to asteroids, are an attractive possibility for dark matter. In addition, other forms of dark matter could form compact dark objects (CDO). We search for small tidal accelerations from low mass black holes or CDOs orbiting near the Earth, and find none. Using about 10 years of data from the superconducting gravimeters in the Black Forest Observatory in South-Western Germany and at Djougou, Northern Benin in Western Africa we set an upper limit on the maximum mass of any dark object orbiting the Earth as a function of orbital radius. For semi-major axis less than two earth radii we exclude all black holes or CDOs with masses larger than 6.7x10^{13} kg. Lower mass primordial black holes may be strongly constrained by Hawking radiation. We conclude that near Earth black holes are extremely unlikely.

# Gravitational search for near Earth black holes or other compact dark objects

## Key claim
The authors search for tidal accelerations from low-mass primordial black holes (PBH) or compact dark objects (CDO) in near-Earth orbits using superconducting gravimeter data, and find no signal. They conclude that near-Earth black holes are extremely unlikely, but the search is limited to objects with masses above approximately 6.7×10¹³ kg for orbits with semi-major axis less than two Earth radii; lower-mass PBH remain constrained primarily by Hawking radiation rather than by this gravitational search.

## Method
The authors numerically integrate orbital trajectories for dark objects using the Velocity Verlet algorithm with a 1-second time step, computing the time-dependent vertical tidal acceleration at a gravimeter location from Eq. (2). They compare Fourier amplitude spectra of these computed signals to the 10σ upper-limit noise curves derived from 11.1 years of superconducting gravimeter data from the Black Forest Observatory (BFO, Germany) and 7.9 years from Djougou (Benin), after subtracting synthetic tidal models and correcting for atmospheric pressure fluctuations.

## Key numbers
- For semi-major axis a = 2 R_E, the excluded mass is M_ex = 6.7×10¹³ kg, ruling out all dark objects with masses above this value for orbits within two Earth radii.
- For a circular equatorial orbit at a = 3 R_E observed with the Djougou gravimeter, M_ex = 4.0×10¹⁴ kg; for the BFO gravimeter at the same orbit, M_ex = 5.0×10¹⁴ kg.
- For a circular equatorial orbit at a = 6 R_E observed with Djougou, M_ex = 3.7×10¹⁶ kg.
- For an elliptical orbit with a = 6.05 R_E and eccentricity ε = 0.1 observed with Djougou, M_ex = 2.7×10¹⁶ kg.
- The approximate fit to the upper limit across all inclinations and eccentricities is M_ex ≈ 2.1×10¹² (a/R_E)⁵ kg, valid for a between approximately 1 and 4.5 R_E.
- The BFO noise limit is fit as A_BFO(f) ≈ 1.25×10⁻⁴ (1 Hz / f)^(1.1833) pm/s² for 10⁻⁶ Hz < f < 2×10⁻⁴ Hz.
- The Djougou noise limit is fit as A_DJ(f) ≈ 4.71×10⁻³ (1 Hz / f)^(0.909) pm/s² for the same frequency range.

## Context
This work significantly extends the earlier search by the same group (PhysRevLett.124.051102, 2020) for CDOs moving inside the Earth, now covering orbits partially or completely outside the Earth with longer orbital periods. The results complement constraints from Hawking radiation (PhysRevLett.126.171101, 2021) for PBH below 10¹⁴ kg and microlensing surveys (Alcock et al., 2000; Tisserand et al., 2006) for PBH above 10¹⁹ kg, leaving the window between 10¹⁴ and 10¹⁹ kg partially constrained by this work for near-Earth orbits. The method enables future coherent searches using multiple gravimeters and could be extended to other solar system bodies such as the Moon, where atmospheric noise would be absent.
