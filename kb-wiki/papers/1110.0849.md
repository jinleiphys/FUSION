# On the Phase Structure of QCD in a Finite Volume

> The chiral phase transition in QCD at finite chemical potential and temperature can be characterized for small chemical potential by its curvature and the transition temperature. The curvature is accessible to QCD lattice simulations, which are always performed at finite pion masses and in finite simulation volumes. We investigate the effect of a finite volume on the curvature of the chiral phase transition line. We use functional renormalization group methods with a two flavor quark-meson model to obtain the effective action in a finite volume, including both quark and meson fluctuation effects. Depending on the chosen boundary conditions and the pion mass, we find pronounced finite-volume effects. For periodic quark boundary conditions in spatial directions, we observe a decrease in the curvature in intermediate volume sizes, which we interpret in terms of finite-volume quark effects. Our results have implications for the phase structure of QCD in a finite volume, where the location of a possible critical endpoint might be shifted compared to the infinite-volume case.

# On the Phase Structure of QCD in a Finite Volume

## Key claim
The curvature of the chiral phase transition line in QCD at finite chemical potential and temperature exhibits a pronounced dependence on the spatial volume size, with a decrease in curvature observed for intermediate volume sizes when periodic boundary conditions for quarks are used. This finite-volume effect is most significant for small pion masses and may shift the location of a possible critical endpoint compared to the infinite-volume case.

## Method
The authors employed functional renormalization group (RG) methods using a two-flavor quark-meson model to compute the effective action in a finite cubic volume of side length L at finite temperature T and quark chemical potential μ. They solved the Wetterich flow equation for the effective mesonic potential expanded in a Taylor series to order φ⁴ (Nmax=2) around its scale-dependent minimum, using optimized regulator functions and discretizing spatial momentum integrals to account for finite-volume effects with either periodic or antiperiodic boundary conditions for the quark fields.

## Key numbers
- For a pion mass of 100 MeV, the curvature κ deviates almost 20% at its minimum (L ≃ 3 fm) from its infinite-volume limit value of κ(L→∞)=1.357(18).
- For a pion mass of 138 MeV (near-physical value), the curvature deviates on the order of 10% from its infinite-volume limit value of κ(L→∞)=1.375(63).
- For a pion mass of 200 MeV, the curvature deviates about 2% at its minimum (L ≃ 3 fm) from its infinite-volume limit value of κ(L→∞)=1.409(59).
- For pion masses above 300 MeV, the deviation of the curvature from its infinite-volume limit is less than 1% for L ≳ 2 fm.
- The UV cutoff was set to Λ = 1.5 GeV, with a constant Yukawa coupling g = 3.258.
- The critical volume size Lc (location of the minimum in Δκ) depends on the pion mass, with Lc becoming smaller for larger mπ⁽⁰⁾.

## Context
This work extends previous functional RG studies of the quark-meson model in a finite volume by Braun (2005) and Braun (2005), which examined finite-volume effects on the chiral condensate and transition temperature, to the curvature of the phase transition line at finite chemical potential. The results are consistent with earlier proper-time renormalization group (PTRG) calculations by Klein (2010). The findings have implications for interpreting lattice QCD simulations, which are always performed in finite volumes, and suggest that extrapolation to infinite volume is safe for large pion masses but requires more elaborate methods for physical pion masses. The results also indicate that the location of a possible critical endpoint in the QCD phase diagram may be shifted in finite volumes compared to infinite-volume calculations such as those by Schaefer (2004).
