# Dynamics of vortices in chiral media: the chiral propulsion effect

> We study the motion of vortex filaments in chiral media, and find a semi-classical analog of the anomaly-induced chiral magnetic effect. The helical solitonic excitations on vortices in a parity-breaking medium are found to carry an additional energy flow along the vortex in the direction dictated by the sign of chirality imbalance; we call this new transport phenomenon the Chiral Propulsion Effect (CPE). The dynamics of the filament is described by a modified version of the localized induction equation in the parity-breaking background. We analyze the linear stability of simple vortex configurations, and study the effects of chiral media on the excitation spectrum and the growth rate of the unstable modes. It is also shown that, if the equation of motion of the filament is symmetric under the simultaneous reversal of parity and time, the resulting planar solution cannot transport energy.

## Key claim

The authors find a semi-classical analog of the anomaly-induced chiral magnetic effect in the motion of vortex filaments, where helical solitonic excitations carry an additional energy flow along the vortex in a direction dictated by the sign of chirality imbalance; this effect, called the Chiral Propulsion Effect (CPE), is conditional on the breaking of parity in the medium, and the authors show that if the equation of motion is symmetric under simultaneous reversal of parity and time, the resulting planar solution cannot transport energy.

## Method

The authors model vortex filament dynamics using a modified localized induction equation (the Fukumoto-Miyazaki equation) that includes a term proportional to the chiral chemical potential μ, derived from supplementing the non-relativistic Abelian Higgs model with a magnetic helicity term. They find explicit helical and solitonic solutions via the Hasimoto transformation to the Hirota equation, compute the Poynting vector momentum of these solutions, and analyze linear stability around constant-curvature configurations (circle, helix, straight line) using the Frenet-Serret frame to derive dispersion relations. They also prove a general theorem about the absence of energy transport for planar solutions under PT symmetry using the recursion operator of the LIE hierarchy.

## Key numbers

- The phase velocity of the helical solution is given by \( v_p = \tau_0 + \mu (\tau_0^2 - \kappa_0^2/2) \), where \( \kappa_0 \) and \( \tau_0 \) are the curvature and torsion of the helix, and \( \mu \) is the chiral chemical potential.
- The group velocity of the helical solution is \( v_g = -\kappa_0^2/\tau_0 - (3\kappa_0^2/2)\mu \).
- The kinetic energy of a soliton is \( E = 4\epsilon \), independent of \( \mu \).
- The z-component of momentum per unit length for the helix solution is \( (\bar{P}_{\rm helix})_z = (\kappa_0^2/\tau_0)(1 + (3/2)\mu\tau_0) \varphi^2 M^2 \), where \( \varphi \) is the magnetic flux and \( M \) is the inverse core size.
- The z-component of momentum for the soliton solution is \( (P_{\rm sol})_z = [4\epsilon(2\tau_0 + \mu(3\tau_0^2 - \epsilon^2)) / (\epsilon^2 + \tau_0^2)] \varphi^2 M^2 \).
- The dispersion relation for fluctuations around constant curvature and torsion is \( \omega = 2p\tau + \mu p(p^2 - (3/2)\kappa^2 + 3\tau^2) \pm \sqrt{p^2(p^2 - \kappa^2)(1 + 3\mu\tau)^2} \).
- For a circle (\( \tau = 0 \)), the frequency is \( \omega = \pm \kappa^2 \sqrt{n^2(n^2 - 1)} + \mu \kappa^3 n (n^2 - 3/2) \), with integer \( n \).
- In the small \( p \) limit for the helix, the growth rate of unstable modes is \( {\rm Im}\, \omega = \pm \sqrt{\kappa^2 (1+3\mu\tau)^2} \, p + O(p^2) \).
- In the straight-line limit (\( \kappa \rightarrow 0, \tau \rightarrow 0 \)), the dispersion is \( \omega = \pm p^2 + \mu p^3 \).

## Context

This work extends the study of chiral transport phenomena, specifically the Chiral Magnetic Effect (CME) and Chiral Vortical Effect (CVE) discovered by Kharzeev (2004-2007) and others, into the dynamics of vortex filaments in parity-breaking media. The authors build on the localized induction equation (LIE) formalism from classical vortex dynamics (Saffman, 1992) and the Fukumoto-Miyazaki equation (Fukumoto and Miyazaki, 1991) for vortices with axial flow, connecting these to the anomaly-induced transport through the chiral chemical potential. The results enable future studies of vortex instabilities in chiral systems such as the quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy-ion collisions, Dirac and Weyl semimetals, and primordial electroweak plasma, and provide a framework for understanding how chirality imbalance affects the propagation and stability of topological defects.
