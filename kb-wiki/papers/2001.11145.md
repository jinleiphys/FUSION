# Computing real time correlation functions on a hybrid classical/quantum computer

> Quantum devices may overcome limitations of classical computers in studies of nuclear structure functions and parton Wigner distributions of protons and nuclei. In this talk, we discuss a worldline approach to compute nuclear structure functions in the high energy Regge limit of QCD using a hybrid quantum computer, by expressing the fermion determinant in the QCD path integral as a quantum mechanical path integral over $0+1$-dimensional fermionic and bosonic world-lines in background gauge fields. Our simplest example of computing the well-known dipole model result for the structure function $F_2$ in the high energy Regge limit is feasible with NISQ era technology using few qubits and shallow circuits. This example can be scaled up in complexity and extended in scope to compute structure functions, scattering amplitudes and other real-time correlation functions in QCD, relevant for example to describe non-equilibrium transport of quarks and gluons in a Quark-Gluon-Plasma.

## Key claim

The authors present a hybrid classical/quantum algorithm for computing the structure function \(F_2\) in the high energy Regge limit of QCD, claiming feasibility on NISQ-era devices with few qubits and shallow circuits, but only for the simplest dipole model example; scaling to full proton structure requires solving a combined worldline and Yang-Mills density matrix evolution that remains impractical on current hardware.

## Method

The authors express the fermion determinant in the QCD Schwinger-Keldysh path integral as a quantum mechanical path integral over \(0+1\)-dimensional bosonic and fermionic worldlines in background gauge fields. They then restrict to the high energy Regge limit (small Bjorken \(x_{\rm Bj}\)), where the Color Glass Condensate effective field theory approximates the slow degrees of freedom as classical color fields coupled to static sources. The hadron tensor projection \(F_2\) is written as a Grassmann integral over worldline operators, which is mapped to a quantum circuit involving a controlled gate of the operator \(\Omega_{L,T}\) acting on two qubits in a maximally mixed state, with measurements of Pauli operators yielding the trace.

## Key numbers

* The structure function \(F_2\) is computed in the Regge limit defined by fixed photon virtuality \(Q^2 = -q^2\) and small Bjorken \(x_{\rm Bj} \approx Q^2/s \to 0\), where \(s \approx 2P^+ q^- \to \infty\) is the squared center of mass energy.
* The quantum circuit for the spin trace uses \(n=2\) qubits, with the controlled gate of \(\Omega_{L,T}\) acting on a maximally mixed state \(\hat{\rho}_n = \mathbb{I}_n / 2^n\).
* The worldline Hamiltonian contains terms with coupling \(g\) to the Yang-Mills field strength \(F_{\mu\nu}[A]\) and coupling \(e\) to the electromagnetic field strength \(F_{\mu\nu}[a]\).

## Context

This work extends the worldline formalism of Strassler (1992) and D'Hoker and Gagnon (1995) to a hybrid quantum computing approach for QCD structure functions, building on prior work by Mueller (2017) on worldline methods for parton distributions. It aims to overcome limitations of classical lattice Monte Carlo techniques, which are restricted to Euclidean spacetime and cannot directly compute the Minkowskian matrix elements required for structure functions. The approach enables future computation of real-time correlation functions relevant for non-equilibrium transport in the Quark-Gluon-Plasma, and can be extended to multi-leg and multi-loop scattering amplitudes following the program of Jordan, Lee, and Preskill (2011).
