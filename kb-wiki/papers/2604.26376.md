# Quantum Complexity and New Directions in Nuclear Physics and High-Energy Physics Phenomenology

> Advances in quantum information science (QIS) are providing transformative insights into the complexity of quantum many-body systems, potentially defining new frontiers in nuclear and high-energy physics. This review explores how QIS-derived techniques are fostering new analytic frameworks and algorithms - both classical and quantum - to tackle (some of the) present barriers to discovery in fundamental physics, with applicability to other science domains. We highlight how these techniques are shedding new light on the structure and dynamics of hadrons, nuclei, matter in extreme conditions, and beyond. Importantly, they are expected to play an essential role in the development of large-scale quantum simulations of such systems, particularly in setting the balance among quantum and classical computational resources.

## Key claim

The paper argues that quantum information science provides rigorous, resource-theoretic definitions of "complexity" for many-body quantum states, based on entanglement, non-stabilizerness (magic), and non-Gaussianity, which together determine the hardness of classical simulation and the quantum computational resources required, but the precise boundary between "simple" and "complex" states depends on the interplay of these resources and remains incompletely mapped for general systems.

## Method

The review synthesizes concepts from quantum information theory, including stabilizer formalism, tensor network methods, Gaussian states, and resource theories, to classify many-body quantum states by their entanglement structure and non-stabilizerness content. It surveys measures such as von Neumann and Rényi entanglement entropies, stabilizer Rényi entropies (SREs), robustness of magic (RoM), and non-local magic, and discusses their computability in tensor network representations and their relevance to nuclear and high-energy physics simulations.

## Key numbers

- The stabilizer 2-Rényi entropy for a single qubit reaches a maximum of \(\mathcal{M}_2^{N=1, \mathrm{max}} = \log(3/2) \simeq 0.585\) for T-type states.
- The maximal stabilizer 2-Rényi entropy for an \(N\)-qubit tensor product state is \(\mathcal{M}_2^{N, TP, \mathrm{max}} = N \log(3/2)\).
- The upper bound for the stabilizer 2-Rényi entropy for any \(N\)-qubit state is \(\mathcal{M}_2^{N, \mathrm{max}} = \log((2^N+1)/2)\).
- Any stabilizer state can be specified with \(N(2N+1) = O(N^2)\) bits, where \(N\) is the number of qubits.
- The number of stabilizer states grows doubly-exponentially as \(2^{(1/2 + o(1))N^2}\).
- For qubit systems, stabilizer states form a complex projective 3-design; for qudit systems with dimension equal to a power of a prime, they form a 2-design.
- Gaussian states (both bosonic and fermionic) admit an efficient classical description with \(O(N^2)\) bits, where \(N\) is the number of modes.

## Context

This review extends the line of work connecting quantum information theory to many-body physics, building on foundational results such as the Gottesman-Knill theorem (Gottesman, 1998) and the development of tensor network methods including DMRG (White, 1992) and MPS (McCulloch, 2007; Schollwöck, 2011). It enables future work on quantifying the quantum computational resources needed for simulating nuclear and high-energy systems, particularly in setting the balance between classical and quantum resources for large-scale simulations, and opens questions about the exact form of the entanglement-magic boundary for general many-body systems.
