# Strongly Interacting Matter Under Rotation: An Introduction

> Ultrarelativistic collisions between heavy nuclei briefly generate the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), a new state of matter characterized by deconfined partons last seen microseconds after the Big Bang. The properties of the QGP are of intense interest, and a large community has developed over several decades, to produce, measure and understand this primordial plasma. The plasma is now recognized to be a strongly-coupled fluid with remarkable properties, and hydrodynamics is commonly used to quantify and model the system. An important feature of any fluid is its vorticity, related to the local angular momentum density; however, this degree of freedom has received relatively little attention because no experimental signals of vorticity had been detected. Thanks to recent high-statistics datasets from experiments with precision tracking and complete kinemetic coverage at collider energies, hyperon spin polarization measurements have begun to uncover the vorticity of the QGP created at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider. The injection of this new degree of freedom into a relatively mature field of research represents an enormous opportunity to generate new insights into the physics of the QGP. The community has responded with enthusiasm, and this book (to be published as a volume of Lecture Notes in Physics series by Springer) represents some of the diverse lines of inquiry into aspects of strongly interacting matter under rotation.

## Key claim

The paper introduces the study of strongly interacting matter under rotation, arguing that the recent experimental observation of global hyperon polarization has opened a new direction in the mature field of relativistic heavy ion physics, though the theoretical framework based on local thermodynamic equilibrium and thermal vorticity works well for hyperon polarization but fails to explain vector meson spin alignment measurements.

## Method

The authors review the hydrodynamic approach to vorticity and spin polarization, where thermal vorticity is defined as the antisymmetric derivative of the four-temperature vector field and the mean spin vector of spin-1/2 fermions is obtained from an integral over the hadronization hypersurface involving thermal vorticity. Experimental measurements of global Lambda hyperon polarization are performed by correlating the first-order event plane angle from forward detectors with the daughter proton azimuthal angle in the hyperon rest frame, using the parity-violating weak decay of Lambda hyperons. Vector meson spin alignment is measured through the spin-density matrix element rho_00 extracted from the angular distribution of daughter particles in strong decays.

## Key numbers

- Global Lambda polarization in heavy ion collisions is predicted to be at the level of a few percent (from 2013 derivation)
- The decay parameter alpha_Lambda = 0.732 determines the strength of the polarization effect in the angular distribution of daughter protons
- Angular momentum in relativistic heavy ion collisions can be of order 10^3 to 10^5 hbar
- Vector meson spin alignment measurements show |1/3 - rho_00| approximately 0.1, two orders of magnitude larger than expectations based on hyperon polarization and vorticity considerations (which would give 1/3 - rho_00 approximately 10^-3)
- Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 200 GeV are used for longitudinal polarization measurements showing sub-percent polarization signals
- Pb+Pb collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV are used for ALICE vector meson alignment measurements

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic paradigm that successfully described collective flow in heavy ion collisions since the 2005 RHIC discoveries of the quark-gluon plasma as a strongly coupled fluid, applying it to the new observable of spin polarization. The paper contrasts the successful hydrodynamic description of global Lambda polarization (consistent with predictions by Becattini (2013) and Jiang (2016)) with the puzzling vector meson spin alignment results that cannot be explained by the equilibrium vorticity framework, pointing to possible explanations involving polarized quark fragmentation (Liang and Wang, 2005) or novel mean field effects (Sheng, 2019). The work enables future experimental studies at the RHIC Beam Energy Scan phase II, NICA, and FAIR facilities, as well as theoretical investigations of vorticity substructure, magnetic field effects, and spin-spin correlations.
