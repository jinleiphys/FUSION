# Fitting theories of nuclear binding energies

> In developing theories of nuclear binding energy such as density-functional theory, the effort required to make a fit can be daunting due to the large number of parameters that may be in the theory and the large number of nuclei in the mass table. For theories based on the Skyrme interaction, the effort can be reduced considerably by using the singular value decomposition to reduce the size of the parameter space. We find that the sensitive parameters define a space of dimension four or so, and within this space a linear refit is adequate for a number of Skyrme parameters sets from the literature. We do not find marked differences in the quality of the fit between the SLy4, the Bky4 and SkP parameter sets. The r.m.s. residual error in even-even nuclei is about 1.5 MeV, half the value of the liquid drop model. We also discuss an alternative norm for evaluating mass fits, the Chebyshev norm. It focuses attention on the cases with the largest discrepancies between theory and experiment. We show how it works with the liquid drop model and make some applications to models based on Skyrme energy functionals. The Chebyshev norm seems to be more sensitive to new experimental data than the root-mean-square norm. The method also has the advantage that candidate improvements to the theories can be assessed with computations on smaller sets of nuclei.

# Fitting theories of nuclear binding energies

## Key claim

The sensitive degrees of freedom in Skyrme energy functionals for nuclear binding energies define a parameter space of dimension four or so, and within this space a linear refit is adequate for a number of Skyrme parameter sets from the literature; however, the authors do not find marked differences in the quality of the fit between the SLy4, BSk4 and SkP parameter sets, and the Chebyshev norm appears more sensitive to new experimental data than the root-mean-square norm.

## Method

The authors used singular value decomposition to reduce the parameter space of Skyrme energy functionals from nine linear parameters to a subspace of dimension four, then applied linear least-squares refits to even-even nuclei from the 2003 mass table using the Feynman-Hellman theorem to obtain derivatives. They also implemented minimax fits using the Chebyshev norm (maximum absolute residual) for both the liquid drop model and Skyrme-based self-consistent mean-field theories, applying an ascent algorithm to identify the critical set of N+1 nuclei that define the minimax condition.

## Key numbers

- The r.m.s. residual error in even-even nuclei for Skyrme-based theories is about 1.5 MeV, half the value of the liquid drop model which gives 2.95 MeV r.m.s. residual when fitted to the 2003 mass table.
- The eigenvalues of the least-squares fitting matrix for the SLy4 functional span nearly 10 orders of magnitude, with the first eigenvalue 3 orders of magnitude larger than any of the others.
- The original SLy4 parameterization has a 3.3 MeV r.m.s. residual, improving to about 1.7 MeV for parameter spaces of dimension N greater than or equal to 3.
- The nuclear matter binding energy from the SLy4 refit is -16.06 MeV, and the symmetry energy is 32.0 MeV.
- The liquid drop model minimax fit to the 2003 mass table gives a C-norm of 9.2 MeV, while the 1995 mass table gives 8.0 MeV; restricting to nuclei with experimental errors less than 200 keV gives 8.4 MeV.
- The SLy4-based minimax fit gives a C-norm of 4.8 MeV, the BSk4-based fit gives 4.7 MeV, and the Skxce-based fit gives 4.4 MeV.
- Adding J=0 projection to the SLy4 baseline reduces the C-norm from 4.84 MeV to 4.36 MeV; adding N,Z particle number projection gives 4.40 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of Skyrme energy functionals for nuclear binding energies, building on the density-functional theory approach and the Bethe-Weizsaecker semiempirical mass formula as a calibration point. The authors use the Paris-Brussels code ev8 for binding energy calculations and reference the work of Bender et al. (2004) on correlation energies and Goriely et al. (2003) on the BSk4 functional. The paper enables more efficient screening of candidate improvements to Skyrme-based theories by using the Chebyshev norm to focus computational effort on the critical set of nuclei with the largest discrepancies, rather than requiring calculations on the full mass table.
