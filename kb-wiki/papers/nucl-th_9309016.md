# Combinatorial nuclear level density by a Monte Carlo method

> We present a new combinatorial method for the calculation of the nuclear level density. It is based on a Monte Carlo technique, in order to avoid a direct counting procedure which is generally impracticable for high-A nuclei. The Monte Carlo simulation, making use of the Metropolis sampling scheme, allows a computationally fast estimate of the level density for many fermion systems in large shell model spaces. We emphasize the advantages of this Monte Carlo approach, particularly concerning the prediction of the spin and parity distributions of the excited states, and compare our results with those derived from a traditional combinatorial or a statistical method. Such a Monte Carlo technique seems very promising to determine accurate level densities in a large energy range for nuclear reaction calculations.

## Key claim

The paper presents a Monte Carlo method, based on the Metropolis sampling scheme, as an alternative to direct combinatorial counting for calculating nuclear level densities; the method yields an exact level density save for statistical errors, and is computationally feasible for high-A nuclei and large shell model spaces where direct counting is impracticable.

## Method

The authors implement a Metropolis random walk through the configuration space of a spherical nucleus, where each configuration is defined by occupation numbers for neutron and proton single-particle states. At each step, a single nucleon is moved to a randomly chosen available single-particle state, and the move is accepted or rejected according to a weight function chosen to ensure approximately uniform sampling across the excitation energy interval of interest. The spin and parity of each sampled configuration are assigned randomly using the Mayer-Jensen table for subshell spins and a recursive random coupling procedure, and the absolute level density is obtained by normalizing the Monte Carlo sample to the total number of states up to the maximum energy, calculated via the Williams recursive method.

## Key numbers

- For $^{56}$Fe up to 30 MeV excitation energy, a Monte Carlo sample size of $N = 10^6$ was used, sampling a fraction of about $3 \times 10^{-4}$ of the whole set of excited states.
- For $^{208}$Pb, the same sample size $N = 10^6$ sampled a fraction of about $10^{-7}$ of all excited states.
- For $^{140}$La, the same sample size $N = 10^6$ sampled a fraction of about $2 \times 10^{-9}$ of all excited states.
- For the spin distribution comparison in $^{56}$Fe in the energy interval [20–21 MeV], a sample size of $N = 50 \times 10^6$ was used, where the number of excited levels is as high as $2 \times 10^6$.
- The classical assumption of equipartition of both parities is only valid above about 15 MeV for $^{56}$Fe.

## Context

This work extends the combinatorial method for nuclear level densities, which was previously limited to low excitation energies or small shell model spaces due to computational cost (Grove and Hill, 1960s; Ford, 1960s), and contrasts with the recursive method of Williams (1972) which cannot treat residual interactions. The Monte Carlo approach enables calculation of spin and parity distributions without the saddle-point approximation used in the statistical model (Bethe, 1936; Ericson, 1960), and is intended to provide accurate level densities for nuclear reaction calculations over a large energy range, with the inclusion of pairing interaction addressed in a later section of the paper.
