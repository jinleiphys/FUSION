# Sensitivity of transfer cross sections to the bound-state wave functions

> We test the sensitivity of transfer reactions to the bound state wave functions within a distorted wave Born approximation formalism. Using supersymmetric transformations, we remove the Pauli-forbidden states from the two-body potentials and generate an equivalent supersymmetric partner. Wave functions from these potentials have the same asymptotics, but they differ in the nuclear interior. This allows us to study the influence of the nuclear interior on transfer cross sections. We apply the calculations to the $^{16}$O($d, p$)$^{17}$O and $^{12}$C($^7$Li, $t$)$^{16}$O reactions, which are typical examples of nucleon and $α$ transfer, respectively. The spectroscopic factors for $^{17}$O are decreased by about 30\% when using supersymmetric potentials. For $^{16}$O, the differences are smaller. However, we show that ambiguities exist in the determination of the spectroscopic factors, due to the choice of the angular range where the fit is performed.

# Sensitivity of transfer cross sections to the bound-state wave functions

## Key claim
Using supersymmetric transformations to remove Pauli-forbidden states from two-body potentials, the authors show that transfer reaction cross sections and extracted spectroscopic factors are sensitive to the nuclear interior region of bound-state wave functions, not just the asymptotic tail. The spectroscopic factors for 17O are decreased by about 30% when using supersymmetric potentials, but the authors emphasize that ambiguities exist in spectroscopic factor determination due to the choice of angular range where the fit is performed.

## Method
The authors applied supersymmetric (SUSY) transformations to Woods-Saxon bound-state potentials to remove Pauli-forbidden states, generating equivalent SUSY partner potentials with nodeless wave functions that share the same asymptotics but differ in the nuclear interior. They then calculated transfer cross sections within the distorted wave Born approximation (DWBA) formalism using both the original deep Woods-Saxon potentials and their SUSY partners, applying the method to the 16O(d,p)17O reaction (nucleon transfer) and the 12C(7Li,t)16O reaction (alpha transfer). A cutoff radius technique was employed to evaluate the contribution of short distances to the transfer cross section.

## Key numbers
- Spectroscopic factors for the 1/2+ excited state of 17O: 1.73 (WS) vs 1.22 (SUSY) at Ed = 25.4 MeV; 2.08 (WS) vs 1.63 (SUSY) at Ed = 36.0 MeV, a decrease of approximately 20-30% when using SUSY potentials
- Spectroscopic factors for the 0_2+ state of 16O at 28 MeV: 0.18 ± 0.04 (WS) vs 0.19 ± 0.02 (SUSY); at 34 MeV: 0.24 ± 0.04 (WS) vs 0.25 ± 0.03 (SUSY)
- Spectroscopic factors for the 2_1+ state of 16O at 28 MeV: 0.17 ± 0.02 (WS) vs 0.15 ± 0.02 (SUSY); at 34 MeV: 0.16 ± 0.02 (WS) vs 0.14 ± 0.03 (SUSY)
- For the 16O(d,p)17O reaction, significant differences between WS and SUSY calculations appear for cutoff distances r_min between 0 and 3.6 fm
- For deuteron energies well below the Coulomb barrier (V_C = 2.54 MeV), SUSY transformations have negligible effect on cross sections

## Context
This work extends the study of uncertainties in spectroscopic factors extracted from transfer reactions, which have been used for over six decades as a nuclear structure tool (Satchler 1990, Glover 2004). The authors build on prior work using the DWBA formalism (Satchler 1983) and their own R-matrix and Lagrange-mesh methods (Shubhchintak and Descouvemont 2019). The supersymmetric transformation technique follows the approach of Sukumar (1985) and Baye (1987) for removing Pauli-forbidden states. The results show that for the 12C(7Li,t)16O reaction, which is used in indirect measurements of the astrophysically important 12C(alpha,gamma)16O reaction (Oertzen et al. 2012, Brune et al. 1978), the spectroscopic factors remain nearly unchanged when replacing Woods-Saxon with SUSY potentials, but the shape of angular distributions changes significantly. This work enables better quantification of systematic uncertainties in spectroscopic factor extraction and identifies energy regimes where transfer reactions are truly peripheral versus sensitive to nuclear interior contributions.
