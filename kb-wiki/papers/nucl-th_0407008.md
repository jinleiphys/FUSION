# Order parameter fluctuations in percolation: Application to nuclear multifragmentation

> Order parameter fluctuations (the largest cluster size distribution) are studied within a three-dimensional bond percolation model on small lattices. Cumulant ratios measuring the fluctuations exhibit distinct features near the percolation transition (pseudocritical point), providing a method for its identification. The location of the critical point in the continuous limit can be estimated without variation of the system size. This method is remarkably insensitive to finite-size effects and may be applied even for a very small system. The possibility of using various measurable quantities for sorting events makes the procedure useful in studying clusterization phenomena, in particular nuclear multifragmentation. Finite-size scaling and delta-scaling relations are examined. The model shows inconsistency with some of the delta-scaling expectations. The role of surface effects is evaluated by comparing results for free and periodic boundary conditions.

# Order parameter fluctuations in percolation: Application to nuclear multifragmentation

## Key claim

The maximum of the normalized variance of the largest cluster size distribution, $\kappa_{2}/\langle s_{max}\rangle^2$, provides an estimate of the true critical point in the continuous limit that is remarkably insensitive to finite-size effects, even for very small systems, without requiring variation of the system size. However, the $\Delta$-scaling method fails for systems in normal phases and is violated in small systems with open boundaries, contradicting earlier claims that the $\Delta=1$ scaling relation is valid independently of any phenomenological reasons for changing the system size.

## Method

A three-dimensional bond percolation model on simple cubic lattices of size $N=L^3$ with $L=3,4,5,6$ (corresponding to the range of system sizes available in nuclear reactions) was simulated using a Monte Carlo procedure with the Hoshen-Kopelman cluster labeling algorithm. Low-order cumulants (mean, variance, skewness, and kurtosis) of the largest cluster size distribution were studied as functions of the bond probability $p$, with free boundary conditions to account for surface effects in real systems, and compared to results with periodic boundary conditions. Events were also sorted by experimentally accessible quantities (multiplicity, fraction of open bonds, total size of complex fragments) to evaluate the impact of event sorting on the identification of critical behavior.

## Key numbers

- The percolation threshold in the continuous limit is $p_c \simeq 0.2488$.
- For free boundary conditions, the asymptotic values of the cumulant ratios at $p_c$ are approximately $K_2 = 0.24$, $K_3 = 1.2$, and $K_4 = 1.5$.
- The minimum value of $K_4$ (kurtosis) at the pseudocritical point is approximately $-0.9$, with $K_2 \simeq 0.135$ at that point.
- The pseudocritical point scales with system size as $(p-p_c) \simeq 0.5 L^{-1/\nu}$, where the correlation length exponent $\nu = 0.878 \pm 0.005$ (consistent with the expected value $\nu = 0.875$).
- Converting bond probability to temperature using the prescription of Li and Babo gives $T_{pc}/T_c = 0.64, 0.73, 0.78$ for $N = 27, 64, 125$, respectively.
- For sorting by $S_{bound}$, the pseudocritical point is characterized by $K_2 \simeq 0.15$ and position $S_{bound}/N \simeq 0.84$.
- The Fisher exponent for the fragment size distribution at the best power-law fit is $\tau = 2.189$, with normalization constant $q_0 = 0.173$.

## Context

This work extends percolation-based studies of nuclear multifragmentation as a probe of the liquid-gas phase transition in nuclear matter, building on the $\Delta$-scaling method proposed by Botet and P{\l}oszajczak (1999-2001) and applied to nuclear fragmentation data by several groups. The paper directly contradicts the statement in earlier work by Botet, P{\l}oszajczak, and collaborators that the $\Delta=1$ scaling relation is valid independently of any phenomenological reasons for changing the system size, showing instead that it requires variation of system size or specific change of the control parameter. The results enable experimental analysis of multifragmentation data (particularly Aladin data) using cumulant ratios of the largest fragment size distribution without requiring knowledge or variation of the system size, and provide a method to estimate both the critical point and the system size at critical and pseudocritical points.
