# Sub-barrier fusion reactions

> The concept of compound nucleus was proposed by Niels Bohr in 1936 to explain narrow resonances observed in scattering of a slow neutron off atomic nuclei. A compound nucleus is a metastable state with a long lifetime, in which all the degrees of freedom are in a sort of thermal equilibrium. Fusion reactions are defined as reactions to form such compound nucleus by merging two atomic nuclei. Here a short description of heavy-ion fusion reactions at energies close the Coulomb barrier is presented. This includes: (i) an overview of a fusion process, (ii) a strong interplay between nuclear structure and fusion, (iii) fusion and multi-dimensional/multi-particle quantum tunneling, and (iv) fusion for superheavy elements.

## Key claim

This review argues that sub-barrier heavy-ion fusion reactions exhibit a strong interplay between nuclear structure and reaction dynamics, with the sub-barrier enhancement of fusion cross sections being caused by couplings of the relative motion to collective excitations and nuclear deformation, while at deep sub-barrier energies a hindrance phenomenon appears that requires modeling of the dynamics after the two nuclei touch.

## Method

The paper reviews the potential model for fusion cross sections, including the Wong formula derived from a parabolic barrier approximation, and compares it to experimental data for systems such as \(^{14}\)N+\(^{12}\)C and \(^{16}\)O+\(^{154}\)Sm. It then presents the coupled-channels approach, which solves coupled Schrödinger equations to account for couplings between the relative motion and low-lying collective excitations (e.g., quadrupole vibrations and rotational states), and discusses the extraction of fusion barrier distributions from the second energy derivative of \(E\sigma_{\text{fus}}(E)\).

## Key numbers

* The \(^{209}\)Bi(\(^{70}\)Zn,n)\(^{278}\)Nh reaction to form element 113 (Nihonium) was performed at \(E_{\text{c.m.}} = 262\) MeV, while the barrier height from the Bass potential is around 260 MeV.
* For the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{144}\)Sm system, the Coulomb barrier height is \(V_b = 61.25\) MeV.
* For the \(^{14}\)N+\(^{12}\)C system, the Coulomb barrier height is \(V_b \sim 6.9\) MeV.
* For the \(^{16}\)O+\(^{154}\)Sm system, the Coulomb barrier height is \(V_b \sim 59\) MeV.
* The deformation parameters of \(^{154}\)Sm are \(\beta_2 \sim 0.30\) (quadrupole) and \(\beta_4 \sim 0.05\) (hexadecapole).
* The hexadecapole deformation parameter of \(^{24}\)Mg was determined as \(\beta_4 = -0.11 \pm 0.02\) from a Bayesian analysis of quasi-elastic scattering data.
* For the \(^{64}\)Ni+\(^{64}\)Ni system, standard coupled-channels calculations reproduce data above about 89 MeV, but deep sub-barrier hindrance appears at lower energies.

## Context

This review extends earlier work on sub-barrier fusion, including reviews by Balantekin and Takigawa (1998), Hagino and Takigawa (2012), and Dasgupta et al. (1998) on fusion barrier distributions, as well as reviews by Back et al. (2014), Montagnoli and Stefanini (2017), and Jiang et al. (2021) on deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance. It enables further study of fusion for superheavy element synthesis and for neutron-rich nuclei relevant to nuclear astrophysics, while noting that a theoretical model coherently incorporating breakup, transfer, and coupling effects for weakly-bound nuclei has yet to be developed.
