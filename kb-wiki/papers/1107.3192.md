# Magnetic effects in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

> The time-evolution and space-distribution of internal electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion reactions at beam energies between 200 and 2000 MeV/nucleon are studied within an Isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uhling-Uhlenbeck transport model IBUU11. While the magnetic field can reach about $7\times 10^{16}$ G which is significantly higher than the estimated surface magnetic field ($\sim 10^{15}$ G) of magnetars, it has almost no effect on nucleon observables as the Lorentz force is normally much weaker than the nuclear force. Very interestingly, however, the magnetic field generated by the projectile-like (target-like) spectator has a strong focusing/diverging effect on positive/negative pions at forward (backward) rapidities. Consequently, the differential $π^-/π^+$ ratio as a function of rapidity is significantly altered by the magnetic field while the total multiplicities of both positive and negative pions remain about the same. At beam energies above about 1 GeV/nucleon, while the integrated ratio of total $π^-$ to $π^+$ multiplicities is not, the differential $π^-/π^+$ ratio is sensitive to the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$. Our findings suggest that magnetic effects should be carefully considered in future studies of using the differential $π^-/π^+$ ratio as a probe of the $E_{\rm{sym}}(ρ)$ at supra-saturation densities.

# Magnetic effects in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

## Key claim

While the magnetic field generated in heavy-ion collisions at 200–2000 MeV/nucleon can reach about 7×10^16 G, exceeding the surface field of magnetars, it has almost no effect on nucleon observables because the Lorentz force is much weaker than the nuclear force; however, the magnetic field from projectile-like and target-like spectators significantly alters the differential π⁻/π⁺ ratio as a function of rapidity via focusing/diverging effects on charged pions, even though total pion multiplicities remain unchanged.

## Method

The time evolution and spatial distribution of internal electromagnetic fields were calculated using the isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uhling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport model IBUU11, with Liénard-Wiechert potentials including retardation effects. The model incorporates an isospin-dependent three-body force and a readjusted high-momentum tail of the momentum-dependent interaction (MDI) isoscalar potential, with the density dependence of the nuclear symmetry energy controlled by a parameter x in the three-body part of the MDI interaction. Simulations were performed for Au+Au collisions at beam energies from 200 to 2000 MeV/nucleon at various impact parameters.

## Key numbers

- Maximum magnetic field strength: approximately 7×10^16 G, compared to the estimated surface magnetic field of magnetars at about 10^15 G
- For 500 AMeV Au+Au collisions at b=10 fm, the ratio of Lorentz force to Coulomb force (R_x^ME) at the beginning of collision equals (v_0/c)^2 = 0.21
- The nuclear force is several 10 to 10^2 times larger than the Lorentz force for nucleons
- At 2 GeV/nucleon Au+Au collisions, the baryon density can reach about 3.5ρ_0
- Integrated π⁻/π⁺ ratio for b=0 fm: 2.02/1.97 (x=1), 1.81/1.78 (x=0), 1.68/1.67 (x=-1) without/with magnetic field
- Integrated π⁻/π⁺ ratio for b=5 fm: 1.87/1.86 (x=1), 1.79/1.79 (x=0), 1.73/1.73 (x=-1) without/with magnetic field
- Integrated n/p ratio (free nucleons) for b=0 fm: 1.23/1.23 (x=1), 1.24/1.24 (x=0), 1.25/1.25 (x=-1) without/with magnetic field

## Context

This work extends studies of magnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions from high energies at RHIC (Kharzeev 2008, Skokov 2009, Voronyuk 2011) down to intermediate energies (200–2000 MeV/nucleon), where the magnetic field is about 10 times weaker but lasts about 10 times longer than at RHIC energies. The findings directly impact the use of the π⁻/π⁺ ratio as a probe of the nuclear symmetry energy at supra-saturation densities, a method proposed by Li (2002) and pursued by Xia, Feng, and others; previous comparisons of transport model predictions with FOPI Collaboration data (Reisdorf 2007) did not include magnetic effects, so earlier conclusions about the high-density symmetry energy need re-evaluation. The work enables future studies to properly account for magnetic focusing/diverging effects when extracting symmetry energy information from differential π⁻/π⁺ measurements.
