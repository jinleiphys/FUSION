# Classifying near-threshold enhancement using deep neural network

> One of the main issues in hadron spectroscopy is to identify the origin of threshold or near-threshold enhancement. Prior to our study, there is no straightforward way of distinguishing even the lowest channel threshold-enhancement of the nucleon-nucleon system using only the cross-sections. The difficulty lies in the proximity of either a bound or virtual state pole to the threshold which creates an almost identical structure in the scattering region. Identifying the nature of the pole causing the enhancement falls under the general classification problem and supervised machine learning using a feed-forward neural network is known to excel in this task. In this study, we discuss the basic idea behind deep neural network and how it can be used to identify the nature of the pole causing the enhancement. The applicability of the trained network can be explored by using an exact separable potential model to generate a validation dataset. We find that within some acceptable range of the cut-off parameter, the neural network gives high accuracy of inference. The result also reveals the important role played by the background singularities in the training dataset. Finally, we apply the method to nucleon-nucleon scattering data and show that the network was able to give the correct nature of pole, i.e. virtual pole for ${}^1S_0$ partial cross-section and bound state pole for ${}^3S_0$.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a deep neural network can distinguish between bound-state and virtual-state threshold enhancements in scattering cross-sections, even though the cross-sections for the two cases are nearly identical in the absence of background singularities. The key condition is that the network's training must include simulated background singularities (such as branch cuts or distant poles) to break the degeneracy between bound and virtual pole cross-sections.

## Method

The authors generated 4 million labeled s-wave scattering cross-sections using an S-matrix parametrization that includes a left-hand branch-cut background, a simple background pole, and a near-threshold pole whose sign determines bound (positive) or virtual (negative) nature. They trained six feed-forward neural network architectures (with 200 input nodes representing cross-section values from 0-100 MeV, various hidden-layer configurations, and either Adam or AMSGrad optimizers) on 3.2 million of these cross-sections. The trained networks were validated on independent cross-sections generated from separable potential models (energy-independent and energy-dependent coupling forms) and then applied to nucleon-nucleon partial cross-sections from partial-wave analyses and potential models.

## Key numbers

* Training dataset: 4 × 10^6 labeled cross-sections generated from the S-matrix parametrization; 3.2 × 10^6 used for training, 8 × 10^5 held for testing.
* Validation: 1 × 10^5 cross-sections per cut-off range from separable potential models; high inference accuracy observed for cut-off parameters in the range 400-1200 MeV.
* Input cross-sections were sampled on an equal-spaced energy grid from 0 to 100 MeV.
* The network correctly identified the ¹S₀ partial cross-section as due to a virtual state pole and the ³S₁ partial cross-section as due to a bound state pole (the deuteron).

## Context

This work extends the authors' prior efforts (Sombillo 2020, Sombillo 2021) to apply deep learning to final-state scattering data analysis, moving beyond the typical use of machine learning for particle identification at the experimental level. The paper addresses the long-standing difficulty in hadron spectroscopy of distinguishing bound states from virtual states using only cross-section data, a problem previously considered intractable for the nucleon-nucleon system without prior knowledge of the deuteron. The method enables classification of near-threshold enhancements in coupled-channel problems where resonances are short-lived and only scattering data are available, with potential applications to recently discovered exotic hadrons such as the X(3872) and Pc pentaquarks.
