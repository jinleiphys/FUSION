# Charmonium in the vector channel at finite temperature from QCD sum rules

> Thermal Hilbert moment QCD sum rules are used to obtain the temperature dependence of the hadronic parameters of charmonium in the vector channel, i.e. the $J$ / $ψ$ resonance mass, coupling (leptonic decay constant), total width, and continuum threshold. The continuum threshold $s_0$, which signals the end of the resonance region and the onset of perturbative QCD (PQCD), behaves as in all other hadronic channels, i.e. it decreases with increasing temperature until it reaches the PQCD threshold $s_0 = 4 m_Q^2$, with $m_Q$ the charm quark mass, at $T\simeq 1.22 T_c$. The rest of the hadronic parameters behave very differently from those of light-light and heavy-light quark systems. The $J$ / $ψ$ mass is essentially constant in a wide range of temperatures, while the total width grows with temperature up to $T \simeq 1.04 T_c$ beyond which it decreases sharply with increasing T. The resonance coupling is also initially constant and then begins to increase monotonically around $T \simeq T_c$. This behaviour of the total width and of the leptonic decay constant provides a strong indication that the $J$ / $ψ$ resonance might survive beyond the critical temperature for deconfinement.

## Key claim

The J/ψ resonance might survive beyond the critical temperature for deconfinement, contrary to the behavior of light-light and heavy-light quark systems. This conclusion is hedged by the fact that the QCD sum rule framework breaks down at T ≃ 1.1 Tc, beyond which the continuum threshold falls below the J/ψ mass squared, so the survival claim is an extrapolation from the observed behavior of the width and coupling up to that temperature.

## Method

Thermal Hilbert moment QCD sum rules were used to extract the temperature dependence of the J/ψ hadronic parameters. The method involves equating Hilbert moments of the hadronic spectral function (parametrized as a Breit-Wigner resonance plus a perturbative QCD continuum above a threshold s0) with the corresponding QCD expression, which includes the leading-order perturbative contribution in both the time-like (annihilation) and space-like (scattering) regions, plus the non-perturbative gluon condensate contribution from the operator product expansion. The external momentum squared Q² = 10 GeV² and the continuum threshold s0(0) = 11.64 GeV² were fixed by reproducing the experimental J/ψ mass, leptonic decay constant, and width at zero temperature.

## Key numbers

- Continuum threshold s0(T) decreases with increasing temperature and reaches the perturbative QCD threshold s0 = 4 mQ² (with mQ = 1.3 GeV) at T ≃ 1.22 Tc.
- The J/ψ mass is essentially constant up to T ≃ Tc, then decreases slightly by a few percent; the ratio MV(T)/MV(0) remains near unity.
- The total width ΓV(T)/ΓV(0) grows with temperature up to a peak at T ≃ 1.04 Tc, then decreases sharply; the zero-temperature width is ΓV(0) = 93.2 keV.
- The leptonic decay constant fV(T)/fV(0) is constant up to T ≃ 0.8 Tc, then begins to increase monotonically around T ≃ Tc; the zero-temperature value is fV(0) = 196 MeV.
- The critical temperature used for normalization is Tc ≃ 140 MeV, defined as the temperature where s0(T) begins to deviate appreciably from s0(0).
- The gluon condensate at zero temperature is ⟨0| (αs/(12π)) G² |0⟩ ≃ 5 × 10⁻³ GeV⁴.

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature QCD sum rule program, previously applied to light-light and heavy-light quark systems (Bochkarev and Shaposhnikov, 1986; Dominguez and Loewe, various years), to the heavy-heavy quark vector channel. The results contradict the standard picture where hadronic widths diverge and couplings vanish at Tc, as seen in light mesons and D-mesons. The unique behavior of the J/ψ is attributed to the importance of the space-like (scattering) perturbative QCD contribution and the gluon condensate, which are negligible in lighter systems. The findings are consistent with lattice QCD simulations showing charmonium survival above Tc, but differ from non-relativistic approaches (Mocsy and Petreczky, 2007; Bari group) that predict melting at a critical temperature, as those frameworks miss the relativistic space-like cut in the complex energy plane.
