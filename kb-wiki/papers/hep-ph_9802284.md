# Color Superconductivity and Signs of its Formation

> We study finite density QCD in an approximation in which the interaction between quarks is modelled on that induced by instantons. We sketch the mechanism by which chiral symmetry restoration at finite density occurs in this model. At all densities high enough that the chirally symmetric phase fills space, we find that color symmetry is broken by the formation of a $< qq >$ condensate of quark Cooper pairs. The formation of this color superconductor condensate lowers the energy of the system most if the up and down quark chemical potentials are equal. This suggests that the formation of such a condensate in a heavy ion collision may be accompanied by radiation of negative pions, and its decay may yield more protons than were present in the incident nuclei.

## Key claim

At densities high enough that the chirally symmetric phase fills space, color symmetry is broken by a $\langle qq \rangle$ condensate of quark Cooper pairs; the energy lowering from this color superconductor condensate is maximized when up and down quark chemical potentials are equal, suggesting that in heavy ion collisions its formation may expel down quarks and up antiquarks, leading to negative pion radiation and a final state with more protons than the incident nuclei.

## Method

The authors study finite-density QCD using a variational treatment of a two-parameter class of models with two flavors and three colors of massless quarks, where the interaction Hamiltonian is a four-fermion interaction modeled on the instanton vertex from QCD. To mimic asymptotic freedom, they multiply the momentum-space interaction by form factors $F(p)=[\Lambda^2/(p^2 + \Lambda^2)]^\nu$ for each fermion momentum, with $\nu=1/2$ or $\nu=1$ and $\Lambda$ an effective QCD cutoff scale. They fix the coupling $K$ by requiring the chiral gap $\Delta_\chi=400$ MeV at zero density, then derive gap equations for both chiral symmetry breaking and color superconductivity, including the case where up and down quarks have different chemical potentials.

## Key numbers

- Chiral gap at zero density: $\Delta_\chi = 400$ MeV (used to fix coupling $K$ for each choice of $\Lambda$ and $\nu$)
- For $\nu=1$, $\Lambda=800$ MeV, at baryon density eight times nuclear matter (quark number density $n=4.1$ per fm$^3$): $\bar\mu = 0.534$ GeV and $\Delta = 0.156$ GeV for equal up/down chemical potentials
- The superconducting gap $\Delta$ vanishes when $\delta\mu = 0.037$ GeV (for $\bar\mu$ fixed at 0.534 GeV)
- For $^{197}$Au with 315 down quarks and 276 up quarks at fixed density: $\bar\mu=0.535$ GeV, $\delta\mu=0.012$ GeV, and $\Delta$ decreases to $0.128$ GeV
- Energy density favoring the up-down symmetric phase over the gold-nucleus ratio phase at same density: $(99.8\ \text{MeV})^4 \sim 13\ \text{MeV/fm}^3$
- Condensate formation may expel one down quark and one up antiquark per 7.4 fm$^3$ of condensate

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of color superconductivity at finite density (Bailin and Love) by using an instanton-modeled interaction rather than one-gluon exchange, allowing treatment at non-asymptotic densities where interactions are not weak. The authors identify nucleons as droplets of chirally symmetric phase, similar to the MIT bag model philosophy and to results by Buballa in a Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model. The paper enables experimental searches for color superconductivity signatures in heavy ion collisions at the AGS, specifically through event-by-event analysis selecting events with unusually high density and low temperature, and suggests that including strange quarks may yield more dramatic effects.
