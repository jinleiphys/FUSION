# An explanation why the Theta+ is seen in some experiments and not in others

> To understand the whole set of positive and null data on the Theta+(1530) - production, we suggest the hypothesis that multiquark hadrons are mainly generated from many-quark states, which emerge either as short-term hadron fluctuations, or as hadron remnants in hard processes. This approach allows us to describe both non-observation of the Theta+ in current null experiments and peculiar features of its production in positive experiments. Further, we are able to propose new experiments that might be decisive for the problem of the Theta+ existence. Studies of properties and distributions of the Theta+ in such experiments can give important information on the structure of both conventional and multiquark hadrons. It would provide better insight into how QCD works.

# An explanation why the Theta+ is seen in some experiments and not in others

## Key claim

The authors hypothesize that the pentaquark Θ⁺(1530) is produced primarily from many-quark (many-parton) configurations, either short-term hadron fluctuations or hadron remnants in hard processes, rather than from few-quark fragmentation, which would explain why it appears in some experiments (those sensitive to many-quark systems) and not in others (those probing few-quark fragmentation regions). This is a qualitative hypothesis, not yet quantitative, that aims to reconcile positive and null data without rejecting the existence of the Θ⁺.

## Method

The authors review the existing experimental landscape of Θ⁺(1530) searches, separating positive data (ZEUS at HERA, SVD, DIANA, LEPS, HERMES) from null data (Belle, CLAS, COSY-TOF, SPHINX, high-energy collider experiments). For each set, they examine the kinematic conditions and production mechanisms, arguing that positive observations occur in processes where many-quark remnants or fluctuations dominate (e.g., target fragmentation in DIS, soft hadron–hadron collisions with high multiplicity), while null results come from experiments that select few-quark fragmentation regions (e.g., current fragmentation in DIS, diffraction dissociation, high-transverse-energy events at colliders). They then propose new experiments targeting hadron remnants in asymmetric collider configurations and high-multiplicity events.

## Key numbers

- The Θ⁺(1530) width extracted from DIANA data is Γ_Θ⁺ = (0.36 ± 0.11) MeV (Equation \ref{expG}).
- The Belle Collaboration provides an upper limit Γ_Θ⁺ < 0.64 MeV (Equation \ref{bel-w}), which does not exclude the DIANA value.
- The χQSA predicts Γ_Θ⁺ ≤ 15 MeV from 1/N_c expansion methods, while a light-cone Fock component approach yields Γ_Θ⁺ ≈ 0.43 MeV.
- In ZEUS DIS data, the ratio Θ⁺(1530)/Λ(1116) in the same kinematic region is about 4%, with large uncertainties, and shows a slow increase with Q² rather than a decrease.
- The Θ⁺ peak in ZEUS data at Q² > 1 GeV² appears only with the additional restriction W < 125 GeV, corresponding to x > 6.4×10⁻⁵.
- In SVD data, no Θ⁺(1530) is seen at |x_F| > 0.3 or at p_T > 1.5 GeV, indicating a very soft momentum distribution.
- The HERMES Collaboration improves the signal/background ratio for Θ⁺ observation from 1:3 to 2:1 when requiring an additional detected pion.

## Context

This work extends the question raised by Karliner and Lipkin (2004) about why the Θ⁺ is seen in some experiments and not others, but generalizes their suggestion of a specific multiquark resonance (N(2400)) to admit contributions from any many-quark state. It contrasts with the MIT bag model predictions (Jaffe, 1977) that exotic hadrons should be very broad (hundreds of MeV), and with the chiral quark soliton approach (Diakonov, Petrov, Polyakov, 1997) that predicted the Θ⁺ antidecuplet. The hypothesis enables new experimental directions: studying hadron remnants in asymmetric collider configurations (e.g., at RHIC with the BRAHMS forward spectrometer, or at HERA with a less energetic proton beam) and selecting high-multiplicity events to enhance the exotic-to-conventional hadron yield ratio.
