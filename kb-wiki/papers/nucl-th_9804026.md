# The physics of Hanbury Brown--Twiss intensity interferometry: from stars to nuclear collisions

> In the 1950's Hanbury Brown and Twiss showed that one could measure the angular sizes of astronomical radio sources and stars from correlations of signal intensities, rather than amplitudes, in independent detectors. Their subsequent correlation experiments demonstrating quantum bunching of photons in incoherent light beams were seminal in the development of quantum optics. Since that time the technique of "intensity interferometry" has become a valuable probe of high energy nuclear and particle collisions, providing information on the space-time geometry of the collision. The effect is one of the few measurements in elementary particle detection that depends on the wave mechanics of the produced particles. Here we discuss the basic physics of intensity interferometry, and its current applications in high energy nuclear physics, as well as recent applications in condensed matter and atomic physics.

## Key claim

Intensity interferometry, originally developed by Hanbury Brown and Twiss to measure stellar angular diameters, has become a valuable probe of the space-time geometry of high-energy nuclear and particle collisions, with the effect being one of the few measurements in elementary particle detection that depends on the wave mechanics of the produced particles rather than treating them as classical trajectories.

## Method

The paper presents a pedagogical review of the physics underlying Hanbury Brown-Twiss (HBT) intensity interferometry, beginning with a classical wave model of two random point sources and two detectors, then developing the quantum mechanical description using symmetrized two-particle wave functions for identical bosons. The formalism connects the two-particle correlation function C(q) to the Fourier transform of the source distribution in both space and time, and discusses how detector response functions and wavepacket overlap determine the observed correlations.

## Key numbers

- The characteristic falloff distance Δq in momentum of the correlation function for pions is of order 50 MeV/c, corresponding to a source size ℏ/Δq ∼ 4 fm
- The correlation function for pion pairs from 200 GeV/A S on Pb collisions rises only to ∼1.5-1.6 at small momentum differences, not the ideal value of 2 for a perfectly chaotic source
- The angular diameter of Sirius was measured as 0.0068 arcseconds ± 0.0005 arcseconds = 3.1×10⁻⁸ radians, at a distance of 2.7 pc
- For a 1 GeV pion (γ=7) produced within an initial radius of 10 fm travelling 10 meters to a detector, the transverse wavepacket spread is about 20 cm and the thickness grows to half a centimeter
- The coherence time for a star is τ_coh ∼ 10⁻¹⁴ sec, while the signal binning time was ∼10⁻⁸ sec, giving a probability of observing an HBT-correlated photon pair of ∼10⁻⁶
- The time lag for pions from Λ decay relative to directly produced pions is ∼9.7×10⁻¹² sec for a Λ of rapidity 3

## Context

This review extends the foundational work of Hanbury Brown and Twiss (1950s) from astronomical intensity interferometry to high-energy nuclear collisions, building on the first particle physics application by Goldhaber, Goldhaber, Lee, and Pais (1962) who studied pion angular correlations in p-anti-p collisions. The paper enables understanding of why measured correlation functions do not reach the ideal value of 2, how final state Coulomb interactions affect correlations, and how the technique is now being applied in condensed matter and atomic physics, including boson atomic beams as studied by Shimizu and collaborators.
