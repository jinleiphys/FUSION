# Influence of tubular initial conditions on two-particle correlations

> A unified description of the near-side and away-side structures observed in two-particle correlations as function of delta eta-delta phi is proposed for low to moderate transverse momentum. It is based on the combined effect of tubular initial conditions and hydrodynamical expansion.

# Influence of tubular initial conditions on two-particle correlations

## Key claim
The paper proposes that both the near-side and away-side ridge structures observed in two-particle correlations at low to moderate transverse momentum can be explained by a common hydrodynamic origin: the combined effect of longitudinal high-energy-density tubes (leftover from initial particle collisions) and transverse expansion. The claim is conditional on using event-by-event 3+1 hydrodynamics with tubular initial conditions, and the agreement with data is described as qualitative rather than quantitative.

## Method
The authors used event-by-event 3+1 hydrodynamics within the NeXSPheRIO approach, where initial conditions with tubular structures were generated using the NeXus code and then evolved hydrodynamically. To isolate the mechanism, they also studied a simplified 2+1 hydrodynamic model with a single tube embedded in an averaged background, assuming boost-invariant longitudinal expansion and freeze-out at constant temperature. For non-central collisions, they summed contributions from all tube positions and subtracted elliptic flow to extract the two-particle correlation.

## Key numbers
- Peak spacing in the single-particle angular distribution from a single tube is approximately Δφ ∼ 2 (radians), independent of transverse momentum value.
- For central Au+Au collisions at 200 GeV A, the NeXSPheRIO approach reproduces both near-side and away-side structures in two-particle correlations.
- The two-particle correlation from a single tube shows a large central peak at Δφ = 0 (near-side ridge) and two smaller peaks at Δφ ∼ ±2 (double-hump away-side ridge).

## Context
This work extends the event-by-event hydrodynamic approach developed by the SPheRIO collaboration since 2001, and is contemporaneous with similar efforts by Petersen et al. (2007), Werner et al. (2010), and Schenke et al. (2010). The paper provides a mechanistic explanation for the ridge structures observed at RHIC and the LHC, showing that each tube produces a characteristic "two-horn" emission pattern. The authors note that while triangular flow (v₃) from triangular initial conditions has been suggested as an alternative explanation by other researchers, their one-tube model demonstrates that v₃ and the triangularity parameter ε₃ are not simply proportional on an event-by-event basis, leaving this as an open question for further study.
