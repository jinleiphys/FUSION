# Probing the equation of state of neutron star matter with gravitational waves from binary inspirals in light of GW170817: a brief review

> Neutron stars are unique testbeds for exploring the physics of strongly interacting matter in extreme regimes of density, temperature, and isospin that are not accessible anywhere else in the universe. The nature of neutron star matter can now be probed with gravitational-waves (GWs) from binary driven by nonlinear gravity, where phenomena such as tidal effects lead to characteristic matter-dependent GW signatures. We focus here on the dominant tidal GW imprints that were most relevant for the event GW170817. We review the role of the tidal deformability parameter, its definition, computation, and relation to the equation of state. We briefly discuss the implications of GW170817, representing the first-ever constraints on tidal deformability from GW data. Finally, we outline opportunities and challenges for probing subatomic physics with GWs, as the measurements will become more precise and will probe a diversity of the binary neutron star population in the coming years.

## Key claim

This review argues that the tidal deformability parameter, extracted from gravitational-wave signals of binary neutron star inspirals, provides a direct probe of the neutron star equation of state, but the authors emphasize that the current constraints from GW170817 represent only the first step, with significant challenges remaining for precise measurements due to detector sensitivity limitations at high frequencies and the need to disentangle tidal effects from other parameters.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework connecting neutron star microphysics to gravitational-wave observables, which involves solving the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for the equilibrium stellar structure and then solving linearized perturbation equations for the quadrupolar tidal response. The tidal deformability parameter lambda is computed by matching interior and exterior solutions of the perturbed Einstein field equations at the neutron star surface, yielding an algebraic expression for the dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda in terms of the stellar compactness and the logarithmic derivative of the metric perturbation at the surface. The authors then discuss how different equations of state, ranging from purely nucleonic models to strange quark matter and hybrid models, produce distinct mass-radius relations and tidal deformabilities that can be compared with gravitational-wave observations.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear saturation density is rho_sat approximately 2.8 x 10^14 g/cm^3, with neutron star interiors expected to reach 5-10 times this density
- A simple Fermi Gas treatment of neutrons yields a maximum neutron star mass of 0.7 solar masses, while recent measurements show neutron stars reaching about 2 solar masses
- The tidal deformability vanishes for nonspinning black holes (lambda_black_hole = 0)
- The dimensionless tidal deformability Lambda is defined as Lambda = (lambda * c^10) / (G^4 * M^5)
- The tidal Love number k_2 relates to lambda through lambda = (2/(3G)) * k_2 * R^5
- The GW frequency of 1 kHz marks the point beyond which current gravitational-wave detectors become less sensitive
- The authors used an arbitrary cutoff of 1.95 solar masses for the maximum mass when selecting equations of state for their analysis

## Context

This review extends the theoretical framework developed by Flanagan and Hinderer (2008) and Hinderer (2009) for computing tidal deformabilities, and builds upon the matched asymptotic expansion methods of Flanagan (1997) and Racine (2004). The work is directly motivated by the GW170817 event detected by LIGO and Virgo, which provided the first constraints on tidal deformability from gravitational-wave data. The review enables future work by outlining how upcoming observations with improved detectors and a diverse population of binary neutron star systems will allow more precise measurements of the equation of state, complementing X-ray observations from NICER and other facilities.
