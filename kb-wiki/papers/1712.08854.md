# The hyperons in the massive neutron star PSR J0348+0432

> Whether the massive neutron star PSR J0348+0432 can become a hyperon star is examined in the framework of the relativistic mean field theory by adjusting the hyperon coupling constants. It is found that at the central baryon number density of the massive neutron star PSR J0348+0432, the relative particle number density of hyperons is smaller than those of neutrons and so it can not change into a hyperon star. In its center, it is mainly composed of $Λ$, $Ξ^{-}$ and a few $Ξ^{0}$. We also found that for the neutron star with a maximum mass of 1.4 M$_{\odot}$, it can change into a hyperon star and the hyperon star transition density is 0.668 fm$^{-3}$, at which the hyperons are only composed of $Λ$. At its center, the hyperons are also only composed of $Λ$ and the ratio of the hyperons is a little larger than that of the neutrons. This illustrates that the NS1.4M$_{\odot}$ has just changed into a hyperon star.

# The hyperons in the massive neutron star PSR J0348+0432

## Key claim
Using the relativistic mean field theory with the GL85 nucleon coupling set and hyperon coupling constants fitted to experimental well depths, the massive neutron star PSR J0348+0432 (mass 2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙) cannot become a hyperon star because at its central baryon number density the relative particle number density of hyperons remains smaller than that of neutrons. In contrast, a lower-mass neutron star of about 1.4 M⊙ can change into a hyperon star under the same theoretical framework.

## Method
The author employed the relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with the GL85 nucleon coupling set (saturation density ρ0 = 0.145 fm⁻³, binding energy B/A = 15.95 MeV, compression modulus K = 285 MeV, charge symmetry coefficient asym = 36.8 MeV, effective mass m*/m = 0.77). Hyperon coupling constants were determined by fitting to experimental well depths (UΛ(N) = −30 MeV, UΣ(N) = +40 MeV, UΞ(N) = −18 MeV) with SU(6) symmetry constraints for ρ-meson couplings. The Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations were solved to obtain neutron star masses and radii for 20 parameter sets, with sets NO.19 and NO.20 yielding the mass of PSR J0348+0432.

## Key numbers
- PSR J0348+0432 mass: 2.01 ± 0.04 M⊙ (from Antoniadis 2013, cited in the paper)
- Central baryon number density for PSR J0348+0432: 0.9 fm⁻³ (parameter set NO.19) or 0.925 fm⁻³ (parameter set NO.20)
- Hyperon star transition density for PSR J0348+0432: 1.002 fm⁻³ (NO.19) or 1.112 fm⁻³ (NO.20), both larger than the central density
- Hyperon star transition density for the 1.4 M⊙ neutron star (NS1.4M⊙): 0.668 fm⁻³ (parameter set NO.04)
- Central baryon number density for NS1.4M⊙: 0.696 fm⁻³ (parameter set NO.04)
- At the hyperon star transition density of NS1.4M⊙ (0.668 fm⁻³), neutrons and hyperons each have relative particle number density 44.3%, with hyperons composed only of Λ
- At the center of NS1.4M⊙ (0.696 fm⁻³), relative particle number densities: neutrons 42.6%, hyperons 46.4% (only Λ)
- At the center of PSR J0348+0432 (NO.19, 0.9 fm⁻³): neutrons 41%, hyperons 32.3% (Λ 11.5%, Ξ⁻ 18.7%, Ξ⁰ 2.1%)
- At the center of PSR J0348+0432 (NO.20, 0.925 fm⁻³): neutrons 44.8%, hyperons 30.9% (Λ 19.3%, Ξ⁻ 11.6%)
- Radius for PSR J0348+0432: 12.169 km (NO.19) or 11.967 km (NO.20)
- Radius for NS1.4M⊙: 13.245 km (NO.04)

## Context
This work extends the study of hyperon composition in neutron stars using RMF theory, following earlier calculations by Glendenning (1985, 1991, 1997) and the author's own prior work (Zhao 2012). The paper specifically addresses whether the massive pulsar PSR J0348+0432, discovered by Antoniadis (2013), can contain sufficient hyperons to become a hyperon star. The finding that this 2.01 M⊙ star cannot become a hyperon star contrasts with the behavior of lower-mass neutron stars (1.4 M⊙), which can undergo the transition. The results depend sensitively on the choice of hyperon coupling constants and the GL85 nucleon parameter set, and the author notes that other parameter sets (such as CZ11 and GL97) cannot reproduce the observed mass when hyperons are included. This work enables future studies to refine hyperon-nucleon interactions and explore whether alternative coupling choices or extensions beyond RMF theory might allow hyperon star formation in massive neutron stars.
