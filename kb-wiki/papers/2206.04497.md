# Spectrum of light nuclei in a finite volume

> Lattice quantum chromodynamics calculations of multi-baryon systems with physical quark masses would start a new age of ab initio predictions in nuclear physics. Performed on a finite grid, such calculations demand extrapolation of their finite volume numerical results to free-space physical quantities. Such extraction of the physical information can be carried out fitting effective field theories (EFTs) directly to the finite-volume results or utilizing the Lüscher free-space formula or its generalizations for extrapolating the lattice data to infinite volume. To understand better the effect of periodic boundary conditions on the binding energy of few nucleon systems we explore here light nuclei with physical masses in a finite box and in free space. The stochastic variational method is used to solve the few-body systems. Substantial optimizations of the method are introduced to enable efficient calculations in a periodic box. With the optimized code, we perform accurate calculations of light nuclei $A \le 4$ within leading order pionless EFT. Using Lüscher formula for the two-body system, and its generalization for 3- and 4-body systems, we examine the box effect and explore possible limitations of these formulas for the considered nuclear systems.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Lüscher formula and its few-body generalization capture most of the finite-volume effect on binding energies of light nuclei (A ≤ 4) within leading-order pionless EFT, but these asymptotic formulas show large deviations for small boxes (L ≤ 10 fm) and require careful treatment of sub-leading corrections and fitting procedures for accurate extraction of free-space quantities.

## Method

The authors solve the few-body Schrödinger equation for deuteron, triton, and helium-4 using the stochastic variational method (SVM) in free space and in periodic boxes of various sizes (L ∈ [2,70] fm). They introduce substantial optimizations to the Box-SVM code: using relative coordinates to separate center-of-mass motion, implementing dynamical accuracy control on the summation over periodic images, and leveraging a pre-trained free-space basis. The interaction is described by leading-order pionless EFT with two-body and three-body contact interactions, fitted to experimental deuteron binding energy, neutron-neutron scattering length, and triton binding energy.

## Key numbers

- Deuteron binding energy used for fitting: B_d = 2.2246 MeV
- Neutron-neutron spin-singlet scattering length used for fitting: a_0 = -18.95 fm
- Triton binding energy used for fitting: B_t = 8.482 MeV
- Binding momenta for one-nucleon separation: κ_2 = 0.232 fm^{-1}, κ_3 = 0.449 fm^{-1}, κ_4 = 0.931 - 0.814 fm^{-1} (for cutoff Λ between 1 fm^{-1} and 10 fm^{-1})
- Deuteron ANC at Λ=1.25 fm^{-1}: A_2 = 1.30; experimental value: 1.2902(65)
- Triton ANC at Λ=1.25 fm^{-1}: A_3 = 1.82; experimental value: 2.19(2)
- Helium-4 ANC at Λ=1.25 fm^{-1}: A_4 = 4.52; experimental range: 4.11-6.42
- Free-space binding energies at Λ=1 fm^{-1}: deuteron 2.2246 MeV, triton 8.482 MeV, helium-4 32.48 MeV
- Using Lüscher formula with boxes L={6,8,10} fm yields extracted binding energies: deuteron 2.0(8) MeV, triton 9.4(9) MeV, helium-4 32.4(9) MeV
- For deuteron ANC extraction with 2% precision: L ≳ 40 fm using nearest neighbors only; L ≳ 26 fm including next-to-nearest neighbors

## Context

This work extends the approach of Eliyahu et al. (2020), who used pionless EFT to reproduce lattice QCD results at m_π=806 MeV, to physical pion mass and examines the limitations of the Lüscher formalism (Lüscher, 1986, 1991) and its few-body generalization by König and Lee (2018) for extracting free-space binding energies. The results enable benchmarking of extrapolation methods for future lattice QCD calculations at physical quark masses, which are becoming feasible as shown by recent HAL QCD collaboration results (2019-2021). The paper also provides a computational tool (optimized Box-SVM) that can be used to fit EFT low-energy constants directly to finite-volume lattice data, as an alternative to the Lüscher approach.
