# Nuclear pairing: new perspectives

> Nuclear pairing correlations are known to play an important role in various single-particle and collective aspects of nuclear structure. After the first idea by A. Bohr, B. Mottelson and D. Pines on similarity of nuclear pairing to electron superconductivity, S.T. Belyaev gave a thorough analysis of the manifestations of pairing in complex nuclei. The current revival of interest in nuclear pairing is connected to the shift of modern nuclear physics towards nuclei far from stability; many loosely bound nuclei are particle-stable only due to the pairing. The theoretical methods borrowed from macroscopic superconductivity turn out to be insufficient for finite systems as nuclei, in particular for the cases of weak pairing and proximity of continuum states. We suggest a simple numerical procedure of exact solution of the nuclear pairing problem and discuss the physical features of this complete solution. We show also how the continuum states can be naturally included in the consideration bridging the gap between the structure and reactions. The path from coherent pairing to chaos and thermalization and perspectives of new theoretical approaches based on the full solution of pairing are discussed.

# Nuclear pairing: new perspectives

## Key claim

The paper argues that standard BCS and Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov methods, borrowed from macroscopic superconductivity, are insufficient for finite nuclear systems, particularly for weakly paired nuclei and those near the continuum, and that an exact numerical solution of the pairing problem, while numerically simple, eliminates the drawbacks of mean-field approximations and reveals physics (such as pairing correlations persisting below the BCS critical coupling strength) that the approximate methods miss.

## Method

The authors formulate the pairing Hamiltonian in a restricted single-particle space with Kramers-degenerate orbitals and exploit the SU(2) quasispin algebra for each j-subshell, where seniority quantum numbers are conserved. They construct the Hamiltonian matrix in a basis of states with fixed partial seniorities and various occupancy numbers, then perform direct Fock-space diagonalization (using Lanczos iteration for larger systems) to obtain the exact spectrum. The method is applied to a chain of tin isotopes (valence space between neutron magic numbers 50 and 82) and calcium isotopes (fp-shell valence space), using single-particle energies from experiment and pairing matrix elements from G-matrix calculations.

## Key numbers

- For ¹¹⁴Sn, the exact pairing solution gives ground state energy E = -86.308 MeV, while BCS gives E = -85.938 MeV (BCS underpredicts binding by about 0.4 MeV).
- For ⁴⁸Ca, the exact solution gives E = -71.215 MeV, while BCS gives E = -70.591 MeV (BCS underpredicts binding by about 0.6 MeV).
- The critical coupling strength for the BCS phase transition is given by G_c * ν_F = 1, where ν_F is the density of single-particle states at the Fermi energy.
- For the ladder model (ten equally spaced double-degenerate orbitals, half-occupied with N=10 particles), the BCS result deviates significantly from the exact solution near the phase transition around G = 0.5.
- In ¹¹⁴Sn, the BCS occupation numbers for the d₅/₂ level are N_j = 4.14 (BCS) versus N_j = 4.46 (exact); for the g₇/₂ level, N_j = 6.71 (BCS) versus N_j = 6.96 (exact).
- The BCS pairing gaps for ¹¹⁴Sn range from Δ_j = 1.25 MeV (h₁₁/₂) to Δ_j = 1.43 MeV (d₃/₂ and d₅/₂).

## Context

This work extends the exact pairing solution approach of Richardson (1963) and the particle-number-conserving recursive method of Dang and Klein (1966, 1968), while directly contrasting with the BCS theory of Bohr, Mottelson, and Pines (1958) and Belyaev (1959). The paper addresses the inadequacy of mean-field methods for nuclei far from stability, where weak pairing and proximity to the continuum become important, a problem noted by Benn (2002) and Broglia (2000) in the context of drip-line nuclei such as ¹¹Li. The exact solution enables future inclusion of other residual interactions beyond pairing, incorporation of continuum states bridging structure and reactions, and study of the transition from coherent pairing to chaos and thermalization.
