# Gyroid Phase in Nuclear Pasta

> Nuclear matter is considered to be inhomogeneous at subnuclear densities that are realized in supernova cores and neutron star crusts, and the structures of nuclear matter change from spheres to cylinders, slabs, cylindrical holes and spherical holes as the density increases. In this letter, we discuss other possible structures, that is, gyroid and double-diamond morphologies, which are periodic bicontinuous structures discovered in a block copolymer. Utilizing the compressible liquid drop model, we evaluate their surface and Coulomb energies and show that there is a chance of gyroid appearance near the transition point from a cylinder to a slab. This interesting analogy between nuclear and polymer systems is not merely qualitative. The volume fraction at the phase transition is also similar for the two systems. Although the five shapes listed initially have been long thought to be the only major constituents of so-called nuclear pasta at subnuclear densities, our findings imply that this may not be the case and suggest that more detailed studies on nuclear pasta including the gyroid phase are needed.

## Key claim

The gyroid (G) and double-diamond (D) morphologies, which are periodic bicontinuous structures known in block copolymers, may appear in nuclear pasta near the transition point from cylinders to slabs, but the gyroid phase is not the most stable phase for any density; it could exist only as a metastable state or thermal fluctuation due to the very small energy difference (approximately 0.2 keV/fm^3) from the cylinder and slab phases.

## Method

The authors employed the compressible liquid drop model (CLDM), a phenomenological approach also used in earlier nuclear pasta studies, to evaluate the surface and Coulomb energies of gyroid and double-diamond morphologies approximated by level surfaces. The total energy density in a unit cell was minimized with respect to the cell size and then compared across shapes by computing the relative energy density F(u, shape) = g(u, shape)^(2/3) w_C(u, shape)^(1/3), which depends only on the volume fraction u and the nuclear shape. The shape giving the lowest energy was identified for each volume fraction, and the energy differences were quantified using parameters appropriate for supernova matter (saturation density n_0 = 0.165 fm^-3, surface tension sigma = 0.73 MeV fm^-2, proton fraction x^in = 0.3).

## Key numbers

- The gyroid phase has a relative energy density very close to those of the cylinder and slab phases at the transition point from cylinder to slab, which occurs at volume fraction u = 0.35.
- The difference in average energy density between the gyroid phase and the most stable phase is approximately 0.2 keV fm^-3 at n = 0.35 n_0 (u = 0.35).
- This energy difference corresponds to about 3 keV per nucleon at n = 0.35 n_0.
- The gyroid phase becomes bicontinuous only for u > 0.0354; the double-diamond phase becomes bicontinuous only for u > 0.161.
- The gyroid hole phase is bicontinuous only for u < 0.965; the double-diamond hole phase is bicontinuous only for u < 0.839.
- The saturation density used is n_0 = 0.165 fm^-3.
- The surface tension used is sigma = 0.73 MeV fm^-2.
- The proton fraction used is x^in = 0.3.

## Context

This work extends the conventional picture of nuclear pasta, which since Ravenhall (1983) and Hashimoto (1984) has considered only five shapes (spheres, cylinders, slabs, cylindrical holes, spherical holes) at subnuclear densities. The authors draw an analogy to block copolymer systems where gyroid and double-diamond morphologies were discovered experimentally (Bates and Fredrickson, 1999). The results contradict an earlier calculation by Matsuzaki (2006) that found a much larger energy difference for the double-diamond phase, because that work did not minimize the total energy with respect to the cell size. The findings suggest that future studies of nuclear pasta, including quantum molecular dynamics simulations (Watanabe et al., 2005; Sonoda et al., 2008), should consider the gyroid phase as a possible metastable or thermally fluctuating structure, with potential implications for supernova explosions, proto-neutron star cooling, and pulsar glitches.
