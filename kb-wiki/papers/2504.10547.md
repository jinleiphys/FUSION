# Beyond Poincaré Stresses: A Modern Quantum Field Theory Take on Hydrogen's Electromagnetic Mass

> We revisit the longstanding electromagnetic mass problem from a modern quantum field theory perspective. Focusing on a system of two widely separated hydrogen atoms, one in an excited $nS$ state and the other in the ground $1S$ state, we isolate the electromagnetic contribution to the electron's total linear momentum by comparing the full energy-momentum tensor with the predictions of a point-like bound state model. Our analysis reveals that the leading perturbative correction introduces a factor $4/3$, which, along with subsequent corrections, indicates that the effective electromagnetic mass deviates from the conventional relation $E/c^2$. This discrepancy is attributed to the intrinsic nonlocality of the electromagnetic field, rather than to additional compensating mechanisms such as Poincaré stresses. We further contrast our quantum field theory results with the highly accurate predictions of the Schrödinger equation, which, despite neglecting higher-order terms, achieves an average error on the order of $10^{-5}\%$. Attempts to improve this accuracy via perturbative inclusion of the self-interaction of the electron's wave function instead increase the error, prompting a re-examination of the underlying perturbative assumptions. Our findings suggest that a non-perturbative treatment of the tree-level action may be required to fully capture the dynamics of bound states in quantum field theory.

# Beyond Poincaré Stresses: A Modern Quantum Field Theory Take on Hydrogen's Electromagnetic Mass

## Key claim

The leading perturbative correction to the electron's linear momentum in a bound hydrogen atom introduces a factor of 4/3, indicating that the effective electromagnetic mass deviates from the conventional relation E/c² due to the intrinsic nonlocality of the electromagnetic field rather than from compensating mechanisms such as Poincaré stresses; however, this result is obtained under a tree-level truncation of the quantum field theory and the authors suggest a non-perturbative treatment may be required to fully capture bound-state dynamics.

## Method

The authors model the hydrogen atom as a bound state of a complex scalar electron field, a proton modeled as a uniformly charged sphere with radius a, and the electromagnetic field, truncating the effective action to zero-loop (tree-level) order. They consider a system of two widely separated hydrogen atoms, one in an excited nS state moving to the right and one in the ground 1S state moving to the left with equal speed, and compute the total linear momentum by integrating the T₀ᵢ components of the energy-momentum tensor in the boosted frame. This momentum is compared with the relativistic point-like bound state formula, and the discrepancy is analyzed through perturbative expansion of the wave function, energy, and potential in a small parameter ε that controls the electron's self-interaction.

## Key numbers

- The leading-order perturbative calculation yields a factor of 4/3 in the relation between electromagnetic momentum and energy, reproducing the classical Thomson result for the effective electromagnetic mass m_em = (4/3)(E/c²).
- The Schrödinger equation, despite neglecting higher-order terms, achieves an average error on the order of 10⁻⁵% when compared to experimental hydrogen spectra.
- The theoretical mass difference between excited nS and ground 1S states, normalized to the 12S state, yields an average error of (0.13 ± 0.04)% when compared with experimental data from the NIST Atomic Spectra Database.
- The first-order energy corrections for the electron are: E₁⁽¹⁾ = -5q²/(32πa₀*), E₂⁽¹⁾ = -77q²/(2048πa₀*), and E₃⁽¹⁾ = -17q²/(1024πa₀*), where a₀* is the modified Bohr radius.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analysis (Exirifard, 2024) that established a framework for electromagnetic mass in free electrons using localization leading to nonpoint-like behavior, now applied to electrons bound in hydrogen atoms. The paper revisits the longstanding 4/3 problem originally identified by Thomson (1897) and later addressed by Abraham (1903), Lorentz (1909), Poincaré (1905), Fermi (1923), Feynman (1964), Rohrlich (1965), and Schwinger (1973), among others. The findings suggest that a non-perturbative treatment of the tree-level action may be necessary to fully capture bound-state dynamics in quantum field theory, and that attempts to improve the Schrödinger equation's accuracy by perturbatively including the electron's self-interaction instead increase the error, prompting re-examination of the underlying perturbative assumptions.
