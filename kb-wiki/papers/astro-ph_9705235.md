# Signal of Quark Deconfinement in the Timing Structure of Pulsar Spin-Down

> The conversion of nuclear matter to quark matter in the core of a rotating neutron star alters its moment of inertia. Hence the epoch over which conversion takes place will be signaled in the spin-down "signal_prl.tex" 581 lines, 22203 characters characteristics of pulsars. We find that an observable called the braking index should be easily measurable during the transition epoch and can have a value far removed (by orders of magnitude) from the canonical value of three expected for magnetic dipole radiation, and may have either sign. The duration of the transition epoch is governed by the slow loss of angular momentum to radiation and is further prolonged by the reduction in the moment of inertia caused by the phase change which can even introduce an era of spin-up. We estimate that about one in a hundred pulsars may be passing through this phase. The phenomenon is analogous to ``bachbending'' observed in the moment of inertia of rotating nuclei observed in the 1970's, which also signaled a change in internal structure with changing spin.

## Key claim

The conversion of nuclear matter to quark matter in a rotating neutron star's core, paced by slow angular-momentum loss, can produce a braking index far from the canonical value of three (by orders of magnitude and of either sign) during the transition epoch; the authors estimate that about one in a hundred pulsars may be passing through this phase, but note that the calculation does not predict the rotational frequency or stellar mass at which a phase transition will occur, only what the signal could be if conditions are attained.

## Method

The authors solve the equations for rotating stellar configurations in general relativity for a sequence of stars with the same baryon number but different rotational frequencies, using an expression for the moment of inertia that incorporates frame-dragging, centrifugal flattening, and internal constitutional changes (Glendenning and Weber, 1992-1993). Nuclear matter is described by a covariant mean-field theory with parameters constrained by nuclear matter properties and hypernuclei, while quark matter is treated in the MIT bag model with three light flavors (u, d, s) and a bag constant of B^(1/4)=180 MeV.

## Key numbers

- Braking index can deviate from the canonical value of 3 by orders of magnitude and may have either sign during the transition epoch.
- The frequency interval over which the braking index is anomalous (less than zero or greater than six) is approximately ΔΩ ≈ 100 rad/s.
- The duration of the anomalous braking-index epoch is ΔT ≈ 10^5 years, based on a typical period derivative of Ṗ ∼ 10^(-16) s/s.
- Spin-up would last for about 1/6 of the anomalous epoch (roughly 1.7×10^4 years).
- An estimated 1/100 pulsars (about 7 out of more than 700 known) may be passing through the transition epoch.
- The stellar model has a nonrotating counterpart mass of 1.42 M⊙; the pure quark matter core forms below I ∼ 107 km^3 (Ω ≈ 1400 rad/s).
- Nuclear matter parameters: binding energy B/A = -16.3 MeV, saturation density ρ = 0.153 fm^(-3), compression modulus K = 300 MeV, symmetry energy coefficient a_sym = 32.5 MeV, nucleon effective mass at saturation m*_sat = 0.7m.
- Quark masses: m_u = m_d = 0, m_s = 150 MeV; bag constant B^(1/4) = 180 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the concept of "backbending" in nuclear rotational bands (Mottelson and Valatin, 1960; Johnson et al., 1972; Stephens et al., 1972) to neutron stars, where a phase transition from nuclear to quark matter produces analogous anomalous changes in the moment of inertia. The authors note that only four braking indices were known at the time of writing, and that the signal differs from the minuscule spin-up of pulsar glitches (ΔI/I ∼ 10^(-6) or smaller). The work enables observational searches for quark matter in neutron star cores by identifying pulsars with anomalous braking indices, large second derivatives of frequency, or spin-up in isolated pulsars (to avoid confusion with accretion spin-up).
