# Pygmy resonances and symmetry energy

> I present a brief summary of the first three decades of studies of pygmy resonances in nuclei and their relation to the symmetry energy of nuclear matter. I discuss the first experiments and theories dedicated to study the electromagnetic response in halo nuclei and how a low energy peak was initially identified as a candidate for the pygmy resonance. This is followed by the description of a collective state in medium heavy and heavy nuclei which was definitely identified as a pygmy resonance. The role of the slope parameter of the symmetry energy in determining the properties of neutron stars is stressed. The theoretical and experimental information collected on pygmy resonances, neutron skins, and the numerous correlations found with the slope parameter is briefly reviewed.

# Pygmy resonances and symmetry energy

## Key claim

The paper argues that pygmy dipole resonances (PDR) are a real collective phenomenon in neutron-rich nuclei, but the low-energy peak observed in halo nuclei may alternatively be explained as a direct transition to the continuum rather than a distinct resonance, with the distinction remaining ambiguous for light systems. The slope parameter L of the symmetry energy is correlated with PDR properties and neutron skin thickness, but L remains poorly constrained (0–150 MeV) across theoretical models.

## Method

The author reviews the first three decades of experimental and theoretical studies of pygmy resonances, covering two-body and three-body models, hydrodynamical models (Goldhaber-Teller and Steinwedel-Jensen), random phase approximation (RPA) calculations, and time-dependent superfluid local density approximation (TDSLDA) simulations. Experimental approaches discussed include Coulomb breakup, nucleon knockout reactions, electron scattering, and photo-absorption measurements, with emphasis on how the dipole polarizability extracted from these experiments correlates with the symmetry energy slope parameter.

## Key numbers

- The two-neutron separation energy in 11Li is only about 300 keV, explaining the narrow momentum distribution of 9Li fragments in nucleon removal reactions.
- For halo nuclei, the two-body model predicts the E1 response peak at excitation energy E = 8S/5, where S is the separation energy.
- For 11Li with S = 0.3 MeV, a = 1–2 fm, and R = 3 fm, the hydrodynamical model yields E_PDR = 1–2 MeV.
- The TDSLDA calculations for 238U + 238U collisions show considerable electromagnetic strength at low energies around E_x ~ 7 MeV, attributed to PDR excitation.
- The bulk symmetry energy J is approximately 30 MeV from fits to nuclear properties.
- The slope parameter L varies across Skyrme models from 9.91 MeV (SIII) to 129 MeV (SKI5), with other values including 19.7 MeV (SKP), 33.2 MeV (SKX), 39.9 MeV (HFB9), 48.2 MeV (SLY5), 67.1 MeV (SKXS20), and 79.1 MeV (SKO).
- The incompressibility K0 ranges from 201 MeV (SKP) to 355 MeV (SIII) across the same Skyrme models.
- For a hypothetical halo nucleus with S = 100 keV, E_e = 10 MeV, and effective charge e, the electro-disintegration cross section is 25 mb.
- Measurements of dipole polarizability α_D show a large variation of 20–30% across experiments.

## Context

This review extends the earlier work on giant resonances (Baldwin and Klaiber 1947; Bothe and Gentner 1937; Migdal 1944) and the first identification of pygmy resonances (Bartholomew 1961; Brzosko et al. 1969). It builds on the three-fluid model of Mohan et al. (1971) and the hydrodynamical models of Goldhaber and Teller (1948) and Steinwedel and Jensen (1950). The paper connects PDR studies to neutron star structure via the TOV equations and the symmetry energy slope parameter L, which is also correlated with neutron skin measurements (Brown 2000; Piekarewicz et al. 2011; Horowitz and Piekarewicz 2001; Typel and Brown 2001; Furnstahl 2002). The work enables future constraints on the nuclear equation of state from Coulomb excitation experiments, electron-ion colliders (Antipov et al. 2011; Suda et al. 2012), and fragmentation reactions (Aumann et al. 2017).
