# The Rise of High Energy Neutrino Astronomy at Horizon

> High Energy Neutrino Astronomy energy windows may be ruled by a new Upward and Horizontal Tau Air-Showers (UpTaus, HorTaus) detectors (scintillators and optical arrays) located a few kilometers beyond facing High Mountains Chains, or located above their top or flying on Planes, on Balloons and on Satellites facing downward to the Earth Crust Horizon. While looking downward to the Earth seeking for Upward and Horizontal Tau Air-Showers the same optical detectors may often capture cosmic rays Cherenkov lights reflected or diffused by downward Shower cosmic rays hitting the Earth soil (a sea,a lake, ice lands, desert or ground). These new detectors may be built up as a circular hybrid crown array at high quota facing to horizon in correlation with present largest telescopes, like Magic or Shalon or ASHRA ones, if able to look below the Earth edge. The UpTaus and HorTau Astronomy at 10^15 eV up to 10^19 eV energy windows may test the largest primary neutrino fluxes in Z-Burst neutrino model needed to solve the GZK puzzle or even the smaller but inevitable GZK neutrino flux secondary of the same GZK cut-off. These HorTus signals might well be observed in future EUSO experiment.

## Key claim

High energy neutrino astronomy may be enabled by detecting upward and horizontal tau air-showers (UpTaus, HorTaus) using optical detectors located on mountains, balloons, planes, or satellites looking downward at the Earth's horizon, but the actual detection rates depend on the assumed neutrino flux models (Z-Burst or GZK secondary) and the specific detector height and duty cycle.

## Method

The paper calculates the expected event rates and energy thresholds for upward tau air-showers (UPTAUs) and horizontal tau air-showers (HORTAUs) produced by ultra-high energy tau neutrinos interacting in the Earth crust or atmosphere, then decaying in flight. These estimates are applied to proposed detector configurations at various altitudes (2, 5, 25, 500 km) and to the EUSO satellite experiment, using the Z-Burst neutrino model and GZK secondary neutrino fluxes as input. The effective target volumes for tau production are computed for rock, water, and air, accounting for Earth opacity, tau interaction lengths, and detector duty cycles.

## Key numbers

- Relic neutrino mass assumed in Z-Burst model: \(m_{\nu} \simeq 0.4\) eV (or at least \(m_{\nu} \geq 0.1\) eV)
- Resonant incoming neutrino energy for Z-Burst: \(E_{\nu} = 10^{22}\) eV
- Energy window for UpTau and HorTau astronomy: \(10^{15}\) eV up to \(10^{19}\) eV
- Effective target mass for HORTAUs seen by EUSO at \(E_{\nu_{\tau}} = 1.2 \cdot 10^{19}\) eV: \(\simeq 2360\) km\(^3\) (ring volume on sea)
- UPTAUs effective target volume for EUSO in rock: \(1.5 \cdot 10^{-1}\) km\(^3\); in water: \(5.5 \cdot 10^{-2}\) km\(^3\)
- Minimal detectable neutrino fluence for PeV neutrinos by EUSO: \(\Phi_{\nu_{\tau}} \geq 10^2\) eV cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\)
- Expected UHECR event rate above \(3 \cdot 10^{19}\) eV: \(\sim 2 \cdot 10^3\) events/year; \(\sim 6000\) events in 3 years
- Horizontal UHECR shower fraction: \(7.45\%\) (\(\sim 150\) events/year, \(\sim 450\) in 3 years)
- Critical angle for horizontal hadronic shower at 44 km height: \(\theta_h = 6.7^\circ\) below horizon
- EUSO satellite height assumed: 400 km; telescope opening angle: \(30^\circ\); atmosphere area under inspection: \(\sim 1.5 \cdot 10^5\) km\(^2\)
- Neutrino flux at \(10^{22}\) eV for Z-Burst model: \(\Phi_{\nu} \simeq 10^3\) eV cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) sr\(^{-1}\) (AGASA-compatible) or \(\Phi_{\nu} \simeq 2 \cdot 10^2\) eV cm\(^{-2}\) s\(^{-1}\) sr\(^{-1}\) (HIRES-compatible)

## Context

This work extends the concept of tau neutrino astronomy using mountain targets and Earth-skimming neutrinos, previously developed by Fargion (1999-2002), Hou and Huang (2002), and Bertou et al. (2002). It proposes that upward tau air-showers may already have been observed as terrestrial gamma flashes by the BATSE experiment (1991-2000), and that future experiments like EUSO could detect both Z-Burst model neutrinos and the inevitable GZK secondary neutrinos from photopion production of ultra-high energy cosmic rays. The paper enables quantitative comparison between space-based tau neutrino detectors and underground km\(^3\) neutrino telescopes (AMANDA, Baikal, ANTARES, NESTOR, NEMO, IceCube).
