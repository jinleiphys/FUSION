# Calculations of antiproton-nucleus quasi-bound states using the Paris $\bar{N}N$ potential

> An optical potential constructed using the ${\bar p}N$ scattering amplitudes derived from the 2009 version of the Paris ${\bar N}N$ potential is applied in calculations of ${\bar p}$ quasi-bound states in selected nuclei across the periodic table. A proper self-consistent procedure for treating energy dependence of the amplitudes in a nucleus appears crucial for evaluating ${\bar p}$ binding energies and widths. Particular attention is paid to the role of $P$-wave amplitudes. While the $P$-wave potential nearly does not affect calculated ${\bar p}$ binding energies, it reduces considerably the corresponding widths. The Paris $S$-wave potential supplemented by a phenomenological $P$-wave term yields in dynamical calculations ${\bar p}$ binding energies $B_{\bar p}\approx 200$ MeV and widths $Γ_{\bar p}\sim~200 - 230$ MeV, which is very close to the values obtained within the RMF model consistent with ${\bar p}$-atom data.

## Key claim

The Paris $\bar{N}N$ potential, when used to construct an optical potential for antiproton-nucleus quasi-bound states, produces binding energies and widths that are very close to those obtained within the RMF model consistent with antiproton-atom data, but only when the $S$-wave potential is supplemented by a phenomenological $P$-wave term; the Paris $P$-wave amplitudes themselves fail to fit antiproton atom data and produce different width systematics.

## Method

The authors constructed an optical potential in a '$t\rho$' form using free-space $\bar{p}N$ $S$-wave scattering amplitudes derived from the 2009 version of the Paris $\bar{N}N$ potential, with in-medium modifications via the multiple scattering approach of Wass et al. (WRW) accounting for Pauli correlations. They solved the Dirac equation for the antiproton in nuclei across the periodic table, treating the energy dependence of the amplitudes self-consistently by evaluating the Mandelstam variable $\sqrt{s}$ with momentum-dependent terms (denoted $\sqrt{s}_{\rm J}$). The $P$-wave interaction was added as a gradient term, using either the Paris $P$-wave amplitudes or a phenomenological $P$-wave amplitude fitted to antiproton atom data by Friedman and Gal (2015). Both static calculations (fixed nuclear core) and dynamical calculations (allowing the antiproton to polarize the nuclear core within the RMF model NL-SH) were performed.

## Key numbers

* $1s$ $\bar{p}$ binding energy in $^{208}$Pb calculated with static RMF densities and in-medium (WRW) $S$-wave amplitudes: $B_{\bar p} = 188.6$ MeV; corresponding width: $\Gamma_{\bar p} = 233.8$ MeV.
* $1s$ $\bar{p}$ binding energy in $^{208}$Pb calculated with free-space $S$-wave amplitudes: $B_{\bar p} = 184.8$ MeV; corresponding width: $\Gamma_{\bar p} = 318.5$ MeV.
* Dynamical calculations with the Paris $S$-wave potential supplemented by a phenomenological $P$-wave term yield $\bar{p}$ binding energies $B_{\bar p} \approx 200$ MeV and widths $\Gamma_{\bar p} \sim 200-230$ MeV across selected nuclei.
* Self-consistent energy shift $\delta \sqrt{s}_{\rm J}$ in $^{208}$Pb+$\bar{p}$ for static calculations: Paris $S$-wave only: $-210.6$ MeV; Paris $S+P$-wave: $-238.9$ MeV; Paris $S$ + phen. $P$-wave: $-223.6$ MeV.
* The phenomenological $P$-wave amplitude fitted to antiproton atom data is $f^P_{\bar{p}N} = 2.9 + i1.8$ fm$^3$.
* The antiproton lifetime inside the nucleus from dynamical calculations is consistent with $\simeq 1$ fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic approach of Friedman et al. (2015), who confronted the 2009 Paris $\bar{N}N$ potential with antiproton-atom data and found that the Paris $P$-wave amplitudes fail to fit the data, while a phenomenological $P$-wave term succeeds. The present study applies the same $S$-wave amplitudes and both $P$-wave options to the deeper sub-threshold regime of antiproton-nuclear quasi-bound states for the first time. The results are compared with earlier RMF calculations by Hrtánková and Mareš (2016), which used a phenomenological optical potential constrained by antiproton-atom data and included dynamical treatment of annihilation phase-space suppression. The present work enables future comparisons with experiments at FAIR and provides input for transport models such as GiBUU.
