# Pictures of quantum nuclear rotation beyond the correspondence principle

> We analyze the time evolution of simple nuclear rotational wave packets (WP) called circular, linear or elliptic, depending on squeezing parameter $η$, assuming that $E=\hbarω_0 I(I+1)$. The scenario of fractional revivals found by Averbukh and Perelman is adapted to symmetric WP and compared to that which holds for asymmetric WP. In both cases various shapes are identified under these lines in particular many cases of cloning. 'Mutants' WP are found most often. Finally the time evolution of a WP formed by Coulomb excitation on $^{238}$U and calculated by semiclassical theory is also presented.

## Key claim

The paper shows that quantum nuclear rotational wave packets do not rotate like classical rigid bodies; instead, their time evolution is dominated by quantum interference effects that produce fractional revivals, clones, and "mutants" whose shapes depend on a squeezing parameter η and on whether the wave packet is symmetric or asymmetric, with the scenario of Averbukh and Perelman (1992) adapted to symmetric rotors.

## Method

The authors constructed exponential coherent wave packets on the sphere, parameterized by a squeezing parameter η (η=0 for linear, η=1 for circular, intermediate values for elliptic), and expanded them in spherical harmonics. They then evolved these wave packets under the rigid-rotor Hamiltonian E = ħω₀ I(I+1) and applied the theory of fractional revivals from Averbukh and Perelman (1992) to analyze the wave packet structure at rational fractions of the revival time T_rev. For symmetric wave packets (representing even deformed nuclei with K=0), they derived modified selection rules that restrict the sum over angular momentum to even I values, reducing the number of fractional wave packets. They also performed a realistic calculation using Coulomb excitation amplitudes for ²³⁸U bombarded by ¹⁴⁰Ar at 170 MeV, comparing the ideal rigid-rotor case with experimental energies.

## Key numbers

- For circular wave packets (η=1) with N=14, the average angular momentum is Ī = 13.5, corresponding to the average excitation energy of the K=0 rotational band of ²³⁸U obtained from Coulomb excitation by 200 MeV ⁴⁰Ar projectiles.
- The classical period T_cl = 2π/[ω₀(2Ī+1)] and the revival period T_rev = 2π/ω₀, with T_rev >> T_cl for large Ī.
- For symmetric wave packets at t = T_rev/4, the wave packet rotates by π/2: Ψ₁₊(θ,φ,T_rev/4) = Ψ₁₊(θ,φ+π/2,0).
- For symmetric wave packets at t = T_rev/8, the wave packet rotates by -3π/4: Ψ₁₊(θ,φ,T_rev/8) = Ψ₁₊(θ,φ-3π/4,0).
- For the realistic ²³⁸U calculation, the ground state rotational band energies are approximated by E_I ≈ a I(I+1) + b [I(I+1)]² with a = 7.5 MeV and b = -0.004 MeV.
- The absolute time scales for nuclear rotation are T_rev ∼ 10⁻¹⁹ s and T_cl ∼ 10⁻²⁰ s.

## Context

This work extends the theory of fractional revivals developed by Averbukh and Perelman (1992) for one-dimensional systems (e.g., the infinite square well studied by Aronstein and Stroud) to two-dimensional rotational wave packets on a sphere, specifically for nuclear physics applications. It builds upon earlier work by Broglia and Winther (1981) and Fonda et al. (1985) who introduced rotational coherent wave packets for Coulomb excitation, but provides a complete time-evolution analysis that was not previously available. The paper also connects to studies of quantum revivals in the hydrogen atom by Dacic and Boris (early 1990s) and to the work of Berry and Goldberg (1988) on the propagator of a nuclear spin with Hamiltonian L²/(2J). The results enable future experimental searches for rotational wave packet dynamics, though the authors note that current nuclear physics techniques cannot resolve the ∼10⁻¹⁹ s time scales, making molecular physics (with time scales 10⁻¹² to 10⁻¹⁴ s) a more promising arena for observation.
