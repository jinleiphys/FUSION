# Nuclear matter symmetry energy and the symmetry energy coefficient in the mass formula

> Within the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) approach, we show that for a fixed mass number A, both the symmetry energy coefficient a_{sym}(A) in the semi-empirical mass formula and the nuclear matter symmetry energy E_{sym}(ρ_A) at a subsaturation reference density rho_A can be determined essentially by the symmetry energy E_{sym}(rho_0) and its density slope L at saturation density rho_0. Meanwhile, we find the dependence of a_{sym}(A) on E_{sym}(rho_0) or L is approximately linear and is very similar to the corresponding linear dependence displayed by E_{sym}(ρ_A), providing an explanation for the relation E_{sym}(ρ_A) \approx a_{sym}(A). Our results indicate that a value of E_{sym}(ρ_A) leads to a linear correlation between E_{sym}(rho_0) and L and thus can put important constraints on E_{sym}(rho_0) and L. Particularly, the values of E_{sym}(rho_0)= 30.5 +- 3 MeV and L= 52.5 +- 20 MeV are simultaneously obtained by combining the constraints from recently extracted E_{sym}(ρ_A=0.1 fm^{-3}) with those from recent analyses of neutron skin thickness of Sn isotopes in the same SHF approach.

## Key claim

Within the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock approach, the symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) in the semi-empirical mass formula and the nuclear matter symmetry energy E_sym(rho_A) at a subsaturation reference density are both essentially determined by the symmetry energy E_sym(rho_0) and its density slope L at saturation density, providing a microscopic explanation for the previously observed empirical relation E_sym(rho_A) approximately equals a_sym(A). The extracted values of E_sym(rho_0) = 30.5 +/- 3 MeV and L = 52.5 +/- 20 MeV are obtained by combining constraints from nuclear masses with those from neutron skin thickness of Sn isotopes within the same approach.

## Method

The author used the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) energy density functional to analyze the correlations between the nuclear matter symmetry energy E_sym(rho) at subsaturation densities and the symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) of finite nuclei. The 9 Skyrme interaction parameters were expressed analytically in terms of 9 macroscopic quantities, allowing the symmetry energy to be written as a linear function of E_sym(rho_0) and L. The symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) was evaluated using the droplet model approximation with the Q parameter expressed in terms of L and the curvature parameter K_sym, which itself was expressed in terms of macroscopic quantities within the SHF approach.

## Key numbers

- E_sym(rho_0) = 30.5 +/- 3 MeV and L = 52.5 +/- 20 MeV simultaneously extracted by combining constraints from E_sym(rho_A = 0.1 fm^{-3}) with those from neutron skin thickness of Sn isotopes within a 2-sigma uncertainty
- E_sym(rho_A = 0.10 fm^{-3}) = 20.22 to 24.74 MeV extracted from nuclear masses within a 2-sigma uncertainty, used as the constraint from the relation E_sym(rho_A) approximately equals a_sym(A=208)
- For A=208, the reference density rho_A = 0.10 fm^{-3}; for A=116, rho_A = 0.093 fm^{-3}; for A=40, rho_A = 0.08 fm^{-3}
- The MSL0 parameter set used as reference uses rho_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}, E_0(rho_0) = -16 MeV, K_0 = 230 MeV, m_s,0* = 0.8m, m_v,0* = 0.7m, E_sym(rho_0) = 30 MeV, and L = 60 MeV

## Context

This work extends the empirical finding of Centelles et al. (2009) that the symmetry energy coefficient a_sym(A) approximately equals the nuclear matter symmetry energy E_sym(rho_A) at a subsaturation density, providing a microscopic explanation within the Skyrme-Hartree-Fock framework. The extracted values of E_sym(rho_0) = 30.5 +/- 3 MeV and L = 52.5 +/- 20 MeV are consistent with constraints from global nucleon optical potentials (Xu et al., 2010) and from combining neutron skin data with heavy-ion collision analyses (Tsang et al., 2009), but with higher precision. This enables tighter constraints on the density dependence of the symmetry energy, which is essential for understanding neutron star properties and the isovector giant dipole resonance of finite nuclei.
