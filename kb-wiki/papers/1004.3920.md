# Why heavy and light quarks radiate energy with similar rates

> The dead cone effect has been expected to reduce the magnitude of energy loss and jet quenching for heavy flavors produced with large pT in heavy ion collisions. On the contrary, data from RHIC for open charm production demonstrate a flavor independent nuclear suppression. We show that vacuum radiation of a highly virtual quark produced at high pT with its color field stripped off, develops a much wider dead cone, which screens the one related to the quark mass. Lacking the field, gluons cannot be radiated within this cone until the color field is regenerated and the quark virtuality cools down to the scale of the order of the quark mass. However, this takes time longer than is essential for the observed jet quenching. This is the reason why the light and charm quark jets are quenched equally. Open beauty is expected to be suppressed much less within the pT range studied so far.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the observed flavor-independent nuclear suppression of charm and light hadrons at RHIC arises because a highly virtual quark produced in a hard collision has its color field stripped off, creating a much wider "vacuum dead cone" that screens the mass-dependent dead cone; this effect persists over distances longer than the typical path length in the dense medium, so heavy and light quarks radiate energy at similar rates.

## Method

The authors calculate the vacuum energy loss of a high-\(p_T\) quark as a function of path length \(L\) using the gluon radiation spectrum for a quark with initial virtuality \(Q \sim p_T\). They impose a step function \(\Theta(L - l_c)\) in the energy-loss integral, where \(l_c\) is the coherence length, to account for the fact that gluons cannot be radiated until the quark's color field is regenerated. They then compare the energy loss for light (\(m_q=0\)), charm (\(m_c=1.5\) GeV), and bottom (\(m_b=4.5\) GeV) quarks at jet energies of 10, 15, and 20 GeV, and estimate the effective thickness of the emitting layer in central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV.

## Key numbers

- Charm quark mass: \(m_c = 1.5\) GeV; bottom quark mass: \(m_b = 4.5\) GeV.
- For a charm quark with \(E = p_T = 10\) GeV, sensitivity to the quark mass is restored at path lengths \(L \gtrsim 1/x\) fm (where \(x\) is the gluon light-cone momentum fraction).
- The characteristic length for the heavy-quark dead cone to set in is \(L_q = E(1-x)/(x m_q^2)\); for charm at \(E=10\) GeV this can be several fermi.
- In central Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV, the observed suppression factor \(R_{AA} \approx 0.2\) corresponds to an effective emitting layer thickness \(\Delta \approx 0.9\) fm (for nuclear radius \(R_A = 6.5\) fm).
- The infra-red cutoff in the calculation is \(\Lambda = 0.2\) GeV; the running coupling is regularized with \(k_0^2 = 0.5\) GeV\(^2\).

## Context

This work addresses the puzzle that RHIC data (PHENIX and STAR, circa 2006-2008) show similar nuclear suppression for charmed and light hadrons, contrary to the expectation from the dead-cone effect (Dokshitzer and Kharzeev, 2001) and earlier medium-induced energy-loss calculations (Armesto et al., 2004) that predicted weaker jet quenching for heavy flavors. The paper extends the concept of the dead cone to the vacuum radiation of a highly virtual quark, showing that the initial virtuality dominates over the mass effect for the short path lengths relevant in heavy-ion collisions. It predicts that open beauty should be much less suppressed at the same \(p_T\) range, but will reach universal suppression at much larger transverse momenta.
