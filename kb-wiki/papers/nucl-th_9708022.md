# Randomly Broken Nuclei and Disordered Systems

> Similarities between models of fragmenting nuclei and disordered systems in condensed matter suggest corresponding methods. Several theoretical models of fragmentation investigated in this fashion show marked differences, indicating possible new methods for distinguishing models using yield data. Applying nuclear methods to disordered systems also yields interesting results.

# Randomly Broken Nuclei and Disordered Systems

## Key claim

The paper shows that a wide range of nuclear fragmentation models and disordered system models (spin glasses, random maps, randomly broken intervals) can all be generated by a single underlying mechanism, the random sequential breaking of an interval, yet the two classes of models exhibit markedly different statistical behavior when analyzed by the other field's traditional methods, suggesting that the choice of analysis technique can distinguish between critical and disordered behavior even when the underlying generating mechanism is formally similar.

## Method

The authors derive probability distributions for fragment patterns from a generalized random sequential breaking process where an interval of length A is broken into integer-sized pieces according to a probability distribution that depends on a parameter x (and optionally a second parameter γ). They show that the Gibbs-type distribution Pr(n) = (1/Z_A) ∏_k (x_k^{n_k} / n_k!) with x_k = x/k arises from this process, and that by modifying the breaking probabilities they recover distributions for nuclear fragmentation models (with x_k = x/k^τ) and for disordered systems including the Sherrington-Kirkpatrick spin-glass model (with x+γ=1). They then compute ensemble averages of moments, reduced moments, and probability distributions of the second moment Y and largest cluster size k_max for both classes of models using recursive partition function methods.

## Key numbers

- For nuclear fragmentation models with x_k = x/k^τ and τ > 2, the critical point occurs at x_c/A = 1/ζ(τ-1), where ζ is the Riemann zeta function.
- For nuclear fragmentation, the critical exponent τ ≈ 2.2, as reported by the EOS collaboration (Gilkes 1994).
- For the disordered system model with γ=1, the relative fluctuation f(Y) ≈ 2x/((x+2)(x+3)) in the large A limit; for x ≈ 1 the fluctuations are large and the system lacks self-averaging.
- For the spin-glass case (x+γ=1), in the infinite A limit ⟨Y²⟩ = (1/3)⟨Y⟩(1 + 2⟨Y⟩).
- For the generalized model with γ=1 and x=1, the fragment distribution follows ⟨n_k⟩ = 1/k (a power law).
- For the generalized model, the continuum fragment size distribution is f(W) = Γ(x+γ)/(Γ(x)Γ(γ)) (1-W)^{x-1} W^{γ-2}, which is a beta distribution.

## Context

This work extends the correspondence between nuclear fragmentation and disordered systems previously noted in percolation theory applications by Bauer (1985) and Campi (1986), and parallels a similar investigation by Higgs (1995) for biological systems. The authors build on the random sequential breaking model studied by Derrida and Flyvbjerg (1987) and Frontera (1995), and on the nuclear fragmentation models of Sobotka and Moretto (1985), Gross (1990, 1992), and Bondorf (1995). The paper enables future work to use yield data from nuclear fragmentation experiments to distinguish between critical (phase-transition-like) and disordered (non-self-averaging) behavior by applying analysis methods from both fields to the same data.
