# Progress in NN --> NNpi

> We survey the recent developments in the reaction $NN \to NNπ$ in effective field theory. We show that the proper construction of the production operator needs a careful separation of irreducible pieces from reducible ones. The result of this consideration is a complete cancellation of all loops in the production operator at NLO. Moreover, we show that this procedure brings the leading Weinberg-Tomozawa vertex on-shell, thus enhancing the corresponding contribution to the transition amplitude by a factor of 4/3 as compared to the commonly used value. We also discuss the role of the $Δ(1232)$ for the s-wave pion production. Being relatively sizable individually the direct and rescattering mechanisms of the $Δ$ excitation at NLO cancel each other to a large extent. Thus, we conclude that the net effect of the $Δ$ at NLO is very small.

## Key claim

The paper argues that, within a modified power counting that accounts for the large momentum transfer in pion production, all loop diagrams in the production operator cancel at next-to-leading order (NLO), and the leading Weinberg-Tomozawa vertex must be taken on-shell, enhancing its contribution by a factor of 4/3 relative to the commonly used value. The net effect of the Delta(1232) resonance at NLO is very small due to a near-complete cancellation between direct and rescattering mechanisms.

## Method

The authors constructed the transition operator for \(NN \to NN\pi\) up to NLO in chiral perturbation theory (ChPT) using a modified power counting with expansion parameter \(\chi = |\vec p_{\mathrm{thr}}|/M_N = \sqrt{m_\pi/M_N}\). They separated irreducible diagrams (part of the production operator) from reducible diagrams (part of the NN wave functions) based on the presence of two-nucleon cuts, and showed that energy-dependent vertices like the Weinberg-Tomozawa term generate irreducible contributions from formally reducible loop diagrams. The Delta resonance contributions were evaluated using coupled-channel NN models (CCF and Hannover models) to generate the NN and NDelta distortions.

## Key numbers

- The expansion parameter for the modified power counting is \(\chi = \sqrt{m_\pi/M_N}\), where \(m_\pi\) is the pion mass and \(M_N\) the nucleon mass.
- For the channel \(pp \to d\pi^+\), the sum of the irreducible NLO loop diagrams (b)-(d) gave an amplitude \(A_{pp\to d\pi^+}^{b+c+d} = \frac{g_A^3 |\vec q|}{256 f_\pi^5}\), which grows linearly with the final NN relative momentum \(|\vec q|\).
- The on-shell value of the Weinberg-Tomozawa vertex is \(2m_\pi\), and using this on-shell value enhances the isovector \(\pi N\)-rescattering amplitude by a factor of \(4/3\) compared to the traditionally used value of \(3/2 m_\pi\).
- The mass difference between the Delta and nucleon is \(\Delta M = M_\Delta - M_N \simeq 2m_\pi\), making the NDelta propagator scale as \(\sim 1/m_\pi\).
- Each of the two Delta diagrams (direct and rescattering) gives about a 10-15% correction to the transition amplitude, but they enter with opposite signs and largely cancel.
- The theoretical uncertainty of the NLO calculation is estimated as about \(2 m_\pi/M_N \approx 30\%\) for the cross section.

## Context

This work extends the chiral perturbation theory treatment of \(NN \to NN\pi\) by resolving a problem identified in earlier EFT calculations by Park et al. and by Unserd, where NLO corrections were too large and the chiral expansion appeared non-convergent. It builds on the modified power counting proposed by Cohen (1996), da Rocha et al. (2000), and Hanhart and Kaiser (2000), which accounts for the large momentum transfer characteristic of pion production. The result that all NLO loops cancel and the Weinberg-Tomozawa vertex is enhanced by 4/3 leads to a good description of experimental data for \(pp \to d\pi^+\) from TRIUMF, IUCF, COSY, and PSI. The finding that the Delta resonance contributes very little at NLO contradicts the earlier phenomenological study by Niskanen (which found a factor of 3 enhancement) but is consistent with the model calculation by Hanhart et al. using the Jülich meson-nucleon model. The authors note that a computation at NNLO is necessary for more solid conclusions, especially regarding charge-symmetry breaking effects.
