# Anatomy of Roper Resonance

> Sixty years ago, the first excited state of a proton/neutron was ``born.'' During this time, we learned a lot about it, specifically - how unique this case is: a single resonance with two pole positions on different Riemann sheets. Let me present a brief history to remind readers how development progressed. Sure, history is sometimes something that never happened, described by those who were never there...

## Key claim

The Roper resonance N(1440) 1/2+ is unique because it exhibits two pole positions on different Riemann sheets rather than a single isolated pole, making a simple Breit-Wigner parametrization inadequate for describing its structure.

## Method

The paper reviews the history of the Roper resonance discovery and subsequent phenomenological analyses, comparing results from multiple partial-wave analyses including the Karlsruhe-Helsinki (KH) and George Washington/Virginia Tech (GW/VPI) SAID analyses. The author examines pole positions extracted from different groups' analyses of pion-nucleon scattering data, focusing on the P11 partial wave and the influence of nearby thresholds including the pi-delta, eta-N, and rho-N thresholds.

## Key numbers

- The pi-delta threshold lies at W = 1350 - i 50 MeV
- The eta-N threshold lies at W = 1487 - i 0 MeV
- The rho-N threshold lies at W = 1715 - i 73 MeV
- Pole-1 positions from different groups range from 1357 - i 76 MeV (Kamano 2010) to 1387 - i 73 MeV (Doring 2009)
- Pole-2 positions from different groups range from 1360 - i 120 MeV (Cutkosky 1990) to 1410 - i 80 MeV (Arndt 1985)
- The SAID WI08 analysis gives Pole-1 at 1358 - i 80 MeV and Pole-2 at 1388 - i 82 MeV (Workman 2012)

## Context

This review extends the work of Arndt et al. (1985) who first reported the two-pole structure of the Roper resonance, and confirms findings by the Julich group (Doring 2009), ANL-Osaka/EBAC group (Kamano 2010), and Cutkosky and Wang (1990). The paper questions the validity of Breit-Wigner extractions of N(1440) electroexcitation amplitudes from JLab CLAS experiments, suggesting that future CLAS12 measurements should go beyond the Breit-Wigner approximation to study the Q2 evolution of residues at both pole positions and determine whether the two poles originate from a common quark core.
