# From noise to information: The transfer function formalism for uncertainty quantification on nuclear density reconstruction

> The neutron distribution of neutron-rich nuclei provides critical information on the structure of finite nuclei and neutron stars. Parity violating experiments -- such as PREX and CREX -- provide a clean and largely model-independent determination of neutron densities. Such experiments, however, are challenging and expensive which is why sound statistical arguments are required to maximize the information gained. For this goal we introduce a new framework, "the transfer function formalism", aimed at uncertainty quantification, model selection, and experimental design in the context of neutron densities. The transfer functions (TFs) are built analytically by expressing the linear response of the objective function to small perturbations of the data. Using the TF formalism, we are able to analyze the expected overall uncertainty -- quantified in terms of bias and variance -- of the mean square radius and interior density of $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb. Using relativistic mean field models as a proxy for the weak-charge density -- and assuming that a total of five measurements could be performed on the weak form factor of $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb -- we identify the optimal models and experimental locations that minimize the combined radius and interior uncertainty for both nuclei. We also explore the use of the TF formalism to understand the influence of prior distributions for the model parameters, as well as the optimization of model hyperparameters not constrained by the data.

## Key claim
The transfer function formalism provides a linear-response framework to propagate experimental uncertainties from weak form factor measurements to extracted quantities like neutron radii and interior densities, but its validity depends on the assumption that perturbations in the input data are small enough that the system response remains linear.

## Method
The authors built transfer functions analytically by expressing the linear response of the chi-squared objective function to small perturbations in the experimental data points. They used seven different parametrizations of nuclear densities (Fourier-Bessel, Helm, Symmetrized Fermi with 2-4 parameters, and two hybrid models) and evaluated their performance on synthetic weak-charge form factor data generated from five relativistic mean field models (RMF012, RMF016, RMF022, RMF028, RMF032). The performance of each model was assessed using a bias-variance trade-off criterion quantified by the Mean Squared Error, combining both the discrepancy from the true value and the statistical spread from experimental noise.

## Key numbers
- The root-mean-square charge radius of $^{208}$Pb is known with precision: $R_{\rm ch}=5.5012(13)\,{\rm fm}$.
- The neutron skin thickness of $^{208}$Pb from PREX and PREX-II combined is $R_{\rm skin}=R_{n}-R_{p}=(0.283\pm0.071)\,{\rm fm}$.
- The saturation density is approximately $\rho_0\approx 0.15\,{\rm fm}^{-3}$, yielding a radius constant $r_0\approx 1.17\,{\rm fm}$.
- The analysis assumed a total of five measurements could be performed on the weak form factor of $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb.

## Context
This work extends the bias-variance trade-off approach previously applied to proton charge radius extraction (Yan 2018, Higinbotham 2018) to the problem of neutron density reconstruction from parity-violating electron scattering experiments like PREX and CREX. The transfer function formalism builds on concepts from signal processing and control theory (Aastrom 2010) and is related to influence functions (Huber 2004) and adjoint methods (Sullivan 2015), but provides a novel unified treatment of model selection, prior influence, and bias-variance analysis for nuclear density extraction. The formalism enables identification of critical experimental momentum-transfer points that drive most of the uncertainty, which is essential for designing future experiments at facilities like MESA.
