# TeV gamma-rays and neutrinos from photo-disintegration of nuclei in Cygnus OB2

> TeV gamma-rays may provide significant information about high energy astrophysical accelerators. Such gamma-rays can result from the photo-de-excitation of PeV nuclei after their parents have undergone photo-disintegration in an environment of ultraviolet photons. This process is proposed as a candidate explanation of the recently discovered HEGRA source at the edge of the Cygnus OB2 association. The Lyman-alpha background is provided by the rich O and B stellar environment. It is found that (1) the HEGRA flux can be obtained if there is efficient acceleration at the source of lower energy nuclei; (2) the requirement that the Lorentz-boosted ultraviolet photons can excite the Giant Dipole resonance implies a strong suppression of the gamma-ray spectrum compared to an E_γ^{-2} behavior at energies \alt 1 TeV (some of these energies will be probed by the upcoming GLAST mission); (3) a TeV neutrino counterpart from neutron decay following helium photo-disintegration will be observed at IceCube only if a major proportion of the kinetic energy budget of the Cygnus OB2 association is expended in accelerating nuclei.

## Key claim

TeV gamma-rays from the HEGRA source TeV J2032+4130 at the edge of the Cygnus OB2 association can be explained by photo-disintegration of PeV nuclei on the Lyman-alpha ultraviolet photon background from hot O and B stars, followed by de-excitation of daughter nuclei, but only if there is efficient acceleration of lower energy nuclei at the source and the gamma-ray spectrum is strongly suppressed below about 1 TeV compared to an E_gamma^{-2} behavior.

## Method

The authors calculated the photo-disintegration rate of high-energy nuclei (iron, silicon, and helium) propagating through the isotropic ultraviolet photon background of the Cygnus OB2 association, using a narrow-width approximation for the Giant Dipole Resonance cross section with parameters fitted to data. They modeled the stellar photon population as thermal Bose-Einstein distributions from O stars (temperature 40000 K, radius 19 solar radii) and B stars (18000 K, 8 solar radii), with populations of 5% and 95% respectively of 2600 total OB stars, and averaged the inverse-square dilution over a central region of radius 10 pc. The resulting gamma-ray flux from de-excitation was compared to the HEGRA observed spectrum, and the associated neutrino flux from neutron decay following helium photo-disintegration was estimated for IceCube detectability.

## Key numbers

- HEGRA source flux: dF_gamma/dE_gamma = 6.2 (+/- 1.5_stat +/- 1.3_sys) x 10^{-13} (E_gamma/TeV)^{-1.9(+/-0.1_stat +/- 0.3_sys)} cm^{-2} s^{-1} TeV^{-1}
- Integrated flux above 1 TeV: F_gamma(E_gamma > 1 TeV) = 6.9 (+/- 1.8_stat) x 10^{-13} cm^{-2} s^{-1}
- Source luminosity above 1 TeV: approximately 10^{32} erg/s
- Source extension (Gaussian 1-sigma radius): 6.2 arcminutes (+/- 1.2_stat +/- 0.9_sys), corresponding to about 3.07 pc at 1.7 kpc distance
- HEGRA source significance: 7.1 sigma
- Wind mechanical luminosity of Cygnus OB2: (1-2) x 10^{39} erg/s
- Total Lyman continuum luminosity of stars: approximately 10^{51} photons/s
- Distance to Cygnus OB2: about 1.7 kpc
- Age of association: 1-4 Myr
- Giant Dipole Resonance cross section parameters: sigma_0/A = 1.45 x 10^{-27} cm^2, Gamma = 8 MeV, epsilon'_0 = 42.65 A^{-0.21} MeV for A > 4
- Photo-disintegration time for nuclei exceeds diffusion time of about 10^4 years in the association

## Context

This work extends the photo-disintegration mechanism emphasized by Moskalenko and collaborators (1990s) but largely ignored by the gamma-ray community, applying it to the specific HEGRA source TeV J2032+4130 discovered in the Cygnus OB2 region. It contrasts with earlier hadronic models for this source involving proton-proton interactions in dense gas clouds (Butt 2003) or stellar winds (Torres et al. 2004), which faced challenges from revised density estimates and mass-loss rates. The model enables predictions for GLAST (now Fermi) to probe the predicted spectral suppression below 1 TeV and for IceCube to detect a TeV neutrino counterpart if a major fraction of the kinetic energy budget of Cygnus OB2 is expended in accelerating nuclei.
