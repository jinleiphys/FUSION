# Common Approaches in Description of Ordinary Liquids and Hadronic Matter

> This work is an attempt to give a brief overview of the implementation of the statistical ther- modynamics to hadronic matter. The possibility to use the hydrodynamic approach for developing the physical model of the formation of exotic structures in the head-on intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions is discussed. That approach is shown to be able to provide simple analytical expressions describing each step of the collision process. This allows for extracting the data concerning nuclear matter properties (surface tension, compressibility, etc.) from the properties of observed fragments. The advantages of the thermodynamic analysis of phase trajectories of the system in heavy ion collisions are discussed. Within the thermodynamic approach, the method to evaluate the curvature correction to the surface tension from the nuclear matter equation of state is described. The possibility to use statistical thermodynamics in the studies of hadronic matter and quantum liquids is discussed.

# Common Approaches in Description of Ordinary Liquids and Hadronic Matter

## Key claim
The paper argues that statistical thermodynamics and hydrodynamic approaches developed for ordinary liquids can be applied to describe hadronic matter and heavy ion collisions, but this analogy is limited by the small size of nuclear systems, which prevents certain mechanisms (such as spinodal decomposition) from being realized. The hydrodynamic model provides a qualitative picture of exotic structure formation in head-on intermediate-energy heavy ion collisions, but the authors acknowledge that their model "doesn't give the strict results, but rather provides a picture of the involved physical processes as clear as possible."

## Method
The authors review the application of statistical thermodynamics, the Fisher droplet model, and the Guggenheim relation for coexistence curves to nuclear matter and heavy ion collisions. They present a hydrodynamic model for head-on collisions of identical heavy nuclei (e.g., ⁹³Nb + ⁹³Nb) by simplifying the system to a spherical nucleus colliding with a rigid wall, applying slip boundary conditions, and comparing the results with Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport model calculations and experimental data from Bauer (1992), Xu (1993), and Stone (1997). The thermodynamic analysis of phase trajectories in the P-V plane is used to evaluate possible decay channels, including spinodal decomposition, mechanical breakdown, and metastable boiling.

## Key numbers
- Threshold energy for intermediate-mass fragment production: Eth ~ 2–4 MeV/nucleon (Botvina, 2006)
- For ⁹³Nb + ⁹³Nb at 60 MeV/nucleon with stiff EOS (K = 380 MeV): reaction time τ₃ = 118 fm/c (model) vs. 120–160 fm/c (BUU calculations); maximum density ρ/ρ₀ = 1.6 (model) vs. ~1.5 (BUU); spreading size Rl_max/R = 2.6 (model) vs. ~2 (BUU); number of similar-mass intermediate-mass fragments = 5.6 (model) vs. 3–6 (BUU) and 4–6 (experiment)
- For soft EOS: incompressibility at saturation density K₀ = 200 MeV; for stiff EOS: K₀ = 380 MeV
- Nucleon mean free path λ = 1.4 fm at ρ/ρ₀ = 1 and λ = 1.3 fm at ρ/ρ₀ = 1.5 at E = 50 MeV/nucleon (Chen, 2003)
- BUU calculations show exotic topologies at energy interval 60–75 MeV/A for head-on collisions

## Context
This review extends the long-standing analogy between ordinary liquids and nuclear matter, building on the van der Waals equation applied to nuclear systems and the liquid droplet model of Myers (1969). The work connects to prior studies of nuclear multifragmentation using statistical models (Botvina, 1995; Campi, 2003), the Fisher droplet model (Fisher, 1967; Panagiotou, 1984), and hydrodynamic descriptions dating back to Siemens (1979) and Stocker (1980). The authors specifically compare their hydrodynamic results with BUU transport calculations from Bauer (1992) and Xu (1993) and experimental data from Stone (1997). The paper enables further development of combined macroscopic-microscopic approaches to extract nuclear matter properties (surface tension, compressibility, viscosity) from heavy ion collision data, and suggests that the analogy with high-speed ordinary droplet collisions (Bartolo, 2006; Rioboo, 2002) can provide insight into exotic structure formation.
