# Chiral catalysis of nuclear fusion in molecules

> At low energies, nuclear fusion is strongly affected by electron screening of the Coulomb repulsion among the fusing nuclei. It may thus be possible to catalyze nuclear fusion in molecules (i.e., to fuse specific nuclei in situ) through quantum control of electron wave functions in intense laser fields. The circularly polarized (chiral) laser field can effectively squeeze the electron wave functions, greatly enhancing the screening in the spatial region relevant for the fusion process. We estimate the corresponding fusion probabilities, and find that the proposed chiral catalysis of nuclear fusion in molecules may be observable, potentially with important practical applications.

## Key claim

Circularly polarized laser fields can squeeze electron wave functions in molecules to radii significantly smaller than the Bohr radius, thereby enhancing Coulomb screening and potentially making nuclear fusion observable in situ; however, the authors hedge that further theoretical and experimental studies are necessary to establish whether the corresponding fusion rates are observable and suitable for practical applications.

## Method

The authors estimate fusion probabilities by combining the Salpeter approach for electron screening of the Coulomb barrier (using a Debye screened potential) with the Kapitza method for the effective electron-nucleus potential in a circularly polarized laser field. They compute the screened penetration factor in the ultra-low energy limit (E << U0) and multiply it by molecular vibration frequencies to obtain fusion rates for specific molecules. The required laser parameters are estimated by equating the classical excursion parameter rC = eE/(me ω^2) to the desired screening radius.

## Key numbers

* For p + 16O → 17F fusion with screening radius rD ≃ rB/8, the screened penetration factor is exp(−147.2) ≃ 10^(−64), compared to the unscreened Gamow factor exp(−300) ≃ 10^(−130) at E = 10 eV.
* For p + d fusion in HD molecule, the screened penetration factor is P_s^(low)(HD) ∼ 10^(−102), yielding a fusion rate λ_f(HD) ≃ 10^(−88) s^(−1) and a volumetric rate f(HD) ≃ 10^(−64) cm^(−3) s^(−1) at n(HD) ≃ 10^(24) cm^(−3).
* For p + 16O → 17F fusion in water, the fusion rate is λ_f(H2O) ≃ 10^(−50) s^(−1), corresponding to a characteristic lifetime T_f(H2O) ≃ 3 × 10^(42) years, and a volumetric rate f(H2O) ≃ 3 × 10^(−28) cm^(−3) s^(−1) at n(H2O) ≃ 3 × 10^(22) cm^(−3).
* Achieving f(H2O) ∼ 1 cm^(−3) s^(−1) would require decreasing the screening radius by a factor of 5 from its value r_D(H2O) ≃ 0.07 Å.
* A UV Lyman-alpha laser (λ = 122 nm, ω ≃ 2.4 × 10^(15) Hz) at intensity I = 10^(9) W/cm^2 yields r_C ∼ 0.02 Å, about five times smaller than the screening radius in water.

## Context

This work extends the concept of muon-catalyzed fusion (Frank 1947, Sakharov 1948, Zel'dovich 1954; discovered experimentally by Alvarez 1957) by proposing that laser-controlled electrons can mimic the screening enhancement normally provided by muons, without the need for muon production. It also relates to the proposal by Saha, Markmann and Batista (2012) to modify nuclear wave packets with laser pulses, but argues that lasers affect electron wave packets much more strongly than nuclear ones. The paper enables future numerical Floquet studies of water molecules in circularly polarized laser fields and suggests that tabletop UV or soft X-ray laser systems might achieve observable fusion rates.
