# Three-Nucleon Forces for the New Millennium

> Most nuclear physics ranges from insensitive to relatively insensitive to many-nucleon forces. The dominant ingredient in calculations of nuclear properties is the nucleon-nucleon potential. Three-nucleon forces nevertheless play an important role in nuclear physics because of the great precision of modern calculational methods for systems of relatively few nucleons. We explore the reasons why many-body forces are weak in nuclei by using a classification scheme for such forces that is based on dimensional power counting, which is used to organize chiral perturbation theory. An assessment will be made of how close we are to a ``standard'' three-nucleon force. Recent advances in determining the significance of three-nucleon forces will also be discussed.

# Three-Nucleon Forces for the New Millennium

## Key claim
Three-nucleon forces are weak but significant in nuclear physics because the energy scale of QCD (around 1 GeV) suppresses them relative to nucleon-nucleon forces, producing energy shifts of roughly 0.5–1 MeV per triplet rather than the 20–30 MeV per pair typical of two-nucleon interactions. The paper argues that chiral perturbation theory provides a unified organizational scheme for classifying these forces, but cautions that a true "standard" three-nucleon force has not yet been achieved and requires incorporating both adequate phenomenology (such as isobars) and chiral constraints.

## Method
The paper uses dimensional power counting within chiral perturbation theory to classify three-nucleon forces by their relative size, based on two QCD scales: the pion decay constant (fπ ≈ 93 MeV) and the chiral symmetry breaking scale (Λ ≈ 1 GeV). It reviews the historical development of three-nucleon force models, including the Fujita-Miyazawa, Tucson-Melbourne, Urbana-Argonne, and chiral perturbation theory-based models, and compares their predictions against experimental data for triton binding, nucleon-deuteron scattering, and polarization observables.

## Key numbers
- Three-nucleon force energy shift estimate: ⟨ΔV⟩ ∼ 0.5–1 MeV per triplet, derived from ⟨V⟩ ∼ 20–30 MeV per pair divided by the nucleon mass scale (Mc² ≈ 1 GeV)
- Leading-order one-pion-exchange NN potential expectation: ⟨Vπ⟩ ∼ Q³/(fπΛ) ∼ 30 MeV per pair, where Q ∼ mπc is the effective nuclear momentum
- Leading-order three-nucleon force expectation: ⟨V3NF⟩ ∼ Q⁶/(fπ²Λ³) ∼ 1 MeV per triplet
- Triton underbinding with modern NN forces alone: roughly 0.5–1 MeV
- Recommended calculation precision for three-nucleon force studies: errors ≤ 0.1 MeV (∼1% of triton binding energy)
- At 3 MeV nucleon-deuteron scattering, NN-only calculations are about 30% smaller than experimental data at the maximum of the Ay observable; adding the TM 3NF removes about 1/4 of the discrepancy, and adding the d₁-term with strength c₁ = −1 removes another 1/4

## Context
This review extends the line of work on three-nucleon forces initiated by Wigner (1933) and developed through the Fujita-Miyazawa model (based on isobar intermediate states), the Tucson-Melbourne model (exploiting current algebra and PCAC), and the Urbana-Argonne model. It directly addresses the "Sagara discrepancy" in proton-deuteron scattering at 65 MeV and the longstanding Ay puzzle in low-energy nucleon-deuteron scattering, where NN-only calculations significantly underpredict polarization data. The paper enables future work by identifying the most urgent missing calculations, particularly the Born terms of two-pion-exchange forces and the mixed short-range plus pion-range terms, and by establishing chiral perturbation theory as the organizational framework for achieving a consensus "standard model" of three-nucleon forces.
