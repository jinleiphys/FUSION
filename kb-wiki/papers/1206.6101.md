# On the Importance of the Equation of State for the Neutrino-Driven Supernova Explosion Mechanism

> By implementing widely-used equations of state (EOS) from Lattimer & Swesty (LS) and H. Shen et al. (SHEN) in core-collapse supernova simulations, we explore possible impacts of these EOS on the post-bounce dynamics prior to the onset of neutrino-driven explosions. Our spherically symmetric (1D) and axially symmetric (2D) models are based on neutrino radiation hydrodynamics including spectral transport, which is solved by the isotropic diffusion source approximation. We confirm that in 1D simulations neutrino-driven explosions cannot be obtained for any of the employed EOS. Impacts of the EOS on the post-bounce hydrodynamics are more clearly visible in 2D simulations. In 2D models of a 15 M_sun progenitor using the LS EOS, the stalled bounce shock expands to increasingly larger radii, which is not the case using the SHEN EOS. Keeping in mind that the omission of the energy drain by heavy-lepton neutrinos in the present scheme could facilitate explosions, we find that 2D models of an 11.2 M_sun progenitor produce neutrino-driven explosions for all the EOS under investigation. Models using the LS EOS are slightly more energetic compared to those with the SHEN EOS. The more efficient neutrino heating in the LS models coincides with a higher electron antineutrino luminosity and a larger mass that is enclosed within the gain region. The models based on the LS EOS also show a more vigorous and aspherical downflow of accreting matter to the surface of the protoneutron star (PNS). The accretion pattern is essential for the production and strength of outgoing pressure waves, that can push in turn the shock to larger radii and provide more favorable conditions for the explosion. [abbreviated]

## Key claim

The authors find that 2D simulations of an 11.2 solar mass progenitor produce neutrino-driven explosions for all equations of state under investigation, but they note that the omission of energy drain by heavy-lepton neutrinos in their scheme could facilitate explosions, meaning the results may overestimate the likelihood of explosion compared to more complete physics.

## Method

The authors performed spherically symmetric (1D) and axially symmetric (2D) core-collapse supernova simulations using neutrino radiation hydrodynamics with spectral transport solved by the isotropic diffusion source approximation (IDSA). They implemented four equations of state: three variants of the Lattimer and Swesty (LS) EOS with incompressibilities of 180, 220, and 375 MeV, plus the Shen et al. (SHEN) EOS based on relativistic mean field theory. Simulations used a 15 solar mass progenitor from Woosley and Weaver (1995) and an 11.2 solar mass progenitor, with 2D simulations run on a grid of 300 radial zones up to 5000 km and 128 angular zones.

## Key numbers

- LS180 EOS gives a maximum neutron star mass of approximately 1.8 solar masses; LS220 gives approximately 2.0 solar masses; LS375 gives approximately 2.7 solar masses; SHEN gives approximately 2.2 solar masses
- The symmetry energy is 29.3 MeV for LS EOS and 36.9 MeV for SHEN EOS; the incompressibility is 180, 220, and 375 MeV for the three LS variants and 281 MeV for SHEN
- At bounce, the inner core mass is about 0.7 solar masses for LS EOS models and about 0.8 solar masses for SHEN, with central electron fraction of about 0.3 for LS and about 0.32 for SHEN
- The accretion luminosity formula gives approximately 8 times 10 to the 52 erg per second for a 1.5 solar mass enclosed mass, 50 km neutrinosphere radius, and 1 solar mass per second accretion rate
- In 2D simulations of the 15 solar mass progenitor with LS180, the shock at the south pole reaches about 2000 km from center at about 500 ms post bounce, while the north pole reaches about 1000 km
- The maximum entropy in the gain region reaches about 13 k_B per baryon at 200 ms and about 16 k_B per baryon at 300 ms post bounce in 2D simulations

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Thompson (2003) on the effects of incompressibility and by Sumiyoshi et al. (2006, 2007) comparing LS and SHEN EOS in 1D, now providing systematic 2D comparisons. The authors find that the softer LS EOS (LS180) produces slightly more energetic explosions than the stiffer SHEN EOS for the 11.2 solar mass progenitor, consistent with earlier findings by Marek et al. (2009) that softer EOS give more optimistic conditions for explosion onset. The results enable future work to test whether the diagnostic indicators examined here (SASI amplitude, gain region entropy, advection-to-heating timescale ratio, and others) can reliably predict explosion outcomes across different EOS and progenitor masses.
