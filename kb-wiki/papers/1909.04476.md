# Re-visiting gravitational wave events via pulsars

> By now many gravitational wave (GW) signals have been detected by LIGO and Virgo, with the waves reaching earth directly from their respective sources. These waves will also travel to different pulsars and will cause (tiny) transient deformations in the pulsar shape. Some of us have recently shown that the resultant transient change in the pulsar moment of inertia may leave an observable imprint on the pulsar signals as detected on earth, especially at resonance. The pulsars may thus act as remotely stationed Weber gravitational wave detectors. This allows us to revisit the past GW events via pulsars. We give here a list of specific pulsars whose future signals will carry the imprints of past GW events, to be specific we constrain it within 50 years. Some interesting cases are, supernova SN1987A with earliest perturbed signals from pulsars J0709-5923 and B0559-57 expected to reach earth in 2023 and 2024 respectively, Crab supernova, with perturbed signal arrival date from pulsar J1856-3754 in 2057, and GW170814 event with its imprints on the signals on the pulsar J0437-4715 reaching earth between 2035-2043. Even the earliest recorded supernova SN185 event may become observable again via pulsar J0900-3144 with the perturbed pulsar signal reaching us sometime between 2033-2066. Importantly, even though the strength of the signal will depend on the interior properties of the pulsar, the expected dates of signal arrival are completely model independent, depending only on the locations of the source and the relevant pulsar.

## Key claim

Gravitational waves from past events (including those already detected by LIGO/Virgo and historical supernovae) will induce transient deformations in pulsars, and the resulting changes in pulsar spin rate and pulse profile may be detectable on Earth at specific future dates; the predicted arrival dates of these perturbed pulsar signals are model-independent, depending only on source and pulsar locations, though the signal strength depends on uncertain pulsar interior properties such as the quality factor Q for resonance enhancement.

## Method

The authors calculated the light travel time for the path difference between the direct GW signal to Earth and the route via a pulsar (GW to pulsar, then perturbed pulse to Earth) for 2659 pulsars from a catalog, using coordinates and distances of known GW events (LIGO/Virgo detections) and historical supernovae. They then tabulated the predicted arrival dates of the perturbed pulsar signals, limiting results to those arriving within 50 years and including error estimates from uncertainties in source and pulsar locations where available.

## Key numbers

- For supernova SN1987A, perturbed signals from pulsar J0709-5923 expected on 5/9/2023 and from B0559-57 on 7/8/2024.
- For the Crab supernova, perturbed signal from pulsar J1856-3754 expected in March 2057 with error of one year.
- For GW170814 event, imprints on pulsar J0437-4715 signals reaching Earth between 2035-2043.
- For the earliest recorded supernova SN185 (185 AD), perturbed signal from pulsar J0900-3144 reaching Earth between 2033-2066.
- For a prototype pulsar at 1000 light-years from a BNS merger with peak strain h ~ 10^-19 at 130 million light-years, the strain at the pulsar would be enhanced to h ~ 10^-14, giving fractional spin rate change Δν/ν ~ 10^-16.
- The tidal Love number k2 is constrained to 0.05-0.15 from BNS merger observations (Abbott 2017).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous proposal (Biswal et al., 2019) that pulsars can act as remotely stationed Weber gravitational wave detectors, by providing specific predictions for when perturbed signals from known GW events and historical supernovae will reach Earth. It enables a new observational strategy: monitoring specific pulsars at predicted future dates to detect imprints of past GW events, potentially providing additional information about GW source locations (especially for black hole mergers with poor localization) and about neutron star interior properties such as equation of state. The approach contrasts with direct GW detection by LIGO/Virgo, offering the possibility to revisit events whose GW signals were missed, such as galactic supernovae recorded in astronomical records.
