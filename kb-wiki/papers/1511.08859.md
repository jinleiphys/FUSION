# Indirect methods in nuclear astrophysics

> We discuss recent developments in indirect methods used in nuclear astrophysics to determine the capture cross sections and subsequent rates of various stellar burning processes, when it is difficult to perform the corresponding direct measurements. We discuss in brief, the basic concepts of Asymptotic Normalization Coefficients, the Trojan Horse Method, the Coulomb Dissociation Method, (d,p), and charge-exchange reactions.

# Indirect methods in nuclear astrophysics

## Key claim
Indirect methods (ANC, Trojan Horse, Coulomb dissociation, (d,p), and charge-exchange reactions) can extract stellar capture cross sections and reaction rates when direct measurements are impossible due to extremely small cross sections (nanobarns to picobarns) at stellar energies or the short lifetimes of unstable nuclei; however, each method carries specific conditions, such as the requirement that the reaction be peripheral, that the quasi-free condition holds, or that nuclear breakup contributions be negligible, that limit its applicability and accuracy.

## Method
The paper reviews five indirect methods: (1) Asymptotic Normalization Coefficient (ANC) method, which extracts the amplitude of the tail of the nuclear overlap from peripheral transfer or breakup reactions and uses it to compute direct capture cross sections via Eq. (a4); (2) Trojan Horse Method (THM), which uses a two-body-to-three-body reaction under quasi-free kinematics to extract the half-off-energy-shell cross section of the binary sub-process, free from Coulomb barrier and electron screening effects; (3) Coulomb dissociation method, which measures the breakup of a projectile in the Coulomb field of a heavy target and relates it via virtual photon numbers and detailed balance to the radiative capture cross section; (4) (d,p) surrogate reactions, analyzed with the Alt-Grassberger-Sandhas (AGS) Faddeev formalism to extract neutron-induced reaction information; and (5) charge-exchange reactions such as (p,n) and (³He,t), used to obtain Gamow-Teller matrix elements for neutrino scattering and electron capture rates.

## Key numbers
- Stellar energies are typically of the order of tens or hundreds of keV/u, with cross sections as small as nanobarns or picobarns.
- For the Trojan Horse Method, the relative energy of the binary sub-process is given by \(E_{Aa} = \frac{m_a}{m_a+m_A}E_A - \epsilon_{ay}\), which can become very small or even negative despite high projectile energy \(E_A\).
- Charge-exchange reactions are typically performed at energies around 100 MeV/nucleon.
- The Coulomb dissociation method has been applied to calculate radiative neutron capture cross sections on \(^{8}\)Li, \(^{14}\)C, and \(^{15}\)N from the Coulomb breakup of \(^{9}\)Li, \(^{15}\)C, and \(^{16}\)N, respectively.

## Context
This review extends the discussions in earlier reviews by Bertulani and Gade (e.g., Akram and Carlos, Gade) and by Mukhamedzhanov (2015) on ANC applications, and by Baur et al. on the Coulomb dissociation method. It also builds on the Trojan Horse Method developments by Spitaleri and collaborators, and on the AGS formalism for three-body reactions (Alt, Grassberger, Sandhas, 1967). The paper enables future work by identifying open challenges: the need to account for relativistic effects, higher-order and higher-multipole contributions in Coulomb dissociation; the difficulty of treating the Coulomb interaction in AGS equations for (d,p) reactions, which currently works only for light nuclei; and the ongoing theoretical debate about the simplified approach to charge-exchange cross sections (Taddeucci, 1987; criticized by Bertulani, 1993).
