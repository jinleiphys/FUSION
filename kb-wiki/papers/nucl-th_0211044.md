# Comments on the nuclear symmetry energy

> According to standard textbooks, the nuclear symmetry energy originates from the {\it kinetic} energy and the {\it interaction} itself. We argue that this view requires certain modifications. We ascribe the physical origin of the {\it kinetic} term to the discreteness of fermionic levels of, in principle arbitrary binary fermionic systems, and relate its mean value directly to the average level density. Physically it connects this part also to the isoscalar part of the interaction which, at least in self-bound systems like atomic nuclei, decides upon the spatial dimensions of the system. For the general case of binary fermionic systems possible external confining potentials as well as specific boundary conditions will contribute to this part. The reliability of this concept is tested using self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the "kinetic" part of the nuclear symmetry energy should be reinterpreted as originating from the mean level density of fermionic single-particle spectra rather than from kinetic energy itself, and that the interaction part contains a linear term in isospin \(T\) which is quenched by pairing correlations in \(N \sim Z\) nuclei.

## Method

The authors used the isospin cranking model to derive analytic expressions for the symmetry energy in terms of mean level spacing \(\varepsilon\) and an effective isovector strength \(\kappa\). They performed self-consistent Skyrme Hartree-Fock (SHF) calculations for even-even isobaric chains at \(A=48, 68, 88\) using several Skyrme parametrisations (SLy4, SIII, SkO, SkP, SkXc, MSk3), first removing the isovector part of the force to extract \(\varepsilon\), then including the full force to extract \(\kappa\). Schematic BCS calculations with a constant gap were used to study the effect of pairing on the linear term.

## Key numbers

* The mean level spacing extracted from isoscalar SHF calculations for \(A=48,68,88\) chains stabilizes for \(T_z \geq 8\) and, after scaling by the isoscalar effective mass \(m^*/m\), falls within the range \(\varepsilon \approx 53/A\) to \(66/A\) MeV.
* The empirical level density parameter \(a\) ranges between \(a \approx A/10\) MeV\(^{-1}\) and \(A/8\) MeV\(^{-1}\), corresponding to the lower and upper limits of the \(\varepsilon\) estimate.
* In the schematic BCS calculations for \(A=88\), \(T_z=1\) nuclei, the parameter \(x\) in \(E_{sym}^{int} \sim \kappa T(T+x)\) is reduced to approximately 0.3 due to pairing.
* The difference \(\kappa_{BCS} - \kappa_{HF}\) is always negative, confirming that pairing quenches the linear term.

## Context

This work extends the isospin cranking model introduced by Satula et al. (2001) and challenges the textbook decomposition of the symmetry energy into kinetic and interaction parts as presented in Bohr (1969). The finding that Skyrme forces produce a linear term in the interaction part of the symmetry energy, behaving like \(\sim \langle \Delta \mathbf{T}^2 \rangle\), connects to the debate between quadratic \(E_{sym} \sim T^2\) and \(E_{sym} \sim T(T+1)\) forms. The results suggest that large-scale mass calculations using Skyrme forces, such as those by Goriely (2002), should include isospin fluctuations with an inertia given by the mean level spacing, potentially allowing forces with effective mass \(m^* \neq m\).
