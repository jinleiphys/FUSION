# Dark Matter in the Cosmos- Exploiting the Signatures of its Interaction with Nuclei

> We review various issues related to the direct detection of constituents of dark matter, which are assumed to be Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs). We specifically consider heavy WIMPs such as: 1) The lightest supersymmetric particle LSP or neutralino. 2) The lightest Kaluza-Klein particles in theories of extra dimensions and 3) other extensions of the standard model. In order to get the event rates one needs information about the structure of the nucleon as well as as the structure of the nucleus and the WIMP velocity distribution. These are also examined Since the expected event rates for detecting the recoiling nucleus are extremely low and the signal does not have a characteristic signature to discriminate them against background we consider some additional aspects of the WIMP nucleus interaction, such as the periodic behavior of the rates due to the motion of Earth (modulation effect). Since, unfortunately, this is characterized by a small amplitude we consider other options such as directional experiments, which measure not only the energy of the recoiling nuclei but their direction as well. In these, albeit hard, experiments one can exploit two very characteristic signatures: a)large asymmetries and b) interesting modulation patterns. Furthermore we extended our study to include evaluation of the rates for other than recoil searches such as: i) Transitions to excited states, ii) Detection of recoiling electrons produced during the neutralino-nucleus interaction and iii) Observation of hard X-rays following the de-excitation of the ionized atom.

## Key claim

This review argues that while direct detection of WIMP dark matter via nuclear recoils suffers from extremely low event rates and no characteristic background-discriminating signature, additional signatures such as annual modulation (though small in amplitude), directional event rate asymmetries, and non-recoil signals (transitions to excited states, recoiling electrons, and hard X-rays from de-excitation) can be exploited to identify a WIMP signal, particularly for heavy WIMPs like Kaluza-Klein particles with masses around 1 TeV.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for computing WIMP-nucleus event rates, covering three candidate WIMP types: the lightest supersymmetric particle (neutralino), Kaluza-Klein gauge bosons and neutrinos from extra-dimensional theories, and other non-SUSY models. It details the Feynman diagrams for each candidate (Z-exchange, s-quark exchange, Higgs exchange, and K-K quark mediation), the procedure for going from quark-level to nucleon-level couplings (including scalar and spin contributions), and the folding of rates with a Maxwell-Boltzmann velocity distribution for the WIMP halo, including the Earth's motion for modulation and directional studies.

## Key numbers

* The cosmological energy budget is estimated at approximately 5% baryonic matter, 25% cold dark matter, and 70% dark energy.
* The local WIMP density is taken as ρ(0) ≈ 0.3 GeV/cm³.
* For a K-K WIMP with mass 1 TeV, the average WIMP kinetic energy is 0.4 MeV, enabling nuclear excitation and detection of de-excitation photons.
* The characteristic velocity of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution is υ₀ ≈ 2.2 × 10⁵ km/s (229 km/s), with an escape velocity cutoff of υ_esc = 2.84 υ₀.
* The dimensionless energy transfer u is defined with Q₀ = [m_p A b]⁻² = 40 A⁻⁴/³ MeV.
* For the spin contribution in K-K theories, the nucleon-level couplings are a_p = 0.67 and a_n = -0.15, derived from Δu = 0.78 ± 0.02, Δd = -0.48 ± 0.02, Δs = -0.15 ± 0.02.
* For the neutralino scalar coupling, example quark-level parameters are f_d = 0.046, f_u = 0.025, f_s = 0.400, f_c = 0.050, f_b = 0.055, f_t = 0.095.
* The isoscalar and isovector axial renormalization coefficients for the neutralino are g_A⁰ = 0.13 and g_A¹ = 1.26.

## Context

This review extends the theoretical treatment of direct dark matter detection beyond the standard neutralino (LSP) scenario, incorporating Kaluza-Klein WIMPs from extra-dimensional models (Servant 2002, Servant 2002, Cembranos et al. 2002) and other non-SUSY candidates (Ma 2006, Cirelli et al. 2006, Gudnason et al. 2006). It builds on prior work on nuclear matrix elements (Ressell et al. 1993, Suhonen 2003) and velocity distributions (Drukier et al. 1986, Green 2002, Sikivie 1999) to enable more precise predictions for experiments like DAMA, EDELWEISS, and CDMS, and for planned directional detectors such as UKDMC and DRIFT.
