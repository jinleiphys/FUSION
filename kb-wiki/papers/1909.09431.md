# Shell closure at $N=34$ and the $^{48}$Si nucleus

> By using a non-relativistic independent particle model we investigate the mechanism promoting 34 as new magic number. We carried out Hartree-Fock plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer and Quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation calculations by consistently using the same finite-range interaction in all the three steps of our approach. We used four Gogny-like interactions, with and without tensor terms. We find that the shell closure for $N=34$ neutrons appears in isotones with $Z<26\,$ protons. The smaller is the proton number, the more evident is the shell closure at $N=34$. An ideal nucleus to investigate this effect should be $^{48}$Si, as it has been recently suggested. However, some discrepancies occur between the results obtained with the four effective interactions we used concerning the position of the two-neutron drip line and, therefore, the existence of $^{48}$Si. The experimental identification of this nucleus could shed light about the shell evolution in nuclei far from the stability valley and put stringent tests on nuclear structure theories.

## Key claim

The shell closure at N=34 appears in isotones with Z < 26 protons, and the smaller the proton number, the more evident the closure; however, the existence of the ideal test nucleus ⁴⁸Si is uncertain because different Gogny-like interactions disagree on whether it lies inside or beyond the two-neutron drip line.

## Method

The authors performed non-relativistic Hartree-Fock (HF) plus Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer (BCS) calculations for ground states and Quasi-particle Random Phase Approximation (QRPA) calculations for excited states, using the same finite-range Gogny interaction consistently in all three steps. Four Gogny-like parameterizations were employed: D1S, D1M, D1ST2a (with tensor terms), and D1MTd (with tensor terms). The single-particle energies of the 1f₅/₂ and 2p₁/₂ levels were computed for a sequence of N=34 isotones from ⁴⁸Si to ⁶⁶Ge, and pairing-related quantities and 2⁺ excitation energies were calculated for silicon isotopes from ³²Si to ⁴⁸Si.

## Key numbers

- For the Woods-Saxon potential with V₀ = 50 MeV, V_ls = 9 MeV, r₀ = 1.25 fm, a = 0.53 fm, and R = 4.3 fm, the 2p₁/₂ level lies below the 1f₅/₂ level, producing the N=34 shell closure; increasing R to 4.5 fm reverses this ordering.
- The largest relative difference between calculated and experimental binding energies across the investigated nuclei is about 1.3%, found for the most deformed nuclei ⁶⁴Zn and ⁶⁶Ge.
- For ⁴⁸Si, the HF+BCS binding energies are: D1S = -311.2 MeV, D1ST2a = -305.7 MeV, D1M = -308.5 MeV, D1MTd = -306.7 MeV; the numerical uncertainty of the binding energies is about 0.3%.
- For ⁴⁸Si with the D1ST2a interaction, the particle-number fluctuation ΔN² = 7×10⁻⁴, the pairing energy per nucleon B_p/A = 0.10 MeV, and the relative rms radius change Δr = 2×10⁻⁵; with D1MTd, ΔN² = 5×10⁻⁵, B_p/A = 0.07 MeV, and Δr = 7×10⁻⁷.
- The QRPA 2⁺ excitation energy for ⁴⁸Si is 1.61 MeV with both D1ST2a and D1MTd interactions; the B(E2) values are 30.1 e² fm⁴ (D1ST2a) and 24.7 e² fm⁴ (D1MTd).
- For ⁴⁰Si, the calculated 2⁺ excitation energy is 1.45 MeV (D1ST2a) and 1.49 MeV (D1MTd), compared to the experimental value of 0.986 MeV; for ⁴²Si, the calculated values are 1.24 MeV (D1ST2a) and 1.28 MeV (D1MTd), compared to 0.77 MeV experimentally.

## Context

This work extends the investigation of the N=34 magic number beyond the well-studied ⁵⁴Ca (where the closure is established) and ⁵⁶Ti (where conclusions remain unclear), as reviewed by Gade and Glasmacher (2008) and Sorlin and Porquet (2008). The authors confirm the prediction by Li et al. (2018) that ⁴⁸Si is a good candidate for observing the N=34 shell closure, but they find that the nucleus lies at the edge of the two-neutron drip line, with different interactions giving conflicting results; this contrasts with the relativistic calculations of Li et al. (2018) and the Skyrme-based results of Erler et al. (2012), while agreeing with the HFB results from the Amedee database using D1S that place ⁴⁸Si beyond the drip line. Experimental identification of ⁴⁸Si would test shell evolution far from stability and constrain nuclear structure theories.
