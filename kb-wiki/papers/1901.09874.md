# Confronting gravitational-wave observations with modern nuclear physics constraints

> Multi-messenger observations of neutron star (NS) mergers have the potential to revolutionize nuclear astrophysics. They will improve our understanding of nucleosynthesis, provide insights about the equation of state (EOS) of strongly-interacting matter at high densities, and enable tests of the theory of gravity and of dark matter. Here, we focus on the EOS, where both gravitational waves (GWs) from neutron-star mergers and X-ray observations from space-based detectors such as NICER will provide more stringent constraints on the structure of neutron stars. Furthermore, recent advances in nuclear theory have enabled reliable calculations of the EOS at low densities using effective field theory based Hamiltonians and advanced techniques to solve the quantum many-body problem. In this paper, we address how the first observation of GWs from GW170817 can be combined with modern calculations of the EOS to extract useful insights about the EOS of matter encountered inside neutron stars. We analyze the impact of various uncertainties, the role of phase transitions in the NS core, and discuss how future observations will improve our understanding of dense matter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that combining gravitational-wave observations from GW170817 with modern chiral effective field theory (EFT) calculations of neutron matter up to twice saturation density can significantly reduce uncertainties in the neutron-star equation of state (EOS), but only if the chiral EFT constraints are applied up to the higher density of \(2n_{\text{sat}}\) rather than just up to \(n_{\text{sat}}\); the authors explicitly note that the chiral EFT uncertainty bands grow rapidly with density and that the expansion is expected to break down beyond \(2n_{\text{sat}}\).

## Method

The authors construct two complementary EOS models for neutron-star matter: a minimal model (MM) based on a smooth Taylor expansion of the energy per particle around saturation density using empirical nuclear matter parameters, and a maximal model (CSM) that parametrizes the speed of sound above a transition density \(n_{\text{tr}}\) using random linear segments, allowing for strong first-order phase transitions. Both models are constrained at low densities (up to either \(n_{\text{sat}}\) or \(2n_{\text{sat}}\)) by auxiliary-field diffusion Monte Carlo (AFDMC) calculations of pure neutron matter using local chiral EFT interactions at N\(^2\)LO with systematic uncertainty estimates. The authors generate thousands of EOSs via Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo sampling (for the MM) or random sampling (for the CSM), enforcing causality, stability, and a maximum mass constraint of \(M_{\text{max}} \geq 1.9 M_\odot\) based on the two heaviest observed pulsars.

## Key numbers

- The chiral EFT neutron-matter calculations are performed up to \(2n_{\text{sat}}\), where \(n_{\text{sat}} = 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3} = 2.7 \times 10^{14}\ \text{g cm}^{-3}\).
- For the MM with \(n_{\text{tr}} = n_{\text{sat}}\), the radius of a \(1.4 M_\odot\) neutron star ranges from \(10.9\) to \(13.5\) km; for the CSM with the same \(n_{\text{tr}}\), the range is \(8.4\) to \(15.2\) km.
- For the MM with \(n_{\text{tr}} = 2 n_{\text{sat}}\), the radius of a \(1.4 M_\odot\) neutron star ranges from \(10.9\) to \(12.0\) km; for the CSM with the same \(n_{\text{tr}}\), the range is \(8.7\) to \(12.6\) km.
- The maximum neutron-star mass for the CSM is nearly \(4 M_\odot\) when \(n_{\text{tr}} = n_{\text{sat}}\), but reduces to \(2.9 M_\odot\) when \(n_{\text{tr}} = 2 n_{\text{sat}}\).
- The GW170817 tidal deformability constraint \(70 \leq \tilde{\Lambda} \leq 720\) is applied in one analysis case.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier analysis (Tews et al., 2018) by providing additional details on how GW170817 constraints can be combined with chiral EFT calculations of neutron matter. The MM approach builds on the meta-model ELFc described by Margueron et al. (2017), while the CSM extends the speed-of-sound parametrization of Alford et al. (2013) and is similar to piecewise polytrope approaches used by Hebeler et al. (2013) and Annala et al. (2017). The results enable future comparisons with NICER X-ray observations and show that a measured neutron-star radius of about 10 km would imply a softening phase transition, hinting at new phases of matter in neutron-star cores.
