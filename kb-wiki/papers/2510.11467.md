# Proton-rich production of lanthanides: the $νi$ process

> The astrophysical origin of the lanthanides is an open question in nuclear astrophysics. Besides the widely studied $s$, $i$, and $r$ processes in moderately-to-strongly neutron-rich environments, an intriguing alternative site for lanthanide production could in fact be robustly $\textit{proton-rich}$ matter outflows from core-collapse supernovae under specific conditions -- in particular, high-entropy winds with enhanced neutrino luminosity and fast dynamical timescales. In this environment, excess protons present after charged particle reactions have ceased can continue to be converted to neutrons by (anti-)neutrino interactions, producing a neutron capture reaction flow up to A~200. This scenario, christened the $νi$ process in a recent paper, has previously been discussed as a possibility. Here, we examine the prospects for $νi$ process through the lens of stellar abundance patterns, bolometric lightcurves, and galactic chemical evolution models, with a particular focus on hypernovae as candidate sites. We identify specific lanthanide signatures for which the $νi$ process can provide a credible alternative to $r$/$i$ processes.

## Key claim

The paper argues that lanthanides can be produced in proton-rich outflows from core-collapse supernovae under specific conditions (high-entropy winds with enhanced neutrino luminosity and fast dynamical timescales), and that this "nu i process" can provide a credible alternative or supplement to the r and i processes for explaining observed lanthanide abundances in certain metal-poor stars, provided the neutrino luminosity is 3-5 times the baseline value for a standard core-collapse supernova.

## Method

The authors performed nucleosynthesis simulations using the PRISM nuclear reaction network code with REACLIB reaction rates and NUBASE beta-decay properties, adopting parameterized neutrino-driven wind trajectories from Wanajo (2011) and Duan (2011) with enhanced neutrino luminosities (3-5 times baseline) and average energies (9-13 MeV). They compared the resulting abundance patterns to stellar observations using chi-squared minimization fitting procedures, and incorporated the yields into galactic chemical evolution models using the OMEGA+ code with various neutron star merger delay time distributions.

## Key numbers

- Baseline neutrino luminosity: L_nu0 = 9.0 x 10^51 erg/s with decay timescale tau = 3.0 s for each neutrino species
- Neutrino luminosities explored: 3 times baseline (average energy 13.0 MeV, weak equilibrium Y_e ~ 0.579) and 5 times baseline (average energy 9.0 MeV, Y_e ~ 0.613)
- Entropy per baryon for trajectories: s/k = 150 (Wanajo2011) and s/k ~ 200 (Duan2011)
- Maximum mass number produced: A > 200 for Duan2011 trajectory calculations
- Europium enhancement threshold for nu i process: [Eu/Fe]_nu i > +0.3 with [Ba/Eu]_nu i < 0
- Observed star J0453-2437: [Eu/Fe] = +0.59 (r-I star classification)
- Observed star J2036-0714: [C/Fe] = +0.88, [Eu/Fe] = +0.48 (CEMP-r star classification)
- CEMP-r/s stars best-fit nu i process contribution: F_nu i > 40%
- Average [Zr/Eu] ratio for Car 97508 and HE 0243-3044: -0.74
- nu i process occurrence rates explored: 1%, 10%, and 1-10% of normal CCSN rate
- NSM ejecta mass assumed: 10^-2 M_sun per event

## Context

This work extends the nu i process concept introduced by Balantekin (2024) by demonstrating that enhanced neutrino luminosities (consistent with hypernova simulations by Nakazato (2021) and Fujibayashi (2015)) can produce the effect without requiring collective neutrino flavor oscillations. The paper provides an alternative to the r-process (typically associated with neutron star mergers as observed in GW170817) and i-process explanations for lanthanide abundances in metal-poor stars, particularly CEMP-r/s stars that have previously been explained by combinations of s- and r-process nucleosynthesis (Bisterzo 2011, 2012) or i-process models (Hampel 2019, Choplin 2022). The results enable future observational tests by identifying specific elements (Yb, Hf, Os at Z=70, 72, 76) that could distinguish nu i process from weak r-process enrichment in metal-poor stars.
