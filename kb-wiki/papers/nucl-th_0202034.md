# Coupled fermion and boson production in a strong background mean-field

> We derive quantum kinetic equations for fermion and boson production starting from a phi^4 Lagrangian with minimal coupling to fermions. Decomposing the scalar field into a mean-field part and fluctuations we obtain spontaneous pair creation driven by a self-interacting strong background field. The produced fermion and boson pairs are self-consistently coupled. Consequently back reactions arise from fermion and boson currents determining the time dependent self-interacting background mean-field. We explore the numerical solution in flux tube geometry for the time evolution of the mean-field as well as for the number- and energy densities for fermions and bosons. We find that after a characteristic time all energy is converted from the background mean-field to particle creation. Applying this general approach to the production of ``quarks'' and ``gluons'' a typical time scale for the collapse of the flux tube is 1.5 fm/c.

# Coupled fermion and boson production in a strong background mean-field

## Key claim

The authors derive a self-consistent quantum kinetic framework for coupled fermion and boson pair production driven by a dynamical, self-interacting background mean-field, finding that the flux tube collapses at approximately 1.5 fm/c when applied to "quark" and "gluon" production with zero gluon mass; however, this collapse time depends on the inclusion of self-interaction and the boson mass, and for massive gluons weak plasma oscillations persist rather than complete field collapse.

## Method

Starting from a phi^4 Lagrangian with minimal coupling to fermions, the scalar field is decomposed into a space-homogeneous background mean-field and quantum fluctuations. Quantum kinetic equations for the single-particle distribution functions of fermions and bosons are derived via time-dependent Bogolyubov transformation, including Fermi suppression and Bose enhancement factors. The equations are solved numerically in cylinder geometry (flux tube) with boundary conditions that discretize transverse momentum and provide an infrared cutoff, with the background mean-field evolution determined self-consistently by fermionic and bosonic currents (back reaction). The fermion mass is taken as momentum- and temperature-dependent from a QCD Dyson-Schwinger equation model, while the boson mass is treated as either zero or 0.5 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Initial field strength: e_+ sigma(t=0) = 4 GeV^2, corresponding to an initial energy density of approximately 700 GeV/fm^3.
- Flux tube collapse time for zero gluon mass: tau_f ~ 1.5 fm/c (with self-interaction); for massive gluons (m_+ = 0.5 GeV) the field does not disappear completely but oscillates at about 10% of the initial value.
- Plasma oscillation period in the early phase: tau_pl ~ 0.5 fm/c.
- Flux tube radii considered: 1 fm, 2 fm, and 3 fm, giving infrared cutoffs Lambda^IR of 0.48 GeV, 0.24 GeV, and 0.16 GeV respectively.
- Quark degeneracy factor: g_- = 18 (three flavors); gluon degeneracy factor: g_+ = 16.
- Final energy density reached at large times: approximately 200 GeV/fm^3, corresponding to a quasi-equilibrium temperature of about 1 GeV.
- Current quark mass: m_0 = 5 MeV; mass scale parameter eta = 1 GeV.
- For zero gluon mass with self-interaction, the number density becomes constant at tau_f ~ 1.5 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends the quantum kinetic approach to pair creation (the "quantum Vlasov equation") previously developed for fermions or bosons separately in external fields (gsi, kme) by introducing a self-consistent dynamical mean-field that couples fermion and boson production via back reactions. It builds on the flux tube model for spontaneous particle creation in chromo-electric fields (Casher, anders83) and incorporates cylindrical boundary conditions similar to Eisenberg (1995). The approach enables a field-theoretically motivated description of the pre-equilibrium phase in ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions, where the collapse time of the flux tube (1.5 fm/c) provides a characteristic scale for energy conversion from the background field to produced partons, with the system remaining out of equilibrium (anisotropic pressure) even after production stops.
