# On the test of the modified BCS at finite temperature

> The results and conclusions by Ponomarev and Vdovin [Phys. Rev. C {\bf 72}, 034309 (2005)] are inadequate to judge the applicability of the modified BCS because they were obtained either in the temperature region, where the use of zero-temperature single-particle spectra is no longer justified, or in too limited configuration spaces.

## Key claim
The test by Ponomarev and Vdovin (2005) is inadequate to judge the modified BCS (MBCS) applicability because their results were obtained either in temperature regions where zero-temperature single-particle spectra are no longer justified, or within configuration spaces that are too limited for the MBCS to be properly applied.

## Method
The authors performed test calculations of the neutron pairing gap for 120Sn using a temperature-dependent single-particle spectrum where neutron energies were modified as εj' = εj(1 + γT²) with different γ values for levels below and above the 1g9/2 orbital. They also carried out calculations within the picket-fence model (PFM) for systems with N=10 particles and Ω=10 or Ω=11 levels, and for larger systems with Ω=50 and 100, comparing MBCS results with exact solutions and BCS results.

## Key numbers
- For 120Sn, the major-shell spacing is approximately 5 MeV, so temperature T should be small compared to this value for zero-temperature single-particle energies to be valid.
- For Ni isotopes, the major-shell spacing between (28-50) and (50-82) shells is about 3.6 MeV, so the valid temperature region is T much less than 3.6 MeV.
- With a temperature-dependent spectrum for 120Sn (γ = -1.2×10⁻⁴ for levels below 1g9/2, and γ = 0.49×10⁻³ or -0.7×10⁻³ for levels above), the MBCS gap shows a flat tail of around 0.2 MeV from T=5 MeV up to T=7 MeV.
- For the PFM with N=10 particles and Ω=10 levels (G=0.4 MeV), the MBCS breaks down at T close to 1.78 MeV.
- For the PFM with Ω=50 and 100, the maximum temperature TM exceeds 5 MeV, and the MBCS gap behaves qualitatively well up to T approximately 5-6 MeV.
- Adding one valence level (Ω=11) for N=10 particles increases the excitation energy E* by only about 10% at T=2 MeV.

## Context
This Comment extends the debate on the modified BCS theory at finite temperature, which was proposed to account for quasiparticle-number fluctuations that the conventional BCS neglects. The authors argue that the test by Ponomarev and Vdovin (2005) suffers from the same limitations as earlier work by Bonche et al. (1984) showed that temperature effects on single-particle energies cannot be neglected above 4 MeV. The results support the MBCS as a good approximation up to high temperatures, consistent with predictions by Moretto (1972), exact solutions by Egido (1990), and experimental data, while enabling further applications of MBCS in finite-temperature nuclear structure calculations.
