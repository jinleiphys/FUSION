# Inclusive photoproduction of vector quarkonium in ultra-peripheral collisions at the LHC

> We explore the possibility of using ultra-peripheral proton-lead collisions at the LHC to study inclusive vector-quarkonium photoproduction, that occurs when a quasi-real photon emitted by a fully stripped lead ion breaks a proton to produce a vector quarkonium. Owing to the extremely large energies of the colliding hadrons circulating in the LHC, the range of accessible photon-nucleon centre-of-mass energies, W_gamma+p, largely exceeds what has been and will be studied at lepton-hadron colliders, HERA and the EIC. We perform a tune to HERA photoproduction data, use this tune to predict the yields of photoproduced J/psi, and estimate the corresponding transverse-momentum reach at LHC experiments. We also model the hadroproduction background and demonstrate that inclusive photoproduction can be isolated at the LHC from such background by imposing constraints on the hadronic activity in the lead-going direction at mid, forward, or far-forward rapidities depending on the capability of the detector under consideration. We find that the resulting cross sections are large enough to be measured by ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, and LHCb. We estimate the background-to-signal ratio after isolation to be of the order of 0.001 and 0.1 in the low and large transverse-momentum regions, respectively. In addition, we propose and assess the Jacquet-Blondel method to reconstruct the photon-nucleon centre-of-mass energy and the fractional energy of the quarkonium with respect to the photon.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that inclusive photoproduction of vector quarkonia (specifically J/psi) can be isolated from the much larger hadroproduction background in ultra-peripheral proton-lead collisions at the LHC, using detector-specific selection criteria such as rapidity gaps and zero-degree calorimeter information, provided the photon-nucleon centre-of-mass energy reach extends from 240 GeV (HERA) up to 1.4 TeV.

## Method

The authors performed a tune of the HO+PYTHIA Monte Carlo generator to HERA photoproduction data for J/psi, using multiplicative factors in bins of transverse momentum (pT) up to 10 GeV and a linear scale factor for pT above 10 GeV, separately for the color-singlet (3S1[1]) and color-octet (1S0[8]) contributions. They similarly tuned hadroproduction to LHCb proton-proton data at sqrt(s)=5 TeV for the 3S1[1] and 3S1[8] states. Using these tunes, they simulated J/psi yields in proton-lead collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=8.16 TeV and evaluated background rejection via rapidity-gap requirements (Delta_eta_gamma) and zero-neutron tagging from the lead ion, applying the Jacquet-Blondel method to reconstruct W_gamma+p and the fractional energy z.

## Key numbers

- The accessible photon-nucleon centre-of-mass energy W_gamma+p extends from 240 GeV (HERA) to 1.4 TeV at the LHC
- The transverse momentum reach for photoproduced J/psi extends from 10 GeV (HERA) to 20 GeV at the LHC
- The background-to-signal ratio after isolation is estimated to be 0.001 in the low-pT region and 0.1 in the large-pT region
- The total inclusive J/psi photoproduction cross section in pPb collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=8.16 TeV is 55 microbarns
- The integrated signal-over-background ratios after selection are 8 (ALICE), 30 (ATLAS), 160 (CMS low-pT configuration), and 20 (LHCb)
- The probability of zero neutron emission from the lead ion in photoproduction is 99.99% of the total cross section in pPb collisions
- The hadroproduction background is a factor of O(10^2) greater than the signal at low pT and O(10^4) greater at high pT

## Context

This work extends the study of inclusive quarkonium photoproduction from lepton-proton collisions at HERA (H1 and ZEUS experiments, 1996-2010) to hadron-hadron collisions at the LHC, where the photon beam is provided by the electromagnetic field of the lead ion. The authors build on previous exclusive photoproduction studies in ultra-peripheral collisions and propose methods to isolate inclusive photoproduction using rapidity gaps (similar to techniques employed by ATLAS in 2012) and zero-degree calorimeters. This enables future measurements that can better constrain the quarkonium production mechanism and improve knowledge of nucleon structure, extending the kinematic reach beyond what will be achievable at the Electron-Ion Collider.
