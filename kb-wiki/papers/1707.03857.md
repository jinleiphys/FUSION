# Generalizing spin and pseudospin symmetries for relativistic spin 1/2 fermions

> We propose a generalization of pseudospin and spin symmetries, the SU(2) symmetries of Dirac equation with scalar and vector mean-field potentials originally found independently in the 70's by Smith and Tassie, and Bell and Ruegg. As relativistic symmetries, they have been extensively researched and applied to several physical systems for the last 18 years. The main feature of these symmetries is the suppression of the spin-orbit coupling either in the upper or lower components of the Dirac spinor, thereby turning the respective second-order equations into Schrödinger-like equations, i.e, without a matrix structure. In this paper we use the original formalism of Bell and Ruegg to derive general requirements for the Lorentz structures of potentials in order to have these SU(2) symmetries in the Dirac equation, again allowing for the suppression of the matrix structure of the second-order equation of either the upper or lower components of the Dirac spinor. Furthermore, we derive equivalent conditions for spin and pseudospin symmetries with 2- and 1-dimensional potentials and list some possible candidates for 3, 2, and 1 dimensions. We suggest applications for physical systems in three and two dimensions, namely electrons in graphene.

# Generalizing spin and pseudospin symmetries for relativistic spin 1/2 fermions

## Key claim
The authors derive general conditions under which a Dirac Hamiltonian with a potential of general Lorentz structure can exhibit SU(2) spin or pseudospin symmetries, suppressing the matrix structure of the second-order equation for either the upper or lower spinor component. The generalization extends beyond the well-known scalar-plus-vector potential case to include pseudoscalar potentials in three dimensions, and additional vector and tensor potentials in two and one dimensions, provided the fermion motion is constrained accordingly.

## Method
The authors follow the Bell and Ruegg (1970s) projector formalism, introducing operators \(P_\pm = (I \pm O)/2\) where \(O\) is a matrix satisfying \(O^2 = I\) and \(\{\alpha_i, O\} = 0\). They decompose the Dirac spinor into projected components \(\psi_\pm = P_\pm \psi\) and require one of the potential combinations \(V_\pm = V_v \pm V_O\) to be constant, yielding a Schrödinger-like second-order equation for the other component. They then derive the SU(2) generators that commute with the resulting Hamiltonian and examine which matrices \(O\) satisfy the necessary algebraic conditions in 3, 2, and 1 spatial dimensions.

## Key numbers
- The condition for spin symmetry is \(V_- = C_-\) (constant), i.e., \(V_v = V_O + C_-\), leading to a Schrödinger-type equation \(\hat{\mathbf{p}}^2 \psi_+ = (E - C_-)(E - V_+) \psi_+\) for the upper component.
- The condition for pseudospin symmetry is \(V_+ = C_+\) (constant), i.e., \(V_v = -V_O + C_+\), yielding \(\hat{\mathbf{p}}^2 \psi_- = (E - C_+)(E - V_-) \psi_-\) for the lower component.
- In three dimensions, the matrices satisfying the conditions are \(O = \beta = \gamma^0\) (scalar) and \(O = i\gamma^0\gamma^5\) (pseudoscalar).
- For two-dimensional systems, \(O = \alpha_3 = \gamma^0\gamma^3\) with \(\hat{p}_3 \psi = 0\) and \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon} = \epsilon \hat{e}_z\) gives a U(1) symmetry generated by \(\Sigma_3\).
- For one-dimensional systems, \(O = i\beta \boldsymbol{\lambda} \cdot \boldsymbol{\alpha}\) with \(\boldsymbol{\lambda} = \hat{e}_z\) yields a 1-dimensional potential depending only on \(z\).

## Context
This work generalizes the spin and pseudospin symmetries originally found independently by Smith and Tassie (1970s) and Bell and Ruegg (1970s) for scalar and vector potentials, and later extensively studied by Ginocchio (1997) in the context of nuclear single-particle levels near the Fermi surface. The authors extend the formalism to pseudoscalar potentials in three dimensions and to vector and tensor potentials in reduced dimensions, suggesting applications to electrons in graphene described by a massless 3+1 Dirac equation with two-dimensional potentials. This opens the possibility of studying the effects of these symmetries and their breaking in condensed matter systems such as Dirac electrons in graphene, as well as in hadronic systems with pion and omega meson mean fields.
