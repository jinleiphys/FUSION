# Chiral anomaly induces superconducting baryon crystal

> It was previously shown within chiral perturbation theory that the ground state of QCD in a sufficiently large magnetic field and at nonvanishing, but not too large, baryon chemical potential is a so-called chiral soliton lattice. The crucial ingredient of this observation was the chiral anomaly in the form of a Wess-Zumino-Witten term, which couples the baryon chemical potential to the magnetic field and the gradient of the neutral pion field. It was also shown that the chiral soliton lattice becomes unstable towards charged pion condensation at larger magnetic fields. We point out that this instability bears a striking resemblance to the second critical magnetic field of a type-II superconductor, however with the superconducting phase appearing upon increasing the magnetic field. The resulting phase has a periodically varying charged pion condensate that coexists with a neutral pion supercurrent. We construct this phase analytically in the chiral limit and show that it is energetically preferred. Just like an ordinary type-II superconductor, it exhibits a hexagonal array of magnetic flux tubes, and, due to the chiral anomaly, a spatially oscillating baryon number of the same crystalline structure.

## Key claim

The paper claims that the chiral soliton lattice (CSL) phase of QCD in a magnetic field becomes unstable toward a charged pion condensate at a critical field \(B_{c2}\), and that this instability is analogous to the second critical field of a type-II superconductor, but with the superconducting phase appearing upon *increasing* the magnetic field. The resulting phase is constructed analytically only in the chiral limit (\(m_\pi = 0\)), and the authors caution that chiral perturbation theory may not be reliable at the large chemical potentials and magnetic fields involved, and that actual baryonic matter may compete with or replace the predicted phase.

## Method

The authors work within two-flavor chiral perturbation theory, including the chiral anomaly via a Wess-Zumino-Witten term. They linearize the equation of motion for the charged pion field \(\varphi\) around the CSL background to identify the instability at \(B_{c2}\), then construct the nonlinear charged pion condensate above \(B_{c2}\) using an expansion analogous to Abrikosov's Ginzburg-Landau treatment of type-II superconductors. The calculation is performed in the static, locally charge-neutral limit, and the resulting phase is solved analytically in the chiral limit, yielding a hexagonal array of magnetic flux tubes with a spatially oscillating baryon number.

## Key numbers

- Critical magnetic field for the instability: \(B_{c2} = 16\pi^4 f_\pi^4 / (e \mu^2)\), where \(f_\pi\) is the pion decay constant and \(e\) is the elementary charge.
- In natural Heaviside-Lorentz units, \(e\langle B\rangle = 0.1\ \text{GeV}^2 \simeq 5.18\, m_\pi^2\) corresponds to \(e\langle B\rangle \simeq 5.12 \times 10^{18}\ \text{G}\).
- The baryon density in the CSL phase (chiral limit) is \(n_B^{\text{CSL}} = e^2 \mu \langle B\rangle^2 / (16\pi^4 f_\pi^2)\).
- The onset of nuclear matter at zero magnetic field is at \(\mu \simeq 923\ \text{MeV}\).
- The typical scale for chiral symmetry breaking is \(4\pi f_\pi \sim 1\ \text{GeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the earlier prediction of the chiral soliton lattice by Brauner and Yamamoto (2016) and the analysis of its instability toward charged pion condensation by Son and Stephanov (2007) and Brauner and Yamamoto (2016). It constructs the previously unidentified "\(\pi^\pm\) lattice" phase beyond the instability, showing it is energetically preferred over the CSL phase above \(B_{c2}\). The method is directly adapted from Abrikosov's (1957) Ginzburg-Landau theory of type-II superconductors, but with the inverted order of phases (superconductivity appears upon increasing the magnetic field). The results enable future studies of the QCD phase diagram at large magnetic fields, potentially relevant for neutron star interiors and heavy-ion collisions, though the authors note that finite-temperature extensions and inclusion of baryonic degrees of freedom are needed for direct phenomenological application.
