# Towards a unified description of isotopic fragment properties in spontaneous and fusion-induced fission within a 4D dynamical Langevin model

> Spontaneous fission of 252Cf and fusion-induced fission of 250Cf are investigated within a multi-dimensional Langevin model. The potential-energy surface is calculated in the macroscopic-microscopic LSD+Yukawa-folded approach using the four-dimensional Fourier-over-Spheroid shape parametrization. The dynamical evolution described by the Langevin equation is coupled to neutron evaporation, thereby allowing for the possibility of multi-chance fission. Charge equilibration and excitation-energy sharing between the fragments emerging at scission are evaluated, and their de-excitation is finally computed. The correlation between various observables, particularly the isotopic properties of the fragments, is discussed and compared with the experiment whenever available. The theoretical predictions are generally in good agreement with the data.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a unified 4D dynamical Langevin model, which couples shape evolution to neutron evaporation and charge equilibration, can simultaneously describe spontaneous fission of 252Cf and fusion-induced fission of 250Cf at 46 MeV excitation energy. The hedge is that while integral mass and charge yields are reproduced with similar quality for both systems, the isotopic yield predictions are noticeably worse for the higher-energy fusion-induced case, suggesting possible deficiencies in treating multi-chance fission or evaporation.

## Method
The authors use a macroscopic-microscopic approach with the LSD (Lublin-Strasbourg-Drop) formula for the macroscopic part and Yukawa-folded single-particle potential with Strutinsky shell corrections for the microscopic part, all within a 4D Fourier-over-Spheroid (FoS) shape parametrization (elongation c, reflection asymmetry a3, neck a4, and non-axiality eta). The dissipative fission dynamics is described by a 3D Langevin equation (restricted to c, a3, a4) with inertia and friction tensors evaluated in the irrotational flow and wall approximations, coupled to a Weisskopf-like model for neutron evaporation that allows multi-chance fission. Charge equilibration between fragments at scission is treated via a Wigner function based on the LSD energy of the two-fragment system, and fragment de-excitation by neutron emission is computed afterward.

## Key numbers
- Spontaneous fission of 252Cf: ground state minimum at elongation c=1.14 and a4=0.01; asymmetric fission valley ends at c approximately 2.2 with heavy fragment mass Ah approximately 150; symmetric valley ends at c approximately 2.8 with Ah approximately 126.
- For 250Cf at E* = 46 MeV: most probable angular momentum L = 20 hbar; temperature T approximately 1.4 MeV.
- Pre-scission neutron multiplicity for 250Cf: average nu_pre = 2.7; most probable emission is 3 neutrons (57.9% probability); probability of no pre-scission neutron emission is 1.6%.
- Distribution of fissioning Cf isotopes after pre-scission neutron emission: 246Cf (11.5%, Eth=15.8 MeV), 247Cf (57.9%, Eth=20.4 MeV), 248Cf (22.1%, Eth=27.3 MeV), 249Cf (6.9%, Eth=35.7 MeV), 250Cf (1.6%, Eth=45.5 MeV).
- For 250Cf at E* = 46 MeV: TKE approximately 140 MeV for symmetric and extreme mass splits; TKE approximately 160 MeV for fragments around A=110 and A=140.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous studies (Pomorski et al. 2017, 2020, 2021; Liu et al. 2021; Karpov et al. 2021) on fragment mass yields at low excitation energy, and their recent model developments (Pomorski et al. 2023a, 2023b, 2023c) that introduced charge equilibration beyond the Unchanged-Charge-Density assumption and coupled Langevin dynamics to neutron evaporation. The present paper applies this model for the first time to fission at higher excitation energy (46 MeV), testing the temperature dependence of microscopic energy corrections and transport parameters. The results enable future systematic studies of isotopic yields across different excitation energies and fissioning systems, though the authors note that the treatment of multi-chance fission and evaporation may need further refinement.
