# Physics of Strange Matter

> Relativistic heavy ion collisions offer the possibility to produce exotic metastable states of nuclear matter containing (roughly) equal number of strangeness compared to the content in baryon number. The reasoning of both their stability and existence, the possible distillation of strangeness necessary for their formation and the chances for their detection are reviewed. In the later respect emphasize is put on the properties of small lumps of strange quark matter with respect to their stability against strong or weak hadronic decays. In addition, implications in astrophysics like the properties of neutron stars and the issue of baryonic dark matter will be discussed.

# Physics of Strange Matter

## Key claim

Relativistic heavy ion collisions may produce metastable exotic states of nuclear matter with roughly equal strangeness and baryon number, but the actual formation of strangelets requires both strangeness distillation and ongoing cooling during the phase transition, neither of which can be unambiguously shown to occur or excluded within current understanding. The existence of such states depends critically on the bag parameter value, with metastable strangelets possible only for bag constants B^{1/4} below 190 MeV.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical models for two classes of strange matter: strange quark matter (SQM) described by the MIT bag model with finite-size corrections, and metastable exotic multihypernuclear objects (MEMOs) described by relativistic mean-field models including nucleon-nucleon, nucleon-hyperon, and hyperon-hyperon interactions via σ* and φ meson fields. The evolution of a hot quark-gluon plasma fireball is modeled using a two-phase equilibrium description between hadron gas and QGP combined with nonequilibrium radiation from rapid freeze-out, with strangeness distillation occurring as antistrange quarks preferentially form kaons while strange quarks remain in the plasma phase.

## Key numbers

- For bag parameters B^{1/4} ≤ 150 MeV, strangelets could be absolutely stable (true groundstate of nuclear matter); for B^{1/4} < 190 MeV, strange quark droplets can only decay via weak interactions; for larger B values, strangelets are unstable
- MEMO binding energies of -21 MeV and more have been found with net strangeness fraction f_s ≈ 1
- For a small bag constant B^{1/4} = 145 MeV, a cold strangelet emerges with baryon number A_B ≈ 17, radius R ≈ 2.5 fm, net strangeness fraction f_s > 1.5, and charge-to-baryon ratio Z/A ≈ -0.2
- For a large bag constant B^{1/4} = 235 MeV, the system hadronizes completely in Δt ≈ 8 fm/c
- Coalescence estimates yield production rates of 3×10^{-9} events per central Au+Au collision at 11.7 AGeV for the lightest bound Ξ system ⁷_{Ξ⁰ΛΛ}He
- Simple coalescence estimates give production probabilities of order 10^{3-A_B-|S|}; for maximum sensitivity ≈ 10^{-10}, only strangelets or MEMOs with baryon numbers A_B ≤ 8 are expected
- The penalty factor q for an additional unit of baryon number at AGS energies is q ≈ 1/50
- Thermal multiplicities for central Pb+Pb at SPS show metastable hypermatter production probability p < 10^{-8} for A ≥ 4
- Long-lived strangelet candidates appear at A = 8-16 for bag parameters B^{1/4} ≥ 180 MeV; for B^{1/4} ≥ 180 MeV no long-lived candidates were found
- Weak leptonic decay of strangelets occurs on timescale 10^{-4} to 10^{-5} s; weak hadronic decay on timescale 10^{-7} to 10^{-9} s
- Hyperons appear in neutron star matter at about 2-3 times normal nuclear matter density
- Pure SQM stars have radii R < 9 km; hybrid stars have R > 9 km

## Context

This review extends the speculative proposal by Bodmer (1971) on collapsed nuclei and the work by Witten and collaborators (1984) on absolutely stable strange quark matter as a dark matter candidate. It builds on earlier calculations by Greiner and Schaffner (1992, 1993) on MEMOs using relativistic mean-field models, and on strangeness distillation studies by Greiner and collaborators (1987-1998). The paper connects to experimental searches at AGS (E864 collaboration, 1998) and CERN SPS, and to astrophysical applications including hybrid star models by Glendenning (1985) and Schertler and collaborators (1997). The review enables future experimental searches for strangelets with lifetimes of order 10^{-7} to 10^{-9} s, requiring open-geometry detectors capable of handling high-multiplicity backgrounds, and motivates further lattice gauge calculations to determine whether the specific entropy per baryon in the hadron phase exceeds that in the quark phase at finite chemical potentials.
