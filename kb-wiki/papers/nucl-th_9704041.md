# Bose-Einstein Weights for Event Generators

> A simple new algorithm for the calculation of two-particle Bose-Einstein correlations from classical event generators is derived and discussed.

## Key claim
The paper derives a new algorithm for computing two-particle Bose-Einstein correlations from classical event generators that removes unphysical oscillations found in the widely used Pratt algorithm, but the quantum mechanically consistent version requires introducing a free wave-packet width parameter σ that must be tuned to elementary collision data.

## Method
The authors start from the general relation between the two-particle correlation function and the emission function (one-particle Wigner density at freeze-out). They show that the standard Pratt algorithm (1994) makes an incorrect "smoothness assumption" that replaces the average momentum K with individual particle momenta, leading to positivity violations. They derive a corrected classical algorithm by binning the classical phase-space distribution in K-space before computing the correlation function, and then generalize this to a quantum mechanically consistent algorithm by replacing delta-function particles with minimum-uncertainty Gaussian wave packets of width σ.

## Key numbers
- For the analytically solvable 1-dimensional model source with R=10 fm and α=0.02 GeV/fm, the exact correlator gives C(q) = 1 + exp[q²/(2α²R²)], while the Pratt algorithm yields C(q) = 1 + cos(q²/α) which oscillates and becomes zero at regular q-intervals
- The new algorithm with bin width ε=10 MeV and ε=5 MeV shows decreasing discrepancy from the exact result as bin width decreases
- The Gaussian width σ of elementary wave packets is constrained to σ ≳ 0.5 fm from the average transverse momentum ⟨p⊥⟩ ≈ 0.35 GeV in pp and e⁺e⁻ collisions
- The effective source radii from two-particle correlations in elementary collisions are 0.8-2 fm, implying σ ≲ 1 fm
- The HBT radius corresponding to the q-Gaussian in the quantum algorithm is R⊥^(HBT) = σ/√2

## Context
This work extends the theoretical framework of Bose-Einstein correlations in heavy-ion collisions, addressing problems identified by Amelin (1997) and the Marburg-Krakow-Frankfurt group (1996) with the Pratt algorithm (1994) for computing correlations from classical event generators like VENUS (Werner, 1993), RQMD (Sorge et al., 1989), and ARC (Pang et al., 1992). The new algorithm enables quantum mechanically consistent computation of both single-particle spectra and two-particle correlations from event generators, with the free parameter σ to be fixed from elementary e⁺e⁻ and hadron-hadron collisions as suggested by the GGLP paper (1960) and later work by Pratt et al. (1990), Zhang (1996), and Merino and Pajares (1997).
