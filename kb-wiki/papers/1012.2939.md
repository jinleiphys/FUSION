# Maximum Mass of the Hot Neutron Star with the Quark Core

> We have considered a hot neutron star with a quark core, a mixed phase of quark-hadron matter, and a hadronic matter crust and have determined the equation of state of the hadronic phase and the quark phase, we have then found the equation of state of the mixed phase under the Gibbs conditions. Finally, we have computed the structure of hot neutron star with the quark core and compared our results with those of the neutron star without the quark core. For the quark matter calculations, we have used the MIT bag model in which the total energy of the system is considered as the kinetic energy of the particles plus a bag constant. For the hadronic matter calculations, we have used the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) formalism. Our calculations show that the results for the maximum gravitational mass of the hot neutron star with the quark core are substantially different from those of without the quark core.

## Key claim
The maximum gravitational mass of a hot neutron star with a quark core is substantially lower than that of a hot neutron star without a quark core, at temperatures of 10 and 20 MeV. This reduction is attributed to the softening of the equation of state when the quark core is included.

## Method
The authors constructed the equation of state for three phases of neutron star matter: a hadronic phase using the lowest order constrained variational (LOCV) method with the UV14 + TNI nuclear potential, a quark phase using the MIT bag model with a bag constant of 90 MeV fm⁻³, and a mixed hadron-quark phase under Gibbs equilibrium conditions. They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations to compute the gravitational mass and radius of the star for central densities at temperatures of 10 and 20 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous calculations for cold neutron stars with a quark core (Bordbar, 2009) and without a quark core (Bordbar, 2008) to the finite-temperature case relevant to newborn neutron stars. It builds on the approach of Burgio et al. (2000, 2002) who used the Brueckner-Bethe-Goldstone formalism for hadronic matter, but here the LOCV method is employed. The results enable comparisons of the maximum mass and radius of hot neutron stars with and without quark cores, which is important for understanding the fate of newborn neutron stars and their potential collapse to black holes.
