# Nuclear Equation of State and many-body phase-space correlations in the Constrained Molecular Dynamics

> Many-body correlations characterizing the Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD)are analyzed in the case of finite and zero range effective microscopic interactions. The study begins by analyzing the case of infinite nuclear matter at zero temperature. A comparison with the predictions in the mean-field(MF) limit corresponding to different effective masses, highlights non-negligible differences regarding the produced Equation of State (EoS). A procedure is illustrated to determine the necessary corrections of the effective interaction parameters in the CoMD model so to reproduce the chosen EoS. The specific model calculations, the general feature of the discussed correlations gives a wider meaning to the resulting differences, which are in fact strongly related both to the Pauli principle constraint and to the localization effects related to wave-packets dynamics. Moving on to finite systems, preliminary results are shown in relation to the reaction mechanisms in the $^{64}Ni+^{48}Ca$ system described by the CoMD model. Therefore, the topic covered illustrates the effects produced by the correlations of the CoMD dynamics on the EoS and on some observables commonly studied in heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

Many-body correlations inherent to the Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) model produce non-negligible differences in the Equation of State (EoS) compared to mean-field (MF) predictions, even at zero temperature in infinite nuclear matter, and these differences are strongly related to the Pauli principle constraint and to localization effects from wave-packet dynamics; the paper illustrates a procedure to correct the effective interaction parameters in CoMD to reproduce a chosen reference EoS.

## Method

The study simulates infinite nuclear matter at zero temperature using box calculations with periodic boundary conditions and 2000 wave-packets (WPs) with fixed width (sigma_r = 1.15 fm) in the CoMD model. The effective interaction includes finite-range Gaussian terms (range mu = 1.1 fm) for two-body, three-body, and iso-vectorial contributions, plus residual zero-range terms and a spin-spin term, with strength parameters first determined in the mean-field limit to match a reference EoS (saturation density, binding energy, compressibility, symmetry energy, effective mass). The total energy per nucleon is then computed from the WP centroids using the CoMD expressions, and the resulting EoS is compared to the MF predictions to quantify the corrections needed in the interaction parameters.

## Key numbers

- Reference saturation density: rho_0 = 0.165 fm^{-3}
- Reference binding energy at saturation: E(rho_0) = -16 MeV
- Reference compressibility: K(rho_0) = 240 MeV
- Reference symmetry energy: E_sym = 30 MeV
- Reference effective pairing energy: E_pi = -2 MeV per nucleon at saturation for mass around 100
- Relative isoscalar effective mass in MF limit: m* = 0.67
- Neutron-proton effective mass splitting: m_n* - m_p* = 0.4 beta
- Slope parameter of symmetry energy L: range 55 to 105 MeV (three example cases yield L = 63.3, 50.6, and 81.3 MeV)
- Finite-range interaction width: mu = 1.1 fm
- Wave-packet width in CoMD: sigma_r = 1.15 fm
- Number of wave-packets in box calculations: 2000

## Context

This work extends the analysis of many-body correlations in CoMD previously studied by Papa (using zero-range Skyrme interactions in Ref. miosky) to the case of finite-range interactions analogous to the Gogny force. The paper provides a procedure to adjust effective interaction parameters in CoMD so that the model reproduces a chosen nuclear matter EoS, which is necessary because the explicit correlations in QMD-like approaches (including CoMD) produce systematic deviations from mean-field predictions. This enables more reliable use of CoMD for heavy-ion collision simulations, as demonstrated by preliminary results for the 64Ni+48Ca system, and connects to the broader effort of constraining the nuclear EoS through transport model comparisons with experimental data (e.g., works by DiPietro, LeFevre, and the PREX collaboration).
