# Two-nucleon correlation effects in knockout reactions from 12C

> Reactions that involve the direct and sudden removal of a pair of like or unlike nucleons from a fast projectile beam by a light target nucleus are considered. Specifically, we study the three two-nucleon removal channels from $^{12}${C} that populate final states in the $^{10}${Be}, $^{10}${B} and $^{10}${C} reaction residues. The calculated two-nucleon removal cross sections and the residue momentum distributions are compared with available high energy data at 250, 1050 and 2010 MeV per nucleon, data that are inclusive with respect to the bound final-states of the residues. The measured $np$-removal cross sections only are significantly greater than the values calculated, suggesting that the reaction mechanism observes enhanced $np$ spatial correlations compared to those present in the shell-model wave functions.

## Key claim

The measured cross sections for neutron-proton (np) pair removal from 12C are significantly larger than those calculated using shell-model wave functions that include only correlations from antisymmetry and angular momentum coupling, suggesting that the reaction mechanism observes enhanced np spatial correlations beyond those present in the shell-model description. The authors quantify that this enhancement cannot be explained by the simple combinatorics of available valence-nucleon pairs or by channel-dependent fractions of strength leading to bound versus unbound final states.

## Method

The authors used an eikonal reaction model in the isospin formalism to calculate two-nucleon removal cross sections and residue momentum distributions for 12C projectiles incident on carbon targets. The two-nucleon amplitudes (TNAs) were taken from p-shell shell-model calculations using the WBP interaction (with comparisons to the PJT interaction), and the nucleon bound-state radial wave functions were calculated in Woods-Saxon potentials. The nucleon- and residue-target S-matrices were computed in the optical limit of Glauber theory using free nucleon-nucleon cross sections and Gaussian density distributions for the target and residues.

## Key numbers

- The experimental np-removal cross section to 10B at 250 MeV/u is 47.50 ± 2.42 mb, compared to a theoretical value of 21.57 mb, giving a ratio σ_exp/σ_th = 2.20 ± 0.11.
- At 1050 MeV/u, the experimental np-removal cross section is 27.90 ± 2.20 mb versus a theoretical 19.27 mb, ratio 1.45 ± 0.11.
- At 2100 MeV/u, the experimental np-removal cross section is 35.10 ± 3.40 mb versus a theoretical 19.02 mb, ratio 1.84 ± 0.18.
- The experimental pp-removal cross section to 10Be at 2100 MeV/u is 5.81 ± 0.29 mb versus a theoretical 6.52 mb, ratio 0.89 ± 0.04.
- The experimental nn-removal cross section to 10C at 2100 MeV/u is 4.11 ± 0.22 mb versus a theoretical 5.04 mb, ratio 0.82 ± 0.04.
- The experimental ratios σ_np/σ_pp at 250, 1050, and 2100 MeV/u are 8.1, 5.3, and 6.0 respectively; σ_np/σ_nn are 8.9, 6.3, and 8.5 respectively.
- The 12C two-nucleon separation energies are S_2p = 27.184 MeV, S_2n = 31.184 MeV, and S_np = 27.412 MeV.
- The single-nucleon removal cross sections at 2100 MeV/u sum to 95.36 mb for 11C (experiment 46.50 ± 2.30 mb) and 98.81 mb for 11B (experiment 53.80 ± 2.70 mb).

## Context

This work extends the eikonal reaction model for two-nucleon knockout developed by Tostevin and collaborators (Tostevin and Brown 2006, Simpson and Tostevin 2009) to symmetric light nuclei, specifically addressing whether enhanced np spatial correlations observed in electron-induced knockout experiments (Subedi et al. 2008) also appear in nuclear-induced reactions at the nuclear surface. The calculations use the same shell-model framework as previous single-nucleon knockout analyses of 12C (Brown et al. 2002) and enable quantitative comparison of like- and unlike-pair removal yields to test for correlations beyond the shell model. The results suggest that future experiments measuring two-nucleon knockout from 12C and 16O could provide complementary information on np pair correlations at longer length scales than those probed by electron-induced reactions.
