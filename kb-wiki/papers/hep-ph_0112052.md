# Absence of Thermophoretic Flow in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions as an Indicator for the Absence of a Mixed Phase

> If a quark-gluon plasma is formed in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, there may or may not be a mixed phase of quarks, gluons and hadronic clusters when the critical temperature is reached in the expansion of the fireball. If there is a temperature gradient in the fireball, the hadronic clusters, embedded in the heat bath of quarks and gluons, are subjected to a thermophoretic force. It is shown that even for small temperature gradients and short lifetimes of the mixed phase, thermophoresis would lead to a flow essentially stronger than the observed one. The absence of this strong flow provides support for a rapid or sudden hadronization mechanism without a mixed phase.

# Absence of Thermophoretic Flow in Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions as an Indicator for the Absence of a Mixed Phase

## Key claim
If a mixed phase of quarks, gluons, and hadronic clusters existed during the expansion of a quark-gluon plasma fireball, even small temperature gradients (1 MeV/fm) and short mixed-phase lifetimes (1-2 fm/c) would produce a thermophoretic flow stronger than observed flow velocities at SPS. The absence of such strong flow therefore supports a rapid or sudden hadronization mechanism without a mixed phase, though the author notes this conclusion depends on assuming a first-order phase transition and that lattice QCD results show at most a weak first-order transition.

## Method
The author uses elementary kinetic theory, generalized to relativistic plasmas, to estimate the thermophoretic force on hadronic clusters embedded in a thermal quark-gluon plasma with a temperature gradient. The force is derived from momentum transfer per unit time and area, analogous to heat conductivity calculations, using the parton number density, mean free path, hadron radius, and temperature gradient. The final flow velocity is then computed from the work done by the thermophoretic force over the fireball radius, solving the relativistic equation of motion for hadrons of different masses.

## Key numbers
- Critical temperature assumed: T_c = 170 MeV (from QCD lattice computations, Karsch 2001)
- Typical parton mean free path at T = T_c: λ = 1 fm
- Parton number density at T = T_c: n = 5 fm^{-3}
- Hadron radius assumed: R = 1 fm
- Thermophoretic force for dT/dr = 1 MeV/fm: F = -16 MeV/fm
- For pions (m = 140 MeV) with fireball radius d = 10 fm: final velocity v_f = 0.81
- For protons (m = 940 MeV) with fireball radius d = 10 fm: final velocity v_f = 0.40
- Hydrodynamic flow velocity from HBT measurements: v_f ≈ 0.5 (Heinz 1999)
- For short mixed-phase lifetime of 1-2 fm/c (d ≈ 1 fm) with moderate temperature gradient of 10 MeV/fm: same final velocities obtained

## Context
This work extends studies of mixed-phase consequences in relativistic heavy-ion collisions (Rischke and Gyulassy 1996) by proposing a new source of flow from temperature gradients rather than pressure gradients, which had been the focus of prior flow analyses (Ollitrault 1998). The thermophoretic mechanism is analogous to dust particle behavior in low-temperature plasmas (Bouchoule 1999; Rothermel 2001). The predicted flow contradicts experimental observations at SPS (Jacak 2001), which show flow velocities around 0.5 and higher flow for baryons than mesons, opposite to the thermophoretic prediction. The result supports rapid hadronization scenarios (Csernai and Mishustin 1995) and suggests either a continuous or second-order phase transition, or no phase transition at all, in the collisions studied. Future work could investigate other consequences of temperature gradients in heavy-ion fireballs, such as dissipative effects including heat conductivity in hydrodynamical models.
