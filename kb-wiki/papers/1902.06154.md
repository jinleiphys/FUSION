# Non-Empirical Interactions for the Nuclear Shell Model: An Update

> The nuclear shell model has been perhaps the most important conceptual and computational paradigm for the understanding of the structure of atomic nuclei. While the shell model has been predominantly used in a phenomenological context, there have been efforts stretching back over a half century to derive shell model parameters based on a realistic interaction between nucleons. More recently, several ab initio many-body methods---in particular many-body perturbation theory, the no-core shell model, the in-medium similarity renormalization group, and coupled cluster theory---have developed the capability to provide effective shell model Hamiltonians. We provide an update on the status of these methods and investigate the connections between them and potential strengths and weaknesses, with a particular focus on the in-medium similarity renormalization group approach. Three-body forces are demonstrated to be an important ingredient in understanding the modifications needed in phenomenological treatments. We then review some applications of these methods to comparisons with recent experimental measurements, and conclude with some remaining challenges in ab initio shell model theory.

## Key claim

While the nuclear shell model has been predominantly phenomenological, several ab initio many-body methods, particularly many-body perturbation theory, the no-core shell model, the in-medium similarity renormalization group, and coupled cluster theory, have developed the capability to provide effective shell model Hamiltonians, though challenges remain in achieving fully systematic derivations from realistic nuclear forces without ad hoc adjustments.

## Method

The authors review and compare multiple approaches for deriving effective shell model interactions from realistic nucleon-nucleon and three-nucleon forces: quasidegenerate perturbation theory with the Q-box resummation, the Okubo-Lee-Suzuki unitary transformation method, the valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG), and shell model coupled cluster (SMCC). They analyze these methods within a common formalism to illuminate connections between them, and examine the role of three-nucleon forces in the shell model context, relating modifications of semi-empirical interactions to the more systematic treatment of these forces in modern approaches.

## Key numbers

- The universal sd shell interaction (USD) achieves a root-mean-square deviation from experimental levels of 130 keV throughout the sd shell.
- Nuclear matter saturation density is approximately 0.16 fm^{-3}, with the Fermi momentum k_F approximately 1.4 fm^{-1} at saturation density.
- The range of the pion-exchange tail is approximately 1.5 fm, while short-range terms in chiral forces are characterized by a cutoff scale on the order of 0.5 fm or less.
- Large-scale shell model diagonalizations can handle basis dimensions upwards of 10^9 Slater determinants.

## Context

This review extends the long history of efforts to derive shell model parameters from realistic interactions, beginning with the work of Mayer (1948) and Haxel, Jensen, and Suess (1949), and continuing through the pioneering perturbative approaches of Kuo and Brown (1966, 1967, 1968) and the subsequent demonstration by Barrett and Kirson (1970) of convergence problems in G-matrix expansions. The paper connects these historical efforts to modern developments using chiral effective field theory (Weinberg, 1990, 1991) and similarity renormalization group methods (Bogner et al., 2001, 2003, 2007), which have revitalized the program of deriving shell model interactions. The work enables systematic comparisons between ab initio methods and experimental spectroscopic data across the nuclear chart, while identifying remaining challenges such as the treatment of low-lying collective excitations and the need for proper uncertainty quantification.
