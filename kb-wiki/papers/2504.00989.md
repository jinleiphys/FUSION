# Scattering Observables from Few-Body Densities and Compton Scattering on 6Li

> The dynamics of scattering on light nuclei is numerically expensive using standard methods. Fortunately, recent developments allow one to factor the relevant quantities for a given probe into a convolution of an $n$-body Transition Density Amplitude (TDA) and the interaction kernel for a given probe. These TDAs depend only on the target, and not the probe; they are calculated once for each set of kinematics and can be used for different interactions. The kernels depend only on the probe, and not on the target; they can be reused for different targets and different kinematics. The calculation of TDAs becomes numerically difficult for more than four nucleons, but we discuss a new solution through the use of a Similarity Renormalization Group transformation, and a subsequent back-transformation. This technique allows for extending the TDA method to heavier nuclei such as 6Li. We present preliminary results for Compton scattering on 6Li and compare with available data, anticipating an upcoming, more thorough study. We also discuss ongoing extensions to pion-photoproduction and other reactions on light nuclei.

## Key claim
The Transition Density Amplitude (TDA) method can be extended to nuclei with A ≥ 6 by applying a Similarity Renormalization Group (SRG) transformation to the nuclear potential before computing the TDA, then back-transforming the resulting densities to physical momenta, preserving the independence of kernels and densities. Preliminary results for Compton scattering on 6Li show an angle-dependence matching experiment but with a constant offset that may indicate either theoretical or experimental normalization issues.

## Method
The authors factor scattering amplitudes into a convolution of n-body Transition Density Amplitudes (TDAs), which depend only on the target, and interaction kernels, which depend only on the probe. For 6Li, they apply an SRG transformation to the chiral Semilocal Momentum-Space (χSMS) potential to lower the effective momentum cutoff, compute the TDAs in the SRG-evolved space using the No-Core Shell Method (NCSM), then apply an inverse SRG transformation to return the TDAs to physical momenta for use with unevolved kernels. They validate the method by comparing Compton scattering on 4He with and without the SRG transformation, finding deviations ≤ 2% for the furthest SRG evolution considered (Λ_SRG = 1.88 fm⁻¹).

## Key numbers
- The SRG transformation reduces the required momentum cutoff from Λ ≈ 5 fm⁻¹ to Λ ≈ 2 fm⁻¹, yielding at least a factor of (5/2)² = 6.25 in computational efficiency.
- For 4He Compton scattering, the relative deviation between SRG-evolved and non-SRG results is ≤ 2% for Λ_SRG = 1.88 fm⁻¹, even at large momentum transfers.
- Two-body TDA file sizes are approximately 20 MB per energy and angle; one-body TDA file sizes are on the order of a few KB.
- The Compton scattering calculation on 6Li uses chiral EFT up to N³LO with expansion parameter δ ≈ 0.4, and assumes an overall ±10% uncertainty (1σ = 10%).
- The isoscalar electromagnetic polarisabilities used are α_E1 = 11.1 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ and β_M1 = 3.4 × 10⁻⁴ fm³.
- For 3He pion photoproduction, the two-body form factor F_T^(a) - F_T^(b) = -29.4 fm⁻¹ and F_L^(a) - F_L^(b) = -22.9 fm⁻¹ (central values, uncertainty to be announced).

## Context
This work extends the TDA formalism previously applied to 3H and 4He by Griesshammer et al. (2020) to the heavier nucleus 6Li, which was not feasible with standard methods. The SRG transformation technique, developed by Furnstahl et al. (2013) and others, enables this extension by accelerating convergence of the TDA calculations. The preliminary Compton scattering results at 60 MeV are compared to data from Myers et al. (2014) at the HIγS facility, with additional data at 86 MeV available for future comparison. The authors plan to extend the method to pion-photoproduction (building on work by Lenkewitz et al. (2011, 2013) and Braun) and pion scattering on light nuclei, enabling a unified treatment of multiple scattering processes.
