# Thermodynamic interpretation of the uniformity of the phase space probability measure

> Uniformity of the probability measure of phase space is considered in the framework of classical equilibrium thermodynamics. For the canonical and the grand canonical ensembles, relations are given between the phase space uniformities and thermodynamic potentials, their fluctuations and correlations. For the binary system in the vicinity of the critical point the uniformity is interpreted in terms of temperature dependent rates of phases of well defined uniformities. Examples of a liquid-gas system and the mass spectrum of nuclear fragments are presented.

# Thermodynamic interpretation of the uniformity of the phase space probability measure

## Key claim
The uniformity parameter γ_q, originally defined for multifractal measures, can be expressed in terms of standard thermodynamic potentials (free energy, entropy, internal energy) for canonical and grand canonical ensembles, with the Renyi parameter q acting as a temperature scaling factor. The paper claims this formalism can describe two-phase systems near critical points, but the application to nuclear fragmentation data relies on simplifying assumptions (neglecting surface energy, binding energy, and finite size effects) and uses an apparent exponent τ that is not a universal critical exponent.

## Method
The author derives analytic relations between Beck's uniformity γ_q and thermodynamic quantities for the canonical ensemble (using free energy F and temperature T) and for the grand canonical ensemble (using chemical potential μ_c and average particle number ⟨N⟩). For a binary system near the critical point, the overall uniformity is expressed as a weighted average of pure-phase uniformities, with weights given by particle concentrations that follow a power-law temperature dependence. The formalism is then applied to experimental nuclear fragmentation data by treating fragment mass as energy, identifying the equilibrium temperature from the power-law exponent τ, and computing γ_q using the derived formulas.

## Key numbers
- Critical exponent α for liquid-gas system: between 0.33 and 0.36 (used α=0.34 in the example)
- For nuclear fragmentation data: critical temperature T_c = 12.0 ± 0.2 MeV
- Nuclear fragmentation uniformities range from γ_q = 0.29 ± 0.05 (at T=6.0 MeV, q=0.50) to γ_q = 0.98 ± 0.12 (at T=14.0 MeV, q=1.16)
- Power-law exponents τ for nuclear data range from 2.4 to 4.1, with corresponding mean energies ⟨E⟩ from 1.10 to 2.21
- Below T=4 MeV, the contribution of the non-uniform phase is below 10%

## Context
This work extends the concept of Beck uniformity (Beck 1990) from formal systems to equilibrium thermodynamics, building on earlier connections between Renyi entropies and free energy found by Tel (1988) in a different context. The paper applies the formalism to nuclear fragmentation data previously analyzed within condensation theory (referenced as "frag"), where the critical temperature was determined. The approach enables interpretation of phase coexistence in terms of temperature-dependent uniformity, and could be extended to other two-phase systems where the temperature dependence of phase densities is known.
