# Quenching of high-pT hadrons: a non-energy-loss scenario

> A parton produced with a high transverse momentum in a hard collision is regenerating its color field, intensively radiating gluons and losing energy. This process cannot last long, if it ends up with production of a leading hadron carrying the main fraction z_h of the initial parton momentum. So energy conservation imposes severe constraints on the length scale of production of a single hadron with high pT. As a result, the main reason for hadron quenching observed in heavy ion collisions, is not energy loss, but attenuation of the produced colorless dipole in the created dense medium. The latter mechanism, calculated with the path-integral method, explains well the observed suppression of light hadrons and the elliptic flow in a wide range of energies, from the lowest energy of RHIC up to LHC, and in a wide range of transverse momenta. The values of the transport coefficient extracted from data range within 1-2 GeV^2/fm, dependent on energy, and agree well with the theoretical expectations.

## Key claim

The observed suppression of high-\(p_T\) hadrons in heavy ion collisions is primarily caused by attenuation of the produced colorless dipole in the dense medium, not by energy loss of the parton, because energy conservation severely constrains the production length of a leading hadron to be too short for significant medium-induced energy loss.

## Method

The authors calculate the production length \(l_p\) available for gluon radiation using a perturbative hadronization model, finding it is short due to intensive vacuum energy loss and energy conservation constraints. They then model the propagation of color dipoles through the medium using a path-integral method, solving a two-dimensional Schrödinger equation for the dipole Green function with an imaginary potential proportional to the transport coefficient \(\hat q(l)\) and dipole separation squared. The nuclear suppression factor \(R_{AB}\) and elliptic flow \(v_2\) are computed from the ratio of in-medium to vacuum matrix elements, with the transport coefficient parametrized as time-dependent and proportional to the number of participants.

## Key numbers

- Transport coefficient extracted from data: \(\hat q_0 = 1.2\ \mathrm{GeV}^2/\mathrm{fm}\) at \(\sqrt{s}=62\ \mathrm{GeV}\), \(1.6\ \mathrm{GeV}^2/\mathrm{fm}\) at \(\sqrt{s}=200\ \mathrm{GeV}\), and \(2\ \mathrm{GeV}^2/\mathrm{fm}\) at \(\sqrt{s}=2760\ \mathrm{GeV}\).
- The mean production length \(\langle l_p \rangle\) is found to be short and decreases with \(p_T\); for example, at \(z_h=0.5\) it is shown as a function of energy in Fig. 3 (specific numerical values not given in text).
- The effective hadronic cross section for initial-state interactions is \(\sigma_{eff} \approx 20\ \mathrm{mb}\).
- The infra-red cutoff for vacuum energy loss calculations is fixed at \(\Lambda = 0.2\ \mathrm{GeV}\).

## Context

This work extends the dipole-attenuation model previously applied to semi-inclusive deep-inelastic scattering (SIDIS) on nuclei, where predictions by Kopeliovich, Nemchik, and Potashnikova (1995) were confirmed by HERMES data, while energy-loss-based models (e.g., Wang 2004) failed to describe the \(z_h>0.5\) region. The paper enables a consistent description of both nuclear suppression \(R_{AA}\) and elliptic flow \(v_2\) from RHIC to LHC energies using a single transport coefficient per energy, and contrasts with the pure energy-loss scenario which requires an order-of-magnitude larger \(\hat q\) than theoretical expectations (Baier et al. 1997). The results also unify with hydrodynamic descriptions at lower \(p_T\) as shown by recent work (unnamed in text).
