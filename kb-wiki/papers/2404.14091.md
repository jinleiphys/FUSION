# Local univalence versus stability and causality in hydrodynamic models

> Our main objective is to compare the analytic properties of hydrodynamic series with the stability and causality conditions applied to hydrodynamic modes. Analyticity, in this context, implies that the hydrodynamic series behaves as a univalent or single-valued function. Stability and causality adhere to physical constraints where hydrodynamic modes neither exhibit exponential growth nor travel faster than the speed of light. Through an examination of various hydrodynamic models, such as the Muller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) and the first-order hydro models like the BDNK (Bemfica-Disconzi-Noronha-Kovtun) model, we observe no new restrictions stemming from the analyticity limits in the shear channel of these models. However, local univalence is maintained in the sound channel of these models despite the global divergence of the hydrodynamic series. Notably, differences in the sound equations between the MIS and BDNK models lead to distinct analyticity limits. The MIS model's sound mode remains univalent at high momenta within a specific transport range. Conversely, in the BDNK model, the univalence of the sound mode extends to intermediate momenta across all stable and causal regions. Generally, the convergence radius is independent of univalence and the given dispersion relation predominantly influences their correlation. For second-order frequency dispersions, the relationship is precise, i.e. within the convergence radius, the hydro series demonstrates univalence. However, with higher-order dispersions, the hydro series is locally univalent within certain transport regions, which may fall within or outside the stable and causal zones.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in hydrodynamic models, the mathematical property of local univalence (single-valuedness) of the hydrodynamic series holds in specific momentum regions even when the series is globally divergent, and that this local univalence does not generally impose new restrictions beyond those already required by stability and causality conditions, except that the relationship depends on the order of the dispersion relation and the specific transport region.

## Method

The authors analyze the dispersion relations of the Muller-Israel-Stewart (MIS) and BDNK (Bemfica-Disconzi-Noronha-Kovtun) hydrodynamic models, decomposing fluctuations into shear and sound channels. They apply the Bieberbach conjecture bounds on Taylor coefficients of univalent functions by constructing conformal maps from the complex momentum plane to the unit disk, then compare the resulting univalence constraints with standard stability (Routh-Hurwitz criterion) and causality (asymptotic group velocity limit) conditions across the transport parameter space.

## Key numbers

- For the MIS shear channel, the conformal map gives the function f_shear(zeta) = -1/(1 - zeta), which is univalent for all points within the unit disk and independent of transport coefficients.
- For the MIS sound channel, the dimensionless parameter X = -1 + gamma_s/(8 c_s^2 w tau) labels all solutions, with stability requiring X >= -1.
- The causality condition for the MIS sound channel gives gamma_s/(w tau) <= 1 - c_s^2, where c_s is the speed of sound.
- The branch points for the MIS sound channel solutions are at tilde_z_0 = 0 and tilde_z_plus/minus = [9(3+4X) + 8X^2 +/- 8 sqrt(X^3 (1+X))] / (9+8X)^3.
- For the BDNK model, univalence of the sound mode extends to intermediate momenta across all stable and causal regions, regardless of the value of c_s.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of univalent functions applied to hydrodynamic series initiated by Grozdanov (2020) and applied to holographic models by Baggioli (2022). It directly compares mathematical analyticity constraints with the physical stability and causality conditions previously established for the MIS model by Grozdanov (2018) and for the BDNK model by Kovtun (2019) and Bemfica (2017). The results enable future investigation of local univalence in other hydrodynamic models or for different quantities such as energy density and pressure.
