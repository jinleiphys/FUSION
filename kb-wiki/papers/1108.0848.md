# New Equations of State in Simulations of Core-Collapse Supernovae

> We discuss three new equations of state (EOS) in core-collapse supernova simulations. The new EOS are based on the nuclear statistical equilibrium model of Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich (HS), which includes excluded volume effects and relativistic mean-field (RMF) interactions. We consider the RMF parameterizations TM1, TMA, and FSUgold. These EOS are implemented into our spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova model, which is based on general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics and three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport. The results obtained for the new EOS are compared with the widely used EOS of H. Shen et al. and Lattimer & Swesty. The systematic comparison shows that the model description of inhomogeneous nuclear matter is as important as the parameterization of the nuclear interactions for the supernova dynamics and the neutrino signal. Furthermore, several new aspects of nuclear physics are investigated: the HS EOS contains distributions of nuclei, including nuclear shell effects. The appearance of light nuclei, e.g., deuterium and tritium is also explored, which can become as abundant as alphas and free protons. In addition, we investigate the black hole formation in failed core-collapse supernovae, which is mainly determined by the high-density EOS. We find that temperature effects lead to a systematically faster collapse for the non-relativistic LS EOS in comparison to the RMF EOS. We deduce a new correlation for the time until black hole formation, which allows to determine the maximum mass of proto-neutron stars, if the neutrino signal from such a failed supernova would be measured in the future. This would give a constraint for the nuclear EOS at finite entropy, complementary to observations of cold neutron stars.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the model description of inhomogeneous nuclear matter (e.g., using nuclear statistical equilibrium with distributions of nuclei and excluded volume effects) is as important as the choice of nuclear interaction parameterization for core-collapse supernova dynamics and neutrino signals, but this conclusion is conditional on the specific spherically symmetric, general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics model used and the particular set of equations of state compared.

## Method

The authors implemented three new equations of state based on the Hempel-Schaffner-Bielich (HS) nuclear statistical equilibrium model, using the relativistic mean-field parameterizations TM1, TMA, and FSUgold, into a spherically symmetric core-collapse supernova model with general relativistic radiation hydrodynamics and three-flavor Boltzmann neutrino transport. They performed simulations of a 15 solar mass progenitor to compare HS(TM1) with the Lattimer-Swesty (LS) and Shen (STOS) equations of state, and a 40 solar mass progenitor to study black hole formation using five different equations of state: LS(180), LS(220), STOS(TM1), HS(FSUgold), and HS(TMA).

## Key numbers

- Nuclear incompressibility values for the equations of state: TM1 has K = 281 MeV, TMA has K = 318 MeV, FSUgold has K = 230 MeV, LS(180) has K = 180 MeV, and LS(220) has K = 220 MeV
- Symmetry energy coefficients: TM1 has J = 36.9 MeV, TMA has J = 30.7 MeV, FSUgold has J = 32.6 MeV, and both LS(180) and LS(220) have J = 28.6 MeV
- Saturation densities: TM1 at 0.145 fm^-3, TMA at 0.147 fm^-3, FSUgold at 0.148 fm^-3, and both LS variants at 0.155 fm^-3
- The HS EOS tables include nuclei up to mass number A ~ 330, from the neutron to the proton dripline
- The transition to uniform nuclear matter occurs around half times saturation density, approximately 10^14 g/cm^3
- Neutrino transport was discretized in 6 angular bins and 20 energy bins from 3 to 300 MeV
- The HS model neglects nuclei formation for temperatures above 20 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier HS model of Hempel and Schaffner-Bielich (2010) by implementing it into full supernova simulations and comparing with the classic equations of state of Lattimer and Swesty (1991) and Shen et al. (1998). It enables future studies of how detailed nuclear composition, including light nuclei such as deuterium and tritium, affects neutrino transport and supernova dynamics, and provides a correlation for black hole formation time that could constrain the nuclear equation of state at finite entropy if neutrino signals from failed supernovae are measured.
