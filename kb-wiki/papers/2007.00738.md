# Multilayer neutron stars with scalar mesons crossing term

> It is shown that recently proposed RMF model with $σ$ and $δ$ meson interaction agrees with the observational data and presents an interesting structure with phase transition in the outer part of neutron star core.

# Multilayer neutron stars with scalar mesons crossing term

## Key claim

The RMF model extended with a scalar-scalar ($\sigma$-$\delta$) meson crossing term produces an equation of state that is soft at moderate densities (slightly above saturation) and stiff at higher densities, enabling neutron stars that are both compact and have maximum masses above $2 M_\odot$, but only when the crossing coupling constant $g_\alpha$ takes negative values on the order of $10^{-3}$; without the crossing term, the symmetry energy slope $L$ remains greater than 90 MeV.

## Method

The authors construct a relativistic mean field (RMF) model including $\sigma$, $\omega$, $\rho$, and $\delta$ mesons with Yukawa couplings to nucleons, augmented by a $\sigma$-$\delta$ crossing term of the form $\mathcal{L}_{\sigma\delta} = -\tilde{g}_\alpha \sigma^\alpha \vec{\delta}^2$ (focusing on the quadratic case $\alpha=2$). They fix the isoscalar couplings to reproduce symmetric nuclear matter saturation properties ($n_0=0.16$ fm$^{-3}$, $B=-16$ MeV, $K=230$ MeV) and vary the isovector couplings $C_\delta$ and $g_2$ to control the symmetry energy slope $L$ between 45.6 and 88.5 MeV. The resulting equations of state in $\beta$-equilibrium are used to solve the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff (TOV) equations for nonrotating neutron star structure, with the Sly4 model for the crust.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density: $n_0 = 0.16$ fm$^{-3}$, binding energy $B = -16$ MeV, compressibility $K(n_0) = 230$ MeV
- Effective nucleon Dirac mass at saturation: $m^*(n_0)/m = 0.73$
- Symmetry energy at saturation: $S_2(n_0) = 30$ MeV
- Symmetry energy slope $L$ ranges from 88.5 MeV (for $C_\delta^2 = 1.0$ fm$^2$) down to 45.6 MeV (for $C_\delta^2 = 3.8$ fm$^2$)
- Crossing coupling constant for quadratic model: $g_2 = -0.004$
- Maximum masses: $M_{\max}/M_\odot = 2.18$ ($L=88.5$ MeV), 2.25 ($L=68.1$ MeV), 2.27 ($L=55.4$ MeV), 2.34 ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Radii at maximum mass: $R_{\max} = 11.23$ km ($L=88.5$ MeV), 11.51 km ($L=68.1$ MeV), 11.61 km ($L=55.4$ MeV), 11.71 km ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Central densities at maximum mass: $n_c^{\max} = 1.001$ fm$^{-3}$ ($L=88.5$ MeV) to 0.865 fm$^{-3}$ ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Direct Urca threshold densities: $n_{\text{urca}} = 0.315$ fm$^{-3}$ ($L=88.5$ MeV) to 0.360 fm$^{-3}$ ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Corresponding Urca threshold masses: $M_{\text{urca}}/M_\odot = 1.07$ ($L=88.5$ MeV) to 1.29 ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Crust-core transition densities: $n_{c-c} = 0.0700$ fm$^{-3}$ ($L=88.5$ MeV) to 0.0577 fm$^{-3}$ ($L=45.6$ MeV)
- Phase transition appears for $C_\delta^2$ exceeding 3.0 fm$^2$, corresponding to $L$ below 68 MeV
- For the model with $L=55.4$ MeV ($C_\delta^2=3.5$ fm$^2$), the speed of sound drops to approximately $10^{-3}c$ in the phase transition region

## Context

This work extends the RMF model with $\sigma$-$\delta$ crossing term previously proposed by Zabari et al. (2019) for nuclear symmetry energy studies, applying it to neutron star structure. The vector-scalar crossing term (without $\delta$ meson) was considered by Mueller and Serot (1996), and the vector-vector crossing term with $\delta$ meson was analyzed by Bunta and Gmuca (2004). The results contradict the earlier finding that including only the $\delta$ meson without crossing terms makes the EOS stiffer across all densities (Kubis and Kutschera, 1998; Menezes and Providencia, 2004). The models with $L$ below 60 MeV agree with the most massive pulsar PSR J0740+6620 and satisfy both the upper radius bound from GW170817 tidal deformability analysis (Fattoyev et al., 2017) and the lower radius bound from prompt collapse threshold (Bauswein et al., 2017). The multilayer structure with a solid-like internal layer opens possibilities for explaining pulsar glitching and precession, and the crustal moment of inertia results can be compared with Vela pulsar constraints (Andersson et al., 2012) requiring the crust to encompass more than 1.6% of the total moment of inertia.
