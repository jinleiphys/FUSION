# Universality in few-body systems with large scattering length

> Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a powerful framework that exploits a separation of scales in physical systems to perform systematically improvable, model-independent calculations. Particularly interesting are few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length. Such systems display remarkable universal features. In systems with more than two particles, a three-body force with limit cycle behavior is required for consistent renormalization already at leading order. We will review this EFT and some of its applications in the physics of cold atoms and nuclear physics. In particular, we will discuss the possibility of an infrared limit cycle in QCD. Recent extensions of the EFT approach to the four-body system and N-boson droplets in two spatial dimensions will also be addressed.

## Key claim

Effective Field Theory (EFT) provides a systematically improvable, model-independent framework for few-body systems with short-range interactions and large two-body scattering length, but the abstract's claim of "remarkable universal features" glosses over the fact that a three-body force with limit cycle behavior is required for consistent renormalization already at leading order, introducing a three-body parameter that cannot be predicted by the EFT and must be determined from a three-body observable.

## Method

The author reviews the EFT for nonrelativistic bosons with large S-wave scattering length \(a\), starting from a Lagrangian with two-body and three-body contact interactions. The two-body scattering amplitude is obtained by summing bubble diagrams, while the three-body atom-dimer scattering amplitude is given by an integral equation that sums one-atom exchange and three-body interaction contributions to all orders, projected onto S-waves. Renormalization is performed by introducing an ultraviolet cutoff \(\Lambda\) and demanding invariance of low-energy three-body observables under changes of this cutoff, leading to a three-body coupling \(H(\Lambda)\) that runs on a limit cycle with discrete scale invariance.

## Key numbers

- The S-wave nucleon-nucleon scattering lengths are \(a_s = -23.76\) fm (spin-singlet) and \(a_t = 5.42\) fm (spin-triplet), with effective ranges \(r_s = 2.75\) fm and \(r_t = 1.76\) fm respectively.
- For \(^4\)He atoms, the scattering length \(a \approx 104\) Angstroms, more than a factor 10 larger than the typical low-energy length scale \(l \approx 5\) Angstroms given by the van der Waals interaction.
- The three-body parameter \(s_0 = 1.00624...\) solves the transcendental equation \(s_0 \cosh(\pi s_0/2) = (8/\sqrt{3})\sinh(\pi s_0/6)\).
- When the cutoff \(\Lambda\) is increased by a factor of \(\exp(\pi/s_0) \approx 22.7\), the three-body coupling \(H(\Lambda)\) returns to its original value.
- The ratio of binding energies of successively shallower Efimov states approaches \(\lambda_0^2 = \exp(2\pi/s_0) \approx 515\).
- The atom-dimer scattering length for \(a>0\) is given by \(a_{12} = a\, (1.46 - 2.15\tan[s_0 \ln(a\Lambda_*) + 0.09])\,(1 + \mathcal{O}(l/a))\).
- For the triton binding energy \(B_t = 8.48\) MeV as input, the predicted \(\alpha\) particle binding energy is \(B_\alpha = 29.5\) MeV using \(a_s\) and \(a_t\) as two-body input, and \(B_\alpha = 26.9\) MeV using \(a_s\) and \(B_d\) as input, compared to the experimental value \(B_\alpha^{exp} = (29.0 \pm 0.1)\) MeV.
- For \(N\)-boson droplets in two dimensions, the asymptotic ratios for large \(N\) are \(R_{N+1}/R_N \approx 0.3417\) and \(B_{N+1}/B_N \approx 8.567\).
- The three-body system in 2D has ground state binding energy \(B_3^{(0)} = 16.522688(1)\, B_2\) and one excited state with \(B_3^{(1)} = 1.2704091(1)\, B_2\).
- The four-body system in 2D has ground state binding energy \(B_4^{(0)} = 197.3(1)\, B_2\) and one excited state with \(B_4^{(1)} = 25.5(1)\, B_2\).

## Context

This review extends the universal predictions for three-body systems first derived by Efimov (1971, 1979) and builds on the EFT framework developed by Bedaque, Hammer, and van Kolck (1999) for three-body systems with large scattering length. The work connects to nuclear physics through the Phillips line (Phillips, 1968) relating the spin-doublet neutron-deuteron scattering length to the triton binding energy, and the Tjon line (Tjon, 1975) correlating triton and alpha particle binding energies. The author discusses recent extensions to the four-body system by Platter, Hammer, and Meissner (2004) and to \(N\)-boson droplets in two spatial dimensions by Hammer and Son (2004), and conjectures that QCD can be tuned to an infrared limit cycle by adjusting the up and down quark masses, which would manifest as the Efimov effect for the triton.
