# Searching for Chameleon-like Scalar Fields

> Using the 32-m Medicina, 45-m Nobeyama, and 100-m Effelsberg telescopes we found a statistically significant velocity offset Delta V = 27 +/- 3 m/s (1sigma) between the inversion transition in NH3(1,1) and low-J rotational transitions in N2H+(1-0) and HC3N(2-1) arising in cold and dense molecular cores in the Milky Way. Systematic shifts of the line centers caused by turbulent motions and velocity gradients, possible non-thermal hyperfine structure populations, pressure and optical depth effects are shown to be lower than or about 1 m/s and thus can be neglected in the total error budget. The reproducibility of Delta V at the same facility (Effelsberg telescope) on a year-to-year basis is found to be very good. Since the frequencies of the inversion and rotational transitions have different sensitivities to variations in mu = m_e/m_p, the revealed non-zero Delta V may imply that mu changes when measured at high (terrestrial) and low (interstellar) matter densities as predicted by chameleon-like scalar field models - candidates to the dark energy carrier. Thus we are testing whether scalar field models have chameleon-type interactions with ordinary matter. The measured velocity offset corresponds to the ratio Delta mu/mu = (mu_space - mu_lab)/mu_lab of (26 +/- 3)x10^{-9} (1sigma).

# Searching for Chameleon-like Scalar Fields

## Key claim
The measured velocity offset of 27 ± 3 m/s between NH₃ inversion and rotational transitions in cold molecular cores may indicate that the electron-to-proton mass ratio μ differs between terrestrial and low-density interstellar environments, as predicted by chameleon-like scalar field models. However, the authors caution that new laboratory measurements of rest frequencies and additional observations with other molecular transitions are required before reaching more definite conclusions.

## Method
The authors used the 32-m Medicina, 45-m Nobeyama, and 100-m Effelsberg telescopes to observe NH₃(1,1) inversion transitions at 23.694 GHz alongside N₂H⁺(1-0) and HC₃N(2-1) rotational transitions toward cold, dense molecular cores in the Milky Way. They applied strict selection criteria requiring symmetric line profiles, thermally dominated line widths, resolved hyperfine structure components, and low optical depths, then performed Gaussian fitting to extract radial velocities and compute the velocity offset ΔV = V_rot - V_inv, which was converted to Δμ/μ using the sensitivity coefficient Q_inv = 4.46 for the NH₃ inversion transition.

## Key numbers
- Velocity offset ΔV = 27 ± 3 m/s (1σ) between NH₃(1,1) inversion and low-J rotational transitions of N₂H⁺(1-0) and HC₃N(2-1)
- Δμ/μ = (26 ± 3) × 10⁻⁹ (1σ) derived from the velocity offset
- For the reduced sample of 23 sources meeting selection criteria: weighted mean ⟨ΔV⟩_W = 20.7 ± 3.0 m/s, robust M-estimate ⟨ΔV⟩_M = 21.5 ± 2.8 m/s, median ΔV_med = 22 m/s
- For the two quietest cores L1498 and L1512: ⟨ΔV⟩_M = 26.9 ± 1.2_stat ± 3.0_sys m/s, giving Δμ/μ = (26 ± 1_stat ± 3_sys) × 10⁻⁹
- Systematic shifts from turbulent motions, velocity gradients, hyperfine structure effects, pressure, and optical depth are ≤ 1 m/s
- Reproducibility of ΔV at the Effelsberg telescope on a year-to-year basis shows dispersion σ(ΔV) = 2.0 m/s from repeated measurements

## Context
This work extends the ammonia method previously applied to extragalactic sources by Flambaum and Kozlov (2007) and others (Murphy 2008, Mendez 2008, Henkel 2009), achieving 10³ times better sensitivity (10⁻⁹ versus 10⁻⁶) by observing within the Milky Way rather than at cosmological distances. The results tentatively support the chameleon scalar field models of Khoury and Weltman (2004) and Olive and Pospelov (2008), which predict that fundamental constants may vary with local matter density. The authors propose that future observations of H₃O⁺ transitions, which have sensitivity coefficient differences ΔQ = 14.7 (four times larger than the ammonia method), could provide an independent test of the Δμ/μ value.
