# Neutrinos and the Standard Model

> Since their "discovery" by Pauli in 1930, neutrinos have plaed a key part in confirmation of the structure of the standard model of strong and electroweak interactions. After reviewing ways in which this has been manifested in the past, we discuss areas in which neutrinos continue to play this role.

## Key claim

Neutrinos have been and continue to be essential for confirming and refining the Standard Model of strong and electroweak interactions, but the paper argues that beyond well-known tests (chirality, Dirac vs. Majorana character, mass), neutrino scattering also provides unique constraints on subtle QCD predictions and nucleon structure that are not widely appreciated.

## Method

The paper reviews the historical role of neutrinos in establishing the V-A structure of the weak interaction, parity violation, and the Standard Model electroweak sector. It then examines three contemporary applications: using neutrino-scattering measurements of the axial charge radius to test the Goldberger-Treiman relation and chiral perturbation theory predictions; comparing neutrino-scattering and electroproduction determinations of the axial radius to confirm a chiral-loop correction; and using neutral-current neutrino scattering to extract the strange quark axial matrix element of the nucleon.

## Key numbers

- Neutron lifetime: \(887 \pm 2\) sec, yielding \(G_F \simeq 10^{-5} M_p^{-2}\).
- Neutrino scattering cross section at \(E_{\bar{\nu}} = 1\) MeV: \(\sim 10^{-44}\) cm\(^2\).
- Mean free path in earthlike density: \(\Delta x \sim 10^{21}\) cm (\(\sim 10^{10}\) earth radii).
- Limit on Majorana neutrino mass from neutrinoless double beta decay: \(\langle m_\nu^M \rangle \leq 0.2\) eV.
- Direct upper limit on electron neutrino mass from tritium beta decay: 7.2 eV (Weinheimer et al.) and 9.3 eV (Robertson et al.).
- Supernova 1987A neutrino mass limit: \(\sim 20\) eV.
- Goldberger-Treiman discrepancy from neutrino scattering: \(\Delta_\pi = 0.034\) (using axial radius \(r_A = 0.65 \pm 0.03\) fm).
- Dashen-Weinstein prediction for Goldberger-Treiman discrepancy: \(\Delta_\pi = 0.017\).
- Two experimental values for \(\pi N\) coupling: \(g_{\pi NN}(m_\pi^2) = 13.05 \pm 0.08\) (Nijmegen) and \(13.65 \pm 0.30\) (Bugg and Macleidt), giving \(\Delta_\pi = 0.014 \pm 0.006\) and \(0.056 \pm 0.020\) respectively.
- Axial charge radius from electroproduction: \(r_A = 0.59 \pm 0.05\) fm; from neutrino scattering: \(r_A = 0.65 \pm 0.03\) fm; chiral perturbation theory predicts a difference of 0.046 fm\(^2\).
- Strange quark axial matrix element from BNL neutrino scattering: \(\Delta s = -0.15 \pm 0.09\).
- Strange quark helicity from deep inelastic scattering: \(\Delta s = -0.11\).
- Sigma term from \(\pi N\) scattering: \(\sim 60\) MeV, lowered to \(\sim 45\) MeV by chiral corrections; hyperon mass constraint gives \(\sim 25\) MeV (increasing to \(\sim 35\) MeV with higher-order corrections).

## Context

This paper is a review talk presented at a neutrino workshop, summarizing how neutrino physics has historically validated the Standard Model and continues to probe its limits. It extends the work of Goldhaber, Grodzins, and Sunyar (1958) on neutrino helicity, the Cowan-Reines (1956) neutrino discovery, and the theoretical framework of Lee and Yang (1956), Feynman and Gell-Mann (1958), and Weinberg and Salam (1967). It specifically discusses the Goldberger-Treiman relation, the Nambu-Schrauner low-energy theorem (corrected by Bernard, Kaiser, and Meissner in chiral perturbation theory), and the nucleon strangeness content problem (Donoghue and Nappi). The paper enables future precision tests of the Standard Model via improved neutrino scattering experiments and direct mass measurements.
