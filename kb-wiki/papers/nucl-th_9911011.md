# Melting or nucleon transfer in fusion of heavy nuclei?

> The time-dependent transition between a diabatic interaction potential in the entrance channel and an adiabatic potential during the fusion process is investigated within the two-center shell model. A large hindrance is obtained for the motion to smaller elongations of near symmetric dinuclear systems. The comparison of the calculated energy thresholds for the complete fusion in different relevant collective variables shows that the dinuclear system prefers to evolve in the mass asymmetry coordinate by nucleon transfer to the compound nucleus.

## Key claim

The paper argues that in the fusion of heavy, near-symmetric nuclei, the dinuclear system (DNS) preferentially evolves toward the compound nucleus via nucleon transfer in the mass asymmetry coordinate (η-channel) rather than by a collective motion to smaller elongations (λ-channel), because the time needed to transition from a diabatic to an adiabatic potential in the elongation coordinate is longer than the DNS lifetime.

## Method

The authors used the two-center shell model (TCSM) to calculate time-dependent diabatic and adiabatic potential energy surfaces for the elongation coordinate λ. They introduced a relaxation-time approximation for the transition between these potentials, with the relaxation time determined by an average single-particle width. Fusion probabilities in the λ-channel and η-channel were computed using a one-dimensional Kramers rate expression, and the competition between these channels was evaluated by comparing the calculated fusion barriers and probabilities for several symmetric and near-symmetric reactions leading to the compound nuclei ²²⁰U and ²⁴⁶Fm.

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system (DNS) concept of Volkov (V1, V2) by providing a microscopic justification for the dominance of the mass asymmetry coordinate in heavy-ion fusion. It contradicts the traditional "melting" picture where fusion occurs via a collective motion in the elongation coordinate, which overestimates fusion cross sections for near-symmetric reactions such as ¹¹⁰Pd+¹¹⁰Pd (IOC, FR, SW). The results enable a better understanding of fusion hindrance in superheavy element synthesis and support the use of asymmetric entrance channels to enhance fusion probabilities.
