# Low-energy fusion caused by an interference

> Fusion of two deuterons of room temperature energy is studied. The nuclei are in vacuum with no connection to any external source (electric or magnetic field, illumination, surrounding matter, traps, etc.) which may accelerate them. The energy of the two nuclei is conserved and remains small during the motion through the Coulomb barrier. The penetration through this barrier, which is the main obstacle for low-energy fusion, strongly depends on a form of the incident flux on the Coulomb center at large distances from it. In contrast to the usual scattering, the incident wave is not a single plane wave but the certain superposition of plane waves of the same energy and various directions, for example, a convergent conical wave. As a result of interference, the wave function close to the Coulomb center is determined by a cusp caustic which is probed by de Broglie waves. The particle flux gets away from the cusp and moves to the Coulomb center providing a not small probability of fusion (cusp driven tunneling). Getting away from a caustic cusp also occurs in optics and acoustics.

# Low-energy fusion caused by an interference

## Key claim
Two bare deuterons at room temperature energy, isolated from any external acceleration source, can penetrate the Coulomb barrier with a not-small probability and subsequently fuse, provided the incident flux is not a single plane wave but a specific superposition of plane waves of the same energy and various directions (e.g., a convergent conical wave). The claim depends on interference producing a cusp caustic that directs particle flux toward the Coulomb center, a mechanism the author calls "cusp driven tunneling."

## Method
The author solves the Schrödinger equation for two deuterons in the Coulomb field using parabolic coordinates and a semiclassical (Hamilton-Jacobi) approach with a large parameter B = (e²/ħc)√(2Mc²/E). The wave function is constructed as a superposition of partial waves with different separation constants β, weighted by exponential functions exp(∓iBαβ) where α is a fixed positive parameter. The β-integration is performed by the saddle-point method, and the resulting caustic surfaces and flux distributions are analyzed to determine the wave function amplitude near the Coulomb center.

## Key numbers
- For deuterium-deuterium fusion at room temperature T = 300 K ≈ 2.58×10⁻² eV, the de Broglie wavelength is λ ≈ 1.26 Å, and the two deuterons enter the Coulomb barrier at distance e²/E ≈ 557 Å between them.
- The WKB parameter πB ≈ 6174.9 for this system, giving a conventional fusion probability w ∼ 10⁻²⁶⁸².
- The reduced mass for D-D fusion is M = M_D/2, with M_Dc² ≈ 1.87×10⁹ eV; the nuclear Bohr radius is ħ²/Me² ≈ 2.88×10⁻¹² cm.
- The wave function at the left cusp (z = -z_s, r = 0) relative to the right cusp (z = z_s, r = 0) is suppressed by a factor exp(-πB/2).
- The nuclear fusion radius is R₀ ∼ 10⁻¹³ cm.
- The D-D fusion reaction releases 3.27 MeV; the D-T fusion reaction releases 17.59 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the author's prior proposals on interference in tunneling (Ivlev, various years) and contrasts with the standard WKB treatment of low-energy fusion, which predicts exponentially small probabilities for room-temperature deuterons. The mechanism differs from conventional approaches that accelerate nuclei via electric fields (e.g., pyroelectric crystals, as in Putterman 2005) or rely on local heating. The paper builds on the semiclassical treatment of Coulomb scattering in parabolic coordinates (Landau and Lifshitz) and on caustic theory (Landau and Lifshitz). If experimentally realizable, the required convergent conical wave flux could enable measurable low-energy fusion rates without external acceleration, though the paper only briefly discusses experimental conditions.
