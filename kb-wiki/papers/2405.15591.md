# Constraints for rare electron-capture decays mimicking detection of dark-matter particles in nuclear transitions

> We give for the first time, theoretical estimates of unknown rare electron-capture (EC) decay branchings of $^{44}$Ti, $^{57}$Co, and $^{139}$Ce, relevant for searches of (exotic) dark-matter particles. The nuclear-structure calculations have been done exploiting the nuclear shell model (NSM) with well-established Hamiltonians and an advanced theory of $β$ decay. In the absence of experimental measurements of these rare branches, these estimates are of utmost importance for terrestrial searches of dark-matter particles, such as axionic dark matter in the form of axion-like particles (ALPs), anapole dark matter, and dark photons in nuclear transitions. Predictions are made for EC-decay rates of 2$^{nd}$-forbidden unique (FU) and 2$^{nd}$-forbidden non-unique (FNU) EC transitions that can potentially mimic dark-matter-particle detection in dedicated underground experiments designed to observe the absence of the corresponding nuclear electromagnetic transitions.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first theoretical estimates of minimum branching ratios for rare second-forbidden electron-capture (EC) decays in 44Ti, 57Co, and 139Ce, which could mimic dark-matter signals in underground experiments. The authors emphasize that these are lower-limit estimates derived using a range of effective axial-vector coupling values (gA_eff = 0.5-1.27) and a minimization procedure for the small relativistic nuclear matrix element (sNME) in forbidden non-unique transitions, rather than precise predictions.

## Method

The authors performed nuclear shell-model (NSM) calculations using the NuShellX@MSU code with well-established Hamiltonians (NOWPN, GX1APN, KB3GPN, SN100PN) in appropriate model spaces for each nucleus. They computed EC branching ratios for second-forbidden unique (FU) and second-forbidden non-unique (FNU) transitions using the advanced theory of beta decay, treating the effective axial-vector coupling gA_eff as a free parameter in the range 0.5-1.27. For FNU transitions, they identified the unique sNME value that maximizes the EC half-life and thus minimizes the branching ratio, providing conservative lower-limit estimates for experiments.

## Key numbers

- Minimum branching ratio for 44Ti(0+_1) -> 44Sc(2+_1) GS-to-GS decay: 4.72 x 10^-9 (at gA_eff = 1.27), with corresponding maximum half-life t_1/2,max = 3.95 x 10^17 s
- Minimum branching ratio for 57Co(7/2-_1) -> 57Fe(1/2-_1) GS-to-GS decay: 1.63 x 10^-9 (at gA_eff = 0.5), with t_1/2,max = 1.44 x 10^16 s
- Minimum branching ratio for 57Co(7/2-_1) -> 57Fe*(3/2-_1) GS-to-ES decay: 2.47 x 10^-8 (at gA_eff = 0.6-0.7), with t_1/2,max = 9.51 x 10^14 s
- Minimum branching ratio for 139Ce(3/2+_1) -> 139La(7/2+_1) GS-to-GS decay: 1.00 x 10^-9 (at gA_eff = 0.5), with t_1/2,max = 1.19 x 10^16 s
- Experimental upper limit for 139Ce invisible transition rate: Gamma_invisible/Gamma_total < 9.70 x 10^-7 (95% C.L.) from the Minowa experiment
- Compiled NNDC value for 139Ce GS-to-GS branch: 5(5) x 10^-9

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for rare EC decays developed by Suhonen and collaborators (2017, 2021) for 113Cd and 115In, applying it to three isotopes relevant for dark-matter searches. The calculations provide essential background estimates for experiments searching for axion-like particles (ALPs) via M1 transitions in 57Fe and 44Sc, dark photons via E1 transitions in 44Sc, and anapole dark matter via E2 transitions in 57Fe, as originally proposed by Donnelly (1978). The results enable experimental groups to set detector requirements and interpret null results in underground dark-matter searches using radioactive sources.
