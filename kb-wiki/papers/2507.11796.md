# Monopole and Seniority Truncations in the Large-Scale Configuration Interaction Shell Model Approach

> This paper addresses the challenges of solving the quantum many-body problem, particularly within nuclear physics, through the configuration interaction (CI) method. Large-scale shell model calculations often become computationally infeasible for systems with a large number of valence particles, requiring truncation techniques. We propose truncation methods for the nuclear shell model, in which angular momentum is conserved and rotational symmetry is restored. We introduce the monopole-interaction-based truncation and seniority truncation strategies, designed to reduce the dimension of the calculations. These truncations can be established by considering certain partitions based on their importance and selecting physically meaningful states. We examine these truncations for Sn, Xe, and Pb isotopes, demonstrating their effectiveness in overcoming computational limits. These truncations work well for systems with either a single type of valence nucleon or with both types. With these truncations, we are able to achieve good convergence for the energy at a very small portion of the total dimension.

## Method

The authors implement two truncation schemes within the configuration interaction shell model framework: monopole-interaction-based truncation, which selects partitions based on their importance as determined by the monopole part of the effective interaction, and seniority truncation, which restricts the number of unpaired nucleons. These truncations are applied to the nuclear shell model while conserving angular momentum and restoring rotational symmetry. The methods are tested on Sn, Xe, and Pb isotopes for systems with either one or both types of valence nucleons.

## Context

This work extends the large-scale configuration interaction shell model approach, which often becomes computationally infeasible for systems with many valence particles, by introducing truncation techniques that reduce the computational dimension. The monopole-interaction-based truncation builds on the concept of monopole interactions in nuclear structure, while the seniority truncation relates to the seniority scheme used in earlier shell model studies. The results enable future calculations for heavier nuclei that were previously beyond computational reach, particularly for systems with both proton and neutron valence particles.
