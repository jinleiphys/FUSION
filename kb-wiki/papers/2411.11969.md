# Black Hole Supernovae, their Equation of State Dependence and Ejecta Composition

> Recent literature on core-collapse supernovae suggests that a black hole (BH) can form within $\sim 1$ s of shock revival, while still culminating in a successful supernova. We refer to these as black hole supernovae, as they are distinct from other BH formation channels in both timescale and impact on the explosion. We simulate these events self-consistently from core-collapse until $20\text{-}50$ days after collapse using three axisymmetric models of a $60$ M$_\odot$ zero-age main sequence progenitor star and investigate how the composition of the ejecta is impacted by the BH formation. We employ Skyrme-type equations of state (EOSs) and vary the uncertain nucleonic effective mass, which affects the pressure inside the proto-neutron star through the thermal part of the EOS. This results in different BH formation times and explosion energies at BH formation, yielding final explosion energies between $0.06\text{-}0.72\times 10^{51}$ erg with $21.8\text{-}23.3$ M$_\odot$ of ejecta, of which $0\text{-}0.018$ M$_\odot$ is $^{56}$Ni. Compared to expectations from 1D simulations, we find a more nuanced EOS dependence of the explosion dynamics, the mass of the BH remnant, and the elemental composition of the ejecta. We investigate why the explosions survive despite the massive overburden and link the shape of the diagnostic energy curve and character of the ejecta evolution to the progenitor structure.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that black hole supernovae (BHSNe), successful explosions where a black hole forms within ~1 second of shock revival, are viable for a high-compactness 60 M☉ progenitor, but the explosion outcome and ejecta composition depend sensitively on the uncertain thermal part of the nuclear equation of state, specifically the nucleonic effective mass. The explosions survive despite massive overburden because the high accretion rate from the compact progenitor powers sufficient neutrino heating and turbulent convection to revive the shock even for the most thermally stiff equation of state.

## Method

The authors performed axisymmetric (2D) core-collapse supernova simulations using the FLASH code with an effective general relativistic potential, evolving three models of a 60 M☉ zero-age main sequence progenitor from collapse through black hole formation and continuing until 20–50 days post-collapse. They employed Skyrme-type equations of state varying only the nucleonic effective mass (m* = 0.55, 0.75, and 0.95 times the vacuum mass), which controls the thermal pressure in the proto-neutron star, and used backwards integration of trajectories with the SkyNet nuclear reaction network to compute nucleosynthetic yields.

## Key numbers

- Final explosion energies: 0.057 × 10⁵¹ erg (m0.95), 0.723 × 10⁵¹ erg (m0.75), and 0.668 × 10⁵¹ erg (m0.55)
- Ejecta masses: 21.67 M☉ (m0.95), 23.09 M☉ (m0.75), and 22.80 M☉ (m0.55)
- ⁵⁶Ni ejecta: 0.000 M☉ (m0.95), 0.0157 M☉ (m0.75), and 0.0182 M☉ (m0.55)
- Black hole formation times post-bounce: 0.93 s (m0.95), 1.36 s (m0.75), and 1.45 s (m0.55)
- Shock revival times post-bounce: 0.292 s (m0.95), 0.317 s (m0.75), and 0.346 s (m0.55)
- Final black hole gravitational masses: 25.48 M☉ (m0.95), 24.02 M☉ (m0.75), and 24.30 M☉ (m0.55)
- Progenitor compactness ξ₂.₅ = 0.63, with remaining mass 47.31 M☉ at collapse

## Context

This work extends the recent discovery of black hole supernovae, explosions where black hole formation occurs within ~1 second of shock revival, first identified in 3D simulations by Chan (2018, 2020) and Burrows (2023), and in 2D simulations by Powell (2021), Rahman (2022), and Sykes (2023, 2024). The authors systematically vary the thermal part of the equation of state following the approach of Schneider (2019, 2020), who showed that the nucleonic effective mass dominates the uncertainty in core-collapse supernova outcomes, particularly for high-compactness progenitors. The results enable predictions for the black hole mass distribution and nucleosynthetic yields from this newly identified BH formation channel, which is distinct from both failed supernovae and fallback supernovae in its timescale and impact on the explosion.
