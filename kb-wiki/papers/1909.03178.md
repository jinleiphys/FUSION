# The energy and scale dependence of $\hat{q}$ and the JET puzzle

> We present an attempt to probe the underlying structure of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) at high resolution, based on the extracted jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}$. We argue that the exchanged momentum $k$ between the hard parton and the medium varies over a range of scales, and for $k\geq$ 1 GeV, $\hat{q}$ can be expressed in terms of a parton distribution function (PDF). Because the mass of a QGP constituent is unknown, we define a scaling variable $x_N$ to represent the ratio of the parton momentum to the momentum of a self-contained section of the plasma which has a mass of 1 GeV. This scaling variable is used to parametrize the QGP-PDF. Calculations, based on this reconstructed $\hat{q}$ are compared to data sensitive to the hardcore of jets $i.e.,$ the single hadron suppression in terms of the nuclear modification factor $R_{AA}$ and the azimuthal anisotropy parameter $v_{2}$, as a function of transverse momentum $p_{\mathrm{T}}$, centrality and energy of the collision. It is demonstrated that the scale evolution of the QGP-PDF is responsible for the reduction in the normalization of $\hat{q}$ between fits to Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) and Large Hadron Collider (LHC) data; a puzzle, first discovered by the JET collaboration.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the reduction in the normalization of the jet transport coefficient $\hat{q}$ between RHIC and LHC data (the "JET puzzle") can be explained by the scale dependence of $\hat{q}$ on the virtuality of the hard parton, rather than by a cusp-like temperature dependence near $T_c$ as previously proposed by Xu et al. (2015). The authors claim that at higher collision energies, leading partons have higher virtuality and thus probe the QGP at shorter distance scales where it appears more dilute, analogous to DGLAP evolution of PDFs.

## Method

The authors formulate $\hat{q}$ in terms of a QGP parton distribution function (QGP-PDF) using a scaling variable $x_N$ defined as the ratio of the parton momentum to the momentum of a plasma constituent with mass 1 GeV. They implement two prescriptions (labeled [A] and [B]) for integrating the perturbative contribution to $\hat{q}$ over a range of exchanged transverse momentum $k_\perp$ that depends on the hard parton's virtuality $\mu^2$, with the range centered at $\mu$ (prescription [A]) or starting at $\mu$ (prescription [B]). The resulting scale-dependent $\hat{q}$ is then used in a medium-modified DGLAP evolution equation to compute single hadron suppression ($R_{AA}$) and azimuthal anisotropy ($v_2$) at both RHIC and LHC energies.

## Key numbers

- The separation scale between perturbative and non-perturbative contributions to $\hat{q}$ is set at $\mu_0^2 = 1$ GeV$^2$.
- The highest initial temperature at LHC is "a bare 20% higher" than at RHIC, while hadron $p_T$ at LHC ($\sim$100 GeV) is an order of magnitude higher than at RHIC ($\sim$10 GeV).
- Calculations are restricted to $p_T \geq 8$ GeV for RHIC and $p_T \geq 10$ GeV for LHC.
- The formation time is defined as $\tau^{-} = 2q^-/\mu^2$ where $\mu^2 \sim l_\perp^2$.
- The integral range for $\hat{q}$ in prescription [A] is from $\mu^2/4$ to $9\mu^2/4$, and in prescription [B] from $\mu^2$ to $9\mu^2$.

## Context

This work extends the JET collaboration extraction of $\hat{q}$ (Burke et al., 2013) which first identified the puzzle that the interaction strength $\hat{\mathscr{Q}}(T) = \hat{q}(T)/T^3$ at the same temperature is lower at LHC than at RHIC. It contradicts the proposal by Xu et al. (2015) that a cusp-like temperature dependence of $\hat{\mathscr{Q}}$ near $T_c$ (based on magnetic monopole scenarios) explains the discrepancy, and is consistent with findings by Andres et al. (2016) using the ASW formalism that $\hat{\mathscr{Q}}$ depends on collision energy rather than local temperature. The work enables a unified description of jet quenching across different collision energies using scale-dependent transport coefficients, and suggests that future extractions of $\hat{q}$ must account for the virtuality of the probing parton.
