# $β$-decay of key titanium isotopes in stellar environment

> Amongst iron regime nuclei, $β$-decay rates on titanium isotopes are considered to be important during the late phases of evolution of massive stars. The key $β$-decay isotopes during presupernova evolution were searched from available literature and a microscopic calculation of the decay rates were performed using the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory. As per earlier simulation results electron capture and $β$-decay on certain isotopes of titanium are considered to be important for the presupernova evolution of massive stars. Earlier the stellar electron capture rates and neutrino energy loss rates due to relevant titanium isotopes were presented. In this paper we finally present the $β$-decay rates of key titanium isotopes in stellar environment. The results are also compared against previous calculations. The pn-QRPA $β$-decay rates are bigger at high stellar temperatures and smaller at high stellar densities compared to the large scale shell model results. This study can prove useful for the core-collapse simulators.

# β-decay of key titanium isotopes in stellar environment

## Key claim

The pn-QRPA β-decay rates for six titanium isotopes (⁵¹Ti through ⁵⁶Ti) are systematically larger than large-scale shell model (LSSM) rates at high stellar temperatures and smaller at high stellar densities, with the comparison depending on the specific isotope and the temperature-density regime. The authors claim these microscopic rates provide a reliable alternative nuclear physics input for core-collapse simulators, but note that the rates are fragile functions of available phase space and can change by orders of magnitude with a 0.5 MeV uncertainty in Q-values.

## Method

The authors used the proton-neutron quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-QRPA) theory to perform a microscopic state-by-state calculation of β-decay transition probabilities and phase space integrals for six titanium isotopes (⁵¹Ti, ⁵²Ti, ⁵³Ti, ⁵⁴Ti, ⁵⁵Ti, ⁵⁶Ti) identified as important from the survey of Aufderheide et al. (1994). The total stellar β-decay rate was computed as the sum over all initial parent excited states (weighted by Boltzmann occupation probabilities) and final daughter states, using a model space of up to 7ħω oscillator shells. Positron capture rates were also calculated for comparison.

## Key numbers

- At low stellar densities (log ρYₑ = 1.0 g/cm³) and low temperature (T₉ = 0.01 × 10⁹ K), the β-decay rates in log₁₀(s⁻¹) are: ⁵¹Ti = -2.698, ⁵²Ti = -3.465, ⁵³Ti = -2.137, ⁵⁴Ti = -0.466, ⁵⁵Ti = -0.146, ⁵⁶Ti = 1.004.
- At high density (log ρYₑ = 11.0 g/cm³) and low temperature (T₉ = 0.01 × 10⁹ K), all six titanium isotopes have β-decay rates smaller than 10⁻¹⁰⁰ s⁻¹ (tabulated as -100).
- At high temperature (T₉ = 30 × 10⁹ K) and low density (log ρYₑ = 1.0 g/cm³), the β-decay rates in log₁₀(s⁻¹) are: ⁵¹Ti = 0.221, ⁵²Ti = 0.699, ⁵³Ti = 1.172, ⁵⁴Ti = 1.424, ⁵⁵Ti = 1.616, ⁵⁶Ti = 2.138.
- For ⁵⁵Ti, the pn-QRPA β-decay rates are bigger than LSSM rates by a factor of 2-3 at densities ρYₑ = 10¹, 10⁴, 10⁷ g/cm³, but at high densities LSSM rates exceed pn-QRPA rates.
- For ⁵⁶Ti at T₉ = 30 × 10⁹ K, the pn-QRPA decay rates are around a factor of 30 bigger than LSSM rates.
- The Q-value used for ⁵⁵Ti β-decay was 7.34 MeV (pn-QRPA) and 7.81 MeV (LSSM), while FFN used 8.64 MeV.
- Positron capture rates only compete with β-decay rates at high stellar temperatures T₉ ∼ 30 × 10⁹ K, with ratios reaching 2.25×10² for ⁵¹Ti at log ρYₑ = 1.0 g/cm³ and T₉ = 30.

## Context

This work extends the microscopic calculation of weak interaction rates for titanium isotopes, following earlier presentations of stellar electron capture rates (Nabi et al., 2007) and neutrino/antineutrino energy loss rates (Nabi et al., 2010) for the same nuclei. The six isotopes were selected from the survey of Aufderheide et al. (1994), who identified the 71 top β-decay nuclei for presupernova evolution. The results are compared against the pioneering calculations of Fuller, Fowler and Newman (FFN, 1980) and the large-scale shell model (LSSM) calculations of Langanke and collaborators (2000). The study validates the finding that FFN systematically placed back resonances at too low energies, leading to overestimated β-decay rates for even-even and odd-A nuclei, while the pn-QRPA and LSSM show generally fair agreement. The work enables core-collapse simulators to use these microscopic rates as an alternative nuclear physics input, potentially improving the modeling of the lepton-to-baryon ratio (Yₑ) evolution during presupernova phases.
