# Estimation of transport coefficients of dense hadronic and quark matter

> We calculate the transport coefficients like shear viscosity and electrical conductivity with respect to density of dense hadronic and quark matter. By considering the simple massless limit for the quark matter and two different effective models for the hadronic matter, we have estimated the transport coefficients of the two phases separately. We have sketched the density profile of the transport coefficients in two parts viz. the phase-space part and the relaxation time part. By calculating the shear viscosity to density ratio, we have also explored the nearly perfect fluid domain of the quark and hadronic matter.

## Key claim

The paper estimates shear viscosity and electrical conductivity for dense hadronic and quark matter at zero temperature, but the results are explicitly model-dependent and limited to nucleonic degrees of freedom (no hyperons) and two-flavor massless quark matter (no strange quarks), with the hadronic calculations using two different relativistic mean-field models that yield different effective masses and density behaviors.

## Method

The authors calculate shear viscosity and electrical conductivity at zero temperature using the relaxation time approximation (RTA) of the Boltzmann transport equation, with the relaxation time estimated from the scattering cross section and Fermi velocity. For the hadronic phase, they use two different relativistic mean-field models: an effective chiral model (HM1) and an extended RMF model with BSP parametrization (HM2), both considering only nucleons with a fixed 15% proton fraction. For the quark phase, they use the MIT bag model with massless u and d quarks (masses 2.2 MeV and 4.7 MeV respectively), taking the massless limit for the transport coefficient expressions.

## Context

This work extends the approach of comparing transport coefficients along the density axis, analogous to how normalized transport coefficients are studied as functions of temperature in heavy-ion collisions (CAI_JD_SG) and how lattice QCD thermodynamics maps onto hadron resonance gas and perturbative QCD calculations at high temperature. The paper builds on prior microscopic calculations of transport coefficients in dense hadronic matter (Schmitt 2017, Shternin 2020, Shternin 2013, Shternin 2017, Shang 2020, Nandi 2018, Shternin 2021) but focuses only on the strong interaction contribution within QCD time scales, excluding electromagnetic contributions from electrons and muons. The results enable comparison of the nearly perfect fluid behavior between hadronic and quark phases at high baryon density, similar to the pattern seen at high temperature.
