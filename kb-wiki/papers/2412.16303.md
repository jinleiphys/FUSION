# Machine Learning Neutrino-Nucleus Cross Sections

> Neutrino-nucleus scattering cross sections are critical theoretical inputs for long-baseline neutrino oscillation experiments. However, robust modeling of these cross sections remains challenging. For a simple but physically motivated toy model of the DUNE experiment, we demonstrate that an accurate neural-network model of the cross section -- leveraging only Standard-Model symmetries -- can be learned from near-detector data. We perform a neutrino oscillation analysis with simulated far-detector events, finding that oscillation analysis results enabled by our data-driven cross-section model approach the theoretical limit achievable with perfect prior knowledge of the cross section. We further quantify the effects of flux shape and detector resolution uncertainties as well as systematics from cross-section mismodeling. This proof-of-principle study highlights the potential of future neutrino near-detector datasets and data-driven cross-section models.

## Key claim
For a toy model of the DUNE experiment, a neural-network model of the neutrino-nucleus cross section that uses only Standard-Model symmetries can be learned from near-detector data and yields oscillation analysis results approaching the theoretical limit achievable with perfect prior knowledge of the cross section, but only when the near-detector and far-detector marginal distributions over (x, Q²) strongly overlap.

## Method
The authors constructed a neural-network parameterization of three nuclear structure functions W_i(x,Q²) using a multi-layer perceptron with 4 hidden layers of width 64 and LeakyReLU activations, trained on mock near-detector data consisting of outgoing muon energy and angle distributions. They performed a closure test using a toy cross-section model based on CT18NNLO parton distribution functions and the quark-parton model, with DUNE neutrino fluxes and oscillation probabilities computed using NuFast. The trained cross-section model was then applied to infer oscillation parameters (sin²(2θ₂₃) and Δm²₃₁) by maximizing likelihood over 6200 simulated far-detector events, with uncertainties estimated via bootstrap resampling with 25000 synthetic datasets.

## Key numbers
- The neural network used 4 hidden layers of width 64 with LeakyReLU activations, trained for 10⁴ steps of the Adam optimizer
- Near-detector and far-detector event densities were computed on a 256 × 128² grid over E_ν (0–10 GeV), v₁ (0.25–2.5 GeV^{1/2}), and v₂ (0–0.65 GeV^{1/2})
- The oscillation analysis used 6200 simulated far-detector events, matching DUNE statistics expected after 3.5 years of running in neutrino mode
- DUNE is projected to observe N_ND ∼ 30 × 10⁶ ν_μ events at the near detector in 3.5 years
- True oscillation parameters were taken from NuFit-6.0: sin²θ₂₃=0.561, Δm²₃₁=2.534×10⁻³ eV², with a baseline of 1300 km
- Discrepancies between true and model near-detector densities were at the ∼1% level; far-detector discrepancies were at the few-percent level
- Approximately 3% of model cross-section values were negative and clamped to zero during far-detector inference

## Context
This work extends the approach of using neural-network parameterizations for parton distribution functions (Ball 2011, AbdulKhalek 2019) and lepton-nucleus cross sections (Forte 2002, Candido 2023, Sobczyk 2024) to the problem of neutrino oscillation analysis. It contrasts with traditional near-detector tuning methods used by NOvA (2020, 2021) and T2K (2021, 2023), which can absorb mismodeling through unphysical parameter shifts. The proof-of-principle study enables future applications to experimental data and event generators, and establishes a framework for incorporating systematic uncertainties from flux shape, detector resolution, and finite near-detector statistics.
