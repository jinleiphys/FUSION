# Negative Kaons in Dense Baryonic Matter

> Kaon polarization operator in dense baryonic matter of arbitrary isotopic composition is calculated including s- and p-wave kaon-baryon interactions. The regular part of the polarization operator is extracted from the realistic kaon-nucleon interaction based on the chiral and 1/N_c expansion. Contributions of the Lambda(1116), Sigma(1195), Sigma*(1385) resonances are taken explicitly into account in the pole and regular terms with inclusion of mean-field potentials. The baryon-baryon correlations are incorporated and fluctuation contributions are estimated. Results are applied for K- in neutron star matter. Within our model a second-order phase transition to the s-wave K- condensate state occurs at $rho_c \gsim 4 ρ_0$ once the baryon-baryon correlations are included. We show that the second-order phase transition to the p-wave $K^-$ condensate state may occur at densities $ρ_c \sim 3÷5 ρ_0$ in dependence on the parameter choice. We demonstrate that a first-order phase transition to a proton-enriched (approximately isospin-symmetric) nucleon matter with a p-wave K- condensate can occur at smaller densities, $ρ\lsim 2 ρ_0$. The transition is accompanied by the suppression of hyperon concentrations.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that a first-order phase transition to a proton-enriched nucleon matter with a p-wave K- condensate can occur at densities as low as approximately twice nuclear saturation density, while second-order phase transitions to s-wave or p-wave condensate states require higher densities (above 4 times saturation density for s-wave, and between 3 and 5 times saturation density for p-wave, depending on parameter choices). The key qualifier is that these results depend on the inclusion of baryon-baryon correlations and on the specific parameter choices for hyperon-nucleon interactions.

## Method

The authors constructed the K- polarization operator in dense baryonic matter using realistic kaon-nucleon scattering amplitudes derived from chiral and 1/N_c expansion, explicitly separating pole contributions from Lambda(1116), Sigma(1195), and Sigma*(1385) resonances. They incorporated baryon-baryon correlations via short-range correlation factors and included mean-field potentials acting on baryons within a relativistic mean-field model. The kaon dispersion relation was solved to find the minimum K- energy as a function of momentum, and phase transitions were identified when this minimum energy equals the electron chemical potential, with first-order transitions analyzed via Maxwell construction.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear saturation density: rho_0 = 0.17 fm^{-3}
- Second-order s-wave K- condensate transition occurs at critical density rho_c >= 4 rho_0 when baryon-baryon correlations are included
- Second-order p-wave K- condensate transition may occur at rho_c ~ 3-5 rho_0 depending on parameter choice
- First-order phase transition to proton-enriched matter with p-wave K- condensate can occur at densities rho <= 2 rho_0
- Hyperons appear in neutron star matter at densities rho > rho_{c,H} ~ 2.5-3 rho_0
- Binding energy of Lambda hyperons: E_bind^Lambda = -30 MeV
- Binding energy of Xi hyperons: E_bind^Xi = -18 MeV
- Nuclear compressibility modulus: K = 210 MeV
- Symmetry energy: a_sym = 36.8 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation: m_N^*(rho_0) = 0.85 m_N
- Kaon-nucleon-Lambda coupling constant: C_{KNLambda} ~ -0.68/m_pi
- Kaon-nucleon-Sigma coupling constant: C_{KNSigma} ~ 0.34/m_pi
- Kaon-nucleon-Sigma* coupling constant: C_{KNSigma*} ~ 0.84/m_pi

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of kaon condensation in neutron stars by Kaplan and Nelson (1986) for s-wave condensation and by Kolomeitsev and Voskresensky (1995) for p-wave condensation, incorporating improved kaon-nucleon scattering amplitudes from Lutz and Kolomeitsev (2001) that include Sigma*(1385) contributions and regular background terms. The results enable more realistic predictions for neutron star equations of state, maximum masses, and cooling scenarios, and can be tested via kaon production in heavy-ion collisions and antineutrino scattering on nuclei.
