# Shear viscosity and electrical conductivity of relativistic fluid in presence of magnetic field: a massless case

> We have explored the shear viscosity and electrical conductivity calculations for bosonic and fermionic medium, which goes from without to with magnetic field picture and then their simplified massless expressions. In presence of magnetic field, 5 independent velocity gradient tensors can be designed, so their corresponding proportional coefficients, connected with the viscous stress tensor provide us 5 shear viscosity coefficients. In existing litterateurs, two sets of tensors are available. Starting from them, present work has obtained two sets of expressions for 5 shear viscosity coefficients, which can be ultimately classified into three basic components: parallel, perpendicular and Hall components as one get same for electrical conductivity at finite magnetic field. Our calculations are based on kinetic theory approach in relaxation time approximation. Repeating same mathematical steps for finite magnetic field picture, which traditionally practiced for without field case, we have obtained 2 sets of 5 shear viscosity components, whose final expressions are in well agreements with earlier references, although a difference in methodology or steps can be clearly noticed. Realizing the massless results of viscosity and conductivity for Maxwell-Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein distribution function, we have applied them for massless quark gluon plasma and hadronic matter phases, which can provide us a rough order of strength, within which actual results will vary during quark-hadron phase transition. Present work also indicates that magnetic field might have some role for building perfect fluid nature in RHIC or LHC matter. The lower bound expectation of shear viscosity to entropy density ratio is also discussed.

## Key claim

The paper derives two sets of five shear viscosity coefficients for relativistic bosonic and fermionic fluids in a magnetic field using kinetic theory in the relaxation time approximation, showing that these coefficients can be classified into parallel, perpendicular, and Hall components, and that the massless expressions provide a rough order-of-magnitude estimate for quark-gluon plasma and hadronic matter during the quark-hadron phase transition, rather than precise predictions.

## Method

The authors start from the relativistic Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation and introduce a magnetic field via the electromagnetic field-strength tensor term. They assume a deviation from equilibrium distribution function expressed as a sum over five independent traceless velocity gradient tensors, using two different tensor sets from Landau (1936) and from Xu, Greiner, and Huang (2008). By comparing tensor structures on both sides of the Boltzmann equation, they solve for the five unknown constants and obtain general expressions for the five shear viscosity coefficients, then take the massless limit for Maxwell-Boltzmann, Fermi-Dirac, and Bose-Einstein statistics. The same procedure is repeated for electrical conductivity components.

## Key numbers

- For massless Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics at zero magnetic field, entropy density \( s = \frac{4g}{\pi^2} T^3 \).
- For massless Bose-Einstein statistics at zero magnetic field, entropy density \( s = \frac{4g\pi^2}{90} T^3 \).
- For massless Fermi-Dirac statistics at zero magnetic field, entropy density \( s = \frac{7g\pi^2}{180} T^3 \).
- In the strong field limit (\( B \rightarrow \infty \), \( \tau_B \rightarrow 0 \)), the perpendicular shear viscosity component \( \eta_2 \) becomes \( \eta_2^{SF} = \frac{g\beta}{15} \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{\tau_B^2}{\tau_c} \left( \frac{k^2}{\omega} \right)^2 f_0(1-af_0) \).
- In the strong field limit, the Hall shear viscosity component \( \eta_4 \) becomes \( \eta_4^{SF} = \frac{g\beta}{15} \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \tau_B \left( \frac{k^2}{\omega} \right)^2 f_0(1-af_0) \).
- In the strong field limit, the perpendicular electrical conductivity component \( \sigma_0 \) becomes \( \sigma_0^{SF} = \frac{g\beta e^2}{3} \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \frac{\tau_B^2}{\tau_c} \left( \frac{k^2}{\omega} \right)^2 f_0(1-af_0) \).
- In the strong field limit, the Hall electrical conductivity component \( \sigma_1 \) becomes \( \sigma_1^{SF} = \frac{g\beta e^2}{3} \int \frac{d^3k}{(2\pi)^3} \tau_B \left( \frac{k^2}{\omega} \right)^2 f_0(1-af_0) \).

## Context

This work extends earlier calculations of shear viscosity in a magnetic field by Tuchin (2012), Asutosh (2018), and Ghosh et al. (2018) using the tensor set of Landau (1936), and by Denicol (2018) and the BAMPS group using the tensor set of Xu, Greiner, and Huang (2008). The paper provides a unified derivation of both tensor sets within the same kinetic theory framework and clarifies the relationships between the two sets of viscosity coefficients. The results enable phenomenological estimates of transport coefficients for magnetohydrodynamic simulations of quark-gluon plasma in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC, where magnetic fields can reach up to \( m_\pi^2 \) and \( 10 m_\pi^2 \) respectively, as shown by Tuchin (2013).
