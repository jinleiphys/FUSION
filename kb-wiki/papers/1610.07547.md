# Model Selection for Pion Photoproduction

> Partial-wave analysis of meson and photon-induced reactions is needed to enable the comparison of many theoretical approaches to data. In both energy-dependent and independent parametrizations of partial waves, the selection of the model amplitude is crucial. Principles of the $S$-matrix are implemented to different degree in different approaches, but a many times overlooked aspect concerns the selection of undetermined coefficients and functional forms for fitting, leading to a minimal yet sufficient parametrization. We present an analysis of low-energy neutral pion photoproduction using the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) in combination with criteria from information theory and $K$-fold cross validation. These methods are not yet widely known in the analysis of excited hadrons but will become relevant in the era of precision spectroscopy. The principle is first illustrated with synthetic data, then, its feasibility for real data is demonstrated by analyzing the latest available measurements of differential cross sections ($dσ/dΩ$), photon-beam asymmetries ($Σ$), and target asymmetry differential cross sections ($dσ_T/dΩ$) in the low energy regime.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO), combined with information criteria and cross validation, can automatically select a minimal yet sufficient parametrization for partial-wave analysis of pion photoproduction, but the method's success is demonstrated only for low-energy neutral pion photoproduction where high-quality data from a single experimental setup minimize systematic uncertainties.

## Method

The authors parametrize the real and imaginary parts of S, P, and D-wave multipoles for $\gamma p\to\pi^0p$ using a Taylor expansion in momentum-squared around threshold, starting with 46 free parameters. They apply LASSO by minimizing a penalized $\chi^2_T = \chi^2 + \lambda^4 \sum |a_i|$ over a range of penalty values $\lambda$, then use the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), corrected AIC (AICc), Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), and 5-fold cross validation to identify the optimal $\lambda$ that yields the simplest model. The method is first validated on synthetic data generated from a known 9-parameter benchmark model, then applied to real experimental data for differential cross sections, photon-beam asymmetries, and target asymmetry differential cross sections.

## Key numbers

* The benchmark model used 9 parameters (2 for each P-wave multipole and 3 for the S wave) to generate synthetic data; LASSO reduced the full 46-parameter model to 10 parameters, close to the true 9.
* The synthetic data set contained 1373 data points; the 46-parameter unpenalized fit gave $\chi^2=1232$, below the lower 95% confidence limit of Pearson's $\chi^2$ test at $\chi^2=1243$, indicating overfitting.
* The F-test comparing the 46-parameter fit and the optimal 10-parameter fit gave $y=1.64$, below the 90% confidence level interval ending at $y=2.63$, confirming the simpler model is not significantly worse.
* For the benchmark model, all three information criteria (AIC, AICc, BIC) exhibited a minimum at $\lambda=3$, with cross validation showing a broad minimum from $\lambda\approx 2.5$ through $\lambda\approx 3.5$.
* The real data analysis used measurements of $d\sigma/d\Omega$, $\Sigma$, and $d\sigma_T/d\Omega$ from MAMI, with data up to $E_\gamma \simeq 170$ MeV (HBChPT) and $E_\gamma \simeq 185$ MeV (polynomial parametrizations with unitarity).

## Context

This work extends the partial-wave analysis methodology of Fernandez-Ramirez (2009) and Hornidge (2012) for low-energy neutral pion photoproduction, where previous analyses determined that up to D waves are needed but manual parameter selection was required. The LASSO approach provides an automated alternative to manual model selection and mass scan techniques used in approaches like SAID (Workman, 2012) and Bayesian evidence methods (DeCruz, 2011, 2012). The method is presented as a template for future precision spectroscopy analyses at facilities such as CLAS12, GlueX, and MAMI, where the increasing volume of high-precision data will require systematic model selection to avoid false positive resonance signals.
