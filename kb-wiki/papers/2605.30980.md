# High-Dimensional Bayesian Calibration of Expensive Nuclear Models with Differentiable Emulation

> Full Bayesian calibration of expensive nuclear models has been blocked not by the cost of any single solve, but by the absence of exact likelihood gradients in legacy parameter-dependent operators, which forces gradient-free samplers to spend $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ evaluations exploring high-dimensional correlated posteriors. I introduce DREAM, a differentiable calibration strategy in which the parameter-dependent operator is sampled offline by any legacy code, compressed by singular value decomposition, and reconstructed online in a differentiable framework so that automatic differentiation delivers exact likelihood gradients through the full forward solve at the cost of one additional evaluation per Hamiltonian Monte Carlo step. The construction is operator-level and depends only on smooth, compressible parameter dependence; the underlying physics solver is treated as a black box. As a representative demonstration, DREAM is applied to a continuum-discretized coupled-channels (CDCC) analysis of $d$+$^{58}$Ni elastic scattering at $20$~MeV with eighteen optical-potential parameters, for which No-U-Turn Sampling converges on a single GPU in under ten minutes from a cold start with zero divergent transitions, yielding a full Bayesian posterior for a breakup reaction. The mean emulator error is more than an order of magnitude below the inferred model discrepancy, so the posterior is set by the reaction model rather than the surrogate. Treating the Koning-Delaroche systematics as an informative prior, the data update the well-determined parameter combinations, raising the mean deuteron surface absorption about $36\%$ above the Koning-Delaroche value, while the under-determined directions remain at the prior; this is a representative payoff that the multi-energy datasets DREAM is designed to accommodate can sharpen into a full physics interpretation.

# High-Dimensional Bayesian Calibration of Expensive Nuclear Models with Differentiable Emulation

## Key claim

The paper introduces DREAM, a differentiable calibration strategy that enables full Bayesian posterior inference for expensive nuclear models with non-differentiable legacy solvers, by compressing the parameter-dependent operator offline via SVD and reconstructing it online with automatic differentiation to provide exact likelihood gradients for Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampling. The claim is conditional on the operator having smooth, compressible parameter dependence; the underlying physics solver is treated as a black box and the emulator error must remain well below the inferred model discrepancy for the posterior to be governed by the reaction model rather than the surrogate.

## Method

The offline stage samples the parameter-dependent operator (here, the CDCC Hamiltonian matrix elements) on a dense grid using a legacy Fortran solver, compresses the snapshot ensemble via singular value decomposition into a reduced basis (POD modes), and stores the mean matrix, basis matrices, and scalar expansion coefficients. The online stage, executed at each MCMC step, reconstructs the operator by bilinear interpolation of the precomputed coefficients, solves the reduced Galerkin system by direct linear algebra, extracts elastic S-matrix elements and the angular distribution, and evaluates a Gaussian likelihood with a multiplicative model-discrepancy term; reverse-mode automatic differentiation through the entire chain, including implicit differentiation through the linear solve, returns the exact likelihood gradient at the cost of one additional forward evaluation independent of the number of parameters.

## Key numbers

- No-U-Turn Sampling (NUTS) converges on a single GPU in approximately 9 minutes from a cold start, yielding 1.6 x 10^4 post-warmup samples with zero divergent transitions and split-R-hat <= 1.001 across all 19 parameters (18 optical-potential parameters plus the model-discrepancy hyperparameter beta).
- The online emulator solves the reduced scattering equations and computes the angular distribution in 0.34 ms on a single GPU; the full 18-parameter gradient is returned in 0.99 ms.
- The mean emulator error is more than an order of magnitude below the inferred model discrepancy, which converges to beta = 0.071 +/- 0.027 (approximately 7% systematic theory error).
- The mean deuteron surface absorption is raised to (W_d^p + W_d^n)/2 = 11.6 +/- 0.7 MeV (approximately 6% precision), about 36% above the Koning-Delaroche elastic-systematics value of 8.5 MeV.
- The symmetric real-volume depth is determined as V_v_bar = 48.2 +/- 1.2 MeV.
- The SVD compression requires only K_f = 6-11 modes per form factor to achieve reconstruction errors below 10^-4, despite each matrix having approximately 10^4 entries.
- The form-factor geometry grid is 20 x 20 points spanning +/- 20% around nominal (R_f, a_f) values, with cell width approximately 0.026 fm in the surface form factor.

## Context

This work extends reduced-basis emulation techniques for nuclear reactions (Lei 2025) by adding differentiability, enabling gradient-based Bayesian inference that was previously blocked by the absence of exact likelihood gradients in legacy parameter-dependent operators. It contrasts with gradient-free Bayesian studies using emulator acceleration (King 2019, Drischler 2021, Pruitt 2023, Beyer 2026) and with differentiable single-channel surrogates built from physics-informed networks (Lei) or neural wavefunction emulators (BiLNN), which provide differentiable forward maps but do not close the loop to gradient-based Bayesian inference of coupled-channel reaction observables. The framework is designed to accommodate multi-energy and polarization datasets that could break the optical-model continuous ambiguity and sharpen the physics interpretation of the deuteron-target effective interaction.
