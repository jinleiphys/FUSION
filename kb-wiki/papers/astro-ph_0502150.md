# The Concordance Cosmic Star Formation Rate: Implications from and for the Supernova Neutrino and Gamma Ray Backgrounds

> We constrain the Cosmic Star Formation Rate (CSFR) by requiring that massive stars produce the observed UV, optical, and IR light while at the same time not overproduce the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background as bounded by Super-Kamiokande. With the massive star component so constrained we then show that a reasonable choice of stellar Initial Mass Function and other parameters results in SNIa rates and iron yields in good agreement with data. In this way we define a `concordance' CSFR that predicts the optical SNII rate and the SNIa contribution to the MeV Cosmic Gamma-Ray Background. The CSFR constrained to reproduce these and other proxies of intermediate and massive star formation is more clearly delineated than if it were measured by any one technique and has the following testable consequences: (1) SNIa contribute only a small fraction of the MeV Cosmic Gamma-Ray Background, (2) massive star core-collapse is nearly always accompanied by a successful optical SNII, and (3) the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background is tantalizingly close to detectability.

## Key claim

The authors construct a "concordance" Cosmic Star Formation Rate (CSFR) by requiring that massive stars produce the observed UV, optical, and IR light while not overproducing the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) as bounded by Super-Kamiokande, finding that the allowed CSFR normalization is concentrated near the DSNB upper limit rather than being independently determined by any single measurement.

## Method

The authors adopt a broken power-law parametrization for the CSFR (with slope beta ~ 2 below redshift z_p = 1 and flat beyond) and convert it to the SNII rate using a Salpeter initial mass function (IMF) with slope m^{-2.35} above 0.5 solar masses. They then compute the DSNB flux from core-collapse supernovae and use the Super-Kamiokande upper limit on the electron antineutrino flux above 19.3 MeV to constrain the CSFR normalization at z=0. The SNIa rate is derived from the intermediate-mass (3-8 solar masses) CSFR using a 3 Gyr constant time delay and an efficiency parameter f_Ia determined by fitting observed SNIa rate evolution.

## Key numbers

- Super-Kamiokande upper limit on electron antineutrino flux above 19.3 MeV: 1.2 cm^{-2} s^{-1}
- Concordance CSFR normalization at z=0: R_SF(0) approximately 2 x 10^{-2} solar masses yr^{-1} Mpc^{-3} (upper bound) and approximately 1 x 10^{-2} solar masses yr^{-1} Mpc^{-3} (lower bound from astronomical data)
- SNIa efficiency parameter f_Ia = (1/700 - 1/1000) solar masses^{-1} to remain consistent with SNIa rate evolution
- Predicted iron density parameter from SNII: Omega_Fe_SNII approximately 0.6 x 10^{-5} (using 0.06 solar masses Ni per event)
- Predicted iron density parameter from SNIa: Omega_Fe_SNIa approximately 0.4 x 10^{-5} (using 0.5 solar masses Ni per event)
- Observed iron density parameter: Omega_Fe_Obs approximately 2 x 10^{-5}
- Present SNIa rate from concordance model: R_SNIa(z=0) = 3 x 10^{-5} yr^{-1} Mpc^{-3}
- COMPTEL CGB spectrum fit: (1.1 +/- 0.2) x 10^{-4} (E/5 MeV)^{-2.4 +/- 0.2} MeV^{-1} cm^{-2} s^{-1} sr^{-1}

## Context

This work extends previous DSNB predictions by Ando (2003), Fukugita (2003), and Strigari (2003) by using the Super-Kamiokande bound to constrain the CSFR normalization, and contradicts earlier conclusions by Ruiz-Lapuente (2000) and Watanabe (1998) that SNIa could account for the MeV Cosmic Gamma-Ray Background. The concordance model enables future tests: the DSNB is predicted to be near detectability by Super-Kamiokande, and the SNIa contribution to the MeV CGB is shown to be nearly an order of magnitude below the observed flux, requiring alternative sources in the 1-3 MeV range.
