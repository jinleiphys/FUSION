# Navier-Stokes Equation by Stochastic Variational Method

> We show for the first time that the stochastic variational method can naturally derive the Navier-Stokes equation starting from the action of ideal fluid. In the frame work of the stochastic variational method, the dynamical variables are extended to stochastic quantities. Then the effect of dissipation is realized as the direct consequence of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. The present result reveals the potential availability of this approach to describe more general dissipative processes.

## Key claim

The authors show for the first time that the stochastic variational method (SVM) can derive the full compressible Navier-Stokes equation from the action of an ideal fluid, but only under the condition that the entropy variation is modeled as a first-order expansion in the ratio of microscopic to hydrodynamic timescales, and that a higher-order term in the fluctuation magnitude and spatial derivatives is discarded as negligible.

## Method

The authors start from the classical Lagrangian for an ideal fluid expressed in Lagrangian coordinates, then extend the dynamical variables to stochastic quantities by introducing a Wiener noise term into the time evolution of fluid element positions. They define forward and backward stochastic differential equations and their associated mean derivatives, then apply a variational principle to the stochastic action. The entropy variation is treated as a functional of the density and its time derivative, expanded to first order in the microscopic relaxation time, which introduces the bulk viscosity term. The resulting equation is transformed from the diffusion velocity to the mass velocity, and a term of third order in spatial derivatives is discarded to recover the standard compressible Navier-Stokes equation.

## Key numbers

- Shear viscosity is identified as \(\eta = \nu \rho\), where \(\nu\) is the noise coefficient from the Wiener process and \(\rho\) is the mass density.
- Bulk viscosity is identified as \(\zeta = \mu + 2\eta/3\), where \(\mu = -\rho^3 g(\rho)/m (\partial T/\partial \rho)_{\hat{s}}\) arises from the entropy variation model.
- The Einstein relation for shear viscosity is given as \(\eta = \frac{\rho}{3} \int_0^\infty dt \, E[\delta \hat{\mathbf{v}}(t) \cdot \delta \hat{\mathbf{v}}(0)]\), where \(\delta \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \sqrt{2\nu} d\mathbf{W}/dt\).
- The discarded higher-order term is of second order in the fluctuation magnitude \(\nu = \eta/\rho\) and third order in spatial derivatives.

## Context

This work extends prior applications of the stochastic variational method, which had previously only derived the Navier-Stokes equation for incompressible fluids (Yasue 1979, Morato 1982), to the full compressible case. The approach uses the same action as that employed for relativistic hydrodynamics (RelHydro) but differs from earlier classical variational formulations of hydrodynamics (Lanczos, Yasue 1979). The result enables systematic derivation of dissipative equations from known reversible Lagrangians and suggests potential applications to generalized hydrodynamics, diffusion processes with memory effects, and complex fluids, while leaving open the question of SVM's applicability to field-theoretical and relativistic systems.
