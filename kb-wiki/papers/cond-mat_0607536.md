# Structure and Fragmentation in Colloidal Artificial Molecules and Nuclei

> Motivated by recent experiments on colloidal systems with competing attractive and repulsive interactions, we simulate a two-dimensional system of colloids with competing interactions that can undergo fragmentation. In the absence of any other confining potential, the colloids can form stable clusters depending on the strength of the short range attractive term. By suddenly changing the strength of one of the interaction terms we find a rich variety of fragmentation behavior which is affected by the existence of "magic" cluster numbers. Such soft matter systems can be used to construct artificial nuclei.

# Structure and Fragmentation in Colloidal Artificial Molecules and Nuclei

## Key claim
Colloidal clusters with competing short-range attraction and long-range repulsion can form stable, self-confined clusters without any external trapping potential, and their fragmentation behavior upon quenching the attractive interaction mimics nuclear multifragmentation processes. The authors emphasize that the existence of "magic" cluster numbers (such as N=7, 12, and 19) significantly affects the fragmentation patterns, with symmetric magic clusters showing fewer decay modes than asymmetric clusters.

## Method
The authors performed overdamped Langevin dynamics simulations of N colloids interacting via a potential U(r) = 1/r - B exp(-κr), with fixed inverse screening length κ=1 and variable attractive strength B, in two dimensions with open boundary conditions and no confining potential. They identified the critical attractive strength B_c(N) above which clusters are stable, then studied fragmentation by suddenly quenching B to a lower value B* < B_c and recording the resulting fragment size distributions over 20 independent runs per quench depth.

## Key numbers
- Critical attractive strength B_c increases approximately logarithmically with N: fit to B_c = 4.2 + 0.5 ln(N) for clusters up to N=80
- Magic clusters identified at N=7, 12, and 19; N=12 forms a nonhexagonal segment of a triangular lattice, not following the criterion N = 1 + 3p(p-1)
- Second shell appears at N=7, third shell at N≥18, fourth shell at N≥29; all three shells show a preference for occupancy N_shell=6
- Complete disintegration into individual fragments occurs at B* = 4.78 for N=5, B* = 4.76 for N=12, and B* = 4.62 for N=15
- For the magic cluster N=7, only a single decay mode of complete disintegration (1^7) is observed unless B* is extremely close to B_c

## Context
This work extends studies of pattern formation in systems with competing short-range attraction and long-range repulsion, previously investigated in magnetic films, Langmuir monolayers, diblock copolymers (Seul 1995), and nuclear "pasta" phases in neutron star crusts (Ravenhall 1983; Watanabe 2004). The authors draw direct analogies to nuclear fragmentation processes studied in both two-dimensional (Strachan 1997) and three-dimensional (Lenk 1986) geometries, and to artificial atoms in quantum dots (Reed) and confined colloidal systems (Bubeck). The results enable using tunable colloidal systems as experimental models for nuclear multifragmentation, where the interactions between charged fragments during decay can be directly observed at the single-particle level.
