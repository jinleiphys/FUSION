# Long-lasting accretion-powered chemical heating of millisecond pulsars

> We analyze the effect of magnetic field in superconducting neutron-star cores on the chemical heating of millisecond pulsars (MSPs). We argue that the magnetic field destroys proton superconductivity in some volume fraction of the stellar core, thus allowing for unsuppressed non-equilibrium reactions of particle mutual transformations there. The reactions transform the chemical energy, accumulated by a neutron star core during the low-mass X-ray binary stage, into heat. This heating may keep an NS warm at the MSP stage (with the surface temperature $\sim 10^5\,\rm K$) for more than a billion of years after ceasing of accretion, without appealing to the rotochemical heating mechanism.

## Key claim

The paper argues that magnetic fields in superconducting neutron-star cores can destroy proton superconductivity in a volume fraction of the core, allowing unsuppressed non-equilibrium reactions that convert chemical energy into heat; this mechanism may keep millisecond pulsars warm (surface temperature ~10^5 K) for over a billion years after accretion ceases, without requiring the rotochemical heating mechanism.

## Method

The authors model the combined thermal and chemical evolution of a 1.4 solar mass neutron star using the BSk24 equation of state, accounting for compression from both accretion during the low-mass X-ray binary stage and spin-down via magneto-dipole losses. They treat the core magnetic field as a free parameter and assume that non-equilibrium modified Urca reactions are fully unsuppressed inside flux tubes or normal domains (volume fraction ~B/H_crit) and completely forbidden outside, using analytic reduction factors for proton superconductivity from Reisenegger (1997) and Petrovich and Reisenegger (2010). The evolution equations for chemical potential imbalances and temperature are solved for different core magnetic field strengths, with and without the preceding accretion phase.

## Key numbers

- Surface temperature of PSR J0437-4715: T_s^∞ = (1.0-1.5) × 10^5 K (Durant 2012; Bogdanov 2019), with about 15% uncertainty
- Rotation rate of PSR J0437-4715: ν = 173.7 Hz; spin-down rate: ν_dot = -4.14 × 10^{-16} Hz s^{-1}
- Surface temperature of PSR J2124-3358: (0.5-2.1) × 10^5 K assuming R_∞ = 12 km (Rangelov 2017)
- Adopted accretion rate: M_dot = 10^{-10} M_sun/yr; heat released per accreted baryon: q = 0.5 MeV/baryon
- Adopted proton critical temperature: T_cp^∞ = 2 × 10^9 K (lower limit for 1.4 M_sun model)
- Critical magnetic field: H_crit ≈ 2 × 10^15 G (typical value for both type-I and type-II superconductors)
- For core magnetic field B ≥ 10^12 G, the rotochemical mechanism becomes unable to explain the observed temperature of J0437 for the adopted stellar model
- For B ≥ 10^6 G, imbalances do not reach the proton energy gap Δ_p^∞ for the assumed spin-down parameters

## Context

This work extends the rotochemical heating framework of Reisenegger (1995), Petrovich and Reisenegger (2010), and Reisenegger et al. (2015) by incorporating the effect of magnetic fields in superconducting neutron-star cores, which was previously neglected. It also generalizes the approach of Fernandez and Reisenegger (2005) to account for compression from accretion during the low-mass X-ray binary stage. The mechanism can explain the observed surface temperatures of millisecond pulsars like PSR J0437-4715 and PSR J2124-3358 without appealing to vortex creep (Alpar 1984) or crustal rotochemical heating (Gusakov et al. 2015). The results enable future work to constrain core magnetic field strengths from observed pulsar temperatures and to study the interplay between magnetic field structure and chemical heating in neutron stars.
