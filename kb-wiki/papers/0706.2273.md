# Evolution of Anisotropy of Parton System from Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Evolution of anisotropy in momentum and coordinate space of the parton system produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions is discussed within the free-streaming approximation. The momentum distribution evolves from the prolate shape - elongated along the beam - to the oblate one - squeezed along the beam. At the same time the eccentricity in the coordinate space, which occurs at finite values of impact parameter, decreases. It is argued that the parton system reaches local thermodynamic equilibrium before the momentum distribution becomes oblate.

## Key claim

Using a free-streaming model, the authors argue that the parton system produced in relativistic heavy-ion collisions reaches local thermodynamic equilibrium while the local momentum distribution is still prolate (elongated along the beam axis), before it would evolve to an oblate shape. The conclusion depends on the estimated equilibration time being shorter than the time needed for the momentum distribution to become oblate.

## Method

The authors model the parton system produced in the overlap region of colliding nuclei as massless particles obeying the collisionless Boltzmann equation, with initial spatial distributions given by three-dimensional Gaussians and a Gaussian rapidity distribution. They compute the time evolution of the coordinate-space eccentricity and of a momentum-anisotropy parameter ρ = 2⟨p_z²⟩/⟨p_T²⟩ within a Gaussian-weighted box, both with and without a finite formation time for the produced partons.

## Key numbers

- The width of the rapidity distribution of charged pions in Au-Au collisions at √s = 200 GeV is ΔY = 2.3 (Bearden 2004).
- For Au-Au collisions with impact parameter b ≈ 1.4R (R = 7 fm for gold), the eccentricity is reduced to 75% of its initial value at 1.5 fm/c, giving an upper limit on equilibration time of 1.5 fm/c.
- For ΔY = 2.5 (relevant for RHIC), it takes 6-8 fm/c for the momentum distribution to become oblate when formation time is neglected.
- With a finite formation time τ = 0.3 fm/c or τ = 0.8 fm/c, the momentum distribution becomes oblate only after 3-5 fm/c.
- The more elaborated estimate of equilibration time from Heinz (2004) is 0.6 fm/c.

## Context

This work extends earlier derivations of eccentricity decay (Kolb 2003, Kolb 2000) by including a finite rapidity distribution width and a finite formation time for partons. The conclusion that the momentum distribution is prolate at equilibration time contradicts the assumption in many studies of plasma equilibration (reviewed in Mrowczynski 2005) that the system starts with an oblate momentum distribution; the authors argue those studies describe how equilibrium is sustained rather than how it is reached. The result motivates analyzing color instabilities and thermalization in systems with prolate momentum distributions, as in Mrowczynski (1994) and Randrup (2003).
