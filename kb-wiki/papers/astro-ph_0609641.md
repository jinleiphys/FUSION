# Thermal evolution of rotating hybrid stars

> As a neutron star spins down, the nuclear matter continuously is converted into quark matter due to the core density increase and then latent heat is released. We have investigated the thermal evolution of neutron stars undergoing such deconfinement phase transition. We have taken into account the conversion in the frame of the general theory of relativity. The released energy has been estimated as a function of change rate of deconfinement baryon number. Numerical solutions to cooling equation are obtained to be very different from the without heating effect. The results show that neutron stars may be heated to higher temperature which is well-matched with pulsar's data despite onset of fast cooling in neutron stars with quark matter core. It is also found that heating effect has magnetic field strength dependence. This feature could be particularly interesting for high temperature of low-field millisecond pulsar at late stage. The high temperature could fit the observed temperature for PSR J0437-4715.

# Thermal evolution of rotating hybrid stars

## Key claim

The paper claims that deconfinement heating (latent heat released when nuclear matter converts to quark matter during spin-down) can keep hybrid stars hot enough to match observed pulsar temperatures, even when fast direct Urca cooling is active. However, the quantitative agreement is only illustrative: the heat release per nucleon is treated as a free parameter (taken as 0.1 MeV), and the authors state that precise fitting requires future improvements including superfluidity, superconductivity, and a density-dependent heat release.

## Method

The authors construct rotating hybrid star models using Glendenning's two-phase equation of state, combining relativistic mean field theory for hadronic matter with an effective-mass MIT bag model for quark matter (bag constant B^{1/4}=160 MeV, s quark mass m_s=150 MeV, coupling g=3). They apply Hartle's perturbative approach to general relativity to compute the change in internal structure as the star spins down due to magnetic dipole radiation, tracking the conversion of hadronic matter to quark matter via the mixed phase under Gibbs conditions. The deconfinement heating luminosity is calculated as the product of the latent heat per nucleon (taken as 0.1 MeV) and the rate of change of the deconfined baryon number, and the cooling equation including neutrino emission (nucleon and quark direct Urca, modified Urca, and bremsstrahlung processes) and photon emission is solved numerically.

## Key numbers

- Bag constant B^{1/4} = 160 MeV, s quark mass m_s = 150 MeV, coupling constant g = 3 for the quark matter equation of state.
- Nuclear saturation density n_b = 0.16 fm^{-3}, the threshold above which deconfinement is expected.
- For a 1.4 M_sun hybrid star, the deconfined baryon number as a function of spin frequency is fitted as N_q = N_q^0 (1 - 0.716 nu_3^2 + 0.055 nu_3^3 - 0.032 nu_3^4), where N_q^0 ≈ 0.22 N_sun (N_sun is the total baryon number of a 1.4 M_sun star) and nu_3 = nu / 10^3 Hz.
- Latent heat release per nucleon q_n is taken as 0.1 MeV (estimated from the enthalpy difference between hadron and mixed phases).
- Cooling curves are computed for magnetic fields from 10^9 G to 10^13 G, with initial temperature T_0 = 10^9 K and magnetic tilt angle theta = 45 degrees.
- For weak-field stars (B < 10^10 G), the model predicts surface temperatures above 10^5 K at ages up to ~10^10 years.

## Context

This work extends prior studies of heating mechanisms in neutron stars, such as rotational energy dissipation via viscous damping (Zheng and Yu) and crust strain release (Cheng et al.), by introducing deconfinement heating as a new heat source specific to hybrid stars with quark matter cores. It builds on the hybrid star models of Glendenning and the Hartle perturbation theory approach used by Chubarian et al. for rotating hybrid stars. The results enable future work to include more realistic physics such as superfluidity, superconductivity, and density-dependent latent heat, which the authors identify as necessary for precise quantitative fits to observed pulsar temperatures like that of PSR J0437-4715.
