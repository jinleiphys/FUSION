# QCD at high density/temperature

> I give a survey of recent progress in our understanding of QCD at high density and temperature. I pay particular attention to color superconductivity, applications of lattice gauge theory at nonzero density as well as high temperature, perturbative results at high temperature, and non-equilibrium calculations.

## Key claim

This survey paper argues that while QCD at high density and temperature is not yet fully understood from first principles, recent progress in color superconductivity, lattice gauge theory at nonzero density, perturbative resummations at high temperature, and nonequilibrium calculations has yielded qualitative predictions and quantitative estimates for the properties of QCD matter, though controlled calculations remain limited to specific regions of the phase diagram.

## Method

The paper reviews theoretical approaches to QCD thermodynamics across the phase diagram: at high density, mean-field Schwinger-Dyson equations (gap equations) are solved using either one-gluon exchange at asymptotically high densities or Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) models with four-fermion interactions normalized to low-density physics; at high temperature and low density, lattice gauge theory with improved staggered and Wilson actions is used for temperatures up to about 4 times the deconfinement temperature, while resummed perturbation theory (hard thermal loop methods and two-particle irreducible effective actions) is applied to extend perturbative calculations to lower temperatures.

## Key numbers

* The color superconducting gap from NJL model calculations rises to values around 100 MeV at densities of physical interest for neutron stars (chemical potential up to about 500 MeV).
* At asymptotically high densities, perturbative calculations apply for chemical potential greater than about 10^8 MeV (Rajagopal and Shuster).
* At high temperature, perturbative resummations apply for temperature greater than about 3 times the critical temperature (about 600 MeV), while lattice calculations cover temperatures up to about 4 times the critical temperature (about 800 MeV).
* At T ~ 4 T_c, the pressure from lattice calculations is about 15 percent less than the Stefan-Boltzmann pressure of free quarks and gluons.
* The surface tension threshold for a mixed phase between nuclear and CFL quark matter is about 40 MeV/fm^2 (0.2 times (200 MeV)^3).
* For a compact star made entirely of CFL quark matter, a gap as small as 1 MeV is ruled out by observations of millisecond pulsars (Madsen).
* Typical compact star masses are close to 1.4 solar masses, with radii of order 10 km.

## Context

This review extends and summarizes work on QCD thermodynamics, covering color superconductivity (building on BCS theory and earlier work by Barrois, Bailin and Love, and Rapp et al.), lattice gauge theory at nonzero density (where the fermion determinant complicates calculations), and perturbative results at high temperature (where naive perturbation theory fails due to large alternating-sign corrections). The paper enables future work on neutron star phenomenology including cooling curves, glitches, r-mode instabilities, and neutrino emission, and identifies open questions about the phase diagram at intermediate densities where controlled calculations are not yet possible.
