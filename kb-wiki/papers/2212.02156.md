# Progress in Nuclear Astrophysics: a multi-disciplinary field with still many open questions

> Nuclear astrophysics is a multi-disciplinary field with a huge demand for nuclear data. Among its various fields, stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis are clearly the most closely related to nuclear physics. The need for nuclear data for astrophysics applications challenges experimental techniques as well as the robustness and predictive power of present nuclear models. Despite impressive progress for the last years, major problems and puzzles remain. In the present contribution, only a few nuclear astrophysics specific aspects are discussed. These concern some experimental progress related to the measurement of key reactions of relevance for the so-called s-and p-processes of nucleosynthesis, the theoretical effort in predicting nuclear properties of exotic neutron-rich nuclei of interest for the r-process nucleosynthesis, and the recent introduction of machine learning techniques in nuclear astrophysics applications.

## Key claim

Despite impressive progress in nuclear astrophysics over the last years, major problems and puzzles remain because experimental nuclear data covers only a minute fraction of the data required for nucleosynthesis applications, and theoretical predictions for exotic nuclei still suffer from uncontrolled uncertainties that can exceed what is acceptable for astrophysical modeling.

## Method

The authors review recent experimental measurements of key reactions for the s-process (specifically the $^{17}$O($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne rate) and p-process nucleosynthesis, and compare theoretical mass models (Skyrme-HFB, Gogny-HFB, and macroscopic-microscopic approaches) for their predictive power in r-process calculations. They also test a pure machine learning neural network (TensorFlow, approximately 5000 parameters) on the 2500 known nuclear masses to evaluate its extrapolation reliability to unknown nuclei.

## Key numbers

- The updated $^{17}$O($\alpha$,$\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne rate from Williams et al. (2022) is about 10 times smaller than the former estimate of Best et al. (2013)
- A reduction of the $^{22}$Ne($\alpha$,n)$^{25}$Mg rate by a factor of 5 with respect to the previously determined rate reduces the efficiency of the s process
- The BSk, BSkG, and D1M mass models fit experimental masses with root-mean-square (rms) deviations lower than 0.8 MeV; the popular SLy4 force gives an rms deviation of about 5 MeV; UNEDF0 and UNEDF1 give rms deviations of 1.36 and 2.07 MeV on the full set of known data
- Mass differences between HFB-31, D1M, and BSkG2 models for exotic nuclei can reach up to typically ±5 MeV, especially around the N=126 and 184 shell closures; neutron capture rates can consequently deviate by 3 to 5 orders of magnitude
- A pure neural network trained on 2000 known masses achieves an rms deviation of about 0.15 MeV on the training set and about 0.6 MeV on a validation set of 250 known masses; when trained on 1792 known masses with Z<82 and applied to the 508 known masses for Z≥82, the rms deviation reaches 37.8 MeV (with deviations up to 120 MeV for the heaviest nuclei around Z=110)
- Six different mass models (all with rms deviation better than 0.8 MeV on known masses) give abundance distributions that agree with each other for A≥90 but show local differences by a factor of 4 in the vicinity of A=135-160 and A≈200

## Context

This review extends the discussions of nuclear data needs for stellar evolution and nucleosynthesis previously summarized by Arnould (2020) and addresses specific open questions in s-process, p-process, and r-process nucleosynthesis. The paper highlights how rotational mixing in massive stars (Choplin 2018) can enhance s-process production at low metallicity, and how the updated $^{17}$O+$\alpha$ rate (Williams 2022) affects both s-process and p-process yields. The work enables future improvements in mass models by emphasizing the need for beyond-mean-field corrections and proper treatment of odd-A and odd-odd nuclei, while cautioning that machine learning techniques (as tested by Perez 2022) show uncontrolled extrapolation when applied to nuclei outside the training region.
