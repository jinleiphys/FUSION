# Empirical pairing gaps, shell effects, and di-neutron spatial correlation in neutron-rich nuclei

> The empirical pairing gaps derived from four different odd-even mass staggering formulas are compared. By performing single-$j$ shell and multi-shell seniority model calculations as well as by using the standard HFB approach with Skyrme force we show that the simplest three-point formula $Δ_C^ {(3)}(N)=\frac{1}{2}\left[B(N,Z)+B(N-2,Z)-2B(N-1,Z)\right]$ can provide a good measure of the neutron pairing gap in even-$N$ nuclei. It removes to a large extent the contribution from the nuclear mean field as well as contributions from shell structure details. It is also less contaminated by the Wigner effect for nuclei around $N=Z$. We also show that the strength of $Δ^ {(3)}_C(N)$ can serve as a good indication of the two-particle spatial correlation in the nucleus of concern and that the weakening of $Δ^ {(3)}_C(N)$ in some neutron-rich nuclei indicates that the di-neutron correlation itself is weak in these nuclei.

## Key claim

The simplest three-point formula Δ_C^(3)(N) = 1/2 [B(N,Z) + B(N-2,Z) - 2B(N-1,Z)] provides a good measure of the neutron pairing gap in even-N nuclei by largely removing contributions from the nuclear mean field and shell structure details, but the authors qualify that this formula is most reliable for open-shell nuclei and that its weakening in some neutron-rich nuclei indicates the di-neutron spatial correlation itself is weak in those systems.

## Method

The authors compared four different odd-even mass staggering formulas (three-point, four-point, five-point, and an averaged three-point formula) using experimental binding energies from nuclear data tables. They performed single-j shell seniority model calculations and multi-shell calculations, as well as Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations with the Skyrme SLy4, SIII, and SV-bas functionals using the HFBRAD and HFBTHO codes, to evaluate how well each formula extracts the pairing gap from binding energy differences.

## Key numbers

- Mean value of Δ_C^(3)(N) across all even-even nuclei: 1.08 ± 0.40 MeV
- Mean values of other formulas: Δ^(3)(N) = 1.46 MeV, Δ^(4)(N) = 1.26 MeV, Δ^(5)(N) = 1.26 MeV
- Mean proton pairing gap from Δ_C^(3)(Z): 1.01 ± 0.36 MeV
- Only nine cases have Δ_C^(3)(N) larger than 2 MeV, including 8Be (4.11 MeV), 10Be (2.57 MeV), 10C (3.53 MeV), 12C (2.8 MeV), 14O (3.15 MeV), 20Ne (2.6 MeV), 22Mg (2.3 MeV), 26Si (2.02 MeV), and 44Ti (2.0 MeV)
- Fitted power-law for Δ_C^(3)(N) in even-even nuclei: (3.66 ± 0.33) N^(-0.30 ± 0.02) with σ = 0.29
- Average residual contribution from liquid-drop model to Δ_C^(3): approximately -60 keV for neutrons and -200 keV for protons
- HFB pairing strength fitted to give mean neutron gap of 1.31 MeV in 120Sn

## Context

This work extends the analysis of odd-even staggering formulas previously studied by Satula and co-workers (2000) and by Bender (2000), who showed that the three-point formula for odd systems minimizes mean-field contributions. The authors argue that Δ_C^(3)(N) is preferable to the more commonly used Δ^(3)(N) because it removes the Wigner effect contamination near N=Z and the particle blocking effects that appear in single-j shell systems. The work enables more reliable extraction of pairing information from binding energies of neutron-rich nuclei near the dripline, where experimental data are scarce and shell evolution is poorly understood, and connects the empirical pairing gap to di-neutron spatial correlations as studied in recent work by Pillet (2007) and Matsuo (2005).
