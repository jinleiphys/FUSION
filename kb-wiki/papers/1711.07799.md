# Single particle in a reflection-asymmetric potential

> Single particles moving in a reflection-asymmetric potential are investigated by solving the Schrödinger equation of the reflection-asymmetric Nilsson Hamiltonian with the imaginary time method in 3D lattice space and the harmonic oscillator basis expansion method. In the 3D lattice calculation, the $\bm{l}^2$ divergence problem is avoided by introducing a damping function, and the$\langle \bm{l^2}\rangle_N$ term in the non-spherical case is calculated by introducing an equivalent $N$-independent operator. The efficiency of these numerical techniques is demonstrated by solving the spherical Nilsson Hamiltonian in 3D lattice space. The evolution of the single-particle levels in a reflection-asymmetric potential is obtained and discussed by the above two numerical methods, and their consistency is shown in the obtained single-particle energies with the differences smaller than 10$^{-4}~[\hbarω_0]$.

# Single particles in a reflection-asymmetric potential

## Key claim

The single-particle energies in a reflection-asymmetric Nilsson potential calculated by the imaginary time method in 3D lattice space agree with those from the harmonic oscillator basis expansion method to within differences smaller than 10⁻⁴ [ℏω₀], but only after introducing a damping function to suppress unphysical high-angular-momentum states and an equivalent operator to handle the ⟨l²⟩_N term in coordinate space.

## Method

The authors solved the Schrödinger equation for the reflection-asymmetric Nilsson Hamiltonian using two independent numerical methods: the imaginary time method (ITM) in 3D lattice space with fast Fourier transformation for spatial derivatives, and diagonalization in a spherical harmonic oscillator basis with N_shell = 20 major shells. In the 3D lattice calculation, a Fermi-type damping function f_D(r) was introduced to suppress the l² divergence problem from high-angular-momentum states, and the ⟨l²⟩_N term was replaced by the operator (ĥ₀² − 9/2) to enable its evaluation in coordinate space without explicit knowledge of the principal quantum number N.

## Key numbers

- Single-particle energy differences between the two numerical methods are smaller than 10⁻⁴ [ℏω₀] for all levels shown in the reflection-asymmetric case.
- For the spherical Nilsson Hamiltonian test, absolute deviations between 3D lattice calculation and exact solutions are smaller than 10⁻⁵ [ℏω₀] for damping parameters a_D = 0.1 and 0.2.
- First-order corrections from the damping function are smaller than 10⁻⁵ [ℏω₀] for a_D = 0.1 and smaller than 10⁻⁷ [ℏω₀] for a_D = 0.2.
- The 3D lattice calculation used grid spacing d = 0.6 [√(ℏ/mω₀)] and grid number n = 30 along each axis.
- For the H.O. basis calculation with N_shell = 20, increasing to N_shell = 24 changed single-particle energies below 7 [ℏω₀] by less than 10⁻⁵ [ℏω₀] at (β₂, β₃) = (0.3, 0.1).
- Potential cutoffs V_cut1 = 40 [ℏω₀] and V_cut2 = 16 [ℏω₀] were used for convergence of the iteration.

## Context

This work extends the reflection-asymmetric Nilsson model originally formulated by Dutt and Mukherjee (1959) and later used in numerous studies of octupole-deformed nuclei, including density functional theory calculations by Geng (2007), Wang (2009), and others. The paper demonstrates that the imaginary time method, previously applied to spherical and quadrupole-deformed systems by Davies (1980) and Hagino (2010), can be adapted to reflection-asymmetric potentials with the new numerical treatments for the l² divergence and ⟨l²⟩_N term. This enables future calculations of single-particle states in highly deformed or fissioning nuclei where large spatial grids are more efficient than large harmonic oscillator basis expansions.
