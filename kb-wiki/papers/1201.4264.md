# Heavy Ions: Results from the Large Hadron Collider

> On November 8, 2010 the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN collided first stable beams of heavy ions (Pb on Pb) at center-of-mass energy of 2.76 TeV/nucleon. The LHC worked exceedingly well during its one month of operation with heavy ions, delivering about 10 microbarn-inverse of data, with peak luminosity reaching to $L_{0} = 2 \times 10^{25}{\rm cm}^{-2}{\rm s}^{-1}$ towards the end of the run. Three experiments, ALICE, ATLAS and CMS, recorded their first heavy ion data, which were analyzed in a record time. The results of the multiplicity, flow, fluctuations, and Bose-Einstein correlations indicate that the fireball formed in nuclear collisions at the LHC is hotter, lives longer, and expands to a larger size at freeze-out as compared to lower energies. We give an overview of these as well as new results on quarkonia and heavy flavour suppression, and jet energy loss.

## Key claim

The fireball formed in Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC at 2.76 TeV/nucleon is hotter, lives longer, and expands to a larger size at freeze-out compared to lower energies, based on measurements of multiplicity, flow, fluctuations, and Bose-Einstein correlations. The abstract's claim is qualified by the fact that these conclusions are drawn from the first one-month heavy-ion run with about 10 microbarn-inverse of integrated luminosity, and many results carry significant statistical and systematic uncertainties.

## Method

The paper reviews results from the ALICE, ATLAS, and CMS experiments at the LHC for Pb-Pb collisions at a center-of-mass energy of 2.76 TeV per nucleon pair. Global observables such as charged particle multiplicity density, anisotropic flow coefficients, and Bose-Einstein correlation radii were measured as functions of collision centrality, which was determined using detector-specific estimators like VZERO amplitude in ALICE, Forward Calorimeter transverse energy in ATLAS, and HF calorimeter energy in CMS. Hard probes including quarkonia suppression, heavy-flavor meson nuclear modification factors, electroweak boson production, and jet energy loss were analyzed via invariant mass distributions, nuclear modification factors (R_AA), and dijet asymmetry measurements.

## Key numbers

- Charged particle multiplicity density dN_ch/dη at central rapidity for central Pb-Pb collisions (0-5% centrality) at sqrt(s_NN) = 2.76 TeV is approximately 1600.
- Centrality resolution ranges from 0.5% in central collisions to 2% for peripheral collisions in ALICE.
- The homogeneity volume from Bose-Einstein correlations is larger by a factor of two at LHC compared to RHIC.
- Decoupling time for mid-rapidity pions exceeds 10 fm/c, which is 40% larger than at RHIC.
- Integrated elliptic flow v_2 in the 20-30% centrality class shows an increase of approximately 30% at LHC compared to RHIC top energy.
- R_AA for J/psi is about 0.5 and independent of centrality, indicating a factor of 2 less suppression at LHC compared to RHIC PHENIX results.
- Nuclear modification factor R_AA for prompt D0 and D+ mesons in central (0-20%) Pb-Pb collisions reaches a factor of 4 to 5 suppression for pT greater than 5 GeV/c.
- Charged particle R_AA at LHC shows stronger suppression compared to RHIC data, measured up to pT of 100 GeV/c by CMS.
- Peak luminosity for the first Pb-Pb run reached L_0 = 2 x 10^25 cm^-2 s^-1, with a final design goal of L_0 = 10^27 cm^-2 s^-1 at sqrt(s_NN) = 5.5 TeV.

## Context

This review summarizes the first heavy-ion results from the LHC, extending measurements from lower-energy facilities such as RHIC (which operated at top energies of 200 GeV per nucleon pair) and the SPS at CERN. The results build on the discovery at RHIC of a strongly interacting near-perfect liquid and the earlier evidence for a new state of matter announced by CERN in 2000 from SPS experiments. The LHC results enable quantitative comparisons with hydrodynamic model calculations, provide new constraints on parton energy loss mechanisms, and motivate future runs including p-Pb collisions planned for 2012 to disentangle cold nuclear matter effects from hot medium effects.
