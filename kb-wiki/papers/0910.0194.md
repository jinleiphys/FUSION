# Differences between stellar and laboratory reaction cross sections

> Nuclear reactions proceed differently in stellar plasmas than in the laboratory due to the thermal effects in the plasma. On one hand, a target nucleus is bombarded by projectiles distributed in energy with a distribution defined by the plasma temperature. The most relevant energies are low by nuclear physics standards and thus require an improved description of low-energy properties, such as optical potentials, required for the calculation of reaction cross sections. Recent studies of low-energy cross sections suggest the necessity of a modification of the proton optical potential. On the other hand, target nuclei are in thermal equilibrium with the plasma and this modifies their reaction cross sections. It is generally expected that this modification is larger for endothermic reactions. We show that there are many exceptions to this rule.

## Key claim

The paper shows that the common expectation that stellar enhancement factors are always larger for endothermic reactions than for exothermic ones is incorrect; there are many exceptions where the endothermic stellar enhancement factor is actually smaller, due to Coulomb suppression of transitions from excited states.

## Method

The author performed Hauser-Feshbach calculations of stellar enhancement factors for thousands of reactions, comparing the endothermic and exothermic directions. For proton-induced reactions at low energies relevant to the p-process, the author tested several optical potentials against experimental (p,γ) and (p,n) data, and systematically varied the imaginary part of the JLM microscopic optical potential to improve agreement with measured cross sections.

## Key numbers

- More than 1200 reactions were found where the stellar enhancement factor of the endothermic direction is lower than that of the exothermic direction, contradicting the general rule.
- An increase by 70% in the strength of the imaginary part of the JLM optical potential considerably improved the reproduction of low-energy proton-induced data in Hauser-Feshbach calculations.
- Proton capture rates at 2-4 GK are important in the γ-process for producing p-nuclei.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework of stellar reaction rates developed by Fowler (1974) and Holmes (1976), who established that stellar rates obey reciprocity while laboratory-based rates do not. The paper contradicts the general expectation that stellar enhancement is always larger for endothermic reactions, a rule previously stated by Holmes (1976), Kiss (2008), and Rauscher (2009). The findings enable improved modeling of the p-process, r-process, rp-process, and νp-process by identifying which reactions require careful treatment of stellar enhancement and by providing a modified optical potential for low-energy proton-induced reactions.
