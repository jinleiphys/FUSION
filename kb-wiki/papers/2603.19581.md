# Global $Λ$ hyperon polarization in low-energy heavy ion collisions -- a scenario without vorticity

> Since its discovery, global polarization of the $Λ$ hyperon in heavy-ion collisions has been firmly established and is widely attributed to the large vorticity generated in the rotating quark-gluon plasma. In contrast, nearly fifty years after the first observation of unexpectedly large transverse $Λ$ polarization in unpolarized hadron collisions, its underlying mechanism remains an open and long-standing puzzle, despite being observed across a broad range of collision systems. Although these two phenomena exhibit notable similarities, they are generally regarded as arising from distinct physical origins. In this work, we propose a direct connection between $Λ$ global polarization in heavy-ion collisions and the long-standing transverse polarization observed in unpolarized collision systems. We demonstrate that the alignment between the $Λ$ production plane and the reaction plane, driven by directed flow, can transfer transverse polarization into the measured global polarization signal. Realistic Monte Carlo simulations of Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 3$ GeV indicate that this mechanism can generate a sizable global polarization, accounting for approximately $23\%\pm6\%$ of the magnitude reported by the STAR Collaboration. Our results establish, for the first time, a quantitative link between these two well-known phenomena and have important implications for the interpretation of $Λ$ global polarization measurements in low-energy heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim

The authors propose that the global polarization of Λ hyperons in low-energy heavy-ion collisions can arise not from quark-gluon plasma vorticity, but from the coupling of the long-standing transverse Λ polarization (observed in unpolarized hadron collisions) to directed flow, which aligns the Λ production plane with the reaction plane. The mechanism accounts for approximately 23% ± 6% of the STAR Collaboration measurement at √sNN = 3 GeV, but the authors explicitly note that current analysis techniques cannot disentangle this contribution from vorticity-driven polarization.

## Method

The authors used the JAM2 Monte Carlo event generator to simulate Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 3 GeV, which has no built-in vorticity-induced global polarization. They implemented transverse Λ polarization as a function of Feynman-x (xF) using a quadratic fit to global data from previous experiments, applied via a kinematic-dependent reweighting technique to each Λ hyperon based on its production plane. They then re-analyzed the modified simulations using the same event-plane method and kinematic selections as the STAR experiment, extracting the global polarization signal both as a direct average and via a sinusoidal fit procedure.

## Key numbers

- The mechanism generates a global polarization accounting for approximately 23% ± 6% of the magnitude reported by the STAR Collaboration for Au+Au collisions at √sNN = 3 GeV
- The xF-integrated transverse polarization magnitude extracted from the simulation is −12.5% ± 0.2%
- The weak decay constant αΛ = 0.747 ± 0.009 is used for the Λ → p + π− decay
- The mechanism cannot be applied to heavy-ion collisions above 14.5 GeV due to the sign reversal and reduced magnitude of directed flow v1
- The global polarization extracted by direct average accounts for about 49% ± 10% of the STAR measurement, while the fit method yields 23% ± 6%

## Context

This work establishes a quantitative link between two phenomena previously considered unrelated: Λ global polarization in heavy-ion collisions (discovered at RHIC and attributed to QGP vorticity following Liang and Wang (2005)) and the transverse Λ polarization puzzle first observed in 1976 in proton-beryllium collisions. The mechanism extends the observation by Liang and Wang (2005) that transverse polarization may enhance global polarization in regions of strong v1 flow, and provides a non-vorticity scenario that must be accounted for when interpreting low-energy measurements. The results imply that current measurements of Λ global polarization at low energies may contain a non-negligible contribution from transverse polarization, complicating comparisons with theoretical calculations such as those by Karpenko and Becattini (2019) or Ivanov (2021).
