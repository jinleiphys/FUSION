# On Tamm's problem in the Vavilov-Cherenkov radiation theory

> We analyse the well-known Tamm problem treating the charge motion on a finite space interval with the velocity exceeding light velocity in medium. By comparing Tamm's formulae with the exact ones we prove that former do not properly describe Cherenkov radiation terms. We also investigate Tamm's formula cos(theta)=1/(beta n) defining the position of maximum of the field strengths Fourier components for the infinite uniform motion of a charge. Numerical analysis of the Fourier components of field strengths shows that they have a pronounced maximum at cos(theta)=1/(beta n) only for the charge motion on the infinitely small interval. As the latter grows, many maxima appear. For the charge motion on an infinite interval there is infinite number of maxima of the same amplitude. The quantum analysis of Tamm's formula leads to the same results.

# On Tamm's problem in the Vavilov-Cherenkov radiation theory

## Key claim

Tamm's approximate formulae for a charge moving on a finite space interval with superluminal velocity do not properly describe Cherenkov radiation terms; they describe only bremsstrahlung, and the widely used Tamm formula cos(theta)=1/(beta n) for the position of the maximum of field-strength Fourier components is valid only for charge motion on an infinitely small interval, breaking down for larger intervals where multiple maxima appear.

## Method

The authors compare Tamm's approximate analytical expressions for the vector potential and field strengths (derived by expanding the distance R to first order in z'/R0) with the exact solution for a point charge moving uniformly on a finite interval (-z0, z0) in a non-dispersive medium, obtained in Ref. [14] (1999). They analyze the space-time evolution of the electromagnetic field on a sphere of radius R0, examining the Fourier components of the vector potential numerically for various interval lengths, and also perform a quantum analysis of Tamm's formula.

## Context

This work reexamines the classic Tamm problem (Tamm, 1939) of superluminal charge motion on a finite interval, which had been used to derive the formula cos(theta)=1/(beta n) for identifying Cherenkov radiation direction. The authors show that Tamm's approximate solution omits second-order terms in the expansion of R, causing the Cherenkov radiation terms to drop out entirely, contrary to the exact solution of Afanasiev et al. (1999) which contains Cherenkov radiation for any motion interval. This resolves paradoxes raised by Zrelov and Ruzicka (numerical studies) and Lawson (qualitative analysis) about the interpretation of Tamm's formulae as interference of two bremsstrahlung waves, and clarifies that the Tamm-Frank formula (1.1) for the Cherenkov cone angle remains valid in the space-time representation even for dispersive media.
