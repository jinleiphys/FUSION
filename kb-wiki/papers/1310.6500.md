# Microscopic approach to coupled-channels effects on fusion

> Background: Near-barrier fusion can be strongly affected by the coupling between relative motion and internal degrees of freedom of the collision partners. The time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory and the coupled-channels (CC) method are standard approaches to investigate this aspect of fusion dynamics. However, both approaches present limitations, such as a lack of tunnelling of the many-body wave function in the former and a need for external parameters to describe the nucleus-nucleus potential and the couplings in the latter. Method: A method combining both approaches is proposed to overcome these limitations. CC calculations are performed using two types of inputs from Hartree-Fock (HF) theory: the nucleus-nucleus potential calculated with the frozen HF method, and the properties of low-lying vibrational states and giant resonances computed from the TDHF linear response. Results: The effect of the couplings to vibrational modes is studied in the $^{40}$Ca$+^{40}$Ca and $^{56}$Ni$+^{56}$Ni systems. This work demonstrates that the main effect of these couplings is a lowering of the barrier, in good agreement with the fusion thresholds predicted by TDHF calculations. Conclusions: As the only phenomenological inputs are the choice of the internal states of the nuclei and the parameters of the energy density functional used in the HF and TDHF calculations, the method presented in this work has a broad range of possible applications, including studies of alternative couplings or reactions involving exotic nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method that combines time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) theory with coupled-channels (CC) calculations to predict fusion cross sections using only the Skyrme effective interaction as input, but the method is demonstrated only for light symmetric doubly magic systems where transfer and rotational couplings are minimized, and the main demonstrated effect is a lowering of the barrier centroid rather than a full reproduction of barrier distributions.

## Method

The authors compute the bare nucleus-nucleus potential using the frozen Hartree-Fock (HF) method, then extract the energies and deformation parameters of low-lying vibrational states and giant resonances from TDHF linear response calculations (equivalent to random-phase approximation). These microscopic inputs are used in standard coupled-channels calculations with the CCFULL code to determine fusion cross sections for the 40Ca+40Ca and 56Ni+56Ni systems, with the TDHF fusion threshold serving as a benchmark for the centroid of the resulting barrier distribution.

## Key numbers

- For 40Ca+40Ca, the TDHF fusion threshold (energy above which central collisions lead to fusion) is compared with the centroid of the coupled-channels barrier distribution; the coupling to vibrational modes lowers the barrier, and the agreement between the TDHF threshold and the coupled-channels centroid validates the method.
- For 56Ni+56Ni, the same comparison is performed to study the role of magic numbers 20 and 28 on vibrational couplings; the paper demonstrates that the main effect of couplings is a lowering of the barrier in good agreement with TDHF predictions.
- The TDHF calculations use a time step of 1.5 x 10^-24 s and a grid size of (28 x 0.8 fm)^3 for vibrational studies, and a time step of 5 x 10^-25 s with grid spacing 0.6 fm for heavy-ion collision calculations.
- The initial distance between centers of mass in TDHF collision calculations is 45.6 fm, and the spurious excitation energy due to violation of Galilean invariance is less than 0.1 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the coupled-channels approach by replacing phenomenological inputs (nucleus-nucleus potential parameters and experimental deformation parameters) with microscopic inputs from Skyrme HF and TDHF calculations, addressing limitations noted in prior work by Hagino (1997) and Dasgupta (1998) regarding the need for external parameters. The method enables future studies of alternative couplings or reactions involving exotic nuclei where experimental structure data are unavailable, and provides a bridge between the macroscopic coupled-channels framework and microscopic TDHF theory, which previously lacked tunneling of the many-body wave function as noted by Negele (1982).
