# NUCLEAR THEORY WHITE PAPER 1995

> We review the accomplishments of nuclear theory during the past five years and identify the future challenges and research opportunities. [Final, substantially revised version of the nuclear theory white paper]

## Key claim

The paper reviews accomplishments in nuclear theory from 1990-1995 and identifies future challenges, but the abstract's claim of covering "all major areas" is qualified by the fact that the document is a town meeting report based on thirteen invited talks and special reports, not a comprehensive survey of every subfield.

## Method

The conveners organized a town meeting at Argonne National Laboratory (January 29-30, 1995) with close to 100 participants representing US nuclear theory research. Thirteen invited speakers reviewed progress in different areas, supplemented by special reports from other town meetings, funding agency representatives, and the Institute of Nuclear Theory. The document synthesizes these presentations into a white paper identifying advances and future research opportunities.

## Key numbers

- Close to 100 participants attended the town meeting, more than twice the attendance of the 1989 town meeting
- The nucleus $^{178}$Hf was measured to have a quadrupole moment of 7.2 eb, comparing well with the theoretical HFB value of 7.3 eb
- Mean field theory predicted binding energies for a nucleus with charge Z=110 to an accuracy in the range 0.1-0.3 MeV
- Lattice QCD calculations confirm a phenomenological average instanton size of 1/3 fm and density of about 1 fm$^{-4}$
- Quenched lattice QCD calculations of hadron masses agree with experiment at the 5% level

## Context

This white paper extends the 1989 Long Range Plan for nuclear theory and summarizes progress since that earlier assessment. It covers developments in ab initio structure calculations using Faddeev and Green's function Monte Carlo methods, advances in the Interacting Shell Model, and progress in understanding the quark structure of matter through lattice QCD and chiral perturbation theory. The report identifies opportunities enabled by new facilities including CEBAF and RHIC, and emphasizes the need for theoretical efforts to interpret data from radioactive beam facilities and to understand nuclear astrophysics processes such as the r-process in supernova envelopes.
