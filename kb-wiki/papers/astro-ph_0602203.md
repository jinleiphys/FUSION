# Hans Albrecht Bethe

## Key claim

This biographical entry presents Hans Bethe as a towering figure across all fields of physics, but the abstract's claim of his "reach and importance in all fields of physics" is qualified by the text's focus on his specific contributions to nuclear physics, astrophysics, and hydrodynamics, with particular emphasis on his role in explaining stellar energy production and supernova mechanisms.

## Method

The biography synthesizes Bethe's published works and known career milestones, including his 1938 paper on the CNO cycle, his collaboration with Critchfield on the proton-proton chain, the "Bethe Bible" review articles with Bacher and Livingston (1936-1937), his wartime work at Los Alamos, and his later collaborations with Brown, Wilson, and others on core-collapse supernovae and neutrino physics. The entry draws on published references including Marshak and Blaker (1966), Bernstein (1981), and Bethe's own selected works (1996).

## Key numbers

- Bethe lived from July 2, 1906 to March 6, 2005 (lifespan 98 years)
- He received his Ph.D. at age 22 in 1928 from the University of Munich
- The "Bethe Bible" review articles were published in 1936 and 1937 in Reviews of Modern Physics
- He published the CNO cycle paper in 1938
- Bethe received the Nobel Prize in 1967, the Max Planck Medal in 1955, and the Enrico Fermi Award in 1961
- He was elected to the National Academy of Sciences in 1944 and became a foreign member of the Royal Society in 1957
- Reines and Cowan observed free neutrinos in 1956, with Reines winning the Nobel Prize in 1995
- The Sudbury Neutrino Observatory confirmed the neutrino oscillation interpretation in 2001

## Context

This biography extends the known narrative of Bethe's career from his early work on the CNO cycle (independently also studied by Weizsacker) and the proton-proton chain with Critchfield, through his foundational nuclear physics reviews, to his later work on core-collapse supernovae with Brown and Wilson. The entry enables understanding of Bethe's role in developing both the prompt and delayed mechanisms of core-collapse supernovae, and his contribution to reconciling solar neutrino observations with the work of Mikheyev, Smirnov, and Wolfenstein. The exact mechanism of core-collapse supernovae remains an active research question.
