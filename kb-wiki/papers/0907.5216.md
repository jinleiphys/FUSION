# Interpretation of the neutron quantum gravitational states in terms of isospectral potentials

> The recently observed quantum states of neutrons bound in a gravitational field are analyzed in the framework of one-parameter isospectral hamiltonians. Potentials isospectral to the usual Newton potential are explicitly constructed for the first time, then constrained using measured properties of the neutron gravitational states. The corresponding wave functions and the neutron fluxes are also calculated and analyzed in a simple model, including the ground state and the excited state contributions. The constructed isospectral potentials are discussed as candidates for a possible modification of Newton's law at a submillimetre scale. Our results indicate that significant deviations from the Newtonian gravity at submillimetre distances could be compatible with experiment.

# Interpretation of the neutron quantum gravitational states in terms of isospectral potentials

## Key claim

Significant deviations from Newtonian gravity at submillimetre distances could be compatible with existing experimental data on neutron quantum gravitational states, provided the modified potentials preserve the exact same energy spectrum as the Newton potential. The authors explicitly note that their simplified model (which omits absorber interactions) prevents direct comparison with existing data, and they suggest a modified experimental arrangement without an absorber to more directly test their results.

## Method

The authors construct a one-parameter family of quantum-mechanical potentials isospectral to the linear Newtonian potential \(V(z) = m g z\) using supersymmetric quantum mechanics techniques, specifically the Abraham-Moses method. They then constrain the parameter \(p\) by requiring that the semiclassical turning points of the ground and first excited states fall within the experimentally measured error bars for the step positions in the neutron flux. Finally, they calculate the ground-state density integral and two-state density integral (coherent superposition of ground and first excited states) for the allowed isospectral potentials and compare these with the measured neutron count rate from the Nesvizhevsky (2002, 2005) experiments.

## Key numbers

- Characteristic length scale: \(l = \left[ \hbar^2/(2 m^2 g) \right]^{1/3} \approx 5.87 \, \mu m\)
- Ground state energy: \(E_1 \approx 1.41 \, \text{peV} \approx 2.25 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{erg}\)
- First excited state energy: \(E_2 \approx 2.46 \, \text{peV} \approx 3.92 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{erg}\)
- Second excited state energy: \(E_3 \approx 3.32 \, \text{peV} \approx 5.29 \times 10^{-24} \, \text{erg}\)
- Experimentally measured ground state turning point: \(z_1^{exp} = (12.2 \pm 1.8_{syst} \pm 0.7_{stat}) \, \mu m\), total error \(\Delta z_1^{exp} \approx 1.9 \, \mu m\) (range [10.3, 14.1] \(\mu m\))
- Experimentally measured first excited state turning point: \(z_2^{exp} = (21.2 \pm 2.2_{syst} \pm 0.7_{stat}) \, \mu m\), total error \(\Delta z_2^{exp} \approx 2.3 \, \mu m\) (range [19.2, 24.0] \(\mu m\))
- Isospectral potentials compatible with ground state turning point: \(p \ge 1.08\) and \(p \in [-1.13, -1.04]\)
- Airy function zeroes: \(\lambda_1 \approx 2.34, \lambda_2 \approx 4.09, \lambda_3 \approx 5.52\)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the first measurements of neutron quantum gravitational states by Nesvizhevsky (2002, 2005), which have been analyzed in phenomenological models by Voronin (2006), Westphal (2007), Nesvizhevsky (2004), Baessler (2007), and Adhikari (2007). While those models can explain the data with unmodified gravity and detailed descriptions of the neutron guide system, the current experimental uncertainties leave room for modifications of Newton's law at submillimetre distances. The isospectral potential approach is novel in this context, using methods from Abraham and Moses (1980), Crum (1954), and Khare (1989), as well as supersymmetric quantum mechanics textbooks by Bagchi (2001) and Cooper (2001). The results suggest that the potential with parameter \(p \approx -1.13\) best describes the data, featuring a barrier between \(z=0\) and the turning point that makes neutron presence near the mirror a quantum tunneling effect. The authors propose that future experiments with the GRANIT spectrometer (Kreuz 2009) or new ultra-cold neutron sources at TRIUMF (Martin 2009) could provide more direct tests.
