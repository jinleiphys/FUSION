# Thermal Radiation from an Analytic Hydrodynamic Model with Hadronic and QGP Sources in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> In high-energy heavy-ion collisions, a nearly perfect fluid is formed, known as the strongly coupled quark-gluon plasma (QGP). After a short thermalization period, the evolution of this medium can be described by the equations of relativistic hydrodynamics. As the system expands and cools, the QGP undergoes a transition into hadronic matter, marking the onset of quark confinement. Direct photons offer insights into an essential stage of evolution, spanning from the onset of thermalization to the suppression of thermal photon production, which occurs within the hadronic phase. This paper builds upon and extends a previously published solution of relativistic hydrodynamics, incorporating an equation of state that falls within the same class as that predicted by lattice QCD. Based on this solution, a completely analytic model is constructed to describe thermal photon production, accounting for the quark-hadron transition. The model is tested against PHENIX measurements of non-prompt direct photon spectra in Au+Au collisions at $\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200$ GeV. Good agreement is observed between the model predictions and the experimental data, enabling the investigation of the centrality dependence of the initial temperature. These results provide a benchmark for future theoretical and experimental studies of thermal radiation in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a fully analytic two-component (QGP and hadronic) hydrodynamic model can describe the PHENIX non-prompt direct photon spectra in Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV, but only within a 1+1 dimensional framework that lacks transverse dynamics, meaning the extracted initial temperatures are qualitative rather than quantitative.

## Method
The author constructs two separate analytic solutions of 1+1 dimensional relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics, one for the hadronic phase and one for the QGP phase, connected at a transition temperature \(T_{\rm tr}\). The equation of state uses a temperature-dependent \(\kappa(T) = \varepsilon/p\) that satisfies a specific differential equation, allowing the solutions to generalize earlier work with constant \(\kappa\). Thermal photon spectra are computed by integrating a Boltzmann-weighted source function over the four-dimensional spacetime volume of the medium, using the temperature and flow fields from the hydrodynamic solutions, with the total spectrum being the sum of hadronic and QGP contributions.

## Key numbers
- The model is tested against PHENIX measurements of non-prompt direct photon spectra in Au+Au collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{NN}} = 200\) GeV across four centrality classes.
- The transition temperature \(T_{\rm tr}\) is introduced as the average temperature at which the QGP and hadronic equations of state are matched.
- The parameter \(C_{\rm q} = 3\) is assigned for the QGP phase, corresponding to the conformal limit \(\varepsilon = 3p\) at asymptotically high temperatures.
- The parameter \(T_{\rm fo}\) in the hadronic equation of state is interpreted as the kinetic freeze-out temperature of hadrons.
- The solution includes an initial proper time \(\tau_0\) and a transition proper time \(\tau_{\rm tr}\) characterizing the space-time location of the quark-hadron transition.

## Context
This work extends the earlier single-component analytic hydrodynamic model of Kasza (2023) that described PHENIX data but predicted initial temperatures with large uncertainties and overestimates. It also builds upon the exact 1+1 dimensional perfect fluid solution of Csörgő (2018) by generalizing from constant \(\kappa\) to a temperature-dependent \(\kappa(T)\) consistent with lattice QCD predictions from Borsanyi (2010). The model provides a benchmark for future theoretical and experimental studies of thermal radiation, though it cannot address the "direct photon puzzle" regarding elliptic flow, which requires 1+3 dimensional frameworks such as the semi-analytic model of Lökös (2024) that simultaneously describes ALICE photon spectra and elliptic flow.
