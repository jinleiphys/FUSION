# Prompt Beta Spectroscopy as a Diagnostic for Mix in Ignited NIF Capsules

> The National Ignition Facility (NIF) technology is designed to drive deuterium-tritium (DT) internal confinement fusion (ICF) targets to ignition using indirect radiation from laser beam energy captured in a hohlraum. Hydrodynamical instabilities at interfaces in the ICF capsule leading to mix between the DT fue l and the ablator shell material are of fundamental physical interest and can affect the performance characteristics of the capsule. In this Letter we describe new radiochemical diagnostics for mix processes in ICF capsules with plastic or Be (0.9%Cu) ablator shells. Reactions of high-energy tritons with shell material produce high-energy $β$-emitters. We show that mix between the DT fuel and the shell material enhances high-energy prompt beta emission from these reactions by more than an order of magnitude over that expected in the absence of mix.

## Key claim

Mix between DT fuel and the ablator shell material enhances high-energy prompt beta emission from triton-induced reactions by more than an order of magnitude over the no-mix case, but this enhancement depends on the type of mix (atomic, chunk, or no-mix with wrinkled interface) and the specific reaction cross sections, many of which are not precisely known.

## Method

The authors calculated the differential knock-on triton fluence produced by 14-MeV neutrons from DT burn, then integrated this fluence over triton energy and shell geometry to estimate reaction yields for four specific triton-induced reactions: ⁹Be(t,α)⁸Li, ⁹Be(t,p)¹¹Be, ¹⁸O(t,n)²⁰F, and ¹³C(t,α)¹²B. They considered three mix scenarios: no-mix with smooth or wrinkled shell interfaces, chunk mix where micron-scale shell pieces are injected into the fuel, and atomic mix where shell material is atomically dispersed in the fuel region.

## Key numbers

- For the no-mix scenario with a smooth interface, the estimated yield for ⁹Be(t,α)⁸Li is 1.4×10¹² reactions, for ⁹Be(t,p)¹¹Be is 4.0×10⁹, for ¹⁸O(t,n)²⁰F is 1.4×10⁸, and for ¹³C(t,α)¹²B is 5.4×10⁹.
- For atomic mix with a mixing length of 4 μm, the estimated yields increase to 9.3×10¹³ for ⁹Be(t,α)⁸Li, 3.6×10¹¹ for ⁹Be(t,p)¹¹Be, 9.3×10⁹ for ¹⁸O(t,n)²⁰F, and 3.5×10¹¹ for ¹³C(t,α)¹²B.
- The minimum number of atoms needed for detection via debris collection is 10¹⁰ atoms; for gas collection of ²⁰F, the minimum is 10⁸ atoms due to a 3.5×10⁻⁴ survival fraction over 90 seconds of collection.
- The ⁹Be(t,α)⁸Li reaction has a cross section of about 17 mb at 0.5 MeV and about 185 mb at 1.5 MeV; the ⁹Be(t,p)¹¹Be reaction has a threshold at 1.55 MeV and a cross section of about 1 mb at 3 MeV.
- The non-dimensional integral I_mix(w*) for atomic mix ranges from 0.12 at w*=0.50 to 1.06 at w*=0.00, about an order of magnitude larger than the corresponding unmixed integral I(w*,χ).

## Context

This work extends the radiochemical diagnostic approach proposed by Stoyer et al. for plastic-shell mixing using the ¹⁸O(α,n)²¹Ne reaction, by instead focusing on prompt beta spectroscopy from triton-induced reactions. The paper provides a framework for using existing NIF diagnostics (Debris Collector and Rad-Chem Station, plus gas collection systems installed at OMEGA) to probe hydrodynamic instabilities at the fuel-ablator interface, which remain a key issue for ICF as noted by Radha. The results enable experimental discrimination between atomic mix, chunk mix, and no-mix scenarios in ignited NIF capsules with plastic or beryllium (0.9% Cu) ablator shells.
