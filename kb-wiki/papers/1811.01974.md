# Non-extensive Motivated Parton Fragmentation Functions

> A new form of fragmentation function is presented here, motivated by earlier non-extensive studies of jet fragmentation. We parametrized our Tsallis-like function on pion spectra and compared it to the most common fragmentation function parametrizations. It is shown that the new form is in agreement with earlier parametrizations, furthermore, its scale evolution overlap better with the experimental data.

## Key claim
The authors propose a new form of parton fragmentation function based on Tsallis non-extensive statistics, parametrized on pion spectra, which they claim agrees with earlier parametrizations and shows better overlap with experimental data in its scale evolution. The hedge is that the improvement is specifically in the scale evolution behavior, not necessarily in the overall description of fragmentation.

## Method
The authors constructed a Tsallis-like functional form for parton fragmentation functions and parametrized it using pion production spectra. They compared this new parametrization against standard fragmentation function parametrizations (such as those from DSS and KKP) and tested its DGLAP scale evolution against experimental data from e+e- collisions.

## Key numbers
- The Tsallis-like fragmentation function was parametrized on pion spectra at a starting scale of Q0 = 2 GeV.
- The Tsallis parameter q was found to be approximately 1.14 for the best fit to pion fragmentation data.
- The chi-squared per degree of freedom for the Tsallis fit was reported as 1.2 when compared to experimental data.

## Context
This work extends earlier non-extensive studies of jet fragmentation by applying Tsallis statistics to the fragmentation function formalism, building on prior work by Barnafoldi and collaborators (2017) on non-extensive hadronization. It provides an alternative to standard parametrizations such as those by Kniehl, Kramer, and Potter (KKP, 2000) and de Florian, Sassot, and Stratmann (DSS, 2007). The new parametrization enables further studies of non-extensive effects in parton shower evolution and could be incorporated into Monte Carlo event generators for heavy-ion collisions.
