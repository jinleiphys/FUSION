# Role of Quarks in Nuclear Structure

> The strong force that binds atomic nuclei is governed by the rules of Quantum Chromodynamics. Here we consider the suggestion the internal quark structure of a nucleon will adjust self-consistently to the local mean scalar field in a nuclear medium and that this may play a profound role in nuclear structure. We show that one can derive an energy density functional based on this idea, which successfully describes the properties of atomic nuclei across the periodic table in terms of a small number of physically motivated parameters. Because this approach amounts to a new paradigm for nuclear theory, it is vital to find ways to test it experimentally and we review a number of the most promising possibilities.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the internal quark structure of nucleons self-consistently adjusts to the strong Lorentz-scalar mean fields present in a nuclear medium, and that this adjustment provides a new mechanism for nuclear saturation and a new paradigm for nuclear structure, but the author acknowledges that the approach is a model (the quark-meson coupling model), not QCD itself, and that experimental tests are vital to validate the concept.

## Method

The author derives an energy density functional (EDF) from the quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, which starts from the MIT bag-model description of nucleons and incorporates the effect of the scalar mean field on confined quarks via a scalar polarizability parameter. The EDF is then used in Hartree-Fock plus BCS calculations (using the Skyax code) to compute ground-state binding energies, charge radii, and deformation parameters for nuclei across the periodic table, with four parameters (the meson-nucleon couplings \(G_\sigma, G_\omega, G_\rho\) and the sigma meson mass \(m_\sigma\)) constrained by nuclear matter saturation properties and then fitted to a large set of nuclear data.

## Key numbers

- The QMC model reproduces binding energies of a large set of nuclei with an rms error of 0.36%, compared with 0.24% for the Skyrme force SV-min (which uses more than twice as many parameters).
- For charge radii, the QMC model gives an rms error of 0.7%, compared with 0.5% for SV-min.
- For superheavy nuclei with \(Z \in (100,110)\) (not included in the fit), the QMC model yields an rms deviation in total binding energy of 0.1%, compared with 0.3% for SV-min.
- The scalar polarizability \(d\) in the bag-model is approximately \(0.2 \, R\), where \(R\) is the bag radius.
- The \(\eta^\prime\) meson feels an attractive potential at nuclear matter density of depth \(37 \pm 10 \pm 10\) MeV (on carbon) and \(41 \pm 10 \pm 15\) MeV (on niobium), as measured by the CBELSA/TAPS Collaboration.
- The \(\Lambda\) hyperon binding in the \(1s\)-state of lead is within 1 MeV of the experimental value of about 27 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the quark-meson coupling model developed by Guichon (1987) and Saito et al. (1995–1996), which itself built upon the relativistic mean-field approach of Quantum Hadro-dynamics (Serot and Horowitz, 1979–1984) and the dispersion-relation analysis of the NN force by the Paris and Stony Brook groups (Lacombe 1980, Brown 1978). The approach offers a new paradigm for nuclear structure that contrasts with traditional effective-field-theory treatments (Weinberg 1990) and phenomenological Skyrme forces (Vautherin and Brink 1971, Kortelainen 2010), by predicting that nucleon internal structure changes in-medium. It enables predictions for the binding of other hadrons (mesons, hyperons) in nuclei with no new parameters, and suggests experimental tests such as precise measurements of the Coulomb sum rule and the EMC effect.
