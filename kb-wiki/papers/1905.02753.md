# Yields of weakly-bound light nuclei as a probe of the statistical hadronization model

> The statistical hadronization model is a simple and efficient phenomenological framework in which the relative yields for very high energy heavy ion collisions are essentially determined by a single model parameter---the chemical freeze-out temperature. Recent measurements of yields of hadrons and light nuclei covering over 9 orders of magnitudes from the ALICE collaboration at the LHC were described by the model with remarkable accuracy with a chemical freeze-out temperature of 156.5 $\pm$ 1.5 MeV. A key physical question is whether the freeze-out temperature can be understood, literally, as the temperature at which the various species of an equilibrated gas of hadrons (including resonances) and nuclei chemically freeze out as the model assumes, or whether it successfully parametrizes the yield data for a different reason. The yields of weakly-bound light nuclei---the deuteron and the hypertriton---provide insights into this issue. The analysis indicates that a key assumption underlying the model---that hadrons (and nuclei), just prior to chemical freeze-out temperature, are in thermal equilibrium and are sufficiently dilute as to have particle distributions accurately described statistically by a nearly ideal gas of hadrons and nuclei with masses given by their free space values---appears to be inconsistent with the chemical freeze-out temperature output by the model, at least for these weakly-bound nuclei.

## Key claim

The statistical hadronization model's assumption that hadrons and nuclei just prior to chemical freeze-out form a nearly ideal gas in thermal equilibrium with masses given by their free-space values appears internally inconsistent with the model's own extracted chemical freeze-out temperature of 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV, at least for weakly-bound light nuclei such as the deuteron and hypertriton.

## Method

The authors test the internal consistency of the statistical hadronization model by analyzing the yields of weakly-bound light nuclei (deuteron and hypertriton) using only the model's outputs, known hadronic properties, and standard kinetic theory assumptions. They establish upper bounds on the characteristic lifetimes of these nuclei in the putative equilibrated hadronic gas at the chemical freeze-out temperature, using the well-determined lifetimes of the Δ and Σ resonances as inputs, without requiring detailed cross-section information for all possible reactions.

## Key numbers

- The chemical freeze-out temperature extracted from ALICE LHC data for Pb-Pb collisions at √s_NN = 2.76–5.02 TeV is 156.5 ± 1.5 MeV, with baryon chemical potential μ_b = 0.7 ± 3.8 MeV and volume V_cf = 5,280 ± 410 fm³
- The upper bound on the characteristic lifetime of the deuteron in the equilibrated gas at T = 156.5 MeV is τ_D < 1.2 fm
- The upper bound on the characteristic lifetime of the hypertriton in the equilibrated gas at T = 156.5 MeV is τ_{³_ΛH} < 1.0 fm
- The deuteron binding energy is approximately 2.2 MeV; the hypertriton separation energy into a Λ and a deuteron is taken as 0.41 MeV from the recent STAR Collaboration measurement (Adam 2019)
- A separate fit to light nuclei only yields T_cf = 159 ± 5 MeV, consistent with the overall fit

## Context

This work extends the ongoing discussion about the interpretation of the statistical hadronization model's parameters, which has been questioned previously regarding time scales for hadronic species formation and thermal equilibration (Heinz 2006, Castorina 2007, Schukraft 2013, Floris 2014). The analysis enables future investigation into why the model works phenomenologically despite apparent internal inconsistencies, connecting to recent studies of partial chemical equilibrium (Xu 2018, Oliinychenko 2018, Vovchenko 2019).
