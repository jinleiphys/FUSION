# Invariant Correlation Entropy and Complexity of Quantum States

> We define correlational (von Neumann) entropy for an individual quantum state of a system whose time-independent hamiltonian contains random parameters and is treated as a member of a statistical ensemble. This entropy is representation independent and can be calculated as a trace functional of the density matrix which describes the system in its interaction with the noise source. We analyze perturbation theory in order to show the evolution from the pure state to the mixed one. Exactly solvable examples illustrate the use of correlational entropy as a measure of the degree of complexity in comparison with other available suggestions such as basis-dependent information entropy. It is shown in particular that a harmonic oscillator in a uniform field of random strength comes to a quasithermal equilibrium; we discuss the relation between effective temperature and canonical equilibrium temperature. The notion of correlational entropy is applied to a realistic numerical caculation in the framework of the nuclear shell model. In this system, which reveals generic signatures of quantum chaos, correlational entropy and information entropy calculated in the mean field basis display similar qualitative behavior.

## Key claim

The paper defines a representation-independent "correlational entropy" (von Neumann entropy) for an individual quantum state by treating the Hamiltonian as containing random parameters drawn from a statistical ensemble, and shows that this entropy can serve as a measure of quantum complexity even for a single energy eigenstate, provided the system is coupled to a noise source.

## Method

The authors consider a quantum system with a time-independent Hamiltonian H(λ) that depends on random parameters λ drawn from a normalized distribution P(λ). For a given eigenstate |α;λ⟩, they construct the pure-state density matrix ρ^(α)(λ) and then average over the ensemble to obtain ρ^(α) = ∫ dλ P(λ) ρ^(α)(λ). The von Neumann entropy S^(α) = -Tr{ρ^(α) ln ρ^(α)} is then computed from the eigenvalues of this averaged density matrix. The method is applied analytically to perturbation theory, two-level systems, a spin in a random field, and a harmonic oscillator in a random uniform field, and numerically to a nuclear shell-model calculation for ²⁴Mg.

## Key numbers

- For the two-level system with uniform noise distribution and original degeneracy (ε=0), correlational entropy evolves from S=0 at Λ=0 to S=ln2 for large noise range Λ when the mixing parameter τ=2V/Λ is small.
- For the two-level system with fluctuating off-diagonal mixing (uniform distribution between -v and v), at weak mixing (κ=2v/ε ≪ 1) the entropy is S ≈ (κ²/12) ln(κ²/12); at strong mixing (κ ≫ 1) it approaches S ≈ ln2 - (1/2)[(ln 2κ)/κ]².
- For the Gaussian orthogonal ensemble (GOE) of 2×2 matrices, the mean information entropy is I̅_GOE = 2 ln2 - 1, while correlational entropy reaches its maximum S = ln2.
- For the Gaussian unitary ensemble (GUE) of 2×2 matrices, the mean information entropy is I̅_GUE = 1/2, while correlational entropy again reaches S = ln2.
- For the harmonic oscillator in a random uniform field, the energy spectrum is E_n(λ) = ħω(n + 1/2 - λ²) where λ = F/(2ħmω³)^{1/2} is the dimensionless noise parameter.

## Context

This work extends the use of von Neumann entropy beyond thermal ensembles to individual quantum states by introducing an ensemble of Hamiltonian parameters as a noise source, building on prior studies of information entropy in quantum chaos by Yoneda (1990), Reichl (1992), Izrailev (1990), and the nuclear shell-model calculations of Zelevinsky, Brown, and collaborators (1993, 1996). The correlational entropy is contrasted with basis-dependent information entropy and with dynamical (Kolmogorov-Sinai) entropy studied by Srinivas (1978), Kosloff (1981), and others. The paper enables a representation-independent measure of quantum complexity that can be applied to realistic many-body systems such as nuclei, where the numerical example for ²⁴Mg shows that correlational entropy becomes a smooth function of excitation energy, similar to thermodynamic entropy.
