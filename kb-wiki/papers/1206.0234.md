# Effective interactions and operators in no-core shell model

> Solutions to the nuclear many-body problem rely on effective interactions, and in general effective operators, to take into account effects not included in calculations. These include effects due to the truncation to finite model spaces where a numerical calculation is tractable, as well as physical terms not included in the description in the first place. In the no-core shell model (NCSM) framework, we discuss two approaches to the effective interactions based on (i) unitary transformations and (ii) effective field theory (EFT) principles. Starting from a given Hamiltonian, the unitary transformation approach is designed to take into account effects induced by the truncation to finite model spaces in which a numerical calculation is performed. This approach was widely applied to the description of nuclear properties of light nuclei; we review the theory and present representative results. In the EFT approach, a Hamiltonian is always constructed in a truncated model space according to the symmetries of the underlying theory, making use of power counting to limit the number of interactions included in the calculations. Hence, physical terms not explicitly included in the calculation are treated on the same footing with the truncation to a finite model space. In this approach, we review results for both nuclear and trapped atomic systems, for which the effective theories are formally similar, albeit describing different underlying physics. Finally, the application of the EFT method of constructing effective interactions to Gamow shell model is briefly discussed.

## Key claim

The paper reviews two approaches to constructing effective interactions for the no-core shell model (NCSM), unitary transformations and effective field theory (EFT), and argues that while the unitary transformation approach suffers from shortcomings such as the neglect of induced higher-body interactions and difficult renormalization of general operators, the EFT approach can mitigate these issues by formulating the problem as an EFT in a discrete basis, where low-energy constants are adjusted to reproduce experimental observables and renormalization-group invariance is desired.

## Method

The unitary transformation approach splits the A-body Hilbert space into a model space and an excluded space, then constructs a unitary transformation that decouples the two; in practice, a cluster approximation (two- or three-body) is used, neglecting induced higher-body interactions. The EFT approach constructs the Hamiltonian directly in a truncated model space according to symmetries and power counting, with low-energy constants fitted to experimental data (e.g., binding energies of light nuclei or trapped atomic spectra) and with regularization via an ultraviolet cutoff; renormalization-group invariance is checked by varying the cutoff. The paper also discusses applying the EFT method to the Gamow shell model for halo nuclei, using a three-body n+n+alpha description of 6He with potentials derived from pionless EFT and a smooth regulator function.

## Key numbers

- The neutron-alpha scattering volume in the P3/2 channel is fixed to the experimental value a_nα = -62.951 fm^3, and the effective "range" to r_nα = -0.8819 fm^{-1}.
- The ground-state energy of 6Li is predicted within 30% of the experimental value at leading order.
- For 7Li, the best agreement of the predicted binding energy with experiment (39.2 MeV) is about 38 MeV for one determination of the three-body low-energy constants (case A), and 36.7 MeV for another (case B).
- For the M1 transition in 12C (0+0 → 1+1), when only two-body forces are included, the B(M1) strength converges to about one third of the experimental value; inclusion of the three-body interaction significantly increases the strength.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the no-core shell method (NCSM) introduced by Navratil (2000) and others, which has been applied to nuclear spectra, electro-weak transitions, and reaction observables. The unitary transformation approach (Okubo-Lee-Suzuki) has been widely used for light nuclei, but the paper contrasts it with a newer EFT-based approach (pioneered by Stetcu (2006) and Stetcu (2007)) that aims to eliminate model dependence and improve renormalization of operators. The EFT method is also applied to the Gamow shell model for halo nuclei (e.g., 6He), building on work by Michel (2002) and Rotureau (2012), and enables systematic, RG-invariant calculations of weakly bound/unbound systems and potentially other three-body resonant states such as the Hoyle state in 12C.
