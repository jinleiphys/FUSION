# From QCD Symmetries to Nuclei and Neutron Stars

> Global symmetries and symmetry breaking patterns of QCD with light quarks, in particular chiral symmetry, provide basic guidance not only for low-energy hadron physics but also for nuclear forces and the nuclear many-body problem. Recent developments of Chiral Effective Field Theory applications to nuclear and neutron matter are summarized, with special emphasis on a (non-perturbative) extension using functional renormalisation group methods. Topics include: nuclear thermodynamics, extrapolations to dense baryonic matter and constraints from neutron star observables.

## Key claim

Chiral Effective Field Theory (ChEFT) provides a systematically improvable framework for nuclear forces and the nuclear many-body problem, but its perturbative expansion in powers of Fermi momentum over cutoff converges only up to densities around twice nuclear saturation density; beyond that, non-perturbative methods such as functional renormalization group (FRG) are required to treat multi-pion fluctuations and many-body correlations that grow rapidly with density.

## Method

The paper reviews the construction of chiral nuclear forces via a hierarchy of one- and multi-pion exchange diagrams plus contact terms, organized in powers of \(Q/\Lambda\) up to fifth order (N\(^4\)LO), with low-energy constants fitted to NN scattering phase shifts. For nuclear and neutron matter, in-medium chiral perturbation theory is applied using the in-medium nucleon propagator with a medium insertion representing the filled Fermi sea, computing the energy per particle as an expansion in Fermi momentum. A non-perturbative extension uses functional renormalization group (FRG) methods, starting from a chiral nucleon-meson Lagrangian (linear sigma model) at the chiral symmetry breaking scale \(\Lambda_\chi \sim 1\) GeV and evolving the effective action down to infrared scales via Wetterich's flow equation, which resums fluctuations of pions and nucleon-hole excitations to all orders.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon sigma term from low-energy pion-nucleon scattering: \(\sigma_N \simeq (45 \pm 8)\) MeV (older range); recent analysis gives \(\sigma_N \simeq (59 \pm 5)\) MeV; lattice QCD suggests \(\sigma_N \simeq 38\) MeV with about 20% uncertainty.
- Pion decay constant: \(f_\pi \simeq 92.2\) MeV; chiral limit value \(f_\pi^{(0)} \simeq 86\) MeV.
- Quark condensate at renormalization scale \(\mu \simeq 2\) GeV: \(\langle \bar{q} q \rangle \simeq -(0.36\ \text{GeV})^3 \simeq -6\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).
- Baryonic core r.m.s. radius from chiral soliton model: \(\langle r_B^2 \rangle^{1/2} \simeq 0.5\) fm; isoscalar charge radius: \(\langle r_S^2 \rangle^{1/2} \simeq 0.8\) fm.
- Normal nuclear matter density: \(n_0 = 0.16\ \text{fm}^{-3}\); Fermi momentum at \(n_0\): \(p_F = 1.33\ \text{fm}^{-1} \simeq 1.9\, m_\pi\).
- Critical temperature of liquid-gas phase transition in symmetric nuclear matter from ChEFT: \(T_c \simeq 17.4\) MeV; empirical value: \(T_c = 17.9 \pm 0.4\) MeV.
- Empirical critical pressure: \(P_c = 0.31 \pm 0.07\ \text{MeV}\cdot\text{fm}^{-3}\); critical density: \(n_c = 0.06 \pm 0.01\ \text{fm}^{-3}\).
- ChEFT calculations with N\(^3\)LO NN interactions and N\(^2\)LO three-body forces are considered reliable up to densities \(n \sim 2\,n_0\).
- Energy-density functional derived from chiral EFT supports two-solar-mass neutron stars with radii around 11–12 km.

## Context

This review summarizes the application of chiral symmetry principles from QCD to nuclear forces and dense matter, extending the line of work by Weinberg (1996) on ChEFT and by Gasser and Leutwyler (1984) on chiral perturbation theory. It contrasts perturbative in-medium chiral perturbation theory (e.g., Holt, Kaiser, and Weise 2013) with non-perturbative FRG methods developed by Drews and Weise (2015, 2017) that can reach higher densities relevant for neutron star cores. The paper enables systematic connections between QCD symmetries, nuclear thermodynamics, and neutron star observables, while identifying open questions such as the tension between different determinations of the nucleon sigma term and the need for non-perturbative treatments beyond twice saturation density.
