# Parametrising yields of nuclear multifragmentation

> We consider a model where, for a finite disintegrating system, yields of composites can be calculated to arbitrary accuracy. An analytic answer for yields is also known in the thermodynamic limit. In the range of temperature and density considered in this work, the model has a phase transition. This phase transition is first order. The analytic expression for yields of composites, in the thermodynamic limit, does not conform to the expression $<n_a>=a^{-τ}f(a^σ (T-T_c))$ where, the usual identification would be that $T_c$ is the critical temperature and $τ,σ$ are critical exponents. Nonetheless, for finite systems, we try to fit the yields with the above expression. A minimisation procedure is adopted to get the parameters $T_c,τ$ and $σ$. While deviations from the formula are not negligible, one might believe that the deviations are consistent with the corrections attributable to finite particle number effects and might then conclude that one has deduced at least approximately the values of critical parameters. This exercise thus points to difficulties of trying to extract critical parameters from data on nuclear disintegration. An interesting result is that the value of $T_c$ deduced from the ``best'' fit is very close to the temperature at which the first order phase transition occurs in the model. The yields calculated in this model can also be fitted quite well by a parametrisation derived from a droplet model.

# Parametrising yields of nuclear multifragmentation

## Key claim

Although the exact yields of composites in the thermodynamic limit do not conform to the standard scaling form `<n_a> = a^{-τ} f(a^σ (T - T_c))`, finite disintegrating systems can be fitted approximately by that expression; however, the extracted `T_c` corresponds not to the true critical temperature of the model (18 MeV) but rather to the first-order phase transition ("boiling") temperature, and the extracted exponents `τ` and `σ` carry no clear physical significance.

## Method

The authors use a canonical statistical model of one kind of particle (and later two kinds with and without Coulomb) where composites have a volume energy, a temperature-dependent surface tension, and a Fermi-gas internal partition function; yields are computed exactly via a recursion relation. They then apply a multi-step minimisation procedure (following Gulminelli and Chomaz (1999)) to fit the yields to the scaling form, extracting best-fit values of `τ`, `σ`, and `T_c`. Separately, they fit the same yields to a Fisher droplet model parametrisation.

## Key numbers

- For a system of one kind of particle with A=174 and freeze-out volume V=3V0, the best-fit parameters are τ=2.72, σ=1.06, and T_c=6.32 MeV.
- For A=240 and V=3V0, the best-fit parameters are τ=2.78, σ=1.23, and T_c=6.54 MeV.
- The true critical temperature of the model is T_c=18 MeV (where surface tension vanishes); the extracted T_c values (6.32 and 6.54 MeV) are very close to the temperatures where the specific heat peaks, which correspond to the first-order phase transition ("boiling") temperatures at the given densities.
- In the droplet model fit (with τ fixed to 2), the parameters α and γ both go to zero near T=6.5 MeV, again suggesting a critical temperature of about 6.5 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the analysis of scaling laws in nuclear multifragmentation, previously applied to percolation models (Stauffer and Aharony, 1992) and the lattice gas model (Pan and Das Gupta, 1995; Gulminelli and Chomaz, 1999), to a class of statistical multifragmentation models (Bondorf et al., 1995) where exact yields can be computed. The results show that fitting finite-system yields to the scaling form can produce apparently reasonable scaling plots even when the underlying model does not obey the scaling law, and that the extracted `T_c` reflects the first-order transition temperature rather than the true critical point. This cautions against interpreting such fits as evidence for a continuous phase transition, and the authors argue that all Hamiltonian-based models to date suggest a first-order phase transition. The work also demonstrates that a Fisher droplet model parametrisation (Fisher, 1967; Goodman, Kapusta and Mekjian, 1984) can fit the yields equally well.
