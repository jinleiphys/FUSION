# Is Seniority a Partial Dynamic Symmetry in the First $νg_{9/2}$ Shell?

> The low-lying structures of the midshell $νg_{9/2}$ Ni isotopes $^{72}$Ni and $^{74}$Ni have been investigated at the RIBF facility in RIKEN within the EURICA collaboration. Previously unobserved low-lying states were accessed for the first time following $β$ decay of the mother nuclei $^{72}$Co and $^{74}$Co. As a result, we provide a complete picture in terms of the seniority scheme up to the first $(8^+)$ levels for both nuclei. The experimental results are compared to shell-model calculations in order to define to what extent the seniority quantum number is preserved in the first neutron $g_{9/2}$ shell. We find that the disappearance of the seniority isomerism in the $(8^+_1)$ states can be explained by a lowering of the seniority-four $(6^+)$ levels as predicted years ago. For $^{74}$Ni, the internal de-excitation pattern of the newly observed $(6^+_2)$ state supports a restoration of the normal seniority ordering up to spin $J=4$. This property, unexplained by the shell-model calculations, is in agreement with a dominance of the single-particle spherical regime near $^{78}$Ni.

## Key claim

Seniority is not fully conserved in the first neutron \(g_{9/2}\) shell for the midshell Ni isotopes \(^{72}\)Ni and \(^{74}\)Ni, but it acts as a partial dynamic symmetry: the disappearance of seniority isomerism in the \((8^+_1)\) states is explained by a lowering of the seniority-four \((6^+)\) levels, while in \(^{74}\)Ni the de-excitation pattern of the newly observed \((6^+_2)\) state supports a restoration of normal seniority ordering up to spin \(J=4\), a property that shell-model calculations cannot reproduce.

## Method

The low-lying structures of \(^{72}\)Ni and \(^{74}\)Ni were investigated following \(\beta\) decay of \(^{72}\)Co and \(^{74}\)Co at the RIBF facility in RIKEN within the EURICA collaboration. Radioactive beams were produced by in-flight fission of a \(^{238}\)U beam at 345 MeV/nucleon, separated in the BigRIPS separator, and implanted in the WAS3ABi active stopper; \(\gamma\) rays were detected by the EURICA array of 12 HPGe cluster detectors and a fast-timing array of 18 LaBr\(_3\)(Ce) detectors. Half-lives were measured using in-flight \(\beta\)-delayed fast-timing spectroscopy, and the experimental level schemes were compared to four shell-model calculations (MCSM, SM Lis, SM 2+5, and SM SCI) to assess seniority conservation.

## Key numbers

- Half-life of the \((6^+_1)\) state in \(^{72}\)Ni: \(t_{1/2}^{6^+} = 860(60)\) ps, corresponding to B(E2) = 34(2) e\(^2\)fm\(^4\) for the \((6^+_1)\rightarrow(4^+_1)\) transition.
- Ratio of reduced transition probabilities in \(^{74}\)Ni: \(R = \frac{B(E2,6^+_2\rightarrow4^+_2)}{B(E2,6^+_2\rightarrow4^+_1)} = 22(5)\), extracted from \(\gamma\) intensities \(I_{\gamma}^{403}=I_{\gamma}^{744}=36(6)\%\).
- \(\beta\)-decay half-lives of \(^{74}\)Co: \(t_{1/2}=31.6(6)\) ms for the high-spin state and \(t_{1/2}=28(3)\) ms for the low-spin state.
- Seniority-conserving predictions for comparison: \(R_{42} \approx 14\) and \(R_{64} \approx 7.6\).
- Time resolution of the fast-timing system: 300 ps (FWHM), allowing half-life measurements down to about 100 ps.
- Implantation events: approximately \(9\times10^5\) for \(^{72}\)Co and \(2\times10^4\) for \(^{74}\)Co.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of seniority isomers in Ni isotopes, particularly the non-observation of \(\mu\)s-range isomers in \(^{72}\)Ni and \(^{74}\)Ni reported by Mazurek (2005) and Sawicka (2003), which was attributed to quasi-degeneracy of \(6^+\) states with seniority \(\upsilon=2\) and \(\upsilon=4\) as predicted by Grawe (2002) and Isacker (2013). The results contradict the simple seniority scheme for \(^{72}\)Ni, where inversion of seniorities in \((6^+)\) and \((4^+)\) states was reported by Morales (2016) and Kolos (2016), but show a recovery of normal seniority ordering in \(^{74}\)Ni up to \(J=4\), consistent with a dominance of the single-particle spherical regime near \(^{78}\)Ni. The findings enable further quantitative studies of electromagnetic transition rates between yrast and yrare \((6^+)\) and \((4^+)\) states, and highlight the role of core excitations across \(Z=28\) and \(N=50\) as well as proton-neutron interactions that do not conserve seniority, as discussed in the context of "type II" shell evolution by Tsunoda (2014).
