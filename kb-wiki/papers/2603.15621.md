# Exclusive Scattering Channels from Entanglement Structure in Real-Time Simulations

> A scattering event in a quantum field theory is a coherent superposition of all processes consistent with its symmetries and kinematics. While real-time simulations have progressed toward resolving individual channels, existing approaches rely on knowledge of the asymptotic particle wavefunctions. This work introduces an experimentally inspired method to isolate scattering channels in Matrix Product State simulations based on the entanglement structure of the late-time wavefunction. Schmidt decompositions at spatial bipartitions of the post-scattering state identify elastic and inelastic contributions, enabling deterministic detection of outgoing particles of specific species. This method may be used in settings beyond scattering and is applied to detect heavy particles produced in a collision in the one-dimensional Ising field theory. Natural extensions to quantum simulations of other systems and higher-order processes are discussed.

## Key claim

The paper introduces a method to isolate exclusive scattering channels in Matrix Product State simulations by using Schmidt decompositions at spatial bipartitions of the late-time wavefunction, without requiring prior knowledge of asymptotic particle wavefunctions. The method is exact only in the limit where outgoing particles belonging to different channels traveling in the same direction are well-separated, requiring sufficiently large collimated wavepackets and sufficiently late times.

## Method

The method performs successive Schmidt decompositions at spatial bipartitions of the post-scattering state in one-dimensional Matrix Product State simulations, where different particle species have different group velocities and therefore separate in space over time. By identifying bipartition locations that separate fast-moving elastic components from slower inelastic components, the wavefunction is decomposed into orthogonal tensor product states corresponding to distinct scattering channels. The Schmidt coefficients from these decompositions directly yield channel probabilities, and particle masses within each exclusive state are confirmed by matching measured velocities to dispersion relations.

## Key numbers

- Couplings used: $g_x=1.25$, $g_z=0.15$ with system size $L=400$
- Initial momentum of colliding $|1\rangle$ particles: $k_i=0.36\pi$, with momentum-space width $\sigma_k=0.059\pi$
- Threshold momentum for inelastic scattering $11\to12$: $k_\text{thr}=0.2412\pi$
- Masses of stable particles: $m_1=1.59$, $m_2=2.97$
- Branching ratios at $k_i=0.36\pi$: $P(11)=0.5638$, $P(12)=0.3395$
- Higher-order inelastic processes have probabilities $O(10^{-2})$
- Energy-momentum classification agrees within 8% error between measured and dispersion-relation predictions

## Context

This work extends previous real-time simulations of elastic collisions (Zemlevskiy 2024, Hite 2025, Chai 2023) and recent inelastic scattering simulations on quantum computers (Schuhmacher 2025, Farrell 2025) by providing a method to resolve individual scattering channels from the inclusive post-scattering wavefunction. Prior work by Jha (2024) and Van Damme (2021) determined particle content by constructing projectors onto localized states with given quantum numbers, while this work leverages entanglement structure directly. The method enables extraction of exclusive cross sections and branching ratios from real-time simulations, and the author suggests extensions to quantum simulations where "detectors" placed throughout the lattice could categorize shot results into channels, analogous to experimental detection protocols at collider facilities.
