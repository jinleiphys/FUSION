# Thermo-Statistics or Topology of the Microcanonical Entropy Surface

> Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V...)=ln W(E,N,V...) allows the interpretation of Statistical Mechanics of a closed system as Pseudo-Riemannian geometry in the space of the conserved parameters E,N,V... (the conserved mechanical parameters in the language of Ruppeiner) without invoking the thermodynamic limit. The topology is controlled by the curvature of S(E,N,V...). The most interesting region is the region of (wrong) positive maximum curvature, the region of phase-separation. This is demonstrated among others for the equilibrium of a typical non-extensive system, a self-gravitating and rotating cloud in a spherical container at various energies and angular-momenta. A rich variety of realistic configurations, as single stars, multi-star systems, rings and finally gas, are obtained as equilibrium microcanonical phases. The global phase diagram, the topology of the curvature, as function of energy and angular-momentum is presented. No exotic form of thermodynamics like Tsallis non-extensive one is necessary. It is further shown that a finite (even mesoscopic) system approaches equilibrium with a change of its entropy Delta S\ge 0 (Second Law) even when its Poincarre recurrence time is not large.

# Thermo-Statistics or Topology of the Microcanonical Entropy Surface

## Key claim

Boltzmann's principle S = ln W allows statistical mechanics of a closed system to be interpreted as Pseudo-Riemannian geometry in the space of conserved parameters (E, N, V) without invoking the thermodynamic limit, and the topology of the entropy surface is controlled by its curvature. The region of positive maximum curvature, which conventional thermodynamics treats as "wrong", is actually the region of phase-separation, and this geometric approach applies even to non-extensive systems like self-gravitating rotating clouds without requiring exotic forms of thermodynamics such as Tsallis non-extensive statistics.

## Method

The author reformulates microcanonical statistical mechanics by treating Boltzmann's principle S(E,N,V) = ln W(E,N,V) as the sole axiom, where W is the area of the microcanonical manifold in N-body phase space. The entropy surface is analyzed through its curvature matrix (Hessian) and its eigenvalues, classifying thermodynamic phases by the local topology of curvature: negative eigenvalues indicate single stable phases, positive eigenvalues indicate phase-separation with surface tension, and the boundary where eigenvalues vanish indicates critical points. The method is demonstrated on a self-gravitating and rotating cloud in a spherical container and on a 2-dimensional Potts-3 lattice gas model.

## Key numbers

- For sodium clusters of N0 = 1000 atoms at 1 atm external pressure: transition temperature T_tr = 990 K, latent heat q_lat = 0.91 eV per atom, surface entropy loss Δs_surf = 0.56, and surface tension σ/T_tr = 5.68 (compared to bulk value 7.41)
- For sodium clusters of N0 = 200 atoms: T_tr = 940 K, q_lat = 0.82 eV, Δs_surf = 0.55, σ/T_tr = 2.75
- For sodium clusters of N0 = 3000 atoms: T_tr = 1095 K, q_lat = 0.94 eV, Δs_surf = 0.44, σ/T_tr = 7.07
- Bulk sodium values: T_tr = 1156 K, q_lat = 0.923 eV, s_boil = 9.267
- The convex intruder depth in the entropy per atom for sodium clusters scales approximately as N^{-1/3}
- The Potts model (q=10) on a 100x100 lattice shows negative specific heat in the coexistence region between energies e_1 and e_3

## Context

This work extends the geometric approach to thermodynamics pioneered by Ruppeiner (1995), who proposed Riemannian geometry of fluctuations but did not address positive curvature of the entropy surface at phase-separation. The author reformulates Ruppeiner's geometry as Pseudo-Riemannian to handle phase-separation regions where the canonical ensemble fails. The paper contradicts the conventional view (held by Lebowitz, 1999, and Lieb, 1998) that the thermodynamic limit and extensivity are necessary for statistical mechanics, and it argues against Tsallis (1988, 1999) non-extensive statistics by showing that microcanonical geometry suffices for non-extensive systems. The work enables the interpretation of realistic astrophysical configurations, single stars, multi-star systems, rings, and gas, as equilibrium microcanonical phases of self-gravitating rotating systems, and it provides a framework for understanding phase-separation in finite systems where the thermodynamic limit does not apply.
