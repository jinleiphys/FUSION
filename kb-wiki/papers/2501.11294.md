# Complex-scaled no-core shell model calculations of bound and unbound nuclear states in light nuclei

> The complex scaling method is commonly used to describe decaying states, but its applications are limited because the Hamiltonian operator must contain only relative coordinates. This has hindered the use of complex scaling in models defined with laboratory single-particle coordinates, and in particular one of the most important model in low-energy nuclear physics, the no-core shell model. We will then present a straightforward procedure for introducing complex scaling in the no-core shell model in order to calculate nuclear resonance states. For that matter, the complex-scaled two-body matrix elements must firstly be determined, and the resulting many-body Hamiltonian complex symmetric matrix must be diagonalized afterwards. Applications pertain to the bound ground states of the lightest nuclei $^2{\rm H}$, $^3{\rm H}$, $^3{\rm He}$, and $^4{\rm He}$, as well as the resonance ground states of $^5$He and $^5$Li, whereby the realistic interaction Daejeon16 is utilized.

## Key claim
The authors present a procedure to apply complex scaling to the no-core shell model (NCSM) by complex-scaling only the intrinsic Hamiltonian while leaving the center-of-mass (CM) Hamiltonian unchanged, thereby circumventing the requirement that the Hamiltonian contain only relative coordinates. The method is validated on bound states of A=2-4 nuclei and resonance ground states of ^5He and ^5Li, but the imaginary parts of bound-state energies in finite model spaces are nonzero (up to ~10 keV for deuteron, ~5 keV for A=3-4 in 12ℏω spaces), indicating residual truncation error.

## Method
The complex-scaled intrinsic Hamiltonian H_int^θ is constructed by replacing coordinates r → r e^{iθ} and momenta p → p e^{-iθ} in the intrinsic kinetic and potential operators. The Lawson-corrected Hamiltonian H_β = H_int^θ + β(H_CM^HO - 3/2 ℏω) is then diagonalized in a harmonic-oscillator (HO) Slater-determinant basis, where the CM part remains unscaled. Complex-scaled two-body matrix elements are computed via Talmi-Brody-Moshinsky decomposition using complex-scaled relative HO states, with the realistic Daejeon16 interaction. For unbound states, the generalized variational principle is applied by finding the complex energy stationary with respect to small changes of the CS angle θ.

## Key numbers
- Deuteron (^2H) ground state energy: -2.205 MeV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, ℏω=12.5 MeV) with imaginary part -8.438×10^{-3} MeV; exact value is -2.224 MeV.
- ^3H ground state energy: -8.440 + i 2.922×10^{-5} MeV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, θ=5°); NCSM gives -8.436 MeV; Ref. [shir] (16ℏω) gives -8.442 MeV.
- ^3He ground state energy: -7.742 – i 5.343×10^{-4} MeV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, θ=5°); NCSM gives -7.738 MeV; Ref. [shir] (16ℏω) gives -7.744 MeV.
- ^4He ground state energy: -28.372 + i 9.526×10^{-5} MeV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, θ=5°); NCSM gives -28.371 MeV; Ref. [shir] (16ℏω) gives -28.372 MeV.
- ^5He resonance ground state: separation energy 1.192 MeV, width 495 keV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, optimal θ≈13°); Ref. [pap13] (NCGSM) gives 1.17 MeV and 400 keV.
- ^5Li resonance ground state: separation energy 2.059 MeV, width 651 keV (CS-NCSM, 12ℏω, optimal θ≈13°).
- Imaginary parts of bound-state energies in 6ℏω spaces range from 50–150 keV; in 12ℏω spaces they are 0.5–5 keV.

## Context
This work extends the complex scaling method, previously limited to relative-coordinate formulations (Aguilar-Balslev-Combes theorem, Refs. Aguilar (1971), Balslev (1971)), to the no-core shell model (NCSM) framework of Barrett (2013) and others. It provides an alternative to the Gamow shell model (GSM) of Michel (2009) and the SS-HORSE method of Shang (2016, 2018) for calculating resonances in light nuclei. The results for ^5He and ^5Li differ from the SS-HORSE values of Shir (2021) by 500 keV to 1 MeV in energy, attributed to different resonance identification methods (generalized variational principle vs. scattering phase shifts). The method enables future ab initio calculations of low-energy nuclear resonances within the widely used NCSM framework.
