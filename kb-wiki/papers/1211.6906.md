# Nucleon-nucleon momentum correlation function as a probe of the density distribution of valence neutron in neutron-rich nucleus

> Proton-neutron, neutron-neutron and proton-proton momentum correlation functions ($C_{pn}$, $C_{nn}$, $C_{pp}$) are systematically investigated for $^{15}$C and other C isotopes induced collisions at different entrance channel conditions within the framework of the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IDQMD) model complemented by the CRAB (correlation after burner) computation code. $^{15}$C is a prime exotic nucleus candidate due to the weakly bound valence neutron coupling with closed-neutron shell nucleus $^{14}$C. In order to study density dependence of correlation function by removing the isospin effect, the initialized $^{15}$C projectiles are sampled from two kinds of density distribution from RMF model, in which the valence neutron of $^{15}$C is populated on both 1$d$5/2 and 2$s$1/2 states, respectively. The results show that the density distributions of valence neutron significantly influence nucleon-nucleon momentum correlation function at large impact parameter and high incident energy. The extended density distribution of valence neutron largely weakens the strength of correlation function. The size of emission source is extracted by fitting correlation function using Gaussian source method. The emission source size as well as the size of final state phase space is larger for projectiles sampling from more extended density distribution of valence neutron corresponding 2$s$1/2 state in RMF model. Therefore momentum correlation function can be considered as a potential valuable tool to diagnose the exotic nuclear structure such as skin and halo.

## Key claim

The nucleon-nucleon momentum correlation function is sensitive to the density distribution of the valence neutron in neutron-rich nuclei, but this sensitivity is only significant at large impact parameters and high incident energies; in central and semi-peripheral collisions the difference in density is washed out.

## Method

The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IDQMD) model as an event generator for \(^{15}\)C + \(^{12}\)C collisions, with the phase-space output fed into the CRAB (correlation after burner) code to compute two-particle momentum correlation functions. The \(^{15}\)C projectiles were initialized with two different density distributions from relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations, placing the valence neutron in either the 1\(d\)5/2 or 2\(s\)1/2 state, to isolate the effect of the valence neutron density while keeping the isospin degree of freedom fixed. The correlation functions were then studied as functions of impact parameter and incident energy, and the emission source size was extracted by fitting the correlation functions with a Gaussian source model.

## Key numbers

* RMS radius of the valence neutron in \(^{15}\)C: 3.85 fm for the 1\(d\)5/2 state and 5.01 fm for the 2\(s\)1/2 state (from RMF calculation).
* Neutron separation energy of \(^{15}\)C: \(S_n = 1.218\) MeV.
* The emission source size extracted from \(C_{nn}\) and \(C_{pn}\) is about 20% larger for projectiles with the more extended (2\(s\)1/2) valence neutron density compared to the less extended (1\(d\)5/2) configuration.
* The emission source size extracted from \(C_{pp}\) is about 15% larger for projectiles with the more extended valence neutron density, despite identical initial proton density distributions.
* After correcting for the finite width of the Gaussian wave packet (\(L = 2.16\) fm\(^2\)), the RMS radii of the emission source from \(C_{nn}\) and \(C_{pn}\) are 5.88 fm (1\(d\)5/2) and 7.37 fm (2\(s\)1/2); from \(C_{pp}\) they are 5.12 fm (1\(d\)5/2) and 6.07 fm (2\(s\)1/2).
* The correlation strength for \(C_{pn}\) and \(C_{nn}\) saturates above approximately 300 MeV/nucleon incident energy.

## Context

This work extends the application of the Hanbury Brown-Twiss (HBT) intensity interferometry method, previously used to study heavy-ion collision dynamics and source sizes, to the diagnosis of exotic nuclear structures such as neutron halos and skins. It builds on prior studies by Wei and Ma (2005) and Ma et al. (2007) that showed linear dependence of correlation strength on binding energy and single-neutron separation energy, and by Fang et al. (2004) who measured reaction cross sections and momentum distributions for \(^{15}\)C. The results suggest that momentum correlation functions at high energy and large impact parameter can serve as a complementary probe to traditional methods like total reaction cross section and momentum distribution measurements for identifying halo and skin structures in neutron-rich nuclei.
