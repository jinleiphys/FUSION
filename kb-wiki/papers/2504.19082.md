# Disentangling effects of nucleon size and nucleus structure in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> While relativistic heavy-ion collisions become an alternative way of studying nucleus structure, the accurate extraction of nucleus structure could be hampered by the uncertainty of nucleon size, and the latter has attracted people's attention in the past few years. We have compared the impacts of nuclear size and nucleus structure on deformation probes in relativistic heavy-ion collisions based on a multiphase transport (AMPT) model. With increasing nucleon size, the absolute values of the deformation probes are generally reduced due to smeared initial density fluctuations. In heavy systems such as $^{197}$Au+$^{197}$Au collisions, neglecting the nucleon size could underestimate or overestimate significantly the extracted deformation parameter depending on the used deformation probe, while the scaled anisotropic flow and the scaled Pearson correlation coefficient of flow and transverse momentum are good probes of the nucleus deformation rather insensitive to the nucleon size. In small systems such as $^{16}$O+$^{16}$O collisions, the deformation probes are generally more sensitive to the nucleon size than to the nucleus structure, and the transverse momentum fluctuation less sensitive to detailed nucleus structure may serve as a good probe of the nucleon size.

# Disentangling effects of nucleon size and nucleus structure in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

## Key claim

The finite size of nucleons (characterized by a Gaussian width parameter w) can significantly bias the extraction of nuclear deformation parameters from heavy-ion collision data, but the scaled anisotropic flow and the scaled Pearson correlation coefficient of flow and transverse momentum remain robust probes of deformation that are insensitive to nucleon size. In small systems like 16O+16O collisions, the nucleon size effect generally dominates over the effect of different nuclear configurations.

## Method

The authors used the string melting version of the AMPT (A Multi-Phase Transport) model to simulate 197Au+197Au and 16O+16O collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=200 GeV. They introduced a finite nucleon size by resampling the transverse coordinates of initial partons according to a Gaussian distribution with width parameter w=0, 0.4, and 0.8 fm, where w=0 corresponds to point-like nucleons and w=0.8 fm corresponds to a nucleon root-mean-square radius of 1.13 fm. For 197Au, they compared spherical (beta_2=0) and deformed (beta_2=0.3) Woods-Saxon density distributions; for 16O, they compared spherical, tetrahedron, square, and Y-shape configurations from a microscopic cluster model. They calculated anisotropic flows v_n, transverse momentum fluctuations, and their correlations using multi-particle cumulant methods with non-flow subtraction.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon width parameters used: w=0, 0.4, and 0.8 fm, corresponding to nucleon root-mean-square radii of 0, 0.57, and 1.13 fm respectively
- For 197Au: radius R_0=6.380 fm, diffuseness d=0.535 fm, deformation parameter beta_2=0 (spherical) and 0.3 (deformed)
- For 16O spherical distribution: R_0=2.608 fm, d=0.513 fm, w_0=-0.051
- Parton scattering cross section set to 1.5 mb with strong coupling alpha_s=0.33 and screening mass mu=3.2 fm^{-1}
- For 197Au+197Au: Lund string parameters a=0.5, b=0.9 GeV^{-2}
- For 16O+16O: Lund string parameters a=2.2, b=0.5 GeV^{-2}
- If the real nucleon size is w=0.8 fm and the real beta_2=0.3, assuming point nucleons leads to extracted beta_2 values of approximately 0.315 from scaled <v_2^2> (overestimate by 5%), 0.228 from scaled <delta p_T^2> (underestimate by 24%), and 0.495 from scaled <v_2^2 delta p_T> (overestimate by 65%)

## Context

This work extends the program of using relativistic heavy-ion collisions to extract nuclear structure parameters, following prior studies that extracted deformation parameters for 96Ru, 96Zr (Zhang 2021), 197Au (Giacalone 2021), and 238U (STAR 2024), and the characterization of 129Xe as a triaxial ellipsoid (Bally 2021, ATLAS 2022, ALICE 2024). The study directly addresses the uncertainty in nucleon size, which has been debated in hydrodynamic simulations where the Gaussian width parameter w ranges from 0.4 fm (Schenke 2010) to 0.8-1.1 fm (Bernhard 2019, Nijs 2021). The results enable future Bayesian analyses that simultaneously constrain both nuclear structure and nucleon size from experimental data, and provide guidance for ongoing experimental analyses of 16O+16O collisions at RHIC (STAR 2023).
