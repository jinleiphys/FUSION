# Numerical study of hot strongly interacting matter

> I review recent progress in study of strongly interacting matter at high temperatures using Monte-Carlo simulations in lattice QCD.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD simulations with improved staggered fermion actions now provide quantitatively reliable predictions for the thermodynamics of hot strongly interacting matter, but the results depend on the specific action used and the continuum extrapolation, with significant differences remaining between actions in the peak region of the trace anomaly near the transition temperature.

## Method

The author reviews lattice QCD Monte-Carlo simulations using improved staggered fermion actions (p4, asqtad, HISQ/tree, and stout) to calculate the equation of state, fluctuations of conserved charges, Polyakov loop, singlet free energy, chiral condensate, and chiral susceptibility. Temperature is varied by changing the lattice spacing at fixed temporal extent Nτ, and the continuum limit corresponds to Nτ → ∞ at fixed physical volume.

## Key numbers

- The trace anomaly (interaction measure) shows a rapid rise in the transition region and peaks at temperatures of about 200 MeV
- For the chiral transition temperature, the analysis gives Tc = (159 ± 9) MeV from HISQ/tree and asqtad actions
- The Budapest-Wuppertal collaboration found Tc = 147(2)(3) MeV, 157(3)(3) MeV, and 155(3)(3) MeV defined from different observables
- The energy density approaches about 90% of the ideal gas value at high temperatures, while the pressure is about 15% below the ideal gas value
- For the stout action, the energy density is about 20% below the ideal gas limit
- The renormalized chiral condensate drops by 50% at temperatures of (150-160) MeV for light quarks, while the strange quark condensate drops by 50% only at about 190 MeV
- Strangeness fluctuations are close to unity at temperatures T > 250 MeV, corresponding to the massless ideal quark gas

## Context

This review extends the lattice QCD thermodynamics program that began with early SU(2) gauge theory calculations by Kuti (1981), McLerran (1981), and Engels (1981), and the solution for pure gluonic theory by Boyd (1996). The calculations with light dynamical quarks using improved staggered actions build on work by the MILC collaboration, Fodor et al., and the HotQCD collaboration. The results enable comparisons with resummed perturbative calculations by Blaizot et al. and with AdS/CFT predictions by Gubser (1998), and provide input for phenomenological models of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and LHC.
