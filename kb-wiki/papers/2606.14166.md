# Thermal dileptons to probe the baryon-rich QCD matter in the forward region of LHC energy heavy-ion collisions

> We investigate thermal dilepton production from a quark-gluon plasma (QGP) with finite baryon chemical potential ($μ_{\text{B}}$) in central Pb-Pb collisions at $\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}}=5.02~\text{TeV}$. Recent studies suggest that sizable baryon densities can be achieved at forward rapidity even at LHC energies. We incorporate finite $μ_{\text{B}}$ into a (3+1)-dimensional hydrodynamic framework and find that $μ_{\text{B}}$ exceeds 500 MeV around $η_\text{s} = 6$ during the medium evolution. Using this framework, we calculate thermal dilepton spectra over a wide rapidity range and evaluate the impact of finite $μ_{\text{B}}$ on dilepton production. A suppression of 3-4% is observed in the forward-rapidity region $5.2 < y < 7.2$ due to the reduced quark-antiquark abundance at finite baryon density. We further examine the effective temperature extracted from dilepton mass spectra in the intermediate-mass region $1.2 < M_{\ell \ell} < 2.6~\text{GeV}$ . The effective temperature remains strongly correlated with the underlying hydrodynamic temperature and retains sensitivity to the early high-temperature stage of the QGP evolution. These results demonstrate that forward-rapidity dileptons remain effective thermometers while providing sensitivity to finite baryon density at the LHC.

## Key claim

The authors show that forward-rapidity thermal dileptons in Pb-Pb collisions at LHC energies remain effective thermometers of the QGP even when a large baryon chemical potential (exceeding 500 MeV) is present, but the abstract's claim of "sensitivity to finite baryon density" is hedged by the finding that the suppression of the dilepton yield due to finite baryon density is only 3-4% in the forward-rapidity region \(5.2 < y < 7.2\), and that this suppression is not detectable at lower rapidities.

## Method

The authors performed (3+1)-dimensional ideal hydrodynamic simulations of central Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}}=5.02\) TeV, incorporating finite baryon chemical potential via the NEOS-4D equation of state. Initial energy and baryon density profiles were generated using a tilted Monte Carlo Glauber model tuned to reproduce ALICE charged-particle multiplicity data and the McDIPPER baryon-stopping predictions. Thermal dilepton production rates were calculated at leading order (Born approximation) for the \(q\bar{q} \to \ell^+\ell^-\) process, integrated over the hydrodynamic evolution from initial time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm until freezeout at energy density \(e_f = 0.26\) GeV/fm\(^3\).

## Key numbers

- The baryon chemical potential \(\mu_{\text{B}}\) exceeds 500 MeV around space-time rapidity \(\eta_s = 6\) during the medium evolution in central Pb-Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s_{\text{NN}}}=5.02\) TeV.
- A suppression of 3-4% in the thermal dilepton yield is observed in the forward-rapidity region \(5.2 < y < 7.2\) due to finite baryon density.
- The initial time for the hydrodynamic calculation is \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm, with initial energy normalization \(e_0 = 150\) GeV/fm\(^3\) and baryon normalization \(n_0 = 290\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- The freezeout boundary is defined at energy density \(e_f = 0.26\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- The intermediate-mass region studied is \(1.2 < M_{\ell\ell} < 2.6\) GeV for effective temperature extraction.

## Context

This work extends previous studies that assumed negligible baryon chemical potential at LHC energies, motivated by recent predictions from the McLerran-Venugopalan model (PhysRevC.99.014906) and the McDIPPER model (PhysRevC.109.044916) that sizable baryon densities can be achieved at forward rapidity. It also builds on next-to-leading order calculations for the RHIC Beam Energy Scan (PhysRevC.109.044915) which found that baryon-density-induced suppression was not detectable at those lower energies. The results enable future experimental searches at the LHC for finite-density QGP effects using forward-rapidity dilepton measurements, and provide a baseline for future viscous hydrodynamic calculations including baryon diffusion.
