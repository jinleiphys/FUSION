# Density dependent quark mass model revisited: Thermodynamic consistency, stability windows and stellar properties

> In this work a density dependent quark model is revisited, its thermodynamic consistency checked and the stability window for absolutely stable quark matter obtained. The hypotheses of both pure quark matter with equal quark chemical potentials and stellar matter subject to chemical stability and charge neutrality are investigated. The parameters that appear in the density dependent mass and satisfy the Bodmer-Witten conjecture are then used to compute the masses and radii of strange stars. We show that the obtained values are compatible with the recently observed massive stars.

## Key claim

The density-dependent quark mass model of Xia et al. (2014) is thermodynamically consistent and can produce strange stars with masses up to 2.37 solar masses, but no single set of model parameters can simultaneously satisfy both the highest observed neutron-star masses (around 2 solar masses) and the radius constraint for a 1.4 solar-mass star (10.4–11.6 km) derived from GW170817.

## Method

The authors revisited a quark model where the masses of up, down, and strange quarks scale with baryon density via two free parameters C and D, plus the current strange-quark mass. They enforced thermodynamic consistency by verifying that the zero-pressure point coincides with the minimum of the energy per baryon. For both pure quark matter (equal chemical potentials) and stellar matter (beta-equilibrium with charge neutrality), they mapped the stability windows where three-flavor quark matter is absolutely stable (energy per baryon below 930 MeV) while two-flavor matter is unstable (above 930 MeV). They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for parameter sets within the stability windows to obtain mass-radius relations for strange stars.

## Key numbers

* Stability criterion: energy per baryon below 930 MeV for absolutely stable strange quark matter, between 930 and 939 MeV for metastable, and above 939 MeV for unstable.
* Maximum stellar mass obtained: 2.37 solar masses, with a radius of 15.17 km, for parameters C = 0.965 and sqrt(D) = 121 MeV with m_s0 = 80 MeV.
* For the parameter set C = 0.305, sqrt(D) = 142.75 MeV with m_s0 = 80 MeV, the maximum mass was 1.83 solar masses with a canonical-star radius (R_1.4) of 11.57 km.
* The highest maximum mass that still satisfied R_1.4 below 11.9 km was close to 1.8 solar masses.
* For m_s0 = 100 MeV, maximum masses ranged from 1.52 to 1.98 solar masses across the stability-window edge parameters listed.
* For m_s0 = 80 MeV, maximum masses ranged from 1.51 to 2.37 solar masses across the stability-window edge parameters listed.

## Context

This work extends the density-dependent quark mass model of Xia et al. (2014) by systematically checking thermodynamic consistency and mapping stability windows for both pure and stellar matter. It contrasts with the MIT bag model, which James et al. (2013) showed cannot produce stars as massive as 2 solar masses, and with the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model, which cannot describe absolutely stable strange quark matter as shown by seminal work and James and Veronica. The results enable future applications to protoquark stars and QCD phase-transition studies at finite temperature, and they highlight a tension between the highest observed neutron-star masses (Demorest 2010, Antoniadis 2013, Cromartie 2019) and the radius constraint from GW170817 (Capano 2020).
