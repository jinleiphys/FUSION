# Proposals for the test of the isospin invariance in the pion-nucleon interaction at low energy

> This work elaborates on former remarks of ours regarding two sets of predictions for the observables of the charge-exchange reaction $π^- p \to π^0 n$ at low energy (pion laboratory kinetic energy $T \leq 100$ MeV). The first prediction is obtained via the triangle identity from the results of fits to low-energy $π^\pm p$ elastic-scattering data, whereas the second is based on the same analysis of the combined low-energy $π^+ p$ and charge-exchange databases. Assuming the integrity of the data used in our fits (i.e., the absence of significant systematic effects in the determination of the absolute normalisation of the datasets) and the insignificance of residual effects in the electromagnetic corrections, a significant difference between these two sets of predictions may be interpreted as departure from the isospin invariance in the low-energy $πN$ interaction. We examine the sensitivity of the standard low-energy observables to the effect, and identify the kinematical regions which are promising for experimental survey. Accurate experiments under the suggested conditions will differentiate between the predictions, and thus provide an independent test of the isospin invariance in the low-energy $πN$ interaction.

# Proposals for the test of the isospin invariance in the pion-nucleon interaction at low energy

## Key claim

A significant difference between two sets of predictions for charge-exchange observables, one derived from fits to elastic-scattering data and the other from fits to combined π⁺p and charge-exchange data, may be interpreted as departure from isospin invariance in the low-energy πN interaction, but only assuming the integrity of the input data (no significant systematic effects in absolute normalisation) and the insignificance of residual electromagnetic corrections.

## Method

The authors compare two sets of predictions for the π⁻p → π⁰n charge-exchange reaction observables (differential cross section, analysing power, and total cross section) at pion laboratory kinetic energies T ≤ 100 MeV. Prediction A comes from fits to the elastic-scattering database (π⁺p and π⁻p), while prediction B comes from fits to the combined π⁺p and charge-exchange database. At each point on a (T,θ) grid, one million Monte-Carlo events are generated using the fitted parameters of the ETH model and the Hessian matrix of each fit, and the two predictions are compared using the absolute normalised difference d and the symmetrised relative difference w.

## Key numbers

- The average normalisation uncertainty of π⁻p charge-exchange differential cross section experiments at low energy is about 5.8 %.
- At the 2.5σ significance level, an experiment with the current average normalisation uncertainty can differentiate between predictions that are at least 2.5 × 5.8 % apart, i.e., more than about 15 % apart.
- The s- and p-wave interference minimum in the π⁻p charge-exchange differential cross section at θ=0° is placed at T = 42.21(11) MeV by prediction A and at T = 44.33(14) MeV by prediction B, a difference of about 2 MeV.
- For the total cross section, the symmetrised relative difference w ranges between about 13.4 % (at T = 10 MeV) and 3.0 % (at T = 100 MeV).
- To differentiate between predictions using total cross section measurements, the required normalisation uncertainty is no more than about 5 % at T ≈ 10 MeV, 4.4 % at T = 40 MeV, and 1.4 % at T = 95 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous analyses (Matsinos 1997a, 1997b, 2006, 2013) and the PSAUpdate (2019) that established isospin breaking at the 5–10 % level in the scattering amplitude, contrary to heavy-baryon Chiral-Perturbation Theory calculations (Hoferichter 2010) that place isospin-breaking effects around the 1 % level. The paper identifies specific kinematical regions, forward scattering (θ ≲ 30°) near the s- and p-wave interference minimum for differential cross section, and a triangular domain below 40 MeV for analysing power, where new experiments could reliably differentiate between the two sets of predictions and thus provide an independent test of isospin invariance. The authors recommend repeating the Fitzgerald (1986) experiment and note that very few low-energy analysing power measurements exist (Stasko 1993, Gaulard 1999), all in kinematical regions unpromising for this test.
