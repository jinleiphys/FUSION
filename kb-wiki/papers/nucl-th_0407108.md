# The method of multiple internal reflections in a description of tunneling evolution of nonrelativistic particles and photons

> A non-stationary method for tunneling description of non-relativistic particles and photons through a barrier on the basis of consideration of the multiple internal reflections of vawe packets in relation of barrier boundaries is presented. The method is described in details and proved in the case of the one-dimentional tunneling of the particle through the rectangular barrier. For problems of the tunneling of the particle through the spherically symmetric barrier and of the photon through the one-dimensional barrier the amplitudes of transmitted and reflected wave packets in relation to the barrier, times of the tunneling and the reflection are found using of the method. Hartman's and Fletcher's effect is analysed.

## Key claim

The paper presents a non-stationary method for describing tunneling that uses multiple internal reflections of wave packets at barrier boundaries, claiming it can separate transmitted and reflected components in cases where stationary methods cannot (such as spherically symmetric barriers), but the method is explicitly limited to sub-barrier energies by inserting a step function that excludes above-barrier components from the wave packet.

## Method

The authors construct time-dependent wave packets for a nonrelativistic particle by integrating stationary wave functions multiplied by a Gaussian weight amplitude over energy, then insert a theta-function to restrict to energies below the barrier height. They decompose the tunneling process into sequential steps where at each barrier boundary the incident wave packet splits into transmitted and reflected components, using only the evanescent (decaying) exponential in the barrier region for forward propagation and the anti-evanescent (growing) exponential for backward propagation, with coefficients determined by continuity conditions at each boundary. Recurrence relations for the coefficients are derived and summed to infinite series, which are shown to converge to the standard stationary transmission and reflection coefficients.

## Key numbers

- For the one-dimensional rectangular barrier, the series sum for the transmission coefficient is given by $\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} A_T^n = \frac{i4k \xi e^{-\xi a-ika}}{F_{sub}}$, where $F_{sub} = (k^2 - \xi^2)(1 - e^{-2\xi a}) + 2ik\xi(1 + e^{-2\xi a})$, $k = \frac{1}{\hbar}\sqrt{2mE}$, $\xi = \frac{1}{\hbar}\sqrt{2m(V_1-E)}$, and $a$ is the barrier width.
- The series sum for the reflection coefficient is $\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} A_R^n = \frac{k_0^2 (1 - e^{-2\xi a})}{F_{sub}}$, where $k_0^2 = k^2 + \xi^2 = \frac{2mV_1}{\hbar^2}$.
- The property $|\sum A_T^n|^2 + |\sum A_R^n|^2 = 1$ is satisfied for the summed series.
- For the spherically symmetric rectangular barrier with $l=0$, the radial potential has three regions: region I ($r < R_1$) with depth $-V_0$, region II ($R_1 < r < R_2$) with height $V_1$, and region III ($r > R_2$) with zero potential.

## Context

This work extends the stationary multiple-internal-reflection approach previously used for above-barrier propagation by Fermor (1966), McVoy (1967), and Anderson (1989) to the non-stationary tunneling regime, and builds on the time-analysis framework developed by Olkhovsky (1992, 1997). The method enables separation of transmitted and reflected wave packets in spherically symmetric scattering problems where stationary methods cannot distinguish them, and allows analysis of Hartman's and Fletcher's effect (apparent superluminal tunneling times). The authors state this is the first time the spherically symmetric problem has been treated with this approach.
