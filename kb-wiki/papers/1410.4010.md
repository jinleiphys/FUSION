# Assessing the accuracy of Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations by use of mass relations

> The accuracy of three different sets of Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov calculations of nuclear binding energies is systematically evaluated. To emphasize minor fluctuations, a second order, four-point mass relation, which almost completely eliminates smooth aspects of the binding energy, is introduced. Applying this mass relation yields more scattered results for the calculated binding energies. By examining the Gaussian distributions of the non-smooth aspects which remain, structural differences can be detected between measured and calculated binding energies. Substructures in regions of rapidly changing deformation, specifically around $(N,Z)=(60,40)$ and $(90,60)$, are clearly seen for the measured values, but are missing from the calculations. A similar three-point mass relation is used to emphasize odd-even effects. A clear decrease with neutron excess is seen continuing outside the experimentally known region for the calculations.

## Key claim

The paper systematically evaluates the accuracy of three Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) nuclear binding energy calculations, finding that while the HFB27 model agrees with experimental binding energies to within a root-mean-square error of 0.65 MeV, all three models fail to reproduce substructures in regions of rapidly changing deformation around (N,Z) = (60,40) and (90,60) that are clearly present in measured values.

## Method

The authors apply second-order four-point mass relations (Δ₂ₙ and Δ₂ₚ) to measured and calculated nuclear binding energies to cancel smooth, systematic effects and isolate minor fluctuations. They also use a three-point mass relation to extract odd-even staggering effects, comparing results from three HFB model sets (HFB27, SkP, and D1SG) against the AME12 experimental compilation. The residual fluctuations after applying the mass relations are analyzed as Gaussian distributions to detect structural differences between theory and experiment.

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square error between HFB27 calculations and experimental binding energies: 0.65 MeV
- Root-mean-square error between D1SG calculations and experimental binding energies: 4.72 MeV
- Root-mean-square error between SkP calculations and experimental binding energies: 3.17 MeV
- Root-mean-square error between HFB27 and D1SG calculations: 14.57 MeV
- Root-mean-square error between HFB27 and SkP calculations: 3.58 MeV
- Root-mean-square error between SkP and D1SG calculations: 13.09 MeV
- Root-mean-square error of Δ₂ₙ applied to HFB27 versus zero inside experimental region: 0.36 MeV
- Root-mean-square error of Δ₂ₙ applied to experimental data versus zero inside experimental region: 0.29 MeV
- Root-mean-square error of Δ₂ₙ applied to HFB27 versus experimental data: 0.20 MeV
- Root-mean-square error of Δ₂ₙ applied to D1SG versus experimental data: 0.41 MeV
- Root-mean-square error of Δ₂ₙ applied to SkP versus experimental data: 0.27 MeV
- Maximum deviation of Δ₂ₙ for experimental data: -5 MeV (for very light nuclei)
- Largest deviation for experimental nuclei with A > 50: approximately 1.5 MeV at double magic ¹³²Sn
- Number of outliers (|Δ₂ₙ| > 2 MeV) for HFB27: 57 nuclei
- Number of outliers (|Δ₂ₙ| > 2 MeV) for D1SG: 23 nuclei, deviating up to 3 MeV
- Number of outliers (|Δ₂ₙ| > 2 MeV) for SkP: 8 nuclei, deviating up to -11 MeV
- Number of outliers (|Δ₂ₙ| > 1.5 MeV) for experimental data: 27 nuclei

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Hove et al. (2013) that applied mass relations to experimental binding energies, now testing three HFB model sets (HFB27 from Goriely et al. (2010), SkP from Dobaczewski et al. (2003), and D1SG from Bertsch et al. (2010)) against the AME12 experimental compilation. The mass relations used are related to the Garvey-Kelson relations (Garvey and Kelson, 1966) and earlier work by Jensen et al. (1984). The results enable improved assessment of where microscopic mean-field models fail to capture local structural features, particularly in regions of rapid deformation change, and suggest that mass relations could be used to improve microscopic models as proposed by Barea et al. (2008). The finding that odd-even effects decrease with neutron excess continues outside the experimentally known region for the HFB27 calculations, providing predictions for nuclei beyond current experimental reach.
