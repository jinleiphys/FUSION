# On calculating response functions via their Lorentz integral transforms

> The accuracy of reconstruction of a response function from its Lorentz integral transform is studied in an exactly solvable model. An inversion procedure is elaborated in detail and features of the procedure are studied. Unlike results in the literature pertaining to the same model, the response function is reconstructed from its Lorentz integral transform with rather high accuracy.

# On calculating response functions via their Lorentz integral transforms

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that, contrary to prior results in the literature (Suzuki, 2015) using the same exactly solvable model, the response function can be reconstructed from its Lorentz integral transform (LIT) with rather high accuracy, provided that the inversion procedure is carefully elaborated and the basis set for the inversion is chosen appropriately, in particular, by modifying the low-energy behavior factor in the basis functions to avoid non-physical oscillations.

## Method

The authors study a one-dimensional model of three-particle photodisintegration with a hypercentral potential, equivalent to a one-body problem with orbital momenta 3/2 (bound state) and 5/2 (continuum). They solve the bound-state Schrödinger equation, the continuum Schrödinger equation, and the inhomogeneous equation for the LIT using expansions over radial oscillator functions with up to 300 basis functions. The response function is computed both directly from the continuum wave functions and by inverting the LIT via a least-squares fit using exponential basis sets with a fall-off parameter, employing either the standard basis set \(f_n(E;\alpha)=E^3 e^{-\alpha E/n}\) or modified sets with higher powers of \(E\).

## Key numbers

- Bound state energy: \(E_0 = -3.492628451703560\) MeV (converged with \(N_{max}=300\))
- Bound state radius: \(\langle \rho^2\rangle^{1/2} = 3.18326851163147\) fm (converged with \(N_{max}=300\))
- Sum rule check: \(\int_0^\infty dE\, r(E) = \langle\rho^2\rangle = 10.13\) fm\(^2\); LIT sum rule integration gives 10.15 fm\(^2\) at \(\sigma_I=5\) MeV
- Phase shift at \(E=1\) MeV: \(9.9603273 \times 10^{-2}\) degrees (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Phase shift at \(E=10\) MeV: \(68.9249289\) degrees (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Phase shift at \(E=20\) MeV: \(96.523603\) degrees (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Direct response at \(E=1\) MeV: \(4.434102 \times 10^{-2}\) fm\(^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Direct response at \(E=8\) MeV: \(1.3210\) fm\(^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Direct response at \(E=20\) MeV: \(4.834 \times 10^{-2}\) fm\(^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (converged with \(N_{max}=250\))
- Inversion at \(\sigma_I=5\) MeV with \(N=45\) basis functions gives \(r(1\ \text{MeV}) = 0.009\) fm\(^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (exact: 0.044); at \(E=4\) MeV and above, two-digit accuracy is achieved
- Inversion at \(\sigma_I=2.5\) MeV with \(N=23-28\) basis functions gives \(r(1\ \text{MeV}) = 0.043-0.044\) fm\(^2\) MeV\(^{-1}\) (exact: 0.044); stable results at all energies shown
- Modified basis with \(f_n(E;\alpha)=E^6 e^{-\alpha E/n}\) at \(\sigma_I=2.5\) MeV and \(N=14-16\) yields very good agreement with true response at low energies

## Context

This work extends the Lorentz integral transform (LIT) method introduced by Efros, Leidemann, and Orlandini (1994) for computing response functions of quantum many-body systems. It directly contradicts the negative results of Suzuki (2015), who used the same model and found that the LIT approach could not accurately reconstruct the response function. The authors show that the discrepancy arises from differences in both the calculated LIT input and the inversion procedure, particularly the choice of the \(\sigma_R\) fitting range and the weight function. The improved inversion techniques demonstrated here enable more reliable extraction of response functions from LIT calculations in few-body and many-body applications, though the authors note that in typical many-body calculations the transform precision is considerably lower than in this model study.
