# Chiral symmetry and effective field theories for hadronic, nuclear and stellar matter

> Chiral symmetry, first entering in nuclear physics in the 1970's for which Gerry Brown played a seminal role, has led to a stunningly successful framework for describing strongly-correlated nuclear dynamics both in finite and infinite systems. We review how the early germinal idea, conceived with the soft-pion theorems in the pre-QCD era, has evolved into a highly predictive theoretical framework for nuclear physics, aptly assessed by Steven Weinberg: "it (chiral effective field theory) allows one to show in a fairly convincing way that what they (nuclear physicists) have been doing all along... is the correct first step in a consistent approximation scheme." Our review recounts both how the theory presently fares in confronting Nature and how one can understand its extremely intricate workings in terms of the multifaceted aspects of chiral symmetry, namely, chiral perturbation theory, skyrmions, Landau Fermi-liquid theory, the Cheshire cat phenomenon, and hidden local and mended symmetries.

## Key claim

Chiral effective field theory provides a systematically improvable framework for nuclear physics that is equivalent to low-energy QCD, but its full predictive power relies on incorporating hidden local symmetry and scale symmetry to describe dense matter, particularly in neutron star cores, where standard chiral perturbation theory may break down.

## Method

The authors review the historical development and modern applications of chiral effective field theory (ChEFT) for hadronic, nuclear, and stellar matter, covering chiral perturbation theory for pions and nucleons, the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio model as a bridge between QCD and hadronic degrees of freedom, hidden local symmetry for vector mesons, skyrmion models for baryons, and the Cheshire Cat principle connecting quark and hadron descriptions. They discuss how these frameworks are applied to nuclear few- and many-body systems, dense matter, and neutron star physics, with emphasis on the role of chiral symmetry breaking patterns and the Weinberg folk theorem as the foundational principle.

## Key numbers

* Pion decay constant: f_pi = 92.4 MeV, defined by the matrix element connecting the pion state with the QCD vacuum via the isovector axial vector current.
* Pion mass: m_pi = 138 MeV, reflecting explicit chiral symmetry breaking by small quark masses with m_pi^2 proportional to m_q.
* Chiral symmetry breaking scale: Lambda_chi = 4 pi f_pi = 1 GeV, the energy scale associated with spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in QCD.
* Nucleon sigma term: sigma_N = (45 +/- 8) MeV, deduced from low-energy pion-nucleon data, measuring the contribution of non-vanishing quark mass to the nucleon mass.
* Chiral condensate: <0|bar q q|0> = -(0.36 GeV)^3 at renormalization scale 2 GeV, with average quark mass (1/2)(m_u + m_d) = 3.5 MeV.
* Critical coupling for spontaneous chiral symmetry breaking in the NJL model: G_crit = 10 GeV^{-2}, above which a non-zero constituent quark mass develops dynamically.

## Context

This review extends and synthesizes work by Brown (1979) on chiral symmetry in the nucleon-nucleon interaction, Weinberg (1979, early 1990s) on chiral effective field theory for nuclear physics, and Witten (1979) on large-N QCD and skyrmions. It builds on the earlier review by Holt, Kaiser, and Weise (hkwreview) and provides a unified perspective connecting chiral perturbation theory, skyrmion models, Landau Fermi-liquid theory, hidden local symmetry, and mended symmetries. The work enables systematic calculations of nuclear interactions and dense matter properties, particularly for neutron star cores, and points toward extending chiral effective field theory beyond its current density limits through hidden local symmetry and scale symmetry frameworks.
