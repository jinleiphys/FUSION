# How long can an atomic nucleus remain standing ? -- a fundamental quantum question

> The shape of an object is of fundamental interest and high importance, but is not a straightforward subject if the object is on quantum scale. We here discuss how a shaped micro-object can be looked at within quantum mechanics. For this purpose, atomic nuclei are suitable, because they are tiny shaped objects. The majority of atomic nuclei are shaped like ellipsoids. Although an ellipsoid is oriented in a direction classically, such a nucleus is pointing in all directions with certain probabilities in quantum eigenstates, fulfilling rotational symmetry. This makes the direct observation of shapes formidably difficult. Here, we show, including examples, that the ellipsoidal nucleus is basically standing in a fixed direction for finite time \sim some 10^{-23} sec, as a robust consequence of time-dependent Schrodinger equation in quantum mechanics and a well-known rotational feature of nuclei. This consequence not only provides Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collisions9 with experimental feasibilities, but also leads to a deeper general understanding of stationary states with restored broken symmetry: time-dependent symmetry-breaking (e.g., ellipsoid shape) properties arise from stationary states with symmetry. This work depicts direct relevance to fusion and fission reactions in terms of time evolution, including applications to the synthesis of superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The paper claims that an ellipsoidal atomic nucleus remains oriented in a fixed direction for a finite time on the order of some 10⁻²³ seconds, as a consequence of the time-dependent Schrödinger equation and the rotational structure of nuclei, but this standing time is 100–1000 times shorter than earlier estimates cited in the context of relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments.

## Method

The authors construct the intrinsic state φᵢ representing an ellipsoidal nucleus and expand it as a superposition of angular-momentum-projected eigenstates ψⱼ of the rotational band, with amplitudes fⱼ obtained from state-of-the-art Configuration Interaction (CI) calculations for ¹⁶⁶Er and ¹⁵⁴Sm. They then compute the time-dependent overlap r(t) = Σⱼ |fⱼ|² exp(−i(Eⱼ−Ē)t/ℏ) and define the standing time τₛ as the time at which |r(t)|² drops below a chosen threshold (rₘᵢₙ = 0.96, corresponding to |r| = 0.98).

## Key numbers

* Standing time for ¹⁶⁶Er: 5 × 10⁻²³ seconds (for the strict criterion |r|² = 0.96).
* Standing time for ¹⁵⁴Sm: 3 × 10⁻²³ seconds (for the same strict criterion).
* The overlap probability |r(t)|² for ¹⁶⁶Er remains above 0.9 until 0.7 × 10⁻²² seconds.
* The estimated time for completing tunneling in fusion of ¹⁶O + ¹⁵⁴Sm is about 6 × 10⁻²² seconds, based on a WKB framework with a standard barrier curvature of about 3.5 MeV.
* The earlier estimate cited from Nakatsukasa (2016) gave 3 × (10⁻²¹–10⁻²⁰) seconds, which is 100–1000 times larger than the present result.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of rotational symmetry restoration in deformed nuclei, building on the angular-momentum projection formalism from Ring and Schuck (1980) and the CI calculations of Otsuka (2025). It directly addresses a controversy raised by Doba (2025) regarding the interpretation of nuclear orientation timescales in relativistic heavy-ion collision experiments (STAR Collaboration, 2024). The results provide a theoretical foundation for fusion reaction studies by Hagino and Takigawa (2012, 2022), showing that the intrinsic orientation of a deformed nucleus persists long enough to influence the tunneling probability in sub-barrier fusion, and offer insights into fission timescales and the synthesis of superheavy elements.
