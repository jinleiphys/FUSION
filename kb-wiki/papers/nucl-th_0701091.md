# Radiative orbital electron capture by the atomic nucleus

> The rate for the photon emission accompanying orbital 1S electron capture by the atomic nucleus is recalculated. While a photon can be emitted by the electron or by the nucleus, the use of the length gauge significantly suppresses the nuclear contribution. Our calculations resolve the long standing discrepancy of theoretical predictions with experimental data for $ΔJ=2$ forbidden transitions. We illustrate the results by comparison with the data established experimentally for the first forbidden unique decays of $^{41}$Ca and $^{204}$Tl.

## Key claim

The long-standing discrepancy between theoretical predictions and experimental data for ΔJ=2 forbidden transitions in radiative electron capture is resolved by using the length gauge for the emitted photon, which suppresses the nuclear contribution and makes the calculations finite, contrary to prior work that used the Coulomb (velocity) gauge and produced divergent or incorrect results.

## Method

The authors recalculate the rate of photon emission accompanying 1S orbital electron capture using the length gauge for the electromagnetic field, employing the Dirac-Coulomb Green's function and decomposing the transition rate into a product of a photon emission term and a nuclear transition term. They derive explicit formulae for the dimensionless shape factor R for allowed, first forbidden non-unique, and first forbidden unique transitions, and compare their results to experimental data for 41Ca and 204Tl.

## Key numbers

- For 41Ca (Q_EC = 421.3 keV, T_1/2 = 10^5 years), the Zon and Rapoport model underestimates the REC spectrum intensity by a factor of about 6, while the authors' calculations agree with experiment.
- For 204Tl (Q_EC = 346.5 keV, T_1/2 = 3.78 years), the Zon and Rapoport model overestimates the spectrum by a factor of about 4, while the authors' results agree with experiment.
- The nonrelativistic limit of the shape factor R for allowed transitions equals 1, verified by explicit calculations.
- In the Coulomb-free limit (Z → 0), the R^(2) function for first forbidden unique transitions equals 1 + 1/k + 2/k^2, which differs from Zon's result where the corresponding function equals 1.
- The Fermi constant used is G_F = 1.16639(1) × 10^(-5) GeV^(-2) and λ = 1.26992(69).

## Context

This work extends the theory of radiative electron capture originally developed by Morrison and Schiff (neglecting Coulomb field), and later advanced by Glauber and Martin (including Coulomb field but limited to allowed decays with ΔJ=0,1). It corrects the general theory extended to any order of forbiddenness by Zon and Rapoport, which failed to describe experimental data for first forbidden unique transitions in 41Ca and 204Tl. The results contradict the claim by Kalinowski et al. that nuclear detour transitions fully account for the missing intensity in 41Ca, and instead show that proper use of the length gauge resolves the discrepancy without invoking nuclear contributions. The work enables accurate predictions for other first forbidden unique transitions such as 81Kr, for which an experiment is underway.
