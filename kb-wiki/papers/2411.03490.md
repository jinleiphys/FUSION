# Assessing the impact of uniform rotation on the structure of neutron stars

> Driven by recent laboratory experiments and astronomical observations, significant advances have deepened our understanding of neutron-star physics. NICER's Pulse Profile Modeling has refined our knowledge of neutron star masses and radii, while gravitational-wave detections have revealed key insights into the structure of neutron stars. Particularly relevant is the extraction of the tidal deformability by the LIGO-Virgo collaboration and the most recent determination of stellar radii by NICER, both suggesting a relatively soft equation of state (EOS) at intermediate densities. Additionally, measurements from the PREX collaboration and from pulsar timing suggest instead that the EOS is stiff in the vicinity of saturation density and at the highest densities accessible to date. But how stiff can the EOS be at these very high densities? Recent events featuring compact objects near the "lower mass gap" have raised questions about the existence of very massive neutron stars. Motivated by this finding and in light of new refinements to theoretical models, we explore the possibility that these massive objects may indeed be rapidly rotating neutron stars. We explore how rotation affects both the maximum neutron star mass and their associated radii, and discuss the implications they may have on the equation of state.

## Key claim

The paper explores whether compact objects near the "lower mass gap" (between about 2.5 and 5 solar masses) detected by LIGO-Virgo-Kagra could be rapidly rotating neutron stars rather than black holes, concluding that under the BigApple model there is a 28% probability that the primary object in GW230529 (mass 3.6 solar masses) is a neutron star at the mass-shedding limit, but that static configurations without rotation can also accommodate very massive neutron stars.

## Method

The authors used covariant density functional theory with three energy density functionals (BigApple, FSUGarnet, FSUGold2) calibrated to finite nuclei and neutron star properties to generate equations of state. These were input into the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations for static configurations and into two publicly available codes (RNS and LORENE) to solve Einstein's equations for uniformly rotating neutron stars, computing mass-radius relations and maximum masses at the Kepler (mass-shedding) frequency.

## Key numbers

- BigApple model: maximum mass of static configuration M_TOV = 2.596 solar masses, equatorial radius R_TOV = 12.590 km; at Kepler frequency (1.481 kHz): M_max = 3.208 solar masses (24% increase), R_max = 16.727 km (33% increase)
- FSUGarnet model: M_TOV = 2.066 solar masses, R_TOV = 11.690 km; at Kepler frequency (1.409 kHz): M_max = 2.519 solar masses (22% increase), R_max = 16.048 km (37% increase)
- FSUGold2 model: M_TOV = 2.073 solar masses, R_TOV = 12.151 km; at Kepler frequency (1.321 kHz): M_max = 2.463 solar masses (19% increase), R_max = 16.609 km (37% increase)
- BigApple predictions for a 1.4 solar mass neutron star: radius R_1.4 = 12.960 km, tidal deformability Lambda_1.4 = 717.3
- The ratio of maximum rotating to static mass is R = 1.255 (+0.047, -0.040) from Musolino, Ecker, and Rezzolla (2023)
- For a model with M_TOV = 2.60 solar masses, the probability that the primary object in GW230529 is a neutron star is 28%
- For a model with M_TOV = 2.80 solar masses, the probability increases to 42%
- The equatorial radius of a low-mass pulsar rotating at 716 Hz can increase by more than 2 km compared to the non-rotating case

## Context

This work extends the earlier analysis by Fattoyev (2020) that explored whether the 2.6 solar mass secondary object in GW190814 could be a neutron star, which concluded that stiffening the equation of state to support such masses was inconsistent with heavy-ion collision constraints from Danielewicz, Lacey, and Lynch (2002) and the low tidal deformability from GW170817. The present paper revisits this question because a Bayesian analysis by Oliinychenko (2022) of STAR flow data favors a hard equation of state at 2-3 times saturation density, and because re-analyses of GW170817 tidal parameters by Gamba (2020) identified systematic errors in waveform approximants. The results enable future work to constrain the nuclear equation of state at high densities using next-generation gravitational wave detectors and improved Pulse Profile Modeling from NICER.
