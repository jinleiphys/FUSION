# Deep learning approaches for nuclear binding energy prediction: a comparative study of RNN, GRU and LSTM Models

> This study investigates the application of deep learning models-recurrent neural networks, gated recurrent units, and long short-term memory networks-for predicting nuclear binding energies. Utilizing data from the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020), we incorporate key nuclear structure features, including proton and neutron numbers, as well as additional terms from the liquid drop model and shell effects. Our comparative analysis demonstrates that the gated recurrent units model achieves the lowest root-mean-square error (σRMSE) of 0.326 MeV, surpassing traditional regression-based approaches. To assess model reliability, we validate predictions using the GarveyKelson relations, obtaining an error of 0.202 MeV, and further test extrapolation capabilities using the WS, WS3, and WS4 models. The extrapolation analysis confirms the robustness of our approach, particularly in predicting binding energies for nuclei near the driplines. These results highlight the effectiveness of deep learning in nuclear BE predictions, highlighting its potential to enhance the accuracy and reliability of theoretical nuclear models.

## Key claim

The gated recurrent unit (GRU) model achieves the lowest root-mean-square error of 0.326 MeV for nuclear binding energy prediction when using 11 input features and the tanh activation function, outperforming both standard RNN and LSTM architectures; however, this best performance is conditional on the specific feature set and activation function, as GRU with relu activation yields substantially higher errors around 0.56 MeV.

## Method

The authors trained simple RNN, GRU, and LSTM models on nuclear binding energy data from the Atomic Mass Evaluation (AME2020) for 3100 nuclei with Z,N >= 8, using three progressively expanded feature sets derived from the Bethe-Weizsacker liquid drop formula: 3 features (N, Z, A), 7 features (adding A^{2/3}, Z(Z-1)/A^{1/3}, (N-Z)^2/A, and pairing term delta_p), and 11 features (further adding valence nucleon numbers V_N and V_Z, and parity indicators Z_eo and N_eo). Models were trained with both tanh and relu activation functions using the Adam optimizer and mean squared error loss, with data splits of 90/5/5, 80/10/10, and 70/15/15 percent for training/validation/testing. Validation was performed using Garvey-Kelson relations, and extrapolation was tested against the WS, WS3, and WS4 models covering 11,248 to 11,879 nuclei.

## Key numbers

- GRU11 with tanh activation achieved the lowest training RMSE of 0.326 MeV (from Table V, 90/5/5 split, training value 0.3294 MeV rounded to 0.326 in abstract)
- GRU11 with tanh achieved validation RMSE of 0.3791 MeV and testing RMSE of 0.4591 MeV (90/5/5 split)
- Garvey-Kelson validation of GRU predictions yielded an error of 0.202 MeV
- Traditional least-squares fit of the Bethe-Weizsacker formula to 3264 nuclei gave RMSE of 3.29 MeV
- GRU7 with tanh achieved testing RMSE of 0.5473 MeV (90/5/5 split), approximately 50% reduction from GRU3
- GRU3 with tanh achieved testing RMSE of 1.1476 MeV (90/5/5 split)
- LSTM3 with relu activation showed worst performance with testing RMSE above 1.6 MeV in some cases

## Context

This work extends the application of deep learning to nuclear binding energy prediction, building on prior neural network approaches for nuclear properties such as those by Liu, Niu, Zhao, and others, as well as kernel ridge regression methods by Wu and collaborators. The study improves upon traditional global models like the liquid drop model and Bethe-Weizsacker formula (which yield 3.29 MeV RMSE with least-squares fitting) and local models like the Garvey-Kelson relations (which achieve approximately 0.2 MeV accuracy in known regions). The results demonstrate that GRU architectures with engineered nuclear features can approach the accuracy of local models while offering extrapolation capabilities to unmeasured regions near the driplines, potentially enabling predictions for the more than 11,000 nuclei that require extrapolation.
