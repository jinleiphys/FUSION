# Detecting non-relativistic cosmic neutrinos by capture on tritium: phenomenology and physics potential

> We study the physics potential of the detection of the Cosmic Neutrino Background via neutrino capture on tritium, taking the proposed PTOLEMY experiment as a case study. With the projected energy resolution of $Δ\sim$ 0.15 eV, the experiment will be sensitive to neutrino masses with degenerate spectrum, $m_1 \simeq m_2 \simeq m_3 = m_ν\gtrsim 0.1$ eV. These neutrinos are non-relativistic today; detecting them would be a unique opportunity to probe this unexplored kinematical regime. The signature of neutrino capture is a peak in the electron spectrum that is displaced by $2 m_ν$ above the beta decay endpoint. The signal would exceed the background from beta decay if the energy resolution is $Δ\lesssim 0.7 m_ν$. Interestingly, the total capture rate depends on the origin of the neutrino mass, being $Γ^{\rm D} \simeq 4$ and $Γ^{\rm M} \simeq 8$ events per year (for a 100 g tritium target) for unclustered Dirac and Majorana neutrinos, respectively. An enhancement of the rate of up to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ is expected due to gravitational clustering, with the unique potential to probe the local overdensity of neutrinos. Turning to more exotic neutrino physics, PTOLEMY could be sensitive to a lepton asymmetry, and reveal the eV-scale sterile neutrino that is favored by short baseline oscillation searches. The experiment would also be sensitive to a neutrino lifetime on the order of the age of the universe and break the degeneracy between neutrino mass and lifetime which affects existing bounds.

## Key claim

The detection of the Cosmic Neutrino Background via neutrino capture on tritium, using the proposed PTOLEMY experiment as a case study, is feasible only if the neutrino mass spectrum is degenerate with $m_1 \simeq m_2 \simeq m_3 = m_\nu \gtrsim 0.1$ eV, given the projected energy resolution of $\Delta \sim 0.15$ eV; the signal exceeds the beta decay background only when the energy resolution satisfies $\Delta \lesssim 0.7 m_\nu$.

## Method

The authors calculated the neutrino capture cross section on tritium using the four-fermion interaction approximation, deriving the squared matrix element with explicit spin-dependent factors for left-helical and right-helical neutrinos. They then computed the capture rate for a 100 g tritium target by integrating over the neutrino momentum distribution from the cosmic neutrino background, accounting for the thermal history of neutrinos from freeze-out to the present epoch, including redshift, quantum decoherence into mass eigenstates, and helicity conservation during free streaming. The signal-to-background ratio was evaluated by modeling the beta decay spectrum as the dominant background and convolving both signal and background with a Gaussian energy resolution function of width $\Delta$.

## Key numbers

- Projected energy resolution of PTOLEMY: $\Delta \sim 0.15$ eV
- Neutrino mass threshold for detection: $m_\nu \gtrsim 0.1$ eV (degenerate spectrum)
- Condition for signal to exceed beta decay background: $\Delta \lesssim 0.7 m_\nu$
- Capture rate for unclustered Dirac neutrinos: $\Gamma^{\rm D} \simeq 4$ events per year (100 g tritium target)
- Capture rate for unclustered Majorana neutrinos: $\Gamma^{\rm M} \simeq 8$ events per year (100 g tritium target)
- Current cosmic neutrino background number density: $n_0 \approx 56$ cm$^{-3}$ per degree of freedom, or $6 n_0 \approx 336$ cm$^{-3}$ total
- Current neutrino temperature: $T_\nu = 0.168$ meV
- Root mean square neutrino momentum today: $\overline{p}_0 \approx 0.603$ meV
- Tritium beta decay Q-value: $Q^0 \approx 18.6$ keV
- Velocity-multiplied capture cross section: $\bar{\sigma} \simeq 3.834 \times 10^{-45}$ cm$^2$

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of cosmic neutrino background detection via neutrino capture on beta-decaying nuclei, originally proposed by Weinberg (1962) and later refined for tritium targets by Cocco et al. (2007), Lazauskas et al. (2007), Blennow et al. (2008), Li et al. (2010), and Faessler et al. (2011). The paper's novel contributions include a detailed treatment of the Dirac versus Majorana nature of neutrinos (finding a factor of 2 difference in capture rate), a more thorough analysis of the beta decay background, and the exploration of gravitational clustering effects that could enhance the rate by up to $\mathcal{O}(1)$. The results enable future experimental searches with PTOLEMY to probe not only standard neutrino properties but also exotic physics such as lepton asymmetry, eV-scale sterile neutrinos favored by short baseline oscillation searches, and neutrino lifetimes on the order of the age of the universe.
