# Reducing the complexity of finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo

> The auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) method is a powerful and widely used technique for ground-state and finite-temperature simulations of quantum many-body systems. We introduce several algorithmic improvements for finite-temperature AFMC calculations of dilute fermionic systems that reduce the computational complexity of most parts of the algorithm. This is principally achieved by reducing the number of single-particle states that contribute at each configuration of the auxiliary fields to a number that is of the order of the number of fermions. Our methods are applicable for both the canonical and grand-canonical ensembles. We demonstrate the reduced computational complexity of the methods for the homogeneous unitary Fermi gas.

## Key claim

The authors introduce algorithmic improvements for finite-temperature auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFMC) that reduce the computational complexity of most parts of the algorithm by identifying and removing mostly unoccupied single-particle states from the calculation, achieving a reduction in the effective single-particle space dimension from \(N_s\) to \(N_{\rm occ}\) (of order the particle number \(N\)), with a controlled error determined by a truncation parameter \(\varepsilon\).

## Method

The method uses a QDR decomposition of the one-body propagator matrix \(U = Q D R\) for each auxiliary-field configuration, then rescales the diagonal matrix \(D\) by a chemical potential factor so that the Fermi energy lies near \(d_k \approx 1\). The diagonal entries are sorted by their contribution to the Frobenius norm of the discarded part, and the smallest entries are omitted when their total contribution falls below a threshold \(\varepsilon\), reducing the rank to \(N_{\rm occ}\). Observables in both grand-canonical and canonical ensembles are then computed using the reduced-rank propagator \(U_{\rm occ} = Q D_{\rm occ} R\), with the canonical ensemble handled via a discrete Fourier transform over \(N_{\rm occ}\) quadrature points. The method is demonstrated for the homogeneous unitary Fermi gas on a cubic lattice.

## Key numbers

- The number of significantly occupied states \(N_{\rm occ}\) is much smaller than \(N_s = N_L^3\) for the unitary Fermi gas with \(N = 66\) particles (spin-balanced, \(N_\uparrow = N_\downarrow = 33\)) in the temperature range \(0.05\,T_F \lesssim T \lesssim 0.25\,T_F\), and is almost independent of lattice size.
- The truncation parameter used to determine \(N_{\rm occ}\) was \(\varepsilon = 10^{-6}\).
- The speedup of matrix diagonalizations (time ratio using complete model space vs. reduced model space) reaches multiple orders of magnitude for larger lattices and lower temperatures, as shown for lattice sizes up to \(N_L^3\) at \(T/T_F = 0.10\).
- The total time per sample (including all operations) shows a speedup of multiple orders of magnitude for larger lattices and lower temperatures, with wall-clock times shown at \(T/T_F = 0.10\) for various lattice sizes.

## Context

This work extends the finite-temperature AFMC method for fermionic systems, building on the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation and particle-number projection techniques of Ormand (1994) and Gilbreth (2015). The model-space truncation approach enables otherwise impractical calculations for systems where \(N_s \gg N\), such as precision thermodynamic studies of the unitary Fermi gas on the lattice, including the pseudogap phenomenon and continuum-limit observables previously reported by Jensen (2020) and Jensen (review). The method is applicable to both canonical and grand-canonical ensembles and reduces the computational scaling from \(O(N_s^3)\) to below \(O(N_s^3)\) for most parts of the algorithm, with the QR decomposition for numerical stabilization remaining the dominant cost.
