# Resonance width distribution in RMT: Weak coupling regime beyond Porter-Thomas

> We employ the random matrix theory (RMT) framework to revisit the distribution of resonance widths in quantum chaotic systems weakly coupled to the continuum via a finite number M of open channels. In contrast to the standard first-order perturbation theory treatment we do not a priory assume the resonance widths being small compared to the mean level spacing. We show that to the leading order in weak coupling the perturbative $χ^2_M$ distribution of the resonance widths (in particular, the Porter-Thomas distribution at M=1) should be corrected by a factor related to a certain average of the ratio of square roots of the characteristic polynomial ("spectral determinant") of the underlying RMT Hamiltonian. A simple single-channel expression is obtained that properly approximates the width distribution also at large resonance overlap, where the Porter-Thomas result is no longer applicable.

# Resonance width distribution in RMT: Weak coupling regime beyond Porter-Thomas

## Key claim

For quantum chaotic systems weakly coupled to the continuum via a finite number M of open channels, the standard perturbative χ²_M distribution of resonance widths (including the Porter-Thomas distribution at M=1) should be corrected by a factor related to an average of the ratio of square roots of the characteristic polynomial of the underlying RMT Hamiltonian, even at leading order in weak coupling, and without assuming resonance widths are small compared to the mean level spacing.

## Method

The authors start from the exact non-Hermitian effective Hamiltonian formulation of RMT for open systems and analyze the exact probability density for resonance widths at β=1 (GOE symmetry) derived previously by Sommers et al. (1999). They perform a systematic weak-coupling expansion (g_c ≫ 1) of the exact three-fold integral expression, keeping the scaled widths y = πΓ_n/Δ arbitrary, and derive a factorized approximation. For M=1 they obtain a closed-form expression involving modified Bessel functions and the complementary error function, and independently verify this result by evaluating a spectral determinant average from a different representation of the resonance density.

## Key numbers

- For M=1, the leading asymptotic for large widths is P₁(y ≫ 1) = e^{-(g-1)y/2} [(g-1)/(2√(2g)) y^{-1} + (4-(g-5)g)/(8√(2g³)) y^{-2} + O(y^{-3})]; formally setting g=1 gives power-law behavior ≈ 1/(√2 y²), reproducing the known exact tail ≈ 1/(2y²).
- The average width ⟨y⟩ computed from the single-channel approximation develops a (1/√2)|ln(g-1)| divergence as g→1, in qualitative agreement with the exact Moldauer-Simonius average ⟨y⟩_MS = (1/2) ln[(g+1)/(g-1)].
- Numerical simulations used 10⁵ realizations of 250 × 250 GOE random matrices, taking 25 levels around the spectrum center per realization, for coupling parameters g = 1.1, 1.2, 1.5, and 3.
- The mean level spacing Δ = λπ/N, where 2λ is the semicircle radius; the mean partial width is Γ̅ = 2γΔ/π ≈ TΔ/(2π) for weak coupling.

## Context

This work extends the classic Porter-Thomas distribution (Porter and Thomas, 1956) for single-channel resonance widths, which was derived from first-order perturbation theory and assumes widths much smaller than the mean level spacing. Recent experiments with improved accuracy (Koehler et al., 2010) found significant deviations from the Porter-Thomas distribution, motivating proposals such as the semi-heuristic correction by Shchedrin and coauthors (2012). The present paper provides a fully controlled RMT derivation that corrects the Porter-Thomas result and remains accurate even at large resonance overlap, where the perturbative result fails. The exact distribution for β=1 was previously derived by Sommers et al. (1999) in a complicated three-fold integral form; the present work simplifies this to practical closed-form expressions for M=1 and M=2. The results enable improved analysis of experimental data from microwave billiards, quantum dots, and nuclear scattering, and connect to ongoing mathematical work on spectral determinants in RMT.
