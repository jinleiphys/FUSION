# Classical Electromagnetic Fields from Quantum Sources in Heavy-Ion Collisions

> Electromagnetic fields are generated in high energy nuclear collisions by spectator valence protons. These fields are traditionally computed by integrating the Maxwell equations with point sources. One might expect that such an approach is valid at distances much larger than the proton size and thus such a classical approach should work well for almost the entire interaction region in the case of heavy nuclei. We argue that, in fact, the contrary is true: due to the quantum diffusion of the proton wave function, the classical approximation breaks down at distances of the order of the system size. We compute the electromagnetic field created by a charged particle described initially as a Gaussian wave packet of width 1 fm and evolving in vacuum according to the Klein-Gordon equation. We completely neglect the medium effects. We show that the dynamics, magnitude and even sign of the electromagnetic field created by classical and quantum sources are different.

## Key claim

The authors argue that the classical approximation of treating spectator protons as point sources in heavy-ion collisions breaks down at distances of the order of the system size, not just at the Compton wavelength, because quantum diffusion of the proton wave function causes the electromagnetic field to differ in dynamics, magnitude, and even sign from the classical result.

## Method

The authors compute the electromagnetic field created by a charged particle initially described as a Gaussian wave packet of width 1 fm, evolving in vacuum according to the Klein-Gordon equation, completely neglecting medium effects. They compare this quantum treatment to the classical calculation using Maxwell equations with point sources moving along straight lines, employing the eikonal approximation. Numerical solutions are obtained for the relativistic quantum case using the exact relativistic dispersion relation.

## Key numbers

- The initial Gaussian wave packet width is set to a = 1 fm, corresponding to the proton size.
- The constituent quark mass is taken as m = 300 MeV.
- The collision energy is γ = 100.
- At very early times (before about t = 0.05 fm), classical and quantum calculations look qualitatively similar.
- At t = 0.25 fm, the magnitude of the magnetic field from quantum sources is an order of magnitude larger than the classical ones.
- In the ultra-relativistic limit, the scalar potential at short distances in the COM frame is approximately φ ≈ (0.3/4π)(eγ/a), where a = 1 fm.
- The characteristic time 0.2 fm is noted as when nuclear color fields lose coherence.

## Context

This work extends the standard approach of computing electromagnetic fields in heavy-ion collisions using point sources, as done by Skokov (2009), Tuchin (2010), Bzdak (2011), Voronyuk (2011), Deng (2012), Bloczynski (2012), and Zakharov (2014). The authors argue that the classical approximation, which contradicts the uncertainty relation, must be replaced by a quantum treatment using wave packets of finite size. This enables more realistic calculations of electromagnetic field effects on observables such as charge separation, as proposed by Kharzeev (2007), and on the Quark Gluon Plasma, as studied by Tuchin (2013) and Huang (2015).
