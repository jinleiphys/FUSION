# Theory of the nuclear excitation by electron transition process near the K-edge

> We propose a model for description of the process of Nuclear Excitation by Electron Transition (NEET) near the $K$-shell ionization threshold of an atom. We explain the experimental results for the $^{197}$Au cross section excitation $σ_{N^*}$ obtained by S.Kishimoto et al. Phys. Rev. C {\bf 74}, 031301(R) (2006) using synchrotron radiation near the Au $K$-edge. We predict the behavior of $σ_{N^*}$ as a function of the incident photon energy for nuclei $^{193}$Ir and $^{189}$Os. We reveal that the $^{189}$Os excitation begins when the energy of incident photons is below the $K$-shell ionization threshold in Os.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a quantum-electrodynamics model for the Nuclear Excitation by Electron Transition (NEET) process near the K-shell ionization threshold, showing that the cross section can be factorized into an ionization cross section and a NEET probability only far from threshold, while near threshold the full resonance structure must be retained. The model explains the experimental data for \(^{197}\)Au from Kishimoto et al. (2006) and predicts that for \(^{189}\)Os, nuclear excitation begins when the incident photon energy is below the K-shell ionization threshold, a subthreshold quantum effect arising from the finite width of the K-shell vacancy.

## Method

The authors construct a third-order Feynman-diagram amplitude (direct and exchange diagrams) for the process where a synchrotron photon ionizes the K-shell, a K-hole propagates, and the nucleus is excited via a virtual photon from the \(M_I \rightarrow K\) hole transition. They integrate the squared amplitude over the emitted electron momentum, replace the delta-function for photoionization with a Lorentzian of width \(\Gamma_K\), and derive an analytic expression for the nuclear excitation cross section \(\sigma_{N^*}\) as a function of incident photon energy \(\omega_{SR}\). The atomic radial matrix elements for the \(M1\) transition are computed using relativistic Dirac-Fock wave functions, and the nuclear reduced transition probabilities \(B(M1)\) are taken from experimental tables.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{197}\)Au: the theoretical NEET probability far from threshold is \(P_{NEET} = (3.4 \pm 0.2) \times 10^{-8}\) using \(B_{W.u.}(M1) = (2.05 \pm 0.08) \times 10^{-3}\) and atomic matrix element \(|{\cal R}_1^M|^2 = 106.8\); the experimental value from Kishimoto et al. (2006) is \((4.5 \pm 0.6) \times 10^{-8}\).
- For \(^{197}\)Au: the NEET edge is predicted at \(49\) eV above the K-edge with a width of \(17\)–\(18\) eV for a synchrotron beam of \(3.5\) eV width; the experimental values are \(40 \pm 2\) eV above the K-edge with a width of \(14 \pm 9\) eV.
- For \(^{197}\)Au: using an alternative nuclear transition energy of \(77.341\) keV (10 eV lower than the tabulated \(77.351\) keV) gives a delay of \(40\) eV and a width of \(17.5\) eV, with \(P_{NEET} = (4.5 \pm 0.2) \times 10^{-8}\).
- For \(^{193}\)Ir: the predicted cross section scale is \(\sigma_{ion}^0 P_{NEET} \simeq 6.6 \times 10^{-30}\) cm\(^2\), with \(\Delta = \omega_N - \omega_A = 107\) eV.
- For \(^{189}\)Os: the atomic transition energy exceeds the nuclear transition energy by \(\omega_A - \omega_N = 1287\) eV; the cross section scale is \(\sigma_{ion}^0 P_{NEET} \simeq 4 \times 10^{-31}\) cm\(^2\), and excitation begins below the K-shell ionization threshold.

## Context

This work extends the QED-based NEET theory developed by Tkalya (1992, 1994, 2000) and others (Ahmad 2000, Harston 2001) to the threshold region near the K-edge, which was previously neglected. It provides the first theoretical explanation for the experimental results on \(^{197}\)Au by Kishimoto et al. (2006), who observed a narrow NEET edge above the K-absorption edge using synchrotron radiation. The model enables predictions for \(^{193}\)Ir and \(^{189}\)Os, and the predicted subthreshold excitation of \(^{189}\)Os offers a new mechanism for nuclear excitation below the ionization threshold, relevant to ongoing searches for external control of isomeric nuclear decay (Tkalya 2005).
