# Sudden To Adiabatic Transition in Beta Decay

> We discuss effects in beta decays at very low beta energies, of the order of the kinetic energies of atomic electrons. As the beta energy is lowered the atomic response changes from sudden to adiabatic. As a consequence, the beta decay rate increases slightly and the ejection of atomic electrons (shake off) and subsequent production of X rays is turned off. We estimate the transition energy and the change in decay rate. The rate increase is largest in heavy atoms, which have a small Q value in their decay. The X ray switch-off is independent of Q value.

# Sudden To Adiabatic Transition in Beta Decay

## Key claim
At very low beta energies (comparable to atomic electron kinetic energies), the atomic response to beta decay transitions from the sudden approximation to an adiabatic regime, causing a slight increase in the beta decay rate and a suppression of atomic electron shake-off and subsequent X-ray emission. The effect is largest in heavy atoms with small Q values, and the X-ray switch-off is independent of Q value.

## Method
The authors model beta decay of a nucleus with a single atomic electron, treating the change in nuclear charge from Z to Z±1 as a small perturbation. They compute the probability that the atomic electron remains in the ground state after decay using time-dependent perturbation theory in both the sudden and adiabatic limits, with a model potential from an outgoing s-wave beta particle. The switchover velocity is estimated by equating expansions of the ground-state probability in powers of beta velocity (adiabatic side) and inverse beta velocity (sudden side).

## Key numbers
- The extra energy gained by the neutrino when switching from sudden to adiabatic regime is ΔE = 1/2 atomic unit per electron, or 1 atomic unit (≈27 eV) per filled electronic shell, independent of nuclear charge Z.
- For a Q value of 100 keV and 4 filled shells contributing, the relative increase in differential decay rate at low beta energies is δR/R ≈ 2 × 10⁻³.
- The switchover velocity from sudden to adiabatic regime occurs at v̄ ≈ 0.3 v_atomic, where v_atomic is the electron velocity in the 1s shell.
- The transition beta energy is approximately E_tr ∼ 1.4 Z² eV; for Z ≈ 50 this is about 3 keV.
- The change in total lifetime for Q ≈ 100 keV is of order 10⁻¹⁰ (relative).

## Context
This work extends the long-standing study of atomic electron effects in beta decay, going back to screening factors by Rose and the sudden approximation by Migdal. It provides explicit estimates for the transition energy and rate change in heavy atoms, which the authors state were absent from prior literature. The results are relevant to accurate experiments in tritium decay and superallowed decays, as discussed by Durand and Lopez (1994), Drukarev and Strikman (1991), and Brown and Zhai (1995). The predicted energy-dependent suppression of atomic X-ray yield at low beta energies could be tested experimentally, building on earlier attempts by Isozumi (1987) that did not reach sufficiently low energies.
