# Testing the Paradigm of Nuclear Many-Body Theory

> Nuclear many-body theory is based on the tenet that nuclear systems can be accurately described as collections of point-like particles. This picture, while providing a remarkably accurate explanation of a wealth of measured properties of atomic nuclei, is bound to break down in the high-density regime, in which degrees of freedom other than protons and neutrons are expected to come into play. Valuable information on the validity of the description of dense nuclear matter in terms of nucleons, needed to firmly establish its limit of applicability, can be obtained from electron-nucleus scattering data at large momentum transfer and low energy transfer. The emergence of y-scaling in this kinematic region, unambiguously showing that the beam particles couple to high-momentum nucleons belonging to strongly correlated pairs, indicates that at densities as large as five times nuclear density -- typical of the neutron star interior -- nuclear matter largely behaves as a collection of nucleons.

## Key claim

The paper argues that nuclear many-body theory's assumption that nuclei behave as collections of point-like nucleons remains valid at densities up to approximately five times nuclear saturation density, as evidenced by y-scaling in electron-nucleus scattering data, but the abstract's claim that matter "largely behaves as a collection of nucleons" at these densities carries the qualifier that this conclusion applies specifically to the kinematic regime of large momentum transfer and low energy transfer where the impulse approximation dominates.

## Method

The author analyzes electron-nucleus scattering data across a broad kinematic region, focusing on the emergence of y-scaling in the nuclear response function at large momentum transfer (above approximately 1 GeV) and low energy transfer. The scaling analysis involves extracting the scaling function F(y,Q^2) from measured cross sections by dividing the nuclear cross section by the sum of elementary electron-proton and electron-neutron cross sections, then taking the Q^2 to infinity limit to obtain the universal scaling function F(y). The connection between y-scaling and the nucleon momentum distribution n(k) is established through the impulse approximation formalism, where the response function reduces to an integral over the momentum distribution weighted by an energy-conserving delta function.

## Key numbers

- Nucleon Fermi momentum of 500 MeV corresponds to a density exceeding the central density of atomic nuclei (rho_0 approximately 0.16 fm^-3) by a factor of about seven
- Correlation effects account for approximately 20% of the normalization of ground-state wave functions
- The density of correlated pairs inferred from Jefferson Lab data can be as high as approximately 5 rho_0
- SLAC experiment collected data at beam energies 3 to 15 GeV with fixed electron scattering angle theta_e = 8 degrees
- Jefferson Lab experiment on Au-197 used incident electron energy E = 4.033 GeV at scattering angles theta_e = 15, 23, 30, 37, 45, and 55 degrees
- Excitation energy of the spectator system in electron-nucleus scattering at momentum transfer around and above 1 GeV can be as high as approximately 80 MeV

## Context

This work extends the y-scaling analyses first performed on He-3 at SLAC in the 1970s (Day 1979) and later systematic studies on targets from He-4 to Au-197 at SLAC (PhysRevLett 1987) and Jefferson Lab (Arrington 2012). The paper connects y-scaling results to neutron star physics, arguing that the scaling behavior provides complementary information to astrophysical observations from LIGO/Virgo gravitational wave detections and NICER mass-radius measurements. The analysis enables establishing the density regime where nucleonic degrees of freedom remain valid before a possible phase transition to quark matter in neutron star cores, as discussed by Baym (2017).
