# The nuclear shell model towards the drip lines

> Applications of configuration mixing methods for nuclei near the proton and neutron drip lines are discussed. A short review of magic numbers is presented. Prospects for advances in the regions of four new "outposts" are highlighted: $^{28}$O, $^{42}$Si, $^{60}$Ca and $^{78}$Ni. Topics include: shell gaps, single-particle properties, islands-of-inversion, collectivity, neutron decay, neutron halos, two-proton decay, effective charge, and quenching in knockout reactions.

## Key claim

Configuration mixing methods can successfully describe nuclei near the proton and neutron drip lines, but the "island-of-inversion" may actually be a "peninsula of inversion" extending from $^{32}$Mg all the way to the neutron drip line, with the first true island-of-inversion possibly lying between $^{60}$Ca and $^{78}$Ni.

## Method

The paper reviews configuration interaction (CI) calculations using "universal" Hamiltonians with a single set of single-particle energies and two-body matrix elements applied across a model space, with empirical modifications to ab-initio starting points via singular value decomposition methods. Results are presented for the FSU Hamiltonian with pure $\Delta$ configurations (where $\Delta=n$ is the number of neutrons excited from $sd$ to $pf$ shells) for nuclei in four neutron-rich regions: $^{28}$O, $^{42}$Si, $^{60}$Ca and $^{78}$Ni, with comparisons to experimental data on excitation energies, decay widths, and knockout reaction cross sections.

## Key numbers

- The $N=20$ shell gap decreases from about 7.0 MeV in $^{34}$Si to about 2.7 MeV in $^{28}$O
- For $^{25}$O one-neutron decay: calculated $Q=1.15(15)$ MeV (experimental $Q=0.749(10)$ MeV), calculated decay width $\Gamma = 75(1)$ keV (experimental $\Gamma = 88(6)$ keV)
- The two-neutron decay of $^{26}$O has a remarkably small $Q$ value of 0.018(5) MeV; theoretical $Q$ from USDC Hamiltonian is 0.02(15) MeV
- For $^{28}$O with the FSU Hamiltonian: the $\Delta=2$ configuration lies 0.8 MeV below the $\Delta=0$ closed-shell configuration; calculated four-neutron decay energy is 1.5 MeV
- The $2^+$ energy in $^{42}$Si is 0.74 MeV, compared to 3.33 MeV in $^{34}$Si and 3.83 MeV in $^{48}$Ca
- For $^{29}$F: the lowest $\Delta=2$ $5/2^+$ state has TNA$[(0p_{3/2})]=0.62$ for the ground state going to the $^{27}$F $\Delta=0$ $5/2^+$ ground state
- The excited $1/2^+$ ($1s_{1/2}$) state of $^{17}$F shows a Thomas-Ehrman energy shift from 0.87 MeV in $^{17}$O to 0.49 MeV in $^{17}$F
- For knockout reactions far from stability: $R_s = \sigma_{exp}/\sigma_{th}$ is near unity for loosely bound nucleons but drops to about 0.3 for deeply bound nucleons

## Context

This review extends the work on magic numbers and shell gaps from Goeppert-Mayer and Jensen (1949) and the concept of islands-of-inversion introduced by Warburton et al. (1990) for the $N=20$ region near $^{32}$Mg. The paper discusses four neutron-rich "outposts" ($^{28}$O, $^{42}$Si, $^{60}$Ca, $^{78}$Ni) that represent the next frontiers for shell model calculations, building on universal Hamiltonians such as USD, USDB, SDPF-U-MIX, and SDPF-M, and comparing with ab-initio methods like VS-IMSRG. The work enables future studies of neutron halos, multi-neutron decay, and the evolution of shell structure toward the drip lines, with particular emphasis on the need for improved experimental masses and excited-state data for $^{60}$Ca and neutron-rich fluorine and neon isotopes.
