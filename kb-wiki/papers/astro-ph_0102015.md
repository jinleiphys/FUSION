# Evolution of Proto-Neutron Stars with Quarks

> Neutrino fluxes from proto-neutron stars with and without quarks are studied. Observable differences become apparent after 10--20 s of evolution. Sufficiently massive stars containing negatively-charged, strongly interacting, particles collapse to black holes during the first minute of evolution. Since the neutrino flux vanishes when a black hole forms, this is the most obvious signal that quarks (or other types of strange matter) have appeared. The metastability timescales for stars with quarks are intermediate between those containing hyperons and kaon condensates.

## Key claim

Observable differences in neutrino fluxes between proto-neutron stars with and without quarks become apparent only after 10–20 seconds of evolution, and the most unambiguous signal of quark (or other strange matter) appearance is the abrupt cessation of neutrino flux when a sufficiently massive star collapses to a black hole during the first minute.

## Method

The authors solved general relativistic neutrino transport and hydrostatic equilibrium equations for proto-neutron stars containing either nucleons only (np) or nucleons plus quark matter (npQ), using a field-theoretic model at mean field level for baryonic matter and a MIT bag-like model for quark matter. They constructed a mixed phase of baryons and quarks by satisfying Gibbs' phase rules for mechanical, chemical, and thermal equilibrium, and used neutrino opacities consistent with the equation of state. Neutrino count rates were estimated for the SuperK detector assuming a Galactic supernova at 8.5 kpc distance, with the total luminosity equally divided among six neutrino species and spectra taken as Fermi-Dirac with zero chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- Stars with baryonic mass \(M_B \lesssim 1.1\) M\(_\odot\) do not contain quarks; those with \(M_B \gtrsim 1.7\) M\(_\odot\) are metastable (for the equation of state with bag constant \(B=150\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\)).
- For a star with \(M_B = 1.6\) M\(_\odot\), quarks appear after about 15 seconds, and the star evolves for a further 15–20 seconds before reaching a new stationary state.
- Metastability lifetimes for npQ stars with \(B=150\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\) are restricted to the range 10–30 seconds, slowly decreasing with increasing mass.
- For \(B=200\) MeV fm\(^{-3}\) (maximum gravitational mass of cold neutron star about 1.85 M\(_\odot\)), metastability timescales lie in a narrow range 40–45 seconds.
- The maximum mass for hot, neutrino-free npQ matter is \(\sim 0.05\) M\(_\odot\) less than the maximum mass for cold, catalyzed npQ matter.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of proto-neutron star evolution with hyperons (PRPLM99) and kaon condensates (Pons01) by providing a benchmark calculation with quarks. The metastability timescales for stars with quarks are intermediate between those containing hyperons (which can be very short) and kaon condensates (generally larger). The results highlight that a single observation of metastability may not distinguish among quark, hyperon, or kaon compositions, but multiple observations or independent mass estimates (as in SN 1987A, THM90BB95) could help differentiate models.
