# Fluid dynamics for relativistic spin-polarized media

> We briefly review the basic features of a new framework for relativistic perfect fluid hydrodynamics of polarized systems consisting of particles with spin one half. Using this approach we numerically study the stability of a stationary vortex-like solution, representing global equilibrium of a rotating medium.

## Key claim

The stationary vortex-like solution representing global equilibrium of a rotating spin-polarized medium is unstable when realistic boundary conditions are imposed, making it unlikely that such a stationary state is realized in Nature.

## Method

The authors derive a closed set of six differential equations for the perfect-fluid hydrodynamics of spin-1/2 particles, consisting of the Euler equations, entropy conservation, baryon number conservation, and a new conservation law for a spin-related charge. They then numerically evolve an initial condition that reproduces the stationary vortex solution in a central region but departs from it at the edges, and observe the time evolution of the temperature and velocity profiles.

## Key numbers

- The stationary vortex solution is defined for a cylinder of finite radius \(R < 1/\tilde{\Omega}\), where \(\tilde{\Omega}\) is the rotation parameter.
- The temperature profile is given by \(T = T_0 \gamma\), with \(\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1 - \tilde{\Omega}^2 r^2}\) and \(r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\).
- The spin polarization tensor has the non-vanishing component \(\omega_{xy} = -\omega_{yx} = \tilde{\Omega}/T_0 = 2\Omega_0/T_0\).
- The system is evolved numerically at times \(t = 0.1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\) fm.

## Context

This work extends the framework of Becattini (2009, 2013) from global equilibrium to local equilibrium for relativistic spin-polarized media, building on the authors' own prior papers (Florkowski 2017, 2018). The numerical demonstration that the stationary vortex solution is unstable under realistic boundary conditions suggests that the observed global spin polarization of Lambda hyperons by the STAR collaboration (2017) cannot be explained by a simple stationary rotating medium, and motivates further study of non-equilibrium spin dynamics in heavy-ion collisions.
