# Nuclear Astrophysics in Rare Isotope Facilities

> I discuss a few of the recent developments in nuclear reactions at very low energies with emphasis on the role of radioactive beam facilities.

# Nuclear Astrophysics in Rare Isotope Facilities

## Key claim
Radioactive beam facilities have opened new indirect methods to study nuclear reactions at astrophysically relevant energies, but the electron screening problem, both in stellar plasmas and in laboratory measurements, remains unresolved, with laboratory screening data systematically disagreeing with theoretical predictions by approximately a factor of two.

## Method
The paper reviews indirect nuclear-reaction techniques enabled by radioactive beams: the Trojan Horse method, knockout reactions, asymptotic normalization coefficients (ANC) from peripheral transfer reactions, charge-exchange reactions for Gamow-Teller strengths, and Coulomb breakup for radiative-capture cross sections. It also discusses the electron screening problem, comparing Debye-Huckel plasma screening calculations with laboratory atomic-electron screening measurements and examining stopping-power models as a possible resolution.

## Key numbers
- The reaction $^{7}Be(p,\gamma)^{8}B$ had an S-factor uncertainty of as much as 30% in earlier analyses (Barker, 1980); recent Coulomb breakup measurements yield $S_{17}(0) = 19.0$ eV.b, compatible with solar model calculations (Bahcall, 1989).
- The $^{12}C(\alpha,\gamma)^{16}O$ cross section should be known to better than 20% for accurate stellar modeling (Weaver and Woosley, 1985); this goal has not yet been achieved.
- Plasma screening enhances reactions such as $^{3}He(^{3}He,2p)^{4}He$ and $^{7}Be(p,\gamma)^{8}B$ by as much as 20% in the Debye-Huckel (Salpeter, 1954) approach.
- Stopping power for proton+hydrogen collisions at low energies is proportional to $v^{\alpha}$ with $\alpha = 1.35$ (Bertulani and Dasgupta, 2000); extrapolations from Andersen-Ziegler tables predict a larger value of $\alpha$.
- For protons on helium targets in the 4 keV energy range, another calculation (Golser and Semrad, 1991) found stopping proportional to $v^{3.34}$.
- The most neutron-rich isotopes along the r-process path have lifetimes of $10^{-2}$ to $10^{-1}$ seconds.

## Context
This review extends the discussion of low-energy nuclear reactions for stellar evolution, building on the foundational plasma-screening work of Salpeter (1954) and the stopping-power tabulations of Andersen and Ziegler (1977). It addresses the persistent laboratory screening discrepancy noted by Rolfs (2001) and others, and surveys indirect methods proposed by Baur (1986) for the Trojan Horse method, Mukhamedzhanov (1990) for ANC techniques, and Baur, Bertulani, and Rebel (1986) for Coulomb breakup. The paper enables future work by identifying which indirect methods require further dynamical corrections (e.g., higher multipolarities, nuclear interaction effects in breakup) before they can be reliably applied to the many radiative-capture reactions needed for r-process and stellar modeling.
