# Polarized Lepton-Nucleon Elastic Scattering and a Search for a Light Scalar Boson

> Lepton-nucleon elastic scattering, using the one-photon and one-scalar-boson exchange mechanisms considering all possible polarizations, is used to study searches for a new scalar boson and suggest new measurements of the nucleon form factors. A new light scalar boson, which feebly couples to leptons and nucleons, may account for the proton radius and muon $g-2$ puzzles. We show that the scalar boson produces relatively large effects in certain kinematic region when using sufficient control of lepton and nucleon spin polarization. We generalize current techniques to measure the ratio $G_E/G_M$ and present a new method to separately measure $G_M^2$ and $G_E^2$ using polarized incoming and outgoing muons.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a light scalar boson (mass around 1 MeV) feebly coupling to leptons and nucleons could produce observable effects in polarized lepton-nucleon elastic scattering, but only in specific kinematic regions where the standard-model one-photon-exchange cross section approaches zero, and only if the experimental challenges of polarizing both incoming and outgoing particles and accessing very forward scattering angles can be overcome.

## Method

The authors compute the differential cross section for lepton-nucleon elastic scattering including one-photon exchange, one-scalar-boson exchange, and their interference, in a general reference frame with all possible combinations of lepton and nucleon spin polarizations. They then examine the parameter space of scattering angle, beam momentum, and polarization angles to identify kinematic regions where the scalar-boson contribution dominates over the one-photon-exchange contribution, using the coupling constraints from TuckerSmith (2010) for a scalar boson mass of 1 MeV. The formalism is also applied to propose new methods for measuring nucleon electromagnetic form factors using polarized muon beams.

## Key numbers

* For a scalar boson mass of 1 MeV, the coupling constraints are: \(g_e/e \lesssim 2.3\times10^{-4}\), \(g_p/e = g_\mu/e \lesssim 1.3\times10^{-3}\), and \(g_n \lesssim 2\times10^{-5}\).
* The parameter \(\lambda = (g_l g_N / e_l e_N) (q^2/(q^2+m_\phi^2))\) is bounded by \(|\lambda| \lesssim 10^{-6}\), suppressing the scalar-boson interference and pure-scalar contributions relative to one-photon exchange.
* For electron-neutron scattering with helicity flip at forward angles and beam momentum \(|\mathbf{p}_1| = 100\) MeV, the differential cross section is around \(10^{-15}\) mb/sr for scattering angles from \(0^\circ\) to \(2^\circ\).
* The muon anomalous magnetic moment discrepancy is \(\Delta a_\mu = (287 \pm 80) \times 10^{-11}\) (Davier 2010) or \((261 \pm 78) \times 10^{-11}\) (Hagiwara 2011).

## Context

This work extends the search for a light scalar boson proposed by TuckerSmith (2010) and Izaguirre (2014) as a simultaneous explanation for the proton radius puzzle (Pohl 2010) and the muon \(g-2\) anomaly (Blum 2013). The paper generalizes the polarization transfer method for measuring the ratio \(G_E/G_M\) developed by Akhiezer (1974) and Arnold (1980) to include lepton mass and non-longitudinal polarizations, and presents a new method to separately measure \(G_E^2\) and \(G_M^2\) using polarized incoming and outgoing muons. The results enable future experimental searches at facilities such as JLab (SoLID), MUSE at PSI, and the J-PARC muon facility, though the authors note that the most promising kinematic region for scalar boson detection lies outside the current polar angle coverage of these experiments.
