# Barrier distribution extraction via Gaussian process regression

> This work presents a novel method for extracting potential barrier distributions from experimental fusion cross sections. We utilize a simple Gaussian process regression (GPR) framework to model the observed cross sections as a function of energy for three nuclear systems. The GPR approach offers a flexible way to represent the experimental data, accommodating potentially complex behavior without introducing strong prior assumptions. This method is applied directly to experimental data and is compared to the traditional direct extraction technique. We discuss the advantages of GPR-based barrier distribution extraction, including the capability to quantify uncertainties and robustness to noise in the experimental data.

## Key claim

Gaussian process regression (GPR) can extract barrier distributions from experimental fusion cross sections with quantified uncertainties and robustness to noise, but the method's fidelity is limited near the barrier energy because the GPR model is optimized to cross sections on a linear scale, causing it to miss fine details where cross sections begin to exponentially decay.

## Method

The author applied GPR with a radial basis function (RBF) kernel to model fusion excitation functions for three nuclear systems, using experimental errors at design points and assuming no errors in the energy domain. Barrier distributions were then computed by taking second derivatives of the energy-weighted cross section via standard finite differences on a fine mesh, with hundreds of samples drawn from the GP to quantify regression uncertainty. The GPR-derived distributions were compared to the traditional pointwise derivative method from Rowley (1991).

## Key numbers

- For the $^{16}$O+$^{208}$Pb system, the primary barrier peak is located around 74 MeV, with GPR samples showing small uncertainty until data becomes sparse and errors grow around 77 MeV.
- For the calcium systems ($^{40}$Ca+$^{40}$Ca, $^{40}$Ca+$^{48}$Ca, $^{48}$Ca+$^{48}$Ca), GPR barrier distributions agree with pointwise values near the primary barrier peak, but the magnitude of secondary peaks at higher energies is underestimated, particularly for the symmetric $^{48}$Ca system.
- At higher energies, the spread in GPR-derived distributions grows substantially due to increasing uncertainty in the original GPR model as one approaches the edge of available data.

## Context

This work extends the traditional barrier distribution extraction method of Rowley (1991) and Dasgupta (1998) by adding a Bayesian regression framework, similar to the local regression approach of Scamps (2018). The method is motivated by the need to handle sparse data expected for reactions with rare isotopes at facilities like FRIB, and it enables future Bayesian studies that require robust uncertainty quantification for near- and sub-barrier fusion reactions. The author notes that more expressive GP kernels (e.g., Matérn kernels) may be needed for light-ion fusion systems exhibiting higher-frequency behavior from $\ell$-barriers and knock-out channels, as seen in recent measurements by Asher (2021) and Hudan (2023).
