# Chiral magnetic effect search in p+Au, d+Au and Au+Au collisions at RHIC

> Metastable domains of fluctuating topological charges can change the chirality of quarks and induce local parity violation in quantum chromodynamics. This can lead to observable charge separation along the direction of the strong magnetic field produced by spectator protons in relativistic heavy-ion collisions, a phenomenon called the chiral magnetic effect (CME). A major background source for CME measurements using the charge-dependent azimuthal correlator ($Δγ$) is the intrinsic particle correlations (such as resonance decays) coupled with the azimuthal elliptical anisotropy ($v_{2}$). In heavy-ion collisions, the magnetic field direction and event plane angle are correlated, thus the CME and the $v_{2}$-induced background are entangled. In this report, we present two studies from STAR to shed further lights on the background issue. (1) The $Δγ$ should be all background in small system p+Au and d+Au collisions, because the event plane angles are dominated by geometry fluctuations uncorrelated to the magnetic field direction. However, significant $Δγ$ is observed, comparable to the peripheral Au+Au data, suggesting a background dominance in the latter, and likely also in the mid-central Au+Au collisions where the multiplicity and $v_{2}$ scaled correlator is similar. (2) A new approach is devised to study $Δγ$ as a function of the particle pair invariant mass ($m_{inv}$) to identify the resonance backgrounds and hence to extract the possible CME signal. Signal is consistent with zero within uncertainties at high $m_{inv}$. Signal at low $m_{inv}$, extracted from a two-component model assuming smooth mass dependence, is consistent with zero within uncertainties.

## Key claim

The measured charge-dependent azimuthal correlator (Δγ) in p+Au and d+Au collisions at RHIC is comparable to that in peripheral Au+Au collisions, suggesting that the signal in heavy-ion collisions is dominated by background from resonance decays coupled with elliptic flow, and that any chiral magnetic effect signal is consistent with zero within current uncertainties.

## Method

The STAR experiment measured the three-particle correlator difference Δγ = γ_OS − γ_SS (where γ = ⟨cos(ϕ_α + ϕ_β − 2ψ_2)⟩) for charged particles with |η| < 1 and 0.2 < p_T < 2.0 GeV/c in p+Au, d+Au, and Au+Au collisions at √s_NN = 200 GeV. In small systems, the event plane angle is dominated by geometry fluctuations uncorrelated to the magnetic field direction, so any CME signal averages to zero while background contributions remain. A second analysis studied Δγ as a function of the particle pair invariant mass (m_inv) to identify resonance backgrounds, using a two-component model to extract possible CME signals at low m_inv.

## Key numbers

- Δγ in p+Au and d+Au collisions is comparable to peripheral Au+Au data at similar multiplicities
- In 50-80% centrality Au+Au collisions, inclusive Δγ = (7.45 ± 0.21) × 10⁻⁴; with m_inv > 1.5 GeV/c² cut, Δγ = (1.3 ± 5.7) × 10⁻⁵, which is (1.8 ± 7.6)% of the inclusive value
- In 20-50% centrality Au+Au collisions, inclusive Δγ = (1.82 ± 0.03) × 10⁻⁴; with m_inv > 1.5 GeV/c² cut, Δγ = (7.7 ± 9.0) × 10⁻⁶, which is (4.3 ± 4.9)% of the inclusive value
- In 0-20% centrality Au+Au collisions, inclusive Δγ = (3.70 ± 0.67) × 10⁻⁵; with m_inv > 1.5 GeV/c² cut, Δγ = (−0.1 ± 1.8) × 10⁻⁵
- Extracted CME signal from two-component model fit in 20-50% Au+Au: constant CME assumption gives (5.9 ± 9.0) × 10⁻⁶ (3.2 ± 4.9% of inclusive); exponential CME assumption gives (3.0 ± 2.0) × 10⁻⁵ (16 ± 11% of inclusive)

## Context

This work extends the control experiment approach of CMS (Khachatryan 2016, Sirunyan 2017) who found comparable Δγ signals in p+Pb and Pb+Pb collisions at LHC energies, to RHIC energies where the magnetic field decays more slowly and CME might be more visible. The invariant mass method is a new approach to identify resonance decay backgrounds (such as K_s⁰, ρ, f⁰) that contribute to the Δγ observable. The results suggest that the Δγ measurements in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC are largely background, but a small finite CME signal is not disallowed given present uncertainties. Future theoretical calculations of the CME mass dependence would be valuable for further progress.
