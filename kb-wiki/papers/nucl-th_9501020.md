# INCORPORATION OF QUANTUM STATISTICAL FEATURES IN MOLECULAR DYNAMICS

> We formulate a method for incorporating quantum fluctuations into molecular- dynamics simulations of many-body systems, such as those employed for energetic nuclear collision processes. Based on Fermi's Golden Rule, we allow spontaneous transitions to occur between the wave packets which are not energy eigenstates. The ensuing diffusive evolution in the space of the wave packet parameters exhibits appealing physical properties, including relaxation towards quantum- statistical equilibrium.

## Key claim

The authors propose augmenting standard molecular-dynamics equations of motion for wave packet parameters with a stochastic term based on Fermi's Golden Rule, which allows spontaneous transitions between wave packets that are not energy eigenstates; this method causes the system to relax toward quantum-statistical equilibrium rather than the classical equilibrium produced by standard molecular dynamics.

## Method

The method starts from the Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) framework, where the A-body system is represented as a product of Gaussian wave packets with fixed width, characterized by a parameter vector Z. The deterministic equations of motion are augmented by a stochastic transition rate derived from Fermi's Golden Rule, w(Z -> Z') = (2 pi / hbar) |<Z'|V|Z>|^2 rho_E(Z'), where V is a residual interaction with no diagonal matrix elements and rho_E is the spectral strength function. The resulting diffusive process in wave packet parameter space is described by a Fokker-Planck equation, with transport coefficients (drift V_n and diffusion D_{nn'}) calculated approximately from the transition rate, requiring only diagonalization of the coefficient matrix C for practical implementation.

## Key numbers

- The variance of the spectral strength distribution for a wave packet Z is given by sigma_Z^2 = <Z|(H - H)^2|Z> = integral dGamma' |<Z'|H - H|Z>|^2, which can be approximated as (del H / del Z) dot C^{-1} dot (del H / del Zbar).
- The total transition rate from a given state Z is w_0(Z) = (2 pi / hbar) gamma_Z^2 rho_E(Z) exp(beta_Z^2 sigma_Z^2), where gamma_Z^2 = <Z|(V - V)^2|Z> is approximated as (del V / del Z) dot C^{-1} dot (del V / del Zbar).
- The diffusion coefficient is 2D_{nn'}(Z) = w_0 [C^{-1}_{nn'} + (1/gamma_Z^2) (C^{-1} dot (del V / del Zbar))_n ((del V / del Z) dot C^{-1})_{n'}], holding to leading order in beta_Z^2.
- The drift coefficient is V_n(Z) = (D dot (del ln rho_E / del Zbar))_n = -beta_Z (D dot (del H / del Zbar))_n.

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier analysis (Ohnishi and Randrup, 1993) of the spectral distribution problem in molecular dynamics, and builds on the AMD framework developed for nuclear collisions (Aichelin, Boal, FMD, AMD). The method addresses a generic shortcoming of molecular dynamics simulations for quantum many-body systems, where the neglect of spectral distribution leads to classical rather than quantal statistics; it is expected to improve descriptions of features sensitive to quantal fluctuations, such as the specific heat at low temperatures. The authors suggest that applying this stochastic extension to nuclear multifragmentation simulations may yield better agreement with observations, as excited fragments can explore their eigenstate spectrum and become trapped in more bound configurations, enhancing survival probability.
