# Dynamical and statistical bimodality in nuclear fragmentation

> The origin of bimodal behavior in the residue distribution experimentally measured in heavy ion reactions is reexamined using Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck simulations. We suggest that, depending on the incident energy and impact parameter of the reaction, both entrance channel and exit channel effects can be at the origin of the observed behavior. Specifically, fluctuations in the reaction mechanism induced by fluctuations in the collision rate, as well as thermal bimodality directly linked to the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition are observed in our simulations. Both phenomenologies were previously proposed in the literature, but presented as incompatible and contradictory interpretations of the experimental measurements. These results indicate that heavy ion collisions at intermediate energies can be viewed as a powerful tool to study both bifurcations induced by out-of-equilibrium critical phenomena, as well as finite size precursors of thermal phase transitions.

## Key claim

The origin of bimodal distributions in nuclear fragmentation can be either dynamical (entrance channel effects from fluctuations in collision rate) or statistical (thermal bimodality from the liquid-gas phase transition), depending on the incident energy and impact parameter; these two mechanisms, previously presented as incompatible, can both occur in heavy ion collisions under different conditions.

## Method

The authors performed Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) transport simulations for the symmetric system ⁴⁰Ca+⁴⁰Ca at incident energies of 40 and 100 A.MeV, for impact parameters from 0 to 9 fm. The dynamical BUU calculation was stopped at a freeze-out time determined by the isotropy of the momentum distribution and the maximum size of the second largest cluster, then coupled to the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) to treat secondary de-excitation. The probability distribution of the largest fragment charge \(A_{max}\) was analyzed as the order parameter.

## Key numbers

* At 100 A.MeV and central collisions (b=0 fm), the freeze-out distribution of \(A_{max}\) shows two peaks, with a mass cut of \(A_{cut}=37\) separating stopped events (isotropic emission, \(P_z \approx 0\)) from crossed events (forward/backward emission).
* At 100 A.MeV and b=3 and 6 fm, the temperature distribution of the largest and second largest fragments peaks near the liquid-gas transition temperature \(T_t \approx 5\) MeV, indicating a thermal phase transition.
* At 40 A.MeV and central collisions (b=0 fm), the dynamical bimodality survives secondary decay because the excitation energy is low enough that the freeze-out and asymptotic distributions are "almost same".
* The freeze-out time varies from \(t_{FO}=150\) fm/c at b=9 fm to \(t_{FO}=200\) fm/c at b=0 fm for 100 A.MeV; at 40 A.MeV the coupling time was approximately \(t=400\) fm/c.

## Context

This work reconciles two previously contradictory interpretations of bimodality in nuclear fragmentation: the dynamical bifurcation mechanism observed in QMD calculations by Lefevre et al. and Zbiri et al., and the thermal bimodality signal linked to the liquid-gas phase transition proposed by Gulminelli et al. and Gargiulo et al. The results show that inclusive measurements (as in the bimodal_asy experiments) are more likely to reflect dynamical bimodality, while binary-selected events (as in the bimodal_zmax experiments) may reveal the thermal phase transition. This enables future experimental studies to distinguish between out-of-equilibrium critical phenomena and finite-size precursors of thermal phase transitions by appropriate selection of reaction conditions.
