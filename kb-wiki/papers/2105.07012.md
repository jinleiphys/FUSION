# Matrix-product state approach to the generalized nuclear pairing Hamiltonian

> We show that from the point of view of the generalized pairing Hamiltonian, the atomic nucleus is a system with small entanglement and can thus be described efficiently using a 1D tensor network (matrix-product state) despite the presence of long-range interactions. The ground state can be obtained using the density-matrix renormalization group (DMRG) algorithm, which is accurate up to machine precision even for large nuclei, is numerically as cheap as the widely used BCS (Bardeen-Cooper-Schrieffer) approach, and does not suffer from any mean-field artifacts. We apply this framework to compute the even-odd mass differences of all known lead isotopes from $^{178}$Pb to $^{220}$Pb in a very large configuration space of 13 shells between the neutron magic numbers 82 and 184 (i.e., two major shells) and find good agreement with the experiment. We also treat pairing with non-zero angular momentum and determine the lowest excited states in the full configuration space of one major shell, which we demonstrate for the $N=126$, $Z\geq 82$ isotones. To demonstrate the capabilities of the method beyond low-lying excitations, we calculate the first 100 excited states of $^{208}$Pb with singlet pairing and the two-neutron removal spectral function of $^{210}$Pb, which relates to a two-neutron pickup experiment.

## Key claim

The authors show that the generalized nuclear pairing Hamiltonian can be efficiently solved using matrix-product state (MPS) methods because the atomic nucleus exhibits low entanglement in the Woods-Saxon single-particle basis, but this efficiency is conditional on staying within the weak-coupling regime where deep shells are nearly filled and only few excitations exist across a softened Fermi edge.

## Method

The authors map the nuclear pairing Hamiltonian onto a one-dimensional chain of sites representing Kramers pairs (time-conjugate orbitals with angular momentum projections ±m), then apply the density-matrix renormalization group (DMRG) algorithm to find ground and excited states. They use a contact interaction (delta-force) with strength V0 as the only free parameter for singlet pairing, and extend to finite angular momentum couplings J=0,2,4,6,8 with separate V0^J parameters fitted to experimental data. The Hamiltonian is represented as a matrix-product operator (MPO) and compressed losslessly by up to 99% using linear dependencies.

## Key numbers

- Even-odd mass differences for lead isotopes from 178Pb to 220Pb computed in a configuration space of 13 shells (chain length L=51) between neutron magic numbers 82 and 184
- For lead isotopes with V0=250 MeV·fm^3, the average deviation from experimental even-odd mass differences is σ≈0.13 MeV
- For tin isotopes with V0=300 MeV·fm^3 in a 10-shell configuration space (L=31), the average deviation from experiment is σ≈0.10 MeV
- The MPS bond dimension required for ground states is χ∼250 for lead at intermediate fillings and χ∼50 for tin
- Energy variance per particle is of order 10^-9 MeV^2 or smaller for converged ground states
- For the N=126 isotones with J>0 pairing, fitted interaction strengths are V0^J=0=320, V0^J=2=360, V0^J=4=230, V0^J=6=220, and V0^J≥8=-210, all in units of MeV·fm^3
- Entanglement entropy reaches maximum S_A|B≈1.4 for open-shell isotopes 186Pb and 220Pb, while closed-shell 208Pb has S_A|B≈0.3

## Context

This work extends the application of DMRG from the exactly solvable Richardson model (studied for superconducting grains by von Delft (2001) and others) to realistic nuclear pairing Hamiltonians with level-dependent matrix elements and finite angular momentum couplings. It contrasts with mean-field BCS theory (Belyaev, 1959) which breaks particle number conservation and predicts a spurious phase transition, and with configuration-space quantum Monte Carlo methods which suffer from sign problems and statistical errors that can reach 1 MeV even for simple models (Mukherjee, 2011). The approach enables computation of ground states and excited states for large configuration spaces (up to 51 sites) that were previously inaccessible to exact diagonalization, and can supersede BCS for a broad range of nuclear pairing problems while remaining numerically as cheap.
