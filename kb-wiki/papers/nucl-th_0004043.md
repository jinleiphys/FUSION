# Effective Field Theory for Dilute Fermi Systems

> The virtues of an effective field theory (EFT) approach to many-body problems are illustrated by deriving the expansion for the energy of an homogeneous, interacting Fermi gas at low density and zero temperature. A renormalization scheme based on dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction leads to a more transparent power-counting procedure and diagrammatic expansion than conventional many-body approaches. Coefficients of terms in the expansion with logarithms of the Fermi momentum are determined by the renormalization properties of the EFT that describes few-body scattering. Lessons for an EFT treatment of nuclear matter are discussed.

# Effective Field Theory for Dilute Fermi Systems

## Key claim

The effective field theory (EFT) approach can reproduce the known low-density expansion for the ground-state energy of a homogeneous, interacting Fermi gas at zero temperature, but only for systems with "natural" scattering lengths (where all effective range parameters are of order the underlying scale R). The expansion is perturbative in the Fermi momentum k_F over the scale Lambda = 1/R, and the coefficients of logarithmic terms in the expansion are determined by the renormalization properties of the EFT that describes few-body scattering, not by detailed many-body calculations.

## Method

The authors construct a local Lagrangian for nonrelativistic fermions with contact interactions, using dimensional regularization with minimal subtraction to eliminate power divergences and maintain transparent power counting. They evaluate Hugenholtz diagrams (closed, connected Feynman diagrams) for the ground-state energy density, applying vacuum power counting with k_F playing the role of external momentum, and match the EFT coefficients to the effective range expansion parameters from two-body scattering.

## Key numbers

- The leading correction to the noninteracting Fermi gas energy per particle is (g-1)*(2/(3*pi))*(k_F*a_s) times the free Fermi energy, where a_s is the s-wave scattering length and g is the spin degeneracy (dating from 1929).
- The next correction at order (k_F*a_s)^2 is (g-1)*(4/(35*pi^2))*(11-2*ln(2))*(k_F*a_s)^2 times the free Fermi energy, first obtained by Huang and Yang in the 1950s.
- At order (k_F*a_s)^3, the numerical coefficients for the s-wave contribution are (0.07550 +/- 0.00003) for the (g-1) term and (0.05741 +/- 0.00002) for the (g-1)(g-3) term.
- The effective range and p-wave contributions at order k_F^8 are (g-1)*(1/(10*pi))*(k_F*a_s)^2*(k_F*r_s) and (g+1)*(1/(5*pi))*(k_F*a_p)^3, respectively, where r_s is the s-wave effective range and a_p is the p-wave scattering length.

## Context

This work extends the EFT approach to many-body problems, following earlier treatments of dilute Bose gases by Braaten and Nieto (1997) and initial steps at finite density by Furnstahl, Serot, and Tang (2000). The EFT method reproduces the low-density expansion previously derived using correlation functions by Amusia and Efros (1968), Goldstone diagram expansions by Baker (1971) and Bishop (1973), and Feynman diagram methods by Bishop (1973), but with a more transparent power-counting procedure. The approach enables systematic extension to higher orders, finite temperature, and ultimately to nuclear matter, where fine-tuned scattering lengths (as in nucleon-nucleon scattering) will require resummation of the perturbation series.
