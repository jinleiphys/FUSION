# Magnetic susceptibility of QCD matter and its decomposition from the lattice

> We determine the magnetic susceptibility of thermal QCD matter by means of first principles lattice simulations using staggered quarks with physical masses. A novel method is employed that only requires simulations at zero background field, thereby circumventing problems related to magnetic flux quantization. After a careful continuum limit extrapolation, diamagnetic behavior (negative susceptibility) is found at low temperatures and strong paramagnetism (positive susceptibility) at high temperatures. We revisit the decomposition of the magnetic susceptibility into spin- and orbital angular momentum-related contributions. The spin term -- related to the normalization of the photon lightcone distribution amplitude at zero temperature -- is calculated non-perturbatively and extrapolated to the continuum limit. Having access to both the full magnetic susceptibility and the spin term, we calculate the orbital angular momentum contribution for the first time. The results reveal the opposite of what might be expected based on a free fermion picture. We provide a simple parametrization of the temperature- and magnetic field-dependence of the QCD equation of state that can be used in phenomenological studies.

# Magnetic susceptibility of QCD matter and its decomposition from the lattice

## Key claim
The magnetic susceptibility of thermal QCD matter is determined from first-principles lattice simulations using a novel method that requires only simulations at zero background field, circumventing magnetic flux quantization problems. After continuum limit extrapolation, diamagnetic behavior (negative susceptibility) is found at low temperatures and strong paramagnetism (positive susceptibility) at high temperatures, with the decomposition into spin and orbital angular momentum contributions revealing the opposite of what might be expected based on a free fermion picture.

## Method
The magnetic susceptibility was computed using current-current correlators in a mixed coordinate- and momentum-space representation at zero background magnetic field, avoiding the flux quantization problem inherent in finite-volume simulations with constant fields. Lattice simulations employed three flavors of stout smeared rooted staggered quarks with physical masses on spatially symmetric N_s^3 x N_t ensembles, using tree-level Symanzik improved gauge action. The tensor coefficient (spin contribution) was calculated non-perturbatively and extrapolated to the continuum limit, with QCD renormalization performed via the RI'-MOM scheme and translated to the MSbar scheme at 2 GeV.

## Key numbers
- The magnetic susceptibility was computed on lattices with geometries 16^3 x 6, 24^3 x 6, 24^3 x 8, 28^3 x 10, and 36^3 x 12 for finite temperature, and 24^3 x 32, 32^3 x 48, and 40^3 x 48 for zero temperature.
- The quark mass ratio was set to m_ud = m_s / R with R = 28.15 along the line of constant physics.
- The tensor coefficient renormalization was performed at the QCD scale mu_QCD = 2 GeV in the MSbar scheme.
- The leading-order QED beta-function coefficient is beta_1 = (1/(4 pi^2)) * sum_f (q_f/e)^2.
- The divergence coefficient for the tensor coefficient is gamma_1^tau = 3/(4 pi^2).
- The free case ratio of spin to total susceptibility is chi^spin : chi = 3 : 2.

## Context
This work extends previous studies by the same authors (Bali et al. 2012, 2013, 2014) by employing a novel calculational method based on current-current correlators at zero field (Bali et al. 2015), carrying out QCD renormalization non-perturbatively via the RI'-MOM scheme, and adding a finer lattice spacing. The results enable phenomenological studies of magnetars, neutron star mergers, off-central heavy-ion collisions, and early universe evolution, and provide a parametrization of the temperature- and magnetic field-dependence of the QCD equation of state. The decomposition into spin and orbital angular momentum contributions is performed for the first time, revealing behavior opposite to the free fermion picture of Pauli (1927) and Landau (1930).
