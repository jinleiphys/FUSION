# Slowly rotating superfluid Newtonian neutron star model with entrainment

> We develop a formalism to model slowly rotating superfluid Newtonian neutron stars. A two-fluid model is used to describe the matter, where one fluid is the superfluid neutrons that are believed to exist in the inner crust and core of mature neutron stars, while the other fluid is a charge neutral conglomerate of the remaining constituents (crust nuclei, core superconducting protons, electrons, etc). We include the entrainment effect, which is a non-dissipative interaction between the two fluids whereby a momentum induced in one of the fluids will cause part of the mass of the other fluid to be carried along. The equations that describe rotational equilibria (i.e. axisymmetric and stationary configurations) are approximated using the slow-rotation approximation; an expansion in terms of the rotation rates of the two fluids where only terms up to second-order are kept. Our formalism allows the neutrons to rotate at a rate different from that of the charged constituents. For a particular equation of state that is quadratic in the two mass-densities and relative velocities of the fluids, we find an analytic solution to the slow-rotation equations. This result provides an elegant generalisation to the two-fluid problem of the standard expressions for the one-fluid polytrope ${\cal E} \propto ρ^2$. The model equation of state includes entrainment and is general enough to allow for realistic values for, say, mass and radius of the star. It also includes a term that can be related to ``symmetry energy'' terms that appear in more realistic equations of state. We use the analytic solution to explore how relative rotation between the two fluids, the ``symmetry energy'' term, and entrainment affect the neutron star's local distribution of particles, as well as global quantities as the Kepler limit, ellipticity, and moments of inertia.

## Key claim

The paper develops a formalism for slowly rotating superfluid Newtonian neutron stars using a two-fluid model that includes entrainment, and finds an analytic solution for a particular equation of state that is quadratic in the two mass-densities and relative velocities. The key hedge is that the analytic solution only applies to this specific quadratic equation of state, and the slow-rotation approximation (keeping only terms up to second order in rotation rates) deteriorates significantly near the Kepler limit, with errors of 15% to 20%.

## Method

The authors generalize the Chandrasekhar-Milne slow-rotation method to a two-fluid model where one fluid represents superfluid neutrons and the other represents a charge-neutral conglomerate of protons, electrons, and crust nuclei. They expand all physical quantities as Taylor series in the two rotation rates Ω_n and Ω_p up to second order, include entrainment via an entrainment function α that couples the momenta of the two fluids, and solve the resulting first integrals of motion together with Poisson's equation for a specific equation of state that is the most general quadratic form in the mass-densities and relative velocity.

## Key numbers

- Proton effective mass ratio m*_p/m_p ranges from approximately 0.3 to 0.7 across nuclear densities, based on calculations by Chao et al (1972) finding 0.6-0.5 for ρ_nuc ≤ ρ ≤ 2.5ρ_nuc, Sjöberg (1976) finding 0.6-0.4 for ρ ≥ ρ_nuc, and Baldo et al (1992) finding approximately 0.7 for the same range as Chao et al.
- Nuclear density ρ_nuc = 2.7 × 10^14 g/cm^3.
- The slow-rotation approximation fails by 15% to 20% near the Kepler limit when compared to numerical results from the LORENE code.
- The Kepler limit is approximated by Ω_K ≈ √(4πGρ̄)/3, where ρ̄ is the mean density.

## Context

This work extends the Newtonian slow-rotation formalism of Prix (1999) by including entrainment, which was excluded in that earlier study, and generalizes the general relativistic slow-rotation scheme of Andersson and Comer (2001) to the Newtonian regime with entrainment included. The formalism enables future linear perturbation calculations of pulsation modes in rotating superfluid neutron stars, which are of interest because several modes are generically unstable due to gravitational radiation emission (Friedman and Schutz 1978; Andersson and Kokkotas 2001), and superfluid-specific modes may be excited during pulsar glitches and produce detectable gravitational waves.
