# Pseudospin Dynamical Symetry in Nuclei

> Pseudospin symmetry has been useful in understanding atomic nuclei. We review the arguments that this symmetry is a relativistic symmetry. The condition for this symmetry is that the sum of the vector and scalar potentials in the Dirac Hamiltonian is a constant. We give the generators of pseudospin symmetry. We review some of the predictions that follow from this insight into the relativistic origins of pseudospin symmetry. Since in nuclei the sum of the scalar and vector potentials is not zero but is small, we discuss preliminary investigations into the conditions on the potentials to produce partial dynamic pseudospin symmetry. Finally we show that approximate pseudospin symmetry in nuclei predicts approximate spin symmetry in anti-nucleon scattering from nuclei.

# Pseudospin Dynamical Symmetry in Nuclei

## Key claim
Pseudospin symmetry in nuclei is a relativistic symmetry of the Dirac Hamiltonian that occurs when the sum of the vector and scalar potentials is a constant, but this condition can never be exactly realized in finite nuclei because no bound nucleon states exist in that limit; the symmetry is therefore only approximate and may be a partial dynamical symmetry.

## Method
The paper reviews the Dirac Hamiltonian with Lorentz scalar and vector potentials, showing that when the sum of these potentials is constant, the Hamiltonian commutes with a set of SU(2) pseudospin generators. The generators are constructed using the helicity unitary operator, and the condition for pseudospin doublets is derived in terms of the radial wavefunctions of the upper Dirac components. For spherical nuclei, the condition that two states form a pseudospin doublet reduces to requiring that certain coefficient functions \(C_{\kappa}^{(i)}\) are equal for the two states in the doublet.

## Key numbers
- The magnetic dipole transition rate calculated for the \(0d_{3/2}\) and \(1s_{1/2}\) pseudospin doublet in \(^{39}\text{K}\) and \(^{39}\text{Ca}\) using the magnetic moment of \(^{39}\text{Ca}\) is about 37% larger than the measured value; modified relations accounting for configuration mixing bring the calculated rate into agreement with experiment within experimental error.
- The non-relativistic shell model calculation of the magnetic dipole transition between the same states gives a rate an order of magnitude lower than the experimental transition, while the relativistic single-nucleon model gives a consistent description of both magnetic dipole and Gamow-Teller transitions in the mass-39 mirror nuclei.

## Context
This work extends the original discovery of pseudospin doublets in spherical nuclei by Arima (1969) and Hecht (1969) and in deformed nuclei by Bohr (1969), and the subsequent identification of pseudospin symmetry as a relativistic symmetry by Ginocchio (1997, 2005). The paper reviews predictions that the lower-component radial wavefunctions of pseudospin doublet states are identical, which was confirmed in relativistic mean field calculations by Ginocchio (1998) and Ring (1998) for spherical nuclei and by Arima (2002) and Ginocchio (2004) for deformed nuclei. The work enables future tests of approximate spin symmetry in anti-nucleon scattering at the GSI anti-proton facility, and opens the question of whether pseudospin symmetry can be realized as a partial dynamical symmetry, with preliminary investigations by Ginocchio and Leviatan.
