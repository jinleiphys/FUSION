# On the Wiedemann-Franz law violation in Graphene and quark-gluon plasma systems

> A comparative study of the thermodynamic and transport properties of the ultra-relativistic quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy ion collisions with the "quasi-relativistic" massless electron-hole plasma in graphene sample has been performed. We observe that the enthalpy per net charge carriers emerges as a useful physical quantity determining the transport variables in hydrodynamic domain. Lorenz ratio is defined as thermal to electrical conductivity ratio, normalized by temperature and Lorenz number $L_{0}=\frac{π^{2}}{3}\left(\frac{k_{B}}{e}\right)^{2}$. The validity of the Wiedemann-Franz law can be checked by evaluating the Lorenz ratio, which is expected to be unity. We investigate the validity of the Wiedemann-Franz law by examining whether the Lorenz ratio equals unity or deviates from it. Our findings indicate that, within the fluid-based framework, the Lorenz ratio consistently leads to a violation of the Wiedemann-Franz law. This is attributed to the proportional relation between Lorenz ratio and enthalpy per net charge carriers in the fluid. Based on the experimental observation, graphene and quark-gluon plasma, both systems at a low net carrier density, violate the Wiedemann-Franz law due to their fluidic nature. However, graphene at a relatively high net carrier density obeys the Wiedemann-Franz law, followed by metals with high Fermi energy or electron density. It indicates a fluid to the non-fluid transition of the graphene system from low to high carrier density. In this regard, the fluid or non-fluid aspect of quark-gluon plasma at high density is yet to be explored by future facilities like Compressed Baryonic Matter and Nuclotron-based Ion Collider fAcility experiments.

## Key claim

The paper claims that within a fluid-based (hydrodynamic) framework, the Lorenz ratio for both graphene and quark-gluon plasma (QGP) consistently deviates from unity, violating the Wiedemann-Franz law, and that this violation is proportional to the enthalpy per net charge carrier; however, graphene at relatively high net carrier density obeys the law, indicating a fluid-to-non-fluid transition.

## Method

The authors developed a microscopic kinetic theory for graphene using the covariant Boltzmann transport equation in the relaxation time approximation (RTA) for a two-band electron-hole system, treating electrons and holes as massless relativistic particles with Fermi velocity as the limiting speed. They derived expressions for electrical and thermal conductivities and the Lorenz ratio from the dissipative part of the particle four-flow and stress-energy tensor, using the Landau-Lifshitz hydrodynamic frame with a constant relaxation time. For QGP, they applied an analogous formalism for a single-flavored u-quark and anti-u-quark plasma to enable direct comparison with the electron-hole plasma of graphene.

## Key numbers

- Lorenz ratio for graphene in the Dirac fluid regime (low net carrier density) shows strong enhancement above unity, with experimental data from Crossno et al. (2016) for samples with carrier density \(n \sim 10^{10} \text{cm}^{-2}\) showing \(L > L_0\) where \(L_0 = \frac{\pi^2}{3} \left( \frac{k_B}{e} \right)^2\).
- The degeneracy factor for graphene is 4, which is the product of spin degeneracy (2) and valley degeneracy (2).
- The Fermi velocity in graphene is \(v_F \approx 10^6 \text{m/s}\), used as the limiting speed in the relativistic formalism.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic fluid dynamics approach previously applied to graphene by Lucas et al. (2016) and to QGP by Jaiswal et al. (2015), and contrasts with the "disorder enabled hydrodynamics" explanation of Zarenia et al. (2019) and the bipolar diffusion model of Tu et al. (2023). The study enables a unified understanding of Wiedemann-Franz law violation in both condensed matter and high-energy physics systems, and suggests that future experiments at the Compressed Baryonic Matter and Nuclotron-based Ion Collider fAcility could explore whether QGP at high baryonic density transitions from fluid to non-fluid behavior, analogous to the graphene case.
