# Lifshitz and Excited State Quantum Phase Transitions in Microwave Dirac Billiards

> We present experimental results for the density of states (DOS) of a superconducting microwave Dirac billiard which serves as an idealized model for the electronic properties of graphene. The DOS exhibits two sharp peaks which evolve into van Hove singularities with increasing system size. They divide the band structure into regions governed by the \emph{relativistic} Dirac equation and by the \emph{non-relativistic} Schrödinger equation, respectively. We demonstrate that in the thermodynamic limit a topological transition appears as a neck-disrupting Lifshitz transition in the number susceptibility and as an excited state transition in the electronic excitations. Furthermore, we recover the finite-size scaling typical for excited state quantum phase transitions involving logarithmic divergences and identify a quasi-order parameter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the topological transition at the saddle points (M points) in the band structure of a microwave Dirac billiard can be identified simultaneously as a neck-disrupting Lifshitz transition in the number susceptibility and as an excited state quantum phase transition (ESQPT) in the electronic excitations, with the crucial hedge that this identification holds only in the absence of fermionic interactions (electron-electron and electron-phonon), which are present in natural graphene but absent in the microwave analog system.

## Method

The authors constructed two superconducting microwave Dirac billiards (photonic crystals) consisting of metallic cylinders in a triangular lattice array between two metal plates, with lattice constants of 20 mm (267 cylinders) and 12 mm (888 cylinders). They measured high-resolution transmission spectra using a Vector Network Analyzer, extracting 1651 eigenfrequencies from resonances with quality factors Q > 5×10^5, and computed the density of states (DOS) by counting states in frequency intervals of 100 MHz. They then compared the experimental DOS to a tight-binding model including nearest-neighbor, second-nearest-neighbor, and third-nearest-neighbor couplings, and computed the Lindhard (particle-hole polarization) function analytically to relate the observed singularities to Lifshitz transitions and ESQPTs.

## Key numbers

- Lattice constants: 20 mm (267 cylinders) and 12 mm (888 cylinders); cylinder radius R = a_L/4
- Billiard dimensions: 420.0 × 249.4 mm²; height h = 3 mm; maximum frequency 50 GHz
- Quality factors: Q > 5×10^5; 1651 eigenfrequencies resolved
- Dirac frequency: f_D = 23.36 GHz; van Hove frequencies: f⁻_VH = 21.98 GHz, f⁺_VH = 24.87 GHz
- Tight-binding fit parameters: t = 4.57, s = 0.26; t₂ = -0.28, s₂ = -0.00001; t₃ = 0.10, s₃ = 0.004
- Finite-size scaling of renormalized DOS maxima: n^max ≈ a N_c (ln(N_c) + b) with universal prefactor a ∼ 0.145–0.155, where N_c is the number of unit cells
- Analytical prefactor from Lifshitz theory: 3/(2π²) ≈ 0.15, confirming the experimental scaling

## Context

This work extends the study of Lifshitz transitions in artificial graphene systems, which had been observed experimentally only recently in cold-atom systems (Tarruell 2012), molecular graphene (Gomes 2012), and microwave tight-binding analogs (Bellec 2013), but not in a gapless topological transition induced by a chemical potential without changing the lattice structure. The microwave Dirac billiard approach avoids the electron-electron and electron-phonon interactions that hampered observation in angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy experiments on doped graphene (McChesney 2010) and that cause broadening and shifting of van Hove singularities in optical conductivity measurements (Mak 2011). The results enable future experimental verification of the full spectral distribution of particle-hole excitations using artificial graphene systems where the Fermi surface can be shifted via doping while electron-hole interactions are turned off.
