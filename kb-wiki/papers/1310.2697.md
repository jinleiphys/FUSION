# Low-energy electric dipole response of Sn isotopes

> We study the low-energy dipole (LED) strength distribution along the Sn isotopic chain in both the isoscalar (IS) and the isovector (IV, or E1) electric channels, to provide testable predictions and guidance for new experiments with stable targets and radioactive beams. We use the self-consistent Quasi-particle Random-Phase Approximation (QRPA) with finite-range interactions and mainly the Gogny D1S force. We analyze also the performance of a realistic two-body interaction supplemented by a phenomenological three-body contact term. We find that from N=50 and up to the N=82 shell closure (132Sn) the lowest-energy part of the IS-LED spectrum is dominated by a collective transition whose properties vary smoothly with neutron number and which cannot be interpreted as a neutron-skin oscillation. For the neutron-rich species this state contributes to the E1 strength below particle threshold, but much more E1 strength is carried by other, weak but numerous transitions around or above threshold. We find that strong structural changes in the spectrum take effect beyond N=82, namely increased LED strength and lower excitation energies. Our results with the Gogny interaction are compatible with existing data. On this basis we predict that a) the summed IS strength below particle threshold shall be of the same order of magnitude for N=50-82, b) the summed E1 strength up to approximately 12 MeV shall be similar for N=50-82 MeV, while c) the summed E1 strength below threshold shall be of the same order of magnitude for N ~ 64 - 82 and much weaker for the lighter, more-symmetric isotopes. We point out a general agreement of our results with other non-relativistic studies, the absence of a collective IS mode in some of those studies, and a possibly radical disagreement with relativistic models.

## Key claim

The low-energy isoscalar dipole (IS-LED) strength in Sn isotopes from N=50 to N=82 is dominated by a collective transition that varies smoothly with neutron number and cannot be interpreted as a neutron-skin oscillation, contrary to some relativistic model predictions; the authors qualify that their QRPA results overestimate excitation energies by about 3 MeV due to the lack of explicit long-range correlations.

## Method

The authors employed self-consistent Quasi-particle Random-Phase Approximation (QRPA) calculations assuming spherical symmetry, using the Gogny D1S finite-range interaction as the primary force and also testing a unitarily transformed Argonne V18 interaction (UCOM(SRG) supplemented with a density-dependent three-body contact term). The QRPA was formulated in the canonical basis of the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) ground state, with single-particle states expanded in a harmonic-oscillator basis of 15 major shells, and all possible two-quasiparticle configurations for J^pi = 1^- were included.

## Key numbers

- The first IS-LED state carries IS strength corresponding to at least 3% and up to about 10% of the total calculated energy-weighted sum of IS strength in the more neutron-rich species.
- For ^124Sn, the calculated IS-LED state appears at about 8.5-9 MeV excitation energy, which after the authors' estimated 3 MeV downward shift agrees with experimental IS strength observed at about 5.5 and 6.5 MeV in (alpha,alpha'gamma) experiments.
- The Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule fraction exhausted up to 27 MeV for the two interactions differs by only up to 15%, with the Gogny D1S interaction yielding results compatible with photoneutron cross section data.
- The spurious center-of-mass state appears at most at a few tens of keV in all cases studied.

## Context

This work extends non-relativistic QRPA studies of dipole response along the Sn isotopic chain, building on prior work by Papakonstantinou et al. (2011, 2012) on Ca isotopes and by Terasaki and Engel (2006) using Skyrme forces. The results agree with other non-relativistic studies but show possibly radical disagreement with relativistic models that predict a collective neutron-skin oscillation mode. The predictions for summed IS and E1 strength below particle threshold for N=50-82 provide testable guidance for planned experiments with stable targets and radioactive beams at facilities such as those studying exotic Sn isotopes.
