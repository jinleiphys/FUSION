# Finite volume and magnetic field effects on the two-pion correlation function in relativistic heavy-ion collisions

> We study the combined effects of a finite volume and an external magnetic field on the charged two-pion correlation function. For these purposes, we consider a dilute system of pions where the finite volume effects are introduced computing the pion wave functions with rigid boundary conditions in a cylindrical geometry in the presence of a uniform and constant magnetic field. We find that for slow pions, namely, for the case where the average pair momentum is small, the correlation function shows a large distortion, as opposed to the case where the average pair momentum is large. For a finite density system, the intercept of the correlation function is reduced, signaling the increasing importance of the pion ground state contribution. An increasing strength of the magnetic field reduces the importance of the ground state and the intercept becomes closer to 2.

## Key claim

The combined effects of finite volume and an external magnetic field distort the charged two-pion correlation function, with the distortion being large for slow pions (small average pair momentum) but not for fast pions; the intercept of the correlation function becomes closer to 2 for increasing magnetic field strength, indicating a reduction of the ground state (condensate) contribution, but this effect is non-monotonic as a function of field intensity.

## Method

The authors solved the Klein-Gordon equation with rigid boundary conditions in a cylindrical geometry (radius R, height L) under a uniform constant magnetic field in the z-direction, using the symmetric gauge and minimal substitution. They computed the Fourier-transformed wave functions and constructed the two-pion correlation function for a dilute, charge-balanced pion system in the grand canonical ensemble, separating the ground state contribution from excited states. The chemical potential was adjusted to fix the total pion number for finite-density cases, and the correlation function was evaluated as a function of the momentum difference q for various average pair momenta P, magnetic field strengths |qB|, temperatures, and cylinder dimensions.

## Key numbers

- For R = 5 fm, L = 10 fm, T = 100 MeV, mu = 0, and P = 500 MeV, changes in the correlation function width appear only when |qB| is greater than or approximately equal to m^2 (where m is the pion mass).
- For R = 5 fm, L = 10 fm, T = 100 MeV, mu = 0, and |qB| = 2 m^2, the width of the correlation function is larger for each considered value of P compared to the |qB| = 0 case.
- For R = 10 fm, L = 5 fm, T = 100 MeV, mu = 0, and |qB| = 2 m^2, the correlation function width first decreases, then increases, then decreases again as P increases.
- For fixed pion multiplicity N = 320, R = 5 fm, L = 10 fm, T = 100 MeV, and P = 300, 500, 700 MeV, the chemical potential takes values such as mu = 178.91 MeV (|qB| = 0), 187.69 MeV (|qB| = 0.5 m^2), 207.14 MeV (|qB| = m^2), 228.51 MeV (|qB| = 1.5 m^2), and 248.65 MeV (|qB| = 2 m^2).
- For N = 320, R = 5 fm, L = 10 fm, T = 150 MeV, and P = 500 MeV, the chemical potential values are mu = 178.67 MeV (|qB| = 0), 187.47 MeV (|qB| = 0.5 m^2), 206.93 MeV (|qB| = m^2), 228.51 MeV (|qB| = 1.5 m^2), and 248.46 MeV (|qB| = 2 m^2).
- The intercept C2(q=0) is a non-monotonic function of |qB| and tends to 2 for the largest field strengths shown (up to |qB| = 2 m^2).

## Context

This work extends the finite-volume study of the two-pion correlation function by Ayala (2001) to include the effects of an external magnetic field, and builds on the finding by Ayala (2016) that a magnetic field increases the critical temperature for pion condensate formation in a finite volume. The results are relevant for interpreting experimental correlation function measurements from relativistic heavy-ion collisions, such as those by ALICE (2015), where a magnetic field of considerable strength is produced in semi-central collisions. The findings enable more accurate extraction of condensate signatures by showing that magnetic field effects, especially for low-momentum charged pions, can distort the correlation function and must be accounted for in analyses.
