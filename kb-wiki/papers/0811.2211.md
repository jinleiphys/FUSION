# Nucleon Form Factors from 5D Skyrmions

> Several aspects of hadron physics are well described by a simple 5D effective field theory. Baryons arise in this scenario as "large" (and therefore calculable) 5D skyrmions. We extend and refine the existing analysis of this 5D soliton, which is fairly non-trivial due to the need of numerical methods. We perform the complete quantization of those collective coordinates which are relevant for computing the static observables like the nucleon form factors. We compare the result with simple expectations about large-N_c QCD and with the experimental data. An agreement within 30% is found.

## Key claim

The 5D Skyrme model predicts nucleon static observables (form factors) with an agreement better than 30% compared to experimental data, except for the axial coupling \(g_A\) which deviates by about 80% (predicted 0.70 versus experimental 1.25); the authors note that an alternative quantization procedure can improve \(g_A\) to 1.17 while leaving other observables unaffected.

## Method

The authors construct a 5D \(U(2)_L \times U(2)_R\) gauge theory on an AdS\(_5\) background with a Chern–Simons term, where baryons appear as topological solitons (5D skyrmions). They numerically solve the time-dependent equations of motion for zero-mode fluctuations around the static soliton, using a 2D ansatz that respects cylindrical symmetry, to determine the collective coordinate dynamics. The collective coordinates are then quantized as a rigid rotor on \(S^3\), and the nucleon form factors are computed from the resulting wave functions.

## Key numbers

* The axial coupling \(g_A\) is predicted to be 0.70, compared to the experimental value of 1.25 (a deviation of about 44% relative to experiment, or 80% of the experimental value).
* With an alternative quantization procedure, \(g_A\) improves to 1.17.
* All other nucleon static observables agree with experimental data within 30%.
* The original 4D Skyrme model gave \(g_A = 0.65\) (Adkins, 1983).

## Context

This work extends the analysis of 5D skyrmions from Pomarol and Wulzer (2008) by performing a complete quantization of collective coordinates and computing nucleon form factors. It contrasts with the Sakai–Sugimoto model (2004), where the small-size expansion (small \(\gamma\)) is used for analytical results (Hashimoto, 2008), whereas here \(\gamma \sim 1\) requires full numerical treatment. The results are significantly more accurate than the original 4D Skyrme model (Adkins, 1983) but not as good as more refined multi-parameter skyrmion models; the authors suggest that including explicit chiral symmetry breaking (pion mass) may improve the agreement, especially for \(g_A\).
