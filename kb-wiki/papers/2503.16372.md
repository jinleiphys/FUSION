# The role of spin-isospin symmetries in nuclear $β$-decays

> A century ago, Wigner's SU(4) symmetry was introduced to explain the properties of atomic nuclei. Despite recent revived interest, its impact on nuclear structure, transitions, and reactions has not been fully explored. Here, we show that a variety of high-fidelity nuclear interactions predict nuclear states that have $\geq 90$\% probability of being in a single SU(4) irreducible representation. Meanwhile, our analysis of axial current operators in chiral effective field theory ($χ$EFT) reveals that one-body currents at low momentum transfer act only within SU(4) irreducible representations, while two-body currents connect different representations. These selection rules interfere with the expected convergence pattern of the $χ$EFT expansion and explain key phenomenological observations, e.g., the unnaturally large two-body corrections to the axial-current matrix elements in eight-body nuclei.

# The role of spin-isospin symmetries in nuclear β-decays

## Key claim

A variety of high-fidelity nuclear interactions predict that nuclear states for A=3-8 have at least 90% probability of being in a single SU(4) irreducible representation, but this symmetry is broken slightly more when three-nucleon forces are included. The approximate SU(4) symmetry explains why one-body Gamow-Teller matrix elements are remarkably stable across different chiral effective field theory orders, while two-body axial currents connect different SU(4) representations and thus produce enhanced corrections in certain transitions.

## Method

The authors performed ab initio No-Core Shell Model (NCSM) calculations for nuclei with A=3-8 using chiral effective field theory (χEFT) interactions at LO, NLO, and N2LO, as well as the NNLOopt interaction without three-nucleon forces. They decomposed the resulting wave functions into SU(4) irreducible representations using a Lanczos-based algorithm that diagonalizes the quadratic Casimir operator, and evaluated Gamow-Teller reduced matrix elements both analytically in the SU(4)-symmetric limit and numerically from the NCSM wave functions.

## Key numbers

- Nuclear states for A=3-8 have more than 90% of their normalization in a specific SU(4) irreducible representation
- For SU(4)-allowed transitions, the relative difference between the analytic SU(4) prediction and ab initio computations is less than 10%, irrespective of the interaction
- In ⁶He, the Gamow-Teller matrix element with a leading-order χEFT wave function is within 4% of the results at O(Q³) (without three-body forces)
- The analytic SU(4) prediction for the ³H(1/2⁺) → ³He(1/2⁺) reduced matrix element is 2.449, compared to NCSM results of 2.267 (LO), 2.332 (NLO), and 2.313 (NNLOopt) at Nmax=18
- The ⁶He(0⁺) → ⁶Li(1⁺) reduced matrix element: SU(4) prediction 2.449, NCSM results 2.178 (LO), 2.259 (NLO), 2.260 (NNLOopt) at Nmax=14
- The ⁸Li(2⁺) → ⁸Be(2⁺) transition (SU(4) forbidden): SU(4) prediction 0.0, NCSM results 0.018 (LO), 0.081 (NLO), 0.093 (NNLOopt) at Nmax=10
- The ⁸He(0⁺) → ⁸Li(1⁺) transition (SU(4) forbidden): SU(4) prediction 0.0, NCSM results 0.066 (LO), 0.364 (NLO), 0.335 (NNLOopt) at Nmax=10
- Two-body corrections to axial-current matrix elements in A=8 nuclei are approximately 30%

## Context

This work extends the revived interest in Wigner's SU(4) symmetry, which was introduced a century ago to explain nuclear properties and has been connected to the large-Nc limit of QCD by Kaplan (1996) and others. The authors build on prior ab initio calculations of β-decay by Gysbers et al. (2019) and King et al. (2020), who observed suppressed Gamow-Teller matrix elements in A=8 systems and large two-body corrections. The SU(4) analysis provides a symmetry-based explanation for these observations, complementing the spatial-structure explanation of Wiringa (2006) and King et al. (2020). The work also connects to the finding by Lin et al. (2022) that SU(4) symmetry explains suppressed M1 capture rates in the A=3 system. This framework enables rigorous uncertainty quantification for β-decay amplitudes needed in searches for physics beyond the Standard Model, and the authors note that characterizing SU(4) content is a prerequisite for such analyses.
