# Dispersion theory of nucleon polarizabilities and outlook on chiral effective field theory

> Based on Compton scattering and meson photoproduction data the polarizabilities of the nucleon are precisely studied and well understood due to recent experimental and theoretical work based on nonsubtracted dispersion relations. The {\it recommended} experimental values are $α_p=12.0\pm 0.6$, $(12.0)$, $β_p=1.9\mp 0.6$, $(1.9)$, $α_n=12.5\pm 1.7$, $(12.7\pm 0.9)$, $β_n=2.7\mp 1.8$, $(2.5\mp 0.9)$ in units of $10^{-4}$fm$^3$ and $γ^{(p)}_π=-36.4\pm 1.5$, $(-36.6)$, $γ^{(n)}_π=+58.6\pm 4.0$, $(58.3)$, $(γ^{(p)}_0=-0.58\pm 0.20)$, $γ^{(n)}_0=0.38\pm 0.22)$ in units of $10^{-4}$fm$^4$ [1]. The numbers given in parentheses are predicted values. # Dispersion theory of nucleon polarizabilities and outlook on chiral effective field theory

## Key claim

The recommended experimental nucleon polarizabilities obtained from nonsubtracted dispersion relations are precise and well-founded, while all versions of chiral effective field theory applied to nucleon polarizabilities and Compton scattering ignore essential effects of omega, rho and sigma meson exchanges and of pseudoscalar pion-nucleon coupling. The chiral EFT predictions for the magnetic polarizability of the proton deviate from the recommended value by a factor of approximately 1.7, and the electric polarizability from one chiral EFT analysis deviates by approximately 1.7 standard deviations.

## Method

The author applies fixed-t dispersion relations of the L'vov et al. (1997) variety to Compton scattering data and meson photoproduction data, using s-channel integrals carried out at constant t and t-channel contributions represented as asymptotic contributions equivalent to a scalar sigma-meson pole term. The t-channel component is calculated via the BEFT sum rule and independently via a sigma-meson pole model with mass m_sigma = 600 MeV, while s-channel components are derived from empirical CGLN amplitudes and the P33(1232) resonance. The results are compared with various chiral EFT calculations including heavy-baryon and relativistic versions with and without explicit Delta degrees of freedom.

## Key numbers

- Recommended proton electric polarizability: alpha_p = 12.0 ± 0.6 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Recommended proton magnetic polarizability: beta_p = 1.9 ∓ 0.6 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Recommended neutron electric polarizability: alpha_n = 12.5 ± 1.7 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Recommended neutron magnetic polarizability: beta_n = 2.7 ∓ 1.8 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Recommended proton backward spin polarizability: gamma_pi^(p) = -36.4 ± 1.5 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
- Recommended neutron backward spin polarizability: gamma_pi^(n) = +58.6 ± 4.0 (in units of 10^-4 fm^4)
- Predicted t-channel contribution to alpha-beta: (alpha-beta)^t = 15.2 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3) from the sigma-meson pole
- s-channel prediction for proton electric polarizability: +4.48 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- s-channel prediction for proton magnetic polarizability: +9.44 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Difference between experimental and s-channel predictions for proton: +7.5 for alpha_p, -7.5 for beta_p (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Sigma-meson mass used in t-channel calculation: m_sigma = 600 MeV
- Predicted neutron polarizabilities from proton data and t-channel: alpha_n = 12.7 ± 0.9, beta_n = 2.5 ∓ 0.9 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- ChPT (Bernard et al., 1992) prediction: alpha_p = 7.4, beta_p = -2.0 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Heavy-baryon ChPT prediction: alpha_p = 12.6, beta_p = 1.3 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)
- Empirical E0+ CGLN amplitude prediction: alpha_p = 3.2, beta_p = -0.3 (in units of 10^-4 fm^3)

## Context

This work extends the dispersion theory framework of L'vov et al. (1997) and the author's own 2005 and 2013 summaries, confirming the recommended polarizabilities against recent chiral EFT analyses by Beane et al. (2003) and McGovern et al. (2013) that yield different values from the same experimental data. The paper demonstrates that the t-channel component, which provides approximately 62% of the electric polarizability and 100% of the diamagnetic polarizability, can be quantitatively predicted from the sigma-meson pole, in contrast to chiral EFT where the corresponding short-distance contribution is treated as an adjustable counterterm. The analysis enables future high-precision neutron experiments by providing benchmark predictions for neutron polarizabilities based on firm theoretical arguments.
