# Constraints on key $^{17}$O($α,γ$)$^{21}$Ne resonances and impact on the weak s-process

> The efficiency of the slow neutron-capture process in massive stars is strongly influenced by neutron-capture reactions on light elements. At low metallicity, $^{16}$O is an important neutron absorber, but the effectiveness of $^{16}$O as a light-element neutron poison is modified by competition between subsequent $^{17}$O$(α,n)^{20}$Ne and $^{17}$O$(α,γ)^{21}$Ne reactions. The strengths of key $^{17}$O$(α,γ)^{21}$Ne resonances within the Gamow window for core helium burning in massive stars are not well constrained by experiment. This work presents more precise measurements of resonances in the energy range $E_{c.m.} = 612 - 1319$ keV. We extract resonance strengths of $ωγ_{638} = 4.85\pm0.79$ $μ$eV, $ωγ_{721} = 13.0^{+3.3}_{-2.4}$ $μ$eV, $ωγ_{814} = 7.72\pm0.55$ meV and $ωγ_{1318} = 136\pm 13$ meV, for resonances at $E_{c.m.} =$ 638, 721, 814 and 1318 keV, respectively. We also report an upper limit for the 612 keV resonance of $ωγ<140$ neV ($95\%$ c.l.), which effectively rules out any significant contribution from this resonance to the reaction rate. From this work, a new $^{17}$O$(α,γ)^{21}$Ne thermonuclear reaction rate is calculated and compared to the literature. The effect of present uncertainties in the $^{17}$O$(α,γ)^{21}$Ne reaction rate on weak s-process yields are then explored using post-processing calculations based on a rotating $20M_{\odot}$ low-metallicity massive star. The resulting $^{17}$O$(α,γ)^{21}$Ne reaction rate is lower with respect to the pre-existing literature and found to enhance weak s-process yields in rotating massive star models.

## Key claim

The new, more precise measurements of key $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne resonances yield a thermonuclear reaction rate that is lower than the pre-existing literature rate by a factor of 4 to 5 in the temperature range relevant for core helium burning (0.2 to 0.3 GK), which in turn enhances weak s-process yields in rotating massive star models; however, the dominant source of uncertainty at these temperatures remains the 305 keV resonance, which is presently inaccessible to direct measurements.

## Method

The $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne reaction was studied in inverse kinematics using the DRAGON facility at TRIUMF, with a $^{17}$O$^{3+}$ beam incident on a windowless helium gas target. Resonance strengths were extracted from thick-target yield measurements at six center-of-mass energies between 612 and 1319 keV, using both singles (recoil only) and coincidence (recoil plus prompt gamma) detection, with improved background suppression achieved by tuning the separator to the $3^{+}$ and $2^{+}$ recoil charge states. A new thermonuclear reaction rate was calculated using a Monte-Carlo code, and its impact on weak s-process yields was explored via one-zone post-processing nucleosynthesis calculations based on a rotating $20M_{\odot}$ low-metallicity ($z=10^{-5}$) massive star model.

## Key numbers

* Resonance strength at $E_{c.m.} = 638$ keV: $\omega\gamma = 4.85 \pm 0.79$ $\mu$eV (uncertainty reduced from 80% to 25% compared to prior work).
* Resonance strength at $E_{c.m.} = 721$ keV: $\omega\gamma = 13.0^{+3.3}_{-2.4}$ $\mu$eV.
* Resonance strength at $E_{c.m.} = 814$ keV: $\omega\gamma = 7.72 \pm 0.55$ meV (adopted weighted average with Best et al. (2011)).
* Resonance strength at $E_{c.m.} = 1318$ keV: $\omega\gamma = 136 \pm 13$ meV (singles data).
* Upper limit for the 612 keV resonance: $\omega\gamma < 140$ neV (95% confidence level), effectively ruling out any significant contribution from this resonance.
* The new reaction rate is lower than the Best et al. (2013) rate by a factor of 4 to 5 in the temperature range 0.2 to 0.3 GK.

## Context

This work extends the direct measurements of Taggart et al. (2019) on the $^{17}$O($\alpha,\gamma$)$^{21}$Ne reaction to lower energies with higher precision, correcting an error in the recoil charge state fractions used in that prior study and superseding its results. The new, lower reaction rate enhances weak s-process yields in rotating massive star models, which is relevant for understanding abundance ratios observed in extremely metal-poor stars as discussed by Chiappini (2011). The dominant remaining uncertainty is the strength of the 305 keV resonance, which is beyond the reach of direct measurements and requires indirect experimental approaches or improved theoretical estimates.
