# Monte Carlo integration in Glauber model analysis of reactions of halo nuclei

> Reaction and elastic differential cross sections are calculated for light nuclei in the framework of the Glauber theory. The optical phase-shift function is evaluated by Monte Carlo integration. This enables us to use the most accurate wave functions and calculate the phase-shift functions without approximation. Examples of proton nucleus (e.g. p-$^6$He, p-$^6$Li) and nucleus-nucleus (e.g. $^6$He$-^{12}$C) scatterings illustrate the effectiveness of the method. This approach gives us a possibility of a more stringent analysis of the high-energy reactions of halo nuclei.

## Key claim

Monte Carlo integration can evaluate the complete Glauber optical phase-shift function without any of the simplifying approximations (such as the optical-limit approximation or density-product assumptions) that have previously been required, enabling the use of realistic, correlated many-body wave functions for both projectile and target.

## Method

The authors compute the optical phase-shift function by Monte Carlo integration of the full multiple-scattering operator, using the Metropolis algorithm to generate integration points weighted by the product of the projectile and target wave functions squared. The wave functions are obtained from quantum Monte Carlo (variational Monte Carlo and Green's function Monte Carlo) calculations with the Argonne \(v_{18}\) two-body and Illinois IL2 three-body interactions for nuclei up to \(A=8\), and from a three-\(\alpha\) microscopic cluster model for \(^{12}\)C. The phase-shift function is evaluated for each discretized impact parameter using the same stored set of configurations, and the reaction and elastic differential cross sections are then obtained by numerical integration over impact parameter.

## Key numbers

- For \(\alpha+\alpha\) scattering at 5.07 GeV/\(c\), the Monte Carlo integration with 100,000 points reproduces the analytically calculated elastic differential cross section and reaction cross section.
- The GFMC matter rms radius of \(^6\)He is 2.61 fm, compared to 2.56 fm from VMC and 2.51 fm from the \(\alpha\)+n+n cluster model.
- For \(^6\)He+\(^{12}\)C at 0.8 GeV/\(A\), the calculated reaction cross section is close to the measured interaction cross section.
- For p+\(^6\)He and p+\(^6\)Li at \(T_{\rm p}=0.7\) GeV, the optical-limit approximation gives a slightly smaller reaction cross section than the full calculation.
- The first dip in the elastic differential cross section for nucleus-\(^{12}\)C scattering at 0.8 GeV/\(A\) moves to smaller angles with increasing mass number, except for \(^6\)He where the halo structure violates the sharp cut-off model expectation.

## Context

This work extends the Glauber theory analysis of reactions by removing the need for approximations such as the optical-limit approximation (OLA) or the use of one-body densities, which previous authors (Bertsch, Ogawa, Al-Khalili, Yabana) had shown to be inadequate for halo nuclei. It builds on the analytic expansion method of B. Abu-Ibrahim (1999) for p+\(^6\)He, which was limited to Gaussian profile functions and light systems. The use of quantum Monte Carlo wave functions from Pieper, Wiringa, and collaborators (2001) provides realistic ab initio nuclear structure input, enabling direct comparison of calculated reaction cross sections with experimental data from Tanihata (1985) and others. The method opens the possibility of testing nuclear wave functions at higher momentum transfer than currently available experimental data.
