# Bjorken flow attractors with transverse dynamics

> In the context of the longitudinally boost-invariant Bjorken flow with transverse expansion, we use three different numerical methods to analyze the emergence of attractor solutions in an ideal gas of massless particles exhibiting constant shear viscosity to entropy density ratio $η/ s$. The fluid energy density is initialized using a Gaussian profile in the transverse plane, while the ratio $χ= \mathcal{P}_L / \mathcal{P}_T$ between the longitudinal and transverse pressures is set at initial time $τ_0$ to a constant value $χ_0$ throughout the system employing the Romatschke-Strickland distribution. We introduce the hydrodynamization time $δτ_H = (τ_H - τ_0)/ τ_0$ based on the time $τ_H$ when the standard deviation $σ(χ)$ of a family of solutions with different $χ_0$ reaches a minimum value at the point of maximum convergence of the solutions. In the $0+1{\rm D}$ setup, $δτ_H$ exhibits scale invariance, being a function only of $(η/ s) / (τ_0 T_0)$. With transverse expansion, we find a similar $δτ_H$ computed with respect to the local initial temperature, $T_0(r)$. We highlight the transition between the regimes where the longitudinal and transverse expansions dominate. We find that the hydrodynamization time required for the attractor solution to be reached increases with the distance from the origin, as expected based on the properties of the $0+1{\rm D}$ system defined by the local initial conditions. We argue that hydrodynamization is predominantly the effect of the longitudinal expansion, being significantly influenced by the transverse dynamics only for small systems or for large values of $η/ s$.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in Bjorken flow with transverse expansion, the hydrodynamization time (when the system loses memory of its initial pressure anisotropy) is predominantly determined by longitudinal expansion, with transverse dynamics significantly affecting hydrodynamization only for small systems or large shear viscosity to entropy density ratios.

## Method

The authors used three numerical methods to analyze attractor solutions in a longitudinally boost-invariant Bjorken flow with transverse expansion: second-order viscous hydrodynamics (vSHASTA), the relaxation time approximation (RTA) of the relativistic Boltzmann equation, and the Boltzmann Approach to Multi-Parton Scattering (BAMPS). They initialized the system with a Gaussian energy density profile in the transverse plane and a constant initial pressure anisotropy ratio χ₀ throughout the system using the Romatschke-Strickland distribution, then tracked the standard deviation σ(χ) of a family of solutions with different χ₀ values to determine the hydrodynamization time τ_H when σ reaches its minimum.

## Key numbers

- The hydrodynamization timescale δτ_H = (τ_H - τ_0)/τ_0 in the 0+1D setup is a function only of (η/s)/(τ_0 T_0), exhibiting scale invariance
- For η/s = 1, the minimum standard deviation σ_min = 4.82 × 10⁻⁴ at τ_H ≈ 1.98 fm (with τ_0 = 0.2 fm and T_0 = 0.5 GeV)
- The hydrodynamization times τ_H for η/s = 0.05, 0.2, 1, and 5 are approximately 0.66, 1.14, 1.98, and 3.42 fm respectively (with τ_0 = 0.2 fm, T_0 = 0.5 GeV)
- In the free-streaming limit of hydrodynamics, the pressure anisotropy χ approaches χ_∞ ≈ -0.132 with a power law decay exponent -2γ where γ = √101/(7√5) ≈ 0.642
- The transseries analysis gives ξ_0 = 6π/5 and γ = 18/35 for the exponential damping factor Ω(ẃ) = ẃ⁻γ exp(-ẃ ξ_0)

## Context

This work extends the study of attractor solutions from the transversally-homogeneous 0+1D Bjorken flow (studied by Heller and Spalinski (2015) and others) to systems with transverse expansion, confirming the expectation from Kurkela et al. (2019) that hydrodynamization in systems with transverse dynamics occurs similarly to the equivalent 0+1D setup when transverse gradients are weaker than longitudinal ones. The results enable quantitative predictions for hydrodynamization times in realistic heavy-ion collision systems of varying sizes (from p-p to A-A collisions) and provide a framework for comparing different kinetic theory and hydrodynamic approaches to early-time dynamics.
