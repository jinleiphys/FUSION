# Efficient emulation of nuclear ground states with neural-network variational Monte Carlo and eigenvector continuation

> An efficient emulator for \emph{ab initio} calculations of nuclear ground-state properties is developed by integrating the neural-network variational Monte Carlo framework, FeynmanNet, with the eigenvector continuation. It enables the calculation of observables for different Hamiltonians with minimal computational cost, while delivering ground-state energies with errors below $0.5\%$ compared to the full FeynmanNet results. With this emulator, the ground-state energies and charge radii of ${}^{16}\mathrm{O}$, ${}^{15}\mathrm{O}$, ${}^{14}\mathrm{O}$, ${}^{15}\mathrm{N}$, and ${}^{14}\mathrm{C}$ are computed using a nuclear Hamiltonian derived from the leading-order pionless effective field theory, with a large number of different values of low-energy constants (LECs). Then, we perform a global sensitivity analysis of the ground-state energies, charge radii, separation energies of selected nuclei for the three LECs in the Hamiltonian, to identify how each LEC contributes to the variances of these observables. It shows that the two-body LEC in the $^3S_1$ channel is the most influential LEC governing these nuclear bulk properties. Finally, the correlations among the ground-state energies of $^4$He, $^{12}$C, and $^{16}$O are investigated by varying the LECs in the Hamiltonian. The analysis reveals that the experimental ground-state energies of $^{12}$C and $^{16}$O cannot be reproduced simultaneously by varying the LECs in the leading-order pionless Hamiltonian. This suggests that additional ingredients in the leading-order Hamiltonian are required to improve its description of light nuclei. The present work establishes an efficient framework for global sensitivity analysis and uncertainty quantification in the quantum Monte Carlo calculations for light and medium-mass nuclei.

## Key claim

By integrating the neural-network variational Monte Carlo framework FeynmanNet with eigenvector continuation, the authors construct an emulator that reproduces full FeynmanNet ground-state energies with errors below 0.5% while reducing computational cost enough to enable global sensitivity analysis; however, the 0.5% accuracy is achieved with only 8 training points, and the authors note that increasing to 10 training points improves accuracy to 0.1%.

## Method

The authors first train FeynmanNet neural-network wave functions at eight training points corresponding to the vertices of a cube in the three-dimensional low-energy constant (LEC) parameter space, where each LEC varies by ±20% around its reference value. They then construct the eigenvector continuation (EC) subspace from these wave functions, computing norm and Hamiltonian matrix elements via Monte Carlo sampling with a geometric-mean estimator that preserves Hermiticity, and apply singular value decomposition with a truncation threshold of ε=10⁻² to stabilize the generalized eigenvalue problem. The emulator is used to compute ground-state energies, charge radii, and separation energies for ¹⁶O, ¹⁵O, ¹⁴O, ¹⁵N, and ¹⁴C using the leading-order pionless effective field theory Hamiltonian (model "o" from Schiavilla (2021)), and Sobol sensitivity indices are estimated via the Saltelli sampling method with 8192 model evaluations.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy emulation errors below 0.5% compared to full FeynmanNet results, with statistical errors between 0.05 MeV and 0.25 MeV for ¹⁵O
- Eight training points (cube vertices) used for EC construction, with each LEC varied by ±20% around reference values
- Approximately 2000 emulated calculations performed in one second on a NVIDIA A800 80GB platform
- Total of 8192 sampled model outputs used for Sobol sensitivity estimation (N=1024, k=3 LECs)
- Truncation threshold ε=10⁻² applied to norm matrix eigenvalues for stable EC emulation
- Reference LEC values: C₀₁ = -5.27518671 fm², C₁₀ = -7.04040080 fm², c_E = 1.0786, with cutoff radii R₀ = 1.54592984 fm and R₁ = 1.83039397 fm

## Context

This work extends the eigenvector continuation approach previously applied to nuclear ground states by Ekstrom (2019) and Koenig (2020) by integrating it with neural-network variational Monte Carlo (FeynmanNet from Yang (2023)), enabling efficient global sensitivity analysis of the essential Hamiltonian (model "o" from Schiavilla (2021)). The analysis reveals that the two-body LEC in the ³S₁ channel (C₁₀) is the most influential parameter governing nuclear bulk properties, and demonstrates that experimental ground-state energies of ¹²C and ¹⁶O cannot be simultaneously reproduced by varying the three LECs in the leading-order pionless Hamiltonian, suggesting that additional Hamiltonian ingredients are needed. This framework enables future uncertainty quantification and sensitivity studies for light and medium-mass nuclei using quantum Monte Carlo methods.
