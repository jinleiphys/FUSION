# Models for RHIC and LHC: New Developments

> We outline inconsistencies in presently used models for high energy nuclear scattering, which make their application quite unreliable. Many "successes" are essentially based on an artificial freedom of parameters, which does not exist when the models are constructed properly. The problem is the fact that any multiple scattering theory requires an appropriate treatment of the energy sharing between the individual interactions, which is technically very difficult to implement. Lacking a satisfying solution to this problem, it has been simply ignored. We introduce a fully self-consistent formulation of the multiple-scattering scheme. Inclusion of soft and hard components - very crucial at high energies - appears in a "natural way", providing a smooth transition from soft to hard physics. We can show that the effect of appropriately considering energy conservation has a big influence on the results, and MUST therefore be included in any serious calculation.

## Key claim

Existing models for high-energy nuclear scattering contain inconsistencies because they ignore the proper treatment of energy sharing between multiple interactions, and many claimed "successes" rely on artificial parameter freedom that disappears when models are constructed self-consistently. The authors introduce a fully self-consistent formulation of the multiple-scattering scheme where energy conservation is treated identically in both cross-section calculations and particle production.

## Method

The authors develop a "Parton-based Gribov-Regge Theory" that treats nucleus-nucleus scattering via the Glauber-Gribov approach, where the scattering amplitude is defined as a sum of contributions from multiple elementary scattering processes between partonic constituents. They define the model via the elastic scattering amplitude, which consists of purely parallel elementary interactions, and enforce momentum conservation through explicit integration over momentum fractions of partons leaving each nucleon. The inelastic cross section is obtained by summing over classes of interference terms (cut diagrams) that contribute to the same final state, keeping explicit dependence on longitudinal momenta to assure energy conservation at every level.

## Key numbers

- The inelastic cross section is expressed as \(\sigma _{AB}(s)=\int d^{2}b\: \sum _{K}\Omega ^{(s,b)}(K)\), where the integration includes impact parameter \(b_0\) and averaging over nuclear coordinates for projectile and target
- The variable \(K\) is characterized by \(AB\) numbers \(m_k\) representing the number of cut elementary diagrams for each possible pair of nucleons, plus all momentum fractions \(x^{+}\) and \(x^{-}\) of these elementary interactions

## Context

This work addresses inconsistencies in the Gribov-Regge approach and the eikonalized parton model as applied to high-energy nuclear collisions, specifically the problem that energy conservation is treated differently in cross-section calculations versus particle production. The authors cite prior work by Gribov (1968, 1969) on the original Gribov-Regge theory, Glauber (1959) on the Glauber approach, and Sjostrand (1987) on the parton model, as well as noting that existing recipes for energy sharing in Monte Carlo applications are not reflected in cross-section calculations (Braun 1990, Abramovsky 1992). The new formalism enables practical calculations using Markov chain Monte Carlo techniques, providing a basis for interpreting data from the RHIC and LHC programs.
