# Classical Physics and Quantum Loops

> The standard picture of the loop expansion associates a factor of h-bar with each loop, suggesting that the tree diagrams are to be associated with classical physics, while loop effects are quantum mechanical in nature. We discuss examples wherein classical effects arise from loop contributions and display the relationship between the classical terms and the long range effects of massless particles.

# Classical Physics and Quantum Loops

## Key claim

The standard claim that the loop expansion in quantum field theory is equivalent to an expansion in powers of ℏ is not generally valid; classical effects can arise from one-loop diagrams when two or more massless propagators are present, producing nonanalytic square-root terms in momentum space that are independent of ℏ.

## Method

The authors compute one-loop QED corrections to the matrix element of the energy-momentum tensor between spinless plane-wave states, extracting the form factors F₁(q²) and F₂(q²). They Fourier transform the momentum-space results to coordinate space and separate terms by their ℏ dependence, then compare the ℏ-independent pieces to the classical energy-momentum tensor of the electromagnetic field of a point charge. The analysis is extended to graviton loops, to the interaction of a charge with a polarizable neutral system, and to gravitational potentials, with a dispersive treatment using Cutkosky rules to trace the origin of classical terms to the on-shell contributions of massless particles.

## Context

This work extends and contradicts the standard textbook argument (found in many field theory texts) that the loop expansion coincides with an expansion in ℏ. The authors provide explicit counterexamples showing that one-loop diagrams containing two or more massless propagators generate classical ℏ-independent terms, identified by square-root nonanalytic behavior in momentum space. The results build on prior calculations of energy-momentum tensor form factors by Donoghue and collaborators (1994), on the dispersive treatment of triangle diagrams by Scharf (1964), and on the two-photon exchange potential calculations by Sucher and Feinberg (1975) and by Bernabeu and Tarrach (1980). The work enables a clearer separation of classical and quantum effects in effective field theory treatments of gravity and electromagnetism, and establishes that classical corrections from loop diagrams are associated with the infrared behavior of massless particle propagation.
