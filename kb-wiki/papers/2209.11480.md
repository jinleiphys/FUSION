# Stellar neutron capture reactions at low and high temperature

> The determination of astrophysical reaction rates requires different approaches depending on the conditions in hydrostatic and explosive burning. The focus here is on astrophysical reaction rates for radiative neutron capture reactions. Relevant nucleosynthesis processes not only involve the s-process but also the i-, r- and $γ$-processes, which from the nuclear perspective mainly differ in the relative interaction energies of neutrons and nuclei, and in the nuclear level densities of the involved nuclei. Emphasis is put on the difference between reactions at low and high temperature. Possible complications in the prediction and measurement of these reaction rates are illustrated and the connection between theory and experiment is addressed.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the astrophysical reaction rate for radiative neutron capture cannot be directly constrained by laboratory measurements for many nuclides even at s-process temperatures, because thermally excited states in the stellar plasma contribute significantly to the rate; the commonly used stellar enhancement factor (SEF) systematically underestimates the importance of excited-state contributions by neglecting the partition function.

## Method

The author reviews the formal definitions of astrophysical reaction rates, stellar cross sections, and Maxwellian Averaged Cross Sections (MACS), then derives the ground-state contribution \(X_0^*\) to the stellar reactivity as a function of temperature. Using tabulated nuclear data for stable and near-stable nuclides, the author computes \(X_0^*\) values and identifies nuclides for which the ground-state contribution drops to 80% or below at specific temperatures, comparing these results with the misleading SEF values. The analysis covers the temperature regimes of the s-process (8–90 keV), i-process, r-process, and \(\gamma\)-process.

## Key numbers

* At \(\mathrm{k_B}T = 30\) keV, \(^{57}\mathrm{Fe}\) has a ground-state contribution \(X_0^* = 0.39\) but a stellar enhancement factor \(f_{\mathrm{SEF}} = 1.1\), showing the SEF underestimates excited-state contributions.
* The effective energy for dominant contributions to the rate integral is \(E_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 1.72 \times 10^{-10} T (\ell + 1/2)\) MeV, with width \(\Delta_E \approx 1.94 \times 10^{-10} T \sqrt{\ell + 1/2}\) MeV, when temperature is in K.
* The s-process temperature range spans from \(\mathrm{k_B}T = 8\) keV (0.09 GK, AGB interpulse burning) to \(\mathrm{k_B}T = 90\) keV (1.04 GK, C-shell burning in massive stars).
* The r-process proceeds at 1–2 GK, and the \(\gamma\)-process operates at 2–3.5 GK.
* A table lists 80 naturally occurring nuclides up to Bi with \(X_0^* \leq 0.8\) at \(\mathrm{k_B}T = 30\) keV, including \(^{57}\mathrm{Fe}\), \(^{151}\mathrm{Sm}\), \(^{153}\mathrm{Gd}\), and \(^{180}\mathrm{Ta}\).

## Context

This work extends the discussion of stellar reaction rates from the classical s-process model of Clayton (1961) and the MACS approach pioneered by Beer and Kappeler (1979, 1980) to modern nucleosynthesis simulations that require rates across a wide temperature range. The paper explicitly corrects the common misuse of the stellar enhancement factor, following Rauscher (2011) and Rauscher (2012), and provides a systematic mapping of ground-state contributions that enables experimentalists to identify which nuclides require combined theory-experiment approaches rather than pure measurements. The analysis enables improved reaction rate libraries for full network calculations in s-process, i-process, r-process, and \(\gamma\)-process simulations.
