# Combustion of a hadronic star into a quark star: the turbulent and the diffusive regimes

> We argue that the full conversion of a hadronic star into a quark or a hybrid star occurs within two different regimes separated by a critical value of the density of the hadronic phase $\overline{n_h}$. The first stage, occurring for $n_h>\overline{n_h}$, is characterized by turbulent combustion and lasts typically a few ms. During this short time-scale neutrino cooling is basically inactive and the star heats up thanks to the heat released in the conversion. In the second stage, occurring for $n_h<\overline{n_h}$, turbulence is not active anymore, and the conversion proceeds on a much longer time scale (of the order of tens of seconds), with a velocity regulated by the diffusion and the production of strange quarks. At the same time, neutrino cooling is also active. The interplay between the heating of the star due to the slow conversion of its outer layers (with densities smaller than $\overline{n_h}$) and the neutrino cooling of the forming quark star leads to a quasi-plateau in the neutrino luminosity which, if observed, would possibly represent a unique signature for the existence of quark matter inside compact stars. We will discuss the phenomenological implications of this scenario in particular in connection with the time structure of long gamma-ray-bursts.

# Combustion of a hadronic star into a quark star: the turbulent and the diffusive regimes

## Key claim

The conversion of a hadronic star into a quark or hybrid star proceeds in two distinct regimes separated by a critical hadronic density $\overline{n_h}$, rather than stopping entirely after the initial turbulent phase; the slow diffusive second stage produces a quasi-plateau in neutrino luminosity that could serve as a unique observational signature of quark matter in compact stars, provided the Coll's condition for exothermicity is violated at lower densities.

## Method

The authors model the conversion as a two-stage process: first, a turbulent deflagration driven by Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities (treated via the flamelet approximation with Hugoniot jump conditions), which converts the inner high-density region on millisecond timescales; second, a slow laminar combustion governed by strangeness diffusion and weak interaction rates ($u+d \rightarrow u+s$), described by a steady-state transport equation for the strangeness imbalance $a(x)$ coupled to the thermal evolution of the star. The critical density $\overline{n_h}$ separating the regimes is determined by the Coll's equality $e_h(p,X)=e_q(p,X)$, where the energy densities of hadronic and quark phases are equal at fixed pressure and dynamical volume. For the diffusive regime, the authors solve coupled differential equations for the flame front position $r_f(t)$ and the star's temperature $T(t)$, assuming infinite thermal conductivity and black-body neutrino surface emission.

## Key numbers

- Laminar burning velocities from kinetic theory: $10^3-10^4$ cm/sec for quark chemical potential $\mu_q \sim 300$ MeV and temperature $T \sim 10$ MeV
- Quark diffusion coefficient: $D = 0.1 (\mu_q/300\,\mathrm{MeV})^{2/3} (T/10\,\mathrm{MeV})^{-5/3}$ cm$^2$/sec
- Weak interaction timescale: $\tau = 1.3 \times 10^{-9} (300\,\mathrm{MeV}/\mu_q)^5$ sec
- Combustion zone width: $\delta \sim 10^{-5}$ cm, estimated from $\delta \sim \sqrt{D\tau}$
- Neutrino diffusion timescale from rapid core conversion: $\tau \sim 3$ s
- Energy released during rapid core conversion: $Q \sim 8.5 \times 10^{52}$ erg
- Unburnt hadronic layer after turbulent regime: approximately $0.5 M_{\odot}$ with width about 3 km, for a $1.5 M_{\odot}$ hadronic star with total baryonic mass $M_B = 1.71 M_{\odot}$
- Neutron drip line density: $n_d = 2.6 \times 10^{-4}$ fm$^{-3}$
- Heat capacity of the star: $C = 2 \times 10^{39} M/M_{\odot} (T/10^9)$ erg/K
- Initial temperature of the outer layer after turbulent regime: approximately 5 MeV

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamical simulations of Herzog (2011) and Pagliara (2013), which showed that turbulent combustion leaves a sizable fraction (few $0.1 M_{\odot}$) of the star unburnt, by demonstrating that the conversion does not truly stop but proceeds via a slow diffusive regime. The authors build on the kinetic theory approach of Olinto (1987) for laminar burning velocities and the flamelet treatment of Lugones (1994) and Drago (2005), while introducing the Coll's condition (Coll, 1978) as the criterion separating turbulent from diffusive regimes. The predicted quasi-plateau in neutrino luminosity provides a potential observational test for the existence of quark matter in compact stars, with implications for understanding the time structure of long gamma-ray bursts.
