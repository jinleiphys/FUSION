# Exploring properties of high-density matter through remnants of neutron-star mergers

> Remnants of neutron-star mergers are essentially massive, hot, differentially rotating neutron stars, which are initially strongly oscillating. They represent a unique probe for high-density matter because the oscillations are detectable via gravitational-wave measurements and are strongly dependent on the equation of state. The impact of the equation of state is apparent in the frequency of the dominant oscillation mode of the remnant. For a fixed total binary mass a tight relation between the dominant postmerger frequency and the radii of nonrotating neutron stars exists. Inferring observationally the dominant postmerger frequency thus determines neutron star radii with high accuracy of the order of a few hundred meters. By considering symmetric and asymmetric binaries of the same chirp mass, we show that the knowledge of the binary mass ratio is not critical for this kind of radius measurements. We summarize different possibilities to deduce the maximum mass of nonrotating neutron stars. We clarify the nature of the three most prominent features of the postmerger gravitational-wave spectrum and argue that the merger remnant can be considered to be a single, isolated, self-gravitating object that can be described by concepts of asteroseismology. The understanding of the different mechanisms shaping the gravitational-wave signal yields a physically motivated analytic model of the gravitational-wave emission, which may form the basis for template-based gravitational-wave data analysis. We explore the observational consequences of a scenario of two families of compact stars including hadronic and quark stars. We find that this scenario leaves a distinctive imprint on the postmerger gravitational-wave signal. In particular, a strong discontinuity in the dominant postmerger frequency as function of the total mass will be a strong indication for two families of compact stars. (abridged)

## Key claim

The dominant postmerger gravitational-wave frequency of a neutron-star merger remnant can determine neutron-star radii with an accuracy of a few hundred meters, but this precision relies on the existence of a tight empirical relation between that frequency and the radius of a nonrotating neutron star of a specific fiducial mass, with the largest deviation from the fit being only about 175 meters for a total binary mass of 2.7 solar masses when using a fiducial mass of 1.6 solar masses.

## Method

The authors performed numerical-relativity simulations of neutron-star mergers for multiple equations of state, varying total binary masses (2.4, 2.7, and 3.0 solar masses) and mass ratios, including symmetric 1.35-1.35 solar-mass mergers and asymmetric 1.2-1.5 solar-mass mergers with the same chirp mass. They extracted the dominant postmerger gravitational-wave peak frequency from each simulation and correlated it with the radius of a nonrotating neutron star computed from the same equation of state via the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations, using a fiducial mass of 1.6 solar masses to tighten the empirical relation.

## Key numbers

* For a total binary mass of 2.7 solar masses, the maximum deviation between the dominant postmerger frequency and the fit to the radius of a 1.6-solar-mass nonrotating neutron star is about 175 meters.
* For symmetric and asymmetric binaries with the same chirp mass of 1.1665 solar masses, the data points deviate by at most 258 meters from a fit constructed from both symmetric and asymmetric data.
* The peak frequency can be measured with an accuracy of about 10 Hz using a coherent burst search analysis with simulated Advanced LIGO and Virgo noise.
* For a chirp mass of 1.1752 solar masses, the total binary mass is constrained to the range 2.7 to 2.73 solar masses without any information about the mass ratio.
* For a chirp mass of 1.3058 solar masses, the total binary mass can be inferred with a precision of plus or minus 0.05 solar masses.

## Context

This work extends the empirical relation between postmerger peak frequency and neutron-star radius established by Bauswein (2012) and Bauswein (2012) to asymmetric binaries and multiple total masses, confirming that knowledge of the binary mass ratio is not critical for radius measurements. It also clarifies the nature of the three most prominent spectral features and argues that the merger remnant can be described by asteroseismology concepts from Stergioulas (2011). The results enable future gravitational-wave detectors like Advanced LIGO and the Einstein Telescope to constrain the high-density equation of state from postmerger signals, complementing inspiral-based tidal deformability measurements.
