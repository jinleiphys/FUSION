# Shell stabilizes nuclear instability: A new phenomenon

> Infinite nuclear matter mass model and the relativistic mean field theory show strong evidence of new neutron magic numbers 100, 150, 164; proton magic number 78 and new islands of stability around N=100, Z $\simeq$ 62; N=150, Z=78; and N= 164, Z $\simeq$ 90 in the drip-line regions of nuclear chart. It is shown that shell effect stabilizes here the instability due to nuclear force giving rise to a new phenomenon; in contrast to the phenomena of fission isomer and super heavy elements where Coulomb instability is overcome by the same.

# Shell stabilizes nuclear instability: A new phenomenon

## Key claim
Shell effects can stabilize nuclei against instability arising from the repulsive part of the nuclear force itself (not Coulomb repulsion), producing new magic numbers and islands of stability in the neutron-rich drip-line regions. This is presented as a phenomenon complementary to fission isomers and superheavy elements, where shell effects overcome Coulomb instability instead.

## Method
The authors used two complementary approaches: the infinite nuclear matter (INM) mass model, which extracts the local energy η(A,Z) from experimental binding energies and extrapolates to unknown nuclei using the generalized Hugenholtz-Van Hove theorem; and relativistic mean field (RMF) theory with NL3 and NL-RA1 interactions, employing constant-gap BCS pairing and harmonic oscillator basis with 12 major shells. Shell correction energies were calculated using the Strutinsky prescription from the RMF single-particle states.

## Key numbers
- Shell correction energies at the predicted magic numbers: −4.3 MeV at N=100 (Z≈62), −3.1 MeV at N=150 (Z=78), and −2.9 MeV at N=164 (Z≈90), compared to typical values of −10 MeV for N=82 and 126 in the valley of stability
- New neutron magic numbers identified: N=100, N=150, N=164
- New proton magic number identified: Z=78
- New islands of stability: N=100, Z≈62; N=150, Z=78; N=164, Z≈90
- In RMF calculations, the neutron energy gaps at N=100, 150, and 164 persist when proton number is varied between Z=56-62, Z=74-86, and Z=88-94 respectively

## Context
This work extends prior studies of shell quenching and new magic numbers in exotic nuclei, building on the INM mass model developed by Satpathy and collaborators (2000-2003) and Nayak (2003). The prediction of N=164 as a magic number had been anticipated in earlier theoretical studies. The results suggest that the traditional magic numbers from the valley of stability do not remain valid as one moves toward the drip-line, and that the nuclear landscape may have a "fuzzed out coast" with tiny islands of stability rather than a sharp boundary. This opens the possibility for experimental searches for these new islands using radioactive ion beam facilities.
