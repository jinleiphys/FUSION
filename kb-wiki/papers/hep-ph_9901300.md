# Supernova Neutrinos and the Neutrino Masses

> Core-collapse supernovae emit of order $10^{58}$ neutrinos and antineutrinos of all flavors over several seconds, with average energies of 10--25 MeV. In the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), a future Galactic supernova at a distance of 10 kpc would cause several hundred events. The $ν_μ$ and $ν_τ$ neutrinos and antineutrinos are of particular interest, as a test of the supernova mechanism. In addition, it is possible to measure or limit their masses by their delay (determined from neutral-current events) relative to the $\barν_e$ neutrinos (determined from charged-current events). Numerical results are presented for such a future supernova as seen in SNO. Under reasonable assumptions, and in the presence of the expected counting statistics, a $ν_μ$ or $ν_τ$ mass down to about 30 eV can be simply and robustly determined. This seems to be the best technique for direct measurement of these masses.

## Key claim

Under reasonable assumptions about supernova neutrino emission and with the expected counting statistics in SNO, a muon or tau neutrino mass down to about 30 eV can be simply and robustly determined by measuring the time-of-flight delay of neutral-current events relative to charged-current events, though this sensitivity depends on using the abundant antineutrino events from SuperKamiokande as a reference clock.

## Method

The author calculates the expected event rates for a future Galactic supernova at 10 kpc in the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), using the neutral-current deuteron breakup reaction (485 events) as the signal for muon and tau neutrinos, and the charged-current inverse beta decay on protons in light water as the reference for massless antineutrinos. A Monte Carlo simulation generates 10,000 statistical realizations of the supernova data for various tau neutrino masses, and the average arrival time difference between the signal and reference events is used as the test statistic. The sensitivity is determined from the distribution of this time difference at different confidence levels.

## Key numbers

- A supernova at 10 kpc would produce about 485 neutral-current events from deuteron breakup in 1 kton of heavy water in SNO, and about 365 charged-current events from antineutrinos on protons in 1.4 kton of light water.
- The SNO detector is sensitive to a tau neutrino mass down to about 30 eV when using the SuperKamiokande reference, and down to about 35 eV when using the SNO reference.
- The average delay in arrival time for a 30 eV neutrino mass is about 0.15 seconds, despite the intrinsic pulse duration of about 10 seconds.
- The dispersion in arrival times becomes statistically significant only for masses of order 80 eV or greater.
- The characteristic energy for the delay in SNO is about 32 MeV.
- The mass limit scales as m_limit ~ T^(3/4) * sqrt(tau), where T is the neutrino temperature and tau is the luminosity timescale, and is independent of the distance to the supernova.

## Context

This work extends the time-of-flight technique previously applied to electron neutrinos by Totani (1998), who showed sensitivity to a 3 eV electron neutrino mass, to the case of muon and tau neutrinos where individual neutrino energies cannot be measured. The author builds on prior calculations for neutral-current signals in SuperKamiokande (Beacom and Vogel, 1998) which gave a sensitivity of about 45 eV, and in SNO (Beacom and Vogel, 1998) which gave a sensitivity of about 30 eV. The technique enables a nearly six order of magnitude improvement over current laboratory limits on muon and tau neutrino masses, and is relevant to testing seesaw models that predict a tau neutrino mass as large as about 30 eV.
