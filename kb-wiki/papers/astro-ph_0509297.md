# Direct Measurement of Supernova Neutrino Emission Parameters with a Gadolinium-Enhanced Super-Kamiokande Detector

> The time-integrated luminosity and average energy of the neutrino emission spectrum are essential diagnostics of core-collapse supernovae. The SN 1987A electron antineutrino observations by the Kamiokande-II and IMB detectors are only roughly consistent with each other and theory. Using new measurements of the star formation rate history, we reinterpret the Super-Kamiokande upper bound on the electron antineutrino flux from all past supernovae as an excluded region in neutrino emission parameter space. A gadolinium-enhanced Super-Kamiokande should be able to jointly measure these parameters, and a future megaton-scale detector would enable precision studies.

# Direct Measurement of Supernova Neutrino Emission Parameters with a Gadolinium-Enhanced Super-Kamiokande Detector

## Key claim

Using improved measurements of the star formation rate history, the Super-Kamiokande upper bound on the electron antineutrino flux from all past supernovae can be reinterpreted as an excluded region in the plane of time-integrated luminosity and average energy, and a gadolinium-enhanced Super-Kamiokande detector should be able to jointly measure these parameters within 5 years, provided the true neutrino emission parameters are near the edge of current detectability.

## Method

The authors calculate the Diffuse Supernova Neutrino Background (DSNB) flux by convolving the core-collapse supernova rate as a function of redshift (derived from GALEX star formation rate data) with the neutrino emission per supernova, parameterized by time-integrated luminosity and average energy assuming a thermal emission spectrum. They reinterpret the existing Super-Kamiokande 90% C.L. flux upper limit of 1.2 cm^-2 s^-1 above 18 MeV as an excluded region in the emission parameter space, then simulate expected event spectra over 5 years for a gadolinium-enhanced detector with reduced backgrounds and lower energy threshold, fitting the spectra of DSNB and background events simultaneously using chi-squared analysis in 4-MeV bins.

## Key numbers

- Super-Kamiokande DSNB flux upper limit: 1.2 cm^-2 s^-1 (90% C.L.) for electron antineutrinos with detection energy threshold of 18 MeV (Malek, 2002)
- Super-Kamiokande event rate limit: approximately 2 yr^-1 in the 18-26 MeV range
- Point A (near SK bound): average energy 11 MeV, time-integrated luminosity 32 x 10^52 erg
- Point B (near SK bound): average energy 15 MeV, time-integrated luminosity 10 x 10^52 erg
- Point C (near SK bound): average energy 25 MeV, time-integrated luminosity 3.5 x 10^52 erg
- Point D (canonical values): average energy 15 MeV, time-integrated luminosity 5 x 10^52 erg
- Canonical neutron star binding energy: 3 x 10^53 erg, assumed shared equally among six flavors
- GALEX star formation rate normalization uncertainty: approximately 30%
- For point D, a gadolinium-enhanced SK would detect approximately 15 events in 5 years

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the SN 1987A neutrino observations by Kamiokande-II and IMB (1987Afit1, 1987Afit2), which yielded banana-shaped allowed regions in the luminosity-energy plane that barely overlap with each other or with canonical theoretical expectations. The authors reinterpret the Super-Kamiokande DSNB limit (Malek, 2002) using improved star formation rate data from GALEX (2003) and the Concordance Model (Ando, 2004), showing that the excluded region is encouragingly close to the SN 1987A allowed regions. The proposed gadolinium-enhanced Super-Kamiokande (GADZOOKS, Beacom and Vagins, 2003) would reduce backgrounds by a factor of approximately 5 and remove spallation backgrounds in the 10-18 MeV range, enabling joint measurement of emission parameters. A future megaton-scale detector would enable precision studies and could accumulate events from identified supernovae within 10 Mpc (PEEPING, Ando et al., 2005).
