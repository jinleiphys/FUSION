# Photonuclear reactions of actinides in the giant dipole resonance region

> Photonuclear reactions at energies covering the giant dipole resonance (GDR) region are analyzed with an approach based on nuclear photoabsorption followed by the process of competition between light particle evaporation and fission for the excited nucleus. The photoabsorption cross section at energies covering the GDR region is contributed by both the Lorentz type GDR cross section and the quasideuteron cross section. The evaporation-fission process of the compound nucleus is simulated in a Monte-Carlo framework. Photofission reaction cross sections are analyzed in a systematic manner in the energy range of $\sim$ 10-20 MeV for the actinides $^{232}$Th, $^{238}$U and $^{237}$Np. Photonuclear cross sections for the medium-mass nuclei $^{63}$Cu and $^{64}$Zn, for which there are no fission events, are also presented. The study reproduces satisfactorily the available experimental data of photofission cross sections at GDR energy region and the increasing trend of nuclear fissility with the fissility parameter $Z^2/A$ for the actinides.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a two-step model (photoabsorption followed by Monte-Carlo evaporation-fission competition) reproduces experimental photofission cross sections for actinides in the GDR region (10–20 MeV) "satisfactorily," but the authors acknowledge that calculated photofission cross sections are systematically overestimated compared to experimental data, implying that the model predicts more neutron evaporation than observed.

## Method

The authors calculate total photoabsorption cross sections as the sum of a Lorentzian GDR component (with two resonance lines for deformed nuclei) and a quasideuteron component. The excited compound nucleus then undergoes de-excitation via light-particle evaporation (n, p, α, etc.) and fission, simulated using a Hauser-Feshbach Monte-Carlo routine with 40,000 events per calculation. Fission widths are computed using the Bohr-Wheeler statistical approach with Sierk's diffuse-surface fission barriers, and Gilbert-Cameron level densities are adopted.

## Key numbers

- For \(^{237}\)Np at \(E_\gamma = 15\) MeV: calculated GDR photoabsorption cross section \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 392.6\) mb, calculated photofission cross section \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 391.7\) mb.
- For \(^{238}\)U at \(E_\gamma = 15\) MeV: calculated \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 360.0\) mb, calculated \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 336.7\) mb; experimental \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 134\) mb.
- For \(^{232}\)Th at \(E_\gamma = 15\) MeV: calculated \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 355.6\) mb, calculated \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 66.5\) mb; experimental \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 37\) mb.
- For \(^{237}\)Np at \(E_\gamma = 10\) MeV: calculated \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 244.0\) mb, calculated \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 237.0\) mb; experimental \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 134\) mb.
- For \(^{238}\)U at \(E_\gamma = 10\) MeV: calculated \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 269.1\) mb, calculated \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 186.4\) mb; experimental \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 59\) mb.
- For \(^{232}\)Th at \(E_\gamma = 10\) MeV: calculated \(\sigma_a^{GDR} = 247.5\) mb, calculated \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 17.7\) mb; experimental \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 16\) mb.
- The quasideuteron contribution to photofission is very small compared to the GDR contribution; e.g., for \(^{238}\)U at 15 MeV, \(\sigma_f^{QD} = 5.011\) mb versus \(\sigma_f^{GDR} = 336.7\) mb.
- For medium-mass nuclei \(^{63}\)Cu and \(^{64}\)Zn at 15–20 MeV, no fission events occur in the Monte-Carlo simulation.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous Monte-Carlo evaporation-fission model (Mukhopadhyay and Basu, 2007, 2009), which was applied to the quasideuteron energy region, down into the GDR energy region (10–20 MeV) for actinides. The study is motivated by the use of photofission of uranium for producing neutron-rich radioactive ion beams, where the GDR region is particularly important. The calculated photoabsorption cross sections agree well with experimental data from Veyssiere et al. (1973), but the systematic overestimation of photofission cross sections suggests either a need for more precise experimental measurements or refinements in the evaporation-fission competition modeling.
