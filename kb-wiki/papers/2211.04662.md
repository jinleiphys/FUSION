# Reverse phase transitions in binary neutron-star systems with exotic-matter cores

> Multi-messenger observations of binary neutron star mergers provide a unique opportunity to constrain the dense-matter equation of state. Although it is known from quantum chromodynamics that hadronic matter will undergo a phase transition to exotic forms of matter, e.g., quark matter, the onset density of such a phase transition cannot be computed from first principles. Hence, it remains an open question if such phase transitions occur inside isolated neutron stars or during binary neutron star mergers, or if they appear at even higher densities that are not realized in the Cosmos. In this article, we perform numerical-relativity simulations of neutron-star mergers and investigate scenarios in which the onset density of such a phase transition is exceeded in at least one inspiralling binary component. Our simulations reveal that shortly before the merger it is possible that such stars undergo a "reverse phase transition", i.e., densities decrease and the quark core inside the star disappears leaving a purely hadronic star at merger. After the merger, when densities increase once more, the phase transition occurs again and leads, in the cases considered in this work, to a rapid formation of a black hole. We compute the gravitational-wave signal and the mass ejection for our simulations of such scenarios and find clear signatures that are related to the post-merger phase transition, e.g., smaller ejecta masses due to the softening of the equation of state through the quark core formation. Unfortunately, we do not find measurable imprints of the reverse phase transition.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that in binary neutron-star mergers where at least one star initially contains a quark-matter core, tidal deformation during the inspiral can cause the central density to drop below the phase-transition threshold, making the quark core disappear before merger (a "reverse phase transition"); however, the authors find no measurable imprint of this reverse phase transition on the gravitational-wave signal or ejecta masses given the numerical precision of their simulations.

## Method

The authors performed numerical-relativity simulations of binary neutron-star mergers using the BAM code with four configurations: two without phase transitions (noPT1, noPT2) and two with first-order phase transitions to quark matter (PT1, PT2), all tuned to the same binary tidal deformability of 250. The equations of state were constructed from chiral effective field theory at low densities with a piecewise-linear speed-of-sound extension at higher densities, and the phase transition was imposed by setting the speed of sound to zero within one piece. Initial data were generated with the SGRID code, and simulations began 14 to 15 orbits before merger at an initial separation of about 75 km.

## Key numbers

- Phase transition onset baryon number density: 0.64 fm^-3 for PT1 and 0.74 fm^-3 for PT2, corresponding to isolated neutron-star gravitational masses of 1.32 solar masses and 1.33 solar masses, respectively.
- Maximum ejecta mass for PT1 at highest resolution (R4): 0.159 x 10^-3 solar masses; for noPT1: 1.62 x 10^-3 solar masses (about an order of magnitude larger).
- Maximum ejecta mass for PT2 at highest resolution (R4): 0.692 x 10^-3 solar masses; for noPT2: 10.1 x 10^-3 solar masses.
- All simulations had dimensionless binary tidal deformability of 250 and initial eccentricities of order 10^-3.
- Maximum baryon number densities reached during post-merger collapse: at least 5 fm^-3.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of phase transitions in binary neutron-star mergers by Most (2018), Bauswein (2018), and Weih (2019), which focused on phase transitions occurring at or after merger, and by Liebling (2020), who found a reverse phase transition in the post-merger phase due to density oscillations. The key new finding is that a reverse phase transition can occur during the inspiral before merger, caused by tidal deformation rather than post-merger oscillations. The results enable future high-precision simulations to search for observable signatures of such transitions, and they suggest that phase transitions can significantly reduce kilonova brightness by suppressing ejecta masses.
