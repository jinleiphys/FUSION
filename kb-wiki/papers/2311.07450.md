# Quark production and thermalization of the quark-gluon plasma

> We first assemble a full set of the Boltzmann Equation in Diffusion Approximation (BEDA) for studying thermalization/hydrodynamization as well as the production of massless quarks and antiquarks in out of equilibrium systems. In the BEDA, the time evolution of a generic system is characterized by the following space-time dependent quantities: the jet quenching parameter, the effective temperature, and two more for each quark flavor that describe the conversion between gluons and quarks/antiquarks via the $2\leftrightarrow2$ processes. Out of the latter two quantities, an effective net quark chemical potential is defined, which equals the net quark chemical potential after thermal equilibration. We then study thermalization and the production of three flavors of massless quarks and antiquarks in spatially homogeneous systems initially filled only with gluons. A parametric understanding of thermalization and quark production is obtained for either initially very dense or dilute systems, which are complemented by detailed numerical simulations for intermediate values of initial gluon occupancy $f_0$. For a wide range of $f_0$, the final equilibration time is determined to be about one order of magnitude longer than that in the corresponding pure gluon systems. Moreover, during the final stage of the thermalization process for $f_0\geq 10^{-4}$, gluons are found to thermalize earlier than quarks and antiquarks, undergoing the top-down thermalization.

## Key claim

The paper claims that in spatially homogeneous systems initially filled only with gluons, the inclusion of three flavors of massless quarks and antiquarks delays the final equilibration time by about one order of magnitude compared to pure gluon systems, and that gluons thermalize before quarks and antiquarks during the final stage for initial gluon occupancies \(f_0 \geq 10^{-4}\), a phenomenon the authors term "top-down thermalization."

## Method

The authors assembled a full set of the Boltzmann Equation in Diffusion Approximation (BEDA) incorporating both \(2\leftrightarrow2\) and \(1\leftrightarrow2\) collision kernels for gluons and massless quarks and antiquarks, extending previous work on pure gluon systems. They solved the BEDA numerically for spatially homogeneous systems with initial gluon distributions inspired by saturation physics, where quarks and antiquarks of three flavors are initially absent, and complemented these simulations with parametric estimates for both very dense and very dilute initial conditions.

## Key numbers

* For initial gluon occupancy \(f_0 = 10^{-2}, 10^{-4}, 10^{-6}\) (under-populated systems), the equilibration time is about one order of magnitude longer than in the corresponding pure gluon systems.
* For \(f_0 \geq 10^{-4}\), gluons thermalize earlier than quarks and antiquarks during the final stage of thermalization.
* The critical initial occupancy separating under-populated from over-populated systems for \(N_f = N_c = 3\) is \(f_{0c} = 0.308\).
* In thermal equilibrium with \(N_f = 3\) and zero chemical potential, quarks and antiquarks together account for over half of the energy density, number density, and entropy density, but contribute only one third of the jet quenching parameter \(\hat{q}_A\) and Debye mass squared \(m_D^2\).
* The soft momentum scale for quarks \(p_{F*}\) is related to the gluon soft scale \(p_{A*}\) by \(p_{F*} \approx 0.466\, p_{A*}\).

## Context

This work extends the BEDA framework previously developed for pure gluon systems by Blaizot et al. (2013, 2014) and Barrera Cabodevila et al. (2022) to include massless quarks and antiquarks with both \(2\leftrightarrow2\) and \(1\leftrightarrow2\) processes. It complements studies using Effective Kinetic Theory by Kurkela et al. (2018) and Du et al. (2020) on the delay of thermalization due to quark production, and provides a more detailed parametric and numerical understanding of the top-down thermalization phenomenon in spatially homogeneous systems. The results enable future investigations of how quark production modifies each stage of thermalization in expanding systems relevant to heavy-ion collisions.
