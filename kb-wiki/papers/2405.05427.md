# Chiral Magnetic Effect in Heavy Ion Collisions: The Present and Future

> The chiral magnetic effect (CME) is a collective quantum phenomenon that arises from the interplay between gauge field topology and fermion chiral anomaly, encompassing a wide range of physical systems from semimetals to quark-gluon plasma. This review, with a focus on CME and related effects in heavy ion collisions, aims to provide an introductory discussion on its conceptual foundation and measurement methodology, a timely update on the present status in terms of experimental findings and theoretical progress, as well as an outlook into the open problems and future developments.

## Key claim

This review argues that while evidence for the chiral magnetic effect (CME) in heavy ion collisions has emerged from RHIC data, a definitive conclusion on its presence or absence requires a concerted experimental and theoretical effort in the final years of RHIC operation, given the fundamental importance of obtaining direct information about the topology of non-Abelian gauge theories.

## Method

The authors provide a comprehensive review of the conceptual foundation of the CME arising from the interplay between gauge field topology and the fermion chiral anomaly, survey experimental measurement methodologies and novel analysis techniques used at RHIC and the LHC, and summarize theoretical progress including lattice QCD results, anomalous hydrodynamics, and quantum kinetic theory for chirality and spin transport. The review also discusses beam energy dependence, chiral magnetic wave measurements, and connections with other heavy ion observables.

## Key numbers

* The CME arises at different scales ranging from femtometers to parsecs, as stated in the review.
* The review reports that evidence for the CME in AuAu collisions has emerged in the RHIC energy region, based on extensive information about charged hadron correlations accumulated from dedicated effort at RHIC and the LHC.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the chiral magnetic effect that began approximately twenty years ago, encompassing both theoretical developments (including lattice QCD computations and analytical approaches) and experimental searches at RHIC and the LHC. The authors note that the experimental discovery of CME in condensed matter systems and confirmation through first-principles computations opens the path toward using CME as a tool for extracting information about the topological structure of QCD from heavy ion collision measurements. The review identifies open theoretical problems and experimental quests for CME as future opportunities requiring high statistics data and sophisticated analysis methods.
