# Isospin effects on two-nucleon correlation functions in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

> Using an isospin-dependent transport model, we study isospin effects on two-nucleon correlation functions in heavy-ion collisions induced by neutron-rich nuclei at intermediate energies. We find that these correlation functions are sensitive to the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy, but not to the incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter and the medium dependence of nucleon-nucleon cross sections. This sensitivity is mainly due to effects of nuclear symmetry energy on the emission times of neutrons and protons as well as their relative emission sequence. We also study the variations of the symmetry energy effects on nucleon-nucleon correlations with respect to the impact parameter, incident energy, and mass number of heavy ion collisions.

# Isospin effects on two-nucleon correlation functions in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

## Key claim

Two-nucleon correlation functions in heavy-ion collisions induced by neutron-rich nuclei at intermediate energies are sensitive to the density dependence of nuclear symmetry energy, but this sensitivity is conditional on the specific choice of symmetry energy parameterization (soft with γ=0.5 versus stiff with γ=2) and is mainly mediated through the effects of symmetry energy on the emission times of neutrons and protons and their relative emission sequence, rather than through the incompressibility of symmetric nuclear matter or the medium dependence of nucleon-nucleon cross sections.

## Method

The authors used an isospin-dependent Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (IBUU) transport model to simulate central collisions of 52Ca + 48Ca at 80 MeV/nucleon, with initial nucleon positions from relativistic mean-field theory and momenta from Thomas-Fermi approximation. They compared two parameterizations of the nuclear symmetry energy (soft with γ=0.5 and stiff with γ=2), two isoscalar equations of state (soft with K0=201 MeV and stiff with K0=380 MeV), and two sets of nucleon-nucleon cross sections (experimental free-space values and in-medium cross sections from the Dirac-Brueckner approach based on the Bonn A potential). Nucleon emission functions and two-nucleon correlation functions were computed from the transport model output using 10,000 events with 100 test particles per physical nucleon.

## Key numbers

- The soft symmetry energy (γ=0.5) gives L = 52.5 MeV and K_sym = -78.8 MeV; the stiff symmetry energy (γ=2) gives L = 210.0 MeV and K_sym = 630.0 MeV.
- The symmetry energy at normal nuclear matter density is taken as E_sym(ρ0) = 35 MeV.
- The soft isoscalar EOS has incompressibility K0 = 201 MeV; the stiff isoscalar EOS has K0 = 380 MeV; both have saturation density ρ0 = 0.16 fm^-3.
- Maximum average central densities reached in the collisions are about 1.46ρ0 for K0 = 380 MeV and 1.66ρ0 for K0 = 201 MeV (both with soft symmetry energy and free cross sections).
- The Coulomb interaction shortens the proton average emission time by factors less than 10% for both soft and stiff symmetry energies in the 52Ca + 48Ca system.
- Nucleons are considered emitted when their local densities are less than ρ0/8.

## Context

This work extends previous studies that identified pre-equilibrium neutron/proton ratios (Li 1997), isospin fractionation (Xu 2000, Baran 2002), isoscaling in multifragmentation (Betty), proton differential elliptic flow (Li), neutron-proton differential transverse flow (Li 2000), and the π- to π+ ratio (Li 2002) as probes of the nuclear symmetry energy in heavy-ion collisions. The paper introduces two-nucleon correlation functions as a new observable sensitive to the density dependence of symmetry energy, building on earlier work showing that correlation functions of nonidentical particles can resolve time delays as short as 1 fm/c and source radius differences of a few tenths of a fm (Kotte 1999). The results enable future experimental studies at facilities such as the planned Rare Isotope Accelerator (RIA) and the GSI facility to extract information about the symmetry energy from correlation function measurements.
