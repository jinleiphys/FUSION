# Millisecond radio pulsars with known masses: parameter values and equation of state models

> The recent fast growth of a population of millisecond pulsars with precisely measured mass provides an excellent opportunity to characterize these compact stars at an unprecedented level. This is because the stellar parameter values can be accurately computed for known mass and spin rate and an assumed equation of state (EoS) model. For each of the 16 such pulsars and for a set of EoS models from nucleonic, hyperonic, strange quark matter and hybrid classes, we numerically compute fast spinning stable stellar parameter values considering the full effect of general relativity. This first detailed catalogue of the computed parameter values of observed millisecond pulsars provides a testbed to probe the physics of compact stars, including their formation, evolution and EoS. We estimate uncertainties on these computed values from the uncertainty of the measured mass, which could be useful to quantitatively constrain EoS models. We note that the largest value of the central density $ρ_{\rm c}$ in our catalogue is $\sim 5.8$ times the nuclear saturation density $ρ_{\rm sat}$, which is much less than the expected maximum value $13 ρ_{\rm sat}$. We argue that the $ρ_{\rm c}$-values of at most a small fraction of compact stars could be much larger than $5.8 ρ_{\rm sat}$. Besides, we find that the constraints on EoS models from accurate radius measurements could be significantly biased for some of our pulsars, if stellar $spinning$ configurations are not used to compute the theoretical radius values.

## Key claim

The paper provides the first detailed catalogue of computed stellar parameters for 16 millisecond pulsars with precisely measured masses, using eight diverse equation of state (EoS) models from four classes, but the authors note that the largest central density found in their catalogue is only about 5.8 times nuclear saturation density, much lower than the expected maximum of 13 times saturation density, implying that only a small fraction of compact stars could have central densities much larger than this value.

## Method

For each of 16 millisecond pulsars with known mass and spin rate, the authors solved the full general relativistic equations for rapidly rotating stellar structure using eight different EoS models (nucleonic, hyperonic, strange quark matter, and hybrid). They performed multi-iteration numerical computations to find stable equilibrium configurations matching the observed mass and spin frequency, then calculated stellar parameters including central density, rest mass, equatorial and polar radii, moment of inertia, and binding energy. Uncertainties on computed parameters were derived from the quoted measurement errors on the pulsar masses.

## Key numbers

- Largest central density in the catalogue: 15.13 × 10^14 g cm^-3 (approximately 5.8 times nuclear saturation density ρ_sat ≈ 2.6 × 10^14 g cm^-3), for PSR J1614-2230 with the soft hybrid EoS model Hb1
- Expected maximum possible central density: approximately 13 ρ_sat, based on the mass of PSR J0348+0432 (2.01 ± 0.04 M_⊙) and the Tolman VII solution argument from Lattimer and Prakash (2005, 2010)
- Binding energy B = M_0 - M_G ranges from approximately 0.1 to 0.5 M_⊙ for the sample, corresponding to about 7-30% of the gravitational mass values (6-23% of rest mass values)
- Equatorial radius range across all pulsars and EoS models: approximately 11-16 km
- Fastest spinning pulsar has polar-to-equatorial radius ratio R_p/R_e ≈ 0.89 for the hardest nucleonic EoS model; slowest spinning pulsar has R_p/R_e consistent with 1.00 for strange matter EoS models
- The relation by Cipolletta et al. (2015) for binding energy gives values consistent within 2% for nucleonic, hyperonic and hybrid EoS models, but errors of 6-12% for strange matter EoS models

## Context

This work extends previous studies of rotating compact star structure (Cook et al. 1994; Datta et al. 1998; Bhattacharyya et al. 2016) by using both measured mass and spin rate for individual pulsars rather than just spin sequences, enabling much tighter constraints on stellar parameters. The catalogue provides a testbed for probing compact star formation, evolution, and EoS models, and the authors argue that radius measurements for these pulsars could significantly bias EoS constraints if spinning rather than non-spinning configurations are used for theoretical radius calculations. The paper enables future work to quantitatively constrain EoS models by comparing observed radii or other parameters against the computed values for individual millisecond pulsars.
