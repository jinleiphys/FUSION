# The Nuclear Spin-Orbit Force in Chiral Effective Field Theories

> A compelling feature of relativistic mean-field phenomenology has been the reproduction of spin-orbit splittings in finite nuclei after fitting only to equilibrium properties of infinite nuclear matter. This successful result occurs when the velocity dependence of the equivalent central potential that leads to saturation arises primarily because of a reduced nucleon effective mass. The spin-orbit interaction is then also specified when one works in a four-component Dirac framework. Here the nature of the spin-orbit force in more general chiral effective field theories of nuclei is examined, with an emphasis on the role of the tensor coupling of the isoscalar vector meson (omega) to the nucleon.

## Key claim

The paper examines whether the strong correlation between the nucleon effective mass in nuclear matter and the spin-orbit force in finite nuclei, which is a hallmark of relativistic mean-field models, survives in more general chiral effective field theories; the authors find that the inclusion of a tensor coupling of the isoscalar vector meson (omega) to the nucleon introduces a trade-off that allows larger effective masses while still reproducing empirical spin-orbit splittings.

## Method

The authors perform two-component reductions of relativistic Dirac single-particle Hamiltonians to extract the spin-orbit potential, using both a Foldy-Wouthuysen reduction and Reinhard's velocity-expansion procedure. They apply a local-density approximation to relate nuclear matter properties to finite-nucleus spin-orbit splittings, and derive an equivalence condition that relates the effective mass at equilibrium to the isoscalar tensor coupling strength. The analysis is applied to a wide class of models including conventional QHD, chiral effective field theories with mesonic degrees of freedom, point-coupling models, and the QMC and ZM models.

## Key numbers

- The empirical spin-orbit splitting for proton p states in 16O is approximately 6 MeV
- For models without tensor coupling, the effective mass at nuclear matter equilibrium is constrained to the range 0.58 ≤ M*0/M ≤ 0.64
- Model G2 from Furnstahl (1997) has M*0/M ≈ 0.66 with tensor coupling included
- Point-coupling models achieve M*0/M as high as 0.74 at nuclear matter equilibrium
- The local-density estimate using the operator hLSR systematically underpredicts Dirac splittings by roughly 5%, while hLSR' overpredicts by about 15%
- The O(v^4) hamiltonians are within 3% of the Dirac splittings
- Free-space constraints from vector dominance give |fv| ≤ 0.2
- The ZM model has M*0/M ≈ 0.85, which would require a very large fv to reproduce spin-orbit splittings

## Context

This work extends the systematic study of chiral effective field theories for nuclei initiated by Furnstahl (1995, 1997) and Rusnak (1997), which showed that effective hadronic Lagrangians with natural coefficients could fit nuclear properties. The paper contradicts the earlier conclusion from Furnstahl (1995) that M*0/M is tightly constrained to 0.58-0.64, by showing that the isoscalar tensor coupling (previously studied by Rufa (1988) and Reinhard (1989) with only small values) can be larger in effective theories and allows higher effective masses. The analysis also shows that the QMC model (Saito, 1996) and ZM model (Biro, 1997) are deficient because their high effective masses cannot produce sufficient spin-orbit strength even with tensor couplings. The results enable future fits to nuclear properties with larger effective masses and provide a framework for understanding the spin-orbit force in relativistic density functional theory.
