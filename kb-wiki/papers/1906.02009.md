# Equation of state effects in the core collapse of a $20$-$M_\odot$ star

> Uncertainties in our knowledge of the properties of dense matter near and above nuclear saturation density are among the main sources of variations in multi-messenger signatures predicted for core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) and the properties of neutron stars (NSs). We construct 97 new finite-temperature equations of state (EOSs) of dense matter that obey current experimental, observational, and theoretical constraints and discuss how systematic variations in the EOS parameters affect the properties of cold nonrotating NSs and the core collapse of a $20\,M_\odot$ progenitor star. The core collapse of the $20\,M_\odot$ progenitor star is simulated in spherical symmetry using the general-relativistic radiation-hydrodynamics code GR1D where neutrino interactions are computed for each EOS using the NuLib library. We conclude that the effective mass of nucleons at densities above nuclear saturation density is the largest source of uncertainty in the CCSN neutrino signal and dynamics even though it plays a subdominant role in most properties of cold NS matter. Meanwhile, changes in other observables affect the properties of cold NSs, while having little effect in CCSNe. To strengthen our conclusions, we perform six octant three-dimensional CCSN simulations varying the effective mass of nucleons at nuclear saturation density. We conclude that neutrino heating and, thus, the likelihood of explosion is significantly increased for EOSs where the effective mass of nucleons at nuclear saturation density is large.

## Key claim

The effective mass of nucleons at densities above nuclear saturation density is the largest source of uncertainty in core-collapse supernova neutrino signals and dynamics, even though it plays a subdominant role in most properties of cold neutron star matter; neutrino heating and the likelihood of explosion are significantly increased for equations of state where the effective mass at saturation density is large.

## Method

The authors constructed 97 finite-temperature equations of state using the meta-modeling formalism with Skyrme-type interactions, varying empirical nuclear matter parameters in four sets (effective mass, symmetry energy, incompressibility, and high-density pressure) within experimental and observational constraints. They simulated the core collapse of a non-rotating 20-solar-mass progenitor star in spherical symmetry using the general-relativistic radiation-hydrodynamics code GR1D with neutrino interactions computed via the NuLib library, and performed six additional octant three-dimensional simulations using the Zelmani code to test the effect of nucleon effective mass variations.

## Key numbers

- 97 new finite-temperature equations of state were constructed and tested in core-collapse simulations
- Nuclear saturation density fixed at n_sat = 0.155 fm^-3 (mass density rho_sat = 2.7 x 10^14 g cm^-3)
- Energy at nuclear saturation density fixed at epsilon_sat = -15.8 MeV per baryon
- Slope of symmetry energy L_sym set to 45 +/- 7.5 MeV (narrower than the literature average of 60 +/- 15 MeV)
- Effective mass at saturation density varied as m* = 0.75 +/- 0.10 m_n (one sigma) and +/- 0.20 m_n (two sigma)
- All equations of state produce neutron stars with masses above 2.01 solar masses, satisfying the PSR J0348+0432 constraint
- Radius of a 1.4-solar-mass neutron star constrained to 12.00 km < R_1.4 < 13.45 km from GW170817 tidal deformability limits

## Context

This work extends the meta-modeling approach of Margueron (2018) by systematically varying individual nuclear matter parameters to disentangle their effects on core-collapse supernovae, overcoming limitations of earlier studies (such as O'Connor 2011, Hempel 2012, Steiner 2013, and Richers 2017) that compared equations of state from different formalisms with small sample sizes. The results enable future work to focus observational and experimental efforts on constraining the nucleon effective mass at high densities, as this parameter most strongly influences supernova dynamics and neutrino signals.
