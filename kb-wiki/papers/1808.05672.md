# Convergence and efficiency of angular momentum projection for many-body systems

> In many so-called "beyond-mean-field" many-body methods, one creates symmetry-breaking states and then projects out states with good quantum number(s); the most important example is angular momentum. Motivated by the computational intensity of symmetry restoration, we investigate the numerical convergence of two competing methods for angular momentum projection with rotations over Euler angles, the textbook-standard projection through quadrature, and a recently introduced projection through linear algebra. We find well-defined patterns of convergence with increasing number of mesh points (for quadrature) and cut-offs (for linear algebra). Because the method of projection through linear algebra requires inverting matrices generated on a mesh of Euler angles, we discuss two methods for robustly reducing the number of required evaluations. Reviewing the literature, we find our inversion involving rotations about the $z$-axis is equivalent to trapezoidal "quadrature" commonly used as well as Fomenko projection used for particle-number projection. The efficiency depends upon the number of angular momentum $J$ to be projected, but in general inversion methods, including Fomenko projection/trapezoidal "quadrature" dramatically improve the efficiency.

# Convergence and efficiency of angular momentum projection for many-body systems

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that angular momentum projection via linear algebra requires significantly fewer evaluations of Hamiltonian kernels than standard Gauss-Legendre quadrature, with the advantage growing for higher angular momentum values J. The authors find that the "mixed" method, using linear algebra inversion (equivalent to Fomenko projection/trapezoidal quadrature) for rotations about the z-axis and Gauss-Legendre quadrature for rotations about the y-axis, also provides substantial efficiency gains over pure quadrature.

## Method
The authors compare three approaches for angular momentum projection: (1) standard Gauss-Legendre quadrature over all three Euler angles (α, β, γ); (2) a "mixed" method using linear algebra inversion for α and γ angles (equivalent to Fomenko projection/trapezoidal quadrature) with Gauss-Legendre quadrature for β; and (3) full linear algebra inversion for all three Euler angles. They test convergence by computing fractional occupations f_J and yrast energies for three nuclides (⁵⁷Fe, ⁶⁸Ga, ⁴⁸Cr) in different model spaces, using the number of evaluations at different Euler angles as the measure of computational cost. The authors also develop a "need-to-know" algorithm that reduces the β-mesh by excluding J-values with negligible fractional occupation.

## Key numbers
- For ⁵⁷Fe, J=21/2⁺: Gauss-Legendre requires 32,768 evaluations, mixed requires 10,648 evaluations, and linear algebra requires 13,500 evaluations to converge energies to within 1 keV.
- For ⁴⁸Cr, J=12⁺: Gauss-Legendre requires 85,184 evaluations, mixed requires 32,768 evaluations, and linear algebra requires 18,513 evaluations to converge energies to within 1 keV.
- For ⁶⁸Ga, J=7⁺: Gauss-Legendre requires 13,824 evaluations, mixed requires 4,096 evaluations, and linear algebra requires 3,610 evaluations to converge energies to within 1 keV.
- For ⁴⁸Cr, J=0⁺: Gauss-Legendre requires 13,824 evaluations, mixed requires 5,832 evaluations, and linear algebra requires 8,125 evaluations.
- The "need-to-know" algorithm reduced evaluations from 12,615 to 6,728 for ⁴⁸Cr and ⁶⁰Fe (prolate even-even nuclides) with energy changes less than 0.4 keV.
- For ⁶²Ni (oblate), need-to-know reduced evaluations from 18,513 to 9,801 with energy changes less than 0.6 keV.
- Fractional occupations below 0.001 could be safely ignored in the angular momentum expansion.

## Context
This work extends the linear algebra projection method introduced in Johnson and Jiao (2017, Phys. Rev. C 96, 064304) by systematically comparing its convergence and efficiency against standard quadrature methods used throughout beyond-mean-field nuclear structure calculations. The paper connects the linear algebra inversion on α,γ angles to the Fomenko projection method (Fomenko, 1970) used for particle-number projection and to trapezoidal quadrature used in prior work by Hara and Sun (1995) and others. The results enable practitioners to choose optimal projection strategies based on the number of angular momentum states needed, with linear algebra methods being particularly advantageous when projecting many J-values simultaneously.
