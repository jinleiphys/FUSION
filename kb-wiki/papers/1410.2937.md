# Uncertainty Quantification in Lattice QCD Calculations for Nuclear Physics

> The numerical technique of Lattice QCD holds the promise of connecting the nuclear forces, nuclei, the spectrum and structure of hadrons, and the properties of matter under extreme conditions with the underlying theory of the strong interactions, quantum chromodynamics. A distinguishing, and thus far unique, feature of this formulation is that all of the associated uncertainties, both statistical and systematic can, in principle, be systematically reduced to any desired precision with sufficient computational and human resources. We review the sources of uncertainty inherent in Lattice QCD calculations for nuclear physics, and discuss how each is quantified in current efforts.

## Key claim

Lattice QCD calculations for nuclear physics can, in principle, have all associated statistical and systematic uncertainties systematically reduced to any desired precision with sufficient computational and human resources, but this promise is conditional on demonstrating that uncertainties behave as expected with increasing lattice volumes, numbers of gauge-field configurations, and decreasing lattice spacings.

## Method

The authors review the sources of uncertainty in Lattice QCD calculations for nuclear physics, covering the generation of gauge-field configurations via Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC), the inclusion of quarks through various fermion discretizations (Wilson, Kogut-Susskind/staggered, domain-wall, and overlap formulations), the computation of quark propagators by solving linear systems with iterative solvers, and the extraction of physical observables from Euclidean-space correlation functions. They discuss how each uncertainty is quantified in current efforts, including statistical uncertainties from Monte Carlo integration, systematic uncertainties from finite lattice spacing, finite volume, quark mass mistuning, and autocorrelation effects.

## Key numbers

- Computational resource requirements for gauge-field generation scale as $C = K (m_\pi/m_\rho)^{-z} V^{1+1/2n} (1/a^7)$, where $n$ is the order of the integrator and $z$ is an exponent ranging between 4 and 6
- For the topological charge, the integrated autocorrelation time scales as $\tau(Q^2) \sim a^{-5}$ as the continuum limit is approached
- A lattice of $64^3 \times 256$ sites is currently practical, involving approximately $10^{10}$ degrees of freedom
- The uncertainty from Monte Carlo integration scales as ${\cal O}(1/\sqrt{N})$ with the number of gauge-field configurations $N$
- The fractional uncertainty in the pion mass scales with number of sources as $b = -0.03(2)$, while for the nucleon it scales as $b = -0.41(3)$
- Typical precision goals for solving the linear system require a relative error on the norm $||DS-G|| < 10^{-10}$ to $10^{-12}$

## Context

This review extends the community-wide effort to compile lattice results for particle physics, as documented in the LQCD compendium (Aoki 2013), and aims to bring these uncertainty quantification methods to the attention of nuclear physicists who are not experts in lattice field theory. The work enables future calculations of nuclear quantities to be added to the LQCD compendium as they mature from exploratory stages, and provides a framework for verifying LQCD as a reliable technique through agreement with experimental measurements.
