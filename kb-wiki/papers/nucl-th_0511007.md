# Zipf's Law and the Universality Class of the Fragmentation Phase Transition

> We show that Zipf's Law for the largest clusters is not valid in an exact sense at the critical point of the fragmentation phase transition, contrary to previous claims. Instead, the extracted distributions of the largest clusters reflects the choice of universality class through the value of the critical exponent tau.

## Key claim
Zipf's Law for the largest clusters is not exactly valid at the critical point of the fragmentation phase transition; instead, the extracted distributions of the largest clusters reflect the choice of universality class through the value of the critical exponent tau, and the observed power-law exponent lambda varies systematically with tau.

## Method
The authors derived exact analytical expressions for the probability distributions of the largest through fourth-largest clusters in a fragmentation event, assuming a power-law cluster size distribution at the critical point (epsilon = 0) with Poissonian statistics for cluster multiplicities. They then computed the average cluster size as a function of rank r for different values of the critical exponent tau (2, 2.18, 2.3 repeating, 2.5, 3) and system sizes V (100, 10,000, 1,000,000), fitting the results to both simple power laws and Zipf-Mandelbrot distributions.

## Key numbers
- For tau = 2, the extracted power-law exponent lambda = 1.26; for tau = 2.18, lambda = 1.16; for tau = 2.3 repeating, lambda = 1.07; for tau = 2.5, lambda = 0.97; for tau = 3, lambda = 0.7 (all for system size V = 10,000).
- The asymptotic approximation relating the exponent lambda to the critical exponent tau is lambda(tau) = 1/(tau - 1).
- The analytical results depend on only two parameters: the size of the fragmenting system V and the value of the critical exponent tau.

## Context
This work extends previous scaling analyses of multifragment emission by Kleine and Elliott (year not given) and addresses claims by Watanabe (year not given) and Ma (2005) that Zipf's Law could be used to detect crossing of the critical threshold in percolation and fragmentation. The authors show that the approximate Zipf-like behavior is coincidental and does not yield additional physics insight, and they note that X. Campi and H. Krivine arrived at somewhat similar conclusions independently. The results enable more precise characterization of the universality class of the fragmentation phase transition through the measured exponent lambda.
