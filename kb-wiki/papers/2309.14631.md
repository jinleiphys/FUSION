# Near-threshold hadron scattering with effective field theory

> When an exotic hadron locates near the threshold with the channel couplings, the internal structure of the exotic hadron is related to the scattering length. To incorporate the threshold effect, the Flatté amplitude has been often used to determine the scattering length. It is however known that an additional constraint is imposed on the Flatte amplitude near the threshold. We discuss this problem by using the effective field theory for the coupled-channel scattering.

## Key claim

The Flatté amplitude, commonly used to extract scattering lengths for near-threshold exotic hadrons, imposes an implicit constraint (γ=0) that reduces its independent parameters from three to two near threshold, meaning the Flatté scattering length a_F may deviate from the correct scattering length a_G by about 0.5 fm in realistic systems.

## Method

The authors compare the Flatté amplitude with a general coupled-channel scattering amplitude derived from non-relativistic effective field theory (EFT) with contact interactions. They introduce a new parametrization (the "general amplitude" f^G) that interpolates between the EFT amplitude and the Flatté amplitude via a dimensionless parameter γ, and analyze the effective range expansion of each channel component near the threshold of the higher channel.

## Key numbers

- In the ππ–K̅K system with f0(980), the constant term in the denominator of f_{ππ} (corresponding to f^G_{11}) was determined from Flatté analysis to be −1.0 − 1.0i GeV (CMD-2, 1999).
- When γ is varied from −0.04 to +0.04, the general scattering length a_G deviates from the Flatté scattering length a_F by approximately 0.5 fm.
- The imaginary part of a_G is independent of γ, as shown analytically from the expression for a_G.

## Context

This work extends the critique by Baru (2004) that the Flatté amplitude has a parameter reduction near threshold, and provides a resolution using the EFT framework of Cohen (2004). The general amplitude enables future analyses of exotic hadrons such as Tcc, X(3872), and f0(980) to extract scattering lengths without the Flatté amplitude's hidden constraint, improving the connection between scattering data and internal structure.
