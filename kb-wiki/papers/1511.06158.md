# Shape coexistence in the microscopically guided interacting boson model

> Shape coexistence has been a subject of great interest in nuclear physics for many decades. In the context of the nuclear shell model, intruder excitations may give rise to remarkably low-lying excited $0^+$ states associated with different intrinsic shapes. In heavy open-shell nuclei, the dimension of the shell-model configuration space that includes such intruder excitations becomes exceedingly large, thus requiring a drastic truncation scheme. Such a framework has been provided by the interacting boson model (IBM). In this article we address the phenomenon of shape coexistence and its relevant spectroscopy from the point of view of the IBM. A special focus is placed on the method developed recently which makes use of the link between the IBM and the self-consistent mean-field approach based on the nuclear energy density functional. The method is extended to deal with various intruder configurations associated with different equilibrium shapes. We assess the predictive power of the method and suggest possible improvements and extensions, by considering illustrative examples in the neutron-deficient Pb region, where shape coexistence has been experimentally studied.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to derive the interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian from self-consistent mean-field calculations based on nuclear energy density functionals, extended to handle multiple intruder configurations associated with different equilibrium shapes; the method's predictive power is assessed for shape coexistence in the neutron-deficient Pb region, but the authors note that the mapping is only approximate and is fulfilled within a limited range of the deformation plane around the global minimum.

## Method

The authors perform constrained Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) or Hartree-Fock plus BCS (HFBCS) calculations using a nuclear energy density functional (e.g., Skyrme SkM*) to obtain microscopic total energy surfaces as functions of quadrupole deformation parameters (β, γ). They then map these fermionic energy surfaces onto the corresponding bosonic energy surfaces of the IBM by equating the expectation values of the Hamiltonians in coherent states, assuming equal deformations for proton and neutron boson systems and a proportionality relation β = β_b/C_β between the fermionic and bosonic deformation variables. The IBM Hamiltonian parameters are fixed to reproduce the topology of the microscopic energy surface in the vicinity of the global minimum, and the method is extended to configuration-mixing IBM by constructing separate Hamiltonians for different intruder configurations (0p-0h, 2p-2h, 4p-4h) and mixing them via specific interaction terms.

## Key numbers

* For ¹⁵⁴Sm, the HFBCS calculation gives a deep minimum at β ≈ 0.35, representing a strongly deformed rotational nucleus.
* For ¹⁴⁸Sm, the HFBCS calculation gives an energy minimum near the origin (β ≈ 0), representing a near-spherical vibrational nucleus.
* The energy difference between neighboring contours in the energy surface plots is 500 keV, with the color scale varying in steps of 50 keV.
* For ¹⁸⁶Pb, three intrinsic shapes (spherical, oblate, prolate) coexist, corresponding to 0p-0h, 2p-2h, and 4p-4h shell-model configurations, respectively.

## Context

This work extends the method of Nomura (2008) for deriving the IBM from self-consistent mean-field calculations to handle shape coexistence, building on the earlier Duval-Barrett procedure (Duval and Barrett, 1981, 1982) for incorporating intruder excitations in the IBM. The approach contrasts with purely phenomenological IBM calculations where Hamiltonian parameters are fitted to experimental data, and instead provides a microscopic foundation using nuclear energy density functionals. The method enables spectroscopic calculations for exotic isotopes and regions where experimental data are scarce, with applications demonstrated for neutron-deficient Pb and Hg isotopes, and for neutron-rich nuclei with mass A ≈ 100.
