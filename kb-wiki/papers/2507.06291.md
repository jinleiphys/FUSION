# High-Dimensional Unfolding in Large Backgrounds

> We propose new methodologies in multi-dimensional unfolding in dense environments, and show that incorporating auxiliary observables can significantly improve performance. Our approach builds on the ML-based OmniFold algorithm, which we extend to account for background, detector acceptance, efficiency, and uncertainties, enabling its application in high-luminosity and heavy-ion collision settings. We derive this algorithm and demonstrate its mathematical and numerical equivalence to expectation-maximization and Iterative Bayesian Unfolding (IBU). We illustrate our method with a realistic jet substructure analysis incorporating both large background and detector simulation. Our analysis includes up to 18 observables, leading to significantly improved performance in the unfolding. We propose a method that integrates calibration and unfolding into a single, consistent framework, and demonstrate enhanced performance relative to traditional methods. These developments lay the groundwork for robust, high-dimensional, ML-based unfolding and calibration in complex collider environments across a wide range of analyses.

## Key claim

The authors extend the ML-based OmniFold algorithm to handle large backgrounds, detector acceptance, efficiency, and uncertainties (calling the extension OmniFold-HI), and demonstrate that incorporating up to 18 auxiliary observables alongside target observables significantly improves unfolding performance compared to traditional methods, though the improvement is conditional on the auxiliary observables being well-correlated with the target ones.

## Method

The authors derive OmniFold-HI as an unbinned, iterative expectation-maximization algorithm mathematically equivalent to Iterative Bayesian Unfolding (IBU), implemented using two neural network classifiers trained on weighted samples to compute likelihood ratios. They apply this method to a realistic jet substructure analysis in a heavy-ion collision background, using Monte Carlo event generators with detector simulation, and compare unfolding performance across varying numbers of observables (up to 18 dimensions) and different calibration strategies.

## Key numbers

- Up to 18 observables are included in the unfolding analysis, leading to "significantly improved performance" relative to lower-dimensional unfolding.
- The high-luminosity LHC is expected to reach an average of 140 pileup collisions per bunch crossing, motivating the need for background-aware unfolding.
- The estimated size of the event phase space is approximately 400 dimensions for 100 reconstructed particles based on their 4-momenta.
- The authors tested their IBU implementation up to 3-4 observables with 10-20 bins and 1 million events, at which point the numpy histogram function ran out of memory.

## Context

This work extends the ML-based OmniFold algorithm (Andreassen 2019, 2021) to account for background, acceptance, efficiency, and uncertainties, enabling its application in high-luminosity and heavy-ion collision settings where traditional binned unfolding methods become computationally impractical. The authors provide a rigorous derivation showing OmniFold-HI is equivalent to expectation-maximization and IBU (D'Agostini 1995), and demonstrate that integrating calibration and unfolding into a single high-dimensional framework reduces analysis uncertainties. This enables future robust, high-dimensional ML-based unfolding and calibration in complex collider environments across a wide range of analyses.
