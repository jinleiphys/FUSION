# Quantum decay law: Critical times and the Equivalence of approaches

> Methods based on the use of Green's functions or the Jost functions and the Fock-Krylov method are apparently very different approaches to understand the time evolution of unstable states. We show that the two former methods are equivalent up to some constants and as an outcome find an analytic expression for the energy density of states in the Fock-Krylov amplitude in terms of the coefficients introduced in the Green's functions and the Jost functions methods. This model-independent density is further used to obtain an analytical expression for the survival amplitude and study its behaviour at large times. Using these expressions, we investigate the origin of the oscillatory behaviour of the decay law in the region of the transition from the exponential to the non-exponential at large times. With the objective to understand the failure of nuclear and particle physics experiments in observing the non-exponential decay law predicted by quantum mechanics for large times, we derive analytical formulae for the critical transition time, $t_c$, from the exponential to the inverse power law behaviour at large times. Evaluating $τ_c = Γt_c$ for some particle resonances and narrow nuclear states which have been tested experimentally to verify the exponential decay law, we conclude that the large time power law in particle and nuclear decay is hard to find experimentally.

## Key claim

The Green's function and Jost function methods for calculating survival amplitudes are equivalent to the Fock-Krylov method up to constants, and the authors derive an analytic expression for the critical transition time from exponential to inverse power law decay; however, evaluating this critical time for realistic particle resonances and narrow nuclear states shows that the large-time power law behavior is experimentally inaccessible because the transition occurs after many half-lives.

## Method

The authors demonstrate the equivalence of the Garcia-Calderon (GC) Green's function method, the van Dijk-Nogami (DN) Jost function method, and the Fock-Krylov (FK) method by expressing the survival amplitude in each formalism as a Fourier transform of an energy density, then identifying the energy density in the GC and DN approaches in terms of the coefficients and poles of the S-matrix. They derive an analytic expression for the survival amplitude using incomplete Gamma functions and apply the steepest descent method to obtain the large-time behavior. Using these expressions, they derive an analytic formula for the critical transition time from exponential to power law decay and evaluate the dimensionless quantity tau_c = Gamma * t_c for specific nuclear and particle resonances.

## Key numbers

- For an isolated resonance, the survival probability at large times is proportional to t^{-3} for S-waves (l=0), consistent with the general expectation of t^{-(2l+3)}.
- The survival amplitude at large times is given by A(t) = -(1/sqrt(4pi)) e^{i pi/4} Im[ sum_p (C_p(k_p) bar{C}_p(k_p) / k_p^3) ] t^{-3/2} + O(t^{-5/2}).
- The energy density in the GC formalism is rho^{GC}(E) = (1/pi) Im[ sum_p C_p(k_p) bar{C}_p(k_p) sqrt(E) / (k_p (k_p^2 - E)) ].
- The energy density in the DN formalism is rho^{DN}(E) = (1/pi) Im[ sum_p (a_p(k_p)/k_p) sqrt(E) / (k_p^2 - E) ].
- The normalization condition for the DN coefficients gives integral_0^infty (1/pi) Im[ sum_p (a_p(k_p)/k_p) sqrt(E) / (k_p^2 - E) ] dE = 1.

## Context

This work extends the theoretical frameworks of Garcia-Calderon (1990s), van Dijk and Nogami (2000s), and the Fock-Krylov method (Fock 1931, Krylov 1947) by proving their formal equivalence and providing a unified analytic expression for the energy density of states. The authors build on the Beth-Uhlenbeck (1937) relation connecting the density of states to the derivative of the scattering phase shift, and on earlier work by Kelkar and collaborators (2010s) that used this relation to compute survival probabilities from experimental phase shifts. The results enable quantitative predictions of the critical time for transition to non-exponential decay, explaining why experiments with nuclei such as 222Rn, 60Co, and 56Mn (Norman 1990s) observed only exponential decay, while non-exponential decay was observed in luminescence decays of organic materials (Rothe 2006).
