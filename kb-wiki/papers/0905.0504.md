# Testing AdS/CFT at LHC

> After an introduction to jet phenomenology and tests of AdS/CFT at LHC we derive the heavy quark drag of a string dangling in a shock metric of AdS space, thus generalizing the AdS/CFT drag calculations in strongly coupled thermal media to momentum loss in both hot and cold nuclear matter.

## Key claim

The heavy quark drag force in a strongly coupled medium can be generalized from thermal black hole metrics to a shock metric that represents both hot and cold nuclear matter, with the result that the momentum loss depends only on a single scale parameter \(\Lambda\) describing the medium; when \(\Lambda = \sqrt{\pi} T\) the shock metric exactly reproduces the thermal black hole result.

## Method

The author embeds a test string in a five-dimensional AdS shock metric (Eq. 2) that represents an incoming dense medium colliding with a heavy quark in its rest frame, using a step function \(\theta(x^-)\) to model the medium. The Nambu-Goto action is varied in static gauge \((\sigma^a = t, z)\) to obtain the equations of motion, which are solved for the static trailing string configuration \(\xi(z) = x_0 \pm \sqrt{\mu} z^3 / 3\). The drag force on the heavy quark is then extracted from the canonical momentum \(\pi^1_x\) and related to the lab-frame momentum loss via a boost transformation, yielding \(dp'/dt' = -\sqrt{\lambda} \Lambda^2 p' / (2 M_q)\).

## Key numbers

- Drag force in the quark rest frame: \(dp/dt = 2\pi \sqrt{\lambda \mu}\), where \(\mu\) is the shock parameter with units GeV\(^4\).
- Lab-frame momentum loss: \(dp'/dt' = -(\sqrt{\lambda}/2) (\Lambda^2 / M_q) p'\), where \(\Lambda\) is the typical momentum scale of the medium constituents.
- For \(\Lambda = \sqrt{\pi} T\), the result reproduces the black hole metric result: \(dp'/dt' = -\pi \sqrt{\lambda} T^2 p' / (2 M_q)\).
- Speed limit for applicability: \(\gamma \leq 4 M_q^2 / (\lambda T^2)\) for \(\Lambda = \sqrt{\pi} T\), identical to the black hole metric bound.

## Context

This work extends previous AdS/CFT drag calculations in thermal black hole metrics (Herzog 2006) to a shock geometry that can represent both hot and cold nuclear matter, following the shock metric approach used for deep inelastic scattering and heavy ion collisions by Albacete (2008). The result that the drag form is independent of medium thermalization, just as in perturbative QCD energy loss, gives greater confidence in the robustness of AdS/CFT predictions for heavy ion physics at LHC. The author notes that a convincing comparison with LHC data will require a separate \(p+Pb\) run or direct photon measurement in \(Pb+Pb\) to disentangle initial from final state effects.
