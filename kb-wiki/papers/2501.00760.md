# Isospin effect on the liquid-gas phase transition for finite nuclei

> The phenomenon of nuclear liquid-gas phase transition is a topic of contemporary interest. In heavy-ion collisions, there is no direct way of accessing the thermodynamic variables like pressure, density, free energy, entropy etc., and unambiguous detection of phase transition becomes difficult. A peak in the first order derivative of total multiplicity with respect to temperature (commonly abbreviated as the multiplicity derivative) has been established as a new experimentally accessible signature of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition. In this work, the effect of isospin asymmetry in the fragmenting system, as well as the nuclear equation of state, on the multiplicity derivative and specific heat at constant volume is investigated within the framework of the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) with a semi-microscopic cluster functional.

## Key claim

The peak in the multiplicity derivative (first derivative of total fragment multiplicity with respect to temperature) and the specific heat at constant volume remain valid signatures of the nuclear liquid-gas phase transition when using a semi-microscopic cluster functional based on realistic nuclear equations of state, but the transition temperature is sensitive to the chosen EoS; however, the phase transition temperature is almost independent of the isospin asymmetry of the fragmenting system for finite nuclei.

## Method

The author uses the Canonical Thermodynamical Model (CTM) of nuclear multifragmentation, upgraded with a semi-microscopic cluster functional where the bulk part of the Helmholtz free energy is derived from a meta-modelling of the nuclear equation of state (EoS) using Sly5, SGII, and NL3 parameter sets. The internal partition function of fragments with Z >= 3 is decomposed into bulk, surface, and Coulomb contributions, with the bulk free energy calculated from baryonic density, isospin asymmetry, and temperature-dependent Fermi integrals. The freeze-out volume is fixed at 6V0, and the model simulates disintegration of specific fragmenting systems expected from central heavy-ion collisions with 25% pre-equilibrium emission.

## Key numbers

- For the fragmenting system A0=72, Z0=30 (expected from 48Ca+48Ca), the peak temperatures of specific heat and multiplicity derivative are almost identical for each EoS, but shift: SGII gives lower transition temperature, NL3 gives higher transition temperature, compared to Sly5.
- For the fragmenting system A0=186, Z0=75 (expected from 124Sn+124Sn), the same EoS-dependent shift in transition temperature is observed.
- The Sly5 EoS parameters used: saturation density rho0 = 0.1604 fm^-3, saturation energy Esat = -15.98 MeV, symmetry energy Esym = 32.03 MeV, slope Lsym = 48.3 MeV, incompressibility Ksat = 230 MeV.
- The SGII EoS parameters: rho0 = 0.1583 fm^-3, Esat = -15.59 MeV, Esym = 26.83 MeV, Lsym = 37.6 MeV, Ksat = 215 MeV.
- The NL3 EoS parameters: rho0 = 0.1480 fm^-3, Esat = -16.24 MeV, Esym = 37.35 MeV, Lsym = 118.3 MeV, Ksat = 271 MeV.
- For fixed Z0=30 with A0=60, 66, and 72, the peak positions of specific heat and multiplicity derivative are almost independent of isospin asymmetry.
- For fixed Z0=75 with A0=168, 177, and 186, the peak positions are also almost independent of isospin asymmetry.
- After secondary decay, the peak position in multiplicity derivative remains unchanged for the three systems with Z0=30 and different A0, and the peaks become sharper.

## Context

This work extends the earlier theoretical proposal of the multiplicity derivative as a phase transition signal (Mallik 2016) by incorporating a semi-microscopic cluster functional based on realistic nuclear EoS, rather than the conventional liquid-drop binding and Fermi-gas excitation. It builds on the upgraded CTM with meta-modelling of the EoS (Mallik 2025) and tests the sensitivity of phase transition signatures to different EoS parameter sets (Sly5, SGII, NL3). The finding that the transition temperature is nearly independent of isospin asymmetry for finite nuclei contrasts with expectations from infinite nuclear matter studies (Ducoin) and enables experimental extraction of EoS information from the peak position without needing precise knowledge of the fragmenting system's isospin.
