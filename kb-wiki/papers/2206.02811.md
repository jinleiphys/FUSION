# A unified picture of medium-induced radiation

> We revisit the picture of jets propagating in the quark-gluon plasma. In addition to vacuum radiation, partons scatter on the medium constituents resulting in induced emissions. Analytical approaches to including these interactions have traditionally dealt separately with multiple, soft, or rare, hard scatterings. A full description has so far only been available using numerical methods. We achieve full analytical control of the relevant scales and map out the dominant physical processes in the full phase space. To this aim, we extend existing expansion schemes for the medium-induced spectrum to the Bethe--Heitler regime. This covers the whole phase space from early to late times, and from hard splittings to emissions below the thermal scale. Based on the separation of scales, a space-time picture naturally emerges: at early times, induced emissions start to build from rare scatterings with the medium. At a later stage, induced emissions due to multiple soft scatterings result in a turbulent cascade that rapidly degrades energy down to, and including, the Bethe--Heitler regime. We quantify the impact of such an improved picture, compared to the current state-of-the-art factorization that includes only soft scatterings, by both analytical and numerical methods for the medium-induced energy distribution function. Our work serves to improve our understanding of jet quenching from small to large systems and for future upgrades of Monte Carlo generators.

## Key claim

The authors achieve full analytical control of the medium-induced gluon radiation spectrum across the entire phase space, from hard splittings to emissions below the thermal scale, by extending existing expansion schemes to the Bethe–Heitler regime; this unified description was previously only available through numerical methods.

## Method

The authors revisit and extend three analytical expansion schemes for the medium-induced emission spectrum: the opacity expansion (OE), the improved opacity expansion (IOE), and a novel resummed opacity expansion (ROE) that covers the Bethe–Heitler regime. They derive the spectrum at arbitrary order and calculate it exactly up to second order in both the OE and ROE, establishing the regions of convergence for each scheme. The resulting splitting rates are then used to resum multiple emissions numerically and analytically, combining a fixed-order expansion of rare hard emissions with an all-order resummation of soft splittings to compute the energy distribution function.

## Key numbers

* The characteristic energy scale in the dilute regime is \(\bar \omega_c = \frac{1}{2} \mu^2 L\), where \(\mu\) is the screening mass and \(L\) the medium length.
* The characteristic energy scale in the dense regime (multiple soft scatterings) is \(\omega_c = \frac{1}{2} \hat q L^2\), where \(\hat q\) is the transverse momentum diffusion constant.
* The Bethe–Heitler scale is \(\omega_{\rm BH} = \frac{1}{2} \mu^2 \lambda\), where \(\lambda\) is the mean free path.
* In the dense regime, the BDMPS-Z spectrum for soft emissions scales as \(\omega \, dI/d\omega \sim \alpha_s \sqrt{\hat q L^2 / \omega}\) for \(\omega_{\rm BH} \ll \omega \ll \omega_c\).
* In the hard tail (\(\omega \gg \omega_c\)), the spectrum scales as \(\omega \, dI/d\omega \sim \alpha_s (L/\lambda) (\bar \omega_c / \omega)\).
* In the Bethe–Heitler regime (\(\omega < \omega_{\rm BH}\)), the spectrum scales as \(\omega \, dI/d\omega \sim \alpha_s L/\lambda\).
* The critical medium length for rare hard scatterings is \(L_c = \sqrt{2E / \hat q}\) (or \(L_c = \sqrt{E/(2\hat q)}\) for finite splitting fraction).

## Context

This work extends the analytical treatment of medium-induced radiation beyond the separate regimes previously addressed by the opacity expansion (Gyulassy, Levai, Vitev 1999; Wiedemann 2000) and the harmonic oscillator approximation (Baier, Dokshitzer, Mueller, Peigne, Schiff 1996; Zakharov 1997), and builds on the improved opacity expansion (Mehtar-Tani, Tywoniuk 2019; Barata et al. 2020-2021). It provides a unified framework that covers the full phase space, enabling systematic improvements to Monte Carlo generators for jet quenching phenomenology in both small and large collision systems.
