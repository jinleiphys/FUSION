# Influence of Transport Variables on Isospin Transport Ratios

> The symmetry energy in the nuclear equation of state affects many aspects of nuclear astrophysics, nuclear structure, and nuclear reactions. Recent constraints from heavy ion collisions, including isospin diffusion observables, have started to put constraints on the symmetry energy below nuclear saturation density, but these constraints depend on the employed transport model and input physics other than the symmetry energy. To understand these dependencies, we study the influence of the symmetry energy, isoscaler mean field compressibility and momentum dependence, in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections, and light cluster production on isospin diffusion within the pBUU transport code. In addition to the symmetry energy, several uncertain issues strongly affect isospin diffusion, most notably the cross sections and cluster production. In addition, there is a difference in the calculated isospin transport ratios, depending upon whether they are computed using the isospin asymmetry of either the residue or of all forward moving fragments. Measurements that compare the isospin transport ratios of these two quantities would help place constraints on the input physics, such as the density dependence of the symmetry energy.

## Key claim

The isospin transport ratio, a key observable for constraining the symmetry energy below nuclear saturation density, is strongly affected not only by the symmetry energy itself but also by in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections and light cluster production, and the calculated ratio differs depending on whether it is computed using the isospin asymmetry of the residue or of all forward-moving fragments.

## Method

The authors used the pBUU transport code, a Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck model, to simulate semi-peripheral collisions of 124Sn + 112Sn and 112Sn + 124Sn at 50 MeV/nucleon and an impact parameter of 6 fm. They systematically varied the symmetry energy parameters (density dependence gamma_i and interaction strength S_int), the isoscalar mean field compressibility (K=210 MeV and K=380 MeV) and momentum dependence (effective mass m* = 0.7 m_N), two forms of in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross sections (screened and Rostock), and the optional production of light clusters up to mass 3, then calculated the isospin transport ratio from the isospin asymmetry of the projectile-like residue or of all forward-moving fragments.

## Key numbers

- The symmetry energy was parameterized as E_sym(rho/rho_0) = 12.5 MeV (rho/rho_0)^(2/3) + S_int (rho/rho_0)^(gamma_i), with S_int varied and gamma_i = 1/3, 1, and 2.
- For the middle set of calculations, S_int was set to 19 MeV, giving E_sym(rho_0) = 31.5 MeV.
- The isoscalar compressibility values studied were K=210 MeV (near the accepted value of 231 +/- 5 MeV) and K=380 MeV.
- The momentum dependent mean field was characterized by an effective mass m* = 0.7 m_N.
- The screened cross section used sigma = sigma_0 tanh(sigma_free/sigma_0) with sigma_0 = 0.85 rho^(-2/3); the Rostock cross section used sigma = sigma_free exp(-0.6 (rho/rho_0) / (1 + (T_cm/150 MeV)^2)).
- Analysis was performed at 270 fm/c after the start of the simulation with a density cut of rho_0/20.
- Complete equilibration for the mass-asymmetric system gave R_i,eq = 0.051 from the simple calculation (A1 - A2)/(A1 + A2).

## Context

This work extends earlier isospin diffusion studies using the IBUU04 code (Baoan et al., 2005) and the ImQMD05 code (Tsang et al., 2009), which produced overlapping but different constraints on the symmetry energy. The results contradict the findings of Rizzo et al. (2008) using the stochastic mean field model, who reported that momentum dependence reduces diffusion by shortening interaction time, whereas this study finds momentum dependence increases diffusion due to neck anchoring by intermediate mass fragments. The work enables future experimental comparisons of isospin transport ratios from residues versus all forward-moving fragments to help constrain the density dependence of the symmetry energy.
