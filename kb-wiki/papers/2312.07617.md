# Quantum Computing for Nuclear Physics

> Future quantum computers are anticipated to be able to perform simulations of quantum many-body systems and quantum field theories that lie beyond the capabilities of classical computation. This will lead to new insights and predictions for systems ranging from dense non-equilibrium matter, to low-energy nuclear structure and reactions, to high-energy collisions. I present an overview of digital quantum simulations in nuclear physics, with select examples relevant for studies of quark matter.

# Quantum Computing for Nuclear Physics

## Key claim
Future quantum computers are anticipated to enable simulations of quantum many-body systems and quantum field theories beyond classical capabilities, but current devices remain limited to modest-sized systems of noisy qubits with short coherence times (the NISQ era), and near-term results with relatively large uncertainties (e.g., 10% or even 100%) would still constitute an improvement over classical computation alone for certain processes.

## Method
The paper provides an overview of digital quantum simulations in nuclear physics, reviewing lattice gauge theory implementations (including the Schwinger model in 1+1D), hybrid classical-quantum approaches for low-energy nuclear structure and reactions, quantum algorithms for thermalization studies (including quench dynamics and the Eigenstate Thermalization Hypothesis), and simulations of neutrino flavor evolution using all-to-all connected spin models mapped onto trapped-ion quantum computers. The review covers mappings of gauge theories to qubits, state preparation techniques, and the distinction between analog and digital quantum simulation.

## Key numbers
- Schwinger model simulations have progressed from 4 qubits on a trapped-ion quantum computer (Innsbruck team, 2016) to 100 qubits on an IBM 127-qubit quantum computer (Farrell et al., 2023)
- Trapped-ion systems from IonQ and Quantinuum now operate with more than 30 qubits and all-to-all connectivity
- IBM is developing modular superconducting-qubit systems with hundreds approaching thousands of qubits with heavy-hexagonal connectivity
- Neutrino simulations have been performed for systems with up to 20 neutrinos on quantum computers and simulators (Amitrano et al., 2022)

## Context
This review extends the foundational scaling analysis of lattice scalar field theory by Jordan, Lee and Preskill (2012) and the pioneering work of Feynman (1981) on quantum simulation. It builds on the Kogut-Susskind lattice QCD Hamiltonian (1974) and recent benchmarking of gauge theory implementations for U(1), SU(2) and SU(3) systems. The paper enables future work on real-time simulations of hadronization, low-energy nuclear reactions, thermalization, coherent neutrino flavor evolution, and early-universe matter production, while noting that particle detection for composite states remains to be established.
