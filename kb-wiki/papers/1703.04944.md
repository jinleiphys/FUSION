# Kinetic freeze-out temperatures in central and peripheral collisions: Which one is larger?

> The kinetic freeze-out temperatures, $T_0$, in nucleus-nucleus collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) and Large Hadron Collider (LHC) energies are extracted by four methods: i) the Blast-Wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (the BGBW model), ii) the Blast-Wave model with Tsallis statistics (the TBW model), iii) the Tsallis distribution with flow effect (the improved Tsallis distribution), and iv) the intercept in $T=T_0+am_0$ (the alternative method), where $m_0$ denotes the rest mass and $T$ denotes the effective temperature which can be obtained by different distribution functions. It is found that the relative sizes of $T_0$ in central and peripheral collisions obtained by the conventional BGBW model which uses a zero or nearly zero transverse flow velocity, $β_T$, are contradictory in tendency with other methods. With a re-examination for $β_T$ in the first method in which $β_T$ is taken to be $\sim(0.40\pm0.07)c$, a recalculation presents a consistent result with others. Finally, our results show that the kinetic freeze-out temperature in central collisions is larger than that in peripheral collisions.

## Key claim

The kinetic freeze-out temperature in central nucleus-nucleus collisions is larger than that in peripheral collisions, but the conventional Blast-Wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (BGBW) that assumes zero or nearly zero transverse flow velocity gives the opposite trend; only after re-examining the transverse flow velocity to be about (0.40 ± 0.07)c does the BGBW method yield a consistent result with other methods.

## Method

The authors extracted kinetic freeze-out temperatures from transverse momentum spectra of pions, kaons, protons, and antiprotons in Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV (RHIC) and Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV (LHC) using four methods: (i) the Blast-Wave model with Boltzmann-Gibbs statistics (BGBW), (ii) the Blast-Wave model with Tsallis statistics (TBW), (iii) the improved Tsallis distribution with flow effect, and (iv) the intercept method using T = T0 + a m0. For each method, the soft-process contribution was combined with an inverse power-law hard-process component to fit the full pT range, and the resulting parameters were compared between central and peripheral collision classes.

## Key numbers

- For central Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV, the BGBW method (method i) gave T0 = 0.113 ± 0.004 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.413 ± 0.006 c, and T0 = 0.127 ± 0.005 GeV for protons with βT = 0.392 ± 0.006 c.
- For peripheral Au-Au collisions at √sNN = 200 GeV, the BGBW method gave T0 = 0.160 ± 0.004 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.000⁺⁰·⁰¹⁶₋₀ c, and T0 = 0.251 ± 0.005 GeV for protons with βT = 0.000⁺⁰·⁰¹⁶₋₀ c.
- For central Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, the BGBW method gave T0 = 0.128 ± 0.004 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.434 ± 0.007 c, and T0 = 0.429 ± 0.005 GeV for protons with βT = 0.145 ± 0.005 c.
- For peripheral Pb-Pb collisions at √sNN = 2.76 TeV, the BGBW method gave T0 = 0.183 ± 0.004 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.000⁺⁰·⁰¹⁷₋₀ c, and T0 = 0.338 ± 0.004 GeV for protons with βT = 0.000⁺⁰·⁰¹⁷₋₀ c.
- The TBW method (method ii) for central Au-Au collisions gave T0 = 0.108 ± 0.004 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.472 ± 0.010 c and q = 1.008 ± 0.005.
- The improved Tsallis distribution (method iii) for central Au-Au collisions gave T0 = 0.113 ± 0.006 GeV for π⁺ with βT = 0.634 ± 0.009 c and q = 1.017 ± 0.007.

## Context

This work resolves a contradiction in the literature regarding whether kinetic freeze-out temperatures are higher in central or peripheral collisions, which had arisen from the conventional BGBW model's assumption of zero transverse flow in peripheral events. The study extends the analyses of Schnedermann et al. (1993) and the blast-wave formalism, and it enables more reliable comparisons of freeze-out conditions across different collision centralities at RHIC and LHC energies. The results support the physical expectation that more violent interactions in central collisions lead to higher kinetic freeze-out temperatures.
