# Questioning quark-gluon plasma formation in small collision systems

> A recent letter published in the journal Nature reports observation at the relativistic heavy ion collider (RHIC) of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) formation in small asymmetric collision systems denoted as $p$-Au, $d$-Au and $^3$He-Au. The claimed phenomenon, described as `short-lived QGP droplets,' is inferred from a combination of Glauber Monte Carlo simulations, measurement of two azimuth Fourier amplitudes $v_2$ and $v_3$ and hydro theory calculations. While that claim follows a trend in recent years to report `signals' conventionally attributed to QGP as appearing also in smaller collision systems, the new result remains surprising in the context of expectations before first RHIC operation that small systems, e.g. $d$-Au collisions, would provide control experiments in which a QGP was unlikely to appear. An alternative interpretation of the recent RHIC result is that small-system control experiments do convey an important message: The `signals' conventionally attributed to QGP formation in larger A-A collisions do not actually represent that phenomenon in any system. The present study reviews a broad array of experimental evidence for or against QGP formation in several collision systems. It examines in particular hydro theory descriptions of spectra and correlations usually interpreted to support QGP formation. Available evidence suggests that data features conventionally attributed to QGP formation represent either minimum-bias jet production or a nonjet azimuth quadrupole with properties inconsistent with a hydro hypothesis.

## Key claim
The paper argues that the claim of quark-gluon plasma (QGP) droplet formation in small asymmetric collision systems (p-Au, d-Au, 3He-Au) at RHIC is unsupported, because the Fourier amplitudes v2 and v3 conventionally attributed to QGP formation in larger A-A collisions may instead represent either minimum-bias jet production or a nonjet azimuth quadrupole with properties inconsistent with a hydrodynamic hypothesis.

## Method
The study reviews experimental evidence from multiple collision systems (p-p, d-Au, p-Pb, Au-Au, Pb-Pb) across energies from SPS (17 GeV) to LHC (13 TeV) using the two-component (soft + hard) model (TCM) framework. It applies differential analysis of single-particle p_t spectra, 2D angular correlations, and Fourier amplitudes v_n to distinguish jet-related from nonjet contributions, comparing results against hydrodynamic model predictions and Glauber Monte Carlo simulations.

## Key numbers
- Quadrupole source boost Delta y_t0 ≈ 0.6, common to all hadron species and all Au-Au collision centralities at 200 GeV, inconsistent with a Hubble-expanding dense medium
- Quadrupole spectrum temperature T2 ≈ 90 MeV, compared to single-particle spectrum temperature T0 ≈ 145 MeV for 200 GeV Au-Au collisions
- Statistical-model abundance scaling factors (1, 7, 26) for pion, kaon, and Lambda hadron species at T_chem ≈ 150 MeV describe quadrupole spectra across species
- Ratio of correlated pairs associated with the nonjet quadrupole scaled by (N_part/2) N_bin epsilon_opt^2 remains constant within 10% from most-peripheral to most-central 200 GeV Au-Au collisions
- Sharp transition in jet properties occurs near mid-centrality in Au-Au collisions, while nonjet quadrupole follows the same trend across all centralities

## Context
This work directly challenges the Nature letter (2019) claiming QGP droplet formation in small systems, which itself extends earlier claims of perfect liquid formation in Au-Au collisions from RHIC white papers (2005). The author argues that the conventional interpretation of v_n as flow signals is contradicted by TCM analysis showing that quadrupole spectra are cold (90 MeV) and independent of centrality, unlike expectations for a thermalized expanding medium. The study enables a re-evaluation of whether QGP signatures in all collision systems might instead arise from jet production and nonjet azimuth quadrupole correlations, with implications for interpreting results from both RHIC and LHC experiments.
