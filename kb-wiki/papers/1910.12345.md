# Experiments in machine learning of alpha-decay half-lives

> Artificial neural networks are trained by a standard backpropagation learning algorithm with regularization to model and predict the systematics of -decay of heavy and superheavy nuclei. This approach to regression is implemented in two alternative modes: (i) construction of a statistical global model based solely on available experimental data for alpha-decay half-lives, and (ii) modeling of the {\it residuals} between the predictions of state-of-the-art phenomenological model (specifically, the effective liquid-drop model (ELDM)) and experiment. Analysis of the results provide insights on the strengths and limitations of this application of machine learning (ML) to exploration of the nuclear landscape in regions beyond the valley of stability.

## Key claim

Artificial neural networks trained on alpha-decay half-lives of heavy and superheavy nuclei achieve a modest reduction in prediction error compared to the effective liquid-drop model (ELDM), but contrary to results in nuclear mass modeling, training on the residuals of the theoretical model (Net2) does not appreciably outperform a purely statistical neural network (Net1) trained directly on experimental data.

## Method

Feedforward multilayer perceptrons with one hidden layer and tanh activation functions were trained via backpropagation with Nesterov momentum and L2 regularization, using 7 input features (Z, N, their parities, Q_alpha, and distances from magic numbers) to predict log10(alpha-decay half-life). Two network models were constructed: Net1 trained directly on experimental half-lives of 150 heavy nuclei (Z >= 80), and Net2 trained on the residuals between ELDM predictions and experiment. Model selection used five-fold cross-validation over 2750 hyperparameter combinations (hidden units, regularization strength, learning rate, momentum), with 80% of data for training and 20% held out as a test set.

## Key numbers

- Training data: 150 nuclear isotopes with Z >= 80, assembled from Cui et al. (2018, 2019)
- Net1 achieved sigma(TR) = 0.4468, sigma(VS) = 0.5073, sigma(TS) = 0.4910 with optimal hyperparameters (J=4, lambda=10^-1, alpha=10^-3, mu=0.99)
- Net2 achieved sigma(TR) = 0.4586, sigma(VS) = 0.4756, sigma(TS) = 0.4773 with optimal hyperparameters (J=20, lambda=1, alpha=10^-3, mu=0.99)
- ELDM reference model gave sigma = 0.5690 on training set and 0.5845 on test set
- Elementary perceptron (no hidden layer) Net1 gave sigma(TS) = 0.5153; Net2 gave sigma(TS) = 0.4638

## Context

This work extends the limited prior machine-learning studies of alpha decay by Bayram (2013) and Rodriguez (2017) to a larger dataset and systematic comparison with the effective liquid-drop model (ELDM) of Goncalves and Duarte. Unlike machine learning of nuclear masses, where neural networks significantly outperform phenomenological models (as shown by Utama et al., 2015), the alpha-decay problem suffers from sparse training data (hundreds versus thousands of examples) and larger experimental uncertainties in half-life measurements. The results suggest that for alpha decay, where predictions within an order of magnitude are considered acceptable, simpler models may be as effective as complex neural networks, and future work should incorporate explicit noise modeling via algorithms such as CleanProp.
