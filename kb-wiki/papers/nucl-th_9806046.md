# Complete set of electromagnetic corrections to strongly interacting systems

> We show how to obtain a complete set of electromagnetic corrections to a given nonperturbative model of strong interactions based on integral equations. The gauge invariance of these corrections is a consequence of their completeness.

## Key claim

The paper presents a method to include the *complete* set of lowest-order (e²) electromagnetic corrections into any nonperturbative strong interaction model based on integral equations, with the claim that gauge invariance of these corrections follows directly from their completeness, a condition that previous approaches could not guarantee.

## Method

The authors work in relativistic quantum field theory, starting from a strong interaction Green function G defined by an integral equation with a model potential K. They extend the "gauging of equations" method to derive an integral equation for the complete electromagnetic correction δG, which is then formally solved to obtain δG = G Δ G, where Δ contains all lowest-order electromagnetic corrections to the potential. The key result expresses Δ in terms of the strong interaction solution G itself, plus the one-particle electromagnetic vertex functions, the interaction current K^μ, and the photon propagator D_{μν}.

## Key numbers

- The electromagnetic corrections are computed to lowest order in the coupling constant e, i.e., order e².
- For the bound state mass shift, the result is δM = (1/2M) ψ̄_P Δ ψ_P, where ψ_P is the strong interaction bound state wave function normalized to 1 via i ψ̄_P (∂/∂P²)(G₀⁻¹ − K) ψ_P = 1 at P² = M².

## Context

This work extends the gauging of equations method introduced in Ref. [g4d] to include internal (virtual) photons, solving a long-standing problem in nuclear physics where only incomplete sets of electromagnetic corrections were available. The method is directly relevant to calculations of isospin symmetry breaking and charge symmetry breaking (CSB) in hadronic systems, such as the neutron-proton mass difference (Stephenson, year not given), the binding energy difference of ³H and ³He (Sasakawa, year not given), and CSB measurements in reactions like πN → πN, np → np, and np → dπ⁰ (Miller, year not given). It also applies to extracting π-π scattering lengths from π⁺π⁻ atom lifetime measurements (Rusetsky, year not given). The formalism enables reliable, gauge-invariant calculations of electromagnetic corrections to bound state masses and scattering amplitudes for any number of quarks or hadrons.
