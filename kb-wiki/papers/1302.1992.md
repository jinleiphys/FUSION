# Pygmy and giant electric dipole responses of medium-heavy nuclei in a self-consistent Random Phase Approximation approach with finite-range interaction

> The pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) is studied in various medium-heavy nuclei by using a Gogny interaction in a self-consistent Hartree-Fock plus Random Phase Approximation method. We compare the details of the PDR structure with those of the giant dipole resonance (GDR). In the PDR protons and neutrons vibrate in phase, and the main contributions are given by particle-hole excitations involving the neutrons in excess. On the contrary, in the GDR protons and neutrons vibrate out of phase, and all the nucleons are involved in the excitation. The values of the parameters we used to define the collectivity of an excitation indicate that the PDR is less collective than the GDR. We also investigate the role of the residual interaction in the appearance of the PDR and we find a subtle interplay with the shell structure of the nucleus.

## Key claim

The pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) is less collective than the giant dipole resonance (GDR) and involves protons and neutrons vibrating in phase, with main contributions from neutron-excess particle-hole excitations; however, the appearance of the PDR depends on a subtle interplay with the shell structure of each nucleus, and the residual interaction alone does not guarantee its presence.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Hartree-Fock (HF) plus Random Phase Approximation (RPA) calculations using the finite-range Gogny D1M interaction (and D1S for comparison) for spherical, closed-subshell nuclei. They employed two RPA variants: a continuum RPA (CRPA) that treats unbound single-particle states exactly, and a discrete RPA (DRPA) that confines the system in a spherical box and diagonalizes the RPA matrix. The spurious center-of-mass motion was removed by hand in DRPA results.

## Key numbers

- For $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb, the CRPA/DRPA centroid energies of the GDR are 24.8, 22.0, and 15.84 MeV, respectively, compared to experimental values of 22.9, 20.0, and 14.7 MeV.
- The energy-weighted sum rule (integrated photoabsorption cross section) for $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb is 336, 834, and 4420 mb MeV, respectively; the Thomas-Reiche-Kuhn (TRK) sum rule with a 0.51 enhancement factor gives 360, 900, and 4470 mb MeV.
- The PDR strength (ratio $\mathcal{R}$) is at most about 5% of the total dipole strength for oxygen and calcium isotopes, and about 1–2% for tin isotopes.
- The separation energy $\omega_{\rm sep}$ between PDR and GDR regions ranges from 10.0 MeV ($^{208}$Pb) to 15.5 MeV ($^{16}$O); representative PDR energies $\omega_{\rm PDR}$ range from 6.85 MeV ($^{24}$O) to 13.71 MeV ($^{16}$O), and GDR energies $\omega_{\rm GDR}$ range from 14.47 MeV ($^{208}$Pb) to 25.11 MeV ($^{16}$O).
- For $^{48}$Ca, the PDR strength ratio $\mathcal{R}$ is smaller than 1%.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous phenomenological RPA study (Co' et al., 2009) that used a Woods-Saxon basis and a zero-range Landau-Migdal force, by adopting a fully self-consistent HF+RPA approach with the finite-range Gogny interaction, eliminating the need for nucleus-specific parameter tuning. The results are compared with experimental data for $^{16}$O, $^{40}$Ca, and $^{208}$Pb from Ahrens et al. (1975) and with empirical PDR regions identified in $^{22}$O (Leistenschneider et al., 2001), $^{48}$Ca (Hartmann et al., 2004), $^{68}$Ni (Wieland et al., 2009), $^{132}$Sn (Adrich et al., 2005), and $^{208}$Pb (Ryezayeva et al., 2002). The study enables future investigations of PDR collectivity beyond RPA, as the authors note that discrepancies in $^{48}$Ca and $^{208}$Pb likely arise from missing two-particle–two-hole configurations, as found in second RPA calculations by Gambacurta et al. (2011) and extended theory of finite Fermi systems by Kamerdzhiev et al. (2004).
