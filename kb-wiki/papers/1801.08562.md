# N* Structure and Strong QCD

> In attempting to match QCD with Nature, it is necessary to confront the many complexities of strong, nonlinear dynamics in relativistic quantum field theory, e.g. the loss of particle number conservation, the frame and scale dependence of the explanations and interpretations of observable processes, and the evolving character of the relevant degrees-of-freedom. The peculiarities of QCD ensure that it is also the only known fundamental theory with the capacity to sustain massless elementary degrees-of-freedom, gluons and quarks; and yet gluons and quarks are predicted to acquire mass dynamically so that the only massless systems in QCD are its composite Nambu-Goldstone bosons. All other everyday bound states possess nuclear-size masses, far in excess of anything that can directly be tied to the Higgs boson. These observations highlight fundamental questions within the Standard Model: what is the source of the mass for the vast bulk of visible matter in the Universe, how is its appearance connected with confinement; how is this mass distributed within hadrons and does the distribution differ from one hadron to another? This contribution sketches insights drawn using modern methods for the continuum bound-state problem in QCD, and how they have been informed by empirical information on the hadron spectrum and nucleon-to-resonance transition form factors.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the mass of visible matter arises primarily from dynamical chiral symmetry breaking (DCSB) in QCD, not from the Higgs mechanism, and that this mass generation is inseparable from confinement; the proton mass is generated by gluon self-interactions via the trace anomaly, while the pion remains massless in the chiral limit due to precise cancellations between one-body dressing and two-body binding effects.

## Method

The author uses continuum bound-state methods in QCD, specifically the Dyson-Schwinger equations (gap equation for dressed-quark propagators) and Bethe-Salpeter equations (for meson bound states), combined with a Poincaré-covariant Faddeev equation framework that incorporates diquark correlations as the dominant two-body subsystems within baryons. The analysis is informed by lattice QCD results for the gluon propagator and dressed-quark mass function, and by empirical data on the Bjorken sum rule and nucleon-to-resonance transition form factors.

## Key numbers

* The gluon propagator saturates at infrared momenta with a renormalisation-group-invariant mass scale \(m_0 \approx 0.5\) GeV, approximately half the proton mass.
* The scalar diquark correlation mass scale is \(m_{[ud]_{0^+}} \approx 0.7-0.8\) GeV; the pseudovector diquark mass scale is \(m_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} \approx 0.9-1.1\) GeV.
* The scalar diquark radius satisfies \(r_{[ud]_{0^+}} \gtrsim r_\pi\) and the pseudovector diquark radius satisfies \(r_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} \gtrsim r_\rho\), with \(r_{\{uu\}_{1^+}} > r_{[ud]_{0^+}}\).
* For the \(\rho\)-meson, the scalar form factor at zero momentum transfer gives \(s_\rho(0) \approx 0.06 \, m_\rho^2\), indicating only 6% of its mass-squared comes from the current-quark mass term.
* The proton PDA at \(\zeta=2\) GeV peaks at \(([x]) = (0.55, 0.23, 0.22)\), compared to the conformal limit peak at \((1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\).

## Context

This work extends the continuum bound-state approach to QCD developed by Roberts and collaborators over two decades, building on the Schwinger mechanism for gluon mass generation first shown by Cornwall (1981) and on lattice QCD results for the gluon propagator and quark mass function (Bowman et al., 2005). It enables a unified description of nucleon elastic and transition form factors, distribution amplitudes, and the baryon spectrum, addressing the "missing resonance" problem that plagued earlier quark models (Burkert, 2004) and connecting to modern experimental programs at Jefferson Lab.
