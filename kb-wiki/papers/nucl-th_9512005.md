# Nuclear Flow in Consistent Boltzmann Algorithm Models

> We investigate the stochastic Direct Simulation Monte Carlo method (DSMC) for numerically solving the collision-term in heavy-ion transport theories of the Boltzmann-Uehling-Uhlenbeck (BUU) type. The first major modification we consider is changes in the collision rates due to excluded volume and shadowing/screening effects (Enskog theory). The second effect studied by us is the inclusion of an additional advection term. These modifications ensure a non-vanishing second virial and change the equation of state for the scattering process from that of an ideal gas to that of a hard-sphere gas. We analyse the effect of these modifications on the calculated value of directed nuclear collective flow in heavy ion collisions, and find that the flow slightly increases.

# Nuclear Flow in Consistent Boltzmann Algorithm Models

## Key claim

The modifications to the Direct Simulation Monte Carlo (DSMC) method, specifically the inclusion of excluded volume and shadowing/screening effects (Enskog theory) plus an additional advection term, increase the calculated directed nuclear collective flow in heavy ion collisions, but the effect moves simulation results away from experimental data, indicating the need for an in-medium reduction of the NN cross section.

## Method

The authors modified an existing BUU code (Bauer et al.) from a geometrical collision term to a stochastic DSMC formulation (Lang et al.), then implemented two modifications from Alexander et al.: an extra displacement of collision partners according to their hard-sphere radius, and a modified scattering probability incorporating excluded volume and screening effects via Enskog theory. They simulated (Au,Au) collisions at 250 and 400 MeV projectile energy with impact parameter b=3 fm, using a soft momentum-dependent equation of state, and compared the resulting nuclear flow (average final transverse momentum versus reduced rapidity) between the unmodified and modified algorithms.

## Key numbers

- For 250 MeV (Au,Au) collisions at b=3 fm: nuclear flow increased by approximately 16% with the modified algorithm (from ~147 to ~170 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity))
- For 400 MeV (Au,Au) collisions at b=3 fm: nuclear flow increased by approximately 5% with the modified algorithm (from ~185 to ~195 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity))
- Impact parameter averaged analysis for 250 MeV collisions with b ≤ 5 fm: unmodified flow ~132 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity), modified flow ~148 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity) (12% increase)
- Impact parameter averaged analysis for 400 MeV collisions with b ≤ 5 fm: unmodified flow ~166 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity), modified flow ~185 MeV/(c·Unit of Reduced Rapidity) (11% increase)
- Effective hard-sphere radius a: ~0.84 fm for small Elab, ~0.47 fm in the range 100-400 MeV
- Experimental comparison: Plastic Ball data ~130 MeV/(c·Unit of Red. Rap.) at 250 MeV and ~169 MeV/(c·Unit of Red. Rap.) at 400 MeV; EOS data ~119 and ~151 MeV/(c·Unit of Red. Rap.) respectively

## Context

This work extends the DSMC approach of Lang et al. (1993) and Danielewicz and Bertsch (1992) by incorporating the Consistent Boltzmann Algorithm modifications proposed by Alexander, Garcia, and Alder (1995) and the Enskog theory of dense hard-sphere fluids from Resibois and De Leener (1977). The results contradict the expectation that these modifications would bring simulations closer to experimental data; instead, they move away from experimental values from the Plastic Ball collaboration (Gustafson et al., 1988) and the EOS collaboration (Partlan et al., 1995), suggesting that in-medium reduction of the NN cross section, previously found needed in studies of flow disappearance by Westfall et al. (1993) and in T-matrix theory by Alm et al. (1995), is required for DSMC algorithms addressing the nuclear equation of state.
