# Effect of the Coulomb interaction on the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter

> We investigate the role of the Coulomb interaction on the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter with three different values of proton fraction ($Y_p$=0.5,0.3 and 0.1), relevant for heavy-ion physics as well as various astrophysical scenarios, within the framework of quantum molecular dynamics. We perform simulations for a wide range of density and temperature with and without the Coulomb interaction and calculate the two-point correlation functions of nucleon density fluctuations for all the configurations to determine the phase transition region. We also determine the critical end point of the liquid-gas phase transition for all three values of proton fraction considered. We observe that the Coulomb interaction reduces the transition temperature by about 2 MeV for nuclear matter with $Y_p$=0.5 and 0.3 and by about 1 MeV for nuclear matter with $Y_p$=0.1. However, the critical density is found to be more or less insensitive to the Coulomb interaction.

# Effect of the Coulomb interaction on the liquid-gas phase transition of nuclear matter

## Key claim
The Coulomb interaction reduces the critical temperature of the liquid-gas phase transition by approximately 2-3 MeV for symmetric and moderately asymmetric nuclear matter (proton fractions Yp=0.5 and 0.3) and by about 1 MeV for highly asymmetric matter (Yp=0.1), while the critical density remains largely unchanged regardless of proton fraction. This conclusion holds across different nuclear models, including those with explicit surface terms.

## Method
Quantum molecular dynamics (QMD) simulations were performed for nuclear matter at sub-saturation densities (0.1 to 0.775 times normal nuclear density) and temperatures from 1 to 9 MeV, using the Hamiltonian developed by Maruyama et al. (1998). The simulations used 2048 nucleons for Yp=0.5 and 0.3, and 16384 nucleons for Yp=0.1, with periodic boundary conditions and the Ewald method for Coulomb interactions. The phase transition region was determined by calculating two-point correlation functions of nucleon density fluctuations and identifying where long-range correlations vanish.

## Key numbers
- For symmetric nuclear matter (Yp=0.5): critical temperature Tc is greater than or approximately 5 MeV with Coulomb, and greater than or approximately 7.5 MeV without Coulomb; critical density rho_c is approximately 0.225-0.25 rho_0 in both cases
- For Yp=0.3: critical temperature Tc is greater than or approximately 4.5 MeV with Coulomb, and greater than or approximately 7 MeV without Coulomb; critical density rho_c is approximately 0.2 rho_0 with Coulomb and approximately 0.225 rho_0 without
- For Yp=0.1: critical temperature Tc is greater than or approximately 3 MeV with Coulomb, and greater than or approximately 4 MeV without Coulomb; critical density rho_c is approximately 0.125 rho_0 in both cases
- At zero temperature, the liquid-gas transition density for Yp=0.5 shifts from between 0.725-0.75 rho_0 without Coulomb to between 0.7-0.725 rho_0 with Coulomb
- At zero temperature for Yp=0.3, the transition density shifts from between 0.675-0.7 rho_0 without Coulomb to between 0.625-0.65 rho_0 with Coulomb
- For Yp=0.1 at zero temperature, the transition density shifts from between 0.45-0.475 rho_0 without Coulomb to between 0.475-0.5 rho_0 with Coulomb (slight increase)
- Normal nuclear matter density rho_0 = 0.165 fm^{-3}

## Context
This work extends previous QMD studies of nuclear matter pasta phases by Watanabe et al. (2003, 2004, 2005, 2009) and Nandi et al. (2016) by systematically isolating the effect of the Coulomb interaction on the phase transition. The results confirm earlier findings by Jaqaman et al. (1984) using Skyrme interactions that the Coulomb interaction reduces critical temperature while leaving critical density unchanged. The study contrasts with bulk Gibbs equilibrium calculations by Mueller and Serot (1995), Avancini et al. (2006), and others that ignore finite-size effects, and with the compressible liquid-drop model of Bao et al. (2016) that found finite-size effects significantly reduce the mixed phase region. The results enable more accurate modeling of supernova dynamics and neutron star crust properties, and the authors plan to investigate particle number susceptibilities around the critical endpoint for comparison with heavy-ion collision observables.
