# Mass ordering of differential elliptic flow and its violation for phi mesons

> We simulate the dynamics of Au+Au collisions at the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC) with a hybrid model that treats the dense early quark-gluon plasma (QGP) stage macroscopically as an ideal fluid, but models the dilute late hadron resonance gas (HG) microscopically using a hadronic cascade. By comparing with a pure hydrodynamic approach we identify effects of hadronic viscosity on the transverse momentum spectra and differential elliptic flow v_2(p_T). We investigate the dynamical origins of the observed mass-ordering of v_2(p_T) for identified hadrons, focusing on dissipative effects during the late hadronic stage. We find that, at RHIC energies, much of the finally observed mass-splitting is generated during the hadronic stage, due to build-up of additional radial flow. The phi meson, having a small interaction cross section, does not fully participate in this additional flow. As a result, it violates the mass-ordering pattern for v_2(p_T) that is observed for other hadron species. We also show that the early decoupling of the phi meson from the hadronic rescattering dynamics leads to interesting and unambiguous features in the p_T-dependence of the nuclear suppression factor R_AA and of the phi/p ratio.

## Key claim

The mass ordering of differential elliptic flow \(v_2(p_T)\) for identified hadrons is largely generated during the late hadronic stage by the build-up of additional radial flow, but the phi meson, due to its small interaction cross section, does not fully participate in this flow and therefore violates the mass-ordering pattern observed for other hadron species.

## Method

The authors used a hybrid model that treats the early quark-gluon plasma stage as an ideal fluid via (3+1)-dimensional relativistic hydrodynamics, then switches at a switching temperature of 169 MeV to the JAM hadronic cascade model for the dilute late hadron resonance gas stage. They compared results from this hybrid model with those from a pure hydrodynamic approach that assumes ideal fluid behavior down to a kinetic decoupling temperature of 100 MeV, in order to isolate dissipative effects from hadronic rescattering. Initial conditions were based on a Glauber model with a soft/hard fraction \(\alpha = 0.85\) tuned to reproduce the centrality dependence of charged hadron multiplicity at midrapidity.

## Key numbers

- Switching temperature from hydrodynamics to hadronic cascade: \(T_{\mathrm{sw}} = 169\) MeV.
- Kinetic decoupling temperature in pure hydrodynamic approach: \(T_{\mathrm{th}} = 100\) MeV.
- Critical temperature for the quark-hadron phase transition: \(T_c = 170\) MeV.
- Bag constant used in the QGP equation of state: \(B^{1/4} = 247.19\) MeV.
- Initial time for hydrodynamic evolution: \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\).
- Inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross section used in Glauber model: \(\sigma_{NN}^{\mathrm{in}} = 42\) mb.
- Beam energy: \(\sqrt{s} = 200\,A\) GeV for Au+Au collisions at RHIC.
- Impact parameters studied: \(b = 2.0\), 7.2, and 9.7 fm, corresponding to 0-5%, 20-30%, and 30-40% centrality ranges, respectively.
- The hybrid model reproduces experimental \(p_T\) spectra for pions, kaons, and protons up to \(p_T \sim 1.5\)-2.0 GeV/\(c\).
- The mass ordering \(v_2^\pi(p_T) > v_2^K(p_T) > v_2^p(p_T)\) is correctly described by the hybrid model for low \(p_T\).
- The phi meson lifetime is approximately 46 fm/\(c\), longer than the typical system lifetime of 10-20 fm/\(c\).

## Context

This work extends the hybrid model study presented in Hirano et al. (2007, HHKLN) by focusing on dissipative effects during the late hadronic rescattering stage and their impact on differential elliptic flow. It contrasts with pure hydrodynamic approaches that assume ideal fluid behavior down to kinetic freeze-out, such as those used by Kolb and Heinz (2002) and Huovinen (2007). The finding that the phi meson violates mass ordering due to its weak coupling to the hadronic medium enables future experimental searches for this signature as a clean probe of hadronic viscosity and the decoupling dynamics of rare probes, and it motivates further viscous hydrodynamic studies such as those by Romatschke (2007) and Song (2007) to disentangle early and late viscosity effects.
