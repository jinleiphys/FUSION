# Correlator Analysis of Multiparticle Events

> A procedure for the evaluation of correlators of any order in a reasonable computer time is presented. Connection between correlators and fluctuations of the event mean values of observables is discussed. Extension of the procedure to event-by-event approach is suggested. The usefulness of the method is demonstrated using the events simulated within various models of multipaticle production.

## Key claim

The paper presents a fast procedure for evaluating particle correlators of any order from multiparticle events, but the method's practical usefulness is limited because the relative errors of correlator estimates increase rapidly with order \(l\), making measurements for \(l > 4\) quite uneasy and requiring very high statistics.

## Method

The authors define correlators \(C_l^{(x)}\) as integral moment-type quantities over multivariate probability distribution functions, where for \(l\) mutually different particle indices the correlator measures genuine correlation beyond independent production. They derive a computationally efficient method to estimate correlators using central moments of single-particle distributions within each event (Eqs. KK2-KK4), reducing the number of operations from \(\propto n^l\) to \(\propto n\). The procedure is generalized to events with varying multiplicities and to multiple particle species, and an extension to event-by-event analysis via sub-event splitting is suggested.

## Context

This work extends the correlator formalism of Mrowczynski and Schäfer (2001) by providing a practical computational procedure and clarifying the connection between correlators and event-to-event fluctuations of observable means. The method is demonstrated on events from the parton string model (PSM) of Amelin (2001) and on microcanonical phase-space ensembles, showing that non-zero correlators in high-energy nuclear collisions can signal non-statistical fluctuations from semihard parton collisions, as observed in STAR (2003) measurements at RHIC. The procedure enables future event-by-event correlation studies in relativistic heavy-ion experiments.
