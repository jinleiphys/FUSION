# Data Selection Criteria for Spectroscopic Measurements of Neutron Star Radii with X-ray Bursts

> Data selection and the determination of systematic uncertainties in the spectroscopic measurements of neutron star radii from thermonuclear X-ray bursts have been the subject of numerous recent studies. In one approach, the uncertainties and outliers were determined by a data-driven Bayesian mixture model, whereas in a second approach, data selection was performed by requiring that the observations follow theoretical expectations. We show here that, due to inherent limitations in the data, the theoretically expected trends are not discernible in the majority of X-ray bursts even if they are present. Therefore, the proposed theoretical selection criteria are not practical with the current data for distinguishing clean data sets from outliers. Furthermore, when the data limitations are not taken into account, the theoretically motivated approach selects a small subset of bursts with properties that are in fact inconsistent with the underlying assumptions of the method. We conclude that the data-driven selection methods do not suffer from the limitations of this theoretically motivated one.

## Key claim

The theoretically expected trends in blackbody normalization versus flux are not discernible in the majority of X-ray bursts observed with RXTE, even if those trends are present, due to inherent data limitations; therefore, the theoretically motivated selection criteria of Poutanen et al. (2014) are not practical with current data and, when applied without accounting for these limitations, select a small subset of bursts whose properties are inconsistent with the method's own underlying assumptions.

## Method

The authors analyzed the cooling tails of thermonuclear X-ray bursts from six neutron star sources observed with the Rossi X-ray Timing Explorer (RXTE), using 0.25-second time bin integrations. They compared the data-driven Bayesian Gaussian mixture approach of Guver et al. (2012a, 2012b) to the theoretically motivated selection criteria of Poutanen et al. (2014), which require bursts to follow predicted trends in blackbody normalization from the atmosphere models of Suleimanov et al. (2012). They performed Monte Carlo simulations of mock burst observations using Model #17 of Suleimanov et al. (2012) to demonstrate how scatter in the emitting radius masks theoretical correlations, and they examined the fractional flux and blackbody normalization changes around touchdown points in all photospheric radius expansion (PRE) bursts from the six sources used in Ozel et al. (2015).

## Key numbers

- The typical fractional change in flux between the two 0.25-second time bins adjacent to the touchdown point is in the 5-20% range for all PRE bursts from the six sources used in Ozel et al. (2015).
- The fractional change in blackbody normalization during the 0.25-second time bin prior to touchdown is in the 20-80% range for the same bursts.
- In one 0.25-second time bin after the true touchdown point, the flux typically changes by approximately 10%, causing the color correction factor to drop from approximately 1.9 to 1.6 and the blackbody normalization to change by approximately 50%.
- In the example burst #21 from EXO 1745-248, the X-ray flux evolves by 18% between the two time bins adjacent to the touchdown point.
- The inferred touchdown fluxes of the bursts selected by Poutanen et al. (2014) for 4U 1608-52 are 30% below the touchdown fluxes of securely identified PRE bursts that reach the Eddington limit.

## Context

This paper extends the debate between the data-driven Bayesian mixture model approach of Guver et al. (2012a, 2012b) and the theoretically motivated selection criteria of Poutanen et al. (2014) and Kajava et al. (2014) for measuring neutron star radii from thermonuclear X-ray bursts. The authors argue that the theoretically motivated method is impractical with current RXTE data because the rapid spectral evolution near the Eddington limit cannot be resolved with 0.25-second time bins, and that the method selects bursts that are not actually photospheric radius expansion bursts, violating the method's own assumptions. The work enables future radius measurements with X-ray detectors having larger collecting area and better time resolution, while concluding that the data-driven selection methods of Guver et al. (2012a, 2012b) and the radius measurements of Ozel et al. (2015) are not contradicted by theoretical expectations.
