# Neutrino Interactions with Nucleons and Nuclei: Importance for Long-Baseline Experiments

> This article reviews our present knowledge of neutrino interactions with nucleons and discusses the interactions with nuclei, the target material of all presently running and planned long-baseline experiments. I emphasize descriptions of semi-inclusive reactions and full descriptions of the final state; the latter are needed to reconstruct the incoming neutrino energy from final-state observations. I then discuss Monte Carlo generator and more advanced transport theoretical approaches in connection with experimental results on various reaction mechanisms. Finally, I describe the effects of uncertainties in the reconstruction of the incoming neutrino energy on oscillation parameters. The review argues that the precision era of neutrino physics also needs precision-era generators.

## Key claim
This review argues that achieving the precision needed for neutrino oscillation measurements in long-baseline experiments (such as DUNE) requires moving beyond current Monte Carlo event generators, which often rely on outdated physics and parameter tunes, to precision-era generators based on consistent transport theory frameworks like the Kadanoff–Baym equations.

## Method
The paper reviews the theoretical description of neutrino interactions with nucleons (quasi-elastic scattering, pion production via resonances, and deep-inelastic scattering) and with nuclei, emphasizing the role of final-state interactions. It compares the transport-theory approach (specifically the GiBUU framework, which solves the Boltzmann–Uehling–Uhlenbeck equations) with standard neutrino event generators (GENIE, NEUT) that use simplified quasi-particle approximations, neglect in-medium potentials, and rely on phenomenological tunes.

## Key numbers
- The axial mass \(M_A\) extracted from neutrino experiments on nucleons or deuterons assumes a value of approximately 1 GeV, with a world average of 1.03 GeV.
- The neutrino energy must be determined to better than approximately 100 MeV to distinguish oscillation signals at DUNE (1300 km baseline).
- The ANL and BNL single-pion production data sets on proton targets differ by approximately 30% at higher energies; after flux recalibration the BNL data agreed with the ANL data.
- For quasi-elastic scattering on nuclei, the inclusive cross section scales approximately linearly with target mass number \(A\), with a slight disturbance of about 10% at the QE peak (Sealock et al., 1989).
- The impulse approximation for quasi-elastic scattering is expected to be reliable for momentum transfers larger than approximately 300 MeV.

## Context
This review extends the theoretical framework of neutrino-nucleus interactions developed by groups such as the Valencia and Torino groups (Gil et al., 1997; Alberico et al., 1997) and the scaling approach of Day et al. (1990). It contrasts the transport-theory method (GiBUU) with standard generators like GENIE (Andreopoulos et al., 2009) and NEUT (Hayato, 2009), which are widely used in long-baseline experiments (T2K, MINOS, NOvA, DUNE). The review enables a path toward more reliable energy reconstruction and oscillation parameter extraction by advocating for generators built on consistent many-body theory rather than empirical tunes.
