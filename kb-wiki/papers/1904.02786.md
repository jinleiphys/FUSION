# Radioactive beams and inverse kinematics: probing the quantal texture of the nuclear vacuum

> The properties of the quantum electrodynamic (QED) vacuum in general, and of the nuclear vacuum (ground) state in particular are determined by virtual processes implying the excitation of a photon and of an electron--positron pair in the first case and of, for example, the excitation of a collective quadrupole surface vibration and a particle--hole pair in the nuclear case. Signals of these processes can be detected in the laboratory in terms of what can be considered a nuclear analogue of Hawking radiation. An analogy which extends to other physical processes involving QED vacuum fluctuations like the Lamb shift, pair creation by $γ-$rays, van der Waals forces and the Casimir effect, to the extent that one concentrates on the eventual outcome resulting by forcing a virtual process to become real, and not on the role of the black hole role in defining the event horizon. In the nuclear case, the role of this event is taken over at a microscopic, fully quantum mechanical level, by nuclear probes (reactions) acting on a virtual particle of the zero point fluctuation (ZPF) of the nuclear vacuum in a similar irreversible, no--return, fashion as the event horizon does, letting the other particle, entangled with the first one, escape to infinity, and eventually be detected. With this proviso in mind one can posit that the reactions $^1$H($^{11}$Be,$^{10}$Be$(2^+$;3.37 ${\rm MeV}$))$^2$H and $^{1}$H($^{11}$Li,$^9$Li($1/2^-$; 2.69 ${\rm MeV}$))$^3$H together with the associated $γ-$decay processes indicate a possible nuclear analogy of Hawking radiation.

## Key claim

The paper posits that certain nuclear reactions involving halo nuclei, specifically \(^{1}\text{H}(^{11}\text{Be},^{10}\text{Be}(2^+;3.37\ \text{MeV}))^{2}\text{H}\) and \(^{1}\text{H}(^{11}\text{Li},^{9}\text{Li}(1/2^-;2.69\ \text{MeV}))^{3}\text{H}\), can be interpreted as a nuclear analogue of Hawking radiation, provided one accepts that the irreversible action of a nuclear probe on a virtual particle of the nuclear vacuum zero-point fluctuation plays the same role as the event horizon in the gravitational case.

## Method

The authors apply Nuclear Field Theory (NFT), a diagrammatic framework modeled after QED, to describe the nuclear vacuum as composed of virtual particle-hole excitations and collective vibrations (the nuclear analogues of electron-positron pairs and photons). They analyze one- and two-nucleon pickup reactions on the halo nuclei \(^{11}\text{Be}\) and \(^{11}\text{Li}\) in inverse kinematics, calculating absolute differential cross sections for populating specific excited states of the residual cores, and compare these to experimental data. The calculations incorporate self-energy and vertex-correction diagrams renormalized to infinite order via Dyson-like equations, using the \(v_{14}\) Argonne potential as the bare nucleon-nucleon interaction.

## Key numbers

* The zero-point fluctuation (ZPF) contribution to the binding energy of \(^{11}\text{Be}\) is 4110 keV, amounting to 6.3% of the total binding energy (65477.94 keV); for \(^{11}\text{Li}\) the ZPF contribution is 2150 keV, or 4.7% of the total binding energy (45709.18 keV).
* In \(^{11}\text{Be}\), 86% of the ZPF mass defect comes from quadrupole modes; in \(^{11}\text{Li}\), 74% comes from quadrupole modes.
* The Pauli principle correction (repulsion) in \(^{11}\text{Be}\) is approximately 2.86 MeV, while the self-energy clothing of the \(2s_{1/2}\) level by the quadrupole mode lowers its energy by about 570 keV.
* The two-neutron separation energy \(S_{2n}\) for \(^{11}\text{Li}\) is 380 keV.
* The differential cross section for \(^{9}\text{Li}(d,p)^{10}\text{Li}(1/2^-)\) at the maximum angle is approximately 0.8 mb/sr.

## Context

This work extends the analogy between QED vacuum fluctuations and nuclear vacuum fluctuations previously developed in NFT (Bes 1974, Bortignon 1977, Broglia 2016) to the specific phenomenon of Hawking radiation, building on the observation by Hawking (1975) that particle creation by a black hole is analogous to that caused by a deep potential well in flat space-time. The paper connects to experimental studies of halo nuclei at TRIUMF (Tanihata 2013), GANIL (Keeley 2004), and RIKEN (Motobayashi 2012), and to earlier theoretical work on parity inversion in \(^{11}\text{Be}\) and induced pairing in \(^{11}\text{Li}\) (Barranco 2001, Baroni 2004). It enables future coincidence experiments that would detect the \(\gamma\)-ray from the de-excitation of the core vibration in coincidence with the outgoing light particle (deuteron or triton), providing a controlled nuclear testbed for concepts of entanglement and irreversible measurement that parallel the black-hole information problem.
