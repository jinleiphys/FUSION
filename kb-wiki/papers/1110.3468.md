# Method to solve integral equations of the first kind with an approximate input

> Techniques are proposed for solving integral equations of the first kind with an input known not precisely. The requirement that the solution sought for includes a given number of maxima and minima is imposed. It is shown that when the deviation of the approximate input from the true one is sufficiently small and some additional conditions are fulfilled the method leads to an approximate solution that is necessarily close to the true solution. No regularization is required in the present approach. Requirements on features of the solution at integration limits are also imposed. The problem is treated with the help of an ansatz proposed for the derivative of the solution. The ansatz is the most general one compatible with the above mentioned requirements. The techniques are tested with exactly solvable examples. Inversions of the Lorentz, Stieltjes and Laplace integral transforms are performed, and very satisfactory results are obtained. The method is useful, in particular, for the calculation of quantum-mechanical reaction amplitudes and inclusive spectra of perturbation-induced reactions in the framework of the integral transform approach.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a method for solving integral equations of the first kind when the input is known only approximately, by imposing the requirement that the solution has a prescribed number of maxima and minima; the method is shown to converge to the true solution when the deviation of the approximate input from the true one is sufficiently small and additional conditions are fulfilled, and it requires no regularization.

## Method

The integral equation is rewritten in terms of the derivative of the solution, and an ansatz for the derivative is constructed that is the most general form compatible with a given number of maxima and minima, known threshold behavior, and power-law decrease at infinity. The fitting parameters in the ansatz are determined by a least-squares fit of the transform of the approximate solution to the approximate input, using a norm that minimizes the average square of the relative deviation. The method is tested by inverting Lorentz, Stieltjes, and Laplace integral transforms using exactly solvable examples.

## Key numbers

- For the Lorentz transform with exact input and the new method using two fitting parameters, the solution accuracy (average relative deviation) was \(1.6 \times 10^{-8}\) at \(\sigma_I = 10\) MeV, \(1.6 \times 10^{-8}\) at \(\sigma_I = 2\) MeV, and \(3.3 \times 10^{-7}\) at \(\sigma_I = 100\) MeV.
- For the standard inversion method (expansion in basis functions) with exact input and \(\sigma_I = 10\) MeV, the best solution accuracy was \(1.3 \times 10^{-2}\) at \(N=9\) basis functions, with fit quality \(6.2 \times 10^{-6}\).
- The exact solution used for testing was \(f(E) = \frac{4}{\pi E_0} \frac{\sqrt{E/E_0}}{[(E/E_0)+1]^4}\) with \(E_0 = 20.7212603615\) MeV, derived from a one-particle free-motion Hamiltonian with orbital momentum zero and a source \(Q(r) = (1/\sqrt{4\pi}) e^{-\eta r}\) with \(\eta = 1\) fm\(^{-1}\).
- The sum rule value for this test case is \(1/(4\eta^3)\).
- The input range for the Lorentz transform was \(-2 \le \sigma_R \le 41.4\) MeV.

## Context

This work extends the integral transform approach for calculating quantum-mechanical reaction amplitudes and inclusive spectra, previously developed by Efros (1985, 1980) and others, where regularization via truncation of basis expansions (e.g., the "standard inversion" using basis functions like \(E^{n-1/2}e^{-\alpha E}\)) was always used. The new method avoids regularization entirely and achieves much higher accuracy, as demonstrated by comparisons with the standard inversion approach of Efros et al. (2007). The technique is designed for applications in nuclear-physics few-body calculations where the input accuracy is typically at the percent level, and it enables more reliable inversion of Lorentz, Stieltjes, and Laplace transforms for reaction observables.
