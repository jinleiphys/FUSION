# Quantum Fluctuations Drive Angular Momenta in Nuclear Fission

> Quantum fluctuations are ubiquitous and play crucial roles across various scales and systems, such as the Big Bang, black hole dynamics, quantum phase transitions in microscopic many-body systems, and so on. Nuclear fission manifests as a complex nuclear shape stretching until it splits into fragments with substantial angular momenta, also exhibiting complex quantum fluctuations and specifically shape fluctuations. For over 40 years, researchers have puzzled how the fission fragment angular momenta are generated dynamically from (almost) zero spin, as well as the particular role played by quantum fluctuations. Here, for the first time, we report the quantum shape fluctuations that drive fragment angular momenta during nuclear fission, based on a global, microscopic, and dynamical simulation. The calculated probability distributions of fragment angular momenta are in good agreement with the experimental measurements, and the sawtooth-like mass dependence of average angular momenta is reproduced very well. It is noteworthy to find that the shape fluctuations -- multiple rotations, vibrations, and their couplings -- drive the generation and chaotic evolution of fragment angular momenta during fission fragment formation and induce strong correlations between angular momentum orientations of partner fragments at small, medium, and large opening angles ($ϕ_{LH}\approx 30^\circ, 90^\circ, 160^\circ$). Our work not only deepens the fundamental understanding of the nuclear fission mechanism but also has implications for the $γ$-ray heating problem in nuclear reactors and the synthesis of superheavy elements.

## Key claim

The authors report that quantum shape fluctuations, specifically multiple rotations, vibrations, and their couplings, drive the generation and chaotic evolution of fission fragment angular momenta during fragment formation, based on a global, microscopic, and dynamical simulation for the first time. The claim is hedged by the fact that the calculation is performed for a single compound nucleus (²⁴⁰Pu) along a reduced one-dimensional collective path, and the quadrupole-quadrupole interaction strength is tuned to reproduce experimental data for a specific fragment pair.

## Method

The authors constructed a time-dependent collective Hamiltonian that includes collective shape vibrations, rotations of the two forming fragments, their relative orbital angular momentum, and quadrupole-quadrupole couplings between them. They solved the resulting time-dependent coupled-channel equation using a finite-difference method and Crank-Nicolson time integration, evolving a collective wave function from the equilibrium state of ²⁴⁰Pu to a fragment separation distance beyond 23 fm. The collective parameters (potential energy surfaces, collective masses, moments of inertia, and quadrupole deformations) were calculated microscopically using constrained covariant density functional theory (CDFT) with the PC-PK1 functional, and the Inglis-Beliaev formula was used for fragment moments of inertia.

## Key numbers

- The quadrupole-quadrupole interaction strength was set to c = 0.11 MeV/b², determined by reproducing experimental angular momentum distributions for a certain fragment pair.
- Angular momenta were truncated at 36 ħ for the heavy fragment (S_H) and 40 ħ for the light fragment (S_L) and relative orbital angular momentum (S_Λ).
- The calculation was performed for three fission channels of ²⁴⁰Pu: ¹³⁴Te+¹⁰⁶Mo, ¹⁴⁰Xe+¹⁰⁰Zr, and ¹⁵⁰Ce+⁹⁰Kr.
- The peak of the heavy fragment angular momentum distribution moved from 2 ħ (¹³⁴Te) to 10 ħ (¹⁵⁰Ce) as mass asymmetry increased.
- Strong correlations between fragment angular momentum orientations were found at opening angles φ_LH ≈ 30°, 90°, and 160°.
- The fragment separation distance for full decoupling was set to d_c.m.^(FFs) > 23 fm.

## Context

This work extends the experimental measurements of Wilson et al. (2021), who observed a sawtooth-like mass dependence of fission fragment angular momenta with high precision across three fission reactions. It goes beyond previous theoretical approaches such as statistical models (Randrup 2014, Vogt 2017, Randrup 2021), microscopic angular momentum projection (Bulgac 2021, Bulgac 2022), and collective Hamiltonian methods (Scamps 2023) by providing a fully dynamical simulation that tracks the generation and evolution of angular momenta from equilibrium to large fragment separation. The results enable future experimental studies of γ-γ correlations from fission fragments to quantitatively test the predicted anisotropic correlations of fragment angular momentum orientations, and have implications for the γ-ray heating problem in nuclear reactors and superheavy element synthesis.
