# Role of BCS-type pairing in light deformed nuclei: A relativistic mean field approach

> We calculate the binding energy and deformation parameter for light nuclei with and without pairing using a deformed relativistic mean field model. The role of BCS-type pairing effect is analyzed for Ne and F isotopes. The calculated odd-even staggering and the deformation parameters argue strongly against the role of pairing in the light nuclei.

## Key claim

The paper argues that BCS-type pairing correlations are not the cause of odd-even staggering (OES) in light deformed nuclei, and that OES in these systems is instead a mean-field (Jahn-Teller) effect. The authors qualify this by noting that their calculations do not carefully include time-reversal symmetry breaking for odd nucleons, but they argue this approximation is sufficient for bulk properties like binding energy and quadrupole moment.

## Method

The authors performed axially deformed relativistic mean field (RMF) calculations using the NL3 parameter set for Ne and F isotopes, expanding Dirac spinors and boson fields in a deformed harmonic oscillator basis with 12 oscillator shells. They computed binding energies and quadrupole deformation parameters both with and without BCS-type pairing in the constant gap approximation, using experimental or extrapolated binding energies to determine the pairing gaps, or the Bohr-Mottelson formula \(\Delta \approx 12/A^{1/2}\) MeV when data were unavailable. The odd nucleon in odd-even and odd-odd nuclei was treated in a time-reversal symmetric mean-field approximation by averaging over \(\pm m\) configurations.

## Key numbers

- The pairing energy for a nuclear system of mass number \(A=160\) is about \(-0.8\) MeV (from Bohr and Mottelson, cited as context for heavy nuclei).
- The constant gap approximation uses the formula \(\Delta \approx 12/A^{1/2}\) MeV when experimental data are unavailable.
- For Ne isotopes, the one-neutron separation energy \(S_n\) fits well with pairing only for \(A=19\) and \(A=20\); for all other isotopes the results without pairing are closer to experiment.
- For F isotopes, the inclusion of pairing gives severe overbinding compared to experimental data.
- The quadrupole deformation parameter \(\beta_2\) values without pairing give a better fit to experimental measurements for known cases, while RMF calculations predict very small \(\beta_2\) in the presence of pairing.
- For \(^{27,28,29}\)F, the quadrupole deformation is almost identical in both with and without pairing cases.

## Context

This work extends the debate between Hakkinen et al. (who argued OES in light nuclei is a deformation effect) and Satula et al. (who claimed OES is strongly affected by both pairing and deformed mean field). The authors' RMF calculations support the Hakkinen et al. position, showing that BCS-type pairing does not improve agreement with experimental binding energies, separation energies, or deformation parameters for Ne and F isotopes. The paper enables future work to focus on mean-field (Jahn-Teller) explanations for OES in light nuclei rather than BCS or HFB pairing models, and suggests that zero pairing strength is preferred in the light and light-medium nuclei region.
