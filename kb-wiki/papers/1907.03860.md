# Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular Dynamics (PHQMD) -- A Novel Microscopic N-Body Transport Approach for Heavy-Ion Collisions, Dynamical Cluster Formation and Hypernuclei Production

> Cluster and hypernuclei production in heavy-ion collisions is presently under active experimental and theoretical investigation. Since clusters are weekly bound objects, their production is very sensitive to the dynamical evolution of the system and its interactions. The theoretical description of cluster formation is related to the n-body problem. Here we present the novel n-body dynamical transport approach PHQMD (Parton-Hadron-Quantum-Molecular Dynamics) which is designed to provide a microscopic description of nuclear cluster and hypernucleus formation as well as of general particle production in heavy-ion reactions at relativistic energies. In difference to the coalescence or statistical models, often used for the cluster formation, in PHQMD clusters are formed dynamically due to the interactions between baryons described on a basis of Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD)which allows to propagate the n-body Wigner density and n-body correlations in phase-space, essential for the cluster formation. The clusters are identified by the MST (Minimum Spanning Tree) or the SACA ('Simulated Annealing Cluster Algorithm') algorithm which finds the most bound configuration of nucleons and clusters. Collisions among hadrons as well as Quark-Gluon-Plasma formation and parton dynamics in PHQMD are treated in the same way as in the established PHSD (Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics)transport approach. In order to verify our approach with respect to the general dynamics we present here the first PHQMD results for general 'bulk' observables such as rapidity distributions and transverse mass spectra for hadrons ($π, K, \bar K, p, \bar p, Λ, \bar Λ$) from SIS to RHIC energies. We find a good description of the 'bulk' dynamics which allows us to proceed with the results on cluster production, including hypernuclei.

## Key claim

PHQMD provides a fully microscopic n-body dynamical description of cluster and hypernucleus formation in heavy-ion collisions, where clusters are formed dynamically through baryon interactions rather than imposed via coalescence or statistical models; the authors present first results for "bulk" observables (rapidity distributions and transverse mass spectra) from SIS to RHIC energies to validate the general dynamics before proceeding to cluster production.

## Method

The PHQMD approach combines Quantum Molecular Dynamics (QMD) propagation of baryons, using density-dependent two-body potentials that conserve n-body phase-space correlations, with the collision integral and partonic dynamics of the established Parton-Hadron-String Dynamics (PHSD) approach, which handles hadron collisions, Quark-Gluon-Plasma formation, and hadronization. Clusters are identified at the late stage by either the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) algorithm based on spatial correlations or the Simulated Annealing Cluster Algorithm (SACA), which finds the most bound configuration of nucleons and clusters using the Weizsäcker mass formula.

## Key numbers

- Gaussian width parameter \(L = 2.16\) fm\(^2\) used for the single-particle Wigner density of nucleons.
- Wood-Saxon parameters for Au nuclei: \(r_0 = 1.125\) fm, \(\rho_0 = 0.1695\) fm\(^{-2}\), \(a = 0.535\) fm.
- Critical energy density for deconfinement: \(\epsilon_c \sim 0.5\) GeV/fm\(^3\).
- String tension \(\kappa \approx 0.176\) GeV\(^2\) used in the Schwinger formula for strange quark suppression; default strangeness suppression factor \(\gamma_s \approx 0.3\).
- Formation time of prehadronic states: \(\tau_f \sim 0.8\) fm/c in the string rest frame.
- In central Au+Au collisions at 1.5 \(A\)GeV, only 65% of the total baryon charge is observed as protons (FOPI Collaboration), the rest being bound in light clusters.

## Context

This work extends the PHSD transport approach (Cassing 2008, 2009; Bratkovskaya 2011; Linnyk 2015) by replacing its mean-field baryon propagation with QMD dynamics (Aichelin 1991, 1987, 1988; Hartnack 1997), enabling the propagation of n-body Wigner densities and correlations essential for cluster formation. It contrasts with coalescence models (Zhu 2015; Feckova 2016) and statistical fragmentation models (Botvina; Andronic 2010), which impose cluster formation at a fixed time or assume thermal equilibrium, and instead allows clusters to emerge dynamically from the same potential interactions that govern the entire time evolution. The approach enables future studies of cluster and hypernuclei production at FAIR and NICA energies, as well as investigation of the phase transition between hadronic matter and QGP at high baryon densities.
