# Effects of the higher partial waves and relativistic terms on the accuracy of the calculation of the hypertriton electroproduction

> We have investigated the accuracies of calculations made by omitting the higher partial waves of nuclear wave functions and the elementary relativistic terms in the hypertriton electroproduction. We found that an accurate calculation would still be obtained if we used at least three lowest partial waves with isospin T = 0. Furthermore, we found that the omission of the relativistic terms in the elementary process amplitude could lead to a large deviation from the full calculation. We also present the cpu-times required to calculate the cross sections. For future consideration the use of these lowest partial waves is suggested, since the calculated cross section deviates only about 0.17 nb/sr (approximately 4%), at most, from the full calculation, whereas the cpu-time is reduced by a factor of 60. Comparison of our result with the available experimental data supports these findings.

## Key claim

An accurate calculation of hypertriton electroproduction cross sections can be obtained using only the three lowest partial waves with isospin T=0 (α ≤ 4), with a maximum deviation of about 0.17 nb/sr (approximately 4%) from the full calculation, while reducing CPU-time by a factor of about 60; however, omitting the relativistic terms in the elementary process amplitude leads to large deviations.

## Method

The differential cross section for the reaction e + ³He → e' + K⁺ + ³_ΛH was calculated using an impulse approximation with the elementary operator Kaon-Maid sandwiched between ³He and hypertriton wave functions obtained from Faddeev equation solutions. The full calculation used 34 partial waves for ³He and 16 for hypertriton, requiring integration over approximately 156 million grid points after applying selection rules. The effects of truncating the partial wave expansion and of omitting the relativistic "small-small" (SS) and "small-big" (SB) terms from the elementary amplitude were systematically compared to the full calculation at kinematics with k² = -0.35 GeV² and ε = 0.762.

## Key numbers

- Using partial waves with α ≤ 4 (three lowest T=0 partial waves): maximum deviation from full calculation is 0.17 nb/sr (less than 4%), average deviation 0.038 nb/sr, CPU-time reduced by factor of about 60 (from 15,344 min to 294 min for 961 points)
- Using partial waves with α ≤ 5: maximum deviation 0.15 nb/sr (less than 3%), average deviation 0.019 nb/sr, CPU-time 511 min
- Using only s-waves (α=2,4): maximum deviation almost 1 nb/sr at the highest cross section peak, CPU-time 46 min (factor of 300 reduction)
- Excluding SS-terms (using F₁-F₁₅): maximum deviation 0.10 nb/sr, CPU-time 498 min
- Excluding SB-terms (using F₁-F₅): maximum deviation 8.03 nb/sr, CPU-time 487 min
- Using only F₁ (Kroll-Ruderman term): maximum deviation 4.97 nb/sr, CPU-time 485 min
- Full calculation CPU-time: 15,344 min (approximately 11 days) on a single processor Pentium-4 PC for 961 points
- Kinematic range: total c.m. energy W from 3.5 GeV to 5 GeV, kaon angle θ_K from 0° to 31°

## Context

This work extends the analysis of hypertriton photoproduction by Mart (2008), which showed that four lowest partial waves (α ≤ 4) approximate the full calculation for photoproduction at W=4.04 GeV. The present study investigates the electroproduction case across a wider kinematic range and also examines the role of relativistic terms in the elementary amplitude, corroborating the finding of Adelseck (1986) that excluding SB-terms or using an extreme nonrelativistic approach is not a good approximation. The results enable future fitting procedures to extract elementary amplitude information from nuclear cross sections, which would be computationally prohibitive with the full calculation, and suggest that new experimental data with approximately 10% error bars could clarify the validity of these approximations.
