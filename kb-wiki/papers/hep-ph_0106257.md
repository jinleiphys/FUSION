# Langevin Evolution of Disoriented Chiral Condensate

> As the matter produced in a relativistic heavy ion collision cools through the QCD phase transition, the dynamical evolution of the chiral condensate will be driven out of thermal equilibrium. As a prelude to analyzing this evolution, and in particular as a prelude to learning how rapid the cooling must be in order for significant deviations from equilibrium to develop, we present a detailed analysis of the time-evolution of an idealized region of disoriented chiral condensate. We set up a Langevin field equation which can describe the evolution of these (or more realistic) linear sigma model configurations in contact with a heat bath representing the presence of other shorter wavelength degrees of freedom. We first analyze the model in equilibrium, paying particular attention to subtracting ultraviolet divergent classical terms and replacing them by their finite quantum counterparts. We use known results from lattice gauge theory and chiral perturbation theory to fix nonuniversal constants. The result is a theory which is ultraviolet cutoff independent and that reproduces quantitatively the expected equilibrium behavior of the quantum field theory of pions and sigma fields over a wide range of temperatures. Finally, we estimate the viscosity $η(T)$, which controls the dynamical timescale in the Langevin equation, by requiring that the timescale for DCC decay agrees with previous calculations. The resulting $η(T)$ is larger than that found perturbatively. We also determine the temperature below which the classical field Langevin equation ceases to be a good model for the quantum field dynamics.

## Key claim

The authors construct a Langevin field equation for the linear sigma model that can describe the nonequilibrium evolution of disoriented chiral condensate (DCC) in contact with a heat bath, but they note that the classical treatment is applied beyond its regime of quantitative validity because the physical pion mass at zero temperature means that $m_{\sigma,\pi}$ are not much smaller than the temperature near $T_c$.

## Method

The authors set up a $3+1$-dimensional Langevin equation for the $O(4)$ linear sigma model fields (pions and sigma) with additive stochastic noise and dissipation, where the heat bath represents shorter wavelength degrees of freedom. They subtract ultraviolet-divergent classical tadpole and sunset diagrams and replace them with their finite quantum counterparts, then fix finite counterterms by requiring agreement with chiral perturbation theory at low temperature and with lattice QCD calculations of the critical temperature. The viscosity $\eta(T)$ is determined by requiring that the decay timescale of a spatially uniform DCC perturbation matches the calculation of Steele and Koch (2000), which uses zero-temperature scattering amplitudes and Bose-Einstein distributions.

## Key numbers

- The linear sigma model parameters are set to $(f_\pi, m_\pi, m_\sigma) = (92.4, 135, 600)$ MeV, giving $H = (119\ \mathrm{MeV})^3$, $\lambda = 20.0$, and $v = 87.3\ \mathrm{MeV}$.
- The critical temperature for $H=0$ is pushed to $T_c \simeq 150$ MeV by adding finite counterterms $b_1 = 0.425$ and $b_2 = -0.066$ (in dimensionless units on a $64^3$ lattice with $\ell_s = 0.4$).
- The crossover temperature (where $m_\sigma$ is minimal) is $T_{\mathrm{cross}} \simeq 180$ MeV; at this temperature $m_\pi \simeq 220$ MeV and $m_\sigma \simeq 280$ MeV.
- The viscosity $\eta(T)$ required to reproduce the DCC decay timescale is about a factor of five larger than the perturbative prediction of Rischke (1998).

## Context

This work extends the purely classical analysis of DCC evolution by Rajagopal and Wilczek (1993) and by Asakawa, Huang, and Wang (1995) by introducing a Langevin heat bath that allows the long-wavelength modes to exchange energy with other degrees of freedom. It complements the perturbative Langevin derivations of Greiner and Muller (1997) and Rischke (1998) by working in $3+1$ dimensions with spatially varying configurations, and improves upon the lattice treatment of Chaudhuri (2000) by introducing temperature-dependent counterterms that make results independent of lattice spacing. The resulting framework is intended to answer how rapid the cooling must be in a heavy ion collision for significant deviations from equilibrium (such as DCC formation) to develop, which the authors leave to a subsequent paper.
