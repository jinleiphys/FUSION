# Relativistic diffusion and heavy-ion collisions

> We study first and second order theories of relativistic diffusion coupled to hydrodynamics under the approximation, valid at mid-rapidity in the RHIC and LHC, that conserved number densities are much smaller than the entropy density. We identify experimentally accessible quantities of interest, and show that the first and second order theories may lead to radically different evolutions of these quantities. In the first order theory the memory of the initial state is almost completely washed out, whereas in the second order theory it is possible that freezeout occurs at a time when transient dynamics is still on, and the memory of the initial state remains. There are observational consequences which we touch upon. In the first order theory, and for initial conditions when the second order theory mimics the first order, one may be able to put a bound on the diffusion constant.

## Key claim

First-order (Fick) and second-order (Kelly) relativistic diffusion theories coupled to longitudinal hydrodynamics lead to radically different evolutions of the Fourier coefficients of conserved number densities at mid-rapidity, with the key qualifier that this analysis is performed under the approximation that conserved number densities are much smaller than the entropy density (valid at RHIC and LHC). In the first-order theory the memory of the initial state is almost completely washed out, whereas in the second-order theory freezeout may occur while transient dynamics is still active, preserving memory of the initial state.

## Method

The authors study the coupled evolution of conserved number densities (baryon number, electric charge, strangeness) with boost-invariant longitudinal hydrodynamic flow, linearizing the equations in the small parameter mu/T. They derive and solve the relativistic diffusion equation for first-order (Fick) theory and the second-order (Kelly) theory with a relaxation time tau_R, performing Fourier transforms in space-time rapidity eta to obtain evolution equations for the Fourier modes n(tau,k) and the dissipative part nu(tau,k). The evolution of the power spectrum P(tau,k) = |n(tau,k)|^2 and the event-to-event distribution of the observable power spectrum at freezeout are compared between the two theories using Monte Carlo simulations with random initial conditions.

## Key numbers

- The dimensionless quantity comparing diffusion to flow is W = lambda^2/(tau D) = tau/D sinh^2(Delta eta); when W << 1 diffusive effects dominate over flow
- In the high-temperature phase, D T = 1/(2 pi) from AdS/CFT computations, with T proportional to 1/tau^{(1+c_s^2)/4}
- The time when flow dominates over diffusion is tau_fl = D / sinh^2(Delta eta)
- For the second-order theory, the relaxation time tau_R could be of order 1 fm or as small as 0.1 fm in AdS/QCD scenarios
- Transient growth occurs at tau approximately e tau_R, and at tau approximately e^2 tau_R the evolution still does not resemble Bjorken attenuation
- If tau_R is in the range 1/2 to 2 fm, number density evolution may be dominated by transients
- In the Kelly theory, structure in the number density profile at scales of Delta eta approximately 1 persists even at times tau approximately 7.5 tau_R
- The upper bound on the diffusion constant from first-order theory is D <= tau_f sinh^2(Delta eta)

## Context

This work extends the study of relativistic diffusion in heavy-ion collisions beyond previous work on heavy quark diffusion using weak-coupling theory, the Boltzmann equation, AdS/CFT techniques, and Langevin dynamics (various authors, various years). It also builds on earlier diffusive theories for stopping and early-time entropy production (Wolschin, various years). The paper enables experimental distinction between first-order (Fick) and second-order (Kelly) diffusion theories through event-to-event distributions of the power spectrum of conserved charges, which is essential for interpreting fluctuation signals of the QCD critical point. The authors note that the widely studied event-to-event fluctuations of conserved quantities within a rapidity acceptance window could be strongly influenced by whether the transport process is first or second order, and that understanding the nature of diffusion must be improved before such fluctuations can be interpreted.
