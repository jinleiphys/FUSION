# Charge radii of exotic potassium isotopes challenge nuclear theory and the magic character of $N = 32$

> Nuclear charge radii are sensitive probes of different aspects of the nucleon-nucleon interaction and the bulk properties of nuclear matter; thus, they provide a stringent test and challenge for nuclear theory. The calcium region has been of particular interest, as experimental evidence has suggested a new magic number at $N = 32$ [1-3], while the unexpectedly large increases in the charge radii [4,5] open new questions about the evolution of nuclear size in neutron-rich systems. By combining the collinear resonance ionization spectroscopy method with $β$-decay detection, we were able to extend the charge radii measurement of potassium ($Z =19$) isotopes up to the exotic $^{52}$K ($t_{1/2}$ = 110 ms), produced in minute quantities. Our work provides the first charge radii measurement beyond $N = 32$ in the region, revealing no signature of the magic character at this neutron number. The results are interpreted with two state-of-the-art nuclear theories. For the first time, a long sequence of isotopes could be calculated with coupled-cluster calculations based on newly developed nuclear interactions. The strong increase in the charge radii beyond $N = 28$ is not well captured by these calculations, but is well reproduced by Fayans nuclear density functional theory, which, however, overestimates the odd-even staggering effect. These findings highlight our limited understanding on the nuclear size of neutron-rich systems, and expose pressing problems that are present in some of the best current models of nuclear theory.

## Key claim

The first measurement of the nuclear charge radius of \(^{52}\)K (\(N=33\)) reveals no signature of the proposed magic character at \(N=32\), contradicting expectations from binding-energy and excitation-energy systematics; however, the authors note that the spin assignment of \(^{52}\)K is tentative, and the conclusion of non-magicity depends on the assumed \(I=2\) assignment, as alternative spin assumptions (\(I=1\) or \(I=3\)) produce unrealistically small or large radii.

## Method

The collinear resonance ionization spectroscopy (CRIS) method was combined with beta-decay detection at the ISOLDE facility at CERN to measure the hyperfine structure of \(^{47-52}\)K isotopes. For the most exotic isotope \(^{52}\)K, which had a half-life of 110 ms and was produced with a beam purity ratio of \(2\times10^4\) contamination to signal, a plastic scintillator detector system was used to count beta particles from the decay of \(^{52}\)K, enabling background-free detection. The isotope shifts were extracted from the hyperfine spectra, and the changes in mean-square charge radii were calculated using atomic field shift and specific mass shift factors obtained from the newly developed analytic response relativistic coupled-cluster (ARRCC) theory.

## Key numbers

*   Charge radius of \(^{52}\)K: \(R_{\rm ch} = 3.549(7)[19]\) fm (for the adopted \(I^\pi=(2^{-})\) assignment).
*   Change in mean-square charge radius from \(^{39}\)K to \(^{52}\)K: \(\delta\langle r^2\rangle^{39,52} = 0.79(5)[132]\) fm\(^2\).
*   Atomic field shift factor: \(F = -107.2(5)\) MHz fm\(^{-2}\), calculated with the ARRCC method.
*   Specific mass shift factor: \(K_{\rm SMS} = -14.0(22)\) GHz u, calculated with the ARRCC method.
*   Half-life of \(^{52}\)K: \(t_{1/2} = 110\) ms.
*   Isobaric contamination at mass \(A=52\) was measured to be \(2\times10^4\) times more intense than the \(^{52}\)K beam.

## Context

This work extends the charge radii measurements of potassium isotopes beyond \(N=32\) for the first time in the calcium region, following earlier measurements of potassium charge radii up to \(N=32\) by Kreim et al. (2014) and calcium charge radii by Garcia Ruiz et al. (2016). The results challenge the proposed magic character of \(N=32\) that was suggested by binding-energy measurements from Wienholtz et al. (2013) and excitation-energy data from Huck et al. (1985). The data are compared with two state-of-the-art nuclear theories: coupled-cluster calculations using the newly developed \(\Delta\)NNLO\(_{\rm GO}(450)\) interaction (which improves on the NNLO\(_{\rm sat}\) interaction used by Hagen et al. (2016)) and Fayans nuclear density functional theory (Fy(\(\Delta\)r,HFB) functional). The failure of the coupled-cluster calculations to reproduce the steep increase beyond \(N=28\) and the overestimation of odd-even staggering by Fayans-DFT highlight open problems in current nuclear models and will motivate further theoretical developments.
