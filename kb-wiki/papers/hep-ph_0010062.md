# The Coulomb Phase Revisited

> Motivated by the forthcoming data from the E950 experiment at BNL for small angle polarized proton-carbon scattering we revisit the problem of Coulomb phase. The approximation usually used with the momentum transfer squared (q^2) small compared to the inverse elastic slope is not justified within the kinematics of the E950 experiment. We go beyond this approximation and derive a new rather simple expression which recovers the formula of Bethe at small q^2, but is valid at any momentum transfer.

## Key claim
The paper derives a new analytic expression for the Coulomb phase that is valid at any momentum transfer \(q^2\), provided the electromagnetic formfactor and hadronic amplitude have Gaussian \(q\)-dependence; this goes beyond the usual approximation that \(q^2\) is small compared to the inverse elastic slope, which is not justified for the kinematics of the E950 experiment.

## Method
The authors start from the eikonal representation of the elastic scattering amplitude, separating pure Coulomb, nuclear, and Coulomb-nuclear interference terms. They compute the Coulomb phase \(\Phi_C(q)\) and the nuclear-Coulomb phase \(\Phi_{NC}(q)\) to first order in \(\alpha Z_1Z_2\), assuming Gaussian formfactors for both the electromagnetic and hadronic amplitudes. The resulting integrals are evaluated analytically using exponential integral functions, yielding closed-form expressions for the phase difference \(\Delta\Phi = \Phi_C - \Phi_{NC}\).

## Key numbers
- The E950 experiment covers momentum transfer up to \(q^2 = 0.05\ \text{GeV}^2\), where the product \(B q^2 = 3\) for an elastic slope \(B \approx 60\ \text{GeV}^{-2}\).
- For a proton target, the electromagnetic slope parameter is \(a = 4/\Lambda^2 = 5.6\ \text{GeV}^{-2}\) with \(\Lambda^2 = 0.71\ \text{GeV}^2\).
- For proton-carbon scattering, the calculation uses \(a = 30\ \text{GeV}^{-2}\).
- The relative inelastic correction to the Coulomb phase is estimated to be about \(10^{-2}\).

## Context
This work revisits the Coulomb phase problem originally formulated by Bethe (1958) and later extended by Cahn (1982) with next-to-leading corrections of order \(q^2 \ln(q^2)\). The authors show that at the higher \(q^2\) values probed by the E950 experiment at BNL, next-to-next-to-leading order terms become important, and the earlier approximations are no longer valid. The new expression enables accurate extraction of the hadronic spin-flip amplitude from polarized proton-carbon scattering data, particularly the Coulomb-nuclear interference asymmetry.
