# The Influence of Beta Decay Rates on r-Process Observables

> The rapid neutron capture process (r-process) is one of the main mechanisms whereby elements heavier than iron are synthesized, and is entirely responsible for the natural production of the actinides. Kilonova emissions are modeled as being largely powered by the radioactive decay of species synthesized via the r -process. Given that the r -process occurs far from nuclear stability, unmeasured beta decay rates play an essential role in setting the time scale for the r -process. In an effort to better understand the sensitivity of kilonova modeling to different theoretical global beta-decay descriptions, we incorporate these into nucleosynthesis calculations. We compare the results of these calculations and highlight differences in kilonova nuclear energy generation and light curve predictions, as well as final abundances and their implications for nuclear cosmochronometry. We investigate scenarios where differences in beta decay rates are responsible for increased nuclear heating on time scales of days that propagates into a significantly increased average bolometric luminosity between 1-10 days post-merger. We identify key nuclei, both measured and unmeasured, whose decay rates are directly impact nuclear heating generation on timescales responsible for light curve evolution. We also find that uncertainties in beta decay rates significantly impact ages estimates from cosmochronometry.

# The Influence of Beta Decay Rates on r-Process Observables

## Key claim

The choice of theoretical beta decay rate model indirectly but strongly influences kilonova light curves and nuclear heating, primarily by altering the populations of nuclei that later undergo alpha decay and spontaneous fission, rather than by directly changing the beta decay heating contribution itself. The effect is most pronounced in low electron fraction ejecta where fission cycling occurs, with variations in total heating spanning up to a factor of 6.6 between different beta decay rate sets for certain mass models.

## Method

The authors performed nucleosynthesis calculations using the PRISM network code with three different global beta decay rate models: NES (finite amplitude method with Skyrme functionals), MKT (covariant density functional theory with Gogny interactions), and MLR (finite range droplet model with quasiparticle random phase approximation). They combined these with eight different nuclear mass models and corresponding fission barrier descriptions, using single-electron-fraction trajectories at Ye values of 0.02, 0.18, and 0.21, as well as combined multi-trajectory distributions. Effective heating rates were computed by coupling decay Q-values with thermalization efficiencies, and bolometric light curves were generated using a semi-analytic model with 100 velocity layers from 0.1c to 0.4c, following the method of Zhu (2021) and Barnes (2021).

## Key numbers

- For the SLY4 mass model at Ye=0.18, the NES beta decay rates produced an average heating ratio of 1.797 relative to MLR rates over 1-10 days, with a luminosity ratio of 1.642
- For the HFB22 mass model at Ye=0.02, the NES rates produced an average heating ratio of 6.591 relative to MLR rates over 1-10 days, with a luminosity ratio of 2.806
- For the HFB27 mass model at Ye=0.02, the MKT rates produced an average heating ratio of 0.260 relative to MLR rates over 1-10 days, with a luminosity ratio of 0.348
- For the ETFSI mass model at Ye=0.18, the MKT rates produced an average heating ratio of 0.333 relative to MLR rates over 1-10 days, with a luminosity ratio of 0.499
- For the Ye=0.21 single trajectory, the total heating variation across all beta decay rate sets spanned only about a factor of 2 at 1 day
- The semi-analytic light curve model used an ejecta mass of 0.05 solar masses, an ejecta velocity of 0.15c, and a maximum opacity of 100 cm^2/g for lanthanide-bearing ejecta
- Cosmochronometry calculations used equations relating observed Th/Eu, U/Eu, and U/Th ratios to ages, with coefficients of 46.67 Gyr, 14.84 Gyr, and 21.80 Gyr respectively

## Context

This work extends the sensitivity studies of Zhu (2021) and Barnes (2021), who found that spontaneous fission and alpha decay rates introduce up to an order of magnitude uncertainty in nuclear heating on light curve timescales. The authors demonstrate that beta decay rate uncertainties propagate indirectly through their influence on the populations of alpha-decaying nuclei such as Ra-224 and Rn-222 (with half-lives of 3.6 and 3.8 days respectively) and their daughter polonium isotopes Po-212, Po-214, and Po-216. The results enable identification of specific unmeasured nuclei whose beta decay rates most strongly impact kilonova observables, and show that cosmochronometric age estimates for r-process-enhanced metal-poor stars are significantly affected by beta decay rate uncertainties. The study uses six r-II stars including CS22892-052, HE1523-0901, CS29497-004, CS31082-001, J2038-0023, and J0954+5246, with observed europium, thorium, and uranium abundances from Sneden (2003), Frebel (2007), Hill (2017), and others.
