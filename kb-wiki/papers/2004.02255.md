# Brief history of the search for critical structures in heavy-ion collisions

> The paper briefly presents history, status, and plans of the search for the critical structures - the onset of fireball, the onset of deconfinement, and the deconfinement critical point - in high energy nucleus-nucleus collisions. First, the basic ideas are introduced, the history of the observation of strongly interacting matter in heavy ion collisions is reviewed, and the path towards the quark-gluon plasma discovery is sketched. Then the status of the search for critical structures is discussed - the discovery of the onset of deconfinement, indications for the onset of fireball, and still inconclusive results concerning the deconfinement critical point. Finally, an attempt to formulate priorities for future measurements - charm quarks vs the onset of deconfinement and detailed study of the onset of fireball - closes the paper.

## Key claim

The paper argues that while the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) was claimed as discovered at CERN in 2000 based on signals such as J/ψ suppression and strangeness enhancement, unambiguous evidence remains missing because these signals can also be explained by non-QGP processes; the authors therefore advocate that the clearest path to confirmation is to search for the *onset* of deconfinement, the energy threshold where QGP creation begins, rather than relying on single-signature interpretations.

## Method

The authors review the historical development of heavy-ion collision experiments from the 1970s onward, focusing on measurements at the CERN SPS (NA35, NA49, NA50, NA57, NA60, WA98, and others) and the AGS. They compare particle yields (pions, kaons, hyperons, J/ψ, dileptons, photons) across collision energies and system sizes, and confront these data with predictions from the statistical hadron-resonance gas model, the Hagedorn model, and QCD-based expectations for the QGP transition. The search for critical structures, onset of fireball, onset of deconfinement, and the deconfinement critical point, is presented as a program of energy scans at fixed-target facilities.

## Key numbers

* Energy density above 1 GeV/fm³ was reached in S+A collisions at the top CERN SPS energy (200 AGeV), as measured by NA34 and NA35.
* The Hagedorn limiting temperature was estimated as T_H = 150–160 MeV from the hadron mass spectrum and from inverse slope parameters of transverse momentum spectra.
* The chemical freeze-out temperature from fits to hadron multiplicities in A+A collisions is T_ch = 140–160 MeV, close to T_H.
* The QGP transition temperature from recent QCD-based estimates is T_C ≈ 150 MeV, consistent with T_H.
* The strangeness-to-pion double ratio R in central S+S collisions at 200 AGeV is about two times higher than in nucleon-nucleon interactions at the same energy per nucleon.
* At the AGS at 2–10 AGeV, the strangeness enhancement factor R reached values of 14–5, larger than at the CERN SPS.
* The effective temperature of directly produced dimuons and photons in central Pb+Pb collisions at 158 AGeV is about 200 MeV, above the expected transition temperature.
* The shear viscosity to entropy density ratio of the QGP was estimated at RHIC as η/s ≈ 0.1.

## Context

This paper reviews and extends the line of work initiated by the CERN SPS heavy-ion program (NA34–NA60, WA80–WA98) and the subsequent RHIC program (2000–2010), which established the standard picture of QGP formation. It builds on the theoretical framework of the Hagedorn model (Hagedorn, 1965) and the statistical model of the early stage (Gazdzicki, 1998) to propose that the onset of deconfinement, rather than isolated signals, provides a more robust test. The paper sets the stage for future measurements of charm quarks relative to the onset of deconfinement and detailed studies of the onset of fireball, as pursued by the NA61/SHINE experiment and planned at FAIR, NICA, and J-PARC-HI.
