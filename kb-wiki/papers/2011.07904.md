# Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov mass models on a 3D Mesh: Effect of triaxial shape

> The modeling of nuclear reactions and radioactive decays in astrophysical or earth-based conditions requires detailed knowledge of the masses of essentially all nuclei. Microscopic mass models based on nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs) can be descriptive and used to provide this information. The concept of intrinsic symmetry breaking is central to the predictive power of EDF approaches, yet is generally not exploited to the utmost by mass models because of the computational demands of adjusting up to about two dozen parameters to thousands of nuclear masses. We report on a first step to bridge the gap between what is presently feasible for studies of individual nuclei and large-scale models: we present a new Skyrme-EDF-based model that was adjusted using a three-dimensional coordinate-space representation, for the first time allowing for both axial and triaxial deformations during the adjustment process. To compensate for the substantial increase in computational cost brought by the latter, we have employed a committee of multilayer neural networks to model the objective function in parameter space and guide us towards the overall best fit. The resulting mass model BSkG1 is computed with the EDF model independently of the neural network. It yields a root mean square (rms) deviation on the 2457 known masses of 741 keV and an rms deviation on the 884 measured charge radii of 0.024 fm.

## Key claim

The authors present a new Skyrme-EDF-based mass model, BSkG1, that for the first time allows for both axial and triaxial deformations during the parameter adjustment process, achieving a root mean square deviation on known masses of 741 keV. The model uses a three-dimensional coordinate-space representation and a committee of multilayer neural networks to guide the optimization, but the final mass model is computed independently of the neural network.

## Method

The authors performed self-consistent Skyrme-Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov (HFB) calculations using the MOCCa code on a three-dimensional Cartesian Lagrange mesh with 32x32x32 points and 0.8 fm spacing, later expanded to 36x36x36 for the final mass table. They employed a semivariational strategy that scans the quadrupole deformation plane (beta-gamma) with a resolution of 0.005 for each nucleus, including blocked calculations for odd-A and odd-odd nuclei using the equal-filling method. To handle the 22 adjustable parameters, they used a committee of multilayer neural networks to model the objective function in parameter space and guide the search toward the overall best fit.

## Key numbers

- Root mean square deviation on the 2457 known masses: 741 keV
- Root mean square deviation on the 884 measured charge radii: 0.024 fm
- Mesh spacing: 0.8 fm, with cubic meshes of 32 points per dimension during adjustment and 36 points per dimension for the final calculation
- For each nucleus during adjustment, 160 extra neutron states and 100 extra proton states were iterated; for the final mass table, these were increased to 400 extra neutron states and 240 extra proton states
- The pairing cutoff parameter mu_pair = 0.5 MeV
- The saturation density used in the pairing interaction: rho_sat = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The proton rms radius used for Coulomb form factors: r_p = 0.895 fm
- The neutron mean square charge radius used: -0.116 fm^2
- The gamma parameter in the Skyrme functional was fixed to 0.3 during fitting
- The symmetry energy at saturation density was constrained to J = 32 MeV

## Context

This work extends the Brussels-Montreal (BSk) series of mass models (Goriely et al., 2009, 2013, 2014, 2016) by moving from axial symmetry and harmonic oscillator basis expansions to a three-dimensional coordinate-space representation that allows triaxial deformations during the adjustment process. The new model BSkG1 achieves comparable accuracy to the BSk models while removing the symmetry restrictions that limited previous approaches. This enables future studies of fission processes using time-dependent mean-field methods and of nuclear pasta phases in neutron star crusts, and provides a foundation for incorporating octupole deformation and time-odd terms in subsequent mass fits.
