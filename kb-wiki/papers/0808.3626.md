# Hard Break-Up of Two-Nucleons and QCD Dynamics of NN Interaction

> We discus recent developments in theory of high energy two-body break-up of few-nucleon systems. The characteristics of these reactions are such that the hard two-body quasielastic subprocess can be clearly separated from the accompanying soft subprocesses. We discuss in details the hard rescattering model(HRM) in which hard photodisintegration develops in two stages. At first, photon knocks-out an energetic quark which rescatters subsequently with a quark of the other nucleon. The latter provides a mechanism of sharing the initial high momentum of the photon between two outgoing nucleons. This final state hard rescattering can be expressed through the hard NN scattering amplitude. Within HRM we discuss hard break-up reactions involving D and 3He targets and demonstrate how these reactions are sensitive to the dynamics of hard pn and pp interaction. Another development of HRM is the prediction of new helicity selection mechanism for hard two-body reactions, which was apparently confirmed in the recent JLab experiment.

## Key claim

The hard rescattering model (HRM) explains the underlying dynamics of high-energy, large-center-of-mass-angle two-body break-up of nucleon pairs from deuteron and helium-3 targets, with the prediction that the unpolarized cross section at 90 degrees for deuteron photodisintegration is parameter-free and requires no further adjustments.

## Method

The author reviews the hard rescattering model (HRM), in which a high-energy photon first knocks out an energetic quark from one nucleon; that quark then undergoes a hard, high-momentum-transfer rescattering with a quark from the second nucleon. This final-state hard rescattering is expressed through the experimentally measured hard nucleon-nucleon (NN) scattering amplitude, allowing the sum of many nonperturbative diagrams to be replaced by a single measured amplitude. The formalism is applied to both deuteron (pn) and helium-3 (pp) break-up reactions, and predictions for helicity transfer are compared with recent Jefferson Lab data.

## Key numbers

* At photon energy \(E_\gamma > 2\) GeV, the invariant momentum transfer \(-t\) at 90 degrees exceeds 4 GeV\(^2\) and the invariant mass of the NN system \(M_{NN} = \sqrt{s}\) exceeds 2 GeV.
* The quark-counting rules predict that the differential cross section for \(\gamma d \rightarrow pn\) scales as \(d\sigma/dt \sim s^{-11}\), a dependence confirmed experimentally starting at \(E_\gamma = 1\) GeV.
* At 90 degrees center-of-mass scattering, the function \(C(\theta_{cm}=90^0) \approx 1\), making the HRM prediction for the \(\gamma d \rightarrow pn\) cross section parameter-free.
* The HRM prediction for the \(\gamma d \rightarrow pn\) cross section includes an additional \(1/s^\prime\) factor (where \(s^\prime = s - 4m_N^2\)) on top of the \(s^{-10}\) scaling of the pn cross section, yielding the same \(s^{-11}\) dependence as the quark-counting rule.
* The angular dependence of the cross section is shown for \(E_\gamma = 2.4\) GeV (bottom panel of Fig. 3).
* The longitudinal polarization asymmetry \(C_{z'}\) is predicted to approach unity with increasing photon energy at 90 degrees CM scattering; recent JLab data at \(E_\gamma = 1.9\) GeV appear to confirm the HRM prediction for large values of \(C_{z'}\) (Fig. 5).

## Context

This work extends the hard rescattering model (HRM) previously developed by Sargsian and collaborators (2005) for deuteron photodisintegration to include two-proton break-up from helium-3 targets, and contrasts HRM with the reduced nuclear amplitude (RNA) formalism and the quark-gluon string (QGS) model, both of which require absolute normalization. The paper enables future tests of HRM predictions for energy oscillations in \(\gamma + ^3He \rightarrow (pp) + n\) cross sections similar to those observed in hard elastic pp scattering, and for helicity transfer asymmetries that distinguish between pn and pp break-up channels. The recent JLab experiment on high-energy pp break-up (2007) provides the first data to discriminate among these models.
