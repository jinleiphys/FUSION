# Electron capture cross sections and nuclear partition functions for fp-shell nuclei

> We present calculation of electron capture cross sections (ECC), in the limit of zero momentum transfer, using the pn QRPA model in stellar matter. Towards this aim we make use of our recently introduced recipe for estimation of nuclear partition functions. For low momentum transfer q tends to zero, the nuclear matrix elements of the Pστ plus operator provide the leading contribution to the total cross section which we estimate using the pn QRPA model in a multi shell single particle space with a schematic interaction. Key f p shell nuclei (odd A, even even and odd odd) bearing astrophysical importance were selected for the calculation of ECC in stellar environment. These f p shell nuclei play crucial role in pre supernova evolution of massive stars and core collapse. We further present microscopic calculation of ground and excited states Gamow Teller strength distributions and stellar electron capture rates on these suite of nuclei. We used two different sets of empirically determined pairing gaps to calculate the ECC and electron capture rates. Results are compared with experimental data and previous computations. Our calculated ECC are systematically smaller at low electron incident energies as compared to the shell model results.

# Electron capture cross sections and nuclear partition functions for fp-shell nuclei

## Key claim

The authors present pn-QRPA calculations of electron capture cross sections (ECC) in the zero-momentum-transfer limit for fp-shell nuclei, finding that their computed ECC are systematically smaller at low electron incident energies compared to shell-model results, with the discrepancy attributed to the use of a tighter quenching factor (0.6 versus 0.8) and differences in model space.

## Method

The authors employed the proton-neutron quasiparticle random-phase approximation (pn-QRPA) with a deformed Nilsson potential and BCS pairing correlations, using two different empirical schemes for pairing gaps (Scheme-I: global systematics Δ = 12/√A; Scheme-II: 3-point formula from empirical masses). Nuclear partition functions (NPFs) were calculated using discrete energy levels up to 10 MeV excitation energy supplemented by experimental data, with a uniform Fermi gas level density assumed above that threshold. The GT strength was quenched by a constant factor of 0.6, and the Brink-Axel hypothesis was not assumed for excited-state GT distributions.

## Key numbers

- Pairing gaps were calculated using Scheme-I: Δₙ = Δₚ = 12/√A MeV, and Scheme-II: 3-point formula from empirical masses (Table 1 in paper)
- NPFs were computed for temperatures from 0.01 GK to 30 GK; at 30 GK, Rauscher's NPFs are orders of magnitude larger due to high-temperature corrections not included in this work
- ECC were calculated for stellar temperatures of 5.8, 11.6, and 17.4 GK, with incident electron energies up to 30 MeV
- For ⁴⁵Sc at low w (~2.5 MeV) and T = 5.8 GK, the pn-QRPA ECC is approximately three orders of magnitude smaller than SMMC results
- For ⁵⁸Fe at w = 29.1 MeV, the pn-QRPA ECC is roughly a factor 8 larger than SMMC despite the tighter quenching factor of 0.6
- Scheme-I ECC were bigger at most by a factor 2.5 for ⁵⁸Mn at w = 2 MeV; Scheme-II ECC were at most a factor 1.5 bigger for ⁵⁶Mn at w = 10 MeV
- For ⁴⁵Sc at 30 GK and low stellar density, the calculated electron capture rate is 41 s⁻¹

## Context

This work extends the pn-QRPA approach of Nabi and Klapdor-Kleingrothaus (1999, 2004) for stellar weak-interaction rates, applying it to electron capture cross sections on nine astrophysically important fp-shell nuclei (⁴⁵Sc, ⁵¹V, ⁵⁵Fe, ⁵⁹Co, ⁵⁴Fe, ⁵⁸Fe, ⁵⁶Mn, ⁵⁸Mn). The results are compared with shell-model Monte Carlo calculations of Dean et al. (1998), finite-temperature relativistic RPA of Niu et al. (2011), and large-scale shell model calculations of Langanke and Martinez-Pinedo (2000). The authors demonstrate that the Brink-Axel hypothesis is not a good approximation for stellar ECC and EC rate calculations, consistent with earlier findings by Nabi and collaborators and Dzhioev et al. (2010). The work enables improved modeling of presupernova evolution and core-collapse supernova simulations by providing microscopic GT strength distributions and NPFs without relying on the Brink-Axel hypothesis.
