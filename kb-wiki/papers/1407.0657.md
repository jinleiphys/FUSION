# A recipe for EFT uncertainty quantification in nuclear physics

> The application of effective field theory (EFT) methods to nuclear systems provides the opportunity to rigorously estimate the uncertainties originating in the nuclear Hamiltonian. Yet this is just one source of uncertainty in the observables predicted by calculations based on nuclear EFTs. We discuss the goals of uncertainty quantification in such calculations and outline a recipe to obtain statistically meaningful error bars for their predictions. We argue that the different sources of theory error can be accounted for within a Bayesian framework, as we illustrate using a toy model.

# A recipe for EFT uncertainty quantification in nuclear physics

## Key claim
The paper argues that a Bayesian framework can provide statistically meaningful error bars for nuclear EFT calculations by accounting for all sources of theory uncertainty, but notes that current cutoff-variation error bands often do not behave consistently with their interpretation as theoretical uncertainties, for example, N²LO bands can be wider than NLO bands in predicted observables, and the shift from NLO to N²LO can appear larger than expected if the NLO band represents a 1σ confidence level.

## Method
The authors propose a Bayesian recipe for uncertainty quantification in nuclear EFTs that incorporates prior information encoding naturalness of low-energy constants (LECs), marginalization over nuisance parameters representing higher-order EFT contributions, and model selection via evidence ratios. They illustrate the approach using a toy model and discuss how error plots (Lepage plots) can diagnose whether EFT residuals scale according to the assumed power counting and whether regulator artifacts are under control.

## Key numbers
- Chiral EFT NN Hamiltonians at O(Q³) contain 9 NN LECs; at O(Q⁴) they contain 24 NN LECs.
- Cutoffs for NN LEC fits are typically in the range 500–800 MeV (450–600 MeV at O(Q⁴)).
- The Nijmegen analysis fit NN data up to 350 MeV lab energy; Ref. Epelbaum (1999) determined LECs using data at E_lab ≤ 100 MeV; Ref. Ekstrom (2013) fit NN data to 125 MeV.
- In proton Compton scattering, fits become unstable once E_max > 200 MeV because the EFT cannot describe data where the Δ(1232) resonance plays a marked role.
- In pionless EFT, the difference k cot δ(k) at two cutoffs (600 MeV and 200 MeV) shows omitted operators at LO, NLO, and N²LO behave as (p/Λ_b)^(n+1) with n as expected from power counting above 70 MeV.

## Context
This work extends the Bayesian uncertainty quantification approach of Schindler (2008) to nuclear EFTs, addressing the limitations of conventional cutoff-variation error bands that have been used in chiral EFT calculations by Epelbaum (2008) and others. The paper contrasts with earlier approaches where LEC extraction involved fitting data to different maximum energies and checking stability, as done for proton Compton scattering by Beane (2004) and McGovern (2012). The proposed framework enables rigorous treatment of the trade-off between including more data at higher energies versus the decreasing accuracy of the EFT as momentum approaches the breakdown scale, and could provide quantitative criteria for testing different EFT power countings.
