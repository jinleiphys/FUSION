# Neutral nuclear core vs super charged one

> Based on the Thomas-Fermi approach, we describe and distinguish the electron distributions around extended nuclear cores: (i) in the case that cores are neutral for electrons bound by protons inside cores and proton and electron numbers are the same; (ii) in the case that super charged cores are bare, electrons (positrons) produced by vacuum polarization are bound by (fly into) cores (infinity).

# Neutral nuclear core vs super charged one

## Key claim

The Thomas-Fermi equations for electron distributions around neutral nuclear cores (where proton and electron numbers are equal) and super charged bare cores (where vacuum polarization produces electrons that screen the core) are structurally similar in the ultra-relativistic regime where the Coulomb potential greatly exceeds the electron rest mass, but differ in the relativistic and non-relativistic regimes near the edge of the electron distribution, with the super charged case having a finite radius \(r_0\) where the electron density vanishes.

## Method

The authors solve the Thomas-Fermi model for two distinct scenarios: (i) neutral cores with equal proton and electron numbers, where the equilibrium condition is \(\mathcal{E}_e(P_e^F)=0\) (balance of kinetic and potential energies); (ii) super charged bare cores, where vacuum polarization produces electron-positron pairs and the equilibrium condition is \(\mathcal{E}_e(P_e^F)=-2m_ec^2\) (electrons fill all negative energy levels up to \(-m_ec^2\)). In both cases, the Poisson equation is solved with a constant proton density inside the core radius \(R_c\) and zero outside, using degenerate electron densities derived from the respective Fermi momenta. An ultra-relativistic approximation (\(V_{\rm coul}(r)\gg 2m_ec^2\)) is applied to obtain an analytical solution for the Coulomb potential, and the electron densities in the two cases are compared.

## Key numbers

- The ultra-relativistic solution gives the Coulomb potential at the core center: \(V_{\rm coul}(0)=\hbar c(3\pi^2 n_p)^{1/3}\sim m_\pi c^2\), where \(m_\pi\) is the pion mass.
- At the core surface \(r=R_c\), the potential is \(V_{\rm coul}(R_c)=3/4 V_{\rm coul}(0)\gg m_ec^2\).
- The scaled radial variable is \(x=2(\pi/3)^{1/6}\alpha^{1/2} n_p^{1/3} (r-R_c)\sim 0.1 (r-R_c)/\lambda_\pi\), where \(\lambda_\pi=\hbar/(m_\pi c)\) is the pion Compton length.
- For \(r<r_0\) (where \(V_{\rm coul}(r)>2m_ec^2\)), the inequality \(n_e^{\rm neutral}(r) > n_e^{\rm charged}(r)\) holds.
- In the super charged case, the total electron number is \(N^{\rm ion}_e=\int_0^{r_0} 4\pi r^2 dr n_e(r) < N_p\), and the electron distribution vanishes at the finite radius \(r_0\) where \(V_{\rm coul}(r_0)=2m_ec^2\).

## Context

This work extends the Thomas-Fermi approach previously applied to neutral nuclear cores by Ruffini and Stella (1980, 1981) and Rotondo, Ruffini, and Xue (2006), and contrasts it with the super charged core scenario studied by Muller (1975) and Migdal (1976) in the context of vacuum polarization around highly charged nuclei. The comparison clarifies the different equilibrium conditions and electron density profiles in the two cases, and the ultra-relativistic solution provides a unified analytical description for both scenarios in the regime where the Coulomb potential is much larger than the electron rest mass. The results enable further study of electron distributions around dense nuclear matter, with implications for the structure of neutron stars and superheavy nuclei.
