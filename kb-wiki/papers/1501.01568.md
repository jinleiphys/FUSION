# Predicting the production of neutron rich heavy nuclei in multi-nucleon transfer reactions using GRAZING-F

> Background: Multi-nucleon transfer reactions have recently attracted attention as a possible path to the synthesis of new neutron-rich heavy nuclei. Purpose: We study transfer reactions involving massive nuclei with the intention of understanding if the semi-classical model GRAZING coupled to an evaporation and fission competition model can satisfactory reproduce experimental data on transfer reactions in which fission plays a role. Methods: We have taken the computer code GRAZING and have added fission competition to it (GRAZING-F) using our current understanding of $Γ_n/Γ_f$, fission barriers and level densities. Results: The code GRAZING-F seems to satisfactory reproduce experimental data for $+1p$, $+2p$ and $+3p$ transfers, but has limitations in reproducing measurements of larger above-target and below-target transfers. Nonetheless, we use GRAZING-F to estimate production rates of neutron-rich $N=126$ nuclei, actinides and transactinides. Conclusions: The GRAZING code, with appropriate modifications to account for fission decay as well as neutron emission by excited primary fragments, does not predict large cross sections for multi-nucleon transfer reactions leading to neutron-rich transactinide nuclei, but predicts opportunities to produce new neutron-rich actinide isotopes.

## Key claim

The semi-classical model GRAZING, when modified to include fission competition (GRAZING-F), can satisfactorily reproduce experimental data for +1p, +2p, and +3p transfers in massive multi-nucleon transfer reactions, but has limitations in reproducing larger above-target and below-target transfers, primarily because GRAZING underestimates the flow of neutrons in those channels.

## Method

The authors took the existing semi-classical computer code GRAZING, which models multi-step nucleon exchange along classical trajectories with Coulomb plus nuclear interactions, and added fission competition using the Vandenbosch-Huizenga formalism for Γ_n/Γ_f. Fission barriers were taken as the sum of Thomas-Fermi barriers plus shell corrections from the Finite Range Droplet Model, with angular momentum effects treated by reducing available excitation energy by rotational energy and scaling barriers with the Sierk barrier. For each primary fragment (Z, A, E*, J) from GRAZING, the code randomly decides between neutron evaporation and fission, iterating until excitation energy falls below the neutron separation energy, with 10^12 cascades simulated per reaction using high-performance computing.

## Key numbers

- In the 238U+248Cm reaction at entering energy 1760 MeV (mid-target 1650 MeV), GRAZING-F reproduces experimental cross sections for +1p, +2p, and +3p transfers "remarkably well," but for Fm and Md nuclei at least 2 and 4 additional neutrons would be required on average in the primary products to match data.
- In the 238U+238U reaction at 2059 MeV, the transfer-fission cross section in the first slice is ~90% of the transfer cross section, decreasing to ~4% in the last slice; at least 5 additional neutrons would be needed on average to reproduce the locations of maximum yields.
- For the 136Xe+208Pb reaction, simulated yields for 203Ir and 204Pt peak at E_c.m. ~750 MeV with cross sections of 1.7 μb and 23 μb, respectively; assuming 100 pnA beam and 1 mg/cm^2 target, production rates would be 3 s^-1 and 40 s^-1.
- For the 136Xe+198Pt reaction, GRAZING-F predicts a transfer-fission cross section of ~30 mb compared to a transfer cross section of ~5 b, indicating fission does not play a significant role.
- For the 94Kr+248Cm reaction at E_c.m./V_C = 1.45, the predicted production cross section for 248Pu is ~0.5 mb, compared to ~0.02 mb in the 86Kr-induced reaction; however, maximum production rate of 248Pu would be ~15 per year assuming current CARIBU beam intensity of 15 s^-1.
- For the 238U+249Bk reaction at high energy, GRAZING-F predicts production of unknown neutron-rich Md, No, and Lr isotopes with cross sections below 1 nb; under assumed conditions, 261Md would have yield ~30, 261No ~10, and 263Lr ~1 nuclei per day.

## Context

This work extends the semi-classical GRAZING model, previously limited to neutron evaporation only and mainly applied to light projectile reactions with medium-to-heavy targets (Corradi et al., 2009; Dasso, 1994), by adding fission competition to handle reactions where fissility is high, such as U+Cm. The study directly compares GRAZING-F predictions to experimental data from Schädel (1978, 1982), Moody (1986, 1987), Welch (1987), and Gregorich (1987), and contrasts its performance with the Langevin-based dynamical model of Zagrebaev and Greiner (2006, 2008), finding that GRAZING-F better reproduces few-nucleon transfers while the Langevin model better handles massive transfers. The predictions enable assessment of specific reactions (e.g., 136Xe+208Pb, 136Xe+198Pt, 238U+249Bk) as potential sources of neutron-rich N=126 nuclei and unknown actinide isotopes, with the caveat that all predictions require experimental verification.
