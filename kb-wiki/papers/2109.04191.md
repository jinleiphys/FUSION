# Confirming the existence of twin stars in a NICER way

> We demonstrate that future radius measurement of the NICER mission have the potential to reveal the existence of a strong phase transition in dense neutron star matter by confirming the existence of so called twin stars, compact star configurations with the same mass but different radii. The latest radius constraints from NICER for the pulsars J0740+6620 as well as J0030+0451 are discussed using relativistic mean field equations of state with varying stiffness, connected with a first order phase transition to quark matter. We show, that twin star solutions are compatible with the new radius constraint but are located at radii \emph{below} the present constraints from NICER serving as a smoking gun for a strong phase transition in neutron star matter. This scenario is realized if a strong phase transition takes place in neutron stars of the first branch with masses above $2\,M_\odot$.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that twin star solutions (compact stars with the same mass but different radii, arising from a strong first-order phase transition) remain compatible with the latest NICER radius constraints for pulsars J0740+6620 and J0030+0451, but only if the second branch of twin stars lies at radii *below* the current NICER measurements. This scenario requires a strong phase transition occurring in neutron stars of the first branch with masses above 2 solar masses.

## Method

The authors use a generalized relativistic mean field equation of state for hadronic matter, with adjustable effective nucleon mass, symmetry energy, and slope parameter, connected to a quark matter equation of state via a constant speed of sound approach with a first-order phase transition. They classify twin star solutions into four categories based on the masses of the first and second maxima in the mass-radius relation, and systematically explore the parameter space of transition pressure and energy density discontinuity to determine which configurations satisfy constraints from NICER, the 2 solar mass pulsar observations, and the gravitational wave event GW170817.

## Key numbers

- A jump in energy density of Δε = 350 MeV/fm³ always results in at least a 0.1 solar mass gap between the two branches, defining twin stars.
- For category I and II twin stars with effective mass m*/m = 0.65, the second branch can reach masses as low as about 1.46 solar masses and radii as low as about 9 km.
- For category I and II twin stars with effective mass m*/m = 0.70, the second branch can reach masses as low as about 1.55 solar masses and radii as low as about 9.6 km.
- Category III phase transitions require jumps in energy density Δε ≤ 380 MeV/fm³ to satisfy the 2 solar mass constraint, but cannot satisfy the new NICER constraints.
- Effective masses m*/m < 0.65 cannot generate 2 solar masses in the first branch while remaining compatible with GW170817.

## Context

This work extends previous studies of twin stars and hybrid stars (Christian 2017, Christian 2019, Christian 2020) by incorporating the latest NICER radius measurements from Miller (2021) and Riley (2021) for J0740+6620. It contradicts claims by Pang (2021), Annala (2021), and Huth (2021) that the new NICER data require all neutron stars to have radii of at least 11 km, showing instead that a second branch of twin stars at smaller radii remains viable. The results enable future NICER observations of pulsars like J1614-2230 and J0437-4715 to potentially confirm the existence of a strong phase transition by finding neutron stars with smaller radii than J0740+6620 at known masses below 2 solar masses.
