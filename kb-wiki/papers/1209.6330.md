# Event-by-event anisotropic flow in heavy-ion collisions from combined Yang-Mills and viscous fluid dynamics

> Anisotropic flow coefficients v_1-v_5 in heavy ion collisions are computed by combining a classical Yang-Mills description of the early time glasma flow with the subsequent relativistic viscous hydrodynamic evolution of matter through the quark-gluon plasma and hadron gas phases. The glasma dynamics, as realized in the IP-Glasma model, takes into account event-by-event geometric fluctuations in nucleon positions and intrinsic sub-nucleon scale color charge fluctuations; the pre-equilibrium flow of matter is then matched to the MUSIC algorithm describing viscous hydrodynamic flow and particle production at freeze-out. The IP-Glasma+MUSIC model describes well both transverse momentum dependent and integrated v_n data measured at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) and the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider (RHIC). The model also reproduces the event-by-event distributions of v_2, v_3 and v_4 measured by the ATLAS collaboration. The implications of our results for better understanding of the dynamics of the glasma as well as for the extraction of transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma are outlined.

## Key claim

The IP-Glasma+MUSIC model reproduces both transverse-momentum-dependent and integrated anisotropic flow coefficients v_2 through v_5 measured at the LHC and RHIC, as well as event-by-event distributions of v_2, v_3, and v_4 from ATLAS, but the authors note that the initial value of the viscous tensor is set to zero by assuming an efficient (but not yet computed) mechanism for its rapid quenching, and that the switching time from classical Yang-Mills to hydrodynamics is chosen as tau_switch = 0.2 fm/c with results that are statistically consistent when varied to 0.4 fm/c.

## Method

The IP-Glasma model combines the IP-Sat parametrization of nucleon and nuclear wavefunctions with classical Yang-Mills dynamics to generate event-by-event initial energy density and flow velocity in the transverse plane at a switching time tau_switch ~ 1/Q_s. These initial conditions are then matched to the 3+1 dimensional relativistic viscous hydrodynamic code MUSIC, which evolves the system through the quark-gluon plasma and hadron gas phases using the s95p-PCE equation of state and a Cooper-Frye freeze-out at T_FO = 120 MeV. Anisotropic flow coefficients v_1 through v_5 are computed from the exact event plane in each event using the thermal distribution functions from the Cooper-Frye prescription including resonance decays up to 1.3 GeV.

## Key numbers

- Switching time from classical Yang-Mills to hydrodynamics: tau_switch = 0.2 fm/c (also tested at 0.4 fm/c)
- Temperature for partial chemical equilibrium: T_PCE = 150 MeV
- Kinetic freeze-out temperature: T_FO = 120 MeV
- Resonance decays included up to mass 1.3 GeV
- Constant shear viscosity to entropy density ratio used at LHC: eta/s = 0.2
- Constant eta/s used at RHIC: eta/s = 0.12 (about 40% smaller than the LHC value)
- Temperature-dependent eta/s parametrization (HH-HQ) with minimum eta/s(T) = 0.08 at T = T_tr = 180 MeV
- Soft pions at p_T < 300 MeV are underestimated in the transverse momentum spectra
- 200 events per centrality used for flow coefficient calculations; 1300 events for event-by-event distributions

## Context

This work extends prior studies of anisotropic flow by replacing earlier initial condition models (such as MC-Glauber and MC-KLN, which failed to reproduce event-by-event v_n distributions in 20-25% centrality) with the IP-Glasma model that includes sub-nucleon scale color charge fluctuations and classical Yang-Mills dynamics. The IP-Glasma+MUSIC framework significantly improves upon previous approaches by Luzum (2008), Takahashi (2009), Holopainen (2010), Song (2011), Qiu (2011), Schenke (2011), and Bozek (2011) that omitted the early-time glasma dynamics. The results enable systematic extraction of temperature-dependent transport properties of the quark-gluon plasma through combined analysis of flow harmonics at both RHIC and LHC, and the framework can incorporate ongoing work on early-time quantum fluctuations and instabilities that drive the system toward isotropy.
