# Large-scale Continuum Random Phase Approximation predictions of dipole strength for astrophysical applications

> Large-scale calculations of the E1 strength are performed within the random phase approximation (RPA) based on the relativistic point-coupling mean field approach in order to derive the radiative neutron capture cross sections for all nuclei of astrophysical interest. While the coupling to the single-particle continuum is taken into account in an explicit and self-consistent way, additional corrections like the coupling to complex configurations and the temperature and deformation effects are included in a phenomenological way to account for a complete description of the nuclear dynamical problem. It is shown that the resulting E1-strength function based on the PCF1 force is in close agreement with photoabsorption data as well as the available experimental E1 strength data at low energies. For neutron-rich nuclei, as well as light neutron-deficient nuclei, a low-lying so-called pygmy resonance is found systematically in the 5-10 MeV region. The corresponding strength can reach 10% of the giant dipole strength in the neutron-rich region and about 5% in the neutron-deficient region, and is found to be reduced in the vicinity of the shell closures. Finally, the neutron capture reaction rates of neutron-rich nuclei is found to be about 2-5 times larger than those predicted on the basis of the nonrelativistic RPA calculation and about a factor 50 larger than obtained with traditional Lorentzian-type approaches.

## Key claim

The paper claims that its large-scale continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) calculations, based on the relativistic point-coupling PCF1 force and including phenomenological corrections for complex configurations, temperature, and deformation, can reproduce experimental photoabsorption and low-energy E1 strength data across the nuclear chart. However, the agreement is achieved only after adjusting two free interference parameters (C_ST^(E) = -0.85 and C_ST^(Gamma) = -0.59) through a minimization procedure against measured giant dipole resonance (GDR) energies and widths for about 80 nuclei.

## Method

The authors perform relativistic continuum random phase approximation (CRPA) calculations using the point-coupling Lagrangian PCF1 to describe the nuclear ground state and the linearized Bethe-Salpeter equation for the response to an external E1 field. The coupling to the single-particle continuum is treated exactly via a nonspectral Green's function method, while pairing correlations for open-shell nuclei are included through the BCS approximation. The resulting CRPA strength is then folded with a Lorentzian function that incorporates an empirical energy-dependent width (including contributions from coupling to complex configurations), a deformation-induced splitting of the GDR into two components for axially deformed nuclei, and a temperature-dependent width for the deexcitation strength, with the interference factors and temperature parameter alpha adjusted to fit experimental data.

## Key numbers

- The best fit to experimental GDR data yields interference factors C_ST^(E) = -0.85 and C_ST^(Gamma) = -0.59, giving rms deviations of f_rms^(E) = 0.66 MeV for GDR energies and f_rms^(Gamma) = 1.81 MeV for GDR widths across about 80 nuclei.
- The temperature-dependent width parameter alpha = 2.30 gives the best overall agreement with experimental low-energy E1 strength data from thermal capture measurements on nuclei from Mg to U.
- For neutron-rich nuclei, the pygmy dipole resonance (PDR) strength can reach 10% of the giant dipole strength; for neutron-deficient nuclei it reaches about 5%.
- The PDR strength is reduced near shell closures (N = 20, 50, 82, 126) and for neutron-rich nuclei with N > 200 remains constant at about 5% of the GDR strength.
- For extremely neutron-rich nuclei with N > 200, the GDR width remains rather constant around 8 MeV.
- Neutron capture reaction rates for neutron-rich nuclei are about 2-5 times larger than those from nonrelativistic RPA calculations and about a factor 50 larger than those from traditional Lorentzian-type approaches.

## Context

This work extends the relativistic continuum RPA approach developed by Daoutidis and Ring (2009, 2011) to large-scale calculations covering about 8000 nuclei from Z=8 to Z=110, incorporating phenomenological corrections for spreading width, deformation, and temperature that were previously treated separately in approaches such as the second RPA (DNSW 1990) or particle-vibration coupling (LPT 2010, LRT 2009, AGKK 2011). The resulting E1 strength functions and neutron capture rates are intended for use in astrophysical reaction network calculations, particularly for neutron-rich nuclei where experimental data are unavailable, and the paper finds significant deviations from predictions based on nonrelativistic RPA and traditional Lorentzian models.
