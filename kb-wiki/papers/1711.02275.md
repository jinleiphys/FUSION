# Quantum self-organization and nuclear collectivities

> The quantum self-organization is introduced as one of the major underlying mechanisms of the quantum many-body systems, for instance, atomic nuclei. It is shown that atomic nuclei are not necessarily like simple rigid vases containing almost free nucleons, in contrast to the naive Fermi liquid picture. Nuclear forces are demonstrated to be rich enough to change single-particle energies for each eigenstate, so as to enhance the relevant collective mode. When the quantum self-organization occurs, single-particle energies can be self-organized (or self-optimized), being enhanced by (i) two quantum liquids, e.g., protons and neutrons, (ii) two major force components, e.g., quadrupole interaction (to drive collective mode) and monopole interaction (to control resistance). Type II shell evolution is considered to be a simple visible case involving excitations across a (sub)magic gap. Actual cases such as shape coexistence, quantum phase transition, octupole vibration/deformation, super deformation, etc. can be studied with this scope. The quantum self-organization becomes more important in heavier nuclei where the number of active orbits and the number of active nucleons are larger. With larger numbers of them, the effects of the organization can be more significant. The quantum self-organization is a general phenomenon, and is expected to be found in other quantum systems.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that atomic nuclei can "self-organize" by changing their single-particle energies for each eigenstate through orbit-dependent monopole interactions, thereby reducing the resistance to collective deformation modes. This mechanism is presented as a general phenomenon in quantum many-body systems, with the hedge that it becomes more important in heavier nuclei where the number of active orbits and nucleons is larger, and may not be so visible in light nuclei except for particular cases.

## Method

The authors use Monte Carlo Shell Model (MCSM) calculations with a single Hamiltonian to describe both spherical and deformed states in Zr isotopes, comparing occupation numbers of proton orbits and effective single-particle energies across different eigenstates. They also perform constrained Hartree-Fock calculations for 68Ni, comparing the potential energy surface from the original Hamiltonian with one where the monopole interactions are artificially averaged to suppress the quantum self-organization effect.

## Key numbers

- In 98Zr, the spacing between the neutron d5/2 and g7/2 orbits is nearly 5 MeV for the 0+1 (spherical) state, but is reduced to about 2 MeV in the 0+2 (deformed) state.
- In 100Zr, the 0+1 state (strongly deformed) also shows the reduced splitting of about 2 MeV between the d5/2 and g7/2 orbits.
- For 68Ni, when the quantum self-organization is suppressed by averaging monopole interactions, the prolate local minimum in the potential energy surface is pushed up by about 4 MeV compared to the original calculation.
- The 2+1 level in Zr isotopes drops abruptly between N=58 and N=60, indicating a quantum phase transition from spherical to strongly deformed shapes.

## Context

This work extends the understanding of the interplay between single-particle states and collective modes, a problem highlighted by Bohr and Mottelson (1975) and pursued by G.E. Brown as quoted in Schaefer (2014). It builds on Type II shell evolution introduced by Otsuka and Tsunoda (2016), which involves particle-hole excitations across magic or sub-magic gaps, and generalizes this concept to more complex cases such as shape coexistence and quantum phase transitions. The mechanism is demonstrated for Zr isotopes building on calculations by Togashi et al. (2016) and Kremer et al. (2016), and for Ni isotopes following Tsunoda et al. (2014). The work enables future study of shape coexistence in Hg/Pb isotopes, octupole deformation, superdeformation, and potentially links to fission and superheavy elements.
