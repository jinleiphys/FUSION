# Phase transitions in the early and the present Universe: from the big bang to heavy ion collisions

> In these lectures I discuss cosmological phase transitions with the goal of establishing the possibility of observational consequences. I argue that the {\em only} phase transition amenable of experimental study within the foreseeable future is that predicted by QCD and discuss some of the potential observational cosmological consequences associated with this phase transition(s). I describe the experimental effort to study the QCD phase transition(s) at RHIC and SPS and summarize some of the recent experimental results. The possibility of novel phases of QCD in the core of pulsars is discussed along with the suggested observational consequences. A brief review of standard big bang cosmology as well as the astrophysics of compact stars sets the stage for understanding the observational cosmological and astrophysical consequences of phase transitions in the standard model.

## Key claim
The only cosmological phase transition amenable to experimental study within the foreseeable future is the QCD phase transition, because GUT-scale and electroweak phase transitions are either too speculative or cannot produce observable non-equilibrium conditions in the minimal standard model.

## Method
The paper reviews standard hot big bang cosmology and the astrophysics of compact stars, then examines three cosmological phase transitions: GUT-scale (associated with inflation), electroweak (associated with baryogenesis), and QCD (associated with the quark-gluon plasma). For each, the author connects theoretical predictions (e.g., spinodal instabilities, bubble nucleation, chiral symmetry breaking) to potential observational signatures in the cosmic microwave background, light element abundances, heavy ion collision experiments at RHIC and SPS, and pulsar observations.

## Key numbers
- Hubble constant today: H0 ~ 65 km/s/Mpc
- CMB temperature today: T0 = 2.73 K
- CMB temperature anisotropies: ΔT/T0 ~ 10^(-5)
- Light element abundances by mass: ~76% Hydrogen, ~24% ^4He, ^3He ~10^(-5), deuterium ~10^(-5), ^7Li ~10^(-10) relative to Hydrogen
- QCD scale: Λ_QCD ~ 200 MeV, corresponding to T_QCD ~ 10^12 K
- Electroweak phase transition temperature: T_EW ~ 100 GeV, occurring at t ~ 10^(-12) seconds after the Big Bang
- Baryon-to-photon ratio from nucleosynthesis: η between 4×10^(-10) and 7×10^(-10)
- COBE four-year DMR result: n_s ~ 1.2 ± 0.3 for the spectral index
- Higgs mass lower bound from LEP: M_H ≳ 115 GeV
- Weak interaction rate to expansion rate ratio at T_EW: Γ/H ~ 10^15

## Context
This lecture reviews and extends the standard picture of cosmological phase transitions as presented in textbooks by Kolb and Turner and by Liddle and Lyth, and in reviews by Turner and Trodden. The author argues that while GUT-scale phase transitions could produce a red-tilted power spectrum (n_s < 1) via spinodal instabilities during inflation (building on work by Guth and Pi, and by Boyanovsky, de Vega, and Sanchez), and the electroweak phase transition could generate the baryon asymmetry via Sakharov conditions, the minimal standard model likely cannot provide a strong first-order electroweak transition or sufficient CP violation. The paper instead focuses on the QCD phase transition as the only one currently accessible through heavy ion collision experiments at RHIC and SPS, and through observations of pulsar cores, citing work by Heinz and by Braun-Munzinger on QGP evidence.
