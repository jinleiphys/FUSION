# Kaon Condensation in Proto-Neutron Star Matter

> We study the equation of state (EOS) of kaon-condensed matter including the effects of temperature and trapped neutrinos. It is found that the order of the phase transition to a kaon-condensed phase, and whether or not Gibbs' rules for phase equilibrium can be satisfied in the case of a first order transition, depend sensitively on the choice of the kaon-nucleon interaction. The main effect of finite temperature, for any value of the lepton fraction, is to mute the effects of a first order transition, so that the thermodynamics becomes similar to that of a second order transition. Above a critical temperature, found to be at least 30--60 MeV depending upon the interaction, the first order transition disappears. The phase boundaries in baryon density versus lepton number and baryon density versus temperature planes are delineated. We find that the thermal effects on the maximum gravitational mass of neutron stars are as important as the effects of trapped neutrinos, in contrast to previously studied cases in which the matter contained only nucleons or in which hyperons and/or quark matter were considered. Kaon-condensed EOSs permit the existence of metastable neutron stars, because the maximum mass of an initially hot, lepton-rich protoneutron star is greater than that of a cold, deleptonized neutron star. The large thermal effects imply that a metastable protoneutron star's collapse to a black hole could occur much later than in previously studied cases that allow metastable configurations.

## Key claim

The order of the phase transition to kaon-condensed matter (first-order vs. second-order) and the possibility of satisfying Gibbs' phase equilibrium criteria depend sensitively on the choice of the kaon-nucleon interaction model; a first-order transition is possible only when the magnitude of the kaon-nucleus optical potential exceeds approximately 100 MeV.

## Method

The authors constructed equations of state for proto-neutron star matter using relativistic mean-field models for nucleons (Walecka-type with GM and ZM parametrizations) and three different meson-exchange Lagrangians for kaons (KPE, GS, and a new TW form). They solved the mean-field equations for the meson fields and kaon condensate amplitude at finite temperature with trapped neutrinos, imposing charge neutrality and chemical equilibrium via weak interactions. For first-order transitions, they applied Gibbs' criteria for phase equilibrium between normal and kaon-condensed phases, and delineated phase boundaries in the baryon density versus lepton number and baryon density versus temperature planes.

## Key numbers

- A first-order phase transition to kaon-condensed matter occurs only for magnitudes of the kaon-nucleus optical potential greater than 100 MeV.
- The critical temperature above which the first-order transition disappears is at least 30–60 MeV, depending on the interaction model.
- The kaon optical potential from the omega-meson contribution alone is -48.5 MeV (using g_omegaK = g_omega/3).
- The kaon condensate appears at densities up to approximately 8 times nuclear saturation density (n_0).
- The saturation density n_0 used for nuclear matter is 0.16 fm^{-3} (implied by the Negele-Vautherin and Baym-Bethe-Pethick regimes cited: 0.001 < n < 0.08 fm^{-3} and n < 0.001 fm^{-3}).

## Context

This work extends prior studies of kaon condensation in neutron stars by Kaplan and Nelson (1986), Prakash et al. (1995), and Thorsson et al. (1994) to include finite temperature and trapped neutrinos, relevant for proto-neutron star evolution. It also builds on the mixed-phase treatment of Glendenning and Schaffner-Bielich (1999) by testing multiple kaon-nucleon interaction forms and showing that thermal effects mute first-order transitions. The results enable future dynamical simulations of proto-neutron star evolution with consistent kaon-condensed equations of state and neutrino opacities, and suggest that metastable neutron stars could collapse to black holes much later than in previously studied cases with hyperons or quark matter.
