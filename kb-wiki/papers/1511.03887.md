# Shell model description of Gamow-Teller strengths in $pf$-shell nuclei

> A systematic shell model description of the experimental Gamow-Teller transition strength distributions in $^{42}$Ti, $^{46}$Cr, $^{50}$Fe and $^{54}$Ni is presented. These transitions have been recently measured via $β$ decay of these $T_z$=-1 nuclei, produced in fragmentation reactions at GSI and also with ($^3${He},$t$) charge-exchange (CE) reactions corresponding to $T_z = + 1$ to $T_z = 0$ carried out at RCNP-Osaka.The calculations are performed in the $pf$ model space, using the GXPF1a and KB3G effective interactions. Qualitative agreement is obtained for the individual transitions, while the calculated summed transition strengths closely reproduce the observed ones.

# Shell model description of Gamow-Teller strengths in $pf$-shell nuclei

## Key claim
The shell model calculations using the KB3G and GXPF1a effective interactions in the $pf$ model space provide qualitative agreement for individual Gamow-Teller transitions in $^{42}$Ti, $^{46}$Cr, $^{50}$Fe, and $^{54}$Ni, while the calculated summed transition strengths closely reproduce the observed ones; however, for $^{54}$Ni a truncation to a maximum of four nucleon excitations from the $f_{7/2}$ shell was necessary, and the KB3G interaction in this truncated space fails to reproduce the experimental data.

## Method
The authors performed large-scale shell model diagonalizations in the $pf$ valence space using the NuShellX@MSU code, employing the KB3G and GXPF1a effective interactions. For $^{42}$Ti, $^{46}$Cr, and $^{50}$Fe the full $pf$ shell model space was used, while for $^{54}$Ni a truncation allowing a maximum of four nucleon excitations from the $f_{7/2}$ shell to the rest of the $pf$ orbitals was imposed due to computational limitations. The calculated Gamow-Teller strength distributions were compared with experimental data from $\beta$-decay measurements at GSI and ($^3$He,$t$) charge-exchange reactions at RCNP-Osaka, and a quenching factor of $q = 0.768 \pm 0.005$ was extracted from a comparison of experimental and theoretical $M(GT)$ matrix elements using the KB3G interaction.

## Key numbers
- Quenching factor $q = 0.768 \pm 0.005$, extracted from the slope of experimental versus theoretical $R(GT)$ values using the KB3G interaction.
- For $^{42}$Ti $\rightarrow$ $^{42}$Sc: experimental summed B(GT) strength from $\beta$-decay is 2.372, from charge-exchange reaction is 2.297; shell model with KB3G gives 6.000, with GXPF1a gives 6.000, and extreme single particle model (ESPM) gives 6.00.
- For $^{46}$Cr $\rightarrow$ $^{46}$V: experimental summed B(GT) strength from $\beta$-decay is 2.047, from charge-exchange reaction is 2.219; shell model with KB3G gives 7.231, with GXPF1a gives 7.613, and ESPM gives 10.70.
- For $^{50}$Fe $\rightarrow$ $^{50}$Mn: experimental summed B(GT) strength from $\beta$-decay is 1.344, from charge-exchange reaction is 1.859; shell model with KB3G gives 9.464, with GXPF1a gives 10.277, and ESPM gives 14.14.
- For $^{54}$Ni $\rightarrow$ $^{54}$Co: experimental summed B(GT) strength from $\beta$-decay is 1.082, from charge-exchange reaction is 1.117; shell model with KB3G gives 12.197, with GXPF1a gives 13.362, and ESPM gives 16.29.
- The dominant transition in $^{42}$Ti $\rightarrow$ $^{42}$Sc is to the $1_1^+$ state at experimental energy 611 keV; the second excited $1^+$ state at 1888 keV is missed in both calculations.
- For $^{50}$Fe $\rightarrow$ $^{50}$Mn, the intense isolated B(GT) transition to the first $1^+$ state is observed at 651 keV.

## Context
This work extends the systematic shell model studies of Gamow-Teller strengths in $pf$-shell nuclei previously reported by Caurier et al. (1999) using the KB3G interaction and by Honma et al. (2004) using the GXPF1a interaction, and complements the experimental results of Molina et al. (2014) and Adachi et al. (2014). The calculations provide a theoretical description of the $\beta$-decay measurements from fragmentation reactions at GSI and charge-exchange reactions at RCNP-Osaka, adding information to the work of Fujita et al. (2014). The results enable improved estimates of electron-capture rates for astrophysical applications and neutrino-nucleus cross sections for supernova neutrinos, as discussed in the context of Balasi et al. (2015).
