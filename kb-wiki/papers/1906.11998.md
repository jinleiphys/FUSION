# 30 years of jet quenching

> In the last 30 years, the physics of jet quenching has gone from an early stage of a pure theoretical idea to initial theoretical calculations, experimental verification and now a powerful diagnostic tool for studying properties of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP) in high-energy heavy-ion collisions. I will describe my collaboration with Miklos Gyulassy in this exciting area of high-energy nuclear physics in the past 30 years on this special occasion of his 70th birthday and discuss what is ahead of us in jet tomographic study of QGP in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
Jet quenching has evolved from a purely theoretical idea into a powerful tomographic probe of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), but the paper emphasizes that the field is now entering a stage requiring more precision studies, with many open questions remaining about color coherence, multiple gluon emissions with overlapping formation time, and jet-induced medium response.

## Method
The paper reviews the historical development of jet quenching theory, starting with the HIJING Monte Carlo model that combined jet production with an adjustable constant parton energy loss. It then describes the evolution of multiple theoretical frameworks for parton energy loss: the GW model for non-Abelian Landau-Pomeranchuk-Migdal (LPM) interference, the BDMPS approach relating energy loss to the jet transport coefficient \(\hat q\), the GLV opacity expansion, the higher-twist (HT) framework for medium-modified fragmentation functions, and the AMY approach using hard thermal loop resummation. The paper also presents the Linear Boltzmann Transport (LBT) model developed by the author and collaborators, which includes both elastic scattering and inelastic gluon bremsstrahlung with a Poisson distribution for multiple gluon emissions, and explicitly tracks recoil medium partons as "negative" partons to account for back-reaction.

## Key numbers
- The HIJING model with a default value of \(dE/dx = 1\) GeV/fm predicted charged hadron suppression in central \(Au+Au\) collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV that agreed with later RHIC results from PHENIX and STAR.
- For a quark with initial energy of 10 GeV, the jet transport parameter \(\hat q \approx 1.2 \pm 0.3\) GeV\(^2\)/fm at an initial time \(\tau_0 = 0.6\) fm/\(c\) in \(Au+Au\) collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV/n, and \(\hat q \approx 1.9 \pm 0.7\) GeV\(^2\)/fm in Pb+Pb collisions at \(\sqrt{s}=2.76\) TeV/n, as extracted by the JET Collaboration.
- The transverse jet energy loss at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 5.02\) TeV is about 15% larger than at \(\sqrt{s_{\rm NN}} = 2.76\) TeV in the \(p_T = 50-400\) GeV/\(c\) range for 0-10% central Pb+Pb collisions.
- The initial parton density at 5.02 TeV is about 20% higher than at 2.76 TeV.

## Context
This paper reviews 30 years of jet quenching research, extending the early theoretical work of Bjorken (1982) on elastic parton energy loss and the subsequent inelastic energy loss studies by Gyulassy and Pluemer (1990). It describes the development of multiple theoretical frameworks (BDMPS, GLV, HT, AMY, SCET\(_G\)) that form the foundation for modern jet tomography. The work enables future precision studies of QGP properties using jets, with open questions including higher-order correlations, color coherence, multiple gluon emissions with overlapping formation time, and the full potential of jet-induced medium response as a probe of QGP transport properties.
