# Short timescale behavior of colliding heavy nuclei at intermediate energies

> An Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics model is used to explore the collision of $^{114}$Cd projectiles with $^{92}$Mo target nuclei at E/A=50 MeV over a broad range in impact parameter. The atomic number (Z), velocity, and emission pattern of the reaction products are examined as a function of the impact parameter and the cluster recognition time. The non-central collisions are found to be essentially binary in character resulting in the formation of an excited projectile-like fragment (PLF$^*$) and target-like fragment (TLF$^*$). The decay of these fragments occurs on a short timescale, 100$\le$t$\le$300 fm/c. The average excitation energy deduced for the PLF$^*$ and TLF$^*$ `saturates for mid-central collisions, 3.5$\le$b$\le$6 fm, with its magnitude depending on the cluster recognition time. For short cluster recognition times (t=150 fm/c), an average excitation energy as high as $\approx$6 MeV is predicted. Short timescale emission leads to a loss of initial correlations and results in features such as an anisotropic emission pattern of both IMFs and alpha particles emitted from the PLF$^*$ and TLF$^*$ in peripheral collisions.

## Key claim

The Antisymmetrized Molecular Dynamics (AMD) model predicts that the average excitation energy of projectile-like and target-like fragments saturates for mid-central collisions (impact parameter 3.5 ≤ b ≤ 6 fm), but the magnitude of this saturation depends on the chosen cluster recognition time, with earlier recognition times yielding higher excitation energies.

## Method

The authors used the AMD model with the Gogny effective interaction to simulate collisions of ¹¹⁴Cd projectiles with ⁹²Mo targets at E/A = 50 MeV across impact parameters from 0 to 12 fm. They tracked the system evolution until 300 fm/c, applied a cluster recognition algorithm at selected times (150–300 fm/c), then propagated the resulting fragments along Coulomb trajectories and allowed statistical decay using the Pühlhofer sequential binary decay model (for Z < 20) and the GEMINI code (for Z ≥ 20). Approximately 25,000 collisions were simulated on a 646-CPU parallel system.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: E/A = 50 MeV for the ¹¹⁴Cd + ⁹²Mo system
- Impact parameter range sampled: 0 ≤ b ≤ 12 fm, with maximum impact parameter b_max = 12 fm
- Cluster recognition times examined: t = 150, 180, 210, 240, 270, and 300 fm/c
- For short cluster recognition time (t = 150 fm/c), average excitation energy as high as ≈ 6 MeV is predicted for the PLF*
- For t = 300 fm/c, average excitation energy saturates at ≈ 4 MeV for b ≤ 6 fm
- For the most peripheral collisions (b > 10 fm), ⟨Z_PLF*⟩ ≈ 48 (the atomic number of the projectile)
- For b ≤ 4 fm, ⟨Z_PLF*⟩ ≈ 19 with no further dependence on impact parameter
- For 8.5 < b ≤ 10 fm, the yield ratio of Z = 3–6 over Z = 45–47 is 0.31/0.37 ≈ 0.84
- For 7 < b ≤ 8.5 fm, the yield ratio of Z = 3–6 over Z = 41–43 is 0.66/0.19 ≈ 3.47
- The touching sphere configuration distance is 11.2 fm (R = 1.2 × (A_P¹ᐟ³ + A_T¹ᐟ³))
- Each collision required 12 to 24 CPU-hours on a single node

## Context

This work extends AMD model calculations previously applied to central Xe + Sn collisions at 50 MeV/nucleon (Ono, 2004) to a broader range of impact parameters for the Cd + Mo system. The study directly tests the validity of the common two-stage hybrid approach (dynamical model followed by statistical decay) by examining whether initial correlations survive the decay stage. The results enable future work with radioactive beams by quantifying how the N/Z ratio of an exotic projectile can be preserved while the system is excited, and the observed charge correlation patterns (asymmetric to symmetric splits with increasing centrality) match experimental observations by Davin (2002) and others.
