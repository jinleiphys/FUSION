# Solving a Set of Truncated Dyson-Schwinger Equations with a Globally Converging Method

> A globally converging numerical method to solve coupled sets of non-linear integral equations is presented. Such systems occur e.g. in the study of Dyson-Schwinger equations of Yang-Mills theory and QCD. The method is based on the knowledge of the qualitative properties of the solution functions in the far infrared and ultraviolet. Using this input, the full solutions are constructed using a globally convergent modified Newton iteration. Two different systems will be treated as examples: The Dyson-Schwinger equations of 3-dimensional Yang-Mills-Higgs theory provide a system of finite integrals, while those of 4-dimensional Yang-Mills theory at high temperatures are only finite after renormalization.

# Solving a Set of Truncated Dyson-Schwinger Equations with a Globally Converging Method

## Key claim

The paper presents a globally convergent numerical method for solving coupled sets of non-linear integral equations (specifically Dyson-Schwinger equations) that requires no prior input data, provided the qualitative asymptotic behavior of the solution functions in the far infrared and ultraviolet is known analytically. The method achieves global convergence through a modified Newton iteration with backtracking, combined with an outer iteration that refits the asymptotic coefficients.

## Method

The method represents dressing functions (ghost G, gluon Z, Higgs H) using analytical infrared power-law forms and ultraviolet perturbative forms, with the intermediate momentum region expanded in Chebychev polynomials of the logarithm of momentum. A global Newton method with backtracking (micro-cycles) solves for the Chebychev coefficients at the polynomial zeros, while an outer fix-point iteration (macro-cycles) determines the infrared asymptotic coefficients by requiring continuous matching between the infrared and numerical solutions. Super-cycles progressively reduce the infrared matching scale epsilon to approach the final solution. The method is applied to two systems: 3-dimensional Yang-Mills-Higgs theory (finite integrals) and 4-dimensional finite-temperature Yang-Mills theory (requiring renormalization).

## Key numbers

- Infrared ghost exponent: e_G = 1/2 for one solution branch, or e_G in (1/4, 1/2] for the other branch depending on the projector parameter zeta
- Infrared Higgs exponent: e_H = -1 (uniquely determined by the renormalized mass term)
- Gluon exponent e_Z related to ghost exponent by: e_G = -(1/2)(e_Z + 1/2)
- Ultraviolet perturbative coefficients: w_G = 1/16, w_Z = 9/64, w_H = 1/4 for 3-dimensional Yang-Mills theory
- Higgs mass parameter: m_h = 0.88 g_3^2 (fixed by lattice Monte Carlo calculations of Cucchieri (2001))
- Typical expansion range for 3-dimensional case: [epsilon, Lambda] = [10^{-2} g_3^2, 2 x 10^3 g_3^2]
- Number of Chebychev polynomials: N_ch = 30-40 for standard calculations, up to 100-500 for special purposes
- Integration grid: N_a = 80 angular points, N_delta + N_epsilon + N_p + N_Lambda = 60 + 100 + 100 + 60 = 320 radial points
- Integration cutoffs: [delta, Omega] = [10^{-5} g_3^2, 10^7 g_3^2]
- Largest system treated for finite-temperature case: N = 21 Matsubara modes (3N independent functions)
- Achieved mean deviation: |E| ~ 10^{-8} relative to tree-level terms of order 1

## Context

This work extends earlier approaches to solving Dyson-Schwinger equations in Yang-Mills theory, which used direct iteration (Hauck, 1996) or local Newton iteration requiring discretized input data (Fischer, 2002; Fischer, 2003). The method builds on the analytical infrared analysis of Zwanziger (2001) and the Chebychev expansion technique of Atkinson (1997) and Bloch (1995). The specific physical systems treated are the infinite-temperature limit of 4-dimensional Yang-Mills theory (3-dimensional Yang-Mills-Higgs) and finite-temperature 4-dimensional Yang-Mills theory in the Matsubara formalism, with detailed physics results presented in Maas (2004, 2005). The method enables systematic solution of truncated Dyson-Schwinger equations without requiring prior numerical solutions from discretized versions, and can be extended to other coupled non-linear integral equation systems where asymptotic behavior is known.
