# 10,000 Standard Solar Models: a Monte Carlo Simulation

> We have evolved 10,000 solar models using 21 input parameters that are randomly drawn for each model from separate probability distributions for every parameter. We use the results of these models to determine the theoretical uncertainties in the predicted surface helium abundance, the profile of the sound speed versus radius, the profile of the density versus radius, the depth of the solar convective zone, the eight principal solar neutrino fluxes, and the fractions of nuclear reactions that occur in the CNO cycle or in the three branches of the p-p chains. We also determine the correlation coefficients of the neutrino fluxes for use in analysis of solar neutrino oscillations. Our calculations include the most accurate available input parameters, including radiative opacity, equation of state, and nuclear cross sections. We incorporate both the recently determined heavy element abundances recommended by Asplund, Grevesse & Sauval (2005) and the older (higher) heavy element abundances recommended by Grevesse & Sauval (1998). We present best-estimates of many characteristics of the standard solar model for both sets of recommended heavy element compositions.

## Key claim

The paper uses Monte Carlo simulations of 10,000 solar models to provide a quantitative statistical basis for deciding whether solar model predictions agree or disagree with helioseismological and neutrino measurements, explicitly acknowledging that models using the newer lower heavy element abundances (AGS05) disagree strongly with helioseismology while models using the older higher abundances (GS98) agree well.

## Method

The authors evolved 10,000 standard solar models using a stellar evolution code, with 21 input parameters drawn randomly for each model from separate probability distributions. For each model, 19 scalar parameters (including 7 nuclear reaction rates, solar age, luminosity, diffusion coefficient, and 9 heavy element abundances) were randomly sampled, while radiative opacity and equation of state were treated as the remaining 2 input parameters with separate uncertainty evaluations. The simulations were performed for three composition choices: 5,000 models with AGS05 abundances and optimistic uncertainties, 5,000 models with GS98 abundances and conservative uncertainties, and 1,000 models with AGS05 abundances and conservative uncertainties.

## Key numbers

- 10,000 total solar models evolved, with 21 input parameters drawn from probability distributions
- 5,000 models used AGS05 abundances with optimistic uncertainties; 5,000 used GS98 abundances with conservative uncertainties; 1,000 used AGS05 abundances with conservative uncertainties
- p-p reaction cross section factor: 3.94 x 10^-25 MeV b with 0.4% uncertainty
- ^3He+^3He reaction: 5.4 MeV b with 6.0% uncertainty
- ^3He+^4He reaction: 0.53 keV b with 9.4% uncertainty
- ^7Be+p reaction: 20.6 eV b with 3.8% uncertainty
- hep reaction: 8.6 x 10^-20 keV b with 15.1% uncertainty
- ^14N+p reaction: 1.69 keV b with 8.4% uncertainty
- Solar age: 4.57 x 10^9 yr with 0.44% uncertainty
- Solar luminosity: 3.842 x 10^33 erg s^-1 with 0.4% uncertainty
- Diffusion coefficient: 1.0 (relative to Thoul et al. 1994 value) with 15.0% uncertainty
- Conservative abundance uncertainties for key elements: O = 0.17 dex, Ne = 0.24 dex, C = 0.13 dex, N = 0.14 dex
- Optimistic abundance uncertainties for key elements: O = 0.05 dex, Ne = 0.06 dex, C = 0.05 dex, N = 0.06 dex

## Context

This work extends the earlier Monte Carlo simulation by Bahcall (1988) which used 1,000 solar models and only 5 input parameters to determine uncertainties in solar neutrino predictions. The present paper provides the first full determination of correlation coefficients between predicted neutrino fluxes, going beyond previous power-law approximations used by Fogli et al. (1995, 2002). The results enable quantitative statistical comparisons between solar model predictions and helioseismological measurements, and provide correlation coefficients for improved analysis of solar neutrino oscillations. The paper explicitly addresses the dilemma that newer abundance determinations by Asplund, Grevesse and Sauval (2005) lead to models in strong disagreement with helioseismology, while older abundances by Grevesse and Sauval (1998) produce good agreement.
