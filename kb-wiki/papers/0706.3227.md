# Final state interactions and hadron quenching in cold nuclear matter

> I examine the role of final state interactions in cold nuclear matter in modifying hadron production on nuclear targets with leptonic or hadronic beams. I demonstrate the extent to which available experimental data in electron-nucleus collisions can give direct information on final state effects in hadron-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions. For hadron-nucleus collisions, a theoretical estimate based on a parton energy loss model tested in lepton-nucleus collisions shows a large effect on mid-rapidity hadrons at fixed target experiments. At RHIC energy, the effect is large for negative rapidity hadrons, but mild at midrapidity. This final state cold hadron quenching needs to be taken into account in jet tomographic analysis of the medium created in nucleus-nucleus collisions.

## Key claim

Final state interactions in cold nuclear matter produce a non-negligible suppression of hadron production in hadron-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions across the whole backward rapidity hemisphere up to RHIC energy, contrary to the common assumption that such effects are small; at the LHC they will be important only at very backward rapidity \(y-y_{cm}\lesssim 3\).

## Method

The author constructs a kinematic dictionary relating the variables of semi-inclusive deep inelastic scattering (DIS) and nucleon-nucleon (NN) collisions at leading order in perturbative QCD, using light-cone coordinates and collinear factorization. This dictionary is then used to map the phase spaces of existing lepton-nucleus experiments (HERMES, EMC) onto the equivalent NN phase spaces at SPS, FNAL, and RHIC energies. A parton energy loss model previously tuned to lepton-nucleus data is applied to estimate the magnitude of cold hadron quenching in hadron-nucleus collisions at various rapidities.

## Key numbers

* At RHIC top energy (\(\sqrt{s}=200\) GeV), the HERMES experiment (\(E_e=27.6\) GeV) covers less than one third of the available RHIC \(p_T\) range at \(y_1 \approx -3\), with shrinking \(p_T\) coverage at larger rapidity; in the SPS/FNAL midrapidity region it reaches \(p_T = 2.5\) GeV at most.
* The EMC experiment (\(E_e=100-280\) GeV) covers a larger rapidity span extending to \(y_1 \gtrsim 0\), but only the \(p_T \lesssim 3\) GeV region has been explored.
* At HERMES, the experimental acceptance \(0.07 < y < 0.85\) translates into a rapidity difference between outgoing partons of \(-1.1 < \Delta y_{|DIS} < 0.75\).
* For an observed parton with \(y_1=-2\) and \(p_T = 2\) GeV (middle of the HERMES DIS phase space), the allowed rapidity difference in NN collisions spans \(-2.5 < \Delta y_{|NN} < 6.6\).
* RHIC trajectories with \(y_h \lesssim -2\) span \(\nu \lesssim 60\) GeV and \(z_h \gtrsim 0.5\), where EMC and HERMES experiments have shown non-negligible cold nuclear matter suppression.

## Context

This work extends the kinematic comparison between DIS and hadron-nucleus collisions to quantify final state cold nuclear matter effects, building on the observed hadron suppression in lepton-nucleus experiments by Osborne (1978), Ashman (1991), and Airapetian (2000, 2003, 2007). It directly addresses the interpretation of the Cronin effect and forward rapidity suppression in deuteron-gold collisions at RHIC, which have been attributed variously to Color Glass Condensate (Kharzeev, 2004), initial state energy loss (Vitev, 2006), leading-twist shadowing (Vogt, 2004), Sudakov suppression (Kopeliovich, 2005), or final state parton recombination (Hwa, 2004). The paper enables a more rigorous separation of initial-state and final-state effects in jet tomographic analyses of the medium created in nucleus-nucleus collisions, and motivates a rebinning of existing EMC data in terms of NN variables to provide direct experimental measurements of final state nuclear effects at SPS and FNAL energies.
