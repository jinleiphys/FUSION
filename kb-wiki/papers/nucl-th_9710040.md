# A Dynmical study of Fission Process and estimation of Prescission Neutron Multiplicity

> The dynamics of fission has been studied by solving Euler-Lagrange equation with dissipation generated through one and two body nuclear friction. The average kinetic energies of the fission fragments, prescission neutron multiplicities and the mean energies of the prescission neutrons have been calculated and compared with experimental values and they agree quite well. A single value of friction coefficient has been used to reproduce the experimental data for both symmetric and asymmetric splitting of the fissioning systems over a wide range of masses and excitation energies. It has been observed that a stronger friction is required in the saddle to scission region as compared to that in the ground state to saddle region.

# A Dynamical Study of Fission Process and Estimation of Prescission Neutron Multiplicity

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a single value of the two-body viscosity coefficient (μ₀ = 4 × 10⁻²³ MeV·sec·fm⁻³) can reproduce experimental prescission neutron multiplicity data across a wide range of compound nuclear masses (A_CN ∼ 150–250) and excitation energies (E*_CN ∼ 60–160 MeV), but only when one-body wall friction (attenuated to 10% of its original value) is used in the ground-state-to-saddle region and two-body friction is used in the saddle-to-scission region. The model successfully reproduces symmetric fission data but requires an additional asymmetry-dependent factor h(α) = exp(−K α²) with K = 161 ± 3 to describe asymmetric fission data.

## Method

The authors solve Euler-Lagrange equations for the collective variables (elongation, neck radius, mass asymmetry) using a realistic shape parametrization by Brack et al. (1972) for heavy fissioning nuclei above the Businaro-Gallone point. Dissipation is included through one-body (wall) friction in the ground-state-to-saddle region and two-body friction (via the Werner-Wheeler method) in the saddle-to-scission region, with randomness introduced only at the initial level by assigning a random fraction of excitation energy to collective degrees of freedom. Neutron emission along each trajectory is simulated via Monte Carlo sampling using a Poisson process criterion, and observables are averaged over initial angular momenta and asymmetry parameters weighted by level densities.

## Key numbers

- Two-body viscosity coefficient: μ₀ = 4 × 10⁻²³ MeV·sec·fm⁻³, corresponding to 0.06 TP (1 TP = 6.24 × 10⁻²³ MeV·sec·fm⁻³)
- One-body wall friction attenuated to 10% of the original value in the ground-state-to-saddle region
- Scission point centre-to-centre separation: ∼18 fm for a representative compound system of mass A_CN = 200
- Fission time scales for symmetric fission: ∼(1–2) × 10⁻²⁰ sec
- Asymmetry-dependent friction factor: h(α) = exp(−K α²) with K = 161 ± 3, independent of compound system mass
- Reduced friction coefficient β values from earlier work cited: range 2 × 10⁻²¹ sec⁻¹ to 20 × 10⁻²¹ sec⁻¹ (Frobrich et al. used β = 2 × 10⁻²¹ sec⁻¹ for ground-state-to-saddle, reaching up to 30 × 10⁻²¹ sec⁻¹ at scission)
- Compound nuclear masses studied: A_CN ∼ 150–250
- Excitation energies studied: E*_CN ∼ 60–160 MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' earlier dynamical model (Dhara et al., 1995; Bhattacharya et al., 1995) from lighter systems below the Businaro-Gallone point to heavier fissioning nuclei, replacing a schematic two-spheres-plus-cylinder shape with the more realistic Brack et al. (1972) parametrization. It contrasts with stochastic approaches such as Langevin equation models (Abe et al., 1993; Frobrich et al., 1993) and Fokker-Planck equation methods (Pomorski et al., 1993) by using deterministic dynamics with initial-level randomization. The finding that a single friction coefficient works across a wide mass range supports the Werner-Wheeler prediction of a universal reduced friction coefficient, contrary to earlier system-dependent results (Mavlitov et al., 1992). The model enables systematic calculation of prescission neutron multiplicities, total kinetic energies, and mean neutron energies for both symmetric and asymmetric fission, and identifies the need for asymmetry-dependent friction form factors as an open question for future theoretical work.
