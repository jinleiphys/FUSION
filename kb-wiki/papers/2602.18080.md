# Observation of Robust and Coherent Non-Abelian Hadron Dynamics on Noisy Quantum Processors

> The real-time evolution of strongly interacting matter remains a frontier of fundamental physics, as classical simulations are hampered by exponential Hilbert space growth and rapid, unmanageable growth of quantum entanglement. This study reports the quantum simulation of hadron dynamics within a $(1+1)$-dimensional SU(2) lattice gauge theory using a 156-qubit IBM superconducting processor. Leveraging a hardware-efficient Loop-String-Hadron (LSH) encoding, we simulate the dynamics of the physical degrees of freedom on a $60$-site lattice in the weak-coupling regime, as a crucial step toward the continuum limit. The hardware data reveal confined meson propagation and early-time oscillations of the mesonic profile, from which we extract a breathing-mode frequency as a spectroscopic observable. Benchmarking against tensor-network simulations of the full LSH Hamiltonian and Pauli-propagation simulations of the noiseless circuit supports the validity of the physical approximation, the quantum algorithm and the observed dynamics within the accessible time window. These results show that physics-native encodings can enable scalable access to coherent non-Abelian real-time dynamics on noisy quantum hardware.

# Observation of Robust and Coherent Non-Abelian Hadron Dynamics on Noisy Quantum Processors

## Key claim
The authors report that quantum simulation of hadron dynamics in a (1+1)-dimensional SU(2) lattice gauge theory on a 156-qubit IBM processor yields coherent signals for confined meson propagation and internal oscillations, but only within an accessible early-time window where the weak-coupling approximation, the quantum algorithm, and the hardware implementation can be validated against classical baselines; the results do not claim advantage beyond this controlled regime.

## Method
The authors implement a Loop-String-Hadron (LSH) encoding of the SU(2) Kogut-Susskind Hamiltonian on a 60-site staggered lattice, using a weak-coupling approximated version of the LSH Hamiltonian that reduces circuit depth. They prepare two initial states (strong-coupling vacuum and a meson placed at the lattice center) on an IBM superconducting processor, evolve them via first-order Trotterization for up to 25 time steps with a fixed 10,000-shot budget per step, and extract observables through a differential measurement protocol that subtracts the vacuum signal from the mesonic signal. The QPU results are benchmarked against tensor-network simulations of the full LSH Hamiltonian using 2-site TDVP with bond dimension up to 200, and against Pauli-propagation simulations of the noiseless circuit with truncation thresholds.

## Key numbers
- 156 qubits used on the IBM superconducting processor
- 60-site staggered lattice simulated
- Coupling parameter x = 100 for the primary simulation (with x = 1/(g²a²))
- Circuit contained more than 17,000 two-qubit gates and 90,000 single-qubit gates
- 2-qubit gate depth reached 259 for 20 Trotter steps and 324 for 25 Trotter steps
- Fixed shot budget of 10,000 shots per Trotter step
- QPU runtime constant at 20 seconds per Trotter step
- Global charge Q conserved within 0.33% and net lattice flux q within 2% on the QPU
- Tensor-network calculation used maximum bond dimension D_max = 200 and bosonic cutoff 2j_max = 5
- Pauli propagation used coefficient threshold of 10⁻⁵ for the GPU implementation

## Context
This work extends the Loop-String-Hadron framework developed by Raychowdhury (2019) for SU(2) lattice gauge theory, building on prior quantum simulations of Abelian models such as U(1) and Z₂ lattice gauge theories by Martinez (2016), Yang (2020), and others. The results demonstrate that physics-native encodings can extract coherent non-Abelian dynamics from noisy quantum hardware, enabling future studies of hadron spectroscopy and real-time dynamics in regimes where classical tensor-network methods face an entanglement barrier and Pauli propagation faces exponential growth in non-Clifford complexity. The work provides a pathway toward simulating SU(3) gauge theory in higher dimensions on fault-tolerant devices.
