# Neutron-rich rare isotope production from projectile fission of heavy beams in the energy range of 20 MeV/nucleon

> We investigate the possibilities of producing neutron-rich nuclides in projectile fission of heavy beams in the energy range of 20 MeV/nucleon expected from low-energy facilities. We report our efforts to theoretically describe the reaction mechanism of projectile fission following a multinucleon transfer collision at this energy range. Our calculations are mainly based on a two-step approach: the dynamical stage of the collision is described with either the phenomenological Deep-Inelastic Transfer model (DIT), or with the microscopic Constrained Molecular Dynamics model (CoMD). The deexcitation/fission of the hot heavy projectile fragments is performed with the Statistical Mul- tifragmentation Model (SMM). We compared our model calculations with our previous experimental projectile-fission data of 238U (20 MeV/nucleon)+208Pb and 197Au (20 MeV/nucleon)+197Au and found an overall reasonable agreement. Our study suggests that projectile fission following periph- eral heavy-ion collisions at this energy range offers an effective route to access very neutron-rich rare isotopes toward and beyond the astrophysical r-process path.

# Neutron-rich rare isotope production from projectile fission of heavy beams in the energy range of 20 MeV/nucleon

## Key claim

Projectile fission following peripheral heavy-ion collisions at 20 MeV/nucleon may offer an effective route to access very neutron-rich rare isotopes toward and beyond the astrophysical r-process path, provided that the two-step model framework (dynamical stage plus statistical deexcitation) can adequately describe experimental data and allow dependable predictions of production rates.

## Method

The authors performed a two-stage theoretical calculation: the dynamical stage of peripheral and semiperipheral heavy-ion collisions was described using either the phenomenological Deep-Inelastic Transfer (DIT) model or the microscopic Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) model, and the subsequent deexcitation and fission of the hot heavy projectile fragments was treated with the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM). The calculations were compared to previously measured experimental projectile-fission data from 20 MeV/nucleon 238U+208Pb and 197Au+197Au reactions obtained at the NSCL A1200 fragment separator. Additionally, full CoMD calculations following the complete reaction dynamics for up to 15000 fm/c were performed for a subset of events.

## Key numbers

- 238U beam at 20 MeV/nucleon with current 0.05 particle nA (3.1×10^8 particles/sec) struck a 208Pb target of 5.8 mg/cm^2 thickness
- 197Au beam at 20 MeV/nucleon with current 0.5 particle nA (3.1×10^9 particles/sec) struck a 197Au target of 2 mg/cm^2 thickness
- A1200 spectrometer operated with angular acceptance ΔΩ = 0.8 msr (Δθ = 20 mr, Δφ = 40 mr) and momentum acceptance Δp/p = 3%
- Atomic number Z determination had resolution (FWHM) of 0.5 Z units; mass number A determination had resolution of about 0.7 A units
- Ionic charge q had resolution of 0.4 units (FWHM)
- Magnetic rigidity Bρ settings covered the range 1.7–2.0 Tm for U+Pb and 1.4–1.8 Tm for Au+Au
- CoMD calculations were terminated at t = 300 fm/c for the dynamical stage; full CoMD calculations followed evolution up to t = 15000 fm/c (5.0×10^-20 sec)
- For full CoMD calculations, impact parameters were chosen in the range b = 0–13 fm following a triangular distribution, with 1000 events collected per reaction
- Typical fission times for the quasiprojectile were in the range 2000–4000 fm/c
- An additional decay time t_decay = 5000 fm/c after scission was allowed for deexcitation of fission fragments
- The SMM freeze-out density was assumed to be 1/3 of normal nuclear matter density
- A multifragmentation threshold value ε*_mult = 0.2 MeV/nucleon was used as the optimum parameter for describing neutron-rich projectile fission fragments

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous experimental studies of projectile fission at 20 MeV/nucleon (Souliotis 1997, Souliotis 1999 for 238U+208Pb; Souliotis 1998, Souliotis 2002 for 197Au-induced reactions) and their theoretical framework for multinucleon transfer reactions at Fermi energies (Fountas 2014, Souliotis 2011). The approach builds on the DIT model of Tassan-Got and the CoMD model of Bonasera and Papa, combined with the SMM deexcitation model. The study is motivated by developments at facilities such as GANIL, Argonne, Texas A&M, and RISP that will offer intense heavy beams at 15–25 MeV/nucleon, and aims to enable predictions of production rates for selecting optimal projectile/target combinations and separator configurations for very neutron-rich nuclei.
