# Time-dependent density-functional description of nuclear dynamics

> We present the basic concepts and recent developments in the time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) for describing nuclear dynamics at low energy. The symmetry breaking is inherent in nuclear energy density functionals (EDFs), which provides a practical description of important correlations at the ground state. Properties of elementary modes of excitation are strongly influenced by the symmetry breaking and can be studied with TDDFT. In particular, a number of recent developments in the linear response calculation have demonstrated their usefulness in description of collective modes of excitation in nuclei. Unrestricted real-time calculations have also become available in recent years, with new developments for quantitative description of nuclear collision phenomena. There are, however, limitations in the real-time approach; for instance, it cannot describe the many-body quantum tunneling. Thus, we treat the quantum fluctuations associated with slow collective motions assuming that time evolution of densities are determined by a few collective coordinates and momenta. The concept of collective submanifold is introduced in the phase space associated with the TDDFT and used to quantize the collective dynamics. Selected applications are presented to demonstrate the usefulness and quality of the new approaches. Finally, conceptual differences between nuclear and electronic TDDFT are discussed, with some recent applications to studies of electron dynamics in the linear response and under a strong laser field.

## Key claim

The paper argues that time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) provides a feasible description of nuclear many-body dynamics at low energy, but the real-time approach cannot describe many-body quantum tunneling; therefore, quantum fluctuations associated with slow collective motions must be treated by assuming that time evolution of densities is determined by a few collective coordinates and momenta, introducing the concept of a collective submanifold in phase space to quantize the collective dynamics.

## Method

The authors review the theoretical framework of nuclear TDDFT, starting from the Kohn-Sham scheme and its extension to the Bogoliubov-de-Gennes-Kohn-Sham equations for superfluid systems. They discuss linear response calculations using the finite amplitude method and Green's function method for quasiparticle formalisms, as well as unrestricted real-time calculations for nuclear collision phenomena. For large-amplitude collective motions such as spontaneous fission and shape transitions, they introduce a collective submanifold in the TDDFT phase space to identify a small number of canonical variables for quantizing the collective dynamics.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: \(\rho_0 \approx 0.17\) fm\(^{-3}\)
- Binding energy per nucleon in infinite nuclear matter: \(B/A \approx 16\) MeV
- Fermi momentum at saturation: \(k_F \approx 1.36\) fm\(^{-1}\)
- Fermi energy (maximum kinetic energy): \(T_F \approx 40\) MeV
- Effective mass from saturation arguments: \(m^*/m \approx 0.4\) (noted to disagree with empirical values \(0.7 \lesssim m^*/m \lesssim 1\))
- Time scale for symmetry restoration in deformed nuclei: \(\tau_{\text{SSB}} \sim 10^{-20}\) to \(10^{-19}\) s
- Time scale for symmetry restoration in gauge space (pair rotation): \(\tau_{\text{SSB}} = 10^{-21}\) to \(10^{-20}\) s
- Periodic time of single-particle motion: \(\tau_F \sim 10^{-22}\) s

## Context

This review extends the concepts of nuclear density functional theory (DFT) and time-dependent DFT, which are analogous to electronic DFT used in atomic, molecular, and solid state physics, but with major conceptual differences due to spontaneous symmetry breaking in finite nuclei. The work builds on the unified model of Bohr and Mottelson (1969, 1975) and the self-consistent mean-field models reviewed by Bender, Heenen, and Reinhard (2003) and Lunney, Pearson, and Thibault (2003). It enables future applications to nuclear excitations including vibration, rotation, clustering, fusion, fission, particle transfer, and collective excitations in neutron star crusts and interiors.
