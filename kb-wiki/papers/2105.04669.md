# European Spallation Source: a future for Coherent Neutrino Nucleus Scattering

> The European Spallation Source (ESS), currently finishing its construction, will soon provide the most intense neutron beams for multi-disciplinary science. At the same time, it will also produce a high-intensity neutrino flux with an energy suitable for precision measurements of Coherent Elastic Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering. We describe some physics prospects, within and beyond the Standard Model, of employing innovative detector technologies to take the most out of this large flux. We show that, compared to current measurements, the ESS will provide a much more precise understanding of neutrino and nuclear properties.

## Key claim

The European Spallation Source (ESS) will provide a neutrino flux larger by about one order of magnitude than the COHERENT experiment's source at Oak Ridge, enabling precision measurements of Coherent Elastic Neutrino-Nucleus Scattering (CEνNS) that are currently limited by statistical uncertainties.

## Method

The paper describes the physics prospects of using the ESS, a neutron spallation source that produces neutrinos through pion decay at rest, to measure CEνNS with innovative low-threshold detectors placed approximately 20 m from the target. It evaluates two specific scenarios: constraining non-standard neutrino interactions (NSI) via four-fermion operators at dimension 6, and measuring neutron radii of nuclei through the CEνNS cross-section's sensitivity to neutron distributions.

## Key numbers

- The ESS neutrino flux is larger than the COHERENT source at Oak Ridge Spallation Neutron Source by about one order of magnitude.
- Coherent scattering requires momentum transfer |q| less than approximately 50 MeV for medium-sized nuclei.
- The expected integrated CEνNS rate is calculated for detectors placed 20 m away from the ESS target, with nuclear recoil thresholds in the few keV to sub-keV range.
- After 3 years of data taking at the ESS, 90% confidence level bounds on vector NSI parameters (ε_{ee}^{u,V}, ε_{μμ}^{u,V}) would significantly tighten present constraints.
- The ESS sensitivity on neutron radii of various nuclei reaches the order of the spread among theoretical nuclear physics model predictions.

## Context

This work extends the first detection of CEνNS by the COHERENT collaboration (Akimov, 2017), which was limited by statistical uncertainties, and builds on the facility design described by Baxter (2019). The ESS measurements would provide model-independent neutron radius determinations complementary to proton densities from elastic electron scattering (Erler, 2012; Tanihata, 2013), with implications for neutron star properties (Lattimer, 2004; Lattimer, 2006) and first-principles nuclear calculations (Tsang, 2012).
