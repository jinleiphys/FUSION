# Relativistic Approach to Isoscalar Giant Resonances in 208Pb

> We calculate the longitudinal response of 208Pb using a relativistic random-phase approximation to three different parameterizations of the Walecka model with scalar self-interactions. From a nonspectral calculation of the response-that automatically includes the mixing between positive- and negative-energy states-we extract the distribution of strength for the isoscalar monopole, dipole, and high-energy octupole resonances. We employ a consistent formalism that uses the same interaction in the calculation of the ground state as in the calculation of the response. As a result, the conservation of the vector current is strictly maintained throughout the calculation. Further, at small momentum transfers the spurious dipole strength-associated with the uniform translation of the center-of-mass-gets shifted to zero excitation energy and is cleanly separated from the sole remaining physical fragment located at an excitation energy of about 24 MeV; no additional dipole strength is observed. The best description of the collective modes is obtained using a ``soft'' parameterization having a compression modulus of K=224 MeV.

# Relativistic Approach to Isoscalar Giant Resonances in 208Pb

## Key claim

Using a fully consistent relativistic random-phase approximation (RPA) that includes the mixing between positive- and negative-energy states, the isoscalar giant dipole resonance (ISGDR) in 208Pb appears as a single physical fragment at about 24 MeV excitation energy at low momentum transfer, contrary to recent claims of a large discrepancy between theory and experiment. The best description of the collective modes is obtained with a "soft" parameterization having a compression modulus of K=224 MeV, though the agreement with experiment is "by no means perfect."

## Method

The longitudinal response of 208Pb was calculated using a relativistic RPA applied to three parameterizations of the Walecka model with scalar self-interactions (L2, NLB, and NLC). A nonspectral calculation of the response was employed that automatically includes the mixing between positive- and negative-energy states, using the same interaction for both the ground state mean-field calculation and the residual particle-hole interaction in the RPA. The isoscalar monopole, dipole, and high-energy octupole strength distributions were extracted from the longitudinal response by isolating the relevant J-pi channel at specific momentum transfers.

## Key numbers

- The "soft" parameterization NLC has a compression modulus of K=224 MeV, compared to K=547 MeV for the linear model L2 and K=421 MeV for NLB
- At momentum transfer q=46 MeV (0.23 fm^-1), the ISGDR in the NLC model appears at E=24.4 MeV; the experimental value is 22.4 +/- 0.5 MeV (Davis et al., 1997)
- The isoscalar giant monopole resonance (GMR) energies for the three models are: L2 at 20.1 MeV, NLB at 18.1 MeV, and NLC at 13.1 MeV; the experimental value is 14.2 +/- 0.1 MeV (Youngblood et al., 1999)
- The high-energy octupole resonance (HEOR) energies are: L2 at 25.1 MeV, NLB at 23.4 MeV, and NLC at 21.9 MeV; the experimental value is 19.7 +/- 0.5 MeV (Davis et al., 1997)
- The GMR energies scale as the square root of the compressibility, with ratios of 1:1.38:1.53 compared to the square root of K ratios of 1:1.37:1.56
- The NLC parameterization yields an upper limit for neutron star mass of M=2.8 solar masses

## Context

This work extends the relativistic RPA formalism of Dawson and Furnstahl (1990) by incorporating scalar self-interactions (non-linear terms) into the calculation of the nuclear response, which had previously been applied only to linear models. It directly addresses the claim by Ma and collaborators (1997, 1999) that "a large discrepancy remains between theory and experiment in the case of the dipole compression mode," showing instead that when the full momentum dependence of the longitudinal response is included, a single physical fragment emerges at low momentum transfer. The results enable constraints on the nuclear matter compression modulus (around K=224 MeV) and provide a connection to neutron star properties through the equation of state, suggesting that neutron stars may harbor exotic states of matter given the narrow observed mass distribution of M=(1.35 +/- 0.04) solar masses (Thorsett and Chakrabarty, 1999).
