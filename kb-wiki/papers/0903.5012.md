# Correlation between Charge Inhomogeneities and Structure in Graphene and Other Electronic Crystalline Membranes

> Only one atom thick and not inclined to lattice defects, graphene represents the ultimate crystalline membrane. However, its structure reveals unique features not found in other crystalline membranes, in particular the existence of ripples with wavelength of 100-300 Angstroms. Here, I trace the origin of this difference to the free electrons in the membrane. The deformation energy of the lattice creates a coupling between charge fluctuations and the structure, resulting in ripples on the membrane, correlated with charge inhomogeneities. In graphene this mechanism reproduces the experimental result for both charge puddles and ripples.

## Key claim

The ripples (wavelength 100–300 Å) and charge puddles observed in graphene arise because graphene is an *electronic* crystalline membrane, where free π-band electrons couple to lattice deformations via a deformation potential; this mechanism reproduces both phenomena simultaneously, whereas purely thermal crystalline-membrane theory (scale-invariant, with no ripples at these wavelengths) cannot.

## Method

The author models the membrane with an elastic free energy (bending rigidity κ, Lamé coefficients μ and λ) plus a deformation-potential coupling \(D\,u_{ii}\,\delta n\) between the in-plane strain and the fluctuating electron density. The electron–electron interaction is taken as a screened Coulomb term with static dielectric constant ℰ ≈ 3–4. After integrating out the in-plane displacement field and the charge fluctuations, an effective free energy for the out-of-plane height field \(h\) is obtained, with a wavevector-dependent Young modulus \(K(q)\) that becomes negative in a finite range around \(q_0\). The normal–normal correlation function \(G(q)\) is computed in a one-loop approximation, and its peak position and width are used to predict ripple wavelength and amplitude.

## Key numbers

- Ripple wavelength predicted: **100–300 Å**, matching the experimental range of Meyer et al. (2007) and others.
- Ripple amplitude: **3–10 Å** (experimental value, reproduced by the model).
- Bending rigidity of graphene: **κ ≈ 1.1 eV**.
- Bulk modulus: **λ+μ ≈ 7.3 eV Å⁻²**; shear modulus: **μ ≈ 5.7 eV Å⁻²**; resulting Young modulus **K₀ ≈ 13 eV Å⁻²**.
- Deformation potential: **D ≈ 29 eV** (from charge-carrier mobility measurements).
- Static dielectric constant: **ℰ ≈ 3–4** (from Kotov et al., 2008).
- Thermal length scale in graphene: **λ_T ≈ 12 Å**.
- The electronic length scale \(\xi_0 = 2\pi/q_0\) is estimated as **≈ 20 Å** for D=29 eV and ℰ=4.

## Context

This work extends the theory of crystalline membranes (Nelson, 1987; David, 1988; Aronovitz, 1988; Bowick, 2001) by adding the free-electron degree of freedom, which is absent in standard models that predict only scale-invariant, thermally roughened surfaces. It directly addresses the discrepancy between those predictions and the ripples observed in free-standing graphene by Meyer et al. (2007) and others. The mechanism predicts that charge puddles (correlation length ≈300 Å, as measured by Martin et al., 2008) are correlated with Gaussian curvature, not mean curvature (contrary to Kim, 2008). The results enable future simultaneous measurements of topography and charge density on suspended graphene and suggest that insulating graphane (Elias, 2009) should lack such ripples.
