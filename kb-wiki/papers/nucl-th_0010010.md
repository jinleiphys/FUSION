# Parity Violating Measurements of Neutron Densities and Nuclear Structure

> Parity violating electron nucleus scattering is a clean and powerful tool for measuring the spatial distributions of neutrons in nuclei with unprecedented accuracy. Parity violation arises from the interference of electromagnetic and weak neutral amplitudes, and the $Z^0$ of the Standard Model couples primarily to neutrons at low $Q^2$. Experiments are now feasible at existing facilities. We show that theoretical corrections are either small or well understood, which makes the interpretation clean. A neutron density measurement may have many implications for nuclear structure, atomic parity nonconservation experiments, and the structure of neutron stars.

## Key claim

Parity-violating electron scattering can measure neutron densities in nuclei with unprecedented accuracy, and theoretical corrections (such as Coulomb distortions, meson exchange currents, and dispersion corrections) are either small or well understood, making the interpretation clean, but the largest correction, from Coulomb distortions, is of order \(Z\alpha/\pi\) and reaches 20% for \({}^{208}\)Pb.

## Method

The parity-violating asymmetry \(A_{LR}\) in elastic polarized electron-nucleus scattering is computed in Born approximation, where it is proportional to the ratio of the neutron and proton form factors \(F_n(Q^2)/F_p(Q^2)\). Coulomb distortion corrections are calculated by numerically solving the Dirac equation for an electron moving in Coulomb and axial-vector weak potentials, from which phase shifts yield all elastic scattering observables including the asymmetry. Additional small corrections from meson exchange currents, parity admixtures in the ground state, dispersion corrections, the neutron electric form factor, strange quarks, and the dependence of the extracted radius on surface shape are discussed but found to be small.

## Key numbers

- The root-mean-square neutron radius in \({}^{208}\)Pb, \(R_n\), is thought to be about 0.25 fm larger than the proton radius \(R_p \approx 5.45\) fm.
- Coulomb distortion corrections are of order \(Z\alpha/\pi\), which is 20% for \({}^{208}\)Pb.
- The parity-violating asymmetry is of order a part per million.

## Context

This work extends the suggestion by Donnelly, Dubach and Sick (1989) that parity-violating electron scattering can measure neutron densities, building on experimental progress from the early SLAC experiment (Prescott et al., 1979), the Bates \({}^{12}\)C experiment (Souder et al., 1990), Mainz \({}^{9}\)Be experiment (Heil et al., 1989), SAMPLE (Mueller et al., 1997), and HAPPEX (Aniol et al., 1999). The approach contrasts with hadronic probes such as pion or proton elastic scattering (Ray and Hoffmann, 1985), which suffer from potentially serious theoretical systematic errors. A neutron density measurement would enable precise low-energy Standard Model tests when combined with atomic parity nonconservation experiments (Pollock et al., 1992; Chen and Vogel, 1993), and would inform the equation of state of dense matter relevant to neutron star radii (e.g., Geminga and RX J185635-3754).
