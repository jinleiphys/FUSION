# Isospin effects on the mass dependence of balance energy

> We study the effect of isospin degree of freedom on balance energy throughout the mass range between 50 and 350 for two sets of isotopic systems with N/Z = 1.16 and 1.33 as well as isobaric systems with N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4. Our findings indicate that different values of balance energy for two isobaric systems may be mainly due to the Coulomb repulsion. We also demonstrate clearly the dominance of Coulomb repulsion over symmetry energy.

## Key claim
The difference in balance energy between two isobaric systems (same mass, different N/Z) is mainly due to Coulomb repulsion, which dominates over the symmetry energy contribution throughout the mass range from 50 to 350.

## Method
The authors used the isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (IQMD) model with a soft equation of state including momentum-dependent interactions (SMD) and an anisotropic standard isospin- and energy-dependent nucleon-nucleon cross section (σ = 0.8 σ_NN^free). They simulated collisions for isotopic systems with N/Z = 1.16 and 1.33, and isobaric systems with N/Z = 1.0 and 1.4, at semicentral impact parameters (0.35–0.45 of the maximum) over incident energies from 40 to 150 MeV/nucleon in steps of 10 MeV/nucleon.

## Key numbers
- Power law exponent τ = -0.44 ± 0.01 for isotopic systems with N/Z = 1.16 (less neutron content) and τ = -0.42 ± 0.01 for N/Z = 1.33 (more neutron content).
- Power law exponent τ = -0.45 ± 0.01 for isobaric systems with N/Z = 1.4 and τ = -0.50 ± 0.01 for N/Z = 1.0.
- When Coulomb was reduced by a factor of 100, τ became -0.28 ± 0.02 for N/Z = 1.4 and -0.25 ± 0.02 for N/Z = 1.0.
- The theoretical calculations reproduced experimental balance energy for ⁵⁸Ni + ⁵⁸Ni and ⁵⁸Fe + ⁵⁸Fe within 3% on average over all colliding geometries.

## Context
This work extends the power-law mass dependence studies of balance energy by Sood and Puri (2009) and Magestro (2000), who reported exponents near -0.45 and deviations from the earlier -1/3 value of Mota (1997). The authors show that isospin effects are negligible for isotopic systems but significant for isobaric systems, and they demonstrate that Coulomb repulsion, not symmetry energy, is the dominant cause. This clarifies the relative importance of reaction mechanisms in isospin-dependent collective flow and supports the interpretation of Daffin (2000) regarding Coulomb dominance.
