# Pulse Modulation as a Signature of the Asteroid-Neutron Star Collision Model for High-Energy Transients

> Asteroid-neutron star collision models have been proposed as possible sources of high-energy transients, such as gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and fast radio bursts (FRBs). The sequence of events following the impact of the asteroid and finally dissolving into the neutron star can have several other observable consequences. We propose that due to the development of the off-diagonal moment of inertia (MI) components, the merger's aftermath can lead to the wobbling of the pulsar (assuming the neutron star happens to be a pulsar). Using sample values of various parameters, viz., size, shape, the locations of the deposits, and the pre-existing pulsar deformation parameter ($η$), we calculate the detailed pulse profile modulation of the pulsar. We observe a distinct pattern of pulse profile modulation on a characteristic timescale enhanced by a factor of $1/η$ compared to the pulse timing. Importantly, even small changes in the MI components, of order $ε$, can produce large pulse profile modulations of order $ε/η$ (depending on the relative location of asteroid material deposition). Thus, if an asteroid-neutron star collision is responsible for a high-energy transient, the associated pulse profile modulation may serve as a falsifiable observational signature of such an event.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that asteroid-neutron star collisions, which have been suggested as sources of gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and fast radio bursts (FRBs), can produce a falsifiable observational signature in the form of pulse profile modulation of the pulsar, with the modulation amplitude enhanced by a factor of \(\epsilon/\eta\) relative to the perturbation size, where \(\eta\) is the pulsar's pre-existing oblateness and \(\epsilon\) is the fractional change in moment of inertia.

## Method

The authors adopt the Colgate and Petschek (1981) model of asteroid-neutron star collisions, where asteroid material is deposited as two rectangular slabs at magnetic conjugate points on the pulsar crust. They compute the perturbed moment of inertia tensor from these deposits, diagonalize it to find the new principal axes, and solve Euler's equations for the resulting precessional motion. Using a standard conical emission geometry with a Gaussian pulse profile, they numerically integrate the rotational dynamics to simulate the temporal evolution of the observed flux and quantify the pulse profile modulation.

## Key numbers

- Asteroid mass of \(\sim 10^{18}\) g is used as the canonical value for the collision model.
- Each deposited slab has dimensions: length \(l \simeq 2.5\ \mathrm{km}\), depth \(d \simeq 20\ \mathrm{m}\), width \(w \simeq 100\ \mathrm{m}\), with mass density of order \(10^6\ \mathrm{g~cm^{-3}}\).
- The fractional perturbation in moment of inertia from the asteroid is \(\epsilon \simeq (m / M_\odot) \simeq 10^{-15}\).
- For simulations, the authors adopt \(\eta = 10^{-2}\) and \(\epsilon = 10^{-5}\) due to computational constraints, yielding a flux modulation timescale \(T_\Omega \simeq 0.1\ \mathrm{s}\) for a millisecond pulsar.
- The tilt angle \(\theta_0\) of the new principal axis ranges from \(7.4 \times 10^{-6}\) rad (for \(\delta = 90^\circ\), \(\phi_A = 90^\circ\)) to \(10^{-3}\) rad (for \(\delta = 90^\circ\), \(\phi_A = 45^\circ\)).
- The maximum fractional flux change \((\delta F/F)\) is of order \(\simeq 10^{-3}\).
- The precession frequency reduces to \(\Omega \simeq \eta \omega\) for \(\epsilon \ll \eta\), giving a modulation timescale \(T_\Omega \simeq T_\omega / \eta\).

## Context

This work extends the asteroid-neutron star collision model of Colgate and Petschek (1981) by proposing a new observational signature beyond the prompt burst emission. It builds on the methodology developed by Bagchi et al. (2022) for studying pulse profile modulations from phase transitions in neutron star cores, applying it to the case of asteroid impacts. The results enable future searches for correlated pulse profile modulations following high-energy transients, potentially providing a falsifiable test of the collision model for GRBs and FRBs, and complementing existing observational constraints on pulsar precession from Stairs et al. (2000), Jones (2012), and Haskell (2015).
