# Absorbing-boundary-condition method for drip-line nuclei

> Absorbing-boundary-condition method and its applications to nuclear responses and breakup reactions are reported. The method facilitates calculations of the continuum states in the coordinate space of many degrees of freedom. Properties of nuclei near drip lines are discussed.

## Key claim

The absorbing-boundary-condition (ABC) method enables continuum calculations for nuclear systems with many degrees of freedom by replacing the infinitesimal imaginary part in the Green's function with a finite, coordinate-dependent function that is zero in the interacting region and positive outside, allowing the use of vanishing boundary conditions while still obtaining correct wave functions in the region where interactions occur.

## Method

The ABC method solves the Schrödinger equation with a source term by replacing the standard infinitesimal \(i\epsilon\) with a finite, coordinate-dependent \(i\epsilon(\mathbf{r})\) that is zero in the interacting region and positive outside it. For nuclear breakup reactions, the method solves an inhomogeneous three-body scattering equation with localized source terms, using the ABC to impose vanishing boundary conditions on a finite spatial grid. For giant dipole resonance calculations, the ABC is incorporated into time-dependent Hartree-Fock (TDHF) simulations on a three-dimensional coordinate grid, where the linear response is computed by applying an isovector-dipole impulse and Fourier transforming the time-dependent expectation values.

## Key numbers

- Phase shifts for nucleon scattering from a square-well potential (\(V_0 = -20\) MeV, \(r_0 = 3\) fm) calculated with the ABC method are identical to those from the standard numerical procedure.
- For the \(^{11}\)Be-\(^{12}\)C breakup reaction, the radial region extends to 30 fm for projectile-target coordinate \(R\) and 50 fm for neutron-core coordinate \(r\); the ABC is non-zero in the region 20 fm \(< R <\) 30 fm and 25 fm \(< r <\) 50 fm.
- The elastic breakup cross section from the ABC calculation is substantially larger than the eikonal approximation at incident energies below 50 MeV/A.
- For the \(^{14}\)Be giant dipole resonance calculation, the model space is a sphere of radius 22 fm, the ABC is zero for \(r < 10\) fm and non-zero for \(r > 10\) fm, and time evolution is carried out up to \(T = 10\ \hbar/\)MeV.
- The \(E1\) oscillator strength calculation for \(^{14}\)Be uses a smoothing parameter of \(\Gamma = 1\) MeV and predicts a large deformation splitting of about 10 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the ABC method, previously developed for chemical reactions (Seideman and Miller, 1992), to nuclear physics problems involving weakly-bound drip-line nuclei. The method provides an alternative to the continuum discretized coupled channel (CDCC) approach for breakup reactions, with which the authors' deuteron breakup results agree, and overcomes difficulties in constructing Green's functions for deformed nuclei in random-phase approximation (RPA) calculations (Shlomo and Bertsch, 1975). The results enable quantum-mechanical treatment of three-body continuum problems at low incident energies where the eikonal approximation fails, and suggest that significant damping widths in giant dipole resonances may be a peculiar feature of drip-line nuclei.
