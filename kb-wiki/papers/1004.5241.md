# Numerical solution of the Boltzmann equation for the collective modes of trapped Fermi gases

> We numerically solve the Boltzmann equation for trapped fermions in the normal phase using the test-particle method. After discussing a couple of tests in order to estimate the reliability of the method, we apply it to the description of collective modes in a spherical harmonic trap. The numerical results are compared with those obtained previously by taking moments of the Boltzmann equation. We find that the general shape of the response function is very similar in both methods, but the relaxation time obtained from the simulation is significantly longer than that predicted by the method of moments. It is shown that the result of the method of moments can be corrected by including fourth-order moments in addition to the usual second-order ones and that this method agrees very well with our numerical simulations.

## Key claim

The numerical solution of the Boltzmann equation using the test-particle method yields a relaxation time for collective modes that is significantly longer than that predicted by the method of moments when only second-order moments are included; however, the agreement becomes very good once fourth-order moments are incorporated into the method of moments.

## Method

The authors solved the Boltzmann equation for a two-component normal-fluid Fermi gas in a spherical harmonic trap using the test-particle (pseudoparticle) method, where the continuous phase-space distribution is represented by a sum of delta functions (or Gaussians for smoothing). The classical equations of motion for the test particles were integrated with the velocity Verlet algorithm, and collisions between opposite-spin particles were simulated stochastically using the free s-wave cross section, with Pauli blocking implemented via occupation numbers computed from the Gaussian-smoothed distribution. The method was applied to compute the response of the system to a delta-function perturbation, and the time evolution of the quadrupole moment was Fourier-transformed to extract the mode frequency and damping rate.

## Key numbers

* For a system of N = 10000 atoms with interaction strength 1/(k_F a) = -0.5 at T/T_F = 0.2, the energy distribution from the simulation after t = 30/ω_0 showed satisfactory agreement with the initial Fermi distribution, using N_test = 50000 test particles, Gaussian widths w_r = 1.5 l_ho and w_p = 1.5/l_ho, and time step Δt = 0.02/ω_0.
* The simulated collision rate with Pauli blocking agreed well with the exact result for temperatures above approximately 0.35 T_F at 1/(k_F a) = -1.0, and above 0.3 T_F at 1/(k_F a) = -0.1.
* The energy conservation in the simulation was better than |⟨E⟩(t) − ⟨E⟩(0)|/⟨E⟩(0) ≈ 10^{-5} for all times considered when collisions were included.
* The quadrupole mode frequency shifted from ω = 2ω_0 in the collisionless regime (high temperature) toward ω = √2 ω_0 in the hydrodynamic regime (low temperature), with the spectrum broadened at intermediate temperatures.

## Context

This work extends the semi-analytical method-of-moments approach used by Massignan and Bruun (2006), Bruun and Smith (2007), Riedl et al. (2008), and Chiacchiera et al. (2009) by providing a direct numerical solution of the Boltzmann equation. It also builds on the numerical test-particle method previously applied to trapped Fermi gases by Toschi et al. (2006) and to nuclear heavy-ion collisions by Bertsch and Das Gupta (1988). The results enable more accurate predictions for collective mode damping in ultracold Fermi gases, particularly in regimes where the relaxation-time approximation fails, and can be extended to include anharmonic trap potentials and expansion dynamics.
