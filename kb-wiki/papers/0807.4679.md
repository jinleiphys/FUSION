# Relations Involving Static Quadrupole Moments of $2^+$ states and B(E2)'s

> We define the ``quadrupole ratio'' $r_Q = \dfrac{Q_0(S)}{Q_0(B)}$ where $Q_0(S)$ is the intrinsic quadrupole moment obtained from the static quadrupole moment of the $2_1^+$ state of an even-even nucleus and $Q_0(B)$ the intrinsic quadrupole moment obtained from $B(E2)_{0 \to 2}$. In both cases we assume a simple rotational formula connecting the rotating frame to the laboratory frame. The quantity $r_Q$ would be one if the rotational model were perfect and the energy ratio $E(4)/E(2)$ would be 10/3. In the simple vibrational model, $r_Q$ would be zero and $E(4)/E(2)$ would be two. There are some regions where the rotational limit is almost met and fewer where the vibrational limit is also almost met. For most cases, however, it is between these two limits, i.e. $0 < r_Q < 1$. There are a few cases where $r_Q$ is bigger than one, especially for light nuclei.

# Relations Involving Static Quadrupole Moments of 2⁺ states and B(E2)'s

## Key claim

The paper defines a "quadrupole ratio" r_Q = Q_0(S)/Q_0(B) that quantifies how closely a nucleus follows the rotational model (r_Q = 1) versus the vibrational model (r_Q = 0), but the abstract's claim that most nuclei fall between these limits (0 < |r_Q| < 1) is qualified by the finding that a few light nuclei yield r_Q > 1, and that the analysis is limited to nuclei up to ²⁰⁶Pb because Stone's table of measured Q(2⁺) values does not extend beyond that point.

## Method

The authors extracted Q(2⁺) values from Stone's compilation, selecting the latest measurement for each nucleus, and B(E2) values from the consensus table of Raman et al. (2001). They then computed the intrinsic quadrupole moments Q_0(S) and Q_0(B) using rotational-model formulas connecting laboratory-frame and intrinsic-frame quantities, and formed the ratio r_Q = Q_0(S)/Q_0(B) = -1.1038 × Q(2⁺)/√[B(E2)₀→₂]. The energy ratio E(4)/E(2) was also tabulated for comparison with the rotational limit (10/3) and vibrational limit (2).

## Key numbers

- The rotational limit corresponds to r_Q = 1 and E(4)/E(2) = 10/3 ≈ 3.33; the vibrational limit corresponds to r_Q = 0 and E(4)/E(2) = 2.
- For ⁴²Ca, the f₇/₂ single-particle model yields r_Q = 0.78, compared to the experimental value of 1.023.
- Nuclei in the mass range 112–120 (including ¹¹²Sn, ¹¹⁸Sn, ¹²⁰Sn, ¹²²Sn, ¹²⁴Sn, and ¹²⁸Te) show r_Q near zero and E(4)/E(2) close to 2, nearly realizing the vibrational model.
- Nuclei in the mass range 152–186 (including ¹⁴⁶Nd, ¹⁴⁸Nd, ¹⁵⁰Nd, ¹⁵²Sm, ¹⁵⁴Sm, Gd isotopes, ¹⁶⁰Dy, ¹⁶⁴Dy, Er isotopes, Yb isotopes, ¹⁸²W, ¹⁸⁴W, ¹⁸⁶W, most Os isotopes, and ²⁰²Hg) show r_Q close to 1 and E(4)/E(2) close to 10/3, nearly realizing the rotational model.
- In the midrange region from about mass 70 to 110, the quadrupole ratio is around 0.5 and E(4)/E(2) lies between 2 and 3.

## Context

This work extends the 2006 study by Robinson et al. (2006), which considered only light nuclei, to essentially all even-even nuclei where Q(2⁺) has been measured. The results provide a systematic survey against which microscopic calculations can be compared, such as those using the Gogny interaction by Bertsch et al. (2007), Skyrme Hartree-Fock calculations by Jaqaman et al. (1984), and the random-matrix studies of Zelevinsky and Volya (2004), who found that r_Q tends to be either 1 or 0 with high probability. The compilation also enables future comparisons with phase-transition studies in heavier nuclei, such as the platinum isotopes work by Morales et al. (2008).
