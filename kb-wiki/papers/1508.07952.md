# Evaluation of the forward Compton scattering off protons: I. Spin-independent amplitude

> We evaluate the forward Compton scattering off the proton, based on Kramers-Kronig kind of relations which express the Compton amplitudes in terms of integrals of total photoabsorption cross sections. We obtain two distinct fits to the world data on the unpolarized total photoabsorption cross section, and evaluate the various spin-independent sum rules using these fits. For the sum of proton electric and magnetic dipole polarizabilities, governed by the Baldin sum rule, we obtain the following average (between the two fits): $α_{E1}+β_{M1}=14.0(2)\times 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{fm}^3$. An analogous sum rule involving the quadrupole polarizabilities of the proton is evaluated too. The spin-independent forward amplitude of proton Compton scattering is evaluated in a broad energy range. The results are compared with previous evaluations and the only experimental data point for this amplitude (at 2.2 GeV). We remark on sum rules for the elastic component of polarizabilities.

## Key claim

The paper provides updated evaluations of the forward spin-independent Compton amplitude and associated sum rules for the proton, but the authors explicitly note that the existing database of unpolarized total photoabsorption cross sections is not entirely consistent, leading them to produce two distinct fits (fit I and fit II) whose differences reflect a systematic uncertainty rather than a single definitive result.

## Method

The authors used Kramers-Kronig dispersion relations to express the real part of the forward spin-independent Compton amplitude as an integral over the total photoabsorption cross section. They constructed two separate fits to the world data on the unpolarized total photoabsorption cross section: fit I used MAID partial-wave analysis plus LEGS and Armstrong data, while fit II used SAID partial-wave analysis plus MacCormick data; both fits incorporated Bartalini data and high-energy Regge parameterizations. The integrals were then evaluated numerically to obtain the Baldin sum rule, the fourth-order sum rule, and the full forward amplitude over a broad energy range.

## Key numbers

* Baldin sum rule (sum of electric and magnetic dipole polarizabilities): weighted average between the two fits gives α_E1 + β_M1 = 14.0(2) × 10⁻⁴ fm³
* Fit I Baldin sum rule: 14.29 ± 0.27 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
* Fit II Baldin sum rule: 13.85 ± 0.22 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
* Fourth-order sum rule (involving quadrupole polarizabilities): weighted average 6.04(4) × 10⁻⁴ fm⁵; fit I gives 6.08 ± 0.12 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁵, fit II gives 6.01 ± 0.11 × 10⁻⁴ fm⁵
* Real part of the forward amplitude at ν = 2.2 GeV: fit I gives −10.35 μb·GeV, fit II gives −9.97 μb·GeV, compared to the experimental value of −12.3 ± 2.4 μb·GeV from Alvensleben et al. (1973)
* Low-energy region contribution to Baldin sum rule: fit I = 6.12 ± 0.12 × 10⁻⁴ fm³, fit II = 6.06 ± 0.12 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
* Medium-energy region contribution to Baldin sum rule: fit I = 7.53 ± 0.13 × 10⁻⁴ fm³, fit II = 7.15 ± 0.08 × 10⁻⁴ fm³
* High-energy region contribution to Baldin sum rule: 0.64 ± 0.02 × 10⁻⁴ fm³ for both fits

## Context

This work updates the earlier evaluations of the forward Compton amplitude by Damashek and Gilman (1970) and Armstrong et al. (1972), which had remained the only full amplitude evaluations despite substantial additions to the photoabsorption database. The newer Baldin sum rule evaluations by Babusci et al. (1998) and the A2 Collaboration (Olmos et al., 2010) obtained lower values than the older works, and the present paper shows this difference arises from systematic inconsistencies between older and newer experimental data, particularly the 1996 DAPHNE@MAMI experiment of MacCormick et al. superseding the 1972 Armstrong experiment. The results enable future comparisons with planned dilepton photoproduction experiments at MAMI and provide a model-independent constraint on higher-order polarizabilities that can test chiral perturbation theory calculations such as those by Lensky et al. (2009) and Holstein et al. (1999).
