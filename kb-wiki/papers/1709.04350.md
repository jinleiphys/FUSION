# Towards microscopic studies of survival probabilities of compound superheavy nuclei

> The microscopic approach of fission rates and neutron emission rates in compound nuclei have been applied to $^{258}$No and $^{286}$Cn. The microscopic framework is based on the finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations, in which the fission barriers and mass parameters are self-consistently temperature dependent. The fission rates from low to high temperatures can be obtained based on the imaginary free energy method. The neutron emission rates are obtained with neutron gases at surfaces. Finally the survival probabilities of superheavy nuclei can be calculated microscopically. The microscopic approach has been compared with the widely used statistical models. Generally, there are still large uncertainties in descriptions of fission rates.

## Key claim

The authors propose a fully microscopic framework for calculating survival probabilities of compound superheavy nuclei based on finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS theory, but acknowledge that large uncertainties remain in the description of fission rates compared to neutron emission rates, and that the microscopic results for $^{258}$No underestimate the experimental survival probability.

## Method

The authors performed finite-temperature Skyrme-Hartree-Fock+BCS calculations to obtain temperature-dependent fission barriers and collective inertia mass parameters for $^{258}$No and $^{286}$Cn. Fission rates were computed using the imaginary free energy method, which treats barrier tunneling at low temperatures and barrier reflection at high temperatures. Neutron emission rates were derived from the self-consistently calculated neutron gas density at the nuclear surface, without invoking level densities or free parameters. The microscopic results were compared with conventional statistical model calculations using the Bohr-Wheeler fission formula and Fermi-gas level densities.

## Key numbers

- For $^{258}$No at excitation energy $E^* = 56.9$ MeV, the microscopic calculation yields neutron emission width $\Gamma_n = 2.09 \times 10^{-2}$ MeV and fission width $\Gamma_f = 2.22 \times 10^{-2}$ MeV, giving a survival probability $\Gamma_n/(\Gamma_n+\Gamma_f) = 0.515$, which is smaller than the experimental value of $0.840 \pm 0.050$ at $E^* = 61$ MeV from Peterson (2009).
- For $^{286}$Cn, the experimental lower limit on the survival probability is $7 \times 10^{-11}$ from Itkis (2015), and the microscopic results are generally comparable to the statistical model but larger due to a very small curvature $\omega_0$ at the equilibrium point.
- The level density parameter at the saddle point is taken as $a_{s.d.} = 1.1A/12$ MeV$^{-1}$ in the statistical model, while the ground-state parameter is $a = A/12$ MeV$^{-1}$.
- The pairing correction for even-even nuclei adopts $\delta = 12/\sqrt{A}$.
- The barrier curvature in the statistical model is fixed at $\hbar\omega_{s.d.} = 2.2$ MeV following Zubov (2005) and Xia (2011).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous microscopic studies of fission rates (Zhu 2016) and neutron emission rates (Zhu 2014) to the calculation of survival probabilities for superheavy nuclei, providing a parameter-free alternative to conventional statistical models such as those of Zubov (2002, 2005) and Xia (2011). The approach enables future improvements through multi-dimensional fission calculations and better effective nuclear forces, as noted by Xiong (2016), and addresses the open question of whether fission barriers and level density parameters are temperature-dependent, raised by Swiatecki (2008) and Adamian (2010).
