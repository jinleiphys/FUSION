# Realistic modelling of jets in heavy-ion collisions

> The reconstruction of jets in heavy-ion collisions provides insight into the dynamics of hard partons in media. Unlike the spectrum of single hadrons, the spectrum of jets is highly sensitive to $\hat{q}_{\perp}$, as well as being sensitive to partonic energy loss and radiative processes. We use MARTINI, an event generator, to study how finite-temperature processes at leading order affect dijets.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that the dijet asymmetry observed in heavy-ion collisions can be explained not primarily by strong energy loss, but by enhanced decollimation of jets caused by the combination of elastic scattering and radiative processes in the medium, where radiation evolves partons to small momentum fractions that are then more easily deflected by elastic scatterings.

## Method

The authors used MARTINI, a Monte Carlo event generator for high-momentum observables in heavy-ion collisions, to simulate jet evolution through a thermal medium. Jets were sampled using PYTHIA8, evolved according to finite-temperature leading-order rates (both elastic 2→2 scattering with hard thermal loop approximation and radiative processes with LPM resummation), and propagated through a 3+1-dimensional relativistic hydrodynamic medium from MUSIC. After hadronization, jets were reconstructed using the anti-kT algorithm via FASTJET to match experimental techniques.

## Key numbers

- Dijet asymmetry distribution dN/dAj in 0-10% centrality Pb+Pb collisions at 2.76 TeV is flat from Aj = 0 up to Aj = 0.5, significantly different from proton-proton collisions where the distribution is strongly peaked at Aj = 0
- Using αs ≈ 0.3 with only elastic processes does not produce a large average dijet asymmetry
- Using αs ≈ 0.3 with both elastic and radiative processes simultaneously yields agreement with ATLAS and CMS dijet asymmetry data
- For the nuclear modification factor RAA in 0-5% centrality at the LHC, a slow rise with pT is observed consistent with the LPM effect
- The momentum scale of splitting vertices is given by ⟨|kT|⟩ = (q̂ k)^(1/4)

## Context

This work extends the study of jet quenching from single-hadron observables to dijet observables, showing that jet spectra are highly sensitive to q̂⊥ and radiative processes in ways that single-hadron spectra are not. The authors build on prior work by Arnold, Moore, and Yaffe (2002) for LPM resummation, and on the MARTINI event generator framework from Schenke, Jeon, and Gale (2009). The results enable more realistic modeling of jet modification in heavy-ion collisions by demonstrating that full Monte Carlo solution of integral equations (rather than Fokker-Planck approximations) is necessary to correctly describe the distribution of partons at small momentum fractions, and that finite-size effects and running coupling must be incorporated for accurate RAA predictions.
