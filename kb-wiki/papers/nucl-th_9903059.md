# Testing the Space-Time Structure of Event Generators

> 16 pages LaTeX, including 5 postscript figures. To appear in the Proceedings of ``RHIC Physics and Beyond - Kay Kay Gee Day'' (Brookhaven National Laboratory, 23 Oct 1998), ed. by B. Muller and R.D. Pisarski, AIP Conference Proceedings

## Key claim

The paper presents two alternative algorithms ("classical" and "quantum") for extracting Bose-Einstein correlation functions from event generator output that includes space-time information, but notes that the ambiguity between these prescriptions must be resolved by physical consistency requirements rather than theoretical derivation alone.

## Method

The authors implemented two algorithms to compute two-pion Bose-Einstein correlations from the phase-space points generated by the VNI event generator: a "classical" algorithm that treats the generated points as a discrete approximation to the Wigner density, and a "quantum" algorithm that associates minimum-uncertainty wave packets with each generated point. They tested both algorithms on the analytical Zajc model, which allows controlled variation of position-momentum correlations, and then applied the quantum algorithm to simulated e+e- collisions at LEP I (91.5 GeV) and LEP II (183 GeV) energies using VNI.

## Key numbers

- The hadronization condition in VNI requires partons to get closer than 0.8 fm in coordinate space (in their pair rest frame) to form a hadronic cluster.
- The Zajc model analysis shows that for the classical algorithm, the bin width epsilon must satisfy epsilon << sqrt(2) * P0, where P0 is the width of the generated momentum distribution.
- The quantum algorithm introduces a wave packet width sigma, with the constraint that sigma < R_HBT (the measured HBT radius) from data.
- Numerical tests used 50 events of multiplicity 1000 to demonstrate agreement between analytical and numerical results.
- The simulated e+e- collisions were at sqrt(s) = 91.5 GeV for Z0 events and sqrt(s) = 183 GeV for W+W- events.

## Context

This work extends the Parton Cascade Model of Klaus Kinder-Geiger, implemented in the VNI event generator, by adding an afterburner for Bose-Einstein correlations. It contrasts with simpler approaches like JETSET (Lonnblad and Sjostrand, 1995) and PYTHIA, which reproduce single-particle yields and spectra without space-time information. The paper enables multidimensional Bose-Einstein correlation analyses that can test the space-time dynamics predicted by VNI against high-statistics data samples such as the approximately 20 million hadronic Z0 decays collected at LEP I.
