# Novel insight into centre-vortex geometry in four dimensions

> Centre-vortex surfaces are mapped out in four dimensions within the framework of SU(3) lattice gauge theory to understand the role of secondary loops that develop in three-dimensional visualisations of centre-vortex structure, appearing separate from the percolating cluster. Loops that initially appear disconnected in three-dimensional slices can originate from the same connected surface in four dimensions depending on the surface's curvature. For the first time, these secondary loops are identified as "connected" or "disconnected" with respect to the vortex sheet, allowing new insight into the evolution of centre-vortex geometry through the finite-temperature phase transition. At low temperatures, we find that secondary loops of any length primarily lie in the same sheet percolating the four-dimensional volume. Only a handful of small secondary sheets disconnected from the percolating sheet are identified. Above the phase transition, the vortex structure is still found to be dominated by a single large sheet but one that has aligned with the temporal dimension. With the near absence of any curvature orthogonal to the temporal dimension, connected secondary loops become vanishingly rare. Other novel quantities, such as the four-dimensional density of secondary sheets and the sheet sizes themselves, are analysed to build a complete picture of centre-vortex geometry in four dimensions.

## Key claim

Secondary vortex loops that appear disconnected in three-dimensional slices of the lattice are shown to originate from the same connected four-dimensional sheet when the sheet has curvature, and this connectedness changes dramatically across the deconfinement phase transition. The claim is hedged by the fact that this behavior depends on the sheet's curvature in four dimensions, and that above the phase transition connected secondary loops become vanishingly rare due to the near absence of curvature orthogonal to the temporal dimension.

## Method

Centre-vortex sheets were identified in four-dimensional SU(3) lattice gauge theory by extending the three-dimensional cluster-finding algorithm: for each nontrivial plaquette pierced by a vortex, connecting plaquettes were found by checking the faces of elementary cubes in both orthogonal directions, then iterating recursively. Visualisations were produced with two colour schemes, one colouring by three-dimensional loop identity and one by four-dimensional sheet identity, to compare the apparent and true connectedness of vortex structures across six ensembles with temporal extents Nτ = 64, 12, 8, 6, 5, and 4, corresponding to temperatures from 0.10 Td to 1.65 Td.

## Key numbers

- The proportion of vortices belonging to the largest (primary) sheet sits consistently above 99% across the full temperature range, with a drop just above Td.
- The four-dimensional density of secondary sheets is approximately constant at about 1/3 fm⁻⁴ across all temperatures, except for a spike just above Td.
- The smallest secondary sheet size is six vortices (a 1×1×1 elementary cube on the dual lattice); the largest secondary sheet found below Td comprised 129 vortices, while above Td sheets with greater than several hundred vortices appear.
- The normalised maximum Euclidean distance d_norm for the primary sheet equals exactly 1 at every temperature, confirming percolation in four dimensions at all temperatures.

## Context

This work extends the three-dimensional slice analysis of centre-vortex geometry in Mickley (2024) by identifying the full four-dimensional sheet structure for the first time in SU(3). It builds on prior studies of centre-vortex percolation and the deconfinement phase transition in Engelhardt (1999), Langfeld (1998), and Bertle (1999), and on the visualisation techniques developed in Biddle (2019, 2023). The finding that secondary loops are predominantly connected to the percolating sheet at low temperatures but disconnected at high temperatures enables a deeper understanding of how centre-vortex geometry evolves through the phase transition, and suggests that the proliferation of small loops observed with dynamical fermions in Biddle (2023) may arise from additional fluctuations in the four-dimensional sheet structure.
