# Concavity for nuclear binding energies, thermodynamical functions and density functionals

> Sequences of experimental ground-state energies for both odd and even $A$ are mapped onto concave patterns cured from convexities due to pairing and/or shell effects. The same patterns, completed by a list of excitation energies, give numerical estimates of the grand potential $Ω(β,μ)$ for a mixture of nuclei at low or moderate temperatures $T=β^{-1}$ and at many chemical potentials $μ.$ The average nucleon number $<{\bf A} >(β,μ)$ then becomes a continuous variable, allowing extrapolations towards nuclear masses closer to drip lines. We study the possible concavity of several thermodynamical functions, such as the free energy and the average energy, as functions of $<{\bf A} >.$ Concavity, which always occur for the free energy and is usually present for the average energy, allows easy interpolations and extrapolations providing upper and lower bounds, respectively, to binding energies. Such bounds define an error bar for the prediction of binding energies. Finally we show how concavity and universality are related in the theory of the nuclear density functional.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that experimental nuclear ground-state binding energies, after removing pairing staggering and adding a minimal parabolic correction, form a concave pattern that allows extrapolations providing lower bounds and interpolations providing upper bounds to unknown binding energies; the concavity of the average energy as a function of average nucleon number is not guaranteed by thermodynamic theorems but holds numerically for most temperatures tested.

## Method
The authors take experimental ground-state binding energies for tin isotopes (A=110 to 134) and apply two empirical corrections: a fixed pairing correction of 1250 keV added to each even-nucleus energy, and a parabolic term of 75 keV times (A-122)^2 added to all energies. They then construct the grand partition function using the first 10 excited states of each isotope, compute the grand potential, average nucleon number, average energy, and free energy as functions of chemical potential at fixed temperatures (60 keV and 150 keV), and invert to express these as functions of the continuous variable ⟨A⟩.

## Key numbers
- Pairing correction parameter p = 1250 keV applied to even Sn isotopes to remove staggering
- Parabolic correction coefficient P = 75 keV with A₀ = 122 used to ensure concavity
- Extrapolation for ¹¹⁰Sn from concave pattern gave overbinding of 36 keV, compared to raw extrapolation underbinding of 2614 keV
- Extrapolation for ¹³²Sn from concave pattern gave overbinding of 586 keV, compared to raw extrapolation underbinding of 2064 keV
- For ¹¹⁷Sn, extrapolation from lower masses gave overbinding of 271 keV (concave) versus 2671 keV (raw); interpolation gave underbinding of 133 keV (concave) versus overbinding of 1192 keV (raw)
- For ¹³⁵Sn, the lower bound estimate from the formula 3E′_A − 2E′_{A−1} gave −1111320 keV, compared to the predicted value of −1113208 keV from Ref. Wap03
- Suggested temperature T ∼ 150 keV gives an error bar of ∼ 500 keV between energy and free energy curves

## Context
This work extends the long tradition of nuclear mass formulae dating back to Weizsäcker (1935) and continuing with modern approaches such as Cakir (2005), by providing a thermodynamic framework that uses experimental data directly rather than parametric fits. The concavity property connects to the requirement of universality in nuclear density functional theory as pursued by the UNEDF collaboration (2007). The method enables extrapolations toward the neutron and proton drip lines where experimental data are scarce, with error bars defined by the band between the average energy and free energy curves.
