# Excitation of Color Degrees of Freedom of Nuclear Matter and $J/ψ$ Suppression

> In high energy nuclear collisions, the conventional Glauber model is commonly used to evaluate the contribution to $J/ψ$ suppression originating from the inelastic interaction with colorless bound nucleons. This requires an effective value for the $J/ψ$-nucleon absorption cross section which is larger than theoretically expected. On the other hand, multiple nucleon-nucleon collisions mediated by color exchange interactions, excite their color degrees of freedom. We investigate the importance of this effect and find that these excited states provide a larger cross section for $J/ψ$ absorption. We conclude that the related corrections are important to explain the effective value extrapolated from experiment.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the conventional Glauber model for J/ψ suppression in high-energy nuclear collisions must be corrected to account for the excitation of color degrees of freedom in nucleons after multiple nucleon-nucleon collisions, because these colored states provide a larger cross section for J/ψ absorption. However, the authors acknowledge that this color mechanism alone cannot fully explain the experimental suppression observed in Pb+Pb collisions at the CERN-SPS, and that the analysis of proton-nucleus data remains inconclusive due to large experimental uncertainties and competing effects such as gluon anti-shadowing.

## Method

The authors calculate the inelastic cross section for charmonium (Ψ) scattering on nucleons in different color SU(3) representations (singlet, octet, and decuplet) using the two-gluon exchange model of Low and Nussinov, with light-cone wave functions for the charmonium and nucleon. They then analyze proton-nucleus (pA) collision data from the NA3, NA38/51, and E866 experiments by fitting an effective absorption cross section within the Glauber model, both with and without corrections for gluon anti-shadowing in nuclei. Finally, they extend the analysis to nucleus-nucleus (AB) collisions by deriving the probability distribution for a nucleon to be in each color state after multiple scatterings, using an evolution equation for the color density matrix of the three-quark system.

## Key numbers

- The absorption cross section for Ψ on a color octet nucleon is 7.8 mb, which is 35% larger than the singlet value of 5.8 mb
- The absorption cross section for Ψ on a color decuplet nucleon is 9.9 mb, which is 70% larger than the singlet value
- For pA collisions with Ψ momentum 50 GeV, the effective absorption cross section from Glauber fits (without anti-shadowing corrections) averages to 3.5 ± 0.6 mb, while with anti-shadowing corrections the average is 5.7 ± 0.6 mb
- The theoretical expectation for the effective cross section on singlet nucleons (including formation time effects) is 3.8 mb, and for octet nucleons it is 5.1 mb
- The retardation length L for the charmonium to separate from its parent projectile is 2 fm for 50 GeV charmonia from 200 GeV protons
- The charmonium formation time τ_f is 0.3 fm, calculated as (m_ψ' - m_J/ψ)^{-1}
- The inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross section σ^{in}_{NN} used in the color evolution calculation is 30 mb

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of J/ψ suppression beyond the conventional Glauber model, which requires an effective J/ψ-nucleon absorption cross section larger than theoretically expected. The authors build on earlier work by Kopeliovich and Zakharov (1991), Hüfner, Kopeliovich, and Nemchik (1996), and Hüfner and Kopeliovich (1996) on formation length effects, and on Hüfner and Kopeliovich (1998) which first mentioned that colliding nucleons become colored but neglected the effect. The paper also references the two-gluon exchange model of Low and Nussinov (1975) and calculations by Hüfner, Ivanov, Kopeliovich, and Tarasov for realistic charmonium-nucleon cross sections. The results enable future work to incorporate color dynamics into more complete models of J/ψ suppression that also include quark-gluon plasma effects and comover interactions, though the authors note that the present treatment cannot explain the full Pb+Pb suppression data.
