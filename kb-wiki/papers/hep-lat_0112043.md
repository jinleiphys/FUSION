# The Gluon Propagator without lattice Gribov copies on a finer lattice

> We extend our study of the gluon propagator in quenched lattice QCD using the Laplacian gauge to a finer lattice. We verify the existence of a pole mass as we take the continuum limit and deduce a value of $\sim 600^{+150}_{-30}$ MeV for this pole mass. We find a finite value of $(454(5){\rm MeV})^{-2}$ for the renormalized zero-momentum propagator, in agreement with results on coarser lattices.

## Key claim

The authors find strong evidence for a pole in the gluon propagator with a mass of approximately 600 MeV, but the systematic error is asymmetric (-30, +150 MeV) due to the ambiguity of analytic continuation to negative momentum squared.

## Method

The authors performed quenched lattice QCD simulations using the Laplacian gauge to avoid Gribov copy ambiguities, extending their previous work to a finer lattice at beta=6.2 (24^3 x 48 lattice). They measured the transverse gluon propagator D(q^2) and extrapolated the inverse propagator to negative q^2 using polynomial fits and Cornwall's analytic model to determine the pole mass, also checking consistency with a time-slice correlator measurement.

## Key numbers

- Pole mass estimate: ~600^{+150}_{-30} MeV, obtained from continuum extrapolation combining beta=5.8, 6.0, and 6.2 data
- Renormalized zero-momentum propagator: (454(5) MeV)^{-2} at renormalization point 1.943 GeV
- Lattice spacing at beta=6.2: a^{-1}=2.718 GeV (from string tension sqrt(sigma)=440 MeV)
- Scaling ratio of lattice spacings: a_{beta=6.2}/a_{beta=6.0} = 0.758 +/- 0.017
- Continuum pole mass from quadratic polynomial fit: 632(38) MeV
- Continuum pole mass from Cornwall's ansatz: 592(14) MeV
- Pole mass from time-slice correlator direct fit: 739(81) MeV

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study (paperI) on coarser lattices to a finer lattice, confirming the existence of a gluon pole mass. The results contradict infrared-enhanced propagator forms like 1/q^4 (Pennington) and Gribov-type infrared suppression (Stingl), while supporting Cornwall's dynamically generated gluon mass model. The pole mass of ~600 MeV is close to one third of the glueball mass (1.73 GeV from Morningstar) and falls within the range needed for phenomenological studies of hadron-hadron scattering (Parisi, pomeron models of Landshoff and Nachtmann).
