# Hadron Structure and Modern Spectroscopy

> The colour, flavour, spin and J^PC of glueballs and hybrid mesons and baryons are constructed in an intuitive manner in both the gluon counting and adiabatic definitions. Glueball decay, production and mixing and hybrid meson decay selection rules and production are clarified.

## Key claim

The paper argues that the existence of gluonic excitations (glueballs and hybrid mesons) has overturned the traditional taxonomy of hadrons, but the only robust experimental result is the discovery of a scalar glueball whose exact mass location remains unpinned down; the evidence for hybrid mesons is strong but not yet definitive.

## Method

The author constructs the colour, flavour, spin, and JPC quantum numbers of glueballs and hybrid mesons and baryons using both the gluon counting definition (enumerating explicit gluon content) and the adiabatic definition (quarks moving on excited adiabatic potentials from fixed quark positions). Decay selection rules are derived from connected decay topologies with spin-1 quark-antiquark pair creation, and production mechanisms are classified by naive power counting in the strong coupling constant alpha_S.

## Key numbers

* Primitive scalar glueball mass from lattice QCD: 1.6 +/- 0.1 GeV, close to the observed f0(1500) and fJ(1710) states.
* The decay pi2(1670) to b1 pi has a branching ratio less than 0.2% at the 2 sigma confidence level, supporting the spin selection rule for conventional mesons.
* The Isgur-Godfrey relativized quark model expects only two scalar resonances below 1.7 GeV, while more than two states are observed in that mass region.
* The low-lying TE hybrid mesons in the gluon counting definition yield four states, while the adiabatic definition yields eight low-lying hybrids.

## Context

This lecture paper reviews and synthesizes the phenomenology of gluonic excitations, extending the quark model of Close (1979) and lattice QCD results from Sharpe (1999) and Richards (2000). It clarifies the relationship between the gluon counting and adiabatic definitions of hybrids, and explains selection rules originally noted by Lipkin, LeYaouanc, Oliver, Pene, and Raynal (1988-89). The work enables experimental searches for hybrid mesons and glueballs by providing clear decay signatures and production mechanisms that distinguish gluonic excitations from conventional hadrons.
