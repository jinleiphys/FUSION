# Sensitivity of the Moment of Inertia of Neutron Stars to the Equation of State of Neutron-Rich Matter

> The sensitivity of the stellar moment of inertia to the neutron-star matter equation of state is examined using accurately-calibrated relativistic mean-field models. We probe this sensitivity by tuning both the density dependence of the symmetry energy and the high density component of the equation of state, properties that are at present poorly constrained by existing laboratory data. Particularly attractive is the study of the fraction of the moment of inertia contained in the solid crust. Analytic treatments of the crustal moment of inertia reveal a high sensitivity to the transition pressure at the core-crust interface. This may suggest the existence of a strong correlation between the density dependence of the symmetry energy and the crustal moment of inertia. However, no correlation was found. We conclude that constraining the density dependence of the symmetry energy - through, for example, the measurement of the neutron skin thickness in 208Pb - will place no significant bound on either the transition pressure or the crustal moment of inertia.

## Key claim

The authors find that despite analytic expectations of a strong correlation between the density dependence of the symmetry energy and the crustal moment of inertia, no such correlation actually exists; consequently, constraining the symmetry energy through measurements like the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb will not place significant bounds on either the transition pressure at the core-crust interface or the crustal moment of inertia.

## Method

The authors used three accurately-calibrated relativistic mean-field models (NL3, MS, and FSUGold) to compute neutron-star equations of state, then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations within the slow-rotation approximation of Hartle and Thorne to obtain stellar moments of inertia. They tuned two empirical parameters, ζ controlling the high-density behavior of the equation of state and Λ_v controlling the density dependence of the symmetry energy, and derived an analytic expression for the crustal moment of inertia to test correlations with the transition pressure at the core-crust interface.

## Key numbers

- NL3 model predicts a maximum neutron star mass of 2.78 solar masses, a radius of 15.05 km for a 1.4 solar mass star, and a transition pressure at the core-crust interface of 0.212 MeV fm^{-3}
- MS model predicts a maximum neutron star mass of 1.81 solar masses, a radius of 13.78 km for a 1.4 solar mass star, and a transition pressure of 0.216 MeV fm^{-3}
- FSUGold model predicts a maximum neutron star mass of 1.72 solar masses, a radius of 12.66 km for a 1.4 solar mass star, and a transition pressure of 0.402 MeV fm^{-3}
- The Vela pulsar glitch analysis requires at least 1.4% of the total moment of inertia to reside in the solid crust
- The analytic expression for crustal moment of inertia is accurate to a few percent, with corrections of order P_t/ℰ_t ≲ 0.01

## Context

This work extends the study of correlations between neutron-star properties and laboratory observables previously explored by Horowitz and Piekarewicz (2001), who found a strong correlation between the neutron skin thickness of 208Pb and the neutron star radius. The paper contradicts the expectation, suggested by Link and collaborators (1999) and Lattimer and Prakash (2006), that the crustal moment of inertia would be strongly correlated with the density dependence of the symmetry energy. The results imply that the Parity Radius Experiment (PREx) at Jefferson Laboratory, which aims to measure the neutron radius of 208Pb, will not provide significant constraints on the crustal moment of inertia, leaving pulsar glitch observations and other astrophysical measurements as complementary probes of the equation of state.
