# Spatial and Momentum Imaging of the Pion and Kaon

> We determine the leading Fock state light front wave functions (LFWFs) of the pion and kaon via light front projections of the covariant Bethe-Salpeter wave function. Using these LFWFs we study the multi-dimensional images of the valence quarks in the pion and kaon that are provided by their generalized parton distribution functions (GPDs) and transverse momentum dependent parton distribution functions (TMDs). Moments of the GPDs are taken to obtain the electromagnetic and gravitational form factors of the pion and kaon, and comparisons to available experimental and lattice data are made. Highlights from this study include predictions that the mean-squared impact parameter for the quarks in the pion and kaon are: $\langle \vec{b}_T^2\rangle_{u}^π=0.11$fm$^2$, $\langle \vec{b}_T^2\rangle_{s}^K=0.08$fm$^2$, and $\langle \vec{b}_T^2\rangle_{u}^K=0.13$fm$^2$, and therefore the $s$ quark in the kaon is much closer to the center of transverse momentum than the $u$ quark. From the electromagnetic and gravitational form factors we find that the light-cone energy radii are about 60\% smaller than the light-cone charge radii for each quark sector in the pion and kaon. A quantitative measure of the importance of the leading Fock state is obtained via comparison with a full DSE calculation (containing an infinite tower of Fock states) for the pion form factor.

## Key claim

The leading Fock state light front wave functions (LFWFs) of the pion and kaon, obtained from covariant Bethe-Salpeter wave functions via Dyson-Schwinger equations (DSE), provide multi-dimensional images of valence quarks through generalized parton distribution functions (GPDs) and transverse momentum dependent parton distribution functions (TMDs), but the leading Fock state alone cannot fully describe the electromagnetic form factor at all momentum transfers because higher Fock states and vertex dressing effects are needed.

## Method

The authors determined the leading Fock state LFWFs of the pion and kaon by projecting the covariant Bethe-Salpeter wave function onto the light front, using a DCSB-improved (DB) truncation of the DSEs. They then computed the GPDs at zero skewness via overlap representations of these LFWFs, and obtained impact parameter dependent parton distributions (IPDs) through two-dimensional Fourier transforms of the GPDs. Moments of the GPDs were taken to extract electromagnetic and gravitational form factors, and the results were compared to experimental data and lattice QCD calculations.

## Key numbers

- Mean-squared impact parameter for the u quark in the pion: ⟨b_T^2⟩_u^π = 0.11 fm^2
- Mean-squared impact parameter for the s quark in the kaon: ⟨b_T^2⟩_s^K = 0.08 fm^2
- Mean-squared impact parameter for the u quark in the kaon: ⟨b_T^2⟩_u^K = 0.13 fm^2
- Contribution of the ψ_1 LFWF (relative p-wave) to the quark number sum rule: 0.36 for the pion, 0.31 for the kaon
- Model scale μ_0 = 520 MeV, determined by matching the pion valence quark momentum fraction ⟨x⟩_v = 0.5 at μ_0 to evolve to ⟨x⟩_v = 0.24 at 2 GeV
- Pion charge radius from leading Fock state calculation: r_c = 0.41 fm (experimental value: 0.67 fm)
- Pion charge radius after including a dressed vertex modification: r_c = 0.59 fm
- Light-cone charge radius for the u quark in the pion: r_c,LC^u,π = 0.331 fm
- Light-cone energy radii are about 60% smaller than light-cone charge radii for each quark sector in the pion and kaon

## Context

This work extends the approach of Shi (2018), who obtained pion LFWFs from DSE Bethe-Salpeter wave functions and studied the pion TMD, to include the kaon and to study GPDs. The results are compared to full DSE calculations by Maris (2000) that include an infinite tower of Fock states, and to lattice QCD data from Brommel (2007). The paper enables future studies of higher Fock state contributions and dressed operator effects in light front QCD, and provides predictions for the spatial and momentum distributions of valence quarks in pions and kaons that can be tested by experiments using pion and kaon beams or through the virtual meson cloud around nucleon targets.
