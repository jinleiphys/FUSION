# Spectroscopy of one-neutron halo nuclei from the Coulomb breakup reactions

> We review the current status of obtaining the spectroscopic information on the one-neutron halo nuclei from the Coulomb breakup reactions. The theory of these reactions formulated in the framework of the Distorted Wave Born Approximation, allows the use of the realistic wave functions corresponding to any orbital angular momentum structure for the core-valence neutron relative motion in the ground state of the projectile. The energy, angular and parallel momentum distributions of the projectile fragments calculated within this theory are selective about the ground state wave function of the projectile. Therefore, firm conclusions can be drawn about the structure of the projectile ground state by comparing the calculations with the corresponding data.

# Spectroscopy of one-neutron halo nuclei from the Coulomb breakup reactions

## Key claim
The finite-range Distorted Wave Born Approximation (FRDWBA) theory of Coulomb breakup reactions can extract spectroscopic information about one-neutron halo nuclei, but the method is most reliable for the ground state of the core fragment because transitions to excited core states are suppressed by several orders of magnitude in pure Coulomb breakup compared to nuclear breakup.

## Method
The authors apply a post-form DWBA theory with finite-range effects (FRDWBA) to calculate energy, angular, and parallel momentum distributions of projectile fragments from Coulomb breakup reactions. The theory uses realistic single-particle wave functions for the core-valence neutron relative motion, constructed from Woods-Saxon potentials with depths adjusted to reproduce experimental one-neutron separation energies. Calculations are compared with experimental data for exclusive breakup reactions A(a,b or n)X and one-neutron removal reactions A(a,bγ)X on heavy targets.

## Key numbers
- For $^{11}$Be ground state: one-neutron separation energy $S_{n-^{10}Be}$ = 504 keV with spectroscopic factor 0.74 for the $s$-wave configuration [$1s_{1/2}\nu \otimes ^{10}$Be$(0^+)$]; root mean square radius of valence neutron = 6.7 fm; rms radius of $^{11}$Be = 2.91 fm (with $^{10}$Be core radius 2.28 fm)
- Parallel momentum distribution (PMD) of $^{10}$Be from $^{11}$Be breakup: FWHM = 44 MeV/c (U target) and 43 MeV/c (Ta target) at 63 MeV/nucleon, matching experimental average 43.6 ± 1.1 MeV/c for the $s$-wave configuration
- For $^{19}$C ground state: best fit with $S_{n-^{18}C}$ = 530 keV and $s$-wave configuration [$1s_{1/2}\nu \otimes ^{18}$C$(0^+)$]; PMD of $^{18}$C has FWHM = 40 MeV/c, matching experimental value 41 ± 3 MeV/c at 88 MeV/nucleon on Ta target
- In pure Coulomb breakup of $^{11}$Be on $^{208}$Pb at 60 MeV/nucleon, sum of partial cross sections to all excited $^{10}$Be states is less than 1% of the cross section to the ground state, compared to ~22% for nuclear breakup on $^{9}$Be

## Context
This work extends the FRDWBA formalism developed by Chatterjee (2000) for Coulomb breakup of halo nuclei, and applies it to $^{11}$Be and $^{19}$C. The results contradict earlier assumptions that Coulomb and nuclear breakup populate core excited states similarly. The finding that Coulomb breakup predominantly populates the core ground state explains the null result for core-excited transitions observed by Guimaraes (2000) in $^{14}$B breakup on $^{197}$Au. This enables a cleaner spectroscopic tool for studying halo ground states, complementary to the nuclear breakup studies on light targets by Aumann (2000) and the adiabatic model approach of Tostevin (1998) and Johnson (1997).
