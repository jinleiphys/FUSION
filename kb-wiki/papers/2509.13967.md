# Dilated coordinate method for solving nuclear lattice effective field theory

> We introduce a dilated coordinate method to address computational challenges in nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT) for weakly-bound few-body systems. The approach employs adaptive mesh refinement via analytic coordinate transformations, dynamically adjusting spatial resolution to resolve short-range nuclear interactions with fine grids while efficiently capturing long-range wave function tails with coarse grids. Numerical demonstrations for two- and three-body systems confirm accelerated convergence towards infinite-volume limit compared to uniform lattices, particularly beneficial for accessing highly excited states and shallow bound states near the continuum threshold. This method establishes a foundation for \textit{ab initio} studies of exotic nuclear systems near the dripline and light hypernuclei, with direct extensions to scattering and reaction processes.

# Dilated coordinate method for solving nuclear lattice effective field theory

## Key claim

The dilated coordinate method accelerates convergence to the infinite-volume limit for weakly-bound few-body systems in nuclear lattice effective field theory (NLEFT), but the residual difference between uniform and dilated lattice extrapolated energies is approximately 0.36% for the three-dimensional three-boson test case, indicating that dilation parameters require further optimization to fully eliminate lattice artifacts.

## Method

The authors introduce an analytic coordinate transformation that maps a uniform computational lattice in auxiliary s-space to a non-uniform physical lattice in r-space, using a scaling function that leaves lattice spacing nearly unchanged within a chosen radius R and asymptotically increases spacing by a factor λ at large distances. They solve the transformed Schrödinger equation on the uniform s-grid using finite difference methods and the Lanczos algorithm, applying wave function symmetrization for three-body systems to remove bias from particle labeling. Numerical benchmarks are performed for one- and three-dimensional two- and three-boson systems with Gaussian potentials, a deuteron from pionless effective field theory, and an attractive Coulomb potential.

## Key numbers

- One-dimensional two-boson system: continuum limit ground state energy E = -0.246 MeV with Gaussian potential (C = -1.5 MeV, σ = 2.0 fm, M = 938.92 MeV)
- Three-dimensional two-boson system: infinite-volume limit E = -2.09 MeV with Gaussian potential (C = -40 MeV, σ = 1.5 fm, a = 0.99 fm)
- Deuteron binding energy: both uniform and dilated lattices converge to experimental value -2.224 MeV; dilated lattice prediction is approximately 0.03 MeV lower than uniform lattice result
- Three-dimensional three-boson system: extrapolated energies E = -4.565 MeV (uniform) and E = -4.581 MeV (dilated), with residual difference of 0.0165 MeV (0.36% of total binding energy)
- Three-dimensional three-boson system: convergence requires L/a = 20 for uniform lattice versus L/a ≈ 16 for dilated lattice, yielding approximately (20/16)^6 ≈ 4 times acceleration in computational cost
- Attractive Coulomb potential: contact term strength V_cont = -537.4 MeV (uniform) and V_cont = -533.5 MeV (dilated) to reproduce E_1/E_2 = 1/4 ratio
- One-dimensional three-boson system: dilated lattice gives extrapolated energies E = -2.04, -0.28, -0.05 MeV for interaction strengths C = -3, -1, -0.4 MeV respectively

## Context

This work extends the adaptive mesh refinement (AMR) approach, widely used in numerical relativity, computational fluid dynamics, and lattice QCD (PhysRevLett.100.041601, PhysRevLett.105.201602), to nuclear lattice effective field theory for the first time. The method addresses the inefficiency of standard uniform cubic lattices in NLEFT for shallow bound states near the continuum threshold, such as the Λ-hypernucleus ³_ΛH with Λ-separation energy approximately 0.102 MeV (PRL131.102302) and the deuteron with binding energy -2.224 MeV. The technique enables future ab initio studies of exotic nuclear systems near the dripline and light hypernuclei, with direct extensions to scattering and reaction processes, building on prior NLEFT work using direct diagonalization methods (NPA768-179, EPJA41-125) and finite-volume extrapolations (EPJA54-121, EPJA60-215).
