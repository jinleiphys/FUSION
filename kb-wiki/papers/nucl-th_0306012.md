# Electro and gamma nuclear physics in Geant4

> Adequate description of electro and gamma nuclear physics is of utmost importance in studies of electron beam-dumps and intense electron beam accelerators. I also is mandatory to describe neutron backgrounds and activation in linear colliders. This physics was elaborated in Geant4 over the last year, and now entered into the stage of practical application. In the {\sc Geant4} Photo-nuclear data base there are at present about 50 nuclei for which the Photo-nuclear absorption cross sections have been measured. Of these, data on 14 nuclei are used to parametrize the gamma nuclear reaction cross-section The resulting cross section is a complex, factorized function of $A$ and $e = log(E_γ)$, where $E_γ$ is the energy of the incident photon. Electro-nuclear reactions are so closely connected with Photo-nuclear reactions that sometimes they are often called ``Photo-nuclear''. The one-photon exchange mechanism dominates in Electro-nuclear reactions, and the electron can be substituted by a flux of photons. Folding this flux with the gamma-nuclear cross-section, we arrive at an acceptable description of the electro-nuclear physics. Final states in gamma and electro nuclear physics are described using chiral invariant phase-space decay at low gamma or equivalent photon energies, and quark gluon string model at high energies. We will present the modeling of this physics in {\sc Geant4}, and show results from practical applications.

# Electro and gamma nuclear physics in Geant4

## Key claim

The authors present a parameterized model for photo-nuclear and electro-nuclear reactions implemented in Geant4, claiming that the chiral invariant phase-space (CHIPS) approach combined with quark-exchange mechanisms can reproduce experimental proton spectra from photonuclear reactions, though they acknowledge that the angular dependence of secondary protons remains "a challenging experimental fact which is difficult to explain in any model."

## Method

The photo-nuclear cross section is parameterized as a factorized function of atomic mass A and e = log(E_γ), covering five energy regions: Giant Dipole Resonance, quasi-deuteron, Δ isobar, Roper resonance, and Reggeon-Pomeron, using data from 14 nuclei in the Geant4 photo-nuclear database. Final states at low energies are generated using the chiral invariant phase-space (CHIPS) model, which treats excited hadronic systems as Quasmons containing massless quark-partons at a critical temperature T_c = 180-200 MeV, while high-energy final states use the quark-gluon string model. Electro-nuclear reactions are treated via the Equivalent Photon Approximation (EPA), where the electron is replaced by a flux of virtual photons folded with the gamma-nuclear cross section, with separate treatment of low-Q^2 and high-Q^2 regions.

## Key numbers

- The Geant4 Photo-nuclear data base contains about 50 nuclei with measured photo-nuclear absorption cross sections, of which data on 14 nuclei are used for parameterization: ^1H, ^2H, ^4He, ^6Li, ^7Li, ^9Be, ^12C, ^16O, ^27Al, ^40Ca, Cu, Sn, Pb, and U
- The critical temperature parameter T_c = 180-200 MeV is the principal parameter of the CHIPS model
- For the Δ isobar region, the normalization factor d = 0.41·A (with 0.55 for ^1H and 0.88 for ^2H), meaning the Δ yield is proportional to A
- The dipole nucleon form-factor is G^2_D(Q^2) ≈ (1 + Q^2/(843 MeV)^2)^{-2}
- The total photo-nuclear cross section for Ca is 5.4 mb (used for comparison at 59-65 MeV)
- The total photo-nuclear cross section for ^12C is 1.8 mb (used for comparison at 123 MeV)
- The b(K) function is approximated as b(K) ≈ (K/185 MeV)^{0.85} and c(K) ≈ -(K/1390 MeV)^3
- For the ^12C(e,e'p) reaction, the energy transfer is 210 MeV and momentum transfer is 585 MeV/c

## Context

This work extends the Geant4 simulation framework to include electro and gamma nuclear physics, building on the Equivalent Photon Approximation originally proposed by Fermi and developed by Weizsacker, Williams, Landau, and Lifshitz. The CHIPS model for final state generation draws on the concept of a critical temperature with a 35-year history starting with Hagedorn, and the authors compare their results against experimental data from Ryckebusch (photonuclear reactions on ^40Ca at 59-65 MeV) and Harty (photonuclear reactions on ^12C at 123 and 151 MeV), as well as Bates data on ^12C(e,e'p) reactions. The work enables improved simulation of neutron backgrounds and activation in linear colliders and electron beam-dump studies within the Geant4 framework.
