# Radial excitations and their potential impact on Fermi $β$-decay rates

> We investigate the contribution of radial excitations to Fermi $β$-decay matrix element. To this end, exact no-core shell model calculations are performed for the mirror $β$ decay of tritium, where full convergence can be achieved on an ordinary computer. The differences between the isospin-mixing correction values obtained in the full and in a restricted model spaces are matched to the radial overlap correction term, analogous to that required in the shell-model approach, where the configuration space is extremely limited. We examine this complementary correction term using a nonorthogonal harmonic-oscillator basis, generated by slightly differentiating the oscillator frequencies between the initial and final nuclei, while all desirable properties, including translational invariance, are still preserved. For $N_{\rm max}\le8$, we find that the radial excitation contribution is negative, with a typical magnitude of approximately 10\,\% to 20\,\% of the radial diagonal contribution. This effect becomes more pronounced as the model space increases. Therefore, the $δ_{C2}$ values obtained in the shell model approach, where radial excitations are not explicitly included, are likely overestimated. Based on experimental $ft$ data and the corrective terms adopted in the survey by Hardy and Towner [Phys. Rev. C {\bf 102}, 045501 (2020)], we show that the incorporation of radial excitations for the superallowed $0^+\rightarrow0^+$ nuclear $β$ decay tends however to worsen agreement with the Standard Model.

## Key claim

The paper finds that radial excitation contributions to the Fermi beta-decay matrix element are negative and have a typical magnitude of approximately 10% to 20% of the radial diagonal contribution for model spaces up to Nmax=8, but incorporating this effect into superallowed 0+ to 0+ nuclear beta decay calculations tends to worsen agreement with the Standard Model, contrary to theoretical expectations.

## Method

The authors performed exact no-core shell model (NCSM) calculations for the mirror beta decay of tritium (3H to 3He) using a chiral effective field theory interaction evolved via similarity renormalization group, with up to N4LO terms and a 500 MeV regulator cutoff. They introduced a nonorthogonal harmonic-oscillator basis by slightly differentiating the oscillator frequencies between the initial and final nuclei to generate radial mismatch, including radial excitations, while preserving translational invariance. The isospin-mixing correction delta_C1 was calculated in the conventional orthogonal basis, and the radial mismatch correction delta_C2 was extracted as the difference between the total correction delta_C and delta_C1, with the converged total correction delta_C = 0.077% obtained at Nmax approximately 30.

## Key numbers

*   Total isospin-symmetry breaking correction for tritium beta decay: delta_C = 0.077% (converged value at Nmax approximately 30).
*   Radial excitation contribution ratio kappa (delta_C2_re / delta_C2_sm) at hbar_omega_i = 30 MeV: approximately -13% for Nmax=2, -15% for Nmax=4, -16% for Nmax=6, and -18% for Nmax=8.
*   Extracted oscillator frequency differences Delta_hbar_omega required to match delta_C2 values: ranging from 0.23 MeV to 0.85 MeV across the considered hbar_omega_i and Nmax values.
*   Standard Model constraint yields Ft_st = 3067.26(91) s for the superallowed 0+ to 0+ decays.

## Context

This work directly addresses the criticism by Miller and Schwenk (2009) that shell-model calculations of isospin-symmetry breaking corrections lack the contribution of radial excitations, which had previously only been demonstrated using a schematic model. The study extends the approach of Damgaard (1969), who derived an analytical formula for delta_C based solely on nodal mixing of harmonic oscillator functions driven by a schematic one-body Coulomb potential. The results enable future work on more flexible scenarios with unequal oscillator frequencies for protons and neutrons or state-dependent frequencies, though extending the approach to heavier nuclei would require significant effort in transforming interaction matrix elements.
