# Data-driven quark and gluon jet modification in heavy-ion collisions

> Whether quark- and gluon-initiated jets are modified differently by the quark-gluon plasma produced in heavy-ion collisions is a long-standing question that has thus far eluded a definitive experimental answer. A crucial complication for quark-gluon discrimination in both proton-proton and heavy-ion collisions is that all measurements necessarily average over the (unknown) quark-gluon composition of a jet sample. In the heavy-ion context, the simultaneous modification of both the fractions and substructure of quark and gluon jets by the quark-gluon plasma further obscures the interpretation. Here, we demonstrate a fully data-driven method for separating quark and gluon contributions to jet observables using a statistical technique called topic modeling. Assuming that jet distributions are a mixture of underlying "quark-like" and "gluon-like" distributions, we show how to extract quark and gluon jet fractions and constituent multiplicity distributions as a function of the jet transverse momentum. This proof-of-concept study is based on proton-proton and heavy-ion collision events from the Monte Carlo event generator Jewel with statistics accessible in Run 4 of the Large Hadron Collider. These results suggest the potential for an experimental determination of quark and gluon jet modifications.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates a fully data-driven method, based on topic modeling, to extract separate quark-like and gluon-like jet fractions and their constituent multiplicity distributions in both proton-proton and heavy-ion collisions, using Monte Carlo event generator Jewel with statistics comparable to those anticipated in LHC Run 4. The method does not require templates or the assumption that substructure observables are unmodified by the quark-gluon plasma, allowing for simultaneous estimates of the modification of quark and gluon jet fractions and their distributions.

## Method

The method applies the Demix statistical technique to two mixed jet samples (photon-jet and dijet events) to extract mutually irreducible "quark-like" and "gluon-like" base distributions (topics) from the observable of jet constituent multiplicity. To handle finite statistics, the input histograms are simultaneously fit to sums of skew-normal distributions using Markov Chain Monte Carlo posterior estimation, from which reducibility factors kappa are computed and used to extract the topics and their fractions as a function of jet transverse momentum. The analysis uses anti-kT jets with radius parameter R=0.4 from Jewel 2.1.0 events at 5.02 TeV, with heavy-ion samples downsampled to match the expected integrated luminosity of 13 nb^-1 after LHC Run 4.

## Key numbers

- Jet constituent multiplicity distributions extracted for quark-like and gluon-like topics in both proton-proton and heavy-ion collisions at sqrt(s_NN)=5.02 TeV
- Heavy-ion samples downsampled to match expected integrated luminosity of 13 nb^-1 after LHC Run 4
- After unweighting, the proton-proton dijet sample has 63603 jets and gamma+jet sample has 42466 jets with pT in [100,120] GeV
- After downsampling to 13 nb^-1, the heavy-ion gamma+jet sample has 6453 jets with pT in [100,120] GeV, 2825 jets with pT in [120,140] GeV, and 1436 jets with pT in [140,160] GeV
- The heavy-ion dijet sample has 71098, 32318, and 15747 jets in the same respective pT bins
- MC-level parton matching within Delta R=0.4 is satisfied by 87% of gamma+jet and 90% of dijet jets in heavy-ion samples (pT 100-120 GeV), compared to 94% and 95% in proton-proton samples

## Context

This work extends the topic modeling approach pioneered for quark-gluon separation in proton-proton collisions by Metodiev and Thaler (2018) and Komiske et al. (2018), and applied experimentally by Aad et al. (2019), to the heavy-ion collision environment. It contrasts with a recent CMS analysis by Sirunyan et al. (2020) that used jet charge templates assuming unmodified quark and gluon distributions, whereas this method does not require such assumptions. The results enable future experimental determination of separate quark and gluon jet modifications in LHC Run 4 data, which would have consequences for understanding the microscopic structure of the quark-gluon plasma.
