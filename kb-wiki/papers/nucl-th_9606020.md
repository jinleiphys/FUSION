# Self-consistent description of finite nuclei based on a relativistic quark model

> Relativistic Hartree equations for spherical nuclei have been derived from a relativistic quark model of the structure of bound nucleons which interact through the (self-consistent) exchange of scalar ($σ$) and vector ($ω$ and $ρ$) mesons. The coupling constants and the mass of the $σ$-meson are determined from the properties of symmetric nuclear matter and the rms charge radius in $^{40}$Ca. Calculated properties of static, closed-shell nuclei from $^{16}$O to $^{208}$Pb are compared with experimental data and with results of Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD). The dependence of the results on the nucleon size and the quark mass is investigated. Several possible extensions of the model are also discussed.

## Key claim

The quark-meson coupling (QMC) model, which treats nucleons as composite objects whose internal quark structure responds to the surrounding scalar field, can describe finite nuclei from 16O to 208Pb with results that agree favorably with experimental data, provided the sigma-meson mass is adjusted to fit the rms charge radius of 40Ca. The key hedge is that the Born-Oppenheimer approximation is used to separate the slow nucleon motion from the fast quark dynamics, and the model relies on the MIT bag model for nucleon structure, though the authors argue that the specific details of the bag model are not essential.

## Method

The authors derived relativistic Hartree equations for spherical nuclei starting from a Lagrangian where nucleons are treated as MIT bags containing quarks that couple to scalar (sigma) and vector (omega and rho) mesons and the photon. The meson fields are treated in mean-field approximation, and the nucleon effective mass becomes a function of the scalar field determined by solving the quark wavefunctions in the bag self-consistently. The coupling constants and sigma-meson mass were fixed by fitting symmetric nuclear matter properties (binding energy -15.7 MeV per nucleon at saturation density 0.15 fm^-3) and the experimental rms charge radius of 40Ca (3.48 fm), then the coupled Dirac and Klein-Gordon equations were solved iteratively for closed-shell nuclei from 16O to 208Pb.

## Key numbers

- Saturation density of symmetric nuclear matter: rho_0 = 0.15 fm^-3 (k_F^0 = 1.305 fm^-1)
- Binding energy per nucleon in nuclear matter: -15.7 MeV
- Nuclear incompressibility K ranges from 266 to 295 MeV depending on bag radius and quark mass, within the experimental range of 200-300 MeV
- Effective nucleon mass at saturation density M_N^* ranges from 728 to 774 MeV (for bag radii 0.6-1.0 fm and quark masses 0-10 MeV), compared to 522 MeV in QHD
- Sigma-meson mass m_sigma adjusted to fit 40Ca rms charge radius: ranges from 388 to 443 MeV depending on bag radius and quark mass
- g_sigma^2/4pi ranges from 2.51 to 5.87, g_omega^2/4pi ranges from 4.50 to 6.37, g_rho^2/4pi ranges from 6.78 to 7.03
- Bag radius shrinks by 2% at saturation density (delta R_B^*/R_B = -0.02)
- Quark rms radius increases by 1-3% at saturation density

## Context

This work extends the QMC model, originally proposed by Guichon (1988) for nuclear matter and refined by Saito and Thomas (1994), from infinite matter to finite nuclei using a self-consistent mean-field approach rather than the local-density approximation used in earlier applications. The model improves upon Quantum Hadrodynamics (QHD) by incorporating the internal quark structure of the nucleon, which naturally yields a nuclear incompressibility within the experimental range (200-300 MeV) compared to QHD's value of 540 MeV, and gives a larger effective nucleon mass in medium. The approach enables future studies of the Okamoto-Nolen-Schiffer anomaly, corrections to CKM matrix element extraction from super-allowed Fermi beta-decay, and the nuclear EMC effect, all of which had been previously treated only semi-quantitatively using the local-density approximation.
