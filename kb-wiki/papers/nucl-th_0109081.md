# Statistical Modeling of Nuclear Systematics

> Statistical modeling of data sets by neural-network techniques is offered as an alternative to traditional semiempirical approaches to global modeling of nuclear properties. New results are presented to support the position that such novel techniques can rival conventional theory in predictive power, if not in economy of description. Examples include the statistical inference of atomic masses and beta-decay halflives based on the information contained in existing databases. Neural network modeling, as well as other statistical strategies based on new algorithms for artificial intelligence, may prove to be a useful asset in the further exploration of nuclear phenomena far from stability.

## Key claim

Neural-network models of atomic masses and beta-decay halflives can rival conventional theoretical models in predictive accuracy, but they require many more adjustable parameters and do not yet match the best traditional models (e.g., the FRDM of Möller et al.) on the most stringent tests of extrapolation to nuclei far from stability.

## Method

Multilayer feedforward neural networks with pairwise couplings were trained by backpropagation (vanilla or modified) on existing nuclear databases. For atomic masses, networks with various input coding schemes (binary, analog, or mixed encoding of Z and N) and architectures (e.g., 4+10+10+10+1) were trained on 1323 "old" masses from the Möller-Nix database and validated on 351 "new" masses, with a separate test set of 158 newer nuclides from NUBASE and 10 rare-earth isotopes from ISOLTRAP. For beta-decay halflives, a (17+10+1) network with binary Z and N coding plus an analog Q-value input was trained on 518 ground-state beta-minus decays and tested on 174 withheld cases, using the Klapdor error measure and the Möller et al. figures of merit M^(10) and sigma_M^(10).

## Key numbers

- Best neural-network mass model (4+10+10+10+1)†[281] achieved sigma_rms = 1.200 MeV on the N validation set (351 nuclei), compared to 0.735 MeV for the Möller-Nix model
- On the NB test set (158 newer nuclides), the same network gave sigma_rms = 1.462 MeV versus 0.697 MeV for the FRDM model of Möller et al.
- On the ISO test set (10 rare-earth nuclides), the network gave sigma_rms = 0.963 MeV versus 0.500 MeV for FRDM
- For beta-decay halflives, the (17+10+1)[191] network gave M^(10) = 1.19 and sigma_M^(10) = 2.13 on the training set for all nuclides with T < 1000 sec, compared to Möller et al. values of M^(10) = 3.50 and sigma_M^(10) = 72.02
- The beta-decay database contained 766 examples with halflives ranging from 0.15 × 10^(-2) sec (Na-35) to 0.2932 × 10^24 sec (Cd-143), truncated to 692 nuclides with lifetimes ≤ 10^6 y

## Context

This work extends earlier neural-network studies of nuclear masses by Clark et al. (1996-2000) and Kalman (1998) with improved input coding and a modified backpropagation algorithm, and refines the beta-decay halflife modeling of Ref. 10 (Luso et al.). The results are compared directly against the macroscopic/microscopic FRDM model of Möller, Nix, and collaborators (1995) and the beta-decay models of Homma et al. (1996) and Möller et al. (1997). The improved extrapolation to nuclei far from stability suggests that neural-network methods could become a useful tool for predicting properties of nuclides accessible at radioactive beam facilities and for reaction-network calculations in nucleosynthesis models.
