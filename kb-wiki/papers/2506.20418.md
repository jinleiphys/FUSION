# Framework for phase transitions between the Maxwell and Gibbs constructions at finite temperature

> The characteristics of the hadron-to-quark first-order phase transition differ depending on whether charge neutrality is locally or globally fulfilled. In $β$-equilibrated matter, these two possibilities correspond to the Maxwell and Gibbs constructions. Recently, we presented a new framework in which a continuously-varying parameter allows one to describe a first-order phase transition in intermediate scenarios to the two extremes of fully local and fully global charge neutrality. In this work, we extend the previous framework to finite temperatures and out-of-$β$ equilibrium conditions, making it available for simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers. We investigate its impact on key thermodynamic quantities across a range of baryon densities, temperatures, and electron fractions. We find that when matter is not in $β$-equilibrium, the pressure in the mixed phase is not constant even for the case of fully-local charge neutrality. Moreover, we compute the thermal index using three different approaches, demonstrating that the finite-temperature extension of an equation of state using a constant thermal index can be ill-defined when applied to the mixed phase.

## Key claim

The authors extend their previously developed framework (Constantinou, 2023) that interpolates between the Maxwell and Gibbs constructions for first-order hadron-to-quark phase transitions to finite temperatures and out-of-beta-equilibrium conditions, but they note that the parameter eta controlling the degree of local versus global charge neutrality is kept as a free variable rather than determined by minimization, meaning the framework describes intermediate scenarios without specifying the surface tension that would select a particular eta value.

## Method

The framework minimizes the total free energy density of a system containing hadrons (neutrons and protons described by the ZL model), quarks (up, down, strange described by the vMIT model), electrons, positrons, and photons, subject to baryon number conservation, lepton number conservation, and charge neutrality constraints that depend on the parameter eta (ranging from 0 for global charge neutrality to 1 for local charge neutrality). The equilibrium conditions are derived from minimization with respect to particle fractions, the hadron volume fraction chi, and eta, yielding modified chemical potential relations and a mechanical equilibrium condition between hadronic and quark pressures that includes electron contributions weighted by eta. The JEL method is used for numerical evaluation of thermodynamic integrals at finite temperature.

## Key numbers

- The critical surface tension separating the Maxwell and Gibbs regimes is given as sigma_c = 9.1 [MeV/fm^2] x (n_e / 0.1 [fm^{-3}]), scaling linearly with the global electron density n_e
- The ZL model parameters include a_0 = -96.64 MeV, b_0 = 58.85 MeV, gamma = 1.40, a_1 = -26.06 MeV, b_1 = 7.34 MeV, gamma_1 = 2.45, with saturation density n_0 = 0.16 fm^{-3}
- The vMIT model parameters include quark masses m_u = 5.0 MeV, m_d = 7.0 MeV, m_s = 150.0 MeV, vector coupling a = 0.20 fm^2, and bag constant B^{1/4} = 180 MeV
- The temperature range considered for core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers is up to approximately 100 MeV
- The baryon density range for astrophysical equation of state applications spans approximately 10^{-8} to 10 times nuclear saturation density n_0

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature framework of Constantinou (2023) that introduced the eta parameter to interpolate between the Maxwell construction (local charge neutrality, eta=1) and Gibbs construction (global charge neutrality, eta=0) for first-order hadron-to-quark phase transitions. The finite-temperature extension makes the framework applicable to simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers, where temperatures up to 100 MeV and out-of-beta-equilibrium conditions are relevant, building on prior work by Fischer (2017), Most (2019), and Bauswein (2018) that used either Maxwell or Gibbs constructions separately in such simulations. The authors find that when matter is not in beta-equilibrium, the pressure in the mixed phase is not constant even for the fully-local charge neutrality case, and they demonstrate that extending an equation of state to finite temperatures using a constant thermal index can be ill-defined when applied to the mixed phase.
