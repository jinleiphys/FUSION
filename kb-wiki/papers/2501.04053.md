# Decay spectroscopy of heavy and superheavy nuclei

> After more than half a century since the first predictions of the so-called "island of stability of superheavy nuclei", exploring the limits of nuclear stability at highest atomic numbers is still one of the most prominent challenges in low-energy nuclear physics. These exotic nuclear species reveal their character and details of some of their properties through their induced or spontaneous disintegration. The achievements in the field of superheavy nuclei (SHN) research, which involves studying the production and decay of the heaviest nuclear species, have been reported in a number of review papers. In the introduction of this paper, references are provided to review papers, summarizing the many aspects of SHN research in other disciplines, like chemistry, atomic physics, and earlier work on nuclear structure, including in-beam spectroscopy, and superheavy element (SHE) synthesis. This review is an attempt to summarize the experimental progress that has been made in recent years by employing the versatile tool park of Decay Spectroscopy After Separation (DSAS) for the heaviest isotopes from Z=99 (einsteinium) to Z=118 (oganesson). DSAS, with its major instrumentation components heavy-ion accelerator, separator and decay detection, is the only way to access the heaviest nuclei up to oganesson. While in-beam γ-spectroscopy has reached 256Rf in terms of the highest atomic number Z and mass number A, SHE chemistry succeeded to sort flerovium (Z = 114) as the heaviest element into the periodic table. Laser spectroscopy and precise mass measurements are limited basically to the nobelium/fermium region, with high-precision Penning-trap mass-measurements being performed for 256Lr and 257Rf, and with the 257Db mass obtained, using a multi-reflection time-of-flight mass spectrometer (MRToF MS).

## Key claim

This review summarizes experimental progress in decay spectroscopy after separation (DSAS) for the heaviest isotopes from Z=99 (einsteinium) to Z=118 (oganesson), updating the known nuclear structure information with 16 newly discovered isotopes since the author's previous review (Ackermann 2017), while noting that many results remain debated or uncertain, such as the observation of 267Ds and the competition between spontaneous fission and electron capture in several dubnium isotopes.

## Method

The paper reviews the DSAS methodology, where evaporation residues from heavy-ion fusion-evaporation reactions are separated from the primary beam using velocity filters (SHIP, SHELS) or gas-filled separators (RITU, DGFRS, GARIS, BGS, TASCA, AGFA) and implanted into position-sensitive silicon detectors. Alpha particles, conversion electrons, fission fragments, and coincident gamma rays and x-rays are registered to establish genetic decay chains for particle identification and to extract spectroscopic information on nuclear structure, including single-particle states, K-isomers, and decay-mode competition.

## Key numbers

* 262 isotopes are known for elements from Z=96 (curium) to Z=118, 18 more than in the author's earlier review (Ackermann 2017)
* In-beam gamma-spectroscopy has reached 256Rf as the highest atomic number Z and mass number A studied
* High-precision Penning-trap mass measurements have been performed for 256Lr and 257Rf, and the 257Db mass obtained using a multi-reflection time-of-flight mass spectrometer (MRToF MS)
* Laser spectroscopy studies have yielded systematic results for isotopic chains of nobelium and fermium, including charge radii and shell structure around Z=100 and N=152 at 252Fm
* The heaviest K isomers observed are 270mDs and 266mHs
* New isotopes reported include 275,276Ds from 48Ca+232Th reactions, 280Ds from the 288Fl decay chain, 244Md with its daughters 240Es and 236Bk, 251Lr, 252Rf, 257Sg, 286Mc, 264Lr, and 288,289Lv

## Context

This review extends the author's previous work with Christophe Theisen (Ackermann 2017) on nuclear structure features of very heavy and superheavy nuclei, and complements other recent reviews on superheavy element synthesis (Oganessian 2017), chemical properties of transactinides (Tuerler 2013), laser spectroscopy of actinides (Block 2021), and the comprehensive collection in the 2015 Nuclear Physics A special issue and the 2025 European Physical Journal A special issue on SHE/SHN research. The compilation enables future systematic studies of single-particle configurations, K-isomerism, and decay-mode competition across the heaviest elements, and provides a reference table of decay properties for all known isotopes from einsteinium to oganesson.
