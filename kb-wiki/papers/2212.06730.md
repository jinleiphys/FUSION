# Boltzmann Distributions on a Quantum Computer via Active Cooling

> Quantum computing raises the possibility of solving a variety of problems in physics that are presently intractable. A number of such problems involves the physics of systems in or near thermal equilibrium. There are two main ways to compute thermal expectation values on a quantum computer: construct a thermal state that reproduces thermal expectation values, or sample various energy eigenstates from a Boltzmann distribution of a given temperature. In this paper we address the second approach and propose an algorithm that uses active cooling to produce the distribution. While this algorithm is quite general and applicable to a wide variety of systems, it was developed with the specific intention of simulating thermal configurations of non-Abelian gauge theories such as QCD, which would allow the study of quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions.

## Key claim
The paper proposes an algorithm that uses active cooling, repeatedly coupling a system of interest to a small refrigerator, time-evolving, then reinitializing the refrigerator, to sample energy eigenstates from a Boltzmann distribution, with the ability to predict and control the characteristic energy of the system so that a chosen temperature range can be targeted.

## Method
The algorithm cycles through four steps: (1) initialize the refrigerator to its ground state while decoupled from the system; (2) couple the system and refrigerator via a gauge-invariant coupling Hamiltonian; (3) time-evolve the combined system under the full Hamiltonian; (4) switch off the coupling and reinitialize the refrigerator to its ground state, measuring the energy removed. The energy removed per cycle is tracked, and because the ground-state energy of the refrigerator is known a priori, the characteristic energy of the system can be inferred. A mapping between energy and temperature (obtained via classical Monte Carlo) then allows targeting a specific temperature range.

## Key numbers
- The number of cycles required scales as \( N^{cycles} = A \log(E_i / E_f) \), where \(A\) is a constant and \(E_i\) and \(E_f\) are the initial and final energies of the system.
- The coupling strength \(\sigma\) must satisfy \(\langle H^{coup} \rangle \ll \langle H^{sys} \rangle - \langle H^{sys} \rangle_{vac}\) at all times to avoid heating the system.
- The refrigerator size can be on the same scale as the system of interest or even smaller and still work efficiently.

## Context
This work extends the heat-bath approach outlined in Ball and Cohen (2022) by replacing a single large heat bath with a small refrigerator that is repeatedly reinitialized, analogous to a Maxwell-demon refrigerator. It contrasts with methods such as adiabatic state preparation (which requires long coherent evolution), quantum phase estimation (which requires significant overlap with the target eigenstate), and METTS (which suffers from correlations between samples). The algorithm is designed for eventual application to non-Abelian gauge theories such as QCD, enabling the study of transport coefficients in quark-gluon plasma created in heavy-ion collisions.
