# Predicting nuclear masses with product-unit networks

> Accurate estimation of nuclear masses and their prediction beyond the experimentally explored domains of the nuclear landscape are crucial to an understanding of the fundamental origin of nuclear properties and to many applications of nuclear science, most notably in quantifying the $r$-process of stellar nucleosynthesis. Neural networks have been applied with some success to the prediction of nuclear masses, but they are known to have shortcomings in application to extrapolation tasks. In this work, we propose and explore a novel type of neural network for mass prediction in which the usual neuron-like processing units are replaced by complex-valued product units that permit multiplicative couplings of inputs to be learned from the input data. This generalized network model is tested on both interpolation and extrapolation data sets drawn from the Atomic Mass Evaluation. Its performance is compared with that of several neural-network architectures, substantiating its suitability for nuclear mass prediction. Additionally, a prediction-uncertainty measure for such complex-valued networks is proposed that serves to identify regions of expected low prediction error.

## Key claim
Complex-valued product-unit networks (CPUNs) can outperform both classical multilayer perceptrons and real-valued product-unit networks in nuclear mass prediction, particularly for extrapolation tasks, by eliminating threshold activations that impair extrapolation; the imaginary component of the network output provides a prediction-uncertainty measure that identifies subsets of extrapolation data with RMS errors as low as 0.39 MeV.

## Method
The authors replaced standard neuron-like processing units with complex-valued product units that compute multiplicative couplings of inputs via logarithmic and exponential activations, allowing the network to learn interactions between input features such as proton number Z, neutron number N, distances to magic numbers, and parity indicators. They trained the networks on data from the Atomic Mass Evaluation AME2020 and FRDM2012 using a loss function combining the squared real-part error with the squared imaginary part, and compared performance across multiple architectures (CPUN, RPUN, HPUN, MLP) on interpolation and extrapolation test sets.

## Key numbers
- CPUN-L achieved a training RMS error of 0.15 MeV on Train-B (2099 nuclei from FRDM2012), outperforming the 0.263 MeV reported by Li et al. (2022) for a similar data set
- On the extrapolation set Extra-B (1022 nuclei from AME2020), CPUN-L achieved an RMS error of 4.12 MeV overall, but applying a prediction-uncertainty threshold of 0.04 reduced the error to 0.39 MeV for a subset of 30 nuclei
- On the interpolation test set Inter-B (50 nuclei), CPUN-L achieved an RMS error of 0.36 MeV, which improved to 0.18 MeV when applying a threshold of 0.1
- In Experiment C, CPUN-L achieved RMS errors of 0.17 MeV on Train-C, 0.26 MeV on Inter-C, 0.39 MeV on Extra-C1, and 0.65 MeV on Extra-C2, outperforming all other architectures tested

## Context
This work extends the product-unit network approach previously applied to function and pattern extrapolation by Dellen et al. (2019) to the specific problem of nuclear mass prediction, where traditional neural networks have shown poor extrapolation performance due to threshold activations. The authors compare their results against recent deep-learning approaches by Li et al. (2022), which required nearly a million parameters to achieve 0.263 MeV training error, and the probabilistic neural network of Lovell et al. (2022), which achieved 0.56-3.9 MeV RMS errors. The proposed CPUN-L achieves better performance with far fewer parameters and introduces a novel uncertainty measure that enables reliable identification of low-error predictions in extrapolation regions, which is critical for applications such as r-process nucleosynthesis calculations.
