# Validation and extrapolation of atomic mass with physics-informed fully connected neural network

> Machine learning offers a powerful framework for validating and predicting atomic mass. We compare three improved neural network methods for representation and extrapolation for atomic mass prediction. The powerful method, adopting a macroscopic-microscopic approach and treating complex nuclear effects as output labels, achieves superior accuracy in AME2020, yielding a much lower root-mean-square deviation of 0.122 MeV in the test set, significantly lower than alternative methods. It also exhibits a better extrapolation performance when predicting AME2020 from AME2016, with a root-mean-square deviation of 0.191 MeV. We further conduct sensitivity analyses against the model inputs to verify interpretable alignment beyond statistical metrics. Incorporating theoretical predictions of magic numbers and masses, our fully connected neural networks reproduce key nuclear phenomena including nucleon pairing correlation and magic number effects. The extrapolation capability of the framework is discussed and the accuracy of predicting new mass measurements for isotope chains has also been tested.

## Key claim

The paper claims that a physics-informed fully connected neural network (FCNN) using a macroscopic-microscopic approach, where complex nuclear effects are treated as output labels (Method III), achieves superior accuracy in atomic mass prediction compared to direct mass prediction or residual correction methods, but this performance is conditional on the specific choice of input features, output labels, and network architecture, and is demonstrated only for nuclei with proton and neutron numbers greater than 20 and experimental errors below 100 keV.

## Method

The authors trained fully connected neural networks with two hidden layers (80-120 and 40-60 neurons) using GELU activation, Huber loss, and the Adam optimizer. Three methods were compared: Method I predicted experimental binding energy directly from macroscopic and microscopic input features; Method II used multi-output learning with three labels (experimental binding energy, liquid-drop model binding energy, and their difference); Method III refined Method II by using a modified Coulomb term, a different pairing term encoding, and a deformation term replacing the curvature parameters. Models were trained on AME2020 and AME2016 datasets, with sensitivity analysis performed via normalized gradient methods to assess input feature importance.

## Key numbers

- Method III achieved a test set root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) of 0.122 MeV on AME2020, with a training set RMSD of 0.052 MeV and MAE of 0.039 MeV
- Method III achieved an extrapolation RMSD of 0.191 MeV when predicting AME2020 from AME2016, with a training set RMSD of 0.125 MeV and MAE of 0.096 MeV
- Method II achieved a test set RMSD of 0.143 MeV on AME2020, with a training set RMSD of 0.087 MeV
- Method I achieved a test set RMSD of 0.204 MeV on AME2020, with a training set RMSD of 0.135 MeV
- Method III with deformation term removed gave training and test RMSD of 0.069 MeV and 0.154 MeV, respectively, in ablation tests
- The dataset comprised 2162 nuclei from AME2020 and 2087 from AME2016, all with Z, N > 20 and experimental errors less than 100 keV

## Context

This work extends machine learning approaches to nuclear mass prediction, building on prior neural network studies by Utama (2017), Niu (2022), and others, as well as macroscopic-microscopic models like the Finite Range Droplet Model (FRDM2012) by Moller (2015) and the Weizsacker-Skyrme model (WS4) by Wang (2014), which achieved an RMSD of 0.298 MeV. The method enables improved extrapolation to neutron-rich nuclei and superheavy elements, with potential applications to predicting masses for r-process nucleosynthesis studies and testing theoretical predictions of magic numbers at Z=114, 120 and N=184, 198.
