# Application of artificial intelligence in the determination of impact parameter in heavy-ion collisions at intermediate energies

> The impact parameter is one of the crucial physical quantities of heavy-ion collisions (HICs), and can affect obviously many observables at the final state, such as the multifragmentation and the collective flow. Usually, it cannot be measured directly in experiments but might be inferred from observables at the final state. Artificial intelligence has had great success in learning complex representations of data, which enables novel modeling and data processing approaches in physical sciences. In this article, we employ two of commonly used algorithms in the field of artificial intelligence, the Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM), to improve the accuracy of determining impact parameter by analyzing the proton spectra in transverse momentum and rapidity on the event-by-event basis. Au+Au collisions with the impact parameter of 0$\leq$$b$$\leq$10 fm at intermediate energies ($E_{\rm lab}$=$0.2$-$1.0$ GeV$/$nucleon) are simulated with the ultrarelativistic quantum molecular dynamics (UrQMD) model to generate the proton spectra data. It is found that the average difference between the true impact parameter and the estimated one can be smaller than 0.1 fm. The LightGBM algorithm shows an improved performance with respect to the CNN on the task in this work. By using the LightGBM's visualization algorithm, one can obtain the important feature map of the distribution of transverse momentum and rapidity, which may be helpful in inferring the impact parameter or centrality in heavy-ion experiments.

## Key claim
The average difference between the true impact parameter and the AI-predicted one can be smaller than 0.1 fm for Au+Au collisions at intermediate energies, but this precision is achieved only when the training and testing data are generated with the same model parameters; the precision degrades significantly (to about 0.3 fm or larger) when the model parameters are varied.

## Method
Au+Au collisions at beam energies of 0.2–1.0 GeV/nucleon and impact parameters 0–10 fm were simulated with the Ultrarelativistic Quantum Molecular Dynamics (UrQMD) transport model. Two-dimensional transverse momentum versus rapidity spectra of protons from each event were used as input to two machine learning algorithms: a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with two convolutional layers and a fully-connected layer, and a Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) based on gradient-boosted decision trees. The models were trained on 50,000 events and tested on 2,000 events, with performance quantified by the average absolute difference Δb between true and predicted impact parameters.

## Key numbers
- With 50,000 training events at 1.0 GeV/nucleon and a 30×30 input grid, Δb = 0.16 fm for CNN and Δb = 0.08 fm for LightGBM.
- Increasing training data from 2,000 to 50,000 events reduced Δb from about 0.3 fm to about 0.1 fm for both algorithms.
- At 1.0 GeV/nucleon, Δb was 0.12 fm for impact parameters 0–3 fm and 0.06 fm for 3–6 fm with LightGBM.
- At beam energy 0.2 GeV/nucleon, Δb increased to 0.43 fm (CNN) and 0.42 fm (LightGBM); at 0.6 GeV/nucleon, Δb = 0.15 fm (CNN) and 0.08 fm (LightGBM).
- When training on a soft equation of state at 1.0 GeV/nucleon and testing on a hard equation of state at 1.0 GeV/nucleon, Δb = 0.28 fm for both CNN and LightGBM; at 0.4 GeV/nucleon under the same cross-model test, Δb ≈ 0.88 fm.

## Context
This work extends earlier efforts using support vector machines and neural networks (e.g., Bass et al. 1990s) that achieved Δb ≈ 0.2 fm, by applying modern deep learning and gradient boosting methods to improve precision to below 0.1 fm under matched conditions. The study demonstrates that AI can extract impact parameter information from proton spectra more accurately than traditional centrality estimators such as total charged multiplicity (which yields an uncertainty of roughly 3 fm). The results enable future work using experimental data or multiple transport models to disentangle model-dependent effects, and suggest that the importance maps from LightGBM can identify which kinematic regions (e.g., near projectile/target rapidity at low transverse momentum) are most sensitive to impact parameter.
