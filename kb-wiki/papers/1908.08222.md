# Optimal Control for the Quantum Simulation of Nuclear Dynamics

> We propose a method for enacting the unitary time propagation of two interacting neutrons at leading order of chiral effective field theory by efficiently encoding the nuclear dynamics into a single multi-level quantum device. The emulated output of the quantum simulation shows that, by applying a single gate that draws on the underlying characteristics of the device, it is possible to observe multiple cycles of the nucleons' dynamics before the onset of decoherence. Owing to the signal's longevity, we can then extract spectroscopic properties of the simulated nuclear system. This allows us to validate the encoding of the nuclear Hamiltonian and the robustness of the simulation in the presence of quantum-hardware noise by comparing the extracted spectroscopic information to exact calculations. This work paves the way for transformative calculations of dynamical properties of nuclei on near-term quantum devices.

## Key claim

The authors propose a method for enacting unitary time propagation of two interacting neutrons at leading order of chiral effective field theory by encoding the nuclear dynamics into a single multi-level quantum device, but the demonstration is limited to a "frozen" approximation where the neutrons' relative position is fixed and only the spin-dependent part of the interaction is simulated, disregarding spatial wave function evolution.

## Method

The authors map the four spin states of two neutrons (up-up, up-down, down-up, down-down) onto the four lowest energy levels of a 3D transmon superconducting circuit. They use the gradient ascent pulse engineering (GRAPE) algorithm to numerically optimize a single 100-nanosecond control pulse that implements the short-time propagator of the spin-dependent nuclear interaction at leading order of chiral effective field theory. The performance is evaluated by solving a Markovian Lindblad master equation that includes realistic dissipation and dephasing noise, with the transmon energy relaxation time set to 30 microseconds and dephasing time set to 50 microseconds.

## Key numbers

- The pulse duration is 100 ns, with a maximum drive strength of 20 MHz (corresponding to a 50 ns Rabi period)
- The transmon anharmonicity used in simulations is 200 MHz
- The energy relaxation time T1 is 30 microseconds and dephasing time Tphi is 50 microseconds, yielding a total coherence time T2* of approximately 27 microseconds
- The optimization achieves an infidelity threshold of less than 10^{-4} after about 100 iterations
- The extracted eigenvalue differences from the noisy simulation are: 2.55(2) MeV (exact 2.5254 MeV), 3.41(3) MeV (exact 3.3951 MeV), and 5.93(1) MeV (exact 5.9205 MeV)
- The extracted absolute eigenvalues are: lambda_{0,1} = -2.3(2) MeV (exact -2.329 MeV), lambda_2 = 0.9(6) MeV (exact 1.066 MeV), lambda_3 = 3.6(2) MeV (exact 3.592 MeV)

## Context

This work extends the approach of hardware-efficient quantum computation using multi-level superconducting devices, building on prior demonstrations of high-fidelity operations in superconducting circuits by Chow (2010) and numerically optimized pulse sequences for quantum error correction by Ofek (2016) and Heeres (2017). It contrasts with earlier nuclear physics quantum simulations by Dumitrescu (2018) that used variational quantum eigensolver methods with schematic nuclear interaction models and were limited to studies without real-time unitary evolution. The method enables a classical-quantum co-processing protocol where spin propagation runs on a quantum processor while spatial propagation runs on classical computers, potentially addressing the exponential growth of spin configurations that limits quantum Monte Carlo methods for larger numbers of nucleons.
