# Unified Royer law revision for alpha-decay half-lives: shell corrections, pairing,and orbital-angular-momentum

> The Royer law is a widely used empirical relation for calculating alpha-decay half-lives; however, it requires 12 parity-dependent parameters.It exhibits systematic deviations near the shell closure. We propose an improved Royer law by adding a shell-correction term, an odd-even pairing indicator, and an orbital-angular-momentum contribution. This unified framework reduces the number of free parameters to just four, leading to significant improvements in accuracy. The root-mean-square deviation across 550 experimental data points decreases from 0.520 to 0.279, corresponding to a 66.7% reduction in parameters and 46.3% improvement in accuracy. Using this refined formalism, we predict alpha-decay half-lives for superheavy nuclei with atomic numbers.

# Unified Royer Law Revision for Alpha-Decay Half-Lives: Shell Corrections, Pairing, and Orbital-Angular-Momentum

## Key claim

The improved Royer law reduces the number of free parameters from 12 to 4 while improving accuracy, but only for the 550 ground-state-to-ground-state alpha decays in the fitting dataset that satisfy selection criteria including experimental uncertainties below 50% and branching-ratio uncertainties smaller than the branching ratio itself. The model's predictive power for superheavy nuclei with Z = 117-120 relies on using the WS4+RBF mass table for nuclei lacking experimental Q-values or binding energies.

## Method

The authors modified the original Royer law by adding three physically motivated terms: a shell-correction energy term E_sh (computed as the difference between experimental binding energy from AME2020 and a liquid-drop model binding energy fitted to 2463 nuclei), a unified pairing term [(-1)^Z + (-1)^N] that replaces parity-dependent parameter sets, and an orbital-angular-momentum term l(l+1)/2 using the minimum allowed l value from angular-momentum and parity conservation rules. The four free parameters (a, b, c, d) were determined by least-squares fitting to 550 experimental alpha-decay half-lives drawn from NUBASE2020 plus 11 additional nuclei from recent publications, with the dataset comprising 406 favored decays (l=0) and 144 unfavored decays (l not equal to 0).

## Key numbers

- Root-mean-square deviation across 550 experimental data points: 0.520 for original Royer law, 0.279 for improved Royer law (46.3% improvement)
- Number of free parameters reduced from 12 to 4 (66.7% reduction)
- For 406 favored alpha decays (l=0): RMSD reduced from 0.311 to 0.242
- For 144 unfavored alpha decays (l not equal to 0): RMSD reduced from 0.871 to 0.363 (58.3% improvement)
- Fitted parameters: a = -28.1919 +/- 0.1510 s, b = -1.0853 +/- 0.0055 s, c = 1.6260 +/- 0.0039 s*sqrt(MeV), d = 0.1078 +/- 0.0026 s
- Liquid-drop model parameters fitted to 2463 nuclei: a_v = 15.5287 MeV, a_s = 16.9043 MeV, a_a = 91.9686 MeV, a_c = 0.7025 MeV, a_p = 12.4439 MeV, with RMSD of 3.02 MeV
- Comparison with other formulas for total 550 nuclei: DZR model (Ref. 2020gyy) RMSD = 0.358, Wang et al. (Ref. Wangzy2015) RMSD = 0.334, Ismail et al. (Ref. Ismail2022) RMSD = 0.393

## Context

This work extends the original Royer law (2000) which required 12 parity-dependent parameters and showed systematic deviations near the N=126 shell closure, particularly for even-even polonium isotopes. The improved formula builds on prior efforts by Wang et al. (2015) who added a phenomenological shell-correction factor with a fixed constant S=0.5, and by the Deng-Zhang-Royer (DZR) formula (2020) and Ismail et al. (2022) which incorporated angular-momentum terms. The unified treatment of pairing effects using a single term [(-1)^Z + (-1)^N] is novel, and the shell-correction energy approach was previously employed in spontaneous fission half-life calculations by Bao et al. (2015) and Karpov et al. (2012). The improved formula enables predictions for superheavy nuclei with Z=117-120 and suggests possible magic numbers or neutron subshell structures at N_d = 178, 184, and 196.
