# Fermi breakup and the Statistical Multifragmentation Model

> We demonstrate the close similarity of a generalized Fermi breakup model, in which densities of excited states are taken into account, to the microcanonical statistical multifragmentation model used to describe the desintegration of highly excited fragments of nuclear reactions.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that a generalized Fermi breakup model (FBM) which includes densities of excited states is mathematically equivalent to the microcanonical statistical multifragmentation model (SMM), provided the one-fragment configuration is excluded from the SMM and the FBM is extended to include particle-unstable excited states.

## Method

The authors generalized the standard FBM phase-space integral by replacing ground-state spin multiplicities with energy-dependent level densities for each fragment, then performed the integrals analytically using Laplace transforms and the method of steepest descent to obtain an expression for the density of final states. They compared this generalized FBM to the microcanonical SMM of Souza et al. (2009) by showing that both models use the same Helmholtz free energy to define entropy and the same energy conservation condition to determine the configuration temperature. Numerical comparisons were performed for 62 MeV protons incident on 16O, using the Monte Carlo exciton cascade model of Blann and Chadwick (1990s) to generate the primary compound nucleus population.

## Key numbers

- For 62 MeV protons on 16O, the primary 16O compound nucleus population corresponds to 66% of the reaction cross section of approximately 400 mb, with 15O and 15N compound nuclei accounting for another 32% of the reaction cross section.
- The mean primary fragment multiplicity decreases from 2.8 for the ground-state-only calculation to 2.4 when particle-stable states are included, to 2.3 when all excited states from the RIPL-2 library are included.
- The contribution of three-fragment modes to the primary fragment distribution is about 80% in the ground-state-only calculation, about 40% in the particle-stable calculation, and only 25% in the calculation containing all excited states.
- The steepest-descent approximation yields a multiplicative factor R(3(n-1)/2) that is approximately 1.06 for n=2, 1.03 for n=3, and decreases to one as n increases.
- The FBM normalization volume is typically taken as V_n = (1+chi)V_0 with chi=1, while the SMM uses V_n = chi V_0 with chi between 2 and 5, excluding fragment volumes.

## Context

This work extends the formal connection between two widely used nuclear decay models: the Fermi breakup model, originally proposed by Fermi (1950) for high-energy cosmic-ray collisions and later applied to spallation reactions by Lepore (1960s) and Epherre (1970s), and the statistical multifragmentation model developed systematically by Bondorf et al. (1985) and Botvina et al. (1990s). The paper shows that the generalized FBM can replace the arbitrary division between sequential compound-nucleus decay for heavy nuclei and Fermi breakup for light nuclei, enabling a consistent equilibrium statistical decay model where decay modes are determined by available energy rather than modeler choice. The authors note that work toward an improved FBM/SMM combining Weisskopf-like two-body decay with n-body extensions is in progress.
