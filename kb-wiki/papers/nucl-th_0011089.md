# Two-body effects in coherent eta-meson photoproduction on the deuteron in the region of the S_11(1535) resonance

> Coherent $η$-meson photoproduction on the deuteron has been studied, where the emphasis is on the relative importance of two-body contributions from hadronic rescattering and electromagnetic meson exchange currents besides the impulse approximation. For the elementary photoproduction amplitude a coupled resonance model developed by Bennhold and Tanabe has been used which fits reasonably well the experimental data. The rescattering effects are treated within a coupled channel approach considering the intermediate excitation of the $P_{11}(1440)$, $D_{13}(1520)$, and $S_{11}(1535)$ nucleon resonances. The hadronic interaction between nucleon and resonances is modeled by one boson exchange potentials, which we have considered both in the static approximation as well as fully retarded. The sum of all considered two-body effects results in an enhancement of the total cross section between 10 in the maximum and 25 percent closer to threshold around 680 MeV if the hadronic interaction is treated retarded. This enhancement shows up in the differential cross sections mainly at backward angles. It increases steadily from only a few percent at $0^\circ$ to more than a factor two at $180^\circ$ for a photon energy of 680 MeV. Two-body effects become also significant in certain polarization observables. Finally, no discrepancy has been found for the ratio of the isoscalar amplitude to the proton amplitude between coherent and incoherent $η$-photoproduction on the deuteron due to a nonvanishing complex and energy dependent phase relation.

## Key claim

The previously reported discrepancy between the ratio of the isoscalar amplitude to the proton amplitude extracted from coherent versus incoherent η-photoproduction on the deuteron is resolved by including a nonvanishing complex and energy-dependent phase relation, rather than by two-body effects alone.

## Method

The authors used a coupled-channel resonance model for elementary η-photoproduction on the nucleon, including the S11(1535), D13(1520), and P11(1440) resonances. For the deuteron, they computed the impulse approximation plus two-body contributions from hadronic rescattering (via coupled Lippmann-Schwinger equations with one-boson exchange potentials) and electromagnetic meson exchange currents, treating the hadronic interaction both in the static approximation and with full retardation.

## Key numbers

- Total cross section enhancement from all two-body effects: between 10% at the maximum and 25% closer to threshold around 680 MeV (with retarded hadronic interaction).
- Differential cross section enhancement at 680 MeV: from a few percent at 0° to more than a factor of two at 180°.
- Ratio of neutron to proton total cross sections at resonance: |An/Ap|^2 = 0.68 (approximately 2/3).
- Ratio of isoscalar amplitude to proton amplitude: As/Ap = 0.25 e^(-i 0.969).
- Neutron amplitude at resonance: An = (-114 - i1.7) × 10^(-3) GeV^(-1/2).
- Proton amplitude at resonance: Ap = (120.9 - i66.1) × 10^(-3) GeV^(-1/2).

## Context

This work extends earlier impulse-approximation studies by Effenborn and Beck (no year given) and addresses the discrepancy reported by Hoffmann and collaborators (no year given), who found (As/Ap)_coh = 0.2 versus (As/Ap)_inc = 0.09. It improves upon the first-order rescattering calculation of Hoshi et al. (no year given) and the one-loop approximation of Halderson and Rosenthal (no year given), which both found large η-exchange dominance, and the multiple-scattering approach of Kamalov et al. (no year given), which found very small two-body effects. The present work demonstrates that π-exchange contributions, previously neglected, are important and that retardation in the hadronic interaction reverses the sign of the two-body effect compared to the static approximation used in the authors' own earlier work (Ritz and Arenhoevel, no year given). The results enable reliable extraction of the neutron amplitude from deuteron data and clarify the phase information accessible in coherent versus incoherent reactions.
