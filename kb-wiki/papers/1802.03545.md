# Correspondence between the physics of extremal black holes and that of stable heavy atomic nuclei

> Extremal black holes are immune of Hawking evaporation. On the other hand, some heavy atomic nuclei feature extraordinary stability to spontaneous transmutations changing their mass numbers. The fact that extremal black holes and stable nuclei share a common trait, that of defying spontaneous ejection of their constituents, suggests that a good part of nuclear physics is modelled on physics of extremal black holes through a simple version of gauge/gravity duality. A general criterion for discriminating between stable and unstable microscopic systems can be formulated to gain a new insight into some imperfectly understood phenomena, such as instability of truly neutral spinless particles (Higgs bosons, $π_0$, quarkonia, glueballs).

## Key claim

The paper proposes a holographic correspondence between extremal black holes (which do not evaporate) and stable heavy atomic nuclei (which resist spontaneous transmutation), arguing that both systems are governed by "semiclassical, feeble quantum" dynamics. The authors explicitly acknowledge that this is a speculative extension of the AdS/CFT conjecture, requiring additional assumptions to relate 5-dimensional gravity to 4-dimensional nuclear physics.

## Method

The authors map the 5-dimensional Dirac equation in an extremal Reissner-Nordström anti-de Sitter black hole background onto a 4-dimensional Dirac equation for a single quark moving in the mean field of a nucleus. They show that the effective potential for a Dirac particle in the extremal black hole background can form a spherical cavity (infinite square well) under a specific condition (Equation 34), which they argue is analogous to the pseudospin symmetry condition (U_S = -U_V + constant) used in nuclear physics. The radial equations from both sides are reduced to Schrödinger-like equations, and the effective potentials are compared qualitatively.

## Key numbers

- The nuclear radius relation R = R_0 A^{1/3} is reproduced with R_0 ≈ 1 fm, using parameters α_s = 0.7 and σ = 0.1 GeV², with quark mass m = 0.33 GeV
- The pseudospin symmetry condition U_S = -U_V + C_c shifts the quark mass from current to constituent: m → m_c = m + C_c
- The effective potential singularity occurs at r_sc = [(ε + m) + √((ε + m)² + 4σα_s)] / (2σ), with example parameters m = 0.33 GeV, ε = 1 GeV, α_s = 0.7, σ = 0.14 GeV², κ = 1
- The extremal black hole condition requires Q² < (8/9)M² (Equation 17)
- The confinement condition for the Dirac particle in the black hole background is (mL)² x_* - (4Λ₀²(eQ)²)/(L²x_*) ≥ 1/(4Λ₀²) - κ² (Equation 34)
- The calculated nuclear magnetic dipoles agree with observed values within approximately 20%

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous phenomenological approach (Kosyakov, Popov, Vronsky, 2015, 2017) where pseudospin and spin symmetry conditions were used to describe nuclei and free hadrons in terms of a single quark degree of freedom. The paper contrasts with the meson-exchange approach to nuclear forces (Weinberg, 1990; Epelbaum; Machleidt) and with earlier bag model attempts (Petry) which gave R ~ N^{1/4} rather than the observed R ~ A^{1/3}. The holographic mapping is a simplified version of the AdS/CFT correspondence (Maldacena, 1998; Witten, 1998; Gubser, Klebanov, Polyakov, 1998), reduced from quantum field theory to single-particle quantum mechanics. The authors suggest this approach could provide insight into the instability of truly neutral spinless particles such as Higgs bosons, π₀, quarkonia, and glueballs.
