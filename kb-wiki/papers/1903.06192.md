# Self-consistent calculation of the reactor antineutrino spectra including forbidden transitions

> With the goal of determining the $θ_{13}$ neutrino oscillation mixing angle, the measurements of reactor antineutrino fluxes at the Double Chooz, RENO and Daya Bay experimental facilities have uncovered a systematic discrepancy between the number of observed events and theoretical expectations. In the \emph{ab initio} approach, the total reactor antineutrino spectrum is a weighted sum of spectra resulting from all $β$ branches of all fission products in the reactor core. At all three facilities a systematic deviation of the number of observed events from the number of predicted events was noticed, i.e., approximately 6\% of the predicted neutrinos were not observed. This discrepancy was named the reactor neutrino anomaly. In theoretical studies it is assumed that all the decays are allowed in shape, but a quarter of all transitions are actually forbidden and may have a complex energy dependence that will affect the total reactor antineutrino spectrum. In order to estimate the effect of forbidden transitions, we perform a fully self-consistent calculation of spectra from all contributing transitions and compare the results with a purely allowed approximation.

## Key claim

The self-consistent calculation including first-forbidden transitions reduces the theoretical antineutrino spectrum of \(^{235}\)U by up to 2% in the 2.5–6 MeV region, roughly half the size of the reactor antineutrino anomaly, but the overall calculated spectra still deviate significantly from measurements due to limitations in modeling nuclear structure, especially for odd-A nuclei and low-lying states.

## Method

The authors used the relativistic Hartree-Bogoliubov (RHB) model for nuclear ground states and the proton-neutron relativistic quasiparticle random phase approximation (pn-RQRPA) in the canonical single-nucleon basis to compute excited states and \(\beta\)-decay transitions for all fission fragments. They generated electron and antineutrino spectra for each transition using energy-dependent shape factors for first-forbidden transitions (including \(0^{-}\), \(1^{-}\), and \(2^{-}\) multipolarities), weighted these by branching ratios and cumulative fission yields from the JAEA database, and summed them to obtain total spectra per fission for \(^{235}\)U, \(^{238}\)U, \(^{239}\)Pu, and \(^{241}\)Pu.

## Key numbers

- The reduction in the theoretical antineutrino spectrum of \(^{235}\)U due to first-forbidden transitions is up to **2%** in the energy region from **2.5 to 6 MeV**, with the largest effect centered around **5 MeV**.
- The total change of the antineutrino spectra from including first-forbidden transitions is approximately **3%**, comparable to the magnitude of the reactor antineutrino anomaly (approximately **6%**).
- The four isotopes \(^{235}\)U, \(^{238}\)U, \(^{239}\)Pu, and \(^{241}\)Pu are responsible for more than **99.7%** of all antineutrinos from a reactor core.
- \(^{235}\)U and \(^{239}\)Pu contribute more than **90%** of fissions in a reactor.

## Context

This work extends the approach of Hayes (2014), who found that uncertainties from forbidden transitions equal approximately 4% and treated all unique forbidden transitions as unique first-forbidden, by performing the first fully self-consistent theoretical calculation of reactor antineutrino spectra including explicit shape factors for each first-forbidden transition. It also builds on microscopic calculations by Hayen (2019) that showed differences in cumulative spectra when explicitly calculating shape factors. The results enable more precise determination of reactor antineutrino spectra for high-precision neutrino oscillation experiments such as Double Chooz, RENO, and Daya Bay, and suggest that proper treatment of first-forbidden transitions is essential for resolving the reactor antineutrino anomaly.
