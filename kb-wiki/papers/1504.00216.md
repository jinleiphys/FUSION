# Diffuse approximation to the kinetic theory in a Fermi system

> We suggest the diffuse approach to the relaxation processes within the kinetic theory for the Wigner distribution function. The diffusion and drift coefficients are evaluated taking into consideration the interparticle collisions on the distorted Fermi surface. Using the finite range interaction, we show that the momentum dependence of the diffuse coefficient $D_{p}(p)$ has a maximum at Fermi momentum $p=p_{F}$ whereas the drift coefficient $K_{p}(p)$ is negative and reaches a minimum at $p\approx p_{F}$. For a cold Fermi system the diffusion coefficient takes the non-zero value which is caused by the relaxation on the distorted Fermi-surface at temperature $T=0$. The numerical solution of the diffusion equation was performed for the particle-hole excitation in a nucleus with $A=16$. The evaluated relaxation time $τ_{r}\approx 8.3\cdot 10^{-23}\mathrm{s}$ is close to the corresponding result in a nuclear Fermi-liquid obtained within the kinetic theory.

# Diffuse approximation to the kinetic theory in a Fermi system

## Key claim

The authors propose that the collisional Landau-Vlasov kinetic equation for the Wigner distribution function can be reduced to a diffusion equation, provided the interparticle interaction ensures forward scattering with small momentum transfer. The diffusion coefficient remains non-zero even at zero temperature due to relaxation on the distorted Fermi surface, contrary to what a naive classical diffusion picture would suggest.

## Method

The authors start from the kinetic equation for the Wigner distribution function with a collision integral in the Born approximation. They expand the transition probabilities and distribution functions over small momentum transfer s = p3 - p1, using a finite-range Gaussian interparticle potential to ensure the smallness of s. This expansion reduces the collision integral to a diffusion equation with momentum-dependent diffusion coefficient Dp(p) and drift coefficient Kp(p), which are evaluated numerically for a nuclear Fermi-liquid using a Fermi distribution function at various temperatures. The diffusion equation is then solved numerically for an initial particle-hole (1p1h) excitation in a nucleus with A = 16 to obtain the time evolution of the distribution function and the relaxation time.

## Key numbers

- Diffusion coefficient at Fermi momentum and zero temperature: Dp(pF, T=0) = 2.1 × 10^(-22) MeV^2·fm^(-2)·s
- Relaxation time for a 1p1h excitation in A=16 nucleus: τr ≈ 8.3 × 10^(-23) s
- Fermi energy used: εF = 37 MeV
- Potential parameters: r0 = 0.8 fm, v0 = -33 MeV, providing total in-medium nucleon-nucleon cross section σtot ≈ 20 mb
- Diffusion coefficient near Fermi momentum: Dp(pF) ≈ 10^(-22) MeV^2·fm^(-2)·s
- Drift coefficient near Fermi momentum: Kp(pF) ≈ -10^(-23) MeV·fm^(-2)·s
- Initial excitation energy for the numerical calculation: Eex = 30 MeV

## Context

This work extends the diffuse approach of Lipi (1981) to the kinetic theory of Fermi systems, specifically applying it to nuclear Fermi-liquid theory. The authors compare their results to the phenomenological transport coefficients used earlier by Wols (1982) and find agreement. The calculated relaxation time falls within the typical collisional relaxation time range of 10^(-23) to 10^(-22) s for nuclei, consistent with earlier estimates by Bert (1978), Shko (2005), Khol (1982), and Wegm (1974). This approach enables the description of damping of collective and particle-hole excitations as well as large-amplitude dynamics in finite Fermi systems.
