# A Global Model of $β^-$-Decay Half-Lives Using Neural Networks

> Statistical modeling of nuclear data using artificial neural networks (ANNs) and, more recently, support vector machines (SVMs), is providing novel approaches to systematics that are complementary to phenomenological and semi-microscopic theories. We present a global model of $β^-$-decay halflives of the class of nuclei that decay 100% by $β^-$ mode in their ground states. A fully-connected multilayered feed forward network has been trained using the Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm, Bayesian regularization, and cross-validation. The halflife estimates generated by the model are discussed and compared with the available experimental data, with previous results obtained with neural networks, and with estimates coming from traditional global nuclear models. Predictions of the new neural-network model are given for nuclei far from stability, with particular attention to those involved in r-process nucleosynthesis. This study demonstrates that in the framework of the $β^-$-decay problem considered here, global models based on ANNs can at least match the predictive performance of the best conventional global models rooted in nuclear theory. Accordingly, such statistical models can provide a valuable tool for further mapping of the nuclidic chart.

## Key claim

The authors claim that a feedforward artificial neural network (FF-ANN) model can at least match the predictive performance of the best conventional global models rooted in nuclear theory for beta-minus decay half-lives, but this claim is qualified by the fact that the prediction set used for evaluation primarily involves interpolation rather than extrapolation to unknown regions.

## Method

A fully-connected multilayered feedforward network with architecture [3-5-5-5-5-1|116] was trained using the Levenberg-Marquardt optimization algorithm combined with Bayesian regularization and cross-validation. The three input units encoded atomic number Z, neutron number N, and a pairing constant delta, while the single output unit decoded the base-10 logarithm of the beta-minus half-life. The network was trained on 503 nuclides (learning set) drawn from a restricted set of 843 nuclides with half-lives less than 10^6 seconds, with 167 used for validation and 168 reserved for the prediction set.

## Key numbers

* RMSE on the learning set: 0.53; on the validation set: 0.60; on the prediction set: 0.65; on the overall set: 0.57 (all in log10(seconds))
* MAE on the learning set: 0.38; on the validation set: 0.41; on the prediction set: 0.46; on the overall set: 0.40 (all in log10(seconds))
* For the overall set with half-lives less than 10^6 seconds: 92.0% of nuclides predicted within a factor of 10, 82.8% within a factor of 5, and 53.5% within a factor of 2
* For the prediction set with half-lives less than 10^6 seconds: 90.5% of nuclides predicted within a factor of 10, 79.2% within a factor of 5, and 49.4% within a factor of 2
* Experimental half-lives in the dataset range from 0.15 x 10^-2 s for 35Na to 2.43 x 10^23 s for 113Cd

## Context

This work extends prior neural-network modeling of nuclear half-life systematics by Costiris, Mavrommatis, Gernoth, and Clark (2006) and provides a more sophisticated alternative to conventional models such as the NBSC+pnQRPA model of Homma and coworkers and the FRDM+pnQRPA model of Moller and coworkers. The model enables improved predictions for nuclei far from stability, particularly those involved in r-process nucleosynthesis, and demonstrates that statistical approaches can complement phenomenological and semi-microscopic theories for mapping the nuclidic chart.
