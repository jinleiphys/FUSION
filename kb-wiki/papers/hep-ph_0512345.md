# Fragmentation, NRQCD and Factorization in Heavy Quarkonium Production

> We discuss factorization in heavy quarkonium production in high energy collisions using NRQCD. Infrared divergences at NNLO are not matched by conventional NRQCD matrix elements. However, we show that gauge invariance and factorization require that conventional NRQCD production matrix elements be modified to include Wilson lines or non-abelian gauge links. With this modification NRQCD factorization for heavy quarkonium production is restored at NNLO.

## Key claim

The authors show that NRQCD factorization for heavy quarkonium production at next-to-next-to-leading order (NNLO) is restored only if the conventional NRQCD production matrix elements are modified to include Wilson lines (non-abelian gauge links), because infrared divergences at NNLO are not matched by conventional NRQCD matrix elements.

## Method

The authors analyze representative NNLO diagrams for the gluon-to-charm-anticharm fragmentation function in eikonal approximation, focusing on diagrams that connect octet to singlet quark states and are not topologically factorized. They compute the infrared pole contributions from diagrams with three-gluon interactions and compare them to the pole terms generated by gauge-completed NRQCD matrix elements containing Wilson lines.

## Key numbers

- The noncancelling real infrared pole in the fragmentation function at NNLO is given by \(\Sigma(P,q,l) = \alpha_s^2 \frac{4}{3 \varepsilon} \frac{\vec{q}^2}{4m_c^2} = \alpha_s^2 \frac{1}{3 \varepsilon} \frac{\vec{v}^2}{4}\), where \(\vec{v}\) is the relative velocity of the heavy quark pair.
- The analysis is performed to order \(v^2\) in the relative velocity expansion.

## Context

This work extends the NRQCD factorization framework of Bodwin, Braaten, and Lepage (1995) by identifying a failure of topological factorization at NNLO that was not anticipated in the original formulation. The paper builds on the authors' prior work (Nayak, Qiu, and Sterman, 2005) and enables a consistent treatment of heavy quarkonium production at higher orders in perturbation theory, with implications for interpreting data from collider experiments such as those at the Tevatron.
