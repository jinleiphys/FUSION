# Shear modulus of the hadron-quark mixed phase

> Robust arguments predict that a hadron-quark mixed phase may exist in the cores of some "neutron" stars. Such a phase forms a crystalline lattice with a shear modulus higher than that of the crust due to the high density and charge separation, even allowing for the effects of charge screening. This may lead to strong continuous gravitational-wave emission from rapidly rotating neutron stars and gravitational-wave bursts associated with magnetar flares and pulsar glitches. We present the first detailed calculation of the shear modulus of the mixed phase. We describe the quark phase using the bag model plus first-order quantum chromodynamics corrections and the hadronic phase using relativistic mean-field models with parameters allowed by the most massive pulsar. Most of the calculation involves treating the "pasta phases" of the lattice via dimensional continuation, and we give a general method for computing dimensionally continued lattice sums including the Debye model of charge screening. We compute all the shear components of the elastic modulus tensor and angle average them to obtain the effective (scalar) shear modulus for the case where the mixed phase is a polycrystal. We include the contributions from changing the cell size, which are necessary for the stability of the lower-dimensional portions of the lattice. Stability also requires a minimum surface tension, generally tens of MeV/fm^2 depending on the equation of state. We find that the shear modulus can be a few times 10^33 erg/cm^3, two orders of magnitude higher than the first estimate, over a significant fraction of the maximum mass stable star for certain parameter choices.

## Key claim

The shear modulus of the hadron-quark mixed phase in neutron star cores can reach a few times 10^33 erg/cm^3, two orders of magnitude higher than the first estimate, but this result depends on specific equation-of-state parameter choices and requires a minimum surface tension generally tens of MeV/fm^2 for lattice stability.

## Method

The authors model the hadronic phase using relativistic mean-field theory and the quark phase using the MIT bag model with first-order QCD corrections, then apply the Gibbs equilibrium conditions to determine the mixed phase's bulk properties. They treat the varying dimensionality of the "pasta phases" (spherical drops, cylindrical rods, and slabs) using dimensional continuation, developing a generalized Ewald method for dimensionally continued lattice sums that includes Debye-model charge screening. They compute all shear components of the elastic modulus tensor and angle-average them to obtain an effective scalar shear modulus for a polycrystalline mixed phase, including contributions from cell-size changes necessary for stability of lower-dimensional lattices.

## Key numbers

- Shear modulus can reach a few times 10^33 erg/cm^3, two orders of magnitude higher than the first estimate by Owen (2005)
- Minimum surface tension required for stability is generally tens of MeV/fm^2, dependent on the equation of state
- For the Hy1 EOS, the maximum mass of a stable hybrid star is 2.057 solar masses, with hybrid matter occupying up to 57.7% of the star's radius
- For the LKR1 EOS, the maximum mass is 1.955 solar masses, with hybrid matter occupying up to 72.5% of the star's radius
- The quark chemical potentials in the densest regions of stable stars reach up to approximately 480 MeV
- The blob energy density is less than or approximately 2% of the total energy density; the lattice electrostatic pressure is less than or approximately 3% of the bulk pressure

## Context

This work provides the first detailed calculation of the shear modulus of the hadron-quark mixed phase, significantly improving upon the rough estimate by Owen (2005) and the partial calculation by Nayyar (2008). The results extend the earlier work on nuclear pasta phases by Ravenhall, Pethick, and Wilson (1983) and the mixed-phase lattice structure studies by Heiselberg, Pethick, and Staubo (1993) and Glendenning (1991, 1992). The higher shear modulus values enable stronger continuous gravitational-wave emission from rapidly rotating neutron stars and gravitational-wave bursts from magnetar flares and pulsar glitches, making these predictions testable with current and upcoming gravitational-wave detectors.
