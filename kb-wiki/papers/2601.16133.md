# Computational schemes for the Magnus expansion of the in-medium similarity renormalization group

> The in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG) is a popular many-body method used for computations of nuclei. It solves the many-body Schrödinger equation through a continuous unitary transformation of the many-body Hamiltonian. The IMSRG transformation is typically truncated at the normal-ordered two-body level, the IMSRG(2), but recently several approaches have been developed to capture the effects of normal-ordered three-body operators, the IMSRG(3). In particular, a factorized approximation to the IMSRG(3) proposes to capture the leading effects of three-body operators at the same computational cost as the IMSRG(2) approximation. This approach often employs an approximate scheme for solving the IMSRG equations, the so-called hunter-gatherer scheme. In this work, I study the uncertainty associated with this scheme. I find that the hunter-gatherer scheme differs by up to $7\,\mathrm{MeV}$ for ground-state energies and $0.5\,\mathrm{MeV}$ for excitation energies from standard IMSRG(2) approaches. These differences are in some cases comparable to the expected size of IMSRG(3) corrections.

## Key claim

The hunter-gatherer scheme for solving the IMSRG equations introduces uncertainties that are small for single-reference calculations but can become comparable to expected IMSRG(3) corrections (up to 7 MeV for ground-state energies and 0.5 MeV for excitation energies) in valence-space calculations, especially for larger valence spaces.

## Method

The author compares three computational schemes for solving the IMSRG(2) equations: direct integration of the flow equation, the split Magnus approach (which splits the unitary transformation into small steps based on a threshold parameter epsilon_split), and the hunter-gatherer scheme (which uses only two Magnus operators, a "hunter" that remains small and a "gatherer" that accumulates the transformation). The comparison is performed for single-reference IMSRG(2) and valence-space IMSRG(2) calculations of several nuclei using the 1.8/2.0 (EM) Hamiltonian in model spaces of 13 major harmonic oscillator shells with hbar-omega = 16 MeV.

## Key numbers

- The hunter-gatherer scheme differs from standard IMSRG(2) approaches by up to 7 MeV for ground-state energies and 0.5 MeV for excitation energies
- For single-reference IMSRG(2) calculations of Ca-48, the split Magnus approach with epsilon_split = 2.0 differs from the flow equation result by only 22 keV for the ground-state energy
- For single-reference IMSRG(2) calculations of Ca-48, the hunter-gatherer approach deviates from the flow equation by approximately 300 keV and 0.0015 fm for the charge radius
- For VS-IMSRG(2) calculations of Ca-48 with a neutron pf-shell valence space, the hunter-gatherer approach differs by over 1.2 MeV for the ground-state energy and over 0.004 fm for the charge radius
- For VS-IMSRG(2) calculations of Ca-48 with a full pf-shell valence space, the hunter-gatherer approach differs by over 2 MeV for the ground-state energy and over 0.008 fm for the charge radius
- For Bi-209, the hunter-gatherer scheme gives a ground-state energy of -1642.9 MeV compared to -1635.8 MeV from the split Magnus approach
- The expected IMSRG(3) corrections are estimated to be approximately 3 MeV for ground-state energies and 0.03 fm for charge radii (Heinz 2025)

## Context

This work extends the analysis of the IMSRG(3f2) factorized approximation introduced by He (2024) and the IMSRG(3) developments by Heinz (2021, 2025) and Stroberg (2024). The hunter-gatherer scheme was previously standardized for the Magnus expansion approach (Stroberg 2024). The findings show that while the hunter-gatherer scheme is reliable for single-reference calculations, its uncertainty grows with valence space size and should be quantified when used, particularly for the IMSRG(3f2) approximation. The results enable improved uncertainty quantification for IMSRG calculations and motivate development of improved approximations that reduce the scheme's uncertainty.
