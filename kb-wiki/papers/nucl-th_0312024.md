# Observable implications of geometrical and dynamical aspects of freeze-out in heavy ion collisions

> Using an analytical parameterization of hadronic freeze-out in relativistic heavy ion collisions, we present a detailed study of the connections between features of the freeze-out configuration and physical observables. We focus especially on anisotropic freeze-out configurations (expected in general for collisions at finite impact parameter), azimuthally-sensitive HBT interferometry, and final-state interactions between non-identical particles. Model calculations are compared with data taken in the first year of running at RHIC; while not perfect, good agreement is found, raising the hope that a consistent understanding of the full freeze-out scenario at RHIC is possible, an important first step towards understanding the physics of the system prior to freeze-out.

## Key claim

Using an analytical blast-wave parameterization of freeze-out that includes anisotropic flow and anisotropic source shapes, the authors find good but not perfect agreement with first-year RHIC data across multiple observables, raising the hope that a consistent understanding of the full freeze-out scenario at RHIC is possible, though the model has eight freely-tunable parameters and remains only a parameterization with little predictive power.

## Method

The authors construct an emission function based on a longitudinally boost-invariant blast-wave model with an elliptical transverse source shape, a surface diffuseness parameter, a Gaussian distribution in proper time, and a collective flow rapidity that depends linearly on the normalized elliptical radius with a second-order azimuthal oscillation. They calculate single-particle transverse momentum spectra, elliptic flow, azimuthally-sensitive HBT radii, and non-identical particle correlations by integrating the emission function over space-time coordinates, and compare these calculations to published RHIC data for Au+Au collisions at sqrt(s_NN) = 130 GeV.

## Key numbers

- The source weighting function uses a Fermi-Dirac form with surface diffuseness parameter a_s; setting a_s = 0 gives a hard-edge (box) profile, while a_s ≈ 0.3 approximates a Gaussian shape.
- The transverse flow rapidity is given by ρ(r, φ_s) = r̃(ρ_0 + ρ_2 cos(2φ_b)), where r̃ is the normalized elliptical radius and φ_b is the boost azimuthal angle.
- The proper time distribution is Gaussian with mean τ_0 and width Δτ; the τ integral yields H_0 = √(2π)·Δτ·τ_0 for the zeroth moment.
- The relation between the STAR anisotropy parameter s_2 and the geometric anisotropy in the blast-wave model (for ρ_2 = 0) is s_2 = (1/2)[(R_y/R_x)^2 - 1]/[(R_y/R_x)^2 + 1].

## Context

This work extends earlier blast-wave parameterizations by Schnedermann, Sollfrank, and Heinz (1993) and by Huovinen, Kolb, Heinz, Ruuskanen, and Voloshin (2001) to include anisotropic source shapes, azimuthally-sensitive HBT, and non-identical particle correlations. It addresses the difficulty faced by hydrodynamic models (Heinz and Kolb, 2002) and hybrid transport models like AMPT (Zhang, Ko, Li, and Lin, 2000-2001) in simultaneously reproducing spectra, elliptic flow, and HBT radii with a single parameter set. The study enables future comparisons with as-yet unpublished RHIC analyses of azimuthally-sensitive HBT and non-identical particle correlations, providing feedback to theorists constructing dynamical models of the collision.
