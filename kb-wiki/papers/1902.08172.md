# Scattering amplitudes versus potentials in nuclear effective field theory: search for a potential compromise

> In effective field theory physical quantities, in particular observables, are expressed as a power series in terms of a small expansion parameter. For non-perturbative systems, for instance nuclear physics, this requires the non-perturbative treatment of at least part of the interaction (or the potential, if one is dealing with a non-relativistic system), while the rest of the interaction is included as perturbations. This is not entirely trivial and as a consequence different interpretations on how to treat these systems have appeared. A practical approach is to expand the effective potential, where this potential is later fully iterated in the Schroedinger equation for obtaining amplitudes and observables. The expectation is that this will lead to observables that will have an implicit power counting expansion. Here I explicitly check whether the amplitudes (when expanded according to the counting) are actually following the same power counting as the potential. It happens that reality does not necessarily conform to expectations and the amplitudes will sometimes violate the power counting with which the potential has been expanded. A more formal approach is to formulate the expansion directly in terms of amplitudes and observables, which is the original aim of the effective field theory idea. Yet this second approach is technically complicated. I explore here the possibility of constructing potentials that when fully iterated will make sure that amplitudes are indeed expansible in terms of a small expansion parameter.

## Key claim

The paper explicitly checks whether iterating an effective-field-theory (EFT) potential in the Schroedinger equation yields scattering amplitudes that follow the same power counting as the potential, and finds that amplitudes "will sometimes violate the power counting with which the potential has been expanded," particularly in the \(^1S_0\) channel at momenta above about 100 MeV.

## Method

The author computes the \(^1S_0\) phase shift at N\(^2\)LO (including terms up to \(Q^3\)) using the standard Weinberg prescription (full iteration of the expanded potential) and compares it to the phase shift obtained by expanding the scattering amplitude perturbatively order by order, with subleading corrections treated in first-order perturbation theory. The contact-range couplings in the amplitude-based expansion are fitted to the phase shifts from the potential-based (Weinberg) calculation, and the difference between the two is used to extract a breakdown scale \(M\) via a defined truncation-error criterion.

## Key numbers

* The breakdown scale for the \(^1S_0\) channel in the Weinberg counting (with a momentum-space cutoff \(\Lambda = 400\ \mathrm{MeV}\)) is \(M = 210\ \mathrm{MeV}\).
* For the second-generation potential of Epelbaum, Gloeckle and Meissner (2003), the breakdown scales are \(M = 310\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(^1S_0\)) and \(M = 270\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(^3P_0\)).
* For the third-generation potential of Gezerlis et al. (2014), the breakdown scales are \(M = 380\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(^1S_0\)) and \(M = 190\ \mathrm{MeV}\) (\(^3P_0\)).
* In an alternative counting where two-pion exchange is promoted to leading order and one-pion exchange demoted to N\(^2\)LO, the breakdown scale rises to \(M = 400\ \mathrm{MeV}\) for the \(^1S_0\) channel.

## Context

This work extends the long-standing debate on whether the Weinberg prescription for nuclear EFT (Weinberg, 1990, 1991) yields observables with a well-defined power counting, following earlier toy-model studies by Epelbaum (2009) and renormalization-group analyses by Nogga, Timmermans, and van Kolck (2005) and by Long and van Kolck (2007, 2011, 2012). The paper shows that the failure of power counting in the Weinberg approach is not merely a renormalization artifact at hard cutoffs but appears already at moderate cutoffs (\(\Lambda = 400\ \mathrm{MeV}\)), motivating further development of alternative counting schemes (e.g., those with perturbative pions or promoted two-pion exchange) and more systematic error quantification in nuclear EFT.
