# Alpha Decay in the Complex Energy Shell Model

> Alpha emission from a nucleus is a fundamental decay process in which the alpha particle formed inside the nucleus tunnels out through the potential barrier. We describe alpha decay of $^{212}$Po and $^{104}$Te by means of the configuration interaction approach. To compute the preformation factor and penetrability, we use the complex-energy shell model with a separable T=1 interaction. The single-particle space is expanded in a Woods-Saxon basis that consists of bound and unbound resonant states. Special attention is paid to the treatment of the norm kernel appearing in the definition of the formation amplitude that guarantees the normalization of the channel function. Without explicitly considering the alpha-cluster component in the wave function of the parent nucleus, we reproduce the experimental alpha-decay width of $^{212}$Po and predict an upper limit of T_{1/2}=5.5x10^{-7} sec for the half-life of $^{104}$Te. The complex-energy shell model in a large valence configuration space is capable of providing a microscopic description of the alpha decay of heavy nuclei having two valence protons and two valence neutrons outside the doubly magic core. The inclusion of proton-neutron interaction between the valence nucleons is likely to shorten the predicted half-live of $^{104}$Te.

## Key claim

Without explicitly including an alpha-cluster component in the parent-nucleus wave function, the complex-energy shell model with a separable T=1 pairing interaction can reproduce the experimental alpha-decay width of 212Po and predict an upper limit for the half-life of 104Te, though the authors note that including proton-neutron interaction between valence nucleons is likely to shorten the predicted half-life of 104Te.

## Method

The authors used the complex-energy shell model with a separable T=1 pairing interaction to compute the preformation factor and penetrability for alpha decay. The single-particle space was expanded in a Woods-Saxon basis consisting of bound and unbound resonant (Gamow) states, and special attention was paid to the treatment of the norm kernel appearing in the definition of the formation amplitude to guarantee normalization of the channel function. The parent-nucleus wave function was approximated as a product of two-neutron and two-proton seniority-zero states around doubly-magic cores (208Pb and 100Sn), and the alpha-decay width was obtained using both the R-matrix expression and the spectroscopic factor formulation.

## Key numbers

- For 212Po: experimental Q_alpha = 8.986 MeV (after electron screening correction of 31.8 keV from E_alpha = 8.785 MeV); the complex energy of the metastable alpha state was calculated as (8.986 - i0.632 x 10^-13) MeV
- For 104Te: predicted Q_alpha = 5.151 MeV (extrapolated from binding energy differences in 108Te at 3.445 MeV and 106Te at 4.290 MeV, with screening correction of 16.1 keV); the complex energy of the metastable alpha state was calculated as (5.151 - i0.814 x 10^-13) MeV
- Predicted upper limit for the half-life of 104Te: T_1/2 = 5.5 x 10^-7 sec
- The alpha-particle wave function used a Gaussian parameter beta = 0.057 fm^-2, corresponding to a root-mean-square alpha radius r_alpha = 1.57 fm
- The delta-function approximation used an alpha particle radius s_alpha = 2.34 fm

## Context

This work extends the configuration mixing approach to alpha decay previously applied by Harada (1961) and Tonozuka and Arima (1979) for 212Po, and by Varga (1992) who used combined cluster and shell model components. The study is motivated by recent experimental data above doubly-magic 100Sn from Janas (2005) and Liddick (2006) showing very fast alpha decays, with observed enhancement of reduced widths in 105,106Te relative to 213,212Po by a factor of two to three. The complex-energy shell model in a large valence configuration space enables microscopic description of alpha decay for heavy nuclei with two valence protons and two valence neutrons outside doubly-magic cores, and the authors note that inclusion of proton-neutron interaction between valence nucleons is likely to shorten the predicted half-life of 104Te.
