# Towards a Mass and Radius Determination of the Nearby Isolated Neutron Star RX J185635-3754

> We discuss efforts to determine the mass, radius, and surface composition of the nearby compact object RX J185635-3754 from its multi-wavelength spectral energy distribution. We compute non-magnetized model atmospheres and emergent spectra for selected compositions and gravities, and discuss efforts to fit existing and new observational data from ROSAT, EUVE and the HST. The spectral energy distribution matches that expected from a heavy-element dominated atmosphere, but not from a uniform temperature blackbody. Non-magnetic light element atmospheres cannot be simultaneously reconciled with the optical and X-ray data. We extend previous studies, which were limited to one fixed neutron star mass and radius. For uniform temperature models dominated by heavy elements, the redshift z is constrained to be 0.3 < z < 0.4 and the best-fit mass and radius are M approx 0.9 solar masses and R approx 6 km (for a 61 pc distance). These values for M and R together are not permitted for any plausible equation of state, including that of a self-bound strange quark star. A simplified two-temperature model allows masses and radii up to about 50% larger, or a factor of 2 in the case of a black body. The observed luminosity is consistent with the thermal emission of an isolated neutron star no older than about 1 million years, the age inferred from available proper motion and parallax information

## Key claim

The paper finds that for uniform temperature models dominated by heavy elements, the best-fit mass and radius of RX J185635-3754 (M ≈ 0.9 solar masses, R ≈ 6 km) are not permitted by any plausible equation of state, including that of a self-bound strange quark star; however, a simplified two-temperature model allows masses and radii up to about 50% larger, or a factor of 2 in the case of a blackbody.

## Method

The authors computed non-magnetized model atmospheres and emergent spectra for selected compositions (pure H, pure He, pure Fe, and a Si-ash mixture) using standard radiative, local thermodynamic equilibrium, plane-parallel atmosphere techniques with opacities from the Los Alamos opacity project. They fit these models to multi-wavelength observational data from ROSAT, EUVE, and the Hubble Space Telescope, treating the redshift z, interstellar column density n_H, and angular diameter R_infinity/D as free parameters while fixing the gravitational acceleration to values corresponding to canonical neutron star parameters.

## Key numbers

- Best-fit mass M ≈ 0.9 M_sun and radius R ≈ 6 km for uniform temperature heavy-element models at a distance of 61 pc
- Redshift z constrained to 0.3 < z < 0.4 for uniform temperature heavy-element dominated models
- Distance D = 61 (+9, -8) pc from HST parallax measurement (16.5 ± 2.3 mas)
- Proper motion 332 ± 1 mas/yr, implying transverse velocity about 108 ± 15 km/s
- Star age about 9 ± 2 × 10^5 years from proper motion and parallax information
- Upper limit of 6% on the amplitude of any rotational modulation in soft X-rays
- EUVE net flux 2.5 ± 0.3 × 10^(-13) erg cm^(-2) s^(-1) in the 75-95 Angstrom interval
- ROSAT PSPC best-fit blackbody temperature T_infinity = 57 eV (from Walter et al. 1996)
- V magnitude 25.70 ± 0.22 from NTT observations

## Context

This work extends previous studies by Walter et al. (1996) and Pavlov et al. (1996) that were limited to one fixed neutron star mass and radius, and it incorporates new parallax and proper motion measurements from Walter (2001). The paper contradicts the possibility of a simple blackbody interpretation and shows that non-magnetic light element atmospheres cannot simultaneously fit the optical and X-ray data. The results enable future tighter constraints on the dense matter equation of state once higher-resolution X-ray spectra become available from the next generation of X-ray satellites.
