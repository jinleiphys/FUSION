# Variational calculation of nuclear matter in finite particle number approach using unitary correlation operator and high-momentum pair methods

> We propose a new variational method for describing nuclear matter from nucleon-nucleon interaction. We use the unitary correlation operator method (UCOM) for central correlation to treat the short-range repulsion and further include the two-particle two-hole (2p2h) excitations of nucleon pair involving a large relative momentum, which is called 'high-momentum pair'(HM). We describe nuclear matter in finite size with finite particle number on periodic boundary condition and increase the 2p2h configurations until we get the convergence of the total energy per particle. We demonstrate the validity of this 'UCOM+HM' framework by applying it to the symmetric nuclear and neutron matters with the Argonne V4$^\prime$ potential having short-range repulsion. The nuclear equations of state obtained in UCOM+HM are fairly consistent to those of other calculations such as Brueckner-Hartree-Fock and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo in the overall density region.

## Key claim

The paper proposes a new variational method ("UCOM+HM") for nuclear matter that combines the unitary correlation operator method (UCOM) for short-range repulsion with explicit two-particle two-hole (2p2h) excitations carrying high relative momentum, and demonstrates that this finite-particle-number approach yields equations of state for symmetric nuclear and neutron matter that are "fairly consistent" with Brueckner-Hartree-Fock and auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo results, but only for the Argonne V4' central potential which lacks tensor and spin-orbit forces.

## Method

The authors place a finite number of nucleons (magic numbers such as A=132 for symmetric matter) in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, using plane-wave single-particle states on a momentum-space lattice. They first apply the central-UCOM transformation to the Hamiltonian to soften the short-range repulsion, then construct a variational wave function as a linear combination of the 0p0h (Fermi sphere) state and all 2p2h configurations that conserve momentum, spin, and isospin, with the transfer momentum q controlled by a maximum mode number n_q^max. The coefficients are determined by solving the eigenvalue problem of the Hamiltonian matrix, and convergence is checked by increasing n_q^max.

## Key numbers

- For symmetric nuclear matter at normal density (rho = 0.17 fm^-3) with A=132, the effect of high-momentum pairs (2p2h) on the total energy per particle is 1.2 MeV, while the UCOM effect on kinetic energy is about 12 MeV per particle.
- The momentum gap for A=132 at rho = 0.17 fm^-3 is Delta k = 0.68 fm^-1; with n_q^max = 7, the maximum transfer momentum |q| = 4.8 fm^-1, more than three times the empirical Fermi momentum k_F = 1.4 fm^-1.
- For symmetric nuclear matter, the 0p0h+UCOM calculation with A=132 reproduces the infinite-matter Hartree-Fock+UCOM results with relative errors less than 1% for both kinetic and potential energies at densities 0.17 fm^-3 and 0.40 fm^-3.
- The UCOM shift functions for symmetric nuclear matter give a maximum shift of 0.20-0.25 fm at about 0.25 fm relative distance for even channels, and 0.13-0.15 fm at about 0.5 fm for odd channels.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous TOSM+UCOM and HM-AMD methods for finite nuclei to nuclear matter, and is similar in spirit to the tensor-optimized Fermi sphere (TOFS) approach of Yamada (2018) which also uses correlation functions multiplied onto the Fermi-sphere state. The finite-particle-number technique with periodic boundary conditions follows the same methodology used in Green's function Monte Carlo (Carlson 2003), auxiliary field diffusion Monte Carlo (Sarsa 2003, Gandolfi 2009), and coupled cluster theory (Hagen 2014). The present results are limited to the central AV4' potential; the authors state that treatment of tensor force remains a key challenge, as benchmark calculations by Baldo (2012) showed large differences between theories when tensor and LS forces are included.
