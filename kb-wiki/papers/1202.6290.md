# On the origin of nuclear clustering

> Clustering is one of the most complex phenomena known to the structure of atomic nuclei. A comprehensive description of this ubiquitous phenomenon goes beyond standard shell model and cluster model frameworks. We argue that clustering is a consequence of an openness of the nuclear many-body system. To illustrate this point, we study the near-threshold behavior of exceptional points in ^16 Ne and ^24 S.

# On the origin of nuclear clustering

## Key claim
Clustering in atomic nuclei is argued to be a generic consequence of the openness of the nuclear many-body system, specifically the coupling of shell-model eigenstates to nearby particle-decay channels, rather than arising from any particular property of the Hamiltonian or symmetry. The authors demonstrate this by studying exceptional points of the complex-extended continuum shell model Hamiltonian near the proton emission threshold in ¹⁶Ne and ²⁴S, showing that the effect is strongest for light nuclei and suppressed by the Coulomb barrier in heavier systems.

## Method
The authors employ the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum (SMEC), a real-energy continuum shell model that uses a projection formalism to divide Hilbert space into subspaces with different numbers of particles in the scattering continuum. They study the energy-dependent effective Hamiltonian for Jπ=0⁺ states in ¹⁶Ne (using the ZBM Hamiltonian in a 0p₁/₂, 0d₅/₂, 1s₁/₂ model space) and ²⁴S (using the USDB interaction in a 1s₁/₂, 0d₅/₂, 0d₃/₂ model space), analyzing exceptional threads, trajectories of exceptional points in the complex plane of the continuum-coupling strength V₀, to characterize the mixing of shell-model eigenstates induced by coupling to an ℓ=0 proton decay channel.

## Key numbers
- The full width at half maximum of the continuum-coupling correlation energy peak is typically in the range 2 MeV ≤ σ ≤ 3 MeV.
- For ¹⁶Ne, the experimental decay widths of the ground state 1/2₁⁺ and first excited state 5/2₁⁺ in ¹⁵F are reproduced by taking V₀ = -3500 ± 450 MeV·fm³ and V₀ = -1100 ± 50 MeV·fm³, respectively.
- The proton separation energy in ¹⁶Ne is approximately Sₚ ≈ 100 keV.
- The minimum of the lowest eigenvalue E₁ in ¹⁶Ne is shifted above the threshold by about 0.55 MeV due to the Coulomb interaction.
- The height of the Coulomb barrier for Zₜ = 9 in ¹⁶Ne is V_C = 1.69 ± 0.01 MeV.
- For ²⁴S, the height of the Coulomb barrier is V_C = 2.76 ± 0.03 MeV; with a reduced Coulomb potential (Zₜ = 5), V_C = 0.82 ± 0.007 MeV.
- The turning point of exceptional threads in ²⁴S is at 1.25 MeV for the standard Coulomb potential and 0.17 MeV for the reduced Coulomb potential.
- The aligned state in ¹⁶Ne exhausts approximately 80% of the continuum-coupling correlation energy.

## Context
This work extends the observation of Ikeda et al. (1968) that α-cluster states appear near α-particle decay thresholds, reformulating it as a general consequence of open quantum system dynamics rather than a specific structural property. The authors use the Shell Model Embedded in the Continuum (SMEC) framework developed by the same group, building on the Gamow Shell Model of Michel et al. and the real-energy continuum shell model. The results enable a unified understanding of clustering across all nuclei, explaining why clustering is prominent in light nuclei (e.g., the Hoyle state in ¹²C, Borromean systems like ¹¹Li) but suppressed in heavier systems due to Coulomb barrier shielding, and suggest that charged-cluster configurations appear above the emission threshold while neutral-cluster configurations appear primarily below it.
