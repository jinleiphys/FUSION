# Phase Transitions in Dense Baryonic Matter and Cooling of Rotating Neutron Stars

> New astrophysical instruments such as skA (square kilometer Array) and IXO (formerly Constellation X) promise the discovery of tens of thousands of new isolated rotating neutron stars (pulsars), neutron stars in low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs), anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs), and soft gamma repeaters (SGRs). Many of these neutron stars will experience dramatic density changes over their active lifetimes, driven by either stellar spin-up or spin-down, which may trigger phase transitions in their dense baryonic cores. More than that, accretion of matter onto neutron stars in LMXBs is believed to cause pycno-nuclear fusion reactions in the inner crusts of neutron stars. The associated reaction rates may be drastically altered if strange quark matter would be absolutely stable. This paper outlines the investigative steps that need to be performed in order to explore the thermal response of neutron stars to rotationally-driven phase transitions in their cores as well as to nuclear burning scenarios in their crusts. Such research complements the exploration of the phase diagram of dense baryonic matter through particle collider experiments, as performed at RHIC in the USA and as planned at the future Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR) in Darmstadt, Germany.

## Key claim

This paper outlines the investigative steps needed to explore the thermal response of neutron stars to rotationally-driven phase transitions in their cores and to nuclear burning scenarios in their crusts, but does not itself present new numerical results or completed simulations. The authors emphasize that a fully self-consistent general relativistic treatment of the cooling of rotating neutron stars has not been achieved yet.

## Method

The authors derive the general relativistic transport equations for rotating, non-spherical neutron stars using the metric of a rotationally deformed fluid, including frame dragging (Lense-Thirring) effects. They show how these equations reduce to the standard cooling equations of spherically symmetric, non-rotating neutron stars when rotation is absent. The proposed approach combines a 2-dimensional general relativistic stellar rotation code (to determine metric functions, frame dragging frequency, pressure, density gradients, and particle compositions as functions of rotational frequency) with a 2-dimensional general relativistic thermal evolution code (to determine luminosity and surface temperature).

## Key numbers

- The most rapidly rotating known neutron star, PSR J1748-2446ad, rotates at a period of 1.39 ms (rotational frequency of 719 Hz).
- PSR B1937+21 rotates at 1.58 ms (633 Hz) and PSR B1957+20 rotates at 1.61 ms (621 Hz).
- X-ray burst oscillations from XTE J1739-285 may suggest an ultrafast neutron star rotating at 1122 Hz.
- Density changes in neutron stars in binary stellar systems can be as large as 60% due to spin-up or spin-down.
- The initial temperature for the thermal evolution calculations is typically chosen as 10^11 K.
- The accretion-powered millisecond pulsar SAX J1808.4-3658 rotates at 2.5 ms (400 Hz).

## Context

This work extends the theoretical framework for neutron star cooling developed in earlier studies (Weber 1999, Page 2005) by incorporating rotationally-driven density changes that can open or close cooling channels over time. The research complements experimental exploration of the phase diagram of dense baryonic matter at particle colliders such as RHIC and the planned Facility for Antiproton and Ion Research (FAIR). The approach enables future studies of latent heat release from quark-hadron phase transitions, pycnonuclear burning in inner crusts, and the thermal response of soft X-ray transients like SAX J1808.4-3658, which could probe core compositions of neutron stars.
