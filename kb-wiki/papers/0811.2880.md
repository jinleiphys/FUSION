# Isospin Dynamics in Heavy Ion Collisions: from Coulomb Barrier to Quark Gluon Plasma

> Heavy Ion Collisions (HIC) represent a unique tool to probe the in-medium nuclear interaction in regions away from saturation. In this report we present a selection of new reaction observables in dissipative collisions particularly sensitive to the symmetry term of the nuclear Equation of State (Iso-EoS). We will first discuss the Isospin Equilibration Dynamics. At low energies this manifests via the recently observed Dynamical Dipole Radiation, due to a collective neutron-proton oscillation with the symmetry term acting as a restoring force. At higher beam energies Iso-EoS effects will be seen in Imbalance Ratio Measurements, in particular from the correlations with the total kinetic energy loss. For fragmentation reactions in central events we suggest to look at the coupling between isospin distillation and radial flow. In Neck Fragmentation reactions important $Iso-EoS$ information can be obtained from the correlation between isospin content and alignement. The high density symmetry term can be probed from isospin effects on heavy ion reactions at relativistic energies (few AGeV range). Rather isospin sensitive observables are proposed from nucleon/cluster emissions, collective flows and meson production. The possibility to shed light on the controversial neutron/proton effective mass splitting in asymmetric matter is also suggested. A large symmetry repulsion at high baryon density will also lead to an "earlier" hadron-deconfinement transition in n-rich matter. A suitable treatment of the isovector interaction in the partonic EoS appears very relevant.

## Key claim

The symmetry term of the nuclear Equation of State (Iso-EoS) can be probed across a wide range of beam energies, from just above the Coulomb barrier to the AGeV range, through specific reaction observables, but the sensitivity and interpretation depend critically on the density region probed and on disentangling the density dependence from the momentum dependence of the isovector interaction.

## Method

The authors employ non-relativistic Landau-Vlasov transport simulations for low-to-Fermi energy collisions and a fully covariant transport approach based on effective field exchange models for relativistic collisions, both incorporating mean-field dynamics with hard nucleon-nucleon collisions and stochastic forces. They test two parametrizations of the symmetry energy: "Asysoft" (weak density dependence, with the potential part decreasing at suprasaturation) and "Asystiff" (linear density dependence with C=32 MeV constant coefficient), while keeping the isoscalar part of the EoS fixed with a compressibility around 220 MeV. For high-density studies, a BGBD-like mean field with different neutron/proton momentum dependence is implemented to explore effective mass splitting.

## Key numbers

- The Asysoft parametrization uses C(ρ)/ρ₀ = 482 - 1638ρ (MeV fm³), giving a weak density dependence of the symmetry potential near saturation
- The Asystiff parametrization uses a constant coefficient C = 32 MeV, giving a linear density dependence of the interaction part of the symmetry term
- For the proposed exotic reaction ¹³²Sn+⁵⁸Ni at 10 AMeV, the initial dipole moment D(t=0) is approximately 50 fm, about two times the largest values probed so far
- In Sn+Sn collisions at 35 and 50 AMeV, the difference between stiff and soft Iso-EoS in the imbalance ratio versus total kinetic energy loss correlation is systematic at about 20 percent
- For central collisions of ¹³²Sn+¹²⁴Sn at 400 AMeV, baryon densities of about 1.7-1.8 ρ₀ are reached in the interacting zone for a transient time of 15-20 fm/c, with freeze-out around 50 fm/c
- The crossing point in (n/p) and ³H/³He yield ratios versus transverse momentum occurs at p_t ≈ 2.13 fm⁻¹, corresponding to a source at baryon density ρ ≈ 1.6 ρ₀

## Context

This review extends the line of work on the symmetry energy of nuclear matter discussed in Fuchs and Wolter (2006) and Fantoni (2008), and builds on prior transport code developments by Baran et al. (2005), Colonna et al. (1998), and Chomaz et al. (2004). The paper proposes new observables, such as the correlation between imbalance ratio and total kinetic energy loss, and the N/Z versus kinetic energy correlation for intermediate mass fragments, that could be tested at existing and future radioactive beam facilities like SPIRAL2, and connects low-density constraints from structure data (e.g., Garg et al. 2007 on monopole resonances in Sn isotopes) to high-density behavior relevant for neutron star structure and the hadron-deconfinement transition in neutron-rich matter.
