# On the separability of microscopic optical model potentials and emerging bell-shape Perey-Buck nonlocality

> After nearly sixty years since its introduction, the phenomenological bell-shape Perey-Buck spatial nonlocality in the optical model potential for nucleon-nucleus scattering has remained unaccounted for from a microscopic standpoint. In this article we provide a quantitative account for such nonlocality considering fully nonlocal optical potentials in momentum space. The framework is based on a momentum-space in-medium folding model, where infinite nuclear matter $g$ matrices in Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation are folded to the target one-body mixed density. The study is based on chiral next-to-next-to-next-to-leading order (N3LO) as well as Argonne $v_{18}$ nucleon-nucleon bare interaction models. Applications focus on $^{40}$Ca($p,p$) scattering at beam energies in the range 11-200 MeV, resulting in the identification of a separable structure of the momentum-space optical potential of a form we coin as $JvH$, with a nonlocality form factor as one of its terms. The resulting nonlocaliy form factor features a bell-shape with nonlocality range $β$ between 0.86 and 0.89 fm, for both proton and neutron beams at energies below 65 MeV. An analytic toy model is introduced to elucidate the underlying mechanism for the nonlocality in the optical model, providing an estimate of its range based on the Fermi motion of the target nucleons and the long-range part of the $NN$ interaction.

## Key claim

The paper provides a quantitative, parameter-free microscopic account for the bell-shaped Perey-Buck spatial nonlocality in the optical model potential for nucleon-nucleus scattering, showing that the momentum-space optical potential exhibits a separable structure (termed JvH) with a nonlocality form factor that emerges naturally from the folding of Brueckner-Hartree-Fock g matrices with the target one-body mixed density, rather than being assumed phenomenologically.

## Method

The authors constructed fully nonlocal optical potentials in momentum space using a folding model where infinite nuclear matter g matrices in Brueckner-Hartree-Fock approximation are folded with the target one-body mixed density, employing the Slater approximation. They used chiral N3LO and Argonne v18 bare nucleon-nucleon interactions, applied to 40Ca(p,p) scattering at beam energies from 11 to 200 MeV, and analyzed the momentum-space structure of the resulting potentials in terms of momentum transfer q, mean momentum K, and their relative angle w.

## Key numbers

* Nonlocality range beta for the central part of the potential for proton and neutron beams on 40Ca at energies below 65 MeV lies between 0.86 and 0.89 fm, for both N3LO- and AV18-based interactions.
* For proton scattering on 40Ca at 11.42 MeV, beta_pA = 0.89 fm (both N3LO and AV18); at 40.0 MeV, beta_pA = 0.88 fm (both interactions).
* The difference between proton-neutron and proton-proton nonlocality ranges is beta_pn - beta_pp ≈ 0.22 fm for all cases below 100 MeV.
* For the spin-orbit term, the nonlocality range is smaller than for the central part by about 0.3-0.4 fm, with values around 0.57-0.58 fm for N3LO-based potentials at energies below 65 MeV.
* The JvH separable representation reproduces the full potential results for differential cross sections up to about 40 MeV beam energy, with discrepancies appearing above the second minimum at higher energies.

## Context

This work provides the first microscopic explanation for the phenomenological bell-shaped nonlocality introduced by Perey and Buck (1962), who found a nonlocality range of about 0.85 fm from fits to neutron scattering data on lead. The results are consistent with the Perey-Buck value and with later parametrizations by Tian et al. (2015), who reported beta_n = 0.90 fm and beta_p = 0.88 fm. The study extends prior microscopic efforts by Ripka (1963), Giannini (1976), and Jeukenne (1976), and enables parameter-free nonlocal optical potentials for nucleon-nucleus scattering that can be used in transfer and capture reaction calculations, as well as in dispersive optical models.
