# A non-equilibrium picture of the chemical freeze-out in hadronic thermal models

> Thermal models have proven to be an useful and simple tool used to make theoretical predictions and data analysis in relativistic and ultra-relativistic heavy ion collisions. A new version of these models is presented here, incorporating a non equilibrium feature to the description of the intermediate fireball state formed at the chemical freeze-out. Two different effective temperatures are attributed to the expanding fireball, regarding its baryonic and mesonic sectors. The proposal is not merely to include an additional degree of freedom to reach a better adjustment to the data, but to open a room in the model conception for considerations on the non-equilibrium scenario of the system evolution. A set of well consolidated data for particles production is used to validated the reformulated version of thermal models presented here. A rather good performance of the extended version was verified, both for the quality of particle ratio data fittings as well as for describing the asymptotic energy behavior of temperatures and baryochemical potential of the colliding nuclear system.

## Key claim

The paper proposes that the chemical freeze-out stage in heavy-ion collisions is not characterized by a single global temperature, but instead by two distinct effective temperatures for baryonic and mesonic sectors, representing a non-equilibrium scenario. The authors claim this two-temperature model improves the quality of fits to particle ratio data compared to the conventional one-temperature model, though they acknowledge that further validation with comprehensive data sets is necessary.

## Method

The authors modified the conventional thermal model by assigning separate temperatures (T_b for baryons and T_m for mesons) in the Fermi-Dirac and Bose-Einstein distribution functions used to calculate particle densities. They included the lightest baryonic octet, decuplet, and mesonic nonet, plus approximately 200 additional baryonic and mesonic resonances with masses up to 2 GeV, and reformulated the resonance decay feeding processes to be consistent with the two-temperature approach. The model was fitted to particle ratio data from five collision systems (Ni+Ni at 2.32 GeV, Pb+Pb at 17.3 GeV, and Au+Au at 62.4, 130, and 200 GeV) using Monte Carlo sampling of parameter values, and results were compared with the conventional one-temperature model.

## Key numbers

- For Pb+Pb at 17.3 GeV: 1T-model gives T = 147.4 MeV, mu_b = 287.2 MeV, chi^2 = 1.667; 2T-model gives T_m = 133.36 MeV, T_b = 140.05 MeV, mu_b = 251.80 MeV, chi^2 = 0.9997
- For Au+Au at 200 GeV: 1T-model gives T = 169.50 MeV, mu_b = 23.00 MeV, chi^2 = 3.159; 2T-model gives T_m = 128.01 MeV, T_b = 147.36 MeV, mu_b = 23.21 MeV, chi^2 = 1.0
- For Ni+Ni at 2.32 GeV: 1T-model gives T = 29.6 MeV, mu_b = 772.9 MeV, chi^2 = 0.160; 2T-model gives T_m = 27.9 MeV, T_b = 29.6 MeV, mu_b = 772.0 MeV, chi^2 = 0.9666
- For Au+Au at 62.4 GeV: 1T-model gives T = 150.5 MeV, mu_b = 60.5 MeV, chi^2 = 7.435; 2T-model gives T_m = 131.50 MeV, T_b = 145.50 MeV, mu_b = 59.0 MeV, chi^2 = 0.9996
- For Au+Au at 130 GeV: 1T-model gives T = 156.50 MeV, mu_b = 29.50 MeV, chi^2 = 5.876; 2T-model gives T_m = 129.74 MeV, T_b = 146.32 MeV, mu_b = 24.91 MeV, chi^2 = 0.9999
- In the 2T-model, the meson-baryon entropy ratio indicates a change from baryon dominance to meson dominance regime around sqrt(s_NN) = 7 GeV

## Context

This work extends previous thermal model approaches that used a single freeze-out temperature (Kraus 2009, Hirsch 2010) and addresses known limitations where thermal models overestimate strange particle abundances and underestimate pion yields (Cleymans 1994, Yen 1999). The two-temperature concept connects to earlier ideas by Fermi (1950) and Heisenberg (1949) about treating baryons and mesons as distinct thermal sectors, and to Hagedorn's resonance mass spectra approach where different effective temperatures for baryons and mesons were extracted (Lu 2002, Choi 2011). The model enables a closer connection between simple thermal models and more detailed descriptions of fireball expansion, such as hydrodynamical models and non-equilibrium kinetic equation approaches (Bass 2000, Huovinen 2008).
