# Thermalization from quantum entanglement: jet simulations in the massive Schwinger model

> We investigate the emergence of thermalization in a quantum field-theoretic model mimicking the production of jets in QCD -- the massive Schwinger model coupled to external sources. Specifically, we compute the expectation values of local operators as functions of time and compare them to their thermal counterparts, quantify the overlap between the evolving density matrix and the thermal one, and compare the dynamics of the energy-momentum tensor to predictions from relativistic hydrodynamics. Through these studies, we find that the system approaches thermalization at late times and elucidate the mechanisms by which quantum entanglement drives thermalization in closed field-theoretic systems. Our results show how thermodynamic behavior emerges in real time from unitary quantum dynamics.

## Key claim

The massive Schwinger model coupled to external sources (mimicking QCD jet production) approaches thermalization at late times, but only for intermediate fermion mass values (m/g ~ 0.5) that are sufficiently far from the integrable limits of the theory; at very large or very small fermion mass the system does not show strong evidence of thermalization.

## Method

The authors perform real-time Hamiltonian simulations of the massive Schwinger model on a lattice using matrix product states (MPS) with the time-dependent variational principle (TDVP). They initialize the system in the ground state and then evolve unitarily with a time-dependent Hamiltonian that includes external sources representing two jets propagating back to back along the light cone. They compute expectation values of local operators (chiral condensate, kinetic energy density, condensate two-point function), entanglement entropy of subsystems, and overlaps between the reduced density matrix of the jet system and thermal density matrices, extracting effective temperatures from each observable.

## Key numbers

- For the system with parameters g = 0.5/a and m = 0.5 g, after time t approximately 30/M_s, the effective temperature extracted from local observables converges to T/M_s = 0.53 ± 0.02.
- The entanglement entropy of central subsystems of size L = 8, 10, 12 (physical sites) follows a volume law at late times, with the vacuum-subtracted entropy density reaching a plateau.
- The thermodynamic temperature computed from the energy density, pressure, and entanglement entropy density agrees with the temperature from local observables at late times.
- For m/g = 0.9, thermalization is slower compared to m/g = 0.5; for m/g = 0.2, the accessible time interval is shorter and observables equilibrate to slightly different effective temperatures.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of real-time thermalization in the massive Schwinger model (Florio 2023, Florio 2024) by providing multiple independent measures of thermalization beyond entanglement entropy. The setup was originally proposed by Casher, Kogut, and Susskind (1974) for modeling jet fragmentation, and the massless integrable case was solved analytically by Loshaj (2011). The results demonstrate that quantum entanglement drives thermalization in closed field-theoretic systems, connecting entanglement entropy to Gibbs entropy and supporting the conjecture that thermalization in heavy-ion collisions may originate from quantum entanglement. The findings enable future studies of symmetry-resolved entanglement and thermalization in the presence of conserved charges.
