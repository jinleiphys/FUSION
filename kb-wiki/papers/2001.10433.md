# Many-body perturbation theories for finite nuclei

> In recent years many-body perturbation theory encountered a renaissance in the field of ab initio nuclear structure theory. In various applications it was shown that perturbation theory, including novel flavors of it, constitutes a useful tool to describe atomic nuclei, either as a full-fledged many-body approach or as an auxiliary method to support more sophisticated non-perturbative many-body schemes. In this work the current status of many-body perturbation theory in the field of nuclear structure is discussed and novel results are provided that highlight its power as a efficient and yet accurate (pre-processing) approach to systematically investigate medium-mass nuclei. Eventually a new generation of chiral nuclear Hamiltonians is benchmarked using several state-of-the-art flavours of many-body perturbation theory.

## Key claim

Many-body perturbation theory (MBPT) is not intrinsically unsuited to nuclear structure; rather, its success depends critically on choosing an optimized unperturbed Hamiltonian \(H_0\) and reference state, and on pre-processing the nuclear Hamiltonian via similarity renormalization group (SRG) transformations to tame ultraviolet couplings, with the caveat that infrared divergences can still arise in open-shell nuclei unless more general (e.g., symmetry-broken or multi-determinantal) reference states are employed.

## Method

The authors review and benchmark several flavors of MBPT for finite nuclei: standard single-reference MBPT based on a Slater-determinant reference for closed-shell systems, multi-configurational perturbation theory (MCPT) using a reference state from a prior no-core shell model calculation, and Bogoliubov MBPT (BMBPT) based on a particle-number-breaking Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov reference for open-shell nuclei. They apply these methods to ground-state energies of medium-mass nuclei using chiral Hamiltonians at N3LO (two-nucleon) and N2LO (three-nucleon) with SRG evolution, and they also use MBPT as a pre-processing tool to accelerate non-perturbative calculations (e.g., configuration preselection and natural-orbital optimization).

## Key numbers

* Ground-state energy of \(^{16}\)O: the MBPT series at second order (MBPT2) yields a value that depends on the particle-hole gap; a step-wise reduction of this gap (from a large value down to near zero) causes the MBPT2 contribution to diverge, illustrating the emergence of an infrared divergence (see Figure in text, no single numerical energy given for the divergence threshold).
* The chiral Hamiltonian used as the "standard" employs a 2N interaction at N3LO with cutoff \(\Lambda_{2\text{N}} = 500\,\text{MeV}/c\) and a 3N interaction at N2LO with cutoff \(\Lambda_{3\text{N}} = 400\,\text{MeV}\).
* SRG evolution is applied with a continuous parameter \(\alpha\); the optimal parameter range is chosen to improve model-space convergence while minimizing induced many-body operators (no single \(\alpha\) value specified as universal).

## Context

This work reviews and extends the renaissance of MBPT in ab initio nuclear structure, following earlier non-perturbative methods such as the no-core shell model (NCSM) (Navratil, 2009), coupled cluster (CC) (Hagen, 2014), and in-medium similarity renormalization group (IMSRG) (Hergert, 2016). It contrasts with the historical view that the nuclear problem is intrinsically non-perturbative, showing that SRG softening and generalized reference states (e.g., BMBPT) can make MBPT convergent for medium-mass nuclei up to isotopic chains. The results enable MBPT to serve as a cheap pre-processing tool for non-perturbative methods and as a systematic benchmark for new chiral Hamiltonians, with open questions remaining about the treatment of doubly open-shell nuclei via symmetry restoration and about resummation techniques such as eigenvector continuation.
