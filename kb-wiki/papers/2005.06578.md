# Limitations of the Geiger-Nuttall Law in heavy cluster decay

> Geiger-Nuttall Law is the simplest relation in radioactive decay relating the half-life and the decay energy. Initially restricted to $α$ decay of individual isotopes, generalizations unifying different isotopes and decay modes under a single set of constant coefficients were subsequently achieved. This motivates investigating to what extent such generalizations are possible. We also examine whether there exists a universal Geiger-Nuttall Law that can simultaneously describe all decay modes and nuclei including heavy clusters. We show that the validity of Geiger-Nuttall Law and its generalizations hinges on the assumption that half-life can be approximated linearly as a function of the square root of the ratio of the decay energy to the Coulomb barrier height. Systematic calculation of the ratio across the nuclear chart for 12 decay modes reveals that it varies over its whole range between 0 and 1. Consequently, no linear approximation can unify all the nuclei and decay modes under a single set of coefficients, and thus no universal Geiger-Nuttall Law is possible in contrast to previous claims. In cluster decay, the ratio varies within 0.6-1 where non-linearity becomes significant such that no generalized Geiger-Nuttall description of heavy clusters is possible. In the ongoing attempts of unification, it might be necessary to go beyond the Geiger-Nuttall Law and incorporate additional terms proportional to the decay energy and/or its square root.

## Key claim

The paper shows that no universal Geiger-Nuttall law can simultaneously describe all decay modes and nuclei including heavy clusters, because the validity of the law depends on the linearity assumption that the half-life can be approximated linearly as a function of the square root of the ratio of decay energy to Coulomb barrier height; this ratio varies over its entire possible range (0 to 1) across the nuclear chart, making a single linear approximation impossible.

## Method

The authors used R-matrix theory to derive the general expression for decay half-life and identified the critical assumption that the function gamma_l(x) must be linearizable in x, where x is the square root of Q divided by the Coulomb barrier height. They systematically calculated x for 5130 heavy and superheavy nuclei (mass numbers 162 to 350, proton numbers 78 to 132) across 12 decay modes from proton emission up to titanium-54 emission, using experimental Q-values where available and theoretical values from the WS4 mass model with radial basis function correction.

## Key numbers

- x varies over its whole possible range between 0 and 1, with a minimum of 0.004 and a maximum of 0.904 across all nuclei and decay modes considered
- Average x increases progressively from 0.26 for proton decay to 0.43 for alpha decay up to 0.78 for titanium-54 decay
- For 520 experimentally observed alpha-decaying nuclei with Z between 78 and 110, all x values lie in the range 0 < x < 0.6, where the linearity assumption holds
- The root-mean-square deviation in log10(T1/2) from linearization for alpha decay in the interval 0 < x < 0.6 is 0.33
- For 11 trans-lead nuclei decaying by cluster emission, x values range from 0.63 to 0.80, where non-linearity becomes significant

## Context

This work extends the theoretical understanding of Geiger-Nuttall laws by testing the linearity assumption that was first made by Gamow (1928) and has been repeated by subsequent authors including the developers of the Universal Decay Law (UDL) by Qi et al. (2009) and the Unified Formula (UF) by Santhosh et al. (2010). The paper contradicts the claim by UDL authors that the law should become more exact for heavier cluster-daughter systems, showing instead that x increases with cluster size. The findings explain the large discrepancies between different generalized Geiger-Nuttall laws in predicting heavy cluster decay half-lives, as noted in prior studies by Poenaru et al. (2011) and others, and suggest that future unification attempts must incorporate additional terms proportional to decay energy or its square root.
