# Collinear cluster tripartition as sequential binary fission in the $^{235}$U(n$_{\rm th}$,f) reaction

> The mechanism leading to the formation of the observed products of the collinear cluster tripartition is carried out within the framework of the model based on the dinuclear system concept. The yield of fission products is calculated using the statistical model based on the driving potentials for the fissionable system. The minima of potential energy of the decaying system correspond to the charge numbers of the products which are produced with large probabilities in the sequential fission (partial case of the collinear cluster tripartition) of the compound nucleus. The realization of this mechanism supposes the asymmetric fission channel as the first stage of sequential mechanism. It is shown that only the use of the driving potential calculated by the binding energies with the shell correction allows us to explain the yield of the true ternary fission products. The theoretical model is applied to research collinear cluster tripartition in the reaction $^{235}$U(n$_{\rm th}$,f). Calculations showed that in the first stage of this fission reaction, the isotopes $^{82}$Ge and $^{154}$Nd are formed with relatively large probabilities and in the second stage of sequential fission of the isotope Nd mainly Ni and Ge are formed. This is in agreement with the yield of the isotope $^{68}$Ni which is observed as the product of the collinear cluster tripartition in the experiment.

## Key claim

The collinear cluster tripartition (CCT) in the $^{235}$U(n$_{\rm th}$,f) reaction can be explained as a sequential two-stage binary fission process, where the compound nucleus first undergoes asymmetric binary fission and the heavy fragment subsequently fissions, rather than as a simultaneous three-body breakup. The model specifically shows that only by using driving potentials calculated from binding energies that include shell corrections can the observed yields of true ternary fission products be explained.

## Method

The authors applied the dinuclear system (DNS) concept to model the fission of $^{236}$U$^{*}$ formed by thermal neutron capture on $^{235}$U. They calculated the driving potential as the sum of the nucleus-nucleus interaction potential (using a double folding potential with Migdal forces) and the $Q_{\rm gg}$-value from experimental binding energies, then determined fragment yields using a statistical model where the yield $Y(Z)=Y_0 P(Z) W(Z)$ depends on the probability of DNS formation $P(Z) \propto e^{-U_{\rm dr}(Z)/T_{\rm DNS}(Z)}$ and the decay probability $W(Z) \propto e^{-B_{\rm DNS}(Z)/T_B(Z)}$. For the second stage, the heavy fragment from the first fission (e.g., $^{154}$Nd) was treated as the fissioning nucleus and its fragment yields were calculated using the same method.

## Key numbers

- Excitation energy of the compound nucleus $^{236}$U$^{*}$ formed in the reaction: 6.54 MeV (from $E_{\rm n_{th}}=0.025$ eV plus $Q_{\rm gg}$).
- Total kinetic energy for the first stage channel $^{82}$Ge+$^{154}$Nd: $TKE=159.83$ MeV.
- Total kinetic energy for the second stage channel $^{72}$Ni+$^{82}$Ge: $TKE=72.9$ MeV.
- Excitation energy of $^{154}$Nd$^{*}$ after the first stage: 25.3 MeV.
- Fission barrier for $^{154}$Nd in the first stage: $B^H_f=36.33$ MeV.
- Probability of the main CCT channel $^{81}$Ge+$^{70}$Ni+$^{82}$Ge+3n: $1.06\times10^{-3}\times2.46\times10^{-1}\approx3\times10^{-4}$.
- Relative probability of CCT to binary fission: approximately $10^{-3}$.
- Two-neutron separation energy from $^{72}$Ni: 10.93 MeV; from $^{82}$Ge: 12.25 MeV.

## Context

This work extends the dinuclear system concept, previously applied to deep inelastic transfer, fusion, and quasifission by Nasirov (2005) and others, to the specific case of collinear cluster tripartition observed by the FOBOS collaboration (Pyatkov 2003, 2010). It contrasts with the three-cluster model of Manimaran (2009) and the ternary fission model of Andreev (2006), which assumed a ternary system existing briefly before decay; instead, this work treats CCT as sequential binary fissions. The results enable predictions of fragment yields for other fission channels and suggest the model can be extended to quantitative mass distribution calculations for other fissioning systems.
