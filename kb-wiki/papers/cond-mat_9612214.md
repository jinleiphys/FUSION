# Quantum Fluctuations Affect the Critical Properties of Noble Gases

> Interacting argon atoms are simulated with a recently developed quantum Langevin transport treatment that takes approximate account of the quantum fluctuations inherent in microscopic many-body descriptions based on wave packets. The mass distribution of the atomic clusters is affected significantly near the critical temperature and thus it may be important to take account of quantum fluctuations in molecular-dynamics simulations of cluster formation processes.

## Key claim

Quantum fluctuations, approximated via a quantal Langevin force, significantly shift the critical temperature of the gas-liquid phase transition in argon clusters compared to purely classical molecular-dynamics simulations; the effect is system-dependent and the direction of the temperature shift can differ for different observables (e.g., cluster mass distribution vs. intrinsic excitation energy distribution).

## Method

The authors simulated 100 argon atoms in a cubic box with periodic boundary conditions, interacting via a Lennard-Jones potential truncated at 6σ. They compared classical Langevin dynamics (with a thermal reservoir) to a quantal Langevin treatment that adds a quantum fluctuation term derived from Gaussian wave-packet dynamics, including a drift reduction factor α = (T/D)(1 − e^(−D/T)) and a cooling procedure to account for thermal distortion of the wave packets. Cluster analysis was performed at each time step using a separation criterion of 2σ to define cluster membership.

## Key numbers

- The Lennard-Jones parameters for argon: σ = 3.405 Å, ε = 119.8 K; the potential minimum occurs at r₀ ≈ 1.1225 σ with depth V₀ = −ε.
- The quantum level spacing for argon: D ≡ ħω ≈ 0.2265 ε.
- The classical critical temperature for the gas-liquid transition: T^cl ≈ 0.6 ε.
- The quantal critical temperature: T^qu ≈ 0.5 ε.
- The effective classical temperature corresponding to the quantal model at T = 0.5 ε is T_eff = 0.62 ε, derived from the Einstein relation T_eff = D/(1 − e^(−D/T)).
- The atomic density used in the simulations: ρ = 0.025 σ^(−3).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previously developed quantal Langevin treatment (Ohnishi and Randrup, 1995; 1996) from nuclear fragmentation to atomic cluster formation, specifically addressing noble gases. It contradicts the common assumption that quantum fluctuations are negligible for heavy particles like argon atoms, showing significant effects on the critical temperature. The paper enables future dynamical simulations of cluster formation in expanding and cooling noble gases, and suggests that simultaneous measurement of cluster mass distribution and intrinsic excitation energy distribution could reveal opposite temperature shifts that cannot be reproduced by a simple classical temperature adjustment. The authors note that Ikeshoji et al. (1996) had already recognized the importance of quantum features in magic-cluster formation.
