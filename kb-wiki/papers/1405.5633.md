# On the Validity of the Geiger-Nuttall Alpha-Decay Law and its Microscopic Basis

> The Geiger-Nuttall (GN) law relates the partial $α$-decay half-life with the energy of the escaping $α$ particle and contains for every isotopic chain two experimentally determined coefficients. The expression is supported by several phenomenological approaches, however its coefficients lack a fully microscopic basis. In this paper we will show that: 1) the empirical coefficients that appear in the GN law have a deep physical meaning and 2) the GN law is successful within the restricted experimental data sets available so far, but is not valid in general. We will show that, when the dependence of logarithm values of the $α$ formation probability on the neutron number is not linear or constant, the GN law is broken. For the $α$ decay of neutron-deficient nucleus $^{186}$Po, the difference between the experimental half-life and that predicted by the GN Law is as large as one order of magnitude.

# On the Validity of the Geiger-Nuttall Alpha-Decay Law and its Microscopic Basis

## Key claim
The Geiger-Nuttall (GN) law, while extremely successful for most known alpha emitters, is not valid in general; it breaks down when the logarithm of the alpha formation probability depends nonlinearly on neutron number, as demonstrated by the one-order-of-magnitude deviation observed for the neutron-deficient nucleus 186Po.

## Method
The authors applied Thomas's microscopic expression for the alpha-decay half-life, which includes the formation amplitude of the alpha cluster at the nuclear surface, to derive a generalized form of the GN law. They analyzed experimental alpha-decay data for even-even nuclei across four regions of the nuclear chart (defined by Z above/below 82 and N above/below 126) and compared the empirical GN coefficients A(Z) and B(Z) with the microscopic parameters a, b, and c extracted from the Thomas formalism. The alpha formation probabilities were extracted from experimental half-lives and examined as functions of neutron number and Q_alpha.

## Key numbers
- For 186Po, the difference between the experimental half-life and that predicted by the GN law is as large as one order of magnitude.
- The GN law reproduces most experimental data within a factor of 2 to 3 for nuclei in the Yb-Ra region (apart from the Po chain).
- For region I (Z ≤ 82, N ≤ 126), the fitted coefficients give A(Z) = 2.41Z - 66.7 and B(Z) = -0.54Z - 6.61.
- For region III (Z > 82, N > 126), the fitted coefficients give A(Z) = 2.27Z - 65.0 and B(Z) = -0.47Z - 9.36.
- The alpha formation probability |RF_alpha(R)|^2 varies over one order of magnitude for isotopes approaching the N = 126 shell closure in region II.

## Context
This work extends the original Geiger-Nuttall law (1911) and the quantum tunneling explanation by Gamow (1929) and by Condon and Gurney (1928), as well as Thomas's first microscopic theory (1954). It builds on the generalized GN law developed by Qi (2009) and on recent experimental data from the SHIP facility at GSI on neutron-deficient polonium isotopes reported by Andreyev (2012). The paper explains why the GN coefficients A and B show different linear Z-dependences in different regions of the nuclear chart, particularly when crossing the N = 126 and Z = 82 shell gaps, and demonstrates that the GN law's apparent validity in most regions is a consequence of limited data ranges where the formation probability varies linearly with neutron number. The findings enable more reliable predictions of alpha-decay half-lives in unknown nuclei and motivate extending experimental measurements toward more neutron-deficient radon, radium, and thorium isotopes, as well as more neutron-rich lead and mercury isotopes.
