# Description of the shape of medium and heavy nuclei using a finite deformed one-particle potential with deformation-dependent diffuseness

> Adjustment of the behavior of the potential energy of nuclear deformation, defined as the sum of the energies of lowest-lying occupied single-particle levels in a deformed finite potential with a pairing correction, is considered by taking into account the dependence of the diffuseness of the surface of the potential on deformation. To verify this approach we construct a non-axial ellipsoidally-deformed potential with a slight (of the order of 1\%) variation of the surface diffuseness at moderate deformations. Parameters of the variation are determined from a calculation for a group of 36 deformed, transitional, and spherical nuclei in the range $50 \le A \le 241$ using existing experimental data on static quadrupole moments. In order to test the performed parameter choice, we applied the model to description of the ground-state deformation of the isotopic chains $^{95-132}$Cd and $^{74-106}$Sr. The obtained description of the ground state deformation of the considered nuclei is in agreement with experimental data and results of macro-microscopic models and calculations using the Hartree-Fock-Bogoliubov method.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that introducing a small (of the order of 1%) variation of the surface diffuseness parameter of a deformed Woods–Saxon potential as a function of deformation can approximately incorporate the effect of residual interactions, allowing the sum of single-particle energies (with a pairing correction) to correctly describe ground-state deformations for a wide range of nuclei, including those outside the beta-stability valley.

## Method

The authors construct a non-axial ellipsoidally-deformed Woods–Saxon one-particle potential starting from the real part of the global spherical optical potential of Koning and Delaroche (2003) evaluated at the Fermi energy. The diffuseness parameter of the potential is made deformation-dependent via a linear term in the axial deformation parameter β, with a single adjustable parameter k controlling the magnitude and sign of the variation. Single-particle states are computed by diagonalizing the Hamiltonian in a truncated harmonic-oscillator basis (N_max = 11), and the ground-state deformation is found by minimizing the sum of occupied single-particle energies plus a pairing correction. The parameter k is calibrated using experimental static quadrupole moments for a set of 36 nuclei spanning A = 50 to 241, and the model is then tested on the isotopic chains ⁹⁵–¹³²Cd and ⁷⁴–¹⁰⁶Sr.

## Key numbers

- The variation of the surface diffuseness at moderate deformations is of the order of 1%.
- The calibration set consists of 36 deformed, transitional, and spherical nuclei in the mass range 50 ≤ A ≤ 241.
- The model is tested on the isotopic chains ⁹⁵–¹³²Cd and ⁷⁴–¹⁰⁶Sr.
- The oscillator basis truncation N_max = 11 is checked to be reliable for masses 0 ≤ A ≲ 240 with deformations −0.5 ≤ β ≤ 0.5.
- The spherical potential parameters include a nuclear well depth U_nucl = (59.3 + (−1)^{1−q}·21·(N−Z)/A − 0.024·A) × (1 + q·(0.007067 + 4.23×10⁻⁶·A)·(1.73·Z/R_Coul)) MeV, a spin-orbit depth U_ls = 5.922 + 0.003·A MeV, and a spherical diffuseness a_nucl = 0.6778 − 1.487×10⁻⁴·A fm.

## Context

This work extends the classical Nilsson (1955) method of summing single-particle energies in a deformed potential, which was known to fail for lighter and less strongly deformed nuclei without modifications (Brack 1972, Brack 1992). The approach is contrasted with modern macro-microscopic models such as the Finite Range Droplet Model (Moeller 1995, 2012, 2016) and self-consistent Hartree–Fock–Bogoliubov calculations using Skyrme forces (Bender 2003, Ryssens 2019, Goriely 2013) or Gogny interactions (Dechargé 1980, Hilaire 2007). The proposed simple phenomenological model aims to provide a computationally efficient description of ground-state deformations for the majority of nucleon-stable nuclei, including those far from stability, and could enable systematic surveys of nuclear shapes across the nuclear chart.
