# Nature of the first excited state of He-4

> We study the first excited state of He4 in a microscopic {H3+p,He3+n} cluster model, including H3 and He3 distortions. The phenomenological 1S0 H3+p scattering phase shift is well reproduced. We localize a complex pole of the S-matrix between the H3+p and He3+n thresholds. The corresponding resonance parameters are E_r=93 keV position relative to H3+p, and Gamma=390 keV width. A pole search is also performed in an extended R-matrix method, and a resonance is found with parameters E_r=114 keV and Gamma=392 keV. The R-matrix approach gives several additional poles, some of which may be connected with an enhanced threshold effect.

## Key claim

The first excited state of He-4 is a conventional S-matrix pole (not a shadow pole) lying between the H-3+p and He-3+n thresholds, with resonance parameters that differ substantially from the previously quoted experimental values when extracted from complex-energy rather than real-energy analysis.

## Method

The authors used a microscopic two-cluster Resonating Group Model (RGM) with H-3+p, He-3+n, and d+d configurations, including H-3 and He-3 cluster distortions, to calculate the S-matrix at complex energies and search for poles on multiple Riemann sheets. They also performed an extended R-matrix analysis of experimental data from the A=4 system, continuing the S-matrix onto unphysical sheets to locate poles.

## Key numbers

- RGM pole position: E_r = 93 keV (relative to H-3+p threshold), Gamma = 390 keV
- R-matrix pole position: E_r = 114 keV (relative to H-3+p threshold), Gamma = 392 keV
- Ground state energy from best RGM model: -20.53 MeV relative to H-3+p threshold
- H-3+p to He-3+n threshold energy difference: 0.770 MeV in the N_t=N_h=3 model space (experimental: 0.763 MeV)
- R-matrix shadow poles found at energies between 3.0 and 3.6 MeV with widths 6-8 MeV
- Additional R-matrix pole at epsilon = (7.68 - i3.57) MeV on the [---] sheet

## Context

This work resolves a long-standing puzzle about the nature of the He-4 first excited state, which earlier shell-model calculations (Zheng et al., 1993-1995; Navratil and Barrett, 1996) had failed to reproduce at the correct excitation energy. The study extends the complex-energy pole-search method previously applied to He-5 and Li-5 (Csoto and Hale, 1997) and contradicts the speculation in Tilley et al. (1992) that the state might originate from a shadow pole far from the physical region. The results enable future microscopic calculations to correctly identify resonance parameters from complex-energy analysis rather than real-energy prescriptions.
