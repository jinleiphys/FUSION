# Photons from the Early Stages of Relativistic Heavy Ion Collisions

> We present results about photons production in relativistic heavy ion collisions. The main novelty of our study is the calculation of the contribution of the early stage photons to the photon spectrum. The initial stage is modeled by an ensemble of classical gluon fields which decay to a quark-gluon plasma via the Schwinger mechanism, and the evolution of the system is studied by coupling classical field equations to relativistic kinetic theory; photons production is then computed by including the pertinent collision processes into the collision integral. We find that the contribution of the early stage photons to the direct photon spectrum is substantial for $p_T \approx 2$ GeV and higher, the exact value depending on the collision energy; therefore we identify this part of the photon spectrum as the sign of the early stage. Moreover, the amount of photons produced during the early stage is not negligible with respect to those produced by a thermalized quark-gluon plasma: we support the idea that there is no dark age in relativistic heavy ion collisions.

## Key claim

The early stage of relativistic heavy ion collisions, modeled by classical gluon fields decaying via the Schwinger mechanism, produces a substantial contribution to the direct photon spectrum for transverse momentum \(p_T \approx 2\) GeV and higher, with the exact value depending on the collision energy; this challenges the idea of a "dark age" in the pre-equilibrium phase.

## Method

The authors model the initial stage as an ensemble of classical color fields that decay into a quark-gluon plasma via the Schwinger mechanism, then couple the classical field equations to relativistic kinetic theory solved with the test particles method. Photon production is implemented by adding Compton scattering and quark-antiquark annihilation processes into the collision integral, with the cross sections scaled by a temperature-dependent factor to reproduce the Arnold-Moore-Yaffe (AMY) production rate in equilibrium. The initial color field is given a Glauber-type transverse profile, with parameters tuned to match the eccentricity and total multiplicity of a standard Glauber initialization at the typical hydrodynamical starting time.

## Key numbers

* For Au-Au collisions at 200 AGeV (20-40% centrality), the photon abundance in the Abelian Flux Tube model (AFTm) is approximately 30% higher than that obtained with the Glauber initialization.
* The peak value of the initial color electric field is set to \(E_0 = 3.0\) GeV\(^2\) for Au-Au at 200 AGeV and \(E_0 = 6.0\) GeV\(^2\) for Pb-Pb at 2.76 ATeV, both for 20-40% centrality.
* The early stage photon spectrum is defined at \(t_0 = 0.6\) fm/c for RHIC and \(t_0 = 0.3\) fm/c for LHC; the enhancement of the photon spectrum from the early stage is most important for \(p_T \gtrsim 1.5\) GeV.
* Within the AFTm, quarks are formed within 0.4 fm/c after the collision, and the relative abundance of quarks to gluons is not far from chemical equilibrium values.

## Context

This work extends the study of pre-equilibrium photon production beyond the bottom-up thermalization scenario examined by Berges (2017) and the transport approach of Greif (2016), where quarks are produced solely by inelastic scatterings. The key difference is that here quarks and gluons are produced on the same footing by the decay of the initial classical gluon field, leading to quicker photon production in the early stage. The results support the absence of a dark age in relativistic heavy ion collisions and identify the momentum region \(p_T \gtrsim 1.5\) GeV as the signature of early stage photons, enabling future comparisons with experimental direct photon spectra.
