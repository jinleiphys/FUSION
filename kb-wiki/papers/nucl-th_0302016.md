# Dynamics of many-particle fragmentation in a Cellular Automaton model

> A 3D Cellular Automaton model developed by the authors to deal with the dynamics of N-body interactions has been adapted to investigate the head-on collision of two identical bound clusters of particles, and the ensuing process of fragmentation. The range of impact energies is chosen low enough, to secure that a compound bound cluster can be formed. The model is devised to simulate the laboratory set-up of fragmentation experiments as monitored by 4pi detectors. The particles interact via a Lennard-Jones potential. At low impact energies the numerical experiments following the dynamics of the individual particles indicate a phase of energy sharing among all the particles of the compound cluster. Fragments of all sizes are then found to evaporate from the latter cluster. The cluster sizes, measured in our set-up by simulated 4pi detectors, conform to a power law of exponent around 2.6.

## Key claim
The paper claims that a 3D Cellular Automaton model can simulate head-on collisions of identical bound clusters at low impact energies, producing fragment size distributions that follow a power law with exponent approximately 2.6. However, the authors explicitly caution that the fragmentation system remains far from thermodynamic equilibrium, and different temperature definitions generate different curve patterns, meaning the system cannot be described by a single equilibrium temperature.

## Method
The authors implemented a 3D Cellular Automaton on a cubic lattice of size L=127 with toroidal topology, where particles interact via a step-potential approximation to the Lennard-Jones potential with three energy parameters (V_o = -3.0 MeV, V_1 = -0.3 MeV, ΔV = +1.0 MeV). Two identical clusters of 150 particles each were placed on the x-axis with head-on initial velocities, and the dynamics were followed for approximately 70 timesteps. Fragment detection was performed by counting clusters crossing six detector planes at distance d_D ≈ 50 from the collision center, mimicking 4π detector coverage. The authors reconstructed a cylindrically symmetric (D_∞h) spatial distribution from the CA's D_4h-symmetric distribution by truncating a Legendre polynomial expansion after the dipole term.

## Key numbers
- Fragment size distribution follows a power law with exponent approximately 2.6, measured by simulated 4π detectors
- Each initial cluster contains A = 150 particles, with total system of 300 particles
- Lattice cell size λ = 1.95 fm, chosen to match empirical R-A relation for A = 150
- Impact energy per nucleon E_imp/A = 3.75 MeV for the detailed dynamical analysis
- Compound cluster decay time τ_dis = 6.58 timesteps for the specific experiment shown
- Interaction neighborhood extends to distance d_int ≈ 20 cells; detector distance d_D ≈ 50 cells
- Potential parameters: V_o = -3.0 MeV, V_1 = -0.3 MeV, ΔV = +1.0 MeV
- Reference volume for compound nucleus analysis: cube of 21³ cells

## Context
This work extends the authors' previous Cellular Automaton models (Lejeune, Perdang, Richter 1999 in 2D and 2000 in 3D) by moving from particles confined to a finite box to an effectively infinite lattice space that simulates laboratory 4π detector setups. The power-law exponent of approximately 2.6 matches experimental values of τ = 2.64 for Xe and 2.65 for Kr targets from proton-induced fragmentation experiments (Minich 1982), and is compared to the mean-field critical exponent τ = 2.33 from liquid-gas phase transition theory (Jagannathan 1983). The approach contrasts with statistical equilibrium models (such as those reviewed by Richert 2001) and classical molecular dynamics calculations (Aichelin 1988, Fink 1986), as the authors explicitly avoid assuming thermodynamic equilibrium. The work enables future extension to non-zero impact parameter collisions and provides a framework for comparing theoretical caloric curves with experimental data from Au-Au collisions (NuPECC report 1997).
