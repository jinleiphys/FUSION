# Backward pion-nucleon scattering

> A global analysis of the world data on differential cross sections and polarization asymmetries of backward pion-nucleon scattering for invariant collision energies above 3 GeV is performed in a Regge model. Including the $N_α$, $N_γ$, $Δ_δ$ and $Δ_β$ trajectories, we reproduce both angular distributions and polarization data for small values of the Mandelstam variable $u$, in contrast to previous analyses. The model amplitude is used to obtain evidence for baryon resonances with mass below 3 GeV. Our analysis suggests a $G_{39}$ resonance with a mass of 2.83 GeV as member of the $Δ_β$ trajectory from the corresponding Chew-Frautschi plot.

## Key claim

The authors claim that by including a second Delta trajectory (the $\Delta_\beta$ trajectory) in addition to the standard $N_\alpha$, $N_\gamma$, and $\Delta_\delta$ trajectories, they can simultaneously reproduce both differential cross sections and polarization asymmetries for backward pion-nucleon scattering at invariant collision energies above 3 GeV, which previous Regge models could not achieve. The analysis suggests a $G_{39}$ resonance with mass 2.83 GeV as a member of the $\Delta_\beta$ trajectory from the Chew-Frautschi plot, but this is presented as a suggestion rather than a definitive discovery.

## Method

The authors performed a global Regge model analysis of world data on differential cross sections and polarization asymmetries for $\pi^+p \to \pi^+p$, $\pi^-p \to \pi^-p$, and $\pi^-p \to \pi^0n$ backward scattering at invariant collision energies $\sqrt{s} \geq 3$ GeV. The $s$-channel helicity amplitudes were parameterized using four baryon Regge trajectories ($N_\alpha$, $N_\gamma$, $\Delta_\delta$, and $\Delta_\beta$) with linear trajectory functions and residue functions linear in the Mandelstam variable $u$, with a common slope parameter for all trajectories. A total of 505 data points were fitted with 21 free parameters, yielding a $\chi^2$ per data point of 1.84.

## Key numbers

- Total fit to 505 data points gave $\chi^2$ per data point of 1.84
- For $\pi^+p \to \pi^+p$ differential cross sections: 227 data points, $\chi^2$/ND = 2.32
- For $\pi^-p \to \pi^-p$ differential cross sections: 187 data points, $\chi^2$/ND = 1.43
- For $\pi^-p \to \pi^0n$ (charge-exchange) differential cross sections: 59 data points, $\chi^2$/ND = 1.94
- For $\pi^+p \to \pi^+p$ polarization: 20 data points, $\chi^2$/ND = 0.71
- For $\pi^-p \to \pi^-p$ polarization: 12 data points, $\chi^2$/ND = 0.65
- Common slope parameter for all trajectories: $\alpha' = 0.908$ GeV$^{-2}$
- $N_\alpha$ trajectory intercept: $\alpha_0 = -0.36$
- $N_\gamma$ trajectory intercept: $\alpha_0 = -0.62$
- $\Delta_\delta$ trajectory intercept: $\alpha_0 = 0.03$
- $\Delta_\beta$ trajectory intercept: $\alpha_0 = -2.65$
- The dip in $\pi^+p \to \pi^+p$ differential cross section occurs near $u \simeq -0.15$ GeV$^2$, where the $N_\alpha$ trajectory passes through $\alpha(u) \simeq -1/2$
- Polarization data at $\sqrt{s} = 3.49$ GeV (pion beam momentum 6 GeV) were reproduced
- A $G_{39}$ resonance with mass 2.83 GeV is suggested as a member of the $\Delta_\beta$ trajectory

## Context

This work extends the Regge phenomenology of backward pion-nucleon scattering beyond earlier analyses by Barger and Cline (1967, 1968), Berger and Fox (1971), and Storrow (1975), which could not simultaneously describe differential cross sections and polarization data. Previous attempts by Birsa et al. (1977) to fit data at 3.5 and 6 GeV separately could not achieve a simultaneous description. The inclusion of the $\Delta_\beta$ trajectory resolves the long-standing problem of predicting zero polarization for $\pi^-p \to \pi^-p$ scattering, which plagued models using only the $\Delta_\delta$ trajectory. The extrapolation of the Regge amplitude to energies below 3 GeV enables identification of possible resonance contributions, and the Chew-Frautschi plot analysis connects the scattering trajectories to the baryon spectrum from partial wave analyses by the Karlsruhe-Helsinki group (Hoehler, 1983) and the George Washington University group (Arndt, 2004).
