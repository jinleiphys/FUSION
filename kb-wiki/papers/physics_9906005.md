# Electronic entropy, shell structure, and size-evolutionary patterns of metal clusters

> We show that electronic-entropy effects in the size-evolutionary patterns of relatively small (as small as 20 atoms), simple-metal clusters become prominent already at moderate temperatures. Detailed agreement between our finite-temperature-shell-correction-method calculations and experimental results is obtained for certain temperatures. This agreement includes a size-dependent smearing out of fine-structure features, accompanied by a measurable reduction of the heights of the steps marking major-shell and subshell closings, thus allowing for a quantitative analysis of cluster temperatures.

# Electronic entropy, shell structure, and size-evolutionary patterns of metal clusters

## Key claim

Electronic-entropy effects in the size-evolutionary patterns of simple-metal clusters become prominent already at moderate temperatures for clusters as small as 20 atoms, contrary to prior suggestions that such effects are unimportant below 100 atoms. The agreement between finite-temperature shell-correction-method calculations and experimental data is obtained only for specific temperatures (300 K for potassium clusters, 800 K for silver clusters), with higher temperatures obliterating the agreement.

## Method

The authors developed a finite-temperature shell-correction method (FT-SCM) that incorporates triaxial (ellipsoidal) deformations, electronic entropy via a canonical ensemble with number projection, and thermal shape fluctuations via a Boltzmann-type average over the free-energy surface. They calculated ionization potentials (IPs) and monomer separation energies (MSEs) for K_N and Ag_N clusters at various temperatures, comparing with experimental measurements from Saunders (1986) for potassium and Jackschath (1992) for silver.

## Key numbers

- For K_N clusters at T=10 K, the theoretical IP steps at major-shell closures (N=40, 58, 92) are three-to-five times larger than experimental values; at T=300 K these steps are reduced drastically and become comparable to experiment
- For K_N clusters at T=300 K, the fine-structure features above N=21 are smeared out, with three rounded hump-like formations surviving at subshell closures N=26, 30, and 34
- For K_N clusters at T=500 K, steps at subshell closures N=26 and 30 and major-shell closures N=40, 58, and 92 are rounded and smeared out over several clusters
- For Ag_N clusters, T=800 K gives IP drops at N=6, 8, 14, 20, 26, 30, and 34 comparable to experimental values; at T=2000 K both major and fine structure vanish for N > 8
- Experimental uncertainties for K_N IPs: 0.06 eV for N ≤ 30 and 0.03 eV for N > 30
- The shell structure "melts" for temperatures as low as T/(hbar omega_sh) = 0.25, where the shell-correction term in the free energy depends on T as Delta F_sh(T) = Delta F_sh(0) * tau / sinh(tau), with tau = 2 pi^2 T / (hbar omega_sh)

## Context

This work extends the zero-temperature shell-correction method of Yannouleas and Landman (1995) by adding finite-temperature effects, and contradicts earlier studies by Brack (1993) and Frauendorf and Pashkevich (1996) which suggested electronic-entropy effects are unimportant for clusters with less than 100 atoms at moderate temperatures. The method enables quantitative determination of cluster temperatures from experimental size-evolutionary patterns, and the authors note that unlike nuclear and atomic physics (where experimental separation energies and IPs correspond strictly to zero temperature), metal clusters provide a unique system for studying finite-temperature fermionic shell structure.
