# Extraction of the symmetry energy coefficients from the masses differences of isobaric nuclei

> The nuclear symmetry energy coefficients of finite nuclei are extracted by using the differences between the masses of isobaric nuclei. Based on the masses of more than 2400 nuclei with $A=9-270$, we investigate the model dependence in the extraction of symmetry energy coefficient. We find that the extraction of the symmetry energy coefficients is strongly correlated with the forms of the Coulomb energy and the mass dependence of the symmetry energy coefficient adopted. The values of the extracted symmetry energy coefficients increase by about 2 MeV for heavy nuclei when the Coulomb correction term is involved. We obtain the bulk symmetry energy coefficient $S_0=28.26\pm1.3$ MeV and the surface-to-volume ratio $κ=1.26\pm 0.25 $ MeV if assuming the mass dependence of symmetry energy coefficient $a_{\rm sym}(A)=S_0(1-κ/A^{1/3})$, and $S_0=32.80\pm1.7$ MeV, $κ=2.82\pm0.57$ MeV when $a_{\rm sym}(A)=S_0 (1+κ/A^{1/3})^{-1}$ is adopted.

# Extraction of the symmetry energy coefficients from the masses differences of isobaric nuclei

## Key claim

The extracted values of the bulk symmetry energy coefficient S0 and the surface-to-volume ratio κ depend strongly on the assumed functional form of the mass dependence of the symmetry energy coefficient and on whether Coulomb correction terms (charge exchange and surface diffuseness) are included; the extracted S0 changes by about 2 MeV for heavy nuclei when the Coulomb correction term is added, and the two-parameter fits yield S0 = 28.26 ± 1.3 MeV or S0 = 32.80 ± 1.7 MeV depending on which of two common parameterizations is adopted.

## Method

The authors extract the symmetry energy coefficient asym(A) for finite nuclei by taking differences of experimental binding energies of isobaric nuclei (from the 2012 Atomic Mass Evaluation) for more than 2400 nuclei with mass numbers A = 9–270, after subtracting shell corrections (from the KTUY model) and Wigner energy. The Coulomb energy coefficients are first determined from the binding energy differences of 88 mirror nuclei pairs in the region 11 ≤ A ≤ 75, using four different Coulomb energy expressions (with and without a charge-exchange/surface-diffuseness correction term bZ−2/3). The extracted asym(A) values are then fitted to two parameterizations of the mass dependence: asym(A) = S0(1 − κ/A1/3) and asym(A) = S0(1 + κ/A1/3)−1.

## Key numbers

- Bulk symmetry energy coefficient S0 = 28.26 ± 1.3 MeV and surface-to-volume ratio κ = 1.26 ± 0.25 MeV, obtained from fitting the extracted asym(A) with the form asym(A) = S0(1 − κ/A1/3) using the Coulomb energy expression set IV and KTUY shell corrections for 262 data points.
- Bulk symmetry energy coefficient S0 = 32.80 ± 1.7 MeV and κ = 2.82 ± 0.57 MeV, obtained from the same data but using the alternative form asym(A) = S0(1 + κ/A1/3)−1.
- Coulomb energy coefficient ac = 0.704 MeV and correction parameter b = 0.985 for the expression Ec = (ac/A1/3) Z(Z−1)(1 − bZ−2/3) (set IV), determined from 88 mirror pairs with a root-mean-squared deviation of 118 keV.
- Coulomb energy coefficient ac = 0.715 MeV and b = 1.374 for the expression Ec = (ac/A1/3) Z2(1 − bZ−2/3) (set II), with a root-mean-squared deviation of 121 keV.
- The extracted symmetry energy coefficients increase by about 2 MeV for heavy nuclei when the Coulomb correction term is included.

## Context

This work extends the liquid-drop mass formula approach of Bethe and Weizsacker (1935–1936) and the method of extracting symmetry energy from mirror-nuclei binding energy differences used by Kirson (2008). The authors compare their results with those of Liu et al. (2010), who obtained S0 = 31.1 MeV and κ = 2.31 MeV using the WS shell corrections and a different Coulomb expression. The strong model dependence demonstrated here, particularly the sensitivity to the form of the Coulomb energy and the mass dependence of asym, indicates that future extractions of the symmetry energy must carefully specify these choices, and the results enable more reliable input for nuclear structure calculations and astrophysical simulations of core-collapse supernovae.
