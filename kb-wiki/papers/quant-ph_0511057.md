# Casimir interaction between normal or superfluid grains in the Fermi sea

> We report on a new force that acts on cavities (literally empty regions of space) when they are immersed in a background of non-interacting fermionic matter fields. The interaction follows from the obstructions to the (quantum mechanical) motions of the fermions caused by the presence of bubbles or other (heavy) particles in the Fermi sea, as, for example, nuclei in the neutron sea in the inner crust of a neutron star or superfluid grains in a normal Fermi liquid. The effect resembles the traditional Casimir interaction between metallic mirrors in the vacuum. However, the fluctuating electromagnetic fields are replaced by fermionic matter fields. We show that the fermionic Casimir problem for a system of spherical cavities can be solved exactly, since the calculation can be mapped onto a quantum mechanical billiard problem of a point-particle scattered off a finite number of non-overlapping spheres or disks. Finally we generalize the map method to other Casimir systems, especially to the case of a fluctuating scalar field between two spheres or a sphere and a plate under Dirichlet boundary conditions.

# Casimir interaction between normal or superfluid grains in the Fermi sea

## Key claim
A new, long-ranged force exists between cavities (empty regions) immersed in a background of non-interacting fermionic matter fields, arising from the obstruction of fermion motion by the cavities. Unlike the standard electromagnetic Casimir effect, this fermionic Casimir energy oscillates in sign as a function of separation rather than being purely attractive, because the chemical potential introduces a second scale beyond the geometric ones.

## Method
The calculation maps the fermionic Casimir problem for spherical cavities onto a quantum mechanical billiard problem of a point particle scattered off non-overlapping spheres or disks, using Krein's trace formula to link the variation in level density to the energy-variation of the phase shift of the multi-sphere scattering matrix. The geometry-dependent part of the density of states is extracted from the multiple-scattering part of the S-matrix via a modified Krein equation formulated in terms of the inverse multi-scattering matrix M(k). For two small spherical cavities, the integrated density of states is approximated analytically in the s-wave limit and compared with the semiclassical Gutzwiller trace formula.

## Key numbers
- For two small spherical cavities of common radius a and center-to-center separation r, the integrated geometry-dependent density of states in the s-wave approximation is: N_C^oo(eps) ≈ nu_deg * (a^2 / (pi * r^2)) * sin[2(r - a)k] + O((ka)^3), where nu_deg is the spin/isospin degeneracy factor.
- The semiclassical approximation for the same system gives: N_C,sc^oo(eps) = nu_deg * (a^2 / (4*pi*r*(r-2a))) * sin[2(r-2a)k], suppressed by a factor of 1/4 relative to the s-wave result.
- The Casimir energy for two spherical cavities, valid for k_F*a > 1, is: Ec^oo ≈ -nu_deg * mu * (a^2 / (2*pi*r*(r-2a))) * j_1[2(r-2a)k_F], which scales as 1/L^3 with L = r-2a.
- For the sphere-plate system, the Casimir energy is: Ec^o| ≈ -nu_deg * mu * (a / (2*pi*(r-a))) * j_1[2(r-a)k_F], scaling as 1/L^2 with L = r-a.
- For the scalar Dirichlet sphere-plate system at large separations, the Casimir energy is enhanced by a factor of 2 * 90/pi^4 relative to the proximity force approximation.

## Context
This work extends the concept of the Casimir effect, originally formulated by Casimir (1948) for electromagnetic fields between metallic plates, to fermionic matter fields. It builds on prior work by Bulgac and Magierski showing that shell correction energies (fermionic Casimir energies) in low-density neutron matter with bubbles are comparable to liquid-drop-model phase differences. The map method generalizes to other Casimir systems, particularly the scalar Dirichlet problem for two spheres or sphere-plate configurations, where the s-wave approximation yields results compatible with the wordline approach of Gies and Scardicchio. Applications include the inner crust of neutron stars (nuclei in a neutron sea), quark droplets in hadronic matter, and laboratory systems such as C60 buckyballs in liquid mercury or liquid 3He.
