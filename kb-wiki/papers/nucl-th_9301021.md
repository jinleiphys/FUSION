# Self-Similarity and Scaling Behavior in Nuclear Collision

> A simple model is used to explore issues related to self-similarity, intermittency and scaling behavior in nuclear collisions. Both scaled factorial moments and power moments are considered in the investigation of these features. The product mass yields are used to construct probability distributions and generalized Renyi entropies. The scaling properties of the resulting Renyi entropies are studied. Both exponential and power law behavior are found. A discussion of dimensions associated with these scaling properties is also given. An analysis of some experimental data is presented.

## Key claim

The paper uses an exactly soluble fragmentation model to show that both exponential and power-law scaling behavior can appear in nuclear collision fragment distributions, but the specific scaling form (exponential vs. power law) depends on the model parameter \(x\) (related to temperature or interaction volume), with the power-law case (\(x=1\)) corresponding to scale invariance and self-similarity, while the abstract does not specify this conditional dependence on the parameter value.

## Method

The authors employ a canonical ensemble fragmentation model where each partition of \(A\) nucleons into clusters is weighted by a product of factors \((x_j/j)^{n_j}/n_j!\), with \(x_j\) parameters characterizing clusters of size \(j\). They compute ensemble-averaged factorial moments \(\langle \tilde n_k^q \rangle\) and scaled factorial moments \(F_q(L)\) for bins of size \(L\) in cluster-size space, as well as power moments and generalized Renyi entropies from the mass fraction distribution \(P_A(k,\vec x) = k n_k / A\). The model is solved analytically for the "\(x\) model" (all \(x_j = x\)) and the "\(x\)-\(y\) model" (\(x_1 = xy\), \(x_i = x\) for \(i \ge 2\)), and results are compared to experimental data.

## Key numbers

- For the \(x\) model with \(x=1\), the mean cluster number \(\langle n_k \rangle = 1/k\), exhibiting power-law behavior and scale invariance.
- For \(x=1\) and \(A=200\), the mean number of free nucleons \(\langle n_1 \rangle = 1\) and the variance \(\sigma^2 = 1\).
- For \(x=1\), the scaled factorial moment \(F_q(L=1) = \left[\sum_{k=1}^{[A/q]} 1/k^q\right] / \left[\sum_{k=1}^A 1/k^q\right]\).
- For large \(x\) (high temperature), \(F_q(L)\) decreases exponentially with a correlation length inversely proportional to \((q-1)^2\) for small \(L\).
- For small \(x\) (low temperature), \(F_q(L)\) follows a power law with intermittency exponent \(\alpha(q)\) proportional to \(q\).
- For \(A=200\) and \(\langle n_1 \rangle = 7.3823\) (corresponding to \(x=7.6269\)), the distribution \(P_A(n_1,x)\) is indistinguishable from a Poisson distribution.

## Context

This work extends the Bialas-Peschanski (1986) approach to intermittency using scaled factorial moments, which was originally applied to rapidity distributions in cosmic ray experiments and later taken up by Ploszajczak and Tucholski (1990) for nuclear fragment distributions. The model provides an exactly soluble framework that connects fragmentation statistics to Renyi entropies and generalized dimensions (Hausdorff, information, correlation), enabling quantitative comparison with experimental mass-yield data from proton-nucleus and nucleus-nucleus collisions. The results enable future studies of whether observed intermittency signals in nuclear collisions arise from genuine scale invariance or from mixing of events with different thermodynamic parameters.
