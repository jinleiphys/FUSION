# Summit of the N=40 Island of Inversion: precision mass measurements and ab initio calculations of neutron-rich chromium isotopes

> Mass measurements continue to provide invaluable information for elucidating nuclear structure and scenarios of astrophysical interest. The transition region between the $Z = 20$ and $28$ proton shell closures is particularly interesting due to the onset and evolution of nuclear deformation as nuclei become more neutron rich. This provides a critical testing ground for emerging ab-initio nuclear structure models. Here, we present high-precision mass measurements of neutron-rich chromium isotopes using the sensitive electrostatic Multiple-Reflection Time-Of-Flight Mass Spectrometer (MR-TOF-MS) at TRIUMF's Ion Trap for Atomic and Nuclear Science (TITAN) facility. Our high-precision mass measurements of $^{59, 61-63}$Cr confirm previous results, and the improved precision in measurements of $^{64-65}$Cr refine the mass surface beyond N=40. With the ab initio in-medium similarity renormalization group, we examine the trends in collectivity in chromium isotopes and give a complete picture of the N=40 island of inversion from calcium to nickel.

## Key claim

High-precision mass measurements of neutron-rich chromium isotopes, including the first direct measurements of 64Cr and 65Cr, show that the two-neutron separation energies do not follow the upward trend observed for lighter Cr isotopes when approaching N=40, contradicting earlier expectations of a gradual onset of collectivity and instead pointing to maximal collectivity centered at 64Cr as the pinnacle of the N=40 island of inversion.

## Method

Mass measurements of 59,61-65Cr were performed using the Multiple-Reflection Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometer (MR-TOF-MS) at TRIUMF's TITAN facility, with ions produced by a 480 MeV proton beam on a UCX target and selectively ionized using resonant laser ionization. The time-of-flight spectra were converted to mass spectra using a calibration function with time-resolved calibration to correct for drifts, and peak centroids were determined by fitting a hyper-EMG function. The measured masses were used to calculate two-neutron separation energies and pairing gap energies, which were compared to ab initio valence-space in-medium similarity renormalization group (VS-IMSRG) calculations performed in a proton pf and neutron pf5/2g9/2d5/2 valence space above a 48Ca core.

## Key numbers

- Mass excess of 64Cr: -33573(26) keV, first high-precision direct measurement
- Mass excess of 65Cr: -28208(45) keV, first high-precision direct measurement
- Mass excess of 59Cr: -48113(7) keV, consistent with previous ISOLTRAP value of -48115.9(0.7) keV
- Mass excess of 61Cr: -42497(14) keV, consistent with previous ISOLTRAP value of -42496.5(1.8) keV
- Mass excess of 62Cr: -40855(19) keV, consistent with previous ISOLTRAP value of -40852.6(3.5) keV
- Mass excess of 63Cr: -36204(18) keV, consistent with previous ISOLTRAP value of -36178(73) keV
- Mass resolution of the MR-TOF-MS: nearly 300,000 FWHM
- Systematic uncertainty of the mass measurements: delta m/m of 1 x 10^-7
- Yield at the MR-TOF-MS dropped from approximately 10^3 pps for 59,61Cr to less than 1 pps for 64-65Cr
- Half-life determined for 65Cr from storage analysis: 23(12) ms, consistent with literature value of 27.5(2.1) ms

## Context

This work extends the precise mass measurements of Mougeot (2018) for 59-63Cr to include 64,65Cr, and contradicts the earlier suggestion from that work that the upward S2n trend would continue through N=40. The results are compared to LNPS shell-model calculations (Lenzi 2010) and to ab initio VS-IMSRG calculations (Miyagi 2020), with the VS-IMSRG systematically underestimating S2n values for N greater than 35, indicating missing many-body correlations beyond the IMSRG(2) approximation. The intruder configuration analysis shows that 64Cr has the strongest intruder contribution among elements from calcium to nickel, establishing it as the center of the N=40 island of inversion, and the work calls for further high-precision mass measurements beyond N=41 and advancement of ab initio theory to the IMSRG(3) level.
