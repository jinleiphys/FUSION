# Microscopic model analyses of proton scattering from 12C, 20Ne, 24Mg, 28Si and 40Ca

> Differential cross sections and analyzing powers for elastic scattering from, and for inelastic proton scattering to a set of $2^+_1$ states in, ${}^{12}$C, ${}^{20}$Ne, ${}^{24}$Mg, ${}^{28}$Si and ${}^{40}$Ca, and for a set of energies between 35 to 250 MeV, have been analyzed. A $g$-folding model has been used to determine optical potentials and a microscopic distorted wave approximation taken to analyze the inelastic data. The effective nucleon-nucleon interactions used to specify the optical potentials have also been used as the transition operators in the inelastic scattering processes. Shell and large space Hartree-Fock models of structure have been used to describe the nuclear states.

## Key claim

The g-folding model with microscopic optical potentials and distorted wave approximation (DWA) can reproduce elastic and inelastic proton scattering data across multiple nuclei and energies without a posteriori adjustment, provided that good nuclear structure models (shell model or Skyrme-Hartree-Fock) are used; however, at lower energies (35 and 51.93 MeV) for 12C, the calculations underestimate inelastic cross sections by factors of 2.0 and 1.5 respectively, likely due to neglected second-order processes involving virtual excitation of giant resonances.

## Method

The authors used a g-folding model to construct microscopic, complex, nonlocal, energy-dependent optical potentials from folding an effective nucleon-nucleon (NN) interaction (the Melbourne interaction) with one-body density matrix elements (OBDME) from nuclear structure models. For elastic scattering, these potentials were used directly; for inelastic scattering to 2+1 states, the same effective NN interaction served as the transition operator in a microscopic distorted wave approximation (DWA) using the DWBA98 code, which explicitly evaluates knock-out exchange amplitudes to preserve the Pauli principle. Structure inputs came from a no-core shell model (with a complete (0+2)h-bar-omega basis) for 12C, and from projected Hartree-Fock (PHF) plus Skyrme-Hartree-Fock (SHF) models for 20Ne, 24Mg, 28Si, and 40Ca, using harmonic oscillator (HO) or Woods-Saxon (WS) single-particle bound-state wave functions.

## Key numbers

- For 12C, the no-core shell model predicted the 2+1 state excitation energy at 4.62 MeV (experimental: 4.43 MeV)
- The calculated B(E2; 2+ to 0+ gs) for 12C using HO wave functions with oscillator length 1.7 fm gave 6.26 e2-fm4, compared to the known value of 7.77 e2-fm4 (Cohen-Kurath 0h-bar-omega shell model gives 3.26 e2-fm4)
- For 12C at 35 MeV, g-folding results underestimate inelastic cross section data by a factor of 2.0; at 51.93 MeV, by a factor of 1.5
- Ground-state shell occupancies for 12C from the no-core shell model: 0s1/2 (1.964), 0p3/2 (3.054), 0p1/2 (0.842) for both protons and neutrons, with remaining 0.14 nucleons in higher orbits
- For 40Ca, SHF plus shell model gave shell occupancies including 0d5/2 (6.000), 0d3/2 (3.090), 1s1/2 (1.800), and 0f7/2 (0.990)
- Elastic scattering data analyzed at energies: 35, 51.93, 120, 160, 200, and 250 MeV for 12C

## Context

This work extends the g-folding and DWA methods previously established by Amos et al. (2000) for nucleon-nucleus scattering in the energy range ~30 to ~300 MeV, applying them systematically to five nuclei from 12C to 40Ca. The approach contrasts with phenomenological Woods-Saxon optical potentials, which the authors argue can produce spurious states, violate the Pauli principle in coupled-channel problems, and yield wave functions that differ significantly from those of non-local potentials. The results enable future testing of nuclear structure models through scattering data, particularly awaiting SHF studies that provide excitation spectra and transition OBDME for sd-shell nuclei, as the current work had to use PHF transition OBDME with SHF wave functions inconsistently. The identification of giant resonance contributions as necessary corrections at lower energies points to needed extensions of the reaction model.
