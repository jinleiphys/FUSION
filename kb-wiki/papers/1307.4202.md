# Clusterized nuclear matter in the (proto-)neutron star crust and the symmetry energy

> Though generally agreed that the symmetry energy plays a dramatic role in determining the structure of neutron stars and the evolution of core-collapsing supernovae, little is known in what concerns its value away from normal nuclear matter density and, even more important, the correct definition of this quantity in the case of unhomogeneous matter. Indeed, nuclear matter traditionally addressed by mean-field models is uniform while clusters are known to exist in the dilute baryonic matter which constitutes the main component of compact objects outer shells. In the present work we investigate the meaning of symmetry energy in the case of clusterized systems and the sensitivity of the proto-neutron star composition and equation of state to the effective interaction. To this aim an improved Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium (NSE) model is developed, where the same effective interaction is consistently used to determine the clusters and unbound particles energy functionals in the self-consistent mean-field approximation. In the same framework, in-medium modifications to the cluster energies due to the presence of the nuclear gas are evaluated. We show that the excluded volume effect does not exhaust the in-medium effects and an extra isospin and density dependent energy shift has to be considered to consistently determine the composition of subsaturation stellar matter. The symmetry energy of diluted matter is seen to depend on the isovector properties of the effective interaction, but its behavior with density and its quantitative value are strongly modified by clusterization.

# Clusterized nuclear matter in the (proto-)neutron star crust and the symmetry energy

## Key claim

The symmetry energy of dilute baryonic matter in the (proto-)neutron star crust depends on the isovector properties of the effective nuclear interaction, but its behavior with density and quantitative value are strongly modified by clusterization, meaning the parabolic approximation for the symmetry energy is not justified in inhomogeneous matter and the excluded volume effect alone does not exhaust in-medium modifications.

## Method

The authors developed an improved Nuclear Statistical Equilibrium (NSE) model where the same Skyrme effective interaction is consistently used to determine both the cluster and unbound particle energy functionals in the self-consistent mean-field approximation, using the Danielewicz and Lee (2009) parametrization for cluster ground-state energies fitted on Hartree-Fock calculations. Four Skyrme interactions (SLY4, SGI, SkI3, LNS) with similar isoscalar properties but different isovector behavior were employed to study sensitivity to the symmetry energy, and in-medium modifications to cluster energies from the surrounding nuclear gas were evaluated including both bulk excluded-volume effects and an additional isospin and density dependent surface energy shift. The model was applied to low-temperature (T=1 MeV) matter in beta-equilibrium appropriate for the (proto-)neutron star crust and pre-bounce core-collapse supernova dynamics.

## Key numbers

- Saturation densities for symmetric matter range from 0.1544 fm^-3 (SGI) to 0.1746 fm^-3 (LNS) across the four interactions
- Slope parameter L of the symmetry energy ranges from 46.0 MeV (SLY4) to 100.5 MeV (SkI3)
- Curvature K_sym of the symmetry energy ranges from -127.4 MeV (LNS) to 73.0 MeV (SkI3)
- Compression modulus K ranges from 210.8 MeV (LNS) to 261.8 MeV (SGI)
- At T=1 MeV and beta-equilibrium, the overall proton fraction Y_p decreases monotonically from slightly below 0.4 at density approximately 10^-7 fm^-3 to almost zero at density greater than 5 x 10^-2 fm^-3
- The average cluster mass shows a gentle increase over several orders of magnitude in density, with a sudden fall for density above 10^-2 fm^-3

## Context

This work extends the NSE approach of Raduta et al. (2010) and the excluded-volume models of Hempel et al. (2010) by consistently using the same effective interaction for both cluster and gas components, addressing the inconsistency where previous models used experimental binding energies or phenomenological liquid-drop parametrizations for clusters while treating unbound nucleons with mean-field theory. The results confirm the earlier zero-temperature findings of Negele and Vautherin (1973) that clusters survive in extremely neutron-rich environments, and show that the sensitivity of cluster properties to the symmetry energy persists even in clusterized matter, enabling more reliable predictions for neutron star crust structure and core-collapse supernova simulations.
