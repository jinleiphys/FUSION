# Thermodynamic nature of upbend resonance and validity of Brink-Axel hypothesis in the low-energy region

> The nature of low-energy enhancement in the radiative strength function (RSF), which is known as the upbend resonance (UBR) and has a crucial role in the description of neutron-captured cross section and stellar nucleosynthesis, is still under debate. The present letter extends the exact thermal pairing plus phonon damping model to explore the microscopic nature of the UBR and its thermodynamic origin over a wide mass range of odd-odd, odd-A, and even-even systems, from $^{44}$Sc to $^{153}$Sm, whose experimental RSFs, including the UBRs, are available. The results of our calculations indicate that the UBR originates from non-collective particle-particle and hole-hole excitations with a strength three times stronger than that of the giant dipole resonance. Moreover, our results reveal that the UBR, which emerges only at finite temperatures within the present framework, invalidates the Brink-Axel hypothesis in the very low $E_γ$ region. Last but not least, a global relation between the integrated strength of the RSF in the UBR region to that of the total RSF and the mass number is reported, for the first time, within the present study.

# Thermodynamic nature of upbend resonance and validity of Brink-Axel hypothesis in the low-energy region

## Key claim

The upbend resonance (UBR) originates from non-collective particle-particle and hole-hole excitations that exist only at finite temperature, with a coupling strength three times stronger than that of the giant dipole resonance (GDR); this temperature-dependent emergence invalidates the Brink-Axel hypothesis specifically in the very low gamma-ray energy region (E_gamma below approximately 3 MeV), contrary to the hypothesis's assumption that the radiative strength function depends only on gamma-ray energy.

## Method

The authors extended the exact thermal pairing plus phonon damping model (EP+PDM) by introducing a separate "UBR phonon" collective excitation in the very low-energy region, completely decoupled from the GDR phonon. The damping of this UBR phonon is described microscopically via couplings to particle-hole (ph), particle-particle (pp), and hole-hole (hh) configurations, where the pp and hh couplings become active only at nonzero temperature due to Fermi-surface distortion. The model was applied to calculate radiative strength functions for 22 nuclei from 44Sc to 153Sm, using single-particle spectra from an axially deformed Woods-Saxon potential and temperature-independent coupling strengths determined systematically.

## Key numbers

- The coupling strengths F^UBR_ph and F^UBR_ss' for the UBR phonon are consistently three times larger than those corresponding to the GDR across all considered nuclei.
- The UBR contributes approximately 5% of the total RSF in light nuclei, with its contribution decreasing exponentially as mass number A increases.
- A global relation for the ratio R of integrated UBR strength to total RSF strength in the mass range A=40-160 is given by R_fit = 243.18*e^(-A/11.45) + 1.17*ln(A) - 5.05, with coefficient of determination R^2 = 0.95.
- The temperatures at which total RSFs were calculated are T = 1.3 MeV for 56,57Fe, T = 0.7 MeV for 76Ge, T = 0.8 MeV for 97Mo, and T = 0.5 MeV for 146,147Nd.
- The UBR is absent at T = 0 and emerges only at finite temperatures, with the pp configurations providing the dominant contribution while hh contributions are small but non-negligible.
- The UBR is predominantly of M1 dipole character, with negligible E1 contribution.

## Context

This work extends the EP+PDM framework previously developed by Hung et al. (2020) and applied to several nuclei, which had shown strong temperature dependence in the RSF at low energies but could not describe the UBR because it constrained all gamma transitions to a single GDR phonon oscillation regime. The present results contradict the Brink-Axel hypothesis in the very low E_gamma region, reinforcing earlier studies by Nabi, Farooq, Herrera, Sieja (2023), Misch, Hung et al. (2020), and others that reported BAH violations. The approach overcomes limitations of prior mean-field methods such as thermal continuum QRPA (Litvinova 2013) and finite-temperature relativistic time blocking approximation (Wibowo), which could not fully reproduce experimental UBR data, and avoids the computational constraints of shell-model calculations for heavy nuclei. The finding that UBR arises from pp and hh excitations at finite temperature provides a thermodynamic explanation for the resonance, with implications for improving neutron-capture cross-section calculations and stellar nucleosynthesis rates where UBR effects can cause discrepancies exceeding an order of magnitude in neutron-rich nuclei (Larsen 2010, Spyrou 2024).
