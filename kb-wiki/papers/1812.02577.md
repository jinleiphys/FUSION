# Structure of hot strange quark stars: an NJL model approach at finite temperature

> In this paper, we investigated the thermodynamic properties of strange quark matter using Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite temperatures where we considered the dynamical mass as the effective interaction between quarks. By considering the pressure of strange quark matter (SQM) at finite temperatures, we showed that the equation of state of this system gets stiffer by increasing temperature. In addition, we investigated the energy conditions and stability of the equation of state and showed that the equation of state of SQM satisfy the conditions of stability. Finally, we computed the structure properties of hot strange quark stars (SQS) including the gravitational mass, radius, Schwarzschild radius, average density, compactness and gravitational redshift. Our calculations showed that in this model, the maximum mass and radius of SQS increase by increasing temperature. Furthermore it was shown that the average density of SQS is greater than the normal nuclear density, and it is an increasing function of temperature. We also discussed the temperature dependence of the maximum gravitational mass calculated from different methods.

## Key claim

Using the Nambu-Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model at finite temperature, the maximum gravitational mass and radius of strange quark stars (SQS) increase with increasing temperature, contrary to the behavior seen in some MIT bag model calculations where they decrease; this result is conditional on the specific treatment of dynamical quark mass and the chosen NJL model parameters (cut-off lambda = 602.3 MeV, coupling constants G and K).

## Method

The authors computed the thermodynamic properties of strange quark matter (SQM) using the NJL model at finite temperatures (T = 30, 45, 70, 90, and 150 MeV), calculating the dynamical quark masses, free energy, entropy, and pressure via the Helmholtz free energy. They then solved the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkov (TOV) equations using the resulting equation of state (EOS) to determine the structure of non-rotating, spherically symmetric strange quark stars, including mass, radius, average density, compactness, and gravitational redshift.

## Key numbers

- Minimum energy per particle of SQM: 408.77 MeV at T = 30 MeV and 928.55 MeV at T = 90 MeV, ensuring stability relative to iron (930.4 MeV).
- Maximum gravitational mass of SQS: 0.650 solar masses at T = 30 MeV, increasing to 1.002 solar masses at T = 90 MeV.
- Radius of SQS at maximum mass: 5.305 km at T = 30 MeV, increasing to 6.339 km at T = 90 MeV.
- Average density of SQS at maximum mass: 1.86 x 10^15 g/cm^3 at T = 30 MeV and 1.85 x 10^15 g/cm^3 at T = 90 MeV, both above normal nuclear matter density (2.7 x 10^14 g/cm^3).
- Central density of SQS: 7 x 10^15 g/cm^3 for T = 30 to 90 MeV, and 6.5 x 10^15 g/cm^3 at T = 150 MeV.
- Gravitational redshift at maximum mass: 0.239 at T = 30 MeV, increasing to 0.346 at T = 90 MeV; the latter is about 0.4% less than the observational value for candidate RX J185635-3754 (0.35 +/- 0.15).

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous studies of SQS structure using the MIT bag model at finite temperature (Bordbar 2011, 2012, 2013, 2014) and their zero-temperature NJL model calculation (Bordbar 2012). It contrasts with those MIT bag model results, where maximum mass and radius decreased with temperature, and aligns with the NJL-based results of Chu (2017) and some versions of the Quark-mass density-dependent model (Dexheimer 2013), which also show increasing mass and radius with temperature. The findings enable further investigation of hot proto-quark stars and provide a basis for comparing different effective QCD models in the context of compact star phenomenology.
