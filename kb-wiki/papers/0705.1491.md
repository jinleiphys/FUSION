# Why is Tin so soft?

> The distribution of isoscalar monopole strength in the neutron-even 112-124Sn-isotopes has been computed using a relativistic random-phase-approximation approach. The accurately-calibrated model used here (``FSUGold'') has been successful in reproducing both ground-state observables as well as collective excitations - including the giant monopole resonance (GMR) in 90Zr, 144Sm, and 208Pb. Yet this same model significantly overestimates the GMR energies in the Sn isotopes. It is argued that the question of ``Why is Tin so soft?'' becomes an important challenge to the field and one that should be answered without sacrificing the success already achieved by several theoretical models.

## Key claim

The FSUGold relativistic model, which accurately reproduces the giant monopole resonance (GMR) in 90Zr, 144Sm, and 208Pb, significantly overestimates the GMR centroid energies in the 112-124Sn isotopic chain, and the discrepancy grows with increasing neutron excess; this poses a challenge that must be resolved without sacrificing the model's success on other nuclei.

## Method

The distribution of isoscalar monopole strength was computed using a relativistic random-phase-approximation (RPA) approach for the neutron-even 112-124Sn isotopes. The longitudinal response was evaluated at a small momentum transfer of q = 0.23 fm^-1 to mimic forward-angle scattering, and centroid energies were extracted from the ratio of the m1 to m0 moments integrated over the 10 to 20 MeV excitation energy interval. Theoretical predictions from the FSUGold and NL3 models were compared against experimental data measured at the Research Center for Nuclear Physics (RCNP) in Osaka, Japan.

## Key numbers

- GMR centroid energy in 112Sn: FSUGold predicts 16.45 MeV, experiment gives 16.2 ± 0.1 MeV
- GMR centroid energy in 124Sn: FSUGold predicts 15.97 MeV, experiment gives 15.3 ± 0.1 MeV
- GMR centroid energy in 116Sn: FSUGold predicts 16.27 MeV, experiment gives 15.8 ± 0.1 MeV
- NL3 model predicts GMR centroid energies ranging from 16.98 MeV (112Sn) to 16.43 MeV (124Sn)
- The experimental slope of centroid energy versus neutron excess is more than 50% larger than the slope predicted by the NL3 model
- The FSUGold model predicts an incompressibility coefficient for symmetric nuclear matter of K = 230 MeV
- The consensus value for the incompressibility coefficient of symmetric nuclear matter is K = 230 ± 10 MeV

## Context

This work extends the earlier finding by Piekarewicz (2002, 2003) that the GMR in 208Pb constrains the incompressibility of neutron-rich matter rather than symmetric nuclear matter, and that the density dependence of the symmetry energy is critical. The new experimental data on the Sn isotopic chain from Garg (2006, 2007) provides a hadronic complement to the PREX parity-violating electron scattering experiment at Jefferson Laboratory. The puzzle challenges both non-relativistic models (Colo, 2004, 2007) and relativistic models to explain why Tin is anomalously soft without compromising the successful description of ground-state observables and collective excitations in other nuclei.
