# Elliptic Flow from a Transversally Thermalized Fireball

> The agreement of elliptic flow data at RHIC at central rapidity with the hydrodynamic model has led to the conclusion of very rapid thermalization. This conclusion is based on the intuitive argument that hydrodynamics, which assumes instantaneous local thermalization, produces the largest possible elliptic flow values and that the data seem to saturate this limit. We here investigate the question whether incompletely thermalized viscous systems may actually produce more elliptic flow than ideal hydrodynamics. Motivated by the extremely fast primordial longitudinal expansion of the reaction zone, we investigate a toy model which exhibits thermalization only in the transverse directions but undergoes collisionless free-streaming expansion in the longitudinal direction. For collisions at RHIC energies, elliptic flow results from the model are compared with those from hydrodynamics. With the final particle yield and $\kt$-distribution fixed, the transversally thermalized model is shown not to be able to produce the measured amount of elliptic flow. This investigation provides further support for very rapid local kinetic equilibration at RHIC. It also yields interesting novel results for the elliptic flow of massless particles such as direct photons.

## Key claim

The paper tests whether an incompletely thermalized viscous system can produce more elliptic flow than ideal hydrodynamics, which would undermine the argument that agreement between hydrodynamic predictions and RHIC data proves very rapid thermalization. The authors find that their transversally thermalized model (TTHM), which assumes thermalization only in transverse directions with collisionless free-streaming longitudinally, cannot reproduce the measured elliptic flow when final particle yield and transverse momentum distribution are fixed, thereby providing further support for rapid local kinetic equilibration at RHIC.

## Method

The authors construct a toy model (TTHM) of a gluon-dominated system at RHIC energies where transverse momenta are thermalized but longitudinal expansion proceeds via collisionless free-streaming, with longitudinal boost invariance assumed. They derive macroscopic evolution equations from the energy-momentum tensor using a phase-space distribution function that includes a delta function enforcing the free-streaming condition, then solve these equations numerically with initial conditions proportional to the binary nucleon collision density from a Woods-Saxon nuclear density distribution. Elliptic flow coefficients are computed via the Cooper-Frye freeze-out prescription along surfaces of constant energy density, and results are compared to ideal hydrodynamic model (HDM) simulations with either identical initial conditions or retuned initial conditions that match the same multiplicity density and spectral slopes.

## Key numbers

- For identical initial conditions with maximum initial energy density 23.0 GeV/fm^3 at initial time 0.6 fm/c, the TTHM generates larger integrated elliptic flow v_2 than hydrodynamics at both freeze-out energy densities tested (0.6 and 0.8 GeV/fm^3).
- At freeze-out energy density 0.6 GeV/fm^3 for central collisions, TTHM gives dN/dy = 780 and freeze-out time 6.95 fm/c, while HDM gives dN/dy = 944 and freeze-out time 5.97 fm/c.
- At freeze-out energy density 0.8 GeV/fm^3 for central collisions, TTHM gives dN/dy = 780 and freeze-out time 6.48 fm/c, while HDM gives dN/dy = 940 and freeze-out time 5.36 fm/c.
- The nuclear density uses a Woods-Saxon parametrization with R_0 = 1.14 A^(1/3) fm and xi = 0.54 fm, with inelastic nucleon-nucleon cross section sigma_0 = 40 mb.

## Context

This work extends the hydrodynamic modeling of elliptic flow at RHIC by Heinz and Kolb (2001) and Kolb, Heinz, Huovinen, and Ruuskanen (2001), which showed that RHIC data from STAR, PHENIX, and PHOBOS agree with hydrodynamic predictions and concluded that very rapid thermalization occurs. The paper directly tests the assumption that hydrodynamics produces the maximum possible elliptic flow, showing that while a transversally thermalized model with no longitudinal pressure can generate larger v_2 than hydrodynamics for identical initial conditions, it cannot match the data when final particle yields and spectral slopes are fixed. This enables stronger confidence in the rapid thermalization conclusion at RHIC and provides a framework for studying elliptic flow of massless particles such as direct photons.
