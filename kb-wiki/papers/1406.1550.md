# Beyond Nuclear Pasta: Phase Transitions and Neutrino Opacity of Non-Traditional Pasta

> In this work, we focus on different length scales within the dynamics of nucleons in conditions according to the neutron star crust, with a semiclassical molecular dynamics model, studying isospin symmetric matter at subsaturation densities. While varying the temperature, we find that a solid-liquid phase transition exists, that can be also characterized with a morphology transition. For higher temperatures, above this phase transition, we study the neutrino opacity, and find that in the liquid phase, the scattering of low momenta neutrinos remain high, even though the morphology of the structures differ significatively from those of the traditional nuclear pasta.

# Beyond Nuclear Pasta: Phase Transitions and Neutrino Opacity of Non-Traditional Pasta

## Key claim
Using semiclassical molecular dynamics simulations of isospin-symmetric matter at subsaturation densities, the authors find that a solid-liquid phase transition occurs at very low temperatures (0.35–0.65 MeV depending on density), and that above this transition, the liquid phase retains pasta-like structures that produce significant low-momentum neutrino scattering, even when the morphology differs from traditional nuclear pasta shapes.

## Method
The authors employed a classical molecular dynamics (CMD) model with Yukawa-type two-body potentials and screened Coulomb interactions (Thomas–Fermi approximation with screening length λ = 20 fm) to simulate 5000 nucleons (2500 protons, 2500 neutrons) in periodic cubic boxes at densities between 0.03 fm⁻³ and 0.13 fm⁻³. Systems were cooled from T = 0.8 MeV to T = 0.2 MeV using isothermal molecular dynamics with a Nosé–Hoover thermostat in small temperature steps (ΔT = 0.01 MeV). The solid-liquid phase transition was characterized via the Lindemann coefficient, caloric curves (energy vs. temperature), and Minkowski functionals (volume, surface area, Euler characteristic, and integral mean curvature). Neutrino opacity was assessed through the static structure factor S(k) at low momenta.

## Key numbers
- Equilibrium nuclear density: ρ₀ = 0.16 fm⁻³
- Binding energy: E(ρ₀) = 16 MeV/nucleon
- Compressibility: 250 MeV
- Interaction cutoff radius: r_c = 5.4 fm
- Screening length: λ = 20 fm
- System size: 5000 nucleons (2500 protons, 2500 neutrons)
- Density range studied: 0.03 fm⁻³ ≤ ρ ≤ 0.13 fm⁻³
- Temperature range: 0.2 MeV to 0.8 MeV
- Phase transition temperature range: T_l = 0.35 MeV to T_h = 0.65 MeV, depending on density
- Low-momentum peak in S(k) analyzed for k < 0.5 fm⁻¹ (wavelength λ ≳ 13 fm)
- Temperature steps: ΔT = 0.01 MeV

## Context
This work extends prior molecular dynamics studies of nuclear pasta by Dorso et al. (2012) and earlier mean-field and liquid-drop models by Ravenhall et al. (1983) and Hashimoto et al. (1984), which identified the traditional pasta phases (lasagna, spaghetti, gnocchi). The authors go beyond these traditional phases by demonstrating that at finite temperatures above the solid-liquid transition, non-traditional "sponge-like" structures can produce higher low-momentum neutrino scattering peaks than conventional pasta shapes. This finding suggests that the actual state of neutron star crust matter at low but finite temperatures should be described as an ensemble of multiple shapes rather than a single pasta structure, with implications for neutrino opacity calculations in supernova and neutron star cooling models. The work also connects to studies by Nakazato et al. (2009), who found gyroid and double-diamond structures using compressible liquid-drop models, and to Horowitz et al. (2004), who studied the static structure factor of nuclear pasta.
