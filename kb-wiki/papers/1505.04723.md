# Interpretations of Elastic Electron Scattering

> Elastic scattering of relativistic electrons from the nucleon yields Lorentz invariant form factors that describe the fundamental distribution of charge and magnetism. The spatial dependence of the nucleon's charge and magnetism is typically interpreted in the Breit reference frame which is related by a Lorentz boost from the laboratory frame, where the nucleon is at rest. We construct a toy model to estimate how the charge and magnetic radii of the nucleon are modified between the Breit and lab. frames. This has implications for the ratio of the proton electric to magnetic elastic form factors as a function of momentum transfer as well as for determinations of the proton charge radius. Predicted corrections based on the model are provided for the rms charge radii of the deuteron, the triton, and the helium isotopes.

# Interpretations of Elastic Electron Scattering

## Key claim

The authors construct a toy model to estimate corrections between the Breit and laboratory reference frames for elastic electron scattering from the nucleon, arguing that the commonly used momentum-space proton charge radius differs from the coordinate-space radius by a boost-dependent correction of order 0.033 fm², which reduces the electron-scattering determination of the proton charge radius toward the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift value by roughly half the original discrepancy.

## Method

The authors develop a non-relativistic harmonic oscillator shell model for a single nucleon confined in a deep potential, then establish a correspondence between the non-relativistic form factors and the relativistic Sachs form factors by introducing "de-relativized" expressions that incorporate the Lorentz boost factor γ = √(1+τ) between the Breit and laboratory frames. They expand these expressions at low momentum transfer to derive analytic corrections relating the relativistic (momentum-space) and non-relativistic (coordinate-space) definitions of the charge and magnetic root-mean-square radii for the proton and neutron, and extend the correction to few-body nuclei by scaling with the nuclear mass.

## Key numbers

- The boost correction to the squared charge radius for the proton is Δ_p = 3/(4m_p²) = 0.0332 fm²; for the neutron it is Δ_n = 3/(4m_n²) = 0.0331 fm²
- The corrected (coordinate-space) proton charge radius from electron scattering is r_{E,p}^{nr} = 0.860 ± 0.008 fm, compared to the uncorrected (momentum-space) value r_{E,p}^{rel} = 0.879 ± 0.008 fm from Bernauer (2010)
- The corrected proton magnetic radius is r_{M,p}^{nr} = 0.7981 ± 0.013 ± 0.010 fm, from the uncorrected value r_{M,p}^{rel} = 0.777 fm
- The corrected neutron magnetic radius is r_{M,n}^{nr} = 0.8810^{+0.009}_{-0.008} fm, from the uncorrected value r_{M,n}^{rel} = 0.862 fm
- The corrected neutron charge squared radius is [r_{E,n}^{nr}]² = -0.1492 ± 0.0022 fm², from the uncorrected value [r_{E,n}^{rel}]² = -0.1161 fm²
- The corrected deuteron charge radius from electron scattering is 2.12 ± 0.01 fm, compared to the uncorrected value 2.13 ± 0.01 fm and the muonic deuterium value 2.12562 ± 0.00078 fm
- The corrected triton charge radius is 1.751 ± 0.087 fm (uncorrected: 1.755 ± 0.087 fm)
- The corrected ³He charge radius is 1.955 ± 0.034 fm (uncorrected: 1.959 ± 0.034 fm)
- The corrected ⁴He charge radius is 1.678 ± 0.005 fm (uncorrected: 1.680 ± 0.005 fm)

## Context

This work extends earlier derivations of boost corrections for the charge distribution by Licht (1970), DeForest (1984), Robson (2013), and Giannini (2013), providing a more general model applied to both charge and magnetic form factors at the largest measured momentum transfers. The paper addresses the proton radius puzzle, where the electron scattering determination from Bernauer (2010) disagrees with the muonic hydrogen Lamb shift value from Pohl (2010) and Antognini (2013); the authors' correction reduces this discrepancy from over 4% to about 2%. The results enable predictions for ongoing muonic atom experiments on helium isotopes and suggest that a precision comparison of electron scattering from the proton versus the deuteron at low Q² should show a deviation at the 1.5% level.
