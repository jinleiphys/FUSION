# Production cross sections and angular distributions of neutron-rich rare isotopes from 15 MeV/nucleon Kr-induced collisions: toward the r-process path

> We present our recent study of cross sections and angular distributions of projectile fragments from heavy-ion reactions at beam energy of 15 MeV/nucleon. We studied the production cross sections and the angular distributions of neutron-rich nuclides from collisions of a 86 Kr (15 MeV/nucleon) beam with heavy targets ( 64 Ni, 124 Sn and 238 U). Experimental data from our previous work at Texas A & M were compared with model calculations. Our calculations were based on a two-step approach: the dynamical stage of the collision was described with, first, the phenomenological Deep-Inelastic Transfer model (DIT) and, alternatively, with the microscopic Constrained Molecular Dynamics model (CoMD). The de-excitation of the hot heavy projectile fragments was performed with the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM). An overall good discription of the available data was obtained with the models employed. Furthermore, we performed calculations with a radioactive beam of 92 Kr (15 MeV/nucleon) interacting with a target of 238 U. We observed that the multinucleon transfer mechanism leads to extremely neutron-rich nuclides toward and beyond the astrophysical r-process path.

## Key claim

Multinucleon transfer reactions at 15 MeV/nucleon, particularly with a radioactive beam of 92Kr on a 238U target, can produce extremely neutron-rich nuclides that reach and even exceed the astrophysical r-process path near Z=30-36, though the calculations for the 86Kr+238U system lack experimental validation and the angular distribution comparisons show several discrepancies that require further investigation.

## Method

The authors compared previously published experimental data from 15 MeV/nucleon 86Kr beams on 64Ni, 124Sn, and 238U targets with a two-step theoretical approach: the dynamical collision stage was modeled using either the phenomenological Deep-Inelastic Transfer (DIT) model or the microscopic Constrained Molecular Dynamics (CoMD) model, followed by de-excitation of hot projectile fragments using the Statistical Multifragmentation Model (SMM). For the CoMD calculations, three variants were employed: standard parameters, a version with phenomenological n-n and p-p pairing, and a version with increased nuclear matter compressibility K=272 MeV (versus the standard K=200 MeV). The authors also performed DIT/SMM calculations for a radioactive 92Kr beam (15 MeV/nucleon) on a 238U target.

## Key numbers

- Beam energy: 15 MeV/nucleon for all reactions studied
- Grazing angle for 86Kr+124Sn system: approximately 9.0 degrees
- Grazing angle for 86Kr+64Ni system: approximately 6.0 degrees
- Experimental angular settings: 4.0 degrees and 7.5 degrees, covering polar angular ranges of Delta-theta=2.2-5.8 degrees and Delta-theta=5.6-9.2 degrees, respectively
- For the 92Kr+238U reaction, calculated cross section for 93Kr (-0p+1n): 12 mb, with estimated rate 3.6x10^3 per second assuming 1.0 pnA beam (6.2x10^9 particles/s) on 20 mg/cm^2 238U target
- For 92Kr+238U, calculated cross section for 96Kr (-0p+4n): 0.05 mb, with estimated rate 15 per second
- For 92Kr+238U, calculated cross section for 96Br (-1p+5n): 0.008 mb, with estimated rate 2 per second
- For 92Kr+238U, calculated cross section for 93Se (-2p+3n): 0.02 mb, with estimated rate 6 per second
- For bromine (Z=35) from 92Kr+238U, isotopes with up to 15 more neutrons (A=96) than the stable isotope (A=81) can be obtained

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous experimental and theoretical studies of projectile fragments from 25 MeV/nucleon 86Kr reactions on 64Ni and 124Sn targets (Souliotis et al., 2002, 2003) and their 15 MeV/nucleon studies (Souliotis et al., 2011; Fountas et al., 2014; Vonta et al., 2016; Papageorgiou et al., 2018). The multinucleon transfer mechanism at Fermi energies (15-35 MeV/nucleon) is presented as an alternative to traditional production routes such as spallation, fission, and high-energy projectile fragmentation (above 100 MeV/nucleon) for reaching neutron-rich nuclides toward the r-process path. The results enable predictions for production rates at upcoming rare-isotope facilities including the RISP facility in Korea with the KOBRA spectrometer, the Cyclotron Institute at Texas A&M with the MARS separator, and LNS/Catania with the MAGNEX spectrometer.
