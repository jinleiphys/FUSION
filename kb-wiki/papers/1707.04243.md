# Radioactive Decay Seen as Temporal Canonical Ensemble

## Key claim
Radioactive decay can be reinterpreted as a temporal canonical ensemble, where the exponential decay law becomes the Boltzmann distribution and the radioactive constant appears as the analog of absolute temperature multiplied by Boltzmann constant. The author claims this approach explains the stochastic character of decay and why decay is characterized by a constant rather than a variable parameter, but the analogy is limited because changing the "persistence" (the temporal analog of temperature) is hard to manage due to the impossibility of exceeding the speed of light.

## Method
The author applies the operator of time formalism, introducing a separate Hilbert space for time and energy operators (analogous to the standard treatment of coordinate and momentum), with commutation relations imposed between time and energy. A "second Schrodinger equation" is introduced where the operator of time and its dynamical counterpart G(q,p) equally act on states, analogous to the original Schrodinger equation relating energy and the Hamiltonian. The statistical operator for the temporal canonical ensemble is then constructed by replacing energy eigenvalues with time eigenvalues and the Boltzmann factor beta with the radioactive constant lambda.

## Key numbers
- The exponential decay law is given as N(t_i) = N_0 e^{-lambda t_i}, where lambda is the radioactive constant characteristic for the decaying system.
- The probability that a system will last until time t_i is given by e^{-lambda t_i} / sum_i e^{-lambda t_i}.
- For a harmonic oscillator example, the eigenvalues of G are t_n = (hbar^2 omega / m^2 c^4)(n + 1/2), with d = hbar^2 omega / m^2 c^4.
- The relation between lambda and the mean value of G for the harmonic oscillator example is given as: <G> = (1/2) coth(lambda d / 2).

## Context
This work extends the author's prior formalism of the operator of time (Prvanovic, 2015-2017) to radioactive decay, building on approaches similar to those in references [10] and [11] that treat time and energy as coordinate and momentum are treated. The paper offers a new perspective on why radioactive decay appears stochastic: it is not essentially different from any other mixed state regarding randomness, since we do not know which temporal eigenstate a particular system occupies. The formalism enables a connection between the radioactive constant and the mean value of the dynamical time operator G, potentially allowing measurement of G through half-life determination.
