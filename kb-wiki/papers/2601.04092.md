# Scattering phase shift in quantum mechanics on quantum computers

> We investigate the feasibility of extracting infinite volume scattering phase shift on quantum computers in a simple one-dimensional quantum mechanical model, using the formalism established in Ref.~\cite{Guo:2023ecc} that relates the integrated correlation functions (ICF) for a trapped system to the infinite volume scattering phase shifts through a weighted integral. The system is first discretized in a finite box with periodic boundary conditions, and the formalism in real time is verified by employing a contact interaction potential with exact solutions. Quantum circuits are then designed and constructed to implement the formalism on current quantum computing architectures. To overcome the fast oscillatory behavior of the integrated correlation functions in real-time simulation, different methods of post-data analysis are proposed and discussed. Test results on IBM hardware show that good agreement can be achieved with two qubits, but complete failure ensues with three qubits due to two-qubit gate operation errors and thermal relaxation errors.

## Key claim
The paper demonstrates that extracting infinite-volume scattering phase shifts via the integrated correlation function (ICF) formalism is feasible on current quantum computers for a simple one-dimensional quantum mechanical model, but only up to two qubits; with three qubits, two-qubit gate operation errors and thermal relaxation errors cause complete failure.

## Method
The authors discretize a one-dimensional quantum mechanical system in a finite periodic box and compute the integrated correlation function C(t) = Tr[e^{-iHt}] for both interacting and non-interacting Hamiltonians with a contact interaction potential V(x) = V0 δ(x). They map the Hamiltonian matrices onto quantum circuits using Trotterization for time evolution and the Hadamard test to extract the real and imaginary parts of the correlation functions. To handle the fast oscillatory behavior of the real-time correlation functions, they propose two post-data analysis methods: an E+iε prescription and an L→iL rotation, both of which regularize the pole structure of the integral-transformed correlation functions.

## Key numbers
- For single-qubit tests on IBM QPUs with L=8, m=1, V0=2, δt=0.04, and 1000 shots, the real and imaginary parts of ΔC(t) = C(t) - C0(t) show good agreement with exact results.
- With two qubits, good agreement can be achieved on IBM hardware.
- With three qubits, complete failure ensues due to two-qubit gate operation errors and thermal relaxation errors.
- The analytic infinite-volume limit of the ICF relation is given by C(t) - C0(t) → (1/2) erfc(m V0 √(it/2m)) e^( (m V0)² it/2m ) - 1/2, valid for both repulsive and attractive potentials.
- For the E+iε prescription verification, L=100, ε=0.1, V0=2, m=1, N=4000, and a≈0.025 were used, with agreement up to approximately E=0.5 before significant deterioration.

## Context
This work extends the ICF formalism established by Guo (2023) to quantum computing architectures, building on prior finite-volume approaches such as the Lüscher method (Lüscher, 1990) and the HALQCD interaction potential method (Ishii, 2007). It enables future quantum simulations of scattering in more realistic scenarios, including scalar field theory models, and addresses the challenge of real-time simulation on noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices. The paper also relates to recent work on quantum simulation of reaction processes, including the combined Variational Quantum Eigensolver and Lüscher-like formula approach (2024) and wave packet time delay methods (2021).
