# Validity of Brink Axel Hypothesis for calculations of allowed stellar weak rates of heavy nuclei

> The knowledge of beta decay transitional probabilities and GamowTeller (GT) strength functions from highly excited states of nuclides is of particular importance for applications to astrophysical network calculations of nucleosynthesis in explosive stellar events. These quantities are challenging to achieve from measurements or computations using various nuclear models. Due to unavailability of feasible alternatives, many theoretical studies often rely on the Brink Axel (BA) hypothesis, that is, the response of strength functions depends merely on the transition energy of the parent nuclear ground state and is independent of the underlying details of the parent state, for the calculation of stellar rates. BA hypothesis has been used in many applications from nuclear structure determination to nucleosynthesis yield in the astrophysical matter.

## Key claim

The Brink-Axel (BA) hypothesis, which assumes that Gamow-Teller (GT) strength functions depend only on transition energy and not on the details of the parent state, does not yield reliable calculations of stellar weak rates for fp- and fpg-shell nuclides under the extreme temperature-density conditions characteristic of presupernova and supernova evolution; deviations between BA-based rates and state-by-state microscopic calculations become significant as early as the neon burning phase, with beta-decay rates differing by up to three orders of magnitude.

## Method

The authors employed the fully microscopic proton-neutron quasi-particle random-phase approximation (pn-QRPA) model to compute GT strength functions and stellar beta-decay and electron capture weak rates for the top 80 fp- and fpg-shell nuclides relevant to post-silicon burning phases of pre-supernova stars. The model uses a large model space of 7 major oscillatory shells, calculates state-by-state rates in a microscopic fashion covering roughly 15 MeV excitation energy in parent and daughter nuclei, and incorporates finite-temperature effects in phase-space calculations via Boltzmann population of parent excited states, though nuclear matrix elements are computed at zero temperature. Calculations were performed across a density range of rho*Y_e = (10 - 10^11) g/cm^3 and temperature range of T = (1-30) GK relevant to pre-collapse astrophysical environments.

## Key numbers

- Beta-decay rates calculated using the BA hypothesis deviate from state-by-state microscopic calculations by up to three orders of magnitude for the studied fp- and fpg-shell nuclides.
- The density grid spans rho*Y_e = (10 - 10^11) g/cm^3 and the temperature grid spans T = (1-30) GK, covering pre-collapse astrophysical conditions.
- The deviation between BA-based and microscopic calculations becomes significant as early as the neon burning phase of massive stars.

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous study on the validity of the BA hypothesis for sd-shell nuclides (Nabi, 2022) to heavier fp- and fpg-shell nuclides. It contradicts the generalized BA hypothesis adopted by Fuller, Fowler, and Newman (1982) and widely used in subsequent weak rate calculations (Aufderheide, 1994; Langanke, 2000; and others), and aligns with earlier pn-QRPA studies (Nabi, 2004, 2007, 2009, 2012, 2019) that found orders-of-magnitude differences between BA-based and microscopic rates. The results also support findings from configuration-interaction shell model studies (Johnson, 2015; Lu, 2018) that reported violation of the BA hypothesis for GT strengths, and from finite-temperature QRPA studies (Dzhioev, 2010; Yüksel, 2020) showing that GT strengths evolve with temperature. The work enables improved stellar weak rate inputs for astrophysical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and nucleosynthesis.
