# Tsunamis, Viscosity and the HBT Puzzle

> The equation of state and bulk and shear viscosities are shown to be able to affect the transverse dynamics of a central heavy ion collision. The net entropy, along with the femtoscopic radii are shown to be affected at the 10-20% level by both shear and bulk viscosity. The degree to which these effects help build a tsunami-like pulse is also discussed.

# Tsunamis, Viscosity and the HBT Puzzle

## Key claim
Viscous effects (both shear and bulk) can change the shape of the femtoscopic source function at the 10-20% level, in a direction that may make hydrodynamic models more consistent with experimental HBT data, but quantitative conclusions are precluded by the crude treatment of the breakup stage.

## Method
A simple hydrodynamic model assuming cylindrical symmetry and boost invariance (reducing the problem to one dimension) was solved numerically using a co-moving mesh with Israel-Stewart viscous hydrodynamics. Calculations began at proper time 0.25 fm/c with an energy density profile adjusted to maintain fixed transverse energy per unit rapidity; particle emission was calculated with Cooper-Frye criteria, and r.m.s. radii were generated from direct pions (excluding decay products) rather than full correlation functions.

## Key numbers
- Entropy per unit rapidity extracted from 130A GeV Au+Au data: dS/dy = 4450 ± 10%
- Lattice results are consistent with this extraction if average energy density at τ = 1 fm/c is in the neighborhood of 8 GeV/fm³
- Shear viscosity was varied with values 4πη/s = 0, 1, 2; higher shear viscosity led to lower entropy densities and lower Rout/Rside ratios
- Bulk viscosity was set to maximum values of Bmax/s = 2/4π or 5/4π within the mixed-phase region
- Calculations used a breakup hypersurface defined by energy density above 50 MeV/fm³
- Initial proper time for calculations: 0.25 fm/c
- Speed of sound in the high-temperature phase was set to c_s² = 0.3

## Context
This work addresses the "HBT puzzle", the failure of hydrodynamic models with soft equations of state to describe two-particle correlation measurements, while microscopic models with stiffer equations of state come closer but still have difficulty at the 10-20% level. The paper extends prior work on viscous hydrodynamics by Teaney, Heinz, and Romatschke (mid-2000s), and on supercooling by Csörgő and Csernai (2000s), surface emission by Heiselberg (1990s), and mean-field refraction by Cramer and Miller (2000s). The results suggest that bulk viscosity near the phase transition temperature may simultaneously accommodate larger entropy densities and smaller Rout/Rside ratios, motivating more precise extraction of bulk viscosity from lattice data as proposed by Kharzeev (2006).
