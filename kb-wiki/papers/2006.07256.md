# Improved method for the experimental determination of in-medium effects from heavy-ion collisions

> The equation of state with light clusters for nuclear and stellar matter is determined using chemical equilibrium constants evaluated from the analysis of the recently published (Xe$+$Sn) heavy ion data, corresponding to three reactions with different isotopic contents of the emission source. The measured multiplicities are used to extract the thermodynamic properties, and an in-medium correction to the ideal gas internal partition function of the clusters is included in the analysis. This in-medium correction and its respective uncertainty are calculated via a Bayesian analysis, with the unique hypothesis that the different nuclear species in a given sample must correspond to a unique common value for the density of the expanding source. Different parameter sets for the correction are tested, and the effect of the radius of the clusters on the thermodynamics and on the chemical equilibrium constants is also addressed. It is shown that the equilibrium constants obtained are almost independent of the isospin content of the analysed systems. Finally, a comparison with a relativistic mean field model proves that data are consistent with a universal in-medium correction of the scalar $σ$-meson coupling for nucleons bound in clusters. The obtained value, $g_s/g_s^0 = 0.92 \pm 0.02$, is larger than that obtained in a previous study not including in-medium effects in the data analysis. This result implies a smaller effect on the binding energy of the clusters and, as a consequence, larger melting densities, and an increased cluster contribution in supernova matter.

## Key claim

The paper demonstrates that including an in-medium correction to the ideal gas internal partition function of clusters, determined via Bayesian analysis, resolves the inconsistency in volume estimations from different nuclear species and yields chemical equilibrium constants that are almost independent of the isospin content of the reaction systems, with the extracted scalar sigma-meson coupling reduction factor \(g_s/g_s^0 = 0.92 \pm 0.02\) being larger than previous estimates that neglected in-medium effects.

## Method

The authors analyzed central Xe+Sn collisions at 32 MeV/nucleon measured by the INDRA apparatus, sorting detected particles by Coulomb-corrected surface velocity to follow the time evolution of the expanding intermediate-velocity source. They introduced a Boltzmann-factor correction \(C_{AZ} = \exp[-\Delta_{AZ} / T(A-1)]\) to the ideal gas expression for the free volume, with \(\Delta_{AZ}\) parameterized as a function of cluster mass number \(A\) and isospin \(I\), and determined the correction parameters via Bayesian inference imposing that different cluster species yield a common source volume. The corrected free volumes were then used to compute baryon densities and chemical equilibrium constants for each velocity bin.

## Key numbers

- Scalar sigma-meson coupling reduction factor: \(g_s/g_s^0 = 0.92 \pm 0.02\), obtained from comparison with a relativistic mean field model
- Bombarding energy: 32 MeV/nucleon for all three Xe+Sn reactions
- Velocity bin width: \(\Delta v_{surf} = 0.2\) cm/ns used for sorting particle multiplicities
- Coulomb correction energy: \(E_C = 10\) MeV applied to particle spectra
- Prior parameter ranges for the correction: \(0 \le a_1 \le 15\) MeV, \(0 \le a_3 \le a_1\) MeV, \(-1 \le a_2 \le 1\), \(0 \le a_4 \le 4\)
- Temperature extracted from double isotope ratio formula using yields of \(^2\)H, \(^3\)H, \(^3\)He, and \(^4\)He

## Context

This work extends the analysis method of Bougault et al. (2019) and the NIMROD collaboration (Qin et al., 2012) by explicitly incorporating in-medium effects that were previously neglected in the ideal gas assumption used to extract source volumes from heavy-ion collision data. The results enable improved constraints on theoretical models of cluster formation in dense matter, such as the relativistic mean field model of Pais et al. (2017), and have implications for the equation of state used in supernova and neutron star merger simulations where cluster melting densities and binding energy shifts affect neutrino transport and nucleosynthesis conditions.
