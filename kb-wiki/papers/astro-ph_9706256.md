# Selected Topics in Neutrino Astrophysics

> This review covers a subset of the astrophysical phenomena where neutrinos play a significant role and where the underlying microphysics is nuclear physics. The current status of the solar neutrino problem, atmospheric neutrino experiments, the role of neutrinos in determining the dynamics of type-II supernovae, and recent developments in exploring neutrino propagation in stochastic media are reviewed.

# Selected Topics in Neutrino Astrophysics

## Key claim

This review argues that the observed deficits in solar and atmospheric neutrino fluxes relative to standard model predictions are most plausibly explained by new neutrino physics (nonzero masses and mixing) rather than astrophysical modifications, with the MSW matter-enhanced oscillation mechanism currently the "most-favored solution" to the solar neutrino problem, though the author notes that alternative astrophysical solutions (such as slow core mixing proposed by Cumming and Haxton) are not completely eliminated, only "severely limited" by model-independent flux constraints.

## Method

The paper reviews the theoretical framework for neutrino vacuum oscillations and matter-enhanced (MSW) oscillations, then compares standard solar model predictions (Bahcall-Pinsonneault SSM1 and Turck-Chièze SSM2) against experimental data from the Homestake chlorine detector, Kamiokande and Super-Kamiokande water Cerenkov detectors, and the GALLEX and SAGE gallium detectors. It also surveys atmospheric neutrino measurements from Kamioka, Super-Kamiokande, IMB, Soudan 2, Nusex, Frejus, and Baksan, presenting the ratio of muon-to-electron neutrino events (the "ratio of ratios" R) as the key observable. The review further discusses neutrino propagation in supernova environments and stochastic media, and describes calibration experiments using artificial ⁵¹Cr neutrino sources to validate the radiochemical detection method.

## Context

This review synthesizes the state of neutrino astrophysics as of mid-1997, extending the earlier standard solar model calculations of Bahcall and Pinsonneault and the MSW mechanism originally developed by Wolfenstein (1978) and Mikheyev and Smirnov (1985). It enables future experimental tests of the oscillation hypothesis through the upcoming Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO), which will measure both charged-current and neutral-current reactions on deuterium to detect flavor conversion, and BOREXINO, which will measure the ⁷Be neutrino flux with a 250 keV threshold. The review also notes that helioseismological data from the GONG network and the SOHO satellite's SOI/MDI project now provide independent constraints on solar structure that rule out simple temperature-reduction astrophysical solutions, while the Cumming and Haxton (1996) slow-mixing model remains to be tested against these seismic data.
