# Using Light Charged Particles to Probe the Asymmetry Dependence of the Nuclear Caloric Curve

> Recently, we observed a clear dependence of the nuclear caloric curve on neutron-proton asymmetry $\frac{N-Z}{A}$ through examination of fully reconstructed equilibrated quasi-projectile sources produced in heavy ion collisions at E/A = 35 MeV. In the present work, we extend our analysis using multiple light charged particle probes of the temperature. Temperatures are extracted with five distinct probes using a kinetic thermometer approach. Additionally, temperatures are extracted using two probes within a chemical thermometer approach (Albergo method). All seven measurements show a significant linear dependence of the source temperature on the source asymmetry. For the kinetic thermometer, the strength of the asymmetry dependence varies with the probe particle species in a way which is consistent with an average emission-time ordering.

# Using Light Charged Particles to Probe the Asymmetry Dependence of the Nuclear Caloric Curve

## Key claim

All seven temperature measurements (five kinetic thermometers using protons, deuterons, tritons, helions, and alpha particles; two chemical thermometers using H/He and Li/He yield ratios) show a significant linear dependence of the quasi-projectile source temperature on the source neutron-proton asymmetry, with the strength of the asymmetry dependence varying by probe particle species in a way consistent with average emission-time ordering. The true dependence may be even greater than observed because the method correlates the initial QP composition with temperatures probed by particles emitted over a range of times, rather than the composition at the instant of emission.

## Method

Quasi-projectile sources were reconstructed from collisions of 70Zn+70Zn, 64Zn+64Zn, and 64Ni+64Ni at E/A = 35 MeV measured in the NIMROD-ISiS 4pi detector array, with events selected for mass (48 ≤ A ≤ 52), spherical shape, and velocity cuts to isolate equilibrated sources. Temperatures were extracted using the momentum quadrupole fluctuation (MQF) kinetic thermometer with five separate light charged particle probes (p, d, t, 3He, alpha), and using the Albergo chemical thermometer with two isotopic yield ratio combinations (d/t over h/alpha and 6Li/7Li over h/alpha), with secondary decay corrections applied.

## Key numbers

- At E*/A = 2.5 MeV, MQF temperatures range between 5.5 and 9.5 MeV across probe species; by E*/A = 8.5 MeV, temperatures range between 10.5 and 15.5 MeV
- An increase of 0.15 units in source asymmetry ms corresponds to a decrease in MQF temperature by about 1.3 MeV
- Slopes of linear fits for ΔT/Δms: -7.3 ± 0.2 (protons), -9.2 ± 0.3 (deuterons), -9.4 ± 0.3 (tritons), -10.6 ± 0.7 (helions), and -5.5 ± 0.1 (alpha particles) MeV per unit change in ms
- Slopes for Albergo thermometers: -1.01 ± 0.07 (H/He) and -0.94 ± 0.15 (Li/He) MeV per unit change in ms
- Albergo temperatures rise from about 3 MeV at E*/A = 2 MeV to about 4 MeV at E*/A = 8 MeV
- An increase of 0.15 units in ms corresponds to a decrease in Albergo temperature on the order of 0.15 MeV
- The five asymmetry bins have average ms values of 0.0640, 0.0988, 0.1370, 0.1758, and 0.2145
- Uncertainty in free neutron multiplicity due to detector efficiency (≈70%) increases the width of the neutron distribution by at most 9% at highest excitation energies; background contributes less than 6% additional width

## Context

This work extends the authors' previous observation (McIntosh 2013) of asymmetry-dependent temperatures using proton probes, now demonstrating the effect across multiple light charged particle species and two thermometer types. Previous experimental efforts by Sfienti (2009) and Trautmann (2008) showed only small asymmetry dependence, while Wuenschel (2010, 2009) found no discernible dependence, likely because those studies did not accurately select the asymmetry of the reconstructed quasi-projectile source. Theoretical predictions are conflicting: the Thomas-Fermi model (Kolomietz 2001) and mononucleus model (Hoel 2007) predict higher critical temperatures for neutron-poor systems, while the hot liquid drop model (Besprosvany 1989), statistical multifragmentation model (Ogul 2002), and isospin-dependent quantum molecular dynamics (Su 2011) predict higher temperatures for neutron-rich systems. The results enable refined interpretation of isotopic fragment yield data (e.g., isoscaling) that previously assumed temperature independence from asymmetry, and may offer a new avenue to constrain the asymmetry energy in the nuclear equation of state.
