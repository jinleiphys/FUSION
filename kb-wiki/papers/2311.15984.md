# Extraction of the microscopic properties of quasi-particles using deep neural networks

> We use deep neural networks (DNN) to obtain the microscopic characteristics of partons in terms of dynamical degrees of freedom on the basis of an off-shell quasiparticle description. We aim to infer masses and widths of quasi-gluons, up/down, and strange quarks using constraints on the macroscopic thermodynamic observables obtained by the first-principles calculations lattice QCD. In this work, we use 3 independent dimensionless thermodynamic observables from lQCD for minimization. First, we train our DNN using the DQPM (Dynamical QuasiParticle Model) Ansatz for the masses and widths. Furthermore, we use the DNN capabilities to generalize this Ansatz, to evaluate which quasiparticle characteristics are desirable to describe different thermodynamic functions simultaneously. To evaluate consistently the microscopic properties obtained by the DNN in the case of off-shell quarks and gluons, we compute transport coefficients using the spectral function within Kubo-Zubarev formalism in different setups. In particular, we make a comprehensive comparison in the case of the dimensionless ratios of shear viscosity over entropy density $η/s$ and electric conductivity over temperature $σ_Q/T$, which provide additional constraints for the parameter generalization of the considered models.

## Key claim

The authors use deep neural networks (DNNs) to infer the temperature-dependent masses and widths of quasi-gluons, up/down quarks, and strange quarks from lattice QCD thermodynamic data, but the extracted microscopic properties are not unique: different parameter sets that reproduce the same equation of state can yield significantly different transport coefficients, meaning the DNN method identifies a family of possible quasiparticle descriptions rather than a single definitive one.

## Method

The authors train feed-forward neural networks (FFNNs) with three hidden layers (24, 12, 12 neurons, sigmoid activation) on three dimensionless thermodynamic observables from lattice QCD: entropy density s/T^3, baryon susceptibility χ_2^B, and strange susceptibility χ_2^S, all at vanishing chemical potential. They first train the DNN to extract the effective coupling g^2(T) using the DQPM parametrization for masses and widths, then generalize the DNN to directly output masses m_i/T and widths γ_i/T for each parton species. To speed up training, they employ separate surrogate neural networks that approximate the thermodynamic integrals relating microscopic properties to macroscopic observables. Transport coefficients (shear viscosity η/s and electric conductivity σ_Q/T) are computed from the extracted spectral functions using the Kubo-Zubarev formalism.

## Key numbers

* The DNN uses 3 independent dimensionless thermodynamic observables from lattice QCD (s/T^3, χ_2^B, χ_2^S) for minimization, with the critical temperature T_c = 0.158 GeV taken from the WB Collaboration data.
* The DNN architecture has 3 hidden layers with 24, 12, and 12 neurons respectively, trained for 4 × 10^3 epochs with an initial learning rate of 0.005 decaying by 90% every 1000 epochs.
* The strange quark mass is given by m_s(T) = m_l(T) + Δm_ls, where Δm_ls = 0.03 GeV is a constant mass shift.
* The DQPM coupling constant parametrization uses parameters d = 169.934, e = -0.178434, and f = 1.14631, fixed to reproduce the entropy density s(T) from the WB Collaboration.
* The thermal width parametrization uses c_m = 14.4, a constant related to a magnetic cut-off.

## Context

This work extends the Dynamical Quasi-Particle Model (DQPM) framework developed by Peshier (2005), Cassing (2007), and later works by Berrehrah (2016), Moreau (2019), and Soloveva (2020), which describes the quark-gluon plasma using off-shell quasiparticles with complex self-energies. The DNN approach generalizes the DQPM Ansatz by relaxing the assumption that all quark widths are equal and that masses follow the Hard Thermal Loop (HTL) scaling, enabling a systematic search for quasiparticle properties consistent with lattice QCD thermodynamics from the WB Collaboration (Borsanyi 2013, 2022). The method enables future application at finite baryon chemical potential and provides a framework for testing whether different microscopic descriptions that yield identical thermodynamics can be distinguished by their transport coefficients, building on the authors' previous comparison (Soloveva 2021) showing that different models with nearly identical equations of state can produce markedly different transport coefficients.
