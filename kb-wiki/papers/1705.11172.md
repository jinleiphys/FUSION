# Effect of Pauli repulsion and transfer on fusion

> The effect of the Pauli exclusion principle on the nucleus-nucleus bare potential is studied using a new density-constrained extension of the Frozen-Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) technique. The resulting potentials exhibit a repulsion at short distance. The charge product dependence of this Pauli repulsion is investigated. Dynamical effects are then included in the potential with the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DCTDHF) method. In particular, isovector contributions to this potential are used to investigate the role of transfer on fusion, resulting in a lowering of the inner part of the potential for systems with positive Q-value transfer channels.

## Key claim

The Pauli exclusion principle between nucleons of different collision partners produces a repulsive contribution to the nucleus-nucleus bare potential at short distances, which widens the fusion barrier and can contribute to deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance; however, this effect provides only part of the solution, as discrepancies with experimental data remain.

## Method

The authors introduce a density-constrained extension of the Frozen-Hartree-Fock (DCFHF) technique, which constrains the total one-body density to the sum of the ground-state densities of the two nuclei at a given separation R, while enforcing full antisymmetrization of the many-body wavefunction. Dynamical effects are then incorporated using the density-constrained time-dependent Hartree-Fock (DCTDHF) method, where densities from a TDHF collision above the barrier are used as constraints. The isoscalar and isovector contributions to the DCTDHF potential are separated to isolate the effect of transfer on the inner part of the potential.

## Key numbers

- In the DCFHF potential for 16O+16O, the Pauli repulsion becomes significant only inside the barrier, increasing the barrier width compared to the frozen Hartree-Fock (FHF) potential.
- For 48Ca+208Pb, the DCFHF calculation predicts that fusion is impossible at 3% below the barrier, with the pocket almost disappearing.
- The DCTDHF potential for 16O+208Pb at Ec.m. = 75 MeV shows a large reduction of the potential inside the barrier due to isovector transfer, opposite to the Pauli repulsion.
- For 40Ca+132Sn at Ec.m. = 120 MeV, isovector reduction of the potential is observed, while for 48Ca+132Sn at the same energy, no such reduction occurs because the system has no positive Q-value transfer channels.

## Context

This work extends the frozen Hartree-Fock approach of Brueckner (1968) and the density-constrained TDHF method of Umar et al. (2006) to include exact Pauli repulsion via the new DCFHF technique. It addresses the deep sub-barrier fusion hindrance puzzle observed experimentally by Jiang et al. (2002), Dasgupta et al. (2007), and Stefanini et al. (2010), and complements other proposed mechanisms such as the incompressibility effect of Misicu and Esbensen (2006), the sudden-to-adiabatic transition of Ichikawa et al. (2009), and the decoherence mechanism of Dasgupta et al. (2007). The isovector analysis provides a microscopic explanation for why standard coupled-channel calculations neglecting transfer reproduce fusion in 48Ca+132Sn but underpredict it in 40Ca+132Sn, as observed by Kolata et al. (2012).
