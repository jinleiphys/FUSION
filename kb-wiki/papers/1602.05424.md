# Weak and strong coupling limits of the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation

> We consider a momentum dependent relaxation time for the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation time approximation. We employ a power law parametrization for the momentum dependence of the relaxation time, and calculate the shear and bulk viscosity, as well as, the charge and heat conductivity. We show, that for the two popular parametrizations, referred to as the linear and quadratic ansatz, one can obtain transport coefficients which corresponds to the weak and strong coupling regimes, respectively. We also show that, for a system of massless particles with vanishing chemical potential, the off-equilibrium corrections to the phase-space distribution function calculated with the quadratic ansatz are identical with those of the Grad's 14-moment method.

## Key claim

The paper shows that a momentum-dependent relaxation time in the Boltzmann equation, parametrized as a power law in particle energy, can reproduce transport coefficients corresponding to either weak or strong coupling regimes depending on the exponent; specifically, the linear ansatz (exponent a=0) yields results matching the Chapman-Enskog method and weak coupling, while the quadratic ansatz (a=1) reproduces Grad's 14-moment method and strong coupling, but this correspondence is demonstrated only for a system of massless particles with vanishing chemical potential.

## Method

The authors solve the Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation using an iterative Chapman-Enskog expansion to first order in gradients, with a power-law parametrization of the relaxation time tau_R = tau_0 (u dot p / T)^a. They consider two limiting cases: a=0 (linear ansatz) and a=1 (quadratic ansatz), and compute the shear viscosity, bulk viscosity, charge conductivity, and heat conductivity for a Boltzmann gas of a single particle species. The calculations are performed separately for three scenarios: massless particles with zero chemical potential, massive particles with zero chemical potential, and massless particles with nonzero chemical potential.

## Key numbers

- For the linear ansatz (a=0) with m=0, mu=0: shear viscosity coefficient beta_pi = (1/5)(epsilon+P), giving eta = tau_0 * (1/5)(epsilon+P)
- For the quadratic ansatz (a=1) with m=0, mu=0: shear viscosity coefficient beta_pi = epsilon+P, giving eta = tau_0 * (epsilon+P)
- Ratio of bulk to shear viscosity for linear ansatz (small-z expansion to O(z^4)): zeta/eta = 75*(1/3 - c_s^2)^2
- Ratio of bulk to shear viscosity for quadratic ansatz (exact): zeta/eta = 2*(1/3 - c_s^2)
- Ratio of heat conductivity to shear viscosity for linear ansatz in small alpha limit: kappa_q/eta = (20/3)*(T/mu^2)
- Ratio of heat conductivity to shear viscosity for quadratic ansatz in small alpha limit: kappa_q/eta = 4*(T/mu^2)

## Context

This work extends the Anderson-Witting relaxation-time model (Anderson and Witting, 1974) by introducing a momentum-dependent relaxation time, building on earlier work by Dusling and collaborators (2009) who noted that different microscopic theories yield different momentum dependencies. The results connect two previously distinct approaches: the iterative Chapman-Enskog method and Grad's 14-moment method, showing they correspond to different choices of the relaxation-time momentum dependence. The paper enables future work extending these results to second-order dissipative hydrodynamics and to more complex systems beyond a single-species Boltzmann gas.
