# Validity of the distorted-wave impulse-approximation description of ${}^{40}$Ca$(e,e'p)^{39}$K data using only ingredients from a nonlocal dispersive optical model

> The nonlocal implementation of the dispersive optical model (DOM) provides all the ingredients for distorted-wave impulse-approximation (DWIA) calculations of the $(e,e'p)$ reaction. It provides both the overlap function, including its normalization, and the outgoing proton distorted wave. This framework is applied to describe the knockout of a proton from the $0\textrm{d}\frac{3}{2}$ and $1\textrm{s}\frac{1}{2}$ orbitals in ${}^{40}$Ca with fixed normalizations of 0.71 and 0.60, respectively. Data were obtained in parallel kinematics for three outgoing proton energies: 70, 100, and 135 MeV. Agreement with the data is as good as, or better than, previous descriptions employing local optical potentials and overlap functions from Woods-Saxon potentials - both with standard nonlocality corrections - whose normalization (spectroscopic factor) and radius were fitted to the data. The present analysis suggests that slightly larger spectroscopic factors are obtained when nonlocal optical potentials are employed than those generated with local potentials. The results further suggest that the chosen kinematical window around 100 MeV proton energy provides the best and cleanest method to employ the DWIA for the analysis of this reaction. The conclusion that substantial ground-state correlations cannot be ignored when describing a closed-shell atomic nucleus is therefore confirmed in detail. To reach these conclusions, it is essential to have a complete description of the nucleon single-particle propagator that accounts for all elastic nucleon-scattering observables in a wide energy domain up to 200 MeV. The current nonlocal implementation of the DOM fulfills this requirement.

## Key claim

The nonlocal dispersive optical model (DOM) provides all ingredients for distorted-wave impulse-approximation (DWIA) calculations of the ${}^{40}$Ca$(e,e'p)^{39}$K reaction without free parameters, yielding agreement with data as good as or better than previous analyses that fitted spectroscopic factors and radii to the data; the authors suggest that slightly larger spectroscopic factors are obtained with nonlocal optical potentials than with local ones, and that the 100 MeV outgoing proton energy window provides the cleanest DWIA analysis.

## Method

The authors employed a nonlocal implementation of the dispersive optical model (DOM) to generate both the overlap functions (with their normalizations) and the outgoing proton distorted waves for the ${}^{40}$Ca$(e,e'p)^{39}$K reaction. The DOM self-energy was constrained by fitting elastic nucleon-scattering observables, particle number, charge density, and separation energies up to 200 MeV. DWIA calculations were performed for proton knockout from the $0\textrm{d}\frac{3}{2}$ and $1\textrm{s}\frac{1}{2}$ orbitals in parallel kinematics at three outgoing proton energies (70, 100, and 135 MeV), and the resulting reduced cross sections were compared to experimental data from Nikhef without adjusting any free parameters.

## Key numbers

- Spectroscopic factors fixed by the DOM: 0.71 for the $0\textrm{d}\frac{3}{2}$ orbital and 0.60 for the $1\textrm{s}\frac{1}{2}$ orbital in ${}^{40}$Ca
- Data measured at three outgoing proton kinetic energies: 70, 100, and 135 MeV
- Electron beam energies ranged from 299 to 532 MeV
- Missing-energy resolution: 130-200 keV
- Coincidence detection efficiency: 97.1% for the 70 MeV and 135 MeV data sets, 98.5% for the 100 MeV data set
- Total systematic error: 6.0% for the 70 MeV and 135 MeV data sets, 2.8% for the 100 MeV data set
- DOM calculation for ${}^{40}$Ca yielded proton number Z=19.8 and neutron number N=19.7 (experimental values are 20 each)
- DOM constrained by elastic nucleon-scattering data up to 200 MeV

## Context

This work extends the dispersive optical model approach of Mahaux and Sartor (1991) and the nonlocal DOM implementation of Mahzoon et al. (2014) to provide a fully consistent description of $(e,e'p)$ reactions. It directly tests the validity of the DWIA framework previously employed by the Nikhef group (Den Herder, 1988; Kramer, 1989; Lapikas, 1993), which used separate local optical potentials and Woods-Saxon bound-state wave functions with fitted spectroscopic factors and radii. The results confirm that substantial ground-state correlations cannot be ignored in closed-shell nuclei and enable future DWIA analyses using only DOM ingredients, potentially resolving questions raised by Zhanov (2010), Furnstahl (2010), and Jennings (2011) about the interpretation of spectroscopic factors.
