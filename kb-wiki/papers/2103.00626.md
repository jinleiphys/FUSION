# Analysis of critical parameters for nonrelativistic models in symmetric nuclear matter

> In this work we have analyzed several features of symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) at finite temperature described by different zero- and finite-range nonrelativistic families of models, namely, Skyrme, Gogny, Momentum-dependent interaction (MDI), Michigan three-range Yukawa (M3Y) and Simple Effective Interaction (SEI). We have calculated the critical parameters (CP) associated to the liquid-gas phase coexistence for nuclear matter from these parametrizations and show that they are in agreement with their experimental and theoretical values obtained in the literature. Our study also points out to a strong evidence of universality presented by the hadronic models, namely, model independence in the gaseous phase and distinguishability among different interactions in the liquid phase. We have performed a correlation study among different CP and SNM properties. Such studies involving different finite range interactions are scarce in literature. The analyzed models show an overall increasing trend of the critical temperature as a function of critical pressure.

## Key claim

The authors demonstrate that the liquid-gas phase coexistence in symmetric nuclear matter, described by a wide range of nonrelativistic models (Skyrme, Gogny, MDI, M3Y, and SEI), exhibits a universal behavior: the scaled pressure-density isotherms are model-independent in the gaseous phase but distinguishable among different interactions in the liquid phase, a pattern previously observed only for relativistic mean-field models.

## Method

The authors calculated the critical parameters (critical temperature T_c, critical density ρ_c, and critical pressure P_c) for 20 Skyrme parametrizations, 7 Gogny parametrizations, 1 MDI parametrization, 9 M3Y parametrizations, and 8 SEI parametrizations by solving the conditions for the liquid-gas phase transition (vanishing first and second derivatives of pressure with respect to density) using finite-temperature mean-field theory. For zero-range Skyrme forces, the pressure was obtained directly from the energy density with Fermi-Dirac distributions, while for finite-range interactions (Gogny, MDI, M3Y, SEI) the coupled system of single-particle energies and momentum distributions was solved self-consistently to determine the pressure via the thermodynamical relation P = μρ - ℋ + 𝒮T.

## Key numbers

- Experimental critical temperature from Elliott et al. (2013): T_c = 17.9 ± 0.4 MeV
- Experimental critical pressure from Elliott et al. (2013): P_c = 0.31 ± 0.07 MeV/fm³
- Experimental critical density from Elliott et al. (2013): ρ_c = 0.06 ± 0.01 fm⁻³
- Skyrme parametrizations yield T_c values ranging from 14.36 MeV (SKRA) to 18.33 MeV (Skχ414)
- Gogny parametrizations yield T_c values ranging from 15.21 MeV (D280) to 17.16 MeV (D250)
- M3Y parametrizations yield T_c values ranging from 15.66 MeV (M3Y-P2) to 16.33 MeV (M3Y-P7)
- SEI parametrizations yield T_c values ranging from 14.55 MeV (SEIG207) to 17.35 MeV (SEIY282)
- All finite-range parametrizations (Gogny, MDI, M3Y, SEI) fall within the experimental ranges for both P_c and ρ_c
- Ten out of twenty Skyrme parametrizations lie below the experimental lower limit for P_c (those with m* ≤ 0.80, except Skxs20)
- The compressibility factor Z_c = P_c/(ρ_c T_c) for all parametrizations is smaller than the van der Waals value of 0.375

## Context

This work extends the previous investigation by Lourenço et al. (2017) on relativistic mean-field models to nonrelativistic models, providing the first comprehensive tabulation of critical parameters for almost all nonrelativistic finite-range effective nucleon-nucleon interactions available in the literature. The results are compared with experimental data from multifragmentation studies by Elliott et al. (2013) and with theoretical calculations from chiral effective field theory by Carbone et al. (2018). The observed universality in the scaled isotherms enables future studies to constrain the nuclear equation of state by linking critical parameters to bulk properties such as incompressibility and effective mass, which may help refine predictions for neutron star structure and heavy-ion collision dynamics.
