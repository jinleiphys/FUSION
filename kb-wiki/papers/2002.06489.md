# On the light-front wave functions of quarkonia

> The light-front wave functions of hadrons allow us to calculate a wide range of physical observables; however, the wave functions themselves cannot be measured. We discuss recent results for quarkonia obtained in basis light-front quantization using an effective Hamiltonian with a confining model in both the transverse and longitudinal directions and with explicit one-gluon exchange. In particular, we focus on the numerical convergence of the basis expansion, as well as the asymptotic behavior of the light-front wave functions. We also illustrate that, for mesons with unequal quark masses, the maxima of the light-front wave functions depend in a non-trivial way on the valence quark-mass difference.

## Key claim

The light-front wave functions of quarkonia obtained from basis light-front quantization with an effective Hamiltonian show that the maxima of the wave functions for unequal-mass mesons depend on the transverse momentum \(k_\perp\) in a non-trivial way, shifting from \(x > \frac{m_Q}{m_Q+m_q}\) at \(k_\perp = 0\) toward \(x = \frac{1}{2}\) as \(k_\perp \to \infty\), contrary to the naive expectation of a fixed peak at \(\frac{m_Q}{m_Q+m_q}\).

## Method

The authors diagonalize an effective light-front Hamiltonian for a quark-antiquark pair in a color-singlet, which includes a confining potential in both transverse and longitudinal directions (with strength \(\kappa\)) and explicit one-gluon exchange, using a basis expansion in 2-dimensional harmonic oscillator functions \(\phi_{nm}\) and Jacobi polynomials \(\chi_l\). The Hamiltonian matrix is solved numerically for the mass spectrum and light-front wave functions, with truncation parameters \(N_{\max}\) and \(L_{\max}\) controlling the basis size and \(n_x\) and \(n_k\) controlling the numerical integration points for the longitudinal and transverse coordinates.

## Key numbers

- The meson masses converge monotonically with increasing basis truncation \(N_{\max}\) when \(n_x\) and \(n_k\) are fixed, consistent with the variational principle; parameters were fitted at \(N_{\max} = L_{\max} = 32\) and \(n_x = n_k = 64\) to the lowest 14 states of bottomonium and 8 states of charmonium.
- The decay constants depend strongly on \(N_{\max}\) but are almost independent of \(n_x\) and \(n_k\); a UV cutoff \(\mu \approx 1.7 m_q\) was used in Ref. Li (2017) to truncate the transverse integral for the pseudoscalar decay constant.
- The effective UV truncation scale for the transverse basis is \(\Lambda = \kappa \sqrt{x(1-x) N_{\max}}\); below this scale the LFWFs of \(\eta_b\) and \(\eta_c\) are almost independent of \(N_{\max}\), while above it they oscillate and fall off like a Gaussian.
- The transverse distribution amplitude \(\phi_\perp(k_\perp)\) for \(\eta_b\) falls off faster than \(1/k_\perp^2\); the best fit gives \(k_\perp \phi_\perp(\vec{k}_\perp) \sim e^{-c k_\perp}\).
- The decay constants are extrapolated using \(f(N_{\max}) = f(N_{\max}=\infty) + a e^{-c \sqrt{N_{\max}}}\), yielding values significantly larger than those reported in Li (2017) and Tang (2018) which were truncated at a finite scale.
- For the \(B_c\) meson, the peak position in \(x\) at fixed \(k_\perp\) follows \(x = \left(1 + \sqrt{\frac{k_\perp^2 + m_q^2}{k_\perp^2 + m_Q^2}}\right)^{-1}\), with the same effective masses for the lowest pseudoscalar and vector states.

## Context

This work extends the basis light-front quantization approach developed in Li (2015) and Li (2017) for quarkonia by focusing on numerical convergence and asymptotic behavior of the light-front wave functions, and by analyzing the peak structure for unequal-mass mesons. The results enable more accurate calculations of observables such as decay constants, form factors, and distribution amplitudes, and connect to the light-front parton gas model of Jia (2018) for interpreting the momentum-space probability density. The asymptotic behavior of the transverse distribution amplitude suggests that the decay constant integral is finite even in a complete basis, but the authors note that alternative computational tools may be needed for a precise determination of the asymptotics.
