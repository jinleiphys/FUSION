# Maximal mass of neutron stars constrained by neutron star observations

> We investigate constraints on the high-density equation of state (EOS) of neutron star matter by analyzing the probability distributions of the endpoints of mass-radius M(R) sequences within a Bayesian weighting framework. Starting from two representative hadronic baseline EOSs, SFHo and DD2, matched at higher densities to an extended linear sigma model description and constrained to approach perturbative QCD (pQCD) results, we construct families of causal hybrid EOSs spanning a broad range of stiffness at supranuclear densities. Observational constraints from the binary neutron-star merger GW170817, mass-radius measurements from the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER), and candidate low-mass and mass-gap compact objects are incorporated through Bayesian likelihood weighting. This approach allows us to determine probability distributions for the maximum neutron-star mass M$_{\rm TOV}$ and the corresponding radius R$_{\rm TOV}$, i.e., the endpoints of the M(R) sequences. We find that the maximum-mass distributions are largely determined by observational constraints and show only weak sensitivity to the choice of baseline EOS, favoring values around 2.2-2.3 M$_\odot$ when the most robust constraints are applied. In contrast, the corresponding radius distributions exhibit a stronger dependence on the underlying hadronic EOS, with typical preferred values near $12\pm 1$ km. Additional tidal-deformability constraints further restrict the allowed parameter space and disfavor very stiff EOS realizations when interpreted together with the possible mass-gap neutron-star candidate. Our results demonstrate that endpoint distributions of M(R) sequences provide a sensitive and complementary diagnostic for constraining the high-density behavior of the neutron-star EOS within a multimessenger Bayesian framework.

## Key claim

The probability distributions for the maximum neutron-star mass (M_TOV) are largely determined by observational constraints and show only weak sensitivity to the choice of baseline hadronic equation of state, favoring values around 2.2-2.3 solar masses when the most robust constraints are applied, while the corresponding radius distributions exhibit a stronger dependence on the underlying hadronic EOS.

## Method

The authors constructed families of causal hybrid equations of state by matching two representative hadronic baseline EOSs (SFHo and DD2) at higher densities to an extended linear sigma model description, constrained to approach perturbative QCD results. They incorporated observational constraints from the binary neutron-star merger GW170817, NICER mass-radius measurements for five pulsars, and candidate low-mass and mass-gap compact objects through Bayesian likelihood weighting to determine probability distributions for the maximum neutron-star mass and corresponding radius.

## Key numbers

- Maximum-mass distributions favor values around 2.2-2.3 solar masses when the most robust constraints (pQCD + GW170817 + NICER) are applied simultaneously
- Radius distributions show typical preferred values near 12 +/- 1 km
- For the SFHo+eLSM EOS with all well-established constraints, the radius distribution peaks at approximately 11.8 km
- For the DD2+eLSM EOS with all well-established constraints, the radius distribution peaks at approximately 12.4 km
- The pQCD reference point used: mu_QCD = 2.6 GeV, rho_QCD = 6.47 fm^-3, p_QCD = 3823 MeV/fm^3
- The conservative tidal-deformability bound from GW170817: Lambda-tilde < 720
- The stronger tidal-deformability constraint (Lambda_190): 70 < Lambda_1.4 < 580, 9.1 km < R_1.4 < 12.8 km (90% confidence)
- Low-mass candidate HESS J1731-347: M = 0.77 (+0.20/-0.17) solar masses, R = 10.4 (+0.86/-0.78) km
- Mass-gap object GW190814: M = 2.59 (+0.08/-0.09) solar masses
- Population analysis yields conservative lower bound M_max >= 2.22 solar masses (Romani 2022)

## Context

This work extends the Bayesian framework from Kasza (2026) by focusing specifically on the endpoint distributions of mass-radius sequences rather than using maximum mass constraints directly. The results are consistent with the most massive neutron star currently known, PSR J0952-0607 with mass 2.35 +/- 0.17 solar masses (Romani 2022). The approach demonstrates that endpoint distributions of M(R) sequences provide a sensitive and complementary diagnostic for constraining the high-density behavior of the neutron-star EOS within a multimessenger Bayesian framework, and the combined constraints consistently favor maximum neutron-star masses near 2.2 to 2.3 solar masses.
