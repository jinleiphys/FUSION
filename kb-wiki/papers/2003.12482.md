# Anomalous chiral transports and spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions

> Relativistic heavy-ion collisions create hot quark-gluon plasma as well as very strong electromagnetic (EM) and fluid vortical fields. The strong EM field and vorticity can induce intriguing macroscopic quantum phenomena such as chiral magnetic, chiral separation, chiral electric separation, and chiral vortical effects as well as the spin polarization of hadrons. These phenomena provide us with experimentally feasible means to study the nontrivial topological sector of quantum chromodynamics, the possible parity violation of strong interaction at high temperature, and the subatomic spintronics of quark-gluon plasma. These studies, both in theory and in experiments, are strongly connected with other subfields of physics such as condensed matter physics, astrophysics, and cold atomic physics, and thus form an emerging interdisciplinary research area. We give an introduction to the aforementioned phenomena induced by the EM field and vorticity and an overview of the current status of their experimental research in heavy-ion collisions. We also briefly discuss spin hydrodynamics as well as chiral and spin kinetic theories.

## Key claim

This review argues that anomalous chiral transport phenomena (such as the chiral magnetic effect, chiral separation effect, and chiral vortical effect) and spin polarization in heavy-ion collisions provide experimentally feasible probes of the nontrivial topological sector of quantum chromodynamics and possible parity violation at high temperature, but the experimental observation of the chiral magnetic effect remains inconclusive because non-CME background effects from transverse momentum conservation and local charge conservation cannot yet be convincingly subtracted.

## Method

The paper provides a pedagogical introduction to the theoretical foundations of anomalous chiral transports, deriving the chiral anomaly from the Landau level picture and showing how the chiral magnetic effect, chiral separation effect, and chiral vortical effect emerge from the lowest Landau level in strong magnetic fields or rotating frames. It then reviews experimental measurements of the charge-dependent azimuthal correlation observable gamma_alpha_beta by the STAR, ALICE, and CMS Collaborations, and discusses the background contamination from transverse momentum conservation and local charge conservation that must be disentangled before claiming observation of the chiral magnetic effect.

## Key numbers

- For Au + Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV with impact parameter b = 10 fm, the estimated magnetic field strength is eB_y approximately -10 m_pi^2, which is about 10^19 Gauss.
- The peak value of the averaged kinematic vorticity in Au + Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV is about 10 MeV, corresponding to about 10^21 s^{-1}.
- For RHIC Au + Au collisions at sqrt(s) = 200 GeV and b = 10 fm, the total angular momentum is about 10^6 hbar.
- The chiral magnetic wave equations show propagation velocities determined by e^2/(4 pi^2 chi) times the magnetic field, where chi is the number susceptibility.
- The chiral electric separation effect current for two-flavor QCD at leading-log accuracy is proportional to 14.5163 Tr(Q_e Q_A) (mu_V mu_A / T^2) (eT / (g^4 ln(1/g))) times the electric field.

## Context

This review extends the line of work on anomalous chiral transports initiated by Kharzeev (2007), Fukushima (2008), and Son (2009) for the chiral magnetic effect, and by Vilenkin (1979), Erdmenger (2008), and Banerjee (2008) for the chiral vortical effect. It connects these phenomena to experimental measurements at RHIC and LHC, where the STAR Collaboration (2009) first measured the gamma correlation in Au + Au collisions at 200 GeV, and subsequent measurements by ALICE and CMS Collaborations have been performed at LHC energies. The review enables future work on disentangling the elliptic-flow-driven background from the magnetic-field-driven signal in the gamma correlation observable, and on developing spin hydrodynamics and chiral kinetic theories for the quark-gluon plasma.
