# Partial Dynamical Symmetry in Deformed Nuclei

> We discuss the notion of partial dynamical symmetry in relation to nuclear spectroscopy. Explicit forms of Hamiltonians with partial $SU(3)$ symmetry are presented in the framework of the interacting boson model of nuclei. An analysis of the resulting spectrum and electromagnetic transitions demonstrates the relevance of such partial symmetry to the spectroscopy of axially deformed nuclei.

# Partial Dynamical Symmetry in Deformed Nuclei

## Key claim
Partial dynamical SU(3) symmetry can be realized in real nuclei, specifically in the deformed nucleus ¹⁶⁸Er, where the ground and γ bands retain pure SU(3) character while the β band is mixed, thereby lifting the β-γ degeneracy that plagues exact SU(3) dynamical symmetry while preserving analytic solvability for a subset of states.

## Method
The author constructs an interacting boson model (IBM) Hamiltonian from boson pair operators with angular momentum L=0 and L=2, which is not an SU(3) scalar but has a subset of eigenstates with good SU(3) symmetry. These solvable eigenstates are obtained by projecting from intrinsic states representing γᵏ bands, yielding analytic expressions for energies and E2 transition rates for the ground and γ bands, while the β band contains SU(3) admixtures. The Hamiltonian parameters are fitted to three experimental energies in ¹⁶⁸Er (N=16 bosons), and the resulting spectrum and E2 rates are compared to data and to prior broken-SU(3) calculations by Casten, Warner, and Davidson (1991).

## Key numbers
- For ¹⁶⁸Er, the boson number is N=16.
- The ground band has pure SU(3) character (32,0); the γ band has pure SU(3) character (28,2).
- The β band contains 10% (26,0) and 3% (24,4) admixtures into the dominant (28,2) irrep.
- The E2 operator parameter ratio extracted from experiment is θ/α = 4.261.
- For 2⁺β → Jf transitions, calculated B(E2) ratios are (0.42:1.44:2.59:4.77:100.0) for Jf = (0⁺g, 4⁺g, 2⁺γ, 3⁺γ, 0⁺β); experimental ratios are (0.23:1.4:4.0:4.9:100.0).
- The exact SU(3) prediction for the same ratios is (0.47, 1.62, 0.93, 1.66, 100.0).
- The γ-band head energy is given by Eγ(L) = 6h₂(2N-1) + λL(L+1); the ground band by Eg(L) = λL(L+1).

## Context
This work extends the mathematical concept of partial dynamical symmetries (PDS), previously formalized by the author and collaborators, to a realistic nuclear application. It directly addresses the well-known failure of exact SU(3) dynamical symmetry in the IBM to reproduce the observed β-γ band splitting in deformed nuclei, a problem previously treated by completely breaking SU(3) as in the work of Casten, Warner, and Davidson (1991) or in the consistent Q formalism. The partial SU(3) symmetry approach retains analytic solvability for ground and γ bands while allowing mixing in the β band, achieving comparable agreement with data to the fully broken calculations. This enables future studies of mixed regular-chaotic systems in nuclear spectroscopy and can be extended to other semi-simple groups using the general PDS algorithm.
