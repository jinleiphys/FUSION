# Auxiliary-Field Quantum Monte Carlo Simulations of Neutron Matter in Chiral Effective Field Theory

> We present variational Monte Carlo calculations of the neutron matter equation of state using chiral nuclear forces. The ground-state wavefunction of neutron matter, containing non-perturbative many-body correlations, is obtained from auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo simulations of up to about 340 neutrons interacting on a 10^3 discretized lattice. The evolution Hamiltonian is chosen to be attractive and spin-independent in order to avoid the fermion sign problem and is constructed to best reproduce broad features of the chiral nuclear force. This is facilitated by choosing a lattice spacing of 1.5 fm, corresponding to a momentum-space cutoff of Lambda = 414 MeV/c, a resolution scale at which strongly repulsive features of nuclear two-body forces are suppressed. Differences between the evolution potential and the full chiral nuclear interaction (Entem and Machleidt Lambda = 414 MeV) are then treated perturbatively. Our results for the equation of state are compared to previous quantum Monte Carlo simulations which employed chiral two-body forces at next-to-next-to-leading order (N2LO). In addition we include the effects of three-body forces at N2LO, which provide important repulsion at densities higher than 0.02 fm^-3, as well as two-body forces at N3LO.

## Key claim

The authors present a novel auxiliary-field quantum Monte Carlo (AFQMC) method for neutron matter that avoids the fermion sign problem by constructing an attractive, spin-independent evolution Hamiltonian, but the ground-state wavefunction obtained from this evolution Hamiltonian only approximates the true ground state of the full chiral Hamiltonian, with differences treated perturbatively and discretization errors estimated as the dominant systematic uncertainty.

## Method

The authors simulate neutron matter on a 10^3 cubic lattice with spacing 1.5 fm (momentum cutoff 414 MeV/c) using AFQMC to project out the ground state of an evolution Hamiltonian that is attractive and spin-independent, thereby avoiding the fermion sign problem. The evolution potential is fit to reproduce NN phase shifts and the perturbative equation of state of the full chiral N3LO two-body plus N2LO three-body interaction (Entem and Machleidt 414 MeV cutoff). The expectation value of the full chiral Hamiltonian in the evolved ground state provides a variational upper bound on the energy, with the difference between evolution and full potentials treated as a first-order perturbative correction.

## Key numbers

- Simulations performed for densities from 0.01 fm^{-3} to 0.10 fm^{-3}, corresponding to particle numbers from 38 to 342 neutrons.
- Lattice spacing of 1.5 fm gives momentum-space cutoff Lambda = 414 MeV/c.
- Three-body forces at N2LO provide important repulsion at densities higher than 0.02 fm^{-3}.
- Imaginary time evolution requires about tau = 0.1 MeV^{-1} (approximately 300 time steps) for convergence.
- First-order perturbative correction between evolution and full chiral potentials is at most 10% of the total energy.
- Statistical error from Monte Carlo quadrature is below 1%.
- At density n = 0.011 fm^{-3}, the evolution potential and chiral two-body potential expectation values are shown with a nearly constant shift in imaginary time.
- The chiral N3LO potential reproduces NN phase shifts up to 200 MeV lab energy with chi^2/DOF = 1.44.
- Results for the energy per particle using only two-body forces are more repulsive by about 1 MeV compared to the local 400 MeV cutoff potential of Gezerlis et al. (2013).

## Context

This work extends previous quantum Monte Carlo studies of neutron matter that employed chiral two-body forces at N2LO (Gezerlis et al., 2013; Roggero et al., 2014) by introducing a sign-problem-free AFQMC method capable of simulating larger particle numbers and including three-body forces at N2LO and two-body forces at N3LO. The method enables future studies of nucleon spectral properties, linear response, and transport properties of dilute neutron matter, similar to what has been demonstrated for the unitary Fermi gas by Wlazlowski et al. (2013) and others.
