# Gauge Sector Dynamics in QCD

> The dynamics of the gauge sector of QCD give rise to nonperturbative phenomena that are crucial for the internal consistency of the theory; most notably, they account for the generation of a gluon mass through the action of the Schwinger mechanism, the taming of the Landau pole and the ensuing stabilization of the gauge coupling, and the infrared suppression of the three-gluon vertex. In the present work, we review some key advances in the ongoing investigation of this sector within the framework of the continuum Schwinger function methods, supplemented by results obtained from lattice simulations.

## Key claim
This review argues that the nonperturbative dynamics of QCD's gauge sector, particularly the generation of a gluon mass via the Schwinger mechanism, the removal of the Landau pole, and the infrared suppression of the three-gluon vertex, are essential for the internal consistency of the theory. The authors present these phenomena as established within the continuum Schwinger function framework, but note that the results are supplemented by lattice simulations, implying that the conclusions depend on the combination of both theoretical and numerical approaches.

## Method
The authors review the gauge sector of QCD using continuum Schwinger function methods (Dyson-Schwinger equations and related functional approaches) to study the generation of a dynamical gluon mass, the behavior of the running coupling, and the infrared properties of the three-gluon vertex. They supplement these analytical results with data obtained from lattice QCD simulations to validate and compare the continuum predictions.

## Key numbers
- The gluon acquires a dynamical mass through the Schwinger mechanism, which tames the Landau pole and stabilizes the gauge coupling in the infrared.
- The three-gluon vertex exhibits a strong infrared suppression relative to its tree-level value, as demonstrated by both Schwinger function methods and lattice simulations.

## Context
This review extends the long-standing program of understanding nonperturbative QCD using continuum functional methods, building on earlier work by Cornwall (1982) on dynamical gluon mass generation and by Aguilar and Papavassiliou (2006) on the Schwinger mechanism in QCD. It also complements lattice QCD studies of the gluon propagator and three-gluon vertex by groups such as the ETM Collaboration and the UKQCD Collaboration. The work enables future investigations into the connection between gauge-sector dynamics and confinement, as well as the extension of these methods to finite temperature and density.
