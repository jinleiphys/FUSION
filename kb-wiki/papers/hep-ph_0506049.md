# RHIC-tested predictions for low-$p_T$ and high-$p_T$ hadron spectra in nearly central Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC

> We study the hadron spectra in nearly central $A$+$A$ collisions at RHIC and LHC in a broad transverse momentum range. We cover the low-$p_T$ spectra using longitudinally boost-invariant hydrodynamics with initial energy and net-baryon number densities from the perturbative QCD (pQCD)+saturation model. Build-up of the transverse flow and sensitivity of the spectra to a single decoupling temperature $\Tdec$ are studied. Comparison with RHIC data at $\ssNN=130$ and 200 GeV suggests a rather high value $\Tdec=150$ MeV. The high-$p_T$ spectra are computed using factorized pQCD cross sections, nuclear parton distributions, fragmentation functions, and describing partonic energy loss in the quark-gluon plasma by quenching weights. Overall normalization is fixed on the basis of p+$\bar{\rm p}$(p) data and the strength of energy loss is determined from RHIC Au+Au data. Uncertainties are discussed. With constraints from RHIC data, we predict the $p_T$ spectra of hadrons in 5 % most central Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC energy $\ssNN=5500$ GeV. Due to the closed framework for primary production, we can also predict the net-baryon number at midrapidity, as well as the strength of partonic energy losses at the LHC. Both at the LHC and RHIC, we recognize a rather narrow crossover region in the $p_T$ spectra, where the hydrodynamic and pQCD fragmentation components become of equal size. We argue that in this crossover region the two contributions are to a good approximation mutually independent. In particular, our results suggest a wider $p_T$-region of applicability for hydrodynamical models at the LHC than at RHIC.

## Key claim

The paper predicts hadron spectra in 5% most central Pb+Pb collisions at the LHC (√sNN = 5500 GeV) by combining a hydrodynamic description at low pT with a pQCD fragmentation description at high pT, with both components constrained by RHIC data at √sNN = 130 and 200 GeV; the authors hedge that the two contributions are to a good approximation mutually independent in the crossover region where they become of equal size.

## Method

The low-pT spectra were computed using longitudinally boost-invariant hydrodynamics with initial energy and net-baryon number densities from the pQCD+saturation model (EKRT), assuming fast thermalization at formation time τ0 = 1/psat and a single decoupling temperature Tdec. The high-pT spectra were computed using collinearly factorized pQCD cross sections with nuclear parton distributions (EKS98 parametrization) and fragmentation functions, incorporating partonic energy loss in the quark-gluon plasma via quenching weights. The overall normalization was fixed from p+p̄(p) data, and the energy loss strength was determined from RHIC Au+Au data.

## Key numbers

- Saturation scale psat = 1.16 GeV for 0% central Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV; psat = 2.03 GeV for 0% central Pb+Pb at √sNN = 5500 GeV
- Formation time τ0 = 0.17 fm/c for 0% central Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV; τ0 = 0.10 fm/c for 0% central Pb+Pb at √sNN = 5500 GeV
- Decoupling temperature Tdec = 150 MeV suggested by comparison with RHIC data at √sNN = 130 and 200 GeV
- Net-baryon number at midrapidity: N(B−B̄) = 14.0 in 5% most central Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV; predicted N(B−B̄) = 3.11 in 5% most central Pb+Pb at √sNN = 5500 GeV
- Average initial energy density at RHIC (√sNN = 200 GeV) estimated at ε ≈ 5 GeV/fm³ at τ ∼ 1 fm from Bjorken estimate using dET/dη = 606–620 GeV
- σ⟨ET⟩ = 83.6 mb·GeV for 0% central Au+Au at √sNN = 200 GeV; σ⟨ET⟩ = 468 mb·GeV for 0% central Pb+Pb at √sNN = 5500 GeV

## Context

This work extends the pQCD+saturation+hydrodynamics model previously applied to RHIC at √sNN = 130 GeV (Eskola et al., 2002) to both √sNN = 200 GeV at RHIC and √sNN = 5500 GeV at the LHC, providing the first comprehensive predictions for LHC Pb+Pb collisions within this closed framework. The study builds on the saturation criterion of Eskola et al. (2000) and the quenching weights approach of Salgado and Wiedemann (2003), and it enables future LHC measurements to test the predicted wider pT-region of applicability for hydrodynamical models compared to RHIC, as well as the predicted net-baryon number and partonic energy loss strength at LHC energies.
