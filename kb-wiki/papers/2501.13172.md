# Comparison of Three Methods for Triggering Core-collapse Supernova Explosions in Spherical Symmetry

> Despite the three-dimensional nature of core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe), simulations in spherical symmetry (1D) play an important role to study large model sets for the progenitor-remnant connection, explosion properties, remnant masses, and CCSN nucleosynthesis. To trigger explosions in 1D, various numerical recipes have been applied, mostly with gross simplifications of the complex microphysics governing stellar core collapse, the formation of the compact remnant, and the mechanism of the explosion. Here we investigate the two most popular treatments, piston-driven and thermal-bomb explosions, in comparison to 1D explosions powered by a parametric neutrino engine in the P-HOTB code. For this comparison we calculate CCSNe for eight stars and evolution times up to 10,000 s, targeting the same progenitor-specific explosion energies as obtained by the neutrino-engine results. Otherwise we employ widely-used ("classic") modelling assumptions, and alternatively to the standard contraction-expansion trajectory for pistons, we also test suitably selected Lagrangian mass shells adopted from the neutrino-driven explosions as "special trajectories." Although the 56Ni production agrees within roughly a factor of two between the different explosion triggers, neither piston nor thermal bombs can reproduce the correlation of 56Ni yields and explosion energies found in neutrino-driven explosions. This shortcoming as well as the problem of massive fallback witnessed in classical piston models, which diminishes or extinguishes the ejected nickel, can be largely cured by the special trajectories. These and the choice of the explosion energies, however, make the modelling dependent on pre-existing neutrino-driven explosion results.

## Key claim
Neither piston-driven nor thermal-bomb explosion triggers can reproduce the correlation of 56Ni yields and explosion energies found in neutrino-driven explosions, although the 56Ni production agrees within roughly a factor of two between the different triggers. This shortcoming, along with the problem of massive fallback in classical piston models, can be largely cured by using "special trajectories" adopted from neutrino-driven results, but this makes the modelling dependent on pre-existing neutrino-driven explosion results.

## Method
The authors performed 1D core-collapse supernova simulations for eight red supergiant progenitors using the Prometheus-HOTB code, comparing three explosion triggers: a parametric neutrino engine (P-HOTB), a piston-driven trigger, and a thermal-bomb trigger. For each progenitor, the piston and thermal-bomb setups were tuned to yield the same explosion energy (within 3% accuracy) as the neutrino-driven explosion for that same progenitor. In addition to the "classic" piston method, the authors also tested "special trajectories" for the piston, which use the time-dependent radial positions of Lagrangian mass shells from the neutrino-driven explosions to define the piston motion.

## Key numbers
- 56Ni production between the different explosion triggers agrees within roughly a factor of two.
- The explosion energy for each progenitor was matched between the three methods within 3% accuracy in the worst cases.
- The terminal value of the explosion energy is usually reached after around 80 seconds of simulation time for thermal bomb and piston-driven explosions.
- The neutrino engine was calibrated using three parameter sets (W18, S19.8, W20) with final core radii of 6.0 km, 6.5 km, and 6.0 km, respectively.
- The "classical" piston method used a collapse time of 0.45 seconds and a minimum radius of 5 x 10^7 cm.
- The special-trajectory piston models used progenitor-specific collapse times ranging from 0.575 s to 1.842 s and minimum radii from 1.233 x 10^7 cm to 1.373 x 10^7 cm.

## Context
This work extends the comparison of 1D explosion triggers by Aubert et al. (1991) and Young et al. (2007) by directly comparing thermal-bomb and piston-triggered explosions to neutrino-engine-driven explosions from the P-HOTB code. The study uses presupernova models from Sukhbold et al. (2014) and builds on the calibration of the P-HOTB neutrino engine by Sukhbold et al. (2016) and Ertl et al. (2016). The results enable a better understanding of the systematic uncertainties in 1D supernova models used for nucleosynthesis and progenitor-remnant connection studies, and they highlight the need for caution when interpreting results from simplified explosion triggers.
