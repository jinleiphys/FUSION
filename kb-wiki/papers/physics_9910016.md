# Binding Energies and Scattering Observables in the 4^He_3 Atomic System

> The ^4He_3 bound states and the scattering of a ^4He atom off a ^4He dimer at ultra-low energies are investigated using a hard-core version of the Faddeev differential equations. Various realistic ^4He-^4He interactions were employed, amomg them the LM2M2 potential by Aziz and Slaman and the recent TTY potential by Tang, Toennies and Yiu. The ground state and the excited (Efimov) state obtained are compared with other results. The scattering lengths and the atom-diatom phase shifts were calculated for center of mass energies up to 2.45 mK. It was found that the LM2M2 and TTY potentials, although of quite different structure, give practically the same bound-state and scattering results.

# Binding Energies and Scattering Observables in the \(^4\)He\(_3\) Atomic System

## Key claim

The LM2M2 and TTY interatomic potentials, despite having quite different functional forms, produce practically identical bound-state energies and scattering observables for the \(^4\)He trimer system, including the ground state, the excited Efimov state, scattering lengths, and ultra-low-energy phase shifts up to 2.45 mK center-of-mass energy. This equivalence holds within the accuracy of the hard-core Faddeev differential equation calculations, which employed grids of up to 800×800 points and partial waves up to \(l_{\text{max}}=4\).

## Method

The authors solved the hard-core version of the Faddeev differential equations in configuration space for three identical bosons with total angular momentum \(L=0\), using a Boundary Condition Model (BCM) that replaces the strong short-range repulsion of the He–He interaction by a hard-core boundary condition at radius \(c=1.0\) Å. The partial-wave equations were discretized on a polar grid \((\rho,\theta)\) with up to 800 radial and 800 angular knots, extending to \(\rho_{\text{max}}=200\)–1000 Å, and solved via a block three-diagonal matrix sweep method. Four He–He potentials were employed: HFDHE2 (Aziz 1979), HFD-B (Aziz 1987), LM2M2 (Aziz and Slaman 1991), and TTY (Tang, Toennies, and Yiu 1995), with the dimer binding energy and scattering length precomputed for each.

## Key numbers

- Ground-state energy of the \(^4\)He trimer: for LM2M2 potential, \(E_t^{(0)} = -126.4\) mK (Table III, as referenced in text); for TTY potential, \(E_t^{(0)} = -126.3\) mK; contributions from \(l=0\) and \(l=2\) partial waves dominate, with \(l=4\) contributing 3–4 %.
- Excited (Efimov) state energy: for LM2M2, \(E_t^{(1)} = -2.27\) mK; for TTY, \(E_t^{(1)} = -2.27\) mK; the \(l=0\) partial wave contributes about 71 %, \(l=2\) contributes 25–26 %, and \(l=4\) contributes 3–4 %.
- Atom-dimer scattering length \(\ell_{\text{sc}}\): for HFD-B potential, \(\ell_{\text{sc}} = 145 \pm 5\) Å (improved from earlier work with smaller grid); for LM2M2 and TTY, the scattering lengths are exactly the same within numerical accuracy.
- Phase shifts were computed for center-of-mass energies up to 2.45 mK; the difference between phase shifts \(\delta_0^{(2)}\) (including \(l=0,2\)) and \(\delta_0^{(4)}\) (including \(l=0,2,4\)) is only about 0.5 %.
- The inverse wave number \(\varkappa^{-1}\) for the trimer excited state (where \(\varkappa = 2\sqrt{(\epsilon_d - E_t^{(1)})/3}\)) is about 1.3–1.7 times smaller than the corresponding atom-dimer scattering length \(\ell_{\text{sc}}\).

## Context

This work extends earlier studies of the \(^4\)He trimer by the same authors (Motovilov et al., 1998–1999) using improved numerical methods, larger grids, and more partial waves, and by other groups using variational methods (Rick, Lynch, Doll; Pandharipande; Barnett; Lewerenz; Guardiola; Gonzalez), hyperspherical harmonics (Esry, Lin, Greene; Nielsen), and momentum-space integral equations (Nakai; Gloeckle). The results confirm the Efimov nature of the excited state, previously identified by Gonzalez, Esry et al., Gloeckle, and the present authors, and provide the first systematic comparison of scattering observables (phase shifts and scattering lengths) for the LM2M2 and TTY potentials. The hard-core BCM formalism enables future studies of ultra-cold three-atom systems, including cross-sections and recombination rates, and can be applied to other atomic species where two-body interactions have strong repulsive cores.
